# Cancer Therapy Mechanisms and Evidence: A Cross-Literature Synthesis

**Authors:** Ezequiel Lares

**Target journal:** *Trends in Cancer* / *Nature Reviews Cancer*

**Article type:** Perspective

---

## Abstract

Cancer-therapy literatures often mature in parallel without being compared on shared axes such as evidence depth, resistant-state biology, pathway dependencies, or delivery constraints. Here we present a cross-literature analysis of 4,830 PubMed-indexed full-text cancer research articles (803 journals, 19 mechanisms, 22 cancer types, 2001-2026), alongside a separate archive of 5,585 abstract-only records, to map where research is concentrated (immunotherapy is by far the most-studied mechanism, ~1,685 articles), where apparent gaps are likely to be artifacts, and which mechanistic ideas deserve broader comparison. We measure the quality of our own evidence tagger (46% exact-label accuracy, 96% binary evidence-presence precision, 55% recall on a 100-article gold set), derive tissue-of-origin and weighted-evidence layers to sharpen mechanism comparisons, and introduce a diagnostic-to-therapy matching framework covering six translational chains. On top of this landscape, Monte Carlo and spatial simulations act as a claim-testing engine, used to validate or disprove specific mechanistic claims across cell phenotypes, tissue depths, drug penetration gradients, drug combinations, and oxygen-mediated tumor-microenvironment effects. The defensible contributions are directional and methodological, not magnitudes: dual-pathway ferroptosis depletion outperforms single-pathway; the cross-modality resistance asymmetry (physical ROS less vulnerable than pharmacologic induction to penetration and microenvironment barriers) holds in direction, though the hypoxia leg is genuinely contested; and apparent literature gaps are largely taxonomy and retrieval artifacts, not biology. The representative magnitudes below are order-of-magnitude estimates, none point-estimable under the documented parameter uncertainty (`analysis/identifiability-report.md`), each reported with its uncertainty interval: (1) dual inhibition of GPX4 and FSP1 produces ~1.99× Bliss synergy (95% prior-predictive ~1.0× to 5.2×, so the supra-additive direction is robust while the magnitude is not); (2) under simulated hypoxia, pharmacologic ferroptosis (RSL3) kill collapses from 3.7% to 0.1% while sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is far less affected, but only under the contested O2-independent assumption, so the SDT hypoxic-zone advantage brackets 0% to 86.6% (it collapses to roughly 0% if SDT's ROS is instead fully O2-dependent, the regime the lead clinical agent SONALA-001 occupies); and (3) tissue-specific drug penetration reduces a RSL3-like drug from 40% (2D) to 1.8% behind the blood-brain barrier, a penetration ordering that is parameter-robust across draws though the absolute magnitudes carry wide intervals. These illustrate how mechanistically distinct approaches meet different barrier landscapes; they are computational predictions, not modality verdicts. Within this broader frame, one conditional hypothesis is identified as worth testing rather than assuming: that physical ROS-generating modalities (especially SDT) might retain efficacy under hypoxia, at depth, or where drug penetration is limited, conditions where pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers fail. The simulations neither validate nor disprove it; they bound the conditions under which it could hold. The useful conclusion is not which modality looks interesting, but which interventions remain plausible after accounting for resistance state, tissue access, tumor microenvironment, evidence maturity, and known corpus artifacts. All simulation code (MIT-licensed Rust library with Python bindings), analysis pipelines, and data are openly available for replication and extension.

**Keywords:** cross-literature analysis, cancer therapy, evidence synthesis, resistant states, evidence gaps, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, ferroptosis, photodynamic therapy, sonodynamic therapy, radioligand therapy, pathway targets

# Part I: Why This Exists

## Chapter 1: The Problem

### 1.1 The Scale of Loss

Cancer accounted for nearly 10 million deaths worldwide in 2022, making it one of the leading causes of death on Earth [^news1]. Lung cancer alone produced 2.5 million new cases that year. Behind each of those numbers is a person, a family, a dinner table with an empty chair. The statistics are so large that they lose their human weight — but the weight is there.

This is not a new crisis. Governments, pharmaceutical companies, academic institutions, and advocacy organizations have spent decades and hundreds of billions of dollars fighting cancer. And in some areas, the investment has paid off remarkably well. Five-year survival rates for childhood acute lymphoblastic leukemia — once a near-certain death sentence — now exceed 90% (NCI SEER, 2014-2020). In the 1960s, a child diagnosed with ALL faced roughly a 10% chance of surviving five years. Today, through a combination of multi-agent chemotherapy, risk-stratified treatment protocols, and improvements in supportive care, that number has climbed above 90%. Female breast cancer, caught at the localized stage, has a five-year survival rate of nearly 100% (NCI SEER, 2015-2021). Overall, breast cancer survival stands at 91.7%, reflecting decades of investment in screening, surgical technique, and targeted therapies such as tamoxifen and trastuzumab. Testicular cancer is among the most curable solid tumors, with overall cure rates exceeding 95%.

These are genuine triumphs. They represent what cancer medicine can accomplish when early detection is possible, when effective therapies exist, and when the biology of the disease cooperates.

But the picture is radically uneven. Pancreatic cancer has an overall five-year survival rate of 13.3% (NCI SEER, 2015-2021). More than half of patients are diagnosed only after the disease has spread to distant sites, where survival drops to 3.2%. Even among the 15% of patients whose cancer is detected at the localized stage, the five-year survival rate is only 43.6% — far below the near-100% rates seen for localized breast cancer. Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma is lethal in approximately 88% of patients [^pmid37165196], a statistic that has improved only marginally over the past several decades. For recent reporting on early detection efforts for this cancer, see [^news2].

Glioblastoma, the most common malignant primary brain tumor, has a median survival of less than two years even with maximal treatment including surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy [^pmid32478924]. The addition of tumor-treating fields — a novel device-based approach — extended median survival to approximately 21 months compared to 16 months with chemotherapy alone [^pmid29260225], a meaningful improvement but still measured in months, not years.

Lung cancer, despite the revolution in immunotherapy, still carries a 28.1% five-year survival rate overall (NCI SEER, 2015-2021). For patients diagnosed with distant-stage disease — which represents the majority of cases for several lung cancer subtypes — that number falls to 9.7%.

The contrast is stark. For cancers where early detection works and biology cooperates, survival rates approach 100%. For cancers that are diagnosed late, grow in hard-to-reach locations, and harbor cells that resist treatment, survival rates remain in the single or low double digits despite decades of effort.

The question is not whether cancer research is making progress. It is. The question is why progress is so uneven — and what keeps certain cancers stubbornly resistant to everything we throw at them.

### 1.2 Where Treatments Stall

The cancers with the worst outcomes share a pattern. They tend to be diagnosed late, when the disease has already spread. They tend to involve tissues that are hard to reach surgically or with drugs. And critically, they tend to harbor cells that survive initial treatment and come back stronger.

Consider the trajectory of a patient with pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. The disease is lethal in approximately 88% of patients [^pmid37165196]. Surgery is possible in only a minority of cases, because most tumors are locally advanced or metastatic at diagnosis. Chemotherapy can shrink tumors and extend survival by months, sometimes years. But in the vast majority of cases, the disease returns. Not because the chemotherapy did nothing — it often does a great deal — but because a small population of cells survived, adapted, and eventually re-established the tumor.

The same pattern appears in glioblastoma, where surgical resection followed by radiation and temozolomide produces a median progression-free survival of approximately 7 months before the tumor recurs. It appears in metastatic melanoma, where immunotherapy produces dramatic responses in some patients but leaves others with progressive disease driven by resistant clones. It appears in ovarian cancer, where platinum-based chemotherapy often produces an initial complete response, only for the disease to recur in a platinum-resistant form.

This pattern — initial response followed by relapse driven by surviving cells — is the central obstacle to durable remission. It is the reason why a 99% tumor reduction can still end in death. The cancer doesn't need to outsmart the treatment at every turn. It only needs to survive it once.

Understanding why those cells survive, what makes them different from the cells that responded, and whether their survival creates new vulnerabilities that could be exploited before they recover — these are among the most important questions in modern oncology. For public coverage of ongoing research into how cancers evade immune surveillance, see [^news3].

### 1.3 Why Treatments Fail

Three biological realities explain why cancer is so difficult to eradicate, even when initial treatment works well.

**Tumors are populations, not clones.** A single tumor can contain billions of cells, and not all of them are the same. They carry different mutations, express different proteins, and rely on different metabolic strategies. When a drug targets one characteristic — say, a specific mutation — it kills the cells that depend on that characteristic. But the cells that don't depend on it survive. The tumor doesn't fight back in any conscious sense. It is simply a diverse population being filtered by selective pressure.

This is the same principle that drives antibiotic resistance in bacteria. When you expose a bacterial population to an antibiotic, the resistant survivors repopulate the colony. The antibiotic didn't create resistance; it selected for it from the existing diversity. Cancer operates on the same selection principle, though the mechanisms of diversification differ — bacteria can share resistance genes between cells, while tumor cell diversity arises primarily from mutation and epigenetic changes during cell division.

**Drug resistance is an almost inevitable consequence of treatment.** The cells that survive a first round of chemotherapy are, by definition, the ones that had some trait allowing them to survive. When those cells divide, they produce progeny that share that trait. Over time, the population shifts from drug-sensitive to drug-resistant. This is why many targeted therapies that produce dramatic initial responses — shrinking tumors by 90% or more — eventually fail. The responding cells are eliminated; the resistant cells remain and expand into the space left behind.

Resistance can emerge through many routes. Some cells physically pump drugs out before they can take effect, using molecular transporters that act like bilge pumps ejecting water from a leaking ship. Others alter the drug's molecular target so the drug can no longer bind to it — like changing a lock so the key no longer fits. Still others activate alternative survival pathways that bypass the one being blocked, maintaining cell survival through a different route even when the original pathway is shut down. And some cells simply stop dividing, entering a dormant state where they consume less energy, present fewer targets, and wait out the treatment like a bear in hibernation.

The details vary by cancer type and treatment, but the principle is consistent: **selective pressure drives adaptation.** This is not a bug in cancer biology. It is a fundamental feature of how evolution works at the cellular level, and it operates on timescales of weeks to months — fast enough to undermine even initially successful therapies.

**Killing most of a tumor is not the same as curing the disease.** This is perhaps the hardest reality for patients and families to understand. Imaging shows the tumor has shrunk. Blood markers have dropped. The clinical response looks excellent. But if even a small population of altered cells persists — hidden in tissue niches, metabolically dormant, invisible to standard detection — those cells can eventually reawaken and seed a recurrence that is harder to treat than the original.

This is not a failure of medicine so much as a reflection of how evolution works at the cellular level. Treatment changes the selective landscape. The cells that emerge from that landscape are the ones that were least affected by the treatment. And the next treatment must contend with a population that has already been filtered for survival.

### 1.4 The Residual Disease Problem

The cells that survive treatment are not merely leftovers. Increasingly, research shows that treatment itself can push cancer cells into altered states — sometimes called drug-tolerant persister states — where their biology is fundamentally different from the original tumor [^pmid41481741].

These residual cells may shift their metabolism, relying on different energy sources than the cells that responded to treatment. Where the original tumor might have relied heavily on rapid glucose consumption — the so-called Warburg effect, a preference for fermenting glucose even when oxygen is available that has defined cancer metabolism for over a century, — the surviving cells may switch to mitochondrial respiration, fatty acid oxidation, or other metabolic strategies that are less affected by the treatment that just killed their neighbors.

They may enter a state of suspended proliferation, dividing slowly or not at all. This makes them less visible to drugs that target rapidly dividing cells and less detectable by standard imaging methods that rely on metabolic activity. They may also upregulate protective mechanisms — molecular defenses that scavenge the toxic byproducts of cellular stress and repair damage faster than it accumulates.

And critically, these altered states may be temporary. Given time — days, weeks, perhaps months — the cells can revert, resume growing, and establish a recurrence that carries the resistance lessons learned from the first treatment. The clock is ticking in both directions: the patient needs the residual cells eliminated before they recover, and the residual cells need time to restore the defenses that will make them resistant to the next round of treatment.

This creates both a challenge and an opportunity. The challenge is obvious: residual cells are hard to find, hard to target, and biologically different from the cells we know how to kill. Standard approaches that worked against the original tumor may not work against the survivors because the survivors are not the same cells.

The opportunity is less obvious but potentially more important: if the altered state that makes these cells resistant to one type of attack also makes them vulnerable to a different type of attack, there may be a window — possibly brief — during which they could be eliminated before they recover. Whether such a window reliably exists in human tumors, how long it lasts, and whether any therapeutic approach can exploit it in time are open questions, not established facts.

This concept — that resistance to one insult might create sensitivity to another — is not new in biology. It has been observed in antibiotic combination therapy, where bacteria resistant to one antibiotic sometimes become hypersensitive to another. It appears in the concept of collateral sensitivity, where the evolutionary trade-offs required for resistance to one drug create dependencies that a second drug can exploit. And it is the foundation of synthetic lethality approaches in cancer, where two genetic events that are individually survivable become lethal in combination.

What is less clear is which specific vulnerabilities residual cancer cells develop, how long those vulnerabilities persist, which therapeutic approaches could realistically exploit them, and whether the exploitation window is long enough to matter clinically. A vulnerability that lasts 48 hours is biologically real but may be clinically useless if the treatment can't be delivered in time. A vulnerability that lasts three months is clinically actionable — but does it actually exist?

These are the questions that motivated this project. Not the claim that one particular approach is the answer, but the recognition that answering these questions requires comparing across therapeutic modalities and biological mechanisms in a way that the current literature — organized by individual modalities and studied within individual research communities — does not easily support.

### 1.5 The Current Landscape

The field is not short of promising approaches.

**Immune checkpoint inhibitors** have transformed outcomes for some cancers. The immune system has an inherent ability to recognize and attack cancer cells, but tumors often deploy molecular signals that tell immune cells to stand down. Checkpoint inhibitors block those signals, releasing the brakes on the immune response. Drugs such as pembrolizumab and nivolumab have produced durable responses in melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma, and several other tumor types — in some cases, converting previously terminal diagnoses into long-term survivorship. But the response is far from universal. Many patients do not respond at all. Among those who do, resistance can develop over time. And the tumor microenvironment — the complex ecosystem of immune cells, blood vessels, structural proteins, and signaling molecules that surrounds the tumor — plays a decisive role in determining whether immunotherapy succeeds or fails. "Cold" tumors that exclude immune cells remain largely impervious to checkpoint blockade.

**Engineered cell therapies**, particularly chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T), represent a different strategy: rather than releasing the brakes on existing immune cells, they manufacture new immune cells specifically designed to attack cancer. CAR-T therapy has been transformative in certain blood cancers. Patients with previously untreatable B-cell leukemias and lymphomas have achieved complete remissions that would have been unthinkable a decade ago. But extending this success to solid tumors has proven far more difficult. Solid tumors create hostile environments that suppress immune cells attempting to infiltrate them, surround themselves with physical barriers of connective tissue and extracellular matrix (the structural scaffolding between cells), and present a moving target as tumor cell populations evolve and change their surface markers.

**Targeted therapies** — drugs designed to exploit specific molecular features of a patient's cancer — represent the precision medicine ideal. When a tumor depends on a single mutated protein for its growth, a drug that blocks that protein can produce rapid, dramatic shrinkage. But resistance is the rule rather than the exception. Within months, cancer cells find alternative molecular routes to the same outcome, bypass the blocked pathway entirely, or mutate the drug target so it is no longer recognizable. The targeted therapy literature is filled with stories of dramatic initial responses followed by inevitable relapse.

**Newer modalities** are advancing through clinical trials and early adoption. Radioligand therapy attaches radioactive atoms to molecules that seek out specific receptors on cancer cells, delivering targeted radiation directly to tumors while sparing surrounding tissue. Bispecific antibodies are engineered proteins that physically bridge immune cells and cancer cells, forcing them into contact. mRNA cancer vaccines train the immune system to recognize tumor-specific mutations unique to each patient's cancer. Antibody-drug conjugates deliver toxic payloads directly to cancer cells using antibody guidance, like molecular guided missiles. Each of these approaches addresses a real limitation of earlier strategies, and several are producing encouraging results in clinical trials.

The challenge is not that we lack ideas. It is that each approach is typically developed, studied, and promoted within its own research community. The immunotherapy literature compares checkpoint inhibitors to each other. The cell therapy literature compares CAR-T designs. The targeted therapy literature compares small molecules against specific mutations. But the questions that matter most to patients — which approaches work under which conditions, for which types of resistance, in which tissue contexts — require comparisons that cut across these boundaries.

That cross-boundary comparison is what this project attempts.

## Chapter 2: What We Set Out To Do

### 2.1 The Open-Science Bet

This project started from a simple conviction: breakthroughs against diseases that destroy lives should be a human right, not a revenue stream. That conviction shapes every decision in this repository, from the MIT license on all code to the publicly available data and the openly documented analysis pipeline.

Open science is not a marketing strategy. It is a deliberate bet that the work has more value in the hands of the global research community than behind any paywall or patent wall. Every script, every simulation, every analysis document, and every draft of this manuscript is publicly accessible. Anyone can re-run the analysis from scratch, challenge the conclusions, extend the simulations, or take an idea in a direction we never considered. If someone takes something from this work, validates it in a lab, and it helps a patient — that is the point.

The trade-off is real. Open-sourcing everything means forgoing patent protection, proprietary advantage, and the kind of controlled dissemination that academic publishing traditionally provides. We accept that trade-off because the goal is contribution, not credit.

Concretely, this means: all code is MIT-licensed. The ferroptosis-core simulation engine is published as a standalone Rust library with Python bindings, so any researcher can use it independently of this manuscript. The corpus analysis pipeline is fully documented and re-runnable. The repository welcomes contributions — open an issue, submit a pull request, fork the entire project and take it somewhere better. Nothing is hidden, nothing requires special access, and nothing requires our permission.

### 2.2 Two Computational Lenses

This project uses two complementary computational approaches, neither of which replaces laboratory experiments but both of which can help focus them.

**Cross-literature analysis.** We used software to systematically read and categorize 4,830 full-text cancer research articles from 803 journals, spanning 19 therapeutic mechanisms and 22 cancer types. A separate archive of 5,585 abstract-only records provides additional coverage. The software tags each article by mechanism, cancer type, evidence level, tissue of origin, and other categories, then maps the relationships between them.

This reveals patterns that individual researchers are unlikely to see, because most researchers read within their field. An immunotherapy specialist reads immunotherapy papers. A nanomedicine researcher reads nanoparticle papers. A ferroptosis biologist reads ferroptosis papers. Each sees their own literature clearly but may not know what adjacent fields have found — or failed to find — about the same biological questions. Are the mechanisms that make cancer cells resistant to one type of treatment the same mechanisms that another field has already learned to exploit? Are two different research communities studying the same biological process under different names? Is a "gap" in one field's literature actually well-covered in another? These are the kinds of questions that emerge when you read across fields rather than within them.

The cross-literature analysis maps the full landscape so that comparisons across boundaries become visible. It does not replace expert judgment. It maps terrain; experts navigate it. And it has known limitations: the evidence tagger that classifies articles has 96% binary evidence-presence precision but only 55% recall, meaning what it tags is almost always real, but it misses a substantial share of valid evidence. That 55% is the evidence-level recall; mechanism-level recall, measured separately and non-circularly against expert MeSH descriptors (Section 3.6), is higher, about 90% for the mechanisms where it can be checked, so mechanism gap claims rest on a firmer footing than the evidence-recall figure alone would suggest. Absence in our data is not absence in the field. Every gap claim in this book should be read as "not detected in our keyword-based analysis of 4,830 articles" rather than "does not exist."

**Monte Carlo biochemical simulation.** We built a computational model of a specific cell-death pathway — ferroptosis, a type of cell death driven by iron-catalyzed damage to the lipid membranes that surround cells — and ran it millions of times with random variation to understand which conditions make cells more or less vulnerable.

Monte Carlo simulation is named after the casino in Monaco, but the output is statistical knowledge, not luck. By running the same model thousands or millions of times with slightly different starting conditions, we generate not a single answer but a distribution of possible outcomes. This tells us which results are robust (they happen every time regardless of variation) and which are fragile (they depend on specific parameter values being exactly right). When a simulation result holds across a wide range of random variation, it suggests a pattern worth investigating in the laboratory. When it doesn't, it flags an assumption that needs experimental verification.

The simulations are hypothesis-generating tools. They identify which biological questions are worth asking and which parameter combinations lead to interesting outcomes. They do not prove that any particular treatment works. Computation without experimental validation is speculation. But experiment without computational guidance is inefficient when there are more hypotheses to test than any laboratory can pursue.

### 2.3 What We Can and Cannot Contribute

The value of this work lies in the questions it raises and the comparisons it surfaces. Every finding in this book should be read as: "Under these assumptions, the model suggests X. Here is what would need to be true for X to hold in reality, and here is what would falsify it." That framing — hypothesis with falsification criteria — is the contribution.

What constrains it: we are not clinicians, and nothing here is medical advice or treatment guidance. We are not experimentalists — our simulations use estimated parameters and produce directional insights, not quantitative predictions. A simulation showing that drug efficacy drops under low-oxygen conditions identifies a mechanism worth testing; it does not predict what will happen in a specific patient.

Our corpus is 4,830 full-text articles — a meaningful sample but a small fraction of PubMed's millions of records. The archive was constructed from keyword queries targeting our 19 mechanism categories, which means articles using non-standard terminology, articles in non-English languages, and articles outside our search scope are not included. We also maintain 5,585 abstract-only records that extend coverage without full-text depth. When we report that a combination has not been studied, we mean it was not detected in our local analysis — not that it does not exist. Some landmark papers are known to be missing.

Our evidence tagger has 96% binary evidence-presence precision but only 55% recall: what we tag is almost always real, but we miss nearly half of valid evidence. We have documented these gaps throughout the book — not to excuse them, but because transparency about what we don't know is as important as reporting what we found.

# Part II: What We Found

## Chapter 3: The Literature Landscape

### 3.1 Introduction

Cancer research is rich in promising mechanisms but poor at comparing them on a common frame. Fields grow around modalities, delivery platforms, or molecular targets, yet the real translational questions often cut across those boundaries: which interventions have credible human evidence, which are trapped in preclinical loops, which resistant states are actually being targeted, and which conclusions are artifacts of search design rather than biology. This problem becomes acute when one literature becomes rhetorically compelling before it has been properly compared against adjacent approaches.

Residual disease, the cells that survive initial therapy, is a central obstacle to durable remission across modalities: survivors adapt their metabolism, evade immune pressure, or enter dormancy. Drug-tolerant persister cells are one well-studied example, and recent work shows that some persister states are sensitized to ferroptosis, an iron-dependent cell-death pathway driven by lethal lipid peroxidation [^pmid41481741]. The field has focused largely on pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers such as RSL3, erastin, and GPX4-directed strategies [^pmid35338310], but that is one delivery logic among many. The broader question this work asks is which resistant-state vulnerabilities are actually being pursued across the literature, where the evidence is concentrated, and whether some should be matched to different delivery logics entirely, including spatially selective physical modalities, immune-engaging strategies, or pathway-directed approaches that sit outside any single literature silo.

This article therefore takes a broader starting point than our earlier framing. We first map the full-text corpus across 19 mechanism tags and 22 cancer types, assess evidence maturity, and highlight where gap claims are unstable because of taxonomy, missing full text, or incomplete evidence coverage. We then use that broader landscape to evaluate one narrower implication: whether physical ROS-generating modalities such as photodynamic therapy (PDT), sonodynamic therapy (SDT), and more speculatively cold atmospheric plasma (CAP) should be treated as candidate tools for locally targetable, ferroptosis-prone residual states rather than as generic alternatives to drugs. This reframing matters because PDT/SDT are not the only adjacent directions that emerge when the repo is examined without modality loyalty. Radioligand therapy, phagocytosis checkpoints, targeted protein degradation, stromal shielding, senescence programs, and lipid-remodeling defenses all compete for attention once the analysis is widened.

We present a systematic analysis of 4,830 full-text cancer research articles spanning 19 therapeutic mechanisms and 22 cancer types (803 journals, 2001-2026), with a separate archive of 5,585 abstract-only records, together with simulation work that tests specific mechanistic claims (ferroptosis sensitivity, drug penetration, microenvironment effects, and combinations) across cell phenotypes and tissue conditions. The aim is not to prove that one modality is the answer. It is to identify which hypotheses remain plausible after accounting for evidence depth, known corpus artifacts, and the biology of residual disease.

### 3.2 Search Strategy and Corpus Construction

We conducted a systematic literature search of PubMed using the NCBI E-utilities API, covering publications indexed through March 2026. The search strategy employed mechanism-specific query sets using established MeSH terms and free-text synonyms: for example, "tumor treating fields" OR "TTFields" OR "Optune" for TTFields; "chimeric antigen receptor" OR "CAR-T" OR "CAR T cell" for CAR-T therapy; "sonodynamic therapy" OR "sonosensitizer" for sonodynamic approaches. During revision, we also identified additional underrepresented areas for future corpus expansion, including CAP, radioligand therapy/theranostics, targeted protein degradation, phagocytosis checkpoints, autophagy, senescence/SASP, stromal biology, cuproptosis, and disulfidptosis. The quantitative results reported in this manuscript still refer to the frozen 19-mechanism corpus analyzed here; the expanded taxonomy is a next-pass scaffold rather than a retroactive redefinition of the present results. That scaffold is now executed concretely: the working taxonomy carries 25 mechanism definitions (the frozen 19 plus six scaffold mechanisms, including the cuproptosis and disulfidptosis regulated-cell-death modalities), and a per-mechanism coverage report (PubMed literature size and local-corpus coverage) is maintained outside the book in `analysis/taxonomy-coverage-report.md`; it does not re-tag the frozen corpus or alter any number reported here. Results were enriched with metadata from OpenAlex (open access status, citation counts, topic classification) and PubMed Central or publisher-hosted OA URLs (full-text retrieval for open access articles).

### 3.3 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

Articles were included if they were returned by the PubMed search queries and addressed at least one of the 19 therapeutic mechanisms in the context of cancer treatment or cancer biology. No additional manual inclusion or exclusion filtering was applied beyond the search query constraints. The resulting archive therefore includes original research, clinical trial reports, systematic reviews, narrative reviews, and some case series. For quantitative analyses in this manuscript, we indexed only the 4,830 records for which local full text was available; the remaining 5,585 metadata/abstract-only records were retained in a separate archive and not used for the full-text-derived corpus statistics reported below. This distinction is important: article counts in the Results section refer to the full-text corpus unless explicitly stated otherwise.

#### 3.3.1 Corpus Retrieval Bias

The corpus construction pipeline introduces bias at three stages, quantified below.

**Retrieval-flow denominator chain.** Of 10,415 unique PubMed records returned by the 19 mechanism-specific queries, 4,830 (46.4%) had locally available full text and were indexed for quantitative analysis. The remaining 5,585 (53.6%) were retained as abstract-only records. Of the 4,830 full-text articles, 2,038 (42.2%) received automated evidence-level tags; the other 2,792 were indexed but not evidence-classified (see tagger recall limitations in Section 3.6). The quantitative results in this manuscript are drawn from the 4,830 full-text subset — 46.4% of all PubMed records retrieved and 19.6% of those with evidence tags.

**Open-access bias.** The full-text corpus is overwhelmingly open access: 4,769 of 4,830 articles (98.7%) are classified as OA by OpenAlex. This is not a random sample of PubMed results — the pipeline retrieves full text from PubMed Central and publisher OA endpoints, systematically excluding paywalled articles. Journals with lower OA rates (common in engineering, physics, and device-focused fields) are therefore under-represented in the full-text corpus even when their articles appear in PubMed.

**Abstract-vs-full-text sensitivity check.** To assess whether the full-text restriction materially changes mechanism rankings, we compared mechanism tag frequencies between the 4,830 full-text articles and the 5,585 abstract-only records (93.0% of which have mechanism tags from the same keyword-based tagger operating on abstract text). The top two mechanisms — immunotherapy (2,297 full-text vs 1,800 abstract-only articles) and nanoparticle delivery (515 vs 982) — maintain their rank positions. However, four physical/energy modalities shift substantially upward in abstract-only rankings: bioelectric modulation rises from full-text rank 14 to abstract rank 3 (+11 positions, 182 vs 595 articles), HIFU from 17 to 7 (+10, 81 vs 382), sonodynamic therapy from 11 to 5 (+6, 187 vs 428), and electrochemical therapy from 12 to 8 (+4, 185 vs 378). Conversely, several mechanisms drop in abstract-only rankings: metabolic targeting falls from rank 8 to 15 (−7), and both oncolytic virus and synthetic lethality drop 6 positions. All four rising modalities are physical/energy approaches published in engineering and device-focused journals with lower OA rates.

This sensitivity check should not be treated as interchangeable with the full-text analysis — abstract-derived tags operate on shorter text with potentially different precision/recall characteristics. However, the directional finding is clear: physical modalities are systematically under-represented in the full-text corpus. Mechanism rankings and article-count-based claims for these modalities should be interpreted with this bias in mind. The full-text analysis remains the primary evidence base for this manuscript because it enables richer tagging (evidence levels, pathway targets, tissue categories) that abstracts alone cannot support.

The full per-mechanism open-access-rate table and the ranking-shift figures above are now regenerated from committed corpus metadata by `scripts/oa_bias_analysis.py` (output `analysis/oa-bias-report.md`), so they are reproducible rather than hand-derived. That report also quantifies the magnitude of the distortion the rank shifts imply. The abstract-only archive (5,585 records, including the VISION radioligand abstract recovered in issue #345) is only 29.1% open access (versus 98.7% for the full-text corpus), and the full-text-versus-abstract-only ranking correlation is moderate (Spearman 0.66), driven almost entirely by the physical/device modalities. Immunotherapy's share of all mechanism tags falls from 34.4% in the full-text corpus to 28.7% once the non-open-access abstract literature is folded in, so its dominance survives the correction but its apparent margin is inflated by open-access bias. The physical/energy class (TTFields, HIFU, sonodynamic, bioelectric, electrolysis, electrochemical, CAP, frequency) rises in the opposite direction, from 14.7% to 22.4% of mechanism tags. The honest reading is therefore that immunotherapy dominance is real but over-stated by open access, and that physical-modality under-representation reflects open-access access patterns as much as research volume.

**Query-design bias.** The cross-mechanism volume counts carry a third, query-level bias that is independent of open access. The newer and more novel mechanisms (CRISPR, CAR-T, antibody-drug conjugates, bispecific antibodies, mRNA vaccines, synthetic lethality, oncolytic viruses, epigenetic therapy, targeted protein degradation, radioligand therapy, and related lanes) were deliberately retrieved with a recent date window (a 2020, 2022, or 2023 lower bound through 2026), and every query was fetched with a per-query cap of 500 records, while the historically broader physical, frequency, and immunotherapy modalities were retrieved without a date window. This was an intentional first-pass design choice, documented in `scripts/queries.txt`, to keep the newer lanes from overwhelming the older 19-mechanism corpus, but it has a consequence that must be stated plainly: the per-mechanism article counts are a within-fetch comparison, not a field census. The date-capped mechanisms' counts are floor-limited to their recent window and truncated at 500 records per query, so their apparent volume understates their true literature size, and the cross-mechanism volume ranking should be read as a comparison of what this specific retrieval design returned rather than as a measurement of each field's absolute size. This particular bias does not inflate immunotherapy (which is not itself date-capped) and, if anything, it under-counts the date-capped novel mechanisms relative to the uncapped physical and historical ones.

### 3.4 Categorization Framework

Each article was tagged along three axes:

**Therapeutic mechanism(s):** Articles were assigned one or more of 19 tags representing distinct therapeutic modalities. We use "mechanism" broadly to encompass molecular targets (e.g., synthetic lethality), drug classes (e.g., ADCs), delivery platforms (e.g., nanoparticles), device-based interventions (e.g., TTFields), and biological strategies (e.g., microbiome modulation). This heterogeneous taxonomy reflects the diversity of the contemporary oncology toolkit; we prioritized practical distinctness over strict ontological consistency. The 19 tags are: (1) antibody-drug conjugate (ADC), (2) bioelectric modulation, (3) bispecific antibody, (4) CAR-T cell therapy, (5) CRISPR gene editing, (6) electrochemical therapy (including irreversible electroporation), (7) electrolysis, (8) epigenetic therapy, (9) frequency therapy (including pulsed electromagnetic fields), (10) high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), (11) checkpoint immunotherapy, (12) mRNA cancer vaccine, (13) metabolic targeting, (14) microbiome modulation, (15) nanoparticle delivery, (16) oncolytic virotherapy, (17) sonodynamic therapy, (18) synthetic lethality, and (19) tumor-treating fields (TTFields). Articles addressing multiple mechanisms received multiple tags, enabling convergence analysis.

**Cancer type(s):** Articles were assigned to one or more of 22 cancer types: bladder, breast, cervical, colorectal, esophageal, gastric, glioblastoma, head-and-neck, kidney, leukemia, liver, lung, lymphoma, melanoma, mesothelioma, myeloma, neuroblastoma, ovarian, pancreatic, prostate, sarcoma, and thyroid.

**Evidence level:** Original-research-like articles were classified into one of seven tiers: (1) Phase III clinical trial or randomized controlled trial, (2) Phase II clinical trial, (3) Phase I clinical trial, (4) non-phase clinical / patient-series evidence (`clinical-other`), (5) preclinical in vivo, (6) preclinical in vitro, or (7) theoretical/computational. Review-like articles (reviews, systematic reviews, meta-analyses, evidence maps) and protocols were intentionally left unclassified rather than being forced into an evidence tier. Classification was based on keyword detection plus publication-type metadata for the highest level of evidence reported in the article text. The `clinical-other` bucket is deliberately conservative: it indicates patient-level evidence exists, but should not be interpreted as equivalent to phase-labeled trial maturity.

### 3.5 Analytical Approach

Four primary analyses were conducted:

*Mechanism-cancer matrix:* A 19 x 22 cross-tabulation of article counts by mechanism and cancer type, identifying concentration patterns and gaps.

*Convergence map:* Identification and quantification of all mechanism-pair co-occurrences across the corpus, revealing which multi-mechanism combinations are most actively researched.

*Evidence-tier analysis:* Assessment of the highest clinical evidence level achieved for each mechanism, identifying translational bottlenecks.

*Gap analysis:* Identification of mechanism-cancer and mechanism-mechanism pairs with zero or near-zero co-occurrence in our corpus, noting that some gaps reflect search query design rather than genuine research absence.

Impact metrics (citation counts, iCite Relative Citation Ratio) were used to identify landmark publications and assess field-normalized influence. Publication volume was tracked annually to identify growth trajectories.

### 3.6 Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, all tagging was performed by automated keyword matching against curated dictionaries (no manual article-level review), introducing potential misclassification. A 100-article stratified gold-set evaluation of the evidence tagger measured 46% exact-label accuracy, with binary evidence-detection precision of 96%, recall of 55%, and F1 of 0.701 [FIGURE 9]. The tagger therefore behaves as a conservative detector: when it assigns an evidence label, that label is almost always correct, but it misses roughly half of valid evidence-bearing records — particularly in the clinical-other (23% recall), theoretical (5% recall), and preclinical-in-vitro (35% recall) categories. These metrics should be used to calibrate how absence claims are interpreted throughout.

[FIGURE 9: Evidence tagger performance — precision and recall per evidence label from 100-article stratified gold-set evaluation.]

The 55% figure above is the evidence-level recall, and it is the only recall the 100-article gold set can measure. Because that sample was drawn from the tagger's own positive predictions, every sampled article already carries its mechanism tag, so the gold set has no mechanism false-negatives by construction and cannot measure mechanism recall at all. We therefore measured mechanism recall separately and non-circularly, using expert-assigned MeSH descriptors as an independent reference label (a controlled-vocabulary concordance check, not manual ground truth). To remove the circularity, the tagger was re-run on a leakage-free text (title and abstract only, with the curated MeSH and annotation fields excluded so the reference label is never visible to the matcher), and the headline used only MeSH leaves whose own string would not trivially trip a mechanism's keywords. Across the 9 mechanisms that have such an independent MeSH leaf with a usable pool, mechanism recall is 90.6% (volume-weighted) and 89.2% (macro), far above the 55% evidence-level recall, so the tagger recovers the large majority of expert-labelled mechanism records from title and abstract alone where this can be checked without circularity. The lowest measurable value is epigenetic, where a real keyword-coverage gap was the larger of two causes: the keyword set originally lacked common field terms such as "epigenetic regulation/modification" and EZH2 inhibitor. Adding those precision-first terms lifted epigenetic recall from 54.7% to 69.8%; the residual gap reflects the breadth of its lone independent descriptor, since "DNA Methylation" also tags basic methylation biology that is not epigenetic therapy. Several modalities have no discriminative MeSH descriptor and are reported as not MeSH-measurable rather than as zero recall: the device and field therapies (TTFields, electrolysis, bioelectric modulation, cold atmospheric plasma), the regulated-cell-death newcomers (cuproptosis, disulfidptosis), and targeted protein degradation and radioligand therapy. The practical consequence for the coverage caveats throughout this book is that absence claims rest on mechanism detection, which, where it can be measured without circularity, is high (about 90%), so for those mechanisms a non-detection carries more weight; for the not-measurable modalities the original provisional-absence caution still applies. Full method and per-mechanism results are in `analysis/mechanism-recall-report.md`.

Short or ambiguous terms may generate false positives, and articles using non-standard terminology may be missed. Second, the archive was constructed from PubMed queries only and does not capture every relevant publication, particularly articles in non-English languages, preprints not yet indexed in PubMed, or studies using unconventional terminology for established mechanisms (Section 3.3.1 quantifies the retrieval bias and its effect on mechanism rankings). Third, the quantitative corpus analyzed here is restricted to the 4,830 records with local full text; although this improves traceability and reproducibility, it also excludes 5,585 abstract-only records that may materially change article-count-based field comparisons. A second-order consequence is that some landmark papers are missing from the local full-text archive even when they are clearly field-defining at the PubMed level; radioligand therapy is the clearest current example because the VISION trial (PMID 34161051) is not locally present. Fourth, evidence-level classification was based on keyword detection plus publication-type metadata (e.g., "Clinical Trial, Phase III", "phase 3", "pilot study", "case report", "in vivo") rather than independent trial registry verification; importantly, evidence-tier coverage remains incomplete even after adding a conservative `clinical-other` bucket for non-phase patient studies. Claims about the absence of clinical trials for specific mechanisms should therefore be interpreted as "not detected in our local keyword-based analysis" rather than definitive statements. Fifth, multi-mechanism co-occurrence counts reflect articles tagged with multiple mechanism keywords, which includes both intentional combination studies and reviews that discuss multiple mechanisms without testing them in combination; a corpus-wide designed-combination classifier (Section 3.13) measures the designed-combination rate at 19.7%, substantially below the 40% co-occurrence rate. Sixth, we tested the 19-mechanism taxonomy for sensitivity by collapsing the 7 physical/energy categories into 1-3 broader groups under 3 alternative groupings (see analysis/taxonomy-sensitivity-preregistration.md for pre-registered targets). Zero-publication gaps in the mechanism-cancer matrix drop from 94 (22.5% of cells) under the baseline 19-mechanism taxonomy to 29-38 (10-12%) under collapsed groupings — a 60-69% reduction — confirming that gap counts are taxonomy-dependent. The three "must survive" conclusions (immunotherapy dominance, nanoparticle maturity gap, existence of Phase III physical modality evidence) hold under all groupings. However, the specific gap count and the apparent fragmentation of physical modalities are artifacts of the fine-grained taxonomy. Seventh, our current simulation framework does not fully model in vivo lipid remodeling, including SCD-driven MUFA enrichment and other backup ferroptosis defense programs, so computational support for ferroptosis-based interventions remains stronger for in vitro-like assumptions than for established human tumor biology. Finally, article counts serve as a proxy for research intensity but do not directly measure quality, reproducibility, or clinical significance, and the corpus inherits any publication biases present in the indexed literature.

### 3.7 Corpus Overview

The final full-text corpus comprised 4,830 articles from 803 journals, with impact metrics available for 3,739 articles via iCite. Publication volume increased 31-fold over the study period, from 38 full-text articles in 2015 to 1,167 in 2025, with 137 articles already indexed in the first quarter of 2026. Growth was non-linear, with a marked inflection point in 2020 (257 full-text articles, a 2.4-fold increase over 2019's 106 articles), coinciding with the maturation of checkpoint immunotherapy, cell therapy, and RNA-platform attention. One important revision-era lesson is that not every post-2020 rise should be read as cancer-therapy-specific signal: for example, mRNA-vaccine counts were materially inflated until supportive and infectious-vaccine spillover was removed from that tag.

[FIGURE 2: Publication volume by year (2015-2026), stacked by mechanism category, showing the exponential growth trajectory and the 2020 inflection point.]

The fastest-growing mechanisms by absolute article count between 2020 and 2025 were immunotherapy (+504% annual articles), nanoparticle delivery (+800%), CAR-T therapy (+423%), and sonodynamic therapy (+355%). These growth rates reflect both the maturation of established approaches and the emergence of new platform technologies.

### 3.8 Physical and Energy-Based Mechanisms

#### 3.8.1 Tumor-Treating Fields (TTFields)

TTFields, which deliver low-intensity (1-3 V/cm), intermediate-frequency (100-300 kHz) alternating electric fields to disrupt mitotic spindle formation and organelle assembly in dividing cells [^pmid33144698], represent the most clinically advanced physical therapy mechanism in our corpus. We identified 262 TTFields-tagged full-text articles across 14 of 22 cancer types. The evidence-level analysis identified 19 articles classified at the Phase III tier; however, this count still includes secondary analyses and trial-adjacent reports in addition to primary Phase III trial publications. The true number of distinct Phase III TTFields trial reports is therefore smaller than the keyword-derived total.

The evidence base is anchored by the EF-14 trial, a pivotal Phase III randomized clinical trial demonstrating that TTFields plus maintenance temozolomide significantly improved median overall survival (20.9 vs. 16.0 months, p < 0.001) and 5-year survival (13% vs. 5%) in newly diagnosed glioblastoma [^pmid29260225]. This trial, with 2,494 citations, is the fifth-highest-impact article in our entire corpus (RCR 69.5, 100th percentile). An earlier randomized trial [^pmid26670971] (1,203 citations) established the foundation for FDA approval of the Optune device.

Beyond glioblastoma, TTFields are being investigated in mesothelioma (38 articles), lung cancer (51 articles), pancreatic cancer (27 articles), and ovarian cancer (15 articles). The LUNAR Phase III trial demonstrated improved overall survival in metastatic non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC) when TTFields were added to standard systemic therapy [^pmid37657460]. A comprehensive review characterizes TTFields as a distinct fourth modality of cancer treatment alongside surgery, radiation, and pharmacotherapy [^pmid33144698].

TTFields have zero or near-zero publications in prostate cancer, lymphoma, myeloma, bladder cancer, and cervical cancer in our corpus, though some of these gaps may reflect search query design rather than genuine absence.

#### 3.8.2 High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound (HIFU)

HIFU uses convergent acoustic energy to achieve thermal ablation of tumor tissue at a defined focal point without incision. Our full-text corpus contains 81 HIFU articles across 12 cancer types. The dominant application is prostate cancer (29 full-text articles), where HIFU is established as a focal therapy option [^refs_group1]. Notably, a multicentric study reported 5-year outcomes of focal HIFU for clinically significant nonmetastatic prostate cancer [^pmid29960750] (309 citations), demonstrating favorable oncologic control with preserved quality of life.

HIFU research in breast cancer (42 articles), pancreatic cancer (50 articles), and liver cancer (22 articles) is expanding. A systematic review positions HIFU alongside radiofrequency ablation, microwave ablation, and cryoablation as viable alternatives to surgical resection for small tumors [^pmid33840627].

#### 3.8.3 Sonodynamic Therapy

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) exploits low-intensity ultrasound to activate sonosensitizer molecules that generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) within tumor tissue. Our full-text corpus contains 187 SDT articles across 15 cancer types, making it one of the most active physical modalities in the energy-based subset. However, SDT faces significant translational challenges. Our keyword-based evidence classification detected only 1 Phase I article in the local full-text corpus, with the balance of the literature remaining heavily preclinical. Broader PubMed verification suggests more early clinical activity may exist outside the locally indexed full-text set, but within the reproducible corpus analyzed here SDT remains overwhelmingly a preclinical field with no detected Phase II or Phase III full-text signal.

The preclinical literature is large and growing. Landmark studies have demonstrated nanoenzyme-augmented SDT with catalytic tumor oxygenation [^pmid29613770] (571 citations), graphene-augmented sonocatalytic tumor eradication [^pmid28829584] (300 citations), and piezotronic effect-augmented sonosensitizers [^pmid35699224] (218 citations). The convergence of SDT with nanoparticle delivery and immunotherapy is particularly active, with emerging evidence for SDT-based immunogenic cell death [^refs_group2].

A distinctive molecular signature emerges from our full-text corpus analysis that keeps SDT relevant to the ferroptosis conversation even after broadening the manuscript frame. SDT is strongly associated with **ferroptosis** language — iron-dependent, lipid peroxidation-mediated cell death — with 36 full-text articles linking SDT to ferroptosis, compared to 0 for TTFields, 1 for HIFU, and 3 for IRE in the frozen corpus. More specifically, 79 SDT full-text articles (42% of the SDT corpus) mention the glutathione/GPX4 axis, underscoring how centrally redox buffering appears in this literature. The proposed causal chain — ultrasound-activated ROS generation → glutathione depletion → GPX4 inactivation → lipid peroxidation → ferroptosis → DAMP release → immunogenic cell death — is supported at each individual link by published preclinical data [^refs_group3]. What this pattern does **not** establish is clinical distinctiveness over PDT; that comparison is addressed in the Discussion.

The primary barriers to clinical translation include the lack of standardized sonosensitizer formulations approved for human use, the absence of validated ultrasound dosimetry protocols for SDT (as distinct from diagnostic or HIFU applications), and the challenge of demonstrating SDT superiority or complementarity to established modalities in registrational trials. Breast cancer is the most studied SDT target in the full-text corpus (42 articles), followed by liver cancer (19 articles) and pancreatic cancer (12 articles).

#### 3.8.4 Frequency Therapy and Electrolysis

Frequency therapy — encompassing pulsed electromagnetic fields (PEMF), radiofrequency ablation in immunomodulatory contexts, and resonance-based approaches — is represented by 71 full-text articles across 11 cancer types. This is a nascent field with mechanistic diversity that complicates systematic evaluation. A foundational review [^pmid27748048] (113 citations) described mechanisms of PEMF in oncology, including disruption of mitotic spindle formation, induction of apoptosis, and immunomodulatory effects.

Electrolysis as a direct antitumor mechanism is the smallest category in our full-text corpus (11 articles across only a handful of cancer contexts), but includes innovative work on amorphous liquid metal electrodes for conformable electrochemical tumor therapy [^pmid28918265] (114 citations) and platinum nanoparticle-enabled electrodynamic therapy [^pmid30734370] (211 citations).

#### 3.8.5 Electrochemical Therapy

Electrochemical therapy (EChT), dominated by irreversible electroporation (IRE), is represented by 185 full-text articles across 14 cancer types. Pancreatic cancer (61 articles) and prostate cancer (30 articles) are the primary applications. A foundational paper introduced the concept of tissue ablation through irreversible electroporation [^pmid15771276] (1,241 citations), and subsequent clinical development has demonstrated safety and feasibility in locally advanced pancreatic cancer [^pmid26258317] (400 citations) and localized prostate cancer [^refs_group4].

A pivotal development is the combination of IRE with immunotherapy: high-frequency IRE has been shown to induce immunogenic cell death [^pmid31130474] (168 citations), and emerging data demonstrate synergy between IRE and checkpoint blockade plus TLR7 stimulation [^pmid31409607] (111 citations). The IRE-immunotherapy convergence (45 articles) represents a growing axis of investigation. However, lymphoma, leukemia, gastric cancer, and myeloma have zero EChT publications despite established clinical experience in other solid tumors.

### 3.9 Bioelectric Modulation

Bioelectric research encompasses membrane potential manipulation, ion channel modulation, voltage-gated signaling in cancer biology, and electroporation-based drug delivery. Our full-text corpus contains 182 articles across 19 cancer types, with broad conceptual range but limited direct clinical antitumor translation.

A landmark Cell paper established the conceptual framework for bioelectric signaling as a reprogrammable circuit underlying embryogenesis, regeneration, and cancer [^pmid33826908] (463 citations), arguing that endogenous bioelectric patterns constitute an informational layer that can be therapeutically manipulated. Earlier work on endogenous voltage gradients controlling growth and form [^pmid28633567] (289 citations) provided the biophysical foundation. The identification that mitochondrial membrane potential marks cells with enhanced stemness [^pmid26674251] (370 citations) has implications for targeting cancer stem cell populations.

Despite this conceptual richness, translational progress toward direct antitumor bioelectric interventions has been limited. In the current evidence-tier rerun, no phase-labeled trial evidence was detected for direct bioelectric antitumor therapy. The practical implication is that the bioelectric literature remains conceptually rich but clinically immature within this corpus.

### 3.10 Immune-Based Approaches

#### 3.10.1 Checkpoint Immunotherapy

Checkpoint immunotherapy is the dominant mechanism in our full-text corpus, with 2,297 articles across all 22 cancer types. This reflects its established clinical role across multiple tumor types. The highest concentrations are in lung cancer (250 articles), melanoma (207 articles), breast cancer (162 articles), pancreatic cancer (138 articles), colorectal cancer (136 articles), liver cancer (133 articles), and glioblastoma (117 articles).

Key landmark articles include a comprehensive review of PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 checkpoint inhibitors [^pmid35621637] (1,191 citations), the comprehensive management review establishing temozolomide plus TTFields as the standard of care in glioblastoma and surveying immunotherapy as a promising but significantly challenged area due to the blood-brain barrier and unique immune microenvironment [^pmid32478924] (1,989 citations), and combination immunotherapy for hepatocellular carcinoma reviewing how approximately 30% of HCC exhibits intrinsic resistance to single-agent ICIs and how combination strategies (anti-PD-1/PD-L1 with anti-angiogenics or dual checkpoint blockade) have improved first-line outcomes [^pmid36933770] (310 citations). STAT3 targeting as a convergence point for oncogenic signaling and immunomodulation has been extensively reviewed [^pmid32972405] (1,011 citations).

The most active convergence axes for immunotherapy in the full-text corpus include combinations with CAR-T (417 articles), oncolytic viruses (262 articles), nanoparticle delivery (193 articles), mRNA vaccines (131 articles), CRISPR (166 articles), bispecific antibodies (139 articles), metabolic targeting (127 articles), microbiome modulation (100 articles), ADCs (78 articles), SDT (53 articles), and TTFields (47 articles). Immunotherapy remains the central hub of the convergence network.

#### 3.10.2 CAR-T Cell Therapy

CAR-T therapy is represented by 474 full-text articles across all 22 cancer types. The literature has evolved from early hematologic successes in B-cell leukemia and lymphoma [^pmid31848460] (1,400 citations) toward aggressive expansion into solid tumors.

Key developments include: IL-15-armored GPC3-targeted CAR-T cells for solid cancers [^pmid39604730] (170 citations), representing a next-generation approach to overcoming the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment; Claudin18.2-specific CAR-T cells showing promising Phase I results in gastrointestinal cancers [^pmid35534566] (546 citations); and IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells that resist dysfunction and mediate durable clearance of solid tumors [^pmid38168996] (228 citations), demonstrating that cytokine engineering of CAR-T cells can overcome exhaustion.

CRISPR-based engineering of CAR-T cells is a rapidly growing convergence axis (95 full-text articles), exemplified by RASA2 ablation enhancing antigen sensitivity [^pmid36002574] (207 citations) and adenosine A2A receptor deletion enhancing efficacy [^pmid34050151] (199 citations). The allogeneic CRISPR-Cas9-engineered CAR-T therapy CTX130, targeting CD70 in T-cell lymphoma, has demonstrated safety in Phase I [^pmid39617017] (58 citations), representing a milestone for gene-edited cell therapies. CAR-T combined with oncolytic viruses (40 articles) and bispecific antibodies (30 articles) represents additional emerging convergence zones.

### 3.12 Delivery and Microenvironment

#### 3.12.1 Nanoparticle Delivery

Nanoparticle-based delivery systems constitute the second-largest mechanism category in our full-text corpus: 515 articles across 20 cancer types. An important distinction must be drawn between established and experimental nanoplatforms. Several nanomedicines have achieved FDA approval, including liposomal doxorubicin (Doxil, 1995), nab-paclitaxel (Abraxane, 2005), liposomal irinotecan (Onivyde, 2015), and liposomal daunorubicin-cytarabine (Vyxeos, 2017) [^pmid37047585]. These approved nanomedicines are typically described by drug name rather than "nanoparticle" in the literature and are therefore underrepresented in our corpus. The translational gap identified here primarily applies to novel experimental nanoplatforms — metal, polymer, and inorganic nanoparticles — which remain overwhelmingly preclinical in our full-text corpus. This distinction between proven liposomal/albumin-bound formulations and the broader experimental nanoparticle literature is critical: the former demonstrate that nanomedicine can succeed clinically, while the latter face substantial barriers to replicating that success.

The nanoparticle literature spans gold, silver, and iron oxide nanoparticles [^pmid39501968] (88 citations), liposomal formulations [^pmid37047585] (215 citations), lipid nanoparticles for mRNA delivery [^pmid35969778] (371 citations), and precision nanomedicine for tumor microenvironment modulation [^pmid40457437] (101 citations). The convergence with immunotherapy (193 articles) and sonodynamic therapy (116 articles) is extensive, as nanoparticles serve as both therapeutic agents and delivery vehicles for other modalities.

The fundamental translational barriers include: (a) heterogeneous nanoparticle pharmacokinetics and biodistribution that are difficult to predict from preclinical models; (b) manufacturing scalability and reproducibility challenges; (c) regulatory uncertainty around the classification and approval pathway for nanoparticle-drug combinations; and (d) the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, which is robust in rodent tumors but inconsistent in human cancers. Breast cancer (172 articles), lung cancer (76 articles), and liver cancer (66 articles) are the primary targets. Mesothelioma has zero nanoparticle publications despite the appeal of local delivery to pleural surfaces.

### 4.1 Evidence Maturity and Weighted Scoring

Evidence tags are currently populated for 2,038 of 4,830 full-text records (42.2%). Reviews and meta-analyses (1,921) and protocols (29) are intentionally left unclassified; among the remaining primary-study-like records, coverage is 2,038 of 2,880 (70.8%). The gold-set evaluation described in Section 3.6 confirms that this coverage reflects a conservative detector: tagged evidence is almost always real, but a substantial share of valid evidence goes undetected, particularly in the clinical-other, theoretical, and preclinical-in-vitro categories.

[FIGURE 10: Evidence tier composition by mechanism (stacked horizontal bar). Only articles with detected evidence tags shown.]

To move beyond raw counts, we computed a weighted evidence score for each mechanism that combines evidence tier (Phase III = 12 points, down to theoretical = 0.5), citation percentile (1.0-1.5x modifier), and publication recency (0.9-1.1x modifier). The resulting ranking shows immunotherapy (2,617), bispecific antibodies (687), TTFields (568), and ADCs (558) as the most evidence-mature mechanisms. Under this baseline weighting, nanoparticles rank sixth by weighted evidence despite ranking second by article count, reflecting their overwhelmingly preclinical composition. Bioelectric modulation has the lowest average weight per tagged article (1.69), despite 144 tagged evidence rows, indicating high volume but minimal clinical signal.

A weight-sensitivity analysis tested this ranking under 5 alternative weighting schemes (see analysis/weight-sensitivity-preregistration.md for pre-registered targets and stability definitions). Immunotherapy dominance (rank 1) and bottom-tier ordering are stable across all 6 schemes. However, the nanoparticle maturity drop is tier-weight-dependent: under equal-tier weighting (all tiers = 1.0), nanoparticle volume dominates and the mechanism returns to rank 2; under flattened tiers (Phase III = 4× instead of 24× in-vitro), it rises to rank 3. The drop holds under 4 of 6 schemes — those that weight clinical evidence heavily — but fails under the two schemes that reduce tier dominance. This demonstrates that the nanoparticle maturity gap reflects our editorial choice to weight clinical evidence strongly, not an inherent property of the nanoparticle literature. The maximum rank shift under equal-tier weighting is 7 positions (TTFields), confirming that tier weights are the dominant driver of ranking differences.

### 4.2 Main Contribution: A Broader Frame Before A Narrower Hypothesis

The most important contribution of this article is not a claim that one modality should replace another. It is a change in framing. The frozen 19-mechanism corpus shows that oncology is not short of therapeutic ideas; it is short of disciplined ways to compare them. Mechanism counts, convergence maps, and evidence tiers reveal a field dominated by a few high-volume hubs, but they also show how quickly apparently clean conclusions can be destabilized by taxonomy choices, missing full text, incomplete evidence tagging, and tag contamination from adjacent literatures. That matters because many attractive “gaps” are not true absences, and many active literatures are not equally mature.

Within that broader frame, one narrower implication remains worth pursuing. If residual disease states are genuinely OXPHOS-skewed, lipid-peroxidation-prone, and locally targetable, then physical ROS-generating modalities may be worth evaluating alongside pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers rather than outside the conversation. That is a conditional hypothesis downstream of the landscape analysis, not the article's starting assumption.

A tissue-of-origin layer derived from existing cancer-type tags provides a coarser but more interpretable view of where research is concentrated. Of the 4,830 full-text records, 2,995 unique articles (62.0%) have at least one derived tissue category. Because a single article can study multiple cancer types and therefore map to more than one tissue group, the per-category counts below are overlapping and sum to 3,241 rather than 2,995: epithelial (2,212), neuroectodermal (667), hematologic (245), mesenchymal (77), and mesothelial (40). The physical-modality literature is overwhelmingly concentrated in epithelial and neuroectodermal contexts, with hematologic and mesothelial categories containing almost no physical-modality signal [FIGURE 11]. This serves as a check against over-generalizing localized solid-tumor strategies to all cancers. The tissue layer is derived from the same cancer-type tags used in the mechanism-cancer matrix — it improves interpretation but does not fix upstream tagging errors.

### 4.3 What The Corpus Actually Supports About PDT, SDT, and CAP

The corpus and external cross-checking support a more modest position than our earlier framing implied.

**First, PDT is the established precedent.** PDT has the larger ferroptosis and ferroptosis-plus-ICD literature, more mature clinical history, and approved photosensitizers. Any argument for SDT must therefore explain why it adds something beyond a depth extension of an older modality rather than pretending SDT is uniquely linked to ferroptosis or ICD.

**Second, SDT's real advantage is anatomical, not conceptual.** Ultrasound reaches deep soft tissue that light cannot. For pancreatic, hepatic, or pelvic disease, SDT has a genuine physical-access advantage over PDT. But the mechanism remains broadly shared: externally triggered ROS, redox-buffer exhaustion, lipid peroxidation, and potential DAMP release. The clinically relevant question is therefore where depth changes the therapeutic opportunity enough to matter.

**Third, CAP is a speculative adjacent lane, not a co-equal conclusion.** CAP broadens the class of externally imposed ROS therapies, but in the current local corpus it is represented by only 3 full-text articles and should therefore be treated as a hypothesis-generating comparator for accessible or locoregional settings, not as a mature pillar of the argument.

**Fourth, the strongest version of the PDT/SDT claim is now conditional.** Physical ROS modalities are most plausible when residual disease is locally targetable, when spatial selectivity matters more than systemic reach, and when ferroptosis-related vulnerability is expected to persist long enough for intervention. The unresolved in vivo problem is exactly where this claim can fail: SCD/MUFA remodeling, DHODH-linked backup defenses, NRF2 compensation, stromal buffering, and other context-dependent escape programs may narrow or erase the apparent window.

### 4.4 Diagnostic-to-Therapy Matching

The corpus supports a pilot diagnostic-to-therapy matching framework that connects diagnostic modalities through targetable biological features to therapeutic interventions. Ten translational chains are evaluated, requiring the intervention link plus at least one of the diagnostic or feature links to reduce false positives from articles discussing diagnostics or therapies in isolation. The original six chains (radioligand, somatostatin-receptor PRRT, PD-L1 checkpoint selection, TMB/MSI immunotherapy, neoantigen mRNA vaccine, and oncolytic virotherapy) are joined by the four most clinically deployed predictive-biomarker-to-targeted-drug pairings, added in issue #441: HER2 testing to trastuzumab, BRCA mutation to PARP inhibitor, EGFR mutation to EGFR inhibitor, and KRAS-G12C mutation to sotorasib. Chain membership is recomputed from the frozen corpus text using the current chain set, so expanding the chains leaves the frozen index and every other corpus number unchanged.

Of 4,830 full-text articles, 240 (5.0%) match at least one diagnostic-therapy chain, up from 129 across the original six chains. The distribution is uneven. Neoantigen-profiling-to-mRNA-vaccine still leads with 79 articles, reflecting the rapid growth of personalized vaccine research, followed by BRCA-mutation-to-PARP-inhibitor with 71 (concentrated in ovarian and breast cancer), TMB/MSI-to-immunotherapy with 33, HER2-testing-to-trastuzumab with 28 (mostly breast, with gastric and lung), PD-L1-IHC-to-checkpoint with 13, EGFR-mutation-to-EGFR-inhibitor with 12 (all in lung), PSMA-imaging-to-radioligand with 4 (a number that reflects the corpus's known gap for the VISION trial, PMID 34161051, rather than the clinical maturity of this chain), and oncolytic-susceptibility-to-virotherapy with 1. Two chains return zero in this corpus: SSTR-imaging-to-PRRT and KRAS-G12C-mutation-to-sotorasib.

The low match rates do not mean these chains are clinically unimportant. They mean the local corpus was constructed around mechanism keywords, not around diagnostic-therapy pairings, so many translational papers use terminology the matching rules do not capture, and the targeted-therapy chains in particular read far below their true clinical literature volume. EGFR-mutation-to-EGFR-inhibitor recovers only a dozen of the thousands of EGFR-targeted-therapy papers in the wider literature, and KRAS-G12C-mutation-to-sotorasib returns nothing at all, which says the corpus lacks those emerging-targeted-therapy papers, not that the chain is unimportant. The framework is useful for identifying where the corpus has translational depth (mRNA vaccines, BRCA-PARP, checkpoint biomarkers, HER2) versus where it does not (radioligand theranostics, neuroendocrine PRRT, KRAS-G12C inhibitors).

### 4.5 Known Corpus Gaps

The local full-text corpus is a sample, not a census. Five clinically important papers have been confirmed as missing from the full-text archive, each large enough to distort mechanism-level claims if their absence is not acknowledged:

The VISION trial (PMID 34161051) — the field-defining Phase III study for lutetium-177 PSMA-617 in metastatic prostate cancer — is absent from both the full-text and abstract archives. Its absence makes "no phase-labeled trial evidence detected" for radioligand therapy a corpus artifact rather than a reflection of the modality's clinical maturity. Radioligand therapy is FDA-approved and clinically established; the corpus makes it look immature.

PANOVA-3 (PMID 40448572), a pivotal Phase III TTFields study in locally advanced pancreatic cancer, is present in the abstract archive but missing from full text. Its absence affects how mature TTFields appears outside its original glioblastoma indication. Three mRNA vaccine studies (PMIDs 33016924, 36027916, 35970920) are similarly present in abstracts but missing from full text, weakening the apparent depth of patient-level evidence for the vaccine pipeline.

These gaps reinforce the caveat stated throughout this book: absence in the local corpus is not absence in the field. Any mechanism-level claim that depends on article counts — especially claims about what the corpus does NOT contain — should be cross-checked against PubMed before being treated as evidence of immaturity or disinterest.

# Part III: What The Simulations Show

## Chapter 5: The Ferroptosis Engine

### 5.1 What the Model Represents

Before presenting what the simulations found, it is worth explaining what they model and why. The chapters that follow (Chapters 6 and 7) present specific predictions about drug combinations, tissue penetration, and tumor microenvironment resistance. Every one of those predictions depends on the model described here. Understanding the model — what it captures, what it simplifies, and what it ignores — is necessary to judge whether any individual prediction deserves experimental follow-up.

The simulation models ferroptosis, a specific type of cell death in which uncontrolled oxidation of the lipid membranes that enclose cells leads to membrane rupture and death. The process involves five key components, each represented in the model:

**Glutathione (GSH)** is the cell's primary antioxidant buffer — a supply of chemical that both scavenges reactive oxygen species (ROS) directly and serves as the essential cofactor that GPX4 requires to do its repair work. Without GSH, GPX4 cannot function. In the model, GSH is consumed by ROS at a rate governed by Michaelis-Menten kinetics — the more GSH available, the faster ROS is scavenged, but with diminishing returns at high concentrations.

**GPX4** is the enzyme that converts dangerous lipid peroxides back to harmless products. It is the cell's primary firefighter — the last line of defense before lipid damage becomes self-sustaining. In the model, GPX4 activity is dynamic: it is degraded by oxidative stress and upregulated by the NRF2 transcription factor. RSL3, a pharmacologic ferroptosis inducer, works by covalently inactivating GPX4.

**FSP1** is a backup repair pathway, independent of GPX4 and GSH. It acts like a sprinkler system — a second line of defense that operates through a different mechanism (CoQ10-mediated radical trapping rather than GSH-dependent peroxide reduction). In the persister cell phenotype, FSP1 is downregulated, leaving GPX4 as the sole repair mechanism and making these cells more vulnerable.

**Lipid peroxidation (LP)** represents the accumulating damage to cell membrane lipids. This is the fire. Critically, lipid peroxidation is autocatalytic: each damaged lipid molecule generates a radical that can damage adjacent lipid molecules, creating a chain reaction. In the model, once LP exceeds the cell's repair capacity, the cascade accelerates — one damaged molecule becomes two, then four, then eight. This creates a bistable switch: cells either recover (repair outpaces damage) or collapse irreversibly (damage outpaces repair).

**The death threshold (10.0)** is the LP level at which the cell is considered dead (a model-defined threshold, not a measured biological constant). Below this threshold, the cell can in principle recover if repair mechanisms are restored. Above it, the membrane is compromised beyond recovery. This is the point where the fire becomes uncontrollable.

Each treatment acts on this system differently. **RSL3** covalently inactivates GPX4 — it disables the firefighter, leaving only the backup sprinkler system (FSP1). **SDT and PDT** deliver large doses of exogenous ROS — they start a much bigger fire than the cell's normal metabolism produces. **FSP1 inhibitors** disable the backup sprinkler system, which is devastating when combined with GPX4 inhibition because it leaves the cell with no repair pathway at all.

### 5.2 Simulation Design

To test whether the proposed persister ferroptosis vulnerability is biochemically plausible, we developed a Monte Carlo simulation of the ferroptosis cascade. The model simulates the ROS → GSH depletion → GPX4/FSP1 inactivation → lipid peroxidation → cell death chain for individual cells with stochastic parameter variation, using literature-sourced biochemical values (Methods in Supplementary). We simulated 1,000,000 cells for each of 16 conditions (4 phenotypes × 4 treatments = 16,000,000 total cells).

**Model features**: (i) autocatalytic lipid peroxidation propagation gated by GSH/GPX4 — creating a bistable switch where cells either recover or collapse irreversibly[^porter2005]; (ii) dynamic GPX4 regulation — degraded by oxidative stress, upregulated by NRF2[^dodson2019]; (iii) FSP1 as a GPX4-independent repair pathway[^bersuker2019], downregulated in the persister phenotype[^pmid41481741]; (iv) uncapped GSH depletion under exogenous ROS. The model used for these results carries only the GSH/GPX4 and FSP1 defenses; the library has since added the other two established GPX4-independent axes, DHODH (mitochondrial CoQ10 reduction) and GCH1/BH4 (radical-trapping), as off-by-default terms (ferroptosis-core 0.34.0), so combinations such as GPX4i plus DHODHi can be explored, but they are uncalibrated and not used for any number in this book. The MUFA lipid-remodeling defense also gained an optional acute-dosing mode (ferroptosis-core 0.35.0): a freshly dosed tumor can start below the established MUFA steady state and accumulate protection over the run, instead of beginning fully protected. This adds an explicit acute-versus-established handle to the in-vivo lipid-remodeling gap flagged in Section 3.3.1, though like the other additions it is off by default, uncalibrated against the 48 to 72 hour SCD1 enrichment timescale, and unused for any number reported here. A later refinement (ferroptosis-core 0.45.0, extended in 0.46.0) makes both the MUFA accumulation rate and its carrying capacity phenotype-specific, so a drug-tolerant persister can build MUFA protection at a different rate, and saturate at a different steady state, than a glycolytic cell rather than sharing one global rate and cap; the per-phenotype direction is itself uncertain (SCD1/MUFA confers resistance, yet persisters are also GPX4-dependent and ferroptosis-vulnerable), so it is off by default, uncalibrated, and likewise unused for any number here. A companion off-by-default ether-lipid axis (ferroptosis-core 0.36.0) represents the polyunsaturated ether phospholipid pool (made via FAR1 and AGPS) as extra oxidizable substrate that increases ferroptosis, so its loss is the resistance route; this is the well-supported direction (Zou 2020[^pmid32939090]; Cui 2021[^pmid33731874]) and the opposite sign to the loose reading that a shift toward ether lipids is itself an escape. The contested plasmalogen and TMEM189 vinyl-ether sub-step is deliberately left unmodeled. A third lipid-remodeling source, MBOAT1 and MBOAT2 (ferroptosis-core 0.37.0), is exposed as an optional constant MUFA-enrichment term: these hormone-regulated acyltransferases push membranes toward MUFA and suppress ferroptosis independently of GPX4, so the knob represents an estrogen or androgen driven protective axis (Liang 2023[^pmid37267948]). All three new lipid axes are off by default, uncalibrated, and unused for any number reported here. Separately, the static labile-iron assumption gained an off-by-default dynamic path (ferroptosis-core 0.38.0): NCOA4-mediated ferritinophagy releases stored iron into the labile pool over the run, feeding Fenton chemistry (Mancias 2014[^pmid24695223]; Hou 2016[^pmid27245739]), with a companion hypoxia coupling in which low oxygen raises iron import. That coupling qualifies the hypoxia-leg framing of Section 7.1: hypoxia can raise the iron contribution even as it lowers the oxygen-dependent sonodynamic yield, so a blanket "hypoxia protects against RSL3" is too strong. It is now wired into the spatial model (Section 7.1): enabling it raises RSL3 ferroptosis kill several-fold, including in the deep hypoxic core. That deep-core rise is a model artifact, because the default Fenton ROS term is oxygen-independent (a real anoxic core would also lose the H2O2 substrate that drives Fenton). A follow-up off-by-default option (ferroptosis-core 0.44.0, issue #383) now couples the Fenton substrate to local oxygen, and enabling it removes the deep-core rescue, confirming it was a substrate artifact (Section 7.1); the honest takeaway remains the direction, hypoxia can raise the iron contribution, not the deep-core magnitude. Like the lipid axes these knobs are uncalibrated and unused for any frozen result in this book.

**Key findings from the simulation**:

1. **RSL3 selectively kills persisters (42.5%) while sparing glycolytic cells (0%)**, consistent with experimental findings (PMID:41481741). The model correctly reproduces the published biology: FSP1 downregulation leaves GPX4 as the sole lipid repair pathway, and its pharmacologic inhibition is lethal.

2. **SDT/PDT kills all cell types at high rates (87-100%)** because the exogenous ROS burst overwhelms GSH defense regardless of phenotype. This is an important finding: **physical ROS modalities are not inherently selective for persisters**. Their advantage over RSL3 is not selectivity but spatial confinement (focused ultrasound/light limits damage to the tumor volume). Any ICD advantage remains a biological hypothesis, not a result established by this model.

3. **The bistable switch is the critical mechanism.** Under SDT, GSH is catastrophically depleted (5.0 → 0.03 mM), which unlocks autocatalytic lipid peroxidation propagation. Cells that maintain GSH above the threshold recover; those that don't collapse irreversibly. This is qualitatively different from a linear dose-response.

4. **NRF2 compensation fully rescues from RSL3** (0.05% death, matching NRF2's known role in GPX4 upregulation) **but NOT from SDT** (99.5% death). The ROS burst from SDT overwhelms even NRF2-elevated GSH. This suggests that the NRF2 compensation failure mode we identified as the paper's primary risk may be less problematic for SDT than for pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers — a novel prediction from the simulation.

5. **Sensitivity analysis**: The qualitative result tested in the sensitivity suite — Persister > Glycolytic under SDT — holds in 22/22 parameter perturbation conditions (±50% on each of 11 rate constants), indicating robustness to parameter uncertainty for that specific comparison. A global sensitivity analysis using PRCC (Partial Rank Correlation Coefficients) with 2,000 Latin Hypercube Samples across the same 11 parameters formally ranked parameter importance. For Persister × RSL3, the LP cascade parameters dominate: lp_propagation (PRCC = +0.92), gpx4_rate (−0.87), and lp_rate (+0.80) — the death outcome is a balance between LP accumulation and enzymatic repair, with five parameters showing |PRCC| > 0.4. For Persister × SDT, a single parameter dominates: sdt_ros (+0.63, exogenous ROS dose); all other parameters have |PRCC| < 0.16. This asymmetry quantifies the manuscript's central mechanism: SDT overwhelms cellular defenses with exogenous ROS, making the death rate insensitive to the biochemical parameters that determine RSL3's outcome. A Pearson-vs-Spearman divergence check flagged 2 of 66 parameter×condition pairs (sdt_ros in two SDT conditions, reflecting a ceiling effect at high ROS rather than true non-monotonicity), below the >2 threshold for Sobol escalation. Stage 2 (Sobol indices) is therefore not warranted for this parameter set (see analysis/prcc-results.json for the full PRCC matrix across 6 conditions). As a model-qualification check beyond that escalation criterion, we nonetheless computed variance-based Sobol indices for the Persister × RSL3 kill rate (issue #331; analysis/sobol-sensitivity-report.md, scripts/sobol_sensitivity.py), screening the same biochemical rate constants over the same ranges as the PRCC (the PRCC's eleven parameters minus the SDT-only sdt_ros). They confirm the PRCC ranking: the total-effect indices rank lp_propagation (ST = 0.50), gpx4_rate (0.29), and lp_rate (0.18) as the top three, the same three the PRCC ranks for Persister × RSL3 and in the same order, together about 89% of the kill-rate variance as first-order effects. They also confirm the "Stage 2 not warranted" call from the other direction, since first-order effects explain essentially all the variance (ΣS1 ≈ 1.0), so parameter interactions are negligible and the univariate PRCC ranking was adequate. The same screen yields a practical-identifiability verdict: six of the ten screened rate constants (fenton_rate, gsh_scav_efficiency, fsp1_rate, nrf2_gsh_rate, death_threshold, gpx4_degradation_by_ros) move the kill rate negligibly (ST < 0.05), so they are not constrainable from the kill-rate observable and are flagged accordingly in simulations/calibration/parameter_provenance.md rather than presented as if data-fitted. Beyond the single-cell switch, a per-headline Morris elementary-effects screen (issue #331; analysis/headline-sensitivity-report.md, scripts/headline_sensitivity.py) extends this to a spatial/combination headline by perturbing the same rate constants through the simulation binaries themselves. For the RSL3 plus FSP1i Bliss synergy, the same LP-cascade and GSH/GPX4 constants (lp_propagation, gsh_scav_efficiency, gpx4_rate) lead the ranking, but unlike the single-cell kill rate the synergy is strongly non-additive: the Morris interaction/nonlinearity indicator (sigma) exceeds the importance index (mu-star) for every active parameter, a structure the univariate PRCC cannot see (Morris sigma does not separate interaction from single-parameter nonlinearity, but either way the effect is non-additive). Two parameters are structurally inert for this headline (sdt_ros, because no SDT acts in the pair, and rsl3_gpx4_inhib, because the combination model applies RSL3 as a fixed drug effect rather than through that parameter), so they are not constrainable from the Bliss observable. The hypoxia kill-collapse headline (the SDT-minus-RSL3 hypoxic-zone kill gap, from the spatial sim-tme model) is now screened the same way: the SDT exogenous-ROS dose (sdt_ros) is the single largest driver, the model-side confirmation that SDT's hypoxic efficacy is primarily dose-driven, which is why it survives hypoxia where RSL3 collapses. The screen is more nuanced than a pure dose story, though. Because the SDT hypoxic kill sits high (around 0.87) but is not saturated, the LP-cascade and GSH-defense constants (lp_rate, gsh_scav_efficiency, lp_propagation) also move the gap substantially, so SDT's hypoxic efficacy is dose-driven but defense-modulated, not defense-independent, consistent with the framing that the SDT advantage is real but not absolute. This screen holds SDT oxygen-independent (the optimistic upper bound of Section 7.1); the contested oxygen-dependent SDT mode is a separate knob, not one of the screened rate constants. The immune-amplification headline is screened the same way, completing all three headlines. At the canonical condition the raw immune-kill ratio matches the headline (SDT far above RSL3, roughly 104 to 1 at the default gradient-120 immune-on, the same asymmetry shown in the immune figure), because SDT's dense ferroptotic death builds a large DAMP field that amplifies immune killing far more than RSL3's sparse death. The raw immune-kill count is, however, confounded for a sensitivity screen by pool depletion, since SDT ferroptotically clears most of the tumor first, so a parameter that raises SDT's ferroptosis shrinks the residual pool the immune layer acts on, which can lower the immune-kill count even as the per-cell amplification rises. A faithful sensitivity observable therefore controls for that pool, namely the de-confounded immune kill rate (SDT's immune kills divided by the non-ferroptotic cells), which is bounded between zero and one because the immune kills are a subset of that pool. Screening that observable (from the same sim-tme runs as the hypoxia gap) puts the LP-cascade constants (lp_propagation, lp_rate) at the top, with the SDT dose close behind, so the per-cell immune amplification rides on the same ferroptosis death switch the single-cell and hypoxia screens rank: the DAMP that primes immune killing is the ferroptotic-death density, set by how readily cells tip into ferroptosis more than by the SDT dose alone. This is the informative contrast with the hypoxia gap, where the SDT dose led. SDT's hypoxic survival is dose-driven, but its per-cell immune amplification is death-density-driven, so the immune amplification is not a structurally independent axis but a downstream readout of the same biochemistry. The immune-coupling parameters themselves (DAMP diffusion, dendritic-cell activation, immune kill rate) are not in the biochemical rate-constant set screened here, so this says which biochemical constants move the amplification, not how the immune coupling is tuned. Finally, to read the single-cell death rates as intervals rather than point estimates, we propagated the same parameter uncertainty forward (issue #332; analysis/uncertainty-intervals-report.md, scripts/uncertainty_intervals.py): placing a uniform prior over each rate constant across its PRCC range and Monte-Carlo sampling the joint prior gives the prior-predictive distribution of the death rate for each phenotype and treatment. The result reinforces the order-of-magnitude reading. The pharmacologic RSL3 death rates carry very wide intervals because the bistable tipping point sits inside the plausible range (Persister × RSL3 spans roughly 2% to 100% around its 42.5% point estimate), so the RSL3 numbers are the least parameter-constrained, whereas the Glycolytic × RSL3 floor (near 0% across the whole prior) and the Persister × SDT ceiling (above 98% across the prior) are robust; Glycolytic × SDT and OXPHOS × SDT, by contrast, are wider (2.5th percentiles of roughly 4% and 68% respectively), because the lower end of the sdt_ros prior does not fully overwhelm those defenses. The NRF2-rescued persister, which the manuscript flags as the primary RSL3-resistance risk, stays resistant at the point estimate (0%) but its interval reaches roughly 38%, so even that resistance is not parameter-robust. The point estimates here are computed at the Figure 7 sample size (a million cells) so they reproduce the frozen Figure 7 values, while the intervals come from the smaller per-draw ensemble where the parameter spread dominates. This is a prior-predictive (forward) uncertainty, not a data-conditioned Bayesian posterior: turning the uniform priors into a posterior requires external drug-response data (the GDSC/DepMap calibration of issue #330). Propagating the same priors through the spatial/combination headline outputs has now begun with the cheapest such binary, the RSL3 + FSP1i Bliss synergy from sim-combo-mech (issue #332; analysis/headline-uncertainty-report.md, scripts/headline_uncertainty.py): under the same uniform priors the synergy spans a 95% prior-predictive interval of roughly 1.0× to 5.2× (median ~1.35×) around its 1.99× point estimate, so the supra-additive direction is robust at the lower 95% bound (a small tail of the prior reaches ~0.95×, i.e. just sub-additive) while the magnitude is not, consistent with Section 6.3's directional-not-quantitative reading. Propagating the priors through the two costlier sim-tme headlines has since been done at a smaller 100-draw ensemble (analysis/headline-uncertainty-tme-report.md, the same scripts/headline_uncertainty.py harness; sim-tme costs roughly four minutes per run, so the tails are read cautiously): the hypoxia kill-collapse gap (the SDT-minus-RSL3 hypoxic-zone kill) stays positive across its entire 95% prior-predictive interval, roughly 0.16 to 1.00 (median 0.96, around its 0.87 point estimate), so the collapse-asymmetry direction (SDT holds, RSL3 collapses) is parameter-robust even though its magnitude is wide; and SDT's pool-de-confounded immune kill rate spans roughly 0.009 to 0.171 (median 0.042), robustly low-but-positive. The penetration headline has likewise been propagated (analysis/headline-uncertainty-penetration-report.md): perturbing the same biochem priors at each tissue's fixed (uncalibrated) transport scenario, the vessel-wall RSL3 kill carries very wide intervals at every tissue (well-vascularized median 23%, 95% interval roughly 0 to 93%; poorly-vascularized median 8%, 0 to 83%; CNS/BBB median 4%, 0 to 77%), because the bistable RSL3 switch dominates as it does for the single-cell rates, but the across-tissue ORDERING (well-vascularized > poorly-vascularized > CNS/BBB) is preserved within every draw. The ordering is robust as a within-draw paired comparison rather than from the marginal intervals (which overlap almost completely because the biochem prior, not the tissue, dominates the spread): each draw applies the same biochem rate constants to all three tissues at their decreasing fixed vessel-wall concentrations, a shared monotone dose-response, and the well-vascularized >= poorly-vascularized >= CNS/BBB ordering held in 300 of 300 draws. So the penetration-gradient direction is parameter-robust while the absolute per-tissue magnitudes are not. With that, all four headline families have been read as prior-predictive intervals over the documented rate-constant ranges; the remaining step is the data-conditioned ABC posterior, which requires the external drug-response data (the GDSC/DepMap calibration of issue #330). The single-cell kill rate and the immune ratio are directional-only; the Bliss synergy, the hypoxia asymmetry, and the penetration gap are direction-robust but magnitude-uncalibrated. So every reported magnitude should be read as order-of-magnitude or directional, exactly as this book labels it.

### 5.3 Where the Numbers Come From

The simulation uses approximately 30 parameters. Some are drawn from published experimental measurements: GSH concentrations in cancer cells, GPX4 catalytic rates, the Michaelis-Menten constants for antioxidant scavenging, and the physical attenuation coefficients for light and ultrasound in tissue. Others are estimated from indirect evidence: FSP1 repair rates, NRF2 upregulation kinetics, the rate at which exogenous ROS generates lipid radicals, and the stochastic variation range for each parameter.

Each cell in the simulation receives its own parameter set, drawn from a ±20% uniform random variation around the population mean. This reflects the biological reality that no two cells are identical — even genetically identical cells in culture show measurable variation in protein expression and metabolic state. By running 1,000,000 cells per condition, the simulation produces statistically robust population-level outcomes (95% confidence intervals reported for all death rates).

Five calibration constraints anchor the model to reality: (1) all phenotypes must show less than 2% death under Control conditions, reflecting baseline cell viability; (2) RSL3 must selectively kill persister cells at a rate consistent with published experimental observations; (3) the vulnerability window must close on a timescale consistent with known defense-pathway recovery kinetics. Two additional constraints verify the physics: PDT attenuation and SDT penetration must match published tissue optics and acoustics data.

### 5.4 What the Model Cannot Do

This is a qualitative model testing directional predictions, not a quantitative predictor of clinical outcomes. The absolute percentages (87%, 42%, etc.) depend on model-specific rate constants and should not be interpreted as clinical predictions. The model's value is in demonstrating that the proposed biochemical chain produces the expected differential sensitivity under biologically plausible parameters, and in identifying which conditions favor which outcomes.

Specific limitations the reader should keep in mind throughout Chapters 6 and 7:

The model does not include upstream signaling — iron import, System Xc- cystine transport, and ACSL4-mediated lipid remodeling are not represented in the 2D engine these chapters use. (The library has since grown an uncalibrated `contact` layer that models the cell-cell-contact downregulation of two of these, ACSL4-driven PUFA remodeling and TFRC iron import, via E-cadherin/NF2-YAP signaling; it represents those two axes at reduced levels, not the import or remodeling dynamics, and is not used for any result in this book. See "The 3D simulation suite" in Section 8.4.) It does not model adaptive evolution — there is no mutation, no selection over generations, no population dynamics beyond the single 180-step treatment window. Spatial drug distribution is handled separately in Chapter 6's spatial simulation, not in the baseline model. Immune response is handled separately in Chapter 7's immune coupling layer.

All fields (oxygen, pH, drug concentration) are steady-state — they are computed once based on distance from the tumor edge, not dynamically updated as cells consume resources or die. In a real tumor, blood flow fluctuates, metabolites are consumed and replenished, and the microenvironment evolves over hours to days. The steady-state approximation captures the spatial gradient but not the temporal dynamics.

The model is a hypothesis-testing tool, not a digital twin of a tumor. It identifies which biological conditions matter for ferroptosis sensitivity, which parameter combinations produce interesting outcomes, and which assumptions are load-bearing. The experiments proposed in Chapters 6 and 7 are designed to test whether those assumptions hold in real biology.

## Chapter 6: Drug Combinations and Penetration

### 6.1 Spatial Tumor Simulation: Depth-Dependent Energy Deposition

A drug that works in a petri dish may fail in a patient for reasons that have nothing to do with biology. Tumors are three-dimensional structures with blood vessels at the periphery and drug-starved interiors. Light, ultrasound, and drug molecules each attenuate differently as they travel through tissue. If a treatment can't physically reach cancer cells deep inside a tumor, its biochemical potency is irrelevant.

To address the single-cell model's lack of spatial context, we extended the simulation to a 2D heterogeneous tumor grid (500 x 500 cells, 20 μm/cell = 1 cm x 1 cm tissue cross-section) with physics-based energy attenuation. The tumor architecture consists of a glycolytic core (80% glycolytic, 15% OXPHOS, 5% persister), an OXPHOS-rich periphery (70% OXPHOS, 20% glycolytic, 8% persister, 2% persister+NRF2), persister cluster pockets, and a stromal border — totaling 159,033 tumor cells per simulation.

Energy is applied from the tissue surface (row 0) with physically grounded attenuation models:

- **PDT**: Modified Beer-Lambert exponential decay with effective attenuation coefficient 0.31 per mm, yielding a penetration depth of approximately 3.2 mm at 630 nm (Jacques, Phys Med Biol 2013)
- **SDT**: Acoustic intensity attenuation at 0.7 dB/cm/MHz and 1 MHz operating frequency (Cobbold, Foundations of Biomedical Ultrasound, 2007)
- **RSL3**: Uniform concentration (systemic drug, no depth dependence)

Dead cells release labile iron to their Moore neighborhood (8-neighbors), modeling the bystander ferroptosis effect. Iron is consumed by Fenton chemistry each timestep rather than accumulating permanently.

**Key findings from the spatial simulation**:

1. **PDT kill rate collapses beyond 3 mm.** At 1 mm depth, PDT kills 90.8% of tumor cells; at 5 mm, only 15.7%; at 9 mm, 0.5%. This directly reflects the exponential light attenuation in tissue and quantifies why PDT is limited to superficial tumors.

2. **SDT maintains >88% kill rate through the full 1 cm depth.** Ultrasound attenuation at 1 MHz is mild (~0.7 dB/cm), preserving sufficient acoustic intensity to activate sonosensitizers at centimeter depths. This validates the paper's central claim: SDT extends ROS-ferroptosis to deep-seated tumors where PDT cannot reach.

3. **RSL3 kills uniformly but weakly (~3-6%).** As a systemic drug, RSL3 shows no depth dependence — it reaches all cells equally. However, it kills selectively: 42.2% of persisters (4,815/11,391) versus 0% of glycolytic cells, consistent with the single-cell model.

4. **Model-predicted overall tumor kill: SDT 91.9%, PDT 26.1%, RSL3 3.7%, Control 0.1%.** PDT killed only 26.1% of the heterogeneous tumor because 74% of cells lie beyond its effective penetration depth. SDT killed 91.9%, roughly 3.5-fold higher modeled kill than PDT in this 1 cm tissue model (a light-versus-ultrasound penetration contrast between two physical modalities, not a drug-versus-physical claim).

[FIGURE 8: Depth-kill curves: tissue penetration sets modality reach (2D model). (a) Observed tumor kill versus depth across 1 cm of tissue: SDT (ultrasound) stays near 95 to 100% throughout, PDT (light) collapses from about 93% at the surface to near zero by 10 mm, and RSL3 (systemic drug) is a flat, near-zero baseline at every depth. (b) The driving physics computed from the model's own equations: PDT light decays by Beer-Lambert (effective attenuation 0.31 per mm, penetration depth about 3.2 mm), SDT acoustic attenuation is mild (0.7 dB/cm/MHz at 1 MHz), and RSL3 is depth-independent. RSL3 reaches every depth yet kills little, a biochemical limit rather than a penetration one. SDT is modeled as O2-independent, an optimistic upper bound (Section 7.1).]

The spatial model treats RSL3 as uniformly distributed, but real drug concentration drops with distance from the nearest blood vessel. A tissue-specific drug penetration module using the exponential Krogh cylinder approximation (C(r) = C₀ × exp(-r/λ), where λ = √(D/k) is the penetration length) shows that even at the vessel wall (the highest-concentration position), tissue-specific vascular permeability reduces effective drug exposure — yielding model-predicted vessel-wall persister kill rates of 12.1% in well-vascularized epithelial tissue, 2.6% in poorly-vascularized tissue (pancreatic), and 1.8% behind the blood-brain barrier (glioblastoma), with overall tumor kill rates even lower (6.6%, 2.1%, 1.5% respectively). These are substantially lower than the 40% kill rate observed in 2D culture, reinforcing the in-vitro-to-in-vivo translation gap. All transport parameters are estimated, not calibrated — the comparison across tissue types is directionally informative, not quantitatively precise. Since then, the exponential penetration FORM and the reference-drug penetration LENGTH have been validated against published measured data (issue #335; `analysis/calibration/penetration-validation.md`). The model carries a doxorubicin transport reference, a well-characterized small molecule used here for its penetration physics only, not its DNA-damage pharmacology (the cell-level kill still runs through the RSL3 / GPX4-inhibition pathway). Primeau 2005 (PMID 16361566) and Tannock 2002 (PMID 11895922) both report drug concentration declining exponentially with distance from the vessel in vivo, and that doxorubicin reference (half-distance 34.7 micrometers) falls within Tannock 2002's measured 25 to 75 micrometer range and about 13 percent below the Primeau 2005 and Minchinton 2006 ranges (40 to 80 micrometers), so the functional form and order of magnitude are anchored at the conservative end. Two pieces remain unvalidated and are flagged as such. First, the RSL3-specific penetration length stays an extrapolation, since no ferroptosis inducer has published spatial penetration data. Second, a dose-dependent binding-site barrier is not a feature of this passive-diffusion model at all: its uptake is linear, so penetration depth is dose-independent, whereas a true binding-site barrier saturates and deepens with dose. A data-availability review concluded that this mechanism is not warranted for the small molecules the model targets, so it is deliberately not added rather than introduced as unvalidatable complexity. The strong dose-dependent binding-site barrier is fundamentally an antibody phenomenon: it was discovered with antibodies (Fujimori and Weinstein 1990, PMID 2362198), the canonical dose-titration showing penetration deepening with dose is an antibody study (Saga 1995, PMID 7568060), and the closed-form signature, penetration distance scaling as the square root of dose, is derived for antibodies (Thurber and Wittrup 2008, PMID 18541331). For small molecules the effect is physically weak, since a deep and slowly saturating binding sink barely saturates at achievable doses (the El-Kareh and Secomb tumor-cord model predicts little dose-through deepening for doxorubicin), and there is no extractable small-molecule dose-resolved penetration-depth dataset to validate such a term. Were the model ever extended to antibodies or antibody-drug conjugates, a saturable Michaelis-Menten uptake term validated against that antibody dose-titration data would be the appropriate addition.

**What this means.** For deep-seated tumors (pancreatic, hepatic, pelvic), any drug that cannot penetrate centimeters into tissue will underperform its in-vitro promise regardless of its biochemical potency. Part of the in-vitro-to-in-vivo gap is physics, not biology, and it applies to RSL3 and to any systemically delivered drug with similar tissue distribution limitations. Ultrasound penetrates centimeters with modest attenuation, so SDT's energy delivery is far less depth-limited than light (PDT), which is absorbed within millimeters; whether that delivered energy actually yields ferroptosis at depth depends on the modeled biochemistry (the established physics is the penetration, not the downstream kill).

**How to test this prediction.** Grow persister-enriched cancer cells in 3D tumor spheroids (500 μm diameter or larger) and apply PDT, SDT, and RSL3. Measure cell viability at multiple depths within the spheroid using confocal sectioning or optical clearing with viability staining.

*Expected if prediction holds*: PDT kill exceeds 80% at the spheroid surface but drops sharply toward the core (within 250 μm for a 500 μm spheroid). SDT kill remains high at all measured depths. RSL3 kill drops with distance from the medium supply surface. For larger tissue constructs or in vivo models, the differential should scale with the larger dimensions predicted by the 1 cm spatial simulation.

*Expected if prediction fails*: All three modalities show similar depth-dependent kill profiles. This would indicate that the model's differential attenuation parameters are wrong, or that drug penetration in 3D culture is better than the Krogh cylinder approximation predicts. Either result would be informative for refining the model.

### 6.2 Vulnerability Window: How Long Does the Ferroptosis-Sensitive Window Stay Open?

If residual cancer cells are temporarily more vulnerable to ferroptosis after initial treatment, the critical practical question becomes: how long does the window stay open? A vulnerability that exists but closes within hours is biologically real but clinically useless — there is no time to administer a follow-up treatment. A window that lasts weeks gives clinicians a realistic intervention opportunity.

A critical question for clinical translation is timing: how long after chemotherapy withdrawal do persister cells remain ferroptosis-sensitive? We modeled the recovery of defense pathways (FSP1, GPX4, NRF2, GSH) using exponential kinetics with literature-estimated half-times (FSP1: 7 days, GPX4: 3 days, NRF2: 5 days, GSH: 1 day) and simulated 100,000 cells per condition at each timepoint.

| Time post-chemo | RSL3 kill | SDT kill |
|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| 0 hours | 42.4% | 100.0% |
| 6 hours | 34.3% | 100.0% |
| 1 day | 16.1% | 100.0% |
| 3 days | 1.4% | 100.0% |
| 1 week | <0.01% | 99.95% |
| 2 weeks | 0.00% | 99.8% |
| 4 weeks | 0.00% | 99.5% |

**The RSL3 vulnerability window closes within 3 days** as GPX4 is re-expressed. By one week, RSL3 is completely ineffective (<0.01%). **The SDT window remains open for at least 4 weeks** (99.5% kill at day 28), because the exogenous ROS burst overwhelms even partially recovered antioxidant defenses. If these estimates hold, the scheduling implication is that pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers would require intervention within days of chemotherapy, while physical modalities are predicted to allow a wider window. These window estimates depend on literature-derived half-life parameters and should be treated as approximate until experimentally validated; whether either window is actionable in human tumors is untested.

(a) Post-chemotherapy kill rate over time: RSL3 collapses from 42.4% to 1.4% by day 3 and to near-zero by day 7 as GPX4 is re-expressed, while SDT (and PDT, which tracks it) holds ~100%→99.5% through day 28; the shaded region marks the open RSL3 window. (b) The RSL3 collapse tracks mean GPX4 recovery (twin axis) — the re-expression of the primary defense is what closes the window. Recovery half-times are literature-estimated, so durations are approximate (medium confidence).]

**What this means.** If the model is correct, the logistics of treatment timing become a critical variable — not just for efficacy, but for determining which modality class is even feasible. A GPX4 inhibitor administered one week after chemotherapy would arrive to find a population of cells that has already re-expressed its primary defense. A physical ROS modality administered at the same timepoint would still find cells whose antioxidant reserves, while partially recovered, remain overwhelmed by the exogenous ROS dose. This converts a biological question (how long are cells vulnerable?) into a practical scheduling question (how quickly can the follow-up treatment be delivered?).

**How to test this prediction.** After drug withdrawal from a persister-inducing therapy (e.g., osimertinib withdrawal from EGFR-mutant lung cancer cells), measure GPX4, FSP1, NRF2, and GSH levels at serial timepoints (0h, 6h, 1d, 3d, 1wk, 2wk, 4wk). At each timepoint, assess ferroptosis sensitivity by treating matched samples with RSL3 and measuring cell death.

*Expected if prediction holds*: GPX4 protein levels recover to baseline within 3-5 days (half-life ~3 days). FSP1 recovers more slowly (half-life ~7 days). RSL3 sensitivity drops in parallel with GPX4 recovery. Sensitivity to exogenous oxidative stress (e.g., tert-butyl hydroperoxide as a proxy for exogenous ROS, though it does not replicate SDT's spatial or mechanistic features) persists longer because the ROS dose overwhelms partially recovered defenses.

*Expected if prediction fails*: All defense pathways recover simultaneously rather than sequentially. This would mean the differential half-life estimates in the model are wrong, and there is no pharmacologic-vs-physical window difference — both modality classes face the same timing constraint. Alternatively, recovery may be much faster than modeled (hours, not days), in which case the window is too narrow for any follow-up treatment to exploit.

### 6.3 Mechanistic Combination Modeling: Why Drug Pairs Synergize

The question behind drug combination design is straightforward: if the cell has two parallel defense mechanisms, what happens when you attack both at once? The ferroptosis pathway has two parallel repair arms: GPX4 (GSH-dependent) and FSP1 (CoQ10-dependent). These feed into a shared antioxidant quench term that suppresses autocatalytic lipid peroxidation propagation. Depleting both arms simultaneously should produce mechanistic synergy — the simulation lets us test this prediction quantitatively.

Running pairwise combinations of four interventions (RSL3/GPX4 inhibitor, SDT/exogenous ROS, FSP1 inhibitor, HDAC inhibitor/basal ROS upregulation) on 1,000 persister cells per condition and comparing against Bliss-independent predictions reveals three synergistic pairs. RSL3 + FSP1i is the strongest (Bliss synergy score 1.99): single-agent RSL3 kills 40% and FSP1i kills 3.7%, but the combination kills 84.1% — nearly double the 42.2% Bliss prediction. The pathway traces show why: with both GPX4 and FSP1 depleted, mean lipid peroxidation reaches 8.7 (near the 10.0 death threshold), whereas RSL3 alone only drives LP to 4.5 because intact FSP1 partially compensates. RSL3 + HDACi (1.25x) and FSP1i + HDACi (1.21x) are also synergistic. SDT combinations are merely additive because SDT alone already kills 100% of persisters (ceiling effect). Drug potency parameters (FSP1i 85% inhibition, HDACi 2x ROS) are estimates — exact synergy scores depend on these assumptions, but the directional finding (dual-pathway depletion > single-pathway) is robust to parameter variation.

[FIGURE 22: Dual-pathway depletion synergy. (a) Single-agent RSL3 (40%) and FSP1i (3.7%) versus the observed combination (84.1%) and the Bliss-independent prediction (42.2%) — the combination nearly doubles the expected kill (1.99× synergy). (b) Pairwise Bliss synergy scores across the four interventions; SDT pairs are excluded because SDT alone kills 100% of persisters (ceiling). Drug potencies are estimates; the directional result (dual-pathway depletion outperforms single-pathway) held across the ±50% sensitivity sweep.]

**What this means.** The 1.99× Bliss synergy is not about the specific drug pair RSL3 + FSP1i. It is about the principle: ferroptosis has two parallel repair pathways, and disabling both is mechanistically more effective than disabling either alone. Any drug combination that simultaneously depletes GPX4-dependent and GPX4-independent repair should produce synergy, regardless of the specific molecules used. This generalizes the prediction beyond the compounds in the simulation — it identifies a strategy (dual-pathway depletion) rather than endorsing a specific regimen. The practical implication for drug development: screen combination strategies by their pathway coverage, not just by their individual potency.

**How to test this prediction.** Treat persister-enriched cancer cells with a GPX4 inhibitor (RSL3 or ML162) and an FSP1 inhibitor (iFSP1 or DHODH inhibitor brequinar) across a dose matrix. Compute the combination index using Chou-Talalay analysis or compare against Bliss independence predictions. Measure lipid peroxidation (C11-BODIPY) to confirm the mechanistic pathway.

*Expected if prediction holds*: Combination index < 0.5 (strong synergy) at concentrations where single agents produce 20-50% kill. Lipid peroxidation in the combination group exceeds single-agent levels by at least 50%, consistent with dual-pathway depletion removing the compensation that limits single-agent LP accumulation.

*Expected if prediction fails*: Combination index > 0.8 (additive or antagonistic). This would indicate that FSP1 does not function as a truly independent parallel repair pathway — perhaps it shares upstream regulators with GPX4, or perhaps the model's independence assumption overstates their biochemical separation. An antagonistic result (CI > 1.0) would be particularly informative, suggesting the two pathways interact in ways the model does not capture.

## Chapter 7: Resistance Mechanisms

The simulations in Chapter 5 showed that persister cells are selectively vulnerable to ferroptosis. The spatial and combination models in Chapter 6 showed that this vulnerability is modulated by depth, timing, and pathway targeting. But all of those models assumed a uniform, well-controlled environment — the biological equivalent of a petri dish. Real tumors are nothing like petri dishes.

This chapter examines four features of the tumor microenvironment (TME) that the model predicts will alter treatment outcomes. Three are resistance mechanisms that selectively penalize pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers while leaving physical modalities relatively unaffected: oxygen gradients (Section 7.1), stromal metabolic support (Section 7.3), and acidic pH (Section 7.4). One is an amplification mechanism that favors treatments producing dense spatial kill patterns: immune coupling through DAMP signaling (Section 7.2). Together, they share a structural asymmetry: pharmacologic approaches depend on endogenous cellular processes — basal ROS generation, GSH depletion, drug bioavailability — that the TME can disrupt. Physical modalities deliver exogenous energy that bypasses these dependencies. Whether this asymmetry holds in real tumors is an open question; the experiments proposed in each section below are designed to test it.

### 7.1 Tumor Microenvironment: Oxygen Gradients and Hypoxia

The center of a solid tumor has almost no oxygen. Blood vessels penetrate only the outer 100-150 micrometers of tissue, and cells deeper than that rely on whatever oxygen diffuses inward — which, for many tumors, is close to zero. This matters for ferroptosis because the earliest step in the cascade — generation of basal reactive oxygen species by the mitochondrial electron transport chain — requires oxygen as the terminal electron acceptor. If there is no oxygen, there is no basal ROS, and a drug like RSL3 that works by disabling the cell's defense against ROS-driven lipid damage has nothing to defend against. It is like hiring a firefighter in a town where no fires ever start.

The preceding simulations assumed uniform oxygenation — every cell generates the same basal ROS regardless of position. Real tumors have oxygen gradients: O2 penetrates approximately 100-150 μm from the vasculature[^vaupel1989], modeled via the Krogh cylinder approximation. This creates hypoxic cores where mitochondrial electron transport chain activity drops and basal ROS production falls — oxygen is the terminal electron acceptor that drives the chain[^bio2e_74].

[^vaupel1989]: Vaupel P et al. Blood flow, oxygen and nutrient supply, and metabolic microenvironment of human tumors. Cancer Research (1989). O2 transport physiology consistent with OpenStax Anatomy & Physiology 2e, Ch.22.4.
[^bio2e_74]: OpenStax Biology 2e, Chapter 7, Section 7.4: Oxidative Phosphorylation.

To quantify this, we applied an exponential O2 gradient from the tumor edge inward (O2(d) = exp(-d/λ), same Krogh approximation used for drug penetration) and compared treatment efficacy under uniform vs gradient conditions. Because the model only modulates basal ROS (not Fenton chemistry or SDT/PDT dose), this is a conservative estimate of hypoxia's protective effect.

The model produces a clear differential. Under the O2 gradient (λ = 120μm), RSL3 overall kill collapses from 3.7% to 0.1%, and in the hypoxic zone (deeper than 360μm from the tumor edge) RSL3 kills zero cells. SDT overall kill drops only modestly, from 91.9% to 87.8%, and maintains 86.6% kill even in the hypoxic zone, though as the caveat below details this depends entirely on treating SDT as O2-independent (re-running the identical comparison under the opposite, fully O2-dependent assumption collapses the SDT overall gradient kill to 4.0% at this λ, 87.8% to 4.0%, and the hypoxic-zone kill to 0%, so 87.8% is an upper bound, not a central estimate; both bounds are reported in the caveat below). This differential is robust across all tested penetration lengths (λ = 80, 100, 120, 150μm) and emerges directly from the biochemistry: RSL3 inhibits GPX4 but depends on the cell's own mitochondrial ROS to initiate lipid peroxidation. In hypoxia, there is no basal ROS to drive the cascade, regardless of how thoroughly GPX4 is inhibited. SDT delivers exogenous ROS via sonosensitizer activation, which the model treats as a primarily Type I, electron-transfer mechanism (less O2-dependent than the Type II singlet-oxygen pathway) and therefore able to bypass the mitochondrial bottleneck. That "primarily Type I" assumption is itself contested: by the same Dong 2023 reference cited below, the SDT ROS mechanism is "not fully elucidated" and may include O2-dependent routes, so this paragraph states the model's premise, with the caveat that follows correcting the record.

This finding has two implications. First, it quantifies a specific mechanism by which the tumor microenvironment protects cells from pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers — not through upregulation of defense pathways (the NRF2/MUFA mechanisms modeled in Chapter 5), but through the absence of the upstream trigger (basal ROS). Second, it provides a mechanistic rationale for why physical ROS modalities might maintain efficacy in contexts where pharmacologic approaches fail: they supply what hypoxic cells lack. Independent in vivo evidence is consistent with the pharmacologic-inducer side of this mechanism: in pancreatic cancer the HIF-2 transcription factor coordinates ferroptosis resistance through two arms — upregulating cysteine/glutathione antioxidant defense (system Xc- and the transsulfuration enzymes), and inducing Parkin-mediated mitophagy that suppresses mitochondrial function and ROS generation[^pmid41932308]. The second arm matches the loss-of-upstream-trigger mechanism the model encodes (no basal ROS, no cascade); the first is precisely the defense-pathway upregulation this section distinguishes from it. So the paper supports the broader claim — hypoxia/HIF-2 confers ferroptosis resistance in vivo — while showing the real biology combines both routes rather than the single mechanism the model isolates. The relationship is not universal, though. In clear-cell renal carcinoma the HIF-2/HILPDA axis instead enriches polyunsaturated lipids and *sensitizes* cells to GPX4 inhibitors[^pmid30962421], so "hypoxia disables RSL3" is tumor-type-specific rather than a general law, and any tissue where hypoxia signaling enriches peroxidation-prone lipids would erase the modeled SDT advantage. This is the same depth-vs-penetration argument made in Section 8.2, but now grounded in TME biology rather than tissue optics alone.

We have since made the contested hypoxia leg runnable from BOTH sides, not just caveated. On the SDT side, an O2-dependent (Type II) exo-ROS mode (Section 7.1 caveat, issue #358) collapses the SDT hypoxic kill from 87.8% to roughly 4% if SDT's ROS is fully oxygen-dependent. On the RSL3 side, wiring the NCOA4-ferritinophagy plus hypoxia-iron coupling (ferroptosis-core 0.38.0, issue #365) into the 3D spatial model lets hypoxia RAISE the Fenton iron substrate: enabling it lifts RSL3 ferroptosis kill several-fold (in one 3D configuration, grid 60, λ = 50 μm, 180 steps, with the ferritinophagy-release and hypoxia-iron-sensitivity knobs both at 2.0, overall kill rose from 0.5% to 2.9%). Crucially, the rise reaches the deep, near-anoxic core too: the hypoxic-zone kill goes from 0% to 1.3% at λ = 50, and at a more anoxic λ = 40 the deep-zone kill rises further still. That deep-core rescue is, however, a MODEL ARTIFACT worth flagging rather than a finding. The model's Fenton ROS term is oxygen-independent: it scales with iron and a constant rate and is added to total ROS independently of basal ROS, and the model modulates only basal ROS, not the Fenton rate (see the caveat below). So the iron boost, which is largest exactly where oxygen is lowest, raises Fenton ROS in the core regardless of how anoxic it is. A real anoxic core would also lose the superoxide/SOD-derived H2O2 substrate that drives Fenton, which this model does not couple to oxygen, so the model OVERSTATES the deep-core rescue. The robust, defensible conclusion is the direction, not the deep-core magnitude: hypoxia can raise the RSL3 Fenton-iron contribution, so "hypoxia uniformly protects RSL3" is too strong, and combined with the O2-dependent SDT leg the SDT-versus-RSL3 hypoxia asymmetry shrinks from both sides; whether a genuinely anoxic core is rescued turns on whether its Fenton H2O2 substrate survives hypoxia. The default model assumes that substrate away (an O2-independent Fenton term), but the question is now runnable rather than only flagged. The model adds an off-by-default O2-dependent Fenton option (ferroptosis-core 0.44.0, issue #383) that scales the effective Fenton flux down where oxygen is low, on the grounds that the Fenton substrate H2O2 is itself O2-derived (superoxide, then SOD, then H2O2, the Haber-Weiss chain[^pmid10963860]) and that ferroptotic lipid peroxidation is an oxygen-consuming lipid-radical chain (reviewed in the ferroptosis Primer[^pmid28985560]). Re-running the same configuration with the hypoxia-iron coupling on and the Fenton fully O2-coupled removes the deep-core rescue: the near-anoxic-zone kill falls back down (in the spatial A/B it drops by more than half, toward the un-rescued baseline), which confirms that the rescue was a substrate artifact rather than a biological prediction. The coherent picture once both effects are active is that hypoxia raises the labile iron but starves the Fenton reaction of H2O2, so the net deep-core effect is no rescue, while the iron rise can still raise kill in the oxygenated rim and mid-zone where some H2O2 remains. All three knobs (ferritinophagy release, hypoxia-iron sensitivity, Fenton O2-dependence) are off by default and uncalibrated; the numbers are directional sensitivities, not book results.

[FIGURE 15: Hypoxia cross-section showing O2 gradient from blood vessel into tumor core. RSL3 efficacy collapses in the hypoxic interior; SDT maintains efficacy via exogenous ROS delivery that bypasses the mitochondrial O2 requirement.]

[FIGURE 21: Quantitative hypoxia kill-collapse (2D model, immune off). (a) Normoxic versus hypoxic overall kill: RSL3 collapses from 3.7% to ~0.1% (a ~37-fold drop) while SDT holds at 91.9%→87.8%. (b) The hypoxic-zone result is robust across O2 penetration lengths λ = 80–150 µm (flat lines), with normoxic baselines shown as dashed references. **How to test this prediction.** Compare RSL3 and SDT efficacy in normoxic (21% O2) versus hypoxic (1% O2) conditions using a hypoxia chamber or 3D tumor spheroids with verified hypoxic cores (pimonidazole staining).

*Expected if prediction holds*: RSL3 kill drops more than 10-fold in 1% O2 conditions, paralleling the simulation's 3.7% → 0.1% collapse. SDT kill drops less than 2-fold (the simulation predicts 91.9% → 87.8%). Lipid peroxidation measurements (C11-BODIPY) should show that RSL3-treated cells in hypoxia accumulate minimal LP, confirming that the basal-ROS-dependent cascade cannot initiate without oxygen.

*Expected if prediction fails*: Both RSL3 and SDT show similar efficacy reductions in hypoxia. This would mean SDT's ROS generation is more O2-dependent than the model assumes — specifically, that the Type I electron-transfer mechanism (which we model as O2-independent) actually requires significant oxygen. Alternatively, RSL3 may maintain efficacy in hypoxia through a mechanism the model does not capture (e.g., direct GPX4 inactivation triggering LP accumulation through non-mitochondrial ROS sources). Either result would revise the model's central assumption about the ROS-source asymmetry.

Caveats — and this is the most important caveat in the chapter: the O2 model treats SDT/PDT as fully O2-independent, which is the single most optimistic assumption in this analysis and is contested by the broader literature. The Type II photodynamic mechanism requires O2 for singlet-oxygen generation, and the mainstream sonodynamic-therapy field treats hypoxia as a primary SDT bottleneck whose ROS-generation mechanism remains "not fully elucidated"[^pmid37621595]. Tellingly, the leading clinical SDT agent (SONALA-001, a 5-ALA-derived protoporphyrin IX activated by focused ultrasound) is a Type II, oxygen-dependent sensitizer[^sonala2026] — not the Type I, O2-independent mechanism we model. The modeled 87.8% hypoxic-zone SDT kill should therefore be read as an upper bound, and "physical ROS bypasses hypoxia" as a hypothesis to be tested (Section 8.2), not an established result. The Fenton reaction substrate (H2O2) is also O2-derived via the superoxide→SOD pathway, but the default model modulates only basal_ros, not the Fenton rate directly (an off-by-default O2-dependent Fenton option, ferroptosis-core 0.44.0, issue #383, now couples the Fenton substrate to local O2; enabling it removes the deep-core hypoxia-iron rescue described above, confirming that rescue was a substrate artifact). The O2 field is steady-state (no consumption dynamics) and uses distance from the tumor edge as the O2 source (no explicit vasculature in this 2D analysis; the 3D suite adds an explicit but uncalibrated `vasculature` module — see Section 8.4). These simplifications cut both ways: the RSL3 collapse is well-grounded (the basal-ROS dependence is independently supported, above), but if SDT's own ROS generation is O2-limited — as the Type II clinical agent implies — the SDT-versus-RSL3 asymmetry shrinks rather than grows. The honest position is that the *direction* (SDT less O2-dependent than RSL3) is plausible while the *magnitude* of the gap, and whether it survives at all in a given tumor, is unresolved. To make that contested assumption testable rather than only caveated, the model now includes an off-by-default oxygen-dependent SDT mode (a configurable Type II fraction that scales the exogenous ROS yield by local O2, so SDT loses efficacy in hypoxic zones like the clinical SONALA-001 agent; ferroptosis-core 0.33.0), exposed in the 2D `sim-tme` via the `--sdt-o2-dependence` flag and in the 3D suite (issue #358). Re-running the same 2D hypoxia comparison under full O2-dependence (the Type II extreme) collapses the SDT result: gradient SDT overall kill falls from 87.8% to roughly 3.7% (the mean across λ; 4.0% at λ = 120 μm, the λ matching the 87.8% upper bound, with a range of 2.7% to 4.9% across λ = 80 to 150 μm) and the hypoxic-zone SDT kill falls from 86.6% to 0%. In the deep hypoxic zone the SDT-versus-RSL3 asymmetry disappears entirely (both fall to roughly 0%, matching RSL3's 86.6%-zone collapse to 0%). In the overall metric a residual SDT advantage remains (roughly 3.7% to 4.0% versus RSL3's 0.1%), but it is rim-driven: it comes only from the still-oxygenated tumor edge that SDT's O2-dependent ROS can still reach and RSL3 cannot. The real hypoxic SDT efficacy lies between the two bounds, because the actual Type I versus Type II split is sonosensitizer-specific and uncalibrated here: the O2-independent upper bound (SDT 87.8% at λ = 120) and the fully O2-dependent lower bound (SDT 4.0% at λ = 120) bracket it. This converts the contested leg from "the model assumes O2-independence" into a quantified range, and it makes the honest reading unambiguous: the *direction* (SDT at most as O2-dependent as RSL3, plausibly less) may survive, but the *magnitude* of any hypoxia advantage is unresolved and could be zero.

### 7.2 Immune Coupling: DAMP-Mediated T Cell Activation

When cancer cells die from ferroptosis, they release molecular alarm signals — damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) — that can activate nearby immune cells. But not all ferroptotic deaths are equal. The amount and spatial pattern of DAMP release depends on how the cell died: a cell overwhelmed by a massive exogenous ROS burst releases more membrane debris and more inflammatory signals than a cell that died from a slow, drug-mediated lipid cascade. If physical modalities produce both more killing and more immunogenic killing, the advantage compounds.

Ferroptotic cell death releases damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) — particularly HMGB1, calreticulin, and ATP — that activate dendritic cells and prime local T cell responses (immunogenic cell death, ICD). To explore whether the kill-density and kill-quality differential between SDT and RSL3 translates into differential immune activation, we added a spatial immune coupling layer to the TME simulation. All immune parameters in this layer — T cell kill rate, DAMP diffusion coefficient, DC activation threshold — are estimated, not calibrated to experimental data, placing this section's predictions at medium confidence (Section 9.2). Dead cells release DAMPs into a diffusion field; local DAMP concentration activates resident T cells via a half-maximal response function (Kd = 50.0); activated T cells kill adjacent tumor cells at rate 0.02 per step, suppressed by a PD-1 brake (70% inhibition baseline, reduced to 14% with simulated anti-PD-1).

The DAMP quality per dead cell is not equal across treatments. Lipid peroxidation is an autocatalytic free-radical chain reaction: each peroxyl radical abstracts a hydrogen from an adjacent polyunsaturated fatty acid, generating a new radical and propagating the cascade (established biochemistry[^bio2e_74]). For high-ROS treatments, this cascade continues post-threshold: the simulation now continues LP accumulation for 5 steps after death (with zero repair — the cell's defenses have failed), producing emergent overshoot. SDT/PDT Persister LP reaches ~20 (2.0× the death threshold) because exogenous ROS sustains the chain reaction, while RSL3's slower LP accumulation reaches ~7.8. The DAMP differential therefore comes from both kill *density* (SDT kills ~140K cells vs RSL3's ~163) and kill *quality* (each SDT dead cell releases ~2.6× more DAMPs).

[FIGURE 14: DAMP spatial distribution after immune coupling (O2 gradient λ=120μm, per-panel scaling — intensity is not comparable across panels). Control: negligible signal. RSL3: sparse isolated hotspots (163 kills). SDT: DAMP signal covers the full tumor area (139,640 ferroptosis kills, 521 immune kills). The spatial coverage pattern, not color intensity, illustrates why SDT generates orders-of-magnitude more immune activation.]

Results (O2 gradient λ=120μm, 500×500 grid): SDT with immune coupling produces 139,640 ferroptotic kills plus 521 immune kills (88.1% total), compared to 87.8% with ferroptosis alone. Adding simulated anti-PD-1 increases immune kills to 1,477 (88.7% total) — a 2.8× amplification. RSL3 produces only 5 immune kills without anti-PD-1 and 18 with it, because its sparse kill field generates negligible local DAMP concentration. The model-predicted SDT-to-RSL3 immune kill ratio is 104:1 without anti-PD-1 and 82:1 with it — a theoretical ceiling that depends on estimated parameters and should not be interpreted as a quantitative prediction (see caveats below and Section 9.2).

This 104:1 figure is the model's most over-extrapolated number, and a verified-literature review (issue #288) bounds it from two directions. First, the *direction* is well grounded: pharmacologic ferroptosis (RSL3/GPX4 inhibition) is non- to weakly-immunogenic[^wiernicki2022] (with the genuine, still-unresolved caveat that *early* ferroptotic cells can mature dendritic cells and vaccinate[^pmid33188036]), whereas sonodynamic and photodynamic therapy reproducibly drive dendritic-cell maturation, CD8 T-cell infiltration, and systemic antitumor immunity, including DAMP induction in tumor spheroids[^pmid34669472][^pmid35568916][^pmid38232641]. A ratio well above 1 is therefore defensible. Second, the *magnitude* is not — and the model's own mechanics explain why it inflates: the immune layer above activates T cells through a *saturating* (Michaelis-Menten) response to local DAMP concentration (half-maximal at Kd = 50), so a dense 2D kill field drives the DAMP signal deep into saturation, amplifying the SDT-to-RSL3 ratio. Re-running the model in 3D, where volumetric DAMP dilution keeps the signal below saturation, drops the ratio to roughly 4:1 (Section 8.4) — squarely in the low-single-digit range the experimental literature supports. We therefore treat the *direction* (dense physical-ROS kill primes immunity far more than sparse pharmacologic kill) as the robust, literature-anchored claim, and the specific ratio as illustrative. Notably, no published study runs SDT and an RSL3-only arm against a shared immune readout, so the exact ratio cannot be measured directly — only its direction and order can be anchored.

The absolute immune contribution is modest (~0.3-0.9% additional kill for SDT), which is expected for a resident T cell model without systemic immune recruitment. The qualitative finding matters more: treatments that produce dense spatial kill patterns generate self-reinforcing immune activation that pharmacologic approaches with sparse kill cannot achieve. This suggests that the physical-modality advantage extends beyond direct ROS delivery — the spatial kill density may create a secondary immune mechanism that could further widen the gap.

**How to test this prediction.** Treat persister-enriched cancer cells with SDT versus RSL3 and measure immunogenic cell death (ICD) markers: calreticulin surface exposure (flow cytometry), HMGB1 release (ELISA on culture supernatant), and ATP release (luminescence assay). For the spatial-density component, compare ICD marker release in 2D monolayer (uniform kill) versus 3D spheroid (spatially structured kill).

In 3D spheroids, dense SDT kill regions show measurably higher DAMP concentrations and immune cell recruitment (if co-cultured with dendritic cells or PBMCs) than sparse RSL3 kill regions.

*Expected if prediction fails*: DAMP release is proportional to the number of dead cells regardless of treatment modality or spatial pattern. Caveats: the immune model represents resident T cell activation only (0-48h), not systemic immune recruitment or adaptive immune memory. Furthermore, recent evidence complicates the pro-immunogenic assumption in two specific ways that bear directly on the kill-density logic. First, immunogenicity is governed by the *balance* of stimulatory and inhibitory DAMPs, not their absolute quantity: gemcitabine releases abundant calreticulin, HSP70, and HMGB1 yet fails to induce immunogenic cell death because it co-releases inhibitory PGE2[^pmid33288764]. A large SDT ROS burst could therefore release more inhibitory DAMPs (oxidized lipids, PGE2) and be *less* immunogenic per cell — the opposite of what the LP-overshoot-driven DAMP-quality assumption predicts. Second, ferroptotic cancer cells can impair dendritic cell maturation and suppress antitumor immunity[^wiernicki2022], and ferroptosis can be outright pro-tumorigenic: in vivo, GPX4 loss or high iron accelerates KRAS-driven pancreatic tumorigenesis through an 8-OHG/STING-dependent macrophage pathway[^pmid33311482]. Because pancreatic cancer is among this work's named candidate contexts (Chapter 9), this is not a peripheral caveat — on current in vivo evidence, inducing ferroptosis there could aid the tumor rather than the patient. Together these push the immune-coupling result firmly toward "directional hypothesis" and away from "quantitative prediction." To let this suppressive arm be explored in-model rather than only flagged in prose, the simulation now carries an off-by-default immunosuppressive-ferroptosis term (ferroptosis-core 0.39.0): a fraction of the ferroptotic-death signal scales dendritic-cell priming and CD8 kill DOWN as local death density rises, modeling extracellular GPX4 binding dendritic-cell ZP3 receptors[^pmid41494530] and co-released oxidized lipids and PGE2[^pmid36385526], so under sustained dense kill the net immune effect can flip from immune-promoting to immune-suppressive. The term is uncalibrated, its sign is the result rather than its magnitude, and it is unused for any number reported here.

### 7.3 Stromal Shielding: CAF-Mediated Protection at the Tumor Periphery

The preceding TME features modeled the tumor interior (hypoxia, immune coupling). But the tumor boundary presents a different biology: cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) at the tumor-stroma interface supply neighboring tumor cells with cysteine (for GSH synthesis via the GGT1/SLC7A11 pathway) and oleic acid (MUFA for membrane remodeling via ACSL3-mediated uptake). This metabolic cooperation has been documented in CAF co-culture studies [^pmid34373744] and lipid-ferroptosis research [^refs_group6], but has not been quantified in a spatial ferroptosis simulation.

To test whether stromal shielding meaningfully affects treatment outcomes, we identified the 1,796 tumor cells (1.1% of 159,033 total) with at least one stromal Moore neighbor and applied per-step CAF-mediated boosts: GSH +0.06/step (2.4× the endogenous NRF2 recovery rate, capped at 18.0 mM) and MUFA +0.003/step (capped at 0.25). These are estimated parameters — no textbook coverage exists for CAF biology in any of the reference textbooks used in this project. The underlying biochemistry (GSH scavenging, MUFA-mediated PUFA displacement) is handled by the existing ferroptosis-core engine; the CAF contribution simply increases the supply of two defensive metabolites to boundary cells.

The result is treatment-selective. Among the 1,796 stromal-adjacent cells, CAF protection halves the RSL3 kill rate from 3.0% to 1.5% (50% relative reduction) but reduces the SDT kill rate from only 96.1% to 91.2% (5.1% relative). The hypoxic zone kill rate is unchanged for all treatments — CAFs are at the boundary, not the interior. The overall kill rate change is small (~0.06% for SDT, ~0.02% for RSL3) because only 1.1% of tumor cells are affected.

The mechanism behind the treatment selectivity is the same ROS imbalance that drives the hypoxia finding: RSL3 depends on slow basal ROS (~0.3 relative units) to drive the LP cascade through compromised GPX4. A 2.4× GSH supply boost meaningfully slows this cascade — each step, more ROS is scavenged before it reaches lipid membranes. SDT delivers ~17× higher exogenous ROS (~5.0 relative units), overwhelming the CAF defense within a few steps regardless of the GSH boost. The Michaelis-Menten scavenging kinetics (GSH of 18 provides only 26% better scavenging than GSH of 5) ensure that even maximal CAF supply cannot rescue cells from SDT's ROS flood.

This finding complements the hypoxia and immune coupling results: physical modalities bypass both hypoxic protection (Section 7.1) and CAF-mediated stromal shielding, while also generating stronger downstream immune activation (Section 7.2). Hypoxia and stromal shielding are direct resistance mechanisms that blunt pharmacologic ferroptosis induction; immune coupling is a separate amplification effect that favors treatments with dense spatial kill patterns.

**How to test this prediction.** Co-culture persister-enriched cancer cells with cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) isolated from patient tumors or established CAF cell lines. Compare ferroptosis markers (C11-BODIPY lipid peroxidation, GSH quantification, cell viability) in four conditions: tumor cells alone + RSL3, tumor cells alone + SDT, tumor cells with CAFs + RSL3, tumor cells with CAFs + SDT.

*Expected if prediction holds*: CAF co-culture reduces RSL3-induced cell death by more than 30% (the simulation predicts 50% relative reduction) while reducing SDT-induced death by less than 10% (simulation: 5.1% relative). GSH levels in tumor cells adjacent to CAFs should be measurably higher than in tumor cells without CAF contact, confirming the metabolic supply mechanism.

*Expected if prediction fails*: CAFs protect equally against both RSL3 and SDT. This would indicate either (a) CAFs employ defense mechanisms beyond GSH and MUFA supply that also attenuate exogenous ROS, or (b) the model's ROS-dose asymmetry (0.3 basal vs 5.0 exogenous) overstates the real difference, and CAF-supplied antioxidants CAN neutralize SDT's ROS burst. A finding that CAFs provide NO protection against either treatment would suggest the entire metabolic-cooperation model is weaker than the literature implies.

The model treats all boundary stromal cells as equally protective CAFs (in reality, CAF density and phenotype vary). CAF metabolic exhaustion is not modeled — the supply rate is constant regardless of demand. These results establish that stromal shielding is treatment-selective in the model, but the absolute magnitudes depend on estimated parameters.

### 7.4 Acidic pH: Iron Release vs Drug Ion Trapping

Tumor cores are acidic (pH 6.5-6.8) due to lactic acid accumulation from aerobic glycolysis (the Warburg effect — not covered in the reference textbooks, which associate lactic acid fermentation with anaerobic conditions only). This acidity creates two competing effects on ferroptosis: (1) ferritin protein shells destabilize below pH ~6.5, releasing stored iron into the labile iron pool and increasing Fenton-driven ROS generation (pro-ferroptosis); (2) weak-base drugs become protonated at low extracellular pH and are trapped in acidic compartments, reducing intracellular drug availability (anti-ferroptosis, governed by Henderson-Hasselbalch equilibria — Chemistry2e Sec.14.2). The net effect on ferroptosis is not obvious from first principles: does the iron boost or the drug loss dominate?

To quantify this, we applied an exponential pH gradient from the tumor edge inward (pH(d) = 7.4 - 0.9 × (1 - exp(-d/λ)), λ=120μm), yielding pH 7.4 at the well-perfused periphery and ~6.5 in the deep core. The iron modulation (2.35× at pH 6.5) increases the Fenton ROS term, while the ion trapping (36% drug loss at pH 6.5, linearized Henderson-Hasselbalch) reduces RSL3's effective GPX4 inhibition from 92% to 59%.

In the model, drug trapping dominates. RSL3 ferroptosis kills drop from 163 to 77 (53% reduction) because the GPX4 restoration (from 8% to 41% of normal activity) is a 5.1× repair boost, while the iron-driven ROS increase is only 7%. SDT is barely affected (+0.8%, from 139,640 to 140,693 ferroptosis kills) because ultrasound energy delivery is pH-independent — there is no drug to trap — and the iron boost adds only 0.4% to SDT's total ROS (dwarfed by exogenous ROS of 5.0 relative units). Control shows a tiny increase (1 to 2 ferroptosis kills) from the Fenton boost alone.

This adds a third direct resistance mechanism — alongside hypoxia (Section 7.1) and stromal shielding (Section 7.3) — that selectively penalizes pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers while leaving physical modalities unaffected. All three share the same structural asymmetry: pharmacologic approaches depend on endogenous processes that the TME can disrupt — basal ROS generation (removed by hypoxia), GSH depletion (counteracted by stromal cysteine supply), and intracellular drug bioavailability (reduced by pH-driven ion trapping) — while physical modalities deliver exogenous energy that bypasses these vulnerabilities.

[FIGURE 18: pH gradient with ion trapping. Acidic tumor core (pH 6.5) protonates RSL3 molecules, trapping them outside cells and reducing intracellular drug concentration by 36%. SDT ultrasound energy is pH-independent.]

**How to test this prediction.** Measure RSL3 efficacy at pH 7.4 versus pH 6.5 in buffered culture medium. Additionally, measure intracellular RSL3 concentration (or a fluorescent RSL3 analog) at both pH values using HPLC or fluorescence microscopy to directly test the ion-trapping mechanism.

*Expected if prediction holds*: RSL3-induced cell death drops by more than 40% at pH 6.5 compared to pH 7.4. Intracellular RSL3 concentration at pH 6.5 is measurably lower than at pH 7.4, consistent with extracellular protonation and trapping. SDT efficacy is pH-independent (no drug to trap).

*Expected if prediction fails*: RSL3 efficacy is pH-independent. This would be a significant finding: it would mean that either (a) RSL3 is not subject to Henderson-Hasselbalch ion trapping because its chloroacetamide chemistry behaves differently from classic weak-base drugs (the most likely explanation for failure, since RSL3's pKa is poorly characterized), or (b) the iron release at low pH compensates for any drug trapping, producing a net-neutral effect. Either result would narrow the model's predictions substantially — the pH resistance mechanism would be invalidated, leaving only hypoxia and stromal shielding as TME resistance factors.

This is the finding most in need of direct experimental validation, because the ion_trap_sensitivity parameter is the most uncertain value in the model. RSL3 is a chloroacetamide, not a classic weak base, and its behavior at acidic pH has not been empirically characterized.

Caveats: all pH parameters are estimated. ### 7.5 The Cumulative Picture

The four TME features explored in this chapter are not independent effects that operate in isolation. In a real solid tumor, a cancer cell deep in the hypoxic core (Section 7.1) sits in an acidic microenvironment (Section 7.4), may receive metabolic support from nearby stromal cells if it is close to the tumor boundary (Section 7.3), and — if killed — contributes to a DAMP field that either does or does not activate local immune cells depending on the spatial density of surrounding cell death (Section 7.2).

The three resistance mechanisms share a structural asymmetry: pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers depend on endogenous cellular processes — basal ROS generation (removed by hypoxia), GSH homeostasis (supplemented by stromal supply), and intracellular drug concentration (reduced by pH-driven ion trapping) — while physical modalities deliver exogenous energy that bypasses all three dependencies. The immune coupling mechanism compounds this by favoring the dense, high-quality kill patterns that physical modalities produce.

Whether this asymmetry translates to real tumors is an empirical question. The experimental proposals in each section above are designed to test the individual mechanisms. If any one mechanism fails to replicate, the others remain independently testable — they share a common theme but are mechanistically distinct. If all three resistance mechanisms replicate, the cumulative effect in a real tumor would be substantial: pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers would face hypoxic protection in the core, stromal protection at the boundary, and chemical trapping throughout — while physical modalities are predicted to be less vulnerable to all three at once. One important qualification: the hypoxia leg is the most contested of the three. As Section 7.1 details, sonodynamic therapy's own ROS generation is widely held to be oxygen-dependent (its leading clinical agent is an O2-dependent Type II sensitizer), so the simultaneous-bypass argument is strongest for the stromal and pH barriers and weakest — possibly absent in some tumor types — for hypoxia. We therefore present this cumulative asymmetry as the prediction most worth testing, not as a settled conclusion.

## Chapter 8: Counterarguments and Failure Modes

### 8.1 Other Directions The Repo Was Underweighting

One reason to widen the article framing is that the repo is no longer only telling a PDT/SDT story. Even under conservative tagging, several adjacent directions deserve explicit mention because they may compete with or complement the physical-ROS hypothesis.

**Radioligand therapy** is the clearest example of a clinically advancing, spatially selective modality-class competitor. It shares the intuitive appeal of localized tumor killing while operating within a much more mature theranostic and dosimetry framework.

**Immune residual-disease strategies** — especially TIL/TCR-style cell therapy, therapeutic mRNA or neoantigen vaccination, and phagocytosis-checkpoint manipulation — are important because they aim not just to kill a vulnerable state, but to convert residual disease into an immune-recognizable target. The revised mRNA-vaccine tagging pass is a concrete reminder that this lane must be interpreted through therapeutic-cancer-vaccine papers rather than the broader post-COVID vaccine literature.

**Pathway-target layers** such as SCD/MUFA remodeling, DHODH-linked backup defenses, DHCR7/7-DHC biology, and TRIM25/GPX4 degradation matter because they may explain why ferroptosis-looking vulnerabilities repeatedly weaken in vivo.

**Stromal shielding, senescence programs, and broader tumor-microenvironment biology** are not side notes. They may be the main reason apparently elegant modality-level ideas stall during translation.

The practical implication is that resistant-state matching should become the primary decision layer. Modality choice matters, but it should come after the biology of residual survival is defined. These alternative directions are not merely interesting — some represent clinically established competitors (radioligand therapy, checkpoint immunotherapy), others represent biological realities that constrain feasibility (stromal shielding, lipid remodeling), and all represent the landscape against which any new hypothesis must justify its additional complexity and risk.

### 8.2 The Modality Question: PDT, SDT, and the Tissue-Penetration Tradeoff

If OXPHOS-resistant persister cells are ferroptosis-vulnerable, which modality best exploits this? Our initial corpus analysis identified SDT as unusually aligned with ferroptosis among physical ablative modalities. However, broader PubMed verification reveals a critical context we must address honestly:

|----------|---------------------------|-------------------|-------------|

| **SDT** | **121** | **25** | Deep (cm) |

**PDT dominates SDT on every ferroptosis and ICD metric by approximately 3:1.** This fundamentally challenges any claim that SDT is uniquely positioned for ferroptosis-ICD. PDT — using light rather than ultrasound to activate sensitizers — triggers the same ROS → GSH depletion → ferroptosis → ICD chain with a larger evidence base, more clinical experience, and approved photosensitizers.

**SDT's remaining advantage is tissue penetration.** Ultrasound penetrates centimeters into tissue; light penetrates millimeters. For deep-seated tumors (pancreatic, hepatic, pelvic), SDT can reach targets that PDT cannot. For accessible superficial tumors (skin, oral, esophageal, bladder surface), PDT is the established modality and SDT offers no advantage.

**The honest framing is therefore**: SDT is not the unique ferroptosis-ICD modality — PDT is the established one. SDT is a **depth-extended variant** of the same photosensitizer-based ROS-ferroptosis-ICD approach, using ultrasound instead of light to reach tumors that PDT cannot access. This is a meaningful clinical advantage for deep tumors, but it is incremental, not paradigmatic.

The mechanism is shared: sensitizer-activated ROS → GSH depletion → GPX4 inactivation → ferroptosis → DAMP release → ICD [^refs_group7]. SDT-specific data shows 72 GSH/GPX4 articles (11.7% of SDT-tagged articles) and 6 dual-pathway articles [^refs_group8]. A recent Nature Communications paper demonstrates that IRE can also be combined with ferroptosis via iron-based nanoparticles [^pmid41554743], further indicating that the ferroptosis-physical connection is broader than SDT alone.

**Critical caveats remain**: Most SDT-ferroptosis data involves engineered nanosonosensitizers [^refs_group9]. No clinical study has verified SDT-induced ferroptosis or ICD in human tumors. The PDT precedent — 40 years of development without demonstrating robust ICD-mediated immune synergy in randomized trials — applies as a direct cautionary parallel.

### 8.3 Counterarguments, Precedents, and Reasons This Could Fail

The following counterarguments address whether the physical-ROS hypothesis holds biologically and clinically — whether the real-world biology supports the idea, independent of the computational model that generated it.

**The photodynamic therapy precedent.** PDT is conceptually identical to SDT: physical energy (light vs ultrasound) activates a sensitizer to generate ROS, causing cell death with ICD potential. PDT has been in clinical development for 40+ years and has achieved approval for a few indications (esophageal, skin) but has never demonstrated the "ROS → ICD → immune synergy → durable response" chain in a randomized clinical trial. PDT + immunotherapy combinations remain at the case report level [^pmid41833681]. If this logic worked robustly, PDT would have shown it by now. SDT's claimed advantages over PDT — deeper tissue penetration and potentially superior ferroptosis engagement via engineered nanosonosensitizers — are plausible but incremental, not paradigmatic. The SDT hypothesis must explain why it would succeed where PDT has not demonstrated clear immune synergy. For recent coverage of TTFields clinical progress, including immune-system engagement beyond glioblastoma, see [^news6].

**The ferroptosis vulnerability window may be transient.** Persister cells downregulate FSP1 as part of the mesenchymal/dedifferentiated state [^pmid41481741]. However, persisters are a transitional population: they eventually either die or re-enter proliferation with restored drug resistance. The ferroptosis-sensitive window may last days to weeks. If the real window closes before any follow-up treatment can be administered, the therapeutic opportunity is lost regardless of which modality is used.

**The lipid-remodeling and backup-defense problem.** OXPHOS-resistant cells may co-upregulate NRF2-driven antioxidant defenses, SCD/MUFA remodeling, DHODH-linked backup defenses, DHCR7/7-DHC biology, TRIM25-linked GPX4 degradation dynamics, and other lipid-handling programs that neutralize ferroptosis vulnerability even as the metabolic preconditions accumulate. The pathway-target layer now shows that these escape axes are present in the corpus, but mostly in review-heavy or early mechanistic form. They therefore strengthen the need for caution more than they strengthen any single therapeutic claim.

**The nanosonosensitizer confound.** Most SDT-ferroptosis papers use engineered nanoparticles *designed* to deplete GSH and release iron. If plain SDT with conventional sonosensitizers doesn't trigger ferroptosis, the insight is about nanoparticle design, not SDT as a modality — and the regulatory path becomes device+drug+nanoparticle, the most complex category.

**The ICD-to-immunity chain has repeatedly underperformed clinically.** Pexa-Vec (oncolytic virus ICD, failed Phase 3) [^pmid31413923], CheckMate-498 (immunotherapy in GBM, failed) [^pmid35419607], KEYNOTE-361 (ICI+chemo in urothelial, failed) [^pmid34051178], and BIND-014 (nanoparticle, failed Phase 2) [^pmid29978216] all demonstrate that preclinical rationale frequently fails in translation. SDT has no reason to be exempt from this base rate. For an example of how combination strategies are being tested against aggressive cancers in current trials, see [^news7].

**Narrow applicability.** The physical-ROS hypothesis applies only to accessible or image-localizable solid tumors, immunocompetent settings where ICD could matter, tumors that actually enter a ferroptosis-prone residual state, and a specific post-therapy time window. Hematologic malignancies, diffuse metastatic disease, and many non-localizable residual states fall outside its plausible range.

**Evolutionary limitation.** This hypothesis addresses one resistance transition. After SDT kills ferroptosis-sensitive persisters, survivors are selected for ferroptosis resistance (GPX4 upregulation, NRF2 activation, FSP1 restoration). The approach adds one step to the therapeutic arms race but does not end it. Durable eradication would require the ICD-triggered immune response to eliminate residual heterogeneous disease — an outcome that has not been demonstrated for any ICD-based strategy in randomized trials.

**Prior art.** Collateral sensitivity[^pluchino2012], adaptive therapy[^gatenby2009], and synthetic lethality are established frameworks for exploiting resistance tradeoffs. What our analysis adds is the specific SDT connection, not the general principle.

### 8.4 Structural Limitations of the Simulation Architecture

The counterarguments above address whether the physical-ROS hypothesis holds biologically and clinically. A separate question is whether the computational model that generated the hypothesis is structurally adequate to support it. Section 5.4 listed what the model omits. Here we assess how those omissions affect the specific predictions in Chapters 6 and 7.

**Steady-state environmental fields.** The oxygen, pH, and drug concentration fields in the TME simulations are computed once based on distance from the tumor edge. They do not change as cells consume oxygen, produce lactate, or metabolize drugs. In real tumors, blood flow fluctuates on timescales of minutes to hours, and cells in the transition zone between oxygenated and hypoxic regions experience intermittent hypoxia — cycling between states rather than remaining permanently in one. Intermittent hypoxia is documented to produce biological responses distinct from chronic hypoxia, including increased angiogenesis, enhanced metastatic potential, and treatment resistance through mechanisms that differ from those modeled here. To test whether the hypoxia finding survives under dynamic conditions, we implemented a square-wave O2 cycling mode that alternates the penetration length between 80μm (deep hypoxia) and 150μm (normoxic) every 30 steps (period = 60 steps, 50% duty cycle). Under cycling, RSL3 overall kill remains at 0.1% — essentially identical to steady-state hypoxia (0.1% at λ=120μm). The transition zone shows a modest increase (0.2% cycling vs 0.1% steady-state), confirming that reoxygenation windows allow limited RSL3-driven LP initiation in cells that would otherwise remain quiescent under chronic hypoxia. Normoxic-zone and overall kill rates are unchanged between conditions. SDT overall kill is unchanged at 87.8%. The directional claim — RSL3 collapses under hypoxia while SDT maintains — holds under cycling conditions. The pH result (Section 7.4) faces the same steady-state limitation but has not been tested with dynamic pH (deferred). Overall direction: the resistance mechanisms identified here persist under dynamic O2, their spatial boundaries would be less sharp than the smooth gradients imply, but the pharmacologic-vs-physical asymmetry is robust.

**Simplified immune layer.** The immune coupling model (Section 7.2) represents resident T cell activation over a 0-48 hour window. It does not model systemic immune recruitment from blood or lymph nodes, adaptive immune memory formation, immune editing of tumor populations, or regulatory immune suppression. The 104:1 SDT-to-RSL3 immune kill ratio is a local phenomenon driven by local DAMP concentration. Whether these local patterns translate to systemic anti-tumor immunity — the outcome that would matter clinically — depends on processes the model does not represent. Two opposing outcomes are plausible. If local DAMP patterns recruit circulating immune effectors, the SDT advantage would compound. If systemic immune regulation dominates — through regulatory T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, or checkpoint upregulation — the local response would be dampened regardless of DAMP density, and the 104:1 ratio would overstate the real differential. Given the clinical trial failure rate documented in Section 8.3 — where ICD-to-immunity chains have repeatedly underperformed across multiple modalities — the dampening scenario is the more probable default. The amplification scenario would require SDT to produce qualitatively different immune activation than the modalities that have already failed, a claim for which no evidence currently exists. The experimental test proposed in Section 7.2 (ICD marker measurement with immune cell co-culture) is designed to provide the first empirical constraint, but resolving the systemic question will require in vivo models that the current computational framework cannot replace.

**No adaptive evolution (in the analyses presented here).** The simulation runs 180 steps within a single treatment window, and the 2D analyses in Chapters 6 and 7 do not model mutation, clonal selection, or population dynamics across treatment cycles. Section 8.3 already notes the biological reality: survivors of ferroptosis-inducing treatment will be selected for ferroptosis resistance (GPX4 upregulation, NRF2 activation, FSP1 restoration). The computational limitation, for the results in this book, is that they do not quantify how fast this erosion occurs or whether repeated treatment cycles produce accumulating benefit or diminishing returns. (The library has since grown uncalibrated `clonal` and `persister` layers that begin to model exactly this — see "The 3D simulation suite" below — but they are not calibrated and are not used for any number in this book.) The persister layer in particular now carries an off-by-default reversible-to-irreversible epigenetic-locking transition (ferroptosis-core 0.40.0): sustained continuous exposure can move part of the persister pool into a non-reverting locked state, while intermittent dosing keeps the sustained-exposure tracker below threshold and does not lock, a first model handle on the intermittent-versus-continuous dosing question (FSP1/HDAC-suppressed persister durability, Higuchi 2026[^pmid41481741]); it is uncalibrated and unused for any number here. The treatment advantages shown in Chapters 6 and 7 — hypoxic resistance bypass, spatial kill density, dual-pathway synergy — are snapshots of what happens during one treatment cycle. Whether those per-cycle advantages translate to durable clinical benefit depends on the rate of evolutionary escape relative to the rate of immune clearance, a question that requires population-dynamics modeling across weeks to months. Such modeling does not yet exist for the ferroptosis context, and building it is a separate research challenge.

**Estimated parameters and the quantitative-versus-directional distinction.** Many model parameters — CAF supply rates, DAMP diffusion coefficients, T cell kill rates, FSP1 repair kinetics, ion trapping sensitivity — are estimated from indirect evidence rather than calibrated to direct experimental measurements. The sensitivity analysis in Chapter 5 tested robustness to ±50% perturbation on 11 rate constants and found that the qualitative result (Persister cells are more sensitive than Glycolytic cells under SDT) held in 22 of 22 conditions, indicating directional robustness. But directional robustness is not quantitative accuracy. The specific numbers reported throughout this book — 1.99× Bliss synergy (Section 6.3), 53% pH-driven RSL3 kill reduction (Section 7.4), 104:1 immune kill ratio (Section 7.2), 50% stromal protection of RSL3 (Section 7.3) — depend on parameter values that could change substantially with better data. Different FSP1 inhibitor potency would change the synergy score. Different RSL3 pKa would change the pH finding entirely — Section 7.4 explicitly flags this as the most uncertain parameter in the model. The distinction the reader should draw: directional findings (dual-pathway depletion outperforms single-pathway; physical ROS is less vulnerable than pharmacologic induction to the stromal and pH barriers — the hypoxia leg is genuinely contested, see Section 7.1; dense kill patterns generate more immune activation than sparse patterns, subject to the inhibitory-DAMP caveat in Section 7.2) are more robust to parameter variation than the specific magnitudes and are the predictions most worth testing. Specific magnitudes are not robust and should be treated as order-of-magnitude estimates, not precise forecasts. Each experiment proposed in Chapters 6 and 7 is designed to provide calibration data the model currently lacks — turning estimated parameters into measured ones, and testing whether the directional predictions survive contact with real biology.

**The 3D simulation suite, and why its numbers are not in this book.** Everything above describes the 2D and single-cell engine that produced the results in Chapters 6 and 7. Since those analyses were frozen, the codebase has grown a second track — the `sim-tme-3d` capstone and more than a dozen additional "realism layers" in the `ferroptosis-core` library: explicit 3D Two further layers have since been partially anchored to independent published data, neither altering this book's numbers: the spheroid module's radial zone geometry was checked against the Browning 2021 size-resolved confocal dataset and matches it only for spheroid radius at or above 300 micrometers (#333, geometry only, the per-zone biochemical gradient strengths remaining uncalibrated; `analysis/calibration/spheroid-structure-validation.md`), and a follow-up added an opt-in size-aware zone-threshold variant that ramps the zones with spheroid size so small spheroids are correctly mostly proliferating with no necrotic core (cutting the mean boundary error on the Browning bins from 0.338 to 0.025 while reducing to the validated fixed structure at large radius), leaving the per-zone biochemistry uncalibrated. The size-resolved kill leg was then characterized with a simulation sweep (RSL3 kill across spheroid sizes): with the fixed thresholds the supply gradient reproduces the universal bigger-spheroids-resist-more direction (kill falls roughly nineteen-fold over 144 to 540 micrometers radius, within the measured cytotoxic size-resistance range), while the size-aware structure yields a falsifiable prediction specific to a persister-targeting ferroptosis inducer, that small, all-proliferating spheroids resist RSL3 (no persister target) and vulnerability emerges only as the persister core appears, the opposite size-direction to generic cytotoxics. The kill magnitude stays uncalibrated and a ferroptosis-inducer multi-size kill experiment, not yet available, is what would validate it, and the tumor-PK layer was anchored to the only public ferroptosis-specific in-vivo pharmacokinetics with a paired plasma and tumor concentration-time course, imidazole ketone erastin (a system xc- inducer), which gives a measured tissue-to-plasma partition near 0.90 against the presets' estimated 0.15 to 0.50, a plasma-to-tumor delay of about two hours, and a closed-form proof that the disposition is multi-compartment, with a sorafenib human population-PK forward check (Cavg,ss near 4.1 mg/L at 400 mg twice daily, inside the clinical 3 to 5 mg/L range); the per-tumor presets are not recalibrated, since no public per-tumor pharmacokinetics exist for a ferroptosis inducer, and the RSL3/ML tool-compound PK gap is flagged rather than fabricated (#334; `analysis/calibration/pk-calibration.md`). Two consequences follow. First, several limitations stated above as things the model "cannot do" are now things the library can do but has not calibrated: the clonal and persister layers do model adaptive evolution and acquired tolerance; the suppressor and multi-checkpoint layers do model Treg/MDSC and checkpoint dampening; an IFN-γ coupling layer now closes the immune-amplification loop in the reverse direction — Chapter 7's coupling runs ferroptotic death to DAMP to CD8 priming, and this layer adds the molecular return arm, in which activated-CD8 IFN-γ represses tumor System Xc- (SLC7A11/SLC3A2) and so sensitizes those cells back to ferroptosis (Wang et al., Nature 2019, PMID 31043744), an off-by-default and uncalibrated handle on the positive-feedback loop between immune killing and ferroptosis (#443); an explicit-vasculature module can replace the edge-distance oxygen proxy; a `contact` layer models the ACSL4 (PUFA) and TFRC (iron-import) downregulation that Section 5.4 lists as "not represented," driven by E-cadherin/NF2-YAP cell-cell contact (Wu et al., Nature 2019, PMID 31341276, the foundational density-dependent ferroptosis-resistance result); and a `senescence` program represents therapy-induced senescence as a ferroptosis state whose direction is genuinely contested and which the model therefore makes bidirectional rather than forcing one sign: senescent cells resist upstream (cystine/iron) triggers but are a senolytic target under the direct GPX4 inhibition this work's RSL3 actually applies (Masaldan et al., Redox Biol 2018, PMID 28888202; D'Ambrosio et al., Nature Cell Biology 2026, PMID 42032311), so the simpler "senescence resists ferroptosis" assumption is incomplete here. That senescence layer has since been extended with a CDK4/6-inhibitor-primed senescence-to-ferroptosis combination (palbociclib or ribociclib induces senescence over a multi-day window, then a ferroptosis inducer challenges the primed population), which yields a falsifiable prediction that longer priming recruits a larger senolytic-vulnerable fraction and the vulnerability window closes as senescence resolves; the induction and recovery kinetics are uncalibrated placeholders grounded only in the one-to-three-week senescence timescale, so the direction is the result, not the day-precise magnitudes (#447). A companion `acsl4` layer turns ACSL4 expression status into a stratification biomarker: ACSL4 is the enzyme that ligates the polyunsaturated fatty acids ferroptosis requires into membranes, so an ACSL4-high tumor is ferroptosis-sensitive while an ACSL4-low or ACSL4-negative tumor (for instance some hepatocellular-carcinoma and acute-myeloid-leukemia subtypes) is intrinsically refractory through a collapsed-substrate mechanism distinct from the GPX4, glutathione, and FSP1 defenses (Doll et al., Nature Chemical Biology 2017, PMID 27842070). The model reproduces that ordering (ACSL4-high cells die more under a fixed ferroptosis-inducer dose, ACSL4-negative cells die less), which gives the falsifiable clinical-stratification prediction that ferroptosis inducers will benefit ACSL4-high cancers and spare ACSL4-negative ones. The status-to-sensitivity mapping is an uncalibrated linear placeholder, and the two pieces that would calibrate it, a per-cancer-type ACSL4 expression and deletion prevalence survey and a cell-line meta-analysis pairing ACSL4 status with measured ferroptosis-inducer dose-response, are data that the repository does not yet carry, so they are flagged as collaborator tasks rather than fabricated (#444). That prevalence leg has since been partly closed (#462): a fetch of all thirty-two TCGA PanCancer Atlas studies from the public cBioPortal interface gives a real per-cancer-type ACSL4 distribution, and the within-cohort low-ACSL4 fraction (about eleven to nineteen percent of tumors per cancer type) is the population prior for how many tumors fall in the refractory-leaning tail, with the model's status scale now bridged to a real expression z-score. The honest surprise is that bulk tumor messenger-RNA does not show liver and myeloid cancers as ACSL4-low the way the cell-line literature implies (liver cancer in fact has the highest bulk ACSL4), so the refractory ACSL4-low state is a protein-level or subtype property that bulk expression medians miss, and the magnitude of the ACSL4-to-sensitivity mapping still awaits a cell-line dose-response meta-analysis. A further layer arrived together with its calibration, the closest the suite has come to tying a spatial prediction to a measurement: a `trigger_wave` module models ferroptosis spreading as a constant-speed lipid-peroxide front (a bistable reaction-diffusion wave, the spatial complement of the single-cell switch), and it is anchored to the Co 2024 measured ferroptotic trigger-wave speeds (5.52 micrometers per minute at baseline, iron-tunable to 2.33 under iron chelation and 9.40 under iron loading; PMID 38987590). The model reproduces the measured iron-dose response shape, front speed proportional to the square root of labile iron, at biologically plausible iron fold-changes, so the iron tuning is the predicted result rather than a fit; only the absolute diffusion coefficient is absorbed into a one-point calibration of the baseline speed, and the front-speed magnitude is therefore anchored in shape and order of magnitude, not derived from first principles (#482). The honest statement is not that the model is structurally incapable, but that the analyses in this book do not use those layers and the layers are not yet calibrated. A reaction-diffusion solver added to the 3D suite (`reaction_diffusion`, issue #343; the full comparison is in `analysis/reaction-diffusion-benchmark.md`) confirms that this nearest-vessel proxy is optimistic in sparse networks: in the λ ≲ vessel-spacing regime (the regime of the 2D analyses here, λ = 120 µm at the 20 µm grid resolution), a true reaction-diffusion solve over the same explicit-vessel geometry yields lower supply fields than the exponential proxy, driven chiefly by 3-D point-source geometry (a real vessel's field is the Yukawa form exp(-r/λ)/r) versus the planar single-source field the proxy assumes — a single isolated vessel reads 0.85 (proxy) versus 0.31 (RD), and across a sparse network the proxy calls the hypoxic fraction 62% while the RD solve calls it 99%. These numbers are directional and representation-dependent (each vessel is a single Dirichlet voxel at uncalibrated radius and grid resolution), and the sign reverses at λ ≫ spacing (where superposition between vessels enriches the field above the proxy), so the proxy-is-optimistic direction holds only in the λ ≲ spacing regime. Two caveats keep this from re-touching the book's numbers. First, the finding bears on an explicit-vessel network, not on the edge-distance O2 proxy that Section 7.1 actually uses or the single-vessel-wall Krogh limit that Section 6.1 reports — that 1-D planar-source limit is the one case the benchmark finds the proxy gets exactly right. Second, every layer involved is uncalibrated. The honest reading is therefore directional only: a sparse tumor solved with explicit-vessel reaction-diffusion would likely look more hypoxic than the edge-distance and Krogh proxies imply, which is one more reason the hypoxia-resistance and drug-penetration findings in Chapters 6 and 7 are presented as directional rather than as calibrated magnitudes. Second, where a result has been re-run in 3D it can shift by an order of magnitude: the 104:1 SDT-to-RSL3 immune ratio (Section 7.2) falls to roughly 4:1 in the 3D geometry, plausibly from volumetric DAMP dilution and finite-size effects, which is exactly why that ratio is presented as a directional ceiling rather than a number.

These structural limitations define the boundaries of what the computational work can claim. Whether the directional findings survive contact with real biology is the open question that this book cannot answer computationally. The experiments proposed in Chapters 6 and 7 would begin to answer it — but none have been performed, and each could fail for reasons the model does not anticipate.

# Part IV: What Should Happen Next

## Chapter 9: Research Roadmap

### 9.1 From Predictions to Experiments

Chapter 8 catalogued the reasons this hypothesis could fail — biologically, clinically, and computationally. This chapter proposes a research program designed to test whether it does. The program is organized not by which finding seems most exciting but by which predictions rest on the best-characterized parameters, because the failure of a high-confidence prediction is more informative than the failure of one that depends on uncertain estimates.

Chapters 6 and 7 each proposed specific experiments attached to individual simulation findings — seven in total, each with a hypothesis, model system, and expected outcomes if the prediction holds or fails. This chapter does not restate those proposals. Instead, it organizes them into a phased program: which experiments to run first and why, which experiments gate which follow-ups, what new experiments are needed beyond the individual-finding tests, and what the field learns if the entire program fails.

This chapter is for research prioritization, not treatment advice. The experimental suggestions identify what to test and why — they are not clinical protocols, statistical designs, or grant-ready specifications. A researcher using this chapter as a starting point would still need to optimize assay conditions, determine sample sizes, and adapt the proposals to their specific model systems and expertise.

### 9.2 Confidence Tiers

Not all simulation predictions are equally trustworthy. Some rest on well-characterized biochemistry and measured physical constants; others depend on parameters that are estimated from indirect evidence or, in one case, entirely uncharacterized. Assigning a confidence tier to each prediction makes the program's risk profile explicit and helps a researcher decide where to invest effort first.

The tiers are defined by parameter provenance:

- **High confidence**: The prediction depends primarily on textbook biochemistry or well-measured physical constants. Sensitivity analysis confirms directional robustness. Failure of a high-confidence prediction would require a fundamental assumption to be wrong.
- **Medium confidence**: The underlying mechanism is published, but key magnitude parameters are estimated from indirect evidence rather than measured in the model system. The prediction's direction is robust but its quantitative magnitude could shift substantially with better data.
- **Low confidence**: The prediction depends on parameters that are explicitly flagged as the model's most uncertain. The prediction could change qualitatively — not just quantitatively — if the parameter is wrong.

**High confidence:**

*Hypoxia resistance (Section 7.1).* The model predicts RSL3 kill collapses more than 30-fold in hypoxic conditions while SDT maintains greater than 85% kill. The RSL3 collapse rests on established biochemistry: oxygen is the terminal electron acceptor for mitochondrial ROS generation (textbook), so a GPX4 inhibitor that depends on basal ROS has little to act on in hypoxia. That direction is the high-confidence part. The SDT half is the contested leg: SDT's own ROS generation is widely held to be O2-dependent (its leading clinical agent is a Type II, O2-dependent sensitizer), so SDT fully maintaining kill under hypoxia is an optimistic upper bound, not a settled result (Section 7.1; Figure 24 tiers this panel as contested). The high-confidence claim is therefore the endogenous-vs-exogenous ROS asymmetry direction (the most fundamental claim in the simulation work), not the magnitude of SDT's hypoxic advantage.

*Spatial penetration differential (Section 6.1).* PDT kill collapses beyond 3 mm depth; SDT maintains efficacy through 1 cm. This is physics, not biology: light attenuation at 630 nm and acoustic attenuation at 1 MHz are well-measured constants. If this prediction fails in a spheroid experiment, the attenuation model is wrong — which would be surprising for well-characterized physics but would be immediately informative.

*Dual-pathway synergy (Section 6.3).* RSL3 + FSP1 inhibitor produces approximately 2x Bliss synergy through dual-pathway depletion. GPX4 and FSP1 independence is published.[^bersuker2019] The directional prediction (dual > single) is robust to parameter variation. If the combination index shows additivity rather than synergy, FSP1's role as an independent parallel repair pathway needs reassessment — a finding with implications well beyond this project.

**Medium confidence:**

*Vulnerability window timing (Section 6.2).* RSL3 sensitivity window closes within 3 days; exogenous-ROS sensitivity persists for weeks. The defense-pathway recovery half-lives (GPX4: 3 days, FSP1: 7 days) are literature-estimated, not measured in the specific persister model system the simulation uses. If recovery is much faster than estimated (hours instead of days), the therapeutic window may be too narrow for any follow-up treatment to exploit.

*Immune coupling (Section 7.2).* SDT generates 104x more immune kills than RSL3 through spatial DAMP density. The LP overshoot that drives DAMP quality is emergent from the biochemistry, but all immune parameters (T cell kill rate, DAMP diffusion, DC activation threshold) are estimated. Chapter 8.4 flags that systemic immune dynamics could amplify or suppress the local advantage — and the clinical trial failure rate (Section 8.3) suggests suppression is the more probable default.

*Stromal shielding (Section 7.3).* CAF-mediated GSH and MUFA supply halves RSL3 kill at the tumor boundary while barely affecting SDT. The CAF supply mechanism is published, but the specific supply rates used in the model have no textbook source. The overall tumor impact is small (1.1% of cells affected) even if the per-cell effect is large.

**Low confidence:**

*pH ion trapping (Section 7.4).* Acidic pH reduces RSL3 kill by 53% through Henderson-Hasselbalch-driven drug trapping. RSL3 is a chloroacetamide, not a classic weak base — its pKa is uncharacterized, and the ion trapping sensitivity is the most uncertain parameter in the entire model. This prediction is the most likely to fail experimentally. If it does fail, the pH resistance mechanism is eliminated, leaving only hypoxia and stromal shielding as TME resistance factors. If it succeeds, it identifies a previously unrecognized pharmacokinetic limitation of chloroacetamide-class compounds in acidic tumors.

### 9.3 Program Structure

The experimental program has a gated structure designed to maximize information per experiment and minimize wasted effort.

**Phase 1 — High-confidence in-vitro tests.** Run the hypoxia (7.1), spatial penetration (6.1), and dual-pathway synergy (6.3) experiments first. These test the cleanest predictions using the best-characterized parameters. If any of these fails, the specific model assumption that broke should be identified before proceeding — it may require revising the simulation architecture before the remaining predictions are worth testing.

**Phase 2 — Medium-confidence refinements.** If Phase 1 confirms the directional findings, run the vulnerability window (6.2), immune coupling (7.2), and stromal shielding (7.3) experiments. These refine the picture: how long is the treatment window? Does kill density actually translate to immune activation? Does stromal protection matter at the population level? Phase 2 experiments depend on the directional findings from Phase 1 holding — if drugs and physical modalities show similar hypoxic sensitivity, the TME-resistance framing needs revision before investing in refinement experiments.

**Phase 3 — Low-confidence test.** Run the pH ion-trapping experiment (7.4) last among the in-vitro studies. This is the most likely to fail and the result either validates or eliminates one of the three proposed TME resistance mechanisms. Running it after Phases 1-2 means the broader program isn't derailed by a single uncertain parameter.

**Running in parallel — Broader comparison (Section 9.4).** This is analytical, not experimental. It runs alongside the wet-lab program and asks whether the entire approach justifies its complexity relative to existing alternatives.

**Gated — In-vivo integration (Section 9.5).** This is the definitive test and the most expensive. It should only proceed after the in-vitro program confirms the directional predictions. Running it prematurely would risk investing substantial resources in an approach whose foundational assumptions haven't been verified.

### 9.4 Broader Comparison: Does This Hypothesis Justify Its Complexity?

This is not an experiment. It is an analytical evaluation of whether the physical-ROS ferroptosis approach is worth pursuing relative to existing alternatives — a question the simulation cannot answer because it doesn't model the alternatives.

The comparison should evaluate each approach on shared axes: regulatory maturity (how far from clinical use?), evidence depth (clinical trials vs preclinical vs computational?), delivery burden (how complex is the treatment logistics?), and applicability to residual disease (does it target the residual-cell biology this project focuses on?). The comparison is necessarily imperfect — these approaches operate on fundamentally different biological principles (ionizing radiation, immune activation, ROS-mediated lipid damage) and a shared-axis evaluation cannot capture all dimensions of their respective strengths and weaknesses.

The most informative comparison is against radioligand therapy. Radioligand uses targeted radiation delivered by molecules that seek specific receptors on cancer cells — it shares the intuitive appeal of spatially selective tumor killing while operating within a more mature regulatory framework (FDA approvals for prostate and neuroendocrine cancers, established dosimetry pipelines, and a large clinical trial base). If radioligand therapy addresses the same clinical need — treating localized residual disease — with a simpler development path, the physical-ROS hypothesis becomes academically interesting but clinically unnecessary.

The comparison is not winner-take-all. Different approaches may be optimal for different clinical contexts: radioligand for tumors with targetable receptors, checkpoint combinations for immunologically hot tumors, and physical-ROS modalities for deep-seated tumors without targetable receptors in immunocompetent settings where ICD might matter. The value of the comparison is not in crowning a winner but in defining the niche — if any — where the physical-ROS approach adds something the alternatives cannot.

### 9.5 In-Vivo Integration

The in-vitro experiments in Phases 1-3 test individual predictions in controlled conditions. The in-vivo integration experiment tests whether those predictions survive the full complexity of a living system — where hypoxia, pH gradients, stromal shielding, immune activation, drug penetration, and evolutionary dynamics all operate simultaneously.

The experiment requires an immunocompetent model (so the immune coupling prediction can be tested) with residual disease generated by initial treatment and recovery. The critical design constraint: the study must include honest comparators. Any in-vivo test that includes only SDT and untreated controls cannot determine whether SDT offers an advantage over its closest precedent (PDT) or over pharmacologic ferroptosis approaches. At minimum, the study needs physical-modality arms, pharmacologic arms, and combination arms to disentangle the modality effect from the drug effect and the combination effect.

This experiment is expensive, time-consuming, and tests multiple predictions simultaneously. Running it without in-vitro confirmation from Phases 1-2 would be premature because failure would be uninterpretable — you would not know which prediction failed or why. Running it after in-vitro confirmation means failure would identify the specific gap between controlled conditions and biological reality, which is itself valuable.

If the predictions hold: SDT plus anti-PD-1 outperforms other arms in deep tumors with hypoxic cores, with higher immune infiltrate and ferroptosis markers at depth than PDT or RSL3 arms. If the predictions fail: the TME resistance asymmetry does not translate in vivo, which could mean that (a) the in-vitro-to-in-vivo gap is larger than the spatial simulation predicts, (b) the immune coupling does not scale from local DAMP signaling to systemic anti-tumor immunity, or (c) evolutionary escape operates faster than the one-cycle snapshot suggests.

### 9.6 What the Field Learns If the Program Fails

The most important question a research program can answer is not "does it work?" but "what do we learn either way?" A program that only produces knowledge when it succeeds is advocacy, not science. Each phase of this program has defined failure criteria that advance understanding regardless of outcome.

If the high-confidence experiments fail — if RSL3 and SDT show similar sensitivity to hypoxia, or if light and sound attenuate similarly in tissue, or if dual-pathway depletion produces additive rather than synergistic kill — the most fundamental predictions of the model are wrong. The ROS-source asymmetry (endogenous versus exogenous) is smaller than the simulation estimates, or it doesn't exist at the treatment-relevant scale. The field learns that the biochemical rationale for the physical-modality advantage does not hold — the depth advantage of ultrasound over light is already established physics and does not require this project to demonstrate.

If the medium-confidence experiments fail — if the vulnerability window is much shorter than predicted, if DAMP release does not correlate with treatment modality, or if CAF co-culture provides no detectable protection — the directional findings from Phase 1 may still hold, but the quantitative magnitudes are wrong and the model's parameter estimates need substantial revision. The field learns what calibration data is needed for the next generation of ferroptosis models.

If the entire program fails — if every in-vitro experiment shows no modality-dependent differential — the directional findings were computational artifacts produced by estimated parameters that do not reflect real biology. The biochemical model of ferroptosis sensitivity that this project built would be empirically invalidated, and the experimental predictions derived from it would be retracted as modeling artifacts rather than biological patterns.

The operational side of this roadmap, candidate publication venues for the corpus synthesis and the simulation methods (kept as separate contributions), a preregistration draft of this chapter's falsifiable predictions to be registered before any calibration, and a collaborator-facing brief of the cheapest validating experiments, is maintained outside the book in `analysis/contribution-plan-2026.md`.

## Chapter 10: The Broader Landscape

### 10.1 Conclusion

This article began as a modality-centered question and ends in a broader place.

**What the corpus analysis shows.** Cancer-therapy literatures need to be compared on resistant-state relevance, evidence maturity, delivery constraints, and known analytic artifacts before any one mechanism is over-privileged. Our evidence tagger has 96% binary evidence-presence precision but only 55% evidence-level recall, meaning tagged evidence is almost always real; mechanism gap claims, however, rest on mechanism-level recall (about 90% where it can be measured non-circularly, Section 3.6), so they require less qualification than the 55% evidence figure alone would imply. The tissue-of-origin layer reveals that among the 695 physical-modality articles with tissue tags, research is overwhelmingly concentrated in epithelial (66%) and neuroectodermal (37%) contexts (overlapping — articles can span multiple tissue categories), with hematologic and mesothelial contexts nearly absent. The designed-combination classifier shows that only 19.7% of multi-mechanism papers represent actual combination studies — the rest are reviews or incidental co-mentions.

**What the simulations show.** The mechanistic simulations produce three findings that, if validated experimentally, would have translational implications:

1. *Drug combination synergy through dual-pathway depletion.* In the simulation, RSL3 (GPX4 inhibitor) + FSP1 inhibitor produces 1.99× Bliss synergy because depleting both parallel repair pathways drops total antioxidant defense below the autocatalytic LP propagation threshold. This suggests combination strategies targeting GPX4 AND FSP1 simultaneously, rather than either alone.

2. *Within ferroptosis-targeted therapy, the tumor microenvironment affects pharmacologic and physical approaches differently (three resistance mechanisms and one amplification effect, all conditional on the model's estimated parameters and the narrowest, most context-specific of the three findings).* (a) Hypoxia (model-predicted): RSL3 kill collapses from 3.7% to 0.1% under oxygen gradients; SDT is less affected (87.8%). (b) Stromal shielding (model-predicted): CAF-mediated GSH/MUFA supply halves RSL3's peripheral kill rate (3.0% to 1.5%); SDT barely affected (96.1% to 91.2%). (c) Acidic pH (model-predicted): ion trapping halves RSL3 ferroptosis kills (163 to 77, 53% reduction) as drug bioavailability drops 36% at pH 6.5; SDT gains only 0.8% (iron boost negligible vs exogenous ROS). These three resistance mechanisms share a structural asymmetry: pharmacologic inducers depend on slow endogenous processes that the TME disrupts, while physical modalities deliver exogenous energy that is predicted to be less affected. As Section 7.1 cautions, sonodynamic therapy's own oxygen-dependence makes the hypoxia component (a) the least certain of the three, and in some tumor types (e.g. clear-cell renal carcinoma) hypoxia signaling can instead sensitize cells to GPX4 inhibition. (d) Immune coupling is a downstream amplification that, in the model, also differs between the two: SDT generates 104× more immune kills than RSL3 (521 vs 5, medium confidence, Section 9.2; this ratio falls to ~4:1 when re-run in 3D, Section 8.4, and is challenged by inhibitory-DAMP-balance evidence, Section 7.2), amplified 2.8× by anti-PD-1 (to 1,477).

3. *Tissue-specific drug penetration creates a substantial in-vitro-to-in-vivo translation gap.* Even at the blood vessel wall, tissue-specific vascular permeability reduces model-predicted RSL3-like drug kill from 40% (2D culture) to 12.1% (well-vascularized epithelial) to 2.6% (poorly-vascularized pancreatic) to 1.8% (CNS/BBB). The gap is even larger once the penetration gradient into the tissue is considered.

**What remains conditional.** Within the broader frame, the simulation neither validates nor disproves the hypothesis that physical ROS-generating modalities may be useful for locally targetable, ferroptosis-prone residual states; instead it bounds the question, identifying specific conditions under which the model predicts it could hold (hypoxic cores, deep tissue, limited drug penetration) and specific conditions under which it would not (hematologic malignancies, tumors without ferroptosis-prone states, settings where the ICD-to-immunity chain has historically underperformed). PDT is the established precedent, SDT is the depth-extended variant, and all are constrained by nanosensitizer dependence, uncertain in vivo ferroptosis durability, and the historical failure rate of preclinical immune logic in trials.

**The deeper lesson is methodological.** Gap claims can be artifacts. Mechanism counts are not maturity. Patient-study signal is not phase evidence. A compelling literature bridge is still only the start of a research program. But the simulation work adds something that literature analysis alone cannot: it identifies which biological conditions would need to be true for the hypothesis to work, and which would falsify it. The RSL3+FSP1i synergy, the hypoxia differential, and the tissue penetration gap are all testable in the lab.

**Openness and reproducibility.** All code, data, and analysis pipelines are openly available under the MIT license. The ferroptosis-core simulation engine is published as a standalone Rust library with Python bindings, so any researcher can reproduce, challenge, or extend the computational work. We believe this kind of radical openness — where the tools are as available as the conclusions — is essential for research that aims to help people rather than to be right about one hypothesis.

The right next step is therefore not to defend one favored modality, but to run the comparative experiments and cross-lane evaluations that could falsify it quickly.

### 10.2 Where the Field Is Going

The cancer research landscape in 2026 is defined by several structural trends that provide context for where this work sits and where it might become irrelevant.

**Immunotherapy remains the dominant paradigm, but resistance is the dominant problem.** Checkpoint inhibitors have transformed outcomes for some cancers — melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma — and their use continues to expand. But the core challenge has shifted from "does immunotherapy work?" to "why doesn't it work for most patients?" Primary resistance (tumors that never respond), acquired resistance (tumors that respond and then escape), and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment are now the central obstacles. The same TME features modeled in Chapter 7 — hypoxia, stromal shielding, acidic pH — are recognized as barriers to immunotherapy effectiveness, not just to ferroptosis induction.

**Engineered cell therapies face the solid-tumor wall.** CAR-T cells have been transformative in B-cell leukemias and lymphomas, but extending their success to solid tumors remains the field's greatest challenge. The tumor microenvironment suppresses, exhausts, and excludes T cells attempting to infiltrate solid tumors. Every advance in CAR-T for solid tumors is, at root, an advance in overcoming TME suppression — the same biological landscape this project models from a different angle.

**Radioligand therapy is a clinically mature example of spatially selective killing, well ahead of any physical-ROS hypothesis.** Lutetium-177 PSMA-617 (Pluvicto) is FDA-approved for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. Lutetium-177 dotatate (Lutathera) is approved for gastroenteropancreatic neuroendocrine tumors. The theranostic pipeline, using imaging to identify targetable tumors and then treating with the same targeting molecule carrying a radioactive payload, is far more clinically mature than anything in the ferroptosis-SDT space. For tumors with targetable receptors, radioligand therapy may already deliver what the physical-ROS hypothesis only proposes: spatially selective killing with systemic delivery. This is a reminder that the conditional hypothesis tested here sits within a landscape where other spatially selective approaches are already in the clinic.

**AI and computational biology are accelerating hypothesis generation.** Machine learning is transforming drug target identification, compound screening, and biomarker discovery. This project is one instance of a broader trend: using computational tools to map research landscapes and identify gaps that individual researchers miss. The specific tools (Monte Carlo biochemical simulation, cross-literature corpus analysis) are distinctive, but the underlying approach — computational hypothesis generation followed by experimental validation — is becoming standard practice. For recent clinical reporting on focused ultrasound applications advancing in adjacent fields, see [^news8].

**mRNA cancer vaccines are advancing toward personalized treatment.** Individualized neoantigen vaccines that train the immune system to recognize mutations specific to each patient's tumor are in late-stage clinical trials. This approach addresses the immune-recognition problem directly rather than relying on immunogenic cell death to trigger an immune response. If personalized mRNA vaccines prove effective at eliminating residual disease, the ICD component of the physical-ROS hypothesis becomes less important — though the direct cytotoxic advantage of physical modalities in hypoxic tumors would remain.

### 10.3 Where This Work Fits — and Where It Doesn't

This project contributes a computational framework for comparing therapeutic approaches across the boundaries that normally separate research communities. Its value lies in the questions it raises and the comparisons it surfaces, not in clinical claims.

**Where it fits.** The hypothesis generation is most relevant for locally targetable, deep-seated solid tumors with evidence of ferroptosis-prone residual states — specifically, tumors where pharmacologic drug delivery faces TME barriers (hypoxia, stromal shielding, acidic pH) that the simulation predicts physical modalities can bypass. Pancreatic, hepatic, and pelvic tumors in immunocompetent patients during a post-treatment vulnerability window are the narrowest and most defensible application context.

**Where it does not fit.** Hematologic malignancies (no spatial targeting needed). Diffuse metastatic disease (no localizable target for focused ultrasound or light). Tumors without ferroptosis-prone residual states (no vulnerability to exploit). Immunocompromised patients (ICD-to-immunity chain cannot operate). Settings where the vulnerability window has closed (persister cells have already restored defenses). This is not a general theory of cancer treatment. It is a conditional prediction about a specific clinical context.

**The honest framing.** This work identifies questions worth asking, not answers worth implementing. Every simulation finding is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated clinical advantage. The experimental program in Chapter 9 is designed to determine which — if any — of these hypotheses survive contact with real biology. If none do, the contribution is the computational framework and the cross-literature methodology, which remain useful for other hypotheses and other research communities.

### 10.4 Competing Hypotheses Worth Taking Seriously

Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that the ferroptosis-persister-physical-ROS hypothesis could be rendered irrelevant by developments in adjacent fields, independent of whether its specific predictions hold.

**The ferroptosis-persister connection might be a niche phenomenon.** The evidence that persister cells are ferroptosis-sensitive comes from specific model systems (primarily EGFR-mutant lung cancer persisters). Whether this sensitivity generalizes across cancer types, treatment histories, and genetic backgrounds is unknown. If it doesn't generalize, the hypothesis applies to a narrow population rather than the broad residual-disease problem described in Chapter 1.

**Radioligand therapy might render the spatial-selectivity argument unnecessary.** For tumors with targetable surface receptors, radioligand delivers spatially selective killing through systemic injection — no focused ultrasound or light source required. If the receptor-targeting approach expands to cover more tumor types, the physical-modality advantage narrows to the subset of tumors without targetable receptors.

**Immune checkpoint combinations might solve residual disease independently.** If next-generation checkpoint combinations (anti-PD-1 + anti-CTLA-4 + anti-LAG-3, or checkpoint plus costimulatory agonists) prove effective at eliminating minimal residual disease, the ferroptosis-ICD pathway becomes one of many ways to achieve immune clearance rather than a uniquely valuable approach.

**The nanoparticle engineering confound might mean there is no SDT-as-modality advantage.** As Chapter 8.3 noted, most SDT-ferroptosis data comes from engineered nanoparticles designed to deplete GSH and release iron. If plain SDT with conventional sonosensitizers does not trigger ferroptosis, the entire hypothesis is about nanoparticle design, not about ultrasound as a therapeutic modality.

For recent research on immune-pathway targets that could compete with or complement ferroptosis approaches, see [^news9].

**Ferroptosis itself may prove to be a secondary phenomenon in persister-cell biology rather than a primary vulnerability.** The current evidence that persisters are ferroptosis-sensitive comes from specific model systems. If broader studies reveal that ferroptosis sensitivity is a transient artifact of the experimental conditions used to generate persisters rather than an intrinsic feature of the persister state, the entire project's biological foundation would need revision.

The field may move past the ferroptosis-persister hypothesis entirely. If it does, this project's lasting contribution would be the computational methodology — the ferroptosis-core simulation engine, the cross-literature analysis pipeline, and the framework for comparing therapeutic approaches on shared axes. Those tools are useful regardless of whether the specific hypothesis they were built to test turns out to be correct.

## Chapter 11: How To Contribute

This project is built to be extended, challenged, and improved. Everything — the code, the data, the analysis pipeline, and this manuscript — is publicly available under the MIT license. This chapter describes specific ways to engage, organized by background.

### 11.1 For Researchers

The ferroptosis-core simulation engine is a standalone Rust library designed to be used independently of this manuscript. It models the ferroptosis cascade (ROS generation, GSH depletion, GPX4/FSP1 repair, lipid peroxidation, and cell death) with configurable parameters and stochastic variation.

The simplest way to engage is to challenge the predictions. Every simulation finding in Chapters 6 and 7 depends on specific parameter values documented in the parameter provenance file (see `simulations/calibration/parameter_provenance.md`); the calibration tier of every layer, including the 3D realism layers whose parameters that file does not yet enumerate, is summarized in `simulations/calibration/CALIBRATION_STATUS.md`. Changing a parameter and rerunning the simulation tests whether the directional result survives. If it doesn't, you've identified a load-bearing assumption — which is exactly what the experimental program in Chapter 9 aims to do with wet-lab experiments. The Python bindings (`pip install ferroptosis-core`) make this accessible without learning Rust: import the library, call `sim_cell` or `sim_batch` with modified parameters, and compare against the published results.

If you have experimental data that could calibrate model parameters — measured GPX4 recovery kinetics after drug withdrawal, quantified CAF supply rates of GSH or MUFA, or RSL3 intracellular concentration at different pH values — those measurements would directly improve the model's parameter estimates and change the confidence tiers in Chapter 9. The project welcomes data contributions as GitHub issues linking to published or preprint data.

If you find articles missing from the local corpus that would materially change the analysis — particularly landmark papers that the corpus-gaps document (`analysis/landmark-corpus-gaps.md`) already identifies — submitting a pull request or issue with the PMID helps close known coverage gaps.

### 11.2 For Engineers and Developers

The simulation architecture is modular by design. The ferroptosis-core library exposes more than twenty modules; the README (`simulations/ferroptosis-core/README.md`) is the authoritative, current list. The original core ten are `cell` (types and phenotypes), `params` (rate constants), `biochem` (core engine), `stats` (confidence intervals and parallel Monte Carlo), `physics` (depth-dependent energy), `grid` (2D/3D tumor architecture), `immune` (ICD/DAMP cascade), `io` (JSON/CSV output), `drug_transport` (Krogh cylinder penetration), and `tumor_pk` (two-compartment pharmacokinetics). A second wave added spatial/TME modules (`oxygen`, `ph`, `stromal`, `immune_spatial`, `photosensitizer_pk`, `dose_schedule`) and the 3D "realism layers" (`vasculature`, `spheroid`, `slab`, `clonal`, `persister`, `contact`) discussed in Section 8.4 — most of which are uncalibrated; see `simulations/calibration/CALIBRATION_STATUS.md`. Each module can be extended independently.

**Adding new TME features.** The sim-tme binary demonstrates how to compose ferroptosis-core with spatial features (O2 gradients, pH fields, stromal layers, immune coupling). Adding a new feature follows the same pattern: define the spatial field, modify cell parameters per-step based on the cell's position, and run the existing biochemistry engine. The `sim-tme-3d` binary extends this pattern to three dimensions and is where the realism layers (Section 8.4) compose. The adaptive-evolution direction once flagged here as the most impactful extension now exists in prototype form (the `clonal` and `persister` modules), but it is uncalibrated — turning those layers from mechanistic scaffolding into calibrated, predictive components is the higher-impact work now (see the calibration roadmap in `simulations/calibration/CALIBRATION_STATUS.md`).

**Python bindings.** The ferroptosis-python package (see `simulations/ferroptosis-python/README.md`) wraps the Rust engine via PyO3. Install with `pip install ferroptosis-core` (no Rust toolchain required). The API exposes `default_params()`, `invivo_params()`, `sim_cell()`, and `sim_batch()` with full parameter override support. Performance is native Rust speed with the GIL released during computation.

**C FFI for PhysiCell integration.** The ferroptosis-ffi crate (see `simulations/ferroptosis-ffi/`) provides a C-compatible API for embedding the ferroptosis engine in agent-based modeling platforms like PhysiCell. This enables multi-scale simulations combining ferroptosis biochemistry with cell migration, proliferation, and tissue-level dynamics that the current model does not capture.

### 11.3 For Students

This project is designed to be a starting point, not a finished product. Several of the limitations identified in Chapter 8.4 translate directly into thesis-level research projects.

**Computational biology thesis ideas:**

*Calibrate the vulnerability window model against experimental data (extends Chapter 6.2).* The current model uses literature-estimated recovery half-lives for GPX4, FSP1, NRF2, and GSH. A project that measures these recovery kinetics in a specific persister cell model and re-parameterizes the simulation would convert a medium-confidence prediction into a high-confidence one — or reveal that the model's assumptions are wrong. Skills needed: cell culture, Western blot/qPCR for protein quantification, basic Rust or Python for model modification.

*Build the systemic immune model that Chapter 8.4 identifies as missing (extends Chapter 7.2).* The current immune layer models resident T cell activation over 0-48 hours. A systemic model incorporating circulating immune cell recruitment, regulatory T cell suppression, and adaptive memory formation would resolve the "amplification vs dampening" uncertainty identified in Chapter 8.4. Skills needed: computational modeling, immunology background, Rust programming.

*Add adaptive evolution to the simulation (addresses Chapter 8.4's structural limitation).* The current model simulates one treatment cycle. A population-dynamics extension that models mutation, clonal selection, and resistance evolution across multiple cycles would answer the durability question the current model cannot address. Skills needed: evolutionary dynamics, agent-based modeling, Rust or Python.

### 11.4 For Clinicians

This project does not make treatment recommendations. Nothing in this book should be interpreted as clinical guidance. The simulation predictions are hypothesis-generating tools, not validated clinical evidence.

What clinicians can watch for: any clinical trial that tests ferroptosis-inducing agents in hypoxic solid tumors would provide real-world evidence for or against the predictions in Chapter 7. Any trial combining SDT with immunotherapy would test the immune-coupling prediction from Section 7.2. Any trial measuring ferroptosis markers as secondary endpoints after physical-modality treatment would provide calibration data for the model.

How to interpret the simulation predictions: directional findings (such as the differential effect of hypoxia on pharmacologic versus physical modalities) are more robust to parameter variation than specific quantitative values (the exact percentages, ratios, and synergy scores), which should be treated as order-of-magnitude estimates, not precise forecasts. The confidence tiers in Chapter 9 provide a guide to which predictions rest on better-characterized parameters.

The contribution clinicians can make is judgment. Computational biologists can identify patterns in data; clinicians can judge whether those patterns matter in the context of real patient care, real treatment logistics, and real clinical endpoints. If the experimental program in Chapter 9 produces results, clinician input on study design for the in-vivo integration experiment (Section 9.5) would be valuable — particularly on endpoint selection, comparator arm design, and the clinical context most likely to test the hypothesis fairly.

### A.1 Source of Truth

This appendix documents the core-engine simulation parameters as of ferroptosis-core version 0.66.0 (the 3D realism layers added since are inventoried, with their calibration status, in `simulations/calibration/CALIBRATION_STATUS.md`). Two authorities exist for parameter information: the Rust source code (`simulations/ferroptosis-core/src/params.rs`) is authoritative for DEFAULT VALUES — if the code and this appendix disagree on a number, the code is correct. This appendix is authoritative for BIOLOGICAL MEANING, LITERATURE SOURCES, and CONFIDENCE ASSESSMENTS — these are documentation that the code comments may abbreviate or omit. The full parameter provenance table with literature citations is maintained at `simulations/calibration/parameter_provenance.md`.

Each parameter is classified as **Grounded** (derived from published experimental measurements), **Estimated** (informed by literature but not directly calibrated against the specific biological system modeled), or **Assumed** (mechanistic placeholder without direct experimental support). Sensitivity is rated as Critical (the qualitative result changes), High (quantitative outcomes shift substantially), Moderate (measurable but non-critical impact), or Low (minimal impact on results).

### A.2 Core Biochemistry Parameters

**fenton_rate** (default: 0.02). The rate at which labile iron catalyzes the Fenton reaction, converting hydrogen peroxide to hydroxyl radicals that initiate lipid peroxidation. This is the cell's baseline ROS source. Estimated from published literature.[^kakhlon2002]

[^kakhlon2002]: Kakhlon O & Cabantchik ZI. The labile iron pool. Free Radical Biology and Medicine (2002). At 2x, basal ROS increases and all phenotypes become more ferroptosis-sensitive; at 0.5x, the control death rate drops further below the 2% calibration ceiling.

**gsh_scav_efficiency** (default: 0.5). The fraction of ROS neutralized by glutathione per interaction event. Modeled with Michaelis-Menten kinetics (see gsh_km). Assumed value based on empirical fit. At 2x, GSH becomes a stronger shield and RSL3 kill rates drop; at 0.5x, even non-persister cells become more vulnerable.

**gsh_km** (default: 2.0 mM). The Michaelis-Menten saturation constant for GSH-dependent ROS scavenging — the GSH concentration at which scavenging reaches half its maximum rate. Assumed. Low sensitivity; the qualitative results are robust to this value.

**nrf2_gsh_rate** (default: 0.025). The rate at which the NRF2 transcription factor drives GSH resynthesis. NRF2 is the master antioxidant regulator that persister+NRF2 cells upregulate. Estimated from published literature.[^dodson2019] Moderate sensitivity; higher values make NRF2-compensated cells more resistant to RSL3.

**lp_rate** (default: 0.06). The rate at which unscavenged ROS generates lipid peroxides in the cell membrane. This is the initiation rate of the lipid damage cascade. Estimated from published literature.[^yang2016]

[^yang2016]: Yang WS et al. Peroxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids by lipoxygenases drives ferroptosis. Cell (2016). High sensitivity; directly controls how fast the damage accumulates.

**lp_propagation** (default: 0.10). The autocatalytic propagation rate — the rate at which each lipid peroxide generates new radicals that damage adjacent lipids. This is the parameter that creates the bistable switch: above a threshold, damage becomes self-sustaining. Estimated from published literature.[^porter2005] **Critical** sensitivity; this is the gate for the all-or-nothing death behavior.

**gpx4_rate** (default: 0.30). The catalytic efficiency of GPX4 in converting lipid peroxides to harmless lipid alcohols. GPX4 is the cell's primary defense against membrane lipid damage. Estimated from published literature.[^ursini1995]

[^ursini1995]: Ursini F et al. Phospholipid hydroperoxide glutathione peroxidase. Free Radical Biology and Medicine (1995). Moderate sensitivity; higher values make cells more resistant to ferroptosis.

**fsp1_rate** (default: 0.08). The catalytic efficiency of the FSP1/CoQ10 backup pathway per unit of FSP1 activity. This rate constant is the same for all phenotypes; what changes is the cell's FSP1 activity level. Persister cells have a mean FSP1 activity of 0.15 (versus 1.0 for glycolytic cells), so their effective FSP1 repair is 0.15 × 0.08 = 0.012 — roughly 7× weaker than non-persister cells. Estimated from published literature.[^bersuker_mao]

[^bersuker_mao]: Bersuker K et al., Nature (2019); Mao C et al., Nature (2021). **Critical** sensitivity; this parameter determines the persister vulnerability that the entire hypothesis rests on.

**sdt_ros / pdt_ros** (default: 5.0 each). The exogenous ROS dose delivered by SDT and PDT respectively. Set equal for controlled comparison; the modalities differ in tissue penetration, not in ROS generation at the cellular level. Estimated for ~1 MHz ultrasound. **Critical** sensitivity; this value relative to basal ROS (~0.3 for persister cells) determines whether physical modalities overwhelm cellular defenses.

**rsl3_gpx4_inhib** (default: 0.92). The fraction of GPX4 activity inhibited by RSL3. At 92% inhibition, GPX4 is nearly eliminated, leaving only FSP1 for repair. Estimated from pharmacokinetic IC50 data. High sensitivity; lower values reduce RSL3's selectivity for persisters.

**death_threshold** (default: 10.0). The lipid peroxidation level at which the cell is considered dead — a model-defined parameter, not a measured biological constant. Assumed; set by calibration to produce biologically plausible death rates. **Critical** sensitivity; this threshold determines the LP level required for the bistable switch to trigger irreversible death.

### A.3 Spatial and Physics Parameters

**pdt_mu_eff** (default: 0.31 /mm). The effective attenuation coefficient for red light (630 nm) in tissue, governing PDT's depth-dependent intensity via the Beer-Lambert law. **Grounded** from Jacques (Phys Med Biol, 2013). Yields a penetration depth of approximately 3.2 mm. **Critical** sensitivity; this parameter directly determines where PDT transitions from effective to ineffective.

**sdt_alpha** (default: 0.7 dB/cm/MHz). Acoustic attenuation coefficient in soft tissue at 1 MHz. **Grounded** from Cobbold (Foundations of Biomedical Ultrasound, 2007). High sensitivity; determines SDT's depth advantage over PDT.

**cell_size_um** (default: 20.0). Tumor cell diameter determining grid resolution. **Grounded** from typical tumor cell measurements. Low sensitivity.

**iron_release_per_death** (default: 2.0 uM). Iron released per dead cell into the Moore neighborhood, driving bystander ferroptosis via Fenton chemistry. Assumed. Low sensitivity; the bystander effect is a secondary mechanism.

### A.4 Immune Cascade Parameters

**damp_per_lp** (default: 1.0). DAMP signal released per unit of lipid peroxidation at cell death. Because SDT-killed cells have higher LP at death (~20 vs ~7.8 for RSL3), they release proportionally more DAMPs. Estimated from published literature.[^krysko2012]

[^krysko2012]: Krysko DV et al. Immunogenic cell death and DAMPs in cancer therapy. Nature Reviews Cancer (2012). Moderate sensitivity.

**dc_activation_kd** (default: 50.0). The half-maximal DAMP concentration for dendritic cell activation — the DAMP level at which DC activation reaches 50% of its maximum. Assumed; no direct measurement exists. Moderate sensitivity; determines the DAMP density threshold for immune engagement.

**pd1_brake** (default: 0.7). The fraction of T cell killing suppressed by PD-1 checkpoint signaling. At 70% suppression, only 30% of potential immune kills occur without anti-PD-1 treatment. Estimated from clinical response rates. Moderate sensitivity.

**anti_pd1_efficacy** (default: 0.8). The fraction of PD-1 brake removed by anti-PD-1 treatment. At 80% removal, the brake drops from 70% to 14% suppression. Estimated. Moderate sensitivity; determines the amplification factor for immunotherapy combination.

### A.5 Recovery Rate Parameters

**fsp1_half_recovery_days** (default: 7.0). The half-life for FSP1 re-expression after drug withdrawal, governing how quickly persisters restore their backup defense. Estimated from epigenetic recovery kinetics. High sensitivity; this is the rate-limiting step for vulnerability window closure against exogenous ROS.

**gpx4_half_recovery_days** (default: 3.0). GPX4 protein re-expression half-life. Faster than FSP1 because GPX4 recovery involves transcriptional upregulation rather than epigenetic remodeling. Estimated. High sensitivity; determines how quickly RSL3 sensitivity is lost.

**nrf2_half_recovery_days** (default: 5.0). NRF2 master-regulator recovery half-life. Estimated. Moderate sensitivity; NRF2 drives both GSH resynthesis and GPX4 upregulation.

**gsh_half_recovery_days** (default: 1.0). GSH pool recovery half-life — the fastest-recovering defense pathway because GSH synthesis is enzymatic and does not require new protein expression. Estimated. Moderate sensitivity.

## Appendix B: Reproduction Guide

### B.1 Prerequisites

- **Git** and **Git LFS** (the `books/` directory uses LFS for large reference files)
- **Python 3.10+** with pip
- **Rust 1.75+** (install via rustup.rs)
- **LaTeX**: pdflatex and standard packages (texlive-latex-extra on Linux; MacTeX on macOS)

### B.2 Clone and Install

```
git clone https://github.com/ELares/cancer_research.git
cd cancer_research
pip install -r requirements.txt
```

The corpus (243 MB) is included in the repository. Git LFS must be installed for the `books/` directory, but the corpus itself uses standard Git.

If `pip install` fails, check the Python version (`python3 --version`). The scripts require Python 3.10+ for type-hint syntax.

### B.3 Build Simulations

```
cd simulations
cargo build --release
```

This compiles all simulation binaries (sim-original, sim-spatial, sim-tme, sim-combo-mech, sim-window, sim-tissue-pk, sim-invivo, sim-combo, sim-icd, plus the 3D capstone sim-tme-3d and sim-tumor-pk) and the ferroptosis-core library. Expected build time: 1-3 minutes depending on hardware.

If the Rust toolchain is not installed: `curl --proto '=https' --tlsv1.2 -sSf https://sh.rustup.rs | sh` and follow the prompts.

### B.4 Run Simulations

Each binary produces JSON or CSV output in its directory:

```
cd simulations
./target/release/sim-original          # Baseline Monte Carlo (1M cells, ~30 seconds)
./target/release/sim-spatial           # Spatial tumor grid (500x500, ~2 minutes)
./target/release/sim-tme               # TME features (O2, immune, stromal, pH, ~5 minutes)
./target/release/sim-combo-mech        # Drug combinations (1K cells, ~5 seconds)
```

Output files are written to `simulations/` (simulation_results.json, spatial output, etc.). Compare against the committed output files to verify reproduction.

### B.5 Run Analysis Pipeline

```
cd scripts
python3 enrich_metadata.py             # Enrich corpus with mechanism/cancer-type tags
python3 build_index.py                 # Rebuild INDEX.jsonl
python3 generate_figures.py            # Generate all publication figures
```

The enrichment scripts require the full corpus in `corpus/by-pmid/`. If running for the first time, the scripts may take 10-30 minutes depending on network speed (some enrichment steps query PubMed/OpenAlex APIs).

### B.6 Compile Manuscript

```
python3 scripts/generate_latex.py
cd article/drafts
pdflatex v1 && pdflatex v1
```

The first command converts `v1.md` to `v1.tex`. Two pdflatex passes resolve cross-references and the table of contents (citations are inline footnotes, so no bibtex pass is needed). Expected output: `v1.pdf` at the current page count.

### B.7 Run Tests

```
python3 -m pytest tests/ -q
```

Expected: 116 passed. The smoke tests verify that the analysis pipeline, tagging functions, and configuration are internally consistent.

### B.8 Install Python Bindings (Optional)

```
pip install ferroptosis-core
```

This installs the Python bindings for the ferroptosis-core Rust library. No Rust toolchain is required — the package includes precompiled wheels for common platforms. Use `sim_cell()` and `sim_batch()` to run the ferroptosis simulation from Python.

## Appendix C: Glossary

Terms are defined in plain language for non-specialist readers. The chapter reference indicates where the term is first substantively used or explained.

**ACSL4 (Acyl-CoA Synthetase Long-Chain Family Member 4)** — An enzyme that incorporates polyunsaturated fatty acids into cell membrane phospholipids, making membranes more susceptible to ferroptotic lipid peroxidation. The more PUFA-rich a membrane, the more fuel for the lipid chain reaction. (Chapter 5)

**Angiogenesis** — The growth of new blood vessels. Tumors stimulate angiogenesis to supply themselves with oxygen and nutrients. The resulting vasculature is often disorganized, contributing to hypoxia in the tumor interior. (Chapter 7)

**Anti-PD-1 / Anti-PD-L1** — Checkpoint inhibitor drugs that block the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction, releasing the immune system's brakes on T cell-mediated cancer cell killing. Examples include pembrolizumab and nivolumab. (Chapter 1)

**Antibody-drug conjugate (ADC)** — A targeted therapy that combines an antibody (which finds cancer cells) with a toxic drug payload (which kills them). The antibody guides the drug to the tumor, reducing damage to healthy tissue. (Chapter 1)

**Autocatalytic propagation** — A self-amplifying process where the products of a reaction accelerate the same reaction. In ferroptosis, each damaged lipid molecule creates radicals that damage adjacent lipids, producing an accelerating chain reaction. (Chapter 5)

**Bistable switch** — A system with two stable states and no stable intermediate. In the ferroptosis model, cells either recover (repair outpaces damage) or collapse irreversibly (damage outpaces repair). There is no stable "partially damaged" state. (Chapter 5)

**Bliss independence** — A statistical model for predicting how two drugs perform together if they act independently. If the actual combination kills more than the Bliss prediction, the drugs are synergistic. Quantified as a synergy score where values above 1.0 indicate synergy. (Chapter 6)

**CAF (Cancer-Associated Fibroblast)** — A type of stromal cell found at the tumor boundary that supplies neighboring tumor cells with protective metabolites including cysteine (for GSH synthesis) and oleic acid (for membrane remodeling). In the model, CAFs provide metabolic shielding that selectively protects against pharmacologic but not physical ferroptosis inducers. (Chapter 7)

**CAR-T (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T cell)** — An engineered immune cell therapy where a patient's T cells are modified to express a receptor targeting a specific cancer surface protein. Transformative in blood cancers; struggling in solid tumors due to the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. (Chapter 1)

**Checkpoint inhibitor** — A drug that blocks molecular signals tumors use to evade immune recognition (e.g., PD-1/PD-L1, CTLA-4). By releasing these "brakes" on the immune response, checkpoint inhibitors can enable the immune system to attack cancer cells. (Chapter 1)

**Chou-Talalay method** — A mathematical framework for quantifying drug combination interactions. Produces a combination index (CI) where CI < 1 indicates synergy, CI = 1 indicates additivity, and CI > 1 indicates antagonism. (Chapter 6)

**Collateral sensitivity** — The phenomenon where acquiring resistance to one drug creates increased sensitivity to a different drug. Arises from evolutionary trade-offs: the adaptations needed for resistance create new dependencies. (Chapter 1)

**Combination index (CI)** — A quantitative measure of drug interaction from the Chou-Talalay method. CI < 1 means the drugs are synergistic (better together than expected); CI > 1 means antagonistic (worse together). (Chapter 6)

**Corpus** — The collection of research articles analyzed in this project. The full-text corpus contains 4,830 PubMed-indexed articles; a separate abstract-only archive contains 5,585 additional records. (Chapter 2)

**DAMP (Damage-Associated Molecular Pattern)** — Molecules released by dying cells that alert the immune system. Key DAMPs in ferroptosis include HMGB1, calreticulin, and ATP. The amount and spatial pattern of DAMP release determines the strength of immune activation. (Chapter 7)

**Death threshold** — The lipid peroxidation level (default: 10.0) at which the model considers a cell dead. A model-defined parameter, not a measured biological constant. Below this threshold, cells can in principle recover; above it, membrane damage is irreversible. (Chapter 5)

**Designed combination** — A multi-mechanism article that describes an intentionally designed combination study, as opposed to a review or incidental co-mention. Only 19.7% of multi-mechanism articles in the corpus represent actual designed combinations. (Chapter 3)

**Drug-tolerant persister cell** — A cancer cell that survives initial drug treatment by entering an altered biological state. Persisters are not genetically resistant; they adopt a different metabolic and transcriptional profile that makes them temporarily invulnerable to the drug that killed their neighbors. (Chapter 1)

**Evidence tier** — A classification of a research article's level of clinical evidence, ranging from Phase III clinical trial (highest maturity) through Phase II, Phase I, other clinical studies, preclinical in vivo, preclinical in vitro, to theoretical/computational (lowest maturity). (Chapter 4)

**Fenton reaction** — A chemical reaction where iron catalyzes the conversion of hydrogen peroxide to highly reactive hydroxyl radicals. In ferroptosis, the Fenton reaction provides a key source of reactive oxygen species that initiate lipid damage. (Chapter 5)

**Ferroptosis** — A type of regulated cell death caused by iron-catalyzed, uncontrolled oxidation of polyunsaturated fatty acids in cell membranes. Distinguished from apoptosis by its dependence on iron and lipid peroxidation rather than caspase activation. (Chapter 2)

**FSP1 (Ferroptosis Suppressor Protein 1)** — A GPX4-independent enzyme that suppresses ferroptosis by reducing ubiquinone (CoQ10) to ubiquinol, which then traps lipid radicals. Acts as a backup repair pathway independent of glutathione. Downregulated in persister cells. (Chapter 5)

**GPX4 (Glutathione Peroxidase 4)** — The primary enzyme that protects cell membranes from ferroptosis by converting toxic lipid hydroperoxides to harmless lipid alcohols. Requires glutathione (GSH) as a cofactor. RSL3 works by covalently inactivating GPX4. (Chapter 5)

**GSH (Glutathione)** — The cell's primary antioxidant buffer molecule. Serves dual roles: directly scavenging reactive oxygen species and providing the essential cofactor that GPX4 requires for lipid peroxide repair. Depleted under oxidative stress. (Chapter 5)

**Henderson-Hasselbalch equation** — A chemistry equation relating pH to the ionization state of weak acids and bases. In the ferroptosis context, predicts how acidic tumor pH protonates and traps drug molecules outside cells, reducing their intracellular availability. (Chapter 7)

**ICD (Immunogenic Cell Death)** — A form of cell death that releases molecular signals (DAMPs) activating the immune system against remaining tumor cells. Not all cell death is immunogenic; the manner of death determines whether immune activation occurs. (Chapter 7)

**Krogh cylinder** — A mathematical model for oxygen and drug diffusion from a blood vessel into surrounding tissue. Predicts how concentration decreases exponentially with distance from the vessel. Used in the simulation to model drug penetration depth. (Chapter 6)

**Lipid peroxidation (LP)** — Oxidative damage to polyunsaturated fatty acids in cell membranes. The central damage process in ferroptosis. Autocatalytic: each damaged lipid generates radicals that damage adjacent lipids, creating a chain reaction. (Chapter 5)

**Michaelis-Menten kinetics** — A mathematical model describing how enzyme reaction rates depend on substrate concentration. In the simulation, governs how GSH-dependent ROS scavenging saturates at high GSH levels — providing diminishing returns as GSH increases. (Chapter 5)

**Monte Carlo simulation** — A computational method that runs a model thousands or millions of times with random variation to understand the range of possible outcomes. Named after the casino in Monaco. Produces distributions rather than single answers, revealing which results are robust and which are fragile. (Chapter 2)

**MUFA (Monounsaturated Fatty Acid)** — A type of membrane lipid with only one double bond, making it resistant to the radical chain reaction that drives ferroptosis. SCD1-mediated MUFA enrichment displaces PUFAs from membranes, reducing ferroptosis sensitivity. (Chapter 5)

**NRF2** — A master transcription factor that activates antioxidant defense genes. When activated, NRF2 drives GSH resynthesis and GPX4 upregulation, counteracting ferroptosis. The Persister+NRF2 phenotype in the simulation represents cells with constitutive NRF2 activation. (Chapter 5)

**OXPHOS (Oxidative Phosphorylation)** — The mitochondrial process that generates most of a cell's energy (ATP) using oxygen as the terminal electron acceptor. OXPHOS-dependent cells generate more basal ROS than glycolytic cells, making them more susceptible to ferroptosis under certain conditions. (Chapter 5)

**PDT (Photodynamic Therapy)** — A treatment that uses light to activate a photosensitizing compound, which then generates reactive oxygen species that kill nearby cells. Limited to superficial tumors (millimeters of penetration) because light is absorbed by tissue. (Chapter 6)

**Persister cell** — See drug-tolerant persister cell. In the simulation, the "Persister (FSP1 downregulated)" phenotype represents cells with reduced FSP1 backup defense, making GPX4 the sole repair pathway. (Chapter 5)

**PUFA (Polyunsaturated Fatty Acid)** — A type of membrane lipid with multiple double bonds that are highly susceptible to the radical chain reaction driving ferroptosis. The ratio of PUFAs to MUFAs in cell membranes determines ferroptosis vulnerability. (Chapter 5)

**Radioligand therapy** — A treatment that attaches radioactive atoms to molecules targeting specific cancer cell receptors, delivering radiation directly to tumors. Clinically established (FDA-approved for prostate and neuroendocrine cancers) with mature dosimetry. (Chapter 1)

**Residual disease** — Cancer cells that survive initial treatment. These cells are the source of most cancer relapses. May exist in altered biological states (see drug-tolerant persister cell) that differ from the original tumor. (Chapter 1)

**ROS (Reactive Oxygen Species)** — Chemically reactive molecules containing oxygen, including superoxide, hydrogen peroxide, and hydroxyl radicals. Generated as byproducts of mitochondrial respiration. In excess, ROS damage lipids, proteins, and DNA. The balance between ROS production and antioxidant defense determines ferroptosis sensitivity. (Chapter 5)

**RSL3** — A small-molecule ferroptosis inducer that works by covalently binding to and inactivating GPX4, the cell's primary lipid peroxide repair enzyme. Used in the simulation as the representative pharmacologic ferroptosis inducer. (Chapter 5)

**SDT (Sonodynamic Therapy)** — A treatment that uses ultrasound to activate a sonosensitizing compound, generating reactive oxygen species that kill nearby cells. Penetrates centimeters into tissue (compared to millimeters for light-based PDT), enabling treatment of deep-seated tumors. (Chapter 6)

**Sensitivity analysis** — A systematic test of how simulation results change when input parameters are varied. In this project, ±50% perturbation on 11 rate constants tested whether the key qualitative result (persister cells are more sensitive than glycolytic cells under SDT) holds. It held in 22 of 22 conditions. (Chapter 5)

**Sonosensitizer** — A compound activated by ultrasound to generate reactive oxygen species. Analogous to a photosensitizer (activated by light). Most SDT-ferroptosis research uses engineered nanosonosensitizers designed to additionally deplete GSH and release iron. (Chapter 8)

**Synthetic lethality** — A concept where two genetic or pharmacologic events that are individually survivable become lethal in combination. In the ferroptosis context, simultaneous inhibition of both GPX4 and FSP1 (individually tolerable) produces synergistic cell death. (Chapter 6)

**TME (Tumor Microenvironment)** — The complex ecosystem surrounding a tumor, including immune cells, blood vessels, fibroblasts, extracellular matrix, and signaling molecules. TME features such as hypoxia, acidity, and stromal shielding modify how cancer cells respond to treatment. (Chapter 7)

**Tumor heterogeneity** — The diversity of cell types, mutations, and metabolic states within a single tumor. Heterogeneity is the biological basis of drug resistance: treatment selects for the subpopulation best equipped to survive it. (Chapter 1)

**Vulnerability window** — The period after initial treatment during which residual cancer cells remain in an altered state that is sensitive to follow-up intervention. The window closes as defense pathways recover. In the simulation, the RSL3 window closes within 3 days while the SDT window remains open for at least 4 weeks. (Chapter 6)

**Apoptosis** — The most common form of programmed cell death, characterized by cell shrinkage, DNA fragmentation, and orderly dismantling. Unlike ferroptosis, apoptosis is caspase-dependent and does not involve lipid peroxidation or iron dependence. (Chapter 5)

**C11-BODIPY** — A fluorescent lipid peroxidation probe that shifts emission from red to green upon oxidation. The standard experimental tool for measuring ferroptotic lipid damage in living cells. (Chapter 6)

**Calreticulin** — A protein normally found inside cells that translocates to the cell surface during immunogenic cell death. Surface calreticulin acts as an "eat me" signal for immune cells. Detected by flow cytometry. (Chapter 7)

**CAP (Cold Atmospheric Plasma)** — A partially ionized gas at room temperature that generates reactive oxygen and nitrogen species. Proposed as a physical modality for localized cancer treatment but represented by only 3 articles in the local corpus. Speculative. (Chapter 3)

**Caspase** — A family of protease enzymes that execute apoptotic cell death. Ferroptosis is caspase-independent, distinguishing it mechanistically from apoptosis. (Chapter 5)

**Chemotherapy** — Cancer treatment using chemical compounds that kill rapidly dividing cells. Often produces initial tumor reduction but selects for resistant subpopulations, leading to eventual relapse. (Chapter 1)

**CoQ10 (Coenzyme Q10 / Ubiquinone)** — A molecule in cell membranes that FSP1 reduces to ubiquinol, which then acts as a radical-trapping antioxidant. The biochemical basis of the FSP1 backup defense pathway against ferroptosis. (Chapter 5)

**Cross-literature analysis** — A systematic comparison of research articles across multiple therapeutic mechanism categories. Reveals patterns invisible within individual fields because most researchers read within their own specialty. (Chapter 2)

**CRISPR** — A gene-editing technology that enables precise modification of DNA sequences. In cancer research, used both as a research tool (to identify gene dependencies) and as a potential therapeutic (to engineer immune cells). (Chapter 3)

**DHCR7 / 7-DHC axis** — A cholesterol biosynthesis pathway that, when disrupted, produces 7-dehydrocholesterol — a lipid with natural radical-trapping antioxidant properties that suppress ferroptosis. A potential escape mechanism for ferroptosis-targeted therapies. (Chapter 8)

**DHODH (Dihydroorotate Dehydrogenase)** — A mitochondrial enzyme involved in pyrimidine biosynthesis that also functions as a third ferroptosis defense pathway, independent of both GPX4 and FSP1. Inhibited by brequinar. (Chapter 8)

**Epigenetic** — Changes in gene expression that do not involve alterations to the DNA sequence itself. Drug-tolerant persister states often involve epigenetic reprogramming rather than genetic mutation, making them potentially reversible. (Chapter 1)

**Gold-set evaluation** — A validation method where human experts manually label a stratified sample of articles, then compare the automated tagger's output against these labels. In this project: 100 articles, 46% exact-label accuracy, 96% binary evidence-presence precision, 55% recall. (Chapter 4)

**HIFU (High-Intensity Focused Ultrasound)** — A physical modality that uses tightly focused ultrasound to thermally ablate tissue. Unlike SDT, HIFU kills by heating rather than by generating ROS through sonosensitizer activation. Only 3 ferroptosis-related articles in PubMed. (Chapter 3)

**HMGB1 (High Mobility Group Box 1)** — A nuclear protein released by dying cells as a damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP). Extracellular HMGB1 activates dendritic cells and promotes inflammatory immune responses. Detected by ELISA on culture supernatant. (Chapter 7)

**Hypoxia** — Low oxygen conditions. Tumor cores are typically hypoxic because oxygen diffuses only 100-150 micrometers from blood vessels. Hypoxia eliminates basal mitochondrial ROS production, protecting cells from pharmacologic ferroptosis inducers that depend on this ROS source. (Chapter 7)

**Immunotherapy** — Cancer treatment that harnesses or enhances the immune system's ability to recognize and destroy cancer cells. Includes checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cells, cancer vaccines, and cytokine therapies. (Chapter 1)

**Ion trapping** — A pharmacokinetic phenomenon where ionizable drugs become charged (protonated) in acidic environments and unable to cross cell membranes. In acidic tumor cores, drug molecules are trapped outside cells, reducing their intracellular concentration and efficacy. (Chapter 7)

**IRE (Irreversible Electroporation)** — A physical ablation technique using high-voltage electrical pulses to permanently disrupt cell membranes. Can be combined with ferroptosis via iron-based nanoparticles. Requires invasive needle placement. (Chapter 8)

**Labile iron pool** — The pool of loosely bound, catalytically active iron within cells. Drives the Fenton reaction that generates hydroxyl radicals. Increased by ferritin degradation at acidic pH. (Chapter 5)

**Metastasis** — The spread of cancer from its original site to distant organs. Metastatic disease is generally harder to treat than localized disease because the cancer is no longer confined to a single targetable location. (Chapter 1)

**mRNA cancer vaccine** — A therapeutic vaccine using messenger RNA encoding tumor-specific mutations (neoantigens) to train the immune system to recognize and attack cancer cells. Personalized to each patient's tumor. (Chapter 1)

**Nanosonosensitizer** — An engineered nanoparticle designed for sonodynamic therapy that typically incorporates a sonosensitizer plus additional components (e.g., GSH-depleting agents, iron-releasing agents) to enhance ferroptosis. Most SDT-ferroptosis literature uses these rather than conventional sonosensitizers. (Chapter 8)

**Photosensitizer** — A compound that absorbs light and transfers that energy to generate reactive oxygen species. The active component in photodynamic therapy (PDT). Examples include porphyrins and chlorins. (Chapter 6)

**Pimonidazole** — A chemical probe that forms covalent bonds with cellular molecules under hypoxic conditions (below approximately 1.3% O2). The standard immunohistochemical method for verifying tumor hypoxia. (Chapter 7)

**Precision** — In the context of the evidence tagger, the reported 96% is BINARY evidence-presence precision: of the records the tagger flags as carrying any phase or clinical evidence, the fraction that genuinely do. It is NOT per-tier precision (the fraction of records assigned to a specific maturity tier, for example Phase III, that truly belong to that tier), which is a separate, small-sample, and weaker quantity. So "96% binary evidence-presence precision" means the tagger rarely invents evidence where there is none; it does NOT mean the per-mechanism Phase III/II counts are 96% accurate (those carry catchable mechanism false-positives, see the per-tier caveat in `analysis/evidence-tiers.md`). (Chapter 4)

**PRRT (Peptide Receptor Radionuclide Therapy)** — A form of radioligand therapy targeting somatostatin receptors, primarily used for neuroendocrine tumors. Lutathera (lutetium-177 dotatate) is FDA-approved for this indication. (Chapter 3)

**Recall** — In the context of the evidence tagger: the fraction of articles that actually belong to a specific evidence tier that the tagger correctly identifies. At 55% evidence-level recall, the tagger misses nearly half of valid evidence. (This evidence-level figure is distinct from mechanism-level recall, measured separately at about 90% in Section 3.6.) (Chapter 4)

**SCD1 (Stearoyl-CoA Desaturase 1)** — An enzyme that converts saturated fatty acids to monounsaturated fatty acids (MUFAs). SCD1-mediated MUFA enrichment of cell membranes reduces ferroptosis susceptibility by displacing the PUFAs that fuel the lipid chain reaction. (Chapter 5)

**SLC7A11 / xCT / System Xc-** — The cystine-glutamate antiporter that imports cystine (used to synthesize GSH) into cells. A critical upstream regulator of ferroptosis defense — inhibiting System Xc- (e.g., by erastin) depletes intracellular GSH and sensitizes cells to ferroptosis. (Chapter 5)

**TTFields (Tumor-Treating Fields)** — A device-based cancer therapy using alternating electric fields to disrupt cell division. FDA-approved for glioblastoma and mesothelioma. No direct ferroptosis engagement (0 PubMed ferroptosis articles). (Chapter 3)

**Warburg effect** — The observation, first described by Otto Warburg in the 1920s, that cancer cells preferentially ferment glucose even when oxygen is available. The resulting lactic acid production acidifies the tumor microenvironment. (Chapter 1)

**Weighted evidence score** — A composite metric combining evidence tier (Phase III = 12 points, down to theoretical = 0.5), citation percentile (1.0-1.5x), and publication recency (0.9-1.1x) to rank mechanism maturity beyond raw article counts. (Chapter 4)
