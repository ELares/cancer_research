# Near-Infrared-Triggered Nanodroplets for Imaging-Guided Macrophage-Mediated Cancer Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Immunotherapy has transformed cancer treatment; however, solid tumors often evade checkpoint blockade by co-opting tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) and creating a hypoxic, immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Here, we report a laser-triggered theranostic nanodroplet capable of simultaneously remodeling the tumor microenvironment and visualizing therapeutic responses in real-time. These double-emulsion perfluorocarbon droplets encapsulate clinically translatable components: a toll-like receptor-7/8 agonist and near-infrared indocyanine green dye. Upon activation by a near-infrared laser pulse, the nanodroplets transform into echogenic microbubbles, facilitating imaging and targeted therapeutic release. In a mouse model of human triple-negative breast cancer, a single nanodroplet injection followed by periodic illumination repolarized M2-like TAMs into an M1 phenotype, alleviated intratumoral hypoxia, and synergistically anti-programmed-cell-death-protein-1 therapy, resulting in a six-fold reduction in tumor size compared to checkpoint blockade alone. Concurrent non-invasive dual-modal ultrasound and multispectral photoacoustic imaging tracked nanodroplet biodistribution, macrophage recruitment, and oxygen saturation longitudinally, enabling real-time guidance of dosing schedules without biopsy. The nanodroplets demonstrated greater than 60% loading efficiency, minimal hemolysis, and high biocompatibility in vitro. By coupling spatiotemporally controlled immunomodulation with quantitative imaging, this platform addresses critical challenges in treating refractory solid tumors and provides a roadmap for adaptive, image-guided combination immunotherapy.

Near‐Infrared‐Triggered Nanodroplets for Imaging‐Guided Macrophage‐Mediated Cancer Immunotherapy

Abstract

Schematic illustration of the Nanodroplets and their theranostics application–The engineered nanodroplets integrate a multifunctional design, encapsulating the immunostimulant resiquimod (R848), indocyanine green (ICG), and volatile perfluoropentane (PFP), within a biocompatible double‐emulsion structure. Upon near‐infrared laser activation, the nanodroplets undergo a rapid phase transition that simultaneously triggers localized drug release and enhances imaging contrast via microbubble formation. This enables spatiotemporally controlled macrophage reprogramming and real‐time ultrasound/photoacoustic tracking of therapeutic response, establishing nanodroplets as an innovative theranostic platform for precision cancer immunotherapy.  

Introduction

Nanomedicine has shown promising results in cancer therapy by reducing the off‐targeting side effects, stimuli response release, and potency to deliver combination therapy.[  ] Recently, immunotherapy by immune checkpoint blockers such as anti‐programmed cell death‐1 (PD‐1) has shown promising results for various cancers in clinics. PD‐1 inhibitors (PD‐1i) work by blocking the binding of the T cell's PD‐1 receptors to the PD‐L1 ligand in the cancer cells.[  ] However, the effectiveness of immune checkpoint inhibitors is greatly affected by the immunosuppressive microenvironment of the cancer.[  ] Tumor microenvironment (TME) is composed of many elements, such as immune cells, fibroblasts, epithelial cells, and acidic/hypoxic conditions, which are main cause of therapy failure and resistance.[  ] Tumor‐associated macrophages (TAMs) became an attractive target since they are abundant in TME, have high accessibility to cancer cells, and promote several pro‐tumoral processes. Macrophages in the TME can be roughly divided into M1‐phenotype and M2‐phenotype macrophages. M1‐like macrophages acted as tumor‐suppressive immunocytes and secreted IL‐1, IL‐12, and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)‐α to recruit natural killer (NK) cells and T cells. In contrast, M2‐like macrophages in most tumors supported tumor cell proliferation by secreting anti‐inflammatory factors such as IL‐10 and tumor growth factor‐β (TGF‐β). M2‐like macrophages, as tumor‐associated macrophages, alter tissue architecture and promote tumor cell motility, invasion, and metastasis.[  ] TAMs can also suppress the immune response by secreting programmed death‐ligand 1 (PD‐L1),[  ] expressing PD‐1,[  ] or recruiting immune‐suppressive regulatory T cells (Tregs).[  ] Therefore, undermining the M2‐phenotype or transforming macrophage from M2 to M1‐phenotype in the TME becomes a critical strategy.[  ] Macrophage colony‐stimulating factor 1 receptor inhibitors (CSF1Ri),[  ] tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKi), and toll‐like receptor (TLR) agonists[  ] are three types of M1 polarization drugs.[  ] However, Rodell et al. found that toll‐like receptor agonists, especially TLR7 and TLR8 agonists, re‐educated M2‐phenotype macrophages more effectively than colony‐stimulating factor 1 receptor inhibitors and tyrosine kinase inhibitors after they conducted a panel of 38 drugs screening.[  ] One of the TLR7/TLR8 agonists, resiquimod (R848), demonstrated the highest potential to repolarize M2‐like macrophages.[  ] Yet, although several pre‐clinical studies and clinical trials of TLR7/TLR8 agonists are ongoing,[  ] some obstacles still need to be resolved. R848 is an immune activation drug that activates different immune cells like dendric cells, T cells, and macrophages, which cause excessive or uncontrolled immune activation, inflammatory side effects, toxicity, and inconsistent efficacy.[  ] It is imperative to have appropriate drug delivery vehicles with properly controlled release profiles to overcome these obstacles. Also, there is an unmet need to track changes in the TME after treatment for an effective treatment regimen. Tracking the treatment in real‐time is a difficult process, although it can work as the best way to check the effectiveness of the treatment.[  ] To address real‐time tracking treatment, many researchers have developed imaging modalities to guide and understand the treatment, including PET/ SPECT, MRI, ultrasound (US), and photoacoustic (PA), and these systems track the change in the TME noninvasively.[  ] Phase‐shift nanodroplets (NDs) composed of low‐boiling point perfluorocarbons have gained attention as promising drug carriers for controlled release and as contrast agents for US and PA imaging.[  ] When these nanodroplets are loaded with light‐absorbing molecules, they can be vaporized by a biocompatible near‐infrared (NIR) laser. Upon laser triggering, the nanodroplets expand into microbubbles, which eventually burst, releasing the encapsulated drug.[  ] The size increase, along with the dye, enhances both US and PA imaging signals, enabling real‐time imaging to monitor the drug's biodistribution.[  ] 

In this study, we developed a double‐emulsion perfluorocarbon ND system designed for immunotherapy, integrating pulsed NIR laser activation with PA and US imaging for theranostic applications. Compared to conventional controlled‐release nanoplatforms, our system leverages perfluoropentane‐based NDs for localized and efficient drug release upon laser activation. Previous studies have demonstrated laser‐triggered nanocapsules for reactive oxygen species (ROS) therapy,[  ] while others have employed nanoparticle‐based immunotherapy strategies, such as R848‐NP formulations combined with radiotherapy.[  ] Our approach differs by using pulsed NIR laser activation to trigger R848‐loaded perfluorocarbon NDs, offering a non‐ionizing, highly tunable activation mechanism that minimizes collateral damage. Additionally, while previous studies have explored US‐triggered PFH‐R848‐ND systems[  ] or magnetic hyperthermia‐assisted ND activation,[  ] our work introduces laser‐induced phase transition of low‐boiling‐point perfluoropentane NDs, enhancing drug release efficiency. Notably, unlike conventional ND formulations utilizing perfluorooctyl bromide (PFOB),[  ] our system benefits from the lower boiling point of perfluoropentane, which undergoes phase transition under NIR laser exposure. Here in this paper, we have studied the effect of macrophage modulation on PD‐1 therapy effectiveness and developed NDs that can be tracked at the tumor site. To achieve the mentioned goal, we simultaneously developed double emulsion NDs encapsulating hydrophilic R848 for TAM‐based immunotherapy and diagnosis. By doping the NDs with the FDA‐approved dye indocyanine green (ICG), we were able to trigger their bursting under laser irradiation at an energy density of 20 mJ cm−2 and produce PA images.[  ] Nanodroplet encapsulated with R848 can remodel the TME.[  ] Double emulsion NDs have the advantages of high loading capacity of water‐soluble molecules,[  ] and controlled release upon light triggering. Thus, the goal of this study was to fabricate novel double emulsion‐based NDs that encapsulated TLR7/8 and ICG to diagnose and treat cancer. Importantly, we integrate PA and US imaging to assess TME oxygen saturation, enabling real‐time monitoring of hypoxia dynamics post‐treatment, a crucial factor in evaluating therapeutic efficacy. By combining laser‐assisted drug activation, real‐time biodistribution tracking via PA imaging, and post‐treatment hypoxia assessment, our system provides a comprehensive theranostic platform for immunotherapy.

Results and Discussion

Nanodroplets have recently been in the limelight due to their higher stability in the bloodstream and spatiotemporally activated into microbubbles to provide US contrast signals.[  ] Here, we developed NDs for PA imaging and TAM modulation for immunotherapy. To achieve this goal, we have developed double‐emulsion NDs. First, an emulsion of NDs was prepared to load ICG and R848 in the water phase, and PFP in the oil phase stabilized with perfluoropolyether (PFPE). Further, these NDs were stabilized in oil in water emulsion prepared using a 1.75% F68 surfactant solution. Here, we have used a novel ND system formulated for the ICG and PFC, where laser energy will be absorbed by ICG to raise the temperature for a short period. Hence, the phase transition of the PFP will burst the NDs and help with laser‐assisted drug release. These NDs are used for macrophage polarization for M1‐like macrophages to modulate the TME using laser‐assisted popping up of the nanoparticles for instant R848 release. TME modulation significantly improved PD‐1‐based immunotherapy. PFP NDs are phase conversion NDs and hence convert from nano to microbubble in the presence of the lessor/US, increasing the signal for the US imaging and helping in real‐time monitoring of the nanoparticle's accumulation, hypoxia, and tumor size.

Fabrication and Characterization of Nanodroplets (NDs)

We prepared NDs loaded with PFP as the oil phase and water‐soluble ICG or R848 in water, stabilized by PFEF in the water in oil emulsion. Further prepared NDs were added in 1.75% F68 surfactant solution to make the stable double emulsion formulation shown in Figure  1a. The prepared NDs were characterized by the TEM analysis for the size and shape, as shown in Figure 1b. Results have shown the particles were spherical with 149 ± 39 nm size. NDs show the core with light contrast, which can be attributed to the first emulsion of PFC and PFPE and shell with the solid core made up of the F‐68 surfactant. The morphology of the NDs changed when exposed to the TEM electron beam for about 30 sec, which can be attributed to the bursting of the nanoparticles and the release of PFC from the NDs (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The Prepared formulation showed a hydrodynamic diameter of 357 ± 20 nm with a PDI of 0.3176 ± 0.0030 measured by DLS (Figure 1c). Further, to confirm the size distributions of NDs, we have done nanoparticle tracking analysis (NanoSight) (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The data indicates that 50% of the particles (D‐50) in both formulations are 206 and 216 nm, with the mean size of the particles being around 406 and 419 nm for ICG‐ND and ICG‐R848‐ND, respectively. This information also supports the DLS data and confirms the NDs’ sizes of both formulations. The Zeta potential of prepared nanoparticles obtained a negative value, i.e., −11.5 ± 0.6 and −11.4 ± 0.4 mV for ICG‐ND and ICG‐R848 NDs, respectively, shown in Figure 1d. The negative charge of the NDs could be attributed to the hydroxyl groups on F68, a triblock copolymer consisting of polyethylene oxide‐polypropylene oxide‐polyethylene oxide.[  ] Further stability of the NDs was studied in different conditions, i.e., in media (RPMI ‐1640), 0.9% NaCl, and PBS for 14 days, and data is shown in Figure 1e. The initial hydrodynamic diameter of the NDs was 357 ± 20 nm. Upon addition to different media, the particle size increased to 928 ± 163, 1000 ± 193, and 1021 ± 139 nm in RPMI‐1640, NaCl, and PBS, respectively, within the first few hours and then stabilized over two days. Furthermore, we extended the stability study to two weeks. After two weeks, the average hydrodynamic diameter in PBS increased slightly to 1148±171 nm, while in RPMI‐1640 and 0.9% saline, the particle size and size distribution exhibited more pronounced enlargement, reaching 1922 ± 457 and 2488 ± 817 nm, respectively. This change in the NDs size can be attributed to the boiling point of the PFP being 28 °C, which evaporates slowly and contributes to the instability of the NDs. To study the stability in different temperatures, we conducted stability studies at both 4  and 37 °C over various time points (Figure S3, Supporting Information). At 4 °C, the initial hydrodynamic diameter at 2 h was 406 ± 11 nm, which increased moderately to 976 ± 11 nm at 24 h, suggesting that NDs remained relatively stable with minimal aggregation. In contrast, at 37 °C, the diameter was 490 ± 23 nm at 2 h, but it increased significantly to 3055 ± 569 nm at 24 h, indicating substantial size expansion likely due to premature activation or aggregation. After loading, NDs with ICG formulation turned into a greenish emulsion due to the characteristic ICG green color shown in Figure 1f. ICG loading is quantified by UV‐vis spectroscopy and shown in Figure 1g. UV‐vis spectra show a characteristic peak of the ICG in both the spectrum at 690 and 800 nm wavelength, and no quenching or degradation of the ICG was observed, and it has similar spectra as plain ICG. However, we observed a notable bathochromic shift in the graph of the ICG‐ND compared to plain ICG, which could be attributed to the refractive index of the surfactant, nano‐size droplets, and the interaction of the ICG with PFP.[  ] NDs have shown a loading encapsulation efficiency of around 70% for ICG, quantified by the UV‐vis spectrophotometer in both formulations, ICG‐ND, and ICG‐R848‐ND (Figure 1h). The particles were loaded with R848, achieving an encapsulation efficiency of 60.5 ± 0.8% (n = 4) qualified by HLPC (‐Figure S4, Supporting Information).

Nanodroplets Pop‐Up

The short‐pulse laser could activate NDs and transform tiny droplets into microbubbles (Figure  2a,b). Before the laser activation, NDs were stable with a size of 357 ± 20 nm, and later, these nanobubbles changed to microbubbles with a size of more than 1 µm (Figure 2b), and the solution became more translucent with bubbles at the surface (Figure 2a). After laser activation, the beam caused an increase in the turbidity of the emulsion, clearly demonstrating the growth in particle size (Figure 2a,b). Under laser irradiation of 750 nm, 10 Hz, and < 20 mJ cm−2, the NDs in the emulsion burst rapidly (Video 1, Supporting Information). This transformation can be attributed to the boiling point of the PFP, which is nearly 28 °C at room temperature. However, when it is in ND form, it stays as liquid at 37 °C due to Laplace pressure.[  ] During laser irradiation, the temperature rises, and an increase in the pressure leads to the formation of microbubbles. After transformation, the NDs immediately release the R848 within tumor sites, recruiting surrounding macrophage cells and transforming M2/M0‐like macrophage cells to M1‐type. The release efficiency was found to be 54.0 ± 1.3% (n = 3), determined by HPLC, a method explained in supplementary with R848 standard plot curve (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Formed microbubbles can increase contrast in the tumor site; hence, increasing the resolution of US Imaging and ICG can improve PA imaging.

In Vitro Biocompatibility

Nanodroplets are made up of different components and need to be evaluated for biocompatibility profile. Figure 2c,d shows the cytotoxicity studies after ICG‐ND and ICG‐R848‐ND treatment in 4T1 cells and IMR‐90 for 24 h. Results show that both formulations are biocompatible with more than 80% cell viability in all the concentrations in 4T1 cancer cells and IMR‐90 fibroblast. This data shows that in both cells, ICG‐ND and R848‐ICG‐ND are nontoxic. However, this formulation worked as an anti‐cancer by activating the macrophages, which helps in activating the immune system and ultimately enhances the effect of the PD‐1i. The Hemolysis assay results obtained showed that the NDs were compatible with the red blood cells (RBC). As shown in the photographs in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), after centrifugation, an RBC pellet was observed at the bottom of the PBS, ICG‐ND, and ICG‐R848‐ND sample vials. Following 30 min of incubation with NDs, the hemolysis ratio was 11 ± 0.23% for ICG‐R848‐NDs, 12 ± 0.2% for ICG‐NDs, and 11 ± 0.24% for the negative control (PBS solution), while the positive control (water solution) exhibited 100 ± 0.1% hemolysis. There was almost no hemolysis observed in the NDs, which is similar to the positive control and very low compared to the negative control (water), which had 100% hemolysis. (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Red blood cells were examined under the microscope at 20x, and there were no changes in the shape, size, and morphology of the cell observed, which proves the blood compatibility of the NDs.

Cell Internalization Studies

Nanoparticle internalization is an essential factor to consider for the precise and targeted delivery of drugs in the cells. To investigate the internalization of the NDs in the macrophage cells, NDs were loaded with doxorubicin (DOX) as a fluorescent tracer and treated in cells for 3 h.[  ] RAW 264.7 cells were treated with DOX and DOX loaded‐ND (DOX‐ND) at 1 µg mL−1 concertation for 3 h. Internalization studies shown in Figure 2e depict that free DOX‐treated cells are barely fluorescent due to the DOX internalization in the cytoplasm and the nucleus. However, when DOX‐ND treatment is given to cells, high fluorescence intensity is observed in the cytoplasm, and there is no internalization of the DOX in the nucleus. Further, we have also checked the internalization studies of NDs in the 4T1 cells under the same condition shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information). This data demonstrates the internalization of the NDs in the 4T1 cancer cells. However, compared to RAW 264.7 cells, very few NDs were taken by 4T1 cells. This higher internalization behavior of macrophages could be attributed to the phagocytic nature of macrophages, which is not present in 4T1 cells.[  ] This study confirms that macrophages internalize the nanoparticles, which helps enhance drug delivery efficacy, minimizing toxicity and improving the therapeutic outcomes.

Macrophage Polarization

Macrophage plasticity leads to the change of M0‐like macrophages to M1‐like macrophages or M2‐like macrophages, depending upon the stimuli they receive from the environment. While transforming from one phase to another, macrophages change their shape/size and release many types of cytokines that need to be tracked to evaluate the change (Figure  3a). Macrophage cells (RAW 264.7) were treated with R848 and observed for the polarization of the M0‐like macrophages to M1‐like macrophages for TME modulation. Four concentrations (2.5, 5, 7.5, and 10 µg) were chosen and monitored after 24 h. Macrophages started to show M1‐type features after being treated with R848 after 24 h (Figure S7a, Supporting Information). Although the higher dose of R848 seemed to trigger more M1‐type macrophage cells, the ratio of M1/M0 stagnated after the dosage exceeded 10 µg mL−1. Thus, we further inspected the activation macrophage cells within 10 µg mL−1 concentration. Inducible nitric oxide synthase‐2 (iNOS2) is a wide‐expressed gene in M1‐like macrophage cells and is widely used to observe M1‐like macrophage cells' expression levels.[  ] Hence, after 24 h of R848 treatment in macrophage cells, we analyzed the iNOS2 gene expression using real‐time PCR (RT‐PCR). The iNOS2 gene expression enhanced proportionally when R848, a TLR agonist, increased from 2.5 to 7.5 µg mL−1 (Figure S7b, Supporting Information). However, the iNOS2 gene expression dropped to half at the concentration of 10 µg mL−1 compared to 7.5 µg mL−1 concentration. The result indicated that the maximal applicable dosage of R848 should be 7.5 µg mL−1. The ratio of M1/M0 cell images also illustrated how macrophage cells would transform under different concentrations. Compared to M0‐like macrophage cells, M1‐like macrophage cells enlarged the perimeter and radius and became less stereoscopic (Figure S7b, Supporting Information). To evaluate R848 encapsulated efficiency by nanodroplets, we treated macrophage cells with R848‐only, ICG‐ND, and ICG‐R848‐ND. The RT‐PCR results showed that ICG‐R848‐ND strongly triggered macrophage cells compared to the ICG‐ND only (Figure 3). To examine the activity of the R848, experiments were performed in two settings, i.e., without IL‐4 (M0‐like macrophages) and with IL‐4(M2‐like macrophages). It is reported that IL‐4 can convert the M0 microphage to M2‐like macrophages, which are highly abundant in TME. Results are shown in Figures 3b,c for the M1‐like macrophage gene expression iNOS2 and TNF‐α. We have also examined expressions of M2‐like macrophage (CD206 and Arg) genes in cells to verify the conversion of the M2‐like macrophage and M1‐like macrophages Figures 3d,e. Compared with a control group and ICG ‐ND group, R848 and ICG‐R848‐ND expressed high TNF‐α and iNOS2 (M1‐like) but lower Arg and CD206 (M2‐like). The same results were also observed in the IL4‐treated groups, and there was high expression of TNF‐α and iNOS2 in R848 and ICG‐R848‐ND treated Cells without significant differences in the IL‐4‐treated or non‐treated group. However, M2‐like macrophages gene CD206 and Arg were high when treated with IL‐4 in control and ICG‐ND and reduced when treated with R848 and ICG‐R848‐ND, which shows the conversion of M2‐like macrophages to M1‐like macrophages. Moreover, as macrophages undergo polarization, their morphology changes depending on their polarization state. As shown in Figure S8 (Supporting Information), bright‐field optical microscopy images of macrophages reveal that cells treated with R848 and ICG‐R848‐NDs underwent morphological changes, increasing in both size and length compared to those treated with ICG‐NDs or control cells, which remained in compact clusters with smaller cell sizes. Notably, the dark staining observed in ICG‐ND‐ and ICG‐R848‐ND‐treated cells is due to light scattering from the NDs, confirming their uptake by macrophages. It's worth noting that the concentration of R848 within NDs was adjustable since this R848 is water‐soluble. Further, to confirm the results, we have done immunostaining for the CCR‐7 biomarker for M1 Phenotypes.[  ] When treated with only ICG‐ND, no shape change was observed in RAW 264.7 cells, and cells showed very faded fluorescence. However, when treated with the ICG‐R848‐ND, the cell elongated and showed high fluorescence compared to ICG‐ND. This cell behavior depicts the successful modulation of the RAW 264.7 cells in Figure 3f. In the case of the RAW 264.7 cells, ICG‐ND are biocompatible in the lower concentrations, and 100% of cells are viable. In ICG‐R848‐ND treatment, cells were viable at lower concertation, similar to ICG‐ND treatment; however, at higher concertation, AlamarBlue reduction was reduced to 50% (Figure 3g). This behavior could be attributed to RAW 264.7 cells' differentiation into the M1‐like. Hence, all metabolic resources and energy consumption shifted toward pro‐inflammatory and immune response rather than growth/division and proliferation.[  ] In summary, we developed novel NDs that can encapsulate both ICG and R848, FDA‐approved drugs with adjustable dosage loading and release. These nanoparticles are compatible with cells and change the TME by modulating the macrophages. To validate our in vitro results, we have started testing the formulation in the in vivo for breast cancer treatment.

In Vivo Synergistically Enhanced Cancer Immunotherapy

Developed NDs have shown in vitro macrophage polarization to M1, which can weaken the TME by modulating cytokine release. We also have hypothesized that modulation in TME can improve the therapeutic efficacy of existing immunotherapies like PD‐1 inhibitors. To evaluate this concept, we have evaluated the anti‐tumor effects using the 4T1 breast cancer xenograft mouse model. Before investigating NDs, we first established a 4T1‐Luc cell line to allow us to get fluorescence and luminescent images. Next, 4T1‐Luc cells with a Matrigel matrix were injected under a mammary fat pad and waited for the breast tumor to develop. The tumor was developed after 8 days of the injection and varied by the bioluminescence images before starting the treatment regimen tabulated in Figure  4a. Mice were administered for the following different treatments, including ICG‐NDs (as Ctrl.) (Group1), R848 (Group2), PD‐1i (Group3), ICG‐R848‐ND (Group 4), ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i (Group5) (Figure 4b). To assess tumor growth and immunotherapy, animals were evaluated periodically on days 0, 3, 6, and 9 using bioluminescence and US/PA imaging. Bioluminescence images of changes in tumor size and volume for the different treatments are shown in Figure 4c. Tumor volume, tumor tissue mass, and animal weight were monitored during these evaluations, and after 9 days of treatment, the tumors were harvested for further analysis. Quantitative tumor volume measurements are shown in Figure 4d, demonstrating that Group 5 (ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i) exhibits a significant reduction in tumor growth (tumor reduced to less than 300 mm3) compared to the control Group 1 with tumor volume 1500 mm3. Harvested tumor pictures are shown in Figure 4e, further verifying the results. Additionally, results depict that R848 and PD‐1i alone also showed tumor size reduction; however, tumor reduction is almost half of the NDs combined treatment. Control Group 1, treated with only NDs (ICG‐ND), does not have tumor suppression effects. These results depict that the R848‐loaded NDs help in the improvement of the anti‐tumor activity of PD‐1 immunotherapy. Also shown in Figure 4d, there is no significant change in the animals’ weight during the treatment. We also performed the RT‐PCR with harvested tumor tissue by extracting the RNA. Results in Figure 4g show the significant increment of iNOS2 (M1‐like macrophage) in Group 5 (ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i) and Group 4 (ICG‐R848‐ND) compared to R848 and PD‐1. These results depict an increment of M1 macrophages at the tumor site, which can significantly increase anti‐tumor activity. R848 (Group 2) alone has better M1 macrophage polarization compared to the PD‐1i (Group 3) and control ICG‐ND (Group 1). RT‐PCR for the other M1‐like (TNF‐α) and M2‐like macrophages (Arg) gene expression is shown in Figure 4f,g. The result indicates that M1‐like macrophage gene (iNOS2 and TNF‐α) expression was high in the treated group, R848(G2) and ICG‐ R848 ND(G4) compared to the Control (G1), and ICG ‐ND (G3) group, which conforms to the presence of the M1‐like macrophage requirement after treatment. Additionally, there is a reduction of the M2‐like gene (Arg) in the treated groups compared to the control group, confirming the reduction of the M2‐like macrophages. The results are consistent with our in vitro findings and align with previously reported data.[  ] Together, this evidence strongly suggests that R848‐loaded NDs induce anti‐tumor effects by promoting macrophage polarization, facilitating the transformation of M2‐like macrophages into M1‐like macrophages.

Biodistribution and Assessment of Tissue Damage

Biodistribution is the crucial parameter that directly affects the performance of nanoparticle‐based anti‐cancer therapy. Here, we have given the NDs injection in the intertumoral site; therefore, the initial environment is localized to the injection site (Figure  5a). Further, we checked the signal intensity at the tumor injection site to check the subsequent retention of NDs at the tumor site for up to 24 h. Quantitative analysis of the fluorescence signal at different time points was plotted in Figure 5b. Results depicted that after 24 h, NDs were still in the tumor site, with the signal intensity reduced to less than 40%. Reduction in signals could be attributed to the NDs degradation and diffusion from the tumor site or the lymphatic and natural clearance mechanism of the body. Further, to check the diffusion and accumulation of NDs in other parts of the body, mice were euthanized after 48 h to harvest tumors and major organs for ex‐vivo fluorescence imaging (Figure 5c,d). Strong signals were detected in the tumor compared to another organ, which suggests the retention of NDs in the tumor site and leakage were too low to detect in the other organs. H&E staining of tumor slices shown in Figure  6a, cells were densely packed in the tumors of control mice, whereas, in treated groups, the tumor cells exhibited fewer cells with nonuniform nuclei with cytoplasm. Additionally, a reduction in tumor cell density was observed in Group 4 (ICG‐R848‐ND) and Group 5 (ICG‐R848‐ND+PD1i) compared to Control Group 1 (ICG‐ND). Groups 4 and 5 exhibited an increased presence of red‐stained areas, indicative of pre‐necrotic cells, compared to Control Group 1. H&E staining shows the microstructure of the various organs, including the heart muscle fibers, alveoli, hepatic lobules, and renal tubules, all of which were arranged regularly and exhibited the normal morphology as control, without any inflammation and tissue necrosis (Figure S9, Supporting Information).

In Vivo Monitoring of Tumor Microenvironment by Photoacoustic Imaging

Apart from bioluminescence imaging and tumor size measurement, we inquisitively investigated the tumor microenvironment during the treatment in real‐time. US and PA imaging were performed to track the biodistribution of NDs, the change in the tumor size, and tumor hypoxia. PA signals come from NIR absorptive molecules hemoglobin (Hb), oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO2), and ICG. Signal sources can be differential by spectrum unmixing. We use the PA signal at 800 nm, mainly from ICG, to track the biodistribution of the drug. Immediately after the injection, only R848 did not show increased PA signals, while the PA signal in the ICG‐ND and ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i group was higher in the tumor (Figure 6a) compared to the signal before injection. The change in PA signals shows that after injecting NDs at the tumor site, NDs accumulated in the injection sites. Then, NDs pop‐up with NIR laser, and NDs turn into microbubbles. US intensity increases in ICG‐ND and ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i due to increased echogenicity by microbubble, and after 24 h of the injection, the PA signal distributes more evenly within the tumor site. This change indicates the diffusion of the released drug in the tumor. With time, the signal started reducing, and at 72 h, the signal was much lower compared to 24 h. (Figure 6a). This reflects further diffusion, clearance, and metabolic degradation of ICG. This information in real‐time nanoparticle tracking helps us to determine the dose interval for in vivo studies (Figure 4), and hence, we injected formulation in 3 day intervals. There is no signal in the tumor without the NDs injection (Figure S10, Supporting Information). As a result, prepared NDs aided in monitoring the course of treatment and choosing a dosage schedule. Maximum intensity projection (MIP) of the 3D volume of tumors also shows an intense signal when treated with ICG‐NDs and ICG‐R848‐NDs compared to only R848 treatment (Figure 6b). Hypoechoic mass in US images indicates tumor in mice (Figure 6a). The minimum growth of the hypoechoic area is found in the ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i group compared to the ICG‐ND and R848 group, which shows synergistic of R848 and PD‐1i. (Figure 6a).

Blood oxygen saturation (sO2) is defined as the ratio of oxygenated hemoglobin (HbO2) to total hemoglobin (Hb). Hypoxic TME is a hallmark of cancer progression. Reducing the hypoxia condition can improve the treatment efficacy of chemotherapy and immunotherapy. Since TLR agonist treatment could alternate the tumor environment,[  ] monitoring the oxygen level of tumors would be crucial to our NDs. To investigate hypoxia changes in this environment across different treatment groups, we perform spectral unmixing using seven wavelengths ranging from 690 to 820 nm. This approach allows us to generate sO2 maps of the tumors, providing insights into the variations in oxygen levels within the TME (Figure 6c–f). In sO2 maps, red means more oxygen‐saturated tissue, and blue means more hypoxia area. Before treatment, blue regions aligned with the hypoechoic area appealed to all groups. This demonstrates that TME is hypoxia. Twenty‐four hours after the treatment, we observed variations in sO2 profiles. In the control group ICG‐ND, hypoxia is large and deep in the area (Figure 6c). In the TLR7/8 agonist R848 group, hypoxia has been partially alleviated (Figure 6d). In the traditional immunotherapy PD‐1i group, the degree of hypoxia has also reduced (Figure 6e). In the synergy therapy group ICG‐R848‐ND + PD‐1i, hypoxia becomes rare 24 h after the treatment. (Figure 6f). These observations highlight the potential of combined therapies in effectively reducing tumor hypoxia.

In this paper, we have developed the NDs for laser‐assisted drug delivery, real‐time monitoring of the particles, and after‐treatment TME tracking. Upon pulsed near‐infrared laser irradiation, the ICG‐mediated photothermal effect induces a phase transition of the PFP core, leading to rapid vaporization, mechanical disruption of the nanodroplets, and localized release of R848 at tumor sites. This activation promotes the recruitment of M1‐type macrophages and enhances anti‐tumor immune responses. Simultaneously, the formation of microbubbles boosts ultrasound and photoacoustic signal intensity, enabling longitudinal tracking of nanodroplet biodistribution and therapeutic response. The excellent NDs and dual imaging systems using US and PA imaging modalities make it possible to visualize the NDs' location and distribution in the tumor and, after treatment, the change in the TME using PA imaging. This new approach combines the treatment and real‐time imaging and leads to tracking the major change in the TME, which, hence, positively helps improve the treatment.

Conclusion 

Macrophages are the main component of TME, and modulating macrophages can effectively improve immunotherapy. Human breast cancer is a high metastasis type among other cancers, and it also accounts for the leading cause of cancer death in women worldwide (Redig & McAllister, 2013).  Our work employed a nanomaterial approach to provide immunotherapy and imaging to track the treatment in real time. We fabricated NDs for TME modulation‐based immunotherapy and real‐time US and acoustic imaging for theragnostic applications. We encapsulated the adjustable dosage of R848 and ICG into NDs and obtained a loading efficiency of more than 60% for ICG and R848. NDs of 357 ± 20 nm can effectively pop up by the lesser power of less than 20 mJ cm−2, which is safe for the body. Further, in vitro studies showed that the NDs are biocompatible with 4T1 cells and RAW 264.7 cells. Developed NDs showed the uptake in RAW 264.7 cells and successfully polarizing RAW 264.7 cells to M1 macrophages as desired for anti‐cancer cancer therapy. In vivo experiments showed promising results in well‐established mouse breast cancer models. Compared to free R848 and PD‐1 immunotherapy alone, ICG‐R848‐ND has synergistically demonstrated more substantial anti‐cancer effects and enhanced PD‐1 immunotherapy. Further, using the developed NDs, PA and US imaging was successfully performed to monitor the treatment effect, NDs accumulation in the cancer site, reduction in cancer volume, and change in hypoxia after treatment in a real‐time setting. These new theranostic treatment approaches can help develop more targeted and effective treatment regimens for cancer in the future.

Experiment Section

Preparation of Double Emulsion NDs

Double emulsion NDs were prepared using sequential emulsification under sonication. The core water phase (Solution 1) consisted of 2 mg mL−1 ICG (Sigma‐Aldrich) and 332 µg mL−1 water‐soluble Resiquimod (R848) (InvivoGen, San Diego, CA, USA). The oil phase (Solution 2) contained 5 mg of perfluoropolyether, fluorosurfactant‐008 (RAN Biotechnologies, Beverly, MA, USA) dissolved in 500 µL of perfluoropentane (FluoroMed), while the outer water phase (Solution 3) was autoclaved Milli‐Q water with 1.75% Pluronic F‐68. To prepare the primary emulsion, solution 1 was added dropwise to Solution 2 at a 4:5 volume ratio, followed by sonication (Branson 450, 20 kHz, 3.2 mm diameter, 15% output power, continuous mode) for 30–60 sec in an ice‐water bath. The primary emulsion was then added to Solution 3 at a 1:4 volume ratio, and sonication was continued for another 30–60 sec under the same conditions while ensuring the sample remained in the ice‐water bath to maintain thermal stability. The double emulsion NDs were centrifuged at 100 g for 2 min. Then, the supernatant was transferred to a new tube. Centrifuged the supernatant again at 500 g for 2 min and collected the precipitation.

Ultrasound/Photoacoustic Imaging

An ultrasound imaging research system (Vantage 256, Verasonics Inc., Kirkland, WA, USA) was used with a 15 MHz linear array transducer, MS200 (FUJIFILM VisualSonics, Inc), a wavelength tunable (690–950 nm) OPO laser source with 7‐ns pulse duration and 10 Hz pulse repetition rate (Phocus Essential, Opotek, Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA). Animal Studies

All the animal studies were performed in compliance with the Institutional Animal Care Use Committee (IACUC) at the University of Illinois Urbana‐Champaign (protocol 19 124).

Establishment of 4T1 Synergistic Mouse Model

4T1‐Luc cells were suspended in OPTI‐MEM (Sigma‐Aldrich) and mixed with an equal volume of Matrigel Matrix (Corning Inc., Corning, NY, USA). The mice were 6‐week‐old female BALB/c mice (Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, ME, USA). The breast cancer syngeneic model was developed by injecting 50 µL of 1 × 106 cells subcutaneously near the inguinal lymph node (ILN) via a 27G insulin needle. After two weeks, injected 150 mg kg−1 Luciferin (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA, USA) intraperitoneally and imaged with a PerkinElmer IVIS Spectrum CT live‐animal imaging system (PerkinElmer). For the treatment with different formulations, 50 µL of the formulations were injected intratumorally to reach anesthetized mice on every 3rd day during treatment. At the endpoint of the treatment, the mice were euthanized by CO2. Tumors and other organs were harvested for further analysis.

Statistical Analysis

The results were expressed as the mean ± the standard error (SE). A two‐tailed Student's t‐test was conducted to compare the two groups, and a one‐way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for multigroup analysis.
