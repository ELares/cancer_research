# The Anti-FRα Antibody-Drug Conjugate Luveltamab Tazevibulin Demonstrates Efficacy in Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer Preclinical Models and Induces Immunogenic Cell Death.

## Abstract

Luveltamab tazevibulin is a folate receptor α (FRα)-targeting antibody-drug conjugate currently being evaluated in phase I and II/III clinical trials in endometrial and ovarian cancers (NCT03748186 and NCT05870748), respectively. In this study, we report non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) as an additional cancer subtype enriched for FRα expression. In patient-derived xenograft models of NSCLC, FRα-expressing tumors demonstrated robust tumor growth inhibition following luveltamab tazevibulin treatment, demonstrating its potential use for NSCLC treatment. Luveltamab tazevibulin was additionally identified as a potent inducer of immunogenic cell death (ICD). In in vitro cell killing assays, luveltamab tazevibulin induced all three hallmarks of ICD-high mobility group box 1 release, ATP release, and surface exposure of calreticulin. Furthermore, in in vivo vaccination studies, injection of luveltamab tazevibulin-treated tumor cells established protective immunity against subsequent tumor challenge. Consistent with ICD induction, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment in tumor-bearing mice also altered tumor immune cell infiltrate and activation, demonstrating its ability to modulate the tumor immune microenvironment. Given the success of immune checkpoint therapy in NSCLC and luveltamab tazevibulin's ability to potentiate the immune response, we evaluated the combination therapy of luveltamab tazevibulin with immune checkpoint blockade in syngeneic mouse models and demonstrated that combination treatment results in enhanced efficacy compared with either monotherapy alone. This improved activity with combination therapy was associated with increased tumoral infiltration of CD8+ T cells. In conclusion, the work presented here provides rationale for evaluating luveltamab tazevibulin in NSCLC either as monotherapy or in combination with immune checkpoint blockade.

The Anti-FRα Antibody–Drug Conjugate Luveltamab Tazevibulin Demonstrates Efficacy in Non–Small Cell Lung Cancer Preclinical Models and Induces Immunogenic Cell Death

Introduction

Folate receptor α (FRα) is a single-chain glycosylphosphatidylinositol-anchored membrane glycoprotein that plays a critical role in transporting folate into cells. In oncology, FRα has garnered substantial interest for targeted therapy because of its restricted expression pattern in normal cells and its overexpression in multiple cancers, such as ovarian cancer, endometrial cancer, non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), and triple-negative breast cancer. Recently, the FDA approved the FRα-targeting antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) mirvetuximab soravtansine for the treatment of FRα-high–expressing platinum-resistant ovarian cancer, providing clinical validation for FRα as a therapeutic target. Although the prevalence of FRα is often reported to be the highest in gynecologic malignancies, such as ovarian and endometrial cancers, NSCLC has also been reported to express high levels of FRα—particularly in the adenocarcinoma subtype—identifying NSCLC as a potential indication of interest for FRα-targeted therapies.

Luveltamab tazevibulin is a novel ADC composed of an anti-FRα–targeting antibody conjugated to a tubulin-targeting 3-aminophenyl hemiasterlin payload (SC209) via a cathepsin-B-cleavable val-cit linker. Incorporation of non-natural amino acids and the use of Sutro’s XpressCF+ cell-free expression system allow for site-specific conjugation of the cathepsin-cleavable 3-aminophenyl hemiasterlin linker payload, yielding a homogenous ADC with a drug–antibody ratio of four. In preclinical studies, luveltamab tazevibulin demonstrated tumor-selective release of the payload and robust antitumor activity in multiple ovarian cancer xenograft and endometrial cancer patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models, leading to its current evaluation in phase I and II/III clinical trials in patients with endometrial and ovarian cancer (NCT03748186 and NCT05870748), respectively. Its activity in NSCLC, however, has not been previously evaluated.

Although NSCLC was historically considered to be a non-immunogenic tumor, emerging data and the success of immune checkpoint blockade in NSCLC have transformed our understanding of the importance of the immune response in this indication. Given this, interest in cancer therapeutics that can bolster patient responses through immunomodulatory mechanisms has rapidly gained traction. One such method of boosting the immune response is through induction of immunogenic cell death (ICD), a specialized death pathway that can activate antigen-presenting cells to enhance presentation of tumor antigens, ultimately resulting in an improved T-cell response against the tumor. Therapies inducing ICD, then, have the potential to combine with immunotherapies to greatly enhance efficacy through complementary mechanisms. For example, treatment with anthracyclins or doxorubicin—chemotherapies known to induce ICD—prior to IL-2 or anti–PD-1 immunotherapy, respectively, helped increase the number of objective responses and improve overall survival in patients with acute myeloid leukemia and breast cancer. In addition to chemotherapies, ADCs have also been shown to stimulate and prime the antitumor immune response in the preclinical setting. ADCs against HER2 and HER3 carrying the deruxtecan payload and an ADC against CD30 carrying a monomethyl auristatin E payload (MMAE) have been reported to stimulate both innate and adaptive antitumor immunity and demonstrate combinatorial efficacy with anti–PD-1. Clinical proof of concept was also recently demonstrated with the recent approval of enfortumab vedotin, an anti–Nectin-4 ADC with an MMAE payload, in combination with anti–PD-1 in metastatic urothelial cancer. Thus, ADCs that can induce ICD may prove to be advantageous in indications in which immune checkpoint inhibitors are the standard of care, such as NSCLC.

Here, we report that FRα is highly expressed in lung adenocarcinomas (LUAD), consistent with published reports, and demonstrate that FRα-expressing NSCLC PDX models respond robustly to luveltamab tazevibulin treatment, highlighting the therapeutic potential of the ADC in this indication. Furthermore, through in vitro and in vivo studies, we identify luveltamab tazevibulin to be a potent ICD inducer capable of modulating the tumor immune microenvironment, and consistent with immune engagement, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment demonstrated combination benefit when co-administered with immune checkpoint blockade therapies. Mice receiving combination treatment overall exhibited higher complete response (CR) rates and greater tumoral CD8+ T-cell infiltration compared with those treated with either monotherapy and additionally demonstrated long-lasting protective immune memory. Overall, these data provide a strong rationale for evaluating luveltamab tazevibulin for the treatment of NSCLC and demonstrate evidence of an immune-mediated mechanism of luveltamab tazevibulin cell killing and potential synergy with immune checkpoint blockade.

Materials and Methods

Cell lines

KB, A549, and LLC cell lines were purchased from ATCC, and MC38 was purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. All cell lines were tested for Mycoplasma and confirmed for cell identity via short tandem repeat analyses (IDEXX BioAnalytics). Cell lines were maintained in DMEM/F12 (1:1), high glucose (Corning) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS (Sigma-Aldrich), 2 mmol/L GlutaMAX (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and 1× penicillin/streptomycin (Corning). The MC38-hFRα and LLC-hFRα cell lines were generated using Lipofectamine 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) to transfect wild-type cells with a piggyBac transposon vector encoding human FRα (hFRα). The transfected pool was selected using 20 μg/mL puromycin and maintained in cell culture medium with 2 to 10 μg/mL puromycin to maintain FRα expression in MC38-hFRα or LLC-hFRα cells. The transfected pool of LLC-hFRα was subsequently single-cell cloned to obtain the final LLC-hFRα cell line used for studies here. All cells were maintained in an incubator at 37°C with CO2.

Mice

For syngeneic tumor studies, female C57BL/6 mice around 6 to 12 weeks of age were purchased from Charles River Laboratories. For PDX studies, athymic Foxn1nu mice around 6 to 8 weeks of age were purchased from Envigo. All mice were housed in American Association for the Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care–accredited animal facilities and maintained in specific pathogen-free conditions and fed standard rodent chow or sterilized rodent chow ad libitum. All in vivo procedures were carried out during the light cycle and conducted in compliance with the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) at Sutro Biopharma or the commissioned contract research organization (CRO). Specifically, the NSCLC PDX study was carried out with the approval of the IACUC at Champions Oncology; the MC38-hFRα efficacy and rechallenge studies were carried out with the approval of the IACUC at Crown Bioscience; and all other mouse studies were carried out with the approval of the IACUC at Sutro Biopharma.

Tumor measurements and tumor growth inhibition calculations

Tumor volumes were calculated using the formula (L × W2)/2 in which length (L) and width (W) are the longer and shorter values, respectively. For average tumor growth curves or percent body weight change plots, average values were plotted up until the first mouse in the group was euthanized. Biological outliers were defined as those mice for which tumors developed large ulcers prior to reaching the study endpoint and were removed from the final analyses. Tumor growth inhibition (TGI) was determined on the day the vehicle group reached the study endpoint and was calculated as a percentage using the formula (1-([Tfinal-Tinitial]/[Cfinal-Cinitial]))×100, in which T is the volume of a treatment tumor, C is the mean of the vehicle control tumors, and initial size is the tumor size on the first day of treatment (day 0). TGI >100% indicates tumor regression below the starting tumor volume. For syngeneic cell line studies, a complete responder (CR) was defined as the absence of measurable tumor at the end of the study. Percent body weight change from the start of treatment initiation is also reported for in vivo studies involving therapeutic intervention.

Generation and analysis of luveltamab tazevibulin

The generation and analysis of luveltamab tazevibulin has been previously described.

The Cancer Genome Atlas FOLR1 expression

RNA sequencing data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database were analyzed using the University of California Santa Cruz Xena browser tool and used to plot patient tumor expression of FOLR1.

IHC

FRα protein expression in patient tumors was determined by staining commercially procured human tumor tissue microarrays (TMA; AMS Biotechnology and Pantomics). Staining was conducted by Ensigna CRO using a biotinylated and humanized anti–human FRα antibody (clone 26B3.F2). Human lung cancer tumor tissue TMAs (NSC152, LUC1021, and LUC1501) were processed using citrate-based pH 6.2 heat-induced epitope retrieval (Biocare Medical) and blocked for endogenous peroxidase with Biocare Peroxidase Blocker (Biocare Medical), for nonspecific background with Background Punisher (Biocare Medical), and for endogenous biotin with the Avidin-Biotin Kit (Biocare Medical). After blocking, sections were incubated with the biotinylated anti–human FRα antibody. Primary antibody was detected using a streptavidin-HRP polymer detection system (Biocare Medical), followed by chromogenic detection and counterstaining using the intelliPATH FLX 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (DAB) chromogen kit (Biocare Medical), IntelliPATH hematoxylin (Biocare Medical), and Ventana Bluing reagent (Ventana). Stained slides were then digitized with a TissueScope LE whole-slide scanner (Huron Digital Pathology) and subsequently evaluated for membrane tumor proportion score (TPS) by a trained pathologist. The TPS represents the % of viable tumor cells showing membrane staining at an intensity of one or higher.

Sections of formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) NSCLC PDX tissues from Champions Oncology CRO were processed using the Diva Decloaker citrate-based heat-induced antigen retrieval (Biocare Medical) and blocked for endogenous peroxidase with Vector Bloxall (Vector Laboratories) and endogenous biotin with the Avidin-Biotin kit (Biocare Medical). Additional blocking was carried out with BSA (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and donkey serum (Jackson ImmunoResearch). After blocking, the sections were incubated with a humanized anti–human FRα antibody (clone 26B3.F2) or human IgG as an isotype control. Primary antibody was detected using a biotinylated anti-human secondary antibody (Jackson ImmunoResearch), and signal amplification was performed using the Vector ABC reagent (Vector Laboratories). Slides were developed with the DAB chromogen (Sigma-Aldrich) and counterstained with hematoxylin (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Stained slides were then imaged using an Olympus BX53 light microscope (Olympus Life Science) and evaluated for membrane TPS by a trained pathologist.

Staining of CD8+ T cells in mouse tumors was performed at Ensigna CRO. Mouse tumor sections were stained on the Biocare IntelliPATH automated staining platform using the manufacturer’s recommended settings. For CD8+ T-cell staining, FFPE sections were processed using the Diva Decloaker for citrate-based heat-induced antigen retrieval and blocked with Biocare Peroxidase Blocker and Background Punisher. The sections were then stained with a rabbit anti–mouse CD8α antibody (Cell Signaling Technology) or isotype control rabbit IgG. The antibody stain was then detected with the MACH4 HRP-polymer Detection System (Biocare) and the IntelliPATH FLX DAB chromogen kit. The sections were counterstained with IntelliPATH hematoxylin and Ventana Bluing reagent. Stained slides were digitized with a TissueScope LE whole-slide scanner. Quantification of the CD8 staining was performed with the HALO Image Analysis software from Indica Labs using the CytoNuclear Quantification module. The area analyzed excluded necrotic regions and the tumor capsule, and the software was tuned to detect negative nuclei based on the hematoxylin stain (blue color) and CD8-positive cells based on the DAB staining (brown color). The percentage of CD8-positive cells was reported as proportion of positive cells (brown) to the total number of cells (blue + brown).

NSCLC PDX models

PDX model studies were carried out at Champions Oncology CRO. To establish tumors, PDX models were first passaged in stock animals. When tumors maintained in stock animals reached a volume of roughly 1,000 mm3, tumors were harvested for subcutaneous implantation in the left flank of pre-study animals. When tumors in pre-study animals reached an average volume of 150 to 300 mm3, animals were randomized, enrolled into the study, and dosed with test articles. The dosing day was designated day 0. Animals were treated with luveltamab tazevibulin (10 mg/kg) intravenously every week for up to 4 weeks. For each model, best response was defined as the largest reduction in average tumor volume observed, compared with the baseline tumor volume, after at least 10 days after treatment initiation. Best responses were categorized according to the modified RECIST criteria: progressive disease > 35% tumor volume change; stable disease ≤ 35% tumor volume change; partial response ≤ −50% tumor volume change; and CR ≤ −95% tumor volume change. At the end of the studies, vehicle-treated tumors were harvested for FFPE blocks for analysis of FRα expression.

Free drug and ADC cytotoxicity assay

KB or MC38-hFRα cells were incubated with a serial dilution of test articles (isotype control ADC, unconjugated anti-FRα antibody, luveltamab tazevibulin, or SC209) for 120 or 72 hours, respectively. The isotype control ADC is a non-targeting anti-GFP IgG1 antibody synthesized via Sutro’s XpressCF+ system and conjugated to an identical linker-payload—and at the same DAR—as that used in luveltamab tazevibulin. To measure cell viability, CellTiter-Glo (Promega) was added to each well prior to obtaining relative luminescence measurements on an EnVision plate reader (PerkinElmer). Percent viability was normalized to control-untreated cells. Curves were fitted using a log (inhibitor) versus response, variable slope, four-parameter non-linear regression fit in GraphPad Prism.

ICD markers

KB and A549 cells were incubated with 50 nmol/L isotype control ADC, luveltamab tazevibulin, or SC209 and harvested 72 hours after treatment for analysis of HMGB1 release, ATP release, and surface calreticulin expression. For measuring HMGB1 release, a sandwich ELISA was performed using a mouse anti-HMGB1 capture antibody (clone 2F6, Sigma-Aldrich) and a rabbit anti-HMGB1 detection antibody (ab18256, Abcam). Detection of the analyte was performed using polyclonal anti-rabbit antibodies conjugated to peroxidase (Jackson ImmunoResearch) and Ultra TMB/Stop Solution (Thermo Fisher Scientific) before reading on a SpectraMax M5 (Molecular Devices) at 450 nm. For measuring ATP release, the culture supernatant was analyzed using the ENLITEN ATP Assay kit (Promega), and chemiluminescence was measured using an EnVision plate reader. For measuring calreticulin expression, cells were dissociated from plates and stained with an anti–human calreticulin Alexa Fluor 647 antibody (ab196159, Abcam) and propidium iodide (PI) before being read on an Attune NxT cytometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). ICD markers following the treatment of MC38-hFRα and MC38 wild-type cells were performed in a similar manner as above, with minor differences: cells were treated for 48 hours before analysis of ICD markers; calreticulin was detected using a rabbit anti–mouse calreticulin primary antibody (#12238, Cell Signaling Technology) and a goat anti–rabbit IgG Alexa Fluor 647 secondary antibody (Invitrogen); and HMGB1 detection was performed using Ultra TMB and TMB BlueSTOP Solution (Seracare) and read at 650 nm. For all markers, fold change values were normalized to medium-only wells.

Monocyte activation and phagocytosis assay

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were isolated from human blood donors using Leucosep tubes (Greiner Bio-One) and NycoPrep 1.077 (Axis-Shield) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. PBMCs were cultured in RPMI (Corning) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, and 2 mmol/L GlutaMAX before the assay. On the day of the assay, KB or A549 cells stained with CellTrace Oregon Green (Thermo Fisher Scientific) or MC38 or MC38-hFRα cells were co-cultured with the PBMCs and the test articles (isotype control ADC, luveltamab tazevibulin, or SC209) at the designated concentrations. After 48 hours, the cells were harvested and blocked with human Fc block (BD Biosciences) before being stained with Fixable Viability Dye eFluor 450 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and anti-hCD14 APC (clone M5E2, BioLegend) and anti-hCD86 PE/Cy7 (clone IT2.2, Thermo Fisher Scientific). Samples were then read on an Attune NxT cytometer. Curves were fitted using a log (agonist) versus response, variable slope, four-parameter non-linear regression fit in GraphPad Prism. For MC38-hFRα- or MC38–human PBMC co-cultures, the proportion of CD86+ monocytes at baseline was subtracted from all values to isolate test article–related changes in the CD86+ monocyte population.

hFRα surface expression

For in vitro confirmation of hFRα expression, MC38-hFRα cells in culture were lifted with Accutase (Innovative Cell Technologies) and stained with LIVE/DEAD Fixable Near-IR dye (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and an in-house anti-FRα Alexa Fluor 647 antibody. The in-house anti-FRα staining antibody comprised of the same antibody backbone as luveltamab tazevibulin but was conjugated to Alexa Fluor 647 dyes instead of cytotoxic linker-payloads. For in vivo confirmation of retention of hFRα expression, MC38-hFRα tumors were implanted in C57BL/6 mice and harvested at around a volume of 150 to 200 mm3. Tumors were digested using the Tumor Dissociation Kit, Mouse (Miltenyi Biotec) and then subsequently incubated with LIVE/DEAD Fixable Near-IR, mouse Fc block (BioLegend), anti–mouse CD45 FITC (clone 30-F11, BioLegend), and anti-FRα Alexa Fluor 647. Stained cells were then read on an Attune NxT flow cytometer.

 In vivo vaccination

Methodology generally followed the standard in vivo vaccination protocol, with minor adjustments. Briefly, MC38-hFRα cells were treated in vitro with either 50 nmol/L SC209, 50 nmol/L luveltamab tazevibulin, or 300 μmol/L cisplatin (Sigma-Aldrich) for 72 hours. Dead cells in suspension were collected along with adherent cells and resuspended in PBS to form the vaccine inoculum. Then 0.5 to 1 × 106 total treated cells were injected subcutaneously into the left flank of female C57BL/6 mice. Fourteen days later, control naïve mice or vaccinated mice were challenged on the contralateral flank with 2 to 5 × 105 untreated MC38-hFRα cells. Tumor take and growth were then monitored for 39 days after rechallenge. Mice that developed vaccine-site tumors were excluded from the final analyses. On the day of vaccination, some of the vaccine inoculums, along with untreated cells, were taken for annexin V/PI analysis using the FITC Annexin V Apoptosis Detection Kit with PI (BioLegend) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were read on an Attune NxT flow cytometer.

Immune phenotyping

Tumors from mice treated with vehicle or 10 mg/kg luveltamab tazevibulin were harvested 5 days after treatment initiation. Tumors were minced and digested using the Tumor Dissociation Kit, Mouse to achieve a single-cell suspension. Two million cells were used for subsequent staining. Tumor cells were first stained with LIVE/DEAD Fixable Aqua or Near IR or Zombie UV (BioLegend) before blocking in mouse TruStain FcX (BioLegend) and True-Stain Monocyte Blocker (BioLegend). The cells were then stained with a combination of the following primary antibodies: anti–CD45-PerCP (BioLegend) or -AF432 (Thermo Fisher Scientific), anti–CD3-AF700 (BioLegend), anti–CD49b-BV650 or -BUV661 (BD Biosciences), anti–CD4-APC/Cy7 (BioLegend), anti–CD8α-FITC (BioLegend), anti–Thy1.2-AF700 (BioLegend), anti–PD-1-eFluor450 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) or -APC (BioLegend), anti–CD11b-BV711 or -BV510 (BioLegend), anti–Ly6G-BV605 (BioLegend) or -BV750 (BD Biosciences), anti–Ly6C-PerCP or -APC/Cy7 (BioLegend), anti–MHC II-FITC (BioLegend) or -BUV496 (BD Biosciences), anti–F4/80-PE/Cy7 or -BV421 (BioLegend), anti–CD11c-APC (BioLegend) or -BUV737 (BD Biosciences), or anti–CD86-BV785 (BioLegend) or -BUV563 (BD Biosciences). Following staining, the cells were fixed and permeabilized using the FOXP3 staining kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Following fixation and permeabilization, intracellular staining was performed using anti–FOXP3-PE/eFluor610 or -PE/eFluor710 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and anti–granzyme B-PE/Cy7 or -PE/CF594 (BioLegend). Flow cytometry data acquisition was performed on an Attune NxT or Cytek Aurora (Cytek) flow cytometer. Immune cell populations were defined using sequential gating. In brief, the following cell populations were gated: CD8+ T cells (CD45+ CD49b−/lo CD90+/CD3+ CD8α+), CD4+ helper T cells (CD45+ CD49b−/lo CD90+/CD3+ CD4+ FOXP3−), Tregs (CD45+ CD49b−/lo CD90+/CD3+ CD4+ FOXP3+), neutrophils (CD45+ CD11b+ Ly6G+), monocytes (CD45+ CD11b+ Ly6G− Ly6Chi MHC II−), macrophages (CD45+ CD11b+ Ly6G− Ly6Clo MHC II+ F4/80+), and dendritic cells (CD45+ CD11b+ Ly6G− Ly6Clo MHC II+ F4/80−).

Luveltamab tazevibulin dose response and combination treatment in MC38-hFRα and LLC-hFRα

To establish tumors in C57BL/6 mice, 1 × 106 MC38-hFRα or LLC-hFRα cells were implanted in the right hind flank. When tumors reached an average tumor volume of 100 to 150 mm3, mice were randomized, enrolled into the study, and dosed with test articles. The day of dosing initiation was designated day 0. Animals were treated with luveltamab tazevibulin intravenously in the dose–response study or with luveltamab tazevibulin intravenously and/or anti–PD-L1 (avelumab, Pfizer) or anti–PD-1 (clone RMP1-14, Bio X Cell) intraperitoneally for combination studies at the indicated dosages and dosing schedules described in the figures. The combination study from which the tumor growth curves were derived was performed at Crown Bioscience CRO. In MC38-hFRα combination studies, around 5 weeks after dosing, complete responders from the combination group or naïve mice were rechallenged with 1 × 106 MC38-hFRα or MC38 wild-type cells and monitored for tumor take and growth for 59 days after rechallenge. For CD8+ T-cell quantification following combination treatment, MC38-hFRα tumors from treated mice were harvested 7 days after treatment initiation and formalin fixed and paraffin embedded.

Statistical analyses

For ICD markers (HMGB1, ATP, and calreticulin), tumor volumes, and CD8+ T-cell infiltration, statistical analyses were performed using a one-way ANOVA with a Dunnett multiple comparison test in GraphPad Prism. All groups were compared with medium-only or vehicle-treated groups. For immunophenotyping analysis, Student t tests were performed in GraphPad Prism. For tumor-free mice Kaplan–Meier plots, pairwise comparisons with the naïve mouse group were performed using a log-rank test with Bonferroni correction. For correlation analysis of H scores and best responses in the PDX study, a linear regression fit was performed using GraphPad Prism. *, P < 0.05; **, P < 0.01; ***, P < 0.001; ****, P < 0.0001.

Results

FRα is expressed in LUADs and lung squamous cell carcinomas

Using publicly available RNA sequencing data from TCGA database, we determined the expression of FOLR1 across tumor types and found that expression was highest in ovarian tumor samples, followed by LUAD (Fig. 1A). FOLR1 was also expressed in lung squamous cell carcinoma, another histologic subtype of NSCLC, albeit at lower levels compared with LUAD. To confirm FOLR1 expression in NSCLC at the protein level, we stained several human TMAs from patients with NSCLC for FRα and determined the percentage of tumor cells with any amount of membranous FRα staining (TPS). Representative images showing a range of TPS scores can be found in Fig. 1B. The FRα TPS was stratified into four categories: TPS = 0/<1%, 1% ≤ TPS < 25%, 25% ≤ TPS < 75%, and TPS ≥75%, reflecting varying levels of FRα expression among patient tumors. Aggregating the data from the lung cancer microarrays, we observed a prevalence of 48% and 16% FRα-expressing tumors (TPS ≥1%) in non-squamous and squamous NSCLC tumors, respectively, a prevalence of 29% and 4% for non-squamous and squamous NSCLC tumors, respectively, with TPS ≥25% (Table 1). Consistent with TCGA data, non-squamous NSCLC, which largely consists of adenocarcinomas, exhibits a higher prevalence of FRα+ tumors than squamous NSCLC. Overall, NSCLC, especially those with adenocarcinoma histology, expresses high levels of FRα and therefore may be a good target for FRα-directed therapies.

Luveltamab tazevibulin demonstrates robust efficacy in NSCLC PDX models

Given promising expression levels of FRα in tumors from patients with NSCLC, we sought to evaluate the efficacy of luveltamab tazevibulin, a FRα-targeting ADC with a hemiasterlin payload, in NSCLC using PDX models. We tested luveltamab tazevibulin treatment in 13 NSCLC models (10 adenocarcinomas and three squamous cell carcinomas) covering a range of FRα TPS, spanning from 0 to 100 (Fig. 2A). To assess the efficacy of luveltamab tazevibulin in these models, animals bearing established NSCLC PDX tumors were administered four weekly treatments of vehicle or 10 mg/kg luveltamab tazevibulin and evaluated for best response, defined as the maximum reduction in tumor volume relative to baseline after at least 10 days after treatment initiation. Luveltamab tazevibulin treatment was well tolerated, with no body weight loss observed following treatment (Supplementary Fig. S1A). Overall, objective responses, determined by modified RECIST for preclinical studies, were observed in four of 13 (31%) NSCLC PDX models tested, inclusive of both FRα+ and FRα− models. If a cutoff of 25% FRα TPS is applied, the objective response rate improves to four of seven models (57%; Fig. 2B). Of the seven models with TPS ≥25%, one achieved a CR and three achieved PRs whereas no objective responses were observed in models with TPS <25%. Best response was also shown to correlate well with TPS levels (Fig. 2C). Of note, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment demonstrated sustained suppression of tumor growth in a majority of the models with TPS ≥25%, with six of seven models maintaining tumor regression until the end of the study (Fig. 2D; Supplementary Fig. S1B). Altogether, luveltamab tazevibulin proved effective in treating FRα+ NSCLC PDX tumors.

Luveltamab tazevibulin induces hallmarks of ICD in vitro

A recent study in patients with NSCLC reported that an ICD-related gene signature can be associated with an immune-inflamed phenotype and improved response to immunotherapy. Given the promising activity of luveltamab tazevibulin in NSCLC PDX models, we next sought to assess whether luveltamab tazevibulin treatment could engage the immune system through induction of ICD. The ICD-inducing potential of hemiasterlin has not yet been described; thus, to identify whether hemiasterlin, and by extension, luveltamab tazevibulin, can be classified as an ICD inducer, we treated KB (FRα+) and A549 (FRα−) cells in vitro with three test articles: an isotype control ADC, the 3-amine phenyl hemiasterlin payload (SC209), and the luveltamab tazevibulin ADC. The isotype control ADC is a non–target-binding anti-GFP antibody of the same human IgG1 isotype conjugated to an identical hemiasterlin linker-payload at the same conjugation sites as luveltamab tazevibulin. The anti-FRα targeting moiety and the SC209 payload were critical for cell killing as the isotype control ADC and the unconjugated anti-FRα antibody demonstrated little to no cell killing of FRα+ KB cells (Supplementary Fig. S2A). The treatment of KB cells and A549 cells with the three aforementioned test articles revealed that both luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209, but not the isotype control ADC, were able to induce the three canonical markers of ICD: HMGB1 release, ATP release, and calreticulin surface expression (Fig. 3A–C). Importantly, although SC209 was able to induce these markers on both FRα+ KB cells and FRα− A549 cells, luveltamab tazevibulin was only able to induce ICD markers in the FRα+ KB cells, demonstrating the target-specific and target-independent activity of luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209, respectively. Altogether, these data indicate that luveltamab tazevibulin and the SC209 payload induce the canonical markers of ICD in in vitro cell killing assays.

Luveltamab tazevibulin activates monocytes in co-cultures of FRα+ tumor cells and human PBMCs

To further corroborate the ICD-inducing potential of luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209, we incubated the same three test articles in tumor cell–human PBMC co-cultures to examine whether immune cells could become activated in the presence of the dying tumor cells. Incubation of FRα+ KB cells and PBMCs with luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209, but not the isotype control ADC, resulted in monocyte activation as demonstrated by increased expression of the co-stimulatory molecule CD86 and increased phagocytosis of tumor cells (Fig. 3D and E). In FRα− A549–PBMC co-cultures, however, only SC209 was able to induce CD86 expression in the monocytes, indicating on-target activation of monocytes following luveltamab tazevibulin treatment. Importantly, similar results in monocyte activation and phagocytosis were obtained with a second PBMC donor, suggesting that these results are not dependent on the donor.

Development of the MC38-hFRα murine syngeneic model

Although several cytotoxic drugs can induce ICD hallmarks in vitro, the gold standard method for determining whether an anticancer treatment induces true ICD is still in vivo vaccination in immune-competent mice. Thus, in order to evaluate the ability of luveltamab tazevibulin to induce ICD in an immune-competent in vivo model, we engineered the murine MC38 colorectal cancer cell line to express human FRα on the cell surface (MC38-hFRα; Supplementary Fig. S2B). In vitro cell killing assays using the MC38-hFRα cell line showed potent cell killing with SC209 and luveltamab tazevibulin treatment and a lack of cytotoxic activity with the isotype control ADC and unconjugated anti-FRα antibody (Supplementary Fig. S2C). Cell killing assays using MC38 wild-type additionally demonstrated the target-dependent activity of luveltamab tazevibulin (Supplementary Fig. S2D). Importantly, the MC38-hFRα line retains hFRα expression in vivo and exhibits sensitivity to luveltamab tazevibulin treatment when implanted in C57BL/6 mice (Supplementary Fig. S2E and S2F).

To evaluate ICD induction in MC38-hFRα cells, we incubated MC38 wild-type and MC38-hFRα cells with luveltamab tazevibulin or SC209 and measured levels of surface calreticulin and HMGB1 and ATP release. We found that, similar to observations made for the KB cell line, both luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209 were able to induce all three markers of ICD (Fig. 3F–H), albeit in a target-dependent and target-independent manner, respectively. Additionally, PBMCs co-cultured with MC38-hFRα cells treated with luveltamab tazevibulin or SC209 resulted in CD86 upregulation on monocytes (Fig. 3I). This observation, however, was not replicated with an isotype control ADC, indicating that monocyte activation was dependent on tumor cell killing and the FRα-targeting moiety. In co-cultures with PBMCs and wild-type MC38, activation of monocytes was only observed with SC209 treatment, supporting the target-specific activity of luveltamab tazevibulin. Thus, similar to our findings with the human KB tumor cells, luveltamab tazevibulin and SC209 treatment of MC38-hFRα cells induced markers of ICD and monocyte activation in vitro.

 In vivo vaccination with luveltamab tazevibulin–treated MC38-hFRα protects against tumor challenge

To further establish the ICD-inducing properties of luveltamab tazevibulin, we carried out a series of in vivo vaccination studies, the gold standard assay for validating ICD induction. In this assay, the ability of a drug or agent to induce ICD is evaluated by vaccinating mice with drug-treated tumor cells and assessing their subsequent ability to reject tumor challenge (Fig. 4A). Given that an intact immune system is requisite for ICD evaluation in vivo, we utilized syngeneic C57BL/6 mice and the MC38-hFRα cell line. To begin, we vaccinated mice with MC38-hFRα cells treated in vitro with either 300 μmol/L cisplatin, a known non-ICD inducer, or 50 nmol/L luveltamab tazevibulin for 72 hours. Percent dead and dying cells for each treatment are shown in Supplementary Fig. S3. Fourteen days later, naïve mice and vaccinated mice were challenged with MC38-hFRα cells in the contralateral flank and outgrowth of the MC38-hFRα tumors was determined. As expected, mice vaccinated with the cisplatin-treated cells rejected tumor challenge at similar rates compared with naïve mice (13.3% vs. 16% tumor-free mice, respectively; Fig. 4B and C). Mice vaccinated with luveltamab tazevibulin–treated cells, however, exhibited a robust ability to reject tumor challenge, with 86% of mice remaining tumor-free by day 39 after challenge. These data demonstrate that vaccination with luveltamab tazevibulin–treated MC38-hFRα cells can generate a protective immune response, corroborating the ICD-inducing property of luveltamab tazevibulin.

To determine whether the ICD-inducing property of luveltamab tazevibulin could be attributed to the SC209 payload alone, we performed another in vivo vaccination study comparing vaccination with luveltamab tazevibulin–treated and SC209-treated MC38-hFRα cells. Percent dead and dying cells for each treatment are shown in Fig. S3. Vaccination with SC209-treated cells robustly protected mice from subsequent challenge, to almost the same degree as vaccination with luveltamab tazevibulin–treated cells (76% vs. 82%, respectively; Fig. 4D and E). Thus, the ICD-inducing property of luveltamab tazevibulin can largely be attributed to SC209-mediated cell killing.

Luveltamab tazevibulin activates tumoral CD8+ T cells and macrophages

Given the ability of luveltamab tazevibulin to induce ICD, we sought to determine whether luveltamab tazevibulin treatment could modulate the tumor immune environment when administered therapeutically. To examine this, immunophenotyping in luveltamab tazevibulin–treated MC38-hFRα tumors was performed to identify ADC-induced immune cell changes in the tumor microenvironment. Overall, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment resulted in changes in myeloid cell, but not T-cell, numbers (Fig. 5A and B), with an observed decrease in macrophages and increase in monocytes present in the tumor. Macrophages in the tumor also exhibited upregulation of CD86 following luveltamab tazevibulin treatment, suggesting a greater capacity to stimulate T cells (Fig. 5C). Despite the lack of change in the number of tumoral CD8+ T cells, CD8+ T cells showed increased activation and cytotoxic potential as demonstrated by increased proportions of PD-1+ cells and granzyme B+ cells, respectively (Fig. 5D). Thus, consistent with its ICD-inducing properties, luveltamab tazevibulin can modulate the tumor immune microenvironment and lead to activation of T cells and macrophages.

Luveltamab tazevibulin combination treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors generates potent and durable antitumor immunity

Checkpoint blockade therapy has revolutionized treatment for NSCLC, highlighting the importance of the immune response in this cancer type. Given that luveltamab tazevibulin treatment can activate tumoral CD8+ T cells and macrophages, we further explored whether the combination treatment of luveltamab tazevibulin with immune checkpoint blockade could achieve even greater therapeutic benefit. LLC is a syngeneic lung model known to respond poorly to immune checkpoint therapy. To investigate the combination potential of luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-1 in this model, we engineered LLC to express hFRα and treated LLC-hFRα–bearing mice with vehicle, luveltamab tazevibulin, anti–PD-1, or luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-1 in combination. Although luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-1 monotherapies achieved moderate TGI (43% and 35% TGI, respectively), combination treatment led to even greater efficacy (73% TGI; Fig. 6A). Notably, the combination treatment group was the only group to achieve CRs (2/10). Luveltamab tazevibulin also showed good combination potential in the MC38-hFRα model with a different checkpoint inhibitor (anti–PD-L1). Luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 monotherapy in MC38-hFRα achieved 44% and 67% TGI, respectively, whereas combination treatment achieved 94% TGI (Fig. 6B) and a 40% CR rate. No complete responders were observed in either single agent–treated group. In both models, drug treatments were well tolerated, with no body weight loss observed following treatment (Supplementary Fig. S4).

The MC38-hFolRa complete responders treated with the combination therapy were subsequently rechallenged with either MC38-hFRα or MC38 wild type to assess the durability of the acquired antitumor immunity. All complete responders challenged with MC38-hFRα cells rejected the rechallenge whereas four of seven mice challenged with the MC38 wild-type cells were able to reject the parental cell rechallenge (Fig. 6C). In naïve mice, however, challenge with both MC38-hFRα and MC38 wild type exhibited full tumor penetrance, with no mice remaining tumor free. These data suggest that the complete responder mice in the combination group developed robust immunity against MC38-hFRα but that some of the epitopes responsible for protection are derived from parental MC38, leading to partial protection against wild-type MC38 rechallenge. Overall, we demonstrate that combination treatment with luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 results in the development of robust antitumor immunity and the ability to reject subsequent tumor rechallenge.

To understand the impact of luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 combination treatment on the tumor immune microenvironment, we examined the infiltration of CD8+ T cells in the tumor via IHC 7 days after initial treatment. Both luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 single-agent treatment failed to increase the density of tumoral CD8+ T cells compared with vehicle treatment, but consistent with greater TGI, the luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 combination treatment demonstrated enhanced levels of infiltrating CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6D). Thus, combination treatment with luveltamab tazevibulin and anti–PD-L1 synergized to stimulate a greater CD8+ T-cell response.

Discussion

FRα is overexpressed in numerous tumor types and has recently been clinically validated as a target with the approval of mirvetuximab soravtansine, an anti-FRα ADC, for late-stage ovarian cancer. Although FRα is highly prevalent in gynecologic cancers, such as ovarian and endometrial cancers, RNA data from TCGA and previously published reports have identified NSCLC as another indication with significant FRα expression, suggesting the potential benefit of FRα-targeted therapies in treating this indication. FRα expression has also been shown to be highly concordant between biopsies, primary tumors, and distant and local metastases obtained from patients with NSCLC, supporting the utility of targeting FRα in even late-stage patients presenting with multiple metastases. Of note, analysis of FRα expression in NSCLC by both RNA and IHC revealed a significant difference between the two major histologic subtypes of NSCLC, with adenocarcinomas exhibiting higher FRα expression and prevalence compared with squamous cell carcinomas, consistent with previous reports. Differences such as this may help inform patient selection in the clinic, and it would be interesting to understand the contribution of histological subtypes and molecular subtypes (e.g., EGFR and KRAS status) to differences in FRα expression.

Given the level of expression of FRα observed in NSCLC, we sought to determine whether our clinical anti-FRα ADC, luveltamab tazevibulin, could potentially be utilized to treat NSCLC. To evaluate this, we assessed the activity of luveltamab tazevibulin in NSCLC PDX models spanning a wide range of FRα expression. Following treatment, we observed the greatest benefit in tumors with higher FRα expression. PDX models with FRα TPS <25% showed little to no inhibition in tumor growth whereas those with TPS ≥25% showed robust and sustained TGI. This 25% cutoff was determined by the model with the lowest TPS in which no efficacy was observed. All partial and CRs were exclusive to models with TPS ≥25%. Efficacy also correlated well with FRα expression, supporting the target-dependent activity of luveltamab tazevibulin. Of note, models with intermediate TPS were underrepresented in our data set; a greater number of models would need to be tested to determine a more precise cutoff for efficacy. Interestingly, one of the 13 PDX models was EGFRmutant (CTG-2534), and although it did not achieve an objective response, tumor growth was greatly inhibited by luveltamab tazevibulin treatment such that tumor regression was maintained until the end of the study. However, further study is required to parse out any differential effects in EGFRmutant or EGFRwild-type NSCLC tumors. Additionally, although squamous cell carcinomas on the whole exhibit lower FRα expression and prevalence, our PDX results demonstrated that squamous cell carcinomas with substantial FRα expression still respond well to luveltamab tazevibulin treatment, with one model achieving a CR. Thus, although a smaller proportion of patients with squamous cell carcinoma may be FRα+, these patients may still respond well to luveltamab tazevibulin treatment.

Connecting the FRα expression level to clinical response is important for understanding which patient population stands to benefit from ADC treatment. In ovarian cancer, clinical benefit following treatment with luveltamab tazevibulin is observed across a range of FRα expression, including in patients with low-to-medium FRα expression, with a cutoff of ≥25% FRα-positive tumor cells at any staining intensity; in the phase I trial, patients with TPS ≤25%, 25% < TPS ≤75%, and TPS >75% demonstrated objective response rates of 11.1%, 33.3%, and 40%, respectively. Importantly, these TPS levels were determined by the approved FOLR1 IHC Companion Diagnostic Assay (Ventana). Although the 25% TPS cutoff seems to be shared between the clinical data for patients with ovarian cancer and the NSCLC PDX data presented here, it is important to note that the IHC assay performed for this preclinical NSCLC study utilized a different anti-FRα clone than the one used in the FRα companion diagnostic. Thus, further studies are needed to determine the most clinically relevant cutoff that may be used for potential patient selection in NSCLC.

Although NSCLC has historically been considered to be a nonimmunogenic cancer, the recent clinical success of immune checkpoint blockade in NSCLC has highlighted the contribution and importance of the immune response in this indication. With this increased understanding, more attention has been brought to other cancer therapeutics and the way in which they can modulate or augment the antitumor immune response to better combine with immune therapies. Chemotherapy-induced ICD, for example, is observed to increase objective responses following subsequent treatment with IL-2 or anti–PD-1 in acute myeloid leukemia and breast cancer, respectively, and ADCs with other linker-payloads, consisting of topoisomerase I inhibitors or other microtubule-targeting agents, such as MMAE, have been shown to stimulate the immune system and combine with anti–PD-1 therapy. Interestingly, the mechanism of MMAE and hemiasterlin is similar as both inhibit microtubule polymerization by binding to the vinca-binding site on tubulin. To determine whether luveltamab tazevibulin could exhibit immune-modulating properties, we evaluated the ICD-inducing properties of luveltamab tazevibulin. Both luveltamab tazevibulin and the hemiasterlin payload were capable of inducing the three in vitro hallmarks of ICD—HMGB1 release, ATP release, and surface exposure of calreticulin—and activating monocytes and increasing their phagocytic capabilities in immune cell–tumor cell co-cultures, suggesting improved uptake of tumor antigens and improved licensing of the APC for productive T-cell activation. Confirmation of luvetamab tazevibulin and hemiasterlin as bona fide ICD inducers then followed, using the in vivo vaccination model. We demonstrated that vaccination with either luveltamab tazevibulin–treated or hemiasterlin-treated, but not cisplatin-treated, MC38-hFRα cells could protect mice from subsequent tumor challenge, solidifying luveltamab tazevibulin and the hemiasterlin payload as true ICD inducers. Notably, to our knowledge, this is the first report detailing the ICD-inducing properties of hemiasterlin. Overall, these data imply that luveltamab tazevibulin inhibits tumor growth not only through its direct cytotoxic activity on tumors but also through engagement and stimulation of the immune system.

This immune stimulation is further demonstrated in vivo in tumor immune phenotyping studies following luveltamab tazevibulin treatment. Here, we report that, under the conditions tested, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment modified myeloid cell numbers (monocytes and macrophages) but not T-cell numbers in the tumor. Although CD8+ T-cell numbers did not increase, the activation state and cytotoxic potential of the CD8+ T cells were improved, with CD8+ T cells exhibiting greater PD-1 and granzyme B expression, indicative of T-cell activation and cytotoxic potential, respectively. Interestingly, a lack of increased CD8+ T-cell numbers following ADC treatment was also observed in patients with breast cancer treated with ladiratuzumab vedotin, an anti-LIV1 ADC with an MMAE payload; despite no apparent change in intratumoral CD8+ T-cell infiltration, patients were still able to derive combination benefit with checkpoint blockade therapy, suggesting that other metrics of the immune response may be important for understanding ADC-related immune modulation and combinatorial potential. We also found that the macrophages at the tumor site upregulated CD86, mimicking the observations for monocytes in vitro. This change reflects modulation of not only the adaptive immune system but also the innate immune system that arms the CD8+ T-cell response. Thus, luveltamab tazevibulin treatment can activate CD8+ T cells and macrophages to skew toward an improved antitumor response, corroborating the immunostimulatory properties of luveltamab tazevibulin.

Immune checkpoint blockade therapy is standard-of-care treatment for late-stage NSCLC, but overall response rates following immune checkpoint blockade are often poor in non-selected patient populations of NSCLC, with only 20% to 30% of patients responding to therapy. Patients exhibiting PD-L1 TPS ≥50% have been shown to benefit the most from checkpoint blockade therapy, but these patients only make up roughly 25% of the total patient population of NSCLC.Thus, despite the advances checkpoint blockade has conferred, there is still significant room for improvement. Recently, substantial interest has turned to ADCs as partners for combination therapy, and although some combination treatments have shown promise, only one has, so far, attained FDA approval: enfortumab vedotin, an anti–Nectin-4 ADC with an MMAE payload, in combination with pembrolizumab in metastatic urothelial cancer. Given the ICD-inducing properties of luveltamab tazevibulin, we became interested in examining whether luveltamab tazevibulin could complement or potentiate the activity of checkpoint blockade. We evaluated the combination treatment of luveltamab tazevibulin with anti–PD-1 and anti–PD-L1 in the syngeneic LLC-hFRα (murine lung cancer) and MC38-hFRα (murine colorectal cancer) models, respectively, and found that, in both models, efficacy was enhanced in the combination group compared with the monotherapy groups. Furthermore, in the MC38-hFRα model, the combination group was the only group to achieve CRs, and these complete responder mice additionally demonstrated a robust development of long-lasting antitumor immunity. Analysis into the potential mechanism underlying the combination benefit revealed an increase in tumoral CD8+ T-cell infiltration associated with only the combination treatment group and not monotherapy-treated groups, implying a synergistic benefit. Interestingly, in our immunophenotyping studies, although luveltamab tazevibulin treatment alone was not able to increase CD8+ T-cell numbers in the tumor, we found that a greater proportion of CD8+ T cells had upregulated PD-1, which could possibly render them more sensitive to enhanced activation through anti–PD-1 and anti–PD-L1 blockade. By combining the non-overlapping mechanisms of tumor-intrinsic cell death via ADCs and tumor-extrinsic activation of the patient’s immune system via checkpoint blockade, it seems that ADCs that are capable of bridging the two mechanisms through immune modulation will exhibit the greatest potential for combination and enhanced patient benefit in clinic.

In conclusion, the work presented here demonstrates that FRα may be an important target in NSCLC and that luveltamab tazevibulin bears potent antitumor activity in FRα-expressing preclinical models of NSCLC. Furthermore, as an added mechanism to direct tumor cell killing, luveltamab tazevibulin can induce ICD to engage the immune system toward antitumor activity and combine with immune checkpoint blockade to elicit robust TGI and long-lasting immune memory. REFRaME-L1 is a global phase II study (NCT06555263) that is now investigating the safety and efficacy of luveltamab tazevibulin in previously treated patients with advanced or metastatic NSCLC with positive FRα expression.
