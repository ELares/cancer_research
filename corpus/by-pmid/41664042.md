# STING-Activating nanoplatforms for image-guided sonodynamic cancer therapy.

## Abstract

Tumor precision treatment still faces the challenge of being unable to achieve real-time monitoring. The rapid advancements in nanomedicine within the biomedical sector have highlighted the potential of integrated nano-platforms for tumor diagnosis and treatment. Herein, we create a multimodal diagnostic nanodrug, ⁹⁹mTc-NPAIE-MSA, which combines sonosensitizers, STING agonists, and radionuclides for immuno-sonodynamic therapy targeting breast cancer. This platform utilizes polymers with aggregation-induced luminescence properties that, when activated by US, generate cytotoxic reactive oxygen species, causing DNA and mitochondrial damage, activating the cGAS-STING pathway, and releasing the STING agonist MSA-2 to enhance the immune response. Additionally, ⁹⁹mTc incorporation facilitates SPECT/CT and near-infrared fluorescence imaging, thereby improving tumor targeting and therapeutic monitoring precision. Both in vitro and in vivo studies confirm the significant anti-tumor effects and immunomodulation achieved by this nano-platform under US, presenting a novel strategy for precise tumor diagnosis and treatment.

Introduction

Breast cancer, the most prevalent malignancy in women, poses challenges related to easy early metastasis and limited diagnostic and therapeutic precision. How to effectively overcome the above difficulties is a key scientific issue in combating tumors such as breast cancer. The integration of diagnostic and therapeutic functions into a single nanoparticle—termed “theranostic nanoplatforms”—has revolutionized precision medicine, particularly in challenging cancers like breast cancer where early detection and precise treatment are critical. Research in tumor theranostics predominantly utilizes nanoparticle carrier platforms, which show great promise for breast cancer management. These nanocarriers, with their distinct acoustic, optical, electrical, and magnetic properties, offer innovative methods for sensitive and reliable tumor diagnosis. Moreover, integrating multiple imaging modalities within a single nanocarrier optimizes their benefits and mitigates their limitations, providing complementary diagnostic insights. Despite these advancements, significant challenges persist. However, there are still significant challenges in integrating the commonly used clinical diagnostic and therapeutic tools into nanoparticle carrier.

Radionuclides have emerged as common and powerful tools in modern medicine, with their unique radioactive properties enabling both diagnostic imaging and therapeutic applications. Among these, the radionuclide 99mTc stands out as one of the most extensively used in nuclear medicine, particularly crucial for diagnosing bone metastases and early detection of skeletal lesions in breast cancer and other cancers. Coupled with SPECT/CT, 99mTc facilitates simultaneous evaluation of metabolic activity and precise anatomical localization, significantly enhancing clinical decision-making in oncology. This combination excels in real-time biodistribution tracking and dosimetric precision, making it invaluable for monitoring disease progression and treatment response. Nonetheless, conventional 99mTc-labeled carriers often encounter challenges such as isotope dissociation and inadequate tumor accumulation, limiting their diagnostic and therapeutic efficacy. Moreover, nanostructures often suffer from insufficient drug-loading capacity or carrier dissociation below the critical micelle concentration, which may lead to suboptimal theranostic outcomes. These challenges underscore the urgent need for improved nanocarrier materials. 

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is a traditional form of clinical physical therapy and it demonstrates significant potential in cancer treatment by utilizing ultrasound (US)-activated sensitizers to generate cytotoxic reactive oxygen species (ROS), overcoming the depth limitations of light-dependent therapies with its superior tissue penetration (> 5 cm) and precise spatiotemporal control. Aggregation-induced emission (AIE) materials further enhance this approach by avoiding the aggregation-caused quenching (ACQ) effect through their non-planar, three-dimensional structures, substantially improving fluorescence intensity and ROS production efficiency under US activation. The integration of near-infrared (NIR-II) fluorescence and radionuclide imaging with SDT provides complementary diagnostic capabilities. NIR-II fluorescence offers high-resolution, real-time visualization of tumor margins, while radionuclide imaging, such as SPECT/CT, enables metabolic activity assessment and anatomical localization. This multimodal imaging approach enhances diagnostic accuracy and therapeutic monitoring. Despite these advantages, the direct in vivo application of AIE materials faces challenges, including poor metabolic stability and insufficient tumor targeting. These limitations can be mitigated by incorporating AIE sonosensitizers into nanocarriers, which optimize pharmacokinetics and biodistribution. Recent advancements in polymeric composite nanomaterials, combining AIE sensitizers with radionuclides, demonstrate promising potential for precision-targeted delivery, improving both therapeutic efficacy and clinical translatability in tumor theranostics. 

We developed an US-responsive polymeric nanoplatform (99mTc-NPAIE-MSA) incorporating AIE and radionuclides to synergistically enhance sonodynamic therapy, activate the STING pathway - a critical immune signaling pathway for antitumor immunity - and enable multimodal imaging (Scheme 1). Initially, a dihydroxy-functionalized AIE luminogen was synthesized and copolymerized with a ROS-sensitive monomer (RSM) through condensation polymerization, forming biodegradable amphiphilic polymer (Poly-(HPMDA-RSM-AIE)-PEG, RSP-AIE). RSP-AIE self-assembled with STING agonist MSA-2 to form nanoparticles (NPAIE-MSA). Upon US exposure, NPAIE-MSA activated the sonosensitizer to produce cytotoxic ROS, causing tumor cell DNA damage and mitochondrial dysfunction, releasing mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) that activates the cGAS-STING pathway, while the released MSA-2 further amplifies this activation, creating a potent immunostimulatory effect alongside direct tumor cytotoxicity. For imaging, 99mTc-loaded polymer (99mTc-RSP) was synthesized, and self-assembled with RSP-AIE and MSA-2 into nanoparticles (99mTc-NPAIE-MSA), enabling simultaneous NIR-II fluorescence and radionuclide imaging. This integrated theranostic platform combines radionuclide imaging with sonodynamic therapy and STING activation for precise breast cancer diagnosis and treatment.

Results

Backbone-Integrated AIE sonosensitizer: mPEG-Capped Poly-(AIE-HPMDA-RSM) nanoparticles with controlled drug release

AIE materials overcome the ACQ effect through RIM mechanism, enhancing fluorescence quantum yield for improved tumor imaging and ROS generation in SDT, though their clinical application remains limited by poor metabolic stability and targeting efficiency. To overcome this, we designed and synthesized a dihydroxy-functionalized AIE monomer as the key building block - this bifunctional design enables direct incorporation of AIE into the polymer backbone through condensation polymerization, which is crucial for achieving controlled drug delivery and tumor targeting. The structure of the AIE monomer was confirmed using 1H NMR and 13C-NMR spectroscopy (Fig. 1A and Figure S1, S2). By copolymerizing it with 1,2,4,5-cyclohexanetetracarboxylic dianhydride (HPMDA) and RSM, followed by mPEG5k-OH end-capping, we successfully constructed a sonosensitizer-embedded ROS-responsive polymer (Poly-(AIE-HPMDA-RSM)-mPEG, RSP-AIE) with verified structure (Fig. 1B and Figure S3). This backbone-integration strategy ensures stable AIE loading and responsive release capability for enhanced tumor theranostics.

The amphiphilic RSP-AIE self-assembled with the STING agonist MSA-2 to form nanoparticles (NPAIE-MSA) (Fig. 1C). Dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis showed that NPAIE-MSA had an average hydrodynamic diameter of 112 nm (polydispersity index, PDI = 0.10) and a zeta potential of −19.9 mV (Figure S4A, B). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) confirmed a uniform spherical morphology with a diameter of about 110 nm (Fig. 1D). The nanoparticles exhibited high stability, with size variation remaining below 4.5% (within 115 nm) over 30 days at 4 °C, and the PDI consistently maintained below 0.15 (Figure S4C, D). Additionally, UV-Vis absorption and fluorescence emission spectra indicate that NPAIE-MSA retains the characteristic absorption profile of the AIE molecules in the near-infrared region (450–900 nm) and is capable of generating fluorescence signals in the near-infrared region (850–1200 nm) upon 808 nm excitation (Fig. 1E), thereby preserving its NIR‑II imaging performance. Notably, the fluorescence quantum yields of NPAIE-MSA was calculated to be 0.62%. at 808 nm excitation. These results collectively demonstrate the successful preparation of structurally stable, uniformly sized, and negatively charged spherical nanoparticles.

The capability of sonosensitizers to produce ROS under US is key to assessing their sonodynamic effects. Electron spin resonance (ESR) spectroscopy detected characteristic triplet radical peaks when 2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidine (TEMP) was trapped post-US, confirming that NPAIE-MSA effectively generates singlet oxygen (1O₂) (Fig. 1F). Further analysis using the 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF) probe showed a marked absorbance decrease at 410 nm after 120 s of US exposure, with a half-life of approximately 60 s, quantitatively demonstrating NPAIE-MSA’s efficient ROS generation under low-intensity US (Fig. 1G and H). Structural analysis revealed that the ROS-sensitive linkages (RSM) in the RSP-AIE polymer chains imparted ROS-specific responsiveness to the NPAIE-MSA nanoparticles. Under ultrasound stimulation, the RSM bonds were efficiently cleaved, leading to the directional disassembly of the polymer backbone. Gel permeation chromatography (GPC) indicated a decrease in the number-average molecular weight of RSP-AIE from 11,900 Da to 6,500 Da (a 45.4% reduction) post-ultrasonic treatment, confirming main-chain scission (Fig. 1I). TEM images showed the dissociation of NPAIE-MSA from uniform spherical structures (~ 110 nm) into fragmented aggregates upon sonication (Fig. 1J). DLS revealed a disordered particle size distribution after sonication (PDI > 0.5), suggesting a loss of sample homogeneity following disassembly (Figure S4E). Furthermore, the US-induced release of MSA-2 achieved 90% within 12 h, significantly higher than the 8% release observed in the PBS control group (Fig. 1K). These findings collectively validate that NPAIE-MSA effectively combines potent sonodynamic effects with controlled drug release by utilizing an ROS-responsive backbone disassembly mechanism.

Cellular uptake and cytotoxicity of NPAIE-MSA + US

Flow cytometry and confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) were employed to investigate the endocytosis of NPAIE-MSA by the mammary carcinoma cell line (4T1 cells). Results showed distinct cytoplasmic fluorescence after 4 h of co-incubation (Fig. 2A), with signal intensity increasing over time, indicating rapid internalization by 4T1 cells and pronounced time-dependent endocytic behavior (Fig. 2B and C, Figure S5). MTT cytotoxicity assays indicated that both free MSA-2 and non-sonicated NPAIE-MSA maintained over 75% cell viability in 4T1, MCF-7 and MCF-10 A cells at 40 µM, whereas NPAIE-MSA + US reduced cell viability to below 10% (Fig. 2D). Following treatment with NPAIE-MSA + US, the IC50 values for 4T1, MCF-7, and MCF-10 A cells were 3.21 ± 0.19 µM, 4.11 ± 0.32 µM, and 10.22 ± 0.81 µM, respectively (Fig. 2E). These findings demonstrated that NPAIE-MSA + US exhibited greater cytotoxic effects on cancer cells compared to normal cells. Apoptosis assays revealed that NPAIE-MSA + US treatment led to an apoptosis rate of approximately 83% in 4T1 cells, four times higher than the 20% observed with MSA-2 or NPAIE-MSA (Fig. 2F and G). Live/dead cell staining and colony formation assays further demonstrated an increased proportion of red fluorescent cells and reduced clonogenic capacity in the NPAIE-MSA + US-treated cells (Fig. 2H-K). In conclusion, NPAIE-MSA shows significant tumor cell-killing effects upon US activation, driven by enhanced endocytic efficiency and synergistic sonodynamic effects.

NPAIE-MSA + US induced ROS cytotoxicity and activated the cGAS-STING pathway

Preliminary experiments demonstrated that NPAIE-MSA effectively generates ROS in vitro. Using the ROS specific fluorescent probe DCFH-DA, we assessed intracellular ROS levels in cells treated with NPAIE-MSA + US. Flow cytometry analysis quantified the intracellular ROS fluorescence signal in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated cells at 2.4 × 105, approximately 24-fold higher than that in NPAIE-MSA-treated cells (Figure S6). CLSM results further showed a marked increase in fluorescence intensity in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated cells compared to both untreated and NPAIE-MSA-treated cells, indicating higher intracellular ROS levels (Fig. 3A). In addition, three-channel images visually demonstrated colocalization, yielding a Pearson’s R > 0.94 (Fig. 3B). Together, these observations confirmed that ultrasound-activated ROS bursts were precisely localized to regions of NPAIE-MSA accumulation.

Intracellular ROS can lead to mitochondrial and DNA damage. Mitochondrial membrane potential changes were evaluated using the JC-1-based Mitochondrial Membrane Potential Assay Kit, where a shift in JC-1 fluorescence from red to green signifies a decrease in membrane potential, serving as an early indicator of apoptosis. CLSM showed that cells treated with PBS, MSA-2, and NPAIE-MSA emitted bright red fluorescence, while those treated with NPAIE-MSA + US had a marked reduction in red fluorescence and displayed bright green fluorescence, suggesting ROS-induced mitochondrial dysfunction (Fig. 3C and D). The γ-H2AX level is a key marker of DNA damage. Nuclear γ-H2AX levels were measured to assess DNA damage in cancer cells treated with NPAIE-MSA + US. CLSM results indicated that NPAIE-MSA + US-treated cells displayed stronger nuclear fluorescence compared to MSA-2 and NPAIE-MSA-treated cells, signifying increased DNA damage in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated cells (Figure S7).

Since NPAIE-MSA could release the STING agonist MSA-2 under US conditions, to investigate NPAIE-MSA + US’s effect on cGAS-STING pathway activation in vitro, phosphorylated STING protein expression was assessed via immunofluorescence staining. Western blot analysis showed that NPAIE-MSA + US significantly upregulated p-TBK1, p-IRF-3 and p-STING proteins in 4T1 cells (Fig. 3E and F). Similarly, NPAIE-MSA + US also enhanced p-STING protein expression in MCF-7 cells (Figure S8). Subsequent CLSM revealed that MSA-2 and NPAIE-MSA + US treatments resulted in significant red fluorescence (Fig. 3G and H), indicating effective STING phosphorylation in 4T1 cells. After confirming STING pathway activation by NPAIE-MSA + US, its impact on dendritic cells (DCs) maturation was evaluated. Co-culturing NPAIE-MSA + US-treated 4T1 cells with mouse bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) and subsequent flow cytometry analysis demonstrated that NPAIE-MSA + US treatment resulted in the highest level of BMDC maturation, approximately 60% (Fig. 3G-I). Furthermore, BMDCs exposed to NPAIE-MSA + US exhibited significantly increased p-STING protein expression (Figure S9). These results suggest that exposure of NPAIE-MSA to US induces mitochondrial and DNA damage in cancer cells, leading to the release of MSA-2, which activates the cGAS-STING pathway, thereby enhancing inflammatory and immune responses.

To elucidate the effects of NPAIE-MSA on DCs more comprehensively, we first investigated the endocytic uptake of NPAIE-MSA by DC2.4 cells using flow cytometry and CLSM. The results revealed distinct cytoplasmic fluorescence after 4 h of co-incubation (Figure S10A), with signal intensity increasing over time, indicating rapid internalization by 4T1 cells and a clear time-dependent endocytic behavior (Figure S10B, C). Second, apoptosis assays demonstrated that NPAIE-MSA combined with ultrasound (NPAIE-MSA + US) induced an apoptosis rate of approximately 50% in DC2.4 cells, which was roughly four times higher than the rate observed with MSA-2 or NPAIE-MSA alone (~ 16%) (Figure S10D, E). Finally, flow cytometric analysis of DC2.4 cells following NPAIE-MSA + US treatment assessed their activation status. The results indicated that any treatment group containing MSA-2 induced a DC2.4 maturation rate of approximately 45% (Figure S10F, G). Collectively, these findings demonstrated that NPAIE-MSA was taken up by DCs and that the released STING agonist (MSA-2) could induce DCs maturation.

99mTc-NPAIE-MSA Enable Dual-Modal Tumor Imaging

99mTc is a pivotal radioisotope in nuclear medicine, enabling early bone metastasis detection via SPECT/CT with superior biodistribution tracking, yet its clinical utility is limited by isotope dissociation and poor tumor targeting in conventional carriers. To address these, we designed and synthesized a DOTA-based polymeric monomer (DOTA-OH) for 99mTc loading, confirmed via 1H NMR spectroscopy (Scheme S3, Figure S11). DOTA-OH was copolymerized with HPMDA and RSM through condensation polymerization, followed by mPEG5k-OH end-capping, resulting in a ROS-responsive polymer with DOTA integrated into the backbone (Poly(HPMDA-RSM-DOTA)-PEG, Polymer-DOTA), confirmed via 1H NMR spectroscopy (Scheme S4, Figure S12, S13). 99mTc binds effectively to Polymer-DOTA, forming a stable radiolabeled polymer (Poly(HPMDA-RSM-DOTA-99mTc)-PEG, 99mTc-RSP) suitable for SPECT/CT imaging (Fig. 4A). This innovation mitigates isotope dissociation risks and addresses the limitations of conventional 99mTc labeling, such as insufficient tumor accumulation due to off-target effects or premature carrier degradation. 99mTc-RSP can self-assemble with RSP-AIE and MSA-2 into nanoparticles (99mTc-NPAIE-MSA) for SPECT/CT and NIR-II imaging. SPECT/CT results show that 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA significantly enhances tumor targeting and retention of 99mTc (Fig. 4B). In tumor-bearing mice, intravenously administered free Na99mTcO4 displayed a short half-life, primarily accumulating in the thyroid with minimal tumor presence. In contrast, 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA extended the biological half-life of 99mTc and preferentially accumulated at tumor sites, with detectable signals within 0.5 h post-injection and strong radioactive signals persisting for up to 12 h (Fig. 4C). The results showed that the blood half-life of Na99mTcO4 was far shorter than 15 min, whereas 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA substantially prolonged the blood half-life of 99mTc to approximately 30 min (Fig. 4D). This enhancement is due to the ROS-responsive polymer and optimized nanostructure, improving drug delivery, tumor selectivity, and sustained imaging. These results demonstrate that the Polymer-DOTA design effectively stabilizes 99mTc loading, addressing stability issues and enhancing tumor accumulation compared to conventional 99mTc labeling methods.

Since NPAIE-MSA retains the characteristic absorption and emission of AIE molecules in the near-infrared region (Fig. 1E), to evaluate the NIR-II imaging capability and tumor-targeted accumulation of NPAIE-MSA in vivo, we administered NPAIE-MSA to 4T1 tumor-bearing mice and monitored fluorescence using an NIR-II imaging system. Blood pharmacokinetic analysis revealed an elimination half-life of approximately 9 h for NPAIE-MSA in circulation (Figure S14), demonstrating a markedly prolonged blood residence time of the AIE moiety. In addition, strong fluorescence was detected at the tumor periphery within 1-hour post-injection. Over time, the fluorescence intensified and permeated the entire tumor, demonstrating that NPAIE-MSA effectively penetrated the tumor vasculature and accumulated specifically in tumor tissues (Fig. 4E). This confirms the NIR-II system’s ability to detect signals from both peripheral and deep tumor regions, underscoring NPAIE-MSA’s potential as an NIR-II probe for high signal-to-noise ratio and deep-tissue imaging.

The fluorescent signal at the tumor site peaked at 12 h and remained strong through 84 h (Fig. 4F), confirming the prolonged retention properties of NPAIE-MSA. This retention supported sustained diagnosis and therapy and enabled a spatiotemporally complementary multimodal imaging strategy with SPECT/CT. Specifically, 99mTc-SPECT, because of its ultrahigh sensitivity, accurately traced the early targeted dynamics of nanoparticles during the first 12 h after injection, which corresponded to the critical EPR window, and its quantitative biodistribution measurements served as the gold standard for evaluating targeting efficiency. After 99mTc decay, NIR-II imaging continued to visualize tumor retention for up to 84 h, providing mid- to long-term surveillance. Ex vivo imaging showed tumor fluorescence intensity that was significantly higher than that of non-target organs (Fig. 4G and H), which corroborated the in vivo results and supported a dual hepatic/renal clearance mechanism. As a clinically translatable radionuclide and a pure γ-emitter with low radiation, 99mTc had a short half-life that matched the early targeting study window, while the chemical stability of the NIR-II probe ensured reliable subsequent monitoring. The concordant dual-modal data mutually validated each other and emphasized the nanoparticle platform’s efficient tumor accumulation.

NPAIE-MSA + US-mediated sonodynamic anticancer effect on a 4T1 cancer model

To evaluate the therapeutic efficacy of NPAIE-MSA + US in the 4T1 tumor model, mice bearing tumors of approximately 100 mm³ were stratified into five groups (n = 5) and subjected to treatments with PBS, PBS + US, MSA-2, MSA-2 + US, NPAIE-MSA, and NPAIE-MSA + US. In the NPAIE-MSA + US group, mice were exposed to US therapy (1 W, 1 MHz, 50% duty cycle, 1 min) 24 h post NPAIE-MSA injection (Fig. 5A). Tumor volumes were tracked throughout the study. The control group experienced rapid tumor growth, with volumes averaging 980 mm3 by day 12. PBS + US, MSA-2, MSA-2 + US, NPAIE-MSA groups showed modest tumor suppression, achieving inhibition rates of 0.04% (~ 940 mm3), 25% (~ 740 mm3), 35% (630 mm3) and 49% (500 mm3), respectively. In contrast, NPAIE-MSA + US treatment markedly impeded tumor growth, reducing tumor volume to approximately 250 mm3 by day 12, corresponding to an inhibition rate of around 75% (Fig. 5B and C). No notable changes in body weight were observed across all groups (Fig. 5D). On day 12 post-treatment, tumor specimens from each group were excised, weighed, and imaged. The mean tumor weights for the PBS, PBS + US, MSA-2, MSA-2 + US, and NPAIE-MSA groups were approximately 0.95 g, 0.89 g, 0.59 g, 0.54 g, and 0.38 g, respectively, while the NPAIE-MSA + US group exhibited an average weight of about 0.30 g (Fig. 5E and F). To assess the in vivo biosafety of NPAIE-MSA with ultrasound activation, we monitored changes in body weight and performed necropsies on one randomly selected mouse per group to examine major organs after treatment. Body weights did not differ significantly between groups (Fig. 5D). Serum biochemical indices remained within normal ranges, and hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of major organs showed no apparent tissue damage (Figure S15, S16). Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining and TUNEL assays were performed to assess necrosis and apoptosis. H&E staining indicated significant nuclear condensation and DNA damage in the NPAIE-MSA + US-treated tumors, confirming a high apoptotic rate (Fig. 5G). These findings demonstrate that NPAIE-MSA has antitumor effects when activated by US.

NPAIE-MSA + US-mediated SDT reshaped the anti-tumor immune microenvironment

To investigate the immunomodulatory mechanisms behind NPAIE-MSA + US’s therapeutic efficacy, tumor tissues and tumor-draining lymph nodes (TDLNs) were collected from 4T1 tumor-bearing mice treated with PBS, MSA-2, NPAIE-MSA, or NPAIE-MSA + US. Immune responses were examined using immunofluorescence staining and flow cytometry. Immunohistochemical and fluorescence imaging of tumor sections showed stronger signals in the NPAIE-MSA + US-treated group compared to those treated with PBS or MSA-2, indicating enhanced the expression of p-STING protein and infiltration of CD8+ T cell within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 6A). These findings suggest that the observed immune response in the murine model was instigated by the activation of the cGAS-STING pathway.

We assessed key indicators of effective adaptive immune responses, focusing on infiltrating CD8+ T cells, myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), antigen-presenting cells (APCs), and macrophages (Fig. 6B-K). Flow cytometry revealed that NPAIE-MSA + US-treated mice exhibited increased proportions of CD8+ T cells and APCs in tumors, at 36.2% and 22.9%, respectively—approximately 1.3-fold and 2-fold higher than the PBS group (Fig. 6B,C,H and Figure S17, S18). MDSCs, known for their role in tumor immune evasion, were significantly reduced in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated mice (43.9%) compared to PBS-treated mice (60.5%) (Fig. 6D and E and Figure S18). Further analysis showed that in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated mice, M1-TAMs and M2-TAMs were approximately 50.2% and 15.56%, respectively, yielding an M1-TAMs/M2-TAMs ratio of about 3.7 (Fig. 6F,G and I and Figure S19), which is approximately 3.7-fold higher than in the PBS-treated model.

TDLNs play a crucial role in antitumor immunity. Our analysis focused on the changes in mature dendritic cells (mDCs) within these nodes. Flow cytometry showed that mDCs in NPAIE-MSA + US-treated mice increased significantly to about 77.2%, nearly 3.3 times higher than in the PBS-treated group (Fig. 6J and Figure S20). The spleen, an essential immune organ, coordinates immune suppression. Flow cytometry revealed that NPAIE-MSA + US-treated mice had a significantly higher proportion of splenic memory T cells, particularly central memory T cells (TCM), reaching approximately 13.3%, compared to 6.5% in PBS-treated controls (Fig. 6K and Figure S21). This observation suggests the potential of NPAIE-MSA + US treatment to augment the production and persistence of memory T cells in the spleen, which is essential for sustained antitumor immune responses. In conclusion, NPAIE-MSA + US treatment could activate the cGAS-STING pathway, enhance CD8+ T cell infiltration and antigen presentation in the tumor microenvironment, decrease MDSC proportions, weaken their capacity to inhibit T cell function, and trigger enduring protective immunity in the TDLNs and spleens.

Discussion

This study presents the successful development of an US-responsive polymer nanoplatform, denoted as 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA, which integrates sonodynamic therapy, immunotherapy, and multimodal imaging. This platform offers a novel approach for precise diagnosis and treatment of breast cancer. The incorporation of Tc radionuclide enables SPECT/CT and NIR-II fluorescence imaging, enhancing tumor localization accuracy and real-time monitoring capabilities during treatment. This feature serves as a pivotal factor in clinical decision-making. Furthermore, the polymer RSP-AIE, characterized by its AIE properties, generates abundant ROS upon US stimulation. This mechanism leads to direct induction of tumor cell apoptosis and augments anti-tumor immune responses by activating the cGAS-STING innate immune pathway, effectively suppressing tumor progression.

NPAIE-MSA demonstrates effective cellular internalization and US-triggered ROS production, leading to substantial cytotoxicity and apoptosis. Studies investigating its mechanism reveal that ROS-induced DNA and mitochondrial damage are the primary pathways for inducing cell death, while the activated cGAS-STING pathway enhances antigen presentation and activates T cells. In preclinical models, the NPAIE-MSA + US treatment group shows remarkable inhibition of tumor growth, with a tumor volume inhibition rate of nearly 75%, and no significant weight loss is observed, indicating the treatment’s safety profile. Furthermore, immunohistochemical and flow cytometry analyses validate that NPAIE-MSA + US treatment significantly boosts CD8+ T cell infiltration and the presence of antigen-presenting cells in the tumor microenvironment, while decreasing the proportion of MDSCs, thereby enhancing the immune milieu within the tumor.

This study confirms the efficacy and safety of the 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA nanoplatform for breast cancer diagnosis and treatment. It also offers insights for the advancement of nano-diagnostic agents utilizing AIE polymers and STING pathways. Subsequent research will delve into the nanoplatform’s applicability in other types of tumors and refine its pharmacokinetics and biodistribution profiles to facilitate clinical implementation. In summary, this study significantly contributes to the progress of precise tumor diagnosis and therapy.

Methods

Synthesis of compound 3

P-nitrophenol (1.39 g, 1 mmol) and potassium carbonate (1.38 g, 1 mmol) were dissolved in anhydrous N, N-dimethylformamide (DMF) (200 mL) and stirred for 10 min. Subsequently, 3-bromo-1-propanol (1.39 g, 1 mmol) was dissolved in DMF (50mL) and added dropwise to the reaction mixture. The reaction was heated to reflux at 140℃ for 6 h. Synthesis of RSM

The RSM was prepared with slight modifications from a previously reported procedure. 

Synthesis of Poly-(HPMDA-RSM-AIE)-PEG, RSP-AIE

Under nitrogen protection, the RSM (68 mg, 0.34 mmol), HPMDA (60 mg, 0.27 mmol) and AIE monomer (200 mg, 0.14 mmol) was dissolved in ultra-dry DMF (10 mL), and stirred at 35℃ for 48 h. Subsequently, polyethylene glycol monomethyl ether-5000 (mPEG5k-OH, 240 mg, 0.05 mmol) was added and stirred at 40℃ for 24 h. Following the reaction, all solutions in the system were transferred to dialysis bags, dialyzed for over 48 h to eliminate the DMF solvent, and vacuum-dried to obtain ROS-sensitive polymers Poly-(HPMDA-RSM-AIE)-PEG, RSP-AIE. The 1H NMR spectrum of RSP-AIE was presented in Figure S3.

Synthesis of DOTA-OH

Dissolve 3-Amino-1,2-propanediol (182 mg, 2 mmol) and triethylamine (2 mL) in DMF (10 mL), then slowly add to a DMF (10 mL) solution containing DOTA-tris(tBu)ester NHS ester (DOTA-NHS, 1340 mg, 2 mmol), and stirred at 35℃ for 24 h. DOTA-OH was purified by column chromatography. 1H NMR (300 MHz, Chloroform-d) δ 7.05 (s, 1H), 3.86–3.22 (m, 10 H), 2.70 (s, 24 H), 1.46 (s, 27 H).

Synthesis of Poly(HPMDA-RSM-DOTA)-PEG, Polymer-DOTA

Under nitrogen protection, the RSM (627.4 mg, 3.2 mmol), DOTA-OH (258.2 mg, 0.4 mmol), and HPMDA (896.2 mg, 4 mmol) was dissolved in ultra-dry DMF (15 mL), and stirred at 50℃ for 48 h. Subsequently, mPEG5k-OH (400 mg, 0.8 mmol) was added and stirred at 40℃ for 24 h. Following the reaction, all solutions in the system were transferred to dialysis bags, dialyzed for over 48 h to eliminate the DMF solvent, and vacuum-dried to obtain Poly(HPMDA-RSM-DOTA)-PEG, Polymer-DOTA.

Preparation and characterization of NPAIE-MSA

RSP-AIE (100 mg) and MSA-2 (10 mg) in DMF (1 mL) was added dropwise into distillated water (10 mL) for 10 min. The organic solvent was removed by dialysis (Mw = 3500 Da). The supernatant solution (NPAIE-MSA) was collected by centrifugation (5000 rpm, 5 min) using a TDZ5-WS centrifuge. The amount of incorporated AIE monomer was quantified by UV absorption spectroscopy and the amount of incorporated MSA-2 complex was quantified by HPLC, where the ratio of the mass concentration of AIE to the molar concentration of MSA-2 is approximately 1:1.

Preparation of ⁹⁹mTc-NPAIE-MSA

To preparate 99mTc-NPAIE-MSA, RSP-AIE (10 mg), Polymer-DOTA (10 mg) and MSA-2 (2 mg) in DMF (2 mL) was added dropwise into distillated water (20 mL) for 10 min. The organic solvent was removed by dialysis (Mw = 3500 Da). The supernatant solution was collected by centrifugation (5000 rpm, 5 min) using a TDZ5-WS centrifuge, and labeled with Na99mTcO4 to form ⁹⁹mTc-NPAIE-MSA. Labeling procedure and Quality control:

Stannous chloride (SnCl2, 1 mg) was dissolved in 2 mL hydrochloric acid (0.1 M) to create a stable stock solution containing divalent tin ions. Subsequently, 2 mL of a well-characterized weakly acidic nanoparticle suspension (pH ~ 5–6) was taken for pH compatibility assessment to ensure the acid–base environment was optimally suited for 99mTc labelling. To confirm this, 100 µL of the aforementioned stannous chloride solution was added to the nanoparticle suspension and thoroughly mixed by vertexing. Following this, 5 mCi of sodium pertechnetate (Na99mTcO4, roughly 150 µL) was introduced into the mixture, which was then incubated in a 45 °C water bath for 30 min to finalize the labelling process. Lastly, quality control was executed through radio-thin-layer chromatography and radio-high-performance liquid chromatography to validate the efficiency and stability of the labelling.

In vitro ROS monitoring

The efficacy of the reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation was quantified using the specific ROS scavenger 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran according to a previous protocol. The NPAIE-MSA (10 µg/mL of AIE monomer) and DPBF (10 µM) solved in water. The samples were then exposed to ultrasound (1 W, 1 MHz). The decomposition of the probe was time-dependently recorded at 415 nm using a UV/VIS absorption spectrometer.

The ability of NPAIE-MSA to produce ROS under ultrasonic conditions was confirmed through ESR analysis. A mixture containing TEMP (3 µL) and NPAIE-MSA (100 µL, 10 µg/mL) was sonicated for 1 min at 1 W, 1 MHz. The identification of singlet oxygen (1O2) generated by NPAIE-MSA under ultrasonic conditions was accomplished using ESR spectroscopy. For comparative analysis, a control group containing pure water and TEMP underwent the same ultrasonic treatment.

In vitro detection of MSA-2 releases

The morphology of NPAIE-MSA nanoparticles following ultrasonic treatment (1 W, 1 MHz, 1 min) was examined using TEM and compared with untreated samples. To assess drug release in response to ultrasound, 1 mL of post-ultrasonic treated NPAIE-MSA solution (1 W, 1 MHz, 1 min) was introduced into a dialysis (Mw = 1000 Da) and submerged in a beaker with 100 mL of water. Samples were withdrawn from the dialysis bag at specified intervals and the MSA-2 concentration was determined using HPLC. A drug release profile was generated based on the concentration variations relative to the initial MSA-2 concentration in the NPAIE-MSA solution.

Cell culture

The mouse breast cancer (4T1) cells and breast cancer cells (MCF-7) were cultured in 1640 media supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. All cell lines were obtained from the National Collection of Authenticated Cell Cultures and imodels (Beijing) Biotechnology, and cultured at 37 °C and 5% CO2.

In vitro cellular uptake of NPAIE-MSA.

Intracellular uptake of NPAIE-MSA was assessed using flow cytometry and CLSM. 4T1 cells were exposed to NPAIE-MSA (10 µg/mL AIE monomer) for 1, 4, 7 h. Then the cells were assessed by flow cytometry. In addition, the cells were treated with FITC Phalloidin as per the manufacturer’s instructions. The cells were then stained with DAPI and examined using CLSM.

In vitro cytotoxicity study

MTT assay was utilized to examine the viabilities of NPAIE-MSA on cancer cells (4T1, MCF-7). Cells were then incubated with MSA-2 and NPAIE-MSA at final concentrations ranging from 0.05, 0.5, 5, 10, 20, and 40 µM based on MSA-2 for 48 h. Cells were incubated with NPAIE-MSA at final concentrations ranging from 0.05, 0.5, 5, 10, 20, and 40 µg/mL based on AIE monomer for 6 h, and irradiated with ultrasound. MTT was added in the wells for another 4 h, 10% SDS was then added for another 12 h.

ROS generation in cancer cells

4T1 cells (2 × 104 cells per well) were seeded on 24-well plates, and the cells were incubated with the NPAIE-MSA (10 µg/mL AIE monomer) for 6 h. The cells were further incubated with the ROS specific fluorescent probe for 0.5 h. The cells were exposed to ultrasound (1 W, 1 MHz, 1 min). The ROS production inside the cells was assessed using CLSM and flow cytometry.

Detection of DNA damage hallmarks – γ-H2AX by CLSM

4T1 cells (2 × 104 cells per well) were seeded on 24-well plates, and the cells were incubated with the NPAIE-MSA (10 µg/mL AIE monomer) for 6 h. The cells were then subjected ultrasound irradiation (1 W, 1 MHz, 1 min). Finally, immunofluorescence staining was performed using a γ-H2AX detection kit, and the cytoplasmic fluorescence distribution was visualized using CLSM.

Optical system for NIR-II fluorescence

Female BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks old) were used to establish a syngeneic tumor model by subcutaneously injecting 2 × 106 4T1 cells into the right hindlimb. When tumor volumes reached approximately 300 mm3, animals were enrolled in experiments, with n = 3 per group. Tumor-bearing mice received an intravenous injection of NPAIE-MSA at a dose corresponding to 5 mg/kg of AIE monomer. Fluorescence intensity of the AIE monomer was monitored with an NIR-II animal imaging system at multiple time points post-injection (1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 24, 36, 48, 72, and 84 h). Additionally, the biodistribution was assessed 84 h post-injection by euthanizing the animals and analyzing the fluorescence of the AIE monomer through NIR-Ⅱ animal imaging. Prior to optical imaging, fur over the tumor and surrounding area was uniformly removed to minimize signal attenuation and scattering. NIR-II fluorescence imaging parameters: excitation wavelength 808 nm, emission collection at 1000 nm, exposure time 500 ms. Image analysis of all NIR-II fluorescence images was conducted using ImageJ software.

In vivo anticancer evaluation

Tumor-bearing mice were randomly separated into 4 groups (n = 5): (1) PBS; (2) MSA-2 (3 mg kg− 1); (3) NPAIE-MSA (10 mg kg− 1 AIE monomer); (4) NPAIE-MSA (10 mg kg− 1 AIE monomer) + US (1 W, 1 MHz, 5 min). Tumor volume was calculated by the following formula: Volume = (Length * Width2) / 2. The tumors were collected and a slice of each one was fixated with 4% paraformaldehyde. The obtained slices were stained with H&E or TUNEL. To assess p-STING expression, tumor slices were subjected to immunohistochemical analysis with an anti-p-STING antibody. Tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells were characterized using anti-mouse CD8a antibodies. The immune response was further investigated by digesting tumor tissue and analyzing dissociated cells via flow cytometry. All the antibodies used above were purchased from BioLegend. FCM data acquisition was performed with CytExpert software, and the data were processed using FlowJo software.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis of the entire dataset was conducted using GraphPad Prism 8.0.1.
