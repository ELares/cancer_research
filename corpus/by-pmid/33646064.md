Perspectives on PARP inhibitors as pharmacotherapeutic strategies for breast cancer

Introduction

Approximately 10% of all breast cancer cases occur in individuals who have germline pathogenic variants of the BRCA 1, BRCA 2, and other genes associated with impaired DNA damage repair that is associated with an increased risk of breast, ovarian, and other cancers. Inhibitors of poly-ADP ribose polymerase (PARP) induce synthetic lethality in cancer cells harboring such pathogenic variants.

Area covered

In this review, the authors review the mechanisms of action, antitumor activity, and adverse events associated with PARP inhibitors for the treatment of advanced breast cancer. The authors then summarize the area and provide their expert perspectives on the area.

Expert opinion

Two PARP inhibitors are approved in metastatic breast cancer, including olaparib and talozaparib. Both agents were approved based on phase III trials demonstrating that they were associated with improved progression-free survival compared with treatment of physician’s choice in patients receiving second-third line therapy for locally advanced, inoperable or metastatic breast cancer in patients with germline pathogenic BRCA 1 or BRCA2 variants.

Introduction

Poly-ADP-ribose polymerase (PARP) comprises group of nuclear proteins that are activated upon binding to damaged DNA and are involved multiple repair processes. Mechanisms of DNA damage and their main repair pathways are summarized in Table 1. The main function of PARP proteins is to detect single strand breaks (SSB) or double strand breaks (DSB) in DNA, recruit DNA repair machinery, and stabilize replication forks during DNA repair. At least 17 distinct proteins have been identified as members of the PARP family in mammalian cells; however, only PARP-1, PARP-2, and PARP-3 are known to function in DNA repair .

As shown in Figure 1A), PARP proteins include an amino terminal domain involved in DNA binding, an auto-modification/ribosylation domain involved in protein-protein interactions, and a C-terminal catalytic domain involved in transfer of ADP-ribose from NAD+ to protein acceptors, forming poly-ADP-ribose (pADP), which has two-fold higher charge density than DNA .

Function of PARP inhibitors and synthetic lethality

As depicted in Figure 1B), a major function of PARP proteins is generation of poly (ADP -ribose) chains, which in-turn facilitate the chromatin rectification and lay on the protein complexes critical to DNA repair.They are involved in the slowing of the replication fork speed during repair of the damaged DNA during HR mediated repair, a critical step to reduce the replication stress and protect genomic stability.  The PARP inhibitors bind at the same location, at the catalytic center of the enzyme, to block the binding of its substrate, nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+), and prevent poly(ADP-ribose) production. Also the PARP inhibitor induces allosteric changes in the PARP 1 structure inhibiting PARP autophosphorylation. As a result, the PARP1 is trapped on the DNA lesion, forming a toxic complex which in turn stalls the progression of replication forks. Upon PARP inhibition, the DNA SSBs persist, eventually resulting in DSBs. In normal cells, accumulated DSBs are repaired by error- free homologous recombination (HR), where BRCA 1/2 are mainly involved, or by error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ). As depicted in Figure 1C), PARP inhibitors induce “synthetic lethality” in breast cancers occurring in individuals with germline pathogenic variants of the BRCA1 or BRCA2 gene (gBRCA+), resulting in impaired homologous recombination, which in turn leads to aggregation of DSBs and eventual cell death. Although the knowledge of the biological mechanism behind synthetic lethality is still incomplete the discovery of this natural interdependence between BRCA and PARP initiated the research of PARP inhibition as treatment for cancers with BRCA1/2 defects.

HRD beyond BRCA mutation

A case-control analysis of gene panel in 60,000 women with breast cancer revealed mutation of twelve genes displayed a significant association (p<0.05) with breast cancer. Nine of these genes, including ATM, RAD51D, NF1, and MSH6, were associated with moderate risk (RR>2.0) of breast cancer, and three genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2) were associated with high risk (RR>5.0) of breast cancer. Although tumors that are associated with germline BRCA 1/2 mutations (gBRCA+) are known to respond to PARP inhibitors, recent studies showed that patients with breast, ovarian and prostate cancer who do not carry germline BRCA 1/2 mutations also benefited from PARP inhibitors. Homologous recombination repair is a multifactorial process that involves many proteins beyond BRCA1 and BRCA2. The precise regulation and factors involved in these multiple steps are still unknown and multiple different mutations can lead to loss of BRCA1 or BRCA2 function. Analyses of tumor-derived genome sequences have revealed that loss of BRCA1 or BRCA2 yields a distinct pattern of base-substitution mutations, termed “Signature 3”, and further investigation showed that germline nonsense and frameshift variants in PALB2, but not ATM or CHEK2, and epigenetic silencing of RAD51C and BRCA1 by promoter methylation are associated with signature 3.. Other suggested pathogenesis in homologous recombination repair defect include telomeric allelic imbalance, loss of heterozygosity (LOH) and large scale transition score, and total number of coding mutations. Currently, it is not well elucidated in predicting which HRD will respond to PARP inhibitors. Preclinical studies with breast cancer cells have demonstrated PARP inhibitor sensitivity in deficiency of RAD51, RAD54, DSS1, RPA1, NBS1, ATR, ATM, CHK1, CHK2, FANCD2, FANCA, or FANCC . Numerous studies are underway to define which HRD would be a target of PARP inhibitor as monotherapy or in combination with other targets. Moreover multiple targeting agents against DNA damage response are in development .

Breast cancers occurring in patients having pathogenic BRCA1 or BRCA2 variants are also known to exhibit sensitivity to platinum agents, though not via a synthetic lethality mechanism. Several studies have evaluated efficacy of platinum in patients with germline BRCA mutations. A phase 2 trial showed 80% response rate among 20 patients with metastatic breast cancer and BRCA1 mutation who received cisplatin. Carboplatin was evaluated among patients with metastatic triple negative breast cancer and harboring BRCA 1 and 2 mutation (TNT trial) . The trial included 376 subjects with metastatic TNBC who were randomly assigned to receive carboplatin (AUC 6 every 3 weeks) or docetaxel (75 mg/m2 every 3 weeks), with crossover to the other agent at disease progression; the primary trial endpoint was objective response rate (ORR). Although the ORR were similar for the two arms in the overall population (31.4% versus 34.0%, respectively; P = 0.66), carboplatin was more effective than docetaxel among 43 patients with gBRCA+ (68% vs. 33% ORR, respectively; biomarker, treatment interaction P = 0.01). Such benefit was not observed for subjects with tumors associated with BRCA1 methylation (N=33), low BRCA1 mRNA expression (N=31), high HRD score in the Myriad HRD assay (N=86), or “basal” tumors by the PAM50 gene expression assay (N=170). Therefore, although tumors occurring in patients with gBRCA+ exhibit greater sensitivity to platinum agents, there are currently no assays that reliably identify other subsets of TNBC that share many the molecular features of BRCA1-associated cancers and sensitivity to platinum. As described in subsequent sections of the review, patients who have platinum-resistant cancer exhibit resistance to PARP inhibitors.

Clinical Development of PARP Inhibitors

PARP inhibitors first came into attention in 2003 in combination with DNA-damaging cytotoxic agents on the basis of preclinical data showing both chemopotentiation with this class of compounds. In 2005, the preclinical data showed the concept of synthetic lethality between BRCA1/2 genetic defects and pharmacologic PARP inhibition, and these data suggested that there may be single-agent activity with PARP inhibitors. Given these potential effects olaparib entered in the clinical trials as a single agent in 2005. The olaparib and rucaparib showed promising data in ovarian cancer patients carrying gBRCA+, leading to the first U.S. Food and Drug Administration FDA approval in 2014 and 2016, respectively. Four PARP inhibitors are currently approved for clinical use in breast, ovarian, pancreas, and prostate cancer this review was written, including olaparib, talazoparib, rucaparib, and niraparib; veliparib has also been extensively studied in breast cancer in phase III trials, but is not currently approved. Currently olaparib and talazoparib are approved by FDA as single agent in use for metastatic breast cancer and gBRCA+ based on two phase 3 clinical trials. Olaparib

Monotherapy.

Olaparib is the first approved PARP inhibitor in breast cancer. A phase 1 trial in 60 patients with disease that was refractory to standard therapies or for which there were no suitable effective standard treatments (9 patients with breast cancer) determined the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) to be 400mg twice daily with antitumor activity was seen only in individuals with gBRCA+. A proof-of-concept phase 2 trial evaluating two different doses (400mg vs 100mg twice daily) in individuals with gBRCA+ and advanced breast cancer revealed an overall response rate (ORR) of 41% (11 of 27) for the 400mg cohort and 22% (6 of 27) for the 100mg cohort . A subsequent phase 2 trial with olaparib monotherapy (400mg twice daily) in 298 patients with gBRCA+ and advanced solid cancers including breast cancer who received ≥ 3 chemotherapy showed response rate of 12.9% (8 of 62) among patients with breast cancer . Another phase 2 trial of olaparib (400mg twice daily) among 91 patients with advanced ovarian cancer (n=65) or triple negative breast cancer (TNBC) (n=26, of whom 10 had gBRCA+) did not show objective response in breast cancer . Adverse events in this study included fatigue (any grade, 70% with ovarian cancer and 50% with breast cancer), nausea (any grade, 66% and 62%), vomiting (any grade, 39% and 35%), and decreased appetite (any grade, 36% and 27%).

The OlympiAD trial was a randomized phase 3 study in 302 patients with HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer and germline pathogenic BRCA 1 or 2 variants who were randomized 2:1 to receive either olaparib (oral, 300 mg, twice daily) or standard therapy with one of prespecified chemotherapy regimens (treatment of physician’s choice, TPC): capecitabine (oral, 2500mg/m2 divided into two doses for 14 days, repeat every 21 days), eribulin (IV, 1.4mg/m2, day 1 and day 8, repeat every 21 days) or vinorelbine (IV, 30mg/m2 day 1 and day 8, repeat every 21 days). The primary trial endpoint was progression-free survival (PFS), which was assessed by blinded independent central review and was analyzed on an intention-to-treat basis. Crossover to olaparib was not permitted. Results demonstrated significant improvement of median PFS (7.0 months in olaparib arm vs. 4.2 months in TPC arm; hazard ratio (HR) 0.58, 95% confidence interval (CI), 0.43 – 0.80; p<0.001) and ORR (59.9% vs 28.8%) . The overall survivor (OS) did not differ significantly between the two arms (HR, 0.90; 95% CI, 0.63–1.29; p=0.57). Follow up analysis was reported at 64% data maturity, and the final OS result did not show statistically significant benefit with olaparib compared to TPC (19.3 months vs 17.1 months, HR 0.90, 95% CI 0.66–1.23; p= 0.513). HR for OS with olaparib versus TPC in prespecified subgroups were: no prior chemotherapy: 0.51 (95% CI 0.29–0.90); prior chemotherapy: 1.13 (0.79–1.64); triple negative: 0.93 (0.62–1.43); hormone receptor positive: 0.86 (0.55–1.36); prior platinum use: 0.83 (0.49–1.45) and no prior platinum use: 0.91 (0.64–1.33). While there was no statistically significant improvement in OS with olaparib compared to TPC in overall population, subgroup analysis suggested a greater OS benefit among patients who had not received prior chemotherapy (22.6 months vs 14.7 months, HR 0.51 (95% CI 0.29–0.90; p=0.02). Safety data of OlympiAD showed that most adverse events (AE) in olaparib arm were grade 1 or 2, and the rate of grade 3 or higher was lower in the olaparib group compared to TCP arm (36.6% and 50.5%, respectively). Anemia (grade≥ 3, 16.1% in olaparib group vs 4.4% with standard TPC), nausea (any grade, 58.0% vs 35.2%), vomiting (any grade, 29.8% vs 15.4%), fatigue (any grade 28.8% vs 23.1%), headache (any grade, 20% vs 15.4%), and cough (any grade, 17.1% vs 6.6%) occurred more frequently in the olaparib arm. Neutropenia (grade ≥3, 9.3% vs 26.4%), palmar–plantar erythrodysesthesia (any grade, 0.5% vs 20.9%), and an increase in liver-function enzymes (any grade, 20.5% vs 34.1%) were more frequent in TPC arm. The incidence of treatment discontinuation owing to any grade AE was 4.9% in olaparib arm and 7.7% in TPC arm. AE leading to treatment discontinuation in the olaparib arm were anemia (2.0%), decreased platelet count (1.0%), increased intracranial pressure, abdominal pain, dyspnea, erythema nodosum, and thrombocytopenia (all 0.5%). AE leading to discontinuation in TPC arm were anemia (2.2%) neutropenia (2.2%), leukopenia, decreased neutrophil count, radiation skin injury, palmar-plantar erythrodysesthesia syndrome, peripheral motor neuropathy and vomiting (all 1.1%). Dose reduction due to AE was 25.4% in the olaparib and 30.8% in the TPC. Dose reduction due to adverse event was most commonly due to anemia in the olaparib arm (13.7%) and palmar-plantar erythrodysethesia in the TPC arm (7.7%). Treatment interruption or delay was 35.1% in the olaparib arm and 27.5% in the TPC arm. Patient reported outcome also favored olaparib. QLQ-C30 at baseline was 63.2±21.0 in the olaparib arm and 63.3±21.2 in the standard-therapy arm. The adjusted mean (±SE) change from baseline across all time points was 3.9±1.2 in the olaparib arm and −3.6±2.2 in the standard-therapy arm, with a corresponding estimated difference of 7.5 points (95% CI, 2.5 to 12.4; P=0.004). The median time to a clinically meaningful decrease in QLQ-C30 score (≥10 points) was not reached in the olaparib arm and was 15.3 months in the standard-therapy arm (HR, 0.44; 95% CI, 0.25 to 0.77; P=0.004). In Jan 2018 FDA approved olaparib as a treatment following chemotherapy and appropriate hormonal therapy in patients carrying gBRCA+ and have advanced nonHER-2 breast cancer.

Olaparib was assessed in metastatic breast cancer with somatic BRCA 1/2 mutation or germline or somatic mutations in DNA damage repair (DDR) pathway genes other than BRCA 1/2 (NCT03344965, TBCRC 048). Cohort 1 (germline mutation in nonBRCA DDR gene) and cohort 2 (somatic mutation in DDR genes or BRCA 1/2) were treated with olaparib 300mg twice daily. The primary endpoint was ORR, defined as confirmed complete response (CR) and partial response (PR) according to modified RECIST, version 1.1. In cohort 1, ORR was 33% (90% CI, 19% to 51%) and in cohort 2, 31% (90% CI, 15% to 49%). Response was gene specific; confirmed responses were seen only with gPALB2 (ORR, 82%) and sBRCA1/2 (ORR, 50%) mutations. Median PFS was 13.3 months (90% CI, 12 months to not available/computable [NA]) for gPALB2 and 6.3 months (90% CI, 4.4 months to NA) for sBRCA1/2 mutation carriers. No responses were observed with ATM or CHEK2 mutations alone.

Combination with chemotherapy.

Efforts to use olaparib in combination with chemotherapy have been hampered by prohibitive toxicities. For example, a phase 1 study evaluating olaparib plus carboplatin in HER2 negative breast cancer was terminated early after recruiting 15 patients due to toxicity (NCT02561832). A phase 1 study evaluating olaparib (oral, 200mg, twice daily) plus paclitaxel (IV, 90mg/m2, weekly) in 19 patients with metastatic TNBC as 1st or 2nd line found that dose modifications due to neutropenia and requiring granulocyte-colony stimulating factor were often necessary . The most frequent adverse events were diarrhea (any grade, 63%), nausea (any grade, 58%) and neutropenia (any grade, 58%). Seven patients (37%) achieved partial response.

Combination with immunotherapy without or with chemotherapy.

The efficacy and safety of combination of durvalumab, a PD-L1 targeting agent and olaparib were evaluated in 34 patients with metastatic gBRCA+ HER2 negative breast cancer (MEDIOLA) Patients received olaparib 300 mg twice daily for a 4 weeks upfront followed by a combination of olaparib 300 mg twice daily and durvalumab 1.5 g IV every 4 wks. The primary endpoints were disease control rate (DCR) at 12 weeks, safety and tolerability. Efficacy data showed that the DCR was 80% (90% CI: 64.3%, 90.9%) at 12 weeks and 50% (90% CI: 33.9%, 66.1%) at 28 weeks. The ORR was 63.3% (95% CI:48.9–80.1%): 3% complete response, 60% partial response, and 17% stable disease. Median duration of response was 9.2 months and median PFS was 6.7 months. The most common Grade 3 or 4 AE were anemia (12%), neutropenia (9%), and pancreatitis (6%).

Combination with an antiangiogenic agent.

Bevacizumab (vascular endothelial growth factor inhibitor) is being used in various cancers but it has not been shown to have significant efficacy in breast cancer. One mechanism of resistance to antiangiogenic therapy is induction of hypoxia as a response to vessel regression caused by these agents, resulting in an increase of DNA damage and genetic instability. Hypoxia suppresses HDR in human cells via transcriptional downregulation of BRCA1 and RAD51. Tumor cells exposed to chronic hypoxia acquire defects in homologous recombination and increased sensitivity to PARP inhibition. A phase 1 trial assessed safety and tolerability of olaparib in combination with bevacizumab in 12 patients with advanced solid tumors including breast cancer. The most common AE related to olaparib were grade 1/2 nausea and fatigue. No hematological parameters were reported as AE. No serious AE related to olaparib or DLTs were reported. Preliminary efficacy data of phase 2 trial of cediranib, a pan-vascular endothelial growth factor receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitor, (oral, 30mg daily) and olaparib (oral, 200mg twice daily) combination in 37 patients with metastatic TNBC showed ORR (primary endpoint) 14% (95% CI: 0.025, 0.2453). Median duration of response was 2.0 months with a range of 1.8 to 6.3 months. Disease control rate ((# of patients with CR, PR or SD)/(# of evaluable patients)) was 81% (95% CI: 0.69–0.94). Median PFS was 3.7 months (95% CI: 2.1–4.3). Grade 3/4 AE, irrespective of attribution, occurred in 25 of 38 patients (66%). G3/4 AE occurring in >5% of patients were hypertension (24%), dyspnea (11%), diarrhea (8%) and vomiting (8%).

In other malignancies

Ovarian cancer

Olaparib is approved as 1) monotherapy as a first line treatment of BRCA-mutated advanced ovarian cancer, 2) combination treatment with bevacizumab as first line maintenance treatment for HRD positive advanced ovarian cancer, 3) monotherapy as a maintenance treatment for relapsed ovarian cancer that is sensitive to platinum, and 4) monotherapy in advanced germline BRCA mutated ovarian cancer that were treated 3 or more prior chemotherapy.

Pancreatic cancer

Olaparib is approved as maintenance treatment for germline mutated advanced pancreatic cancer.

Prostate cancer

Olaparib was FDA approved in May 2020 for patients with deleterious or suspected deleterious germline or somatic HRR gene-mutated metastatic CRPC who have had disease progression following prior treatment with enzalutamide or abiraterone.

Talazoparib

Monotherapy.

Talazoparib is a potent PARP inhibitor with both strong catalytic inhibition and PARP-trapping potential that is approximately 100 times greater than that of other PARP inhibitors . A phase 1 dose escalation study of 113 patients with solid tumor established MTD 1.0mg/day, and 50% of breast cancer patients (7 of 14) who carried gBRCA+ had an objective response . A phase II study (ABRAZO) in 84 patients with gBRCA+ and advanced breast cancer included patients who had prior response to platinum and no progression of disease within 8 weeks of last dose (cohort 1) or no prior platinum (cohort 2). Primary endpoint was ORR by independent radiological assessment. The response rate was 21% (10 of 48) among patients in cohort 1 and 37% (13 of 35) among patients in cohort 2. Exploratory subgroup analyses indicated that longer platinum-free interval was associated with greater clinical activity. None of 7 patients that entered cohort 1 whose disease progressed within 8 weeks of the last dose of platinum responded to talazoparib.

The EMBRCA study include 431 patients with HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer and gBRCA+ who were randomized 2:1 to receive talazoparib (oral,1mg daily) or standard single agent therapy of physician’s choice (TPC): capecitabine (oral, 1250 mg/m2, twice daily for 14 days, repeat every 21 days), eribulin (IV, 1.4 mg/m2, days 1 and 8, repeat every 21 days), gemcitabine (IV, 1250 mg/m2, Day 1 and 8, repeat every 21 days), or vinorelbine (IV, 30 mg/m2, days 1, 8 and 15, repeat 21 days). The primary trial endpoint was PFS which was assessed by blinded independent central review. The median PFS was significantly improved for talazoparib (8.6 vs. 5.6 months; HR 0.54, 95% CI, 0.41–0.71; P<0.001), as was the ORR (62.6% vs 27.2%; odd ratio, 5.0; 95% CI, 2.9–8.8; P<0.001). Improved PFS was observed in all subgroups examined, including those with central nervous system disease. The median overall survival at the interim analysis was 22.3 months (95% CI, 18.1–26.2) in the talazoparib arm and 19.5 months (95% CI, 16.3–22.4) in the TPC arm (HR, 0.76; 95% CI, 0.55–1.06, P=0.11). Final OS data showed HR for OS with talazoparib versus chemotherapy was 0.848 (95% CI 0.670–1.073;p=0.17); median (95% CI) 19.3 months versus 19.5 months (17.4–22.4). OS was not significantly improved with talazoparib when compared to TPC. Safety data showed grade 3 or 4 hematologic AE occurred in 55% of the patients in talazoparib arm and in 38% of the patients in TPC arm. Grade 3 nonhematologic AE occurred in 32% of patients in the talazoparib group and in 38% of patients in the TPC group. AE resulting in discontinuation of the drug occurred 5.9% in talazoparib group and 8.7% in TCP group. AE resulting in dose modification (reduction or interruption) occurred 66% in talazoparib group and 60% of TCP group. Most common AE leading to dose modification in talazoparib group were anemia (any grade, 52.8% in talazoparib vs 18.3% in TCP), neutropenia (any grade, 34.6% vs 42.9%) and thrombocytopenia (any grade, 26.9% vs 7.1%). Most common AE leading to dose modification in TCP arm were neutropenia, palmar-plantar erythrodysethesia (any grade 1.4% vs 22.2%), nausea (any grade, 48.6% vs 46.8%) and diarrhea (any grade, 22.0% vs 26.2%). The patient reported outcome also favored talazoparib as compared to standard chemotherapy. A significant improvement in the estimated overall mean change from baseline in the global health status–quality-of-life scale on the EORTC QLQ-C30 was documented in the talazoparib group, as compared with a significant deterioration in the standard-therapy group (3.0 [95% CI, 1.2 to 4.8] vs. −5.4 [95% CI, −8.8 to −2.0]; P<0.001) A statistically significant greater delay was observed in TTD (a time to definitive clinically meaningful deterioration) in GHS/QoL, 24.3 months versus 6.3 months (hazard ratio, 0.38, 95% CI 0.26 to 0.55; P < 0.0001) The FDA approved Talazoparib in October 2018 as a treatment option for patients with germline BRCA-mutated advanced HER2-negative breast cancer.

Rucaparib

Monotherapy.

A phase 2 trial was conducted in gBRCA+ patients with advanced ovarian and breast cancer. Different dose schedule was administered and rucaparib did not show activity in breast cancer patients in this study. RUBY, a phase 2 trial evaluated efficacy of rucaparib in 41 patients with metastatic breast cancer and germline BRCA wild type, and whose tumor present with HRD as assessed by genome-wide Loss of Heterozygosity (LOH) score. Rucaparib demonstrated clinical benefit (CR+PR+SD) 13.5% in a subset of germline BRCA wild type metastatic breast cancer patients whose tumor has high LOH.

Combination with chemotherapy.

A phase 1 study was conducted to evaluate safety, clinical activities and pharmacodynamics of intravenous and oral rucaparib combined with chemotherapy in 85 patients with advanced solid tumors including 22 patients with breast cancer most of whom were not tested for germline pathogenic BRCA variants . Patients received escalating doses of intravenous rucaparib combined with carboplatin, carboplatin/paclitaxel, cisplatin/pemetrexed, or epirubicin/cyclophosphamide. Subsequently, the study was amended to focus on oral rucaparib (once daily, days 1–14) combined with carboplatin (day 1 in 21-day cycles). The MTD of combination was rucaparib oral 240gm and carboplatin IV AUC 5. Neutropenia (27.1%) and thrombocytopenia (18.8%) were the most common grade ⩾3 toxicities across combinations and were DLTs with the oral rucaparib/carboplatin combination.

In other malignancies

Ovarian cancer.

Rucaparib was approved as maintenance treatment of recurrent ovarian cancer and treatment of BRCA mutated ovarian cancer after 2 or more chemotherapies.

Prostate cancer

The FDA granted accelerated approval to rucaparib for patients with metastatic CRPC and deleterious BRCA1/2 mutations (germline and/or somatic) in May 2020.

Niraparib

Monotherapy.

A phase 1 dose escalation study established MTD of 300mg/day. Twelve of 100 patients had breast cancer and 4 of them were evaluable for response. Two of 4 patients showed partial response.

Combination with other agents.

TOPACIO, a single arm phase 2 trial assessed the clinical activity of combination treatment with niraparib (oral, 200mg daily) and pembrolizumab (IV 200mg every 21 days) using the primary end point of ORR in 55 patients with advanced TNBC irrespective of PDL-1 or BRCA status, and it showed ORR 21% (90% CI, 12%−33%) and disease control rate (DCR, a proportion of patients with CR+PR+SD) 49% (90% CI, 36%−62%). In 15 evaluable patients with tumor BRCA mutations, ORR was 47% (90% CI, 24%−70%), DCR was 80% (CI, 56%−94%) and PFS 8.3 months (95% CI, 2.1 months to not estimable). In 27 patients with wild type tumors ORR was 11% (90% CI,3%−26%), DCR 33% (90% CI, 19%−51%) and PFS 2.1 months (95% CI, 1.4–2.5 months).

In other malignancies

Ovarian cancer

Niraparib is approved as first line maintenance treatment of advanced ovarian cancer, maintenance treatment of recurrent ovarian cancer and treatment for advanced ovarian cancer after three or more chemotherapies.

Iniparib

Combination with other agents.

Phase 1 trials with Iniparib either alone or in combination with chemotherapy in patients with advanced solid tumors have shown iniparib to have mild toxicity, and MTD was not reached. Iniparib and irinotecan, a topoisomerase 1 inhibitor were evaluated in 37 patients. Median TTP was 2.14 months and median OS was 7.8 months. Most prevalent toxicities were diarrhea (51%), nausea (46%), and fatigue (32%). Grade ≥3 toxicities include diarrhea (3%), fatigue (3%), neutropenia (14%), and thrombocytopenia (3%). A phase 2 trial was conducted to evaluate efficacy of iniparib when added to carboplatin and gemcitabine among 123 patients with metastatic triple negative breast cancer. Primary end points were the rate of clinical benefit (CR+PR+SD > 6months) and safety. Additional end points included ORR, PFS and OS. This study showed an improvement in clinical benefit rate from 34% to 56% (p=0.01), ORR from 32% to 52% (p=0.02), median PFS from 3.6 months to 5.9 months (HR 0.59; p=0.01), and OS from 7.7 months to 12.3 months (HR 0.57; p=0.01) for the iniparib containing arm. However subsequent phase 3 trial with 519 patients failed to show PFS (4.1 vs 5.1 months, HR = 0.79; 95% CI, 0.65 to 0.98; p = 0.027) or OS (11.8 vs 11.1 months, HR=0.88; 95% CI, 0.69 to 1.12; p=0.28) benefit with addition of iniparib to carboplatin and gemcitabine . Iniparib was considered to be a PARP inhibitor and entered clinical trials. However more data have suggested that iniparib is not only structurally distinct from other PARP inhibitors, but is also a poor inhibitor of PARP activity. It did not exhibit the properties of a classic PARP inhibitor. Results of iniparib trial are not included in Table 3.

Veliparib

Monotherapy.

Phase 1 study evaluated MTD, DLT, PK, PD and preliminary efficacy of single agent veliparib in 88 patients with gBRCA+ and platinum refractory ovarian or basal-like breast cancer (BRCA wild type, wtBRCA). The RP2D was established at 400 mg twice daily. The ORR (CR + PR) was 23% in gBRCA and 4% in wtBRCA. The CBR (CR+PR+SD) was 58% in gBRCA and 38% in wtBRCA.

Combination with chemotherapy.

Veliparib was evaluated in combination with various chemotherapies including methotrexate , cyclophosphamide, pegylated liposomal doxorubicin  temozolomide  carboplatin and paclitaxel, irinotecan, capecitabine and oxaliplatin, cisplatin and vinorelbine

BRCODAE 2 was a randomized phase 2 trial in 290 patients with recurrent/metastatic breast cancer and deleterious BRCA 1/2 mutation who were assigned to one of 3 arms, including veliparib or placebo in combination with carboplatin-paclitaxel, or temozolomide plus veliparib. The primary end point was PFS by independent central review and secondary end points were OS and ORR. In comparing veliparib with placebo when combined with carboplatin/paclitaxel, there were no significant differences in median PFS (14.1 vs. 12.3 months, HR 0.789; 95% CI 0.536–1.162; p = 0.227). Interim median OS was 28.3 and 25.9 months (HR 0.750; 95% CI 0.503–1.117; P = 0.156), and ORR was 77.8% and 61.3% (P = 0.027). For the veliparib-temozolomide arm, outcomes were inferior to the carboplatin/paclitaxel control arm, including median PFS (7.4 months, HR 1.858; 95% CI 1.278–2.702; P = 0.001), OS (19.1 months, HR 1.483; 95% CI 1.032–2.131; P = 0.032), and ORR (28.6%, P < 0.001)

Another randomized phase 2 trial evaluated efficacy of cisplatin and veliparib combination in 323 patients with recurrent or metastatic TNBC and/or BRCA associated breast cancer (SWOG S1416). Patients were randomized 1:1 to receive cisplatin at 75 mg/m2 on day 1 every 21 days plus either placebo (P) or oral veliparib (Vel) at 300 mg twice daily from day 1 to day 14 every 21 days. The primary end point of the trial was PFS in the 3 prespecified groups: germline BRCA mutation (gBRCA+, n=37); BRCA-like (n=101); and non-BRCA-like (n=110). Patients with wild type BRCA were classified into BRCA-like; and non-BRCA-like via multipronged biomarker panel. Secondary end-points included ORR, OS, and toxicity. In the gBRCA+ group, numerically better but not statistically significant PFS was noted with Vel compared to P (HR=0.64; p=0.26). In BRCA-like group PFS was better with Vel vs P (5.7 vs 4.3 months HR=0.58; p=0.023, 1 years PFS 20% vs 7%). Numerically better in OS (13.7 vs 12.1 months, HR=0.66; p=0.14) and ORR (45% vs 35%, p=0.38) in BRCA-like group. Non-BRCA-like group did not show benefit of veliparib for PFS (HR=0.85; p=0.43). Grade 3/4 neutropenia (46% vs 19%) and anemia (23% vs 7%) occurred at higher frequency in Vel arm compared to P.

BROCADE 3 trial (NCT02163694) was a phase 3 study that compared the efficacy of carboplatin and paclitaxel with veliparib (VCP) or placebo (CP) in 337 patients with metastatic/advanced HER2 negative, gBRCA+ breast cancer who previously received 2 or fewer lines of cytotoxic therapy for metastatic breast cancer, of whom 8% had received prior platinum therapy and 19% had received chemotherapy for metastatic disease. Patients were randomized 2:1 to receive oral veliparib (120 mg twice daily) or placebo on days 2 to 5, administered with carboplatin (AUC 6 on day 1) and weekly paclitaxel (80 mg/m2 on days 1, 8, and 15 in 21 day cycles). The median patient age was 47 years, 48% of patients were ER− /PR−, and 4% had a history of CNS metastases. The primary endpoint was investigator assessed PFS and secondary endpoints included OS and ORR. In comparing the veliparib and placebo arms, median PFS was improved with addition of veliparib (14.5 vs 12.6 months, HR 0.71; 95% CI=0.57–0.88, P=0.002), although results were similar for ORR, CBR, and median OS (Table 3). Any grade AE included neutropenia (91% in both arms), thrombocytopenia (82% vs 72%), anemia (81% vs 70%), and nausea and vomiting (75% vs 68%) of patients in the VCP versus CP arms, respectively. The most common study drug-related G3 or higher AE occurring ≥20% of patients were anemia (27% vs 17%), neutropenia (52% vs 50%), and thrombocytopenia (25% vs 15%) in the VCP versus CP arms, respectively. Dose reductions of carboplatin were made for 88% vs 86% and dose reduction of paclitaxel were made in 74% versus 70% in VCP vs CP, respectively. Patients who were randomized to CP were allowed to receive veliparib monotherapy (300–400mg, twice daily) after disease progression, and mean (range) platinum-free interval at time of first dose of veliparib was 3.1 months (0.4–10) vs 8.1 months (1.0–34.9) in patients who had progressed vs had not progressed by 24 weeks after first dose of veliparib after crossover.

PARP inhibitors in early stage breast cancer

Chemotherapy is frequently used for locally advanced breast cancer especially in TNBC. Given favorable results of PARP inhibitors in advanced breast cancer the incorporation of PARP inhibitors in early stage is being investigated as monotherapy or in combination. Randomized phase 2 and phase 3 trials of PARP inhibitors in early stage breast cancer are summarized in table 5.

Olaparib

GeparOLA trial (NCT02789332) was a randomized phase II trial to assess the efficacy of paclitaxel and olaparib in comparison to paclitaxel/carboplatin followed by epirubicin/cyclophosphamide in the neoadjuvant setting among 102 patients with HER2-negative early breast cancer and homologous recombination deficiency. The primary endpoint was pathologic complete response (pCR) rate. There was no significant difference in the pCR rate for paclitaxel/olaparib (55.1%, 90%CI 44.5%−65.3%) vs paclitaxel/carboplatin (48.6%, 90%CI 34.3%−63.2%). Exploratory subgroup analysis for the stratified subgroups showed higher pCR rate with paclitaxel/olaparib in the cohorts of patients age < 40 (n=32, 76.2% vs 45.5%) and hormone receptor (HR)+ patients (n=29, 52.6% vs 20.0%). In patients age ≥ 40 (n= 74) pCR rate was 45.8% vs 50.0% and in HR− patients (n=77) pCR rate was 56.0% vs 59.3%, paclitaxel/olaparib vs paclitaxel/carboplatin respectively. I-SPY2 result showed that durvalumab/olaparib/paclitaxel followed by doxorubicin/cyclophosphamide (experimental arm, n=73) improved pCR rate compared to paclitaxel followed by doxorubicin/cyclophosphamide (control arm, n=299) in patients with high risk HER2 negative stage 2–3 breast cancer. In HER2-negative cancers, the pCR rate increased from 20% to 37% overall, including from 27% to 47% in the triple negative cohort, and from 14% to 28% in the hormone receptor–positive cohort. The estimated probability that the experimental arm is superior to chemotherapy alone was greater than 98% in the subsets. Grade 3–4 AE occurred in 58% in the durvalumab/olaparib/paclitaxel arm and 41% in the paclitaxel control arm, including febrile neutropenia (16.3 vs 8.4%), colitis (7.0 vs 0.4%), adrenal insufficiency (7.0 vs 0%), alanine aminotransferase increase (4.7 vs 2.0%), dehydration (4.7 vs 0.8%), and vaginal infection (4.7 vs 0%).

Talazoparib

A feasibility study of neoadjuvant Talazoparib was conducted in 13 patients with operable breast cancer who carried germline BRCA mutation. Two months of treatment with Talazoparib in the neoadjuvant setting showed decrease in tumor volume rating 30–98%. A pilot study of neoadjuvant Talazoparib monotherapy (oral, 1mg daily) for 6 months was conducted in 20 patients with known gBRCA+ and 1cm or larger invasive tumor that are operable. Fifteen patients had TNBC and 5 patients had HR+ breast cancer. Residual cancer burden (RCB)-0 (pCR) was 53% and RCB-0/1 was 63%. Eight patients (40%) had grade 3 anemia and required a transfusion, 3 patients had grade 3 neutropenia, and 1 patient had grade 4 thrombocytopenia. Common grade 1 or 2 toxicities were nausea, fatigue, neutropenia, alopecia, dizziness, and dyspnea. Toxicities were managed by dose reduction and transfusions. Nine patients required dose reduction. Despite small sample size, this study is the first trial showing relevant pCR rate with a single targeted agent without chemotherapy in BRCA mutated breast cancer patient.

Veliparib

I-SPY2, a phase 2, adaptively randomized trial predicted 88% probability of success in a phase 3 trial when veliparib and carboplatin added to standard therapy in patients with stage 2 or 3 breast cancer. Seventy two patients with TNBC were randomly assigned to receive veliparib–carboplatin, and 44 patients with TNBC were assigned to receive control therapy. The estimated rates of pCR in the TNBC population were 51% (95% Bayesian probability interval [PI], 36 to 66%) in the veliparib–carboplatin group versus 26% (95% PI, 9 to 43%) in the control group. The rate of grade 3 or 4 hematologic toxic effects were higher in the veliparib–carboplatin group than in the control group: neutropenia (71% vs 2%), febrile neutropenia (1% vs 0%), thrombocytopenia (21% vs 0%), and anemia (28% vs 0%). The AE was also higher during doxorubicin/cyclophosphamide (AC) treatment among patients who had received veliparib–carboplatin than among those who had received control therapy: febrile neutropenia (12 vs 5%), and rates of neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, and anemia were also higher in the veliparib–carboplatin group. Dose reductions of paclitaxel occurred in 23 patients (32%) in the veliparib–carboplatin group and none in the control group. Dose reductions of carboplatin occurred in 34 patients (47%). During paclitaxel treatment, 13 patients (18%) in the veliparib–carboplatin group, as compared with 2 patients (5%) in the control group, discontinued therapy early.

BrighTNess was a phase 3, placebo-controlled, double blind trial which was designed to assess the addition of veliparib (oral, 50mg, twice daily) plus carboplatin (IV, AUC 6, day 1, repeat every 3 weeks) or carboplatin to standard neoadjuvant chemotherapy in stage 2–3 TNBC. Six hundred and thirty four patients were randomly assigned to receive paclitaxel/carboplatin/veliparib, paclitaxel/carboplatin, or paclitaxel arm (2:1:1). All patients received AC afterwards. About 15% of patients carried germline deleterious BRCA 1/2 mutation. The pCR rate was higher in the paclitaxel/carboplatin/veliparib group than in patients receiving paclitaxel alone (53%, 168 of 316 patients vs 31%, 49 of 158; p<0·0001), but not compared with patients receiving paclitaxel/carboplatin (58%, 92 of 160 patients, p=0·36). Grade 3 or 4 toxicities, and serious adverse events were more common in patients receiving carboplatin, whereas veliparib did not substantially increase toxicity. The most common grade 3 or 4 events overall were neutropenia (57 vs 3%), anemia (25 vs 0%), and thrombocytopenia (11 vs 0%), paclitaxel/carboplatin/veliparib vs paclitaxel, respectively. Authors concluded that addition of veliparib to paclitaxel and carboplatin did not improve pCR rate.

Ongoing trials with PARP inhibitors in advanced breast cancer

PARP inhibitors have demonstrated durable responses, mostly in patients with BRCA 1 and 2 mutation cancers, although majority of patients will develop resistance. Resistance to PARP inhibitors occurs by three general mechanisms; acquisition of aberration that increase HR repair capacity; activation of signaling pathways that decrease cell cycle progression and replication stress; and miscellaneous alterations. Other mechanisms include promoter demethylation of BRCA1/2 genes, point mutations in PARP1, and drug-efflux pumps affecting drug pharmacokinetics. Strategies to overcome such resistance mechanisms have included the development of multiple drug combinations, which also serve to synergize antitumor efficacy.

Various combination therapy of PARP inhibitors with chemotherapy, DNA damage response targeting drugs, other signaling targeting drugs and immunotherapy are actively undergoing evaluation. Currently ongoing PARPi trials in advanced breast cancer are summarized in Table 7.

Olaparib

Numerous trials of olaparib are under investigation in combination with various partners. Sapacitabine is an orally bioavailable nucleoside analog and its active metabolite, CNDAC (2′-C-cyano-2′-deoxy-1-β-D-arabino-pentofuranosylcytosine), causes SSB after incorporation into DNA, which are converted into DSB when cells enter a second S-phase. CNDAC-induced DSBs are predominantly repaired through homologous recombination (HR). Cells deficient in HR components are greatly sensitized to CNDAC. Therefore combination of sapacitabine and PARP inhibitor potentially result synergistic antineoplastic effect. Phase 1/2 trial of combination with olaparib and sapacitabine (CYC682) in gBRCA+ metastatic nonHER2 breast cancer is ongoing to define MTD, RP2D and ORR. (NCT03641755).

Histone deacetylase (HDAC) plays multiple roles in DNA repair. Vorinostat is HDAC inhibitor that is used in hematologic malignancy. Phase 1 trial is evaluating MTD of Olaparib and vorinostat in relapsed and refractory metastatic breast cancer (NCT03742245).

The synergy between PARPi and immune check point inhibitors is likely from increased neoantigens due to accumulation of DNA damage by PARPi which are potential targets of the immune checkpoint inhibitors Preclinical study with breast cancer cell line and xenograft model showed that PARPi upregulated PD-L1 expression primarily through GSK3b inactivation, and combination therapy of PARPi and anti-PD-L1 antibody demonstrated better therapeutic benefit than each treatment alone . Olaparib in combination with pembrolizumab, a PD-1 inhibitor is under investigation in phase 2 trial to evaluate ORR in metastatic BRCA mutated breast cancer (NCT03025035). KEYLYNK-009 is a phase 2/3, open-label, randomized study of pembrolizumab/olaparib or pembrolizumab/chemotherapy after induction with pembrolizumab/chemotherapy in patients with locally recurrent, inoperable TNBC or mTNBC to asses PFS and OS (NCT04191135). Another phase 2 trial of olaparib with or without durvalumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor in platinum treated metastatic TNBC is ongoing to assess PFS (DORA, NCT03167619). A phase 2 trial of olaparib and durvalumab in metastatic TNBC and BRCA wild type is also ongoing to evaluate efficacy with primary objective of ORR (NCT03801369). Olaparib is also being tested in combination with atezolizumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor to assess PFS in metastatic nonHER2 breast cancer and homologous DNA repair deficient (NCT02849496). About 60–70% of breast cancer expresses hormone receptor. A phase 2 trial is evaluating efficacy (primary endpoint PFS) of olaparib/durvalumab/fulvestrant combination in patients with metastatic ER and/or PR positive and HER2 negative breast cancer and carrying gBRCA+, deleterious germline/somatic alterations implicated in the HRR pathway or MSI status (DOLAF, NCT04053322).

Most PARP inhibitor studies in breast cancer were done in HER2 negative disease. About 15–25% of breast cancer express HER2. Efficacy (primary endpoint ORR and PFS) of olaparib in combination with trastuzumab is being tested in phase 2 trial in BRCA mutated or HRD advanced breast cancer (OPHELIA, NCT03931551).

PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway has been reported to maintain HR steady state and stabilize and preserve DSB repair by interacting with the HR complex. PI3K has been demonstrated to play a critical role in RAD51 recruitment which is a key protein involved in HR. A preclinical study showed that in BRCA proficient TNBC cells, PI3K inhibition led DNA damage, downregulation of BRCA1/2, and subsequent sensitization to PARP inhibition. In TNBC patient–derived primary tumor xenografts, dual PI3K and PARP inhibition reduced the growth of tumors displaying BRCA1/2 downregulation following PI3K inhibition suppression. A phase 1 trial of combination therapy of olaparib and oral PIK3kinase inhibitors (either BKM120 or BYL719) is being evaluated in patients with recurrent TNBC or high grade serous ovarian cancer (NCT01623349).

Synthetic lethality is a concept to describe the situation whereby a defect in either one of two genes has little effect on the cell or organism, but a combination of defects in both genes result in death. The pairing of PARP inhibitors with other DDR pathway inhibitors, such as ATR and WEE1 inhibitors, is being under investigation. WEE 1 is a nuclear kinase that regulates mitotic entry and nucleotide pools in coordination with DNA damage response, and ATR has crucial role in DNA damage repair signaling and in maintaining replication fork stability and regulate cell cycle control checkpoints. Preclinical data have shown that olaparib-resistant cancer models may be resensitized to olaparib when combined with a WEE1 inhibitor or an ATR inhibitor. A phase 2 study is underway to evaluate efficacy (primary endpoint PFS) of olaparib single treatment vs olaparib + AZD6738 (ceralasertib, ATR inhibitor) vs olaparib + adavosertib (WEE1 inhibitor) in second or third line setting in patients with TNBC prospectively stratified by presence/absence of qualifying tumor mutation in genes involved in the homologous recombination repair (HRR) pathway (NCT03330847).

A phase 1/2 trial of olaparib in combination with cediranib (VEGF inhibitor) is undergoing to assess MTD, DLT and PFS in recurrent ovarian, fallopian tube, peritoneal cancer or recurrent TNBC (NCT01116648). A phase 2 trial of olaparib with either VEGF inhibitor (cediranib) or ATR inhibitor (ceralasertib) are ongoing to assess ORR (primary objective) in patients with metastatic nonHER2 breast cancer and gBRCA + who were previously treated with PARP inhibitors (NCT04090567).

The Ras/Raf/MEK/ERK signaling pathway regulates key cellular processes such as proliferation, differentiation and survival. In preclinical study MEK1/2 lead to down regulation of BRCA2 protein. Selumetinib, a MEK1/2 inhibitor plus olaparib is being evaluated in phase 1/2 trial to define MTD in solid tumor including breast cancer (NCT03162627).

Efficacy of olaparib is being evaluated in patients with non-BRCA mutated metastatic breast cancer with homologous repair deficit in phase 2 study with primary objective of clinical benefit rate (NCT03367689, NOBROLA).

Talazoparib

Sacituzumab govitecan is an antibody drug conjugate that contains SN-38, the active metabolite of irinotecan. In the phase 1/2 cohort of 108 individuals with TNBC, patients had received a median of 3 previous therapies. The response rate was 33.3 %, median PFS was 5.5. months, and OS was 13.0 months The confirmatory phase 3 ASCENT study exploring sacituzumab govitecan in patients with TNBC has been stopped due to compelling evidence of efficacy. It got FDA approval in April 2020 for patients with metastatic TNBC who received at least 2 prior therapy for metastatic disease. Sacituzumab govitecan and talazoparib combination is being tested in phase 1/2 trial to define DLT in patients with metastatic TNBC (NCT04039230).

A phase 1/2 trial is assessing safety and tolerability of talazoparib and avelumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor combination is being evaluated in advanced breast cancer(TALAVE, NCT03964532).

The bromodomain and extraterminal (BET) protein BRD4 promotes gene transcription by RNA polymerase II. Preclinical study demonstrated treatment of BRCA-proficient ovarian cancer cells with the BET inhibitor JQ1 downregulated the G2-M cell-cycle checkpoint regulator WEE1 and the DNA-damage response factor TOPBP1. Combining PARP inhibitor with the BET inhibitor synergistically increased in DNA damage and checkpoint defects, which allowed cells to enter mitosis despite the accumulation of DNA damage, ultimately causing mitotic catastrophe. This combination showed synergistic suppression of growth of BRCA-proficient cancer in vivo in a xenograft ovarian cancer mouse model. A phase 2 trial of combination treatment of talazoparib and ZEN003694, a BET inhibitor, in metastatic TNBC (without gBRCA+) is evaluating safety, DLT and objective response rate (NCT03901469).

Hypomethylating agent inhibit DNA methylation and induce DNA damage by inactivating and trapping DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) to DNA, with the damaged DNA being repaired by the base excision repair (BER) machinery. PARP inhibitor disrupts the repair of hypomethylating agent induced DNA methytransferase DNA lesions by preventing relocation of the BER enzyme XRCC1 to DNA damage sites. The combination of PARP inhibitor with the hypomethylating agent provided synergistic cytotoxicity. Preclinical data showed combination of DNMT inhibition and talazoparib significantly reduced cell colony in ovarian cancer regardless BRCA and platinum sensitivity status. ASTX727 is a novel agent, a combination of the hypomethylating agent decitabine and the novel cytidine deaminase inhibitor, E7727 (cedazuridine). Safety of combination therapy of ASTX727 plus talazoparib in patients with TNBC or hormone resistant/HER2 negative metastatic breast cancer is being conducted (NCT04134884).

PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway has been reported to maintain homologous repair (HR) steady state and stabilize and preserve DSB repair by interacting with the HR complex. Gedatolisib is an intravenous PI3K and mTOR inhibitor and its combination with talazoparib is ongoing to evaluate MTD and ORR in patients with advanced TNBC without known BRCA 1/2 mutation or HER2 negative and germline BRCA 1/2 mutated breast cancer (NCT03911973).

The efficacy (primary objective ORR) of talazoparib in non-HER2 advanced breast tumors with mutations in other non-BRCA1/2 HR pathway genes is being evaluated in phase 2 trial (NCT02401347). A single arm, phase 2 trial is evaluating efficacy (PFS as primary objective) of talazoparib in somatic BRCA 1/2 mutation carriers with metastatic breast cancer. In this trial patients with gBRCA+ are excluded and somatic deleterious tumor BRCA mutation are detected by cell-free circulating tumor DNA (NCT03990896).

Many tumor cells utilize the amino acid glutamine to meet the elevated bioenergetic and biosynthetic demands of rapid cell growth. The enzyme glutaminase converts glutamine to glutamate, which is used to fuel the TCA cycle, synthesize amino acids and nucleotides, and balance cellular oxidative stress. In preclinical trial CB-839, a glutaminase inhibitor, treatment in combination with the PARP inhibitors niraparib and talazoparib led to synergistic anti-proliferative activity . A phase 1/2 study is evaluating safety and tolerability of combination of talazoparib and CB-839 (telaglenastat), a novel glutaminase inhibitor, in advanced solid tumors including TNBC (NCT03875313).

Rucaparib

Combination of rucaparib with lucitanib, a VEGF 1–3 TKI or sacituzumab govitecan is under investigation in phase1/2 trial to define DLT, ORR and safety in advanced solid tumor including TNBC (SEASTAR, NCT03992131). DLT and RP2D of rucaparib in combination with atezolizumab in advanced gynecological cancer and TNBC are being evaluated in phase 1 trial (NCT03101280). A phase 1/2 is evaluating dose, safety, pharmacokinetics, and efficacy of combination of ipatasertib, an AKT inhibitor and rucaparib in advanced breast, prostate and ovarian cancer to (NCT03840200).

Niraparib

BRAVO is phase 3 trial comparing niraparib vs physician’s choice in patients with germline mutated BRCA and metastatic nonHER2 breast cancer. Many patients in physician’s choice arm did not continue the treatment and withdrew consent. Steering committee decided to complete the planned enrolment and the study is ongoing. A phase 2 trial of niraparib plus aromatase inhibitor is undergoing to evaluate clinical benefit in patients with unresectable locally advanced or metastatic HR-positive/HER2-negative breast cancer patients harboring either germline BRCA mutation or BRCA wild type and HRD positive (NCT04240106).

A phase 1/2 study of trastuzumab and niraparib is undergoing to evaluate DLT and ORR in advanced HER2 positive breast cancer patients who failed at least one line of treatment (NCT03368729). Everolimus, a mTOR inhibitor, plus niraparib is in early stage of clinical development in advanced ovarian and breast cancer (NCT03154281)

Conclusion

Over the past decade, PARP inhibitors have emerged as a relatively well-tolerated and effective treatment for ovarian cancer and now for advanced germline BRCA-mutant breast cancers, pancreatic cancer and castrate resistant prostate cancer. Numerous studies are actively investigating PARP inhibitors in combination with other targeted therapies to overcome resistance and synergize efficacy in breast cancer. Also they are being evaluated in metastatic breast cancers without germline BRCA mutation that are homologous repair deficient. With more outcome being available we expect that indications of PARP inhibitors to widen and be more precise.

Expert opinion

Poly-ADP-ribose polymerase (PARP) inhibitors are approved as monotherapy for patients with HER2-negative metastatic breast cancer who have pathogenic germline BRCA 1 and/or 2 variants, and thus exhibit deficiency in homologous DNA damage repair. PARP inhibitors are also approved for the treatment of selected patients with ovarian, pancreatic, and prostate cancer. Two phase 3 trials of PARP inhibitors, OlympiAD and EMBRACA, led to FDA approval of olaparib and talazoparib, respectively, for this indication. Both olaparib and talazoparib significantly prolonged progression free survival (PFS) and improved patient reported outcome when compared to standard chemotherapy. Though overall survival was not statistically significantly improved with PARPi which is likely confounded by subsequent treatment upon progression, superiority of PFS and PRO with PARPi provide therapeutic value of PARPi for patients with incurable metastatic breast cancer. Agents producing DNA damage such as platinum also exhibit clinical activity in this scenario, and patients who have had prior exposure and resistance to platinum may also be resistant to PARP inhibitors. Currently olaparib or olaparib in combination with durvalumab is being studied in platinum-treated metastatic triple negative breast cancer which would answer this question (DORA). The efficacy between PARPi and platinum in TNBC has not been directly compared and it is not known whether PARPi is superior to platinum or not. Up to 20 % of patients with triple negative breast cancer carry pathogenic germline BRCA1 and/or BRCA2 variants, indicating that most such patients should be screened for such pathogenic variants, especially if there is metastatic disease. Recent proof-of-principle study (TBCRC 048) showed the response to olaparib in metastatic breast cancer that harbors mutations in homologous recombination-related genes was gene specific. SWOG S1416 showed improvement of PFS with veliparib in BRCA like-BRCA wildtype group which was classified by multipronged biomarker panel including myChoice HRD score, somatic BRCA1 and 2 mutations, BRCA1 methylation and non-BRCA1 and 2 homologous repair germline mutations. Rucaparib demonstrated 13.5% clinical benefit in a subset of germline BRCA wild type metastatic breast cancer patients whose tumor has high loss of heterozygosity score (RUBY trial). These findings will need to be confirmed in a larger study but it highlights the importance of better biomarkers to identify potential candidates for PARPi. Currently olaparib, talazoparib, rucaparib and niraparib as single agent or in combination with other targeted therapy or immunotherapy are being evaluated in breast cancer with homologous repair defect other than germline BRCA 1/2 mutation. PARPi showed promising results in patients with early stage breast cancer who carried gBRCA1/2 mutation in early phase study, and it is currently under active investigation in this setting. Ongoing areas of investigation including use of PARP inhibitors in combination with other DNA damage repair pathway targets and immune checkpoint blockade, and efficacy in individuals with pathogenic variants in other genes associated with defective DNA repair or somatic deficiencies in DNA repair. The results of these trials may ultimately identify patients with other germline or somatic pathogenic variants who may benefit from PARP inhibitors, broaden the indications for PARP inhibitor use, and identify combinations including PARP inhibitors associated with greater efficacy.
