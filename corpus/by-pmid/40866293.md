Abstract

 Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), the most common primary liver neoplasia, often presents with advanced disease defined by macrovascular involvement and/or extrahepatic metastases. Atezolizumab and bevacizumab, tremelimumab plus durvalumab, and ipilimumab plus nivolumab have all received regulatory approval for the treatment of advanced HCC based on superior overall survival (OS) when compared with historic multitargeted tyrosine kinase inhibitor monotherapy. With these current front-line immunotherapy combinations, patients may attain objective response rates (ORR) by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) version 1.1 of 20.1–36%, median progression-free survival (PFS) of 3.8–9.1 months, median OS of 16.4–23.7 months, and an estimated 5-year OS of approximately 19%.

Assessment of immunotherapy at early and intermediate stages of liver-limited HCC is an understandable and critical next step in drug development. Intermediate stage liver-limited HCC is typically treated with regional therapies (ie, transarterial embolization, transarterial chemoembolization (TACE), or transarterial radioembolization), and these embolic treatments lead to quantifiable changes in the tumor immune microenvironment and the peripheral immune compartment that may serve to augment or be further augmented by immune checkpoint inhibition (ICI). Thus, numerous studies have explored such pairings and have now shown safety and clear and intriguing efficacy.

In this issue of the Journal, Li and colleagues present a single-arm, prospective, phase 2 trial of sintilimab, an anti-PD-1 antibody, with TACE for intermediate stageBarcelona-Clinic Liver Clinic (BCLC) stage B HCC. The study enrolled 20 patients, enriched with hepatitis B virus (HBV, 55%) associated HCC given single center accrual from the east, and a high tumor volume, given the set inclusion criterium of beyond the “up-to-seven” criteria. The latter indicates such patients were not candidates for curative intent liver resection or liver transplant. Patients were initially treated with TACE, with follow-up sessions per the investigator’s discretion, and anti-PD-1 therapy began no more than 3 days after the first TACE session.

The evaluated approach was safe—an expectedly high proportion of patients had transient Grade 1/2 liver function abnormalities given the use of TACE, though no cases of liver failure or Grade 4/5 toxicity were observed. Study treatment led to some degree of tumor shrinkage in all patients, with a 30% ORR by RECIST V.1.1 and 4 of 20 converting to surgical resection. With a median follow-up of 22 months, the PFS was 8 months, and the OS was not yet reached. No correlative science was presented—a relative weakness and a future requirement for such work—though radiomics were used to predict tumors likely to respond to combined treatment. The limitations of the small sample size and exploratory nature of the work were well described. Li and colleagues should be congratulated for their contribution, and their work brings up several points that require discussion in the contemporary scope of the field.

First, is there an optimal patient population for the combination of regional therapy and immunotherapy? Liver-limited disease is a continuum, where, for example, 3 tumors each measuring 4–5 cm occupying the right hepatic lobe, are staged the same way as 20 tumors all 1 cm occupying the entirety of the liver. In these extreme examples, differential treatments may yield diverse outcomes, and arguably, the latter example would be a candidate for immunotherapy combinations alone, for which there is already a documented survival benefit. Recently, randomized phase 3 studies of TACE plus ICI (EMERALD-1 and LEAP-012) included a small proportion of patients that had advanced HCC; thus, interpretation of positive efficacy data for intermediate stage HCC must be balanced with how patients are selected for treatment. Second, will all forms of regional therapy offer the same outcome, and is there an optimal pair for ICI? Bland embolization, versus chemoembolization, versus radioembolization each have varying degrees of evidence to support a survival advantage for intermediate stage HCC (the highest level of evidence supports TACE—though not all patients are candidates for such an approach), and different regional approaches may have differential effects on immune microenvironment. Future comparative studies and their associated correlative programs should define an optimal locoregional treatment approach to pair with ICI. Third, is PD-1 monotherapy sufficient to combine with TACE for intermediate stage HCC? Li and colleagues completed their study prior to the advent of combination immunotherapy—it is now well established that in the advanced setting single agent anti-PD-1/L1 therapy is not superior to sorafenib. EMERALD-1, a global, randomized, phase 3 study assessed TACE plus placebo versus TACE plus durvalumab and placebo, versus TACE plus durvalumab and bevacizumab in patients with intermediate stage HCC. Although the primary endpoint was PFS assessed by blinded independent central review between TACE and TACE plus durvalumab and bevacizumab, the addition of durvalumab alone added little to TACE efficacy (PFS HR 0.94 (95% CI 0.75 to 1.19; p=0.64)). Importantly, TACE plus durvalumab and bevacizumab was superior to TACE alone with a median PFS of 15.0 versus 8.2 months (HR 0.77, 95% CI 0.61 to 0.98, p=0.032). OS was immature. The observed efficacy was also offset by toxicity (treatment-related severe adverse event 19 vs 5%; treatment-related severe adverse event (TRAE) Grade 3/4 27 vs 6%). LEAP-012, a global randomized phase 3, assessed TACE plus lenvatinib and pembrolizumab versus TACE plus placebo in intermediate stage HCC. The co-primary endpoints of the trial were PFS by blinded independent central review and OS. With a median follow-up of 25.6 months, PFS favored triple therapy over TACE alone (median PFS 14.6 months vs 10.0 months; HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.51 to 0.84, p=0.002), OS was not fully mature, and additional toxicity was observed with the addition of ICI to TACE (TRAE Grade ≥ 3 71% vs 32%). Thus, combinatorial ICI is required to improve outcomes when paired with TACE, and ongoing studies are testing the additional checkpoint inhibitor combinations with anti-CTLA-4-based treatment. Finally, can we enrich for benefit with novel biomarkers? Genomic alterations (ie, WNT-beta catenin activation) are not predictive for ICI response in HCC, while assays that assess functional immune response both in tumor and peripheral compartment appear more promising with their validation ongoing. Radiomics or functional imaging assay, which seeks to be a non-invasive measures of tumor immunogenomic as well as prognostic and predictive tool to ICI treatment, remains in its nascency.

Given the above data, should all patients with intermediate stage HCC who undergo TACE receive combinatorial ICI in the absence, as of yet, a clear OS advantage? ORR/PFS are not surrogates for OS in HCC (indeed, an HR<0.6 has been suggested to be an optimal predictor of improved OS, which was not achieved in either EMERALD-1 or LEAP-012). The observed PFS advantages with the addition of ICI in EMERALD-1 and LEAP-012 are compelling, but in light of added toxicity (though managable) and required medicalization of patients, a shared decision making model is certainly now required prior to routine implementation for all patients. Longer follow-up of the pivotal phase 3 studies and other ongoing trials will likely provide more clarity in the years ahead, though a survival advantage may not be realized as patients who progess on TACE will likely cross over to immunotherapy at the time of progression. TACE plus ICI combinations certainly will have a role in downstaging tumor for resection or transplant, or in fit patients with high-risk disease, though at present, it does not appear to be a one-size-fits-all approach. In sum, regional treatment with ICI combinations for intermediate stage disease is now rightly at the forefront of HCC drug development, though the correct patient characteristics, specific biologic factors, and optimal combinations required ongoing investigation.
