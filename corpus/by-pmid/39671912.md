# Identification of TUBB3 as an immunotherapy target in lung cancer by genome wide in vivo CRISPR screening.

## Abstract

Recent development of immune checkpoint inhibitors has revolutionized cancer immunotherapy. Although these drugs show dramatic effects on a subset of cancer patients, many other tumors are non-responsive and the pathological mechanism of the resistance is largely unknown. To identify genes underlying anti-PD-1 immunotherapy resistance using a systematic approach, we performed an in vivo genome wide CRISPR screening in lung cancer cells. We integrated our results with multi-omics clinical data and performed both in vitro and in vivo assays to evaluate the role of the top candidate in regulating cytotoxic T cell killing. We identified TUBB3 as a potential target to overcome the resistance and enhance the efficacy of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. TUBB3 expression is upregulated in lung cancer patients, and its higher expression correlates with poorer patients' survival. We found that TUBB3 expression was significantly elevated in the non-responders compared to responders in our patient cohort that received immunotherapies. Importantly, the results of our preclinical experiments showed that inhibition of TUBB3 with a small molecule inhibitor synergized with anti-PD-1 treatment and enhanced tumor cell killing by cytotoxic T cells. Consistently, anti-PD-1 resistant cells showed significantly higher expression of TUBB3; however, TUBB3 inhibition rendered the resistant cells more susceptible to T cell killing. Mechanistic studies revealed that blocking TUBB3 suppressed the expression of PD-L1 through the EMT-related SNAI1 gene. Our results provide a rationale for a novel combination therapy consisting of the TUBB3 inhibition and anti-PD-1 immunotherapy for lung cancer.

Introduction

Tumor progression is strongly influenced by the function of cancer immunoediting, which shapes tumor immunogenicity and controls the growth of tumors by both adaptive and innate immune system. Cancer immunoediting consists of three steps: elimination, equilibrium and escape. Cytotoxic T cell, which is one of the major components of the immune system, mediates the elimination of cancer cells through recognition of specific antigens on cancer cell surface. However, tumor cells escape T cell killing by expressing immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1. To overcome this issue, immune checkpoint blockade therapy was developed. The use of monoclonal antibodies against well-established immune-inhibitory molecules including CTLA4, PD-1 and PD-L1 has been shown to dramatically suppress tumor progression in several types of cancer, and they became as a standard of care for many cancer types including melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer. The result of a recent retrospective cross-sectional study showed that the estimated population of cancer patients eligible for immune checkpoint blockade has been increased from 1.54 % to 43 %. However, the response rates to this therapy remain relatively low. For example, ∼20 % of lung cancer and <10 % of breast cancer patients show complete or partial responses, and both tumor intrinsic and extrinsic factors are involved in underlying mechanisms of the resistance. Some cancer cells alter expression of the genes in the antigen presentation pathway to impair tumor recognition by immune cells, while other cancer cells express a high level of immunosuppressive molecules, alter metabolism in the tumor microenvironment or activate the co-inhibitory molecules to cause T cell dysfunction or T cell exclusion from the tumor microenvironment. However, recent evidence indicates that there are many more unknown pathways that contribute to the resistance to checkpoint inhibitor therapy. Accordingly, to comprehensively investigate the mechanisms of resistance to checkpoint inhibitors, we adopted a systematic approach by performing a genome wide CRISPR screening in this study.

CRISPR/Cas9 is a new genome modification technology, which allows by the great flexibility, to introduce mutations on any known genes and has opened new opportunities to investigate complex disease physiology and powered drug discoveries. We used the mouse GeCKOv2 sgRNA library that targets 20 611 genes in a syngeneic lung cancer model of anti-PD-1 responses to elucidate the tumor intrinsic mechanisms of anti-PD-1 resistance. This is the first genome wide in vivo CRISPR screening to investigate anti-PD-1 resistance in lung cancer. Our study identified TUBB3 as a novel lung cancer anti-PD-1 resistance gene, revealed a functional connection between TUBB3 and PD-L1 and provided a rationale for the use of combination of a TUBB3 inhibitor and anti-PD-1 immunotherapy for lung cancer patients.

Results

Functional in vivo screening using CRISPR sgRNA library identified TUBB3 as anti-PD-1 resistant gene

To identify anti-PD-1 resistant genes in vivo using the CRISPR sgRNA library, we chose the model of murine lung cancer cell, CMT167, and the syngeneic mouse C57BL/6 because it is known to respond to anti-PD-1 treatment. We first confirmed its responsiveness to anti-PD-1 treatment in vivo. As shown in Fig. 1 A, the anti-PD-1 treatment suppressed the tumor growth by 30 %, which indicated that the model was appropriate for the screening of the library. We then engineered the CMT167 cells to express CAS9 and confirmed its expression by western blot (Fig. 1 B). Next, we examined CAS9 activity and its DNA editing ability by the T7E1 endonuclease assay using a reporter plasmid pXPR_011 which contains both EGFP and EGFP sgRNA. We observed two cleaved fragments as a result of CRISPR editing at the target site while no gene editing was observed in parental cells (Fig. S1A). We also confirmed a loss of EGFP expression by immunofluorescence examination and western blot (Fig. S1B). After the confirmation of CAS9 activity, we transduced the cells with the GeCKO v2 crispr library at MOI=0.2 and cultured the cells in vitro for two weeks to allow gene editing to take place. To examine the quality of the library, we isolated five single colonies from the library, and performed PCR amplification of the sgRNA insert region followed by sequencing. We found that all isolated colonies were with only single sgRNA insert (Fig. S1C). We also sequenced the baseline cell library and found that >97 % of the sgRNAs were present in the cell library (65.8 K out of 67.4K) and are evenly distributed with a Gini index of 0.09, which confirmed the quality of viral library packaging and efficient transduction (Fig. S1D, E). The screening process is illustrated in Fig. 1 C. In brief, 14 million cells were transplanted into both flanks of 10 syngeneic C57BL/6 mice for each group to achieve ∼ 200 fold coverage of the library, followed by treatment of the mice with either control IgG or anti-PD-1 antibody. The tumor size was monitored twice a week. We found significant reduction in tumor size and tumor weight in the anti-PD-1 treatment group (Fig. 1 D, E). After 24 days of the treatment, we collected the tumors, extracted the genomic DNA, and sequenced the amplified sgRNAs. The MAGeCK tool was used to compare the library representation between the two groups. Consistent with our expectations, a typical negative screening result was observed. This screening identified 710 genes that were significantly depleted or enriched with at least two sgRNAs (out of three for each gene). Among these genes, we selected genes whose sequences are well conserved in both mouse and human and have all three sgRNAs depleted or enriched. Accordingly, 132 genes were found to be significantly depleted (negatively selected), while only two genes were significantly enriched (positively selected) (supplementary table S1). We then further filtered these 132 genes using the Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE), which is the most up-to-date tool in predicting patients’ response to immunotherapy. We found that 12 of these 132 genes were highly expressed in predicted non-responders compared to responders for both the lung adenocarcinoma (TCGA-LUAD) and the lung squamous carcinoma (TCGA-LUSC) cohorts in TCGA database (Fig. 1 F and 1G). We also acquired treatment naïve lung cancer tissue samples from 16 lung cancer patients, of which eight were responders and the other eight were non-responders. We isolated RNAs from these FFPE tissues by micro-dissecting tumor regions and examined the expression of the twelve anti-PD-1 immune resistance genes we identified in the screening (Fig. 1 H). We found significantly higher expression of TUBB3 and GOPC in the non-responders compared with responders. Because Tubb3 showed the highest sequence homology between human and mouse (99.8 %) and a targeted drug is available, we focused on Tubb3 for the subsequent experiments. As shown in Fig. S1F, TUBB3 expression was higher in patients with high TIDE score (predicted non-responders) compared to low TIDE score patients (predicted responders) in both the TCGA-LUAD and TCGA-LUSC cohorts. To further confirm this observation, IHC analysis for TUBB3 was performed on the treatment-naïve human lung cancer samples from eight each responders and non-responders who were treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (Fig. 1 I). We found that TUBB3 expression was prominent in cancer cells as shown by the higher H scores (Fig. 1 I-J) in the non-responders, suggesting that TUBB3 plays a role in anti-PD-1 resistance.

To validate the clinical relevance of TUBB3 in lung cancer, we interrogated both LCE (Lung Cancer Explorer) and PRECOG (Prediction of Clinical Outcomes from Genomics Profiles) databases. In LCE, meta-analyses of TUBB3 expression from 18 independent cohorts containing 2324 tumor samples and 591 normal control samples (Fig. S1G) and the combined patients’ survival data of 26 independent cohorts totaling 4 553 patients (Fig. S1H) revealed that TUBB3 was significantly upregulated in lung cancer and correlated with poorer survival. In PRECOG, we found that higher expression of TUBB3 significantly correlated with poorer survival in three lung cancer cohorts (GSE31320, GSE8894 and GSE10245, Fig. S1I). Taken together, these findings suggest that the expression of TUBB3 in lung cancer correlates with poorer patient outcome and that Tubb3 plays a role in resistant to anti-PD-1 therapy in lung cancer.

TUBB3 inhibition sensitizes cancer cells to T cell killing in vitro

Our screening results showed that out of 67,400 sgRNAs, three of those that all targeted TUBB3 rendered cells death and elimination under the treatment with anti-PD-1. We then hypothesized that inhibition of TUBB3 sensitized cancer cells to the anti-PD-1 treatment, and tested this hypothesis by the in vitro T cell killing assay. We used the inhibitor of TUBB3, Ixabepilone, which has been approved for clinical use for treating cancer patients, and tested the potential synergistic effect of this drug with anti-PD-1 treatment. The IC50 of Ixabepilone on CMT167 cells was found to be 4 nM (S2A). We then performed the T cell killing assays using the dose range of 1 and 3 nM of Ixabepilone and 20-40 µg/mL of anti-PD-1. We calculated their synergistic effects by the isobologram method using the ComPuSyn software. As shown in Fig. 2 A-B and S2B, Ixabepilone and anti- PD-1 showed significant synergistic effect on CMT167 cells. We then repeated the assays in another lung cancer cell line 344SQ which also responds well to anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. We adjusted the design for this model by changing the concentration of Ixabepilone to be 2.5-5 nM because the IC50 of Ixabepilone for 344SQ cells was 5nM (S2C). Again, we observed significant synergy of Ixabepilone and anti-PD-1 (Fig. 2 C-D and S2D). These results suggest that TUBB3 inhibition and anti-PD-1 treatment have synergistic effect in vitro. Additionally, we examined the efficiency of Tubb3 inhibition in combination with anti-PD-1 immune checkpoint blockade in LLC cells and found that Tubb3 inhibition significantly reduces cell viability when combined with anti-PD-1 immune checkpoint blockade (S2 E-H).To further confirm the function of TUBB3 in anti-PD-1 resistance, we established a model of resistance using the CMT167 cells (Fig. 2 E) by three round of in vivo selections of resistant cells to anti-PD-1. This process established two resistant cell lines, namely CMT-Res-#1 and #2 (R1 and R2 hereafter). We then examined the expression of TUBB3 in these two clones as well as the parental cells by RT-PCR and western blot analyses. We found that the expression of TUBB3 was significantly elevated in the anti-PD-1 resistant clones (Fig. 2 F), suggesting that high expression of TUBB3 is required for anti-PD-1 resistance. Next, we knocked down Tubb3 expression in these cells using shRNA followed by confirmation of the knockdown by RT-PCR and Western blot (Fig. 2 G). We then co-cultured the cells with CD8+ T cells. As shown in Fig. 2 H, the Tubb3 knockdown in the resistant cells restored their sensitivity to T cell killing. Taken together, these data indicate that TUBB3 is a potential driver for anti-PD-1 resistance in lung cancer and that TUBB3 inhibition sensitizes cancer cells to T cell killing in vitro.

TUBB3 inhibition synergizes with anti-PD-1 checkpoint inhibition in vivo

Our results suggest a potential use of the TUBB3 inhibitor to enhance the efficacy of PD-1 blockade treatment of lung cancer. We explored this possibility by implanting CMT167 cells in mice (Fig. 3 A) and treated them with the combination of anti-PD-1 and Ixabepilone. We found that the combination treatment indeed significantly suppressed tumors compared to each single treatment (Fig. 3 B, C). We also examined the potential toxic effect of ixabepilone and anti PD-1 treatment by measuring the body weight and serum AST levels at end-point. We found that there was no statistical difference in either animal weight (Fig. 3 E) or serum AST (Fig. 3 F) We also examined the lung tissues of these animals by HE staining and found that the group of combination treatment had significantly less number of lung metastases nodules (Fig. 3 G-H). These data support the notion that Ixabepilone could be used to synergize with PD-1 blockade for lung cancer treatment.

TUBB3 upregulates PD-L1 expression

To investigate how TUBB3 mediates sensitization to anti-PD-1, we first examined the correlation between TUBB3 and key immune checkpoint molecules using three independent lung cancer cohorts, GSE31210 (n=228), GSE10245 (n=58) and GSE8894 (n=138). We found that Tubb3mRNA expression significantly correlated with PD-L1 (Fig. 4 A and S3A-B). Moreover, using data from the CPTAC-LUAD cohort which contains proteome data for 110 lung cancer patients, we found that protein expressions of TUBB3 and PD-L1 were also positively correlated and that TUBB3-high patients had significantly high PD-L1 expression (Fig. 4 B). We then hypothesized that TUBB3 may directly regulate the expression of PD-L1. To test this hypothesis, we knocked down Tubb3 in CMT167 or 344SQ cells and found that Pd-l1 expression significantly decreased after the Tubb3 knockdown (Fig. 4 C and Fig. S3C). We also found that Pd-l1 expression was significantly elevated in the anti-PD-1 resistant lines R1 and R2 (Fig. 4 D). We then confirmed that Tubb3 interference reduced PD-L1 expression in R1 and R2 cells (Fig. 4 E-F). Furthermore, both Tubb3 and Pd-l1 showed higher expression in 4T1, EO771 and LL2 cell lines that are all non-responsive to anti-PD-1 compared with the responsive cell lines including CMT167 and 344SQ (Fig. 4 G, S3D). Interestingly, gene co-dependency data from the human Cancer Dependency Map (DepMap, https://depmap.org/portal/) project GeCKO 19Q1 which contained screening data of 43 cancer cell lines with the GeCKO CRISPR-Cas9 library revealed that TUBB3 and PD-L1 are significantly co-dependent, further supporting the functional connection between TUBB3 and PD-L1 (Fig. 4 H). Moreover, we examined the PD-L1 expression by immunohistochemistry in tumor tissues isolated from the animal experiment performed in Fig. 3 and found that TUBB3 inhibition in combination with anti PD-1 significantly reduced PD-L1 positive cells (Fig. 4 I and J). One of the important pathways involving TUBB3 is the EMT, and TUBB3 was shown to control the EMT-related transcription factor, SNAI1. We also found that there was a highly conserved DNA binding site of SNAI1 on the PD-L1 promoter region (Fig. 4 K). Therefore, TUBB3 may regulate the expression of PD-L1 via SNAI1. Accordingly, we ectopically expressed SNAI1 in human lung cancer cells H2030 and found that the PD-L1 expression dramatically increased (Fig. 4 L). We then examined the effect of SNAI1 on the PD-L1 promoter by performing the dual luciferase reporter assay. We found that SNAI1 directly enhanced PD-L1 promoter activity (Fig. 4 M). In addition, SNAI1 was found to be highly expressed in cell lines that are non-responsive to anti-PD-1 compared to responsive cell lines (Fig. S3E). These results are consistent with a recently published study showing that SNAI1 directly upregulated PD-L1 expression in prostate cancer cells. The role of TUBB3-PD-L1 axis in tumor immune response was further supported by the result of Gene Set Enrichment Analysis, which indicated that patients with higher expression of TUBB3 showed a higher level of EMT signatures and inflammatory responses (Fig. S3F). To examine the effect of SNAIL1 inhibition on the sensitization to other chemotherapeutic drugs, such as paclitaxel and carboplatin, we treated CMT167 cells with the SNAIL1 inhibitor CYD-19 either alone or in combination with carboplatin and paclitaxel. We found that CYD-19 sensitized CMT167 cells to both chemotherapeutic agents. Based on these findings, we infer that SNAIL1 contributes to resistance against paclitaxel and carboplatin (Fig. S3G). Similarly, it has been demonstrated that SNAIL1 confers resistance to paclitaxel and platinum-based drugs, such as cisplatin, in ovarian cancer. Altogether, these data suggest that TUBB3 upregulates PD-L1 expression in lung cancer, which mediates anti-PD-1 resistance (Fig. 4 N) and also sensitizes to chemotherapy response.

Discussion

In this study, our result of CRSPR/Cas9 library screening for the first time established a link between TUBB3 and anti-PD-1 resistance. TUBB3 belongs to the tubulin family and encodes the βIII-tubulin and it was originally found as a neuronal marker. Recently, prevalence of TUBB3 in human cancers was noted and it was shown to be strongly expressed in a broad spectrum of tumors including lung cancer. TUBB3 serves as a biomarker for resistance to taxanes, and its expression predicts poor outcome in various cancer patients. Meta-analyses in our studies showed TUBB3 as a potential oncogenic factor in lung cancer (Fig. S1G-I). Furthermore, TUBB3 expression was found to be higher in our preclinical model as well as in patients who were resistant to the treatment of immune checkpoint blockade (Fig. 1 I, Fig. 2 F and Fig. 4 G). Notably, we found that in one melanoma cohort, TUBB3 expression was significantly higher in patients who were resistant to anti-PD-1 treatment (Fig. S4A). In another cohort, TUBB3 expression significantly decreased in the responders upon treatment, while the expression remained same in non-responders (Fig. S4B). Most interestingly, patients who were treated with anti-PD-1 and expressed a high level of TUBB3 showed significantly poorer progression-free survival (Fig. S4C). These data indicate a strong correlation of TUBB3 with anti-PD-1 therapeutic outcomes, which is in accordance with our results showing the critical role of TUBB3 in anti-PD-1 response.

There are two potential mechanisms of tumor immune evasion: T cell dysfunction and T cell exclusion, and lung cancer progression is strongly associated with T cell exclusion. It was previously discovered that the mesenchymal phenotype and PD-L1 expression correlate with T cell exclusion. Importantly, TUBB3 was reported to directly regulate the expression of EMT genes such as E-cadherin and Vimentin. Here, we found that TUBB3 expression correlated with the EMT phenotype in patients and upregulated the expression of PD-L1 (Fig. 4 and Fig. S3). We also identified a functional connection between TUBB3 and PD-L1 and found a synergistic effect of TUBB3 inhibition on PD-L1 blockade in cancer cells (Fig. 4 and Fig. S3). In four independent studies (Fig. 4 A-B, Figs. S3A-B), the expression of mRNA and protein of the two genes were significantly correlated. Notably, Homiscsko et al. found that PD-L1-positive melanoma patients (n=541) had 2.5 times higher level of TUBB3 expression. In another study, TUBB3 expression was also found to be associated with PD-L1 expression in clear cell renal cell carcinoma (n=137).

Conclusion

Together, our studies revealed that TUBB3 inhibitor Ixabepilone synergizes the effect of anti-PD-1 (Fig. 2, Fig. 3) in our animal model. Ixabepilone is an FDA approved drug to treat late-stage breast cancer and thus our study provides strong rationale to use Ixabepilone to synergize or overcome the resistance of andi-PD-1 therapy in lung cancer.

Experimental model and subject details

Cell lines, plasmids and reagents

Mouse mammary epithelial cancer cell lines 4T1 and EO771 were purchased from ATCC. Mouse lung cancer cell line CMT167 was purchased from MilliporeSigma. Mouse lung cancer cell line 344SQ was a kind gift from Dr. Jonathan Kurie at MD Anderson Cancer Center. 293T cell was purchased from ATCC. 4T1, CMT167 and 293T cells were cultured in DMEM (#11995, Thermo Fisher) with 10 % FBS. EO771 and 344SQ cells were grown in RPMI1640 (#11875, Thermo Fisher) with 10 % FBS. LentiCas9-Blast (Addgene # 52962) and mouse GeCKOv2 CRISPR knockout pooled library (Addgene #1000000052) were gifts from Dr. Feng Zhang. psPAX2 and pMD2.G (Addgene # 12259, # 12260) were provided by Dr. Didier Trono. pXPR_011 (Addgene # 59702) was a gift from Drs. John Doench & David Root. The PD-L1 promoter luciferase reporter plasmid, which contains the 2kb proximal promoter region of PD-L1 was kindly provided by Dr. Julian Downward (Addgene # 107003). SNAI1 expression plasmid was a gift from Dr. Paul Wade (Addgene # 31697). Tubb3 shRNA (TRCN0000238808) was purchased from MilliporeSigma. Control rat IgG (2A3) and anti-PD-1 (RMP1-14) were purchased from BioXcell. DMSO (D2650) and Ixabepilone (SML2615) was obtained from MilliporeSigma.

Establishment of Cas9-expressing CMT167 and confirmation of CAS9 activity

Virus preparation was done by co-transfection of lentiCas9-Blast with psPAX2 and pMD2.G. Cells after lentiviral infection were selected by 10 µg/mL Blasticidin (Thermo Fisher, R21001). CAS9 overexpression was confirmed by WB using anti-CAS9 antibody (1:1000, 14697T, Cell Signaling Technology). CAS9 activity was validated by infection of pXPR_011, a lentiviral plasmid which contains both EGFP and an EGFP targeting sgRNA, into CAS9 overexpressing CMT167 cells. In the presence of functional levels of Cas9, the sgRNA expressed from the U6 cassette targets EGFP, and thus cells will no longer express EGFP. In addition, T7 endonuclease I assay was performed to confirm the gene editing. In brief, the Cas9 target sites within the EGFP loci were PCR amplified using Q5 Hot Start High-Fidelity Taq (M0494, NEB), 100 ng of genomic DNA and 500 nM each of the forward and reverse primer (Forward: TTCTCAAGTTGGCGGGAGAC and Reverse: TCTCGTTGGGGTCTTTGCTC). PCR was performed as follows: 98°C for 30 s, followed by 35 rounds of 98°C for 5 s, 68°C for 10 s, 72°C for 20 s, with a final extension at 72°C for 5 min. PCR products were purified using the PCR purification kit (Qiagen #28104). The T7 endonuclease I assay (M0302, NEB) was performed following the manufacture's protocol. In brief, 200 ng PCR products were heat-denatured and re-annealed in a thermocycler using the following hybridization conditions: 95°C for 5 min, ramp from 95 to 85°C at −2°C per second, ramp from 85 to 25°C at −0.1°C per second, followed by incubation with T7 endonuclease at 37°C for 15 min. After the digestion, products were analyzed by gel electrophoresis followed by visualization.

Experimental animals

Experimental protocols were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Wake Forest Health Science. Mice were housed in a 12h light/12 h dark cycle with temperature controlled room. The mice were fed with a standard chow and water ad libitum.

In vivo confirmation of response to anti-PD-1 for CMT167

Briefly, 1 million cells were injected in the flank of 8-10 week old female C57BL/6 mice. Mice were randomized into two groups by simple randomization (N= 3 for each group) and treated with either IgG or anti-PD-1 antibody by i.p. injection at 200 µg/mice every three days until humane endpoint. Tumor volume was measured and calculated as V = LW2/2.

In vivo CRISPR screening in CMT167 lung cancer cells

Lentivirus preparation for the mouse GeCKOv2 CRISPR knockout pooled library A (a total of 67.4 K sgRNAs) was done by Duke Genomic Core. CMT167-CAS9 cells were infected at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.2 to ensure that the majority of cells were infected with only one sgRNA-carrying virus. Cells were selected with 1 µg/mL puromycin and expanded in vitro for two weeks (genomic DNA sample collection as baseline). The quality of the library cells were confirmed by the following: First, multiple single cell clones were isolated from the baseline cell library, and genomic DNA for each single cell clone were isolated and PCR analysis was performed using the primers for the sgRNA insert (Forward: GGACTATCATATGCTTACCGTAACT and Reverse: CGCCAAAGTGGATCTCTGCT). PCR was performed as follows: 98°C for 30 s, followed by 30 rounds of 98°C for 5 s, 64°C for 10 s, 72°C for 15 s, with a final extension at 72°C for 5 min. PCR products (387bp) were sequenced to make sure each single cell contains only single sgRNA. Second, genomic DNA from the baseline cell library were prepared and sequenced to make sure that >90 % of all sgRNAs were present in the baseline cell library. Third, a Gini index was calculated for the baseline cell library to confirm the even distribution of sgRNAs. The sgRNA library consisted of ∼70,000 guided RNAs, and we expanded the cells transduced with library to achieve ∼ 200 fold coverage of the library. Then we implanted the 28 million cells in 20 mice (1.4 million cells /mouse, 14 million/group) followed by treatment with either empty vehicle or anti PD-1 immune checkpoint blockade for in-vivo selection. Tumor sizes were recorded until humane endpoint and as a confirmation of the anti-PD-1 selection pressure. Tumor DNA was prepared from whole tumor tissue using the genomic DNA isolation kit (D4075, Zymo Research). PCR amplification of sgRNA and following sequencing of the product was performed to determine sgRNA abundance at Duke Genomics Core. Significantly enriched or depleted sgRNAs were identified using the MAGeCK algorithm.

Prediction of immunotherapeutic response

The Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE) algorithm was used to predict clinical response to immune checkpoints. The TCGA-LUAD and TCGA-LUSC pan-cancer normalized gene expression data were downloaded from UCSC Xena (https://xena.ucsc.edu/) and analyzed by TIDE to calculate the TIDE score. Patients with TIDE scores > 1 are considered non-responders and those with scores < 0 are considered responders.

T cell killing assay

Cancer cells were inoculated in the flank of syngeneic mice. Three weeks later, CD8+ T cells were isolated by MACS from the mouse spleen, and they were incubated with cancer cell lysate. T cells were activated and expanded by CD3/28 microbeads (130-093-627, Miltenyl Biotec, Auburn, CA, USA) and 100 u/mL IL2 (575404, Biolegend, San Diego, CA, USA) for 7 d. The T cells were then co-cultured with tumor cells at 20:1 effector to target cell ratio for 3 d. Cells were washed with ice cold PBS 3 times to remove T cells and survived tumor cells were stained with 0.2 % crystal violet. The dyes were dissolved in 10 % acetic acid and readouts were obtained at 490nm with plate reader (Molecular devices). To examine the synergistic drug effects, different concentrations of anti-PD-1 (20 or 40 µg/mL) or Ixabepilone (1 or 3 nM for CMT167; 2.5, or 5 nM for 344SQ cells) or combinations of both drugs were used. Synergy was calculated using the ComPusyn and the SynergyFinder tools.

Establishment of anti-PD-1 resistant CMT167 cells

Animal experiments were performed in line with a protocol approved by Wake Forest Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. To generate the anti-PD-1 resistant cell line, CMT167 parental cells (0.5 × 106 in 100 μL of sterile PBS) were injected subcutaneously into the flank of syngeneic C57BL/6 mice (female, 8-10 weeks old). The anti-PD-1 or control IgG antibodies (10 mg/kg) were injected into the mice, starting on day 7 after inoculation and every three days for a total of eight doses. The nonresponsive tumor was digested into single cells and cultured in vitro and subjected to another two cycles of in vivo passage in the syngeneic mice with anti-PD-1 treatment (Fig. 2 E). The cells were designated as CMT-Res-#1 and #2 (R1 and R2). All animals were randomized before the experiment and sacrificed when reached the human endpoint.

Tumor inoculation and treatments

Animal experiments were performed in line with a protocol approved by Wake Forest Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. CMT167 cells (1 × 105 cells) were subcutaneously injected into both flanks of 8-10 weeks old C57BL/6 mice. Sample size was decided according to a previous publication. The mice were randomized into four groups (N=5 each group, N=20 in total) by simple randomization that were treated with vehicle control, anti-PD-1 alone (10 mg/kg, i.p. injection every three days), Ixebepiline (20 mg/kg, i.p. injection every week), or combination of anti-PD-1 and Ixabepilone, respectively. Tumor volume was measured and calculated using the formula: V = LW2/2. All animals were randomized before the experiment and sacrificed when reached the human endpoint.

Quantitative real-time PCR

For RNA isolation from cultured cells, the Direct-zol RNA Miniprep kit (Zymo Research, R2051) was used. For RNA isolation from FFPE sections, one section was H&E stained to allow the determination of tumor areas and then a razor blade was used to microdissect the tumors under a microscope. RNA was isolated using the RNeasy FFPE Kit (Qiagen, #73504). cDNA synthesis was performed using the iScript™ cDNA Synthesis Kit (# 1708891, Bio-Rad). Realtime PCR was performed using CFX Conect (Bio-Rad) and the iTaq SYBR Green supermix (Bio-Rad). Human GAPDH or mouse beta-actin were used as an internal control. Quantification of gene expression levels was performed using the comparative ▵▵CT method. Primer sequences used in real time PCR for each gene are listed in Supplementary table S2 and were adopted from the PrimerBank or a previous publication or designed using Primer-BLAST.

Immunoblotting

Cell lysate preparation and Western blotting were performed as previously described. Briefly, cells or tissues were lysed using RIPA buffer (Thermo Fisher, 89900) supplemented with the protease and phosphatase inhibitors. Protein concentrations were determined using the Pierce BCA protein assay kit (Thermo Fisher) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Cell lysates containing equal amounts of proteins were subjected to SDS-PAGE, transferred to a nitrocellulose membrane, incubated with antibodies, and visualized using Amersham ECL (RPN2236, GE Healthcare). The following antibodies were used in this study: CAS9 (CST #14697, 1:1000), GFP (Thermo Fisher #MA5-15256, 1:2000), TUBB3 (CST #5666, 1:1000), PD-L1 (CST #13684, 1:1000), Alpha-tubulin (CST #2125, 1:2000), GAPDH (CST #5174, 1:2000).

Immunohistochemistry

Sections of 5-µm thickness from formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded samples from our histopathology archive at the Tumor Tissue and Pathology Shared Resource (TTPSR) at Wake Forest Baptist Comprehensive Cancer Center were stained for TUBB3. The sections were deparaffinized and antigens were retrieved by heating the slides in Tris-EDTA buffer (pH9) at 95°C for 10min. The slides were treated with 3 % H2O2 and incubated overnight at 4°C with anti-TUBB3 antibody (CST #5666, 1:100). Isotype control antibody was used as a negative control. The sections were then incubated with secondary antibodies and visualized using the DAB substrate chromogen (K3467, Dako) followed by counter-staining with hematoxylin (Ab245880, Abcam, Cambridge, MA, USA). Images were taken under a microscope (BZ-X710, Keyence) connected to a computer equipped with imaging software (BZ-X Viewer). Staining intensity (0, 1+, 2+, or 3+) was determined for each cell in a fixed field. H scores were calculated using the following formula: [1 × (% cells 1+) + 2 × (% cells 2+) + 3 × (% cells 3+)] as previously described.

Dual luciferase reporter assay

Dual luciferase assay was performed as previously described. In brief, 400 ng of PD-L1 promoter reporter plasmid (Addgene # 107003) and 1500 ng of vector control or SNAI1 expression plasmid (Addgene # 31697) were co-transfected with 10 ng of phRG-TK Renilla luciferase internal control plasmid (Promega) into 293T cells using Lipofectamine 3000 reagent (Thermo Fisher). After 48 hours, luciferase activities were measured using the Dual-luciferase reporter assay system (Promega, E1910). Data were presented as the ratio of firefly to renilla luciferase activity (FLuc/RLuc).

Cancer dependency map portal data analysis

The depmap portal (https://depmap.org/portal/) and the CRISPR (Gecko) Public 19Q1 data set were used for this analysis. No samples were excluded from the data set in this analysis. Following the depmap instruction, the dependency scores of genes were downloaded and then plotted as dot plots. Pearson correlation was calculated using Prism.

Gene set enrichment analyses

Gene set enrichement analyses was performed similarly as previous described. In brief, the Gene Cluster Text file (.gct) was generated using the TCGA-LUAD cohort and the categorical class file (.cls) was generated based on the TUBB3 expression level in each patient. Comparison was done between top 25 % and lower 25 % of patients (N=129 each). Normalized enrichment scores (NES) and p values for the 50 Hallmark gene sets were calculated using the GSEA software. The number of permutations was set to 1000.

Statistical analyses

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism. A two-tailed Student's t test was used to determine statistical differences between two groups, and one-way analysis of variance was used to compare three or more groups. P<0.05 were considered statistically significant.
