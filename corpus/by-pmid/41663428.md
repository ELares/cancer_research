# Triple targeting of STING, TGF-β, and PD-L1 boosts CXCL16-CXCR6 signaling for potent antitumor response.

## Abstract

Antibodies targeting TGF-β and PD-L1 initially showed promise as second-generation PD-L1 agents. However, consecutive trial failures have limited their clinical success. Our study reveals that the efficacy of the TGF-β×PD-L1 bispecific antibody (BsAb) is compromised by insufficient activation of innate immune responses. To address this, we combine STING agonists with the BsAb, significantly enhancing tumor suppression beyond that achieved with standard STING agonist plus anti-PD-L1 combinations in preclinical models. Unexpectedly, even STING agonist monotherapy is improved by TGF-β blockade, suggesting that TGF-β suppresses STING-driven immune activation. We find that this synergy is mediated by the CXCL16-CXCR6 axis, where STING activation and TGF-β blockade promote CXCL16 expression in macrophages and dendritic cells, recruiting and sustaining cytotoxic CXCR6+ T cells. Additionally, PD-L1 blockade further enhances their antitumor activity. To optimize this strategy, we develop Y101S, an antibody-drug conjugate targeting TGF-β, PD-L1, and STING, which demonstrates superior tumor control and immune modulation in preclinical models. These findings highlight the therapeutic potential of this triple-targeting approach.

Introduction

The advent of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), particularly those targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis, has revolutionized cancer therapy. Despite their success, many patients do not respond to ICI therapy due to resistance. In these cases, PD-1/PD-L1 signaling may not be the primary mechanism driving tumor immune evasion, suggesting that other immune pathways are also compromised. Addressing these imbalances within the tumor microenvironment (TME) is essential for restoring effective antitumor immunity.

Combining ICIs with other therapeutic strategies has emerged as a promising approach to enhance their efficacy and overcome resistance. Numerous studies have identified agents that exhibit synergistic effects when combined with PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors. However, these combination strategies have achieved only limited success. For instance, the STING agonist ADU-S100, when combined with PD-1 blockade, yields an overall response rate of only 10% in patients with advanced tumors, highlighting the need for more effective approaches. Understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying ICI combination therapy and the interplay of signaling pathways in the TME is crucial for advancing immunotherapy.

TGF-β plays a dual role in cancer. In early tumor development, it suppresses tumor growth by regulating cell proliferation, apoptosis, and differentiation. However, in advanced tumors, mutations in TGF-β signaling often inactivate its tumor-suppressive effects. Instead, TGF-β promotes tumor progression by inducing epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), enhancing angiogenesis, and activating cancer-associated fibroblasts. Elevated TGF-β levels in the TME also contribute to immune evasion by inhibiting T cell and NK cell activity, impairing dendritic cell (DC) antigen presentation, and promoting immunosuppressive cell differentiation. High TGF-β expression correlates with poor prognosis and resistance to immunotherapy, making TGF-β inhibition a potential strategy for improving ICI efficacy. To counteract TGF-β-mediated immune suppression and resistance to immune checkpoint blockade, we developed YM101, an anti-TGF-β×PD-L1 bispecific antibody (BsAb). While YM101 demonstrates greater efficacy than PD-L1 inhibitors alone, some tumors remain unresponsive. This challenge is common among second-generation anti-PD-L1 antibodies, such as M7824 and SHR-1701, underscoring the complexity of TGF-β signaling and the need to investigate the mechanisms behind treatment failures.

Our preliminary studies revealed a strong synergistic effect between YM101 and STING agonists. In multiple preclinical tumor models, adding a STING agonist markedly inhibited tumor growth and extended survival in YM101-treated mice. Logically, STING agonists initiate antitumor immune responses early in the cancer-immunity cycle, enhancing innate immunity. However, the mechanisms underlying the interaction among these three signaling pathways remain unclear. Investigating how these pathways cooperate to regulate antitumor immunity is essential.

In this work, we clarify how STING activation enhances the antitumor activity of TGF-β and PD-L1 dual blockade. We show that STING agonists synergize with the anti-TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb to produce stronger tumor control than conventional combinations in multiple murine models. Mechanistically, STING activation together with TGF-β blockade increases CXCL16 expression in myeloid cells and sustains cytotoxic CXCR6+ T cells, which are further potentiated by PD-L1 blockade. Building on these insights, we develop Y101S, an immunostimulatory antibody conjugate (ISAC) that integrates the anti-TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb with a cleavable STING agonist payload. This ISAC simultaneously targets all three pathways, offering strategic advantages over conventional multi-drug combinations and potentially improving therapeutic outcomes in cancer.

Results

Enhancing antitumor efficacy with STING agonist and YM101

As we previously reported, YM101 demonstrated considerable antitumor efficacy in the EMT-6 model, with most tumors responding positively to treatment (Fig. 1A). However, a subset of tumors exhibited limited response, defined by a tumor growth inhibition (TGI) value below 50% compared with the control group (Fig. 1B). To investigate the molecular basis underlying this heterogeneous response, we performed bulk RNA-seq analysis on YM101-sensitive and YM101-resistant tumors. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) revealed that responder tumors were markedly enriched in innate immune response-related pathways (Fig. 1C). To further delineate the specific molecular mechanisms driving innate activation, we examined all significantly enriched Gene Ontology (GO) terms and focused on those directly associated with immune signaling and cytokine responses. According to normalized enrichment scores (NES), the top five pathways were response to IFN-β, response to IFN-II, IL-1β production, TNF production, and IL-6 production, all with false discovery rate (FDR) values below 0.05 (Fig. 1D). Except for the IFN-II response, these immune and cytokine pathways represent major downstream programs of STING signaling, suggesting that activation of the STING pathway is a defining feature of YM101 responder tumors.

We next validated these findings in another previously established YM101-sensitive model CT26. Consistent with the EMT-6 results, a subset of CT26 tumors displayed limited response to YM101, while GSEA of responder versus nonresponder tumors confirmed significant enrichment of innate immune response and response to IFN-β pathways (Fig. 1E–H). The overlapping upregulated genes between the two responder models included canonical STING downstream effectors, such as Ifnb1 and Tnf, as well as the tissue-resident T cell-associated gene Cxcr6 (Fig. 1I). Functional enrichment of these shared genes confirmed the activation of innate immune response, cytokine–cytokine receptor interaction, response to IFN-β, IL-6 production, and the cytosolic DNA-sensing pathway (Fig. 1J).

To further validate these transcriptomic findings across models with distinct responsiveness to YM101, we compared the gene expression profiles of YM101-sensitive EMT-6 and CT26 tumors with the resistant B16 model. As we previously observed, B16 tumors showed poor response to YM101, with no significant reduction in tumor growth compared to the isotype control. In contrast, EMT-6 and CT26 models exhibited substantial tumor growth inhibition and improved survival upon YM101 treatment, confirming their sensitivity. Comparative transcriptomic analysis revealed that EMT-6 and CT26 tumors exhibited stronger enrichment of innate immune activation and cytosolic DNA-sensing pathways relative to B16 (Supplementary Fig. 1A–D). The overlapping upregulated genes from the EMT-6 vs B16 and CT26 vs B16 comparisons were also significantly enriched in innate immune response-related pathways (Supplementary Fig. 1E, F). At the protein level, cytokine profiling in EMT-6 tumors demonstrated that responder tumors expressed higher levels of STING downstream cytokines, including IFN-β, TNF-α, CCL3, CCL4, CCL5, CXCL9, and CXCL10 (Fig. 1K). Collectively, these results suggest that robust innate immune activation and elevated STING pathway activity are hallmarks of YM101-sensitive tumors.

Given this evidence, we hypothesized that YM101 resistance may result from insufficient innate immune activation, which could be overcome through pharmacologic stimulation of the STING pathway. To test this hypothesis, we evaluated the combination of the STING agonist MSA-2 with YM101 (M + Y) in EMT-6, CT26, and B16 models. The combination therapy significantly inhibited tumor growth and prolonged survival in multiple models (Fig. 1L–Q and Supplementary Fig. 1G, H). In addition, tumor rechallenge experiments showed that mice which had achieved complete regression of the primary tumors after M + Y treatment were protected from subsequent tumor outgrowth, indicating the induction of durable systemic antitumor immunity (Supplementary Fig. 2). Notably, triple-targeted therapy produced superior tumor control and survival benefits compared with the commonly tested STING agonist plus anti-PD-L1 antibody combination (M + α-P). Moreover, MSA-2 plus anti-TGF-β (M + α-T) also outperformed MSA-2 monotherapy, indicating that TGF-β may act as a limiting factor in STING-mediated antitumor immunity. Together, these findings extend beyond overcoming resistance to dual TGF-β/PD-L1 blockade. They highlight the broader therapeutic potential of integrating TGF-β inhibition into STING agonist-based immunotherapy, suggesting that simultaneous targeting of STING, TGF-β, and PD-L1 may offer a powerful strategy to achieve superior clinical outcomes.

Triple targeting of STING, TGF-β, and PD-L1 expands Cxcr6+ T cells

To investigate how STING activation enhances the antitumor effects of the anti-TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb, we treated EMT-6 tumor-bearing mice with MSA-2 and YM101, followed by tumor tissue collection for scRNA-seq (Fig. 2A–C and Supplementary Fig. 3A, B). Previous studies have shown that immunotherapy efficacy often correlates with the expansion of specific T cell subsets. Therefore, we conducted differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis on T cells from all four treatment groups and identified 53 significantly upregulated genes in the combination group, many associated with immune-mediated killing (Fig. 2D and Supplementary Fig. 3C).

Among these genes, we focused on Cxcr6, which encodes the chemokine receptor CXCR6, a key factor in tumor tissue residency (Fig. 2E). Using median Cxcr6 expression, we categorized T cells into Cxcr6+ and Cxcr6− populations. The proportion of Cxcr6+ T cells increased in both the YM101 and MSA-2 groups and peaked with combination therapy (Fig. 2F). Comparison of gene expression profiles between Cxcr6+ and Cxcr6− T cells revealed considerable enrichment in pathways related to cell killing, lymphocyte-mediated immunity, T cell proliferation, T cell activation, and Th1 cytokine production (Fig. 2G and Supplementary Fig. 3D). GSEA further confirmed that Cxcr6+ T cells exhibited high cytotoxic activity, including granzyme-mediated cell death and leukocyte-mediated cytotoxicity (Fig. 2H). These cells also showed increased enrichment in antitumor immune response pathways (Fig. 2I). Additionally, Cxcr6+ T cells expressed higher levels of key cytotoxic genes, such as Fasl, Prf1, Gzmk, and Ifng, as well as T cell activation markers (Trbc2, Pdcd1, and Il2rb) (Fig. 2J).

These findings indicate that Cxcr6+ T cells have potent immune-killing capabilities. Since approximately 90% of the Cxcr6+ T cells identified by scRNA-seq are Cd8a+ (Supplementary Fig. 3E, F), we hypothesize that the enhanced antitumor effects observed with combination therapy result from CXCR6+CD8+ T cell expansion. Consistent with this, enrichment analysis of tumor cells revealed significant downregulation of oncogenic signaling pathways in the combination therapy group (Fig. 2K). In line with an immune-mediated mechanism, in vitro proliferation assays showed that M + Y treatment did not exert appreciable direct growth inhibition on tumor cells (Supplementary Fig.4). These results suggest that MSA-2 and YM101 suppress tumor progression by expanding CXCR6+CD8+ T cells, thereby enhancing immune-mediated tumor cell killing (Fig. 2L).

STING agonist and anti-TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb enhance CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling

To investigate how the combination therapy increases Cxcr6+ T cells, we analyzed scRNA-seq data, focusing on chemokine signaling. Since CXCR6 is involved in classical chemokine signaling, we examined cell-to-cell communication in the TME (Supplementary Fig. 5). Our analysis showed that the combination therapy significantly enhanced CXCL signaling to T cells compared with other treatments (Fig. 3A and Supplementary Fig. 6A). Specifically, macrophages and cDCs were the main sources of CXCL signaling to T cells, with the strongest signals observed in the combination therapy group (Fig. 3B–D). Among CXCL family members, CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling was notably enhanced, aligning with the expansion of Cxcr6+ T cells (Fig. 3E and Supplementary Fig. 6B, C). Macrophages and cDCs were the primary sources of CXCL16, with their interactions with T cells playing a dominant role in driving CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling (Fig. 3F–H).

We hypothesized that increased Cxcl16 expression in macrophages and cDCs contributed to Cxcr6+ T cell expansion. DEG analysis identified 49 upregulated genes in macrophages from the combination group, including Cxcl16 and antigen-presentation-related genes like Cd40 and H2-Aa (Fig. 3I, J and Supplementary Fig. 7A, B). Notably, Cxcl16 expression was increased across nearly all macrophage subsets rather than being confined to a specific subset (Fig. 3K). Similarly, Cxcl16 expression was elevated in cDCs, with the highest levels in the combination therapy group (Supplementary Fig. 7C–G). These scRNA-seq findings were validated by bulk RNA-seq analysis, which confirmed a significant increase in Cxcl16 expression in B16 tumor tissues treated with M + Y therapy (Supplementary Fig. 7H).

Validation of enhanced CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling at the protein level

Building on our scRNA-seq findings, which indicated enhanced CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling following the combination treatment, we validated these results at the protein level using the EMT-6 model (Supplementary Fig. 8). Tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells were analyzed after treatment with M + Y. Flow cytometry revealed that combination therapy significantly increased the number of tumor-infiltrating CXCR6+CD8+ T cells. Additionally, the highest number of cytotoxic, activated, and proliferating CD8+ T cells was observed in the M + Y group (Fig. 4A). Compared to their CXCR6− counterparts, CXCR6+ T cells exhibited greater cytotoxicity, activation, and proliferation potential (Fig. 4B–E).

To further investigate the functional differences between CXCR6+ and CXCR6−CD8+ T cells, we isolated these subsets from EMT-6 tumor tissues for proteome analysis (Fig. 4F). Consistent with the flow cytometry data, CXCR6+CD8+ T cells expressed higher levels of immune-killing-related proteins, including perforin, granzyme-B, granzyme-K, and Rab27a, as well as inflammatory proteins, such as CD48 and IFN pathway components (STAT1, IIGP1, and GBP1) (Fig. 4G). Enrichment analysis of these differentially expressed proteins identified pathways associated with immune killing and clearance, including defense response, T cell-mediated cytotoxicity, NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity, and cellular responses to IFN-β (Fig. 4H).

CXCR6+CD8+ T cells exhibited notably higher PD-1 expression (Fig. 4I), suggesting an active antitumor immune response that could be enhanced by blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway. To test this, OT-1-derived CXCR6+CD8+ T cells were co-cultured with E.G7-OVA cells. CXCR6+CD8+ T cells demonstrated stronger tumor-specific killing, and their cytotoxic activity further increased upon treatment with an anti-PD-L1 antibody (Fig. 4J).

Additionally, we observed a significant increase in CXCL16 expression in tumor-infiltrating macrophages and DCs following treatment with M + Y (Fig. 4K). This increase in CXCL16 expression at the protein level was consistent with our scRNA-seq data. However, CXCL16 expression was similarly increased in the M + α-T and M + Y groups, indicating that PD-L1 blockade did not further boost CXCL16 production beyond STING activation plus TGF-β blockade. In contrast, PD-L1 blockade predominantly affects CXCR6+ T cells, which highly express PD-1. Together, these results indicate that triple targeting of STING, TGF-β, and PD-L1 enhances both the source (macrophages and DCs) and the target (CD8+ T cells) of the CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling axis.

CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling drives the antitumor effects of triple-targeted therapy

To define the role of CXCR6+CD8+ T cells in the combination therapy, we generated a conditional knockout (cKO) mouse model in which Cxcr6 expression was selectively silenced in T cells (Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre) (Fig. 5A). In these mice, the synergistic antitumor effect of MSA-2 combined with YM101 was abolished. However, adoptive transfer of CXCR6+CD8+ T cells from Cxcr6fl/fl mice restored the combination therapy’s efficacy, confirming the critical role of CXCR6+CD8+ T cells in mediating its antitumor effects (Fig. 5B–D). Immunofluorescence (IF) staining of tumor tissues confirmed the loss of CXCR6+ T cells in Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre mice, while adoptive transfer reinstated tumor-infiltrating CXCR6+CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5E, F). To further elucidate the function of CXCR6+CD8+ T cells, transcriptome sequencing was performed on tumor tissues from mice receiving adoptive cell transfer. The results showed that CXCR6+CD8+ T cell transfer significantly enhanced immune-killing pathways, further supporting their role in promoting antitumor immunity (Fig. 5G–I).

To further establish that the synergistic antitumor effect of M + Y is mediated by CXCR6⁺ CD8⁺ T cells rather than other effector populations, such as NK cells, we depleted NK cells in tumor-bearing mice using anti-NK1.1 antibody. In this NK-depleted setting, the enhanced antitumor effect of M + Y was preserved and remained significantly superior to either MSA-2 or YM101 monotherapy (Supplementary Fig. 9A–C). In addition, to exclude the contribution from CD4⁺ T cells, we performed adoptive transfer of CXCR6⁺ CD4⁺ T cells into Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre mice. CXCR6⁺CD4⁺ T cell transfer failed to rescue the antitumor effect of M + Y (Supplementary Fig. 9D–F). These findings indicate that CXCR6⁺CD8⁺ T cells play a central role in mediating the antitumor efficacy of the combination therapy.

We hypothesized that the expansion of CXCR6+CD8+ T cells in response to triple-targeted therapy is driven by increased CXCL16 signaling. To test this, we used a neutralizing antibody against CXCL16 to disrupt the CXCL16–CXCR6 axis in vivo. Blocking CXCL16 significantly reduced the antitumor effects of M + Y (Fig. 6A–C). In vivo flow cytometry further demonstrated that the increase in tumor-infiltrating CXCR6+CD8+ T cells observed with the combination therapy was abolished by CXCL16 neutralization (Fig. 6D; Supplementary Figs. 10 and 11). Other cytotoxic CD8+ T cell subsets, including granzyme B+, IFN-γ+, perforin+, CD107a+, TNF-α+, Ki67+, and PD-1+CD8+ T cells, followed the same trend, indicating that CXCL16 blockade suppressed the enhanced antitumor T cell response induced by the combination therapy.

To further examine the impact of CXCL16 neutralization on immune responses, we performed bulk RNA-seq analysis. The results revealed that M + Y treatment upregulated Cxcr6 and other immune-killing-related genes, which were significantly downregulated by CXCL16 blockade (Fig. 6E, F). Enrichment analysis of DEGs confirmed that the combination therapy activated pathways related to chemokine signaling, adaptive immunity, T cell co-stimulation, T cell receptor signaling, T cell activation, and T cell proliferation (Fig. 6G). By contrast, these pathways were significantly inhibited by CXCL16 blockade. Using the transcriptome expression profile, we constructed several T cell immunity-related signatures. Calculating the scores for these signatures revealed that CXCL16 blockade significantly suppressed the enhanced T cell responses induced by the combination therapy (Fig. 6H, I). Overall, these in vivo data support our hypothesis that the synergistic antitumor effects of triple targeting of TGF-β, PD-L1, and STING depend on CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling.

STING signaling induces CXCL16 expression by STAT1

Our in vivo flow cytometry data suggested that STING activation and TGF-β blockade enhance CXCL16 expression, whereas PD-L1 blockade has negligible effects. To validate this in a human system, we differentiated macrophages and DCs from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) of healthy donors and evaluated CXCL16 expression following STING pathway stimulation. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) demonstrated that STING activation markedly increased CXCL16 mRNA and protein levels in human macrophages and DCs, while recombinant TGF-β1 strongly suppressed this induction (Fig. 7A). An anti-TGF-β antibody reversed this inhibition, whereas PD-L1 blockade plays a minimal role in CXCL16 regulation.

To determine whether this effect occurs at the transcriptional level, we generated a CXCL16-luciferase reporter (CXCL16-Luc). Bioluminescence assays confirmed that TGF-β1 suppressed STING agonist-induced CXCL16 transcription, while PD-L1 blockade again had little impact. Consistent with these findings, murine bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) and DCs (BMDCs) displayed the same regulatory pattern (Supplementary Fig. 12A), confirming that this mechanism is conserved across species.

CXCL16 is not a classical downstream target of STING signaling, and its regulatory mechanisms remain unclear. To investigate its transcriptional regulation, we performed transcription factor prediction using the Cistrome_DB database. STAT1 emerged as the most likely regulator of CXCL16 expression (Supplementary Fig. 12B, C). ChIP-seq data analysis revealed strong STAT1 binding peaks near the CXCL16 promoter (Fig. 7B). Correlation analysis using TCGA data further confirmed a significant positive correlation between STAT1, TMEM173, and CXCL16 expression across multiple cancer types, suggesting that STAT1 is a key driver of CXCL16 gene expression (Fig. 7C). Next, we identified three potential STAT1 binding sites within the CXCL16 promoter using the JASPAR database. A dual-luciferase reporter assay showed that mutating or truncating the R1 site nearly abolished CXCL16 promoter activity (Fig. 7D, E). ChIP-PCR further confirmed STAT1 binding at the R1 site, demonstrating that STAT1 is essential for STING agonist-induced CXCL16 transcription (Fig. 7F).

TGF-β blockade enhances STING-driven CXCL16 expression

The canonical STING pathway induces IFN-I, which exerts its downstream biological effects via the IFNAR–JAK1/2–STAT1 axis. In human macrophages and DCs, si-STAT1 or IFNAR1 blockade abrogated STING agonist-induced CXCL16 expression, indicating that IFN-I–STAT1 signaling is required for CXCL16 induction. The results confirmed that STING agonists drive CXCL16 expression through IFN-I signaling and subsequent STAT1 activation (Fig. 7G). TGF-β1 is a known inhibitor of STING–IFN-I signaling. In vitro, we validated that TGF-β1 downregulates IFN-I expression (Fig. 7H). Western blot analysis further demonstrated that exogenous TGF-β1 suppresses IRF3 phosphorylation within the STING signaling cascade (Fig. 7I). Previous studies suggest this inhibition may involve reactive oxygen species (ROS)-mediated translocation of HDAC4 from the nucleus to the cytoplasm. Consistent with these findings, we observed marked redistribution of HDAC4 in human macrophages following TGF-β1 treatment, accompanied by reduced p-IRF3 levels, and both changes were reversed by ROS inhibitor (ROSi) (Supplementary Fig. 12D). Accordingly, treatment with either ROSi or si-HDAC4 effectively reversed TGF-β1-mediated suppression of IFN-β and CXCL16 expression (Fig. 7J).

Our scRNA-seq data indicated that myeloid cells, particularly macrophages and DCs, are the primary sources of CXCL16 production (Fig. 7K). Consistently, in vitro stimulation with MSA-2 induced only minimal CXCL16 upregulation in murine tumor cells, whereas BMDMs and BMDCs showed robust CXCL16 induction (Supplementary Fig. 12E). Given that many tumor cells harbor defects in IFN-I signaling, we hypothesized that myeloid cells are key mediators of STING activation and TGF-β blockade, and that the synergistic antitumor effects of MSA-2 and YM101 rely on an intact STING–IFN-I–STAT1–CXCL16 axis within them. To test this hypothesis, we generated Stat1fl/fl Lyz2-CreERT2 mice to selectively silence Stat1 in myeloid cells (Fig. 7L). In this model, the synergistic effect of MSA-2 and YM101 was completely abolished, demonstrating that the enhanced antitumor activity of the triple-targeted therapy depends on intact STAT1 signaling in myeloid cells (Fig. 7M–O). This loss of CXCL16 expression in tumor-infiltrating macrophages and DCs was further confirmed by flow cytometry (Supplementary Fig. 12F).

Development and antitumor efficacy of the TGF-β×PD-L1–STING ISAC Y101S

Building on the above findings, we aimed to develop a therapeutic agent capable of simultaneously targeting three key signaling pathways. To achieve this, we synthesized a lysosome-cleavable linker using Mal-PEG4-NHS and Fmoc-Val-Cit-PAB, and then conjugated YM101 to the STING agonist MSA-2 via this linker to construct the ISAC Y101S (Fig. 8A). In principle, Y101S is designed to inhibit TGF-β and PD-L1 signaling while promoting CXCL16 expression in myeloid cells through antibody-mediated internalization and intracellular release of MSA-2.

In both CT26 and 4T1 tumor models, Y101S demonstrated superior antitumor efficacy compared with YM101, significantly suppressing tumor growth and prolonging survival in tumor-bearing mice (Fig. 8B–G). Notably, its antitumor activity was comparable to that of the free combination of YM101 with standard-dose MSA-2 (50 mg/kg), while containing only 0.05 mg/kg of conjugated MSA-2 payload per 9 mg/kg Y101S dose. The potent efficacy of Y101S was further confirmed in the EMT-6 model (Fig. 8H–J). Consistent with our mechanistic hypothesis that the CXCL16–CXCR6 axis is a key mediator of response, the enhanced antitumor effects of Y101S were dependent on CXCL16 signaling (Fig. 8H–J and Supplementary Fig. 13). Moreover, tumor rechallenge experiments demonstrated that Y101S induced durable immunological memory, effectively preventing the outgrowth of recurrent tumors (Supplementary Fig. 14). Flow cytometry analysis of EMT-6 tumors after Y101S treatment revealed a marked increase in CXCL16⁺ macrophages and DCs, accompanied by an elevated frequency of CXCR6⁺CD8⁺ T cells (Fig. 8K). In line with these findings, multiplex IF staining of 4T1 tumors further confirmed that Y101S treatment markedly increased the infiltration of CXCR6⁺CD8⁺ T cells (Supplementary Fig. 15).

Based on the bispecific design of YM101, PD-L1⁺ myeloid populations are expected to be the primary cellular targets through which Y101S exerts its effects. Indeed, in all murine models examined, we detected substantial fractions of PD-L1⁺ myeloid cells within the TME (Supplementary Fig. 16A). Following Y101S treatment, these PD-L1⁺ myeloid subsets displayed higher CXCL16 expression than their PD-L1− counterparts (Supplementary Fig. 16B), indicating that PD-L1⁺ myeloid cells are preferentially engaged to drive CXCL16 production and amplify the CXCL16–CXCR6 cascade. PD-L1 expression can be induced not only as a feedback response to immune activation but also by tumor-promoting cues. In advanced malignancies, TGF-β and PD-L1 signaling are often co-upregulated and jointly contribute to immune evasion. Analysis of TCGA datasets revealed a significant positive correlation between TGF-β1 and PD-L1 expression across most tumor types (Supplementary Fig. 16C), and our multiplex IF staining data further showed that regions with strong TGF-β1 staining also exhibited higher PD-L1 expression (Supplementary Fig. 16D). Consistent with preclinical studies of the PD-L1/TGF-β trap M7824, which reported TGF-β-driven upregulation of PD-L1 on tumor cells, we observed that exogenous TGF-β1 robustly enhanced PD-L1 expression on BMDMs and BMDCs in vitro (Supplementary Fig. 16E). Together, these findings suggest that a pre-existing, strongly T cell-inflamed TME is not a strict prerequisite for Y101S activity. Instead, late-stage tumors that have hijacked TGF-β signaling to induce PD-L1 on myeloid cells and evade immune surveillance may represent the most relevant indication for Y101S.

To further characterize the pharmacological properties of Y101S, we next evaluated its stability and payload release profile. In vitro, only a small fraction of the MSA-2 payload was released over 72 h, with <10% cumulative release in mouse serum and approximately 5% in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (Supplementary Fig. 17A), indicating that Y101S is sufficiently stable under physiological conditions. Consistently, LC–MS/MS analysis of EMT-6 tumor tissues showed that free MSA-2 was readily detectable after Y101S treatment, whereas YM101-treated tumors contained undetectable amounts of MSA-2 (Fig. 8L and Supplementary Fig. 17B), confirming efficient linker cleavage and intratumoral release of the STING agonist. These data indicate that Y101S remains stable in circulation but undergoes effective payload release within the TME. These findings prompted us to hypothesize that ISAC-mediated targeted delivery of MSA-2 has the potential to outperform free STING agonist plus YM101 regimens by enhancing intratumoral immune activation while limiting off-tumor effects.

To address this, we compared 9 mg/kg Y101S with two free-combination regimens containing 9 mg/kg YM101: (i) 9 mg/kg YM101 plus low-dose MSA-2 (0.05 mg/kg, equimolar to the MSA-2 payload delivered by Y101S), and (ii) 9 mg/kg YM101 plus standard-dose MSA-2 (50 mg/kg). Although Y101S did not outperform the standard-dose free combination in terms of tumor growth control and immune activation, it achieved significantly stronger antitumor effects and intratumoral immune activation than the low-dose free combination (Supplementary Fig. 18A–C). Flow cytometric analysis of tumor-infiltrating immune cells further indicated that, compared with the equimolar low-dose MSA-2 plus YM101 regimen, Y101S more effectively remodeled the TME. Y101S treatment resulted in higher frequencies of CXCR6⁺CD8⁺ T cells, Ki67⁺CD8⁺ T cells, CD69⁺CD8⁺ T cells, granzyme-B⁺CD8⁺ T cells, and perforin⁺CD8⁺ T cells, as well as increased proportions of CXCL16⁺ DCs and macrophages within tumors (Supplementary Fig. 18D). Importantly, at 24 h after dosing, Y101S induced higher transcript levels of Ifnb, Tnf, Il6, and Cxcl16 in tumors than in non-tumor organs, indicating a preferential activation of STING signaling within the TME (Supplementary Fig. 18E). Compared with the low-dose free combination, Y101S induced more robust immune activation within tumors. In contrast, when compared with the standard-dose combination, Y101S achieved comparable tumor control while showing reduced inflammatory signaling in non-tumor organs. These data support the notion that Y101S can deliver an effective STING agonist dose to the tumor while limiting off-tumor inflammatory risk.

To evaluate the in vivo safety of Y101S, we monitored body weight and found no significant weight loss in treated mice (Supplementary Fig. 19A). At the end of the in vivo study, biochemical analysis of peripheral blood revealed no significant increases in markers of hepatic, renal, or cardiac injury (Supplementary Fig. 19B). Furthermore, histopathological examination of major organs, including the heart, liver, spleen, lungs, and kidneys, showed no apparent pathological changes (Supplementary Fig. 19C). Collectively, these results indicate that Y101S is well tolerated and exhibits a favorable safety profile in vivo.

Discussion

TGF-β plays a pivotal role in immune suppression and resistance to immunotherapy across various cancers. Dual blockade targeting TGF-β and PD-L1 has shown considerable promise for enhancing immune responses, with bifunctional antibodies, such as M7824, demonstrating success in preclinical and early-phase clinical trials. However, results from larger-scale studies have revealed unforeseen challenges, suggesting that TGF-β×PD-L1 blockade alone may not be sufficient to provide durable clinical benefits in many cases. These findings underscore the need to refine this therapeutic strategy and to elucidate the mechanisms underlying resistance to dual blockade.

Despite being conducted in genetically identical inbred mouse models, our study revealed heterogeneous therapeutic responses to YM101, with a subset of tumors showing limited regression. Such intra-model heterogeneity has also been documented in other immunotherapy settings, including GL261 glioma, CT26 colon carcinoma, and E0771 breast tumors, and is now recognized as a biologically meaningful phenomenon rather than technical variability. Comparative analyses between responders and nonresponders within the same model consistently revealed substantial differences in the TME, particularly in the abundance and function of infiltrating immune cells and the intensity of chemokine and cytokine signaling. Key immune pathways, most prominently IFN-I signaling, varied markedly among tumors within the same model and closely correlated with treatment efficacy. Given that antitumor immunity involves multiple regulatory thresholds, feedback, and feed-forward loops, stochastic activation of immune signaling is likely inevitable, resulting in divergent therapeutic outcomes even under identical genetic and experimental conditions. Moreover, recent genomic analyses have shown that even commonly used inbred mouse strains may harbor vendor-specific genetic variations that subtly influence immune signaling and gene regulation, further contributing to response variability.

In line with these observations, the combination of MSA-2 and YM101 in our models also failed to induce complete tumor rejection in all animals, with complete responses plateauing at approximately half of the treated mice. We consider this pattern to be biologically plausible rather than merely technical noise. Under strong immune pressure from STING activation and dual TGF-β/PD-L1 blockade, tumor subclones with reduced antigenicity, impaired interferon responsiveness, or compensatory upregulation of additional inhibitory pathways are likely to gain a selective advantage. Such immunoediting and the emergence of alternative escape routes, together with intrinsic variability in the engagement of the STING–IFN-I–STAT1–CXCL16 axis across tumors, probably contribute to the persistence of residual disease despite overall robust tumor regression. A more detailed dissection of these resistance mechanisms will require longitudinal and clonal-resolution analyses and represents an important direction for future work.

These heterogeneous responses prompted us to explore the molecular basis underlying YM101 resistance. Transcriptomic analysis of YM101-resistant tumors revealed markedly reduced innate immune activation, particularly in IFN-I-related pathways, suggesting that insufficient innate immune signaling may contribute to therapeutic failure. Based on these findings, we hypothesized that co-administration of a STING agonist could boost IFN-I responses and thereby enhance the antitumor efficacy of YM101. In vivo experiments confirmed this hypothesis, demonstrating that the combination of the STING agonist MSA-2 and YM101 significantly improved tumor control across multiple mouse models. Our panel of syngeneic models covers a broad spectrum of immune phenotypes, ranging from the immune-inflamed “hot” MC38 model to relatively non-inflamed EMT-6, CT26, 4T1, and B16 tumors, which exhibit either immune-excluded or immune-desert features. These results are consistent with previous studies showing that STING activation promotes antigen presentation and tumor immunogenicity, thereby amplifying antitumor immune responses and strengthening the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade.

Mechanistically, transcriptional profiling revealed that combination treatment led to the downregulation of multiple oncogenic pathways, including cell-cycle, ERBB, WNT, insulin, and mTOR signaling. These pathways are typically upregulated in highly proliferative tumor subclones and promote malignant progression. However, their suppression likely reflects the dynamic interplay between cancer cells and an activated immune microenvironment rather than a direct cytotoxic effect of the drugs. Consistent with this interpretation, the therapeutic synergy was abrogated in Cxcr6-cKO mice, indicating that these transcriptional changes were predominantly immune-mediated. Furthermore, CCK-8 assays confirmed that YM101 combined with MSA-2 exerted minimal direct effects on tumor cell proliferation in vitro. Together, these findings demonstrate that STING activation synergizes with TGF-β×PD-L1 blockade primarily through the enhancement of antitumor immunity within the TME, indirectly suppressing oncogenic signaling in tumor cells.

Notably, STING activation combined with TGF-β×PD-L1 blockade demonstrated superior efficacy compared with the widely studied combination of STING agonists and ICIs. Our findings reveal a mechanistic link between the TGF-β and STING pathways, in which TGF-β suppresses STING-mediated immune activation. This highlights the therapeutic potential of targeting TGF-β in conjunction with STING activation to restore immune responses and improve treatment efficacy. The STING pathway is a key component of innate immune sensing and plays a central role in orchestrating antitumor immunity. Although STING agonists have been explored clinically as adjuvants to enhance PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, their efficacy has been limited. For example, a phase I trial evaluating the STING agonist ADU-S100 in combination with the anti-PD-1 antibody Spartalizumab achieved an objective response rate of only 10% in patients with advanced solid tumors. Our results suggest that TGF-β signaling constitutes a major barrier to STING agonist efficacy. By simultaneously targeting STING, TGF-β, and PD-L1, we observed markedly enhanced tumor regression, demonstrating the potential of this triple-targeted approach to overcome resistance and broaden the therapeutic window of STING agonists.

The scRNA-sequencing analysis of EMT-6 tumors treated with the combination of STING agonist and YM101 provided further mechanistic insights. This regimen markedly increased the infiltration of CXCR6⁺ T cells and strengthened CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling between T cells and myeloid populations, particularly DCs and macrophages. Given that CXCR6⁺ T cells are highly cytotoxic and play a key role in sustaining antitumor immune responses, impaired recruitment of these cells promotes tumor progression and therapeutic resistance. Disrupting the CXCL16–CXCR6 axis, either through CXCL16 neutralization or Cxcr6 knockout in T cells, significantly reduced the efficacy of the triple-targeted therapy, underscoring the critical importance of this signaling axis in mediating therapeutic outcomes.

CXCL16 is not a canonical downstream target of STING signaling. However, our study revealed its robust upregulation in DCs and macrophages following STING agonist treatment. Analysis of public ChIP-seq datasets from Cistrome_DB identified STAT1 as a key transcription factor regulating CXCL16 expression. Luciferase reporter assays and IFN-I-neutralizing experiments further confirmed that CXCL16 induction is dependent on the IFN-I–STAT1 axis. Importantly, TGF-β negatively regulated CXCL16 expression induced by STING agonists. While TGF-β blockade synergized with STING agonists to increase CXCL16⁺ DCs and macrophages, PD-L1 blockade had little impact on CXCL16 expression. Further analyses revealed that exogenous TGF-β1 inhibited both CXCL16 and IFN-β expression in DCs and macrophages by reducing IRF3 phosphorylation, without affecting the upstream activation of STING or TBK1, suggesting a selective inhibition of IRF3.

We propose that this inhibitory effect of TGF-β is mediated by HDAC4, a histone deacetylase that shuttles between the nucleus and cytoplasm. Cytoplasmic HDAC4 can interfere with IRF3 activation in a deacetylase-independent manner. Our results indicate that TGF-β promotes HDAC4 nuclear export through a ROS-dependent mechanism, consistent with prior work implicating the Nox4–ROS pathway. Notably, ROS inhibitors reversed TGF-β1-induced HDAC4 export, restored IRF3 activation, and rescued CXCL16 expression. Together, these findings suggest that within the TME, TGF-β suppresses STING-mediated immune activation by promoting HDAC4 translocation, thereby attenuating CXCL16 expression and contributing to immunotherapy resistance (Supplementary Fig. 20).

Building on the STING/TGF-β regulatory network, our findings identify CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling as a key downstream effector driving antitumor immunity. This chemokine axis is known to maintain the residency and activation of CXCR6⁺ effector T cells within the TME. CXCR6 identifies a subset of tissue-resident or tumor-infiltrating CD8⁺ T cells with strong cytotoxic potential, while CXCL16 expression by DCs facilitates their positioning and survival through IL-15 trans-presentation at specialized DC–T cell immune synapses. Recent translational studies further indicate that the level of CXCL16 within the TME is a critical determinant of CXCR6⁺ T-cell recruitment and the efficacy of immunotherapy. Consistent with this, CXCL16 neutralization in our model completely abrogated T cell activation, indicating that CXCL16 is required not only for spatial retention but also for maintaining effective DC–T cell interactions and sustained T cell activation.

Although pharmacologic activation of CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling by STING/TGF-β/PD-L1 triple targeting reinforces T cell-mediated immunity, accumulating evidence suggests that this pathway exerts context-dependent effects across cancers. In certain tumor types, such as glioblastoma, CXCL16 produced by tumor-associated macrophages can drive persistent CXCR6 engagement and T cell exhaustion, leading to immune suppression and resistance to immunotherapy. Moreover, CXCL16 has been implicated in tumor progression through CXCR6-dependent pathways that promote epithelial-mesenchymal transition, metastasis, and angiogenesis. Therefore, while activation of this axis generally supports antitumor immunity and correlates with improved immunotherapy responsiveness, constitutive or chronic stimulation of CXCL16–CXCR6 signaling in tumors may instead favor tumor progression, suggesting that therapeutic activation of this pathway could present tumor-type-specific limitations.

Building on these findings, we propose that TGF-β signaling acts as a negative regulator of STING activation, potentially contributing to the limited efficacy of STING agonists observed in clinical trials. Recognizing that STING agonists are typically paired with ICIs, we developed a novel ISAC, Y101S, derived from YM101. This innovative agent simultaneously targets TGF-β, PD-L1, and STING pathways, offering a localized therapeutic approach tailored to tumors with high TGF-β and PD-L1 expression. Y101S delivers STING agonists directly to the tumor, potentially enhancing accessibility and minimizing systemic side effects. Unlike conventional antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) that deliver cytotoxic payloads to induce tumor-cell killing, Y101S is designed as an ISAC, in which a STING agonist is conjugated to the TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb via a cleavable linker. In classical ADCs, intracellular release of cytotoxic agents can lead to a bystander effect, in which neighboring tumor cells are secondarily killed by the liberated payload. In contrast, the payload of Y101S is an immune agonist with minimal direct cytotoxicity toward tumor cells. Thus, the antitumor effect of Y101S is primarily mediated through immune activation within the TME rather than direct cytotoxicity or bystander killing. This immunomodulatory design enables local delivery of STING activation signals while simultaneously neutralizing TGF-β and PD-L1, offering a unique approach to overcome immune suppression within the TME.

PD-L1 is not an exclusively tumor-specific antigen. Nevertheless, its preferential enrichment on tumor cells and immunosuppressive stromal populations, such as macrophages, provides a therapeutic window for selective targeting. In our models, tumor-infiltrating myeloid cells are highly abundant and constitute a major PD-L1⁺ compartment within the TME. We further found that TGF-β directly upregulates PD-L1 on macrophages and dendritic cells, thereby creating a substantial pool of myeloid PD-L1 targets even in relatively non-inflamed tumors. These observations suggest that the efficacy of STING/TGF-β/PD-L1 triple targeting is largely driven by myeloid PD-L1 and myeloid-derived CXCL16 rather than by tumor-cell PD-L1 alone, and may therefore extend to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade-refractory, TGF-β-high, myeloid-rich tumors in which tumor-cell PD-L1 expression is low or clinically PD-L1 negative.

Dual targeting of TGF-β and PD-L1 further enhances delivery precision within immune-excluded tumor regions where both molecules are highly expressed. In line with this rationale, the triple-targeting design of Y101S not only improves tumor selectivity and addresses the well-recognized limitations of STING agonists, such as poor bioavailability and limited tissue permeability, but also neutralizes TGF-β, a major inhibitory factor that constrains STING-mediated immune activation within the TME. Preliminary in vivo studies demonstrated that Y101S is not only safe but also exhibits superior antitumor efficacy compared to TGF-β×PD-L1 BsAb or STING agonists alone. Additionally, flow cytometry and RNA-seq assays highlighted Y101S’s potent immunostimulatory effects, particularly its ability to reshape the tumor immune microenvironment positively. These promising results provide a strong foundation for advancing STING agonist-based therapies and support the future clinical development of this novel ISAC drug.

In summary, we identified the CXCL16–CXCR6 axis as a key mediator of the enhanced efficacy observed when combining TGF-β/PD-L1 BsAb therapy with STING activation. Our findings demonstrate that TGF-β signaling suppresses STING-mediated immune activation, whereas its blockade, in conjunction with STING pathway engagement, markedly enhances antitumor immunity by recruiting and sustaining highly cytotoxic CXCR6⁺ T cells. Building on these insights, we established a triple-targeting ISAC, Y101S, that blocks TGF-β, PD-L1, and STING modulation within a single molecule. These results provide a mechanistic rationale and translational framework for multi-targeted strategies aimed at overcoming resistance and maximizing the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy.

Methods

Ethics approval

Ethics approval for this study was granted by the Clinical Research Ethics Committee of the First Affiliated Hospital, Zhejiang University School of Medicine (No. 2023-844). All participants received detailed information about the study and provided written informed consent before participation. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and all relevant ethical guidelines. The animal operations in this study were evaluated and approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of The First Affiliated Hospital, College of Medicine, Zhejiang University (No. 2023-1051).

Cell lines and agents

EMT-6 (SCSP-5499), 4T1 (SCSP-5056), MC38 (SCSP-5431), CT26 (SCSP-523), and B16 (SCSP-5096) murine tumor cell lines were obtained from the Cell Bank of Type Culture Collection, Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). EMT-6, 4T1, MC38, and CT26 murine tumor cell lines were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium (C11875500BT, Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (FSP500, Excell), whereas B16 cells were maintained in high-glucose DMEM (C11995500BT, Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS. The therapeutic antibodies used in this study included IgG (isotype control), blocking antibodies against TGF-β and PD-L1, and the TGF-β×PD-L1 bispecific antibody YM101; all therapeutic antibodies were developed by Wuhan YZY Biopharma. A CXCL16-blocking antibody and the corresponding isotype control IgG (MAB503 and MAB006) were purchased from R&D Systems. The STING agonist MSA-2 was obtained from MCE (HY-136927).

Murine tumor models for evaluating YM101 antitumor efficacy

Female BALB/c mice (strain code: 211) and C57BL/6N mice (strain code: 213) (6–8 weeks old) were purchased from Beijing Vital River Laboratories and housed under specific pathogen-free (SPF) conditions. Experimental and control animals were housed in separate cages but kept under identical conditions. Mice were maintained under a 12-h light/dark cycle (lights on from 06:00 to 18:00). The animal room temperature was set to 21 ± 2 °C with ~50% relative humidity. Mice had free access to food and water.

To investigate mechanisms of response and resistance to YM101, we used EMT-6 mammary carcinoma and CT26 colorectal carcinoma models in BALB/c mice. For the EMT-6 model, 2 × 105 EMT-6 cells were orthotopically inoculated into the right mammary fat pad. The CT26 model was generated by subcutaneously inoculating 1 × 106 CT26 cells into the right inguinal region of BALB/c mice. When tumors reached approximately 100 mm³ in volume, mice were randomized to receive intravenous injections of YM101 (9 mg/kg) or isotype control IgG (CTL, 6.6 mg/kg) every other day for a total of three doses. Treatment response was defined using the TGI metric, which is widely applied in preclinical efficacy studies. TGI (%) was calculated as 100 × [1−(ΔT/ΔC)], where ΔT and ΔC represent the change in tumor volume from treatment initiation to the analysis time point in the YM101-treated and CTL groups, respectively. Tumors with TGI < 50% were classified as nonresponders, whereas those with TGI ≥ 50% were classified as responders. Tumors from both responders and nonresponders were collected for bulk RNA-seq analysis on day 19 (EMT-6) and day 18 (CT26) after tumor inoculation. Moreover, the EMT-6 model was independently repeated for intratumoral cytokine analysis, and tumors were harvested 6 days after the last dose. Total protein concentrations in tumor tissue homogenates were measured using a BCA protein assay kit, and cytokine levels were quantified using an IFN-β ELISA kit (MIFNB0, R&D Systems) and multiplex fluorescence-encoded bead-based assays (741043 and 740451, BioLegend).

The combined antitumor efficacy of MSA-2 and YM101 was evaluated using EMT-6, CT26, and B16 murine tumor models. The EMT-6 and CT26 models were established as described above. B16 tumors were generated by subcutaneous implantation of 2 × 105 B16 cells into the right groin of C57BL/6N mice. Mice were randomized to receive three intravenous injections via the tail vein of one of the following regimens: isotype control IgG (CTL, 6.6 mg/kg), anti-TGF-β (6.6 mg/kg), anti-PD-L1 (6.6 mg/kg), or YM101 (9 mg/kg). These antibody doses were selected to be equimolar, based on their respective molecular weights. MSA-2 was administered as a single oral dose (50 mg/kg) at the initiation of antibody therapy.

Tumor size was measured using calipers, and tumor volume was calculated as (length × width²) × 0.5. Mice were monitored for tumor burden and general health status every 2 days. The maximum tumor size permitted by the institutional ethical board was tumor volume ≤ 2000 mm³ and/or a maximal diameter ≤ 20 mm, and all experiments adhered to these limits. Humane endpoint criteria for early termination included tumor burden exceeding the approved limit, tumor ulceration/necrosis, >20% body weight loss, inability to access food or water, severe respiratory distress, impaired mobility, or other signs of severe distress. Tumor-bearing mice were euthanized by CO₂ inhalation followed by cervical dislocation when humane endpoints were reached or at the predefined experimental endpoint.

This experiment was repeated in independent cohorts to evaluate overall survival in tumor-bearing mice. In separate mechanistic cohorts designed to obtain adequate tumor tissue for bulk RNA-seq and flow cytometry, treatment was initiated at a larger tumor volume (200–250 mm³), and tumors were collected 24 h after completion of the dosing schedule. Unless otherwise specified, tumor samples used for TME characterization were randomly selected within each treatment group without pre-stratification based on tumor regression.

Bulk RNA-seq analysis

Following the in vivo experiments, fresh tumor tissues were collected for bulk RNA-seq with the assistance of Seqhealth (Wuhan, China). Tumor samples were pulverized in liquid nitrogen, and total RNA was extracted using TRIzol. After quality control, ribosomal RNA was depleted, and stranded RNA-seq libraries were prepared using the KC-Digital Stranded mRNA Library Prep Kit (DR08502, Seqhealth), which incorporates unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) to minimize duplication bias. Libraries were then sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform.

Deduplicated reads were aligned to the Mus musculus reference genome using the STAR aligner (v2.5.3a). Exon-mapping reads were quantified at the gene level. Raw counts were used for differential expression analysis, and reads per kilobase per million mapped reads were additionally calculated for visualization. DEG analysis was performed using the DESeq2 package (v1.42.1) in R (v4.3.1), with DEG defined by an adjusted P-value below 0.05 and a fold change greater than 2. Gene expression profiles were visualized using the pheatmap package (v1.0.12), and PCA was conducted using the prcomp function. To interpret biological differences, functional enrichment analysis was performed using the DAVID online tool, with pathways considered statistically significant if they had a false discovery rate below 0.05. T cell response-associated signatures were constructed and scored as described in previous studies.

scRNA-seq analysis

To elucidate the synergistic mechanisms underlying YM101 in combination with MSA-2, we conducted a comprehensive analysis using the PRJNA837188 dataset with an updated analysis pipeline. This dataset includes single-cell transcriptome profiles of EMT-6 tumors treated with CTL, YM101, MSA-2, and their combination. Sample demultiplexing, barcode processing, and single-cell 5’ gene sequencing were performed using the standardized CellRanger pipeline (v6.1.2). For downstream analysis, we employed the Seurat package (v4.3.0.1) in R for quality control, identification of highly variable genes, PCA, and unsupervised clustering. Dimensionality reduction was carried out using t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding or uniform manifold approximation and projection to visualize cellular distribution in two-dimensional space.

Cluster-specific markers were identified using the FindAllMarkers function in Seurat, with cell annotations based on canonical markers. The Seurat pipeline parameters and cell annotation strategies were consistent with those described in our previous work. Tumor cell identification was performed using the InferCNV package (v1.16.0). DEGs between cell populations were identified using the FindMarkers function with default parameters. Functional enrichment analysis of these DEGs was conducted using the ClusterProfiler package (v4.8.2), focusing on biological functions and pathways with an adjusted P-value below 0.05 considered significant. Additionally, GSEA was applied to characterize the functional features of specific cell subsets using the Singleseqgset package (v1.2.9). To infer potential cell-cell communication networks, receptor-ligand interactions were analyzed using the CellChat package (v1.6.1) and the CellChatDB.mouse database. Intercellular communication probabilities were calculated using the computeCommunProb and aggregateNet functions, and the netVisual functions were used to visualize communication networks mediated by ligand-receptor pairs.

Flow cytometry assays of tumor tissues

Tumor tissues were finely minced using ophthalmic scissors and transferred into a digestion solution containing 1 mg/mL collagenase B (11088807001, Roche), 1 mg/mL hyaluronidase (H3884, Sigma-Aldrich), and 0.5 mg/mL DNase I (abs47047435, Absin). The samples were incubated in a 37 °C incubator for 30 min with gentle shaking every 10 min to enhance tissue dissociation. Digestion was halted by adding FBS, and single-cell suspensions were obtained by filtering the solution through a 40 μm strainer to remove undigested tissue fragments, as we previously described.

The filtered cells were centrifuged at 300 × g, and the pellet was resuspended in erythrocyte lysis buffer to remove red blood cells. After shaking and a second centrifugation, dead cells were labeled using fixable viability dye, and the supernatant was discarded. The remaining cells were resuspended in PBS containing 1% FBS, blocked with CD16/CD32 antibody, and incubated for 15 min. Following Fc blocking, flow cytometry antibodies targeting specific cell surface proteins were added, and samples were incubated in the dark for 30 min. After incubation, the cells were centrifuged at 300 × g, the supernatant was discarded, and the cells were fixed and permeabilized for intracellular staining. Flow cytometry antibodies targeting intracellular proteins were then added, and the samples were incubated overnight at 4 °C. The following day, cells were washed by centrifugation, resuspended in PBS containing 1% FBS, and prepared for analysis. The number of tumor-infiltrating immune cells was quantified by flow cytometry. A list of flow cytometry antibodies and reagents used is provided in Table S1.

Single-pot and miniaturized in-solution digestion method-based trace sample proteome analysis

To investigate proteomic differences between CXCR6+ and CXCR6−CD8+ T cells, we conducted a trace sample proteome analysis with support from APTBIO (Shanghai, China). These cells were isolated from EMT-6 tumors using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS), and approximately 5000 viable cells were collected per sample on the BD FACSAria III platform. The sorted cells were immediately cryopreserved in liquid nitrogen for subsequent analysis using the single-pot and miniaturized in-solution digestion-based method, an ultrasensitive trace sample proteomics approach. This method outperformed conventional techniques by considerably improving time efficiency, minimizing sample loss, and accommodating a broad range of cell inputs (10–100,000 cells). Sample preparation began with in-solution digestion, in which cells were treated with a 20 mM detergent solution and incubated at 37 °C. After incubation, an alkylating agent was added to a final concentration of 40 mM. The mixture then underwent a series of specified reactions, including trypsin digestion. Peptides were subsequently desalted, concentrated, and quantified based on UV absorbance at 280 nm.

For data-independent acquisition analysis, we used a timsTOF Pro mass spectrometer paired with NanoElute liquid chromatography. The instrument operated in positive ion mode, capturing ion mobility MS spectra across a broad m/z range. Data analysis was performed using SpectronautTM software, incorporating dynamic iRT for precise retention time prediction, MS2-level interference correction, and cross-run normalization to ensure data consistency. A Q-value threshold of 0.01 (equivalent to FDR below 1%) was applied for data filtering. To further analyze protein sequences and domain signatures, InterProScan software was used, referencing the Pfam database from the InterPro consortium. Downstream analyses, including differential protein expression and functional enrichment analysis, were conducted using R software.

In vitro cytotoxicity of OT-1 CD8+ T cells

Ovalbumin (OVA)-specific cytotoxic T lymphocytes were isolated from OT-1 mice originally obtained from Liangzhu Laboratory, Zhejiang University (Hangzhou, China) and maintained in-house. Following red blood cell lysis, OT-1 splenocytes were cultured in complete growth medium supplemented with IL-2 (50 U/mL, Beijing Fourrings) and the OVA peptide SIINFEKL (1 μM, RP10611, GenScript) for 2 days. After culture, CXCR6+ and CXCR6−CD8+ T cells were sorted by FACS using fluorochrome-conjugated anti-CXCR6 (151109, BioLegend) and anti-CD8 (E-AB-F1104, Elabscience) antibodies. To assess in vitro cytotoxicity, 1 × 105 OT-1 CD8+ T cells were co-cultured with 1 × 104 murine lymphoma cells stably expressing the OVA antigen (E.G7-OVA, CTCC-001-0136, MeisenCTCC) in 96-well plates. Before co-culture, E.G7-OVA cells were labeled with CFSE (5 μM, 150347-59-4, MCE). Anti-PD-L1 or an isotype control antibody was then added to the co-culture. The plates were incubated at 37 °C for 2 days, after which dying E.G7-OVA cells were identified via PI staining (40 μg/mL, 25535-16-4, MCE) and quantified by flow cytometry.

In vivo tumor model with CXCL16 blockade

To investigate the role of CXCL16 in the therapeutic efficacy of MSA-2 combined with YM101, a CXCL16 neutralizing antibody (MAB503, R&D) was used in the EMT-6 tumor model, with rat IgG2A (MAB006, R&D) as the isotype control. Intratumoral injections of the CXCL16 neutralizing antibody (100 ng in 10 μL PBS) were administered once tumors reached approximately 100 mm³ in volume. MSA-2 and YM101 treatments began the day after the first anti-CXCL16 antibody injection and followed the protocol above described. The anti-CXCL16 antibody was administered on alternate days for a total of six doses. After euthanasia, tumor tissues were collected for bulk RNA-seq and flow cytometry analysis. The experiment was also repeated to assess survival outcomes.

NK cell in vivo depletion assay

NK cell depletion was evaluated in the MC38 tumor model in C57BL/6N mice. MC38 tumors were established as described above. To deplete NK cells, mice received intravenous injections of anti-NK1.1 monoclonal antibody (BE0036, BioXCell) at 500 μg on day −2 relative to treatment initiation, followed by 250 μg on day −1 and 250 μg once weekly thereafter for the duration of the experiment, as previously reported. Control mice were treated with an equivalent dosing schedule of IgG isotype control (BE0085, BioXCell). This depletion regimen was applied in parallel with YM101 plus MSA-2 or control treatments to assess whether the synergistic antitumor effect depended on NK cells.

Generating cKO mice

Transgenic mice used in this study were obtained from the Shanghai Model Organisms Center. Cxcr6flox mice were engineered by inserting loxP sites flanking exon 2 of the Cxcr6 gene. Cxcr6fl/WT mice (NM-CKO-2104656) were interbred to generate homozygous Cxcr6fl/fl mice. Meanwhile, Cxcr6fl/WT mice were crossed with Cd3e-Cre mice (NM-KI-234082) to produce Cxcr6fl/WT Cd3e-Cre offspring. To generate Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre mice, homozygous Cxcr6fl/fl mice were crossed with Cxcr6fl/WT Cd3e-Cre mice, resulting in cKO of Cxcr6 in T cells.

Stat1flox mice (NM-CKO-200139) were engineered by inserting loxP sites flanking exons 2–5 of the Stat1 gene. Homozygous Stat1fl/fl mice were obtained by interbreeding Stat1fl/WT mice. Stat1fl/WT mice were then crossed with Lyz2-CreERT2 mice (NM-KI-18018), generating Stat1fl/WT Lyz2-CreERT2 offspring. To establish the Stat1fl/fl Lyz2-CreERT2 model, Stat1fl/fl mice were crossed with Stat1fl/WT Lyz2-CreERT2 mice. At 5–6 weeks of age, Stat1fl/fl Lyz2-CreERT2 mice were administered tamoxifen intraperitoneally (75 mg/kg) once daily for 5 consecutive days. Ten days after tamoxifen treatment, these mice were inoculated with MC38 tumor cells.

Adoptive transfer of CXCR6+ T cells

Primary CD8+ T cells were isolated from the spleens of Cxcr6fl/fl mice. Spleens were mechanically dissociated and passed through a 40 μm filter to obtain a single-cell suspension, followed by red blood cell lysis. CD8⁺ and CD4⁺ T cells were then purified using immunomagnetic negative selection kits (mouse CD8⁺ T cell isolation kit, 19853; mouse CD4⁺ T cell isolation kit, 19852; both from STEMCELL technologies) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. CXCR6+ T cells were induced and expanded using previously established methods. CD8+ T cells from Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre mice served as negative controls and underwent the same treatment. At the end of CXCR6 induction, approximately 80% of the cells were CXCR6+ T cells. For in vivo experiments, 1 × 106 MC38 cells were subcutaneously injected into the right flank of Cxcr6fl/fl Cd3e-Cre mice. On day 6, 2 × 106 in vitro-induced CXCR6+CD8+ T cells were administered intravenously to tumor-bearing mice. Adoptive cell transfer was performed every 4 days for a total of three treatments. In parallel, an analogous adoptive transfer experiment was performed using in vitro-induced CXCR6⁺CD4⁺ T cells following the same dosing and scheduling regimen.

Multicolor IF staining of tumor tissues

Fresh tumor tissues were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 48 h, embedded in paraffin wax, and sectioned at approximately 4-μm thickness. IF staining was performed using the tyramide signal amplification method. Primary antibodies were applied according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Additional primary antibodies used for multiplex IF are listed in Table S2. After staining, sections were counterstained with DAPI (4083, CST), mounted with an antifade reagent, and imaged using a confocal microscope. Image analysis was conducted with ImageJ software (v1.53), and expression levels were quantified as the percentage of positive pixels.

Quantitative PCR assay

Human DCs and macrophages were differentiated in vitro from PBMCs obtained from locally recruited healthy adult donors (n = 3; sex/age: male/24, male/27, female/28; no known acute or chronic illness). Similarly, murine DCs and macrophages were derived from bone marrow cells isolated from wild-type BALB/c mice. Total RNA was extracted using the FastPure cell/tissue total RNA isolation kit V2 (RC112, Vazyme) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Approximately 1 μg of RNA was reverse-transcribed into cDNA using HiScript II Q Select RT SuperMix for qPCR (R233, Vazyme). The primers used in this study are listed in Table S3. Quantitative PCR was performed on an ABI 7500 platform under the following cycling conditions: initial denaturation at 95 °C for 30 s, followed by 45 cycles at 95 °C for 15 s, and 60 °C for 60 s. Relative gene expression levels were calculated using the 2−ΔΔCt method, with GAPDH as the internal control.

CXCL16-Luc activity

Synthetic CXCL16 gene promoters were obtained from Paivibio (Wuhan) and subcloned into the pGL3-basic vector to investigate transcriptional regulation. Potential STAT1 binding sites within the CXCL16 promoter region were predicted using the JASPAR database (http://jaspar.genereg.net/), an online resource for transcription factor binding site analysis. To assess promoter activity, HEK293 cells (SCSP-5209; Cell Bank of Type Culture Collection, Chinese Academy of Sciences) were co-transfected with CXCL16-Luc and pRL-TK vectors. Luciferase assays were conducted to quantify promoter-driven expression, with firefly luciferase activity measured using the dual-luciferase reporter assay kit (DL101, Vazyme). Renilla luciferase activity was used as an internal control for normalization of transfection efficiency and cell viability.

ChIP-PCR

ChIP assays were performed using the high-sensitivity ChIP kit (ab185913, Abcam) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, human macrophages derived from PBMCs were crosslinked with 1% formaldehyde in culture medium and incubated at 4 °C on a rotary mixer. Crosslinking was quenched by adding 1 mL of 1.25 M glycine per 9 mL of cell suspension. Cells were then washed with ice-cold PBS and lysed in working lysis buffer on ice with intermittent vortexing and centrifugation. The resulting pellet was resuspended in ChIP buffer and incubated at 4 °C for 10 min. Chromatin was sheared into appropriate fragment sizes via sonication, and debris was removed by centrifugation. The supernatant was incubated overnight at 4 °C with 2 µg of anti-STAT1 antibody (9172, CST), whereas nonimmune IgG served as a negative control (2729, CST). The ChIP reaction was assembled by combining the supernatant with ChIP buffer, enrichment enhancer, and blocker solution in designated wells. After washing, crosslinks were reversed using the DNA release buffer. The extracted DNA was analyzed by qPCR. Primer sequences used in the ChIP-PCR assay are listed in Table S3.

ELISA

Macrophages and DCs were treated with various stimuli in vitro to evaluate the concentrations of IFN-β and CXCL16. After 48 h of treatment, cell culture supernatants were collected and analyzed using ELISA kits, including the human IFN-β DuoSet ELISA (DY814-05, R&D), human CXCL16 Quantikine ELISA Kit (DCX160, R&D), and mouse CXCL16 DuoSet ELISA (DY503, R&D) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, 100 μL of each sample was added to a well and incubated at room temperature for 2 h. After washing, 100 μL of detection antibody was added and incubated for another 2 h. Following additional washes, 100 μL of streptavidin-HRP working dilution was added, and the plate was incubated at room temperature for 20 min. Next, 100 μL of substrate solution was added and incubated for 20 min, followed by the addition of 50 μL of stop solution. The optical density of each well was measured at 450 nm using a microplate reader.

For in vitro treatments, cells were stimulated with MSA-2 (5 μg/mL, HY-136927, MCE), TGF-β1 (20 ng/mL, Z03411, GenScript), anti-IFNAR1 (100 μg/mL, HY-P990439, MCE), anti-TGF-β (100 μg/mL, YZY Biopharma), anti-PD-L1 (100 μg/mL, YZY Biopharma), or N-acetylcysteine (2 mM, HY-B0215, MCE), alone or in combination as indicated. During transfection, si-STAT1 (Tsingke), si-HDAC4 (Tsingke), and Hieff Trans® in vitro siRNA/miRNA Transfection Reagent (40806ES03, Yeasen) were used according to the manufacturer’s protocol (Table S4). For experiments involving siRNA-mediated knockdown, cells were transfected with the indicated siRNAs 24 h before subsequent treatment.

Public database analysis

The correlations between CXCL16 and STAT1, CXCL16 and TMEM173, and CD274 and TGFB1 across various cancer types in the TCGA database were analyzed using the TIMER2.0 online tool. Visualization of the results was performed in R using the ggplot2 package (v3.5.0).

Western blotting

Proteins were extracted from cells using RIPA buffer supplemented with protease and phosphatase inhibitors. Protein concentration was determined by BCA assay (P0012, Beyotime). Equal amounts of protein were separated on SDS-PAGE and transferred onto PVDF membranes. Membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat milk in TBST for 1 h at room temperature, followed by overnight incubation at 4 °C with primary antibodies (Table S5). After washing with TBST, membranes were incubated with HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 h at room temperature. Protein bands were visualized using the ECL detection kit (34577, Thermo Fisher) and imaged with the ChemiDoc XRS+ Imaging System (Bio-Rad). Band intensities were quantified using ImageJ software.

Construction of TGF-β×PD-L1–STING ISAC

The linker-payload complex was synthesized using Fmoc-Val-Cit-PAB (Y-PE-4775), Mal-PEG4-NHS (Y-PE-2017) from New Research Biosciences, and MSA-2 (HY-136927, MCE) (Supplementary Fig. 21). For ISAC synthesis, YM101 (20 mg) was reacted with Traut’s reagent (20 equivalents) (BD75935, Bidepharm) in PBS to introduce sulfhydryl groups, resulting in a sulfhydryl-functionalized antibody. Excess Traut’s reagent was removed using an Amicon ultra centrifugal filter unit (UFC501096, Millipore), and the modification level (sulfhydryl content per antibody) was quantified using Ellman’s reagent (60306ES08, Yeasen). The sulfhydryl-functionalized antibody was then combined with the linker-payload complex and stirred at room temperature for 5 h to facilitate conjugation. Unconjugated small molecules were removed using a desalting column with PBS. The purified ISAC was used for downstream biological evaluations.

To determine the drug-antibody ratio (DAR), the purified Y101S and unconjugated YM101 were reduced with 50 mM TCEP (ST045, Beyotime) and analyzed by Q-TOF mass spectrometry. Deconvoluted mass spectra showed that the light chain mass of Y101S increased from ~37.3 to ~39.3 kDa, whereas the heavy chain mass remained essentially unchanged (Supplementary Fig. 22). This ~2.0 kDa increase in light chain mass is consistent with conjugation of approximately two linker-payload molecules per light chain. Given that each antibody contains two light chains, this corresponds to an average DAR of approximately 4 for Y101S, in line with the design specification.

Antitumor activity and safety evaluation of Y101S

The antitumor activity of Y101S was evaluated in CT26, 4T1, and EMT-6 tumor models established as described above. When tumor volumes reached approximately 100 mm³, mice were randomized into treatment groups. Because Y101S and YM101 have similar molecular weights, both antibodies were administered at 9 mg/kg by intravenous (tail vein) injection every other day for a total of three doses. In the 4T1 model, Y101S was further compared with two free-combination regimens containing 9 mg/kg YM101 plus MSA-2. Mice received 9 mg/kg YM101 combined with either low-dose MSA-2 (0.05 mg/kg, equimolar to the MSA-2 payload delivered by 9 mg/kg Y101S) or standard-dose MSA-2 (50 mg/kg). For the low-dose regimen, MSA-2 was administered orally every other day for three doses, concurrent with each YM101 injection. For the standard-dose regimen, MSA-2 was administered as a single oral dose (50 mg/kg) at the initiation of antibody treatment. To assess whether the antitumor efficacy of Y101S was dependent on CXCL16 signaling, CXCL16 neutralization was also performed in Y101S-treated mice. In EMT-6, CT26, 4T1, and B16 tumor models, Y101S was combined with intratumoral injections of a CXCL16-neutralizing antibody or isotype control, using the same intratumoral dosing schedule as for CXCL16 blockade experiments.

After completion of treatment, major organs and peripheral blood samples were collected to assess the safety profile of Y101S. The heart, liver, spleen, lungs, and kidneys were harvested, fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde, embedded in paraffin, sectioned, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin for histopathological evaluation of potential treatment-induced changes. Tissue sections were independently evaluated by two experienced pathologists who were blinded to group allocation. To assess systemic toxicity, a panel of biochemical markers commonly used to evaluate liver, kidney, and cardiac function was measured in peripheral blood. These markers included alanine aminotransferase, aspartate aminotransferase, blood urea nitrogen, creatinine, uric acid, creatine kinase, and creatine kinase isoenzyme. Biochemical analyses were conducted using an automatic biochemical analyzer (LW C400, Landwind).

In a separate cohort of tumor-bearing mice, mRNA levels of Cxcl16 and the STING downstream genes Ifnb1, Tnf, and Il6 were quantified in tumors and in normal tissues (lung and liver) 24 h after a single dose of Y101S or control treatments, in order to evaluate the extent of tumor-specific immune activation. Gene expressions were measured by qPCR as described above.

In vitro stability and intratumoral release of Y101S

To assess the in vitro stability of Y101S and payload release, Y101S was diluted to 10 μg/mL in either PBS or mouse serum and incubated at 37 °C for the indicated durations. At each time point (0, 24, and 72 h), 50 μL of the incubation mixture was collected and immediately mixed with 150 μL acetonitrile containing 0.2% formic acid. Samples were vortexed for 10 min and centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C. The supernatants were transferred to fresh tubes and stored at −80 °C until analysis. The amount of released MSA-2 was quantified by LC–MS/MS, and the percentage of payload release was calculated relative to the theoretical maximal MSA-2 level assuming complete linker cleavage.

For intratumoral payload release, EMT-6 tumor-bearing mice were treated with a single intravenous dose of Y101S or YM101 (9 mg/kg). Tumors were harvested 24 h after dosing, weighed, and homogenized in an equal volume of ice-cold PBS (1 mL per 1 g tissue). Three volumes of methanol were then added to each homogenate (final methanol:PBS ratio 3:1, v/v), and the mixtures were vortexed thoroughly and centrifuged at 10,000 × g for 10 min at 4 °C. The resulting supernatants were subjected to LC–MS/MS analysis to determine free MSA-2 concentrations in tumor tissue. YM101-treated tumors served as negative controls for background MSA-2 signal.

Statistical analyses and reproducibility

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 8 and R software. For comparisons between two groups with normally distributed data, Student’s t test was used, with Welch’s correction applied when variances were unequal. For non-normally distributed data, the Mann–Whitney U-test was used. For comparisons among multiple groups, one-way ANOVA was performed, followed by multiple-comparisons test. Survival analysis of tumor-bearing mice was assessed using the log-rank test. Data are expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD) unless otherwise indicated. All statistical tests were two-sided, with a P < 0.05 considered statistically significant. In vivo studies were performed in at least two independent models, yielding consistent results. In vitro assays were repeated in at least three independent experiments with similar outcomes.
