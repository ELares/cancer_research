# Implications of DNA damage response and immunotherapy in tumor therapy.

## Abstract

Tumorigenesis is closely related to defects in DNA damage response (DDR), which plays an important role in maintaining genomic integrity. The DDR process includes DNA damage recognition, cell cycle checkpoint pathway activation, and DNA damage repair. On the one hand, defects in DDR increase the instability of the cellular genome, which ultimately leads to tumorigenesis. On the other hand, tumor therapy can take advantage of DDR defects to induce apoptosis by causing damage to tumor cells, thus achieving therapeutic goals. Tumor immunotherapy is an emerging method of tumor treatment that attacks and kills cancer cells by inhibiting negative regulatory factors, enhancing the ability of immune cells to recognize antigens on the surface of tumor cells and activating the patient's own immune system. Recent research evidence suggests that DDR inhibition enhances antitumor immune responses and acts in synergy with immunotherapy. This review describes the different categories of DNA damage, their corresponding DNA damage repair pathways, the antitumor mechanisms of action targeting DDR inhibition, and the prominent contributions of these mechanisms to the immunotherapeutic response. By exploring recent advances in the interactions between targeted DDR and immunotherapy, this review provides novel therapeutic insights into the treatment of DNA damage-associated cancers as well as the development of combination immunotherapy.

Introduction

The development of tumors is closely related to defects in DNA damage response (DDR), which plays a crucial role in maintaining genomic integrity. The DDR process includes DNA damage recognition, activation of cell cycle checkpoint pathways, and DNA damage repair. Both exogenous and endogenous damage can lead to DNA damage in oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes. If the DDR pathway is defective and cannot repair the damaged DNA in a timely and accurate manner, it ultimately leads to the development of tumors. However, when a DDR pathway is inactivated, tumor cells may increase their dependence on other repair pathways, resulting in a unique therapeutic target. By targeting and inhibiting bypass repair pathways, it is possible to selectively kill tumor cells with defective DDR repair mechanisms. Developed based on this synthetic lethality principle, DDR-targeting inhibitors are novel drugs that exert antitumor effects by blocking DDR. Currently, DDR inhibitors such as polyadenosine diphosphate ribose polymerase (PARP) inhibitors, ataxia telangiectasia and Rad3-related (ATR) inhibitors, and checkpoint kinase 1 (CHK1) inhibitors, show promising clinical application prospects in various tumor types and are particularly effective against tumors with genetic defects in key DNA repair genes, such as BRCA1/2- and BRCA-related genes, and with ataxia-telangiectasia mutated (ATM) mutations.

Another promising cancer therapy is immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy. Immune checkpoint proteins such as programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic t-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA4) regulate the immune system by binding to chaperone proteins on antigen-presenting cells, ensuring that healthy host cells are not targeted by the host immune response. ICIs work by blocking the binding of immune checkpoint proteins to their ligands, allowing T cells to target tumor cells and achieve antitumor effects. However, in clinical practice, only a small portion of patients receiving immunotherapy respond effectively to ICIs, calling for identification of reliable ICIs biomarkers and methods to enhance the effectiveness of ICI treatment.

Interestingly, recent studies continue to show a close link between DDR signaling pathways and the immune system. On one hand, tumors with DDR defects respond significantly better to ICIs. Research indicates that DDR inhibitors can promote antitumor immune responses, exhibiting synergistic effects with immunotherapy. Additionally, ICIs show a good synergistic-promoting effect with traditional cytotoxic cancer treatments, such as radiotherapy and chemotherapy. On the other hand, the DDR signaling pathway itself plays a crucial role in immune responses, such as regulating the development of T cells and B cells, influencing T-cell differentiation, modulating B-cell maturation, and enhancing the ability of antigen-presenting cells to take up antigens. Therefore, investigating the dual regulatory role of the DDR signaling pathway in the immune system may provide new insights into and potential applications for the development of treatments and immunotherapies for tumors associated with DNA damage. In this review, we summarize different types of DNA damage and repair pathways and the mechanisms of action of DDR-targeting inhibitors. We then explore the current progress into understanding the interaction between DDR signaling pathway molecules and immune responses (Fig. 1), which may provide a theoretical basis for new DDR inhibitor and ICI therapeutic combinations.

DNA damage repair and tumor development

Genomic integrity is critical for cell survival and genetic stability. One of the main hallmarks of cancer is genomic instability, which may be caused by defects or malfunctions in the cellular DDR mechanism due to external and internal damage. Because genes are exposed to a variety of endogenous (e.g., reactive oxygen species) and exogenous (e.g., ionizing radiation, ultraviolet rays, and nicotine) damaging factors, cells can produce types of DNA damage such as DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs), DNA single-strand breaks (SSBs), and DNA cross-links. Organisms constantly monitor the integrity of the genome to ensure that genetic information is delivered stably and accurately to daughter cells. When DNA damage occurs, the body activates the DDR, its endogenous alarm system, which proceeds through three major steps. First, damage-sensing proteins in the cell, such as ATM and ATR, undergo autophosphorylation activation, which phosphorylates the DNA damage protein γH2AX. Subsequently, the phosphorylation of γH2AX recruits molecules such as MDC1, promoting the amplification and transmission of the damage signal, initiating the DNA damage sensing pathway. Second, damaged DNA activates cell cycle checkpoint pathways. Phosphorylated ATM and ATR regulate CHK1 and CHK2, causing the cell to arrest at the corresponding phase of the cell cycle to provide sufficient time for DNA repair and maintain chromosomal stability. Finally, the cell selects the appropriate repair mechanisms to repair the damage. In the DNA repair mechanism, the DDR signaling pathways are not mutually independent but works together. Therefore, when one pathway is damaged, other pathways in the DDR network take over. If these signaling pathways function properly, damage is detected and repaired effectively promptly, and the cell returns to normal functioning. Conversely, if damaged DNA is not properly repaired, development of cancerous cell clusters may occur. Thus, defects in cellular DDR pathways are closely related to the development of cancer.

Double-strand breaks

DSBs, the most severe form of DNA damage among all damage types, can lead to cell cycle arrest, apoptosis, cellular senescence, and carcinogenesis. When ATM is activated in response to DSBs, it undergoes autophosphorylation, resulting in CHK2-mediated cell cycle arrest. DSBs can be repaired through homologous recombination (HR), non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), microhomology-mediated end joining (MMEJ), or single-strand annealing (SSA). Among them, HR, which primarily acts on cells in the S phase and G2 phase of the cell cycle, has the highest fidelity, being able to accurately repair the most severe and complex DNA damage. DSBs can activate the ATM-CHK2 or ATR-CHK1 pathway in ATM or ATR disorders. During the HR process, these pathways initiate a signal cascade after damage is recognized. First, the Mre11-Rad50-NBS1 complex (MRN) senses DSBs and localizes to bind the DNA ends. Under RAD50 mediation, MRE11 initiates the DNA end resection pathway for HR repair. The processed DNA binds to the undamaged DNA through strand invasion to synthesize new complementary DNA strands, synthesizing new complementary DNA strands. Germline mutations in the HR pathway can increase the risk of breast, ovarian, prostate, and pancreatic cancer. For example, germline biallelic mutations in BRCA1/2 can cause the hereditary syndrome Fanconi anemia and increase the risk of early-onset hematological malignancies.

NHEJ, another major pathway for repairing DSBs, primarily acts on all cell cycle phases except the M phase. However, as HEJ ignores sequence homology and does not require a homologous template, directly joining the ends of broken DNA, it is unable to achieve precise repair. NHEJ repairs broken DNA strands by the terminal binding of proteins Ku70-Ku80 and 53BP1. Replication timing regulatory factor 1 (RIF1) prevents DNA from being removed while recruiting the DNA-dependent protein kinase catalytic subunit (DNA-PKcs) for final processing. Finally, DNA replication, repair, and recombination factor (XRCC4) and DNA ligase IV are recruited to the DSB to join DNA ends and fill in the gaps in the sequence. Cells with germline mutations in NHEJ signaling pathway proteins exhibit immunodeficiency but have higher radiosensitivity. In many cancer types, reduced or missing expression of some NHEJ signaling pathway proteins can lead to formation of BRCA1-deficient tumors resistant to PARP inhibitors.

Not as accurate as NHEJ repair, MMEJ repair results in large deletions, insertional mutations, and many chromosomal translocations. After PARP-1 binds to the DSB terminal to recruit MRN at the injury site, MRE11 cleaves the DSB terminal to produce short single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) and then stabilizes the DNA terminal with the help of the RAD50 protein. ERC1/XPF and flap endonuclease 1 (FEN1) remove the 3’ end to ensure that the micro-homologous sequence is exposed, and FEN1 removes the 5’end to prevent specific annealing interference with repair. DNA polymerase theta (POLQ) then fills the gap through DNA synthesis, and DNA connects to LIG3/XRCC1. Many studies have shown that the level of POLQ in many solid tumors increases when the HR repair pathway is missing or malfunctioning, indicating that this pathway plays a greater repair role in HR-deficient tumors. Inhibition of POLQ in HR-deficient cells can cause tumor cell death by triggering synthetic lethality.

SSA, a less studied form of DSB repair involving Rad50, Rad52, and RPA, is a relatively low-fidelity pathway that can lead to deletion of repetitive sequences. In SSA, the damaged and broken DNA ends are excised into single strands by nucleases, and the processed single-stranded DNA seek DNA sequences that complement it. Annealing occurs between the two single-stranded DNA, and the repair is completed by excising and connecting the broken ends. The functional inhibition of Rad52 exhibits a synthetic lethal effect in cells with HR defects. For example, in tumor cells with BRCA1/2 deficiency, targeting Rad52 significantly inhibits cell proliferation. Combining RAD52 inhibitors and PARP inhibitors to exert dual synthetic lethal effects may further enhance the therapeutic effect when treating BRCA-deficient cancers.

Single-strand breaks

SSBs, base damage, and DNA mismatches can be repaired by nucleotide excision repair (NER), base-excision repair (BER), or mismatch repair (MMR) pathways. If SSBs are not properly repaired, more severe DSBs may occur. NER can remove distortions in the double helix structure of the DNA backbone, including cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers, chemical adducts, and discontinuous DNA backbones. NER can be divided into two subpathways: global genomic NER (GG-NER) and transcription-coupled NER (TC-NER). The difference between GG-NER and TC-NER lies in their activation pathways: GG-NER is activated through the SOS pathway and involves XPC and XPE proteins, while TC-NER is recognized by RNA polymerase during transcription and recruits Cockayne syndrome proteins CSB and CSA to repair the damage. Transcription factor II H (TFIIH) then creates double incisions before DNA polymerase and DNA ligase, along with flap endonuclease 1 (FEN1) and the LIGIII-XRCC1 complex, seal the "gaps" to complete the repair. XPC and its allelic gene mutations increase the risk of developing head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, and XRCC3 gene mutations are associated with the development of melanoma.

BER mainly repairs small DNA damage, such as single base abnormality, oxidation, alkylation, methylation, and small numbers of base deletions. BER includes repair by short sequence (single nucleotide) base excision (SP-BER) and long sequence (2–10 nucleotides) base excision repair (LP-BER). Short sequences are repaired by DNA β polymeraseβ (Polβ) and XRCC1 and long sequences by the polymerases Polσ and Polε and XRCC1. Biphasic germline mutations in the BER glycosylase MUTYH increase the risk of developing MUTYH-related polyposis (MAP) and induce the occurrence of colorectal cancer. Polymorphisms in other BER genes increase the risk of lung, stomach, and esophageal cancer.

MMR can repair base mismatches, insertions, or deletions that occur during DNA replication and recombination involving the MutS (MSH2, MSH3, and MSH6) and MutL (MLH1, MLH3, PMS1, and PMS2) families. MSH2 to MSH6 recognize mismatched nucleotides; MSH2 to MSH3 recognize inserted and missing mutated nucleotides; MLH1 to PMS2, PMS2, and EXO1 remove mismatched DNA; and DNA polymerase III and DNA ligase synthesize correctly paired double-stranded DNA. When the MMR gene undergoes germline mutations or methylation, changes in microsatellite sequence length or gene composition, known as microsatellite instability (MSI), may occur. MSI is associated with an increased risk of colorectal cancer, gastric cancer, non-small cell lung cancer, and endometrial cancer.

Fanconi anemia

The Fanconi anemia (FA) pathway repairs DNA interstrand crosslinks (ICLs). Intrachain crosslinks are repaired through the NER pathway, while ICLs are mainly repaired through the FA pathway. Various endogenous metabolites, such as lipid peroxides and ethanol metabolites, and chemotherapeutic drugs, such as platinum-based drugs, can form ICLs, which form covalent bonds between two nucleotides, inhibiting processes such as DNA replication and transcription. The FA core complex can activate two proteins, FANCD2 and FANCI, which can recruit DNA repair proteins to the DNA ICL region, thereby removing the ICLs. Mutations in the FA protein pathway are associated with the development of cancers such as Fanconi anemia, acute myeloid leukemia, ovarian cancer, breast cancer, and pancreatic cancer.

Trans-damage DNA synthesis

Translesion DNA synthesis (TLS) is an emergency repair mechanism for DNA damage. Unrepaired or newly formed DNA damage can hinder DNA replication. High-fidelity DNA polymerases will identify the damaged bases in the template; cluster around the mutation site; and then, under the mediation of proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA), transition to low-fidelity DNA polymerases to extend replication at the site of DNA damage. Unlike high-fidelity repair pathways (e.g., the NER, BER, and MMR pathways), the TLS pathway can bypass multiple types of DNA damage, and it also has broader applicability; however, its polymerase fidelity is generally low, making it prone to introducing incorrect bases. Upregulation or downregulation of TLS repair pathway proteins are both potential factors in promoting tumorigenesis. For example, POLκ is overexpressed in glioblastoma and lung cancer, and leukemia and lymphoma are associated with mutations in POLη, whose deficiency leads to the development of xeroderma pigmentosum variant, a genetic disorder characterized by a high susceptibility to skin cancer. Murine models with TLS polymerase function defects show an increase in the incidence of lymphoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and breast tumors.

Genetic defects in DDR and unique antitumor targets

DDR pathway molecules are currently considered one of the most promising cancer treatment targets. Tumor cells carrying DDR defects have increased genomic instability and rely on other DNA repair pathways to survive. Application of the concept of synthetic lethality, whereby the deletion of one cellular pathway leads to dependence on another pathway, allows for the targeted inhibition of other DNA repair pathways to treat certain tumors with DDR mechanism defects. Targeted DDR inhibitors inactivate key DNA repair pathways, leading to the accumulation of DNA DSBs while increasing dependence on MMRs, exceeding the cell's damage tolerance and ultimately leading to the death of cancer cells. Currently, a variety of small molecule inhibitors targeting DNA repair pathways, such as PARP inhibitors, are being explored clinically. In addition, the S-phase checkpoint kinase CHK1 and G2 checkpoint kinase WEE1, as well as ATM, ATR, and DNA-PK, have become the most promising targets for small molecule inhibitors, and several of compounds are currently under development (Table 1).

PARP inhibitors

ARP plays a key role in the process of DNA single-strand base excision and repair. If PARP inhibitors are used to block single-strand repair in tumor cells with homologous recombination defect (HRD), DNA damage in tumor cells cannot be repaired. Therefore, many SSBs in cancer cells evolve into DSBs, forming a "synthetic lethal" effect, which eventually leads to the death of tumor cells. However, normal cells are not killed by PARP inhibitors due to the normal repair function of HR; low replication pressure; and existence of other repair pathways that can partially compensate for the loss of PARP inhibitor function, such as such as NER and BER.

Inhibitors of the DNA repair enzyme PARP have been approved for the treatment of numerous tumor types. To date, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has approved four different PARP inhibitors—olaparib, niraparib, lucaparib, and talazoparib—for use as antitumor-targeted drugs. PARP enzymes, mainly PARP1 and PARP2, play an important role in DNA damage repair. PARP inhibitors have two mechanisms of action on PARP: (1) They compete with nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD +) at the PARP active site, where they inhibit PARP formation and block parylation and recruitment of DNA repair effectors to the ssDNA break site, and (2) bind to the PARP domain, where they cause molecular conformation change and promote PARP’s ability to maintain its binding to DNA, thus greatly enhancing the longevity of the DNA-PARP complex and inhibiting the subsequent DNA repair process.

BRCA-deficient tumors have been found to be more sensitive to PARP inhibitors. BRCA1 plays a key role in promoting the repair of DNA DSBs. BRCA1 and BRCA2 play an important role in regulating HR repair and replication fork stability, and the deletion of BRCA1 or BRCA2 can seriously damage the cell's HR repair ability. Therefore, BRCA-deficient cells are more dependent on error-prone DSB repair mechanisms, such as NHEJ and MMEJ. BRCA loss and PARP inhibition synergize to produce a large accumulation of DNA DSBs, which, combined with reliance on error-prone repair mechanisms, reduce cell damage tolerance and lead to tumor cell death. In addition to BRCA gene mutation, damage or deletion of other HR repair genes, such as RAD51, ATM, PALB2, and MRE11, can lead to homological recombination deficiency (HRD). Therefore, HRD is widely used in clinical practice as a biomarker of PARP inhibitor sensitivity.

Tumors with mismatch repair deficiency (MMRD) are also sensitive to PARP inhibitors. Genetic defects in key mismatch repair genes, such as MLH1, PMS2, MSH2, and MSH, can lead to MMRD. Similar to BRCA-deficient tumors, MMRD-deficient tumors are more prone to the accumulation of double-stranded DNA breaks compared to tumors without MMR deficiency. Therefore, PARP inhibition can intensify the accumulation of DNA DSBs, which eventually leads to the death of tumor cells. Deficiency of excision repair cross complementation group 1 (ERCC1) leads to accumulation of toxic DNA DSBs that synergizes with PARP inhibition, thereby exacerbating genomic instability and promoting cell death. Additionally, defects in the DNA repair enzymes PBRM1 and ARID1A can sensitize cells to PARP inhibition by increasing the accumulation of endogenous and therapy-induced DNA DSBs.

ATR inhibitors

ATR in tumor cells is currently the focus of much research because ATR plays a crucial role in protecting stalled DNA replication forks, regulating replication origin initiation, and enabling cells to transition from the G2 phase to the mitotic M phase by strengthening the G2/M cell cycle checkpoint control. Targeted ATR inhibition has shown a unique therapeutic effect in ATM-deficient tumors. In ATM-deficient cells, blocking ATR signals can disrupt the cellular mechanisms that coordinate cell cycle stagnation and DNA repair. This disruption forces cells carrying unrepaired DNA damage or incomplete genomes to forcibly enter the mitotic stage, resulting in abnormal chromosome separation and mitotic catastrophe, which eventually leads to cell death.

CHK1 inhibitors

As the checkpoint kinase CHK1 is an effector kinase of ATR, CHK1 actively participates in the DNA damage response initiated by ATR and ATM through phosphorylation and recruitment of a series of regulatory proteins. CHK1 regulates the activity of CDK1 by inhibiting CDC25C and activating WEE1 to achieve G2/M phase blocking. In addition, CHK1 can phosphorylate RAD51, promoting its interaction with BRCA2 during HR. CHK1 can coordinate several basic cellular processes simultaneously, including cell cycle stagnation, replication fork stabilization, and inhibition of the replication initiation, thereby enabling cells to cope with increased replication pressure. Elevated CHK1 levels are associated with poor prognosis, disease recurrence, and treatment resistance, which further supports the therapeutic potential of targeted CHK1 inhibition. In the case of ERCC1 deletion, the cell's NER and inter-chain crosslinking repair ability also decrease, resulting in cell replication pressure and high dependence on the ATR-CHK1 pathway. Inhibition of CHK1 in ERC1-deficient cells promotes the accumulation of DNA DSBs, which leads to the death of cancer cells.

WEE1 inhibitors

WEE1 protein is a serine/threonine protein kinase that plays an important role in cell cycle regulation and DDR. Activated ATR phosphorylates CHK1, which in turn phosphorylates WEE1 and CDC25. Activated WEE1 then phosphorylates Tyr15 and Thr14 on downstream CDK1 to inhibit its activity, resulting in G2/M cycle blockage, leaving time for DDR. By phosphorylating Tyr15 on CDK1, WEE1 can also prevent the progress of the S phase to the G2 phase before DNA replication is completed. Currently, most clinical studies focus on the combined application of WEE1 inhibition and chemotherapy drugs.

DDR inhibitor promotion of antitumor immune response

DDR inhibition-induced cancer cell death promotion of T-cell-mediated antitumor responses

DDR inhibitor-induced cancer cell death can occur through "programmed" or "immunogenic" death mechanisms. Programmed cell death allows cells to maintain membrane integrity during the death process, while immunogenic cell death is accompanied by membrane rupture, leading to the release of cellular contents as well as damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and neoantigens into the tumor microenvironment. After these substances enter the tumor microenvironment, they can activate resident dendritic cells (DCs), prompting them to take up and present new antigens to CD4 + and CD8 + T cells, thereby generating tumor-specific adaptive immune responses. Therefore, DDR inhibitor-mediated cancer cell death plays an important role in enhancing antitumor adaptive immune responses.

DDR inhibition activation of cGAS-STING signaling pathway to increase antitumor immune response

Targeting DDR inhibition also increases the body's immune response by activating the cGAS-STING signaling pathway in cancer cells. The cGAS-STING pathway was initially used as a mechanism to help mammalian cells generate antiviral immunity by sensing cytoplasmic DNA and triggering interferon responses. Because the cytoplasm is a DNA-free region when in a non-disease state, viral infection can cause viral DNA to leak into the host cell cytoplasm, where cGAS can recognize and bind to it, thereby triggering an interferon response. During DDR inhibition treatment, the integrity of the nucleus is compromised, resulting in the release of DNA into the cytoplasm. The cGAS-STING pathway is an innate immunosensor that triggers a cellular immune response when cytoplasmic DNA is detected. First, cGAS, as a cytosolic nucleotidyl transferase, catalyzes the synthesis of ATP and GTP into 2',3'-cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) upon binding to cytosolic DNA. cGAMP acts as a second messenger to activate the interferon gene stimulator (STING) protein located in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) before the STING protein migrates to the ER-Golgi intermediate compartment. After activation, STING interacts with TANK-binding kinase 1 (TBK1) and IkB kinase (IKK) to induce the phosphorylation of interferon regulatory factor 3 (IRF3) and nuclear factor kappa-B (NF-κB), thereby activating the transcription of cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-6, and IL-1β and type I interferons (IFNs). Once these cytokines are secreted into the microenvironment, they help activate resident DCs in the tissue, ultimately promoting immune responses mediated by CD4 + and CD8 + T cells, thereby enhancing antitumor immune responses (Fig. 2).

In addition to this direct activation, cGAMP can diffuse into the microenvironment, serving as a second messenger to nearby myeloid and lymphoid cell populations, further stimulating IFN production, which in turn activates natural killer (NK) cell antitumor immunity. PARP inhibitors can also promote the release of IFN-α/β by activating the cGAS-STING pathway, thereby activating the Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcriptions and the interferon regulatory factor (JAK-STAT-IRF) pathway to upregulate the expression of PD-L1. The increased expression level of PD-L1 provides a therapeutic target for PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors.

Synergistic effect of DDR and immune checkpoint inhibition

Immunotherapy is a new type of cancer treatment that utilizes a patient's immune system to attack tumor cells. By inhibiting negative regulatory factors or enhancing the ability of immune cells to recognize antigens on the surface of tumor cells, it activates the patient's own immune system to achieve the goal of killing cancer cells. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) work by blocking the binding of immune checkpoint proteins to their ligand proteins, enabling T cells to target tumor cells. The most widely studied immune checkpoint inhibitors are PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors. The function of PD-1, an immune checkpoint expressed on CD4 + and CD8 + T cells, is to prevent autoimmune reactions and create an immunosuppressive microenvironment by regulating T-cell proliferation and IFNγ production. PD-L1, its binding ligand, is primarily expressed on antigen-presenting cells, such as DCs and tumor cells. Blockade of PD-1/PD-L1 binding relieves immune checkpoint-mediated immunosuppression, thereby reactivating immune cells to exert antitumor effects. Clinical applications of PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors have been widely tested in many different types of cancer, such as melanoma, lung cancer, and colon cancer. Currently, multiple PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors, including pembrolizumab, cindilimab, nivolumab, toripalimab, atezolizumab, tislelizumab, and durvalumab, have been approved by the FDA for cancer treatment.

Immune checkpoint inhibition combined with DDR inhibition has a synergistic effect. DDR inhibition combined with immune checkpoint inhibition has greater efficacy and stronger antitumor effects than monotherapy. In murine models of a variety of cancers, targeted DDR inhibition combined with immunotherapy yielded a significant synergistic effect. In studies of small cell lung cancer, tumors in murine models regressed significantly when PARP inhibitors and PD-L1 inhibitors were combined. PARP inhibitors block the DDR function of cancer cells while activating the STING/TBK1/IRF3 innate immune response, ultimately enhancing the expression of type I IFN genes, such as IFNβ, and downstream chemokines, such as CXCL10 and CCL5, to induce the activation and function of cytotoxic T lymphocytes. DDR inhibition also increases PD-L1 expression, producing a synergistic effect with immunotherapy.

Currently, combination therapies targeting DDR inhibition and immunotherapy are being tested in clinical trials for various types of cancer. Clinical trials combining PARP inhibitors with ICIs are underway in cancers such as ovarian, breast, and pancreatic cancer to assess their safety and efficacy. For example, the MEDIOLA clinical trial demonstrated that the combined treatment of the PARP inhibitor olaparib and the PD-L1 inhibitor durvalumab exhibited greater antitumor efficacy than monotherapy in BRCA-mutated breast cancer. The TOPACIO/KEYNOTE-162 clinical trial, which studied the efficacy of the PARP inhibitor niraparib and the PD-L1 inhibitor pembrolizumab in platinum-resistant advanced or metastatic triple-negative breast cancer and recurrent ovarian cancer, including BRCA-mutated and wild-type patients, showed that patients with ovarian cancer responded better to combination therapy than expected, compared to single-agent therapy.

To screen patients most appropriate for combination therapy, it is necessary to identify biomarkers related to the efficacy of combination therapy. For example, the mutation status of certain DDR pathway-related genes, such as MSI and dMMR; the expression levels of specific immune cell subsets; and tumor mutation burden (TMB), may all be related to the response to combination therapy. By studying biomarkers, the precision of combined treatments can be improved, allowing for the selection of appropriate populations to maximize patient benefit. Additionally, researchers are exploring the effects of combining different targeted DDR inhibitors, including PARP inhibitors, and ICIs to find the optimal combined treatment regimen, as well as the effects of combining multiple DDR inhibitors with immunotherapy. Further research is needed to determine the optimal sequence of treatments—whether targeted DDR inhibitors or immunotherapies should be used first or both treatments should be used simultaneously—to identify the optimal treatment sequence.

The development of cGAS-STING pathway agonists as complementary therapies for cancer immune checkpoint blockade and immunotherapy has become a research area of great interest. STING agonists are classified into natural cyclic dinucleotides (CDN), CDN analogues, and non-CDN chemotypes. Natural CDNs include bacterial-derived c-di-GMP, c-di-AMP, 3'−3'-cGAMP, and mammalian endogenous 2'−3'-cGAMP. CDN analogues are synthetic molecules that mimic native cyclic dinucleotides (e.g., cGAMP) involved in the activation of STING pathways and enhancement of STING-binding capacity through structural optimization. One example is ADU-S100, which improves STING protein binding capacity through cyclic phosphodiester bond modification. However, CDNs are challenging in terms of delivery and bioavailability due to their hydrophilicity, small size, and low stability. For example, CDNs are easily degraded by enzymes such as ENPP1. Recognizing these disadvantages, researchers have investigated CDN degradation inhibitors, including ENPP1, a Type II transmembrane glycoprotein that can degrade STING ligands and whose inhibition can enhance the effect of immunotherapy. TREX1 is an exonuclease that also antagonizes the cGAS-STING pathway in cells and degrades cytoplasmic DNA. Therefore, inhibition of TREX1 is also an effective strategy to enhance immunotherapy. Research into novel delivery systems such as exosome engineering for delivery, bacterial carriers, small molecule-nucleic acid hybrids, and nanoparticle carriers is expected to overcome the limitations of traditional STING agonists, ensuring the development of therapies with better pharmacokinetic properties and more targeted immune activation.

Role of DDR components in immune responses

Role of DDR in development and function of T cells and B cells

The development of T cells and B cells requires V(D)J recombination. The B-cell receptor (BCR) and T-cell receptor (TCR) genes undergo V(D)J recombination to generate diverse BCRs and TCRs. By randomly combining numerous gene segments, a vast number of different antigen receptors can be produced, enabling the immune system to recognize a wide variety of foreign antigens. DSBs are mediated by the RAG1/RAG2 complex and then repaired by NHEJs. DNA-PK is directly involved in NHEJ repair, and ATM regulates cell cycle stagnation and apoptosis by activating downstream CHK2 and P53. 53BP1 can prevent excessive excision of the DSB terminal and promote long DNA terminal ligation in the V(D)J recombination process, ensuring the accuracy of recombination process. DDR defects lead to errors in V(D)J recombination, which can trigger the occurrence of immunodeficiency and lymphoma.

The PARP1 in the DDR component can affect the differentiation of CD4+ T cells by activating the T-cell nuclear factor NFAT. When PARP1 is absent, it leads to a reduction in the expression of NFAT-dependent cytokines such as IL-2 and IL-4, thereby affecting the differentiation of immune cells. The absence or inhibition of PARP1 can also promote the differentiation of CD4+ T cells into type 1 T helper cells (Th1). At the same time, PARP1 regulates the expression of transforming growth factor (TGF-β) receptors on CD4+ T cells.

During the immune response process, T cells are activated by antigen-presenting cells (APCs). DDR-related proteins can regulate the expression of genes downstream of the T-cell receptor signaling pathway, affecting the production of cytokines such as interleukin-2 (IL-2), which is very important for T-cell proliferation and differentiation. Appropriate DDR signals can ensure that T cells produce an adequate amount of IL-2 after activation, thereby promoting T-cell proliferation and enhancing the immune response.

DDR component regulation of B-cell maturation

The DDR components regulate B-cell maturation by maintaining the genomic stability of the cell. DDR recognizes and repairs DNA damage experienced by B cells during their development and differentiation, ensuring the integrity of the B-cell genome during proliferation and differentiation. The DDR mechanisms also participate in regulating B-cell immune functions, including antibody production and antigen presentation, to ensure that B cells function normally during immune responses. B cells must undergo high-frequency somatic mutation (SHM) and category conversion recombination, also known as class switch recombination (CSR) binding, to mature in the germinal center. These two processes depend on the precise regulation of DDR components, such as ATM and ATR, which induces the activity of adenosine deaminase (AID) by regulating activation, which affects mutation frequency and category conversion efficiency.

The MRN complex and ATM work synergistically to promote the formation and repair of DSBs. Deletion of MRN can lead to CSR defects and affect the production of IgG/IgA antibodies. PARP-1 and PARP-2 play an important role in B-cell development. The co-expression of DNA repair enzymes PARP-1 and PARP-2 can promote central B cell development and maintain peripheral B cell homeostasis. Researchers have crossed PARP-1-deficient mice with mice carrying a B cell-specific deletion of the PARP-2 gene, and they found that the resulting double PARP-1 and PARP-2-deficient mice exhibited disrupted bone marrow B cell development, as well as severe peripheral depletion of transitional and follicular B cells. In murine models with c-Myc overexpression, PARP-2 deletion inhibits the expansion of pro-B cells, resulting in more DNA damage and replication stress, while PARP-1 deletion promotes pro-B proliferation and accelerates lymphoma formation. In some cases, such as autoimmune diseases, abnormal activation of the DDR pathway may lead to abnormal maturation and dysfunction of B cells. For example, in patients with systemic lupus erythematosus, the DDR pathway of B cells is abnormal, resulting in B-cell hyperactivity and enhanced autoimmune response.

Role of DDR in immunostimulatory ability of antigen-presenting cells

Activation of the DDR signaling pathway can enhance the ability of APCs to take up antigens. Activated DDR signals can regulate dynamic changes in the cytoskeleton, enabling APCs to more effectively phagocytize pathogens and other antigens. Taking DCs as an example, DDR-related proteins may interact with the signaling pathway of phagocytosis receptors to promote the formation of phagocytosis vesicles, thereby improving the efficiency of the uptake of antigens such as virus particles or bacteria. DDR can also optimize the antigen-processing process: DDR-related kinases may phosphorylate certain subunits of the proteasome and change its enzymatic activity so that the processed antigen peptide is more conducive to binding to major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I) molecules, thereby improving the quality of antigen presentation.

Activation of the DDR signaling pathway can also enhance the ability of APCs to uptake antigens. Activated DDR signals can regulate the dynamic changes in the cytoskeleton, enabling APCs to phagocytose pathogens and other antigens more effectively. As previously described regarding DCs, DDR-related proteins may interact with the signaling pathway of phagocytosis receptors to improve the efficiency of the uptake of antigens, and DDR can optimize the antigen-processing process. At the same time, DDR can regulate the transport process of MHC molecules from the ER to the cell surface, ensuring that APCs can effectively present antigen peptide-MHC complexes to the cell surface for better recognition by T cells. The DDR signal can regulate the expression of co-stimulatory molecules as well as MHC molecules, thereby enhancing the immunostimulatory capacity of APCs toward T cells.

The DDR signaling pathway is intertwined with other signaling pathways in antigen-presenting cells to jointly regulate the activation state of the cells. For example, the NF-kB and MAPK pathways, which are activated during the DDR process, are involved in regulating the inflammatory and immune response of cells. Abnormalities in DDR can lead to abnormalities in the function of antigen-presenting cells, which affects the efficiency of the immune response. For example, in some autoimmune diseases, abnormal activation of the DDR pathway may lead to overactivity of antigen-presenting cells, triggering an abnormal immune response.

Association between traditional cytotoxic cancer treatment and immunotherapy response

Radiation promotes tumor antigen presentation: (a) Radiation promotes protein degradation, leading to an increase in intracellular peptide pools, which induces the expression of MHC-I and facilitates tumor antigen presentation. (b) Radiotherapy can lead to immunogenic death of tumor cells; promote the accumulation of tumor antigens in the microenvironment; and induce the release of endogenous DAMPs, such as cadherin, HMGB1, and ATP, which contributes to the presentation of tumor antigens and the maturation and activation of T cells. (c) Radiotherapy can upregulate the expression of MHC-I and promote the antigen presentation process by releasing cytokines such as IFN-γ or activating the IFN⁃β signaling pathway in tumor cells.

Radiotherapy enhances adaptive immunity by activating the cGAS-STING pathway: Radiotherapy-induced DNA damage and micronuclear DNA released into the cytoplasm can activate the cGAS-STING pathway and release cytokines, chemokines, and type I IFNs, promoting adaptive immune responses mediated by CD4 + and CD8 + T cells.

Radiotherapy enhances the killing ability of immune cells by upregulating relevant ligands: Radiotherapy can upregulate the expression of ICAM-1 and VCAM on the surface of lymphatic endothelial cells, leading to increased adhesion of human T lymphocytes to irradiated lymphatic endothelial cells. 

Radiotherapy induces the expression of inflammatory chemokines, increasing NK cell and T-cell infiltration: Radiotherapy can induce the expression of inflammatory chemokines, such as CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL16, accelerating the aggregation of NK and T cells into the tumor microenvironment and enhancing the infiltration of CD8 + T cells.

Traditional cytotoxic therapy can cause DNA damage and stimulate an immune response. Radiotherapy not only directly causes cytotoxic DNA damage but also promotes a tumor-specific immune response. Beyond radiotherapy, chemotherapeutic agents that induce DNA damage promote antitumor immune responses through diverse mechanisms, with molecular and cellular pathways varying by drug class. For instance, the topoisomerase II inhibitor teniposide triggers STING-dependent type I IFN signaling and NF-κB activation, eliciting DC-mediated and T-cell-mediated antitumor immunity, including enhanced immune cell infiltration. Doxorubicin augments IL-12-driven IFN-γ accumulation in tumors. Paclitaxel stimulates tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) to produce IL-12 and TNF, thereby activating DCs, NK cells, and cytotoxic T lymphocytes while upregulating calreticulin expression to facilitate immune recognition of tumor cells. Numerous clinical trials evaluating combinatorial chemoimmunotherapy are now underway, testing various drugs at different doses and administration schedules. Given the complex interactions between DNA-damaging agents and immune function, the combination and optimal timing of ICB and other immunotherapies with DNA-damaging chemotherapy may vary depending on the disease setting.

Advances in novel immunotherapy strategies

The field of tumor immunotherapy is at the forefront of research, with various innovative immunotherapy methods continuously emerging. In addition to ICIs, a series of novel immunotherapies and immunotherapy drugs, such as chimeric antigen receptor-modified (CAR) T-cell therapy, engineered T-cell receptor (TCR) T-cell therapy, tumor immune vaccines, and oncolytic viruses, have been approved for clinical application.

Adoptive cell therapies

Adoptive cell therapies (ACTs) modify autoimmune cells to target and destroy tumor cells. After the patient’s immune cells, such as T cells or NK cells, are collected, they are modified or expanded in vitro before being reinfused into the patient to enhance their ability to recognize and kill tumors. ACTs includes therapies such as CAR T-cell therapy, TCR T-cell therapy, tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy, cytokine-induced killer (CIK) cell therapy, and NK cell therapy. CAR T-cell therapy enhances the ability of the patient’s own T cells to recognize and eliminate tumor cells by modifying them. CAR T-cell therapy requires collecting the patient's T cells and performing genetic modifications in vitro to make the cells express CARs that can specifically recognize tumor cells. The modified T cells are then reinfused into the patient's body to target and recognize CD19, CD22, BCMA, and other antigens to effectively attack tumor cells expressing these specific antigens.

CAR T-cell therapy has shown excellent therapeutic effect in the treatment of leukemia and lymphoma, and its application in advanced solid tumors is being widely studied. CAR T-cell therapy has also been found to have a synergistic effect with ICI blockade therapy. In a study of the PAPR inhibitor olaparib and EGFRvIII targeting CAR (806-28Z CAR) T cells, olaparib inhibited the migration of murine myeloid-derived suppressor cells through the SDF1α/CXCR4 axis and promoted the survival of CD8 + T cells in tumor tissues, increasing the antitumor activity of CAR T-cell therapy.

CAR NK cell therapy genetically modifies natural killer (NK) cells to express CARs, redirecting their cytotoxicity toward specific tumor-associated antigens. Compared to CAR T-cell therapy, CAR NK cell therapy offers several significant advantages, including a better safety profile; lower risk of graft-versus-host disease, cytokine release syndrome, and neurotoxicity; increased infiltration in solid tumors; enhanced ability to overcome drug resistance in the tumor microenvironment; and elevated proliferation and persistence in vivo. Activated NK and T cells can express some T-cell immunological checkpoint molecules, such as PD-1, CTLA-4, LAG3, and TIM3, and their antitumor activity can be enhanced after being blocked by ICIs. Therefore, combining CAR NK cells with ICB therapy or knocking out immune checkpoint molecules through gene knockout appears to be promising approach for treating tumors.

Engineered T-cell receptor T-cell therapy

As the specific recognition receptor on the surface of T cells, the T-cell receptor (TCR) activates T-cell responses by recognizing and binding antigens presented by the MHC. TCR T-cell therapy transfers the TCR gene into T cells in the peripheral blood, and after cultivation and amplification in vitro, reinfuses them into the patient to produce a potent antitumor effect. Although CAR T-cell therapy and TCR T-cell therapy share similarities in their mechanisms of action, they have fundamental differences in their antigen recognition mechanisms. TCR T-cell therapy has the potential to recognize a broader range of tumor-specific antigens, but its application is limited by MHC restrictions. Whereas CAR T-cell therapy does not have any MHC restrictions and can directly identify tumor cells while bypassing the MHC mechanism, it can only recognize antigens on the surface of tumor cells, being unable to recognize nearly 90% of intracellular antigens. Currently, the targets of multiple TCR T-cell therapies are under investigation, such as targeting NY-ESO-1 and gp100 for melanoma treatment, carcinoembryonic antigen for colorectal cancer treatment, and HPV-16 E7 for metastatic HPV-associated epidermoid carcinoma.

CIK therapy

CIK cells are a heterogeneous cell population rich in CD3+ and CD56+ phenotypes that are generated by co-cultivating monocytes from peripheral blood with a series of cytokines (e.g., INF-γ and IL-2) in vitro. CIK cells exhibit antitumor activity that is not restricted by the MHC and can effectively recognize and kill various types of tumor cells. CIK therapy has shown excellent therapeutic effects in the clinical treatment of multiple solid tumors and hematological malignancies.

Tumor vaccine immunotherapy

Acting either preventively or therapeutically, tumor vaccines aim to activate the immune system to kill tumor cells by utilizing tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) or tumor-specific antigens (TSAs). They can be categorized into three types: DC vaccines, gene vaccines, and polypeptide vaccines. As exogenous antigen-presenting cells, DC vaccines capture tumor antigens and present them to lymphocytes, thereby triggering an immune response against tumor cells. Gene vaccines use genetic engineering to insert genes encoding tumor-specific antigens into viral vectors or plasmids. After these recombinant genes enter the body, they express tumor-associated antigens that can stimulate the immune response against tumor cells, ensuring the maintenance of the immune response. The tumor-associated antigen peptide contained in polypeptide vaccines, the most widely studied and applied tumor vaccines, can be specifically recognized by T cells, thus triggering the active immune response of the host immune system and further killing of tumor tissue. Highly specific and safe, relatively mature peptide vaccines currently available include Nelipepimut-S (NeuVax) for early HER2 1 + and 2 + breast cancer patients for whom standard HER2 treatment is not appropriate, CIMAvax-EGF targeting epidermal growth factor in non-small cell lung cancer, and peptide vaccines based on MUC1 that focus on glycoproteins abnormally expressed in cancers such as breast and pancreatic cancer.

Oncolytic virus immunotherapy

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are a type of virus that can infect and kill tumor cells while selectively replicating within them, whereas they are prevented from replicating in normal cells. From the initial stage of oncolytic virus treatment, the lysis of tumor cells gradually increases as lytic viruses proliferate extensively within tumor cells, leading to their rupture and death, while simultaneously releasing progeny viruses that continue to infect surrounding tumor cells. Additionally, the lysis of tumor cells releases tumor-associated antigens, further activating the human immune system. OVs can exist naturally or be obtained through genetic engineering. The Ovs currently used in most clinical studies are genetically modified viruses, such as adenoviruses, poxviruses, and herpes simplex viruses. For example, type 1 herpes simplex virus has been engineered to express human granulocyte–macrophage colony-stimulating factor to produce a therapy known as talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC) that can be used to treat unresectable melanoma. Recent research indicates that OVs can also infect tumor-associated endothelial cells, block tumor angiogenesis, and thus indirectly kill tumor cells. In addition to T-VEC, the FDA has approved two other OV-based therapies, RIGVIR and Oncorine, for clinical use.

Conclusions

Genomic instability is an important tumor marker and often the foundation of tumor development. Defects in DDR increase genomic instability, leading to tumor development. When tumor cells have DDR pathway defects, DDR inhibition, a novel form of tumor-directed therapy, can specifically kill tumor cells without harming normal cells by blocking their compensatory repair pathways. Currently, various DDR inhibitors, including PARP, ATR, CHK1, and WEEI inhibitors, are undergoing clinical research, and several PARP inhibitors have already been approved for clinical use. DDR inhibition can enhance the immune response, act synergistically with immunotherapy, and stimulate the antitumor immune response by generating new antigens to activate DCs or the cGAS-STING pathway through cytoplasmic DNA to produce type I IFN responses and promote CD4 + and CD8 + T cell-mediated immune responses. Combination therapy consisting of DDR inhibitors with ICIs targeting PD-1, PD-L1, and CTLA-4 shows better antitumor therapeutic effects than monotherapy, and further research is needed to identify the appropriate biomarkers and optimize the combination and sequence of the two therapies. cGAS-STING pathway agonist therapy can be a complementary treatment to DDR inhibition and immunotherapy. In addition to these novel therapies, traditional cytotoxic cancer therapy shows a synergistic effect with immunotherapy. The next generation of immunotherapy methods, such as CAR T-cell, CIK cell, and TCR T-cell therapies, have brought new hope to providers of cancer treatment, and their combination with DDR inhibitors and traditional cytotoxic therapy has shown new possibilities. Looking forward, the rational design of DDR inhibition, the combination of traditional cytotoxic therapy with immunotherapy, and the clinical application of emerging biomarkers will help improve the survival and prognosis of patients with cancer.
