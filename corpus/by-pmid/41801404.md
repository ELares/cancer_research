# Impact of proton pump inhibitors on immunotherapy is modulated by prior chemotherapy and linked to gut microbiome-immune cell signatures.

## Abstract

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are one of the most widely used medications in the world. They have been associated with an altered microbiome, which is demonstrated to be important for immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) response. We sought to determine whether PPI use was associated with shorter overall survival (OS) in patients treated with ICIs, and whether these changes were associated with altered microbiomes and immune cell composition. Our retrospective study of patients with advanced cancer (n = 1078) evaluated the impact of PPI use on OS. We also analyzed stool samples from melanoma patients treated with ICIs (n = 42) and stool and blood samples from patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and renal cell carcinoma treated with ICIs (n = 8). With the data from our prospective study, we assessed microbiome composition from stool samples using metagenomic whole-genome shotgun; immune cell populations from blood samples were determined using CyTOF. Associations between PPI use, clinical outcomes, the microbiome, and immune cell populations were evaluated using survival analyses, diversity metrics, and multivariable models. PPI use was associated with shorter OS in patients with advanced cancers treated with ICIs, with the strongest effects seen in melanoma. PPI use was associated with worse clinical outcomes and microbiome alterations in patients with advanced cancers treated with ICIs, suggesting that its use may influence the efficacy of immunotherapy; prospective studies implicate its effect on the microbiome. These findings underscore the importance of considering the microbiome and concomitant medications when to enhance treatment response and efficacy.

Impact of proton pump inhibitors on immunotherapy is modulated by prior chemotherapy and linked to gut microbiome–immune cell signatures

Introduction

Overall survival (OS) in patients with cancer has improved with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI), but less than 40% of treated patients show a durable clinical benefit, depending on the cancer type. One factor affecting response is the gut microbiome. Patients whose tumors progress on ICIs can be resensitized via fecal transplantation from a donor whose tumor responded to treatment, and supplementation with individual microbes (i.e., probiotics) improves ICI response in model systems. While this is a promising approach to improve patient outcomes, progress is hindered by a limited mechanistic understanding of which microbes or microbial metabolites alter ICI response and how to optimize their effects. Several mechanisms by which microbes affect response to ICI have been described; however, many more are expected. For example, Bifidobacterium longum has been observed to secrete inosine, which activates gut T-cells via the A2AAR receptor, leading to improved CD8 + T-cell-mediated tumor killing. Lactobacillus reuteri has been found to secrete indole-3-aldehyde, which activates CD8 + T-cells via the Aryl hydrocarbon receptor. However, in observational studies, many more microbes have been associated with response to ICI, and the mechanisms by which they modulate ICI response are unknown.

Many factors are known to modulate the gut microbiome, including diet and concomitant medications. In particular, antibiotics have been associated with worse outcomes from ICI in retrospective studies. Despite some contradictory results, recent meta-analyses have shown that antibiotic use is associated with worse treatment outcomes in cancer patients, specifically with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) are among the top 10 medications currently prescribed in the USA and are approved as short-term treatment for gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), as part of triple therapy for Helicobacter pylori, and for risk reduction for gastric ulcers in patients using NSAIDs. Data have also shown that PPIs, which are commonly over-prescribed, adversely impact the intestinal microbiome. However, the significance of these changing microbial profiles remains unclear. Outside of corticosteroids and antibiotics, however, limited data exist on the impact of medications on the microbiome and outcomes of patients treated with ICI.

To further understand the role of PPI use and its relationship with other therapies in patients who undergo ICI treatment, we first conducted a retrospective study to evaluate the impacts of PPI use on OS in patients with advanced cancer treated with ICI, further stratifying based on timing of PPI use, line of therapy, prior chemotherapy, and combination of therapy. Second, to further understand the potential impact of PPI on the microbiome as well as immune cell populations, we conducted an exploratory analysis of microbiome and immune cell populations from two prospective cohorts of patients with melanoma, NSCLC, or renal cell carcinoma (RCC) who were treated with ICI.

Materials and methods

Cohort 1: all cancers cohort

We performed a retrospective study of patients with advanced cancers treated with ICIs between 2011 and 2019 at The Ohio State University. Eligible patients received at least one dose of an ICI in any line of treatment for advanced cancer as part of standard of care or as a participant in a clinical trial. Checkpoint inhibitors included monoclonal antibodies to PD-1, PD-L1, or CTLA-4 alone or in combination therapies. Baseline patient characteristics were assessed from the physician’s clinical care documentation and electronic medical record. Research Electronic Data Capture (REDCap) was used for data collection. Clinical data, including concomitant medication (CM), toxicity, and survival, were abstracted from chart review. Patients were classified as PPI users if a PPI was listed as an active medication at the start of ICI treatment using a combination of large-scale data retrieval and manual review. All medications prescribed to patients for 12 months before the start of ICI treatment and until the last follow-up or death were retrieved from the electronic medical record data lake and parsed for initiation dates and durations. These were coupled with provider notes, in which clinical staff manually reviewed all medications, including over-the-counter and supplements, at every clinical encounter, including before and during ICIs. Exposure data, including both prescription and over-the-counter use, were captured via manual chart review. OS was evaluated to the date of death or last contact. The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of the Ohio State University (IRB#2016C0070). The package UpSetR in R was used to visualize the different concomitant medication combinations in the cohort. Associations between OS and PPI use were studied using log-rank tests and univariable and multivariable Cox regression analyses, overall and stratified by line of therapy, receipt of prior chemotherapy (yes or no), and timing from chemotherapy to ICI (within one year or more than one year). Survival and Cox regression analyses were calculated using the survival package in R. The timing analysis used a 30-day sliding window to assess the effect of PPI and H2B prescription timing on the association with OS.

Cohort 2: melanoma cohort

We utilized data from the study by Dravillas et al., which included a cohort of patients with stage IV melanoma receiving ICIs, along with both blood and gut microbiome samples. Alpha diversity was assessed using the Shannon index. Statistical significance was calculated using a T-test. Beta diversity was assessed using the Bray–Curtis dissimilarity index and then visualized through principal component analysis. Statistical significance was determined using PERMANOVA. Diversity metrics were calculated using the vegan package in R. Using ANCOM-BC2 (Analysis of Composition Microbiome with Bias Correction 2), differential abundance analysis was performed to identify significant taxonomic differences between patients who used PPIs and those who did not. P-values were adjusted using the false discovery rate (FDR) method. Taxa were considered differentially abundant if they met a significance threshold of q < 0.05 and an absolute log2 fold change > 1.

Cohort 3: NSCLC and RCC cohort

This cohort is part of a non-interventional study approved by the Institutional Review Board of the Ohio State University (IRB#2020C0048, NCT06630429). Patients either had NSCLC or RCC and were receiving standard-of-care ICIs at The Ohio State University. Blood samples were collected at baseline, post-ICI infusion during the first four treatment cycles, and at the 3-month follow-up. Gut microbiome samples were collected at baseline and at 3-month follow-up. This cohort was filtered to include those who had both two blood draws and a baseline microbiome sample. Metagenomic whole-genome shotgun (WGS) sequencing was performed using the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. Prior to taxonomic classification, FastQC was employed to assess the quality of WGS reads. Spearman’s rank correlation was used to assess the strength and direction of associations between microbes and immune cells.

Cytometry by time of flight (CyTOF) mass spectrometry analysis was completed on baseline human blood samples, using methods from Remaily et al.. The antibodies used are described in Supplementary Table S1. Gating was performed as described in Supplementary Figures S1 and S2. The data were processed using Cytobank and analyzed using linear mixed-effects regression with the lme4 package in R.

Results

PPI use is associated with reduced OS in patients treated with ICIs, regardless of the line of therapy

Cohort 1 included 1078 patients with any advanced cancer treated with ICIs, of whom 413 (38%) received PPIs at the time of the first dose of ICI (Fig. 1A). The most common cancers were NSCLC and melanoma, and the most common therapy was single-agent PD-1/L1 (Table 1).

In this cohort, PPIs were the most used concomitant medication, with NSAIDs and the combination of NSAIDs and statins following as the second and third most used (Fig. 1B). PPI use was associated with shorter OS across all cancer types (HR 1.39, p < 0.001 [1.21, 1.60] (Fig. 1C and 1D). PPI use has the strongest association with melanoma (Fig. 1C). PPI use was associated with shorter OS in patients treated with first-line therapy (HR = 1.57, p = 0.0015 [1.19, 2.08]) (Fig. 1E) and in second-line and beyond (HR = 1.25, p = 0.0073 [1.06, 1.46]) (Fig. 1F).

Chemotherapy within one year ameliorates the effect of PPIs on OS

PPI use was associated with shorter OS in patients treated with ICIs who either had not received prior chemotherapy (HR 1.57, p = 0.0001 [1.27, 1.97]) (Fig. 2A) or who were previously treated with chemotherapy (HR 1.20, p = 0.047 [1.00, 1.43]) (Fig. 2B). When evaluating the timing from chemotherapy in relation to ICI start, PPI use was associated with shorter OS only in patients where the last chemotherapy was at least 1 year from time of ICI start (HR = 1.79, p = 0.01 [1.14, 2.81]) (Fig. 2C) but not for patients who had received chemotherapy within 1 year of ICI start (HR = 1.09, p = 0.36 [0.90, 1.33]) (Fig. 2D). To investigate the roles of different acid reducers, we assessed the timing of PPI and H2-blocker (H2B) prescriptions to ICI treatment start and their associations with OS. PPIs showed a greater HR than H2Bs before ICI start and within 100 days post-ICI start (Fig. 2E), and H2Bs were not associated with OS at the time of ICI start. H2B use at start of ICI treatment was not associated with a difference in OS in all cancers, first-line ICI therapy, second-line ICI therapy, patients who received prior chemotherapy, patients who have not received prior chemotherapy, patients who have received chemotherapy within one year of ICI start, and patients who have received chemotherapy longer than one year from ICI start (Figure S2A–G). Next, we examined the impact of PPIs and H2Bs controlling for three separate models: antibiotics (Abx), cancer staging, and the Charlson Comorbidity Index (CCI) (Fig. 2F, G). While PPI use was associated with a significantly increased risk when adjusting for staging (HR = 1.42, p < 0.001 [1.22, 1.86]) and CCI HR = 1.53, p < 0.001 [1.27, 1.84]), that significance was lost when controlling for Abx (Fig. 2F). In contrast, H2B use was not significant across any of the multivariable models (Fig. 2G).

PPI use is associated with changes in the gut microbiome in a melanoma cohort

The second cohort is composed of patients with stage IV melanoma who were scheduled to receive ICIs at OSUCCC. The study lasted 16 weeks, during which both blood samples and gut microbiome samples were collected (Fig. 3A). This cohort included 42 patients with at least one microbiome sample, of whom 9 (21%) received PPIs. There were no significant differences in demographic variables, such as age and sex, among patients with or without PPI use (Table S2). To explore the potential trends of PPI use on the microbiome in patients undergoing ICIs, we compared the differences in the gut microbial communities between PPI users and non-users. Both the PPI users and non-users’ baseline microbiomes were dominated by the Firmicutes and Bacteroidota (Fig. 3B). While there were no significant differences in the alpha or beta diversity of the baseline microbiomes between the PPI users and non-users’ groups (Fig. 3C, D), exploratory taxonomic analysis suggested apparent differences in specific taxa. Specifically, in this patient subset, PPI use was characterized by a higher relative abundance of Lactobacillaceae and Veillonella, while patients who were not using PPIs tended to have more Clostridiaceae spp. and Bacteroides spp. (Fig. 3E). Additionally, we observed a set of microbes that were detected only in non-PPI users, which included some Bifidobacterium species (Fig. 3F).

Exploratory analysis of PPI-associated microbiome trends and circulating immune cell populations

The third cohort consisted of patients with either NSCLC or RCC and was scheduled to receive ICIs. This study collected microbiome samples at baseline and at the 3-month follow-up, as well as blood samples at baseline, the first day of the first 4 ICI cycles, and at the 3-month follow-up (Fig. 4A). There were 8 patients who had both a microbiome sample and at least two blood draws, of whom 3 (37%) received PPIs, which allowed preliminary investigations. The patients did not differ in terms of demographic variables such as age and sex (Table S3). To preliminarily assess the potential impact of PPI use on the microbiome in patients undergoing ICIs, we compared the differences in gut microbial communities between PPI users and non-users. Both the PPI users and non-users’ baseline microbiomes were dominated by the Firmicutes and Bacteroidota (Fig. 4B). To survey the potential relationship of PPI use on circulating immune populations in patients undergoing immunotherapy treatment, we employed a mass cytometry-based approach to quantify the abundance of 40 circulating immune cells using surface and intracellular protein markers on the day of the patients’ first treatment cycle. While monocytic dendritic cells, non-monocytic (CD45 + CD14-), and non-B cell leukocytes (CD45 + CD20-) were the most abundant (Fig. 4C), no significant difference was observed between PPI users and non-users in this small subset. We subsequently explored potential correlations between PPI-associated microbes (Fig. 3E) and immune cell abundances. In this hypotheses-generating analysis, Enterocloster clostridioformis showed a significant correlation with the abundance of terminal effector CD4 T-cells (Fig. 4D).

Discussion

In this study, our objective was to further understand the role of PPI use and its relationship with other therapies in patients undergoing ICI treatment with advanced cancers, specifically evaluating the timing of PPI use, line of therapy, and prior chemotherapy. We found that PPI use was associated with shorter OS in patients with advanced cancers treated with ICI, regardless of line of therapy. However, the association was not observed in patients who had received chemotherapy within 1 year of starting ICI therapy. The association was time-dependent and strongest around the start of ICIs. A similar drug class, H2B, was not associated with reduced survival. PPIs continued to show increased risk when controlling for two separate models: disease staging and CCI. Following the hypothesis that the gut microbiome may mediate the effects on ICI response, we evaluated samples from two prospective studies. Our evaluation noted alterations in the gut microbiome of PPI users. Intriguingly, these taxonomic variations showed exploratory correlations with circulating immune cells, providing a basis for further investigations and mechanistic studies.

PPIs are among the top 10 medications currently prescribed in the USA, and PPIs were demonstrated to be superior to placebo and H2-receptor antagonists in multiple trials. They are approved as short-term treatment for gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), as part of triple therapy for Helicobacter pylori, and for risk reduction for gastric ulcers in patients using NSAIDs. However, a recent study has found that PPIs can inhibit lysosomal enzyme activity by the formation of PPI-peptide arrangements when PPIs are activated at a low pH. Lysosomal enzymes play a role in antigen presentation and the ultimate removal of dysfunctional cells, like tumor cells, by cytotoxic T-lymphocytes. This can negatively impact tumor surveillance by the immune system. However, these studies did not assess potential effects on the microbiome.

We found that PPIs were associated with a higher HR than H2Bs. This trend remained consistent regardless of prescription timing, both within 100 days before ICI start and more than 50 days after ICI start, and persisted across both univariable and multivariable analyses. Despite the benefits of PPI therapy, risks associated with PPI use include decreased immune surveillance as well as an increased incidence of Clostridium difficile-associated diarrhea, which may lead to significant morbidity. In addition to negatively impacting ICI outcomes, Cortazar et al. found that PPI use was an independent risk factor for the development of ICI-induced acute kidney injury (AKI), along with combined CTLA-4 and PD-L1/PD-1 inhibitor therapy and lower eGFR. PPIs are known to rarely cause tubulointerstitial nephritis. Given these concerns, the topic of H2-blockers arises and their role in treating GERD and related disorders while a patient is undergoing ICI treatment. There is currently no significant data on the role of H2-blockers, but it remains an area of further investigation. It is important to note that the number of patients receiving H2Bs in this study is small, particularly in the subgroup analysis. While the association between cancer outcomes and PPI was not seen with H2B, caution should be used in modifying clinical management until these findings can be tested in larger cohorts and causality more directly defined.

Our results are consistent with prior studies from the OAK and POPLAR trials, where the pooled analysis of both trials, totaling 1512 patients, demonstrated that patients treated with atezolizumab who used PPIs had a greater risk of disease progression or death compared to patients who did not use PPIs. Another recent study, totaling 1228 patients receiving ICIs, found that PPI use was associated with worse OS and increased risk of a greater severity gastrointestinal immune-related adverse event. In parallel, our results are consistent with those of Niu et al.. They performed a large retrospective analysis of cancer patients treated with ICIs that encompassed more than 35,000 patients and found that concomitant use of PPIs with ICIs increases the risk of mortality and ICU admissions across multiple cancers. Further meta-analysis studies have also recurrently shown that patients who receive PPIs in combination with ICI have shorter OS and increased risk for progression of disease as well as a higher risk of death. However, these studies were not powered to assess differences by PPI use, nor were comorbidities controlled for. We also found a shorter OS in patients with melanoma who received PPIs as well. Prior research has shown an impact of PPI use on objective response rate and OS in patients with melanoma treated with combination PD-1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors.

While several meta-analyses suggest a negative effect of PPIs on ICI outcomes, the literature remained divided, and several studies have reported no significant interactions. For instance, Li et al. found that PPI use did not significantly impact OS or progression-free survival. Another study, Liu, found that PPIs were associated with OS but not progression-free survival. These differences suggest that the impact of PPIs may be modulated by tumor site, gut microbiome diversity, or other population-specific variables. This heterogeneity highlights the need for prospective trials that control for these variables.

Emerging research indicates that alterations in the intestinal microbiome influence the outcomes of immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy in both mouse and human models. This suggests that concomitant medication use may impact the host microbiome, potentially mitigating tumor-immune responsiveness. Tomita et al. demonstrated reduced efficacy of ICI in patients with NSCLC taking PPIs, while also proving the concept of microbiome modulation in improving ICI efficacy. They supplemented a healthy, commensal anaerobic bacterium, C. butyricum, and demonstrated improved ICI efficacy in patients with NSCLC. Our analyses reveal that PPI use in certain settings is associated with a worsened OS. However, PPI use in patients with advanced cancer who had received prior chemotherapy within 1 year of starting ICI therapy was not associated with changes in OS, and when controlling for Abx. We hypothesize that the variable impact of PPI use may be due to the magnitude of impact chemotherapy and antibiotics have on the microbiome as compared to PPIs. Prior research has shown this effect from antibiotics as well. Several prospective studies have observed an effect of chemotherapy on the microbiome and vice versa. However, the interplay between chemotherapy and PPIs to impact the immune system remains unclear. Further study is needed to determine the impact of concomitant medications, including PPI, on outcomes of patients treated with ICI.

In this study, PPI users exhibited trends toward a distinct microbiome composition, notable characterized by increases in Lactobacillaceae and Veillonella. These findings align with previous large-scale analyses. Jackson et al. analyzed stool samples from a cohort of adults to investigate the effects of PPI use on gut microbiota composition. They found 24 species that were positively associated with PPI use, and the family Lactobacillaceae was notably abundant. McCulloch et al. performed a meta-analysis of melanoma patients treated with anti-PD-1 ICIs. While investigating the role of the PPIs, they similarly reported elevated levels of Veillonella in PPI users. An increase in Lactobacillaceae and Veillonella abundance has been linked to C. difficile infection . An increased abundance of Veillonella has been known to be associated with ulcerative colitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, Crohn’s disease, and COPD; their specific impact on ICI outcomes remains to be fully understood.

There is growing evidence that the microbiome may play a role in immune system response, which in turn impacts cancer therapy response, including immunotherapy. In our exploratory and hypothesis-generating analysis correlating microbes most strongly associated with PPI use with immune cell abundances, we observed a significant correlation between the abundance of terminal effector CD4 T-cells and the relative abundance of Enterocloster clostridioformis (Fig. 4). This observation is particularly intriguing in light of recent findings by Fidelle et al., who demonstrated that after treatment with antibiotics, the genus Enterocloster, in particular, Enterocloster clostridioformis, recolonized the gut. The recolonization, either naturally post-antibiotics or by oral gavage, leads to the downregulation of MAdCAM-1 in the ileum. This downregulation led to the induction of tumor immunosuppressive Treg17 cells and reduced the effectiveness of PD-1 ICI therapy. While our data does not allow for a direct mechanistic link, they provide a preliminary clinical parallel to these experimental models. More research is needed to better understand the role of Enterocloster clostridioformis and other microbes in shaping immune cell composition and function and ICI response.

Several limitations of this study warrant consideration. First, the sample size of the two prospective studies (the melanoma cohort (n = 42) and the NSCLC and RCC cohort (n = 8)) is relatively small. While these cohorts provided critical data, the limited number of participants reduces the statistical power to detect subtle taxonomic shifts. We acknowledge that differential abundance in small cohorts can be sensitive to compositional artifacts and require validations in larger datasets. Consequently, these findings should be interpreted as hypothesis-generating rather than as definitive evidence of an underlying mechanism or for assessing clinical outcomes. Second, all three cohorts were single-center studies at Ohio State University, which may also limit the generalizability of our findings to other geographic or clinical populations. Third, the retrospective nature of our study introduces the risk of residual confounding. Furthermore, lifestyle factors known to affect microbiomes were not controlled for, which could confound the observed associations. Fourth, as an observational study, no causal link between PPI-induced dysbiosis and ICI outcome can be established. Lastly, a notable limitation is that the identification of over-the-counter PPI use relied on self-reporting by patients, which may be susceptible to underreporting. However, the inclusion of these data provides a more comprehensive reflection of PPI usage in the population. Despite these limitations, the consistency of the survival outcomes across different lines of therapy and the alignment of our microbiome data with larger population studies provide a strong rationale for further investigating the impact of acid-reduction concomitant medications on immunotherapy efficacy.

Further research to build on the results of this review includes evaluating human intestinal microbiome samples of patients with advanced cancers throughout their treatment to evaluate for changes in the tumor microbiome. Longitudinal sampling could help to create an individualized microbial profile for each patient and individual patterns of resistance. Buti et al. proposed a novel prognostic index to help stratify patients based on cumulative exposure to CM (e.g., PPI, antibiotics) and response to ICI. The index predicted that cumulative exposure to CM, like steroids [HR 2.3], antibiotics [HR 2.07], and PPI [HR 1.57, CI or p-value], is associated with worse outcomes with ICI. The intestinal microbiome is a complex system, but insights into changes in the microbiome can be tested in model systems or in future clinical trials with concomitant medications, like antibiotics or probiotics.

Conclusion

Our findings suggest that PPI use was associated with shorter OS in patients treated with ICIs across a broad variety of cancers, an association that persisted regardless of line of therapy. In patients with advanced cancers who had recent chemotherapy (< 1 year) exposure, PPI use was not associated with OS, which is hypothesized to be due to disruption of the microbiome by chemotherapy. The impact of PPIs remained significant while controlling for cancer staging and CCI. H2-blockers did not show these associations, highlighting their potential as an alternative to PPI use for patients undergoing immunotherapy. In addition, we observed distinct microbial signatures in the baseline microbiome between PPI users and non-users, with several taxa significantly enriched in each group. These exploratory findings suggest that PPI use may influence microbiome composition. Finally, we observed a significant correlation between the relative abundance of Enterocloster clostridioformis and the abundance of terminal effector CD4 T-cells, suggesting that PPIs may influence microbial composition and, in turn, affect immune cell composition. While these preliminary results suggest that PPI-associated microbiome changes may be linked to circulating immune cell subsets, further research is required to elucidate the mechanisms by which the microbiome might be manipulated to enhance therapeutic effectiveness.
