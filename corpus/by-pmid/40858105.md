# In vivo neutrophils hitchhiking for tumor targeting and microenvironment regulation boosts oncolytic virus therapy.

## Abstract

Neutrophils constitute a substantial proportion of the immune cell population infiltrating tumors, where they play a pivotal role in establishing an immunosuppressive microenvironment to facilitate tumor growth. Our clinical investigation has unveiled that, following oncolytic virus (OV) treatment, immunosuppressive neutrophils could lead to T cell exhaustion and compromised antitumor efficacy. In this study, we devise a dual-functional conjugation strategy that enables OVs to selectively bind with circulating neutrophils and initiate their death. Prior to dysfunction, neutrophils can harbor OVs and facilitate their infiltration into tumors, leading to a 5.38-fold increase in OV levels within tumors compared to direct intravenous injection. Additionally, infiltrated neutrophils undergo dying after 8 h, which promotes T cell priming, reduces T cell exhaustion, and remodels the tumor immune microenvironment. Our findings illuminate the determinants influencing the efficacy of OVs and propose targeted solutions, thereby offering insights for the clinical translation of these therapeutic agents.

Introduction

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) represent a class of therapeutic agents capable of selectively eliminating tumor cells through targeted cytotoxicity and induction of systemic antitumor immune responses. Currently, four OV products, namely Delytact, Lmlygic, Oncorine, and Rigivir, have received approval for clinical oncology treatment. OVs possess the ability to selectively replicate within tumor cells and induce their lysis. The subsequent release of tumor neoantigens efficiently activates immune cells such as dendritic cells (DCs) and T cells, thereby eliciting robust antitumor immune responses. The efficacy of OVs in combating tumors primarily relies on their potent capacity to elicit anti-tumor immune responses. However, the complex immune microenvironment within tumor tissue often hampers the ability of OVs to induce effective anti-tumor immunity, and the key factors influencing their efficacy remain elusive.

Neutrophils play a multifaceted role in the tumor microenvironment, exhibiting both pro-tumorigenic effects and negative regulatory functions. They have been demonstrated to facilitate tumor growth through diverse mechanisms, including secretion of angiogenic factors such as vascular endothelial growth factor, thereby promoting neovascularization that sustains tumor nourishment. Furthermore, within the context of the tumor immune microenvironment, neutrophils exert an inhibitory effect by suppressing immune cell activity, encompassing T cells and natural killer cells, through production of immunosuppressive molecules like arginase. This immunosuppressive function impedes effective anti-tumor immune responses while fostering immune evasion by malignant cells. The intricate role of neutrophils within the tumor tissue necessitates a comprehensive understanding of their interplay with the tumor microenvironment, which is pivotal for devising efficacious therapeutic strategies aimed at harnessing the potential of the immune system in combating cancer.

In a clinical trial (clinical trial information: ChiCTR2300069323), a patient receiving OV treatment was noted to have an elevated proportion of exhausted cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. Cellular chat results indicated that this phenomenon might be associated with the increased infiltration of neutrophils into the tumor post-OV treatment. In the tumor-bearing mice, following the depletion of neutrophils, a reduction in T cell exhaustion and enhancement in the antitumor effectiveness of OVs were observed. Inspired by this, we develop a dual-functional conjugation strategy that facilitates the specific binding of OVs to neutrophils in the bloodstream and triggers neutrophil death in tumor sites. Specifically, prior to neutrophils dysfunction, OVs enhance their enrichment into tumor tissues through the neutrophils-recruiting ability of OV therapy. Simultaneously, the apoptotic signaling pathway of hijacked neutrophils is activated, leading to gradual apoptosis in tumor tissues within 8 h and thereby alleviating immune suppression in the tumor microenvironment. The enrichment of OVs leveraging neutrophils recruitment in tumors is found to be increased by nearly 5.38-fold in a xenograft tumor model. Importantly, this strategy leads to a decrease of 68% in CD8+ T cells with an exhausted phenotype. Therefore, this approach effectively eliminates infiltrating neutrophils from the tumor microenvironment, promotes T cell priming, reduces T cell exhaustion, and remodels the immune landscape within the tumor.

Results

Clinical trial results demonstrated a correlation between the exhaustion of T cells and neutrophils

In the clinical trial (clinical trial registration: ChiCTR2300069323), we examined two tumor tissue samples from the metastatic tissues of a gastric cancer patient: one acquired prior to treatment (sample 1) and the other post-additional OV treatment (sample 2) for single-cell RNA sequencing analysis (scRNA-seq). After filtering out low-quality cells and removing doublet reads, a total of 19,428 single cells were included in the final dataset. We identified nine major cell types according to specific marker genes, including epithelial cells, neurons, stromal cells, myeloid cells, B cells, plasma cells, mast cells, endothelial cells, and T cells (Figures 1A and S1A). Subsequently, we analyzed the cells from samples 1 and 2 separately, revealing a significantly higher proportion of tumor cells in sample 2 compared to sample 1 (Figure S1B). This observation aligned with clinical observations, indicating disease progression in the patient following OV treatment. Subsequently, further analyses of immune cells revealed an increase in myeloid cells following OV treatment (Figure 1B). Thereafter, re-clustering of myeloid cells identified three cell types: macrophages, neutrophils, and DCs (Figures 1C and S1C). Meanwhile, a notable presence of exhausted CD8+ T cells was observed in both sample 1 and sample 2, with sample 2 displaying a higher signature score of CD8+ T cell exhaustion than sample 1 (Figures 1D, 1E, and S1D). Further cell-cell interaction analysis using the CellChat algorithm indicated that myeloid cells, especially for the macrophages and neutrophils, exhibited higher interaction with the exhaustion of CD8+ T cells after receiving OV treatment (Figure S2). The relationship between macrophages and CD8+ T cell exhaustion has been well documented in various studies. What is more, given the extensive subtyping of macrophages, it is challenging to definitively pinpoint the specific subtype that plays a pivotal role and to develop targeted therapies aimed at enhancing the efficacy of OVs. Hence, our primary emphasis is on neutrophils (Figure 1F), seeking to establish whether depleting neutrophils genuinely amplify the antitumor effectiveness of OVs, alongside proposing a complementary therapeutic approach.

To further corroborate the impact of neutrophils on the efficacy of OVs, the inhibitory effect of neutrophils on the antitumor potential of oncolytic adenovirus serotype (ad11) was validated through a series of experiments. TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice were treated intratumorally with PBS, ad11 alone, or ad11 in conjunction with intraperitoneal administration of anti-Ly6G antibodies (anti-Ly6G, which depletes neutrophils) to form the combination treatment group. Remarkably, the combination therapy exhibited superior antitumor effects and conferred a long-term survival advantage compared to ad11 monotherapy (Figures 1G, 1H, and S3A–S3C). In an effort to elucidate the impact of the combination therapy on CD8+ T cells, tumor tissues from TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice subjected to various treatments akin to those in Figure 1G were collected 5 days after the initial treatment. Flow cytometry analysis revealed a significant increase in neutrophil infiltration following ad11 treatment (Figure 1I). Moreover, compared to the PBS group, a higher proportion of CD8+ T cells displaying an exhaustion phenotype (PD-1+ Tim-3+, PD-1+ CD39+) was observed within the tumors after ad11 treatment (Figures 1J and 1K). Significantly, neutrophil depletion treatment did not affect the quantity of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells but markedly reduced the levels of neutrophils and PD-1+ Tim-3+ and PD-1+ CD39+ CD8+ T cells subsequent to ad11 treatment and led to a higher proportion of interferon (IFN)-γ+ CD8+ T cells in tumor tissue (Figures 1J–1M). These collective findings underscored that OV therapy induced the recruitment of neutrophils to the tumor microenvironment, where they may contribute to T cell exhaustion, thereby compromising the antitumor efficacy of OVs.

Characterization of the bioorthogonal OV delivery (BOVDs) system

In light of the heightened presence of neutrophils in the tumor microenvironment after OV therapy, our objective was to leverage neutrophils as vehicle to enhance the targeted delivery of OVs to tumors. However, mitigating the neutrophil-mediated antitumor immune suppression posed a significant challenge. The exhaustion-inducing impact of neutrophils on T cells was effectively mitigated through the implementation of a dual-functional bioorthogonal oncolytic virus delivery system (as illustrated in Figure 2A). The initial step involved the administration of neutrophil depletion antibodies modified with dibenzocyclooctyne (DBCO), enabling the subsequent conjugation of azide (N3)-functionalized OVs onto the surface of neutrophils. To pinpoint the antibodies capable of labeling neutrophils recruited by tumor tissues, three Cy5.5-modified antibodies (anti-Ly6C, anti-Ly6G, and anti-CD11b) were intravenously injected into TC-1-bearing mice (Figures S4A and S4B). Among these, anti-Ly6G-targeted cells exhibited the highest accumulation in the tumor regions. Based on this outcome, DBCO-decorated anti-Ly6G (anti-Ly6G-DBCO) was selected for further investigation.

Extending the circulation duration of the ad11 formulation holds the potential to augment the bioorthogonal reaction efficacy between ad11 and neutrophils. This was achieved by enhancing the ad11 circulation profile through the preparation of ad11-N3 using polyethylene imine and serum albumin, effectively preventing the formation of virus-protein corona as previously reported. Subsequently, ad11-N3 was synthesized by functionalizing with a Distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-polyethylene glycol-azide (DSPE-PEG-N3) decorated liposome to facilitate rapid access to anti-Ly6G-DBCO. The diameter of ad11-N3 measured 218 nm, larger than that of ad11 (Figure S4C). To investigate the distribution of ad11-N3 in vitro, immunofluorescence staining was conducted using endogenous neutrophils derived from TC-1-bearing mice (Figure S4D). Co-localization of fluorescent signals from the neutrophil membrane and ad11-N3 was observed upon the addition of anti-Ly6G-DBCO, indicating the prevention of ad11 endocytosis by neutrophils (Figure 2B, G3 in Figure 2B). Control experiments utilizing anti-Ly6G without DBCO modification (termed as ad@N3, G1 in Figure 2B) or ad11 formulation without N3 functionalization (ad11-Lip formulation, termed as ad@Lip, G2 in Figure 2B) emphasized the specific binding of the BOVDs and the necessity of the bioorthogonal reaction. Moreover, quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) assays demonstrated the inhibitory effect of anti-Ly6G on neutrophil endocytosis of ad11 (Figure S4E). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images further confirmed the binding of ad11-N3 to the surface of neutrophils (Figure 2C). To confirm the conjugation between ad11-N3 and the neutrophils membrane via the bioorthogonal reaction, Cy5.5-labeled ad11-N3 and fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-modified anti-Ly6G-DBCO were subjected to dual-color fluorescence cross-correlation spectroscopy measurements (Figures 2D and S4F). Additionally, a flow cytometry assay also confirmed that the click reaction occurred on the surface of neutrophils (Figures S4G and S4H). The results indicated a strong cross-correlation in the BOVDs group, highlighting the bioorthogonal reaction-dependent coupling of ad11 with the neutrophils membrane.

The function of BOVDs in vitro and in vivo

To optimize the in vivo bioorthogonal reaction between ad11 and neutrophils, we investigated the dosing interval between ad11-N3 and anti-Ly6G-DBCO to ensure the occurrence of the bioorthogonal reaction prior to anti-Ly6G-induced neutrophils apoptosis. Following intravenous administration of anti-Ly6G-DBCO, a minimal decrease in the neutrophil count in peripheral blood was observed within 12 h (Figure S5A). Additionally, intravenous administration of anti-Ly6G-DBCO did not induce significant apoptotic effects on neutrophils in the peripheral blood within the first 12 h after dosing, as evidenced by the labeling of less than 2% of neutrophils with Zombie UV dye (Figures 3A, 3B, and S5B). To evaluate the impact of anti-Ly6G-DBCO on the tumor-targeting capability of neutrophils, TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice were intravenously administered with fluorescence-labeled neutrophils, with or without anti-Ly6G-DBCO treatment. The recruitment of neutrophils by tumor tissues was quantified using the in vivo imaging system (IVIS). The IVIS results revealed that the tumor-targeting ability of neutrophils remained unaffected, with no significant difference in the accumulation of neutrophils in tumors between the anti-Ly6G-DBCO group and the PBS group within 8 h post-administration (Figure 3C). Subsequent to 8 h post-administration, a gradual decrease in fluorescence intensity was observed in the anti-Ly6G-DBCO group within tumor tissues, while no significant changes were noted in the PBS group (Figures 3D and S5C). Notably, there was no difference in the number of fluorescence-labeled neutrophils in the peripheral blood between the anti-Ly6G-DBCO group and the PBS group at 9 hours post-injection, indicating that neutrophils were indeed cleared within the tumor rather than redistributed in the peripheral blood (Figures S5D–S5F). These observations suggested that anti-Ly6G-DBCO effectively enhanced the clearance of neutrophils within tumor tissues without compromising their ability to migrate into the tumor site within 8 hours post anti-Ly6G-DBCO injection.

To explore the in vivo functionality of BOVDs, TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice were sequentially intravenously injected with fluorescence-modified anti-Ly6G-DBCO and ad11-N3. Subsequently, blood cells were extracted for flow cytometry analysis. The results indicated that nearly 100% of neutrophils were labeled with anti-Ly6G-DBCO after 2 hours of injection (Figure S5G), with 9.07% ± 1.60% of neutrophils being targeted by ad11 (Figure 3E). As expected, Lip-ad11-N3, which did not undergo the virus-protein corona replacement strategy but utilized liposomes containing N3 terminally to coat ad11, exhibited limited in vivo binding efficiency to neutrophils due to its restricted circulation profile (Figure S5H). To confirm the binding of ad11 to the surface of neutrophils rather than its endocytosis, neutrophils were extracted from the peripheral blood of mice 1 hour post-BOVDs or ad@Lip treatment for ex vivo immunofluorescence staining. Co-localization was observed between the membrane of neutrophils and ad11-N3 but not with ad11-Lip, confirming that BOVDs could bind to the surface of neutrophils (Figure 3F). Subsequently, the in vivo function of BOVDs was observed using two-photon confocal microscopy. Motile cells labeled with both types of BOVDs fluorescence were visualized in tumor vasculature, indicating the successful cell-mediated delivery of ad11 (Figure 3G). A notable observation was the co-localization of ad11-N3 and anti-Ly6G-DBCO, displaying a distinct cell membrane-like ring structure, thereby confirming the binding of ad11 to the surface of neutrophils rather than its endocytosis by neutrophils (Figure 3G, amplified field). The impact of BOVDs in tumor tissues was further evaluated. Tumor tissues were extracted from TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice 2 hours after intravenous treatment with fluorescence-modified BOVDs or ad@Lip. Slices were prepared for immunofluorescence staining, revealing that ad11-N3 co-localized with neutrophils in tumor tissues while ad11-Lip did not (Figure 3H). To evaluate the translational feasibility of the BOVDs strategy for human applications, we performed experiments using patient blood samples. Flow cytometry and qPCR assays validated the effective binding of ad11 to neutrophils (Figures S5I–S5K). Furthermore, virus titer assays illustrated that the BOVDs strategy could maintain the biological activity of ad11 even in human serum (Figure S5L). In conclusion, these findings collectively showcased that BOVDs enabled the binding of ad11 to neutrophil surfaces without undergoing endocytosis, suggesting promising potential for human applications.

The recruitment of neutrophils into tumors suggested the potential for BOVDs to enhance ad11 enrichment in tumor tissues. To investigate the distribution of ad11 in BOVDs, Cy5.5-labeled ad11 formulation was intravenously administered to TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice. The IVIS assay results revealed a higher concentration of ad11 at the tumor site in the BOVDs group compared to the other groups after 8 h post-injection (Figures 4A and 4B). Moreover, the ex vivo analysis of tumor tissue in the BOVDs group showed an increased fluorescence intensity in tumor tissues at 48 h post-administration (Figures 4C and 4D). Following this, the distribution of ad11 in various organs among different treatment groups was evaluated using a qPCR assay. The findings revealed that ad11 in the BOVDs group accumulated in tumors at significantly higher levels compared to other groups, with 1.71-fold and 5.38-fold increases over the ad11-N3 group and the bare ad11 group, respectively. Moreover, the BOVDs group exhibited a reduced liver distribution compared to both the ad11-N3 group and the bare ad11 group, with reductions of 64.8% relative to the bare ad11 group and 45.2% relative to the ad11-N3 group (Figures 4E and 4F). To evaluate the efficacy of BOVDs in achieving intratumoral accumulation in vivo, we analyzed intratumor viral titers in each treatment group. The result revealed that the virus titer of BOVDs within the tumor exceeded that of the other two groups (Figure 4G). Subsequently, we examined the transfection capacity of the intratumoral virus. HEK293 cells were co-incubated with tumor homogenates from tumor-bearing mice treated with formulations containing ad11-GFP (ad11 carrying the green fluorescent protein gene). Analysis using fluorescence confocal microscopy and flow cytometry demonstrated a significantly higher virus quantity in the tumor homogenate of mice treated with BOVDs compared to the other two groups (Figures 4H–4K). These findings confirmed that BOVDs could enhance the enrichment of ad11 in tumor tissues and demonstrated the potential of utilizing BOVDs to improve the targeted delivery and accumulation of OVs in tumor sites, offering promising implications for enhancing oncolytic viral therapy efficacy.

BOVDs promote the antitumor efficacy of ad11 in a neutrophil-dependent manner

To assess the neutrophil depletion efficacy of BOVDs, TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice were intravenously treated with various formulations including PBS, bare ad11, anti-Ly6G, ad11-N3, ad@N3, or BOVDs (Figure 5A). The impact on the tumor microenvironment post-bare ad11 treatment appeared negligible, suggesting that intravenous ad11 administration may not significantly affect the tumor due to inadequate enrichment (Figures 5B, 5C, and S6A). In contrast, mice treated with ad11-N3 exhibited an increased presence of neutrophils (Figures 5B, 5C, and S6A). This indicated that OV treatment could boost the infiltration of neutrophils into tumor tissues. Of significance, similar to anti-Ly6G treatment and the co-administration of ad11-N3 with anti-Ly6G treatment (ad@N3), BOVDs treatment resulted in a substantial reduction in neutrophil count within tumors, affirming their potent ability for neutrophils clearance (Figures 5B, 5C, and S6A). This observation underscored the efficacy of BOVDs in depleting neutrophils from the tumor microenvironment.

With efficient neutrophil clearance, BOVDs demonstrated potent antitumor capabilities (Figure 5A). Consistent with previous findings, ad11-N3 exhibited superior antitumor efficacy compared to bare ad11, attributed to its enhanced tumor enrichment capacity and prolonged circulation profile. Notably, BOVDs showcased the most significant inhibition of tumor growth, with the BOVDs group displaying the lowest tumor weight and highest percent survival among all treatment groups (Figures 5D–5F and S6B). Besides these, compared to other groups, the BOVDs group exhibited higher concentrations of tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α), IFN-γ, and interleukin (IL)-6, underscoring the superior antitumor efficacy induced by BOVDs (Figure S6C). Before the 6th dosing, approximately 48.53% ± 5.12% of neutrophils remained in the peripheral blood of the BOVDs group, indicating sufficient neutrophil presence to support BOVDs’ function during the final treatment (Figure S6D). Furthermore, no evident damage was observed following the various treatments. After 22 days from the initial treatment, the number of neutrophils in the peripheral blood had returned to normal level (Figure S6E). No changes in body weight were observed (Figure S6F), and there was no evidence of tissue histological damage in major organs (Figure S6G). Moreover, no alterations in blood routine or blood biochemical indicators were noted at the endpoint of the study (day 12, Figures S6H–S6N). To explore the long-term toxicity of these treatments, we conducted extended toxicity assessments in C57BL/6, ICR, BALB/c, and SD rats. The outcomes revealed no apparent organ damage, abnormal blood biochemistry, or irregularities in blood routine throughout the experiment, affirming the safety of the BOVDs strategy (Figures S7–S10).

To elucidate the role of neutrophils in the treatment process of BOVDs, the tumor therapeutic effects of ad11-N3 and BOVDs were evaluated in the neutrophil pre-depletion model. The neutrophil pre-depletion model was established by initially administering anti-Ly6G antibodies intra-abdominally, followed by similar intravenous treatment procedures as described in the subcutaneous tumor model (Figure 5G). Following neutrophils depletion, the antitumor advantage of BOVDs over ad11-N3 vanished, confirming the critical role of neutrophils in the efficacy of BOVDs (Figures 5H–5J). These findings collectively demonstrated that BOVDs enhance the antitumor efficacy of ad11 through a mechanism dependent on neutrophils.

BOVDs promote the antitumor immune response and avoid the exhaustion of T cells

The immune responses triggered by each treatment were subsequently evaluated. TC-1 subcutaneous tumor-bearing mice underwent three treatment cycles, and tumor tissues were harvested on day 5 for immune analysis. Compared to the other treatment groups, BOVDs led to an increase in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells, particularly cytotoxic CD8+ T cells (IFN-γ+ CD8+, Figures 6A–6C, S11, and S12). Notably, the BOVDs group exhibited the highest levels of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells among all groups. Additionally, the expression of the PD-1 and Tim-3 on CD8+ T cells, individually or in combination, decreased in the BOVDs group and the anti-Ly6G group (Figures 6D–6I, S11, and S12). The percentage of exhausted CD8+ T cells (PD-1+ TIM-3+) in the BOVDs group was only 28.34% of that in the ad11-N3 group, indicating that BOVDs mitigated the immune exhaustion state within tumors. Notably, the exhaustion state of CD8+ T cells was comparable between the BOVDs group and the ad@N3 group. In summary, BOVDs effectively decreased the amount of neutrophils and alleviated the depletion of CD8+ T cells, pointing toward their ability to modulate the tumor microenvironment toward a more immunostimulatory state.

To further understand the impact of BOVDs strategy on CD8+ T cells, CD45+ immune cells were collected from tumor-bearing mice after treatment with PBS, ad11-N3, and BOVDs for scRNA-seq. After quality control and filtration, we obtained 16757 CD45+ immune cells and further identified 10 cell types according to the expression of cluster-specific genes and canonical marker genes (Figure S13). To further dissect the mechanism of exhausted CD8+ T cells induced by neutrophils, we performed in-depth analysis of T cells. Re-clustering of T cells found that CD8+ T cells were the most abundant among other subtypes of T cells (Figure S14). We further estimated the exhaustion scores of T cells using an exhaustion signature reported in a previous study. Within CD8+ T cells in each group, it could be found that a higher proportion of exhausted CD8+ T cells were present in the ad11-N3 group, compared to PBS and BOVDs groups (Figures 7A, S15A, and S15B), while the BOVDs group showed a less exhausted state (Figures 7A, S15A, and S15B). Meanwhile, Monocle 3 trajectory analysis of CD8+ T cell differentiation revealed two primary divergent trajectories (Figure 7B). Notably, the ad11-N3 group exhibited a significantly higher proportion of exhausted CD8+ T cells compared to both the PBS and BOVDs groups (Figure 7B).

We subsequently conducted a detailed examination of CD8+ T cells, classifying them into six distinct clusters (cluster 0–cluster 5, C0–C5, as depicted in Figure 7C). A separate analysis of the CD8+ T cells revealed the distinct distribution and representation of each cluster, with an increase in the fractions of cells in C4, an exhausted-like cluster based on its relatively high expression of Ctla4, Pdcd1, Lag-3, and Havcr2 (Figure 7D). Additionally, an increase in C2 (tissue-resident memory CD8+T cells, Lgals3, Gzma, and Gzmb) and a reduction in C3 (effector-like CD8+ T cells, Ifng, and Ccl5) were also noted (Figure 7D). Furthermore, Monocle 3 analysis confirmed the exhausted state of C4 (Figure S15C). We further explored the detailed mechanism of exhaustion of CD8+ T cells induced by neutrophils. Cell-cell interaction analysis revealed augmented interactions between neutrophils and cluster C4 in the ad11-N3 group compared to the PBS and BOVDs groups, with increased number and weight of interactions (Figures S15D and S15E). These findings underscored that ad11-N3 treatment enhanced cellular communication between neutrophils and cluster C4, ultimately leading to cellular exhaustion. Subsequently, we delved into investigating the potential pathways mediating this exhaustion. Ad11-N3 treatment increased multiple pathways and associated receptor-ligand interactions between neutrophils and C4, including Cd274-Pdcd1, a well-known exhaustion-related pathway (Figure 7E). Remarkably, C4 treated with ad11-N3 exhibited heightened interactions with neutrophils via Cd274-Pdcd1, surpassing those observed in the PBS and BOVDs groups (Figures 7F and 7G). Consistent with this result, flow cytometry assay demonstrated that ad11-N3 treatment induced an increased proportion of PD-L1+ neutrophils in tumors, compared with PBS group (Figures S16A and S16B), while fewer PD-L1+ neutrophils were observed in tumors after BOVDs treatments (Figures S16A and S16B). To further investigate the Cd274-Pdcd1 pathway between T cells and neutrophils, we extracted neutrophils from tumor tissues in C57 mice following ad11-N3 treatment and co-cultured them with T cells in the presence or absence of anti-PD-L1 blockade. We observed that, upon co-culture with neutrophils, T cells exhibited signs of exhaustion (upregulation of PD-1, Tim-3, and Lag-3, as well as downregulation of CD25, Granzyme B, and TNF-α, accompanied by a reduced proliferative capacity), which were alleviated by treatment with PD-L1 blockade (Figures S16C–S16I). After that, the synergistic effect of BOVDs in combination with PD-1 blockade was investigated (Figure S16J). It was observed that immune checkpoint block treatment enhanced the antitumor efficacy of BOVDs (Figures S16K–S16M). All these results demonstrated that BOVDs treatment probably alleviated CD8+ T cells via the depletion neutrophils and block the Cd274-Pdcd1 axis.

Discussion

The comprehensive exploration of key factors influencing the effectiveness of OVs remains incomplete. Understanding the intricate factors that govern the efficacy of OV therapy within the tumor microenvironment is an endeavor in modern oncology. This nuanced understanding has the potential to revolutionize cancer treatment paradigms by elucidating the complex interplay between viral dynamics, host immune responses, and tumor stroma. The importance of this research lies in its capacity to reveal previously unrecognized vulnerabilities and therapeutic opportunities and provide an approach, ultimately paving the way for precision medicine strategies tailored to individual tumors. Recent advancements in this field have provided promising insights into the critical determinants that influence outcomes of OV therapy. Studies have elucidated how the tumor immune landscape can either impede or facilitate viral replication and dissemination, thereby affecting treatment efficacy. Furthermore, research into the role of stromal components has highlighted their significant impact on therapeutic response and resistance mechanisms. The identification of potential biomarkers for predicting therapy outcomes marks a crucial step toward personalized cancer care. By exploring these complexities in greater depth, researchers are paving the way for more effective and targeted OV therapies that capitalize on the characteristics of the tumor microenvironment. This progress not only improved treatment outcomes for cancer patients but also represents a significant advancement toward precision oncology approaches in the fight against cancer.

In our study, a comprehensive analysis of scRNA-seq data from both patients and mice has unveiled the pivotal role of neutrophils in OV therapy. To harness the tumor-homing capabilities of neutrophils while effectively managing their clearance, we have introduced the innovative BOVDs strategy. This approach not only tackles the issue of neutrophil-mediated viral clearance but also capitalizes on the intrinsic immune functions of these cells. Neutrophils, as crucial components of the innate immune system, exhibit the capacity to phagocytose viruses and combat infections, potentially compromising the effectiveness of OVs. Through the BOVDs strategy, we have successfully engineered a mechanism to ensure that OVs remain tethered to the surface of neutrophils, preventing their internalization and subsequent clearance. This breakthrough not only enhances the therapeutic potential of OVs but also showcases a strategic advancement in leveraging the immune system’s capabilities to optimize cancer treatment outcomes.

While we have undertaken functional validations regarding the impact of neutrophils on OVs, a deeper exploration into the underlying molecular mechanisms is essential to enrich our understanding of the role of neutrophils in OV therapy. Notably, the literature suggests that neutrophils play a key role in eliminating infectious agents, including viruses, from the bloodstream. Concurrently, our developed strategy, BOVDs, not only facilitated neutrophil depletion and enhanced OVs accumulation within tumors but also played a pivotal role in circumventing neutrophil-mediated OV endocytosis in the peripheral blood. Through empirical investigations, we have substantiated that OVs exhibit a specific binding affinity to the surface of neutrophils, evading internalization into intracellular compartments. The impediment of viral phagocytosis by neutrophils within the BOVDs framework further contributes to the antitumor effectiveness of the approach. These findings underscore the multifaceted benefits of the BOVDs strategy in optimizing OV therapy and highlight the intricate interplay between neutrophils and viral dynamics within the tumor microenvironment.

In the context of clinical translation, the BOVDs strategy offers a promising avenue that necessitates only intermittent injections to bolster the antitumor efficacy of OVs. Notably, the current clinical landscape predominantly integrates OVs with immune checkpoint inhibitors like Opdivo and Keytruda, encompassing anti-PD-1 or anti-PD-L1 monoclonal antibodies, aligning with NCCN (National Comprehensive Cancer Network) guidelines. Our research introduces a combination therapy as a viable clinical alternative. This approach not only presents a feasible option for enhancing therapeutic outcomes but also showcases potential applicability across a spectrum of biological therapeutics. By extending this methodology, the development of other biological agents, such as chimeric antigen receptor T cells, can be facilitated for targeted elimination of efficacy-hindering cells, thereby fostering organism enrichment and amplifying their antitumor efficacy. This research signifies a significant stride toward advancing precision medicine approaches and underscores the potential for synergistic therapeutic strategies in the realm of cancer treatment.

Limitations of the study

Our study has certain limitations, primarily due to the restricted number of clinical samples, which hindered a more comprehensive analysis of the interaction between myeloid cells and the OV. Furthermore, the safety of BOVDs has been validated exclusively in small animal models. The absence of experimental data from larger animals or human trials represents an additional limitation of this study.

Method details

Single-cell RNA sequencing

Human sample collection: The specimens were derived from a colon metastasis of a 55-year-old male patient with gastric cancer who before (sample 1) and after (sample 2) received additional oncolytic virus therapy toward his colon metastasis (Clinical trial information: ChiCTR2300069323).

Mice sample collection: Mice intravenously received PBS, ad11-N3, or BOVDs treatment for six times with two-day interval. 12 days after the first injection, mice were sacrificed and tumors were collected. After preparing single-cell suspensions from tumor tissues of five mice that received identical treatments, the suspensions were thoroughly mixed. Subsequently, CD45+ cells were isolated using a CD45+ selection kit (EasySep Mouse CD45 Positive Selection Kit, Catalog # 18945) for further processing.

Cell capture and cDNA synthesis: Cell capture and complementary DNA (cDNA) amplification and library construction was performed on the 10x Genomics Chromium Platform. Briefly, the single cell suspension counted by Count Star, was loaded onto the Chromium single-cell controller (10x Genomics) to generate single-cell gel beads in an emulsion. The cells were partitioned into GEM to achieve single cell resolution. Captured cells were lysed and their released RNA was barcoded through reverse transcription in individual GEMs. The resulting cDNA was amplified, and its quality assessed using an Agilent Bioanalyzer system (performed by CapitalBio Technology, Beijing). Single Cell 5′ Library and Gel Bead Kit V3 were utilized for constructing single-cell RNA-seq libraries. The libraries were submitted to an Illumina Novaseq6000 (Illumina) and pair-end (150 bp) sequencing was performed.

scRNA-seq data processing

Raw scRNA-seq data were processed using Cell Ranger Software (version 4.0.0, 10x Genomics). For human samples, the sequenced raw read data were aligned to the human reference genome GRCh38. For mouse samples, the sequenced data was applied to align reads to the mouse reference genome GRCm38(mm10). The raw gene expression matrix was converted into a Seurat object via Seurat R package (V5.2.1). Human cells with >6000 or <300 genes or >20% mitochondrial genes were discarded. Mouse cells with >8000 or <500 genes or >10% mitochondrial genes were filtered out. Doublet Finder was used to identify and remove potential doublets. Data were normalized, scaled, and subjected to principal component analysis. After selecting 2000 highly variable genes by the Find Variable Features function in Seurat, principal components analysis (PCA) was performed. The integrated data were visualized using Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP). Cell clusters were identified by a shared nearest-neighbor (SNN) modularity optimization-based clustering algorithm set with a resolution of 0.6 for human cells and 0.8 for mouse cells. Differentially expressed genes of each cluster were determined using the Find All Markers module, and genes expressed in more than 25% of the cells were selected. Cell types were identified based on the expression of canonical marker genes. The cell-cell interaction network was analyzed and visualized using the CellChat R package (version 1.6.1), which contains a high-quality database of signaling molecules that interact with each other. CellChat was also applied to compare the differences of the ligand-receptor interactions between neutrophils and T cells. To evaluate the exhaustion state of T cells, we estimated the scores of exhausted signatures proposed by the previous study using the AUCell R package (version 1.24.0). Pseudotime trajectory based on the gene expression profiles of T cells was performed using Monocle 3(version 1.3.7).

Preparation of ad11 formulations

Liposome were formulated utilizing a thin-film hydration process as previously described. In brief, a mixture of 3.6 mg Lecithin, 0.4 mg DSPE-PEG-N3, and 1.2 mg cholesterol was dissolved in 4 mL dichloromethane within a 50 mL round-bottom flask, followed by vacuum evaporation to generate a thin lipid film. Subsequently, 2 mL of PBS was added to the flask for hydration of the thin layer at a temperature of 37°C in a water bath, resulting in Lipo-N3. Then, ad11 (at a concentration of 5 × 108 pfu/mL) was successively mixed with PEI (at a concentration of 25.4 μg/mL) and BSA (at a concentration of 50 mg/mL) to obtain ad11-BSA. The mixture was then added to Lipo-N3 (2 mg/mL) and subjected to sonication at 4°C for 10 min, leading to the formation of ad11-N3. Ad11-Lipo was prepared similarly as ad11-N3, with DSPE-PEG2000 replacing DSPE-PEG-N3. The Lip-ad11-N3 was prepared by sonicating a mixture of ad11 (at a concentration of 108 pfu/mL) and Lipo-N3 (2 mg/mL) at 4°C for 10 min.

Preparation of anti-Ly6G-DBCO

The anti-Ly6G antibodies were dissolved in 0.1 M NaHCO3 buffer (pH 8.5) to achieve a final concentration of 2.3 mg/mL and incubated with DBCO-PEG4-NHS (10 equivalents) for 3.5 h at room temperature under moderate agitation. Prior to this, DBCO-PEG4-NHS was dissolved in anhydrous DMF to prepare a stock solution (5 mg/mL). After incubation, the antibody was purified through desalting and subsequently retrieved by centrifugation using 30-kDa Ultra ultrafiltration centrifuge columns at 14000 g for 15 min to remove any unbound small molecules. The purified antibody was then stored in PBS at 4°C. Anti-CD15-DBCO was synthesized similarly to the method described above. To label the anti-Ly6G-DBCO with FITC, it was dissolved in a NaHCO3 buffer (pH 8.5) with a final concentration of 2 mg/mL. This solution was incubated with three equivalents of FITC-NHS for 3 h at room temperature under moderate agitation. Following the incubation, the antibody underwent successive purification steps involving desalting and ultrafiltration before being stored in PBS at 4°C.

Dual color fluorescence cross-correlation spectroscopy

The anti-Ly6G-DBCO-FITC at a concentration of 0.2 nM and ad11-Cy5.5-N3 at a concentration of 0.6 nM were dispersed in PBS and incubated for 1 h at room temperature to facilitate the click reaction. The DBCO-omitting group and N3-omitting group were employed as the control groups. The conjugation efficiency of the reaction was assessed by quantifying the relative cross-correlation amplitudes.

Intravital imaging

The in vivo intravital imaging of the bioorthogonal reaction was performed following a similar protocol as previously reported. Briefly, the TC-1 tumor-bearing mouse was intravenously administered with anti-Ly6G-DBCO-FITC (0.2 mg per mouse) initially. Following a 4-h interval, rhodamine-ad11-N3 (7.5 × 107 pfu per mouse) was subsequently injected intravenously. The dorsal skinfold chamber was observed under the two-photon microscope.

Isolation of neutrophils

The neutrophils were freshly isolated from bone marrow of tumor bearing mice using immunomagnetic negative selection. Mice were euthanized using carbon dioxide overdose. Two hindlimb bones (femur and tibia) were collected, followed by thorough removal of excess muscle tissue and sterilization using 70% ethanol. Subsequently, the epiphyses were sectioned to allow flushing with PBS, and cells were obtained through centrifugation. Neutrophils were isolated from bone marrow cells utilizing the Mouse Neutrophil Enrichment Kit (Miltenyi Biotec), following the manufacturer’s protocol.

Real-time quantitative PCR

The real-time quantitative PCR (qPCR) assay was performed according to previously described methods. Briefly, DNA was extracted from cells or fresh tissues using the Fast-pure Cell/Tissue DNA Isolation Mini Kit (Vazyme, China). The qPCR analysis was conducted on the Applied Biosystems QuantStudio 3.0 (ABI Quantitative 3.0 system) platform to detect E1A expression levels using specific primers ad11-F (TTGGACGGCTCCTGGAATAG) and ad11-R (TGTGACGGAAACAACCCTGACT). Relative results are presented relative to the values observed in the PBS-treated group. The measurements in the PBS group are normalized to a value of 1.

Cellular uptake assay

Neutrophils were initially incubated with PBS, anti-Ly6G, and anti-Ly6G-DBCO for a duration of 2 h under 37°C. Subsequently, the cells underwent multiple washing steps with PBS before being incubated with ad11-N3 for an additional 2 h. Following thorough washing, the cellular uptake of ad11-N3 was quantified using qPCR (Applied Biosystems QuantStudio 3.0, ThermoFisher).

Analysis of bioorthogonal reaction of each formulation

For the analysis of bioorthogonal reactions in vitro, one million bone marrow cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS initially. Subsequently, cells (1×106/sample) were labeled with anti-Ly6G-DBCO-FITC (20 μg) in 0.1 mL PBS for 30 min at room temperature. After centrifugation and removal of the antibody containing solution, the antibody-labeled cells were directly suspended in ad11-Cy5.5-N3 (108 pfu) and incubated for 30 min at room temperature. Following two washes with PBS through centrifugation, the cells were stained with 1,1′-dioctadecyl-3,3,3′,3′-tetramethylindocarbocyanine perchlorate (DiI) to visualize the cell membrane and 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) to stain the nucleus before being examined using Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (CLSM, Olympus IXplore SpinSR). For the in vivo analysis of bioorthogonal reactions, TC-1 bearing mice were injected with anti-Ly6G-DBCO-FITC (0.2 mg per mouse) and ad11-Cy5.5-N3 (7.5 × 107 pfu per mouse) at a four-hour interval. One hour later, peripheral blood and tumors were collected for subsequent CLSM imaging, flow cytometry assays (CytoFlex S, Beckman Coulter), and immunofluorescence slice staining. And the data was analyzed with FlowJo V10 software. The DBCO-omitting group, N3-omitting group and virus protein corona replacement strategy-omitting group were employed as the control groups.

Scanning electron microscopy assay

The neutrophils (106/sample) isolated from tumor-bearing mice were incubated with Ly6G-DBCO (20 μg) or PBS for 0.5 h at 37°C. Following the washing step, unbound antibodies were effectively eliminated, and ad11-N3 (108 pfu) was subsequently introduced and incubated for an additional 0.5 h. Subsequently, the neutrophils were collected and fixed with 2.5% glutaraldehyde for SEM analysis.

The investigation of neutrophil tumor-target ability

Male C57 mice were subcutaneously inoculated with 2×106 TC-1 tumor cells in the right flank. Upon reaching a volume of 100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into two groups. Bone marrow-derived neutrophils from C57 mice were extracted using a previously established method and subsequently labeled with Cell-Tracker Red CMTPX. The control group received intravenous injection of one million Cell-Tracker-labeled neutrophils, while the experimental group received intravenous injection of one million Cell-Tracker-labeled neutrophils after in vitro incubation with 0.02 mg anti-Ly6G-DBCO for 15 min at room temperature. After eight hours of intravenous administration, the mice were sacrificed and tumor tissues were extracted for ex vivo imaging using the In Vivo Imaging System (IVIS) (PerkinElmer, USA).

Male C57 mice were subcutaneously inoculated with 2×106 TC-1 tumor cells in the right flank. Upon reaching a volume of 100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into two groups. Bone marrow neutrophils from C57 mice were extracted using a previously established method and labeled with DiD Perchlorate. The control group received an intravenous injection of one million DiD-labeled neutrophils, while the experimental group received an intravenous injection of one million DiD-labeled neutrophils after in vitro incubation with anti-Ly6G-DBCO (0.02 mg) for 15 min at room temperature. Fluorescence imaging using IVIS was performed at different time points (2h, 4h, 6h, 8h, 10h, and 12h) post-injection for both groups.

anti-Ly6G-induced neutrophil apoptosis

TC-1 bearing mice were intravenously injected with 0.2mg of anti-Ly6G-DBCO, and peripheral blood samples were collected via orbital sampling at 2h, 4h, 8h, and 12h for subsequent flow cytometry analysis. Neutrophils were identified by gating strategy showed in results, followed by further identification of apoptotic cells within the neutrophil population using zombie UV dye.

T cells exhaustion investigation

TC-1 bearing mice were intravenously received ad11-N3 treatment for three times with one-day interval. After that, mice were sacrificed and tumors were collected and incubated with collagenase type IV (1 mg/mL) for one hour. Neutrophils in tumors were extracted as utilizing the Mouse Tumor-infiltrating Neutrophil Enrichment Kit (Solarbio, cat#P2430), following the manufacturer’s protocol. And the extracted neutrophils were than further purified by using Mouse Neutrophil Enrichment Kit (Miltenyi Biotec), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Spleens were collected from TC-1 tumor-bearing mice that did not receive any treatment, and CD8+ T cells were isolated using a mouse CD8+ T cell isolation kit (Selleck) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. 105 of T cells and 5 × 104 neutrophils were mixture and added to U plate of 96-well plate in the T cell media. For the blockade group, anti-PD-L1 antibody (10 μg/mL, Biolegend, Cat#124318) were added. After 72 h of co-incubation, T cells were harvested for surface marker and intracellular cytokine staining.

T cell media

For the stimulation and culture of T cells, T cell media were prepared by the following components: 435 mL of RPMI-1640 (Gibco), 50 mL of heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco), 100X sodium pyruvate (1 mM, Wuhan Pricella Biotechnology Co., Ltd.), 100X non-essential amino acid (1 mM, Wuhan Pricella Biotechnology Co., Ltd.), N-2-Hydroxyethylpiperazine-N-2-Ethane Sulfonic Acid (HEPES, 20 mM, Wuhan Pricella Biotechnology Co., Ltd), anti-CD3 (5 μg/mL, Biolegend, Cat#100340), anti-CD28 (5 μg/mL, BioXcell, Cat#BE0015-1), IL-2 (20 ng/mL, ABclonal, Cat#RP01384).

Tissue distribution

The TC-1 bearing mouse model was established by inoculating 2×106 cells into the right flank. In Vivo Imaging System was subsequently employed to monitor the accumulation of ad11 in the tumor area at each time point. After intravenous administration for 48 h, mice were sacrificed and tumors were extracted for ex vivo imaging. For the distribution assay, mice were sacrificed three hours after administration of bare ad11, ad11-N3, or BOVDs, and vital organs were collected. Subsequently, DNA was extracted from each organ and the distribution of ad11 in organs was determined using qPCR assay as described previously. For the investigation of re-distribution of neutrophils in peripheral blood. DiD-labeled neutrophils were intravenously injected into tumor-bearing mice with or without anti-Ly6G-DBCO treatment. Peripheral blood samples were collected at one-hour intervals starting nine hours post-injection, and the fluorescence intensity of the DiD-labeled neutrophils was measured using a microplate reader.

Titer assay

After receiving different treatment, mice were euthanized, and the tumors were excised. Tumor tissues were subsequently homogenized at 4°C, followed by centrifugation at 10,000 g, and the resulting supernatant was collected for further use. HEK-293 cells were seeded for 3,000 cells per well in the 96-well plate and cultured overnight. 20 μL of supernatant mentioned above was then added and incubated for 3 days. Finally, cytopathic effect (CPE) in each well was recorded and the titer of ad11 was determined by Reed and Mench assay.

Immunofluorescence assay

Ad11-GFP (a recombinant adenovirus carrying the Green Fluorescent Protein gene) was utilized to prepare each formulation, which was then administered to tumor-bearing mice. After that, mice were euthanized, and the tumors were excised. Tumor tissues were subsequently homogenized at 4°C, followed by centrifugation at 10,000 g, and the resulting supernatant was collected for further use. Concurrently, HEK-293 cells were seeded at a density of 100,000 cells per well in confocal dishes and incubated overnight. Subsequently, the supernatants were added to the HEK-293 cells and incubated for 24 h. The expression of GFP in each group was then examined using confocal microscopy.

BOVDs strategy in human neutrophils

Peripheral blood was extracted from patients enrolled in the Phase I clinical trial. Ad11-N3 and anti-CD15-DBCO (with CD15 serving as a marker for human neutrophils) were prepared as mentioned. Anti-CD15-DBCO and ad11-N3 were then sequentially added to the human peripheral blood with a one-hour interval. The efficacy of ad11-N3 hijacking in human peripheral blood was subsequently assessed using qPCR and flow cytometry. Meanwhile, the survival of the ad11 formulation in human serum was determined by evaluating the titers of ad11 in human serum and in PBS.

Safety study

Safety study of long-term toxicity was performed in C57BL/6 mice, ICR mice, Balb/c mice and SD rat. Mice or rat were intravenous administration with PBS, anti-Ly6G, ad11-N3, ad@N3, bare ad11 or BOVDs treatment for six times. Peripheral blood was collected in routine interval mentioned in supplemental figures, and centrifuged at 5,000 g for 10 min to separate serum. After 30 days of the first injection, mice and rat were sacrificed and the tissues of heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney were collected and immediately fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for histopathological examinations. For short-term toxicity, C57BL/6 mice received different treatments were sacrificed at day 12, and peripheral blood and major organs were collected for safety analysis.

Flow cytometry

After each treatment, tumors from each group were collected for flow cytometry analysis. Specifically, the tumors were dissected and incubated with collagenase type IV (1mg/mL) for one hour. The resulting mixture was then passed through a 40 μm screen mesh to obtain a single cell suspension. Quantification and statistical analysis

All data are presented as mean ± s.d., with a minimum of three independent experiments performed for each study. GraphPad Prism (version 7.0) and SPSS software (version 22.0) was used for statistical analysis. One-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post hoc test was used for multiple-group analyses, and unpaired and paired student’s t-tests were performed for analyses of two unrelated and related groups, respectively. The differences between experimental groups and control groups were considered to be statistically significant at p < 0.05; ∗p < 0.05, ∗∗p < 0.01, ∗∗∗p < 0.001.
