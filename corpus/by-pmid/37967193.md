# Salvage therapy expands highly cytotoxic and metabolically fit resilient CD8+ T cells via ME1 up-regulation.

## Abstract

Patients with advanced cancers who either do not experience initial response to or progress while on immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) receive salvage radiotherapy to reduce tumor burden and tumor-related symptoms. Occasionally, some patients experience substantial global tumor regression with a rebound of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. We have termed the rebound of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells in response to salvage therapy as T cell resilience and examined the underlying mechanisms of resilience. Resilient T cells are enriched for CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low mitochondrial membrane potential, accumulate less reactive oxygen species (ROS), and express more malic enzyme 1 (ME1). ME1 overexpression enhanced the cytotoxicity and expansion of effector CD8+ T cells partially via the type I interferon pathway. ME1 also increased mitochondrial respiration while maintaining the redox state balance. ME1 increased the cytotoxicity of peripheral lymphocytes from patients with advanced cancers. Thus, preserved resilient T cells in patients rebound after salvage therapy and ME1 enhances their resiliency.

Salvage therapy expands resilient CD8+ T cells that are highly cytotoxic and metabolically fit.

INTRODUCTION

Patients with advanced cancers that are refractory to current therapies are treated with salvage radiotherapy or chemotherapy with or without a combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). Occasionally, some of these patients experience substantial tumor regression following salvage therapy in combination with ICI therapy. It has been reported that a successful ICI therapy expands tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells with effector phenotype in peripheral blood, which have the potential to replace exhausted CD8+ T cells inside tumor tissue. We and others have identified tumoricidal CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells that are responsive to ICI therapy in both preclinical and clinical settings. CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells also increased in patients with metastatic diseases who do not respond to initial ICI therapy but respond to subsequent combination therapy (chemotherapy plus ICI). Since CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells are highly cytotoxic and effective in preclinical models and in the peripheral blood of patients who respond to ICI therapy, we proposed the term “T cell resilience” to explain such clinical observations, where effective cytotoxic T cells rebound in response to appropriate combination therapy and can reduce systemic tumor burden. Accordingly, a computational model of T cell resilience has been established in the prediction of patient response to ICI therapy. However, the underlying mechanisms of T cell resilience are yet to be defined.

To distinguish resilient CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells that are responsive to combined therapy from CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells that are responsive to conventional ICI therapy, we sought to identify a marker that may reflect the “resiliency” of T cells. Since the CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells are also identified as a transitory progeny of stem-like T cells, we wondered whether the resiliency of CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells in patients was due to a trait of stem-like T cells. Although transcription factor TCF-1 is a common marker of stem-like T cells, as an intracellular molecule it cannot be used to sort live human stem-like T cells for further functional study. We instead used TMRM, a dye for mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP) that has been used to identify stem-like T cells with low MMP. TMRM is not toxic to T cells and can be used for T cell staining and sorting live T cells according to their level of MMP. Recently, TMRM has been used to identify the functionality of mitochondria in response to ICI from CD8+ T cells in patients with advanced melanoma. We then used a combination of CX3CR1 and TMRM (MMP) to identify resilient CD8+ T cells, as they might have both effector phenotype and stem-like features.

Here, we found that CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP underwent a rapid cytotoxic response in patients with advanced cancers after salvage radiotherapy. From there, we found that CD8+ T cells with low MMP were highly cytotoxic and accumulated less ROS (reactive oxygen species) but expressed more malic enzyme 1 (ME1). Additionally, we found that ME1 overexpression enhanced cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells and expanded CX3CR1+ NKG7+ effector CD8+ T cells, partially through type I interferon (IFN) pathway. Although ME1 overexpression enhanced the mitochondrial respiration and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) production, ME1 balanced the redox state to avoid accumulation of extra ROS. We found that transducing ME1 mRNA increased the cytotoxicity of peripheral lymphocytes from patients with advanced cancers. Our study suggests that resilient CD8+ T cells may use ME1 to balance their energy needs and redox state to maintain their metabolic fitness and high cytotoxic capacity. Thus, the preserved tumor-reactive resilient T cells in patients rebound after salvage therapy and ME1 improves their metabolic fitness for an effective cancer immunotherapy.

RESULTS

CX3CR1 and low MMP identify resilient CD8+ T cells in patients with advanced cancers

To reduce tumor burden in patients with large tumors (5 to 10 cm or more in diameter), spatially fractionated radiotherapy (SFRT) has been used in treatment to intentionally deliver a heterogeneous dose distribution with alternating regions of very high and low doses of radiation throughout the tumor (Fig. 1A). In some patients, SFRT can produce a marked relief of severe symptoms, cause substantial tumor regression, and achieve above-average local disease control rates. While the biological mechanisms of the effectiveness of SFRT are still unknown, the heterogeneous dose distribution is hypothesized to be responsible for modifying tumor-reactive immune responses. Thus, immune monitoring of patients treated with SFRT allows us to identify any resilient CD8+ T cells in patients that may rebound after radiotherapy. To that end, we measured the degranulation (CD107a expression) of CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low or high MMP to determine their cytotoxic capacity (Fig. 1B and fig. S1A). We found that CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP, but not high MMP, showed improved cytotoxicity as early as 1 day after SFRT (Fig. 1C and fig. S1B). One week after SFRT, the cytotoxicity of CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells remained higher in low MMP cells than in high MMP cells (fig. S1C). In this cohort of patients, one patient achieved a complete response 5 months after SFRT therapy followed by fractionated radiation and ICI therapy (Fig. 1D). This patient not only had a rapid increase of CD107a+ cells among CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP (Fig. 1E) but also had a persistent presence of CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP for up to 2 weeks after initial radiotherapy (Fig. 1F).

Next, we sought to determine whether the presence of resilient CD8+ T cells would be able to predict clinical responses to radiotherapy in patients with advanced melanoma who did not respond to initial ICI therapy. First, we confirmed whether low MMP cells are enriched within CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells, which have been shown as a representative of active effector cells. We compared the frequency of low or high MMP populations among CX3CR1+ or CX3CR1− CD8+ T cells. We found that low MMP population was enriched in CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells, while CX3CR1− CD8+ T cells harbored comparable frequency of low or high MMP populations (fig. S1D). Accordingly, we found that patients with a higher frequency (>28%) of CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP at baseline demonstrated superior overall survival (OS) in response to radiotherapy (fig. S1E). Together, our clinical observations suggest that, in patients who are responsive to salvage radiotherapy, CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP are endowed with a functional resiliency.

Resilient CD8+ T cells are highly cytotoxic and less exhausted

To determine why CD8+ T cells featuring low MMP have a functional resiliency, we sorted human peripheral CD8+ T cells into low or high MMP using TMRM staining as reported, followed by a bulk RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) analysis (Fig. 2A). We found that CD8+ T cells with low MMP expressed more genes that encode for cytotoxic effector molecules such as GZMB, PRF1, and NKG7 compared to CD8+ T cells with high MMP (Fig. 2B), which were confirmed in reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) and flow cytometry analysis (Fig. 2, C and D, and fig. S2, A and B). In a T cell–mediated tumor cytotoxicity assay, we found that activated CD8+ T cells with low MMP demonstrated higher cytolytic activity compared to CD8+ T cells with high MMP toward two human tumor target cells (Fig. 2E), along with a significantly increased degranulation (CD107a expression) (Fig. 2F). The gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) using C7 immunological signature revealed that low MMP cells were enriched with genes up-regulated in effector/PD-1 low CD8+ T cells compared to exhausted/PD-1 high CD8+ T cells, while high MMP cells were enriched with genes that were down-regulated in effector CD8+ cells upon viral infection (Fig. 2G). Thus, the immunological gene signatures of low MMP cells are consistent with the high effector function of low MMP cells as we show here (Fig. 2, C to F).

On the other hand, a phenotype analysis based on CD45RA and CCR7 expression did not show a clear functional association of low or high MMP cells with naïve or memory phenotype. We found that the low MMP cells from both healthy donors and patients had more naïve-phenotype cells compared to high MMP cells (fig. S2, C and D). Using sorted naïve and memory CD8+ T cells, we found that memory T cells demonstrated more degranulation capacity compared to naïve cells (fig. S2E). However, both naïve and memory cells had comparable degranulation capacity in low MMP CX3CR1+ population (fig. S2F). Since memory CD8+ T cells had higher expression of effector molecules compared to the naïve cells (fig. S2G) and low MMP cells have fewer memory cells, the higher cytotoxic capacity of low MMP cells cannot simply be explained by their naïve and memory phenotype in our study.

To determine what transcription factors may be responsible for a high cytotoxic capability of CD8+ T cells with low MMP, we measured two key transcriptional factors, Eomesodermin (Eomes) and TBX21 (T-bet), which are involved in regulation of effector genes in CD8+ T cells, and the ratio of Eomes/T-bet can assess T cell exhaustion and immune pathology. Although the transcription of Eomes and tbx21 (T-bet) was higher in CD8+ T cells with low MMP compared to high MMP cells, the protein levels of Eomes and T-bet were comparable between them (Fig. 3, A and B), suggesting that a high effector capacity may be programmed at the transcription levels.

Although CD8+ T cells with low MMP had a higher cytotoxicity, they did not express more TOX, a causative factor of T cell exhaustion, and PD-1 compared to cells with high MMP (Fig. 3, C and D, and fig. S3A) but are enriched with TCF-1+PD-1+ stem-like cells (Fig. 3E). Accordingly, CD8+ T cells with low MMP were more responsive to anti–PD-L1 antibody compared to CD8+ T cells with high MMP in their cytotoxicity (Fig. 3F), in line with an up-regulation of anti–PD-1/L1 cancer immunotherapy pathway in RNA-seq analysis (fig. S3B). Collectively, our results suggest that resilient CD8+ T cells are programmed with cytotoxic capacity and a trait of stem-like cells that may allow them to avoid a trend to become exhausted.

Resilient CD8+ T cells have a balanced mitochondrial respiration and redox state

As the levels of MMP of CD8+ T cells reflect a metabolism state of T cells, we wondered whether the functional resiliency may be regulated via T cell metabolism. We compared the glycolysis [extracellular acidification rate (ECAR)] and mitochondria respiration [oxygen consumption rate (OCR)] between CD8+ T cells with low or high MMP. Effector CD8+ T cells rapidly use glycolysis as a quick source of energy to carry out their proliferation and effector functions, and rely on oxidative phosphorylation to maintain cytotoxic function. However, we found that CD8+ T cells with low MMP had lower glycolysis and glycolytic capacity than CD8+ T cells with high MMP (Fig. 4, A and B), while their mitochondria respiration (OCR) was comparable between them (Fig. 4, C and D), resulting in a higher OCR/ECAR ratio in CD8+ T cells with low MMP compared to high MMP cells (Fig. 4E). Since the mitochondrial-linked ATP, spare respiratory capacity (SRC), and GLUT1 (intake of glucose) were comparable between CD8+ T cells with low MMP and high MMP (Fig. 4, D, F, and G), our data suggest that the lower glycolysis of CD8+ T cells with low MMP is not due to a lower intake of glucose. Additionally, other metabolic pathways such as mTORC1 and fatty acid metabolism were differentially enriched in low and high MMP cells, indicating that the energy production and redox state were differentially regulated in these two cell populations (fig. S4, A and B).

To determine whether a redox state would explain the functional resiliency of effector T cells, we measured the levels of ROS, a by-product of mitochondrial respiration. We found that the cytosolic levels of ROS were lower in CD8+ T cells with low MMP compared to high MMP cells at both resting and activation stages (Fig. 5, A and B), and the lower levels of mitochondrial ROS were maintained up to 7 days during in vitro culture (Fig. 5C). We found a trend of lower mass and numbers of mitochondria in CD8+ T cells with low MMP, but this difference did not reach statistical significance (Fig. 5, D and E), suggesting that other mechanisms may contribute to the lower levels of ROS.

Since excessive ROS signals result in DNA damage and functional exhaustion in T cells, the ability to avoid an increase of ROS level in CD8+ T cells with low MMP would allow them to tolerate high levels of ROS in tumor environments or radiotherapy that might cause accumulation of ROS in radiated tissues. To that end, we found that a lower level of ROS was consistently maintained in CD8+ T cells with low MMP compared to cells with high MMP before and after SFRT in patients with lung cancer and sarcoma (Fig. 5F, n = 9). Similar findings were confirmed in patients with advanced prostate cancer receiving Stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) (Fig. 5G, n = 21). Although a trend of lower levels of ROS levels was observed in memory cells compared to naïve CD8+ T cells in both healthy donors and patients (fig. S5, A and B), the frequency of low MMP cells and high MMP cells is comparable between memory and naïve cells (fig. S5, C and D). Since low MMP cells had fewer memory cells (fig. S2, C and D), our data suggest that CD45RA-based naïve and memory phenotype cannot clearly distinguish a functional state of resilient T cells. As a proof of concept, a recent report indicates that CX3CR1, but not CD45RA, can define a functional state during T cell differentiation. Thus, the use of CX3CR1 and low MMP allows us to identify the functional and metabolic state of resilient T cells in patients with cancer.

ME1 is up-regulated in resilient CD8+ T cells with a balanced mitochondria respiration and redox state

To understand how resilient CD8+ T cells maintain and balance their energy needs and redox state, we performed RNA-seq in sorted CD8+ T cells with low or high MMP at both resting and activated state. We found ME1 among the most up-regulated genes in CD8+ T cells with low MMP compared to cells with high MMP after activation (Fig. 6A and fig. S6A). We confirmed ME1 up-regulation in low MMP cells with RT-PCR and Western blot (Fig. 6B and fig. S6B). ME1 is a cytosolic catalytic enzyme that converts malate into pyruvate with NADPH [reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP+)] as a by-product (Fig. 6C). ME1 plays a key role in tumor cell survival as it helps tumor cells to maintain a safe intracellular level of ROS to counteract a higher extracellular level of ROS in tumor microenvironments. However, the role of ME1 in regulation of T cell function has not been clearly defined.

To examine the role of ME1 overexpression in CD8+ T cells, we transduced ME1 mRNA into human primary CD8+ T cells and confirmed the increased expression of ME1 protein in T cells by Western blot (Fig. 6D and fig. S6C). We then measured the metabolic capacity of CD8+ T cells thereafter. We found that ME1 overexpression significantly increased the basal and maximal mitochondrial respiration as well as a higher ATP-linked respiration in CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6, E and F) along with an augmented SRC (fig. S6D). Accordingly, GSEA analysis identified an enrichment in the genes regulating oxidative phosphorylation pathway in ME1-overexpressing CD8+ T cells (fig. S6E). However, ME1 overexpression did not increase the cytosolic or mitochondrial levels of ROS among CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6, G and H). To further understand how ME1 regulates the energy production and redox state, we performed central carbon metabolism (CCM) analysis. We found that ME1 overexpression significantly reduced the d-fructose 1,6-bisphosphate and 2-phosphoglyceric acid, the intermediate metabolites in the glycolytic pathway, but increased ribose 5-phosphate (R5P), a key metabolite in the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), a main pathway that produces NADPH (Fig. 6I). Accordingly, ME1-overexpressing CD8+ T cells had higher NADPH concentration at 24 hours after activation compared to control cells but not after 48 hours (Fig. 6J and fig. S6F). However, Seahorse analysis did not identify a significant change in glycolysis of ME1-overexpressing CD8+ T cells (fig. S6, G and H). Collectively, our data suggest that ME1 overexpression selectively targets pentose phosphate metabolic pathway to balance excessive ROS from an increased mitochondrial respiration in CD8+ T cells.

ME1 increases the cytotoxic capacity of CD8+ T cells

Given that ME1 is up-regulated in CD8+ T cells with low MMP, which have high cytotoxic capacity, we wondered whether ME1 may affect the cytotoxic capacity of CD8+ T cells. To that end, we transduced ME1 mRNA into human primary CD8+ T cells isolated from healthy donors and measured their cytotoxicity. We found that ME1 overexpression significantly increased the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells in killing tumor cells in vitro (Fig. 7A). Accordingly, we found that ME1 increased the frequency of effector CX3CR1+ NKG7+ CD8+ T cells among CD8+ T cells upon T cell activation but decreased PD-1 levels (Fig. 7B and fig. S7A). To determine the strength of antigen in T cell cytotoxicity, we added SEB (a superantigen) in Raji target cells as they can bind to SEB. As expected, ME1 overexpression in CD8+ T cells increases their killing of Raji cells compared to the control mRNA. The addition of SEB increased the cytotoxicity of both T cells transfected with ME1 and control mRNA. However, SEB did not further increase the cytotoxicity of T cells transfected with ME1 mRNA compared to control mRNA (Fig. 7C). Our results suggest that ME1 overexpression can increase T cell cytotoxicity against natural antigens on target cells with no further increase in the killing of superantigen-coated target cells.

To address the mechanism of ME1 regulation of T cell cytotoxicity, we performed bulk RNA-seq analysis on CD8+ T cells overexpressing ME1 compared to control mRNA. We found that genes (PRF1, GZMB, GZMH, GZMA) that encode cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) function increased upon ME1 overexpression in CD8+ T cells (fig. S7B) along with an immunological signature of genes up-regulated in effector CX3CR1+ or memory CD8+ T cells after antigen stimulation (fig. S7C). Among the genes that were significantly up-regulated in ME1 mRNA–transfected CD8+ T cells, we found genes belonging to the type I IFN-stimulated gene (ISG) family (Fig. 7D). To determine whether the signals of type I IFN signaling pathway would affect ME1-enhanced cytotoxicity, we used Janus kinase 1 (JAK1) inhibitor during activation of T cells transfected with ME1 or control mRNA. We found that JAK1 inhibitor (upadacitinib) demonstrated a dose-dependent effect in inhibition of cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells transfected with ME1 mRNA but not in T cells transfected with control mRNA (Fig. 7E). Our data suggest that ME1 overexpression promotes T cell cytotoxicity partially through reprogramming the type I IFN pathway.

Given the potential of ME1 overexpression in promoting the cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, we wondered whether ME1 may have a therapeutic implication in improving CTL function of peripheral lymphocytes in patients with advanced cancers and did not respond to initial ICI therapy. To this end, we introduced ME1 mRNA into peripheral lymphocytes isolated from patients with advanced melanoma or prostate cancer. We found that ME1 overexpression increased the tumor-killing ability of peripheral lymphocytes in about 50% of patients with advanced cancers (Fig. 7F). To test the ME1 expression among CD8+ T cells that received ME1 mRNA transduction, we found that three of six patients had increased ME1 protein expression, while other three patients had reduced ME1 protein expression (Fig. 7G). Of note, three patients with reduced ME1 expression had higher basal levels of ME1 compared to the other three patients who had increased ME1 expression after ME1 mRNA transfection. Thus, the difference of ME1 expression may explain why some patients were responsive to ME1 transfection in the context of increased cytotoxicity.

To understand how ME1 expression is regulated, we measured the expression by flow cytometry and RT-PCR. Resting CD8+ T cells expressed a basal level of ME1 that can be increased by T cell receptor stimulation (fig. S7D). Since our bulk RNA-seq data of low MMP cells showed an up-regulated interleukin-18 receptor (IL-18R) and down-regulated IL-9R along with ME1 up-regulation, we tested the role of IL-18 and IL-9 in the expression of ME1 in CD8+ T cells. We found that IL-18, but not IL-9, increased ME1 expression at 24 hours of culture in resting CD8+ T cells as assessed with quantitative PCR (fig. S7E). To determine whether the basal levels of ME1 would affect the function of CD8+ T cells, we knocked down ME1 with small interfering RNA (siRNA) in resting CD8+ T cells followed by stimulation. ME1 siRNA did not significantly change the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells (fig. S7F). Additionally, ME1 siRNA did not change the level of the mitochondrial potential in either low or high MMP cells (fig. S7G). Collectively, our data suggest that the impact of ME1 in T cell function may be dependent on an up-regulation of ME1 rather than at its basal levels.

DISCUSSION

Here, we report the presence and rebound of resilient CD8+ T cells in patients with advanced cancers in response to salvage radiation therapy. The discovery of resilient T cells implies that an appropriate combination of salvage therapy with ICI can expand the resilient CD8+ T cells for a better clinical outcome. To that end, resilient T cells are a population of effector cells with high cytotoxic capacity and metabolic fitness, both of which are improved by ME1 up-regulation.

Currently, there are two major explanations of clinical responses to cancer immunotherapy: An increase of stem-like T cells explains a better clinical response, while an accumulation of exhausted T cells explains a poor clinical response. However, these explanations do not precisely explain why some patients with advanced cancers, who did not initially respond to ICI therapy, can respond to salvage therapy. Here, we propose T cell resilience to explain a functional rebound in T cell response in patients who respond to salvage radiation or chemotherapy in combination with ICI. On the basis of our study, the unique features of resilient CD8+ T cells include high cytotoxic capacity and metabolic fitness. To that end, we found that ME1 up-regulation can improve both cytotoxicity and metabolic fitness. ME1 is a NADP+-dependent cytosolic enzyme that is required for catalytic activity of a reaction that converts malate into pyruvate with NAPDH as a by-product. However, a clear role of ME1 in T cells has not been defined. We found that ME1 overexpression in CD8+ T cells increased their cytotoxicity partially through the up-regulation of genes and signals in type I IFN pathway. On the other hand, ME1 overexpression increased mitochondria respiration for energy production but balanced the redox state to avoid accumulation of extra ROS. In doing so, ME1 may reprogram effector cells to use PPP for NADPH production. NADPH can then act as a reducing agent to prevent accumulation of excessive ROS, which may trigger T cell death and T cell exhaustion. Of note, although ME1 has the potential to reduce cytosolic ROS through NADPH, our data suggest that ME1 is not a scavenger of cytosolic or mitochondrial ROS in T cells that needs a basal level of ROS for activation. Finally, ME1 may also have a regulatory role in transcription of IFN pathway in CD8+ T cells. It has been reported that the IFN signaling pathway is critical for expansion of effector CD8+ T cells. Accordingly, to evade immune attack, tumor cells have developed a strategy to disarm the IFN pathway using tumor-derived metabolites to suppress expression of ISGs. To this end, the up-regulation of ISGs by ME1 may be a mechanism for effector CD8+ T cells to curtail the down-regulation of ISGs caused by oncometabolites in tumor microenvironments. Collectively, our results suggest that an up-regulation of ME1 may program a resiliency phenotype for effector CD8+ T cells to have metabolic fitness and high cytotoxic capacity.

Although the concept of resilient T cells in response to cancer immunotherapy has been conceived recently, no markers have been defined to identify them in patients with advanced cancers. Our study suggest that CX3CR1+ CD8+ T cells with low MMP can be used as a surrogate marker of resilient T cells, based on several features we identified in this report, such as (i) their CTL function (degranulation/CD107a expression) can be recovered quickly after radiotherapy; (ii) they maintain a low profile of glycolysis but produce optimal levels of ATP via mitochondrial respiration; (iii) they are not prone to exhaustion due to lower expression of PD-1 and TOX; (iv) they share a feature of stem-like T cells (TCF-1+PD-1+) that allow them to be responsive again to ICI therapy; (v) they maintain a relative lower level of ROS to avoid exhaustion but do not compromise T cell activation; (vi) they up-regulate ME1 expression to balance energy needs and redox state. Among these unique features of resilient T cells, it is important to point out that ME1 expression is not only a T cell biomarker for T cell resiliency but also a potential therapeutic target in cancer immunotherapy. We propose that a transduction of ME1 into T cells is a previously unidentified option to improve the efficacy of T cell therapy.

Although our current studies are mainly focusing on the circulating CD8+ T cells in patients with advanced disease, it is warranted to investigate whether CD8+ T cells within tumor tissues would share the features of resilient T cells. On the other hand, one open question remains as why some CD8+ T cells have low or high MMP status and how it is maintained. It is not clear to what degree the heterogeneity of MMP within human circulating CD8+ T cells would be related with a systemic antitumor immunity, as resilient T cells need to maintain a depolarized/low MMP to avoid the consequences associated with high MMP such as increased ROS or exhaustion. By adapting to this unique feature, resilient T cells can protect the organism from metastatic cancer and chronic viral infections.

In summary, we reported a discovery of a presence and rebound of resilient cytotoxic T cells in patients inflicted with advanced cancers in response to combination therapy. The high cytotoxicity and metabolic fitness of resilient T cells is linked with a role of ME1 in the balance of energy production and redox state. Leveraging the knowledge of T cell resiliency may improve the efficacy of T cell–based immunotherapy to control metastatic malignancies.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Specimen collection and T cell isolation

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) as a source of peripheral lymphocytes were isolated from healthy donors or patients via centrifugation with Lymphoprep and SepMate conical tubes. From there, CD8+ T cells or CD8+ T cell subsets were then isolated using a magnet-based cell isolation kit and used immediately for experiments. Some experiments used PBMCs (patient samples) that were stored in liquid nitrogen. After thawing, PBMCs would be incubated in CTL medium [RPMI 1640 complete medium, recombinant human IL-2 (rhIL-2): 10 U/ml, rhIL-15: 5 ng/ml; rhIL-7: 5 ng/ml] at 37°C overnight for recovery before transfection.

 Patient information 

Peripheral blood for this study was collected after written consent was obtained from each participant. Clinical course, treatment information, and outcomes in patients treated with anti–PD-1/L1 therapy and radiotherapy at Mayo Clinic were retrospectively collected. Response to treatment was evaluated according to standard clinical practice guidelines using Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST). Note that the response listed for anti–PD-1/PD-L1 therapy (R, complete response; NR, progressive disease; SD, stable disease) was apparent (using RECIST) at the 12-week post-initiation of therapy time point for all patients. For prostate cancer (receiving SFRT and SBRT), the response was evaluated based on the prostate-specific antigen (PSA) levels and distance recurrence.

 Study approval 

Peripheral blood for this study from healthy people was acquired from anonymous donors who had consented for blood donation at the Blood Transfusion Center at Mayo Clinic. All donors and patients provided signed informed written consent; the study was approved by the Mayo Clinic Rochester Institutional Review Board and was conducted according to Declaration of Helsinki principles.

TMRM staining and cell sorting

CD8+ T cells were washed once with 1× phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and adjusted to a concentration of 1 × 106 cells/ml and stained with 0.02 μM final concentration of tetramethylrhodamine methyl ester (TMRM) or 2 nM final concentration of carbonyl cyanide 3-chlorophenylhydrazone (CCCP) as control. The TMRM-stained cells were incubated at 37°C for 30 min with intermittent shaking, while the CCCP-stained cells were incubated at 37°C for 5 min followed by addition of TMRM and incubation at 37°C for 30 min. The cells were then washed twice with 1× PBS and resuspended at 10 × 106 to 15 × 106 cells/ml of cell culture medium for sorting. The cells were sorted with BD FACSMelody Cell Sorter using the 100-μm sort nozzle and on the phycoerythrin (PE) channel (561-nm excitation).

siRNA transfection

Sorted CD8+ T cells with low or high MMP were centrifuged at 200g for 10 min before nucleofection (4D Nucleofector System, Lonza). Cells (1 × 106) were combined with 200 pmol of siRNA (siControl or siME1) in 20 μl of P3 nucleofection medium (Lonza) per well of the 16-well Nucleocuvette strips (X unit). Program FI-115 was used. Following nucleofection, cells were rested in warm RPMI [no fetal bovine serum (FBS), no cytokines] for 4 hours before adding 2× CTL medium (10% FBS and IL-2, IL-7, IL-15) for an overnight recovery followed by T cell activation and functional analysis.

mRNA transfection

T cells were transfected with control mRNA or ME1 mRNA (220 μg/ml), using the P3 nucleofection kit and program FI-115 on the 4D Nucleofector (Lonza). Following nucleofection, cells were rested in warm RPMI (no FBS, no cytokines) for 4 hours before adding 2× CTL medium (10% FBS and IL-2, IL-7, IL-15) for an overnight recovery followed by T cell activation and functional analysis.

Cytotoxicity assay

Target tumor cells were first washed twice with Hanks’ balanced salt solution (HBSS), then labeled with calcein-AM (5 μM) for 30 min, and incubated at 37°C in the dark, shaking occasionally. The cells were then washed with HBSS twice and resuspended at 1 × 105/ml in CTL medium with no FBS. Preactivated CD8+ T cells and target cells were mixed at 1:20 or 1:10 (target to effector ratio) in CTL medium without FBS and seeded into 96-well U-bottom plate at 200 μl per well. Saponin (0.1%) or Triton X-100 (2%) was added to wells that contained tumor cells only to provide a value for “maximum calcein release,” in each assay; tumor cell–only wells were included to measure spontaneous release of calcein. All experimental and control conditions were performed in triplicate wells. The plate was briefly centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 30 s, followed by incubation at 37°C for 4 hours. For the select killing assays, super antigen staphylococcus enterotoxin B (SEB) was added to the coculture at 100 ng/ml. Degranulation assay

T cells were adjusted at a concentration of 1 × 106 cells/100 μl of CTL medium including GolgiStop and GolgiPlug and incubated with 5 μl of CD107a antibody and 5 μl of anti-CD3/CD28 beads. The cells were then briefly centrifuged at 300g for 1 min and incubated at 37°C for 5 hours. ROS detection

T cells were stained with 250 nM CellROX or 1 μM MitoSOX in complete medium and incubated at 37°C for 45 min. From there, the cells were resuspended in fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer (1× PBS, 2 mM EDTA, and 3% FBS) at a concentration of 1 × 106 cells/ml followed by staining with antibodies for surface molecules for 20 min at room temperature in the dark. Cells were then washed once in FACS buffer, resuspended in 200 μl, and analyzed on Bio-Rad ZE5 Cell Analyzer. Analysis was performed using FlowJo V10.

Flow cytometry analysis

T cells were adjusted to 0.5 × 106 to 1 × 106 cells/ml with 1× PBS and stained with live/dead dye and incubated at 4°C for 30 min. The cells were then washed once with 1× PBS followed by staining for cell surface molecules. For intracellular molecule staining, cells were incubated with FoxP3 Fixation Buffer overnight at 4°C. After fixation, cells were washed once with 1× permeabilization buffer and then stained with antibodies for intracellular molecules such as ME1, TCF-1/7, TOX, Eomes, T-bet, Ki-67, HIF1-α, NKG7, and Glut1 for 1 hour at 4°C in 100 μl of 1× permeabilization buffer (see Table 1 for the full list of antibodies). Following intracellular staining, cells were washed twice with 1× permeabilization buffer and then resuspended in 200 μl of FACS buffer for flow cytometry analysis, which was completed on a Cytoflex LX (Beckman Coulter) (DAQ version V2.233, MCB version V3.01) running CytExpert software. Flow cytometric analysis was performed using FlowJo V10 (Table 2).

Metabolic assays

Seahorse Xfe96 Bioanalyser (Agilent) was used to determine OCR and ECAR. Sorted cells or total CD8+ T cells were washed in XF Base medium (Seahorse XF RPMI medium with 2 mM glutamine, 10 mM glucose, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, and 5 mM Hepes, pH 7.4 at 37°C) for OCR or XF medium with 2 mM glutamine for ECAR before being plated onto Seahorse cell culture plates coated with Cell-Tak (Corning #354240) at 1 × 105 cells per well. The cells were allowed to adhere to culture plate for 1 hour, and the assay medium was topped to 180 μl. The OCR was measured via Seahorse Mito Stress assay (Agilent), with addition of oligomycin (2 μM), carbonyl cyanide 4-(trifluoromethoxy) phenylhydrazone (FCCP; 1.2 μM), and rotenone and antimycin (1.0 μM). ECAR was measured with addition of 10 mM glucose, 2 μM oligomycin, and 50 nM 2-deoxy-d-glucose (2-DG). Assay parameters were as follows: 3 min mix, no wait, 3 min measurement, repeated three to four times at basal and after each addition. SRC was calculated as OCR at maximum rate (OCRMax) − OCR in basal state (OCRBas). Mitochondrial ATP production was calculated by subtracting the minimum respiration rate after oligomycin injection from the basal respiration rate before oligomycin injection.

Central carbon metabolites on liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry method (dMRM)

CD8+ T cells overexpressing ME1 or control mRNA were washed twice with PBS, pelleted in Eppendorf tube, and quickly frozen at −80°C. Central carbon metabolites (219 compounds) were monitored and measured on an Agilent 6460 triple quadrupole mass spectrometer coupled with a 1290 Infinity II quaternary pump. Acquisition was captured in negative electrospray ionization and dynamic multiple reaction monitoring (dMRM) after ion-pairing reversed-phase chromatographic separation. Analytes were searched and confirmed against a curated dMRM database with retention time. Relative abundances between samples set are derived via multivariate analysis on Agilent Mass Professional Profile software (MPP).

NADPH concentration measurement

CD8+ T cells were transfected with either control or ME1 mRNA and rested overnight in CTL medium followed by activation with anti-CD3/CD28 antibodies (STEMCELL Technologies) for 24 or 48 hours. After culture, the cells were quickly washed with cold PBS and counted. NADPH levels were measured with NADPH assay kits (Abnova, Walnut, CA).

Bulk RNA sequencing

RNA was isolated using the Qiagen RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (74134, Qiagen). The bulk RNA paired-end sequencing reads were processed through the Mayo bioinformatics pipelines MAP-Rseq (v3.0) as we previously reported. Reads were aligned to human reference genomes (hg38). The RNA aligned reads were quantified for gene expression using the Subread package. Differences across groups were assessed using bioinformatics package edgeR 2.6.2 to identify differentially expressed genes. Such genes were reported with magnitude of change (log2 scale) and their level of significance [false discovery rate (FDR) < 5%]. For pathway analysis, T cells with low or high MMP were randomized and put through GSEA as described in the user guide.

Quantitative PCR

RNA was isolated using the Qiagen RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (74134, Qiagen). RT reaction was completed using SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase (18080-400, Invitrogen). Quantitative RT-PCR (qRT-PCR) analysis was completed using Quant-Studio 3 Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) and TaqMan Fast Advanced Master Mix (444455, Applied Biosystems). TaqMan assays included Hs01120688-gl, Human ME1-FAM and Hs99999901-Sl, Human-18s-FAM, both from Applied Biosystems. All samples were run in doublet. Relative expression was calculated using the 2−∆Ct method. The following primers were used: granzyme B (forward: 5′-TACCATTGAGTTGTGCGTGGG-3′, reverse: 5′-GCCATTGTTTCGTCCATAGGAGA-3′), ME1 (forward: 5′-GGGAGACCTTGGCTGTAATGG-3′, reverse: 5′-TTCGGTTCCCACATCCAGAAT-3′), and UBE (forward: 5′-GTACTCTTGTCCATCTGTTCTCTG-3′, reverse: 5′-CCATTCCCGAGCTATTCTGTT-3′) (Table 3). RNA input was normalized to 20 ng/μl with 10-μl input for the RT reaction. After RT reaction, complementary DNA (cDNA) was diluted 1:5 before amplification on QuantStudio 3 in the following volume per well: 5 μl of cDNA template, 10 μl of SYBR Green (Applied Biosystems), 3 μl of H2O, and 1 μl of 10 mM forward/reverse primer.

Western blots

Cell pellets were lysed in NP-40 buffer, and concentrations were measured via protein assay using Bio-Rad reagent (#500-0006). Once diluted to equal concentrations, samples were combined with Laemmli sample buffer (Bio-Rad) and run using a Mini-PROTEAN Electrophoresis and Transfer system (Bio-Rad). Membranes were incubated with primary antibody overnight at 4°C [ME1, β-actin, or glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (GAPDH)]. Secondary antibodies were added the next morning for 2 hours (horseradish peroxidase–conjugated anti-mouse). Signal was visualized using the SuperSignal West Pico PLUS Chemiluminescence Substrate Kit (34577, Thermo Fisher Scientific) on a Syngene G: Box Chemi XX6 system running GeneSys software (V1.6.1.0). Full images of all membranes are included in the Supplementary Materials.

Transmission electron microscopy analysis of mitochondria

Cells were fixed in Trump fixative for 1 hour at room temperature or at 4°C overnight followed by a fixation for 1 hour in 1% osmium tetroxide. The samples were dehydrated, embedded in Spurrs resin, sectioned at 90 nm, and observed using a Joel 1400 electron microscope (Joel USA Inc.). For quantification, images of individual T cell in a single field of view were downloaded into JPEG images and the number of mitochondria structures within the T cells was counted manually by two different readers.

Mitochondria mass staining

Cells at a concentration of 1 × 106 cell/ml were washed once with 1× PBS and incubated with 100 nM MitoTracker Green FM dye for 30 min at 37°C. The cells were then washed and resuspended in FACS buffer and analyzed by flow cytometry.
