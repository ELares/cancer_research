# Biodegradable Mn-LDH-Ce6 potentiates cGas-STING pathway activation through sonodynamic-propelled pyroptosis for synergistic tumor immunotherapy.

## Abstract

While immunotherapy represents a breakthrough in cancer treatment by enabling specific tumor targeting with reduced toxicity, its success is often impeded by the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Conditions within the TME, particularly hypoxia and high glutathione (GSH) concentrations, actively suppress immune activation, leading to suboptimal therapeutic outcomes. To address these limitations, we developed a manganese-doped layered double hydroxide nanosystem loaded with chlorin e6 (Mn-LDH-Ce6). By catalyzing the decomposition of endogenous H2O2 into oxygen, the platform's catalase (CAT)-mimetic function enhances the sonodynamic therapy under ultrasound. This synergistically results in a pronounced increase in ROS and oxidative stress, while the subsequent irradiation further propagates ROS generation and directly initiates pyroptosis. However, the elevated glutathione levels in the TME paradoxically counteract this effect by scavenging oxidative species and exacerbating immunosuppression, ultimately compromising therapeutic outcomes. To disrupt this defense mechanism, Mn-LDH-Ce6 harnesses its glutathione oxidase (GSHOx)-like activity to deplete GSH, which in turn induces its own degradation within the TME and enables Ce6 release, collectively implementing a dual-action strategy that reinstates potent antitumor activity. Notably, released Mn2+ ions activate the cGAS-STING pathway, promoting STING phosphorylation and interferon-mediated immunity. Through the integrated sonodynamic amplification of oxidative stress, pyroptosis induction, and cGAS-STING pathway activation, this nanoplatform effectively reverses immunosuppression and curbs tumor growth, thereby establishing an innovative design concept and a theoretical framework for combined sonodynamic therapy and immunotherapy.

Introduction

As a revolutionary paradigm in oncology, tumor immunotherapy offers considerable advantages including high specificity, long-lasting efficacy, and reduced susceptibility to drug resistance. However, persistent limitations such as low immunogenicity, inadequate immune infiltration, and a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) must be addressed to improve clinical outcomes. Characterized by hypoxia and elevated glutathione (GSH) concentrations (1–10 mM), the TME significantly suppresses immunogenic cell death (ICD), thereby disrupting effective antigen presentation and ultimately promoting an immune-tolerant state. In recent years, nanozymes that mimic natural enzymatic functions and elicit antitumor immune responses have garnered significant research interest. By modulating intracellular biochemical reactions or introducing exogenous reactants, nanozymes make it possible to achieve precise manipulation of the TME for therapeutic purposes. Their favorable pharmacokinetic properties, including passive targeting, high stability, and broad catalytic adaptability, further underscore their potential in oncology. However, despite these catalytic advantages for TME modulation, nanozyme monotherapy often fails to sufficiently enhance tumor immunogenicity or initiate potent antitumor immunity. Consequently, it is imperative to develop integrated therapeutic strategies that not only provoke robust immune activation but also cooperatively alleviate the immunosuppressive TME, thereby potentiating the effectiveness of established immunotherapies.

Pyroptosis is a lytic programmed cell death characterized by gasdermin-mediated plasma membrane perforation, which precipitates subsequent cellular swelling and membrane disintegration, enabling the substantial release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) including high mobility group protein 1 (HMGB1), calreticulin (CRT), and adenosine triphosphate (ATP). Acting as potent inflammatory signals, these DAMPs enhance antigen presentation to promote dendritic cells (DCs) maturation and cross-presentation, which in turn orchestrates the coordinated activation of both innate and adaptive immune pathways. Previous attempts to leverage pyroptosis in oncology have been limited by the inability of conventional inducers to distinguish malignant from healthy tissues, coupled with a lack of regulatable activation kinetics. Developing intelligent systems that permit both accurate targeting and on-demand initiation of pyroptosis therefore represents an urgent priority for advancing this emerging therapeutic modality. Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) has emerged as a favorable non-invasive approach that utilizes ultrasound to activate sonosensitizers, resulting in localized ROS generation and tumor cell death. Its superior tissue penetration compared to light-based therapies makes it particularly suitable for deep-seated tumors. The precision and controllability of SDT minimize collateral damage, enhancing its clinical translatability. Importantly, SDT-induced pyroptosis can be harnessed to initiate robust immune activation, transforming a localized treatment into a systemic antitumor strategy that overcomes the constraints of conventional therapies. Hence, leveraging SDT-induced pyroptosis and the consequent immune activation emerges as a promising therapeutic strategy, which not only achieves localized tumor eradication but also initiates a systemic antitumor immune response, thereby offering a robust solution to overcome the limitations of conventional cancer therapies.

Over recent decades, two-dimensional (2D) nanomaterials, including graphene, metal–organic frameworks, and layered double hydroxides (LDHs), have attracted substantial interest for biomedical applications such as drug delivery, diagnostic imaging, biosensing, and energy transfer, owing to their distinctive physicochemical characteristics. Among them, LDHs have gained particular attention due to their high biocompatibility, facile surface modification, and structural versatility for hosting diverse functional molecules. These advantageous properties enable LDHs to serve as excellent platforms for delivering immunotherapeutic agents, such as antigens, adjuvants, and cytokines, making them particularly attractive for developing immunotherapy strategies. Manganese, an essential trace element in human physiology, serves as a vital cofactor in a range of metalloenzymes and nanozymes. Notably, Mn2+ has been identified as a potent activator of the cGAS-STING signaling pathway, a central regulator of innate immune responses. Mechanistically, this pathway senses cytosolic DNA via cGAS, triggering a signaling cascade through STING that culminates in the production of type I interferons (especially IFN-β) and proinflammatory cytokines. The activation of cGAS-STING not only bridges innate and adaptive immunity but also promotes DCs maturation and enhances the function of multiple immune cell types, thereby eliciting potent antitumor immunity. Given these immunostimulatory roles, the cGAS-STING pathway represents a highly promising target for advancing cancer immunotherapy.

Based on this rationale, we developed an innovative immunotherapy strategy based on a biodegradable manganese-layered double hydroxide (Mn-LDH) nanozyme platform that integrates sonodynamic-driven pyroptosis with cGAS-STING pathway activation. The Mn-LDH nanosystem exhibits dual enzyme-mimetic activities which include GSHOx-mimicking for depleting overexpressed GSH and CAT-mimicking for decomposing endogenous H2O2 into oxygen, thereby alleviating hypoxia and reversing the immunosuppressive TME. Furthermore, Ce6 was incorporated as a sonosensitizer to significantly enhance reactive ROS generation under US irradiation, effectively promoting tumor cell pyroptosis. These ROS also activate the NLRP3 inflammasome to specifically initiate the pyroptotic pathway, leading to the release of DAMPs that enhance tumor immunogenicity. Crucially, the Mn2+ ions in situ released during the GSH-consuming catalytic process and acid- and GSH-driven degradation of Mn-LDH potently activate the cGAS-STING pathway, thereby stimulating IFN-β secretion which in turn promotes DCs maturation and ultimately elicits a systemic antitumor immune response. By combining TME remodeling, dual-mode cell death induction, and metal ion-mediated immune activation, this strategy offers a potential and effective approach to overcoming current limitations in cancer immunotherapy.

Experimental section

Synthesis of Mn-LDH and Mn-LDH-Ce6

Mn-LDH was synthesized through a one-step co-precipitation approach, where in 10 mL of a mixed metal salt solution (0.4 M of MgCl2·6H2O and 0.2 M of MnCl2) was rapidly introduced into 20 mL of NaOH (0.3 M) under continuous stirring. The resulting suspension was subsequently heated to 100 ℃ and held at this temperature for 4 h. The final product was isolated via centrifugation. The as-prepared Mn-LDH was stirred with Ce6 overnight, yielding the final product Mn-LDH-Ce6.

GSHOx-like and CAT-like activity of Mn-LDH

Individual solution of GSH and the chromogenic agent 5, 5′-dithiobis (2-nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB, both at 1.5 mg·mL−1) were prepared, where upon reaction, DTNB yields the yellow-colored derivative 2-nitro-5-thiobenzoic acid (TNB). To evaluate the GSH depletion capability, Mn-LDH with varied concentrations (0, 50, 100, 150 and 200 μg·mL−1) was incubated with GSH for 2 h. Temporal dynamics were further assessed using a fixed Mn-LDH (200 μg·mL−1) over intervals ranging from 0 to 3 h. After the reaction, DTNB was added to each mixture, followed by 5 min of incubation prior to absorbance measurement with an ultraviolet spectrophotometer.

To evaluate the CAT-like activity, Mn-LDH with the same concentration as mentioned above was incubated with 0.15% H2O2. The dissolved oxygen concentration was continuously monitored using a portable dissolved oxygen meter (JJB-607A), with data recorded at 30 s intervals.

SDT effect of Mn-LDH-Ce6

The generation of singlet oxygen (1O2) was monitored by employing 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF) as a fluorescent probe. The mixture containing 2 mL of Mn-LDH-Ce6 (200 μg·mL−1) and 50 µL of DPBF (1.5 mg·mL−1) was subjected to US irradiation (1.5 W·cm−2, 1 MHz, 50% duty cycle) for varying durations (0, 4, 6, 8, 12, and 16 min). The degradation of DPBF was tracked by assessing its absorbance change via ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) spectra.

Intracellular ROS detection

4T1 cells were seeded in six-well plates and divided into four experimental groups: Control, Mn-LDH, Mn-LDH-Ce6, and Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US (at a concentration of 200 μg·mL−1). After adding Mn-LDH-Ce6, all groups were incubated for 6 h. Subsequently, the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group underwent US irradiation treatment, which involved five separate exposure points, each irradiated for 5 min with parameters at a power density of 1.5 W·cm−2, frequency of 1 MHz, and a 50% duty cycle. Following an additional 2-hour incubation, the cells were treated with 20 μM of 2′, 7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA) for 20 min. After three washes with PBS, the intracellular fluorescence was observed using an inverted fluorescence microscope.

Detection of intracellular GSDMD

Before initiating experimental procedures, 4T1 cells were seeded into 6-well culture plates at a density of 1 × 105 cells per well and allowed to adhere for 24 h under standard incubation conditions. Then the cells were then randomly divided into four identical groups above. All material-treated groups were incubated for 6 h at a consistent concentration (200 μg·mL−1 Mn-LDH or Mn-LDH-Ce6). Following incubation, the ultrasonic irradiation of Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group was the same as above with five predefined locations. Subsequently, the supernatant was aspirated and cells underwent fixation (immunostaining fixative, 10 min). After three washes with immunostaining buffer, the samples were blocked for 1h and further gently mixed with diluted rabbit monoclonal anti-GSDMD antibody during overnight incubation at 4 ℃. Following additional washes, the samples were shaken gently for one hour during incubation with a secondary antibody (Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG). Nuclear counterstaining employed Hoechst 33,342 (10 min), followed by the PBS wash and fixation with 4% paraformaldehyde. Final imaging was conducted using laser scanning confocal microscopy.

Mitochondrial membrane potential assay

After adhering for 24 h in six-well plates at a density of 1 × 105 cells per well, 4T1 cells were assigned to four identical experimental groups above. Following 10 h of incubation with the materials, the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group underwent US irradiation applied at five points with the power density of 1.5 W·cm−2, frequency of 1 MHz, duty cycle of 50%, and duration of 5 min. Alteration in mitochondrial membrane potential was measured following a 30-minute incubation period using the JC-1 fluorescent probe. Fluorescence microscopy was employed to detect J-aggregates (red channel, indicating intact potential) and J-monomers (green channel, indicating depolarization).

Intracellular CRT and HMGB1 detection

4T1 cells were seeded in six-well plates and incubated for 24 h before being divided into four groups: Control, Mn-LDH, Mn-LDH-Ce6, and Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US. Cells were treated with Mn-LDH or Mn-LDH-Ce6 (200 μg·mL−1) for 6 h. The Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group was subjected to ultrasound irradiation at five distinct sites with the power density of 1.5 W·cm−2, frequency of 1 MHz, duty cycle of 50%, and duration of 5 min. Following a 2 h incubation, the cells were fixed with immunostaining fixative (10 min), subjected to three sequential washes using immunostaining buffer (5 min per wash), and blocked for 1h. Subsequently, samples were probed overnight at 4 ℃ under agitation using rabbit monoclonal antibodies targeting CRT and HMGB1. After washing, Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG secondary antibody was applied for 1h under shaking conditions. After incubation, the samples were washed again, and DAPI was employed for nuclear staining. Fluorescence imaging was performed by the microscope.

In vitro DCs maturation analysis

4T1 cells were plated in six-well plates according to the established protocol and assigned to one of four experimental groups: Control, Mn-LDH, Mn-LDH-Ce6, and Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US. Each treatment group received Mn-LDH or Mn-LDH-Ce6 at a dose of 200 μg·mL−1. Following a 12-hour incubation period, the expression of CD11c, CD80, and CD86 in the surface of DCs was measured via flow cytometry.

In vivo antitumor immune studies

Single-cell suspensions were prepared from collected tissue samples by mechanical dissociation in PBS followed by filtration. To assess DCs maturation, the cells were labelled with APC-labelled CD80, PE-labelled CD86, and FITC-labelled CD11c followed by flow cytometry analysis. For characterization of tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) polarization, single-cell suspensions from tumor tissues were stained with APC-labelled CD206, PE-labelled CD86, and FITC-labelled F4/80, followed via flow cytometric assessment. To evaluate T cells activation, splenocytes were isolated by homogenization and erythrocyte lysis, then stained with APC-labelled CD8a, PE-labelled CD3, and FITC-labelled CD4 for flow cytometry. Supernatants from homogenized splenic tissues were retained for subsequent cytokine detection. Memory T cells were identified by staining splenocytes with APC-labelled CD8a, PE-labelled CD44, and FITC-labelled CD62L followed by flow cytometric analysis. To analyze regulatory T cells (Tregs), the cells were stained using a combination of fluorescent antibodies with APC-labelled CD25, PE-labelled FoxP3, FITC-labelled CD4. IFN-β levels were quantified with commercial ELISA kits.

Evaluation of antitumor efficacy in a murine bilateral 4T1 tumor Setup

To establish the model, 4T1 cells (1 × 106 cells per mouse) were initially inoculated into the right flank of Balb/c mice to initiate primary tumor formation. Secondary tumors were induced five days later through injection of an identical cell line (5 × 105 cells per mouse) into the left flank. Mice were subsequently allocated randomly into four experimental groups: Control, Mn-LDH, Mn-LDH-Ce6, and Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US. When the primary tumor volume reached approximately 100 mm3, each group received intravenous injections via the tail vein with 100 µL of PBS, Mn-LDH, or Mn-LDH-Ce6 (20 mg·kg−1) on days 0, 2, and 4. The Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group was additionally treated with US irradiation (1.5 W·cm−2, 1 MHz, 50% duty cycle, 5 min) after each administration. Tumor dimensions and animal body weight were measured at two-day intervals. Tumor volume was calculated based on the formula (length × width2) / 2. Following a 14-day treatment period, selected mice were euthanized in accordance with ethical guidelines, and neoplastic tissues were harvested for subsequent histological examination through hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining.

Statistical Methods

All experiments were conducted with a minimum of three independent replicates, and data are presented as mean ± standard deviation. Group comparisons were performed using a two-tailed unpaired Student's t-test, with statistical significance denoted as *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, and ***p < 0.001.

Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization of the Mn-LDH-Ce6

The detailed synthesis procedures for Mn-LDH-Ce6 were illustrated in Scheme 1A. As shown in Fig. 1A, the characteristic diffraction peaks of Mn-LDH closely aligned with the standard reference JCPDS No. 51-1526, confirming the successful preparation of nanoparticles. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis demonstrated the layered structure of the synthesized Mn-LDH, displaying homogeneous morphology with a mean diameter of about 50 nm (Fig. 1B). Subsequently, the chemical states of the elements in Mn-LDH were examined by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). With signals corresponding to Mn, Mg, O, and C, the wide-scan XPS spectra provided clear evidence for the effective synthesis of the Mn-LDH nanosheets (Fig. 1C). Furthermore, the manganese content in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite was determined to be 4.13% (w/w) through inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). Given the crucial role of oxidation states in enzyme-like catalytic processes, the valence state of Mn in Mn-LDH was further investigated. As shown in Fig. 1D, the high-resolution Mn 2p spectrum was fitted with two peaks located at 642.85 eV and 641.50 eV, corresponding to Mn3+ and Mn2+, respectively. In addition, Fig. S1 presented the narrow-scan XPS spectra of Mg, O, and C elements within the Mn-LDH material. UV–Vis absorption spectroscopy demonstrated that the absorbance of Mn-LDH nanomaterials increased significantly with rising concentration, suggesting good dispersibility and optical stability in solution (Fig. 1E). Subsequently, to further enhance the therapeutic utility, the sonosensitizer Ce6 was integrated into the system, resulting in a multifunctional nanocomposite capable of synergistically combining nanozyme-mediated catalysis with US-triggered sonodynamic therapy. Mn-LDH-Ce6 was prepared by mixing Mn-LDH and Ce6 at a mass ratio of 5:1 under overnight stirring. The successful loading of Ce6 was confirmed by monitoring its characteristic absorption peak at 405 nm (Fig. S4A). Quantitative analysis further revealed that the drug loading efficiency and drug loading content reached 16.33% and 97.99%, respectively (Fig. S4B, C and Table S1), indicating the high loading capacity of Mn-LDH for Ce6. Measurements by dynamic light scattering (DLS) yielded a hydrodynamic size of 78.80 nm for the Mn-LDH, which increased to 122 nm after Ce6 loading (Fig. 1F). The long-term stability of the Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite was evaluated in deionized water and PBS buffer. The nanoparticles were dispersed in each medium at a concentration of 200 μg mL−1 and stored at room temperature. The hydrodynamic diameter and particle size distribution were monitored using DLS at predetermined time intervals (0, 12, 24, 36, 48, and 72 h, Fig. S2). Simultaneously, photographic images of the dispersions were recorded at 0, 12, 24, 48, and 72 h to assess macroscopic stability (Fig. S3). The results confirm the excellent long-term stability of the Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite in both H2O and PBS environments, which is crucial for its potential biomedical applications. The observed increase in hydrodynamic diameter corresponds to the successful incorporation of Ce6 molecules onto the Mn-LDH surface, forming an extended hydration layer that further confirms effective sonosensitizer loading. Furthermore, hemocompatibility assessment revealed excellent blood compatibility, with hemolysis assays demonstrating negligible red blood cell lysis by Mn-LDH-Ce6 at concentrations up to 600 μg·mL−1, supporting its potential for intravenous administration (Fig. S5). Subsequently, an investigation was conducted using TEM to evaluate the degradation behavior of Mn-LDH under TME conditions mimicking the acidic and high-GSH. The results showed that Mn-LDH degraded completely within 8h at pH 5.5 (Fig. 1G). Moreover, following the introduction of 10 mM of GSH, the layered structure of the material collapsed rapidly, with complete decomposition occurring within 4 h (Fig. 1H). To verify the drug release behavior of our nanoplatform in the tumor microenvironment, we evaluated the in vitro release kinetics of Ce6 from Mn-LDH-Ce6 under a high GSH concentration (10 mM). As shown in Fig. S6, a rapid and sustained release of Ce6 was observed, with a cumulative release rate reaching approximately 77% within 3 h. This pronounced release profile can be attributed to the GSH-triggered degradation of the Mn-LDH nanosheets, which disrupts the structure and consequently releases the encapsulated Ce6 sonosensitizer. These results collectively confirm the successful preparation and good stability of Mn-LDH-Ce6, while its responsive degradation under tumor-mimicking conditions supports its potential for targeted therapeutic applications, thereby laying a solid foundation for further cellular-level studies.

The sonodynamic and nanozyme activities of Mn-LDH-Ce6

In response to the inherently strong antioxidant defense mechanisms of tumor cells, a Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite system with dual nanozyme activities including GSHOx-like and CAT-like functionalities was rationally designed. Following the schematic illustration of the system's design and core mechanism (Fig. 2A), the nanozyme activities of Mn-LDH-Ce6 were systematically evaluated, starting with its GSHOx-like activity using the thiol-specific indicator DTNB. TNB is produced through the reaction between DTNB and reduced glutathione GSH, which under neutral or alkaline conditions is converted to the yellow TNB2- anion with a characteristic absorption peak at 412 nm. Thereafter, the dynamic consumption of GSH by different concentrations of Mn-LDH-Ce6 was measured (Fig. 2B). With increasing concentration of Mn-LDH-Ce6, the absorbance of TNB2- at 412 nm exhibited a marked decline before eventually reaching a plateau, indicating effective catalysis of GSH oxidation in a concentration-dependent manner. Further time-dependent experiments at a fixed Mn-LDH-Ce6 concentration (200 μg·mL−1) showed a continuous decrease in the characteristic absorption of TNB2- over time, confirming efficient and sustained enzyme-mimicking activity (Fig. 2C). Subsequently, the CAT-like activity of Mn-LDH-Ce6 was investigated by monitoring real-time oxygen generation in the solution containing 0.15% H2O2 using a dissolved oxygen meter. A significant increase in oxygen production was observed with higher concentrations of Mn-LDH-Ce6, demonstrating a clear concentration-dependent behavior (Fig. 2D). These results confirm that Mn-LDH-Ce6 effectively catalyzes H2O2 decomposition to produce oxygen, exhibiting excellent CAT-mimicking activity, which provides an experimental basis for alleviating tumor hypoxia and reversing the immunosuppressive TME. To evaluate the ability of the system to generate 1O2 under US irradiation, DPBF was used as a 1O2-specific probe, and its characteristic absorption at 432 nm was monitored quantitatively. After adding DPBF to the solution of Mn-LDH-Ce6 (200 μg·mL−1) and subjecting it to US with the power density of 1.5 W·cm−2, frequency of 1 MHz, duty cycle of 50%, and duration of 5 min, the absorbance was measured at different time points. The absorbance of DPBF at 432 nm dropped markedly within 16 min (Fig. 2E, F), indicating rapid 1O2 generation and effective oxidation of DPBF, fully confirming the high sonodynamic efficacy of Mn-LDH-Ce6 under US conditions. Collectively, these results establish that the Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanosystem exhibits potent dual nanozyme activities and significant sonodynamic effects in vitro, thereby providing a robust experimental foundation for subsequent investigations into its capacity to induce oxidative stress and ICD in cellular environments.

Sonodynamic-induced pyroptosis and cGAS-STING pathway activation by Mn-LDH-Ce6 for antitumor therapy in vitro

Prior to systematically evaluating the cytotoxicity of the Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanomaterial, we first investigated the cellular endocytosis behavior of the nanocomposite by an endocytosis assay. As shown in Fig. S7, the Rhodamine B (RhB)-labelled Mn-LDH-Ce6 gradually increased with prolonged incubation time, indicating effective internalization of the nanomaterial by the cells. To further quantitatively assess its cytotoxicity, cell viability was examined using the MTT assay. Compared to the Mn-LDH group, Mn-LDH-Ce6 under US irradiation demonstrated a rise in toxicity corresponding to higher concentrations (Fig. 2G). At a dosage of 200 μg·mL−1, the cell viability in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group decreased to 27.10%. Notably, under the same therapeutic regimens, human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) maintained over 80% viability (Fig. 2H), demonstrating the selective cytotoxicity and biosafety of the therapeutic system. Subsequently, 4T1 cells exposed to Mn-LDH-Ce6 for 6 h exhibited typical pyroptotic morphological features under microscopic observation, including cellular swelling, increased volume, and the formation of numerous bubble-like protrusions on the plasma membrane (Fig. 3A). US irradiation significantly enhanced the formation of these membrane vesicles in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 group, suggesting that the sonodynamic effect promotes pyroptosis. Further examination of ultrastructural changes using biological transmission electron microscopy (bio-TEM) revealed severe membrane rupture and organelle damage in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group, while the control cells remained intact, consistent with the optical observations (Fig. 3B). In sharp contrast, normal cells treated with Mn-LDH-Ce6 alone exhibited intact cell membranes and normal morphology, indistinguishable from the untreated control cells (Fig. S8). This clearly indicates the high biocompatibility and minimal toxicity of the nanomaterial itself toward normal cells. Due to redox enzyme-mimicking properties of Mn-LDH-Ce6, intracellular ROS levels were quantified using the DCFH-DA probe. This probe is oxidized by various ROS species, including •OH and H2O2, to yield the highly fluorescent DCF. Consistent with the nanozyme activity, significantly enhanced green fluorescence was observed in Mn-LDH-Ce6-treated cells relative to the Control and Mn-LDH-only groups. This fluorescent signal demonstrated further intensification after ultrasound irradiation, confirming that the sonodynamic activation of Ce6, synergistically augmented by Mn-LDH-mediated modulation of the tumor microenvironment, induces a substantial increase in intracellular ROS production (Fig. 3C). Furthermore, Mn-LDH-Ce6 was shown to significantly deplete reduced GSH levels in 4T1 cells owing to its intrinsic GSHOx-like enzymatic activity (Fig. S9). These collective findings strongly indicate that oxidative stress serves as a pivotal mechanistic driver in the induction of pyroptosis.

The molecular mechanisms of pyroptosis were further investigated through a multi-faceted experimental approach, including immunofluorescence, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) release assay, and Western Blot (WB). Immunofluorescence staining showed a significant increase in GSDMD-N signal in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group (Fig. 3D), along with the highest LDH release (Fig. 3E), indicating severe loss of membrane integrity. WB results further confirmed the marked upregulation of cleaved Caspase-1 and GSDMD-N (Fig. 3F), demonstrating activation of the Caspase-1/GSDMD pathway consistent with canonical pyroptosis. Moreover, the previous investigations have demonstrated that Mn2+ is a known agonist of the cGAS-STING pathway. Accordingly, WB analysis revealed a significant upregulation of key proteins in the cGAS-STING signaling pathway in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group (Fig. 3F). Specifically, the expression of the innate immune sensor STING was markedly increased. More importantly, the phosphorylation levels of its downstream effectors, p-TBK1 and p-IRF3, were substantially enhanced, indicating effective activation of the entire signaling cascade. This result provides direct mechanistic support for the systemic antitumor immune effects mediated by Mn-LDH-Ce6. Under US activation, the internalized Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite significantly generates ROS, which directly induces Caspase-1/GSDMD-mediated pyroptosis in tumor cells, manifested by membrane perforation, LDH release, and leakage of inflammatory factors. Concurrently, sustained release of Mn2+ effectively leads to the activation of the cGAS-STING pathway and the subsequent promotion of TBK1 phosphorylation and IRF3 nuclear translocation, ultimately driving the expression of IFN-β and initiating a systemic antitumor immune response (Fig. 3G). The combined action of ROS overproduction and STING pathway stimulation contributes to direct tumor cell eradication while simultaneously reprogramming the immunosuppressive tumor milieu, thereby improving antigen presentation and promoting T cells infiltration.

To further elucidate the molecular mechanisms of cell death induced by the combined Mn-LDH-Ce6 and US treatment, we first performed an Annexin V-FITC/PI apoptosis assay combined with fluorescence microscopy observation (Fig. 4A). Flow cytometry analysis revealed that the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group demonstrated a significantly higher proportion of dual-stained cells (Annexin V+/PI+), indicative of late apoptosis and/or necrosis, compared to all other groups. This result confirms that the combined treatment most effectively induces tumor cell apoptosis and culminates in the most severe overall cell death. Calcein-AM/PI live-dead staining results (Fig. S10) showed that, in contrast to the Control group, green fluorescence (live cells) was nearly absent in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US treatment group, while red fluorescence (dead cells) was widespread throughout the field of view, further confirming the highest cell mortality in this group. JC-1 mitochondrial membrane potential assay results (Fig. 4B) demonstrated that normal mitochondria, due to their high membrane potential, facilitate the formation of red JC-1 aggregates; however, in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group, green fluorescence (monomers) was significantly enhanced, and red fluorescence was markedly reduced, indicating a severe loss of mitochondrial membrane potential accompanied by the greatest degree of functional impairment. Collectively, these results demonstrate that US-activated Mn-LDH-Ce6 significantly enhances tumor cell death by inducing mitochondrial dysfunction and promoting apoptosis/necrosis, further corroborating the antitumor activity of this nanoplatform.

Evaluating cellular immune responses mediated by Mn-LDH-Ce6

Inspired by the potent tumor-killing efficacy of Mn-LDH-Ce6 in vitro, we further investigated whether the pyroptosis could trigger ICD, thereby activating antitumor immune responses. Examination of key DAMPs indicated markedly enhanced ICD hallmarks in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group, including substantial HMGB1 release, increased CRT exposure, and elevated extracellular ATP secretion compared to other groups (Fig. 4C and D, and Fig. S11). These established biomarkers collectively confirm the effective induction of ICD by US-activated Mn-LDH-Ce6. Building upon the confirmed DAMP release, the functional impact of these molecules on DCs maturation was next evaluated by incubating DCs with supernatants from differentially treated 4T1 cells and assessing the surface marker expression via flow cytometry. Notably, the maturation rate of DCs induced by the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US treatment reached 29.30%, which was superior to that in the Control group (6.78%, Fig. 4E). This result confirms that the combination of Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US effectively elicits innate immune responses through the induction of ICD, thereby providing a solid foundation for subsequent in vivo investigations.

Capitalizing on the favorable performance of Mn-LDH-Ce6 in reversing the immunosuppressive TME, we further evaluated its immune-activating effects in the mice implanted with 4T1 tumors. Results showed that under US irradiation, the proportion of M2 macrophages significantly decreased from 53.60% in the Control group to 27.20%, while the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group exhibited an increase in M1 macrophages to 43.80%, indicating effective remodeling of the immunosuppressive TME (Fig. 5A and B). As specialized antigen-presenting cells, DCs are pivotal for naïve T cells activation. According to Fig. 5C and D, the DCs maturation rate within tumor-draining lymph nodes rose markedly to 25.70% after Mn-LDH-Ce6 treatment, relative to 10.00% in the Control group, and reached 34.2% with additional ultrasound stimulation (1 MHz, 1.5 W·cm−2, 50% duty cycle, 5 min). Correspondingly, T cells activation assays revealed that the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group exhibited the most substantial infiltration of both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5E and F), indicating effective activation of local immune responses. To evaluate the establishment of immunological memory, the splenic distribution of effector memory T cells (Tem) and central memory T cells (Tcm) was examined. The Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group demonstrated a marked rise in both Tem and Tcm populations, suggesting the induction of durable immune memory (Fig. 5G). Meanwhile, a reduction was observed in the percentage of regulatory T cells (Tregs) from 10.20% in the Control group to 3.28% in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group, demonstrating effective suppression of Tregs by US-activated Mn-LDH-Ce6 (Fig. S12). Additionally, splenocyte supernatant analysis revealed a significant increase in IFN-β secretion after Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US treatment (Fig. S13). Collectively, these data substantiate that US-activated Mn-LDH-Ce6 initiates a positive immunoregulatory cascade, which effectively reverses the immunosuppressive TME, promotes the maturation of DCs and activation of T cells, while concurrently suppressing Tregs.

Antitumor efficacy of Mn-LDH-Ce6 in vivo

Building upon the promising immune activation results, subsequent experiments evaluated the in vivo antitumor efficacy of Mn-LDH-Ce6 under US activation. Initially, RhB-labelled Mn-LDH-Ce6 was delivered intravenously through the tail vein, and in vivo imaging analysis revealed significant accumulation of the nanodrug delivery system within the tumor tissue. The strongest fluorescence intensity was observed at the tumor location 12 h after administration, highlighting the time-dependent accumulation (Fig. S14). Based on this pharmacokinetic profile, this time point was selected as the optimal intervention time for subsequent treatments. To evaluate the therapeutic efficacy, a bilateral 4T1 breast cancer model was established in Balb/c mice (Fig. 6A). When the tumor size approached about 100 mm3, the mice were randomly assigned to one of the following four treatment groups (Control, Mn-LDH, Mn-LDH-Ce6, and Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US). Each group received treatments every other day via tail vein injection for a total of three cycles. The Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group underwent US irradiation with the power density of 1.5 W·cm−2, frequency of 1 MHz, duty cycle of 50%, duration of 5 min at 12 h after each injection. The results demonstrated that the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group exhibited the strongest tumor suppression. Tumor volume in both primary and distant sites grew rapidly in the Control group, with average final values of 1041 mm3 and 511.50 mm3, which were 13.13-fold and 8.67-fold larger, respectively, than those in the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group (Fig. 6B and C). Although other treatment groups also showed certain levels of tumor growth inhibition, their effects were significantly weaker than that of the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group. Ex vivo tumor images and growth curves further confirmed the absence of tumor suppression in the Control group, while all treatment groups exhibited differed in their antitumor efficacy, with the Mn-LDH-Ce6 + US group showing the most pronounced effect (Fig. 6D-G). During the course of therapy, all groups maintained stable body weights without notable changes, suggesting good tolerability of the treatments (Fig. 6H). H&E staining of tumor tissues revealed significant apoptotic signals in all treatment groups. No significant pathological alterations were detected in critical visceral organs, including the heart, liver, spleen, lungs, and kidneys (Fig. 6I and Fig. S15). Additionally, blood biochemical indices and complete blood count results remained within normal ranges (Table S2), indicating favorable biosafety of Mn-LDH-Ce6.

Conclusion

In summary, a multifunctional Mn-LDH-Ce6 nanocomposite integrating enzyme-mimetic catalytic activity and sonodynamic capabilities was successfully constructed for synergistic remodeling of the tumor microenvironment (TME). This system initiates a catalytic-immunological cascade within the TME, wherein its dual nanozyme activities orchestrate a metabolic shift through glutathione depletion and oxygen generation. This process amplifies the ultrasound-triggered reactive oxygen species (ROS) storm, which not only directly induces pyroptosis but also potently activates the cGAS–STING pathway. Thereby, the localized sonodynamic response is effectively converted into a systemic antitumor immune attack. This therapeutic transformation was demonstrated in a bilateral 4T1 tumor model, where the treatment led to concurrent suppression of both primary and distant tumors. Our work establishes a new paradigm for spatiotemporally controlled cancer immunotherapy achieved through catalytic reprogramming of the TME.
