# Nanotechnology-driven Epigenetic Cancer Therapy: Precision Delivery and Sustained Release of DNA Methylation Modulators.

## Abstract

Aberrant DNA methylation contributes to cancer by silencing tumor suppressor genes and facilitating tumor growth. DNA methyltransferase inhibitors (DNMTis), including 5-azacytidine (5-AZA) and decitabine (DAC), can potentially mitigate these consequences and revive gene expression. Their therapeutic effectiveness is hindered by factors like poor bioavailability, swift degradation, and unintended toxicity. Advances in nanotechnology enable targeted DNMTi delivery, enhancing efficacy while minimizing toxicity. This mini-review explores several key types of nanoformulations for DNMTi delivery, including PLGA nanoparticles, liposomes, solid lipid nanoparticles (SLNs), bentonite-based nanoparticles, gold nanoparticles (AuNPs), amphiphilic prodrugs, PEGylated nanoparticles, bionic nanoparticles, gelatinase-sensitive nanoparticles, and chitosan-based pH-responsive systems. This paper emphasizes the integration of epigenetic therapies and nanotechnology in oncology, focusing on the creation of nanocarriers for precise delivery of methylation modulators and new formulations for prolonged drug release. By addressing these advancements, the review underscores the potential of nanotechnology to revolutionize epigenetic cancer therapy, offering new hope for improved patient outcomes in precision oncology.

Introduction

Epigenetics is a relatively new emerging science that defines somatic heritable changes in gene expression that are not associated with DNA sequence modifications. It includes DNA and RNA methylation, histone changes epigenetics, noncoding RNA control, and nucleosome placement. Epigenetic modifications are heritable, reversible changes in gene activity that occur without altering the DNA sequence. Among these, DNA methylation – the covalent addition of a methyl group to the 5-carbon of cytosine (usually in CpG dinucleotides) – is a major regulator of gene expression. DNA methyltransferase inhibitors (DNMTis) have emerged as a promising therapeutic strategy targeting epigenetic regulation in cancer and other diseases. DNMTs are responsible for DNA methylation, a process that can silence tumor suppressor genes (Figure 1A) and promote cancer progression through hypermethylation. In cancer, aberrant hypermethylation of tumor suppressor gene promoters (Figure 1A) is a frequent mechanism of gene silencing, contributing to oncogenesis. This epigenetic inactivation of cell cycle regulators, DNA repair genes, and other tumor suppressors drives tumor initiation and progression, making the DNA methylation machinery a promising therapeutic target.

DNMTis like 5-azacytidine (5-AZA) and decitabine (DAC) aim to reverse abnormal DNA methylation and reactivate silenced genes. These nucleoside analogs are incorporated into DNA during replication, trapping DNMT enzymes in covalent adducts, causing DNMT degradation and passive DNA demethylation in daughter cells. DNMTis restore silenced tumor suppressor gene expression and modify aberrant epigenetic programs. Clinical success of 5-AZA and DAC in myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) validates epigenetic therapy, highlighting the potential to reprogram cancer cells. Unlike permanent genetic mutations, epigenetic changes are reversible, making DNMT enzymes appealing drug targets.

Both AZA and DAC are FDA approved for treating hematological malignancies like AML, chronic myelomonocytic leukemia, and MDS. However, their efficacy is limited by poor bioavailability, rapid degradation, and short half-life. In solid tumors, DNMTis have shown limited success due to intratumoral heterogeneity and redundant epigenetic suppressive mechanisms, requiring combination therapies with histone deacetylase inhibitors. These drugs are administered subcutaneously or intravenously due to rapid inactivation by cytidine deaminase (CDA) in the gut and liver, contributing to poor oral bioavailability and rapid clearance during first-pass metabolism. Their short half-life is further affected by genetic factors, such as increased CDA expression, particularly in males, potentially leading to worse clinical outcomes.

Nanoparticle-based delivery systems address these limitations by enhancing cellular uptake, enabling targeted and sustained drug release, reducing systemic toxicity, and improving therapeutic outcomes. This mini-review examines nanoparticle formulations for DNMTis, focusing on their design, mechanisms, and efficacy in blood cancers and solid tumors. These systems promise to advance hypomethylating agent therapies in oncology by optimizing pharmacokinetics and reducing off-target effects.

Mechanisms of DNA Methyltransferase Inhibition

DNMTis function by targeting the DNA methylation process at the molecular level. The nucleoside analog DNMTis 5-AZA and DAC mimic cytidine and become incorporated into DNA (and RNA, in the case of 5-AZA) during S-phase. When a DNMT enzyme attempts to methylate a cytosine analog incorporated into DNA, it becomes irreversibly trapped in a covalent complex. This mechanism — often termed “suicide inhibition” — leads to depletion of active DNMT enzymes via proteasomal degradation. After DNA replication, methylation marks are not rewritten on daughter strands, leading to passive hypomethylation and reactivation of silenced genes. Higher doses create DNA-DNMT adducts, triggering DNA damage responses and apoptosis, enhancing the cytotoxic effect of these drugs. Thus, DNMTis have a dual action: demethylating DNA and directly inducing DNA damage, suppressing tumor cell growth. Notably, 5-AZA also incorporates into RNA, causing ribosome disassembly and disrupting protein synthesis, contributing to its antineoplastic activity and toxicity in normal cells .

5-AZA and DAC are considered first-generation DNMTis and remain the most widely used “hypomethylating agents” in oncology. They target three major DNA methyltransferases (DNMT1, DNMT3A, DNMT3B) and induce hypomethylation. However, their instability and lack of specificity have led to next-generation DNMTis like guadecitabine, a dinucleotide prodrug of decitabine designed to resist cytidine deaminase degradation. Guadecitabine’s modification increases stability and half-life, allowing prolonged drug exposure and DNMT inhibition. Although phase III trials in relapsed AML and MDS did not show guadecitabine’s superiority over conventional treatment, its development exemplifies enhanced DNMT inhibitor pharmacokinetics. Other second-generation nucleoside analogs (eg, zebularine, 5-fluoro-2’-deoxycytidine) carry structural tweaks to improve cellular uptake or reduce toxicity, allowing sustained hypomethylating activity. In addition, non-nucleoside DNMTis are emerging. For instance, GSK-J1 (example code: GSK3685032) is a first-in-class reversible DNMT1-selective inhibitor that does not get incorporated into DNA. Instead, it competes with the enzyme’s binding to hemimethylated DNA, producing rapid DNA demethylation and robust re-expression of genes, with improved tolerability compared to decitabine in preclinical models. Such next-generation agents aim to overcome the off-target effects of 5-AZA analogs, providing more targeted epigenetic modulation.

DNMTis are clinically approved for MDS and AML, where they can induce remissions or disease stabilization. Their success in hematologic malignancies validates the concept of epigenetic therapy. DNMTis show variable results in solid tumors due to pharmacodynamic challenges, such as inadequate drug levels and intrinsic resistance. Tumor cells may resist treatment by upregulating drug clearance enzymes, altering nucleoside transporters, or acquiring mutations that counteract hypomethylation effects. Furthermore, DNMTis lack selectivity for cancer cells, affecting normal cells and causing limiting side effects. Off-target gene activation and unfavorable PK/PD profiles also concern first-generation DNMTis due to their low selectivity and toxicity. These issues have driven the search for better DNMTis and combination strategies. However, DNMTis’ ability to epigenetically reprogram tumors remains a unique treatment option, with efforts focused on optimizing their delivery and use.

Nanoformulations for Effective Delivery of DNMTis

Nanotechnology has revolutionized the field of medicine by enabling the development of advanced drug delivery systems that enhance therapeutic efficacy, reduce side effects, and allow for targeted treatment. In oncology, nanoparticles are particularly valuable for delivering chemotherapeutic and epigenetic drugs with improved bioavailability and controlled release. Various synthesis methods are employed to produce nanoparticles with tailored properties such as size, surface charge, and drug-loading capacity. Common nanoparticle synthesis techniques include emulsion-based methods (eg, single or double emulsion solvent evaporation), nanoprecipitation, thin film hydration, high-pressure homogenization, and self-assembly of amphiphilic molecules. Each method offers distinct advantages depending on the type of nanoparticle being produced—such as polymeric nanoparticles, liposomes, solid lipid nanoparticles, or metallic nanoparticles—and the therapeutic application intended.

One of the earliest and most widely studied nanoparticle systems for 5-AZA delivery is PLGA nanoparticles. PLGA is a biocompatible and biodegradable polymer, making it an ideal candidate for drug delivery. In an investigation, AZA-loaded PLGA nanoparticles were prepared using a double emulsion (w/o/w) solvent evaporation technique. These nanoparticles exhibited a biphasic release profile, with an initial burst release followed by sustained release over 48 hours. The dual release mechanism ensures an initial cytotoxic dose followed by a maintenance dose, which is critical for maintaining therapeutic drug levels in the tumor microenvironment. The formulation parameters, such as polymer concentration, were optimized to achieve high entrapment efficiency and desired particle size. In another research, AZA-loaded liposomes (AZA-LIPO) were optimized for breast cancer therapy using a Box-Behnken design. Liposomes, prepared via thin film hydration, exhibited a mean size of 127 nm, 85.2% encapsulation efficiency, and 6.82% drug loading. In vitro drug release followed mixed-order kinetics, with pH-dependent burst release (36% at 2 hours) transitioning to sustained release (82% at 36 hours) under acidic conditions (Figure 1B). AZA-LIPO demonstrated enhanced cytotoxicity and pro-apoptotic effects in MCF-7 cells compared to free 5-AZA, highlighting its potential for targeted therapeutic delivery. One innovative investigation explored the effectiveness of encapsulating 5-AZA into SLNs using a double emulsification method with stearic acid, soy lecithin, and poloxamer 407, and evaluated its in vitro effects on MCF-7 cell lines. The formulation F5, with 55.84±0.46% entrapment efficiency and zero-order drug release kinetics, was selected for testing, revealing significantly higher cytotoxicity of the encapsulated drug compared to the free drug after 48 hours, as assessed by MTT assay (Figure 1E,F), DAPI staining (showing apoptotic nucleus morphology), and Rhodamine B uptake (indicating time-dependent SLN accumulation in the cytoplasm). However, real-time qRT-PCR showed no significant difference (p>0.05) in RARβ2 gene expression between cells treated with free or encapsulated drug. These findings suggest that SLN encapsulation enhances 5-AZA cytotoxicity, potentially guiding future dosage form development.

Bentonite (BNT)-based nanoparticles for 5-AZA delivery were explored to mitigate high-dose toxicity and instability in myeloid leukemia treatment. BNT-AZA nanoparticles demonstrated controlled 5-AZA release (50% at 2 h, 90% at 4 h, sustained beyond 8 h), enhancing bioavailability and reducing off-target effects. In THP-1 and K562 cells, CCK-8 assays revealed dose-dependent antiproliferative activity (10-100 µg/mL BNT), with low cytotoxicity at lower doses. Synergy was observed: 1 µM AZA + 10 µg/mL BNT matched 10 µM 5-AZA alone in efficacy, suggesting dose reduction potential. Apoptosis assays confirmed cell death, while fluorescence tracking verified nanoparticle uptake. BNT’s high drug-loading capacity, tumor targeting, and sustained release highlight its promise for optimizing 5-AZA therapy in AML, warranting clinical translation studies. Gold nanoparticles have also been explored as a delivery platform for 5-AZA. In one study, 5-AZA was loaded onto gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) modified with mercaptobenzoic acid (MBA) linkers. These nanoparticles were further functionalized with folic acid ligands to target cancer cells overexpressing folic acid receptors. The AuNPs exhibited high entrapment efficiency and selective accumulation in tumor tissues, as demonstrated by biodistribution studies. The drug release from these nanoparticles was pH-sensitive, with minimal release at physiological pH (7.4) and continuous release at acidic pH (4.0 and 5.5), mimicking the tumor microenvironment. This targeted and controlled release system enhances the therapeutic efficacy of 5-AZA while minimizing off-target effects.

Traditional cancer treatments often result in metastasis and relapse, while immunotherapy, though promising, is hindered by the tumor microenvironment’s immunosuppressive effects. The study introduces Nigericin (Nig + DAC)@HmA nanoparticles as a novel strategy to induce pyroptosis in cancer cells, a process that enhances systemic antitumor immunity by alleviating immunosuppression. This approach utilizes Nig to activate NLRP3 and DAC to up-regulate Gasdermin D expression, which is typically low in tumor cells but essential for pyroptosis. By promoting effective drug delivery, (Nig + DAC)@HmA aims to improve immune responses and address the limitations of conventional therapies, offering a potential advancement in tumor immunotherapy for solid cancers.

Another approach to improve the stability of drugs is by developing its prodrug form. Amphiphilic prodrugs of 5-AZA conjugated with omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) were developed to address limitations of 5-AZA. Prodrug synthesis via nanoprecipitation yielded self-assembled nanoparticles (190 nm diameter, positive zeta potential) with multilamellar vesicle structures confirmed by cryo-TEM (Figure 1C,D). The conjugates exhibited enhanced stability, achieving critical aggregation concentrations of 400 µM (AzaEPA) and 688 µM (AzaDHA), with structural stability observed after 5 days. While cytotoxicity of AzaDHA and AzaEPA against leukemia cells was lower than free AZA, AzaDHA demonstrated superior efficacy, suggesting potential for improved drug delivery, prolonged circulation, and reduced toxicity. These findings highlight the prodrugs’ promise in optimizing therapeutic outcomes for MDS and AML.

DAC, a DNA methyltransferase inhibitor used for leukemia treatment, is highly unstable, necessitating an innovative delivery approach. This study utilized a Quality by Design method to develop stable, decitabine-loaded nanoparticles via emulsion solvent diffusion, optimized through fractional factorial and Box-Behnken designs. The resulting uncoated and PEGylated nanoparticles, with particle sizes of 194.5 nm and 177.9 nm and zeta potentials of -32.9 mV and -31.0 mV, achieved encapsulation efficiencies of 85.2% and 90.9%, respectively. Lyophilization enhanced stability, and the formulations demonstrated sustained drug release over 7 hours. In vivo evaluations confirmed improved pharmacokinetics compared to a drug solution, highlighting the potential of this nano-therapeutic system for leukemia treatment and its broader implications in nanomedicine. Bionic nanoparticles, constructed from tumor cell membranes, have also been explored for decitabine delivery. These nanoparticles retain tumor cell surface antigens and receptors, enabling homologous targeting and immune evasion. A study demonstrated that decitabine-loaded bionic nanoparticles coated with PD-L1 antibody and macrophage membrane (Dec@PLGA@aMM) significantly enhanced tumor suppression in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) models. The nanoparticles restored tumor suppressor gene expression and blocked PD-L1, providing a novel approach for immunotherapy.

Hypermethylation of transcription factor AP-2 epsilon (TFAP2E) in gastric cancer is associated with 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) resistance, and DAC can reverse this hypermethylation, potentially re-sensitizing cancer cells. However, DAC’s instability limits its efficacy, which is addressed by developing gelatinase-sensitive nanoparticles (DAC-TNPs) for targeted delivery. This approach addresses the challenge of chemoresistance, a major barrier in gastric cancer treatment, by leveraging epigenetic modulation and targeted drug delivery. These polyethylene glycol (PEG) and poly-ε-caprolactone (PCL) nanoparticles were designed to deliver DAC specifically to gastric cancer cells. The study evaluated the efficacy of DAC-TNPs and DAC-NPs in combination with 5-FU in both in vitro and in vivo settings. GC cells and human GC xenografts in nude mice were treated with DAC-TNPs or DAC-NPs, followed by 5-FU administration 72 hours later. Both cell culture and mouse xenograft models demonstrated that combining DAC-TNPs and 5-FU greatly improved tumor suppression (Figure 1G), particularly in models with hypermethylated TFAP2E, such as MKN45 cells. This sequential administration enhanced the demethylating effects of DAC, leading to significant tumor growth inhibition compared to controls. The control DAC-NPs, lacking gelatinase features, showed reduced efficacy, underscoring the importance of targeted delivery in gastric cancer.

Zebularine, a newer DNMTi, has shown significant potential in cancer treatment by demethylating TSG and re-sensitizing drug-resistant cancer cells, such as in ovarian cancer, where it demonstrated a 16-fold reduction in the IC50 of cisplatin-resistant cells. The development of zebularine-loaded nanoparticles, such as the pH-responsive chitosan-zebularine (CS-Zeb) nanoparticles, has further improved its therapeutic efficacy. These nanoparticles facilitate targeted drug release in acidic tumor environments, significantly reducing tumor burden and enhancing survival in murine models, while also boosting CD8+ T cell infiltration in tumors. Zebularine also enhances tumor immunogenicity and antigen processing through the cGAS-STING pathway, promoting effective T cell-mediated tumor cell killing. Moreover, zebularine’s ability to induce S phase delay and cell death in malignant mesothelioma cells, without affecting normal fibroblasts, underscores its selective anticancer activity . Challenges

Nanotechnology-based delivery systems, including those employing DNA nanostructures, face significant challenges related to their chemical and colloidal stability in physiological environments. These systems are often prone to rapid degradation or clearance from the bloodstream, limiting their circulation half-life and reducing their ability to effectively accumulate at tumor sites. Moreover, critical aspects such as pharmacokinetics, clearance pathways, and dose-dependent cytotoxicity of these nanocarriers remain poorly characterized, posing substantial obstacles to clinical translation. Another concern is the potential for immune system recognition, which may lead to rapid clearance or unintended immunogenic responses. Despite efforts to improve biocompatibility, nanocarriers can still elicit acute adverse effects, and their long-term safety—particularly with repeated dosing—remains inadequately understood .

While nanocarriers are engineered to enable targeted delivery of DNA methylation modulators, achieving precise and efficient tumor-specific targeting continues to be a significant hurdle. Limitations such as suboptimal cellular uptake, incomplete targeting accuracy, and potential off-target effects can reduce therapeutic efficacy . Additionally, many systems struggle to escape lysosomal degradation and fail to provide controlled, site-specific drug release, which further compromises treatment outcomes. Importantly, most existing research remains at the proof-of-concept stage, with limited in vivo data on long-term efficacy, safety, and behavior in complex biological systems. This lack of translational evidence significantly impedes the advancement of these technologies toward clinical application.

Future Perspectives

The inter-patient variability of cancer epigenomes suggests that a one-size-fits-all approach may not be optimal. Tumors from different patients (even of the same type) harbor distinct DNA methylation landscapes, and even within a single tumor there can be epigenetic heterogeneity. These differences underline the need for personalized epigenetic therapies tailored to an individual’s tumor methylation profile. In the future, clinicians may perform genome-wide methylation analyses to identify which tumor suppressor pathways are epigenetically silenced in a given patient, and then select a DNMTi or combination to target those specific alterations. Nanotechnology can aid this personalized approach by enabling patient-specific drug delivery systems. For example, if a patient’s tumor overexpresses a certain surface receptor, one could choose a nanoparticle decorated with the corresponding targeting ligand to home in on that tumor. Similarly, dosing and release kinetics might be adjusted (using different nanoparticle formulations) based on a patient’s pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic response. Recent advances in AI and ML are poised to accelerate such customization. AI-driven formulation design can optimize nanoparticle properties by analyzing large datasets of composition, size, release profiles, and outcomes, identifying the ideal formulation for a given therapeutic context. ML models have been used to predict nanoparticle stability and drug release behavior under various conditions, aiding in the rational design of carriers with enhanced performance. In personalized medicine, AI could help match the right nanocarrier to the right patient by accounting for individual factors (genetics, tumor microenvironment, metabolic rates, etc.). Although these innovations are in early stages, they point toward a future where epigenetic cancer therapy is not only precise in targeting tumors, but also personalized to each patient’s epigenomic and physiological profile. Liquid biopsy, particularly through circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) methylation profiling, is emerging as a powerful, minimally invasive tool for real-time monitoring of cancer progression and treatment response. By capturing tumor-specific aberrant DNA methylation patterns, ctDNA analysis provides insights into the tumor’s epigenetic landscape, enabling early detection, risk stratification, and dynamic assessment of therapeutic efficacy or resistance. This approach allows for repeated sampling without invasive procedures, making it ideal for tracking tumor evolution and guiding personalized treatment strategies across various cancer types.

Conclusion

Nanotechnology-driven DNMTi therapy represents a promising frontier in oncology, offering a means to reboot the cancer epigenome in a controlled and targeted fashion. Diverse nanoparticle platforms now stabilize fragile epigenetic drugs and deliver them to tumors with high precision. These advances address many limitations of conventional DNMTis, as sustained-release formulations protect the drug from degradation, enhance bioavailability, and concentrate action in cancerous tissue. Such nano-formulations have shown enhanced therapeutic efficacy in preclinical studies, reactivating tumor suppressor genes more effectively with fewer systemic effects than free drugs. Nanocarriers also enable co-delivery strategies for potent combination regimens that leverage epigenetic reprogramming alongside other treatments. Ongoing research drives these innovations toward clinical translation, optimizing nanoparticle production and conducting early-phase trials of DNMTi nanoparticles in patients. If successful, these efforts will validate that precise nanomedicine delivery can amplify the benefits of epigenetic drugs while minimizing toxicity. The vision is a form of precision epigenetic therapy, targeting an individual’s tumor epigenome with tailor-made nanocarriers to disrupt cancer growth.
