# Therapeutic targeting of cell death-immune crosstalk in cancer to rewire the tumor immune microenvironment.

## Abstract

The immune system plays a central role in anticancer defense by coordinating antitumor responses that inhibit the initiation, progression, and metastasis of malignancies. Emerging evidence underscores the crucial interplay between regulated cell death (RCD) pathways and immune activation, particularly immunogenic cell death (ICD). ICD refers to a specific form of regulated cell death in which tumor cells undergo death and release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and other signaling molecules, thereby reshaping the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME). While the canonical RCD pathways are well-established, the inducers of ICD, the spatiotemporal regulation of TIME dynamics, and the functional states of immune cells remain incompletely understood. We thoroughly evaluated contemporary therapeutic approaches that exploit the mechanisms of RCD-driven immunomodulatory effects, including strategies to potentiate ICD and amplify antitumor immune responses. The novelty of this review lies in its dual perspective: delineating how tumor-intrinsic death programs reprogram the tumor TIME and how immune-cell death directly dictates its polarization toward immunostimulatory versus immunosuppressive states, ultimately shaping therapeutic outcomes. We analyzed in depth how different forms of RCD shape antigen presentation, guide immune cell infiltration, and affect checkpoint signaling, underscoring their dual potential to support or hinder antitumor responses. Furthermore, we summarized the current landscape of ICD-inducing interventions, including chemotherapy, radiotherapy, oncolytic viruses, tumor vaccines, and nanotechnology-based platforms, underscoring their potential to convert immunologically "cold" tumors into "hot" ones and to synergize with immune checkpoint blockade. In addition, we highlighted promising combination strategies aimed at directly modulating immune cell death pathways to sustain effector cell function and overcome exhaustion, offering novel insights for the development of next-generation precision immunotherapies. By delineating the crosstalk between tumor/immune cell death pathways, this study provides a roadmap for developing precise immunotherapies that exploit RCD-TIME interactions to overcome treatment resistance.

Background

The immune system is a critical defense mechanism against malignancies that acts by orchestrating immune responses to suppress tumorigenesis, cancer progression, and metastasis. The dynamic interplay between regulated cell death (RCD) pathways and immune activation is a cornerstone of cancer immunological research. However, the capacity of distinct RCD modalities to elicit ICD continues to be debated. Early studies proposed that tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) alone lack sufficient immunostimulatory potential to drive robust antitumor immunity, casting doubt on the immunological relevance of tumor cell death. This paradigm was revolutionized by the “Danger model” hypothesis, which posited that pathological cell death triggers immune activation through DAMP release, fundamentally reshaping our understanding of ICD and its therapeutic implications.

Subsequent investigations have delineated how diverse RCD pathways induce ICD via unique molecular mechanisms. These pathways drive DAMP secretion, remodeling the TIME and modulating tumor immune interactions. For example, necroptosis promotes immune cell infiltration and antigen-presenting cell (APC) maturation, ferroptosis enhances lipid peroxidation and releases immunostimulatory mediators such as HMGB1 and ATP7, and copper-dependent cell death activates dendritic cells (DCs) and cytotoxic T lymphocytes, inducing ICD.

The therapeutic exploitation of RCD pathways to potentiate immunotherapy has gained considerable attention. Pharmacological induction of ICD via RCD-targeted agents synergizes with immune activation to suppress tumor growth. For instance, ferroptosis inducers combined with nanomaterials amplify antitumor immunity and attenuate disease progression. Similarly, the pyroptosis-driven release of pro-inflammatory cytokines enhances immune cell recruitment and activation within the TIME.

Despite evidence linking RCD to immune-mediated tumor control, key questions persist regarding its precise effects on TIME composition and immune cell functionality. Recent advances highlight the therapeutic potential of combining RCD inducers with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) to synergistically overcome resistance in immunotherapy-refractory tumors. Elucidating the regulation of immune cell subset regulation by RCD pathways during ICD is critical to optimizing combinatorial strategies.

We, therefore, aimed to review the characteristics of distinct RCD modalities and their implications for antitumor immunity, with an emphasis on tumor cell-mediated DAMP release and TME modulation. We further examine the influence of immune cell death within the TME on immunosuppression and immunostimulation. We also systematically evaluated contemporary therapeutic approaches that exploit the mechanisms of RCD-driven immunomodulatory effects, including strategies to potentiate ICD and amplify antitumor immune responses.

Cell death

Tumor cells evade normal cell death mechanisms, including RCD and non-programmed cell death. RCD is crucial for tumor growth and resistance. Tumor cells resist apoptosis, survive stress via autophagy, and avoid ferroptosis by disrupting iron homeostasis. These mechanisms contribute to tumor development and metastasis, offering new therapeutic opportunities. Each form of cell death has a detailed mechanism (Fig. 1). Table 1 provides an overview of these cell death types, highlighting their mechanisms, characteristics, and future therapeutic potential.

Apoptosis

Caspases that activate the intrinsic and extrinsic pathways via proteolytic cleavage of specific substrates mediate apoptosis. The intrinsic pathway is initiated through mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP), which drives cytochrome c release, APAF1-dependent apoptosome assembly, and caspase-9 activation to execute apoptosis. Inhibitors of apoptosis proteins suppress this cascade by antagonizing caspase activity, a process counteracted by the mitochondrial proteins Diablo/Smac and HtrA2, which bind XIAP to derepress caspases. Extrinsic apoptosis is initiated by death receptor-ligand interactions that recruit FADD and activate caspase-8. Caspase-8 then cleaves effector caspases and the pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family member, BID, generating tBID. tBID promotes MOMP, cytochrome c release, and activation of the intrinsic pathway, establishing a feed-forward apoptotic loop. Concurrent cytokine release during extrinsic signaling enhances apoptosis. Caspase-independent apoptosis can be initiated via the mitochondrial factors AIF and EndoG, inducing DNA fragmentation and cell death, particularly under caspase-compromised conditions.

Necroptosis

A regulated form of necrotic cell death, necroptosis critically modulates inflammatory and immune processes and is initiated through the activation of death receptors or intracellular nucleic acid sensors, which converge upon RIPK3 phosphorylation. RIPK3 activation occurs via distinct pathways: TNFR1 engagement triggers RIPK1-RIPK3 association through the RIP homotypic interaction motif (RHIM), whereas TLR3/4 and ZBP1 activate RIPK3 via RHIM-containing adaptors. Activated RIPK3 phosphorylates MLKL, inducing oligomerization into necrosomal complexes that translocate to the plasma membrane, causing lethal membrane permeabilization and organelle damage. The subsequent release of pro-inflammatory mediators amplifies immune activation. Pharmacological inhibitors, such as necrostatin-1, block this pathway.

RIPK1 is a key molecule in necroptosis, a regulated form of necrotic cell death, and has recently emerged as a critical target for several small molecule inhibitors. Notably, SAR443820 (NCT05237284) and SAR443122 (NCT04781816) have entered Phase II clinical trials, demonstrating the potential of RIPK1 inhibitors in treating autoimmune, inflammatory, and neurodegenerative diseases. Additionally, GSK3145095, a RIPK1 inhibitor, shows excellent pharmacokinetic data and is being developed for monotherapy in advanced or metastatic pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, as well as in combination with pembrolizumab and other anticancer agents for treating various solid tumors, including pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, non-small cell lung cancer, triple-negative breast cancer, and melanoma (NCT03681951). However, this study was discontinued due to toxicity concerns, highlighting the need for further exploration of molecular selectivity, dose control, and combination therapy strategies. Moreover, PROTAC technology, an emerging protein degradation strategy, has shown promising potential for the selective degradation of RIPK1. Therefore, dysregulation of necroptosis is a promising therapeutic target for oncology.

Pyroptosis

Pyroptosis, a pro-inflammatory form of RCD, is orchestrated by inflammatory caspases and inflammasomes, which are activated by pathogen- or damage-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs/DAMPs). Central to this process is gasdermin (GSDM) family pore-forming activity. Inflammasomes, such as the NLRP3 inflammasome, are multi-protein complexes composed of a sensor protein (NLRP3, AIM2, or NLRC4), adapter protein ASC, and effector protein caspase-1. Inflammasome activation leads to caspase-1 activation, which processes gasdermin D (GSDMD). This cleavage generates N-terminal fragments that oligomerize to form pores in the plasma membrane, driving cytoplasmic swelling, chromatin condensation, and the release of pro-inflammatory mediators. Canonical pyroptosis involves inflammasome-dependent caspase-1 activation and subsequent GSDMD processing. Contrastingly, non-canonical pathways are initiated by gram-negative bacterial LPS via caspase-4/5 (human) or caspase-11 (murine), which directly cleave GSDMD. Alternatively, chemotherapeutic agents can activate caspase-3 to induce gasdermin E (GSDME) and lytic cell death. Cytotoxic lymphocytes such as natural killer (NK) cells and CD8+T cells exploit pyroptosis for antitumor immunity. Granzyme B from NK cells cleaves GSDME, and gasdermin B (GSDMB)-expressing targets undergo caspase-4-dependent pyroptosis via T cell-derived granzyme A. Pyroptosis is, therefore, a dual effector of inflammation and immune surveillance, and an actionable target for oncotherapy.

In recent years, GSDMD has emerged as a key target for inflammation-related diseases and a druggable target in cancer immunotherapy. For example, Ouyang et al. developed a "bionic nano-cocrystal" combining mitoxantrone and gambogic acid, which induced ribosomal stress in a TNBC model, triggering pyroptotic cell death through GSDMD pathway activation. Additionally, the small molecule DMB directly activates GSDMD without cleavage, inducing pyroptosis and stimulating antitumor immunity in mouse models. These findings underscore the potential of GSDMD modulators, particularly small molecules that directly activate GSDMD, as promising candidates for future cancer therapies.

Autophagy

Autophagy is an essential homeostatic mechanism that sustains cellular homeostasis through the lysosomal degradation of superfluous or damaged cytoplasmic components. This catabolic process operates through three evolutionarily conserved pathways: macroautophagy, microautophagy, and chaperone-mediated autophagy (CMA), which employ distinct mechanisms converging on lysosomal substrate processing.

The macroautophagy pathway is orchestrated through the initiation, nucleation, elongation, and fusion stages of autophagosome biogenesis mediated by autophagy-related (Atg) proteins. Key regulators of macroautophagy include beclin1, a pivotal protein that facilitates the formation of the autophagic vesicles, and LC3, which is involved in the elongation of the autophagosome membrane and is a hallmark marker of autophagic activity. These processes are dynamically modulated by nutrient-sensing pathways, particularly the mTORC1 (repressive) and AMPK (activating) pathways, which coordinate the induction of autophagy with metabolic stress and energy status. Furthermore, PERK-mediated autophagy is a response to endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress and plays a significant role in cancer cells by modulating autophagic flux under conditions of cellular stress. Autophagy plays a dual role in oncogenesis: during tumor initiation, it acts as a tumor suppressor by eliminating genotoxic components and maintaining genomic stability, while in established cancers, it promotes tumor cell survival under nutrient deprivation and facilitates metastatic progression through adaptive metabolic reprogramming. This duality positions autophagy as a therapeutic vulnerability that requires precise, stage-specific modulation in cancer treatment strategies.

Ferroptosis

Ferroptosis is a non-apoptotic, iron-dependent, RCD modality distinguished by morphological characteristics and driven by inactivation of the cystine/glutamate antiporter system Xc− (SLC7A11/SLC3A2), which compromises glutathione (GSH) biosynthesis and disables GPX4-a selenoprotein critical for neutralizing lipid peroxides. Consequently, unchecked propagation of lipid peroxides culminates in oxidative membrane damage and cell lysis. Autophagy-related mechanisms further potentiate ferroptosis by regulating iron metabolism. Key autophagy genes (ATG3, ATG5, BECN1) facilitate ferritinophagy—a selective autophagic process mediated by NCOA4 that liberates redox-active Fe2⁺ from ferritin-iron complexes, amplifying intracellular iron pools and Fenton reaction-derived reactive oxygen species (ROS). Additionally, FSP1 (ferroptosis suppressor protein 1) and COQ (coenzyme Q) have emerged as key regulators of ferroptosis resistance. FSP1, through its interaction with coenzyme Q, prevents lipid peroxidation by stabilizing cell membranes and counteracting ROS production, thereby serving as an important factor in cellular defense against ferroptosis. Hendricks et al. identified FSEN1 as a non-competitive inhibitor of FSP1, selectively enhancing ferroptosis sensitivity in various tumor cells. It also synergizes with endogenous peroxidase ferroptosis inducers. Cancer cells exhibit heightened sensitivity to ferroptosis, highlighting its unique potential as a therapeutic strategy in cancer treatment. The induction of ferroptosis has shown promising antitumor effects against various primary cancers and chemotherapy-resistant cancer cells.

Cuproptosis

Cuproptosis is a novel Cu-dependent cell death modality mechanistically linked to mitochondrial tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle dysfunction that has emerged as the focus of translational research in oncology because of its therapeutic potential. This pathway is initiated by the copper ionophore elesclomol, which facilitates mitochondrial uptake of Cu(II). The subsequent reduction to Cu(I) by ferredoxin 1 (FDX1) enables copper to bind to lipoylated TCA cycle enzymes such as dihydrolipoamide S-acetyltransferase (DLAT), inducing the oligomerization of lipoylated proteins, destabilization of iron-sulfur (Fe-S) clusters, and proteotoxic collapse. Cuproptosis remains refractory to canonical inhibitors of apoptosis (Z-VAD-FMK), ferroptosis (ferrostatin-1), and necroptosis (necrostatin-1), highlighting its unique mechanisms. Elesclomol, a Cuproptosis inducer, forms a complex with Cu(II) and preferentially accumulates in cells with high mitochondrial membrane potential, disrupting energy metabolism and activating Cuproptosis. Recent studies have shown that elesclomol exhibits synergistic effects with various drugs. Additionally, the combination of elesclomol with nanomaterials has attracted significant attention. For example, elesclomol-loaded copper oxide (CuO) nanoparticles (ES@CuO) induce Cuproptosis, significantly inhibiting tumor growth and reshaping the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. This enhances tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and inflammatory cytokine levels, showing synergistic effects with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy and improving anti-tumor efficacy. The dependency of cancer cells on dysregulated copper homeostasis, coupled with frequent TCA cycle adaptations in malignancies, makes cuproptosis induction a therapeutically exploitable vulnerability to overcome therapy-resistant cancers.

PANoptosis

PANoptosis, first conceptualized by Kanneganti et al. in 2019, is a distinct form of inflammatory RCD characterized by the simultaneous activation of pyroptosis, apoptosis, and necroptosis. PANoptosis represents a specialized host defense mechanism against pathogens and external insults, contributing to cellular and organismal homeostasis. Notably, although PANoptosis integrates essential features of the three canonical cell death pathways, it cannot be explained by any one of them in isolation. Once activated, inhibition of a single pathway is insufficient to block cell death or cytokine storm-driven pathology, primarily due to the central role of the PANoptosome signaling platform. The PANoptosome serves as the molecular scaffold of PANoptosis, assembling in a stimulus-dependent manner by integrating upstream sensors with downstream effectors to concurrently engage multiple death pathways. For instance, Z-DNA binding protein 1 (ZBP1) and AIM2 drive the formation of ZBP1-PANoptosome and AIM2-PANoptosome complexes, respectively. In addition, RIPK1-PANoptosome and NLRP12-PANoptosome complexes have been identified. Conversely, molecules such as TAK1, PSTPIP2, SHARPIN, HOIP, HOIL-1, and A20 act as negative regulators that inhibit PANoptosome assembly and activation. Recent studies have highlighted the critical role of PANoptosis in malignant tumors. Upstream sensors including ZBP1, RIPK1, and AIM2 detect specific stimuli and trigger PANoptosome assembly, thereby integrating multiple cell death programs into a unified response. Functionally, PANoptosis enhances the immunogenicity of tumor cell death and counteracts immune evasion strategies. Thus, PANoptosis is emerging as a central nexus linking inflammation, cell death, and tumor progression, and represents a promising therapeutic target for precision oncology.

Disulfidptosis

Oxidative stress is a hallmark of malignant tumors, and cancer cells maintain redox balance to detoxify excessive ROS by activating the GSH biosynthetic pathway. As noted above, the cystine/glutamate antiporter system Xc⁻ is a key regulator of GSH synthesis, in which SLC7A11-mediated cystine uptake is indispensable for sustaining GSH production. Inhibition of SLC7A11 therefore induces ferroptosis in cancer cells. In 2020, Liu et al., demonstrated that under glucose deprivation, SLC7A11 overexpression drives excessive NADPH consumption, leading to abnormal accumulation of cystine-derived disulfides and triggering disulfide stress, ultimately causing rapid cell death. These findings suggest that SLC7A11 exerts dual functions in regulating both redox homeostasis and programmed cell death. In 2023, Gan et al. elucidated the underlying mechanism of disulfide stress–induced cell death and designated this novel form of cell death as “disulfidptosis”. Under physiological conditions, NADPH supplies reducing power to counteract disulfide stress and preserve cellular homeostasis. However, under certain conditions, NADPH depletion results in massive accumulation of disulfide-bonded molecules. This, in turn, promotes aberrant disulfide bonding among cytoskeletal proteins, particularly F-actin, leading to cytoskeletal collapse and ultimately cell death driven by disulfide overload.

NETosis

In 2004, Brinkmann et al. first reported that upon stimulation, neutrophils release extracellular web-like structures composed of decondensed chromatin and granular antimicrobial proteins. These structures, termed neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs), effectively capture and eliminate pathogens, thereby limiting their spread, clearing infections, and enhancing host immune defenses. As a unique innate immune mechanism, NETs play an indispensable role in frontline host defense. However, under certain circumstances, NET formation can provoke immunopathological damage with detrimental consequences for the host. With the progression of this field, the term “NETosis” was introduced to describe the distinctive, suicidal form of programmed cell death by which neutrophils release NETs, a mechanism distinct from apoptosis and necrosis. Early studies have suggested that NETs exert antitumor effects. However, accumulating evidence in recent years has revealed that excessive NETosis is linked to tumor progression, the establishment of pre-metastatic niches, chemotherapy resistance, and thrombosis. Notably, emerging studies indicate that therapeutic inhibition of aberrant NETosis can restore antitumor immunity and enhance the efficacy of existing cancer therapies. These insights broaden our understanding of the biological functions of NETs and highlight NETosis as a promising target for developing precision cancer therapies.

TIME and connection with cell death pathways

The TIME is a central determinant of tumor initiation, progression, and immune evasion, comprising tumor cells, immune cells, cytokines, and chemokines, which interact to dictate tumor immunogenicity and therapeutic outcomes. Immune cells in the TIME are key effectors, with their balance between anti-tumor and pro-tumor functions significantly influencing tumor initiation, progression, and therapy response.

Under normal conditions, various immune cells collaborate to mount anti-tumor responses. Effector CD8⁺T cells kill tumor cells directly by releasing perforin and granzyme, whereas CD4⁺ T cells enhance immunity and maintain memory. NK cells and some γδT cells contribute to immune surveillance by targeting stressed or MHC-I deficient tumor cells. DCs, as potent antigen-presenting cells, capture and present tumor antigens, linking innate and adaptive immunity. Macrophages exhibit dual roles depending on their polarization. M1 macrophages promote anti-tumor immunity through pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IL-12 and TNF-α. However, chronic inflammation and tumor evolution drive M1 macrophages to adopt an M2 phenotype, which secretes IL-10, TGF-β, and pro-angiogenic factors that promote angiogenesis, tissue remodeling, and immune suppression, correlating with tumor progression and poor prognosis.

In addition to anti-tumor immune cells, pro-tumor immune cells play a significant role in the TIME. Regulatory T cells (Tregs), which maintain immune tolerance and prevent autoimmunity, suppress anti-tumor responses by secreting immunosuppressive cytokines (IL-10, TGF-β) and interacting directly with effector T cells and NK cells. Myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) accumulate in the TIME, suppressing T cell function through arginase secretion, reactive oxygen species, and cytokines, thereby protecting tumors from immune clearance. Together, the accumulation of immunosuppressive and effector cell exhaustion contributes to the "cold tumor" phenotype, hindering durable responses to immunotherapy. Immune cells in the TIME demonstrate high plasticity and duality: they perform anti-tumor functions under normal conditions, whereas tumor-induced immune tolerance and suppressive signaling can reprogram certain subsets into pro-tumor allies. Understanding these mechanisms will provide key insights for developing more effective and targeted immunotherapies.

ICD, a unique form of RCD, bridges tumor cell death and immune activation via the release of DAMPs, which activate immune cells such as DCs, driving antigen presentation and effector T cell activation, thereby initiating tumor-specific immunity (Fig. 2). Other RCD mechanisms, including apoptosis, necroptosis, and pyroptosis, exert varying effects on the TIME.

While apoptosis is traditionally viewed as immunologically silent, chemotherapies, such as anthracyclines and platinum-based agents, induce apoptosis with concurrent DAMP release, eliciting ICD-like immune activation. Necroptosis, characterized by uncontrolled cell lysis, triggers inflammatory cytokine release that amplifies immune responses within the TME. Pyroptosis remodels the TME through robust pro-inflammatory signaling, converting immunologically “cold” tumors into “hot” niches via immune cell recruitment. Autophagy, ferroptosis, and cuproptosis affect immune responses by modulating DAMP release and immune cell activation. The interplay between ICD and diverse RCD pathways fundamentally shapes the TIME, highlighting therapeutic opportunities to harness cell death modalities to enhance antitumor immunity and overcome resistance to conventional immunotherapies.

Cancer cell death influences immune cell infiltration into the TIME

Deciphering the governance of immune cell infiltration into the TIME needs to be elucidated to determine the interplay between cancer cell death modalities and immune dynamics. Furthermore, the mechanisms by which the release of DAMPs and the activation of immune pathways serve as key mediators of immune cell recruitment and activation need to be investigated. By shaping the immune landscape, these RCD mechanisms not only promote antitumor immunity but also impact the effectiveness of immunotherapies. Tumor apoptosis influences the TIME

Apoptosis-inducing agents such as oxaliplatin and anthracyclines can promote ICD through the release of DAMPs, including CRT exposure and HMGB1 secretion. For instance, CD1c+DCs exposed to oxaliplatin-treated tumor cells exhibit enhanced phagocytic capacity and promote allogeneic T cell proliferation. Apoptosis induction also leads to the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and DAMPs, which facilitate DC maturation and T cell activation. Similarly, treatment with the flavonoid compound LW-213 enhances APC activity and increases CD8+T cell infiltration in the tumor microenvironment. Therefore, tumor cell apoptosis is no longer considered a traditionally “silent” form of apoptosis.

The antitumor efficacy of platinum-based chemotherapies can partly act as ICD inducers and regulators of the TIME. However, the effectiveness in combination with ICIs requires further validation in clinical trials. Chemotherapy drugs that traditionally induce apoptosis increase the phagocytosis of tumor fragments by human blood DCs and enhance the phenotypic maturation of myeloid and plasmacytoid DCs. However, cell death induced by these drugs may not activate DCs or promote antigen presentation. Further evidence is required to support this hypothesis.

DAMPs can activate NK cells by engaging the NKG2D receptor, thereby enhancing IFN-γ secretion and establishing a positive feedback loop that further activates DCs and T cells. Deletion of the Xkr8 gene disrupts the clearance of apoptotic cells and promotes ICD, which significantly enhances the infiltration of proliferative NK cells and activated macrophages into the TME when combined with chemotherapy. Moreover, activation of the p53/AKT/WEE1 signaling axis enhances the expression of stress ligands recognized by NK cells, increasing tumor immunogenicity and facilitating NK cell-mediated cytolysis.

Within the TME, immunosuppressive cell populations play pivotal roles in tumor immune evasion. However, the release of DAMPs reprograms suppressed cells, polarizing TAMs to a pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype, reducing Treg infiltration and function, and attenuating MDSC-mediated suppression of effector T cells. Furthermore, nanodoxorubicin can reprogram TAMs into an antitumor phenotype. Similarly, cell death activates macrophages, reduces Tregs, and suppresses neutrophil infiltration in the TME. ATP and CRT signals disrupt MDSC-mediated inhibition of T cells, whereas photothermal therapy combining DAMPs with outer membrane vesicles reduces MDSC prevalence and enhances T cell activation.

Therefore, apoptosis is not merely a silent cell death process but rather plays a pivotal role in modulating stimulatory and suppressive mechanisms within the TIME. This evolving understanding provides strategic insights into leveraging apoptosis to enhance antitumor immunity and improve the efficacy of immunotherapy.

Tumor necroptosis influences the TIME

Necroptosis results in tumor cell membrane rupture and DAMPs release, triggering robust antitumor immune responses. Necroptotic tumors present with high infiltration of CD8+ T cells and DCs and show an improved response to immunotherapy. Necroptotic cancer cells can drive DC maturation, cross-priming of cytotoxic T cells, and IFN-γ production, forming a potent adaptive immune response. In liver cancer, ATO-induced mitochondrial damage activates the cGAS-STING pathway and enhances CD8+T cell infiltration. Among DAMPs, HMGB1 plays a central role in engaging TLR4 on DCs, upregulating costimulatory molecules and enhancing antigen presentation and T cell activation. HMGB1-derived adjuvants similarly boost CD8+T cell priming via TLR4. Meanwhile, extracellular ATP attracts and activates DCs by binding P2X7 receptors, promoting the release of IL-1β/IL-18, further stimulating CD8⁺T cells. Concurrently, pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-6 create an immunogenic TME, supporting T cell infiltration and effector functions. Necroptosis also enhances NK cell activation, amplifying the cytotoxic lymphocyte response.

The inflammatory microenvironment, shaped by necroptosis, significantly reshapes suppressive immune populations. TAMs can be reprogrammed toward a pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype in response to HMGB1, TNF-α, and IL-1β released during necroptosis. These M1-polarized TAMs exhibit enhanced antitumor activity and secrete cytokines that recruit and activate effector immune cells. In MDSCs, necroptosis-derived DAMPs reduce immunosuppressive functions and limit their accumulation. In liver cancer, SWD-induced necroptosis decreases MDSC infiltration, whereas DAC-mediated epigenetic modulation reduces MDSC expansion by altering RIP1-related pathways. HMGB1 signaling through RAGE or TLR4 further impairs the MDSC-mediated suppression of T and NK cells, and necroptosis-derived TNF-α and IL-6 negatively impact Tregs by interfering with their differentiation and function. TNF-α via TNFR2 directly inhibits Treg immunosuppressive activity, reducing their numbers within the TME. This enables effector cells to engage with tumor cells more effectively.

Through the direct activation of immune effector cells and indirect mitigation of suppressive cell populations, necroptosis orchestrates a shift toward an immunostimulatory tumor microenvironment, offering novel therapeutic avenues for enhancing antitumor immunity.

Tumor pyroptosis influences the TIME

Pyroptosis significantly modulates immune responses in the TME through the release of DAMPs and cytokines such as IL-1β and IL-18. Unlike apoptosis and necroptosis, it directly promotes ICD and enhances the recruitment of anti-tumor cells to the TME. The pro-inflammatory traits of pyroptosis significantly affect immune responses in the TME and inhibit tumor growth, and pyroptosis can convert “cold” tumors into “hot” tumors.

Pyroptosis induces the M1 polarization of tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), reduces immune suppression, and amplifies antitumor immune responses. In addition to activating effector immune cells, pyroptosis suppresses immunosuppressive cells in the TME, particularly Tregs and MDSCs. Pyroptosis-induced DAMPs inhibit Treg proliferation, thereby reducing immune suppression and enhancing antitumor immunity. Drug therapy and phototherapy-induced pyroptosis also decrease Treg numbers, further enhancing antitumor immunity. Additionally, pyroptosis combats MDSC-mediated immunosuppression. Recent advances, such as M (M + H)@ZIF/HA nanoparticles, weaken MDSC function and activate CD8+T cells, leading to tumor elimination in preclinical models.

In summary, pyroptosis plays a pivotal role in reshaping the TIME. By inducing ICD, pyroptosis enhances TAM polarization to the M1 phenotype, suppresses Tregs, and significantly improves antitumor immunity, making it a promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy. The dual effects of pyroptosis, which activate immune responses while reducing immunosuppressive barriers, make it a critical target to enhance tumor immunotherapy, particularly in combination with immune checkpoint blockade therapies. Ongoing research on overcoming immune evasion mechanisms is essential to further optimize pyroptosis-based therapeutic strategies.

Tumor autophagy influences the TIME

Autophagy in tumor cells can inhibit and enhance immune responses. Autophagy impairs antigen presentation by degrading TAAs and reducing MHC-I expression, which diminishes the ability of DCs to present antigens and activate CD8⁺T cells.

In PDAC, autophagy enhances ICD and improving immune cell responses in the TME. Tumor cell autophagy reduces NK cell recognition by lowering the expression of stress proteins. Hypoxia activates autophagy, reducing breast cancer cell sensitivity to NK cell lysis owing to the degradation of granzyme B in autophagosomes. Inhibition of beclin1 (BECN1) restores granzyme B levels, enhances NK cell cytotoxicity, and promotes tumor regression. This finding reveals the resistance mechanism of hypoxia-induced autophagy in NK cell-mediated antitumor immunity.

Autophagy also plays a significant role in regulating immunosuppressive cells in the TME. Tumor cells can exploit autophagy to secrete immunosuppressive factors such as IL-10 and TGF-β, which promote Treg activity and inhibit effector T cell function. Hypoxia-induced autophagy in breast cancer cells reduces NK cell sensitivity by degrading granzyme B, which can be restored by inhibiting beclin1. This demonstrates that autophagy can limit the effectiveness of NK cell-mediated tumor killing.

Moreover, autophagy supports the survival and immunosuppressive functions of MDSCs, which suppress antitumor immunity. Inducing ICD induction in metastatic melanoma (B16-F10) cells has shown that CA5 treatment induces autophagy, calreticulin exposure, and ATP release. CA5-treated cells activate antigen-presenting cells and T-lymphocytes, enhancing antitumor immunity. Autophagy may enhance the TME of immune cells by improving tumor cell metabolism and reducing oxidative stress.

However, targeting autophagy has shown promise for reversing these effects.For example, CB-5083, an autophagy inhibitor, enhances ICD and promotes DAMP release in PDAC, improving immune cell function. MoO3-x nanowires combined with PTT induce autophagy and ICD, leading to tumor antigen release, DC maturation, and CTL-mediated immune responses. Furthermore, combination therapy with PD-1 blockade enhanced antitumor immunity.

In summary, autophagy in tumor cells has complex effects on immune cells, promoting immune evasion by inhibiting antigen presentation, activating immunosuppressive cells, and impairing NK and CD8+T cell functions. However, immune cells can also enhance antitumor immunity by stabilizing antigens and optimizing metabolic conditions. Targeting autophagy can improve cancer immunotherapy by reducing the number of immunosuppressive cells and activating effector T cells, leading to stronger antitumor responses.

Tumor ferroptosis influences the TME

Ferroptosis is a form of RCD driven by iron accumulation and ROS-mediated lipid peroxidation, leading to plasma membrane damage and release of DAMPs, which promote ICD. Ferroptotic tumor cells induce BMDC maturation and vaccine-like immune responses in immunocompetent mice, however, these effects are absent in immunodeficient models, highlighting the role of ferroptosis in antigen presentation and adaptive immunity.

Nanomaterials can be used to enhance ferroptosis-induced ICD, and nanoplatforms enhance DAMP release, stimulate DC and CD8+T cell infiltration, and improve therapeutic efficacy. Several ferroptosis-inducing nanomedicines have entered early clinical trials. CNSI-Fe(II), which triggers ferroptosis via the Fenton reaction in the tumor microenvironment, can be combined with near-infrared photothermal therapy to enhance tumor ablation. Preclinical studies show significant tumor growth inhibition, and the drug has entered Phase I trials. Additionally, the radiosensitizer NBTXR3, delivered via tumor injection, enhances local radiation energy deposition during radiotherapy, inducing ferroptosis. NBTXR3 is currently in Phase I trials for treating pancreatic and other solid tumors. As clinical evidence accumulates, ferroptosis-inducing nanomedicines are poised to become a promising strategy for cancer therapy. Moreover, traditional cancer treatments such as cisplatin, sorafenib, and radiotherapy enhance ferroptosis and tumor immunogenicity, indicating their broad therapeutic potential in immune activation.

Despite its immunostimulatory potential, ferroptosis may contribute to immune suppression in the TME. Cui et al. showed that iron overload in NK cells impairs their viability and anti-tumor functions. Furthermore, oxidative lipid accumulation from ferroptosis can inhibit DC phagocytosis and antigen cross-presentation, thereby limiting effective immune activation. Ferroptosis-driven inflammation may inadvertently recruit immunosuppressive cells, such as Tregs and M2-polarized TAMs. Studies using organoid models have shown that blocking ferroptosis using deferoxamine or FSTL1 antibodies rescues NK cell function. Li et al. reported that in glioblastoma, ferroptosis increases FTL expression in TAMs, promoting iron accumulation and M2 polarization, thus reinforcing an immunosuppressive TME. Under certain conditions, ferroptosis may promote tumor growth, especially in the absence of effective immune regulation, presenting challenges for its use in antitumor therapies. Ferroptosis in the TME can enhance antitumor immunity by boosting antigen presentation and immune effector cell activation but may also suppress immunity by activating immune-suppressive cells. Therefore, further investigation into the bidirectional regulation of immune cells by ferroptosis will help develop more targeted strategies for enhancing antitumor immunotherapy.

Tumor cuproptosis influences the TIME

Cuproptosis activates ICD by triggering DAMP release, which enhances antigen presentation and immune responses. Copper-based nanoparticles can induce cuproptosis, promoting DC maturation and CD8 + T cell infiltration. For example, Cel-Cu NP induces cuproptosis, enhancing ICD, T cell activation, and synergizing with immune checkpoint blockade for effective tumor eradication. Similarly, other studies have used copper-loaded carriers to trigger cuproptosis and induce ICD, promoting the release of DAMPs and boosting DC maturation, CD4+/CD8+ T cell ratios, and antitumor immune responses. However, as cuproptosis may also increase ROS levels, excessive lipid peroxidation can trigger inflammation that might recruit additional immunosuppressive cells, posing challenges to the therapeutic potential.

Bioinformatics analysis and the development of new carriers have shown that cuproptosis significantly enhances ICD. This leads to the upregulation of immunostimulatory molecules, which improve the efficacy of both radiotherapy and immunotherapy while inhibiting tumor immune evasion by reducing PD-L1 expression. Combining cuproptosis with anti-PD-L1 antibodies further enhances the antitumor effect, highlighting the potential as a promising immunotherapy approach. Additionally, enhancing cellular cuproptosis by delivering copper ions through a catalase-functionalized spherical nucleic acid nanoplatform (CAT-ecSNA-Cu) alleviates tumor hypoxia, induces ICD, activates DCs, upregulates PD-L1 for checkpoint inhibition, and promotes effector T cell activation, ultimately enhancing antitumor immunity. Combining cuproptosis with therapies like sonodynamic treatment and inhalable copper-based metal–organic frameworks induces ICD, promotes DC maturation, and enhances CD8+T cell infiltration, significantly improving immunotherapy outcomes. Moreover, combining cuproptosis with anti-PD-L1 antibodies effectively enhances immunotherapy, reducing PD-L1 expression and stimulating cytotoxic T cells. Yan et al. developed an inhalable nanoplatform to efficiently induce cuproptosis in lung metastasis by enhancing copper accumulation, inhibiting glycolysis, and blocking copper export. This simultaneously triggers ICD, reprograms the immunosuppressive TME through PD-L1 upregulation and synergy with anti-PD-L1 therapy, enhancing antitumor efficacy. Although these studies have shown promising results, further research is required to optimize combination therapies and elucidate the full scope of their clinical potential in cancer treatment.

Tumor PANoptosis and disulfidptosis influence the TIME

PANoptosis, a distinctive form of inflammatory cell death, is recognized as an immunogenic mode of cell death accompanied by the release of DAMPs. A growing body of research has substantiated the immunostimulatory potential of PANoptosis. Wang et al. demonstrated that oncolytic virus therapy triggers PANoptosis, promoting immune activation, enhancing T cell infiltration, reprogramming macrophages to an M1-like phenotype, and depleting Tregs to amplify inflammatory responses. In hepatocellular carcinoma models, DNASE1L3-mediated PANoptosis was associated with an enhanced antitumor immune response, where high levels of DNASE1L3 expression correlated with increased inflammatory mediators and enhanced infiltration of CD4⁺/CD8⁺T cells, NK cells, and macrophages. Similarly, in breast cancer and glioblastoma models, the induction of PANoptosis resulted in comparable immune responses. Collectively, these findings underscore PANoptosis as a critical driver of antitumor immunity by simultaneously amplifying immune activation and exhausting immunosuppressive cells. Nevertheless, multiple studies have revealed heterogeneous immune landscapes associated with PANoptosis across different tumor types and patient cohorts. Such variability highlights the complexity of tumor heterogeneity and may, at least in part, explain divergent responses to immunotherapy, underscoring both the opportunities and challenges of targeting PANoptosis in cancer treatment.

Similarly, disulfidptosis, a metabolism-associated regulated form of cell death, has attracted considerable attention. A pan-cancer analysis defined a disulfidptosis-related gene signature linking high scores to poor prognosis, with the SLC7A11-INF2 axis identified as a key regulator. Notably, the GLUT1 inhibitor BAY-876 induced disulfidptosis in SLC7A11-high ovarian cancer cells, suppressing tumor growth. Importantly, disulfidptosis releases DAMPs that reshape the TIME. Li et al. developed deformable albumin nanocapsules that deplete glucose/glutamine or combine ferroptosis induction, inducing ROS storms and durable antitumor immunity. Moreover, disulfidptosis can synergize with other RCD forms, such as ferroptosis and pyroptosis, to amplify immune activation. For instance, PEGylated Fe-Bi-SS MOFs loaded with glucose oxidase (GOx) overcome SLC7A11-mediated ferroptosis resistance and deplete NADPH, triggering disulfidptosis. This dual-lethal mechanism not only strengthens tumor eradication but also promotes ICD, thereby enhancing antitumor immunity. Similarly, Pd₂Sn@GOx-SP nanocomposites were shown to induce pyroptosis via ROS storms while simultaneously triggering disulfide-dependent cell death through cystine accumulation, thereby promoting both ICD and immune activation within the TIME.

Taken together, disulfidptosis represents a critical nexus between metabolic stress, cell death, and tumor immunity. Future work should clarify its regulation across tumor and immune contexts and its interplay with other RCD forms, thereby facilitating the translation of precision metabolic–immune therapies targeting PANoptosis and disulfidptosis.

Immune cell death reprogramming effect on antitumor immune responses

The interaction between immune cell death and antitumor immune responses occurs within a dynamic bidirectional regulatory network. Immune cell death influences the activation and infiltration of immune cells and shapes the immune landscape by modulating key immune checkpoints, cytokine production, and immune cell polarization (Fig. 5). This interplay between immune cells and the TME enhances the effectiveness of antitumor immunity and contributes to immune evasion mechanisms.

Immune cell apoptosis modulates antitumor responses

Immune cell apoptosis serves as a pivotal modulator of antitumor immunity by shaping immune cell function, survival, and tumor infiltration within the TME. As a form of RCD, immune cell apoptosis can exert both immunostimulatory and immunosuppressive effects, depending on the cellular context and activation state.

NK cells rely on the mitochondrial apoptosis (mtApoptosis) pathway to enhance their cytotoxic activity. Induction of mtApoptosis, through caspase activation and mitochondrial damage, sensitizes tumor cells to NK cell-mediated killing, particularly at physiologically relevant effector-to-target ratios. Following NK cell therapy, residual tumor cells often remain at the threshold of apoptosis. Thus, anticancer agents capable of triggering mitochondrial apoptosis, such as BH3 mimetics, can cooperate with NK cells to push tumor cells beyond the apoptotic threshold, thereby markedly enhancing NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity. Indeed, the combination of the BCL-XL inhibitor A1331852 and the MCL1 inhibitor S63845 has been shown to significantly improve NK cell–mediated tumor clearance in both hematologic malignancies and solid tumors. SMAC mimetics are a class of small-molecule agents that antagonize IAPs. By binding to and neutralizing IAPs, they relieve caspase inhibition and thereby promote apoptosis. Recent studies have revealed that SMAC mimetics, such as NVP-LCL161 and birinapant, can enhance the cytotoxicity of NK cells against chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) by upregulating NF-κB target genes in NK cells. Cytokines can either confer resistance to BH3 mimetics by modulating BCL-2 family members or synergize with them to promote apoptosis. For example, activated NK cells exhibit reduced susceptibility to apoptosis and acquire resistance to BCL-2, MCL-1, and BCL-XL inhibition. Therefore, cytokine stimulation, such as with IL-15, can effectively protect NK cells against the pro-apoptotic effects mediated by BCL-2 inhibitors. TNF-α/NF-κB signaling induces Bcl-2 expression, enhancing prostate cancer cell resistance to apoptosis. In B-cell chronic lymphocytic leukemia, CD5⁺ B cells undergo rapid apoptosis upon Bcl-2 downregulation, but cytokines such as IL-4, IFN-γ, IL-2, IL-6, IL-13, and TNF-α delay this process by inhibiting Bcl-2 downregulation. These cytokine-driven upregulation of BCL-2 family proteins may undermine the effects of BH3 mimetics. Conversely, in melanoma, IFN-γ sensitizes PD-1 resistant cells to apoptosis, and combining BH3 mimetics further amplifies this effect. Natural compounds, such as diallyl disulfide (DADS) from garlic, also modulate cytokines and BCL-2 pathways, enhancing apoptosis in melanoma by upregulating p53 and caspase pathways while inhibiting NF-κB and its downstream Bcl-2 expression. Thus, understanding the cytokine-BCL-2 network is crucial for optimizing BH3 mimetic therapies, designing combination strategies, and overcoming resistance.

DCs are susceptible to apoptosis in the TME. TNF-α induces apoptosis of bone marrow-derived DCs via TNFR1 signaling in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), limiting DC availability and antigen presentation capacity. These findings offer a rationale for targeting TNFR1 to preserve DC function and enhance their immunogenicity.

CD8+T cells are frequently driven toward apoptosis under chronic antigen exposure, metabolic stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction in the TME. Survival cytokines activate the JAK-STAT and PI3K-AKT pathways to inhibit apoptosis; however, their signaling is often suppressed in tumors, leading to functional exhaustion and cell death. Metabolic pathways play crucial roles in CD8+T cell apoptosis. Folate and methionine metabolism are essential for T cell survival and the inhibition of apoptosis. Disruption of FOCM(One-Carbon Metabolism) metabolism reduces the number of CD4+T cell effectors and memory formation, whereas abnormal methionine metabolism leads to apoptosis via epigenetic mechanisms. Supplementation with key metabolites or a combination of ICIs can enhance T cell function and reduce apoptosis, offering new strategies to improve immunotherapy.

Cholesterol metabolism is also essential to maintain CD8+T cell function, and disruption thereof can promote T cell autophagic apoptosis by affecting mTORC1 signaling. The inhibition of cholesterol efflux improves metabolic adaptability, reduces apoptosis, and boosts antitumor effects. Therefore, enhancing T cell cholesterol levels or regulating metabolic pathways can significantly improve survival and tumor-killing ability, enhancing the efficacy of immunotherapy.

The role of Tregs in tumor immunity is closely related to their unique responses to TCR signaling. Foxp3-dependent Treg cells evade cell death in the TME by inhibiting the expression of TCR signaling molecules, thereby suppressing immune responses and promoting immune evasion. Additionally, Treg cells rely on TCR signaling for proliferation, and the loss of this signal leads to caspase-mediated apoptosis. Therefore, targeting TCR signaling may selectively induce apoptosis in effector Treg cells, enhancing antitumor immune responses.

Collectively, the modulation of immune cell apoptosis through mitochondrial pathways, cytokine signaling, and metabolic reprogramming presents a compelling strategy for amplifying antitumor immunity. Future therapies aimed at fine tuning apoptotic thresholds in NK cells, DCs, T cells, and Tregs may offer synergistic potential when combined with ICIs and metabolic modulators.

Immune cell necroptosis modulates antitumor responses

Necroptosis has gained increasing interest in tumor immunology for its dual role in promoting inflammation and modulating immune cell functions, driven by RIPK1 and RIPK3 activation. Emerging evidence suggests that immune cells are also subject to necroptotic regulation, which shapes the efficacy of antitumor immunity.

In T cells, necroptosis may serve as an alternative death pathway when apoptosis is inhibited. However, the physiological relevance of T cell necroptosis under homeostatic conditions remains unclear. Necroptosis also plays an immunosuppressive role in the TME. RIPK3-mediated necroptosis induces the expression of CXCL chemokines that recruit MDSCs to the TME, secreting IL-6, establishing a STAT3–DNMT epigenetic axis that inhibits TNF-α-induced RIP1-dependent necroptosis in MDSCs, thereby supporting their accumulation and immunosuppressive activity. RIPK3 deficiency reduces MDSC infiltration and enhances the presence of T and B cells, thereby restoring immune responsiveness.

Tregs are highly susceptible to necroptosis in the TME. Specific activation of necroptotic signaling can decrease Treg survival, thereby disrupting immune tolerance and enhancing antitumor immunity. For example, under inflammatory conditions, caspase-8-deficient Tregs undergo MLKL-dependent necroptosis, leading to enhanced immunogenicity and T cell activation. Targeting Treg-specific necroptosis may offer a novel approach to rebalance immune tolerance and effective tumor immunity.

Necroptosis also critically influences TAMs. Suppression of RIPK3 in TAMs promotes the accumulation of M2-polarized macrophages, thus supporting an immunosuppressive microenvironment. Contrastingly, the inhibition of RIPK1 phosphorylation preferentially induces necroptosis in M2 macrophages while sparing M1 macrophages, promoting a shift toward a pro-inflammatory, M1-dominant state. Therefore, necroptosis modulation may serve as a means to control macrophage polarization and reprogram the TME toward antitumor immunity. Targeting necroptosis in immune cells may, therefore, enhance cancer immunotherapy.

Immune cell pyroptosis modulates antitumor responses

Pyroptosis serves as a double-edged sword in the TME, regulating tumor cell death and the function of immune cells. In the context of antitumor immunity, CD8⁺T cells and NK cells mediate cytotoxicity in part through pyroptotic pathways. Engagement of these pathways promotes pyroptosis, facilitating immune cell infiltration, and amplifying antitumor effects. GSDMD plays an important role in CD8+T cell-mediated cytotoxicity and co-localizes with GZMB at the immunological synapse, contributing to pore formation and the release of cytotoxic granules. Traditionally, perforin has been recognized as the primary pore-forming protein driving CTL-mediated cytotoxicity. However, recent findings suggest that GSDMD may also play a role in pore formation within target tumor cells. The mechanisms governing GSDMD trafficking from CD8+T cells to tumor cells, however, remain to be fully elucidated.

In certain contexts, CD8+T cells may undergo pyroptosis, and this intrinsic pyroptosis impairing their function, contributing to immune evasion. However, through the modulation of pathways such as the NLRP3 inflammasome, pyroptosis can be redirected to enhance antitumor immunity.

During pyroptosis, the activation of inflammasomes plays a pivotal role in coordinating immune responses. The NLRP3 inflammasome promotes the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-18, which activate NK cells and CD4+T cells, thereby strengthening immune responses and protecting epithelial cells from damage. IL-18-induced maturation of hepatic NK cells enhances the expression of FasL and increases cytotoxicity against FasL-sensitive tumor cells, thus suppressing liver metastasis in colorectal cancer. As key players in innate immunity, NK cells rely on pyroptosis-mediated signaling to maintain their antitumor activity. Studies have shown that in the tumor microenvironment, NK cells release ATP during pyroptosis, which activates P2X7 purinergic receptors and subsequently triggers the NLRP3 inflammasome, enhancing NK cell cytotoxicity and facilitating immune-mediated tumor clearance. Furthermore, other molecules released during NK cell pyroptosis, such as IL-18 and IL-12, further amplify immune responses by promoting the activation and proliferation of effector T cells, driving the progression of antitumor immunity.

Pyroptosis in immune cells acts as a dual regulator of tumor immunity, both amplifying immune responses and potentially causing immune cell dysfunction or death. Elucidating the fine balance and mechanistic control of these pathways provides a crucial foundation for developing novel immunotherapeutic strategies that leverage immune cell-derived pyroptosis to enhance antitumor efficacy.

Immune cell autophagy modulates antitumor responses

Basal autophagy and glycolysis are essential to maintain the quiescent and differentiation potential of hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs). Atg7 deletion results in mitochondrial and genomic instability, impairing differentiation into myeloid and lymphoid lineages, potentially leading to myeloproliferative disorders. Autophagy, regulated by the FoxO3A transcription factor, supports metabolic adaptation and stress resistance in HSCs, whereas CMA ensures proteostasis following HSC activation.

NK cells depend on autophagy for their development and function. During maturation, autophagy eliminates damaged mitochondria and reduces ROS levels, thereby protecting immature NK cells from oxidative stress-induced apoptosis. The loss of autophagic activity disrupts NK cell differentiation and cytotoxic potential. FoxO1, a negative regulator of NK cell maturation, modulates autophagy via multiple signaling pathways. Upon DNAM-1 receptor engagement, FoxO1 is phosphorylated via the AKT pathway, inducing autophagy and enhancing iNK cell functions. The role of autophagy in NK cell-mediated tumor immunity is context-dependent. Autophagy enhances NK cell cytotoxicity, whereas genetic or pharmacological inhibition of autophagy suppresses tumor growth and promotes NK cell infiltration.

Autophagy is indispensable for T cell survival and memory. Deletion of Atg5 or Atg7 increases apoptosis and impairs CD8+T cell memory, a pattern also observed in CD4+T cells. However, its role in tumor settings remains controversial. Defects in KCMF1(Kalirin-containing membrane-associated factor 1)-mediated autophagy limit CD8+ memory T cell survival, whereas carbon monoxide (CO)-induced autophagy via the PERK pathway boosts antitumor activity. Moreover, deletion of Beclin1 or Atg7 impairs survival, and Beclin1-deficient CD4+T cells accumulate death-promoting proteins and undergo apoptosis. These findings underscore the dual context-sensitive role of autophagy in T cell-mediated immunity.

Tregs require autophagy to maintain their suppressive function in the TME. Deletion of Atg5 or Atg7 in Tregs enhances IFN-γ production, activates mTOR and c-Myc, and increases glycolysis, leading to metabolic dysfunction and loss of immunosuppressive activity. Autophagy restricts STING pathway activation and reduces IFN-γ levels, limiting CD4⁺ and CD8⁺T cell exhaustion. Similarly, the deletion of Vps34 in T cells impairs Treg differentiation, whereas acute Vps34 ablation in mature Tregs improves antitumor immunity via enhanced T cell activation. Moreover, autophagy in α-SMA⁺ cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) fosters immune tolerance by facilitating Treg recruitment. Loss of Atg5 in these CAFs alters their inflammatory profile and reduces Treg infiltration. Inhibition of autophagy can also contribute to Treg regulation. Therefore, balancing autophagic flux is critical to regulate Treg stability and function in cancer.

TAMs use the non-canonical LC3-associated phagocytic (LAP) pathway to clear apoptotic cells and maintain immune tolerance. Although LAP shares components with canonical autophagy, it is governed by receptors, such as Rubicon and ATG7, which modulate immune responses. In the TME, LAP promotes immunosuppression, especially in peritoneal metastases, whereas the loss of LAP regulators, such as Rubicon, leads to localized immune activation. Additionally, TAMs deficient in FIP200 accumulate mitochondrial ROS, increasing apoptosis of TIM4⁺ macrophages and improving T cell-mediated immunity. In contrast, TIM4− TAMs, which lack autophagy activity, exacerbate oxidative stress and suppress T cell responses, promoting tumor metastasis. These findings highlight the importance of regulating TAM autophagy to enhance antitumor immunity.

Immune cell ferroptosis modulates antitumor responses

Ferroptosis exerts a dual influence on immune cells within the TME, functioning either as a promoter of antitumor immunity or as a facilitator of immune suppression, depending on the cellular context and metabolic cues. In NK cells, ferroptosis is detrimental to antitumor immunity. Lipid peroxidation-driven ferroptosis impairs NK cell cytotoxicity in the TME. L-kynurenine (L-KYN) induces NK cell ferroptosis through an AHR-independent pathway, significantly reducing NK cell infiltration. Conversely, ferroptosis inhibitors restore NK cell viability and enhance tumor killing. Activation of NRF2 signaling can rescue NK cell function under oxidative stress. In organoid models, targeting CAFs with deferoxamine and FSTL1-neutralizing antibodies attenuates CAF-induced NK ferroptosis and enhances antitumor activity.

T cells show resistance to ferroptosis, allowing the selective targeting of tumor cells without impairing T cell-mediated immunity. The deletion of SLC7A11 limits tumor growth while preserving T cell function and enhancing the efficacy of ICI. The DEPDC5-mTORC1 axis plays a key role in protecting CD8+T cells from ferroptosis under oxidative stress. Induction of ferroptosis by cysteine deprivation or arachidonic acid supplementation can inhibit tumor growth without harming T cell proliferation and may even improve T cell responses. However, the inhibition of ACSL4 suppresses T cell-mediated tumor immunity. The deletion of GPX4 in T cells induces ferroptosis and impairs the immune response during infection. Conflicting results have emerged regarding GPX4 inhibition. While it enhances T cell activity in some triple-negative breast cancer models, CD8+T cells are highly sensitive to lipid peroxidation and may undergo ferroptosis more readily than tumor cells upon GPX4 loss. High CD36 expression in tumor-infiltrating CD8+T cells facilitates fatty acid uptake, promotes lipid peroxidation, and triggers ferroptosis, thereby weakening antitumor capacity. Blocking CD36 or inhibiting ferroptosis reverses this effect. Furthermore, PD-1 signaling suppresses PLPP1 via the AKT-GATA1 axis, enhancing ferroptosis and reducing CD8+T cell efficacy.

DCs are sensitive to ferroptosis under stressful conditions, and APE1 expression in DCs increases oxidative stress and ferroptosis, leading to impaired immune function. APE1 overexpression alleviates this effect, whereas its inhibition correlates with poor outcomes, suggesting a potential therapeutic target to modulate DC ferroptosis.

Tregs demonstrate intrinsic resistance to ferroptosis, likely because of their high GPX4 levels. Specific deletion of Gpx4 in Tregs induces ferroptosis and enhances antitumor immunity. Similarly, follicular helper T cells (Tfh) rely on GPX4 for their survival and function. In MDSCs, resistance to ferroptosis supports immune evasion. Inhibition of ASAH2 can trigger ferroptosis in MDSCs, thereby enhancing CD8+T cell infiltration. This resistance is partly mediated by ACOD1 in neutrophils, which promotes mevalonate synthesis and activates NRF2-dependent antioxidant defense. However, although PMN-MDSCs may undergo spontaneous ferroptosis in the TME, their lipid peroxidation products can suppress T cell function. Therefore, inhibition of ferroptosis may reduce lipid-mediated immunosuppression, whereas inducers may inadvertently promote tumor progression.

TAMs are typically skewed toward an immunosuppressive M2 phenotype in the TME, contributing to tumor immune evasion. Reprogramming M2 TAMs into M1-like pro-inflammatory cells is a key immunotherapeutic strategy. Although TAMs exhibit low spontaneous ferroptosis, M2 macrophages are more sensitive than M1 macrophages, partly due to differences in iNOS and ferritin heavy chain (FTH1) expression, which regulate intracellular NO levels and iron homeostasis. Ferroptosis-inducing therapies show potential to selectively eliminate M2 TAMs while preserving the M1 population. However, not all findings are consistent, and inducing ferroptosis may promote M1 reprogramming, whereas inhibiting ferroptosis restores the immune balance.

Ferroptosis intricately modulates the activity, survival, and polarization of immune cells within the TME. Its regulation varies across immune subsets; while targeting ferroptosis can enhance NK cell and T cell function, eliminate M2 TAMs, or suppress MDSC-mediated immunosuppression, improper modulation may result in immune escape, T cell dysfunction, or tumor progression. Precisely tuning ferroptosis in immune cells holds great promise as a complex yet compelling frontier in cancer immunotherapy.

Immune cell cuproptosis modulates antitumor responses

Cuproptosis promotes antitumor immunity and suppresses immune cell function via immune inhibitory mechanisms. Cuproptosis enhances the expression of MHC-II-HLA-DRA by generating ROS, which promotes the upregulation of chemokines, enhancing the infiltration of CD4+ and CD8+T cells and inhibiting the formation of a protumor microenvironment. This improves the sensitivity to ICI. Li et al. developed a nanocomposite that induces ICD, promotes dendritic cell maturation and CD8+T cell infiltration, and effectively inhibits tumor growth, providing a promising therapeutic strategy for radioimmunotherapy in carcinoproptosis-related cancers. In microsatellite stable (MSS) colorectal cancer (CRC) cells, cuproptosis enhances CD8+T cell cytotoxicity by suppressing the WNT signaling pathway and reducing PD-L1 expression.

Inhibiting cuproptosis can enhance antitumor immune responses by improving immune cell function. For instance, a glioma study showed that Gal-3 promotes copper uptake and induces cuproptosis in CD8+T cells, while TRIM16, by inhibiting Gal-3, protects T cells from damage and enhances their immune activity. Additionally, copper chelators inhibit the activity of the copper transporter CTR-1, reduce PD-L1 expression, and increase the number of CD8+T and NK cells infiltrating the tumor, suppressing tumor growth. Overall, as a novel form of cell death, cuproptosis has great potential in cancer immunotherapy. Cuproptosis plays a crucial role in regulating the TME. Thus, investigating the mechanisms thereof cuproptosis in antitumor immunity will provide new insights into cancer immunotherapy and theoretical support for the development of cuproptosis-inducing agents.

Immune cell PANoptosis and disulfidptosis modulate antitumor responses

Within the TIME, not only tumor cells undergo PANoptosis, but immune cells also express PANoptosis-related genes and can experience PANoptosis. Some studies have demonstrated strong associations between immune cell PANoptosis and infectious or inflammatory diseases; however, its precise role in tumor initiation and progression remains unclear, and the underlying mechanisms require further investigation. In macrophages, ADAR1(Adenosine Deaminase Acting on RNA 1) suppresses PANoptosis by interacting with the Zα2 domain of ZBP1, thereby limiting ZBP1-RIPK3 interactions. In contrast, combined treatment with IFNs and nuclear export inhibitors induces ZBP1-dependent PANoptosis. TANs(Tumor-Associated Neutrophils) exhibit elevated expression of PANoptosis-related genes and promote tumorigenesis by engaging DCs through the GATA2-HMGB1-TIM-3 axis, thereby impairing antitumor immunity in non-small cell lung cancer and facilitating immune evasion. Sequencing analyses have further revealed immune cell type–specific PANoptosis patterns. Yi et al. reported that CD8⁺T cells, B cells, and DCs displayed higher PANoptosis signature scores than other immune subsets in prostate adenocarcinoma, suggesting more pronounced PANoptosis in these populations. Such efforts will be critical for advancing our understanding of its role within the TIME and for informing the development of therapeutic interventions aimed at reprogramming immune cell function to enhance antitumor immunity.

Recent studies show that disulfidptosis is elevated in tumor-infiltrating exhausted CD8⁺T cells, contributing to their functional decline. G6PD deficiency impairs NADPH production, exacerbating disulfidptosis. STAT3 upregulates LDHB, which interacts with G6PD to restrict its activity, driving disulfidptosis and T cell exhaustion. This finding identifies the STAT3-LDHB-G6PD axis as a key metabolic checkpoint in T cell dysfunction. However, the role of disulfidptosis in other immune subsets and its broader impact on antitumor immunity remain to be explored in future research.

Novel therapeutic strategies

Various strategies, including chemotherapy, targeted drugs, oncolytic viruses, tumor vaccines, and physical therapies, effectively induce ICD as standalone or combination therapies. These treatments release DAMPs and tumor antigens, activating immune responses via pattern recognition receptors in the innate and adaptive immune systems (Table 2). Ongoing research aims to optimize ICD inducers, and future ICD inducers, modulators, and enhancers are expected to offer personalized cancer treatments that maximize the potential of ICD across different malignancies.

Targeting tumor cells to induce ICD

Chemotherapy and small-molecule compounds

Chemotherapy remains a cornerstone of cancer treatment by inhibiting tumor cell proliferation and driving tumor cells through specific death pathways that promote ICD. Chemotherapeutic agents induce tumor cell death through various mechanisms, including apoptosis, and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress. This triggers ICD responses and stimulates antitumor immune responses. For instance, oxaliplatin and paclitaxel induce tumor cell apoptosis through a TLR4-dependent mechanism, releasing ICD markers that enhance immunotherapy efficacy. Similarly, the novel antimetabolic drug trifluridine/tipiracil induces ICD by activating eIF2α phosphorylation, thereby improving the effectiveness of oxaliplatin-based treatments. Withaferin A (WE) induces ICD and demonstrates significant anti-leukemic activity by promoting intracellular calcium accumulation and ROS generation. Cisplatin, through its activation of the DNA damage response, induces apoptosis and enhances ICD by activating immune-related pathways that activate DCs and amplify antitumor immunity.

The type of cell death induced by chemotherapeutic drugs directly influences the ICD induction efficiency. Combining small-molecule death inducers with chemotherapy amplifies this effect.

Small-molecule targeting of cell death has emerged as a critical strategy for enhancing ICD and improving antitumor immune responses by activating various cell death pathways. For instance, BH3 mimetics, such as ABT-199 (Venetoclax), which target BCL-2 family proteins, induce apoptosis in tumor cells, thereby promoting ICD. Additionally, small molecules such as paclitaxel and cisplatin, induce pyroptosis in tumor cells, further augmenting antitumor immunity. These compounds promote tumor cell death and the release of DAMPs, triggering the ICD response via mechanisms like activating ER stress, phosphorylating eIF2α, and activating the PERK pathway. The combination of chemotherapy and small molecules can synergistically induce stronger ICD effects, leading to more effective antitumor immune responses.

Although the potential of chemotherapy and small molecules to induce ICD presents new opportunities for cancer treatment, some challenges remain. One limitation is the variability in ICD response across different tumor types and individual patient profiles, which complicates the generalization of these strategies. Additionally, the effectiveness of ICD induction may be reduced by tumor resistance mechanisms or the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, which limits the activation of antitumor immunity. Future research should focus on gaining a better understanding of the molecular mechanisms underlying these responses and explore combination therapies that optimize the ICD effect, enhance immune activation, and overcome resistance. Developing more precise chemotherapy regimens that synergize with immunotherapy could significantly improve treatment outcomes, particularly for cancers that are resistant to conventional therapies.

Physicotherapeutics

Physical therapies such as radiotherapy (RT), photodynamic therapy (PDT), and photothermal therapy (PTT) have emerged as essential non-pharmacological modalities in cancer treatment because they mediate direct tumor cell destruction and serve as potent inducers of ICD. By releasing DAMPs, reshaping the TIME and enhancing systemic antitumor immunity. Recent advances in nanotechnology and combination therapies have further amplified immunogenic effects, offering promising strategies for overcoming tumor immune evasion and improving clinical outcomes in various malignancies.

RT induces apoptosis, necroptosis, and ferroptosis in tumor cells, activating ICD and enhancing antitumor immune responses. For instance, DNA double-strand breaks and ROS generated by RT not only lead to cell apoptosis, but also trigger DAMP release, activate DCs, promote antigen presentation, and activate CD8+T cells. RT activates host antitumor immune responses in the TIME by inducing ICD and enhancing antitumor responses at the primary and distal tumor sites. Moreover, necrotic cell death induced by RT releases large amounts of ATP, which stimulates tumor-infiltrating immune cells through the P2RX7 receptor, thereby enhancing antitumor responses. Nanoparticles are widely used to further enhance the effects of RT-induced ICD, and combination therapies also show synergistic potential for enhancing ICD. RT combined with anti-PD-1 antibodies significantly enhances ICD responses and T cell immune memory. Additionally, RT combined with small molecules, such as sulforaphane, amplifies the ICD effect by inducing tumor ER stress and CRT release, improving tumor treatment outcomes.

FLASH radiotherapy (FLASH-RT), an ultra-high dose rate irradiation technique, induces ICD through the cGAS-STING pathway by reducing tumor vascular collapse and immune cell infiltration, thereby enhancing antitumor immune responses. It has shown effects comparable to those of conventional RT in upregulating T cell activation markers and enhancing immune responses. FLASH-RT also reduces radiation-induced lung injury (RILI), inducing a more immediate and transient cellular response than conventional RT. It enhances the activation of CD4+CD40L+Th cells and reduces pro-inflammatory responses, promoting tissue repair.

Despite the considerable potential of RT to induce ICD, its effectiveness is limited by immune cell damage and the presence of an immunosuppressive TME. Future research should focus on optimizing dose fractionation, using more efficient radiosensitizers, and combining RT with immunotherapy to further enhance RT-induced ICD and provide better treatment options for patients with cancer.

PDT is a noninvasive treatment that combines photosensitizers, light of specific wavelengths, and molecular oxygen to generate ROS, induce tumor cell death, and modulate the tumor microenvironment. Apoptosis is the most common form of cell death induced by PDT. However, at very high ROS levels, necroptosis, ferroptosis, and autophagy cell death may also occur, activating ICD. Despite its advantages in inducing ICD, PDT faces challenges in deep tumors, including limited light penetration, hypoxia-reducing ROS generation, and nonspecific photosensitizer distribution, causing toxicity to normal tissues. To overcome these issues, researchers have focused on optimizing photosensitizer design and nanotechnology. For instance, a 2024 study explored the application of BRJ and its main component, betanin, in liposomal nanoparticles as a green photosensitizer for cancer PDT. The study found that liposome-encapsulated betanin significantly enhanced cytotoxicity to cancer cells under light irradiation, while exhibiting lower toxicity in normal cells. ChiP-ceR nanoparticles enhance PDT-induced ICD by accumulating at the tumor site, activating antitumor immunity, polarizing M2 macrophages to M1, boosting T cell responses, and exhibiting significant antitumor effects in breast cancer and lung metastases. Additionally, liposomal nanoparticles loaded with doxycycline (Doxy) and chlorin e6 (Ce6) enhance photodynamic immunotherapy. Ce6 generates ROS upon laser irradiation to induce ICD, whereas Doxy inhibits autophagy and induces mitochondrial dysfunction, increasing ROS generation, and enhancing PDT and ICD effects. A new therapeutic system (Pd-Dox@TGM NPs) combines chemotherapy and photothermal therapy to trigger ICD, enhancing the immunogenicity of dying tumor cells, and improving CTL infiltration, demonstrating effective treatment for colorectal tumors. The combination of PDT and ICIs not only enhances the ICD induction effect but also improves the tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment.

The antitumor effects of talaporfin sodium (TS)-PDT and the synergistic action with anti-PD-1 antibodies show that TS-PDT-induced apoptosis, necroptosis, and autophagy in tumor cells, releasing DAMPs to activate the innate immune response. Therefore, TS-PDT combined with anti-PD-1 antibodies significantly inhibits tumor growth in vivo. The combination of PDT and immunotherapy significantly enhances antitumor immune effects and prolonged survival. In the future, optimizing personalized PDT treatment strategies will be a key research focus, particularly in the selection of efficient photosensitizers, the development of deep-tissue light delivery technologies, and the integration of nanotechnology to improve the hypoxic microenvironment. These strategies may enhance PDT-induced cell death and ICD effects, thereby providing precise and effective solutions for cancer therapy.

PTT utilizes photothermal agents to absorb near-infrared light (NIR) and convert it into heat, which directly induces tumor cell death. The main mechanism of PTT involves localized heating, which causes cellular thermal damage leading to tumor cell death, particularly in hypoxic environments. Moreover, the thermal effect directly kills tumor cells and significantly induces ICD by releasing DAMPs. Chen et al. developed small organic chromophore nanoparticles (TTHM NPs) with high photothermal conversion efficiency. These nanoparticles induce mitochondrial damage via PTT and iron accumulation, triggering ICD and boosting antitumor immunity. The Cu3P/1-MT nanocomposite enhanced the efficacy of PTT, induced ICD, and optimized the immune microenvironment by modulating immune cells, thereby improving antitumor therapeutic efficiency. The combination of AuNP@mSiO2-based PTT and DOX enhances the ICD effect, promoting DCs maturation and T cell infiltration, which inhibits tumor growth and metastasis. The combination of PTT with nanotechnology and synergistic therapies demonstrates stronger ICD induction capabilities. For example, combining PTT with nanocomposite materials, such as GA@MPDA and PCD@Cu, effectively inhibits tumor growth, induces ICD, activates the immune system by increasing effector T cell infiltration and cytokine levels, offering a promising strategy for the treatment of triple-negative breast cancer. Additionally, a dual-target nanoparticle delivery system (GOx@FeNPs) mediates ICD through photothermal effects and produces large amounts of ROS, promoting ferroptosis, enhancing the Fenton reaction, and improving the antitumor effects of PTT. This system releases tumor-specific antigens, thereby activating antitumor immune responses. Anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy is successful in clinical breast cancer treatment but is limited by weak immune responses. Zhou et al. developed an A6-MPDA@PAL nanoplatform, combining PTT and palbociclib, which enhanced anti-PD-L1 therapy by inducing ICD, activating T cell infiltration, and reversing the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Although PTT shows significant potential for ICD induction, challenges remain, including limited light penetration depth, interference from the TIME, and possible accumulation of toxicity from photothermal agents. Future research should focus on developing more efficient and low-toxicity photothermal agents, such as smart-responsive nanoparticles, to improve tumor targeting and treatment safety. Combining PTT with other therapeutic strategies, such as ICIs or PDT, is also expected to further enhance ICD induction and improve the tumor immune microenvironment, thereby improving overall treatment outcomes.

Oncolytic viruses (OVs)

OVs are a promising approach in cancer therapy that can selectively infect and kill tumor cells, thereby inducing ICD. Upon invading tumor cells, OVs hijack the cellular protein synthesis machinery for replication, causing viral proteins to misfold in the ER, triggering ER stress, and initiating ICD. Different OVs may induce immune responses through various forms of cell death, releasing DAMPs that activate immune cells and initiate antitumor immune responses. Lin J et al. found that vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) and Parapoxvirus ovis (ORFV) enhance anti-tumor immune responses through GSDME-mediated pyroptosis, converting "cold" tumors into "hot" tumors and thereby improving the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors. Ma et al. explored the synergistic anti-tumor effects of high-dose vitamin C (Vit C) combined with oncolytic adenoviruses (oAds), where oAds induced ICD and significantly increased T-cell infiltration and activation in the tumor microenvironment, with the anti-tumor effect relying on CD8+T cells.

Despite the immense potential of OVs as ICD inducers, the clinical effectiveness of monotherapy remains limited, and their impact on improving ICD and the tumor immune microenvironment is not significant. However, the combination of OVs with other therapeutic modalities may synergistically amplify the immunogenic death response, further enhancing the efficacy of antitumor immunotherapy. Combining radiotherapy with vaccinia virus significantly inhibits the growth of head and neck cancers. Moreover, engineered oncolytic adenoviruses (OAds) combined with ICIs activate antitumor immune responses by inducing ICD. Different OAd variants work in different tumor immune environments, overcoming the immunosuppressive TME and promoting systemic antitumor immunity. Injecting an oncolytic vaccinia virus encoding IL-7 and IL-12 could activate immune-deficient tumors, leading to complete tumor regression and long-term tumor-specific immune memory. This approach also enhances tumor sensitivity to ICIs. OBP-502 induces the release of ICD molecules from CT26 and PAN02 cells, recruits CD8+T-lymphocytes, and inhibits the tumor infiltration of Foxp3+ lymphocytes, enhancing the potential of PD-1 antibody combination therapy. A recombinant oncolytic virus, NDV-GT, was developed by incorporating the porcine α1,3-galactosyltransferase (α1,3-GT) gene into Newcastle disease virus (NDV). Upon infection, NDV-GT induces expression of the non-human antigen αGal on tumor cells, eliciting a robust hyperacute rejection response mediated by pre-existing anti-αGal antibodies, markedly enhancing tumor clearance. In a clinical study, NDV-GT achieved a 90% disease control rate, with durable responses and no serious adverse events, demonstrating favorable safety and broad-spectrum antitumor activity. However, the clinical efficacy and safety thereof warrant further validation in larger clinical trials.

OVs show great promise in inducing ICD and activating antitumor immunity. However, their clinical translation still faces significant challenges, including immune clearance, poor tumor targeting, and safety concerns, such as off-target toxicity and viral dissemination. Future research should focus on engineering safer and more efficient OVs and validating their therapeutic safety and clinical potential across broader patient cohorts.

Tumor vaccines

As an immunotherapy strategy, cancer vaccines primarily activate the immune system by targeting TAAs and tumor-specific antigens (TSAs). Cancer vaccines include cellular, viral, peptide, and nucleic acid vaccines that activate T cell-mediated immune responses through five key steps: antigen loading, lymph node drainage, DCs uptake, DCs maturation, and MHC-I presentation of antigens to T cells. Enhancing these steps can lead to effective antitumor immunity. Inoculation with dead tumor cells can activate immune responses, suggesting that dead tumor cells may serve as effective cancer vaccines.

Compared to the direct delivery of tumor antigens, nanoparticles can encapsulate antigen molecules, delivering antigenic information to DCs more efficiently and precisely. Nanoparticle-based vaccines have garnered significant attention owing to their efficient antigen delivery and activation of tumor-specific immunity. Nanoliposomal doxorubicin improves the immunosuppressive TME and enhances the expression of immune molecules on tumor cell membranes, prompting tumor membrane antigen vaccines to trigger stronger immune responses and provide more robust immune protection than single doxorubicin therapy alone. Recent studies have designed an innovative whole-tumor-cell vaccine platform using photothermal Cu₂₋ₓSe nanoparticles to encapsulate tumor-associated antigens and endogenous immune-stimulating molecules, triggering DAMPs expression through near-infrared irradiation, offering a new vaccine strategy for personalized cancer therapy. Currently, the main strategy for enhancing the immune cascade in nanoparticle vaccines focuses on the co-delivery of adjuvants with antigens. However, Zhao et al. designed biodegradable hybrid nanoparticles (MP) composed of manganese oxide nanoparticles and cationic polymers, which effectively loaded antigens, drained to lymph nodes, were internalized by DCs, induced DC maturation via the cGAS-STING pathway, and promoted ICD to induce in situ production of TAAs. This approach of using MP hybrid nanoparticles offers a new strategy for constructing efficient tumor nanovaccines.

The development of mRNA delivery systems with innate immune-stimulating functions has become a major focus in mRNA vaccine research. For example, the CATCH strategy uses LNP-mRNA (CD40L) to trigger local tumor ICD, thereby clearing primary tumors and inhibiting distant metastases. The POctS platform amplifies anti-tumor responses by activating the STING pathway, while BioNTech's personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccine, Autogene cevumeran, has shown promising efficacy in preclinical studies, with combination therapy significantly reducing cancer recurrence. To enhance immunogenicity while maintaining safety, M. Zhang et al. developed PD-LNP, which boosts mRNA vaccine efficacy through the STING pathway, and H. Yang et al. optimized LNPs with innovative amphoteric ionizable lipids (AOL), improving tissue targeting and immune characteristics while reducing liver inflammation. These studies demonstrate the vast potential of personalized mRNA vaccines in enhancing T cell responses, improving therapeutic efficacy, and reducing recurrence risk, particularly in refractory cancers.

Cancer vaccines have demonstrated promising efficacy in both preclinical and clinical studies. However, their effectiveness is limited in certain patients due to T cell dysfunction within the immunosuppressive TME. Combining vaccines with ICIs is a promising strategy to enhance therapeutic outcomes. Virus-based vaccines such as HPV vaccines have successfully prevented several virus-related cancers. Vaccine development for non-viral tumors is shifting toward precision immunotherapy by targeting TAAs and TSAs, underscoring the trend toward personalized cancer vaccination strategies.

Targeting immune cells to enhance antitumor activity

Immune cells are indispensable orchestrators of antitumor responses, and their functional integrity and survival are pivotal determinants of immunotherapeutic efficacy. Within the TME, tumor cells exploit a repertoire of immunosuppressive mechanisms to induce exhaustion and apoptosis of effector T cells and NK cells, thereby dampening cytotoxic immunity. Consequently, therapeutic strategies aimed at neutralizing suppressive signals and modulating immune cell death pathways hold substantial promise for reinvigorating immune effector function and sustaining their persistence in hostile tumor niches.

Targeting T cell exhaustion

Immune checkpoints are crucial molecules and signaling pathways that regulate the intensity of immune activation and participate in immune tolerance, immune response regulation, and self-defense processes. In the TME, tumor cells manipulate immune checkpoint signals to suppress T cell function, thus evading immune system clearance. Tumor cells overexpress PD-L1, which binds to PD-1 on T cells and suppresses their activity. This leads to T cell exhaustion and may also trigger T cell death via apoptosis or ferroptosis, significantly weakening the antitumor immune response. ICIs targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway are breakthroughs in cancer immunotherapy. By blocking this signaling pathway, PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors not only restore T cell recognition by tumor cells, but also reverse their exhausted state. These inhibitors significantly enhance T cell proliferation and cytokine secretion, thereby boosting T cell cytotoxicity. Furthermore, PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors activate systemic immune responses by improving the tumor microenvironment, further enhancing therapeutic efficacy. PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors have significant efficacy against various solid tumors, including non-small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal cell carcinoma. However, these treatments are ineffective in some patients, likely because of the heterogeneity of tumor immune escape and the complexity of the tumor microenvironment. Thus, combining other immune therapies has become a key strategy for improving their efficacy.

Targeting costimulatory receptors also plays an important role in enhancing T cell antitumor immunity. The activation of these receptors significantly enhances T cell proliferation, effector function, and persistence, reversing exhaustion-related cell death. CD137 is a key costimulatory molecule in T and NK cells, and CD137 agonists enhance CD8+T cell tumor infiltration, while protecting them from apoptosis. CD137 signaling stimulates the proliferation and differentiation of exhausted T cell precursors via NF-κB subunits RelA and cRel and Tox-dependent chromatin remodeling mechanisms. Furthermore, the combination of CD137 agonists and anti-PD-1 therapy significantly improves therapeutic outcomes. Currently, anti-CD137, anti-OX40 monoclonal antibodies, and antibodies against CD27 and GITR are in clinical trials. Preliminary data suggest that these drugs exhibit stronger antitumor activity when combined with PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors. Additionally, costimulatory receptor agonists can induce significant antitumor immune responses independently in certain cases.

T cell exhaustion and its associated death mechanisms are critical for tumor immune escape. By targeting immune checkpoints and activating costimulatory receptors, immunotherapy can effectively reverse T cell exhaustion, restore antitumor function, and extend survival. These strategies have led to significant progress in clinical settings and provide a foundation for optimizing immunotherapy. Future research should explore combination therapies to improve efficacy and overcome resistance. Optimizing immunotherapies in the tumor microenvironment will also promote the development of personalized treatments.

Targeting adoptive cell therapy

Adoptive cell immunotherapy enhances the immune function and antitumor effects of patients by isolating, activating, or genetically modifying autologous or allogeneic immune cells and reintroducing them into the patient’s body. This therapy effectively overcomes tumor immune escape mechanisms, significantly boosts immune responses, and has emerged as a promising cancer treatment approach.

A deep understanding of the survival challenges and functional exhaustion of adoptively transferred T cells within the TME is pivotal for the precise optimization and clinical translation of next-generation T cell immunotherapies. Zhang et al. discovered a novel T cell death mechanism known as “ammonia-induced death”. During CD8+T cell proliferation, ammonia, a byproduct of glutamine metabolism, induces T cell death by damaging lysosomes and mitochondria. Ammonia enters lysosomes via the RHCG transporter, disrupting membrane structures and activating a cascade of cellular damage. Further investigation into ammonia clearance mechanisms and their regulation within the tumor microenvironment may provide key insights into the optimization of adoptive T cell therapy. Blocking ammonia-induced T cell death can prolong T cell survival and enhance antitumor functionality, thereby improving the efficacy of adoptive T cell therapy. Moreover, combining ammonia metabolism intervention with CAR-T therapy or ICIs could potentially produce synergistic effects, enhancing the durability and effectiveness of adoptive T cell therapy.

NK cells are indispensable effectors of the innate immune system and exert potent antitumor activities through both cytolytic and immunoregulatory mechanisms. The adoptive transfer of NK cells carries a relatively low risk of GVHD and CRS. The mitochondrial apoptotic pathway plays a pivotal role in the antitumor activity of NK cells. Modulating the initiation of mitochondrial apoptosis can increase cancer cell susceptibility to NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity. Combining NK cell therapy with antitumor agents can further enhance NK cell-mediated antitumor effects. With a deeper understanding of tumor immune escape mechanisms, the clinical application of adoptive cell immunotherapy has shown great promise. Future studies should focus on optimizing immune cell function and durability, reducing immune cell death, enhancing cytotoxicity, improving the immunosuppressive microenvironment, and combining metabolic modulation with other immune therapies.

Targeting treg cells

Tregs act as multifaceted suppressors of antitumor immunity and are critical immunoregulatory nodes requiring precise therapeutic modulation in cancer immunotherapy. Targeted strategies against Tregs have been proposed to relieve immunosuppression and enhance antitumor immunity. The chemotherapeutic drug cyclophosphamide (CTX) selectively depletes Treg cells, restores effector T cell function, and enhances antitumor immune responses; however, the efficacy of CTX is influenced by dosage, administration route, and treatment protocol, requiring further optimization to balance efficacy and side effects. A common strategy involves targeting the high expression of the IL-2 receptor alpha chain (CD25) in Treg cells. Daclizumab was initially used to prevent kidney transplant rejection and was later used in Treg depletion studies in patients with cancer. Daclizumab effectively reduces peripheral blood Treg levels and enhances vaccine-induced T cell responses. However, because CD25 is also expressed on activated CD4+ and CD8+T cells, nonspecific depletion may lead to immune dysregulation, limiting the application of daclizumab.

To overcome the limitations of traditional CD25 antibodies, next-generation antibodies such as anti-CD25 F(ab')2 fragments conjugated with photoactivatable dyes (IRDye 700DX) were developed. Under near-infrared light, this technique allows the precise depletion of Treg cells in the TME while preserving systemic immune function. This approach significantly activates the function of CD8+T cells and NK cells, extending survival. Similar photoimmunotherapies targeting EGFR have entered Phase I clinical trials, laying the foundation for the clinical translation of this technology. Additionally, Ontak, an anti-CD25 antibody conjugated with diphtheria toxin, has been approved by the FDA for the treatment of multiple cancers, and enhances immune responses by systemically depleting CD25+Treg cells. However, its clinical application is limited owing to the potential systemic immune imbalance. Future research should focus on precisely depleting tumor-associated Treg cells without compromising normal immune function.

Studies have highlighted the significant effect of oxidative stress on Treg cell apoptosis in the TME. Owing to a weaker NRF2-related antioxidant system, Treg cells are particularly vulnerable to free radicals in the tumor microenvironment, leading to their apoptosis. Notably, apoptotic Treg cells release ATP via CD39 and CD73, which is converted into adenosine, and through the adenosine A2a receptor pathway, they mediate immune suppression, rather than through typical immune-suppressive factors. Oxidative stress affects Treg cell survival and immune suppression, and promotes immune escape in tumors. Targeting oxidative stress to enhance Treg cell apoptosis could improve immunotherapeutic outcomes. Future studies should focus on modulating oxidative stress or boosting antioxidant responses to improve cancer treatment strategies.

The depletion of Treg cells holds promise in tumor immunotherapy by activating effector T and NK cells, enhancing antitumor immunity. However, balancing immune suppression and avoiding dysregulation remain challenging. Optimizing treatment and exploring strategies such as photoimmunotherapy and oxidative stress modulation will improve efficacy and reduce side effects, thereby advancing personalized cancer therapy.

Targeting immune cell ferroptosis pathways

Given the crucial role of immune cell ferroptosis in regulating antitumor effects, numerous studies suggest that targeting ferroptosis pathways in immune cells represents a promising strategy in cancer therapy.

Compared to PMN-MDSCs, TAMs in the TME showed less evidence of spontaneous ferroptosis. TAMs exhibit varying sensitivities to ferroptosis, with M2-type TAMs being more vulnerable owing to their lower production of iNOS and NO. NO free radicals can interact with lipid metabolites produced by lipid peroxidation, inhibiting ferroptosis and making M2 TAMs more susceptible to ferroptosis. Targeting TAMs to enhance current cell-based immunotherapies may be a viable strategy. Repolarization of M2 to M1 and the induction of ferroptosis could be an effective therapeutic approach.

Tumor-associated NK cells exhibit ferroptosis characteristics, including lipid peroxidation and oxidative damage. This is associated with impaired glucose metabolism in the NK cells within the TME, leading to functional deficits. NRF2 activation regulates the expression of various antioxidant molecules, thereby rescuing NK cell function. In vivo inhibition of ferroptosis using liproxstatin-1 increases tumor NK cell survival. Therefore, ferroptosis plays a protumor role in NK cell survival and function within the TME.

Studies on ferroptosis in CD8+T cells suggested its therapeutic benefits in cancer. CD8+T cells exert antitumor activity by releasing IFN-γ, which sensitizes cancer cells to ferroptosis. However, the role of ferroptosis in T cells remains unclear. A comprehensive screening study identified the metabolic vulnerability of tumor-associated CD8+T cells to ferroptosis. Activated CD8+ T cells were found to be more sensitive to ferroptosis inducers (such as GPX4 inhibitors) than cancer cells. However, increased CD36 expression in CD8+T cells within the TME promotes fatty acid uptake, induces lipid peroxidation, and activates ferroptosis, impairing effector T cell function and antitumor activity. The immunological impact of ferroptosis has been highlighted in neighboring immune cells on CD8+T cells, demonstrating that ferroptosis in PMN-MDSCs within the TME inhibits tumor-associated T cell function, thereby suppressing CD8+T cell antitumor activity. Therefore, complex intercellular interactions within the TME may influence the ferroptosis sensitivity of both immune and cancer cells.

In contrast to CD8+T cells, the role of ferroptosis in CD4+T cell function remains unclear. Although Tregs are less vulnerable to ferroptosis than tumor-specific CD8+T cells, the activation of Tregs leads to a loss of immune suppression when GPX4 is deleted, and ferroptosis inhibitors can restore this suppressive function. Therefore, when considering the therapeutic potential of ferroptosis inducers and inhibitors in cancer treatment, it is crucial to assess the vulnerability of different cancer types to ferroptosis and determine whether the TME is infiltrated by T cells.

Discussion and future perspective

This review emphasizes the critical role of various forms of cell death in shaping the TIME and their influence on immune responses, particularly through ICD induction. With the deepening of research, apoptosis, necroptosis, pyroptosis, autophagy and ferroptosis have been clearly recognized as RCD modalities that play central roles in tumorigenesis, immune evasion, and therapeutic response. Importantly, these pathways can amplify immunogenicity through the release of DAMPs, thereby reshaping the TIME and profoundly influencing cancer progression. Recently, emerging forms of cell death, such as PANoptosis, cuproptosis, and disulfidptosis, have been implicated as critical regulators of immune responses within the TIME. With the discovery of ICD and its capacity to trigger antitumor immune responses, targeting cell death pathways to convert “cold” tumors into “hot” tumors is becoming a major direction in cancer immunotherapy. Notably, cell death is not confined to tumor cells but also involves immune and stromal cells, and their dynamic interplay is a key determinant of whether the TIME becomes immunosuppressive or immunostimulatory. However, the marked heterogeneity across tumor types and the differential sensitivity of immune cells to death signals may fundamentally reshape their roles in tumor progression and immune regulation. Against this backdrop, the rapid development of single-cell and spatial multi-omics technologies provides unprecedented opportunities to chart the atlas of cell death interactions within the TIME. These approaches not only reveal the spatial distribution of distinct cell death programs but also capture the complex crosstalk between tumor and immune cells, offering a panoramic view to decipher the triggers of ICD and the mechanisms of TIME remodeling. ICD has emerged as a promising therapeutic target with unique potential in cancer immunotherapy. A range of ICD-inducing strategies has shown encouraging results in preclinical and early clinical studies; however, their clinical translation remains challenging. In particular, off-target effects and toxicity to normal tissues require rigorous evaluation. Striking a balance between effective immune activation and avoiding excessive inflammation remains a long-standing challenge. The advancement of precision therapies also depends on robust biomarker systems to support patient stratification and predict responses to cell death inducers. Moreover, optimizing combination regimens, reducing adverse effects, and continuously monitoring DAMP release and its systemic immune consequences are essential to ensure safety and efficacy. In summary, dissecting the intricate crosstalk between different forms of cell death and immune cells will deepen our understanding of TIME remodeling and tumor immune evasion, while uncovering critical ICD targets for therapeutic intervention. Future research should prioritize the integration of single-cell and spatial multi-omics data to systematically map cell death interaction networks, and combine these insights with innovative delivery systems and patient stratification strategies. Such efforts will be instrumental in driving the precision and clinical translation of next-generation cancer immunotherapies.
