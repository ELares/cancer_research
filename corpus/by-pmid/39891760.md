# Elevated SAMD3 expression in T cells predicts improved survival in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma patients.

## Abstract

**OBJECTIVE**: Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) has an immune-suppressive tumor microenvironment that contributes to resistance to immunotherapy. This study aimed to demonstrate that elevated sterile alpha motif domain-containing protein 3 (SAMD3) expression in effector CD8+ T cells was associated with improved survival in PDAC patients.

**DESIGN**: We investigated the heterogeneity and gene expression profiles of T cells using a single-cell RNA sequencing (sc-RNA-seq) dataset comprised of human PDAC samples. SAMD3 mRNA expression was further evaluated in a tumor-specific OT-I/ovalbumin (OVA) mouse model. SAMD3 levels and their clinical significance were evaluated via immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis.

**RESULTS**: SAMD3 was highly expressed in cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, with expression significantly downregulated during T cell exhaustion. SAMD3 levels were positively correlated with CD8+ T cell function. In PDAC patients, high SAMD3 levels in T cells were associated with improved overall survival (OS), disease-free survival (DFS), and T cell infiltration. A patient exhibiting partial response to combination immunotherapy also showed high SAMD3 levels in T cells.

**CONCLUSION**: SAMD3 is a biomarker of T cell function, with elevated expression in T cells predicting improved survival. These findings highlight the potential of precision immunotherapy approaches for treating PDAC.

Introduction

PDAC, which accounts for up to 95% of all pancreatic cancer cases, is the third leading cause of cancer-related death globally in both males and females. Over the last decade, the mortality rate has continued to rise, with a 5-year OS rate of approximately 10%. Currently, surgery remains the only curative option for PDAC patients. However, fewer than 20% of the cases are eligible for resection due to the lack of screening tests for early detection. Systematic treatment options for advanced or recurrent PDAC primarily include chemotherapy and radiotherapy in patients without metastatic disease, and targeted therapies in patients with unstable microsatellites, germline homologous recombination deficiencies, or fusion genes. Unfortunately, poor prognosis is further exacerbated by resistance to therapy and high recurrence rates.

Immunotherapy has significantly improved the long-term survival of cancer patients by inducing robust anti-tumor immune responses, leading to the adoption of immunologic checkpoint blockade (ICB) as a standard treatment for more than 20 types of malignancies. However, ICB has not been integrated into treatment protocols for PDAC. The use of ICB alone or in combination with chemotherapy has resulted in disappointing results, with minimal improvement in response and survival outcomes. Over the years, tumor genomic and immune signature analyses have provided insights into immunotherapy refractoriness in PDAC, which was reported to be associated with the inherent immune-suppressive tumor microenvironment, inter-tumoral and intra-tumoral heterogeneity, crosstalk between tumor cells and tumor stroma, and the strong desmoplastic reaction, which may impede the efficacy of therapeutic agents. The immune microenvironment in pancreatic cancer is noticeably “cold,” characterized by the infiltration of inactive CD8+ T cells and myeloid cells. Although CD8+ T cells are crucial for anti-tumor immunity, they were sparsely presented within the PDAC environment; however, their prevalence was heterogeneous across different patients. Sc-RNA-seq analysis indicated that most tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells in PDAC had an exhausted phenotype. It was reported that higher levels of CD8+ T cells were significantly associated with longer survival in PDAC patients.

Here, we investigated the heterogeneity and gene expression profile of T cells using a previously published sc-RNA-seq dataset (CRA001160) that included 24 human PDAC samples. This analysis revealed that SAMD3 was most highly expressed in effector CD8+ T cells. Conversely, SAMD3 was either absent or expressed at extremely low levels in exhausted CD8+ T cells or CD4+ T cells.

SAMD3 is a member of the SAMD protein family, which is involved in various cell biological functions, such as protein–protein interactions, translational regulation, and cell cycle control. A previous study has shown that Samd3 was specifically expressed in NK cells, memory CD8+ T cells, and precursor exhausted T cells during viral infections in mouse models. Unfortunately, the functions and molecular mechanisms of SAMD3 remain unclear.

In this study, we demonstrated that Samd3 expression was upregulated in effector CD8+ T cells and significantly downregulated during the establishment of T cell exhaustion in a tumor-specific OT-I/OVA mouse model. SAMD3 levels were positively correlated with CD8+ T cell function. High SAMD3 expression in T cells was associated with an improved OS, DFS, and T cell infiltration in PDAC patients. In contrast, SAMD3 expression had no clinical significance in tumor cells. Notably, a patient who experienced a partial response to combination immunotherapy exhibited high SAMD3 expression in T cells. Collectively, we identified SAMD3 as a biomarker of T cell function, with high expression in T cells predicting better survival and offering new insights into potential precision immunotherapy for PDAC.

Methods

Sc-RNA-seq analysis

The raw sc-RNA-seq data, comprising 24 human PDAC samples and 16 non-tumor tissue controls, were downloaded from the Genome Sequence Archive database (https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/gsa/browse/CRA001160). Raw sequencing fastq files were processed using Cell Ranger software with default and recommended parameters. The output was then imported into the Seurat R package for subsequent analysis. Low-quality cells, defined as cells with fewer than 250 or more than 2500 expressed genes, or cells with a mitochondrial gene expression fraction exceeding 0.2, were excluded. Highly variable genes were analyzed using the FindVariableFeatures function in the Seurat R package using the “mean.var.plot” method. PCA dimensionality reduction was performed with default parameters using the RunPCA function. For nearest-neighbor graph construction and clustering, the FindNeighbors and FindClusters functions were used, with “dims” set to “1:15”. Finally, these cells were visualized in t-SNE plots. Cell types were annotated based on typical markers: CD3D, CD3E, CD4, CD8A, CD8B (T cell); FOXP3 (Treg); IFNG, EOMES, GZMB (cytotoxic T cell); PDCD1, TIGIT, HAVCR2 (exhausted T cell); CD79A, CD79B (B cell); CD68, CD163 (macrophage); VWF, FCN3 (endothelial cell); DCN, PDGFR, LUM (fibroblast); TPSAB1, TPSB2, CPA3 (mast cell); EPCAM (epithelial cell); FXYD2, LEFTY1, SLC4A4 (ductal cell type 1); TFF1, TFF2, CEACAM (ductal cell type 2); TTR, PCSKIN (endocrine cell); RGS5 (stellate cell) and PRSS1 (acinar cell).

OT-I/OVA model

OT-I TCR transgenic mice [6–10 weeks] were killed, and spleens and mesenteric lymph nodes were collected and passed through a 70 µm cell strainer (BD Biosciences). Red blood cells were removed using ACK lysis buffer (155 mmol/L ammonium chloride, 10 mmol/L potassium bicarbonate, 1 mmol/L EDTA; Sigma). CD8+ T cells were isolated using a Dynabeads mouse CD8 negative selection kit (Invitrogen). Cells with high purity (> 90%) were washed with PBS and cultured in complete RPMI-1640 medium with 10% FBS, 20 mmol/L HEPES, 1 mmol/L sodium pyruvate, 0.05 mmol/L 2-mercaptoethanol, 2 mmol/L L-glutamine, 100 mg/mL streptomycin, and 100 mg/mL penicillin (Invitrogen). For in vitro stimulation, OT-I cells were resuspended at 2 × 106 cells/mL and stimulated with the SIINFEKL (OVA257-264) peptide (GenScript) at a concentration of 1 µg/mL for 3 days.

Lentiviruses carrying the SIINFEKL epitope were introduced into Panc02 cells, a murine pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma cell line established from C57BL/6 mice. Panc02-OVA tumor cells (2 × 105) were subcutaneously inoculated into the inguinal area of C57BL/6 mice aged 6–8 weeks. Stimulated OT-I cells were transferred (3 × 106) into the mice by intravenous injection. OT-I cells were sorted from tumors three or nine days after injection. For sorting, 15 to 20 mice were pooled. Lymphocytes were enriched by density gradient centrifugation and stained with BV421-conjugated anti-CD8a and FITC-conjugated anti-CD45.1. Primary and secondary sorting were performed to exclude tumor contamination and achieve a purity of more than 98% for OT-I cells after secondary sorting. Finally, about 0.5 million OT-I cells were used for RNA extraction.

C57BL/6 mice were acquired from the Guangdong Medical Lab Animal Center. OT-I transgenic mice were procured from the Jackson Laboratory. All mice were housed under specific pathogen-free conditions and maintained in accordance with animal experimental guidelines at Sun Yat-sen University. Ethical approval for all animal procedures was granted by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Sun Yat-sen University (L102012022005T).

Bulk RNA-seq analysis

The RNA-seq data of PDAC patients from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database were downloaded from the UCSC Xena website (http://xena.ucsc.edu). The “Correlation Analysis” function in the Gene Expression Profiling Interactive Analysis website (GEPIA; http://gepia.cancer-pku.cn) was used to analyze the correlation between SAMD3 mRNA expression and other genes, including CD8, CD69, CCL4, CCL5, PRF1, GZMA, GZMB, and GZMK . Pearson’s coefficient was used as the correlation coefficient for this analysis.

Differentially expressed gene enrichment

Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified using the “DESeq2” R package in the TCGA PDAC cohort. PDAC patients were classified into two groups based on their SAMD3 TPM expression values. Specifically, the top quartile of patients, sorted by descending TPM values, was classified into the high SAMD3 expression group, while the bottom quartile of patients was classified into the low SAMD3 expression group. Then, DEGs with a P value < 0.05 and a log fold change value > 2 were selected. These DEGs were then analyzed using the Metascape webtool (http://metascape.org) to perform enrichment analysis. The results of the biological process (GO BP) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) enrichment analyses were displayed in bubble plots.

IHC

The study cohort was comprised of 307 PDAC patients who underwent surgery at Sun Yat-sen University Cancer Center (SYSUCC). None of these patients had received any anti-tumor therapy prior to diagnosis. All patients had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status (ECOG) of 0–1. Formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) samples and corresponding clinical data were collected, and SAMD3 protein levels were detected via IHC staining.

Ethical approval was granted by the SYSUCC Institute Research Ethics Committee, adhering to the International Ethical Guidelines for Biomedical Research Involving Human Subjects (CIOMS) (GZR2022-054). The authors affirmed that human research participants provided informed consent for publication. Written informed consent was obtained from each patient or their legal guardians.

Samples were sectioned into 4–5 mm thick slices, and mounted onto slides, followed by incubation in a 60 °C oven for 2 h. These slides then underwent deparaffinization, rehydration, antigen retrieval, and endogenous peroxidase activity blockade. Subsequently, the slides were incubated in a blocking solution containing 5% horse serum, 3% BSA, and 0.1% Tween-20 in PBS for 1 h at 25 ℃, followed by incubation with anti-SAMD3 (PA5-50,875, Invitrogen) and anti-CD8 (ZA-0508, Zsbio) overnight at 4 °C in a humidified chamber. On the following day, the slides were washed three times with PBS, incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-labeled goat anti-rabbit IgG (SAP-9100, Zsbio) for 1 h at 25 °C, washed again with PBS, stained with 3,3'-diaminobenzidine tetrahydrochloride (DAB), and counterstained with hematoxylin.

Criteria used for the quantification the staining: (1) The expression of SAMD3 expression in the cytoplasm of T cells or tumor cells was separately evaluated; (2) Staining at the margin of the tissue was not included; (3) The evaluation involved considering the staining intensity and the percentage of stained cells; (3) The intensity was semi-quantitatively evaluated: no staining, or very faint staining in less than 10% of the cells (scored 0), faint or weak staining (scored 1), moderate staining (scored 2), intense staining (scored 3); (4) The score for each tissue was calculated by multiplying the staining intensity value by the percentage of stained cells; (5) Three independent pathologists, who were blinded to the clinical information of the patients, assessed and scored the staining. The final score was determined as the average score provided by the three pathologists; (6) The IHC cut-off for classifying target protein expression as high or low was determined through receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis. Patients with scores exceeding the obtained cut-off value were categorized as having high expression, while those below were classified as having low expression.

Immune infiltration

CD8+ T cell infiltration levels were analyzed using the TIMER2 webtool (http://timer.cistrome.org/). The TIMER2 web server provides more robust methods for estimating immune infiltration levels in TCGA tumor samples. This tool also integrates different estimated approaches, such as CIBERSORT, MCPCOUNTER, QUANTISEQ, and XCELL.

Survival analysis

We used R software with R packages “survival and survminer” to analyze the survival of the SYSUCC PDAC cohort. The IHC cutoff for classifying target protein expression as high or low was determined via receiver operating characteristic curve (ROC) analysis. Patients with scores exceeding the obtained cutoff value were categorized as having high expression levels, while those with scores below the cutoff were classified as having low expression levels. Patients lacking sufficient clinical information were excluded from the analysis.

The GEPIA2 webtool (http://gepia2.cancer-pku.cn/#index) was used to analyze the survival of the TCGA PDAC cohort. Patients were divided into high- or low- SAMD3 groups based on the median log-normalized expression of SAMD3.

OS and DFS were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method and assessed using the log-rank test. OS was defined as the duration from diagnosis to death. DFS was defined as the interval from diagnosis to the first progression, relapse, or cancer-related death.

Statistical analysis

Adequate sample sizes were employed to ensure robust statistical analyses, which were conducted utilizing GraphPad Prism and SPSS software. Combined data were presented as either mean ± SD or mean ± SEM values. A two-tailed Student's t-test was used to compare two means. One-way ANOVA was applied for comparisons involving multiple datasets with a single control group. When data were not normally distributed, the two-tailed Mann–Whitney U test was used to compare results between two independent groups. Statistical significance was determined at P < 0.05, as described in the figure legends.

Results

SAMD3 was highly expressed in cytotoxic CD8+ T cells

We investigated the heterogeneity and gene expression profiles of 57,530 cells from a previously published sc-RNA-seq dataset (CRA001160). The PDAC microenvironment consisted of various cell types, including T cell, B cell, epithelial cell, endocrine cell, endothelial cell, fibroblast, macrophage, etc. (Fig. 1A). SAMD3 expression was predominantly observed in T cells (Fig. 1B). We then focused on a patient with abundant tumor-infiltrating immune cells for further analysis. CD8+ T cells were categorized as cytotoxic or exhausted cell types based on typical markers. As shown in Fig. 1C, SAMD3 expression was higher in cytotoxic CD8+ T cells than in other cell types (Fig. 1D). However, SAMD3 expression was absent or extremely low in exhausted CD8+ T cells or CD4+ T cells (Fig. 1E). Moreover, a gene expression heatmap suggested that cytotoxic CD8+ T cells were characterized by high expression of SAMD3, IFNG, EOMES, and GZMB, while exhausted CD8+ T cells were characterized by high expression of PDCD1, TIGIT, and CXCL13 (Fig. 1F).

Samd3 was downregulated during the establishment of T-cell exhaustion

To further investigate the SAMD3 expression profile, we utilized the OT-I/OVA mouse model, where OT-I TCR transgenic T cells could specifically recognize the surrogate tumor antigen OVA expressed in Panc02 cells. Mice bearing Panc02-OVA tumors received activated OT-I cells via tail-vein injection, and OT-I cells were isolated either three or nine days after injection (early or late stage). Naive OT-I cells, which did not recognize or kill tumor cells, served as controls (Fig. 2A).

As shown in Fig. 2B, the adoptive transfer of OT-I cells delayed tumor growth slightly in the early stage, but the tumors subsequently grew at an accelerated pace. OT-I cells from early-stage tumors displayed an effector phenotype, characterized by the low expression of co-inhibitory receptors such as PD-1 and TIM-3. In contrast, OT-I cells from late-stage tumors displayed an exhausted phenotype, marked by the increased expression of these co-inhibitory receptors (Fig. 2C).

We sorted OT-I cells at different stages for bulk RNA-seq analysis. OT-I cells from early-stage tumors exhibited transcriptional profiles similar to those of effector T cells, with high expression of effector molecules such as Ifng, Il2, Gzma, and Gzmb. In contrast, OT-I cells from late-stage tumors displayed exhausted transcriptional profiles, characterized by the elevated expression of co-inhibitory receptors, such as Tigit, Havcr2, Lag3, and Ctla4, and attenuated expression of effector cytokines (Fig. 2D). Samd3 was expressed at low levels in naive cells, significantly upregulated in effector cells, and downregulated in exhausted cells (Fig. 2E).

SAMD3 level was positively correlated with the function of CD8+ T cells

To explore whether SAMD3 contributes to the function of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, we explored the correlation between SAMD3 and cytotoxic genes in the TCGA PDAC bulk RNA-seq cohort. SAMD3 expression was positively correlated with chemokine and cytotoxic factors, such as CCL4, CCL5, PRF1, GZMA, GZMB, and GZMK (Fig. 3A). Patients were then categorized into high- or low-SAMD3 expression groups, and DEGs were enriched using the Metascape web tool. Interestingly, we found that SAMD3 was mainly involved in biological processes and pathways related to immune reactions. In GO BP enrichment analysis, SAMD3 was found to be positively correlated with adaptive immune responses, production of immune response mediators, and immunoglobin-mediated immune responses (Fig. 3B). Similarly, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis revealed that SAMD3 was linked to cytokine-cytokine receptor interactions, chemokine signaling pathways, and T-cell receptor signaling pathways (Fig. 3C). These findings suggest that SAMD3 might regulate T cell function and play a crucial role in the immune microenvironment of PDAC.

SAMD3 expression in T cells indicated a favorable prognosis

We assembled a cohort of 307 PDAC patients and conducted IHC staining using a tissue microarray. SAMD3 expression was evaluated separately in T cells and tumor cells. The SAMD3 score for each tissue sample was calculated by multiplying the staining intensity by the percentage of positively stained cells. The average of the scores provided by three independent pathologists was determined to be the final score.

The SAMD3 level was categorized as either low or high (Fig. 4A). The association of SAMD3 expression with clinicopathological features of PDAC patients is summarized in Table 1. Prognostic factors were analyzed using the Cox proportional hazards model, with results summarized in Table 2.

Kaplan–Meier analysis revealed that high SAMD3 expression in T cells was significantly associated with prolonged OS (Fig. 4B) as well as DFS (Fig. 4C). However, SAMD3 levels in tumor cells showed no correlation with patient survival outcomes (Fig. 4D-E). An analysis of data from the TCGA database supported our findings, as SAMD3 mRNA expression was not associated with prognosis (Fig. 4F-G). These data demonstrated that SAMD3 expression in T cells indicated a favorable prognosis, and may help to identify PDAC patients who could benefit from immunotherapy.

SAMD3 expression was positively correlated with CD8+ T cell infiltration

As described earlier, SAMD3 may play an important role in shaping the PDAC immune microenvironment. We explored the infiltration of CD8+ T cells using online tools. The results revealed a significant positive correlation between SAMD3 expression and CD8+ T cell infiltration across multiple databases, including TIMER (Fig. 5A), CIBERSORT (Fig. 5B), MCPCOUNTER (Fig. 5C), QUANTISEQ (Fig. 5D), and XCELL (Fig. 5E). These findings were consistent with our cohort data, where SAMD3 protein expression was positively correlated with the number of tumor-infiltrating CD8 + T cells (Fig. 5F-G).

Clinical response to combinational immunotherapy

Neoadjuvant therapy for locally advanced PDAC typically involves chemotherapy, in combination with radiotherapy, immunotherapy, and targeted therapy. However, no standard regimens have been recommended for such cases. At our center, immunotherapy is considered based on disease characteristics, patient preference, and financial considerations.

A 55-year-old male presented with abdominal pain in April 2023. CT scan revealed a 58 × 48 mm mass in the body of the pancreas, invading the splenic vein and encasing several arteries and veins. Fine needle biopsy suggested the diagnosis of PDAC. Unfortunately, we did not obtain enough tissues for further IHC staining. The patient initially underwent four cycles of chemotherapy with the FOLFIRINOX regimen, resulting in stable disease. Subsequently, the treatment was switched to a combination of the AG regimen plus PD-1 Ab (Triplizumab) and Lenvatinib. Notably, CT scan performed about ten weeks after initiating this combination immunotherapy showed a 50% reduction in the total tumor burden, indicating a partial response. He received six cycles of the AG regimen with PD-1 Ab and Lenvatinib, followed by an additional twelve cycles of combination of PD-1 Ab and Lenvatinib. Follow-up CT scans showed no signs of tumor progression. In August 2024, he underwent laparoscopic irreversible electroporation ablation (nano-knife) therapy and biopsy. Surgery was not an option because of the involvement of the tumor with surrounding blood vessels. The patient had no hepatobiliary disorders, rashes, diarrhea, or other signs of toxicity during therapy. There was no significant change in his appetite and body weight.

The schematic representation of the treatment timeline is shown in Fig. 6A. Representative CT images and tumor diameters are shown in Fig. 6B-C. A significant decrease in serum CA199 levels was observed (Fig. 6D). The patient showed abundant tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells and high levels of SAMD3 expression in these cells, indicating the activation of antitumor immunity (Fig. 6E).

Discussion

Immunotherapy has not yet been incorporated into the standard treatment regimen for PDAC because of its low response rate. Our analysis of sc-RNA-seq data revealed a significant presence of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells in some patients. However, most of these cells exhibited an inactive or exhausted phenotype, which was consistent with previous findings. The “cold” immune microenvironment of PDAC contributes to resistance to immunotherapy. It is necessary to clarify the mechanism of T cell dysfunction and restore the activity of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells.

In this study, we explored the heterogeneity and gene expression profile of T cells in the PDAC immune microenvironment and revealed that SAMD3 was specifically expressed in cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. Further, using the OT-I/OVA mouse model, we demonstrated that Samd3 RNA levels were elevated in tumor-specific OT-I cells when T cells transitioned from a naive to an effector state, while Samd3 expression was significantly decreased in exhausted T cells.

Previous studies have shown that the SAMD protein family was involved in various cell biological functions, including protein–protein interactions, translational regulation, and cell cycle control. SAMD family members were also reported to bind to RNAs and lipids. Moreover, mutations or abnormal expression of certain SAMD family members, such as human sterile α motif and HD domain-containing protein 1 (SAMHD1), were associated with various types of malignancies. Only one study suggested that Samd3 was expressed in mouse NK cells and T cells. Researchers have attempted to explore the regulation and mechanism of Smad3, but no definitive conclusions were reached. Our gene enrichment analysis showed that SAMD3 was closely related to the T cell-mediated immune response, which was consistent with previous findings. This suggests that SAMD3 may play an important role in regulating CD8+ T cell function. Further research is necessary to clarify the precise molecular mechanisms involved in this process.

We analyzed the correlation between SAMD3 expression levels and clinical outcomes in PDAC patients. Interestingly, in the sc-RNA-seq data, SAMD3 was only expressed in CD8+ T cells, with no expression detected in other cell types, including epithelial cells, CD4+ T cells, and B cells. However, IHC analysis showed positive SAMD3 staining in T cells as well as tumor cells. This discrepancy might be attributable to the intricate regulation of gene expression, involving transcription, translation, and turnover of mRNAs and proteins. Alternatively, it could be attributable to non-specific staining, a limitation of the IHC technique. Importantly, only SAMD3 levels in T cells were associated with survival, while its expression in tumor cells had no prognostic value. These findings suggest that the regulation of SAMD3 involves intricate and still largely unknown mechanisms. Future studies involving full-length transcriptome sequencing and proteomics might provide deeper insights into these regulatory processes.

We demonstrated a positive correlation between SAMD3 expression and CD8+ T cell infiltration in PDAC. We further evaluated the formation of the tertiary lymphoid structure (TLS) in the PDAC tumor microenvironment. Interestingly, SAMD3 was expressed in both scattered CD8+ T cells and CD8+ T cells enriched in TLS. Sporadic SAMD3 expression was also observed in certain tumors lacking TLS. However, no correlation was found between SAMD3 expression and TLS formation (data not shown). Notably, a patient who showed a partial response to combination immunotherapy exhibited abundant CD8+ T cell infiltration and high SAMD3 expression in these cells. These data suggested that SAMD3 may play an important role in maintaining CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity, rather than cell migration or recruitment.

Our study has some limitations. First, findings in mouse models which focus on a highly controlled immune environment may not equivalently translate to the complex immune landscape in human PDAC. The validation of these data in animal models with larger sample sizes or, ideally, in clinical settings, is essential to ensure their relevance in the treatment of PDAC. Second, though some pathways were mentioned in the current study, the precise molecular mechanisms by which SAMD3 regulates T cell function such as activation, differentiation, cytotoxicity, and exhaustion remain unclear. We will further elucidate these mechanisms by using transgenic mouse models and various in vitro and in vivo experiments. Third, immunotherapy has achieved limited success in PDAC. Our study suggested that SAMD3 may serve as a biomarker to identify PDAC patients who might benefit from immunotherapy, monitor treatment efficacy, and track changes in the immune landscape during therapy. Integration of SAMD3 into the current treatment regimen of PDAC needs to be further explored.

In conclusion, we identified high SAMD3 expression levels in effector CD8+ T cells, establishing it as a potential marker for predicting survival in PDAC patients. Future studies to elucidate the mechanisms by which SAMD3 regulates T cell activation, exhaustion, and anti-tumor immunity could pave the way for novel therapeutic strategies targeting SAMD3 in PDAC.
