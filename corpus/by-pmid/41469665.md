# Gut microbiota reshaping the pancreatic cancer immune microenvironment: new avenues for immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Pancreatic cancer remains one of the deadliest malignancies, primarily due to its highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) and poor response to conventional therapies. Increasing evidence highlights the gut microbiota as a pivotal regulator of antitumor immunity, modulating T cell activation, macrophage polarization, and dendritic cell function. Microbial communities and their metabolites can either inhibit or enhance immune surveillance, thereby influencing the efficacy of immunotherapies such as immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and CAR-T cell therapy. Approaches including dietary modulation, probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and microbial metabolite supplementation show promise in restoring immune homeostasis and improving treatment outcomes. Additionally, gut microbiome profiling has emerged as a potential source of biomarkers for predicting therapeutic response and immune-related adverse events. This review summarizes current insights into microbiota-immune interactions in pancreatic cancer, emphasizes microbiome-targeted therapeutic strategies, and explores future opportunities for precision immunotherapy guided by microbial modulation.

Gut microbiota reshaping the pancreatic cancer immune microenvironment: new avenues for immunotherapy

Introduction

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) ranks among the deadliest cancers, with the five-year survival remaining below 10% even after progress in surgical techniques, chemotherapy, and targeted treatments. In contrast to malignancies like melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer, PDAC responds poorly to immune checkpoint blockade, which is mainly attributed to its distinctively immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). The PDAC TME is characterized by dense desmoplasia, regulatory immune cell infiltration, and a paucity of effector T cells, all of which collectively dampen effective antitumor immunity. Therefore, unraveling novel mechanisms that reshape the immune landscape of PDAC is critical to improve immunotherapy outcomes.

Recent studies emphasize that gut microbiota functions as an important external regulator, shaping systemic as well as tumor-related immune activities. Beyond its role within the gastrointestinal tract, gut microbiota significantly impacts cancer development by modulating both innate and adaptive immune processes, regulating metabolism, and mediating microbial metabolite signaling. Preclinical studies have demonstrated that gut microbial dysbiosis accelerates PDAC progression by skewing macrophage polarization toward an M2-like phenotype, suppressing NK cell infiltration, and altering CD4⁺ T-cell differentiation within the TME. Conversely, microbial ablation by antibiotics or germ-free mouse models has been shown to restore antitumor immunity, enhance M1 macrophage activity, and drive CD4+T cells toward Th1 polarization, leading to suppressed tumor growth.

Importantly, not all microbiota–cancer interactions are detrimental. Certain commensal or probiotic strains, such as Lactobacillus casei (L. casei) and Lactobacillus reuteri (L. reuteri), have been reported to inhibit TLR4 signaling, promote M1 macrophage polarization, and increase NK cell infiltration, collectively suppressing PDAC progression. In addition, segmented filamentous bacteria (SFB) are capable of driving Th17 cell differentiation, whereas microbial metabolites like short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) regulate the Treg/Th17 equilibrium and enhance sensitivity to immune checkpoint blockade. These observations highlight the dual functions of gut microbiota, which may facilitate immune escape or, conversely, strengthen immune surveillance, indicating that precise microbial modulation holds promise as a new therapeutic avenue for PDAC.

This article provides an overview of the latest progress in elucidating the role of gut microbiota in remodeling the immune landscape of pancreatic cancer. We emphasize mechanistic perspectives on microbiota–immune interactions, with particular attention to macrophages, T cells, NK cells, and dendritic cells (DCs). In addition, we explore novel microbiota-based therapeutic strategies—such as the use of probiotics, prebiotics, nutritional modulation, antibiotics, and fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)—as promising approaches to enhance immunotherapy outcomes in PDAC.

Gut microbiota–driven regulation of immune responses in pancreatic cancer

Gut microbial communities are key determinants of the pancreatic cancer immune milieu, shaping the activity of both innate and adaptive immune compartments. Clarifying these complex interactions is essential for understanding how the microbiota influences antitumor immunity and may provide valuable insights for developing innovative therapeutic strategies (Table 1).

Gut microbiota in innate immune responses

The innate immune system serves as a frontline defense mechanism by identifying microbial threats through the recognition of microbe-associated molecular patterns (MAMPs) via pattern recognition receptors (PRRs). Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) can be broadly divided into several categories, including Toll-like (TLRs), NOD-like (NLRs), RIG-I-like (RLRs), and C-type lectin receptors (CLRs). Typically, TLRs localize to the plasma membrane or endosomal vesicles, while NLRs function within the cytoplasm; collectively, they are essential for detecting microbial components. Once engaged, these receptors activate intracellular signaling cascades that lead to the activation of innate immune responses.

The innate immune system depends on various effector populations, including macrophages, NK cells, and DCs, as well as granulocytic lineages such as neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, and mast cells. Recent studies indicate that intestinal microbial communities are deeply involved in cancer progression by promoting M2-type macrophage polarization through TLR-mediated signaling pathways. Moreover, microbial metabolites, particularly SCFAs, have been shown to enhance TLR3-dependent autophagic activity in cancer cells. This autophagic response boosts the secretion of chemokines like CCL20, which reshapes the tumor microenvironment by attracting macrophages and favoring their differentiation into an M2-like state, thereby supporting tumor development. Of special interest, in a KC model of PDAC, oral or gut-derived bacteria were observed to translocate to the pancreas and actively remodel the TME. Antibiotic-mediated microbial depletion markedly reduced CD206⁺ M2-polarized tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), and at the same time promoted the infiltration of M1-like TAMs, which exhibited elevated MHC-II, CD86, IL-6, and TNF-α levels. In addition, relative to PDAC tumors in untreated mice, those from antibiotic-exposed hosts showed a pronounced reduction in PRR expression and associated downstream signaling components. Collectively, the findings demonstrate that gut microbiota facilitate pancreatic tumor development by promoting M2-type macrophage polarization through TLR-dependent signaling within the TME. Furthermore, gut microbial communities have been implicated in suppressing intratumoral NK cell infiltration and activation, thereby promoting the progression of PDAC. Unlike prior studies that identified intratumoral bacteria in tumor tissues, xenograft models failed to detect microbial colonization within the tumors. This discrepancy suggests an alternative mechanism by which gut microbes may influence distant organs, potentially through microbe-derived components rather than direct bacterial translocation. These microbial products may exert either detrimental or beneficial effects on the host’s antitumor immune response. Gut microbiota-driven alterations in bile acid metabolism have been reported to decrease CXCL16 levels in liver sinusoidal endothelial cells, leading to diminished recruitment and activation of CXCR6+NKT cells in the liver. Future investigations should aim to identify microbial species and metabolites that influence NK cell recruitment and functionality within the tumor milieu, thereby elucidating their roles in PDAC progression.

Nevertheless, growing evidence highlights the beneficial influence of gut microbiota in modulating innate immune activity and enhancing antitumor defense. For example, commensal microbes were reported to enhance T cell–driven antitumor responses in melanoma models receiving radiotherapy, largely via DC activation through TLR4 signaling. Activation of TLR1/TLR2 through bacterial lipoproteins (BLPs) promotes the conversion of monocyte-derived MDSCs toward M1-polarized macrophages, resulting in a marked reduction in tumor growth. Similarly, in pancreatic cancer, L. casei and L. reuteri suppress TLR4 signaling to promote M1 macrophage polarization, which contributes to the inhibition of tumor progression. Evidence suggests that L. reuteri augments NK cell accumulation in the TME, leading to heightened innate immunity and inhibition of PDAC development. Parallel studies suggest that commensal gut bacteria can promote antitumor immunity through DC activation. In melanoma models undergoing radiotherapy, they activate DCs in a TLR4-dependent fashion, which in turn boosts T cell–driven antitumor responses. Consistently, vancomycin-induced alterations in gut microbiota composition enhanced IL-12 production from CD8α⁺ dendritic cells, leading to improved CD8⁺ T-cell cytotoxic activity in murine models of TC1 tumors. Taken together, these results underscore the ability of gut microbiota to reshape the immune landscape of tumors via DC regulation, and suggest that DCs might contribute to microbiota-mediated inhibition of pancreatic cancer.

Contributions of gut microbial communities to adaptive immunity

The gut microbiota play a crucial role in shaping adaptive immune responses within both mucosal and systemic environments. By influencing antigen presentation, cytokine production, and lymphocyte differentiation, microbial communities can modulate the balance between immune activation and tolerance, thereby affecting tumor immune surveillance (Fig. 1).

B cells

B cells are central players in adaptive humoral immunity, largely maintaining intestinal equilibrium through the secretion of secretory immunoglobulin A (sIgA). This antibody layer coats commensal microbes and soluble antigens, ensuring a balanced host–microbiota relationship. In germ-free (GF) mice, IgA levels are strikingly reduced, accompanied by impaired B-cell functions. In contrast, colonization by certain gut microbes, such as Escherichia coli (E. coli), Bifidobacterium, and SFB, promotes B-cell differentiation and drives the secretion of antigen-targeted IgA. Notably, epidemiological studies have shown that changes in IgA-related microbial populations correlate with an increased incidence of breast malignancy, implying that microbiota-driven regulation of B-cell function could affect cancer susceptibility. Despite these observations, the precise mechanisms through which gut microbes orchestrate IgA secretion and the downstream consequences of IgA on immune regulation in breast cancer remain largely unresolved.

Beyond IgA generation, B-cell responses are shaped by multiple factors, including gut microbial influences and metabolite signaling. Contributions from IECs, DCs, T cells, and eosinophils act as pivotal cues that, together with cytokines, drive plasma cell maturation and sustained IgA production. In addition, microbial products such as metabolites originating from Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus, together with molecules including L-fuculose derivatives, lactic acid, and 1,2-propanediol, have been reported to stimulate B cells via B-cell receptor (BCR)-mediated signaling cascades. SCFAs are recognized as key modulators of immune regulation. Among them, butyrate promotes the generation of IL-10⁺IgM⁺ plasma cells within splenic B cells of mice by suppressing histone deacetylase (HDAC) activity, which results in enhanced histone acetylation, reduced mitochondrial superoxide, and elevated IL-10 production, thereby facilitating plasma cell differentiation. Activated B cells subsequently secrete IgA, which not only maintains mucosal homeostasis but also contributes to tumor suppression. In parallel, gut microbial activity can elevate cytokines like IL-1β and IL-6, which facilitate the maturation of naïve B cells toward a regulatory B cell phenotype, highlighting the multifaceted regulatory network linking microbiota to host immune function.

CD4 + T cells

Evidence from experimental studies demonstrates that transferring fecal microbiota from pancreatic cancer–bearing mice into Kras-mutant pancreatic cancer models significantly elevated CD4+ T-cell infiltration in the TME, suggesting that gut microbiota regulates antitumor immunity through modulation of CD4+ T-cell activity. Notably, the gut microbiota not only affects the abundance of CD4+ T cells but also drives their differentiation into distinct functional subsets, thereby initiating and shaping adaptive cellular immunity. As shown by Pushalkar et al., gut microbiota can translocate into the pancreas and thereby alter its microenvironment. In mouse models of pancreatic cancer, microbial ablation was associated with enhanced intratumoral T-cell infiltration and increased CD4+Th1 polarization, evidenced by upregulation of T-bet, a key Th1-specific transcription factor.

Moreover, alterations in gut microbial composition strongly influence how naïve CD4+T cells differentiate, directing them toward Treg or Th17 lineages. Th17 cells act as key defenders against extracellular pathogens, while Tregs help preserve immune tolerance but also foster an immunosuppressive TME, ultimately facilitating pancreatic cancer progression. Pancreatic tumorigenesis has been linked to an altered Th17/Treg ratio. The intestinal microbiota is considered a key determinant in sustaining this equilibrium and thereby influencing host immune dynamics within tumors. In one example, intestinal colonization by SFB leads to their adhesion to epithelial cells, which stimulates naïve CD4+T cells to differentiate into Th17 cells. This differentiation is promoted by increased serum amyloid A (SAA) expression and reactive oxygen intermediates (ROS), leading to an expanded Th17 population. Similarly, probiotic supplementation has been shown to remodel the gut microbial community, enhance the production of SCFAs, increase Th17 responses and are connected with tumor suppression in vivo. Moreover, fucoidan, a marine-derived polysaccharide, modulates microbial composition and augments SCFA levels, particularly acetate and butyrate, thereby limiting Treg development and enhancing the efficacy of anti-PD-1 treatment. On the other hand, some symbiotic microbes, including Bacteroides fragilis and members of the Clostridium genus, generate polysaccharide A (PSA), which exerts strong immunomodulatory effects. PSA can engage dendritic cells or trigger TLR signaling, thereby directing naïve CD4+T cells toward a FOXP3+Treg phenotype and suppressing Th17 cell development. Collectively, these findings suggest that modulating intestinal microbiota or their derived metabolites to rebalance Th17/Treg homeostasis may provide a promising strategy for pancreatic cancer immunotherapy.

CD8 + T cells

CD8⁺ T cells play a pivotal role in antiviral and antitumor immunity, as they can effectively discriminate between infected or malignant cells and normal counterparts. Evidence has shown that gut-derived metabolites can modulate CD8+T-cell function, thereby contributing to enhanced antitumor immunity in pancreatic cancer. For instance, trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO), a metabolite derived from gut microbiota, was found to enhance CD8+T-cell activity in pancreatic cancer mouse models, reflected by elevated frequencies of IFN-γ+TNF-α+CD8+T cells, even though total CD8+T-cell infiltration was not significantly altered. Mechanistically, TMAO reprogrammed macrophages toward a pro-immunogenic state, subsequently boosting effector T-cell activity and limiting PDAC progression. Furthermore, TMAO exposure markedly increased PD-1 and Tim-3 levels on CD8+T cells, making PDAC tumors more responsive to blockade of PD-1 or Tim-3 immune checkpoints. In another investigation, pentanoate produced by Megasphaera massiliensis along with butyrate was found to stimulate cytokine secretion in CD8+T cells. These metabolites acted by blocking class I HDACs and triggering the mTOR pathway. As a result, antigen-specific CD8+T cells treated with pentanoate and butyrate exhibited stronger antitumor responses in pancreatic cancer mice and improved the performance of CAR T-cell therapy. Collectively, the evidence underscores the intricate relationship between microbial metabolites and CD8+T-cell responses, and supports the notion that targeting these metabolites could be applied as an auxiliary strategy to mitigate the immunosuppressive milieu of PDAC. In our view, integrating microbiota-targeted interventions with existing immunotherapies—particularly immune checkpoint blockade or CAR T-cell therapy—may represent a feasible approach to convert PDAC from an immune “cold” tumor into a more “immunologically active” one, thereby improving patient responsiveness to treatment.

On the other hand, dysbiosis of gut microbiota has been associated with intensified inflammation and greater tumor risk, during which CD8+T-cell activity is compromised and the cells progressively enter an exhausted state. One study found that reducing microbial populations led to a significant enrichment of IFN-γ–expressing CD8+T cells within the pancreatic tumor milieu. Similarly, another study demonstrated that antibiotic ablation not only suppressed pancreatic cancer progression but also enhanced the acquisition of a cytotoxic CD8⁺ T-cell phenotype. These findings suggest that, under certain conditions, the gut microbiota may restrain the activation and effector function of CD8+T cells, thus contributing to the establishment of an immunosuppressive milieu. Interestingly, antibiotic exposure in mice was found to predominantly alter microbial groups within the phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes. An open question for future research is whether these microbial phyla substantially contribute to shaping the immunosuppressive regulation of effector CD8+T cells. A recent investigation highlighted that the Klebsiella oxytoca strain UMK01, obtained from PDAC patients, can both degrade gemcitabine and release indole metabolites. These processes diminish CD8⁺ T-cell activity and help establish a tumor milieu characterized by immunosuppression.

Overall, current evidence points to a contradictory influence of the intestinal microbiota on CD8+T-cell dynamics, where microbial composition and metabolic activity determine whether cytotoxic functions are stimulated or immune-suppressive pathways are activated. In our view, this duality underscores the necessity of moving beyond a binary perspective of “beneficial versus harmful microbes.” Instead, future efforts should focus on dissecting the precise microbial taxa and metabolites that dictate CD8+T-cell fate in PDAC. These findings could provide a foundation for the development of microbiome-based strategies aimed at eliminating immunosuppressive taxa, while retaining or amplifying microbes that enhance CD8+T-cell–driven antitumor responses.

Gut microbiota and their impact on immunotherapy in pancreatic cancer

Immune checkpoint inhibitor–based therapy

Monoclonal antibodies directed against PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4, collectively known as immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), now represent fundamental approaches in modern cancer treatment.

PD-1/PD-L1 blockade

Growing data derived from both experimental models and patient-based studies demonstrate that differences in commensal microbial communities are critical for determining interindividual responses to immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy. Baseline fecal microbiome analyses from patients treated with monoclonal antibodies—employing multi-omic approaches including metagenomic and 16 S rRNA sequencing together with metabolomic profiling—have revealed that diminished microbial diversity strongly correlates with inferior therapeutic efficacy and reduced survival outcomes. A similar trend has been observed in PDAC, where the composition and diversity of the intratumoral microbiota were significantly correlated with patient outcomes. Interestingly, comparative 16 S rRNA-based metagenomic profiling of pancreatic tumors and adjacent normal tissues revealed that PD-L1 expression was strongly tied to differences in the composition of bacterial communities detected in tumor niches. These findings underscore that microbial diversity not only impacts PDAC clinical outcomes but also modulates sensitivity to PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors, offering a rationale for integrating microbiota profiling into precision immunotherapy strategies. Emerging data suggest that specific gut microbiota substantially shape the therapeutic response to PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors in PDAC. Notably, combining resveratrol with anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody significantly enhanced intratumoral CD8+T-cell infiltration and activation, thereby resulting in pronounced suppression of PDAC growth. Importantly, in vivo experiments employing antibiotic-mediated microbiota depletion confirmed that the ability of resveratrol to potentiate the antitumor efficacy of α-PD-1 was dependent on the presence of gut microbiota. Mechanistically, bacterial families such as Desulfovibrionaceae and Deferribacteraceae were shown to play central roles, with Desulfovibrionaceae emerging as the dominant taxon. Notably, Desulfovibrionaceae enhanced the antitumor activity of α-PD-1 by increasing the abundance of prostaglandin D2 (PGD2), thereby amplifying CD8⁺ T-cell–mediated immune responses.

A growing body of clinical research has revealed that gut microbiota composition strongly correlates with the therapeutic response to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, and this association is gradually being extended into clinical applications. For instance, Zheng et al. reported that among HCC patients treated with PD-1 blockade following prior sorafenib therapy, responders exhibited greater microbial diversity and higher gene abundance within the gut microbial composition of their stool samples compared with patients who failed to respond, suggesting that gut microbial communities could represent a modifiable target to improve treatment outcomes. In addition, a recent phase II trial evaluated a therapeutic regimen combining nivolumab with tadalafil and oral vancomycin in patients presenting with pancreatic cancer liver metastases. The results showed that this combined regimen did not provide clinical benefit in terms of improved outcomes, although it was generally safe and well tolerated. Importantly, shotgun metagenomic sequencing of fecal samples revealed significant shifts in gut microbiota composition following vancomycin administration, with notable increases in the relative abundance of Lactobacillus, Akkermansia, and Veillonella. While Lactobacillus and Akkermansia have been consistently associated with enhanced responses to PD-1 blockade, the enrichment of Veillonella showed the opposite trend. Mechanistic studies indicated that Veillonella could translocate into the PDAC tumor microenvironment, where it acted as a pro-tumorigenic factor. More critically, elevated levels of Veillonella were closely linked to reduced progression-free survival (PFS) and diminished overall survival (OS) in patients receiving PD-1 blockade therapy. Therefore, it seems reasonable to propose that the limited efficacy of the combination regimen may arise from particular bacterial taxa—such as Veillonella—that exert immunosuppressive activity in PDAC, thereby counteracting the therapeutic benefits of PD-1 inhibition. Future research should aim to differentiate commensal microorganisms with protective roles from those that may drive tumor progression, and investigate targeted microbiota-modulating approaches to enhance the clinical effectiveness of PD-1/PD-L1 blockade in pancreatic cancer.

CTLA-4 blockade

Therapeutic antibodies such as ipilimumab and tremelimumab enhance antitumor immune function by blocking CTLA-4–mediated immunosuppressive signaling. Preliminary investigations in pancreatic cancer have assessed their therapeutic potential. In a phase II randomized study (NCT02866383), SBRT was combined with nivolumab, either alone or alongside ipilimumab, in patients with treatment-refractory metastatic pancreatic cancer (mPC). Findings revealed that the clinical benefit rate (CBR) reached 17.1% (95% CI: 8.0–30.6) in the SBRT plus nivolumab arm, while the inclusion of ipilimumab elevated the CBR to 37.2% (95% CI: 24.0–52.1). In terms of safety, grade ≥ 3 treatment-related adverse effects were documented in 24.4% (10 cases) of participants in the SBRT/nivolumab cohort and in 30.2% (13 cases) of those administered the triple regimen with ipilimumab. Overall, these findings indicate that SBRT combined with nivolumab provides measurable antitumor activity in refractory mPC, and the addition of ipilimumab can further enhance clinical benefit, albeit with a modest increase in toxicity. Another open-label, randomized phase 1b/2 clinical trial evaluated the efficacy and safety of niraparib combined with either nivolumab or ipilimumab in patients with advanced pancreatic cancer. The niraparib plus ipilimumab maintenance group achieved the primary endpoint of 6-month PFS, whereas the niraparib plus nivolumab group demonstrated a comparatively lower PFS rate. Regarding safety, ≥ grade 3 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 10 of 46 patients (22%) in the niraparib plus nivolumab group and 23 of 45 patients (50%) in the niraparib plus ipilimumab group. These findings suggest that CTLA-4 blockade may provide greater antitumor activity than PD-1 inhibition when combined with PARP inhibition in advanced pancreatic cancer, albeit at the cost of increased treatment-related toxicity, underscoring the need to balance efficacy with safety.

Mechanistically, blocking CTLA-4 primarily influences Th1-oriented helper T cells (CD4⁺), which typically express the inducible costimulatory receptor (ICOS). Effector T cells together with Tregs are major cellular populations influenced by CTLA-4 inhibition. Growing evidence indicates that gut microbial alterations can shape the therapeutic outcomes of CTLA-4 inhibitors. Initial investigations suggested that changes in intestinal microbiota under CTLA-4 suppression may trigger IL-12–mediated Th1 activity, ultimately strengthening antitumor immune responses. Consistent with this view, Chaput and colleagues reported in a forward-looking study that patients showing increased Firmicutes abundance had longer PFS and OS, along with elevated ICOS levels in CD4+T cells and higher serum sCD25 concentrations. In parallel, another comprehensive analysis integrating pancreatic cancer transcriptomics with microbiome profiling explored how intratumoral microbial composition influences responses to CTLA-4 inhibition. Twenty-six microbial genera with prognostic relevance were identified, allowing pancreatic cancer cohorts to be classified into two microbiome-related categories, designated Mcluster A and Mcluster B. Enrichment of Azohydromonas, Derxia, Holospora, Hylemonella, Paucibacter, Silanimonas, Starkeya, and Xanthobacter characterized Mcluster A, while Alpharetrovirus, Indibacter, Riemerella, and Ruegeria predominated in Mcluster B. Importantly, Mcluster A displayed a significantly elevated CTLA-4 blockade immunophenoscore, indicating that individuals in this group might derive superior benefit from CTLA-4 inhibition. Whether the microbial genera enriched in Mcluster A directly mediate enhanced sensitivity to CTLA-4 blockade remains an important question for future investigation. Nonetheless, not every microbiota-derived signal can potentiate CTLA-4 inhibition. Emerging evidence indicates that the microbial metabolite butyrate might impair CTLA-4–directed therapy. Circulating butyrate has been reported to suppress DC differentiation and interfere with CD28-mediated costimulation, processes that are critical for the antitumor efficacy of ipilimumab. However, conclusive evidence connecting gut microbiota changes with the clinical performance of CTLA-4 inhibitors is still absent. Therefore, future studies should utilize in vivo pancreatic cancer models to further investigate the therapeutic potential of combining microbiota transplantation or metabolite modulation with CTLA-4 blockade.

Chimeric antigen receptor T cells

ICIs exert their therapeutic effects mainly through reactivating pre-existing T cells that specifically target tumors in the host. In tumors with poor immunogenicity, chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T therapy is increasingly recognized as a viable alternative or supplement to existing treatments. This approach involves engineering autologous T cells from peripheral blood with artificial CAR constructs, followed by reinfusion into the host, which facilitates precise recognition and elimination of tumor cells. Recently, increasing evidence has highlighted the critical influence of the TME on CAR-T performance, with particular focus on the regulatory function of the gut microbiota. Modulating microbial composition may augment immune responsiveness, counteract tumor-induced immunosuppression, and ultimately strengthen the therapeutic efficacy of CAR-T cells. Preclinical investigations corroborate this view; in breast cancer mouse models, vancomycin in combination with CD19-directed CAR-T markedly enhanced tumor control and promoted more efficient antigen cross-presentation compared to CAR-T alone. Furthermore, the microbiota has been reported to modulate Claudin 18.2+ CAR-T cell activity through the butyrate/GPR109A/HOPX pathway, thereby impeding gastric tumor progression. Collectively, these observations underscore the microbiota as a pivotal determinant of CAR-T function.

Pancreatic cancer has recently become a focus for CAR-T research. Importantly, ROR1-directed CAR-T therapy demonstrated robust suppression of pancreatic tumor progression in both animal models and 3D culture systems. Moreover, gut microbiota–derived SCFAs, particularly pentanoate and butyrate, were shown to boost the efficacy of ROR1-targeted CAR-T cells by influencing metabolic pathways and inducing epigenetic remodeling, thereby improving immune performance and promoting tumor eradication. Collectively, these results underscore the critical contribution of gut microbiota in regulating CAR-T functionality and shaping therapeutic outcomes in pancreatic cancer.

Despite compelling preclinical evidence indicating that gut microbiota profoundly influences CAR-T cell efficacy, solid clinical confirmation is still lacking in pancreatic cancer and other malignancies. Consequently, future studies should prioritize rigorously designed clinical trials to elucidate these mechanisms. Future investigations should therefore prioritize well-designed clinical trials to dissect the underlying mechanisms of microbiota–CAR-T interactions and to evaluate whether microbiota-targeted approaches could be harnessed as adjunctive strategies to strengthen CAR-T–driven antitumor responses.

Exploiting the gut microbiota: novel therapeutic avenues and biomarkers in pancreatic cancer immunotherapy

The gut microbiota have emerged as a promising modulator of pancreatic cancer immunotherapy. As shown in Fig. 2, microbial interventions such as probiotics, antibiotics, and FMT may reshape the tumor immune microenvironment and improve therapeutic efficacy.

Innovative strategies to optimize immunotherapy for pancreatic cancer

Before immunotherapy is initiated, many patients typically receive conventional modalities like chemotherapy or radiation, which are recognized to disrupt the gut microbial balance. Increasing research highlights the importance of developing microbiota-informed therapeutic combinations that take into account the long-standing coadaptation between host and gut microbiota. To maximize clinical benefit, such strategies should be individualized by incorporating patient-specific variables—such as age, genetic predisposition, lifestyle, coexisting diseases, and concomitant medications—thereby paving the way for precision oncology.

Dietary intervention

Diet strongly impacts the diversity and activity of gut microbes, with both major nutrients (carbohydrates, proteins, fats) and minor dietary factors (vitamins, minerals, and polyphenols) shaping their abundance and functionality. For example, in KPC models of PDAC, inhibition of VB6-dependent one-carbon metabolism was shown to enhance NK cell–mediated antitumor immunity and suppress tumor progression.

Among dietary components, fiber exerts particularly strong effects on microbial ecology. As dietary fibers escape digestion in the human gastrointestinal tract, they undergo microbial fermentation, producing SCFAs such as acetate, propionate, and butyrate. These metabolites acidify the colonic milieu, thereby suppressing pathogens like Clostridium difficile and simultaneously promoting the expansion of beneficial bacterial groups. SCFA levels are strongly shaped by both dietary patterns and microbial metabolic activity. These metabolites not only boost immune function and regulate glucose and lipid metabolism but also exhibit potent anticancer properties. Diets deficient in fiber, leading to diminished SCFA generation, are linked to heightened susceptibility to malignancies and inflammatory disorders, notably breast and gastric cancers. In PDAC specifically, SCFAs have been shown to modulate CD8⁺ T cell responses and enhance the efficacy of adoptive cell therapies.

Conversely, adverse lifestyle factors such as excessive alcohol consumption can disrupt microbial balance and promote pancreatic carcinogenesis. Chronic alcohol intake enriches Verrucomicrobia, Actinobacteria, and Proteobacteria, while reducing Firmicutes and Lactobacillus, accompanied by a decline in Firmicutes and Lactobacillus. The combined effect of these alterations and ethanol-derived acetaldehyde promotes chronic inflammatory processes, which elevate the likelihood of pancreatitis and tumorigenesis. Dietary fat is another major determinant of microbiota composition. High intake of saturated or total fat typically exerts detrimental effects, decreasing beneficial microbes such as Bifidobacterium and Faecalibacterium while elevating the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio. In contrast, unsaturated fats promote the expansion of probiotics including Bifidobacterium and Akkermansia, reduce the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, and suppress opportunistic pathogens such as Escherichia coli and Streptococcus. High-fat diet–induced dysbiosis can further alter carbohydrate metabolism, impair SCFA production, and compromise intestinal barrier integrity, allowing bacterial byproducts, including LPS, to enter the bloodstream and drive body-wide inflammatory responses.

In summary, dietary composition—including macronutrient distribution and fiber content—exerts profound effects on gut microbiota structure, microbial metabolite production, and ultimately host immunity and cancer progression.

FMT

FMT, with origins tracing back to fourth-century China, has developed into a clinically applicable strategy aimed at re-establishing intestinal microbial homeostasis, leading to extensive discussion regarding its methodology and mechanisms. The method consists of introducing stool-derived microbiota from healthy individuals into the digestive system of patients with dysbiosis-related conditions.

FMT can be administered through multiple routes, each adaptable to specific clinical needs and patient preferences. These include: Colonoscopy, in which donor stool is delivered directly into the colon via a flexible endoscope, ensuring broad distribution throughout the colon; Enema, where donor-derived stool suspension is instilled into the rectum through medical devices such as catheters or syringes, and can be performed under medical supervision in clinical or home settings; Nasogastric (NG) tube delivery, enabling fecal material to reach the stomach or small intestine directly; and Oral capsules, a non-invasive option containing freeze-dried fecal matter that dissolves in the gastrointestinal tract upon ingestion. This diversity in delivery methods provides flexibility for tailoring treatment according to patient and clinical requirements.

Originally, FMT originally served as a therapeutic approach for recurrent Clostridium difficile infection. Its high efficacy in this context has catalyzed investigations into its potential application for a range of gastrointestinal and systemic disorders linked to microbial imbalance. More recently, FMT has shown promise in addressing treatment-resistant colitis induced by ICIs. In an early clinical attempt, two individuals demonstrated substantial clinical improvement following FMT during follow-up monitoring. Similarly, a recent study performed FMT using stool using fecal material obtained from cancer-bearing mice kept under enriched environmental conditions (EE), which markedly suppressed tumor expansion in subcutaneous as well as orthotopic PDAC systems. Importantly, 16 S rRNA profiling showed that EE enhanced microbial diversity and preferentially promoted probiotic taxa such as Lactobacillus, especially L. reuteri. Treatment with L. reuteri alone markedly suppressed pancreatic tumor development and facilitated NK cell recruitment within the TME, thereby enhancing immune responses against tumors. Collectively, these results suggest that FMT can modulate intestinal microbial communities to enhance antitumor immune responses in pancreatic cancer, highlighting its potential as a therapeutic strategy to improve immunotherapy outcomes.

Probiotics

Most commercially available probiotics originate mainly from Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium genera, together with strains including lactic acid bacteria, Bacillus, Saccharomyces boulardii, and Escherichia coli. These microbes are available in diverse formulations, ranging from fermented products like yogurt, cheese, and kimchi to powders, capsules, or tablets containing either single or mixed strains. In parallel, prebiotics, including fructo-oligosaccharides and galacto-oligosaccharides, represent indigestible nutrients that are fermented by Bifidobacterium and other resident microbes, leading to the production of SCFAs such as acetate, propionate, and butyrate—key metabolites essential for maintaining intestinal and overall physiological health. Interestingly, prebiotic oligosaccharides can mimic host glycan structures and competitively bind to bacterial receptors, thereby blocking pathogen attachment to epithelial cells and reducing colonization. A growing body of research suggests that prebiotics help maintain microbial balance, which in turn enhances cancer therapy efficacy while limiting treatment-related side effects. However, no direct evidence has yet established a link between prebiotics and PDAC. Abdul Rahman et al. proposed that prebiotics may also act through mechanisms independent of probiotics.

In contrast, evidence for probiotics in pancreatic diseases is more substantial. Oláh and colleagues investigated Lactobacillus plantarum 299 in human models of pancreatitis and reported that this strain was well tolerated, reduced septic complications, and decreased the need for surgical interventions. More recent studies indicate that probiotics and their metabolites not only exert inhibitory effects on pancreatic cancer cells but also significantly lower postoperative infections following pancreaticoduodenectomy. A notable example is ferrichrome, produced by Lactobacillus casei ATCC 334, which suppressed pancreatic cancer cell proliferation by activating M1-type TAMs through a TLR4-dependent pathway. This immunomodulatory effect may further enhance responses to immune checkpoint blockade. Additional findings highlight the role of other probiotic species in modulating antitumor immunity. For instance, the gram-negative bacterium Akkermansia muciniphila inhibited pancreatic tumor cell growth in rats by boosting gut immune responses and cytokine release. Broader investigations have revealed that probiotics can influence cancer biology through multiple mechanisms, including induction of apoptosis, inhibition of mutagenesis, downregulation of oncogenes, activation of autophagy, suppression of kinase signaling, reactivation of tumor suppressor pathways, and prevention of metastasis. Collectively, these data support the concept that probiotics may improve the efficacy of anticancer therapies—including immunotherapies—while minimizing adverse effects.

Despite encouraging preclinical data, the clinical utility of probiotics in oncology remains controversial. Many commercial probiotic supplements suffer from inconsistent strain composition, dosage variability, and poor quality control. Moreover, patient outcomes have been inconsistent, with some trials reporting minimal or no benefit when probiotics were combined with immunotherapy. These limitations underscore the necessity for rigorous clinical studies and the development of standardized, mechanism-based probiotic formulations to achieve reproducible and clinically meaningful effects.

Emerging biomarkers for forecasting patient responses to immunotherapy in pancreatic cancer

Emerging studies suggest that gut microbiome profiling provides valuable insights into patient heterogeneity in response to ICIs. Variations in microbial composition and their metabolic outputs are increasingly recognized as potential noninvasive biomarkers that can forecast treatment effectiveness as well as the risk of immune-associated toxicities (irAEs).

Specific microbial taxa have been repeatedly associated with favorable responses. Enrichment of Akkermansia muciniphila (A. muciniphila), Bifidobacterium longum (B. longum), Bacteroides fragilis (B. fragilis), together with members of the Ruminococcaceae family, has been linked to favorable therapeutic responses and enhanced clinical efficacy under PD-1, PD-L1, or CTLA-4 blockade. Conversely, dominance of Faecalibacterium and Clostridium species has often been linked to diminished benefit from immunotherapy. Within pancreatic cancer, increased microbial α-diversity has been identified as a potential predictor, as greater diversity was associated with enhanced tolerance and efficacy of chemo-immunotherapy.

In addition to ICIs, metabolomic features influencing CAR-T therapy have been explored. Long-term responders displayed metabolic profiles enriched in peptidoglycan biosynthesis pathways, whereas activation of the non-oxidative pentose phosphate pathway was related to heightened treatment toxicity. These observations emphasize the prognostic and predictive significance of specific microbial metabolites.

Moving forward, predictive models that integrate multiple dimensions—microbial composition, metabolite signatures, tumor genomic alterations, host comorbidities, age, and germline background—are anticipated to establish a refined framework for predicting which individuals with pancreatic cancer are most likely to respond favorably to immunotherapy.

Challenges and future perspectives

Although accumulating data underscore the importance of gut microbiota in modulating the immune milieu of pancreatic cancer, major obstacles persist in converting these findings into practical therapeutic applications.

A major obstacle is the high inter-individual variability of the gut microbiota, influenced by genetics, diet, lifestyle, medication use, and environmental exposures. This diversity complicates the identification of universal microbial biomarkers or therapeutic targets. Over the past decade, progress in multi-omics approaches has generated effective strategies to confront this issue. Techniques such as metagenomics and transcriptomics allow detailed characterization of microbial ecosystems and their host interactions, whereas metabolomics and proteomics elucidate how microbial metabolites contribute to immune regulation. Moreover, single-cell and spatial transcriptomic technologies enable high-resolution profiling of microbiota effects on the PDAC immune landscape. Integrating such datasets with AI-based analytical frameworks allows the establishment of more precise models to uncover essential microbial species and corresponding metabolic signatures, ultimately supporting the development of more effective immunotherapeutic interventions for PDAC.

A critical barrier is the inadequate mechanistic clarity on how gut microbiota shape immune regulation, impacting PDAC progression and treatment response. Functional studies in preclinical models are vital for pinpointing microbial metabolites with tumor-modulatory roles. In parallel, the application of single-cell and spatial transcriptomics promises transformative insights into the intricate molecular interplay between microbiota and immune cells within the TME.

Current microbiota-directed strategies, including probiotics, prebiotics, and FMT, remain without unified protocols. The most suitable bacterial compositions have not been established, and investigations vary between employing mono-strain and multi-strain preparations, leading to heterogeneity and limited cross-study comparability. Additionally, dosing regimens, treatment duration, and administration routes are inconsistent, all of which influence therapeutic efficacy. To overcome these limitations, a standardized framework is needed for strain selection, dosing, and delivery methods. Personalized approaches based on individual microbiome profiling can help identify core beneficial strains, while engineered probiotics with specific immunomodulatory functions can enhance consistency and clinical applicability.

Safety remains a key concern. FMT, for instance, carries risks such as pathogen transmission or disruption of the host microbiome if not strictly regulated. Strict evaluation of donors and meticulous quality assurance remain indispensable. Advances in engineered biology provide novel opportunities, particularly through probiotics tailored to function specifically within the PDAC tumor microenvironment, thereby reducing systemic toxicity and enhancing therapeutic accuracy.

Moreover, most current studies examining the relationship between gut microbiota and pancreatic cancer immunity have used subcutaneous or orthotopic transplantation models. Although these models are experimentally convenient and allow controlled microbiota manipulation, they do not fully recapitulate the dense stroma and immunosuppressive microenvironment typical of human PDAC. In contrast, genetically engineered mouse models such as KC and KPC more accurately mimic spontaneous tumorigenesis and immune evolution within the native pancreatic tissue. However, microbiota-related studies using these models remain limited. Future investigations incorporating microbial modulation strategies—such as antibiotics, probiotics, or fecal microbiota transplantation—into KC or KPC systems are needed to verify whether microbiota-driven immune mechanisms observed in transplantation models also occur in spontaneous PDAC.

Looking ahead, integrating personalized microbiome profiling with synthetic biology could enable precise modulation of host immunity in pancreatic cancer. Combining multi-omics analyses with AI-driven modeling and engineered probiotics may help address current barriers, promote the identification of new biomarkers and potential therapeutic avenues, and accelerate the clinical adoption of microbiota-driven strategies. Furthermore, integrating microbiota-based approaches into established immunotherapy regimens could strengthen immune defense against tumors and enhance clinical outcomes, provided issues of ethics and equitable access are properly addressed.
