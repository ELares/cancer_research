Metabolic regulation by p53: Implications for cancer therapy

The tumor suppressor p53, long known for its roles in maintaining genomic integrity and suppressing tumorigenesis, has recently been recognized as a key regulator of cellular metabolism. Here, we review p53’s emerging metabolic functions, highlighting its ability to orchestrate glucose, amino acid, and lipid metabolism. By promoting oxidative phosphorylation while inhibiting glycolysis and anabolic pathways, wild-type p53 counters metabolic reprogramming characteristic of cancer cells, such as the Warburg effect, and protects cells from mild cellular stresses. In contrast, mutant p53 disrupts these processes, fostering metabolic adaptations that support tumor progression. These findings pave the way for therapeutic approaches targeting p53-driven metabolic vulnerabilities in cancer.

INTRODUCTION

The tumor suppressor protein p53, known as the "guardian of the genome," is crucial for maintaining genomic stability. It regulates cell cycle progression, DNA repair, senescence, and apoptosis, primarily functioning as a sequence-specific transcription factor that acts as a major barrier to cancer initiation and progression.

Beyond its classical role as a tumor suppressor, p53 has emerged as a critical metabolic regulator, controlling enzymes involved in glycolysis, oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), lipid metabolism, nucleotide biosynthesis, and redox balance. These functions enable cells to adapt metabolically to cellular stress while preventing malignant transformation. In cancer, metabolic reprogramming—such as the Warburg effect, which favors aerobic glycolysis—allows tumor cells to meet their intense bioenergetic and anabolic demands for rapid proliferation. p53 counteracts these shifts by promoting OXPHOS for efficient ATP generation and minimizing oxidative stress.

Despite substantial progress in understanding p53’s metabolic roles, key questions remain about how p53 integrates metabolic signals and regulates specific genes under stress. Moreover, the interplay between p53 and its regulators, such as AMPK, adds complexity to its role as a metabolic sensor. By exploring the molecular mechanisms underpinning metabolic regulation by wild-type and mutant forms of p53, this review highlights the potential for targeting p53-mediated metabolic dependencies to develop novel therapeutic strategies for cancer.

p53-Governed Central Carbon Metabolism

Healthy cells primarily metabolize glucose through OXPHOS for efficient energy production. In contrast, cancer cells rely on aerobic glycolysis, converting glucose to lactate to support anabolic pathways and reduce oxidative stress. The tumor suppressor p53 regulates glucose metabolism by suppressing glycolysis and promoting OXPHOS. Consequently, its loss drives the Warburg effect, increasing aerobic glycolysis and enabling cancer cells to meet the high anabolic and redox demands required for tumor growth and survival.

Glycolytic Flux

p53 suppresses glucose uptake by downregulating glucose transporters (GLUTs, encoded by SLC2A), including GLUT1 and GLUT4. Additionally, it sequesters NF-κB, inhibiting GLUT3 expression. Conversely, mutant (Missense) p53 induces GLUT1 activity to boost glucose uptake and glycolytic flux, highlighting p53’s essential role in glucose metabolism.

p53 indeed influences key flux-controlling steps in glycolysis, including glucose import and phosphorylation, fructose-1,6-bisphosphate (F1,6BP) production, and lactate export (Fig. 1). Once glucose enters cells, it is phosphorylated into glucose-6-phosphate (G6P) by hexokinase (HK) family. p53 suppresses HK2 via miRNA-mediated gene silencing by inducing miR-143 and miR-34a. Elevated HK2 levels have been observed in prostate cancer with p53/PTEN deficiency, supporting tumor growth in vivo.

At another critical step in glycolysis, TP53-inducible glycolysis and apoptosis regulator (TIGAR), a p53 target, regulates glycolysis through its phosphatase activity on fructose-2,6-bisphosphate (F2,6BP). When glucose is in excess, phosphofructokinase-2 (PFK-2) converts fructose-6-phosphate into F2,6BP, an allosteric activator of PFK-1, thereby enhancing glycolytic flux. TIGAR lowers F2,6BP levels by converting it into fructose-6-phosphate, suppressing glycolysis, and reducing reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels.

p53 also affects the diversion of glycolytic intermediates into the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP). Studies suggest that p53 supports the PPP flux via TIGAR-mediated PFK-1 inhibition and HK2 activation, and transcriptionally repressing PFKFB3, leading to the accumulation of G6P. This accumulation fuels oxidative PPP and NADPH production, supporting nucleotide synthesis and reducing ROS. However, other studies show that p53 inhibits glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, the rate-limiting enzyme in the oxidative PPP. Using isotope-labeled glucose (2-13C-glucose) tracing, researchers confirmed the lowered PPP flux by p53. In addition, p53 transcriptionally downregulates phosphoglycerate mutase (PGAM1 or PGM1), reducing the conversion of 3-phosphoglycerate to 2-phosphoglycerate, leading to 3-phosphoglycerate accumulation—a precursor for the serine synthesis pathway. Despite this accumulation, p53 inhibits de novo serine synthesis by repressing phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PHGDH), the rate-limiting enzyme. Given the tumor-promoting roles of aerobic glycolysis and its branching pathways, disrupting both fluxes may be essential for p53’s tumor-suppressive function.

Lactate production and secretion, key steps in glycolysis, are inhibited by p53. Lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA) converts pyruvate to lactate, regenerating NAD+ to sustain aerobic glycolysis. The introduction of wild-type p53 disrupts LDHA activity, suppressing lactate production and slowing p53-null breast cancer growth. Additionally, p53 represses MCT1, a lactate transporter, preventing lactate secretion and the metabolic shift from OXPHOS to glycolysis.

TCA Cycle and OXPHOS

Hindering glycolytic flux, p53 enhances glucose oxidation. Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, a multienzyme complex including pyruvate dehydrogenase E1 converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA, linking glycolysis to the TCA cycle. This critical step in aerobic metabolism is tightly controlled by pyruvate dehydrogenase complex kinase activity, which inactivates PDH through phosphorylation (Fig. 1).

p53 positively regulates pyruvate dehydrogenase E1 by promoting E3 ligase Parkin expression and reducing pyruvate dehydrogenase complex kinase 2 expression, thereby enhancing pyruvate dehydrogenase complex function to maintain the TCA cycle. Pyruvate carboxylase, often elevated in cancer, provides an anaplerotic pathway by converting pyruvate to oxaloacetate to support the TCA cycle. While promoting PDH activity to channel glycolytic flux into the TCA cycle, p53 suppresses pyruvate carboxylase expression, limiting anaplerotic flux to inhibit tumor progression.

p53 transcriptionally represses malic enzymes ME1 and ME2, which decarboxylate malate to pyruvate, generating NADPH essential for fatty acid synthesis and redox balance. Notably, ME2 has a novel catalytic activity, converting pyruvate into 2-hydroxyglutarate, which stabilizes mutant (Missense) p53 protein and supports tumor growth.

In addition to promoting the TCA cycle, p53 enhances mitochondrial respiration. Cytochrome c oxidase subunit 2, a key component of electron transfer complex IV, is a transcriptional target of p53 and supports ATP production. Notably, we confirmed mutant p53 tumors display higher levels of glycolytic genes (GLUT1, PFKB3, PGAM1, LDHA, MCT1, and PHGDH) and ME1, and lower levels of PARK2 (Fig. 2). Overall, extensive research highlights p53’s role as a glycolysis suppressor, directing glucose into the TCA cycle for complete oxidation. This regulation helps cells meet energy demands while preventing tumor progression.

However, in some tumors where wild-type p53 is retained, p53 has also been reported to support aerobic glycolysis for tumor growth. For example, in wild-type p53 hepatocellular carcinoma, phosphorylated p53-upregulated modulator of apoptosis (PUMA) enhances glucose flux into anaerobic glycolysis instead of the TCA cycle and OXPHOS by disrupting mitochondrial pyruvate uptake through its binding to the mitochondrial pyruvate carrier . Therefore, the role of p53 as a metabolic switch should be carefully examined in relation to the biological context and its mutant variants.

Beyond the Warburg effect, increasing evidence suggests that the TCA cycle and OXPHOS remain crucial for tumor growth, as they provide macromolecules necessary for rapid cell proliferation. To meet the heightened demands of central carbon metabolism, cancer cells utilize amino acids and lipids as alternative fuel sources. This metabolic flexibility enables cancer cells to sustain their biosynthetic needs and maintain redox balance. Moreover, certain human cancers, such as diffuse large B-cell lymphoma, depend on OXPHOS, which is primarily fueled by lipids rather than aerobic glycolysis. In the following sections, we explore how p53 regulates amino acid and lipid metabolism in both nontransformed and transformed cells.

The Tumor-Suppressive Role of p53 in Regulating Amino Acid Metabolism

Altered amino acid metabolism, a hallmark of cancer, supplies carbon and nitrogen for anaplerosis, macromolecule synthesis, and ROS homeostasis. p53 acts as a metabolic gatekeeper by regulating amino acid pathways, including glutamine, aspartate, asparagine, and proline, which fuel cancer cell proliferation and survival.

Glutamine replenishes TCA cycle intermediates for macromolecule synthesis and energy production, while its nitrogen supports nucleotide, amino acid, and glutathione synthesis, aiding proliferation and oxidative stress protection.

p53 transactivates GLS2, producing glutamate to fuel glutathione synthesis and lower ROS levels. Upon mild and repairable genotoxic stress, this regulation protects cells from apoptosis by mitigating oxidative stress. p53 also increases the α-ketoglutarate/succinate ratio. As a ten-eleven translocation dioxygenase cosubstrate, α-ketoglutarate elevates 5-hydroxymethylcytosine levels, driving DNA demethylation and transcriptional changes linked to premalignant differentiation. This epigenetic regulation may contribute to p53’s tumor-suppressive role.

Asparagine metabolism supports cancer cell survival and proliferation. De novo asparagine synthesis is catalyzed by asparagine synthetase (ASNS), using aspartate as a precursor. Given the high demand for aspartate to support the synthesis of amino acids, nucleotides, and the TCA cycle, extracellular asparagine often becomes a vital source for tumor progression and is targetable with L-asparaginase therapy. While p53 transcriptionally represses ASNS to inhibit malignant transformation, its loss causes asparagine accumulation, driving tumor initiation.

Serine and glycine are key carbon sources for one-carbon metabolism, supporting nucleotide synthesis and ROS maintenance. p53 represses MTHFD2, a critical enzyme in this pathway converting 5,10-methylene-THF to 10-formyl-THF. In p53-null or mutant (Missense) cancers, elevated MTHFD2 expression may promote one-carbon metabolism, aiding nucleotide synthesis and NADPH production to support tumor growth.

Proline is essential for protein synthesis and redox balance, with its biosynthesis pathway regulated by p53. Proline dehydrogenase (PRODH) converts proline into pyrroline-5-carboxylate, which spontaneously hydrolyzes into glutamate gamma-semialdehyde and is converted to glutamate by ALDH4 (Fig. 1). p53 activates PRODH and ALDH4 to drive proline catabolism and reduce ROS generated from the proline cycle. These reactions also generate FADH2 and NADH, suggesting that p53 may enhance OXPHOS and ATP production by upregulating these enzymes.

Emerging studies reveal p53’s role in the urea cycle, where it represses enzymes including carbamoyl phosphate synthetase, ornithine transcarbamylase, and arginase 1, to reduce ureagenesis, and leads to ammonia accumulation. This excess ammonia inhibits polyamine synthesis, required for cell proliferation, by suppressing ornithine decarboxylase. p53-null tumors enhance urea cycle activity to remove ammonia and maintain polyamine pools for their growth. In contrast, p53 also transactivates the urea cycle enzyme argininosuccinate synthase 1 to protect cells from genotoxic stress.

p53 also regulates amino acid transporters under metabolic stress conditions. Upon glutamine deprivation, p53 promotes glutamate/aspartate antiporter (SLC1A3) expression, increasing intracellular aspartate to sustain the TCA cycle and nucleotide synthesis. It also upregulates SLC7A3 to enhance arginine uptake, protecting cells from glutamine-deprived stress. Conversely, p53 represses the cystine/glutamate antiporter SLC7A11 (xCT), reducing cystine uptake and sensitizing cells to ROS-induced ferroptosis.

Notably, our pan-cancer analysis confirmed lower levels of GLS2, PRODH, ALDH4, and SLC7A3, and higher levels of ASNS, MTHFD2, and carbamoyl phosphate synthetase in mutant p53 tumors (Fig. 2).

In general, p53 inhibits tumor-promoting amino acid metabolism to prevent tumor progression and protects cells from oxidative stress upon cellular stresses.

Emerging Roles of p53 in Lipid Metabolism

Cancer cells reprogram lipid metabolism to meet demands for energy, membranes, and signaling molecules, supporting rapid proliferation. p53 regulates lipid metabolism by suppressing synthesis and promoting oxidation, contributing to its tumor-suppressive role.

Sterol regulatory element-binding transcription protein-1c, a transcription factor, activates several genes involved in cholesterol, fatty acid, triacylglycerol, and phospholipid synthesis, which are essential for lipogenesis and lipid homeostasis. Wild-type p53 transcriptionally represses sterol regulatory element-binding transcription protein-1c, leading to the downregulation of fatty acid synthase, ATP citrate lyase, spot 14, and glycerol-3-phosphate dehydrogenase. This repression results in a reduction in lipid anabolism. Moreover, p53 inhibits cholesterol synthesis and uptake. It induces retrograde sterol movement from the plasma membrane to the endoplasmic reticulum, resulting in an immature SREBP-2 protein that primarily regulates genes involved in cholesterol synthesis. This leads to a decrease in the expression of SREBP-2 target genes, including HMG-CoA reductase, lanosterol synthase, and squalene monooxygenase, which facilitate the mevalonate pathway. To support its tumor-suppressive role through regulating lipid metabolism, mutant (Missense) p53 enhances SREBP-1 activity by interfering with AMPK-mediated inhibitory phosphorylation, to promote fatty acid synthesis. In addition, disruption of AMPK activity by mutant (Missense) p53 reduces inhibitory phosphorylation of acetyl-CoA carboxylase, which catalyzes the rate-limiting step in fatty acid synthesis by converting acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA. This enhances lipid anabolism in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma.

NADPH is an essential cofactor for fatty acid synthesis and is produced by metabolic processes, such as glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase in the PPP, the malic enzyme, and MTHFD in one-carbon metabolism. As mentioned earlier in this review, p53 inhibits NADPH production via suppressing those enzymes, thereby reducing fatty acid synthesis. Indeed, p53-null colorectal cancer cells accumulate lipid droplets. Of note, under genotoxic stress, p53 transactivates ceramide synthase 5, ceramide synthase 6, and sphingomyelin phosphodiesterase 3 to promote ceramide biosynthesis to induce apoptosis.

Concurrently, p53 has been reported to induce lipid catabolism. p53 transactivates acyl-CoA dehydrogenase to induce fatty acid beta-oxidation, and Lipin-1 to support lipid catabolism by promoting diacylglycerol production. Additionally, 2 enzymes promoting lipid catabolism, HMG-CoA lyase-like 1, which is involved in ketone body metabolism, and malonyl-CoA decarboxylase, which converts malonyl-CoA into acetyl-CoA, are transcriptionally activated by p53. The acetyl-CoA generated from lipid catabolism serves as anaplerotic source for the TCA cycle in transformed cells. p53 also promotes cholesterol excretion and reduces intracellular lipid pools through transcriptional activation of ATP binding cassette subfamily A member 12, Phospholipid transfer lipid protein, carboxyl ester lipase, apolipoprotein B, and caveolin-1 . In contrast, upon metabolic stresses, p53 transactivates carnitine palmitoyltransferase 1 C, an enzyme critical for fatty acid oxidation that promotes tumor progression. This oncogenic role of fatty acid oxidation may protect cancer cells from nutrient stress. Thus, further research is needed to explore the interplay between p53 and lipid catabolism to hinder malignant transformation.

We also observed the lower levels of genes linked to lipid catabolism (acyl-CoA dehydrogenase, HMG-CoA lyase-like 1, and malonyl-CoA decarboxylase) and cholesterol efflux genes (phospholipid transfer lipid protein and carboxyl ester lipase) in mutant p53 tumors (Fig. 2). Of interest, cholesterol-lowering medications, statins (also known as HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors), have shown potential anti-tumor activity, and mutant (Missense) p53 renders cancer cells more sensitive to statin treatment in triple-negative breast and lung cancers. This suggests that mutant p53 plays a pivotal role in maintaining lipid anabolism, which is required for cancer cell growth, and that cholesterol metabolism could be an attractive therapeutic target for p53-mutant tumors.

Overall, p53 regulates lipid metabolic enzymes, at least in part through interactions with signaling molecules to manage lipid metabolism and prevent intracellular lipid accumulation, potentially inhibiting malignant transformation.

CONCLUDING REMARKS

p53 plays a crucial role in maintaining genomic integrity and regulating metabolism to preserve cellular homeostasis and prevent cancer. It controls key pathways in glucose, lipid, and amino acid metabolism, countering the metabolic rewiring seen in cancer cells.

However, challenges in understanding p53's metabolic function arise from studies focusing on changes in enzyme expression or activity rather than assessing the metabolic fluxes. Future investigations should incorporate metabolomics and isotope tracing experiments.

The effects of p53 status on tumor metabolism remain unclear. Wild-type p53 is thought to suppress oncogenic metabolic shifts, while mutant p53 fosters metabolic adaptations that support tumor growth. Nevertheless, investigating global metabolic changes resulting from p53 loss during tumorigenesis, as well as metabolic flux shifts in advanced tumors, caused by p53 deficiency or the acquisition of gain-of-function mutations, are necessary for understanding p53’s role in tumor metabolism.

Emerging insights into p53-mediated metabolic responses offer therapeutic potential. However, p53's ability to protect cells or induce cell death depends on stress type and severity, highlighting the need for further investigation. Research should examine the outcomes of p53 modulation in different environments to inform precision medicine strategies for cancer treatment. As our understanding of p53’s metabolic roles continues to evolve, translating these insights into metabolic interventions will be crucial for overcoming current therapeutic challenges.
