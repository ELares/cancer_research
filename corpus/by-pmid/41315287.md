# REACtiVe-2: phase I evaluation of dendritic cell vaccination and agonistic CD40 therapy following (m)FOLFIRINOX in metastatic pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

In pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), the dense desmoplastic stroma and insufficient infiltrating T cells represent a significant barrier to effective immunotherapy. In this phase I, dose-escalation study, previously registered at the Dutch Trial Register and later on at ClinicalTrial (NCT05650918), we administer an autologous dendritic cell (DC) vaccine (MesoPher) with an agonistic CD40-specific antibody (mitazalimab) to metastatic PDAC patients (n = 16) after (m)FOLFIRINOX treatment. We included patients with WHO performance status 0-1 with accessible metastatic lesions, and excluded patients with history of previous immunotherapy or malignant ascites. Primary objectives include safety and tolerability. Immune modulation and clinical outcomes are monitored as secondary objectives. MesoPher (25 × 106 DCs) is co-administered with 300, 600, or 1200 μg/kg mitazalimab. MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy is safe and well-tolerated, and the primary endpoint is met. One transient dose-limiting toxicity (DLT) is observed (grade 3 fever). MesoPher/mitazalimab induces a systemic increase in activated and vaccine-specific T cell responses. In post-therapy tumor biopsies, increased T cell infiltration and decreased collagen deposition are observed. No objective radiological response is observed, but eight patients (50%) show stable disease after three administrations. In conclusion, MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy is safe and tolerable in patients with metastatic PDAC and enhances systemic immune activation and local immune responses. Future research should evaluate the efficacy of this promising approach as maintenance therapy shortly after completing chemotherapy.

The dense desmoplastic stroma and insufficient infiltrating T cells limit effective immunotherapy in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC). The authors here administer an autologous dendritic cell (DC) vaccine (MesoPher) with an agonistic CD40-specific antibody (mitazalimab) to patients with metastatic PDAC after (m)FOLFIRINOX standard-of-care treatment and find MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy is safe and tolerable, accompanied with enhanced systemic and local immune responses.

Introduction

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is the seventh-leading cause of cancer-related death worldwide and the third-leading cause in the USA. The incidence is increasing by 1.3% annually, and it is projected to become the second-leading cause of cancer-related death by 2030. The 5-year overall survival (OS) rate is around 12%, among the lowest in human malignancies. Most patients are diagnosed with either locally advanced pancreatic cancer (LAPC) or metastatic PDAC. In these cases, multi-agent chemotherapeutic regimens such as (m)FOLFIRINOX (5-fluorouracil, leucovorin, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin) or gemcitabine + nab-paclitaxel are the primary treatment options. Despite this intensive therapy, the prognosis remains dismal, underscoring the need for novel therapeutic strategies.

Immunotherapy, especially immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), is an upcoming treatment option for various solid tumors. However, responses to ICB in patients with advanced PDAC remain extremely low due to the highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), dense desmoplastic stroma, poor immunogenicity, and sparse tumor-specific effector T cell infiltration. Various anti-cancer vaccination strategies, including a personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccine and a mutant KRAS-targeting peptide vaccine, have been developed to reinforce tumor-specific T cell reactivity and show promising clinical results.

Dendritic cells (DCs) are the most potent antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and play a crucial role in the cancer-immunity cycle. DCs are mostly excluded from the TME of PDAC, and increasing their presence in early PDAC lesions has been shown to restore anti-tumor T cell immunity. In our previous single-center, open-label, single-arm, phase I/II REACtiVe trial, patients with resected PDAC were treated with MesoPher after standard-of-care (SOC) chemotherapy. MesoPher is a DC-based immunotherapeutic strategy consisting of autologous monocyte-derived DCs pulsed with an allogeneic-mesothelioma-cell lysate. Due to the presence of shared antigens such as mesothelin, WT1, and survivin between mesothelioma and PDAC, MesoPher could also induce PDAC-reactive immunity. In PDAC, MesoPher therapy was considered safe and feasible, coincided with an estimated 2-year recurrence-free survival (RFS) rate of 64% and 2-year OS rate of 83%, and induced circulating vaccine-specific T cells. These findings aligned with preclinical data obtained in a PDAC murine model, where prophylactic DC vaccination induced CD4+ T cell responses and reduced tumor outgrowth. However, in mice with established tumors, DC vaccination had to be combined with an agonistic CD40 antibody therapy (aCD40) to improve intratumoral T cell influx and delay tumor outgrowth.

Targeting the CD40 receptor with agonistic antibodies could enhance anti-tumor immunity through different mechanisms. aCD40 therapy licenses DCs to cross-present tumor antigens to T cells, thereby promoting tumor-specific T cell responses. It also induces stromalysis, allowing T cells and DCs to infiltrate the tumor, and can reduce intratumoral levels of immunosuppressive M2-like macrophages. aCD40 therapy has demonstrated a good safety profile when administered intravenously in patients with solid tumors. We hypothesized that combining DC vaccination (MesoPher) with a CD40 agonist (mitazalimab) could work synergistically in patients with metastatic PDAC by priming of PDAC-specific T cells and reduction of the intratumoral desmoplastic stromal content, thereby optimally enhancing PDAC-specific immune responses.

In this REACtiVe-2 trial, we aim to overcome the dense desmoplastic stroma and limited intratumoral T cell content in metastatic PDAC by treating patients with DC vaccination and agonistic CD40 antibodies after treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX. This clinical trial is registered before the patients’ recruitment at the Dutch Trial Register, https://onderzoekmetmensen.nl/en/trial/52234, and later on registered at ClinicalTrial.gov with registration number NCT05650918. This combination therapy is safe and tolerable, and induces systemic and local vaccine-specific T cell responses and reduces intratumoral collagen content. This combination therapy may serve as a maintenance therapy following (m)FOLFIRINOX, aiming to stimulate PDAC-specific immune responses in patients with metastatic disease, a major and underserved patient population with an urgent need for effective therapies.

Results

Patient characteristics

Between August 2021 and January 2023, twenty patients with metastatic PDAC were enrolled and underwent a leukapheresis procedure. Figure 1A presents a timeline of the study. Four patients were excluded before starting the study treatment due to clinical deterioration. MesoPher was administered to sixteen patients at a fixed dose of approximately 25 × 106 DCs per administration (1/3 intradermally, 2/3 intravenously). Mitazalimab was administered intravenously, and the dosage was increased according to the 3 + 3 dose escalation design, using three different dose levels: [1] 300 µg/kg, [2] 600 µg/kg, and [3] 1200 µg/kg (predetermined maximum administered dose). Six patients (38%) received mitazalimab dose level 1, three (19%) dose level 2, and seven (44%) dose level 3 (Fig. 1B). The cut-off date for clinical analysis was October 1, 2024.

Baseline characteristics of patients who received the study treatment are presented in Table 1. All patients had received (m)FOLFIRINOX, with a median of 12 cycles (IQR 10.3 – 12.0) before MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy was initiated. Before the start of study treatment, eight patients (50%) had progressive disease (PD), five patients (31%) had stable disease (SD), two patients (13%) had a complete response (CR), and one patient (6.3%) had a partial response (PR), determined according to the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) version 1.1. The first five patients (RT2002-RT2006) were included according to the initial study protocol and had PD after treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX. The following eleven patients (RT2008-RT2022) were included after amendment of the study protocol and had SD or response (CR or PR) after treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX. Furthermore, two patients (RT2005 and RT2006) received two cycles of gemcitabine-nab-paclitaxel as bridging chemotherapy during the waiting period in the vaccine production phase. The baseline and tumor characteristics of individual patients are shown in Supplementary Tables 1 and 2. CA 19.9 levels were >1000 (U/ml) in five patients (31%). The evaluation of CA 19.9 levels for each patient during the study is shown in Supplementary Table 3.

Treatment characteristics

The median interval between inclusion (signing informed consent) and leukapheresis was 0.6 months (IQR: 0.38 – 0.89). The median interval between leukapheresis and first administration of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy was 1.4 months (IQR: 1.28 – 1.80). The majority of patients (thirteen; 81%) received three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations (in weeks 0, 2, and 4), and two patients (RT2010 and RT2014) received two additional booster administrations (at 3 and 6 months). Pre-therapy biopsies of a metastatic lesion could be obtained from twelve patients (75%). Post-therapy biopsies (after three administrations) were obtained from six patients (38%). In total, paired pre- and post-therapy biopsies of a metastatic lesion were available from six patients (38%), and only four of these patients (25%) had paired biopsies containing sufficient tumor tissue for the isolation of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs). One of these four patients (RT2017) had a post-therapy biopsy from a different liver segment than the initial pre-therapy biopsy. The other three patients (RT2019, RT2021, RT2022) had paired biopsies that were obtained from the same metastatic lesion. Biopsy sites per patient are described in Supplementary Table 4. The median follow-up time following inclusion was 6.6 months (IQR: 5.10 – 15.66), calculated from the date of inclusion until death or the cut-off date October 1, 2024.

Safety

Adverse events and radiological responses were assessed for all sixteen patients. To evaluate the DLT, at least six DLT-evaluable patients had to be treated on the highest safe dose level. In compliance with the protocol, the predetermined maximum administered dose was 25 × 106 DCs with 1200 µg/kg mitazalimab per administration. Seven patients were treated on this predetermined maximum administered dose (dose level 3). The MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy was well-tolerated. Most observed treatment-related adverse events (AEs) were grade 1 and 2 (Table 2). Cytokine Release Syndrome (CRS) grade 1, scored according to the ASTCT guidelines, was observed in fourteen patients (88%) and consisted solely of fever. Grade 2 or higher CRS was not observed. Only one DLT was observed; patient RT2017 in dose level 3 experienced a treatment-related grade 3 AE (fever grade 3 (40.4 °C) after the second MesoPher/mitazalimab administration). The fever resolved to grade 2 within 1.5 hours after oral paracetamol, and all symptoms resolved the next day. No grade 4 AE was observed, and a maximum tolerable dose was not reached. All related adverse events subdivided per dose level are listed in Supplementary Table 5.

Radiological responses and survival

The survival per patient is depicted in a swimmer plot with a cut-off date of October 1, 2024 (Fig. 2A). Eight patients (50%) had SD and six patients (38%) had PD on the first evaluation CT after three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations (Supplementary Table 1). No radiological response assessment was possible in two patients (13%) due to clinical deterioration. One patient (RT2004) had PD on (m)FOLFIRINOX before the start of the study treatment and showed SD on CT scan evaluation two weeks after the third administration. One patient (RT2010) had an ongoing complete response (CR) from baseline until the cut-off date.

At the time of data cut-off, five patients (31%) had surpassed a survival of 9 months in the study. These five patients had radiological responses at baseline before starting the study treatment. One patient (RT2010) with CR at baseline has an ongoing CR after study treatment for a remarkable 26 months. Patients with radiological non-progressive disease at baseline, before the start of study treatment (non-PD at baseline), had a longer PFS and OS compared to patients with disease progression (PD at baseline) (Fig. 2B and C). In non-PD at baseline patients, median PFS and OS were 2.76 (IQR: 2.40 − 6.86) and 12.1 months (IQR: 5.74 − 21.77), respectively, with a 1-year PFS rate of 13% and OS rate of 50%. In PD at baseline patients, the median PFS and OS were 1.17 (IQR: 0.86 − 1.40) and 2.5 months (IQR: 1.26 − 3.88), respectively. When all patients are combined, the median PFS and OS from the start date of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy was 1.9 (IQR: 1.2 – 2.8) and 4.4 months (IQR: 2.4 – 13.0), respectively (Supplementary Fig. 1). The 1-year PFS rate was 6.3%, and the 1-year OS rate was 25%.

Following this clinical study, six patients initiated subsequent systemic therapy. Of these, five patients started this systemic therapy following PD after receiving three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations. One patient had started after five administrations. Further details about subsequent systemic therapy per patient are listed in Supplementary Table 1.

Systemic immune activation

Peripheral blood samples collected at different time points during the study were used to assess the magnitude and specificity of systemic immune responses induced by MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy. Blood samples from week 0, week 2 (two weeks after the first administration), week 4 (two weeks after the second administration), and week 6 (two weeks after the third administration) from patients of dose levels 2 and 3 (n = 10) were included in the immune monitoring analysis.

The direct effect of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy on leukocyte subsets in the peripheral blood was quantified directly ex vivo. A significant increase in the absolute number of granulocytes (week 0 vs 6, p = 0.037), mature neutrophils (week 0 vs 6, p = 0.027), and NK cells was observed (week 0 vs 2, p = 0.049; week 0 vs 6, p = 0.049), while the absolute number of B cells was significantly decreased (week 0 vs 2, p = 0.004; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.002; week 0 vs 6, p = 0.027) (Supplementary Fig. 2). In contrast, the absolute numbers of CD3+, CD4+, CD8+ T cells, and DCs remained unchanged.

Next, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were analyzed with flow cytometry to examine the activation status of the immune cells on a protein level. Most systemic immune changes were observed after the first administration and were restricted to the lymphoid compartment. No major differences were observed between dose levels 2 and 3 (Fig. 3A, Supplementary Fig. 3, 4).

Within the T cell compartment, CD3+ T cell frequencies remained unchanged. The frequency of CD8+ T cells was significantly decreased following the first MesoPher/mitazalimab administration (p = 0.002), with subsequent stabilization during the study treatment period. In contrast to CD8+ T cells, the frequency of regulatory CD4+ T cells (CD4+ Tregs) increased after the first administration (p = 0.004) but stabilized thereafter (Fig. 3A). Compared to week 0, we observed an increased frequency of conventional CD4+ T cells within the total CD3+ T cell population after the first administration (p = 0.027). Within this population, the frequency of central memory CD4+ T cells (Tcm) increased after the first administration (p = 0.020), while the frequency of effector memory CD4+ T cells (Tem) was decreased (p = 0.033) (Fig. 3B). A similar decrease was observed in CD4+ effector memory T cells re-expressing CD45RA (Temra). No treatment-induced difference was observed within the distribution of CD8+ T cell subsets. Next, we assessed the activation status of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. Activation marker ICOS was significantly higher expressed on conventional CD4+ T cells after the first and second administrations (week 0 vs 2, p = 0.002; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.010), and expression of HLA-DR was similarly upregulated (Fig. 3C). Within the conventional CD4+ T cell population, these markers were specifically upregulated on CD4+ Tcm (HLA-DR: week 0 vs 2, p = 0.002; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.004; week 0 vs 6, p = 0.049 and ICOS: week 0 vs 2, p = 0.002; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.011) and Tem cells (HLA-DR: week 0 vs 2, p = 0.025; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.010 and ICOS: week 0 vs 2, p = 0.004; week 0 vs 4, p = 0.010) but not on CD8+ Tcm or Tem cells (Fig. 3D).

To gain a broader insight into the activation status of the systemic lymphoid and myeloid immune compartments, we performed gene-expression profiling on PBMCs using the NanoString nCounter platform (Fig. 4A). Using a likelihood-ratio test, 74 genes were found to be differentially expressed over time (Fig. 4B, Supplementary Data 1). Most of these genes were specifically increased in their expression after the first administration (week 2). Several of these DEGs are involved in pathways associated with MHC class-II-mediated antigen processing and presentation (e.g., HLA-DMB, HLA-DPA1, HLA-DPB1), inflammation (e.g., CASP1, MYD88, IRF5) or T cell activation and regulation (CD86, CD46, RELA, TNFRSF14) (Fig. 4C). These data demonstrate that MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy induces T cell activation.

Systemic vaccine-specific T cell responses

Since increased systemic T cell activation was observed after MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations, we performed an in vitro co-culture assay to investigate whether these T cells were vaccine-specific (Fig. 5A). PMBCs obtained pre-therapy (week 0) or post-therapy (week 6) were co-cultured with MesoPher from the same patient (n = 5). To exclude KLH-specific responses, MesoPher was prepared with and without the immunostimulatory carrier protein keyhole limpet hemocyanin (KLH) for each patient.

Co-culture of PBMCs with MesoPher with KLH ( + KLH) demonstrated the vaccine-directed reactivity of CD4+ T cells and CD8+ T cells in post-therapy samples (Fig. 5B). On CD4+ T cells obtained post therapy, expression of CD25 (p = 0.017), CD69 (p = 0.008) and ICOS (p < 0.0001) was significantly increased after co-culture with MesoPher + KLH, while CD25 (p = 0.010), CD69 (p = 0.009) and CD137 (p = 0.046) expression was increased on CD8+ T cells obtained post therapy (Fig. 5C).

To study the specific response to tumor antigens, PBMCs were co-cultured with MesoPher without KLH (- KLH), revealing a significantly higher expression of activation markers CD137 (p = 0.027), CD25 (p = 0.003), ICOS (p = 0.007), and increased expression of CD69 on CD4+ T cells that were obtained post-therapy, but not CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5D, E). Additionally, we observed significantly increased IFN-γ (p = 0.029) and Granzyme B levels (pre: p = 0.039 and post: p = 0.002) in the supernatant, especially in the post-therapy setting (Fig. 5F). These results demonstrate that three administrations of MesoPher/mitazalimab can induce vaccine-specific effector CD4+ T cells in the circulation.

T cell presence in biopsy tissue

We next assessed whether MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy could induce changes in the tumor microenvironment. First, we performed an immunohistochemical staining for CD3 and CD8 on the available pre- and post-therapy biopsies from the metastatic lesions to specifically investigate the effect of three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations on the intratumoral presence of cells (Fig. 6A). Although we did not observe a significant difference in CD3+ T cell content between all available pre- and post-therapy biopsies (Fig. 6B), analysis of the paired biopsies from four patients revealed a significant increase in intratumoral CD3+ T cell presence post-therapy (p = 0.028) (Fig. 6C). A similar trend was observed regarding the intratumoral presence of CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6A-C).

Specificity of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes

To assess whether tumor-infiltrating T cells (TILs) were vaccine-specific, co-cultures with expanded TILs and MesoPher + KLH or MesoPher – KLH were performed. Both CD4+ (Fig. 7A) and CD8+ (Fig. 7B) TILs demonstrated increased activation when co-cultured with MesoPher + KLH, with increased expression of activation markers such as CD137, ICOS, and CD25 specifically on TILs obtained after therapy. Post-therapy TILs also produced significantly more IFN-γ after co-culture with MesoPher + KLH (Fig. 7C). Importantly, post-therapy TILs also responded to MesoPher - KLH (Fig. 7D-F), indicating their specificity toward tumor antigens presented by these DCs.

Collagen density in biopsy tissue

PDAC tumors are often characterized by dense desmoplastic stroma, a barrier to effective T cell infiltration. Since MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy resulted in increased intratumoral T cell presence, we hypothesized that this could be attributed to the stromalysis-inducing properties of mitazalimab. Therefore, we assessed the level of α smooth muscle actin (αSMA, a marker for fibroblasts) and collagen (Col1a1) in biopsy tissue (Fig. 8A, B). In a pooled analysis of pre- and post-therapy biopsies, we did not observe a difference in αSMA levels, but the presence of collagen was significantly reduced by almost 50% (p = 0.039) (Fig. 8C). These findings show that MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy can increase intratumoral T cell content and reduce intratumoral collagen content.

Discussion

In this single-arm, open-label, dose-escalation phase I study, we investigated the safety and tolerability of MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy. We explored its immunomodulatory effects in patients with metastatic PDAC after completion of SOC treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX. MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy was considered a safe and well-tolerated treatment option. This combination therapy increased the systemic frequency and activation of CD4+ T cells and induced vaccine-specific T cell responses. In post-therapy biopsies, a decrease in intratumoral collagen deposition coincided with increased intratumoral T cell presence.

The primary goal of this study was to evaluate the safety of MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy. Treatment with MesoPher/mitazalimab demonstrated a comparable toxicity profile as MesoPher and mitazalimab monotherapies. Only one DLT was observed, which was resolved within two hours. Fever, injection site reaction, chills, and fatigue were the most common grade 1 adverse events, indicating therapy-induced activation of the immune system. Based on these observed AEs, MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy is less toxic than SOC chemotherapeutic regimens.

Similar to the observations in our recent REACtiVe study which investigated MesoPher monotherapy in resected PDAC patients, we found that MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy preferentially activated the CD4+ T cell compartment. Gene expression profiling showed that MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy predominantly induces antigen processing and presentation via MHC-II, further confirming the dominance of CD4+ T cell responses over CD8+ T cell responses. Similar activation of CD4+ T cells was observed in pleural mesothelioma patients treated with MesoPher monotherapy, confirming that MesoPher can induce potent CD4+ T cell responses in multiple tumor types.

We further demonstrated that a proportion of these CD4+ T cell responses was directed against vaccine-specific antigens. In contrast to the CD4+ Tcm cells, the frequency of circulating CD4+ Tem cells and CD8+ T cells decreased after the first MesoPher/mitazalimab administration. This reduction might be explained by the migration of these cells towards the tumor. Analysis of the post-therapy biopsies confirmed the increased presence of intratumoral T cells, accompanied by a reduction of intratumoral collagen deposition. Mitazalimab might have mediated this observed decrease in collagen. Since a dense desmoplastic stroma (including collagen fibers) has been proposed as one of the main contributors to immune exclusion in PDAC, Mitazalimab-mediated stromalysis might have contributed to the increased intratumoral T cell presence.

This small and heterogenous cohort of patients limited the evaluation of clinical benefit of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy in metastatic PDAC. Furthermore, obtaining paired biopsies from the same lesion containing sufficient tumor was challenging due to anatomical constraints or clinical considerations, resulting in a small number of paired biopsy samples. However, the observed immune activation and vaccine-specific responses, as well as increased T cell presence in the tumor, do underscore the potential applicability of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy in PDAC, mainly when initiated soon after or simultaneously with the completion of systemic chemotherapy. However, additional therapeutic strategies are needed to enhance clinical benefit. For instance, both the OPTIMIZE-1 and the PRINCE trials have added aCD40 antibodies to first-line chemotherapy (gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel or (m)FOLFIRINOX, respectively) in metastatic PDAC patients. In the OPTIMIZE-1 trial, the combination of (m)FOLFIRINOX and mitazalimab resulted in a 1-year OS rate of 59.3% compared to the historical 1-year OS of 48.1% for (m)FOLFIRINOX monotherapy. In the PRINCE trial, the combination of aCD40 (sotigalimab) and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel resulted in a 1-year OS of 48.1% compared to a historical 1-year OS of 35% for gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel monotherapy.

Although a combination of MesoPher and nivolumab has already been shown to be safe and feasible and induce clinical efficacy in patients with malignant pleural mesothelioma, the addition of immune checkpoint inhibitor nivolumab to aCD40 and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel did not improve OS (1-year OS rate: 41.3%) in the PRINCE trial but was deemed safe and feasible. Interestingly, the combination of nivolumab and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel (without aCD40) resulted in a 1-year OS of 57.5%. The authors note that the PRINCE study was not powered to compare treatment arms, and we should not conclude that the combination of aCD40 and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel is inferior to combined nivolumab and gemcitabine/nab-paclitaxel therapy. Instead, this study advocates for the identification of predictive biomarkers associated with survival for different therapeutic regimens, thereby allowing the selection of patient subsets that are most likely to benefit from a specific therapeutic regimen.

To conclude, MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy demonstrates a favorable safety and tolerability profile for patients with metastatic PDAC who have undergone prior treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX. This therapy induces vaccine-specific T cell responses, enhances intratumoral T cell infiltration, and reduces intratumoral collagen deposition. In a future study, we will further evaluate the clinical efficacy of the combination of agonistic CD40 antibodies and MesoPher in combination with gemcitabine to further increase cytotoxicity and immunomodulation in patients with metastatic PDAC without disease progression after (m)FOLFIRINOX.

Methods

Study design

This was a single-center, open-label, dose-escalation phase I study. Patients were treated with MesoPher (monocyte-derived DCs pulsed with an allogeneic-mesothelioma-cell lysate) and mitazalimab (an agonistic CD40 antibody) provided by Alligator Bioscience AB. Dose escalation was done using a modified toxicity probability interval (mTPI) design. Predefined rules based on the number of patients treated on a specific dose and the number of observed dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs) determine the dose of the following patient (Supplementary Table 6). MesoPher was given at a fixed dose of approximately 25 × 106 DCs per administration. The dose of mitazalimab was increased using three different dose levels: [1] 300 µg/kg, [2] 600 µg/kg, and [3] 1200 µg/kg mitazalimab (predetermined maximum administered dose). If toxicities would occur on dose level 1, de-escalation to dose level −1, and if needed −2, was possible. At least three patients were treated before escalating to the next dose level. A minimum of six patients were treated on the predetermined maximum administered dose.

The Central Committee on Research Involving Human Subjects (NL76592.000.21) approved the study protocol and all amendments as defined by the Medical Research Involving Human Subjects Act. The procedures followed were in accordance with the ethical standards of these committees on human experimentation and with the Helsinki Declaration of 1975, as revised in 2008. The trial was initially registered in the Dutch Trial Register (Netherlands Trial Register, NTR; https://onderzoekmetmensen.nl/en/trial/52234) before the patients’ recruitment, followed by registration on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05650918). Written informed consent was obtained from each patient, which includes consent to publish (de-identified) clinical data and results.

Patients

The first patient was enrolled on August 30, 2021, and the last patient was enrolled on January 31, 2023. Key inclusion criteria were: (1) standard-of-care treatment with (m)FOLFIRINOX for LAPC or metastatic PDAC, (2) inclusion ≤6 weeks after the last cycle of (m)FOLFIRINOX, (3) Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of ≤ 1, and (4) negative HIV, hepatitis B, hepatitis C, HTLV type 1 or type 2 viral screening and negative lues serology. Exclusion criteria were: (1) prior second-line chemotherapy for LAPC or metastatic PDAC, (2) current or previous use of anti-tumor vaccinations or aCD40, (3) use of steroids, (4) abdominal ascites and (5) previous other malignancies. All further inclusion and exclusion criteria are listed in detail in the clinical trial protocol, which is added as Supplementary Note 1.

Procedures

A leukapheresis procedure was performed to harvest CD14+ monocytes, which were used to produce MesoPher (autologous DCs pulsed with an allogeneic mesothelioma tumor cell lysate) in a 10-day culture. The detailed procedure for generating MesoPher was described previously. Just before the start of the study treatment, a baseline CT scan and, if possible, a tumor biopsy of an accessible metastasis was performed. The study treatment comprised three bi-weekly administrations of MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy, followed by a fourth and fifth administration three and six months later as a booster in case of no disease progression. MesoPher was administered intradermally on the volar side of the forearm (1/3 of total vaccine volume) and intravenously in the brachialis vein of the ipsilateral arm (2/3 of total vaccine volume). On the same day, just after DC administration, mitazalimab was administered intravenously according to the protocol (Supplementary Note 1). After the infusion, patients were monitored for four hours after the first administration and two hours after subsequent administrations. Patients used medication before and after the mitazalimab infusion to minimize the risk of infusion-related adverse events (AEs). Patients used cetirizine (10 mg) and montelukast (10 mg) daily, starting three days before and continuing until two days after the infusion. In addition, they received paracetamol (1000 mg), famotidine (40 mg), and ondansetron (8 mg) on the day of the infusion. Two weeks after the third administration, a response CT scan and, if possible, a post-therapy tumor biopsy were performed. In case a tumor biopsy was not feasible, a delayed-type hypersensitivity (DHT) skin test with MesoPher (positive control) and infusion medium (negative control) was performed. In case of a positive response (i.e., ≥ 2 mm induration) to MesoPher and no response to infusion medium after 48 hours, a 3 mm punch biopsy was performed. Regular blood draws were performed for clinical assessment and immune monitoring purposes. Regular CT scans were made to monitor disease evolution. Some disease progression would allow for continuation with study treatment in case of no other treatment options and adequate tolerability. The clinical trial protocol lists all procedures in detail (Supplementary Note 1).

Protocol amendment

Initially, we only included patients with disease progression on (m)FOLFIRINOX. However, this resulted in unacceptable dropouts before initiation of study treatment (due to late referral, screening and vaccine production time) or early after the first administration. Therefore, we amended the protocol, and only patients with stable disease or response on (m)FOLFIRINOX were included. Furthermore, we added the criterion that patients had to be included within four weeks after stopping (m)FOLFIRINOX chemotherapy to decrease the time between referral and first administration of the study treatment. Lastly, because patients were included earlier after (m)FOLFIRINOX, bridging chemotherapy with gemcitabine-nab-paclitaxel was no longer an option. Patients RT2002-RT2006 were included with the initial criteria (Supplementary Note 2) and were allowed to receive gemcitabine-nab-paclitaxel to bridge the time between leukapheresis and start of the study treatment. Patients RT2008-RT2022 were included using the requirements of the amended protocol (Supplementary Note 1).

Clinical outcomes

The primary endpoint of this study was the frequency of DLTs. Toxicities were scored according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (version 5.0, published November 27, 2017). Any therapy-related grade 3 or higher AE occurring within six weeks of the first administration was considered a DLT, except for toxicities listed in Supplementary Table 7. A secondary endpoint was the assessment of radiological response according to the RECIST version 1.1.

Immune monitoring samples

Peripheral blood samples were collected at different time points during the study, as shown in Fig. 1A. Analysis was limited to patients in dose levels 2 and 3 (n = 10) since patients in dose level 1 had clinical deterioration and blood samples could not be collected. We also excluded peripheral blood samples obtained after the fourth and fifth MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations since only two patients received these booster administrations. In addition to the peripheral blood samples, biopsies of metastatic lesions were collected when feasible, before start of the study treatment and after three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations (week 6). Biopsy sites and the performed analysis are depicted in Supplementary Table 4.

Flow cytometry

Whole blood was freshly stained for flow cytometry to enumerate different immune cell subsets. In addition, cryopreserved peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were stained for flow cytometry analyses to investigate peripheral blood T cell activation after treatment. PBMCs from four time points were included in the analysis: week 0, before the first vaccination; week 2, two weeks after the first vaccination; week 4, two weeks after the second vaccination; and week 6, two weeks after the third vaccination. In short, PBMCs were thawed and stained with fluorescently labeled antibodies for surface markers for 30 minutes at 4° Celsius (Supplementary Table 8). Cells were subsequently incubated with Fixable Viability Dye (#65-0863-18, eBioscience) for 15 minutes at 4° Celsius, followed by fixation and permeabilization (FoxP3 Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set, #00-5523-00, eBioscience). Finally, cells were incubated with fluorescently labeled antibodies targeting intracellular markers for 30 minutes at 4° Celsius. Acquisition was performed by a FACSymphony A5 (BD Biosciences) using BD FACSDiva software (BD Biosciences) and analyzed by FlowJo v10.7. The gating strategy is depicted in Supplementary Fig. 5.

Gene expression profiling

Total mRNA was isolated from PBMCs of patients using a Tempus Spin RNA Isolation kit (#4380204, Applied Biosystems, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA was hybridized with mRNA detection probes (Supplementary Data 2) from two NanoString nCounter gene expression panels: the Myeloid V2 Panel and the PanCancer Immune Profiling Panel (NanoString, Seattle, WA, USA) according to manufacturer’s instructions. Samples were further processed on a NanoString Prep Station, and gene expression data readout was generated using NanoString nCounter Digital Analyzer.

Raw counts and standard NanoString nCounter QC parameters were determined with nSolver v4.0 software (NanoString). A limit of detection (LOD) was calculated as the mean level of detection of all negative control probes in all samples that passed standard NanoString nCounter quality control plus three standard deviations. Samples with fewer than 400 genes with raw counts above LOD were excluded. Genes not expressed above LOD at least one PBMC collection point (week 0, 2, 4, or 6) were excluded. Raw expression counts were normalized using Upper Quartile normalization followed by removal of unwanted variation using RUVseq as previously described, using RUVr with k = 1. Differential expression analysis was performed with DESeq2, using a Likelihood Ratio Test with full model: [~ Dose + W_1 + Collection_time] and reduced model: [~ Dose + W_1], where W_1 are the RUV factors. An adjusted p-value of <0.01 was considered statistically significant. Log2-transformed normalized counts were used to generate one heatmap using Morpheus. When a gene was present in both panels, we included only the expression data from the PanCancer Immune Profiling Panel. One minus Pearson correlation was used for the hierarchical clustering of the rows.

Generation of T cell cultures from biopsies

In addition to the peripheral blood samples, paired pre- and post-therapy biopsies of metastases were collected from six patients after three MesoPher/mitazalimab administrations to isolate and expand tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs). Tumor biopsies were dissociated using a tumor dissociation kit (#130-095-929, Miltenyi Biotec), and CD3+ T cells were isolated by MACS sorting (#130-097-043, Milteny Biotec) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. CD3+ T cells were cultured in T cell culture medium consisting of IMDM (#12440053, Gibco) supplemented with fetal calf serum (10%; Bodinco), penicillin (100 IU/mL; #15140122, ThermoFisher Scientific), streptomycin (100 µg/mL; #15140122, ThermoFisher Scientific), and β-Mercaptoethanol (0.1%; #M6250-100ML, Sigma-Aldrich). CD3+ T cells were expanded for 21 days in T cell culture medium in the presence of Dynabeads Human T-activator CD3/CD28 (ratio 1:3; #11131D, Gibco), IL-2/TCGF (10%; #0801114, ZeptoMetrix), IL-7 (5 ng/mL; #200-07-10UG, PeproTech), IL-15 (5 ng/mL; #200-15-10UG, PeproTech), and IL-21 (5 ng/mL; #200-21-10UG, PeproTech). Irradiated EBV-transformed B cells (BLCL; 75 Gray) and PBMCs (35 Gray) were used as feeder cells and added to the CD3+ T cells in a ratio of 1:5 (BLCL) and 5:1 (PBMCs). Cultures were split when necessary, and the T cell culture medium was refreshed every 2-3 days. T cell expansion of both pre- and post-therapy biopsies was successful in four out of six patients. After 21 days, expanded T cell cultures were obtained, cryopreserved and stored for further analysis.

Vaccine-specific responses

To investigate whether the MesoPher/mitazalimab combination therapy induced vaccine-specific immune responses, PBMCs and TILs from blood or tissue obtained pre-therapy or post-therapy (week 6) were co-cultured with the DC vaccine at a 10:1 Effector: Target ratio for 24 hours at 37° Celsius. The MesoPher DC vaccine contains keyhole limpet hemocyanin (KLH), an immunostimulant. Since KLH might also drive a cell-mediated immune response, we prepared individual MesoPher vaccines without KLH for all patients included in the co-culture (n = 5 for PBMCs, n = 4 for TILs) to separate vaccine-specific responses from KLH-specific responses. After 24 hours of co-culture with the DC vaccine (with or without KLH), PBMCs or TILs were harvested and subjected to flow cytometry as described above (see Supplementary Table 8 for a list of antibodies used). IFN-γ (#DY285B-05, R&D Systems) and Granzyme B (#DY2906-05, R&D Systems) levels in the supernatant were detected using ELISA.

Immunohistochemistry

Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tissue from metastatic PDAC tissue in the liver, soft tissue, or lymph node was obtained for immunohistochemistry analysis. Paired pre- and post-therapy biopsies were available from four patients (Supplementary Table 4). Immunohistochemistry staining was performed on 4 µm sections by automated IHC using the Ventana Benchmark ULTRA (Ventana Medical Systems Inc.). After short heat-induced antigen retrieval with CC1 (#950-500, Ventana), sections were stained for CD3 (0.1 µg/mL, 12 minutes, Ventana), αSMA (0.21 µg/mL, 32 minutes, DAKO), or COL1a1 (1:3000, 32 minutes, Novus Biologicals) at 37° Celsius. Hereafter, sections were counterstained using hematoxylin II for 8 minutes (Ventana), and the signal was detected using Optiview detection (#760-700, Ventana; CD3, COL1a1) or Ultraview detection (#760-500, Ventana; αSMA). Stained sections were digitized using a NanoZoomer automated digital slide scanner (Hamamatsu Photonics). For each biopsy, tumor regions were digitally annotated by a dedicated pathologist using the NDP.view 2 program (version 2.9.25). The number of intratumoral CD3+ T cells per µm2 tumor was calculated using positive cell detection in QuPath (version 0.3.2). Pixel classification was used to calculate the percentage of intratumoral area stained positive for αSMA or COL1a1. Scripts were generated for the analysis of the immunohistochemical stainings and ran on all individual biopsies to automate the detection process (Supplementary Note 3). The fold change between pre- and post-therapy biopsies was calculated, and data was visualized and analyzed using GraphPad Prism software (version 8.0.2).

Statistical analysis

Progression free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) were calculated from the start date of MesoPher/mitazalimab therapy until the date of progression or date of death using the Kaplan-Meier method. Statistical analyses and data visualization were performed using R (version 4.3.2), IBM SPSS Statistics (version 28.0.1.0), and GraphPad Prism (version 8.0.2). NanoString data was analyzed as described above. For flow cytometry data, differences between individual time points and baseline (week 0) were compared using the Wilcoxon matched-paired signed rank test. For histology data, the fold change between pre- and post-therapy biopsies was calculated and significant differences were determined using paired t tests. Co-culture data was analyzed by two-way ANOVA using Sidak’s multiple comparisons test. Significance was assumed when p < 0.05.
