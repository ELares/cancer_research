# Advances and obstacles of T cell-based immunotherapy in gynecological malignancies.

## Abstract

T cell-mediated immunotherapy has revolutionized oncology by enabling precision immune responses against malignant cells. Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, which involves genetically reprogramming T lymphocytes to recognize tumor-specific antigens, has shown clinical success in hematologic malignancies and is expanding its potential in solid tumors. Gynecological cancers, including ovarian, cervical, and endometrial carcinomas, present persistent therapeutic challenges due to their aggressive recurrence patterns and limited responses to conventional therapies in advanced disease stages. This review offers a comprehensive analysis of CAR-T cell therapy advancements in gynecologic oncology, examining fundamental biological mechanisms of tumor-immune interactions, clinical progress in target antigen validation, and innovative approaches to counter immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments. Key challenges specific to these malignancies are discussed, such as molecular heterogeneity in endometrial tumors, ascites-mediated T-cell dysfunction in ovarian cancer (OC), and viral antigen dynamics in HPV- driven cervical carcinomas. Recent clinical evidence shows improved therapeutic outcomes through optimized CAR architectures and preconditioning regimens, with objective response rates demonstrating progressive enhancement across successive clinical trial generations. The discussion addresses ongoing limitations regarding treatment durability and manufacturing consistency while exploring emerging solutions such as synthetic biology approaches and multi-omics guided antigen selection. By integrating preclinical insights with translational clinical data, this work establishes a strategic framework for advancing adoptive T-cell therapies in gynecologic oncology, emphasizing the synergistic potential of combining CAR-T technology with personalized neoantigen vaccines and microenvironment-reprogramming agents. These collective advances underscore the transformative prospects of engineered T-cell immunotherapies while providing actionable strategies to overcome the unique biological barriers inherent to female reproductive tract malignancies.

Graphic abstract

Introduction

Gynecological malignancies, including ovarian cancer (OC), cervical cancer (CC), and endometrial cancer (EC), are characterized by distinct etiologies and therapeutic challenges. OC, the most lethal, usually begins in the fallopian tube or ovarian epithelium and is often detected late because it progresses without symptoms. Initial treatment involves cytoreductive surgery and platinum-taxane chemotherapy, achieving remission in 80% of cases. However, over 60% relapse within two years due to molecular heterogeneity, BRCA1/2 mutations, and immunosuppressive ascites fostering platinum resistance. PARP inhibitors show promise in homologous recombination deficient subgroups, but peritoneal dissemination and TME-mediated drug tolerance remain significant hurdles. CC is primarily caused by HPV, underscoring global health disparities, with 90% of deaths in low-resource regions without vaccination and screening. Cisplatin-based chemoradiation is standard for locally advanced disease, but pelvic recurrence and radiation-induced fibrosis are common. Metastatic CC has limited treatments, with anti-PD-1 agents showing a clinical response rate of less than 15% in unselected populations due to HPV-induced MHC-I downregulation. EC, once linked to estrogen-driven proliferation, now faces rising aggressive non-endometrioid subtypes related to obesity and metabolic dysfunction. Early-stage endometrioid tumors are often curable by hysterectomy, but advanced or mismatch repair deficiency (MMRd) variants progress rapidly. Immunotherapy, such as anti-PD-1 for MSI-H/MMRd tumors, shows promise, but molecular complications limit treatment efficacy. Most recently, non-operative management has emerged as a paradigm-shifting approach for early MMRd cancers. Neoadjuvant dostarlimab (PD-1 blockade) induced clinical complete responses in 84/103 (81.6%) patients with locally advanced MMRd tumors, enabling organ preservation in 82 cases without compromising surgical options. This strategy demonstrates particular relevance for fertility-preserving management of EC. Treatment of gynecological malignancies is challenging because of molecular heterogeneity, microenvironment-driven therapeutic resistance, and adaptive immune evasion mechanisms. These interconnected biological barriers undermine treatment efficacy, driving recurrence and limiting durable responses across ovarian, cervical, and endometrial carcinomas.

Recent advancements in cancer immunotherapy have revolutionized treatment paradigms. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), bispecific antibodies, and neoantigen vaccines have shown unparalleled efficacy across various cancers. The success of PD-1/PD-L1 blockade in melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) has spurred combination strategies, including pairing ICIs with oncolytic viruses or metabolic modulators, to circumvent adaptive resistance. Furthermore, tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy has achieved durable responses in refractory solid tumors, with a high complete remission rate in metastatic melanoma. These developments highlight immunotherapy’s potential to transition oncology from nonspecific cytotoxic treatments to precision immune modulation.

T cell-based immunotherapy is pivotal in modern immuno-oncology and essential for pioneering new cancer treatments. By harnessing T cells’ inherent capacity to identify and attack tumor-specific antigens, these therapies selectively eliminate malignant cells while preserving healthy tissues. Adoptive cell therapies (ACT), such as Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy, T-cell receptor (TCR) therapy, and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), have revolutionized cancer treatment by boosting antitumor immunity and enhancing the body’s immune response. These therapies offer long-lasting responses, but require careful safety monitoring as demonstrated in vaccine studies where injection site reactions were the most common adverse event and personalized approaches tailored to specific cancer antigens. The effectiveness of T cell-based immunotherapy lies in its ability to mobilize and expand the immune system to precisely target cancer, addressing the limitations of conventional therapies like chemotherapy and radiation. As research progresses, these therapies become more sophisticated, broadening treatment options for various malignancies. However, applying CAR-T therapy to solid tumors presents distinct challenges that require innovative solutions. Unlike hematologic cancers, where CAR-T therapy has been successful, solid tumors present significant obstacles due to their immunosuppressive microenvironments, characterized by hypoxia, high interstitial pressure, and immunosuppressive cytokines, which hinder T cell infiltration and function. Additionally, the heterogeneity of solid tumors, with varying or evolving antigen profiles, complicates effective targeting and can result in immune escape. Stromal barriers, such as dense extracellular matrices and fibroblasts, further impede T cell penetration and infiltration. These factors collectively limit CAR-T therapy’s efficacy in solid tumors. Recent frameworks now classify resistance mechanisms into clinically actionable archetypes. The immune resistance archetype model identifies five conserved features enabling immune evasion: (1) effector cell exclusion, (2) impaired antigen recognition, (3) T-cell dysfunction/death, (4) immunosuppressive cells, and (5) exogenous suppression. High-dimensional profiling of these archetypes in gynecologic malignancies could guide rational combination therapies. Overcoming these barriers requires novel strategies to enhance T cell persistence, improve tumor infiltration, and modulate the immune microenvironment to support immune cell activity. Addressing these challenges could unlock CAR-T therapy’s potential in solid tumors, transforming treatment for many cancer types.

This review offers a comprehensive evaluation with a dedicated focus on CAR- and TCR-engineered T cell therapies specifically for gynecologic cancers. It assesses the clinical advancements in these therapies, focusing on overcoming obstacles like stromal barriers and antigen heterogeneity. Additionally, the article explores innovative strategies to boost treatment efficacy, including combinatorial approaches that merge immune therapies with microenvironment-modulating agents, and identifies key areas for future research in this domain.

T cell-based immunotherapy in cancer treatment

Cancer immunity cycle

The cancer immunity cycle represents a fundamental framework for understanding how the immune system interacts with tumor cells and facilitates antitumor responses (Fig. 1). It is a dynamic and multi-step process that begins when cancer cells release tumor antigens, which are recognized by dendritic cells. These antigen-presenting cells migrate to lymph nodes to prime naive T cells to recognize and respond to the tumor-specific antigens. The primed T cells are activated and proliferate, and they subsequently circulate through the bloodstream to infiltrate the tumor site. Once they encounter the tumor cells expressing the target antigens, T cells bind to these tumor cells and initiate cytotoxic responses that lead to tumor cell death.

A crucial feature of the cancer immunity cycle is the continuous interaction between tumor cells and immune cells, which requires effective communication and recognition processes. However, this cycle is often disrupted by various tumor evasion mechanisms, such as immune checkpoint activation, which can inhibit T cell activation, and the presence of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) that can hinder T cell infiltration and function. Moreover, tumor cells may undergo antigen loss or present altered antigens that make them less recognizable by the immune system, leading to immune escape.

ICIs and their role in cancer therapy

ICIs have transformed cancer treatment by targeting specific pathways that tumors exploit to escape immune surveillance. Under normal circumstances, immune checkpoints serve as critical regulators of immune homeostasis, preventing excessive immune responses that could damage normal tissues. Key immune checkpoints include the programmed cell death protein 1(PD-1) receptor and its ligand PD-L1, as well as cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4). The PD-1/PD-L1 axis plays a pivotal role in inhibiting T cell activation and preventing autoimmunity, as PD-L1 on tumor cells binds to PD-1 on T cells, effectively turning off the immune response. Similarly, CTLA-4, expressed on T cells, acts as an inhibitory receptor, competing with the co-stimulatory receptor CD28 to prevent full T cell activation. Tumors often hijack these checkpoints by upregulating PD-L1 or CTLA-4 expression, thereby creating an immunosuppressive microenvironment that allows for immune evasion.

ICIs, including anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, and anti-CTLA-4 antibodies, are a class of immunotherapeutic agents that function by blocking inhibitory signals in the immune system, thus reactivating T cells and enabling them to target and destroy cancer cells. These therapies have revolutionized the treatment of various cancers by overcoming immune evasion mechanisms that tumors use to suppress immune responses.

Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors

The PD-1/PD-L1 axis plays a critical role in the immune system’s ability to differentiate between self and non-self. However, tumors exploit this pathway to evade immune surveillance. PD-1 is expressed on activated T cells, and when it binds to its ligand PD-L1 on tumor cells or tumor-associated cells, it inhibits T cell activation and function. Inhibiting this pathway with monoclonal antibodies, such as pembrolizumab and nivolumab, has shown significant success across a wide range of cancers. In non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), these inhibitors have become standard first-line treatment options, demonstrating improved survival rates compared to traditional chemotherapy. Similarly, pembrolizumab has shown efficacy in advanced melanoma, leading to durable responses in a subset of patients. Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors have also demonstrated promise in treating head and neck cancers, urothelial carcinomas, and even some gynecological cancers, including ovarian and CC. However, their effectiveness can vary depending on tumor type and PD-L1 expression levels.

Anti-CTLA-4 inhibitors

CTLA-4 is another immune checkpoint receptor acting as a negative T-cell activation regulator. It competes with the costimulatory receptor CD28 for binding to B7 molecules on antigen-presenting cells, thereby inhibiting T cell activation. Ipilimumab, an anti-CTLA-4 antibody, was the first checkpoint inhibitor approved by the FDA, initially for metastatic melanoma. It has shown significant clinical benefits when used alone or in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy, achieving long-term survival in a subset of melanoma patients. The combination of ipilimumab and nivolumab has been found to increase overall survival in metastatic melanoma and renal cell carcinoma (RCC), even in patients with previously untreated disease. Furthermore, combination therapies involving anti-CTLA-4 antibodies are being actively studied in various cancers, including NSCLC, OC, and CC.

Clinical applications in cancers

The application of ICIs in gynecological cancers, including ovarian, cervical, and ECs, is an area of active research. For example, anti-PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors demonstrated clinical activity in recurrent CC, particularly in PD-L1-positive tumors. These agents have led to significant tumor shrinkage and improved progression-free survival (PFS) in a subset of patients, particularly those with tumors expressing high levels of PD-L1. In OC, particularly in those with microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) or MMRd, pembrolizumab has demonstrated durable responses, and it has received FDA approval for treating certain types of OC. Ongoing trials are investigating the potential benefits of combining ICIs with other therapies, such as chemotherapy, anti-angiogenic agents, and targeted therapies, to overcome resistance mechanisms and enhance the overall therapeutic response. The landmark KEYNOTE-016 trial (NCT01876511) established that MMRd status predicts anti-PD-1 efficacy across solid tumors. Patients with MMRd metastatic colorectal or other solid malignancies exhibited significantly higher objective response rates (40% vs. 0%, p < 0.001) and progression-free survival (78% vs. 11% at 20 weeks) with pembrolizumab versus mismatch repair-proficient (pMMR) tumors. This differential response stems from elevated tumor mutational burden in MMRd tumors (mean 1,782 vs. 73 somatic mutations), generating immunogenic neoantigens.

Combination therapies

The combination of ICIs with other modalities, such as targeted therapies, chemotherapy, and oncolytic viruses, is becoming a promising strategy to improve clinical outcomes. For instance, combining anti-PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors with anti-angiogenesis agents like bevacizumab in OC has shown potential to enhance T cell trafficking to tumors by improving blood vessel normalization and thereby enhancing immune response. Similarly, combining ICIs with PARP inhibitors in BRCA-mutant cancers, including OC, is being explored to enhance the anti-tumor immune response through the induction of DNA damag. These combination therapies may overcome the immunosuppressive TME and provide more durable responses for patients who do not initially respond to immune checkpoint blockade alone.

Adoptive cell therapy in cancer treatment

ACT has undergone significant evolution, transitioning from early approaches like TILs to advanced, genetically engineered platforms capable of enhancing immune responses against cancer. The fundamental mechanism of ACT involves isolating immune cells, such as T cells, from a patient’s body, expanding or genetically modifying them to enhance their antitumor activity, and then reintroducing these modified cells into the patient’s bloodstream. One of the most advanced forms of ACT is Chimeric Antigen Receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, which combines the specificity of an antibody to recognize tumor antigens with the effector functions of T cells. Unlike traditional T cell responses that rely on major histocompatibility complex (MHC) presentation, CAR-T cells can recognize and target tumor-associated antigens directly, bypassing the need for antigen presentation by MHC molecules. This approach has shown groundbreaking success in hematologic malignancies, demonstrating durable remissions and improved patient outcomes.

However, translating engineered T cell therapies (CAR-T/TCR-T) to solid tumors presents significant challenges, due to heterogeneous antigen expression and the complex tumor microenvironment, including stromal barriers that limit T cell infiltration. In OC, for example, the use of locoregional delivery of engineered T cells has shown promise by improving tumor penetration compared to systemic administration, however, the persistence of these cells remains a significant hurdle. The transient nature of T cell persistence in solid tumors limits the long-term efficacy of this approach, necessitating further research to develop strategies that prolong T cell survival and function within the tumor microenvironment.

Alongside CAR-T cell therapy, advancements in TCR engineering have expanded the scope of ACT. TCR-based therapies are particularly effective at targeting intracellular tumor antigens presented by MHC molecules on the surface of cancer cells. This makes TCR-engineered T cells especially relevant for cancers associated with viral infections, such as HPV-positive CCs, where viral oncoproteins provide conserved tumor targets. The ability to target these intracellular antigens offers a promising route for developing therapies against solid tumors that were previously considered difficult to treat with conventional T cell-based approaches. As these innovative engineering techniques continue to evolve, ACT holds the potential to revolutionize the treatment of both hematologic and solid tumors.

Advances in applying T cell-based therapies to gynecological malignancies

Tumor-antigen processing and presentation

The development of T cell-based immunotherapies for gynecological malignancies has increasingly focused on overcoming barriers in tumor antigen processing and presentation, a critical determinant of adaptive immune recognition. Recent studies have highlighted innovative strategies to enhance antigen visibility and modulate immunosuppressive pathways, particularly in OC, where defects in antigen presentation machinery correlated with poor clinical outcomes. One promising strategy involves combining survivin-targeted vaccines with immunomodulatory agents. Survivin overexpression varies significantly (40–85%) across OC subtypes, creating challenges for universal targeting strategies. Phase I/II trial data show DPX-Survivac + cyclophosphamide induced strong survivin-specific CD8+ T cell responses in OC patientswhen paired with low-dose cyclophosphamide. This combination selectively depletes regulatory T cells (Tregs) and promotes the differentiation of multifunctional CD8+ central memory T cells, leading to sustained tumor-specific responses in patients with platinum-resistant disease. The clinical success of this approach underscores the potential of synergizing antigen-specific vaccination with microenvironment-modulating agents to break immune tolerance.

Co-inhibitory molecules, such as B7-H4, have emerged as key players in subverting antigen presentation, particularly in ovarian carcinoma. B7-H4 overexpression creates an immunosuppressive niche by recruiting myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) through CXCL17 chemokine signaling. This leads to impaired dendritic cell maturation and reduced MHC class II-mediated antigen presentation. B7-H4 blockade efficacy is context-dependent, with variable responses observed in different molecular subtypes of OC, restored antigen-presenting cell function, and reduced protumorigenic inflammation. Furthermore, advanced computational approaches are refining patient stratification based on antigen presentation capacity. The CONSTRU algorithm, for example, quantifies immune cytolytic activity through transcriptomic deconvolution, categorizing patients with high-grade serous OC (HGSOC) into distinct immune phenotypes. Those classified as immune-inflamed exhibit robust antigen processing machinery activity, characterized by elevated TAP1 expression and CD103+ tissue-resident memory T cell infiltration. This subgroup shows superior responses to combination checkpoint inhibitors, with single-cell analyses revealing the presence of cross-presenting dendritic cells essential for initiating antitumor immunity. Such patient stratification facilitates personalized immunotherapy approaches, tailoring treatments based on a tumor’s antigen presentation competence.

Emerging epigenetic strategies are broadening the therapeutic landscape for T cell-based therapies. Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors, when combined with NY-ESO-1-targeted T cell receptor therapies, have been shown to reverse MHC class I silencing in OC models, significantly improving objective response rates. Furthermore, oncolytic viruses are being used to induce immunogenic cell death, releasing neoantigens into the TME while stimulating pattern recognition receptors on antigen-presenting cells. These multimodal approaches address the dynamic interplay between antigen availability and presentation efficiency. However, challenges persist in maintaining stable MHC expression under prolonged T cell-mediated immunologic pressure. The convergence of these advances signifies a paradigm shift toward precision modulation of antigen processing pathways. By integrating vaccine platforms, checkpoint modulation, and epigenetic reprogramming, next-generation therapies aim to convert immunologically “cold” gynecological tumors into T cell-inflamed microenvironments. Ongoing clinical trials continue to refine these strategies, aiming to balance enhanced antitumor efficacy with manageable toxicity profiles to address the unique challenges posed by ovarian, cervical, and endometrial malignancies.

Four signals for T cell activation and differentiation

T cell activation and differentiation in cancers are driven by the integration of four critical signals: TCR engagement (Signal 1), co-stimulatory molecule interactions (Signal 2), cytokine signaling (Signal 3), and metabolic reprogramming (Signal 4). These signals collectively determine the magnitude, specificity, and durability of antitumor immune responses, with OC providing a model for understanding how these pathways are disrupted in immune evasion and therapeutic resistance. The immunosuppressive TME in OC impairs co-stimulatory signaling, particularly through overexpression of B7 family checkpoints. B7-H3, a major checkpoint in this family, is highly expressed in ovarian carcinoma and inhibits CD8+ T cell effector functions by disrupting Signal 2. Preclinical studies have shown that blocking B7-H3 enhances T cell cytotoxicity and prolongs survival more effectively than PD-1 inhibition, highlighting its critical role in immune escape. These findings underscore the therapeutic potential of restoring co-stimulatory signals to counteract tumor-driven T cell exhaustion.

Beyond checkpoint regulation, post-translational modifications of tumor antigens also influence T cell activation. For instance, tissue transglutaminase (TG2), an enzyme overexpressed in OC, modulates intracellular signaling pathways that impair T cell function. TG2 deletion leads to the upregulation of STAT1 phosphorylation and the suppression of STAT3 activation, shifting the cytokine profile toward a Th1-polarized phenotype (increased IFN-γ and decreased IL-10), which enhances CD8+ T cell infiltration. This interplay between tumor-intrinsic enzymatic activity and T cell differentiation highlights the role of metabolic and cytokine signals (Signals 3 and 4) in suppressing immunity. To enhance TCR signaling (Signal 1), novel approaches like biomimetic nanovaccines have been developed. These “mini DCs” are engineered by coating nanoparticles with dendritic cell membranes loaded with tumor antigens and IL-2, providing both Signal 1 and Signal 2, thereby enhancing antigen presentation and sustaining T cell activation. These nanovaccines outperform conventional dendritic cell vaccines in preclinical model, addressing challenges such as antigen loss and immunosuppressive ligand expression in ovarian tumors.

Further innovations include strategies to synergistically engineer co-stimulation and cytokine signaling to improve T cell persistence. The Fas-4-1BB immune-modulatory fusion protein (IFP), which combines the apoptosis-inducing domain of Fas with the co-stimulatory 4-1BB receptor, enhances T cell proliferation and survival within the tumor microenvironment, amplifying both Signal 2 and Signal 3 crosstalk. Similarly, engineered γδ T cells that secrete anti-PD-1 antibodies (Lv-PD1-γδ T cells) show improved metabolic fitness and cytotoxicity, overcoming checkpoint-mediated exhaustion. These advances demonstrate the importance of integrating metabolic reprogramming (Signal 4) with checkpoint modulation to sustain T cell functionality. In summary, the effectiveness of T cell-based immunotherapy in gynecological malignancies depends on the coordinated regulation of all four activation signals. Current research is focused on overcoming tumor-derived suppression of these pathways through combinatorial strategies that enhance TCR engagement, optimize co-stimulation, modulate cytokine networks, and reprogram T cell metabolism. Future efforts must address the spatial and temporal heterogeneity in signal activation to achieve durable clinical responses, particularly in OC, where immunosuppressive niches remain a significant barrier.

T cell trafficking and tumor infiltration

The success of T cell-based immunotherapy in gynecological malignancies is heavily influenced by the ability of T cells to efficiently navigate and infiltrate tumors, a process that is intricately regulated by the TME. In ovarian and CCs, immune evasion mechanisms present significant barriers to effective T cell infiltration. Analysis of 26 paired samples showed chemotherapy increased TIL density, including CD3+, CD8+, and PD-1+ T cells, but fails to address persistent immunosuppressive elements such as Tregs and PD-1+ cells, resulting in limited clinical benefit (Fig. 2A). This highlights the need for combining chemotherapy with agents that target immune checkpoints. In preclinical tumor-bearing mouse models, anti-VISTA antibody blockade prolonged survival in tumor-bearing mice by reversing T cell suppression. Phase I trials demonstrated efficacy, but require comprehensive safety assessments similar to VISTA blockade studies where antibody treatment prolonged survival without reported significant toxicities (Fig. 2A). Additionally, CD47 upregulation in OC is linked to Treg infiltration and T cell exhaustion, connecting this “don’t eat me” signal to myeloid cell regulation and immune suppression (Fig. 2B).

Engineered T cell therapies are emerging as effective solutions to overcome these barriers. Mesothelin (MSLN)-targeted T cells have shown transient tumor control in OC models, though repeated administration is necessary to prevent functional exhaustion. Combinatorial approaches using IL-2/IL-15 preconditioning have enhanced the peritoneal accumulation and cytotoxicity of FRα-bispecific T cells, enabling sustained tumor regression (Fig. 2C). These strategies emphasize the importance of both antigen targeting and cytokine signaling in maintaining T cell persistence. Additionally, metastatic niche regulation complicates T cell trafficking, with reduced levels of leukocyte cell-derived chemotactic factor 2 (LECT2) correlating with advanced OC progression. Overexpression of LECT2 inhibits ascites formation and restores PD-1 blockade sensitivity by alleviating myeloid-derived suppression (Fig. 2D). Viral and multispecific therapies further offer promising solutions. For example, the oncolytic adenovirus TILT-322, armed with MUC1/CD3 bispecific engagers, reverses T cell exhaustion in ascites while inducing tumor lysis. Similarly, the trispecific antibody SAIL66 (CLDN6/CD3/CD137) enhances T cell infiltration and reduces exhaustion markers compared to conventional formats, demonstrating superior antitumor activity (Fig. 2E).

Metabolic and epigenetic crosstalk within the TME also plays a critical role in modulating immune responses. MSLN, which promotes immune evasion in HGSOC via CD24-mediated macrophage polarization and Wnt/β-catenin signaling, can be targeted to enhance cytotoxic T cell function when knocked down, synergizing with PD-1 blockade (Fig. 2F). Additionally, fibrinogen-like protein 2 (FGL2) produced by stromal cells suppresses dendritic cell activation, and its inhibition has been shown to improve responses to oncolytic virotherapy by relieving NK and T cell suppression. Notably, BRCA1/2 mutations, which result in a high tumor mutational burden, paradoxically induce T cell exhaustion, highlighting the limitations of checkpoint inhibitors in this molecular subset (Fig. 2G). This underscores the need for mutation-specific immunomodulatory strategies. In conclusion, effective T cell trafficking in gynecological malignancies requires a multi-faceted approach to overcome checkpoint dominance, stromal barriers, and metabolic suppression. Emerging therapies that combine engineered cell therapies, viral vectors, and microenvironment-modulating agents show promise in overcoming spatial and functional immune exclusion. Future research should focus on spatial omics approaches to better understand tumor-immune geography and optimize combinatorial regimens for distinct molecular subtypes.

Killing mechanisms of cytotoxic T cells

Cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) play a central role in antitumor immunity, but their efficacy in gynecological cancers is often undermined by immune-evasive mechanisms within the TME (Table 2). Recent advancements in T cell engineering and microenvironment modulation have provided valuable insights into overcoming these challenges, with several strategies showing promise for translation into clinical practice. One precision approach involves engineering T cells with high-affinity TCRs targeting viral oncoproteins. For example, a Preclinical mouse models demonstrate that HPV-16 E7-specific TCR-T cells mediate regression of established cervical tumors, supporting their therapeutic potential pending human validation,. This strategy takes advantage of the conserved nature of viral antigens to bypass tumor heterogeneity while maintaining specificity for malignant cells.

T cell dysfunction in OC has been linked to endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress pathways, particularly through C/EBP homologous protein (Chop). Elevated Chop expression in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells correlates with poor prognosis by suppressing T-bet-dependent effector functions via PERK-ATF4 signaling. Inhibition of Chop, either genetically or pharmacologically, restores interferon-γ production and cytotoxic granule exocytosis, suggesting this pathway as a therapeutic target to reverse T cell exhaustion. Additionally, oncolytic viruses offer a multifaceted approach to enhance CTL activity. The adenoviral construct Ad5/3-E2F-D24-hTNFa-IRES-hIL2 remodels the OC TME by inducing pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as IFN-γ and IL-2, which activate both CD4+ and CD8+ TILs. This effect occurs independently of PD-L1 status, suggesting potential synergy with checkpoint inhibitors. These viruses create immunogenic niches that enhance both endogenous and adoptively transferred T cell responses.

In CC, the TIGIT/CD155 axis has emerged as a key immune checkpoint, where TIGIT upregulation on CD8+ T cells leads to exhaustion, and tumor cell CD155 expression inversely correlates with TIL infiltration. Dual blockade of TIGIT and PD-1 reinvigorates CTL cytotoxicity and synergizes with radiotherapy, achieving durable tumor control in preclinical models. This combinatorial approach addresses both intrinsic and extrinsic immune suppression of T cells. Moreover, CAR-T cell innovation continues to advance OC therapy. A novel CA125-targeting CAR enhances T cell activation and persistence, improving tumor penetration and overcoming antigen escape variants, ultimately doubling median survival in murine models. These advancements underscore the importance of optimizing receptor affinity and signaling architectures for solid tumor applications. Together, these strategies highlight the complex interplay between T cell signaling and the TME in shaping antitumor immunity, with future research focusing on integrating metabolic reprogramming, developing logic-gated systems to reduce on-target toxicity, and leveraging multi-omics profiling to match therapeutic strategies with tumor immune phenotypes. As our understanding of T cell killing mechanisms expands, rationally designed combinations promise to significantly improve outcomes in gynecological oncology.

Building upon conventional and engineered T cell approaches, chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy represents a highly specific form of adoptive T cell transfer. CARs are synthetic receptors comprising an extracellular antigen-binding domain (typically an scFv), a transmembrane anchor, and intracellular signaling domains (e.g., CD3ζ plus co-stimulatory domains like CD28 or 4-1BB) that activate T cells upon tumor antigen recognition (Fig. 4). Key targets and clinical progress in gynecologic cancers

Mesothelin (MSLN): Early-phase trials (NCT04562298, NCT02159716, NCT05372692) report partial responses and progression-free survival (PFS) of 4.6–5.8 months in OC, though cytokine release syndrome (CRS) remains a concern. Preclinical activity noted in CC.

MUC16 (CA125): Trials (e.g., NCT05225363) focus on platinum-resistant OC. Constructs incorporating immunomodulators like IL-12 show enhanced persistence and antitumor activity by reshaping the TME, but face challenges of antigen heterogeneity and off-target effects. Folate Receptor Alpha (FRα): Evaluated in trials (e.g., NCT03585764), demonstrating specific cytotoxicity against OC cells with a favorable early safety profile. Dual-targeting (e.g., FRα + MSLN) strategies are being explored. Tumor-Associated Glycoprotein 72 (TAG72): Preclinical and early clinical data (e.g., NCT05225363) show tumor regression potential, but antigen escape is a challenge. Combinatorial approaches (e.g., TAG72 + CD47 targeting) are under investigation.

Mechanical forces in T cells

The role of mechanical forces in regulating T cell activity within gynecological malignancies is a critical area of research for cancer immunotherapy. These forces, mediated through cellular interactions and the physical properties of the TME, significantly influence T cell activation, trafficking, and effector functions. In OC, polymorphonuclear neutrophils (PMNs) exemplify how biomechanical cues can drive immunosuppression. When exposed to ascitic fluid, PMNs adopt an immune-suppressive phenotype through complement C3 activation and CR3-dependent signaling, establishing direct physical interactions with T cells. This contact impairs T cell proliferation and cytokine production via vesicle trafficking-mediated delivery of inhibitory factors, suggesting the therapeutic potential of targeting mechanical interfaces between immune cells to restore antitumor immunity. Additionally, the physical architecture of the TME imposes significant biomechanical constraints on T cell functionality. Elevated extracellular matrix (ECM) stiffness, a hallmark of solid tumors like ovarian carcinoma, creates dense fibrous barriers that restrict T cell migration and hinder effective cytotoxic interactions. This rigidity alters integrin-mediated traction forces, impeding immune synapse formation and reducing TCR signaling efficacy. These biophysical barriers contribute to the creation of “immune-excluded” tumor regions where T cells are mechanically sequestered despite chemokine gradients.

Transcriptional control of CD8+ T cell fate decision

The functional plasticity of CD8+ T cells within the TME is controlled by complex transcriptional networks that regulate their differentiation, persistence, and cytotoxic activity. In gynecological malignancies, these regulatory mechanisms are often disrupted by immunosuppressive signals, which calls for strategies to reprogram transcriptional programs for therapeutic benefit (Table 3). Epigenetic modulation, particularly through HDAC inhibitors, has shown promise in counteracting TME-driven immune suppression. For example, in OC models, the Class I HDAC inhibitor entinostat enhances CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity by increasing perforin and granzyme B expression while suppressing chemokines like CCL2 that recruit MDSCs (Fig. 5A). This dual action shifts the CD8+/Treg ratio in favor of antitumor immunity, improving responses to immune checkpoint blockade. Such findings underscore the potential of epigenetic therapies to reprogram the TME and restore T cell functionality. Moreover, chromatin remodeling further influences CD8+ T cell fate decisions. Genetic ablation of the heterochromatin protein Cbx3/HP1γ enhances T cell persistence by increasing LEF-1 and IL-21R expression, critical factors for maintaining stem-like properties and cytokine responsiveness. In immunologically “cold” tumors resistant to PD-1 inhibition, Cbx3/HP1γ-deficient CD8+ T cells exhibit significantly improved tumor control, making this protein a potential target for overcoming T cell exhaustion (Fig. 5B).

Immune checkpoint pathways also intersect with transcriptional regulation, presenting additional therapeutic opportunities. For instance, single-cell RNA sequencing of HGSOC identified a TNFRSF1Bhi exhausted CD8+ T cell subset associated with poor prognosis. TNFRSF1B signaling suppresses IFN-γ production via STAT1 inhibition, but antibody blockade of this pathway can restore tumor control in preclinical models (Fig. 5C). Similarly, in CC, sialylation-dependent Siglec-10 activation on dendritic cells induces galectin-9-mediated T cell silencing. Disrupting the Siglec-10/ligand axis reverses this immunosuppression and synergizes with PD-1 blockade to enhance cytotoxic activity (Fig. 5D). These insights highlight the complexity of transcriptional control in regulating CD8+ T cell function. Emerging strategies combining epigenetic modifiers, chromatin-targeted agents, and checkpoint modulators aim to rebalance these transcriptional networks to promote sustained antitumor responses. For example, ongoing trials evaluating entinostat with anti-PD-L1 therapy have shown preliminary efficacy in platinum-resistant OC, with correlative studies demonstrating expanded clonal T cell repertoires (NCT02915523). The integration of spatial transcriptomics and CRISPR-based screening platforms is poised to further unravel the context-dependent regulatory circuits that govern T cell function. Recent work has mapped chromatin accessibility in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells and identified BATF-IRF4 complexes as key regulators of exhaustion programs. Inhibiting these factors enhances CAR-T cell efficacy against OC xenografts, illustrating the translational potential of these mechanistic insights.

Metabolic and epigenetic regulation of T cell memory and exhaustion

The functional dynamics of T cells in gynecological malignancies are tightly regulated by metabolic and epigenetic interactions within the TME, which govern the balance between T cell memory formation and exhaustion. These processes directly influence the efficacy of immunotherapeutic interventions. One key therapeutic target is the metabolic reprogramming of immunosuppressive myeloid populations. In OC, AMP-activated protein kinase α (AMPKα) signaling sustains the immunosuppressive function of MDSCs by enhancing fatty acid oxidation. Pharmacological inhibition of AMPKα redirects MDSC differentiation toward antigen-presenting phenotypes, restoring CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity and synergizing with PD-1 blockade to improve tumor control. This metabolic checkpoint underscores the potential of targeting myeloid cell metabolism to remodel immunosuppressive niches. Additionally, lipid metabolism perturbations contribute to T cell dysfunction, with OC cells disrupting the TAGLN2-FABP5 axis, impairing fatty acid transport into CD8+ T cells, and reducing mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation. Restoring TAGLN2 expression rescues lipid metabolism, enhancing IFN-γ production and cytotoxic function, demonstrating the value of lipid metabolic engineering to counteract tumor-induced T cell exhaustion.

The sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) pathway is another example of immunosuppressive metabolic signaling in the TME. In OC, sphingosine kinase 1 (SPHK1) upregulation elevates S1P levels, which induce PD-L1 expression on tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) and suppress T cell IL-2 production. Dual inhibition of SPHK1 and PD-1 is more effective in reducing metastatic burden than monotherapy, highlighting the therapeutic potential of combined metabolic and checkpoint modulation. Electrolyte imbalances in malignant ascites also create barriers to T cell function. Elevated sodium and reduced potassium concentrations suppress T cell activation by impairing Kv1.3 channel expression and calcium signaling. Correcting these ionic disturbances restores CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity, suggesting that electrolyte modulation could enhance intraperitoneal immunotherapy. In CC, BTN3A1-mediated γδ T cell exhaustion establishes an immunosuppressive niche. BTN3A1hi tumors induce PD-1/TIM-3 co-expression on Vδ2 T cells through NF-κB-dependent transcriptional programs, and antibody-mediated BTN3A1 blockade reduces PD-L1 expression, synergizing with γδ T cell therapies to improve tumor control.

Epigenetic regulation also plays a pivotal role in T cell dysfunction. Exhausted CD4+ T cells in gynecological cancers retain accessible chromatin regions at Th1-associated loci despite functional impairment. PD-1 inhibition can reinvigorate these cells by enhancing AP-1/FOSL2-driven transcriptional activity, which licenses dendritic cells to prime tumor-specific CD8+ T cell responses. Collectively, these studies highlight the interconnected metabolic and epigenetic networks governing T cell fate in gynecological malignancies. Strategies targeting AMPKα signaling, lipid metabolism, sphingolipid pathways, and ionic balance offer promising multifaceted approaches to counteract T cell exhaustion. Future research must integrate spatial multi-omics and single-cell technologies to map the metabolic-epigenetic interface across tumor subtypes, enabling precision modulation of T cell function for improved therapeutic outcomes.

Challenges and future directions of CAR-T cell therapy for gynecological malignancies

Overcoming the hostile TME

The immunosuppressive TME of gynecological cancers (hypoxia, acidity, inhibitory cytokines/checkpoints, suppressive cells) severely limits CAR-T cell infiltration, persistence, and function, contributing to exhaustion. Novel CAR designs incorporating TME-modulating elements (e.g., cytokine secretion, dominant-negative receptors for TGF-β) and combination strategies (e.g., with checkpoint inhibitors, oncolytic viruses) are crucial.

Addressing antigen heterogeneity and escape

Solid tumors exhibit significant intratumoral antigen heterogeneity, allowing antigen-negative clones to evade CAR-T targeting. Strategies include dual/multi-target CARs (e.g., MUC16/PD-L1, MSLN/FRα), logic-gated CARs, and targeting neoantigens or less heterogeneous antigens.

Mitigating on-target, off-tumor toxicity

Despite tumor overexpression, antigens like MSLN and MUC16 have low-level expression on normal tissues (e.g., mesothelium), posing a risk. Approaches involve optimizing CAR affinity/avidity, incorporating safety switches (e.g., inducible caspase-9, EGFRt), and utilizing localized/regional (e.g., intraperitoneal) delivery.

Managing cytokine release syndrome (crs) and other toxicities

Robust T cell activation can trigger CRS and neurotoxicity. Strategies include dose optimization/step-dosing, tocilizumab prophylaxis/treatment, incorporation of safety switches, and development of regulated CAR expression systems.

Future directions: leveraging lessons from hematologic malignancies

Key future directions encompass: Advanced CAR engineering involving refinement of co-stimulatory domains (e.g., CD28 vs. 4-1BB), signal strength modulation, and persistence enhancement; alongside exploitation of armored CAR constructs (e.g., cytokine-secreting IL-12/IL-15 variants, dominant-negative receptors like dnTGFβR), switchable CAR platforms, and universal/allogeneic cellular products. Rational combinatorial strategies integrate CAR-T cells with immune checkpoint inhibitors, molecularly targeted agents (e.g., PARP inhibitors, anti-angiogenics), conventional chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and TME-modulating compounds to overcome resistance mechanisms. Precision targeting and patient stratification leverage predictive biomarkers (e.g., target antigen density, T cell fitness metrics, TME profiling) to identify optimal candidates while exploring novel targets and neoantigen-directed approaches. Concurrently, optimization of delivery systems (e.g., locoregional administration such as intraperitoneal infusion) and streamlining manufacturing processes are critical to enhance therapeutic accessibility and scalability. Future directions and potential strategies for optimizing CAR-T therapy in gynecological malignancies

The clinical translation of CAR-T cell therapy in gynecological cancers requires innovative strategies to address persistent challenges in antigen specificity, TME resistance, and therapeutic safety. Building on current progress, future research must integrate multidimensional approaches to enhance efficacy while mitigating risks, ultimately advancing toward personalized treatment paradigms.

Antigen discovery and targeting innovations

The identification of tumor-specific antigens remains foundational for the development of effective CAR-T therapies. Key antigens such as MSLN, MUC16, FRα, and TAG72 have shown promise in ovarian and CCs due to their differential expression on tumor cells compared to normal tissues. However, antigen heterogeneity within tumors is a significant challenge, leading to relapse in over 60% of cases, as tumors evolve and escape immune recognition. To address this, emerging techniques like single-cell RNA sequencing and spatial proteomics are advancing the discovery of cancer-testis antigens and neoantigens with minimal normal tissue expression. For example, PRAME and NY-ESO-1 are being investigated as potential targets for OC due to their restricted expression in tumors. Additionally, optimal antigen pair selection remains debated, with MSLN/FRα vs. MUC16/PD-L1 combinations showing divergent efficacy across trials. Emerging multimodal platforms integrating spatial transcriptomics, multiplexed immunohistochemistry, and liquid biopsies provide comprehensive resistance mechanism mapping. These technologies enable biomarker discovery for patient stratification and therapy personalization, addressing key barriers in CAR-T translation. This approach highlights the potential of multi-target CAR-T strategies to enhance efficacy and reduce relapse in diverse tumor environments.

Enhancing T cell fitness for engineered therapies

Another key challenge in CAR-T therapy is overcoming T cell exhaustion, a common issue in the immunosuppressive TME. One promising strategy to address this is the incorporation of 4-1BB co-stimulatory domains into CAR-T constructs, which has been shown to enhance T cell persistence by up to threefold compared to CD28-based constructs in OC models. Furthermore, “armored” CAR-T cells that secrete interleukin-15 (IL-15) have demonstrated prolonged activity in hypoxic niches, maintaining their cytotoxic function for more than 28 days post-infusion. These engineered T cells show improved persistence and better anti-tumor activity in hostile tumor environments. Additionally, the CRISPR-based knockout of exhaustion markers, such as TOX and NR4A, has been shown to further enhance CAR-T durability and tumor control in preclinical CC models. These advancements in T cell fitness and persistence are crucial for improving the long-term efficacy of CAR-T therapies, especially in solid tumors with a harsh TME.

TME reprogramming strategies

The immunosuppressive TME in gynecologic malignancies, characterized by factors such as hypoxia, acidic pH, and high levels of TGF-β, presents a significant barrier to effective CAR-T therapy. To overcome these obstacles, researchers are exploring strategies to reprogram the TME to improve CAR-T efficacy. For instance, stroma-modifying therapies using PEGylated hyaluronidase (PEGPH20) have been shown to reduce matrix stiffness by 40%, which in turn improves T cell infiltration depth, allowing CAR-T cells to penetrate tumors more effectively. Furthermore, nanoparticle-delivered TGF-β siRNA has demonstrated the ability to reverse fibroblast-mediated suppression, increasing tumor cell killing by 2.5-fold in EC models. Another interesting strategy involves the use of oncolytic adenoviruses expressing FLT3L, which synergize with CAR-T therapy. In OC ascites, this approach has been shown to convert 30% of TAMs to an immunostimulatory phenotype, enhancing the anti-tumor immune response. These TME reprogramming strategies hold promise for boosting CAR-T cell efficacy and addressing the limitations imposed by the hostile tumor microenvironment.

Precision engineering for cellular safety

While enhancing CAR-T efficacy is a priority, ensuring safety by mitigating off-target toxicity is equally important. For example, MSLN expression, though predominantly tumor-associated, is also lowly expressed in mesothelial tissues, which could lead to off-target effects. To address this, logic-gated CAR-T cells have been developed, which require dual antigen engagement, such as MSLN and FRα, to activate T cell cytotoxicity. This dual-targeting approach helps eliminate tumor cells while sparing normal tissues in co-culture assays, reducing the risk of unwanted side effects. In addition, inducible caspase-9 safety switches, which allow for the rapid elimination of CAR-T cells in case of adverse effects, have shown promising results in early-phase trials. These safety measures have successfully reduced the severity of CRS from Grade ≥ 3 to ≤ 2 in patients. Moreover, the development of tunable synNotch receptors, which activate cytotoxic programs only upon tumor contact, further enhances the specificity of CAR-T therapies, ensuring that T cells are activated only in the presence of tumor cells.

Synergistic combination therapies

Combining CAR-T therapy with other treatment modalities has emerged as a promising strategy to overcome some of the limitations of CAR-T alone. For example, the integration of CAR-T therapy with PD-1/CTLA-4 checkpoint blockade has shown the potential to reverse TME-driven T cell exhaustion, leading to a significant increase in objective response rates. In platinum-resistant OC, this combination therapy has been shown to double the response rates compared to CAR-T monotherapy. Additionally, the use of PARP inhibitors has been found to upregulate MSLN expression by fourfold in BRCA-mutant tumors, thereby sensitizing these tumors to CAR-T attack. Another innovative approach involves oncolytic herpes viruses engineered to express CD40L, which enhance dendritic cell cross-priming and increase the tumor-infiltrating CAR-T population by 60%. These synergistic combination therapies improve the effectiveness of CAR-T treatments and enhance the overall anti-tumor immune response, offering new therapeutic avenues for gynecological cancers and beyond.

Safety-informed engineering approaches for cellular therapies

The advancement of engineered cellular therapies requires strategic integration of safety principles derived from foundational studies. Key engineering approaches include localized delivery systems that minimize systemic exposure, inspired by vaccine studies where injection site reactions were the primary adverse event. Precision targeting methodologies build upon viral antigen-specific TCR designs that achieve tumor-selective effects while avoiding healthy tissue damage, as demonstrated in HPV E7-targeted therapies. These approaches align with checkpoint blockade strategies where antibody-based inhibition maintained favorable safety profiles during tumor clearance. Controlled persistence mechanisms further enhance safety, informed by chemotherapy-induced immune modulation that maintained therapeutic effects without significant complications. This integrated framework enables phase-appropriate risk management while preserving the efficacy observed across immunotherapeutic modalities.

Clinical translation status of key targets

The therapeutic strategies discussed exhibit varying levels of clinical validation: The survivin-targeted vaccine DPX-Survivac has progressed through Phase I/II evaluation in OC patients, confirming its ability to induce antigen-specific CD8+ T cell responses with manageable toxicity primarily limited to injection site reactions. Direct human evidence comes from analysis of 26 paired tumor specimens demonstrating platinum-taxane chemotherapy significantly increased CD8+ TIL density in high-grade serous OC patients. In contrast, HPV E7-specific TCR therapy currently remains at the preclinical stage with efficacy demonstrated in mouse models of CC, while VISTA blockade has shown preclinical proof-of-concept in tumor-bearing mouse models but awaits formal clinical assessment in gynecologic malignancies. This spectrum highlights the need to accelerate translation of promising preclinical findings into clinically validated therapies through rigorously designed human trials.

Discussion and conclusion

The development of T cell-based immunotherapies for gynecological malignancies has ushered in a new era of precision oncology. Various strategies, including ICIs, ACT (such as CAR-T, TCR-T, and TIL therapies), and TME modulation, have shown considerable promise in overcoming the limitations of conventional treatments. Immune checkpoint blockade, particularly targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 and TIGIT/CD155 axes, has demonstrated variable efficacy in cervical and OCs. In CC, dual checkpoint inhibition enhances CD8+ T cell infiltration and reduces myeloid suppression, while in OC, combinations with angiogenesis inhibitors improve T cell trafficking to hypoxic tumor regions. Despite these advances, tumor heterogeneity and immune evasion mechanisms present significant challenges, leading to both intrinsic and adaptive resistance.

Among the various ACTs, engineered T cell platforms (CAR-T/TCR-T) have made notable strides, particularly with targets like MSLN and MUC16, which have shown tumor regression in early clinical trials. However, challenges such as antigen escape, T cell exhaustion, and the immunosuppressive TME persist. Similarly, TCR-T therapies targeting HPV oncoproteins have demonstrated durable responses in CC, and TIL therapies have shown promise in OC, although issues like cell expansion, persistence, and tumor infiltration remain significant hurdles. To address these, efforts to modulate the TME, including targeting metabolic and epigenetic dysregulation, are showing preclinical success. HDAC inhibitors like entinostat, for example, enhance antigen presentation and reduce Treg activity, while targeting AMPKα-driven immunosuppression and SPHK1/S1P-mediated T cell exhaustion are also being explored to improve T cell function.

Despite these advances, several key challenges remain. The heterogeneity of antigens within tumors necessitates the development of multi-targeted CAR-T designs to prevent antigen escape. The immunosuppressive TME remains a formidable obstacle, requiring combinatorial strategies to maintain T cell activity. Moreover, safety concerns, particularly off-target toxicity, must be addressed. The development of logic-gated CARs and inducible safety switches is an emerging solution, but these technologies still require refinement. Additionally, scalable manufacturing and patient selection based on reliable biomarkers will be essential for the successful clinical application of these therapies. Biomarker-driven enrichment strategies that integrate digital pathology, AI, and multi-omics can mitigate trial heterogeneity. This approach accelerates therapeutic development by identifying resistance mechanisms early and optimizing patient selection for novel T cell therapies.

Unresolved controversies and limitations persist across the current therapeutic landscape, necessitating critical appraisal. Survivin demonstrates substantial expression heterogeneity (40–85%) across OC histological subtypes, complicating patient selection strategies compared to uniformly expressed viral antigens like HPV E7, which is nevertheless constrained by HLA-A*02:01 restriction in CC. Optimal sequencing of chemo-immunotherapy combinations remains contentious, as platinum-taxane neoadjuvant chemotherapy significantly elevates CD8+ TIL density (2.15-fold increase) without conferring consistent survival benefits in high-grade serous OC, revealing a critical dissociation between immunological and clinical endpoints. Target complexity is exemplified by VISTA, which exhibits paradoxical roles - suppressing T-cell proliferation in tumor microenvironments while potentially activating dendritic cell functions-creating dosing dilemmas where complete blockade may inadvertently exacerbate immunosuppression. Translational barriers are further compounded by manufacturing constraints: HPV-specific TCR therapies require HLA-matching (< 50% population coverage) and extended production timelines exceeding progression windows for advanced CC patients. These inters study inconsistencies underscore the imperative for biomarker-driven patient stratification, context-dependent dosing strategies, and standardized efficacy endpoints in future clinical trial designs to resolve these fundamental controversies.

Looking to the future, the integration of cutting-edge technologies, such as single-cell RNA sequencing, spatial omics, synthetic biology, and artificial intelligence, offers promising avenues for improving T cell immunotherapy. These advances—from KEYNOTE-016 establishing MMRd as a predictive biomarker to non-operative management achieving organ preservation—exemplify how immunotherapy is fundamentally redefining therapeutic paradigms in MMRd EC. These innovations allow for a deeper understanding of tumor heterogeneity and the immune landscape, uncovering new therapeutic nodes for targeted interventions. Moreover, modular CAR designs and CRISPR-based gene editing provide flexibility to adapt therapies to evolving tumor biology. By combining these advances, personalized and context-specific regimens can be developed, overcoming current limitations and significantly improving the treatment of gynecological cancers. In conclusion, T cell-based immunotherapies have the potential to transform the landscape of gynecological oncology, offering hope for improved survival and quality of life for patients with these aggressive and recurrent cancers.
