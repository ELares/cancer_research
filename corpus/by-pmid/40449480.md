# Combined therapy with DR5-targeting antibody-drug conjugate and CDK inhibitors as a strategy for advanced colorectal cancer.

## Abstract

Targeted therapies for advanced microsatellite stable (MSS) subtype colorectal cancer (MSS-CRC) remain a clinical challenge. Here, we show that death receptor 5 (DR5) is elevated in both MSS and microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) colorectal cancer (CRC) cohorts, highlighting its potential as a clinical target. Oba01, a clinical-stage DR5-targeting antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) delivering the microtubule-disrupting agent monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE), shows superior efficacy in CRC cell lines, patient-derived xenografts and their corresponding organoids, irrespective of MSS or MSI-H status. Importantly, our functional multi-omics analysis reveals that the cell cycle pathway and cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) are key synergistic targets of Oba01's tumor-killing activity. We further show that Oba01 synergizes with the Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved CDK inhibitor abemaciclib in clinically relevant in vivo models. This synergy is also observed with other CDK inhibitors, underscoring the potential of combining Oba01 with CDK inhibition as a therapeutic strategy for advanced CRC, particularly the refractory MSS subtype.

Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common causes of cancer-related death and the second in terms of mortality, accounting for 10% of all cancer cases and related death. Among 20% of newly diagnosed patients with metastatic CRC, less than 20% survive for more than 5 years, making it a major public health and socioeconomic concern.

The CRC patient population is characterized by significant fragmentation as a result of a diverse array of genomic alterations, which can be classified into two distinct subtypes based on mechanisms of genomic instability: microsatellite instability (MSI), accounted for 15% of CRC patients, and microsatellite stability (MSS), accounted for 85% of patients. The differences in genomic profiles between these two subsets lead to notable variations in prognosis and responses to treatment. For instance, patients with MSS typically exhibit lower overall survival and disease-free survival rates compared to those with MSI-high (MSI-H), especially in advanced stages of the disease. Early-stage CRC may be treated with surgery alone, while advanced cases often require a combination of chemotherapy and targeted therapies. Common chemotherapy regimens, such as FOLFOX and FOLFIRI, typically achieve a low response rate in the range of 30%–40% in advanced CRC. The addition of targeted drugs like cetuximab or bevacizumab can increase the overall response rate to 40%–60% in MSI-H patients but not in MSS patients. Immunotherapy, particularly with immune checkpoint inhibitors like pembrolizumab and nivolumab, emerges as a promising option for advanced CRC. However, its effectiveness is primarily limited to MSI-H tumors and not in tumors with MSS. So far, efforts to develop effective targeted therapies for MSS tumors have proven challenging and are greatly needed to enhance outcomes for patients with advanced CRC.

Death receptor 5 (DR5 or tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand [TRAIL]-R2) is a type I membrane receptor from the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily. It plays a crucial role in regulating apoptosis by triggering the extrinsic pathway of cell death in response to different signals, such as binding with the ligand called TRAIL. When DR5 is activated, it triggers the formation of a complex known as the death-inducing signaling complex, which then activates caspases leading to cell death. The specific increase in DR5 expression in almost all tumors makes it an important target for cancer treatment, as its activation can selectively induce cell death in cancer cells while leaving normal cells unharmed. Clinical trials suggest that recombinant human soluble TRAIL (rsTRAIL) or agonistic antibodies to DR5 are well tolerated and exhibit some antitumor efficacy.

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) combine the precision of monoclonal antibodies with chemotherapy drugs to deliver potent tumor-killing activity, sparing healthy cells and reducing side effects. Their success in treating various cancers has garnered significant attention in academic and pharmaceutical communities. Previously, we introduced Oba01, an ADC composed of zaptuzumab, a humanized anti-DR5 antibody (IgG1), coupled with a cleavable valine citrulline-dipeptide linker (PY-MAA-Val-Cit-PAB) and a highly potent microtubule-disrupting toxin monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) by using ThioBridge technology. The ADC was 97.05% monodispersed as determined by size-exclusion chromatography. The drug/antibody ratio determined by hydrophobic interaction chromatography was 4.0. Our preclinical studies showed promising results with Oba01 effectively inhibiting tumor growth in various DR5-positive cancers such as leukemia, pancreatic cancer, and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Additionally, Oba01 demonstrated excellent safety and tolerability in animals, leading to its advancement to phase 1 clinical trials for DR5-positive acute lymphoblastic leukemia and NSCLC. Notably, there is strong evidence indicating that CRC frequently exhibits increased levels of DR5 expression, suggesting that CRC could be a promising candidate for Oba01-based therapy.

In this study, we initially analyzed the expression levels of DR5 in an advanced CRC patient cohort. Subsequently, we demonstrated that Oba01 exhibits potent anti-tumor efficacy in various clinically relevant CRC models, including patient-derived xenografts (PDXs) and PDX tumor-derived organoids (PDXOs) derived from patients with advanced CRC belonging to both the MSS and MSI-H subsets. By utilizing RNA sequencing and proteomic analysis to explore the global changes in protein expression triggered by Oba01 treatment, we identified signaling pathways that may work in conjunction with Oba01. Notably, our findings revealed that Oba01 synergized effectively with cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK) inhibitors such as abemaciclib to suppress tumor growth, even at significantly lower doses than when administered separately. Together, these discoveries indicate that targeting DR5, microtubule assembly, and CDK simultaneously could be an effective treatment approach for both patients with MSS and MSI-H CRC who exhibit DR5 expression.

Results

Expression profiles of DR5 in advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC patient cohorts

Initially, we aimed to assess the expression of DR5 in patients with CRC. Examination of a group of 367 patients with CRC, including 275 cases of colon adenocarcinoma and 92 cases of rectal adenocarcinoma, through RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data from The Cancer Genome Atlas database revealed a notable increase in the expression of DR5 in CRC tissues compared to normal tissues (Figures 1A and 1B), consistent with previous reports. To confirm the findings at the protein level, we assessed the expression of DR5 through immunohistochemical (IHC) staining on tissue microarrays derived from a cohort of 509 patients. The status of MSS and MSI-H was determined by IHC staining of four established mismatch repair (MMR) proteins (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2). The distribution of MSI-H and MSS subsets in this cohort, with 7.1% (36/509) classified as MSI-H and 92.9% (473/509) as MSS, was consistent with previous reports, indicating the representativeness of the cohort used in our study (Figures 1C–1E). Specifically, 18 cases of MLH1/PMS2-positive, 6 cases of MSH2/MSH6-positive, 8 cases of MLH1-positive, and 4 cases of PMS2-positive were observed in 36 MSI-H patients (Figures 1D and 1E). Following that, we analyze the expression of DR5 in the group of patients by IHC assays. Specifically, DR5 expression was high in 13.95% (66/455), moderate in 38.47% (182/455), low in 43.76% (207/455), and negative in 3.82% (18/455) of MSS cases, while it was high in 11.11% (4/36), moderate in 50% (18/36), and low in 38.89% (14/36) of MSI-H cases (Figures 1F–1H). No correlations were found between the expression level of DR5 and cancer stages or tumor subtypes. Therefore, these findings suggest that DR5 is upregulated to varying degrees in tumor tissues of patients with MSS and MSI-H CRC, making it a potential target for ADC.

Oba01 monotherapy remarkably inhibited the proliferation of CRC cells

To investigate the antitumor effects of Oba01 on CRC cells, we conducted assays to evaluate its impact on cell viability. We identified P53R, RKO, HCT116, and Colo205 as DR5-positive CRC cell lines through western blotting and immunofluorescence (Figures 2A and 2B). Both DR5 isoforms, as previously reported, are detected to varying degrees in the tested cell lines. While all four cell lines exhibited high sensitivity to MMAE, treatment with Oba01 resulted in dose-dependent cell death in these cells, with varying IC50 values (21.51, 3.396, 1.92, and 18.89 nM for P53R, RKO, HCT116, and Colo205 cells, respectively). In contrast, these cells showed significant resistance to the non-conjugated antibody zaptuzumab, indicating that the cytotoxic effects are primarily mediated by DR5-mediated delivery of MMAE. Importantly, the cytotoxic effects of Oba01 were found to be more potent than those of irinotecan and oxaliplatin, two commonly used chemotherapies for treating CRC in clinical settings (Figure 2C). This conclusion was further validated by conducting a thorough analysis of the time-dependent growth inhibition caused by these drugs utilizing the IncuCyte live cell imaging system (Figures 2D–2H) and quantified by the EdU assay (Figure 2I). Cell death in Oba01-treated CRC cells was achieved in a dose-dependent manner by triggering apoptosis (Figures 2J and 2K). Collectively, our data indicated that treatment with Oba01 dose-dependently induces tumor cell death by inducing apoptosis in these CRC cells.

Oba01 exhibited potent in vivo antitumor activities in both MSS and MSI-H CRC

To assess the in vivo effectiveness of Oba01, we have developed a panel of ten PDX models using samples from patients with advanced CRC who have either MSS or MSI-H status (Figure 3A; Table S1). Histological analysis using H&E staining of primary tumors and corresponding PDX tissues revealed that PDXs maintained the diverse morphologies observed in CRC primary tumors (Figure S1A). Immunohistochemistry analysis demonstrated similar expression levels of markers such as pan-cytokeratin (CK-pan), CK20, CDX2, and β-catenin in both primary tumors and PDX tissues (Figure S1B). Furthermore, somatic genetic characteristics were compared between PDX tumors and primary tumors using whole exom sequencing (WES) analysis, revealing a comparable gene landscape and similar somatic mutations in high-frequency mutated genes between the two types of tumors (Figures S1C–S1E). Analysis of single-nucleotide site variation (SNV) indicated that the most common mutation types, C>T/G>A and T>C/A>G, accounted for over 60% of all mutations in both primary tumors and PDXs, with consistent characteristics and frequencies observed between the two types of tissues (Figures S1F and S1G).

These results indicated that our CRC PDXs well recapitulated the architecture, biology, and molecular characteristics of primary CRC tumors. Noteworthy, eight PDXs are characterized as MSS based on the expression of four well-known MMR proteins (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, and PMS2) by IHC assays, while two PDXs are classified as MSI-H (Figure S2A). All models are positive for DR5, as determined by IHC and immunofluorescence assays (Figure 3B).

To assess the effectiveness of Oba01 in vivo, mice with human tumor xenografts ranging from 100 to 200 mm3 were randomly divided into groups. They were then given either 5 mg/kg of Oba01, 5 mg/kg of zaptuzumab (which contains an equal amount of MMAE as Oba01), 0.1 mg/kg of MMAE (equivalent to the MMAE content in 5 mg/kg of Oba01), or doses of 10.0 mg/kg of irinotecan or oxaliplatin. The results revealed that Oba01 at 5 mg/kg caused significant tumor regression in both MSS (CRC082) and MSI-H (CRC114) PDX models. In contrast, treatments with 5 mg/kg of zaptuzumab, 0.1 mg/kg of MMAE, 10.0 mg/kg of irinotecan, or 10.0 mg/kg of oxaliplatin did not demonstrate notable inhibition of tumor growth in these PDX models (Figures 3C and 3D). Additional research on the in vivo anti-cancer effects of Oba01, involving 7 MSS and 1 MSI-H tumors, confirmed the significant and sustained tumor-suppressing properties of Oba01. These results also indicated a substantial improvement in survival rates compared to the control cohorts (Figures 3E–3L). Remarkably, the therapeutic effectiveness of Oba01 was shown to last for a significant duration in mouse models of CRC054, CRC073, CRC085, and CRC196 (284, 98, 75, and 66 days, respectively) without causing notable changes in the mice’s body weight, indicating a positive safety profile (Figures S2B–S2K). In conclusion, our data strongly support Oba01 monotherapy as a viable treatment option for both MSS and MSI-H CRC.

Mapping the synergistic signaling pathways of Oba01 by functional RNA-seq

In cancer treatment, it is well established that employing a blend of therapeutic agents with distinct mechanisms of action can significantly improve the chances of attaining complete remission and potentially curing the disease, especially when faced with the challenges of tumor heterogeneity. To overcome resistance and amplify the efficacy of ADC therapies, it is essential to combine ADCs with diverse therapeutic approaches that can work in synergy with the ADCs to eradicate tumors. To identify potential signaling pathways that may synergize with the anti-tumor effects of Oba01, we conducted RNA-seq analysis on four CRC PDXs models before (control) and after (Oba01) treatments with 5 mg/kg Oba01. These models included two MSS types (CRC082 and CRC196) and two MSI types (CRC090 and CRC114). Our analysis revealed a total of 469 and 259 unique genes expressed in the drug-treated and control groups across the four PDXs, with 12,638 genes being commonly expressed (Figure 4A). Subsequent volcano plot analysis on the shared 12,638 genes identified 941 differentially expressed genes (DEGs), comprising 454 upregulated and 487 downregulated genes (fold change >2 and p value < 0.05; Figures 4B and 4C). Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis showed that the 941 DEGs were enriched in signaling pathways related to cell cycle regulation, DNA damage and repair, purine and pyrimidine metabolism, chemokines, FoxO, interleukin-17, and P53 (Figure 4D). Notably, the cell cycle regulation pathway exhibited the most significant difference between the two groups (Figure 4D). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) further highlighted a significant decrease in the expression of genes associated with cell cycle regulation, such as CDKs, following Oba01 treatment (Figures 4E and 4F).

In recent studies, CDKs have shown promise as a potential therapeutic approach for various cancer types including CRC. Consistent with this, we verified through immunofluorescence assays that CDK4 is highly expressed in the panel of CRC cell lines, primary tumors, and their corresponding PDXs used in this study (Figures 4G and S3A).

To investigate whether the efficacy of Oba01 is mediated by CDK4, we created CDK4-knockdown RKO and HCT116 cell lines using two distinct CDK4-specific short hairpin RNAs (shRNAs). The effectiveness of these shRNAs in silencing CDK4 expression was confirmed through western blotting and qPCR (Figures 4H and 4I). The CDK4-silenced RKO and HCT116 cells exhibited increased sensitivity to Oba01 compared to control cells with intact CDK4 expression (Figure 4J). These results suggest that CDK4 plays a role in modulating cellular sensitivity to Oba01.

To systematically identify the most potent CDK inhibitors for CRC, we conducted a functional screening in P53R, RKO, HCT116, and Colo205 cell lines using a panel of 48 CDK inhibitors. Data indicated that 21 of these inhibitors effectively suppressed the growth of CRC cell lines in a dose-dependent manner (Figure S3B; Table S2). Among these candidates, abemaciclib (short for abemaciclib mesylate, Abem), a CDK4/6 inhibitor, has already been approved for the treatment of advanced breast cancer and is recognized as the CDK4/6 inhibitor capable of reducing the risk of tumor recurrence. Nevertheless, the efficacy of Abem as a monotherapy or in combination therapy for CRC remains insufficiently explored. Additionally, repurposing Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved drugs holds promise for rapid translation into clinical applications. Consequently, we systematically investigated the anti-tumor effects of Abem on CRC cells. Our study demonstrated that Abem induced cell death in P53R, RKO, HCT116, and Colo205 cells in a dose- and time-dependent manner, as evidenced by the CellTiter-Glo luminescent cell viability assay and IncuCyte live cell imaging (Figures S3C and S3D). Furthermore, Abem dose-dependently triggered cell apoptosis and impeded cell cycle progression in these cell lines (Figures S3E–S3H). Data from in vivo studies showed that Abem produced substantial and long-lasting tumor shrinkage in MSS and MSI-H CRC PDXs (CRC082, CRC196, and CRC090) in a dose-dependent manner. However, resistance to the drug was observed in the CRC114 PDX model (Figure 4H).

Collectively, our findings indicate that CDK inhibitors such as Abem could be a promising treatment for both MSS and MSI-H CRC. Combining this with Oba01 may have a synergistic effect in treating both types of CRC.

Oba01 synergized with Abem to suppress the growth of MSS and MSI-H CRC cell lines

To investigate the potential synergy between Oba01 and Abem, we conducted cell viability studies on P53R, RKO, HCT116, and Colo205 cell lines. The cells were treated with Oba01 and Abem individually as well as in combination, revealing synergistic effects at different combination dosages. All combinational doses showed synergistic effects in P53R and HCT116 cells, while only three combinational doses (such as 0.5 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.5 μM Abem, 1.0 μg/mL Oba01 and 1.0 μM Abem, and 2.0 μg/mL Oba01 and 2.0 μM Abem) showed synergistic effects in RKO cells. Therefore, we chose P53R, RKO, and HCT116 cells treated with 1.0 μg/mL Oba01 alone or in combination with 1.0 μM Abem to perform the subsequent in vitro experiments. In addition, four combinational doses (such as 3 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.10 μM Abem, 4 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.15 μM Abem, 5 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.20 μM Abem, and 6 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.25 μM Abem) showed synergistic effects in Colo205 cells, and a combination of 3.0 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.1 μM Abem was used to treat Colo205 cells (Figure 5A). This synergistic effect dependent on dosage was further validated through IncuCyte live cell imaging, cell proliferation assays, and colony formation assays (Figures 5B–5D and S4A–S4D). Additionally, the combinational therapy significantly increased cell apoptosis (Figures 5E and 5F). These findings indicate that the combined approach effectively suppresses cell proliferation and apoptosis in CRC cells.

In order to better understand the mechanisms behind the synergistic effects, we conducted RNA-seq analysis on P53R cells treated with 1 μg/mL of Oba01, 1 μM of Abem, or a combination of both. Analysis of the DEGs in each treatment group using multidimensional scaling revealed that the combination therapy significantly influenced gene expression, with 7,529 genes (28.14%) showing altered expression. Specifically, 3,869 genes (14.46%) were upregulated, and 3,660 genes (13.68%) were downregulated (Figures S4E–S4G). KEGG analysis of DEGs showed that Oba01 treatment most significantly influenced the cell cycle pathways. It also affects various signaling pathways such as FOXO, cellular senescence, mammalian target of rapamycin (MTOR), adenosine 5‘-monophosphate (AMP)-activated protein kinase (AMPK), and apoptosis (Figure 5G). Conversely, the GSEA of common genes in P53R cells treated with Oba01 in combination with Abem revealed a negative enrichment in gene sets associated with cell cycle progression and proliferation, including the MTOR pathway (FDR = 0.189; NES = −1.990), AMPK pathway (FDR = 0.144; NES = −2.210), FOXO pathway (FDR = 0.135; NES = −1.804), apoptosis (FDR = 0.137; NES = −1.711), cell cycle (FDR = 0.117; NES = −1.702), and cellular senescence (FDR < 0.0001; NES = −2.436) (Figure 5H). In line with the RNA-seq analysis, combined treatment with Oba01 and Abem demonstrated a pronounced cell type-specific inhibitory effect on several key signaling effectors, including the mitogenic regulator extracellular regulated protein kinases (ERKs) and survival regulator protein kinase B (AKT). Additionally, phosphorylation of P38, a regulator of cell cycle progression, was significantly reduced in RKO cells but showed minimal change in the rest of the cell lines (Figure 5I, left panel). The combination therapy also led to a significant reduction in the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2 across all cell lines, although Abem monotherapy had a similar effect in P53R cells. Tumor suppressor retinoblastoma protein (Rb) signaling was disrupted to varying degrees by the combination therapy across different cell lines, as evidenced by lower levels of Rb protein and its phosphorylation. However, Abem monotherapy was likely responsible for most of this effect. The level of the cell cycle regulator cyclin D2 was also decreased in RKO and HCT116 cells but not in P53R cells (Figure 5I, right panel). The aforementioned analysis clearly indicates that the combination therapy caused a widespread disruption of cell signaling by deregulating critical cancer-related pathways involved in the regulation of proliferation, the cell cycle, and apoptosis.

To investigate whether the potent combined anti-tumor effects of Oba01 and Abem are consistent across CDK pathway inhibition, we examined the impact of co-administering Oba01 with 8 different CDK inhibitors in multiple cell lines. Our findings demonstrated that these combinations effectively decreased cell growth over a 72-h time frame as compared to using the individual agents alone (Figure S5).

Overall, the findings of the aforementioned in vitro study indicate that Oba01 works together with Abem to effectively inhibit the growth of both MSS and MSI-H CRC cells by targeting a wider range of critical oncogenic signaling pathways.

Combination of Oba01 and Abem synergistically inhibited PDXO growth of advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC

Although PDX models closely mimic human cancer and are frequently utilized in drug evaluation, their expensive nature and extended time required for experimentation restrict their use in high-throughput drug screening. As an alternative, patient tumor-derived organoid or PDX tumor-derived organoid (PDXO) models have emerged as a well-established ex vivo method to model patient disease. In our study, we established a set of CRC PDXO models based on CRC PDX models (Figure 6A). Consistent with previous data, PDXOs exhibited a wide range of growth rates and morphologies (Figure S6A). The hematoxylin-eosin (H&E) assay revealed that PDXOs maintained the heterogeneous morphologies observed in PDX models, ranging from thin-walled cystic structures to solid/compact structures (Figure S6B). Subsequent IHC assays showed that PDXOs and their corresponding PDX tumors exhibited similar expression levels of markers such as CK-pan, CK20, CDX2, and β-catenin (Figure S6C). Additionally, immunofluorescence assays demonstrated that CDK4 and DR5 were overexpressed and exhibited similar subcellular localization in PDXOs (Figure 6B). Furthermore, we conducted WES analysis to compare the somatic genetic features of CRC PDXs and their corresponding PDXOs. Our findings revealed that CRC PDXOs exhibit a gene landscape that is largely similar to their corresponding PDXs, with high-frequency mutated genes displaying consistent somatic mutations between the two models (Figures 6C and 6D). Subsequent analysis of SNV indicated that point mutations of C>T/G>A and T>C/A>G made up more than 60% of all mutation types in the samples. The point mutation patterns and frequencies were consistent between the two models (Figures 6E, S6D, and S6E). These findings validate that our PDXO models faithfully mirror the histological characteristics and marker expressions of the original tumor tissues, preserving the genetic attributes of their PDX counterparts. However, slight variations among different tumors were also observed, in line with existing research.

Subsequently, we examined the ex vivo anti-tumor impact of Oba01 on four colorectal cancer patient-derived xenograft organoid models—two with MSS and two with MSI-H characteristics using IncuCyte live cell imaging. Our findings revealed a dose-dependent suppression of tumor growth by Oba01, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin in these PDXOs. However, these organoids exhibited insensitivity to zaptuzumab, while displaying high sensitivity to MMAE (Figure S7).

Next, we proceeded to investigate the combined impact of Oba01 and Abem on cell proliferation in MSS (CRC082 and CRC196) and MSI-H (CRC90 and CRC114) PDXOs. Through live-cell imaging analysis, we observed a notable reduction in proliferation in the combination treatment group compared to individual treatments with Oba01 or Abem alone across all four PDXOs over a period of 0–144 h (Figures 6F and S8). The synergistic effect is observed across different pairings of Oba01 and CDK inhibitors (Figure S9), aligning with previous findings in CRC cell lines. The modest anti-tumor effects observed in some organoid models are likely due to poor pharmacokinetics of large molecule drug, such as ADCs, in ex vivo cultures. In support of this, the strong in vivo efficacy of Oba01 observed in the PDX model CRC090-P is notably reduced in the corresponding organoid model (O-090), highlighting a limitation of the method and the need for in vivo validation. Nevertheless, these findings suggest that combining Oba01 and CDK inhibitors may produce synergistic anti-cancer effects, presenting a more comprehensive therapeutic strategy for leveraging Oba01 in advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC.

To further unravel the synergistic mechanisms of Oba01 combined with Abem, we profiled protein expressions of 4 PDXOs treated with vehicle, Oba01, Abem, or the combination by mass spectrometry. By enrichment analyses of proteomic data, we identified several pathways that significantly changed in the combination group but not in the Oba01 monotherapy group (Figure 6G). Among them, Notch signaling pathway was significantly downregulated (Figure 6H). This was further confirmed by immunoblotting of Notch family members (Figure 6I). Notch signaling plays a critical role in maintaining the balance between cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis, and its deregulation may lead to the initiation and progression of CRC. Notch receptors and their ligands are aberrantly activated in many human cancers, and their overexpression is correlated with increased progression, metastatic potential, and poor prognosis in various cancers, including CRC. We therefore investigated whether inhibiting Notch signaling could sensitize tumors to the growth suppression induced by the combination therapy of Oba01 and Abem. Preliminary data using the pan-Notch inhibitor FLI-06 showed no enhancement of the tumor-killing activity when combined with Oba01 and Abem (Figures 6J and S10). This suggests that the role of Notch signaling in the anti-tumor effects of the combination therapy requires further comprehensive studies in the future.

Oba01 combined with Abem cooperatively improved the anti-tumor activity in multiple PDXs of advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC

The cooperative enhancement of anti-tumor activity in multiple PDXs of advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC was observed when Oba01 was combined with Abem. Following the positive results from cell lines and PDXOs, further investigations were carried out to assess the effects of combining Oba01 and Abem at a modest therapeutic dose in four advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC PDX models. In the MSS CRC054 PDX model, treatment with 0.5 mg/kg Oba01 alone resulted in a tumor growth inhibitory rate (TGI) of 74.50%, while 25 mg/kg Abem alone achieved a TGI of 40.38% (Figure 7A). The combination treatment significantly enhanced the therapeutic effect, leading to a TGI of 79.66%. Similarly, in the MSS CRC082 PDX model, the combination of Oba01 and Abem demonstrated synergistic inhibitory effects, with a TGI of 68.25%, compared to the individual TGIs of 27.55% for Oba01 and 34.10% for Abem (Figure 7B). In the MSS CRC085 PDX model, the combination treatment showed a TGI of 77.53%, outperforming the TGIs of 34.49% for Oba01 and 56.82% for Abem when administered alone (Figure 7C). Additionally, in the MSI-H CRC114 PDX model, the combination treatment exhibited a TGI of 81.39%, surpassing the individual TGIs of 34.49% for Oba01 and 56.82% for Abem (Figure 7D). Notably, in the CRC054 PDX model, the dose of Oba01 was too high, leading to almost 100% tumor inhibition with the monotherapy, making it difficult to determine whether a synergistic effect exists with the combination therapy. This does not negate the possibility of a synergistic effect. CRC is a highly heterogeneous tumor, and different patients exhibit varying responses to the same therapy, making it more challenging to determine the optimal treatment dosage. However, the combination therapy clearly demonstrated a synergistic effect in the other three PDX models. Collectively, these results showcase the powerful potential of the combination therapy with Oba01 and Abem in treating advanced MSS and MSI-H CRC.

The PDX xenograft tumors were then subjected to molecular and pathological analysis to validate the findings from in vitro studies and provide insights into therapeutic efficacy. Treatment with Oba01 alone and in combination with Abem led to significant inhibitory effects on the number of Ki67-positive cells compared to the vehicle group. Notably, the combination treatment exhibited a more pronounced antiproliferative effect on tumor cells than Oba01 or Abem alone (Figures 7E–7L). Moreover, the combination treatments were well tolerated in the PDX models, as evidenced by no significant changes in body weight during the experimental period (Figures S11A–S11D). Additionally, no apparent toxic side effects on the heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney were observed with the combination of Oba01 and Abem, indicating negligible in vivo toxicity (Figures S11E–S11H).

In conclusion, these findings highlight the synergistic enhancement of tumoricidal activity in vivo with the combination of Oba01 and Abem. This provides a strong rationale for advancing Oba01 for clinical development in the treatment of patients with CRC.

Discussion

Effective treatments for patients with advanced CRC with MSS status are still highly challenging. Neither chemotherapy nor targeted therapies such as immune checkpoint blockade show satisfactory efficacy for this subset of patients with CRC.

DR5 was initially seen as a promising target for treating different types of carcinomas due to its frequent overexpression in many cancer types. However, early clinical trials using non-targeting antibodies did not show sufficient clinical benefits despite having well-tolerated safety profiles. Various alternative strategies are now being actively tested in clinical trials, including multivalent antibodies, bispecific antibodies, agonistic small molecules, and rsTRAILs with enhanced stability. Oba01, a clinical-stage ADC designed to target DR5 and deliver MMAE, is the latest addition to the growing category of DR5-targeting therapies developed in our group. While no ADCs have been clinically approved for the treatment of CRC to date, certain candidates such as trastuzumab deruxtecan (DS-8201a) and sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy) are undergoing clinical trials (phase 1/2/3).

In this study, we found increased expression of DR5 in patients with advanced CRC, suggesting it as a potential therapeutic target. Oba01 showed strong antitumor efficacy in various CRC models, including PDXs and PDXOs from patients with advanced CRC, particularly in the MSS subset. The inhibitory effects were long-lasting and suppressed multiple signaling pathways, contributing to the robust antitumor activity of this ADC.

The clinical efficacy of ADCs is often limited by drug resistance and off-target toxicity. To tackle this, we carried out RNA-seq and proteomic analyses to investigate the impact of the drug Oba01 on overall protein expression. Our data suggested the CDK signaling pathway as the most affected pathway in Oba01-treated cells. CDKs play a crucial role in regulating the cell cycle by controlling cell cycle entry through the phosphorylation of the Rb. This helps to prevent cell cycle progression and inhibit tumor cell proliferation. In human CRCs, CDKs and cyclins are often dysregulated. For example, cyclin B1 is overexpressed in 90% of cases, while cyclin D1 is overexpressed in about 30% of primary tumors. Studies have shown promising anti-cancer effects of CDK inhibitors such as flavopiridol and CDK4/6 inhibitors like palbociclib, ribociclib, and Abem in various cancer types. However, the therapeutic potential of CDK inhibitors as standalone treatments is often limited, leading to interest in exploring their combination with other targeted therapies to improve cancer treatment effectiveness. These findings prompt us to investigate whether CDK inhibitors can enhance the anti-cancer effects of Oba01 in CRC.

Our findings showed that combining Abem, an FDA-approved drug for advanced breast cancer, with Oba01 significantly improved the in vivo inhibitory impact on the growth of MSS and MSI-H CRC PDXs without causing toxic effects or inducing tolerance. This effect was observed even at lower doses compared to when each drug was used individually. The synergistic effect can be extended to other CDK inhibitors and target a wider range of critical oncogenic signaling pathways.

Limitations of the study

Although the current study supports DR5 as a strong candidate for ADC targeting, a major limitation is that only one type of toxin was tested. Auristatin derivatives, such as MMAE, kill tumor cells by disrupting microtubule assembly. While several MMAE-based ADCs have been approved for solid tumors worldwide, CRC is generally considered relatively resistant to MMAE, making our findings somewhat unexpected.

However, due to the complex mechanisms underlying ADC-mediated cytotoxicity, various factors could influence overall efficacy, including antigen target levels, internalization mechanisms, toxin release efficiency, linker properties, and the characteristics of both the targeting antibody and the payload. A definitive conclusion must be established through further experimental studies. Consequently, several microtubule inhibitor-carrying ADCs are still actively being investigated in both clinical and preclinical development for CRC.

A more rational selection of toxins could be achieved through a comprehensive analysis of biomarkers linked to ADC sensitivities using multi-omics approaches. In fact, several genes, including RPL28, PGLS, ODC1, and HNRNPF, which are highly expressed in CRC, have been shown to be associated with increased sensitivity to microtubule inhibitors, supporting further investigation in clinical trials. Notably, CRC is also one of several cancers that exhibit high expression of DNA topoisomerase 1 (TOP1), making ADCs conjugated to TOP1 inhibitors, such as exatecan derivatives, an attractive option for colon cancer. In support, ABBV400, a c-Met targeting antibody telisotuzumab conjugated to a TOP1 inhibitor, showed early signs of clinical efficacy in patients with CRC. Therefore, our ongoing study is investigating the potential of conjugating an anti-DR5 antibody with TOP1 inhibitors for CRC treatment.

A significant limitation of this study is that the data do not adequately capture the heterogeneous nature of CRC, primarily because only a relatively small number of PDX models were examined due to the study’s limited scope. For instance, while our findings suggest that Oba01 demonstrates sustained efficacy in most PDX models, we have observed tumor recurrence at later treatment time points in some models. It remains to be determined whether this recurrence is linked to hyperactivation of CDKs or the co-activation of uncharacterized signaling pathways, which warrants further investigation. Considering the intricate mechanisms through which ADCs exert their tumor-killing effects, it is not surprising that multiple signaling pathways play a role in determining sensitivity to Oba01. Consequently, we have noticed profound activation patterns of these pathways across different CRC models. A more comprehensive study, including multi-omic analysis across a broader range of CRC PDX models, could yield further insights into this phenomenon. Additionally, the comparative effectiveness of the combined therapy utilizing Oba01 and CDK inhibitors versus existing standards of care for CRC should be more comprehensively assessed in animal models, along with an evaluation of their safety profile.

Despite aforementioned limitations, our current data strongly support the use of DR5-mediated MMAE delivery and the CDK inhibitor Abem as an innovative therapeutic approach for treating MSS CRC. The ability to replicate the synergistic effect between Oba01 and various CDK inhibitors indicates that tumor cell death, achieved by impairing microtubule function, can be enhanced through concurrent inhibition of the CDK pathway. Indeed, a phase 2 study is investigating the combination of the FDA-approved targeting-human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) ADC (T-DM1) with Abem for the treatment of metastatic HER2-positive breast cancer (NCT04351230). This presents avenues for investigating this treatment approach in CRC as well as other cancer types.

Method details

In vivo antitumor efficacy in PDX models

Tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into 2–4 groups (n = 3–6) once tumor volumes reached 100–150 mm3. In the monotherapy study, mice received vehicle, 5 mg/kg Oba01 (IV, once weekly for 3 weeks), or 50 mg/kg and 100 mg/kg Abem (oral, daily) until the end of the experiment. In the combination therapy study, mice were treated with vehicle, 0.5 or 2.5 mg/kg Oba01 (IV, weekly for 3 weeks), 25 mg/kg Abem (oral, daily), or both. Tumor size and body weight were monitored twice weekly, and tumor volumes were calculated using the formula: tumor volume (mm3) = length × (width) × 0.5. Tumor growth inhibition (TGI) was calculated as (1 - T/C) × 100%, with T/C = treated tumor volume/control tumor volume × 100%. Mice were sacrificed and organs collected once tumor volume reached 2000 mm3 for further analysis.

In vitro cytotoxicity assay

Cells were seeded at 3000–5000 cells/well and treated with specified drugs or drug combination, using a 3-fold gradient dilution, for 72 h. Cell viability was measured by Cell Titer-Glo luminescent cell viability assay kit (Promega, Madison, USA) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instruction. The luminescence was recorded by microplate multiplate reader (TECAN, SPARK, Switzerland). For real-time live cell analysis, cells were treated with specified drugs for 72 h. Cell proliferation was monitored using the IncuCyte live cell analysis system (Sartorius, S3, Germany), and confluence curves were automatically generated by analyzing captured images.

Coefficient of drug interaction

The coefficient of drug interaction (CDI) was calculated to evaluate the interaction between two drugs. The CDI was calculated relative to cell survival rate of each group as follows: CDI = AB/(A×B). AB is the cell survival rate of the combination group, and A and B are the cell survival rate of each single-agent group, respectively. CDI value <, = , or >1 represents a synergistic, additive or antagonistic effect, respectively.

Cell apoptosis and cell cycle assay

Cells were seeded at the density of 2 × 105 cells/well and exposed to Oba01 and/or Abem at the different concentrations for 48 h. Apoptosis was assessed using the Annexin V-FITC and PI Apoptosis Kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) and cell cycle status was analyzed by staining with the cell cycle assay kit (Beyotime Biotechnology, Shanghai, China). Apoptosis and the cell cycle status were measured by flow cytometry (BD, FACSCantoⅡ, USA).

EdU staining assay

Cells were seeded on coverslips in 12-well plates at 2×105 cells/well and treated with Oba01 and/or Abem for 48 h. After washing with PBS, cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 30 min, permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 for 15 min, and then stained using the BeyoClick EdU Cell Proliferation Kit (C0075S, Beyotime, Shanghai, China) for 30 min at 37°C. Fluorescent images were captured using a laser confocal microscope (Zeiss, LSM880, Germany) after mounting the slides with anti-fade media containing DAPI (Beyotime, China).

Clonogenic assays

Cells were seeded in 12-well plates at 3,000 cells/well and treated with Oba01 and/or Abem at varying concentrations for 1–2 weeks. Colonies were washed with PBS, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 30 min, and stained with 0.01% crystal violet for 15 min. The stained colonies were scanned, and the total colony area was quantified using ImageJ software.

CDK4 knockdown

shRNAs targeting CDK4 were subcloned into the pLKO.1 plasmid (Addgene, USA). Lentivirus was produced by transfecting HEK293T cells with plasmids pMD2.G, psPAX2 (Addgene plasmid, USA) and targeting plasmids. Viruses were collected 72 h after transfection. For knockdown, cells were infected with the viruse in the presence of 3 μg/mL polybrene (Polyscience, USA) for 24 h.After removing virus-containing medium, cells were cultured for 72 h in fresh medium containing 2 μg/mL puromycin (Beyotime, China). shRNAs sequences as follows: sh-EV targeted sequence: 5′-GCTCCGTGAACGGCCACGAGT-3’; shCDK4#2 targeted sequence: 5′-GTTCTTCTGCAGTCCACATAT-3’; shCDK4#3 targeted sequence: 5′-ACAGTTCGTGAGGTGGCTTTA-3’. Total RNAs were extracted using TransZol Up (ET111-01-V2, TransGen Biotech). mRNA expression level was detected by PerfectStart Green qPCR SuperMix (AQ602-02-V2, TransGen Biotech) on an C1000 Touch (Bio-Rad, USA). All the primers were purchased from Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). Primers as follows: CDK4-forward: CTGGTGTTTGAGCATGTAGACC; CDK4-reverse: GATCCTTGATCGTTTCGGCTG; Actin-forward: GCCAACACAGTGCTGTCTGG; Actin-reverse: CTCAGGAGGAGCAATGATCTTG.

Western blotting

HCT116, P53R and RKO cells were incubated with 1 μg/mL Oba01 and 1μM Abem for two days, while Colo205 cells were incubated with 3 μg/mL Oba01 and 0.1μM Abem. Subsequently, cells were lysed with SDS lysis buffer (Beyotime, China) containing PMSF (Solarbio, China) and Phosphatase Inhibitor Cocktail (Beyotime, China), denatured at 100°C, seperated by SDS-PAGE, and transferred onto PVDF membranes (Millipore, Germany). The membranes were blocked with 5% BSA for 1 h at room temperature, incubated overnight with primary antibodies at 4°C, followed by incubation with secondary antibodies. The protein band was detected using ECL reagent (Cytiva, USA) and imaged with the Bio-Rad Multifunctional chemiluminescence imaging system(Bio-Red, USA).

Immunofluorescence assay

Cells were seeded on coverslips in 12-well plates at 2×105 cells/well, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 30 min, permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 for 15 min, and blocked with 1% BSA for 1 h. They were then incubated overnight at 4°C with DR5 and CDK4 antibodies, followed by a 1-h incubation with fluorescent secondary antibodies at room temperature. Images were captured using a Zeiss LSM880 laser confocal microscope after mounting with DAPI-containing anti-fade media (Beyotime, China).

CRC tumor tissue was fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 24 h, dehydrated with sucrose gradient, embedded in O.C.T. Compound, and sectioned at 10μm. Slides were washed in PBS, blocked with 5% BSA for 1 h, incubated overnight at 4°C with DR5 antibody, and then with Goat anti-Rabbit IgG Alexa Fluor 488 secondary antibody for 1 h at room temperature. Images were captured using the Zeiss LSM880 microscope.

Organoid culture

Fresh PDX tumor tissues were prepared by removing non-epithelial components, rinsing with DPBS, and cutting into 1–3 mm3 fragments. These fragments were washed with DPBS and digested with Tumor Tissue Digestion Solution (bioGenous, China) at 37°C for 0.5–1.5 h. After digestion, FBS was added to stop the process, and cells were filtered through a 100 μm strainer, centrifuged at 250 g for 3 min at 4°C, and washed twice with Cancer Organoid Basal Medium (bioGenous, China). The cells were resuspended in Organoid Culture ECM (bioGenous, China), plated at the center of a 24-well plate, and incubated at 37°C to solidify. 500 μL of CRC organoid complete medium was added to each well, and the organoids were cultured at 37°C with 5% CO2, with medium changes every 3–4 days. Organoids were passaged 7–10 days post-plating. To passage, the ECM was disrupted, the suspension centrifuged, and organoids were dissociated using Organoid Dissociation Solution (bioGenous, China). After washing, the pellet was resuspended in 70% ECM and plated in droplets. Subsequent steps followed the same procedure as primary culture.

In vitro real-time organoid viability analysis

CRC organoids were seeded in 96-well plates and cultured for 2 days, then treated with specified drugs or drug combination at indicated concentrations, using a 3-fold gradient dilution, for 6 days. Organoid viability was measured by the IncuCyte live cell analysis system (Sartorius, S3, Germany). For the CRC organoids combinational treatment, 50 μg/mL Oba01 and 200nM Abem or other CDKi were used. For the CRC organoids rescue experiments, 50 μg/mL Oba01, 200nM Abem and 2μM FLI-06 were used.

Whole exome sequencing analysis

Our dataset includes ten matched tumor/PDX pairs and four matched tumor/PDX/PDXO triplets. Exome sequencing was performed on genomic DNA using Agilent SureSelect Human All Exon V6 and Agilent SureSelectXT Mouse. All Exon libraries (Agilent, USA) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Genomic DNA was fragmented using Covaris LE220R-plus (Covaris, USA), then end-repaired, A-tailed, and ligated with paired-end adapters. Enriched DNA fragments were amplified by PCR, indexed, and purified with the AMPure XP system (Beckman Coulter, USA). Library size distribution was assessed with an Agilent 5400 system, and concentration was quantified by real-time PCR (1.5 nM). The libraries were pooled and sequenced on Illumina platforms using the PE150 strategy. Clean data was mapped to the reference genome by Burrows Wheeler Aligner (BWA) software to generate BAM file. Subsequently, Sambamba was used to sort BAM files and mark duplicate reads according to chromosome position. Somatic mutation detection is commonly applied to normal and tumor-paired samples. The somatic SNV was detected by MuTect. ANNOVAR was used to perform variant annotation.

Bulk RNA-Seq analysis

PDX tumor tissues from mice treated with 5 mg/kg Oba01 (n = 3) were isolated and frozen in liquid nitrogen. P53R cells were divided into control (n = 3), Oba01 (n = 3), Abem (n = 3), and Oba01/Abem combination (COM; n = 3) groups. Total RNA was extracted from PDX tumor tissues and P53R cells. RNA quantity and integrity were assessed using the RNA Nano 6000 Assay Kit on the Bioanalyzer 2100 system (Agilent Technologies, USA). mRNA was purified using poly-T oligo-attached magnetic beads, and library fragments were purified with AMPure XP beads (Beckman Coulter, USA). The sequencing library was constructed, and libraries were pooled based on effective concentration and target data amount. Sequencing was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 with a 150 bp paired-end read. KEGG pathways and differentially expressed genes were analyzed with a threshold P-value <0.05.

Proteomics analysis

PDOs treated with 50 μg/mL Oba01, 200nM Abem or Oba01/Abem combination for 48 h and lysed with DB lysis buffer (8 M Urea, 100 mM TEAB, pH 8.5) and ultrasonication. Upon centrifugation at 12000g for 15 min at 4°C, the supernatant was reduced with 1M DTT for 1 h at 56°C, and alkylated with sufficient iodoacetamide for 1 h at room temperature in dark, and subject to proteolysis by trypsin and desalting. Resulting peptide mixtures were analyzed in DIA mode using the Orbitrap Q ExactiveTM HF-X mass spectrometer couple with the EASY-nLCTM 1200 UHPLC system (Thermo Fisher, Germany), which was performed by Novogene Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China). The spectra were searched against a database using Spectronaut-Pulsar (Biognosys) search engine. DIA data were imported into Spectronaut to generate a DDA library, and ion-pair chromatographic peaks were extracted. Ion matching and peak area calculation enabled qualitative and quantitative peptide analysis. Protein quantitation was statistically analyzed by T-test, and proteins with significant differences (p < 0.05, FC > 1.5) between experimental and control groups were considered differentially expressed proteins (DEP). KEGG was used to analyze protein families and pathways.

Histopathology and immunohistochemistry analysis

PDX xenograft and patient tumor tissues were prepared into 4-μm paraffin tissue microarrays (TMA). Slides were deparaffinized with xylene, rehydrated with graded ethanol, and antigen retrieval was performed using EDTA buffer. DR5 and KI67 expression were detected with the UltraSensitiveTM SP IHC Kit (MXBiotechnologies, China), followed by hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining. Imaging was done using TissueFAXS Plus (version 7.0, TissueGnostics GmbH, Austria). Staining intensity (negative (×0), weak (×1), medium (×2), strong (×3)) and percentage of positively stained cells were quantified. The semi-quantitative H-score was calculated as: IHC Score = Percentage of positive cells × staining intensity. Scores of 0, <100, 100–150, and >150 were classified as negative, low, moderate, and high expression, respectively. H-score analysis was performed by pathologists.
