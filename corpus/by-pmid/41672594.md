# Phase 1 open-label study of ASP9801, an oncolytic virus, in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: ASP9801, an oncolytic virus encoding interleukin-7 and interleukin-12, was assessed for safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics, and antitumor activity in patients with advanced solid tumors in a Phase 1 study.

**METHODS**: The study comprised Part 1 ASP9801 monotherapy dose escalation and Part 2 dose expansion with ASP9801 monotherapy or plus pembrolizumab. Each part was divided into Groups A (ASP9801 injection into cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) and B (ASP9801 injection into visceral lesions). Primary objectives were safety and maximum tolerated dose and/or recommended Phase 2 dose expansion (RP2D); secondary objectives included antitumor activity and pharmacokinetics.

**RESULTS**: In dose escalation, 17 and 10 patients in Groups A and B, respectively, received treatment. One dose-limiting toxicity (DLT; grade 3 cytokine release syndrome) was reported in Group A. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) in ≥30% of patients in Group A were pyrexia and in Group B were pyrexia, chills, vomiting, nausea, fatigue, and headache. AEs leading to death occurred in two patients in Group A (one each for malignant neoplasm progression and sepsis); neither was attributed to ASP9801. The RP2D of ASP9801 was 5×108 plaque-forming unit/mL. In Part 2 dose expansion Group A, 12 patients received monotherapy RP2D, 5 received monotherapy induction, and 2 received ASP9801 plus pembrolizumab. In Group B, 9 and 11 patients received ASP9801 monotherapy or plus pembrolizumab, respectively. One DLT occurred in Group B combination safety cohort (grade 2 pyrexia and mental status change; grade 3 hypoxia, lymphopenia, and pneumonitis). During dose expansion, TRAEs in ≥30% of any cohort (>1 patient) in Group A were nausea, fatigue, chills, pyrexia, and headache and in Group B were nausea, vomiting, fatigue, chills, pyrexia, and lymphocyte count decreased. AEs led to death in two patients in Group A monotherapy and one patient in Group B monotherapy cohorts, all due to malignant neoplasm progression; none were attributed to ASP9801. One patient in Group B combination safety cohort achieved confirmed partial response (objective response rate, 9.1% (95% CI 0.3% to 52.7%)).

**CONCLUSIONS**: Treatment with ASP9801 alone or plus pembrolizumab was generally well tolerated; however, the study was stopped due to lack of efficacy during dose expansion.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Oncolytic virus (OV) immunotherapies represent a new category of targeted cancer treatments that induce innate and adaptive immune response resulting in selective tumor cell death.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This first-in-human, Phase 1 study investigated the safety and efficacy of ASP9801, an attenuated recombinant oncolytic vaccinia virus encoding interleukin-7 (IL-7) and IL-12 in patients with solid tumors. The safety profile was manageable. While ASP9801 did not meet the efficacy endpoint as monotherapy, the drug showed antitumor activity in combination with pembrolizumab.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE, OR POLICY

Based on the findings, the antitumor activity of ASP9801 in combination with pembrolizumab supports further evaluation of OV as combination therapy with pembrolizumab and other anticancer agents.

Introduction 

 Substantial advances in cancer immunotherapy have been made over the past decade. However, alternative treatments that minimize side effects and prevent relapse and recurrence are needed. Oncolytic virus (OV) immunotherapies are an emerging class of targeted cancer treatments that use native or genetically modified viruses to selectively replicate within tumor cells and induce immunogenic cell death.

ASP9801 is a novel attenuated recombinant oncolytic vaccinia virus expressing human transgenes for interleukins 7 and 12 (IL-7 and IL-12). In ASP9801, the virulence genes for vaccinia growth factor and O1L were functionally inactivated by insertion of human IL-12 and IL-7 genes into these two loci, respectively. IL-12 activates both innate and adaptive immunity through interferon-gamma (IFNγ) secretion from natural killer cells, CD8+ T cells, and CD4+ T cells. IL-7, when combined with IL-12, maintains T-cell homeostasis and exerts additive stimulation of T cells. Encoding both IL-7 and IL-12 in ASP9801 was designed to boost the inflammatory immune status to elicit systemic antitumor efficacy. Additionally, the B5R membrane protein within ASP9801 was modified by removing the four extracellular domains to reduce antigenicity, which could reduce clearance of the virus by the host immune system.

ASP9801 demonstrated cytotoxicity against human cancer cell lines from several tumor sites including skin, lung, kidney, bladder, head and neck, and others. The key advantage of OV virus therapy is the ability to selectively deliver therapeutic payloads specifically to tumors. Following infection, OVs cause cancer cells to burst, releasing antigens that trigger immune responses to eliminate remaining tumor cells locally and throughout the body. Thus, OV therapy is an emerging therapeutic option for cancer treatment due to the potential for influencing the tumor microenvironment and producing additive effects when combined with other anticancer therapies.

OV can turn “cold” tumors into “hot” tumors by stimulating infiltration of CD8+ T cells into the tumor and thus enhancing the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors. Currently, many clinical trials are ongoing with novel OVs used as monotherapy or in combination with other cancer therapies such as programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) inhibitors. However, only a few have demonstrated clinically meaningful activity in solid tumors thus far. ASP9801-surrogate in combination with PD-1 or anticytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) antibodies showed a greater antitumor effect than monotherapy with ASP9801-surrogate, anti-PD-1, or anti-CTLA-4 antibodies. ASP9801-surrogate increased tumor infiltration by lymphocytes and upregulated programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression by cancer cells, which sensitized the tumor to immune checkpoint blockade. Hence, combining ASP9801 with immune checkpoint inhibitors such as pembrolizumab may result in improved clinical outcomes.

Here we describe the results of a Phase 1 study of ASP9801 alone or in combination with PD-1 inhibitor pembrolizumab in patients with advanced solid tumors.

Materials and methods

Patients

Patients were enrolled at 15 centers in the USA. Adults with histologically or cytologically confirmed advanced or metastatic solid tumor(s) with an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status of 0 or 1 and measurable disease as determined by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) V.1.1 were eligible. Patients had disease progression after, were intolerant to, or had refused available therapies; the number of prior treatment regimens was not limited. At least one lesion measuring ≥10 mm and ≤60 mm in longest diameter was needed for patients receiving intratumoral injections. Patients were excluded if they had ongoing toxicity of grade ≥2 per National Cancer Institute-Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (NCI-CTCAE) attributable to prior antineoplastic therapies and considered clinically significant by the investigator. Patients who received prior therapy with an anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, or anti-PD-L2 agent or an agent directed to another stimulatory or coinhibitory T-cell receptor (eg, CTLA-4, OX40, CD137) were excluded if they discontinued that treatment due to an immune-related adverse event (irAE).

Study design and treatment

This was a Phase 1, first-in-human, two-part, open-label, multicenter study (figure 1). Part 1 was dose escalation of ASP9801 in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors; Part 2 was dose expansion. Each study part was divided into Group A (injection of ASP9801 into cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) and Group B (injection of ASP9801 into visceral lesions).

All lesions selected for intratumoral injection with ASP9801 within the maximum number per organ per RECIST V.1.1 were selected as target lesions. For each lesion, the actual injected volume, tumor size (eg, longest dimension), and tumor shape (eg, spherical, superficial, oval, irregular) were recorded. Tumor sizes ranged from 1.0 cm to 6.0 cm. The recommended injection volume per longest dimension was estimated based on an ellipsoid tumor shape. Less than the maximum indicated injection volume could be administered to target lesions at the investigator’s discretion if the lesion shape was flatter and had lower tumor volume (eg, melanoma). Recommended ASP9801 injection volumes were 1.0 mL, 2.0 mL, 4.0 mL, and 6.0 mL for tumor sizes of ≥1.0 to <2.0 cm, ≥2.0 to <3.0 cm, ≥3.0 to <4.0 cm, and ≥4.0 to 6.0 cm, respectively, to maintain consistent virus exposure to the individual tumor lesions. The largest and/or most symptomatic lesions within the specified size range were prioritized for injection. If a selected lesion required only 1 or 2 mL for injection and additional target lesions existed, multiple lesions were recommended for injection. The total volume of ASP9801 administered did not exceed 6 mL per patient per administration. At least one untreated tumor was required for biopsy collection at baseline and cycle 2 day 1. The same lesion was selected for injection during cycles 1 and 2. During the extended treatment period, previously untreated lesions (eg, biopsied lesions) could be selected for injection, but the total dose did not exceed 6 mL.

Part 1: monotherapy dose escalation

Dose escalation began with Group A (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) at dose level 1 (1×107 plaque-forming unit (pfu)/mL). After dose level 2 had been cleared in Group A, dose escalation for Group B (visceral lesions) began at dose level 2 (1×108 pfu/mL). During the initial treatment period, patients received ASP9801 via injection to tumors as selected by the investigator on day 1 and 15 of each of two 28-day cycles. ASP9801 was given at the volume previously specified. The same tumors were injected with ASP9801 at each time point in cycles 1 and 2. Patients were evaluated for clinical benefits following cycle 2; those in the extended treatment period received additional intratumoral injection of ASP9801 on days 1 and 15 of each cycle until treatment discontinuation criteria were met. In each dose escalation cohort, at least 7 days’ gap was maintained (staggered) between treatment of the first patient at each dose level and subsequent patients at that level.

Part 2: dose expansion as monotherapy or in combination with pembrolizumab

Dose expansion based on recommended Phase 2 dose (RP2D) was planned as two stages. Stage 1 for Group A (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) included ASP9801 monotherapy RP2D, ASP9801 monotherapy induction, and combination (ASP9801 plus pembrolizumab) safety cohorts. For Stage 1 in Group B (visceral lesions), combination (ASP9801 plus pembrolizumab) safety cohort and monotherapy RP2D cohorts were included. Stage 2 was designed to further explore antitumor activity of ASP9801 monotherapy or in combination with pembrolizumab in selected tumor types/dose regimens based on data observed in Stage 1.

The starting dose of ASP9801 for the combination safety and induction cohorts was the monotherapy RP2D as determined during dose escalation. Patients in the monotherapy cohorts received intratumoral injections of ASP9801 at a dose of 5×108 pfu/mL. Patients in induction cohorts had weekly injections (days 1, 8, 15, and 22) during the first cycle, and then on days 1 and 15 of each subsequent cycle. The same tumors were injected with ASP9801 at each time point in cycles 1 and 2. Patients in the combination safety cohort received intratumoral injections of ASP9801 5×108 pfu/mL plus pembrolizumab (400 mg every 6 weeks (Q6W) starting from cycle 1 day 1).

Study endpoints

The primary endpoints were safety, tolerability, and maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and/or RP2D of ASP9801 as monotherapy or plus pembrolizumab. Select secondary endpoints were antitumor activity (ie, percent change from baseline in sum of diameters of injected tumors); objective response rate (ORR) of injected tumors; pharmacokinetics, and viral shedding of ASP9801 alone or in combination with pembrolizumab; and change in PD-L1 status of tumors. Select exploratory endpoints included pharmacodynamics, biomarkers to detect ASP9801 response or resistance, and ORR of non-injected tumors.

Safety

Safety and tolerability were measured by dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs), adverse events (AEs), serious AEs (SAEs), treatment-emergent AEs (TEAEs), AEs of special interest, and laboratory assessments (ie, hematology, clinical chemistry, and urinalysis). A TEAE was defined as an AE observed after starting administration of study intervention and up to 16 weeks after the final administration of study intervention. An AE was considered treatment-related (TRAE) when there was a reasonable possibility that it might have been caused by the study drug intervention as assessed by the investigator. AEs were categorized by system organ class and preferred terms using Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities V.26.0 and were graded according to NCI-CTCAE V.5.0.

A DLT was defined as any single event occurring during the 28-day DLT assessment period that was considered possibly, probably, or definitely related to study intervention. DLTs were confirmed by the dose escalation and safety committee. Criteria for DLT sections are discussed in online supplemental methods. The DLT evaluation analysis set included patients who completed the 28-day DLT evaluation period following the first administration of ASP9801. Aside from Part 1 dose escalation, patients in Part 2 dose expansion were also evaluated for DLTs during cycle 1 of the monotherapy induction, combination safety, and combination induction cohorts.

MTD was defined as the dose level at which the rate of DLTs, estimated using Bayesian Optimal Interval design, was closest to the target DLT rate of 33.0%. RP2D of ASP9801 was determined by the sponsor based on available safety, pharmacodynamics, and efficacy data of ASP9801. MTD and RP2D dose levels were determined based on data from at least six patients. MTD and RP2D were determined separately for Group A (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) and Group B (visceral lesions).

Efficacy

Efficacy assessments included ORR reporting best overall response (BOR), complete response (CR), partial response (PR), progressive disease, and stable disease (SD) by RECIST V.1.1 and immune-modified RECIST (imRECIST) V.1.1. Disease assessments appropriate for treated lesions (eg, radiographic imaging for visceral tumors, calipers for cutaneous lesions) were conducted at baseline (up to 28 days prior to first injection), end of cycle 2, at the end of every even cycle prior to day 1 of the next cycle during the extended treatment period, and at the 10-week safety follow-up visit. Skin lesions were documented by color photography, including a ruler to estimate the size of the lesion. Additional disease assessments were conducted per standard of care at the investigator’s discretion. Survival follow-up was performed according to institutional practice but not less than every 12±2 weeks for all patients who received at least one treatment. Follow-up was continued until death, withdrawal of consent, study termination by the sponsor, or the patient was lost to follow-up. Patients were evaluated for clinical benefits (defined as radiological response or SD or reduction in disease-related symptoms) at the end of cycle 2. Patients were allowed to continue the extended treatment period at the investigator’s discretion, if there was a clinical benefit, and no discontinuation criteria were met. The target lesions selected at baseline were measured at all subsequent disease assessments according to RECIST V.1.1, including after progression.

Pharmacokinetics

In Part 1 dose escalation and Part 2 dose expansion, ASP9801 viral DNA load was investigated in whole blood by quantitative PCR (qPCR) assay. During cycles 1 and 2, samples were collected on day 1 (predose; 1, 3, 6 hours postdose); day 2 (24-hours postdose); day 4; day 8; and predose on day 15.

Pharmacodynamics

Pharmacodynamic endpoints included IL-7, IL-12, and chemokine (CXCL10) parameters (in blood) and PD-L1 and CD8 parameters (in tumor biopsies). Standalone Meso Scale Discovery assays were used for evaluation of IL-7 (V-PLEX Human IL-7; catalog# K151RCD-2), and IL-12 (V-PLEX Human IL-12p70 (Catalog# K151QV)), and the Multiplex Luminex assay, Myriad, RBM was used for measurement of CXCL10 in human blood. Immunohistochemistry (IHC) and SP142 assays from CellCarta were used for measurement of CD8 and PD-L1, respectively.

Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics (means, standard deviation, and medians for continuous data; and frequency and percentages for categorical data) were used to summarize baseline demographics, characteristics, and efficacy parameters. ORR was summarized with two-sided exact 95% confidence intervals (CIs) based on the binomial distribution. AEs and DLTs were summarized by count and percentages. Summary descriptive statistics of pharmacokinetic concentrations and parameters (AUClast, Cmax, Ctrough, and tmax) of ASP9801 viral DNA load were tabulated. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS V.9.4 on Unix.

Results

Patients

Patient demographics and baseline disease characteristics are reported in table 1. As of data cut-off (August 23, 2023), in Part 1 dose escalation, 17 patients in Group A (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) and 10 patients in Group B (visceral lesions) received study treatment. In the Part 2 RP2D expansion Stage 1, a total of 19 patients received study treatment in Group A cohorts (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions): 12 in monotherapy RP2D (dermatologic, n=5; genitourinary/gynecologic, n=2; gastrointestinal, n=3; other, n=2), 5 in monotherapy induction, and 2 in combination safety. A total of 20 patients received study treatment in Group B cohorts (visceral lesions): 9 in monotherapy RP2D and 11 in combination safety. Disease progression was the primary reason for study discontinuation for both dose escalation and dose expansion cohorts (figure 2).

The median age as well as the number of patients receiving prior antitumor medication was generally similar across the cohorts under dose escalation and dose expansion.

RP2D

RP2D of ASP9801 was 5×108 pfu/mL both as monotherapy and in combination with pembrolizumab 400 mg Q6W; this was determined separately for Groups A and B by the sponsor based on the available data on safety, pharmacodynamics, and efficacy of ASP9801. MTD was not reached, and RP2D did not exceed MTD.

Safety

Safety was evaluated in the safety analysis set, and DLTs were evaluated in the DLT evaluation analysis set, unless otherwise specified.

DLTs

One patient with cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions in dose escalation (ASP9801 5×108 pfu/mL) reported a DLT of grade 3 cytokine release syndrome (CRS) that was considered an SAE leading to interruption of ASP9801 and requiring hospitalization or prolonged hospitalization; the event resolved 8 days after onset. A patient with visceral lesions in the dose expansion combination safety cohort reported DLTs of grade 2 mental status change and pyrexia and grade 3 hypoxia, lymphopenia, and pneumonitis. The MTD was not reached.

AEs

Safety data reporting TRAEs are shown in table 2. irAEs and SAEs are reported in the supplemental section. All patients reported ≥1 TEAE, with pyrexia being the most common TRAE (occurred in ∼50% of patients) regardless of lesion location or study part (dose escalation vs dose expansion). Other common TRAEs in the dose escalation cohort were chills, nausea, vomiting, and fatigue. In the dose escalation cohorts, 12 patients with cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions and 2 patients with visceral lesions died. Two deaths in the former group were due to TEAEs; one patient in the 1×107 pfu/mL cohort died from malignant neoplasm progression and one patient in the 1×108 pfu/mL cohort died from sepsis. Neither death was attributed to the study intervention. Eight patients died due to disease progression, one died in hospice, and one died due to uncontrolled pain. Among the two deaths in patients with visceral lesions, one was due to metastatic disease and one was due to death with disease; none were due to TEAEs.

During dose expansion, the most common TRAEs were pyrexia, chills, nausea, fatigue, and vomiting. In the monotherapy expansion cohorts, three patients with cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions died; two were due to AEs of malignant neoplasm progression and one was due to disease progression. Two patients with visceral lesions also died; one due to AE of malignant neoplasm progression and one due to disease progression. In the combination safety cohort, three patients with visceral lesions died; causes of death were unknown for two patients while one died due to primary cancer diagnosis. No deaths in the expansion phase were attributed to study intervention.

Efficacy

Efficacy data are shown in table 3 for the overall patient population and for injected versus non-injected tumors per RECIST and imRECIST. One patient with visceral lesions in the combination safety cohort (ASP9801 5×108 pfu/mL plus pembrolizumab) achieved a confirmed PR (ORR of 9.1% (95% CI 0.3% to 52.7%)) and imPR (9.1% (95% CI 0.4% to 64.1%)). This was the same patient who showed a response based on non-injected tumors across cohorts.

During dose escalation, for injected lesions, one patient with cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions achieved confirmed PR using RECIST V.1.1 (ORR of 5.9% (95% CI 0.2% to 41.3%)), and the same patient had an imBOR of confirmed imPR (imORR of 5.9% (95% CI 0.2% to 41.3%)).

Similarly, one patient with visceral lesions (5×108 pfu/mL cohort) achieved BOR of confirmed PR (ORR of 10.0% (95% CI 0.3% to 52.7%)), and the same patient had imBOR of confirmed imPR (imORR of 10.0% (95% CI 0.3% to 52.7%)).

Confirmed responses in patients with specific tumor types under Part 2 of the RP2D expansion cohort are shown in online supplemental table 1. Overall, no patients in this cohort achieved confirmed CR or PR. One patient with dermatologic cancer and two patients with genitourinary/gynecologic cancer experienced SD, irrespective of lesion type per RECIST V.1.1, while two patients with dermatologic cancer and one patient with genitourinary/gynecologic cancer experienced imSD, irrespective of lesion type per imRECIST V.1.1.

The study was terminated early at the dose expansion phase for lack of efficacy. Efficacy of ASP9801 against non-injected tumors is provided in table 3.

Pharmacokinetic data

In Part 1 dose escalation, following intratumoral administration of ASP9801 (1×107 and 1×108 pfu/mL), levels of viral DNA in blood were mostly below the lower limit of quantification (LLOQ, <20 DNA copies/µg). At the dose of 5×108 pfu/mL, viral DNA reached Cmax rapidly after intratumoral injection (median tmax at approximately 1 hour), and then was eliminated rapidly below LLOQ by 24 hours postdose. Cmax and AUClast values were not calculated for some patients because of missing evaluable data or high intrapatient variability. In Part 2 dose expansion, a similar trend was observed at RP2D (5×108 pfu/mL), in which the viral DNA load in the blood rapidly peaked after intratumoral injection followed by rapid elimination among monotherapy RP2D, monotherapy induction, and combination safety cohorts.

Pharmacodynamic data

Increased serum IL-12 levels were observed in most patients while only a minor increase in IL-7 was detected, likely due to poor stability of IL-7 protein in human blood (figure 3A). Increased serum levels of inflammatory cytokines (ie, IFN-γ, CXCL10, IL-6, and MCP-1) were also observed in patients following ASP9801 injection (figure 3B); response was stronger after the second injection of ASP9801 potentially due to the enhanced inflammatory reaction mediated by antidrug antibodies. However, the elevation of IL-6 was relatively mild and did not correlate with the occurrence of CRS in patients. An increase in the frequency of activated/proliferating T cells in blood was also indicated by expression of the Ki67 marker via flow cytometry (figure 3C). The maximal increase in blood cell biomarkers was observed on day 8 post treatment, whereas the maximal peak for soluble cytokines occurred within 24 hours and mostly resolved to baseline level at 24 hours (cycle 1 day 2) except for IL-12 levels. Analysis of on-treatment tumor biopsy samples demonstrated a significant increase in CD8+ T cells and CD3+ T cells in the injected tumor lesions in most patients. A similar but milder response was also observed in the untreated tumor (figure 3D), suggesting a weak systemic antitumor immune response despite significant local reactions in injected lesions. Additionally, the increased PD-L1 expression observed in several patients was much stronger in patients with cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions than patients with visceral lesions. Other IHC assays of tumor biopsy samples, focusing on myeloid cells (CD68), did not reveal any significant changes (data not shown).

PD-L1 evaluation

PD-L1 expression was evaluated in both the dose escalation and RP2D expansion phases in patients treated with ASP9801 at a dose of 5×108 pfu/mL. Tumor biopsy samples were collected at baseline and on cycle 2 day 1. Percentages of PD-L1-positive cells were assessed using the SP142 assay. A summary of the observed changes in PD-L1 expression is presented in figure 4.

Data from the dose escalation and expansion cohorts were combined and analyzed for Group A (cutaneous/subcutaneous lesions) and Group B (visceral lesions). Paired tumor samples were available for 11 treated and 10 untreated lesions in Group A, and for 9 treated and 8 untreated lesions in Group B.

In Group A, treated lesions showed a significant increase in PD-L1 expression in two patients, with levels rising to 79% and 39%. In these patients, baseline PD-L1 expression was low, approximately 6% and 3%. However, no similar increases in PD-L1 expression were observed in untreated lesions from the same patients, indicating a lack of an abscopal effect or systemic antitumor immune response. Conversely, significant elevation in PD-L1 expression in untreated lesions was observed in two other patients: one was PD-L1-negative at baseline based on an untreated lesion biopsy; however, PD-L1 evaluation of the treated lesion was not possible due to poor biopsy quality. The second patient exhibited mild PD-L1 positivity in both untreated and treated lesions.

Group B demonstrated generally lower levels of PD-L1 expression compared with Group A and was predominantly PD-L1-negative at baseline. Significant increases in PD-L1 expression were observed in treated lesions for two patients, with approximately 15% positivity. In untreated lesions, only one patient exhibited a mild increase in PD-L1 expression (from 0% to approximately 2%). In the same patient, the treated lesion showed mostly negative expression (<1%).

Paired biopsy samples were available from three patients treated in the combination cohort. Increased PD-L1 expression was observed in two patients who were PD-L1-negative at baseline (data not shown). However, untreated lesion biopsy samples were not collected for these patients, precluding conclusions about whether the observed increases were localized or systemic.

Discussion

OV therapy is an emerging therapeutic option for cancer treatment due to the potential for influencing the tumor microenvironment and producing enhanced effects when combined with other anticancer therapies. Several OVs are under development for the treatment of cancer. To date, talimogene laherparepvac, an engineered herpes simplex virus encoding human granulocyte macrophage colony-stimulating factor, is the only US Food and Drug Administration-approved OV therapy for metastatic melanoma.

In preclinical studies, ASP9801-surrogate (expressing murine IL-12 instead of human IL-12) demonstrated antitumor activity against human cancer cells in immunocompetent mouse models (BALB/c mice, C57BL/6 mice, and nude mouse models injected with B16-F10, CT26.WT, LLC, and TRAMP-C2 cell lines) by increased tumor infiltration of CD8+ and CD4+ T cells. An abscopal effect of ASP9801-surrogate was observed, with tumor regression in both injected and contralateral non-injected tumors. Based on the activity observed in vivo, this first-in-human study examined the efficacy and safety of ASP9801 as monotherapy and in combination with pembrolizumab in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors. Overall, ASP9801 was generally well tolerated up to 5×108 pfu/mL when administered as monotherapy or in combination with pembrolizumab. Disease progression was the most frequent reason for discontinuing the study intervention, regardless of lesion type (cutaneous/subcutaneous vs visceral).

No patients in the ASP9801 monotherapy cohorts achieved an objective response, although a few patients across cohorts demonstrated SD with tumor shrinkage, or only injected lesions achieved a confirmed response. In the combination cohort, one patient achieved objective response and maintained the response for more than 60 weeks (data not shown). This patient was diagnosed with small bowel adenocarcinoma with metastases, including four target lesions across two target organs, specifically in the left lower and right middle lung lobes. From cycle 2 and onward, the patient demonstrated good tolerability and remained on treatment, maintaining PR and imPR. The duration of response in patients treated with the combination regimen was prolonged compared with ASP9801 monotherapy. Among patients who demonstrated at least SD and/or tumor shrinkage, the most predominant tumor type was breast cancer (3 out of 10, data not shown); remarkably, the injected lesion in one of these patients showed a best regression of more than half in diameter. While a slight increase in CD3 was observed in tumor biopsy samples obtained after treatment from this patient included in the dose escalation cohort, the limited number of patients with various cancers in this cohort did not allow us to definitively identify any markers associated with the response. Molecular biological analysis was not performed for the patients due to premature study termination.

Additionally, the abscopal effect was observed in 1 patient receiving monotherapy with a dosing frequency of every 2 weeks, where injection of ASP9801 led to tumor shrinkage in both injected and contralateral non-injected tumors and 2 (out of 10) patients with intestinal cancer (1 each for colon cancer and small bowel adenocarcinoma; 1 of them had previously undergone up to 10 lines of treatment). Although patients with other tumor types achieved either SD or tumor shrinkage, the limited efficacy data on each tumor type may hinder accurate clinical decisions and the most appropriate selection of target patients.

OVs in combination with other anticancer therapies such as PD-L1 inhibitors or anti-CTLA-4 may represent a better therapeutic approach for maximizing efficacy. In a Phase 2 study of PD-1-refractory melanoma, OV plus pembrolizumab demonstrated measurable antitumor activity. In a syngeneic mouse model, ASP9801 plus oxaliplatin improved the abscopal effect of ASP9801 through reorganization of intratumoral macrophages. ASP1012, an OV encoding leptin–IL-2 fusion protein, is currently being tested as monotherapy and plus pembrolizumab in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors in a Phase 1 trial (NCT06171178). ASP1012 demonstrated cytotoxicity against human cancer cells in vitro and antitumor activity in vivo. ASP1012 is administered intravenously, enabling widespread OV exposure to all lesions and reducing the need for complex procedures. Thus, intravenous delivery of OVs offers an advantage over intratumoral delivery and is worth exploring further.

Viral DNA detected in the blood after intratumoral administration of ASP9801 at the highest dose level (5×108 pfu/mL) was rapidly eliminated to undetectable levels within 24 hours postdose. Overall, the biomarker data confirm the immune response to ASP9801 injection, as evidenced by both peripheral and tumor biopsy biomarkers. Blood cell biomarkers peaked on day 8 post treatment, while soluble cytokines peaked within 24 hours and returned to baseline soon after, except for IL-12 levels. The changes in IL-12 level indicated a more complex pattern, likely due to IL-12 being produced both by inflammatory cells in response to the virus injection and by cells transfected by ASP9801, in which IL-12 is a transgene. Tumor biopsy samples showed an increase in CD8+ and CD3+ T cells in injected lesions in most patients, suggesting possible favorable immunomodulation; however, this could be due to simply reactive lymphocytes responding to virus injection/replication as well since no further assays were conducted to differentiate the virus-reactive CD3+ T/CD8+ T cells versus antitumor CTLs. No significant correlations were observed between peripheral or tumor biopsy biomarker changes and clinical benefits; however, the limited number of patients who responded precluded a meaningful evaluation. Despite occasional elevations in PD-L1 levels in individual patients, no obvious trends were observed between cohorts. Additionally, the significant intertumor variability and heterogeneity combined with the small number of posttreatment biopsy samples limited the effectiveness of PD-L1 analysis in this study. However, the study, as well as the development program for ASP9801, was terminated for lack of efficacy during RP2D expansion. Limited data from the PD-L1 22C3 assay were available due to the early trial termination, preventing more comprehensive PD-L1 analysis. While the results of the SP142 PD-L1 assay should be interpreted with caution for pembrolizumab-specific outcomes, it is scientifically valid and aids in elucidating the changes in tumor microenvironment in the absence of data from the 22C3 assay.

Conclusions

In summary, ASP9801 has a mechanism of action that is largely distinct from other therapeutic modalities with an acceptable safety profile. While OVs hold potential as anticancer therapies, identifying new delivery methods and predictive biomarkers to better understand OVs’ impact on the tumor microenvironment as well as optimal patient selection is critical to maximize their effectiveness.
