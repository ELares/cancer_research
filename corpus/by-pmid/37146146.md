# A rationally designed ICAM1 antibody drug conjugate eradicates late-stage and refractory triple-negative breast tumors in vivo.

## Abstract

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) remains the most lethal form of breast cancer, and effective targeted therapeutics are in urgent need to improve the poor prognosis of TNBC patients. Here, we report the development of a rationally designed antibody drug conjugate (ADC) for the treatment of late-stage and refractory TNBC. We determined that intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM1), a cell surface receptor overexpressed in TNBC, efficiently facilitates receptor-mediated antibody internalization. We next constructed a panel of four ICAM1 ADCs using different chemical linkers and warheads and compared their in vitro and in vivo efficacies against multiple human TNBC cell lines and a series of standard, late-stage, and refractory TNBC in vivo models. An ICAM1 antibody conjugated with monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) via a protease-cleavable valine-citrulline linker was identified as the optimal ADC formulation owing to its outstanding efficacy and safety, representing an effective ADC candidate for TNBC therapy.

INTRODUCTION

Late-stage and refractory triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) represents a central challenge to the improvement of clinical outcomes of breast cancer patients. In 2021, more than 28,000 patients are estimated to be diagnosed with TNBC in the United States, representing 10 to 15% of breast cancer incidence. TNBC is more prevalent in non-Hispanic Black women, young women under the age of 40, and women carrying breast cancer type 1 or 2 (BRCA1/2) gene mutations. Moreover, patients with late-stage or refractory TNBC tumors respond poorly to first-line chemotherapy and, unfortunately, do not respond to established hormone and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)–targeted therapeutics due to the lack of expression of these molecular targets, thereby markedly exacerbating their poor clinical outcomes. As a result, the current 5-year survival rate of patients with TNBC is less than 77%, thereby substantially lower than the over 90% of patients with other types of breast cancers.

While few targeted therapeutics including poly(adenosine diphosphate–ribose) polymerase inhibitors (e.g., olaparib and talazoparib) and immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., atezolizumab and pembrolizumab) have been approved for TNBC, their clinical benefits are limited to small subsets (~20 to 30%) of patients TNBC due to limited BRCA1/2 mutation rates or programmed death-ligand 1 expression. Developing novel TNBC-targeted therapeutics with different mechanisms of action that can work in synergy with these approved modalities are likely to further increase clinical efficacy. Antibody drug conjugate (ADCs) are an emerging class of immuno-chemotherapeutics featuring the structure of a monoclonal antibody conjugated to cytotoxic agents (warheads) via chemical linkers. The monoclonal antibody of an ADC functions as a tumor-homing ligand to guide its warhead to selectively ablate antigen-overexpressing tumors while sparing normal tissues. This approach has demonstrated promising clinical efficacy in various cancers. For instance, sacituzumab govitecan, a trophoblast antigen 2 (TROP2)–targeted ADC, was approved for treating refractory TNBC patients with a ~33% response rate and a median progression-free survival of 5.5 months. Given the poor prognosis and unimpressive efficacy of current TNBC treatment modalities, there remains an urgent and unmet need to identify previously unknown druggable targets and to develop more effective ADCs for TNBC therapy.

Intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM1) is a cell surface glycoprotein that regulates intercellular adhesion during inflammatory injury, viral infection, and tumorigenesis. We have previously identified ICAM1 as a TNBC cell surface marker through an unbiased and quantitative screening of G protein–coupled receptor signaling proteins. ICAM1 protein was found to be highly enriched on the cancer cell surface of human TNBC tumors relative to normal mammary tissues. The nuclear factor κB pathway has likewise been implicated in aberrantly up-regulating ICAM1 expression in cancer cells by binding to the ICAM1 promoter and causing its hyperactive transcription. ICAM1 neutralizing antibody has been investigated as a potential cancer therapeutic; however, blocking ICAM1 signaling cascades alone has yet yielded satisfying clinical efficacy. Thus, the goal of this study was to leverage the outstanding TNBC-targeting activity of ICAM1 antibody with the potent antitumor activity of ADC warheads to develop ICAM1 ADCs. We hypothesized that a rationally designed ICAM1 ADC would precisely target and eradicate TNBC tumors while sparing healthy organs and tissues in vivo. To test this hypothesis, we constructed a panel of four ICAM1 ADCs with different chemical linkers and warheads and evaluated their in vitro efficacy against multiple TNBC cells and their in vivo efficacy in a series of standard, late-stage, and refractory TNBC models.

RESULTS

ICAM1 is overexpressed in human TNBC tumors

We previously measured ICAM1 protein expression in a cohort of 149 cases of human breast tumor tissues along with 144 human normal tissues including breast and 19 other organs using immunohistochemistry. We found that ICAM1 staining is present at a significantly stronger level in the cancer cells of TNBC tumors versus other breast cancer subtypes and is completely absent in normal mammary tissues. In this study, we used genomic analyses to interrogate the relationship of ICAM1 expression with more detailed TNBC characteristics including its molecular subtype, tumor differentiation, and oncogene mutation status by querying the R2: Genomics Analysis and Visualization Platform (https://hgserver1.amc.nl). We first quantitatively compared the ICAM1 mRNA expression in three breast cancer subtypes: estrogen receptor (ER) positive, HER2 positive, and TNBC (Fig. 1A). Consistent with pathological staining results, ICAM1 mRNA levels in TNBC tumor tissues (n = 116) are significantly higher than those of HER2-positive (n = 40) and ER-positive (n = 808) breast tumors. Because TNBC has recently been identified as a heterogeneous disease classified into four distinct molecular subtypes: basal-like immunosuppressed (BLIS), basal-like immunoactivated (BLIA), luminal androgen receptor (L-AR), and mesenchymal (MES), we evaluated ICAM1 mRNA levels in these molecular subtypes, and BLIA (n = 54) showed the highest ICAM1 expression, followed by MES (n = 47), L-AR (n = 60), and BLIS (n = 37). We also analyzed the correlation between ICAM1 expression and TNBC tumor differentiation and found that ICAM1 expression is significantly higher within poorly differentiated TNBC tumors (grade 3, n = 108) versus moderately differentiated tumors (grade 2, n = 53). It is known that poorly differentiated TNBC tumors are strongly associated with worse prognoses; thus, ICAM1 may serve as a potential biomarker for predicting clinical outcomes of patients with TNBC as well. We further interrogated the relationship between ICAM1 expression and cancer mutation status and found that ICAM1 levels positively correlate with BRCA1/2 or TP53 mutations, occurring in 20 and 80% TNBC tumors, respectively.

Given that TROP2 is a clinically approved ADC target for TNBC therapy, we selected it as a positive control to evaluate the potential of ICAM1 for ADC development. In Fig. 1B, we compared the cell surface expression of ICAM1 and TROP2 in three established human TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-157, and MDA-MB-231) along with normal human mammary epithelial MCF10A cells using flow cytometry. The ICAM1 expression levels of three human TNBC cells were determined as being 1,047,000; 530,000; and 194,000 molecules per cell for MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-568, and MDA-MB-231 cells, respectively, which are approximately 4- to 23-fold higher than that of MCF10A cells (46,000 molecules per cell). These receptor densities are considered to be suitable with respect to ADC targets based on established criteria. In comparison, the cell surface expression of ICAM1 is 11- to 35-fold higher than TROP2 on two TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-436 and MDA-MB-468) and at an equivalent level on the third one (MDA-MB-231). We have noted that our determined TROP2 expression level on MDA-MB-231 cells is relatively higher than a previous report, which is possibly caused by the use of different types and clones of TROP2 antibodies. ICAM1 surface expression on normal MCF10A cells is sevenfold lower than TROP2. These findings were confirmed by immunofluorescent (IF) staining of ICAM1 and TROP2 in the same cells (Fig. 1C). The subcellular location of ICAM1 is predominantly on the plasma membranes of TNBC cells and is absent on normal MCF10A cells. These results provide evidence that ICAM1 is more overexpressed and tumor specific than TROP2 in human TNBC cells, potentially contributing to a higher tumor specificity and fewer on-target/off-tumor adverse effects during in vivo drug delivery as reported below.

Cellular internalization is another important factor affecting ADC efficacy. To determine whether ICAM1 ADCs can selectively enter TNBC cells and rapidly transit into cell endosomes for warhead release, we quantified the cellular internalization rates of ICAM1 antibody in multiple TNBC cell lines along with normal MCF10A cells using imaging flow cytometry. Confocal fluorescent images revealed that ICAM1 antibodies were rapidly internalized by all three TNBC cell lines studied (MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-157 and MDA-MB-231) after a 1-hour incubation (Fig. 1D). The internalized ICAM1 antibodies are aggregated, rather than dispersed in the cytoplasm of TNBC cells, indicating that they were trafficked into endosomes/lysosomes by receptor-mediated endocytosis, where ADCs are degraded to release their warheads. In contrast, no internalization was observed in normal MCF10A cells due to their lack of ICAM1 antigen expression. By quantifying these data, cellular internalization rates of ICAM1 antibody in three TNBC cells are approximately 42- to 109-fold higher than those in MCF10A cells and approximately 21- to 129-fold higher than those of nontargeting immunoglobulin G (IgG) (Fig. 1E). These results validated that ICAM1 is highly overexpressed in TNBC cells and that it facilitates efficient cellular internalization. Furthermore, we investigated cell surface antigen down-regulation after ICAM1 antibody internalization. As shown in fig. S1, unbound ICAM1 antigens on MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-157 cell surfaces were reduced by approximately 40 to 50% after incubation with ICAM1 antibodies. Along with ICAM1 antibody internalization, we did not observe an ICAM1 antigen replenishment for up to 4 hours, suggesting that this cell surface antigen down-regulation persists during ICAM1 ADC treatment, which may impact continuous uptake rates of ICAM1 ADCs.

Design, prepare, and characterize ICAM1 ADCs

To identify the optimal ADC formulation for TNBC, we designed and constructed a panel of ICAM1 ADCs by conjugating a monoclonal human/mouse chimeric ICAM1 antibody (clone #R6.5) with four different ADC linker–warhead combinations including MC-Vc-Pab-MMAE (monomethyl auristatin E), MC-MMAF (monomethyl auristatin F), Mal-EBE-Mal-DM1, and Mal-EBE-Mal-DM4, respectively (Fig. 2, A and B). The constructed ICAM1 ADCs were named on the basis of their antibodies and warheads: ICAM1 antibody–conjugated MC-Vc-Pab-MMAE (IC1-MMAE), ICAM1 antibody–conjugated MC-MMAF (IC1-MMAF), ICAM1 antibody–conjugated Mal-EBE-Mal-DM1 (IC1-DM1), and ICAM1 antibody–conjugated Mal-EBE-Mal-DM4 (IC1-DM4). This ICAM1 antibody selection is based on the excellent human safety profile of human/mouse chimeric ICAM1 antibody (clone #R6.5), which has been previously determined in phase I/II clinical trials. We next focused on ADC linker–warhead selection. MC-VC-Pab-MMAE features an enzyme-cleavable linker that can be selectively cleaved by cathepsin B proteases in cell endosomes/lysosomes, resulting in a rapid ADC warhead release upon cellular internalization while maintaining ADC integrity during blood circulation. The other maleimidocaproyl (MC) and Mal-EBE-Mal linkers are noncleavable; thus, ADC warhead release depends on lysosomal degradation of the adjoining antibody. Both enzyme-cleavable and noncleavable linkers are now used in clinically approved ADCs for treating different cancers. All four warheads used in our ICAM1 ADCs are potent microtubule inhibitors whose cytotoxicities are approximately 100- to 1000-fold higher than standard-of-care chemodrugs (e.g., paclitaxel or gemcitabine). MMAE, MMAF, DM1, and DM4 are clinically used ADC warheads. However, it remains unclear which ADC linker–warhead combination is more effective for TNBC therapy in the absence of an unbiased and quantitative screening. Moreover, the drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR), defined as the number of warheads conjugated per antibody, is another important factor affecting ADC efficacy and human safety. The DAR value of 4.0 was well established for microtubule inhibitor ADC warheads in multiple clinically approved ADCs (e.g., trastuzumab emtansine and brentuximab vedotin) demonstrating excellent clinical efficacy and human safety profiles. Thus, we fine-tuned the DAR of four constructed ICAM1 ADCs to an equivalent value of 4 by controlling antibody/warhead input ratios during the ADC conjugation. The resulting DAR values of four ICAM1 ADCs were determined by hydrophobic interaction chromatography (HIC) as being 3.96 for IC1-DM1, 3.83 for IC1-DM4, 3.92 for IC1-MMAE, and 4.09 for IC1-MMAF, respectively (fig. S2).

We next quantitatively screened the in vitro cytotoxicity of the four ICAM1 ADCs against a panel of TNBC cell lines, and their half maximum inhibitory concentrations (IC50s) were quantified using the Dojindo assay. Among the four ICAM1 ADCs, exposure to IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF elicited a significantly higher in vitro efficacy relative to IC1-DM1 and IC-DM4 in three of four tested TNBC cell lines. Quantified IC50s of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF are 13.1 and 7.3 pM for MDA-MB-436, 250.0 and 68.7 pM for MDA-MB-468, 221.7 and 116.0 pM for MDA-MB-157, respectively (Fig. 2C). ICAM1 antibody alone was not cytotoxic to either human TNBC cells or non-neoplastic control cells (fig. S3). It is noteworthy that MDA-MB-436 and MDA-MB-157 cells represent both Caucasian and African American TNBC cell lines, respectively. In contrast, the IC50s for IC1-DM1 and IC1-DM4 are at least fivefold higher in these TNBC cells. Notably, the IC50s of IC-MMAE and IC-MMAF are approximately 1000-fold lower than doxorubicin, a standard-of-care chemodrug used in TNBC treatment. We observed that MDA-MB-231 cells exhibited strong drug resistance to all four ICAM1 ADCs. Because of the fact that MDA-MB-231 cells were also reported to be resistant to sacituzumab govitecan, the clinically used TROP2 ADC for TNBC, we selected this TNBC cell line to construct an ADC-resistant TNBC model in the following animal study. Our ICAM1 ADCs’ cytotoxicity was also evaluated in non-neoplastic MCF10A and human embryonic kidney (HEK) 293 cells, both of which lack ICAM1 antigen expression. As expected, no cytotoxicity was observed for all tested ICAM1 ADCs in ICAM1-negative MCF10A and HEK293 cells until a very high ADC concentration of more than 6.7 nM was reached, thereby indicating that the potency of ICAM1 ADC in ablating TNBC cells is ICAM1 dependent. Collectively, these in vitro efficacy screening data support the conclusion that IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF are more potent ADC formulations than IC1-DM1 and IC1-DM4 for TNBC, warranting further investigation in TNBC in vivo models.

ICAM1 antibody and ADC selectively target TNBC tumors in vivo

We evaluated the tumor specificity and biodistribution of our ICAM1 ADC using both an immunocompromised nude mouse model with human TNBC tumors (MDA-MB-436) and an immunocompetent BALB/c mouse model with murine TNBC tumors (4T1) to recapitulate different immune system backgrounds. First, the nude mouse model was used to determine the tumor specificity of ICAM1 antibody and ADC in orthotopic human TNBC tumors (Fig. 3A). We intravenously administered near-infrared fluorescent dye Cy5.5-labeled anti-human ICAM1 antibody (IC1-Cy5.5) and its constructed IC1-MMAE (IC1-MMAE-Cy5.5) and compared these to nontargeting IgG conjugated with Cy5.5 (IgG-Cy5.5) at an equivalent dosage of 5 mg/kg. At 24 hours after injection, nude mice were imaged by in vivo near-infrared (NIR) fluorescent imaging. As shown in Fig. 3 (B and D), nude mice treated with IC1-Cy5.5 and IC1-MMAE-Cy5.5 showed comparable intratumoral accumulation, approximately fivefold higher than IgG-Cy5.5. These in vivo tumor accumulation data precisely matched the ex vivo NIR images of excised tumors (Fig. 3C). These results suggest that IC1-Cy5.5 and IC1-MMAE-Cy5.5 selectively recognize and bind human TNBC tumors in vivo relative to nontargeting IgG-Cy5.5. We further examined the biodistribution of IC1-Cy5.5, IC1-MMAE-Cy5.5, and IgG-Cy5.5 in six major organs, namely, the brain, lung, heart, liver, spleen, and kidney. As shown in Fig. 3 (E and F), the liver and kidney were the major off-tumor accumulation sites of IC1-Cy5 and IC1-MMAE in these tumor-bearing nude mice. These are common off-tumor accumulation sites, and here, we determined that any toxicities were well tolerated using their serum biomarkers in the following animal studies.

A potential concern in using ICAM1 as a drug target for developing TNBC-targeted ADCs is that we have considered its on-target/off-tumor toxicities in normal organs and tissues expressing ICAM1. In the nude mouse model, anti-human ICAM1 antibodies cannot recognize mouse ICAM1 antigens expressed in normal mouse organs due to the lack of species cross-reactivity. To solve this issue, we re-evaluated normal organ biodistribution by using anti-mouse ICAM1 antibodies in an immunocompetent BALB/c mouse model with murine 4T1 tumors (Fig. 4A). Notably, this biodistribution study was conducted in immunocompetent BALB/c mice with their homologous 4T1 tumors, featuring a complete immune system that can more faithfully recapitulate the interactions between the TNBC tumor microenvironment and ICAM1 antibodies in comparison with the immunocompromised nude mouse model. As shown in Fig. 4B, anti-mouse IC1-Cy5.5 maintains potent 4T1 tumor specificity in BALB/c mice, in excellent correlation with anti-human IC1-Cy5.5 in nude mice. The biodistribution of anti-mouse IC1-Cy5.5 was examined in six normal organs relative to IgG-Cy5.5 (Fig. 4C). No preferable accumulation of anti-mouse IC1-Cy5.5 was observed in the brain, lung, heart, liver, and kidney save for the spleen. Moreover, we also investigated the interaction between the peripheral immune system with ICAM1 antibodies by comparing amount of IC1-Cy5.5 and IgG-Cy5.5 taken up by circulating leukocytes in tumor-bearing BALB/c mice. As shown in Fig. 4D, IC1-Cy5.5 showed the same level of circulating leukocyte uptake as IgG-Cy5.5, suggesting that ICAM1 antibodies do not target normal leukocytes in blood circulation.

ICAM1 ADCs eradicate TNBC tumors in vivo

To evaluate the in vivo efficacy of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF, the two potent ICAM1 ADCs derived from our in vitro screening, we established a series of orthotopic TNBC models in standard, late-stage, and refractory tumor settings. We first determined the in vivo efficacy of IC1-MMAE or IC1-MMAF in the standard setting of an orthotopic TNBC model (MDA-MB-436) by initiating systemic IC1-MMAE or IC1-MMAF treatment at the dose of 5 mg/kg once the tumor volume reached 100 mm3 (Fig. 5A). Separate cohorts of tumor-bearing mice were treated with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (sham) or ICAM1 antibody alone (IC1 Ab) or doxorubicin (free Dox) as controls at an equivalent dosage. Both IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF elicited significant and durable tumor regression in every animal tested throughout the duration of the experiment (Fig. 5, B to D). The antitumor activities of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF were determined by reducing tumor weights and found to be 99.5 and 99.0%, respectively, in comparison with the PBS group (Fig. 5D). IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF successfully eradicated MDA-MB-436 tumors in 70% (7 of 10) and 50% (4 of 8) animals without tumor recurrence over a period of 24 days. In comparison, the free Dox group yielded very limited efficacy against MDA-MB-436 tumors, and the IC1 monoclonal antibodies demonstrated a moderate antitumor efficacy as previously reported by others, which was significantly less effective than IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF treatment. Because ICAM1 antibody itself has no effect on TNBC cell proliferation in vitro (fig. S3), we speculated that the TNBC tumor reduction mediated by the ICAM1 antibody is most likely attributed to its antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity by attracting natural killer cells to target cells at tumor sites. Similar results have also been observed with trastuzumab, a clinically approved HER2 antibody, which has been reported to exert a strong antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity against human xenograft tumors in nude mice.

To demonstrate the potential of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF in combating even more aggressive TNBC tumors, we evaluated the in vivo efficacy of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF in the late-stage setting of the same orthotopic TNBC model (MDA-MB-436). In the late-stage setting, MDA-MB-436 tumors were allowed to grow to 500 mm3 before systemic administration of IC1-MMAE or IC1-MMAF treatments at the same dosage of 5 mg/kg (Fig. 5A). IC1-MMAE significantly inhibited late-stage MDA-MB-436 tumor growth by 93.5% in all cases in comparison with the PBS control group (Fig. 5, F and G). While IC1-MMAF achieved 47.8% inhibitory activity in tumor growth, significantly less effective than IC1-MMAE. Moreover, IC1-MMAE also eradicated late-stage MDA-MB-436 tumors in 20% (one of five) animals compared with no tumor eradication for IC1-MMAF. In both standard and late-stage settings, no weight loss was observed by both IC1-MMAF and IC1-MMAE treatments (Fig. 5, E and H), suggesting that these two ICAM1 ADCs are well tolerated in TNBC tumor-bearing nude mice at a dosage of 5 mg/kg.

We next investigated the in vivo efficacy of IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF in treating refractory (ADC-resistant) TNBC tumors. In this study, refractory TNBC tumors were established by using ADC-resistant MDA-MB-231 cells previously established in the in vitro ADC efficacy test, and IC1-MMAE or IC1-MMAF was systemically administered at the same dosage of 5 mg/kg (Fig. 6A). As shown in Fig. 6 (B to D), both IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF potently attenuated refractory MDA-MB-231 tumor growth by 96.7 and 84.3%, respectively, in comparison with the PBS control group. IC1-MMAE also eradicated refractory tumors in 50% (three of six) animals with a lack of effect by IC1-MMAF. Similarly, no body weight loss was observed for IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF treatment at the dose of 5 mg/kg in this refractory TNBC model (Fig. 6E). These data demonstrate that IC1-MMAE and IC1-MMAF are both effective in treating orthotopic TNBC tumors in the standard setting of targeted therapy but that IC1-MMAE is significantly more effective than IC1-MMAF in treating late-stage and refractory TNBC tumors. We postulate that the higher efficacy of IC1-MMAE is likely due to its protease-cleavable MC-Vc-Pab linker that mediates a faster bystander killing effect than IC1-MMAF with noncleavable linkers. On the basis of these in vivo efficacy results, we selected IC1-MMAE as the optimized ICAM1 ADC formation for TNBC therapy.

To optimize the DAR of IC1-MMAE, we constructed two IC1-MMAE at DARs of 2 and 4 and compared their in vivo efficacies in an orthotopic TNBC model (MDA-MB-468). These tumor-bearing animals received intravenous administration of IC1-MMAE at DAR4 (2.4 mg/kg), DAR2 (2.4 mg/kg), or DAR2 (4.8 mg/kg). As shown in fig. S4A, all DAR4 and DAR2 groups elicit potent and durable tumor regression and the DAR4 (2.4 mg/kg) group exhibited the highest tumor inhibition at 94% in comparison with 69% of DAR2 (4.8 mg/kg) and 73% of DAR2 (2.4 mg/kg), but their differences are not significant. In addition, none of the DAR4 and DAR2 treatments affected body weights (fig. S4B). These results suggested that both DAR4 and DAR2 are safe and effective in treating TNBC tumors; DAR4 was selected for use in our study due to its higher tumor inhibition rate.

Next, to determine the optimal dosage of IC1-MMAE for TNBC therapy, we re-evaluated the in vivo efficacy of IC1-MMAE in a dosage-dependent manner. In the standard setting of the orthotopic TNBC model (MDA-MB-436), three ascending dosages of IC1-MMAE (1, 5, or 10 mg/kg) were systemically administered along with the PBS control (Fig. 6F). As shown in Fig. 6 (G to I), the antitumor activities of IC1-MMAE at 1, 5, and 10 mg/kg were determined as being 50.3, 96.8, and 94.5%, respectively, compared with the PBS control group. The tumor eradication rates for IC1-MMAE at 1, 5, and 10 mg/kg were determined as being 0% (zero of five), 40% (two of five), and 60% (three of five), respectively. These results suggest that IC1-MMAE at a dose of 5 mg/kg or higher is capable of eliciting significant and durable TNBC tumor regression and eradication in vivo. Thus, 5 mg/kg was selected as the optimized dosage for IC1-MMAE treatment in preclinical animal models.

We also investigated any potential in vivo toxicity of IC1-MMAE at 1, 5, and 10 mg/kg dosages using standard blood chemistry analyses. No body weight loss was observed as a consequence of IC1-MMAE at all tested dosages (Fig. 6J). At the end of this study (day 24), we collected mouse blood samples from each group via cardiac puncture and tested these sera for the levels of aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) levels, two established serum biomarkers of liver toxicity. As shown in Fig. 6K, among all IC1-MMAE dosages, none of them, including even the highest drug dosage (10 mg/kg), induced any elevation in either AST or ALT levels relative to the PBS group. Similarly, we evaluated the renal toxicity of IC1-MMAE by measuring creatinine and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) levels, and we observed no renal toxicity among these IC1-MMAE dosage groups (Fig. 6K).

We determined the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) of IC1-MMAE in healthy BALB/c mice by intravenously administering IC1-MMAE at ascending dosages of 25, 50, or 75 mg/kg, approximately 5- to 15-fold higher than IC1-MMAE dosage (5 mg/kg) used in the in vivo efficacy studies. As shown in fig. S5, no body weight loss was observed in all IC-MMAE dosages tested, even at highest dosage of 75 mg/kg. Collectively, these in vivo toxicity studies strongly support the conclusion that IC1-MMAE is very well tolerated as a TNBC treatment in preclinical in vivo models.

DISCUSSION

Here, we report the development of a rationally designed ADC, IC1-MMAE, as a potent targeted therapeutic for treating late-stage and refractory TNBC tumors in vivo. In comparison with other TNBC-targeted therapeutics, our IC1-MMAE features the following advantages: First, ICAM1, the cancer target of IC1-MMAE, was proved to be an effective ADC target. Second, ICAM1 antibody, the tumor homing ligand of IC1-MMAE, efficiently guides ADCs to recognize and attack TNBC tumors in multiple animal models of different immune system backgrounds. Third, MC-Vc-PAB-MMAE, the chemical linker and warhead of IC1-MMAE, was specifically optimized for late-stage and refractory TNBC treatment via an unbiased in vitro and in vivo drug formulation screening. By validating its in vivo efficacy in a series of TNBC models, IC1-MMAE has been proven to be effective and well tolerated in the treatment of standard, late-stage, and refractory TNBC tumor models in vivo.

To date, our results provide the first experimental evidence of using ICAM1 as an effective ADC target for TNBC. Different from conventional drug targets, ICAM1 as an ADC target not only requires its high overexpression on TNBC cell surfaces and low normal organ expression for tumor recognition but also requires TNBC cell surface–overexpressed ICAM1 to promote cell entry of ICAM1 ADCs by using receptor-mediated endocytosis. Thus, in this study, we quantitatively determined ICAM1 overexpression levels and its cellular internalization rates in a panel of human TNBC and normal mammary cells. We found that ICAM1 is up to 125-fold more effective than nontargeting IgG in binding and entering TNBC cells without affecting normal mammary cells. We compared the TNBC specificity of ICAM1 with TROP2, the sole clinically approved ADC target for TNBC. We found that, in comparison with TROP2, ICAM1 is 11-to-35 fold more overexpressed in multiple TNBC cells and 7-fold less expressed in normal mammary epithelial cells, potentially contributing to its higher TNBC-targeted efficacy and lower on-target/off-tumor adverse effects in ADC design. One potential limitation of ICAM1 targeting is its “on-target, off-tumor” toxicity. ICAM1 expression can be induced on normal endothelial cells by inflammatory cytokines released as a result of other pathological conditions such as chronic inflammation or infection, which might cause toxicity of the ICAM1 ADC at nontumor sites. In clinical settings, these toxicities can avoided by pretreating patients with these comorbidities before administering the ICAM1 ADC to control the ICAM1 expression levels in normal tissues. Moreover, clinically approved ADCs as late-stage treatments are not used on patients in the presence of concomitant diseases including uncontrolled recurrent infections or inflammatory diseases in general.

In addition to ADC target selection, another key factor in ICAM1 ADC development is the optimization of the ADC linkers and warheads for the TNBC tumor microenvironment. In this study, we performed an unbiased ADC formulation screening by constructing a panel of ICAM1 ADCs with four different clinically validated linkers and warheads, followed by comparing their in vitro efficacy against multiple TNBC cell lines and its in vivo efficacy in standard, late-stage, and refractory settings of TNBC animal models. Among four tested ICAM1 ADCs, we identified IC1-MMAE with a protease-cleavable linker and microtubule inhibitor warhead as the optimized formulation for TNBC therapy, owing to its highest and consistent efficacy for complete and durable TNBC tumor regression and eradication.

The selection of DAR, a critical parameter affecting both ADC efficacy and safety, should take several cofounding factors into consideration. First, tumor-saturating dosages of ADCs directly affect the results of DAR comparison studies. Because ADCs with higher DARs will always outperform those with low DARs at tumor-saturating dose due to greater cytotoxic payloads, DAR comparison experiments should be conducted in subsaturating clinical doses. Second, target expression levels also affect DAR comparison experiments because a low target expression on cancer cells can reduce the tumor saturating dose of ADCs. Third, potency of payload directly affects the DAR selection via their clinical MTD. For instance, high potency payloads such as pyrrolobenzodiazepine (PBD) dimers are poorly tolerated at DARs greater than 2 in the clinic, whereas low potency payloads such as DXd and SN38 show better efficacies at DARs of 4 or 8.

Drug delivery into late-stage solid tumors, particularly large tumors, is very challenging due to their target expression heterogeneity, poor vascularization, and desmoplasia. The cleavable linker of IC1-MMAE plays a pivotal role in overcoming these biological barriers. Compared with noncleavable IC1-MMAF, IC1-MMAE features an enzyme-cleavage peptide linker MC-VC-PAB, providing it with a faster payload release rate after cellular internalization. It has been known that most ADCs bind target cells at perivascular regions of late-stage tumors at the initiation stage of ADC treatment. When IC1-MMAEs were internalized into target cells at perivascular regions, their peptide linker was selectively cleaved by cathepsin, an endosomal protease in endosomes or lysosomes, resulting in a faster release of MMAE warheads in comparison with noncleavable IC1-MMAF. The released MMAE has a remarkably reduced molecular weight in comparison with the entire ADC, allowing it to facilitate a deeper tumor penetration and the killing of antigen-negative cells in the tumor microenvironments, subsequently exerting a more effective antitumor activity than noncleavable IC1-MMAF in vivo.

We also investigated the on-target/off-tumor toxicity of IC1-MMAE in normal organs and tissues expressing ICAM1. Given that ICAM1 is commonly considered to be as an inflammatory biomarker in human blood vessels, we have previously quantified the ICAM1 surface expression levels on normal human umbilical vein endothelial cells and human microvascular endothelial cells and found it to be 8- to 81-fold lower than those of human TNBC cells. In this current study, we provided in vivo experimental evidence that there are no liver and renal toxicities observed in systemic IC1-MMAE treatment at dosages of 1 to 10 mg/kg. Nevertheless, given the species difference, these results may not accurately translate to human patients and still requires a toxicological safety evaluation in nonhuman primates.

Together, our study provides critical insights into the development and testing of a promising ADC candidate to address the current unmet need in TNBC treatment. Owing to its encouraging efficacy in attenuating both early and late-stage and refractory tumor burdens in TNBC animal models, our IC1-MMAE warrants further exploration as a promising ADC candidate for TNBC-targeted therapy.

METHODS

Phycoerythrin (PE)–conjugated anti-human ICAM1 antibody (clone: HCD54), PE-conjugated mouse IgG isotype (PE-IgG), anti-mouse ICAM1 antibody (clone #YN1/1.7.4), and red blood cell (RBC) lysis buffer were purchased from BioLegend (San Diego, CA, USA). PE-conjugated anti-human TROP-2 antibody (clone #77220) and mouse and rat IgG isotype controls were purchased from R&D Systems (Minneapolis, MN, USA). Purified anti-human CD54 Antibody (clone: R6.5), MC-VC-PAB-MMAE, MC-MMAF, Mal-EBE-MaL-DM1, and Mal-EBE-Mal-DM4 were obtained from MabPlex (Yantai, China). Lab-Tek II Chamber Slide System was obtained from Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Doxorubicin, bovine serum albumin (BSA), AST activity assay kit, ALT activity assay kit, creatinine activity assay kit, and urea activity assay kit were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). Dulbecco’s PBS, Dulbecco’s PBS, 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI), Gibco Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM), Gibco DMEM/F12(1:1), and RPMI-1640 medium were purchased from Invitrogen (Carlsbad, CA, USA). The Dojindo Cell Counting Kit CCK-8 was purchased from Dojindo Molecular Technologies (Rockville, MD, USA).

Cell culture

Four human TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-468, MDA-MB-157, and MDA-MB-231), one murine TNBC cell line (4T1), non-neoplastic human mammary MCF10A cells, and human embryonic kidney HEK293 cells were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (Manassas, VA, USA). MDA-MB-436, MDA-MB-468, MDA-MB-157, MDA-MB-231, and HEK293 cells were cultured in DMEM, 4T1 cells were cultured in RPMI-1640, and MCF10A cells were cultured in DMEM/F12 (1:1), with all recommended supplements. All cells were maintained at 37°C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2.

Genomic analysis of ICAM1 expression in patients with TNBC

The ICAM1 mRNA expression of human breast tumors and normal breast tissues was analyzed using the R2: Genomics Analysis and Visualization Platform database (https://hgserver1.amc.nl/). The following four datasets were used in our genomic analysis: Tumor Breast Invasive Carcinoma-TCGA-1097, Tumor Breast (TNBC)-Brown-198, Tumor Breast compendium- Halfwerk-947, Tumor Breast (mutation status)-Meijers-Heijboer-155. The detailed information of microarray experiments and clinical samples is publically available in the R2: Genomics Analysis and Visualization Platform database.

Flow cytometry

A total of 1 × 106 cells were collected and rinsed twice with PBS. Obtained cells were blocked by 1% BSA in PBS for 30 min in an ice bath. After BSA blockage, cells were incubated with PE-conjugated ICAM1 or TROP2 antibodies for 1 hour at room temperature, respectively. Non-targeting PE-conjugated IgG was used as a control. Cells were rinsed with 1% BSA in PBS three times, resuspended in PBS, and evaluated using a BD FACSCalibur Flow Cytometer (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).

IF staining

A total of 2 × 104 cells were seeded in a Lab-Tek II Chamber Slide System with a 2 ml of cell culture medium overnight at 37°C. After the medium was removed, cells were rinsed twice with PBS and fixed with 4% formaldehyde in PBS at room temperature (RT) for 10 min, followed by washing with PBS. Samples were blocked with 1% BSA in PBS for 30 min in an ice bath. After BSA blocking, samples were costained with PE-conjugated ICAM1 or TROP2 antibody for 1 hour and rinsed with PBS. DAPI was used to stain cell nuclei. IF-stained samples were dried overnight in the dark and then examined using a Leica TCS SP5 confocal fluorescent microscope (Leica Microsystems, Buffalo Grove, IL, USA).

Imaging flow cytometry

A total of 1 × 106 cells were seeded in each well of six-well chamber slides and allowed to attach overnight. Then, attached cells were incubated with PE-conjugated ICAM1 antibody for 1 hour in 2 ml of cell culture media with 1% FBS at 37°C. Then, the cell monolayer was collected and rinsed with cold PBS twice and resuspended. The cellular internalization rate of ICAM1 antibody in treated cells was evaluated using an Amnis ImageStreamX Mark II imaging flow cytometry (Luminex, Austin, TX, USA).

Preparation and characterization of ICAM1 ADC

Four different ICAM1 ADCs (IC1-DM1, IC1-DM4, IC1-MMAE, and IC1-MMAF) were prepared via cysteine coupling as described elsewhere. Briefly, intermolecule disulfide bonds of the antibody were partially reduced with tris (2-carboxyethyl) phosphine (TCEP) (5 mM) followed by the conjugation of the four linker-warhead combinations mentioned above. Reduction and conjugation time as well as the concentration of TCEP and linker-warheads were adjusted to yield the average DAR of approximately 4. The average DAR was calculated from the integrated areas of major peaks corresponding to DAR of 0, 2, 4, 6, and 8, which were resolved using HIC on a Butyl-NPR column (4.6 mm by 3.5 cm; Tosoh, Japan). The HPLC system was a U3000 HPLC (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Sunnyvale, CA, USA) equipped with a diode array detector. Approximately 30 μg of ADCs was loaded onto the Butyl-NPR column at a flow rate of 1 ml/min in the two-component mixture of buffer A (25 mM sodium phosphate and 1.5 M ammonium sulfate) and buffer B [25 mM sodium phosphate containing 25% (v/v) isopropanol] over a 15-min linear gradient with ulraviolet monitoring at 280 nm. To monitor the potential aggregation of ADCs, size exclusion chromatography (SEC) was used on a TSKgel G3000SWXL SEC column (7.8 mm by 30 cm; Tosoh, Japan) with 20 mM sodium phosphate and 300 mM sodium chloride flowed at 1 ml/min to measure size variants of proteins. The ADC purification and buffer exchange were accomplished via ultrafiltration (30-kDa molecular weight cut-off filter). Concentrations of purified products were calculated on the basis of the absorbance at 280 nm and the molar extinction coefficient of the corresponding antibody.

In vitro efficacy tests

Cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at a density of 5 × 103 cells per well and allowed to attach overnight. Then, cells were treated with four different ICAM1 ADCs (IC1-DM1, IC1-DM4, IC1-MMAE, and IC1-MMAF) at different drug concentrations ranging from 0 to 10 μg/ml using a serial dilution factor of 10. After cells were cultured for another 48 hours, the cytotoxicity was determined by CCK-8 assay. The plate was read at the absorbance wavelength of 450 nm using a Molecular Devices SpectraMax microplate reader (San Jose, CA, USA). Cell viability was determined by comparing the absorbance of cells incubated with drugs to that of the control cells incubated without the presence of the drug.

In vivo studies

Mouse studies presented in this study were performed according to the protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Boston Children’s Hospital. For tumor specificity and in vivo biodistribution studies, orthotopic TNBCs were implanted by injecting 2 × 106 TNBC cells (MDA-MB-436 or 4T1) into the fourth right mammary fat pad of 6- to 8-week-old female nude or BALB/c mice (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA, USA). Tumors were allowed to develop for 2 to 3 weeks until they were at least 200 to 300 mm3 in volume, and then tumor-bearing mice were randomized into various treatment groups (n ≥ 5 per group) and received an intravenous injection of IgG-Cy5.5, IC1-Cy5.5, or IC1-MMAE-Cy5.5 at a dosage of 5 mg/kg mouse weight. At 48 hours after injection, in vivo NIR fluorescence imaging was performed on treated animals using an IVIS Lumina II System (Caliper, Hopkinton, MA, USA). Then, mice were euthanized by CO2 and 500 μl of mouse blood was collected immediately via cardiac puncture. The NIR fluorescence intensities of various organs (brain, heart, liver, lung, kidney, and spleen) and excised tumors were measured using IVIS Lumina II. Mouse leukocytes were isolated from whole blood via centrifugation at 1200 rpm for 15 min, followed by removing RBCs using RBC lysis buffer. The fluorescence intensity of IgG-Cy5.5 or IC1-Cy5.5 uptaken leukocytes were quantified using a BD FACSCalibur Flow Cytometer (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA).

For the in vivo efficacy studies, orthotopic TNBC tumors (MDA-MB-436 or MDA-MB-231) were established in 6- to 8-week-old female nude mice as described above. Tumors were grown to reach 100 mm3 for standard setting treatment or 500 mm3 for late-stage treatment. Then, tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into various treatment groups (n ≥ 5 per group) and received treatment of PBS (sham), free Dox, IC1 Ab, IC1-MMAE, or IC1-MMAF at an equivalent dosage of 5 mg/kg per week for 3 weeks via tail vein injection. Tumor growth was monitored weekly using a caliper. At the end point, animals were euthanized with CO2 and orthotopic tumors were excised to measure their mass. In dosage-dependent experiments, three ascending IC1-MMAE dosages (1, 5, and 10 mg/kg) were evaluated in orthotopic TNBC tumors (MDA-MB-436) using the same protocol. Then, mice were euthanized with CO2 and 500 μl of mouse blood was collected immediately via cardiac puncture. Collected blood was incubated for 20 min at RT to allow clotting, and then mouse serum was isolated after centrifuging at 2000g for 10 min in a refrigerated centrifuge. Serum levels of ALT, AST, creatinine, and BUN were determined using their activity assay kits purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA) with provided protocols. In MTD studies, the in vivo tolerability of IC1-MMAE was examined in 6- to 8-week-old female BALB/c mice. Healthy mice were randomized into various treatment groups (n = 10 per group) and received intravenous injections of IC1-MMAE at three ascending dosages (25, 50, and 75 mg/kg) for one injection. After injection, mouse body weight loss was used as the acute toxicity indicator and was closely monitored for up to 14 days.

For the in vivo efficacy study of IC1-MMAE with varying DARs, orthotopic TNBC tumors (MDA-MB-468) were established in 6- to 8-week-old female nude mice as described above. Tumor-bearing mice were treated with IC1-MMAE (DAR2, 2.4 mg/kg), IC1-MMAE(DAR4, 2.4 mg/kg), or IC1-MMAE(DAR2, 4.8 mg/kg) as a single intravenous injection when tumors were palpable (approximately 50 mm3 in volume). Tumor growth was monitored using a caliper, and animals were euthanized with CO2.

Statistical analysis

All of the experimental data were obtained in triplicate and are presented as means ± SD unless otherwise mentioned. One- and two-way analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with Bonferroni post hoc tests were used to analyze statistical variance when making multiple comparisons. All statistical analysis was performed using OriginPro 8 software.
