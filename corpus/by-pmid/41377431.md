## Abstract

Globally, over 13.5 billion mRNA vaccine doses have been administered, establishing a strong safety and adaptability record. In surgical oncology, where 25-40% of patients experience postoperative recurrence, mRNA vaccines offer a new avenue for durable immune surveillance.  To the Editor, 

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines have redefined immunotherapy, with global COVID-19 vaccination exceeding 13.5 billion doses and demonstrating platform safety across diverse populations. This success has accelerated interest in extending mRNA technology to oncology, particularly as an adjunct in surgical cancer care, where postoperative recurrence rates remain 25–40% in several solid tumors.

In surgical oncology, mRNA vaccines could bridge the critical gap between tumor excision and durable immune surveillance. By encoding patient-specific tumor antigens, these vaccines can elicit cytotoxic T-cell responses that eliminate micrometastases. Clinical data are promising that personalized mRNA cancer vaccines have achieved objective response rates of 44% in melanoma and recurrence reduction of nearly 50% when combined with checkpoint inhibitors. In pancreatic adenocarcinoma, which has a 5-year survival of only 12%, a phase I trial using a patient-tailored mRNA vaccine induced neoantigen-specific T-cell expansion in 50% of recipients, delaying recurrence beyond 1 year.

mRNA vaccines can mitigate postoperative immune suppression, a phenomenon seen in up to 60% of surgical cancer patients, thereby enhancing recovery and long-term tumor control. Integration of perioperative vaccination could thus redefine adjuvant therapy frameworks, offering a safer, programmable, and rapidly manufacturable option.

A newly emerging field, pharmacomicrobiomics, adds another dimension to this strategy. Gut microbiota diversity directly influences vaccine pharmacodynamics, modulating antigen presentation and cytokine responses. A 2025 preprint by Shahid et al reported that shifts in gut microbiota composition altered mRNA vaccine-induced T-cell activation by up to 35%, suggesting that microbial profiling could predict vaccine responsiveness. Such host–microbiome interactions could explain the interindividual variability observed in cancer vaccine efficacy and may inform future precision immunization models.

For mRNA vaccine adoption in surgical oncology, standardized trial endpoints, immune correlates, and biomarker-guided patient selection must be established. Early cost-effectiveness models estimate that integrating perioperative mRNA vaccination could reduce recurrence-associated hospital readmissions by 15–20%, offering not only clinical but also economic benefit.

mRNA vaccines herald a new era in neoplasm immunotherapy and precision surgical oncology. Their adaptability, safety, and ability to synergize with immune checkpoint inhibitors make them a compelling adjunct to surgical cancer care.
