# "Top-down and Bottom-up" cholesterol-depleting biomimetic nanoparticle for enhancing sonodynamic therapy against hepatocellular carcinoma.

## Abstract

Cholesterol depletion in tumor cells has been demonstrated as an effective strategy for cancer therapy. Previous studies have shown that cholesterol oxidase (COD) could promote cholesterol consumption. However, these tumor cells typically upregulate cholesterol synthesis as a compensatory mechanism to meet the rapid proliferation demands. To address this issue, we synthesized HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, which simultaneously facilitates cholesterol depletion and inhibits cholesterol synthesis to enhance sonodynamic therapy against hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). This HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 biomimetic nanoparticle was constructed by encapsulating cholesterol oxidase (COD) and the sonosensitizer indocyanine green (IR820) into ZIF-8 nanoparticle, followed by coating with a hybrid cell membrane from tumor cells and erythrocyte membrane. The hybrid membrane provides tumor-targeting capability, enabling HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle homes to Hepa1-6 tumor and disassembles in response to the acidic microenvironment and ultrasound stimulation. In vitro and in vivo studies confirmed significant cholesterol depletion and alleviation of hypoxia in Hepa1-6 cells. Upon ultrasound activation, a significant amount of reactive oxygen species (ROS) was generated, thereby enhancing the therapeutic effect on the inoculated tumors. Non-targeted metabolomics analysis further validated the downregulation of cholesterol metabolism-related pathways, which was consistent with the Filipin staining results observed in cellular experiments. Importantly, Hepa1-6 tumor growth was significantly suppressed and the inhibition rate reached 90%. These findings highlight HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 as a promising biomimetic nanoparticle orchestrates a two-pronged attack on hepatocellular carcinoma cholesterol metabolism: top-down suppression of cholesterol biosynthetic pathways, and bottom-up elimination of existing cholesterol stores, yielding outstanding therapeutic effects against HCC.

Introduction

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is one of the most common malignant tumors worldwide, which is characterized by complex etiologies, rapid progression, and high mortality rate. The liver is the largest metabolic organ and the abnormal cholesterol metabolism is closely linked to the development of HCC. Hepatocellular carcinoma cells accumulate cholesterol by sequestering LDLR and enhance AcCoA-mediated cholesterol biosynthesis through the MVA pathway to promote proliferation, while cholesterol accumulation also induces endoplasmic reticulum stress and T-cell depletion, and up-regulates immune checkpoints such as PD-1 and CD244 to promote tumor metastasis. Data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) indicate that genes involved in cholesterol synthesis and metabolism are upregulated, such as HMGCR, SQLE, and LDLR, while cholesterol is significantly overexpressed across various cancer types, including prostate, breast, colorectal, and liver cancers. Pan-cancer analysis further reveals that cholesterol metabolic reprogramming is a universal hallmark of cancer, enabling rapid tumor cell proliferation and survival. This reprogramming also affects the function of immune cells in the tumor microenvironment and promotes immune escape, and disrupting cholesterol homeostasis in tumor cells was widely applied in tumor therapy. Cholesterol regulates the activation of mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1(mTORC1) through the SNHG6-FAF2-mTOR axis, which in turn promotes the proliferation of liver cancer cells. Cholesterol oxidase is a potent catalyzer that not only degrades cholesterol but also disrupts lamellar pseudopodia and mitochondrial function, thereby enhancing Caspase-1-mediated apoptosis by updating the expression of the pro-apoptotic protein Bax, decreasing the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2, and inhibiting the tumor cell proliferation. Consequently, Cholesterol-depleting is promising for the inhibition of tumor migration and growth. However, the high consumption of cholesterol commonly triggers metabolic reprogramming and rapidly accelerates its synthesis. Therefore, simultaneously enhancing cholesterol depletion while inhibiting its synthesis, establishing a “Top-down and Bottom-up” cholesterol-depleting strategy, is ingenious for effectively disrupting cholesterol homeostasis in tumor cells.

Zinc is an essential trace element involved in the synthesis of metalloenzymes and the regulation of various transcription factors. Zinc-binding proteins contribute to numerous vital biochemical processes, such as glycolysis and DNA synthesis, which are essential for maintaining normal physiological functions. Both zinc deficiency and excess can disrupt the body's homeostasis, leading to abnormal functions such as oxidative stress, membrane damage, and protein dysfunction. Zinc deficiency for example, may impair protein function, including the inactivation of New Delhi Metallo-β-lactamase-1 (NDM-1), a key enzyme contributing to bacterial drug resistance, thereby potentially reversing drug resistance. Conversely, excessive zinc accumulation could inhibit glycolysis and induce metabolic disturbances by depleting cellular adenosine triphosphate (ATP) and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) levels. Since ATP serves as a primary energy source and NADPH functions as a crucial hydrogen donor in cholesterol biosynthesis, promoting excessive zinc accumulation in tumor cells may effectively suppress cholesterol synthesis.

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) are polymers composed of inorganic metal ions and organic ligands, which are widely used in nanodrug delivery systems due to their adjustable size, high porosity, and strong loading capacity. Among various MOFs, zeolitic imidazolate framework-8 (ZIF-8) stands out due to its unique pH responsiveness. ZIF-8 remains stable under neutral conditions but disintegrates in acidic environments, thereby facilitating controlled drug release. However, the application of standalone MOFs as drug delivery systems is limited by the lack of inherent targeting capabilities, which reduces their efficiency in reaching tumor sites and increases the risk of rapid systemic clearance. To overcome these limitations, surface modification of MOFs is necessary to enhance their circulation time in vivo. One effective strategy involves coating the MOF surface with biological membranes, such as erythrocyte or tumor cell membranes. This biomimetic camouflage not only enables MOFs homologous targeting to tumor cells but also endows them with viral catalytic activity, thereby enhancing therapeutic efficacy. For example, red blood cell membranes retain catalase activity from their source cells, enabling them to utilize excess H2O2 in the tumor microenvironment, which produces oxygen and alleviates cellular hypoxia. Such biomimetic MOFs coated with cell membranes show great promise for enhancing drug delivery and tumor therapy.

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) has gained much attention due to its advantages, including noninvasiveness, deeper tissue penetration, and favorable safety profile. Profiting by the deep penetration, SDT has been extensively applied in controlled drug release, enhanced antimicrobial effects and the treatment of various diseases and tumors, consistently yielding promising results. For instance, Liu et al. demonstrated that the phytochorion acoustic sensitizer (C34) can reduce interfacial tension and enhance acoustic efficiency, thereby effectively inhibiting breast cancer tumor growth and triggering an immune response to prevent tumor metastasis. SDT employs low-frequency ultrasound waves, typically ranging from 1 to 3 MHz, which can penetrate deep into tissues and concentrate energy at the target tumor site, while minimizing damage to surrounding healthy tissue. Upon ultrasound exposure, SDT induces localized temperature increases and causes vacuolization within the tumor tissue. Additionally, the mechanical energy could disrupt nanocarriers and activate the sensitizer to generate high levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS). The accumulated ROS within tumor cells and cause oxidative stress till ultimately killing the tumor cells. However, SDT rapidly exacerbates tumor hypoxia due to the by oxygen depletion and ROS generation, which not only reduces its own therapeutic efficacy but also induces resistance to ROS-based therapies through activation of HIF-1α. There have several strategies have been used to improve tumor oxygen supply, such as catalyzing oxygen production from endogenous H2O2, while SDT still faces a dual challenge of primary hypoxia and treatment-induced hypoxia. Further optimization of oxygen enhancement strategies is necessary to overcome these therapeutic limitations. Nowadays, the commonly used sonosensitizers, such as inorganic metal oxides, chlorin E6 (CE6) and new indocyanine green (IR820), exhibit sensitivity to ultrasound but are limited by low bioavailability, poor stability, and short half-life. The design of novel nanomaterial carrier systems to optimize the delivery efficiency, stability and tumor targeting of sonosensitizers has become a key research direction to improve the therapeutic efficacy of SDT.

To address this, we developed a ZIF-8 nanoparticle loaded with cholesterol oxidase (COD) and the sonosensitizer new indocyanine green (IR820), and encapsulated it within the heterogeneous membrane composed of red blood cell membrane and Hepa1-6 tumor cell membrane, termed as HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 (Scheme 1). Leveraging the homologous targeting capability conferred by the Hepa1-6 cell membrane, the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle selectively targets to Hepa1-6 tumors and undergoes disintegration in response to the acidic tumor microenvironment and ultrasound stimulation. The released Zn2+ and COD synergistically deplete cholesterol by disrupting the supply of ATP and NADP+ to inhibit cholesterol synthesis and enhance cholesterol oxidation to exacerbate the consumption. H2O2 generated by COD is further catalyzed by catalase present in the erythrocyte membrane, producing O2, which alleviates cellular hypoxia. Under ultrasound stimulation, the sonosensitizer IR820 provoked O2 to produce significant amounts of singlet oxygen, resulting in effective tumor cell destruction. Moreover, the action of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle significantly upregulates the expression of the pro-apoptotic protein Bax while downregulating the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2 to promote Hepa1-6 tumor cell apoptosis. These findings suggest that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle holds great potential for enhancing sonodynamic therapy of hepatocellular carcinoma through cholesterol depletion.

Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle

ZIF-8 was synthesized using the method previously reported. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) results showed that ZIF-8 exhibited a spherical morphology with an average diameter of approximately 111.16 ± 8.32 nm (Fig. 1A I, Ⅱ). After encapsulation with a hybrid membrane approximately 10 nm thick, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle retained their spherical structure, with an increased diameter of approximately 120.16 ± 4.16 nm (Fig. 1A Ⅲ, Ⅳ). Dynamic light scattering (DLS) measurements showed that the hydrodynamic diameter of ZIF-8, ZIF-8@COD/IR820, and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle were approximately 190 nm, 220 nm, and 295 nm respectively (Fig. 1D). The increasing hydrodynamic diameter was consistent with the trend observed in TEM results, which possibly due to the encapsulation of COD and IR820 within the ZIF-8 structure, as well as the coating of HM on the nanoparticle surface. Notably, ZIF-8 disintegrated under mildly acidic conditions (Fig. 1C) while remaining stable in the normal physiological environment (Supplementary Fig. 1), which is beneficial for the targeted release of COD in the acidic microenvironment of hepatocellular carcinoma. As shown in the UV–vis absorption spectra (Supplementary Fig. 2), the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle solution exhibited a characteristic absorption peak of IR820 at 820 nm, confirming its successful loading. To further verify the encapsulation of IR820 and COD, energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed on the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle. The presence of Zn, Na, and P elements in the EDS image (Fig. 1B), indicated the incorporation of IR820 and COD within the ZIF-8 framework. As illustrated in Fig. 1E, the zeta potential of ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle increased, likely due to the loading of COD and IR820. However, the zeta potential of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was decreased, which can be attributed to the surface coating of the hybrid membrane. The nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms of ZIF-8 and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle exhibited type I profiles, indicative of mesoporous structures without pore collapse, and the pore sizes of ZIF-8 and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle were relatively small (Fig. 1F). Notably, the specific surface area of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (389.0436 m2/g) was compared to that of ZIF-8 nanoparticle (592.4497 m2/g), confirming the successful loading of IR820 and COD, as well as the HM coating (Supplementary Table 1). Western blot and SDS-PAGE were performed to vertify the HM coating on the surface of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle. The SDS-PAGE results showed that the protein bands of the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle and hybrid cell membranes were similar, indicating the hybrid cell membranes were encapsulated on the surface of the nanoparticle (Fig. 1K). As shown in Fig. 1G, the presence of GPC3 protein (from Hepa1-6 cell membranes) and CD47 protein (from red blood cell membranes) on HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle furtherly confirmed the successful incorporation of HM on the nanoparticle surface.

The excellent stability and biocompatibility of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle is fundamental for its application. When dispersed in water and 10% FBS for 72 h, these HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticles exhibited favorable polydispersity indices (Fig. 1M) with only slight changes in particle size (Fig. 1L), indicating good stability. Meanwhile, the UV–vis absorbance of IR820 in these HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticles remained stable, with no significant change observed over 72 h in both water and 10% FBS. (Fig. 1N and Supplementary Fig. 3A and B). Similarly, the fluorescence signal of these nanoparticles slightly weakened, which might be caused by photobleaching (Supplementary Fig. 3C). These results suggest that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle is suitable candidate for tumor therapy. To assess circulation performance, the half-life of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was determined in the blood. Curve fitting revealed that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle had a longer half-life (3.953 h) compared to ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (1.749 h), likely due to the protective effect of the hybrid cell membrane coating (Fig. 1H). Hemocompatibility was assessed through hemolysis assays at various concentrations (5, 10, 20, 50, and 100 μg/mL). All hemolysis rates were below 5%, complying with international safety standards (Fig. 1I). Additionally, red blood cells incubated with these nanoparticles retained their characteristic biconcave morphology (Supplementary Fig. 4), further confirming excellent blood compatibility. Biocompatibility was evaluated using the CCK-8 assay. As shown in Fig. 1J, the cell viability of AML12 cells and Hepa1-6 cells was concentration-dependent. After 48 h of incubation with 100 μg/mL nanoparticles, over 80% of AML12 cells and approximately 60% of Hepa1-6 cells remained viable, indicating that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle is well-tolerated by both cell types at this concentration.

Validation of enzyme activity, ROS production, and tumor cell killing ability

The enzyme activities of COD and catalase are crucial for the therapeutic potential of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle. To evaluate the catalytic capacity, cholesterol (4 μmol/dL) was incubated with a series of concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle for 2 h. As shown in Fig. 2A, cholesterol levels decreased significantly with the increasing HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle concentrations, indicating strong catalytic activity. Subsequently, H2O2 was quantified as a byproduct of cholesterol oxidation. Excess cholesterol that was incubated with different concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (10, 30, 60, 90, 100 μg/mL), and the resulting H2O2 was quantified via its reaction with TMB. The absorbance at 650 nm increased with higher concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, further confirming their strong catalytic capacity (Fig. 2B). In addition, the enzyme activity of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was tested in different environmental conditions. Compared with free COD, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle exhibited enhanced catalytic ability and greater pH stability, which is advantageous for effective cholesterol degradation in acidic tumor microenvironment (Fig. 2C). Interestingly, the absorbance of TMB at 650 nm gradually increased with the prolongation of the reaction time, indicating that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle can continuously catalyze cholesterol oxidation when cholesterol is sufficiently available (Fig. 2D). To evaluate the activity of natural catalase on erythrocyte membrane, the generation of oxygen was monitored using a dissolved oxygen meter. As shown in Fig. 2E, oxygen was produced immediately and reached peak levels within 240 s, highlighting the effective and rapid catalytic capacity of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle. Especially, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle maintained high relative activity under acidic conditions, suggesting their strong potential to function as catalase mimics within the acidic tumor microenvironment (Fig. 2F).

Moreover, singlet oxygen was detected by the active oxygen indicator probe DPBF. The characteristic UV–vis peak at ∼410 nm decreased rapidly upon treatment with HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, indicating effective singlet oxygen production. This decline was both time-dependent and influenced by ultrasound power, confirming ultrasound-responsive ROS generation (Fig. 2G and H). These results suggest that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle is potentially suitable for ultrasonic therapy. To further investigate cellular uptake, the fluorescence distribution of DIO-labeled RBCM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was examined after 4 h of incubation with Hepa1-6 cells. The fluorescence intensity of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 group was significantly higher than that of the RBCM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 group (Fig. 2I). Compared to normal hepatocytes AML12 cell (Supplementary Fig. 4), the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle showed a preferential uptake by Hepa1-6 cells, with the particles primarily localized in the perinuclear cytoplasmic region. All these results demonstrate the strong homologous targeting capability conferred by the Hepa1-6 cell membrane coating. As previously described, the acidic tumor microenvironment facilitated the degradation of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, which in turn promotes the release of zinc ions and COD. Notably, ultrasonic stimulation notably accelerated the release of zinc ions and COD, leading to enhanced accumulation (Fig. 2K). In addition, ROS production in the various treatment groups was traced by DCFH-DA probe (Fig. 2J). Hepa1-6 cells treated with HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle group exhibited a stronger green fluorescence signal, indicating enhanced ROS generation in this group.

To evaluate the tumor therapeutic efficacy of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, Hepa1-6 cells from different treatments were stained with Propidium Iodide and Calcein-AM to distinguish live and dead cells, respectively. As shown in Fig. 2M, Hepa1-6 cells incubated with PBS and HM/ZIF-8@IR820 nanoparticle present light green color, while cells in HM/ZIF-8@IR820+US group, ZIF-8@IR820+US group, and HM/ZIF-8@IR820 group displayed strong red fluorescence, signifying extensive cell death. Notably, more than 90% of the Hepa1-6 cells died in HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group. This pronounced cytotoxicity is likely due to the combined effects of cholesterol depletion and increased ROS generation induced by ultrasound exposure. Importantly, Western blotting results showed a significant downregulation of the anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2 and an upregulation of the pro-apoptotic protein Bax in the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group (Fig. 2N). This was consistent with the live/dead cell staining assay and further confirm the synergistic effectiveness of the combination therapy.

Reduction of intracellular cholesterol levels and inhibition of cell migration

Elevated intracellular cholesterol levels are closely associated with HCC, and the effective strategies for cholesterol reduction are urgently needed. As shown in Fig. 3A, we expected that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle would selectively target to Hepa1-6 tumor cells via homologous membrane recognition. Once internalized, the nanoparticle would degrade in response to the acidic tumor microenvironment and ultrasound stimulation. This degradation facilitates the release of Zn2+, which accumulate in tumor cells and inhibit the production of ATP and NADP+, thereby suppressing the synthesis of cholesterol. At the same time, the remaining intracellular cholesterol was oxidized by COD and produced hydrogen peroxide to enhance the antitumor effect. The total intracellular zinc concentration in Hepa1-6 cells treated with different concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (equivalent ZIF-8 concentration = 5, 10, 20, 50, and 100 μg/mL) was measured by inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometer (ICP-OES). As the concentration of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle increased, a corresponding and significant rise of zinc was observed in intracellular (Fig. 3B). Interestingly, the levels of ATP and NADP+ in Hepa1-6 cells gradually decreased following treatment with increasing concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (Fig. 3C and D). Notably, the use of TPEN, a zinc chelator, alleviated the reduction in ATP and NADP+, suggesting that zinc ion accumulation plays a crucial role in disrupting cellular energy metabolism. Oppositely, ultrasonic stimulation further exacerbated the depletion of ATP and NADP+ content, likely due to the accelerated degradation of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle and the consequent release of zinc ions. Since ATP and NADP+ are essential energy carriers and hydrogen donors in cholesterol biosynthesis, their depletion contributes to the suppression of cholesterol production. Concurrently, COD loaded in nanoparticle catalyzed the oxidation of residual cholesterol, thereby promoting cholesterol consumption. To assess intracellular cholesterol levels, Filipin staining was performed. As shown in Fig. 3E, treatment with ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle led to the initial reduction in intracellular cholesterol. There was little cholesterol remnant in the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (5, 10, 20, 50, and 100 μg/mL) groups, cholesterol levels were markedly diminished, with staining intensity decreasing in a dose-dependent manner. These results were accordant with the cellular uptake of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, and furtherly confirm their effectiveness in depleting the intracellular cholesterol. In addition, compared with the PBS group, HM/ZIF-8 nanoparticle and HM/ZIF-8@COD nanoparticle demonstrated an initial inhibitory effect on the migration of Hepa1-6 cells, potentially due to the accumulation of intracellular zinc ions (Fig. 3F). Notably, cell migration was significantly suppressed after 12 and 24 h of treatment with HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (100 μg/mL) in combination with ultrasound treatment, which may be attributed to nanoparticles’ potent intracellular cholesterol depleting capability. Overall, these findings suggest that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle hold promise for effectively depleting cholesterol and inhibiting Hepa1-6 cell migration.

In vivo fluorescence imaging

To determine the optimal concentration for treatment, we first measured the fluorescence intensity of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle at different concentrations. As shown in Fig. 4B and C, the fluorescence intensity of the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle gradually increases within the concentration and peak at 1 mg/mL, while a decline was observed likely due to quenching effects at higher concentrations. Based on this result, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (1 mg/mL) was injected vis tail vein into Hepa1-6 tumor bearing C57BL/6J mice and fluorescence imaging was detected at a series of time points (1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 24, 36, 48, and 72 h) as shown in Fig. 4A. Free IR820 and ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle were set as the control, and fluorescence intensity increased along with time. As shown in Fig. 4D, the fluorescence signals in the free IR820 group gradually decreased with the extension of the administration time, and almost no fluorescent signals were observed 8 h later. This result may be attributed to the rapidly blood circulation and active metabolism of free IR820. The ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle fluorescence signal at the tumor site gradually increased and peaked at 8 h, followed by the fast clearance within 24 h. In contrast, in the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 group, maximum fluorescence intensity was observed at 12 h, with signal retention extended up to 72 h. This prolonged retention is probably owed to the homologous targeting capacity conferred by the Hepa1-6 tumor cell membrane coating on the surface of the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, consistent with the results of the blood half-life study. Furthermore, the fluorescence intensities of major organs and tumors were evaluated at 12 h time point. As shown in Fig. 4E and F, tumor in HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 group exhibited a markedly stronger fluorescence signal. Collectively, these findings indicated that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle possess strong potential for in vivo fluorescence imaging in HCC therapy.

Evaluation of the tumor therapy efficacy

To further evaluate the therapeutic effect of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, C57BL/6J mice bearing Hepa1-6 tumors (volume: 60–100 mm3) were randomly divided into five groups and subjected to different treatment: saline, HM/ZIF-8, HM/ZIF-8@COD, HM/ZIF-8@IR820+US, and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US (Fig. 5A). As shown in Fig. 5B and C, tumor growth was obviously suppressed in HM/ZIF-8@COD group, HM/ZIF-8@IR820+US group, and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group were obviously suppressed. Notably, the most striking therapeutic effect was observed in the combination therapy group, where complete tumor regression (defined as disappearance of palpable mass) was achieved in two treated mice (dashed circles, Fig. 5B). Compared with the HM/ZIF-8@COD group and HM/ZIF-8@IR820+US group, tumors in HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group emerged the greatest inhibition rate and the lowest tumor weigh, which probably attributed to the enhanced therapy effect resulting from efficient intracellular cholesterol depletion (Fig. 5E and F). Furthermore, no significant changes in body weight were observed among the treatment groups, and viscera coefficient analysis showed no evidence of apparent systemic toxicity (Fig. 5D and G). After 15 days of treatment, all the mice were euthanized to collect blood, tumors and the major organs. No significant differences were observed in blood chemical analysis of ALT (Fig. 5H) and AST (Fig. 5I). In addition, H&E staining revealed no signs of atrophy, degeneration, or necrosis in the organs (Heart, Liver, Spleen, Lung and Kidney), indicating that HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was well tolerated and exhibited good biocompatibility in vivo. Compared with the saline group, tumors in HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group were significantly disrupted and presented cavitation, which is perhaps attributed to the synergistic effects of cholesterol depletion and ultrasonic stimulation (Fig. 5J). Collectively, all these results demonstrated HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle is both safe for use in C57BL/6J mice and effective for HCC therapy.

Previous reports have shown that zinc ions play an important role in many metabolic pathways. As the acid tumor microenvironment triggered ultrasound-enhanced degradation of the nanoparticles, the zinc ion content accumulates in tumor tissues, which is followed by gradual metabolism of the nanoparticles. To further study the mechanism of intracellular cholesterol depletion, the accumulation of zinc ions at different time points was assessed using the following ICP-OES. As shown in Fig. 6A, the highest zinc concentration was observed in tumors after 12 h post-treatment, which is possibly owing to the targeted accumulation of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle at the tumor site. After 15 days of treatment, the zinc content remained higher than those observed at the initial time point but slightly lower than the peak at 12 h, suggesting partial metabolism and gradual accumulation of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle in vivo. Compared with the saline group, the NADP+ content in tumors from the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group showed a significant decrease, dropping from 36.6 nmol/g to 18.7 nmol/g (Fig. 6B). Similarly, the ATP levels present a parallel decline (Fig. 6C). Importantly, an obvious cholesterol reduction was found accompanied with the decreases in both ATP and NADP+, further supporting the correlation between intracellular cholesterol depletion and the disruption of ATP/NADP+-associated metabolic processes (Fig. 6D). To explore the underlying mechanisms of the antitumor effect of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, immunofluorescence assays were conducted to assess the expression of key biomarkers. HIF-1α and pimonidazole, a well-established hypoxia-inducible factor, were utilized to evaluate the tumor hypoxic environment. As shown in Fig. 6E and F, both hypoxic intensity and HIF-1α expression were significantly reduced in HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US group. This alleviation of hypoxia may be attributed to the catalytic activity of catalase on the erythrocyte membrane and the enhanced oxygen diffusion promoted by ultrasound irradiation. Furthermore, the expression of Bax and Bcl-2 was closely associated with intracellular cholesterol homeostasis and were examined. As shown in Fig. 6G and H, the green fluorescence of pro-apoptotic protein Bax was significantly increased, while the red fluorescence of anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2 was notably decreased. These results were consistent with the western blot results in Hepa1-6 cells. Collectively, these findings indicated that the remarkable antitumor efficacy can be attributed to the cholesterol depletion and the enhanced effects of ultrasound therapy.

Metabolic alteration in hepatocellular carcinoma

To further investigate metabolic alterations in hepatocellular carcinoma, non-targeted metabolomics analysis was performed on Hepa1-6-tumor-bearing mice with either saline or HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US. The volcano plot of differential metabolites revealed that, compared to the saline group, 52 metabolites were significantly upregulated, 60 metabolites were downregulated, and 573 metabolites no significant changes (Fig. 7A). Heatmap analysis demonstrated marked differences in levels of amino acids, lipids, and carbohydrates in the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820+US treatment group compared to the saline group. Among the identified metabolites, taurine was found to inhibit cholesterol synthesis, while bile acids were associated with enhanced cholesterol excretion (Fig. 7B). Differential metabolites were subsequently classified and quantified based on the KEGG database. Notably, three compounds were related to energy metabolism, and thirteen compounds were related to lipid metabolism in the metabolic classification (Fig. 7C). KEGG pathway enrichment analysis was conducted to explore changes in metabolic pathways were explored. As shown in Fig. 7D, the alterations in metabolite abundance were linked to several multiple metabolic pathways, including terpene skeleton biosynthesis, nitrogen metabolism, primary bile acid biosynthesis, taurine and hypo taurine metabolism, arginine and proline metabolism, cysteine and methionine metabolism, glycolysis, and the pentose phosphate pathway. The metabolic network analysis revealed that multiple metabolic pathways interacted with each other, characterized by with the obvious downregulation of lipoic acid metabolism, linoleic acid metabolism, and the pentose phosphate pathway, alongside the upregulation of unsaturated fatty acid biosynthesis (Fig. 7E). These metabolic shifts suggest a dual effect: disrupting energy supply while promoting cholesterol metabolism. This evidence supports the hypothesis that the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, in combination with sonodynamic therapy, could modulate the pentose phosphate pathway and cholesterol-related metabolic pathways, thereby influencing both energy metabolism and cholesterol metabolites. In summary, all these results were consistent with cellular and in vivo experimental results, demonstrating a significantly enhanced therapeutic effect in hepatocellular carcinoma.

Conclusion

In this study, we synthesized HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, which exerts a dual action of promoting cholesterol depletion and hindering cholesterol synthesis, thereby enhancing the therapeutic efficacy against hepatocellular carcinoma. Notably, the nanoparticle exhibited low cytotoxicity and high biocompatibility, indicating promising potential for future clinical applications. Leveraging the homologous targeting capability of the tumor cell membrane, the nanoparticle specifically accumulated in tumor tissues, resulting in substantial zinc ion enrichment and an accelerated release of cholesterol oxidase (COD) under the acidic tumor microenvironment and ultrasound stimulation. Mechanistically, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle not only impaired ATP and NADP+ production through zinc ion accumulation—effectively suppressing cholesterol synthesis—but also directly catalyzed cholesterol degradation via COD-mediated oxidation. The reduction in cholesterol levels facilitated the up-regulation of pro-apoptotic protein Bax and down-regulation of anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2, which promoting apoptosis in Hepa1-6 tumor cells. Additionally, the elevated levels of hydrogen peroxide in the tumor microenvironment triggered a cascade reaction with erythrocyte membrane vesicles. Meanwhile, the sonosensitizer IR820, when activated by ultrasound, generated singlet oxygen species that further accelerated Hepa1-6 tumor cell death. In brief, HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle effectively improved the hypoxic tumor environment, enhanced cholesterol depletion, and significantly inhibited Hepa1-6 tumor growth. The innovative approach of combining zinc ion accumulation, COD catalysis, and ultrasound-responsive sonosensitizer provides a unique strategy for cholesterol-depletion-based therapy. This study not only offers insights into an innovative cancer therapy mechanism but also holds considerable potential for clinical translation, offering a novel therapeutic approach for hepatocellular carcinoma treatment.

Materials

Dimethylimidazole (C4H6N2, 98%, MW 82.1) and the CCK-8 kit were purchased from Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Zinc acetate dihydrate (C4H6O4Zn.2H2O, MW 219.51) was obtained from Sigma-Aldrich (Shanghai, China). Cholesterol oxidase (COD), IR820(C46H50ClN2NaO6S2, MW 849.47), the Filipin complex and Pimonidazole were obtained from MedChemExpress (Shanghai, China). ATP and NADP+ assay kits were supplied from Nanjing Jiancheng Biotechnology (Jiangsu, China). Total cholesterol, ALT and AST assay kits were obtained from Solarbio (Beijing, China). The remaining reagents were stored in the laboratory.

Preparation of ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle

2-methylimidazole (656.8 mg) was dissolved in 50 mL of ultrapure water, where it was fully dissolved to prepare solution A. Zinc acetate dihydrate (74.35 mg) was dissolved in 25 mL of ultrapure water to prepare solution B. Subsequently, 1 mL of COD aqueous solution (1 mg/mL) and 200 μL of IR820 aqueous solution (1 mg/mL) were added to solution A, followed by stirring at room temperature for 10 min. Solution B was then added dropwise to the mixture under continuous stirring at 200 rpm for another 10 min. The reaction mixture was centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 20 min. The supernatant was then discarded, and the precipitate was washed twice with ultrapure water. The final product, purified ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle, was obtained.

Preparation of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle

Hepa1-6 cells were cultured, harvested, and resuspended in membrane protein extraction buffer. The cell suspension was repeatedly frozen and thawed in liquid nitrogen 5–10 times, then centrifuged at 1500 rpm to remove the cell debris. The resulting supernatant was further centrifuged at 12,000 rpm to collect the membrane fraction. The resulting precipitate was washed twice with water to obtain Hepa1-6 cell membrane fragments. Blood was collected from the mouse orbit and centrifuged at 3500 rpm for 5 min at 4°C to separate the plasma. The erythrocytes were washed with pre-cooled 1 × PBS, lysed in 5 vol of 0.25 × PBS in an ice bath for 30 min, and then centrifuged at 8000 rpm for 10 min. The resulting precipitate was washed twice with water to obtain and the erythrocyte membrane fragments. Erythrocyte membrane, tumor cell membrane, and ZIF@COD/IR820 nanoparticle were prepared by repeated extrusion through an Avanti micro-extruder (200 nm polycarbonate porous membrane) at a ratio of 1:1:2 for 15–20 cycles to obtain the coated nanoparticle, named as HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820.

Characterization of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle

The HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was characterized using transmission electron microscopy (TEM), UV–vis spectroscopy, specific surface area and pore distribution analysis, and dynamic light scattering to evaluate the physical and chemical properties such as morphology, elemental composition, absorption spectra, porosity, hydrated dynamic particle size, and zeta potential. The presence of the Hepa1-6 cell membrane-specific protein GPC3 and the erythrocyte surface-specific protein CD47 on HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was evaluated by Western blot analysis. SDS-PAGE was conducted to confirm that the heterozygous cell membrane successfully encapsulated the ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle.

Determination of blood half-life

A total of 100 μL of ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle or HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 (1 mg/mL) nanoparticle was injected into C57BL/6J mice via the tail vein. Blood samples were collected from the tail at 5 min, 1, 2, 4, 8, 12, 24, and 48 h post-injection. The collected blood was digested, filtered, diluted with 2% nitric acid, and the concentration of Zn was detected by ICP-OES. The half-life of ZIF-8@COD/IR820 and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was determined by fitting the corresponding pharmacokinetic curves.

Detections of COD and enzyme activities on erythrocyte membranes

Firstly, cholesterol solutions (4 μmol/dL) were incubated with different concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle to evaluate their catalytic activity. After 2 h of reaction, the residual cholesterol content was detected using the assay kit.

To further assess catalytic efficiency, 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) was used as a chromogenic indicator, various concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (10, 30, 60, 90, and 100 μg/mL) were reacted with excess cholesterol and TMB for 30 min, followed by scanning using a UV–vis spectrophotometer across the full wavelength range to monitor the catalytic reaction.

To investigate the pH dependence of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 and free COD, buffer solution with pH range of 4.5–6.5 was prepared, along with a 100 μg/mL cholesterol solution was used as the substrate. Then, 20 μL of prepared TMB (final concentration of 1 mM), HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 (final concentration of 100 μg/mL), and TMB (final concentration of 100 μg/mL) were sequentially added to the reaction mixture and incubated for 30 min. The solutions were scanned by a UV–vis spectrophotometer, with the absorbance measures across the full wavelength range to assess the catalytic activity under different pH conditions.

To investigate the time-dependent catalytic activity of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820, three experimental groups were applied using TMB as the chromogenic substrate: (1) HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 + cholesterol solution + TMB, (2) HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 + TMB and (3) cholesterol + TMB. The prepared TMB solution (final concentration of 1 mM), cholesterol (final concentration of 100 μg/mL), and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 solution (final concentration of 100 μg/mL) were added (20 μL each), and the three solution groups were recorded using a UV–vis spectrophotometer at 1 minute intervals over a total of 50 min, scanning across the full wavelength range at different time points to monitor the progression of the reaction.

Deionized water was purged with nitrogen to remove dissolved oxygen, and the H2O2 solution was diluted to a final concentration of 1%. Different concentrations of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was added to the 1% H2O2 solution, and the oxygen production at different time intervals was measured using a dissolved oxygen meter. Then experiments were repeated by preparing buffer solutions at pH values of 4.0, 4.5, 5.0, 5.5, 6.0, and 6.5.

COD and Zn2+ releases

4 mL of HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (1 mg/mL) were divided into four equal portions and sealed in dialysis bags, which were securely tied at both ends. Then the bags were immersed in phosphate buffers at pH 7.4 and pH 6.3, corresponding to four experimental groups: 1) pH 7.4 with ultrasound group, 2) pH 7.4 without ultrasound group, 3) pH 6.3 with ultrasound group, 4) pH 6.3 without ultrasound group. The dialysis fluid was collected at different durations (0, 0.5, 1, 1.5, 2, 2.5, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, and 12 h), and the cumulative release of COD content was measured using the BCA kit, while the release of zinc ions was determined using ICP-OES. The release of COD was calculated based on the standard curve.

Singlet oxygen detection

3 mL of the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (20 μg/mL) was mixed with DPBF (40 μg/mL) and sonicated for 2 min at 0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.8, 1, 1.25, 1.5, and 1.75 W/cm2, respectively. Then the mixture was transferred to a cuvette for UV–vis absorption spectrum analysis. Additionally, 3 mL of the HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (20 μg/mL) and DPBF (40 μg/mL) were mixed and sonicated at 1.5 W/cm2 for varying durations (0, 30, 60, 90, 120, 150, and 180 s). UV–vis absorbance spectra of the mixed solution at different time points were measured.

In vitro ROS generation

Hepa1-6 cells were seeded in 24-well cell culture plates and incubated overnight. The media containing PBS, HM/ZIF-8@COD, ZIF-8@COD/IR820, or HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (100 μg/mL) were added into each well and incubated with Hepa1-6 cells at 37°C in a 5% CO2 incubator for 4 h. Then each group was sonicated at 1 W/cm2 for 60 s. Hepa1-6 cells were washed three times with PBS and incubated with culture medium containing DCFH-DA for 30 min and washed three times with PBS. Finally, intracellular ROS levels were then visualized and imaged by fluorescence microscopy.

Cellular uptake

AML12 cells were used as the control group, while Hepa1-6 cells were used as the experimental group. AML12 cells and Hepa1-6 cells were seeded in 24-well plates and incubated overnight in a humidified incubator at 37°C with 5% CO2. DIO-labeled nanoparticle was prepared in advance. After removing the original culture medium, and DIO-labeled RBCM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle was added to the wells at a volume of 1 mL per well. Then cells were incubated at 37°C for 4 h under standard culture conditions. Following incubation, the culture medium was aspirated and washed three times with PBS. Then, 100 μL of 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI) was added to each well and incubated in the dark for 15–20 min. Fluorescence distribution was observed using fluorescence microscopy, with green fluorescence indicating the nanoparticle's location and blue fluorescence from DAPI indicating the nucleus.

AM/PI staining experiments

Hepa1-6 cells were seeded in 24-well plates and incubated until they were fully adhered to the surface. The medium was replaced with solutions containing PBS, HM/ZIF-8@IR820, ZIF-8@COD/IR820, or HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (100 μg/mL), followed by incubation for 4 h. After incubation, the cells were subjected to ultrasonication at 1 W/cm2 for 60 s and cultured for an additional hour. Hepa1-6 cells were washed three times with PBS and incubated with Propidium Iodide and Calcein-AM for 20 min away from light. Then excess dye was washed off with PBS. The live and dead cells were observed using fluorescence microscopy.

Detection of the ATP and NADP+ content

Hepa1-6 cells were seeded in 6-well plates and incubated until they were fully adhered. Different concentrations of HM/ZIF-8 nanoparticle and excess TPEN were added to each well. Ultrasound was performed 6 h after treatment of Hepa1-6 cells. ATP and NADP+ levels were measured 2 h post-sonication using an assay kit. Tissues were harvested and purified by ice rubbing before detection with the appropriate assay kit.

Detection of zinc levels

Hepa1-6 cells were digested and seeded into 6-well plates, and then treated with media containing various concentrations of PBS, ZIF-8@COD/IR820, and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle for 12 h. Then, Hepa1-6 cells were collected and subjected to ablation with concentrated nitric acid. Zinc content was measured using ICP-OES by diluting with 2% nitric acid and leaching impurities.

C57BL/6J mice were euthanized at 0, 12, 72 h, as well as 15 days after intravenous injection of 100 μL HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (1 mg/mL) via the tail vein. Tissues from the heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, small intestine, and tumor were collected, weighed, and digested for further analysis by adding 1 mL of concentrated nitric acid per 1 mg of tissue. Then zinc content in each organ and tumor tissue was determined by ICP-OES.

Detection of cholesterol levels

Hepa1-6 cells in the logarithmic growth phase were seeded into 48-well plates and incubated overnight. After discarding the old medium, Hepa1-6 cells were treated with medium-diluted PBS, ZIF-8@COD/IR820, and HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle at different concentrations for 8 h. Following treatments, the cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 30 min, washed three times with PBS, and then incubated with 100 μL of Filipin working solution for 2 h in the dark. After staining, Hepa1-6 cells were washed three times with PBS and observed under the fluorescence microscope to assess intracellular cholesterol levels.

Separately, tissue cholesterol was extracted and collected in an ice bath, quantified using the total cholesterol assay kit.

Cell scratching assay

Hepa1-6 cells were seeded into 6-well plates at a density of 1.5 × 105 cells/well. Once the cells reached full confluence the medium was removed. A vertical scratch was made across the cell monolayer using a sterile pipette tip, and the scratched area was gently washed with medium three times to remove detached cells. The different nanoparticles (diluted in medium) were then added to the wells, and Hepa1-6 cells were incubated for further analysis. Cell migration was observed and photographed at 0, 12, and 24 h.

Western blot

Hepa1-6 cells were treated with different nanoparticle for 24 h and washed with PBS and then lysed on ice with an appropriate lysis buffer. Cellular proteins were extracted, and protein concentrations were determined by the BCA kit. Western blot was performed to detect the expression levels of Bcl-2 and Bax.

Tumor models

All animal experiments were approved by the Zhengzhou University Animal Ethics and Protection Committee (Approval No. ZZUIRB2023-241). Healthy male C57BL/6J mice (4 weeks old) were obtained from Zhengzhou University Experimental Animal Centered. Hepa1-6 cells were cultured, digested and resuspended in PBS and kept on ice prior to injection. A 100 μL of suspension containing approximately 1 × 106 cells was injected into the subcutaneously into the right hind limb of each mouse. A visible bulge formed at the injection site and the needle was withdrawn gently after slight lateral movement to prevent leakage of the cell suspension. Then, mice were then housed under standard conditions and monitored periodically. Successful tumor formation was confirmed once the tumor volume reached 60–100 mm3.

Near infrared imaging in vivo

Male C57BL/6J mice were randomly divided into three groups: (1) Free IR820, (2) the ZIF-8@COD/IR820 group and (3) the HM/ZIF-8@ COD/IR820 group. In vivo imaging was performed at multiple time points using the IVIS Small Animal Real-Time Imaging System following tail vein injection, at an excitation wavelength of 820 nm. After the 1st, 2nd, 4th, 8th, 12th, 24th, 36th, 48th, and 72 nd h post-injection, mice were anesthetized and imaged at an excitation wavelength of 820 nm. The interval between nanoparticle administrations was based on the estimated nanoparticle metabolism clearance rate in the mice. Ex vivo imaging of major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, and tumor) were conducted at the 12 h time point, corresponding to the peak fluorescence intensity observed in vivo.

In vivo tumor therapy effect

Male C57BL/6J mice were randomly divided into five groups (n = 5 per group): saline group, HM/ZIF-8 group, HM/ZIF-8@IR820+US group, HM/ZIF-8@COD group, and HM/ZIF-8@IR820/COD + US group. Mice in each group receiver intravenous injections of the corresponding nanoparticles. Body weight, tumor size and the tumor images were recorded at regular intervals. For groups receiving ultrasound treatment, ultrasonication was performed 12 h post-injection at a power of 1 W/cm2 for 60 s. Tumor volume was calculated using the following formula: volume = width2 × length/2. And tumor suppression rate (%) was determined using the following formula: Tumor suppression rate (%) = (W0 - W)/W0, where W represents the tumor weight of the nanoparticle groups, and W0 represents the average tumor weight of the saline group.

During the final treatment, the mice were injected with pimonidazole and sonicated 2 h later, and sacrificed 1 h thereafter. Heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, and tumor tissues were then harvested. Each organ and tumor tissue were weighed using an electronic balance, and the organ index was calculated as follows: organ index = organ weight (g)/mouse body weight (g). H&E staining and immunofluorescence assays (different markers from the same sample) were performed by Servicebio (Wuhan China).

Metabolism experiments

Hepa1-6 tumor-bearing mice were established and divided into two groups: a saline group and a treatment with HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle combined SDT treatment group, with five mice in each group. Mice in the treatment group were intravenously injected with HM/ZIF-8@COD/IR820 nanoparticle (1 mg/mL) via the tail vein. The mice underwent ultrasound treatment at a power of 1 W/cm2 for 60 s after 12 h post-injection. After two treatment cycles, the mice were sacrificed, and the tumor tissues were immediately harvested and frozen in liquid nitrogen. Subsequently, non-targeted metabolomic sequencing was conducted by Cosmos Wisdom Company (China).

Statistical analysis

All data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism software. The differences between any two groups were compared using a two-tailed unpaired Student's t-test, while multiple groups comparisons were assessed using one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey's multiple comparisons test. Grouped data were assessed using two-way ANOVA with Dunnett's multiple comparisons test. All results were presented as mean ± SD. ∗p < 0.05, ∗∗p < 0.01, ∗∗∗p < 0.001, ∗∗∗∗p < 0.0001 indicating statistical significance.
