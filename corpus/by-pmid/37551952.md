# Application of natural killer immunotherapy in blood cancers and solid tumors.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE OF REVIEW**: Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphoid cells characterized by their ability to attack aberrant and cancerous cells. In contrast to the activation of T-cells, NK cell activation is controlled by the interaction of NK cell receptors and their target cells in a manner independent of antigen organization. Due to NK cells' broad array of activation cues, they have gained great attention as a potential therapeutic agent in cancer immunotherapy.

**RECENT FINDINGS**: Ex vivo activation, expansion, and genetic modifications, such as the addition of a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR), will allow the next generation of NK cells to enhance cytotoxicity, promote survival, and create "off-the-shelf" products. In addition to these that are poised to greatly enhance their clinical activity, the inherent lack of potential for causing graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) and cytokine release syndrome (CRS) suggest that CAR NK cells have the potential to be complementary to CAR-T cells as a component of therapeutic strategies for cancer.

**SUMMARY**: In this review, we will provide a general understanding of NK cell biology, CAR-NK cell advantages over CAR-T cell therapy, barriers to making NK cell immunotherapy viable, and current NK cell clinical trials for hematological malignancies and solid tumors. The next generation of NK cells has potential to change the circumstances guiding present cancer immunotherapies.

Application of natural killer immunotherapy in blood cancers and solid tumors

Purpose of Review

Natural Killer (NK) cells are innate lymphoid cells characterized by their ability to attack aberrant and cancerous cells. In contrast to the activation of T-cells, NK cell activation is controlled by the interaction of NK cell receptors and their target cells in a manner independent of antigen organization. Due to NK cells’ broad array of activation cues, they have gained great attention as a potential therapeutic agent in cancer immunotherapy.

Recent Findings

Ex vivo activation, expansion, and genetic modifications, such as the addition of a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR), will allow the next generation of NK cells to enhance cytotoxicity, promote survival, and create “off-the-shelf” products. In addition to these that are poised to greatly enhance their clinical activity, the inherent lack of potential for causing graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) and cytokine release syndrome (CRS) suggest that CAR NK cells have the potential to be complementary to CAR-T cells as a component of therapeutic strategies for cancer.

Summary

In this review, we will provide a general understanding of NK cell biology, CAR-NK cell advantages over CAR-T cell therapy, barriers to making NK cell immunotherapy viable, and current NK cell clinical trials for hematological malignancies and solid tumors. The next generation of NK cells has potential to change the circumstances guiding present cancer immunotherapies.

Introduction

The field of natural killer (NK) cell-based immunotherapy of cancer is at an exciting upward trend, showing promising results as a clinically viable therapeutic agent to help defeat cancer. Due to the remarkable success of chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cells in patients, CAR-NK cells have raised great interest as a potential clinical application that could sidestep the limitations of CAR-T cell therapy. Currently, there are about three main issues regarding CAR-T cell therapy that are drawing major investigations: constructing methods to enhance anti-tumor efficacy and limit antigen escape, finding suitable strategies to improve in vivo persistence and tumor infiltration, and mitigating toxic side effects, such as cytokine release syndrome (CRS), associated with CAR-T cell therapy.

NK cells can detect and destroy malignant cells without the need of prior sensitization due to their utilization of germline-encoded receptors. NK cells are found in peripheral blood, lymphoid organs and nonlymphoid organs such as the lungs and liver at levels of 5–15% of lymphocytes in the peripheral blood. NK cells are classified into two groups: CD56bright CD16– and CD56dim CD16+. CD56dim CD16+ cells are believed to be mature and more cytotoxic while CD56bright CD16– cells are the adolescent, but more potent cytokine producing cells. Once NK cells are activated, both subsets of NK cells release an abundance of cytotoxic granules that directly lyse cancerous cells. Additionally, NK cells recruit other immune cells through the production of chemokines, such as C–C motif chemokine ligand 1 CCL-1, CCL-2, etc.. Cytokines such as interleukin (IL)-2, IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, and IL-21 have been shown to activate and boost NK cell function (▪). Even though all these cytokines show promising results to activate NK cells, IL-15 stands out amongst the crowd. To enhance NK cells’ survival, persistence, and activation in vivo, researchers have genetically engineered soluble or membrane-bound IL-15 into NK cells.

Due to NK cells’ robust innate ability to directly kill cancer cells, their safety profile as a viable clinical therapeutic agent has been investigated for years. As shown in early clinical trials, even in an allogenic environment, NK cell infusion exhibits a safe profile. In contrast to T-cells, NK cells lack surface T-cell receptors (TCRs) and have been shown not to cause GVHD in pioneering studies from the last two decades. As a result of these observations, NK cells have great potential to become an “off-the-shelf” cancer immunotherapy, capable of being expanded, enhanced, and optimized to volumes of patients in fast turnaround times. In this review, we provide a summary of the significant advantages of CAR-NK cell therapy, explicate how we are overcoming barriers to make NK cell-based therapy viable, and highlight current NK cell-based clinical trials for hematological malignancies and solid tumors.

CAR-NK cells pick up where CAR-T cells left off

Even though CAR-T cell-based therapy has widened the horizon of precision-based medicine and progressed substantially in the last decade, there still remain a few major challenges, which include but are not limited to: (i) it takes a substantial amount of time to generate a therapeutic dose of T-cells, which can create ambivalent circumstances for patients with quickly progressing disorders; (ii) it is difficult to obtain enough T-cells from immunocompromised patients, and (iii) majority of patients who receive CAR-T cell therapy experience CRS. As an alternative, CAR-NK cell therapy not only bypasses these challenges, but also presents its own advantages. CAR-NK cells pick up where CAR-T cells leave off. The major advantages are: (i) scientists can utilize allogeneic sources of NK cells without the worry of the GVHD effect; (ii) ability to pursue “off-the-shelf” potential via several sources such as peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), cord blood, and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs); (iii) ability to kill cancer cells using native NK cell receptors; and (iv) no HLA matching restriction. Beyond the list of advantages stated above, CAR-NK cell therapy provides a new method to help treat solid tumors, a boundary of cancer in which CAR-T cell therapy has not successfully crossed. For example, prostate stem cell antigen-directed CAR NK cells have shown promising therapeutic efficacy for pancreatic cancer in a preclinical study. Engineered CAR-NK cells targeting CD44, a marker abundantly expressed by ovarian cancer cells, showed potent cytotoxicity against CD44-positive ovarian cancer cells. In lung adenocarcinoma, CAR-NK92 cells targeting mesenchymal-epithelial transition factor (C-Met) showed promising results in lung xenograft models. These experiments provide insight into the bright future of utilizing engineered CAR-NK cells as a solid tumor cancer therapeutic.

Although NK cells have immense potential and have shown various advantages over T-cell therapy, there are disadvantages to NK cell therapy that need to be discussed: (i) difficult to modify, especially with frozen cells; (ii) if the cells are derived from iPSCs or cord blood, they often express low levels of CD16 which is a sign of decreased antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity; (iii) short in vivo lifespan; (iv) low viability after being defrosted from cryopreservation; (v) NK cell exhaustion; and (vi) high chance of inhibition by inhibitory receptors. These are the hurdles that current investigators are attempting to jump. In the next section, we discuss current strategies to enhance, prime, and expand NK cells as depicted in Fig. 1.

Overcoming the Viability Barriers of NK Cell-Based Immunotherapy:

New Experimental Activating Strategies

Research has shown that resting NK cells, compared to “primed”, are generally less effective and lytic against aberrant cells. Even though engineering a chimeric antigen receptor towards a specific target has shown success, there are active investigations in discovering other strategies, ex vivo and in vivo, to effectively “prime” NK cells for optimal tumor cell killing after infusion. Cytokine-mediated in vivo activation of NK cells, for example, with a combination of IL-2 and IL-12, has been extensively studied for the past two decades. Moreover, a combination of IL-12, IL-15, and IL-18 can generate cytokine-induced memory-like properties (CIML) in NK cells. A study by Cooper et al., reported that rested NK cells stimulated by this combination of cytokines demonstrated higher IFN-γ production following cytokine restimulation, compared to non-preactivated NK cells. In a clinical setting, CIML-NK cells have shown promise. A group of researchers discovered that CIML-NK cells persisted for at least two months after HCT in patients in leukemia. Even though years of research has shown that cytokines are effective at priming NK cells, new research has sparked interest in other creative methods. For example, activation of STING, a stimulator of interferon genes, effectively primes NK and CAR-NK cells. Additionally, another group of researchers found that activating STING, using cGAMP, enhanced anti-tumor activity of CAR-NK cells against pancreatic cancer. This is just one of many “non-cytokine” related inducing experiments being conducted. Overall, “priming” strategies are experimental and diverse, but as more publications roll through, we are gaining a better understanding of the abundant discoveries that show various methods to activate NK cells.

Enhancing Antitumor Function and Tumor Infiltration

NK cell-based immunotherapy is an attractive cancer defeating strategy, but effectiveness in clinical trials has yet to be clearly elucidated. To enhance the function of NK cell therapy in vivo, researchers are investigating experimental methods to enhance anti-tumor functions by amplifying cell lysing functions and increasing tumor infiltration. There are several brain stimulating experiments conducted recently that open the boundaries of how NK cells can be enhanced. In a recent study conducted by Chockley et al., they investigated the effects of tuning CAR immune synapses by adding an intracellular scaffolding protein site to the receptor. With the addition of a PDZ binding motif, they found that this design increased effector cell functionality in vivo and ex vivo. The tuned CAR-NK cells showed enhanced cytokine secretion, tumor cell killing, and cell survival. This experiment is just one example of how the addition of a molecule, in this case a scaffolding protein, can increase NK cell function, but other studies are keen to explore the functions of other genes on NK cell efficacy.

Another exciting outlet to increase NK cell anti-tumor activity is investigating the inhibition of glycogen synthase kinase-3 (GSK3). Inhibition of GSK-3, via TWS119, increased primary NK cell cytokine secretion, degranulation activity, and cellular toxicity when exposed to myeloma cell lines. More recently, a study reported how to enhance CAR NK cell activity by overcoming trogocytosis-mediated fratricide. CAR activation in NK cells promoted the transfer of the CAR cognate antigen from tumor to NK cells, resulting in: (i) lower tumor antigen density, inhibiting the ability of CAR-NK cells to attach with their target, (ii) NK cell hyporesponsiveness, and (iii) fratricide of trogocytic antigen-expressing NK cells. Furthermore, they recognized that killer-cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) based inhibitory CARs overcome CAR-NK cell trogocytosis-mediated fratricide and tumor escape. This research provides a pathway into exploring KIR based experiments to enhance NK cell effector functions. More recently, a group utilized tumor penetrating peptides (iRGD) to deliver activated CIML NK cells, stimulated by IL12/IL15/IL18 combination, deeply into tumor cells. They concluded that the addition of iRGD enhanced infiltration and targeted destruction of multicellular spheroids and that iRGD-modified CIML NK cells effectively destroyed hepatocellular carcinoma cells. Consequently, research has shown that IL12/IL-15/IL-18-preactivated NK cells have great potential to be a part of long-term cancer immunotherapy. Overall, there are many investigations aimed at understanding how we can increase NK cell function in vivo, but until more clinical trials are administered it is hard to gauge the viability of each method.

NK Cell Survival Related to Exhaustion

Studying survival pathways related to NK cells is imperative to pursuing NK cells as an “off-the-shelf” therapeutic method for generations to come. For over twenty years, researchers have been continuously building upon previous discoveries in understanding the general mechanisms by which NK cells survive in vivo. As we continue to study the pathways involved in NK cell survival, another topic has arisen that is critical to keeping NK cells functionally active: NK cell exhaustion. NK cell exhaustion is caused by chronic exposure to activating stimuli during viral infection, tumorigenesis, and prolonged cytokine exposure. As of right now, compared to T-cell exhaustion investigations, not many recent studies have been conducted to fully understand NK cell exhaustion and how to reverse this process. A study orchestrated by Meyers et al., provided novel insights into the underlying mechanisms of NK cell exhaustion in humans. Firstly, they provided concluding evidence that overstimulating activating receptors NKp46 and NKG2D induces a definitive exhaustion phenotype: overexpression of TIGIT, TIM-3, and LAG-3 markers. Secondly, they discovered that by simultaneously engaging NKp46, NKG2D, and NKG2A (an inhibitory receptor of NK cells), declines in cytokine production and cytotoxicity were mitigated. These results suggest that a balance of positive and negative signals can mitigate NK cell exhaustion.

Lately, the status quo of reversing NK cell exhaustion has been targeting immune checkpoints. Researchers have stated that blocking immune checkpoints not only rescues NK cells from exhaustion but also increases their robust anti-tumor functions. Deleting TIPE2 and CISH, two intracellular checkpoint genes, had a synergistic effect in preventing NK cell exhaustion as well as improving anti-tumor activity. Previous research studies have shown that disrupting the PD-1 pathway induces the production of cytokines, which may in turn increase tumor infiltration. Encountering tumor cells or being stimulated by cytokine combinations such as IL-12 plus IL-18, NK cells can also express programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), while PD-L1 expression marks a highly activated subset of NK cells. The PD-L1 expressed on NK cells has the potential to inhibit T cells by binding to PD-1 on T cells, thus inhibiting PD-L1 expression on tumor cells and NK cells may have great potential to overcome T-cell exhaustion. Currently, there are several clinical trials including ours (NCT05069935, NCT04143711, NCT05334329, NCT05597839) deploying NK cell therapy in combination with immune check point inhibitors, including anti-PD-1 or anti-PD-L1 in the forms of atezolizumab, avelumab, and durvalumab. For example, Wang et al., reported that the combined treatment of anti-prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) CAR NK-92 and anti-PD-L1 monoclonal antibody enhances the antitumor efficacy against prostate cancer.

Defeating the Suppressive Tumor Microenvironment

Apart from the already stated ability of CAR-NK cells to target tumor antigens and lyse cells using their cytotoxicity, researchers have seen intriguing results regarding elimination of immunosuppressive immune cells by NK cells. Human NK cells bearing a chimeric receptor in which the activating receptor NKG2D is fused to the cytotoxic ζ-chain of the T-cell receptor (NKG2D.ζ) can eliminate NKG2D ligand-expressing myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and improve GD2 CAR-T cell tumor infiltration. This study provides awareness on how CAR-NK cells can be utilized not only to defeat the immunosuppressive effects of the tumor microenvironment (TME), but also in combination with CAR-T cells to achieve optimal results. Additionally to the fact that CAR-NK cells can be directed towards killing immunosuppressive immune cells, CAR-NK cells also have the innate ability to maintain high cytotoxic function in the presence of immunosuppressive factors enriched in solid tumors, such as prostaglandin E2 and transforming growth factor-β. Additionally, a side-by-side comparison study showed that HER2 CAR-NK cells demonstrate greater cytotoxic function than HER2 CAR-T cells against HER2-positive target cells in vitro. With continued research, we can get a further grasp on how NK cells can be extremely effective in penetrating the tumor microenvironment. Overall, NK cells have shown potential to overcome the immunosuppressive TME that has been challenging therapeutic agents in recent years.

Current NK Cell-Based Clinical Trials for Hematological Malignancies

The safety of NK cell infusion therapy for hematological malignancies has been well established throughout the years. With new efforts to expand, enhance, and optimize NK cell-based immunotherapy, various clinical trials have arisen to evaluate these new advancements. Currently, there are a great number of clinical trials deploying NK cells to treat hematological malignancies. A brand new phase I clinical trial (NCT05580601) is aiming to study the effects of CIML NK cell infusions on patients with relapsed or refractory acute myeloid leukemia. The goal of this treatment is to achieve remission and proceed to hematopoietic stem cell transplant. Furthermore, there are six other on-going clinical trials (NCT02782546, NCT03068819, NCT04024761, NCT04354025, NCT04634435, and NCT04893915, from clinicaltrials.gov) that are utilizing CIML NK cells in different hematological malignancies such as acute myeloid leukemia and multiple myeloma. A recent study reported that four out of six patients with relapsed myeloid diseases after haploidentical hematopoietic cell transplantation infused with CIML NK cells showed positive responses and that three patients achieved complete remission. Moreover, a clinical trial (NCT03056339) conducted at M.D Anderson Cancer Center aimed at utilizing CD19-targeted CAR-NK cell therapy in patients with B-lymphoid malignancies is showing exciting results. The treatment was cord blood-derived NK cells expressing IL-15 after lymphodepleting chemotherapy with cyclophosphamide and for patients with relapsed/refractory CD19+ B lymphoid malignancies. Preliminary data from the clinical trial showed that eight out of eleven patients showed positive responses to treatment without developing major adverse effects.

Current NK Cell-Based Clinical Trials for Solid Tumors

On top of clinical trials aiming to investigate hematological malignancies, there are a great number of ongoing clinical trials related to the use of NK cells to target solid tumors. A clinical trial underway (NCT05334329) at City of Hope National Medical Center in collaboration with CytoImmune is related to the use of NK cell therapy, colloquially termed “CYTO-102”, for the potential treatment of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). “CYTO-102” is a brand-new NK cell-based immunotherapy based on tumor-reactive and anti-PD-L1 co-stimulated killer cells (TRACK-NK). These TRACK-NK cells, primed with IL-12 and IL-18 during manufacturing, are genetically modified to secrete high levels of IL-15 with PD-L1 expression. In turn, these modified NK cells are naturally directed toward the tumor microenvironment to kill cancer cells and recruit other adaptive immune cells. Furthermore, a clinical trial just getting underway (NCT05410717) is utilizing CAR-NK cells to target Claudin6-positive advanced solid tumors (ovarian, testis, and endometrial cancer). These CAR-NK cells are genetically engineered to express and secrete IL-7/CCL19 against immune checkpoints such as PD-1. There are several clinical trials (NCT05194709, NCT04319757, NCT05528341) with engineered CAR-NK cells to target HER2, NKG2D, and 5T4 oncofoetal antigen in hopes of treating advanced solid tumors. Besides the use of CAR to attack solid tumors, scientists from the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute are preparing to employ CIML NK cells to target advanced head and neck cancer (NCT04290546). These are just a few of many ongoing clinical trials that are expanding the horizon of how NK cells can extend the status quo of cancer immunotherapy. With a quick search on clinicaltrials.gov, one can see for themselves the plethora of solid tumor and hematological malignancy clinical trials that NK cells are spearheading.

Conclusions and Perspectives

NK cells are innate lymphoid cells characterized by their ability to attack aberrant and cancerous cells in a quick and potent manner. The broad theory of utilizing these innate immune cells in cancer immunotherapy has been investigated for decades in labs and clinical trials. With new understandings regarding expansion, enhancement, and optimization, it is time to explore NK cells as an alternative to T-cell therapy. Although current challenges, such as NK cell exhaustion, exist that haven’t been fully understood, emerging pre-clinical and clinical studies are set to elucidate these concerns, catapulting NK cell therapy to one of the top immunotherapy options.
