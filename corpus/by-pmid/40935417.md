# Neoadjuvant stereotactic body radiotherapy combined with chemotherapy and Ivonescimab for Chinese luminal-type breast cancer patients: study protocol for a single-arm, open-label, phase II trial.

## Abstract

**INTRODUCTION**: Immunotherapy combined with chemotherapy has shown potential in improving the pathological complete response (pCR) rate in luminal-type breast cancer. This study explores whether the addition of stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) and the bispecific antibody AK112 (Ivonescimab) further enhances treatment efficacy.

**METHODS AND ANALYSIS**: This is a single-centre, prospective, phase II trial using Simon's two-stage design to evaluate the efficacy and safety of neoadjuvant SBRT combined with Ivonescimab and chemotherapy. A total of 50 patients will be enrolled. The primary endpoint is pCR. Secondary endpoints include objective response rate, disease control rate, Residual Cancer Burden Index, 12-month event-free survival, safety and quality of life. Exploratory endpoints include six-point minimal residual disease (MRD) assessment. Data will be analysed using the Kaplan-Meier method and one-sided exact binomial test (alpha=0.05).

**ETHICS AND DISSEMINATION**: This study has been approved by the ethics committee of Hubei Cancer Hospital (Approval No.: LCKY2024011). Results will be disseminated through peer-reviewed journals and conference presentations.

**TRIAL REGISTRATION NUMBER**: NCT06402435.

STRENGTHS AND LIMITATIONS OF THIS STUDY

The trial uses a Simon’s two-stage phase II design to assess early treatment efficacy while minimising patient exposure.

Neoadjuvant therapy model combines stereotactic body radiotherapy, chemotherapy and Ivonescimab for patients with HR+/HER2− breast cancer.

Minimal residual disease is measured at six predefined time points to explore its utility in treatment monitoring.

This is merely a phase II single-arm clinical study. Compared with phase III large-scale randomised controlled trials, its primary limitations stem from the ‘absence of concurrent controls’ and ‘inadequate sample representativeness’.

Introduction

 Neoadjuvant therapy (NAT) has been widely used for the treatment of certain locally advanced breast cancer cases (T3-4, N+) or for patients seeking tumour downstaging to enable breast-conserving surgery. Previous studies have demonstrated a positive correlation between pathological complete response (pCR) following NAT and improved overall survival (OS). For hormone receptor (HR)-positive (luminal-type) breast cancer, prior research has reported a pCR rate of 7–14% after neoadjuvant chemotherapy. Consequently, enhancing the pCR rate following NAT has become a key focus of clinical research. The advent of immunotherapy has provided new hope for improving the efficacy of neoadjuvant treatment in locally advanced breast cancer and ultimately enhancing long-term survival outcomes.

Recent studies have also shown that integrating immunotherapy with chemotherapy as a novel neoadjuvant treatment strategy may further improve the pCR rate in luminal-type breast cancer patients undergoing NAT. The KEYNOTE-756 trial demonstrated that, compared with chemotherapy alone, the addition of pembrolizumab to neoadjuvant chemotherapy increased the pCR rate from 15.6% to 24.3%. Similarly, in the CheckMate 7FL study, a neoadjuvant regimen combining nivolumab with chemotherapy significantly improved the pCR rate compared with chemotherapy alone (24.5% vs 13.8%, p=0.0021). Importantly, both trials demonstrated efficacy mainly in PD-L1-expressing tumours and the Estrogen Receptor (ER)-low population. In KEYNOTE-756, a numerically higher rate of pCR difference was observed with higher tumour PD-L1 expression. The estimated treatment differences in the prespecified subgroups based on PD-L1 Combined Positive Score of <1, ≥1, ≥10 and ≥20 were 4.6 (7.2 vs 2.6), 10.1 (29.7 vs 19.6), 13.3 (42.3 vs 29.0) and 17.2 (53.6 vs 36.4) percentage points, respectively. Additionally, pCR benefit for pembrolizumab-chemotherapy was observed both in patients with ER positivity <10% and ≥10%, although with a greater magnitude among those with ER positivity <10%. Among patients with ER positivity <10%, the pCR rate was 55.9% in the pembrolizumab arm versus 30.2% in the placebo arm, whereas among patients with ER positivity ≥10%, the pCR was 22.5% vs 14.5%. In CheckMate 7FL, the pCR rate among patients with PD-L1 ≥1% tumours was 44.3% with nivolumab arm versus 20.2% with placebo arm, while among PD-L1-negative tumours (<1%), the benefit was minimal (14.2% vs 10.7%). Similarly, tumours with low oestrogen receptor expression (<50%) were more likely to respond to treatment, indicating enhanced sensitivity in the ER-low subgroup. These findings suggest that immunotherapy combined with chemotherapy can effectively enhance pCR rates in selected luminal-type breast cancer patients, particularly those with PD-L1 positivity or lower ER expression and support its further exploration as a biomarker-driven neoadjuvant strategy in this population.

Furthermore, radiotherapy, particularly stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT), has been shown to enhance the release of tumour neoantigens, thereby not only improving local control but also potentially augmenting the efficacy of immunotherapy through the abscopal effect. This concept has been supported by early studies in non-small cell lung cancer, where SBRT combined with immunotherapy and chemotherapy achieved markedly high pCR rates. These promising results raise the question of whether this SBRT + immunotherapy + chemotherapy approach could be successfully applied to breast cancer patients, warranting further investigation.

Ivonescimab (AK112) is a bispecific antibody targeting PD-1 and VEGF-A, designed to simultaneously inhibit PD-1-mediated immune suppression and VEGF-induced tumour angiogenesis, thereby enhancing immune response and improving the tumour microenvironment. A phase I/II study conducted by Jermaine et al evaluated the safety, pharmacokinetics and antitumour activity of Ivonescimab in patients with advanced solid tumours, enrolling 42 participants who received Ivonescimab monotherapy at doses ranging from 1.0 mg/kg to 30 mg/kg Q2W. The study demonstrated good tolerability, with no dose-limiting toxicities or treatment-related deaths at 30 mg/kg Q2W. The most common treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) (≥5%) included arthralgia (19%), fatigue (14.3%), hypertension (11.9%), diarrhoea (11.9%), rash (11.9%), pruritus (7.1%) and headache (7.1%), with grade ≥3 TRAEs observed in 19% of patients, primarily hypertension (9.5%), colitis (2.4%), liver dysfunction (4.8%) and stomatitis (2.4%). Among the 27 patients at doses ≥3 mg/kg who were eligible for efficacy evaluation, 6 patients achieved partial response, with an objective response rate (ORR) of 22.2% and a disease control rate (DCR) of 74.1%, indicating promising antitumour activity with favourable safety and tolerability. Subsequently, in the Chinese Ib/II non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) study (NCT04900363), Ivonescimab monotherapy was assessed for efficacy and safety in first-line or second-line treatment of advanced NSCLC, confirming its antitumour activity across different PD-L1 expression levels. The study by Zhang et al further explored its combination with chemotherapy in NSCLC, demonstrating manageable safety and superior efficacy compared with monotherapy. Additionally, a new study evaluated Ivonescimab in combination with carboplatin and etoposide for first-line treatment of extensive-stage small cell lung cancer (SCLC), achieving an ORR of 80%, a DCR of 91.4% and a median follow-up of 13.3 months, highlighting strong antitumour activity with favourable safety and tolerability. Overall, Ivonescimab has shown promising safety and efficacy in both NSCLC and SCLC, supporting its further clinical development and validation.

Our centre has previously conducted preclinical animal studies and prospective clinical research, demonstrating that SBRT combined with immunotherapy and anti-angiogenic therapy significantly enhances antitumour efficacy, both in primary and metastatic lesions. Additionally, we have explored this approach in three luminal-type breast cancer patients. Among them, one patient (cTumour3Node0Metastasis0) achieved pCR after neoadjuvant treatment with SBRT, anti-PD-1 immunotherapy and chemotherapy, followed by surgery. The other two patients exhibited significant tumour regression during the NAT. Postoperative pathological evaluation was performed using the Residual Cancer Burden (RCB) Index, which quantifies the extent of residual disease after neoadjuvant treatment. The results indicated RCB classifications of RCB-1 and RCB-2 for the two patients, respectively. For reference, the RCB score is a continuous variable categorised into four classes: RCB-0 (score=0, equivalent to pCR), RCB-1 (score 0–1.36), RCB-2 (score 1.37–3.28) and RCB-3 (score>3.28), reflecting increasing levels of residual disease burden. Based on these findings, we plan to conduct a single-centre, prospective, exploratory clinical study to evaluate the safety and efficacy of SBRT combined with AK112 and chemotherapy as a neoadjuvant treatment strategy for luminal-type breast cancer. This study aims to provide a theoretical foundation for future clinical applications.

Methods and analysis

Objectives

This phase II clinical study aims to evaluate the safety and efficacy of SBRT combined with chemotherapy and Ivonescimab as a NAT for luminal-type breast cancer. The primary objective is to improve the pCR rate, as assessed by the pathology department based on the AJCC staging system (ypT0/Tis, ypN0), thereby enhancing progression-free survival and OS outcomes. Secondary endpoints include ORR and DCR according to Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumours (RECIST) version 1.1 and Immune Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumours (iRECIST), RCB score, 12-month event-free survival (EFS), safety and tolerability assessments, quality of life evaluation using the European Organization for Research and Treatment of Cancer (EORTC) Quality of Life Questionnaire (QLQ) breast cancer–specific module (QLQ-BR23) and the core questionnaire (QLQ-C30). Exploratory endpoints involve six assessments of minimal residual disease (MRD) at baseline, after four and eight cycles of NAT and at 1 month, 6 months and 1 year post surgery.

Enrolment criteria

This trial aims to enrol patients who voluntarily participate and provide written informed consent. Eligible participants must have histologically confirmed, treatment-naïve, HR+/HER2-negative breast cancer (HER2 immunohistochemistry 0, 1+, or 2+/Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (FISH)-). Patients must meet at least one of the following criteria: (1) tumour size >2 cm, (2) axillary lymph node metastasis or (3) intent for breast-conserving surgery, but tumour-to-breast volume ratio makes preservation challenging. Additional eligibility requirements include age ≥18 years, an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0–1 and baseline laboratory values within acceptable ranges: white blood cell count ≥2.0 × 10⁹/L, absolute neutrophil count ≥1.5 × 10⁹/L, platelet count ≥100 × 10⁹/L, haemoglobin ≥90 g/L. Liver function parameters must be within ≤1.5 × upper limit of normal (ULN) for total bilirubin and ≤3× ULN for alanine aminotransferase and aspartate aminotransferase. Renal function must be preserved, with serum creatinine (Cr) ≤1.5 × ULN or if Cr exceeds this limit, the Cr clearance rate should be ≥50 mL/min (calculated using the Cockcroft-Gault formula). Coagulation parameters should meet the following thresholds: activated partial thromboplastin time ≤1.5 × ULN and prothrombin time or international normalised ratio ≤1.5 × ULN.

Patients will be excluded if they have received chemotherapy, targeted therapy or radiotherapy within 12 months before the first dose of the investigational drug or have undergone solid organ or haematologic transplantation. Other exclusion criteria include a history of myocardial infarction or uncontrolled arrhythmias (Corrected QT interval (QTc) ≥470 ms by Fridericia’s formula) within 6 months before the first dose, NYHA class III–IV heart failure, left ventricular ejection fraction <50%, or uncontrolled hypertension (systolic blood pressure (BP) ≥150 mm Hg and/or diastolic BP≥100 mm Hg). Patients with active HIV, tuberculosis, interstitial lung disease, severe pulmonary impairment or autoimmune diseases requiring systemic immunosuppression will also be excluded. Participants must not have received a live vaccine within 28 days prior to the first dose, though inactivated influenza vaccines are permitted. Patients requiring systemic corticosteroids (>10 mg/day prednisone equivalent) or immunosuppressants within 14 days before the first dose will be excluded, except for short-term or low-dose corticosteroids, localised applications and adrenal replacement therapy. Additionally, exclusion applies to patients with active infections requiring systemic therapy within 14 days prior to enrolment, hepatitis B or C with detectable viral DNA/RNA, history of prior treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1 or anti-CTLA-4 antibodies), participation in another clinical trial within 14 days, major surgery within 4 weeks before the first dose (except for biopsy procedures), severe allergic reactions to monoclonal antibodies or study drug components, pregnancy or lactation, active psychiatric disorders or substance abuse history. Patients who have ceased alcohol consumption may be included. Finally, investigators may exclude participants based on any other conditions deemed inappropriate for study participation.

Treatment plan

This study adopts a Simon’s two-stage design to evaluate the efficacy of SBRT combined with chemotherapy and Ivonescimab as a neoadjuvant treatment for HR+/HER2- (luminal-type) breast cancer. The study aims to increase the pCR rate from 24% to 35%, requiring a total of 50 patients. In stage 1, 30 patients will be enrolled, and if ≥seven achieve pCR, an additional 20 patients will be recruited. If >14 patients achieve pCR overall, the study will be considered successful, whereas if ≤six patients achieve pCR in stage 1, the trial will be terminated. Patients will receive eight cycles of neoadjuvant chemotherapy and immunotherapy, with SBRT administered before the first cycle. For patients without axillary lymph node metastasis, the primary tumour will receive 8 Gy×3 fractions, while for those with axillary involvement, the primary tumour and lymph nodes will receive 6 Gy×3 fractions. The first cycle of chemotherapy+AK112 will be given within 24 hours after the end of SBRT. Chemotherapy will include doxorubicin (50 mg/m², Q3W) + cyclophosphamide (600 mg/m², Q3W) for four cycles, followed by nab-paclitaxel (125 mg/m², days one and eight) for another four cycles. Ivonescimab (20 mg/kg) will be administered every 3 weeks with chemotherapy for 24 weeks. Surgery will be performed 4–6 weeks the last neoadjuvant treatment cycle. In cases where indicated, patients received adjuvant RT to the breast, chest wall and lymph node regions in accordance with local standards of care for the fractionation schedule. RT boosts to the tumour bed were not permitted, as the preoperative RT was regarded as a planned boost. Adjuvant endocrine treatment was administered in accordance with standard of care protocols. Patients will be withdrawn if they experience severe adverse events (AEs), tumour progression (>25% increase in primary tumour and lymph nodes per RECIST criteria), voluntary withdrawal or investigator decision. This study aims to assess the safety and efficacy of SBRT combined with chemotherapy and Ivonescimab, potentially improving pCR rates and long-term outcomes for luminal-type breast cancer.

Follow-up

All enrolled patients underwent regular follow-up assessments, including physical examination, ECOG performance status evaluation, complete blood count, urinalysis, serum biochemistry (liver and renal function, electrolytes, cardiac enzymes), bilateral breast and axillary/supraclavicular ultrasonography, chest CT, abdominal CT or ultrasound, bone scan, cranial CT and/or MRI (if clinically indicated), ECG and echocardiography. The administration of investigational drugs, drug dispensing and return records, AEs and scheduling of the next visit were documented at each visit.

AEs were assessed by the treating oncologist throughout the trial and for 30 days after discontinuation of treatment (90 days for serious AEs) and graded according to Common Terminology Criteria for AEs (CTCAE), V.5.0. Assessment of immune-related AEs will be performed at 2-cycle intervals throughout the chemoimmunotherapy regimen. Follow-up visits were scheduled every 2–3 months in the first year until the completion of systemic therapy. In the second year, follow-up visits were conducted every 4–6 months and every 6–8 months thereafter. A trial participant timeline is presented in figure 1.

Exploratory research

The tumour tissue sample obtained from the initial biopsy will be subjected to PD-L1 and ER expression analysis. Concurrently, MRD detection technology will be employed for longitudinal monitoring of both the patient’s primary tissue sample (from the initial biopsy) and serial peripheral blood specimens. Monitoring time points encompass: pre-NAT, after completion of 4 cycles of NAT, after 8 cycles of NAT (preoperatively), 1 month postoperatively, 6 months postoperatively and 1 year postoperatively. This study aims to investigate PD-L1 positivity or lower ER expression as the potential biomarkers of therapeutic efficacy for this novel neoadjuvant treatment regimen and evaluate the feasibility of using MRD as a monitoring indicator for treatment response and disease recurrence through these analyses.

Statistical analysis

In two previous phase III clinical trials (KEYNOTE-756 and CheckMate 7FL), the neoadjuvant regimen combining chemotherapy with immunotherapy demonstrated an increase in pCR rate from 14% to 24% in luminal-type breast cancer compared with chemotherapy alone. In a preliminary phase I exploratory study, six patients with early-stage luminal B breast cancer received NAT consisting of SBRT, durvalumab, oleclumab (anti-CD73 monoclonal antibody) and chemotherapy. No grade 4 or 5 AEs were observed, with a pCR rate of 33.3% (2/6). Therefore, this study employs Simon’s two-stage design to assess whether the neoadjuvant regimen increases the pCR rate from 24% to 35%. The study began on 28 November 2024, with primary completion expected by 31 August 2026 and full completion anticipated by 1 September 2027. A total of 50 patients will be enrolled; if ≥ seven of the first 30 achieve pCR, the trial will proceed to stage II with an additional 20 patients. The study will be considered successful if more than 14 patients achieve pCR overall. The primary endpoint (pCR rate) will be reported with 95% CIs and assessed using a one-sided exact binomial test (α=0.05). Secondary endpoints, including the ORR, DCR according to RECIST v1.1 and iRECIST and 12 month EFS, will be descriptively analysed, with EFS estimated using the Kaplan-Meier method. Safety will be evaluated based on CTCAE V.5.0. Exploratory analysis of MRD will be performed at six time points to monitor dynamic changes and explore its potential predictive value.

Data management and monitoring

All study data will be collected using a standardised case report form and stored in a password-protected electronic database. Regular data monitoring will be conducted by the study coordinator to ensure completeness and accuracy in accordance with Good Clinical Practice guidelines.

Ethics and dissemination

Ethics approval for this study was granted by the ethics committee of Hubei Cancer Hospital on 21 May 2024 (Approval Number: LCKY2024011). This study will be conducted in accordance with the ethical principles outlined in the Declaration of Helsinki and the Declaration of Taipei. Written informed consent will be obtained from all study participants prior to enrolment. For patients who have the mental capacity to provide informed consent, their decision to participate will be respected and not overridden by family members. In cases where patients have impaired cognitive function, consent will be obtained from their legally authorised representatives in accordance with institutional and national ethical guidelines. The findings from this study will be disseminated through publications in peer-reviewed journals and presentations at national and international oncology conferences.
