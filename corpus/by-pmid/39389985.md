# Improved antitumor effectiveness of oncolytic HSV-1 viruses engineered with IL-15/IL-15Rα complex combined with oncolytic HSV-1-aPD1 targets colon cancer.

## Abstract

Oncolytic virotherapy is emerging as a promising therapeutic avenue for cancer treatment, harnessing both innate and tumor-specific immune responses for targeted tumor elimination. In this study, we present a novel oncolytic virus (oHSV1-IL15B) derived from herpes simplex virus-1 (HSV-1), armed with IL-15/IL-15Rα complex, with a focus on treating colon cancer combined with oncolytic HSV-1 expressing anti-PD-1 antibody (oHSV1-aPD1). Results from our study reveal that recombinant oHSV-1 virus equipped with IL-15/IL-15Rα complex exhibited significant anti-tumor effects in a murine CT26 colon adenocarcinoma model. Notably, oHSV1-IL15B combined with oHSV-1-aPD1 demonstrates superior tumor inhibition and prolonged overall survival compared to oHSV1-mock and monotherapy groups. Further exploration highlights the impact of oHSV1-IL15B, oHSV-1-aPD1 and combined group on antitumor capacity, revealing a substantial increase in CD8+ T and CD4+ T cell proportions of CT26-bearing BALB/c mice and promoting apoptosis in tumor tissue. The study emphasizes the pivotal role of cytotoxic CD8+T cells in oncolytic virotherapy, demonstrating that recombinant oHSV1-IL15B combined with oncolytic HSV-1-aPD1 induces a robust tumor-specific T cell response. RNA sequence analysis highlighted oHSV1-IL15B combined with oHSV1-aPD1 improved tumors immune microenvironment on immune response, antiviral response-related genes and apoptosis-related genes, which contributed to anti-tumor immunotherapy. The findings underscore the promising antitumor activity achieved through the combination of IL-15/IL-15Rα complex and anti-PD-1 antibody with oHSV-1. This research opens avenues for diverse therapeutic strategies, suggesting the potential of synergistically utilizing cytokines and anti-PD-1 antibody with oncolytic viruses to enhance immunotherapy for cancer management.

Introduction

Oncolytic virotherapy has garnered increasing interest as a novel therapeutic approach for human malignancies, leveraging replication-selective oncolytic viruses (OVs) and genetically engineered virus-based vectors. OVs are repurposed to selectively infect tumor cells, inducing direct tumor-killing effects. Notably, OVs can elicit antigen-specific antitumor immune responses by triggering immunogenic cell death (ICD) in infected tumor cells, while also mitigating the immunosuppressive nature of the tumor microenvironment. Local administration of OVs has demonstrated heightened immune cell infiltration, occasionally leading to tumor regression in distant untreated tumors via systemic antitumor immunity. The combination of oncolytic viruses with immune modulatory molecules, such as cytokines and costimulatory/inhibitory molecules, or other cancer treatments, is on the rise to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of oncolytic virotherapy.

Oncolytic viruses derived from herpes simplex virus-1 (oHSVs) exhibit substantial promise as a potential cancer treatment option. HSV-1’s broad oncolytic activity against human tumor cells and its capacity to accommodate large exogenous genes activate both innate and adaptive immunity, enhancing its anti-tumor activity. Oncolytic viruses show promise as a therapeutic modality for the treatment of solid tumors. With the recent FDA approval of talimogene laherpaprevec (T-VEC), the first oHSV-1 expressing GM-CSF for melanoma, several oHSVs have progressed to Phase I or II clinical trials as a potential treatment for various human malignancies.

In our prior studies, we constructed the recombinant virus VT09X and evaluated its antitumor effects in hPD-1 knock-in mice bearing non-immunogenic B16-F10 melanoma. The combination of VT09X and Keytruda demonstrated potent antitumor effects, significantly prolonging the survival rate of mice and enhancing CD8+ and CD4+ T cell infiltration, IFN-γ secretion, and PD-L1 expression in tumors. Subsequently, we generated a recombinant oHSV-1, HSV-aPD-1, with a humanized anti-PD-1 antibody gene inserted into VT09X, showing inhibition of tumor growth and improvement in the immune microenvironment. Two other recombinant viruses, VT1092M and VT1093M, expressing the mouse-derived IL-12 gene and mouse-derived anti-PD-1 antibody gene, were constructed using the VT09X vector and demonstrated significant anti-tumor effects in a murine CT26 colon adenocarcinoma model. Intratumoral combined injection with VT1092M and VT1093M inhibited the growth of the primary tumor, even in the contralateral untreated tumor. Both VT1092 (CTR20220801) expressing the human-derived IL-12 gene and VT1093 (CTR20210659) expressing human-derived anti-PD-1 antibody gene, entered clinical phase I studies to evaluate their clinical antitumor efficacy and safety in advanced solid tumor patients.

Interleukin-15 (IL-15) emerges as a key immunoregulatory cytokine with the ability to promote inflammatory responses. Expressed by various cell types and tissues, IL-15 plays a crucial role in innate and adaptive immunity, promoting the differentiation and expansion of T cells, B cells, and NK cells. Operating through the formation of a complex with IL-15 receptor subunit α (IL-15Rα), IL-15 exerts its effects via the trans-presentation mechanism to immune cells expressing the IL-2/IL-15Rβ-γc. The IL-15/IL-15Rα complex is recognized as a heterodimeric cytokine with applications in cancer and microbial infections. Combining IL-15 with IL-15Rα enhances IL-15 stability and secretion, facilitating improved trans-presentation to NK cells and CD8+ T cells, thereby enhancing the anti-tumor efficacy of IL-15. The Fc domain of IgG1 has proven effective in prolonging the biologic half-life of IL-15, binding specifically to NK and T cells. The IL-15 superagonist complex is now developed as a potent antitumor immunotherapeutic agent and is in clinical trials.

In this study, we aimed to engineer recombinant oncolytic HSV-1 viruses armed with the human IL-15/IL-15Rα complex with the Fc domain of IgG1 and explore their anti-colon cancer activities through in vitro and in vivo assays combined with oncolytic HSV-1-aPD1. We hypothesized that the combined use of oncolytic viruses engineered to express cytokines and those expressing PD1 antibodies may synergistically enhance the anti-tumor efficacy. Our results demonstrated that this combination therapy exerts a profound antitumor effect against CT26 colon adenocarcinoma-bearing BALB/c mice. Notably, the oHSV1-IL15B virus combined with oncolytic HSV-1-aPD1 induce an antitumoral immune response through the accumulation of leukocyte infiltration, underscoring its potential as a promising therapeutic approach.

Results

Construction and transgene expression of recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus

In the current study, oHSV1-mock was utilized to generate recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus (Fig. 1A). The human-derived IL-15 and IL-15Rα sushi domain DNA coding sequences with the Fc domain of IgG1, was inserted into the ICP34.5 locus of oHSV1-mock, resulting in oHSV1-IL15B (Fig. 1A). The confirmation of recombinant virus was achieved through PCR analysis (Fig. S1) and DNA sequencing, and the primers used are listed in Table S1.

Initially, we assessed the infectivity of recombinant viruses towards Vero and CT26 cells to evaluate the impact of transgenes on virus characteristics. Microscopy observation revealed that both recombinant viruses successfully infected Vero and CT26 cells (Fig. S2), resulting in the formation of viral plaques. Then, we compared the replication ability of the recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus in Vero cells. At 24 h post-viral transfection, oHSV1-mock exhibited superior replication capacity compared to oHSV1 viruses (P < 0.05), suggesting that the introduction of exogenous genes may temporarily weaken the virus’s proliferation ability. However, oHSV1-IL15B virus demonstrated slightly higher replication capacity than oHSV1-mock at 48 and 72 h post-viral transfection, suggesting that the introduction of exogenous genes did not impact the growth characteristics of the viruses (Fig. S3).

Then, we assessed the IL15 expression levels of recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus using ELISA. CT26 cells were infected with the recombinant virus (MOI = 0, 0.1, 1, 5, and 20) for 24, 48, and 72 h. The IL-15 expression demonstrated a linear increase with MOI ranging from 0 to 20, indicating normal exogenous gene expression in infected cells. Moreover, IL-15 expression at 72 h was significantly higher than that at 24–48 h (Fig. 1B).

In vitro cytotoxicity tests of oHSV1-IL15B virus

To assess the cytotoxic activity of recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus against tumor cells in vitro, CCK8 assays were employed to evaluate the proliferation of CT26 cells following virus infection. The results (Fig. 1C–E) indicated that both recombinant oHSV1 viruses and oHSV1-mock exhibited effectiveness against CT26 cells. The cytolytic capacity against CT26 cells displayed a direct proportional relationship between the dose and time. Notably, at an MOI of 1, oHSV1-IL15B demonstrated a significantly more potent cytolytic capacity against CT26 cells compared to the oHSV1-mock group (p < 0.001) and oHSV1-aPD1(p < 0.01). These findings suggest that recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus may be effective against colon cancer cell line.

Antitumor efficacy of oHSV1-IL15B virus combined with oHSV1-aPD1 virus in vivo

Subsequently, we conducted an in vivo study to assess the anti-tumor effects of recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus and combined treatment with oHSV1-aPD1 in a murine model of colon carcinoma CT26. BALB/c mice were subcutaneously injected with CT26 cells. After tumor volume reached 100∼150 mm3, tumor-bearing mice were randomly assigned into five groups and underwent six rounds of treatment with 1 × 107 PFU of oHSV1-mock, oHSV1-IL15B, oHSV1-aPD1, oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1, and the vehicle (Fig. 2A). Tumor volumes were measured every 3 days. The results demonstrated that both recombinant oHSV1-IL15B group (P < 0.01) and oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 group (P < 0.001) significantly inhibited tumor growth after 11 days’ treatment compared to the vehicle group (Fig. 2B). Notably, the oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 group exhibited stronger inhibitory effects than the oHSV1-mock group (P < 0.05). The results indicate that the recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus and combined treatment possess antitumor functionality and demonstrate more promising efficacy.

Furthermore, we analyzed the 50-day survival and monitored tumor growth for each group (n = 5 mice). In the vehicle control group, mice began to succumb on days 16, with a 100% mortality rate by days 22. Mice in the oHSV1-mock group became moribund from days 22, whereas in the other groups, mortality occurred at days 25. Compared to the negative vehicle group, all the oHSVs groups exhibited shrinking tumors and significantly prolonged the overall survival of mice (P < 0.01) (Fig. 2C,D). Importantly, the overall survival of mice in the oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 treatment group was significantly prolonged compared to the oHSV1-mock group (P < 0.05) and even with one mouse in oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 group showing complete tumor regression at the end of the survival period (Fig. 2C,D). Additionally, all groups showed a steady increase in body weight, and no significant signs of toxicity were observed during the study, indicating the safety of recombinant oHSV1 viruses (Fig. 2E).

Besides, we employed TUNEL staining to confirm apoptotic cell presence in tumor tissues derived from mice treated with oHSV1-IL15B or combined with oHSV1-aPD1 virus. The results demonstrated that a large amount of apoptotic cells in tumor tissues collected from the mice treated with oHSV1-mock, oHSV1-IL15B, oHSV1-aPD1 and combined group, while the negative vehicle groups presented the lowest level of green fluorescence. Notably, the combined group showed higher induction of apoptosis compared to the oHSV1-mock group, which supports that treatment with oHSV1-IL15B combined with oHSV1-aPD1 virus boosts tumor cell apoptosis, leading to tumor growth inhibition (Fig. 3).

oHSV1-IL15B virus combined with oHSV1-aPD1 improved the antitumoral immune response in colon cancer

To delve deeper into the role of the immune system, specifically CD8+ T and CD4+ T cells, in the antitumor effect triggered by the recombinant oHSV1-IL15B virus and combined treatment, we conducted studies using CT26 tumor-bearing BALB/c mice. The mice were treated with oHSV1 viruses as previously described in Fig. 2A. Three days after the final treatment, immune cells harvested from the peripheral blood, spleens and tumors of these mice were analyzed by flow cytometry and the gating strategy of flow cytometric analysis of T cells was shown in Figure S4.

The percentages of immune cells in the peripheral blood, spleens significantly increased, with statistically significant differences observed in CD3+ T, CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells in oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 group compared to both oHSV1-mock group and vehicle group (Fig. 4A,B). Notably, the oHSV1-IL15B virus exhibited a superior effect in increasing the number of these T cells compared to vehicle group in tumor tissues (Fig. 4C). Furthermore, the difference in CD8+ T cells was statistically significant in the oHSV1-IL15B group compared to the vehicle group (Fig. 4A,C). In summary, oHSV1-IL15B virus and combined treatment demonstrated the ability to activate an antitumoral immune response to some extent through the accumulation of leukocyte infiltration.

oHSV1-IL15B virus combined with oHSV1-aPD1 enhanced the cytotoxicity ability to CT26 cells

Next, we conducted an in vitro tumor cell killing assay to directly measure the antitumor cytotoxicity ability of immune cells derived from oHSV1-IL15B virus-treated mice against CT26 cells. Tumor-infiltrating leukocytes (CD45+) isolated from mice with CT26 tumor treated with various viruses were co-cultured with CT26 cells at different ratios. The cytotoxicity of tumor-infiltrating leukocytes to CT26 cells in each group was assessed through a flow cytometry assay.

The tumor-infiltrating leukocytes from oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1-treated mice exhibited superior cytotoxicity ability to CT26 cells in an effect-to-target cell ratio-dependent manner compared to other groups with statistically significant difference when compared to the vehicle group (Fig. S5). This data suggests that the oHSV1-IL15B combined oHSV1-aPD1 virus enhances the cytotoxicity ability of tumor-infiltrating leukocytes within the tumor microenvironment.

Combined treatment remodels the tumor microenvironment at the RNA level

In the final phase of our investigation, to further investigate the impact of intrinsic factors on the tumor microenvironment following combined treatment, RNA sequencing was performed on tumor tissues obtained from mice treated with vehicle, oHSV1-mock, and oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 viruses. Bioinformatics analysis of the data unveiled that a total of 818 transcripts exhibited significant upregulation, while 426 transcripts displayed substantial downregulation (|Log2FC| ≥1, p < 0.05) in tumor issues treated with the oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 viruses compared to those treated with vehicle. To explore the dynamic effects of the combined treatment on tumors immune microenvironment, heatmaps were constructed from the sequencing data to visualize the effects of combined group vs. oHSV1-mock and vehicle on immune response, antiviral response-related genes and apoptosis-related genes. The analysis results showed that the combination treatment effectively induced an aggregation of these genes (Fig. 5A). The ability of combined treatment to induce an effective tumor immune response was further confirmed by volcano plot analysis, showing that leukocyte-mediated immunity, cytokine interaction and apoptosis-related genes were significantly upregulated in oHSV1-IL15B + oHSV1-aPD1 group compared to the vehicle counterpart. Interestingly, while the same analysis detected several differentially expressed genes (DEGs) with significance between the oHSV1-mock and vehicle samples, leukocyte-mediated immunity-related genes did not appear in this list (Fig. 5B–D). Gene Ontology (GO) and the Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) reveal enrichment and alterations in pathways related to immune response, inflammatory response, cell adhesion, as well as TNF, B cells receptor, IL-17 and PI3K signaling pathways in the combined treatment group. These findings suggest that the combined treatment may modulate the tumor microenvironment and immune system, potentially inhibiting tumor development and progression (Fig. 5E,F).

Discussion

Oncolytic virotherapy emerges as a promising therapeutic strategy against malignant cancers, exhibiting anti-tumor effects through direct oncolysis of tumor cells and the delivery of therapeutic genes in vivo. The HSV-1 vector, in particular, has evolved into a versatile immune-modulating platform, expressing factors like immune checkpoint inhibitors, tumor antigens, cytokines, and interferons based on its safety and functional properties. Our previous studies demonstrated the potential of oHSV1-mock in elevating CD8+ T cell levels within tumor tissues, enhancing anti-tumor CD8+ T cell responses. The recombinant oHSV-aPD-1, with anti-PD-1 antibody gene inserted into VT09X, showing inhibition of growth of the primary and contralateral untreated tumor, improvement in the immune microenvironment inhibited, and activated antigen-specific T cell responses of mice. OVs encoding immune checkpoint inhibitors or cytokine genes have shown promise in initiating both viral-mediated and immune-mediated attacks against cancers, suggesting the effectiveness of combination therapy in cancer treatment.

IL-15, a pivotal cytokine with immense potential for immunotherapy, plays a crucial role in promoting the proliferation and activation of T cells, as well as the activation and survival of NK cells and memory CD8+ T cells expressing the IL-2/IL-15Rβ-γc receptor. IL-15Rα enhances the biological activity of IL-15 through cells expressing the IL-15Rβ/γc receptor subunits, making the soluble IL-15/IL-15Rα complex an attractive strategy for tumor treatment. The IL-15 variant, IL-15N72D, exhibits enhanced immunostimulatory activity due to its stronger binding affinity to the receptor IL-2Rβ/γc on immune cells, making it a valuable component in cancer immunotherapy. Recent evidence supports the anti-tumor effects of OVs expressing IL-15 or IL-15/IL-15Rα complexes.

In this study, we aimed to efficiently express and utilize IL-15/IL-15Rα complex using the HSV-1 vector. The recombinant oHSV1-15B, combined with IL-15/IL-15Rα complex with the Fc domain of IgG1, was constructed. Our studies revealed that oHSV1-IL15B virus had similar titers to oHSV1-mock and could secrete soluble IL-15/IL-15Rα complex from OVs-infected CT26 cells in vitro. Crucially, oHSV1-IL15B virus exhibited heightened cytotoxicity against CT26 cells, underscoring its increased killing efficacy. Furthermore, the oHSV1-IL15B virus or combined with oHSV1-aPD1 demonstrated significant anti-tumor effects in a murine CT26 colon adenocarcinoma model compared to vehicle group. Notably, combined treatment displayed stronger tumor inhibition, boosted tumor cell apoptosis and prolonged overall survival compared to oHSV1-mock group. To unravel the underlying mechanisms of the potential antitumor efficacy mediated by oHSV1-IL15B and combined treatment, we analyzed CD3+ T, CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells in CT26-bearing BALB/c mice. Our findings indicated that oHSV1-IL15B and oHSV1-IL15B combined with oHSV1-aPD1 virus exhibited a superior effect in increasing the percentages of CD8+ T compared to vehicle group in tumor tissues, spleens and peripheral blood, enhancing their cytotoxicity in vivo and emphasizing the critical role of cytotoxic T cells in oncolytic virotherapy. We then analyzed the impact of intrinsic factors on the tumor microenvironment on the transcriptomic level through RNA-sequencing. The analysis results highlighted oHSV1-IL15B combined with oHSV1-aPD1 virus improved tumors immune microenvironment on immune response, antiviral response-related genes and apoptosis-related genes, which contributed to anti-tumor immunotherapy.

In this investigation, oHSV1-IL15B has been engineered to express human IL15 and IL-15Rα, sharing 72.8% and 53.5% identity with mouse IL15 and IL-15Rα. Previous study discovered that stimulation of mouse memory-phenotype CD8+T cells, which express high levels of CD44 and IL2Rβ, with a complex of either human or mouse soluble IL15/IL15Rα resulted in a stronger lymphoproliferative response than IL15 alone. Another study found that a recombinant vaccinia virus expressing human IL-15 could stimulate CD8+ T cell proliferation and sustain strong long-term CD8+ T cell immunity in mice. Our observed limited anti-tumor response of oHSV1-IL15B in the mouse CT26 model may be attributed to these interspecies similarities. The expressed IL-15/IL-15Rα complex binds to the IL-2/IL-15 receptor beta chain (CD122) and the common gamma chain (gamma-C, CD132) on immune cells, thereby exerting their functions. Additionally, CAR T cells modified with IL15 display a less differentiated phenotype, reduced exhaustion, and improved mitochondrial metabolism. Looking forward, the combination of CAR T or CAR NK cells with oHSV1-IL15B therapy holds promise as an effective anticancer strategy.

The findings of our study provide valuable insights into the potential of combining cytokines, anti-PD-1 antibody and oncolytic viruses to enhance antitumor immunotherapy in cancer treatment. Besides, further research is imperative to determine the optimal combination of cytokines, anti-PD-1 antibody and oncolytic viruses that can deliver maximum therapeutic benefit in clinical applications. However, this study has still limitations, including the choice of animal models, humanized or transgenic mice might be more suitable. Further research may also need to examine the in vitro effects of human IL-15/IL-15Rα complexes on mouse immune cells. Overall, this study establishes a robust foundation for continued exploration of oncolytic virotherapy as a promising treatment strategy for various types of cancer.

Materials and methods

Cell lines

The murine colon adenocarcinoma CT26 cell line was sourced from the National Collection of Authenticated Cell Cultures (Shanghai, China), while the African green monkey kidney Vero cell line was procured from the ATCC (Manassas, VA). Both cell lines were cultivated in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Gibco, USA) and maintained in an incubator at 37 °C with 5% CO2 in air.

Viruses

The three recombinant oHSV-1 viruses utilized in this study were procured from WellGene (Beijing, China). oHSV1-mock, previously reported, was derived from the wild strain HL-1 isolated from an Asian patient with herpes, wherein the copies of ICP34.5 and ICP47 genes were deleted. oHSV1-aPD1 (named VT1093M before) expressing mouse-derived anti-PD-1 antibody was derived from the oHSV1-mock. Oncolytic virus oHSV1-IL15B was engineered by incorporating human-derived IL-15 and IL-15Rα sushi domain DNA sequences, along with the Fc domain of IgG1, into the ICP34.5 locus of oHSV1-mock (Fig. 1A). After several rounds of plaque purification, positive clones were selected and confirmed through PCR and DNA sequencing. All recombinant viruses were amplified in Vero cells and purified following previously established procedures. The titers of the recombinant viruses used in this study were 2 × 108 PFU/ml.

Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay(ELISA)for IL-15 expression

CT26 cells were seeded onto 24-well plates (Nunc Maxisorp, USA) at a density of 5 × 104 cells per well and cultured overnight. Subsequently, the cells were individually infected with recombinant HSV-1 viruses at multiplicity of infections (MOIs) of 0.1, 1, 5, and 20 for 24, 48, or 72 h. The cell supernatants were then collected to assess IL-15 expression using the Human IL-15/IL-15Rα Complex ELISA kit (R&D Systems, MN, USA) as per the manufacturer’s instructions. Absorbance values were measured at 450 nm using a VersaMax microplate reader (Molecular Devices, USA).

Cell viability assay of recombinant oHSV1-IL15 viruses

CT26 cells were seeded onto 96-well plates (Nunc Maxisorp, USA) at a density of 5 × 103 cells per well. The following day, the cells were transfected with recombinant viruses at different MOIs for 24, 48, or 72 h. Subsequently, cell viability was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) kit (Guangzhou Cellcook Biotech Co., Ltd, China). oHSV1-mock served as the control, with six parallel wells established in each group. Absorbance values were measured at 450 nm using a VersaMax microplate reader (Molecular Devices, USA).

In vivo evaluation of the anti-tumor activities of recombinant oHSV1-IL15 virus

Female BALB/c mice (5–7 weeks old) were procured from Jinan Pengyue Laboratory Animal Breeding Co. Ltd. (Jinan, China). All animal experiments adhered to the Guidelines for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals and were approved by the Animal Care and Use Committee of Yantai University (IACUC No. 2018-DA-12). CT26 cells (5 × 107 cells per mouse) were washed with serum-free medium and suspended in a 1:1 ratio in serum-free DMEM medium (total 100 µL) with Matrigel basement membrane matrix (BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA). Subcutaneous injection of 0.2 mL tumor cell suspension on the right flank of BALB/c mice was performed to establish CT26 tumor models. When the tumor volume reached 100–150 mm3, tumor-bearing CT26 mice were randomly grouped and intratumorally injected with 1 × 107 PFU of oHSV1-mock, oHSV1-IL15B, oHSV1-aPD1 (at days 1,3,5,7,9,11; 50 µl) or an intratumoral injection treatment of oHSV1-IL15B (at days 1,5,9; 50 µl) and oHSV1-aPD1 (at days 3,7,11; 50 µl) for a total of six dose administrations as indicated. The control group received the same volume of vehicle (procured from WellGene, Beijing, China). Tumor sizes were measured with a caliper and calculated using the standard formula: (length × width2) × 0.5. The mice were anesthetized using isoflurane and subsequently euthanized in a manner that minimized suffering.

Flow cytometric analysis

Flow cytometric analysis was employed to assess the distribution of CD8+ T, CD4+ T, and CD3+ T cells in CT26 tumor-bearing mice and the in vitro tumor cell killing activity. For the analysis of the proportion of CD8+ T, CD4+ T, and CD3+ T cells in mice treated with recombinant oHSV1 viruses, tumors and spleens from individual mice were harvested, and single-cell suspensions were prepared using the Tumor Dissociation Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, Gladbach, Germany) with the gentleMACS™ Octo Dissociator with Heaters (Miltenyi Biotec) for surface labeling. The peripheral blood was collected from mice’s orbital sinus following ethical guidelines and institutional protocols. Mice were anesthetized before collection, a small incision was made near the eye, and blood was withdrawn using a sterile needle or capillary tube. The incision site was treated with antiseptic after collection. Non-specific antibody binding was blocked by pre-incubating single-cell suspensions with 0.5 µg anti-mouse CD16/32 antibody (TruStain fcX™, BioLegend, USA) at 4 °C for 5 min, and dead cells were identified and eliminated using Fixable Viability Stain 510 (BD Biosciences). Gating strategy was illustrated in Figure S4. The following monoclonal antibodies were used for flow cytometry staining: CD45-BV605 (Biolegend, USA, 103140), CD8-BB515 (BD Biosciences, USA, 564422), CD3-percp-cy5.5 (Biolegend, USA, 100328), CD4-APC-cy7 (Biolegend, USA, 100141). The samples were examined on the BD FACSCelesta Flow Cytometer (BD Biosciences).

For the analysis of in vitro cytotoxicity activity of tumor-infiltrating leukocytes (TILs), tumor tissues were harvested after 15 days post-treatment from individual mice for the isolation and purification of tumor-infiltrating immune cells (CD45+ cells). Tumor single-cell suspensions were prepared using the Tumor Dissociation Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, Gladbach, Germany), followed by removal of dead cells using the Dead Cell Removal Kit (Miltenyi Biotec, Germany). CD45+ tumor-infiltrating leukocytes were isolated using mouse CD45+ TIL Microbeads (Miltenyi Biotec, Germany). CT26.WT cells were pre-labeled with Carboxifluorescein diacetate N-succinimidyl ester (CSFE) (Biolegend, USA) as target cells. The tumor-infiltrating immune cells (4 × 105 cells) as effector cells were then co-cultured with target CT26.WT tumor cells at effector: target ratios of 10:1, 20:1, 40:1 in triplicate in 24-well plates for 4 h at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 incubator. Then cells were harvested and stained with propidium iodide (PI). Flow cytometry was used to determine specific killing by measuring PI uptake.

TUNEL assay

To further observe the apoptosis of tumor tissue cells in mice, tumors were collected 15 days post-treatment for TUNEL analysis. TUNEL staining was performed using TUNEL assay Kit (keyGEN BioTECH, KGA7071). Images were captured using a Leica DMi8 fluorescence micro scope (Leica, Germany).

Transcriptome sequencing (RNA-seq)

Total RNA from tumors of mice in the vehicle group, oHSV1-mock group and combined group were prepared for transcriptome sequencing using the Illumina NovaSeq6000 instrument by Baimeke Biotechnology Co. Ltd. Sequencing results were mapped and differential expression analysis of comparing groups (3 biological replicates per group) was performed using the DESeq2 R package.

Statistical analysis

The results are expressed as means ± SD. Data analysis was performed using Student’s t-tests for comparisons between two groups or one-way ANOVAs for comparisons between multiple groups in GraphPad Prism 8.0 software. Mice survival data were analyzed using Kaplan–Meier survival curves. A p-value < 0.05 (*) was considered statistically significant.
