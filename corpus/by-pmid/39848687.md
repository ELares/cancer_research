# Leveraging mRNA technology for antigen based immuno-oncology therapies.

## Abstract

The application of messenger RNA (mRNA) technology in antigen-based immuno-oncology therapies represents a significant advancement in cancer treatment. Cancer vaccines are an effective combinatorial partner to sensitize the host immune system to the tumor and boost the efficacy of immune therapies. Selecting suitable tumor antigens is the key step to devising effective vaccinations and amplifying the immune response. Tumor neoantigens are de novo epitopes derived from somatic mutations, avoiding T-cell central tolerance of self-epitopes and inducing immune responses to tumors. The identification and prioritization of patient-specific tumor neoantigens are based on advanced computational algorithms taking advantage of the profiling with next-generation sequencing considering factors involved in human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-peptide-T-cell receptor (TCR) complex formation, including peptide presentation, HLA-peptide affinity, and TCR recognition. This review discusses the development and clinical application of mRNA vaccines in oncology, with a particular focus on recent clinical trials and the computational workflows and methodologies for identifying both shared and individual antigens. While this review centers on therapeutic mRNA vaccines targeting existing tumors, it does not cover preventative vaccines. Preclinical experimental validations are crucial in cancer vaccine development, but we emphasize the computational approaches that facilitate neoantigen selection and design, highlighting their role in advancing mRNA vaccine development. The versatility and rapid development potential of mRNA make it an ideal platform for personalized neoantigen immunotherapy. We explore various strategies for antigen target identification, including tumor-associated and tumor-specific antigens and the computational tools used to predict epitopes capable of eliciting strong immune responses. We address key design considerations for enhancing the immunogenicity and stability of mRNA vaccines, as well as emerging trends and challenges in the field. This comprehensive overview highlights the therapeutic potential of mRNA-based cancer vaccines and underscores ongoing research efforts aimed at optimizing these therapies for improved clinical outcomes.

Introduction

Active immunization: from anti-infectious to anticancer

 Exposure to antigens can induce an immune response from the adaptive immune system (production of antibodies or activation of specifically sensitized T lymphocytes) and is known as active immunization. The inoculation of a healthy child with fluid from a cowpox pustule by Edward Jenner in the late 18th century (described as “vaccination” from vacca, the Latin word for cow) led to the establishment of active immunization as a preventive strategy against infectious agents. Active immunization can be prophylactic or therapeutic; its first use as a therapeutic anticancer strategy was in the early 20th century by William Coley, and then subsequently in the 1950s with the anticancer application of Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG), which aims to induce non-specific immune activation but is not targeting cancer antigens.

Anticancer active immunization methods may target different antigen categories (see below, “Antigen target identification workflows”) and use a variety of target preparation and delivery methods. Targets prepared ex vivo can be directly introduced, ready for presentation to the immune system, with administration of tumor cells (whole-cell/lysates, autologous/allogeneic), dendritic cells (DC) loaded with antigen, or synthetic peptides. Alternatively, the target antigen(s) can be produced in situ after the administration of genetically engineered vectors carrying the required information. Vectors can be viral, bacterial, plasmid, nanoliposomes and the information can be stored in DNA or RNA (which can also be administered directly). Targets of active immunization include tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) and tumor-specific antigens (TSAs), the latter being oncogenic viral antigens (xenoantigens) or the result of tumor-specific genomic alterations (neoantigens), which can be shared (predefined) or personal (unique to a particular patient).

Anticancer active immunization: clinical trials and approvals

Interest in the anticancer therapeutic application of active immunization emerged again in the late 20th century, reflected in a gradual increase in the number of “cancer vaccine” clinical trials. Results of each query were curated by all authors to retain trials of active immunization for malignant (solid tumors and hematologic malignancies) and premalignant conditions (eg, cervical intraepithelial neoplasia, ductal carcinoma in situ), leaving 1,836 records from the first query and five records from the second query. An additional list of 357 trial IDs was manually curated and submitted to ClinicalTrials.gov to download the records for each National Clinical Trial (NCT) identifier number (2,198 records obtained, available as online supplemental table 1). Figure 1A illustrates the number of these trials that involve messenger RNA (mRNA) modality (n=122) stratified by study status (NCTs/records available as online supplemental table 2), while figure 1B shows the distribution of trials over time by study phase.

These efforts have resulted in few approvals for anticancer vaccines by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Approved agents include the intravesical live BCG (TheraCys, Sanofi Pasteur, approved in 1990, replaced in 2018 by TICE BCG, Organon-Merck) for the treatment and prophylaxis of early-stage urothelial carcinoma; the autologous cellular immunotherapy sipuleucel-T (Provenge, Dendreon Corporation, approved in 2010) for the treatment of asymptomatic or minimally symptomatic metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer; and the adenoviral vector-based interferon alfa-2b inducing intravesical gene therapy nadofaragene firadonevec (Adstiladrin, Ferring Pharmaceuticals, approved in 2022) for high-risk BCG-unresponsive non-muscle invasive bladder cancer. An additional FDA-approved (2015) anticancer oncolytic viral gene therapy considered as a type of cancer therapeutic vaccine is talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC, IMLYGIC, Amgen), for the treatment of advanced melanoma. Notably, only one of these approved agents, sipuleucel-T, contains a TAA (prostatic acid phosphatase) and none are mRNA-based.

Individualized neoantigen active immunization and mRNA

Advances in high-throughput DNA and RNA sequencing and computational biology methods have facilitated the identification of putative neoantigens derived from tumor-specific genomic alterations, which promise target specificity and high immunoreactivity for each patient without autoreactivity. Integration in clinical trials and subsequently in routine clinical workflows require a rapid development timeframe downstream from the target identification pipeline to the delivery of the therapeutic product. mRNA-based vaccines, with characteristics such as rapid timeframe from design to manufacture and testing, high immunogenicity, and negligible risk of insertional mutagenesis are well-suited to this task. Clinical trials of individualized neoantigen-targeted immunotherapies are being conducted, and some trials have reported results (table 1) with good tolerability and immunogenicity, and promising preliminary clinical activity. There are currently (August 5, 2024) 23 trials of active immunization against individualized neoantigens that are actively recruiting participants, with seven having started from 2017 to 2022, 10 in 2023 and 6 in 2024 (table 2).

This review describes the comprehensive process of leveraging mRNA technology for antigen-based immuno-oncology therapies, with a specific emphasis on computational strategies for antigen target identification and neoantigen prediction. Given the extensive scope of mRNA vaccine development, we focus here on computational methods that support the discovery and validation of both shared and patient-specific antigens, which serve as the foundation for vaccine development. We also discuss the criteria and computational tools used to identify epitopes capable of eliciting immune responses. One of the main research topics in this field is related to the strategies for optimizing antigen structure to enhance its immunogenicity and stability. Other critical elements of mRNA vaccine design are related to the computational approaches for codon optimization and effective delivery methods. Lastly, we discuss the emerging trends and potential challenges in the field of mRNA-based immuno-oncology, highlighting areas of ongoing research and development that promise to expand the therapeutic potential of this technology.

Antigen target identification workflows

Antigen-based treatments aim to elicit a de novo immune response by priming novel antigen-specific T-cell response and/or amplifying pre-existing host immune response. Their success depends on the appropriate selection of antigens that can trigger a strong and specific immune response. These antigens can be sourced from various origins, including (1) antigens that are commonly observed in the same cancer types in multiple patients, (2) antigens processed and presented after co-culture of tumor lysates with antigen-presenting cells (APCs), and (3) those selected from the pool of patient-specific somatic alterations and other tumor-associated events (figure 2). There are two main antigen categories. The first class is the set of TAAs, which consists of proteins that are overexpressed in a tissue-specific manner and cancer testis antigens (CTAs) expressed in germline cells and in various types of cancers but not in normal somatic tissues. The second is the set of TSAs, which include viral antigens such as human papillomavirus, hepatitis B virus, Epstein-Barr virus, etc, and neoepitopes resulting from somatic alterations. MAGE-A1 is one of the examples of TAAs recognized by T-cells. It was first identified in melanoma and then shown to be expressed in several tumors, including non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), bladder cancer, and others. It is not expressed in normal tissues except in male germline and trophoblastic cells. Several other proteins, the CTAs, share a similar pattern of expression and immune recognition, such as BAGE, NY-ESO-1 and others. For this reason, CTAs and other predetermined shared antigens have been considered potential targets in mRNA vaccinology. For example, four TAAs, including three CTAs, NY-ESO-1, MAGE-A3, and TPTE, were adopted in a recent phase I trial on 89 patients with melanoma. Results showed that the vaccine alone or with PD1 checkpoint inhibitors induced durable responses in triggering strong CD4+and CD8+ T cell immunity, with cytotoxic T-cell responses comparable to adoptive T-cell therapy. Similar results have been reported in NSCLC, using MAGE-C1, MAGE-C2, and NY-ESO-1 as target antigens, among others, in mRNA vaccination. MAGE-C1, MAGE-A3, and Wilms tumor 1 were used in myeloma, observing a favorable response in the vaccine arm. On the contrary, antigens derived from autologous APC co-cultures (middle panel of figure 2) are typically not defined a priori and, therefore, “anonymous”. Antigens are isolated from tumor cells taken through a biopsy. These cells are processed into a more antigenic form and co-cultured with APCs. Anonymous antigen vaccines can include a wider number and variety of antigens. Multiple trials based on vaccination with anonymous antigens are ongoing in melanoma (NCT01973322), angiosarcoma (NCT05799612), leukemia (NCT00514189), and others (online supplemental table 1).

The most promising source of targets for cancer vaccination is represented by the neoepitopes generated by patient-specific somatic alterations. Somatic mutations result in amino acid sequence changes that can be recognized as foreign by the immune system and, therefore, ideal targets for vaccine design. These antigens fall within the TSA category. Several hot-spot mutations shared among multiple patients have been identified, such as IDH1R132H in glioblastoma and other cancers, BRAFV600E and NRASQ61R in melanoma, KRASG12 in multiple solid tumors. Although polyclonal CD8+T cell responses against shared driver antigens have been observed, for example, against mutant KRAS G12D in metastatic colorectal cancer, shared hot-spot mutations are generally believed to be under positive evolutionary selection and likely evade detection by the immune system. Moreover, the patient-specific human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles may not be suitable presenting alleles for certain driver mutations they carry. Therefore, more individualized antigen selection based on the random nature of somatic alterations, not necessarily a driver, represents both an opportunity and a challenge for the success of cancer vaccines. On the other hand, driver mutations are highly clonal and likely present in more tumor cells throughout tumor evolution, making them attractive targets for a vaccination approach. The discovery and prioritization of neoepitopes from somatic mutations is an active research area, as the features that influence epitope immunogenicity are not completely well characterized. Within a consortium initiative led by the Parker Institute, researchers from 25 institutions independently analyzed shared sequencing data sets from 6 patients to predict immunogenic epitopes. A systematic comparison of the top 100 ranked peptide-major histocompatibility complex (MHC) pairs across teams revealed a low median overlap of 13%, which increased to 32% when comparing each team’s top 100 predicted complexes with the complete set of predictions from other teams. This analysis indicates that variations in prediction results among participants may be influenced by differences in epitope filtering processes, ranking approaches, and the diverse features used within each team’s neoantigen prediction pipeline. Continuous efforts in shared data generation and immunogenicity validation are poised to strengthen our understanding of optimal neoantigens and would enhance the accuracy and consistency of neoantigen predictions across bioinformatics pipelines.

Efficiency of pipeline

A critical challenge to be considered in the discovery of neoepitopes, is the variability in the detection of somatic variants depending of the source sample, such as resection or needle biopsy and fresh frozen (FF) or formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE). Several benchmarking studies of FF versus FFPE show sensitivities ranging from 60% to 90%, with consistent detection of mutations with high allele fractions in both sample types. Differences in variant calling algorithms that can be mitigated using simple ensemble heuristics. Moreover, stringent filters based on multiple features (eg, allele fractions, clonality, expression, mapping quality) help reduce false positives. Independent single nucleotide variant (SNV) somatic mutation analysis performed by Muller et al closely matched those reported by Gartner et al, with 82.2% overlap across 80 patients and an even higher concordance of 94.7% for confirmed immunogenic SNV mutations. Notably, advancements in high-performance computing and hardware accelerators, exemplified by Illumina’s DRAGEN solution, have significantly reduced genomic data processing times and facilitated analytical improvements.

Although multiple classifications of neoepitopes can be defined, a simple approach is based on the genetic alteration generating the neoepitope:

Non-synonymous SNVs involve the substitution of single nucleotides within a gene that results in a coding amino acid change. They are the most frequent cancer alteration type. Several clinical trials on neoepitope prediction from SNVs have been designed. The number of SNVs is proportional to the number of neoantigens, and it has been shown to correlate with tumor cytolytic activity and predict the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibition. Although some shared hot-spot mutations can elicit antigen-specific immune responses leading to tumor regression, for example, IDH1R132H mutation in glioma, shared SNVs are typically limited to small patient subsets and tend to be less antigenic with respect to other sporadic somatic mutations. For this reason, individualized cancer vaccines based on patient-specific SNVs have been the subject of extensive recent development.

Dinucleotide variants are characterized by a single mutational mechanism affecting two adjacent nucleotides simultaneously in a unique mutational event, as they occur at a much higher frequency than individual pairs of independent events in succession, which is very unlikely. It has been shown that the majority of variants near somatic mutations (proximal variants) considered for neoantigen prediction are contributed by dinucleotide polymorphisms. Therefore, their importance in vaccine design has been recently underscored as their inclusion in the neoantigen recognition algorithms significantly improves their prediction accuracy. Unfortunately, commonly adopted variant calling algorithms report dinucleotide polymorphisms (DNPs) as two separate SNVs. Recent efforts have been developed to correctly annotate these events.

Indels involve inserting or deleting one or more nucleotides in the DNA sequence and can significantly impact protein structure and function. They can also lead to the formation of neoepitopes, particularly those that disrupt the canonical reading frame of the affected gene. Pan-cancer studies have shown that the chance of indels to bind to MHC with high affinity is up to threefold higher with respect to non-synonymous SNVs. Nevertheless, correct accurate identification of small indels presents some technical challenges due to potential misalignments, especially in low-complexity regions of the genomes.

Gene fusions. Various chromosomal rearrangements, such as translocations, inversions, or deletions, can generate a chimeric protein with functions and properties different from those of the original proteins. Gene fusions are interesting therapeutic targets as they are typically driver events. Recurrent gene fusions have been identified but tend to be low-frequency. Genetic rearrangements are potential immunogenic neoantigens sources mediating immunotherapies responses. Multiple challenges, however, can limit their use as targets in cancer vaccination as fusion detection algorithms tend to produce a high number of false positives.

Non-canonical antigens arise from unconventional or atypical sources, other than point mutations in protein-coding regions of genes, and can yield tumor-specific or TAAs. Non-coding regions of the genome have been identified as a potential source of immunogenic neoantigens. They include translation of non-canonical open reading frames with or without somatic mutations, splice variants, and others. Recent studies have uncovered the repetitive genome, such as endogenous retroelements, as a novel and large source of immunogenic antigens. Junctions between exons long non-coding RNAs and transposable elements can also encode tumor-specific individualized neoantigens. Technological advances such as mass spectrometry-based immunopeptidome profiling with higher sensitivity and throughput enable direct detection of non-canonical antigens.

There are other categories of neoantigens that are not suitable for mRNA cancer vaccine modality. For example, mRNA editing and post-translational modifications (PTMs) alter the structure and function of proteins after their translation from mRNA and cannot be directly encoded in the genetic code. This complicates the targeting of neoantigens that are dependent on specific PTMs for their immunogenic properties with mRNA vaccines.

Epitope selection

Target antigen identification is typically followed by in silico assessment for the presence of minimal epitopes harbored within the target antigen that is likely to elicit an immune response. Individualized vaccine designs select unique neoantigen-derived epitopes based on a patient’s specific mutations and HLA alleles, maximizing immune response specificity and potentially enhancing effectiveness. However, this approach requires comprehensive genomic sequencing, which may limit scale and feasibility outside of high-resource settings. In contrast, pan-allele “off the shelf” designs aim for broader applicability by selecting shared antigens likely to be effective across a wider range of HLA types. While this approach may sacrifice some specificity for individuals who lack the antigen or those with uncommon HLA types, it enhances accessibility by providing a more universal solution.

Both approaches rely on computational tools for epitope selection. Recent years have witnessed significant advancements in the prediction of peptide-HLA binding and presentation, spurred by the generation of high-quality and high throughput data sets and the integration of domain knowledge-driven feature engineering and deep learning techniques. Algorithms for predicting peptide-MHC interaction have evolved from tools that predict peptide-HLA binding, to integrative models that account for intracellular determinants of peptide-HLA presentation and are now advancing towards modeling the likelihood of immunogenicity and the direct interaction dynamics between peptide-HLA complexes and T-cell receptors (TCRs). These developments have enhanced our understanding of the rules that govern peptide-HLA complex presentations, immunogenicity, and TCR recognition.

Peptide-HLA binding

A cornerstone of designing peptide-based vaccines is the ability to predict which peptide sequences can bind to an individual’s MHC molecules, a process that is highly polymorphic and person-specific. The earliest frameworks developed for peptide-MHC prediction were primarily built on quantitative binding affinity data derived from in vitro recombinant experimental assays. Pivotal algorithms such as the early versions of NetMHC for allele-specific predictions, and NetMHCpan for pan-allele predictions, employed feed-forward neural networks and became the standard, most widely used tools in the field.

Continuously curated database resources such as The Immune Epitope Database (IEDB) support the development of these prediction tools by maintaining a comprehensive record of binding affinity data as well as incorporating novel data sets from emerging assays. To date, the IEDB contains>1.5 million peptides restricted to>1,000 MHCs, as well as B-cell and T-cell epitope data. Similarly, a companion resource to IEDB, The Cancer Epitope Database and Analysis Resource (CEDAR) specializes in antibody and T cell epitopes relevant to cancer. IEDB and CEDAR also offer mechanisms for automatically benchmarking prediction tools against “unseen” epitope data sets prior to their release. The IPD-IMGT/HLA segment of the IMGT database (Immuno Polymorphism Database, International ImMunoGeneTics information system) documents MHC/HLA polymorphisms, providing standardized naming, sequences, and alignment for∼28,000 HLA-I and∼12,000 HLA-II molecules as of January 2024. These HLA sequences and alignments are crucial for training pan-allele models, enhancing the coverage and accuracy of prediction tools.

Endogenous peptide-HLA presentation

It has long been appreciated that algorithms that consider multiple steps in the MHC presentation pathways, upstream of binding, achieve better predictive performance for naturally presented epitopes. For example, factors that contribute to epitope availability such as source protein expression, proteasomal processing (FragPred, NetChop), TAP-mediated endoplasmic reticulum (ER) transport (TAPPred, PRED(TAP)) and others, have been combined with binding affinity information (NetCTLpan, and others) to improve HLA presentation prediction. More recently, improvements in the sensitivity and specificity of mass spectrometry-based immunopeptidomics, a technique that characterizes the repertoire of eluted MHC ligands from living cells, have enabled high-throughput immunopeptidome profiling of multiallelic and monoallelic samples yielding vastly expanded endogenous ligand presentation data (commonly referred to as eluted ligand (EL) data). EL data implicitly captures attributes of naturally processed MHC epitopes and facilitates a deep and more unbiased characterization of cellular determinants of epitope presentation. Indeed, EL data is routinely used by recent peptide-HLA algorithms, such as HLAthena, NetMHCpan-4.1, MixMHCpred, MHCFlurry, EDGE, and others, to boost predictive performance more than 1.5-fold compared with models trained on binding affinity data alone. Additionally, systematic assessment of variables that contribute to presentation from immunopeptidomics data sets has shown that source protein expression is the second most informative feature after binding (+12% positive predictive value (PPV)), followed by likelihood of proteolytic processing (+2–3% PPV), while other contributors, such as localization, level of disorderness, or gene-specific features captured by ESM, a large protein language model, yielded minimal improvements after accounting for expression. These learnings, along with novel machine learning techniques, have led to the development of state-of-the-art mass-spec based algorithms that integrate source transcript expression and likelihood of proteasomal cleavage such as HLAthena, MHCFlurry2.0, EDGE, NetMHCpanExp-1.0, and transformer-inspired BigMHC (sequence-only) and HLApollo. Overall, integrative predictors for endogenous HLA class I presentation significantly outperform binding-only models, nominating more true positive peptides at low false positive rates, a feature highly relevant for designing effective epitope-based cancer vaccines.

The development of experimental techniques for allele-specific HLA class II profiling via affinity purification of Strep-tagged HLA-II beta chains has drastically increased the amount of data available to train HLA-II predictors, especially for under-represented HLA-DQ and HLA-DP heterodimers. To date, more than 1.4 million HLA-II ligands have been identified, spanning>80 HLA-II alleles, of which>40 were profiled by monoallelic ligandomics. Advancements in both data generation and machine learning techniques have been used to refine HLA-II prediction algorithms such as MARIA, neonmhc2, BERTMHC, MixMHC2pred, CAPTAn, and NetMHCIIpan4.3. Similarly to HLA-I, layering multimodal data, such as processing and expression (MARIA, HLAIImaster) or the full sequence of the source protein (CAPTAn), boosted overall performance.

Immunogenicity

Most HLA-presented peptides are not immunogenic. Thus, building on enhanced models for natural processing and presentation as well as accumulating immunogenicity data sets, there has been a resurgence in efforts to develop predictors that evaluate the immunogenic potential of peptide-HLA complexes. Notably, these approaches focus on identifying broad immunogenic characteristics of the peptide-HLA complex, independent of specific TCR sequences. Despite early efforts, progress in this area has been slowed by insufficient peptide-HLA immunogenicity data sets due to the limited throughput of antigen-specific T cell screening assays. However, advancements in experimental techniques, such as barcoded p-MHC multimers or antigen library panning methods (T-Scan, HANSolo, TCR-MAP), along with valuable research initiatives over the past several years by The Tumor Neoantigen Selection Alliance (TESLA), the National Cancer Institute, the Human Integrated Tumor Immunology Discovery Engine (HiTIDE), and others, have vastly expanded publicly available immunogenicity data set to∼1,000 confirmed tumor neoepitope-HLA pairs and>4,000 T cell epitopes and accelerated progress.

A common approach in developing computational models for differentiating immunogenic from non-immunogenic epitopes involves assessing peptide-HLA complex and neoepitope features for their association with immunogenicity and constructing a composite model. While peptide-HLA binding is the most informative feature of immunogenicity, other factors that are predictive of endogenous presentation such as epitope expression have also been demonstrated to improve the likelihood of immunogenicity. Accumulating evidence suggests that the stability of the peptide-HLA complex also significantly contributes to immunogenicity, likely since an extended half-life of the molecule at the cell surface enhances the chance of cognate TCR encounter. Currently the only available tool for in silico pan-allele stability predictions is NetMHCstabpan, trained on an unpublished data set of∼30,000 peptides-MHC stability measurements spanning 75 alleles. Although large-scale public stability data sets are lacking, emerging mass spectrometry-based techniques for pMHC thermostability profiling are poised to address this gap. Analyses of pMHC-TCR crystal structures have also demonstrated that epitope positions P4–P6 are often at the interface of TCR recognition and revealed an over-representation of large and/or aromatic residues. These findings have been corroborated by more recent systematic analysis of non-anchor epitope positions and encoded into the PRIME (PRedictor of class I IMmunogenic Epitopes) immunogenicity predictor. Additionally, sequence dissimilarity between mutated and wildtype epitopes, especially at non-anchor positions, has also been proposed to help separate immunogenic from non-immunogenic epitopes; however a recent analysis did not find this feature to be significantly different. Physicochemical properties of epitope residues such as hydrophobicity and proportion of aromatic residues have also been observed to be predictive. Of note, immunogenic epitopes from different origins, for example viral versus neoantigens, may exhibit different characteristics and care must be taken to construct training data sets according to the predictive task. Computational algorithms that define and incorporate features to model immunogenicity are under active development, examples include PRIME, IMPROVE, ICERFIRE (ICore-based Ensemble Random Forest for neo-epitope Immunogenicity pREdiction) and others, achieving incremental improvements towards more accurate immunogenic epitope prediction.

Protein/peptide level design

With selected antigens and/or epitopes, the next step is to optimize protein or polypeptide sequences. Traditional therapeutic antibody design aims to maintain structure-activity relationships while minimizing immunogenicity by removing T cell epitopes, whereas cancer vaccine protein design has a distinctively different goal to maximize T cell immunogenicity while minimizing the risk of off-target effects or autoimmunity. Nonetheless, existing computational tools and molecular elements in antibody and antigen design toolkits can be readily used to achieve this goal.

One important consideration is to obliviate the antigen source protein’s original function by removing specific functional domains or disrupting protein structure. For example, in the aforementioned 4-TAA melanoma vaccine, tyrosinase antigen sequence was truncated to the first 477 amino acids of its parental protein, effectively abolishing the transmembrane and cytosolic domains.

Another important consideration is to bypass unintended immunological effects driven by specific domains or motifs in the target antigen proteins. For example, immune-inhibitory domains such as Glycine-Alanine repeat (GAr) domain in Epstein-Barr virus nuclear antigen 1, and Conserved Region 1 repeat in LANA peptides from Kaposi’s Sarcoma-Associated Herpesvirus have been heavily investigated in viral associated cancer treatment. In experimental TCR therapies targeting MAGEA3, off-target cross-reactivity with homologous epitopes in MAGEA12 and Titin, expressed in brain and heart tissues, led to fatal outcomes and discontinuation of these treatments. This highlights the critical need for rigorous evaluation of potential unintended immunological effects, not only from the target antigen but also from related sequences in immuno-oncology therapies.

A further consideration is the use of signal or helper peptides that may enhance antigen processing and/or antigenicity, which have been extensively studied over the past several decades and can be valuable design elements for cancer vaccines. For example, the Class II associated invariant chain peptide can be fused to antigens to target the lysosome and MHC-II pathways, as shown by Thibodeau et al. Various substitutes and derivatives have been demonstrated to improve exogenous antigen presentation to the class II pathway. The pan DR-restricted helper epitopes, PADRE, have been shown to enhance antigen processing and antigenicity by binding broadly to various HLA-DR alleles and eliciting strong CD4 T cell activation. PADRE has been used in several experimental therapeutic cancer vaccine developments, including breast cancer, melanoma, glioblastoma, and NSCLC. These studies demonstrated that PADRE can significantly boost immune responses, improve therapeutic effects, and prolong survival in various cancer models. Additionally, other elements such as MHC class I trafficking domain, lysosome-associated membrane proteins LAMP/LAMP2, DC-LAMP, β2-Microglobulin which stabilizes and transports MHC class I molecules to the cell surface, N-terminal signal peptide for secretion signal to help ER translocation, N-terminus ubiquitin fusion, and human beta-defensin have been investigated for their roles in enhancing antigen processing and presentation.

Finally, overcoming immunological tolerance is a critical consideration in the development of effective cancer vaccines. Immunological tolerance of tumor antigens was first described in mice, where naturally derived antitumor T cells were found to be mono/oligoclonal, likely due to insufficient cross-presentation. This tolerance poses a significant challenge, as it prevents the immune system from effectively recognizing and attacking tumor cells. However, this can be overcome through the use of vaccination strategies designed to drive de novo T-cell responses. These strategies aim to expand the clonal diversity of antitumor T cells, thus enhancing the immune system’s ability to target and eliminate cancer cells. The concept of immunotherapy is underpinned by this approach, leveraging vaccines to stimulate a broader and more effective T-cell response. For instance, Linette et al demonstrated that vaccination could significantly boost the repertoire of T cells capable of recognizing tumor antigens, thereby improving the efficacy of cancer immunotherapies. By expanding the diversity and enhancing the functionality of T cells, these vaccination strategies hold promise for overcoming the inherent immunological tolerance to tumor antigens and achieving more robust and durable antitumor responses.

Another innovative approach in cancer vaccine design involves epitope concatenation, where multiple epitopes are combined into a single polypeptide. This strategy requires careful consideration to avoid creating junctional epitopes that could lead to unintended immune responses. Techniques such as assessing sequence similarity to the self-proteome and introducing linker amino acids can help mitigate these risks. Commonly used linkers in multiepitope designs include AAY, EAAAK, KK, and flexible glycine-rich motifs such as GPGPG, GGGGS. As the number of concatenated epitopes increases, optimization algorithms become essential for managing complexity and ensuring vaccine efficacy while minimizing unwanted immune reactions.

mRNA level design

The final stage of the workflow involves designing the mRNA sequences that encode the optimized protein or peptide sequences. Recent advancements in mRNA sequence optimization and nucleotide modification have shown significant progress in improving mRNA stability and translation efficiency. A recent review article highlights the importance of strategic mRNA sequence design, emphasizing that optimal codon usage, sophisticated RNA folding techniques, and the integration of modified nucleotides can significantly enhance the stability and functional output of therapeutic mRNAs. Computational and experimental studies have demonstrated the benefits of these design choices, showcasing how they can increase mRNA stability, improve translational efficiency, and lead to more effective mRNA-based therapies.

mRNA sequence optimization

Enhancing antigen immunogenicity can be achieved through mRNA design. For example, disrupting G-quadruplex through codon optimization for the GAr domain can overcome immune evasion effects on translational inhibition, preventing proteasomal degradation and antigen presentation. Recent work by Zhang et al demonstrated that their mRNA sequence optimization algorithm, LinearDesign, can stabilize mRNA structure to achieve high B-cell and T-cell immunogenicity in vitro and in vivo.

While protein and mRNA sequence design share biological information content and involve constrained combinatorial optimization, combining training objectives at both protein and mRNA codon levels is an attractive research direction in mRNA sequence engineering.

Nucleotide modification and its effects

In the context of antigen-based mRNA therapies, it is essential to understand how design choices influence various immune recognition pathways. Incorporating modified nucleotides such as pseudouridine (Ψ) and N1-methyl-pseudouridine (N1mΨ) into mRNA can evade detection by Toll-like receptors (TLRs), particularly TLR7 and TLR8, thereby evading innate immune recognition. Studies have demonstrated the superior performance of N1mΨ over Ψ or 5-methylcytidine in reducing intracellular innate immunogenicity. Both N1mΨ modification and the reduction of double stranded RNA (dsRNA) impurities are essential for controlling non-specific innate immune response. While reducing innate immunogenicity can decrease potential side effects and increase mRNA stability and efficacy, it must be carefully managed to ensure that antigen-based therapies still elicit a robust T-cell response. Strategies often involve balancing modifications to reduce excessive inflammation while maintaining enough innate immune engagement to promote strong adaptive immunity.

Translational fidelity with modified nucleotide incorporation has been scrutinized, as potential polypeptide sequences resulting from out-of-frame translation may generate undesirable off-target immunogenicity. Some studies found no significant impact of N1mΨ on translational fidelity, while others reported that N 1mΨ causes+1 ribosomal frameshifting in HeLa cells. Therefore, more research with orthogonal experimental systems is warranted. A straightforward computational strategy in mRNA codon optimization can be adopted to reduce risks, such as eliminating “slippery” sequence motifs like four consecutive U’s and screening all possible three-frame translations from the first start codon.

Future directions

mRNA technology offers distinct advantages for cancer vaccine development—rapid manufacturing, versatile therapeutic designs, and antigen processing and presentation within host cells overcome the limitation of synthetic peptides and facilitate more efficient activation of both helper and cytotoxic T cells. A recent review by Sayour et al highlights the transformative potential of mRNA technology in cancer immunotherapy and highlights the importance of optimizing nanoparticle delivery systems and fine-tuning mRNA sequences to balance immunogenicity with safety, a perspective echoed in our work. Additionally, there remains a need to advance computational predictions of immunogenicity and address challenges such as training data limitations and immune dominance in multiantigen presentations. As the field of mRNA vaccines advances, understanding the intricate factors influencing their efficacy, such as adjuvant effects, combination therapy strategies, and the synergy between experimental and computational approaches, becomes increasingly critical.

Adjuvant effects

There is growing evidence that delivery vehicles commonly used for mRNA antigen therapy, especially ionizable lipids, may carry intrinsic adjuvant effects to boost their immunogenicity beyond their primary role of maintaining drug product intracellular stability. For example, Connors et al demonstrated that empty lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) could induce activation and maturation of APCs and promote phagocytosis. Further, fine-tuning the LNP composition and administration route offers the potential to direct immune responses in a more nuanced manner, enhancing either cytotoxic T-cell activation or antibody production as needed for specific therapeutic goals.

Emerging research challenges the classic Th1/Th2 paradigm, suggesting that Th2 responses can also support CD8 T-cell activation under certain conditions. This broader understanding of immune responses provides an opportunity for computational models to integrate complex immune dynamics rather than relying solely on binary Th1/Th2 classifications. By leveraging computational simulations, we can optimize adjuvant choices and delivery strategies to more precisely predict and achieve desired immunogenic profiles.

Additionally, there is growing evidence to support the hypothesis that mRNA-LNP vaccines may trigger broader immunomodulatory effects, including tumor responses. Beyond anecdotal reports of spontaneous tumor regressions on SARS-CoV-2 mRNA vaccination, recent studies have documented cases that underscore the potential of mRNA technology to elicit antitumor immune responses as an incidental effect. This observation further supports the investigation of mRNA vaccines as versatile immunotherapeutics, potentially enhancing antitumor immunity even outside of primary indications. Other adjuvants, such as lipopoly-saccharide, STING, cGAS, CpG oligodeoxynucleotides, and alpha-galactosyleceramide, have also shown promise in preclinical models to improve cellular immune responses of mRNA vaccines.

Combination therapy strategies

Currently, clinical trials of mRNA cancer vaccines primarily test these vaccines in combination with anti-PD1 agents. However, preclinical studies are investigating a variety of combination strategies with other immune modulators. Computational approaches play a complementary role in guiding these experimental efforts by predicting optimal antigen combinations, assessing immunogenicity, and modeling immune interactions. For example, computational simulations can identify promising targets for combination with mRNA vaccines, which can then be validated in preclinical models.

In one study, Jeon et al demonstrated that in a murine tumor model vaccination with TLR agonists combined with anti-CTLA-4 or anti-LAG-3 led to improved antitumor responses, whereas combining TLR agonists with anti-PD-1 impaired antitumor efficacy due to the activation of regulatory T cells. Insights from computational models can aid in interpreting these complex immune dynamics, helping refine combination therapy strategies before clinical translation. This synergy between computational predictions and experimental validation in murine models underscores the potential of computational tools to accelerate and optimize the development of effective combination therapies involving mRNA vaccines.

Experimental and computational synergy

While alterations in antigen processing machinery and HLA loss of heterozygosity could prevent the efficacy of neoantigen therapies, robust predictors of positive response are still needed. Identifying and validating biomarkers of response to individualized neoantigen therapies that can reliably predict efficacy across different tumor microenvironments, whether “cold” or “hot”, remains a considerable challenge and an area of active research requiring interdisciplinary collaboration. Established biomarkers such as tumor mutational burden (TMB) or PD-L1 expression offer valuable insights into immune checkpoint inhibition (ICI) therapies, but their applicability to therapeutic vaccines is limited. Recent studies suggest that TMB alone is insufficient for predicting responses to mRNA cancer vaccines or neoantigen-targeted therapies, as these therapies require a more nuanced understanding of tumor immunogenicity. The concept of persistent TMB (pTMB) has emerged as a promising biomarker, particularly in the context of ICI, where it more accurately predicts sustained therapeutic responses compared with traditional TMB. However, pTMB’s relevance to mRNA vaccines remains to be fully explored. Therefore, extensive translational studies and multi-omics profiling of biopsies from clinical trials, combined with high-throughput immunogenicity assays, are critical to advancing this field. These approaches will help identify new biomarkers and enhance our understanding of the complex interactions between neoantigens and the immune system, potentially leading to more effective shared or personalized cancer immunotherapies.

Furthermore, the importance of experimental validation to support computational predictions cannot be overstated. Despite advancements in T cell epitope profiling and increasing volumes of per-epitope screening data, current methodologies and platforms are significantly limited in their ability to capture the interplay between multiple vaccinating epitopes. While MHC epitope competition and immunodominance are well-documented biological phenomena, the scarcity of data hinders our ability to accurately model these phenomena and refine therapies accordingly. High-throughput longitudinal immunogenicity screening of vaccinated samples is essential for refining bioinformatics tools and ultimately developing better therapeutic strategies.
