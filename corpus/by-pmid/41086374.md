# Humoral immune responses to the second dose of a COVID-19 mRNA vaccine in cancer patients undergoing treatment.

## Abstract

The severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) outbreak in 2020 led to the immediate development of vaccines to combat SARS-CoV-2 infections and coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19). First-generation (2020-2021) COVID-19 vaccines demonstrated high immunogenicity and efficacy, successfully reducing hospitalization, disease severity and COVID-19 related deaths. However, the initial clinical trials for these vaccines focused on overall healthy individuals, and generally excluded high risk populations, such as people with cancer. The SARS-CoV-2 VaccinE Response, Immunological eFfects and safetY in patients with Cancer (VERIFY-C) study formed a unique cohort in early 2021, to capture the initial immune response to BNT162b2 mRNA (Pfizer) vaccination in people with cancer, with or without ongoing/recent exposure to systemic cancer treatment. Fifty-three participants diagnosed with cancer were enrolled. Majority were non-Hispanic, Caucasian females. Patients under cancer treatment had significantly lower antibody responses than those without treatment at pre-second dose and 1-month post-second dose. The levels at 6-months post-second dose were similar between both groups. Patients under treatment had a significantly lower immune response 1-month post-second dose, with an increased immune response at 6-months post-second dose, suggesting a delayed immune response. Further investigations are needed to understand antibody functionality post-vaccination in these individuals.

Humoral immune responses to the second dose of a COVID-19 mRNA vaccine in cancer patients undergoing treatment

ABSTRACT

The severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) outbreak in 2020 led to the immediate development of vaccines to combat SARS-CoV-2 infections and coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19). First-generation (2020–2021) COVID-19 vaccines demonstrated high immunogenicity and efficacy, successfully reducing hospitalization, disease severity and COVID-19 related deaths. Introduction

The outbreak of SARS-CoV-2 infections led to 1.8 million COVID-19 related deaths across the world by the end of 2020, initiating a need to rapidly develop effective COVID-19 vaccines. At the time, the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved an emergency use authorization (EUA) of three COVID vaccines including BNT162b2 (Pfizer), mRNA-1273 (Moderna) and Ad26.COV2.S (Johnson & Johnson) in late 2020 to early 2021.

The initial efficacy was high among all three vaccines. BNT162b2 exhibited 95.0% efficacy at 1-week post-second dose, while mRNA-1273 vaccine efficacy was 94.5% at 2-weeks post-second dose. Meanwhile, the single dose Ad26.COV2.S vaccine demonstrated 66.3% efficacy 2-weeks after vaccination. When comparing the effectiveness of all three vaccines in preventing COVID-19 severe outcomes among adults without immunocompromising conditions, BNT162b2, mRNA-1273 and Ad26.COV2.S showed 88%, 93% and 71% effectiveness against hospitalizations, respectively. However, high-risk populations were largely excluded from these vaccine studies that led to EUA, including people diagnosed with cancer, who were considered high-risk for COVID-19-related deaths.

People diagnosed with cancer or receiving ongoing cancer treatments have weakened immune systems and are more susceptible to infections. In addition, some studies have shown people with cancer have significantly lower vaccine responses compared to people without cancer. Although immune responses were lower, vaccines were safe and well tolerated among people diagnosed with cancer. Early studies on COVID-19 vaccine immunogenicity have demonstrated a higher seroconversion among people with solid tumor cancers compared to hematological cancers (85–95% versus 40–85%, respectively). Additional studies are needed to understand how cancer treatments and their administration timing prior to vaccination can influence immune response to COVID-19 vaccines.

VERIFY-C was a prospective study conducted by Dana-Farber Cancer Institute (DFCI) to evaluate immune responses to BNT162b2 vaccination in people diagnosed with cancer, who underwent systemic cancer treatment. In this study, we assessed the serum antibody responses pre- and post-BNT162b2 vaccination in two groups, including participants who (1) received treatment less than 6-months prior to vaccination and (2) have never received treatment or received treatment greater than 6-months prior to vaccination. The results from this study will improve the understanding of SARS-CoV-2 vaccine responses in people diagnosed with cancer with or without recent cancer treatment.

Materials and methods

Study enrollment

VERIFY-C study enrolled participants to evaluate SARS-CoV-2 vaccine immune responses in people diagnosed with cancer with or without ongoing or recent exposure to systemic cancer treatment. Inclusion criteria include (1) 18 years or older in age, (2) willingness to receive two doses (first and second) of the SARS-CoV-2 vaccine, and (3) confirmation of one or more malignancy diagnoses (including solid and/or hematological malignancy) irrespective of cancer type, disease burden or treatment, and with a primary diagnosis on or after January 1, 1980 to July 17, 2022. Two groups were formulated based on systemic anti-cancer treatment status, including chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy or radiation therapy. Group A (designated as Active-Treatment group) included participants with cancer diagnosis who were receiving systemic anti-cancer treatment within 6-months of the first dose of the COVID-19 vaccine (Supplemental Figure S1A). Group B (designated as Non-Active-Treatment group) included participants with cancer diagnosis who had never received systemic anti-cancer treatment or completed it more than 6-months prior to the first dose of the COVID-19 vaccine. Exclusion criteria include previously confirmed diagnosis of COVID-19 based on past or present positive real-time polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) for SARS-CoV-2. Informed consent was obtained for all participants upon entering the study prior to or at the time of vaccine administration. For the Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups, two doses of BNT162b2 (Pfizer) vaccine were administered from March to June of 2021. A third group, Group C (designated as Non-Cancer group), was selected from a previously published cohort, who were, overall healthy volunteers, not diagnosed with cancer and received two doses of BNT162b2 COVID-19 vaccine and were matched based on age and sex to the Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups in the VERIFY-C cohort.

Sample and data collection

Under the IRB approved DFCI Protocol number 21–049, blood was collected from the study participants and processed at four time points: (1) pre-first COVID vaccine dose, (2) pre-second COVID vaccine dose, (3) 1-month post-second COVID vaccine dose, and (4) 6-months post-second COVID vaccine dose (Supplemental Figure S1B). Frozen sera samples were shipped from the DFCI to the Frederick National Laboratory for Cancer Research (FNLCR) for further serology testing to understand changes in immunological responses to SARS-CoV-2 vaccines in these study participants. Samples were stored at −80°C. Demographic and clinical data were collected through patient-reported outcome questionnaires and medical records, which were then stored in a RedCap database.

Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (ELISA)

Human serum immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibodies to the SARS-CoV-2 spike ancestral strain were quantified by ELISA as previously described. Maxisorp 96-well plates were coated with recombinant SARS-CoV-2 soluble spike and nucleocapsid protein for at least 24 or 48 h, respectively, at 4°C before use. Plates were washed with PBS-Tween (0.2%) wash buffer and blocked with 300 µL PBS-Tween (0.2%)-Skim Milk (4%) blocking buffer at room temperature (RT) for 90 min. Serum samples were initially diluted in blocking buffer at 1:150 or greater before serially diluted at three-fold dilutions. After blocking, plates were washed and incubated with 100 µL of diluted sera at RT for 60 min. After sample incubation, plates were washed and incubated with 100 µL of goat anti-human IgG horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugate (0.067 µg/mL) at RT for 60 min. After conjugate incubation, plates were washed and developed with 100 µL tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) 2-component substrate at RT for 25 min. To stop the TMB reaction, 100 µL of 0.36 N sulfuric acid was added, and plates were read at 450 nm and 620 nm on a plate reader (Molecular Devices, SpectraMax M series). An internal reference standard, calibrated to the World Health Organization (WHO) international standard, was used to calculate the binding antibody units per milliliter (BAU/mL). With our assays, results between batches were comparable with less than 10% variability across quality control (QC) and repeated samples.

Avidity Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay (chaotrope ELISA)

A chaotropic agent, urea, was used at various concentrations to assess the strength of the antibodies bound to SARS-CoV-2 spike. The protocol was the same as above, except an extra step was added after the sample incubation. Plates were washed and incubated with 100 µL of diluted urea (ranging from 0 to 10 molar concentrations [M]) at 25°C for 15 min. After urea incubation, the remaining ELISA procedure was conducted from conjugate to plate reading. Avidity indices (AI80), the molar concentration required to reduce the optical density of the sample to 80% that of untreated wells, were calculated to assess serum avidity.

Statistical analysis

SoftMax Pro GxP 7.0.3 was used to calculate BAU/mL and AI80 responses. GraphPad Prism (v10.3.1) was used to compare immune responses (1) between two groups, at each timepoint, using the nonparametric U Mann–Whitney tests and (2) between multiple time points, within each group, using the nonparametric Kruskal Wallis test with Dunn’s multiple comparisons tests. P-values <.05 were considered significant.

Results

Study participant characteristics

A total of 53 participants were recruited to VERIFY-C, with 20 participants on active treatment and 33 participants not on active treatment (Table 1). The participants on active treatment had an older mean age of 56 years (range: 20–77 years) than the participants not on active treatment (mean age 49 years, range: 24–73 years). Most participants were non-Hispanic, Caucasian, females diagnosed with solid cancer, specifically breast cancer. All of the cancer participants received two doses of the BNT162b2 vaccine, with an overall mean time of 22 days between doses. The Non-Cancer healthy control group (n = 14), with a mean age of 22 years (range: 19–35 years), received two doses of the BNT162b2 vaccine were age- and sex-matched to the Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups. More than half (64%) of the participants were female, and all participants were non-Hispanic, Caucasian (Supplemental Table S1).

All participants in the Active-Treatment group were undergoing cancer treatment within 6-months prior to the first vaccination dose, whereas, in the Non-Active-Treatment group, 79% underwent cancer treatment ≥6-months prior the first dose and 21% never received cancer treatment (Supplemental Table S2). The majority (60% in the Active-Treatment cancer group, 33% in the Non-Active-Treatment group) of those on treatment prior to vaccination underwent a combination of treatments including a mix of chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy or radiation therapy. Post-first vaccine dose, 55% of participants in the Active-Treatment group continued to undergo cancer treatment, with 25% undergoing combination therapy.

Serological IgG responses to SARS-CoV-2 spike protein

IgG antibodies against SARS-CoV-2 spike protein were measured in serum pre- and post-vaccination to investigate serological immune responses to COVID-19 vaccination in participants diagnosed with cancer with and without active treatment. Both patient groups mounted an anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody response as observed from pre-first dose up to 6-months post-second dose (Figure 1A). However, the antibody responses were significantly lower in the Active-Treatment group than in the Non-Active-Treatment group at pre-second dose (41.2 BAU/mL versus 145.4 BAU/mL, p < .01) and 1-month post-second dose (262.3 BAU/mL versus 1458 BAU/mL, p < .001). Compared to the Non-Cancer group (2659 BAU/mL), significantly lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses were observed in patients under active treatment (262.3 BAU/mL, p < .001) and in the Non-Active-Treatment group (1458 BAU/mL, p < .05), at 1-month post-second dose. At 6-months post-second dose, the Non-Cancer group (204.1 BAU/mL) had significantly lower antibody responses compared to Non-Active-Treatment cancer group (470.4 BAU/mL, p < .05). 

When comparing different time points within a group, the Active-Treatment cancer group had significantly higher anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses at 1-month post-second dose (262.3 BAU/mL, p < .01) and 6-months post-second dose (370.1 BAU/mL, p < .001) compared to pre-first dose (26.0 BAU/mL) (Figure 1A and Supplemental Table S3). Anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses were also significantly higher at 6-months post-second dose (370.1 BAU/mL, p < .05) compared to pre-second dose (41.2 BAU/mL). In the Non-Active-Treatment cancer group, there were significantly higher anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses pre-second dose (145.4 BAU/mL, p < .001), 1-month post-second dose (1458 BAU/mL, p < .001), and 6-months post-second dose (470.4 BAU/mL, p < .001) when compared to pre-first dose (21.8 BAU/mL). Anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses were also significantly higher at 1-month post-second dose (1458 BAU/mL, p < .001) compared to pre-second dose (145.4 BAU/mL). Opposite trends were observed at 1-month versus 6-months post-second dose, with higher anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses in patients under treatment and lower responses in the Non-Active-Treatment cancer group at 6-months post-second dose when compared to 1-month post-second dose. No significant differences were observed between 1-month and 6-months post-second dose in the Active- and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups. As expected, and previously found, a significantly higher anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody response was observed in the Non-Cancer group at 1-month compared to 6-months post-second dose (2659 BAU/mL verse 204.1 BAU/mL, p < .001). IgG responses against SARS-CoV-2 Nucleocapsid proteins were also measured but no significant differences were observed between cancer groups or between time points within the cancer groups (data not shown).

Spike IgG Avidity indices (M) were measured to determine antibody strength in binding to spike protein at four time points from pre-first dose to 6-months post-second dose (Figure 1B). No significant differences in avidity indices were observed between the two cancer groups studied at any timepoint. Compared to the Non-Cancer group (3.3 M), significantly higher avidity indices were observed in patients under active cancer treatment (4.0 M, p < .05) at 6-month post-second dose.

When comparing time points within groups, the Active-Treatment cancer group had significantly higher avidity at 6-months post-second dose (4.0 M) when compared to pre-second dose (1.9 M, p < .001) or 1-month post-second dose (2.4 M, p < .01) (Figure 1B and Supplemental Table S3). The Non-Active-Treatment cancer group had significantly higher avidity indices at 1-month post-second dose (2.5 M, p < .01) and 6-months post-second dose (3.9 M, p < .001) when compared to pre-second dose (2.0 M). Avidity index was higher at 6-months post-second dose compared to 1-month post-second dose (3.9 M versus 2.5 M, p < .001). The Non-Cancer control group also had significantly higher avidity at 6-months post-second dose, when compared to 1-month post-second dose (3.3 M verse 2.6 M, p < .01).

Serological responses by cancer type

To determine if cancer types had different trends in IgG responses to COVID-19 vaccination among the groups, the various cancer types were categorized into either hematological or solid cancers, and anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses were compared between the groups (Figure 2A). For solid cancers, the Active-Treatment cancer group had significantly lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses than the Non-Active-Treatment group at pre-second dose (43.5 BAU/mL versus 155.6 BAU/mL, p < .001) and 1-month post-second dose (291.0 BAU/mL versus 1463 BAU/mL, p < .01). The Active-Treatment cancer group (291.0 BAU/mL, p < .001) and the Non-Active-Treatment cancer group (1463 BAU/mL, p < .01) had significantly lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses when compared to the Non-Cancer group (2659 BAU/mL), at 1-month post-second dose. The Non-Active-Treatment cancer group had significantly higher anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses (408.4 BAU/mL, p < .05) when compared to the Non-Cancer group (204.1 BAU/mL) at 6-months post-second dose. For anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses in hematological cancers, no significant differences were observed between the Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups at any time points. However, participants under active cancer treatment had significantly lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses (161.3 BAU/mL, p < .01) when compared to the Non-Cancer group (2659 BAU/mL) at 1-month post-second dose. 

For Spike IgG avidity indices in both cancer types, no significant differences were observed within the Active-Treatment and the Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups at all time points (Figure 2B). However, avidity indices were significantly lower in the Non-Cancer group (3.3 M) compared to solid cancers in the Active-Treatment group (4.0 M, p < .01) at 6-months post-second dose. When comparing time points within each group, solid cancers in the Active-Treatment and the Non-Active-Treatment cancer group had significant increases in avidity indices at multiple time points over time (Figure 2B and Supplemental Table S4) similar to the overall cancer group, in the Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment groups (Figure 1B and Supplemental Table S3). For hematological cancers, significantly higher avidity indices were only observed for the Non-Active-Treatment cancer group at 6-months post-second dose (4.0 M, p < .05) compared to pre-second dose (2.1 M).

Serological response by treatment status

To determine if cancer treatment pre- and post-vaccination resulted in different trends in IgG responses to COVID-19 vaccinations, treatment status was broken down into two groups within each cancer group. In the Active-Treatment cancer group, there are (1) pre-first dose treatment only (on active treatment < 6-months from study entry) and (2) on active treatment pre-first dose and post-second dose. In the Non-Active-Treatment group, there are those (1) pre-first dose treatment only (completed active treatment > 6-months from study entry) and (2) not on active treatment. At each time point, there were no significant differences in antibody responses between the two groups within the Active-Treatment cancer group (Supplemental Figure S2A) and between the two groups within the Non-Active-Treatment group (Supplemental Figure S2B). When comparing antibody responses between time points within each group, there were significant differences in both Active-Treatment and Non-Active-Treatment cancer groups (Supplemental Figure S2B and Supplemental Table S5).

Serological responses by cancer stage

To determine if the cancer stage resulted in different trends for IgG responses to COVID-19 vaccinations, four stages were examined: (1) benign/borderline, (2) local, (3) regional, and (4) distant. In the Active-Treatment cancer group, the majority were local (40%), followed by distant (30%) (Supplemental Table 6). In the Non-Active-Treatment group, the majority were benign/borderline (55%), followed by local (27%). Significant differences between two groups were observed for benign/borderline and local stages. For benign/borderline, the antibody responses were significantly lower in the Active-Treatment cancer group than in the Non-Active-Treatment group at pre-second dose (22.9 BAU/mL versus 201.3 BAU/mL, p < .01) and 1-month post-second dose (186.8 BAU/mL versus 1851 BAU/mL, p < .05) (Supplemental Figure S3A). For local, the Active-Treatment group (92.4 BAU/mL) had a significantly lower antibody response than the Non-Active-Treatment group (967.7 BAU/mL, p < .05) at 1-month post-second dose (Supplemental Figure S3B). No significant differences were observed between the two groups for regional or distant cancer stages (Supplemental Figure S3C-D).

Discussion

The initial clinical trials for COVID-19 vaccines did not include high-risk individuals, such as people diagnosed with cancer, with or without ongoing or recent exposure to systemic treatments. Due to increased susceptibility to severe outcomes of COVID-19, these patients were considered a high priority group for vaccination during the early stages of vaccine distribution. Although patients with cancer were not included in the initial clinical studies in vaccine development, there is a need to understand their immune response to the vaccine, especially if they are currently undergoing or had recent exposure to treatment. Interactions of various types of treatment can weaken immune responses to vaccinations. A more thorough understanding of their immunity will contribute to inform vaccine recommendations to ensure that they are receiving the proper treatment to overcome SARS-CoV-2 infections and severe outcomes of COVID-19. In this study, samples from people diagnosed with cancer from the early stages of COVID vaccine distribution were collected with various types of treatment exposures, with the goal of analyzing the immune responses post-COVID-19 primary vaccination.

Vaccine responses can be affected by the cancer type and treatment regimen, with lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses among those with hematological cancers or undergoing chemotherapy. In this study, individuals with solid cancers on active treatment demonstrated lower anti-SARS-CoV-2 spike antibody responses pre-second dose and 1-month post-second dose compared to those (1) with solid cancer not on active treatment and (2) not diagnosed with cancer. These findings are consistent with previous studies. When assessing the avidity responses, significantly lower avidity responses were only seen in solid cancer patients when compared to healthy, age and sex matched individuals without cancer. In addition, no significant differences between our two cancer groups, with and without treatment, nor between the hematological cancer group and those without cancer. There are limited studies in the literature with IgG avidity activity against SARS-CoV-2 among people with cancer. One study, which compared hematological with solid cancer patients, found that both groups developed anti-receptor-binding domain (RBD) antibodies with similar avidity activity after a second dose. This study is comparable with our findings. Another study examined IgG avidity in people with hematological malignancies after BNT162b2 mRNA vaccination and found that individuals with hematological cancers have significantly higher IgG avidity 2–8 weeks and 4–5 months post-second vaccine compared to people without cancer, in which we didn’t find any significant differences between these two groups in VERIFY-C. A third study also examined IgG avidity in a cohort of individuals with non-multiple myeloma hematological, solid, and no cancer, but only captured responses 1- and 6-months post 3–4 doses of SARS-CoV-2 vaccination, compared to two doses in VERIFY-C. No significant differences in avidity indices were found between patients with solid cancers to the healthy control participants 6-months post 3–4 doses. Overall, our study supports and extends previous findings on IgG avidity for 1- to 6-months post 2 doses of SARS-Cov-2 vaccination.

Strengths of our study include its prospective design and initiation very early in the COVID pandemic, which allowed exclusion of patients with prior COVID infection, and assessment of immunologic parameters in response to the initial vaccine series. Limitations of this study include a small sample size due to the difficulty of recruiting patients with cancer under active treatment, and the need to enroll patients before or at their first COVID-19 vaccination, given rapid and high vaccine uptake rates at the institution. Increased sample size would help confirm and expand our initial findings. Another limitation included only capturing data from 1-month to 6-months after the primary series. Retention of these participants is in general difficult, especially for those undergoing cancer treatments. Capturing later time points post 6-months or after additional vaccinations will be important to understand antibody responses to boosters and their longevity in this population. Additional studies in this population are needed to understand the dynamics of immune response to SARS-CoV-2, either through natural infection or vaccination with more recent COVID-19 variants.

Conclusion

This study provides an initial understanding of immune responses in people diagnosed with cancer, with or without ongoing or recent exposure to systemic treatments. For 1-month post-second dose vaccination, people diagnosed with cancer, with or without treatment have significantly lower antibody responses and avidity activity than people without cancer. Further, larger studies are required to confirm observations that can be broadly generalizable. Studies with increased sample sizes, in different cancer types and different treatments with longer follow-up time are warranted for a better understanding of immune responses in cancer patients under treatment. In addition, further investigations are needed to examine the antibody functionality including neutralization activity and cellular immune responses post vaccination. Overall, our current findings expand the current body of knowledge of immune responses to COVID-19 vaccines in cancer which can help refine COVID-19 policies and recommendations for cancer patients, in particularly for those undergoing cancer treatments.
