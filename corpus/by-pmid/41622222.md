# HIFU postoperative hypoxia-activated metal-organic frameworks modulate the tumor microenvironment to augment immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Limited intensity and duration of high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU)-induced immune response largely hinder postoperative immunotherapy due to low immunogenicity and immunosuppression of tumor microenvironment (TME). In this study, effect-specific metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) were designed based on the severe hypoxia of postoperative tumors through regulating TME to enhance body's antitumor immune response. The combination of iron ions, hypoxic-activated prodrug banoxantrone, and indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) signaling pathway inhibitor NLG919 is utilized to construct MOFs loaded with CaCO3, which achieves intraoperative monitoring via photoacoustic imaging for precise ablation of tumors. Ingeniously, banoxantrone, within the severely hypoxic environment of tumors induced by HIFU, is activated in the manner of converting enemies into friends and cooperates with iron ions to effectively trigger immunogenic cell death (ICD) in tumors. In addition, the immunosuppressive microenvironment exacerbated by postoperative hypoxia is degraded via the cooperation of NLG919, which blocks the IDO-1 signaling pathway and CaCO3, which consumes lactic acid. Based on these improvements, well-designed MOFs effectively inhibit bilateral tumor growth/metastasis and offer a successful paradigm for improving the overall prognosis of HIFU.

Introduction

Malignant tumors pose significant threat to human life and health. HIFU provides a game-changing technology for accurately ablating tumors owing to its noninvasive superiority and spatiotemporal precision. To date, we have witnessed its successes in the clinic. Nevertheless, its broader application has been hindered in that insufficient visualization clarity and energy attenuation of ultrasonic waves in biological tissue led to residual tumor tissue. Our previous research indicated that HIFU stimulates body’s antitumor immune response through the secretion of antigens after ablation. However, in our clinical practice, the intensity and duration of HIFU-induced antitumor immune response are limited and its effectiveness in controlling tumor recurrence and metastasis is unsatisfactory. The reason for this is related to insufficient tumor immunogenicity through weak cavitation or mechanical effects and aggravation of immunosuppressive TME by severe tumor hypoxia after HIFU. Therefore, the development of new universal strategies for simultaneous improvement of tumor immunogenicity and reversal of immunosuppressive metabolic TME after HIFU surgery is of paramount importance.

Fortunately, the rapid development of nanomedicine provides unparalleled opportunities to integrate multifunctional therapeutics and immunologic agents to improve efficacy of immunotherapy. Importantly, alterations in tumor environment characteristics during HIFU treatment constitute fundamental basis for designing HIFU-specific immune synergists. Our previous studies revealed that tumors are severely hypoxic after HIFU ablation. Hypoxia is one of the most well-validated targets in the field of oncology to date. Currently, the majority of HIFU immune synergists are designed to enhance antitumor immune response by alleviating hypoxia. For example, Tang et al. developed nanoparticles encapsulating perfluorocarbons that release oxygen at tumor sites and, along with anti-PD-L1 blockade, activate cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) after HIFU surgery. Yu et al. constructed HIFU-driven nanomotors containing perfluorooctyl bromide and gambogic acid to increase the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and improve robust ferroptosis immunotherapy. The development of targeted imaging and therapy aimed at tumor hypoxia has become a different research direction by turning tumor hypoxia from an intractable issue to selective treatment advantage. Banoxantrone (AQ4N), a representative hypoxia-activated prodrug, is reduced to toxic AQ4 exclusively within hypoxic microenvironment, overcoming hypoxia-induced chemoresistance and resulting in DNA damage and cell death. Surprisingly, in hypoxic environment, AQ4N significantly induces ICD. In addition, AQ4N possesses potent photoacoustic signal, which achieves accurate intraoperative navigation of photoacoustic imaging for tumor ablation and selectively destroys blood vessels. As a result, favorable and severely hypoxic environment is obtained for the subsequent immunotherapy step.

As our understanding of tumor biology and immunotherapy continues to grow, it has become clear that the TME is key factor that hinders cancer immunotherapy through various mechanisms. Hypoxia and acidity, both hallmarks of solid tumors, are known to adversely affect immunotherapy by impairing effector immune cell function and encouraging the growth of immunosuppressive cells, ultimately leading to resistance to chemotherapy, radiotherapy and immunotherapy. Thus, identifying effective strategies to modulate the immunosuppressive TME is of significant practical importance. This link between severe tumor hypoxia after HIFU and immunosuppression prompted us to seek solutions. Blocking the IDO signaling pathway leads to down-regulation of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and increases aggregation of CTLs, thus degrading immunosuppression. NLG919, an exogenous inhibitor, suppresses activity of IDO-1 enzyme in tumor cells, reduces the degradation of tryptophan (Trp) and production of metabolite kynurenine (Kyn) and effectively combats immunosuppression exacerbated by hypoxia. Other studies have shown that lactic acid, byproduct of aerobic and anaerobic glycolysis abundant in TME, is mediated by hypoxia-inducible Factor 1α (HIF-1α) and leads to tumor-associated macrophage polarization toward the M2 phenotype. Recently, CaCO3 has emerged as strong candidate for long-term neutralization of tumor acidity owing to its exceptional proton-scavenging ability and high biocompatibility. The CaCO3 depletion lactate strategy effectively degrades immunosuppression exacerbated by hypoxia and acidity after HIFU in different ways. In our previous study, calcium ions from nanohydroxyapatite were verified to induce T cell infiltration reinforcement. In this study, we further elucidate the underlying mechanisms by which calcium ions contribute to immune regulation.

Guided by the above ideas, structure and composition of nanosystems are designed according to their assigned functions. Accordingly, we constructed carrier-free MOFs loaded with CaCO3 (Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919@CaCO3) by coordinating Fe3+ with AQ4N and NLG919 to enhance immunotherapy through improving tumor immunogenicity and reshaping the TME (Scheme 1). This design fully leverages the high drug-loading capacity of MOFs and advantage of avoiding potential safety issues associated with exogenous carriers. After size regulation, the MOFs efficiently accumulate at tumor site, are triggered to release through low pH in tumor tissues/cellular endosomes and provide tumor cells with large amounts of Fe3+, AQ4N, NLG919 and CaCO3 before HIFU surgery. This enables photoacoustic imaging to more accurately guide tumor ablation and provides severely hypoxic environment. Moreover, in this environment, the introduced AQ4N synergized with Fe3+ to carry out specific treatment of residual tissues and efficiently induced ICD. NLG919, which blocks the IDO-1 signaling pathway and CaCO3-consuming lactate, can subsequently reverse TME immunosuppression aggravated by hypoxia/acidity and thus significantly potentiate ICD immunotherapies toward solid tumors. Ultimately, our strategy effectively induces intense and persistent immune response after HIFU by agonists with different mechanisms, prevents postoperative recurrence and metastasis and provides a successful paradigm for improving overall prognosis of all surgical procedures, including HIFU.

Materials and methods

Materials

Commercially available reagents were used without further purification unless noted otherwise. AQ4N was purchased from MedChemExpress (USA). NLG919, triethylamine (TEA), iron (III) chloride hexahydrate (FeCl3·6H2O), calcium chloride and sodium carbonate were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Milwaukee, USA). 2-(4-amidinophenyl)-6-indolecarbamidine dihydrochloride (DAPI), cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8), HIF-1α antibody, FITC goat anti-rabbit IgG secondary antibody, Calcein acetoxymethyl ester (Calcein-AM), Annexin V-FITC, propidium iodide (PI), Fluo-4 AM, adenosine triphosphate (ATP) Assay Kit and Terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase dUTP Nick-End Labeling (TUNEL) Kit were obtained from Beyotime Biotechnology Co., Ltd (China). Lactic Acid assay kit was purchased from Nanjing Jiancheng Bioengineering Institute. Proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) antibody, calreticulin (CRT) antibody and high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) antibody were purchased from Novus Biologicals (USA). Anti-HIF-1α, CRT, γH2A.X antibodies and Goat anti-rabbit IgG H&L (DyLight® 488), IFN-γ protein were bought from Abcam (USA). Cleaved caspase 1 antibodies, GSDMD-N antibodies, Cleaved caspase 3 antibodies, GSDME-N antibodies, IDO antibodies and Tubulin antibodies were bought from Proteintech Group, Inc. (USA). PE-Cy5.5 anti-mouse CD3 antibody, PE anti-mouse CD8a antibody, PE anti-mouse CD80 antibody, APC anti-mouse CD86, FITC anti-mouse CD11c, APC anti-mouse CD25, PE anti-mouse ForxP3, FITC anti-mouse F4/80, APC anti-mouse CD206 were purchased from BioLegend (USA). FITC anti-mouse CD4 was purchased from 4 A BIOTECH (China). CD40, CD44 and CD66L antibodies were from Elabscience Biotechnology Co., Ltd (China). RPMI 1640 medium, Tripsin-EDTA (0.05%), penicillin-streptomycin and fetal bovine serum (FBS) were purchased from Gibco.

Preparation of Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919@CaCO3 (AFNC MOFs)

Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919@CaCO3 was prepared according to an approach described in the literature with some modifications. First, NLG919 (12.50 mg, 44.27 ) was dissolved in 2.50 mL of DMSO, followed by the addition of 2.50 mL AQ4N (62 mg, 119.83 ) solution. The mixture was stirred at room temperature. After 1 h, 0.50 mL FeCl3·6H2O (1.30 mg, 4.80 ) was added to mixed solution and reacted for 3 h. Subsequently, 0.50 mL of (C2H5)3N was added drop by drop. The mixture was added to 10 mL of H2O under vigorous stirring conditions to form MOFs by self-assembly. After dialysis, pure Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919 (AFN MOFs) was obtained. Next, Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919@CaCO3 was prepared by adding 2.66 mL of CaCl2 (1.42 mg, 12.80 ) to Fe3+/AQ4N/NLG919 and stirring for 2 h, then dropwise adding 2.54 mL of Na2CO3 (12.92 mg, 121.92 ) to the mixture with stirring for 2 h. Finally, purified Fe3+/AQ4N/LG919@CaCO3 was obtained by dialysis and was stored at 4 °C for backup.

Characterization of AFNC MOFs

Detailed characterizations of the prepared formulations were conducted. The zeta potential and hydrodynamic size of nanoparticles were detected by dynamic light scattering (DLS) at 37 ℃ using Malvern Zeta Sizer (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK). The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of AFNC MOFs was obtained by using JEM1200EX transmission electron microscope. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analyses were performed to clarify the elemental composition of AFNC MOFs (Thermo Scientific K-Alpha, USA). XRD patterns were tested utilizing an X-ray powder diffractometer (Rigaku Ultima IV, Japan). FT-IR spectra were determined via Fourier transform infrared spectrometer (Thermo Scientific Nicolet iS20, USA). Ultraviolet–visible (UV–vis) spectra were acquired by using UV–vis spectrophotometer (NanoDrop 2000, USA) with at least three independent scans. Elemental analyses were performed using FEI Tecnai G2 F20 transmission electron microscope (FEI Corporation, USA) equipped with energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS). The in vitro stability of nanoparticles was studied by recording average particle size changes in physiological buffered saline (PBS) (pH 7.4) containing 10% FBS, H2O and RPMI 1640 for 24 h. DLS and TEM were used to detect the size change of AFNC MOFs in various PBS (pH 4.5 and pH 7.4).

Cell culture

4T1 cells were cultured with RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. The cells were incubated humidly at 37 °C with 5% CO2.

In vitro drug release, cellular uptake and cytotoxicity

The pH-dependent drug release profile of AFNC MOFs was assessed by placing 1 mL of formulation into a dialysis tube submerged in 25 mL buffer solutions (pH 7.4 and 4.5) at room temperature. Aliquots (0.02 mL) were taken at intervals of 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, 24 and 48 h. Concentrations of AQ4N and NLG919 were measured at 618 nm and 280 nm using UV–vis spectrophotometry. Calcium ion concentration was detected using a calcium ion detection kit.

Cellular uptake was investigated using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) and flow cytometry (FCM). Briefly, 4T1 cells were incubated with free AQ4N or AFNC MOFs (20 µg/mL AQ4N) for 24 h, followed by washing with PBS and DAPI staining. CLSM was used to visualize fluorescence and FCM was employed for quantitative analysis after 4 h of treatment. Cells were harvested, washed and centrifuged before fluorescence intensity was measured.

Cytotoxicity was assessed using the CCK-8 assay. 4T1 cells (1 × 104 cells per well) were cultured for 24 h, followed by treatment with varying concentrations of free AQ4N or AFNC MOFs (0.01 to 100 µg/mL AQ4N) under normoxic and hypoxic conditions for an additional 24 h. Absorbance at 450 nm was measured 4 h post-incubation with CCK-8 solution (CCK-8:1640 = 1:9). Cytotoxicity on human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) was similarly evaluated across different concentrations.

In vitro live/dead cell staining and apoptosis analysis

Live/dead cell staining was performed using the Calcein-AM/PI kit. 4T1 cells (confocal Petri dish, 4 × 104 per well) were treated with free AQ4N or AFNC MOFs under normoxic or hypoxic conditions for 48 h, washed with PBS, stained with Calcein-AM/PI and visualized by CLSM. Apoptosis was analyzed via FCM. Cells (6-well plates, 2 × 105 per well) were treated with AQ4N or AFNC MOFs under normoxic or hypoxic conditions for 48 h, then washed and stained with Annexin V-FITC/PI using the manufacturer’s protocol before FCM analysis.

In vitro ICD induction capacity analysis

CRT and HMGB1 expression, as well as ATP release, were evaluated in 4T1 cells treated with AQ4N or AFNC MOFs for 48 h under normoxic or hypoxic conditions. Cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, stained with CRT or HMGB1 primary antibodies followed by fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies and examined using CLSM. ATP release was quantified using an ATP assay kit.

4T1 cells were seeded and subjected to the indicated treatments, after which the cells were collected and washed with PBS. For CRT detection, cells were resuspended in staining buffer (PBS containing 1–2% BSA) and incubated with an anti-calreticulin primary antibody under non-permeabilized conditions at 4 °C overnight. On the following day, cells were washed thoroughly and incubated with a FITC-conjugated secondary antibody at 4 °C in the dark for 30–60 min. CRT surface exposure was subsequently analyzed by flow cytometry using the FITC channel.

In vitro evaluation of IDO enzymatic activity, accumulation of intracellular Ca2+ion and lactic acid consumption capacity

The IDO inhibitory activity of AFNC MOFs was then examined by measuring inhibition efficiency of conversion of Trp to Kyn in 4T1 cells. Initially, 4T1 cells were plated into 96-well plates at a density of 5 × 103 cells per well and permitted to proliferate for 24 h. Following this, the cell culture medium was replaced with fresh medium. To induce IDO expression, recombinant mouse IFN-γ (50 ng/mL) was introduced into each well. Concurrently, the cells were subjected to treatment with varying concentrations of AFNC MOFs and free NLG919, with NLG919 concentrations ranging from 0.3125 to 5 µM. After 24 h incubation period, 150 µL of supernatant from each well was collected and mixed with trichloroacetic acid (75 µL, 30%) to hydrolyze N-formylkynurenine into Kyn at 50 °C for 30 min. Subsequently, the mixture was combined with an equal volume of Ehrlich reagent (2% p-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde w/v in glacial acetic acid) and allowed to react for 10 min at room temperature. The absorbance of resultant reaction was then quantified at 490 nm using microplate reader.

To detect the accumulation of intracellular Ca2+ ions, 4T1 cells were seeded into confocal dishes and cultured for 24 h at 37 ℃. The culture medium was subsequently replaced with fresh medium containing AFN MOFs and AFNC MOFs (20 µg/mL) for 4 h of co-culture. Following this, the cells were washed with PBS and incubated with commercial intracellular Ca2+ probe, Fluo-4 AM, for 30 min. The fluorescence was then monitored using CLSM.

To ascertain the impact of AFNC MOFs on lactic acid consumption, PBS, AFN MOFs and AFNC MOFs were subjected to incubation with an equivalent quantity of lactic acid in shaker at 37 °C for 4 h. Subsequently, the residual lactic acid in each group was quantified utilizing lactic acid assay kit.

Laser confocal microscopy to observe morphological changes of treated cells

The laser confocal Petri dish was chosen as carrier for cell culture. 4T1 cells were cultured in 1 mL of medium for 24 h and cell inoculum of each well was controlled at about 1 × 105. The original medium was replaced with 1 mL of fresh medium containing different components (PBS, AFN MOFs or AFNC MOFs) (AQ4N concentration of 200 µg/mL). Subsequently, the cells were rinsed with PBS and immediately after rinsing, 4T1 cells were observed using laser confocal microscope to capture characteristics of morphological changes of cells under different treatment conditions.

In vitro and in vivo PA imaging of AFNC MOFs

Initially, various concentrations of AFNC MOFs were synthesized to evaluate their PA imaging capabilities in vitro. Furthermore, confocal dishes were layered with AFNC MOFs solution (6 mg/mL) and overlaid with pig skin of varying thicknesses (0.25, 0.50, 1.00, 1.50 and 2.00 cm) respectively. The tissue penetration depth of AFNC MOFs (6 mg/mL) was assessed using PA imager at an excitation wavelength of 690 nm and a normalized curve depicting relationship between photoacoustic signal intensity and irradiation depth was derived. In the context of in vivo PA imaging, 200 µL of AFNC MOFs (AQ4N: 2 mg/mL) was administered intravenously to each mouse. Subsequently, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were injected intravenously with either free AQ4N or AFNC MOFs at an equivalent AQ4N dose of 20 mg/kg and PA imaging was conducted at different time points (8, 12 and 24 h) using PA imaging system. Following imaging, the mice were humanely euthanized and tumors were excised for subsequent confocal detection.

Animal

All animal experiments were conducted in accordance with the ARRIVE guidelines and the Declaration of Helsinki and were approved by the Ethical Committee for Laboratory Animals of Chongqing Medical University (Approval No. 2022156). 4T1 tumor modal was generated by the standard subcutaneous inoculation on female Balb/c mice. 1 × 106 4T1 cells were inoculated in left bank of Balb/c mice, followed by second inoculation in right bank of the same mice 4 days later. These mice were applied for treatment when the primary tumor volumes reached 80–100 mm3.

Tumor hypoxia evaluation

Initially, mice bearing 4T1 tumors were administered PBS injections, followed by the irradiation of primary tumors using HIFU. Following treatment intervals of 0, 4, 12, and 24 h, primary tumors were excised from mice and subjected to immunofluorescence staining to assess the expression levels of HIF-1. Subsequently, 4T1 xenografted 24 h post-irradiation, mice were anesthetized and positioned within PA imaging system. Oxygen saturation (sO2) served as an indicator to evaluate the intensity of tumor hypoxia. Ultimately, 24 h post-HIFU ablation, the oxygen partial pressure was measured using an optical oxygen partial pressure microelectrode. An implanted microelectrode was inserted into tumor site at a depth of 1 mm to monitor changes in the partial pressure of oxygen within tissue.

Pharmacokinetics and biodistribution

For pharmacokinetic studies, the mice were randomly divided into two groups (n = 3) and i.v. injected with AQ4N or AFNC MOFs. The blood samples (0.1 mL) were collected from retro-orbital sinus at predetermined intervals post-injection. Each sample was then combined with PBS (900 µL) containing 10.0 mM EDTA as an anticoagulant. Following this, all blood samples underwent centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 10 min to extract the plasma. Subsequently, proteins were precipitated by adding acetonitrile (120 µL) and incubating the mixture for 3 h at 4 ℃. The concentration of AQ4N in resultant supernatant was quantified using UV–vis spectroscopy.

For biodistribution quantification, mice (n = 3) were euthanized at predetermined time points, and tumor tissues were collected. Approximately 0.1 g of homogenized tumor sample was treated with 1 mL of hydrochloric acid to ensure complete dissolution. After centrifugation, the resulting supernatant was subjected to liquid–liquid extraction using a chloroform/isopropanol mixture (3:1, v/v). The extracted phase was subsequently analyzed for AQ4N content using UV–vis spectroscopy.

In vivo antitumor efficacy and anti-metastasis effect evaluation

For in vivo combination treatment, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into seven groups (n = 5) and subjected to the following treatments when their tumors reached a size of 80 ~ 100 mm3: I: PBS; II: AQ4N; III: AF MOFs; IV: HIFU; V: AF MOFs+HIFU; VI: AFN MOFs+HIFU; VII: AFNC MOFs+HIFU. 24 h post intravenous injection, the groups designated to receive HIFU treatment (AF MOFs+HIFU, AFN MOFs+HIFU and AFNC MOFs+HIFU) were exposed to HIFU on their tumors in situ (100 w, 3 s). Treatments were administered every five days for a total of three sessions. The tumors’ dimensions and mice’s body weights were recorded every two days using Vernier caliper and an electronic balance, respectively. Tumor volumes were computed using the formula: tumor volume=(width2×length)/2. Mice were considered deceased when their tumor volume reached 2000 mm3. Upon the completion of treatment, primary tumors underwent dissection to prepare frozen sections. These sections were subsequently stained using markers such as PCNA, TUNEL, HMGB1 and CRT and then examined under CLSM.

For anti-metastasis effect evaluation, mouse models bearing 4T1 tumors were randomly assigned to seven groups: PBS; AQ4N; AF MOFs; HIFU; AF MOFs+HIFU; AFN MOFs+HIFU and AFNC MOFs+HIFU. After 12 h of injection, the primary tumors of mice in AF MOFs+HIFU, AFN MOFs+HIFU and AFNC MOFs+HIFU groups were subjected to HIFU treatment. Following 15-day treatment period, the mice were humanely euthanized and their lung tissues were harvested for Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining to assess tumor metastasis. The extent of lung metastasis was quantified by counting number of tumor nodules present in each lung.

In vivo IDO Inhibition and lactic acid consumption capacity

To assess the inhibitory impact of MOFs on production of intratumoral Kyn, mice were humanely euthanized and their tumors were excised, weighed in their wet state and subsequently homogenized. The homogenates were then treated with a mixed solution of water, trichloroacetic acid and acetonitrile (62%/30%/8%) at 50 ℃ for 4 h to ensure complete hydrolysis of N-formylkynurenine. Following this, the supernatants were obtained through centrifugation and analyzed using microplate reader to determine concentrations of Kyn and Trp, with measurements taken at 280 nm for Trp and 490 nm for Kyn. To investigate the consumption of lactic acid, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into three groups (with three mice in each group) when tumor volumes reached 100 mm3. The groups were then intravenously administered with PBS, AFN MOFs or AFNC MOFs, 24 h post-injection, the lactic acid levels in tumor tissues extracted from mice were quantified using lactic acid detection kit.

In vivo assessment of tumor-infiltrating immune cells

The immune cells within tumor tissue were subjected to analysis using FCM. Upon completion of experimental treatment, tumors were excised, sectioned into minute fragments and subsequently digested using cocktail of enzymes collagenase IV (1 mg/mL), hyaluronidase (0.2 mg/mL) and DNase I (0.2 mg/mL) at 37 ℃ for 30 min. Following the enzymatic digestion, resultant suspension underwent filtration through nylon meshes to yield a single-cell suspension. The immune cell populations residing in tumor tissues were then labeled with their respective antibodies: this encompassed dendritic cells (DCs) maturation markers (CD11c+CD80+CD86+), CTLs (CD3+CD8+), T helper cells (Th cells) (CD3+CD4+), Tregs (CD4+CD25+Foxp3+) and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs, with M1-like TAMs identified by CD11b+F4/80+CD86+ and M2-like TAMs by CD11b+F4/80+CD206+). These cells were subsequently quantified using FCM.

Immune memory detection

To evaluate the induction of immune memory, a tumor rechallenge experiment was conducted. On day 30 post-treatment, 2 × 105 4T1 cells were injected into left flank of surviving mice in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group. Simultaneously, an equal number of 4T1 cells were injected into treatment-naïve mice to serve as control group. Tumor growth and survival status of rechallenged mice were monitored every other day to assess long-term immune protection. To further investigate the immunological mechanisms underlying tumor resistance, flow cytometry analysis was performed on the spleen collected from 4T1 tumor-bearing mice on day 40 post-treatment. Single-cell suspensions were prepared by mechanical dissociation, followed by centrifugation at 1,500 rpm for 5 min. Red blood cells (RBCs) were subsequently lysed using modified RBC lysis buffer for 5 min at room temperature and the remaining cells were washed twice with PBS to ensure sample purity. The maturation status of key immune cell subsets, including central memory T cells (TCM, CD44⁺CD62L⁺) and effector memory T cells (TEM, CD44⁺CD62L⁻), was assessed via flow cytometry, providing insights into establishment of immunological memory.

Western blotting analysis

Upon completion of the treatment regimen in mice, approximately 50 mg of tumor tissue was harvested from each group and homogenized using homogenizer. Subsequently, cells were lysed in cold RIPA buffer supplemented with protease inhibitors for 30 min. The total protein concentration was then ascertained using BCA assay kit. Protein extracts, at a quantity of 30 µg, were resolved by 12% SDS-PAGE gels and subsequently transferred onto PVDF membranes. The membranes were subjected to incubation with a cocktail of primary antibodies, including NFAT, p-NFAT, anti-Cleaved-caspase-1, anti-GSDMD-N, anti-Cleaved-caspase-3, anti-GSDME-N, anti-CRT, anti-HMGB1, anti-γH2A.X, anti-IDO and anti-Tubulin antibodies, at 4 ℃ for duration of one night. Following this, the corresponding secondary antibodies were applied at room temperature for 1 h. Ultimately, images were captured using gel imaging system equipped with darkroom capabilities for chemiluminescence detection. Tubulin served as an internal control and the protein band intensities were quantified using ImageJ software.

Biocompatibility study

To assess biosafety of nanoparticles, the levels of aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine (CREA), red blood cells (RBC), white blood cells (WBC), hemoglobin (HGB), hematocrit (HCT) and platelet (PLT), in serum were determined in order to assess condition of liver and kidneys after 1, 7, 15 and 30 days of injection of AFNC MOFs. Moreover, the major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidneys and tumors) of mice were harvested for histological analysis. To determine the hemolysis rate of AFNC MOFs, red blood cells (RBCs) were extracted from mice and subsequently combined with varying concentrations of AFNC MOFs (1.25, 2.5, 5, 10, 20, 40 and 80 µg/mL) for 4 h at a temperature of 37 °C. Following this, 0.1 mL of the supernatant was transferred to a 96-well plate and absorbance was measured at 541 nm using microplate reader (Infinite M200 Pro N, TECAN, Switzerland). Following a 12 h post-injection period, the primary tumors of mice in AF MOFs+HIFU, AFN MOFs+HIFU and AFNC MOFs+HIFU groups were subjected to HIFU treatment. After 15-day treatment, the mice were humanely euthanized and their organs were harvested for histopathological examination.

Statistics and reproducibility

For quantitative analysis, a minimum of three replicates were examined. Data are displayed as the mean ± SD). Statistical analysis between groups was performed using GraphPad Prism (v.8.0). When comparing two groups, unpaired, two-tailed Student t-tests were applied. The sample sizes (biological replicates), specific statistical tests and main effects of our statistical analyses for each experiment are detailed in each figure legend. Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization of AFNC MOFs

As illustrated in Scheme 1a, the desirable AFN MOFs were prepared through self-assembly of AQ4N, Fe3+ and NLG919 via ligand interactions by adjusting feed ratios. Subsequently, mineralized layer was formed by coating CaCO3 onto the surface of AFN MOFs, thereby creating AFNC MOFs. Notably, the carrier-free nature of AFNC MOFs enabled an exceptionally high loading capacity for each constituent, as determined through UV–vis spectroscopy standard curves and quantitative ICP-OES analysis: 84.21% for AQ4N, 4.39% for Fe3+, 2.10% for NLG919 and 9.30% for CaCO3. DLS and TEM images revealed that AFNC MOFs possess an appropriate size of 160 nm for tumor accumulation through EPR effect, characterized by regular morphology (Fig. 1a and b). Moreover, X-ray diffraction (XRD) indicates that AFNC MOFs has crystalline nature (Fig. 1c). The successful incorporation of components into AFNC MOFs was further confirmed by presence of two distinct peak absorptions at 280 nm for NLG919 and 618 nm for AQ4N in UV–vis absorption spectrogram (Figs. 1d and S1a). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) results indicated the detection of elements such as C, O, N, Fe and Ca within AFNC MOFs (Fig. 1e and f). EDS and elemental mapping (Figs. 1g and S1b) not only confirmed the distribution of elements and verified their presence within the MOFs structure, but also, in conjunction with the particle size analysis of AFN MOFs (Fig. S1c), provided multiple experimental evidences for the successful preparation of AFNC MOFs. The formation of AFNC MOFs was further evidenced by peak at 1408.58 cm− 1, representing C-H in-plane bending vibration of cyclohexyl group of NLG919 and another peak at 1630.79 cm− 1, reflecting C = O stretching vibration for AQ4N, in Fourier transform infrared (FT-IR) spectrum (Fig. 1h, Table S1).

Next, we assessed the selective responsiveness of AFNC MOFs in acidic TME. Results showed that the size of AFNC MOFs remained stable in 10% FBS, RPMI-1640 or H2O at 37 °C for 24 h (Fig. 1i), which is crucial for long circulation and tumor accumulation of MOFs. However, the size rapidly decreased from 160 nm to around 7 nm when exposed to acidic PBS (Fig. S1d). AFNC MOFs exhibited a rapid release pattern in PBS at pH 4.5, achieving the highest cumulative drug release rate compared to that in PBS at pH 7.4 (Figs. 1j, S1e and S1f). In addition, we examined the PDI and zeta potentials of AFNC MOFs at different pH (Figs. S1g and S1h). Collectively, our results demonstrated that AFNC MOFs possessed ideal acid microenvironment responsive drug release behavior, superior drug loading capacity and exceptional stability. In addition, thanks to the mild reaction conditions, straightforward operation process and universal equipment requirements, AFNC MOFs demonstrate excellent prospects for clinical translation. Its drug-loading mechanism based on coordination chemistry exhibits remarkable universality—any chemotherapeutic drug containing lone pair electrons can achieve self-assembly drug loading through metal coordination, which not only broadens the choice of therapeutic regimens, but also provides a reliable technological pathway for large-scale production.

In vitro cell uptake and cytotoxicity

To study the interaction between AFNC MOFs and 4T1 cells in vitro, we first investigated the distribution of AFNC MOFs within 4T1 cells using CLSM. As depicted in the results, AFNC MOFs were efficiently internalized by 4T1 cells via endocytosis and exposed to the mildly acidic endo/lysosomal environment, where pH-sensitive coordination interactions in the MOF framework were destabilized, resulting in the release of AQ4N. The liberated AQ4N then diffused into the cytoplasm and nucleus (Fig. 2a and b). Subsequently, we used the intrinsic fluorescence of AQ4N to quantitatively detect intracellular drug accumulation via flow cytometry. As depicted in Fig. 2c and d, both the AQ4N and AFNC MOFs groups exhibited similar fluorescence intensities, further confirming that AFNC MOFs were effectively internalized by 4T1 cells.

In addition, we carefully assessed the in vitro cytotoxicity of AFNC MOFs on 4T1 cells by CCK-8 assay, apoptosis assay and live/dead cell assay. In normal cells, the CCK-8 assay demonstrated that AFNC MOFs exhibited low cytotoxicity, with acceptably impact on cell viability (Fig. S2a). In contrast, when tested on 4T1 cells, the CCK-8 assay revealed substantial reduction in cell viability within hypoxic AFNC MOFs group, with cell viability decreasing to 19.40% as AQ4N concentration reached 100 µg/mL, compared to the control and normoxic groups (Fig. 2e). In addition, the cytotoxicity profiles of NLG919 and CaCO3 were systematically evaluated under both normoxic and hypoxic conditions. Neither NLG919 nor CaCO3 alone induced significant cytotoxic effects, as cell viability remained above 90% in all cases. These findings indicate that both components are biocompatible and suitable for further combination therapies. Notably, when cells were treated with combination of AQ4N, NLG919 and CaCO3 under hypoxic conditions, a marked decrease in cell viability was observed, dropping to approximately 30% (Figs. S2c-d). This pronounced reduction in cell viability is primarily attributed to the conversion of AQ4N into its cytotoxic form, AQ4, under hypoxic conditions, which significantly enhances its tumoricidal effects.

To further corroborate these findings, an apoptosis assay was conducted, revealing that AFNC MOFs under hypoxic conditions induced the highest apoptosis rate, reaching 65.07%, with early apoptosis accounting for 50.39% and late apoptosis for 14.66% (Fig. 2f and g). Elevated apoptosis rate underscores the enhanced efficacy of AFNC MOFs in hypoxic environments, which may be attributed to the entry of AFNC MOFs into cells through endocytosis, thereby bypassing the cellular ion transport regulatory mechanisms. This process facilitates the influx of a large number of iron and calcium ions, ultimately triggering pyroptosis. Similarly, results from the live/dead cell assay aligned with these observations, as CLSM images depicted that hypoxic AFNC MOFs group exhibited the strongest red fluorescence signal, indicating substantial population of dead cells (Fig. 2h and i). Collectively, these findings highlight that AFNC MOFs not only demonstrate efficient cellular internalization but also exhibit potent anti-tumor effects under hypoxic conditions, which provides the basis for subsequent effective killing of tumor cells under the highly hypoxic environment produced by HIFU in vivo.

In vitro cell uptake and cytotoxicity

Previously, AQ4N has demonstrated potent chemotherapeutic activity primarily through the direct induction of cancer cell death. Here, to further broaden its therapeutic functions and mechanisms, we explored whether it could trigger ICD within tumor cells. To this end, we evaluated the release of key damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), such as ATP, HMGB1 and CRT, which are crucial for antigen presentation and immune system activation. As depicted in Fig. 3a and b and Fig. S3, under hypoxic conditions, AFNC MOFs group exhibited the most intense CRT green fluorescence, indicating substantial CRT exposure on surface of 4T1 cells, which was in agreement with the corresponding flow cytometric analysis of CRT expression. Additionally, AFNC MOFs-treated 4T1 cells displayed weak green fluorescence under hypoxic conditions (Fig. 3a and c), signifying significant extracellular release of HMGB1. Similarly, ATP levels were significantly elevated in AFNC MOFs group under hypoxic conditions (Fig. 3d), further supporting the notion that AFNC MOFs can effectively trigger ICD. Beyond inducing ICD, pyroptosis, as a highly inflammatory form of programmed cell death, has been increasingly recognized for its role in modulating anti-tumor immunity. Given that AFNC MOFs enter cells through endocytosis, they bypass the regulation of cellular ion transport, leading to the accumulation of Fe3+ and Ca2+ within cells. This elevated concentration of Fe3+ and Ca2+ concentrations disrupts the osmotic balance within cells, thereby activating pyroptosis pathways mediated by Caspase-1/gasdermin D (GSDMD) and Caspase-3/gasdermin E (GSDME), respectively.

To determine whether AFNC MOFs can effectively induce pyroptosis, we conducted CLSM imaging and Western blot analyses to assess morphological changes and key protein expression in 4T1 cells following different treatments. In bright-field CLSM images, dead cells exhibited the characteristic features of pyroptosis, including swelling and rupture of plasma membrane. Notably, AFNC MOFs group exhibited significant increase in dead cells, in stark contrast to the predominance of viable cells observed in PBS group (Fig. S4). As shown in Figs. S5a and S6, Western blot analysis further validated these findings, demonstrating that treatment with AFNC MOFs triggered cleavage activation of caspase-1 and caspase-3, along with an increase in expression of GSDMD-N and GSDMD-N, thereby confirming the occurrence of pyroptosis.

On the other hand, evaluating calcium carbonate payload is essential for assessing the ability of AFNC MOFs to modulate immunosuppressive TME by consuming lactate in vitro. To investigate this, we first measured intracellular Ca²⁺ levels using the Fluo-4-AM reagent. As illustrated in Fig. 3e and f, AFNC MOFs group displayed significantly higher green fluorescence intensity in cytoplasm compared to the other groups. This indicates that calcium carbonate was effectively internalized alongside the MOFs, leading to notable increase in cytoplasmic Ca2+ levels. In addition to calcium ion detection, we assessed the intracellular lactate content to evaluate the potential of AFNC MOFs in reducing lactic acid, a key contributor to tumor immunosuppression. As shown in Fig. 3g, the lactate level in AFNC MOFs group was approximately half that of control groups, further confirming successful loading of calcium carbonate and its role in significantly decreasing intracellular lactic acid levels. This reduction in lactate may alleviate the immunosuppressive microenvironment, promoting a more favorable setting for anti-tumor immune responses. To further elucidate the underlying mechanisms by which calcium ions contribute to immune regulation, we investigated whether elevated intracellular calcium could activate specific immunoregulatory signaling pathways. Given the pivotal role of the nuclear factor of activated T cells (NFAT) pathway in calcium-dependent transcriptional regulation, we evaluated its activation as a representative downstream cascade. Western blot analysis revealed pronounced NFAT dephosphorylation in T lymphocytes following AFNC MOF treatment (Fig. S5b), indicating robust activation of the NFAT signaling pathway. These results demonstrate that AFNC MOFs not only deliver functional calcium ions but also engage calcium-sensitive transcriptional programs, thereby broadening the therapeutic mechanisms of calcium-based nanotherapeutics in immunomodulation.

Meanwhile, we hope to introduce small molecule IDO inhibitor NLG919 to block IDO signaling pathway, thereby alleviating the immunosuppressive microenvironment of tumors. Therefore, we measured the Kyn content in 4T1 cells treated with AFNC MOFs and plotted inhibition rate curve of Kyn to evaluate its inhibitory effect on IDO enzyme activity in vitro. Encouragingly, as shown in Fig. 3h, AFNC MOFs group and NLG919 group have comparable IDO enzyme inhibitory effects (the final inhibition rates of Kyn in AFNC MOFs group and NLG919 group are 82.90% and 76.50% respectively). In summary, the experimental results demonstrate that AFNC MOFs significantly consume lactate, inhibit IDO enzyme activity in vitro and elicit potent ICD effect. These findings provide strong basis for potential therapeutic application of AFNC MOFs in enhancing immunogenicity and overcoming immune suppression by modulating TME.

Intratumoral drug accumulation, in vitro and in vivo photoacoustic imaging and assessment of tumor hypoxia levels

To assess the tumor-targeting efficiency and in vivo biodistribution of therapeutic system, we first utilized the intrinsic fluorescence of AQ4N to visualize its localization via CLSM. As illustrated in Fig. 4a and b, 24 h post-injection, significantly stronger red fluorescence was observed in the nuclei of tumor cells in the AFNC MOFs group compared to free AQ4N group, indicating superior tumor accumulation and intracellular delivery mediated by AFNC MOFs. This finding highlights the effective tumor-specific enrichment achieved by the AFNC MOFs system, laying the foundation for its therapeutic potential.

To obtain more quantitative evaluation of biodistribution, we measured AQ4N content in major organs tumor tissues at various time intervals. As shown in Fig. 4c, the tumor accumulation of AFNC MOFs reached 5.34% ID g-1 at 8 h, peaked at 20.54% ID g-1 at 12 h and decreased to 10.30% ID g-1 by 24 h, demonstrating a time-dependent accumulation and retention profile. These data underscore the capacity of AFNC MOFs for efficient tumor localization and prolonged drug availability. Concurrently, pharmacokinetic analysis (Fig. 4d) revealed that 48 h post-injection, 27.45% of AFNC MOFs remained in circulation, while only 7.33% of free AQ4N was detectable, reflecting a markedly prolonged blood half-life. This extended systemic retention likely enhances tumor uptake via the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, thereby improving therapeutic precision.

Building on the favorable biodistribution profile, we next explored the PA imaging capabilities of AFNC MOFs both in vitro and in vivo using the Vevo LAZR imaging system. In vitro PA measurements demonstrated a linear relationship between PA signal intensity and AFNC MOF concentration (Fig. 4e), supporting their suitability for quantitative imaging applications. Encouraged by these results, we evaluated their in vivo imaging performance. In tumor-bearing mice, PA signal intensity at tumor sites peaked around 12 h post-injection (Figs. 4g and S7a), in good agreement with the observed biodistribution trends.

Notably, even within the highly hypoxic tumor microenvironment induced by HIFU treatment, robust PA signals persisted following the bioreduction of AQ4N to AQ4. These observations confirm that AFNC MOFs retain PA imaging functionality under physiological transformation, further reinforcing their value as theranostic agents for image-guided therapy. To further assess the clinical relevance of AFNC MOFs in deep-tissue imaging, in vitro PA imaging was performed under porcine skin layers to simulate human tissue. As illustrated in Fig. 4f, strong PA signals were detectable at a depth of 0.25 cm and gradually attenuated with increasing depth, thereby confirming their promising potential for noninvasive deep-tissue visualization in vivo.

In parallel, we evaluated the hypoxic microenvironment induced by HIFU ablation, which serves as key trigger for AQ4N activation. Multiple complementary methods, including immunofluorescence staining, optical oxygen microelectrode measurement and PA imaging of hypoxia-specific probes, were employed to validate post-HIFU hypoxia at tumor sites. As shown in Figs. 4h and S7b, PA imaging revealed a substantial drop in blood oxygen saturation from 51.25% to 24.27% within 24 h post-HIFU. Consistent with this, microelectrode measurements showed a decline in intratumoral oxygen partial pressure from 54.30 mmHg pre-treatment to 11.87 mmHg post-ablation (Fig. 4i). Immunofluorescence analysis further confirmed significant upregulation of hypoxia-inducible factor 1α (HIF-1α) in HIFU-treated tumors compared to controls (Fig. 4j and k).

Collectively, these findings demonstrate that AFNC MOFs not only offer efficient tumor accumulation and prolonged circulation but also provide robust PA imaging guidance for HIFU-mediated tumor ablation. Simultaneously, HIFU ablation induces profound intratumoral hypoxia, creating a favorable microenvironment for AQ4N bioreduction and therapeutic activation. It is important to note that due to challenges associated with in vitro HIFU studies such as high acoustic impedance mismatch, rapid acoustic energy attenuation and cavitation-induced cellular damage, consistent with prevailing practices in the field, we opted to perform HIFU experiments exclusively in vivo.

In vivo antitumor efficacy and biosafety evaluation

Given the promising in vitro anti-tumor efficacy of AFNC MOFs, we further evaluated their in vivo therapeutic potential using a bilateral 4T1 subcutaneous breast cancer tumor model in mice. Once tumor volumes reached 80–100 mm3, mice were randomized into seven treatment groups (PBS, AQ4N, AF MOFs, HIFU, AF MOFs+HIFU, AFN MOFs+HIFU and AFNC MOFs+HIFU) and treated accordingly (Fig. 5a). During the treatment period, there were no significant changes in body weight among the groups of mice (Fig. S8). As shown in Fig. 5b-d, compared to PBS group, the tumor suppression capability of AQ4N group is not significant, as free AQ4N is rapidly cleared from bloodstream. In contrast, AF MOFs group exhibited degree of tumor suppression, attributable to the nanoparticle-mediated targeting of AQ4N, which enabled its effective accumulation at tumor site. In addition, compared with AF MOFs group and HIFU group, the inhibitory effect on tumor growth was more pronounced in AF MOFs+HIFU group, reflecting that HIFU-induced hypoxia enhanced the therapeutic efficacy, which resulted in successive activation of AQ4N and led to the significant death of cancer cells. The addition of NLG919 further amplified this effect in AFN MOFs+HIFU group, with significantly greater tumor suppression than in AF MOFs+HIFU group. This improvement is due to NLG919’s inhibition of IDO1 enzyme activity, effectively alleviating the tumor’s immunosuppressive microenvironment. Notably, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group demonstrated even greater efficacy, attributed to calcium carbonate’s ability to neutralize lactic acid, which, in synergy with NLG919, enhanced the reversal of tumor-associated immunosuppression.

To further assess anti-tumor efficacy, the mice were euthanized and tumor tissues were harvested for PCNA, TUNEL and H&E staining (Fig. 5e, f and g). In PCNA staining, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group displayed minimal fluorescence, indicating marked inhibition of cell proliferation, whereas other groups exhibited varying levels of fluorescence. TUNEL and H&E staining further corroborated these findings, with AFNC MOFs+HIFU group showing the highest levels of apoptosis and substantial tissue necrosis and contraction. Given that tumor metastasis and recurrence are major contributors to cancer-related mortality, we also investigated the anti-metastatic potential of AFNC MOFs by examining lung tissue post-treatment. Strikingly, H&E staining revealed that, while metastatic nodules were observed in the lungs of other groups, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group had virtually no detectable nodules (Fig. 5j), indicating strong anti-metastatic effect. To assess long-term therapeutic efficacy of our strategy, we extended the evaluation period from day 15 to day 30, allowing for more comprehensive and in-depth analysis of its sustained effects in vivo. The results of tumor weight measurements (Fig. 5h and i) and histological analyses based on TUNEL and PCNA staining (Fig. S9) unequivocally demonstrated that AFNC MOFs+HIFU treatment group consistently maintained robust anti-tumor response throughout the extended evaluation period, exhibiting stable and prolonged therapeutic activity. Notably, the mice receiving AFNC MOFs+HIFU treatment retained survival rate of 80% on day 30 (Fig. S10), underscoring significant survival benefits conferred by this treatment.

In addition to demonstrating potent anti-tumor efficacy, the biosafety profile of therapeutic nanomaterials plays a pivotal role in determining their translational feasibility for clinical use. To this end, we systematically evaluated the biocompatibility of AFNC MOFs through a series of in vitro and in vivo assessments. As a primary indicator of systemic safety, a hemolysis assay was performed to examine blood compatibility. After 4 h of incubation with whole blood, the hemolysis rates of AFNC MOFs across various concentrations remained below the safety threshold of 5% (Fig. S11), thereby confirming their excellent hemocompatibility. To further verify the in vivo biosafety, histopathological examinations of major organs were carried out on days 15 and 30 post-administration. H&E staining of the heart, liver, spleen and kidneys showed no discernible pathological abnormalities (Figs. S12 and S13), suggesting negligible organ toxicity. In parallel, comprehensive hematological and serum biochemical analyses were performed on blood samples collected at multiple time points (days 1, 7, 15 and 30). All tested parameters remained within normal physiological ranges (Fig. S14). These findings collectively underscore the favorable biocompatibility of AFNC MOFs, reinforcing their promise as safe and effective candidates for future clinical translation.

In vivo immunological response

To evaluate the immune response in mice following various treatments, we first assessed CRT expression in tumor tissues via immunofluorescence staining. As depicted in Fig. 6a and b, AQ4N group exhibited weak CRT fluorescence, similar to PBS group, due to the rapid clearance of free AQ4N from bloodstream. In contrast, AF MOFs group showed certain CRT expression, attributed to the nanoparticle-mediated delivery of AQ4N, which facilitated its accumulation in tumor sites. In addition, compared with HIFU group and AF MOFs group, the green fluorescence in AF MOFs+HIFU group was significantly enhanced, indicating that hypoxia conditions induced by HIFU effectively activated AQ4N. With the addition of NLG919, CRT expression in AFN MOFs+HIFU group was markedly higher than that in AF MOFs+HIFU group, a result of NLG919’s inhibition of IDO1 enzyme activity, which alleviated tumor-associated immunosuppression. Notably, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group exhibited the highest green fluorescence intensity, suggesting that calcium carbonate’s ability to neutralize lactate, in synergy with NLG919, further amplified the reversal of immunosuppression within TME. Correspondingly, HMGB1 expression decreased progressively across treatment groups, with minimal fluorescence observed in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group (Fig. 6a and c). These findings were corroborated by Western blot analysis, which revealed similar trends in CRT and HMGB1 protein levels (Figs. 6e, S15a, S15b and S16). Additionally, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group significantly upregulated the DNA damage marker H2A.X, indicating effective induction of apoptosis (Figs. 6e, S15c and S16).

To assess the inhibitory effect of AFNC MOFs on IDO enzyme in vivo, we analyzed Kyn/Trp ratio in primary tumor tissues and performed Western blotting. The results revealed that AFNC MOFs combined with HIFU significantly reduced Kyn/Trp ratio (Fig. 6r). Interestingly, no significant variations in IDO protein expression were found across groups, as demonstrated by the immunoblotting analysis. This indicates that decrease in Kyn levels was mediated not by the downregulation of IDO expression but by inhibition of its enzymatic activity (Figs. 6e, S15d and S16). Additionally, we also assessed the lactate content in tumor sites following the various treatments to evaluate in vivo lactate metabolism modulation by AFNC MOFs. As depicted in Fig. 6d, the lactic acid content in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group was significantly lower, confirming that AFNC MOFs can effectively promote lactate consumption in vivo.

To further investigate the immune response, we performed FCM to quantitatively assess immune cell populations in vivo. As shown in Fig. 6f and l and S17a, the maturation rates of DCs in both in situ and distant tumors were markedly elevated in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group, reaching 44.75% and 44.45%, respectively—more than double that of control group. This demonstrates that AFNC MOFs+HIFU markedly enhance DC maturation. Moreover, treatment resulted in a notable increase in M1 macrophage polarization within tumor tissues, while the proportion of M2 macrophages was halved compared to remaining groups (Fig. 6i, o, p and q, S18a and S18b). Furthermore, the infiltration of CD8+ T cells was dramatically increased in both in situ and distant tumors, with percentages rising from 7.83% to 7.98% to 35.14% and 34.68%, respectively (Fig. 6g and m and S17b). This indicates that AFNC MOFs+HIFU strongly promote the recruitment of CTLs into TME. In parallel, percentage of Tregs in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group was significantly reduced compared to the other groups (Fig. 6h and n and S17c), signifying a reversal of tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment. These findings suggest that AFNC MOFs+HIFU not only induce the repolarization of immunosuppressive M2 macrophages into pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype but also enhance maturation of DCs and infiltration of tumoricidal T cells. Collectively, these effects synergistically reverse the immunosuppressive TME, leading to a more robust antitumor immune response.

Furthermore, to elucidate the potential of long-term anti-tumor immunity, we quantified proportion of memory T cells in spleens of mice at the end of treatment using flow cytometry. Consistent with observed tumor growth inhibition results, AFNC MOFs+HIFU group exhibited a significant increase in proportion of memory T cells compared to other groups (Fig. S19), indicating the formation of a robust anti-tumor immune memory response. Subsequently, after the completion of treatment, tumor rechallenge experiments were conducted separately on mice in AFNC MOFs+HIFU group and healthy mice. Specifically, on day 30, tumor cells were re-inoculated into subcutaneous region of mice, followed by continuous monitoring of tumor growth to evaluate effectiveness of initial treatment in preventing the development of secondary tumors. Notably, mice previously treated with AFNC MOFs+HIFU showed significant inhibition of tumor growth in the re-vaccinated group compared to control group (Figs. 6s and S20), further confirming that AFNC MOFs+HIFU therapy successfully induced persistent immune memory against 4T1 tumors.

Conclusion

In summary, we successfully engineered a hypoxia-activated nanosystem, AFNC MOFs, encapsulating AQ4N, Fe3+, NLG919 and CaCO3, designed to augment the body’s anti-tumor immune response following HIFU ablation. Upon specific accumulation at tumor sites, AFNC MOFs significantly enhance photoacoustic (PA) imaging, providing precise guidance for HIFU surgery to ensure more targeted irradiation. Following this, the hypoxia-activated prodrug AQ4N, triggered by the severe hypoxic conditions induced by HIFU, undergoes metabolic conversion, not only eradicating tumor cells but also inducing a potent ICD response, amplifying the body’s anti-tumor immunity. Simultaneously, calcium carbonate depletes lactate in the TME, while NLG919 inhibits IDO enzyme activity, thereby reversing the immunosuppressive nature of the TME. Detailed in vitro and in vivo experiments have further validated that AFNC MOFs not only significantly improve anti-tumor efficacy but also dramatically enhance the immune response post-HIFU ablation, effectively preventing tumor recurrence and metastasis. Consequently, this study offers a straightforward and efficient strategy for constructing hypoxia-specific nanoplatforms, presenting a promising approach to enhancing immunotherapy following surgical interventions.
