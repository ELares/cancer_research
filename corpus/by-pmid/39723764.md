# Dual-targeted STAb-T cells secreting BCMA and CD19 T cell engagers for improved control of haematological cancers.

## Abstract

Despite recent advances in immunotherapy against B cell malignancies such as BCMA (B cell maturation antigen) and CD19-targeted treatments using soluble T cell-engaging (TCE) antibodies or chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T), there is still an important number of patients experiencing refractory/relapsed (R/R) disease. Approaches to avoid tumor-intrinsic mechanisms of resistance such as immune pressure-mediated antigen downmodulation, are being broadly investigated. These strategies include BCMA/CD19 dual-targeting therapies, which may be of particular interest to patients with B cell lymphoma and multiple myeloma, where a specific double-positive immature subpopulation is commonly associated with poor prognosis and poor response to current treatments. In fact, several clinical trials targeting both antigens through different strategies are currently underway. Here, based on the previously validated STAb (in situ secretion of T cell-redirecting bispecific antibodies) concept, we used two different engineering strategies (pool and co-transduction) to generate dual-targeted STAb-T cells simultaneously secreting BCMA TCE and CD19 TCE that outperformed single-targeted STAb-T cells in different in vitro models. These promising results encourage further preclinical clinical testing of dual STAb-T cells in R/R B-cell malignancies.

Dual-targeted STAb-T cells secreting BCMA and CD19 T cell engagers for improved control of haematological cancers

ABSTRACT

Despite recent advances in immunotherapy against B cell malignancies such as BCMA (B cell maturation antigen) and CD19-targeted treatments using soluble T cell-engaging (TCE) antibodies or chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T), there is still an important number of patients experiencing refractory/relapsed (R/R) disease. Approaches to avoid tumor-intrinsic mechanisms of resistance such as immune pressure-mediated antigen downmodulation, are being broadly investigated. These strategies include BCMA/CD19 dual-targeting therapies, which may be of particular interest to patients with B cell lymphoma and multiple myeloma, where a specific double-positive immature subpopulation is commonly associated with poor prognosis and poor response to current treatments. In fact, several clinical trials targeting both antigens through different strategies are currently underway. Here, based on the previously validated STAb (in situ secretion of T cell-redirecting bispecific antibodies) concept, we used two different engineering strategies (pool and co-transduction) to generate dual-targeted STAb-T cells simultaneously secreting BCMA TCE and CD19 TCE that outperformed single-targeted STAb-T cells in different in vitro models. These promising results encourage further preclinical clinical testing of dual STAb-T cells in R/R B-cell malignancies.

Introduction

B cell cancers arise from different stages of B cell differentiation and comprise a heterogeneous group of malignancies including B cell lymphomas, B cell leukemias and plasma cell (PC) dyscrasias. Although recent therapeutic advances have improved survival in these hematological cancers, a substantial proportion of patients still suffer from refractory/relapsed (R/R) disease. Expression of B cell maturation antigen (BCMA, CD269 or TNFRSF17) is restricted to mature PCs, some mature forms of B lymphocytes, and neoplastic PCs and has therefore been widely investigated as therapeutic target for T cell-redirecting immunotherapies in multiple myeloma (MM). However, the therapeutic potential of BCMA in B cell lymphomas has not yet been investigated clinically. CD19, on the other hand, is widely used as a target antigen for B cell leukemias and lymphomas since its expression is maintained in B cells undergoing malignant transformation. In fact, several T cell-redirecting strategies against CD19 and BCMA have been developed, such as membrane-anchored chimeric antigen receptors (CARs) or soluble bispecific T cell-engaging (TCE) antibodies. These approaches have yielded important clinical responses in patients with R/R diseases, leading to the regulatory approval of several CAR-T and TCE products. Both strategies have been shown to be highly effective, but differences in their mechanisms of action may lead to distinct effector responses. The expansion and persistence associated to adoptive CAR-T therapy helps to generate reservoirs of long-lasting memory cells. Conversely, systemically administered TCEs can recruit polyclonal lymphocytes, but their efficacy depends on continuous administration of TCEs and does not induce long-term memory T cell responses. Despite the promising results obtained with approved BCMA and CD19-specific therapies, a significant number of patients do not achieve a complete response (CR) or relapse after treatment. The main resistance mechanisms associated with lower responses to CAR-based therapies include poor persistence of CAR-T cells, intrinsic tumor heterogeneity and antigen downmodulation after single-targeted treatment, and the presence of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment.

We had developed the STAb-T strategy based on the in vivo secretion of TCE antibodies by genetically modified T cells. Continuous secretion results in sustained plasma concentrations of TCE, in addition to preserving the persistence of adoptively transferred STAb-T cells and the ability to recruit unmodified bystander T cells. Several STAb-T strategies have already demonstrated preclinical efficacy against various hematologic malignancies. CD19- and BCMA-specific STAb-T cells have been shown to be more effective than CAR-T cells in inducing specific cytotoxicity, preventing tumor escape in vitro and inhibiting tumor progression in vitro and in vivo.

Recently, an increasing number of dual-targeting therapies have been developed to overcome tumor heterogeneity and antigen downmodulation associated with single-targeting therapies in B cell malignancies. Of all possible antigen combinations, CD19 and BCMA are interesting candidates for targeting because of their overlapping expression at different stages of the B cell maturation pathway. Indeed, BCMA expression has been observed in CD19+ populations in patients with B cell leukemias and lymphomas. Similarly, a small treatment-resistant BCMA+CD19+ subpopulation with cancer stem cell-like properties has been described in some MM patients with poor prognosis. Clinical trials with immunotherapies combining both CD19 and BCMA antigens are underway for MM, and the potential benefit for patients with B cell lymphomas or B cell leukemias needs further investigation.

Here, we validate the first dual-targeting strategy based on the in situ secretion of two BCMA- and CD19-specific TCEs and confirm that dual STAb-T cells, generated either by mixing single-transduced BCMA- and CD19-STAb-T cells (Pooled STAb-T) or by co-transduction with two different lentiviral vectors (CoT STAb-T), efficiently control tumor escape and could potentially reduce the risk of tumor relapse in MM and other B cell malignancies.

Materials and methods

Cell lines and culture conditions

The cell lines used are detailed in Supplementary Table 1. All were purchased from the American Tissue Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA) and culture conditions are described in Supplemental Methods.

Lentiviral vectors construction, production and titration

The constructs pCCL-BCMA-CD3-His-T2A-tdTo and pCCL-CD19-CD3-His-T2A-GFP and the production of the lentiviral vectors are described in detail in Supplementary Methods.

Primary T cell transduction and culture conditions

Primary T cell and Jurkat T cell transduction and culture conditions are detailed in Supplemental Methods.

Activation assays

Contacting co-culture assays were performed with 1 × 105 non-transduced Jurkat (J-NT) cells or transduced Jurkat STAb-T cells (CD19 J-STAb-T, BCMA J-STAb-T and CoT J-STAb-T cells) and 1 × 105 tumor target cells (NALM6 or U266 or a mixture of both). Non-contact co-culture assays were performed using transwell plates (4.26 mm diameter) with 0.4 μm pores (Cat. No. CLS3381-1EA; Corning, Kennebunk, Me, USA). In non-contact experiments, a mixture of 5 × 104 J-NT cells and 5 × 104 NALM6 or U266 cells was placed in the bottom well chamber, while 1 × 105 J-NT or the different Jurkat STAb T cells were plated in the insert wells. After 24 hours, T cell activation was analyzed by flow cytometry using an APC-conjugated anti-CD69 monoclonal antibody.

Flow cytometry

Antibodies used for flow cytometry are listed in Supplemental Table 2. CD19-TCE and BCMA-TCE cell surface decoration is described in Supplemental Methods. Cell acquisition was performed in a DxFlex flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter, Brea, CA, USA). Analysis was performed using FlowJo V10 software (Tree Star, Ashland, OR USA).

Cytotoxicity assays

For cytotoxicity assays, transduced or non-transduced activated effector (E) T cells were co-cultured with tumor target cells (T) at the indicated effector:target (E:T) ratios. Cytotoxicity assays in a non-contact co-culture transwell system were performed under the same conditions as described above. In both cases, supernatants were collected after 48 hours and stored at −20°C for further analysis of IFNγ secretion. Effector cell-mediated cytotoxicity was assessed by flow cytometry using Trucount™ Absolute Counting Tubes (Cat. No. 663028, BD biosciences) and measuring the number of live cells 7-aminoactinomycin D− (7-AAD−).

Cytokine secretion analysis

IFNγ secretion was analyzed by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Cat. No. 950.000.096; Diaclone, Besancon Cedex, France).

Statistical analysis

Statistical tests indicated in figure legends and data processing were performed using GraphPad Prism version 8 (GraphPad Prism Software, La Jolla, CA, USA). Significant differences were determined by one- or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons post hoc test. Significance was considered only when p values were less than 0.05 and is defined as follows: *p < 0.05; **p < 0.01; ***p < 0.001, ****p < 0.0001). Data are presented as the mean ± SD and N represents the total number of technical and biological replicates performed.

Results

Two different TCEs secreted by pooled STAb-T cells do not interfere with T cell recruitment

Bicistronic T2A-based lentiviral vectors encoding the anti-CD19 × anti-CD3 tandem scFv (CD19-TCE) and the green fluorescent protein (GFP) reporter gene, or the anti-BCMA x anti-CD3 tandem scFv (BCMA-TCE) with the tandem dimer tomato (tdTo) reporter gene, have been described previously (Fig. S1) and were used to generate dual-targeted STAb-T that secrete both TCEs simultaneously (Figure 1a). Jurkat T cells transduced at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 10 homogeneously expressed high levels (>90%) of GFP or tdTo (Figure 1b). Target cells expressing either of the cognate antigens (Fig. S2A) were incubated in the presence of conditioned media from transduced CD19 and BCMA J-STAb-T cells to demonstrate the functionality and specificity of secreted TCEs (Fig. S2B). In human primary T cells, the average transduction efficiency was 29% for CD19 STAb-T cells and 12% for BCMA STAb-T cells (Figure 1c) and correlated with the average vector copy number (VCN), which was slightly higher for CD19 STAb-T cells (0.7 ± 0.245) than for BCMA STAb-T cells (0.5 ± 0.252) (Figure 1c). The proportion of CD4+ and CD8+ cells (Figure 1d) and the relative distribution of naïve (TN), central memory (TCM), effector memory (TEM) and effector (TEF) T cell subsets (Figure 1e) were similar in non-transduced (NT), CD19 STAb-T and BCMA STAb-T cells. 

To test the ability of secreted TCEs to induce Jurkat T cell activation and cytotoxicity with primary T cells, several in vitro non-contacting co-culture assays were designed (Figure 1f,j). As shown in Figure 1f, CD19+BCMA− NALM6 cells were plated with NT-T or BCMA STAb-T Jurkat T cells or primary T cells in the bottom well chamber at a 1:1 effector:target (E:T) cell ratio, and NT-T or CD19 STAb-T Jurkat T cells or primary T cells were plated in the insert well. T cell activation (Figure 1g,h) and tumor cell killing (Figure 1i) were completely dependent on the presence of CD19 STAb-T cells in the insert well and independent of which effector cells (NT-T or BCMA STAb-T) were added to the bottom chamber, indicating that the secreted anti-CD19 TCE from transduced CD19 STAb-T cells in the insert well effectively redirected NT-T cells and BCMA STAb-T cells despite the secretion of CD3-competing anti-BCMA TCE (Figure 1f-i, conditions F3 and F4). Similarly, when CD19−BCMA+ U266 cells (Figure 1j) were cocultured with NT-T or CD19 STAb-T Jurkat T cells or primary T cells in the bottom well chamber, only BCMA STAb-T cells plated in the insert well were able to redirect NT-T or CD19 STAb-T cells (Figure 1k,l) and kill BCMA+ cells (Figure 1m). These results support the noninterference between locally secreted CD19 and BCMA TCEs and confirm the efficient recruitment of bystander T cells specifically against each individual antigen, depending on the experimental design and the tumor target cell placed in the bottom chamber.

Pooled CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells reduce tumor escape in vitro when compared to single-targeted CD19 or BCMA STAb-T cells

Next, we compared the cytotoxicity and tumor control of dual-targeted STAb-T cells with that of single-targeted CD19 or BCMA STAb-T cells. Co-cultures of Daudi (CD19+ BCMAlow) or U266 (CD19− BCMA+) with single-targeted STAb-T cells or 1:1 mixture of CD19 and BCMA STAb-T cells (pooled STAb-T) were established at decreasing E:T ratios. After 48 hours, similar cytotoxic activity was observed for CD19 and pooled STAb-T cells against Daudi cells, which was significantly superior to the cytotoxicity induced by BCMA STAb-T cells (Fig. S3A). Single-targeted BCMA and pooled STAb-T cells had a similar effect on U266 cells, while no cytotoxicity was observed with CD19 STAb-T cells (Fig. S3B). In long-term co-cultures at decreasing E:T ratios, pooled STAb-T cells were superior in preventing tumor escape of Daudi cells compared to single-targeted CD19 or BCMA STAb-T cells, showing evidence of tumor control even at a 1:8 ratio (Fig. S3C). When using U266 cells, CD19 STAb-T cells failed to eliminate target cells at any E:T ratio, while both BCMA and pooled STAb-T cells showed significant tumor control even at lower ratios (Fig. S3D). To mimic tumor antigen heterogeneity and to compensate for the different proliferation dynamics of the target cells (Fig. S4), a 30/70% mixture of NALM6 and U266 cells was co-cultured with single-targeted or pooled CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells at decreasing E:T ratios. Single-targeted BCMA and pooled STAb-T cells exhibited similar cytotoxic activity, whereas CD19 STAb-T cells displayed a lower cytotoxic effect (Fig. S5A). Accordingly, IFNγ secretion was higher in BCMA and pooled STAb-T cells than in CD19 STAb-T cells (Fig. S5B). In addition, similar long-term co-culture assays were conducted with 30/70% mixtures of NALM6 and U266 cells to investigate the prevention of tumor escape. Pooled STAb-T cells fully controlled tumor progression at 1:1, 1:2 and 1:4 ratios at day 12, while single-targeted CD19 or BCMA STAb-T cells were unable to prevent tumor escape due to progression of non-targeted cells under any of the conditions studied (Fig. S5C).

Co-transduced CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells produce both CD19 and BCMA TCEs, efficiently reducing tumor escape in vitro

After studying the efficacy of pooled CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells, Jurkat and primary T cells were co-transduced with CD19 and BCMA TCE-encoding lentiviral vectors (Fig. S1). Co-transduction (CoT) with the lentiviral vectors at MOI 10 proved to be highly efficient, with > 99% of Jurkat T cells co-expressing GFP and tdTo reporter genes (Figure 2a). Contacting co-cultures assays were performed to demonstrate antigen-specific recognition of dual-targeted CoT STAb-T cells (Figure 2b), which were activated only after co-culturing with NALM6 or U266 target cells, but not in the presence of CD19−BCMA− K562 target cells (Figure 2c). In addition, non-contacting co-culture assays (Figure 2d) were performed to confirm the recruitment of NT Jurkat T (J-NT-T) cells by secreted CD19 and BCMA TCEs (Figure 2e). Then, primary T cells were co-transduced with both lentiviral vectors at an MOI of 10, resulting in similar proportions (10–15%) of single-positive GFP+ (STAb-T CD19) and tdTo+ (STAb-T BCMA) T cells, while the percentage of double-positive T cells represented less than 5% of the product (Figure 2f). The proportion of CD4+ and CD8+ cells was similar in both groups (Figure 2g). Escape experiments were then performed by adjusting single-targeted STAb-T cells with primary NT-T cells to ensure an equivalent proportion of GFP+ or tdTo+ cells in both single- and co-transduced products. A 30/70% mixture of NALM6 and U266 target cells was co-cultured with effector T cells at various E:T ratios and, as previously described with pooled STAb-T cells, CoT STAb-T cells were also more efficient at preventing tumor escape in vitro than single-targeted STAb-T cells (Figure 2h). 

CoT STAb-T cells are as effective as pooled STAb-T cells in controlling tumor progression in vitro

To compare the cytotoxic activity of pooled versus CoT STAb-T cells, additional assays were performed with transduced primary T cells. Transduction efficiencies were similar for CoT CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells and pooled CD19/BCMA STAb-T cells after mixing single-targeted STAb-T cells (Figure 3a), and no differences in CD4/CD8 ratios (Figure 3b) were observed for the different T cell products. Co-cultures of effector T cells and mixed (30/70%) NALM6/U266 target cells were established at decreasing ratios from 1:1 to 1:32. After 48 hours, both pooled and CoT STAb-T cells showed high cytotoxicity against leukemic CD38+CD10+ and myeloma CD38+CD10− target cells, with approximately 70% activity at the 1:16 E:T ratio (Figure 3c). In long-term escape experiments, both dual-targeted STAb-T cell types showed complete control of tumor cell mixture growth at 1:1 to 1:4 ratios (Figure 3d). 

Discussion

Despite therapeutic advances in B cell malignancies, such as CAR-T cells and TCEs, a significant proportion of patients relapse or progress due to various resistance mechanisms. Our previous work in this field has shown that both BCMA-specific and CD19-specific STAb-T cells may provide an alternative to current therapies for patients with MM and B-ALL, respectively. This may be due to the different mechanism of action, the ability of STAb-T cells to induce canonical immunological synapses and the continuous secretion of TCE, which allows therapeutic levels to be maintained for efficient recruitment of the endogenous T cell pool in the proximity of target antigen-expressing cells, as well as the generation of a pool of memory T cells in vivo. In addition, BCMA STAb-T cells are unaffected by the presence of soluble BCMA and show greater efficacy than BCMA CAR-T cells at extremely low E:T ratios; and CD19 STAb-T cells prevent the CD19 downmodulation observed in anti-CD19 CAR-mediated interactions, a major mechanism of resistance in B cell malignancies.

To prevent antigen loss and minimize tumor escape, dual-targeting strategies have been developed and validated in both preclinical models and clinical trials. Several dual-targeting CAR-based strategies have been implemented, such as co-administration of two different single-targeted CAR-T cell products, co-transduction of T cells with two single-targeted CAR vectors, transduction of T cells with a bicistronic vector encoding both single-targeted CARs and joining two different binding domains in a tandem CAR construct. In addition, the combination of two TCEs against different targets was shown to be superior to monotherapy in xenograft models of mixed phenotype acute leukemia. Furthermore, T cells expanded ex vivo and armed with a combination of different TCEs were more effective in preventing clonal escape than T cells armed with a single TCE.

Here, we present the first dual-targeting STAb-T strategy based on previously validated CD19- and BCMA-specific STAb-T cells and demonstrate that pooled STAb-T cells can secrete both CD19 and BCMA TCEs that do not interfere with each other in specific antigen recognition and subsequent T cell activation by CD3ε cross-linking. In addition, pooled and CoT STAb-T cells reduce the risk of tumor escape in vitro in mixed populations, mimicking the heterogeneity commonly found in R/R B cell malignancies, compared to single-targeted STAb-T cells. CoT STAb T cells may offer advantages over pooled STAb T cells, particularly in reducing manufacturing costs and simplifying future clinical development. In addition, co-transduction requires fewer T cells as starting material, which may be relevant given the lymphopenic status of some patients.
