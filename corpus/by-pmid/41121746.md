# Bi-functional integrin degraders cause stronger anti-proliferative effects than a blocking antibody by modulating distinct downstream signaling.

## Abstract

Integrins play a critical role in signaling events that regulate cell adhesion, migration, and proliferation, processes that, when dysregulated, contribute to cancer progression. Recently, innovative approaches that target extracellular proteins for degradation have suggested potential in cancer treatment by removing specific cell surface receptors and trafficking them to the lysosome for breakdown. In this context, two integrin degraders were engineered, each exploiting different lysosomal targeting pathways: the asialoglycoprotein receptor (ASGPR) and zinc and ring finger 3 (ZNRF3), respectively. Both degraders use a pan-selective human αv antibody that fully blocks ligand binding, effectively internalizing and degrading αv integrins in vitro and in vivo. Degrading cell-surface αv integrins, rather than merely blocking their interactions with the extracellular matrix (ECM), produces a stronger anti-proliferative effect by more robustly modulating oncogenic signaling pathways. Notably, these degraders exploit distinct mechanisms, including the first reporting of more profound changes in tumor-promoting integrin signaling cascades compared to ligand blocking alone, highlighting a novel avenue for integrin-targeted drug development. This strategy leads to greater suppression of tumor cell growth making it a promising and potentially superior approach to cancer therapy. Similar to other reports, in vivo efficacy has yet to be shown with extracellular or membrane-targeted protein degraders. Through careful pharmacokinetics characterizations, we showed that lack of in vivo efficacy may be due to insufficient exposure and target engagement. Our study provides useful lessons for the field to fully realize the full potential for the next generation of degrader therapeutics.

Introduction

Integrins are a family of 24 cell surface adhesion receptors that signal bi-directionally transmitting information into and out of the cell. These transmembrane receptors are α and β heterodimers, with each subunit comprising a large extracellular domain and a small cytosolic tail. Integrin activation begins with extracellular ligand binding, which stabilizes the extended-open conformation, followed by recruitment of intracellular adaptor proteins to the cytosolic tail. Upon extracellular matrix (ECM) binding, integrins cluster to form adhesion complexes that trigger signaling pathways essential for cell proliferation, adhesion, and migration. Both the extracellular and intracellular domains are critical for integrin function.

Integrins have been extensively studied as therapeutic targets in various diseases, including cancer, autoimmune disorders, and fibrosis. Notably, αv integrins are highly upregulated in many solid tumors. Dysregulation of αv integrins in cancer promotes tumor growth, immune evasion, and metastasis through enhanced proliferation mediated by interconnected signaling pathways such as FAK, Ras-ERK, PI3K/Akt, TGF-β, and YAP/TAZ. The five αv integrins, αvβ1, αvβ3, αvβ5, αvβ6, and αvβ8, have been previously demonstrated to play a role in tumor progression. To date, clinical trials targeting αv integrins have failed. These include two pan-αv blocking antibodies (abituzumab (Abt) and intetumumab), a blocking αvβ3 antibody (etaracizumab), and the small molecule cilengitide (targeting αvβ3/αvβ5). All share a common mechanism: inhibiting integrin binding to ECM and soluble ligands. However, β1 cytosolic signaling can promote tumorigenesis independently of ECM adhesion. Integrins were also shown to transmit pro-cancer signals in the endosome. Therefore, selectively removing αv integrins from the cell surface and intracellularly, thereby eliminating all functional domains and signaling, may improve therapeutic outcomes.

Targeting αv integrins for degradation may represent a better therapeutic strategy for cancer treatment. Targeted protein degradation (TPD) has emerged as a promising therapeutic approach, with several proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs) in clinical development. TPD exploits endogenous cellular proteolysis pathways, directing proteins of interest to lysosomes or proteosomes for degradation. This is achieved by bi-functional molecules that bridge the target protein and proteolysis machinery, forming a ternary complex. Initially limited to intracellular proteins, TPD strategies have recently expanded to degrade extracellular and membrane-bound proteins via lysosomal pathways. Notable platforms include lysosomal-targeting chimeras (LYTACs), proteolysis-targeting antibodies (PROTABs), and antibody-based PROTACs (AbTACs).

The previously reported PIP-N-Acetyl-D-galactosamine (GalNAc) LYTAC is a pan-arginylglycylaspartic acid (RGD) integrin peptide conjugated to a triGalNAc moiety, which targets ASGPR, a cell-surface lysosomal targeting receptor expressed on hepatocytes. This PIP-GalNAc LYTAC effectively removes integrins from the cell surface and exhibits a greater anti-proliferative effect compared to the PIP peptide alone. Despite these promising initial results, the PIP peptide’s low affinity, broad specificity for all RGD integrins, and presumably short in vivo half-life (compared to an antibody) limit its therapeutic potential. As depicted in Scheme 1, the Abt-triGalNAc degrader is conceptually different from the PIP-LYTAC, where an intact, highly potent αv blocking antibody Abt is used to target αv integrins. We believe Abt’s blocking functionality of our integrin degraders is superior to an RGD peptide-based degrader since the RGD moiety of PIP-LYTAC induces integrin headpiece opening, which could result in undesirable partial agonism. In addition, the chemical linker between the peptide and the triGalNAc moiety may be enzymatically liable, and cleaved RGD peptide could bind integrins on the cell surface and induce headpiece opening and promote downstream signaling. 

The second αv integrin degrader we engineered (Scheme 1) is based on the PROTAB (also known as AbTAC) technology reported by Genentech and Cotton et al. PROTABs are bispecific antibody degraders that engage a membrane-bound lysosomal-targeting E3 ligase such as ZNRF3. To date, PROTABs have not been reported to degrade large, multi-subunit membrane receptors such as integrins. By recruiting lysosomal targeting receptors like ASGPR and ZNRF3, these bi-functional molecules facilitate internalization and trafficking of the target protein to the lysosome for degradation.

In this study, we engineered two bi-functional integrin degrader antibodies that inhibit ligand-binding and induce degradation of αv integrins. We also compare the biological effects of the degraders to those of the blocking antibody alone. One molecule uses GalNAc moieties to engage ASGPR, while the other is a bispecific antibody that recruits ZNRF3. Both induce internalization of αv integrins from the cell surface and degrade αv from the total pool of the cell. A greater anti-proliferative effect is achieved with bi-functional integrin degraders compared to the blocking antibody alone, and this effect is shown to be mediated by the removal of integrin αvβ5, which has been previously demonstrated to play a role in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) tumor progression. The greater anti-proliferative effect is supported by the stronger response in modulating downstream protein expression. Some of these pathways include cytoskeletal and adhesion dynamics where phosphorylated zyxin and VASP were reduced, cell cycle regulation where phosphorylated A-Raf and MEK1 were decreased, and protein synthesis as shown by a reduction in phosphorylated ribosomal protein S6. This proof-of-concept study could serve as the basis for a new therapeutic avenue to target integrins.

Results

Generation of bi-functional αv integrin degraders

The removal of αv integrins from the cell surface is achieved by bi-functional antibodies that bind αv integrins and engage a lysosomal targeting receptor (Scheme 1). The integrin-binding antibody used is a pan-αv blocking antibody, Abt (Abt). Abt binds to the beta-propeller domain of αv subunit near the RGD peptide binding groove and sterically hinders native ligand binding at the interface of the integrin heterodimers. The αv native ligands reported to be blocked by Abt include vitronectin, fibrinogen, developmental endothelial locus-1 (DEL1), osteopontin, and latent TGF-β. Of note, Mitjans et al. has shown Abt completely inhibits αv integrin binding to vitronectin (the native ligand of αvβ5) with an IC50 of 0.5 nM in a cell adhesion assay.

Previously described antibody-based TPD platforms served as a starting point in the design of the molecules in this study. The bispecific antibody degrader, Abt-anti-ZNRF3, is composed of Abt, the anti-αv arm and an anti-ZNRF3 (anti-ZNRF3-5) arm. Abt-anti-ZNRF3 was generated by assembling Abt half human IgG1 with anti-ZNRF3-5 half human IgG1. The half IgGs were expressed separately with knob-in-hole mutations in the CH3 domain to guide heterodimer assembly, reduce homodimerization, and prevent light chain mismatching. The half IgGs were assembled after purification using common reducing conditions (Supplementary Fig. S1A). The assembly reaction was monitored by SDS-PAGE, and analytical HPLC-SEC (Supplementary Fig. S1B, C). The assembled bispecific antibody was tested by ELISA to ensure binding to both αv integrin and ZNRF3 with EC50 values of 0.2 nM (Supplementary Fig. S1D, E).

Abt-triGalNAc conjugate is composed of the Abt human IgG1 labeled via lysine residues with triGalNAc moieties (Supplementary Fig. S3A, B). The triGalNAc moieties engage ASGPR (Supplementary Fig. S2A). The DBCO-triGalNAc was synthesized as described previously. Abt was first activated with amine-reactive NHS-PEG4-azido handles. The DBCO-triGalNAc moieties were mixed with azido-activated-Abt to conjugate via copper-free, strain-promoted cycloaddition. The conjugation reaction was monitored by SDS-PAGE, and analytical HPLC-SEC (Supplementary Fig. S2B, C). Abt-triGalNAc was tested by ELISA to ensure Abt-triGalNAc binds to both αv integrin and ASGPR with EC50 values of 1.7 nM and 4 nM, respectively (Supplementary Fig. S2D, E).

Before identifying the anti-ZNRF3 arm for Abt-anti-ZNRF3, five anti-ZNRF3 half IgGs and five anti-RNF43 half IgGs were paired with Abt half IgG, and the assembled bispecific antibodies were then screened for αv degradation by Western blot (WB) after 48 hours of 100 nM treatment. These ten ZNRF3/RNF43 antibodies were selected based on reported affinities (0.01–0.3 nM) and surface removal (52–76%) of IGF1R. Degradation of αv integrin was seen with the anti-ZNRF3-5 pairing, but not with any other anti-ZNRF3 pairings (Supplementary Fig. S4). Forming a ternary complex that facilitates efficient degradation has been reported as a challenge, and while these data are not sufficient to explain why one epitope degrades more efficiently, they support this observation.

Internalization and degradation of αv and αvβ5 in HEPG2 cells

The degrader molecules were tested for their ability to internalize and degrade αv integrins from the cell surface of HEPG2 cells, a model cell line for HCC. This HCC cell line was selected due to its expression of integrins αvβ5 and αvβ1 (both are inhibited by Abt), as well as ASGPR and ZNRF3, enabling comparison of integrin degraders with two different mechanisms in the same cancer cell line. The expression of the receptors in HEPG2 was confirmed by flow cytometry (Supplementary Fig. S5).

The focus was placed on αvβ5 because studies indicate it mediates the anti-proliferative effects observed in HEPG2 cells whereas αvβ1, the only other αv integrin expressed on the cell surface (Supplementary Fig. S5), does not (Figure 4). HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with a degrader concentration range of 0.1–300 nM for 48 hours, after which αvβ5 surface expression was assessed by flow cytometry (Clone #P5H9) (Figure 1A). At 10 nM, Abt-anti-ZNRF3 removed 69.3 ± 1.2%, Abt-triGalNAc removed 80.3 ± 1.5%, and Abt removed 35.0 ± 1.5%. Similar surface reduction of αvβ5 was achieved for both degraders and Abt alone at concentrations of 10 nM and higher (Figure 1B). Based on the flow cytometry results, we concluded that degrader molecules removed significantly more αvβ5 from the cell surface than the parent antibody Abt, and that the ASGPR-mediated integrin degrader achieved 10% greater internalization than Abt-anti-ZNRF3. 

Total levels of αv (αvβ1 and αvβ5) and β5 subunits in whole cell lysates were quantified by WB. HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with 10 nM and 100 nM of each degrader for 48 hours prior to harvesting and lysing. Treatment with Abt-anti-ZNRF3 at 10 nM and 100 nM reduced the total αv pool by 53% and 43%, respectively, compared to untreated controls. Similarly, Abt-anti-ZNRF3 decreased the total β5 pool by 41% and 36% at the same concentrations. Abt-triGalNAc treatment led to a 61% and 70% reduction in total αv levels and a 47% and 43% reduction in total β5 levels at 10 and 100 nM, respectively. In contrast, the parent antibody Abt reduced total αv by 23.5 ± 3.5% and 17.5 ± 2.1%, and total β5 by 13 ± 4.2% and 15 ± 0% at 10 and 100 nM, respectively (Figure 1C). Additional control groups further support that the reduction of αvβ5 is specifically mediated by the degraders (Supplementary Fig. S6A-C). These results demonstrate that, beyond enhanced internalization, the degrader molecules achieve greater degradation of total αv and β5 pools relative to treatment with Abt alone.

To investigate the kinetics of internalization and degradation, HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with 10 nM of each degrader for 2 hours, followed by analysis via flow cytometry and WB. After 2 hours, Abt-anti-ZNRF3 reduced αvβ5 cell surface levels by 25% (compared to 69% at 48 hours), Abt-triGalNAc reduced surface αvβ5 by 69% (compared to 80% at 48 hours), and Abt reduced surface αvβ5 by 34% (35% at 48 hours; Supplementary Fig. S7A). Western blot analysis showed similar total αv integrin levels across all treatment groups at 2 hours, indicating minimal degradation at this early timepoint (Supplementary Fig. S7B). Comparison of 2- and 48-hour treatments suggests that αvβ5 internalization initiates within 2 hours, with ZNRF3-mediated internalization proceeding more slowly than ASGPR-driven internalization. Importantly, degradation of αvβ5 (WB) requires a longer duration to become apparent.

To assess the potential for further reduction of surface αvβ5 integrin, a double-treatment regimen was used, administering two consecutive doses of 100 nM degrader at 0 and 24 hours, as the stability of the degraders in the tissue culture media was not measured. After 48 hours, flow cytometry analysis revealed that Abt-anti-ZNRF3 removed 65% and Abt-triGalNAc removed 83% of surface αvβ5, comparable to single-treatment reductions of 67% and 85%, respectively (Supplementary Fig. S7C). These results indicate that an additional dose within the 48-hour did not further enhance αvβ5 internalization, consistent with the high affinity and stability of both integrin degraders.

Integrin degraders exhibit a significantly stronger anti-proliferative effect in vitro

Following the confirmation that Abt-anti-ZNRF3 and Abt-triGalNAc internalize and degrade αv integrins, the effect of integrin degradation on cell proliferation was investigated. Overexpression of αv integrins in tumors is implicated in tumor growth and metastasis. In addition to dysregulated proliferative signaling, integrins (αvβ5) facilitate scaffolding functions that connect tumor cells with the ECM in the tumor microenvironment. The removal of integrins could block ECM adhesion as well as scaffolding functions important for tumorigenesis. The anti-proliferative effects of the degraders on HEPG2 cells were examined and compared to those of the anti-αv blocking antibody, Abt.

HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with 10 nM bi-functional degraders, with proliferation monitored over the course of 5 days by live cell imaging. Phase confluence measurements were used to quantify percent proliferation. By day 5, Abt-anti-ZNRF3 inhibited proliferation by approximately 65%, and Abt-triGalNAc by approximately 80%, whereas Abt alone inhibited proliferation by about 40% (Figure 2A). The anti-proliferative effects of the degraders were significantly greater than those of Abt. Neither the anti-ZNRF3 antibody nor triGalNAc alone affected proliferation. Additional control groups further support the anti-proliferative effects that are mediated by the degraders (Supplementary Fig. S8). The viable cell count (trypan blue staining) of three experiments of 48-hour treatments with 10 nM concentrations, serves as an orthogonal proliferation measurement alongside live cell imaging. The degrader-treated cells show 25–50% fewer viable cells compared to untreated controls, corroborating the stronger anti-proliferative effects observed with degrader treatment (Figure 2B). 

In addition to inhibiting proliferation, the degrader molecules change the morphology of the HEPG2 cells. By day 5, the degrader-treated cells no longer look epithelial-like, rather they form small, round clusters (Figure 2C). Similar qualitative observations were made with the PIP-LYTAC degrader.

A dose response was established spanning a concentration range of 0.14–300 nM using the proliferation assay. Consistent with previous experiments, HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates, treated and monitored over the course of 5 days by live cell imaging. The measured phase confluence was used to calculate the percent proliferation for each degrader and Abt treatment (Figure 2A,B and Supplementary Fig. S9A). The anti-proliferative effect on day 5 over the concentration range was used to calculate EC50 values. Abt-anti-ZNRF3 showed a maximum anti-proliferative effect of about 65% with an EC50 of 0.19 nM. Abt-triGalNAc showed a maximum anti-proliferative effect of approximately 85% with an EC50 of 0.12 nM (Figure 2C). Abt showed a maximum anti-proliferative effect of approximately 40% across all concentrations (Supplementary Fig. S9B).

HEPG2 cells express two αv integrins, αvβ1 and αvβ5 (Supplementary Fig. S5). To elucidate which αv integrin is the main mediator of cell proliferation, an anti-αvβ5 (Clone ALULA) and an anti-αvβ1 (Clone mAb5) antibody were included at 10 nM in the proliferation assay (Figure 4A). Both antibodies block αvβ5 and αvβ1 binding to their corresponding ligands. Inhibition of αvβ1 had a negligible effect on proliferation. Surprisingly, functional blocking of αvβ5 by the ALULA antibody inhibited proliferation by 80%, more comparable with the bi-functional αv degraders than with the non-degrading αv blocking antibody, Abt. As previously described, Abt is a potent ligand-binding blocking antibody for all 5 αv integrins, including αvβ5 (IC50~0.5 nM)22. Thus, treatment of Abt at 10 nM should inhibit >95% of αvβ5 function. The reduction of relative cell surface αvβ5 by the anti-αvβ5 antibody ALULA was then assessed by flow cytometry (Figure 4B) after 48 hours. The anti-αvβ5 antibody reduced the cell surface αvβ5 by 90%, similar to the αv degraders, whereas the anti-αvβ1 antibody showed little to no change in αvβ5 surface levels (Figure 4C).

The strong anti-proliferative effect and reduction of αvβ5 surface levels by the anti-αvβ5 prompted an investigation into whether the antibody also induces degradation of αvβ5 without recruiting other lysosomal targeting components. Other anti-integrin antibodies have been reported to cause internalization, although degradation was not reported. The total reduction of αv and β5 subunits in the whole cell lysates was measured by WB. HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with 10 nM of the anti-αvβ5 antibody for 48 hours before harvesting and lysing the cells. The anti-αvβ5 antibody reduced the total αv pool by 15% and showed no reduction of the total β5 pool compared to the no treatment control (Fig. 54). This suggests removal of αvβ5 from the cell surface, presumably via integrin internalization induced by anti-αvβ5 antibody treatment, is sufficient for the greater anti-proliferative effect compared to a functional blocking antibody, i.e., Abt, that does not cause high levels of internalization.

Bi-functional integrin degraders modulate downstream protein expression and phosphorylation

To gain mechanistic insights into why degrader molecules exhibit stronger anti-proliferative effects than ligand blocking alone, a proteomics study was conducted measuring changes in the expression and phosphorylation of 160 signaling proteins involved in cell growth and oncogenic signaling (Hyperplex, Assay Engineers). HEPG2 cells were plated on vitronectin-coated plates and treated with 10 nM of each degrader or antibody (Abt) for 48 hours before harvesting. Proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE, transferred to a membrane, biotinylated, and captured on neutravidin-coated magnetic beads. Samples were incubated with primary and phycoerythrin-labeled secondary antibodies and protein expression was analyzed on a Luminex instrument.

Of the 160 proteins selected for profiling, 120 showed signal changes in at least one treatment compared to untreated control (Supplementary Fig. S10). Several proteins exhibited a log2 fold change value of 0.58 or greater in degrader-treated samples than in Abt-treated samples, meaning a ≥ 50% change in expression was observed. These changes spanned multiple proliferation pathways including MAPK/ERK and mTOR pathways, supporting the enhanced anti-proliferative phenotype observed with degrader treatment. Consistent with Western blot results, αv integrin levels decreased by 65% with degraders versus 15% with the blocking antibody. Correspondingly, β1 integrin, which pairs with αv, showed greater reduction with degraders. Interestingly, α6 integrin increased, suggesting an integrin compensatory response.

In Figure 5, we highlighted a subset of proteins and phospho-proteins that are more profoundly modulated by the degraders in the integrin-mediated cellular functions, including cytoskeletal and adhesion dynamics, cell cycle control, protein synthesis, and cell survival.

Degradation of integrins impairs cell adhesion to the extracellular matrix, weakening integrin signaling and destabilizing focal adhesion complexes. This attenuation reduces downstream signaling via the MAPK pathway, as evidenced by decreased phosphorylation of A-Raf (Y301) and MEK1 (S298), which likely impairs ERK-driven transcription of proliferation genes such as FRA1 and Cyclin D1 (Figure 5A,B).

Downregulation of Cyclin D1 and reduced phosphorylation of CDK2 (T160) and CDK1 (Y15) disrupts cell cycle progression. Concurrently, increased acetylation of p53 (Ac-K305) stabilizes this tumor suppressor, enabling negative regulation of proliferative pathways like PI3K/Akt via PTEN upregulation (Figure 5B).

Reduced phosphorylation of p70 S6K (T421) and ribosomal protein S6 (S235, S240) suggests dampened mTOR signaling, decreasing translation of growth-related genes. Suppressed STAT3 (S727) signaling lowers transcription of survival genes such as Survivin, weakening anti-apoptotic defenses and mitotic progression (Figure 5A,B).

Additionally, decreased phosphorylation of focal adhesion proteins zyxin (S142) and VASP (S157) disrupts cytoskeletal remodeling, actin polymerization, and adhesion turnover, impairing cellular motility (Figure 5B). Collectively, these signaling changes lead to reduced adhesion, proliferation, survival, and migration, explaining the stronger anti-proliferative effects observed with degrader treatment compared to ligand blocking alone. These findings align with observed changes in cell morphology and support the stronger anti-proliferative effect of degraders (Figure 2C).

Bispecific antibody degrader abt-anti-ZNRF3 degrades αv in vivo

Having demonstrated the functional effect of the degraders in vitro, an investigation was conducted to determine whether this effect would translate in vivo to inhibit tumor growth more effectively than Abt alone. Previous characterization showed that Abt is not cross-reactive with mouse αv integrin, leading to the establishment of a xenograft model in which HEPG2 cells were implanted into nude BALB/c mice. Pharmacokinetics (PK) studies were performed with each degrader to determine the dose necessary for the efficacy study. Serum samples collected were analyzed for the presence of degrader using a Meso Scale Diagnostics assay (Supplementary Fig. S11A, B). The result indicated that achieving the predicted efficacious tumor concentration of 10 nM for Abt-triGalNAc would necessitate an exceptionally high dose. This requirement stems from the low subcutaneous bioavailability (< 1%) and its short half-life of 16 hours, making it impractical for in vivo applications (Supplementary Fig. S11A).

Four doses of Abt-anti-ZNRF3, 0.3 mg/kg, 1 mg/kg, 3 mg/kg, and 10 mg/kg were administered subcutaneously once a week for three weeks in nude BALB/c mice. At the 10 mg/kg dose, the serum concentration was determined to be approximately 85 nM within the first 3 hours and approximately 2,200 nM after the third dose (Supplementary Fig. S11B). Assuming an antibody biodistribution coefficient of 4–16% at the tumor, this dose was predicted to achieve a concentration of ~10 nM in the tumor after the first dose, increasing to a range of 66–350 nM with each subsequent dose (Supplementary Fig. S11C), which is considered efficacious based on the in vitro data (Figure 3C). 

Following tumor establishment (100 mm3), Abt-anti-ZNRF3, Abt and an isotype control were injected subcutaneously at a 10 mg/kg dose over the course of four weeks. Among the three treatment groups there was no significant difference in tumor volume (Supplementary Fig. S12A), rendering the efficacy inconclusive. However, we observed Abt-anti-ZNRF3 achieved greater degradation of αv (3 representative mice show an average of 42% αv degradation and an average of 16% β5 degradation) compared to Abt (3 representative mice show an average of 20% αv and β5 degradation) in treated tumor lysates compared to the isotype-treated control group (Supplementary Fig. S12B). The degradation of the αv integrin in tumors induced by Abt-anti-ZNRF3 was slightly lower than observed in vitro (approximately 10% less), while the β5 degradation in tumors was less than half that in vitro. By the end of the study, the antibody concentrations were measured to be ~15 nM (Supplementary Fig. S12C). The in vitro studies suggest this is an efficacious concentration, but it is significantly lower than the expected concentration of 66–350 nM, which could explain the reduced degradation.

Discussion

Although αv integrins have long been recognized as key players in cancer pathology, no clinically successful treatments targeting them have emerged, despite extensive efforts over the past four decades with both small molecules and biologics. An explanation for this is the requirement to remove all functional domains of integrins to observe clinical efficacy. With the rise of the extracellular targeted protein degradation field, targeting αv integrins to the lysosome for their removal from the cell surface could offer an advantageous therapeutic approach.

To investigate, two antibody-based integrin degraders were generated that remove αvβ5 from the cell via two distinct lysosomal targeting mechanisms. These degraders remove 70–80% of αvβ5 from the cell surface, reduce the total αv and β5 subunits by approximately 50%, and result in a 65–80% greater anti-proliferative effect compared to the functional blocking antibody alone. The bi-functional αv integrin degraders achieve a stronger anti-proliferative effect than the blocking antibody Abt by modulating downstream signaling proteins. The study also demonstrates αvβ5 internalization from the cell surface is sufficient to achieve an enhanced functional effect. However, the mechanism by which the anti-αvβ5 antibody internalizes αvβ5 remains unknown, making the design of a discovery campaign that selects for this type of antibody challenging. Furthermore, degrader molecules may offer an advantage over internalizing antibodies by providing a more sustained pharmacodynamic effect. Future studies could investigate the rate of integrin resynthesis and recycling, which may reduce dosing requirements and inform the design of more effective degraders.

In addition to this work, two other integrin degraders have been reported by others. The PIP-LYTAC, a pan RGD peptide with one triGalNAc moiety conjugated to it, and HB-3–20, a bi-functional peptide that engages transglutaminase 2 (TG2), and αvβ5. The PIP-LYTAC removed αv integrins from the cell surface and inhibits proliferation more than PIP alone. HB-3–20 degrades αvβ5, via the TG2-α2M-LRP1 lysosomal targeting pathway, from IMR-90 fibroblast cells. Degradation of αvβ5 significantly decreased α-smooth muscle actin expression (α-SMA) compared to no change when treated with the RGDVF peptide (αvβ5 binder) alone. α-SMA and αvβ5 are closely connected in mediating scaffolding functions necessary for a fibrous tumor stroma, which supports tumor growth. Eliminating tumorigenic scaffolding functions in addition to ECM adhesion could be another potential advantage of degrading integrins. Of note, neither study demonstrated any in vivo efficacy.

The increased anti-proliferative effect observed with the degraders is attributed to the elimination of all functional domains of the integrin from the cell surface. This aligns with studies demonstrating that ECM-independent integrin interactions are as important as matrix-dependent interactions in the development of cancer. Haake et al. showed that the β1 cytosolic tail promotes proliferative signaling independently of ECM binding in adenocarcinoma. Interestingly, blocking αvβ1 showed no effect on proliferation in this study, whereas complete removal of αvβ5 from the cell surface resulted in a stronger anti-proliferative effect than ECM binding inhibition alone (Figure 4A). This suggests that the cytosolic tail can signal independently of the ECM ligands when present on the cell surface. Haake et al. also reported reduced phosphorylation of FAK (Y397) when integrin β1 was knocked out or its cytosolic tail was mutated to disrupt protein binding. Conversely, cells expressing a nonfunctional extracellular domain but retaining a functional β1 cytosolic tail exhibited FAK activation levels comparable to wild-type cells. In contrast to blocking antibody-treated cells, which decreased phosphorylation of FAK (Y397), degrader-treated cells showed no decrease or even an increase in phosphorylated FAK levels (Supplementary Fig. S13). This discrepancy may be attributed to the compensatory upregulation of integrin α6 expression observed after degrader treatment (Figure 5A). Increased α6 expression is consistent with the increased α6 gene expression Haake et al. observed in β1 knockout cells. Furthermore, the decreased phosphorylation of A-Raf (Y301) and MEK1 (S298) observed with degrader treatment suggests the downregulation of the ERK signaling pathway. These findings imply that the anti-proliferative effects induced by integrin degraders may occur via a FAK-independent mechanism.  

While other bifunctional degraders targeting extracellular proteins have demonstrated in vivo degradation, no study has yet shown in vivo efficacy. It is still thought anti-tumor activity can be achieved with sufficient β5 degradation. Achieving in vivo efficacy requires optimizing the PK of these molecules, a challenge that, although not widely reported, is likely common across the field. Similarly, early intracellular targeted protein degradation drug development faced significant PK hurdles that demanded collaborative efforts to overcome. This suggests the extracellular targeted protein degradation field will similarly advance through studies like this one. We used a HEPG2 xenograft in vivo model, which Abt has not been reported to have been studied in this model. Previously, Abt was studied in patients with colorectal cancer and xenograft in vivo melanoma cancer models for efficacy, but there are no reports of tumor exposure nor target engagement levels. Nonetheless, we chose to start with a single-dose study in the HEPG2 xenograft model based on our positive in vitro data and other reported data. After careful in vivo PK profiling of both degrader molecules, the low bioavailability (< 1%) observed with Abt-triGalNAc suggested it was not a suitable candidate for the efficacy study. Extensive engineering is likely required to identify the appropriate site for specific conjugation using methods we have previously studied. The PK of the bispecific antibody degrader, Abt-anti-ZNRF3, predicted a 10 mg/kg dose would achieve a concentration of ~10 nM at the tumor after the first dose.

While this study did not show a significant difference in tumor volume between the treatment groups, which prevents us from drawing conclusions about the degraders’ efficacy, the results demonstrate a positive pharmacodynamic effect: the degradation of αv integrins. PK studies indicated an issue of low tumor exposure for both Abt and Abt-anti-ZNRF3, which was much lower than expected at the end of the study. This lower-than-expected exposure could explain the reduced degradation observed. Escalating the in vivo dose may potentially address this issue. However, if increasing the systemic dose cannot overcome low exposure in the tumor, biologics based on VHH or single-chain Fv fragments, or small molecule degraders, might offer higher permeability and improved tumor penetration. Establishing the proper in vivo dose is critical, but often very challenging to predict. While we did not observe a significant hook effect in our in vitro assays, it is a known challenge with bispecific molecules and has been reported with bispecific degrader antibodies. One approach to balancing the high-dose requirement while limiting the hook effect is to modulate the affinity of each Fab of the antibody. Another limitation of this study is that the HEPG2 xenograft model lacks a host immune component. Integrin inhibition is associated with T-regulatory suppression and increased tumor infiltration of effector T-cells, leading to anti-tumor immunity. As we were limited to the xenograft nude mouse model due to Abt’s lack of cross-reactivity to murine αv integrin, we were unable to assess any potential immune effects as a result of integrin degradation. Although the lack of efficacy from Abt alone prevents us from drawing definitive conclusions about the therapeutic potential of targeting αvβ5 in this study, the evident in vivo degradation of the target is a promising start. It suggests that with further optimization of PK, the biophysical properties of the antibodies, and the animal model, the encouraging in vitro findings may be successfully translated to in vivo. The observed degradation of αv integrins provides a foundation for future studies aimed at improving tumor exposure and overall effectiveness.

Bi-functional integrin degraders offer promising new therapies by not only blocking integrin-ligand interactions, but also by promoting integrin internalization and lysosomal degradation resulting in greater modulation of downstream signaling than ligand blocking alone. A limitation of this study is the lack of in vivo efficacy, but insights we provided here may be useful for the design and engineering of the next generation of integrin degraders so in vivo efficacy can be achieved. Beyond oncology, integrin degraders may improve treatments for other indications like inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) by removing integrin α4β7 from the relevant immune cells, and potentially down-regulate pro-inflammatory signaling pathways. While the clinically approved α4β7 blocking antibody, vedolizumab, is safe and addresses a significant population of IBD patients, the clinical remission rate of vedolizumab appeared inferior to that of anti-IL-23 and anti-TL1a therapies. Notably, vedolizumab was demonstrated to induce internalization of α4β7, yet the integrin is rapidly re-surfaced. It is therefore conceivable that a bi-functional α4β7 degrader may be more efficacious than vedolizumab. Furthermore, this research shows degradation platforms like LYTAC, AbTAC, and PROTAB are broadly applicable to a variety of membrane-bound proteins including historically challenging targets like integrins.

Materials and methods

Cell lines

HEPG2 (ATCC, HB-8065) cells were grown and maintained in T75 flasks (Corning, 430,641 U) at 37°C and 5% CO2 in DMEM (Gibco, 11,995–065) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (HI-FBS; Gibco, A38401) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Gibco, 15,140,122).

Cell surface protein internalization analysis by flow cytometry

The 6-well cell culture plates were coated with vitronectin (R&D Systems 2308-VN; 100 µg/well; 1 mL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)/well) by incubating for 1 hour at 37°C. PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) was aspirated from each well and HEPG2 cells were plated (350,000 cells per well) and incubated overnight. Treatment or controls were added to the cells in 2.5 mL complete growth media and incubated for the indicated time. Media was transferred to a conical tube, cells were washed with PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) and PBS was transferred to the conical tube, cells were lifted with Accutase (BioLegend, 423,201) and transferred to the conical tube. Cells were centrifuged and washed with PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) three times (degrader-treated cells no longer adhered well to the plate requiring careful washing as described). Cells were transferred to a 96-well V-bottom plate (Corning, 3344). Cells were washed three times with PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) + 0.5% BSA (Fisher, BP1605) (wash buffer). Cells were incubated with human Fc block (BD Biosciences, 564,228) for 15 minutes on ice, washed three times with wash buffer. Cells were incubated with primary antibodies for 1 hour on ice and then washed three times with wash buffer. Cells were incubated with secondary antibody for 1 hour on ice and washed three times with wash buffer. Cells were resuspended in wash buffer supplemented with propidium iodide (BD Biosciences, 51-66211E). Flow cytometry was performed using a MacsQuant16 (Miltenyi Biotec). FlowJo software was used for analysis. Antibodies used can be found in Supplementary Table S1.

Protein degradation analysis by Western blotting

The 6-well cell culture plates were coated with vitronectin (R&D Systems 2308-VN; 100 µg/well; 1 mL PBS/well) by incubating for 1 hour at 37°C. PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) was aspirated from each well and HEPG2 cells were plated (350,000 cells per well) and incubated overnight. Treatment or controls were added to the cells in 2.5 mL complete growth media and incubated for the indicated time. Cells were washed and lifted as described above. Cells were lysed with RIPA buffer (Thermo, 89,900) + protease inhibitor cocktail (Thermo, A32965) + 0.1% bezonase (Sigma, 707,463) + phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Thermo, A32957) on ice for 45 minutes. Cells were centrifuged at 21,000 g for 15 minutes at 4°C. Supernatant was transferred to an Eppendorf tube. The total protein concentration was measured using a BCA assay (Thermo, A55861). Equal amounts of cell lysate were loaded onto a 4–12% Bis-Tris (Invitrogen, NW04120BOX) gel. The gel was transferred to nitrocellulose membrane (Invitrogen, IB33002), blocked with iBind blocking solution (Invitrogen, SLF2020), incubated with primary and secondary antibodies and washed using the iBind. Total protein labeling was used where indicated as a loading control (Thermo, A44449). The membrane was developed using SuperSignal West Pico Plus chemiluminescence solution (Thermo, 34,578) and imaged using the iBright and analyzed using ImageJ Analysis Software. Antibodies used can be found in Supplementary Table S1.

Anti-proliferation assay

The 24-well cell culture plates were coated with vitronectin (R&D Systems 2308-VN; 20 µg/well; 1 mL PBS/well) by incubating for 1 hour at 37°C. PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) was aspirated from each well and HEPG2 cells were plated (100,000 cells/well) and incubated overnight at 37°C. Media was aspirated and treatments in fresh media were added to cells. Cells were imaged every 4 hours in the IncuCyteS3/SX5 Live-Cell Imaging system using a 10X objective in the phase-imaging channel.

The Incucyte Analysis Software was used to quantify the phase confluence (%). The following parameters were set for the analysis: segmentation adjustment = 1, hole fill (µm2) = 300, and area minimum (µm2) = 150.

Percent proliferation was then calculated as described previously.

Proteomics of downstream protein expression

The 6-well cell culture plates were coated with vitronectin (R&D Systems 2308-VN; 100 µg/well; 1 mL PBS/well) by incubating for 1 hour at 37°C. PBS (Cytiva, SH30256.01) was aspirated from each well and HEPG2 cells were plated (350,000 cells per well) and incubated overnight. Treatment or controls were added to the cells in 2.5 mL complete growth media and incubated for the indicated time. Cells were washed and lifted as described above. Cells were lysed (2% LDS, 25 mM DTT, PhosSTOP Phosphatase Inhibitor, cOmplete Protease Inhibitor) for 10 minutes at 95°C. Cells were centrifuged at 16,000 g for 5 minutes at 4°C. Supernatant was transferred to an Eppendorf tube. Samples were further processed as previously described. The samples were read on a Luminex instrument. Antibodies are proprietary to Assay Engineers (Hyperplex).
