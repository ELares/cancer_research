# A novel anti-PD-L1/IL-8 bispecific antibody BP2402 enhances antitumor immunity and modulates inflammatory signaling in triple-negative breast cancer mice model.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is an aggressive malignancy with limited therapeutic options. Immune checkpoint inhibitors targeting the programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) pathway show restricted efficacy in TNBC, with response rates of only 5-10% as monotherapy. Interleukin-8 (IL-8/CXCL8) signaling promotes immunosuppression and mediates resistance to anti-PD-L1 therapy, necessitating combination approaches to overcome these limitations. However, the underlying mechanisms of enhanced efficacy from dual pathway targeting require further investigation.

**METHODS**: We generated humanized mouse models by reconstituting immunodeficient mice with human PBMCs from five donors (n = 5 mice/group). MDA-MB-231 TNBC cells were implanted subcutaneously, and mice were treated with vehicle control, atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1), HuMax-IL8 (anti-IL-8), combination therapy, or a novel bispecific antibody BP2402 targeting both PD-L1 and IL-8. Antitumor activity was assessed alongside single-cell RNA sequencing of tumors and mechanistic analyses including immunofluorescence and Western blot.

**RESULTS**: Combination therapy demonstrated significantly enhanced tumor growth inhibition compared to atezolizumab monotherapy in responsive donor models (51.28% vs. 39.13% for donor 3, p < 0.01; 44.01% vs. 6.57% for donor 4, p < 0.01). Single-cell RNA sequencing showed higher intratumoral T-cell fractions with combination therapy (donor 3: 80.5% vs. 26.7%; donor 4: 63.6% vs. 13.0% compared to control). BP2402 maintained high binding affinity for both IL-8 (KD = 2.132 nM) and PD-L1 (KD = 1.473 nM), and demonstrated superior antitumor efficacy compared to monotherapies (p < 0.001 vs. vehicle, p < 0.01 vs. individual antibodies). BP2402 treatment significantly reduced CXCL8 and VEGFA expression, suppressed JAK1/STAT1 signaling pathway activation, and upregulated pro-apoptotic proteins including FAS and BAX while effectively modulating T cell exhaustion markers PD-1 and TIM-3.

**CONCLUSIONS**: These results indicate that dual targeting of PD-L1 and IL-8 pathways represents a promising therapeutic strategy for TNBC. The bispecific antibody approach offers superior therapeutic potential by simultaneously modulating immune checkpoints, inflammatory signaling, and angiogenesis, effectively addressing resistance mechanisms. Additional preclinical optimization and clinical studies are required to fully assess the therapeutic potential of this novel immunotherapeutic approach.

Background

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is recognized as a highly aggressive subtype of breast cancer, accounting for approximately 10–15% of all breast cancer cases. Characterized by the absence of estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), TNBC poses significant challenges due to its rapid progression, high metastatic potential, and limited treatment options. Patients with TNBC experience significantly poorer prognoses compared to those with other breast cancer subtypes, with five-year survival rates markedly lower than those of hormone receptor-positive breast cancers.

While immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), notably PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors, have improved overall survival in various advanced solid tumors, many patients still exhibit inadequate responses to these treatments. Immunosuppressive conditions within the tumor microenvironment (TME) hinder the effectiveness of ICIs, thereby reducing their clinical efficacy. Therefore, there is an urgent need for novel treatment combinations that enhance the TME, making it more conducive to effective immune checkpoint inhibition.

Interleukin-8 (IL-8) has emerged as a crucial mediator of tumor progression and immune evasion across multiple cancer types, including TNBC. This proinflammatory chemokine is expressed by numerous solid tumors, including those of the brain, breast, cervical, colon, gastric, lung, melanoma, mesothelioma, ovarian, prostate, renal, and thyroid tissues, facilitating their growth, survival, and spread. Elevated serum levels of IL-8 have been associated with tumor progression and poor clinical outcomes in patients treated with PD-L1 inhibitors. Specifically, high circulating IL-8 levels correlate with reduced clinical benefit from immune checkpoint inhibitors, suggesting that suppressing IL-8 may enhance treatment efficacy.

Moreover, IL-8 plays a pivotal role in shaping the TME by facilitating the infiltration of neutrophils and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), collectively contributing to an immunosuppressive milieu that hinders T cell activity. Importantly, IL-8 is closely linked to the vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) signaling pathway, promoting angiogenesis and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)—prominent processes that aid in metastasis.

Recent studies have suggested that targeting both PD-L1 and IL-8 might represent a novel therapeutic strategy. By simultaneously inhibiting these immunosuppressive mechanisms, this approach aims to overcome the limitations of existing therapies. The proposed bispecific antibody that simultaneously targets PD-L1 and IL-8 not only aims to enhance anti-tumor immune responses in TNBC but also seeks to alter the TME to improve the clinical efficacy of immunotherapy.

The primary objectives of this study are to develop and characterize this novel bispecific antibody, evaluate its potential to enhance anti-tumor immune responses, and investigate its efficacy in overcoming resistance to immunotherapy in TNBC. By addressing these critical immunosuppressive pathways, this strategy endeavors to tackle the significant challenges associated with TNBC immunotherapy and potentially improve clinical outcomes for patients suffering from this malignant disease.

Materials and methods

Mice

Female C-NKG mice (6–8 weeks old) were purchased from Cyagen Biosciences (Suzhou) Co., Ltd. Animal care and experimental procedures were performed under SPF conditions. IVC mouse cages were used, with five mice per cage. All procedures involving mice were conducted in accordance with the guidelines outlined in the National Research Council's Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals, as well as the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC). The procedures also followed the guidelines provided by the Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International (AAALAC). Animals were housed in a temperature and humidity-controlled room with a 12-h light/dark cycle, and were provided with standard laboratory water and food.

In vivo experiments

MDA-MB-231 cells were cultured as a monolayer in Leibovitz’s L-15 medium supplemented with 15% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Gibco) and 1% antibiotic–antimycotic solution at 37 °C in a CO2-free incubator. When the cells reached 80–90% confluence, they were harvested and resuspended in serum-free L-15 medium at a concentration of 1 × 108 cells/mL. The cell suspension was then mixed with an equal volume of Matrigel® (Corning) to prepare the inoculum. Each mouse was subcutaneously injected with 0.2 mL of the cell/Matrigel® mixture into the right scapular region.

Three days prior to tumor cell inoculation, human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (hPBMCs) from five donors were thawed, collected by centrifugation, and passed through a 70-μm cell strainer. The cells were then resuspended in serum-free RPMI-1640 medium and adjusted to a final concentration of 2.5 × 10⁷ cells/mL. Each mouse received 0.2 mL of the hPBMC suspension via tail vein injection. Upon reaching an average tumor volume of approximately 120 mm3, the animals were stratified into randomized groups based on tumor volume, ensuring a variance within each group of less than 10% of the mean. Animal tumor volumes were measured thrice weekly by experimenters who were unaware of the specific agents administered. Tumor length and width were measured using digital calipers, and tumor volume was estimated from these measurements. The tumor volume was calculated using the following formula: tumor volume (mm3) = [(width)2 × length]/2.

Human donor characteristics

In this study, PBMCs from five donors were utilized. The PBMCs were obtained from healthy donors and patients recruited at Shanghai Zhaxin Hospital of Traditional Chinese and Western Medicine. All PBMC collection and screening procedures were conducted under the approval of the Ethics Committee of Shanghai Zhaxin Hospital of Traditional Chinese and Western Medicine (approval number: XF0102232W) and in full compliance with the Declaration of Helsinki and medical ethics guidelines. All donors met the established inclusion criteria and were screened according to standard clinical protocols. All five donors were healthy males aged 20–30 years. Donor 3 detailed HLA typings are as follows: HLA-A: 33:03, 66:01; HLA-B: 41:XX, 58:01; and HLA-C: 03:02, 17:03. Similarly, Donor-4, HLA typings include HLA-A: 23:01, 34:02; HLA-B: 15:17, 53:01; and HLA-C: 04:01, 05:01. Both donors provided informed consent in accordance with ethical guidelines. The compatibility of their HLA types with the MDA-MB-231 cell line was evaluated, and it was noted that while Donor-3 may have a partial match, no complete matches were identified for either donor's HLA types with the MDA-MB-231 cells. These comprehensive details about the donors enhance the transparency and rigor of our study.

Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) staining

Tissue samples were fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin for at least 24 h, processed through graded ethanol and xylene, and embedded in paraffin. Sections (4 μm) were cut, placed on glass slides, and dried. Standard H&E staining was performed: slides were dewaxed, rehydrated, stained with hematoxylin and eosin, dehydrated, cleared, and coverslipped. Histological analysis was conducted under a light microscope.

Single-cell sequencing

Single-cell preparation, library synthesis, RNA sequencing, and data analysis were completed by Gene Denovo (Guangzhou, China) using 10 × Genomics Chromium Single Cell Controller. Fresh tumor tissues were dissociated with Gentle MACS Dissociator (Miltenyi Biotec) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Next, single cells were counted using an automated cell counter and adjusted to a concentration of 1000 cells/µL. After initial quality control, cellular suspensions were loaded on a 10 × Genomics GemCode Single-cell instrument that generates single-cell Gel Beads-In-Emulsions. The cells were lysed, and RNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA. Libraries were generated from the cDNA with Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3′ Reagent Kit V.3.1. Finally, the cDNA libraries were sequenced using the Nova 6000 sequencing system (Illumina). 10 × Genomics Cell Ranger software (version 3.1.0) was used to convert raw BCL files to FASTQ files, alignment and counts quantification.

Subsequent data analysis, including principal component analysis and t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (t-SNE analysis), was achieved using the Seurat software. Seurat embed cells in a shared-nearest neighbor graph, with edges drawn between cells based on similar gene expression patterns. Unsupervised density-based clustering by density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (DBSCAN) clustering algorithm on the t-SNE analysis was performed to separate tumor cells into the distinct homogenous groups.

Public dataset analysis

To validate our preclinical findings, we analyzed scRNA-seq data from TNBC patients treated with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy (GSE169246). Among 10 patients, we selected three with highest and three with lowest intratumoral IL-8 gene expression for comparative analysis. Data processing and cell type annotation were performed using Seurat (v5.3.0) in R (v4.5.1). Major cell types and T cell subtypes were identified using canonical immune cell markers as described in references.

RNA isolation and quantittive real-time PCR (qPCR)

For each tumor section, the entire tumor area was outlined, and three independent measurements were taken within this region. Mean gray values were obtained after applying the threshold and measuring the selected area. The three measurements were averaged to generate a representative value for each tumor section, minimizing potential regional variations. Data were statistically analyzed using GraphPad Prism software, with p < 0.05 considered statistically significant. Results

Combined blockade of PD-L1 and IL-8 enhances anti-tumor efficacy in humanized triple-negative breast cancer mice models

Immune checkpoint inhibitors targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis have shown limited efficacy in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), with response rates of only 5–10% as monotherapy, highlighting the need for combination strategies to overcome resistance mechanisms.

We established humanized mouse models using C-NKG mice reconstituted with human PBMCs from five different donors. This multi-donor approach aimed to capture the heterogeneity observed in clinical responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors, as patient-specific factors such as immune cell composition significantly influence treatment outcomes. MDA-MB-231 TNBC cells were implanted subcutaneously, and mice were randomized into four treatment groups with 5 mice per group: vehicle control (without PBMC reconstitution), PBMC reconstitution only, and treated with PBS, atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1, 5 mg/kg), or combination of atezolizumab (5 mg/kg) and HuMax-IL8 (5 mg/kg).

Tumor growth curves revealed donor-dependent responses to immunotherapy interventions (Fig. 1A). Notably, in mice reconstituted with PBMCs from donors 3 and 4, combination therapy demonstrated significantly enhanced tumor growth inhibition compared to atezolizumab monotherapy (51.28% vs. 39.13% for donor 3, p < 0.01; 44.01% vs. 6.57% for donor 4, p < 0.01). In contrast, mice reconstituted with PBMCs from donors 1 and 5 showed more modest differences between combination therapy and monotherapy, mice reconstituted with PBMCs from donors 2 showed that atezolizumab monotherapy exerted better efficacy, highlighting the heterogeneity in treatment response across different immune backgrounds.

This donor-dependent pattern aligns with clinical observations where only a subset of patients responds to immune checkpoint blockade. The variable response to PD-L1 blockade across donors may reflect differences in T cell receptor repertoire or memory T cell populations. The enhanced efficacy observed with combination therapy specifically in donors 3 and 4 suggests that dual targeting of PD-L1 and IL-8 may be particularly effective in certain immune contexts, potentially overcoming resistance mechanisms present in the tumor microenvironment.

Consistent with previous observations of donor-dependent variability in humanized models, flow cytometric analysis of peripheral blood at days 15 and 22 after PBMC infusion showed notable differences in human immune cell engraftment among donors (Fig. 1B). The mean proportion of hCD45+ cells was approximately 50% in donors 1, 3, and 5 models, indicating efficient immune reconstitution, while donors 2 and 4 models exhibited lower engraftment levels (approximately 20%).Notably, despite these differences, enhanced anti-tumor efficacy of combination treatment was observed not only in the high-engraftment donor 3 model, but also in the donor 4 model with moderate immune chimerism. This suggests that, beyond overall engraftment efficiency, the functional status of immune cells or tumor microenvironmental factors may play a key role in modulating therapeutic responses.

Based on these findings, we selected the responsive models (donors 3 and 4) for further mechanistic investigations to understand the cellular and molecular basis for the enhanced efficacy of dual PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade. These models provided an opportunity to elucidate how targeting the IL-8 axis might complement PD-L1 blockade to improve anti-tumor immunity in TNBC.

Single-cell RNA sequencing reveals enhanced T cell infiltration following combination therapy

To further investigate the cellular mechanisms underlying the superior anti-tumor efficacy observed with combined PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade, we performed single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) on tumors harvested from representative donors 3 and 4 PBMC-reconstituted mice across three treatment groups: PBS, atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1, 5 mg/kg),and combination of atezolizumab (5 mg/kg) and HuMax-IL-8 (5 mg/kg). One representative mouse was selected from each treatment group within each donor cohort for exploratory profiling. Although the limited sample size does not permit statistical comparisons or definitive quantitative conclusions, this approach may facilitate the generation of mechanistic hypotheses in early-stage studies.

After stringent quality control, transcriptome profiling and unsupervised clustering using the Seurat pipeline, four major cell populations in the tumor microenvironment were identified: tumor epithelial cells (EPCAM+, KRT8+, KRT18+), cancer-associated fibroblasts (PDGFRA+, COL1A1+,ACTA2+), macrophages (AIF1+, CD163+, MRC1+), and T cells (CD3D+, CD3E+, CD3G+) (Fig. 2A). Marker gene expression was further validated by t-SNE feature plots (Fig. 2B) and dimensionality reduction (Fig. 2C), which demonstrated clear segregation of these populations.

Remarkably, both donors 3 and 4 models demonstrated a consistent pattern of increased T-cell proportions in tumors from mice treated with the combination of atezolizumab and HuMax-IL-8. Representative t-SNE plots illustrated this expansion of the T cell compartment, particularly within the combination therapy group (Fig. 2D). In the donor 3 model, the proportion of T cells increased from 26.73% in PBS controls to 57.14% in the atezolizumab monotherapy group, and further to 80.49% in the combination therapy group (Fig. 2E). A similar pattern was observed in donor 4, where T cell proportions rose from 12.98% in PBS to 50.88% following atezolizumab treatment, and reached 63.56% in the combination therapy group. (Fig. 2F). The consistency of this pattern across two distinct donor models supports the reproducibility of this biological phenomenon, though further validation with larger sample sizes will be necessary to confirm these findings.

We next subclustered T cells to resolve functional states using canonical markers (Fig. 2G). Representative t-SNE plots demonstrated that combination therapy promoted an expansion of effector/cytotoxic T-cell populations (68.08% vs. 55.98% for PBS and 65.39% for atezolizumab monotherapy) (Fig. 2H–J). Notably, this enhanced effector T-cell representation occurred in the context of substantially increased overall T-cell infiltration (Fig. 2E, F), indicating that combined PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade enhances both the absolute numbers and relative enrichment of therapeutically relevant T-cell subsets. These complementary changes suggest that dual pathway inhibition may overcome immunosuppressive barriers more effectively than PD-L1 blockade alone, promoting a more robust antitumor T-cell response.

Consistent with previous reports on PBMC-humanized mouse models, we did not detect discrete B cell clusters or MDSC populations in our scRNA-seq analysis. This absence suggests that, in this immunodeficient mouse model and following immune reconstitution, transplanted tumors may not involve MDSC participation. This finding implies that the enhanced therapeutic efficacy observed with the combination treatment may operate through alternative mechanisms, potentially involving increased T cell activation and function without the suppressive influence of MDSCs.

Enhanced efficacy of combination therapy is mediated by concurrent anti-inflammatory, pro-immune, and anti-angiogenic effects

To identify the molecular mechanisms underlying the enhanced efficacy of combined PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade, we conducted differential gene expression and pathway analyses on the scRNA-seq datasets from both donors 3 and 4 models. The KEGG pathway enrichment analysis of differentially expressed genes in the donor 3 model revealed that the HIF-1 signaling pathway was the most significantly enriched, followed by pathways related to rheumatoid arthritis, PI3K-AKT signaling, and cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, which collectively underscore inflammatory processes within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 3A). The presence of enriched pathways related to cytokine signaling highlights the inflammatory milieu that can influence tumor progression and response to therapy. Notably, the T cell receptor signaling pathway was also enriched, correlating with the increased T cell infiltration observed following combination therapy. This suggests that the combination of PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade not only enhances T cell recruitment but also improves the functionality of these immune cells in the tumor environment.

Volcano plot analysis revealed distinct transcriptional changes in both donor 3 (Fig. 3B, C) and donor 4 (Fig. 3E, F) models. Comparison between combination therapy and atezolizumab alone identified significant upregulation of T cell activation genes (including CD28, CXCL9, GZMK) and downregulation of VEGFA and TGFBI in donor 3 groups. The comparison between combination therapy and PBS control exhibited more pronounced differential expression, with significant upregulation of immune effector molecules and downregulation of multiple immunosuppressive factors.

Based on pathway analysis and differential expression patterns, we examined the expression of key genes involved in these critical biological processes (Fig. 3D, G).

Results showed that combined therapy decreased expression of pro-inflammatory mediators IL-8, IL-6, and IL-18 compared to both monotherapy and PBS controls. These cytokines have been implicated in promoting immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments and therapy resistance. Moreover, increased expression of IFNG with concurrent elevated expression of NFKB1. IFNG is essential for cytotoxic T cell function and antigen presentation, while increased NFKB1 (encoding the p105/p50 subunit) may help limit excessive inflammatory signaling by forming inhibitory p50 homodimers, thereby promoting a balanced immune response that favors antitumor T-cell activity over chronic inflammation.

Combination therapy also markedly reduced VEGFA and TGFBI expression relative to both comparison groups. VEGFA, closely linked to HIF-1 signaling, serves as a critical driver of tumor angiogenesis and vascular abnormalities that contribute to hypoxia and immunosuppression, while TGFBI plays a crucial role in breast cancer by regulating tumor and metastasis initiation through its effects on the tumor microenvironment and hypoxia. The observed decrease in both VEGFA and TGFBI expression (Fig. 3D, G) suggests the potential of combination therapy to disrupt these pathways and remodel the tumor microenvironment.

These results suggest that the combination of PD-L1 and IL-8 blockade reduces immunosuppressive inflammation, enhances effector T cell activation, and inhibits tumor angiogenesis. The consistency of these patterns across both donor 3 and donor 4 models, despite differences in immune reconstitution efficiency, indicates robust molecular reprogramming associated with the combination therapy.

Low intratumoral IL-8 expression correlates with enhanced T cell infiltration following anti-PD-1 immunotherapy in clinical TNBC

To validate our preclinical findings from mouse models in a clinical context, we analyzed a public TNBC dataset (GSE169246) from patients who received anti-PD-1 immunotherapy combined with paclitaxel. Among 10 patients treated with anti-PD-1 therapy, we selected 3 patients with the highest intratumoral IL-8 expression and 3 patients with the lowest expression to maximize the contrast and examine whether IL-8 levels influence immune cell composition as observed in our preclinical studies (Fig. 4A).

Single-cell RNA sequencing analysis revealed remarkable differences in immune cell infiltration that strongly supported our preclinical observations. Most notably, IL-8 Low patients demonstrated substantially higher T cell infiltration (18.92% vs. 7.35% in IL-8 High patients), accompanied by a dramatic reduction in MDSC populations (0.19% vs. 9.69% in IL-8 High patients). Additionally, IL-8 Low tumors showed reduced cancer-associated fibroblasts (1.54% vs. 17.85%), increased tumor epithelial cells (8.40% vs. 1.59%), and elevated macrophage infiltration (16.86% vs. 10.88%), indicating comprehensive immune microenvironment remodeling associated with low IL-8 signaling (Fig. 4B, C).

Detailed analysis of T cell subpopulations revealed that the enhanced T cell infiltration in IL-8 Low patients was accompanied by a more favorable effector profile. IL-8 Low tumors contained higher proportions of effector T cells (14.22% vs. 0.76%) and activated T cells (5.46% vs. 3.88%) compared to IL-8 High patients (Fig. 4C). Conversely, IL-8 High patients showed enrichment in CD8-CXCL13 T cells (48.73% vs. 0.73%) and higher MAIT cell proportions (1.25% vs. 4.49%), potentially reflecting different activation states or exhaustion patterns (Fig. 4D, E). These clinical data provide compelling validation of our preclinical findings.

Design, expression and in vitro characterization of a novel bispecific antibody targeting PD-L1 and IL-8

To concurrently target PD-L1 and IL-8 within the tumor microenvironment, we developed a bispecific antibody (BP2402) that incorporates the clinically validated atezolizumab sequence for PD-L1 binding, alongside a novel humanized nanobody aimed at IL-8 signal pathway blocking (Fig. 5A). The nanobody format was selected for the anti-IL-8 component to leverage several advantages including smaller size, and its single-chain structure, which simplifies production and enhances stability compared to traditional bispecific antibodies.

BP2402 was expressed in CHO-S cells and purified by Protein A affinity chromatography. SDS-PAGE analysis under reducing (Lane 1) and non-reducing (Lane 2) conditions demonstrated high purity (> 95%) and the expected molecular weight profiles (Fig. 5B). Under reducing conditions, distinct bands corresponding to the heavy chain (approximately 65 kDa) and light chain (approximately 25 kDa) components were observed, while non-reducing conditions revealed a predominant high molecular weight band (approximately 200 kDa) representing the intact bispecific antibody.

SPR analysis demonstrated that BP2402 maintained high affinity for both targets (Fig. 5C, D). The bispecific antibody bound to IL-8 with association (ka) and dissociation (kd) rates of 1.455 × 105 (1/Ms) and 3.102 × 10–4 (1/s), respectively, yielding an equilibrium dissociation constant (KD) of 2.132 nM. For PD-L1, BP2402 exhibited slightly higher affinity with ka = 1.886 × 105 (1/Ms), kd = 2.777 × 10–4 (1/s), and KD = 1.473 nM. These findings indicate that BP2402 possesses favorable affinity for both IL-8 and PD-L1.

The bispecific antibody was evaluated for its capacity to inhibit both IL-8 and PD-L1 signaling pathways. BP2402 effectively blocked IL-8/CXCR1 signaling with an IC50 of 14.86 nM, which is less potent than the benchmark anti-IL-8 antibody HuMax-IL8, with an IC50 of 0.93 nM (Fig. 5E). Similarly, BP2402 inhibited IL-8/CXCR2 signaling with an IC50 of 88.18 nM, also demonstrating lower potency compared to HuMax-IL-8 (IC50 = 16.51 nM). This lower efficacy may be attributable to the distinct binding characteristics of nanobodies compared to full-length antibodies targeting IL-8 (Fig. 5F).

For PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, BP2402 demonstrated potent activity with an EC50 of 0.3329, nearly identical to the atezolizumab biosimilar (EC50 = 0.3565) (Fig. 5G). These results confirm that BP2402 maintains the functional activity of both parental antibodies, effectively inhibiting both the immunosuppressive PD-1/PD-L1 axis and pro-inflammatory/pro-angiogenic IL-8 signaling pathways.

Bispecific antibody BP2402 demonstrates superior antitumor efficacy in vivo compared to monotherapies

To evaluate the in vivo efficacy of BP2402, we conducted a therapeutic study using humanized mouse models, specifically C-NKG mice reconstituted with human PBMCs from Donor 3. Mice (n = 6 per group) bearing established MDA-MB-231 tumors were randomly assigned to receive either vehicle, anti-PD-L1 mAb (5 mg/kg), HuMax-IL8 (5 mg/kg), a combination of anti-PD-L1 mAb (5 mg/kg) and HuMax-IL8 (5 mg/kg), or BP2402 (8 mg/kg).

Treatment with BP2402 resulted in significant tumor growth inhibition compared to vehicle control, with superior efficacy to either monotherapy (Fig. 6A). By day 22, the mean tumor volume in the BP2402 group was significantly lower than in the vehicle (p < 0.05), anti-PD-L1 mAb (p < 0.01), and HuMax-IL8 (p < 0.05) groups. Interestingly, BP2402 demonstrated slightly greater tumor growth inhibition than the combination therapy, although this difference did not reach statistical significance. Despite BP2402 exhibiting lower in vitro activity than HuMax-IL-8 in blocking IL-8/CXCR1 and CXCR2 signaling pathways (Fig. 5E, F), BP2402 achieved superior in vivo efficacy, suggesting that the bispecific format provides unique advantages through enhanced tumor localization and concurrent pathway targeting. Individual tumor growth curves (Fig. 6C) revealed consistent responses across animals within each treatment group, with BP2402-treated mice showing the most uniform tumor growth inhibition pattern. All treatments were well-tolerated, with no significant differences in body weight observed throughout the study period (Fig. 6B).

Histopathological examination of tumor sections by H&E staining revealed marked differences in immune cell infiltration across treatment groups (Fig. 6D). While vehicle-treated tumors showed minimal immune infiltrates, tumors from anti-PD-L1 mAb and HuMax-IL8 monotherapy groups exhibited modest increases in infiltrating immune cells (black arrows). Notably, both the combination therapy and BP2402-treated tumors displayed substantially enhanced immune cell infiltration. These histological observations are consistent with our single-cell RNA sequencing findings of increased T cell recruitment following these treatments and align with the comprehensive immune cell profiling from public clinical datasets (Fig. 4B, C), which demonstrated enhanced infiltration of multiple immune effector populations including T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells in IL-8 low tumors. This convergent evidence supports that IL-8 blockade promotes broad immune cell recruitment and activation within the tumor microenvironment.

BP2402 enhances T cell antitumor immunity by modulating the expression of exhaustion and activation markers

Following our in vivo efficacy studies, we sought to validate transcriptomic findings from single-cell RNA sequencing at both mRNA and protein levels. Tumor tissues from three mice per treatment group were analyzed for expression of key T cell function-related genes and proteins associated with T cell exhaustion and activation (Fig. 7).

mRNA analysis revealed that BP2402 treatment significantly upregulated PDCD1 expression compared to vehicle control, anti-PD-L1 monotherapy, and HuMax-IL8 (Fig. 7A). Similarly, HAVCR2 level were elevated after PD-L1 blockade treatment, suggesting a potential feedback regulation in response to immune checkpoint inhibition (Fig. 7B). CD28 expression, crucial for T cell activation, showed a trend towards higher levels in the BP2402 group (Fig. 7C).

Immunofluorescence staining confirmed these findings at the protein level. PD-1 protein expression was significantly reduced in tumors treated with BP2402 compared to all other treatment groups, including the combination therapy (Fig. 7D, F), indicating the efficacy of BP2402 in mitigating adaptive resistance. Notably, TIM-3 protein levels were lower in BP2402-treated tumors, contrasting with the IL-8 monotherapy group, which exhibited elevated TIM-3 levels (Fig. 7E, G).

Overall, these results indicate that BP2402 effectively modulates key immune checkpoint molecules. Its ability to simultaneously regulate PD-1 and TIM-3 while maintaining CD28 expression likely contributes to enhanced antitumor efficacy observed in vivo. Targeting both pathways with a single bispecific antibody may provide a more effective strategy than utilizing separate antibodies.

BP2402 enhances T cell activation and promotes pro-apoptotic processes in the tumor microenvironment

To validate our previous findings, we further investigated the effects of BP2402 on T cell activation and tumor cell apoptosis. Results showed that BP2402 treatment significantly upregulated CD44 mRNA expression compared to vehicle control and anti-PD-L1 monotherapy (Fig. 8A). CD44, a marker of effector and memory T cells, is crucial for T cell activation, proliferation, and migration, indicating enhanced T cell activation with BP2402. However, immunofluorescence staining revealed that CD44 protein levels were highest in the IL-8 monotherapy group, with BP2402 demonstrating superior effects compared to the combination therapy but lower than that of the IL-8 group, and no significant difference compared to the vehicle and anti-PD-L1 groups (Fig. 8D, F).

Significantly, FAS (CD95) expression was elevated at the mRNA level in BP2402-treated samples compared to vehicle control and anti-PD-L1 monotherapy (Fig. 8B). FAS is a key death receptor that triggers extrinsic apoptosis upon engagement with FAS ligand (FASL) from activated T cells. Immunofluorescence analysis confirmed robust FAS protein expression in BP2402-treated tumors, significantly higher than in anti-PD-L1 and HuMax-IL8 monotherapy groups, and comparable to the combination therapy (Fig. 8E, G).

We also assessed BAX expression, a pro-apoptotic member of the Bcl-2 family. BP2402 treatment led to increased BAX mRNA levels compared to vehicle control and anti-PD-L1 monotherapy (Fig. 8C), although this increase did not reach statistical significance when compared to the HuMax-IL8 or combination therapy groups.

BP2402 effectively suppresses IL-8 mediated signaling pathways

In alignment with our scRNA-seq findings, we examined the expression of key factors involved in IL-8 signaling pathways. Our results showed that anti-PD-L1 monotherapy significantly increased CXCL8 expression compared to vehicle control (Fig. 9A), consistent with observed compensatory upregulation of inflammatory pathways following immune checkpoint blockade. In contrast, BP2402 treatment maintained lower CXCL8 levels compared to anti-PD-L1 monotherapy, highlighting its efficacy in blocking IL-8 signaling pathways.

Additionally, BP2402 significantly reduced VEGFA expression compared to vehicle control and all other treatment groups, including combination therapy (Fig. 9B). This potent anti-angiogenic effect was accompanied by a substantial reduction in HIF1A expression (Fig. 9C), a key transcriptional regulator of hypoxia and angiogenesis, previously identified as enriched in our transcriptomic analysis.

Immunofluorescence analysis confirmed these findings at the protein level. BP2402 significantly reduced IL-8 protein expression compared to vehicle control and anti-PD-L1 monotherapy, achieving levels comparable to or lower than those seen with HuMax-IL8 or combination therapy (Fig. 9D, F). Notably, VEGFA protein expression was nearly abolished in BP2402-treated tumors (Fig. 9E, G), significantly lower than all other treatment groups, including combination therapy.

These results underscore the superiority of BP2402 in simultaneously targeting multiple tumor-promoting pathways. While both BP2402 and HuMax-IL8 target IL-8, BP2402 exhibits lower in vitro activity than HuMax-IL8 in blocking the IL-8 signaling pathway. However, the structural advantages of BP2402 enhance its localization within tumor tissues, facilitating more effective inhibition of both IL-8 and VEGF signaling. This is reflected in the greater reduction of VEGF and IL-8 protein levels observed in vivo, which validates the enhanced therapeutic potential of this bispecific antibody format compared to conventional combination therapy.

BP2402 effectively inhibits JAK1/STAT1 signaling while enhancing pro-apoptotic Bax expression

As our results show, BP2402 exerts efficacy in blocking IL-8 singnal pathway. The IL-8 signaling pathway is involved in inflammatory responses, and our previous scRNA-seq results indicated that BP2402 regulates anti-tumor immunity by modulating inflammatory pathways. To further elucidate the molecular mechanisms underlying the efficacy of BP2402, we performed Western blot analysis of key inflammatory signaling pathways in tumor tissues from each treatment group.

Our Western blot analysis revealed a critical role for the JAK1/STAT1 signaling pathway in mediating the differential effects of these therapeutic interventions. Anti-PD-L1 monotherapy significantly increased JAK1 expression and activation of STAT1 (evidenced by increased phosphorylation) compared to vehicle control (Fig. 10A–D). This upregulation of JAK1/STAT1 signaling likely reflects compensatory inflammatory signaling triggered by PD-L1 blockade, which has been associated with treatment resistance mechanisms.

In contrast, BP2402 treatment resulted in marked suppression of JAK1 expression and STAT1 activation, with levels comparable to HuMax-IL8 monotherapy and significantly lower than both anti-PD-L1 monotherapy and combination therapy (Fig. 9B–D). The p-STAT1/STAT1 ratio, a direct measure of pathway activation status, was significantly reduced in the BP2402 group compared to anti-PD-L1 monotherapy and combination therapy (Fig. 10E). This efficient inhibition of JAK1/STAT1 signaling by BP2402 aligns with its superior suppression of inflammatory cytokines and angiogenic factors observed in our previous analyses.

Concurrently, BP2402 treatment robustly increased expression of the pro-apoptotic protein Bax compared to all other treatment groups (Fig. 10A, F). This upregulation of Bax provides further evidence for the ability of BP2402 to prime tumor cells for apoptosis, complementing our findings on increased FAS expression and consistent with the enhanced antitumor efficacy observed in vivo.

Discussion

Recognizing the fundamental challenge posed by limited response rates to immune checkpoint inhibitors and the emergence of resistance mechanisms, this study aims to explore strategies to enhance the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy. Our goal was to develop a more effective therapeutic strategy by simultaneously targeting immune checkpoint pathways and addressing the complex interplay of IL-8 in tumorigenesis and the tumor microenvironment. This approach is supported by accumulating evidence that indicates inflammatory mediators, particularly IL-8, can promote immune suppression and mediate resistance to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy. To address this, we first evaluated the efficacy of combining anti-PD-L1 (atezolizumab) and anti-IL-8 (HuMax-IL-8) antibodies before developing our novel bispecific antibody (BP2402) targeting both pathways.

Our results demonstrate that simultaneous blockade of PD-L1 and IL-8 significantly enhances antitumor efficacy compared to single-agent PD-L1 therapy, as evidenced by the significant tumor growth inhibition (Fig. 1A). This finding lays the foundation for further exploring the mechanisms behind variable responses in clinical settings. Notably, single-cell RNA sequencing revealed that anti-PD-L1 and anti-IL-8 cooperatively reshape the tumor microenvironment, with an increased T cell infiltration following combination therapy compared to control and atezolizumab groups (Fig. 2E, F). Furthermore, T cell subcluster analysis revealed that combination therapy promoted expansion of effector/cytotoxic T-cell populations (Fig. 2H, J), which was validated by clinical data showing IL-8 low patients had higher proportions of effector T cells and lower exhausted T cell subsets compared to IL-8 high patients (Fig. 4D, E). Although recent studies indicate IL-8 upregulation following PD-1 blockade contributes to adaptive resistance through the recruitment of immunosuppressive myeloid cells, our models did not detect significant levels of MDSCs in the tumor microenvironment. This unexpected finding suggests that IL-8 blockade may enhance tumor immunity through mechanisms that are independent of MDSC recruitment, highlighting the need to explore alternative pathways in our research.

Further analysis, including KEGG enrichment and differential gene expression profiling, reveals that the enhanced efficacy of combination therapy is mediated by concurrent anti-inflammatory, pro-immune, and anti-angiogenic effects. Notably, the identification of HIF-1 signaling as the most significantly enriched pathway following dual targeting is particularly intriguing. We hypothesize that this enhanced efficacy may also stem from the dual targeting of PD-L1 and IL-8, which effectively disrupts the HIF-1α pathway that regulates hypoxic responses and angiogenesis. By inhibiting IL-8, the bispecific antibody alleviates hypoxic conditions in the tumor microenvironment, downregulating HIF-1α and VEGF. This reduction in hypoxia fosters a more favorable environment for T cell activation. The observed reduction in pro-inflammatory cytokines, including CXCL8 and IL-6, alongside increased production, suggests a shift in the inflammatory environment from tumor-promoting to tumor-suppressive, enhancing effector T cell activation while reducing immunosuppressive signals.

Dual targeting of PD‑L1 and IL‑8 provides a clear combinatorial advantage. Neutralizing IL‑8 interrupts the IL‑8–CXCR1/2 amplification loop and limits recruitment and activation of myeloid cells as well as pro‑angiogenic programs. In parallel, PD‑L1 blockade restores T‑cell cytotoxicity and increases IFN‑γ, strengthening anti‑tumor immunity. These effects reinforce one another, since weaker IL‑8 signaling and fewer suppressive myeloid inputs allow better T‑cell function, and heightened T‑cell activity further shifts the tumor environment away from myeloid and angiogenic pathways. Consistent with this cooperative mechanism, our transcriptomic data show higher IFNG and T‑cell cytotoxic markers GZMK and CXCL9 together with lower angiogenesis‑related transcripts VEGFA and TGFBI relative to anti‑PD‑L1 and PBS (Fig. 3).

To address potential limitations regarding sample size in our preliminary studies and to provide clinical validation of our findings, we analyzed the GSE169246 dataset from TNBC patients receiving anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. This clinical single-cell RNA sequencing data provided critical validation for our initial observations, demonstrating that patients with low intratumoral IL-8 expression exhibited significantly enhanced infiltration of multiple immune effector populations, including T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells compared to IL-8 high patients. Importantly, this clinical dataset also revealed a dramatic reduction in MDSC populations in IL-8 low tumors, which was absent in our humanized mouse models, thereby providing complementary insights into IL-8-mediated immunosuppression mechanisms (Fig. 4B). While we acknowledge that public dataset analysis serves as surrogate validation rather than direct experimental confirmation, these clinical findings strongly support our mechanistic hypotheses and provide translational relevance for our therapeutic approach. The consistency between our preliminary single-cell sequencing results and clinical data underscores the robustness of IL-8 blockade in promoting immune activation across different experimental systems and validates the clinical potential of our dual-targeting strategy.

To support these conclusions, we designed BP2402 to target both PD-L1 and IL-8, utilizing the humanization and screening of an IL-8 nanobody integrated into our bispecific construct. In vitro characterization confirmed the ability of BP2402 to inhibit both PD-L1 and IL-8 pathways. In vivo efficacy studies showed that BP2402 outperformed monotherapies and slightly surpassed the combination of the two monoclonal antibodies, implying a synergistic effect from concurrent pathway targeting.

Interestingly, although BP2402 showed reduced efficacy against IL-8 signaling in vitro compared to the HuMax-IL-8 monoclonal antibody, it achieved significant antitumor effects in vivo. This may indicate that the bispecific antibody's structural design enhances its concentration in tumor tissue through PD-L1 targeting, further inhibiting IL-8 expression and promoting a favorable tumor microenvironment for enhanced therapeutic efficacy, highlighting its potential as a more effective therapeutic strategy for overcoming resistance in tumor immunotherapy.

Additionally, due to the challenges in selecting HLA-matched donors during model establishment, human Donor 3 PBMCs have only a partial HLA match, while Donor 4 PBMCs do not match the HLA of the MDA-MB-231 cells. This limitation indicates that our in vivo results involve contributions from HLA-independent T cell pathways, represented as T-cell receptor independent responses. This means that dual-target inhibition enhances both HLA-dependent and HLA-independent T cell activity by modulating the tumor microenvironment. While we acknowledge the presence of HLA-independent contributions in our findings, we have accounted for these effects in our comparisons with the control group. Overall, our bispecific antibody amplifies T cell functions, reinforcing its therapeutic efficacy. Although the influence of HLA-independent pathways is significant, it does not diminish our conclusions regarding the essential role of BP2402 in TNBC therapy.

Based on our previous results, we undertook validation studies to elucidate the mechanisms underlying the efficacy of the bispecific antibody BP2402. We conducted experiments and analyses including the assessment of T cell exhaustion markers (PD-1, TIM-3), VEGF expression suppression, and modulation of the JAK1/STAT1 signaling pathway. Our findings demonstrated that BP2402 effectively reduced T cell exhaustion markers while maintaining CD28 expression, indicating its capability to prevent T cell dysfunction—a common limitation of prolonged PD-L1 blockade. Furthermore, the pronounced suppression of VEGF expression by BP2402 exceeded the effects observed with other anti-angiogenic therapies, likely facilitating tumor vasculature normalization and enhancing drug delivery and T cell infiltration.

Future investigations will focus on evaluating BP2402 in other cancer types associated with IL-8, such as non-small cell lung cancer and melanoma. Additionally, optimizing treatment schedules and exploring combinations with chemotherapy or radiotherapy are essential. We will also enhance the efficacy of BP2402 by screening for new antibodies and utilizing scFv technology to improve the activity of anti-IL-8 and enhance the binding affinity to PD-L1, thereby increasing its localization within the tumor microenvironment. Furthermore, we plan to identify and utilize more appropriate models to validate the efficacy of our bispecific antibody. Currently, our experiments are conducted using immunodeficient mice reconstituted with PBMCs, which lack MDSCs. By employing new models that include MDSCs, we believe that our bispecific antibody may provide better therapeutic effects through the suppression of MDSCs.

Conclusion

In conclusion, dual targeting of PD-L1 and IL-8 with BP2402 represents a promising strategy to improve cancer immunotherapy. Our findings demonstrate its superior antitumor activity compared to individual antibody combinations by concurrently modulating immune checkpoints, inflammatory signaling, and angiogenesis, effectively addressing resistance mechanisms and creating a more favorable environment for effective immune-mediated tumor control. These insights provide a strong rationale for the clinical development of BP2402 as a novel immunotherapeutic agent.
