# A signature of enhanced proliferation associated with response and survival to anti-PD-L1 therapy in early-stage non-small cell lung cancer.

## Abstract

In early-stage non-small cell lung cancer, the combination of neoadjuvant anti-PD-L1 and subablative stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) is associated with higher rates of major pathologic response compared to anti-PD-L1 alone. Here, we identify a 140-gene set, enriched in genes characteristic of highly proliferating cells, associated with response to the dual therapy. Analysis of on-treatment transcriptome data indicate roles for T and B cells in response. The 140-gene set is associated with disease-free survival when applied to the combined trial arms. This 140-gene set identifies a subclass of tumors in all 7 of The Cancer Genome Atlas tumor types examined. Worse survival is associated with the 140-gene signature in 5 of these tumor types. Collectively, our data support that this 140-gene set, discovered in association with response to combined anti-PD-L1 and SBRT, identifies a clinically aggressive subclass of solid tumors that may be more likely to respond to immunotherapies.

Introduction

Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapies have transformed the management of solid tumors, although these successes are tempered by the low number (∼20%) of patients achieving durable response with ICB monotherapy. Many variables likely contribute to ICB response and resistance, including tumor type and stage, cancer cell intrinsic mechanisms (e.g., oncogene driver, antigen presentation, mutational burden, immune checkpoint expression), tumor microenvironment factors (e.g., immune landscape), and individual intrinsic factors (e.g., metabolic health).

We recently reported data from a Phase II trial of stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) as an immunomodulator to enhance anti-programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) ICB in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Patients with stages I–IIIA NSCLC were randomly assigned to either two three-weekly cycles of neoadjuvant anti-PD-L1 or the same ICB therapy combined with a subablative (clinically noncurative) dose of SBRT (8 Gy × 3) delivered with the first anti-PD-L1 treatment (Figure S1A). The trial was based on the hypothesis that this radiation dose would have immune-enhancing effects that synergize with the ICB. The primary endpoint of the trial was major pathologic response (MPR) in the resected tumor, defined as a residual viable tumor of ≤10% of the tumor bed. MPR was achieved in 6.66% (2 of 30 subjects) in the monotherapy anti-PD-L1 arm (arm 1) and in 53.3% (16 of 30 subjects) in the dual therapy anti-PD-L1 and SBRT arm (arm 2). Responses in arm 2 remained significantly higher than in arm 1, even after adjustment for baseline PD-L1 expression. These data suggest that a subablative dose of 8 Gy × 3 SBRT enhances antitumor immune response following neoadjuvant anti-PD-L1 therapy. Pretreatment (n = 32, 16 per arm) and posttreatment (n = 46, 22 arm 1 and 24 arm 2) tumor tissue samples were available for studies to identify biological correlates of response and resistance. Here, we report on the results of those analyses.

Results

Response to combination ICB and SBRT therapy was associated with a gene expression profile of increased proliferation

To discover NSCLC tumor features associated with response to neoadjuvant combination anti-PD-L1 (durvalumab) and SBRT therapy (trial arm 2), we interrogated RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data from 16 pretreatment tumors: 10 that achieved MPR and 6 that did not (Figure S1B; Table S1). There was no significant difference in the time from the last neoadjuvant therapy to surgery between tumors with and without response, eliminating it as a contributing variable to differences in MPR between the groups (Figure 1A). Although patients were randomly assigned to the treatment arms, the percentage of PD-L1 cancer cells was higher in arm 2. Of the arm 2 cases with pretreatment RNA-seq data, all 10 of the responding tumors had ≥1% PD-L1+ cancer cells, whereas only 1 of 6 nonresponding tumors had ≥1% PD-L1+ cancer cells (Figure 1B). In NSCLC, PD-L1 expression by cancer cells is, by itself, an imperfect biomarker of response to ICB. Consequently, to identify additional tumor features associated with response to the combination therapy, we compared pretreatment transcriptional profiles of tumors in the combination therapy arm that achieved MPR (n = 10) to those that did not (n = 6). The gene expression profile of tumors achieving MPR showed significant upregulation of 135 genes and downregulation of 5 genes (false discovery rate [FDR] ≤ 0.05, Benjamini-Hochberg [BH] correction) (Figures 1C and S1C; Table S2).

Approximately 70% of the genes upregulated in the MPR tumor group were annotated to a cell-cycle Gene Ontology group (GO: 0007049), suggesting enhanced cell proliferation as a pretreatment characteristic of response to combination therapy. A proliferation index (PI), calculated using a proliferating cell nuclear antigen-associated gene expression gene set (Table S3), supported elevated proliferation in the MPR tumor group (Figure 1D). Because 40 of the 113 genes used to generate the PI are part of the 140 differentially expressed genes, we recalculated a modified PI excluding those 40 genes (Table S3). The modified PI's of the MPR tumor group were increased relative to the no MPR group, further supporting the increased PI's of the MPR tumor group (Figure 1D). The tumor cellularity of pretreatment biopsies was higher in the MPR group, supporting the hypothesis that cancer cells contributed to the increased gene expression proliferation signal (Figure 1E). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) revealed significant upregulation (FDR <0.05) in the MPR tumor group of pathways associated with proliferation, including although not limited to mitotic spindle, G2M checkpoint, DNA repair, mammalian target of rapamycin and Myc signaling and unfolded protein response (Figure 1F). In addition, metabolic and signaling pathways related to rapid proliferation are enriched in the MPR group, although they did not achieve significance at FDR < 0.05.

To query whether potential differences between tumor groups with and without MPR are associated with differences in the tumor microenvironment, we used xCell deconvolution of the bulk RNA-seq data to explore the tumor immune landscape. Of the 35 immune cell types imputed from this analysis, the only significant difference was an increased abundance of CD4+ Th2 cells (Figure 1G; Table S4).

We next clustered the pretreatment RNA-seq data of the 16 arm 1 tumors by the 140-gene set. The samples clustered into 2 groups of 8 tumors each, demonstrating that the high PI subgroup was also present in the pretreatment arm 1 tumors (Figure S2A). To increase the sample numbers for analyses, we clustered all of the pretreatment samples, 16 arm 1 and 16 arm 2, by the expressions of the 140 genes. The samples clustered into 2 groups: a high PI group (n = 18, 8 arm 1 and 10 arm 2, 56% of tumors) and a low PI (n = 14, 8 arm 1 and 6 arm 2, 44% of tumors) (Figure 2A; Table S1). Hereafter, we refer to these as the “high” and “low” PI groups. All of the tumors that achieved MPR, including the sole arm 1 tumor with MPR, were in the high PI cluster. The PI's (full gene set and modified gene set) and Ki67 mRNA (MKI67) expression were elevated in high PI tumors (Figures 2B–2D). The PI's and the expressions of Ki67 of the arm 1 tumors were indistinguishable from those of the arm 2 tumors within the same clusters. Gene signatures for cell-cycle phases were also significantly upregulated in the high PI group, suggesting an increase of cells in S, G2, and M phases of the cell cycle (Figure S2B). The differences in cell-cycle phases between tumors with and without MPR remain when restricting analyses to arm 2 tumors alone, more directly associating pretreatment differences in cell cycle with response to dual therapy (Figure S2C). Both pretreatment tumor cellularity and cancer cell Ki67 protein expressions (immunofluorescence of a subset of the samples, five from each PI cluster) were higher in the High PI tumor group compared to the low PI tumor group (Figures 2E and 2F). The percentage of PD-L1+ cancer cells (immunohistochemistry) and PD-L1 mRNA expression (CD274) were elevated in the high PI tumors compared to the low PI tumors (Figures 2G and 2H). PD-L1 gene expression was not one of the 140 genes used in the clustering because the difference was not significant when corrected for multiple testing.

We identified differentially expressed genes between the high and low PI clusters of all 32 pretreatment tumors (FDR ≤0.05, BH correction, ≥2-fold difference in expression): 408 genes upregulated and 433 downregulated in the high PI tumor group (Figure S2D; Table S5). As expected, many of the upregulated genes (39.7%) are annotated to biological pathways associated with cell proliferation. GO analysis also identified genes of the glycolytic process GO group (GO: 0006096) as significantly enriched among genes upregulated in the high PI tumor group (11-fold enrichment, FDR 1.3 × 10−4) (Figure 3A). GSEA also identified significant (FDR <0.05) upregulation of Hallmark cell proliferation pathways and glycolysis gene sets (Figure S2E). Supporting an increased glycolytic activity of the high PI tumors, pretreatment SUVmax (diagnostic fluorodeoxyglucose-positron emission tomography), a surrogate for glycolytic metabolism, was higher in the high PI tumor group (Figure 3B). These data suggest a hyperglycolytic phenotype as an additional characteristic of the high PI tumors, thereby associating pretreatment glycolytic metabolism with response to the dual therapy.

GO analysis was less informative on the impact of the 433 genes downregulated in high PI tumors compared to low PI tumors. The predominant biological pathways enriched in the downregulated genes were related to ion transport, including transmembrane transport (GO: 0055085, FDR = 1.9 × 10−3) and cation transport (GO: 0006812, FDR = 4.7 × 10−2). No Hallmark gene sets were significantly downregulated (FDR <0.05) in GSEA comparison of the high to low PI tumor groups.

Strikingly, no immune-related GO pathways were enriched among the differentially expressed genes (up or downregulated), nor were there differences in the expressions of the genes for inhibitory checkpoints (other than PD-L1), co-stimulatory receptors, or expressions of genes involved in major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC class I) antigen processing/presentation between the groups (Figures S2F and S2G). There were, however, reductions in MHC class II genes in high PI tumors. As a group, MHC class II genes were significantly downregulated in the high PI group (p < 0.0001, Kruskal-Wallis ANOVA), with reductions of four of the paralogs achieving significance when corrected for multiple testing (Figure 3C). Nonetheless, deconvolution did not reveal differences between the two tumor groups in dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, or B cells, the major MHC class II-expressing cell types (Figures S2H‒S2J; Table S6). T helper 2 (Th2) and Th1 CD4+ T cells were enriched in the high PI tumor group (Figures 3D and 3E; Table S6).

Multiplex immunofluorescence of five pretreatment samples from each PI cluster demonstrated no differences in the densities of CD3+ between the clusters (Figure 3F). Similarly, there was no difference in CD8+ T cell density between the PI clusters determined by immunofluorescence or deconvolution (Figures 3G and S2K). There were, however, differences in CD8+ subtypes detected by immunofluorescence, with increases in CD8+PD1+ and CD8+ granzyme B+ T cells in the high PI tumors, supporting a difference in the activation state of CD8+ T cells (Figures 3H and 3I). There was an increase of T regulatory cells (Tregs; FoxP3+ CD4+ cells) detected by immunofluorescence in the high PI tumors (Figure 3J) that was not identified by deconvolution (Table S6).

Pretreatment tumor mutational burdens (TMBs) were quantified for 8 low PI and 13 high PI tumors. TMBs were elevated in the high PI tumor group (Figure 3K). Previous studies have identified increases in TMB to be associated with response to monotherapy ICB, although it is appreciated that TMB is only one contributing factor to the response.

Comparisons of clinical covariates of the two PI clusters (14 low PI and 18 high PI) demonstrated that former and current smokers and adenocarcinoma and squamous cell subtypes were represented among high PI tumors (Figures 2A and S2L; Table S1). There was, however, an enrichment of adenocarcinoma subtype in the low PI cluster (p = 0.0067, two-sided Fisher’s exact test). There was one epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) mutant tumor among the 18 high PI tumors, whereas there were 7 EGFR mutant tumors among the 14 low PI group (p = 0.01, two-sided Fisher’s exact test).

Tumors with MPR are characterized by posttreatment enrichments of genes involved in B cell biology and tertiary lymphoid structures (TLSs)

To explore the effect of the combination treatment with pathology response, we compared the posttreatment (RNA-seq of resected tumors) transcriptional profiles of arm 2 tumors that achieved MPR (n = 14) to those that did not (n = 10) (Table S1). The posttreatment expressions of 105 genes were upregulated and 445 were downregulated in the MPR group (FDR <0.05, BH corrected, and fold change in expression ≥2; Table S7). B cell biology pathways were significantly enriched among the genes upregulated in the MPR tumors (Figure 4A). Deconvolution analysis supported significant enrichments of B cells and B cell subtypes in the MPR tumor group (Figures 4B‒4F; Table S8). Because B cells are frequently found in TLSs, we interrogated the posttreatment RNA-seq data for two different TLS gene signatures, a compendium Hallmark-TLS (7 genes) and a melanoma-derived TLS (9 genes) (Table S9). These analyses support a posttreatment difference in TLS between tumors with MPR compared to those without MPR, with the melanoma score achieving significance (p = 0.0005) and the Hallmark score trending (p = 0.0841) (Figure 4G). Pretreatment TLS scores were not associated with response to the dual therapy (Figure S3A). Supporting a role for T cells in an effective antitumor immune response in the MPR group, deconvolution analyses demonstrated posttreatment increases in a number of immune cells, including CD8 cell subtypes (Table S8). Biological pathways enriched among the 445 genes downregulated, posttreatment, on arm 2 tumors with MPR included the regulation of wound healing, cell migration, epithelium development, and cell adhesion (Figure S3B).

MPR in arm 2 is associated with posttreatment antitumor immune response

To further explore the interaction between dual therapy and MPR, we contrasted posttreatment with pretreatment transcriptomes of arm 2 tumors that achieved MPR (n = 10 pre- and n = 14 posttreatment) (Table S1). There were 746 genes upregulated and 845 genes downregulated in posttreatment relative to pretreatment (FDR ≤0.05, BH correction, and ≥ 2-fold difference in expression) (Table S10). Immune-related biological pathways dominated the pathways enriched among the genes upregulated in the MPR group (Figure 4H). In addition, pathways of tissue repair (e.g., migration, signaling) are enriched among the upregulated genes. The pretreatment 140-gene expression signature associated with MPR was reversed posttreatment, whereas there were no pre- to posttreatment differences in the expressions of the 140 genes for the arm 2 tumors that did not achieve MPR (Figure S3C). In addition, expressions of MHC class II genes were increased and expressions of glycolytic pathway genes decreased posttreatment relative to pretreatment for the tumors that achieved MPR but unchanged in arm 2 tumors that did not achieve MPR (Figures S3D and S3E). Differential pre- to post- gene expression comparing matched arm 2 tumors with MPR (n = 10) identified 217 genes upregulated and 334 genes downregulated posttreatment (FDR ≤0.05, BH correction, and ≥2-fold difference in expression) (Table S11). Of the upregulated genes, 206 are in the analyses of the nonmatched samples and of the downregulated genes, and 333 are in the analyses of the nonmatched samples.

Deconvolution of the posttreatment RNA-seq data revealed an increase in CD8+ T cells, DCs, and M1 and M2 macrophages—all changes supporting increased antitumor immunity in the responding tumors (Figures 4I‒4K; Table S12). The increases in DCs and macrophages, two major immune cell types expressing MHC class II, are in line with the increases in MHC class II genes.

There were no significant differences in gene expression (FDR ≤0.05, BH correction, and ≥ 2-fold difference in expression) between pre- and posttreatment for arm 2 tumors that did not achieve MPR, nor were there pre- to posttreatment differences in immune cell composition (deconvolution) for arm 2 tumors that did not achieve MPR (Table S13). There were no pre- to posttreatment gene expression differences (FDR ≤0.05, BH correction, and ≥ 2-fold difference in expression) for arm 1 tumors that did not achieve MPR.

The 140-gene set identifies two molecular subclasses of NSCLC tumors

The 140-gene set classified pretreatment tumors from the trial into two molecular subtypes (Figure 2). To validate these results, we separately clustered The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) NSCLC adenocarcinoma and squamous samples by expressions of these 140 genes. Of the 455 primary TCGA lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) samples, 407 (89.4%) were clustered into 2 subclasses by the 140-gene set (Figures 5A and S4A). There were 196 LUAD samples in the low PI group (43.1% of TCGA LUADs) and 211 in the high PI group (46.4% of TCGA LUADs). The LUAD high PI group was also characterized by reductions in MHC class II genes and increases in glycolysis genes, similar to the differences between our clinical trial clusters (Figures S4B and S4C). Since both MHC class II genes and glycolysis genes were not a part of the 140-gene set used to cluster the tumors, we concluded that the observed patterns of expression for these genes was a distinctive feature of the LUAD high PI group. There were no differences between LUAD low and high PI groups in expressions of MHC class I genes, nor did the gene expression profile support robust differences in antigen processing and presentation (Figure S4D). There were, however, increases in the expressions of some immunomodulatory genes, prominently PD-L1, PD1, LAG3, and IDO1 (Figure S4E).

Immune deconvolution revealed significant increases in Th1 and Th2 CD4+ cells in the LUAD high PI group (Figures S4F and S4G), extending the molecular similarities between our trial groups and the LUAD groups. Similar to the high and low PI subclasses in our trial data, there were no differences in the enrichments of macrophages or B cells between the LUAD groups (Figures S4H and S4I). However, there was a reduction in DCs in the LUAD high PI group that was not seen in our trial samples (Figure S4J). TMB was higher in the LUAD high PI group, as was the case for our trial groups (Figure S4K). The overall similarities in LUAD high PI tumors and our trial high PI tumors reenforces our proposal that increased proliferation, glycolysis, PD-L1 expression, TMB, Th2 CD4+ cells, and reduced MHC class II expression are characteristics of this molecular subclass of NSCLC.

There was an enrichment of EGFR mutant tumors in the low PI group of TCGA LUAD tumors, similar to our trial samples: 19% of the low PI TCGA LUAD tumor group and 9% of the high PI TCGA LUAD tumor group had mutations in EGFR (Figure S4L). There was an enrichment of p53 mutations in the high PI tumor group of TCGA LUAD, 26% in the low PI tumor group, and 68% in the high PI tumor group. Loss of p53 function is associated with increased glycolysis, a feature of the high PI tumor group.

Unlike the TCGA LUAD samples, in which 89.0% of tumors were clustered, only 67.8% (331 of 488) of TCGA primary lung squamous cancers (LUSCs) were clustered into 2 groups (Figures 5B and S5A). In the LUSC tumors, 198 tumors (40.5% of total TCGA LUSCs) clustered in the high PI group and 132 (27.0% of total TCGA LUSCs) in the low PI group. Thus, a significantly greater percentage of LUSC tumors (p = 0.0306, Fisher’s exact test) were in the high PI group as compared to the distribution of LUAD tumors between the two subclasses, perhaps reflecting fundamental differences between these two histotypes of NSCLC.

As was the case for the LUAD clusters, there were reduced expressions of MHC class II genes, increased PD-L1 expression, and increased Th2 cells in the high PI tumor group (Figures S5B‒S5D). In addition, there were decreases in macrophages and DCs in the LUSC high PI tumors (Figures S5E and S5F). TMB was also increased in the LUSC high PI tumors, although to a smaller degree than in the LUAD tumors (Figure S5G). There were, however, some notable differences between the LUAD and LUSC tumor groups. In the high PI LUSC tumor group, there were reductions in MHC class I presentation genes (although of small magnitudes), no differences in the expressions of glycolysis genes, and no enrichment of EGFR mutations in the LUSC low PI tumor group (Figures S5H‒S5J).

Association of the 140-gene set with survival

In the TCGA LUAD samples, high PI tumors were associated with reduced disease-free survival (DFS) and reduced overall survival (OS), demonstrating that a worse outcome is associated with the high PI 140-gene signature (Figures 5C and 5D). We next generated a gene enrichment score of the 140 genes for each of the TCGA LUAD tumors. In a multivariate Cox proportional hazards model, higher expression of the 140-gene set score was significantly associated with worse survival (hazard ratio [HR] = 1.60, p = 3.04 × 10−6), after adjusting for EGFR mutation status, TMB, PD-L1, and PD1 expression, tumor stage, patient’s age, and gender (Figure 5E). Thus, although TMB, cancer cell PD-L1 expression, and frequency of EGFR mutations differed between the high and low PI clusters, the 140-gene signature captures biology associated with survival that is independent of those tumor features. In the Cox regression model, tumor stage (II and III stages referenced to stage I) was also associated with increased risk.

There were no differences in DFS or OS between the LUSC tumor groups (Figures 5F and 5G). In a multivariate Cox proportional hazards model, only stage III and age at diagnosis in LUSC were associated with increased risk (Figure 5H). Although the 140-gene signature robustly clustered the TCGA LUSC tumors into two groups, the value of the 140-gene score of the low PI cluster of the LUSC tumor group was higher than that of the LUAD low PI group—on par with the LUAD high PI group—which may account for the gene signature clusters of LUSC not differing in survival (Figure 5I).

High PI subclasses in the solid tumors

We next used the 140-gene signature to cluster TCGA RNA-seq data of five different solid tumor types (Figures 6A‒6E and S6). All five tumor types were robustly clustered into two groups. The distributions of tumors between the high and low PI groups varied among the tumor types, with prostate having the fewest high-proliferation tumors (24%) and melanoma the most (67%). The high-proliferation phenotype was associated with reduced DFS in melanoma, breast, prostate, and pancreas (Figures 6F–6I). However, in colon cancer, the high proliferation signature was not associated with survival.

Association of proliferation signature with DFS

The 140-gene set was derived by contrasting gene expression profiles of arm 2 tumors with or without MPR (measured within a few weeks of the therapy at the time of resection). We next determined whether there were differences in DFS between patients in low and high PI clusters when combining the trial arms (low PI cluster, n = 14 and high PI cluster, n = 18). DFS was significantly higher in patients who had the high PI signature (p < 0.0244, Kaplan-Meier log-rank test) (Figure 7A). However, this comparison of DFS between patients with high and low PI signatures in which subjects from both trial arms are grouped may be biased by the higher proportion of patients with MPR in the high PI group (i.e., those from arm 2). Therefore, we repeated the analysis of DFS in each treatment arm separately. The high PI signature trended with better DFS regardless of treatment assignment, although in neither case did the difference achieve statistical significance, likely due to the small sample numbers (arm 1: n = 16, p = 0.1203 and arm 2: n = 16, p = 0.1792) (Figures 7B and 7C). Thus, although the 140-gene set was established based on MPR within arm 2, these data suggest an association with DFS that is independent of treatment arm, in which case the 140-gene set may be predictive of improved DFS to anti-PD-L1-based therapy.

Discussion

Our transcriptomic analysis identified a pretreatment molecular subclass of early-stage NSCLC. Our trial results suggest that this molecularly defined subclass is predictive of response to ICB with or without combination SBRT therapy. This subclass is characterized by the differential expression of 140 genes, dominated by the increased expressions of cell-cycle genes. Elevated Ki67 protein expression supports increased cancer cell proliferation in this subclass. Other features of this molecular subclass are consistent with increased proliferation: (1) increased expression of glycolysis genes and increased SUVmax, both of which are correlates of increased glycolytic activity. Increased glycolysis is appreciated as a hallmark of rapid cell proliferation. (2) TMB is elevated in the subclass. TMB has been positively associated with the cell cycle and a positive correlation between proliferation and TMB has been reported in breast cancer and melanoma. (3) In this subclass, features of the tumor microenvironment are biased toward immune suppression, including increased cancer cell PD-L1 expression, reduced MHC class II expression, and increased abundance of Tregs. MHC class II expression is highest in the professional antigen-presenting cell types of DCs, macrophages, and B cells, and therefore the reduction in tumor MHC class II expression may reflect a mechanism for the high PI tumors to evade immune surveillance. Increased CD4 Th2 cells are also a characteristic of this subclass. These cells, mediators of type 2 immunity, are best described for their roles in response to helminths and in wound healing, although there is emerging evidence for a role of these cells in cancer.

We can only speculate as to why the high PI tumors escape immunosurveillance differently than do low PI tumors. The survival data for the TCGA tumors, in which the high PI subclass of 5 of 7 solid tumors have worse DFS, support the high PI tumors as more clinically aggressive, which may reflect more robust immune escape. Analyses of the high PI tumors subclass from our trial suggest that tumor infiltration by T cells does not distinguish the low and high PI tumor groups, as would be the case if the high proliferation tumors were “immunologically cold” tumors. There are increases in CD8+PD1+ and Tregs (CD4+FoxP3+) in the high PI tumor group relative to the low PI tumor group, suggesting a history of, or ongoing, antitumor immune response (increased CD8+PD1+ and CD8+GZB+ cells) as well as Treg-mediated suppression of the antitumor immune response in the high PI tumors. In addition, the transcriptome analyses (reduced MHC class II gene expression and xCell deconvolution) support reduced macrophages and DCs in the high PI tumor group relative to the low PI tumor group, which may contribute to immune escape in the high PI tumors. Perhaps high PI tumors are at a dynamic and liable stage of immune escape, potentially making them more responsive to therapies that enhance a fledgling antitumor immune response.

Radiation enhancement of ICB therapy

Radiation has a number of possible immunomodulatory effects that can synergize with ICB, including the cancer cell-intrinsic effects of increased release of tumor antigens, induction of damage-associated molecular patterns, induction of immunologic cell death that promotes release of type I interferon, and effects on the tumor stromal components such as activation of antigen-presenting cells and the recruitment of T cells into the tumor bed. Based on MPR as a metric of response, our data suggest that the effects of radiation that augment the response to anti-PD-L1 therapy are linked to the proliferative status of the cells. The positive association of SBRT-mediated augmentation of ICB response with proliferation of the cancer cells agrees with in vitro studies demonstrating that more rapidly proliferating cells have an increased sensitivity to the effects of lower doses of radiation, with cells in G2-M being the most sensitive. More rapidly dividing cells may be more subject to the DNA-damaging effects of radiation because of the increased sensitivity of single-stranded DNA to radiation damage and to damage by oxidative products induced by radiation.

The radiation dose used in our trial was, by clinical standards, a noncurative dose of radiation and therefore would not be anticipated to be tumor ablative. However, we cannot rule out the possibility that the radiation regimen on its own induces limited cancer cell killing in high PI tumors because of the increased susceptibility of more rapidly proliferating cells to radiation. Nonetheless, cell death by radiation damage is known to cause immunogenic death, which in turn will enhance an antitumor immune response. Hence, the greater susceptibility of the high PI tumors to radiation could result in increased immunogenic cell death relative to low PI tumors and a corresponding enhancement of local antitumor immune response resulting in the increased MPR of high PI tumors groups. Even though the present study cannot provide a mechanistic understanding of the effect of the radiation therapy on ICB response, our data demonstrate that higher cancer cell proliferation is a tumor feature associated with an enhanced response to combination subablative SBRT and anti-PD-L1 therapy.

The posttreatment analyses of tumors achieving MPR in response to ICB radiation combination therapy revealed an induction of adaptive and humoral immune responses. These findings extend previous studies documenting a stimulation of adaptive T cell-mediated immune responses associated with pathologic responses for neoadjuvant ICB and neoadjuvant chemoimmunotherapy in NSCLC. Moreover, in our trial, MPR was associated with a posttreatment transcriptional profile supporting a robust B cell-mediated immune response. In line with a posttreatment increase in B cells in tumors with MPR, Th2 CD4+ T cells, which secrete cytokines promoting B cell development), were increased in the MPR tumor group. The associations of B cells and TLS in antitumor immunity and for response to ICB have been reported for multiple tumor types, including NSCLC. Our findings on posttreatment immune responses associated with MPR are based on the analyses of responders and nonresponders from the radiation IBC arm 2. Only two subjects from the ICB arm 1 achieved MPR. Therefore, we unfortunately do not have sufficient data to explore potential differences between the treatment arms in immune responses associated with MPR.

One immunomodulatory effect of radiation is through cyclic guanosine monophosphate-AMP synthase-stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS/STING), an antiviral cytosolic DNA sensing pathway leading to an activation of an interferon I gene transcription program. Despite response to the combination therapy, we did not detect an interferon transcriptional program in posttreatment arm 2 MPR tumors, which may reflect the effect of radiation, or any signature that would support increased immunogenic cell death in the MPR tumor group. Posttreatment analyses were performed on tumor tissue secured at the time of surgical resection, which was 2–3 weeks after the neoadjuvant therapy. We speculate that direct therapy-induced changes (e.g., cGAS/STING activation), underlying an effective antitumor immune response, were resolved by the time of resection, with only the downstream effects of the immune response evident at the time of resection.

The 140-gene set is associated with response to and survival following anti-PD-L1 therapy

The 140-gene set was discovered by exploring gene expression programs associated with the SBRT enhancement of PD-L1 ICB based on pathology response, a metric of early antitumor response because it is determined within a few weeks (15.5 days median) of the neoadjuvant therapy. The 140-gene set was also associated with improved DFS, which may have been expected based on the recently documented association between MPR and improved DFS in NSCLC. However, we also found that improvement in DFS in patients with the high PI was independent of MPR because it was also associated with improved DFS in patients in arm 1, only one of which had MPR. Thus, our findings suggest that the constellation of biological features identifying subclass tumors as more proliferative is potentially predictive of MPR after anti-PD-L1 plus SBRT dual therapy as well as an improved DFS in response to anti-PD-L1 monotherapy. The monotherapy anti-PD-L1 may be particularly effective in shifting the balance toward antitumor immunity in high PI tumors, inducing a systemic immune response sufficient to reduce distal disease and improved survival even in the absence of MPR.

The 140-gene set identifies a tumor subclass in five other solid tumor types examined

We validated our discovery of this molecular subclass in five additional TCGA solid tumor types. This subclass is not rare, ranging from about one-fourth to two-thirds, varying by tumor type, of the seven tumor types examined. The high PI molecular subclass was associated with worse DFS in five of the tumor types (LUAD, melanoma, breast, prostate, and pancreas), demonstrating the prognostic value of the 140-gene set to identify a more proliferative, and therefore potentially more virulent, tumor subclass. Multivariable regression analysis of DFS in TCGA LUAD samples confirmed the prognostic value of the 140-gene set independent of other features commonly linked to survival, including TMB, EGFR mutation status, and PD-L1 gene expression. We interpret the two tumor types in which worse DFS was not associated with the subclass, LUSC, and colon, as an indication that biological features not captured by the 140-gene signature are more determinative of survival in those tumors.

Others have found a correlation between proliferation and survival. For example, the PI index used here has been negatively associated with survival in 7 of the 19 TCGA cancers examined, including LUAD. One contribution of our study is that we propose high PI as one characteristic of a molecular tumor subclass that is broadly associated with worse survival in untreated individuals and potentially associated with improved survival after anti-PD-L1 therapy.

Biomarkers of ICB response

Proliferation has also been associated with response to ICB. In one recent analysis, cell cycle and DNA repair genes were associated with an improved response to anti-PD-L1 in locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC and in metastatic urothelial carcinoma. In another study of stage IV NSCLC patients, tumors were segregated into tertiles of proliferation based on the expressions of 10 genes. Patients in the middle proliferation tertile had improved survival to ICB when compared to those in the pooled lowest and highest tertiles. Our study links molecular determinants of highly proliferative tumors to response and survival after PD-L1 blockade.

Accumulating data suggest that biomarkers may be specific to stage and the specifics of ICB therapy. A recent study identified aneuploidy as a biomarker of response to combination ablative radiotherapy and ICB (ipilimumab plus nivolumab) in metastatic NSCLC. Proliferation was not reported to be a biomarker of response in that trial. This discordance is likely due to differences in tumor stage and or the specifics of the therapy. We did not have sufficient exome sequencing data to assess aneuploidy in our cohort.

Advances in profiling methods have led to improvements in molecular subtyping organ-specific cancers beyond traditional histology classifications and driver mutations. In one such effort, six immune landscape subtypes were identified across 30 nonhematologic cancer types using immuno-oncologic gene signatures to cluster the tumors into six immune subtypes. That study captured tumor/stroma differences among the subclasses, including subclass-associated macrophage and lymphocytes signatures, genome stability, intratumor heterogeneity, and proliferation. In our study, we identified a set of differentially expressed genes between MPR and no MPR tumors, uncovering differences in proliferation between the responders and nonresponders in the absence of a prespecified hypothesis regarding the biology associated with response. We then leveraged those results to identify a high PI subclass in seven solid tumor types examined, based on TCGA gene expression data. We propose that this constellation of molecular features are interrelated to the elevated proliferation and enhanced response to ICB. Although our analyses are a more limited subclassification than those of Thorsson et al. (two versus six), we believe that the results of the two studies are complementary, together highlighting the contributions of molecular and cellular heterogeneity among tumors as a consideration in response to therapy.

Limitations of the study

The small sample numbers, restricting the statistical power of some comparisons, is a major limitation of this study. In addition, the trial used a radiation dose lower than the standard clinical ablative dose. The lower dose was chosen as an immunomodulatory dose rather than an ablative dose. Because of this difference, the conclusions of our study cannot be validated against published studies using the higher dose of radiation. With those constraints in mind, validation of our findings requires a larger trial testing the same treatment regimen. Another practical constraint is the amount of pretreatment tissue available for study. Because pretreatment samples were limited to pretreatment biopsies, we were mostly limited to RNA-seq transcriptome characterizations, although for a small number of samples we were able to provide some correlative microscopy studies. This limitation in tissue for correlative studies ultimately constrained the depth of the analyses. With what we have learned from this study, we can design correlative studies of future trials to test our hypothesis that high PI tumors have a better pathology response to the combination therapy and an improved DFS in response to anti-PD-L1 monotherapy. Finally, our findings are necessarily correlative in nature, associating phenotypes, inferred from gene expression profiles, with outcomes. The study was designed to develop rather than test hypotheses for the anticipated difference in response to the mono- and dual therapies of the clinical trial.

Experimental model and study participant details

Human subjects

Biopsy tissue samples and tissue from resected tumors for this study were obtained from patients participating in a clinical trial approved by the New York-Presbyterian-Weill Cornell Medicine institutional review board and patient consent was obtained for biospecimen collection and analysis. Participant profile information is provided in Table S1.

Method details

PD-L1 expression

The immunohistochemistry for PD-L1 was performed with the Ventana PD-L1 (SP263) FDA approved platform that uses clone SP263 on a Ventana Benchmark instrument (Ventana Medical Systems Inc, Tucson AZ). All tumor samples were analyzed using tumor proportion score of the percentage of tumor cells showing any intensity of membranous staining, with a minimum of 100 cells needed for analysis.

Pathologic assessment

All resection specimens were reviewed and scored by an expert pulmonary pathologist (ACB) who was blinded to treatment assignment. The measurement of tumor percentage was performed as previously described. In brief, the tumor bed was measured macroscopically and tumors under 3.0 cm submitted in toto. For tumors over 3.0 cm, a full cross section of tumor was submitted using a grid to produce sections of tumor bed of similar size to assist in the estimation of residual tumor percentage, in addition to standard sections. Elements of the tumor bed only as determined by macroscopic and microscopic correlation were scored (tumor, fibrosis, necrosis) and tumor percentage (out of 100%) reported. MPR was defined as the presence of 10% or fewer viable tumor cells in the primary tumor, as per current College of American Pathologists synoptic reporting. Residual tumor percentage was calculated in all cases (0–90%).

Tumor cellularity

A Hematoxylin and eosin stain slide was obtained after unstained sections were cut for molecular studies and the tissue dimensions compared to the prior unstained slides. Tumor containing areas were assessed for the relative area of tumor cells from non-tumor cells using the method of Dudley et al. with the modification of using 10% intervals. These tumor rich areas were marked on the unstained slides for manual microdissection of material slated for nucleic acid extraction.

RNA-seq

RNA was extracted from FFPE samples with RNeasy FFPE kit from Qiagen, Part# 73504. Tissue from tumor adjacent lung were used as controls. NEBNext Ultra II Directional RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB #E7760, New England BioLabs, Ipswich, MA) was used for sample library preparation according to the manufacturer’s instruction. PCR enrichment of adaptor ligated cDNAs were pooled and hybridized with probes from Twist Exome Kit #2 (Twist Bioscience, San Francisco, CA). After post capture PCR amplification and purification, the quality of final libraries was checked and loaded to Illumina NovaSeq6000 for sequencing at PE2x100 cycles. The raw sequencing reads in BCL format were processed through bcl2fastq 2.19 (Illumina) for FASTQ conversion and demultiplexing.

All reads were independently aligned with STAR_2.4.0f1 for sequence alignment against the human genome build hg19, downloaded via the UCSC genome browser, and SAMTOOLS v0.1.19 for sorting and indexing reads. HTSeq was used to quantity counts. Cufflinks (2.0.2) was used to get the expression values (FPKMs), and Gencode v19 GTF file for annotation. Differential gene expression was determined using Benjamini-Hochberg correction at FDR ≤0.05 and an expression difference of 2-fold or greater. Gene ontology pathway enrichment analyses were performed using Panther online software. Gene set scores (signatures) were constructed as the sum of the z-scores of the individual genes of the gene set. Cell compositions of the nodules were investigated by deconvolution of the RNA-seq data. Two methods, xCell and CIBERSORT were used.

TCGA data acquisition

The RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) data processed using the established Genomic Data Commons (GDC) guidelines as gene counts and FPKMs (Fragments Per Kilobase of transcript per Million mapped reads) were obtained from the GDC Data Portal (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov). These data used the human reference genome GRCh38 (https://gdc.cancer.gov/about-data/gdc-data-harmonization). The corresponding clinical traits data (including OS and Progression free survival PFS) was obtained from a previous publication. Mutation data processed according to the Multi Center Mutation Calling in Multiple Cancers (MC3) pipeline was also downloaded from the GDC data portal.

140-Gene set cluster analysis in TCGA

The cluster analysis to identify Low and High PI sample groups within each TCGA cancer type were performed using ConsensusClusterPlus supervised using the 140-gene signature. Prior to clustering, signature genes with zero variance across all samples were excluded and the sample were restricted to primary tumors. The data was median centered and clustered using pam, k-means, and hierarchal clustering methods (with 1,000 random selections, 80% re-sampling). Samples classified into the same cluster by all three methods were retained. A silhouette analysis was performed using the cluster package in R, and samples identified as being misclassified in this analysis were removed from further analysis. These steps led to the identification of two robust clusters of samples with Low and High PI characteristics. Differentially expressed genes (DEGs) were identified between the clusters using DESeq2 (with apeglm shrinkage) applied to gene counts for protein coding genes. Significant pathways associate with the ranked DEGs were identified using the MSigDB pathway database in fgsea R package (FDR q < 0.25). Cell type deconvolution was done in xCell using FPKM expression profiles. The data was visualized as heatmaps with ComplexHeatmap in R. Survival analysis was performed using the Cox Proportional-Hazards (Cox-PH) models and results were visualized as Kaplan-Meier curves or forest plots.

Multiplex imaging

Immunofluorescence imaging of a subset of pretherapy biopsy samples (four Arm1 and six Arm2) was performed using the Neogenomics platform as previously described. Briefly, formalin-fixed tissue slides were baked at 65°C for 1 h. Slides were deparaffinized with xylene, rehydrated by decreasing ethanol concentration washes, and then processed for antigen retrieval. A two-step antigen retrieval was adopted to allow antibodies with different antigen retrieval conditions to be used together on the same samples. Samples were blocked against nonspecific binding with 10% (w/v) donkey serum and 3% (w/v) bovine serum albumin (BSA) in phosphate-buffered solution (PBS) for 1 h at room temperature and stained with DAPI for 15 min. Directly conjugated primary antibodies were diluted in PBS supplied with 3% (w/v) BSA to optimized concentrations and applied for 1 h at room temperature on a Leica Bond III Stainer. In the case of primary-secondary antibody staining, samples were incubated with primary antibody, followed by incubation with species-specific secondary antibodies conjugated to either Cyanine 3 (Cy3) or Cyanine 5 (cy5). Visualized proteins are listed in Table S14.

Stained images were collected on INCell analyzer 2200 microscope (GE Healthcare Life Sciences) equipped with high efficiency fluorochrome specific filter sets for DAPI, cy3 and cy5. For multiplexed staining where co-localization was desired, the regions of interest (∼0.4–0.6 mm2 tissue area) were imaged, and stage coordinates were saved. The coordinates of each image region were then recalled for each subsequent round after minor readjustment using reference points from the first-round DAPI image and determining the appropriate offset. The exposure times were set at a fixed value for all images of a given marker. For image analyses, microscopy images were exported as full-resolution TIFF images in grayscale for each individual channel collected.

MultiOmyx image analytics

The acquired images from sequential rounds were registered using DAPI images acquired in the first round of staining via a rigid registration algorithm for each region of interest. The parameters of transformation were then applied to the subsequent rounds, which ensured that the pixel coordinates across all the imaging rounds corresponded to the same physical locations on the tissue. Classification and co-expression analysis were performed in multiple stages. First, a nuclear segmentation algorithm was applied on the DAPI image to delineate and identify individual cells. Location information and expression of all the markers were computed for every cell identified. Then, morphologic image analysis and shape detection were performed using proprietary algorithms (Neogenomics Laboratories; https://neogenomics.com/pharma-services/lab-services/multiomyx). These algorithms detect and classify cells as positive or negative for each marker depending on their subcellular localization and morphology. A tissue-quality algorithm was also applied to the images to ensure image artifacts that arose owing to tissue folding or tear did not affect cell classification. Co-expression analysis and phenotype identification were performed by combining individual marker classification results.

Quantification and statistical analysis

Prism GraphPad Software (version 9.1.2), R studio, Microsoft excel, GSEA (https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/index.jsp) and Morpheus online software (https://software.broadinstitute.org/morpheus) were used for statistical analyses, graphics and data archiving. Unpaired student’s T test with Benjamin-Hochberg correction for multiple testing, Mann-Whitney test for non-parametric data, or ANOVA with Tukey’s or Dunn’s correction for multiple testing were used for comparisons, as appropriate. Statistical details of experiments are reported in the figure legends.
