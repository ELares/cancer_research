# Engineering lipid nanoparticle-stabilized emulsions for spatiotemporal mRNA delivery and enhanced T cell immunity.

## Abstract

Achieving strong T cell responses remains a key challenge in mRNA vaccines and therapeutics. Here, we develop a colloid-engineered, lipid nanoparticle-stabilized emulsion (LSE) to study how spatiotemporal mRNA delivery influences immune dynamics. Multi-omic analyses (single-cell RNA sequencing [scRNA-seq], flow cytometry, and enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay [ELISA]) illustrate that LSE facilitates localizing mRNA to immunocytes, increasing antigen presentation while reducing off-target antigen secretion, and non-immune cell cross-presentation, which are key factors linked to T cell exhaustion in conventional LNP-based systems. This targeted delivery induces durable interferon (IFN)-γ+ and interleukin (IL)-2+ T cell responses lasting up to 300 days and expands the T cell repertoire in mice compared to the AS01-adjuvanted Shingrix vaccine. Furthermore, LSE elicits potent protective and therapeutic effects against B16-OVA and LLC-NY-ESO1 inoculation in mice. These results indicate the potential of spatially controlled mRNA delivery for enhanced mRNA vaccinations.

Introduction

Cell-mediated immunity is vital for combating intracellular pathogens and malignancies. While mRNA vaccines generate antigens intracellularly to provide advantages for inducing cellular immunity, clinical outcomes for non-COVID applications, such as rabies and varicella zoster virus (VZV) vaccines, revealed no stronger CD8+ and CD4+ T cell responses compared to adjuvanted recombinant-protein-based vaccinations. Thus, inducing durable and potent cellular immunity through mRNA vaccines remains a challenge.

Benchmark lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) indiscriminately transfect surrounding cells at the intramuscular injection site, primarily uptake and express in non-immune cells such as fibroblasts and endothelial cells. Notably, antigen presentation by these non-immunocytes without co-stimulatory molecules may induce T cell exhaustion, similar to that in the tumor microenvironment, collectively compromising the immune outcomes of the mRNA vaccine. Thus, precise spatiotemporal control over mRNA delivery—regulating where, when, and how antigens are expressed and presented—is pivotal for dictating immune dynamics, facilitating professional antigen presentation, and eliciting robust cellular immunity.

To address this, we developed a droplet-based mRNA vehicle, termed LSE (LNP-stabilized emulsion). Previous studies show that particle size and interfacial properties govern cell-type-specific uptake at injection sites. In contrast to LNPs (∼80 nm), which preferentially accumulate in stromal cells such as fibroblasts, larger or submicron particles are more efficiently internalized by antigen-presenting cells (APCs), including macrophages and dendritic cells (DCs). Moreover, emulsion-based carriers with partially exposed oil-water interfaces can promote local inflammatory signaling and APC recruitment, enhancing antigen uptake and immune engagement. Accordingly, LSE with larger size and alternating oil-water interfacial architecture are expected to bias mRNA delivery toward immunocytes, enabling direct antigen presentation in APCs while minimizing off-target expression.

By cross-mapping the delivery kinetics (uptake, expression, and secretion) and immune dynamics (APC recruitment, antigen presentation, and T cell activation) of LNP and LSE, we uncovered how mRNA delivery kinetics shaped immune outcomes. Unlike LNPs, the LSE promoted APC tropism, localized antigen expression, and enhanced chemokine-driven APC activation, fostering Th1-polarized immunity. For anti-tumor therapeutics, LSE outperformed LNP with increased tumor suppression and survival rates, with 20% of the mice remaining tumor free for over 100 days post-tumor inoculation in a prophylactic B16-OVA tumor model. In VZV vaccination, LSE-delivered glycoprotein E (gE) mRNA elicited durable CD4+ T cell responses at 300 days and a broader, more diverse TCR repertoire than clinical Shingrix. These results underscore that optimizing mRNA spatiotemporal delivery to synchronize antigen expression with APC activation enhances T cell quality and longevity, advancing mRNA vaccine and immune therapeutics for evolving demands of precise, potent, and sustained cellular immunity.

Results

Engineering, optimization, and characterization of LSE

LSE was stabilized by covering LNP at the squalene/water interface, where mRNA was encapsulated within the LNPs. One-step sonication facilitates the interfacial adsorption of LNPs, but hydrophobic interactions may compromise the integrity, potentially reducing mRNA encapsulation. We screened 6 groups of 24 LNP formulations with similar sizes and encapsulation efficiency to identify the optimal LSE (Figures 1A and S1A–S1C), maximizing interfacial integrity while minimizing mRNA loss. mRNA encapsulation for LSE was assessed via agarose gel electrophoresis (Figure S1D). In Figures S1E and S1F, the addition of cationic lipid DOTAP improved LNP integrity in LSE, while the ratio of the helper lipid DSPC and cholesterol showed minimal impact on the mRNA entrapment. Reducing the ratio of ionizable lipid or both ionizable lipid and cationic lipid decreased the mRNA loading efficacy. Following evaluation, we identified LNP formulation (C4:MC3:DSPC:cholesterol:DMG-PEG2000:DOTAP = 42:10:38.5:1.5:8) to prepare the optimal LSE (Figure 1C). Additionally, compared to LNP with conventional formulations (MC3:DSPC:cholesterol:DMG-PEG2000 = 50:10:38.5:1.5), C4 LNP demonstrated similar physicochemical properties (size, zeta potential, and pKa) and cellular internalization, mRNA expression in vitro, and mRNA tissue expression in vivo (Figures S2A–S2F). In latter analysis, LNP with the conventional formulation (denoted as LNP) was used as the control group, as it has been clinically approved.

Accordingly, LSE was with sizes of 450–500 nm, larger than the corresponding LNPs, which measured around 70–80 nm (Figures 1B and 1D). With Cy5-labeled mRNA embedded, confocal images revealed a uniform distribution of LNPs at the oil-water interface, a characteristic feature of Pickering emulsions (Figure 1E). Both LNP and LSE demonstrated stability over 28 days, as evaluated by size, zeta potential, and mRNA protection by gel electrophoresis (Figures S2G, S2H, and S2J). With different mRNAs loaded, LSE presented similar sizes around 450 nm (Figures S2I and S12).

We next evaluated the in vivo performance in intramuscular luciferase mRNA (sequence listed in Table S1) delivery of both LNP and LSE. The larger LSE limited mRNA delivery and expression primarily to the injection site, minimizing hepatic expression and reducing the risk of liver toxicity (Figure 1F). By contrast, LNPs can drain through interstitial fluid into lymphatics and capillaries, leading to higher antigen expression in lymph node (Figures S3A–S3C) and also systemic circulation and liver accumulation.

Subsequently, the in vitro delivery was assessed in DC2.4 (a mouse DC line) and C2C12 cells (a mouse myoblast cell line) (Figure 1G). LSE exhibited significantly higher cellular internalization in DC2.4 cells than in C2C12 cells, whereas LNPs facilitated internalization in both cell types (Figures 1H and 1I). Similar trend was observed in the GFP mRNA delivery and expression in bone-marrow DCs, further suggesting the increased APC tropisms of LSE (Figure S3D). This may be attributed to the increased particle size and interfacial hydrophobicity, shifting the dominant internalization routes (Figures S3F and S3G).

Spatiotemporal control of mRNA delivery for enhanced APC-tropic antigen expression and innate immune activation

Next, we used single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) to examine the differential cellular-level mRNA delivery between LSE and LNP at the injection site. 24 h after intramuscular administration of PBS, LNP, or LSE (each carrying 5 μg OVA mRNA [sequence listed in Table S1]), cell suspensions from the injection site were isolated and enriched with CD45+ cells (Figure S4A). scRNA-seq libraries were generated via 10× Chromium and analyzed via Omicsmart online platform (http://www.omicsmart.com). Unsupervised clustering identified 18 distinct cell types, including immune and stromal cells, with no new subpopulations observed in the PBS-, LNP- and LSE-treated groups. Both LNP and LSE formulations significantly increased innate immune cell populations, such as macrophages (Macro) and neutrophils (NEUT_1 and NEUT_2), compared to PBS control (Figures 2A and S4B).

To assess differential mRNA delivery and immunogenicity, we visualized the expression of shared highly variable genes and the exogenous marker mRNA within the clusters semi-quantitatively at the single-cell resolution using t-distributed stochastic neighbor embedding (Figure 2B). The PBS group showed no detectable exogenous mRNA, confirming assay specificity. LNP-delivered mRNA was widespread across myeloid and non-immune cells, with notable accumulation in fibro-adipogenic progenitors (FAPs), consistent with prior reports. In contrast, LSE showed higher tropism for immunocytes, with enriched OVA mRNA signals in macrophages, DCs, and neutrophils.

An unbiased Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes enrichment analysis in FAPs revealed strong upregulation of Endocytosis (57 of 268 genes, p = 1.26 × 10−6) and Protein processing in endoplasmic reticulum (53 of 173 genes, p = 3.24 × 10−12), indicating that LNP and LSE groups facilitated differential levels of mRNA uptake and expression in the FAP cell clusters. Conversely, DCs with LSE-targeted tropism showed lower enrichment for these pathways (Endocytosis: 21 of 268 genes, p = 3.44 × 10−2; Protein processing: 18 of 173 genes, p = 3.26 × 10−3). These findings indicated that while LSE facilitated mRNA delivery and expression in DCs similar to LNP, it did not achieve similar delivery efficacy in FAPs.

Antigen levels in intracellular and intercellular compartments at the injection site were quantified at 1- and 3-day post-administration. ELISA analysis revealed that antigen expressed from LSEs was predominantly retained intracellularly, whereas LNPs produced antigen distributed across both intracellular and extracellular compartments (Figure 2C). Accordingly, these data imply that LSE favors mRNA uptake and intracellular expression by immunocytes such as DCs, while LNP induces broad, indiscriminate mRNA delivery and antigen expression in both immune and non-immune cells, including promotion of antigen secretion in cells such as FAP cells.

Regarding the immune profile, LSE significantly upregulated chemokine production genes such as Cxcl5 and CCl2 levels in the FAP cell clusters, while also increased Cxcl2 levels in the DC cell clusters, compared with LNP (Figure S4C). This transcriptome analysis corresponded with increased secretion of inflammatory cytokines (interleukin [IL]-6 and IL-12p70) and chemokines (Cxcl10) at the injection site 1 day post-treatment (Figure S4D), driving significant recruitment of immunocytes, such as DCs, monocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils (Figure 2D). Furthermore, the cell-cell communication was upregulated. The LSE-treated group notably recruited the immune-stimulatory neutrophils, identified as NEUT-1 (Tnf, Nlrp3, and Ccl3) (Figure S4E). Compared to LNP-treated group, intercellular interaction analysis revealed stronger NEUT-1/DC communication in the LSE group, through TNF-Tnfrsf1a ligand-receptor pairing (Figure S4F), which could potentially enhance further DC activations.

Dictating antigen presentation and T cell dynamics at the injection site

Next, we evaluated the impact on the antigen presentation and T cell activations. As shown in Figure 2E, LSE significantly increased the expression of co-stimulatory molecules, including CD40 (290% increase) and CD86 (284% increase), as well as antigen-presenting molecules like SIINFEKL-MHC I (major histocompatibility complex class 1) (212% increase) and MHC II (350% increase), suggesting the early DC activation and antigen presentation induced by LSE. By day 7, both LSE and LNP showed similar expression levels of MHC I, MHC II, and CD86, but the higher CD40 expression in LSE-treated DCs particularly indicated a more sustained priming for T cell activation (Figure S4G).

Afterward, we analyzed antigen presentation-related genes in DC and FAP using scRNA-seq data. Transcriptome analysis demonstrated that among the DC cluster, antigen-presentation-related genes (H2-Q7, H2-K1, B2M, and PSME1) were upregulated in both LNP- and LSE-treated groups, indicating enhanced antigen presentation (Figure S4C). Intriguingly, LNP also led to a moderate increase in the H2-Q7 and B2M genes in the FAP cell clusters, indicating potential antigen presentation by non-APCs. However, the absence of co-stimulatory molecules, such as CD40, in these cells, may limit adaptive immune activation and instead contributed to immunosuppression (Figure S4C). Additionally, the LNP-treated group showed increased expression of exhaustion markers (Lag3, Batf, and Irf4) and decreased Icos levels in the T cell cluster compared to LSE (Figure S4H), indicating LNP-related T cell exhaustion. Additionally, flow cytometry data showed increased Lag-3, Tim-3, and CTLA-4 expressions among the recruited T cells in LNP-treated group at day 3 post-injection, while LSE induced higher Icos expression, indicating stronger activations of the recruited T cells (Figure 2F). No significant differences were observed on day 1, likely due to the lag between transcriptomic and protein-level expression (Figure S4I).

Coordinating mRNA expression kinetics with immune dynamics for Th1-biased cellular immunity

To characterize mRNA delivery and expression kinetics, we employed the Cre-LoxP Ai9 mouse model, which expresses Lox-stop-Lox tdTomato, and upon successful Cre-recombinase mRNA delivery, the translated Cre protein deletes the stop cassette, activating tdTomato expression (red fluorescence) (Figure S5A). Flow cytometry analysis of tdTomato fluorescence at the injection site indicated the distinct temporal dynamics in the mRNA expression between LNP and LSE, with LNP inducing a rapid peak around day 7 post-injection, while LSE displayed a slower, more sustained expression profile (Figure S5B). This correlated with prolonged retention of Cy5-labeled mRNA, indicating an antigen depot effect at the injection site (Figure S3E).

Given these divergent expression profiles, we evaluated spatiotemporal immune dynamics at the injection site and lymph node, using SARS-CoV-2 mRNA (Figure 3A). At the injection site, neutrophils, monocytes, and macrophages demonstrated similar temporal patterns in both LNP and LSE group, while LSE treatment resulted in higher immune cell recruitment compared to LNP-treated mice (Figures S5C–S5F). For DCs, LNP induced a single peak of DC recruitment between days 3 and 7, corresponding with its peak antigen expression. In contrast, LSE elicited biphasic DC response, with an initial influx during days 1–3, followed by a secondary surge between days 10 and 14 (Figure 3B). These observations suggested that DC recruitment was shaped by both antigen availability and the evolving immune microenvironment. The early wave of DC infiltration in LSE-treated mice correlated with rapid chemokine secretion and upregulated CCR7 on local cells (Figure S5G), facilitating their migration to the lymph nodes. Concurrently, LSE’s sustained antigen expression established an immune-amplifying loop, driving late-phase DC recruitment. This resulted in a dual-phase antigen presentation, coupled with enhanced DC cross-presentation, promoting robust and long-lasting T cell priming.

In the draining lymph nodes (dLNs), LNP induced rapid expansion of germinal center (GC) B cells and T follicular helper (Tfh) cells between days 7 and 14, followed by decline (Figures 3C and 3D), reflecting its strong but transient mRNA expression (Figure S5B). Conversely, LSE gradually increased GC B and Tfh cell populations, peaking at day 21. One day after the booster dose, GC B and Tfh cells in the LNP group surged sharply but declined by day 7, whereas LSE exhibited an opposite trend, with a steady increase (Figures 3E and 3F). These kinetics aligned with humoral responses, where LNP induced a rapid rise in antigen-specific antibody levels by day 7, peaking within 7 days post-boost, while the antibodies in LSE-treated mice increased steadily, peaking between 7 and 14 days post-boost (Figure 3G).

Next, the cellular immune responses were examined. LSE induced a greater number of interferon (IFN)-γ+ CD4+ T cells in the lymph nodes from 1 to 14 days (Figures S5I and S5J), highlighting its strong potential to activate Th1 cell-mediated immunity, likely driven by sustained and multiple rounds of DC-mediated antigen priming. The elevated IgG2a/IgG1 ratio further demonstrated a Th1-polarized immune response elicited by LSE (Figure 3H). In the spleen, the temporal dynamics of functional CD4+ T cells, including IFN-γ+ CD4+ T cells, IL-2+ CD4+ T cells, and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α+ CD4+ T cells, revealed sustained cellular immunity throughout the immunization process (Figure 3I). Compared to LNP, LSE-treated groups showcased significantly stronger and prolonged CD4+ T cell responses, as evidenced by the sustained high levels of CD4+ T cells to 28 days following both the initial and booster vaccination. Notably, LSE increased proportions of IFN-γ+, IL-2+, and TNF-α+ CD4+ T cells by 138%, 153%, and 132%, respectively, at 28 days. Additionally, LSE significantly increased the number of antigen-specific IFN-γ-secreting splenocytes by 208% and IL-4-spot-forming splenocytes by 126% (Figures 3J and 3K), with higher IFN-γ/IL-4 ratio, contrasting to the LNP group (Figure S5K). These results indicate that LSE induces superior Th1 activation, likely due to its prolonged antigen expression and DC-mediated presentation for durable T cell priming.

Potent and long-term T cell-mediated immunity in VZV vaccines

Next, we evaluated the cellular immune efficacy of LSE in VZV vaccines. Unlike acute infections, latent VZV cannot be controlled by neutralizing antibodies alone, underscoring the need for vaccines to induce potent and durable T cell-mediated immunity to control reactivation and prevent shingles. While mRNA-based strategies have been explored to enhance antigen cross-presentation via MHC I, clinical trials have shown no higher cellular immunity, compared with the Food and Drug Administration-approved Shingrix vaccine (GSK, adjuvanted recombinant zoster vaccine) at equivalent doses.

Here, we engineered an mRNA encoding a truncated form of VZV gE) mRNA sequence listed in Table S1), mirroring the antigen used in the Shingrix, and administrated in a prime-boost regimen (Figure 4A). Analysis of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) collected at 56, 70, 120, and 300 day post-vaccination allowed us to map the trajectory of antigen-specific T cell reactions. While the VZV-specific T cell response gradually declined over time, LSE sustained IFN-γ+ and IL-2+ CD4+ T cell responses significantly longer than LNP, persisting for up to 300 days post-immunization (Figure 4B). A similar trend was observed in the IFN-γ+ and IL-2+ CD8+ T cell responses (Figures S6A and S6B). This was further validated by a more pronounced Th1-skewed cytokine profile, including IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2, and IL-21, in the supernatant of splenocytes at 120 days after immunization (Figure 4C) as well as the enzyme-linked immunosorbent spot (ELISpot) analysis (Figure 4D).

Furthermore, LSE significantly increased central memory T cells (TCM) and effector memory T cells (TEM) within the CD4+ T cell population by 130% (p < 0.001) and 146% (p < 0.001), respectively, compared to LNP (Figures 4E, S6C, and S6D), suggesting increased long-term immune response. Notably, LNP and LSE triggered comparable Th2-type T cell responses, illustrated by similar levels of IL-4-secreting T cells (Figure S6E) and memory B cells in the spleen at 120 days post-immunization (Figure S6F). At 300 days post-immunization, LSE elicited a 131% increase in IFN-γ-secreting T cells compared to LNP (Figure S6G) and improved antigen-specific proliferation in both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Figure 4F).

Comparison of cellular immunity in response to VZV gE mRNA vaccines to Shingrix in mice

Next, we conducted a comparative evaluation of the immune response against the benchmark vaccine, Shingrix, and performed the bulk T cell receptor (TCR) β chain (TCRβ) sequencing of the hypervariable region in both total and gE-reactive CD4+ T cells at 27 and 56 days post-immunization (Figure 4G). At 120 days post-immunization, all three groups elicited potent gE-specific T cell responses, exhibiting a higher frequency of IFN-γ+, IL-2+, and TNF-α+ CD4+ and CD8+ T cells compared to PBS control. Notably, LSE induced stronger CD4+ T cell responses not only over LNP (increasing by 168%, 158%, and 141%, respectively) but also over Shingrix (increasing by 132%, 135%, and 132%, respectively) (Figure 4H). Additionally, the CD8+ T cells also exhibited evident activation (Figure 4I), along with IFN-γ-secreting and IL-2-secreting T cells in splenocytes via ELISpot assays (Figure 4J). Moreover, at 120 days post-immunization, LSE induced significantly higher frequencies of TCM and TEM among both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Figures 4K and 4L) and promoted greater antigen-specific proliferation in CD4+ T cell subsets (Figure 4M), compared to the LNP and Shingrix groups.

To identify gE-reactive T cells for bulk TCRβ sequencing, carboxyfluorescein diacetate succinimidyl ester (CFSE)-labeled splenocytes were cultured with a pool of gE-overlapping peptides for 72 h, followed by fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) of CFSEdim CD4+ T cells (Figure 4G). Three TCR datasets were analyzed, including the total TCR repertoire (Set A), the gE-reactive TCR repertoire (Set B), and their intersection (Set C), enabling molecular-level comparison of cellular immune responses induced by LNP, LSE, and the Shingrix vaccine (Figure 4N). CD4+ T cells from the three groups exhibited extensive proliferation upon stimulation with gE peptides (Figures 4O and S6I). Post-booster immunization, LSE induced a marked increase in the clonality index and in the fraction of gE-reactive clonotypes within the total CD4+ T cell TCR repertoire (Figure 4O), surpassing both LNP and Shingrix.

Recent studies suggest a potential correlation between CD4+ TCR diversity and the efficacy of herpes zoster prevention and treatment. We assessed the TCR repertoire diversity in the gE-reactive repertoire (Set B) after primary and booster immunizations. After boosting, the Shingrix group exhibited reduced TCR specificity within Set C, whereas the LSE group showed a sharp increase in specificity and LNP showed a relative gradual rise (Figure 4P). Similarly, TCR diversity within Set B decreased post-booster in both the LNP and Shingrix groups but increased in the LSE group (Figure 4Q). Analysis of high-frequency clonotypes within Set B revealed a progressive decline across all groups during immunization (Figure 4R).

Advancing anti-tumor immune responses against melanoma

We next evaluated the anti-tumor effects of LSE and LNP based on OVA-encoded mRNA (Figure 5A). A single LSE dosage (5 μg OVA-mRNA per mouse) induced more OVA-specific CD8+ T cells than LNP at both 28 and 42 day post-immunization (Figures 5B and S7A). LSE induced comparable cytotoxic T cells with LNP, at days 28, but significantly higher frequencies of IFN-γ+ (p < 0.0001) and granzyme B+ CD8+ T cells (p < 0.0001), at 42 days post-immunization. In a therapeutic tumor model, mouse received a single dose 7 days post-tumor inoculation (Figure 5C); LSE-treated mice exhibited significantly reduced tumor volumes at all time points (Figures 5D and S7A) and demonstrated prolonged survival, compared with LNP-treated controls (Figure 5E).

For prophylactic efficacy evaluation, C57BL/6 mice were immunized on days 0 and 28 and challenged with tumor cells (1 × 106 cells per mouse) on day 56 (Figure 5F). Both formulations demonstrated elevated tumor suppression, with median survival times of 45 days (LNP) and 51 days (LSE), compared to 21 days in the PBS group (Figures 5G–5I). Notably, LSE conferred long-term protection, with 20% of mice remaining tumor free for over 100 days post-inoculation, whereas no tumor-free survivors were observed in the LNP group.

Analysis of the tumor microenvironment further revealed that LSE enhanced intratumoral T cell immunity (Figures 5J and 5K). LSE administration diminished the expression of inhibitory receptors PD-1 and LAG-3 on T cells (Figure 5L) and increased the frequencies of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells producing IFN-γ, TNF-α, and granzyme B (Figure 5M).

To assess antimetastatic efficacy, a B16-OVA lung metastasis model in C57BL/6 was established. The mice were intravenously inoculated with 1 × 106 tumor cells, followed by prime-boost vaccinations with LSE on days 7 and 14 post-inoculation (Figure 5N). By day 28, a marked reduction in the number of pulmonary melanoma nodules was observed in the LSE-treated group compared to the LNP control group (p < 0.0001, Figure 5O). indicating immune-mediated suppression of metastatic dissemination.

In a recurrence model following surgical resection of primary tumors, LSE vaccination inhibited tumor relapse. Two doses of LSE were administered to the mice 7 and 14 days after the tumor was surgically removed (Figure 5P). Tumor volumes were reduced by approximately 55% (day 12) compared with the LNP group (p < 0.01, Figure 5Q) and median survival was extended to 38 days post-surgery (Figure 5R), demonstrating that LSE achieved potent inhibition of tumor recurrence.

Anti-tumor effects against Lewis lung carcinoma

To evaluate antigen-specific antitumor immunity in a clinically relevant setting, we employed an engineered Lewis lung carcinoma (LLC) model expressing human NY-ESO-1. NY-ESO-1 is a prototypical cancer-testis antigen aberrantly expressed in subsets of non-small cell lung cancer and has been explored clinically as a target for cancer immunotherapy, including mRNA-based vaccines. Owing to the absence of a murine homolog, antigen-specific immune responses were evaluated using NY-ESO-1-expressing LLC tumors.

For protective evaluation, C57BL/6 mice were administrated with LSE or LNP in a prime-boost regimen at a 14-day interval and challenged with LLC tumor cells on day 63 (Figure 5S). As illustrated in Figure 4T, LSE vaccination reduced tumor onset, compared with LNP. Specifically, LSE administration resulted in a 62% reduction in tumor volume by day 78, indicating the enhanced immune responses against tumor challenge.

For therapeutic assessment, C57BL/6 mice were subcutaneously inoculated with LLC cells (1 × 106 cells/mice), and subsequently vaccinated intramuscularly with LSE, LNP, or PBS on days 7 and 14 post-inoculation (Figure 5U). Compared with LNP, the tumor volume of LSE decreased by 74% on day 15 (Figure 5V) and extended the overall survival of the mice (Figure 5W), illustrating increased anti-tumor effects.

To mimic post-surgical tumor recurrence, we established a post-resection model. Once subcutaneous tumors in C57BL/6 mice reached approximately 100 mm3, primary tumors were surgically excised. This was followed by two administrations of the vaccine post-operation (Figure 5X). Consistent with the therapeutic model results, vaccination with LSE more effectively controlled tumor recurrence. By day 13 after tumor resection, the average tumor volume in the LSE group was reduced by 54% compared to the LNP group (p < 0.01, Figure 5Y) and extended survival compared with LNP (Figure 5Z).

Collectively, results from prophylactic, therapeutic, and rechallenge models demonstrate that LSE consistently enhances antitumor immunity relative to conventional LNP-based mRNA vaccines.

Discussion

The success of LNP-mRNA vaccines against COVID-19 has highlighted their advantages over conventional vaccines, including high antigen expression, scalable manufacturing, and favorable safety profile. Current mRNA vaccine development primarily focuses on maximizing in vivo antigen generation or modulating biodistribution through LNP formulation optimizations. However, the intricate relationship between mRNA delivery, expression kinetics, antigen presentation, and subsequent immune activation dynamics remains poorly understood. Moreover, challenge persists. mRNA vaccines often fail to induce durable T cell immunity, which is crucial for infections like VZV and for therapeutic and prophylactic mRNA-based cancer vaccines, where sustained T cell immunity is considered crucial.

In this study, we mapped the in vivo fate of mRNA vaccines by comparing conventional LNPs with a tailored LSE. While LNPs delivered mRNA to both immune and non-immune cells, leading to antigen secretion and suboptimal T cell activation due to inefficient cross-presentation, LSE selectively targeted APCs. This specific delivery enabled direct antigen expression, professional cross-presentation, and enhanced APC recruitment via chemokine signaling, driving potent and sustained T cell responses. LSE-based VZV mRNA vaccines elicited durable T cell immunity for 300 days, exceeding the clinical benchmark Shingrix. Additionally, LSE induced increased TCR diversity and clonality. Furthermore, LSE demonstrated increased efficacy in protective, therapeutic, and rechallenging tumor models, surpassing LNPs, suppressing tumor growth and extending survival.

We attribute LSE’s superior efficacy to optimized spatiotemporal control of mRNA delivery, rather than incremental improvement of mRNA expressions. By integrating scRNA-seq with mRNA delivery kinetics (flow cytometry and in vivo imaging) and expression dynamics (flow cytometry and ELISA assay), we delineate three distinct antigen processing pathways (Figure 6): Path A, direct mRNA delivery to APCs, resulting in intracellular antigen expression and MHC I-mediated T cell priming; Path B, mRNA uptake by immune or non-immune cells followed by antigen secretion, immunocyte recruitment, and predominantly MHC II-driven humoral or mixed immune responses; and Path C, antigen expression in non-immune cells (e.g., FAPs and endothelial cells) without adequate co-stimulation, potentially resulting in T cell exhaustion.

LNPs deliver mRNA across all three pathways. Particularly, we found that LNPs induced higher levels of antigen secretion (Figure 2C), which likely biases responses toward Path B and humoral responses. This explains clinical observations of robust antibody induction by current mRNA vaccines, despite their theoretical capacity to drive intracellular antigen processing and MHC I-restricted T cell responses. LSE, on the other hand, preferentially engages Path A by directing mRNA delivery to APCs. This APC-centric delivery enhances immune cell infiltration (DCs, macrophages, and neutrophils), activation, and cytokine production at the injection site, establishing an immunostimulatory microenvironment that promotes professional antigen presentation and robust T cell priming.

Despite enabling increased antigen generation, LNP-mRNA vaccines fail to achieve the durable T cell immunity observed with adjuvanted subunit vaccines. Similarly, clinical data for Moderna’s VZV vaccine (mRNA-1468) show no advantage over Shingrix, which we attribute to LNPs’ reliance on Path B and Path C, limiting direct APC engagement and promoting T cell exhaustion. We demonstrate that LNPs accumulate in the liver (Figure 1F), causing delayed DC recruitment, while LSE target APCs, rapidly activating DCs (Figures 2 and 3) and enhancing T cell priming for sustained anti-VZV immunity (Figure 4) and superior tumor control (Figure 5). Safety assessments confirm LSE’s biocompatibility, with no significant inflammation or tissue damage (Figure S8). Notably, LSE’s enhanced T cell immunity was independent of squalene, as adding MF59 to LNPs reduced antibody titers without improving cellular responses (Figure S9).

By modifying the delivery pattern and onset of immunity, we achieve durable and potent T cell responses for mRNA vaccines. Our findings may provide a roadmap for optimizing mRNA vaccine design. In addition to LSE construction, future efforts can be expected in refining ionizable lipids and surface properties (e.g., antibody conjugation) or integrating LNPs with stimuli-responsive hydrogels and self-assembled scaffolds to fine-tune delivery pathways. These strategies could extend beyond infectious diseases to cancer immunotherapy or autoimmune therapies. For example, suppressing antigen secretion could benefit vaccines targeting broad antibody responses, such as those for Klebsiella pneumoniae or Pseudomonas aeruginosa vaccines, or producing therapeutic antibodies in model animals. Targeted delivery to non-immune cells could induce immune tolerance for conditions like lupus erythematosus and rheumatoid arthritis.

Limitation of this study

In this study, the relatively larger size of LSE hinders lymphatic drainage, resulting in reduced mRNA accumulation and expression in the dLN (Figures S3A–S3C). While LSE still induces robust Tfh and GC B cell responses through enhanced local APC targeting and activation, further size optimization may facilitate intercellular transfer and lymph node accumulation. For scalability, LSE was produced using high-pressure homogenization, which is widely employed for clinically approved emulsion adjuvants such as MF59 and AS03, yielding reproducible particle size and mRNA encapsulation across batches (Figures S12A–S12D). However, the large particle size may preclude sterile filtration, necessitating aseptic upstream manufacturing and filling processes, which could add complexity to industrial-scale production. Future efforts are expected to focus on further process optimization to improve scalability, sterilization compatibility, and formulation stability during large-scale manufacturing.

Method details

Preparation and characterization of LNP and LSE

LNP was formulated by mixing an aqueous phase containing the mRNA with an ethanol phase containing the lipids using a microfluidic chip device at a 3:1 ratio, according to the previously described method. The ethanol phase was prepared by dissolving a mixture of DLin-MC3-DMA (MedChemExpress), cholesterol (Sigma-Aldrich), DMG-PEG2000 (Avanti), helper lipid DSPC (Avanti), and cationic lipid DOTAP (MedChemExpress) at pre-determined molar ratios. The aqueous phase was prepared in a 10 mM citrate buffer (pH 4.0) with corresponding mRNA (Luciferase, EGFP, Cy5-mRNA, Cre-recombinase, OVA, SARS-CoV-2 Spike, VZV gE and NYESO-1). The sequence of the respective RNA is listed in the Table S1. The resultant LNP underwent overnight dialysis against 1× PBS at a 10K MWCO dialysis devices (ThermoFisher) at 4°C, followed by sterile filtration through a 0.2-μm filter and storage at 4°C until further use. LNP-stabilized emulsion (LSE) was synthesized via a one-step ultrasonication process (Branson Digital Sonifier, total Time = 120 s, Power = 12%, interval time = 4 s), with various formulations of LNP solutions as the water phase and squalene ((Sigma-Aldrich) as the dispersion phase, at a volume ratio of 98:2. The resulting LSE was stored at 4°C until use.

The particle size and zeta potential of LNP and LSE were assessed using dynamic light scattering (Malvern NanoZS Zetasizer), with diameters reported as the intensity mean average. For DLS measurements, LNP and LSE samples were diluted 100× in 1× PBS before analysis.

The encapsulation efficiency (EE) of RNA within the LNPs was quantified using the Quant-iT Ribogreen Assay (Invitrogen). Briefly, LNPs were treated with 1% w/v Triton X-100 (Solarbio) for the disruption of the structure and mRNA release. The concentrations of unencapsulated mRNA and the total mRNA were determined by measuring the fluorescence signal (excitation: 480 nm, emission: 520 nm). The morphology of LSE was observed via confocal laser scanning microscopy (Leica SP8 STED 3×), with Cy5-mRNA loaded.

In vitro uptake and transfection experiments

1.0 × 105 C2C12 and DC2.4 cells were seeded onto confocal dishes (Biosharp) 8 h before sample treatment. Subsequently, they were treated with 20 μl LNP or LSE containing Cy5-labeled mRNA (50 μg/mL). All cells were cultured at 37°C under 5% CO2 and during sample treatment, cells were incubated at 37°C under 5% CO2. After 6 h of incubation, cells were collected and washed three times with PBS. The cell membrane was stained with FITC Phalloidin (SolarBio), and the nuclei were stained with DAPI (SolarBio) after immobilization. The cellular uptake was observed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM, A1/SIM/STORM, Nikon).

To evaluate the mRNA transfection efficiency in BMDCs, 1.0 × 105 cells were seeded onto poly-L-lysine-coated confocal dishes (Biosharp) and incubated for 8 h. Subsequently, they were transfected with 20 μL of LNP or LSE containing EGFP-mRNA (50 μg/mL). Following 48 h of incubation, EGFP expression efficiency was observed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM, A1/SIM/STORM, Nikon).

In vivo bioluminescence

Female BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks) were intramuscularly administered with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg of Cy5-mRNA. After administration, the fluorescence signals at the injection site were collected using an in vivo imaging system FX Pro (Kodak) at 0, 6, 12, 24-, 48-, 96-, and 120 h post-injection.

Female BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks) were intramuscularly administered with LNP, or LSE containing 5 μg of Luciferase mRNA (APExBIO). Six hours post-administration, bioluminescence intensities were evaluated following an intraperitoneal injection of 100 μL D-luciferin (15 mg/mL) and imaged using the IVIS imaging system (PerkinElmer).

Mouse immunization

For immunization, LNP or LSE (100 μL per mouse containing 5 μg respective antigen mRNA) was injected intramuscularly. At various point post-immunization, samples from blood, draining lymph nodes (dLN), and spleens were collected from each group, and the levels of antigen-specific antibodies, GC B cells, Tfh cells, and cellular immune responses were assessed. Female mice were used in this initial immunogenicity study due to their generally enhanced immune responsiveness to vaccines and to maintain consistency with translational development pipelines.

Single-cell RNA sequencing

Female C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 6) were immunized with LNP or LSE as described previously. On day 1 post-vaccination, the mice were euthanized, and their muscles were harvested. The muscles were minced with scissors and then treated with a mixture of enzymes, including trypsin, hyaluronidase, collagenase, and DNase, to dissociate them into a single-cell suspension. The single-cell suspension was passed through a 40 μm Cell Strainer (BD Falcon) and resuspended in PBS. The single-cell suspension from six mice in each group was pooled. CD45+ cells were isolated from the single-cell suspension in each group using CD45 MicroBead (Miltenyi), and these CD45+ single-cell suspensions were subsequently combined with the total cell suspension at a 1:1 ratio. The mixed single-cell suspensions in each group were then loaded onto a 10× Genomics GemCode Single-cell instrument to generate single-cell Gel Bead-In-Emulsion (GEMs). Libraries were generated and sequenced from the cDNAs with Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 3′ Reagent Kits v3.1. The GEM generation, cDNA amplification, library preparation, and sequencing were performed by Gene Denovo Biotechnology Co. as part of this study. Overall, 8374 cells from the PBS group, 3934 cells from the LNP group, and 9269 cells from the LSE group passed the quality control threshold of more than 200 genes identified in each cell. The differentially expressed genes were obtained by model-based analysis of single-cell transcriptomics (MAST) analysis. Sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq platform with a sequencing depth of approximately 50,000 reads per cell. Quality control criteria included ≥200 detected genes per cell, ≤20% mitochondrial gene content, and ≥500 total reads per cell.

Analysis of immune cell recruitment in the injection site

To evaluate immune cell recruitment at the injection site, C57BL/6 mice (n = 6) were intramuscularly administered with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg OVA mRNA. Mice were euthanized at 1-, 3-, 5-, 7-, 10-, and 14 days post-administration. For the isolation of muscle cells, tissues from the injection site were selected and immersed in collagenase type II in PBS (0.5%, w/v) for 2 h at 37°C, allowing for tissue digestion and the isolation of single-cell suspensions. Then the cells were stained with Ghost Dye UV450 and then treated with anti-CD11c-Violet 450, anti-CD11b-APC-Cy7, anti-CD45-Red 710, anti-Ly6c-FITC, anti-Ly6G-PE-Cy7, and anti-F4/80-APC (total stain buffer volume 50 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

Analysis of protein expression in the injection site

To evaluate protein expression at the injection site, Ai9 mice (n = 6) were intramuscularly administered with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg Cre mRNA, and mice were euthanized at 3-, 7-, 10-, and 14-day post-administration. The muscle cell suspensions were stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and then treated with CD45-Red 710 (stain buffer volume 50 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

Analysis of DCs activation in injection site

To evaluate DCs activation at the injection site, C57BL/6 mice (n = 6) were intramuscularly administered with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg OVA mRNA, and mice were euthanized at 3- and 7 days post-administration. The muscle cells suspensions were stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and then treated with anti-CD11c-Red 710, anti-CD80-PE, anti-CD86-FITC, anti-CD40-APC, anti-MHC II-Violet 450, SIINFEKL-MHC I-PE-Cy7 and anti-CCR7-Percp Cy5.5 (total stain buffer volume 50 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

Analysis of germinal center activations in draining lymph node

To evaluate germinal center activations in lymph nodes (LNs), BALB/c mice (n = 6) were intramuscularly administered with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg Spike mRNA, and mice were euthanized at different time points post-administration. For isolation of lymph node cells, lymph nodes located in the popliteal fossa between the biceps femoris and semitendinosus were mechanically digested using a 70 μm Cell Strainer (BD Falcon) to prepare single-cell suspensions. The resulting cell suspensions were then stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and subsequently treated with anti-B220-FITC, anti-CD4-Violet 450, anti-PD-1-PE, anti-CXCR5-PE-Cy7, anti-GL-7-APC, and anti-FAS-BV605 (total stain buffer volume 50 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

Analysis of T cells in spleen

For analysis of T cell response in the spleen, C57BL/6 mice or BALB/c mice were sacrificed and the spleens were harvested at different time points post-vaccination. For splenocyte isolation, spleens from immunized mice were carefully passed through a 100 μm cell strainer (BD Falcon). Subsequently, erythrocyte lysis buffer was added, followed by three washes with RPMI 1640 to obtain single-cell suspensions. Then, the single-cell suspensions were re-stimulated with the antigen peptide pool (1 μg/mL) at 37°C and 5% CO2.

For the analysis of antigen-specific T cells, splenocytes were obtained without culture and washed twice with buffer. They were then stained with Pentamer Ovalbumin 257-264 PE (Proimmune) for 20 min at room temperature, followed by staining with anti-CD45-Red 710, anti-CD3-Percp Cy5.5, and anti-CD8-FITC for 30 min at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

For functional T cell testing, 6 h after stimulation with the antigen peptide pool, 1 μL/test of Brefeldin A was added to each well. After 12 h of culture, splenocytes were collected and stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510. Subsequently, they were stained with antibodies against surface markers (anti-CD3-Percp Cy5.5, anti-CD4-Violet 450, and anti-CD8-Red 710) for 30 min at 4°C. Following washing of the stained cells, they were fixed and permeabilized using the Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Kit (Tonbo, TNB-0607-KIT) and then stained with antibodies against intracellular cytokines (anti-IL-2-PE, anti-IFN-γ-APC, anti-TNF-α-FITC, and anti-Granzyme B-PE-Cy7). After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

For the memory T cell assay, after 72 h of culture, splenocytes were collected and stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and then stained with anti-CD4-FITC, anti-CD3-Violet 450, anti-CD8-Percp Cy5.5, anti-CD44-APC, and anti-CD62L-PE for 30 min at 4°C. Then the stained cells were washed twice and resuspended in the buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

For the T cell proliferation assay, splenocytes were stained with CFSE (5μM) for 5 min at room temperature and washed twice with wash buffer (PBS containing 5% FBS). Subsequently, the cells were cultured for 72 h. After culture, the cells were collected and stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510. They were then treated with anti-CD3-Percp Cy5.5, anti-CD4 Violet 450, and anti-CD8-Red 710 (total stain buffer volume 50 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. Then the stained cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for CD4+ T cell sorting.

All the above flow cytometry data were acquired using a Beckman CytoFlex LX flow cytometer, and CD4+ T cells were sorted using a BD FACSAria III Cell Sorter. All flow cytometry data were analyzed using FlowJo software.

ELISA analysis of intracellular and secreted antigens

Female C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 6) were immunized with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg of gE mRNA. At one and three-days post-administration, the mice were euthanized, and their muscles were harvested. The muscles were minced with scissors and centrifuged at 5000 g for 10 min. The supernatant was collected to detect extracellular gE protein, while the remaining tissue was used for intracellular detection. The concentration of the gE protein in the samples was quantified using ELISA kits, with support from Sinovac.

ELISPOT assay

For ELISPOT evaluations, PVDF-based membrane plates (Millipore) were activated by 35% ethyl alcohol. Then, the plates were coated with anti-mouse IFN-γ, IL-4, or IL-2 antibodies (15 μg/mL) and stored at 4°C overnight. The next day, the plates were blocked with culture medium (RPMI 1640, 10% FBS) for more than 1 h. Splenocytes from vaccinated mice were then added into the wells at a density of 106 cells/well immediately and subsequently re-stimulated with the antigen peptide pool (1 μg/mL). After 18–24 h of culture at 37°C and 5% CO2, the plates were incubated with a detection antibody (1 μg/mL) for 2 h at room temperature after removing cells, followed by incubation with streptavidin-ALP detection antibody (1 μg/mL) for 1 h at room temperature. Next, BCIP/NBT was added to the plates for about 3–10 min in the dark. Each of the above steps required washing the plates 5 times. Finally, the plates were washed with distilled water at suitable time points and allowed to air-dry in the dark. The spots were scanned and counted using an ELISPOT Analyzer (AT-Spot 2100, China).

Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)

For analysis of cytokine secretions at the injection site, muscle tissues from C57BL/6 mice (n = 4) were ground after intramuscular administration with LNP or LSE. Following the cutting of muscle tissues into small pieces, tissue homogenates were obtained by ultrasonic tissue disruption on ice. Subsequently, the supernatant was collected by centrifugation at 5000g for 30 min at 4°C. The collected supernatant underwent BCA assay for protein concentration measurement and was then analyzed for the concentrations of IL-1β, MIP1b, MIP-2, CXCL10, IL-12p70, and IL-6 using ELISA kits (LAIZEE BIOTECH, Shanghai, China).

For analysis of cytokine secretions from T cells in the spleen, supernatants (n = 6) were collected after splenocytes were re-stimulated with the antigen peptide pool (1 μg/mL) at 37°C and 5% CO2 for 48 h. The collected supernatants were then subjected to IL-2, IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-21 concentration assays using ELISA kits (Solarbio Life Sciences, Beijing, China).

For analysis of antigen-specific antibody titers, serum samples (n = 6) were collected at various time points after administration for analysis using ELISA. Briefly, 96-well plates were coated with antigen diluent overnight at 4°C, washed with PBST (0.05% Tween 20), and blocked with 1% BSA in PBS for 90 min at 37°C. Serum samples were then added, and incubated at 37°C for 60 min, followed by incubation with goat anti-mouse HRP-conjugated IgG (1:50,000) for 20 min at 37°C. After six washes, TMB substrate was added, and the reaction was stopped after 10–15 min. OD450 values were measured using a microplate reader, and antibody titers were determined as the dilutions at which the OD450 value was twice that of the negative control group. Antigen-specific IgG1 and IgG2a were also detected similarly.

Tumor models

For EG7-OVA therapeutic models, C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 5) were selected, and each mouse was inoculated subcutaneously with 5×105 EG7 tumor cells. When the tumor volume reached approximately 30 mm3–50 mm3, the tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into groups. Seven days after tumor cell inoculation, the mice were immunized intramuscularly with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg of OVA mRNA.

For the B16-OVA and LLC therapeutic models, C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 6) were inoculated subcutaneously with 1 × 106 B16-OVA cells or NY-ESO-1-highly expressing LLC cells. The mice then received intramuscular injections of 5 μg OVA mRNA or NY-ESO-1 mRNA encapsulated in either LNP or LSE on days 7 and 14 post-inoculation.

On day 20, B16-OVA tumors were harvested and dissected into small fragments. The fragments were digested in a solution containing collagenase I and IV at 37°C for 2 h. Following digestion, red blood cells were lysed using a red blood cell lysis buffer to obtain a single-cell suspension from the tumor microenvironment (TME).

For analysis of Treg cells, the resulting cell suspensions were then stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and subsequently treated with anti-CD3-PE, anti-CD4-APC-Cy7, anti-CD8-FITC, anti-CD25-APC, anti-Foxp3-PE-Cy7 (total stain buffer volume 100 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C.

For analysis of T cell exhaustion, the resulting cell suspensions were then stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and subsequently treated with anti-CD3-PE, anti-CD4-APC-Cy7, anti-CD8-FITC, anti-LAG3-APC, anti-PD-1-BV605, anti-TIM3-Percp5.5 (total stain buffer volume 100 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C.

For analysis of cytokine, the resulting cell suspensions were then stained with Ghost Dye Violet 510 and subsequently treated with anti-CD3-Percp5.5, anti-CD4-APC-Cy7, anti-CD8-FITC, anti-IFN-γ-APC, anti-TNF-α-PE, anti-GranB-PE-Cy7 (total stain buffer volume 100 μL) for 30 min in the dark at 4°C. After staining, the cells were washed twice and resuspended in buffer for flow cytometry analysis.

For B16-OVA prophylactic models, C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 5) were immunized twice, with a 28-day interval, using LNP or LNP-PE containing 5 μg of OVA mRNA. Twenty-eight days after the secondary immunization, the mice were subcutaneously injected with 1 × 106 B16-OVA cells to induce tumors. Tumor volumes were recorded every 3 days and calculated using the formula: (long axis diameter) × (short axis diameter) 2/2. When the tumor volume reached 2000 mm3, the mice were humanely sacrificed.

For the long-term prophylactic tumor model, female C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 6) were immunized with LNP or LSE containing 5 μg of NY-ESO-1 mRNA on day 0 and day 14. On day 63 after the initial immunization, the mice were subcutaneously challenged with 1×106 NY-ESO-1-highly expressing LLC cells. Tumor volume was measured every two days.

For the B16-OVA metastatic tumor model, C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks, n = 5) were intravenously inoculated with 1 × 106 B16-OVA cells. The mice then immunized with LNP or LSE encapsulating 5 μg of OVA mRNA on days 7 and 14. On day 28, the mice were euthanized, and lungs were harvested. The number of surface melanoma nodules on each lung was counted to assess metastatic burden.

For rechallenge tumor model, C57BL/6 mice (6–8 weeks) were subcutaneously inoculated with 1 × 106 B16-OVA cells or NY-ESO-1-highly expressing LLC cells. The primary tumors were surgically removed on day 12 (for B16-OVA, n = 6) or day 8 (for LLC, n = 5) post-inoculation. Subsequently, the mice received two intramuscular injections of LNP or LSE formulations containing 5 μg of the corresponding antigen mRNA (OVA or NY-ESO-1) on day 7 and day 14 after surgery. Tumor recurrence at the surgical site was monitored by measuring the tumor growth curve, and overall survival was recorded.

TCR repertoires sequencing

Total RNA in total CD4+ T cells and gE-reactive proliferating CD4+ T cells from different groups and time points were extracted using the Trizol reagent kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA). Then RNA samples were analyzed by high-throughput sequencing of TRB using the ImmuHub TCR profiling system (ImmuQuad Biotech, Hangzhou, China). Briefly, a 5′ RACE unbiased amplification protocol with unique molecular barcodes (UMBs) was used to control bottlenecks and eliminate PCR and sequencing errors. Sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq system with PE150 mode. A common adaptor with UMB was added on the 5′ end of cDNA during first-strand synthesis, and reverse primers corresponding to the constant (C) regions of each TRB were used for less biased PCR amplification. UMBs were used to correct errors and remove duplicates. V, D, J, and C segments were mapped with NCBI, CDR3 regions were extracted, and TRB clonotypes were assembled. Nucleotide and amino acid sequences of CDR3 of TRB were determined, and out-of-frame and stop codon sequences were removed. Clonotype amounts were defined by adding the numbers of TRB clones sharing the same CDR3 nucleotide sequence.

Quantification and statistical analysis

For the snRNA-seq analysis, cell type annotation, differentially expressed genes analysis, KEGG pathway and Gene Ontology enrichment analysis were performed on Omicsmart (https://www.omicsmart.com). For other data, GraphPad Prism 10.6.0 was used for plotting and statistical analysis, with values expressed as mean ± s.e.m. Data were analyzed using t-tests, one-way, or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with either Dunnet’s, Šídák’s or Tukey’s multiple comparisons tests as indicated in the figure captions. Survival curve analysis was generated using GraphPad Prism 10.6.0. p values less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.
