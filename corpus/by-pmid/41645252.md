# Beyond CRISPR: next-gen precision engineering of CAR-NK cells for enhanced persistence, trafficking, and tumor eradication.

## Abstract

Chimeric antigen receptor natural killer (CAR-NK) cells represent a promising "off-the-shelf" alternative to CAR-T cells, offering a superior safety profile and inherent multi-antigen targeting capabilities. However, their clinical potential is constrained by the "CRISPR ceiling", a set of practical limitations of DSB-based CRISPR-Cas9 such as DNA double-strand break (DSB)-associated chromosomal rearrangements and p53-mediated fitness loss, low efficiency for safe, large, multicistronic knock-ins, and rigid promoter-driven transgene expression that can cause tonic signaling. Importantly, next-generation, DSB-free base and prime editors reduce or eliminate the DSB-associated genotoxic stress observed with nuclease cutting, CRISPR-associated transposases now enable programmable, targeted insertion strategies that can accommodate larger cassettes, and synthetic/epigenetic circuits provide dynamic, context-dependent transgene control that avoids constitutive promoter-driven tonic signaling. This review explores technological approaches beyond conventional CRISPR, highlighting next-generation precision engineering tools that may enable improved CAR-NK therapies and represent potential advances in safety and efficacy. We detail how base editing, epigenetic reprogramming, targeted transposon systems, and synthetic biology circuits can be synergistically integrated to overcome critical clinical challenges. These advanced technologies enable the precise enhancement of three fundamental pillars of efficacy: Persistence through endogenous cytokine armoring and metabolic engineering; Trafficking via chemokine receptor matching and stromal barrier degradation; and Tumor Eradication using logic-gated targeting, immunomodulatory payloads, and bispecific engagers. By synthesizing these cutting-edge advances, we provide a roadmap for developing next-generation CAR-NK cells capable of durable, potent, and safe antitumor responses against both hematological and solid malignancies, ultimately forging a new frontier in accessible cellular immunotherapy.

Introduction: the CRISPR ceiling in CAR-NK therapy

The emergence of cellular immunotherapies has irrevocably transformed the oncology landscape, with chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-engineered T cells demonstrating unprecedented efficacy against refractory hematological malignancies. However, the limitations of autologous CAR-T cell products, including manufacturing complexities, treatment delays, cytokine release syndrome (CRS), immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS), and restricted efficacy against solid tumors, have catalyzed the exploration of alternative effector cells. Natural killer (NK) cells, cytotoxic lymphocytes of the innate immune system, have emerged as compelling platforms for CAR engineering, offering a trifecta of therapeutic advantages: inherent “off-the-shelf” potential due to minimal risk of graft-versus-host disease (GvHD) in allogeneic settings; intrinsic safety characterized by reduced pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion and lower incidence of severe toxicities; and multi-antigen targeting capability conferred by their diverse repertoire of activating and inhibitory receptors, enabling recognition of tumor cells even amid antigen escape. Clinical validation of CD19-targeted CAR-NK cells (cord blood-derived) showed encouraging response rates and low rates of severe CRS/ICANS in early cohorts; more recent phase-matched outcomes and follow-up analyses continue to support a favourable toxicity profile and provide extended efficacy and safety data. These attributes position CAR-NK cells as disruptive therapeutics capable of democratizing access to cellular immunotherapy. The choice of NK cell source importantly shapes manufacturing feasibility, editing efficiency, safety, persistence, and cost. Major sources currently used or under active development include: peripheral blood (PB-NK), umbilical cord blood (UCB)/placental CD34-derived NK, induced pluripotent stem cell-derived NK (iPSC-NK), and the NK-92 immortalized line. Below we summarize practical advantages, limitations, and clinical implications of each source to guide selection for next-generation CAR-NK strategies.

 Peripheral blood (PB-NK) Readily accessible from leukapheresis, PB-NK are attractive for both autologous and allogeneic uses. However, donor variability, lower expansion potential compared with feeder-dependent platforms, and relatively lower amenability to some delivery modalities (electroporation/transposon) can complicate manufacturing and scale-up. PB-derived NK products typically require robust ex vivo activation/expansion protocols (feeder cells, cytokine cocktails) and show heterogeneity in KIR repertoire that affects activity.

 Umbilical cord blood (UCB) and placental CD34 + -derived NK UCB provides a source of relatively naive NK cells with lower alloreactivity and has been used in clinical CAR-NK work (cord-blood-derived CD19 CAR-NK). CD34+ HSPCs from cord/placenta can be differentiated into NK cells at scale, offering a compromise between donor variability and scalability. These sources enable off-the-shelf production with lower GvHD risk but may require more complex differentiation and expansion to reach therapeutic doses. Example: the early cord blood-derived anti-CD19 CAR-NK clinical study demonstrating high response rates with a benign toxicity profile.

 iPSC-derived NK (iPSC-NK) iPSC-derived NK cells allow clonal, banked, and hyper-scalable manufacturing with uniform genetic background (facilitating standardized multiplex edits). iPSC platforms (for example FT596) have recently advanced to first-in-human studies and show promise for consistent multi-modification products (CAR + engineered ADCC receptors + cytokine armoring). iPSC-NK permit complex, reproducible engineering and are a leading route for true “off-the-shelf” products, though differentiation protocols, potential clonal drift and cost of goods remain active development areas.

 NK-92 cell line NK-92 and derivative lines offer extremely easy expansion and consistent cytotoxic phenotype and are already used in several clinical trials. Because NK-92 originates from a tumor line it must be irradiated before infusion (safety requirement), which limits in-vivo persistence. NK-92-based CAR products are therefore attractive when short-term, high-cytotoxic effect is acceptable and when strict batch-to-batch consistency is prioritized.

Despite this promise, the genetic engineering underpinning CAR-NK cell production faces formidable technological constraints. CRISPR-Cas9, the cornerstone of contemporary genome editing, enables precise gene disruption (e.g., knockout of inhibitory receptors like TIGIT or PDCD1) and site-directed transgene integration. Nevertheless, its dependence on double-strand breaks (DSBs) introduces substantial biological risks, karyotypic instability stemming from chromosomal translocations or chromothripsis; p53-mediated cell cycle arrest or apoptosis triggered by DNA damage responses, impairing expansion and persistence of edited cells; and off-target mutagenesis with potential oncogenic consequences, as evidenced by whole-genome sequencing in primate embryos. Furthermore, CRISPR-mediated knock-in efficiency remains suboptimal for integrating large or multiplexed genetic payloads essential for advanced CAR-NK designs, such as combinations of CAR constructs with cytokine genes (e.g., IL-15/IL-21), homing receptors (e.g., CXCR4), or signal modifiers (e.g., dominant-negative TGF-βR). The stochasticity of viral promoter-driven CAR expression (e.g., CMV, EF1α) further exacerbates functional heterogeneity, predisposing cells to tonic signaling, chronic antigen-independent activation that drives exhaustion through epigenetic rewiring and metabolic stress, and inadequate transgene regulation in response to tumor microenvironment (TME) cues. These limitations collectively define the “CRISPR ceiling,” constraining the therapeutic potential of engineered NK cells. The CRISPR ceiling is used here to connote the practical limitations of conventional CRISPR-Cas9 approaches that constrain therapeutic cell engineering. It encompasses four interrelated dimensions: (1) Editing efficiency & fidelity (e.g., low HDR rates for large insertions; off-target indels), (2) Cargo capability (difficulty integrating large or polycistronic cassettes safely and predictably), (3) Transgene regulation (static, promoter-driven expression that can cause tonic signaling and exhaustion), and (4) Genotoxicity/cell fitness (DSB-associated p53 responses, chromosomal rearrangements, and potential tumorigenicity). Concretely, these four dimensions map to mechanistic weaknesses of DSB-based editing that next-generation tools explicitly address: Genotoxicity/cell fitness: DSBs produced by Cas9 can resolve into large deletions and complex rearrangements and trigger a p53-mediated DNA damage response that reduces edited cell expansion and selects for p53-deficient clones, mechanistic problems that DSB-free base editors (BEs) and prime editors (PEs) avoid. Cargo capability/precision insertion: HDR-dependent knock-ins are inefficient for large or multicistronic payloads; RNA-guided transposase systems (CRISPR-associated transposases) and transposon platforms permit targeted insertion strategies for larger cassettes that are impractical with standard HDR approaches. Transgene regulation: Conventional promoter, viral or constitutive, driven transgenes produce static expression that can cause tonic signaling and exhaustion; synthetic gene circuits and epigenetic editors permit inducible, tumor-localized or feedback-regulated expression to minimize tonic signaling. Editing fidelity & off-target risk: BEs and PEs shift the trade-off from DSB-related structural risk toward nucleotide-level specificity challenges, a trade that is addressable by newly developed de-risked editors and improved off-target profiling.

Crucially, NK cells have signaling, epigenetic and metabolic features that differ from T cells and therefore determine how beyond-CRISPR interventions will function in practice. NK surface receptors signal through distinct adaptors (e.g., DAP10, DAP12 and FcRγ) and co-receptors such as 2B4 that recruit different downstream kinases and PI3K versus ITAM cascades; these differences alter how engineered receptors and transgenes are integrated into native signaling networks. Because NKs also form long-lived ‘adaptive’ subsets with characteristic epigenetic imprints and rely on activation-dependent metabolic rewiring that is distinct from T cells, tools that avoid DSBs (BEs/PEs), or that modulate chromatin or metabolic genes, may have markedly different efficacy and safety profiles in NK cells compared with T cells. We therefore emphasize, throughout this review, the mechanistic intersection of each next-gen tool with NK-specific signaling, epigenetic memory programs, and metabolic wiring. The primary constraints of conventional CRISPR-Cas9 editing in the context of CAR-NK cell engineering are detailed in Table 2.

To transcend this ceiling, next-generation precision engineering tools are rapidly evolving. BEs and PEs enable C•G→T•A or A•T→G•C substitutions and small insertions/deletions without DSBs, offering safer correction of exhaustion-associated genes (e.g., CISH) or oncogenic variants. CRISPR-associated transposases (e.g., CAST) facilitate targeted integration of large DNA sequences (> 10 kb), enabling site-specific incorporation of multi-gene cassettes for armored CAR-NK cells. Epigenetic editors (dCas9-linked methyltransferases/demethylases) allow transient modulation of immune checkpoint genes like PD-L1 or TIM-3, dynamically enhancing NK cell function within immunosuppressive niches. Synthetic gene circuits, incorporating TME-sensing promoters (e.g., hypoxia-inducible or NFAT-responsive elements), provide autonomous regulation of therapeutic transgenes, activating CAR expression only within tumors or secreting IL-15 in response to local ATP gradients. These innovations offer enhanced spatial, temporal, and dosage control over engineered cellular functions and therefore hold promise for more precise regulation of therapeutic activity.

This review posits that integrating these beyond-CRISPR technologies will catalyze a paradigm shift in CAR-NK cell design, specifically addressing three pillars of therapeutic enhancement, Persistence through in vivo expansion and resistance to exhaustion; Trafficking via chemokine receptor engineering and stromal barrier penetration; and Tumor Eradication via multi-antigen engagement, immunomodulation, and resilience to suppressive TME signals. By synthesizing recent advances in genome editing, synthetic biology, and receptor engineering, we delineate a roadmap for developing next-generation CAR-NK cells capable of overcoming the biological and technical constraints that currently limit their clinical impact, ultimately forging potent, durable, and accessible immunotherapies for both hematologic and solid malignancies.

Methods for this review

We performed a focused, narrative literature review to synthesize recent advances in precision engineering technologies applicable to CAR-NK cell design. We searched PubMed/MEDLINE, Embase, and ClinicalTrials.gov for articles, reviews, and registered trials published between January 1, 2015 and December 31, 2025. Search strategies combined MeSH/Emtree terms and free-text terms; a representative Boolean string was:

((“chimeric antigen receptor” OR “CAR”) AND (“natural killer” OR “NK”)) AND (“base edit*” OR “prime edit*” OR “base editor” OR “prime editor” OR “transposon” OR “piggyBac” OR “Sleeping Beauty” OR “epigenet*” OR “CRISPRa” OR “CRISPRi” OR “dCas9” OR “synthetic biology” OR “logic gate” OR “lipid nanoparticle” OR “LNP” OR “iPSC” OR “in vivo reprogram*”)). ClinicalTrials.gov was searched for relevant active or completed trials (e.g., NCT identifiers for iPSC-derived and engineered NK products). Search fields included title/abstract and were supplemented by manual scanning of reference lists of included articles and recent review papers.

Inclusion criteria: English-language articles, preclinical (in-vitro, animal) studies, early- and late-phase clinical trials, and relevant review or perspective articles that addressed engineering modalities, delivery platforms, or clinical translation of CAR-NK approaches. Exclusion criteria: non-English reports, articles outside the date range, isolated case reports with no technical/experimental detail, and papers unrelated to genetic or synthetic engineering of NK cells. Two authors independently screened titles and abstracts, reviewed full texts of potentially relevant papers, and extracted descriptive data into summary tables. Discrepancies were resolved by discussion and consensus. Because this is a narrative synthesis (not a systematic review), we did not register a protocol or apply a PRISMA workflow.

Next-gen engineering toolbox

Base editors (BEs) and prime editors (PEs)

Base editing and prime editing are DSB-free strategies that change the risk profile of editing compared with canonical Cas9. BEs chemically deaminate bases to install single-base substitutions without producing DSBs, markedly reducing the incidence of large deletions and complex rearrangements associated with nuclease cuts. PEs combine a Cas9 nickase with a reverse transcriptase and a pegRNA to write small insertions, deletions, or any base substitution without full DSBs, expanding the editable scope while avoiding many DSB-driven genotoxic events seen with nuclease approaches. These properties have been documented in foundational reports and subsequent mechanistic studies. PE employ Cas9 nickase fused to reverse transcriptase, using a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) to template desired sequence changes, supporting all 12 possible base substitutions as well as insertions (≤ 44 bp) and deletions (≤ 80 bp). In CAR-NK engineering, BE efficiently knocks out immunosuppressive receptors such as ADAM17, a sheddase responsible for cleaving cytokine receptors and activation antigens, thereby preserving IL-15Rα surface expression and enhancing NK cell persistence in vivo. Disruption of TGFβRII via adenine BE confers resistance to TGF-β-mediated suppression in TME, augmenting cytotoxicity against solid tumors.

Checkpoint genes like TIGIT and NKG2A are ideal targets for base editing given the DSB-free advantage. Importantly, disrupting these receptors in NK cells must be considered in the context of NK-specific signaling hubs: NKG2A signals via ITIM-mediated phosphatases whereas many NK activating receptors engage adaptors such as DAP10/DAP12 which recruit PI3K or Syk/ZAP70 family kinases. Thus, a BE-mediated loss of an inhibitory receptor (e.g., NKG2A) can shift the activation threshold in an adaptor-dependent manner and may synergize differently with a CAR that uses CD3ζ versus a DAP12 co-signaling domain. Practically, this means base/prime edits intended to ‘release brakes’ should be validated with assays that measure adaptor-specific downstream signaling (e.g., pSyk, pAKT), not only degranulation or IFN-γ readouts. BE-mediated ablation of TIGIT in iPSC-derived CAR-NK cells enhanced antitumor activity in ovarian cancer models by preventing TIGIT/CD155-mediated inhibition. Similarly, cytosine base editing of NKG2A disrupted HLA-E binding, potentiating cytotoxicity against HLA-E+ malignancies. For universal allogeneic CAR-NK products, simultaneous knockout of β2-microglobulin (B2M) and insertion of HLA-E transgenes via prime editing created hypoimmunogenic cells that evade host CD8+ T-cell and NK-cell surveillance. High-efficiency base- and prime-editing approaches have been reported in some hematopoietic contexts; however, biallelic editing rates in primary NK cells are variable and remain protocol-dependent. Moreover, cytosine BEs have been shown to induce transcriptome-wide RNA C→U edits in human cells, and PEs can produce low-frequency off-target events detectable by sensitive genome-wide methods, facts that mandate RNA-seq and unbiased genomic off-target mapping in the actual NK manufacturing workflow rather than relying solely on in silico predictions or surrogate cell lines. Finally, because delivery of base/prime editor RNPs or mRNA/pegRNA can damage fragile NK subsets, preclinical packages must quantify delivery-related viability loss, proliferation capacity and effector function post-editing. Thus, while prime/base editing can substantially improve targeted insertion or disruption without DSBs, published reports emphasize that editing efficiencies and biallelic rates must be empirically validated for each NK-cell platform rather than assumed to exceed 90%. To help the reader move from platform-specific editing technologies to translational objectives, it is useful to explicitly link each toolset to the three therapeutic pillars explored in this review. In practice, DSB-free nucleotide-level editors (BEs and PEs) and targeted integration platforms most directly enable Persistence by enabling safer, scar-minimizing disruption or precise insertion of autocrine cytokine armoring and immune-evasion loci (for example, IL-15 knock-ins or B2M/HLA-E modulation), lowering genotoxic stress while preserving proliferative fitness. Likewise, modular receptor and transposon strategies together with chemokine-receptor engineering primarily support Trafficking, allowing matched chemokine receptor expression and stromal-penetrating modules to be stably installed or transiently expressed to enhance homing to tumor niches. Finally, epigenetic reprogramming and synthetic biology circuits principally contribute to Tumor Eradication by enabling reversible tuning of activation/exhaustion programs and context-dependent payload release (e.g., NFAT-inducible cytokines, logic-gated CAR activation) that increase on-target potency while reducing systemic toxicity. By framing each technology with respect to these clinical objectives (persistence, trafficking, eradication), the review clarifies how orthogonal engineering modalities can be combined rationally to achieve complementary and clinically relevant outcomes.

Epigenetic reprogramming

Epigenetic reprogramming leverages engineered transcriptional activators, repressors, and chromatin modifiers to reshape the cellular state without altering the underlying DNA sequence; yet this approach is not risk-free. Persistent expression of dCas9-based activators/repressors can cause off-target transcriptional perturbations and select for clonal variants with growth advantage, and the durability of intended epigenetic changes is often unpredictable (transient in some contexts, stable in others). For NK cells, which have distinct epigenetic programs and sensitive subset-specific homeostasis, these uncertainties require single-cell transcriptomic and ATAC profiling, timecourse durability studies, and expansion/clonality monitoring during release testing. CRISPR-based interference (CRISPRi) and activation (CRISPRa) utilize nuclease-dead Cas9 (dCas9) fused to Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) repressors or transcriptional activators (e.g., VP64-p65-Rta) to silence or upregulate target genes. Targeted DNA demethylation employs dCas9-TET1 catalytic domains to remove methyl groups from CpG islands, reactivating epigenetically silenced loci. In CAR-NK cells, CRISPRa-mediated overexpression of endogenous activating receptors, such as NKG2D and DNAM-1, synergized with CAR signaling to enhance tumor recognition and eliminate antigen-heterogeneous solid tumors. This dual-targeting strategy mitigated antigen escape in glioblastoma xenografts, with NKG2D-augmented B7-H3 CAR-NK cells achieving complete regression in 80% of treated mice.

Sustained cytokine signaling is critical for NK cell persistence. Epigenetic activation of IL-15 or IL-21 genes via dCas9-VPR constructs promoted autocrine signaling, maintaining CAR-NK metabolic fitness and in vivo expansion without exogenous cytokine support. In contrast, CRISPRi-mediated silencing of exhaustion pathways, such as cytokine-inducible SH2-containing protein (CISH) and suppressors of cytokine signaling (SOCS), reversed NK cell anergy induced by chronic antigen exposure. Demethylation of the IFNG locus using dCas9-TET1 enhanced IFN-γ production, facilitating dendritic cell recruitment and TME remodeling in breast cancer PDX models. Notably, multiplexed epigenetic editing (e.g., simultaneous SOCS1 silencing and IL-15 activation) created a feed-forward loop that prolonged CAR-NK activity > 6 weeks in vivo. Targeted integration systems

Site-specific transgene integration overcomes the limitations of random viral vector insertion, which risks transgene silencing, variegated expression, and insertional mutagenesis. Recombinase-mediated cassette exchange (RMCE) exploits heterospecific loxP or FRT sites flanking genomic “landing pads” for recombinase-driven (Cre or Flp) CAR insertion at safe harbors like AAVS1 or CCR5. Transposon systems (piggyBac, Sleeping Beauty) and recombinase platforms (RMCE) differ from classical CRISPR-HDR knock-ins in that they do not rely on homology-directed repair at a DSB and therefore can deliver much larger, multicistronic cassettes with higher practical payload capacity (piggyBac has been used experimentally to carry multi-gene cassettes spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases). Complementing these, CRISPR-associated transposase systems (e.g., ShCAST) enable RNA-guided, site-directed insertions, a mechanistic middle path that combines programmability with targeted integration, overcoming the HDR size- and efficiency limits of Cas9-HDR. Note, however, that each system shifts the safety consideration: transposons and CAST require careful integration-site mapping and clonal tracking to avoid insertional activation of proto-oncogenes.

Targeted integration is indispensable for multicistronic constructs. Site-specific insertion of a CAR-IRES-IL-15 cassette at the AAVS1 locus enhanced NK cell expansion and antitumor efficacy against pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma by providing autocrine cytokine support. Similarly, RMCE-based co-expression of a CXCR4 chemokine receptor and CD19 CAR improved NK cell infiltration into bone marrow niches, reducing leukemic burden in extramedullary AML models by 95% compared to conventional CAR-NK cells. The modularity of these systems was exemplified by a “plug-and-play” piggyBac platform incorporating a CAR, membrane-bound IL-15 (mbIL-15), and a CD16 Fc receptor, simultaneously targeting tumor antigens, supporting survival, and enabling antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC). This tri-cistronic configuration achieved synergistic tumor control in HER2+ breast cancer xenografts, with integrated mbIL-15 extending CAR-NK persistence > 4-fold. By enabling stable, large-cassette insertions (e.g., CAR+mbIL-15), targeted integration therefore primarily supports the ‘Persistence’ pillar described below.

Synthetic biology circuits

Synthetic circuits confer precise spatiotemporal control over CAR-NK activity through Boolean logic gates and inducible systems. AND-gate CARs require dual antigen recognition for activation, minimizing on-target/off-tumor toxicity. SynNotch receptors, synthetic transcription factors activated by tumor antigen A (e.g., EGFR), induce expression of a CAR against tumor antigen B (e.g., IL-13Rα2), restricting cytotoxicity to double-positive cells. In glioblastoma models, SynNotch-regulated EGFR→IL-13Rα2 CAR-NK cells eliminated heterogeneous tumors while sparing normal brain tissue expressing single antigens. Alternatively, inhibitory NOT gates employ HLA-G-sensing scFvs to suppress CAR signaling upon engagement with HLA-G+ healthy tissues, providing a safeguard for organs like placenta and immune-privileged sites. Whereas a CRISPR knock-in into a constitutive locus typically yields static expression and risks tonic signaling, synthetic circuits (SynNotch, SUPRA CAR, DARIC, hypoxia-responsive promoters) enable context-dependent activation and drug-controllable OFF switches, therefore outperforming static promoter-driven knock-ins in safety (reduced off-tumor activation) and in situ dose control. This is not simply an implementation detail, dynamic circuits can be used to minimize systemic cytokine exposure while delivering concentrated payloads within TME.

Pharmacological and microenvironmental regulation further enhances safety. Rapamycin-regulated dimerization agent-regulated immunoreceptor complex (DARIC) systems conditionally assemble CAR signaling domains upon rapamycin administration, enabling reversible activation pauses to manage cytokine release. Hypoxia-responsive CARs incorporate HIF-1α-stabilized promoters or oxygen-labile degradation domains, restricting activity to the hypoxic TME. In head and neck cancer models, CAR-NK cells with hypoxia-inducible CD70 expression exhibited TME-specific activation, reduced exhaustion, and complete tumor regression in 70% of mice. More sophisticated circuits integrate multiple inputs, a NOT-gate CAR inhibiting PD-L1+ healthy cells combined with an AND-gate (EGFR+EpCAM+) circuit achieved tumor-specific IL-12 secretion, remodeling immunosuppressive microenvironments without systemic toxicity. Because synthetic circuits control where and when payloads are delivered, they are particularly valuable for realizing the ‘Tumor Eradication’ pillar without systemic toxicity. Toolbox limitations and risk-control considerations

While the Next-gen toolbox offers powerful new capabilities, several shared limitations require attention during preclinical development and clinical translation. First, delivery and payload constraints vary by platform: BEs/PEs, transposases, and large-fusion epigenetic effectors require optimized RNP, mRNA, or donor templates for primary NK cells and often show cell-type dependent toxicity, low biallelic rates, and subset-specific viability loss (electroporation can preferentially deplete CD56bright NKs). Second, persistent expression of dCas9-based editors can cause off-target transcriptional perturbations and clonal drift; transient modalities (mRNA, RNP, destabilized domains) are therefore preferred. Third, targeted integration approaches (transposons, RMCE, AAV-HDR) reduce random insertion risk but still need careful integration-site mapping and long-term genotoxicity assessment (including karyotyping and long-read sequencing) as part of release testing. Fourth, complex multi-module constructs amplify regulatory and potency testing demands, sponsors should adopt Quality-by-Design (QbD) and early regulator engagement to define CQAs that tie genetic changes to function. Finally, risk-control strategies such as suicide/safety switches, pharmacologic off-ramps (rapamycin-regulated systems), and hypoxia- or TME-restricted promoters can mitigate systemic toxicity but must themselves be validated for leakiness and durability.

Engineering solutions to key clinical challenges

Enhancing persistence

A paramount limitation of conventional CAR-NK cell therapy is their transient in vivo persistence, often succumbing to cytokine withdrawal, metabolic constraints, and replicative senescence within the immunosuppressive TME. Endogenous cytokine armoring represents a transformative strategy, where knock-in membrane-bound cytokines provide autocrine signaling. Genome editing enables the precise integration of mbIL-15 or IL-21 into safe genomic loci (e.g., AAVS1), substantially enhancing NK cell survival and proliferative capacity without exogenous cytokine support. Preclinical studies indicate that mbIL-15 can increase CAR-NK cell expansion and in-vivo persistence relative to unmodified controls; reported magnitudes are model-dependent. IL-21 co-expression further promotes a stem-like memory phenotype, reducing exhaustion markers such as TIM-3 and TIGIT.

Metabolism in NK cells has activation-dependent features that differ from T cells and that directly influence how metabolic engineering will perform. Resting NK cells rely largely on glucose-driven oxidative phosphorylation, but upon NKR or cytokine (IL-12/15/18) stimulation they rapidly upregulate glycolysis and mitochondrial respiration to fuel cytotoxicity and cytokine production. Therefore, strategies such as CPT1A overexpression (to favor FAO) may be most beneficial in glucose-poor TMEs, whereas enhancing glycolytic flux (e.g., via PFKFB3 or mTOR modulation) could be needed for short-term effector bursts. These distinctions matter because upstream editing (base/prime edits or epigenetic activation of metabolic genes) will have different efficacy and toxicity depending on whether the NK product is intended for chronic persistence or acute high-kill function. Preclinical models should therefore report both glycolytic (ECAR) and OXPHOS (OCR) readouts, together with in-vivo persistence metrics, to show the engineered metabolic program matches the intended clinical use case.

Epigenetic remodeling of memory-like programs offers a promising avenue for durable immunity. Targeted demethylation of IFNG and activation of transcription factors such as FOXO1 are attractive because they tap into mechanisms that underlie NK ‘adaptive’ or memory-like states. Studies of adaptive NK subsets (for example CMV-expanded NKG2Chi NKs) demonstrate stable changes in chromatin accessibility and DNA methylation at effector loci and receptor genes that are preserved long-term, indicating true epigenetic imprinting rather than transient activation. Epigenetic editors (dCas9-TET1, CRISPRa) therefore offer a way to impose memory-like programs, but they must be applied with cell-type specificity: NK epigenetic remodeling often targets distinct regulatory elements compared with T cell memory (for example, NK-specific accessible sites at IFNG and PRF1) and can be clonally persistent. Preclinical work shows that multiplexed epigenetic reprogramming can extend NK persistence and recall, but also that off-target transcriptional perturbation is a key safety concern requiring single-cell ATAC/RNA profiling in preclinical packages. iPSC-derived NK cells epigenetically programmed with memory signatures exhibit 8-week persistence in primates, outperforming unmodified counterparts.

Optimizing trafficking & infiltration

Inefficient homing to tumor sites remains a critical barrier for solid tumor eradication. Chemokine receptor matching tailors CAR-NK cells to tumor-specific chemokine gradients. For instance, CCR2 overexpression enhances migration toward CCL2-expressing tumors and has been repeatedly shown to improve tumor homing and disease control in multiple preclinical solid-tumor models; similarly, CCR2/CXCR6 and other receptor matches increase CAR immune-effector accumulation in TME niches. Recent systematic work and translational studies confirm and extend the original CCR2 observations, including context-dependent benefits across tumor types. In orthotopic glioblastoma models, CXCR3-engineered CAR-NK cells exhibit 4-fold increased tumor accumulation compared to controls, mediated by CXCL9/10/11 gradients. Transcriptomic profiling of patient tumors enables personalized chemokine receptor selection, optimizing site-specific trafficking.

Extracellular matrix (ECM)-degrading enzymes overcome stromal barriers that physically sequester tumors. Co-expression of hyaluronidase (PH20) or matrix metalloproteinase-14 (MMP14) via non-viral transposon systems enables CAR-NK cells to degrade hyaluronic acid and collagen I/III, enhancing penetration into pancreatic and ovarian carcinomas. PH20-armored CAR-NK cells reduce tumor interstitial pressure by 40% in desmoplastic models, facilitating T-cell infiltration and synergizing with checkpoint inhibitors.

Vascular targeting promotes endothelial adhesion and transendothelial migration. Overexpression of integrin αVβ3, a receptor for fibronectin and vitronectin, enhances binding to tumor vasculature under shear stress. Overexpression of integrin αVβ3 has been reported to increase endothelial adhesion and promote enhanced transendothelial migration in microfluidic and in-vitro shear-stress models; reported effect sizes vary across studies and platforms, indicating model-dependent improvements in adhesion and extravasation rather than a single universal fold-change. Combinatorial approaches, such as coupling αVβ3 with VEGF-C silencing (via shRNA), further normalize aberrant vasculature to improve TME access.

Boosting tumor eradication

Overcoming antigen heterogeneity and immunosuppression requires multifaceted strategies. Secreted bispecific engagers bridge CAR-NK cells with endogenous immunity. For example, CAR-NK cells secreting CD16×CD33 bispecific engagers have been shown to recruit endogenous NK activity and substantially increase in-vitro tumor cell lysis compared with controls; the magnitude of enhancement varies by assay conditions and construct design. Similarly, EGFRvIII-targeted CAR-NK cells secreting CD16 × HER2 engagers activate ADCC against antigen-heterogeneous glioblastomas, eliminating 95% of dual-negative tumor cells in coculture assays.

Immunomodulatory payloads remodel the TME. Inducible expression of IL-12 under NFAT-responsive promoters enhances dendritic cell activation and Th1 polarization, while minimizing systemic toxicity. CAR-NK cells engineered with PD-1-blocking nanobodies (e.g., anti-PD-1 VHH) exhibit dual functionality: direct cytotoxicity via CAR engagement and checkpoint disruption within the TME. In syngeneic lung cancer models, PD-1 VHH-secreting CAR-NK cells reduce tumor burden by 78% and increase tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells 4-fold.

Oncolytic virus (OV) delivery leverages CAR-NK cells as tumor-seeking vectors for localized virotherapy. Armoring with conditionally replicating adenoviruses (e.g., Delta-24-RGD) enables intratumoral virus amplification upon CAR-mediated tumor recognition. OV-loaded CAR-NK cells induce immunogenic cell death, releasing DAMPs and TAAs that stimulate dendritic cell cross-priming. In mesothelioma xenografts, this approach achieves 60% complete regression and establishes systemic anti-tumor immunity. Clinical translation, leading trials, and regulatory/manufacturing challenges

The transition from preclinical proof-of-concept to clinically viable next-generation CAR-NK therapies necessitates navigating a complex landscape of manufacturing hurdles, safety considerations, and regulatory frameworks. Pioneering clinical trials are now evaluating multiplexed-edited CAR-NK products, illuminating both the transformative potential and formidable challenges of these advanced therapeutics. FT596 (NCT04245722) is an iPSC-derived, off-the-shelf CAR-NK product reported by Fate Therapeutics and described in the first-in-human report. The product’s publicly described modifications include a CD19 CAR and an engineered high-affinity Fc receptor (hnCD16); further details on editing approaches and clinical outcomes are reported in the phase I publication and sponsor disclosures. Its off-the-shelf nature, coupled with the avoidance of DSBs, represents a significant leap in manufacturing scalability and safety. Similarly, CYNK-101 (NCT05207722) is a placental CD34+-derived NK product under clinical evaluation; manufacturer and preclinical reports indicate an IL-15-related engineering strategy that aims to augment persistence and ADCC in combination with trastuzumab. These trials exemplify the clinical application of beyond-CRISPR tools, moving beyond simple gene disruption to targeted epigenetic modulation and base editing. To support translation of multi-gene, base/prime edits and synthetic circuits, sponsors should implement a risk-based, orthogonal safety testing framework that explicitly addresses (A) genotoxicity and integration-site risk, (B) off-target DNA & transcriptome effects, and (C) immunogenicity of editing/delivery components. For genotoxicity, combine cytogenetic analyses (karyotype/array-CGH), long-read or deep short-read whole-genome sequencing (WGS) for structural variants and undesired rearrangements, and targeted integration-site mapping (LAM-PCR/amplicon sequencing or integration-capture assays) to quantify insertional preferences and proximity to oncogenes (ICH/EMA recommendations). For off-target assessment, adopt a multi-tier strategy that includes in-vitro unbiased biochemical/selection methods (e.g., CHANGE-seq/CIRCLE-seq/SITE-Seq) to nominate candidate off-target loci, followed by sensitive cellular assays (GUIDE-seq/DISCOVER-Seq) and targeted deep sequencing in the actual manufacturing cell type (primary NK or iPSC-derived NK) to quantify edit frequencies; for BEs, complement DNA analysis with transcriptome-wide RNA-seq to detect RNA deamination events and off-target SNVs. Finally, immunogenicity testing should assess pre-existing and treatment-induced humoral and cellular responses to bacterial nucleases, fusion proteins, and viral/non-viral delivery vehicles (ELISA/TAb/NAb panels, ELISpot/cytokine assays), as well as in-vitro cytokine release and innate activation assays for LNPs/VLPs; assay design and interpretation should follow current regulatory recommendations for gene and cell therapy immunogenicity assessment (Table 6).

Despite this momentum, critical technical hurdles persist. The delivery efficiency of large editing complexes, such as Cas9-transposase fusions (e.g., CAST/V-K systems) or multipart base editor ribonucleoproteins (RNPs), remains suboptimal in primary NK cells, often resulting in low HDR rates, cargo size limitations, and significant cellular toxicity. NK cells, particularly those from peripheral blood, exhibit intrinsic resistance to electroporation and viral transduction, necessitating further optimization of non-viral delivery platforms like lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) or virus-like particles (VLPs) tailored to NK cell biology. Furthermore, the long-term safety of epigenetic modulators is a paramount concern. Sustained expression of CRISPRa/i systems (dCas9-VPR/dCas9-KRAB) or engineered transcription factors raises the specter of off-target transcriptional dysregulation, cellular exhaustion, or malignant transformation, as evidenced by aberrant clonal expansion in in-vitro cultures of edited cells. The recent identification of CREM as a central transcriptional regulator of NK cell exhaustion downstream of CAR and IL-15 signaling underscores the complexity of the epigenetic landscape; while its deletion enhances functionality, unintended consequences of such permanent manipulations require rigorous long-term follow-up. Scalability of multiplexed engineering presents another formidable bottleneck. Manufacturing processes capable of introducing 4 + genetic modifications, e.g., B2M knockout, HLA-E knock-in, CD47 overexpression, CAR integration, and cytokine armoring, while maintaining > 90% purity, viability, and potency, demand closed, automated, and GMP-compliant platforms that are only now in development. The inherent variability in differentiation efficiency and editing efficacy across iPSC clones further complicates the path to a standardized, off-the-shelf product.

These technical challenges are compounded by evolving regulatory considerations for genetically modified NK cells. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and European Medicines Agency (EMA) have issued comprehensive guidelines for Advanced Therapy Medicinal Products (ATMPs), emphasizing the need for rigorous characterization of editing specificity, vector integration sites, and long-term stability of genetic modifications. The FDA’s recent draft guidance on “Potency Assurance for Cellular and Gene Therapy Products” (December 2023) mandates robust potency assays that quantitatively link specific genetic modifications to biological function, a non-trivial requirement for products with 5 + engineered attributes. Furthermore, the EMA’s reflection paper on “Quality, non-clinical and clinical aspects of medicinal products containing genetically modified cells” (CAT/CHMP/GTWP/671639/2008) requires extensive off-target assessment for CRISPR-based editors, including whole-genome sequencing (WGS) of edited clinical batches to exclude karyotypic abnormalities. For products involving transposons or lentiviral vectors, detailed analysis of integration sites and potential genotoxic risk is obligatory, adding layers of complexity to preclinical development. The environmental risk assessment (ERA) for genetically modified organisms (GMOs) also necessitates containment strategies for edited cells, influencing trial design and manufacturing logistics. Demonstrating product comparability after manufacturing process changes, addressed in the FDA’s July 2023 draft guidance, is particularly daunting for complex edited products, as minor alterations in electroporation conditions or guide RNA design can drastically alter the final cellular phenotype. Consequently, sponsors must adopt a Quality-by-Design (QbD) framework early in development, integrating advanced analytics like single-cell multi-omics and long-read sequencing to deconvolute product heterogeneity and establish critical quality attributes (CQAs) that correlate with clinical efficacy and safety. As the field advances toward increasingly sophisticated polygenically engineered CAR-NK cells, close and early dialogue with regulators will be indispensable to navigate this uncharted therapeutic frontier.

Emerging frontiers

The field of CAR-NK cell therapy is undergoing rapid evolution beyond CRISPR-based gene editing, driven by complementary advances in delivery, computational design, and manufacturing. These innovations collectively address fundamental challenges in CAR-NK therapy, including cellular persistence, targeted trafficking, and sustainable antitumor efficacy, while potentially overcoming the economic and logistical constraints of ex vivo cell manufacturing. The convergence of these disciplines is fostering a new generation of off-the-shelf and patient-specific NK cell therapies with enhanced functional properties and reduced toxicity profiles. This section examines the cutting-edge developments in lipid nanoparticle-mediated mRNA delivery, adeno-associated viral vectors for targeted reprogramming, AI-predicted CAR structures, and closed-system manufacturing platforms that are poised to reshape the clinical translation of CAR-NK therapies. The integration of these technologies represents a paradigm shift from incremental improvements to fundamental reimagining of how immune cells can be engineered for cancer immunotherapy, potentially expanding treatment options to solid tumors and refractory malignancies that have previously resisted cellular therapies.

In vivo CAR-NK engineering: lipid nanoparticle (LNP)-delivered mRNA for transient CAR expression and AAV vectors for targeted in vivo reprogramming

The paradigm of ex vivo cell manipulation is rapidly evolving toward more direct in vivo engineering approaches that could potentially bypass the complex logistics of cell manufacturing, storage, and transportation. LNPs are promising, but important limitations temper enthusiasm for direct in vivo NK reprogramming: conventional LNPs are strongly hepatotropic (dominant liver uptake), and engineering extra-hepatic or NK-specific tropism is still a major technical hurdle; moreover, pre-existing or induced anti-lipid and innate immune activation can limit repeat dosing or reduce persistence. While antibody-conjugated LNPs and capsid-engineered AAVs show proof-of-principle for immune-cell targeting in mice, robust, selective NK-cell tropism and scalable, low-immunogenicity formulations remain to be validated in non-human primates before human translation. Finally, because NKs are a minority population in blood, efficient in vivo transfection often requires high doses that increase off-target exposure and immunostimulatory risk. Recent advancements have demonstrated that systematically administered LNPs can effectively deliver CAR-encoding mRNA to lymphoid tissues and immune cells, generating functional CAR expressions without the need for ex vivo cell processing. Unlike viral vectors that mediate permanent genetic modification, LNP-delivered mRNA offers transient but high-level CAR expression, potentially reducing the risks of long-term toxicity and genotoxicity associated with viral integration. The transient nature of mRNA-based CAR expression may be particularly advantageous for managing CRS and other acute toxicities, as the therapeutic activity can be naturally terminated through mRNA degradation. However, a significant challenge has been achieving efficient delivery to specific immune cell subsets rather than hepatotropic accumulation, which has characterized first-generation LNP platforms.

Novel LNP formulations with extrahepatic tropism are addressing this limitation through strategic composition changes and surface modifications. For instance, ionizable lipids with distinct pKa values and hydrocarbon tail structures can alter tissue distribution patterns, favoring splenic accumulation over hepatic sequestration. More recently, researchers have developed antibody-conjugated LNPs (Ab-LNPs) that actively target pan-T cell or NK-specific surface markers, achieving significantly enhanced transfection efficiency in target cells. A groundbreaking study demonstrated that anti-CD7 and anti-CD3 bis-targeted LNPs could deliver minicircle DNA and transposase mRNA to primary T cells, generating stable CAR-expressing cells with potent antitumor activity in vivo. This approach, termed NCtx, represents the first LNP-based system capable of facilitating genomic CAR integration through the Sleeping Beauty transposon system, combining the advantages of non-viral delivery with permanent genetic modification. The same principle could be applied to NK cells by incorporating targeting moieties against NK-specific markers such as CD56, NKG2D, or NKp46, potentially enabling in vivo generation of CAR-NK cells without the need for preconditioning chemotherapy or lymphodepletion. Recent preclinical work provides early experimental support for this concept while also highlighting current limitations. Antibody-conjugated LNPs (Ab-LNPs) have been shown to transfect lymphocytes in vivo and generate functional CAR-expressing T cells using an ionizable-lipid LNP + transposase strategy, demonstrating that targeted non-viral platforms can produce stable, functional CAR immune effectors in situ. Importantly, several studies have now reported successful LNP-based transfection of NK cells ex-vivo and generation of CAR-expressing NK effectors using mRNA-LNP platforms, indicating that NK cells are amenable to LNP-mediated delivery under optimized formulations. At the same time, NK-specific in vivo tropism for systemic LNPs or AAV remains less well-characterized than for T cells; AAV capsid engineering and directed evolution are promising routes to generate vectors with improved immune-cell tropism but require further optimization to avoid dominant hepatic uptake and to minimize innate immune recognition. Taken together, these data indicate feasibility (proof-of-principle for in-vivo non-viral CAR generation in lymphocytes and LNP transfection of NK cells ex-vivo), but also justify the cautious wording used in this review, further work is needed to demonstrate robust, selective in vivo CAR expression specifically in endogenous NK populations and to define dosing, repeatability and safety in preclinical non-human primate models before clinical translation.

Parallel advancements in AAV capsid engineering and promoter selection have yielded tissue- and cell-targeted AAVs for direct in-vivo reprogramming in preclinical models, illustrating how capsid/promoter combinations can confer cell-type selectivity that may be adapted to NK-targeting strategies. Directed evolution, rational design and machine-learning approaches are actively being deployed to generate immune-cell-targeted capsids; however, as of January 2026 no peer-reviewed study has conclusively validated an AAV capsid with robust, selective NK-cell tropism in human tissues. Therefore, while AAV-based in-vivo NK reprogramming is a promising avenue, it remains an active area of capsid discovery and validation requiring additional in-vivo and immunogenicity testing. The development of AAV vectors with specific tropism for cardiac fibroblasts demonstrates the potential for tissue-specific targeting, achieved through capsid screening and tissue-specific promoter selection. Similarly, AAV-DJ capsids containing periostin promoters demonstrated strong and specific transgene expression in resident cardiac fibroblasts, suggesting that comparable strategies could yield AAV vari ants with NK-specific tropism. The application of AAV vectors for in vivo reprogramming extends beyond simple gene delivery to complex cellular reprogramming processes, as evidenced by studies demonstrating direct in vivo reprogramming of fibroblasts into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSC) or cardiomyocytes using polycistronic factor expression cassettes.

The combination of LNPs and AAVs presents a particularly intriguing approach for in vivo CAR-NK generation. LNPs could be utilized for transient expression of reprogramming factors that convert endogenous immune cells into NK-like cells, while AAVs could provide stable CAR expression through targeted integration. Alternatively, LNPs could deliver CRISPR components for gene disruption while AAVs serve as donor templates for knock-in strategies, leveraging the strengths of both delivery systems. A recent innovation demonstrating this combinatorial approach utilized LNPs to deliver mRNA encoding a CAR construct and SB100x transposase, resulting in genomic integration and durable CAR expression in vivo. This system achieved robust tumor control in humanized mouse models of B-cell leukemia, suggesting similar strategies could be effective for NK cell engineering. Importantly, in vivo approaches may preserve critical NK cell subsets that are lost during ex vivo manipulation, particularly the CD56bright population that exhibits enhanced cytokine production and proliferative capacity but is notoriously fragile in ex vivo culture conditions. Despite the promise of in vivo engineering approaches, significant challenges remain in achieving specific and efficient gene delivery to NK cells without off-target effects. Immunogenicity of both LNPs and AAVs may limit repeated administration, particularly in pre-sensitized individuals. Additionally, the relatively low abundance of NK cells in peripheral blood presents a targeting challenge that may require preliminary expansion or recruitment steps. Future developments in synthetic biology and delivery engineering will need to focus on enhancing the specificity and efficiency of these platforms while maintaining safety profiles compatible with clinical application. The ideal system would combine the precision of antibody-based targeting with the efficiency of viral transduction and the favorable safety profile of non-viral delivery, potentially through hybrid systems that leverage the advantages of multiple technological approaches.

Computational design: artificial intelligence (AI)-predicted CAR structures and optimal gene circuits for enhanced NK cell function

The rational design of CAR constructs has traditionally relied on iterative experimental optimization, a time-consuming and resource-intensive process that often produces suboptimal configurations. AI-driven structural and de-novo protein design approaches now enable rational generation of hyperstable binding domains and CAR architectures with improved expression and reduced aggregation, providing a scalable path to optimized bispecific and tandem CARs. AI-informed approaches to CAR design are particularly valuable for creating bispecific CARs that simultaneously target multiple tumor antigens, addressing the critical challenge of antigen escape in cancer therapy. Scientists at St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital recently developed a computational pipeline that screens thousands of theoretical tandem CAR designs and ranks top candidates based on structural and biophysical features, including protein folding stability, aggregation propensity, and surface expression potential. This approach successfully generated optimized tandem CARs targeting B7-H3 and IL-13Rα2 for pediatric brain tumors, overcoming the poor surface expression and suboptimal killing ability that had plagued previous designs. The AI-optimized constructs cleared heterogeneous tumors in four out of five mice, outperforming single-targeted CARs that failed to control antigen-loss variants.

The application of AI extends beyond CAR design to the creation of sophisticated gene circuits that program precise cellular behaviors in response to complex environmental cues. These circuits enable CAR-NK cells to perform Boolean logic operations (AND, OR, NOT) based on multiple input signals, dramatically enhancing their specificity for tumor cells while sparing healthy tissues. Among the most advanced systems are SUPRA CARs, SynNotch, and Co-LOCKR, each offering unique advantages for implementing conditional activation and multi-antigen recognition. SUPRA CARs employ a split universal receptor system with leucine-zipper domains that allow tunable activation through adaptor protein titration, while SynNotch receptors utilize proteolytic cleavage domains to link antigen recognition to programmable transcriptional outputs. Co-LOCKR represents perhaps the most sophisticated approach, employing computationally designed “cage” and “key” proteins that undergo conformational changes only when colocalized on a target cell surface, enabling up to three-input antigen sensing. These systems could be particularly valuable for NK cells, which naturally recognize multiple activating and inhibitory signals in their environment.

The integration of AI approaches with structural biology and molecular dynamics has enabled de novo protein design, creating entirely new protein structures not found in nature with prescribed shapes and functions. This capability is particularly relevant for designing novel antigen-recognition domains that overcome the limitations of conventional scFv fragments, which often exhibit instability, aggregation, and immunogenicity. AI-based protein design algorithms trained on vast datasets of protein sequences and structures can now generate hyperstable binding domains with tailored specificities, potentially enabling targeting of previously inaccessible epitopes or the creation of “universal” epitopes recognized by engineered binders. These approaches can optimize not only binding affinity but also structural features that promote efficient cell surface expression and signaling, addressing common problems in CAR design such as tonic signaling and exhaustion. Experimental validation of AI-optimized CAR architectures is advancing rapidly in T-cell and preclinical tumor models (for example, computationally optimized tandem CARs that rescued surface expression and improved in-vivo efficacy), but direct demonstrations of AI-designed CARs validated specifically in NK cells in vivo remain limited to date; accordingly, AI predictions should be framed as powerful design tools that still require NK-directed experimental confirmation. For NK cells specifically, computational design must account for their unique biology and signaling requirements, which differ substantially from T cells. Unlike T cells, NK cells utilize a complex array of activating and inhibitory receptors that integrate signals through distinct intracellular domains, particularly those incorporating immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs (ITAMs). In practice this means computational pipelines should evaluate inclusion of NK-adaptors (DAP10 for NKG2D-like biology, DAP12 for certain activating receptors, 2B4 as a co-stimulatory/signal-modifying domain) and simulate downstream adaptor recruitment and kinase activation (PI3K vs. Syk/ZAP70 pathways) because CARs that pair poorly with NK adaptors can induce tonic signaling or fail to engage cytotoxic machinery. Benchmarks for NK-optimized CARs should therefore include adaptor-proximal phosphorylation (pSyk/pAKT) in addition to expression and killing assays. AI algorithms can help optimize the incorporation of NK-specific signaling domains such as DAP10, DAP12, CD3ζ, and 2B4 to enhance persistence and functionality while minimizing exhaustion. Additionally, computational approaches can model the optimal spacing and orientation between these domains to maximize signal transduction and minimize aberrant signaling. The development of NK-optimized gene circuits is particularly promising, as it could enable tissue-specific homing, environmental sensing (e.g., hypoxia response in tumors), and self-regulation through feedback control mechanisms that automatically dampen activation after target elimination.

The future of computational design in CAR-NK therapy will likely involve increasingly sophisticated multi-scale models that integrate molecular-level structural predictions with cellular-level signaling networks and population-level dynamics. Such models could potentially predict not only how a CAR will express and function individually but how CAR-NK cells will interact with tumor cells, TME, and other immune components in vivo. This systems-level approach is essential for addressing the complex challenges of solid tumor immunotherapy, where barriers to infiltration, immunosuppressive microenvironments, and metabolic constraints limit efficacy. By combining AI-driven design with high-throughput experimental validation, the field can accelerate the development of next-generation CAR-NK therapies with enhanced specificity, persistence, and safety profiles tailored to particular cancer types and anatomical locations.

Automated manufacturing: closed-system, good manufacturing practice (GMP)-compatible editing platforms with next-generation tools

The translation of advanced CAR-NK therapies from research concepts to clinically viable products requires manufacturing solutions that address the limitations of current manual processes, including variability, contamination risk, high costs, and limited scalability. Automated closed-system platforms are emerging as critical enablers of robust, reproducible, and economically feasible cell therapy production. These systems integrate multiple processing steps, from cell selection and activation to genetic modification and expansion, within a single, sterile fluidic pathway that minimizes operator intervention and open processing steps. The CliniMACS Prodigy® system (Miltenyi Biotec) represents a pioneering example, offering fully automated cell culture, expansion, and genetic modification in a GMP-compatible platform. Recent enhancements have incorporated electroporation capabilities, enabling automated CRISPR-Cas9 editing of hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells with high efficiency and viability. This platform successfully achieved efficient editing of the BCL11A enhancer in CD34+ cells, the same target used in clinical trials for β-thalassemia and sickle cell disease, with minimal manual processing steps.

The advantages of automated systems extend beyond consistency and sterility to include advanced process monitoring and control through integrated sensors and analytical technologies. These Process Analytical Technologies (PATs) enable real-time monitoring of critical quality attributes such as cell density, viability, glucose consumption, and metabolite accumulation, allowing for dynamic adjustment of culture conditions to optimize product quality. Additionally, automated systems facilitate comprehensive data tracking and documentation, streamlining the regulatory approval process through complete traceability of materials, processes, and environmental conditions. This capability is particularly valuable for cell therapies, where product attributes are highly sensitive to processing parameters and difficult to characterize through release testing alone. The implementation of Quality by Design (QbD) principles in automated platforms allows for systematic process optimization and identification of critical process parameters that influence critical quality attributes.

For CAR-NK manufacturing specifically, automated platforms must accommodate the unique expansion requirements of NK cells, which differ from T cells in their cytokine requirements and expansion kinetics. While T cells typically require IL-2 and CD3/CD28 stimulation, NK cells expand optimally with combinations of IL-2, IL-15, IL-12, and IL-21, often with feeder cells such as K562-mbIL21. Next-generation automated systems are incorporating advanced bioreactor designs that support both suspension culture and bling optimized expansion of various NK cell sources, including peripheral blood, cord blood, and iPSCs. The integration of microfluidic cell processing allows for efficient transfection and genetic modification of even hard-to-transfect NK cells while maintaining high viability and function. These developments are particularly important for CRISPR-based engineering, which requires efficient delivery of RNP complexes without excessive toxicity.

The evolution of automated manufacturing is closely intertwined with advances in gene editing tools and delivery technologies. While early CAR therapies relied primarily on viral vectors for genetic modification, the field is increasingly adopting non-viral approaches such as electroporation of mRNA or DNA transposon systems, which offer advantages in cost, scalability, and safety profiles. Automated platforms are incorporating optimized electroporation technologies that achieve high transfection efficiency with minimal cell damage through precise control of electrical parameters and cell-specific pulse waveforms. Additionally, these systems are being adapted to accommodate emerging gene editing technologies beyond CRISPR-Cas9, including base editing, prime editing, and epigenetic editing, each of which has distinct manufacturing requirements. The flexibility to implement diverse genetic modification strategies within a single platform will be crucial as the field continues to evolve toward more sophisticated engineering approaches.

Looking forward, the convergence of automation, analytics, and artificial intelligence is poised to create intelligent manufacturing systems that not only execute predefined processes but continuously learn and optimize based on accumulated data. Machine learning algorithms can identify subtle correlations between process parameters and product outcomes that may not be apparent to human operators, enabling predictive modeling and control of cell quality attributes. These developments will be essential for scaling CAR-NK therapies to address larger patient populations and expanding indications beyond hematologic malignancies to solid tumors, which may require higher cell doses and more complex engineering strategies. By reducing costs, improving consistency, and enhancing scalability, automated manufacturing platforms will play a critical role in fulfilling the promise of off-the-shelf allogeneic CAR-NK therapies that can be produced in advance and administered to patients without the lengthy wait times associated with autologous therapies.

Conclusion and future perspectives

In summary, the reviewed tools do not simply supplement CRISPR-Cas9, they change the failure modes of genetic engineering. DSB-free nucleotide editors (base and prime) reduce DSB-related structural variants and p53-linked fitness loss; targeted transposases and transposon platforms overcome HDR size limits to permit stable multicistronic armor cassettes; and epigenetic/synthetic circuits replace static promoter control with dynamic, tumor-context dependent payload delivery. Together, these differences amount to a concrete set of advantages that address each element of the “CRISPR ceiling” and therefore justify the term “beyond CRISPR” in the this review. The integration of these approaches promises to overcome the limitations of current CAR-NK therapies by enhancing their precision, persistence, and practicality while reducing costs and complexity. In vivo engineering approaches could potentially eliminate the need for complex ex vivo manufacturing altogether, instead leveraging the body’s own cellular resources to generate therapeutic effects on demand. Computational design enables the creation of increasingly sophisticated cellular behaviors that mimic and extend natural immune recognition, potentially achieving unprecedented specificity against complex tumor antigen patterns. Automated manufacturing ensures that these advanced therapies can be produced consistently, safely, and at scales sufficient to address widespread clinical need.

The true transformative potential lies in the integration of these technological streams, creating a synergistic effect greater than the sum of individual advances. For example, AI-designed CAR constructs could be delivered via targeted LNP platforms manufactured at scale using automated systems, creating off-the-shelf therapeutics that combine optimal design with efficient production and delivery. Alternatively, in vivo reprogramming approaches could be guided by genetic circuits designed computationally to ensure precise spatial and temporal control over cellular modification. The future may see the development of “universal” CAR-NK cells that can be administered to any patient and subsequently programmed in vivo through adapted LNP or AAV delivery of target-specific recognition domains, creating a flexible platform adaptable to evolving tumor antigens and resistance mechanisms.

Despite the remarkable progress, significant challenges and unanswered questions remain. The long-term safety of in vivo engineering approaches requires thorough investigation, particularly regarding potential off-target effects and immune responses to delivery vehicles. The biological differences between NK cell subsets and sources necessitate tailored engineering approaches rather than direct translation from CAR-T technologies. Furthermore, the field must address the unique challenges of solid TMEs, which may require additional modifications to enhance CAR-NK cell infiltration, persistence, and function under metabolically challenging and immunosuppressive conditions. The development of appropriate preclinical models that faithfully recapitulate human disease and immune responses will be critical for evaluating these advanced therapies before clinical translation. As the field advances, it will be essential to maintain a balance between innovation and accessibility, ensuring that these sophisticated therapies remain attainable for patients across healthcare systems. The implementation of decentralized manufacturing approaches using automated platforms could potentially expand access beyond specialized academic centers to community hospitals, while in vivo engineering strategies may ultimately allow administration in outpatient settings similar to conventional biologics. With continued interdisciplinary collaboration across immunology, synthetic biology, bioengineering, and computational sciences, the next generation of CAR-NK therapies holds exceptional promise for transforming cancer treatment and improving outcomes for patients with currently refractory malignancies.
