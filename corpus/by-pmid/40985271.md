# Vitamin D-induced mechanisms in cancer prevention and therapy: Recent advances and future opportunities.

## Abstract

Vitamin D, with its diverse molecular pathways and immunomodulatory properties, has become a crucial tool in the prevention and treatment of various cancers. It controls angiogenesis, apoptosis, differentiation, and cellular proliferation, inhibiting cancer through immune surveillance, DNA repair, and tumor suppression genes. Additionally, vitamin D signaling impacts tumor growth and metastasis in various cancer types by interacting with key oncogenic pathways like Wnt/β-catenin, NF-κB, PI3K/Akt, and p53. This review demonstrates the molecular and therapeutic implications of vitamin D in oncology, focusing on its potential as a safe, adjuvant treatment method. It emphasizes the role of vitamin D in epigenetic modification, its impact on tumor microenvironment, and its synergistic benefits when combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors and chemotherapeutic drugs. Despite promising results, genetic variations in the VDR gene continue to cause issues with bioavailability, ideal dosage, and interindividual response variability. The review also proposes future research on vitamin D's potentiality as a therapeutic adjuvant in various malignancies, including colorectal, prostate, and breast cancers, and suggests the development of non-calcemic vitamin D analogs and the incorporation of vitamin D-based methods into personalized oncology treatments.

Vitamin D‐induced mechanisms in cancer prevention and therapy: Recent advances and future opportunities

Abstract

Vitamin D, with its diverse molecular pathways and immunomodulatory properties, has become a crucial tool in the prevention and treatment of various cancers. It controls angiogenesis, apoptosis, differentiation, and cellular proliferation, inhibiting cancer through immune surveillance, DNA repair, and tumor suppression genes. Additionally, vitamin D signaling impacts tumor growth and metastasis in various cancer types by interacting with key oncogenic pathways like Wnt/β‐catenin, NF‐κB, PI3K/Akt, and p53. This review demonstrates the molecular and therapeutic implications of vitamin D in oncology, focusing on its potential as a safe, adjuvant treatment method. It emphasizes the role of vitamin D in epigenetic modification, its impact on tumor microenvironment, and its synergistic benefits when combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors and chemotherapeutic drugs. Despite promising results, genetic variations in the VDR gene continue to cause issues with bioavailability, ideal dosage, and interindividual response variability. The review also proposes future research on vitamin D's potentiality as a therapeutic adjuvant in various malignancies, including colorectal, prostate, and breast cancers, and suggests the development of non‐calcemic vitamin D analogs and the incorporation of vitamin D‐based methods into personalized oncology treatments.

Vitamin D, traditionally known to promote calcium and bone health, is being explored for its role in cancer prevention and treatment through its influence on cell proliferation, differentiation, apoptosis, and immune response. The vitamin D receptor (VDR) regulates gene expression and interacts with key signaling pathways such as Wnt/β‐catenin, MAPK/ERK, and PI3K/Akt, playing a critical role in tumor suppression. Vitamin D enhances innate and adaptive immune responses, modifies the tumor microenvironment, and boosts antitumor immunity. Promising outcomes from studies linking supplementation with vitamin D or its analogs to cancer prevention and treatment suggest synergistic effects with conventional therapies. Research emphasizes optimizing personalized dosing, combination strategies, and targeted delivery systems for enhanced therapeutic efficacy. 

INTRODUCTION

Calcitriol, the most physiologically potent form of vitamin D, regulates calcium balance by exerting its effects on the intestines, bones, kidneys, and parathyroid glands. The hormone exerts its effects via the vitamin D receptor (VDR), which belongs to the nuclear receptor superfamily. VDR is found not just in cells and tissues responsible for calcium regulation, but also in a diverse range of other cells, including cancerous cells. Recently, it has been recognized that calcitriol has antiproliferative and differentiating actions on certain cancerous cells. Additionally, it has been observed to inhibit the genesis and progression of tumors in animal models, suggesting its potential as an anticancer drug. Vitamin D, found naturally in foods and synthesized in humans, has been linked to cancer prevention, prompting preclinical investigations to understand its potential impact. Numerous epidemiological studies and clinical trials have investigated the link between vitamin D levels and cancer risk. 

In addition, vitamin D is a necessary precursor of the steroid hormone calcitriol. It is produced in humans by the process of skin exposure to ultraviolet radiation, namely ultraviolet‐B (UVB) rays with a wavelength of 280–320 nm. Alternatively, it can also be obtained by consuming fortified foods or taking supplements. The hypothesis that vitamin D could potentially decrease the risk of cancer (Figure 1) is based on a study that found a correlation between insufficient sunlight exposure and a higher possibility of developing colon cancer. Vitamin D has been shown to possess anticancer actions. It reduced the risk of cancer incidence and mortality for 23 types of cancer, and an inverse correlation between serum 25‐hydroxyvitamin D and cancer incidence has been demonstrated. It also inhibits tumor cell proliferation, dedifferentiation, and invasion, and modulates stromal cell biology to interfere with the occurrence of metastasis. Vitamin D acts as a transcription factor, influencing tumorigenesis, growth, cell differentiation, and apoptosis. Exposure to sunlight and increased vitamin D levels have been related to reduced cancer occurrence and mortality. Recent research also explores the role of vitamin D receptor polymorphisms, such as Fok1, Bsm1, Taq1, Apa1, EcoRV, and Cdx2, in tumorigenesis. Calcitriol controls various signaling pathways related to cell growth, cell death, cell specialization, inflammation, cell invasion, blood vessel formation, and the spread of cancer cells. As a result, it can influence the development and progression of cancer. Calcitriol also controls the expression of microRNA and could potentially impact the biology of cancer stem cells. 

Vitamin D, crucial for cellular growth and development, can slow down malignant growth, and countries with high cancer rates should increase vitamin D supplementation to prevent cancer. Low vitamin D status is generally accepted as a link to increased cancer risk, with cell‐based studies showing that 1,25(OH)2D regulates gene transcription through the VDR. Vitamin D may also be involved in chemoprotective mechanisms, such as DNA repair, antioxidant protection, and immunomodulation, and may contribute to cancer prevention via various cell targets. The findings that vitamin D and its analogues may prevent malignant transformation and progression of human tumors suggest their potential clinical use as novel chemopreventive and anticancer agents. We also highlight the crucial role that vitamin D plays in epigenetic modification and the tumor microenvironment, and its synergistic benefits with immune checkpoint inhibitors and chemotherapeutic drugs. Genetic variations have been shown to cause issues with bioavailability and dosage, and future research should explore vitamin D therapeutic adjuvants.

1,25‐DIHYDROXYVITAMIN D3 SYNTHESIS IN THE BODY

When exposed to the sun's ultraviolet (UVB) radiation, the skin generates vitamin D (cholecalciferol) from 7‐dehydrocholesterol. Eggs, dairy products, and fatty fish are dietary sources of vitamin D, and supplements may also be consumed. The cytochrome P450 enzyme CYP2R1 hydroxylates vitamin D as it enters the body, turning it into the precursor metabolite Vitamin D3 25‐hydroxyl (calciferol; 25(OH)D3). 25(OH)D3 is the most prevalent form of vitamin D in the blood. Because of this, the value used to quantify a person's status of vitamin D is their serum concentration of the 25(OH)D3 metabolite. A serum 25(OH)D3 level below 20 ng/mL is considered to indicate vitamin D insufficiency by the Institute of Medicine (IOM), while the Endocrine Society defines insufficiency as <30 ng/mL. Further research has elucidated the role of extrarenal CYP27B1 expression, particularly in melanoma cells.Eventually, 25(OH)D3 and 25(OH)2D3 in the blood are bound by the protein that binds vitamin D (DBP), which can then 24‐hydroxylate and catabolize both substances to render them inactive. 

THE WAYS THAT VITAMIN D WORKS

Vitamin D not only affects transcription directly but also indirectly affects the genetic regulation of cell signaling pathways. Numerous in‐depth reviews have investigated the hypothesized mechanisms through which vitamin D fights cancer. In summary, as soon as 25 (OH)2D3 reaches the cell, it interacts with the VDR, the vitamin D receptor. This makes it possible for VDR and retinoic RXR (X Receptor) to heterodimerize in the nucleus. The VDR‐RXR‐dimer attaches to target genes containing vitamin D response elements (VDREs), which attract the required co‐modulators and RNA polymerase II to initiate transcription of the target genes. Inhibiting angiogenesis, triggering apoptosis, promoting differentiation, lowering inflammation, preventing invasion and metastasis, and inhibiting proliferation are just a few of the ways that vitamin D modulates transcription. In vitro experiments on cancer cell lines make it difficult to identify specific biological processes preventing vitamin D's molecular effects. Vitamin D may reduce angiogenesis, metastasis, and invasion in cancer development. Ras is produced as a result of vitamin D binding to 1,25(OH)phospholipase C (PLC) and PKC. Phosphorylation of Ras activates the RAF protein kinase, which activates MEK1/2 and MAPK1/2, which in turn activate the enzyme ERK, a mitogen‐activated protein kinase. Many of the effects of ERK‐MAPK1/2 activation on genomic control are also present in the direct transcriptional regulation by the 1,25(OH)2D3‐VDR complex. Using RNA‐Seq, a profile of 1,25(OH)2D3's primary transcriptional targets has been produced. After being exposed to 1,25(OH)2D3 for 3 h, 88 genes had significant up‐regulation, while 23 had significant down‐regulation. Apoptosis, endocytosis, adhesion, regulation of angiogenesis, hexose transport, actin cytoskeleton regulation, growth control, and immune modulation were early transcription targets. Other transcriptional aspects are also shown to be under 1,25(OH)2D3's control, which indirectly indicates that 1,25(OH)2D3 has wider effects on transcription. 

VITAMIN D SOURCES AND METABOLISM

Vitamin D is naturally present in very few foods. At the start of the 20th century, rickets caused over 80% of children in developed European North and Northeastern US towns to be handicapped. Vitamin D3 or D2 is commonly found in sun‐dried mushrooms and oily seafoods such as salmon, mackerel, herring, and cod liver oil. Nowadays the majority of cereals, breads, yogurts, cheeses, milk, and orange juice in the US are vitamin D‐fortified. Before restrictions were established to limit vitamin D fortification of milk and several additional food products in response to an outbreak of vitamin D poisoning, fortification of foods such as dairy products and custards was a popular practice in Europe. The effects of sunlight on the skin, primarily forming vitamin D3, account for 90%–95% of the benefits of vitamin D. UVB radiation, with wavelengths in the range of 290–315 nm, converts 7‐dehydrocholesterol into previtamin D3 in the epidermis and dermis. The previtamin D3 is rapidly converted to a D3 precursor vitamin by the body's temperature. Vitamin D3 is produced and released from the plasma membrane by binding to proteins in the extracellular space, and is converted into 25‐hydroxy [25(OH)D], the primary vitamin D produced by the liver. The hormone 1,25(OH)2D in the small intestine activates the vitamin D receptor, triggering the production of an epithelial calcium channel that transports calcium into the bloodstream. Additionally, RANKL, a receptor activator of the NF‐κB ligand, is produced by osteoblasts when their VDRs interact with 1,25(OH)2D. As a result, there is an increase in osteoclast precursor mobilization, which eventually leads to production of osteoclasts. To maintain calcium homeostasis, mature osteoclasts mobilize calcium reserves from the skeleton. 

MECHANISMS OF CALCITRIOL'S ANTICANCER EFFECTS

In vitro studies on malignant cells in culture have demonstrated that calcitriol possesses both pro‐differentiating and antiproliferative characteristics. Furthermore, in vivo research on cancer models in animals has demonstrated that calcitriol suppresses tumor growth. Numerous important pathways have been connected to calcitriol's anticancer effects. The molecular messengers involved in calcitriol's activities are currently the subject of in‐depth study and characterization. The anticancer activities of Vitamin D are shown in Figure 2.

Preventing invasion and metastasis

Calcitriol, found in mouse models of prostate and lung cancer, reduces the invasion and spread of numerous malignant cells. This effect is attributed to suppression of angiogenesis, a process that manipulates the synthesis of essential chemicals involved in metastasis and invasion like the elements of the matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and plasminogen activator (PA) systems, less Tenascin‐C expression is an extracellular matrix protein that encourages angiogenesis, invasion, and proliferation, the expression of integrins a6 and b4 is downregulated, and more of an increase in the cancer‐suppressing gene E‐cadherin, whose level is associated with a lower likelihood of metastatic spread. 

Arresting growth and differentiating it

Numerous cancerous cells are stopped from growing by calcitriol, which causes cell cycle arrest and a build‐up of cells in the G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle. Calcitriol was used in prostate cancer (PCa) cells to induce arrest at G1/G0. This procedure was dependent on the p53 gene. The main mechanism in this process involved an increase in the expression of cyclin‐dependent kinase inhibitors p21Waf/Cip1 and p27Kip1, which largely reduce CDK2's (cyclin‐dependent kinase 2) activity. Additionally, calcitriol causes retinoblastoma protein (pRb) hypophosphorylation. In addition to its effect on p53, calcitriol increases the expression of p73, a p53 homolog linked to apoptosis in various human and mouse tumors. The inhibition of p73 can decrease the cytotoxic effects of calcitriol on squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), potentially increasing the cytotoxic effects of medications. Additionally, cancer cells that are treated with calcitriol express more CDK inhibitors than control cancer cells. Calcitriol controls the manifestation and behavior of crucial growth elements, which in turn influence the proliferation of cancer cells. The presence of calcitriol in PCa cells leads to increased production of IGFBP‐3, which primarily reduces cell proliferation, partly through upregulating p21Waf/Cip1 expression. Calcitriol promotes differentiation in various neoplastic cells, leading to the development of more developed, less cancerous cells. Calcitriol's effect on cancer cell differentiation is influenced by signaling pathways, transcription factors, and NF‐κB activities, with specific effects depending on cell type. 

Apoptosis

Calcitriol increases apoptosis in various types of cancer cells. It enhances the apoptotic pathway in PCa and BC cells, promotes mitochondrial disease, releases cytochromes, and generates ROS, which is linked to the prevention of proteins that prevent apoptosis, such as Bcl2. Additionally, proapoptotic proteins like Bax and Bad are expressed more frequently. By immediately activating caspases, calcitriol also induces death in certain cells. Furthermore, it has been shown that calcitriol analogues increase the mortality of cancer cells in response to radiation and chemotherapy drugs. 

IMPACTS OF VITAMIN D ON VARIOUS TYPES OF CANCERS

Vitamin D is used for the prevention and treatment of various types of cancer (Table 1).

Vitamin D and prostate cancer

A rise in PCa incidence has been observed over the past 20 years, which is at least partially attributable to an aging population. PCa remains the third most prevalent cancer‐related cause of mortality in men, despite many effective treatment options. A study suggests that men with PCa perform better during summer when vitamin D blood levels and sunlight exposure are higher. Vitamin D may improve PCa prognosis in patients with higher serum calcitriol levels compared to those with levels below 18.2 and 20 ng. Studies have shown that vitamin D concentrations above 34 ng/mL are a poor prognostic indicator. A study has successfully linked variations in outcome, PCa risk, and polymorphisms to VDR. High calcium‐sensing receptor levels may not necessarily lead to worse prognosis in individuals with normal or high tumor VDR expression, but decreased expression has been linked to inferior outcomes. VDR SNPs are correlated with changes in grade and risk, and a person's susceptibility to PCa may be predicted by the VDR FokI polymorphism. MiRNAs 126‐3p, 154‐5p, and 21‐5p, as well as the miRNA processing ribonuclease DICER, are all regulated by VDR expression, which is also involved in the regulation of other miRNAs. Population research in Pakistan and Jordan has identified certain variants of VDR, some of which are protective and others linked to poorer prognosis. They were also associated with more aggressive forms of PCa in Pakistani populations. African‐American men have a higher incidence of PCa compared to European‐born men, often exhibiting more aggressive symptoms. Additionally, TaqI and ApaI VDR polymorphisms were found to be related to a worse result in the African population. 

Vitamin D and melanoma

One of the most immunogenic tumors is melanoma. Vitamin D's impact on melanoma is challenging to study, as sunlight exposure is a significant risk factor and a source of vitamin D. Despite the risk factor of sunburn, according to research by Shipman et al., sunlight has a preventative effect, and Gandini et al. and Hoel et al. discovered that the hazards come more from the type and wavelength of sunlight than from the extent of exposure. Research indicates that sun exposure generally benefits the skin, but it can also negatively impact melanoma and other skin cancer types. It improves conditions like psoriasis, eczema, vitiligo, and acne. According to research by Berwick et al., there is no evidence linking sun exposure to any types of skin cancer other than squamous cell carcinoma. Individuals living at higher latitudes where UVB exposure is reduced appear to have an increase in melanoma incidence, which may be connected to reduced vitamin D generation. Wyatt et al. found that a higher Breslow score indicates vitamin D insufficiency at diagnosis, but results may be lower if proper supplements are provided. In contrast, vitamin D blood levels were shown to be predictive during follow‐up but not at the time of melanoma diagnosis. Additionally, Wyatt et al. showed that increased vitamin D serum levels may result in a better prognosis and a lower tumor grade at diagnosis. Vitamin D interacts with both the VDR and the orphan retinoic acid receptors. It significantly reduces melanoma cancer line multiplication, enabling malignant cells to migrate, survive, and transition between different differentiation stages. 

Vitamin D and breast cancer

BC is the most prevalent type of cancer in women globally. The numerous molecular patterns of BC necessitate a unique approach to care, resulting in varying patient outcomes. Triple‐negative BC involving a lack of human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) is a challenging condition to cure due to the absence of HER2 from estrogen‐dependent and prostaglandin receptors. Ineffective medications for treating this type of BC inhibit hormones and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 treatment. Recent research indicates that the cytotoxic and proapoptotic peptide, cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide (CAMP), is reduced in individuals with triple‐negative BC. Patients with estrogen‐negative tumors produce less CAMP, but calcitriol may stimulate production. Vitamin D significantly influences cancer stem cell development in triple‐negative BC by reducing CD44, a glycoprotein linked to cancer growth and metastasis. Ca2+‐activated K+ channels, often found in high‐grade tumors, are a significant feature of a poor prognosis in BC. Vitamin D and its analogs effectively reduce the expression of the Ca2+‐activated K+ channels, indicating that this channel is lower on the VDR signaling hierarchy. Vitamin D and its metabolite MART10 significantly impact the expression of various surface proteins, potentially reducing the spread of cancer cells. The downregulation of metalloproteinase 9 and the conversion of N‐cadherin to E‐cadherin can reduce the risk of cancer spreading. Additionally, Id1, a cancer gene implicated in the development and spread of tumors, exhibits reduced expression, and the metabolism of BC cells is altered by the downregulation of pyruvate carboxylase. Vitamin D's beneficial effects on BC may be due to interactions with the miRNA‐199a/miRNA‐214 cluster, a known tumor promoter. The hedgehog pathway blocker and repressor of the fused gene are inhibited by miRNA‐199a/miRNA‐214. VDR signaling inhibits fusion gene activity by preventing the formation of miRNA‐199a/miRNA‐214 clusters, which may open up a novel treatment route. Fok1, Bsm1, Taq1, and Apa1 are the four VDR polymorphisms currently of interest in BC research. Studies suggest that discrepancies in SNPs in white populations may be the root cause of BC growth in this population. One study found that a black group experienced reduced BC incidence and increased expression of VDR distinct SNPs compared to a white group, despite lower serum vitamin D levels. 

Vitamin D and colorectal cancer

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common cancer type in terms of mortality and incidence.  The first to suggest a connection between the risk of CRC and vitamin D production from sunlight was Garland et al., who reported that individuals with reduced sun exposure had greater rates of CRC incidence and mortality. Epidemiologic investigations have consistently shown a negative correlation between vitamin D intake and the risk of CRC. Despite the enormous number of people who had been screened (1 000 000), the majority of studies have concentrated on extensive meta‐analyses without direct evaluation of the part played by vitamin D in the development of tumors – a gap emphasizing the demand for clinical trials. The role of calcium in both diseased and healthy states is believed to influence various pathways and underlies a protective action through vitamin D receptor expression. Vitamin D is primarily expressed at the start of cancer and significantly decreases in advanced metastasizing CRC. The Wnt/β‐catenin pathway and the transcription factor SNAIL both exhibit significant dysregulation in human CRC. They cause inflammation, tumor growth, and overexpression. The calcitriol/VDR interaction can prevent abnormal activation of the Wnt/β‐catenin pathway, a common issue in 90% of CRC cases, by promoting β‐catenin nuclear spread, preventing the progression of Wnt/β‐catenin‐derived tumors. The transcriptional factor SNAIL was discovered to be a VDR repressor in both xenograft mice and cellular lines. The Wnt/β‐catenin pathway controls how much of it moves into the nucleus. The expression of calcitriol is linked to VDR inhibition; reducing the interaction between VDR and calcitriol, suppresses its anticancer effect. SNAIL expression has been suggested as a diagnostic tool for individuals who do not respond well to vitamin D therapy. Numerous studies using CRC animal models have demonstrated beneficial outcomes in CRC treatment using vitamin D. Vitamin D + calcium supplementation reduced colon tumor frequency in xenograft mouse models, while other chemicals or vitamin D supplementation alone did not significantly impact carcinogenesis. Vitamin D trials have been started to explore its potential as a cancer cure, although some tests have not significantly improved patient outcomes. 

Vitamin D and colon cancer

In individuals with low blood levels of 25(OH)D, colon cancer risk was almost doubled compared to those with higher levels, while people with levels under 20 ng/mL had a threefold increased risk. In another study, low blood levels of 25(OH)D were persistently positively, though non‐significantly, linked to colon cancer risk. A greater frequency of colorectal adenomas has been observed in individuals with 25(OH)D levels less than 30 ng/mL. Other studies have linked colon cancer risk and 25(OH)D over an extended time period, proving that vitamin D metabolites may be active throughout the entire carcinogenic process. In two case–control studies, 25(OH)D had an impact on colon cancer risk only in the men in the CPS‐II Cohort. Reduced vitamin D intake has been linked to an increased risk of colon cancer, according to one study; however, a multivariate analysis of results from the study showed that the differences were no longer statistically significant. Two further studies discovered a conflicting link between calcium and the relationship between vitamin D intake and the likelihood of developing rectal cancer and indicated that a lower vitamin D intake increased the likelihood of developing adenomas. 

Vitamin D and colon cancer

Epidemiological research suggests an opposite connection between the prevalence of colon cancer and vitamin D consumption or pre‐vitamin D activation by UV light. Obesity, a high‐fat diet, and poor vitamin D intake are linked to a higher risk of developing colorectal cancer (CRC). The expression of VDR increases with tumor progression, and a worsening prediction is linked to an increase in vitamin D‐metabolizing enzymes. Vitamin D has an antiproliferative impact on a broad range of cancer cell lines, with a number of animal studies supporting the anti‐cancer benefits of vitamin D. Vitamin D and its analogues protect against tumor development and progression in carcinogen‐treated animal models. VDR−/− mice develop colonic epithelial hyperplasia but not cancer, while VDR−/− mice have an increased tumor incidence. 

Vitamin D and lung cancer

Studies indicate that age and exposure to sunlight significantly impact the generation of vitamin D in the skin. It has been shown that the dosage of UV light received by the skin varies dramatically from south to north in the UK and Norway. Furthermore, the death rates from lung cancer in these nations varied with seasonal variations and latitudinal gradients in UV exposure. The UK population experiences significant variation in vitamin D concentration between the south and the north of the country and between summer/autumn and winter/spring seasons. A comprehensive cohort study of 45 000 Norwegians was conducted to evaluate their lung cancer status. Patients under 50 have a 15% lower risk of death during summer and fall lung cancer seasons compared to winter and spring during the first 18 months post‐diagnosis. A meta‐analysis of 111 populations found a link between latitude, UVB exposure, and lung cancer risk, suggesting a lower risk of developing the disease. Furthermore, the UVB spectrum has been linked to lung cancer risk, and lower latitudes and solar exposure were linked to vitamin D levels in patients with lung cancer. Thus, seasonal and latitude variations can affect skin vitamin D levels and increase the risk of lung carcinoma. A number of studies indicate a correlation between vitamin D serum levels and the risk of lung cancer. One study found that higher levels of vitamin D in the blood were linked to reduced mortality rates in individuals with pulmonary cancer.  A similar study looked at 6937 cases of lung cancer and found no link between vitamin D levels in the blood and men or those over the age of 50. A case–control study involving 500 male smokers, 500 matched controls, and 500 people with lung cancer discovered a link between vitamin D levels and lung cancer risk throughout the winter but no link was discovered between lung cancer risk in the summertime and vitamin D levels or across the entire survey. The third US National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey indicates a correlation between low vitamin D levels and higher lung cancer mortality rates. Additional patient follow‐up revealed that higher serum vitamin D levels were linked to a higher death risk in male lung cancer patients. Furthermore, the Lung Cancer Cohort Consortium found no significant association between 25(OH)D and lung cancer risk in 5313 individuals with lung cancer and 5313 healthy individuals. Thus there are conflicting results regarding the effectiveness of vitamin D in preventing lung cancer. While high blood levels of 25(OH)D have been shown to reduce lung cancer mortality, with the link between lung cancer patients' serum 25(OH)D levels and survival rates showing a favorable trend, a meta‐analysis published in 2018 found no discernible dose–response connection between serum 25(OH)D levels and lung cancer risk. However, a dose–response linear regression analysis revealed that each 100 IU/day rise in vitamin D consumption lowered the incidence of lung cancer by 2.4%. Another study discovered that a high vitamin D level consumption offers no protection against the development of pulmonary carcinoma. 

CLINICAL STUDIES

Cancer incidence and mortality rates are increasing globally, necessitating innovative treatments. The sun UVB‐vitamin D‐cancer theory was proposed in 1980, but observational studies have not consistently supported its findings. Vitamin D has been shown to decrease cancer incidence and improve survival in various cancer forms, including breast, colon, lung, ovarian, pancreatic, and prostate cancers. Research shows that vitamin D can have anti‐tumorigenic and protective effects, slowing cancer progression. Public health acceptance of vitamin D for cancer prevention and treatment requires stronger support from clinical trials. 

One study aimed at determining if monthly high‐dose vitamin D supplementation without calcium is linked to a lower incidence and mortality rate of cancer in the general population. The Vitamin D Assessment (ViDA) study, conducted from 2011 to 2012, involved 5110 participants from family practices and community groups in Auckland, New Zealand (Trial Registration anzctr.org.au, Identifier: ACTRN12611000402943). The primary analysis included all participants, except two who revoked their consent; 58.1% of the 5108 participants were male, with 4253 being European or another race/ethnicity. The mean follow‐up 25‐hydroxyvitamin D concentration was consistently higher in the vitamin D group compared to the placebo group. More research may be necessary before daily or weekly dosing for an extended length of time is considered necessary. A population‐based randomized clinical trial aimed at determining if vitamin D3 and calcium supplements lower the risk of cancer in older women involved 2303 healthy postmenopausal women aged 55 and above, with 1156 assigned to the treatment group and 1147 to the placebo group. The treatment group received 2000 IU/d of vitamin D3 and 1500 mg/d of calcium. The study found that vitamin D3 and calcium supplements did not significantly reduce the incidence of all types of cancer at 4 years in healthy postmenopausal older women with a mean baseline blood 25‐hydroxyvitamin D level of 32.8 ng/mL compared to a placebo. More investigation is required to evaluate vitamin D's potential contribution to cancer prevention. The trial registration on clinicaltrials.gov has the identifier NCT01052051. 

Vitamin D supplementation may lower cancer mortality and metastatic cancer, according to epidemiologic and clinical research. The Vitamin D and Omega‐3 Trial (VITAL) is a two‐by‐two factorial, randomized, double‐blind, placebo‐controlled clinical study that aimed to assess the potential reduction in cancer death. The study involved men and women aged 50 years or older and those with cardiovascular disease and cancer at baseline. The primary outcome for the follow‐up analysis was a composite incidence of metastatic and fatal invasive total cancer. Results showed that individuals randomly assigned to the vitamin D group had a significantly lower risk of advanced malignancies than those assigned to a placebo. Those with normal BMI had a significantly lower risk of incident metastatic or fatal cancer when stratified by BMI than those with overweight or obesity. Vitamin D treatment decreased the whole cohort's incidence of advanced cancer, with those with normal weight showing the largest risk reduction. The ClinicalTrials.gov trial registration number is NCT01169259. 

Vitamin D is linked to health benefits, but randomized controlled studies have failed to validate these findings due to improper design, conduct, analysis, and interpretation. A hybrid observational approach based on serum 25(OH)D concentration is suggested, which requires knowledge of the relationship between serum 25(OH)D concentration and health outcomes and the ability to measure baseline values, recruit non‐complete subjects, and adjust supplemental doses during trials. A randomized clinical trial found that daily vitamin D and calcium supplementation for 3–5 years did not significantly lower the incidence of recurring colorectal adenomas, despite preclinical and epidemiological evidence suggesting these nutrients suppress colorectal carcinogenesis. The study analyzed 41 potential single‐nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) in 2259 people across 11 clinical sites across the US. The results showed that vitamin D3 supplementation decreased the risk associated with rs7968585 by 64% among people with the AA genotype (26%), and increased the risk by 41% among those with 1 or 2 G alleles (74%). Genes and calcium supplementation did not significantly interact. The study concluded that the effects of vitamin D3 supplementation in preventing advanced colorectal adenomas can differ depending on the genotype of the vitamin D receptor. The trial registration on clinicaltrials.gov has the identifier NCT00153816. Another comprehensive review and meta‐analysis of randomized controlled trials comparing vitamin D supplementation with placebo or no therapy was conducted to examine the impact of vitamin D supplementation on BC incidence. The study included 5372 postmenopausal women and found no significant impact of vitamin D dosage or method on BC risk. However, the greatest dosage of vitamin D and its combination with calcium resulted in somewhat higher therapeutic efficacy. The study concludes that postmenopausal women's use of vitamin D does not appear to be linked to a lower risk of developing BC. 

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

Vitamin D, through its active metabolite calcitriol, is a promising tool in combating cancer due to its ability to regulate crucial tumor cell growth and spread pathways. However, despite its potential to modify neoplastic processes through VDR‐mediated regulation of pathways like Wnt/β‐catenin, PI3K/Akt, and NF‐κB, vitamin D's therapeutic potential in cancer treatment is under‐utilized. The molecular knowledge acquired to date can be effectively utilized in future tumor therapy through the use of beneficial techniques. The development of novel non‐calcemic vitamin D analogs with increased tumor‐specific activity could address the drawbacks of high‐dose vitamin D therapy. Combination therapy involves combining vitamin D or its analogs with immune checkpoint inhibitors, targeted therapy, or current chemotherapeutic drugs to enhance efficacy and reduce toxicity. Patient stratification and personalized medicine can optimize treatment outcomes by identifying patient subgroups based on tumor sensitivity to vitamin D, expression levels, and VDR polymorphisms. Vitamin D supplementation, when carefully monitored, can potentially lower cancer risk in at‐risk groups as part of dietary and lifestyle interventions. Future research should prioritize robust clinical studies to confirm the safety and effectiveness of vitamin D‐based treatments for various cancer forms. Furthermore, investigating how vitamin D signaling interacts with immune modulation, tumor metabolism, and epigenetic control may open up new therapeutic options. Vitamin D holds great promise as a preventive measure and adjuvant in integrated cancer treatment.
