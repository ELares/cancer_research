# Neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy for colorectal cancer: clinical and molecular predictors of pathological complete response and safety assessment.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: To identify the clinical and molecular factors that effectively predict pathological complete response (pCR) and assess the safety of patients receiving neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy.

**METHODS**: This retrospective study evaluated 81 patients with colorectal cancer (CRC) at a Chinese tertiary center between 2015 and 2023. The cohort included 24 patients with deficient mismatch repair (dMMR) and 57 patients with proficient mismatch repair (pMMR) tumors. Patients were treated with a neoadjuvant combination of immunotherapy and surgery.

**RESULTS**: We divided 81 patients into pCR (40.7%) and non-pCR (59.3%) groups. The factors significantly associated with a higher pCR rate after neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy were younger age, low carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) level, high tumor mutational burden (TMB) level before treatment, clinical stage III, absence of lymph node metastasis before treatment, MSI-H level, dMMR, and pole status mutation. Preoperative combined chemotherapy and targeted therapy also influenced the pCR rate. Neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy showed an overall adverse event (AE) rate of 29.6%, with none of grades 3‒4. Surgery-related adverse reactions (srAEs) were also absent for grades 3‒4; 14 of the 81 patients experienced grade 1‒2 AEs.

**CONCLUSION**: Neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy resulted in a favorable pCR rate in patients with CRC. Young age, pretreatment CEA level, TMB level, clinical stage, lymph node metastasis, MSI, MMR, and pole status can be used as indicators of the efficacy of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy. The incidence of AEs from neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy and surgery was low, indicating that this regimen is safe and feasible.

Background

In recent years, immunotherapy has become a popular research topic in patients with CRC. The results of the KEYNOTE-016 study reported at the 2015 ASCO annual meeting led to the adoption of immunotherapy for CRC. The NICHE-1 was the first to apply immunotherapy to colon cancer neoadjuvant therapy, and the results showed that short-term preoperative immunotherapy was safe and feasible. NICHE-2 enrolled 120 patients with locally advanced dMMR colon cancer, and the results showed a pCR rate of 67% (72/107). It is well known that dMMR or microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) patients belong to the “hot tumor” population that is sensitive to immunotherapy. However, they account for only 10 ~ 15% of CRC, and a larger proportion of patients with pMMR or microsatellite stability (MSS) belong to the “cold tumor” population that is insensitive to immunotherapy. An increasing number of studies have shown that some patients with pMMR or MSS respond to neoadjuvant immunotherapy combined with other treatment methods such as radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy. However, there is no consensus on how to identify effective pCR predictors, and the safety of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy has seldom been reported. Besides serum and histological markers, a multicenter CT-radiomics study noninvasively predicted KRAS/NRAS/BRAF mutations, with AUCs ranging from 0.818 to 0.825 across all cohorts. Therefore, we conducted a retrospective exploratory analysis to evaluate the effectiveness and safety of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy and identify the clinical and molecular predictors of pCR.

Methods

Patients

This study retrospectively evaluated a cohort of patients with CRC who received immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI). We extracted all cases from February 2015 to September 2023 from a prospectively collected institutional database and tumor registry. The following inclusion had to be met by the participants: (1) colonoscopy confirmed adenocarcinoma, (2) Preoperative treatment included ICI, (3) complete primary lesion surgery, and (4) pretreatment imaging data and MMR status by immunohistochemistry (IHC). We excluded patients who (1) received other types of antitumor or experimental treatment (2), previously received ICI treatment, and (3) had other malignant tumors in the past 5 years.

Treatment procedure

The treatment plan used ICI (manufacturer unlimited, dosage according to the opinion of the attending physician) combined with other treatments, including chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and targeted therapy. Checkpoint inhibitors included anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1 (± anti-CTLA-4), and chemotherapy backbones included CAPOX, FOLFOX, or FOLFIRI, all administered per label-recommended doses and schedules; patient distribution by regimen is summarized in Table 3. Following the completion of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy, the patients were re-staged with chest, abdominal, and pelvic CT scans. Restaging with MRI, flexible endoscopy, and/or EUS were used to evaluate lesion regression. All the patients underwent laparoscopic or open surgery. For patients with distant metastases before treatment, if residual metastases were considered after treatment, a multidisciplinary consultation was conducted to decide the need for combined metastasis resection.

Data collection

The data included basic patient information such as sex, age, body mass index (BMI), family history, tumor location, preoperative imaging data, colonoscopy pathology data, carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) level, hemoglobin (Hb) level, and neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR). All CEA measurements were obtained at baseline prior to the start of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy. Additionally, prior to treatment, all patients underwent MMR protein immunohistochemistry, and—provided that sample quantity and quality were sufficient—MSI status and mutation analyses of KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, TP53, POLE, and PIK3CA genes were performed. Due to sample limitations, some patients did not complete all tests; detailed completion rates are presented in Table 2. Finally, the time intervals for each treatment stage and the postoperative pathological outcomes—such as pathological TNM staging (pTNM) and tumor regression grade (TRG)—were recorded.

Evaluation of efficacy and safety

According to the Dowrak/Rödel TRG system, it has four levels: (1) TRG-0 is no response; (2) TRG-1 is less than 25% fibrosis in the tumor; (3) TRG2 is 25%−50% fibrosis in the tumor; (3) TRG-3 is more than 50% fibrosis in the tumor; and (3) TRG-4 is complete remission. Consequently, pCR was defined as tumor regression induced by neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy without residual cancer or positive lymph nodes (pCR = TRG-4). AEs were assessed using the Common Adverse Event Evaluation Criteria (CTCAE) version 5.0, published by the US Department of Health and Human Services. Immune-related adverse events (irAEs) were evaluated according to literature standards.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using the IBM SPSS Statistics version 26 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). Categorical variables were compared using Pearson’s chi-square and Fisher’s exact tests, whereas continuous variables were analyzed using the Kruskal–Wallis H test. Statistical significance was set at p < 0.05.

Results

Patient characteristics

This study included 81 CRC patients who met the inclusion criteria between February 2015 and September 2023. A total of 33 patients (40.7%) achieved pCR, while 48 patients (59.3%) did not. The study’s flowchart is shown in Fig. 1. The average age was 54.6 ± 12.3 years, with a median BMI of 23.8 kg/m2 (IQR 21.5–26.0), and a median CEA level of 4.0ng/mL (IQR (1.8–17.2)) before treatment. Of the 81 patients, 44 were male (54.3%) and 37 were female (45.7%). According to the primary tumor site, 43 patients had rectal cancer (53.1%), 15 had sigmoid colon cancer (18.5%), and 23 had ascending colon cancer (28.4%). According to the clinical stage before treatment, there were 11 patients with stage II (13.6%), 49 with stage III (60.5%), and 21 with stage IV disease (25.9%). According to MMR status, there were 24 cases of dMMR (29.6%) and 57 cases of pMMR (70.4%).

Clinical and molecular predictors of pCR

Patients with younger age (50 vs. 58 years; p = 0.003), low CEA level (2.1 vs. 8.6; p < 0.001), high TMB level (68 vs. 5; p < 0.001) before treatment, clinical stage III (69.7% vs. 54.2%; p < 0.001), and no lymph node metastasis before treatment (24.2% vs. 6.3%; p = 0.049) were more likely to achieve pCR after receiving neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy. There were no significant differences in sex, BMI, family history, primary tumor site, NLR, Hb level, lesion length, or T stage before treatment between the pCR and non-pCR groups (p > 0.05) (Table 1).

Pre-treatment pathological, IHC, and genetic testing results were analyzed., we found that patients with MSI-H (42.4% vs. 12.5%, p = 0.003), dMMR (48.5% vs. 18.7%, p = 0.002), and combined pole gene mutations (21.2% vs. 2.1%, p = 0.008) were more likely to achieve pCR (p < 0.05). However, we did not find any statistically significant differences in tumor differentiation, Lynch syndrome, KRAS, NRAS, BRAF, TP53, or PIK3CA mutations between the pCR and non-pCR groups (Table 2).

Among 81 patients, the median time from diagnosis to the start of their first treatment was 28 days (IQR: 14-45.5). From the start of neoadjuvant therapy to its completion, the median time was 100 days (IQR: 71.5-145.5). Following neoadjuvant therapy, there was a median interval of 46 days (IQR: 34.5–63) until surgery. Notably, there were no statistically significant differences in the time intervals between the pCR and non-pCR groups (Table 3).

Among these cases, 62 utilized PD-1 inhibitors, 9 used PD-L1 inhibitors, and 10 used a combination of PD-1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors. The primary preoperative chemotherapy regimens included CAPOX (71 cases) and FOLFOX (4 cases). In addition, 22 patients with rectal cancer underwent synchronous preoperative radiotherapy and chemotherapy. The chemotherapy regimen was primarily based on capecitabine, with 13 patients undergoing short-course radiotherapy (SCRT) and nine receiving long-course radiotherapy (LCRT). Furthermore, preoperative combination chemotherapy (p = 0.021) and targeted therapy (p = 0.01) affected postoperative pCR rates (Table 3).

Postoperative pathological outcomes

Based on the MMR status, 81 patients were categorized into two groups: dMMR (n = 24) and pMMR (n = 57). Remarkably, all patients achieved an objective response rate (ORR). In the dMMR group, a significantly higher proportion of patients achieved pCR than in the pMMR group (66.7% vs. 29.8%, p < 0.05). Additionally, the dMMR group exhibited a higher rate of postoperative N0 status than the pMMR group (83.3% vs. 61.4%, p = 0.20). Furthermore, the dMMR group had a lower incidence of vascular (12.5% vs. 22.8%, p = 0.43) and perineural invasion (12.5% vs. 29.8%, p = 0.099) than the pMMR group (Table 4).

Neoadjuvant therapy-related and surgical-related adverse events

The overall AEs rate associated with neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy (nCRT) and neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NAC) was 70.3%. Among these reactions, 3–4 grade events account for 16.0%. In contrast, the total AEs rate of neoadjuvant ICIs was 29.6%, with no observed 3–4 grade adverse reactions. The main irAEs were fever (4.9%), fatigue (4.9%), rash or pruritus (7.4%), and diarrhea (8.6%). In contrast, the AEs associated with nCRT/NAC predominantly included hand and foot syndromes (19.8%), nausea (17.3%), and vomiting (12.3%). Remarkably, after neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy and surgery, no 3–4 grade srAEs were observed. Instead, 1–2 grade adverse reactions included blood loss > 100 ml in seven cases, anastomotic leakage in two cases, ileus in one case, chylous fistula in one case, incisional hernia in one case, perineal incision in three cases, and stoma infection in one case Neoadjuvant ICIs group had an overall adverse event rate of 29.6%, significantly lower than the 70.3% observed in the nCRT/NAC group, and no grade 3–4 adverse reactions were observed (Table 5).

Discussion

In this single-center retrospective study, all patients received immunotherapy before surgery. After surgery, more than 40% of the patients achieved pCR, with 66.7% and 29.8% of patients with dMMR and pMMR colorectal cancer achieving pCR, respectively. Additionally, this study suggests that factors predicting pCR include younger age, lower CEA levels before treatment, clinical stage III, negative clinical lymph nodes, and molecular pathology including MSI-H, dMMR, pole gene mutations, and higher TMB levels. Notably, the proportion of dMMR patients in our cohort (29.6%) exceeded the 10–15% prevalence reported in general CRC populations, likely reflecting selection bias whereby only patients with pre-treatment biopsy-confirmed dMMR were allocated to neoadjuvant immunotherapy.

The traditional neoadjuvant treatment methods for colon cancer include chemotherapy, radiotherapy, targeted therapy, and combination therapy. Currently, neoadjuvant treatment for rectal cancer is based on a combination of radiotherapy and chemotherapy. The ORR of colorectal cancer with monotherapy is 40%, and the pCR rate is 5%. The pCR rate of combined radiotherapy and chemotherapy is 10–15%. Garcia-Aguilar et al. found that TNT therapy achieved pCR in 38% of rectal cancer patients. Our research found that, compared with traditional treatment methods, adopting neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy can increase the rate of complete pathological remission after surgery, with more than 40% of patients achieving pCR, including 39.4 (13/43) of patients with rectal cancer who achieved pCR. This shows that neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy can improve the rate of pCR compared with traditional treatment methods. In addition, a Voltage study explored the new neoadjuvant immunotherapy of nivolumab after long-distance synchronous radiotherapy and chemotherapy for locally advanced rectal cancer. The pCR rate in the dMMR group reached 60%, which is consistent with our results. Among the 24 patients with dMMR, 16 (66.7%) achieved pCR. Lin et al. reported that the pCR rate of 26 patients with pMMR rectal cancer, receiving short-term radiotherapy combined with camrelizumab and CapeOx regimens was 46.2%. In our study, 29.8% (17/57) of patients achieved complete pathological remission. Since our study included both colon and rectal cancer cases and there were mixed factors such as treatment plan, treatment cycle, small data volume, and tumor staging, the optimal treatment plan for combination therapy cannot be determined at present.

In patients with pMMR or MSS CRC, optimizing new neoadjuvant immunotherapy regimens and identifying effective biomarkers to predict therapeutic efficacy are important research directions. Currently, there are a few potential markers that can predict the efficacy of immunotherapy, such as POLE or POLD1 gene mutations, TMB, immune score, PD-L1 expression, CD8+/Treg ratio, and intestinal flora. In a study of patients with MSI-H mCRC receiving PD-1 and PD-L1 treatment, all 13 patients with high TMB achieved objective remission, while six of nine patients with low TMB experienced tumor progression. Therefore, TMB is an effective biomarker; however, there is controversy over its optimal cutoff value when applied as a marker for predicting the efficacy of immunotherapy. As different studies are based on different detection platforms and panels, they have different cutoff values. Determining the optimal cut-off value for TMB is another direction that needs to be explored in the future. Studies have shown that colorectal tumors with POLE or POLD1 gene mutations are MSS-type and can benefit from PD-1 monoclonal antibody treatment; however, this type of mutation accounts for only 1% of patients with colorectal cancer. In a retrospective study using CEA to predict pCR, lower CEA levels were associated with pCR. Additionally, CEA levels before and after neoadjuvant therapy can be used to predict pCR. Clinical staging is related to patient risk stratification, which distinguishes patient risk by analyzing patient images and pathological results combined with T stage, N stage, and other factors. However, the specific application of these indicators to predict the efficacy of neoadjuvant therapy and define the weight of each indicator requires further exploration. From a mechanistic perspective, high TMB tumors generate more neoantigen peptides, bolstering CD8⁺ T cell recognition and cytotoxicity and thereby increasing sensitivity to PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors; elevated CEA expression not only reflects tumor burden but also promotes the recruitment of regulatory T cells and myeloid-derived suppressor cells, creating an immunosuppressive microenvironment that diminishes therapeutic efficacy; and dMMR/MSI-H induces abundant frameshift mutations, leading to highly immunogenic neoantigen production that underpins this population’s pronounced sensitivity to immune checkpoint blockade.

Among the 81 patients included in the study, AEs, such as fever (4.9%), fatigue (4.9%), rash or pruritus (7.4%), and diarrhea (8.6%) were observed; however, there were no treatment-related deaths or severe adverse reactions. In the KEYNOTE-177 study, the pembrolizumab group had a 31% incidence of immune- and infusion-related adverse reactions, of which 9% were grade 3 or 4 with colonic inflammation and hepatitis being the most common. The adverse reaction outcomes of this study were similar to those of the KEYNOTE-177 study; conversely, no grade 3–4 adverse reaction events were observed, indicating that neoadjuvant immunotherapy for colorectal cancer is safe. Grade 1–2 adverse reactions caused by neoCRT/NAC were mainly hand-and-foot syndrome (19.8%), nausea (17.3%), and vomiting (12.3%). In the NICHE study, 20% of the 40 patients who underwent colon resection surgery had grade III surgical complications, and the incidence of anastomotic leakage was as high as 10%, including five cases of abdominal (or incision) infection, one case of intestinal obstruction, one case of pneumonia, and one case of small intestinal injury; the high incidence of anastomotic leakage may be related to the combined use of PD-1 and CTLA-4. In this study, there were only two cases of anastomotic leakage after colorectal cancer surgery, and both improved with conservative treatment. Seven patients had intraoperative bleeding > 100 ml, one had ileus, one had a chylous fistula, one had an incisional hernia, three had a perineal incision, and one had a stoma infection; however, there were no perioperative deaths. In addition, the lower incidence of adverse events in the neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy group compared with chemotherapy alone may reflect selection bias, since clinicians often reserve more intensive combination regimens for patients with superior baseline performance status and nutritional condition.

This single-center, retrospective study has several limitations. First, the study only included patients with colorectal cancer who underwent surgical treatment, excluding patients who achieved complete clinical remission after neoadjuvant therapy and those whose disease progressed and who could not undergo surgery, which may have caused selection bias. Second, the study included patients with colon and rectal cancers, including those with advanced colorectal cancer, and there were differences in the preoperative medication plans and cycles—which in our cohort also meant that immunotherapy timing and cycle number varied and were not standardized—potentially affecting the statistical results. Finally, the retrospective design and modest sample size of this study limited our ability to perform subgroup analyses by MMR status. Future multicenter, prospective studies with larger cohorts are required to validate and thoroughly stratify the associations of CEA and TMB with pCR in both dMMR and pMMR patient populations.

Conclusion

Neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy has a favorable pCR rate in patients with CRC. Young age, pretreatment CEA level, TMB level, clinical stage, lymph node metastasis, and MSI, MMR, and pole statuses can be used as indicators of the efficacy of neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy. No severe adverse reactions were observed in the AEs of patients in this group, indicating that neoadjuvant combination immunotherapy is safe and reliable.
