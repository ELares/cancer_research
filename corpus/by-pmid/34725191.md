# Combination Therapy of Hepatocellular Carcinoma by GPC3-Targeted Bispecific Antibody and Irinotecan is Potent in Suppressing Tumor Growth in Mice.

## Abstract

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is a world leading cause of cancer-related mortality, and currently no curative treatment for advanced HCC is available. Glypican-3 (GPC3) is an attractive target for HCC immunotherapy. This study explored the efficacy of six GPC3-targeted bispecific antibodies, alone or in combination with chemotherapeutic drug Irinotecan, for the treatment of HCC. The bispecific antibodies were constructed using three different structures, knob-into-hole (KH), scFv-scFv-hFc, and scFv-hFc-scFv, where CD3-targeting mAb OKT3 (scFv) was paired with two representative GPC3 mAbs hYP7 (scFv) and HN3 (VH only) that target different epitopes. The In vitro cell killing assay revealed that all bispecific antibodies efficiently killed GPC3 positive cancer cells, with hYP7-KH, hYP7-OKT3-hFc, and HN3-KH being most potent. In vivo xenograft mouse studies demonstrated that all bispecific antibodies suppressed tumor growth similarly, with hYP7-OKT3-hFc performing slightly better. Combination of hYP7-OKT3-hFc with Irinotecan dramatically improved the efficacy and arrested tumor growth of HepG2, Hep3B, and G1 in xenograft mice. Our results demonstrated that the cell surface proximal bispecific antibody hYP7-OKT3-hFc was superior in terms of potency and the GPC3-targeted bispecific antibody combined with Irinotecan was much potent to control HCC growth.

Introduction

Liver cancer is the sixth most common cancer and the fourth leading cause of cancer-related mortality. Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common form of primary liver cancer, accounting for approximately 90% of the cases. Because the symptoms of HCC at the early stage are relatively insidious and unconspicuous, the disease is often diagnosed in the middle and late stage, which passed the best time window for surgical treatment. Currently, FDA-approved targeted chemotherapies of HCC include Sorafenib, Lenvatinib, and Regorafenib, all of which have shown some efficacy, but most patients subsequently developed drug resistance. Under such scenario, immunotherapy alone or in combination with chemotherapy may provide new opportunities for treating HCC.

In the past few years, immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI), particularly antibodies targeting the programmed cell death-1 (PD-1) and programmed cell death ligand-1 (PD-L1), have brought revolutionary outcomes in treating different cancer types. ICI therapies on HCC have also been rigorously explored in clinical trials, which led to the accelerated approval of Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab, and the combination of Nivolumab plus Ipilimumab by FDA for treatment of HCC. However, the overall objective response rate (ORR) and the median overall survival (OS) of ICI therapy in HCC were only approximately 15% and 1 year. Therefore, seeking other forms of immunotherapy with higher potency is urgently needed, such as T cell-engaged bispecific antibody and CAR-T.

GPC3, a GPI-anchored cell surface heparan sulfate proteoglycan, is an attractive target for HCC immunotherapy because it is selectively expressed on HCC and promotes HCC cell growth and migration. GPC3-targeted CAR-T therapy has been extensively investigated in more than a dozen of pre-clinical studies and one clinical trial. The outcome of phase I GPC3 CAR-T trial (NCT02395250, NCT03146234) treated 13 HCC patients, and the overall efficacy was not very promising, with only two out of thirteen having partial response (PR) and the 3-year survival rates being only 10.5%. GPC3-targeted bispecific antibody was relatively less studied. Most of the current GPC3 bispecific antibodies were overwhelmingly based on the monoclonal antibody GC33 that targets the C-terminal epitope of GPC3. One such exemplary bispecific antibody was ERY974 that had two different heavy chains (VH) targeting GPC3 and CD3 respectively, and a common light chain (VL) to maintain the whole structure. ERY974 is currently undergoing a multicenter international phase I clinical trial in the United States and Europe (NCT02748837) to treat HCC, stomach, esophageal, and other GPC3-expressing cancers. Other GPC3 bispecific antibodies that are under development include ABP-110 (https://abpro.com/pipeline/), and others that are targeting CD3, CD16, or CD47, all of which are in the early pre-clinical stage.

Currently, there are two representative GPC3 monoclonal antibodies, HN3 and hYP7, that target two unique different epitopes of GPC3. HN3 identifies a conformational epitope on GPC3 near the N-lobe and could directly inhibit HCC cell proliferation, while hYP7 identifies a C-terminal linear epitope on GPC3 and could not inhibit HCC cell proliferation. Because the epitope of the tumor antigen-targeting monoclonal antibody could have pivotal impact on the activity of bispecific antibody, we therefore compared the activity of HN3 and hYP7-based bispecific antibody. We also compared the influence of bispecific antibody structures on their activity.

Materials and Methods

Cell lines and peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs)

Three HCC cell lines (HepG2, Hep3B, and Huh-7) and GPC3 negative A431 cell line (Human epithelial carcinoma cell line) were maintained as adherent monolayer cultures in DMEM medium (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (HyClone, Logan, UT), 1% L-glutamine (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA), and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA), and incubated in 5% CO2 with a balance of air at 37°C. The T lymphoma cell line Jurkat was cultured in RPMI1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (HyClone, Logan, UT), 1% L-glutamine, and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). All cell lines (A431, Hep3B, HepG2, Huh-7, and Jurkat) were purchased from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China) and authenticated using short tandem repeat (STR) analysis by Genetic Testing Biotechnology Corporation (Suzhou, China). Cells were passaged every three to four days to maintain a confluence rate of 20–80%. Mycoplasma was tested to be negative with PlasmoTest™ Mycoplasma Detection Kit (Cat. rep-pt1, Invivogen, San Diego, CA) according to the manufacturer’s instruction. An artificial G1 cell line was created by lentiviral transduction of GPC3 negative A431 cells to stably over-express full-length GPC3. Human PBMCs were isolated from whole blood of healthy donors (Wuhan Blood Center) by Ficoll separation (Stem Cell Technologies, Vancouver, BC, Canada) according to the manufacturer’s instruction.

Production and purification of GPC3 bispecific antibodies

Two GPC3-targeted monoclonal antibodies, human VH-only domain antibody HN3 (SEQ ID NO. 2 from patent PCT/US2012/034186) and humanized mouse monoclonal antibody hYP7, were used to make the bispecific antibody. The HN3 sequence was read as QVQLVQSGGGLVQPGGSLRLSCAASYFDFDSYEMSWVRQAPG KGLEWIGSIYHSGSTYYNPSLKSRVTISRDNSKNTLYLQMNTLRAEDTATYYCARVNMDRFDYWGQGTLVTVSS. Three formats of bispecific structure were constructed. The knob-into-hole format was constructed as previously reported. The knob arm consisted of hYP7 scFv or HN3 VH that was fused with hFc knob mutant (N297A/S354C/T366W). The hole arm consisted of the CD3-targeted scFv OKT3 that was fused with hFc hole mutant (N297A/Y349C/T366S/L368A/Y407V). Anti-HIV scFv b12 (VH and VL sequences are from 2NY7_H and 2NY7_L, respectively) was used in the knob arm to make an irrelevant bispecific control. Both knob and hole plasmids were co-introduced into 293F cells to make the bispecific antibody as previously described. The second bispecific format was hYP7scFv-G4S-OKT3scFv-hFc (N297A). The third bispecific format was scfv-hFc-G4S-scFv (shortened as scFv-hFc-scFv). All bispecific antibodies were expressed in 293F cells in a secretory way and purified via protein A chromatography.

ELISA

A Nunc MaxiSorp flat-bottom 96-well plate was coated with 50 μl GPC3 (ECD)-His protein solution (5 μg/ml, in PBS buffer). After coating at 37°C for 30 min, the plate was blocked with 2% BSA in PBST buffer (PBS buffer containing 0.05% Tween-20) at 37°C for 30 min. After removing the blocking buffer, the plate was incubated with 50 μl of 1:3 serial dilutions of bispecific antibody (starting from100 nM) at 37°C for 30 min. After removing the bispecific antibody solution, the plate was washed twice with PBST buffer, and incubated with 50 μl horseradish peroxidase-conjugated goat anti-human secondary antibody solution (1:8000 dilution, Jackson ImmunoResearch Inc, West Grove, PA). After incubating at 37°C for 30 min, the plate was washed four times with PBST buffer. Bispecific antibody binding was visualized by incubating the plate with 50 μl of 3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine substrate (KPL, Gaithersburg, MD). After developing color for five minutes and being terminated by adding 50 μl of 0.5 M sulfuric acid, the plate was read by Multifunctional ELISA Reader at 450 nm.

Flow cytometry

Cells were harvested by detaching with trypsin-EDTA (Invitrogen, Camarillo, CA), washed once with PBS buffer by centrifugation, and resuspended in ice-cold PBS buffer containing 5% BSA. One million of cells were incubated with 5 μg of bispecific antibodies for 1 h on ice. Bound antibodies were detected by incubating with phycoerythrin-conjugated goat anti-human IgG (1:300 dilution, Jackson ImmunoResearch Inc, West Grove, PA) for 30 min on ice. The fluorescence associated with live cells was measured using a FACS Calibur (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, NJ).

In vitro cytotoxicity assay

Cell growth and viability were assessed by firefly luciferase reporter assay. All the tested cancer cells were stably transfected to constitutively express a fire fly luciferase reporter gene (ffLuc2). Two hundred microliters of stably transduced cells were seeded on a 96-well plate (5×103 cells per well), followed by the addition of PBMC cells and bispecific antibodies at the indicated concentrations. After being incubated at 37°C for 48 h, cells were collected by spinning down, followed by lysis via two rounds of freeze-thaw. Released luciferase activity was measured to represent the cell viability. The in vitro cytotoxicity assay was repeated three times, using PBMCs from three donors. Reported was the representative result.

In vivo efficacy study

All mice were housed and treated under the protocol approved by Animal Care and Use Committee of Huazhong Agricultural University (approval no. HZAUMO‑2017‑051). Five millions of HepG2 or Hep3B, or one million of G1 cells were subcutaneously injected into 5-week old female NSG mice. After tumor reached a size of 100–200 mm3, the treatment was started by splenocyte depletion with intraperitoneally (i.p.) injection of 100 mg/kg cyclophosphamide (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) and one day later, the mice were i.p. injected with ten millions of PBMC cells and i.v. injection of bispecific antibodies at the indicated dose. PBMC was administered once and bispecific antibody every four days. In the bispecific antibody/irinotecan combination treatment, mice were treated by two i.p. injections of Irinotecan at 50 mg/kg with two days apart, followed by i.v injection of bispecific antibodies at 0.2 mg/kg starting 4 days after the second Irinotecan injection. Tumor dimensions were determined every two or three days with a caliper. Tumor volume (mm3) was calculated by the formula: (a) × (b2) × 0.5, where a is tumor length and b is tumor width in millimeters. Five mice per group were assigned. For survival comparison, mice were considered at the end point when tumor size exceeded 1,500mm3.

Determination of serum concentration of Irinotecan

Irinotecan was administered to NSG mice via i.p. injections at a dose of 50 mg/kg. Blood samples were withdrawn at 6, 24, 48, 72 and 96 h after the injection. Blood were collected into heparinized tubes and briefly immersed into a dry ice/acetone bath. This was done rapidly with gentle agitation, taking care not to freeze the blood. The samples were then centrifuged for 3 min (4°C; 8000 × g) and 200 μl of plasma were added to an Eppendorf tube containing 200 μl of an ice-cold methanol: acetonitrile mixture (50:50, v/v). The tubes were vortexed 30 s and centrifuged (5 min, 4°C, 12000 × g), and the supernatant was transferred to a new tube and stored in a −70°C freezer. The frozen samples and Irinotecan standards were analyzed by Shimadzu HPLC (LC-20AT) using a C18 reverse phase column (Hanbon Science &. Technology Co., Ltd 200 × 4.6 mm 5 μm column) at a flow rate of 1.0 ml/min. The mobile phase A was 0.1% formic acid in water, and the mobile B was pure acetonitrile. The elution conditions were as follows: 0–10 min 30% B to 100% B, 10–12 min 100% B, 12–13 min 100% B to 30% B, 13–16 min 30% B. The concentration of Irinotecan in the serum sample was calculated by comparing the peak area of the sample with that of the Irinotecan standard of known concentrations. To check the possible existence of trace of Irinotecan in the serum long after i.p. injection, an ultra-sensitive LC-ESI-HRMS analysis was performed on Thermo Scientific HPLC equipped with ESI ion source and orbitrap high resolution mass analyzer using the same analytic chromatography column and gradient elution conditions as above. The MS scan range was set at m/z 300–1000.

Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism5 (GraphPad Software, Inc., La Jolla, CA). Differences between two groups were analyzed using the Paired Student’s t-test (two-tailed). Comparisons among three or more groups were performed using one-way ANOVA. P value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

Preparation of the bispecific antibodies

Bispecific antibodies were constructed using GPC3 monoclonal antibodies HN3 and hYP7 that were paired with CD3 monoclonal antibody OKT3. HN3 was a human heavy-chain variable domain isolated from a combinatorial human VH domain library, and hYP7 was a humanized mouse monoclonal antibody generated via hybridoma. HN3 recognizes a N-lobe epitope of GPC3 and hYP7 recognizes a C-terminal linear epitope (Fig. 1A). Three types of bispecific structures were used (Fig. 1B), and six bispecifics were prepared, namely HN3-KH (classical knob-into-hole structure), hYP7-KH, HN3-OKT3-hFc (scFv-G4S-scFv-hFc(N297A)), hYP7-OKT3-hFc, HN3-hFc-OKT3 (scFv-hFc(N297A)-G4S-scFv), and hYP7-hFc-OKT3. A control bispecific b12-KH that targets HIV gp120 was constructed. All bispecifics were expressed in 293F cells and purified via protein A affinity chromatography. The purity was visually checked on SDS-PAGE (Fig. 1C). The apparent molecular weight of each bispecific on the gel was consistent to the theoretical size.

Protein and cell binding properties of the bispecific antibodies

Protein binding was measured using ELISA with purified GPC3-His protein, which was produced as preciously described. The ELISA result showed that six bispecific antibodies had high affinities to purified GPC3 protein, with the calculated EC50 values of 0.09–0.5 nM (Fig. 2A), and hYP7-based bispecific had a little higher binding than HN3. Cell binding was checked on GPC3 negative and positive cancer cell lines, CD3-expressing T lymphoma cell line Jurkat and PBMC. As shown in Fig. 2B, all bispecifics showed specific binding to GPC3-positive HCC cell lines HepG2, Hep3B, Huh-7, and GPC3-overexpressing G1 cells, but not GPC3-negative A431 cells. Bispecific hYP7-hFc-OKT3 had relatively higher cell binding. The binding of the bispecifics to T cells was confirmed on both Jurkat cells and PBMC (Fig. 2C). The cell binding affinity was further measured by flow cytometry on G1 cells, with the calculated EC50 values ranging from 0.27 to 2.48 nM (Fig. 2D).

Cytotoxicity of GPC3 bispecific antibodies

Cell killing activity and specificity of the bispecific antibodies was tested in HCC cell lines and GPC3 negative A431 cells. All tested cell lines were stably transfected with a lentiviral vector that carried the firefly luciferase gene to precisely indicate the cell viability. We first tested the killing efficiency and specificity of the bispecific antibody at different ratio of PBMC: cancer cells using hYP7-KH as a model, and A431 and G1 as tested cells. As shown in Fig. 3A, efficient killing of G1 cells was achieved at the ratio of 2:1 and 5:1, and maximal killing at 10:1 or 20:1. No significant difference was detected between 10:1 and 20:1, even though the latter seemingly had higher killing. We therefore chosen 20:1 ratio to test all the bispecific antibodies (Fig. 3B). It revealed that all the bispecific antibodies demonstrated efficient killing on GPC3-positive HepG2, Hep3B, Huh-7, and G1 cells in a dose-dependent manner, whereas had no effect on GPC3 negative A431 cells. The required concentration for the maximal killing of G1, HepG2, and Huh-7 cells was approximately 100 ng/ml, and 10 ng/ml for Hep3B cells. Among the 6 bispecifics, hYP7-KH, hYP7-OKT3-hFc, and HN3-KH were most potent in all tested cell lines, especially the first two. HN3-OKT3-hFc and HN3-hFc-OKT3 were much less potent in all tested cell lines.

Impact of hYP7 affinity on its cytotoxicity

The cytotoxicity assays revealed that all hYP7-derived bispecifics showed apparently stronger potency than HN3. To explore whether this was caused by the higher affinity of hYP7 than HN3, we generated affinity attenuated mutant hYP7 bispecific antibodies by changing one of the amino acids in the CDR3 of the VH to alanine, namely F98A, N96A, and Y102A (numbering according to Kabat scheme). Protein binding ELISA showed that all three mutant hYP7 bispecifics had much lower affinity to GPC3-His than HN3 (Fig. 4A). However, the cytotoxicity of all three mutant hYP7 bispecifics was not negatively affected at all (Fig. 4B), which strongly indicated that it was the epitope location of hYP7, and not its affinity, that dominated its stronger potency.

In vivo tumor-suppression activity of the bispecific antibodies

To further compare the in vivo anti-tumor activity of the bispecific antibodies, we subcutaneously inoculated Hep3B cells into NSG mice to establish a xenograft mouse model. To determine a maximally tolerable dosage, we treated the tumor-bearing mice by intraperitoneal injection of ten million PBMC cells and tail vein injection of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5 mg/kg hYP7-OKT3-hFc (Fig. 5A). A pilot experiment showed that a higher dosage of 1.0 mg/kg caused 3 out of 5 mice died after one injection of the antibody, and therefore 0.5 mg/kg was set as the highest dose in the dose escalating experiment. As shown in Fig. 5A, dosage of 0.2 and 0.5mg/kg had indistinguishable tumor-suppression potency. However, 0.5 mg/kg had significant loss of body weight (Fig. 5B), with one mouse died three days later after the antibody injection, and therefore, 0.2 mg/kg was determined as the safe dose for the following experiments. Next, the anti-tumor activity of six bispecific antibodies was compared side by side. A little surprisingly, all six bispecific antibodies had indistinguishable in vivo activity (Fig. 5C), except that hYP7-OKT3-hFc performed slightly better than the rest. Curves of the body weight change indicated that hYP7-OKT3-hFc had comparable safety profile compared to others (Fig. 5D); therefore, this bispecific was chosen for the combination treatment experiment.

Improvement of efficacy by combination of bispecific antibody with Irinotecan

Our previous study indicated that chemotherapeutic drug Irinotecan was very effective to inhibit HCC growth. Therefore, we evaluated the efficacy of combination of bispecific antibody hYP7-OKT3-hFc with Irinotecan in three xenograft mouse models, Hep3B, HepG2, and G1. Treatment was started by two i.p. deliveries of Irinotecan at a dose of 50 mg/kg, followed by the i.v. injection of bispecific antibody at a dose of 0.2 mg/kg and PBMC cells. Considering the possible toxicity of Irinotecan to the subsequently injected PBMC cells, the serum concentrations of Irinotecan at different time points were measured by HPLC and LC-MS. It was found that Irinotecan was fast metabolized (Fig. 6A), and could not be detected by LC-MS after 48 hours (Fig. 6B). For cautiousness, we chose a 4-day interval to proceed to the bispecific antibody/PBMC therapy. Compared to monotherapy, combination of bispecific antibody and Irinotecan significantly boosted the anti-tumor efficacy (Fig. 6 C–E), and temporal tumor disappearance was achieved in Hep3B (1 out of 5) and G1 group (4 out 5). The body weight of the combination treated mice was temporally lost during the initial stage of bispecific antibody treatment and eventually recovered (Fig. 6 F–H). Statistics revealed that combination treatment prolonged the overall survival time by about 30 to 60 days depending on the tumor models (Fig. 6 I–K).

Discussion

HCC is notorious for its malignancy due to its drug resistance and high rate of relapse. Even though some CAR-T, ICI, and bispecific antibodies have been evaluated for their efficacy to treat HCC in preclinical studies or clinical trials, it is still far from being able to control this malignancy. Most of the current HCC therapeutic CAR-T, bispecific antibody, ADC, and immunotoxin are targeting GPC3. CAR-T and T cell-engaged bispecific antibody have similar mode of action that both are able to activate and redirect T cells to kill cancer cells. The potency of CAR-T and T cell-engaged bispecific antibody could be equally well, or in some cases the bispecific antibody could function better than CAR-T. The clinical outcomes of CAR-T in solid tumors revealed some toxicities, such as severe cytokine release syndrome, tumor lysis syndrome, and gene modified T cells of oncogenic insertional mutagenesis. Bispecific antibody could avoid some of these side effects and have other unique properties. GPC3 targeted bispecific antibody ERY974 has shown tumor-suppression activity in mouse studies, and now is in clinical evaluation (NCT02748837). ERY974 is made of OKT3 and hGC33, a humanized mouse monoclonal antibody that targets a C-terminal linear epitope of GPC3 that is close but different from the hYP7 site. Comparing the in vitro cell killing data, hYP7-OKT3-hFc was comparable to ERY974 in terms of potency, with the maximal cell killing being at approximately 100 ng/mL (Fig. 3).

It has been noticed that in some cases a cell surface proximal epitope of the bispecific antibody favors its activity. One of the plausible mechanisms was that the TCR ligand positioning and the inter-cellular spacing between T cells and cancer cells could exert pivotal impact on the optimal activation of T cells. The natural spacing mediated by TCR and its ligand was about 13 nm, which was presumably well suitable for optimal activation of T cell. The maximal threshold of the spacing might be app. 40 nm, which allows the segregation of CD45 on T cells and disabling of its dephosphorylation function that otherwise negatively regulates T cell activation. We therefore raised the question whether this notion applies to GPC3. We selected two representative monoclonal antibodies hYP7 and HN3 that target two different long-range apart epitopes. In vitro cell killing assay showed that the hYP7-based bispecifics overall outperformed HN3 in terms of potency (Fig. 3). We further showed that the potency of hYP7 was not negatively affected by tuning down its affinity to a much lower level than HN3 (Fig. 4). Because the epitope of hYP7 is close to the cell membrane, which once again supports the notion that a cell surface proximal epitope is advantageous for designing potent bispecific antibody.

In addition to the location of the epitope, we also found the structure of the bispecific antibody had major impact on its activity. The scFv-hFc-scFv structure in both hYP7 and HN3 did not perform very well on all the tested cell lines, indicating that this structure may not be ideal although it may have other unique features, e.g. high thermostability and manufacturability.

Surprisingly, even the in vitro activity of the bispecific antibody is quite different; the in vivo efficacy was indistinguishable (Fig. 5C), which suggests that other factors would limit the in vivo activity of bispecific antibody. Further studies on the bio-distribution and pharmacokinetics may shed light on how to maximize the efficacy of bispecific antibodies in vivo.

Unlike antibody drug conjugate or immunotoxin that generally requires high antigen density on the cell surface to efficiently kill cancer cells, bispecific antibody seems do not require high antigen density, which is advantageous for immunotherapy. GPC3 expression level was descended following the order of G1, HepG2, Hep3B, and Huh-7 cells. However, Hep3B cell was most sensitive to bispecific antibody killing, and Huh-7 was not necessarily reluctant to bispecific antibody killing compared to other cell lines (Fig. 3B), even though the GPC3 expression level on Huh-7 is at least one order of magnitude lower than HepG2 and Hep3B. No requirement of high antigen density is a very attractive consideration for designing GPC3-targeted bispecifics, and is beneficial for patients with low GPC3 expression.

Immunotherapy-based combination has demonstrated better efficacy in cancer treatment. Our previous results found that GPC3 immunotoxin combined with Irinotecan was very potent in inhibiting tumor growth. Although this drug has major side effect as systemic administration in HCC patient, it is one of the common options for interventional transarterial chemoembolization (TACE) therapy of HCC and showed a complete response rate of 21.4% in combination with mitomycin in a recent clinical study, which represents a significant advancement than tyrosine kinase inhibitors (e.g. Sorafenib, Lenvatinib, Regorafenib, Cabozantinib, etc.) that typically provides an overall survival time of about 9–12 months. We therefore chose Irinotecan to combine with GPC3 bispecific antibody. The result revealed that this combination was also very potent (Fig. 6). Some of the treated mice in Hep3B and G1 group achieved temporal tumor disappearance. However, the relapse of tumor in the treated mice still happened, and much more mechanistic studies are needed to address the challenge.
