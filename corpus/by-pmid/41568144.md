# Combined prostate cancer vaccine plus immune checkpoint inhibition synergizes to eliminate prostate cancer.

## Abstract

Immunotherapy has improved outcomes in many cancers, yet the clinical benefits remain limited in prostate cancer. We evaluated whether an adenovirus-based bivalent prostate cancer vaccine (Ad-PS2) targeting two tumor antigens could be strengthened by combination with immune checkpoint blockade. Using immunocompetent mouse models, we found that Ad-PS2 combined with low-dose anti-CTLA4 generated robust anti-tumor immunity capable of eliminating established tumors, exceeding the effects of either treatment alone. Tumor-free mice resisted subsequent tumor rechallenge, indicating durable immune protection. Tumor analysis revealed a significant increase in intratumor CD8+ T cell infiltration with Ad-PS2 and anti-CTLA4, whereas anti-PD1 alone produced minimal infiltration and, with the vaccine, provided no therapeutic advantage. These results highlight a mechanistically synergistic interaction between dual antigen-targeted vaccination and CTLA4 blockade and illustrate how rational combination immunotherapy can overcome resistance in prostate cancer. This work defines a strategy that could inform future translational approaches for improving immunologic control of prostate cancer.

Introduction

Prostate cancer is the most common non-cutaneous malignancy among men and remains a leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide. Although early-stage disease is often well-managed with local therapies and androgen deprivation, most patients with advanced prostate cancer eventually develop castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC), a lethal stage with limited treatment options and a median survival of only 2–3 years. Despite decades of research, progress in treating metastatic CRPC has been modest, and outcomes continue to be poor for most patients.

Immunotherapy has transformed cancer treatment in recent years, particularly in malignancies such as melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer, where immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) such as pembrolizumab, ipilimumab, and nivolumab have led to durable responses and improved survival. In contrast, prostate cancer has remained largely unresponsive to ICIs, a failure that has been attributed to its immunologically “cold” tumor microenvironment (TME), characterized by low T cell infiltration, limited neoantigen burden, and active immune suppression.

Therapeutic cancer vaccines provide an opportunity to initiate de novo anti-tumor immunity by targeting tumor-associated antigens, activating cytotoxic T cells, and transforming immunologically inert tumors into immunologically active or “hot” tumors. However, vaccine monotherapy has shown only modest success in clinical trials, underscoring a need for combination approaches that further modulate the immune environment to improve efficacy. Our previous work established the efficacy of a bivalent adenovirus-based vaccine targeting prostate-specific antigen (PSA) and prostate stem cell antigen (PSCA), referred to as Ad-PS2, eliciting robust CD8+ T cell responses and tumor regression in murine models, offering a superior approach compared to single-antigen vaccines.

Nevertheless, given the immune exclusion and suppressive barriers inherent to prostate tumors, we hypothesize that combining Ad-PS2 with immune checkpoint inhibition could produce synergistic benefits. The rationale is that while the vaccine primes a tumor-specific immune T cell response, ICIs—particularly CTLA4 inhibition—could enhance T cell expansion and facilitate their infiltration into the TME. In this study, we evaluate this combination strategy, with the aim of enhancing therapeutic outcomes in prostate cancer and laying the groundwork for translational applications in clinical settings.

Results

Combination therapy of Ad-PS2 vaccine with ICIs

We previously demonstrated the efficacy of the Ad-PS2 vaccine in inhibiting established tumors in mice. To further enhance its therapeutic use and maximize clinical benefit for prostate cancer patients, we investigated the effect of combining Ad-PS2 with ICIs. Following the establishment of tumors, on day 8, tumor-bearing mice received a single subcutaneous injection of the Ad-PS2 vaccine, combined with ICI therapy. Initially, we administered 300 μg of αCTLA4 or αPD1 antibody per injection, once weekly for 3 weeks. Strikingly, the combination of Ad-PS2 and αCTLA4 resulted in complete tumor regression, with 100% of treated mice remaining tumor-free (Figure 1). In contrast, αPD1, either alone or in combination with Ad-PS2, did not improve outcomes. Mice treated with αPD1 alone or with a control vector (Ad-LacZ) developed progressive tumors and eventually succumbed to tumor burden (Figure 1A).

Given the lack of efficacy observed with αPD1 in our model, subsequent experiments focused on αCTLA4. We explored alternative dosing schedules, administering αCTLA4 (100 μg or 200 μg) either 2 days after Ad-PS2 vaccination or at different weekly intervals. Mice received αCTLA4 either at 100 μg twice a week (Monday and Friday) or 200 μg once a week (Monday only) for 3 weeks. In all dosing regimens, the combination of αCTLA4 with Ad-PS2 induced robust anti-tumor responses and completely protected mice from tumor growth. Administration of αCTLA4 prior to Ad-PS2 vaccination led to more rapid tumor clearance. Additionally, combining Ad-PS2 with a higher dose (300 or 200 μg) of αCTLA4 accelerated tumor elimination compared to the combination with 100 μg αCTLA4. In contrast, αCTLA4 alone, as a monotherapy, regardless of dose, offered limited protection, and mice exhibited progressive tumor growth (10/10: 100 μg; 6/10: 200 μg or 4/10: 300 μg). These data underscore the synergy between Ad-PS2 and αCTLA4 in mounting effective anti-tumor immunity (Figure 2).

To further assess the durability of this immune response, we rechallenged tumor-free mice from the Ad-PS2 plus αCTLA4 treatment group (n = 9) on day 93 with a doubled tumor cell dose (1 × 106 cells). No tumor outgrowth was observed through day 128, and all mice remained healthy and tumor-free (Figure 2C). These findings strongly support the potential of Ad-PS2 combined with αCTLA4 to induce long-lasting, possibly permanent, anti-tumor immunity, highlighting its promise as a therapeutic strategy for prostate cancer.

Ad-PS2 vaccine induces CD8 T cell infiltration into the tumors

To understand the mechanism of anti-tumor activity of the Ad-PS2 vaccine, we analyzed tumor tissues for immune cell infiltration. Immunohistochemical staining showed a marked increase in the infiltration of CD8+ T cells in tumors from mice treated with the Ad-PS2 vaccine compared to all other treatment groups (Figure 3). Notably, αCTLA4 monotherapy also led to an elevated presence of CD8+ T cells within the TME. In contrast, the αPD1 treatment did not induce CD8+ T cell infiltration. Since mice were completely cured in the Ad-PS2 and αCTLA4-treated mice, no tumors were available for analysis. We also examined the expression of immune-suppressor cells using markers for FoxP3 (T regulatory cells) and CD163 (tumor-associated macrophages: TAMs), and observed that infiltration of these cells in the tumor was reduced when using the Ad-PS2 vaccine. It was also noted that, with the Ad-PS2 vaccine, CD163-expressing TAMs were mainly located at the tumor periphery, while in the Ad-LacZ control and ICI alone groups, they were within the tumor bed (Figure S1). These observations indicate that the reduced levels of T regulatory cells (FoxP3+) and TAMs (CD163+) in the tumors following the Ad-PS2 vaccine or αCTLA4 may contribute to enhanced anti-tumor immunity. However, further validation is needed to confirm the infiltration patterns of FoxP3 and CD163 cells.

Vaccine-induced histopathological changes and biochemical analysis

To evaluate the functional capacity of various organs following Ad-PS2 vaccine as a monotherapy and in combination with the αCTLA4, we performed histopathologic studies (H&E staining) on the heart, spleen, kidney, and liver. There were no histologic changes in the heart, spleen, or kidney tissues between the age-matched naive mice and the Ad-PS2 vaccine combination. No differences were observed between the vaccine-treated mice and the age-matched naive controls in terms of inflammatory cells or other morphologic characteristics. No tissue necrosis was seen in either group. However, the vaccine-treated mice (Ad-PS2 as monotherapy or in combination) showed an increase in inflammatory cells in the liver compared to the naive group (Figure 4A). The cells mainly consisted of lymphocytes.

To further examine the potential toxic effects of the Ad-PS2 vaccine and combination in the liver, we performed blood chemistry on serum samples. The levels of liver enzymes in the naive mice and the vaccine-treated groups of mice were comparable, with no substantial differences (Figure 4B). Compared to naive mice, the Ad-LacZ control group showed fluctuation, but it was non-significant. Overall, the statistical analysis showed no significant changes in any of the enzymes or the proteins between the groups in the vaccinated mice compared to the naive control mice (Figure 4B). Total bilirubin in all the samples ranged from 0.1 to 0.3 (mg/dL). The lipemia index was normal in all the diluted serum samples.

Discussion

Developing CD8+ T cell-specific responses to tumor cells is considered one of the most effective strategies in cancer immunotherapy. Effector CD8+ T cells (Teff) are particularly valued for their ability to recognize and directly eliminate tumor cells upon antigen engagement. Efforts to harness this cytotoxic potential have primarily focused on two immunotherapeutic strategies: ICIs and cancer vaccines.

ICIs have transformed the treatment landscape, specifically in melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer, where pembrolizumab has produced durable responses and significant improvements in survival. However, this success has not been translated to prostate cancer studies, where clinical trials have consistently demonstrated limited benefit. Similarly, ipilimumab (CTLA4 inhibition) failed to improve overall survival (OS) in multiple settings, despite encouraging immunological activity in early-phase trials in prostate cancer. Together, these findings highlight the limited efficacy of ICIs in prostate cancer and underscore a need for alternative or complementary immunotherapeutic approaches.

Unlike ICIs, which rely on reinvigorating existing T cells, therapeutic cancer vaccines hold significant potential, as they can directly activate the immune system against cancer-specific antigens, enhancing CD8+ T cell responses and facilitating more effective tumor recognition and destruction. By priming tumor-specific CD8+ T cells, vaccines may transform immunologically “cold” tumors into “hot” ones, enhancing immune cell infiltration and sensitizing tumors to subsequent immune-based interventions, including checkpoint inhibition. Beyond generating immediate cytotoxic activity, vaccine-induced responses may also promote long-term immune surveillance through the development of memory T cells, which could contribute to durable tumor control and reduce recurrence risk over time.

Several vaccine platforms have been investigated in prostate cancer. Sipuleucel-T (Provenge), an autologous cellular immunotherapy that targets prostatic acid phosphatase (PAP), demonstrated a modest but significant OS benefit, leading to its FDA approval. However, the adoption of Sipuleucel-T has been limited due to cost, logistical challenges in manufacturing, and a lack of clear surrogate endpoints. PROSTVAC (PSA-TRICOM) is a poxvirus-based cancer vaccine engineered to express PSA along with three costimulatory molecules (B7.1, ICAM-1, and LFA-3) to enhance T cell activation. Early results were promising; however, enthusiasm diminished following the phase III PROSPECT trial, which failed to meet its primary OS endpoint, leading to its discontinuation as a standalone therapy.

Adenovirus-based vaccines, adenovirus serotype 5 (Ad), have also been explored as a form of immunotherapy and have demonstrated the ability to generate strong immune responses in murine models of colon cancer, breast cancer, and melanoma. Several adenovirus-based vaccines are currently in clinical trials or therapeutic use. A phase I clinical trial of Ad5-PSA as monotherapy elicited anti-PSA T cell responses in 68% of patients but did not result in significant tumor regression or survival benefit. More recently, the phase I study of the Ad5-PSA/MUC-1/Brachyury vaccine showed a favorable safety profile and induced measurable T cell activation, but the clinical efficacy remained limited. These findings underscore the immunogenic potential of Ad5-based vaccines, while also highlighting the need for strategies that enhance their clinical efficacy.

The limited efficacy of cancer vaccines as monotherapy in prostate cancer reflects the broader challenges faced by ICIs, which also failed to produce substantial clinical benefits when used as a single agent. Based on such observations, there has been a shift toward combination approaches to enhance immune activation and achieve clinical benefits. Preclinical and early clinical studies have suggested that such combinations may improve T cell priming and infiltration, thereby offering a more effective and sustained immunotherapeutic response. However, most ICI trials combined with prostate cancer vaccines (e.g., Sipuleucel-T targeting PAP or PROSTVAC-VF targeting PSA) remain in various stages of development.

We previously demonstrated that the bivalent adenovirus-based vaccine targeting both PSA and PSCA (Ad-PS2) effectively eliminated established prostate tumors in murine models. Notably, formulating this vaccine in Surgifoam (Gelfoam) significantly enhanced its immunogenicity, leading to a robust CD8+ T cell response. Here, we evaluated the Ad-PS2 vaccine in combination with ICIs targeting PD-1 and CTLA4. We found that pairing the vaccine with αCTLA4 therapy resulted in complete tumor eradication, whereas combining it with αPD-1 therapy failed to improve tumor clearance. In contrast, αCTLA4 treatment facilitated CD8+ T cell infiltration, resulting in tumor growth inhibition. These findings are consistent with both clinical and preclinical evidence showing that prostate cancer is largely resistant to PD-1 inhibition. Unlike immunologically “hot” tumors such as melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer, which respond favorably to PD-1 inhibition, prostate tumors present an immunosuppressive and poorly infiltrated (“cold”) microenvironment. PD-1 inhibitors are designed to reinvigorate exhausted T cells and restore T cell function. In our model, the Ad-PS2 vaccine alone was insufficient to overcome this immune exclusion, and PD-1 blockade did not support T cell trafficking into the tumor. Thus, prostate cancer’s resistance to PD-1 therapy may be driven less by T cell exhaustion and more by the physical exclusion of T cells from the TME.

By contrast, CTLA4 inhibition may offer a more effective strategy in presence of antigenic CD8+ T cells, where CTLA4 inhibition lifts inhibitory signals, allowing vaccine-induced T cells to expand and function effectively. Adenoviral vaccines are known to activate dendritic cells (DCs) and enhance antigen presentation. By targeting both PSA and PSCA, Ad-PS2 primes a robust, antigen-specific CD8+ T cell response against prostate cancer. However, without a mechanism to support intra-tumoral infiltration, these T cells remain largely ineffective at the tumor site. CTLA4 inhibition acts on two key fronts: first, by inhibiting the interaction between CD28 on T cells and B7 ligands on antigen-presenting cells (APCs), it amplifies T cell expansion; and second, by reducing the number and function of immunosuppressive regulatory T cells (Tregs), it lowers the threshold for effective anti-tumor immunity. Meanwhile, the Ad-PS2 vaccine augments this effect by promoting DC maturation and upregulating co-stimulatory signals, thereby further enhancing the anti-tumor immune response. While both the Ad-PS2 vaccine and CTLA4 inhibition alone induce comparable levels of CD8+ T cell infiltration, neither is effective in controlling tumor growth as a single agent. This suggests that the synergy observed with the combination is not solely due to increased T cell trafficking. Rather, CTLA4 inhibition likely enhances the functional activation of vaccine-primed T cells by blocking CTLA4’s interaction with CD80/86 on DCs, thereby freeing these APCs to provide stronger co-stimulatory signals via CD28, while concurrently dismantling local immunosuppression. Additionally, we are intrigued by the observation of a shift in the distribution of CD163+ TAMs following the Ad-PS2 vaccine, indicating a change in the TME. Redistribution of TAMs toward the periphery is likely to impede the direct suppression of cytotoxic T cell function within the tumor, allowing CD8+ T cells to target the tumor cells effectively. However, the alteration in the spatial organization of immunosuppressive cells within the TME following Ad-PS2 vaccine requires further validation. Together, these complementary mechanisms may explain the synergy observed in our model, facilitating an effective anti-tumor immunity resulting in complete tumor regression.

An additional key finding of our study is that combining the vaccine with CTLA4 inhibition not only enhances immune responses but also mitigates the toxicity associated with CTLA4 inhibition. CTLA4 inhibitors, while effective, are often limited by immune-related adverse events (irAEs), making long-term use in prostate cancer a challenge. However, by pairing CTLA4 inhibition with the Ad-PS2 bivalent vaccine, we observed a synergistic effect that enabled tumor eradication at a lower dose of CTLA4 inhibitor, thereby reducing toxicity while maintaining full therapeutic efficacy. Mild hepatic lymphocytic infiltration was observed without biochemical abnormalities, suggesting localized immune activation rather than toxicity. We note that such findings are consistent with transient hepatic immune stimulation seen in prior adenoviral vaccine studies. However, all liver enzyme values remained within normal limits. These findings support further evaluation of vaccine-checkpoint inhibitor combinations in clinical trials, with a particular focus on optimizing efficacy while minimizing toxicity.

Limitations of the study

While our findings demonstrate a strong anti-tumor efficacy in a mouse prostate cancer model, there are several limitations. Our study uses mouse tumors engineered to express human PSA and PSCA, which may not recapitulate the antigen diversity or tumor-immune cell complexity of human prostate cancer. ICIs such as CTLA4 inhibitors are known to cause immune-related toxicities in humans, therefore, a comprehensive safety assessment will be essential to translate this preclinical approach into clinical practice. Further investigation on combination therapies using the Ad-PS2 vaccine could help optimize efficacy and potentially open new treatment modalities in metastatic or CRPC, where ICIs and other forms of immunotherapy have shown limited benefit. Future work should also demonstrate the cytotoxicity of ICIs by measuring cell death in human cells, such as by using DCs in training or T-cells co-cultured with DCs, followed by co-culture of T-cells with tumor cells.

Experimental model and study participant details

Mice

Six-to eight-week-old male BALB/c mice (National Cancer Institute, USA) were used for all experiments. Mice were housed in pathogen-free facilities under controlled temperature and humidity with a 12-h light/dark cycle and provided ad libitum access to food and water. All procedures were performed in accordance with protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee, Medical College of Wisconsin, Milwaukee, WI (Karan #AUA00006411).

Cell lines and tumor model

The RM11 mouse prostate tumor cell line expressing human prostate-specific antigen (PSA) and prostate stem cell antigen (PSCA) was used and maintained in cell culture media with Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM; Cat #10567014) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Cat #A5256901) and 0.05 mg/mL of gentamicin (Cat #15710064) from ThermoFisher Scientific, USA, Briefly, RM11 mouse prostate tumor cell line was kindly provided by Dr. David Lubaroff, University of Iowa, as described in our previous study. Cells were routinely tested for mycoplasma and maintained under standard sterile conditions. All experiments were performed with a mycoplasma-free tumor cell line and were authenticated using short tandem repeat (STR) profiling within the last three years prior to the experiments (Labcorp, Burlington, NC, USA).

Method details

Mouse strain and tumor lines

Six to eight-week-old male BALB/c mice were purchased from the National Cancer Institute and housed in filtered cages. RM11 mouse prostate tumor cells, established to express human prostate-specific antigen (PSA) and prostate stem cell antigen (PSCA), were maintained in regular culture media of Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 0.05 mg/mL of gentamicin (Cat #15710064) from ThermoFisher Scientific, USA.

Vaccine, vaccination, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) antibody, and tumor growth study

Development and characterization of the adenovirus type 5 (Ad5) expressing human PSA and PSCA antigens (Ad-PS2 bivalent vaccine) is described previously. Briefly, 100 μL of cell suspensions containing 2x105 or 5x105 viable tumor cells were injected subcutaneously into the flank of the BALB/c mice. Anti-mouse immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) antibodies for αCTLA4 (Cat. #BP0164; Clone #9D9) and αPD1 (Cat. #BP0146; Clone # RMP1-14) were purchased from BioXcell (Lebanon, NH, USA). Tumor-bearing mice were randomized once the tumor nodules reached 60–70 mm in size and were immunized once with the Ad-PS2 vaccine (1 × 108 pfu), while the αCTLA4 or αPD1 antibodies were administered via intraperitoneal injection. Tumor outgrowth was measured thrice a week, and tumor volume was calculated as described. Each experimental group consisted of five mice and repeated at least twice with similar results. Mice experiments were performed in accordance with guidelines and regulations approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee, Medical College of Wisconsin, Milwaukee, WI (Karan #AUA00006411).

Immunohistochemistry (IHC) and image analysis for tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells

Mouse tumors were formalin-fixed, and IHC was performed at the Histopathology Core (Children’s Research Institute; Medical College of Wisconsin) using an automated system. After antigen retrieval with EDTA, the mouse tumor tissue sections were stained with αCD8 (Cat. #ab209775 at 1:2000 dilution), αCD163 (Cat. #ab182422 at 1:500 dilution) antibody from Abcam (MA, USA), and αFoxP3 (Cat. #12653 at 1:600 dilution) from Cell Signaling. The slides were scanned using a Hamamatsu whole-slide scanner with NDPI Scan Software (version 4.2) at 40x magnification to produce a high-resolution digital image of the entire slide. An automated program within the Visiopharm software (version 4.1) was used to count the tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells.

Histopathological and biochemical analysis of serum

The vital organs, including the heart, spleen, kidney, and liver, were examined using hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining. A small piece of fresh tissue was collected, fixed in 10% formalin, and processed for H&E staining by the pathology core. An expert urologic pathologist analyzed the H&E-stained sections blindly. Blood was drawn, and serum was sent to IDEXX BioAnalytics (www.idexxbioanalytics.com) for analyzing the serum levels of alkaline phosphatase (ALP), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), creatine kinase (CK), albumin (ALB), bilirubin (conjugated, unconjugated, and total), gamma-glutamyltransferase (GGT), total protein (TP), and the lipemia index. Age-matched naive and the Ad-PS2 vaccine immunized mice underwent tissue and blood collection on day 35, while the group receiving a combination of the Ad-PS2 and αCTLA4 (100 μg) had tissue and blood collection on day 90 (tumor-free mice).

Quantification and statistical analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 9 software (9.3.0). Multiple comparisons between groups for significant differences were performed using ordinary one-way ANOVA, substituted for Tukey’s multiple comparisons, and a nonparametric Kruskal-Wallis test.
