# Deciphering enemy tactics - the narrow path to an optimal anti-cancer strategy targeting the Warburg effect.

## Abstract

Metabolic changes in cancer cells are crucial for maintaining their high growth and proliferation rate. As a result, many tumors are characterized by high glucose consumption and intensified aerobic glycolysis, a phenomenon known as the Warburg effect. Through the Warburg effect, cancer cells can rapidly acquire energy, obtain intermediates for biosynthesis, and ensure a source of NAD+ for oxidized biomass synthesis. Altered metabolism and the Warburg effect are characteristic features not only of most transformed proliferating cells but also of normal, rapidly dividing cells, thus posing a challenge for potential anticancer strategies disrupting cellular metabolism. Therefore, targeting the Warburg effect requires a carefully considered strategy so as not to affect the basal metabolism of normal cells and prevent the various side effects in the patient commonly observed with classical chemotherapies targeting DNA replication. On the other hand, strategies/agents that slow metabolic rate are likely to be less toxic to normal cells than to highly metabolically deregulated cancer cells. The aim of this work is to discuss the most optimal approach for inhibiting these favorable metabolic changes in cancer cells while ensuring specificity. The work discusses proteins, enzymes and pathways that, according to the current state of knowledge, can be optimal candidates for cancer specific targeting such as: HK2, PKM2, PFKFB3, PFKFB4, NAD+ de novo metabolism, NADH oxidation, MCT4, MCT1, LDHA and LDHB. In the era of rapid progress in diagnostic tools providing more and more data on molecular changes, the therapeutic strategy should take into account not only the specificity of the cancer, but also a personalized, optimal approach for each individual patient. This article presents an overview, including available databases, showing the heterogeneity of expression of genes involved in metabolic reprogramming among various cancer patients, which clearly suggests the need to develop a specific theranostic approach for targeting the Warburg effect in a personalized manner. Introduction

The majority of tumors exhibit a pronounced dependence on glycolysis, even in the presence of sufficient oxygen. This preference for fermentative glucose metabolism—known as the Warburg effect—is a hallmark of cancer and reflects a profound metabolic reprogramming. While glucose is a major energy source for many cell types, not all cells rely on it equally. Tissue-specific metabolic programs may instead favor substrates such as fatty acids or glutamine, depending on physiological context. Nevertheless, in rapidly proliferating cancer cells, glycolysis becomes the dominant pathway, supporting not only ATP production but also the biosynthetic and redox demands of uncontrolled growth. Importantly, this metabolic shift is not a consequence of defective mitochondria. Oxidative phosphorylation often remains active and operates alongside lactate production, indicating that the Warburg effect does not represent a simple switch from respiration to aerobic glycolysis. Rather, it reflects a broader reprogramming of cellular metabolism. A key advantage of aerobic glycolysis lies in its capacity for rapid ATP generation, which supports high rates of membrane transport and biosynthesis. However, glucose in cancer cells is not only oxidized for energy—it also serves as a carbon source for the anabolic synthesis of nucleotides, amino acids, and lipids. These biosynthetic demands directly compete with lactate production, suggesting that the Warburg effect is not an incidental by-product of proliferation, but rather an integrated component of metabolic strategy.

A central factor driving lactate production in this context is the need to regenerate NAD⁺ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide, oxidized form). Continuous biosynthetic activity requires a stable cytosolic NAD⁺ pool to maintain redox balance and sustain high glycolytic throughput. Under physiological conditions, cells regenerate NAD⁺ via mitochondrial shuttle systems—primarily the malate–aspartate shuttle (MAS) and the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle (G3PS)—which transfer reducing equivalents from cytosolic NADH into mitochondria. However, when glycolysis is highly active, or when anabolic demand outpaces the capacity of these shuttle systems, they become saturated. In such conditions, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) catalyzes the reduction of pyruvate to lactate, thereby oxidizing NADH back to NAD⁺ and allowing glycolysis and biosynthesis to proceed uninterrupted. This is particularly important as NAD⁺ is also consumed in anabolic reactions such as serine and glycine biosynthesis, which feed into nucleotide production via one-carbon metabolism. Beyond its metabolic role, lactate functions as an oncometabolite. It contributes to tumor growth and survival by modulating the tumor microenvironment, suppressing immune responses, and altering gene expression through epigenetic mechanisms. Certain tumors can also utilize extracellular lactate as a fuel source—a phenomenon known as the reverse Warburg effect—further highlighting its functional versatility. In summary, the Warburg effect is not a metabolic anomaly, but rather a multifaceted and adaptive reprogramming that supports tumor progression on multiple levels: energy production, biomass generation, redox maintenance, and immune evasion. This review aims to explore the complex landscape of cancer metabolism, with a focus on identifying key metabolic enzymes and pathways that are altered across tumor types and may represent viable therapeutic targets. By integrating current literature with data from publicly available clinical datasets, we propose a rational framework for prioritizing metabolic targets based on their expression profiles, prognostic relevance, and functional roles in tumor biology.

Background and the aim of the work

In order to find the optimal approach to target an altered metabolism of a cancer cell, the problem should be analyzed from many different perspectives. Certainly, targeting tumor-specific enzymes and proteins involved in metabolic alterations would be a desirable strategy and will be discussed in this paper. Taking into account the complexity of the problem, the current work is an attempt to identify the constellation of anticancer agents, that may constitute the most optimal way for achieving the optimal therapeutic effect. The selection of targets discussed in this work is based on their central role in the metabolic reprogramming of cancer cells and/or their limited expression or activity in normal, non-transformed tissues. Each of the chosen molecules fulfills one or more of the following criteria: (1) critical involvement in glycolysis or associated metabolic pathways, (2) overexpression in a wide range of tumor types, (3) contribution to anabolic biosynthesis, redox balance, and proliferation, and (4) demonstrated therapeutic vulnerability in preclinical or clinical models. Figure 1A presents a diagram in which we have marked, on the core metabolic pathways, the enzymes selected for this review, in order to indicate at which specific steps these enzymes function. The integration of literature data with publicly available clinical datasets provides a more comprehensive perspective on the therapeutic relevance of proposed targets.

In our review, we compiled figures presenting data on the differential expression of selected genes between cancerous and normal cells across a panel of tumor types. Additionally, we utilized publicly available databases to demonstrate the relationship between the expression levels of these targets and overall patient survival in various cancer types. In this way, our literature-based analysis is directly supported by clinical data, allowing the reader to simultaneously assess both the expression patterns and the prognostic significance of the selected targets. In the era of rapid development of molecular diagnostic tools, the growing possibilities of personalized medicine, which allows to look at the problem from the perspective of different types of cancer and individual oncological patients, constitute more and more required and accessible way to find the most effective and safe treatment regimen.

Criteria for literature selection

The references cited in this review were selected based on a focused literature search aimed at exploring the molecular and metabolic foundations of cancer–associated glycolysis, particularly in relation to the Warburg effect. Searches were conducted using PubMed, Google Scholar, and Scopus, with a combination of keywords including: “Warburg effect”, “aerobic glycolysis”, “cancer metabolism”, “oncogenic signaling and metabolism”, “glucose uptake in tumors”, “lactate production“ and “metabolic reprogramming in cancer”. The aim was to identify both seminal papers that first described these associations and recent advances that expanded our understanding of the regulatory mechanisms involved. Additional references were selected by manual screening of citations in key review and original research articles.

Genomic databases and analytical platforms applied

As part of our analyses, we utilized the R2: Genomics Analysis and Visualization Platform (http://r2.amc.nl), developed at the Academic Medical Center (AMC) in Amsterdam. This web–based platform enables comprehensive exploration of transcriptomic data derived from a wide range of publicly available cancer datasets. R2 offers a broad spectrum of analytical tools, including gene expression profiling, co-expression analysis, correlation analysis between genes and clinical parameters, as well as survival analysis. In our study, we employed both correlation analyses and Kaplan-Meier survival analyses to investigate the relationship between the expression of selected genes involved in metabolic reprogramming and overall patient survival across various cancer types.

For survival analysis, the median expression value was used as a cutoff to divide samples into high and low expression groups. In correlation analyses, we examined associations between gene expression in order to uncover potential co–regulated metabolic pathways and interactions that may play a role in tumor progression.

To investigate differences in the expression levels of selected genes involved in metabolic reprogramming between cancer cells and their corresponding normal cells across various cancer types, we used the UCSC Xena Browser platform (https://xenabrowser.net/). This tool enables interactive analysis of publicly available multi-omic data as well as clinical and phenotypic data from various sources, including TCGA and GTEx. The analysis was performed using a panel of cancers, comparing gene expression levels in tumor samples relative to matched normal tissue samples.

Specific glycolysis targeting

Hexokinase 2 (HK2) — a tumor-specific gatekeeper of glycolysis

Glucose metabolism via glycolysis occurs in every cell of the body. Therefore, targeting the heightened glucose metabolism in cancer cells requires inhibiting enzymes that are predominantly or selectively expressed in tumors. Among the glycolytic enzymes that are overexpressed in cancer cells are Hexokinase 2 (HK2) and Pyruvate Kinase Muscle isozyme (PKM2). In mammals, five hexokinase isoenzymes have been identified: HK1, HK2, HK3, HK4, and the recently discovered HKDC1. While HK1 is the most common isoenzyme in all cells, HK2 is often highly expressed in tumors but is also found in muscle, fat cells and heart cells. All hexokinase isoenzymes are located in the cytosol, but HK1 and HK2 can bind to the mitochondrial membrane through their N–terminal region, offering a cytoprotective effect in both normal and cancer cells. Under hypoxic conditions, HK2 forms an active complex with the TIGAR protein, enhancing its activity. TIGAR, a fructose–2,6–bisphosphatase, indirectly inhibits glycolysis by breaking down fructose–2,6–bisphosphate, key glycolysis activator, thereby redirecting glucose to the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP). This process diminished stress caused by hypoxia and re–oxygenation, enabling cells to survive in low–oxygen environments. Numerous studies have shown that high HK2 expression in cancer patients is associated with worse prognosis, tumor progression and treatment resistance. On the other hand, inhibiting HK2 function or expression has been shown to suppress tumor growth and increase sensitivity to therapy. Inhibiting HK2 is significant because it would not only disrupt glucose metabolism via glycolysis but also interfere with glucose utilization in the oxidative and non–oxidative arm of pentose phosphate pathway and glycosylation processes. However, to date, efforts to inhibit the HK2 enzyme have not yielded the desired clinical outcomes. Several studies have explored HK2 inhibition using the glucose analog 2–deoxyglucose. Although both in vitro and in vivo studies showed encouraging results, the compound’s side effects and toxicity led to the suspension of clinical trials. Another compound, Lonidamine, also targeting HK2, advanced to clinical trials but proved ineffective as a single agent. It is now known that Lonidamine not only inhibits HK2 but targets energy metabolism through a number of other activities such as inhibition of the monocarboxylate transporter, mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC), respiratory chain complex I/II and mitochondrial permeability transition (PT) pore. Moreover, it’s important to emphasize that both compounds inhibit multiple hexokinases, not just HK2, which limits their therapeutic potential as selective HK2 inhibitors. Thus, targeting HK2 in cancer remains promising, but the therapeutic success depends on overcoming the challenge of its selective inhibition. One potential approach may involve combination therapy, in which a selective HK2 inhibitor enhances the efficacy of standard chemotherapeutic agents.

Pyruvate kinase M2 (PKM2) — metabolic flexibility in tumor survival

Another enzyme that could potentially serve as a target for cancer therapy is pyruvate kinase (PK). This enzyme exists in four isoforms derived from two genes. Particularly important for cancer cells is the PKM gene, which encodes the muscle isoenzyme of pyruvate kinase. Due to alternative splicing of the PKM gene, two isoforms are produced: PKM1 and PKM2. PKM1 promotes oxidative phosphorylation and is expressed in tissues with high metabolic demand, such as the heart, brain, and skeletal muscles, while PKM2 is primarily expressed in embryonic tissues and tumors, where it promotes aerobic glycolysis. PKM2 can exist in three forms: monomeric, dimeric, and tetrameric. The monomeric form of PKM2 is inactive, the dimeric form exhibits low activity, allowing the accumulation of glycolysis intermediates used for the biosynthesis of lipids, amino acids, and nucleotides necessary for cell proliferation. The switching between the low–activity dimeric form and the highly–active tetrameric form enables cancer cells to adapt to changes in their microenvironment. For example, serine accumulation promotes tetramerization of PKM2 thereby increasing the rate of glucose metabolism via glycolysis, while the serine depletion will lead to the appearance of a low–activity dimer, and thus more 3–phosphoglycerate will be directed to the serine synthesis pathway. Another example concerns hypoxia, in this case PKM2 in the form of a dimer is translocated to the cell nucleus, where, by influencing the accumulation of HIF-1 transcription factor, promotes angiogenesis. The fact that PKM2 is highly expressed in tumors and allows metabolic adaptation to environmental conditions makes this enzyme a promising target for cancer therapy. In fact, a number of studies have shown that inhibition of PKM2 expression inhibits proliferation, induces apoptosis, and increases the sensitivity of cancer cells to chemotherapy. On the other hand, if the dimeric form, which provides substrates for biosynthesis during proliferation and, through the possibility of translocation to the cell nucleus, induces the activity of genes important for cancer cells, is replaced with a tetramer, then this form of PKM2 may also have an inhibitory effect on cancer cells, as shown by independent studies. In this context, it appears that modulating PKM2 activity in cancer cells may be of significant importance in the development of oncological therapy specifically affecting the metabolism of the cancer cell. It also seems that changing in the form of PKM2 (tetramer–dimer–tetramer) may have particular significance during the micorenviromental changes related to oxygen partial pressure during carcinogenesis.

Enzymes at the crossroads — PFKFB isoenzymes in cancer metabolism

PFK-II isoenzymes and their metabolic functions in cancer

Phosphofructokinase II (PFK–II), or more specifically 6–phosphofructo–2–kinase/fructose–2,6–bisphosphatase, is another enzyme, related to the process of glycolysis, which overexpression has been observed in numerous types of cancer. PFK–II represents a group of enzymes with dual function, containing both kinase and phosphatase domains in the same protein complex. The action of the kinase domain results in the phosphorylation of fructose–6–phosphate, producing fructose–2,6–bisphosphate (F–2,6–BP), which is a key allosteric activator of phosphofructokinase I, catalyzing the most important rate-limiting step of glycolysis. Therefore, the activity of the kinase domain will increase glycolytic activity, which not only meets the cell’s energy needs but also supplies intermediates necessary for biosynthesis. Meanwhile, the activity of the phosphatase domain, through the dephosphorylation of fructose–2,6–bisphosphate, will slow down glycolysis, promoting the usage of glucose–6–phosphate in the pentose phosphate pathway. This process is accompanied by the production of NADPH, which is necessary not only for the reductive biosynthesis, but also for cell’s defense against reactive oxygen species, by maintaining the optimal levels of reduced glutathione. Importantly, the activation of the pentose phosphate pathway by the phosphatase domain does not completely inhibit glycolysis; instead, it shifts glucose processing toward glyceraldehyde–3–phosphate. This intermediate, in further transformations, provides 3–phosphoglycerate for serine synthesis, which is a precursor of glycine and a donor of one–carbon fragments for tetrahydrofolate (THF), thereby supplying carbon atoms for nucleotide synthesis. Phosphofructokinase II’s enzyme activity is driven by the presence of different PFK–II isoenzymes encoded by four genes PFKFB1, PFKFB2, PFKFB3 and PFKFB4, which vary widely in their tissue–specific expression, regulation, and the balance of activity between their functional domains. Additionally, the presence of alternative promoters and alternative splicing processes allows for the expression of various isoforms for each gene. Even though the catalytic domains are highly homologous, the isoenzymes and isoforms of PFK–II exhibit unique regulatory and catalytic characteristics. Therefore, their co–expression within cells results in a collaborative regulation of fructose–2,6–bisphosphate levels.

Expression and prognostic relevance of PFKFB3 and PFKFB4 in cancer

Importantly, a number of literature reports indicate that overexpression of PFKFB3 and PFKFB4 isoenzymes is commonly observed in cancer cells. It has been shown, that PFKFB3 exhibits the highest kinase domain activity, which results in stimulation of glycolytic flux, and in consequence promotion of cancer growth. On the contrary, inhibition of PFKFB3 activity led to suppression of glycolytic activity, reduced viability, cell proliferation and migration, and induction of apoptosis. As PFKFB3 can influence the proliferation of cells also in glycolysis–independent manner, studies have shown, that inhibition or silencing of PFKFB3 lead to cell cycle arrest of cells and apoptosis induction, by impairing Akt dependent DNA repair or by inhibition of CDK1 activity and stabilization of p27 respectively. The effect of the PFKFB4 isoenzyme on cancer cell metabolism appears to be more diverse than that of PFKFB3, due to the fact that the catalytic activities of both domains of PFKFB4 are more balanced. Studies show, that selective inhibition of PFKFB4 expression resulted in a diverse response of cells depending on a type of cancer. For example in several cancer cell lines and in lung adenocarcinoma, PFKFB4 inhibition led to reduction of fructose–2,6–bisphosphate levels, and was associated with decreased glucose uptake and tumor growth. On the other hand, in prostate cancer cells and p53–deficient cells it led to an increase in F–2,6–BP levels, leading to reduction of biosynthesis intermediates and accumulation of reactive oxygen species, suggesting that in this type of cancer, the isoenzyme primarily exhibits phosphatase activity. Notably, silencing PFKFB4 only disrupted the pentose phosphate pathway in prostate cancer cells but not in healthy tissue, highlighting the key role of this isoenzyme in cancer development. However, like PFKFB3, PFKFB4 plays several important roles in cancer cells in addition to balancing glycolysis and the PPP. PFKFB4 can also regulate autophagy or influence signaling pathways important for growth of cancer cells. Notably, numerous studies have shown, that mostly high expression of PFKFB3 and PFKFB4 correlates with shorter survival of cancer patients. Interestingly, a study published in Nature showed that high PFKFB4 expression, on the contrary, correlates with better overall survival in colon adenocarcinoma patients. Similar effect was observed in neuroblastoma patients, where high PFKFB4 also correlated with better overall survival (OS), and the effect was even more pronounced with simultaneous low PFKFB3 expression. Moreover, co–silencing of both PFK–II isoenzymes (PFKFB3 and PFKFB4) led to a reduction in F–2,6–BP levels and prevention of PFKFB4 deletion–induced cell apoptosis, which highlights the importance of an appropriate balance between glycolysis and PPP in prostate cancer. Regardless of the molecular mechanism of action of PFKFB3/4 isoenzymes above mentioned studies clearly indicates that in analyzing the effect of PFK–II on the prognosis of cancer patients, the expression of both PFKFB3/4 isozymes should be considered. Furthermore, modulating their mutual activity may be of significant importance for the development of new therapeutic strategies specifically targeting cancer metabolism.

The NAD⁺ dependency of cancer: opportunity for precision therapy

NAD⁺ – a core metabolite linking energy, growth, and DNA integrity

NAD⁺ is involved not only in the transport of reduction equivalents during oxidation processes that drive energy production but is also a key molecule required for the synthesis of oxidized biomass, such as nucleotides and amino acids. Recent data have also shown that the consumption rate of NAD+ in cancer cells exceeds the rate of ATP synthesis. As a result, the energy from the proton gradient is not utilized rapidly enough for efficient ATP production. This leads to a low NAD+/NADH ratio, limiting energy–releasing processes and anabolic synthesis in which oxidized biomass is produced. The requirement of high NAD+/NADH ratio also results in fermentation process and production of lactate. It is worth noting that triggering the ATP hydrolysis, which produces ADP, i.e. a substrate for ATP synthase,—leads to an increase in the NAD⁺/NADH ratio and, in parallel, an increase in cell proliferation. It should be emphasized that the action of NAD+ coenzyme is not confined to the red/ox reactions, but it is also a subject to continuous circulation in NAD+ consuming reactions catalyzed by three classes of enzymes: the NAD+ glycohydrolases (CD38, CD157 and SARM1), the protein deacetylases family of sirtuins and Poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARPS), with broad spectrum of cellular functions. Moreover, NAD+ serves as a nucleotide analog in the process of DNA ligation and RNA capping. NAD is also involved in DNA repair and its level may influence sensitivity of cells to DNA-damaging agents, the same determining the response to chemotherapeutics. For example, as shown by Ruszkiewicz et al., a high level of NAD+ can protect cancer cells from alkylation damage. Therefore, it seems that maintaining NAD+ homeostasis is crucial for the cancer cell considering its role in both anabolic and catabolic processes, DNA repair and protection of genetic material against damage, which is induced both as a result of increased oxidative stress and in response to chemotherapy. Thus, strategies aimed at limiting NAD⁺ availability may serve as a foundation for the design of new drugs that specifically target the altered metabolism of cancer cells.

From pathway to target: the therapeutic promise of NAD⁺ metabolic interference

Undoubtedly, because of the high demand for NAD+, cancer cells must maintain high rate of de novo synthesis of this compound and targeting the enzymes involved in these processes may be important to slow down the growth of cancer cells. Figure 1B presents possible routes for de novo NAD+ synthesis. NAD+ can be synthesized from tryptophan (Trp) by the kynurenine pathway (KP), another route is the Preiss–Handler pathway starting from nicotinic acid, which, through the sequential action of two enzymes – nicotinic acid phosphoribosyltransferase (NAPRT) and nicotinic mononucleotide adenyltransferase (NMNAT) is converted to nicotinic acid adenine dinucleotide (NAAD). In salvage pathway, nicotinamide generated as a side product of the enzymatic activities of NAD+–consuming enzymes is recycled into NAD+. Initially, NAMPT (nicotinamide phosphoribosyltransferase) converts nicotinamide into nicotinamide mononucloetide (NAM), which is then converted to NAD+ by NMNAT. To meet both, the redox and non–redox NAD+ requirements, cancer cells need to maintain high levels of NAD+, enhancing their synthesis primarily through the NAD+ salvage pathway. NAMPT, a rate–limiting enzyme for this pathway, has been identified as an oncogene in some types of cancer. In various malignancies like prostate cancer, ovarian cancer, colorectal cancer, melanoma, breast cancer, myeloma, and gastric cancer, NAMPT is often increased, having an impact on the available NAD+ pool. Therefore, NAMPT is considered, as a potential therapeutic target for anti–cancer therapy. Inhibition of NAMPT not only caused a decrease in the availability of NAD+ but also a decrease in ATP levels. Consequently, this led to proliferation inhibition and cancer cells death, mainly as a result of apoptosis.Therefore, in recent years, a number NAMPT–specific inhibitors have been developed, and their effectiveness has been confirmed in cancer cells. These inhibitors were particularly effective on cell lines with IDH1/2 mutations. This is due to the fact that IDH1/2 mutants convert α–ketoglutarate to D–2–hydroxyglutarate (D–2HG), which is an oncometabolite promoting hypermethylation of the NAPRT gene promoter. Nevertheless, NAMPT inhibitors, despite promising preclinical results, show low efficacy and high toxicity in clinical trials, which slowed down the development of new drugs. To improve efficacy and reduce toxicity, combination therapies and dual–target strategies are suggested. Targeted drug delivery and innovative therapeutic strategies have the potential to significantly enhance treatment outcomes. However, the limited efficacy of these approaches in clinical trials suggests that cancer cells might express additional NAD⁺–producing enzymes. Recent studies highlight the relevance of NAPRT, the rate–limiting enzyme in the Preiss–Handler pathway, in a wide range of human cancers. A comparison of the expression patterns of NAPRT and NAMPT genes in human tumor and non–tumor tissues reveals notable variability, which is dependent on the tissue of origin. Thus, tumors developing from tissues highly expressing NAPRT will be less dependent on NAMPT conversely, to tumors from tissues with low NAPRT expression. In fact, it was shown in vivo, that ovarian cancer xenografts have been less sensitive to the NAMPT inhibitor FK866 if NAPRT enzyme was overexpressed, whereas sensitivity was restored once NAPRT had been inhibited. NAPRT can be inhibited by compounds such as 2–hydroxynicotinic acid (2–HNA), an analogue of nicotinic acid competitively blocking the NAPRT enzyme and indeed it has been observed that this compound can potentiate the cytotoxic effect of the NAMPT inhibitor FK866. It seems that, in light of the above, the use of de novo NAD⁺ synthesis inhibitors in cancer therapy must be preceded by a thorough understanding of the expression profiles of key enzymes.

Inhibition of malic enzyme in cancer and beyond

The malic enzyme is a crucial cellular enzyme providing optimal levels of NADPH, essential not only for reductive biosynthesis but also for protection against reactive oxygen species (ROS). It is worth noting that the malic enzyme is not the only source of NADPH in the cell, as this reduced coenzyme is also formed in a reaction catalyzed by glucose–6–phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) and 6–phosphogluconate dehydrogenase (6PGD) of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP), malic isocitrate dehydrogenases (IDHs1 and IDH2) and also enzymes in the one–carbon–tetrahydrofolate pathway. The malic enzyme exists in three isoforms: ME1, ME2, and ME3, where ME1 is a cytosolic NADP⁺–dependent isoform, ME2 is a mitochondrial isoform with a preference for NAD⁺, and ME3 is a mitochondrial NADP⁺–dependent isoform. In fact, ME1 is responsible for the production of NADPH in an amount similar to glucose–6–phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD), the major source of NADPH in normal cell. Of note, a number of studies have reported higher ME1 expression in cancer cells compared to adjacent non–tumor tissue. It has also been shown that in basal–like breast cancer (BLBC) malic enzyme 1 (ME1) is dramatically upregulated, due to ME1 copy number amplification. In breast cancer patients, ME1 expression is positively correlated with tumor size, high grade, poor survival, and chemotherapy resistance. The relationship between high ME1 expression and poor clinical outcomes has also been observed in cancers such as gastric cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma, human oral squamous cell carcinoma. Interestingly, in the case of human non–small cell lung carcinoma, high expression of ME1 was correlated with lymph node metastasis, but the relationship between overall survival and ME1 levels differed between younger and older patients. With regard to this cancer, it has also been shown that patients with elevated tumoral ME1 expression had worse overall survival after radiotherapy, but this effect was not observed with chemotherapy alone. A number of studies also show that inhibition of the malic enzyme activity can have a significant negative impact on cancer cells. In studies on human cell lines, S. Murai et al. noted that in cancer cells the expression of ME1 dominates over the expression of ME2 and ME3, however, it should be noted that there are also cell lines with the dominant expression of ME2 and ME3. Inhibition of ME1 led to partial growth inhibition, cellular senescence, or apoptosis, depending on the cellular context. It was also noted that ME1 inhibition led to increased glucose uptake and lactate production, resulting from higher activity of the PPP pathway compensating for NADPH deficiency. The increase in lactate was in turn a consequence of the need to recover NAD+ to maintain an appropriate rate of glycolysis It was also noted that cells were particularly sensitive to ME1 deficiency when grown in low-glucose medium. Other studies, both in vitro and in vivo, using knockdown, knockout, or ME1 overexpression, have also confirmed the importance of the malic enzyme for cancer progression. It has been shown, among others, that inhibition of ME1 expression leads to reduced proliferation, apoptosis induction, decreased colony formation, reversal of EMT, cellular senescence, lower NADPH levels, increased ROS, and elevated oxidative stress. Thus, the analysis of the literature data suggests that ME1 inhibition, for optimal effect, could be combined with glucose restriction in cancer cells, inhibition of the PPP, co-administration of pro-oxidative compounds, or inhibition of alternative NADPH-producing enzymes. Here, a particularly interesting observation is made by Frank A Simmen, who observed correlation in the expression of NADPH-generating enzymes in the cytosol (ME1, G6PD, and IDH1) in cancer cell lines, which has also been confirmed by our independent analyses of cancer patient datasets (Fig. 2). Understanding the mechanisms behind the co-regulation of NADPH-generating enzymes could also provide an important point for the development of novel drugs targeting altered cancer cell metabolism.

Metabolic traffic jam: shuttle overload promotes lactate output

The active aerobic glycolysis in cancer cells and the need to generate oxidized biomass create a constant demand for NAD+ in cancer cells. The recovery of NAD+ as a consequence of NADH oxidation is provided in cancer cells by the high activity of lactate dehydrogenase, however, the main route for NAD+ regeneration is the transport of reducing equivalents with the use of a malate–aspartate shuttle (MAS) and glycerol–3–phosphate shuttle (G3PS). The key cytosolic enzymes of MAS and G3PS are malate dehydrogenase (MDH1) and glycerol–3–phosphate dehydrogenase (GPD1), respectively. Both catalyze reversible reactions to re–oxidize NADH to NAD+, with MDH1 converting oxaloacetate (OAA) to malate, while GPD1 converts dihydroxyacetone phosphate (DHAP) to glycerol-3-phosphate (G3P). Since maintaining a high rate of glycolysis requires continuous oxidation of NADH, the transport of reductive equivalents from the cytosol to the mitochondrion plays an important role in tumor metabolism. In fact, as determined by Wang et al. it is the saturation of shuttles that causes a high NAD+/NADH ratio, which stimulates the activity of lactate dehydrogenase towards lactate production in the presence of oxygen, i.e. the Warburg effect. As suggested by Wang et al. lactate production is a consequence of MAS and G3PS saturation and most probably it is not a unique metabolic trigger for cancer cell proliferation. The concept of shuttle saturation as a trigger for lactate production is presented in Fig. 1C. Importantly, Wang et al. also noted that the saturation of shuttles leading to the Warburg effect is not not exclusive to cancer cells, but is also characteristic of normal cells with high proliferative potential. Therefore, the question remains whether inhibiting the shuttles could be a potential strategy for anti–cancer therapies. The study by Hanse et al. revealed, using TCGA and cBioPortal, that 1% of cancers have mutations in the MDH1 gene, and amplification of this gene is more frequently observed than deletion in cancer cells. Interestingly, in the analysis of squamous cell lung carcinoma, both LDHA and MDH1 amplifications were noted, and they were mutually exclusive. Moreover, patients with squamous cell lung carcinoma with amplified MDH1 showed a 50% reduction in disease-free survival. The same authors also revealed, using cancer cell lines and activated primary T cells, that MDH1 in parallel to LDH is involved in NAD+ regeneration. Another study also found higher expression of MDH1 in Non–small Cell Lung Carcinoma patients compared to normal counterparts and high expression of MDH1 was associated with poor prognosis of NSCL carcinoma patients. As for the GPD1 enzyme, a number of studies indicate that its dispersion decreases with the progression of carcinogenesis and a number of studies indicate that GPD1 acts as a suppressor. This may be due to the fact that GPD1 product is glycerol–3–phosphate, which has been shown to inhibit cancer cell growth being a precursor of pro–apoptotic lysophosphatidylcholine. However, there are also studies indicating that GPD1 may have a function to promote the cancer growth. To sum up, it seems that inhibiting the transport of reducing equivalents from the cytosol to the mitochondrion could be important for inhibiting cancer cell growth, as it would significantly reduce the energy yield from glucose metabolism, as well as the ability to oxidize NADH generated during the production of oxidized biomass. Indeed, Contenti et al. showed that in the molecular subtype of medulloblastoma, where both shuttles exhibit high activity, the use of a small molecule inhibitor that target G3PS and MAS led to inhibition of proliferation and induction of apoptosis. On the other hand, Furkan–Alan et al. showed that despite the knockdown of–aspartate glutamate carrier 1 (AGC1), the crucial component of malate–aspartate shuttle, impaired growth of mouse tumor cells but it increased metastasis in vivo, suggesting that metabolic alterations may have different implications for primary tumors and metastases. It is also worth noting the study conducted by Broeks MH et al., who demonstrated that in MAS-deficient HEK293 cells, the synthesis of serine and glycine is significantly reduced, while lactate/pyruvate levels are elevated. The authors generated a panel of HEK293 cell lines with genetic disruptions of MAS enzymes, but the cells lacking MDH1 exhibited the most prominent metabolic changes. These studies revealed that the regeneration of NAD+ using MDH1 is essential for maintaining serine and glycine biosynthesis, which is crucial for nucleotide synthesis and cell proliferation in cancer cells. In general, NADH oxidation via MAS and G3PS allows for more efficient glucose utilization as an energy source compared to the LDHA enzyme. Additionally, malate, as a product of the MDH1 enzyme, contributes to biomass synthesis and, through its conversion to pyruvate by ME1, serves as a source of NADPH, which plays a crucial role in ROS defense mechanisms. However, the lactate produced during NADH oxidation by lactate dehydrogenase should not be regarded merely as a waste product. As an oncometabolite, it can also promote carcinogenesis, as discussed in the next paragraph.

Lactate — last but really not least

The altered metabolism of cancer cells—driven by the demands of rapid proliferation, extensive biosynthesis of macromolecules, and increased energy requirements—leads to, and even forces, excessive lactate production. To maintain a sufficiently high concentration of NAD+, which is required for both ATP production and the synthesis of oxidized biomass, given the saturation of the MAS and G3PS shuttles, pyruvate of both glucose and non–glucose origin is reduced to lactate, by lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) using NADH. Upon closer analysis of the interdependencies within the heterogeneous cancerous environment, the intensified production of this seemingly waste product clearly emerges as part of a strategy that promotes transformation and tumor growth. An important aspect of this puzzle is the diversity and metabolic plasticity of cancer cells, which allows them to utilize substrates and metabolites through multiple pathways finely tuned to the prevailing conditions within the tumor microenvironment. As recent studies have shown, in addition to aerobic glycolysis, the growth of cancer cells is also strongly dependent on the functioning of the Krebs cycle, indicating the existence of (sub)populations of cells with distinct metabolic profiles. Therefore, lactate produced by cells with intensive glucose uptake and a glycolytic profile can be taken up by cells with an aerobic phenotype and used to fuel the Krebs cycle. The cooperation of these two cell populations becomes particularly important in the context of varying oxygen availability within the developing cancer lesion. On the other hand, in the phenomenon known as the reverse Warburg effect, the metabolism of untransformed (normal) cells in the cancer niche, under the pressure of various signals from transformed cells, may be retuned to increase glycolytic lactate production, which ultimately can serve as a substrate for the aerobic metabolic pathways of neighboring neoplastic cells. Lactate, whose concentration increases in the extracellular tumor microenvironment as a result of the Warburg effect (and the reverse Warburg effect), is not only a potential metabolic substrate for cancer cells. Lactate itself, along with the acidification of the microenvironment that accompanies its accumulation, induces a wide range of pro–cancer effects, including the promotion of cancer cell growth and proliferation, angiogenesis, EMT transition and chemotherapy resistance, as well as the suppression of the immune response against transformed cells.

Targeting the hot spot – strategies to control lactate concentration

In view of unmasking of such an important point of potential anticancer therapy, it is difficult to refrain from an enthusiastic “eureka” – the key is to target lactate to halt the enemy’s expansion. Currently developed strategies to reduce the elevated concentration of lactate in the TME include inhibiting its production (LDH inhibitors), disrupting its transport (MCT inhibitors) and removing/decomposing lactate already present in the TME (via LOX or LOX/CAT action).

Hampering lactate production – LDH inhibitors

Among the five isoenzymes of lactate dehydrogenase—a tetramer composed of varying proportions of two subunits, catalyzing the reversible conversion of pyruvate to lactate—research on carcinogenesis mainly focuses on two isoforms: LDHA (tetramer of four A subunits, also referred to as M, indicating muscle origin) and LDHB (tetramer of four B subunits, also referred to as H, indicating cardiac origin). Differences in the amino acid composition around the catalytic centers of subunits A and B result in differences in affinity for substrates. LDHA has a higher affinity for pyruvate, and under physiological conditions primarily catalyzes the conversion of pyruvate to lactate, enabling the maintenance of a high rate of glycolysis, which is particularly important in conditions of limited oxygen access. LDHB, on the other hand, has a higher affinity for lactate and primarily catalyzes the conversion of lactate to pyruvate, thus fueling the TCA cycle. Numerous studies show an increase in LDHA expression/activity in various types of cancer, which has unfavorable prognostic significance, promoting transformation, progression and metastasis, and may also be a manifestation of adaptation to increasing hypoxia occurring in rapidly growing solid tumors. As studies on triple–negative breast cancer and non-small cell lung cancer show, an increase in LDHB expression may also play a role in carcinogenesis, although its function in this context is generally less well defined than that of LDHA. Nevertheless, for many cancers based on aerobic glycolysis, inhibition of LDH activity is expected to produce desirable therapeutic effects. An important rationale for this strategy is the observation that patients with LDH deficiency do not experience life-threatening consequences, but rather only muscle fatigue, cramps, or myoglobinuria. However, despite promising anticancer effect in various cancer lines (e.g. leukemia, breast, nasopharyngeal, medulloblastoma, NSCLC)—including growth and proliferation inhibition, apoptosis induction, reduced migration, and stimulation of the immune response —clinical application faces significant obstacles. One limitation is the lack of selectivity; for example, oxamate inhibits not only LDH but also other glycolytic enzymes such as pyruvate kinase, enolase, aldolase, and glucose–6–phosphate dehydrogenase. Additionally, it inhibits pyruvate–driven oxidative phosphorylation in isolated heart mitochondria. Currently available selective LDHA inhibitors, on the other hand, are characterized by low bioavailability due to their chemical structure and a certain degree of hydrophilicity/reduced lipophilicity. This has also been observed in preclinical studies using cancer cells. Moreover, due to the high concentrations of the LDH enzyme in cancer cells, achieving a sufficiently high intracellular concentration of the inhibitors to elicit the desired biological, anticancer effects may pose a challenge. The effectiveness of selective LDHA inhibitors is further questioned by observations indicating that only the simultaneous inhibition of both LDHA and LDHB isoenzymes can lead to a decrease in lactate production, inhibition of aerobic glycolysis and cancer cell proliferation. So far, only gossypol—a non-specific LDHA inhibitor of natural origin derived from cotton seeds—has been approved for evaluation in several phase I/II clinical trials. However, it has demonstrated little or no efficacy, with few responses observed in single-agent regimens, as thoroughly reviewed and discussed in the literature. Besides, it is unclear whether the anticancer activity of gossypol results from LDH inhibition or from direct effects on proteins involved in cell cycle and apoptosis regulation. Additionally, inhibition of GAPDH and metal ion chelation may pose a risk of dangerous or toxic side effects. Through structural modeling and chemical modifications, new compounds or derivatives of existing ones (NCI–006, NHI–1, NHI–2, FX–11) are continuously being developed in the hope of finding the “Grail” – an appropriately selective LDH inhibitor with favorable pharmacokinetic parameters. Taking into account the limitations mentioned above, LDH inhibitors do not seem to be good candidates for monotherapy, which, however, does not exclude their use as agents supporting/enhancing the effectiveness of therapies based on other mechanisms of action. In this context, the results of preclinical studies are encouraging, revealing the potential of LDH inhibitors as agents capable of overcoming resistance to classic chemotherapy drugs (paclitaxel, cisplatin, doxorubicin, docetaxel), enhancing responses to radiotherapy, or reinvigorating anticancer immune pathways. The development of combination therapy regimens targeting multiple pathways and incorporating various LDH inhibitors may not only enhance anticancer efficacy but also enable a reduction in their effective concentrations to clinically acceptable levels.

Blocking monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs)

Cells with a glycolytic phenotype, dictated by the demands of increased proliferation and/or increasing hypoxia, resulting from activation of aerobic glycolysis and overexpression of LDHA, produce significant amounts of lactic acid/lactate, which necessitates its export to the extracellular environment. In turn, a subpopulation of cancer cells with an aerobic phenotype can take up lactate from the TME and use it preferentially as an energy substrate, thereby preserving glucose for glycolytic cells. To transfer polar lactate/lactic acid across lipid membranes, protein transporters belonging to the family of monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs), carrying monocarboxylates together with protons are necessary. Different types of MCTs (MCT1–MCT4) have the same substrate specificity, carrying, in addition to L-lactate, also pyruvate, ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate), short-chain fatty acids, propionate and butyrate, but differ in their substrate affinity. Their roles and cooperation under normal conditions are well illustrated in skeletal muscles, where MCT4, which has the lowest affinity for lactate, is expressed in the membrane of white (glycolytic) muscle fibers, and MCT1, with much higher affinity, in the membrane of red (oxidative) muscle fibers. This arrangement, in turn, allows cells primarily relying on aerobic metabolism to utilize an apparent waste product of glycolysis — this time anaerobic. Thus, the expression of transporters, mainly MCT4 and MCT1, as demonstrated in numerous studies across various cancers, represents another manifestation of cancer tactics involving cooperation between different cell subpopulations, referred to as metabolic symbiosis. Therefore, blocking MCT4 and/or MCT1 function may bring about the desired effect by uncoupling the two-wheel mechanism—aerobic glycolysis and aerobic lactate utilization—that drives heterogeneous tumor lesions. Inhibition of MCT4 expressed in the glycolytic and/or hypoxic subpopulation of cancer cells would lead to the retention of lactate inside, their progressive acidification and death. In turn, blocking MCT1 in the aerobic/OxPhos subpopulation, cutting off the supply of lactate as the preferred substrate in these conditions, would force a retuning of the metabolism, increased glucose consumption and, indirectly, as a result of competition for a common substrate, the death of hypoxic cells, mainly dependent on glucose. This is confirmed by numerous preclinical studies showing the effectiveness of various MCT inhibitors, such as CHC, phloretin, lonidamine or quercetin, against cells of many types of cancer (breast, colon, prostate, kidney cancer). However, the lack of selectivity of these compounds toward specific MCT isoforms limits their clinical utility. The newer generation of inhibitors is characterized by much greater specificity. Among them, AZD3965, an orally administered MCT1 inhibitor, has passed phase I clinical trials (advanced solid tumors and lymphoma), which, in addition to establishing a metabolically effective and tolerable dose, given the disappointing effectiveness of the therapy, allowed to suggest a more molecularly profiled target group of patients/tumors characterized by high MCT1 and low MCT4 expression. It appears that MCT1 inhibition is compensated by MCT4, as also indicated by the analysis of the response to AZD3965 in a panel of cancer cell lines, among which the most sensitive to the inhibitor were those with high and dominant MCT1 expression compared to resistant or very weakly sensitive lines expressing both MCT1 and MCT4. In this context, digital pathology appears to be a valuable tool for stratifying patients and cancer profiles by revealing the current status of tumor lesions in relation to selected key molecular characteristics, thereby enhancing theranostic approaches for the precise and effective therapy selection. As demonstrated in breast cancer samples and ovarian cancer xenograft models, digital pathology enables detailed molecular profiling of individual tumors with respect to MCT1 and MCT4 expression—which appears essential in the context of lactate transporter inhibitor efficacy—and thus could support patient enrollment into well-characterized clinical trial cohorts and contribute to the development of more personalized treatment strategies. Besides the potential compensatory interplay between MCT1 and MCT4 functions, another limitation is the baseline blood lactate level in recruited patients. The case of lactic acidosis in one of the patients, generated after the first dose of an MCT1 inhibitor, revealed the overlapping effects of enhanced aerobic glycolysis, especially in the case of an advanced stage of disease progression, and the effect of the inhibitor on transporters in normal cells that determine the maintenance of body homeostasis (liver, kidneys, etc.). Observations of altered MCT expression or function in various disorders—such as MCT1 downregulation in neurodegenerative diseases or MCT1 polymorphisms associated with rhabdomyolysis, myoglobinuria, and severe ketoacidosis—highlight the critical role of the lactate shuttle in maintaining cellular and organ homeostasis under normal conditions. At the same time, these findings underscore potential challenges and risks associated with therapeutic strategies that target MCTs. Certainly, a detailed analysis of the relationships/interactions and the selection of an appropriately metabolically and molecularly profiled group of patients/cancers may result in the desired therapeutic effect, although this seems much more likely through combination therapy, either with classic chemotherapeutics, radiotherapy, immunotherapy or, finally, as an appropriate panel of metabolic inhibitors/metabolic drugs. As preclinical studies have shown, regarding the last of these options, the MCT1 inhibitor AZD3965 is, in a way, laying the groundwork for an effective disruption of energy metabolism by subsequent compounds. Inhibition of MCT1 leads to an increase in intracellular lactate concentration via MCT4 and blocks the smooth flow of lactate. At the same time, it results in an increase in the rate of mitochondrial aerobic metabolism, which, when the preferred source of substrate – lactate is cut off, can be effectively inhibited by the simultaneous use of a mitochondrial pyruvate transport inhibitor (UK5099) and a complex I inhibitor (metformin).

Lactate oxidase (LOX)/catalase (CAT) systems

In addition to the previously mentioned limitations in clinical applicability (lack of selectivity, need to use high concentrations, unfavorable pharmacokinetic parameters, risk of disruption of the normal cells/tissues/organs functioning and associated toxicity), the moderate or disappointing in vivo effectiveness of inhibitors of lactate production and transport may be due to their inability to reduce lactate levels already accumulated in the tumor microenvironment (TME). Recent animal studies have demonstrated that the use of lactate oxidase (LOX) holds anticancer therapeutic potential by lowering lactate concentration in the TME, thereby inducing favorable metabolic and immunomodulatory effects. LOX, an enzyme of bacterial origin administered via nanoparticles, catalyzes the oxidation of lactate to pyruvate while reducing O2 to H2O2, which in turn generates other ROS and has a cytotoxic effect on surrounding cells. Consequently, oxygen deficiency is a factor limiting the effectiveness of LOX in the case of hypoxic cancer lesions, but on the other hand, high enzyme activity in normoxia/physioxia conditions may generate serious toxic effects on normal tissue/cells (including the death of laboratory animals). In the context of attempts to increase the effectiveness, and at the same time reduce the toxicity of a potential therapeutic regimen, the results of research involving the use of nanoparticles equipped with two enzymatic activities look promising. In addition to the LOX described above, they also contain catalase (CAT), which catalyzes the dismutation of H2O2 into H2O and O2, so in a hypoxic environment it is an additional source of O2 for LOX and limits the generation of ROS, which is important especially in the case of well–oxygenated normal tissues. Studies on the animal model of cancer (colorectal cancer) showed increased effectiveness of lactate removal in the case of a two-enzyme preparation used under hypoxic conditions in vitro, which translated into the desired anti-cancer and immunomodulatory effects in the tested individuals, without any serious side effects. Nanomedicine offers a broad perspective for designing formulations equipped with various anti-cancer agents, as well as elements/particles enabling appropriate navigation and preferential targeting to diseased areas/places. Lactate, which is present in increased amounts in the tumor lesion environment, can, in addition to being a therapeutic target, also be used as an “attractant” for specially designed nanoparticles, as demonstrated by work using LOX-powered Platinum Nanoflowers (Pt NFs), suggesting the possibility of developing a specific, “intelligent/smart” drug delivery system. Pt NF is a LOX–decorated, platinum–based “H₂O₂ nanozyme” that can alleviate hypoxia in the tumor microenvironment (TME) through a cascading catalytic mechanism. In this process, LOX first catalyzes the oxidation of lactate to produce additional H₂O₂ at the tumor site, which is then converted – along with endogenous H₂O₂ – into molecular oxygen by the catalase–like activity of the platinum nanoparticles. This increases local oxygen levels within the TME and further supports LOX activity. Additionally, the specific construction of Pt NF nanoparticles enables chemotactic movement toward areas with elevated lactate concentrations. An important assumption determining the anticancer effectiveness of the strategies described above is the prevention of increased lactate concentration in the TME, whose immunomodulatory effect has been demonstrated in in vivo studies. In addition to the strictly metabolic consequences, this will also lead to retuning the immune response, restoring the desired ability of the immune system to eradicate cancer cells. However, this assumption is overshadowed by reports regarding attempts to measure the actual concentration of lactate in the extracellular environment of neoplastic lesions in patients. Firstly, such an analysis is burdened with numerous technical limitations, related to both the availability of the tested environment, obtaining a reliable sample and the analysis methodology. Secondly, the few available studies analyzing the TME metabolome indicate only moderate lactate accumulation compared to the concentrations used in studies of immunomodulatory effects in cell cultures. Moreover, measurements of the arterial/venous (A/V) gradient suggest net lactate consumption by some tumors. Therefore, it seems that the immunosuppressive effect accompanying the observed increased expression/activity of LDHA is rather a consequence of glucose deficiency and acidification of the microenvironment. The above-mentioned observations do not exclude lactate metabolism as a target for anticancer drugs. Perhaps, regarding the therapeutic potential of compounds that inhibit the synthesis and/or transport of lactate, further insights necessary for developing effective treatment strategies will emerge from research into the epigenetic mechanisms governing the initiation and progression of cancer transformation, in which the lactate molecule may directly participate through histone lactylation.

Conclusions and future perspectives

The enzymes and processes highlighted and discussed in the current study could, in our opinion, serve as potential promising targets for cancer therapy aimed at metabolic reprogramming (Fig. 1A). The intense glucose metabolism, which provides energy and biosynthetic precursors, is a hallmark of cancer cells. It should also be noted that glucose is the sole metabolite ensuring energy production under conditions of limited oxygen availability. Additionally, glucose metabolism in the oxidative branch of the pentose phosphate pathway (PPP) is a crucial source of NADPH, which protects cells from reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated during cycles of hypoxia and reoxygenation, often characteristic of the tumor microenvironment. Therefore, restricting glucose entry into both glycolysis and the PPP pathway, by specifically inhibiting hexokinase 2 (HK2), is undoubtedly a significant therapeutic target. Another enzyme, pyruvate kinase M2 (PKM2), also appears to be a potential target for cancer therapy, as its ability to switch between dimeric and tetrameric forms could allow modulation of the metabolic control mechanism in cancer cells, directing glucose flow through glycolysis depending on current needs, whether energy production or anabolic processes are prioritized. Our work provides additional value by incorporating expression data analyses comparing the discussed targets — proteins and enzymes — in both normal and cancerous cells (Fig. 3), using representative examples from various tumor types. While these results are not unequivocal, they clearly demonstrate that in many cancers, the selected targets show increased expression compared to normal tissues. Ideally, a therapeutic target should not only be tumor-specific but also show a correlation with patient survival — since only then does its inhibition hold therapeutic promise. This aspect is addressed in Fig. 4 (following Fig. 3), where we present data showing the relationship between the expression levels of selected targets and patient prognosis. Again, the analysis reveals that many of the examined targets significantly impact patients’ survival. However, the direction and magnitude of this impact vary depending on the tumor type, reinforcing the idea that target selection must be both tumor-specific and personalized. Therefore, therapeutic prioritization should first be based on cancer type specificity and then further refined by analyzing the expression profile in individual patients. It is worth noting that our analysis revealed that in all the data sets of cancer patients, the expression of PKM was significantly higher than in normal cells (Fig. 3). For a number of cancers, it has also been shown that high expression of HK2 and PKM is associated with worse overall survival (Fig. 4). Undoubtedly, very interesting cancer–specific enzymes that may be a potential target of anti–cancer therapy in the future are PFKFB3 and PFKFB4. Definitely targeting these proteins requires understanding of their activity. This is very evident in the case of neuroblastoma data sets, where the opposite effect of both enzymatic activities on the overall survival of patients can be seen in three data sets. Again, it can be assumed that it is the directing of glucose between the different pathways, glycolysis and the oxidative arm of the pentose phosphate pathway, that causes the expression of these enzymes to have an opposite effect on survival (Fig. 4). Indeed, according to the literature, PFKFB4 appears to have higher FBPase–2 activity, promoting glucose flux toward the pentose phosphate pathway and providing a source of NADPH. Interestingly, as we demonstrated earlier and as shown in the table, high PFKFB4 expression in neuroblastomas correlates with better overall survival (OS) (Fig. 4). A similar pattern is observed for ME1 expression–the malic enzyme, which is also a source of NADPH. Notably, its high expression in neuroblastoma is also associated with improved patients’ overall survival (Fig. 4). Undoubtedly, in the context of neuroblastoma, this observation warrants further investigation. Another intriguing approach involves targeting the enzyme responsible for de novo NAD+ synthesis. Overcoming the toxicity issues associated with current inhibitors could unlock new therapeutic possibilities. However, a comprehensive analysis of the relationship between the enzymes in the two de novo synthesis pathways would be highly valuable before applying the inhibitors for particular patients. Limiting the ability of cells to obtain NADPH could be a crucial therapeutic target in addressing the altered metabolism of cancer cells. Cells that undergo cycles of hypoxia and re–oxygenation, commonly observed in the tumor microenvironment, may be particularly vulnerable to such interventions. Cancer cells might also exhibit increased sensitivity to the inhibition of malic enzyme activity, especially when glucose availability is restricted, as seen in patients whose cancer treatment is supported by a ketogenic diet. However, it is important to note that the malic enzyme is only one of three enzymes responsible for NADPH production. Our transcriptomic data analysis suggests that, in cancer patients, the expression levels of all three enzymes involved in NADPH generation are positively correlated (Fig. 2). This raises the possibility that targeting the regulatory mechanisms controlling the activity of these three enzymes may provide a more effective therapeutic strategy for modulating the metabolic reporgramming of cancer cells than targeting malic enzyme alone. Blocking lactate production as an oncometabolite seems less promising than initially thought. Its accumulation in the TME is lower than expected, and immunosuppression appears to result more from acidification and glucose deficiency. Nevertheless, lactate may still contribute to the epigenetic regulation of cancer cells. Both preclinical and clinical studies using inhibitors of the discussed enzymes show that, although the results in laboratory models are promising, the effects in real clinical settings doesn’t always lead to effective treatment. Given the complexity and adaptability of cancer cell metabolism, it is also important to consider combined therapy strategies. Specifically, incorporating glycolytic inhibitors alongside conventional cancer treatments—such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or immunotherapy—may enhance therapeutic efficacy and overcome resistance mechanisms. Several studies have already explored the potential of such combinatory approaches, providing a valuable foundation for further clinical development. Summing up the key lies in understanding metabolic interactions — not just individual enzymes, but how they cooperate to sustain tumor growth, redox balance, biosynthesis, and immune evasion. The therapeutic targets of the future will not necessarily be the most altered ones, but rather those that act as critical nodes, whose inhibition disrupts entire pathways or deprives the tumor of adaptability. In some cases, this may involve hitting a dominant flux; in others, it may mean targeting a regulatory enzyme that controls parallel mechanisms. This approach demands more than isolated molecular data. It requires the integration of robust basic knowledge with clinical insight, supported by functional diagnostics and personalized profiling — a concept already emerging as theranostics. Only by combining targeted metabolic intervention with precise tumor characterization can we hope to provide therapies that are not just innovative, but genuinely optimal.
