# Longitudinal tissue analysis reveals microenvironmental changes correlate with combined immunotherapy and targeted therapy response in metastatic breast cancer.

## Abstract

The ability to interrogate changes within the tumor microenvironment (TME) before, during and following therapeutic intervention could yield important understanding of treatment response and causes for disease progression. Yet, the role of investigational tissue analysis faces key challenges in the clinical setting and the value of integrating longitudinal biopsies with emerging multimodal molecular analyses ("Multi-omics") remains to be defined. In this study, we conducted a multicenter phase 2 clinical trial examining the effect of a novel cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4/programmed death-ligand 1 bispecific antibody in combination with a dual-epitope blocking anti-human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 antibody in treatment-resistant metastatic breast cancer. We performed longitudinal sampling of patient tumor tissues before and following treatment. Single-cell RNA and T cell receptor sequencing from 334,183 cells from site-matched tumors reveals significant temporal shift of various immune cell populations and phenotypes within the TME associated with treatment responses. Conversely, regulatory T cells were activated while effector T cells, natural killer cells, and dendritic cells were significantly depleted in non-responding tumors. Taken together, these results support that longitudinal analysis of TME to generate multiomics data that can lead to rich insight into disease process and to provide clinical value in evaluating treatment responses. Trial registration number NCT04521179.

 Trial registration number

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer often develops resistance to standard anti-HER2 therapies, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) is a key contributor to treatment failure, though the dynamic changes during combination immunotherapy and targeted therapy remain poorly characterized.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This study provides longitudinal single-cell multi-omics evidence that the combination of KN046 (anti-PD-L1/CTLA-4) and KN026 (anti-HER2) reinvigorates antitumor immunity in responders, while non-responders exhibit baseline immunosuppressive TME features and post-treatment depletion of effector immune cells.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

The findings support the integration of longitudinal tissue profiling in clinical trials to identify predictive biomarkers and resistance mechanisms, potentially guiding personalized immunotherapy strategies for HER2+ metastatic breast cancer.

Introduction

 Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-positive breast cancer (BC) accounts for 15–20% of all BC cases, with aggressive biological behavior. The overall survival rate of patients with HER2-positive metastatic BC (mBC) shows a consistent elevation across the last decades, however, 11.8–21.4% patients with HER2-positive mBC have developed primary drug resistance to the combination of taxane and two anti-HER2 antibodies trastuzumab and pertuzumab, which is the standard of care in first-line treatment. After the failure of first-line and/or second-line therapies, new treatment strategies including immunotherapy in combination with anti-HER2 antibodies or antibody-drug conjugates have been explored. However, the proportion of patient respond to such treatment remains low, with the resistance rate around 55–88.5%. The molecular mechanisms driving resistance to HER2-targeted therapies may include downregulation of HER2, HER2 variants, ignition of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, escape from antibody-dependent cell cytotoxicity (ADCC), as well as upregulation of estrogen receptor. And different stromal cell subtypes such as cancer-associated fibroblasts, tumor-associated macrophages (TAM), and myeloid-derived suppressor cells, metabolic dysregulation and epigenetic alteration in the tumor microenvironment (TME) were the main mechanisms leading to immunotherapy resistance in BC. Therefore, single-cell and spatial transcriptomics studies may explore the complexity of TME-mediated immunomodulation and anti-HER2 resistance, which may improve the efficacy of anti-HER2 treatment and immunotherapy.

Moreover, in the clinical setting, standard clinical endpoints such as overall survival, progression-free survival (PFS) or radiological evidence of progression are coarse markers of treatment response, and in many cases do not provide clarity as to the changes within the TME and host immune system that contribute to disease regression or progression. While ideally, longitudinal tissue sampling of the same tumor tissues before, during and following treatment, combined with advanced multimodal analysis could provide unprecedented information about the evolution of tumors in response to therapeutic intervention, technical challenges as well as concerns for safety, cost, patient preferences, in addition to lack of concrete evidence supporting its clinical value often prohibit such practice. As a result, longitudinal multimodal tumor-related analysis for the purposes of evaluating treatment response, which is particularly important for cancer immunotherapy, has been underexplored.

In this study, we demonstrate the feasibility of conducting a longitudinal multiomics approach on serially collected biopsies from the same tumors in the setting of a phase 2 clinical trial (NCT04521179). In previous studies, we have assessed the safety and efficacy of a novel combination of two bispecific antibodies (bsAbs), KN046 and KN026, in heavily pretreated patients with HER2+ mBC, achieving an overall response rate (ORR) of 47.2%. KN026 is an innovative recombinant humanized bsAbs that binds simultaneously to two distinct HER2 epitopes, recognized by trastuzumab (domain IV) and pertuzumab (domain II). It might have superior efficacy compared with trastuzumab and pertuzumab in combination. KN046 is the first bsAbs capable of concurrently binding to programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA4), proven to effectively eradicate tumors with reduced side effects. We performed matched pretreatment and on-treatment investigative biopsies on index tumors and performed single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and single-cell T cell receptor sequencing (scTCR-seq) analysis. Our findings suggest that the elevated baseline tumor-infiltrating macrophage (TIM) levels in non-responsive (NR) patients with HER2+ mBC may impede the antitumor effectiveness of the dual bsAb-based tri-blockade therapy. This study demonstrated the promising capability of KN046 bsAb in overcoming the resistance to bsAb/monoclonal antibody (moAb)-based drug therapies and supports the clinical and scientific impetus to develop multimodal analysis methods driven by the need to understand disease progression and tumor-immune evolution over the course of treatment.

Results

Single-cell profiling of trastuzumab-resistant patients with HER2+ mBC before and after PD-L1/CTLA-4/HER2 tri-blockade therapy

Our phase 2 trial (NCT04521179) specifically focused on patients with HER2+ mBC resistant to one line or multiple lines of anti-HER2 therapy combined with chemotherapies (figure 1a, online supplemental table 1 and Methods). Briefly, the study enrolled 36 patients with a median age of 53 years (range: 33–67). Out of the 36 patients, 30 (83.3%) received ≥3 lines of HER2-targeted combinational therapies in the advanced setting. Of these, 33 patients were evaluable for overall response, and all 36 patients were assessed for safety. The ORR was 47.2% (17 of 36, 95% CI: 30.4% to 64.5%), and two patients achieved complete response (CR). The median PFS was 5.6 (95% 4.1–13.8) months. In total, 34 of 36 (94.4%) patients experienced treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) of any grade, and ≥grade 3 TRAEs were observed in 10 (27.8%) patients. No treatment-related deaths were observed.

To unravel the mechanisms behind the varying responses of patients to the tri-blockade combination treatment, we performed comprehensive scRNA-seq on tumor biopsies obtained from a total of 17 patients that were evaluable for overall response. Among them, 13 patients had two biopsies taken, and 4 patients had one biopsy collected either before or during treatment, resulting in 30 samples in total (figure 1a, online supplemental table 1). We categorized patient samples into responders for samples from patients with CR and partial response and non-responders for those from patients with stable disease or progressive disease, according to patient response categories defined by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) V.1.1 criteria.

We followed established protocols for scRNA-seq, which allowed us to capture 334,183 high-quality cells from 30 samples. On average, we detected 39,071 genes per cell (online supplemental table 2). Employing graph-based clustering and marker gene-based cell type assignment, we successfully identified T and natural killer (NK) (T/NK) cells, myeloid cells, epithelial cells, endothelial cells (ECs), B lymphocytes and plasma cells, and fibroblasts (figure 1b and c, online supplemental figure s1a and b, online supplemental table 2). To gain further insights into the T lymphocytes, we conducted scTCR-seq. Our analysis revealed 22,852 TCR sequences from 29.51% (77,445) T cells across all 30 samples examined alongside scRNA-seq data (online supplemental table 3).

The results revealed that the NR patients exhibited a significantly higher baseline level of tumor-infiltrating myeloid cells when compared with responsive (R) patients (figure 1d and e, online supplemental figure s1c and d), suggesting a potential correlation between this major cell type and resistance to combined KN046 and KN026 treatment. Moreover, the composition of T/NK cells within the tumors of NR patients significantly decreased following combined treatment (figure 1e and f, online supplemental figure s1c), suggesting that these cells were excluded from tumors during the treatment of NR patients. This exclusion could potentially compromise the effectiveness of anti-HER2 treatment, which relies on both innate and adaptive immunity.

T and NK cells are significantly depleted in non-responsive patients during combined tri-blockade therapy

In light of the critical role that both T and NK cells play in the efficacy of anti-HER2 therapy, as evidenced in preclinical studies, we explored the responsiveness of various tumor-infiltrating NK and T cell subtypes to combined blockage of HER2, PD-L1, and CTLA4 via two bsAbs, KN046 and KN026. Unsupervised reclustering of 77,445 NK and T cells from multiple patients at both pretreatment and post-treatment time points, we identified 12 distinct cell subtypes (Methods, figure 2a, online supplemental figure 2a and b) involving CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, and NK cells. Baseline quantities of CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, and NK cells were comparable between the R and NR patient groups.

After treatment, NR patients exhibited a marked decrease in the total abundance of CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, NK cells and cycling T cells, in contrast to the slight increase in these cell populations in R patients (figure 2b). Interestingly, the apoptosis scores for CD4+ T, CD8+ T and NK cells significantly elevated in NR patients after treatment, while a minor reduction was observed in R individuals (figure 2c). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) further showed that the apoptosis pathway was activated in CD4+ T, CD8+ T and NK cells exclusively in NR patients during therapy (figure 2d). Therefore, it is plausible that the heightened apoptosis and reduced cell proliferation of T and NK cells may at least in part contribute to their considerable decrease in the TME of NR patients during therapy.

The antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) effect, mediated by CD16+ NK cells, constitutes a critical mechanism through which moAb-based therapies target and eliminate tumor cells. We identified two NK cell subsets: CD16− NK and CD16+ NK cells (figure 2a, online supplemental figure 2a and b). There is a significant correlation between the fractions of CD16− NK and CD16+ NK cells (figure 2e), suggesting the potential key role of CD16− NK in the efficacy of CD16+ NK cell-mediated ADCC. Importantly, both NK cell subsets exhibited a significant decrease in NR patients (figure 2f), potentially compromising the ADCC effect of the anti-HER2 bsAb KN026.

Collectively, our results suggested that the significant decrease in both total T and NK cell abundances during therapy may substantially contribute to the observed resistance in the NR patient group to the combined tri-blockade therapy.

CD8+ T cells are activated in responsive patients during treatment

Subsequently, we characterized the diversity within CD8+ T cells in HER2+ mBC. Within the CD8+ T cell population, we identified naïve CD8+ T cells (CD8+ TN, TCF7, IL-7R), precursors of exhausted CD8+ T cells (TEXP, GZMK, CCL5), cytotoxic CD8+ T cells (TCYTO, GZMH, NR4A2) and exhausted CD8+ T cells (T, GZMB, IFNG1, PDCD1) (figure 2a and b, online supplemental figure 2a and b) based on their marker gene expression. The CD8+ T cell subsets in HER2+ mBC align with known developmental frameworks of CD8+ T cells in cancers, where CD8 T cells develop into a terminally exhausted state, TEX, under the continuous exposure to tumor antigens. Through pseudotime trajectories analysis, we delineated a developmental trajectory starting from CD8+ TN, progressing through TEXP, and ultimately culminating in the TEX state (figure 3a). TCR clonal analysis further supported this observed trajectory, with TEX exhibiting the highest proportion of TCR clonotype sharing with TEXP (figure 3b). Intriguingly, in HER2+ mBC, TCYTO which is highly expressive of GZMK, GZMH, and NR4A2, was also connected to TEXP (figure 3a). This connection is further supported by the substantial TCR clonotype sharing between TEXP and TCYTO (figure 3b).

We next investigated the response of the various T cell subtypes to treatment. In R patients, the diversity of TCR clonotype among total CD8+ T cells remained unchanged following treatment, whereas in NR patients, it significantly increased after treatment (figure 3c, online supplemental figure 3a). Consistently, all CD8+ T cell subtypes demonstrated clonotype expansion in R patients during treatment (figure 3d and e), while a marked contraction of these clonotypes was noted in NR patient post-therapy (figure 3d and e). Notably, among the CD8+ T cell subtypes, TEXP cells exhibited the most pronounced clonotype expansion in R patients, and conversely, the most notable clonotype contraction in NR patients. Moreover, TEXP cells displayed a significant upregulation of immune-checkpoint, tumor-reactive and costimulatory markers in R patients, but not in non-responders (figure 3f). These results were consistent with previous findings that ICI treatments can eradicate tumors through the reinvigoration of TEXP. This suggests that TEXP cells also play an important role in the efficacy of our combined tri-blockade therapy. In line with changes in CD8+ T cell clonality, we observed modest increases in the abundance of all CD8+ T cell subtypes in R patients after treatment. In contrast, there was a significant reduction in the abundance of all CD8+ T cell subtypes in NR patients during treatment (figure 3g). These findings suggest that the reactivation of diverse CD8+ T cell subtypes, facilitated by KN046 therapy, may contribute to the restored sensitivity of anti-HER2 therapy in R patients.

TREG cells are activated in non-responsive patients after therapy

CD4+ T cell is critical for the efficacy of both immunotherapy and anti-HER2 therapy. We subsequently investigated the impact of combined KN046 and KN026 treatment on various CD4+ T cell subtypes. Our analysis identified several distinct CD4+ T subtypes that are based on characteristic gene expression: naïve CD4+ T cells (CD4+ TN, TCF7, SELL), effector/memory CD4+ T cells (CD4+ TEM, IFNG, TNF), regulatory T cells (TREG, FOXP3, CTLA4), SYNE2+CD4+ T cells (CD4+ T-SYNE2), and two distinct CXCL13-expressing CD4+ T cell subsets. These two CXCL13-expressing CD4+ T cells both harbor T follicular helper (TFH)-like features with high expression of IL21, ICOS, CD40LG (figure 2a, online supplemental figure 2a and b). Thus, we classified these cells as tumor-infiltrating TFH. The first subset, designated as TFHX13, is characterized by its high expression of CXCL136,8. The second subset, termed TFH1-like, is distinguished by its elevated expression of IFNG, resembling the previously identified TFH1-like. Notably, TFHX13 and TFH1-like cells exhibited the highest TCR clonotype sharing, suggesting a shared antigen recognition (figure 3h). Pseudotime trajectory analysis indicated that both TFHX13 and TFH1-like cells originated from CD4+ TN (figure 3i).

Although the overall infiltration of CD4+ T cell subtypes in the TME of R patients remained largely unchanged following combined tri-blockade treatment (figure 3j), the abundance of CD4+ TN, TFH1-like, TFHX13, CD4+ TEM and CD4+ T-SYNE2 cells was significantly depleted in the TME of NR patients. Notably, among these depleted CD4+ subpopulations in NR patients, CD4+ TN cells experienced the most substantial reduction during therapy, likely contributing to the observed overall decrease of other CD4+ T cell subtypes within the TME of non-responders. Moreover, consistent with the depletion of diverse CD4+ T cell subtypes in NR patients, a significant contraction in their clonotypes was observed, apart from TREG (figure 3h, online supplemental figure 3a), whereas in R patients, these clonotypes were modestly expanded (figure 3k, online supplemental figure 3b).

Although preclinical mouse models indicated TREG depleting by KN046, our data revealed no significant change in TREG cell levels in either R or NR patients during treatment (figure 3j). This aligns with prior studies demonstrating that anti-CTLA4 therapy does not reduce TREG cells in the TME of patients. Notably, post-treatment TREG cells in NR patients showed markedly higher expression levels of genes associated with their activation and immunosuppressive functions (TNFRSF9, IL1R2, REL, and LAYN) compared with those in R patients (figure 3l). Further analysis via CellChat analysis showed that, during therapy, TREG cells in NR patients displayed markedly stronger interaction with other cell types, including CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, compared with their counterparts in R patients (figure 3m). These results indicate that TREG cells become activated in post-treatment tumors of non-responders, potentially driven by ongoing tumor progression as previously reported, and may play a key role in mediating resistance to therapy. Taken together, our findings underscore the critical role of diverse CD4 T cell subtypes in shaping the outcomes of anti-PD-L1/CTLA4/HER2 tri-blockade therapy.

KN046+KN026 therapy activated cDC1 and cDC2 in responsive patients

Tumor-infiltrating myeloid cells regulate the tumor immunity and patient responsiveness to ICI therapy. We thus investigated the dynamics of various myeloid cell subpopulations in response to tri-blockade treatment. The unsupervised reclustering of myeloid cells identified dendritic cells (DCs, LAMP3, CDC1C), CD14+ canonical monocytes (CD14, FCN1), macrophages (Mϕs) (CD68, CD163), and mast cells (CPA3, KIT), and cycling Mϕs (MKI67, STMN1) (figure 4a and b, online supplemental figure 4a and b). Unlike its impact on T and NK cells (figure 2b), the anti-PD-L1/CTLA4/HER2 tri-blockade treatment did not significantly affect the abundance of diverse major myeloid cell types in the TME across R and NR patient groups (figure 4c). However, we noted a significant upregulation of interferon (IFN)-gamma signaling in both DC and Mϕ cells during therapy exclusively in R patients (figure 4d), suggesting a distinct, effective activation mechanism triggered by the tri-blockade in this patient group.

DCs are a heterogeneous group of immune cells with multiple subtypes. We identified three distinct subtypes of DCs in HER2+ mBC, including conventional type 1 DCs (cDC1, XCR1, CLEC9A), conventional type 2 DCs (cDC2, CD1C, CLEC10A), and mature DCs (LAMP3, CCR7). Notably, while the overall abundance of DC cells remained relatively stable in all patients during treatment (figure 4d), the cDC1 and cDC2 subpopulations were significantly and exclusively depleted in NR patients’ post-treatment (figure 4e). cDC1 and cDC2 play a crucial role in priming CD8 T cells and CD4 T cells for antitumor responses, respectively. Previous studies have highlighted the importance of these cDC subtypes in antitumor immunity and the success of immunotherapy. Our results implied that the efficacy of the anti-PD-L1/CTLA4/HER2 tri-blockade relies on the presence and functional integrity of DC cells. A reduction in these DC cell populations could potentially compromise the therapeutic efficacy of the tri-blockade therapy.

KN046+KN026 treatment induces an inflammation shift in Mϕs of responsive patients

While tumor-infiltrating Mϕs (TIMs) facilitate the antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP) process, effectively killing tumor cells during moAb treatment, they can also create an immunosuppressive environment to undermine the efficacy of ICI immunotherapy. We next investigated the heterogeneity of Mϕs within the TME of HER2+ mBC and their potential impact on the response to tri-blockade therapy. We observed that NR patients showed a significantly higher baseline level of total Mϕs, which highly expressed CD68 and CD163, resembling features of TAMs, compared with R patients (figure 4a, online supplemental figure 5a). Consistently, we also detected a significantly elevated baseline level of monocytes (figures4ac and 5a, online supplemental figure 5b), the precursors to TAM, in NR patients compared with those who responded to the treatment.

Our unsupervised reclustering revealed extensive heterogeneity among the Mϕs, giving rise to multiple subsets, including Mϕ-CCL4L2 (CCL4L2, CCL3L1), Mϕ-MARCO (CD5L, MARCO), Mϕ-APOE (APOE, C1QB), Mϕ-SPP1 (SPP1, FABP4), Mϕ-CXCL10 (CXCL10, CCL8), Mϕ-MT1G (MT1G, MT1H), and Mϕ-CCL5 (CCL5, CD69) (figure 4a and b, online supplemental figure 4a and b). Among these Mϕ subsets, more than half of them, specifically Mϕ-CCL4L2, Mϕ-CXCL10, Mϕ-MT1G, and Mϕ-CCL5, showed significantly elevated baseline levels in NR patients compared with R ones (figure 5b). The remaining Mϕ subsets also demonstrated higher baseline levels in NR patients, although not to a statistically significant extent (figure 5b). The conventional Mϕ dichotomy activation model divides Mϕs into M1 and M2 in vitro. We observed that the diverse Mϕ subsets in HER2+ mBC tumors exhibited concurrent expression of canonical M1 and M2 gene signatures (figure 5c). This pattern mirrors the Mϕs identified in the TME of various other solid tumors.

Notably, Mϕs in the TME of NR patients exhibited higher expression levels of checkpoints than R patients (figure 5d), suggesting stronger immunosuppressive functions. Following tri-blockade treatment using dual bsAbs, diverse Mϕ subpopulations in R patients exhibited a significant upregulation of M1-like pro-inflammatory genes, IFN-gamma signaling, and antigen presentation, and concurrent downregulation of M2-like anti-inflammatory genes (figure 5e and f). This shift suggests a therapeutic reprogramming of the Mϕ phenotype toward a more tumoricidal state. Conversely, in NR patients, only a marginal increase in M1-like proinflammatory expression signatures was detected, with no significant alteration in the M2-like anti-inflammatory expression signatures (figure 5e and f). Moreover, there was an observed elevation in expression signatures related to IFN-gamma signaling, antigen presentation in R patients, a change not evident in NR patients (figure 5f). Collectively, these results suggest a pervasive shift to a proinflammatory phenotype across Mϕ phenotypes among responders (figures4d  5f). Meanwhile, in NR patients, Mϕs have appeared resistant to activation by combined KN046 and KN026 treatment.

Elevated baseline immunosuppressive TME is associated with resistance to tri-blockade treatment

To elucidate the reasons behind the depletion of antitumor immune cells in the TME of NR patients during KN026+KN046 therapy, we investigated the baseline differences in epithelial cells and ECs between R and NR patients. On unsupervised reclustering of epithelial cells, we identified three distinct subclusters (online supplemental figure 6a). Through estimating copy number variation (CNV) at single-cell level, we discovered that one of these three subclusters exhibited a higher CNV score than the other two (online supplemental figure 6b). By excluding cells with low CNV score (online supplemental figure 6c), we obtained 104,476 potential malignant cells. Further reclustering of these cells did not result in the formation of any new subgroup (figure 6a and b, online supplemental figure 6d and e). R patients showed a higher baseline abundance of HER2 expression level in the malignant cells compared with R patients (figure 6c). The tri-blockade therapy more effectively reduced HER2 expression in R patients than in NR ones (figure 6c). Through single-cell GSEA analysis, we observed that tumors from NR patient showed higher activity in pathways promoting immune escape, including complement, JAK/STAT signaling, PI3K/AKT signaling, HIF-1 signaling, and amino acid metabolism (figure 6d). IPRES (innate anti-programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) resistance) analysis showed an elevated level of IPRES signature in non-responders, correlating with reduced efficacy of immunotherapy (online supplemental figure 6f, online supplemental table 2). Notably, malignant cells in NR patients expressed elevated baseline levels of various immune checkpoint genes (figure 6e).

As to ECs, unsupervised re-clustering leads to four distinct EC subgroups: capillary ECs (ACKR1, SELP), arterial ECs (HEY1, SEMA3G), venous ECs (THY1, RGCC), lymphatic ECs (LEC, PDPN, PROX1), and cycling ECs (MKI67, STMN1) (figure 6f and g, online supplemental figure 6j and k). Consistent with the evidence that EC proliferation is HER2-dependent, KN046+KN026 treatment significantly reduced the fraction of cycling ECs in R patients, whereas it had lesser effect in NR individuals (figure 6h). Notably, the tumor of NR patients showed a significantly lower fraction of lymphatic endothelial cells (LECs) than R patients, suggesting a reduced presence of lymphatic vessels (LVs). Considering that the efficacy of ICB is tightly associated with T cell expansion in peripheral blood, driven by the tumor-draining lymph nodes, more LVs in tumors potentially facilitate the activation of antitumor immunity.

Therefore, baseline NR patients exhibit a higher level of Mϕ infiltration and reduced LVs compared with R patients. We found that the ratio of Mϕ fraction to LEC fraction can predict the non-responsiveness in patients with HER2+ mBC (figure 6i). In summary, our data suggest that the KN046 can efficiently reinvigorate antitumor activity in a large number of patients, leading to the efficient eradication of HER2+ mBC tumors by KN026 (figure 7). We further revealed that resistance to the combination stems from the loss of antitumor effector cells during treatment in non-responders, likely driven by their more immunosuppressive baseline microenvironment.

Discussion

In this study, we examined the effect of a novel CTLA-4/PD-L1 bsAb in combination with a dual-epitope blocking anti-HER2 bsAb in treatment-resistant metastatic HER2+ BC. Using scRNA-seq and TCR-seq, we longitudinally analyzed the TME changes before and during treatment. Our findings revealed that NR patients exhibited a higher baseline immunosuppressive TME compared with R patients. The novel bsAb-based combination immunotherapy and targeted therapy successfully activated both innate and adaptive immunity in R patients. Conversely, in non-responders, tumor progression after treatment was accompanied by the activation of TREG cells and a marked depletion of T cells, NK cells and DCs within the TME. Our study shed light on the mechanisms underlying combined therapy using ICIs and therapeutic bsAbs.

Currently, there is a lack of evidence supporting the effectiveness of immunotherapy in patients with HER2+ mBC. Previous preclinical studies using mouse models have shown that anti-PD-1/CTLA-4 immunotherapy failed to effectively eliminate implanted HER2+ breast tumors. Thanks to the bsAb KN046 we employ, we activate antitumor immune responses more effectively than conventional moAbs. Our treatment is able to engage both innate and adaptive immunity in patients with HER2+mBC, both of which are essential for successful anti-HER2 therapy. These data suggest that dual targeting with KN046 and KN026 is required to effectively eliminate drug-resistant and HER2+ mBC, consistent with the PANACEA (pembrolizumab plus trastuzumab in trastuzumab-resistant, advanced, HER2-positive breast cancer) trial’s success using pembrolizumab plus trastuzumab to clear HER2+ disease. Loi et al reported that this efficacy depends on tumor PD-L1 expression. In our study, responders exhibited significantly lower expression levels of PD-L1 than non-responders, suggesting that the activity of our combined treatment may be independent of PD-L1 status—potentially partly owing to CTLA-4 blockade by KN046, and the antitumor effect of KN026.

Previous research conducted by our team and others has demonstrated that continuous moAb treatment in patients can lead to an immunosuppressive TME, which in turn, undermines the efficacy of moAb therapies. This suppression is driven in part by the upregulation of PD-L1 and Indoleamine 2,3-Dioxygenase (IDO) in Mϕs, and PD-L1 blockade can partially reverse these effects and restore moAb antitumor activity. In line with these findings, our KN046 regiment potently reinvigorated key antitumor populations, such as CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, and myeloid cells, all of which are essential for KN026-induced cytotoxicity, thereby facilitating effective tumor eradication.

Non-responders not only failed to reinvigorate antitumor immunity with our combination therapy but also experienced a progressive loss of critical antitumor lymphocytes during treatment, contributing to the therapeutic failure. At baseline, these patients exhibited elevated Mϕ infiltration within a more immunosuppressive TME. Despite higher PD-L1 expression, these Mϕs remained refractory to KN046-mediated activation. We hypothesize that upregulated inhibitory checkpoints—VSIR, SIGLEC10, and LGALS9 —may block Mϕ reactivation by KN046, thereby sustaining an immunosuppressive niche that excludes effector cells and compromises treatment. Targeting these refractory Mϕ subsets may therefore reprogram the TME and enhance the efficacy of KN046 + KN026 therapy. Importantly, baseline Mϕ levels may serve as a predictive biomarker for resistance.

In conclusion, our study demonstrated the high efficacy of a chemotherapy-free regimen, KN046 plus KN026 combined treatment, for patients with anti-HER2 and chemotherapy-resistant HER2+ mBC. Moreover, our multiomics-based longitudinal tissue analysis revealed comprehensive TME changes that correlate with the response to this combined therapy, offering insights for future enhancement. This study highlights the crucial role that longitudinal multiomics tissue analysis can play in clinical trial, potentially serving as a paradigm for trial design.

Methods

Patient population and clinical study

This was an open-label, phase II, multicentered study (NCT04521179), to evaluate the efficacy, safety, and tolerability of KN026 in combination with KN046 in subjects with HER2-positive BC cohort. KN026 is a recombinant anti-Her2 bsAb expressed by CHO cells, targeting two distinct epitopes of the human Her2 protein, specifically Domain IV (the trastuzumab epitope) and Domain II (the pertuzumab epitope). KN046 is a bispecific single-domain antibody-Fc fusion protein that is recombinantly expressed in CHO cells, capable of simultaneously recognizing human PD-L1 and CTLA-4. It effectively blocks the interactions between PD-L1 and PD-1/CD80 as well as between CTLA-4 and CD80/86. The complete KN046 protein is a homodimer formed by interchain disulfide bonds, with each monomer consisting of a PD-L1 single-domain antibody and a CTLA-4 single-domain antibody domain linked in tandem, which is further connected to the crystallizable region (Fc) of human immunoglobulin. All enrolled patients received KN026 at 30 mg/kg every 3 weeks (Q3W) in combination with KN046 at 5 mg/kg Q3W, until disease progression, occurrence of intolerable adverse events or withdrawal of consent. A loading dose of KN026 was added on day 8 of cycle 1.

Between 2020 and 2021, tumor samples obtained by puncture biopsy from 17 patients with advanced HER2-positive BC before and after the treatment. Samples were used for primary immune cell isolation and analysis. All samples were collected from patients who had provided informed consent, and all related procedures were performed with the approval of the internal review and ethics board of Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hospital.

Single-cell RNA-seq library and TCR-seq library preparation and sequencing

Tissue samples were rinsed three times with Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution, mechanically minced into ∼1–2 mm fragments, and enzymatically dissociated in 2 mL of GEXSCOPE Tissue Dissociation Solution (Singleron) at 37°C for 15 min with gentle intermittent agitation. The resulting cell suspension was filtered through a 40 µm sterile strainer and centrifuged at 1,000 rpm for 5 min. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet was resuspended in 1 mL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS; HyClone). When red blood cell contamination was evident, erythrocytes were lysed with 2 mL of GEXSCOPE Red Blood Cell Lysis Buffer (Singleron) at 25°C for 10 min, followed by centrifugation at 500×g for 5 min. The pellet was resuspended in 1 mL PBS, and cell viability was assessed by trypan blue exclusion (Sigma) under light microscopy.

Single-cell suspensions were adjusted to 1×105 cells/mL in PBS and loaded onto microfluidic chips. Single-cell RNA-seq libraries were prepared using the GEXSCOPE Single Cell RNA Library Kit (Singleron Biotechnologies) according to the manufacturer’s protocol, diluted to 4 nM, and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with 150 bp paired-end reads.

scTCR-seq libraries were generated using the GEXSCOPE Single Cell Immuno-TCR Kit (Singleron Biotechnologies) following the manufacturer’s protocol. After cell lysis, magnetic beads bearing cell barcodes and unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) captured poly(A) tails of messenger RNA (mRNA) and TCR V(D)J constant regions via poly(T) sequences and V(D)J constant region-specific probes. Beads were collected from the chip, and captured mRNAs were reverse transcribed into complementary DNA and amplified. Two rounds of targeted enrichment for TCR sequences were performed, followed by PCR amplification to produce libraries compatible with the Illumina sequencing platform. Libraries were sequenced with 150 bp paired-end reads.

Single-cell gene expression analysis

Raw gene expression matrices were generated using CeleScope (Singleron MapSuite platform; https://github.com/singleron-RD/CeleScope). Briefly, read 1 sequence lacking poly(T) were discarded, and valid cell barcodes and UMIs were extracted. Adapters and poly(A) tails were trimmed from Read 2 using fastp (V.1.0). Filtered reads were aligned to the Ensembl reference genome with STAR (V.2.5.3a) and quantified using featureCounts (V.1.6.2). Reads sharing the same cell barcode, UMI, and gene annotation were collapsed to obtain UMI counts per gene per cell.

TCR clonotypes were assigned using the Cell Ranger (V.4.0.0) V(D)J pipeline with the GRCh38 reference genome. A TCR diversity metric containing clonotype frequency and barcode information was obtained. Only cells with one productive TCR α-chain (TRA) and one productive TCR β-chain (TRB) were retained for further analysis. Each unique TRA(s)–TRB(s) pair was defined as a clonotype. Clonotypes detected in at least two cells were classified as clonal, and the number of cells sharing a clonotype was used to quantify its degree of clonality.

Downstream scRNA-seq analysis was performed using Scanpy (V.1.9.6) in Python and Seurat (V.4.4.0) in R. Cells with fewer than 200 or more than 6,000 detected genes or >50% mitochondrial reads were excluded. After merging and normalization, an average of 5,200 genes (or UMIs) per cell was detected. Cell-specific barcodes enabled integration of scRNA-seq data with matched scTCR-seq datasets.

scRNA-seq clustering leading to cell types

Scanpy’s default parameters were used unless otherwise noted. In summary, for clustering all cell types, 2000 variable genes were identified, and principal component (PC) analysis was performed to reduce dimensionality after regressing out the number of UMIs (counts), percentage of mitochondrial genes (the Seurat object was converted to an AnnData object using the R package sceasy V.0.0.7). The top 30 PCs were used for clustering and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) for dimension reduction. Clusters in the two-dimensional UMAP representation consisted of distinct cell types identified by marker gene expression. Then the AnnData object was converted back to a Seurat object using the sceasy package. To ensure the purity of cell types, cells expressing markers of other cell types were removed after the second clustering.

The subclusters were identified based on marker gene expression (NK cells: KLRC1, FCGR3A, NCAM1; T cells: CD3G, CD2, CD4, CD8A, CD8B; Fibroblast cells: COL1A1, DCN, LUM; Myeloid cells: LYZ, CD68, TYROBP; Epithelial cells: CD24, KRT19, EPCAM; B and Plasma cells: CD79A, CD19, MS4A1). After annotation, we subset each cluster and performed a second round of clustering using the same parameters as the first clustering. Before reclustering, we cleared the information from the first calculation. After clustering in the Python environment, we transferred the data from an AnnData object to a Seurat object using the R package sceasy. Cell type annotation was performed using the R package SingleR (V.2.0.0) and the annotation web tool ACT (http://xteam.xbio.top/ACT/).

Single cells copy number analysis

Copy number instability was assessed with the R package inferCNV (https://github.com/broadinstitute/inferCNV; V.1.14.2), which is designed to infer copy number alterations from tumor single-cell RNA-seq data. This package compares the expression intensities of genes across malignant cells and relates this to expression in normal cells. A random subset of ECs, and fibroblasts was used as reference.

Due to the large number of cells in our dataset, we divide the samples for calculation to avoid stack overflow. Infercnv results yield a scoring matrix, and we generate heatmaps based on cell type for each sample. The CNV score for Epi-1 cells is significantly higher than other cell types, suggesting Epi-1 cells are malignant. We use k-means clustering to differentiate between malignant and normal epithelial cells within the Epi-1 subpopulation.

Trajectory inference analyses

To map the differentiation/conversion of cell types, pseudotime trajectory analysis was performed with Slingshot (V.2.6.0). The R package Slingshot was used to define computationally imputed pseudotime trajectories in T cells. The analyses were performed for CD8+ and CD4+ T cells separately. To model the T cell state transition, the diffusion map algorithm, which preserves the global relations and pseudotemporal ordering of cells, was applied to infer the differentiation trajectory. We fed the minicluster expression matrix and the previously calculated PCs matrix into the scanpy pipeline (V.1.9.6). A neighborhood graph based on PCs was constructed using the scanpy.pp.neighbors function. Given the neighborhood graph, the diffusion map was built using the scanpy.tl.diffmap function.

Differential expression and pathway analysis

We used the FindMarkers function from the Seurat package, employing the Wilcoxon likelihood-ratio test with default parameters, to identify differentially expressed genes (DEGs). Genes were considered significant if they were expressed in more than 25% of cells within a cluster, exhibited an average log-fold change greater than 0.25, and had adjusted p values less than 0.05. For cell type annotation and classification of cellular subpopulations within each cluster, we integrated expression profiles of classical marker genes identified from the DEGs with relevant literature information. Visualization of marker gene expression for each cell type was achieved using the FeaturePlot function from Seurat and the Plot_Density_Custom function from the scCustomize package. Additionally, we conducted Gene Ontology Biological Process enrichment analysis and GSEA using the clusterProfiler (V.4.10.1) and fgsea (V.1.28.0) R packages, respectively, both with their default settings. The annotation information of gene sets was downloaded from MSigDB (V.6.2).

Gene signatures analysis

The HALLMARK_INTERFERON_GAMMA_RESPONSE gene set from MSigDB (https://www.gsea-msigdb.org/gsea/msigdb/index.jsp) was used to define the IFN-γ response signature. The immune checkpoints signature included genes such as CTLA4, PDCD1, HAVCR2, ENTPD1, TIGIT, LAG3, and BTLA. Expression averages were calculated using the AverageExpression function from the Seurat R package. Gene set scores were computed as module scores for signatures/pathways using the Seurat function AddModuleScore. For correlation and receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analyses, we determined cell type fractions per patient pretreatment or post-treatment. For individual genes, the average expression was calculated per patient pretreatment or post-treatment in the specified cell (sub)types. Metagenes were defined as the average expression of genes within a gene set, calculated per cell. ROC curves and the associated area under the curve values were generated using the pROC (V.1.16.2) package in R with default parameters.

Assessing and analyzing TCRs

Using CeleScope, we conducted TCR V(D)J sequence assembly, clonotype identification, and analyses of TCR diversity and clonality, integrating these with scRNA-seq data. The assembly process enabled the reconstruction of TCR sequences and paired clonotype identification. For each single cell, CDR3 motifs were identified, and their productivity assessed. We analyzed the clonotype landscape and calculated clonal fractions for each clonotype. The TCR clonotype diversity matrix was generated using the scRepertoire R package (V.1.8.0). Lastly, clonotype data were integrated with T cell phenotypes derived from single-cell gene expression profiles based on shared cell barcodes.

Cell-to-cell communication of scRNA-seq data

We used the R package CellChat (V.2.1.2) to deduce interactions between cells, leveraging a public repository of ligands, receptors, cofactors, and their interactions. The versatile and user-friendly toolkit CellChat, along with its web-based Explorer (http://www.cellchat.org/), facilitates the discovery of novel intercellular communications and the construction of cell–cell communication atlases. For the analysis of cell interactions, expression levels were normalized to the total read counts mapped to the same set of coding genes across all transcriptomes, and then averaged within each single-cell cluster or sample.
