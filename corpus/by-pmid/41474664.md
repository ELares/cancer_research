Phytochemicals as Emerging Antiproliferative Agents in Head and Neck Cancer: Molecular Mechanisms and Therapeutic Strategies

Abstract

Background

Cancer remains a leading cause of mortality worldwide, necessitating innovative therapeutic strategies beyond conventional treatments. The increasing interest in natural and synthetic antiproliferative compounds is driven by their ability to target oncogenic pathways implicated in tumor progression, metastasis, and drug resistance. This review explores the potential of bioactive phytochemicals and molecularly targeted therapies, particularly epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) inhibitors, in modulating cancer cell survival, proliferation, and immune evasion. A central focus is placed on the phosphoinositide 3-kinases (PI3Ks)/protein kinase B (AKT), JAK/STAT, and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MEK)/extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) signaling pathways, which are frequently dysregulated in therapy-resistant cancers, particularly head and neck cancer.

Summary

Emerging evidence suggests that phytochemicals such as curcumin, resveratrol, and flavonoids not only suppress oncogenic signaling but also enhance apoptosis, inhibit epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and regulate oxidative stress responses. Additionally, recent preclinical and clinical studies indicate that combinatorial applications of phytochemicals with targeted agents can sensitize resistant tumors to chemotherapy and immunotherapy, thereby improving therapeutic efficacy. However, a major challenge limiting the clinical translation of phytochemicals is their low bioavailability and rapid metabolism. Advances in nanoparticle-based drug delivery, synthetic derivatives, and CRISPR-mediated genome editing offer promising solutions to overcome these limitations, ensuring optimal stability, targeted delivery, and enhanced anticancer activity.

Key Messages

By integrating traditional phytotherapy with modern molecular oncology, this review highlights novel synergistic strategies that may revolutionize cancer treatment, paving the way for personalized anticancer therapeutics. DBCs and herbal medicine influence key oncogenic pathways, including EGFR, PI3K/AKT, and MEK/ERK. Human papillomavirus (HPV)-positive and HPV-negative tumors exhibit distinct molecular profiles, affecting their response to conventional treatments and adjunctive therapies. Polyphenols such as curcumin, resveratrol, and quercetin, along with herbal extracts including Scutellaria baicalensis and Camellia sinensis, demonstrate the ability to modulate oxidative stress, apoptosis, and immune responses while reducing therapy resistance. Nanoparticle-based formulations improve the bioavailability of these compounds, enhancing their anticancer effects. However, their effectiveness varies based on the HPV status of the tumor, with HPV-positive cancers showing greater sensitivity to immune-modulating compounds.

Introduction

Despite major advances in oncology research and treatment modalities, many cancers remain highly aggressive and therapeutically challenging. Head and neck cancer (HNC) is particularly complex due to its heterogeneous origins, anatomical diversity, and molecular alterations that contribute to therapy resistance. Current treatment regimens, including surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy, remain the standard of care but are associated with severe toxicities, poor patient outcomes, and high recurrence rates. While HPV-positive HNC is often more responsive to radiation therapy, HPV-negative tumors, particularly those driven by EGFR mutations, exhibit greater resistance to conventional treatments. One of the key challenges in HNC treatment is the development of resistance to standard therapies, which limits long-term disease control. The overexpression of EGFR has been observed in up to 90% of HNC cases and is associated with tumor progression, metastasis, and poor prognosis. Although cetuximab, an EGFR-targeted monoclonal antibody (mAb), has demonstrated clinical efficacy, resistance frequently emerges due to compensatory activation of downstream oncogenic pathways, particularly Phosphoinositide 3-kinases (PI3Ks)/protein kinase B (AKT) and mitogen-activated protein kinase (MEK) and extracellular signal-regulated kinase (ERK) signaling. Additionally, standard chemotherapy and radiotherapy are often accompanied by severe side effects such as oral mucositis, dysphagia, and immune suppression, necessitating the development of alternative or adjunctive therapeutic strategies. Given these limitations, natural compounds with antiproliferative properties have gained significant interest in recent years. Phytochemicals derived from medicinal plants exhibit broad-spectrum anticancer activities, including apoptosis induction, modulation of oxidative stress, and inhibition of key oncogenic pathways. Several plant-derived bioactive molecules have been identified as potential EGFR pathway modulators, capable of exerting antiproliferative, proapoptotic, and antimetastatic effects in HNC models. Curcumin and resveratrol have been shown to inhibit EGFR signaling, suppress PI3K/AKT activation, and sensitize tumor cells to standard therapies. Quercetin and apigenin promote EGFR degradation while inducing apoptosis through caspase activation. A significant limitation of phytochemical-based therapies is their low bioavailability and rapid metabolism, which restricts their clinical efficacy. Advances in formulation strategies, such as nanoparticle-based drug delivery and synthetic derivatives, have been explored to improve the pharmacokinetics and therapeutic potential of these compounds. Nanoparticle-encapsulated curcumin has demonstrated enhanced plasma stability and tumor accumulation, while polymeric resveratrol formulations improve oral bioavailability and reduce tumor burden in preclinical models. Additionally, CRISPR-Cas9 technology offers novel opportunities for overcoming therapy resistance by precisely targeting EGFR-mutant subclones and sensitizing tumors to phytochemicals. This review aims to critically evaluate the potential of bioactive phytochemicals and molecularly targeted therapies in the context of HNC treatment. By integrating traditional herbal medicine principles with contemporary molecular oncology, we highlight emerging combinatorial approaches that could overcome resistance mechanisms and enhance treatment efficacy, ultimately advancing precision medicine-based cancer therapeutics.

Methodology

A comprehensive literature search was conducted using PubMed, Web of Science, and Scopus to identify relevant studies evaluating the effects of natural products on HNC in in vitro and in vivo models. To ensure precision and relevance, the search strategy incorporated Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) and Descriptores en Ciencias de la Salud (DeCS) terms, specifically “head and neck cancer” and “natural products,” along with related keywords. The inclusion criteria encompassed peer-reviewed articles published in English between January 2014 and October 2024, focusing on the anticancer properties of natural compounds in preclinical HNC models. Studies that provided molecular insights into key oncogenic pathways, therapeutic resistance, or potential synergies with conventional treatments were prioritized. This rigorous selection process aimed to consolidate the most scientifically robust and clinically relevant findings in the field of phytotherapy and molecular oncology for HNC treatment.

Current Challenges in HNC Treatment

The management of HNC depends on multiple factors, including tumor stage, anatomical site, and surgical feasibility. Standard treatment modalities comprise surgical resection, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy, often used in combination to achieve optimal therapeutic outcomes. Despite their clinical benefits, these therapies impose significant toxicities, such as mucositis, dysphagia, neuropathy, and immunosuppression, which drastically impact patient quality of life. Systemic chemotherapy remains a cornerstone of HNC treatment, but its lack of selectivity results in damage to both malignant and normal cells, leading to substantial morbidity. Given these limitations, the demand for more selective and less toxic treatment strategies has increased. In particular, targeted molecular therapies have gained prominence for their ability to inhibit tumor-specific signaling pathways while sparing healthy tissues. The EGFR and its associated family members human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2/ErbB2), human epidermal growth factor receptor 3 (HER3/ErbB3), and human epidermal growth factor receptor 4 (HER4/ErbB4) play central roles in HNC progression, invasion, and treatment resistance. Overexpression of EGFR is observed in approximately 80–90% of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma cases, establishing it as a key therapeutic target. mAbs such as cetuximab and tyrosine kinase inhibitors such as afatinib and erlotinib have shown promise in targeting EGFR-driven tumors, yet resistance mechanisms often emerge, necessitating combinatory or alternative therapeutic approaches. To address these challenges, this review aimed to analyze the epidemiological trends of HPV-positive and HPV-negative HNC, investigate the mechanistic roles of bioactive natural compounds in modulating key oncogenic pathways, and assess the therapeutic impact of EGFR-targeted treatments and their potential role in overcoming therapy resistance. HNC arises from the mucosal epithelium of the oral cavity, pharynx, and larynx, representing one of the most prevalent malignancies in the head and neck region. While laryngeal and oral cavity cancers are predominantly associated with tobacco and alcohol exposure, pharyngeal cancers are strongly linked to human papillomavirus (HPV) infection. Given their distinct molecular and clinical characteristics, HNC cases are classified into HPV-positive and HPV-negative subtypes. HPV-positive HNC accounts for approximately 60% of all oropharyngeal cancers and exhibits a unique molecular, pathological, and clinical profile. Notable distinctions between HPV-positive and HPV-negative tumors include HPV-positive tumors display higher CD8+ T-cell infiltration, contributing to enhanced immune responsiveness. HPV-positive HNCs exhibit lower EGFR expression, influencing differential sensitivity to EGFR inhibitors. HPV-positive cases are often characterized by inactivation of tumor suppressor genes (TP53 and RB) due to HPV oncoproteins E6 and E7. Frequent activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway in HPV-driven cancers contributes to oncogenesis and tumor progression. Conversely, HPV-negative HNC is associated with higher EGFR expression and pathway activation, contributing to reduced sensitivity to EGFR-targeted therapies; greater resistance to radiotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), necessitating alternative treatment strategies; a stronger association with tobacco and alcohol consumption, leading to higher mutation burdens; more aggressive tumor progression and poorer prognosis, requiring combinatorial treatment approaches. Recent evidence suggests that EGFR family members, particularly EGFR2 (HER2), also contribute to HNC progression. While EGFR overexpression is well documented, studies indicate that HER2 amplification and mutations may serve as additional drivers of tumor aggressiveness. Emerging clinical trials exploring pan-HER inhibitors (e.g., afatinib, dacomitinib) suggest that these agents may provide therapeutic benefits beyond conventional EGFR inhibitors, particularly in treatment-refractory HNC cases. The Pathophysiology and Tumor Microenvironment in HNC

Despite recent advancements in targeted therapies and immunotherapy, less than 20% of HNC patients achieve a durable response, underscoring the urgent need for next-generation treatment strategies. Given its distinct tumor microenvironment and its classification into HPV-positive and HPV-negative subtypes, HNC serves as an ideal model for studying the interplay between immune and nonimmune cells across different anatomical sites and disease stages. The emergence of cutting-edge technologies, such as single-cell RNA sequencing and spatial transcriptomics, has provided a deeper understanding of cellular heterogeneity within this complex tumor microenvironment. Integrating these insights will be crucial for developing more effective, personalized immunotherapies that enhance tumor-specific T-cell responses and improve patient outcomes. HPV status, along with the distinction between locally advanced and recurrent/metastatic (R/M) disease, is critical due to the influence of prior therapies and tumor evolution within the TME, shaping both treatment response and disease progression. While HPV-positive tumors exhibit higher immune infiltration and enhanced radiosensitivity, HPV-negative tumors are highly resistant to standard treatments due to higher mutation burdens and EGFR pathway activation. Prophylactic HPV vaccination has demonstrated long-term efficacy in preventing cervical and anogenital cancers, but its impact on oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma remains uncertain with projections suggesting that a decline in HPV-driven oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma cases may not be apparent until 2060 or beyond. At the molecular level, HNC pathogenesis is driven by the progressive accumulation of genetic alterations in key regulatory genes that control cell cycle progression, signaling cascades, angiogenesis, and apoptosis. These genetic aberrations, including TP53 and CDKN2A mutations, frequently disrupt cell cycle checkpoints, leading to uncontrolled proliferation and tumor development. Additionally, oncogenic drivers such as PIK3CA amplifications contribute to tumor progression and therapeutic resistance, particularly in HPV-negative tumors. Beyond molecular alterations, HNC significantly impacts functional and psychosocial well-being. The disease frequently results in severe impairments in speech, breathing, and swallowing, which are further exacerbated by treatment-related toxicities. Pain management remains a major clinical challenge, influenced by tumor location, nerve infiltration, and treatment-induced inflammation. Addressing these multifaceted challenges requires a paradigm shift toward more selective, personalized, and mechanistically driven treatment strategies.

Foundational Principles for Effective Treatment Strategies in HNC

While advances in treatment for locally advanced and R/M HNC have benefited certain patient cohorts, significant therapeutic gaps remain. Oral cavity HNC is primarily managed through surgical resection, followed by adjuvant chemoradiotherapy. Molecularly targeted therapies, such as EGFR inhibitors, have demonstrated modest efficacy in locally advanced disease. Additionally, ICIs targeting PD-1 are approved for R/M HNC, yet only 15–20% of patients achieve a durable response, despite the tumor’s high PD-1/PD-L1 expression. Thus, overall survival rates remain low due to the highly heterogeneous nature of the disease and the frequent late-stage diagnosis. These challenges underscore the need for more effective, personalized therapeutic strategies in HNC treatment modalities. In addition to limited therapeutic success, conventional treatment options – including surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy – are often associated with severe side effects, leading to both acute and chronic toxicities. Patients frequently experience complications such as dysphagia, mucositis, nausea, vomiting, and persistent difficulty in swallowing, all of which negatively impact overall health and quality of life. These limitations underscore the critical need for innovative therapeutic strategies that enhance treatment efficacy while minimizing toxicity. The development of novel, targeted therapies that improve patient survival and quality of life remains an urgent priority in HNC research. A key aspect of this effort involves the identification and clinical implementation of more selective and less toxic treatment approaches, including molecularly targeted drugs and immunotherapies.

Emerging Therapeutic Innovations

Phytochemical-Based Targeted Therapies

Given the limitations of standard treatments, recent research has turned toward phytochemical-based therapies as potential adjuncts or alternatives to conventional treatments. Plant-derived bioactive compounds have demonstrated the ability to modulate key oncogenic pathways such as PI3K/AKT, MEK/ERK, and JAK/STAT. They have also been shown to enhance tumor immune recognition and reduce immune evasion mechanisms, while improving treatment response by overcoming EGFR-mediated resistance. For example, curcumin, resveratrol, and flavonoids have been reported to downregulate EGFR expression, inhibit PI3K/AKT signaling, and enhance chemosensitivity in HNC models. Sulforaphane (derived from Brassica oleracea) has shown promise in targeting oxidative stress, epigenetic modifications, and tumor metabolism. These compounds not only suppress tumor growth but also minimize toxicity, making them attractive candidates for precision oncology. While the specific mechanisms and clinical applications of phytochemicals in HNC therapy are gaining increasing interest, a more detailed discussion of their molecular interactions, bioavailability challenges, and potential for clinical translation will be explored in the following sections.

Nanoparticle Drug Delivery for Enhanced Bioavailability

One of the major challenges of phytochemicals is poor bioavailability and systemic stability. To address this, nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems have emerged as a promising approach to enhance the solubility, absorption, and tumor-specific targeting of natural anticancer agents. Nanoparticle formulations offer multiple advantages over conventional drug delivery, including increased plasma stability and half-life, reducing the need for high-dosage administration; tumor-selective accumulation via enhanced permeability and retention effect, which minimizes off-target toxicity; controlled and sustained drug release, ensuring prolonged therapeutic action. Several nanoparticle-based approaches have been explored for phytochemical delivery in HNC treatment. Lipid-based nanocarriers: liposomes and solid lipid nanoparticles encapsulating curcumin or resveratrol have demonstrated enhanced oral bioavailability and greater tumor accumulation in preclinical HNC models. Poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) nanoparticles improve the pharmacokinetics of flavonoids like quercetin and apigenin, increasing tumor uptake and reducing oxidative stress in cancer cells. Gold nanoparticle-based systems conjugated with anti-STAT3 oligonucleotides have been developed to radiosensitize HNC cells and enhance EGFR-targeted therapy efficacy. Combination therapies using EGFR inhibitors with nanoparticle-formulated phytochemicals have shown potential in overcoming EGFR resistance and enhancing the efficacy of ICIs. To fully harness the potential of nanoparticle-mediated phytochemical therapy, several advancements must be prioritized. Engineering nanoparticles with EGFR antibodies, aptamers, or transferrin receptors can improve specificity toward HNC tumors. CRISPR-based approaches: CRISPR-/Cas9-mediated gene editing combined with nanoparticle delivery could selectively target therapy-resistant HNC subpopulations, further increasing treatment precision. Combination strategies: Co-delivery of phytochemicals and immune-modulating agents (PD-1 inhibitors) using nanoparticles may potentiate antitumor immunity, creating a dual-action therapeutic approach for HNC.

CRISPR-Based Gene Editing for Tumor Suppression

Recent advancements in gene editing technologies, particularly CRISPR-Cas9, have opened new avenues for enhancing the efficacy of natural antiproliferative agents in cancer therapy. CRISPR-based strategies enable precise genetic modifications that can sensitize cancer cells to phytochemicals by disrupting key resistance mechanisms. For example, CRISPR has been used to knockout genes associated with EGFR-mediated drug resistance, thereby increasing tumor sensitivity to both small molecule inhibitors and plant-derived compounds. The integration of CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing technologies offers an innovative strategy to target oncogenic drivers such as PIK3CA, TP53, and CDKN2A, knockout resistance-associated genes in EGFR-mutant tumors, and sensitize tumors to phytochemicals and immunotherapy agents. By leveraging these approaches, researchers aim to tailor cancer treatments to individual genetic profiles, maximizing therapeutic responses while minimizing toxicity. In addition to gene editing, nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems are being explored to improve the bioavailability and stability of natural compounds. Encapsulating phytochemicals in polymeric or lipid-based nanoparticles enhances their solubility, protects them from metabolic degradation, and facilitates targeted delivery to tumor cells. This approach has been successfully applied to compounds like curcumin and resveratrol, significantly enhancing their anticancer activity in preclinical models. Moreover, synthetic analogs of naturally occurring phytochemicals, such as flavonoid derivatives, are being developed to optimize pharmacokinetics and therapeutic efficacy. These engineered molecules retain the beneficial properties of their natural counterparts while exhibiting improved stability, potency, and tumor specificity.

FDA-Approved Therapeutics for HNC

A variety of chemotherapeutic agents and targeted therapies have been approved for the treatment of HNC, each with specific mechanisms of action and associated toxicities. Bleomycin, a glycopeptide antibiotic with potent antineoplastic properties, is commonly used for HNC, Hodgkin’s lymphoma, and testicular cancer. However, its clinical utility is often limited by the risk of pulmonary toxicity. Cetuximab, a mAb targeting EGFR, has shown efficacy in HNC treatment. However, its use is associated with severe adverse events, including anaphylaxis, as well as respiratory, dermatologic, and hematologic complications. Cisplatin, a platinum-based chemotherapy agent, is widely used in the treatment of various malignancies, including HNC, breast, lung, ovarian, testicular, cervical, and bladder cancers. Its clinical application is restricted due to significant toxicities such as nephrotoxicity, ototoxicity, cardiotoxicity, neurotoxicity, leukemia, and neuropathy. Carboplatin, another platinum-based chemotherapeutic, is often used in combination with adjuvant therapies to treat multiple cancer types. While it mitigates some of the severe side effects of cisplatin, it can still cause bone marrow suppression and renal dysfunction. Docetaxel, a microtubule-stabilizing agent, inhibits tumor cell proliferation by disrupting mitotic processes. Its use is often accompanied by adverse effects such as myelosuppression, vomiting, diarrhea, stomatitis, and colitis. Methotrexate, a folate antagonist, remains a key agent in the treatment of both pediatric and adult malignancies, including acute lymphoblastic leukemia, osteosarcoma, and non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma. However, its administration can lead to severe toxicities, such as neurotoxicity, leukoencephalopathy, and mucositis. Nivolumab and pembrolizumab, both fully human PD-1 IgG4 mAbs, have revolutionized immunotherapy for HNC. Nevertheless, their use is linked to immune-related adverse events. 5-Fluorouracil, a widely used chemotherapeutic agent, inhibits thymidylate synthase and disrupts nucleic acid metabolism, leading to DNA damage. While effective, its clinical application is often complicated by toxicities such as neutropenia, stomatitis, and diarrhea.

Genetic Variation in HNC

Genomic alterations play a crucial role in the initiation, progression, and treatment resistance of HNC. Mutations in genes regulating DNA methylation, histone modification, chromatin remodeling, microRNAs, and other epigenetic mechanisms contribute to tumor development and therapy resistance. A comprehensive analysis of 673 HNC samples from the cBioPortal for Cancer Genomics identified frequent mutations in key genes, including TP53, TTN, FAT1, MUC16, CDKN2A, CSMD3, NOTCH1, SYNE1, LRP1B, and PIK3CA. These mutations are known to drive oncogenesis by promoting unchecked cellular proliferation, invasion, and resistance to apoptosis. Among the most frequently altered genes in HNC, TP53 encodes the tumor suppressor protein p53, which regulates cell cycle arrest, DNA repair, apoptosis, and senescence. p53 also induces DNA repair genes such as GADD45 and XPC and when cellular damage is irreparable, it activates apoptotic pathways via BCL-2-associated X, apoptosis regulator (BAX), PUMA, and NOXA. Given the high prevalence of TP53 mutations in HNC, it has emerged as a promising therapeutic target. Gendicine, a recombinant human TP53 adenovirus, represents a first-in-class gene therapy for HNC, administered via intratumoral injection, intracavitary, or intravascular infusion. Another frequently altered gene in HNC, PIK3CA, encodes the catalytic subunit p110α of PI3K, a key regulator of the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway. This pathway governs cellular processes such as proliferation, survival, metabolism, and migration, and its hyperactivation is strongly associated with tumorigenesis, chemoresistance, and radioresistance. Therapeutic strategies targeting PIK3CA mutations include selective PI3Kα inhibitors (alpelisib), PI3Kδ inhibitors (idelalisib), pan-PI3K inhibitors (copanlisib), and dual PI3K/mTOR inhibitors (BEZ235). TP53 mutations, observed in 62.8% of cases, are the most common, underscoring the critical role of p53 dysfunction in HNC. The presence of frequent mutations in genes such as TTN and MUC16, though not traditionally classified as oncogenes or tumor suppressors, points to their possible involvement in genomic instability and tumor evolution. Understanding these mutational landscapes can provide insights into personalized therapeutic strategies, biomarker discovery, and potential targets for molecular interventions.

Key Signaling Pathways Driving HNC Cancer Cell Proliferation

One of the defining hallmarks of cancer and a key driver of tumor progression is the ability to sustain proliferative signaling. Extensive research has been conducted to identify molecular targets and therapeutic interventions that inhibit these signaling pathways.

PI3K/AKT Signaling Targeting Herbal Medicine

The PI3K/Akt signaling pathway is a critical regulator of cellular processes such as survival, metastasis, and cancer metabolism across various malignancies, including HNC. Given its central role in tumorigenesis, PI3K/Akt inhibition has emerged as an attractive target for anticancer therapy. This review highlights four natural products that modulate PI3K/AKT signaling in HNC cell lines. Seo et al. demonstrated that deoxypodophyllotoxin, isolated from Anthriscus sylvestris L., induced apoptosis by modulating the PI3K/AKT and p38 MAPK signaling pathways in HNC cell lines. Guan et al. investigated curcumol, a compound commonly found in Curcuma species, and found that it inhibited migration, invasion, and metastasis by negatively regulating NCL and EBNA1 expression, thereby inactivating the VEGFA/VEGFR1/PI3K/AKT signaling pathway in HPV-positive HNC cells. Gu reported that icariin, a flavonoid from Epimedium spp., reduced phospho-p85, phospho-AKT, and phospho-STAT3 expression, controlling proliferation, migration, and invasion in HNC cells. Additionally, Wang et al. studied the activity of the chloroform extract of Sophora tonkinensis Gagnep, demonstrating that it decreased levels of P-PI3K, PI3K, P-AKT, AKT, P-mTOR, and mTOR in a concentration-dependent manner, thereby inhibiting proliferation and inducing apoptosis.

PI3K/AKT Signaling Targeting Inhibitor

The upregulation of PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling in tumor cells has led to extensive research on chemotherapeutic agents capable of targeting this pathway. PI3K inhibitors are classified based on their specificity: pan inhibitors targeting multiple enzymes, including PI3K, AKT, and mTOR; dual inhibitors targeting PI3K and mTOR; and isoform-specific inhibitors that selectively target PI3K isoforms. Currently, five PI3K pathway inhibitors have received FDA approval, demonstrating promising results in preclinical and clinical trials. Additionally, combination therapies utilizing PI3K inhibitors alongside ICIs (anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies) or chemotherapy have shown potential in overcoming resistance mechanisms. Recent studies have identified resistance mechanisms to PI3K inhibitors, including activation of alternative oncogenic pathways such as RAS/RAF/MEK/ERK and compensatory upregulation of EGFR signaling. Efforts to overcome these resistance pathways have focused on combination regimens involving PI3K inhibitors with other targeted agents, such as EGFR, HER2, and CDK4/6 inhibitors.

MEK/ERK Signaling Pathway and Herbal Medicine

The MEK/ERK pathway is a critical regulator of cell survival and proliferation. Notably, several mechanisms of therapeutic resistance converge on the reactivation of this pathway, underscoring its importance as a potential therapeutic target in HNC. The dysregulation of the MEK/ERK pathway has been implicated in tumor progression, invasion, and resistance to therapy, particularly in EGFR-mutant HNC tumors. Yang et al. investigated an ethanolic extract from Duchesnea indica, demonstrating that it inhibited the phosphorylation of ERK1/2 in a dose-dependent manner while leaving JNK1/2 and p38 kinase phosphorylation unaffected. Bai et al. found that Ficus septica extract suppressed p-ERK expression, elevated reactive oxygen species production, and inhibited migration in HNC cell lines. Yeh et al. reported that geraniin, a polyphenol isolated from Geranium thunbergii, significantly decreased p-ERK1/2 levels, resulting in reduced proliferation, migration, and invasion of HNC cells. Additionally, Sanghavi et al. demonstrated that an extract of Lycium barbarum reduced ERK1/2 and AKT1 phosphorylation, leading to significant antiproliferative and anti-invasive effects in HNC cell lines. Hsieh et al. studied celastrol, a bioactive compound from Tripterygium wilfordii, and observed that it increased the phosphorylation of p38, ERK1/2, and JNK1/2 in cisplatin-resistant HNC cells, suggesting a role in overcoming drug resistance and therapy adaptation.

Targeting Key Signaling Pathways to Induce Cancer Cell Death in HNC

Cancer therapies rely on the activation or modulation of specific cell death pathways, particularly apoptosis, necroptosis, and ferroptosis, as well as the regulation of cell cycle progression. A comprehensive understanding of these mechanisms is critical for the development of novel therapeutic strategies for HNC.

Cell Cycle Modulation by Herbal Medicine

The mammalian cell cycle consists of four sequential phases: G1, S, G2, and M. In cancer, dysregulation of the cell cycle contributes to uncontrolled tumor growth, therapy resistance, and immune escape. Targeting the cell cycle is an important strategy in chemotherapy, as inhibitors can block proliferation, induce apoptosis, and shape immune responses. Xiao et al. demonstrated that sakuranetin and 6-methoxycapillarisin from A. dracunculus induced cell cycle arrest and decreased mitochondrial membrane potential in HNC cell lines. Hong et al. found that Aster tataricus extract induced cell cycle arrest and inhibited colony formation in HNC cells. Dai et al. reported that Camellia nitidissima extract triggered G0/G1 phase arrest and apoptosis in HNC cells. Additionally, Zhou et al. examined a Chinese medicinal formulation, Erchen decoction plus Huiyanzhuyu decoction, which induced apoptosis, inhibited cell cycle progression, and suppressed migration and invasion in HNC cells. Aggarwal et al. demonstrated that garcinol, a polyphenol from Garcinia indica, induced cell cycle arrest and apoptosis in HNC cells. Jia et al. investigated Tonglian decoction (通莲汤), a formulation containing Hedyotis diffusa, Rehmanniae praeparata, and Angelicae sinensis. Proton Pump Inhibitors in HNC Treatment

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) have been investigated for their potential to enhance the efficacy of various anticancer treatments, including tyrosine kinase inhibitors and ICIs. Recent studies suggest that PPIs may sensitize HNC cells to radiation therapy and chemotherapy by altering tumor acidity and modulating drug resistance mechanisms. Additionally, PPIs play a critical role in managing reflux-related complications in patients undergoing radiotherapy and chemotherapy for HNC. However, the direct antitumor effects of PPIs in HNC remain controversial, with conflicting results regarding their impact on tumor progression and metastasis. While some studies propose that PPIs reduce tumor acidity, inhibit autophagy, and enhance chemosensitivity, others suggest that they may induce adaptive resistance by upregulating survival pathways such as PI3K/AKT and MAPK/ERK. Future studies should focus on elucidating the precise mechanisms of PPIs in HNC pathophysiology, particularly in combination with targeted therapies like EGFR inhibitors and immune checkpoint blockade.

Natural Products and Gene Modulation in HNC

Natural compounds have been extensively studied for their ability to regulate gene expression in HNC, with a focus on oncogenic pathways, tumor suppressors, and epigenetic modifications. These compounds modulate both protein-coding genes and noncoding RNAs, influencing cancer progression, apoptosis, and therapy resistance. Zheng et al. reported that an extract from Actinidia chinensis Planch roots suppressed the proliferation and invasion of hypopharyngeal carcinoma cells by downregulating E2F1 and MNX1-AS1, indicating its role in epigenetic regulation. Yue et al. investigated the antimetastatic effects of Andrographis paniculata extract in esophageal cancer, demonstrating its ability to downregulate CD81 expression and inhibit tumor cell motility. Sophia et al. found that nimbolide, a limonoid compound from Azadirachta indica, inactivated the PI3K/Akt/GSK3β pathway via miR-126 upregulation and HOTAIR downregulation, revealing its potential for targeting epigenetic regulators in HNC. Atwan and Al-Ogaidi developed a nanoencapsulated curcumin formulation, which effectively induced apoptosis by downregulating oncogenic microRNAs miR-20a and miR-21, suggesting its role in enhancing chemotherapy sensitivity in esophageal cancer. Regarding EGFR and PI3K/AKT modulation by natural products, Zhou et al. demonstrated that β-elemene, a sesquiterpene from Curcuma wenyujin, inhibits STAT3, EGFR, and CTNNB1, leading to apoptosis and tumor suppression in esophageal cancer models. Kizaibek et al. found that Daphne altaica extract upregulated PPARγ, a key tumor suppressor that regulates lipid metabolism and tumor growth in esophageal carcinoma cells. Hernandes et al. showed that the triterpenoids maytenin and 22-β-hydroxymaytenin from Maytenus ilicifolia downregulated miR-27a and miR-20a, both of which are linked to chemoresistance in HNC. Ji et al. identified Morin, a flavonoid from Moraceae, as an inhibitor of YAP-targeting genes (CTGF, CYR61, ANKRD), highlighting its role in Hippo pathway regulation and metastasis inhibition in tongue carcinoma. The integration of natural products with standard HNC treatments, particularly EGFR inhibitors, PI3K/AKT modulators, and ICIs, may offer new therapeutic opportunities. However, the precise mechanisms of action require further elucidation through preclinical and clinical studies. The role of PPIs in HNC treatment remains an area of debate, necessitating more research into their long-term impact on tumor progression and therapy resistance. Moving forward, multi-targeted strategies that incorporate natural compounds, epigenetic therapy, and targeted inhibition of key oncogenic pathways will be essential for improving HNC patient outcomes.

Targeting the Caspase-Dependent Apoptosis Pathway with Herbal Medicine

Apoptosis is a highly regulated and evolutionarily conserved form of programmed cell death, essential for normal physiological processes and tumor suppression. The apoptotic cascade is driven by proteolytic cleavage of intracellular proteins, primarily through the activation of effector caspases such as caspase-3, -6, and -7. Given its critical role in cancer therapy, targeting apoptotic pathways has become a widely explored strategy for developing novel anticancer agents. Several studies have demonstrated the efficacy of natural compounds in modulating apoptosis through caspase activation. Wang et al. reported that galangin, isolated from Alpinia officinarum Hance, activated caspase-3 via the PI3K/AKT/NF-κB axis, leading to apoptosis in laryngeal cancer cell. Suzuki et al. demonstrated that andrographolide, derived from Andrographis paniculata, increased c-caspase-3 and c-Poly (ADP-ribose) polymerases (PARPs) expression, inducing apoptosis in HNC cells. Liu et al. found that an ethanolic extract of Antrodia cinnamomea upregulated cleaved caspase-9 and PARP proteins, triggering apoptosis in HNC cells. Branco et al. investigated Araucaria angustifolia extract, demonstrating increased expression of AIF, Bax, PARP, and c-caspase-3, hallmarks of intrinsic apoptosis in HNC cells. Additionally, natural compounds have been shown to modulate mitochondrial apoptotic pathways, influencing Bax/Bcl-2 ratios and cytochrome c release. Al-Afifi et al. showed that Dracaena cinnabari extract triggered caspase-3/7 and caspase-9 activation, leading to mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis in HNC cells. Li et al. demonstrated that the extract, fraction, and compound isolated from Juglans sigillata induced apoptosis with increased expression of Bax proteins, c-caspase-3, and inhibited the migration of HNC cell lines. Han et al. found that Kochia scoparia extract induced dose-dependent caspase-3 and -9 activation, increasing c-PARP expression and apoptotic cell death in HNC cell lines. Huang et al. reported that pristimerin, a bioactive compound from Maytenus ilicifolia, downregulated Bcl-2, while upregulating caspase-3 and -9, leading to apoptosis in a dose-dependent manner. The therapeutic potential of these natural compounds suggests that caspase-dependent apoptosis modulation could be an effective strategy for HNC treatment.

With advancements in cancer research, novel mechanisms of cell death beyond apoptosis have been extensively investigated, including autophagy, necroptosis, anoikis, ferroptosis, and pyroptosis. Elkashty et al. reported that sulforaphane, derived from Brassica oleracea (broccoli), synergistically enhanced chemotherapy efficacy, leading to increased HNC cell apoptosis. Pan et al. examined acetyl-11-keto-beta-boswellic acid, derived from Boswellia sacra, demonstrating its inhibitory effects on HNC proliferation, alongside autophagy induction. The integration of phytochemicals into HNC treatment strategies may provide novel therapeutic options that minimize chemotherapy resistance and toxicity.

Herbal Medicine and Phytochemical Compound against HNC

Due to the limitations of conventional cancer treatments, extensive research has been directed toward identifying novel plant-derived compounds for HNC therapy. Historically, natural products have played a pivotal role in drug discovery, with approximately 65% of all FDA-approved drugs being natural or derived from natural compounds. Notable examples of natural products with clinical relevance include Paclitaxel, extracted from Taxus brevifolia, camptothecin, obtained from Camptotheca acuminata Decne, and the alkaloids vinblastine, vincristine, vindesine, and vinorelbine, all derived from Catharanthus roseus. Table 3 summarizes key FDA-approved phytochemicals utilized for HNC and other cancers. Despite their promise, phytochemical-based therapies face multiple challenges in clinical translation, particularly in the following areas. Many phytochemicals exhibit poor bioavailability due to rapid metabolism and low systemic absorption. Examples include curcumin (Curcuma longa), which undergoes extensive first-pass metabolism, limiting its therapeutic efficacy. Resveratrol, found in grapes, is rapidly metabolized and cleared from circulation, reducing its bioavailability. To overcome these limitations, several formulation strategies have been developed. Encapsulating phytochemicals in nanoparticles enhances their stability, absorption, and targeted delivery. Liposomal paclitaxel has demonstrated improved therapeutic efficacy and reduced toxicity. Self-microemulsifying drug delivery systems: these systems improve the solubility and bioavailability of lipophilic drugs such as curcumin and resveratrol. Bioenhancers: certain natural compounds, such as piperine (from black pepper), inhibit drug-metabolizing enzymes, increasing the bioavailability of coadministered phytochemicals. While phytochemicals show anticancer potential, their efficacy versus standard treatments remains under investigation. Chemotherapy and radiation: these have well-defined clinical protocols, while phytochemicals lack standardized dosing and treatment regimens. Conventional therapies have undergone rigorous phase I–III trials, whereas most phytochemicals remain in preclinical or early-phase trials. To integrate phytochemicals into modern oncology, future research must focus on optimizing bioavailability and formulation strategies for effective clinical application and exploring combinatory approaches, such as synergistic effects with immunotherapy (e.g., PD-1 blockade) and targeted therapies.

Integrating natural products into HNC treatment regimens alongside surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy has emerged as a viable strategy, demonstrating synergistic or additive effects while reducing the toxicities associated with conventional therapies. Future Perspectives and Limitations of Phytotherapeutic Agents in HNC Treatment

Despite significant advancements in oncology, HNC continues to present formidable therapeutic challenges, with poor survival rates largely attributed to late-stage diagnoses, tumor heterogeneity, and the emergence of therapy resistance. Conventional treatment modalities – including surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy – are often accompanied by severe toxicities that compromise patient quality of life. Additionally, resistance to standard-of-care treatments, particularly in EGFR-driven tumors, has necessitated the development of novel therapeutic strategies aimed at improving efficacy while minimizing adverse effects. Phytotherapeutics have emerged as promising alternatives or adjuncts to conventional treatments due to their ability to target multiple oncogenic pathways involved in tumor proliferation, metastasis, and drug resistance. Several key phytochemicals exhibit potent multi-targeting effects against HNC. Deoxypodophyllotoxin (isolated from Anthriscus sylvestris) inhibits the PI3K/AKT pathway, promoting apoptosis and cell cycle arrest. Similarly, celastrol (derived from Tripterygium wilfordii) suppresses MEK/ERK signaling, induces caspase-dependent apoptosis, and regulates oxidative stress responses, making it a compelling candidate for overcoming chemoresistance. A major hurdle in HNC treatment is EGFR-mediated drug resistance, which is observed in approximately 80–90% of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma cases. EGFR overexpression leads to sustained activation of pro-survival signaling cascades, including PI3K/AKT and MEK/ERK, which drive tumor progression and therapy resistance. Although EGFR inhibitors such as cetuximab, afatinib, and erlotinib have been clinically approved, resistance frequently emerges due to compensatory activation of alternative signaling pathways. In this context, phytochemicals offer a promising avenue for circumventing EGFR-driven resistance through multiple mechanism. Curcumin and resveratrol inhibit EGFR signaling and sensitize tumor cells to radiation and chemotherapy by downregulating receptor expression and blocking downstream effectors. Apigenin and quercetin induce EGFR degradation, promote apoptosis in EGFR-mutant cancer cells, and inhibit epithelial-mesenchymal transition, a key process in metastasis and therapy resistance. The advent of CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing presents groundbreaking opportunities to enhance the efficacy of phytotherapeutic agents. CRISPR-based screens can identify specific genetic vulnerabilities in HNC cells, paving the way for precision-targeted phytochemical interventions. This technology enables gene knockouts of resistance-associated mutations, thereby sensitizing tumors to natural and synthetic compounds, enabling targeted modulation of EGFR pathways, reducing oncogenic signaling, and improving responses to phytochemical-based therapies. Furthermore, nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems are being extensively explored to overcome the limitations of phytochemical bioavailability. Many natural compounds suffer from poor solubility, rapid metabolism, and limited systemic circulation, which hinders their clinical efficacy. Advanced formulation strategies, including polymeric nanoparticles, liposomal encapsulation, and lipid-based carriers, have been developed to enhance the stability, absorption, and targeted delivery of phytochemicals, ensuring sustained therapeutic effects in tumor tissues. Despite these promising developments, several critical challenges remain. Comprehensive toxicity evaluations are needed to ensure the safety of phytochemicals in clinical applications. Bioavailability and pharmacokinetic limitations must be addressed through formulation engineering and structural modification. Clinical validation through rigorous trials is essential to determine the efficacy of phytotherapeutics, both as standalone therapies and in combination with standard-of-care treatments.

Conclusion

Phytotherapeutic agents represent an innovative and biologically diverse class of anticancer compounds with the potential to revolutionize HNC treatment strategies. These natural compounds exert significant anticancer activity by targeting multiple dysregulated pathways, including PI3K/AKT, MEK/ERK, apoptotic regulators, and cell cycle checkpoints. Key phytochemicals, including deoxypodophyllotoxin, celastrol, sulforaphane, and polyphenol-rich extracts, have demonstrated the ability to inhibit proliferation, induce apoptosis, and regulate oxidative stress, highlighting their broad therapeutic potential. However, their clinical translation remains a challenge, necessitating further validation through well-structured clinical trials. Understanding optimal dosing, bioavailability-enhancing formulations, and mechanistic pathways will be crucial for the successful integration of phytotherapy into standard oncology treatments. Future research should bridge the gap between preclinical discoveries and clinical applications, ensuring that plant-derived anticancer agents contribute to next-generation, personalized cancer therapeutics.
