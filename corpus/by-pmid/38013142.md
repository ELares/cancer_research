# Oxidative stress enhances rotavirus oncolysis in breast cancer and leukemia, except in melanoma with abundant matrix.

## Abstract

**OBJECTIVES**: This study aimed to explore the impact of oxidative stress and extracellular matrix integrity on rotavirus infection in various cancer cells, including breast cancer, acute lymphoblastic leukemia, and melanoma.

**METHODS**: We induced oxidative stress using ROS-inducing drugs (cisplatin, metronidazole, melatonin, valproic acid, doxorubicin, losartan, nitrofurantoin, and DHA) and investigated the effects on viral infection in MCF-7, Reh, A375, B16-F1, and SK-MEL-28 cells and the generation of virions from infected cells by harvesting the supernatants every two hours, reinfecting other cells, and analyzing cell viability and DNA fragmentation.

**FINDINGS**: In MCF-7 and Reh cells, rotavirus Wt1-5 infection led to increased ROS generation, virion production, membrane permeability, mitochondrial dysfunction, DNA damage, and cell death. These effects were amplified by ROS-inducing drugs. Conversely, melanoma cells (SK-MEL-28 and A375) with a robust extracellular matrix network showed limited sensitivity to the drugs. Notably, losartan, which modulates the extracellular matrix, enhanced viral infection in melanoma cells (99 %).

**CONCLUSIONS**: Oxidative stress promotes oncolytic rotavirus infection in breast cancer and acute lymphoblastic leukemia cells, suggesting potential utility in combination with radiotherapy or chemotherapy due to their shared induction of intracellular oxidative stress.

Introduction

Oncolytic virotherapy is a promising cancer treatment strategy that utilizes both naturally occurring and genetically engineered viruses to target and replicate within cancer cells. However, its efficacy is intricately linked to the tumor microenvironment, known as the peritumoral stroma, which includes various cell types and the extracellular matrix (ECM).

The ECM within solid tumors presents significant challenges to oncolytic viruses (OVs), including limited blood supply, acidic conditions, hypoxia, and elevated proteolytic activity. These barriers hinder efficient OV spread and replication. Moreover, tumor size and location further complicate OV delivery. Although some OVs can be directly injected into accessible tumors, this approach is not universally applicable. The ECM also supports cancer cell survival and regrowth post-treatment, affecting how cells respond to therapy. ECM modification holds promise for enhancing OV effectiveness.

Future advancements in OV therapy will likely involve combining OVs with adjuvant treatments that create a more favorable viral infection environment. Strategies like using enzymes to break down ECM barriers and altering the tumor microenvironment with radiotherapy and chemotherapy can enhance OV efficacy.

The role of the tumor microenvironment in tumor progression is gaining importance in cancer research, with the potential to reshape anti-cancer treatments, including OVs. Understanding the involvement of oxidative stress in cancer development and its impact on viral infection is crucial. Oxidative stress, characterized by an imbalance between reactive oxygen species (ROS) and antioxidants, plays a multifaceted role in disease development. ROS have physiological roles at lower concentrations, but at higher levels, they can damage cellular components.

ROS levels influence the activity of NF-κB, a key player in cancer development, which regulates genes involved in cell growth, apoptosis, invasion, and metastasis. Some viruses, like rotavirus, stimulate NF-κB activity during infection, altering the cellular redox balance and contributing to disease progression.

Understanding the interplay between oxidative stress and viral infection is vital for enhancing oncolytic virotherapy. Investigating how stimulating ROS generation affects rotavirus infection could reveal opportunities to boost the number of virions and the percentage of infected cells, making oncolytic rotavirus a potential complement to established cancer treatments like radiotherapy and chemotherapy.

This study aims to investigate how metabolic changes induced by oxidative stress or alterations in the ECM affect the susceptibility to rotavirus infection of acute lymphocytic leukemia cells, breast cancer cells, and melanoma cells. A deeper understanding of these mechanisms will significantly contribute to our comprehension of the oncolytic potential of rotavirus and its role in the fight against cancer.

Methodology

Cell lines and culture

We used various cell lines, including MCF-7, Reh, A549, B16-F1, A375, SK-MEL-28, Vero, and CHO. These cells were cultured in Dulbecco Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) with 10 % fetal bovine serum, streptomycin, and penicillin in a 5 % CO2 atmosphere at 37 °C.

Rotavirus strains

We obtained rotavirus strains Wt1–5, WTEW, and ECwt-o independently as previously described.

Drugs and treatments

Various drugs, including cisplatin, doxorubicin, hydrogen peroxide, valproic acid, DHA, melatonin, metronidazole, nitrofurantoin, and losartan, were used. These drugs were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide. Cells were seeded, infected with rotaviruses, and treated with drugs. Controls included untreated infected cells and uninfected cells.

Evaluation of mitochondrial membrane potential and cell viability

Cells were treated with specific drugs, and cell viability was assessed using 3-(4,5-dimethylthiazolyl)−2,5-diphenyl tetrazolium bromide. The integrity of cytoplasmic and mitochondrial membranes was evaluated using DiOC6 and 7-AAD. The degree of mitochondrial oxide-reduction activity was measured using resazurin under specific conditions. Control cells were included for comparison.

ROS generation test

We induced reactive oxygen species (ROS) in MCF-7 and Reh cell cultures using ROS-generating drugs at specific concentrations, chosen based on established practices in cell culture studies, to ensure increased infection, elevated ROS levels, and minimal toxicity.

We used the following drugs and concentrations: cisplatin (20 µM), doxorubicin (50 nM), hydrogen peroxide (12.5 µM), valproic acid (10 mM), DHA (50 µM), losartan (100 µM), melatonin (100 nM), metronidazole (1.5 mM), and nitrofurantoin (200 nM). Untreated infected and uninfected cells served as controls.

For the assessment of ROS and Wt1–5 rotavirus infection modifications, approximately 200,000 Reh and MCF-7 cells were seeded on coverslips in 48-well culture plates and infected with the Wt1–5 rotavirus at a MOI of 0.4. After 14 h.p.i., Dihydroethidium was added, and the cells were incubated and stained with DAPI. Fixed cells were analyzed using fluorescence microscopy to measure corrected total cell fluorescence (CTCF) from ten representative photographs for each treatment.

To evaluate markers associated with cell death, cells were fixed, permeabilized, and incubated with specific primary antibodies against relevant markers. After incubation with secondary antibodies, cells were stained with DAPI, and fluorescence was analyzed using microscopy to calculate CTCF from ten representative photographs of each sample.

DNA integrity was assessed using the TUNEL technique, with cells being analyzed for nuclear DNA fragmentation associated with programmed cell death. Controls included uninfected cells and cells treated with H2O2. Positive fluorescence signals were quantified from ten representative photographs for each coverslip to calculate CTCF.

Dose-Dependent analysis of drug effects on infected cells

To investigate the impact of various drugs on Reh-infected cells and MCF-7 cells inoculated with rotavirus Wt1–5 (MOI 0.4, at 37 °C for one hour), three different concentrations of each drug were employed: cisplatin (40, 20, 10 µM), doxorubicin (200, 100, 50 nM), H2O2 (50, 25, 12.5 µM), valproic acid (10, 5, 2.5 mM), DHA (100, 75, 50 µM), melatonin (100, 50, 25 nM), metronidazole (1500, 750, 375 µM), losartan (100, 50, 25 µM), and nitrofurantoin (200, 100, 50 nM). Following drug exposure, the cells were fixed with 4 % formaldehyde, washed, and analyzed by immunocytochemistry to assess the degree of infection. Control groups included uninfected untreated cells and infected untreated cells.

Drug exposure and rotavirus infection time course

A subset of drugs, chosen based on the previous analysis (20 µM cisplatin, 50 nM doxorubicin, 12.5 µM hydrogen peroxide, 10 mM valproic acid, 50 µM DHA, 100 µM losartan, 100 nM melatonin, 1500 µM metronidazole, and 200 nM nitrofurantoin), were administered to Reh and MCF-7 cells, which were subsequently infected with rotavirus Wt1–5 (MOI 0.4). The cells were harvested at intervals from 0 to 12 h post-treatment (h.p.t.), and both supernatant and cell pellet were collected. The supernatant was added back to new Reh or MCF-7 cell cultures, and these newly infected cells were harvested after 14 h.p.i., with viral antigens evaluated using capture ELISA.

Flow cytometry for cell surface protein expression

The expression of Hsp40, HSP60, HSP70, HSP90, Hsc70, integrin β3, and PDI on cell surfaces was assessed by flow cytometry. Cells were fixed with 4 % paraformaldehyde, treated with ammonium chloride and glycine, and then incubated with primary antibodies against the relevant surface proteins. After washes, secondary FITC-conjugated antibodies were added. Flow cytometry was conducted using a FACS Canto II™ flow cytometer, and the analysis was performed with FlowJo vX.0.7 or BD FACSDiva software. A non-related isotype antibody was used as a control for background staining.

Rotavirus infection in melanoma cells

Preliminary tests were conducted on A375 and B16-F1s melanoma cells using different rotaviruses. Wt1–5 did not infect the melanoma cells, but the WTEW and Ecwt-o rotaviruses were found to have a low infection rate, and thus, these were used in subsequent experiments. Various conditions, including exposure to proteases, Mn2+, and drugs, were tested to study the effect of the extracellular matrix on rotavirus infection in melanoma cell lines.

Immunohistochemistry

For immunohistochemistry, Reh cells grown in suspension were fixed, and a primary rabbit polyclonal antibody was used to target rotavirus proteins. Secondary antibodies were applied, and cells were analyzed through light microscopy. A similar procedure was applied to attached MCF-7 cells, and the number of positively stained cells was counted using ImageJ software.

ELISA capture assays

Reh and MCF-7 cells, both infected and uninfected with the Wt1–5 rotavirus, were harvested and lysed. An ELISA plate was prepared with an anti-rotavirus capture antibody, and cell lysates were added. The plate was washed, and secondary antibodies were applied. The substrate for detection was o-Phenylenediamine dihydrochloride (OPD), and the absorbance was measured at 492 nm. A negative control consisted of cell lysate without rotavirus.

Adhesion of rotavirus to peri‑cellular matrix network

A375, SK-MEL, and A549 cells were cultured and infected with the WTEW rotavirus. The cells were fixed and treated with primary antibodies against rotavirus and type I collagen. Subsequently, secondary antibodies and DAPI were applied, and the cells were visualized by epifluorescence microscopy using a 40X objective.

Statistical analysis

The results are presented as mean ± standard error (SE), with ‘n’ indicating the number of experiments, typically performed in triplicate and repeated. The vertical bars in the graphs represent the SE. Differences between means were assessed using the student's t-test for paired or unpaired observations, as appropriate. When comparing more than two groups, we utilized one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). Significance was considered at P<0.05.

Results

We performed initial assessments using literature data to select drug concentrations that boosted viral infection and raised ROS levels, while avoiding excessive toxicity. IC50 values were not considered for certain drugs (valproic acid, DHA, losartan, melatonin, nitrofurantoin) due to diminishing ROS response at higher concentrations. Our emphasis was on identifying the lowest concentrations meeting both criteria.

We employed the MTT assay to assess the toxicity of various substances on uninfected Reh and MCF-7 cells. Cells were exposed to cisplatin (20 µM), doxorubicin (50 nM), hydrogen peroxide (12.5 µM), valproic acid (10 mM), DHA (50 µM), losartan (100 µM), melatonin (100 nM), metronidazole (1500 µM), and nitrofurantoin (200 nM) for 14 h at 37 °C before conducting the MTT assay. The results revealed substantial differences in cell viability reduction, depending on the specific drug and cell type: cisplatin (20 %, 22 %), doxorubicin (60 %, 63 %), hydrogen peroxide (65 %, 50 %), valproic acid (15 %, 48 %), DHA (15 %, 50 %), losartan (17 %, 23 %), melatonin (8 %, 5 %), metronidazole (45 %, 6 %), and nitrofurantoin (11 %, 40 %) for MCF-7 and Reh cells, respectively (Fig. 1a and b).

In Reh and MCF-7 cell lines infected with rotavirus Wt1–5, there was an increased expression of ROS. These cell lines were infected with Wt1–5 (MOI 0.4) and subjected to various drug treatments (cisplatin, doxorubicin, H2O2, valproic acid, DHA, losartan, melatonin, metronidazole, and nitrofurantoin) at concentrations determined for cell viability using MTT.

ROS levels, assessed with dihydroethidium (DHE) and measured as corrected total cell fluorescence (CTCF), increased notably following treatment with doxorubicin or H2O2 in both cancer cell lines (Fig. 1c and d). Metronidazole and nitrofurantoin also raised CTCF levels in Reh cells (Fig. 1d).

Representative fluorescence images of infected MCF-7 cells subjected to different treatments are illustrated (Fig. 1e). In cells infected with Wt1–5, a significant rise in CTCF was observed compared to uninfected and untreated cells, indicating that the infectious process augments ROS generation (Fig. 1f–i). Notably, CTCF values were higher when cells were infected first and then treated with drugs, suggesting that Wt1–5 infection alone induces ROS generation, which is further enhanced by the drugs (Fig. 1f–i).

Conversely, when cells were initially treated with the drug, they activated compensatory biochemical mechanisms, impacting ROS generation. It appears that the rotavirus activates processes that stimulate ROS production and hinders the cell's ability to restore its oxidative balance. MCF-7 cells exhibited higher susceptibility in CTCF generation compared to Reh cells, implying a greater susceptibility to infection and drug treatment.

Infecting MCF-7 and Reh cells with Wt1–5 and subsequent treatment with ROS-inducing drugs significantly increased infection rates

We employed three different drug concentrations to gage infection rates in both cell lines after infecting them with rotavirus Wt1–5 (MOI 0.4). Without drug treatment, the initial infection led to approximately 30 % of MCF-7 cells and 25 % of Reh cells exhibiting positive viral antigen expression (Fig. 2a and b).

Moreover, when cells were initially infected and then treated with varying drug concentrations, a noteworthy escalation in the percentage of infected cells was observed in both cell lines (Fig. 2a and b). Interestingly, cisplatin yielded the lowest infection rates in both tumor cell lines, closely followed by valproic acid, while doxorubicin and H2O2 produced the highest infection rates (Fig. 2a and b). Representative immunocytochemistry images are provided (Fig. 2c). There was no linear correlation between drug concentration and the infection percentage, suggesting that the specific drug concentration utilized in this study does not predominantly dictate intracellular ROS generation. Instead, ROS production seems to be contingent on cellular mechanisms triggered by the drug, rather than the drug concentration itself. The interplay between drug concentration and the viral impact on the cell establishes an environment conducive to enhancing infection rates.

ROS-generating drugs increased production of virions in MCF-7 and Reh tumor cells

ROS-generating drugs were found to enhance the production of virions in both MCF-7 and Reh tumor cells. The experimental procedure involved initially infecting MCF-7 and Reh cells with the Wt1–5 rotavirus (MOI 0.4), followed by drug treatment. Samples were collected at 2-hour intervals from 0 to 12 h post-treatment (h.p.t.). Supernatants were separated from cell pellets and replaced with an equivalent volume of culture medium. Subsequently, the supernatants or cell pellets (lysed by freezing and thawing) were used to infect new cultures of MCF-7 and Reh cells. The evaluation of viral antigens in these newly infected cells was conducted after 14 h post-infection (h.p.i.), employing the capture ELISA technique.

The results indicated a substantial increase in capture ELISA absorbance in cells exposed to the drugs when compared to untreated cells (Fig. 3a–d). This increase commenced at 6 h.p.i., became more pronounced at 8 h.p.i., and reached its peak at 12 h.p.i. Notably, cells treated with drugs consistently exhibited higher values than untreated cells. Among the drugs tested, losartan and valproic acid showed the smallest increase, while doxorubicin exhibited the most significant enhancement in virion production (Fig. 3a–d).

Signals associated with cell death increased in Reh and MCF-7 tumor cells treated with ROS inducing drugs

MCF-7 and Reh cells were infected with the Wt1–5 rotavirus (MOI 0.4) and then treated with drugs. The cells were incubated for 14 h at 37 °C. When uninfected MCF-7 and Reh cells were not exposed to drugs, their viability was approximately 93.4 % and 89.7 %, respectively (Fig. 4a, b). However, in infected MCF-7 cells left untreated with drugs, viability decreased by 83.3 %, while in Reh cells, it decreased by 36.1 %. Among uninfected cells exposed to drugs, most exhibited permeability for DIOC6, ranging from approximately 51.7 % (DOXO) to 93.2 % (CIS) in MCF-7 cells and about 2.44 % (DOXO) to 61.5 % (LOS) in Reh cells (Fig. 4b).

When cells were both infected and treated with drugs, the percentage of DIOC6 positive cells decreased, and permeability for 7AAD increased. In MCF-7 cells, roughly 28.6 % were positive for DIOC6 and 70.8 % for 7AAD with CIS treatment, and 47.9 % were positive for DIOC6 and 46.2 % for 7AAD with DOXO treatment (Fig. 4a). In comparison, Reh cells exhibited 0.01 % positivity for DIOC6 and 82.9 % for 7AAD with DOXO treatment, and 18.2  % positivity for DIOC6 and 65.9 % for 7AAD with LOS treatment (Fig. 4b). These results indicate an increase in the number of cells displaying signs of death associated with changes in the permeability of both mitochondrial (DIOC6) and cytoplasmic (7AAD) membranes.

An immunofluorescence assay was conducted to assess various cell death markers, including BCL-2, BAX, BID, SMAC, cytochrome C, caspase 9, caspase 7, caspase 3, COX 4, pNF-kB, and cleaved PARP. Representative figures are depicted (Fig. 4c and d). In MCF-7 cells, a high ratio of Corrected Total Cell Fluorescence (CTCF) was observed between infected cells treated with drugs and non-infected cells, except for c-PARP, pNFkB, and BCL-2. Notably, doxorubicin exhibited a high ratio for these proteins (Fig. 4e). A similar trend was observed in REH cells, albeit with minor variations (Fig. 4f). Images were captured using a fluorescence microscope (VanGuard) with a 100X objective, and ImageJ 1.44p Java 1.6.0_20 (32-bit) software was employed to calculate CTCF values. Statistically significant differences are indicated by asterisks (***P < 0.001; **P < 0.01; *P < 0.05, n = 3).

The resazurin technique was employed to further evaluate indicators of cell death. Cells exposed to drugs in the absence of the virus exhibited a relative decrease in mitochondrial oxide-reduction activity, with values of 25±1 % for MCF-7 and 30 %±1 % for Reh cells (n = 3). In contrast, cells that were infected and subsequently treated with drugs displayed a more substantial reduction in mitochondrial oxide-reduction, resulting in decreased cell survival, with rates of 62 %±3 % for MCF-7 and 60 %±3 % for Reh. We found that in MCF-7 and Reh the ratio between infected cells treated with drugs / non-infected cells was low in comparison to cells that were not infected and did not receive drugs. Notably, the lowest ratios were observed with doxorubicin, DHA, and melatonin, suggesting that these drugs enhance the reduction in cell viability (Fig. 5a). The in-situ cell death method (TUNEL, Invitrogen) was employed to assess DNA fragmentation in MCF-7 and Reh cell lines infected with Wt1–5 rotavirus and treated with the aforementioned drugs. In general, the infection of MCF-7 and Reh cells with Wt1–5 rotavirus led to an increase in Corrected Total Cell Fluorescence (CTCF) fluorescence intensity within the cell, indicating a higher dUTP addition compared to uninfected tumor cells that remained untreated with drugs (Fig. 5b and c).

Interestingly, when comparing the ratio between infected cells treated with drugs and uninfected cells without drug treatment in MCF-7 and Reh cells, no significant differences were observed, except in the case of Reh cells exposed to doxorubicin and losartan. This suggests that the primary driver of DNA fragmentation is the infectious process itself rather than the influence of the drugs, as depicted in Fig. 5b. Representative figures of the fluorescence of each cell exposed to the analyzed drugs are presented (Fig. 5c).

ROS-generating drugs do not have the same effect on melanoma lines infected by oncolytic rotavirus

In order to investigate whether melanoma cell lines A375 (human) and B16 (mouse) exhibited similar responses to ROS-generating drugs as MCF-7 and Reh tumor lines, we initially examined the presence of various proteins involved in the infection process (Hsp90, Hsp70, Hsp60, Hsp40, Hsc70, PDI, integrin β3) in these alternative tumor lines. Our findings indicated that both cell lines expressed these proteins in their cytoplasmic membrane, although B16 exhibited lower levels of Hsc70, and both cell lines showed reduced expression of β3 integrin (Fig. 6a). However, when we exposed them to WTEW or ECwt-o rotavirus at different multiplicities of infection (MOIs: 10, 5, 2.5, or 1.25), we observed either no infection or suboptimal infection rates, with the highest reaching 15  % (Fig. 6b).

Manganese exposure induced a comprehensive structural change in an engineered αVβ3 ectodomain, transitioning it from a folded conformation to an extended one. β3 integrin is a key player in the rotavirus entry process. To assess whether Mn2+ enhanced infection in B16 F1 cells, we pre-treated the cells with Mn2+ (1 mM) for 30 min before subjecting them to viral infection. Surprisingly, the infectivity did not increase, and the maximum observed infectivity (MOI 10) remained around 15  % (Fig. 6c). Importantly, Mn2+ treatments did not affect cell viability (not shown). For control, we used Reh and MCF-7 cells, which are known to be susceptible to infection, as well as less susceptible cell lines such as non-tumorous Vero (green monkey kidney) and CHO (Chinese hamster ovary) cells.

To explore whether the removal of extracellular collagen from melanoma cells would impact infection rates, we exposed the cells to pronase/collagenase or a dithiothreitol and paraformaldehyde solution. However, our investigations did not reveal any increase in infectivity in A375 cells at any of the MOIs used or the different treatment durations (5 min and 2.5 min) (data not shown). In contrast, pre-treatment of B16F1 cells with collagenase for 5 min before viral inoculation led to an increased infection rate of up to 35 % for WTEW and 30 % for ECwt-o (MOIs 10 and 5, respectively). A 2.5-minute pre-treatment induced infection rates of approximately 20 % for the WTEW variant (MOIs 10 and 5) and 30 % for ECwt-o (Fig. 6d). Notably, there was no observed increase in infection rates in either cell line after a 30-minute exposure to paraformaldehyde/DTT (Fig. 6b and d, respectively). Furthermore, our tests confirmed that the cells remained viable after collagenase and paraformaldehyde/DTT treatments, with viability percentages exceeding 95 % (data not shown).

Extracellular matrix of melanoma cells traps rotavirus, limits its entrance and internalization

Based on the earlier findings, we hypothesized that the dense extracellular matrix in melanoma cell lines could hinder rotavirus entry and internalization. We tested the susceptibility of A375 and B16 melanoma cells to WTEW rotavirus infection under modified extracellular matrix conditions and ROS-generating drug treatments.

In A375 cells, adherent cells showed minimal viral antigen presence (0.1 % at 72 h and 0.5 % at 48 h). In contrast, cells adhered for 2 h prior to infection displayed a higher infection rate of 42 ± 4 %. Non-adherent A375 cells infected with rotavirus exhibited a substantial 70 ± 7 % infection rate. Furthermore, non-adherent A375 cells infected with the virus and treated with ROS-generating drugs showed infection rates similar to untreated non-adherent cells (valproic acid 63 %, cisplatin 80 %, doxorubicin 92 %, melatonin 60 %, metronidazole 85 %) (Fig. 6e). B16 F1 melanoma cells exhibited a similar pattern (Fig. 6f), with high infection rates in non-adherent cells infected with Wt1–5 rotavirus (Figs. 6e, f). These results suggested that A375 and B16 F1 melanoma cell lines were susceptible to infection, but the amount of extracellular and peri‑cellular matrix traps the rotavirus, preventing it from reaching cell receptors and infecting.

We altered the extracellular matrix in A375 cells using losartan and found that infectivity rates increased significantly (losartan 99 %, 98 %, 80 %, and 80 %, respectively). Other drug treatments in adherent A375 cells showed lower infection rates (cisplatin: 1 %, doxorubicin: 5 %, melatonin: 20 %). SK-MEL melanoma cells without drug treatment displayed a 5 % infection rate, while A549 lung cancer cells, with lower extracellular matrix, exhibited an 80 % infection rate (Fig. 7a). We show representative images of each of the experimental groups (Fig. 7b).

Immunofluorescence analysis revealed that in A375 and SK MEL-28 cells, the virus was trapped near the cells, coinciding with the distribution of type I collagen. In contrast, with A549 cells and lower pericellular matrix, the virus was observed near the cell membrane (Fig. 8a).

Discussion

In this study, we utilized pharmacological concentrations of drugs, chosen based on cell culture guidelines. Preliminary tests aimed to identify the optimal drug concentration, balancing the need to induce ROS, enhance rotavirus infection, and minimize toxicity. Our findings demonstrate that these drugs increased ROS levels in uninfected cells, although to a lesser extent than in rotavirus-infected, untreated cells. Notably, the highest fluorescence levels were observed when cells were infected before drug treatment, supporting the notion that rotavirus infection induces ROS, which is potentiated by these drugs. Interestingly, when the drug was applied first, it appears to trigger cellular mechanisms that counteract its effects, suggesting that rotavirus inhibits the cell's ability to control ROS, similar to that reported for the influenza virus.

Variations in ROS levels may be attributed to the unique ROS generation mechanisms of each drug. For example, doxorubicin, a chemotherapeutic agent, induces oxidative stress, inflammation, apoptosis, nucleic acid inhibition, protein synthesis disruption, and mitochondrial abnormalities, resulting in a substantial decrease in cell viability. In contrast, cisplatin, another chemotherapeutic agent known for generating DNA adducts, did not significantly increase ROS levels. These findings suggest that the high cytotoxicity and multiple mechanisms of action of doxorubicin result in greater ROS production compared to other drugs like cisplatin.

Upon infecting MCF-7 and Reh cancer cells (MOI 0.4) without drug treatment, viral antigen-positive cell percentages were 30 % and 25 %, respectively. However, when infected cells were subsequently treated with various drugs, there was a significant increase in infected cell percentages in both cell lines. Notably, there was no direct relationship between drug concentration and infection percentage, implying that ROS generation is determined by the drug's biochemical mechanisms within the cell rather than its concentration as a ROS generator. This suggests that the virus may exploit this effect to enhance its viral antigens and facilitate infection in these cancer cells.

We observed a rise in viral antigens and infectious virion production from 0 to 12 h post-infection in cells treated with drugs, with a more pronounced effect from 6 h post-infection. Doxorubicin, DHA, metronidazole, melatonin, and nitrofurantoin treatments resulted in greater increases in infectious viral particle production, whereas cisplatin, valproic acid, and losartan yielded smaller increases. These outcomes are linked to the distinct ROS generation mechanisms of each drug. For instance, valproic acid, an anticonvulsant, induces histone hyperacetylation and uncouples cytochrome P450, leading to hydrogen peroxide generation, which, in turn, produces hydroxyl radicals. However, the indirect nature of this mechanism may explain its limited ROS generation capacity in MCF-7 and Reh cells infected with Wt1–5 rotavirus.

Cancer cells inherently exhibit higher ROS levels due to their increased metabolism and rapid proliferation. Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), an omega-3 fatty acid, integrates into the mitochondrial membranes of cancer cells, affecting their permeability, reducing membrane potential, and triggering the release of cytochrome C, activating apoptosis. Our findings show that DHA substantially elevates ROS in rotavirus-infected MCF-7 and Reh cells, indicating it generates ROS in these tumor cells.

Melatonin also stimulates ROS production in both tumor and non-tumor cells. It can generate ROS through interactions with calmodulin, mitochondrial complex III, or the mitochondrial permeability transition pore. Additionally, cytochrome P450 utilizes melatonin as a substrate to produce ROS in mitochondria, activating copper-zinc superoxide dismutase.

Metronidazole (MTZ) induces ROS production when nitroreductases (NTR) are present, which can generate superoxide and related reactive oxygen species in aerobic conditions. Nitrofurantoin, a common antibiotic for urinary tract infections, is known to cause pulmonary and liver toxicity in humans due to the generation of reactive oxygen intermediates (ROI) during its redox cycle. The indirect mechanisms employed by most of these drugs may contribute to their limited ability to generate ROS in MCF-7 and Reh cells infected with rotavirus Wt1–5.

MCF-7 and Reh cells, when infected and treated with drugs, exhibited markers associated with cell death. DIOC6-positive cells decreased, while 7AAD permeability increased, indicating that viral infection amplifies cell death linked to mitochondrial (DIOC6) and cytoplasmic (7AAD) membrane permeability.

An immunofluorescence assay examined cell death markers, including BCL-2, BAX, BID, SMAC, cytochrome C, caspase 9, caspase 7, caspase 3, COX 4, PNF-kB, and cleaved PARP. In infected cells treated with drugs compared to non-infected cells, the corrected total cell fluorescence (CTCF) ratio was generally high in MCF-7 and REH, except for c-PARP, pNFkB, and BCL-2. This suggests that viral infection intensifies cell death associated with ROS-inducing drugs.

With the resazurin technique in MCF-7 and Reh, the ratio between infected cells treated with drugs and non-infected cells was consistently low for all treatments involving ROS-inducing drugs, particularly doxorubicin, DHA, and melatonin. This indicates that ROS-inducing drugs enhance cell death in the context of viral infection.

Uninfected cells treated with drugs and untreated infected cells showed results similar to the control group (uninfected and without drugs). This is because the cells were infected with a low MOI of 0.4, representing an average of the cell population, including both infected and uninfected cells.

Among the markers, phosphorylated NF-KB protein displayed higher fluorescence in infected cells treated with drugs. Notably, there were significant differences in cells infected and then treated with drugs compared to those infected without drug treatment, supporting the idea that drugs enhance rotavirus-induced ROS and the expression of cell death markers.

During the early stage of infection (2 to 6 h after virus entry), the NSP1 protein activates survival pathways like PI3K and NFkB, preventing premature host cell death. However, during the late phase of rotavirus infection, the NSP4 protein is associated with cell death through Drp1-dependent mitochondrial fragmentation, resulting in altered mitochondrial syncytia formation due to increased fission and decreased fusion during infection. When DNA fragmentation was assessed using the TUNEL method, infected cells compared to untreated uninfected cells exhibited significantly higher ratios, indicating that Wt1–5 rotavirus infection in MCF-7 and Reh cells led to increased fluorescence intensity through greater dUTP incorporation. However, when comparing the ratios of infected cells treated with drugs to uninfected cells, there were minimal differences, except for Reh cells treated with doxorubicin and losartan. This implies that the primary effect is attributed to the infection process rather than the drugs.

We aimed to determine whether ROS-generating drugs would enhance infection in melanoma tumor lines infected with oncolytic rotavirus, focusing on human A375 and murine B16 F1 melanoma cell lines. While these cell lines expressed the necessary molecules for rotavirus entry, the infection percentage was initially low. Interestingly, we observed that infection percentages were higher when the cells were in suspension compared to when they were adhered. Importantly, the addition of ROS-generating drugs did not significantly change these infection percentages, indicating that ROS generation by the drugs is not the primary factor influencing infection in the melanoma cell line.

The low infection rate in melanoma cells can be attributed to the presence of extracellular and pericellular matrix that traps the rotavirus, preventing it from reaching cell receptors and infecting the cells. We confirmed this when altering extracellular matrix production in the A375 melanoma line using losartan, which resulted in a remarkable increase in rotavirus infectivity to 95 %. Losartan, an angiotensin II receptor antagonist and an inhibitor of TGF-1, affects collagen synthesis, thereby modifying the extracellular matrix composition in cells.

The presence of virus around the cell coincides with type I collagen in melanoma cells like A375 and SK-MEL, suggesting that the limitation of infection is partially due to the abundant extracellular matrix in these cell lines. The action of losartan, in synergy with TGF-beta, influences fibrosis through the angiotensin receptor type 1 (AT1) in various tissues beyond the cardiovascular and renal systems. Therefore, losartan can disrupt the dense collagen network characteristic of melanoma lines such as A375 and SK-MEL. Whether losartan synergizes with rotavirus, potentially leading to a slight increase in ROS levels as seen with other drugs, remains an open question.

In summary, our findings indicate that oxidative stress enhances oncolytic rotavirus WT1–5 infection in breast cancer and leukemia cells. Infection triggers ROS production, which is further amplified by specific drugs. This rise in ROS is associated with increased virus production and cell death from 4 to 6 h post-infection until 12 h post-infection, a novel observation for oncolytic rotavirus.

Moreover, the drug losartan modifies the extracellular matrix, facilitating infection in tumor lines with abundant extracellular matrix like A375 and SK-MEL human melanoma cells. This suggests that combining WT1–5 rotavirus with radiotherapy or chemotherapy, both of which induce intracellular oxidative stress, may prove beneficial.

Future in vivo studies will be necessary to confirm whether administering ROS-inducing drugs enhances the infectivity of oncolytic rotavirus in tumors. We recommend further research involving drug combinations that do not alter the rotavirus structure but influence the extracellular matrix or induce ROS, particularly when applied intratumorally.
