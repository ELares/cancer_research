# Targeting HVEM-GPT2 axis: a novel approach to T cell activation and metabolic reprogramming in non-small cell lung cancer therapy.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: The modulation of tumor microenvironments through immune checkpoint pathways is pivotal for the development of effective cancer immunotherapies. This study aims to explore the role of HVEM in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) microenvironment.

**METHODS**: The lung cancer datasets for this study were directly downloaded from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA). Single-cell data were sourced from the Tumor Immune Single-cell Hub (TISCH). Multiplex immunohistochemistry (mIHC) was used to explore the cellular composition and spatial distribution of HVEM in lung cancer immune microenvironment. The immune microenvironment of HVEM KO mice bearing mouse lung cancer cell was also evaluated. Co-cultured system and phenotype assays facilitated the examination of Jurkat T cells' effect on A549 and H1299 lung cancer cells. Quantitative PCR and Western blotting determined gene and protein expression, respectively, cellular respiration was measured through oxygen consumption rate (OCR) assays. Lung cancer cells co-cultured with Jurkat T cells were xenografted into nude mice to evaluate tumor growth and metastatic potential. Next, RNA-seq, COIP, Dual-luciferase reporter experiment, and CHIP-seq were used to explore the potential underlying mechanism.

**RESULTS**: In our study, we investigated the role of HVEM in the microenvironment of NSCLC and its implications in immunotherapy. Crucially, HVEM, part of the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily, influences T cell activation, potentially impacting immunotherapeutic outcomes. Using the TIDE algorithm, our results showcased a link between HVEM levels and immune dysfunction in NSCLC patients. Delving deeper into the NSCLC microenvironment, we found HVEM predominantly expressed in T cell subpopulations. CD8 + HVEM + and CD4 + HVEM + indicated better prognosis in lung adenocarcinoma tissue microarray using multiplex immunohistochemistry. Activated T cells, particularly from the Jurkat cell line, significantly inhibited NSCLC progression, reducing both proliferation and invasion capabilities of A549 and H1299 lung cancer cell lines. In vivo models reinforced these observations. Manipulating HVEM expression revealed its essential role in T cell survival and activation. In addition, animal experiments revealed the importance of HVEM in maintaining activated peripheral immunity and inflamed local tumor microenvironment. Furthermore, our data suggest that HVEM is pivotal in T cell metabolic reprogramming, transitioning from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis. RNA sequencing illuminated a potential relationship between HVEM and GPT2, an enzyme tied to amino acid metabolism and cellular energetics. Subsequent experiments confirmed that HVEM's influence on T cell activation and metabolism is potentially mediated through its regulation of GPT2. In addition, GATA1 was validated to regulate HVEM expression in activated Jurkat T cells.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our study establishes that HVEM significantly influences T cell functionality and NSCLC cell dynamics, pinpointing the HVEM-GPT2 axis as a promising target for NSCLC therapy.

In our study, we investigated the role of HVEM in the microenvironment of NSCLC and its implications in immunotherapy. Crucially, HVEM, part of the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily, influences T cell activation, potentially impacting immunotherapeutic outcomes. Using the TIDE algorithm, our results showcased a link between HVEM levels and immune dysfunction in NSCLC patients. In addition, animal experiments revealed the importance of HVEM in maintaining activated peripheral immunity and inflamed local tumor microenvironment. RNA sequencing illuminated a potential relationship between HVEM and GPT2, an enzyme tied to amino acid metabolism and cellular energetics. Subsequent experiments confirmed that HVEM’s influence on T cell activation and metabolism is potentially mediated through its regulation of GPT2. In addition, GATA1 was validated to regulate HVEM expression in activated Jurkat T cells.

Conclusions

Our study establishes that HVEM significantly influences T cell functionality and NSCLC cell dynamics, pinpointing the HVEM-GPT2 axis as a promising target for NSCLC therapy.

Introduction

Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) represents the most prevalent subtype of lung cancer, accounting for approximately 85% of all lung cancer cases. It encompasses several histological subtypes, including adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. The etiology of NSCLC is multifactorial, with smoking being the primary risk factor. Other risk factors include environmental exposures (such as asbestos and radon), familial history, and specific genetic mutations. Symptoms of NSCLC can include persistent cough, chest pain, shortness of breath, and unexplained weight loss. While the 5-year survival rate is relatively high with early detection and treatment, many patients are diagnosed at advanced stages due to the often subtle onset of symptoms, making treatment more challenging and reducing survival outcomes. Current therapeutic strategies encompass surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, targeted therapies, and the increasingly prominent immunotherapy.

Immunotherapy has progressively transformed the treatment landscape for NSCLC, offering renewed hope for many patients previously deemed challenging to treat. This approach primarily involves activating and enhancing the patient’s own immune system, especially by using immune checkpoint inhibitors, such as PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors, to combat tumor cells. Over the past few years, multiple clinical trials have demonstrated the efficacy of these inhibitors in improving overall survival rates and progression-free survival times for NSCLC patients. Compared to traditional chemotherapy and radiotherapy, immunotherapy often comes with fewer side effects and improved quality of life. However, while many NSCLC patients respond favorably to immunotherapy, a subset remains unresponsive. Currently, researchers are actively exploring biomarkers associated with immune responses to more accurately predict which patients are most likely to benefit from immunotherapy. Simultaneously, new immunotherapeutic methods and strategies are under investigation to enhance efficacy and expand the beneficiary population.

HVEM (HVEM) is not only a crucial member of the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily but also plays a pivotal role in regulating immune responses. Its interactions with various ligands, such as LIGHT, BTLA, and CD160, place it centrally within the immune regulatory network. Especially in the regulation of T cells, the intricate signaling pathways of HVEM endow it with a dual character: On one hand, it can promote the activation and proliferation of T cells, thus enhancing immune responses against exogenous and endogenous pathogens; on the other, by binding with inhibitory ligands like BTLA and CD160, it can maintain immune homeostasis, preventing excessive immune reactions that lead to autoimmune diseases. These researches showed that the functional impact of HVEM binding to specific ligands in different settings has been complicated by the multiple interactions of HVEM and HVEM binding partners. The herpes virus entry mediator (HVEM) is a co-stimulatory molecule with a novel downstream signaling pathway. Zhang showed that CAR T cells with a HVEM CSD (HVEM-CAR T) displayed more robust cytokine release and cytotoxicity than 4-1BB-CAR T or CD28-CAR T in vitro. Furthermore, HVEM-CAR T showed superior therapeutic efficacy in several mouse tumor models. Mechanistically, the HVEM CSD endowed CAR T cells with attenuated exhaustion, improved function and persistence, and enhanced metabolic activities in tumor tissue compared with 4-1BB-based or CD28-based CAR T cells. These studies establish that the HVEM has the potential to improve the therapeutic efficacy of T cells against solid tumors. However, until now, there were few literatures regarding the role of HVEM in the T cells infiltrated in the NSCLC tissues.

Here, our study illuminates HVEM’s pivotal role in NSCLC’s immune environment, impacting T cell activity and immunotherapy results. HVEM is identified as a key modulator of T cell dynamics, with our findings suggesting it could be instrumental in T cell metabolic shifts and influencing NSCLC progression. The HVEM-GPT2 axis emerges as a potential therapeutic target, underscoring its relevance in NSCLC treatment and immune response modulation.

Methods

Analysis of open-accessed data and public databases

The lung cancer data used in this study were sourced directly from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database (TCGA-NSCLC). Single-cell data of lung cancer were obtained from Tumor Immune Single-cell Hub (TISCH) project (http://tisch.comp-genomics.org/home/), including GSE131907 and GSE176021 cohorts. For GSE131907, researchers performed single-cell RNA sequencing on over 208,000 cells from 58 lung adenocarcinomas in 44 patients. This analysis revealed a detailed cellular landscape of lung adenocarcinoma progression, including various cell types in the tumor environment. For GSE176021, researchers used blood and surgical samples from a clinical trial of neoadjuvant anti-PD-1 (nivolumab) in NSCLC patients. Also, they detected T cell responses and identified the related T cell receptors in six patients. Quantification of the immune microenvironment is achieved through the ssGSEA algorithm. The input file is the combined gene expression profile. Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE) project (http://tide.dfci.harvard.edu) was used calculate the TIDE score of NSCLC patients.

Cell lines

The human NSCLC cell line A549, H1299, and Jurkat T cells were obtained from the Cell Bank of Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). Murine lung cancer cell line Lewis lung carcinoma with luciferase (LLC-LUC) cells and CMT-64 cells were purchased from Procell life technology company (Wuhan, China). A549, H1299, LLC, and CMT-64 cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin. Jurakt T cells were grown in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 medium, enriched with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin–streptomycin. All cell lines were kept in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2 at 37 °C.

Cell transfection

To knock down HVEM and GPT2 expression, corresponding shRNA construct specifically targeting the HVEM and GPT2 gene was employed, and for overexpression, a plasmid encoding GPT2 was utilized. Initially, Jurkat cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS, reaching a density of 0.5–1 × 10^6 cells/mL a day prior to transfection. On the day of transfection, cells were counted and adjusted to 2 × 10^6 cells/mL. The shRNAs and the GPT2 overexpression plasmid were each mixed with Lipofectamine regent, designed for suspension cells, according to the manufacturer’s protocol. After allowing the DNA-reagent complexes to form, they were introduced to the Jurkat cells and incubated at 37 °C in a 5% CO2 atmosphere.

Quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR)

The expression levels of HVEM and GPT2 in Jurkat cells were assessed using qPCR assay. All experiments were conducted in triplicate to ensure accuracy and reproducibility. The primers used were: HVEM, forward, 5′-TCATCGTCATTGTTTGCTCCA-3′, reverse, 5′-ACCTTGACTACATCACCCCTT-3′; GPT2, forward, 5′-GGAGCTAGTGACGGCATTTCTACGA-3′, reverse, 5′-CCCAGGGTTGATTATGCAGAGCA-3′; GAPDH, forward, 5′-GGAGCGAGATCCCTCCAAAAT-3′, reverse, 5′-GGCTGTTGTCATACTTCTCATGG-3′.

Co-culture system

In a transwell co-culture setup (Corning,USA), lung cancer cell lines A549 and H1299 were seeded in the bottom chamber of the transwell plate using DMEM with 10% FBS. Once they reached approximately 70–80% confluence, Jurkat T cells, cultivated in RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS, were placed in the upper chamber of a 0.4 μm pore-sized membrane insert. This specific pore size permits the exchange of soluble factors and cytokines between chambers but prevents the migration of cells, ensuring no direct cell-to-cell contact. This system facilitates the study of paracrine interactions and communication between the immune cells and the tumor cells, mimicking certain aspects of the tumor microenvironment.

Activation of jurkat cells using PMA and ionomycin

Jurkat T cells were seeded in a suitable culture medium and allowed to adhere overnight. The following day, cells were treated with phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA) at a concentration of 50 ng/mL and ionomycin at a concentration of 500 ng/mL. PMA, a potent protein kinase C (PKC) activator, and ionomycin, a calcium ionophore, synergistically stimulate various intracellular pathways mimicking T cell receptor (TCR) activation. After the addition of PMA and ionomycin, cells were incubated under standard culture conditions. After a 4 h, cells can be harvested and analyzed for desired activation markers or further downstream assays.

Analysis of CD69 expression by flow cytometry

Jurkat T cells were carefully collected and subjected to a washing step using phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) to remove any residual media or non-adherent cells. For the detection of CD69 expression, cells were incubated with a PEcy7 conjugated anti-CD69 monoclonal antibody sourced from Biolegend (San Diego, CA, USA). This incubation was performed for 30 min under light-protected conditions to ensure the stability and efficacy of the fluorochrome. Following the staining process, the cells were loaded into a flow cytometer for data acquisition. Through flow cytometry analysis, the distribution of fluorescent signals from each cell was recorded, allowing for the quantification of CD69 expression levels across the cell population. The mean fluorescence intensity, which represents the average fluorescent signal from the stained cells, was subsequently computed to provide a metric for the overall level of CD69 expression.

Flow cytometry analysis of immune cells in spleen, blood, and lung tumor

Flow cytometry was performed to analyze the immune cells number in the mouse spleen, blood, and lung tumor. Fresh spleen and lung tumor was prepared into single-cell suspension. Single-cell suspension of blood, pleen, and lung tumor was stained with the following antibodies which contained two panels: CD3 APCCy7, CD8 Percpcy5.5, CD4 PECy7, Foxp3 APC, CD25 PE,NK1.1 FITC,CD45 AF700; the oher Panel: CD45 APCcy7, CD19 APC, CD11b FITC, CD11C PEcy7, CD86 BV421, CD163 PECy7, and F4/80 Percpcy5.5 (elabscience, China). The following process was described in the above paragraph.

Assessment of mitochondrial respiration

Oxygen consumption rate (OCR) in activated Jurkat cells was assessed using a Seahorse XFp Analyzer, a widely recognized instrument for such evaluations.Firstly, sh-NC and sh-HVEM were used to transfect the Jurkat cells. Then, we used PMA and Ionomycin to activate the Jurkat cells. Next, activated Jurkat cells were cultured under designated experimental conditions and then seeded at a density of approximately 2 × 10^5 cells per well in a specialized XFp cell culture miniplate. Prior to the assay, cells were equilibrated in a CO2-free incubator for 1 h with Seahorse assay medium, which is bicarbonate-free and pre-warmed to 37 °C. For the analysis, the Seahorse XFp Analyzer sequentially injected specific mitochondrial inhibitors into each well, allowing for the real-time measurement of key parameters associated with mitochondrial function. The used inhibitors include oligomycin (to measure ATP-linked respiration), FCCP (to measure maximal respiration by uncoupling the electron transport chain), and a combination of rotenone and antimycin A (to shut down mitochondrial respiration and measure non-mitochondrial respiration). The OCR was then automatically calculated by the analyzer, providing insights into various aspects of mitochondrial function, including basal respiration, ATP production, proton leak, and spare respiratory capacity. In post-assay, results were normalized to cell number or protein content for a more accurate comparative analysis.

Detection of glucose, lactic acid, and adenosine triphosphate (ATP)

Glucose consumption, lactic acid generation, and intracellular ATP levels were assessed utilizing glucose, lactic acid, and ATP assay kits, respectively. All kits were sourced from Jiancheng Corporation (Nanjing, Jiangsu, China) and were applied in accordance with the guidelines provided by the manufacturer.

Cell proliferation detection and transwell assay

Cell proliferation assay and transwell assay were performed according to the our published methods.

In vivo experiments

Animal experiments were conducted receiving the animal ethics approval from Fudan University (20230304Z). To elucidate the potential interactions between Jurkat T cells and lung cancer cells in vivo, we employed a murine tumor xenograft model. A549 or H1299 lung cancer cells, after co-culturing with Jurkat T cells using a transwell system, were harvested and resuspended in a 1:1 mixture of PBS and Matrigel. These cell suspensions were then subcutaneously injected into the flanks of immunocompromised nude mice. Tumor growth was monitored twice a week, and tumor volume was calculated using the formula: length x width^2 × 0.5. After a defined period, the mice were sacrificed, and tumors were excised. For histological analysis, tumor sections were stained with an anti-Ki67 antibody to assess cellular proliferation. Additionally, to explore the metastatic potential, we intravenously injected co-cultured lung cancer cells into the tail vein of the mice. After a specific duration, the lungs were collected, fixed, and stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) to evaluate metastatic nodules.

HVEM Knockout mouses (C57BL/6Smoc-HVEMem1Smoc,Cat. NO. NM-KO-190439) were purchased from Shanghai Model Organisms Center, Inc and 4–6-week-old mice were used. The license number of Shanghai Model Organisms Center is SCXK (Guangdong) 2022–0062. Homozygous mice with HVEM gene knockout were constructed using CRISPR/Cas9 technology. HVEM knockout F0 mice were obtained and bred to F5 generation mice. Genotypes were identified by PCR amplification on genomic DNA extracted from mousetails, four homozygous mice were obtained (B1, B3, B6, and B7 in Figure S1A; two female mouse and two male mouse). No obvious abnormalities were found in the appearance, body weight, and the size of various organs in the HVEM knockout mice compared with wild type mice. HVEM KO and wild type C57BL6 mouses were injected subcutaneously and intravenously (tail vein) with LLC cells to evaluate the tumor weight and the extent of tumor dissemination. In addition, the CMT-64 murine lung cancer cell was also implanted into HVEM KO and wild type mice subcutaneously. For subcutaneously implantation group, all the mice were euthanized at Day 14 after tumor implantation. For tail vein implantation group, at least three mice for each HVEM KO group and wild type group were euthanized at Day 10 and Day 20 after implantation. The serum of mice was used for flow cytometry analysis and cytokine detection. Furthermore, the mouse tumor was used for HE staining, IHC, flow cytometry analysis, and cytokine detection.

Live mouse imaging

Live mouse imaing skill was used to evaluate the distribution of tumor in the lung of mouse. The detailed mthod was as follows: After the induction of anesthesia, the mouse was injected with D-Luciferin potassium salt. LT Living Image 4.3 was used to the relative fluorescence intensity which indicated the tumor burden.

22 mouse cytokines detection in serum and tumor

Multiple cytokines detections can provide us valuable insights into gene function and the signaling mechanisms. RayBio C-Series Mouse Cytokine Antibody Array C1 which included 22 different mouse cytokines antibodies was used in our study (Raybiotech, USA). The mouse serum and lysed tumor tissues were incubated with the array overnight at 4 °C. At last, ImageQuant LAS4000 Scanner was used to scan the membrane, and Excel-based Analysis Software was used to analyze the relative intensities compared with the control group. GO and KEGG analysis was performed by R language according to the differentially expressed proteins.

Lung adenocarcinoma tissue microarray (TMA) and multiplex immunohistochemistry (mIHC)

To explore the cellular composition and spatial distribution of HVEM in lung cancer immune microenvironment, mIHC was performed according to the manufacturer’s instruction using Opal 7-color Manual IHC Kit (PerkinElmer). Tissue microarray containing LUAD and corresponding paracancerous tissues purchasing from Outdo Biotech Ltd (HLugA180Su03, Shanghai, China) was used. In addition, baseline clinical data and the survival situation of corresponding pattients were also recorded. The antibodies were assigned into two panels: Panel I consisted HVEM (SantaCruz#sc365971,1:500), CD8 (Biolegend#100,714,1:3000), CD163 (Proteintech#16,646,1:1000), PD-L1 (Proteintech #66,248, 1:3000), CK (Abcam#ab217916, 1:5); Panel II included HVEM (SantaCruz#sc365971, 1:500), CD4 (Biolegend#317,410, 1:3000), CK (Abcam#ab217916, 1:5). Briefly speaking, the tissue microarray dewaxed and microwave thermal repair method was used for antigen repair. The section was incubated with the primary antibody overnight at room temperature. Then, the tissue microarray was incubated with secondary antibody for 10 min at room temperature, and the signals were amplified for fluorescence staining. In addition, the nuclear was stained by using DAPI (Servicebio). At last, the whole tissue microarray was scanned and photographed through TissueGnostics system. TissueGnostics software was used to quantify the proportion and the number of positive cells in every nucleus area. Meantime, the number of cancer cells was calculated based on the distance analysis (0–20um) from CD4 + HVEM + and CD8 + HVEM + through StrataQuest software.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation assays

ChIP-seq experiments were conducted using Jurkat T cells in an activated state. To initiate the protocol, Jurkat T cells were first cross-linked with a solution of Formaldehyde. To extract the chromatin from the cross-linked cells, Lysis Buffer was added, and the chromatin DNA was sonicated to yield fragments ranging from 100 to 300 bp in size. Then chromatin was precipitated through GATA1 antibody (0.025 ml; Novus) and IgG antibody (0.1 ml; Novus).

Sequencing libraries were prepared using the NEBNext® UltraTM DNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina (NEB, USA, cat. E7645L). The libraries were subsequently subjected to sequencing on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform, generating 150 bp reads.

To validate FXOA2 could bind to the GPT2 promoter, DNA was eluted from beads totally and qRT-PCR was performed.

Statistical analysis

All experiments were performed in triplicate unless otherwise specified. Data were presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Differences between two groups were analyzed using the Student’s t-test, while one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s post hoc test was employed for multiple group comparisons. The relationship between categorical variables was assessed using the Chi-square test. The survival rate was estimated by the Kaplan–Meier method, and differences between survival curves were tested for statistical significance using the log-rank test. For RNA sequencing data, differentially expressed genes were identified using the DESeq2 package with a false discovery rate (FDR) adjusted p-value < 0.05 as the threshold. The Cox model was performed to calculate the multivariate and univariate risk factors. All statistical analyses were performed using the SPSS software (version 24.0, IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA), and GraphPad Prism (version 8.0, GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA, USA). A p-value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

Role of HVEM in NSCLC microenvironment and immunotherapy

In this study, we identified HVEM as our gene of interest. Therefore, we tried to explore its mode of action in NSCLC from multiple angles. First, we try to explore the role and expression patterns of HEVM in public databases. We noticed that the mRNA levels of HVEM showed a significantly higher expression trend in both paired and unpaired NSCLC tumor samples (Fig. 1A-B). Although the statistical significance is not yet significant, patients with high expression of HEVM tend to have a better prognosis (Fig. 1C; HR = 0.66, P = 0.142). HVEM is a member of the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily and plays a pivotal role in T cell activation and regulation. Initially identified in the context of herpesvirus entry, HVEM has been intricately linked with T cell responses, engaging in both co-stimulatory and co-inhibitory interactions. Given its influence on T cell functionality, HVEM holds potential significance in shaping immune therapy outcomes, making it a focal point for investigations into novel immunotherapeutic strategies. Consequently, we quantified the immunotherapy response of NSCLC patients based on the TIDE algorithm. Results indicated a negative correlation between TIDE and HVEM (Fig. 1D, R = − 0.16, P < 0.001). Meanwhile, the immunotherapy responders quantified by TIDE analysis has a higher HVEM level (Fig. 1E); the patients with high HVEM level have a higher level of immune dysfunction (Fig. 1F). Subsequently, we sought to further investigate the expression pattern of HVEM in the NSCLC microenvironment. We found that HVEM is distributed in various cell types and is primarily expressed in the T cell subpopulations (Fig. 1G–H). Based on the immune microenvironment results quantified by ssGSEA, we found that HVEM was positively correlated with iDC, pDC, cytotoxic cells, CD8 + T cells, and T cells, but negatively correlated with Th2 cells, Tgd, and Tcm (Fig. 1I).

CD8 + HVEM + and CD4 + HVEM + indicated better prognosis in human adenocarcinoma tissues

In order to explore the prevalence of HVEM and CD4 or CD8 coexpression in human T cells, TMA including 90 lung adenocarcinoma tissues was assessed by mIHC method. Representative costaining images of HVEM, CK, DAPI, PD-L1, CD163, CD4, and CD8 are shown in Figure S1B-C. The Cox multivariate analysis of Panel I showed that CD8 + HVEM + was protective factor, while tumor grade,tumor size and TNM stage were risk factors for overall survival (Table S1). However, no difference was observed in the clinical data between CD8 + HVEM + high and CD8 + HVEM + low group (Table S2). Position relationship analysis indicated that CD8 + HVEM + cells had a strong positive correlation with its around 0–20 um cancer cells which was distinguished by CK labeling (0–20 um, Figure S1D). Patients were classified into high and low group on the basis of the median number of CD8 + HVEM + within 20 um cancer cells, the KM curve found that patients with high number of tumor cells around CD8 + HVEM + had a better prognosis than the low group (Figure S1E). Similiar results regarding CD4 + HVEM + were also observed in the Panel II (Table S3–4, Figure S1F–G). All the above results indicated CD8 + HVEM + or CD4 + HVEM + tended to penetrate into tumor parenchyma instead of the tumor stroma. Next, we used mIHC to identify the subcellular localization of HVEM and found that the HVEM (green) was expressed in the cytosol of CD4 and CD8T cells (FigS1A–B). In addition,HVEM expression was moderately negatively associated with PD-L1 expression, sugguesting that HVEM could act as a promising target in PD-L1 negative or low expression tumors (Figure S1H).

Activated T cells inhibit NSCLC progression

Considering the nature of HVEM and its primary expression on T cells, we next planned to investigate the impact of T cells on the progression of NSCLC. We selected jurkat cells as our model cell line. When treating jurkat cells, we added PMA and ionomycin to activate them (Figure S2A). Furthermore, in our co-culture system, we established three groups: a control group (without jurkat cells), an activated jurkat group (with PMA + ionomycin), and a jurkat group (without PMA + ionomycin activation). We further investigated the differences between A549 and H1299 cells co-cultured with different groups. To probe into their proliferative capabilities, we conducted CCK8, colony formation, and EdU assays (Fig. 2A–H). The results indicated that activated T cells significantly inhibited the proliferative abilities of A549 and H1299 cells with which they were co-cultured. In contrast, non-activated T cells did not seem to have this pronounced inhibitory effect. Additionally, through the tranwell assay, we found that the activated T cells markedly suppressed the invasive and migratory capacities of co-cultured A549 and H1299 cells (Fig. 2I–L). Yet, similarly, the non-activated T cells did not exhibit such significant inhibitory effects. This suggests that the activation status of T cells plays a crucial role in determining their interaction and impact on tumor cell growth and mobility. Specifically, the activated T cells seem to possess a more potent ability to impede the growth and movement of these lung cancer cell lines compared to their non-activated counterparts. After our in vitro observations, we felt that it was imperative to verify these results within a more complex, physiologically relevant system. Thus, we proceeded with animal model studies. Utilizing nude mouse tumorigenicity assays, our findings were consistent with the cell culture data. Activated T cells not only displayed their ability to suppress tumor growth but did so in a pronounced manner, evident from the significant reductions in both tumor weight and tumor volume (Fig. 3A–D). Furthermore, the Ki67 staining, a reliable marker for cell proliferation, provided compelling evidence that the proliferation rate of NSCLC cells was considerably diminished in the presence of activated T cells (Fig. 3E). This supports the hypothesis that activated T cells exert a tangible anti-proliferative effect against NSCLC cells. Using lung metastasis model, we aimed to gauge the effect of activated T cells on the metastatic potential of NSCLC (Fig. 3F). Lung tumor nodules were markedly reduced when lung cancer cells had been co-cultured with activated T cells.

Knockdown of HVEM promote apoptosis and suppress activation of T cells

The relationship between HVEM and T cells is well-established in immunological studies. Given this backdrop, we hypothesized that manipulating HVEM expression could provide insights into the intricate dynamics of T cell functions. In post-HVEM knockdown, our data unveiled compelling changes in T cell behavior (Figure S2B). Flow cytometry analysis revealed a notable uptick in T cell apoptosis levels (Fig. 4A). This suggests that HVEM might play a pivotal role in T cell survival or maintenance. Furthermore, Western blotting corroborated these findings (Fig. 4B). Moreover, another salient observation was the suppression of T cell activation post-HVEM knockdown (Fig. 4C). This indicates that HVEM might have a more nuanced role, potentially as a modulator of T cell activation pathways. Subsequently, we undertook an endeavor to knock down HVEM in Jurkat cells and then stimulated them with PMA and ionomycin to evaluate the influence of HVEM expression in Jurkat cells on co-cultured lung cancer cells. Our findings indicated that knocking down HVEM in jurkat cells enhanced the proliferation, invasion, and migration capabilities of co-cultured lung cancer cells (Fig. 4D–F).

Currently, there was no literatures systematically discussing the effect of HVEM in lung cancer immune microenvironment in the mouse model. Our results showed the tumor size and Ki67 in the HVEM KO group were significantly bigger and higher than wild type group in LLC cells and CMT-64 cells (Figure S3A–D). HVEM regulates T cell activation by affecting mitochondrial metabolism.

We conducted RNA sequencing for both HVEM knockdown and control groups in jurkat cells to delve deeper into the intrinsic biological roles of HVEM (Supplementary file 3). Enrichment analysis revealed that differentially expressed genes were primarily associated with mitochondrial components and oxidative phosphorylation metabolic pathways (Fig. 5A–B). T cells exhibit metabolic plasticity, allowing them to adjust their nutritional requirements based on the tissue environment. Metabolic reprogramming is not solely a hallmark of tumor cells; it is crucial for various immune cells, including T cells. Some studies have reported a tumor-like Warburg effect during T cell activation, wherein there is a metabolic shift from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis. Given this, we investigated the differences in mitochondrial metabolism between activated and non-activated T cells. We found that the activated T cells (PMA + ionomycin) have a decreased oxygen consumption rate (OCR; reflecting mitochondrial respiration) (Fig. 5C). Glucose utilization, lactic acid detection, and intracellular ATP detection assays revealed that the activated T cells have a significantly enhanced glucose utilization, lactic acid production, and intracellular ATP levels, indicating a higher glycolysis level (Fig. 5D–F). Next, we investigated the effects of HVEM on oxidative phosphorylation in T cells. Results showed that HVEM knockdown increased the oxygen consumption rate but decreased glucose utilization, lactic acid production, and intracellular ATP levels (Fig. 5G–J). These findings indicate that HVEM is involved in the metabolic reprogramming of T cells, shifting from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis and subsequently modulating T cell activation.

HVEM modulates T cell function by regulating GPT2

Next, we explored the downstream genes of HVEM in the glycolytic pathway. We screened out the GPT2 gene based on log2FC (Fold Change) and P-value (Fig. S11A). Through sequencing analysis, we found that GPT2 was significantly downregulated in HVEM knockdown Jurkat cells. GPT2 is well-known for its role in amino acid metabolism in liver function, but researchers have also highlighted its involvement in oxidative phosphorylation and glycolysis. Recently, some studies found the involvement of GPT2 in glucose metabolism and homeostasis.Therefore, we aimed to determine whether HVEM exerts its functions by modulating GPT2. We observed that upon knocking down HVEM, the expression of GPT2 in Jurkat cells was significantly reduced (Figure S2C). Similarly, GPT2 knockdown resulted in an increased oxygen consumption rate but led to a decrease in glucose utilization, lactic acid production, and intracellular ATP levels (Fig. 6A–D). Additionally, the knockdown of GPT2 can suppress the activation of T cells (Figure S11B-C). Through the CCK8 and colony formation assays, we discovered that lung cancer cells co-cultured with GPT2 knockdown T cells exhibited diminished proliferative capacity (Fig. 6E–I). The transwell assay results were also in alignment with this trend (Fig. 6J–L). To determine the interplay between HVEM and GPT2, especially in the context of T cell metabolism and activation, we designed and executed a rescue experiment. We reintroduced GPT2 in the HVEM knockdown Jurkat cells to ascertain whether the phenotypic and functional alterations resulting from HVEM deficiency could be restored (Figure S11D). The T cell metabolic discrepancies observed in the HVEM-deficient cells were largely rectified. The previously reported reduction in oxygen consumption rate and glucose utilization, alongside the diminished lactic acid production and intracellular ATP levels, witnessed a notable rebound (Fig. 7A–C). Also, T cell activation, which was previously suppressed in the HVEM knockdown model, was restored to levels that closely mirrored the control jurkat cells (Figure S11D). This further suggests that the modulation of T cell activation by HVEM might be, at least in part, mediated through its regulation of GPT2. In co-culture experiments, the lung cancer cells exhibited reduced proliferation when co-cultured with HVEM knockdown T cells overexpressing GPT2, compared to those co-cultured with just HVEM knockdown T cells (Fig. 7E–I). The transwell assay also mirrored this trend, indicating that the invasive and migratory capabilities of the lung cancer cells were curtailed in the presence of the GPT2-overexpressing, HVEM-deficient T cells (Fig. 7J–M). In conclusion, our rescue experiment provided compelling evidence that the roles of HVEM in T cell metabolism and activation are indeed mediated, at least to a significant extent, through its regulation of GPT2.

HVEM regulates GPT2 by interacting with its upstream transcription factor FOXA2

To explore the direct mechanism between HVEM and GPT2, IP-MS was performed (Fig. 8A). The results showed that FOXA2 which exerted its importance in the reprogram of glucose metabolism as an underlying protein interacting with HVEM (Supplementary file 4). Then, COIP assay identified the interaction between endogenous HVEM and FOXA2 (Fig. 8B). Immunofluorescence staining confirmed the co-localization of HVEM and FOXA2 in the cytoplasm (Fig. 8C). In addition, molecular docking also revealed the interaction between HVEM and FOXA2 (Fig. 8D). FOXA2 is an important transcription factor in inducing T cells maturation and differentiation. Thus, we identified that whether FOXA2 could directly regulate GPT2 transcription in activated Jurkat T cells. The JASPAR website indicated that FOXA2 can bind to the GPT2 promotor site. Then dual-luciferase activity analysis displayed that Si-FOXA2 could increase the luciferase activity of GPT2-WT but not that of GPT2-MUT (Fig. 8E). In addition, GPT2 mRNA was upregulated after FOXA2 knockdown and downregulated after FOXA2 overxpression, indicating FOXA2 to be the transcription suppressor of GPT2 (Fig. 8F–G). Furthermore, ChIP-qPCR experiment verified that FOXA2 transcriptionally binded to GPT2 promoter (Fig. 8H). Next, we found that HVEM knockdown had no influence on the mRNA expression level of FOXA2 (Fig. 8I). Furthermore, nuclear-cytoplasmic fractionation assay results suggested that HVEM overexpression in T cells decreased FOXA2 in the nucleus and cytoplasm and increased GPT2 in the cytoplasm at protein level. Meantime, the opposite results were found upon HVEM knockdown (Fig. 8J). Thus, in mechanistic terms, HVEM directly decreased FOXA2 expression which acted as the upstream transcription suppressor of GPT2.

GATA1 regulates HVEM expression in activated Jurkat T cells

We used JASPAR and QIAGEN website to predict the possible upstream transcription factors and GATA1 belonged to the intersection of the two prediction tools. Then chromatin was precipitated through GATA1 antibody (0.025 ml; Novus) and IgG antibody (0.1 ml; Novus). Sequencing results showed that GATA1 could interact with HVEM promoter region, as indicated in Figure S12A. Furthermore, GO and KEGG analysis based on the differentitally expressed genes demonstrated that metabolic process and immune process were both enriched, which sugguested the reliability of our study (Figure S12B–C). Furthermore, HVEM mRNA was downregulated after GATA1 overxpression, sugguesting GATA1 to be the transcription suppressor of HVEM (FigureS12D).

Discussion

T cells play a pivotal role in the adaptive immune response and have become central to the success of immunotherapeutic strategies, particularly in the context of cancer treatment. These lymphocytes possess the capability to specifically recognize and eliminate tumor cells, mainly through their T cell receptors (TCRs) which bind to tumor-associated antigens presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules on the cancer cell surface. The groundbreaking development of immune checkpoint inhibitors, targeting proteins like PD-1 and CTLA-4, has revitalized the field of cancer immunotherapy. These inhibitors work by releasing the brakes on T cells, enabling them to more effectively attack tumors. When tumors exploit these checkpoint pathways, they can effectively “switch off” attacking T cells, allowing the tumor to grow unchecked. By inhibiting these checkpoints, the natural tumor-targeting action of T cells can be restored and even amplified. Additionally, strategies like adoptive T cell therapy, including CAR T (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T cell) therapy, involve the modification and expansion of a patient’s own T cells outside the body to equip them with enhanced tumor-targeting capabilities, and then re-infusing them back into the patient. These engineered T cells can then seek out and destroy cancer cells with increased efficiency. Despite the tremendous success of T cell-centered therapies, challenges such as treatment resistance, limited response rates in certain cancers, and potential off-target toxicities remain.

In our study, we investigated the role of HVEM in the microenvironment of NSCLC and its implications in immunotherapy. Crucially, HVEM, part of the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily, influences T cell activation, potentially impacting immunotherapeutic outcomes. Using the TIDE algorithm, our results showcased a link between HVEM levels and immune dysfunction in NSCLC patients. Through lung adenocarcinoma tissue microarray, results of mIHC analysis showed that CD8 + HVEM + and CD4 + HVEM + high expression indicated favorable prognosis. Furthermore, immune cell distance analysis showed CD8 + HVEM + and CD4 + HVEM + tended to penetrate into tumor parenchyma instead of the tumor stroma. Activated T cells, particularly from the Jurkat cell line, significantly inhibited NSCLC progression, reducing both proliferation and invasion capabilities of A549 and H1299 lung cancer cell lines. In vivo models reinforced these observations. In addition, HVEM KO mouse experiments showed the importance of HVEM in regulating the tumor immune microenvironment. Manipulating HVEM expression revealed its essential role in T cell survival and activation. Furthermore, our data suggest that HVEM is pivotal in T cell metabolic reprogramming, transitioning from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis. RNA sequencing illuminated a potential relationship between HVEM and GPT2, an enzyme tied to amino acid metabolism and cellular energetics. Subsequent experiments confirmed that HVEM’s influence on T cell activation and metabolism is potentially mediated through its regulation of GPT2. Our findings underscore the therapeutic potential of targeting the HVEM-GPT2 axis in NSCLC and other immune-related conditions.

Some studies argued that HVEM were negative predictors in gastric, ovarian, and colorectal cancers. The anti-HVEM mAb therapy was also reported to exert anti-timor function in HVEM Knockin lung cancer cells and vivo models expressing human BTLA and HVEM cancer cells. Nevertheless, increased HVEM expression in CD4 or CD8 cells was correlated with favorable outcome in lung adenocarcinoma TMA when using HVEM antibody. Therefore, we displayed that CD8 + HVEM + and CD4 + HVEM + were positive indictors in lung cancer patients. The opposing functions indicated that the expression levels and location of HVEM and corresponding receptors or ligands could influence immune responses in tumor immune microenvironment, thus impacting survival. Patients with different tumor stages could result in the distinct prognostic value of HVEM. Phenomena similar to this have been reported previously in PD-L1 or HHLA2 expression and their prognostic values in cancers. Our study indicated that the function of HVEM in distinct cancer cells or immune cells may be complicated and influenced by multiple immune microenvironment.

T cells, critical components of the adaptive immune response, undergo dramatic metabolic changes upon activation to meet their bioenergetic and biosynthetic demands. The balance between mitochondrial respiration and glycolysis plays a crucial role in determining T cell fate and function. In a resting state, naive T cells predominantly rely on oxidative phosphorylation in the mitochondria for ATP production, maintaining a relatively low rate of glycolysis. Upon activation, T cells experience a metabolic shift, rapidly increasing their rate of glycolysis to support proliferation and effector function acquisition. This glycolytic switch is essential for generating metabolic intermediates necessary for biomass synthesis, supporting the rapid growth and proliferation of activated T cells. However, distinct T cell subsets exhibit differential metabolic profiles. For instance, effector T cells (Teff) rely heavily on glycolysis even under oxygen-rich conditions, a phenomenon termed aerobic glycolysis. In contrast, memory T cells revert to a more oxidative metabolism, utilizing fatty acid oxidation (FAO) and exhibiting increased mitochondrial OCR. This metabolic adaptation allows memory T cells to sustain long-term survival and rapid responses upon re-exposure to antigens. Regulatory T cells (Tregs), which function to suppress immune responses and maintain immune homeostasis, also exhibit unique metabolic characteristics. While they can engage in glycolysis, they predominantly depend on lipid oxidation and mitochondrial respiration. The intricate balance between OCR and glycolysis in T cells is not merely a reflection of their energetic needs but also profoundly impacts their differentiation, function, and survival. Understanding these metabolic nuances offers potential therapeutic avenues, especially in immunotherapy settings where modulating T cell metabolism might enhance or suppress specific T cell functions.

GPT2, which encodes for the enzyme glutamate pyruvate transaminase 2, is central to amino acid metabolism. In the broader context of oncology, disruptions or alterations in such metabolic pathways, especially amino acid metabolism, are seminal hallmarks of tumorigenic cells, facilitating their robust proliferation and adaptation to heterogeneous conditions. Our recent insights underscore GPT2’s unique position in NSCLC, particularly in the nuanced interplay between metabolic reprogramming and T cell functionalities. What makes our discovery exceptionally pivotal is the delineation of GPT2’s regulatory interactions with HVEM in the NSCLC landscape. The RNA sequencing data from our study elucidated a hitherto unexplored relationship between HVEM and GPT2. This relationship becomes critically pertinent as HVEM appears to modulate T cell metabolic shifts via GPT2. Essentially, HVEM’s regulation of GPT2 orchestrates a transition from oxidative phosphorylation to aerobic glycolysis in T cells, a metabolic reprogramming that could substantially alter T cell efficacy in tumor microenvironments.

To explore the direct mechanism between HVEM and GPT2, IP-MS was performed. However, we did not find the direct association between HVEM and GPT2.HVEM-FLAG IP-MS results showed that HVEM may interact with FOXA2 at the highest score, so CoIP assay was performed to validate the prediction. FOXA2 is an important metabolic regulator of glucose metabolism and energy depletion in adipocytes of diet-induced obese mice. It can activate the expression of insulin-sensitizing genes and participate in various biological processes, including metabolism, proliferation, development, differentiation, apoptosis, and immunity. Hence, FOXA2 is an important activator of glucose metabolism genes. In addition, FOXA2 belongs to the forkhead-box (FOX) protein transcription factor superfamily that is DNA-binding proteins regulating transcription. Therefore, we selected FOXA2 for further research. In mechanistic terms, HVEM directly decreased FOXA2 expression which acted as the upstream transcription suppressor of GPT2. However, the detailed mechanism why HVEM could bind to FOXA2 needed to be explored further.

Here, we primarily focused on the role of HVEM in the microenvironment of NSCLC and its influence on immunotherapeutic outcomes. While our analyses suggest a pivotal role for HVEM in T cell activation and its subsequent effects on NSCLC progression, it is important to note a few limitations. Firstly, our findings are based on in vitro experiments and mouse models, which might not fully replicate the complexities of human NSCLC biology. Additionally, while we did observe a relationship between HVEM and GPT2, further studies are needed to decipher the intricate molecular mechanisms at play. This intricate molecular interplay warrants further exploration, especially in the context of therapeutic applications targeting the HVEM-GPT2 axis in immune-related conditions and cancers. Finally, we also predicted GATA1 as a transcription factor of HVEM. Next, rescue experiment and the detailed promoter regions of HVEM binding to the transcription factor GATA1 will be explored.
