# Effects of epigenetic therapy on natural killer cell function and development in hematologic malignancy.

## Abstract

Epigenetic therapy is an emerging field in the treatment of human cancer, including hematologic malignancies. This class of therapeutic agents approved by the US Food and Drug Administration for cancer treatment includes DNA hypomethylating agents, histone deacetylase inhibitors, IDH1/2 inhibitors, EZH2 inhibitors, and numerous preclinical targets/agents. Most studies measuring the biological effects of epigenetic therapy focus their attention on either their direct cytotoxic effects on malignant cells or their effects on modifying tumor cell antigen expression, exposing them to immune surveillance mechanisms. However, a growing body of evidence suggests that epigenetic therapy also has effects on the development and function of the immune system, including natural killer cells, which can alter their response to cancer cells. In this review, we summarize the body of literature studying the effects of different classes of epigenetic therapy on the development and/or function of natural killer cells.

Effects of epigenetic therapy on natural killer cell function and development in hematologic malignancy

Epigenetic therapy is an emerging field in the treatment of human cancer, including hematologic malignancies. This class of therapeutic agents approved by the US Food and Drug Administration for cancer treatment includes DNA hypomethylating agents, histone deacetylase inhibitors, IDH1/2 inhibitors, EZH2 inhibitors, and numerous preclinical targets/agents. Most studies measuring the biological effects of epigenetic therapy focus their attention on either their direct cytotoxic effects on malignant cells or their effects on modifying tumor cell antigen expression, exposing them to immune surveillance mechanisms. However, a growing body of evidence suggests that epigenetic therapy also has effects on the development and function of the immune system, including natural killer cells, which can alter their response to cancer cells. In this review, we summarize the body of literature studying the effects of different classes of epigenetic therapy on the development and/or function of natural killer cells.

Introduction

Epigenetic dysregulation is recognized as a central feature in the development and progression of cancer. In some instances, epigenetic dysregulation is driven by mutations in genes involved in chromatin regulation, including DNA methyltransferase 3A (DNMT3A), ten-eleven translocation 2 (TET2), isocitrate dehydrogenase 1/2 (IDH1/2), and enhancer of zeste homolog 2 (EZH2), among others. In other situations, as highlighted in the seminal series of reviews by Hanahan and Weinberg, there is nonmutational epigenetic reprogramming driven by microenvironmental factors such as hypoxia and soluble factor signaling. Some tumor types with relatively low mutational burden, including hematologic malignancies, appear to be primarily driven by epigenetic dysfunction. This mounting body of evidence suggests a primary role for epigenetic regulation in tumorigenesis and has shifted the paradigm beyond just somatic or germline mutations driving cancer formation and progression.

Given the critical role of epigenetic dysregulation in cancer, the development of epigenetic-modifying therapies has been an area of intense interest for some time. This has led to the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approval of epigenetic therapies for several diseases, including myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), acute myeloid leukemia (AML), multiple myeloma (MM), follicular lymphoma (FL), cutaneous T-cell lymphoma, and peripheral T-cell lymphoma, either as monotherapy or in combination with other drug classes, including immunotherapy. More recently, the effects of epigenetic therapies have extended beyond their direct effects on tumor cells to include effects on other cell types, including the immune system. Natural killer (NK) cells are members of the innate lymphoid cell family that have been extensively studied for their antigen-independent anticancer effector functions. In this review, we highlight the available literature describing the effects of epigenetic therapy in modulating human NK cell development and effector responses (summarized in Table 1).

The role of epigenetics in NK cell development and function

The formation of cytotoxic effector NK cells begins in the bone marrow from hematopoietic progenitors and finishes in secondary lymphoid tissues such as the tonsil. We and others have previously described a stepwise progression of increasingly committed stages of NK cell developmental intermediates (NKDIs), defined as stages 1 to 6 by the acquisition or loss of the surface markers CD34, CD117, CD56, CD94, NKp80, CD16, and CD57. Additionally, NK cell effector responses are tightly regulated through expression of killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) or C-type lectin-like receptors (namely NKG2A, NKG2C, and NKG2D). These receptors provide a balance of activating and inhibitory signals, which, when tipped toward activation, lead to NK cell degranulation and subsequent target cell killing via a perforin/granzyme dependent mechanism.

Recent studies have revealed that NK cell development is tightly controlled by epigenetic regulation of the genes described above, including DNA methylation of the CD16, KIR, and NKp80 genes (discussed in more detail below). As NKDIs progress from stage 1 (earliest) to stage 6 (terminally mature), their genome becomes progressively less methylated. Alterations in DNA methylation have also been implicated in the formation of adaptive NK cells. For example, the development of adaptive NK cells in response to cytomegalovirus infection is associated with global changes in DNA methylation, with the acquisition of hyper- and hypomethylated regions in comparison to mature NK cells. Changes in DNA methylation strongly correlated with altered gene expression, including increased NKG2C expression and reductions in signaling proteins like FcϵRγ and EAT-2. These changes were also associated with decreased expression of the BTB–zinc finger family PLZF that recruits chromatin-modifying enzymes, leading to further changes in DNA methylation. In addition, chronic stimulation of adaptive NK cells leads to promoter hypomethylation and acquisition of an exhausted phenotype.

The importance of timed and coordinated demethylation of these genes in proper NK cell development is highlighted by the pathology associated with its dysregulation. Extranodal NK/T-cell lymphoma, nasal type (ENKTL) is a rare malignancy of NK cell origin that is associated with prior infection with the Epstein–Barr virus. We previously demonstrated that ENKTL is among the most hypermethylated malignancies described. Phenotypically, ENKTL cells largely resemble developmentally arrested NKDIs, typically at a stage 4- or 5-like NK cell. Furthermore, ENKTL samples demonstrate enrichment of EZH2, a component of the polycomb repressor complex involved in gene silencing via methylation. Preclinical studies using in vitro and human patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models revealed that treatment with a hypomethylating agent (5-azacitidine) was capable of differentiating ENKTL cells as measured by KIR and CD57 acquisition, leading to improved survival. These studies highlight the importance of epigenetics in governing NK cell development and function during physiologic and pathophysiologic processes.

DNA hypomethylating agents

DNA methylation occurs at select CpG sites throughout the genome to regulate transcriptional activity. 5-Azacitidine (aza) and 5-aza-2′-deoxycytidine (decitabine, dac) are the only FDA-approved hypomethylating agents. These drugs can be used as single agents, commonly in the treatment of MDS and chronic myelomonocytic leukemia, or in combination with the BCL-2 inhibitor venetoclax or the IDH1 inhibitor ivosidenib in patients with AML unable to receive intensive induction chemotherapy. Both aza and dac are antimetabolites that inhibit the activity of DNA methyltransferases and produce global hypomethylation following incorporation into DNA. At high doses, both aza and dac demonstrate direct cytotoxicity, while effects seen at lower doses are primarily driven by epigenetic changes. Although most studies in hematologic malignancies have described the direct effects of hypomethylating agent (HMA) therapy on malignant cells, including their ability to upregulate the NK cell activating ligands MICA and MICB, some studies have extended this investigation to include their effect on the nonmalignant immune system, including NK cells.

Aza

The effects of HMA therapy on NK cells depend on their proliferative status, as their hypomethylating effects rely on incorporation into DNA during cell division. Among proliferating NK cells (CD3−CD19−CD56+), either in vitro or in patients with high-risk MDS, those treated with aza had higher frequencies of KIR expression and a more diverse KIR repertoire, including both activating and inhibitory KIR, compared with untreated cells, with NKG2A−CD57+ cells expressing the highest number of KIR per NK cell. This observation resembles the upregulation of KIR and CD57 expression observed upon treatment of ENKTL cells with aza. Furthermore, NK cells treated ex vivo with aza exhibited increased IFNγ production and increased killing of K562 target cells. Cells showing upregulation of KIR following aza treatment also had higher Ki-67 positivity, supporting the claim that aza exerts the greatest effect on the proliferating NK cell pool.

The direct effect of aza on NK cells is complex and may be dose and time point specific. A handful of studies have reported inhibitory effects of aza treatment on NK cells when treating ex vivo at micromolar doses. One study found that aza treatment of NK cells at 1 to 5 μM decreased their ability to kill K562 and RAJI cells and produce IFNγ following stimulation with IL-2. This effect was mediated by reduced expression of the activating receptors NKG2D, NKp30, and CD16 upon treatment with aza. Studies have also found upregulation of inhibitory KIR following aza treatment, which resulted in decreased NK cell killing of K562 cells and reduced perforin/granzyme release in the presence of IL-2. It should be noted that neither of these studies measured changes in activating KIR. Nonetheless, this discrepancy could be due to the proliferative state of the NK cells when exposed to aza treatment, as studies reporting inhibitory effects were done at early time points (<3 d, often <24 h), whereas studies showing stimulatory effects reported their findings after 2+ replicative cycles (>4 d). Thus, the timing and titration of aza doses should be carefully considered for clinical translation, including the presence or absence of mitogenic molecules like IL-2, as these can have opposing effects on NK cell function depending on their proliferative status.

Dac

Similar to aza, several studies have focused on the effect of dac on NK cells. One early study assessed the effect of dac on mouse NK cell abundance and cytotoxicity in vivo. Using the B16F10 melanoma model, in vivo treatment with dac at 0.2 mg/kg resulted in decreased NK cell abundance and inhibited their cytotoxic function. While the mechanism underlying this effect was not investigated, they did report reduced expression of Tbet, a master transcription factor regulator of NK cell function, within bulk tumor and lymph nodes after dac treatment. Another study sought to characterize the effect of different doses of dac on NK cell proliferation, viability, cytotoxicity, and receptor expression in vitro in the presence of IL-2. This work showed that dac at doses between 0.02 and 20 μM caused a linear dose-dependent decrease in NK cell viability and proliferation. There were variable effects on NK cell receptor expression, as dac increased KIR and NKp44 expression but decreased NKG2D in a dose-dependent fashion. Interestingly, using the HLA null target 721.221 cell line, dac negatively impacted NK cell cytotoxicity at intermediate doses (0.31–1.25 μM) but had little effect at both low (0.02–0.16 μM) and high doses(2.5–20 μM). In this study, the measurement of NK cell activating and inhibitory receptors was not comprehensive, and the observed differential effect could be driven by modulation of other receptors not examined. Building on this work, Li et al. studied the effect of dac on NK cells derived from patients with AML. Similar to previous work, they found a dose-dependent decrease in NK cell viability with dac treatment. Using K562 cells as targets and in the presence of IL-2, dac at low (0–0.1 μM) and high doses (2–10 μM) again had little effect on NK cell cytotoxicity, but in contrast to the above cited work, they found increased cytotoxicity with intermediate doses (0.4 μM). Similarly, IFNγ production showed a nadir at intermediate doses and no change at low or high doses of dac. Intermediate doses of dac produced increased expression of NKG2D and NKp46, whereas expression of the inhibitory receptor CD158b (KIR2DL2/L3) had a dose-dependent increase with dac treatment. Another study found that dac treatment of NK cells for 24 h with doses ranging from 0.1 to 5 μM led to increased killing of target cells (K562 and RAJI) and upregulation of IFNγ transcripts, suggesting it promotes a poised NK cell state.

More recently, the effect of HMAs on CD34+ hematopoietic stem cell (HSPC)–derived NK cells was explored in the context of adoptive cell therapy for AML. This study found that neither aza nor dac influenced HSPC-NK cell viability under steady-state or proliferative conditions, but aza did significantly impair HSPC-NK cell proliferation at higher doses, whereas dac did not. There was also a differential effect on HSPC-NK cell cytotoxic function, as higher doses of aza inhibited killing of K562 and THP1 target cells, whereas dac did not. HMA treatment did not have any effect on HSPC-NK cell expression of NKG2A or CD16 but increased KIR expression in vitro. In vivo, only dac was able to potentiate the effect of adoptively transferred HSPC-NK cells. This differential effect was not driven by differences in HSPC-NK cell persistence and thus could possibly be attributed to the differential effect of aza and dac on cytotoxic function reported in this article and others. In vivo modeling using NSG mice showed that dac was found to increase HSPC-NK cell expression of NKp44 and KIRs, whereas NKG2D decreased. This correlated with a more activated phenotype, as HSPC-NK expressed higher levels of perforin, TRAIL, and TNF.l The report of increased expression of KIRs with dac in this article and others is consistent with original reports demonstrating KIR expression is regulated by DNA methylation and can be increased following dac treatment. Finally, dac treatment was associated with an increased number of HSPC-NK cells in the bone marrow of mice with AML, suggesting dac enhanced HSPC-NK cell trafficking to this compartment. Together, these findings suggest that in this model, dac could function synergistically with adoptively transferred HSPC-NK cells via directly targeting AML cells while simultaneously enhancing NK cell function and bone marrow homing.

Histone deacetylase inhibitors

Histone acetylation plays an important role in regulating chromatin accessibility and gene expression. This process is regulated by histone deacetylases (HDACs) and histone acetyltransferases (HATs). The mechanisms leading to dysregulated HDAC and HAT function appear to be dichotomous, as HDACs are rarely mutated but frequently overexpressed in a number of malignancies, whereas loss-of-function mutations in HAT genes such as EP300 are more common. Regardless of the mechanism, loss of normal histone acetylation results in dysregulated gene expression that facilitates the development of cancer. To date, the development of HDAC inhibitors (HDACi) has been much more successful than targeting HATs, likely in part due to HATs serving a tumor suppressor role and commonly undergoing inactivating mutations. There are currently 4 HDACi with FDA approvals for cancer treatment, including vorinostat (inhibits class I, II, and IV HDACs), belinostat (inhibits class I, II, and IV HDACs), panobinostat (inhibits class I, II, and IV HDACs), and romidepsin (inhibits class I HDACs).

There have been numerous reports demonstrating that HDACi increase NK cell–mediated cytotoxicity of tumor cells, but most of this work has focused on the ability of HDACi to increase tumor cell expression of NK cell activating ligands such as MICA and MICB. Less information is available on the direct effects of HDACi on NK cell development and function. Early reports investigating the regulation of KIR expression in NK cells suggested a role for histone acetylation as H4K8 acetylation of KIR genes was increased in NK cells compared to progenitor populations. Additional investigation of the direct effects of HDACi on NK cell function has shown mixed results. One study utilizing vorinostat and valproic acid (inhibits class I and II HDACs) showed that both agents inhibited the cytotoxic function of IL-2-activated NK cells. This effect was associated with decreased NF-κB activation and reduced expression of NKp46 and NKp30. Another study using sodium butyrate, trichostatin A, and sodium valproate similarly found that these agents decreased nuclear localization of NF-κB p50 following cytokine stimulation and decreased expression of NKG2D, NKp44, NKp46, and CD25. These effects were associated with decreased NK cell IFNγ production and natural cytotoxicity. Valproic acid was also found to inhibit NKG2D expression via decreased STAT3 phosphorylation. In contrast, entinostat (inhibits class I HDACs) was found to enhance NK cell natural cytotoxicity and antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity of tumor cells and increased expression of NKG2D, NKp30, DNAM-1, and granzyme B. A follow-up study found that entinostat enhanced NK cell function via increased expression of IFIT1 that in turn increased activation of STING and STAT4 pathways. The different observations with these molecules are likely related, at least in part, to differential effects on specific HDAC molecules. Notably, much of this work has been done with HDACi that are not currently in clinical use. Moving forward, it will be important to investigate the effects of the FDA-approved HDACi on both NK cell development and function, with a particular focus on how pan-HDACi (vorinostat, belinostat, and panobinostat) may differentially impact NK cells compared to more selective HDACi such as romidepsin and eitnostat (inhibit class I HDACs). For a more thorough review of differences in the mechanism of various HDACi, please see Table 1 and recent in-depth reviews.

IDH1/2 inhibitors

Isocitrate dehydrogenase (IDH) 1/2 are metabolic enzymes involved in the production of α-ketoglutarate in the tricarboxylic acid cycle. Mutant IDH1/2, often found in hematologic malignancies, including AML, produce the alternative metabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate, which subsequently inhibits ten-eleven translocation (TET) enzymes, resulting in inhibition of active DNA methylation. Ivosidenib, an IDH1 inhibitor, was first approved for the treatment of relapsed refractory AML in 2018 and subsequently was approved for frontline therapy in elderly patients or those unable to tolerate standard induction chemotherapy. More recently, based on the results of the AGILE trial, which showed a significantly improved median overall survival in patients with AML treated with aza plus ivosidenib compared to aza alone, this combination received FDA approval for frontline therapy in patients unable to receive induction chemotherapy. The IDH2 inhibitor enasidenib was approved for relapsed refractory AML in 2017, and trials investigating its potential in combination with aza in the frontline treatment setting are ongoing. Unfortunately, to date, most studies investigating the immune modulatory effects of IDH1/2 inhibitors have focused on their ability to target tumor cells, and there have not been any dedicated studies of their impact on NK cell development or function. However, given that TET enzymes are inhibited downstream of mutant IDH1/2 and serve to regulate DNA methylation, NK cell expression of CD16 and KIR will likely be impacted. One study has suggested the possibility for TET enzymes to regulate NK cell KIR expression. This group found that KIR promoters in CD56dim and CD94bright NK cells were hypomethylated compared to CD56bright NK cells, and this correlated with increased KIR2DL1 and KIR3DL1 expression. Using ascorbic acid as a means of activating TET enzymes, they showed that treatment of CD56bright NK cells with ascorbic acid resulted in increased KIR expression and KIR promoter demethylation. This was also associated with enrichment of TET2, TET3, and RUNX3 binding within KIR promoters. Overexpression of TET3 and RUNX1 in CD56bright NK cells promoted increased KIR expression. Together, these results suggest that TET enzymes play an important role in NK cell acquisition of KIR expression and that this can be modified pharmacologically. Moving forward, it will be important to better understand how this pathway and the drugs targeting mutant IDH1/2 impact NK cell antileukemic function.

EZH2 inhibitors

Enhancer of zeste homolog 2 (EZH2) is a catalytic subunit of the polycomb repressor complex involved in histone methylation that is frequently upregulated in cancer. Deletion of EZH2 increases the number and frequency of NK lineage cells and enhances killing capacity against tumor targets. This increase was found to be due to both promotion of NK cell development as well as attenuation of NK cell apoptosis. The NK cells of EZH2-null mice were also found to express higher levels of NKG2D and CD122 (IL-15Rβ). In patients, genome sequencing projects identified a high frequency of somatic gain-of-function mutations involving the Y646 codon of EZH2 in FL. EZH2 mutations in FL appear to be early/clonal events and remain relatively stable with disease progression, leading to significant interest in testing the clinical activity of EZH2 inhibitors in FL. Results of a phase 2 trial of tazemetostat in relapsed/refractory FL showed an objective response rate of 69% and 35% in EZH2-mutated and wild-type patients, respectively. This led to the FDA approval of tazemetostat for relapsed/refractory FL regardless of EZH2 mutation status. EZH2 was also found to be upregulated in ENKTL cells, which promoted profound genome-wide DNA hypermethylation. Notably, treatment of ENKTL cells with tazemetostat did not result in differentiation of the malignant NK cells or provide any additional survival benefit on top of HMA therapy in vivo. Overall, EZH2 is a promising clinical target in hematologic malignancies, although the effect of its inhibition in modulating the effector NK cell pool and their downstream clinical effects needs further study.

Emerging therapeutics

The promising results seen with the epigenetic therapies described above have fueled research efforts to design and test compounds for other epigenetic targets, including both epigenomic writers and readers. Below, we briefly summarize current drug classes undergoing preclinical testing that have been found to modify NK cell activity.

Brd4 is an epigenetic reader and has become an attractive target for cancer therapy over the past decade. Currently, there are numerous agents in various stages of preclinical and clinical development. There have been several reports demonstrating that Brd4 regulates inflammation and T-cell function. Using the Brd4 inhibitor JQ1, it has been shown that Brd4 blockade reduces IFNγ messenger RNA and protein levels following cytokine stimulation of NK cells. Using a genetic approach, knockdown of Brd4 and Brd2 impaired NK cell IFNγ production, whereas only Brd4 knockdown inhibited NK cell cytotoxicity. JQ1 has been shown to inhibit both BRD2 and BRD4, suggesting that differential target specificity of BRD4 inhibitors may produce varying effects on NK cells. The ability of Brd4 inhibitors to block NK cell cytokine production and cytotoxicity raises the potential for these drugs to have a negative impact on NK cell–mediated tumor control, but this needs to be empirically determined. Interestingly, Brd4 inhibitors are known to suppress Myc transcription, and there is evidence that Myc regulates NK cell function. In addition to JQ1, there are numerous additional agents in preclinical testing targeting BRD4 and other targets involved in epigenetic regulation. Due to their early stages of developmental testing, the effects of many of these agents on NK cells have not been explored and are thus beyond the scope of this review.

Concluding remarks and future directions

Overall, studies assessing the effects of epigenetic therapy have largely focused on their direct tumor effects or modulation of NK cell activating ligands rather than their direct effects on NK cell development and function. In the age of immunotherapy, better understanding of how these therapeutic approaches modulate the immune response is a critical need. Given the relative ease of access to circulating NK cells in hematologic malignancies over the course of treatment, studying the effects of epigenetic therapy on this population over time should be an area of future investigation. Furthermore, hematologic malignancies often directly involve the bone marrow, which possesses its own tissue-resident NK cell population. Therefore, whenever possible, studies should extend their investigation of NK cells to the marrow microenvironment. The effect of epigenetic therapies on NK cell development and function is still an active area of investigation that has the potential to further our understanding of both tumor immunology and NK cell biology. More work in this area will hopefully lead to the development of new therapeutic approaches and improved patient outcomes.
