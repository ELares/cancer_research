# Preclinical Characterization of XB002, an Anti-Tissue Factor Antibody-Drug Conjugate for the Treatment of Solid Tumors.

## Abstract

Tissue factor (TF) is overexpressed in various cancers and is typically associated with poor clinical outcomes. XB002 is an anti-TF antibody-drug conjugate designed to selectively deliver a cytotoxic payload to TF-expressing tumors while minimizing TF-related adverse events, particularly bleeding. The conjugate consists of a zovodotin linker-payload attached to a mAb (clone 25A3) that binds TF with high affinity (KD = 0.86 nmol/L). In vitro coagulation assays confirmed that 25A3 does not interfere with the clotting cascade; even at a concentration of 100 nmol/L, it did not affect the activation of coagulation factor X or thrombin generation. XB002 demonstrated efficient internalization in TF-expressing cancer cell lines, exhibiting potent cytotoxicity at subnanomolar concentrations. In the HPAF-II xenograft model, a regimen of XB002 (1.5 mg/kg, i.v.) administered once weekly for two weeks achieved complete tumor regression, with no detectable tumor growth up to five weeks after the second dose. In murine patient-derived xenograft models, a single dose of XB002 (10 mg/kg, i.v.) inhibited tumor growth across multiple cancer models, including bladder, cervical, gastric, head and neck squamous cell carcinoma, and non-small cell lung cancers. Remarkably, complete tumor regression was observed in the cervical cancer and head and neck squamous cell carcinoma models within 30 days of treatment. In nonhuman primate studies, XB002 demonstrated favorable pharmacokinetics with exposure in the desired therapeutic range and no signs of bleeding or neutropenia. Collectively, these data highlight XB002's broad-spectrum antitumor activity and strongly support its further clinical development.

Introduction

Tissue factor (TF, also known as thromboplastin, coagulation factor III, or CD142) is a 47-kDa transmembrane glycoprotein that plays an important role in coagulation. Abnormal expression of TF outside subendothelial tissues is reported to be associated with cancer. TF is overexpressed in cervical, ovarian, head and neck, bladder, lung, urogenital, gastrointestinal, breast, and other solid tumors, and its expression is associated with poor outcomes, including shorter overall survival and a higher incidence of metastasis. This association of TF expression and cancer development and poor prognosis is believed to be related to the oncogenic activity of TF, including the promotion of angiogenesis, cancer cell proliferation and survival, cancer stem-like cell maintenance, and tumor metastasis.

TF is an appealing target for developing antibody–drug conjugates (ADC) for cancer treatment. Upon binding to TF-expressing cells, the TF–antibody complex is internalized and directed to the lysosome, where the payload is cleaved and released from the ADC. This release disrupts essential cellular processes within cancer cells, leading to their targeted destruction.

Key considerations in the development of anti-TF ADCs include minimizing bleeding-related adverse events, given TF’s critical role in coagulation, as well as optimizing payload selection. TF is normally expressed on subendothelial cells of blood vessels, where upon tissue injury and exposure to circulating blood, it binds to factor VII (FVII), converting it to its active form, FVIIa. The TF/FVIIa complex triggers the extrinsic coagulation pathway, initiating a proteolytic activation cascade that ultimately leads to clot formation.

Anti-TF ADCs in various stages of development include tisotumab vedotin, which has been approved for previously treated advanced cervical cancer, and MRG004A, which is being evaluated in a number of advanced solid tumors. Both ADCs use the microtubule polymerization inhibitor monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) as the payload. Clinical studies have reported an association of tisotumab vedotin with bleeding risk (including 30% incidence of treatment-related epistaxis), as well as an association of vedotin, the linker–MMAE payload, with other toxicities, including neutropenia, peripheral neuropathy, and anemia. There is a need for an anti-TF ADC with a favorable benefit-to-risk profile that does not interfere with the coagulation cascade.

XB002 is an anti-TF ADC that consists of an anti-TF mAb (clone 25A3) with high affinity to TF conjugated at the cysteine residues to the protease-cleavable drug-linker zovodotin. A discovery campaign selected 25A3 for further development based on its affinity, lack of impact on coagulation, and internalization efficiency. Zovodotin contains the auristatin payload ZD02044, and because of its chemical structure and properties, the zovodotin linker–payload has the potential to improve the benefit-to-risk profile of ADCs compared with other auristatin-based linker–payloads. In this report, we present the preclinical characterization of XB002 in cell line, murine, and nonhuman primate models.

Materials and Methods

Cell lines

A431 (human, skin), HCT-116 (human, colon), HPAF-II (human, pancreas), MDA-MB-231 (human, breast), and RF/6A (monkey, eye) cells were obtained from ATCC and maintained as recommended at 5% CO2 at 37°C. Cell lines were authenticated using morphology, karyotyping, and PCR-based methods to eliminate intra- and interspecies contamination. Cells used in assays did not exceed 15 passages in culture. Short tandem repeat profiling was conducted, whereas for interspecies contamination cytochrome oxidase 1 barcoding was used (ATCC) for intraspecies contamination of human and mouse cells. Mycoplasma testing was performed using PCR.

Anti-TF mAb 25A3 and XB002 preparation

Details of the preparation, isolation, and characterization of the antibodies used for the preparation of XB002 were previously reported. Human antibodies against human TF were isolated from a full-length human IgG library using an in vitro yeast selection system. TF-binding mAbs were isolated through multiple rounds of enrichment, incubating biotinylated TF-His monomers at different concentrations with antibody-expressing yeast cells, followed by magnetic bead selection (Miltenyi Biotech) and FACS on a FACSAria II cell sorter (BD Biosciences) using streptavidin secondary reagents. Following enrichment, yeast clones were sequenced and used in light- and heavy-chain affinity maturation to develop 25A3.

XB002 was prepared by conjugating 25A3 to the linker–payload, zovodotin, via stochastic partial-reduction interchain cysteine chemistry. Zovodotin comprises a protease-sensitive valine–citrulline dipeptide sequence coupled to an N-acyl sulfonamide auristatin payload (ZD02044) that inhibits cell division by blocking tubulin polymerization. To prepare 25A3-based ADCs, 25A3 in PBS, pH 7.4 was reduced for 2 hours with 2 to 2.5 mol/L equivalents of tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine at 37°C. After cooling to room temperature, the partially reduced antibody was conjugated to a molar excess of maleimidocaproyl-valine-citrulline-p-aminobenzoyloxycarbonyl-MMAE (MC-vc-PAB-MMAE; vedotin) or maleimidotrioxadodecanoyl-valine-citrulline-ZD02044 (MT-vc-ZD02044; zovodotin) for 1 hour, followed by quenching with N-acetyl-L-cysteine, to generate 25A3–vedotin or XB002, respectively. The reaction was dialyzed against PBS to remove the small–molecular weight reagents.

XB002 characterization

The drug-to-antibody ratio for XB002 was calculated using peak areas obtained from reduced reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). Size-exclusion chromatography assessed the proportion of monomeric XB002. Payload release was confirmed through a cathepsin B cleavage assay, followed by HPLC and LC/MS analysis. Additionally, the relative hydrophobicity of zovodotin compared with vedotin was characterized via HPLC (see Supplementary Materials).

ELISA

Serial dilutions of 25A3, XB002, and corresponding reference standards were added to microtiter plates coated with TF. After incubation, the plates were washed and incubated with peroxidase AffiniPure Goat Anti-Human IgG, Fc Fragment Specific (Jackson ImmunoResearch). Following another wash, horseradish peroxidase substrate (SuperSignal ELISA Pico Chemiluminescent substrate, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was added, and luminescence was measured using a plate reader spectrophotometer. The concentration achieving 50% of maximal binding (EC50) and relative binding affinity to the reference standard were determined from quadruplicate independent measurements.

Biacore measurements

According to the supplier’s protocol, 25A3 was captured via anti–human IgG (Fc) covalently coupled to a CM5 chip (GE Healthcare) using an amine coupling kit (GE Healthcare). Biacore measurements were performed on a Biacore X100 instrument (GE Healthcare) at 25°C using a five-point titration of human or cynomolgus monkey TF (extracellular domain) in triplicate and analyzed using the recommended procedures (GE Healthcare). Kinetic data were analyzed and fitted globally using a 1:1 Langmuir binding model with equilibrium dissociation constant (KD) determined.

Cell-based antibody binding assays using FACS

Approximately 1.2 × 105 human HCT-116, A431 and rhesus monkey RF/6A cells were incubated for 2 hours on ice with a 12-point 1:3 dilution titration of 25A3 starting at 250 or 100 nmol/L. Following two washes, cells labeled with 25A3 were incubated for 30 minutes on ice with 150 nmol/L of PE F(ab’)2 fragment goat anti–human IgG, Fc-gamma fragment specific (Jackson ImmunoResearch). Following two additional washes and staining of dead cells with TO-PRO-3 Iodide (Thermo Fisher Scientific), samples were analyzed on a CytoFLEX (Beckman Coulter) flow cytometer. The EC50 was determined using a four-parameter binding model in Prism (GraphPad) based on plots of the median fluorescence intensities of each dilution.

Coagulation cascade assays

For the activated coagulation factor X (FXa) conversion assay, MDA-MB-231 cells (5 × 104) were plated and incubated overnight at 37°C in 96-well plates (Greiner Bio-One). Following media removal, 200 nmol/L FX in HEPES buffer with 1.5 mmol/L CaCl2 was added, along with titrated 25A3, a positive antibody control, or a no-antibody control. The positive control was an anti-TF antibody (clone TF-011; Genmab), which is an FVII competitor. After a 15-minute incubation at 37°C, cells were treated with FVIIa (final concentration 20 nmol/L), followed by an additional 5-minute incubation at 37°C. The reaction was then quenched with EDTA in a black 96-well plate, and the generated FXa was measured with 50 μmol/L of SN-7 6-amino-1-naphthalenesulfonamide–based fluorogenic substrate (Haematologic Technologies). The percentage of FXa generated in the presence of 25A3 relative to a nonantibody control was reported.

In the thrombin generation assay, relipidated TF was added to a 96-well plate, followed by a mixture of 25A3 and normal pooled plasma (NPP). The 25A3/NPP mixture consisted of diluted 25A3 combined with NPP collected in 11 mmol/L citrate and supplemented with 100 μg/mL corn trypsin inhibitor. TF-011 served as a positive control. Thrombin generation was initiated by adding calcium and a thrombin-specific substrate. The assay was conducted using a calibrated automated thrombogram instrument (Diagnostica Stago SAS), with STAGO software reporting Peak IIa, defined as the highest thrombin concentration (nmol/L) generated on the thrombin generation curve, relative to plasma without the antibody.

TF-positive A431 cells were incubated on ice with a titration of 25A3, IgG1 isotype negative control, or TF-011 for 1 hour for the FVII binding competition assay. FVII-Fc conjugated to A488 was then added (final concentration 20 nmol/L), followed by another 1-hour incubation. Cells were washed, stained with a viability dye, and analyzed by flow cytometry. Cytotoxicity assays

A431 cells were plated in 384-well plates (Greiner Bio-One) at 1.2 × 103 cells/well and treated with serially diluted XB002, 25A3–vedotin, or non-TF binding control for 3 days at 37°C followed by lysis in CellTiter-Glo 2.0 assay reagent (Promega) diluted with nine parts Ultrapure DNase/RNase-free distilled water. CellTiter-Glo 2.0 luminescence was measured using an EnVision Plate reader (PerkinElmer). IC50 was determined in quadruplicate independent measurements.

Internalization assays

The internalization of XB002 and its parental antibody 25A3 was assessed in the TF-expressing A431 tumor cell line. Both XB002 and 25A3 were conjugated with the fluorescent dye Alexa Fluor 647 (AF647) to enable real-time monitoring of their binding and uptake. A431 cells were pretreated with an Fc receptor blocker and then incubated with AF647-labeled proteins at 4°C to facilitate binding. After washing away excess labeling reagents, internalization was triggered by transferring the cells to live-cell imaging conditions (37°C, 5% CO2, 70% humidity) within the Opera Phenix Plus confocal fluorescent imager. The translocation of surface-bound proteins to the intracellular compartment was tracked through a series of time-lapse images. Fluorescence associated with XB002-AF647 and 25A3-AF647 within the cytoplasm was quantified using Harmony 5.0 image analysis software (PerkinElmer), allowing for the determination of internalization kinetics over time.

Murine tumor models

Three HPAF-II xenograft studies were performed: (i) a study comparing XB002 with 25A3–vedotin, (ii) an XB002 dose–response study, and (iii) an XB002 late-intervention study. The studies were performed by Charles River Discovery Services in compliance with Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee procedures. For all studies, HPAF-II pancreatic carcinoma cells (5 × 106 cells in 100 μL PBS) were implanted subcutaneously in the right flank of female athymic nude mice (Crl:NU(NCr)-Foxn1nu, Charles River Laboratories). Animals were randomized (10 mice per arm) and treated when the average tumor size was 150 to 200/175 to 225/450 to 550 mm3 in the XB002 versus 25A3–vedotin/XB002 dose–response/XB002 late-intervention studies, respectively. In a study that compared XB002 with 25A3–vedotin, stock antibody solutions were diluted with PBS on the day of dosing to 0.15 mg/mL. Mice were intravenously treated with 1.5 mg/kg XB002, 1.5 mg/kg 25A3–vedotin, respective isotype controls, or vehicle (PBS) once weekly for 2 weeks. In the dose–response study, stock XB002 was diluted with PBS on the day of dosing to 1 mg/mL and then further diluted for mice to receive a single intravenous dose of 1.25, 2.5, 5, 7.5, or 10 mg/kg XB002 or vehicle (PBS) at a volume of 10 mL/kg. Animals in the 5 mg/kg group also received a second dose on Day 18, once the average tumor size in the group reached 400 mm3. IHC

The slides were then baked at 56°C to 60°C for 30 minutes, followed by deparaffinization and rehydration. Tissue sections were treated with 3% hydrogen peroxide for 5 minutes to block endogenous peroxidase activity, then rinsed 11 times with distilled water (with the final rinse lasting 5 minutes). Next, the sections were incubated with Rip Tide (Mosaic Laboratories) in a water bath at 95°C to 97°C for 40 minutes for antigen retrieval. After cooling for 10 minutes, the sections were rinsed 10 times with distilled water and 10 times with Splash-T buffer (Mosaic Laboratories).

The tissue sections were subsequently stained with the anti–human TF mouse mAb hTF-1 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) or a negative control reagent (BD Pharmingen), both diluted in antibody diluent buffer (Dako), for 30 minutes at room temperature. Following this, the slides were rinsed twice for 5 minutes each with Splash-T buffer and then incubated with EnVision+ mouse horseradish peroxidase (Dako) for 30 minutes at room temperature. After another two 5-minute rinses with Splash-T buffer, the sections were treated with 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (Dako) for 5 minutes to develop the stain, followed by two additional rinses and a 5-minute soak in distilled water.

Nonhuman primate studies

XB002 and 25A3 conjugated to vedotin were administered to female cynomolgus monkeys (three per treatment group) by intravenous infusion every 3 weeks [days 1 and 22 (a total of two doses)] at doses of 3, 6, 12, and 18 mg/kg (XB002) and 1.5, 3, and 6 mg/kg (25A3–vedotin), followed by a 3-week recovery period in an exploratory toxicology study. Clinical signs, body weights, food intake, ophthalmology, clinical pathology parameters (hematology, clinical chemistry, coagulation, and urinalysis), toxicokinetic (TK) parameters, gross necropsy, organ weights, and histopathology (lungs, gross lesions, and eyes for one animal dosed at 18 mg/kg XB002 with ocular symptoms) were evaluated. TK parameters included maximum concentration (Cmax), total exposure or area under the curve (AUC), and half-life (T1/2) of XB002, as well as intact ADCs (XB002 and 25A3–vedotin) and total antibodies (conjugated and nonconjugated XB002 and 25A3–vedotin). The study was performed by Charles River in compliance with Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee procedures.

XB002 was administered to cynomolgus monkeys via intravenous infusion every 3 weeks (days 1, 22, 43, and 64) at doses of 3, 6, 12, and 15 mg/kg in a 10-week toxicology study. Six male and six female cynomolgus monkeys were evaluated in each treatment group. Animals were also evaluated at 6 weeks following the last injection (day 106) for potential reversibility of any findings. Terminal necroscopies (four animals) were performed on day 65, whereas recovery necroscopy (two animals) was performed on day 106. Mortality, clinical observations, body weights, food consumption, ophthalmology, electrocardiology, clinical pathology parameters (hematology, coagulation, clinical chemistry, and urinalysis), antidrug antibody analysis, TK parameters (intact XB002 and total antibody), organ weights, and macroscopic and microscopic examinations were assessed.

Bioanalytical measurement of intact XB002 and total antibody levels

The concentrations of a total 25A3 antibody (unconjugated, partially, or fully conjugated 25A3) were determined from monkey serum samples (collected immediately after infusion and at 1-, 3-, 8-, 24-, 48-, 168-, 336-, and 504-hour after infusion) using a qualified electrochemiluminescence immunoassay method based on the Meso Scale Discovery platform. The method measured total 25A3 by capturing it with a biotinylated recombinant His-tagged human TF (TF-His) on Meso Scale Discovery streptavidin plates and detection with ruthenium-labeled anti–human IgG. The assay quantifiable range in the neat matrix was 76 to 80,000 ng/mL in an exploratory study and 200 to 80,000 ng/mL in a toxicology study. Intact XB002 ADC concentrations (25A3 conjugated with at least one zovodotin molecule) were determined by a similar method, except that a biotinylated anti-zovodotin antibody was employed as the capture reagent. The concentrations of intact 25A3–vedotin (25A3 conjugated with at least one vedotin molecule) were determined by a similar method, except that a biotinylated anti-vedotin antibody was employed as the capture reagent. The sensitivities of intact ADC and corresponding total antibody methods were similar.

Statistical analyses

Statistical analyses were performed using Prism (GraphPad) software (version 5.01) unless specified otherwise. Results are summarized as mean with 95% confidence interval (CI), SD, or SEM, as indicated. Cellular experiments were performed with a minimum of triplicate determinations; EC50 and IC50 were estimated using a four-parameter binding model in Prism. The Kruskal–Wallis Dunn or Mann–Whitney U test was used for comparisons of in vivo tumor growth inhibition. Cmax, AUC, and T1/2 were estimated based on concentration–time data through a noncompartment analysis approach using Phoenix WinNonlin version 8. For the assessment of intact and total antibodies, the concentrations of the analytes were calculated by interpolation of the sample signals from the respective standard curves using 5PL curve fitting and Prism with 1/Y2 weighting.

Results

Characterization of anti-TF mAb 25A3

In binding assays, 25A3 was found to bind with high affinity to the extracellular domain of recombinant human and cynomolgus monkey TF [KD of 0.86 nmol/L (SD 0.03 nmol/L) and 0.65 nmol/L (SD 0.02 nmol/L), respectively; Supplementary Fig. S1]. The target specificity of 25A3 on cancer cells was also assessed in two cell lines with different levels of TF expression: HCT-116 human colorectal carcinoma (20,000 copies of TF/cell) and A431 human squamous cell carcinoma (200,000 copies of TF/cell). 25A3 was found to have subnanomolar affinity to TF in both cell lines: HCT-116 (EC50 = 0.17 nmol/L) and A431 (EC50 = 0.61 nmol/L) cells (Supplementary Fig. S2). As the protein sequences of cynomolgus monkey (Macaca fascicularis) and rhesus monkey (Macaca mulatta) TF are identical, binding of 25A3 was also confirmed on the rhesus monkey RF/6A cell line (EC50 = 0.26 nmol/L).

25A3 showed marginal interference of TF binding to FVII with regard to the impact on coagulation (Supplementary Table S1). At antibody concentrations of 250 nmol/L, binding of TF to FVII was 87% when incubated with 25A3 and 99% with the IgG1 isotype control, but only 2% when incubated with TF-specific antibody TF-011. 25A3 was found to have no effects on the coagulation pathway as evaluated using an FXa conversion assay, FVII binding competition on A431 cells, and a thrombin generation assay. At concentrations of up to 100 nmol/L, 25A3 showed no effect on FX conversion to FXa or on the thrombin generation pathway (Fig. 1), whereas TF-011 at 100 nmol/L led to a >50% decrease in FXa conversion and almost 100% inhibition of thrombin generation. Taken together, these data indicate that 25A3 does not interfere with the coagulation pathway.

Additional characterizations of 25A3 have been reported. The data on 25A3 reported here confirm and expand on these prior findings.

Design of XB002

XB002, which was generated by conjugating 25A3 to the linker–payload, zovodotin (Fig. 2), had a DAR of ∼3.8 as determined from the calculation of peak areas from the reduced reverse-phase HPLC (Supplementary Fig. S3A). Size-exclusion chromatography of the XB002 molecule indicated that the predominant species was monomeric, with minimal high and low molecular weight species observed (Supplementary Fig. S3B). Treatment of XB002 with cathepsin B resulted in the release of the free payload ZD02044, as confirmed by RP-HPLC and LC/MS analysis (Supplementary Fig. S4). In the RP-HPLC analysis of the linker–payloads zovodotin and vedotin, zovodotin was eluted earlier than vedotin, suggesting that zovodotin is less hydrophobic compared with vedotin (Supplementary Fig. S5).

Characterizing the preclinical pharmacology of XB002

The in vitro cytotoxicity of XB002 and 25A3 conjugated to vedotin (25A3–vedotin) was assessed to compare the activity of zovodotin and vedotin. XB002 showed potent cytotoxic activity against A431 cells [IC50 = 56 pmol/L (95% CI, 48–65)], which was consistent with the cytotoxic activity of 25A3–vedotin [IC50 = 82 pmol/L (95% CI, 77–88; Fig. 3)]. In these experiments, the isotype control ADCs did not show any effect on cell viability, confirming that binding to TF is required for activity.

The internalization of fluorescently labeled XB002 and 25A3 was tracked over time in A431 cells. Both conjugates exhibited translocation from the cell surface to the intracellular space, with XB002-AF647 showing a punctate endosomal distribution (Supplementary Fig. S6). The internalization kinetics of XB002 and 25A3 were similar, suggesting that the inclusion of the zovodotin linker–payload had a minimal effect on intracellular trafficking dynamics.

The in vivo antitumor efficacy of XB002 was evaluated in a human pancreatic HPAF-II cell line–derived xenograft model in athymic nude mice. A comparison of XB002 (1.5 mg/kg) with 25A3–vedotin (1.5 mg/kg), both given once weekly for 2 weeks, showed lower mean tumor volumes over time with XB002 (Fig. 4A). Relative to the vehicle control, the percent tumor growth delay was 135% for both anti-TF ADCs, with no tumor growth delay for the isotype control ADCs. The mean tumor volumes when animals were sacrificed at the end of study (Day 42) were 126 mm3 (n = 9) for XB002 and 864 mm3 (n = 7) for 25A3–vedotin. Furthermore, near complete tumor regression was observed with XB002 a week after the second dose. At 5 weeks after second dose, XB002 detected no tumor growth relative to baseline. In contrast, tumor growth was observed 2 weeks after the second dose of 25A3–vedotin. These results indicate that XB002, the zovodotin-based ADC, is more efficacious than the vedotin-based ADC in this model.

In a single dose response study in HPAF-II xenografts with XB002, a delay in tumor growth was observed with the 5, 7.5, and 10 mg/kg dose relative to the vehicle, whereas at the lower doses of 1.25 and 2.5 mg/kg, the time course of tumor growth was similar to that of the vehicle (Fig. 4B). Durable responses were seen with the 10 mg/kg even 6 weeks after the single dose. All doses were well tolerated with no measurable body weight loss.

A late-intervention study using the HPAF-II xenograft model was conducted to evaluate the effect of XB002 on established tumors as large as 500 mm3. A single dose of 7.5 or 10 mg/kg XB002 significantly delayed tumor growth at Day 15 compared with the vehicle, with calculated tumor growth inhibition of 46% and 70%, respectively (Fig. 4C). There was a significant difference in the MTV for vehicle compared with either the 7.5 mg/kg (P < 0.05) or the 10 mg/kg (P < 0.01) dose group on day 15 (Fig. 4C). These results provide further evidence of the antitumor activity of XB002 in both large and small tumors.

XB002-mediated tumor growth inhibition was also evaluated in multiple PDX models. PDX models representing gastric, cervical, bladder, HNSCC, and NSCLC were selected for evaluation, with the H-score for TF expression ranging from 105 to 200. A single 10 mg/kg dose of XB002 inhibited tumor growth compared with the vehicle in all PDX tumor models evaluated with complete regression observed in cervical and HNSCC tumor models (Fig. 5).

Tolerability and toxicology of XB002

In an exploratory toxicology study, XB002 and 25A3–vedotin were administered on days 1 and 22, with a 3-week recovery period. Higher exposure was observed for XB002 compared with similar doses of 25A3–vedotin (Supplementary Table S2). Grade 3 neutropenia (<1,000 cells/μL) occurred in two of the three primates receiving 6 mg/kg 25A3–vedotin, whereas no neutropenia was observed in any of the XB002-treated primates at any dose (Supplementary Fig. S7). Skin toxicities were also noted in primates administered 6 mg/kg 25A3–vedotin and 18 mg/kg XB002. Marked ocular toxicity was observed in one primate administered 18 mg/kg XB002.

XB002 was administered to cynomolgus monkeys on days 1, 22, 43, and 64, followed by a 6-week recovery period in a subsequent 10-week toxicology study. XB002 was tolerated up to 12 mg/kg/dose, with ocular toxicity and adverse secondary clinical signs observed with the 15 mg/kg dose, leading to dose interruption in 1/6 primates. There were no XB002-related effects on body weight (Supplementary Fig. S8), change in body weight, or clotting time (prothrombin time and activated partial thromboplastin time). Reduced appetite was transiently observed in all males at a dose of 15 mg/kg from days 14 to 34. There was no evidence of hemorrhage neutropenia or cumulative neutrophil decreases after multiple dosing. At the terminal necropsy, XB002-related histopathological findings were noted in the eyelid, eyes, and lungs, predominantly at the ≥6 mg/kg dose, and with greater incidence and/or severity at the ≥12 mg/kg dose. Findings in the eyelid included erosion/ulcer of the conjunctival epithelium with hyperplasia and mixed cell inflammation and were associated with secondary findings in the eye (erosion/ulcer of the cornea or conjunctiva, hyperplasia of the conjunctival epithelium, epithelial atrophy of the cornea, pigmentation of the corneal epithelium, and mixed cell inflammation). Lung findings included interstitial inflammation, alveolar macrophages, and/or squamous metaplasia. The bone marrow was not identified as a target organ of XB002.

The TK profile of intact XB002 overlapped with the total mAb (intact XB002 or 25A3 without payload) profile, as shown in Supplementary Fig. S9. No gender differences in the pharmacokinetics (PK) or evidence of XB002 accumulation were observed. The observed nonlinear PK at low doses was likely due to target-mediated drug disposition.

Discussion

XB002 is an ADC developed to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of TF-targeting ADCs in oncology. Key design objectives included: (i) selecting a mAb that does not disrupt the coagulation cascade, thereby minimizing bleeding risks; (ii) optimizing the ADC’s tolerability and PK profile to ensure adequate exposure; and (iii) increasing antitumor potency by utilizing a payload specifically chosen for its enhanced cytotoxic activity against cancer cells (Fig. 6).

XB002 leverages a human anti-TF mAb (25A3) with high affinity binding to both human and cynomolgus monkey TF. Importantly, 25A3 demonstrated no interference with the coagulation process, as confirmed by assays evaluating FVII binding competition, FXa conversion, and thrombin generation. The safety profile was further validated in nonhuman primate studies, in which treatment with XB002 did not impact clotting times or cause bleeding, supporting its potential for safe clinical use.

Another major issue in the development of anti-TF ADCs is the occurrence of adverse events associated with the ADC payload, in addition to the potential to interfere with the coagulation pathway. There are currently two anti-TF ADCs approved or under investigation in addition to XB002, tisotumab vedotin and MRG004A, both of which use an MMAE payload. Synthetic auristatin payloads, including MMAE, have been associated with adverse events, such as neutropenia, peripheral neuropathy, anemia, and ocular toxicities. Treatment-related adverse events ≥ grade 3 reported with tisotumab vedotin in a phase II trial included neutropenia and peripheral neuropathy, whereas bleeding and ocular toxicity treatment-related adverse events of any grade occurred in 39% and 54% of patients, respectively.

The use of the zovodotin linker–payload with XB002 has the potential for improved tolerability compared with a MMAE-based linker–payload because of its chemical structure and properties. ADC hydrophobicity accelerates clearance and reduces exposure; the RP-HPLC analysis suggested that the zovodotin linker–payload is less hydrophobic than the vedotin linker–payload, which potentially produces a less hydrophobic ADC molecule overall, with reduced clearance and increased exposure. In primate studies, higher exposure was observed for XB002 compared with similar doses of 25A3–vedotin. Furthermore, grade 3 neutropenia was observed in 25A3–vedotin–treated animals but not in XB002-treated animals. Serious ocular toxicities were noted only at the highest XB002 dose and may be due in part to TF expression in the ocular epithelium, as has been demonstrated with tisotumab vedotin. Taken together, these data indicate that the use of zovodotin as the linker–payload in XB002 improved exposure and the toxicology profile compared with the use of a vedotin as the linker–payload.

XB002 demonstrated superior potency compared with the 25A3-vedotin conjugate in the HPAF-II cell line-derived xenograft model. Notably, XB002 treatment achieved significant tumor growth inhibition even in larger tumors within this aggressive model. Furthermore, XB002 induced significant tumor growth inhibition, with some cases showing complete regression in PDX models representing various solid tumors. The use of zovodotin as the linker–payload enhanced XB002’s antitumor activity relative to the vedotin linker–payload in the HPAF-II xenograft model. This increased efficacy may result from the zovodotin payload, prolonged exposure, or a combination of both factors, although further studies are required to clarify their relative contributions.

Although the TF-targeting ADC tisotumab vedotin (TF-011–vedotin) was not included as a control for the tumor growth inhibition analyses in the present study, data from previous studies suggest that the antitumor activity of 25A3–vedotin is consistent with that of TF-011–vedotin. 25A-vedotin, the highly related parental ADC of 25A3, displayed similar tumor growth inhibition to that of TF-011–vedotin in CDX and PDX models. In subsequent CDX models, the antitumor activities of 25A-vedotin and 25A3–vedotin were deemed equivalent. Therefore, the use of zovodotin versus vedotin can have a greater impact on the antitumor activity of TF-targeting ADCs compared with the use of 25A3 versus TF-011. However, the use of 25A3 versus TF-011 may translate into a safety benefit because of the lack of interference of 25A3 with the coagulation cascade.

In summary, XB002 is an anti-TF ADC with potent activity in inhibiting cancer cell growth in vitro and in various in vivo human tumor murine xenograft models, with marginal effects on the coagulation pathway. These efficacy data and the potential of the zovodotin linker–payload to provide improved tolerability and exposure compared with vedotin support the evaluation of XB002 in clinical studies; a phase 1 study of XB002 in patients with advanced solid tumors (JEWEL-101; NCT04925284) is ongoing.
