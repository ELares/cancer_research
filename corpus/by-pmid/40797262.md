# Nonhistone lactylation: A hub for tumour metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic regulation.

## Abstract

Recent research has shifted the perception of lactate from being merely a byproduct of metabolism to being a crucial signalling molecule and epigenetic regulator in tumours. This paper provides a comprehensive review of the mechanisms by which the lactylation of nonhistones contributes to tumorigenesis and tumour development. Lactate accumulates in the tumour microenvironment (TME) due to the Warburg effect and serves as a substrate for the covalent modification of nonhistone proteins (such as p53 and PD-L1) through both enzymatic and nonenzymatic processes. These modifications influence gene expression, signalling pathways, autophagy, and DNA damage repair, ultimately facilitating tumour growth, metastasis, and immune evasion. This review also discusses how the lactylation of nonhistones contributes to the chemotherapy and immunotherapy resistance of tumours by increasing homologous recombination repair, bolstering antioxidant defences, and fostering an immunosuppressive environment. Furthermore, therapeutic strategies targeting key enzymes involved in lactate metabolism (such as LDH and MCTs) and those related to lactylation (such as AARS1 and the SIRT family) are explored. Potential applications of glycolysis inhibitors (e.g., 2-DG), lactate transport inhibitors (e.g., α-CHC), and specific activators of depyrogenase are discussed. Future research should focus on the spatiotemporal dynamics of lactylation and the development of targeted interventions to enhance tumour combination therapies.

Introduction

Lactate plays an important role in human physiological metabolism as an end product of glycolysis. Under normal physiological conditions, when tissues and organs are in a hypoxic environment, such as muscle tissue during strenuous exercise, cells rapidly convert glucose to lactate through the glycolytic pathway and produce a small amount of ATP to satisfy the energy needs of the cell. Moreover, lactate can be used as a raw material for gluconeogenesis and is converted to glucose in the liver, kidneys and other organs to maintain stable blood glucose levels. In addition, lactate is involved in the regulation of acid‒base balance and the maintenance of homeostasis in the internal environment. Under physiological conditions, lactate is involved as a signalling molecule in the regulation of neural activity, lipolysis and energy metabolism. However, in the TME, there is a significant shift in the function of lactate. Tumour cells tend to produce large amounts of lactate from glycolysis even under aerobic conditions, known as the “Warburg effect”. These lactate molecules not only provide biosynthetic raw materials and energy for tumour cells but also promote tumour cell invasion, inhibit immune cell function, and induce angiogenesis by acidifying the microenvironment, creating favourable conditions for malignant tumour progression.

Protein posttranslational modifications (PTMs) are important ways for cells to precisely regulate protein function. Cells alter the structure, activity, localization and interaction of proteins by adding or removing chemical groups, such as those involved in phosphorylation, acetylation, and methylation, to specific amino acid residues of proteins, which in turn regulate physiological processes such as cell proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis. Abnormal alterations in PTMs play a key role in tumour development.

Nonhistone lactylation is a novel PTM mediated by lactate that regulates protein function by covalently binding lysine residues. Nonhistone lactylation involves a variety of key proteins, such as p53, PD-L1, and MRE11, which directly affect tumorigenesis, immune escape, and drug resistance. Histone lactylation occurs mainly on lysine residues of histones H3 and H4, which can alter chromatin conformation and affect gene transcription. However, it is not central to tumour metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic regulation because its modification sites are limited, and tumour regulation involves a complex network of DNA methylation, other modifications of histone proteins, synergistic interactions between transcription factors, and metabolites that weaken the centrality of histone lactonization.

In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive review of the mechanisms underlying lactate metabolism and nonhistone lactylation in cancer. We explore the novel roles of nonhistone lactylation in tumorigenesis, tumour immunity, and tumour resistance. Additionally, we investigated the potential of targeting lactate metabolism and the enzymes involved in lactylation for cancer therapy, which could inspire the development of innovative combined targeted therapies and immunotherapy approaches.

Lactate metabolism in cancer cells

Cancer cells exhibit distinct metabolic characteristics that distinguish them from normal cells, particularly exemplified by the Warburg effect. Even in the presence of sufficient oxygen for oxidative phosphorylation, cancer cells preferentially generate energy through glycolysis followed by lactate fermentation in the cytoplasm. This metabolic shift supports the rapid proliferation of tumour cells and significantly alters the TME. The excess lactate produced is exported, leading to TME acidification, which degrades the extracellular matrix and facilitates tumour invasion and metastasis. Furthermore, the acidic environment suppresses immune cell function, assisting cancer cells in evading the immune response. This transition also facilitates biosynthetic processes essential for the rapid proliferation of cancer cells. During the process of glycolysis, glucose is converted into pyruvate. The majority (85%) of pyruvate is converted into lactate by LDH, even in the presence of sufficient oxygen, which is a manifestation of the Warburg effect. Only a small fraction (5%) of pyruvate enters the mitochondria for the TCA cycle, where ATP is produced. Although the glycolytic pathway can only produce 2 molecules of ATP, rapid ATP production can be achieved in cancer cells because the reaction process does not require the involvement of mitochondria and the activity levels of key enzymes are up-regulated. Moreover, cancer cells can further increase their glycolytic flux through the upregulation of glucose transporter proteins and the activation of signalling pathways to satisfy the energy demands of rapid proliferation. Tumour cells exhibit elevated levels of glycolytic enzymes such as hexokinase, phosphofructokinase, and pyruvate kinase, which promote the conversion of glucose to pyruvate and enhance glycolytic activity. Oncogenes such as MYC and RAS increase glycolysis, whereas tumour suppressors such as p53, which typically inhibit glycolysis, are frequently mutated or inactivated in cancer. Under hypoxic tumour conditions, hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)-1 is stabilized, activating genes related to glycolysis, including those encoding glucose transporter protein 1 and various glycolytic enzymes. HIF-1 also stimulates the expression of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), which converts pyruvate into lactate. The Warburg effect has a significant effect on tumour progression. Increased glycolytic activity results in increased production of lactate, which is transported via monocarboxylic transporter (MCT) proteins, primarily MCT1 and MCT4, into and out of cancer cells. MCT1 is expressed in various tissues, including skeletal muscle, heart, brain, liver, and endothelial cells, and plays a vital role in lactate uptake and export on the basis of the metabolic needs of the cellular and tissue environment. In tumour cells, MCT1 imports lactate to support oxidative metabolism and prevent the accumulation of harmful intracellular lactate. MCT4, on the other hand, is a key lactate exporter whose expression is upregulated in highly glycolytic cells, contributing to acidification of the TME. This acidic environment promotes tumour invasion and metastasis by degrading the extracellular matrix, suppressing immune cell function, and promoting the survival of aggressive, apoptosis-resistant cancer cells.

Nonhistone lactylation

KL-la represents a significant lactylation that occurs within cells. This modification is not limited to histones; various nonhistone proteins, including p53, cGAS, PD-L1, CCNE2, eEF1A2, NCL, NSUN2, and METTL16, can also undergo lactylation. Several conditions can trigger the lactylation of nonhistone proteins. For example, hypoxia exposure induces nonhistone lactylation. Elevated copper levels increase the lactylation of METTL16-K229 by increasing the interaction between the lactylation transferases AARS1 and AARS2 with METTL16. Additionally, a restricted silk/glycine diet activates HIF-1α, which increases glycolysis in tumours, leading to increased lactate levels and increased expression of the acetyltransferase GCN5. This cascade ultimately results in the lactylation of lysine residues 810–813 on PD-L1. Lactate levels are significantly greater in oral squamous cell carcinoma (OSCC) tissues than in normal adjacent (NAT) tissues, with a direct correlation between the degree of malignancy and the extent of lactylation—higher malignancy grades correspond to increased nonhistone lactylation levels. The interaction between ALDH1A3 and PKM2 promotes the tetramerization of PKM2, leading to lactate accumulation in glioma stem cells (GSCs) and subsequent lactylation at the XRCC1 K247 locus. Furthermore, liver cancer stem cells (LCSCs) present significantly elevated mRNA and protein levels of glycolysis-related enzymes, resulting in increased glycolytic activity and increased intracellular lactate content, which contributes to a greater overall level of lactylation in LCSCs than in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells. KL‐la initiates with the conversion of L-lactate to lactate coenzyme A, facilitated by lactate coenzyme A synthetase (ACSS2 and GTPSCS). Lactate coenzyme A is then transferred to lysine residues by lactate-modifying enzymes such as the lysyl acetyltransferases CBP/p300, KAT8, TIP60, and HB01. The proteins identified as erasers of KL-la include HDAC1-3, SIRT2, and SIRT3, which primarily target nonhistone Kla. Additionally, lactate sensors and lactate transferases, specifically alanyl tRNA synthetases (AARS1 and AARS2), have been identified in cells, where they bind directly to lactate and catalyse global lysine lactylation. The role of lactate in AARS1-mediated lactylation involves two steps: first, lactate is activated by ATP in AARS1, forming lactate-AMP, an intermediate that creates reactive lactate‒phosphate bonds while releasing inorganic pyrophosphate. Second, activated lactate covalently attaches to the lysine receptor terminus, simultaneously releasing AMP, which facilitates the lactylation of the substrate protein. KD−la is produced through a noncatalytic reaction between LGSH and protein lysine residues, whereas Kce results from a noncatalytic reaction between methylglyoxal (MGO) and protein lysine residues. Mechanisms of tumour regulation by nonhistone lactylation

Regulation of tumorigenesis and development

Gene expression and metabolic reprogramming

Fang Lin’s group reported that RCC2 has a novel role in recruiting RNA-binding proteins and regulating RNA stability. Specifically, KAT2A mediates RCC2 lactylation at K124, which triggers the recruitment of free SERBP1 via the N-terminal domain of RCC2 and leads to the formation of the initial SERBP1-MAD2L1 complex. SERBP1 directly binds and stabilizes MAD2L1 mRNA, ultimately upregulating MAD2L1 protein expression. Under high-glucose culture conditions, the lactylation of RCC2 activates MAD2L1 expression and promotes the proliferation of breast cancer cells. FBXO33 regulates EMT progression by modulating the polyubiquitination of p53 at K291 and K292. Moreover, the upregulation of FBXO33 in gallbladder cancer (GBC) is driven by transcriptional regulation mediated by Yin Yang-1 (YY1). The lactylation of YY1 at K183 was found to be essential for the transcriptional activation of FBXO33. These findings underscore the role of the lactylation-driven FBXO33‒p53 axis in promoting the invasive metastasis of GBC. A recent study revealed that ABCF1-K430la is significantly expressed in HCC tissues, with patients in the high-expression group being more likely to develop lung metastasis, having shorter overall survival, and having poorer prognoses than those in the low-expression group. K430la of ABCF1 facilitates its nuclear entry, where it binds to the KDM3A promoter and activates the KDM3A–H3K9me2–HIF1A axis, influencing HCC progression and metastasis. Additionally, research has identified tumour-derived lactate as a natural repressor of p53, promoting its lactylation. AARS1 functions as an intracellular lactate sensor and lactate transferase, catalysing the formation of ATP-dependent lactate-AMP intermediates that covalently bind to lysine residues. This process leads to the lactylation of p53 at K120 and K139, impairing its ability to bind to DNA, reducing liquid‒liquid phase separation, and diminishing its transcriptional activity, which contributes to tumorigenesis. Using lactonization modification histology, researchers discovered that lactylation is increased in colorectal cancer (CRC) tissues and is correlated with poor prognosis. KAT8 was identified as a panlactate transferase that promotes CRC progression by modifying the lactylation of the eukaryotic translation elongation factor eEF1A2 at lysine 408, thereby increasing protein translation efficiency. Nucleolin (NCL), which is lactylated by p300 at lysine 477 (K477la), enhances the expression of MAP kinase-activated death domain protein (MADD) through an RNA splicing-dependent mechanism, activating the MAPK signalling pathway and promoting intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma (iCCA) development. These findings led to the elucidation of a new connection between metabolic remodelling and key oncogenic pathways, offering new biomarkers for prognostic assessment and targeting in clinical iCCA. Furthermore, a novel mechanism involving the lactylation of CCNE2 in HCC progression was revealed, where decreased SIRT3 expression promoted the lactylation of the cell cycle protein CCNE2, facilitating HCC cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. For example, lactylation at the AK2-K28 locus leads to energy disturbances and cellular deterioration in HCC, promoting cell proliferation and migration, which contribute to disease progression. In prostate cancer (PC), lactate imported via MCT1 stabilizes HIF1α under normoxic conditions through lactylation, acting as a transcriptional enhancer for KIAA1199. Silencing KIAA1199 impairs angiogenesis and vascular mimicry in PC by disrupting HA-mediated VEGFA signalling, which in turn increases sema3A expression and decreases VE-cadherin and p-EphA2 levels.

Lactate also directly influences malignant behaviours such as the proliferation, migration, and stem cell properties of LCSCs through lactylation. Specifically, the lactylation of ALDOA at K230/322 in LCSCs leads to the dissociation of ALDOA from DDX17, allowing DDX17 to enter the nucleus and promote the expression of SOX2, a marker of stemness, thereby enhancing the stem cell characteristics of LCSCs. Additionally, lactate increased DCBLD1 expression, activating the PPP to facilitate the proliferation and metastasis of cervical cancer cells. DCBLD1 primarily stimulates the PPP by increasing glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) expression and enzyme activity. The mechanism involved increased enrichment of HIF-1α in the DCBLD1 promoter region, increasing DCBLD1 mRNA expression. Additionally, lactate-induced DCBLD1 lactylation stabilized DCBLD1 expression. DCBLD1 is an emulsification substrate, and its major emulsification site is located at K172. DCBLD1 overexpression inhibits G6PD autophagic degradation, activates the PPP, and promotes cervical cancer progression. Additionally, BZW2 promotes the lactylation of IDH3G through glycolysis, thereby promoting LUAD cell malignant behaviours while inhibiting apoptosis. Furthermore, Chen et al. reported that NSUN2, a m5C methyltransferase, induces metabolic reprogramming that enhances glucose metabolism in CRC cells, leading to increased lactate production. Specifically, lactate not only activates the transcription of NSUN2 through the lactylation of H3K18 but also induces the lactylation of NSUN2 at the K356 site. This lactylation facilitates the capture of target RNAs and the m5C modification of ENO1 mRNA. Overall, this positive feedback loop involving NSUN2, YBX1, and m5C-ENO1 further upregulates glycolysis and promotes the occurrence and development of CRC.

Signalling pathway regulation

Zhou’s team reported that the key elements of the Hippo pathway, YAP and TEAD1, possess conserved sites for lactylation. They reported that AARS1 can bind to and facilitate the lactylation of both YAP and TEAD1, which enhances their interaction within the nucleus and activates the expression of downstream genes, thereby promoting the proliferation of gastric cancer cells. Additionally, Lv Ling’s team identified a new mechanism through which lactate, a tumour metabolite, influences the TME and fosters tumour development by regulating the lactylation of the MOESIN protein in Treg cells and increasing TGF-β signalling.

Programmed cell death

Inducing Cuproptosis

Cuproptosis induced by copper overload is a specific cell death pathway that differs from apoptosis, pyroptosis, and necroptosis. How the essential proteins involved in cuproptosis are activated, modified, and operated in the disruption of copper homeostasis remains unclear. According to Fan’s team, FDX1, which is responsible for reducing Cu2+ to a more toxic form, Cu+, is dramatically increased under high-level copper conditions; high copper content promotes METTL16-K229 lactylation, and then lactylated METTL16 increases FDX1 mRNA and protein levels via m6A modification of FDX1 mRNA, which ultimately induces DLAT lipolylation and cuproptosis in gastric carcinoma (GC).

Regulating cellular autophagy

Research indicates that the serine/threonine protein kinase ULK1 is responsible for phosphorylating LDHA at S196, which increases LDHA activity and increases lactate production. This lactate then modifies Vps34, increasing its lipid kinase activity and promoting cellular autophagy and lysosomal degradation. This regulation of autophagy is crucial for maintaining skeletal muscle homeostasis and is also involved in tumour progression.

Mediating tumour drug resistance

DNA damage repair

Recent research has highlighted the c of MRE11, a crucial component in homologous recombination repair. Lactylation at K673 of MRE11 enhances its ability to bind DNA, facilitating DNA end resection and homologous recombination repair, which contributes to the chemoresistance observed in tumour cells. This lactylation at K673 has been recognized as a significant response to DNA damage, illustrating how lactylation serves as a key regulator that connects cancer cell metabolism with homologous recombination repair. These findings shed light on the molecular mechanisms by which lactylation influences homologous recombination, offering new perspectives on cancer treatment and potential strategies for tumour therapy. Additionally, findings from recent studies have underscored the critical role of lactylation in mediating tumour chemoresistance through DNA damage repair. Lactate-induced lactylation of NBS1 enhances homologous recombination (HR)-mediated DNA repair. Specifically, the lactylation of lysine 388 (K388) on NBS1 promotes the assembly of the Mre11-Rad50-NBS1 (MRN) complex and increases the accumulation of repair proteins at sites of DNA double-strand breaks. In addition to MRE11 and NBS1, another important HR protein, RAD51, is lactonated at K73 and K40. Significantly, the RAD51 lactylation level is positively correlated with the CDDP concentration, indicating its potential relationship with CDDP resistance, and RAD51K73la markedly affects the CDDP response by increasing HR repair ability. Furthermore, lactylation has been shown to regulate a novel mechanism of tumour drug resistance. High ALDH1A3 expression activates the glycolytic pathway by interacting with PKM2, a key glycolytic enzyme, thereby enhancing lactate metabolism in tumour cells. The accumulation of lactate leads to an increase in lactylation at the K247 site of the DNA damage repair protein XRCC1; this modification alters the surface charge of XRCC1, increasing its affinity for importin α, a crucial molecule for nuclear transport. As a result, XRCC1 is ectopically transported to the nucleus, which significantly enhances DNA damage repair in glioblastoma, ultimately contributing to the therapeutic resistance of tumour cells to radiotherapy.

Repressing ferroptosis

BRAFV600E-mutant melanomas treated with BRAF inhibitors (BRAFis) and MEK inhibitors (MEKis) almost invariably develop drug resistance, accompanied by restored glucose metabolism. How resumed glycolysis controls acquired resistance remains unknown. You’s team revealed that lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1) lactylation, which is induced by the reaccumulation of lactate in both human and murine BRAFi/MEKi-resistant melanoma cells, selectively drives survival via epigenetic reprogramming. Mechanistically, the lactylation of LSD1 promotes its interaction with Fos-related antigen 1 (FosL1), preventing its degradation by the E3 ligase tripartite-motif-containing protein 21 (TRIM21) and selectively enhancing its genomic enrichment. Lactylated LSD1 codirects gene transcription with FosL1 to repress ferroptosis via interference with transferrin receptor protein 1 (TFRC)-mediated iron uptake. LSD1 inhibition activates ferroptosis, resulting in drastic regression of drug-resistant murine melanoma when combined with immunotherapy.

M6A modification and antioxidants

The lactylation of IGF2BP3 increases its affinity for the mRNA of phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase 2 (PCK2) and the oxidative stress regulator NRF2, leading to increased expression levels of both. This process also supports the m6A methylation of PCK2 and NRF2 mRNAs, bolstering the antioxidant system by promoting cellular serine metabolism and the production of S-adenosylmethionine (SAM). Ultimately, this mechanism contributes to the development of lenvatinib resistance in HCC.

Modulation of tumour immunity

A previous study identified two lactylation sites within the CCCH zinc finger domain of METTL3, demonstrating that the lactylation-METTL3-JAK1-STAT3 regulatory axis can effectively promote the immunosuppressive functions of tumour-infiltrating macrophages (TIMs) at the levels of metabolism, epigenetics, and transcription; additionally, lactyl-APOC2 is induced by the secretion of intracellular lactate from tumour cells and promotes lipolysis in tumour interstitial fluid, thereby leading to Treg accumulation. This study also explored the molecular basis of dietary interventions in cancer treatment, specifically focusing on the effects of a silk/glycine-restricted diet on pMMR/MSS CRC and its microenvironment, revealing a significant role of PD-L1 lactonization in immune evasion. The lactylation of PD-L1 was found to delay its degradation, a process influenced by the silk/glycine-restricted diet, which helps convert tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes into a more effective cytotoxic phenotype. Furthermore, diet is associated with increased major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I molecules on tumour cells and increased T-cell receptor (TCR) diversity and specificity, suggesting that this dietary approach may increase T-cell recognition of tumour antigens and promote specific T-cell responses. The silk/glycine-restricted diet al.so activated HIF-1α, which increased glycolysis in tumours, leading to elevated lactate levels and increased expression of the acetyltransferase GCN5. This cascade ultimately results in the lactylation of lysine residues 810–813 on PD-L1, a negative regulatory mechanism that facilitates immune evasion by enhancing PD-L1 stability through metabolic reprogramming and increasing lactylation, thereby helping tumours resist attacks from CD8 + T cells. Treg cell induction increases antitumour immunity and decreases tumour growth in mice. Mechanistically, lactate modulates Treg cell generation through lactylation of Lys72 in MOESIN, which enhances the interaction of MOESIN with transforming growth factor b (TGF-b) receptor I and downstream SMAD3 signalling. Cotreatment with anti-PD-1 and lactate dehydrogenase inhibitors has a stronger antitumour effect than does treatment with anti-PD-1 alone. Individuals with HCC who respond to anti-PD-1 treatment have lower levels of MOESIN lactylation in Treg cells than nonresponding individuals do. Thus, lactate is an essential small molecule that reinforces Treg cells in the TME through lactylation. (Fig. 2) (Table 1).

Nonhistone lactylation has significant therapeutic potential

An integrative analysis of lactate and proteomics in tumours and adjacent liver tissue identified 9,275 lactylation (Kla) sites, with 9,256 found on nonhistone proteins. These findings indicate that Kla is a widespread modification that plays a significant role in protein synthesis and transcriptional regulation within cells. Kla may influence various cellular processes, including gene expression, signalling pathways, and cell morphology. Research has demonstrated that nonhistone lactylation is crucial for numerous biological functions, such as cancer progression, cardiovascular diseases(CVDs), neurological disorders, and disease resistance. Specifically, lactate is also implicated in various CVDs, including cardiac hypertrophy, myocardial injury, heart failure, and atherosclerosis. Additionally, K6 lactylation of UGDH impedes the interaction of UGDH with signal transducer and activator of transcription 1 (STAT1), thus promoting the transcription of mitogen-activated protein kinase kinase kinase 8 (MAP3K8) and activating the MAPK signalling pathway. Importantly, in vitro and in vivo treatment with A485, a specific acyltransferase P300 inhibitor, suppressed UGDH lactylation and rescued chondrocyte ECM degradation and OA progression. Oxidative phosphorylation accelerates Alzheimer’s disease (AD) progression via a positive feedback inhibition loop of IDH3β-lactate-PAX6-IDH3β. YY1 lactylation in microglia plays an important role in retinal neovascularization by upregulating FGF2 expression. Targeting the lactate/p300/YY1 lactylation/FGF2 axis may provide new therapeutic targets for proliferative retinopathies. Furthermore, the increased lactylation of SNAP91 through exercise helps prevent anxiety-like behaviours in CRS mice. As research continues, nonhistone lactylation is expected to lead to new discoveries and breakthroughs in the field of life sciences, providing new ideas and methods for disease diagnosis, treatment and drug development. New treatment strategies

Cellular metabolic programming in cancer is influenced by various oncogenes, including HIF-1, Myc, p53, and the PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway. The application of glycolysis inhibitors, either alone or in combination with other therapeutic agents, has been shown to increase the effectiveness of cancer treatment. Inhibiting glycolytic enzymes such as hexokinase (HK), pyruvate kinase (PK), lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), and glucose transporter (GLUT) proteins has demonstrated tumour-suppressive effects. In addition to these targets, therapies aimed at lactylated transporter proteins and lactylated regulatory proteins, including MCT1, MCT4, P300/CBP, and the SIRT family of deacylating enzymes, have also shown promise in cancer treatment. LDH plays a crucial role in glycolysis by catalysing the conversion of pyruvate to lactate; its expression is often elevated in various cancers and is associated with prognosis in liver, lung, and pancreatic cancers, making it a viable target for cancer therapy. Sodium oxalate (OXA) is a known inhibitor of LDHA that decreases lactate levels, leading to reduced cell migration in oesophageal cancer, enhanced cytotoxicity in combination with docetaxel, apoptosis in PC cells, and increased sensitivity of colon cancer cells to cisplatin, olaparib, and etoposide. 2-Deoxy-D-glucose (2-DG) is another glucose metabolism inhibitor that promotes apoptosis by blocking the activities of hexokinase and glucose-6-phosphate isomerase; it inhibits the glycolytic pathway and has been shown to suppress various malignant tumours. 2-DG is currently undergoing phase I clinical trials; and has also been found to lower lactate levels in patients with myocardial infarction, thereby alleviating this condition. Lactate transport across cell membranes is regulated by a family of monocarboxylate transporters (MCTs), particularly MCT1 and MCT4, which are crucial for tumour invasion. α-Cyano-4-hydroxycinnamate (α CHC), an inhibitor of MCT1/2, disrupts lactate transport; and has been implicated in the treatment of breast, pancreatic, and gastric cancers. In studies related to myocardial infarction, α CHC has been shown to mitigate lactate-induced cardiac dysfunction and endothelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EndoMT). The SIRT family of enzymes also plays a role in demodification processes. AGK2, a selective SIRT2 inhibitor, has been demonstrated in ex vivo studies to increase the sensitivity of GC to electroglobulin, particularly in mucinous adenocarcinoma. Huperzine, a natural compound that activates SIRT3, has been found to increase sensitivity to elesclomol in GC, especially in mucinous adenocarcinomas. This effect is associated with a dose-dependent increase in SIRT3 expression, leading to reduced lactate levels, which in turn inhibits HCC growth and promotes apoptosis in vitro.

Future prospects

The study of nonhistone lactylation as a key hub linking tumour metabolic reprogramming and epigenetic regulation has provided novel perspectives and potential targets for tumour therapy. Although current studies have revealed the central role of lactation modification in tumorigenesis, drug resistance, and immune escape, future in-depth explorations in the following directions are still needed to promote the translation of basic research to clinical applications: analysis of the mechanisms regulating spatiotemporal dynamics and spatial and temporal heterogeneity of lactylation may determine their functional differences at different stages of tumour development. In the future, single-cell sequencing, spatial metabolomics and dynamic imaging technologies should be combined to analyse the spatial and temporal distributions of lactylation in the tumour microenvironment and to clarify their specific roles in primary foci, metastatic foci, and the process of treatment resistance. Development of precision-targeted therapies. The development of highly selective inhibitors or agonists targeting key enzymes (e.g., AARS1, the SIRT family) and metabolic pathways (e.g., LDH, MCTs) involved in lactylation is central to clinical translation. Integration of immunotherapy with metabolic regulation-related modifications shapes the immunosuppressive microenvironment by modulating PD-L1 stability, Treg cell function and macrophage polarization. Dual-targeting strategies can be designed in the future: immune checkpoint inhibitors, for example, anti-PD-1/PD-L1 drugs, can be combined with lactate transport inhibitors (MCT1/4 antagonists) to break the immunosuppressive barrier. Combined metabolic‒epigenetic interventions increase T-cell infiltration and killing activity by inhibiting the HIF-1α or glycolytic pathway and decreasing lactate levels while targeting lactylation-related enzymes. Breakthroughs in drug resistance mechanisms and individualized diagnosis and treatment have led to the establishment of drug resistance marker profiles and the development of dynamic monitoring technologies to guide clinical drug selection and real-time assessment of therapeutic response and drug resistance risk. Integrating advanced technologies such as nanotechnology will be pivotal. Nanosensors could enable real-time monitoring of spatiotemporal lactylation dynamics within the complex tumour microenvironment. Furthermore, nanocarriers offer promising platforms for the targeted delivery of novel lactylation inhibitors (e.g., targeting AARS1, SIRTs, or specific lactylation sites) or synergistic therapeutic agents (e.g., glycolysis inhibitors combined with immunotherapy drugs), enhancing tumour specificity and overcoming biological barriers while minimizing systemic toxicity. Unmet clinical needs and challenges of off-target effects and safety: Lactation modifications are widely involved in normal physiological processes and are needed to ensure the precision of targeted therapies. Metabolic plasticity: Tumour cells may escape treatment through compensatory pathways, requiring the design of combined multipathway interventions. Clinical translation barriers: Promotion of phase I/II clinical trials targeting lactate modifications to clarify pharmacokinetic profiles and optimal dosing strategies.

In conclusion, nonhistone lactylation research is moving from mechanism exploration to clinical translation. Breakthroughs in this field will not only provide new weapons for tumour treatment but also may reshape the metabolism‒epigenetic‒immunomodulation combined therapeutic paradigm and ultimately realize the transition from “treating the symptom” to “treating the root cause.”
