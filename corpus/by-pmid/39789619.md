# A phase Ia study of a novel anti-HER2 antibody-drug conjugate GQ1001 in patients with previously treated HER2 positive advanced solid tumors.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: A novel anti-human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) GQ1001 was assessed in patients with previously treated HER2 positive advanced solid tumors in a global multi-center phase Ia dose escalation trial.

**METHODS**: In this phase Ia trial, a modified 3 + 3 study design was adopted during dose escalation phase. Eligible patients were enrolled, and GQ1001 monotherapy was administered intravenously every 3 weeks. The starting dose was 1.2 mg/kg, followed by 2.4, 3.6, 4.8, 6.0, 7.2 and 8.4 mg/kg. Extra patients were enrolled into 6.0, 7.2, and 8.4 mg/kg cohorts as dose expansion phase. The primary endpoints were safety and to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) based on dose limiting toxicities (DLTs). Pharmacokinetics and anti-tumor efficacy of GQ1001 were assessed. The plasma concentration of free DM1, the payload of GQ1001, was quantitated.

**RESULTS**: A total of 32 patients were enrolled, predominantly in breast (9), gastric or gastro-esophageal junction (9) and salivary gland cancer (4). Median number of prior-line of therapies was 3 (0-11) and 37.5% patients received ≥ 2 lines of anti-HER2 therapies. No DLT was observed during dose escalation. MTD was not reached and dose recommended for dose expansion (DRDE) was determined as 8.4 mg/kg. Grade ≥ 3 treatment-related adverse events rate was 28.1% (9/32) and platelet count decreased (4/32, 12.5%) was the most common one. No drug-related death was observed. Objective response rate and disease control rate of 15 evaluable patients in 7.2 mg/kg and 8.4 mg/kg cohorts were 40.0% (6/15) and 60.0% (9/15). Pharmacokinetics analysis showed low exposure and accumulation of free DM1 in circulation.

**CONCLUSION**: GQ1001 is well tolerated and shows promising efficacy in previously treated HER2-positive advanced solid tumors. DRDE was determined as 8.4 mg/kg for following trials. Trial registration NCT, NCT04450732, Registered 23 June 2020, https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT04450732.

Introduction

Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) is a critical member of the epidermal growth factor receptor family. Its aberrant expression and activation have been demonstrated in multiple forms of cancers. Amplification or overexpression of HER2 occurs in approximately 15–30% of breast cancer and 10–30% of gastric cancer, and is now increasingly identified in esophagus, salivary gland, bladder, colon, lung, uterine cervix, and ovarian cancers. Somatic mutations of HER2 can also be detected by cancer genomic analysis. Intracellular signaling pathways triggered by HER2 activation contribute to proliferation, anti-apoptosis, adhesion, and invasion of cancer cells which make the HER2 oncogene an important therapeutic target. Several anti-HER2 agents have been demonstrated robust anti-tumor activity against HER2 positive solid tumors.

The antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) is a novel category of medication which aims to use monoclonal antibodies to deliver small molecule drugs into tumor sites without damaging healthy tissues. Several ADCs have been approved to treat solid tumors in the past few years. Although the concept of targeted drug delivery was recognized decades ago, investigations of ADC has significantly increased due to the development of modern technologies of conjugation process and novel payloads to improve safety and efficacy. Promising anti-tumor activity of ADCs has been demonstrated in various tumors expressing specific molecular targets, such as HER2 and trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2. Trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1), developed by applying the lysine residue-based conjugation followed by the Michael-addition reaction between sulfhydryl and maleimide remains used in the second conjugation reaction, and Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd), generated by using the chemical conjugation of the Michael-addition between cysteine residues from reduced interchain disulfide bonds and maleimide, are HER2 targeted ADCs that were approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2013 and 2019, respectively, expanding the therapeutic options for the treatment of HER2-dependent solid tumors.

One of the primary purposes of conjugating cytotoxic drugs to monoclonal antibody is to widen the therapeutic window and reduce clinical toxicities. However, severe chemotherapy-related toxicities can still be observed in some patients. The anti-tumor activity and clinical safety of ADC depend on its compositions including the antibody, linker, and payloads. The choice of linker determines most of the pharmacokinetic properties of the ADC that is attributed to its safety and efficacy profiles. In particular, a cleavable linker with high drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) likely correlates to high anti-tumor activity including direct cytotoxicity and bystander effect. On the other hand, unconjugated payloads released in circulation may increase the risk of toxicity.

Mulitple ADCs targeting HER2 with different linker and payload are under development in clinical or preclinical stage. GQ1001 is a novel anti-HER2 ADC developed by GeneQuantum Healthcare based on an innovative conjugation technology known as intelligent Ligase-Dependent Conjugation (iLDC), which is an “one-enzyme, one-step” site-specific conjugation technology based on engineered Sortase A immobilized by agarose resin and prepacked into a column. Humanized anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody (engineered trastuzumab) is combined with the cytotoxic payload DM1 via a non-cleavable open-ring linker. By using high controllable ring-opening process, GQ1001 shows excellent homogeneity and linker stability, which may indicate a better safety profile. Analyzing plasma stability of GQ1001 in experiment animals showed that the concentration of unconjugated DM1 is much lower after GQ1001 treatment compared with T-DM1 treatment. The robust anti-tumor activities of GQ1001 have also been demonstrated in preclinical investigations by in vitro and in vivo experiments in HER2-positive tumor models. Based on our previous preclinical studies, GQ1001 showed potential for the treatment of HER2 positive tumors.

Hererin, we reported the results of a global multi-center phase Ia study which was aimed to assess the safety, pharmacokinetics (PK), and preliminary anti-tumor activity of GQ1001 in patients with HER2 positive advanced solid tumors, and to determine maximum tolerated dose (MTD) as well as dose recommended for dose expansion (DRDE).

Methods

Study design

This was a phase Ia, first-in-human, multi-center, open-label study to treat adult patients with HER2 positive, previously treated, advanced solid tumors which was conducted in Australia (2 centers), United States (1 center), and China (3 centers). The study was performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and the International Conference on Harmonization guideline for Good Clinical Practice. Approval by an independent ethics committee or institutional review board was obtained at each site before study initiation. All the patients signed informed consent. The study is registered with ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT 04450732).

A modified 3 + 3 design with a 21-day dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs) observation period was applied to guide the dose escalation phase. One subject in the first (1.2 mg/kg) and 2 subjects in the subsequent dose cohort (2.4 mg/kg) were enrolled as an accelerated titration design to balance patients’ benefit and risk of toxicities. In the following dose cohorts (3.6 mg/kg, 4.8 mg/kg, 6.0 mg/kg, 7.2 mg/kg and 8.4 mg/kg), standard 3 + 3 design was applied. If no DLT was reported by the first 3 patients, the study proceeded to the next dose cohort. If 1 patient had DLT, 3 more patients would be enrolled into the current dose cohort. If ≥ 1 of the added 3 patients had DLTs, the dose escalation would be terminated, and the previous dose was considered as the MTD. If the additional 3 patients had no DLT, the dose escalation proceeded to the next dose cohort. If 2 out of first 3 patients in a certain dose level had DLTs, the dose escalation would be terminated. Then this dose was the intolerable dose, and the previous dose was MTD. If there were no DLTs in the initial 3 patients of the high-dose cohorts (6.0 mg/kg, 7.2 mg/kg, and 8.4 mg/kg), extra patients could be enrolled in these cohorts as the dose expansion phase to fully assess the safety and efficacy profiles for determination of DRDE, and number of patients was determined by the investigators and sponsors. All subjects were treated with study drug on day 1 in three-weekly cycle by intravenous infusion until disease progression or intolerable toxicity.

Eligibility criteria

Eligible patients were over 18 years of age, male and female, with pathologically documented advanced, unresectable or metastatic HER2 positive solid tumor that was relapsed or refractory to standard therapy or for whom there was no standard therapy. HER2-positivity was defined as immunohistochemical (IHC) 3 + or IHC 2 + plus in situ hybridization positive for all indications. All patients were required to have a life expectancy over 3 months estimated by the investigators based on patient’s performance status and organ functions; Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0 or 1; left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) ≥ 50%. Adequate organ function was required by laboratory examinations 7 days before first treatment, including platelet ≥ 100 × 109/L; hemoglobin ≥ 9 g/dL; neutrophil count ≥ 1.5 × 109/L; blood creatinine (Cr) ≤ 1.5 × upper normal limit (ULN) or creatinine clearance rate ≥ 60 mL/min; total bilirubin ≤ 1.5 × ULN; alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) ≤ 2.5 × ULN; ALT and AST ≤ 5 × ULN for patients with liver metastases. Sufficient wash out period from the former anti-tumor treatment was required before the first dose of GQ1001, including major surgery (≥ 4 weeks), radiotherapy (≥ 4 weeks), autologous transplantation (≥ 3 months), hormonal therapy (≥ 2 weeks), and chemotherapy or targeted therapy (≥ 4 weeks). Key exclusion criteria included clinically active brain metastases, multiple primary malignancies within two years except for melanoma and other solid tumors that have been cured, cardiovascular dysfunction or clinically significant cardiac and lung disease, hypersensitivity to either the drug substances or inactive ingredients in the drug product, cumulative anthracycline dose over 360 mg/m2 doxorubicin or equivalent, uncontrolled infection and women who were lactating or pregnant.

Endpoints

The primary endpoint of this study was to assess the safety profile of GQ1001, identify DLTs, determine the MTD and DRDE. The secondary endpoints included characteristics of PK and preliminary anti-tumor activity of GQ1001.

Safety and tolerability

Safety profiles were assessed by review of DLTs, adverse events (AEs), clinical laboratory tests, physical examination, ECOG performance score, vital signs and cardiac assessments (including LVEF and cardiac enzymes). Safety assessment was initiated since the first dose of GQ1001 until 30 days after last dose of study treatment or initiation of new anti-tumor therapy. A DLT was defined as any of the pre-defined AEs or abnormal laboratory values occurring during the first cycle (≤ 21 days) and assessed as unrelated to disease, disease progression, intercurrent illness, or concomitant medications, including ≥ grade 4 neutropenia ≥ 7 days, ≥ grade 4 febrile neutropenia, ≥ grade 4 anemia, ≥ grade 3 thrombocytopenia with hemorrhage, ≥ grade 4 thrombocytopenia ≥ 7 days, ≥ grade 3 heart failure or decreased LVEF, ≥ grade 4 AST/ALT increased, drug-induced jaundice caused by hepatocellular injury without a significant obstructive component (Hy’s law), and ≥ grade 3 non-hematological toxicities. Any of the toxicities found during the DLT observation period met above criteria, and after evaluation by Safety Review Committee, would be judged as a DLT. AEs were graded according to National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (NCI CTCAE) version 5.0. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) were AEs related to the study drug assessed by the investigators and that occurred or worsened after informed consent was signed. During treatment, the study visit was performed every 21 days or following the treatment plan. Additional assessments were made when serious adverse events occurred outside of scheduled visits. Serious adverse events were managed and reported following the guideline for Good Clinical Practice.

Efficacy

The anti-tumor efficacy of GQ1001 was evaluated by objective response rate (ORR), disease control rate (DCR), and progression-free survival (PFS) per Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST, v1.1). Tumor response was assessed by investigators every 9 weeks (± 7 days) until disease progression based on radiographic evidence. ORR was defined as the proportion of patients with complete response (CR) or partial response (PR). DCR was defined as the proportion of patients with CR, PR, or stable disease (SD). PFS was defined as time from enrollment to first documented progressive disease or death, whichever occurred first.

Pharmacokinetics analysis

Blood samples were collected at pre-dose, end of infusion, 30 min, 2 h, 8 h, 24 h, 48 h, 72 h, 168 h and 336 h post-dose of 1st cycle (C1) and 3rd cycle (C3) for PK analysis. Concentrations of GQ1001 (ADC) and total antibody (TAb) in serum were quantitated using enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay, and free DM1 (fDM1) in plasma was quantitated using liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (Xihuasci Inc., Shanghai, China). The lower limits of quantification were 100 ng/ml for ADC in serum, 200 ng/ml for TAb in serum and 0.05 ng/ml for fDM1 in plasma.

Concentration–time profiles of ADC, TAb, and fDM1 were analyzed. The PK parameters including maximum observed serum concentration (Cmax), time to Cmax (Tmax), area under the plasma concentration–time curve from zero to the last time (AUC0-t), elimination half-life (T1/2), apparent total body clearance (CL), and apparent volume of distribution (Vd), were estimated using non-compartmental analysis (NCA) according to the actual time of blood collection (Phoenix WinNonlin Professional version 8.3, Certara, L.P. Princeton, New Jersey, USA). Accumulation ratio (Rac) of exposure was used to describe the accumulation of drugs in the human body after multiple doses. Rac of individual Cmax, AUC0-t in C3 was calculated using the formula of “Cmax of C3 / Cmax of C1” and “AUC0-t of C3 / AUC0-t of C1”. Rac summarized according to different dose groups.

Statistical analysis

A minimum of 6 evaluable subjects was required for the dose to be declared as MTD or DRDE. Total sample size for the dose escalation was determined by practical consideration. Baseline characteristics and safety data were summarized using descriptive statistics. The efficacy of GQ1001 was summarized by evaluation of ORR, DCR, and PFS. For ORR and DCR, the point estimate and corresponding exact two-sided 95% confidence intervals (95% CIs) was provided using the Clopper-Pearson method. For time to event variables, the Kaplan–Meier method was applied and two-sided 95% CIs for medians were calculated by the Brookmeyer and Crowley method. SAS® 9.4 was used for statistical analysis. PK parameters was analyzed by non-compartmental analysis with WinNonlin.

Results

Clinical characteristics of patients and duration of exposure

From July 2020 to December 2022, a total of 32 patients were enrolled (Figure S1). There were 18 males and 14 females with the median age of 58.0 years old (range, 43.0—78.0), and 16 of them were Asian (Table 1). Major tumor types included breast cancer (9 cases, 28.1%), gastric or gastro-esophageal junction (GEJ) carcinoma (9 cases, 28.1%), salivary gland carcinoma (4 cases, 12.5%), and esophagal cancer (2 cases, 6.3%). Median number of prior-line of therapy was 3 (0—11), and 37.5% of patients previously received ≥ 2 lines of anti-HER2 therapies. As of data cutoff (Oct. 31th, 2023), the median duration of GQ1001 exposure was 22.8 weeks (range, 1.1—82.0 weeks, Figure S2). Intra-patient dose escalation by 1 level occurred for 2 patients in 2.4 mg/kg cohort and 1 patient in 3.6 mg/kg cohort. One patient in 7.2 mg/kg cohort required dose reduction to 6.0 mg/kg due to adverse events.

Safety profiles and determination of MTD and DRDE

All patients were assessable for safety analysis. Median number of applied cycles were 8 (range 1–26). During dose escalation, no DLT was observed in all cohorts up to 8.4 mg/kg, and MTD was not reached. TRAEs occurred in 75% (24/32) of patients (Table S1). The most common TRAEs (≥ 15%) included aspartate aminotransferase increased (37.5%), platelet count decreased (28.1%), alanine aminotransferase increased (25.0%), fever (21.9%), anemia (18.8%), and were mostly stratified as grade 1 or 2. Grade ≥ 3 TRAEs were observed in 28.1% (9/32) of patients. The most common one was platelet count decreased that occurred in 4 patients (12.5%). Other grade ≥ 3 TRAEs included anaemia, lymphocyte count decreased, aspartate aminotransferase increased, gamma-glutamyltransferase increased, vomiting and hypertension (Table 2). Treatment related serious adverse events (SAEs) were platelet count decreased in two patients (7.2 mg/kg and 8.4 mg/kg each) and vomiting in one patient (7.2 mg/kg). No drug related death was observed. Based on the safety profiles, 8.4 mg/kg was determined as DRDE.

Clinical efficacy outcomes

As of Oct. 31th, 2023, 27 patients were evaluable for objective response, 25 of these had target lesions (Figure S2). Objective response was observed in two high-dose cohorts (7.2 mg/kg and 8.4 mg/kg) (Fig. 1A). Of 15 patients in these cohorts, confirmed ORR was 40.0% (6/15), DCR was 60.0% (9/15) (Fig. 1B, Table S2). For seven evaluable breast cancer patients in two high-dose cohorts, ORR was 57.1% (4/7) and DCR was 71.4% (5/7) (Table S2). For all evaluable patients, median duration of treatment was 4.6 months (range 0.7 to 18.2), and median PFS was 6.5 months (95% CI 4.5–8.5). The changes of target lesions for all patients and those in 7.2 mg/kg and 8.4 mg/kg cohorts were illustrated (Fig. 1C and D).

Encouraging efficacy of GQ1001 was also observed in GEJ cancer. A 61-year-old white male (11–035) was diagnosed with HER2-positive (IHC 3 +) stage IV GEJ adenocarcinoma with liver metastasis in September 2019 and had disease progression after multiple standard regimens, including chemotherapy (cisplatin, 5-fluorouracil, oxaliplatin, and capecitabine), HER2-targeting therapy (trastuzumab), and radiotherapy (gastroesophageal and liver lesions). The first dose of 7.2 mg/kg GQ1001 was administrated in April 2022. Grade 1 headache, fatigue, and AST increased were observed after treatment and recovered soon after. In terms of efficacy, the size of the target lesions at baseline was 65.0 mm and diminished to 24.0 mm after 8 doses (Fig. 1E). The lesions continued to decrease in size and achieved a 75.4% tumor reduction at the lastest tumor assessment (August 2023). The duration of response of this subject exceeded over 81 weeks.

Pharmacokinetics analysis

Blood samples for pharmacokinetics analysis were available from all 32 enrolled patients at the first cycle. The total antibody PK data of one patient in 6 mg/kg cohort was excluded due to disturbance of previous anti-HER2 bispecific antibody treatment. Serum/plasma concentration–time curves of ADC, TAb, and fDM1 were showed in Fig. 2. Corresponding PK parameters were listed in Table 3. At the first cycle, mean Tmax of ADC (2.32 h), TAb (2.16 h) and fDM1 (2.21 h) were similar and reached their Cmax at about 60 min after GQ1001 infusion was completed. Mean Cmax of ADC and TAb were similar, while mean AUC0-t of TAb were approximately 1.2 to 2.5 folds higher than ADC and increased following dose escalation. The concentrations of ADC and TAb declined in a linear manner after reaching their Cmax and were similar in different dose cohorts (Figure S3). Mean T1/2 of ADC and TAb were 76.37 ± 14.41 h and 169.33 ± 75.81 h, respectively. Mean T1/2 of TAb were approximately 1.2 to 3.0 folds higher than ADC which was consistent with AUC0-t. The Vd of ADC and TAb were similar. Plasma concentration of fDM1 was significantly lower compared with ADC and TAb in all dose cohorts. Mean Cmax of fDM1 was 0.26 ± 0.09 ng/ml (0–0.39 ng/ml) which was about 4.44 × 105 and 4.74 × 105 folds lower than ADC and TAb, respectively. Mean AUC0-t of fDM1 was 9.48 ± 7.78 h*ng/ml (0–24.11 h*ng/ml) and was also significantly lower compared with ADC and TAb.

Accumulation ratio (Rac) of exposure was assessed after the C3 of GQ1001 administration in 21 assessable patients. Compared to C1, Rac_Cmax and Rac_AUC0-t of ADC were 0.88 to 1.04 and 0.63 to 1.27, respectively. No accumulation of ADC in the different dose groups was observed. Rac_Cmax of TAb was 0.98 to 1.21 indicating no accumulation (Table S3). AUC0-t of TAb increased by about 10% and 30% when compared with those at C1. Mean T1/2 of TAb was 208.25 ± 111.95 h and was not significantly different from C1 (Table 3). Rac_Cmax of fDM1 was calculated to be 0.54 to 1.38 and Rac_AUC0-t of fDM1 was range from 1.16 to 2.49. Free DM1 was completely metabolized on Day 14 for both C1 and C3 which showed no accumulation. (Table S3).

Discussion

In the present study, the anti-HER2 ADC GQ1001 developed based on an innovative enzymatic site-specific conjugation technology demonstrates an excellent tolerability in patients with heavily treated HER2 positive advanced solid tumors. No DLT is observed in all planned dose cohort. The safety profiles of GQ1001 can be attributed to the relative low exposure and accumulation of the free DM1 in circulation. Its promising anti-tumor efficacy warrants further evaluation in a larger trial.

Currently, several anti-HER2 ADCs, including T-DM1, T-DXd and disitamab vedotin (RC48), have been approved to treat HER2 positive solid tumors including, breast cancer, gastric cancer, and urothelial carcinoma. Although the safety profiles of these ADCs are manageable, over 50% of patients still suffer grade 3 or worse adverse events. For T-DM1, the most common grade 3 or worse AEs were platelet count decreased and aminotransferase increased. Haematological toxicities also occurred in patients treated with T-DXd and RC48. Neutropenia and anaemia were the common grade 3 or worse AEs. Interstitial lung disease was observed in early trials of T-DXd and was the major reason for treatment discontinuation. Based on the current GQ1001 study, the frequency of grade 3 or worse AEs was less than 30% which was significantly lower than those from the other HER2-targeting ADCs. For bone marrow, liver, and gastrointestinal tract which are the main organs affected by anti-HER2 ADCs, the frequency of grade 3 or worse AEs of these organs was also mild. DM1 is a derivative of maytansine undergoing hepatic metabolism via the CYP3A4/5 pathway that frequently leads to aminotransferase elevation. AST increased was also the most common AE in this study, but its frequency was 37.5% (12/32), and only 1 patient experienced grade 3 AST increased. For the rest of patients, pharmacological treatment was not required for hepatic toxicity. Further supporting the safety of GQ1001, in the highest planned dose cohort (8.4 mg/kg), 2 patients received treatment over 28 weeks with no severe AEs observed.

The toxicities of ADCs are mainly dependent on their structure. The linker plays an important role in ADC design which is one of the key determinants of the safety of ADC, as well as its efficacy and pharmacokinetics. Premature release of the payloads in circulation results in systemic toxicity. Similar to T-DM1, an uncleavable linker was used to manufacture GQ1001. By using an innovative conjugation technology, the product of GQ1001 shows improved homogeneity and stability which has been demonstrated by the pharmacokinetics analysis in this study. Free DM1 in circulation after administration of GQ1001 was extremely low compared with T-DM1. A slight accumulation of free DM1 could be observed after treatment. The safety profiles and a wide therapeutic window of GQ1001 can be attributed to its pharmacological features.

Platelet count decreased is one of the most common adverse events of T-DM1 leading to dose reduction or treatment discontinuation. In this study, four patients suffered grade ≥ 3 platelet count decreased after administration of GQ1001, and no bleeding event was recorded. Although it is not assessed as DLT, the incidence of platelet count decreased should be noticed in the future clinical trial with a larger sample size. The mechanisms of thrombocytopenia induced by ADC treatment are still under investigation. Based on the previous investigations, T-DM1 can be internalized into differentiating megakaryocyte via Fcγ receptor binding or pinocytosis prior to DM1 release into circulation. For other anti-HER2 ADCs like RC48 which has a cleavable valine-citrulline linker, its cytotoxicity to differentiating neutrophils can be attributed to extracellular proteolysis of the cleavable linker which results in release of free MMAE in the extracellular compartment. Further studies should be performed to investigate the underlying mechanisms of adverse events caused by GQ1001.

The promising anti-tumor activity of GQ1001 observed in this study is comparable to other anti-HER2 ADCs in early-stage clinical trials. HER2 positive breast and gastroesophageal cancers were most likely to respond to GQ1001, while a pan-cancer anti-tumor activity could also be observed. Patients with salivary gland cancer, cervix cancer and appendix cancer achieved disease control after GQ1001 adiminstation. By comparision, in the NCI-MATCH trial evaluting T-DM1 for treatment of HER2 amplified tumors, PR rate and SD rate of patients excluding breast and G/GEJ cancer were 5.6% and 47%, respectively.

Anti-tumor efficacy of ADCs heavily depends on the type and amount of payload. Similar to T-DM1, DM1 is also selected as payload for GQ1001 and DAR is 2. Despite a relativley low DAR, the features of high homogeneity and circulation stability of GQ1001 due to the innovative conjugation techology can transfer the payloads into tumor cells with high efficiency. Meanwhile, the excellent saftey profile of GQ1001 indicates that a higher dosage can be administrated during clincial practices compared with T-DM1. These advantages may be promising for patients who are median sensitive to DM1 such as gastric cancer. In the present study, a heavily treated patient with GEJ adenocarcinoma recieved GQ1001 (7.2 mg/kg) treatment and achieved a partial response and a long-term PFS. These findings suggest that a relative high dose exposure of GQ1001 may be beneficial for these patients.

A potential limitation of GQ1001 is that a non-cleavable linker may limit the by-stander effect as one of its anti-tumor mechanisms. This might impair the efficacy of GQ1001 to treat tumors with heterogenous HER2 expression which was also one of the reasons leading to the failure of anti-HER2 ADC treatment. HER2 status might be altered or lost during disease progression, and heterogenity in HER2 exprssion between primary and metastatic tumors is also reported. Tumor rebiopsy to validiate HER2 status before GQ1001 or other anti-HER2 ADCs treament can help enrich the patients. Molecular imaging using nuclide-marked HER2 antibody might also reveal the intra-patient spatial heterogenity of HER2 expression. In addition, previous pre-clinical studies with GQ1001 showed synergistic anti-tumor activity when combined with chemotherapeutic agents. With the excellent safety profiles, combination of GQ1001 with other anti-tumor agents such as immunotherapy can also be a potential strategy to overcome HER2 heterogenity and improve treatment outcomes of patients.

Conclusion

In summary, this phase Ia trial evaluated the safety profile and preliminary anti-tumor efficacy of GQ1001, a novel anti-HER2 ADC. The results indicate the excellent tolerability of GQ1001 and a promising efficacy in patients with HER2-positive heavily pre-treated solid tumors. The DRDE of GQ1001 was determined as 8.4 mg/kg. The efficacy of GQ1001 will be validated in future studies.
