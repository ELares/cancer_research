# CD40 agonist improves the therapeutic efficacy of irreversible electroporation ablation for metastatic melanoma by promoting unexpected CD8+CD103+ cDC1 and TRM cell responses.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Melanoma is one of the deadliest forms of skin cancer. Irreversible electroporation (IRE) is an innovative, non-thermal ablation technology for treating irresectable solid cancers. However, most IRE treatments are incapable of cancer eradication and only temporarily prolong patient survival.

**METHODS**: In this study, we developed a novel IRE + Combo treatment regimen that combines IRE-ablation with Combo-adjuvant [CpG, anti-PD-L1 antibody (PD-L1-Ab) and CD40-agonist] and investigated its anti-tumor immunity in a mouse BL6-10OVA (BLOVA) melanoma model.

**RESULTS**: We demonstrated that inclusion of the CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment regimen promoted a more robust CD8+ T cell response (6.89%) when compared with IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab (2.67%) or IRE alone (0.21%) treatments, leading to eradication of subcutaneous BLOVA melanoma in 5/8 of BLOVA-bearing mice and simultaneous elimination of lung melanoma metastases. Addition of CD40-agonist to the IRE + Combo treatment regimen also induced a higher frequency (17.1%) of CD8+CD103+ conventional type-1 dendritic cells (cDC1s) with up-regulated expression of CD54, CD80, MHC II, Bcl-xL and 41BBL in tumor-drainage lymph nodes (TDLNs) relative to the control IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab (12.1%) and IRE alone (9.0%) treatment groups. We also show that CD40-agonist stimulated a higher frequency of CD103+TCF1+ tissue-resident memory T (TRM) cells (32.1%) in TDLNs when compared with the two control (15.3% and 6.7%) treatment groups, and that these TRM cells exhibited enhanced mitochondrial content and greater relative expression of the effector cytokines IFN-γ and TNF-α and the transcriptional regulators TRAF1, p38-MAPK and PGC-1α.

**CONCLUSION**: Taken together, this study establishes that the CD40-agonist greatly potentiates the efficacy of IRE-ablation for metastatic melanoma by promoting unexpected CD8+CD103+ cDC1 and CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses and suggests the importance of targeting CD40-signaling to improve the efficacy of cancer IRE-ablation therapy.

Introduction

Irreversible electroporation (IRE) is an innovative, non-thermal tissue ablation technology that applies high voltage electrical pulses to targeted tumor tissue, leading to massive cell apoptosis. Compared to thermal forms of ablation such as radiofrequency ablation, microwaves and high intensity focused ultrasound which all cause heat-induced collateral damage, IRE is a safe treatment regimen without adverse side effects. As such, IRE-ablation therapy has been applied to various malignancies including lung, liver, breast, pancreas and prostate cancers; however, most IRE treatments are incapable of cancer eradication and only temporarily prolong patient survival. IRE combined with PD-1 blockade improved therapeutic efficacy compared to IRE alone. For example, IRE + PD-1 blockade in mouse models resulted in increased tumor-specific CD8+ T cell responses and T cell tumor infiltration that was accompanied by sustained tumor remission in the absence of complete tumor eradication. Moreover, in humans the application of IRE + PD-1 blockade in pancreatic cancer patients was associated with both increased overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS).

Toll-like receptors (TLRs) are a crucial family of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that play a vital role in both innate and adaptive immunity. TLR agonists are commonly applied to improve cancer immunotherapy. TLR9 (CpG) agonist has been found to stimulate CD8+ T cell responses. Immune checkpoint-inhibitor PD-1 blockade using anti-PD-L1 antibody (PD-L1-Ab) has also been commonly used to promote cancer immunotherapy by blocking the inhibitory PD-1/PD-L1 pathway of CD8+PD-1+ T cells, thereby rescuing T cell exhaustion. Combining TLR9 agonist with PD-1 blockade was found to enhance cancer immunotherapies. CD40 is a co-stimulatory molecule on the surface of dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, natural killer (NK) and B cells that belongs to the tumor necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) superfamily. Preclinical and clinical studies have shown CD40-agonist-induced activation of DCs induces potent, anti-tumor CD8+ T cell responses. In addition to enhanced adoptive CD8+ T cell responses, TLR and CD40-agonists have been found to promote innate immunity. For example, TLR9 and CD40-agonists activate NK cell responses, while CD40-agonists also promote the transition of macrophages from an inhibitory type 2 (M2) to an immunogenic (M1) state and activate NK cells. In addition, NK cells also activated by IL-12 secreted from cDC1s secrete IFN-γ and Fms-like tyrosine kinase 3 ligand (Flt3L) which, in turn, promote cDC1 IL-12 production and differentiation and sustain cDC1 viability.

Melanoma is relentless and aggressive malignancy that represents one of the deadliest forms of skin cancer due to its ability to metastasize to other parts of the body if left untreated early on. The increasing worldwide incidence of melanoma has spurred the development of new treatment regimens for advanced metastatic melanomas. Carton et al. showed that IRE combined with intra-tumoral IL-12 gene transduction delayed mouse melanoma growth. Pasquet et al. further demonstrated that IRE combined with peritumoral IL-12 gene transfer inhibited local melanoma growth to prolong survival of melanoma-bearing mice while also inducing systemic activation of anti-tumor immunity to delay the growth of distant melanomas. Lasarte-Cia et al. recently demonstrated that combining IRE ablation therapy with the Stimulator of Interferon Genes (STING) agonist increased the frequency of intra-tumoral CD8+ T cells and delayed the growth of mouse melanoma or hepatocarcinoma but failed to eradicate any of these cancers, indicating that the development of more efficient IRE ablation treatment regimens is urgently required to further improve their therapeutic efficacy.

We previously developed a combinatorial IRE treatment that married IRE ablation with CpG and PD-L1-Ab and investigated its therapeutic efficacy in a mouse model of EG7 lymphoma expressing a nominal tumor antigen chicken ovalbumin (OVA). We found that the combined IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab ablation cooperatively induced potent OVA-specific CD8+ T cell immunity, which led to complete eradication of both the EG7 lymphoma and lung metastases. In this study, we assessed the therapeutic efficacy of the IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab ablation in our mouse OVA-expressing BL6-10OVA (BLOVA) melanoma model and found that while it significantly delayed tumor growth, it failed to eradicate BLOVA melanoma. Therefore, to further improve its therapeutic efficacy we developed a refined IRE + Combo treatment regimen that combined IRE with a new adjuvant Combo cocktail (CpG/PD-L1-Ab and CD40-agonist) and investigated its therapeutic potential in our mouse metastatic melanoma BLOVA model. Herein, we demonstrate that inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment significantly promoted OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses, leading to eradication of subcutaneous (s.c.) BLOVA cancer in 5/8 of s.c. BLOVA-bearing mice while simultaneously eliminating lung melanoma metastases. We further show that the effect of the CD40-agonist on the efficacy of the IRE + Combo treatment was mitigated by potent amplification and stimulation of three subpopulations of conventional type-1 dendritic cell (cDC1) populations including the unexpected CD8+CD103+ cDC1 subset as well as the newly discovered CD44+CD62L+ central memory T (TCM) cell-derived CD103+TCF1+ tissue-resident memory T (TRM) cells in tumor-derived lymph nodes (TDLNs). The CD40-agonist-stimulated TRM cells also exhibited elevated expression of the T cell effector cytokines IFN-γ and TNF-α, increased co-stimulatory 41BB signaling accompanied by augmented abundance of the downstream regulators TRAF1, p38-MAPK and PGC-1α, and higher mitochondrial content to coordinately control CD8+ TRM cell metabolism for TRM cell persistence.

Material and methods

Regents, cells lines and mice

The biotin-conjugated anti-I-A/I-E (MHC II), anti-CD80 and anti-CD54 Abs were purchased from BD Biosciences (San Jose, CA). The rabbit anti-PGC-1α, anti-TRAF1 and anti-TCF1 Abs were obtained from Cell Signaling Technology (Danvers, MA, USA). A FITC-conjugated anti-CD8 Ab was obtained from Bio-Rad (Hercules, CA). A PE-conjugated H-2 Kb/OVA257-264 tetramer (PE-tetramer) was obtained from the Fred Hutchinson Cancer Research Center (Seattle, WA). The FITC-Annexin-V Apoptosis Detection Kit was purchased from BD Biosciences (San Jose, CA). The Prolong™ Gold Antifade Mountant with the DAPI DNA stain was obtained from Life Technologies Inc. (Carlsbad, CA). MitoTracker Green FM and Hoechst 33,342 solution were obtained from Life Technologies Inc. (Carlsbad, CA). EasySep™ Mouse CD8+ T Cell Isolation Kit was obtained from StemCell Technologies (Vancouver, BC, Canada). CpG oligodeoxynucleotides 1826 (CpG ODN 1826) was obtained from Invitrogen Inc (San Diego, CA). The Cytofix/Cytoperm Kit and Lysis Buffer were purchased from BD Biosciences (Franklin Lakes, NJ). Anti-PD-L1 and anti-CD40 Abs for in vivo use were obtained from InvivoGene (San Diego, CA). The OVA transgene-transfected B16 melanoma cell line BL6-10OVA (BLOVA) was generated in our laboratory and was maintained in RPMI medium (Life Technologies, Carlsbad, CA) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum (FCS) plus G418 (0.5 mg/mL; Life Technologies). Six-to eight-week-old female C57BL/6 (B6, CD45.2) and B6.1 (CD45.1) mice were purchased from the Jackson Laboratory (Bar Harbor, ME). All animal experiments were approved by the Animal Research Ethics Board, University of Saskatchewan (Protocol# 20,160,056).

Analysis of in vitro electroporation-induced tumor cell apoptosis

Mouse melanoma BLOVA cells suspended in PBS (1 × 106 cells/mL) were transferred into each electroporation cuvette and then electroporated with a Bio-Electroporator (Bio-Rad) at 0 V, 600 V, 1200 V and 1800 V to induce tumor cell apoptosis. Apoptotic cells were stained with FITC-labeled Annexin-V and propidium iodide (PI) and analyzed by flow cytometry to quantify the percentage of electroporation-induced Annexin-V+PI+ apoptotic tumor cells. To assess the immunogenicity of apoptotic tumor cells, apoptotic BLOVA (ΔBLOVA) cells (1 × 106 cells/mouse) derived from electroporation with 1200 V and 0 V (as a control) were intravenously (i.v) injected into B6 mice (5 mice/group). Mouse tail vein blood samples were collected 8 days post-injection, stained with FITC-anti-CD8 Ab (FITC-CD8) and PE-tetramer and then analyzed by flow cytometry to measure OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses.

Preparation and phenotypic analysis of DCs

B6 mouse bone marrow-derived DCs were prepared as previously described. In brief, bone marrow cells from the tibia of B6 mice were ridded of blood cells with Tris-ammonium chloride (0.84%) and then propagated in DC culture medium [DMEM plus 10% FBS, 20 ng/mL granulocyte macrophage colony stimulation factor (GM-CSF) and 20 ng/mL interleukin 4 (IL − 4)]. The non-adherent granulocytes along with B and T cells suspended in the culture supernatant were removed on day 3, and the adherent, mature DCs were harvested on day 6. BLOVA cells were labeled with 4 μM CFSE (carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester) at 37 °C for 10 min followed by a 1200 V electroporation step. Apoptotic CFSE (green)- ΔBLOVA cells were then co-cultured with DCs overnight at a ratio of 2:1. The cell suspensions from unloaded DCs or DCs with phagocytosis of apoptotic CFSE-BLOVA cells (DC/ΔBLOVA) were stained with PE-anti-CD11c Ab (red) and then analyzed by flow cytometry, while DC nuclei were stained with DAPI (blue) and analyzed by confocal microscopy. To assess the stimulatory effect, B6 mice (5 mice/group) were i.v. injected with DCs with phagocytosis of ΔBLOVA (DC/ΔBLOVA) or unloaded DCs as a control (1 × 106 cells/mouse). Eight days later, mouse tail vein blood samples were stained with FITC-CD8 Ab and PE-tetramer Ab for flow cytometry analysis.

Formulation and application of combinatorial IRE ablation treatments

BLOVA cells (1 × 106 cells/mouse) were subcutaneously (s.c.) injected into the right flank of B6 mice. Tumor volume was monitored daily using a digital caliper and calculated using the formula A/2 × B2 where A and B are the long and short dimensions of the tumor, respectively. When tumors reached 50 mm3 (4.5–5.0 mm in diameter), tumor-bearing mice (8 mice/group) were treated with various IRE ablation protocols; (i) IRE only, (ii) IRE + TLR9 agonists (IRE + CpG), (iii) IRE + PD-L1-Ab, (iv) IRE + CD40-Ab, (v) IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab and (vi) IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab/CD40-Ab (IRE + Combo). Mice were intraperitoneally (i.p.) injected with anti-PD-L1 (150 µg/mouse/each time) or/and anti-CD40 (40 µg/mouse/each time) Abs one day prior to IRE ablation (day -1) and one day after IRE ablation (day 1). Anti-PD-L1 Ab was then i.p. injected at days 3 and 5 for a total of four injections. The IRE ablation was performed at day 0, following intra-tumoral (i.t.) injection with a mixture of CpG (30 μg) in a total PBS volume of 15μL into three different points (5μL/point) in peripheral tumor tissues. The parameters of IRE performance were 1200 V/cm for voltage, 90 µs for pulse duration, 1 Hz for pulse repetition frequency and 100 for repeated pulses. Two electrodes separated by 4 mm were inserted into the center of the tumor and the process was run once. The electrodes were then rotated 90°, reinserted into the tumor and the process repeated. To evaluate OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses, peripheral blood samples of treated mice were stained with FITC-CD8 Ab and PE-tetramer 8 days post-ablation and analyzed by flow cytometry. Tumor growth or regression was monitored daily. For ethical reasons, mice carrying tumors approximately 500 mm3 (9–10 mm) were euthanized and recorded as dead.

Tumor metastatic colony measurements on mouse lung tissue

BLOVA cells (1 × 106 cells/mouse) were s.c. injected into the right flank of B6 mice. Seven days later, tumor-bearing mice and control mice without s.c. injection of melanoma cells (8 mice/group) were i.v. injected with BLOVA cells (5 × 105 cells/mouse). Mice bearing s.c. BLOVA tumors (about 50 mm3) were then treated a week later with the IRE + Combo regimen. Untreated, control mice subjected only to i.v. injection with BLOVA cells and IRE + Combo-treated animals were euthanized 14 days later and lung tissues were collected. Black melanoma colonies on the lung tissue surface were counted and confirmed by histopathological examination.

Histopathological staining

Tumor or lung tissues were fixed with 10% formalin for at least 48 h at room temperature then embedded in paraffin. Paraffin-embedded tissue blocks were cut into 6 μm thick sections and affixed to slides. The slides were deparaffinized and stained with Hematoxylin–eosin. The sections on the slides were dehydrated using an increasing ethanol gradient and xylene and then mounted with a coverslip using a permanent mounting medium. The slides were imaged using a microscope at 50 × and 200 × magnifications.

Analysis of single-cell suspensions derived from TDLNs

TDLNs were harvested from mice (5 mice/group) treated for 8 days with IRE, IRE + CpG + PD-L1-Ab and IRE + Combo. Single-cell suspensions prepared by passing lymphoid tissue through a 40 µm strainer were stained with Abs against cell surface CD8, CD11c, CD103, MHC II, CD54, CD80, CD40 and 41BBL or against cell surface CD3, CD8, CD44, CD62L, CD103 and 41BB for the analysis of cDC1 subsets and CD8+ TRM cells, respectively. Cell suspensions were fixed and permeabilized using the Cytofix/Cytoperm kit (BD Biosciences) and stained with Abs against intracellular Bcl-XL, TCF1, PGC-1α, phospho-p38 MAPK and TRAF1 for flow cytometry analyses of cDC1 subsets or CD8+ TRM cells. To analyze the endogenous cytokine production of CD8+ TRM cells, single-cell suspensions were first stimulated with 3 μg/mL brefeldin A, 0.081 µM PMA, 1.34 µM ionomycin and 2 μM monensin for 3–5 h. Cells were then fixed and permeabilized by using the Cytofix/Cytoperm kit (BD Biosciences) and stained with intracellular TNF-α and IFN-γ Abs and analyzed by flow cytometry. Data were acquired with a CytoFLEX flow cytometer and analyzed using FlowJo software.

Confocal Microscopy

Single-cell suspensions from TDLNs were incubated at 4 °C for 30 min with FITC-conjugated anti-TCF1 antibody and APC-conjugated anti-CD8 Abs (both 1:100 diluted in PBS containing 5% FBS and 0.05% Triton-X-100). After rinsing three times with PBS, the cells were stained using Prolong Gold Antifade Reagent with DAPI and slides were cover slipped for imaging with the Zeiss LSM700 confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, BW, Germany), as we previously described. CD8+ T cells were purified from single-cell suspensions of TDLNs using the EasySep™ Mouse CD8+ T Cell Isolation Kit (Stemcell Technologies) and then incubated with MitoTracker Green FM (10 nM, Life Technologies Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA) for 10 min at 37 °C in the dark, Hoechst 33,342 solution (5 µg/mL Life Technologies Inc., Carlsbad, CA, USA) for 5 min at 37 °C in the dark, plated on microscope slides and imaged with the Zeiss LSM700 confocal microscope (Carl Zeiss, Oberkochen, BW, Germany).

Statistical analysis

GraphPad Prism version 8 (GraphPad Software Inc. La Jolla, CA) was used for statistical analysis. Tumor growth curves were first analyzed with a two-way ANOVA, and groups were compared with Tukey’s post-hoc test. Kaplan–Meier survival curves were analyzed with the log-rank test. The Student’s t test was used to compare different experimental groups. Multiple comparisons were conducted using a one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test. A p value < 0.05 and p < 0.01 was considered to be significant or very significant, respectively. Results are expressed as the mean ± standard error of the mean (SEM).

Results

IRE-induced apoptotic BLOVA melanoma cells are immunogenic

To assess whether IRE-induced apoptotic tumor cells are immunogenic, BLOVA melanoma cells were first electroporated in vitro at various voltages (0 V, 600 V, 1200 V and 1800 V) using a Bio-Rad Electroporator (Fig. 1A). Melanoma cells were then stained with Annexin-V and propidium iodine (PI) and analyzed by flow cytometry. We demonstrated that the percentage of ΔBLOVA cells positive for both Annexin-V (early apoptosis marker) and PI (late stage of apoptosis marker) was positively correlated with the applied voltage (Fig. 1A). When electroporated at 1200 V, which is the same voltage used for in vivo IRE, roughly 88% of BLOVA cells were found to apoptotic (Fig. 1A). To assess whether these apoptotic cells are also immunogenic, we i.v. injected 0 V or 1200 V-treated ΔBLOVA cells into B6 mice and then measured OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses 8 days later by flow cytometry using FITC-anti-CD8 antibody and OVA-specific PE-Tetramer for T cell staining. We showed that 1200 V-treated ΔBLOVA cells stimulate weak OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses (0.83%) compared to control mice (Fig. 1B), indicating that IRE-induced apoptotic ΔBLOVA cells are immunogenic and are capable of stimulating OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses.

DCs with phagocytosed apoptotic BLOVA cells stimulate T cell responses

The observed OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses may be mediated in vivo by mouse DC phagocytosis of ΔBLOVA cells. Therefore, we in vitro labeled BLOVA tumor cells with the green fluorescent dye CFSE and electroporated them with 1200 V to promote apoptosis, and co-cultured these CFSE-labeled ΔBLOVA cells overnight with bone-marrow-derived DCs. DCs that phagocytosed CFSE-labeled ΔBLOVA (DC/ΔBLOVA) cells were quantified flow cytometrically by detecting CFSE-labeled ΔBLOVA fragments (green) (Fig. 1C) and were visualized in DC/ΔBLOVA cells by confocal microscopy (Fig. 1D). To confirm that ΔBLOVA cells are capable of stimulating OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses, we i.v. immunized B6 mice with unloaded DCs or DCs that had phagocytosed ΔBLOVA cells (DC/ΔBLOVA) and assessed OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses 7 days post-immunization. These analyses demonstrated that vaccination of mice with the DC/ΔBLOVA cells stimulated very moderate CD8+ T cell responses (1.32%) (Fig. 1E).

IRE induces massive tumor cell apoptosis in vivo

We performed in vivo IRE when BLOVA melanomas reached ~ 5 mm in diameter using our previously constructed, custom-made IRE device suitable for administration in small animal tumor models (Fig. 2A). We set our IRE device parameters (voltage, 1200 V; pulse duration, 90 μs; pulse repetition frequency, 1 Hz; number of repetition pulses, 100) such that they were identical to those of the NanoKnife (Angiodynamics Inc.) which is used clinically for IRE treatment of cancer patients. To assess IRE-induced tumor cell death, we collected tumors 2 days post-IRE for histopathological examination. We demonstrated that IRE induced a large area of apoptosis at the central part of tumor tissues (~ 80% reduction) (white arrows) with some remaining, intact tissue (~ 20%) at the periphery of the tumor (black arrows) (Fig. 2B).

IRE + Combo induces T cell responses and eradicates BLOVA melanomas

To improve upon the weak IRE-induced CD8+ T cell responses we observed, BLOVA-bearing mice were treated with (i) IRE only, (ii) IRE + TLR9 agonist (IRE + CpG), (iii) IRE + PD-L1-Ab, (iv) IRE + CD40-Ab (i.e. CD40-agonist), (v) IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab and (vi) IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab/CD40-Ab (IRE + Combo) (Fig. 2C) and OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses were measured 8 days later by flow cytometry. We found that IRE + CpG and IRE + CD40-Ab stimulated comparable OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses (2.1% vs 1.85%), which were stronger than those mediated by the IRE + PD-L1-Ab treatment (1.44%) (Fig. 2D). Combined treatment with IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab stimulated an even stronger OVA-specific CD8+ T cell response (2.67%) (Fig. 2D), showing a cooperative effect of targeting the TLR9 agonist and PD-1 blockade in OVA-specific CD8+ T cell immunity. Interestingly, IRE + Combo acted synergistically to stimulate the most potent OVA-specific CD8+ T cell response (6.89%) of all treatment regimens (Fig. 2D). To further investigate their therapeutic effecacy, we performed the same IRE ablation treatments and monitored daily tumor growth using a digital caliper. We found that the extent to which various treatments inhibited melanoma growth and mouse survival displayed similar trends that mirrored the magnitude of the OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses (Fig. 2D-F). Briefly, mice treated with IRE alone showed minimal inhibition of tumor growth, and all animals died within 10 days of treatment (Fig. 2E, ). IRE + CpG and IRE + CD40-Ab treatments inhibited tumor growth to the same extent, and all mice died roughly 14–19 days after treatment (Fig. 2E, ). The IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab ablation without inclusion of CD40-agonist failed to eliminate BLOVA melanomas, though tumor growth was significantly inhibited and mouse survival was markedly prolonged (Fig. 2E, ). Importantly, the IRE + Combo ablation resulted in complete eradication of s.c. BLOVA melanoma in 5/8 of treated mice (Fig. 2E, ). These data collectively indicate that the CD40-agonist plays a critical role in promoting the therapeutic efficacy of this combinatorial IRE + Combo ablation treatment.

IRE + Combo ablation of local melanoma eradicates lung metastases

To assess whether the IRE + Combo treatment of primary melanoma tumors affects existing lung metastases, we i.v. injected highly lung metastatic BLOVA cells into both B6 mice bearing small s.c. BLOVA tumors and naïve, control mice (Fig. 3A). Around one week after i.v. injection when s.c. BLOVA melanomas reached 5 mm in diameter, we performed IRE + Combo treatment of s.c. primary BLOVA tumors as previously described. Mouse lungs were then collected two weeks later and metastatic black melanoma colonies on the tissue surface (Fig. 3B) were visually counted and examined histopathologically (Fig. 3C). We demonstrated that while numerous black BLOVA melanoma colonies were present on the surface of lung tissues in naïve, control mice none were found in IRE + Combo-treated mice (Fig. 3B), indicating that the IRE + Combo ablation of local s.c. BLOVA melanomas was also capable of eradicating BLOVA lung metastases.

CD40-agonist promotes cDC1 responses in TDLNs

TDLNs are lymph organs where early, anti-tumor immunity is initiated through DC-stimulated CD8+ T cell responses. CD8+ and CD103+ cDC1 cell subsets were previously reported to be superior with respect to cross-presentation of tumor antigen and stimulation of CD8+ T cell responses predominantly in TDLNs, though some CD8+ naïve T (Tn) are primed in tumors. To assess whether IRE + Combo ablation promotes such cDC1 cell responses, single cell suspensions were prepared from TDLNs of mice 8 days after IRE, IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab and IRE + Combo treatments and analyzed by flow cytometry. Cells were initially gated as CD11c+ prior to the analysis of the CD8+ and CD103+ cDC1 subsets. Interestingly, we demonstrated that all three treatments induced responses from not only CD8+ and CD103+ cDC1s but also a rarely described CD8+CD103+ cDC1 cell cohort in mouse TDLNs (Fig. 4A), whose presence was once reported in mouse small intestine. Inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment stimulated stronger responses of CD8+ and CD8+CD103+ cDC1s (11.6% and 17.1% in total CD11c+ DCs) when compared to the IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab (7.7% and 12.1%) and IRE (6.6% and 9.0%) control treatment groups (Fig. 4A). To further assess the cDC1 phenotype, CD8+, CD103+ and CD8+CD103+ cDC1 cell subsets were analyzed by flow cytometry to quantify the relative expression of the DC maturation markers CD54, CD80 and MHC II, the DC co-stimulatory molecules 41BBL and CD40 as well as the anti-apoptotic molecule Bcl-xL downstream of CD40 signaling (Fig. 4B, 4C, 4D). These analyses revealed that in general the three cDC1 subsets exhibited the most pronounced increase in the expression of the DC maturation markers, 41BBL, CD40 and Bcl-xL in response to the IRE + Combo ablation treatment (Fig. 4B, 4C, 4D). Our data thus indicate that inclusion of the CD40-agonist in IRE + Combo plays a critical role in promoting the maturation and responses of all cDC1 cells, including the unexpected CD8+CD103+ cDC1 subset.

CD40-agonist triggers potent TRM cell responses in TDLNs

The CD8+ T cell surface molecules CD44 and CD62L (L-selectin) are commonly used to define CD8+ T cell subsets such as CD44−CD62L+ naïve T (Tn), CD44+CD62L− effector memory T (TEM), CD44+CD62L+ central memory T (TCM) and the recently characterized CD44−CD62L− pre-effector T (pTE) or TE cells. Another CD8+ TM cell subset called CD8+ TRM cells express CD69 and CD103, two tissue-resident molecules which promote their retention in peripheral and lymphoid tissues. CD8+ TRM cells showed prompt activation, more potent T cell responses than TCM cells upon antigen reencounter and stronger killing activity against tumors than regular TE cells. TDLNs are also lymph organs where CD8+CD103+ TRM cells accumulated during tumorigenesis and following anti-tumor vaccination or immunotherapy. TCF1 is a transcriptional factor that sustains TRM cell-directed anti-tumor immunity. To assess IRE + Combo-induced CD44+CD62L+ TCM and CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses, single cell suspensions were prepared from TDLNs 8 days after various IRE treatments of local s.c. BLOVA melanomas and analyzed by flow cytometry. We demonstrated that the IRE + Combo treatment stimulated stronger CD44+CD62L+ TCM and CD44+CD62L− TEM cell responses (11.4% and 57.8%) from the whole CD8+ T cell population than the IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab (7.9% and 23.0%) and IRE (4.21% and 17.4%) control treatment groups (Fig. 5A). In contrast, the IRE + Combo-stimulated CD44−CD62L− pTE cell response (32.8%) was weaker than that of the two control treatment groups (69.1% and 78.4%) (Fig. 5A). Further gating of the CD44+CD62L+ TCM cell population to measure the expression of PE-Tetramer, an OVA-specific marker, found that almost all of these cells were PE-Tetramer positive and thus OVA-specific TCM cells. To detect the frequency of CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells in the total TCM cell population, we performed flow cytometry analysis of sorted CD44+CD62L+ TCM cells (Fig. 5B). Although a CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell population could not be clearly defined in IRE, IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab and IRE + Combo images, we were still able to define it by setting up a negative threshold for both CD103 and TCF1 derived from sorted CD44−CD62L− pTE and CD44+CD62L− TEM cells (Fig. 5B, lower two panels). These analyses revealed that IRE + Combo also stimulated stronger CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses (32.1%) in the whole CD8+ TCM cell population than the two control treatment groups (15.3% and 6.7%), while eliciting negligible CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses (2.3% and 0%) derived from CD44+CD62L− TEM and CD44−CD62L− pTE cell subsets, respectively (Fig. 5B). These data collectively indicate that adding CD40-agonist to the IRE + Combo ablation treatment triggers potent CD44+CD62L+ TCM-derived CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses in TDLNs.

CD40-stimulated TRM cells exhibit elevated mitochondrial content and enhanced expression of cytokines and 41BB-related molecules

To further characterize these CD44+CD62L+ TCM-derived CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells, we quantified the expression of T cell effector cytokines TNF-α and IFN-γ. These analyses revealed that inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE treatment regimen was critical to eliciting the strongest response with respect to cytokine production (Fig. 5C). We next measured the abundance of the T cell co-stimulatory molecule 41BB and its downstream regulators TRAF1, p38-MAPK and PGC1α (a critical regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis) by flow cytometry. We found that inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment resulted in more robust expression of all these molecules in TRM cells (Fig. 5C). It has been reported that mitochondrial biogenesis is actively promoted in TM cells, leading to higher organelle content in support of FAO to preserve cellular energy homeostasis. To assess mitochondrial content, we stained single cell suspensions derived from TDLNs with the membrane potential-dependent dye MitoTracker Green and then analyzed them by flow cytometry (Fig. 5D) and confocal microscopy (Fig. 5E). We found that mitochondrial mass was indeed higher in CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells exposed to the IRE + Combo treatment when compared to the two control treatment groups (Fig. 5D, 5E). Our data thus indicate that the CD40-agonist promotes production of the T cell effector cytokines TNF-α and IFN-γ, augments 41BB signaling and promotes the activity of its downstream regulators while also stimulating mitochondrial biogenesis, a well established response of CD8+ TM cells that reflects their reliance on FAO metabolism in CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells for TRM persistence.

Discussion

CD40 is a cell surface, co-stimulatory molecule of DCs which belongs to the TNFR superfamily. CD40 signals through its ligand CD40L which originates from CD4+ Th1 cells to license DCs. The licensed DCs then up-regulate expression of the DC maturation markers CD54, CD80 and MHC II along with the cytokine IL-12 to directly stimulate potent CD8+ T cell responses. DCs can be similarly licensed to promote potent anti-tumor CD8+ T cell responses upon activation of CD40 by CD40-agonist. Recently, Narayana et al. reported that IRE ablation combined with CD40-agonist inhibited liver metastasis in a mouse pancreatic cancer orthotopic model, but not a mouse metastatic melanoma model.

To enhance the therapeutic efficacy of IRE ablation in our BLOVA metastatic melanoma model, we developed a novel protocol that combined IRE ablation with a new immune adjuvant Combo (CpG/PD-L1-Ab/CD40 agonist) capable of simultaneously triggering three distinct immune mechanisms. These include (i) CpG-stimulated CD8+ T cell responses, (ii) PD-1 blockade-reduced T cell exhaustion and (iii) CD40-agonist-licensed DC maturation. It has also been shown that combining CD40-agonist with PD-1 blockade enhanced T cell-mediated anti-tumor immunity. In this study, we investigated the therapeutic efficacy of IRE + Combo against BLOVA melanoma and showed that this combinatorial treatment stimulated potent OVA-specific CD8+ T cell responses in peripheral blood, leading to eradication of local s.c. BLOVA melanoma in 5/8 of melanoma-bearing mice. In addition to eradicating the s.c. melanoma, the IRE + Combo treatment regimen also induced systemic anti-tumor immunity which led to the elimination of lung BLOVA melanoma metastases.

cDC1 is a superior subset of DCs capable of cross-presenting tumor antigens and stimulating potent CD8+ T cell responses in both TDLNs and tumors. In this study, we demonstrated that three types of IRE ablation treatments (IRE, IRE + CpG/PD-L1-Ab and IRE + Combo) all triggered responses from CD8+ and CD103+ cDC1 subsets as well as the rarely observed double-positive CD8+CD103+ cDC1 subset. We also showed that inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment regimen triggered a higher frequency of CD8+ and CD8+CD103+ cDC1 cell subsets and increased their relative expression of cDC1 maturation markers, the co-stimulatory factors 41BBL and CD40 factors and the anti-apoptotic molecule Bcl-xL, findings that are consistent with a previous report. These data collectively suggest that the CD40-agonist stimulates cDC1 activity by promoting robust maturation and prolonging cell survival to ensure potent stimulation of CD8+ T cell responses.

It has recently been demonstrated that CD8+CD103+ TRM cells enhance the cytotoxicity and improve immunity towards metastatic melanoma to prolong patient survival. In this study, we provide the first evidence that inclusion of CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment regimen promoted a higher frequency of CD44+CD62L+ TCM-derived CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells in TDLNs. We further show that these TRM cells produced greater amounts of the T cell effector cytokines TNF-α and IFN-γ, which is consistent with their stronger anti-tumor cytotoxicity than regular TE cells previously reported by others. CD40-agonist-stimulated CD103+TCF1+ TRM cells also exhibited an upregulation in the expression of the T cell co-stimulatory 41BB molecule and its downstream regulators TRAF1, p38-MAPK and PGC1α along with higher mitochondrial content which is a common TRM cell characteristic required to support preferential FAO metabolism. Co-stimulatory 41BB is a cell surface molecule of CD8+ T cells that also belongs to the TNFR family, and its ligand 41BBL originating from DCs has been found to enhance CD8+41BB+ T cell proliferation, survival and memory formation. Therefore, the potent CD103+TCF1+ TRM responses we observed in this study in response to CD40-agonist are most likely derived from strengthening the efficiency of interactions between CD40-agonist-enhanced 41BBL of cDC1s and CD40-agonist-enhanced 41BB of TRMs, in turn leading to activation of the TRAF1-p38-MAPK-PGC1α pathway in TRM cells for TRM persistence, as previously reported.

The tumor microenvironment (TME) is composed of various immunosuppressive immune cells and its conversion is a major obstacle with respect to immunotherapy of solid cancers. This cohort of cells includes M2 macrophages, myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and CD4+ regulatory T (Treg) cells, which all express suppressive PD-L1 and secrete the inhibitory cytokines TGF-β and IL-10. In addition, immunotherapy resistance is further potentiated by tumor-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), which are a major component of the TME stroma and inhibit T cell infiltration and function by creating a physical barrier of dense extracellular matrix (ECM) and releasing immunosuppressive paracrine cytokines. Our data argue that the IRE + Combo treatment regimen which includes the cooperative or synergistic effects of CpG/pIC, PD-1 blockade and CD40-agonist can eradicate metastatic melanoma, leading us to speculate that it has the capacity to modulate the immunosuppressive nature of the TME. This modulatory effect likely relies on (i) CD40-agonist to convert macrophages from a suppressive M2 to an immunogenic M1 state, reduce the abundance of MDSCs, Treg cells and CAFs and remodel the tumor stroma, (ii) CpG to promote the M2 to M1 macrophage transition and (iii) PD-1 blockade to promote the M2 to M1 macrophage transition, precursor exhausted T (TPEX) cell expansion and differentiation into exhausted T (TEX) cells with better killing activity. We are now conducting a systematic analysis of suppressive immune cell profiling by flow cytometry, as we previously described, as well as a 3D analysis of immune cell interactions by single cell RNA sequencing to elucidate the cellular and molecular mechanisms underlying our IRE + Combo-induced eradication of metastatic melanoma.

Taken together, we establish that inclusion of the CD40-agonist in the IRE + Combo treatment regimen directly boosts responses from cDC1 subsets, including the unexpected CD8+CD103+ cDC1 cell cohort, and indirectly triggers the unexposed potent CD44+CD62L+ TCM-derived 41BB+CD103+TCF1+ TRM cell responses likely via activation of the 41BB-mediated TRAF1-p38-MAPK-PGC1α pathway. Therefore, CD40 is a promising molecular signaling node to leverage for the improvement of IRE ablation therapy, and the IRE + Combo protocol represents a novel approach with enhanced efficacy for the treatment of solid malignancies like melanoma, lung, liver, breast, pancreas and prostate cancers.
