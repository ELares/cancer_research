# Harnessing the innate immune system: a novel bispecific antibody targeting CD47 and CD24 for selective tumor clearance.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) abundantly infiltrate tumors and possess potent antitumor capabilities. "Don't eat me" signals like CD47 allow tumors to evade macrophages and proliferate unchecked. CD47 is upregulated in many tumors and interacts with the SIRPα expressed on macrophages to restrict effector function. Similarly, CD24 interacts with the Siglec-10 on TAMs to inhibit engulfment. Despite their potential, there is still a lack of effective therapeutics targeting macrophages. Recent clinical trials targeting CD47 have demonstrated limited efficacy and significant side effects in solid tumors, primarily due to the expression of CD47 on healthy cells such as red blood cells (RBCs). We therefore developed novel anti-CD47 variable domain of heavy chain of heavy-chain antibodies (vHHs) with strong ligand-blocking activity while demonstrating minimal binding to RBCs and incorporated these vHHs to generate an anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific antibody that preserves Fc-effector function and achieves improved tumor targeting while maintaining the blockade of antiphagocytic signals elicited by both CD47 and CD24.

**METHODS**: Yeast display was employed to generate vHHs targeting CD47 and fully human monoclonal antibodies against CD24, respectively. The antigen binding epitopes of the vHHs to CD47 were predicted using AlphaFold3. Bispecific antibodies were designed, constructed, and characterized in vitro. Antitumor efficacy was evaluated in a human immune cell reconstitution mouse model, while safety was evaluated using a humanized syngeneic mouse model. Furthermore, the underlying mechanisms and alterations in tumor microenvironment were explored ex vivo.

**RESULTS**: VHHs targeting CD47 and a fully human antibody against CD24 were identified, all exhibiting potent ligand-blocking activity. The bispecific antibody BiAb-103C, engineered on a human IgG1 scaffold, had strong binding to CD47+CD24+ tumor cells and could effectively inhibit the CD47-SIRPα interaction. Fc-effector activity was observed towards CD24 (but not CD47) single-positive cells to promote phagocytosis and antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity of CD47+CD24+ tumor cells. In mice, antibody candidates demonstrated notable antitumor activity alongside favorable safety observations.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our study presents the discovery of an anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific antibody that offers a promising therapeutic strategy to address the challenges associated with both the efficacy and safety of CD47-targeting agents, offers insight into macrophage-driven cancer immunotherapy, and could potentially provide a therapeutic option for patients non-responsive to immunotherapy.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Macrophage “don’t-eat-me” checkpoints, such as CD47 and CD24, enable tumors to evade innate immune clearance. Although anti-CD47 agents have shown therapeutic activity, their efficacy is limited by antigen-sink effects and on-target hematologic toxicity. Retaining Fc effector function can enhance antitumor efficacy but also increases safety risks when CD47 on healthy cells is engaged. In contrast, CD24 is more tumor-selective, and dual blockade of CD47 and CD24 has been shown to promote macrophage-mediated phagocytosis.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This study develops anti-CD47 vHHs and an IgG1 bispecific antibody (BiAb-103C) targeting CD47/CD24. The variable domain of heavy chain of heavy-chain antibodies (vHHs) potently blocks CD47-SIRPα while showing minimal red-blood-cell binding. BiAb-103C preserves Fc effector function through the CD24 arm yet avoids Fc activity on CD47 single-positive cells, thereby enhancing macrophage phagocytosis and NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity against CD47+CD24+ tumor cells while sparing CD47+CD24⁻ healthy cells (including red blood cells). In vivo, BiAb-103C demonstrates a favorable safety profile versus a 5F9 analog and superior antitumor efficacy in Hu-PBL models.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

This study proposes a design strategy that effectively decouples Fc-mediated efficacy from CD47-related toxicity, advancing the development of next-generation macrophage-targeted immunotherapies. It provides both evidence and a promising molecular candidate supporting the application of CD47/CD24 bispecific antibodies in solid tumors, particularly offering new therapeutic options for patients with poor responses to T cell-based therapies.

Introduction

 Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) targeting programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) or its ligand, programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), have emerged as a powerful and promising therapeutic strategy for cancer. Despite significant breakthroughs in the clinic, immune checkpoint inhibitors are only able to elicit responses in a subset of patients, with growing evidence highlighting tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) as critical mediators of the lack of response to PD-1/PD-L1 therapy through multiple immunosuppressive mechanisms.

Tumor-associated myeloid cells represent one of the most abundant cellular compartments within the tumor microenvironment, among which TAMs constitute the largest subpopulation. TAMs are highly plastic and can adopt either pro-inflammatory (M1-like) or anti-inflammatory (M2-like) phenotypes. M1-like macrophages exert antitumor effects by secreting pro-inflammatory cytokines and enhancing antigen presentation. In contrast, M2-like macrophages facilitate tumor progression by promoting angiogenesis and shaping an immunosuppressive microenvironment. In many solid tumors, TAMs are skewed toward the M2-like, immunosuppressive phenotype, which is associated with poor clinical outcomes. Nevertheless, TAMs also exhibit potent phagocytic activity and play a crucial role in immune surveillance. Therefore, harnessing macrophages represents a promising immunotherapeutic strategy.

CD47 is a macrophage immune suppressor widely expressed on the surface of many cell types, functioning as a “marker of self” to avoid phagocytic clearance and maintain cellular homeostatic processes. Tumor cells often overexpress CD47 to avoid immune attack by hijacking the CD47-SIRPα axis. Blocking the CD47/SIRPα interaction with anti-CD47 antibodies or SIRPα fusion protein may stimulate macrophages to engulf cancer cells, prime antitumor immune responses, and trigger substantial tumor clearance.

Although CD47-targeting therapies have shown their potential, the emergence of toxicity through the ubiquitous expression of CD47 has limited their clinical utility. The most advanced clinical candidate, magrolimab, in combination with azacitidine, demonstrates excellent efficacy in treatment-naive patients with high-risk myelodysplastic syndromes (MDS) and acute myeloid leukemia (AML), with a 100% objective response rate in MDS and a 69% objective response rate in AML. Similarly, the combination of magrolimab and rituximab shows significant efficacy in patients with relapsed or refractory B-cell non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma, achieving a 50% objective response, with 36% of patients achieving a complete response. However, further clinical development has been paused due to safety concerns, which include significant hematological toxicity, such as anemia and thrombocytopenia, as well as hepatotoxicity, evidenced by elevated alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) levels. Additionally, studies suggest that blocking the CD47-SIRPα interaction alone may not be sufficient for effective antitumor immunity, as Fc-FcγR interactions are also a critical component. However, maintaining Fc effector function may also lead to side effects by depleting CD47-expressing healthy cells. To further mitigate the toxicity risks of anti-CD47 agents, strategies such as the targeting of differentiated epitopes, probodies, bispecific antibodies, and pH-dependent antibodies have been explored to improve tumor specificity. However, further preclinical and clinical testing is needed to determine their efficacy and safety.

Prior research has also identified CD24 as another “don’t eat me” macrophage signal and synergizes with CD47 blockade. Compared with CD47, CD24 is minimally expressed in normal tissues and is significantly higher than CD47 in various solid tumors, showing good tumor selectivity. Recent findings suggest that CD24 primarily acts as a target for opsonization and, when combined with anti-CD47, can induce maximal levels of phagocytosis, suggesting that dual targeting of both CD47 and CD24 may represent a promising strategy to overcome mechanisms of tumor immune evasion and enhance macrophage phagocytosis.

In this study, we have discovered unique anti-CD47 vHHs with low Fc-mediated activity and RBC binding that can be used for constructing bispecifics. These CD47 binders could be paired with antibodies towards tumor-associated antigen that would benefit from keeping Fc-effector function. We have engineered a novel therapeutic by fusing the anti-CD47 vHH to a fully human anti-CD24 antibody with an IgG1 backbone. This bispecific antibody exhibits enhanced selectivity and efficacy for CD47+CD24+ malignant cells while de-risking potential toxicity concerns towards CD47+CD24− healthy cells. Our bispecific design offers a clinically viable solution for macrophage-targeted therapies, overcoming key challenges in CD47 pathway modulation while harnessing the synergistic potential of phagocytic checkpoint blockade.

Materials and methods

Methods are detailed in the online supplemental methods section.

Results

Generation and characterization of potent CD47 antagonists that preferentially bind to tumor cells, with strongly reduced binding to RBCs

Variable domain of heavy chain of heavy-chain antibodies (vHHs) has attracted attention in drug development due to their comparable target binding capabilities to conventional monoclonal antibodies while being significantly smaller and more stable than binding modules that are derived from Fab regions. Alpacas were immunized with recombinant huCD47 protein, followed by the construction of a yeast display library (figure 1A and online supplemental figure 1). This library was screened against both human and cynomolgus CD47, resulting in the identification of vHH sequences with species cross-reactivity, encompassing a wide range of binding affinities and epitope coverage. Two promising binders, anti-CD47-003 and anti-CD47-103, were selected and subsequently fused to a human g1-Fc domain to create heavy chain IgGs (hcIgG) (online supplemental figure 2).

Using bio-layer interferometry (BLI), a real-time biomolecular interaction assay with high sensitivity and specificity, anti-CD47-003N and anti-CD47-103N demonstrated high binding affinity to human CD47, with dissociation constants (KD) of 16 nM and 73 nM, respectively (figure 1B). Furthermore, both hcIgGs exhibited dose-dependent binding to Raji lymphoma cells expressing endogenous CD47, comparable to a benchmark (5F9) (figure 1C). To assess the antagonistic activity of these candidates, a cell-based CD47/SIRPα blocking assay was performed, revealing that both candidates exhibited a blocking profile similar to that of 5F9 (figure 1D). Next, the phagocytosis-promoting effects of the two vHHs were determined through flow cytometry-based experiments (online supplemental figure 3). It is important to note that 5F9 exhibited severe hematological toxicity in the clinic due to its binding to CD47-expressing RBCs. Both anti-CD47-003N and anti-CD47-103N exhibited at least 100-fold lower binding to RBCs compared with 5F9, which suggests a potentially lower risk of hematological toxicity (figure 1E).

Binding epitope prediction of anti-CD47 vHHs to the CD47/SIRPα interface

Given the markedly different binding profiles of anti-CD47-003 and anti-CD47-103 on tumor cells versus healthy RBCs, we hypothesized that these clones may bind to a unique epitope on CD47. This would allow for preferential association with tumor-specific CD47. In contrast, 5F9 has similar binding to and may interact with an epitope present on both Raji cells and normal RBCs.

The interaction sites of our antibodies with CD47 were predicted using AlphaFold3. Notably, the two vHHs share a common epitope, which overlaps with the interface between CD47 and SIRPα (PDB:2JJS). However, 5F9, which also blocks the interaction between CD47 and SIRPα, has a different and distinct binding epitope. The residues D51, K56, and T102 of CD47 are involved in the interaction with anti-CD47-003, while these residues are not engaged by 5F9. Additionally, anti-CD47-103 exhibits a larger binding interface that encompasses additional CD47 residues, including E35, K46, D51, T58, and E97, which are not interaction sites for 5F9. Notably, both anti-CD47-003 and anti-CD47-103 also share several binding sites on CD47 with 5F9, including K39, K41, E97, L101, and E104 (figure 1F,G and online supplemental figure 4).

Primary interaction with CD47 is mediated by the CDR2 region of anti-CD47-003, while the CDR3 region plays no significant role. In contrast, both CDR2 and CDR3 of anti-CD47-103 interact with CD47 via multiple hydrogen bonds. Structural analysis using PyMOL Surface Area (PlSA) shows that anti-CD47-103 buries 869.1 Å², compared with 656.5 Å² for anti-CD47-003 (figure 1F). Anti-CD47 vHH as a bispecific component.

Based on the unique epitopes of the two vHHs, we constructed bispecific antibodies with an irrelevant monoclonal antibody (mAb) to test for their bispecific potential. The formats of these bispecific antibodies are illustrated in online supplemental figure 2. The flow cytometric results indicated that the different bispecific antibodies exhibited distinct binding patterns. Specifically, most bispecific antibodies showed reduced binding to Raji cells except BiAb-103C, which maintained its binding activity. As for isolated healthy RBCs, the bispecific antibodies constructed with bivalent anti-CD47-003 exhibited some binding to RBCs when at both the N-terminus and C-terminus but were significantly reduced compared with the parental hcIgGs. Importantly, the bispecific antibodies that were constructed with anti-CD47-103 showed no binding to RBCs in all the tested formats (online supplemental figure 5A and B).

To further confirm these findings, we fused the vHHs to the C-terminus of the IgG1 Fc region only, naming them anti-CD47-003C and anti-CD47-103C (online supplemental figure 2). We then repeated the flow cytometry experiments, and the results were consistent with previous observations in online supplemental figure 5. Both anti-CD47-003C and anti-CD47-103C retained their binding for Raji cells (figure 1H), while anti-CD47-003C showed significantly reduced binding to RBCs and anti-CD47-103C showed no binding to RBCs compared with the hcIgGs (figure 1H).

Characterization of Fc function for CD47 antagonists

Agents that target CD47 have typically adopted human IgG4 or IgG1-null Fc regions to minimize on-target toxicity that is related to the targeting of CD47-expressing healthy cells. However, the Fc region of a CD47-blocking agent is also considered a potential modulator of therapeutic efficacy. Therefore, balancing Fc-mediated effector functions against CD47+ tumor cells with minimized on-target toxicity toward normal cells may offer a broader therapeutic window. To investigate this, luciferase-based assays were performed to evaluate FcγR signal activation in the presence of target cells. The results demonstrated that anti-CD47 antibodies with an IgG1 Fc exhibited stronger antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) (Fc gamma receptor IIIa, FcγRIIIa) and antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP) (Fc gamma receptor IIa, FcγRIIa) activation compared with those with an LALA-mutated IgG1 Fc (reduced Fc function) (figure 2A and B). ADCC/ADCP activity towards RBCs was also detected. The Fc-effector function was significantly diminished or abolished when the vHHs were fused to the C-terminus of the Fc. In contrast, 5F9-hIgG4 showed weak ADCC/ADCP signal activation against both Raji cells and RBCs in these assays.

Subsequently, we performed an ADCC cytotoxicity assay using peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) as effector cells, and the results were consistent with the previous findings in the reporter cell assay in which the vHHs fused to the C-terminus had significantly reduced ADCC activity (figure 2C). In a flow cytometry-based macrophage phagocytosis assay, hcIgs with an IgG1 wild-type Fc promoted phagocytosis more effectively than those with effector-null Fc domains, while vHHs fused to C-terminus showed no ADCP activity. Notably, 5F9-hIgG4 also induced phagocytosis in this cell-based assay (figure 2D).

In summary, these results highlight the role of FcγR signaling in mediating antitumor immune responses. Importantly, maybe due to its unique binding epitope, the fusion of our anti-CD47 vHH at C-terminus (especially for anti-CD47-103) did not induce Fc-effector function against CD47+ cells even in the presence of an active Fc. This allows the design of bispecifics that have FcγR activity towards a second target while maintaining blockade of CD47 without the risk of depleting RBCs or toxicity to other CD47+ healthy cells.

Identification of a tumor-selective anti-CD24 antibody and its synergistic effect with CD47 blockade

In addition to the well-established CD47/SIRPα inhibitory axis, the CD24/Siglec-10 axis has recently been reported to play a significant role in enabling cancer cells to evade the immune system, acting as another “don’t eat me” signal and the combination of anti-CD24 with CD47 blockade facilitates maximal phagocytosis. Analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database reveals that both CD47 and CD24 are upregulated in various cancers compared with corresponding normal tissues (figure 2E). Furthermore, the co-expression of CD47 and CD24 across different tumors was identified using the SynEcoSys database (figure 2F and online supplemental figure 6).

The aim was to generate an anti-CD24 candidate as a backbone for fusing the anti-CD47 vHHs to form an anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific. To generate anti-CD24 IgGs, ATX-GL mice were immunized to generate fully human mAbs that were selected by yeast display. The lead anti-CD24 mAb was cloned into vectors with a human IgG1 framework and lambda light chains. We confirmed that anti-CD24 could bind to human CD24 with high affinity and a KD value of 3.7 nM as measured by BLI (online supplemental figure 7A). In flow cytometry assays, the lead is strongly bound to SK-OV-3 and HEK293-hCD24 cells. Additionally, a flow cytometry-based blocking assay showed that the anti-CD24 mAb effectively inhibited the interaction between Siglec-10 and HEK293-hCD24 cells (online supplemental figure 7B-D).

Unlike CD47, CD24 has limited distribution in healthy tissues/cells but is highly expressed in some tumors. However, CD24 is also expressed in human T and B cells at some level, and thus, we examined the binding of the anti-CD24 candidate to these cell types. The results indicated that the anti-CD24 candidate does not bind to human primary T cells, while it does bind to B cells; however, the binding was significantly weaker compared with the positive control, SWA11. Additionally, another anti-CD24 benchmark SN3, an mAb, has been reported to bind to CD24 at the sialic acid-modified epitope, shows interaction with both T and B cells (online supplemental figure 7E).

The anti-CD24 candidate induced ADCC against CD24+ tumor cells in reporter and PBMC-based cytotoxicity assays (online supplemental figure 8A and B). In addition, the anti-CD24 candidate may promote macrophage-mediated phagocytosis of SK-OV-3 tumor cells (online supplemental figure 8C), as shown by the colocalization of target cells and macrophages. To test whether the blockade of CD47 and CD24 simultaneously would enhance macrophage-mediated phagocytosis against tumor cells, we assessed phagocytic activity using a flow cytometric-based assay and observed enhanced phagocytosis by combining anti-CD24 and anti-CD47 (figure 2G). This supports the rationale for constructing anti-CD47/CD24 bispecifics.

Generation and characterization of anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific antibodies

To explore the potential of combining CD47 and CD24 blockade in a single bispecific, different formats, binding modules, Fc subtypes, and flexible Gly-Ala linkers were used to generate our bispecific candidates (figure 3A). Characterization assays were performed with multiple rounds of screening for binding and blocking activity, RBC binding, antitumor activity, and potential interaction with RBCs. Two human IgG1-based molecules, which have fused anti-CD47 vHHs to the C-terminus, named BiAb-003C and BiAb-103C, were further characterized (figure 3A).

An in vitro cell-based binding assay demonstrated that the binding activities of the bispecific antibodies were comparable to that of the parent anti-CD24 mAb (figure 3B). Consistent with earlier observations, BiAb-103C maintained binding to Raji cells, while some binding reduction was observed with BiAb-003C (figure 3C). Notably, the binding of bispecifics to isolated healthy RBCs was almost abolished compared with parental anti-CD47 mAbs (figure 3D). To exclude the influence of the secondary antibody binding activity on the bispecific antibody, BiAb-103C was directly labeled with Alexa Fluor 488 dye. Consistently, no detectable binding to RBCs was observed (figure 3E). Furthermore, BiAb-103C exhibited comparable CD47/SIRPα blocking activity to that of anti-CD47-103N, consistent with the binding data on Raji cells (figure 3C and F). The ELISA-based co-binding assay demonstrated that the bispecifics could simultaneously bind to CD47 and CD24 (figure 3G). Concordantly, the fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS)-based assay detected a double positive population, indicating simultaneous engagement of both antigens on live cells (online supplemental figure 9A). Finally, the bispecific antibodies showed superior binding and blocking activity to SK-OV-3 cells and HT-29 cells, which endogenously express CD47 and CD24, compared with parental anti-CD47 hcIgGs (figure 3H and I and online supplemental figure 9B and C).

Bispecific antibodies enhance phagocytosis and NK-cell-mediated clearance of CD47+CD24+ tumor cells without affecting CD47+CD24− cells

Human PBMC-derived macrophages were generated as described in the methods. BiAb-003C and BiAb-103C, which have anti-CD47 vHHs fused to the C-terminus, induced low or non-detectable phagocytic activity toward CD47+CD24− Raji cells. For CD47−CD24+ CT26-hCD24 cells, the bispecifics maintained phagocytic activity similar to the anti-CD24 mAbs. However, the bispecifics showed enhanced phagocytosis activity towards CD47+CD24+ double-positive SK-OV-3 tumor cells, compared with either anti-CD47 hcIgGs or anti-CD24 alone, suggesting an additive and/or synergistic activity between the arms targeting CD24 and CD47 (figure 4A). To further assess the potential risk of depleting CD47+CD24− cells, which include RBCs, the more sensitive luciferase-based assay was also conducted to measure FcγRIIa signal activation in the presence of target cells. Consistent with the phagocytosis result in figure 4A, BiAb-003C or BiAb-103C did not activate FcγRIIa signals in the presence of either Raji cells or RBCs (figure 4B).

Natural killer (NK) cells play a crucial role in immunotherapy. The IgG4 isotype selected for most anti-CD47 therapeutic antibodies in the clinic has limited effectiveness in inducing ADCC against tumor cells. To assess whether our bispecific antibodies could promote NK cell-mediated target cell killing, we conducted a CD16a NFAT Jurkat reporter cell-based assay. The two molecules with C-terminal vHHs demonstrated superior activation against CD47+CD24+ SK-OV-3 tumor cells compared with either anti-CD47 hcIgGs or anti-CD24 mAbs alone (figure 4C). Notably, BiAb-003C and BiAb-103C did not induce ADCC against CD47+CD24− Raji cells or RBCs, while the anti-CD47 hcIgG controls induced obvious ADCC signals, further highlighting the potential safety advantages of fusing the anti-CD47 vHHs at the C-terminus (figure 4C). To confirm the findings from the reporter cell assays, a direct killing assay was performed. Target cells were incubated with PBMCs. The results indicated that BiAb-003C and BiAb-103C significantly induced lysis of CD47+CD24+ cells and CD47−CD24+cells, demonstrating equal or improved efficacy compared with parental anti-CD47 and anti-CD24 antibodies, while exhibiting no cytotoxic effects on CD47+CD24− Raji cells (figure 4D and online supplemental figure 9D).

In summary, our anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific candidates, with a functional Fc, can enhance phagocytosis/ADCC activity towards CD47+CD24+ double-positive tumor cells compared with the mono-agents, while minimizing cytotoxicity against CD47+ single-positive cells, which include healthy RBCs.

Anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific exhibits lower toxicity risk in hCD47/hSIRPα mice

Next, we assessed the potential safety of the bispecifics in vivo in a human CD47/SIRPα double KI mouse model (figure 5A). In this study, hCD47/hSIRPα C57BL/6 mice were randomly assigned to four groups and injected intraperitoneally with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), equimolar amounts of monoclonal anti-CD47 (clone anti-CD47-103), anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific (clone anti-CD47-103), and 5F9 analog. To better evaluate our hypothesis, mouse IgG2a was used to generate the chimeric anti-CD47 hcIgG and anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific to preserve Fc effector function in mice, while hIgG4 was used for the 5F9 mAb that mimics the clinical candidate.

The 5F9-hIgG4 group exhibited significant weight loss after dosing, while anti-CD47-mIgG2a showed a trend of weight reduction at the study end, though not statistically significant. Notably, the bispecific antibody (BiAb-103C-mIgG2a) did not induce any detectable weight loss (figure 5B). Blood cell count analysis revealed that 5F9-hIgG4 caused significant erythrocyte depletion, with decreased RBC count, hematocrit, hemoglobin, and mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration, and increased mean corpuscular volume and red cell distribution width. A compensatory increase in nucleated RBCs was observed. In contrast, BiAb-103C-mIgG2a had minimal effects on these parameters (figure 5C and online supplemental figure 10).

For platelet analysis, both anti-CD47-103N-mIgG2a and 5F9-hIgG4 caused a decrease in platelet count and plateletcrit, along with increased mean platelet volume and platelet distribution width, indicating abnormal platelet morphology. In contrast, BiAb-103C-mIgG2a did not affect platelet parameters. Additionally, 5F9-hIgG4, but not bispecific treatment, caused a decrease in lymphocyte count. No significant changes were seen in monocytes, total leukocytes, or neutrophils (figure 5C and online supplemental figure 10).

After the monitoring phase, organ dissection revealed a significant increase in spleen weight in the 5F9-hIgG4 group (figure 5D), resembling splenomegaly seen in hemolytic anemia, possibly due to macrophage and CD47−/− erythrocyte accumulation. H&E staining of liver tissues showed cellular edema in all groups, likely from frequent blood sampling, but severe tissue necrosis was observed only in the 5F9-hIgG4 group (figure 5E), indicating significant hepatotoxicity, consistent with elevated ALT and AST in the magrolimab clinical trials. These findings demonstrate that BiAb-103C showed an excellent safety profile in humanized mouse models.

Anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific demonstrates efficacy in an Hu-PBL model

To evaluate BiAb-103C efficacy in inhibiting tumor growth, we used an Hu-PBL model with NPG mice reconstituted with human PBMC and implanted with RKO-hCD24 (CD47+CD24+) tumors. Briefly, RKO-hCD24 cells were mixed with PBMCs and co-inoculated subcutaneously into NPG mice. Additionally, PBMCs were administered via the tail vein weekly throughout the study. Tumor-bearing mice received equimolar doses two times a week. The bispecific antibody treatment demonstrated superior efficacy compared with the anti-CD24, anti-CD47 and anti-PD-1 single agents, as measured by tumor size (figure 6A–C).

Transcriptomic analysis of tumor tissues revealed that phagocytic cells were significantly increased in the BiAb-103C group. In contrast, T-cell infiltration was reduced in both the bispecific and anti-CD47 groups, consistent with previous reports indicating that anti-CD47 antibodies can mediate the clearance of chimeric antigen receptor T-cells (CAR-T cells) (figure 6D and online supplemental figure 11). Gene Ontology and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes enrichment analyses identified significant enrichment of gene sets and pathways related to innate immunity and phagocytic functions (figure 6E and F). Compared with the PBS group, genes associated with macrophage function were markedly upregulated following treatment, with genes involved in macrophage-mediated clearance (eg, FCGR3A, MRC1, C1QA, C1QC) and macrophage-driven proinflammatory responses (eg, IL1B, TLR4, TLR2, CD14, CLEC5A, SPP1) selectively enriched in the BiAb group (figure 6G). Despite evidence of T-cell infiltration in the anti-PD-1 group (online supplemental figure 11), this did not translate into substantial tumor control. In contrast, the bispecific antibody primarily engaged macrophage-mediated mechanisms, resulting in more effective tumor control (figure 6A–C).

In the NCL-H292 Hu-PBL model, the bispecific antibody also demonstrated superior efficacy compared with monotherapy (online supplemental figure 12A). Although the therapeutic advantage of the bispecific antibody over anti-CD47 monotherapy appears modest in this model, it is important to note that the anti-CD47 antibody used here contains an Fc domain with intact effector function, whereas most clinical trials employ Fc-null variants. Additionally, due to the species specificity of the anti-human CD47 antibody, it does not bind to murine CD47 on normal host cells such as RBCs, thus failing to replicate the “antigen sink” effect or Fc-mediated toxicities potentially encountered in human settings.

H&E staining revealed an increased tumor cell degeneration and necrosis in the antibody-treated group. Additionally, immunohistochemistry demonstrated a significant reduction in the expression of the cell proliferation marker Ki-67 in tumors treated with BiAb-103C compared with the other groups (online supplemental figure 12B and C).

The bispecific antibody induces immune cell infiltration and macrophage activation at the tumor site

To further investigate the mechanism underlying the potential antitumor effects of the treatments, we tested a surrogate agent in a CT26 mouse colon cancer model in wild-type BALB/c mice. An anti-mouse CD47 vHH named A4 was used as a surrogate CD47 binder. Given the absence of mouse CD24 expression in CT26 cells, human CD24 was overexpressed in the CT26 cell line. Additionally, anti-CD24 and A4 were engineered into the same format as BiAb-103C on a mouse IgG2a Fc backbone (BiAb surrogate).

After inoculating mice with CT26-hCD24 cells and administering treatments, tumor tissues were extracted for analysis. Immunofluorescence showed increased infiltration of F4/80+ macrophages in response to anti-CD47 and the BiAb surrogate, surpassing both the PBS and anti-CD24 groups. In contrast, CD206+ M2 macrophage infiltration decreased in the anti-CD24, anti-CD47, and BiAb surrogate groups, suggesting that these infiltrating macrophages were mainly of the M1 type, known for their pro-inflammatory and antitumor properties (figure 7A–C). Both anti-CD47 and the BiAb surrogate increased the M1/M2 ratio in the tumor microenvironment, consistent with the immunofluorescence data (figure 7D).

Finally, anti-CD47 treatment decreased, whereas the BiAb surrogate enhanced CD8+ T-cell infiltration. Anti-CD47 increased interferon-γ production by CD8+ T cells, and both anti-CD24 and the BiAb surrogate also showed increases, though not statistically significant. The BiAb surrogate group also induced significantly higher NK cell infiltration than anti-CD47 treatment alone (figure 7D).

Discussion

In this study, we generated two vHHs targeting CD47 by immunizing alpacas and high-throughput yeast display. The vHHs exhibit single-digit nanomolar affinity for the CD47 protein and demonstrate specific binding to tumor cells that endogenously express CD47. Furthermore, these vHHs effectively blocked the interaction between CD47 and SIRPα.

The ubiquitous expression of CD47 on normal cells, particularly RBCs, presents a significant challenge for therapeutic application, leading to on-target toxicity, and also acts as an antigen sink. Therefore, it is essential to minimize the binding to RBCs. To address this, we used a flow cytometry-based binding assay to identify and exclude vHHs with strong binding to RBCs. The selected vHHs exhibited weak binding to RBCs, enhancing their potential for therapeutic use. Based on predictions from AlphaFold3, they appear to recognize epitopes different from those targeted by the benchmark 5F9, although the mechanistic basis for their distinctive binding profiles remains unknown. Leveraging these findings, bispecific antibodies with different structures were constructed. Notably, when one of the candidates, anti-CD47-103, was fused to the C-terminus of Fc, it demonstrated undetectable binding to RBCs while retaining binding to tumor cells expressing CD47, such as Raji cells.

We systematically evaluated bispecific antibodies in multiple formats derived from selected vHH clones. In the 2+1 format, the shift from bivalent to monovalent CD47 engagement resulted in attenuated binding to both tumor cells and RBCs. In the 2+2 format, clone-specific functional differences were observed: clone anti-CD47-003 exhibited reduced binding to Raji cells regardless of vHH fusion orientation (N-terminal or C-terminal), while retaining detectable RBC binding. In contrast, clone anti-CD47-103 showed minimal RBC binding in both orientations, with C-terminal fusion preserving strong tumor cell binding. Furthermore, Fc effector function against CD47+ cells was partially retained with N-terminal fusion but was completely abolished with C-terminal fusion. Collectively, these results underscore the critical influence of bispecific format, fusion orientation, and epitope specificity on the binding and functional profiles of these antibodies, providing key insights for their rational design.

Blocking the “don’t eat me” signal on macrophages has been proposed as a promising antitumor strategy. In this study, we compared tumor cell elimination via ADCP, ADCC, and phagocytosis checkpoint blockade, highlighting the critical role of Fc effector function in antitumor activity. Most CD47-targeting agents in clinical trials use human IgG4 or IgG1 null Fc regions to minimize toxicity to CD47-expressing healthy cells and avoid hematotoxicity. Our anti-CD47 vHHs, especially clone anti-CD47-103 fused to the C-terminal of Fc, allow the maintenance of Fc effector function without inducing hematotoxicity or potential toxicity to normal cells and tissues. Safety assessment in human CD47/SIRPα double knock-in mice showed that the C-terminal fused anti-CD47 bispecific with a functional Fc backbone had a more favorable safety profile than 5F9 (IgG4 Fc). No obvious toxicity was observed with the bispecific, whereas 5F9 caused hematotoxicity, including erythropenia, thrombocytopenia, splenomegaly, and hepatic injury, similar to magrolimab in the clinical setting. Nevertheless, because the CD24 lead antibody does not cross-react with murine CD24, our current model cannot evaluate CD24-mediated toxicities. As CD47/CD24 double-humanized mouse strains are not yet available, future studies will employ CD24-humanized mouse models, and, ideally, double-humanized models once they become accessible, to enable a more comprehensive assessment of dual-target toxicities.

CD24 offers better tumor selectivity than CD47, being highly expressed in many tumors while remaining low or undetectable in most normal tissues, and has been successfully leveraged for bispecific antibody and antibody drug conjugate (ADC) design. It also serves as a “don’t eat me” checkpoint for macrophages, with synergistic effects observed between anti-CD24 and anti-CD47 agents. We aimed to create an anti-CD47/CD24 bispecific with a wild-type IgG1 Fc region to mediate Fc-dependent activity against CD24 but not CD47. The final design effectively avoided Fc activity on CD47 single-positive cells. The bispecific showed enhanced blockade of CD47/SIRPα, increased ADCC, and macrophage phagocytosis towards double-positive tumor cells compared with the mono-agents. In the Hu-PBL models, the bispecific antibody significantly suppressed tumor growth compared with mono-targeting agents, especially in a model with poor responsiveness to PD-1 blockade, where macrophage-mediated activity plays a larger role in tumor control. However, the bispecific may not be suitable for combination with PD-1 inhibitors. This is supported by our RNA sequencing data, which revealed reduced T-cell signatures following treatment, potentially limiting the benefit of PD-1 mediated responses. In immunocompetent mice, induction of T and NK cell responses was observed, similar to and likely via the Fc of the anti-CD24 mAb. Macrophage activation was also observed at the tumor site, akin to anti-CD47 effects.

Resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) is often associated with an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, characterized by abundant M2-like TAMs and scarce cytotoxic T lymphocytes—a phenotype commonly referred to as a “cold” tumor. Our study shows that the CD47/CD24 bispecific antibody potently enhances macrophage phagocytosis and promotes immune infiltration by simultaneously blocking two distinct “don’t-eat-me” signals, indicating its potential value in ICI-refractory cold tumors. Importantly, recent evidence has shown that hypoxia reinforces CD47–SIRPα signaling, driving TAMs toward an M2-polarized immunosuppressive state and facilitating immune escape. Collectively, these findings underscore the unique therapeutic potential of our bispecific antibody in hypoxic, immune-cold tumors.

Currently, numerous projects are focused on leveraging the immune functions of macrophages to eliminate tumors, with CD47, a key antiphagocytic target, undergoing preclinical and clinical development. The primary challenges include toxic side effects and limited efficacy in solid tumors. Due to the ubiquitous expression of CD47, the use of anti-CD47 therapies in isolation raises considerable safety concerns. In our strategy, the vHHs were combined with anti-CD24 as a binding partner, thereby preventing binding to healthy RBCs expressing CD47 and facilitating the use of a wild-type Fc to preserve effector function towards CD24. Additionally, anti-CD24 enhances tumor specificity and synergizes with CD47 as an additional antagonist to block “don’t eat me” signals, promoting maximal macrophage phagocytosis. Our bispecific design overcomes key challenges in modulating the CD47-SIRPα pathway, while harnessing the synergistic potential of blocking the CD24-Siglec-10 antiphagocytic axis. This approach provides a clinically viable strategy for macrophage-based immunotherapy.

Conclusion

Our research presents the first fully human CD24 mAb and novel anti-CD47 vHHs for constructing a bispecific therapeutic agent. The final lead candidate demonstrates better activity towards CD47/CD24 double-positive tumor cells and an improved safety profile towards CD47 single-positive “healthy” cells. Therefore, our work provides a promising therapeutic strategy to address the challenges related to both the efficacy and safety associated with anti-CD47 therapies for cancer, while also providing new insights into macrophage-based cancer immunotherapies.
