# TRPA1 activation prompts lysosome-mediated Nrf2 degradation enhancing the killing of colorectal cancer cells.

## Abstract

Redox homeostasis is crucial for cancer cell survival and resistance to therapy. The transcription factor NRF2, a master regulator of antioxidant and metabolic genes, is often upregulated in tumors to mitigate oxidative stress. Although NRF2 stability is canonically governed by KEAP1-CUL3-proteasome degradation, emerging evidence implicates lysosomal and autophagic pathways in non-canonical NRF2 turnover. The mechanisms by which these alternative pathways are engaged during chronic oxidative signaling remain unclear. We investigated whether sustained activation of the redox-sensitive ion channel TRPA1 by cannabidiol (CBD) disrupts redox homeostasis and promotes NRF2 degradation in colorectal cancer models. Using five independent CRC cell lines (RKO, HCT116, HT29, SW480, and MC38), we assessed reactive oxygen species (ROS), mitochondrial function, autophagy, and NRF2 protein dynamics through biochemical assays, lysosomal fractionation, and imaging. Xenograft models were used for in vivo validation. Chronic TRPA1 activation induced a biphasic ROS response, characterized by an early increase linked to mitochondrial Ca2+ influx and a delayed ROS surge associated with mitochondrial dysfunction. This oxidative trajectory initially stabilized but subsequently led to its degradation after 24 h via a KEAP1-independent, autophagy-lysosome pathway. Proteasome inhibition failed to rescue NRF2, whereas bafilomycin A1 restored its levels and blocked co-localization with lysosomal markers (e.g., LAMP2A). Importantly, CBD-induced TRPA1 activation sensitized CRC cells to oxaliplatin, triggering apoptotic-not senescent-cell death. These effects were dose-dependent and consistent across all tested cell lines. Our findings reveal a non-canonical bioelectric-lysosomal axis that links TRPA1 activity to NRF2 destabilization in colorectal cancer. This work expands the understanding of NRF2 proteostasis under sustained oxidative stress and highlights TRPA1 as a tractable redox-modulating target for overcoming chemoresistance.

Background

Redox homeostasis is tightly regulated in both normal and malignant cells, and its dysregulation contributes to cancer progression and resistance to therapy. Central to this regulation is nuclear factor erythroid-related factor 2 (NRF2), a transcription factor that orchestrates the expression of genes involved in antioxidant defense, xenobiotic detoxification, and metabolic rewiring. Under homeostatic conditions, NRF2 is continuously ubiquitinated and targeted for proteasomal degradation by Kelch-like ECH-associated protein 1 (KEAP1), a redox-sensitive adaptor protein that serves as a molecular sensor of oxidative stress. Upon oxidative challenge, cysteine modifications on KEAP1 disrupt its ability to bind and degrade NRF2, allowing NRF2 to accumulate and translocate to the nucleus, where it activates a broad transcriptional program for antioxidant defenses.

The KEAP1–NRF2 axis has been extensively studied in cancer, where constitutive activation of NRF2 supports tumor cell survival under conditions of oxidative, metabolic, and therapeutic stress. Persistent NRF2 signaling promotes chemoresistance, immune evasion, and tumor progression across various malignancies. Notably, NRF2 is frequently hyperactivated in cancers with KEAP1 loss or mutation. However, emerging evidence suggests that NRF2 turnover is not solely governed by the KEAP1–proteasome system. Alternative, non-canonical degradation pathways, including autophagy and lysosomal proteolysis, have recently been implicated in NRF2 regulation. In parallel, the PDK1–AKT–GSK3β axis represents an alternative regulatory pathway for NRF2. Under oxidative stress, the activation of AKT leads to the phosphorylation of GSK3β at Ser9, thereby inhibiting its activity and stabilizing NRF2. Conversely, inhibition of this axis—either through upstream kinases or sustained redox pressure—reactivates GSK3β and promotes NRF2 degradation via β-TrCP–mediated ubiquitination revealing additional complexity in NRF2 proteostasis and offering new points of therapeutic intervention.

Ion channels are increasingly recognized as key regulators of tumor cell behavior, impacting membrane potential, calcium signaling, metabolism, and apoptosis. Aberrant expression or function of ion channels has been linked to cancer progression, treatment resistance, and immune modulation, positioning them as novel prognostic markers and therapeutic targets. One such ion channel is the transient receptor potential ankyrin 1 (TRPA1), a calcium-permeable, non-selective cation channel identified initially in sensory neurons. TRPA1 functions as a polymodal sensor of environmental irritants, electrophilic stress, cold, and mechanical stimuli. It is activated by a wide range of exogenous compounds—including cannabinoids (e.g., cannabidiol, CBD), allyl isothiocyanate (wasabi), and menthol—through covalent modification of cysteine residues in its intracellular N-terminus.

Beyond its canonical role in nociception, TRPA1 is ectopically expressed in several human malignancies, including colon, breast, skin, and prostate cancers. Previous studies have shown that short-term CBD exposure activates the NRF2 pathway and induces antioxidant and cytoprotective responses in keratinocytes, glioblastoma cells, and in vivo models of tissue injury, largely through modulation of Keap1/BACH1 and increased nuclear NRF2. However, its functional role in tumor biology remains ambiguous and context-dependent, with evidence supporting both pro-tumorigenic and anti-tumorigenic functions depending on the activating ligand, tumor type, and cellular context. The impact of sustained TRPA1 activation on redox homeostasis and the fate of cancer cells is largely unexplored.

In this study, we investigate whether prolonged activation of TRPA1 acts as a redox-disrupting trigger that initiates lysosome-mediated NRF2 degradation in colon cancer cells. We hypothesize that sustained TRPA1 activity, via mitochondrial Ca2+ influx and ROS generation, promotes NRF2 turnover through a KEAP1- and proteasome-independent route involving autophagy and lysosomal trafficking. Given NRF2's established role in chemoresistance, uncovering mechanisms to destabilize NRF2 offers a novel therapeutic strategy to overcome redox adaptation and sensitize cancer cells to oxidative damage. Our findings reveal a previously unrecognized bioelectric-lysosomal axis linking TRPA1 activation to NRF2 proteostasis, with direct implications for redox-targeted therapy in colorectal cancer.

Results

Sustained activation of TRPA1 by CBD induces apoptosis in colon cancer cells

Whether sustained TRPA1 activation can sizably affect colon cancer cell biology/survival is currently unclear. To fill this gap in our knowledge, we evaluated the long-term impact of cannabidiol (CBD), a selective TRPA1 agonist, on colon cancer survival, indexing apoptosis as a modality of cell death. Extended exposure to CBD (48 h) significantly reduced cell viability (Fig. 1A) in a dose-dependent manner and induced hallmark apoptotic features, including increased Annexin V-positive cells (Fig. 1B; Supplementary Fig. 1A and 1B), elevated cleaved caspase-3 levels, and accumulation of γ-H2AX, a marker of DNA double-strand breaks (Fig. 1C). Crucially, genetic knockdown of TRPA1 via siRNA abolished these cytotoxic effects (Fig. 1B and C; Supplementary Figure 1C), confirming that CBD-induced apoptosis specifically is mediated through TRPA1 activation. These results highlight a pro-apoptotic function of TRPA1 signaling in colon cancer cells.

TRPA1 activation by cannabidiol induces biphasic mitochondrial ROS generation and dysfunction in colon cancer cells

The activation of TRPA1 has been associated with increased ROS emission in colorectal carcinoma cells. However, the impact of this surge on cancer biology is not well-understood. Still, it is relevant to determine because reverting the highly reductive intracellular cancer milieu towards oxidative stress can enhance cancer cell killing. To this end, we first evaluate whether – and to what extent - TRPA1 activation via CBD increases ROS emission from colon cancer cells, using a state-of-the-art, quantitative, and specific approach, such as electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy, as previously reported by us. As shown in Fig. 2A, colon cancer cells were treated with CBD at different time points, ranging from 2 h to 24 h. Remarkably, our findings indicate that the application of CBD rapidly stimulates ROS production, causing a sharp increase that reaches a plateau within the first 2 h (Fig. 2A; Supplementary Figure 1D). However, despite this initial stabilization, prolonged exposure to CBD for 24 h leads to a further significant surge in ROS levels, suggesting a sustained or secondary phase of ROS generation.

Since mitochondria primarily regulate cellular redox, we monitored the effect of TRPA1 activation by CBD on mitochondrial function. We found that CBD produced a rapid mitochondrial Ca2+ entry (Fig. 2B) with consequent mitochondrial depolarization (Fig. 2C). Thus, TRPA1 activation by CBD is associated with a biphasic increase in ROS, which may be driven by early mitochondrial Ca2+ influx followed by sustained mitochondrial dysfunction, suggesting a possible role for mitochondrial impairment in perpetuating oxidative stress.

TRPA1 activation induces a biphasic regulation of NRF2 via a non-canonical degradation pathway

To gain deeper insight into the oxidative stress dynamics induced by TRPA1 activation, we investigated the effect of prolonged cannabidiol (CBD) exposure on NRF2 protein stability. Consistent with the biphasic ROS emission pattern, CBD elicited a biphasic response in terms of overall Nrf2 protein abundance; an initial robust and sustained accumulation of NRF2 protein lasting up to 16 h, followed by a pronounced reduction at later time points (Fig. 2D) without significant changes of TRPA1 protein density (Supplementary Fig. 1E). Consistent with this finding, analysis of mRNA levels for the NRF2 downstream effector HMOX1 (Fig. 2E) mirrored the biphasic trend, further confirming the temporal dynamics of NRF2 activation. To explore the mechanism underlying this shift, we investigated the PDK1/AKT/GSK3β/NRF2 axis, a known pathway by which sustained oxidative stress can trigger NRF2 degradation. After 24 h of CBD treatment, we detected a significant reduction in AKT phosphorylation at Ser308 (Fig. 2F; Supplementary Fig. 1D), accompanied by dephosphorylation of GSK3β at Ser9, consistent with its activation, while the total levels of PDK1, AKT, and GSK3β remain essentially unchanged, suggesting the effect is on signaling rather than expression. Importantly, NRF2 protein levels are reduced following CBD treatment at both time points (Fig. 2F; Supplementary Fig. 1D). Given that AKT normally inhibits GSK3β, and GSK3β promotes NRF2 degradation, the observed data are consistent with a model where CBD inhibits AKT, releases GSK3β activity, and thereby accelerates NRF2 degradation. However, inhibition of GSK3β using SB216763 did not prevent NRF2 degradation or the associated DNA damage (Supplementary Fig. 2A). Additionally, blockade of proteasomal degradation with MG132 failed to restore NRF2 levels (Fig. 2G), ruling out classical proteasomal clearance mechanisms. Intriguingly, we also observed a reduction in KEAP1 protein levels following CBD treatment (Supplementary Fig. 2B and 2C), suggesting an uncoupling of NRF2 turnover from KEAP1-mediated regulation. Collectively, these results indicate that TRPA1 activation by CBD induces a time-dependent regulatory shift in NRF2 stability via a non-canonical degradation mechanism, independent of both GSK3β and the proteasome, and possibly involving lysosomal pathways or other alternative proteolytic systems.

TRPA1 activation promotes lysosome-dependent degradation of NRF2

To elucidate the mechanism underlying the non-canonical degradation of NRF2 following TRPA1 activation, we investigated the role of lysosomal pathways in cannabidiol (CBD)-treated colon cancer cells. Prolonged CBD exposure led to a marked activation of the autophagy–lysosome axis (Fig. 3A), as evidenced by increased phosphorylation of AMPK along with elevated LC3-II levels, indicating autophagy induction. In parallel, lysosomal activity was significantly enhanced, as shown by increased fluorescence from Lysosensor probes (Fig. 3B). Notably, pharmacological inhibition of lysosomal acidification using bafilomycin A1 effectively rescued NRF2 protein levels, indicating that CBD promotes NRF2 degradation via a lysosome-dependent, proteasome-independent mechanism (Fig. 3C).

NRF2 is targeted to lysosomes following TRPA1 activation

To confirm NRF2 trafficking to lysosomal fractions extracted from naïve cells and CBD-treated cells, they were immunoblotted for NRF2 and LAMP2 proteins; lysosomal fractions from control and CBD-treated cells were enriched in the lysosomal marker LAMP2, confirming successful isolation (Fig. 3D). Notably, NRF2 protein increased in lysosomes from CBD-treated cells, indicating that TRPA1 activation promotes NRF2 trafficking to lysosomes for degradation (Fig. 3D). Furthermore, confocal imaging further revealed strong co-localization between NRF2 and the lysosomal marker LAMP2 (Fig. 3E), suggesting lysosome-mediated sequestration of NRF2.

Cannabidiol treatment suppresses NRF2 and elevates ROS in a colorectal cancer xenograft model

To validate our in vitro observations in a translationally relevant pathological context, we assessed the impact of acute cannabidiol (CBD) treatment on NRF2 expression and redox homeostasis in vivo using a human colon cancer xenograft model. We selected HCT116 cells based on several key advantages: they are a well-characterized model of colorectal cancer with wild-type KEAP1 and NRF2, enabling the accurate interrogation of NRF2 regulatory dynamics.They exhibit moderate basal NRF2 activity and well-defined redox responses, making them responsive to both activation and destabilization; and they form robust, reproducible tumors in immunodeficient mice, which facilitates in vivo therapeutic studies. In addition, HCT116 cells are moderately sensitive to oxaliplatin and are commonly used to evaluate redox-based combination therapies, providing a clinically relevant background for testing CBD as a redox-modulating agent.

To this end, HCT116 cells were implanted subcutaneously into NOD-SCID IL2Rγ^null (NSG) mice. CBD-treated mice (20 mg/kg, intraperitoneally, every 2 days) exhibited a marked reduction in tumor volume and weight compared to vehicle controls (Fig. 4A–C). Tumors were harvested and analyzed for NRF2 protein levels and ROS content using EPR spectroscopy. CBD administration led to a significant decrease in NRF2 protein expression (Fig. 4D) and a concurrent increase in ROS accumulation within tumor tissues (Fig. 4E). These findings support a model in which TRPA1 activation by CBD destabilizes NRF2 and disrupts redox balance in vivo, reinforcing its therapeutic potential as a redox-modulating strategy in colon cancer.

TRPA1 activation potentiates oxaliplatin efficacy via redox imbalance and DNA damage in colon cancer

Oxaliplatin, a platinum-based chemotherapeutic agent, exerts its antitumor activity primarily through DNA crosslinking and induction of DNA damage. However, its clinical efficacy is often limited by acquired resistance mechanisms, including enhanced DNA repair capacity and evasion of apoptosis. Building on our findings that the activation of TRPA1 by CBD promotes oxidative stress and DNA damage, we hypothesized that CBD could sensitize colon cancer cells to oxaliplatin. Using the moderately sensitive to oxaliplatin HCT116 colon cancer model, we determined the half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of oxaliplatin alone and in combination with a fixed dose of CBD (43 μM). Co-treatment with CBD significantly lowered the IC50 of oxaliplatin (Fig. 5A and B). Isobologram analysis revealed additive effects, with enhanced cytotoxicity evidenced by increased cleaved caspase-3 levels and elevated γH2AX, indicating amplified apoptosis and DNA damage (Fig. 5C). To validate these results in vivo, we treated tumor-bearing mice with oxaliplatin alone or in combination with CBD (20 mg/kg every two days). Consistent with in vitro findings, the combination treatment resulted in significantly reduced tumor growth compared to oxaliplatin monotherapy (Fig. 5F) and increased ROS levels (Fig. 5E). These data suggest that TRPA1 activation by CBD amplifies oxaliplatin efficacy, potentially by disrupting redox homeostasis and exacerbating DNA damage in resistant colon cancer cells (Fig. 5F).

Discussion

Redox homeostasis is a finely tuned cellular process that governs cell fate decisions and plays a critical role in cancer biology. In the present study, we uncovered a previously unrecognized mechanism whereby sustained activation of the redox-sensitive ion channel TRPA1 by cannabidiol (CBD) disrupts redox balance in colon cancer cells through lysosome-dependent degradation of the antioxidant transcription factor NRF2. Our findings reveal a mechanistic link between ion channel signaling, mitochondrial dysfunction, and non-canonical regulation of the NRF2 axis, establishing TRPA1 as a novel modulator of redox vulnerability in cancer.

We first demonstrate that prolonged TRPA1 activation by CBD initiates apoptosis in colon cancer cells through a dose-dependent induction of mitochondrial dysfunction and DNA damage. A key event in this process is the generation of a biphasic ROS response: an early oxidative burst followed by a delayed, sustained increase in mitochondrial ROS. This pattern mirrors the biphasic regulation of NRF2; indeed, NRF2 is initially stabilized as a protective response but subsequently degraded at later time points. This temporal uncoupling between oxidative stress and NRF2 persistence highlights a critical vulnerability in the cancer cell's redox buffering capacity.

Importantly, our data show that this loss of NRF2 does not follow the classical KEAP1–CUL3–proteasome axis. Instead, we provide multiple lines of evidence supporting lysosomal degradation as the dominant mode of NRF2 clearance following TRPA1 activation. Lysosomal inhibition by bafilomycin A1 rescued NRF2 levels, while proteasome blockade with MG132 did not. Furthermore, we observed co-localization of NRF2 with the lysosomal marker LAMP2A, and immunoblotting confirmed the selective enrichment of NRF2 within lysosomal fractions of CBD-treated cells. This identifies a non-canonical degradation route whereby TRPA1-induced oxidative stress activates the autophagy–lysosome pathway to degrade NRF2, a mechanism not previously described in colon cancer.

Although KEAP1 is a well-established negative regulator of NRF2 stability, our data suggest that canonical KEAP1-mediated proteasomal degradation is not the primary mechanism of NRF2 turnover under conditions of sustained TRPA1 activation by CBD. In our experiments, KEAP1 levels were paradoxically reduced following CBD treatment, yet NRF2 protein abundance also declined, indicating a KEAP1-independent degradation mechanism. Importantly, pharmacologic inhibition of GSK3β—known to stabilize NRF2 via a β-TrCP–mediated, KEAP1-independent route—partially rescued NRF2 levels but did not restore KEAP1, nor did it fully reverse CBD-induced cytotoxicity. These findings suggest that even if KEAP1 is inhibited or lost, the dominant degradative pressure on NRF2 in this context derives from the autophagy–lysosome pathway. As such, KEAP1 inhibition alone may be insufficient to restore NRF2 function or counteract CBD's anti-tumor effects. Future studies involving direct NRF2 stabilization (e.g., via overexpression or pharmacological NRF2 activators) will be necessary to determine whether NRF2 loss is causally required for the cytotoxic effects of TRPA1 activation.

The functional consequences of NRF2 degradation are profound. While transient NRF2 activation is cytoprotective, its sustained loss leads to the accumulation of uncontrolled ROS, impaired redox defenses, and apoptotic cell death. Notably, the systemic, or whole-body, inhibition of NRF2 is known to be toxic, as NRF2 plays essential roles in protecting normal tissues from oxidative damage and inflammation. However, our strategy circumvents these toxicities by inducing NRF2 degradation selectively in tumor cells via a stress-dependent, lysosome-mediated mechanism. In vivo, acute CBD treatment in a colon cancer xenograft model recapitulated this pattern, showing reduced NRF2 expression and elevated ROS in tumor tissues, ultimately suppressing tumor growth. These findings underscore the translational relevance of this redox imbalance and support TRPA1 as a therapeutic target for overcoming antioxidant-based resistance mechanisms in vivo.

While prior studies have reported that NRF2 may transcriptionally upregulate TRPA1, our study investigates explicitly the downstream consequences of sustained TRPA1 activation, rather than its regulation by NRF2. Notably, functional channel activity does not necessarily correlate with its expression levels, as TRPA1 remains inactive unless triggered by specific electrophilic or oxidative stimuli. In our system, chronic pharmacological activation of TRPA1 by cannabidiol (CBD) ensures persistent functional engagement of the channel, independent of endogenous stimuli or transcriptional upregulation. It underscores the relevance of ligand-specific, sustained activation in modulating redox homeostasis and NRF2 turnover.

Although our data suggest that TRPA1 activation by CBD initiates a biphasic increase in ROS, the causal relationship between early mitochondrial Ca2+ influx and subsequent oxidative stress remains to be definitively established. While Rhod-2 imaging indicated transient mitochondrial Ca2+ accumulation, we did not directly assess the reversibility of these changes or whether Ca2+ influx is solely responsible for triggering mitochondrial dysfunction and ROS production. It is also important to note that channel expression does not equate to activity, as TRPA1 is known to respond to transient endogenous electrophilic stimuli. Our experimental design relies on pharmacological activation to ensure chronic TRPA1 engagement, which may bypass or override endogenous regulatory mechanisms. Future studies using targeted inhibition of mitochondrial calcium uptake will be needed to clarify the sequence and contribution of calcium signaling in this redox cascade.

Our study also demonstrates that TRPA1 activation synergizes with oxaliplatin, a DNA-damaging chemotherapeutic whose efficacy is often limited by redox adaptation and enhanced DNA repair. CBD-mediated TRPA1 activation lowered the IC50 of oxaliplatin and increased apoptotic markers in platinum-resistant cells. In vivo co-treatment further reduced tumor burden and increased oxidative stress, indicating that destabilization of NRF2 can resensitize tumors to oxidative damage-based therapies.

At first glance, our results may appear contradictory to prior studies in breast and lung cancer spheroids, where TRPA1 was reported to promote cancer cell survival under oxidative pressure by activating Ca2-dependent anti-apoptotic pathways downstream of NRF2. However, these findings can be reconciled by considering the temporal and context-dependent roles of TRPA1. In the spheroid setting, TRPA1 confers a short-term survival advantage in ROS-rich inner tumor regions, acting as a non-canonical defense against oxidative stress. By contrast, our data reveal that sustained TRPA1 activation induces mitochondrial dysfunction, autophagy, and lysosomal degradation of NRF2, ultimately collapsing the antioxidant defense system. Thus, TRPA1 may function as a dual regulator—protective under acute stress but detrimental under persistent activation, thereby exposing a therapeutic vulnerability that can be exploited in cancer treatment.

Together, our data position TRPA1 as a central node in redox regulation and identify a lysosome-dependent mechanism of NRF2 degradation as a critical effector of its pro-apoptotic function. This work expands the landscape of redox biology by integrating ion channel signaling and autophagy with the regulation of antioxidant defenses. Future studies will be needed to determine the broader applicability of this mechanism in other NRF2-driven malignancies and to assess whether additional lysosome-targeting strategies can further enhance redox-based therapies.

Material and methods

Cell lines and culture

RKO, HCT116, HT29 SW480 and MC38 cell lines obtained from ATCC © were cultured in Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM) (4.5 g/L glucose) (Corning, Manassas, VA, USA), supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Gemini Bio, West Sacramento, CA, USA) and 1 % penicillin (10,000 units/mL)/streptomycin (10,000 μg/mL) (Gibco, Waltham, MA, USA) at 37 °C and 5 % CO2.

Reagents, antibodies, and siRNA

All antibodies were purchased from Cell Signaling Technologies, Inc. (Boston, MA, USA). NRF2 Antibody for Immunohistochemistry (IHC) was purchased from Abcam (Waltham, MA, USA). The anti-TRPA1 antibody was purchased from Alomone Labs. Cannabidiol (CBD) was purchased from Cayman Chemical (Michigan, USA). Oxaliplatin and Bafilomycin A1 were purchased from Thermo Scientific (MA, USA). MG132 was purchased from Enzo Life Sciences (Farmingdale, NY, USA). si-RNA targeting TRPA1 was purchased from Invitrogen (TRPAHSS113276-CA, USA). The transfection reagent was purchased from Origene (Cat. TT320001).

RT-PCR

Total RNA extraction was done with TRIzol reagent (Life Technologies Corporation, Grand Island, NY). RNA was transcribed into cDNA with SuperScript® III First-Strand Synthesis SuperMix (Life Technologies Corporation, Grand Island, NY) and analyzed with SYBR green (Applied Biosystems, Inc., Grand Island, NY) on the 7500 FAST Real Time PCR detection system (Applied Biosystems, Inc., Grand Island, NY). The human primers used are:

HMOX1: forw: 5′- ccaggcagagaatgctgagttc -3′, rev. 5′- aagactgggctctccttgttgc -3′

NQO1: forw.: 5′- cctgccattctgaaaggctggt -3′, rev.: 5′- gtggtgatggaaagcactgcct -3′.

Protein extraction and Western blotting

After RNA extraction from Trizol, 300 μL of 95 % ethanol was added to the phenol-ethanol phase, mixed, and let the mixture stand at RT for 2–3 min. Samples were then centrifuged at 2000×g for 5 min at 4 °C. The upper phase was removed, 750 μL of isopropanol was added to 400 μL of the lower phase, and the samples were incubated for 10 min at RT. After incubation, samples were centrifuged at 12,000×g for 10 min at 4 °C. The supernatant was removed, and the pellets were washed 3 times with cold acetone. The protein pellets were dissolved in 75 μL of water and mixed with 25 μL Laemmli sample buffer containing β-mercaptoethanol. Finally, samples were heated for 10 min at 95 °C and centrifuged at 13,000×g for 5 min. The same amount of protein samples was loaded onto an SDS-polyacrylamide gel and transferred onto a PVDF membrane. Membranes were incubated for 1 h in 5 % nonfat milk in Tris-Buffered Saline (TBS) containing 0.1 % Tween 20 (TBST) and then overnight with primary antibodies at 4 °C. Membranes were washed with TBST three times and then incubated with secondary antibody for 1h at RT, followed by washing with TBST. Proteins were visualized by the Imager (ChemiDoc MP Imaging System, Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). Histone H3 was evaluated for normalization of protein expression.

Measurement of reactive oxygen species (ROS) by electronic paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectroscopy. Following CBD treatment (2–24 h), RKO cells were washed, collected by centrifugation, and stored at −80 °C. Frozen cells were resuspended in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche Applied Science, Indianapolis, IN) and 0.1 mM of the metal chelator, diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid (DTPA), at pH 7.4. ROS measurements on intact cells were conducted immediately using EPR spectroscopy as previously described. Briefly, stock solutions of the spin probe 1-hydroxy-3-methoxycarbonyl-2,2,5,5-tetramethyl-pyrrolidine hydrochloride (CMH; Enzo Life Sciences, Farmingdale, NY) were prepared daily in nitrogen-purged 0.9 % (w/v) NaCl, 25 g/L Chelex 100 (Bio-Rad), and 0.1 mM DTPA, and kept on ice. The samples were treated with 1 mM CMH at room temperature for 10 min, transferred to 50-μl glass capillary tubes, and analyzed immediately on a Bruker E-Scan (Billerica, MA) EPR spectrometer at room temperature. Spectrometer settings were as follows: sweep width, 100 G; microwave frequency, 9.75 GHz; modulation amplitude, 1 G; conversion time, 5.12 ms; receiver gain, 2 x 103; number of scans, 16. EPR signal intensities were normalized to the protein concentrations of the cell lysates as determined by Pierce BCA protein assay kit (Life Technologies).

ROS measurements on tumor tissue were carried out as previously described. Briefly, flash-frozen tissue (∼20 mg) was homogenized using a hand-held homogenizer in PBS containing protease inhibitor cocktail and 0.1 mM DTPA at pH 7.4. The samples were treated with 1 mM CMH at 37 °C for 2 min, transferred to 50-μl glass capillary tubes, and analyzed as described above. EPR signal intensities were normalized to the protein concentrations of the tissue lysates as described above.

Cell apoptosis assay

Cell apoptosis was determined using a BD Pharmingen FITC Annexin V Apoptosis Detection Kit I. Briefly, cells were harvested and washed twice with PBS. The pellets were resuspended in 1 × Binding Buffer and incubated for 15 min at room temperature with 3 μL of FITC-Annexin V and 3 μL of Propidium Iodide in the dark. Then, 400 μL of 1 × Binding Buffer was added to each tube before analysis. Samples were analyzed with a flow cytometer (LSR Fortessa with HTS, BD Biosciences, San Jose, CA, USA) and FlowJo (BD Biosciences, San Jose, California, USA).

Lysosomal pH assessment

RKO colorectal carcinoma cells were seeded in 6-well plates at a density of 3 × 105 cells/well and allowed to adhere overnight in complete DMEM supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum and 1 % penicillin-streptomycin. The following day, cells were treated with CBD or vehicle control (0.1 % DMSO) for 24 h under standard culture conditions (37 °C, 5 % CO2). After treatment, cells were washed once with pre-warmed PBS and incubated with 1 μM LysoSensor™ Yellow/Blue dextran, 10,000 MW, Anionic, Fixable (Invitrogen, Thermo Fisher Scientific, cat# L22460) diluted in DMEM for 3 h at 37 °C. Cells were then washed twice with PBS to remove excess dye and fixed with 4 % paraformaldehyde. Fluorescence imaging was performed using an inverted fluorescence microscope equipped with appropriate filter sets (excitation at 329 nm and 384 nm; emission collected at 440 nm and 540 nm, respectively). The ratio of blue (440 nm) to yellow (540 nm) emission was quantified using ImageJ (NIH) and used as a proxy for lysosomal pH. A decrease in the 440/540 ratio indicates increased acidification. All image acquisition settings were maintained constant between samples.

Lysosomal isolation

RKO cells were plated in 10 cm dishes and grown to ∼80 % confluence in DMEM supplemented with 10 % FBS and 1 % penicillin-streptomycin. Cells were treated with 42 μM cannabidiol (CBD; Sigma-Aldrich) or vehicle control (0.1 % DMSO) for 24 h at 37 °C, 5 % CO2. Post-treatment, cells from two 10 cm dishes (50–100 mg wet cell pellet) were washed twice with PBS and collected by scraping. Cell pellets were centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 2 min at 4 °C. Supernatant was discarded, and pellets were resuspended in 800 μL ice-cold Lysosome Enrichment Reagent A (supplemented with EDTA-free protease inhibitors) and incubated on ice for 2 min. Cells were then lysed via 10–15 pulses of sonication at 6–9 W on ice. Lysosome Enrichment Reagent B (800 μL, with protease inhibitors) was added, gently mixed, and the lysate centrifuged at 500×g for 10 min at 4 °C to remove debris. The post-nuclear supernatant (∼1.6 mL) was transferred to a fresh tube kept on ice. Preparation of OptiPrep Gradient: Equal volumes of Reagents A and B were mixed to produce the Gradient Dilution Buffer. Discontinuous gradients (17 %, 20 %, 23 %, 27 %, 30 % iodixanol) were prepared in 8 mL ultracentrifuge tubes following the manufacturer's instructions (Thermofisher cat#89839). Supernatant (∼1.6 mL) was carefully layered atop the gradient. Samples were centrifuged in a tabletop ultracentrifuge at ∼150,000×g for 2 h at 4 °C. Post-centrifugation, the lysosome-enriched band (typically between the 20–27 % interfaces) was carefully aspirated using a Pasteur pipette, diluted with PBS, and pelleted at 20,000×g for 20 min at 4 °C. The lysosomal pellet was resuspended in 100 μL 1x laemmlli buffer. Aliquots were stored at −20 °C for Western blot analysis.

Xenografts

Four-week-old NOD Scid gamma (NSG) mice were obtained from The Jackson Laboratory. RKO (2.5 × 106 cells in 100 ml PBS/mouse) were injected subcutaneously (S.C.) into the rear area of the right flank. The mice were bred and kept under specific pathogen-free conditions in the animal facility of the Medical University of South Carolina (Charleston, USA). All experimental procedures were conducted in accordance with American Guidelines and were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) under the animal protocol ID IACUC-2022-01505. Fourteen days after the injections, when tumors were palpable in all mice, they were randomized to control or treatment groups (6 mice per group). There were no significant differences regarding age and weight among all four groups. CBD (20 mg/kg in DMSO every other day) and oxaliplatin (in PBS containing 0.5 % Tween 80, 10 mg/kg/twice a week). Control mice were injected with equal amounts of DMSO. Treatment-related toxicity was determined by monitoring mouse weight and appearance every day. The tumor size was measured with a caliper, and the tumor volume was calculated using the following equation: Volume = 0.5 × (length × width2). Mice were euthanized on day 22 after treatment initiation, and tumor samples were collected for further analysis.

Immunohistochemistry

Immunohistochemistry was performed on paraffin-embedded tissue sections. The sections were run on the Leica Bond III using modified Protocol F, which included extra washes between steps. Primary incubation with NRF2 was 10 min. After washing, slides were incubated with the secondary antibody. Microscopy was performed using an Olympus (Tokyo, Japan). For the evaluation of immunohistochemistry slides using ImageJ, the number of positive cells was counted in each image per slide, with two different slides per mouse.

Statistical analysis
