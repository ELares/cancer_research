# Blockade of neutrophil recruitment to tumor sites based on sialic acid-modified nanoplatforms enhances the efficacy of checkpoint blockade immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Checkpoint inhibitors are designed to rejuvenate depleted or suppressed T cells in the tumor microenvironment, relying on the immune system to control and kill tumors. However, accumulating evidence indicates that tumor-infiltrating neutrophils impede the proliferation and activation of T cells and determine the resistance to checkpoint blockade and chemotherapy. In this study, sialic acid ligand-modified colchicine derivative phospholipid complexes specifically targeted tumor-associated neutrophils in the peripheral blood, blocked neutrophil accumulation in tumors, and attenuated the inhibitory effect of infiltrating neutrophils on T cells. Neutrophil blocking therapy enhanced the immunotherapy effect of the PD-L1 antibody in S180 advanced tumors and 4T1 breast cancer. Our study found that PD-L1 antibody monotherapy increased the tumor infiltration of immunosuppressive neutrophils. Combination therapy with neutrophil blocking can greatly reduce tumor-infiltrating neutrophils and increase the proliferation of cytotoxic CD8+ T lymphocytes in the tumor. The combination therapy significantly improved the survival rate of mice with advanced S180 tumors and increased the sensitivity of immune checkpoint inhibitors to 4T1 cold tumors.

Introduction

Immunotherapy is a valuable research area in the field of tumor treatment. Immunotherapy mainly involves the inhibition and killing of tumor cells by altering the body's immune system and enhancing anti-tumor immunity. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) are the most commonly used treatment in tumor immunotherapy. In recent years, ICIs have made revolutionary breakthroughs in the treatment of a variety of advanced solid tumors. However, studies have found that the inhibition rate of ICIs in solid tumors is only 10%–40%, which indicates that a large number of patients cannot benefit from immunotherapy. In addition, ICIs technology has been stagnant in the area breast cancer treatment, because breast cancer was previously considered to be a "cold tumor" with low immunogenicity. With the development of molecular biology techniques and further understanding of the molecular mechanism of breast cancer, it has been found that, compared with other subtypes of breast cancer, triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) has a higher expression level of PD-L1, which theoretically has a molecular basis for immunotherapy. However, in clinical trials, the objective response rate (ORR) of monoclonal antibody therapy is extremely low. ICIs aim to reinvigorate exhausted or suppressed cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) in the tumor microenvironment (TME), but because of the complexity of the immune system, it is difficult to achieve the desired effect with monotherapy.

Among the resistance mechanisms, the influence of tumor-infiltrating neutrophils is one of the central barriers impairing CTLs activity within the tumor bed. Neutrophils, which account for a considerable proportion of tumor-infiltrating immune cells, can express the ligand PD-L1, which binds to PD-1 on CTLs, thereby inhibiting the proliferation of CTLs and the production of cytokines such as IFN-γ and IL-2, resulting in the loss of the anti-tumor function of CTLs. The ability of neutrophils to inhibit CTLs proliferation in vitro has also been well documented in humans and experimental mouse models. Therefore, we reasoned that the combination of neutrophil-blocking therapy with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) may increase the immunotherapy effect.

Neutrophils are extensively recruited under the action of cytokines and chemokines released by the TME, and the expression of L-selectin on their surface is significantly upregulated in this activated state. L-selectin is a type I transmembrane cell adhesion molecule that is expressed on neutrophils. Engagement of L-selectin with endothelial-derived ligands initiates neutrophil tethering and rolling along the luminal walls of post-capillary venules, constituting the first step of the multi-step adhesion cascade. As the ligand of L-selectin, sialic acid (SA) can be modified on the surface of the preparation to specifically target neutrophils that migrate to the tumor in the peripheral blood, greatly improving the uptake ability of the preparation. Colchicine is a potent anti-inflammatory drug that inhibits neutrophil migration and chemotaxis. However, due to its high toxicity and narrow therapeutic window, this drug is rarely used clinically to treat tumors. Therefore, in our previous study, we adopted a newly synthesized colchicine derivative with lower toxicity than that of colchicine (identified as BCS, whose specific synthesis process is presented in the Supplementary Materials: Fig. S1) as our model drug. We prepared a sialic acid (SA) - modified BCS phospholipid complex (the phospholipid is egg phosphatidylglycerol, EPG), and confirmed that this preparation (SA-EPG-BCS) could efficiently target peripheral blood neutrophils, inhibit their migration ability, and block the infiltration of tumor-associated neutrophils.

In this study, we combined PD-L1 antibody (PD-L1 Ab), a representative of ICIs, with the neutrophil-blocking agent SA-EPG-BCS, to investigate whether the combination therapy can effectively enhance the anti-tumor effect of ICB. We established an advanced S180 tumor model and a 4T1 TNBC model to investigate whether neutrophil blockade therapy can improve the therapeutic effect of PD-L1 Ab in inhibiting tumor growth or metastasis. Additionally, we investigated the mechanism of the effect of neutrophil blockade on immunotherapy using flow cytometry, immunohistochemical analysis and immunofluorescence analysis. Owing to the complexity of immunomodulatory mechanisms and the heterogeneity of malignancies, we believe that combination therapy represents the next wave of clinical therapy that can overcome the limitations of monotherapy.

Cells and animals

The S180 murine sarcoma cell line and 4T-1 mouse breast cancer cells were purchased from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). Male Kunming mice (6–7 weeks old) weighing 18–22 g and BALB/c mice (6–7 weeks old) weighing 18–22 g were purchased from the Experimental Animal Center of Shenyang Pharmaceutical University (Shenyang, China). All animal testing and nursing procedures were conducted with the consent of the Shenyang University Animal Welfare Committee.

Preparation and characterization of SA-EPG-BCS

As described in previous work, SA-EPG-BCS is a phospholipid complex prepared from egg phosphatidylglycerol (EPG), a low-toxicity colchicine derivative BCS, and SA-CH, a modified ligand for the synthesis of sialic acid and cholesterol. BCS, EPG, and SA-CH were dissolved in absolute ethanol, refluxed at 40 °C for 0.5 h, and ethanol was evaporated. Isoosmotic glucose was heated to the same temperature and then added and stirred for 10 min. Since pure BCS is almost insoluble in water, the liquid was filtered through 0.80, 0.45 and 0.22 µm microporous membranes to remove the uncompounded BCS particles and sterilize them. Finally, the BCS phospholipid complex (SA-EPG-BCS) was obtained. The particle size, zeta potential, and polydispersity index of the SA-EPG-BCS nanocomplexes were measured using a NICOMPTM 380 submicron particle sizer (CA, USA). The composite efficiency (CE%) was determined by the ratio of the mass of composite BCS to the dosage of BCS. In short, since pure BCS is almost insoluble in water, after the nanocomposite solution was filtered to remove the unrecombined BCS precipitate, it was diluted with demulsifier ethanol in a volumetric flask to destroy its structure and dissolve the BCS. Finally, the absorbance of the samples at 254 nm was measured in triplicate using a UV-1801 UV/Vis spectrophotometer (Beijing Ruili Analytical Instrument Co., Ltd., Beijing, China). The formula for CE% is: CE (%)= (encapsulated BCS mass/added BCS mass) × 100%.

Neutrophil isolation from bone marrow, blood, and tumor tissue

Bone marrow neutrophils were isolated and purified as follows: two lower limbs of mice were removed, the lower limb muscles were carefully dissected, and the tibia and femur were removed and washed by immersion in PBS. PBS was collected with a 1 mL syringe, gently inserted into the bone marrow cavity, aligned with a 4 ml Eppendorf tube, and the cells were flushed out. After passing through the cell screen, the cells were centrifuged at 200 g for 3 min. The upper liquid was discarded, and the red precipitate was resuspended in 2 ml the red cell lysate. The upper red liquid was discarded, and the cells were resuspended in 0.85% NaCl and washed twice. The prepared cell suspension was added to the top layer (55%, 65% and 78%) of three different proportions of Percoll separation solution (1:1:1, v/v/v) and centrifuged horizontally at 500 g for 10 min. The purified cells were separated and washed twice with PBS to obtain bone marrow neutrophils.

Neutrophils from peripheral blood (PBNs) of mice were isolated using density gradient centrifugation. Briefly, blood samples were collected from S180 or 4T1 tumor-bearing mice using vacuum tubes, and an appropriate amount of sodium heparin was added to obtain a final concentration of 5 IU/ml. The red blood cell precipitate (1.5 ml) was added, mixed, and incubated for 1 h. After precipitation, a plasma pool containing 2–3 ml white blood cells was obtained. This pool was carefully added to the gradient interface of 80% and 100% (2/1, v/v) separation medium 1 of the pre-formed murine PBNs separation mixture. The samples were centrifuged at 800 g for 20 min in a horizontal centrifuge at 4 °C. The PBNs were separated and recovered between the 80% and 100% interfaces and washed three times with cold PBS to obtain purified PBNs.

The neutrophil labeling method for tumor tissue was as follows: tumor tissue was harvested, mechanically cut into small pieces, and digested with 1 mg/ml collagenase D (Sigma) and 1 mg/ml DNase I (Roche) in PBS at 37 °C for 30 min. The cells were filtered using a 70-µm cell strainer (Corning) to obtain single-cell suspensions. Neutrophils were positively selected from single-cell suspensions by staining with fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)-labeled Ly-6 G antibody (BioLegend).

Combination therapy against advanced S180 tumor and 4T1 tumor in vivo

The resuscitated S180 cell suspension was inoculated into the abdominal cavities of Kunming mice. After 6–7 d, milky-white viscous ascites were extracted under sterile conditions. The number of tumor cells was 2.0 × 107 cells/ml. Using 75% ethanol for disinfection, the S180 cell suspension was inoculated into the subcutaneous tissue of the right anterior armpit of mice, and each mouse was inoculated with 0.2 ml. It is well known that peripheral blood cells are closely related to tumors. Many studies have reported that neutrophil to lymphocyte ratio (NLR) is closely related to the occurrence and development of many solid tumors, and can be useful for predicting the tumor grade and the prognosis in many tumors. Based on our preliminary experiments (Table S1), we found that the NLR of peripheral blood of tumor-bearing mice at 10 d after inoculation produced a significant difference from that at the early stage (tumor bearing day 3) (P < 0.01), indicating that the immune environment with pro-tumorigenic properties of the organism developed strongly. And the tumor volume of each group was similar without significant difference. We defined the mice inoculated for 10 days as the advanced S180 tumor model.

The resuscitated 4T1 cell suspension was cultured in vitro until the cells covered the bottom of the culture flask, and cells were then counted under an inverted microscope. After the cells were digested with trypsin, they were diluted in cell suspension with PBS, and the dilution ratio was adjusted. After disinfection with 75% alcohol, the 4T1 cell suspension was inoculated on the third pair of breast pads of mice. Each mouse was inoculated with 0.1 ml, and the number of cells was approximately 5 × 106.

The two groups of tumor models were divided into 4 groups, which were divided into a control group, PD-L1 Ab alone group, SA-EPG-BCS alone group, and combination therapy group, with 6 mice in each group. In the advanced S180 tumor model, the dose of PD-L1 Ab was 2 mg/kg, and it was injected intratumorally on Day 11, 13, and 15 after inoculation. The BCS (0.5 mg/kg) was administered by tail vein injection on Day 12, 14, 16, 18 and 20 after inoculation. In the 4T1 breast cancer model, the dose of PD-L1 Ab was also 2 mg/kg, and it was injected intratumorally on Day 8, 10 and 12 after inoculation. The BCS (0.5 mg/kg) was administered by tail vein injection on Day 9, 11, 13, 15 and 17 after inoculation. During the entire efficacy period, the therapeutic effect and toxicity of each preparation were investigated using indicators, such as mouse body weight, tumor volume, and tumor inhibition (TI) index. TI index was calculated using the following formula: TI index (g/g) = body weight (g)/tumor weight (g).

In the 4T1 breast cancer model, mouse lung tissue metastases were stained with Bouin's fixative for better observation and counting of the tumor metastases. The lungs of mice in each group were collected and completely immersed in a centrifuge tube filled with Bouin's staining solution. The tissues were fixed for 1 h at room temperature. After fixation, the tissue was slowly rinsed with water and soaked in 70% ethanol for dehydration. At this time, the tissue was slightly yellow, and the metastases appeared as white spots. Excess dehydration solution was absorbed with filter paper, the number of lung metastases of each mouse in each group was counted, and the mean and SD values were calculated and analyzed. Numerous studies suggest the involvement of MMP-9 and VEGF in various steps of tumorigenesis and metastasis cascade. To further investigate the anti-metastasis ability of the combination therapy, we also measured the concentrations of MMP-9 and VEGF cytokines in tumor tissues and serum. In brife, after the treatment, 0.5 mL blood was collected from the mouse orbit, and the serum was collected by centrifugation after natural coagulation for 30 min. Tumor tissue was collected and homogenized using a tissue homogenizer. After homogenization, the homogenate was centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 10 min, and the supernatant was taken for later use. The cytokine concentrations of MMP-9 and VEGF in serum and tumor tissues were quantified by ELISA kit.

Safety evaluation of combination therapy

To evaluate the safety of the combined treatment to the major organs of mice, histopathological examination of each organ was performed. After the final administration of 5% glucose, PD-L1 Ab, SA-EPG-BCS or combination treatment, major organs from advanced S180 tumor mice or 4T1 breast cancer mice were harvested and sliced into sections. The sections were stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and examined under a digital microscope for histopathological analysis.

Cytokines are key regulators in TME that mediate the recruitment and polarization of immune cells, including Tregs, macrophages, and others, to create an immunosuppressive but tumor-supporting environment that interferes with immunotherapy. Therefore, to explore whether this combination therapy could avoid other immune-related side effects, we examined the concentration levels of inflammatory factors IFN-γ and IL-12, and tumor-promoting factors IL-10 and Leukotriene B4 (LTB4) in the TME after treatment. In brief, tumor tissues from each group were collected into eppendorf tubes, and then homogenized using a tissue homogenizer after adding deionized water. After homogenization, the homogenate was centrifuged at 10,000 rpm for 10 min, and the supernatant was taken for later use. Cytokine concentration levels in tumor tissues were further quantified using ELISA kits.

Flow cytometry analysis

The proportion of neutrophils with a tumor-promoting phenotype (Ly-6G+PD-L1+) from different tissues of S180 tumors and 4T1 TNBC was determined using flow cytometry (FCM) (Beckman Coulter, Fullerton, CA, USA). Neutrophils were first extracted following the extraction method described in Materials and Methods 2.4 and then incubated with anti-Ly-6G-FITC and anti-PD-L1-PE antibodies. To investigate the effect of combination therapy on the infiltration of toxic CD8+ T cells in tumor tissues, we excised tumor tissues from the control and treatment groups 3 d after the final treatment (refer to Section 2.5 for doses administered). The tumor tissues were cut into small pieces and treated with a digestion solution (1 mg/ml collagenase D and 1 mg/mL DNase I) at 37 °C for 30 min. The cells were filtered using a 70-µm cell strainer (Corning) to obtain single-cell suspensions. Finally, the cells were incubated with anti-CD3-FITC, anti-CD8-APC, and anti-PD-1-PE. FlowJo software was used to analyze the data.

Immunohistochemistry

As a specific marker of neutrophils, myeloperoxidase (MPO) is a reliable indicator for evaluating the degree of neutrophil infiltration in tissues. The recruitment of neutrophils in tumor tissues after the final treatment (refer to Section 2.5 for doses administered) can be determined by immunohistochemical analysis of paraffin sections of advanced S180 tumors and 4T1 breast cancer. At the same time, to explore the correlation between neutrophil infiltration and PD-L1 expression at different tumor stages, the expression of MPO and PD-L1 in early and advanced tumor tissues of S180 tumor and 4T1 breast cancer were also investigated by immunohistochemical quantification. Briefly, in the S180 tumor model, tumor tissues were collected at the early stage of tumor bearing (3 d after inoculation) and at the advanced stage of tumor bearing (10 d after inoculation)(early and advanced stages of S180 tumors were determined by TLR values, as detailed in the Table S1). In the 4T1 breast cancer model, the tumor tissues were collected at the early stage of tumor bearing (8 d after inoculation, tumor tissue was initially formed) and at the advanced stage of tumor bearing (25 d after inoculation, lung metastases were already present). Samples were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde. After fixation in good conditions, the samples were trimmed, dehydrated, embedded, sectioned, stained, and sealed in strict accordance with pathological experiments. Image-Pro-Plus 6.0 analysis software was used after imaging was completed. Immunohistochemistry of paraffin embedded mouse tumor using MPO (GB11224) at dilution of 1:1,000 and PD-L1 (GB11339A) at dilution of 1:500.

Immunofluorescence staining

To investigate the proliferation of infiltrating CD8+ T cells in tumor tissues after the combination therapy and whether the proliferation changes are related to the reduction of PD-L1+ neutrophil infiltration, immunofluorescence staining was used to analyze the expression of CD8+ T cells and Ly-6G+ PD-L1+ neutrophils in tumor tissues after different treatments (refer to Section 2.5 for doses administered). After the anti-tumor pharmacodynamics experiment, the tumor tissues of each group were collected, fixed, dehydrated, and sectioned. Tumor sections were incubated with rabbit anti-mouse CD8 or LY-6 G and PD-L1 primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C and then incubated with fluorescein-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 h at 25 °C after washing. 4,6-Diamidino-2-phenylindole dihydrochloride (DAPI) staining was performed to determine nuclear localization. Tumor sections were mounted with an anti-fluorescence quenching mounting medium, observed under a fluorescence microscope, and photographed. Paraffin-embedded mouse tumors were detected by immunofluorescence with 1:400 dilution of Ly-6 G (GB11229), 1:1000 dilution of CD8 (GB114196) or 1:500 dilution of PD-L1 (GB11339A) antibodies.

Survival analysis

Survival analysis is an important indicator of the treatment effect for advanced tumors and triple-negative breast cancer. We established and treated the advanced S180 tumor model and the 4T1 breast cancer model mice according to the method described in Section 2.5. The observation period was set to 40 d, and the time and number of deaths in each group were recorded daily. Survival analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism software. The overall survival probability in the two models has been estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method and the Kaplan–Meier curves compared using the log-rank (Mantel–Cox) test.

Statistical analysis

Data are presented as the mean ± standard deviation (SD). Comparisons between groups were performed using a two-tailed Student's t-test. Statistical significance is indicated by a *P < 0.05,**P < 0.01.

Results and discussion

Characterization of SA-EPG-BCS

The SA-EPG-BCS phospholipid complex was prepared by a modified ethanol injection method, and the particle size, zeta potential, and composite efficiency (CE) of the preparation were used as evaluation indicators. The evaluation results showed that SA-EPG-BCS has an average particle size of 38.3 ± 2.2 nm and a zeta potential of −32.7 ± 2.1 mV. And the SA-EPG-BCS preparation have an extremely high composite efficiency (97.1% ± 0.27%). The polydispersity index (PDI) of SA-EPG-BCS preparations were 0.189 ± 0.009 (Data were shown as mean ± SD, n = 3). Moreover, the hemolytic test is one of the important tests for the safety of intravenously administered drugs. We also investigated the hemolytic rate of SA-EPG-BCS preparation by using conventional in vitro test tube method (Experimental procedures 1.1). And the results showed that the preparation of SA-EPG-BCS had no hemolysis and condensation on rabbit red blood cells and could be used for injection (Table S2). And the other characteristics of drug release, stability, cell viability and neutrophil targeting ability of SA-EPG-BCS preparation were all consistent with the results of our previous studies.

Neutrophils acquire an immunosuppressive phenotype in tumor-bearing mice

It is well known that the binding of PD-L1 expressed by neutrophils to PD-1 on CTLs is an important immunosuppressive mechanism in the TME. We collected neutrophils from the bone marrow, blood, and tumor tissues of mice with advanced S180 tumor and 4T1 breast cancer models, labeled them with Ly-6 G and PD-L1 fluorescent antibodies, and analyzed the phenotypes of neutrophils in different tissues by FCM. As shown in Fig. 1A, in the advanced S180 tumor model, approximately 88.6% neutrophils in the tumor tissue had a Ly-6G+PD-L1+ immunosuppressive phenotype, indicating that most neutrophils had been domesticated into the pro-tumor type in the background of advanced tumors. In addition, neutrophils in the blood circulation (46.4%) and bone marrow (13.0%) are partially acclimated to the PD-L1+ phenotype. Compared with the S180 tumor model, the proportion of Ly-6G+PD-L1+ neutrophils in the 4T1 breast cancer model was slightly lower (Fig. 1B); however, most tumor-infiltrating neutrophils were also immunosuppressive (77.0%), and a small amount of PD-L1 protein was expressed in the bone marrow (11.1%) and blood (29.2%). These results demonstrated that tumors affect immune cells in the tumor microenvironment and the peripheral immune environment to create an immunosuppressive environment that facilitates tumor growth and metastasis.

In addition, to explore the correlation between neutrophil infiltration and PD-L1 expression at different tumor stages, the expression of MPO and PD-L1 in early and advanced tumor tissues of S180 tumor and 4T1 breast cancer were also investigated by immunohistochemical study. As shown in Fig. 2A and 2B, the gray-brown color of the immunohistochemical sections was darker in advanced tumors than in early tumors, indicating that the expression of both PD-L1 and MPO increased with tumor progression. Immunohistochemical quantitative analysis showed that there was a positive correlation between PD-L1 expression and neutrophil infiltration in both S180 tumor and 4T1 breast cancer models. With the development of tumors, the expression of tumor immunosuppressive proteins PD-L1 and MPO showed an upward trend (Fig. 2C and 2D). Based on the results, we speculated that the proportion of tumor infiltrating immunosuppressive neutrophils was increasing as the tumor progressed. In addition, we also found that PD-L1 expression was higher in S180 model tumor tissue than in 4T1 breast cancer tumor tissue. In the S180 model, tumor tissue showed higher PD-L1 expression than MPO, whereas the opposite was observed in 4T1 breast cancer. This also indicates that the tumor immunogenicity of 4T1 breast cancer is lower than that of S180 tumors, but not completely cold tumors. The immunosuppressive neutrophils (Ly-6G+ PD-L1+) may greatly impede ICB treatments. Blocking the recruitment of neutrophils to tumors can improve the sensitivity of immunotherapy.

Combination therapy against advanced S180 and 4T1 tumors in vivo

To evaluate the adjuvant effect of blocking neutrophils on PD-L1 Ab therapy, we set up Control group, PD-L1 Ab monotherapy group, SA-EPG-BCS (neutrophil blockade) monotherapy group, and Combination therapy group for the advanced S180 tumor and 4T1 breast cancer. The assistant effect of blocking neutrophils on the anti-tumor growth and metastasis of ICIs was investigated based on tumor volume, body weight, TI index, and lung metastases.

During the experiments, the long (a) and short (b) diameters of the tumor were measured with a vernier caliper every two days, the tumor volume was calculated according to the formula V = 0.5 × a × b2, and the tumor growth curve was drawn. As shown in Fig. 3A, in the advanced S180 tumor model, compared with the control group, each treatment had a certain tumor inhibitory effect, but none of the single treatment groups produced a significant difference from the control group, indicating that in advanced tumors, a single blockade of neutrophil infiltration in tumors or the PD-L1/PD-1 signaling pathway could not produce significant anti-tumor effects. The combination therapy group significantly inhibited the tumor growth, which verified that blocking the recruitment of immunosuppressive neutrophils could effectively improve the ICB treatment effect. In the 4T1 breast cancer model, PD-L1 Ab treatment alone was less effective than SA-EPG-BCS group (Fig. 3B), suggesting that the neutrophil blockade strategy might be stronger than the ICB treatment effect in the 4T1 cold tumor model. The combination therapy group had the strongest inhibitory effect on tumor growth, which was significantly stronger than PD-L1 Ab alone treatment group, indicating that blocking the recruitment of neutrophils at the tumor site was an important contribution to the anti-tumor effect of ICB.

Reasonable evaluation of body weight changes can quickly reflect the non-specific toxicity of anti-tumor drugs; therefore, the body weight changes of tumor-bearing mice were recorded in the efficacy test. As shown in Fig. 3C and Fig. 3D, the body weight of each group in each model showed a gradual upward trend. And there was no significant difference in body weight between groups in either advanced S180 tumors or 4T1 breast cancer models, indicating that the non-specific damage to the body caused by each administration group was small, and the quality of life of mice was high.

The TI index, taking into account the inhibition of the preparation of tumor cells and non-specific damage to the body, comprehensively evaluated the therapeutic effect of each administration group according to the formula: TI index (g/g) = body weight (g)/tumor weight (g). Meantime, TI index can not only avoid the influence of individual differences between the same groups but also magnify the anti-tumor gap between different groups, which is conducive to better screening of the optimal preparation. By comparing the TI index of each group (Fig. 3E and 3F), we found that there was no significant difference between the ICB monotherapy group and the control group, regardless of the advanced S180 model or the 4T1 breast cancer model. However, compared with the advanced S180 tumor, SA-EPG-BCS group produced more obvious curative effect in the anti-4T1 breast cancer treatment, indicating that under the background of advanced S180 tumors, the immunosuppressive TME is already vast, and a single blockade of neutrophils cannot produce a good anti-tumor effect. And it also showed that neutrophils play a key role in the occurrence and development of breast cancer, which could be a good therapeutic target for 4T1 breast cancer. Both combination therapy groups had the highest TI index, demonstrating that the combination therapy strategy of tumor-associated neutrophil blockade and tumor-associated CTLs activation is feasible.

In addition, in the 4T1 breast cancer model, the mice in the control group and PD-L1 Ab treatment group developed obvious tumor metastasis, and there were many lung metastases (Fig. 3G). The average number of lung metastases in both groups exceeded 16, indicating that ICB alone did not effectively inhibit lung metastasis of 4T1 tumor cells. Compared with the PD-L1 Ab group, the SA-EPG-BCS group significantly inhibited the lung metastasis of tumor cells, suggesting that neutrophils play an important role in promoting lung metastasis of 4T1 breast cancer. The combination therapy group showed the best tumor metastasis suppression effect, indicating that blocking tumor-associated neutrophil recruitment could increase the anti-tumor functional response of CTLs to cold tumors. Moreover, the neutrophil blocking strategy can effectively inhibit the concentration of pro-tumor metastasis factors, both in tumor tissues and in the circulatory system (Fig. 3H and 3I). This is attributed to the functional inhibitory effect of colchicine on neutrophils, which can not only block the migration of neutrophils to tumors, but also inhibit the release of tumor-promoting cytokines from neutrophils, so as to improve the immunosuppressive environment of tumor tissues and peripheral circulation. The combination therapy not only improved the immunosuppressive TME, but also relieved the inhibitory effect on CTLs, so that the anti-tumor immune response was greatly enhanced. This cascade reaction improved the anti-metastasis ability, and had a significant difference with the monotherapy group (P < 0.05).

Survival analysis showed that ICB treatment alone did not prolong the median survival in mice with advanced S180 tumors or 4T1 breast cancer, with mortality rates as high as 50%. Neutrophil blockade alone did not improve survival in advanced tumors but slightly improved survival compared to ICB therapy in 4T1 breast cancer models. After the combined treatment, the median survival of both tumor models was significantly improved (Fig. 3J and 3K).

What's more, we suggested that this combination therapy may not be synergistic, but SA-EPG-BCS effectively reduced the resistance to ICB treatment, thereby improving the immune microenvironment and allowing the anti-tumor effect of PD-L1 Ab to be fully exerted, thus resulting in a good effect of combined treatment. Because in the combined treatment, we used PD-L1 Ab and SA-EPG-BCS to be administered sequentially, instead of injecting SA-EPG-BCS to block neutrophils after all the PD-L1 Ab were administered. Therefore, SA-EPG-BCS could promptly block neutrophil infiltration and improve the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment in time after each PD-L1 Ab injection, thereby reducing PD-L1 Ab resistance and giving full play to the therapeutic effect of ICB. The alternating administration of the two drugs can effectively exert the effects on both CTLs and neutrophils.

Treatment with PD-L1 Ab increases tumor infiltration of immunosuppressive neutrophils

From the results of Section 3.3, we found that ICB treatment alone did not produce significant antitumor effect, whereas its tumor suppressive ability was greatly improved after combined neutrophil blockade. Therefore, we suspected that neutrophils are one of the important reasons for hindering ICIs treatment. In order to explore the mechanism of PD-L1 Ab resistance, MPO immunohistochemical analysis was performed on tumor tissues of each group after treatment. As shown in Fig. 4A and 4B, whether in the advanced S180 tumor model or the 4T1 breast cancer model, the staining intensity was the least in the combination treatment group and the highest in the group treated with PD-L1 Ab alone, which in turn was higher than that in the control group. Immunohistochemical quantitative analysis showed that the positive MPO expression rate in the PD-L1 Ab group was significantly higher than that in the control group (Fig. 4C and 4D) (P < 0.05), suggesting that the recruitment of neutrophils to the tumor increased after PD-L1 Ab treatment alone. In addition, FCM analysis of tumor-associated neutrophils in the PD-L1 Ab monotherapy group showed that the proportion of LY6G+ PD-L1+ neutrophils in the advanced S180 tumor model was 86.4% (Fig. 4E), which was lower than that in the 4T1 breast cancer model (96.1%) (Fig. 4F). This finding suggests that PD-L1 Ab treatment increased the infiltration of neutrophils and recruited a majority of immunosuppressive phenotypes in the 4T1 breast cancer model or the S180 tumor model, which may be one of the reasons for the resistance to immunotherapy in 4T1 breast cancer and the poor effect of immunotherapy observed in advanced S180 tumors.

We hypothesized that the increase of tumor-infiltrating neutrophils caused by ICB treatment alone is due to the compensatory mechanisms of the immune system acclimated by the tumor. When PD-L1 Ab depleted the PD-L1 protein in the tumor tissue, the powerful and complex TME could be immediately supplemented by compensatory mechanisms, increasing the infiltration of immunosuppressive neutrophils expressing PD-L1 protein on the surface. This result reflected that the host immune system is sometimes an accomplice to treatment resistance. Blocking neutrophils can increase the proliferation and anti-tumor ability of CTLs, relieve the immune side effects caused by ICB treatment, and enhance the sensitivity of cold tumors to ICB.

Safety evaluation of combination therapy

In order to explore the safety of the combined treatment, on one hand, we analyzed the potential toxicity of the combined treatment to the major organs of mice by histopathological sections. On the other hand, the determination of tumor-promoting and inflammatory cytokines in the TME was used to analyze whether the combination therapy could relieve immune suppression and avoid immune-related side effects. As shown in Fig. 5A and 5B, myocardial cells were intact in all preparation groups, and no myocardial disruption was observed. For liver, kidney and spleen sections, there was no obvious inflammation in the tissues and no obvious abnormality compared with the control group. For lung sections, there was also no significant histopathological abnormality except for a small inflammatory cell infiltration in the control and PD-L1 Ab groups in the 4T1 breast cancer model (black arrows) (Fig. 5B). This result indicates that the combination therapy has a high safety to the body.

ELISA detection of inflammatory factors IFN-γ and IL-12, and tumor-promoting factors IL-10 and LTB4 in tumor tissue homogenate confirmed that the expression level of tumor-promoting factors was high when PD-L1 Ab was used alone (Fig. 5C and 5D), while inflammatory factor expression levels remained low (Fig. 5E and 5F), with no significant difference from the control group, suggesting that the immunological side effect of increased tumor-associated neutrophil infiltration caused by ICB treatment greatly limits its efficacy. The combined treatment significantly reduced the expression levels of IL-10 and LTB4 in tumors and increased the concentration of inflammatory factors after treatment, indicating that combination therapy can effectively improve the immunosuppressive TME, enhance the anti-tumor immune response, restore tumor immune surveillance, and do not produce new immune side effects. Notably, we found no significant difference in the antitumor cytokine concentrations between the SA-EPG-BCS group and the combination treatment group in the 4T1 breast cancer model (Fig. 5F), from which we speculate that tumor-infiltrating neutrophil-mediated immunosuppression environment has a great influence on breast cancer, which masks the anti-tumor function of CTLs mediated by PD-L1 Ab. Therefore, there is no significant difference in the concentration of anti-tumor factors between single blockade of neutrophils and combination therapy.

Mechanism of neutrophil blockade strategy to improve the therapeutic effect of ICB

To investigate the mechanism by which neutrophil blockade treatment can improve the anti-tumor growth and metastatic ability of PD-L1 Ab, we established advanced S180 tumor-bearing mice and 4T1 breast cancer mouse models. After treatment in each group, the effects on the proliferation and anti-tumor ability of CTLs were studied by tumor section immunofluorescence staining, FCM, and ELISA.

Immunofluorescence analysis showed that the number of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T lymphocytes increased in each treatment group compared to that in the control group, regardless of the advanced S180 tumor model or the 4T1 breast cancer model; however, no significant difference was observed in the PD-L1 Ab treatment group. The combined treatment group showed the strongest fluorescence and largest number of CTLs (Fig. 6C and 6D). Moreover, FCM results showed that only 16.7% of the tumor-infiltrating CTLs (CD3+ CD8+) in the control group and 94.8% of the CTLs were of the PD-1+exhaustion phenotype (Fig. 6A). Treatment with PD-L1 Ab alone did not significantly increase the proportion of CTLs, and 75.4% of the CTLs retained the PD-1+ exhaustion phenotype. The proportion of CD8+ T cells in the SA-EPG-BCS group (28.9%) was slightly higher than that in the PD-L1 Ab treatment group, while the proportion of the exhaustion phenotype (PD-1+) (54.8%) in the SA-EPG-BCS group was lower than that in the PD-L1 Ab group (75.4%). These results indicate that ICIs treatment produced drug resistance and did not relieve the immune suppression effect of TME on CTLs. In contrast, neutrophil blockade may lead to a better anti-tumor immune response. The combination therapy group had the highest proportion (40.3%) of CTLs with the lowest expression of the PD-1+ phenotype (40.1%), consistent with the pharmacodynamic results and revealing that weakening the inhibitory effect of neutrophils on CTLs could effectively improve the anti-tumor effect of immunotherapy. In the 4T1 breast cancer model, the trend of infiltration by exhausted CTLs in the four treatment groups was consistent with that in the advanced S180 tumor model; however, the proportion of CD8+ T cells in the 4T1 breast cancer model was significantly lower than that in the advanced S180 tumor model (Fig. 6B), indicating that the immunogenicity of S180 sarcoma was higher than that in the 4T1 breast cancer model. We also investigated the phenotype of CD8+ T lymphocytes before neutrophil blockade in advanced S180 tumors and 4T1 breast cancer tissues by FCM. Before interference with neutrophil recruitment to the tumor, the proportion of CD8+ T cells in advanced S180 tumors and 4T1 breast cancer infiltrating tumors was significantly lower than that after neutrophil blockade, and most of the phenotypes were exhausted type (Fig. S4).

In addition, we performed immunofluorescence labeling (LY6G+ PDL1+) of tumor-infiltrating neutrophils in advanced S180 tumors and 4T1 breast cancer. The results showed that the fluorescence intensity of neutrophils in the PD-L1 Ab group and the control group was the strongest (Fig. 7A and 7B), and the immunosuppressive neutrophil infiltration in the PD-L1 Ab group exceeded that in the control group, consistent with the results shown in Fig. 3. In addition, the expression of PD-L1 protein was observed in the 4T1 tumor fluorescence section (Fig. 7B), although it was weaker than that in advanced S180 tumors, indicating that ICB treatment should be effective in theory, but the increase in neutrophils caused by ICB treatment alone limited its effect. After the addition of neutrophil blocking therapy, the number of tumor-infiltrating CTLs and the concentration of anti-tumor factors increased, and finally, the anti-tumor effect of ICB was significantly improved. In the combination treatment group, not only were neutrophils less numerous than in the control group, but PD-L1 expression was also significantly reduced in the immunosuppressive phenotype.

Moreover, it was not difficult to find that in our study, the 4T1 breast cancer, a cold tumor model, showed the same trend of enhanced anti-tumor ability as the S180 sarcoma, but the degree of enhancement was significantly different between them. The level of CD8+T cells in 4T1 breast cancer was lower than that in S180 sarcoma in each group. Immunofluorescence sections also showed that the proportion of tumor-infiltrating CD8+T cells in 4T1 breast cancer was much lower than that in S180 tumors (Fig. 6C and 6D), and we found that this subtype of breast cancer was not devoid of PD-L1 protein (Fig. 7B).

To sum up, we thought that there are two potential mechanisms of the increase in the number of CD8+ T cells after the blockage of neutrophil infiltration. On the one hand, immunosuppressive neutrophils bind to the PD-1 receptor on the surface of CTLs through PD-L1 ligand, which directly inhibits the proliferation of CTLs. In addition, the in vitro cell experiment also demonstrated that tumor-stimulated neutrophils could directly inhibit the secretion of INF-γ inflammatory cytokines by CTLs (Fig. S2). On the other hand, we thought that blocking neutrophil infiltration alleviated the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, and various tumor-promoting immune factors of tumor chemotaxis were effectively reduced, relieving the immune suppression effect on CTLs.

Conclusion

In this study, we established two different models of advanced S180 and 4T1 breast cancer and confirmed that one of the factors limiting the application of PD-L1 Ab is that immunotherapy treatment increases immunosuppressive neutrophil infiltration. We blocked the recruitment of neutrophils in tumors using SA-modified colchicine derivative phospholipid complexes and attenuated the inhibitory effect of neutrophils on CTLs. This strategy has a significant effect on immunotherapy. The adjuvant effect enhanced the infiltration of CTLs in advanced S180 tumors and cold tumors and significantly improved the anti-tumor effect of ICB. This study not only highlights the need for combination therapy but also highlights the important contribution of a nanoformulation platform based on active targeting of sialic acid in blocking neutrophils.
