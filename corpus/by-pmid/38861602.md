# Iridium(III) carbene complexes as potent girdin inhibitors against metastatic cancers.

## Abstract

Many cancer-driving protein targets remain undruggable due to a lack of binding molecular scaffolds. In this regard, octahedral metal complexes with unique and versatile three-dimensional structures have rarely been explored as inhibitors of undruggable protein targets. Here, we describe antitumor iridium(III) pyridinium-N-heterocyclic carbene complex 1a, which profoundly reduces the viability of lung and breast cancer cells as well as cancer patient-derived organoids at low micromolar concentrations. Compound 1a effectively inhibits the growth of non-small-cell lung cancer and triple-negative breast cancer xenograft tumors, impedes the metastatic spread of breast cancer cells, and can be modified into an antibody-drug conjugate payload to achieve precise tumor delivery in mice. Identified by thermal proteome profiling, an important molecular target of 1a in cellulo is Girdin, a multifunctional adaptor protein that is overexpressed in cancer cells and unequivocally serves as a signaling hub for multiple pivotal oncogenic pathways. However, specific small-molecule inhibitors of Girdin have not yet been developed. Notably, 1a exhibits high binding affinity to Girdin with a Kd of 1.3 μM and targets the Girdin-linked EGFR/AKT/mTOR/STAT3 cancer-driving pathway, inhibiting cancer cell proliferation and metastatic activity. Our study reveals a potent Girdin-targeting anticancer compound and demonstrates that octahedral metal complexes constitute an untapped library of small-molecule inhibitors that can fit into the ligand-binding pockets of key oncoproteins.

## Full Text

Iridium(III) carbene complexes as potent girdin inhibitors against metastatic cancers

Significance

We demonstrate that substitutionally inert octahedral metal complexes, such as the iridium(III) carbene complex (1a), provide a useful source of potent inhibitors for unexplored oncogenic protein targets through hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions. Complex 1a inhibits metastatic spread of cancer cells in mice. It reduces the viability of lung and triple-negative breast cancer patient-derived organoids more effectively than cisplatin. It fits tightly (with low micromolar affinity) into an unexplored binding pocket of Girdin through hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions. According to bioinformatic analysis, Girdin is a compelling molecular target in different solid tumors and lacks effective inhibitors. This iridium(III) carbene complex is suitable for antibody conjugation strategies to achieve increased tumor delivery in mice and reduced systemic payload distribution in normal tissues.

In this regard, octahedral metal complexes with unique and versatile three-dimensional structures have rarely been explored as inhibitors of undruggable protein targets. Overexpression of oncogenes and crosstalk among oncoprotein-regulated signaling cascades in other intracellular pathways lead to tumorigenesis. Therefore, efficiently and specifically targeting cancer-driving proteins could enable the development of anticancer compounds against advanced metastatic cancers. However, although many identified target proteins lead to uncontrolled cell growth and metastasis, no effective inhibitors of their effects have been identified to date. Typically, synthetic organic compounds and natural products are used to develop small-molecule inhibitors of protein and nucleic acid targets. Recent studies have shown that the use of substitution-inert metal–organic complexes whose structures can be varied by changing the metals and/or ligands is a new strategy for developing structural scaffolds that bind cancer-driving proteins through electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions.

Girdin [also known as girders of the actin filament, AKT phosphorylation enhancer (APE), or Gα-interacting vesicle-associated protein (GIV) ] is a key cancer-associated protein that is highly expressed in many human cancer cells at levels higher than in normal tissue. It serves as a prognostic biomarker for lung cancer, breast cancer, and pancreatic cancer. The key regulatory components of Girdin are the GEF domain and adaptor-binding domain located at the C-terminus, which interact with Gαi3 protein, epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), signal transducers and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3), and actin. Girdin interacts with these proteins to regulate corresponding signaling that is related to cancer cell proliferation, migration, invasion, and angiogenesis. Overwhelming evidence from gene knockdown and overexpression experiments indicates that Girdin is an important cancer-driving protein. Some organic compounds have been reported to exhibit anticancer effects by indirectly inhibiting Girdin expression or downregulating Girdin-regulated signaling. To our knowledge, direct targeting inhibitors of Girdin have not been reported.

In the literature, it is occasionally reported that octahedral metal complexes, represented by cyclometalated iridium(III) complexes, exhibit effective anticancer effects in vitro and also have potent antitumor properties in vivo. Since cyclometalated iridium(III) complexes are substitutional and redox-inert under cellular conditions, their anticancer mechanism of action(s) may originate from their noncovalent interactions with cancer-related biomolecular targets. Octahedral metal complexes have unique 3D structures that differ from most conventional small organic molecules and we are interested in understanding how these 3D structures fit into the ligand-binding pocket of a protein target. Although some studies have revealed the regulation of molecular pathways and protein–protein interactions by cyclometalated iridium(III) complexes, there have been no reports showing that these complexes can directly bind key cancer-driving proteins with specificity and high affinity. The use of comprehensive proteomic analysis to unbiasedly identify specific molecular targets of anticancer metal compounds has rarely been reported in the literature.

Here, we report a luminescent iridium(III) pyridinium N-heterocyclic carbene (NHC) complex that effectively kills cultured cancer cells as well as patient-derived cancer organoids and inhibits cancer cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. This iridium(III) complex exhibits potent antitumor properties in human lung cancer and triple-negative breast cancer xenograft tumors in mice. Using thermal proteome profiling method, we found that 1a binds with high affinity to cellular Girdin, a G protein- and actin-binding protein. Complex 1a inhibits Girdin-G protein interactions and related cancer-driving pathways involving the EGFR/AKT/mTOR/STAT3 cascade, which are known to play critical roles in metastatic lung cancer and triple-negative breast cancer. Our study demonstrates an anticancer iridium(III) compound that interacts with the cancer-driving Girdin protein and reveals an octahedral metal complex for protein targeting with high binding affinity and specificity.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and Properties of Iridium(III) Complexes.

The synthesis and emission properties of complexes 1a–1e have been described previously. These iridium(III) complexes are highly luminescent in deaerated CH2Cl2 at room temperature, with emission maxima ranging from 524 to 612 nm, lifetimes as long as 2.18 μs, and quantum yields as high as 0.81 (SI Appendix, Table S1). In this work, further characterization of 1a in biological media is detailed in SI Appendix (Fig. 1 and SI Appendix, Figs. S1–S4 and Tables S2 and S3). Incubation of this complex in the presence of glutathione for 48 h resulted in no changes in the UV-VIS absorption and high-resolution mass spectra, indicating that 1a is stable under biological conditions (SI Appendix, Figs. S5–S7).

Antiproliferative, Cell Killing, Anti-invasion, Antimetastatic, and Antiangiogenic Properties.

The in vitro cytotoxicity of iridium(III) carbene complexes against a panel of human cancer cell lines was analyzed by MTT assay (Table 1). Complexes 1a–1e exhibited potent in vitro cytotoxicity against cancer cell lines after 72 h of treatment, with IC50 values ranging from submicromolar to low micromolar concentrations (0.08 to 0.82 μM). Compared with cisplatin (DDP), the IC50 values of 1a–1e are generally one to two orders of magnitude lower. Although the IC50 value of cisplatin in DDP-resistant ovarian cancer cells (A2780cis, IC50 = 11.62 μM) was ~37-fold higher than that in cisplatin-sensitive A2780 cells (IC50 =0.31 μM), complexes 1a–1e each exhibited comparable cytotoxicity in A2780 and A2780cis cells. Furthermore, 1a–1e exhibited selective cytotoxicity against cancer cells, whereas the cytotoxicity induced in normal fibroblasts (CCD-19Lu) was significantly attenuated. In cellular uptake experiments, NCI-H460 lung cancer and normal fibroblasts (CCD-19Lu) were treated with 1a, and the iridium content in these cells was measured by ICP-MS. The accumulation of iridium in NCI-H460 cells treated with 1a was ~3-fold higher than that in CCD-19Lu cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S8).

We performed high-content analysis by counting cell nuclei in the image (cells with Hoechst 33342 staining) and measuring cell membrane disruption (by propidium iodide staining) to determine the total number and proportion of dead cells, respectively, after 1a treatment. Results showed that 1a significantly inhibited the proliferation of NCI-H460 and MDA-MB-231 cancer cells at concentrations as low as 0.5 μM (Fig. 2A). Complex 1a at 4 μM markedly induced cancer cell death, killing 94% of the cells within 72 h of treatment. For comparison, 40 μM DDP was found to induce death in 69% of NCI-H460 cancer cells and 37% of MDA-MB-231 cancer cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). After a 24-h treatment with 4 μM 1a, the cell death modalities in NCI-H460 and MDA-MB-231 cells involved apoptosis with, respectively, 32.0 % and 26.0% of cells stained with annexin-V-Alexa 647 (SI Appendix, Fig. S10). Flow cytometry cell cycle analysis showed that 1a induced accumulation of a significant portion of cells in the S phase (30 % for 1a vs. 14% for control) and G2/M phase (12% for 1a vs. 8 % for control) with reduced contents in G1 phase (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). The effect of 1a on cancer stem cell expansion was assessed through a tumor-sphere formation assay, and the results showed that administration of 1 μM 1a significantly reduced the number and size of formed tumor spheres (Fig. 2B). The effect of 1a on cell motility was studied using a wound-healing scratch assay and it showed that cell migration to the wound area was inhibited after 24-h treatment with 0.25 µM 1a (Fig. 2C). The anti-invasive properties of 1a were evaluated through Matrigel Transwell assay. Treatment of NCI-H460 lung cancer cells or MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells at a subcytotoxic concentration of 0.3 µM 1a reduced the amount of cancer cells that passed through the Matrigel-coated membrane (Fig. 2D). By examining the effects of 1a on metastatic MDA-MB-231 cell migration by phase contrast and immunofluorescence imaging, untreated control cells were found to have extensive lamellipodia formation marked by cortactin immunostaining at their leading edges. When cells were exposed to 1 µM 1a for 16 h, the lamellipodia formation was significantly inhibited (Fig. 2E). The antiangiogenic activity of 1a was assessed using the MS-1 endothelial cell tube formation assay. Compared with the vehicle control group, the number of tubular structures in MS-1 cells cultured on Matrigel was significantly reduced after treatment with 1a at a subcytotoxic concentration of 0.3 µM (SI Appendix, Fig. S12).

In Vivo Studies.

Acute toxicity studies of 1a were performed using ICR mice injected intravenously with 1a. The minimum lethal dose of 1a for intravenous injection to mice was found to be 75 mg/kg, and the maximum tolerated dose without significant body weight loss or side effects was 60 mg/kg (SI Appendix, Tables S4 and S5). We also evaluated the chronic toxicity of 1a at different doses of 6, 9, and 15 mg/kg (intravenously three times per week, 28-d drug exposure, and 14-d recovery). There was no marked difference in body weight and food consumption between treatment groups (6 and 9 mg/kg) and control groups during the drug exposure and recovery period. The body weight of male mice in the high dose 1a group (15 mg/kg) decreased significantly on days 6, 10, 17, and 24 compared with the vehicle control group (SI Appendix, Tables S6–S9). Hematological and biochemical parameters of the mice in treatment and control groups were found with normal variations (SI Appendix, Tables S10–S13), and no gross anatomical and histopathological abnormalities were observed. Based on all results, the no observed adverse effect level (NOAEL) for chronic administration of 1a in mice by the end of the examination period was 15 mg/kg.

In vivo antitumor experiments were conducted on nude mice carrying human lung cancer NCI-H460 xenografts and breast cancer MDA-MB-231 xenografts. Treatment of mice with 1a (9 mg/kg, twice weekly intravenously) resulted in a reduction in tumor volume of 77.5% (P < 0.05) for NCI-H460 xenografts and a reduction of 92.2% tumor volume for MDA-MB-231 xenografts. Over the course of the study, mice did not show critical body weight loss or lethality compared with vehicle control group (Fig. 3 A and B and SI Appendix, Fig. S13). In NCI-H460 xenografts, mice given an effective tumor-inhibitory dose of 1a were better tolerated than mice treated with an equivalent molar mass of DDP (10 μmole/kg) (Fig. 3A). When mice were treated with 1a (9 mg/kg; M.W. = 869.25 g/mol) or DDP (3 mg/kg; M.W.= 300.05 g/mol), the tumor growth inhibition rate of 1a treatment and DDP treatment was ~80%. However, DDP-treated mice showed significant body weight loss, whereas 1a-treated mice did not. In addition, the effect of 1a treatment on lung metastasis in nude mice injected intravenously with MDA-MB-231 cells expressing a luciferase reporter was also determined. As shown in bioluminescence images (Fig. 3C), 1a treatment (6 or 9 mg/kg intravenously three times per week) resulted in a significant reduction (P < 0.05) in mean tumor growth in the lungs by 71.1% (1a, 6 mg/kg) or 83.9% (1a, 9 mg/kg). There were negligible changes in the body weight of the 6 mg/kg treatment group compared to that of solvent control over the 31-d period (Fig. 3D). Histological analysis revealed that 1a treatment significantly reduced the number and area of metastatic nodules in the lungs compared with vehicle control (Fig. 3E). After 1a treatment, no abnormalities were found in the histology of liver, kidney, heart, and spleen, and there were no changes in serum markers of kidney injury (creatine) and liver injury markers (aspartate aminotransferase) (SI Appendix, Fig. S14).

Next, nude mice bearing NCI-H460 lung cancer xenografts were injected with 1a (3 to 9 mg/kg) twice a week for 3 wk, and the biodistribution of 1a in tumors and different organs was analyzed by ICP-MS. Iridium is not only present in tumors but also distributed in various organs, with significantly higher accumulation in the kidneys (SI Appendix, Fig. S15). We anticipate increasing the biodistribution and targeted delivery of 1a by conjugating 1a to tumor-targeting ligands. In this regard, the submicromolar anticancer potency of 1a and the availability of functionalized sites make this iridium(III) complex a promising cytotoxic payload for antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs). An ADC containing an anti-HER2 trastuzumab monoclonal antibody and a derivative of 1a (ADC-1a) was prepared (Fig. 4 A–E). We modified 1a to possess the functional groups needed for bioconjugation and prepared the hydroxyl analog of 1a, 1a-OH. The IC50 value of 1a-OH against HER2-positive SKOV-3 ovarian adenocarcinoma cells is comparable to 1a; the IC50 values for 1a and 1a-OH are at submicromolar concentrations. Following the design used for the FDA-approved trastuzumab deruxtecan, we conjugated 1a-OH to trastuzumab via a cathepsin B-cleavable tetrapeptide linker. To examine the tumor targeting effect in vivo, ADC-1a (10 mg/kg) or 1a-OH (0.3 mg/kg) (both with equivalent levels of the 1a unit) was intravenously injected into SKOV-3 tumor-bearing nude mice. In vivo biodistribution of iridium content was analyzed by ICP-MS 1 d and 7 d posttreatment. As shown in Fig. 4 F and G, administration of ADC-1a resulted in a significant 10-fold increase in iridium accumulation in tumors and a 90% reduction in iridium levels in the kidney compared with the effect of 1a-OH treatment. These data demonstrate that 1a is suitable for use in ADC-based strategies, showing increased tumor delivery in vivo and reduced systemic payload distribution in normal tissues.

Using lung and triple-negative breast cancer patient-derived organoids (PDOs) as realistic models for cancer drug testing, administration of 0.5 and 5 μM 1a significantly induced a decrease in the viability of both PDOs in a dose-dependent manner, with 5 µM 1a showing an inhibition rate reaching over 98%. Of note, only 38% inhibition was obtained using DDP at a concentration of 14.4 μM, which is the reference maximum plasma concentration obtained at clinical doses (Fig. 5 A and B).

Intracellular Localization of 1a in Cancer Cells.

We performed confocal imaging of cells exposed to 1a. After NCI-H460 lung cancer cells were incubated with 1a (1 μM for 1 h), the strong photoluminescence of 1a (emission: 490 to 570 nm) mainly occurred in the cytoplasmic region, and this photoluminescence was colocalized with the fluorescence of mitochondria and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) tracking dyes (Fig. 6A). The accumulation of 1a in mitochondria may be attributed to electrostatic interactions between the cationic charge of the complex and the inner mitochondrial membrane, which has a negative membrane potential. Mitochondrial respiratory activity of NCI-H460 cells was measured as oxygen consumption rate (OCR) by Seahorse assay. The results showed that 0.1 and 0.3 µM 1a reduced oxygen consumption, leading to a significant reduction in mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation-mediated ATP production (Fig. 6B). The accumulation of 1a in the endoplasmic reticulum was also found to be accompanied by the induction of ER stress-associated proteins, including CHOP (a proapoptotic transcription factor) and phosphorylated eIF2α (which attenuates protein synthesis and cell proliferation) (Fig. 6C).

1a Targets Girdin in Cancer Cells.

As previously described, we performed thermal proteome profiling (TPP) to identify direct molecular targets of 1a in cancer cells. (Fig. 7A and SI Appendix, Table S14) This method is based on cellular thermal shift assays, where the ligand bound protein is more stable and therefore the rate of heat-induced unfolding or precipitation is reduced even under cellular conditions. Using TPP, Girdin was identified as the protein with the highest degree of thermal stabilization in NCI-H460 cells treated with 1a. Treatment with 1a significantly increased the melting temperature (Tm) of Girdin by approximately 10.3 ± 1.0 °C (P < 0.01 in three TPP MS determinations) without affecting the Tm of the housekeeping protein GAPDH (Fig. 7 A and B and SI Appendix, Fig. S16A). This finding was validated using cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA) and immunoblotting (Fig. 7C and SI Appendix, Fig. S16B). Furthermore, isothermal dose–response (ITDR) analysis showed that the thermal stabilization of Girdin at 45 °C by 1a was dose-dependent from 0.5 to 4 μM (Fig. 7D). CETSA and ITDR assays were also performed on MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S17). The results showed that the Tm of Girdin increased by approximately 2 °C after cancer cells were treated with 1a. Dose-dependent thermal stabilization of Girdin at 45 °C by 1a treatment was observed. All these results indicate that 1a targets Girdin in cancer cells.

The C-terminal domain of Girdin interacts with multiple signaling proteins, and a purified protein encoded by the corresponding sequence (His-Girdin-CT 1660-1870) of Girdin was prepared and used to measure the binding affinity of 1a to Girdin (SI Appendix, Figs. S18 and S19). The dissociation constant Kd of the bound complex between His-Girdin-CT and 1a was approximately 1.3 μM, measured by microscale thermophoresis (MST) (Fig. 7E and SI Appendix, Fig. S20). Binding affinity was also determined by measuring the photoluminescence enhancement at λem = 440 to 800 nm after treatment with 1a (20 μM) and titrated by increasing concentrations of His-Girdin-CT (0.1 to 20 μM). This shows a Kd of 0.5 μM (SI Appendix, Fig. S21). Molecular docking analysis of the C-terminus of Girdin (1702 to 1835) containing the protein binding SH2 domain revealed a pocket formed by conserved motifs (G1740XFXXR1745 and pS1716XXF1719). Docking analysis showed that 1a is sandwiched between two phosphorylation sites at S1716 and Y1743 that regulate Girdin function (Fig. 7F and SI Appendix, Fig. S22 and Table S15). This pocket is bell-shaped with a larger hydrophilic and smaller hydrophobic cavity (Fig. 7F), and the octahedral cation 1a can fit well through favorable hydrophobic and electrostatic interactions. The negatively charged residue D1744 is located on one side of the 4-R-iso-BIPY ligand of 1a, while D1718 is located in the corner formed by the 4-R-iso-BIPY and one of the PPY ligands. The pyrrolidine group of the 4-R-iso-BIPY ligand extends into the small cavity formed by hydrophobic residues L1713, F1719, L1801, and P1802. The cavity between P1802 and D1718 is narrow, which may affect the binding affinity of the Ir(III) complexes containing different 4-R-iso-BIPY ligands.

To further understand the binding of complex 1a to Girdin, we determined the Kd (dissociation constants) of the bound complexes between His-Girdin-CT and a group of Ir1 derivatives containing different 4-R-iso-BIPY ligands [1b R = tert-butyl, Kd = 3.6 μM; 1c R = morpholinyl, Kd = 2.6 μM; 1d R = piperidinyl, Kd = 1.5 μM; 1e R = dimethyl amino, Kd = 1.6 μM]. Complexes 1b to 1e show similar dissociation constants to 1a as measured by MST (Fig. 8E and SI Appendix, Fig. S20). We also prepared a derivative (Irppy-bipy) (Fig. 7G) whose BIPY ligand does not contain any substituted R groups. The synthesis and photophysical studies of Irppy-bipy have been reported in the literature (SI Appendix). Through MST measurements, it was found that the binding pose of Irppy-bipy weakly binds to His-Girdin-CT (Kd > 20 μM; Fig. 7G and SI Appendix, Fig. S20). Collectively, these results indicate that the pyrrolidine group of the 4-R-iso-BIPY ligand plays an important role in the outer-sphere interaction of complex 1a (ligand outer-sphere hydrophobic interaction) with the Girdin binding pocket (Fig. 7H). The Gαi-Girdin binding interface can be a druggable protein–protein interaction site. In this work, protein pull-down assays using purified Girdin C-terminal GST fusion proteins (GST-Girdin-CT 1623-1870) and His-Gαi3 showed that the amount of Gαi3 captured by immobilized GST-Girdin-CT was reduced by 1a, showing that 1a disrupts the Gαi3-Girdin protein-protein interaction (SI Appendix, Figs. S23 and S24). Aurintricarboxylic acid (ATA) has previously been shown to interact with Gαi3, disrupting its interaction with the Girdin C-terminus and reducing the amount of Gαi3 pulled down. The binding affinity of 1a to Gαi3 was determined to be 15 μM, whereas the binding affinity of ATA to Gαi3 was approximately 1.6 μM (SI Appendix, Figs. S25 and S26). Taken together, 1a binds to the Girdin C-terminal domain with high affinity (Fig. 7E), possibly leading to blockade of the Gαi3–Girdin interaction.

Knockdown of Girdin Inhibits Cancer Cell Proliferation and Cell Migration.

The role of Girdin in cancer cell proliferation and migration was evaluated by siRNA knockdown. In NCI-H460 lung cancer cells and MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells, Girdin siRNA effectively reduced Girdin expression (Fig. 8A). The cell viability of Girdin-deficient cells was lower than that of cells transfected with negative control siRNA (Fig. 8B, P < 0.05). In wound healing assay, Girdin-deficient cells exhibited significantly slower cell migration at 24 h in NCI-H460 cells and at 7 h in MDA-MB-231 cells compared with negative control siRNA-treated cells (Fig. 8 C and D, P < 0.05). These data suggest that reduced Girdin expression results in inhibition of cancer cell viability and migration. Through immunoblot assays, we found that the expressions of Girdin protein in cancer cell lines such as liver cancer (HepG2 cells), triple-negative breast cancer (MDA-MB-231 cells), and lung cancer (NCI-H460 cells) are higher than that in normal lung fibroblasts (CCD-19Lu cells) (SI Appendix, Fig. S27).

Complex 1a Inhibits the Molecular Functions of Girdin.

The PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway is an oncogenic signaling pathway frequently activated in cancers, and inhibitors such as alpelisib (PI3K inhibitor) and ipatasertib (AKT inhibitor) have been shown to be effective in advanced lung cancer and triple-negative breast cancer. Girdin activates the oncogenic PI3K/AKT pathway through GEF activity and interacts with AKT, resulting in positive feedback activation. Here, treatment of NCI-H460 cells with 0.1 to 1 μM 1a for 24 h downregulated the Girdin-AKT pathway. This is manifested by reduced expression of Girdin and AKT1, as well as reduced levels of phosphorylated forms of AKT, the mTOR substrate p70S6K kinase, and subsequently the substrate S6 ribosomal protein, whose functions are related to the metabolic requirements of cancer cell proliferation and angiogenesis (Fig. 8E). Expression proteomic analysis of the effect of 1a on protein profiles in NCI-H460 cells was also performed to identify key nodes in the 1a regulatory pathway. Consistent with immunoblot assays, proteomic analysis revealed that 1a treatment (1 µM, 24 h) downregulated Girdin and AKT1 levels. Through topology analysis of differentially expressed proteins in a protein–protein association network, AKT1 was identified as a central node within the network. Girdin was identified to be significantly associated with AKT1 in the network analysis (SI Appendix, Fig. S28). Additionally, evidence was obtained that 1a regulates PI3K/AKT signaling in vivo. Blood glucose levels are regulated by insulin-stimulated glucose uptake in tissues through the receptor-mediated PI3K/AKT signaling activation system. PI3K/AKT inhibitors are known to impair PI3K/AKT activity in systemic tissues, leading to increased blood glucose. Herein, treatment of mice with a single dose of 1a (6 or 9 mg/kg) resulted in a significant rise in blood glucose levels from 5.7 to an average of 7.3 mmol/L within 30 min, followed by a return to baseline levels by 24 h (n = 3, SI Appendix, Fig. S29), which is consistent with the usual systemic effects of PI3K/AKT inhibitors.

Many receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs), especially EGFR, are frequently overexpressed or activated in lung cancer. Activated or phosphorylated EGFR can recruit Girdin through receptor kinase domain interactions, thereby inducing cancer progression signaling. Fig. 8F and SI Appendix, Fig. S30 show that treatment of NCI-H460 cells and MDA-MB-231 cells for 30 min with 1a at doses of 1 to 4 μM resulted in significant downregulation of EGF-stimulated EGFR (Y1068) phosphorylation. This effect was dose-dependent and could be attributed to a direct effect of Girdin on EGFR RTK activity and/or a specific disruption of GEF function induced by 1a treatment.

Signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) is a central transcriptional regulator of cancer progression and metastasis genes. STAT3 directly binds to the Girdin gene promoter and activates Girdin transcription. In turn, Girdin actively autoregulates its own transcription to enhance STAT3 activation through its GEF function. As shown in Fig. 8G, treatment of NCI-H460 cells with 1a (0.3 and 1 μM for 4 to 24 h) significantly reduced the levels of phosphorylated STAT3 (Y705), indicating inactivation of STAT3 activity.

1a induced accumulation of a significant portion of cells in S and G2/M phases (SI Appendix, Fig. S11). In this regard, Girdin was recently identified as a replication initiation factor capable of binding to DNA replication origins and interacting with the origin recognition complex (ORC) during DNA replication in human cells. This may be a possible mechanism by which 1a regulates the cell cycle related to Girdin function.

During cell migration, Girdin is phosphorylated at the leading edge of motile cells and contributes to the formation of short-branched actin filaments in lamellipodia. Girdin is also a phosphorylation substrate of AKT kinase. Treatment of NCI-H460 cells with 1a significantly reduced the level of phosphorylated Girdin (S1416), thereby affecting Girdin function (Fig. 8H).

Girdin is a multidomain signaling molecule that carries key regulatory components at the C-terminus. Through the guanine nucleotide exchange factor (GEF) domain, Girdin links trimeric G-proteins (Gαβγ) and activates the Gαi protein, thereby regulating key signaling networks downstream of various receptors. The extreme C-terminus interacts with growth factor receptor kinases such as epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and cytoskeletons such as actin. In this work, the effect of 1a on Girdin-associated signaling in cancer development was investigated.

Taken together, 1a has a significant impact on the molecular functions of Girdin in controlling cancer cell survival and migration. Multiple parallel but intervening oncogenic and invasion-promoting pathways, such as EGFR, PI3K/AKT, and STAT3, are expected to be inhibited by targeting a single multifunctional cancer protein such as Girdin (Fig. 8I). This effect may be beneficial in treating cancers that are highly heterogeneous and do not express major oncogenic driver proteins, such as certain types of lung cancer and triple-negative breast cancer.

Girdin’s Bioinformatic Analysis.

Girdin is encoded by the CCDC88A gene. Bioinformatic analysis based on The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database showed that missense mutations, gene fusions, and splicing mutations of CCDC88A were found in solid tumors (Fig. 9A and SI Appendix, Table S16). In particular, mutations found in breast and lung tumor tissues occur at a frequency of 3% to 5%, which may lead to the dysregulation of CCDC88A in the corresponding cancers. Analysis of gene expression data (Fig. 9B and SI Appendix, Table S17) showed that CCDC88A mRNA expression in tumors of diverse cancer types was higher than that in paired normal tissues. Furthermore, analysis of functional profile of CCDC88A in cancers using Expression Atlas database showed that CCDC88A mRNA was significantly overexpressed in breast cancer tumor samples (log2 fold change > 1.5 and adjusted P-value < 0.001) (Table 2). In clinical survival analysis, cancer patients with higher CCDC88A expression had significantly lower survival rates than patients with lower CCDC88A expression (SI Appendix, Fig. S31, P < 0.05). In summary, CCDC88A is a potential target in multiple solid cancers, including lung and breast cancers, with supporting evidence from analysis of genomic mutations, tumor tissue mRNA expression, and clinical survival data. Notably, as mentioned above, complex 1a showed good antitumor effects on lung cancer and breast cancer in mice, and Girdin was identified and validated as a molecular target during proteomic and cell signaling studies of 1a-treated lung and breast cancer cells.

Conclusion

We found a unique anticancer iridium(III) compound 1a, which can effectively reduce cancer cell viability and inhibit cancer cell migration and invasion when administered at submicromolar to low micromolar concentrations. The complex can significantly induce cell death in patient-derived lung and breast cancer organoids and exhibit significant in vivo anti-tumor effects in lung and invasive breast cancer models. By using unbiased thermal proteomic analysis, Girdin was identified as a specific cellular target of 1a. Girdin engagement of 1a in cancer cells significantly increases the protein thermal stability. 1a binds to the C-terminus of Girdin with micromolar affinity, and its octahedral scaffold docks favorably into a binding pocket located within the multifunctional protein binding domain. Biological cellular studies have shown that 1a blocks key oncogenic signaling pathways related to Girdin adapter function, including the EGFR and PI3K/AKT pathways. Expression proteomics and protein network topology analysis reaffirmed that 1a downregulates the Girdin-AKT signaling pathway. Although our bioinformatics analysis demonstrates that Girdin is a compelling anticancer molecular target in a variety of solid tumors, including lung and breast cancer, to date, chemical inhibitors directly targeting Girdin have not been recognized.

In summary, we show that 3D spatial structures represented by octahedral metal complexes are unique and versatile structural scaffolds that provide optimal molecular interactions with certain cancer-promoting proteins, such as Girdin described in this work. The optimal fit of this noncovalent intermolecular interaction leading to binding of the metal complex to the oncogenic protein target depends on the structural and electronic properties of the pocket and the molecular geometry of the metal complex and coordinating ligands. Therefore, it is not surprising that, with the right combination of ligands, metal complexes with other molecular geometries may be as good as octahedral metal complexes in finding unique inhibitors of oncogenic proteins.
