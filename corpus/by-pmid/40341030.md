Correspondence on ‘Characteristics of second primary malignancies following bispecific antibodies therapy’ by Liang et al

Abstract

We commend Liang et al for shining a spotlight on second primary malignancies after bispecific antibody (BsAb) therapy, yet several clinical nuances deserve consideration. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS)-derived case-fatality rates likely overstate risk, prior genotoxic treatments confound causality, and pooling heterogeneous BsAb constructs may blur construct-specific signals. The 124-day median latency hints at misclassification, while sparse African data limit global relevance. Risk-adapted surveillance—grounded in clonal haematopoiesis, prior cytotoxic exposure and BsAb construct—may be more pragmatic than blanket monitoring.

We read with great interest the article by Xiaojie Liang et al entitled ‘Characteristics of second primary malignancies following bispecific antibodies therapy’. The authors should be congratulated for addressing an understudied yet clinically consequential safety issue as bispecific antibodies (BsAbs) transition from early-phase trials to routine practice. Nevertheless, we would like to raise several clinically oriented considerations that may refine the interpretation of the data and its translation to patient care.

First, the crude case-fatality rate (CFR ≈ 30 %) for BsAb-associated second primary malignancies (SPMs) vastly exceeds mortality reported in pivotal BsAb trials. Because Food and Drug Administration (FDA) Adverse Event Reporting System (FAERS) is a spontaneous, severity-weighted system, non-fatal SPMs are likely under-reported. It would be helpful if the authors (1) quantified the proportion of BsAb reports with missing outcomes, (2) explored Empirical Bayes shrinkage of extreme CFR values, and (3) contrasted FAERS-derived CFRs with rates from manufacturer-sponsored pharmacovigilance programs to contextualize potential inflation.

Second, most BsAb recipients have previously received alkylating chemotherapy, ionizing radiation, autologous transplantation or chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T therapy—each independently linked to therapy-related myeloid neoplasms. The multivariable model adjusts for age, sex, weight and geography, but FAERS contains medication history fields that could be mined (eg, prior melphalan, fludarabine, radiopharmaceuticals). A sensitivity analysis stratified by antecedent transplant or CAR-T exposure would clarify whether the observed fourfold increase in SPM odds is truly attributable to BsAbs rather than accumulated genotoxic insult.

Third, the analysis aggregates short-course CD19×CD3 bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs) with continuously dosed B-cell maturation antigen (BCMA)×CD3 antibodies and Fc-enhanced constructs such as amivantamab. Because half-life, off-tumor T-cell activation and cumulative cytokine exposure differ markedly, pooling may dilute target-specific risk signals. Restricting the primary analysis to T-cell engagers with similar exposure windows—or presenting construct-level forest plots—could yield more actionable pharmacovigilance signals.

Fourth, a median time-to-onset of 124 days is strikingly shorter than the expected latency for de novo therapy-related malignancies. Without histological review, some events may represent (1) late relapses of the index disease, (2) previously occult synchronous tumors, or (3) mis-codified complications (eg, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disease). Clarifying the proportion of cases with confirmatory histopathology or cytogenetics would strengthen the causal inference that BsAbs accelerate malignant clonal evolution.

Lastly, only 0.3% of BsAb-SPM reports originated from Africa, a continent with high human T-cell leukemia virus type 1 (HTLV-1) and Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) prevalence. Given the established synergy between chronic viral infection and immune dysregulation, the global generalizability of the findings is uncertain. Collaboration with WHO pharmacovigilance centers to capture under-represented regions may uncover distinct viral-driven SPM patterns.

While the manuscript rightly alerts clinicians to the possibility of SPMs, the recommended “uniform intensive surveillance” during the first treatment year requires careful balancing against resource constraints and patient anxiety. In our view, risk-adapted strategies—integrating baseline clonal haematopoiesis screening, prior cytotoxic exposure and specific BsAb construct—would be more pragmatic than a blanket approach.

We appreciate the opportunity to comment on this important work and hope these observations will encourage further prospective, mechanistic studies that can more precisely delineate BsAb-related carcinogenic risk.
