# Clinical and Translational Results from PORTER, a Multicohort Phase I Platform Trial of Combination Immunotherapy in Metastatic Castration-Resistant Prostate Cancer.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Current immune checkpoint therapies offer limited benefits for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. Novel combinations may enhance immunotherapy efficacy.

**PATIENTS AND METHODS**: We conducted an open-label, noncomparative platform trial (NCT03835533) in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer to assess nivolumab-based combinations. The cohorts were as follows: (A) bempegaldesleukin 0.006 mg/kg and nivolumab 360 mg i.v. every 3 weeks; (B) stereotactic body radiotherapy 30 to 50 Gy, CDX-301 75 μg/kg s.c. for 5 days, poly-ICLC 1 mg intramuscularly weekly twice for 3 weeks, and nivolumab 480 mg every 4 weeks; and (C) CDX-301 75 μg/kg for 10 days, INO-5151 3 mg intramuscularly on lead-in day 8, day 1 of cycles 1 to 3, and then every 12 weeks, and nivolumab 480 mg every 4 weeks. The primary endpoint was safety; secondary endpoints included composite response rate (radiographic, PSA, or circulating tumor cell responses), 6-month disease control rate, progression-free survival, and overall survival. Serial blood and tissue samples were analyzed for pharmacodynamics and association with disease control.

**RESULTS**: A total of 43 patients were enrolled (n = 14, 15, and 14 in cohorts A, B, and C, respectively). Grade 3 to 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 10 (71%), 2 (13%), and 2 (14%) patients, respectively, with one grade 5 treatment-related adverse event in cohort A. Composite response rates were 7% (1/14), 33% (5/15), and 7% (1/14). Across cohorts, 6-month disease control was associated with preexisting memory/regulatory T cells, TNFα, and other inflammatory pathways.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Cohort B, which combined radiotherapy with CDX-301, poly-ICLC, and nivolumab, demonstrated encouraging clinical activity. Preexisting rather than treatment-induced immune activation was associated with clinical benefit across cohorts, highlighting the importance of baseline immune fitness.

Translational Relevance

This study provides critical insights into how different combination therapies, including radiation and immune-activating agents, can potentially enhance the efficacy of immune checkpoint therapies in treating metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. The results show the importance of preexisting immune activation, especially robust memory T-cell and regulatory T-cell populations in response to combination immunotherapy for prostate cancer. The identification of key biomarkers, such as TNFα pathway activation and reduced tumor proliferation in patient specimens, could inform the selection of patients most likely to respond to these therapies. These results point toward a future in cancer medicine in which personalized immunotherapy regimens, incorporating both immune-modulating agents and radiotherapy, are tailored based on the immune landscape and tumor biology of individual patients. Such approaches could lead to more effective treatments and improved outcomes for patients with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer by leveraging strategies that modulate the immune microenvironment and enhance antitumor responses.

Introduction

Prostate cancer is the most diagnosed cancer in American men, accounting for 29% of new cancer cases. Although the initial prognosis is generally favorable, approximately one third of patients progress to metastatic disease, which significantly lowers the 5-year survival rate.

Despite approvals of various therapies for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC)—including androgen receptor (AR) signaling pathway inhibitors, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, radiopharmaceuticals, and a cancer vaccine—treatment outcomes remain suboptimal. Immunotherapies have revolutionized the treatment of certain cancers, such as metastatic melanoma and non–small cell lung cancer, but they have shown limited benefit in advanced prostate cancer. Specifically, immune checkpoint therapies (ICT) targeting PD-1, PD-L1, and CTLA-4 have demonstrated limited efficacy in mCRPC.

One major reason for the limited success of immunotherapies in prostate cancer is its characterization as an “immunologically cold” tumor. This is attributed to factors such as low tumor mutational burden, scarcity of T cells within the tumor microenvironment (TME), weak NK cell activity, and the presence of immunosuppressive myeloid cells. Therefore, combination therapies that target these immune evasion mechanisms may enhance the effectiveness of immunotherapies.

In this study, we report the clinical and translational results of a phase I, multicohort, multicenter, open-label platform trial for patients with mCRPC who had progressed on prior secondary AR signaling pathway inhibitor therapy. Each cohort combination was based on complementary mechanisms to enhance antitumor immune responses. Cohort A was designed to activate the immune system by expanding the number of effector T and NK cells using bempegaldesleukin (PEGylated IL-2–conjugated cytokine prodrug) while overcoming adaptive resistance with nivolumab (anti–PD-1 mAb). Cohort B sought to induce immunogenic cell death through stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT), mobilize and activate dendritic cells (DC) using CDX-301 (Flt3 ligand) and poly-ICLC (Toll-like receptor 3 agonist), and restore T-cell effector function with nivolumab. Cohort C aimed to initiate an antitumor T-cell response with INO-5151 [DNA-based therapy expressing prostate-specific membrane antigen (PMSA), PSA, and IL-12], mobilize and activate DCs with CDX-301, and stimulate antitumor CD8+ T cells with nivolumab.

Patients and Methods

Study design

In this phase I nonrandomized, open-label, multicenter platform study (PORTER), males ≥18 years old with mCRPC, defined as castrate-level testosterone <50 ng/dL, were enrolled from five academic hospitals in the United States. Patients must have previously progressed on prior secondary AR signaling inhibitor therapy, with progression defined as follows: PSA ≥1 ng/mL and increasing over two consecutive measurements, soft tissue progression per RECIST version 1.1, or bone disease progression per Prostate Cancer Working Group 3 (PCWG3) criteria. Patients may have received prior chemotherapy for mCRPC and could have measurable or nonmeasurable disease per PCWG3 criteria. Additional eligibility criteria included adequate organ and hematologic function and Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status of 0 or 1. Patients enrolled in cohort B must have had ≥1 lesion amenable to SBRT. At least seven patients per cohort were required to have a fresh tissue biopsy at screening from a nonbone metastatic lesion; all other patients were required to provide an archived formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor specimen. Exclusion criteria included having any known untreated brain metastases; active, known, or suspected autoimmune disease; any condition requiring systemic treatment with either corticosteroids (>10 mg daily prednisone equivalent) or other immunosuppressive medications within 28 days of study treatment initiation; anticancer chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immunotherapy, or investigational agents within 4 weeks of study treatment initiation; and prior anti–PD-L1 therapy. Cohort-specific exclusion criteria included prior IL-2 therapy (cohort A) and prior cancer vaccine therapy (cohort C).

Each cohort consisted of two stages: stage 1 to evaluate safety, biomarkers, and clinical activity and stage 2, an expansion cohort, when warranted based on initial results. Patients were assigned to a cohort by the investigator using their best clinical discretion and the enrollment status of each cohort. Supplementary Table S1 presents data on the representativeness of our study population.

The protocol and all amendments were approved by a central Institutional Review Board and were accepted at all participating sites. The study was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and the International Conference on Harmonization Good Clinical Practice guidelines. All patients provided written informed consent before any study procedures were performed. The study was registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT03835533).

Procedures

Cohort A

Patients received bempegaldesleukin 0.006 mg/kg and nivolumab 360 mg i.v. every 3 weeks for up to 2 years. Patients were assessed radiographically at weeks 9, 18, and 27 (±1 week) and every 12 weeks (±1 week) thereafter until death, radiographic disease progression, or initiation of subsequent therapy.

Cohort B

SBRT was delivered to up to four sites of metastases in 3 to 5 fractions with a total dose of 30 to 50 Gy, starting on cycle 1, day 1 or 2. CDX-301 was administered at 75 μg/kg s.c. once daily for 5 days (days 1–5 of cycle 1). Poly-ICLC was administered at 1 mg intramuscularly on days 1, 5, 8, 12, 15, and 19 of cycle 1. Nivolumab was administered at 480 mg i.v. every 4 weeks for up to 2 years. Patients were assessed radiographically at weeks 8, 16, and 24 (±1 week) and every 12 weeks (±1 week) thereafter until death, radiographic disease progression, or initiation of subsequent therapy.

Cohort C

CDX-301 was administered at 75 μg/kg s.c. once daily on immune-priming lead-in days 1 to 5 and 22 to 26 (10 total doses). INO-5151 was administered at 3 mg intramuscularly, followed by electroporation using the CELLECTRA 2000 device (INOVIO Pharmaceuticals) on immune-priming lead-in day 8; day 1 of cycles 1, 2, and 3; and every 12 weeks thereafter for up to 2 years. Nivolumab was administered at 480 mg i.v. every 4 weeks for up to 2 years. Patients were assessed radiographically at weeks 8, 16, and 24 (±1 week) and every 12 weeks (±1 week) thereafter until death, radiographic disease progression, or initiation of subsequent therapy.

All patients were followed for survival for up to 2.5 years from the initiation of the study intervention. Adverse events were graded using the NCI Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events version 5.0, and terms were coded using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities version 25.0.

Blood samples for isolation of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were shipped overnight and processed at a central location (Infinity BiologiX) over a Ficoll gradient and cryopreserved. Serum was processed within 2 hours of collection at each site and frozen immediately at −80°C and then batch shipped to a central biorepository. Archival tumor samples were accepted as either FFPE tissue blocks or sectioned FFPE tissues on unstained glass slides. Fresh tumor biopsies were immediately snap frozen or formalin-fixed and paraffin-embedded. On-treatment tumor biopsies were collected during cycle 2 (preferably days 2–10), if medically feasible. Due to the limited sample size, results from on-treatment biopsies are not presented.

Outcomes

The primary endpoint was the incidence and severity of treatment-related adverse events (TRAE). Secondary endpoints were the composite response rate (CRR), 6-month disease control rate (DCR), radiographic progression-free survival (rPFS), and overall survival (OS). Exploratory endpoints included the association of tumor and immune biomarkers with clinical outcomes, including the 6-month DCR.

Circulating tumor cells

Blood samples for circulating tumor cell (CTC) analysis were collected on day 1 of each cycle (including immune-priming lead-in day 1 for cohort C), prior to the administration of the study intervention, and collected in a 10 mL CellSave Preservative Tube. The sample was shipped the same day at ambient temperature to Menarini Silicon Biosystems, Inc. for analysis. CTC counts were enumerated using the CELLSEARCH Circulating Tumor Cell assay (Menarini Silicon Biosystems, Inc.) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Samples that were clotted, <7.5 mL in blood volume, or received more than 96 hours after collection were not processed.

Statistical analysis of clinical data

Safety and efficacy analyses were performed on patients who received at least one dose of any component of the combination study intervention.

CTC response: change in CTC from ≥5 cells/7.5 mL of blood at baseline to ≤4 cells/7.5 mL of blood at any postbaseline time point. Patients with a missing baseline CTC value or baseline CTC <5 cells/7.5 mL of blood were not evaluated for this criterion.

Confirmed PSA response: ≥50% reduction in PSA from baseline, with a repeat measurement confirming the results at least 3 weeks later.

Best overall response of complete response (CR) or partial response (PR), as determined by PCWG3-modified RECIST version 1.1. Confirmation of response by a repeat tumor assessment was required.

CRR is a composite endpoint in which response was defined as a patient meeting at least one of the following criteria:

Six-month DCR was defined as the proportion of patients with a best overall CR, PR, or stable disease lasting at least 6 months. The duration of stable disease was defined as the time from treatment initiation until the date of radiographic progression per RECIST or PCWG3 criteria. Confidence intervals (CI) for CRR and DCR were estimated using the Clopper–Pearson method.

OS was defined as the time from treatment initiation until death due to any cause. rPFS was defined as the time from treatment initiation to the first evidence of radiographic progression per RECIST or PCWG3 criteria or death due to any cause, whichever occurred first. OS and rPFS were estimated using Kaplan–Meier techniques.

A Simon two-stage minimax design was implemented for each cohort. The null hypothesis assumed CRR = 10%, and the alternative hypothesis assumed CRR = 25%. In stage 1, 15 participants were enrolled. If the safety profile was deemed acceptable by the study investigators and data monitoring committee and there were ≥2 responses, 15 additional patients could be enrolled in stage 2 for a total of 30 patients. If ≥6 responses were observed in these 30 patients, then the combination would be considered worthy of further investigation. This design yields a 0.07 type I error and 77% power. Under the null hypothesis, the probability of terminating a cohort after stage 1 is 55%.

This study was not intended or powered for hypothesis testing or comparison between cohorts, and no adjustment for multiple comparisons was performed for the clinical endpoints. Statistical analyses of clinical data were performed using R version 4.1.1 (RRID: SCR_001905).

Mass cytometry by time-of-flight immunophenotyping

A broad immunophenotyping panel was used on cryopreserved PBMC by cytometry by time-of-flight (CyTOF) analysis run under uniform protocols at Primity Bio in a blinded fashion. The reagents (Supplementary Table S2), methods, and gating strategy (Supplementary Fig. S1) used established methods.

Serum proteomics profiling

Serum proteins were quantified using Olink multiplex Proximity Extension Assay panels (Olink Proteomics) according to the manufacturer’s instructions and as previously described. The assay was performed at the Olink Analysis Service Center. The final assay readout was reported as normalized protein expression (NPX) values, an arbitrary unit on a log2 scale, in which an increase of one NPX unit corresponds to an approximate doubling of protein concentration. Two Olink panels (Target 96 Immuno-Oncology and Target 96 Immune Response) were used, totaling 172 unique analytes.

Whole-exome and whole-transcriptome sequencing

Paired FFPE tumor and normal PBMC samples were profiled using ImmunoID NeXT (Personalis, Inc.), an augmented exome/transcriptome platform and analysis pipeline. Whole-exome library preparation and sequencing were performed as previously described. DNA extracted from tumor and PBMCs was used to generate whole-exome capture libraries using the KAPA HyperPrep Kit and Agilent’s SureSelect Target Enrichment Kit, according to manufacturers’ recommendations, with the following amendments: (i) Target probes were used to enhance coverage of biomedically and clinically relevant genes, (ii) protocols were modified to yield an average library insert length of approximately 250 bp, and (iii) KAPA HiFi DNA Polymerase (Kapa Biosystems) was used in place of Herculase II DNA polymerase (Agilent). Paired-end sequencing was performed on NovaSeq instrumentation (Illumina).

Whole-transcriptome sequencing results were aligned using STAR (RRID: SCR_004463; ref.). The ImmunoID NeXT DNA and RNA Analysis Pipeline aligned reads to the hs37d5 reference genome build and performed alignment, duplicate removal, and base quality score recalibration using best practices outlined by the Broad Institute. The pipeline used Picard (RRID: SCR_006525) to remove duplicates and GATK (RRID: SCR_001876) to improve sequence alignment and correct base quality scores.

Analysis of biomarker data

To identify differences between patients with and without 6-month disease control, biomarker data from all three cohorts were pooled and analyzed using R version 4.3.2. Outliers were identified and removed through a manual review of the first two principal components.

Pharmacodynamics

Changes from baseline (cycle 1, day 1 for cohorts A and B; lead-in day 1 for cohort C) in serum proteomics were analyzed using two-sided paired t tests, with significance defined as a Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted P value <0.05 and absolute NPX difference ≥1. Changes in CyTOF data (expressed as a percentage of leukocytes) were analyzed similarly, with significance defined as a Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted P value <0.05 and log2 fold change ≥1. For CyTOF analyses, only higher-level populations (e.g., CD4+ and CD8+ T cells) were included, whereas specific subsets (e.g., central and effector memory T cells) were excluded.

Association with disease control

Fisher’s exact test was used to evaluate the relationship between CTC levels (detectable vs. undetectable) and disease control. Differential analysis of serum proteomics and CyTOF data (expressed as a percentage of parent population) was conducted using two-sided t tests with an unadjusted significance threshold of P < 0.05. Differential gene expression between tumors from patients with and without disease control was assessed using DESeq2 version 1.42.0 (RRID: SCR_000154; ref.), with significance defined as a Benjamini–Hochberg-adjusted P value <0.05. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was performed on DESeq2 results using the fgsea R package (version 1.28.0; bioRxiv 060012v3; RRID: SCR_020938) with gene sets from Hallmark (RRID: SCR_016863) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (RRID: SCR_018145) pathways, provided by msigdbr version 7.5.1 (RRID: SCR_022870).

Data availability

Summary datasets, including limited deidentified clinical and processed RNA sequencing, CyTOF, and proteomics data, are available at https://github.com/ParkerICI/porter-trial-data. The raw data generated in this study are not publicly available due to contractual agreements with commercial partners but are available upon reasonable request from the corresponding author. A data transfer agreement, detailing any potential restrictions on data use, may be required.

Results

Patient characteristics

Between July 29, 2019, and October 11, 2021, 43 patients were enrolled and dosed across three cohorts (n = 14, 15, and 14 for cohorts A, B, and C, respectively; Fig. 1). All patients were included in safety and efficacy analyses.

Patient demographics and baseline disease characteristics were generally balanced between cohorts (Table 1). All three cohorts enrolled heavily pretreated patients, with approximately 50% having measurable disease.

At the time of data cutoff (November 16, 2022), all patients had discontinued treatment and completed study follow-up. The median duration of follow-up was 11.1 months (IQR, 10.0–12.2) in cohort A, 26.3 months (17.8–30.0) in cohort B, and 17.4 months [16.6–not estimable (NE)] in cohort C. Patients were treated for a median of 1.4 months (0.7–1.8) in cohort A, 1.9 months (1.0–7.4) in cohort B, and 2.0 months (0.9–4.6) in cohort C (Supplementary Tables S3–S5).

Safety

The primary endpoint for this study was safety, defined as the incidence and severity of TRAEs. TRAEs were observed in most patients: 100% in cohort A, 87% in cohort B, and 64% in cohort C. Grade 3 or 4 TRAEs occurred in 10 (71%) patients in cohort A, 2 (13%) patients in cohort B, and 2 (14%) patients in cohort C. The most common TRAEs were pyrexia (43%) and maculopapular rash (43%) in cohort A, diarrhea (27%) in cohort B, and fatigue (29%) in cohort C (Table 2).

There was one death in each cohort due to an adverse event (AE). In cohort A, one patient died from acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) after being found unresponsive during the bempegaldesleukin infusion on cycle 1, day 1, prior to receiving nivolumab; this AE was assessed by the investigator as related to bempegaldesleukin. The deaths in cohorts B and C occurred after the patients discontinued study treatment and were deemed unrelated to the study intervention (COVID-19 in cohort B and respiratory failure of unknown etiology in cohort C). Three (21%) patients in cohort A and one (7%) in cohort C discontinued all study drugs due to an AE: grade 3 rash, grade 3 acute kidney injury, and grade 5 ARDS in cohort A and grade 3 peripheral motor neuropathy in cohort C. Six (43%) patients in cohort A, four (27%) in cohort B, and one (7%) in cohort C required systemic corticosteroids for the management of an AE.

Pharmacodynamic effects

Unbiased analyses revealed expected pharmacodynamic effects. In all cohorts, nivolumab treatment increased levels of soluble PD-1 and multiple proteins associated with IFN-γ (Fig. 2A). However, qualitative differences were observed between cohorts. Notably, only cohort A showed increases in proteins associated with cytotoxic activity, including granzymes, co-stimulatory molecules, activation markers, and inhibitory receptors. In contrast, cohorts B and C demonstrated increases in IL-12 and markers of DC maturation, antigen presentation, and innate immune responses, such as CD83, LAMP3, and RIGI. Mass cytometry revealed increases in multiple CD4+, CD8+, and T-cell receptor γδ T-cell subsets across all cohorts, with the most pronounced changes observed in cohort A (Fig. 2B). Additionally, cohorts B and C exhibited increases in monocyte, DC, and NK cell subsets. In all cohorts, these pharmacodynamic changes were transient, with most protein and cell populations returning to baseline levels by cycle 2, day 1.

Clinical activity

Secondary endpoints included CRR, 6-month DCR, rPFS, and OS. CRR was defined as meeting at least one of the following criteria: confirmed CR or PR, confirmed PSA response (≥50% reduction from baseline), or CTC response (change from ≥5 cells/7.5 mL blood at baseline to ≤4 at any subsequent time). This study was not designed for direct comparison between cohorts.

For cohort A, the CRR was 7.1% (1/14) with one CTC response (decrease from 15 to 0 cells/7.5 mL blood; Fig. 3; Supplementary Table S6). No PSA or radiographic responses were observed (Supplementary Fig. S2). The 6-month DCR was 14.3% (2/14). The median rPFS was 2.8 months (95% CI, 2.0–7.3), and median OS was not reached (95% CI, 9.0–NE).

For cohort B, the CRR was 33.3% (5/15), consisting of one PR, one CTC response (decrease from 6 to 3 cells/7.5 mL blood), and three confirmed PSA responses (decreases of 99.4%, 87.5%, and 77.6%). No patient achieved more than one CRR criterion. The 6-month DCR was 33.3% (5/15), median rPFS was 5.6 months (95% CI, 3.5–16.6), and median OS was 20.6 months (95% CI, 5.7–27.5).

For cohort C, the CRR was 7.1% (1/14) with one patient having a confirmed PSA response (73.2% decrease) in addition to a PR. No CTC responses were observed. The 6-month DCR was 14.3% (2/14), median rPFS was 5.4 months (95% CI, 2.0–NE), and median OS was 17.3 months (95% CI, 3.5–NE).

Clinical and molecular features associated with disease control

Due to limited clinical activity within each cohort, data were pooled across all three cohorts to identify clinical and molecular features associated with disease control ≥6 months. This approach aimed to identify potential predictors of clinical benefit to ICT-based combinations.

Baseline characteristics were generally similar between patients who achieved 6-month disease control and those who did not. Patients with disease control tended to be older and had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status of 0, fewer metastatic bone lesions, lower baseline serum lactate dehydrogenase levels, and received fewer lines of prior systemic therapy (Supplementary Tables S6 and S7). Importantly, patients with disease control were more likely to have undetectable CTC levels at baseline [5/7 (71%) vs. 5/30 (17%), P = 0.009].

Whole-exome sequencing of tumors from 22 patients showed that all had a low tumor mutational burden (<3 mutations per megabase) and were microsatellite low or stable. Genomic alterations were generally balanced between those with and without disease control (Supplementary Table S7).

RNA sequencing of archival and pretreatment fresh tissue biopsies from 23 patients identified differences in gene expression between patients with (n = 8) and without (n = 15) disease control (Supplementary Table S8). Four genes (IFI6, EEF1A2, BST2, and ISG15) had higher expression in tumors from patients without disease control, whereas one gene, TCHH, had higher expression in tumors from patients with disease control. GSEA revealed that pathways related to TNFα signaling via NF-κB were more active in tumors from patients achieving disease control. These pathways are known to enhance immune cell activation, promote antigen presentation, and increase intratumoral cytotoxic T-cell infiltration (Fig. 4A; Supplementary Table S9; refs.). Additionally, pathways involved in cell-cycle regulation and metabolism showed differences, suggesting that changes in cell growth and energy use might negatively influence treatment outcomes.

We also analyzed pretreatment serum proteomics to identify differences between patients with (n = 9) and without (n = 32) disease control. Three proteins (DCBLD2, TNFA, and LAMP3) were significantly higher in patients with disease control, whereas four proteins (KRT19, CKAP4, MILR1, and PRDX3) were significantly lower (Fig. 4B). DCBLD2 and LAMP3 are involved in immune modulation and activation and may enhance antitumor responses. Higher TNFA levels in patients with disease control suggest a more active basal immune response. Conversely, lower levels of KRT19, CKAP4, MILR1 (Allergin-1), and PRDX3, which are associated with tumor aggressiveness, proliferation, and survival, suggest that tumors in patients with disease control were less aggressive.

Mass cytometry analysis of pretreatment blood samples from 8 patients with disease control and 33 without disease control identified several differentially expressed immune cell populations (Fig. 4C). Patients with disease control exhibited significantly higher levels of CD127+ CD8+ central memory T cells, CD127+ CD4+ Th cells, and CD39+ CD4+ T cells, including regulatory T cells. These populations suggest a more active and regulated immune environment, potentially contributing to better immunotherapy outcomes. Notably, CD39+ T cells may also represent terminally exhausted tumor-specific T cells, in which elevated levels could indicate that nivolumab reinvigorated these exhausted T cells, contributing to improved disease control. Additionally, higher levels of FcER1+ type 2 conventional DCs (cDC2, defined as CD1c+CD141−) were observed, indicating an activated DC profile. Conversely, patients without disease control had higher levels of CD64+ type 1 conventional DCs (cDC1, defined as CD1c+CD141+), CD38+ and CCR2+ CD8+ effector memory T cells, CD69+ CD8+ T cells, and CD40L+ memory T cells. This pattern reflects a more activated peripheral immune phenotype, characterized by higher proportions of effector memory relative to central memory populations. The elevated CD38 expression, a marker of both activation and exhaustion, may indicate an immune state that is active but not effectively targeting the tumor. This suggests that these patients might lack a significant preexisting tumor-specific immune response, potentially explaining their reduced likelihood of benefiting from immunotherapy combination treatment.

Discussion

The PORTER study leveraged a platform design with similar eligibility criteria and endpoints across all cohorts, allowing for the rapid signal finding of multiple immunotherapy combinations in patients with mCRPC. The cohorts were designed to potentiate immune responses through cytokine therapy (cohort A), in situ vaccination with radiation (cohort B), and exogenous vaccination (cohort C), all coupled with an anti–PD-1 backbone. We used an identical suite of translational assays and analysis methods across all cohorts, which minimized potential biases and enabled a comprehensive pooled analysis. This increased the sample size and provided deeper insights into biomarkers associated with disease control.

Our key efficacy endpoint was a composite of ORR, PSA response, and CTC response. This composite endpoint was designed to identify patients showing clinical benefit across several measures, each with its own limitations. For example, patients with nonmeasurable disease (36%–57% of patients across cohorts) cannot achieve a PR per PCWG3-modified RECIST, and the CTC response criterion used in our study could only be assessed in patients with baseline CTC levels ≥5 cells per 7.5 mL of blood (50%–62% of patients across cohorts). As demonstrated by our data, these endpoints showed weak associations with each other, and PSA decrease was the only component that could be applied across all patients. Although this composite endpoint is not suitable for pivotal studies, it allowed for the integration of multiple clinical factors in determining beneficial outcomes and may warrant further refinement in future studies. However, due to the lack of correlation among the CRR components, we utilized a 6-month disease control as the clinically meaningful endpoint for the correlative analyses. The 6-month DCR of 20.9% observed in this study is consistent with prior studies testing ICTs in unselected populations with mCRPC, which report DCRs ranging from 20.2% to 32.4%.

We observed the expected pharmacodynamic effects for each individual agent, and our unbiased analyses revealed qualitative differences in immune response profiles between cohorts. These differences reflect the distinct immune pathways targeted by each cohort: Cohort A was characterized by enhanced cytotoxic and effector T- and NK-cell activation, whereas cohorts B and C showed signatures associated with increased antigen presentation and innate immune activation. Only modest differences were observed between cohorts B and C, potentially due to both regimens containing nivolumab and CDX-301, which may drive similar immune activation profiles. These observations suggest that although each combination initiated discrete immune responses, potential synergies may arise from the complementary activity of these pathways. However, further work is needed to better integrate these effects and design novel combinations that could harmonize with these immune responses to maximize antitumor efficacy.

Cohort A, which tested the combination of nivolumab and bempegaldesleukin, demonstrated limited clinical activity. This finding is consistent with results from a phase III trial for advanced melanoma, in which the combination failed to outperform nivolumab monotherapy. Additionally, 71% of patients in this cohort experienced a grade 3 or higher TRAE, including one death due to ARDS deemed related to bempegaldesleukin. Most patients in cohort A chose to discontinue treatment after being informed of this death, complicating the interpretation of clinical activity for this cohort.

Cohort B combined SBRT, CDX-301, poly-ICLC, and nivolumab in a well-tolerated regimen that showed promising clinical activity, with a 33% CRR. The observed effects likely resulted from synergistic interactions among the components, with SBRT enhancing immunogenicity through the release of tumor antigens, CDX-301 mobilizing DCs, and poly-ICLC maturing DCs to potentially boost antigen presentation and T-cell activation. However, the dual role of SBRT as a directly cytotoxic treatment and immune modulator made it challenging to distinguish the contributions of antitumor immunity from direct tumor killing. For example, in patient 13B, a PSA responder with three bone metastases, SBRT was delivered to a single lesion, whereas the other two lesions remained untreated but stable for 18 months, suggesting potential systemic effects. Patient 8B, another PSA responder, exhibited minor regression of untreated mediastinal lymph nodes though these later progressed, raising the possibility of abscopal responses. These anecdotal observations highlight the need for future studies incorporating molecular imaging and immune profiling to better assess the interplay between radiotherapy and immunotherapy, investigate potential abscopal effects, and optimize dosing strategies for maximal clinical benefit. Although cohort B met the protocol-specified criteria, the cohort was not expanded due to resource constraints.

Cohort C tested INO-5151, a DNA therapy expressing PSMA, PSA, and IL-12, with CDX-301 and nivolumab. Although this combination was safe, limited efficacy was observed. Previous phase III trials of cancer vaccines such as GVAX and PROSTVAC in mCRPC have also yielded negative results, underscoring the difficulty of achieving antitumor responses after vaccination in this setting. These findings suggest that even with the addition of adjuvants like CDX-301 and nivolumab, overcoming the immunosuppressive TME in mCRPC remains a significant challenge. In addition, many patients’ disease progressed during the 28-day immune-priming lead-in phase before initiating nivolumab treatment as evidenced by increases in CTC and PSA levels. Therefore, alternative dosing strategies and therapeutic combinations are required for cancer vaccines to potentially succeed in patients with advanced, rapidly progressing disease.

Across all three cohorts, patients who achieved 6-month disease control tended to have clinical characteristics known for their prognostic value, such as lower lactate dehydrogenase levels, fewer bone metastases, and undetectable CTC levels. This study provides further evidence supporting the prognostic impact of these parameters. Although these characteristics may reflect less aggressive tumor biology, a comprehensive exploration of immune correlatives also suggests that these patients have a more favorable baseline immune microenvironment for an immunotherapy-targeted approach.

Our comprehensive analyses integrating transcriptomics, proteomics, and mass cytometry data highlight that a robust pretreatment immune response is associated with disease control in patients with mCRPC treated with nivolumab-based immunotherapy combinations. Specifically, the elevated presence of cDC2 cells, CD127+ memory T cells, and CD39+ regulatory T cells, indicative of a preactivated immune environment with elements of T-cell exhaustion, suggests a favorable response to immunotherapy. Enhanced TNFα signaling and effective antigen presentation (e.g., LAMP3) further underscore the importance of a preactivated immune environment.

Additionally, lower proliferation and metabolic activity in both the tumor and periphery were associated with better disease control, suggesting that less aggressive tumors may be more susceptible to immune-mediated control. The reduced enrichment of E2F and Myc target pathways in the tumors of patients who achieved disease control underscores the importance of lower proliferative and oncogenic signaling in mediating a favorable response to therapy. TNFα pathway activation, associated with better outcomes in both GSEA and serum proteomics analysis, could be linked to proinflammatory cell death.

Despite mass cytometry data suggesting patients who achieved disease control had a pretreatment immune-active environment, including elevated levels of terminally exhausted tumor-specific T cells, the negative enrichment of IFN signaling pathways from the GSEA is not surprising. It should be noted that mass cytometry assessed peripheral immune cells, whereas GSEA reflects tumor-intrinsic gene expression. Patients achieving disease control may exhibit immune exhaustion in peripheral immune cells, whereas their tumors downregulate IFN signaling as an evasion mechanism. This phenomenon, highlighted in recent studies, supports the rationale for combining JAK inhibitors with anti–PD-1 therapy.

This study contributes to the growing body of evidence that a multifaceted immune response and specific tumor characteristics are essential for achieving disease control with immunotherapy combinations in mCRPC. Overall, our findings underscore the importance of examining the TME and peripheral immune landscape to understand the immune and tumor characteristics associated with disease control in the context of nivolumab-based immunotherapy combinations for mCRPC. Although these results provide valuable insights, further studies are needed to determine whether these findings extend to other immunotherapy strategies. Such efforts will help refine our understanding of immune mechanisms and inform the development of more effective treatments, ultimately improving outcomes for patients with mCRPC.
