# Complex regulatory network of programmed death-ligand 1 in cancer: Cancer immunomodulation from molecular mechanisms to clinical applications.

## Abstract

Immune checkpoint therapy targeting PD-L1/PD-1 represents a key cancer treatment strategy. PD-L1 binds PD-1 on T cells, suppressing activation and enabling tumor immune escape. Although the blockade of the PD-L1/PD-1 pathway has demonstrated significant anti-tumor effects, the overall response rate remains suboptimal in solid tumors. Therefore, understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying PD-L1 deregulation is essential for enhancing the efficacy of PD-L1/PD-1 blockade therapies. As our comprehension of the regulatory mechanisms governing the PD-L1/PD-1 pathway continues to evolve, ongoing exploration of new immunotherapy targets remains critical. This article provides a comprehensive review of recent research concerning the regulation of PD-L1 expression. It encompasses aspects such as its structural characteristics, genomic alterations and rearrangements, epigenetic modifications, inflammatory signaling pathways, oncogenic signaling pathways, microRNA regulation, and post-translational modifications. Additionally, this article illustrates notable advancements and limitations regarding the application of PD-L1 as an immunotherapeutic target in current clinical settings.

Main text

T cells play a crucial role in maintaining the body’s internal environment by selectively recognizing and eliminating pathogens and aberrant cells, such as tumor cells. They achieve this recognition through the binding of the T cell receptor (TCR) to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules on the surface of target cells. This recognition is further refined by co-stimulatory and co-inhibitory receptors and their respective ligands, including CTLA-4/B7, PD-1/PD-L1, TIM-3/galectin-9, and LAG-3/MHC class II. These immune checkpoints help maintain immune homeostasis by regulating T cell activity. Precise checkpoint control is critical, as dysregulated T cell activation can disrupt immune homeostasis, leading to autoimmune diseases. Thus, immune checkpoints play a critical role in preserving the balance of immune responses and preventing autoimmunity.

Immune checkpoints, particularly PD-1/PD-L1, have emerged as prominent targets in the treatment of various malignant tumors, with numerous clinical trials exploring therapies that target this pathway. While anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies have significantly improved prognosis for many patients, durable responses remain limited, affecting only a subset of individuals. High PD-L1 expression is observed in numerous cancers, including breast, non-small cell lung (NSCLC), renal cell carcinoma, colorectal, gastric, papillary thyroid, and testicular cancers, and is often associated with poor patient outcomes. Beyond tumor cells, PD-L1 is also expressed on various host cells, such as dendritic cells, macrophages, fibroblasts, and T cells, within the tumor microenvironment (TME) and lymph nodes. The interaction of PD-L1 with PD-1 on T cells leads to the inhibition of the TCR pathway, which impairs T cell activation and contributes to T cell exhaustion and tumor immune escape. The level of PD-L1 expression in both tumor cells and the TME is closely linked to clinical responses to treatment, highlighting its role in shaping therapeutic outcomes across different cancers.

Structure and function of programmed death-ligand 1

PD-L1, a type I transmembrane protein belonging to the immunoglobulin (Ig) superfamily, comprises an extracellular region with Ig-V and Ig-C-like domains, a transmembrane domain, and a cytoplasmic tail lacking intrinsic signaling motifs. The interaction between PD-L1’s extracellular domain and PD-1 on T cells induces the phosphorylation of PD-1’s cytoplasmic immunoreceptor tyrosine-based inhibition motif (ITIM) and immunoreceptor tyrosine-based switch motif (ITSM) via Src family kinases. This phosphorylation recruits tyrosine phosphatases SHP-1 and SHP-2, which dephosphorylate key components of the TCR signaling cascade, thereby suppressing T cell activation, proliferation, cytokine production, and effector functions. In addition to PD-1, PD-L1 binds CD80 on T cells or antigen-presenting cells (APCs), delivering inhibitory signals that further dampen T cell responses. Beyond T cell regulation, PD-L1 modulates immune activity through non-T cell-mediated mechanisms. For instance, transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) upregulates PD-L1 expression in tumor cells and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) via the AKT/NF-κB or AKT/β-catenin pathways. β-catenin activation not only promotes PD-L1 expression but also drives macrophage polarization toward a TAM phenotype, facilitating tumor progression. Anti-PD-L1 therapy enhances macrophage function by upregulating pro-inflammatory M1 markers (e.g., iNOS, MHC-II, and CD40) and augmenting phagocytic activity, thereby improving tumor clearance. Paradoxically, PD-L1 also exerts tumor-protective effects: PD-L1/PD-1 interaction inhibits Fas-mediated apoptosis in cancer cells, promoting survival, PD-L1 can act independently of PD-1 to shield tumors from type I/II interferon responses and cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) attacks, potentially through interactions with alternative ligands or intrinsic signaling pathways.

Programmed death-ligand 1 expression via epigenetic regulation

Epigenetic modifications play a crucial role in regulating PD-L1 expression. Research has identified promoter the methylation of PD-L1 across various cancers. Specifically, the hypermethylation of the PD-L1 promoter has been associated with reduced PD-L1 levels, which can lead to resistance to PD-1 therapies in NSCLC. Conversely, in acute myeloid leukemia (AML), the use of DNA methyltransferase inhibitors has been shown to significantly increase PD-L1 expression. Additionally, PD-L1 promoter methylation has been closely linked to the recurrence of prostate cancer and the prognosis of patients with colon cancer. In neurogliomas, higher levels of PD-L1 promoter methylation have been observed in patients with IDH mutations.

Histone modifications also play a critical role in regulating PD-L1 expression. The histone methyltransferase MLL1, for instance, enriches the PD-L1 promoter with H3K4 trimethylation (H3K4me3), thereby inducing PD-L1 expression in pancreatic cancer. Silencing MLL1 significantly reduces H3K4me3 levels at the PD-L1 promoter. Additionally, the histone acetylation-binding protein BET enhances PD-L1 mRNA production by binding to the CD274 promoter region. Inhibition of histone deacetylases (HDACs) can increase PD-L1 production, while the suppression of the specific histone deacetylase HDAC6 reduces PD-L1 levels. Research has also shown that the methyltransferase EZH2 represses PD-L1 expression by promoting H3K27 trimethylation, whereas the PARP1 protein increases PD-L1 expression by inhibiting EZH2. Furthermore, the MMK1 protein can directly bind to the CD274 promoter, activate H3K4me3, and thus promote PD-L1 expression.

Genomic alteration and rearrangement regulate programmed death-ligand 1 expression

CD274, the gene encoding PD-L1, is located on chromosome 9p24.1 and can undergo chromosomal rearrangements, including amplification and translocation. JAK2, also located on chromosome 9p, is involved in inflammatory pathways, and its amplification can enhance PD-L1 production by increasing IFN-γ signaling (Figure 1). In patients with primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma, 20% have shown gene displacement and 29% have experienced gene amplification. Gene-trapping technology has identified that PD-L1 genes lacking the 3′ UTR are prevalent in cells with high PD-L1 expression, highlighting its role in regulating PD-L1 production. Knockdown of the 3′ UTR region of PD-L1 in a subset of patients with cancer has led to increased PD-L1 expression across various tumors. CRISPR-Cas9-mediated knockout of the 3′ UTR region stabilizes PD-L1 mRNA expression. Furthermore, patients with ovarian cancer with 3′ UTR deletions in PD-L1 have shown positive responses to PD-1 blockade therapy. Thus, detecting chromosomal rearrangements in the PD-L1 gene may serve as a useful marker for predicting responses to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade therapies.

Programmed death-ligand 1 expression and oncogenic signaling pathways

Oncogenic signaling pathways play a crucial role not only in the initiation and progression of cancers but also in the regulation of PD-L1 production, which facilitates immune evasion by cancer cells. This regulation primarily involves transcriptional regulators, signaling pathway effectors, and upstream receptors. Several transcriptional regulatory factors have been identified as directly influencing PD-L1 expression. For example, hypoxia can activate the expression of downstream genes through hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs). Both HIF-1α and HIF-2α contribute to PD-L1 transcriptional regulation by binding directly to the hypoxia response element (HRE) in the PD-L1 promoter. The transcription factor STAT3, which is modulated by various growth factors and inflammatory signals, is also involved in the regulation of PD-L1. STAT3 directly influences PD-L1 transcription. NF-κB, activated by oncogene mutations and inflammatory signaling pathways, binds to the PD-L1 promoter through its RelA subunit, demonstrating its direct role in regulating PD-L1 production. Inhibition of NF-κB significantly reduces PD-L1 levels (Figure 1). Additionally, the dimeric transcription factor AP-1 regulates PD-L1 transcription by binding to the intron region of the PD-L1 gene. CDK5 modulates PD-L1 transcription indirectly by altering the balance between IRF2 and IRF1. Specifically, CDK5 facilitates the release of IRF2’s inhibition on IRF1 and enhances PD-L1 transcription through IRF1 by post-transcriptionally modifying the co-inhibitory molecule IRF2BP2, which destabilizes IRF2.

The ALK oncogenic signaling pathway often leads to gene translocation and amplification. The NPM-ALK fusion protein enhances PD-L1 production by activating the MEK-ERK and STAT3 signaling pathways in lymphoma. Similarly, the EML4-ALK fusion promotes PD-L1 expression through the activation of downstream ERK, AKT, STAT3, and HIF-1α pathways in NSCLC. The adenosine A1 receptor (ADORA1) inhibits PD-L1 transcriptional regulation through ATF3, a transcription factor activated by the cAMP/CREB axis in NSCLC cells. Additionally, KRAS mutations can increase PD-L1 expression by activating the MEK/ERK signaling pathway (Figure 2). In BRAF mutant melanoma cells, JUN, a downstream molecule of STAT3 and MAPK pathways, promotes PD-L1 production. MEK inhibitors not only significantly block JUN and STAT3 activation but also inhibit TLR ligand-mediated PD-L1 expression. Additionally, the MAPK pathway protein p38 is involved in regulating PD-L1. EGFR mutations enhance PD-L1 expression by activating downstream ERK, mTOR, and STAT3 signaling pathways.

miRNAs regulate PD-L1 expression through both direct and indirect mechanisms. For instance, miR-513 directly binds to the 3′ UTR of PD-L1 mRNA, thereby restraining its expression. This regulation is dynamically influenced by IFN-γ in a miR-513-dependent manner. In NSCLC, the tumor suppressor p53 inhibits PD-L1 expression through miR-34a. Additionally, miR-197 indirectly reduces PD-L1 production by inhibiting the CKS1B-STAT3 signaling pathway in NSCLC cells. Conversely, miR-20 indirectly enhances PD-L1 production by preventing the expression of PTEN in colon cancer.

Post-translational regulations of programmed death-ligand 1

SPOP, an adaptor protein of the Cullin3 E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, mediates PD-L1 ubiquitination and degradation. CDK4 indirectly modulates PD-L1 expression via SPOP phosphorylation. The β-TrCP E3 ligase targets PD-L1 for proteasomal degradation by catalyzing K48-linked ubiquitination at its S176 residue. Members of the CMTM family, particularly CMTM4 and CMTM6, stabilize PD-L1 through direct binding: CMTM6 impedes lysosomal degradation by colocalizing with PD-L1 in recycling endosomes and plasma membranes, prolongs its half-life, and suppresses ubiquitination. In breast cancer, GSK3β interacts with non-glycosylated PD-L1 to promote its degradation. Conversely, the NF-κB pathway enhances PD-L1 stability through CSN5-mediated deubiquitination. Glycosylation critically regulates PD-L1 stability. The extracellular domain of PD-L1 contains glycosylation sites that protect against degradation; mutations at these sites amplify anti-tumor immunity. FKBP51 ensures the proper folding of glycosylated PD-L1 in the endoplasmic reticulum, thereby stabilizing it. The IL-6/JAK1 axis promotes PD-L1 glycosylation via STT3-dependent glycan addition, while EGFR signaling enhances PD-L1/PD-1 interaction through B3GNT3-mediated glycosylation. Cytoplasmic PD-L1 degradation involves GSK3β-mediated phosphorylation and subsequent recruitment of E3 ubiquitin ligases. Epigenetic regulation intersects with immune modulation: IFNγ induces STAT1-TET2 binding, driving TET2-mediated hydroxymethylation of chemokine and PD-L1 genes. Systemic ascorbic acid potentiates TET activity, elevating chemokine expression and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), which synergizes with anti-PD-L1 therapy to improve survival in tumor-bearing mice. This suggests an IFNγ-JAK-STAT-TET axis may govern tumor responses to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. Therapeutic strategies targeting PD-L1 post-translational modifications include D-mannose, which activates AMPK to phosphorylate PD-L1 at S195, triggering aberrant glycosylation and proteasomal degradation. Combined with PD-1 blockade, D-mannose suppresses triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) progression and prolongs survival in murine models. Similarly, CDK5-mediated phosphorylation of PD-L1 at T290 recruits HSC70 for chaperone-mediated autophagic degradation, enhancing anti-PD-1 efficacy and survival in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)-bearing mice.

Inflammation-related signaling pathways participated in the regulation of programmed death-ligand 1

IFN-γ, a pro-inflammatory cytokine secreted primarily by activated T cells and NK cells, regulates PD-L1 production by binding to its receptor and activating the downstream JAK-STAT signaling pathway. STAT1-mediated transcriptional regulators play a central role in modulating PD-L1 expression. Additionally, mutations in JAK1/2 have been identified in patients with cancer who do not respond to PD-1 blockade therapy, suggesting that these tumors may evade immune detection through mechanisms other than the PD-L1 pathway.

In addition to IFN-γ, type I interferons (IFN-α and IFN-β) regulate PD-L1 expression. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) induces type I interferon expression via the TLR4-NF-κB pathway, influencing PD-L1 modulation. In melanoma, inhibiting the NF-κB pathway partially blocked IFN-γ-mediated PD-L1 expression, suggesting that NF-κB does not directly regulate PD-L1 and indicating cross-talk between the two signaling pathways. The role of TGF-β in PD-L1 regulation is context-dependent: while TGF-β suppresses PD-L1 production in vitro in cultured monocytes, it promotes PD-L1 production in an in vivo model of pancreatic islet transplantation. Thus, the inflammatory pathways activated by different cytokines can have varying effects on PD-L1 regulation across different diseases.

Application of programmed death-ligand 1 inhibitors in cancer therapy

Recent clinical studies have demonstrated that blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis can enhance overall survival in patients with solid tumors. Consequently, the FDA has approved several checkpoint inhibitors for various cancers. Atezolizumab, an anti-PD-L1 antibody granted FDA approval in 2016, selectively targets PD-L1 and inhibits its binding to the receptor PD-1. By blocking PD-L1–PD-1 interaction, ATE enhances T cell-mediated antitumor immunity, leading to clinical benefits in urothelial carcinoma, NSCLC, and TNBC. Additionally, it affects PD-L1+ TNBC cells through a cytotoxic reaction mediated by T cells that induces apoptosis. Furthermore, ATE also inhibits cellular invasion and migration by down-regulating focal adhesion kinase (FAK) phosphorylation, demonstrating antitumor activity independent of T cell involvement. FAK plays a pivotal role as a regulator of cellular invasion and migration. Research has revealed a positive correlation between the expression levels of PD-L1 and FAK mRNA in PD-L1+ TNBC cells. Notably, ATE markedly reduces the phosphorylation state of FAK, thereby enhancing the inhibitory effect of FAK inhibitors on the motility and invasiveness of PD-L1+ TNBC cells. Another agent, Nivolumab, restores T cell antitumor activity by disrupting the interaction between the PD-1 receptor and its ligands, thereby inhibiting cellular immune responses. Notably, Nivolumab’s efficacy is not solely dependent on the level of PD-L1 expression within tumor tissues, thus, it is suitable for patients with NSCLC exhibiting varying levels of PD-L1 expression. In 2014, Nivolumab received FDA approval for melanoma treatment. Subsequently, in 2015, it was approved for squamous lung cancer as well as metastatic NSCLC. Durvalumab, a highly selective and affinity-enhanced human IgG1 monoclonal antibody, specifically targets PD-L1. By binding to PD-L1, it effectively disrupts its interaction with PD-1 and CD80 on activated T cells. In clinical trials involving patients with limited-stage small cell lung cancer, adjuvant therapy with durvalumab demonstrated a significant improvement in overall survival and progression-free survival compared to placebo, while maintaining a comparable incidence of adverse events to the placebo group. Avelumab binds PD-L1 on tumors, blocking PD-1/PD-L1-CD80 interactions, enhancing T cell antitumor activity. In patients with advanced urothelial carcinoma who have achieved disease-free status following first-line (1L) platinum-based chemotherapy, the use of avelumab as a 1L maintenance therapy in conjunction with best supportive care (BSC) has significantly prolonged their overall survival and progression-free survival compared to those receiving only BSC. Avelumab is now standard 1L maintenance therapy for advanced urothelial carcinoma. Dosing and adjustment strategies for PD-L1 inhibitors vary by drug. Nivolumab is usually 3 mg/kg every 2 weeks, cautious with Ipilimumab. Pembrolizumab is 2 mg/kg every three weeks. Each has unique dose adjustments for immune-related adverse reactions, e.g., permanent discontinuation for pembrolizumab and nivolumab in severe cases, vs. atezolizumab needing treatment interruption and corticosteroids. Dosing also considers patient factors such as weight, liver/kidney function, and age, with medical advice crucial. Patient responses should be closely monitored. Scientists have enhanced PD-L1 inhibitors' efficacy. Fitzgerald AA et al. found inhibiting dipeptidylpeptidase with BXCL701 boosts anti-PD-1 therapy in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma by increasing immune cells. Success hinges on CD8+ T and NK cells forming antitumor immune memory. A sialic acid-modified liposome (DOX-SAL) loaded with doxorubicin, combined with anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody, nearly eliminates B16F10 tumors and inhibits 4T1 tumors, showing significant therapeutic potential. Furthermore, research has shown that IFNγ prompts STAT1 to bind to TET2, recruiting it to hydroxymethylate chemokine and PD-L1 genes. Systemic injection of TET cofactor ascorbic acid/vitamin C boosts TET activity, increasing chemokines and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, thereby enhancing antitumor immunity and the efficacy of anti-PD-L1 treatment. Moreover, deleting FcγRIIB on CD8+ T cells has been shown to enhance the efficacy of PD-1 inhibitors, potentially due to the presence of tumor-specific memory T cells with high stemness properties within the tumor microenvironment.

A meta-analysis has indicated that immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) combination therapies exhibit better survival benefits compared to chemotherapy, with the optimal ICI combination therapy varying depending on PD-L1 expression levels. For patients with advanced NSCLC, the combination of pembrolizumab and chemotherapy provides the best overall survival benefit, while the combination of atezolizumab, bevacizumab, and chemotherapy offers the best progression-free survival benefit. Another meta-analysis indicated that for patients with advanced esophageal squamous cell carcinoma undergoing first-line treatment, the combinations of toripalimab with chemotherapy and sintilimab with chemotherapy were ranked as the regimens providing the highest overall survival and progression-free survival benefits, respectively.

Identifying accurate biomarkers is crucial for ICI therapy, enhancing efficacy and reducing side effects. PD-L1 IHC testing, which measures tumor proportion score, is FDA-approved for pembrolizumab in advanced NSCLC. A TPS ≥1% indicates PD-L1 expression, with ≥50% considered high. In the KEYNOTE-001 trial, patients with TPS >50% had a higher response rate to pembrolizumab than all patients.Tumor mutational burden (TMB) has emerged as a surrogate indicator of tumor immunogenicity. In 2020, based on the results of the KEYNOTE-158 clinical trial, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved TMB as a companion diagnostic biomarker for pembrolizumab. In this trial, patients with one of the ten types of advanced solid tumors who were treated with pembrolizumab and had at least ten mutations per megabase (i.e., high TMB) had a higher response rate than those with low TMB. Notably, in this study, TMB was a predictor of progression-free survival, rather than overall survival, which supported its approval by the FDA. Microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) stands out as a pan-cancer biomarker for response to ICI, demonstrating robust performance across multiple cancer types. This may be attributed to the fact that this molecular phenotype serves as a mechanistic driver of ICI response. MSI-H, such as TMB, is affected by neoantigens from microsatellite instability, host recognition, and immune response. MSI-H tumors contain many “passenger” mutations. Thus, counting microsatellites alone is not enough; mutation types (via mutation spectra) improve biomarker prediction.

It is crucial to acknowledge that PD-L1 inhibitors can elicit a range of side effects. The most commonly reported treatment-related adverse events associated with Pembrolizumab include fatigue, diarrhea, rash, and pruritus. In patients receiving treatment solely for cervical cancer, rashes and fever were observed. Additionally, some patients experienced immune-mediated adverse events (AEs), such as colitis, Guillain-Barre syndrome, hyperthyroidism, and hypothyroidism. Atezolizumab is associated with elevated liver enzymes (ALT, AST), hepatitis biomarkers, and lipase levels. Severe complications include sepsis, cardiac arrest, pneumonia, septic shock, liver failure, and fulminant hepatitis. The most prevalent treatment-related AEs linked to Nivolumab encompass fatigue, rash, anemia, along with endocrine disorders such as hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism, as well as type 1 diabetes mellitus. An article published detailed a case involving myxedema crisis induced by Opdivo in a patient diagnosed with metastatic lung squamous cell carcinoma during their course of treatment. For diarrhea/colitis after PD-1 inhibitors, if lactoferrin is negative and no infection, manage with a low-fiber diet, anti-diarrheals such as loperamide, and possibly mesalazine/cholestyramine. Immunosuppressants are crucial but may cause severe side effects such as adrenal insufficiency, osteoporosis, and increased infection risk. Research has shown that the early administration of high-dose glucocorticoids during anti-PD-1 therapy can impair the antitumor efficacy of PD-1 inhibitors. Therefore, the use of corticosteroids should be individually adjusted after a comprehensive assessment of the patient’s responsiveness and tolerance. The lowest effective dose of corticosteroids should be used for the shortest possible duration. Elevated intestinal TNF expression in checkpoint inhibitor-induced colitis supports the rationale for TNF blockers as targeted therapy. Studies have demonstrated that TNF blockers effectively alleviate the colitis symptoms of these patients by neutralizing TNF-α. Vedolizumab (VDZ) is an intestinal-selective α4β7 integrin antibody that inhibits the migration of T cells to inflamed gastrointestinal tissues by blocking their binding to mucosal addressin cell adhesion molecule-1 (MAdCAM-1). VDZ has been approved for widespread use in the long-term treatment of inflammatory bowel diseases. A study demonstrated that VDZ induced clinical remission in 86%, endoscopic remission in 54%, and histological remission in 29% of patients with corticosteroid/IFX-refractory. Furthermore, anti-IL-6 receptor monoclonal antibodies, namely tocilizumab and sarilumab, have demonstrated efficacy in treating refractory high-level colitis induced by PD-1 inhibitors without compromising their therapeutic efficacy. However, further research is needed to confirm the role of IL-6 blockers in gastrointestinal toxicity and to establish the optimal dosage for effective control. For the management of the most common dermatological adverse reactions, including lichenoid reactions and granulomatous skin reactions, immunosuppressants are the primary treatment for lichenoid dermatitis and cutaneous granulomas. The choice of dosage form or route of administration varies according to the severity of the condition. Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS) and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) are severe skin reactions linked to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies, needing urgent recognition and treatment. Blocking PD-1/PD-L1 disrupts T cell balance in skin/mucosa, causing autoimmunity and inflammation. For patients with Grade III and IV Stevens-Johnson Syndrome (SJS)/toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN), the first step in management is to discontinue immunotherapy and initiate treatment with high-dose systemic corticosteroids (such as methylprednisolone or prednisone). Once re-epithelialization has occurred following the initiation of immunotherapy, steroid tapering therapy should be initiated. For steroid-refractory skin rashes, intravenous immunoglobulin.

The tumor immune microenvironment not only fosters tumor growth but also constitutes the tumor’s surrounding environment, playing a pivotal role in resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors. The human body’s antitumor response hinges on CD8+ CTLs recognizing tumor antigens presented on HLA class I molecules, which are composed of β2-microglobulin (β2M) and heavy chains. Immunotherapy has the potential to mutate β2M, and heterozygous loss of β2M has been associated with decreased survival rates, suggesting that the loss of functional β2M may serve as a mechanism of resistance to PD-1 therapy. Tumors treated with PD-1/PD-L1 blockers can develop resistance by boosting CD38 levels. Simultaneous targeting of CD38 and PD-L1 boosts antitumor immunity, indicating CD38 as a key mechanism of acquired resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. TGF-β-induced stromal cells create a barrier around tumor cells, hindering infiltration and T cell migration, thereby causing acquired resistance. Persistent antigen exposure induces T cell hyporesponsiveness, marked by decreased cytokine production and sustained inhibitory receptor expression, resulting in T cell exhaustion and tumor resistance. A key mechanism of tumor immune tolerance is CD8+ T cell exhaustion. Persistent tumor antigens drive the epigenetic reprogramming of effector cells, rendering them unresponsive and promoting immune desensitization. Tumor cells usually display antigen peptides via MHC class I. APCs recognize these peptides, engulf them, and present them to T cells via their MHC molecules. This mechanism allows tumor cells to evade T cell recognition by eliminating immunogenic subpopulations. (Figure 3)

The clinical application of PD-1/PD-L1 ICIs has revolutionized cancer treatment, demonstrating substantial potential in therapeutic outcomes. While PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy has markedly enhanced patient survival and prognosis, the development of PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies encounters challenges such as resistance after prolonged use, the need to improve drug efficacy, and the quest for optimal combination therapies. Furthermore, PD-1/PD-L1 blockade therapy exhibits low response rates in certain cancers, lacks effective biomarkers, and is constrained by immune toxicity and resistance. Future research endeavors may concentrate on unraveling new mechanisms of PD-1/PD-L1 in cancer treatment, along with their connections to tumor metabolism, oxidation, and DNA repair. Given the intricate immune microenvironment and tumor heterogeneity, personalized immunotherapy stands as a key priority. As research progresses, we anticipate a comprehensive understanding of the role and resistance mechanisms of the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway in immunity and immunotherapy. This insight will pave the way for innovative strategies in combination immunotherapy for solid tumors, thereby advancing personalized treatment and benefiting a broader patient population.

In conclusion, programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and its cognate ligand, programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), are pivotal immune checkpoint molecules that play a fundamental role in modulating T cell functionality, maintaining immune homeostasis, and facilitating tumor immune evasion. In this article, we examine the multifaceted roles of PD-L1 in cancer immune regulation through the lenses of its structural and functional properties, epigenetic regulation, genomic alterations, post-translational modifications, associated carcinogenic signaling pathways, and the application of PD-L1 inhibitors in cancer treatment. As our understanding of the regulatory mechanisms governing the PD-L1/PD-1 pathway continues to evolve, ongoing exploration for new immunotherapy targets remains essential. The utilization of PD-L1 inhibitors—either alone or in combination with other therapeutic modalities such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and targeted therapy—has introduced novel treatment strategies for various advanced or refractory tumors. However, addressing issues related to resistance development and adverse reactions presents an urgent challenge that must be confronted.

Limitations of the study

This study highlights three critical limitations: First, the multidimensional mechanisms underlying therapeutic resistance remain incompletely characterized. Second, current evidence predominantly focuses on monotherapy efficacy; the optimal combination regimens for different tumor types still need to be explored in depth. Finally, there is a lack of systematic data on the management strategies of long-term immune-related adverse events and their impact on the quality of life, the clinical information needs to be further evaluated.
