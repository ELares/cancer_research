# EMB-01, a Tetravalent Bispecific Antibody, Inducing Co-Degradation of EGFR and c-Met for Enhanced Anti-Tumor Efficacy.

## Abstract

The bispecific antibody EMB-01 (bafisontamab), constructed using EpimAb's proprietary FIT-Ig platform, is designed to simultaneously target epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and receptor tyrosine kinase Met (c-Met). Here, we characterize EMB-01's binding properties, mechanisms of action, and anti-tumor efficacy using in vitro cell line models and in vivo models (patient-derived xenografts, PDXs). EMB-01 exhibits high-affinity binding to EGFR and c-Met, induces co-degradation of both receptors, enhances endocytosis, and elicits strong antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) and complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) effects. Mechanistically, EMB-01 effectively inhibits ligand-induced receptor phosphorylation, downstream AKT activation, and IL-8 secretion. Furthermore, EMB-01 demonstrates potent anti-tumor activity across multiple tumor models, outperforming existing EGFR-targeted therapies and other bispecific antibodies, while maintaining favorable pharmacokinetics with good tolerability in cynomolgus monkeys. These findings support the clinical development of EMB-01 as a promising therapeutic for EGFR/c-Met-driven cancers.

Introduction

Epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) signaling plays a crucial role in cancer development. Consequently, interrupting EGFR signaling, either by blocking its extracellular ligand‐binding sites or inhibiting its intracellular tyrosine kinase activity, has become an effective strategy for treating EGFR‐expressing tumors. Currently, EGFR‐targeted therapies have shown favorable outcomes in various malignancies, including colorectal and lung cancers. However, most patients eventually develop resistance to EGFR inhibitors, posing significant therapeutic challenges. Acquired resistance is multifactorial, and compensatory activation of the adjacent c‐Met receptor has emerged as a key mechanism. Crosstalk between EGFR and c‐Met allows tumors to bypass EGFR inhibition, thereby sustaining proliferative signaling. As a result, dual targeting of EGFR and c‐Met has gained increasing attention as a rational therapeutic approach.

EMB‐01 (bafisontamab) is a bispecific antibody developed using EpimAb's proprietary FIT‐Ig platform to simultaneously target EGFR and c‐Met on tumor cells. In each EMB‐01 structure, anti‐EGFR and anti‐c‐Met Fab domains are fused directly in tandem in a unique crisscross orientation with a wild‐type human IgG1 Fc, without introducing mutations or using peptide linkers. This design results in a tetravalent molecule capable of simultaneous and high‐avidity binding to both receptors on the tumor cell surface. The anti‐tumor activities of EMB‐01 and its underlying mechanisms of action were evaluated through a series of in vitro and in vivo experiments.

Materials and Methods

Generation of EMB‐01

EMB‐01 was constructed and generated as previously reported. In brief, the light chain (VL‐CL) of anti‐c‐Met Fab domain was directly fused to the N terminus of the heavy chain (VH‐CH1‐CH2‐CH3) of anti‐EGFR domain by overlapping polymerase chain reactions, and another 2 chains, VH‐CH1 of anti‐c‐Met and VL‐CL of anti‐EGFR, were also constructed. All 3 chains were subcloned into mammalian expression vectors and were co‐transfected in HEK293 cells, followed by expression and Protein A purification. The concentration of purified FIT‐Ig proteins was measured by A280; homogeneity was analyzed by SDS‐PAGE and SEC‐HPLC.

Affinity Measurements by Biacore

Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) assays were performed on a Biacore T200 instrument (GE Healthcare, USA) to determine the binding kinetics of EMB‐01 to recombinant human EGFR and c‐Met proteins. A goat anti‐human IgG Fc polyclonal antibody (Cat GWB‐20A705, GenWay Biotech) was immobilized onto a CM5 sensor chip. EMB‐01 or control monoclonal antibodies were injected at a flow rate of 5 μL/min. Association (k_on, M−1 s−1) and dissociation (k_off, s−1) rate constants were determined using serial antigen concentrations at 30 μL/min, and the equilibrium dissociation constant (K_D) was calculated as K_D = k_off/k_on.

Simultaneous Binding Assessment by OctetRed System

An OctetRed system (Sartorius) was used to evaluate EMB‐01's ability to simultaneously bind EGFR and c‐Met. EMB‐01 was immobilized onto an AR2G biosensor at 10 μg/mL. Sequential binding assays were performed with soluble EGFR and c‐Met (300 nM or 100 nM) in assay buffer (PBS, pH 7.4, containing 0.1% BSA and 0.02% Tween 20). Binding events were monitored, and the sensor was regenerated using 10 mM glycine (pH 1.5).

Flow Cytometry Analysis

For binding assays, NCI‐H292 (ATCC, Cat CRL‐1848) and EBC‐1 (JCRB, Cat JCRB0820) cells were blocked with 2% FBS and incubated with serial dilutions of EMB‐01, anti‐EGFR mAb, anti‐c‐Met mAb, or a negative control antibody. After primary incubation, Alexa Fluor 488‐conjugated mouse anti‐human IgG (100 μg/mL) was used for detection. All incubations were performed on ice. Mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) was measured using a BD FACVerse flow cytometer.

For tetravalent binding validation, NCI‐H1975 cells (ATCC, Cat CRL‐5908) were divided into groups pretreated with either anti‐EGFR mAb, anti‐c‐Met mAb, or left untreated, followed by EMB‐01 incubation and detection with biotinylated soluble EGFR or c‐Met proteins and Streptavidin Alexa Fluor 488.

Western Blot Analysis for EGFR and c‐Met Degradation

NCI‐H1975 and NCI‐H292 cells were treated overnight at 37°C with 100 nM of EMB‐01, anti‐EGFR mAb, anti‐c‐Met mAb, a combination of anti‐EGFR and anti‐c‐MetmAbs, or a control IgG1. Cell lysates were prepared using Cell Signaling Technology lysis buffer (Cat 9803S). Proteins were separated by SDS‐PAGE, transferred to PVDF membranes, and probed with primary antibodies against EGFR (CST, Cat 2232), c‐Met (CST, Cat 4560), or GAPDH (CST, Cat 571) at a 1:1000 dilution. HRP‐conjugated secondary antibody (CST, Cat 7074) was used at a 1:2000 dilution. Detection was performed with enhanced chemiluminescence.

Confocal Microscopy for Endocytosis

NCI‐H441 cells (ATCC, Cat HTB‐174) were stained with CellTrace CFSE (Invitrogen, Cat C34554, 1:2000 dilution) for plasma membranes and Hoechst 33,342 (Thermo Fisher, Cat 62,249, 1 μg/mL) for nuclei. Cells were incubated with R‐phycoerythrin‐labeled EMB‐01 or control anti‐EGFR mAb (200 nM) at 37°C for 15 min. Fluorescence images were acquired using a ZEISS Cell Observer SD confocal microscope.

Antibody‐Dependent Cellular Cytotoxicity (ADCC) and Complement‐Dependent Cytotoxicity (CDC) Assays

HCC827 cells (ATCC, Cat CRL‐2868) were incubated with serial dilutions of EMB‐01 or a control antibody for 30 min at 37°C. Cells were co‐cultured with NK92/CD16a effector cells (Huabo Biopharm) for 6 h at 37°C. Cytotoxicity was measured using the CytoTox‐ONE Homogeneous Membrane Integrity Assay (Promega, Cat G7891), following the manufacturer's instructions.

HCC827 cells were incubated with serial dilutions of EMB‐01 or control antibody, followed by the addition of normal human serum complement (Quidel Corporation, Cat A113). After overnight incubation at 37°C, cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter‐Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay (Promega, Cat G7573).

 AKT Phosphorylation and IL‐8 Secretion Assays

NCI‐H292 cells were serum‐starved overnight in RPMI‐1640 medium containing 0.1% FBS. Cells were treated with serial dilutions of antibodies for 30 min, followed by stimulation with 40 ng/mL hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) for 5 min. Phospho‐AKT (Ser473) levels were measured using the AKT Phospho‐Ser473 Kit (Cisbio, Cat 64AKSPEG).

Serum‐starved NCI‐H292 cells were treated with 200 ng/mL HGF and serial dilutions of antibodies for 48 h at 37°C. Supernatants were collected and analyzed using the Human IL‐8 ELISA Kit (Invitrogen, Cat KHC0081).

 GLP Toxicity Study and Pharmacokinetics (PK) Studies

Good Laboratory Practice (GLP)‐compliant toxicity and pharmacokinetic (PK) studies were conducted at Covance Pharmaceutical R&D (Shanghai) Co. Ltd. Cynomolgus monkeys were administered weekly intravenous infusions of EMB‐01 at doses of 16, 60, or 160 mg/kg for 5 weeks, followed by a 4‐week recovery period. Toxicity was assessed via clinical observation, hematology, clinical chemistry, and histopathology.

For PK analysis, a single intravenous infusion of EMB‐01 was administered at 4, 16, or 40 mg/kg. Serum concentrations were measured at designated time points using a validated immunoassay based on c‐Met capture and HRP‐labeled human IgG detection.

Patient‐Derived Xenograft (PDX) Models

Colorectal cancer PDX models used in this study were established based on our previously preserved PDX tissues, which were derived from patients with written informed consent for their tumor tissues to be used in future scientific research. All procedures were in accordance with the guidelines of animal experiments. Female NOD‐SCID mice aged 6–8 weeks were purchased from Zhejiang Viton Lihua Experimental Animal Co. Ltd. and the methods of PDX model establishment were according to our previous report. When tumors reached an average volume of approximately 150 mm, mice were randomized into four groups (n = 5–6 per group). Treatment regimens were as follows: first group, Cetuximab (5 mg/kg, intraperitoneal injection, twice weekly); second group, Amivantamab (10 mg/kg, intraperitoneal injection, once weekly); third group, EMB‐01 (16 mg/kg, intraperitoneal injection, once weekly); fourth group, mock with 0.9% NaCl. The molecular weight of EMB‐01 (~240KD) is approximately 1.6 times that of Amivantamab (~150KD) and Cetuximab (~150KD), which justifies the 1.6‐fold higher dosing of EMB‐01 at 16 mg/kg compared to Amivantamab at 10 mg/kg. EMB‐01, Cetuximab, Amivantamab were diluted in 0.9% NaCl. Treatment duration was 4 weeks. Tumor volumes were measured 2 or 3 times per week with calipers according to the first to the fourth group and calculated using the formula V = ½ × (length × width2). Mice were euthanized when tumor volumes exceeded 2000 mm3 or at the experimental endpoint. All procedures were conducted in accordance with institutional animal care and use guidelines of the author's institute.

Statistical Analysis

Data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Statistical comparisons were performed using two‐tailed unpaired t‐tests or one‐way ANOVA with Tukey's post hoc test for multiple comparisons. A p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 8.0 (GraphPad Software).

Results

Schematic Diagram of EMB‐01

EMB‐01 is a tetravalent bispecific antibody targeting epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and receptor tyrosine kinase Met (c‐Met), constructed using EpimAb's proprietary FIT‐Ig platform (Figure 1). The FIT‐Ig design consists of three constructs: (1) a fusion of the light chain (VL‐CL) of the parental anti‐c‐Met monoclonal antibody (mAb) with the heavy chain (VH‐CH1‐CH2‐CH3) of the anti‐EGFR mAb; (2) the heavy chain (VH‐CH1) of the parental anti‐c‐Met mAb; and (3) the light chain (VL‐CL) of the parental anti‐EGFR mAb. These constructs were co‐transfected into mammalian cells to express the final EMB‐01 molecule. The purity of EMB‐01 was characterized by SDS‐PAGE and SEC‐HPLC, as shown in Figure S1.

 EMB‐01 Binds to Soluble EGFR and c‐Met Antigens Individually or Simultaneously

The binding affinities of EMB‐01 to soluble EGFR and c‐Met antigens were assessed using surface plasmon resonance (SPR) and Octet Red assays. EMB‐01 demonstrated high‐affinity binding to EGFR, with a KD of 0.534 nM, and to c‐Met, with a KD of 1.06 nM, which were comparable to its parental monoclonal antibodies (KD = 0.605 nM for anti‐EGFR mAb and 1.27 nM for anti‐c‐Met mAb) (Figure 2a).

Furthermore, EMB‐01 exhibited simultaneous dual‐antigen binding capacity, as demonstrated by sequential binding assays using the Octet Red platform (Figure 2b). Stoichiometry analysis showed that EMB‐01 could bind approximately 1.84 c‐Met molecules followed by 1.94 EGFR molecules per antibody molecule, confirming its tetravalent binding capability, (Figure S2).

 EMB‐01 Binds to Cell Surface EGFR and c‐Met

To evaluate cell surface binding, two cell lines with differential EGFR and c‐Met expression profiles were utilized: NCI‐H292 (EGFRhigh/c‐Metlow) and EBC‐1 (c‐Methigh/EGFRlow), Table S3. Flow cytometry analysis revealed that EMB‐01 bound effectively to both cell lines, with binding curves similar to those of the parental antibody targeting the highly expressed receptor in each cell line. In NCI‐H292 cells, EMB‐01 exhibited binding similar to the anti‐EGFR monoclonal antibody, consistent with high EGFR expression. In contrast, in EBC‐1 cells, EMB‐01 mirrored the binding pattern of the anti‐c‐Met antibody, in line with predominant c‐Met expression (Figure 3a).

The tetravalent binding capability of EMB‐01 to cell membrane receptors was evaluated using NCI‐H1975 cells. As depicted in Figure 3b, the experimental design utilized distinct receptor‐blocking conditions to verify selective and simultaneous binding by EMB‐01. Under the first condition, cells were pretreated with anti‐EGFR monoclonal antibody to block EGFR, thereby directing EMB‐01 binding predominantly to the available c‐Met sites on the cell surface. Consequently, the subsequent addition of biotinylated EGFR bound to the free EGFR binding sites on EMB‐01, enabling detection via streptavidin‐488. Conversely, under the second condition, pretreatment with anti‐c‐Met monoclonal antibody selectively blocked c‐Met, allowing EMB‐01 to bind exclusively to the available EGFR sites on the cell surface, leaving c‐Met binding sites on EMB‐01 accessible for biotinylated c‐Met binding and subsequent detection.

In the untreated control condition, EMB‐01 was expected to simultaneously bind both EGFR and c‐Met receptors, resulting in minimal unoccupied binding sites for detection. This experimental setup was designed to verify that all four binding sites of EMB‐01 could be simultaneously occupied by both targets.

The experimental results confirmed this hypothesis, as shown in Figure 3c. The bio‐c‐Met signal was increased in anti‐c‐MET‐pretreated cells, while the bio‐EGFR signal was notably elevated in anti‐EGFR‐pretreated cells, both demonstrating a clear dose‐dependent response. In untreated cells, both bio‐c‐Met and bio‐EGFR detection signals remained low, consistent with near‐complete simultaneous receptor occupancy by EMB‐01. As shown in Figure 3d, EMB‐01 also exhibited dose‐dependent binding to untreated, anti‐EGFR‐pretreated, and anti‐c‐Met‐pretreated NCI‐H1975 cells. Collectively, these data demonstrate that EMB‐01 can simultaneously engage EGFR and c‐Met through its tetravalent binding structure.

 EMB‐01 Induces Co‐Degradation of EGFR and c‐Met and Enhances Receptor Endocytosis

Western blot analysis revealed that treatment with EMB‐01 led to substantial degradation of both EGFR and c‐Met proteins in NCI‐H1975 and NCI‐H292 cells (Figure 4a). Compared with control IgG1 or treatment with individual anti‐EGFR or anti‐c‐Met monoclonal antibodies, EMB‐01 induced a more pronounced reduction in the levels of both receptors. Notably, even the combination of anti‐EGFR and anti‐c‐Met monoclonal antibodies failed to achieve the degree of receptor degradation observed with EMB‐01, highlighting its unique co‐degradation capability.

Confocal microscopy analysis further demonstrated that EMB‐01 triggered enhanced endocytosis in NCI‐H441 cells compared to anti‐EGFR monoclonal antibody (Figure 4b). Fluorescently labeled EMB‐01 accumulated rapidly inside cells, with visible internalization observed as early as 24 min and progressively increasing at 32, 39, and 46 min. In contrast, anti‐EGFR mAb showed minimal intracellular accumulation over the same time course. These findings indicate that EMB‐01 facilitates faster and more efficient receptor internalization than conventional anti‐EGFR monoclonal antibodies.

 EMB‐01 Exhibits Robust ADCC and CDC Activities

EMB‐01 induced a potent, dose‐dependent antibody‐dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) response when co‐cultured with NK92/CD16a effector cells and HCC827 target cells (Figure 5a). The maximal ADCC response was observed at approximately 1 1 nM and above of EMB‐01. Similarly, EMB‐01 demonstrated dose‐dependent complement‐dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) in HCC827 cells (Figure 5b) whereas human IgG1 control induced negligible cytotoxic effects. These results highlight EMB‐01's dual capability to elicit both ADCC and CDC mechanisms, contributing to its overall anti‐tumor activity.

 EMB‐01 Inhibits Downstream AKT Phosphorylation and IL‐8 Secretion

EMB‐01 effectively inhibited HGF‐induced phosphorylation of AKT (Ser473) in NCI‐H292 cells (Figure 6a). EMB‐01 treatment reduced phospho‐AKT levels at higher concentrations (> 1 nM), while anti‐c‐Met mAb, anti‐EGFR mAb, or their combination achieved only modest inhibition. The human IgG1 control had minimal impact. These results indicate that EMB‐01 provides superior blockade of key oncogenic signaling compared to individual or combined monoclonal antibody treatments.

In parallel, EMB‐01 suppressed HGF‐induced IL‐8 secretion (Figure 6b). EMB‐01 dramatically reduced IL‐8 concentrations, outperforming the inhibitory effects observed with anti‐c‐Met mAb, anti‐EGFR mAb, or their combination. These results highlight EMB‐01's enhanced potency in modulating both intracellular signaling pathways and secreted pro‐inflammatory cytokines.

 EMB‐01 Shows Good Tolerability and Favorable Pharmacokinetics Profile in Cynomolgus Monkeys

The EGFR and c‐Met of cynomolgus monkeys share more than 95% amino acid sequence identity with corresponding human proteins, and EMB‐01 bound to monkey EGFR and c‐Met with comparable binding activity to human (Table S1). In a Good Laboratory Practice (GLP)‐compliant repeated dose toxicology study, EMB‐01 was administered to cynomolgus monkeys at doses of 16, 60 or 160 mg/kg.

The highest non‐severely toxic dose (HNSTD) was conservatively defined as 160 mg/kg since no EMB‐01 direct‐related life‐threatening toxicities or irreversible severe adverse findings were noted in animals. The pharmacokinetics (PK) of EMB‐01 were assessed through a single intravenous infusion of EMB‐01 at a dose of 4, 16, 40 mg/kg. Serum samples were collected over a 28‐day follow‐up period. EMB‐01 showed a half‐life ranging from 10 to 40 h and EMB‐01 exposure increased with dose in a slightly more than dose‐proportional manner (Figure S3 and Table S2).

 EMB‐01 Demonstrates Potent Efficacy in PDX Models

The anti‐tumor efficacy of EMB‐01 was evaluated in colorectal cancer patient‐derived xenograft (PDX) models CH07 and CH11. In both models, EMB‐01 significantly inhibited tumor growth compared to the mock‐treated control group (Figure 7a,b). In the CH07 model, compared to the mock group, EMB‐01 treatment led to a marked reduction in tumor volume with a tumor growth inhibition rate (TGI%) of 79.95% (p = 0.0169), maintaining tumor sizes below 500 mm3 throughout the 30‐day observation period. Tumor volumes in the EMB‐01 group were consistently lower than those in the Cetuximab and Amivantamab groups throughout the study period with TGI% of 73.1% and 63.24%, respectively. In the CH11 model, EMB‐01 also suppressed tumor growth effectively with a TGI% of 88.27% (p = 0.0002) compared with the mock group, with final tumor volumes remaining below 300 mm3 by the end of the experiment.

Additionally, EMB‐01 demonstrated slight superior efficacy over Amivantamab in patient‐derived organoid (PDO) assays, as indicated by lower IC₅₀ values in both CH07‐derived (8.887 μM vs. 15.05 μM) and CH11‐derived (5.296 μM vs. 7.281 μM) PDOs (Figure S4), further supporting information its enhanced anti‐tumor potential in EGFR/c‐Met–dependent colorectal cancers.

Discussion

In this study, we systematically evaluated the binding properties, mechanisms of action, and anti‐tumor efficacy of EMB‐01, a novel tetravalent bispecific antibody targeting EGFR and c‐Met. EMB‐01 demonstrated high‐affinity binding to both EGFR and c‐Met, simultaneously engaging both receptors at the cell surface. Notably, EMB‐01 induced co‐degradation of EGFR and c‐Met, enhanced endocytosis, and potently inhibited downstream signaling pathways, including AKT phosphorylation and IL‐8 secretion. In addition, EMB‐01 effectively triggered immune‐mediated effector functions, including antibody‐dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) and complement‐dependent cytotoxicity (CDC). These combined mechanistic effects translated into robust anti‐tumor activity across multiple in vitro and in vivo models, with favorable pharmacokinetics and tolerability in cynomolgus monkeys.

Dual inhibition of EGFR and c‐Met has long been recognized as a promising strategy to overcome resistance to EGFR‐targeted therapies. Previous bispecific antibodies, such as Amivantamab, have achieved clinical success by targeting both pathways. Compared to Amivantamab, EMB‐01 not only retains bispecific binding but also promotes co‐degradation of EGFR and c‐Met, a mechanism that may further enhance receptor downregulation and therapeutic efficacy. Our comparative analysis in colorectal PDO models revealed that EMB‐01 exhibited slightly superior anti‐tumor activity relative to Amivantamab. In PDX models from this study, as well as in a previously reported cMET exon 14 skipping lung cancer model, EMB‐01 demonstrated anti‐tumor activity comparable to Amivantamab.

The observed co‐degradation of EGFR and c‐Met is particularly noteworthy. While receptor internalization is a common phenomenon upon antibody binding, EMB‐01's ability to enhance simultaneous degradation of two key oncogenic drivers suggests a synergistic mechanism of action. We hypothesize that EMB‐01's tetravalent, same‐cell (cis) co‐ligation promotes receptor clustering, accelerates internalization, and biases trafficking toward lysosomal degradation, thereby enhancing co‐degradation of EGFR and c‐Met. This dual degradation could lead to a more durable suppression of tumor signaling compared to single‐target antibodies or bispecific antibodies that merely inhibit ligand binding without promoting receptor loss.

Emerging evidence has highlighted the critical role of EGFR and c‐Met signaling in shaping the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME). Crosstalk between these pathways can upregulate immunosuppressive factors such as PD‐L1, promote regulatory T‐cell (Treg) infiltration, and impair antigen presentation by tumor cells. A recent review has underscored how EGFR/c‐Met interactions facilitate immune evasion and resistance to immunotherapy. Given EMB‐01's ability to induce co‐degradation of both receptors, it is plausible that EMB‐01 not only directly inhibits tumor cell proliferation but also remodels the immune milieu by mitigating immunosuppressive signaling. Future studies evaluating the effects of EMB‐01 on immune cell infiltration, cytokine profiles, and checkpoint molecule expression in vivo will be essential to fully elucidate its immunomodulatory potential.

EMB‐01 demonstrated a favorable preclinical safety profile, with no drug‐related life‐threatening or irreversible toxicities observed at doses up to the highest non‐severely toxic dose (HNSTD) of 160 mg/kg. This is consistent with the manageable safety profile observed in clinical studies.

EMB‐01 exposure increased in a slightly greater‐than‐dose‐proportional manner, which may be attributed to target‐mediated drug disposition (TMDD). The accelerated clearance observed during the terminal phase across the tested dose cohorts may also be influenced by TMDD, as well as the potential development of anti‐drug antibodies (ADA).

Despite these promising findings, several limitations should be acknowledged. The mechanisms underlying differential sensitivity across tumor models require further investigation. Future studies focusing on biomarker discovery to predict EMB‐01 responsiveness will be critical for patient stratification and optimized clinical outcomes. Combination strategies with immune checkpoint inhibitors or chemotherapy warrant exploration to potentially enhance therapeutic efficacy. Additionally, we did not measure the internalization kinetics of the anti‐c‐Met antibody or perform co‐staining with organelle markers (early/late endosomes and lysosomes), nor did we assess EMB‐01's effects on EGFR ligand–induced MAPK activation, which would be very helpful for a deeper understanding of the mechanism by which EMB‐01 works.

In summary, EMB‐01 is a novel tetravalent bispecific antibody that simultaneously targets EGFR and c‐Met with high affinity, resulting in co‐degradation of both receptors and potent downstream pathway inhibition. Through a series of in vitro assays, patient‐derived organoid (PDO) models, and patient‐derived xenograft (PDX) models, EMB‐01 demonstrated robust anti‐tumor efficacy across EGFR‐ and c‐Met–driven malignancies. In addition, EMB‐01 exhibited favorable pharmacokinetics and an excellent safety profile in non‐human primate studies. Beyond its direct anti‐proliferative effects, EMB‐01 holds promise for modulating the tumor immune microenvironment by attenuating immunosuppressive signaling associated with EGFR/c‐Met activation. Collectively, these findings provide a strong rationale for the ongoing clinical development of EMB‐01 (ClinicalTrials.gov ID: NCT05176665) as a therapeutic candidate for patients with EGFR‐ and c‐Met–dysregulated cancers.
