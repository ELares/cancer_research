# Oncolytic virus-induced IL-1β+ monocyte-IL-6+ CAF axis suppresses dendritic cell-mediated antitumor immunity in pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is characterized by a profoundly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, which features a dense desmoplastic stroma enriched with cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) that collectively impede the efficacy of immunotherapies. Although oncolytic viruses (OVs) have demonstrated promising potential in eliciting antitumor immunity, the mechanisms by which stromal components modulate OV efficacy remain poorly understood.

**METHODS**: To investigate the interplay between immune and stromal components in PDAC following OV treatment, we employed murine and patient-derived tumor models. We characterized immune cell populations using single-cell RNA sequencing and flow cytometry. Functional studies included the engineering of a next-generation oncolytic herpes simplex virus expressing FLT3 ligand, OX40 ligand, and interleukin-12 (IL-12), combined with CD40 agonist antibodies to restore dendritic cell (DC) function and enhance T-cell responses.

**RESULTS**: We identified a novel immunosuppressive circuit wherein monocytes, sensing damage-associated molecular patterns and pathogen-associated molecular patterns from OV-infected tumor cells, secrete interleukin-1β. This, in turn, triggers IL-6 production by CAFs, creating an IL-6-rich milieu that impairs DC maturation and co-stimulatory signaling, thereby attenuating T cell-mediated antitumor immunity and restricting OV therapeutic efficacy. Importantly, combination therapy with the engineered OV and CD40 agonist antibodies restored DC functionality and elicited robust tumor-specific T-cell responses in both murine and patient-derived models.

**CONCLUSION**: Our findings reveal a previously unrecognized monocyte-CAF-DC axis that mediates resistance to OV therapy through IL-1β and IL-6-driven suppression of DC function in PDAC. This study provides a mechanistically informed and translationally promising approach to overcome stromal immune suppression, restore effective antitumor immunity, and improve therapeutic outcomes for patients with PDAC.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is marked by a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment dominated by a dense desmoplastic stroma rich in cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs). This stromal barrier significantly limits the effectiveness of immunotherapies. Although oncolytic viruses (OVs) have shown promise in stimulating antitumor immune responses, the role of stromal elements in modulating OV efficacy remains poorly defined.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This study uncovers a novel immunosuppressive axis involving monocytes, CAFs, and dendritic cells (DCs), mediated by interleukin (IL)-1β and IL-6 signaling, which suppresses DC maturation and T-cell activation, thereby restricting OV therapy effectiveness in PDAC. Using engineered oncolytic herpes simplex virus expressing immune-stimulatory molecules combined with CD40 agonist antibodies, the study demonstrates restoration of DC function and potent tumor-specific T-cell responses in both murine and patient-derived models.

These findings highlight a previously unrecognized stromal immune resistance mechanism to OV therapy in PDAC, suggesting that targeting the monocyte-CAF-DC axis could enhance immunotherapy outcomes. The mechanistic insights provide a strong rationale for combining engineered OVs with agents that restore dendritic cell function, potentially informing future clinical strategies and improving therapeutic efficacy in this challenging cancer.

Introduction

 Pancreatic cancer remains one of the most lethal malignancies, with a 5-year survival rate of less than 10% owing to limited treatment options. Despite the revolutionary success of immunotherapies such as immune checkpoint blockade, adoptive cell transfer therapy, and oncolytic virus (OV) therapy in various cancers, their efficacy in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains limited. This therapeutic resistance is primarily attributed to the highly immunosuppressive nature of the tumor microenvironment (TME) in PDAC, characterized by a scarcity of functional dendritic cells (DCs) and an impaired T-cell response.

Cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), an abundant and functionally diverse cellular population in PDAC, exhibit considerable heterogeneity and comprise several subtypes, including myofibroblastic CAFs, inflammatory CAFs (iCAFs), and antigen-presenting CAFs. iCAFs secrete a broad array of cytokines, chemokines, and immunosuppressive metabolites, which collectively impair immune cell function and foster an immune-tolerant milieu that facilitates tumor progression and resistance to immunotherapy.

Our previous studies of herpes simplex virus (HSV)-1-derived OV demonstrated their antitumor efficacy against pancreatic tumor growth by remodeling the TME with enhanced tumor-infiltrating effector T cells, reprogrammed tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) toward more pro-inflammatory M1-like phenotypes. While studies suggest that bidirectional interactions between tumor cells and stromal fibroblasts can either enhance or suppress oncolysis by regulating innate immune responses in cancer cells, how OV-reprogrammed CAFs contribute to antitumor immunity remains to be elucidated. Furthermore, the mechanistic basis underlying OV-mediated reprogramming of CAF function and phenotypic state remains poorly understood.

In this study, we found that toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) stimulation by damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) released from OV-infected cancer cells, in conjunction with absent in melanoma 2 (AIM2) activation, triggers interleukin (IL)-1β release from monocytes. Consequently, IL-1β induces IL-6 production in CAFs, which partially limits the immunostimulatory effects of OV by suppressing DC activation. These findings indicated a previously unrecognized cancer cell death-monocyte-CAF axis that counteracts the immunostimulatory potential of OV therapy, thereby limiting the therapeutic efficacy of OV in PDAC. The observed upregulation of CD40 and FLT3 on DCs following OV therapy inspired the design of a combination strategy co-targeting CD40 and FLT3 to reactivate DCs, representing a promising approach to sensitize PDAC tumors to OV treatment.

Materials and methods

Cell culture

The murine cell line Krasem4(LSLG12D) Trp53em4(R172H) Pdx1em1(Avi-CreERT2), KPC (Shanghai Model Organisms Center (SMOC)), pancreatic stellate cells (iCell Bioscience), human CAFs (FUHENG BIOLOGY), and Vero cells (American Type Culture Collection, ATCC) were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium supplemented with 10% (v/v) fetal bovine serum (FBS). Panc02 cells (CAMS/PUMC) were maintained in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI)-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, 100 U/mL penicillin, and 100 U/mL streptomycin. Primary tumor cells and immune cells were isolated from a patient with non-small cell lung cancer at Tianjin Medical University General Hospital and cultured in RPMI-1640 medium containing 10% FBS, 100 U/mL penicillin, and 100 µg/mL streptomycin.

Mice

Female C57BL/6J aged 6–8 weeks were purchased from Charles River Laboratories (Beijing, China). C57BL/6-Tg (TcraTcrb)1100Mjb/J (OT-I) mice were obtained from Cyagen Biosciences (Suzhou, China), and Batf3⁻/⁻ mice were acquired from SMOC (Shanghai, China). Mice were euthanized via exposure to CO2 on reaching the experimental endpoint. All animal experiments performed in this study were approved by the Animal Ethics Committee of Nankai University (2023-SYDWLL-000297).

Viruses

The oncolytic herpes simplex virus (oHSV) was previously constructed on the backbone of wild-type HSV-1 (F strain), in which both copies of the ICP34.5 coding sequence were replaced with the GFP gene, and the ICP47 gene was deleted. All recombinant OVs were derived from this oHSV using CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene editing. Donor DNA constructs containing homologous arms flanking ICP34.5 and UL26/UL27 were transfected into HEK293FT-sgGFP cells. Simultaneously, the cells were infected with oHSV at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.1 and 10 µM SCR7 (HY-12742, MCE) was added to enhance homologous recombination efficiency. At 48 hours post-infection (hpi), the produced viral particles were harvested and used to infect Vero cells. Single viral plaques were isolated for genotyping, and flow cytometry and ELISA were performed to evaluate the expression of target proteins. To determine infectious viral titers, Vero cells were plated in 6-well plates and infected with serially diluted viral samples. At 36 hpi, plaques in each well were counted in triplicate, and the mean value was calculated. Virus titers were expressed as plaque-forming units (pfu)/mL.

Cytopathic effect and immunogenic cell death analysis

Panc02 cells were either mock-infected or infected with the indicated viruses at an MOI of 1 for the determination of immunogenic cell death (ICD). At 24 hpi, for the ICD assay, cells were stained with an anti-calreticulin (CRT) antibody (bs-5913R-AF647, Bioss) and subjected to flow cytometry analysis. The culture media were collected to determine ATP and Ifn-β concentration by the Enhanced ATP Assay Kit (S0027, Beyotime) and Ifn-β kit (JL52208, Jonlnbio) following the manufacturer’s instructions.

Tumor models

For the therapeutic of different OV studies, 5×10⁵ Panc02 cells or 1×10⁶ KPC cells were subcutaneously injected into the dorsal region of C57BL/6J mice on day 0. Followed by introtumoral administration of 5×10⁶ pfu of OV on days 10, 13, and 16 in the Panc02 model, and days 7, 10, and 13 in the KPC model.

For the IL-6 neutralizing antibody treatment experiments, 5×10⁵ Panc02 cells or 1×10⁶ KPC cells were subcutaneously injected into the dorsal region of C57BL/6J mice on day 0. Followed by intratumoral administration of 5×10⁶ pfu of HSV-OX40 ligand (OX40L)/IL-12 and Intraperitoneal injection (i.p.) administration of 5 mg/kg IL-6 neutralizing antibody (A2118, Selleck), both administered on days 10, 13 and 16.

For the IL-1β neutralizing antibody treatment experiments, 5×10⁵ Panc02 cells were subcutaneously injected into the dorsal region of C57BL/6J mice on day 0. Followed by 5×10⁶ pfu of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 intratumorally and i.p. administration of 5 mg/kg IL-1β neutralizing antibody (A2960, Selleck), both administered on days 10, 13 and 16.

For the CD40/FLT3 ligand (FLT3L) combination therapy, 5×10⁵ Panc02 cells were subcutaneously injected into the dorsal region of C57BL/6J mice on day 0. Followed by intratumoral administration of 5×10⁶ pfu of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 or HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L and 7.5 mg/kg CD40 agonist antibody (BE0016-2, Bio X Cell), both administered on days 7, 10 and 13.

For the rechallenged studies, 5×105 Panc02 tumor cells were subcutaneously injected into the C57BL/6J mice on day 0, followed by intratumoral administration of 5×10⁶ pfu of HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L and i.p. administration of 7.5 mg/kg CD40 agonist antibody, both administered on days 7, 10 and 13. The cured mice were rechallenged with 5×105 Panc02 tumor cells on the 80th day following the initial tumor cell inoculation. 

For the depletion studies of CD8+ T cells, C57BL/6J mice were inoculated subcutaneously with 5×105 Panc02 tumor cells on day 0. The CD8+ T-cell depletion was maintained by repeated intraperitoneal injections of 300 µg per mouse of anti-mouse CD8a (clone 53–6.7, BE0004-1, Bio X Cell) on days 9, 12, 15 and 18. HSV-OX40L/IL-12 (5×106 pfu) and CD40 agonist antibody (7.5 mg/kg) were subcutaneously injected on days 10, 13 and 16.

For the tumor-draining lymph node (TDLN) resection experiments, the inguinal lymph nodes on the left abdominal side (the tumor-inoculated side) of the mice were surgically removed on day 0, and 5×10⁵ Panc02 cells were subcutaneously injected into the C57BL/6J mice on day 1. Followed by 5×10⁶ pfu of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 or HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L intratumorally and 7.5 mg/kg CD40 agonist antibody intraperitoneal on days 11, 14 and 17.

The tumor volume was measured and calculated as 0.5×length×width2. The endpoint of the survival experiment was defined as either death, or the tumor volume reached 1,500 mm3.

Single-cell RNA sequencing and analysis

Three samples from the same group were treated with different hashtags totalseq-C0301, C0302 and C0303 (155861, 155863, 155865, BioLegend), then washed and combined in equal proportions. Single-cell sequencing was carried out using Chromium Next GEM Single Cell 5ʹ Reagent Kits V.2 (10x Genomics) following the manufacturer’s instructions, and sequencing was performed on NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina).

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data analysis was performed by NovelBio with NovelBrain Cloud Analysis Platform (www.novelbrain.com). We applied Fastp with default parameters, filtering the adapter sequence, and removing the low-quality reads to achieve clean data. Then the feature-barcode matrices were obtained by aligning reads to the mouse genome mm10 (Ensembl: V.100) using CellRanger V.6.1.1. Cells contained over 200 expressed genes and mitochondria Unique Molecular Identifier (UMI) rate below 20% passed the cell quality filtering and mitochondria genes were removed in the expression table.

Seurat package (V.4.0.3, https://satijalab.org/seurat/) was used for cell normalization and regression based on the expression table according to the UMI counts of each sample and the per cent of mitochondria rate to obtain the scaled data. Principal component analysis (PCA) was constructed based on the scaled data with the top 2,000 high variable genes and the top 10 principals were used for t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) construction and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) construction. Using the graph-based cluster method, we acquired the unsupervised cell cluster result based on the PCA top 10 principal and we calculated the marker genes by FindAllMarkers function with Wilcoxon rank-sum test algorithm under the following criteria: (1) log2FC>0.25; (2) p value<0.05; (3) min.pct>0.1. In order to identify the cell type in detail, the clusters of the same cell type were selected for re-t-SNE analysis, graph-based clustering and marker analysis.

Cell communication analysis was performed using the R package CellChat (V.1.1.3) with default parameters. The CellChatDB human was used for analysis. Both groups of samples were normalized together, and then each group was extracted, analyzed, and compared in parallel. This is with the assumption that they were sharing cell types.

To elucidate the biological implications of the Difference Expression Genes (DEGs) and marker genes, Gene Ontology (GO) analysis was performed. GO annotations were downloaded from National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI), the Gene Ontology database and UniProt. Fisher’s exact test was applied to identify the significant GO categories, and a False Discovery Rate (FDR) was used to correct the p values.

GEPIA2

To investigate the correlation between IL-6 and other cytokines in pancreatic tissue, data curation and correlation analysis were performed using The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PAAD) dataset via GEPIA2 (http://gepia2.cancer-pku.cn/), a publicly available web-based tool for gene expression analysis.

Flow cytometry analysis

Surface marker analysis was performed on dissociated cells from in vitro cell culture or single-cell suspensions of digested tissues. Cells were stained with anti-mouse CD16/CD32 antibodies (BioLegend) in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) supplemented with 2% FBS (Fluorescence-activated cell sorting, FACS buffer) at 4°C for 10 min to block unspecific binding, before surface marker staining. Cells were stained with antibodies against specific surface markers by incubating with the antibodies diluted in FACS buffer at 4°C for 40 min, followed by washing with PBS and suspending with FACS buffer before flow cytometry analysis. For staining the dead cells, the cells were stained with Zombie NIR viability dye diluted in PBS at 4°C for 10 min. Intracellular marker staining was performed using True-Nuclear Transcription Factor Buffer Set (BioLegend) to fix and permeabilize the cells first according to the manufacturer’s protocols. Cells were stained with antibodies against specific intracellular markers by incubating with the antibodies diluted in perm buffer at 4°C overnight, followed by washing and suspension with PBS. Flow cytometry analysis was performed on LSRFortessa (BD). Data were analyzed using FlowJo V.10 software. The immune cells were stained using the following panels: tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells: live cells, CD45, CD8; tumor-infiltrating CD4+ T cells: live cells, CD45, CD4; cytotoxic CD8+ T cells: live cells, CD45, CD8, interferon (IFN)-γ, granzyme B; regulatory T cells (Tregs): live cells, CD45, CD4, FOXP3. DC in tumors and TDLNs: CD11c, CD80, CD86, IL-12 and major histocompatibility complex (MHC)-II.

DCs activation

To evaluate the effect of different cytokines on DC activation, Panc02 cells were first seeded into 6-well plates with a density of 3×105 cells/well, and subsequently infected with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 at a MOI of 1. After 24 hpi, the culture supernatants (with or without ultraviolet irradiation) were collected and applied to splenic DCs isolated from the mouse spleen using CD11c MicroBeads UltraPure (130-125-835, Miltenyi). The DC cultures were supplemented with 20 ng/mL of one of the following cytokines: IL-4, IL-6, IL-1α, IL-1β, IFN-γ, or Granulocyte-Macrophage Colony Stimulating Factor (GM-CSF) and incubated for 24 hours. The expressions of CD80 and CD86 on DCs were detected by flow cytometry.

To further examine the effects of FLT3L and/or αCD40 antibody on DC activation and presentation, Panc02-OVA cells were first seeded in the 6-well plates and treated with HSV-OX40L/IL-12, HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L, HSV-OX40L/IL-12 plus αCD40 antibody (10 µg/mL) and HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L plus αCD40 antibody (10 µg/mL), all at an MOI of 1. After 24 hpi, the culture mediums were collected and added to splenic DCs (at a 1:1 cell ratio). The expressions of CD11c, CD80, CD86, and SIINFEKL-H2Kb complexes were determined by flow cytometry.

To evaluate whether the combination of CD40 agonist antibody and FLT3L could reverse IL-6-induced suppression of DC activation. Panc02 cells were seeded and infected with either HSV-OX40L/IL-12 or HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L. After 24 hpi, the culture supernatants were collected and co-cultured with splenic DCs in the presence of 20 ng/mL IL-6 and αCD40 agonist antibody. After 24 hours of incubation, the expression levels of CD80 and CD86 on DCs were analyzed by flow cytometry.

IL-1β and IL-6 secretion

To determine IL-1β production by monocytes, cells were first isolated from Panc02 tumor model using CD11b MicroBeads UltraPure (130-126-725, Miltenyi Biotec) without any prior activation and seeded into 6-well plates at a density of 3×10⁵ cells per well. Monocytes were then incubated either with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 directly or co-cultured with Panc02 tumor cells infected with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 at a 1:1 cell ratio (MOI=1). Panc02 cells infected with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 alone were included as controls. After 24 hours of incubation, the concentration of IL-1β in the culture supernatants was measured using an ELISA kit (E-EL-M0037, Elabscience) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

To evaluate the impact of IL-1β on IL-6 secretion, human CAFs and mouse pancreatic stellate cells (PSCs) cell lines were seeded in the 6-well plates and incubated with 100 ng/mL IL-1β for 24 hours. IL-6 concentrations were quantified by ELISA assay (E-EL-M0044, Elabscience). To further assess the effect of IL-1β produced by monocytes on IL-6 secretion by CAFs, primary monocytes isolated from Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs) using CD14 MicroBeads UltraPure (130-050-201, Miltenyi Biotec) were co-cultured with PANC-1 cells and pancreatic CAFs at a 1:1:2 ratio to establish a triple co-culture system. For viral infection, pre-seeded CAFs and tumor cells in this system were incubated with 1×10⁵ pfu of HSV-OX40L/IL-12, followed by a 2-hour incubation period before monocyte addition. In parallel, murine models were constructed using PSC, Panc02 cells and monocytes at the identical 2:1:1 ratio. Following the co-culture period of 24 hours, supernatants were collected and analyzed via ELISA to quantify IL-1β and IL-6 levels.

ELISpot assay

Panc02-OVA cells were seeded into 6-well plates and subsequently infected with either HSV-OX40L/IL-12 or HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L at an MOI of 1. After 24 hours of incubation, splenocytes isolated from mice were added to the cultures at a 10:1 splenocyte-to-tumor cell ratio. In the HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L group, 10 µg/mL of anti-CD40 antibody (clone: FGK45, Bio X Cell, BE0016-2) was also added. Following an additional 24 hours of co-culture, suspended splenocytes were collected and plated into Enzyme Linked Immunospot Assay (ELISpot) plates (Dakewei, 2210002) at a density of 1×10⁵ cells per well. The ELISpot assay was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and the number of IFN-γ-producing spots was quantified to assess T-cell activation. To evaluate the primary samples from patients, primary tumor cells were co-cultured with cells isolated from TDLNs in the presence of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 with anti-CD40 antibody (10 µg/mL) and FLT3L (10 µg/mL, Sino Biological, 10315-HNAE). After 24 hours of co-culture, suspended cells were harvested and subjected to an ELISpot assay following the manufacturer’s instructions. The number of IFN-γ-producing spots was quantified to assess T-cell activation.

Multiplexed immunofluorescence

Multiplexed immunofluorescence was performed by Univ-bio company. The 4 μm thick formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded whole tissue sections were stained with standard, primary antibodies sequentially and paired with TSA 7-Color Kit (abs50015-100T, Absinbio, Shanghai), followed by staining with 4',6-Diamidino-2-phenylindole dihydrochloride (DAPI). The deparaffinized slides were incubated with anti-CD19 antibody (ab134114, Abcam) for 30 min and then treated with an anti-rabbit/mouse horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody (abs50015-02, Absinbio, Shanghai) for 10 min. Then, labeling was developed for a strictly observed 10 min, using TSA 520 according to the manufacturer’s directions. Slides were washed in TBS-T buffer (Tris-Buffered Saline with 0.5% Tween-20) and then transferred to preheated citrate solution (90°C) before being heat-treated using a microwave set at 20% of maximum power for 15 min. Slides were cooled in the same solution to room temperature. Between all steps, the slides were washed with tris buffered saline (TBS) buffer. The same process was repeated for the following antibodies/fluorescent dyes, in order: anti-CD3/TSA 650, anti-CD11c/ TSA 570. Each slide was then treated with two drops of DAPI (abs47047616, Absinbio, Shanghai), washed in distilled water, and manually coverslipped. Slides were air-dried and took pictures with Pannoramic MIDI II (3DHISTECH). Images were analyzed using Indica Halo software.

Statistical analysis

All data were shown as the means±SD unless otherwise indicated in the figure legends. The multiple comparisons were analyzed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) or two-way ANOVA. Two-group comparisons were performed with two-tailed t-test. Survival curves were conducted with the Kaplan-Meier method and compared by the log-rank test. All the statistical analyses were carried out with Prism (V.9.0; GraphPad Software). P values<0.05 were considered as the statistical significance.

Results

HSV-OX40L/IL-12 inhibits the pancreatic tumor growth and enhances the activation of tumor-infiltrating T cells

We previously engineered a series of oHSVs to express various immunomodulatory molecules, including costimulatory ligands and cytokines, with the goal of enhancing antitumor immune responses (figure 1A). Among them, we developed a dual-functional oHSV, designated HSV-OX40L/IL-12, which encodes both a membrane-anchored, trimerized OX40L and secreted IL-12 to simultaneously lyse tumor cells and reprogram them into artificial antigen-presenting cells. To enable comparative analyses, we included several control oHSVs expressing individual components: Green fluorescent protein (GFP) (oHSV), GM-CSF (HSV-GM-CSF), OX40L (HSV-OX40L), or IL-12 (HSV-IL-12) (figure 1B). Notably, the viral backbones used in oHSV and HSV-GM-CSF are identical to those of the clinically approved oHSVs G47Δ and Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC), respectively. The expression of cytokines and costimulatory molecules confirmed successful transgene expression following viral infection (online supplemental figure S1A–C).

To compare the antitumor efficacy of different engineered oHSV variants in pancreatic cancer, Panc02 tumor-bearing mice were treated with each virus construct (figure 1C). Among these, HSV-OX40L/IL-12 induced the most pronounced tumor regression and significantly prolonged survival (figure 1D). Consistent results were observed in the genetically engineered KPC tumor model, further supporting the superior therapeutic effect of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 (figure 1E,F). Analysis of tumor-infiltrating immune cells revealed that HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment led to a 3-fold and 1.6-fold increase in the frequencies of IFN-γ+CD8+ and GzmB+CD8+ T cells, respectively, in Panc02 tumors relative to untreated controls, a trend also observed in the KPC model, indicating enhanced activation of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (figure 1G,H and online supplemental figure S2).

scRNA-seq further demonstrated an increased proportion of effector CD8+ T cells characterized by high expression of Ccl5, Gzma, Gzmb, and Ifng in HSV-OX40L/IL-12-treated tumors (online supplemental figure S3A–C). These effector CD8+ T cells were characterized by activation-associated and differentiation-associated transcriptional signatures and enriched immune-related pathways, consistent with flow cytometry results (online supplemental figure S3D–F). Collectively, these results indicate that HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment promotes a robust cytotoxic T-cell response and reprograms CD8+ T cells toward an activated effector phenotype within the TME. scRNA-seq analysis also revealed a significant increase in resident memory T cell (Trm) subsets, accompanied by a reduction in Tregs (online supplemental figure S3G,H), consistent with the flow cytometry results. Notably, Trm cells displayed elevated expressions of Ccl5, Cd40lg, Ifng, and Tnf, indicative of an activated functional state (online supplemental figure S3I). Taken together, these data indicate that HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment reshapes the tumor immune landscape by enhancing cytotoxic and resident memory CD8+ T cells and suppressing Tregs.

The suppression of DC maturation is driven by cancer-associated fibroblasts derived IL-6 during oncolytic virotherapy

Given the pivotal role of DCs in promoting T-cell immunity, we next assessed the impact of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment on DC maturation and activation. scRNA-seq analysis was employed to comprehensively profile DC responses within the TME (figure 2A,B). HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment led to a slight increase in the frequency of intratumoral DCs in the Panc002 tumors, whereas no change in DC abundance was found in the KPC tumors. Contrary to expectations, HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment did not promote overt activation or maturation of intratumoral DCs, as evidenced by comparable expression levels of Cd80, Cd86, Il12b, H2-Aa, and H2-Eb1 in both Panc02 and KPC tumors (figure 2C). Furthermore, ligand-receptor interaction analysis revealed a reduction in DC-associated signaling pathways in HSV-OX40L/IL-12-treated tumors compared with untreated controls (figure 2D). This functional impairment of intratumoral DCs was consistently observed by flow cytometry in both Panc02 and KPC models (figure 2E). A sample gating scheme is shown in online supplemental figure S4. Given the limited activation and maturation of intratumoral DCs, we next assessed DCs in the TDLNs, the primary sites for antigen presentation and T-cell priming. Consistent with intratumoral findings, HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment did not enhance the activation or maturation of DCs in TDLNs (figure 2F). To further confirm these findings, we compared the therapeutic efficacy of oHSV and HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment between wild-type mice and Batf3−/− mice which lack type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s). The therapeutic efficacy of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 in Batf3−/− mice did not differ significantly from that in wild-type mice (online supplemental figure S5). These results suggest that the antigen-presenting functions of cDC1s are indispensable for the therapeutic effect of HSV-OX40L/IL-12, suggesting the cDC1 maturation is inhibited during the oncolytic virotherapy treatment.

To investigate the mechanisms underlying the persistence of immature DCs following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment, we examined the levels of cytokines associated with DC activation and maturation within the TME. As shown in figure 3A and online supplemental figure S6A, treatment with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 exhibited a significant upregulation of IL-1β, GM-CSF and IFN-γ, all of which are known to promote DC differentiation and activation. Nevertheless, IL-6, a cytokine previously reported to maintain a more immature phenotype of DC, was also markedly elevated following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment. To further assess the functional impact of these cytokines, DCs were exposed to supernatants from HSV-OX40L/IL-12-infected Panc02 tumor cells and individually stimulated with selected cytokines. Notably, a significant reduction in CD80 and CD86 expression was observed exclusively on IL-6 stimulation, with or without live virus (figure 3B and online supplemental figure S6B), suggesting that IL-6 plays a dominant role in suppressing DC maturation and maintaining an immature phenotype in pancreatic cancer model.

To further substantiate that IL-6 compromises DC function and restricts the antitumor response to HSV-OX40L/IL-12 in vivo, co-administration of an anti-IL-6 neutralizing antibody (αIL-6) with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 markedly restricted tumor growth (online supplemental figure S6C,D) and prolonged median survival in both Panc02 and KPC syngeneic models (figure 3C,D). In addition, co-administration with αIL-6 increased the proportion of DCs within tumor tissues and enhanced the expression of co-stimulatory molecules CD80 and CD86, compared with the HSV-OX40L/IL-12 monotherapy. Importantly, IL-6 blockade also significantly upregulated CD80 and CD86 expression on DCs within TDLNs and increased proportions of IFN-γ+CD8+ T cells in the TDLN following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment. These findings demonstrate that IL-6 contributes to the suppression of DC maturation during HSV-OX40L/IL-12 therapy, and that IL-6 neutralization restores DC activation and improves therapeutic efficacy (figure 3E,F).

Given the observed upregulation of IL-6 following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment, we next sought to identify the cellular source of IL-6 and delineate its potential impact on the tumor immune microenvironment. scRNA-seq analysis revealed that CAFs were the predominant IL-6-producing population in both Panc02 and KPC tumor models (figure 3G). This finding was further validated by quantitative PCR analysis of CAFs isolated from KPC tumors, which showed markedly elevated IL-6 expression on HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment compared with untreated controls (figure 3H). To explore the immunological consequences of CAF-derived IL-6, we performed cell–cell communication analysis using IL-6-centered ligand-receptor pairing. As shown in figure 3I, IL-6-mediated signaling between CAFs and DCs, as well as CAFs and macrophages, was significantly enhanced following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment. These data suggest that CAF-derived IL-6 may contribute to immune suppression by regulating DC activation and maturation.

Monocyte-triggered IL-1β induces IL-6 expression in CAFs

To identify potential upstream regulators of IL-6 expressions in CAFs, we performed Pearson correlation analysis between IL6 and a panel of cytokine-related genes (IL1A, IL1B, IL4, and CSF2) using the TCGA PAAD dataset via GEPIA2. Notably, IL1B expression showed a strong positive correlation with IL6 in both PAAD and normal pancreatic tissue datasets (online supplemental figure S7A). To functionally validate this relationship, human CAFs and mouse PSCs were treated with recombinant human or mouse IL-1β, respectively, which led to a substantial increase in IL-6 production, indicating that IL-1β promotes IL-6 expression in fibroblasts (online supplemental figure S7B). Furthermore, cell–cell communication analysis revealed that intercellular IL-1 signaling between CAFs and monocytes, macrophages, and neutrophils was significantly enhanced following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment (online supplemental figure S7C). Additionally, scRNA-seq analysis of Panc02 and KPC models revealed high Il1b expression in monocytes and significant Il1r1 expression in fibroblasts, suggesting a potential paracrine IL-1β signaling axis between these two cell populations (figure 4A).

Furthermore, in vivo administration of anti-IL-1β neutralizing antibodies following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment significantly reduced IL-6 expression within tumor tissues, restoring it to levels comparable to those observed in untreated controls (figure 4B). These findings supported that IL-1β is a key upstream driver of CAF-mediated IL-6 production following OV treatment, establishing a functional inflammatory axis that may contribute to immune modulation in the TME.

As shown in figure 4C and online supplemental figure S7D, co-culture of Panc02 tumor cells and monocytes in the presence of the oHSV or HSV-OX40L/IL-12 resulted in significant IL-1β production, which is not dependent on transgenic OX40L and IL-12. The same results were observed in the supernatants of Panc02 tumors in vivo. In contrast, minimal IL-1β was detected when either monocytes or tumor cells were absent, indicating that both cell types are required for OV-induced IL-1β expression. Consistent with our in vitro findings, treatment with oHSV also resulted in increased IL-1β levels, although to a lesser extent than that induced by HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment, suggesting the OX40L and/or IL-12 plays some role in the IL-1β+ monocyte-IL-6+ CAF axis in vivo.

Based on this observation, we hypothesized that pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and DAMPs released during oHSV-mediated tumor cell lysis activate monocytic NLRP3 or AIM2 inflammasomes, thereby promoting IL-1β maturation and secretion. As shown in figure 4D, oHSV and HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment triggered ICD markers, including extracellular ATP release and CRT surface translocation. Notably, IL-1β expression in monocytes was markedly reduced by pretreatment with a caspase-1 inhibitor (AC-YVAD-cmk), an AIM2 inflammasome inhibitor (Fructose-arginine), or a TLR4 inhibitor (TAK242) prior to the addition of the infected tumor cells. However, the NLRP3 inhibitor (MCC950) did not affect the IL-1β expression. Our data indicates that both AIM2 inflammasome activation (possibly via phagocytosed double-stranded DNA (dsDNA)) and TLR4 signaling (via DAMPs) are essential for IL-1β production (figure 4E).

To functionally assess the role of the monocyte-IL-1β-CAF-IL-6 axis in the TME, we established a tri-culture system comprising HSV-OX40L/IL-12-infected human PANC-1 tumor cells, cancer patient-derived monocytes, and CAFs. This co-culture set-up resulted in robust production of both IL-1β and IL-6 following viral treatment (figure 4F). A similar cytokine induction pattern was observed in a murine system using infected Panc02 tumor cells, monocytes, and PSCs.

CD40 and FLT3 are potential targets for DC reactivation

scRNA-seq analysis revealed that Cd40 and Flt3 expression on DCs was upregulated following HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment, despite the functionally impaired phenotype of DCs (figure 5A). Consistently, in vitro stimulation with IL-6 markedly increased CD40 and FLT3 messenger RNA expression in DCs (figure 5B). Given the established roles of CD40 and FLT3 in promoting DC maturation and differentiation, we next evaluated these molecules as potential combination targets to reactivate DC in the TME.

We first generated a modified HSV-OX40L/IL-12 virus expressing FLT3L by inserting the FLT3L gene into the UL26-UL27 intergenic region of the HSV genome (online supplemental figure S8A,B). For stimulation of the CD40 targets on DCs, we combined OV treatment with a commercial anti-mouse CD40 agonists (clone FGK45). Notably, the combination of HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L with anti-CD40 agonists induced the highest levels of DC activation markers as well as H-2Kb-SIINFEKL complex presentation on DCs (figure 5C and online supplemental figure S8C). To assess downstream T-cell responses, splenocytes from OT-I mice were co-cultured with various engineered HSV-infected Panc02-OVA tumor cells in the presence or absence of anti-CD40 agonists. As shown in figure 5D, the combination of HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L and anti-CD40 agonists elicited the strongest IFN-γ+ ELISpot response, indicating enhanced antigen-specific CD8+ T-cell activation. Parallel results were obtained in human systems, where the triple combination therapy (HSV-hOX40L/IL-12 (carrying human trimerized OX40L and IL-12 genes), hFLT3L recombinant protein, and anti-CD40 enhanced IFN-γ secretion and expanded IFN-γ+ T cell in TDLN cells co-cultured with autologous HSV-infected tumor cells (figure 5E). Together, these findings support the therapeutic potential of combining FLT3L and CD40 agonists with oncolytic virotherapy to enhance DC activation, promote effective antigen presentation, and augment antigen-specific T-cell responses.

We next evaluated whether this combination strategy could overcome IL-6-induced impairment of DC activation (figure 5F). In the presence of IL-6, DCs co-cultured with tumor cells infected with either HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L or HSV-OX40L/IL-12 combined with anti-CD40 agonists exhibited 2.7-fold and 3.1-fold increase of CD80 and CD86 compared with HSV-OX40L/IL-12 alone. Notably, the combination of HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L and anti-CD40 antibodies resulted in the highest upregulation of these co-stimulatory molecules, indicating a synergistic effect in restoring DC maturation.

Combination strategy enhances antitumor efficacy through DC activation

Inspired by the potential of DC reactivation, we next evaluated whether this combination strategy could enhance antitumor efficacy. Treatment with HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L combined with anti-CD40 agonists led to the most pronounced tumor inhibition, achieving complete regression in 40% of Panc02-bearing mice (figure 6A,B), with no significant liver toxicity observed (online supplemental figure S9A). To assess long-term immune protection, the cured mice were rechallenged with Panc02 cells on day 80 after the initial tumor inoculation. Remarkably, all previously cured mice rejected tumor growth, whereas all age-matched controls developed tumors, indicating the establishment of systemic antitumor immune memory (online supplemental figure S9B,D).

To further investigate the mechanism underlying the enhanced antitumor efficacy, tumor-infiltrating T cells and DCs were analyzed 6 days after the final treatment (figure 6C). The combination of HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L and anti-CD40 agonists induced the highest frequencies of GzmB+CD8+ and IFN-γ+CD8+ T cells, suggesting a synergistic effect of FLT3L and CD40 stimulation on cytotoxic T-cell activation. Notably, this combination also significantly increased the proportions of CD11c+CD45+ and MHC-II+CD11c+ DCs within tumors, accompanied by elevated IL-12 production and upregulated expression of co-stimulatory molecules CD80 and CD86, indicating marked enhancement in both the abundance and functional activation of intratumoral DCs (figure 6D). Consistent with these findings, a similar increase in phenotypically activated DCs was also observed in the TDLNs following combination treatment (figure 6E). Interestingly, multiplexed immunofluorescence analysis revealed the formation of lymphoid clusters containing B cells, CD8+ T cells and DCs within the parenchyma of tumors following treatment with HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L combined with anti-CD40 agonists (figure 6F). Taken together, these findings demonstrate that dual targeting of FLT3 and CD40 potently enhances DC activation and function, both intratumorally and in TDLNs, thereby eliciting robust antitumor immunity.

To further evaluate the contribution of DC to antitumor immune responses, Panc02 tumor cells were implanted into Batf3−/− mice. Remarkably, the antitumor efficacy of the combination therapy was completely abrogated in Batf3−/− mice, in stark contrast to wild-type controls, indicating that cDC1s are indispensable for mediating therapeutic benefit. Similarly, depletion of CD8+ T cells also abolished the antitumor effect of the combination treatment. These results highlight that the enhanced antitumor efficacy of the combination therapy relies on a coordinated immune circuit between cDC1s and CD8+ T cells (figure 6G,H).

To further validate the role of DC-mediated T-cell priming in antitumor immunity, we surgically removed the TDLNs prior to tumor inoculation. TDLN resection abolished the antitumor activity of the HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L/anti-CD40 agonists combination, directly establishing these lymph nodes as the crucial anatomical sites for DC-initiated T-cell priming. Interestingly, the residual antitumor effect observed was comparable to that of HSV-OX40L/IL-12 monotherapy, suggesting that in the absence of TDLNs, the therapeutic benefit was primarily attributed to the direct oncolytic activity and elicitation of tumor-localized T-cell responses, rather than to priming of systemic tumor-specific T cells (figure 6I,J).

Programmed Death-Ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression on tumor cells was significantly increased after HSV-OX40L/IL-12 treatment compared with the untreated group (online supplemental figure S10A). This finding motivated the development of the bispecific antibody MBS307, which combines an agonistic anti-CD40 Fc LALA backbone with PD-L1-targeting single-chain fragment ariable (scFv) and VHH domains to concurrently block immune checkpoint signaling and promote tumor-localized DC activation (online supplemental figure S10B). In vitro, MBS307 activated CD40-expressing reporter cells in a dose-dependent manner with a half maximal Effective Concentration (EC50) of 86.14 ng/mL (online supplemental figure S10C). In humanized CD40/Programmed Death-1/PD-L1 mice, MBS307 plus HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L showed superior tumor inhibition and extended median survival by 9 days over HSV-OX40L/IL-12 alone (online supplemental figure S10D), without affecting body weight or liver function markers (online supplemental figure S10E).

Discussion

In this study, we identified a previously unrecognized cancer cell-monocyte-CAF axis as a key driver of resistance to oncolytic virotherapy in pancreatic cancer. Mechanistically, DAMPs and PAMPs released from dying tumor cells infected by OV stimulate monocytes to secrete IL-1β, which subsequently induces CAFs to upregulate IL-6 expression. This IL-6-mediated signaling cascade impairs the activation and function of DC, thereby diminishing the therapeutic efficacy of virotherapy. Despite observing DC dysfunction following OV treatment, the elevated expression of CD40 and FLT3 on DCs prompted us to develop a combination therapy using anti-CD40 agonists and HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L, which reactivated IL-6 suppressed DCs and elicited potent tumor-specific T-cell immunity. Collectively, our findings elucidate a novel immunosuppressive circuit within the pancreatic TME and provide a mechanistically guided therapeutic approach to overcome OV resistance and improve immunotherapy outcomes in pancreatic cancer (online supplemental 2).

CAFs are increasingly recognized orchestrators of immune evasion within the TME, exerting their suppressive functions largely through the secretion of cytokines and chemokines. Among these factors, IL-6 produced by iCAFs plays a critical role not only in promoting cancer progression but also potently disrupts antitumor immune responses. IL-6 produced by iCAFs has been implicated in various malignancies, including pancreatic, lung, colorectal, and gastric cancers, where it contributes to immune resistance, metastatic dissemination, and poor clinical outcomes. IL-6 mediates immunosuppressive effects of DCs predominantly through the activation of the STAT3 pathway. This signaling cascade inhibits TLR4 ligand-induced and lipopolysaccharide-induced DC activation, suppresses class II MHC expression, and downregulates co-stimulatory molecules, resulting in impaired antigen presentation and T-cell immune responses. Beyond DC suppression, IL-6 establishes a multi-layered immunosuppressive milieu by reinforcing angiogenesis via vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) upregulation, recruiting myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and polarizing macrophages toward the protumoral M2 phenotype. Additionally, IL-6 facilitates tumor cell epithelial-mesenchymal transition, stemness, and metastatic potential, rendering it a central node at the intersection of immune suppression and tumor progression. These multifaceted roles of IL-6 position iCAF-derived cytokines as both mediators and amplifiers of resistance to immunotherapy, underscoring the therapeutic rationale for selectively targeting this stromal axis.

Notably, scRNA-seq data derived from the KPC model has notably demonstrated a significant increase in neutrophil infiltration following OV treatment. It had been reported that neutrophils or MDSCs have been shown to promote pancreatic cancer cell metastasis and immune escape in pancreatic cancer treatment. It is also reported that oncolytic HSV treatment promoted IGF2BP3-mediated formation of neutrophil extracellular trap (NET), which limits their oncolytic efficacy. Inhibiting NETosis enhances viral replication and therapeutic outcomes. Furthermore, Xin Zheng et al reported that hepatocellular carcinoma expressed Gsk3a to promote neutrophil recruitment and NET formation. Targeting Gsk3a effectively enhances cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) function and improves the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors. Notably, Marcus Järås et al found that targeting IL1RAP on CAFs inhibits IL-1-induced chemokine secretion, thereby reducing neutrophil and monocyte infiltration and alleviating the immunosuppressive TME to enhance antitumor immunity. Together, these findings underscore the pivotal role of neutrophils in shaping tumor immunity and support the integration of neutrophil-targeted interventions alongside existing immunotherapies, including OVs, to potentiate antitumor efficacy.

These findings highlight neutrophils as key barriers to effective antitumor immunity and therapy response in solid tumors. In pancreatic cancer, neutrophil-mediated suppression likely hinders OV efficacy by restraining viral replication and immune activation. Thus, targeting neutrophil recruitment or polarization—through COX-2 inhibition, IL-1/IL1RAP blockade, or related pathways—represents a promising strategy to enhance oncolytic virotherapy and improve clinical outcomes in refractory cancers such as PDAC.

In this study, we identified CAFs as the principal source of IL-6 in the TME following OV treatment, and demonstrated that IL-6 significantly impaired DC maturation and function both in tumors and TDLNs. Significantly impaired DC activation and maturation. Importantly, blockade of IL-6 signaling effectively restored DC activation, highlighting the critical role of IL-6 in mediating DC suppression during OV therapy and the potential of targeting IL-6/JAK/STAT3 pathway to enhance oncolytic virotherapy.

Recently, Kabiljo et al reported a novel patient-derived organoids (PDO)/CAF/myeloid cell co-culture system to faithfully recapitulate critical TME features. Treatment with oncolytic influenza A virus preferentially induces IL-6 in CAFs and IL-1β in TAM-like cells, while upregulating IL-1R1 in CAFs. Our results in mouse models validate the utility of this co-culture system for in vitro modeling tumor-immune interactions. Mechanistically, we uncovered OV treatment markedly elevated IL-1β expression in tumor-infiltrating monocytes and IL-1R in CAFs, supporting a paracrine signaling axis whereby IL-1β promotes IL-6 production by CAF via IL-1R engagement. Interestingly, although OVs are known to reprogram TAMs toward a more immunostimulatory M1-like phenotype, these macrophages may paradoxically acquire protumorigenic properties, such as promoting IL-6 production by iCAFs, partly mediated by IL-1β. Given its pleiotropic pro-inflammatory and tumor-promoting effects, IL-1β represents an attractive therapeutic target, as exemplified by ongoing pancreatic cancer trials involving the approved IL-1R antagonist anakinra for pancreatic cancer.

Importantly, our results also reveal that IL-1β secretion by monocytes is not intrinsic to OV exposure but is instead dependent on sensing of DAMPs and PAMPs released from OV-infected tumor cells. Pharmacological inhibition studies identified AIM2, a cytosolic DNA sensor, as the dominant inflammasome responsible for IL-1β maturation, rather than NLRP3 pathway. OV-derived dsDNA likely activates AIM2, which assembles into an inflammasome or, alternatively, forms a multiprotein panoptosome complex to induce panoptosis, a process that promotes IL-1β production. Consistent with this, we observed that IL-1β induction also relies on TLR4 signaling in responses to OV-induced tumor cell death. Together, our results highlight how innate immune sensing of OV-treated tumors, while initiating antitumor responses and being expected to activate DCs, may also trigger compensatory immunosuppressive programs driven by CAFs. Our study highlights the role of AIM2 in HSV infection; however, host innate immune responses to HSV are initiated on recognition of viral nucleic acids by many pattern recognition receptors, including cyclic GMP-AMP synthase, toll-like receptor 9, Z-DNA binding protein 1, IFI16, and heterogeneous nuclear ribonucleoprotein A2/B1 (hnRNPA2B1). The effects of other DNA sensors on HSV-based OVs remain to be elucidated in future research.

Previous studies have highlighted the critical role of DC activation in enhancing oncolytic virotherapy. Labiano et al demonstrated that CD40 agonistic antibody-mediated DC activation potentiates antigen presentation and significantly improves the antitumor efficacy of OV Delta-24-RGD in preclinical diffuse midline glioma models. Similarly, CD40L-armed oHSV was shown to enhance DC maturation and prolong survival in murine models. Furthermore, Svensson-Arvelund et al reported that Flt3L-expressing oncolytic Newcastle disease virus promotes DC recruitment and tumor antigen cross-presentation, thereby augmenting the therapeutic effects of oncolytic virotherapy. Building on the observed upregulation of CD40 and FLT3 on DCs following OV treatment, we developed a combinatorial strategy to overcome DC dysfunction by simultaneously engaging CD40 and FLT3 signaling pathways. This approach, combining HSV-OX40L/IL-12/FLT3L with CD40 agonist, effectively restored cDC1 activation and maturation, leading to significantly improved tumor growth control and enhanced response to oncolytic virotherapy in tumor-bearing mice. Importantly, the therapeutic efficacy of this regimen was abrogated in Batf3⁻/⁻ mice lacking cDC1s and in CD8+ T cell-depleted mice, underscoring the essential role of DC-crosstalk in mediating the antitumor effects of this combination therapy.

Our findings provide a mechanistic foundation for rational combination strategies; however, several important issues warrant further investigation. Although this study focuses on IL-6, other iCAF-secreted factors (eg, VEGF, M-CSF, CXCL1, Wnt2, and MCP-1) may also disrupt DC maturation. In addition, IL-6 exerts broader immunoregulatory effects, such as amplifying MDSC recruitment and driving T-cell exhaustion, beyond its role in DC suppression. Specifically, disrupting the IL-1β–CAF–IL-6 axis—via IL-1R antagonists (eg, anakinra) or IL-6/IL-6R blockade—could also provide a means to interrupt the immunosuppressive crosstalk between monocytes, CAFs, and DCs. These complementary approaches merit further exploration as part of multi-pronged strategies to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of OV-based immunotherapy in pancreatic cancer.

Together, our findings identify a previously unrecognized cancer cell death sensing-monocyte-CAF axis that suppresses DC maturation through IL-1β-induced IL-6 signaling, thereby limiting the efficacy of oncolytic virotherapy in pancreatic cancer. By targeting this immunosuppressive circuit or reactivating DCs through rationally designed FLT3L/CD40-based combinatorial immunotherapy, we demonstrate an effective strategy to restore antitumor immunity and improve the therapeutic outcome of OV treatment in this otherwise poorly responsive malignancy.
