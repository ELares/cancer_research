# High-voltage electrical pulses for pancreatic cancer treatment in the era of precision medicine.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Pancreatic cancer (PC) presents a significant therapeutic challenge due to its immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Emerging evidence supports the efficacy of high-voltage electrical pulses (HVEPs) in PC treatment, leveraging dual benefits of pancreatobiliary duct integrity maintenance and immunogenicity activation.

**OBJECTIVE**: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, and Web of Science were searched from January 2000 to January 2025. This review synthesizes recent advances in HVEPs for PC treatment, specifically focusing on clinical translation, mechanistic insights, and combinatorial strategies with other modalities.

**KEY FINDINGS**: HVEPs induce immunogenic cell death and reprogram the TME, converting immunologically "cold" tumors to "hot." We also highlight the synergistic potential of combining HVEPs with surgery, chemotherapy, immunotherapy (notably for prolonging immune effects and activating abscopal responses), and nanomaterials (which enhance targeting, reduce toxicity, and augment efficacy). Current research must still address several outstanding issues in device engineering, mechanism exploration, designing new immunotherapy combinations, and developing nanomaterials.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Further exploration of the mechanisms of HVEPs and the development of combination approaches based on HVEPs - particularly with immunotherapy and nanomaterials - holds significant promise for advancing personalized, precise, and systemic management of PC.

Introduction

Pancreatic cancer (PC) is projected to become the second leading cause of cancer-related deaths by 2030 in the United States. Despite advances over the past two decades, the prognosis for patients with PC has remained poor, with a 5-year survival rate of only 10%. Due to the challenges of early detection, tumor progression is often advanced, with over 70% of patients unable to undergo surgical treatment and thus resorting to chemotherapy. However, the available chemotherapy regimens for PC are limited and are associated with systemic toxicity. Moreover, effective immunotherapies are lacking due to the unique immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) of PC. Therefore, the development of novel therapeutic approaches for treating PC represents a critical imperative for scientists worldwide.

High-voltage electrical pulses (HVEPs) are a novel nonthermal ablation technology with unique advantages in treating PC. Unlike traditional thermal ablation, HVEP treatment involves applying electrical pulses to the cellular membranes. Since the emergence of HVEPs, the selective inactivation of tumors without damaging important surrounding structures has attracted widespread attention; therefore, the application of HVEPs in PC therapy has great potential.

In 2020, Geboers et al provided a comprehensive review of the definition and principles of HVEPs, detailing their applications across various tumors. However, this review provided insufficient coverage of HVEPs’ immune effects. Given that these immune effects have become a major research focus in recent years and represent one of the most prominent advantages of HVEPs, this area warrants further summarization. Second, the therapeutic efficacy of HVEPs against malignant tumors remains limited, often requiring combination with other therapies to achieve greater effectiveness. Previous reviews have provided limited summaries on this aspect, making a focused review in this field valuable. Third, while most reviews on HVEPs cover a broad range of tumor types, significant differences exist between cancers. PC, with its unique anatomical characteristics and poor response to immunotherapy, represents an optimal target for HVEP research. Therefore, a review specifically focused on the application of HVEPs in PC is merited. Accordingly, a literature search was conducted using PubMed, Web of Science, Embase, Cochrane Library, and ClinicalTrials.gov from 1 January 2000 to 1 May 2023, with results restricted to English-language publications. Search terms were as follows: [(cancer* OR carcinom* OR adenocarcinom* OR neoplas* OR malignan*) AND (pancrea*)] AND (electrical pulse* OR electric field* OR “electroporation”). The review has been reported in line with the TITAN criteria.

This review summarizes the mechanistic breakthroughs and clinical progress of HVEPs in treating PC and emphasizes the potential of HVEPs combined with surgery, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and nanomaterials. It aims to provide new perspectives for the personalized, precise, systemic, and minimally invasive treatment of PC patients.

The basic mechanism of HVEPs

When applied, HVEPs alter the transmembrane resting potential. If the accumulated transmembrane potential exceeds a critical threshold, membrane destabilization occurs, forming nanoscale defects (pores) – a process termed electroporation. The formation of pores increases membrane permeability, permitting transmembrane transport of normally impermeable molecules.

The efficacy of HVEPs is closely dependent on the waveform, amplitude, and frequency of the electrical signal (Fig. 1). Cell membrane permeability increases at a low electric field, increasing drug sensitivity. This effect is reversible and is referred to as reversible electroporation (RE). However, when electrical pulses disrupt cell membranes beyond a certain threshold (too high an electrical field, too long a pulse, or too many pulses), irreversible electroporation (IRE) occurs. This creates irreversible nanoscale pores on the cell membrane, inducing a cellular homeostasis imbalance and cell death. 

HVEP-induced intracellular damage

HVEPs can cause damage to cell membranes. This damage harms cells, resulting in Ca2+ influx, adenosine triphosphate (ATP) depletion, reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation, and DNA damage (Fig. 2). After HVEPs, a significant amount of Ca2+ enters the cell, causing a decrease in intracellular ATP levels. This is due to the activation of Ca-ATPase and the inhibition of ATP production in the mitochondria. Electric pulses can initiate the production of ROS within cells, affecting cell membrane permeability and resealing time, damaging DNA, triggering oxidative stress, and causing cell death. Elevating ROS levels and amplifying DNA damage represent key strategies to enhance HVEP efficacy. 

HVEPs modulates cell fate decisions

Traditionally, apoptosis was traditionally viewed as the primary cell death mechanism in electroporation. Recent studies suggest that enhancing post-HVEP apoptosis improves therapeutic outcomes. However, the actual cell death mode depends on pulse parameters, tissue type, and treatment conditions, manifesting as apoptosis, necrosis, necroptosis, or pyroptosis at different time points. For example, Razakamanantsoa et al observed early apoptosis post-IRE, but rapid ATP depletion prevents Ca2+ influx, shifting cell death toward necrosis. This underscores HVEP-induced death mechanism diversity. Recent research has further elucidated novel death pathways in HVEPs, including autophagy, ferroptosis, and cuproptosis, as potential therapeutic targets. Ye et al demonstrated that IRE activates autophagy in residual cancer cells, promoting tumor recurrence; hydroxychloroquine-mediated autophagy inhibition significantly boosted IRE efficacy. Thus, HVEPs regulate cellular fate through complex mechanisms. Suppressing reparative pathways (e.g., autophagy) while promoting lethal pathways (e.g., apoptosis, ferroptosis) offers a promising strategy for HVEP sensitization.

From cold tumors to hot tumors: immune effects induced by HVEPs

PC has a long history of poor response to immunotherapy and is considered a “cold tumor.” A network of interactions among fibroblasts, cancer cells, and immune cells creates an immunosuppressive TME. Tregs (CD4+CD25+Foxp3+ T cells), tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) secrete interleukin (IL)-10 and transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) to inhibit CD8+ T cell activity, leading to immune evasion, neoangiogenesis, cancer cell proliferation, and migration. Current efforts to alter the immune response in PC are focused on reversing the immunosuppressive TME. HVEP ablation is a potential method for this purpose (Fig. 3). 

HVEP-induced immune effects

HVEP-induced immunogenic cell death

By killing tumor cells, ablative therapy can reverse immunosuppression and even induce tumor-specific T cell growth. ICD is associated with the release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Several studies have shown that HVEPs contribute to the release of ATP, high-mobility group protein 1 (HMGB1), and calreticulin (CRT) and that levels increase with pulse intensity. IRE-generated HMGB1 activates mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) by binding to receptors of advanced glycation endproducts (RAGE). In other ICD studies, ATP released from damaged cells was shown to bind to the P2RX7 in dendritic cells (DCs), leading to DC maturation and the release of IL-1β. Additionally, HVEPs were shown to cause only minor protein and tissue structure changes, resulting in improved antigenic and adjuvant properties. Zhao et al demonstrated that the rate and magnitude of ATP and HMGB1 release induced by IRE were approximately 10-fold greater within 5 minutes than those observed with radiotherapy, which resulted in less than a 3-fold increase over several days, underscoring the advantages of HVEP-induced ICD.

HVEP-induced immune cell activation

HVEPs induce a tumor-specific immune response. The release of DAMPs contributes to the maturation of antigen-presenting cells (APCs), which can activate T cells in tumors or tumor-draining lymph nodes (TdLNs) after HVEPs. This induces an immune response mediated primarily by CD8+ T cells that differentiate into cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) and upregulate Fas ligand (FasL) expression, which participate in target cell apoptosis by releasing substances such as perforin and granzymes. DC and CD8+ T cell numbers increase after HVEPs, and the number of cells increases with pulse amplitude, frequency, and duration. The role of CD4+ cells is controversial, with some studies finding increased numbers of CD4+ cells, but others showing no significant increase in the number of CD4+ cells. However, the numbers of Tregs and MDSCs decrease, suggesting remission of immunosuppression induced by HVEPs. Similar changes have been observed in the peripheral blood, lymph nodes, and spleen, suggesting that HVEPs induce significant cellular immune responses in the TME and systemic immune organs.

HVEP-induced cytokine alterations

HVEP-induced cytokine changes are essential for immune activation. IRE treatment has been shown to significantly increase the serum levels of TNF-α, IL-1, IL-2, IL-3, IL-12p70, and interferon-γ (IFN-γ). For example, Zhao et al reported elevated blood levels of TNF-α and IL-1β after seven days of IRE treatment. High TNF-α, IFN-γ, and IL-2 levels were found in a population by Lin et al. Elevated levels of these cytokines contribute to an enhanced immune response. He et al reported decreased IL-6 and IL-10 in patients undergoing IRE, and coincidentally, Zhao et al reported reduced IL-6 after seven days in a mouse model. IL-6 is associated with increased angiogenesis and subsequent tumorigenesis through several pathways, particularly the JAK-STAT3 pathway. IL-10 may suppress TME immunity by upregulating B7-H4 expression in macrophages and PD-L1 (programmed cell death ligand 1) in monocytes. Overall, antitumoral cytokine levels increase after HVEPs, while protumoral cytokine levels decrease.

HVEPs modulate the tumor stroma

In an animal model, Vogel et al reported that the isolated vessels remained unchanged 24 hours after ablation. Perivascular fibrosis and inflammation were observed 35 days after treatment, but vascular integrity remained intact; this is referred to as the “vascular lock effect”. Based on these findings, Zhao et al reported that decreased levels of hypoxia-inducible factor-1 α (HIF-1α) and carbonic anhydrase IX (CA-IX) in tumor tissue after IRE, and that increased microvessel density (MVD) and vascular permeability ameliorate oxygen deficiency in tumors. In addition to the effects of HVEPs on the tumor vasculature in PC, alterations in the extracellular matrix (ECM) and in CAFs after HVEPs require further investigation.

Transiency of HVEPs modulation

Although HVEPs are recognized to elicit abscopal effects, recent evidence suggests these responses may be localized and transient. In clinical practice, tumors exhibiting irregular morphology or excessive volume frequently result in heterogeneous electric field distribution. This can lead to residual tumor tissue, impeding complete ablation and ultimately contributing to tumor recurrence. Owing to insufficient antigen release, HVEP-induced immune responses may be suboptimal. Furthermore, as HVEP therapy is typically delivered in a single session, the resultant immune activation is constrained to a limited temporal window. Pandit et al reported that PC patients treated with IRE exhibited decreased Treg counts on post-procedure day 1, followed by rebound between days 3–5. Scheffer et al documented analogous findings. These collective observations underscore the importance of combining HVEPs with adjuvant therapies (e.g., chemotherapy) to augment antigen release, while concomitantly administering immunotherapies within an optimal time window to enhance clinical efficacy.

Single HVEPs for pancreatic cancer treatment

Clinical indications and efficacy of HVEPs

HVEPs were first pioneered by Davalos and Rubinsky in 2003. The first-generation technique, the Nano Knife, received FDA approval for soft tissue tumors in 2008. HVEPs have unique advantages over local ablative techniques, such as preserving vascular, biliary, nerve, and other tubule structures around tumors without a heat sink effect while ensuring safety, efficacy, and clear margins (Table 1). 

HVEPs for the treatment of locally advanced pancreatic cancer

Stage III PC is classified into two types based on the degree of vascular invasion: borderline resectable pancreatic cancer (BRPC) and locally advanced pancreatic cancer (LAPC). Currently, surgical resection is not recommended for LAPC patients. Therefore, IRE may be a viable option for local tumor control and pain management.

In 2012, Martin et al first discussed the safety of IRE for treating LAPC. Twenty-seven patients underwent IRE, and no clinical pancreatitis or fistula formation was observed. Subsequently, in 2013, Martin et al demonstrated statistically significant improvements in progression-free survival (PFS) (15 months vs. 9 months) and overall survival (OS) (20 months vs. 13 months) with IRE compared to standard therapy. The results of the PANFIRE-2 multicenter clinical trial (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT01939665) were published in 2020. All 50 patients underwent CT-guided percutaneous IRE, and 68% received neoadjuvant chemotherapy. The median OS of IRE patients was 17 months after diagnosis, which was significantly longer than that of patients receiving standard chemotherapy (12 to 14 months).

HVEPs for the treatment of isolated localized recurrence

Notably, ten out of the 50 patients in the PANFIRE-2 trial who experienced isolated localized recurrence (ILR) after resection had a median OS of 16 months after being diagnosed with ILR. This indicates that IRE is still effective for PC patients with ILR after resection. In a similar study, Månsson et al treated ten PC patients with ILR after surgical resection using percutaneous IRE. The median OS after IRE and resection was 16.5 and 42.7 months, respectively.

Adverse events of HVEPs

IRE is a safe and minimally invasive procedure with a lower adverse event (AE) rate (25%) than pancreatectomy (39%) for the treatment of early-stage disease. The common complications of IRE are abdominal pain, infection, gastrointestinal bleeding, and ascites. However, severe complications (>grade 3) may involve portal vein thrombosis, pancreatitis, and bile or pancreatic fluid leakage. Stephens et al conducted a study on various factors and discovered that high-dose neoadjuvant radiotherapy (OR = 2.4), IRE electrodes covering the supra-mesenteric arteries and veins (OR = 1.9), and biliary stenting for more than six months (OR = 1.7) were associated with a greater risk of severe AEs.

Guidance of HVEPs

The routes for HVEPs can be categorized as image-guided percutaneous (including CT and ultrasound), open, or laparoscopic. More direct comparative studies on the differences in effectiveness and safety between various routes are needed. Sugimoto et al demonstrated that the severe 120-day complication rate was 12.5% for both the percutaneous and open routes and that the OS was 18 months for the percutaneous route and 16 months for the open access route. Therefore, regarding safety and efficacy, both routes were essentially equivalent. On the other hand, the open approach allows for more precise needle placement under direct visualization and the possibility of assessing the presence of distant disease. However, the percutaneous approach offers a significant advantage in saving healthcare costs due to a shorter hospital stay, which in some studies was as fast as 1 day. Therefore, the percutaneous approach is a minimally invasive approach, and stable LAPC patients in poor health should undergo percutaneous IRE rather than surgical IRE. Notably, current comparative clinical studies evaluating the Guidance of HVEPs are predominantly retrospective. Although these investigations indicate superior outcomes with the percutaneous approach, methodological limitations – particularly their retrospective design – hinder definitive conclusions.

Comparative modalities of HVEPs

HVEPs also offer significant advantages over other localized therapies [radiofrequency ablation (RFA), radiotherapy, and surgery, stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy (SABR)]. He et al retrospectively compared IRE and RFA survival outcomes using propensity score matching (PSM) and showed a greater survival benefit with IRE (2-year OS, 53.5% vs. 27.0%; 2-year PFS, 28.4% vs. 6.4%). Using the same method, they found that patients who received IRE had better OS and PFS benefits than those who received radiotherapy. Martin et al analyzed the data of BRPC patients and reported that OS was significantly better in the IRE MA group, i.e., 34.2 months versus 27.9 months for pancreatectomy alone. The CROSSFIRE trial was designed to compare the efficacy and safety of MRI-guided SABR versus CT-guided percutaneous IRE after standard FOLFIRINOX chemotherapy. After randomization, median OS was 16.1 months in the SABR group versus 12.5 months in the IRE group. There was no difference in overall survival between the two groups, and future studies should further investigate the difference in outcomes. However, most existing comparative clinical studies pitting HVEPs against other local therapies, except for the CROSSFIRE trial, are retrospective. This fundamental methodological limitation precludes definitive conclusions, necessitating prospective multi-center comparative trials. In addition, localized therapeutic techniques, such as microwave ablation (MWA) and cryoablation, which have yet to be compared with IRE, deserve further investigation (Tables 2 and 3).  

HVEPs amplify drug effects: HVEPs with chemotherapy for pancreatic cancer treatment

Delayed HVEP ablation with induction chemotherapy (neoadjuvant chemotherapy), initial HVEP ablation with postoperative adjuvant chemotherapy, and HVEP ablation with concurrent chemotherapy are three new strategies for treating cancer. HVEPs create nanoscale pores in cell membranes, enhancing the diffusion of chemotherapy agents into cells and thereby improving the efficacy of combination therapy (Table 4). 

HVEPs with induction or adjuvant chemotherapy

The administration of induction chemotherapy with HVEPs allows physicians to treat micrometastatic disease at an early stage and assess the efficacy of chemotherapy to determine the most appropriate subsequent course. In contrast, performing HVEP ablation before adjuvant chemotherapy reduces tumor burden, consequently permitting lower chemotherapy doses.

HVEPs with induction chemotherapy for pancreatic cancer

Recent research suggests that most patients who receive HVEPs benefit from induction chemotherapy, and several groups have shown that induction chemotherapy influences OS and PFS. The IMPALA trial revealed that patients who received IRE after three months of FOLFIRINOX or gemcitabine induction chemotherapy had a longer OS than did those who did not receive induction chemotherapy (13 months vs. 6 months). Ah et al recommended at least four FOLFIRINOX cycles before IRE. FOLFIRINOX identifies aggressive tumors and avoids potentially dangerous local treatment in patients who do not benefit from IRE. Additionally, FOLFIRINOX may reduce staging, potentially converting 15–25% of lesions to resectable lesions.

HVEPs with adjuvant chemotherapy for pancreatic cancer

The efficacy of adjuvant chemotherapy is controversial and needs to be further explored. In a study conducted by Thomas et al, the OS of patients who received adjuvant chemotherapy was compared with that of patients who did not. The OS from diagnosis was 25.81 months for patients who received adjuvant chemotherapy and 29.97 months for patients who did not receive adjuvant chemotherapy. This difference was found to be statistically nonsignificant. Furthermore, no significant association was identified between adjuvant chemotherapy combined with FOLFIRINOX and gemcitabine-based regimens in terms of OS or PFS.

HVEPs with concurrent chemotherapy

Electrochemotherapy for pancreatic cancer

Electroporation (EP) enhances the cytotoxicity of chemotherapeutic drugs, achieving the same therapeutic effect as fewer drugs, thus leading to the development of electrotherapy (ECT). Research has shown that EP increases the toxicity of bleomycin and cisplatin by 5000 and 12 times, respectively. First developed in 1987 and routinely used in 2006, ECT is now used to treat PC (Table 4).

In 2015, Granata et al reported preliminary results using ECT to treat LAPC patients. Thirteen patients received bleomycin ECT without severe AEs during or after surgery. In 2024, Ebron et al reported on the use of ECT on the surface of tumors after surgical excision. Seven patients had no severe complications or mortality, suggesting that a hybrid approach combining surgery and intraoperative ECT is safe and feasible.

Calcium ion electroporation for the treatment of pancreatic cancer

Calcium ion electroporation (CaEP) is an evolving technique that involves the external application of an HVEP while delivering calcium ions intratumorally to kill cancer cells through ATP depletion. CaEP is safer and more effective than conventional chemotherapy or even ECT. Rudno-Rudzi’nska et al evaluated HVEPs alone or combined with CaCl2 as a new strategy. The OS rate was 16 months in the HVEP group and 19 months in the CaEP group. HVEPs may be more effective when calcium ions are added (Table 4).

HVEPs add marginal accentuation: HVEPs with surgery for pancreatic cancer treatment

HVEPs can be used to consolidate treatment after chemotherapy or radiation in LAPC patients to control ILR after prior radical surgery, and to add marginal accentuation (MA) to surgical resection to target minimal residual disease in patients with BRPC (Table 3).

MA with IRE may reduce the risk of positive margins while maintaining safety. This is especially helpful for tumors close to the superior mesenteric artery, celiac trunk, and portal vein, which are difficult to remove surgically. Kwon et al performed pancreatectomy and IRE MA in 48 patients with LAPC or BRPC. Three patients developed local recurrence with a median time of 22.4 months, showing a lower-than-expected local recurrence rate. Martin et al recruited 200 patients with LAPC – 150 treated with IRE alone, and 50 treated with surgery plus IRE MA. The median OS was 28.3 months for individuals who received surgery plus IRE MA versus 23.2 months for individuals who received IRE alone, demonstrating a significant benefit of surgery plus IRE MA.

HVEPs with immunotherapy for pancreatic cancer treatment: unleashing the abscopal effects

As indicated above, the immune response elicited by HVEPs is transient, and residual tumors in clinical practice may contribute to recurrence. Currently, distant progression is the most common type of recurrence after ablation. We need better ways to unleash abscopal effects to treat micrometastatic disease (Tables 4, 5 and Fig. 4). Notably, current studies on HVEP-immunotherapy combinations remain confined to preclinical investigations or small single-center trials. Consequently, large-scale multicenter randomized controlled trials are imperative to rigorously evaluate the efficacy of this combined approach and provide robust clinical evidence.    

HVEPs with adaptive cell therapy

A critical form of immunotherapy is adaptive cell therapy (ACT). ACT involves the infusion of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes or peripheral blood immune cells (usually T cells). In PC patients, compared to IRE alone, IRE combined with NK cells was found to prolong the median OS (13.2 months vs. 11.4 months). There is evidence that the coadministration of NK cells with IRE improves efficacy, although the benefit may be small.

Another study investigated the antitumor effects of using IRE and allogeneic Vγ9Vδ2 T cells in LAPC patients. Vγ9Vδ2 T cells represent a significant subpopulation of peripheral γδ T cells. Unlike αβ T cells (CD4+ and CD8+ T cells), these cells do not need to be recognized by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules but can recognize and kill transformed cells via endogenous tumor-derived pyrophosphates. γδ T cells also express activated NK receptors, including NKp30, NKp44, and NKG2D, which bind specific cancer cell targets. Survival was longer with the combination of allogeneic γδ T-cell therapy than with IRE alone (median OS: 14.5 months vs. 11 months). They also found that patients treated with multiple courses of γδ T-cell infusions had a longer OS (17 months) than those treated with a single course (13.5 months). Thus, γδ T-cell therapy appears to be superior to NK cell therapy, but larger studies are needed to confirm this.

HVEPs with immune checkpoint blockade

Advances in immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy have improved treatment options for chemotherapy-refractory malignancies. However, except for patients with mismatch repair deficiency (MMR-D), which accounts for less than 1% of all pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) cases, and some patients with germline homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) mutations, ICB alone has not shown meaningful efficacy in unselected patients. Overall remission rates (ORRs) were 0% and 3% for single and double ICB with anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4, respectively.

Zhao et al reported that the combination of IRE and anti-PD-1 therapy cured 36-43% of orthotopic PDAC model mice. This suggests that IRE combined with ICB may have therapeutic potential. A phase 1b clinical trial (NCT03080974) preliminarily evaluated IRE plus nivolumab. The median time to progression was 6.3 months, with a current median OS of 18.0 months. IRE can induce the expression of PD-L1 in vitro, explaining the mechanism of the synergistic interaction between ICB and HVEP. Additional clinical trials are ongoing to validate the efficacy of ICB and HVEPs in treating PC.

HVEPs with immunostimulants

In addition to ICB, some immunostimulants activate the immune system. Toll-like receptors (TLRs), C-type lectin receptors (CLRs), NOD-like receptors (NLRs), and RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs) are pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) to which immunostimulants bind. This binding triggers proinflammatory pathway activation and an adaptive immune response. In addition, immunostimulants can bind to costimulatory receptors on T cells, such as ICOS, CD137, OX40, and GITR, to enhance TCR-dependent signaling.

For example, TLR7/8 is widely expressed on DCs and can induce antitumor responses. Imiquimod, a small-molecule agonist of TLR7, stimulates DCs to produce IFN by activating the TLR7-MyD88 signaling pathway. Narayanan et al demonstrated that the combined use of IRE and TLR7 agonists (1V270) and PD-1 inhibitors significantly increased CD8+ T secretion of IFN-γ, increased the M1/M2 ratios, and prolonged mouse survival.

As a member of the tumor necrosis factor receptor (TNFR) superfamily, OX40 is primarily expressed on activated CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, and OX40 binding activates downstream signaling pathways such as nuclear factor-κB (NF-κB), phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K), and protein kinase B (AKT), which control genes regulating T cell proliferation and survival. In 80% of mice, subcutaneous tumors were wholly eradicated within 120 days of treatment with the combination of IRE and OX40 agonists.

HVEPs with DC vaccine and oncolytic virus

Several other forms of immunotherapy can be used in combination with HVEPs. Yang et al conducted a study confirming that IRE ablation can help overcome TME immunosuppression to improve DC vaccine efficacy in an orthotopic KPC PDAC mouse model. Those receiving IRE and DC vaccines lived an average of 77 days longer than those receiving the DC vaccine alone (49 days) or IRE alone (44 days). Additionally, one of the most promising therapeutic approaches is oncolytic virotherapy; it can target and kill cancer cells while causing minimal damage to healthy tissue.

M1 virus targets zinc-finger antiviral protein-deficient cancer cells and induces endoplasmic reticulum stress-mediated apoptosis. Moreover, IRE can kill PC cells through ROS-dependent apoptosis by blocking the PI3K/Akt pathway. Its therapeutic efficacy is synergistic when combined with the M1 virus, which further inhibits tumor growth and prolongs the survival of Panc02 mice.

HVEPs with nanomaterials for pancreatic cancer treatment: increasing damage capacity in a precise manner

While combining HVEPs with chemotherapy or immunotherapy amplifies tumoricidal effects, achieving precise spatial control remains challenging. In the era of precision medicine, drug safety, targeting, and controllability requirements are increasing, and nanocarrier delivery systems show potential advantages in cancer ablative therapy. Rationally designed drug delivery systems can extend the plasma half-life, prevent drug degradation, improve pharmacodynamic and pharmacokinetic features, and reduce off-target toxicity. In addition, nanocarriers can achieve targeted drug delivery and support the stepwise release of multiple drugs. Combining nanomaterials with HVEPs is a future development (Table 4 ). 

Nanomaterials increase the sensitivity of tumor cells to HVEPs

Ablating large tumors entirely within the safe voltage range for the human body is difficult for the HVEP technique. Increasing the voltage to improve ablation efficacy also increases the risk of damaging surrounding tissues. Therefore, reducing the electric field threshold of HVEPs by enhancing the sensitivity of tumor cells to low field strength is necessary. One possible solution is to use surface-active substances, which can enable the more complete ablation of tumor cells at lower field strengths by affecting the surface tension of the cell membrane. This makes it more challenging for the nanopores created by IRE to return to their original state. Samuel M et al demonstrated that the membrane-dissolved polymer 6-amino-hexyl methacrylate (PAHM) enhanced the ability of IRE to kill human PC cells; the improved efficacy of the combination therapy was attributed to PAHM-mediated sensitization of cancer cells to IRE and direct cell killing by PAHM through membrane lysis.

Nanomaterials enable precise drug delivery

Nanoparticles can be designed to suppress immunosuppressive pathways in the TME and carry immunotherapy drugs, thereby reducing adverse drug reactions. Peng et al found that post-IRE infiltrating neutrophils were polarized toward the N2 phenotype, possibly due to TGF-β. Thus, the authors prepared dMSN-SB, a GSH-responsive degradable mesoporous silica nanoparticles carrying TGF-β1 receptor inhibitor galunisertib, which shifted tumor-associated neutrophils (TANs) toward the antitumor N1 phenotype and reduced the toxicity of galunisertib in the gastrointestinal, immune, and bone/cartilage systems. Nanoparticles must produce metabolites that are either safe for humans or that the body can degrade and eliminate. For example, the silica nanoparticles used by Peng et al are nontoxic, odorless, and environmentally compatible, and their high absorption rate and favorable plasticity make them advantageous for treating diseases.

Nanomaterials reduce HVEPs resistance

HVEPs are theoretically effective against all tumor cells. However, differences in sensitivity to electric pulses among individual cells lead to varying treatment outcomes. Resistance to HVEPs is a significant obstacle to improving treatment outcomes and can lead to metastasis, cancer recurrence, and adverse outcomes. Long et al explored the DNA damage repair (DDR) response at the ablation margin after IRE. Therefore, ROS-sensitive polymers loaded with olaparib and AZD0156, M-TK-OA, were prepared. After IRE, M-TK-OA inhibited homologous recombination (HR) and nonhomologous end joining (NHEJ) signal transduction, thereby reducing DDR induction. This combination prolonged survival in Panc02 and KPC-A548 mouse models and induced CD8+ T cell-mediated antitumor effects.

Nanomaterials enable integrated imaging and therapy

Image-guided [magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), CT, and ultrasound] local therapy or drug delivery can be powerful strategies for improving drug activity at a target site. A variety of nanomaterials, such as dendrimers, micelles, carbon nanotubes, superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles, gold nanoparticles, and quantum dots, are particularly beneficial for image-guided therapy because they provide more precise imaging. Han et al developed a novel multifunctional nanoadjuvant (M-NA) with an iron oxide/gold core and a cationic polymer shell via multilayer synthesis; CpG oligodeoxynucleotide (CpG-ODN) was electrostatically complexed on its surface. Both X-ray and MR imaging could be performed with M-NA. Image-guided therapy offers the advantage of accurately injecting the drug into the lesion and monitoring it over time. Although no similar materials are available for integrating imaging therapy and HVEPs for the treatment of PC, such an approach holds great promise.

Challenges and future perspectives

Combining HVEPs with immunotherapy has the potential to enhance abscopal effects, and combining HVEPs with nanomaterials can achieve precise cellular damage while mitigating systemic toxicity. Despite the numerous achievements that have been made, current research still has several outstanding issues to address in the areas of novel mechanism exploration, new strategies for HVEPs with other treatments, and device innovation.

Exploration of novel HVEPs mechanisms

Novel HVEPs resistance mechanisms

The heterogeneous TME may distort the electric field distribution. In regions where electric field strength falls below the cell-killing threshold (RE zones), tumor cells may survive and resume proliferation after stimulation ceases. Investigating tumor cell resistance mechanisms to HVEPs is crucial for preventing recurrence. Resistance mechanisms have been extensively explored in other energy-based therapies. Unfortunately, few studies have examined HVEP resistance recurrence mechanisms in PC.

Ye et al identified autophagy as a potential HVEP resistance mechanism. Residual cancer cells in IRE-treated pancreatic tumors showed upregulated autophagy, enhancing their IRE resistance. Conversely, disrupting HMGB1-mediated autophagy sensitized PC cells to IRE, suggesting a new strategy to overcome HVEP resistance. Beyond intrinsic tumor cell resistance, other cells in the TME may significantly contribute to HVEP resistance. Peng et al discovered that IRE-induced massive neutrophil infiltration, polarized into a pro-tumor phenotype by TGF-β, promoted tumor recurrence. However, these studies lack systematicity, revealing only the “tip of the iceberg” regarding HVEP resistance mechanisms.

Metabolic reprogramming, is related to glucose, lipid, and amino acid metabolism and other metabolic pathways closely linked to tumor development and radiotherapy resistance. For example, Yang et al demonstrated that radiotherapy can enhance glycolysis in PC cells. This leads to the secretion of high lactate levels that activate MDSCs in the microenvironment via the GPR81/mTOR/HIF-1α/STAT3 pathway. These findings provide important insight into the mechanism of HVEP resistance in PC.

Complex signaling pathways induced by HVEPs

Although factors such as the pulse duration and intensity of HVEPs and tumor tissue specificity may influence ICD efficacy, systematic studies quantifying these impacts are currently lacking. This gap may compromise the reproducibility of HVEP-induced ICD. Future studies should systematically investigate how HVEP treatment parameters influence ICD efficacy. Furthermore, the complex interplay among immune factors and the precise mechanisms underlying HVEP-mediated immune modulation remain largely unexplored, significantly hindering patient selection for HVEP-based therapies. Recent studies have shown that the presence of TNF-α and IFN-γ in tumors activates various immunosuppressive pathways, including indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO-1). The use of IDO inhibitors has been shown to improve the efficacy of IRE by increasing the ratio of intracellular CTLs to Tregs. Overcoming cytokine-induced immunosuppression is a new challenge. Qian et al reported that NK cells attracted CD103+ DCs to tumors treated with nsPEF through CCL5. These CD103+ DCs then released IL-12 to help NK cells release CCL5, which increased immune stimulation. Future research should leverage multi-omics technologies to investigate HVEP-induced remodeling of signaling pathways and the interactions between CAFs, immune cells, and other stromal components.

New strategies for HVEPs with other treatments

Optimizing combination drug selection

Given the promising efficacy of HVEPs combined with immunotherapeutic agents, exploring novel immunotherapies as potential HVEP partners may yield synergistic outcomes. For instance, inhibitors of colony-stimulating factor 1 receptor (CSF1R) and focal adhesion kinase (FAK) in macrophages, inhibitors of CCR2 in MDSCs, targeting CAFs with paricalcitol, using granulocyte–macrophage colony–stimulating factor (GM–CSF) vaccines for APCs, and activating CD137 with agonists have been employed for the treatment of PC patients. In addition, drugs that target the newly discovered ICBs (LAG3, TIM3, TIGIT, and BTLA) have a wide range of potential applications. These drugs could be encapsulated in nanoparticles and combined with HVEPs to increase their efficacy. While current combination regimens predominantly utilize conventional chemotherapeutics, they face efficacy limitations. Optimizing drug selection through the following strategies is crucial for identifying novel drug combinations. First, integrate high-throughput drug screening platforms with artificial intelligence computational models (e.g., molecular docking, QSAR analysis) to systematically identify novel targeted agents and immunomodulators exhibiting synergistic mechanisms. Second, establish predictive biomarker-guided dosing models based on specific tumor molecular subtypes (e.g., KRAS mutation status in PC) and HVEP treatment parameters, enabling dynamic calibration of therapeutic regimens.

Optimizing treatment scheduling and dosage selection

Defining the precise timing for administering combination therapies is critical. Consequently, several key issues warrant attention: First, in traditional PC treatment, improper sequencing of chemotherapy can deplete or inactivate immune cells within the TME, compromising therapeutic efficacy. Furthermore, as HVEPs rapidly activate immune responses, co-administered drugs must be released within this specific time window to maximize their effect. Therefore, the optimal timing for drug administration following HVEPs requires thorough investigation. Second, HVEP-induced electric fields can alter the drug release kinetics of certain nanomaterials, presenting a dual nature. On the one hand, electroresponsive materials can be developed to enable controllable drug release subsequent to HVEP application. On the other hand, the complex interplay between HVEP parameters (e.g., electric field strength, pulse number, frequency) and drug dosage necessitates in-depth exploration.

Implementing large-scale, multicenter, prospective clinical trials

Current clinical trial evidence supporting HVEP-based combinations is largely low-grade. Validating the potential clinical benefits of HVEPs combined with other therapies requires large-scale, multicenter, prospective clinical trials. PANFIRE III (NCT04612530) was designed to evaluate the safety and activity of two experimental regimens, IRE + nivolumab and CpG + IRE + nivolumab, in patients with oligometastatic PC and to compare these regimens to control nivolumab monotherapy. In addition, a single-arm clinical trial (NCT03080974) was designed to evaluate the activity and safety of IRE and nivolumab (Table 6). Additionally, advancing clinical trials evaluating HVEPs combined with nanomaterials is paramount. Currently studied nanoparticles are predominantly TME-responsive drug carriers, often featuring complex multi-drug structures that present substantial barriers to large-scale manufacturing. There is an urgent need to establish standardized, scalable synthesis processes for nanoparticles through dedicated research and to conduct extensive clinical trials. 

Development of new equipment

Shortcomings of existing equipment

Regarding clinical applications, NanoKnife has several drawbacks. For example, a long electrical pulse duration (1 ~ 100 ms) may induce muscle contraction, and there is a risk of arrhythmia. During surgery, neuromuscular blocking agents should be administered to patients under general anesthesia, and electrocardiographic synchronizers must be used for IRE pulse delivery with cardiac inactivity. IRE is still associated with a risk of severe AEs; therefore, new technology development is a promising solution to this problem.

Innovations in the equipment itself

High-frequency irreversible electroporation (H-FIRE) is a novel technique that uses high-frequency, bipolar bursts. Rectangular pulses with an average frequency of approximately 500 kHz create an even and predictable electric field distribution. Compared to the conventional NanoKnife, H-FIRE eliminates signs of muscle tremors and system noise, even during the highest energy burst; it does not require cardiac synchronization or increase arrhythmia risk. This technology has been used to treat prostate cancer.

If the pulse length reaches the nanosecond range and is shorter than the charge time of the cell membrane, charge accumulation on the cell membrane surface will not be possible. As a result, the applied electric field is mainly received by intracellular organelle membranes, which can induce a series of intracellular changes, such as phosphatidylserine externalization, caspase and calpain activation, cytochrome C release, and DNA damage. The first human trial of a nanosecond pulsed electric field (nsPEF) for the skin has been reported 150. In an animal PC model, nsPEF inhibited PC growth and reduced multidrug resistance. Recently, Peng et al noted that conventional microsecond pulsed electric field (μsPEF) are typically used for cell membrane penetration, whereas nsPEF is used to cause cytoskeletal disorganization and cellular damage. The combination of nμPEF is a promising technology that can disrupt cell membranes and intracellular structures, inducing various modes of cell death.

Innovations in ablation guidance

Because the pancreas is obstructed by other organs, conventional acupuncture can be difficult and harmful; therefore, endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) is a promising alternative. EUS has proven to be minimally invasive, safe, and highly effective for RFA, with superior tumor visualization and localization and real-time imaging guidance. Therefore, EUS-HVEP is equally promising. Additionally, the virtual navigation-guided technique is a promising method. This technique uses computer-assisted image navigation systems to help doctors accurately locate and operate on target areas by comparing real-time images with preobtained anatomical information. The aim is to achieve precise treatment.

Conclusions

Although HVEPs represent an effective tumor treatment modality, several challenges remain. Key obstacles include incomplete ablation and post-procedural tumor recurrence. In the precision medicine era, where research perspectives are shifting from macro- to micro-scale analysis, HVEPs hold potential to potentiate immunotherapy by converting immunologically “cold” tumors into “hot” tumors through modulation of the tumor immune microenvironment. Furthermore, combining HVEPs with nanomaterials shows significant therapeutic promise. Addressing these opportunities requires: (1) developing next-generation equipment, (2) designing novel combined immunotherapeutic strategies, and (3) advancing clinical translation of nanomaterials. Through large-scale clinical trials and multi-institutional collaborations, HVEPs may ultimately bridge localized ablation and systemic antitumor immunity.
