# Oncolytic virotherapy potentiates chemo-PD-1 immunotherapy by engaging chemo-resistant bystander CD8+ T cells.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: The efficacy of combined chemotherapy and programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) is constrained by the collateral cytotoxicity of chemotherapy toward proliferating tumor-specific CD8+ T (TTST) cells, a population indispensable for antitumor immunity. This study aimed to overcome this limitation by targeting a potential chemotherapy-resistant immune cell reservoir.

**METHODS**: By using the murine acutely resolved lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) infection and tumor models (colorectal cancer and melanoma), we characterized the susceptibility of CD8+ TTST and virus-specific bystander memory CD8+ T (TBYS) cells to platinum-based chemotherapy-induced cytotoxicity. We next evaluated the antitumor efficacy and underlying mechanisms of combined chemotherapy and TBYS cell-targeted oncolytic virus therapy (OV-BYTE) in tumor-bearing mice with prior LCMV infection or SARS-CoV-2 vaccination. Finally, we assessed the antitumor efficacy of the triple combination regimen (including OV-BYTE, chemotherapy, and PD-1 ICB) in both murine colorectal cancer model and patient-derived colorectal cancer organoid.

**RESULTS**: We first demonstrated that within the tumor microenvironment, CD8+ TTST cells are highly susceptible to platinum-based chemotherapy, whereas CD8+ TBYS cells constitute a quiescent, chemo-resistant population. Leveraging this, CD8+ TBYS-targeted OV-BYTE therapy synergized with chemotherapy to control tumorigenesis in multiple murine models. Mechanistically, this dual combination directly engaged the CD8+ TBYS cell reservoir for tumor killing, which was accompanied by the restoration of CD8+ TTST cell function via reduced apoptotic susceptibility and acquisition of a polyfunctional, effector-like state. Consequently, integrating OV-BYTE into the standard chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen resulted in improved antitumor efficacy in both preclinical and patient-derived tumor models.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our study establishes the chemotherapy-resistant CD8+ TBYS cell niche as a pivotal therapeutic target. By engaging this target, OV-BYTE emerges as a potent combinatorial agent that successfully circumvents a core limitation of standard chemo-immunotherapy, thus offering a rationally designed and translatable strategy to advance combination cancer therapy.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

The combination of chemotherapy and programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) is a cornerstone first-line regimen for multiple advanced cancers. Its efficacy, however, is intrinsically limited by chemotherapy’s indiscriminate depletion of proliferating tumor-specific CD8+ T (TTST) cells that PD-1 ICB aims to expand.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Our study addresses this constraint by leveraging a chemotherapy-resistant bystander CD8+ T (TBYS) cell reservoir. Specifically, we developed a tailored oncolytic virus therapy (termed OV-BYTE) that engaged CD8+ TBYS cells for tumor killing and converted CD8+ TTST cells from a chemotherapy-depleted, exhausted state into a polyfunctional state with strengthened resistance to chemotherapy. Further integration of OV-BYTE into the standard chemo-PD-1 regimen demonstrated superior antitumor efficacy across both preclinical and patient-derived tumor models.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

This study presents a translatable strategy to augment standard chemo-PD-1 immunotherapy by engaging the CD8+ TBYS cell reservoir, thereby addressing the clinical bottleneck of chemotherapy-induced T-cell damage and providing a new avenue for more effective, next-generation combination therapies.

Background

 The combination of chemotherapy and programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) has demonstrated significant antitumor efficacy across a spectrum of malignancies. One key mechanistic rationale for this therapeutic synergy is that chemotherapy eliminates rapidly proliferating tumor cells and releases tumor antigens to prime tumor-specific CD8+ T (TTST) cells, while PD-1 ICB sustains this antitumor response by promoting the proliferation and effector function of the activated CD8+ TTST population. However, CD8+ TTST cells in the tumor microenvironment (TME), especially those driven into a high-proliferating status by PD-1 ICB, are at high risk of chemotherapy-induced cytotoxicity. This intrinsic pharmacological conflict inevitably undermines the full potential of the combination regimen and highlights an imperative for novel strategies to circumvent this limitation.

In contrast to chemo-susceptible CD8+ TTST cells, previous studies have identified that virus-specific memory CD8+ T (TMEM) cells persist after cytotoxic chemotherapy. These virus-specific CD8+ TMEM cells, evoked by either natural infection or vaccination, vastly migrate into the TME and persistently retain their memory features. Due to their lack of tumor specificity, TME-infiltrating virus-specific CD8+ TMEM cells are referred to as bystander CD8+ T (TBYS) cells and can be durably engaged to achieve antitumor therapeutic gains. Among those TBYS-targeted therapies, we reported an engineered oncolytic virus encoding TBYS cell epitopes (OV-BYTE) that redirected TME-infiltrating TBYS cells for tumor killing. Moreover, the OV-BYTE therapy showed synergistic efficacy with PD-1 ICB in reinforcing antitumor immunity of CD8+ TTST cells. However, it remains unknown whether the potential chemo-resistant nature of TBYS cells could entail the OV-BYTE therapy with a unique therapeutic advantage in synergizing with chemotherapy and restoring blunted CD8+ TTST cell response under chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen.

In this study, we first found that while TME-infiltrating CD8+ TTST cells were of high susceptibility to platinum-based chemotherapy-induced cytotoxicity, CD8+ TBYS cells represented a quiescent population that persisted through such treatment. Leveraging the chemo-resistant nature of CD8+ TBYS cells, we next provided proof-of-concept that TBYS-targeted OV-BYTE therapy synergized with platinum-based chemotherapy in controlling tumorigenesis in multiple preclinical tumor models. Mechanistically, this combination not only directly engaged the chemo-resistant TBYS cell reservoir but also restored the antitumor response of chemo-blunted CD8+ TTST cells through reduced susceptibility to chemotherapy-induced apoptosis. Furthermore, the combination of OV-BYTE and chemotherapy synergistically promoted the acquisition of a polyfunctional, effector-like state among CD8+ TTST cells. Most importantly, by using both preclinical murine tumor models and clinically relevant patient-derived colorectal cancer organoid, we demonstrated that integrating OV-BYTE into the standard chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen yielded superior therapeutic outcomes. Therefore, our study established OV-BYTE as a potent combinatorial agent capable of potentiating chemo-immunotherapy by engaging chemo-resistant CD8+ TBYS cells.

Methods

Mice

C57BL/6 (RRID: IMSR_JAX:000664), BALB/c (RRID: IMSR_JAX:000651), OT-1 transgenic (RRID: IMSR_JAX:003831), CD45.1+ congenic (RRID: IMSR_JAX:002014) mice were purchased from the Jackson Laboratories. P14 transgenic mice were gifted from Dr Rafi Ahmed (Emory University). All mice used in animal experiments were 6–8 weeks of age, and both sexes were included, except that only female BALB/c mice were used for the engraftment of 4T1 breast cancer cells. All mice were housed under specific pathogen-free conditions, maintained on a 12-hour light-12-hour dark cycle at a controlled temperature of 20–22°C and humidity of 40–60%. All mouse housing and experiments were conducted in accordance with the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committees of the Third Military Medical University under protocol number AMUWEC20234674.

Viral infection and protein immunization

The lymphocytic choriomeningitis virus (LCMV) Armstrong strain was generously provided by Dr Rafi Ahmed (Emory University) and propagated in our laboratory. For LCMV Armstrong infection, C57BL/6 mice were infected intraperitoneally with 2×105 plaque-forming units of LCMV Armstrong strain. For receptor-binding domain (RBD) immunization, BALB/c mice were immunized intranasally with two doses of 10 µg SARS-CoV-2 RBD protein-mFC tag (Sino Biological, 40592-V05H) conjugated to 10 µg CpG ODN 1826 (Invitrogen, tlrl-1826) at an interval of 2 weeks.

Mouse tumor models

B16-F10 (B16; RRID: CVCL_0159), MC38 (RRID: CVCL_B288) and 4T1 (RRID: CVCL_0125) cells were purchased from ATCC. MC38-OVA (RRID: CVCL_XJ96) cells were purchased from the Shanghai Model Organisms Center. B16-GP cells were generated by CRISPR-Cas9-mediated insertion of the LCMV glycoprotein (GP) gene into the genome of B16-F10 cells. Cell lines were authenticated by flow cytometry on receipt and every 3 months thereafter to ensure identity. Additionally, cells were routinely tested for Mycoplasma contamination using Mycoplasma PCR Detection Kit (Abcam, ab289834) before critical experiments. All tumor cell lines were cultured in complete Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM; Gibco, 11995065) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Gibco, A5256701) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Gibco, 15140122) in a humidified incubator at 37°C and 5% CO2. For B16-GP cells, additional 100 U/mL puromycin (Gibco, A1113803) was added to the culture medium. For subcutaneous tumor models, mice were implanted with 5×105 MC38, MC38-OVA, B16 or B16-GP cells in the left flank. For the breast cancer model, 3×105 4T1 cells were implanted into the mammary fat pad of female BALB/c mice. Tumors were measured every 2–3 days and the tumor volumes were determined in two dimensions with a caliper and calculated using the formula (length×width2)/2. Mice were sacrificed at the indicated time points or when tumors reached the endpoint volume of 2,000 mm3.

Adoptive transfer of T cells

For the LCMV Armstrong infection model, 5×104 naïve splenic P14 CD8+ T cells were adoptively transferred into recipient mice 1 day prior to infection. For the MC38-OVA tumor models, 5×105 naïve splenic OT-1 CD8+ T cells were adoptively transferred into recipient mice 1 day prior to tumor engraftment. For the B16-GP tumor model, 5×105 naïve splenic P14 CD8+ T cells were adoptively transferred into recipient mice 1 day prior to tumor engraftment.

Administration of OV, chemotherapy drugs and anti-programmed death-ligand 1 antibody in mice

For OV treatment, constructions and intratumoral administrations of all oncolytic Newcastle disease virus (NDV) types used in this study were previously described. Tumor-engrafted mice were daily administered with 2×106 plaque-forming unit of NDV-wild-type (WT) or NDV-GP or NDV-RBD in 50 µL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) on days 5 to 11 post tumorigenesis via intratumoral route. For chemotherapy, tumor-engrafted mice were administered a chemotherapy regimen consisted of cisplatin and pemetrexed on days 5, 8, 11, 14 and 17 after tumorigenesis. Cisplatin (Yuanye Bio-Technology, CAS#15663-27-1) was dissolved in 0.9% saline solution and administered intraperitoneally at a dose of 2.5 mg/kg. Pemetrexed (GLPBIO, No. GC11610) was dissolved in PBS and administered intraperitoneally at a dose of 300 mg/kg. For PD-1 ICB treatment, mice received intraperitoneal injections of 200 µg of anti-programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) antibody (Bio X Cell, BE0101, RRID: AB_10949073).

Isolation of lymphocytes

For TME-infiltrating lymphocytes isolation, tumors were dissected, mechanically minced and treated with type 1 collagenase (Diamond, A004194) for 45 min at 37°C. Following digestion, the tumor suspensions were passed through 70 µm nylon mesh filter (BD Falcon, 352350) to obtain single-cell preparations. Subsequently, TME-infiltrating lymphocytes were further enriched using a discontinuous Percoll density gradient (Cytiva, 17-0891-09), harvesting cells from the interface, which were then washed two times prior to downstream applications. Splenocytes were obtained by gently pressing the spleen through a 70 µm nylon mesh filter (BD Falcon, 352350) to produce single-cell suspensions.

Flow cytometry

For surface staining, lymphocytes isolated from tumors were resuspended and stained with flow cytometry staining buffer (PBS containing 2% heat-inactivated FBS and 2 mM EDTA). For cytokine detection, cells were stimulated with 0.2 µg/mL of OVA257-264 peptide, brefeldin A (BD Biosciences, 555029) and Monensin (BD Biosciences, 554724) at 37°C for 5 hours. Intracellular cytokine staining for interleukin (IL)-2, interferon (IFN)-γ and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α was performed with the Cytofix/Cytoperm Fixation/Permeabilization Kit (BD Biosciences, 554714). Staining of Ki67, Bcl-2, Bim and Caspase 3 was performed using the Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set (Invitrogen, 00-5523-00) and then incubated with fluorochrome-conjugated antibodies of specific transcription factors. Apoptosis was assessed using the Annexin V Kit (eBioscience, 88-8102-72) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. For 5-Bromo-2’-deoxyuridine (BrdU) incorporation, mice received BrdU (100 mg/kg, intraperitoneal injection or i.p.; Sigma, B5002-1G) daily for 1 week before sacrifice according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Subsequently, cells were stained with an allophycocyanin (APC)-conjugated anti-BrdU antibody using the eBioscience BrdU Staining Kit for Flow Cytometry APC (eBioscience, clone BU20A, 8817-6600-42, RRID: AB_11040534). The following antibodies were used: anti-CD8α (BioLegend, clone 53–6.7, RRID:AB_2563057; BD Biosciences, clone YTS156, RRID: AB_3687647), anti-CD4 (BioLegend, clone RM4-5, RRID: AB_893325), anti-CD3 (BioLegend, clone 17A2, RRID: AB_2242784), anti-CD44 (BioLegend, clone IM7, RRID: AB_312959), anti-CD45.1 (BioLegend, clone A20, RRID: AB_493733), anti-CD45.2 (BioLegend, clone 104, RRID: AB_492872), anti-CD25 (BioLegend, clone PC61, RRID: AB_312861), anti-PD-1 (BioLegend, clone 29F.1A12, RRID: AB_2561447), anti-CD39 (BioLegend, clone Duha59, RRID: AB_2563394), anti-CD11c (BioLegend, clone N418, RRID:AB_313777), anti-CD11b (BioLegend, clone M1/70, RRID:AB_493705), anti-F4/80 (BioLegend, clone BM8, RRID: AB_893502), anti-B220 (BioLegend, clone RA3-6B2, RRID: AB_893353), anti-I-Ab (BioLegend, clone AF6-120.1, RRID: AB_10574160), anti-Ly6C (BioLegend, clone HK1.4, RRID: AB_1186135), anti-Ly6G (BioLegend, clone 1A8, RRID: AB_2251161), anti-XCR1 (BioLegend, clone ZET, RRID: AB_2563932), anti-IFN-γ (BioLegend, clone XMG1.2, RRID: AB_315402), anti-TNF-α (BD Biosciences, clone MP6-XT22, RRID: AB_3684949), anti-IL-2 (BD Biosciences, clone JES6-5H4, RRID: AB_1645256), anti-Ki67 (BioLegend, clone 16A8, RRID: AB_2629531), anti-Bim (CST, clone C34C5, RRID: AB_1030947), anti-Bcl-2 (BD Biosciences, clone 3F11, RRID: AB_396457), anti-Caspase-3 (BD Biosciences, clone C92-605.rMAb, RRID: AB_3685826), H-2Kb Rpl18 Neoepitope tetramer (KILTFDRL; MBL Life science, TC-5116–4) and anti-Foxp3 (BioLegend, clone MF-14, RRID: AB_2750493). Flow cytometry data were acquired on a BD LSRFortessa (BD Biosciences) using FACSDiva software (V.7.0, RRID: SCR_001456) and analyzed using FlowJo (V.10.8.1, RRID: SCR_008520). Cell sorting experiments were performed with the BD FACSAria III (BD Biosciences).

scRNA-seq library preparation and sequencing

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) libraries were prepared using the BD Rhapsody Express Single-Cell Analysis System in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, individual cells were isolated into nanowells using a microfluidic workflow, followed by cell lysis and reverse transcription incorporating cell-specific barcodes and unique molecular identifiers. The resulting complementary DNA was PCR-amplified, then processed through enzymatic fragmentation, adapter ligation, and purification to generate sequencing-ready libraries. Library size distribution and concentration were assessed using a Bioanalyzer and Qubit, respectively. Libraries were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 instrument (paired-end, 150 bp), allocating one lane per sample. Raw count matrices were obtained by aligning reads to the mm10 reference genome with Cell Ranger (V.6.1.1).

scRNA-seq data processing and analysis

All downstream analyses were performed in R. Raw data were imported into Seurat (V.4.0) for quality control. Cells were retained if they had >1,000 detected genes and <10% mitochondrial read counts, and doublets were identified and excluded using scDblFinder. After filtering, datasets were merged in Seurat, log-normalized with NormalizeData, and scaled using ScaleData. Dimensionality reduction was carried out by Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) using significant principal components selected with ElbowPlot, and clustering resolution was guided by clustree (RRID:SCR_016293). Differential expression was assessed using Seurat’s FindMarkers. To obtain robust marker genes for subpopulation annotation, we applied a stringent cut-off of log fold change (logFC) >0.5 and adjusted p value<0.05 (with an initial pre-filter of logFC >0.01). Functional enrichment was evaluated by Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) on upregulated genes using the clusterProfiler package (RRID:SCR_016884). Differentiation trajectories were inferred by pseudotime analysis with Monocle 3. In addition, functional states along trajectories were quantified by scoring clusters against predefined effector gene sets and calculating the proportions of cells across pseudotime phases.

Organoid culture from colorectal cancer tissues

Patients with colorectal cancer (CRC) were enrolled at Xinqiao Hospital (Army Medical University). All diagnoses were pathologically confirmed. Prior to surgery, each patient was provided with written informed consent for the collection of tissue samples for research purposes. The method of tumor organoid culture was previously described. Briefly, fresh CRC tissues were collected in ice-cold Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 medium (Gibco, 11875093) with 1% penicillin-streptomycin. Samples were minced into 1–5 mm3 fragments and resuspended in Advanced DMEM/F12 (Gibco, 12634010). After sequential filtration through 100 µm and 40 µm cell strainers, the 40–100 µm fraction was collected, pelleted at 1,500 rpm for 5 min at 4°C, and resuspended in cold growth factor-reduced Matrigel. Approximately 50–60 µL of the Matrigel-cell mixture was seeded as domes in ultra-low attachment 48-well plates, solidified at 37°C, and overlaid with CRC organoid culture medium for culture. The CRC organoid culture medium consisted of Advanced DMEM/F12 supplemented with 1% penicillin-streptomycin, 1% GlutaMAX (Gibco, 35050061), 10 mM HEPES (Gibco, 15630080), 1 mM N-acetyl-L-cysteine (Selleckchem, S1623), 5 mM nicotinamide (Selleckchem, S1899), 10 nM recombinant human (Leu15)-gastrin I (Sigma, G9145), 50 ng/mL recombinant human EGF (Gibco, AF-100–15), 100 ng/mL recombinant human Noggin (Gibco, 120–10C), 10 µM forskolin (Selleckchem, S2449), 5 µM A83-01 (TOCRIS, 2939), 10 µM Y27632 (Selleckchem, S1049), 3 µM SB202190 (Selleckchem, S1077), 3 nM dexamethasone (Selleckchem, S1322), and 100 ng/mL Primocin (InvivoGen, ant-pm-05).

Isolation of PBMCs and co-culture with CRC organoids

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated using Lymphoprep density gradient centrifugation (STEMCELL, 18061), cryopreserved in liquid nitrogen, and thawed 1 day prior to use. Cells were cultured overnight in ImmunoCult-XF T Cell Expansion Medium (STEMCELL, 10981) supplemented with 150 U/mL IL-2 at a density of 2×106 cells/mL. CRC organoids were recovered, counted, and resuspended at 5×104 cells/mL. Co-culture was performed at an effector-to-target ratio of 20:1 under eight different treatment conditions, including PD-1 antibody (10 µg/mL; nivolumab, Bio X Cell, SIM0003, RRID: AB_2894724), chemotherapy drugs comprising oxaliplatin (OXP, 10 µM; Selleckchem, S1224) and 5-fluorouracil (5-FU, 10 µM; Selleckchem, S1209), NDV expressing H1N1 NP/NDV-RBD (2×106 PFU/mL), and their combinations.

Fluorescence staining and quantitative evaluation of response index

After 5 days of co-culture, CRC organoid viability was assessed by Calcein-AM/PI (Yeasen, 40 747ES76) and Hoechst 33 342 (10 μg/mL; Beyotime, C1022) staining. Organoids were incubated with dyes at 37°C for 30 min in the dark and imaged using a 10×objective on an Olympus SpinSR confocal microscope. Image analysis was performed using Imaris software (RRID:SCR_007370). Tumor cells were identified by Hoechst staining and defined as cells with a diameter of ~12–13 µm. Viable cells were Hoechst+/Calcein-AM+, and dead cells were Hoechst+/PI+. The response index was calculated as the percentage of dead tumor cells among total tumor cells.

Data availability

The scRNA-seq data of MC38-OVA TME-infiltrating OT-1 TTST cells from four groups (control, CT, NDV-GP and CT+NDV GP) have been deposited in the Gene Expression Omnibus and are available (GSE314954). Public scRNA-seq data reanalyzed here are available under accession codes (GSE290082, DOI: 10.1186/s12943-025-02311-z; GSE222002, DOI: 10.1038/s43018-024-00760-x). Statistical analyses

No statistical method was employed to predetermine the sample size; however, the sample sizes used were comparable to those reported in previous studies. All collected data were included in the analyses without exclusion. The experiments were conducted without randomization, and the investigators were not blinded to group allocation during the experimentation and outcome assessment. All statistical analyses were conducted using Prism V.10.0 (GraphPad Software, RRID:SCR_002798). Comparisons between two unpaired groups were analyzed by an unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-test. Comparisons between two paired groups were analyzed by a paired two-tailed Student’s t-test. For comparisons involving three or more groups, one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) followed by Tukey’s post hoc test was applied. Tumor growth curves were analyzed using two-way ANOVA. Survival analysis was performed using the log-rank (Mantel-Cox) test. Data are presented as individual points with mean±SEM. A p value of <0.05 (p<0.05) was considered statistically significant, and non-significant results were denoted as ns (p>0.05).

Results

CD8+ TBYS cells represent a highly quiescent T-cell population in the TME

The TME harbors a diverse repertoire of CD8+ T cells, with both TTST and TBYS populations representing critical cellular targets for tumor immunotherapies (for definitions and key features, see table 1).

While the distinct functional states of CD8+ TTST and TBYS are established, their potentially divergent cellular fitness (notably in proliferative capacity and survival potential) has not been characterized. Towards this end, congenically marked (CD45.1+CD45.2+) naïve P14 CD8+ T cells specifically recognizing LCMV GP H-2Db GP33-41 epitope were adoptively transferred into naïve C57BL/6 mice (CD45.2+), followed by acutely resolved LCMV Armstrong infection. After the P14 TMEM cell response was established, these recipients received another adoptive transfer of congenically marked (CD45.1+CD45.2−) naïve OT-1 CD8+ T cells specifically recognizing the ovalbumin (OVA) epitope H-2Kb OVA257-264 on day 29 after infection, and were then subcutaneously engrafted with syngeneic MC38 colon adenocarcinoma cells expressing OVA (MC38-OVA) on day 30 after infection, thus establishing tumor-specific OT-1 TTST cells and tumor-non-specific P14 TBYS cells within the same TME (figure 1A). On day 15 post tumorigenesis, we found that OT-1 TTST cells exhibited an expected phenotype of tumor antigen specificity, as demonstrated by abundant PD-1 and CD39 expression. By contrast, P14 TBYS cells of tumor-non-specificity showed negligible expression of these two molecules (online supplemental Figure S1A and S1B), confirming the successful establishment of our model.

To assess proliferation rates of OT-1 TTST cells and P14 TBYS cells within the MC38-OVA TME, we first detected their expression of Ki67, a nuclear protein present in cycling or recently-divided cells. Despite their potential for exhaustion, OT-1 TTST cells showed higher Ki67 expression than the memory-featured P14 TBYS cells under sustained tumor antigen stimulation (figure 1B,C), indicating a more proliferative state in the former CD8+ T-cell population. We next tracked cumulative proliferation capacity of OT-1 TTST cells and P14 TBYS cells by exploiting the BrdU assay. Following daily BrdU pulses from days 9 to 14 after MC38-OVA tumor engraftment (online supplemental Figure S1C), assessment on day 15 demonstrated a higher cumulative BrdU incorporation in OT-1 TTST cells than that in P14 TBYS cells (online supplemental Figure S1D and S1E), confirming the proliferation advantage of OT-1 TTST cells over P14 TBYS cells. We subsequently investigated the survival fitness between the aforementioned OT-1 TTST cells and P14 TBYS cells by profiling their expression of the anti-apoptosis molecule Bcl-2 and its pro-apoptotic counterpart Bim. Indeed, OT-1 TTST cells exhibited a greater propensity for apoptosis, as evidenced by an increase in Bcl-2+Bim+ cells and a concurrent decrease in Bcl-2+Bim− ones in OT-1 TTST cells compared with that in P14 TBYS cells (figure 1D–F). Therefore, these findings indicate that while OT-1 TTST cells are in a state of higher proliferation but concurrently primed for apoptosis, P14 TBYS cells reside in a more quiescent state characterized by lower proliferation yet an enhanced survival potential.

To exclude the potential confounding variable of different epitope affinity between OT-1 and P14 CD8+ T cells, we sought to determine the cellular fitness of P14 CD8+ T cells bearing an identical T-cell receptor when they were engaged as tumor-specific versus tumor-non-specific cell states. To this end, congenic naïve P14 CD8+ T cells were adoptively transferred into C57BL/6 mice, followed by subcutaneous engraftment of syngeneic B16F10 melanoma cells expressing LCMV GP (B16-GP) to establish the P14 TTST model. Alternatively, to establish the P14 TBYS model, mice receiving adoptive transfer of congenic P14 CD8+ T cells were first infected with LCMV Armstrong, followed by subcutaneous engraftment of parental B16F10 melanoma cells on day 30 post infection (figure 1G). On day 15 after tumorigenesis, with tumor antigen specificity being confirmed (Online supplemental Figure S1F and S1G), P14 TTST cells showed heightened proliferation and apoptotic susceptibility, whereas their TBYS counterparts displayed a more quiescent state (figure 1H–L). To further corroborate our conclusions, we integrated external scRNA-seq profiles of TME-infiltrating P14 TTST cells from murine tumor models (including melanoma and pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma) expressing LCMV GP33-41 epitope, along with a scRNA-seq profile of P14 TBYS cells from the MC38 TME. Consistently, GSEA revealed that genes upregulated in P14 TTST cells were linked to proactive immune processes including T-cell proliferation, T-cell activation and activation of immune response (figure 1M). By contrast, the gene signature in P14 TBYS cells pointed toward a state of quiescence and survival, as evidenced by terms including negative regulation of cell proliferation and negative regulation of apoptosis (figure 1M). Collectively, these results identify TME-infiltrating CD8+ TBYS cells as a population of high quiescence, a state standing in direct contrast to the proliferative and activated phenotype of CD8+ TTST cells.

CD8+ TBYS cells are refractory and persist through platinum-based chemotherapy-induced apoptosis

Given T cells with elevated proliferation rates are more susceptible to the cytotoxicity of conventional chemotherapeutics, we next aimed to explore the cellular fitness of TME-infiltrating CD8+ TTST and TBYS cells in the condition of platinum-based chemotherapy. Towards this end, MC38-OVA tumor-engrafted C57BL/6 recipients bearing congenic OT-1 TTST cells and P14 TBYS cells were treated with a combination of cisplatin and pemetrexed. This platinum-based chemotherapy regimen is a widely used clinical standard and has been shown to be effective against the MC38 tumor model and was administered on days 5, 8, 11, 14 and 17 after MC38-OVA engraftment (figure 2A). On day 20 post tumorigenesis, we observed that both the frequency and number of TME-infiltrating OT-1 TTST cells were largely compromised by platinum-based chemotherapeutics, whereas the P14 TBYS population was fully preserved (figure 2B,C). We reasoned that this disparity might stem from their distinct apoptotic thresholds as described in figure 1. Indeed, quantification of Bcl-2 and Bim revealed that a pre-existing, higher Bim-to-Bcl-2 ratio in OT-1 TTST cells was further exacerbated by chemotherapy; meanwhile, the Bim-to-Bcl-2 ratio in P14 TBYS cells was relatively lower and unaffected by chemotherapy (figure 2D–F), thus providing a molecular basis for the differential susceptibility to chemotherapy-induced apoptosis between these two CD8+ T-cell populations.

Having established that platinum-based chemotherapy disrupted the Bcl-2/Bim balance in TTST but not TBYS cells, we next assessed the key apoptotic executioner, caspase-3, to definitively determine the engagement of the apoptotic execution machinery. Echoing the Bcl-2/Bim expression pattern, caspase-3 expression in OT-1 TTST cells was higher than that in P14 TBYS cells and was further augmented on chemotherapy, whereas caspase-3 expression in P14 TBYS cells remained low regardless of chemotherapy (figure 2G,H), indicating that platinum-based chemotherapy initiated distinct engagement of apoptotic execution machinery between TTST and TBYS cells. We then sought to determine their functional apoptotic outcome by exploiting a co-staining strategy of Annexin V and 7-AAD. Consistently, OT-1 TTST cells demonstrated higher fractions of early apoptotic (Annexin V+7-AAD−), late apoptotic (Annexin V+7-AAD+), and dead (Annexin V−7-AAD+) cells compared with P14 TBYS cells (figure 2I–L). On chemotherapy, the frequencies of late apoptosis and cell death were markedly increased in OT-1 TTST cells, whereas the P14 TBYS population was barely affected (figure 2I–L). Taken together, these data indicate that while CD8+ TTST cells are susceptible to platinum-based chemotherapy-induced apoptosis, CD8+ TBYS cells are refractory and persist through this treatment.

CD8+ TBYS cell-targeted OV-BYTE therapy synergizes with platinum-based chemotherapy in controlling tumor progression

Considering the natural chemo-resistance of TME-infiltrating CD8+ TBYS cells, we envisioned that engaging this population through targeted immunotherapy could unlock synergistic antitumor functions in conjunction with platinum-based chemotherapeutics. To provide proof-of-concept for this combinatorial approach, we introduced a tailored OV-BYTE therapy that exploited the oncolytic virus engineered to encode TBYS cell epitopes and thus redirected the cytotoxicity of TME-infiltrating TBYS cells against tumor. Specifically, on day 30 post LCMV Armstrong infection, C57BL/6 mice with established LCMV GP-specific TMEM cell response were subcutaneously engrafted with MC38 tumor cells, followed by daily intratumoral administration of oncolytic NDV encoding LCMV GP (NDV-GP) from day 5 to day 11 after tumorigenesis. Concurrently, MC38 tumor-engrafted mice received five doses of a cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy regimen administered every 3 days from day 5 to day 17 post tumorigenesis (figure 3A). This concurrent initiation was mechanistically supported by the chemo-resistant nature of CD8+ TBYS cells engaged by NDV-GP and aimed to maximize their potential antitumor synergy. Aligned well with previous studies, monotherapy with NDV-GP or chemotherapy efficiently delayed tumor growth and increased survival in MC38 tumor-engrafted mice (figure 3B,C). More importantly, the combination of NDV-GP and chemotherapy profoundly enhanced therapeutic outcomes compared with either agent alone (figure 3B,C), demonstrating a synergistic antitumor effect. Moreover, this therapeutic synergy largely stemmed from TBYS cell epitope-dependent effects, rather than from general oncolytic virus-driven innate immune activation, given the minimal synergistic effects between wild-type NDV (NDV-WT) and chemotherapy (online supplemental Figure S2).

We next sought to extend the combined regimen of OV-BYTE and platinum-based chemotherapy to cold tumor settings. For this purpose, C57BL/6 mice with immune memory to LCMV Armstrong infection were subcutaneously burdened with B16F10 melanoma cells and then subjected to NDV-GP and/or chemotherapy consisting of cisplatin and pemetrexed as shown in figure 3D. While both monotherapies effectively controlled tumor progression, the combination yielded significant tumor growth control and conferred a marked survival benefit (figure 3E,F), indicating a robust synergistic antitumor function in the poorly immunogenic B16F10 model. To further rigorously validate the potential therapeutic efficacy of the combined regimen, BALB/c mice were subjected to a prime-boost immunization regimen with SARS-CoV-2 RBD protein at a 2-week interval. Then, these BALB/c mice with established T-cell memory to RBD protein received orthotopic implantation of murine triple-negative breast cancer 4T1 cells and then administered with the oncolytic NDV encoding RBD protein (NDV-RBD) and/or the cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy regimen (figure 3G). While monotherapies were effective, their combination conferred reinforced protection against immunologically cold 4T1 tumor (figure 3H,I). Thus, these findings collectively indicate that the combination of OV-BYTE and platinum-based chemotherapy achieves potent antitumor efficacy.

To ascertain the necessity of CD8+ TBYS cell engagement in the observed synergistic antitumor effect of the combined OV-BYTE and chemotherapy regimen, LCMV Armstrong-specific CD8+ TMEM cells were isolated and adoptively transferred into naïve C57BL/6 mice without prior LCMV infection. Then, these recipients were subcutaneously engrafted with MC38 tumor cells, thereby establishing the transferred LCMV-specific TMEM cells as tumor-non-specific TBYS cells within the TME and administered with a combined NDV-GP and chemotherapy regimen (figure 3J). As shown in figure 3K, in mice receiving adoptively transferred LCMV-specific CD8+ T cells as the sole TBYS reservoir, the combination therapy worked effectively. By contrast, this synergy was abolished in the absence of such cells. In aggregate, these results indicate that OV-BYTE therapy, by harnessing CD8+ TBYS population of chemoresistance, acts synergistically with platinum-based chemotherapy in controlling tumor progression.

OV-BYTE therapy restores chemotherapy-induced blunted CD8+ TTST cell response in the TME

To gain mechanistic insights into the synergistic therapeutic effects of the OV-BYTE and platinum-based chemotherapy combination, we used MC38-OVA tumor-burdened C57BL/6 mice hosting congenic OT-1 TTST cells and P14 TBYS cells and treated them with NDV-GP and cisplatin/pemetrexed combination as outlined in figure 4A. As anticipated from the documented immunosuppression side effect of the alkylating cisplatin and the antifolate pemetrexed, we found that the cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy induced a pan-leukocytic reduction within the TME, affecting CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells (including regulatory CD4+ T cells, Tregs), B cells, dendritic cells, macrophages, monocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and polymorphonuclear MDSCs (online supplemental Figure S3). Notably, NDV-GP specifically mitigated the chemotherapy-induced loss of CD8+ T cells and restored their population within the TME, without exerting a notable impact on other immune cell types (online supplemental Figure S3). We next looked into transferred OT-1 TTST cells and found that while chemotherapy largely depleted this population, co-administration of NDV-GP completely reversed this loss (figure 4B,C). This restoration was attributed to an NDV-GP treatment-conferred anti-apoptotic resistance to chemotherapy, as evidenced by its reversal of both the pre-existing and the chemotherapy-exacerbated Bcl-2/Bim imbalance in OT-1 TTST cells within the TME (figure 4D–G). Importantly, NDV-GP-mediated reversal of chemotherapy cytotoxicity was not restricted to transferred OT-1 TTST cells but extended to endogenous ribosomal protein L18 (Rpl18)-specific CD8+ T cells, a dominant TTST population within the MC38 TME, where the same phenotypic patterns were consistently observed (figure 4H–L). In the scenario of transferred P14 TBYS cells, monotherapy with either cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy or NDV-GP failed to alter their frequency and number within the TME. Similarly, the combination of both therapies also left the P14 TBYS population barely changed (online supplemental Figure S4). Therefore, OV-BYTE therapy restores CD8+ TTST cells within the TME by ameliorating their apoptotic propensity to platinum-based chemotherapy.

OV-BYTE therapy and chemotherapy synergize to augment effector-like transitory CD8+ TTST cell differentiation

To further untangle the potential rewirings of CD8+ TTST cell fate and function elicited by the combination therapy, transferred OT-1 TTST cells within the TME of all four groups (control, cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy, NDV-GP and combination therapy) were isolated and performed with scRNA-seq profiling. Following integration and UMAP, we identified a total of seven clusters within the OT-1 TTST population, including progenitor TEX (TPEX, Tcf7+Sell+), effector-like transitory TEX-1 (TEX-eff-1, Havcr2+Ccl5+), effector-like transitory TEX-2 (TEX-eff-2, Havcr2+Ccl3+), terminally differentiated TEX (TEX-term, Ctla4+Nr4a2+), proliferating TEX-1 (TEX-proli-1, Havcr2+Cdk4+), proliferating TEX-2 (TEX-proli-2, Havcr2+Mki67+), and terminally differentiated effector T (TEFF-term, Zeb2+Nfkb1+) cells (figure 5A and online supplemental Figure S5), as guided by the consensus definitions in the field. Assessment of the cluster composition showed that the OT-1 TTST population in control and chemotherapy settings was dominated by TEX-term cells. This exhausted state was fundamentally altered by NDV-GP treatment, both alone and in combination with chemotherapy, giving way to a greater proportion of TEX-eff-1 cells characterized by the skewed effector-associated transcriptional program (figure 5B,C).

To understand the cellular fate transitions underlying the aforementioned NDV-GP-mediated shift in cluster composition, we next performed pseudotime trajectory analysis. Adhering to the established notions, we found that the majority of OT-1 TTST cells followed a canonical TPEX → TEX-eff-1 → TEX-term differentiation trajectory; meanwhile, a route of TPEX → TEX-eff-2 → TEX-proli-1 → TEX-proli-2 → TEFF-term emerged as the minor differentiation trajectory among OT-1 TTST cells (figure 5D,E). Importantly, while OT-1 TTST cells under control and chemotherapy conditions were invariably propelled toward terminal exhaustion state, their counterparts under NDV-GP condition (alone and in combination with chemotherapy) were skewed toward a durable, less differentiated effector state (figure 5F), thereby preserving a substantial reservoir of functional TTST cells. To substantiate these in silico findings, we then examined OT-1 TTST cell functionality by ex vivo OVA257-264 peptide stimulation assay. Indeed, OT-1 TTST cells from control and chemotherapy groups exhibited a dysfunctional state reminiscent of terminal T-cell exhaustion, as evidenced by a profound impairment in the secretion of effector cytokines, including IFN-γ, TNF-α and IL-2 (figure 5G–I). In stark contrast, OT-1 TTST cells under NDV-GP administration were featured by robust polyfunctionality, co-expressing IFN-γ, TNF-α and IL-2. Remarkably, this polyfunctional capacity was further augmented in the NDV-GP and chemotherapy combination group (figure 5G–I). In conclusion, these data demonstrate that the combination therapy diverts CD8+ TTST cells from terminal exhaustion state and optimizes their effector polyfunctionality, which underlies the observed therapeutic synergy.

Profound antitumor effects by combined OV-BYTE, chemotherapy and PD-1 blockade

Given that platinum-based chemotherapy combined with PD-1 blockade constitutes a cornerstone regimen in clinical oncology, we next asked whether the addition of TBYS cell-targeted OV-BYTE could yield even greater synergistic benefits in a triple-combination therapy. Towards this end, C57BL/6 mice were infected with LCMV Armstrong and then engrafted with MC38 tumor cells on day 30 post infection. Those tumor-bearing mice received daily intratumoral administration of NDV-GP (days 5–11 after tumorigenesis), cisplatin/pemetrexed every 2 days for five doses (days 5–17), and anti-PD-L1 antibody for three doses (days 18, 21 and 24) (figure 6A). While monotherapies of NDV-GP, cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy, or anti-PD-L1 antibody each demonstrated antitumor activity, all dual-combination regimens exhibited synergistic effects, resulting in reinforced tumor control compared with any single agent (figure 6B). Crucially, the triple combination of NDV-GP, cisplatin/pemetrexed chemotherapy, and anti-PD-L1 antibody generated the most potent tumor growth suppression and represented the peak of therapeutic efficacy in the current model.

To provide more insights into the clinical relevance of the aforementioned triple-combination therapy, we sought to evaluate its therapeutic efficacy by leveraging a patient-derived tumor organoid and autologous immune cell co-culture system. For this purpose, we recruited a cohort of patients with CRC with prior infections to H1N1 influenza A and SARS-CoV-2. Then, CRC organoids were generated from these patients and co-cultured with their autologous PBMCs. To this co-culture system, we added the following components to model the triple therapy, including (1) OV-BYTE regimen, comprising oncolytic NDV engineered to express H1N1 nucleocapsid protein (NDV-NP) and SARS-CoV-2 RBD (NDV-RBD), (2) the standard first-line CRC chemotherapy regimen of OXP and 5-FU, and (3) anti-PD-1 antibody. Following a 5-day co-culture, tumor cell viability was assessed as an index for therapeutic response (figure 6C). Consistently, we observed robust efficacy with any two-agent combination. More importantly, the simultaneous administration of all three agents was uniquely potent, achieving a level of therapeutic response that significantly exceeded all the mono-therapies and dual-therapies (figure 6D,E). Altogether, these findings highlight the profound antitumor effects by the triple combination therapy, comprising the OV-BYTE, platinum-based chemotherapy and PD-1 blockade.

Discussion

This study was motivated by a fundamental pharmacologic conflict in standard chemo-immunotherapy where PD-1 ICB aims to expand CD8+ TTST cells, yet concurrent chemotherapy indiscriminately eliminates these proliferating immune effectors. To circumvent this limitation, we proposed to engage a chemo-resistant immune reservoir within the traditional chemo-immunotherapy regimen. We found that, unlike chemo-susceptible CD8+ TTST cells, TME-infiltrating CD8+ TBYS cells of tumor non-specificity persisted in a quiescent state and were resistant to platinum-based chemotherapy-induced cytotoxicity. Exploiting this, we showed that the TBYS-targeted oncolytic virotherapy OV-BYTE, which redirected CD8+ TBYS to attack tumors, synergized with chemotherapy to control tumor growth. This synergy arose from direct engagement of the CD8+ TBYS reservoir and concurrent restoration of CD8+ TTST cell response. Furthermore, integrating this virotherapy into standard chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen reinforced therapeutic efficacy in preclinical and patient-derived tumor models. Thus, our work establishes a strategy to overcome the inherent conflict between chemotherapy and PD-1 ICB immunotherapy.

The persistence of virus-specific CD8+ TBYS cells through chemotherapy, as shown in our study, aligns with established evidence that certain CD8+ TMEM cells are intrinsically resistant to chemotherapy drugs. Such chemo-resistance has been attributed to two key mechanisms in CD8+ TMEM cells, including the high activity of multidrug efflux transporters that rapidly pump out chemotherapeutic agents, and a distinct cell-intrinsic program that maintains genomic integrity and thereby shields them from drug-induced cytotoxicity. Given that TBYS cells in our scientific context are essentially virus-specific TMEM cells that have migrated into the TME while retaining core memory characteristics, it is plausible that the aforementioned mechanisms of drug efflux and genomic maintenance also underpin their chemo-resistance.

We found that the OV-BYTE and chemotherapy combination fundamentally reshaped the fate of TME-infiltrating CD8+ TTST cells, transforming them from a chemotherapy-susceptible, dysfunctional state into a replenished polyfunctional one. This suggests that the therapeutic impact extends beyond merely protecting existing CD8+ TTST cells from chemotherapy-induced apoptosis, potentially influencing earlier stages of their priming and differentiation. Dendritic cells play an indispensable role in initiating de novo CD8+ TTST cell responses. Building on our prior finding that OV-BYTE monotherapy augmented CD8+ TTST cell response via dendritic cell-mediated epitope spreading, we further observed that OV-BYTE could partially mitigate chemotherapy-induced depletion of dendritic cells in the study. These preserved dendritic cells, empowered by a pro-inflammatory milieu rich in releasing tumor antigens from chemotherapy-induced killing and OV-BYTE-redirected CD8+ TBYS cell cytotoxicity, could potently drive the priming of a more potent wave of de novo CD8+ TTST cell responses. More importantly, we noted that the polyfunctionality of these CD8+ TTST cells was further enabled by a permissive TME generated by the OV-BYTE and chemotherapy combination, which substantially diminished the immunosuppressive Treg population. Tregs are a crucial cell type known to suppress effector function and promote exhaustion program of CD8+ TTST cells, and their reduction likely constitutes a key mechanism by which the therapy facilitates CD8+ TTST cell polyfunctionality.

A key translational insight from our work is the marked potentiation of the standard chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen by OV-BYTE. Although the chemo-immunotherapy combination is widely used, its efficacy is constrained by chemotherapy-induced damage to the proliferating CD8+ TTST cells required for PD-1 ICB efficacy. The sequential administration of chemotherapy followed by PD-1 ICB, a strategy pioneered by a preclinical study using a chronic LCMV clone 13 infection model, was designed to partly mitigate this conflict. However, as established in this prior work and corroborated here, the initial chemotherapy still depleted LCMV-specific or tumor-specific CD8+ T cells under chronic antigen stimulation, leaving a diminished substrate for subsequent immunotherapy. To overcome this limitation, we have conceptually validated a phased triple therapy. In its first phase, OV-BYTE and chemotherapy jointly engaged CD8+ TBYS cells and, crucially, restored the CD8+ TTST cell compartment. This restored compartment is then reinforced by PD-1 ICB in the second phase, thereby maximizing the final antitumor effect.

To advance the integration of OV-BYTE into the current standard chemo-PD-1 regimen, two translational considerations are paramount. First, the transition from localized intratumoral injection in preclinical models to systemic administration in patients is essential for treating metastatic and deep-seated tumors. Developing a targeted systemic delivery platform (eg, cell carriers-based, liposome-based and cell membrane-based platforms) will be crucial to deploy OV-BYTE effectively against metastatic and deep-seated tumors. Second, the efficacy of OV-BYTE depends on a pre-existing reservoir of pathogen-specific TBYS cells. While ubiquitous exposures (eg, to influenza or SARS-CoV-2) provide a broad foundation, pretreatment immune profiling followed by selective boost vaccination could ensure a robust TBYS pool across all patients, thereby maximizing the consistency and potency of the combined regimen in the clinic.

Our study also has limitations that warrant consideration. First, while our patient-derived organoid-PBMC co-culture system functionally validated the therapeutic synergy, it did not permit direct phenotypic identification of the human CD8+ TBYS populations responsible for tumor killing, leaving their precise cellular characteristics in this setting an open question. Second, though prior studies reported that chemotherapy-induced immunosuppression can promote the infection and persistence of a series of oncolytic viruses in tumors by suppressing antiviral immune responses, the effects of chemotherapy on viral kinetics in the context of OV-BYTE strategy warrant further investigation. Third, our study tested the combination of OV-BYTE with two specific chemotherapy regimens. Whether the therapeutic synergy extends broadly to other chemotherapeutic agents remains to be investigated.

In summary, our study addressed a core limitation of chemo-immunotherapy by leveraging the chemotherapy-resistant CD8+ TBYS cell niche. We showed that targeted engagement of this reservoir with OV-BYTE synergized with the chemo-PD-1 ICB regimen to control tumor progression. This synergy was underpinned by the reshaping of CD8+ TTST cells into a polyfunctional state with increased resistance to chemotherapy. Thus, our study provides proof-of-concept for a novel strategy in cancer immunotherapy, and its clinical translation would represent a potent addition to the therapeutic arsenal against cancer.
