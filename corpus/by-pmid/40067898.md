# Dual mRNA nanoparticles strategy for enhanced pancreatic cancer treatment and β-elemene combination therapy.

## Abstract

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is notoriously immune-resistant, limiting the clinical efficacy of single-agent immune modulators and thereby necessitating the exploration of multimodal immunotherapy combinations. Traditional approaches combining conventional immune checkpoint inhibitors with neoantigen vaccines have shown some promise in treating PDAC but are often compromised by intratumoral T lymphocyte exhaustion and systemic toxicity. Hence, novel approaches are needed to address these challenges. Herein, we demonstrate that mRNA polymeric nanoparticles encoding anti-PD-1 antibodies in situ at the tumor site enhance the therapeutic efficacy of neoantigen-based mRNA vaccine for PDAC. This mRNA-based, in situ anti-PD-1 antibody production strategy also protects tumor-infiltrating T cells from PD-1 inhibition, potentially reducing the toxicities induced by systemic checkpoint inhibition. Our study may provide an innovative dual mRNA nanoparticle strategy for effective tumor neoantigen immunotherapy, as well as an mRNA cancer combination therapy strategy with other clinically approved drugs (e.g., β-elemene).

## Full Text

Dual mRNA nanoparticles strategy for enhanced pancreatic cancer treatment and β-elemene combination therapy

Significance

The therapeutic efficacy of personalized neoantigen vaccines is currently limited by the suppressive immune environment. To address the current limitations, we pioneered a dual mRNA nanoparticles strategy to produce anti-PD-1 antibodies at the tumor site during neoantigen-based vaccine therapy for pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. This first-of-its-kind dual mRNA approach effectively reverses immunosuppression by activating tumor-infiltrating T cells and protecting them from PD-1 exhaustion and significantly enhances the therapeutic efficacy (either singly used or for combination therapy).

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is notoriously immune-resistant, limiting the clinical efficacy of single-agent immune modulators and thereby necessitating the exploration of multimodal immunotherapy combinations. Traditional approaches combining conventional immune checkpoint inhibitors with neoantigen vaccines have shown some promise in treating PDAC but are often compromised by intratumoral T lymphocyte exhaustion and systemic toxicity. Hence, novel approaches are needed to address these challenges. Herein, we demonstrate that mRNA polymeric nanoparticles encoding anti-PD-1 antibodies in situ at the tumor site enhance the therapeutic efficacy of neoantigen-based mRNA vaccine for PDAC. This mRNA-based, in situ anti-PD-1 antibody production strategy also protects tumor-infiltrating T cells from PD-1 inhibition, potentially reducing the toxicities induced by systemic checkpoint inhibition. Our study may provide an innovative dual mRNA nanoparticle strategy for effective tumor neoantigen immunotherapy, as well as an mRNA cancer combination therapy strategy with other clinically approved drugs (e.g., β-elemene).

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) leads to the highest mortality rates of all malignancies, ranking fourth in the United States and seventh globally. The 5-y overall survival rate for patients with PDAC is 11%, and surgical resection is the only potentially curative treatment option at present in clinical practice. However, 80% of patients are diagnosed at an advanced stage or with distant metastasis, which limits their eligibility for surgical intervention. Gemcitabine and fluoropyrimidine-based chemotherapy is the standard treatment for advanced and metastatic pancreatic cancer, but its therapeutic efficacy is limited, and recurrence is common. Therefore, novel therapeutic strategies are urgently needed to improve outcomes for PADC patients.

Neoantigens, tumor-specific antigens expressed only in tumor cells, can serve as precise targets recognized by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) to induce adaptive immune responses. Therefore, identifying neoantigens offers viable and specific targets for immunotherapy strategies. Cutting-edge technologies to predict and screen neoantigens have been developed, leading to their increased application in clinical trials of immunotherapies. Recently, a combination of tumor vaccines and immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) strategy was reported to prolong the progression-free survival of PDAC patients, demonstrating a promising approach for solid tumor malignancies. ICIs block coinhibitory signaling pathways of T cells and tumor cells, particularly CTLA-4 and PD-1/PD-L1, showing good clinical responses in various solid tumors and hematological malignancies. However, the efficacy of anti-PD-1 / PD-L1 antibodies in PDAC patients is limited, possibly due to their poor binding to tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes. Besides, systemic administration of ICIs can lead to severe immune-related adverse events, resulting in treatment discontinuation. Enhancing the binding of anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies to T lymphocytes at tumor sites while reducing systemic toxicity could be a promising approach to improve ICI therapy.

mRNA-based nanomedicine has achieved remarkable success in combating the COVID-19 pandemic and has demonstrated tremendous potential in producing therapeutic proteins, antibodies, and tumor vaccines. mRNA technology enables the production of predetermined functional proteins at the target site, thus maximizing the efficacy of proteins. This study proposed using mRNA lipid–polymer hybrid nanoparticles (LPNPs) to produce anti-PD-1 antibodies at the tumor site to maximize their binding to tumor-infiltrating T cells, thereby enhancing the efficacy of ICIs in neoantigen-based mRNA lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) vaccine therapy (Fig. 1). Specifically, we utilize polymer-assisted hybrid nanoparticles for anti-PD-1 mRNA delivery via intravenous (i.v.) injection to efficiently produce anti-PD-1 antibodies at tumor sites and enhance their binding to T cells. This strategy of anti-PD-1 antibody production at the tumor site has demonstrated excellent therapeutic outcomes in orthotopic and metastatic PDAC models. Moreover, the therapeutic efficacy of dual mRNA nanoparticles (NPs) can be further enhanced by combination use together with β-elemene, potentially broadening the applicability of this mRNA NP strategy in combination cancer therapies involving other therapeutic agents.

Results

Validation of Neoantigens and Identification of the Optimal Neoantigens-Based mRNA Delivery Strategies.

To screen the potential neoantigens in the murine pancreatic cancer models, we established an orthotopic model of PDAC by injecting Panc02 cells into the pancreas of 6-wk-old C57BL/6 mice. Thirteen days postinjection, tumor samples were collected for RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and whole exome sequencing to identify expressed nonsynonymous single nucleotide substitutions (Materials and Methods and SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). Then, 324 nonsynonymous mutations were identified, and RNA-seq data confirmed that 298 were consistently expressed. Furthermore, we utilized NetMHCpan to predict binding affinity between mutated peptides and H2-Kb or H2-Db (the MHC I alleles expressed by Panc02 cell), revealing that 14 mutated peptides exhibited strong affinity to H2-Kb or H2-Db (with a binding affinity value of IC50 < 500 nM) and gene expression (measured as Fragments Per Kilobase Million, FPKM) > 5. Notably, peptides exceeding 15 amino acids have previously been shown to induce CD4+ T and CD8+ T cell responses. We, therefore, synthesized 75-bp mRNA sequences with a mutated site at position 38 to produce long peptides with a mutated residue at position 13 (SI Appendix, Table S1).

To identify CD8+ T cells specific to neoantigens, bone marrow–derived dendritic cells (DCs) and CD8+ T cells were extracted from mice bearing orthotopic Panc02 tumors. Then, 14 in vitro transcribed (IVT) neoantigen mRNAs were electroporated into the DCs for 24 h. Following this, the DCs were cocultured with CD8+ T cells for 48 h to conduct an ex vivo interferon gamma (IFN-γ) ELISPOT assay. Notably, eight epitopes exhibited strong immunogenicity significantly compared to the negative control and their wild-type mRNA counterparts (Fig. 2A and SI Appendix, Fig. S1 B and C). Subsequently, we synthesized two neoantigen-mRNA vaccines, termed as NV1 mRNA (containing four epitopes: Atp5lA18T, CpqE281Q, Cog4T273S, and Fbf1A490V), and NV2 mRNA (containing four epitopes: Hif1aS58F, Tcs2L527R, Slc16a13H240N, and Tatdn2K286N), respectively (Fig. 2B). To mitigate RNA-mediated innate immune activation in vivo, pseudo-UTP was used to replace UTP in the mRNA synthesis process. In addition, agarose-gel RNA electrophoresis was employed to qualify the IVT NVs mRNA, with results showing high purity without detectable byproducts (Fig. 2C). Furthermore, EGFP-NVs mRNA, which can encode both enhanced green fluorescence protein (EGFP) and neoantigens at the same time, was constructed and pulsed into the DCs for different time points. Our results revealed the gradual and consistent expression of NVs mRNA within the DCs (Fig. 2D). These findings collectively affirm the high purity of the linear NVs mRNA and its efficacy in inducing IFN-γ secretion by stimulated CD8+ T cells in vitro.

To determine the immunogenicity of NVs mRNA in vivo, we immunized the orthotopic PDAC mice with NVs mRNA encapsulated into LNPs via subcutaneous (s.c.) injection (Fig. 2E). Fourteen days postinjection, we performed a flow cytometry analysis to assess the proportion of cytotoxic T cells and antigen presentation capacity. Notably, the proportion of CD8+ T cells in CD45+CD3+ T cells was increased in the blood and spleen in mice treated with NVs mRNA@LNPs compared to those receiving empty NPs (Fig. 2 F and G and SI Appendix, Fig. S1F). Additionally, an increase in CD80+ CD86+ cells in CD45+ cells was observed in the blood of the NVs mRNA@LNPs group (Fig. 2H and SI Appendix, Fig. S1G). Immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis further evaluated the percentage of CD8+ T cells in the tumor samples, demonstrating elevated intratumoral CD8+ T cells in the NVs mRNA@LNP treated group compared to the empty NPs group (Fig. 2I).

Subsequently, we analyzed the impact of NVs mRNA administration methods on the immune response effect (SI Appendix, Fig. S1D). RT-PCR assays of T cell maturation markers (CCL5, IL1β, IL2, and TNF-α) in the thymus revealed significant stimulation of T cells in mice treated with NVs mRNA@LNPs via s.c. injection compared to other delivery methods (SI Appendix, Fig. S1E). Collectively, these results demonstrate a superior immune response triggered by subcutaneous delivery of NVs mRNA encapsulated in LNPs, confirming its potential as an effective vaccination route.

Profiling of In Situ Antibody Production of Anti-PD-1 mRNA.

Given the challenges associated with the conventional anti-PD-1 antibody administration, such as their poor reachability to tumor sites and accompanied side effects, we employed LPNPs for anti-PD-1 mRNA delivery, leveraging their tumor-accumulation capabilities to overcome the barriers.

In this section, mRNA encoding luciferase (Luc) served as a model mRNA. Initially, to determine the most effective NP formulation for in vivo use, we compared the tumor-accumulation capabilities of DiR-labeled Luc mRNA encapsulated into LNPs and LPNPs, respectively (Fig. 3A). Both formulations were administered intravenously to mice bearing subcutaneous tumors. The distribution of DiR-labeled Luc mRNA NPs in live animals was visualized using a bioluminescence spectrum. The fluorescent signals from different sites of interest were measured by dissecting the specific tissues and using ex vivo imaging. Intriguingly, a significant fluorescence signal was observed in the DiR-labeled Luc mRNA@LPNPs group within the tumor in a time-dependent manner (Fig. 3 B and C). At 36 h postinjection, tumors, and major organs were subjected to ex vivo imaging, with results showing DiR-labeled LPNPs predominantly accumulated in the liver, spleen, and tumor (Fig. 3 D and E). These findings suggest that LPNPs represent a promising nanoplatform for effective mRNA delivery, navigating tumor sites efficiently.

mRNA-encoded antibodies streamline production and show significant potential for cancer treatment. The IVT anti-PD-1 mRNA was synthesized using a 5′ cap structure, 5′ and 3′ UTRs, a signal peptide, a His tag, a PD-1 blocking single-chain variable fragment, a transmembrane domain, and a poly A tail (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). Initially, to verify the expression and cellular localization of the secreted antibody, we engineered mCherry-labeled anti-PD-1 mRNA (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B), which was then loaded into LPNPs. Confocal laser scanning microscopy images (CLSM) showed that mCherry-labeled anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs led to antibody production, with a translation capacity comparable to that of mCherry-labeled anti-PD-1 mRNA@Lipo (Fig. 3F). Next, to assess the biological efficacy of anti-PD-1 mRNA, DCs were transfected with 1 μg of anti-PD-1 mRNA and subsequently cocultured with carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE)-labeled CD8+ T cells for 60 h. Flow cytometry analysis revealed enhanced CD8+ T cell proliferation in cells treated with anti-PD-1 mRNA compared to those treated with commercial anti-PD-1 antibodies (Fig. 3H). Similarly, agarose-gel RNA electrophoresis showed high purity of the IVT anti-PD-1 mRNA, with no byproducts (Fig. 3G), indicating the potential of IVT anti-PD-1 mRNA as an effective alternative tumor treatment strategy.

Furthermore, to evaluate the targeted transfection efficacy of anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs, we established an orthotopic Panc02 tumor-bearing mice model on Day 0 and administered anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs via i.v. injection on Days 4, 7, 10, 13, and 16, respectively (Fig. 3I). Empty NPs served as the control. To assess the expression of anti-PD-1 mRNA, immunofluorescence staining for the His tag was conducted on orthotopic tumor samples on Day 22. A robust anti-PD-1 mRNA expression was observed in the anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs group by CLSM images (Fig. 3J). Having confirmed the excellent targeted performance of anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs in the orthotopic PDAC model, we investigated whether these NPs could effectively stimulate immune responses within the tumor microenvironment (TME). IHC assay results showed that treatment with anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs significantly increased CD8+ T cell expression at the tumor site compared to empty NPs (Fig. 3K). Collectively, results demonstrated that anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs could successfully accumulate in the tumor section and produce antibodies in situ (Fig. 3L), resulting in an enhanced immune response in the orthotopic PDAC model.

Evaluation of Transfection Efficacy, Biocompatibility, and Endosomal Escape Ability.

To evaluate the biological performance of LNPs and LPNPs—both are prominent mRNA delivery nanoplatforms, EGFP mRNA was utilized as a model mRNA loaded into the NPs. We formulated EGFP mRNA@LPNPs by combining mRNA with cationic lipids (G0-C14), poly(lactic-co-glycolic acid) (PLGA), and 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-conjugated polyethylene glycol (DSPE-PEG). The morphology of the mRNA NPs was characterized using transmission electron microscopy (TEM), exhibiting relatively uniform and spherical NP structures (SI Appendix, Fig. S2C). Stability assessments conducted over 72 h at 4 °C showed both NPs remained stable size distributions (SI Appendix, Fig. S2D). Moreover, the CLSM images demonstrated that EGFP mRNA@LNPs and EGFP mRNA@LPNPs achieved transfection levels comparable to EGFP-mRNA@Lipo. This observation was further confirmed by flow cytometry analysis (SI Appendix, Fig. S2E). The biosafety profiles of both NPs were evaluated using an in vitro CCK-8 assay, which showed no cytotoxicity to Panc02 cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S2F). Additionally, both LNPs and LPNPs effectively facilitated the release of mRNAs from endosomes, a critical step for functional mRNA delivery (SI Appendix, Fig. S2G). These findings identify that LNPs and LPNPs present comparable transfection efficacy, biocompatibility, and endosomal escape capabilities.

In Situ Production of Anti-PD-1 Antibodies Enhances the Therapeutic Efficacy of Neoantigen-Based mRNA Vaccines.

To examine the therapeutic efficacy of in situ production of anti-PD-1 antibodies in neoantigen-based nanovaccine therapy in vivo, we established an orthotopic model of PDAC by injecting luciferase-expressing Panc02 (Panc02-Luc) cells into the pancreas of C57BL/6 mice. By detecting the average radiance of the tumor sites, the growth of tumors was monitored using bioluminescence imaging. Mice were randomly divided into five groups with different treatments of saline, empty NPs, NVs mRNA@LNPs, anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs, and NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs. NVs mRNA@LNPs were administrated on Days 0 and 4 via s.c. injection, while anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs were administered on Days 4, 7, 10, 13, and 16 via i.v. delivery, respectively (Fig. 4A). Bioluminescence imaging was conducted on Days 0, 4, 7, 10, 16, and 22, respectively. As shown in Fig. 4 B and C, both the NVs mRNA@LNPs and anti-PD-1 mRNA@LNPs groups demonstrated a moderate effect in inhibiting tumor growth compared to the saline and empty NPs groups. Notably, the combination treatment group exhibited significantly enhanced tumor growth inhibition efficacy compared to all other groups. Hematoxylin–eosin (HE) staining further confirmed that NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs treatment significantly inhibited tumor growth (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A). The biosafety of the in situ production of anti-PD-1 antibodies strategy was also evaluated by testing multiple serological indicators. Specifically, aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), and albumin (Alb) were tested for liver function, uric acid (UA), and creatinine (Cr) for kidney function, and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) for heart function. The results revealed that ALT, AST, UA, Cr, and LDH levels were lowest in the NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs group, accompanied by the highest level of Alb, indicating excellent safety profile of the dual NPs administrated in vivo (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). These findings collectively demonstrate that in situ production of anti-PD-1 antibodies enhances the therapeutic efficacy of neoantigen-based nanovaccine without causing toxicity effects in vivo PDAC model.

In Situ Production of Anti-PD-1 Antibodies Augments the Antitumor Immunity of the Neoantigen-Based Nanovaccine.

To elucidate the immune responses underlying the robust antitumor effect of the NVs mRNA@LNPs and the anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs treatments, we harvested spleen, serum, and tumor samples on Day 22. The ELISA results demonstrated significant increases in IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-2 levels, while IL-6 levels remained stable (Fig. 4D). Furthermore, flow cytometry analysis indicated that, compared to the saline and empty NPs, the proportion of CD8+ T cells significantly increased in the NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs group, while the frequency of CD4+ T cells remained unchanged (Fig. 4 E–H). Similar trends were also observed regarding tumor-infiltrating T cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S3 C–F). Moreover, the combination treatment increased the proportion of DCs, as evidenced by the elevated surface markers of CD80 and CD86 on APCs (Fig. 4 I and J and SI Appendix, Fig. S3 G and H). Furthermore, western blot assays were conducted to assess the intratumoral expression of innate immune stimulators, including STING and IRF3, revealing a notable increase in their protein expression (Fig. 4K). Collectively, these results indicated that in situ production of anti-PD-1 antibodies stimulates robust adaptive and innate immune responses in neoantigen-based nanovaccine therapy.

Furthermore, we conducted a nanostring GeoMx DSP RNA-seq assay to characterize the heterogeneous TME, and 95 regions of interest (ROIs) were obtained from the empty NPs, NVs mRNA@LNPs, anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs, and NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs groups. Initially, we employed gene set variation analysis in order to identify differentially regulated pathways in the TME. The results showed significantly stronger immune responses in the NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs group, including coagulation cascade, complement cascade, interferon-gamma response, and interferon alpha pathways (Fig. 5A). Venn plot showed that a total of 100 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) among the NVs mRNA@LNPs vs. empty NPs, anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs vs. empty NPs, and NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs vs. empty NPs groups (adjusted P-value < 0.05, |log2 (foldchange)| > 1) (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 D and E). Moreover, enrichment analysis of 100 DEGs revealed significant upregulation of antigen processing and presentation of peptide antigen processing in the NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs treatment group (SI Appendix, Fig. S4F). In addition, the transcriptome was interrogated for immune signatures through gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA), and pathways associated with the antigen processing and presentation, humoral immune response, and response to interferon-gamma emerged as significantly enriched in the contrast NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs versus empty NPs group (Fig. 5B). Next, to identify the heterogeneous immune cell populations intratumorally, we focused on ROIs enriched CD45+ immune cells near the tumor area (PanCK), obtaining 9 ROIs for each group in this section (Fig. 5C and SI Appendix, Fig. S4 A–C). The ImmuCellAI method was applied to evaluate the abundance of T-cell subsets, B-cell subsets, macrophages, neutrophils, monocytes, DC, and NK cells. The abundance of adaptive and innate immune cells, including T, γδ T, CD8+ T, CD4+ T, and cDC1 cells, were significantly increased with NVs mRNA@LNPs + anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs treatment (Fig. 5 C and D), while the expression of T cell exhaustion markers, such as Pdcd1, Lag3, and Tigit, was significantly decreased (Fig. 5E).

Together, these results identify that the in situ production of anti-PD-1 antibodies enhances adaptive and innate immune responses while alleviating the exhaustion of T cells in neoantigen-based nanovaccine therapy.

Dual mRNA Nanoparticles Approach Inhibits Tumor Growth in Other PDAC Models.

Given the promising outcomes in the orthotopic model, we investigated the antitumor efficacy of the dual mRNA NP strategy in the lung metastasis PDAC model. As depicted in Fig. 6A, C57BL/6 mice were intravenously injected with Panc02-luc cells ten days before Day 0 (defined as Day -10), while NVs mRNA@LNPs were administered subcutaneously. Subsequently, two doses of NVs mRNA@LNPs were administered on Days 0 and 4, while anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs on Days 4, 7, 10, 13, and 16, respectively. Tumor growth was monitored by bioluminescence signals. A significant inhibition in lung metastatic burden was observed in the NVs mRNA@LNPs and anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs combined group (Fig. 6 B and C). Furthermore, Kaplan–Meier analysis indicated that mice with NVs mRNA@LNPs plus anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs treatment had significantly longer overall survival compared to those treated with saline, empty NPs, NVs mRNA@LNPs, or anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs (Fig. 6D). Moreover, the mRNA NP strategy significantly reduced the number of metastatic nodules in the lungs (Fig. 6 E and F).

It is also worth mentioning that, because of enhanced therapeutic efficacy, combination strategies based on different therapeutics (or drug molecules) are among the most commonly used approaches in the clinic for cancer therapy. To demonstrate whether this developed dual mRNA NP strategy could also be applied together with other anti-cancer drugs for combination therapy and potentially expand its future clinical use, β-elemene (one of the typical chemotherapeutic drugs used in the clinic) was used as a model anti-cancer drug in the subcutaneous PDAC model. Remarkably, we confirmed that the therapeutic efficacy of dual mRNA NPs was enhanced when combined with the β-elemene (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 A–F).

Discussion

Conventional vaccines usually struggle to eliminate tumors because of the immunosuppressive PD-L1/PD-1 pathway intratumorally. Nanomedicine-based strategies to enhance the therapeutic effects of ICIs in pancreatic cancer immunotherapy are gaining increasing attention. Our in situ anti-PD-1 antibody production approach in nano-based vaccine therapy provides a programming strategy that integrates neoantigen self-presentation and reverses immunosuppression, maximizing the cytotoxic potential of T cells and achieving a potent amplification of therapeutic effectiveness.

Previous studies have reported that drug delivery platforms can be engineered with good tumor-accumulating properties, which benefit from the prolonged circulation time by PEGylation, as well as the passively targeting effect of NPs. Therefore, we proposed the utilization of hybrid NPs encapsulating mRNA encoding anti-PD-1 antibodies in combination with nanovaccines (dual mRNA NP strategy) to overcome the inhibitory PDAC immune microenvironment. Previous studies also demonstrated that alternative cell therapies, such as the target delivery of a PD-1 blocking single-chain fragment variable (scFv) by chimeric antigen receptor T cell, exhibited enhanced anti-tumor efficacy in vivo. Another approach involved engineering a scFv targeting PD-1 into a plasmid vector to express membrane-localized anti-PD-1 antibodies in DC vaccines, stimulating robust cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses and immune checkpoint blockade, resulting in remarkable therapeutic efficacy. Similarly, our study identified that IVT anti-PD-1 mRNA expressing PD-1 blockade antibodies exhibited excellent biological effects in vitro and in vivo. Hence, genetic engineering of PD-1-blocking scFv offers another feasible possibility for clinical ICIs therapy. Accordingly, we employed LPNPs for anti-PD-1 mRNA delivery and achieved anti-PD-1 antibody production at the tumor site, resulting in an augmented intratumoral CD8+ T cell expression.

CD8+ T cells play a crucial role as the frontline defense against cancer, as they recognize cognate antigenic peptides which are presented by MHC class I molecules. The ICIs are believed to inhibit pre-existing T-cell immunity. Spontaneous T-cell responses are usually limited in treatment-naive PDAC patients, as evidenced by the absence of activated effector T cells and T-cell receptor clonality in tumor samples. Therefore, combination strategies that confer antigen specificity, enhance T-cell functions, and modulate the TME may sensitize PDAC to ICIs therapy. Besides, effective anti-PD-1 immunotherapies are characterized by polyclonal CD8+ T cells in tumors and blood, which target a limited number of immunodominant mutations identified repeatedly over time. Neoantigen-based immunotherapies have been successful in multiple tumors, including PDAC, gastrointestinal cancer, and melanoma. Our approach combines NVs mRNA@LNPs and anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs, leveraging standard neoantigen procedures to enhance antigen presentation efficiency through self-presentation. This strategy promotes CD8+ T cell-dominated immunity against tumors, exerting robust cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses. Moreover, considering the susceptibility of endogenous tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes to PD-1-mediated suppression in the TEM, the local secretion of PD-1-blocking scFv at the tumor site offers a potential strategy to protect these anti-tumor immune cells. Our study demonstrates that the combination of NVs mRNA@LNPs and anti-PD-1 mRNA@LPNPs enhances the innate and adaptive immune responses by using PDAC orthotopic and metastasis models.

Systemic administration of ICIs can result in organ-specific inflammatory toxicities, necessitating treatment discontinuation. Consequently, there is a critical need for alternative approaches to reduce toxicity. Hybrid NPs formulated with FDA-approved components offer several advantages for drug delivery, including high encapsulation efficiency, increased tumor-accumulating capabilities, and versatility in surface modifications, making them attractive vectors for delivering mRNA therapeutics to tumors and other target organs. Remarkably, the dual NPs used in our study demonstrated excellent biosafety performance in vivo (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). Given this evidence, we expect that our anti-PD-1 blockade delivery strategy might minimize the toxicities associated with systemically administered ICIs. In summary, we present a strategy of in situ anti-PD-1 antibody production, showing enhanced cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses and remarkable therapeutic efficacy, thereby offering a promising approach for clinical translation. Notably, this dual mRNA NP strategy could also be combined with other chemotherapeutic drugs (e.g., β-elemene used in this study) for combination therapy with enhanced efficacy, largely expanding its potential application in the future.
