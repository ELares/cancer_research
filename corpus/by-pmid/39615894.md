# Engineered oncolytic virus expressing B7H3-targeting BiTE enhances antitumor T-cell immune response.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Bispecific T-cell engagers (BiTEs) are recombinant bispecific proteins designed to stimulate polyclonal T-cell immunity. In recent years, B7H3, a pan-cancer antigen, has been considered a promising target for future immunotherapy. However, the B7H3-targeting BiTE faces the challenge of systemic toxicity. Oncolytic viruses (OVs) represent a new class of cancer immunotherapeutics and serve as an appropriate platform for locoregional delivery of therapeutic genes. In this study, we designed an oncolytic adenovirus (OAd) encoding BiTE targeting human B7H3. We hypothesized that OVs encoding B7H3 BiTE deliver this molecule persistently to the tumor site while mediating polyclonal T-cell activation and redirecting it to tumor cells.

**METHODS**: B7H3-targeting BiTE was constructed by linking a single-chain variable fragment (scFv) that recognizes human B7H3 to an scFv that recognizes human CD3. B7H3 BiTE was inserted into OAd to construct OAd-B7H3-BiTE. The function of the OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE was detected via co-culturing B7H3+ target cells and peripheral blood mononuclear cells. A humanized immune system mouse model was used to evaluate the therapeutic effects in vivo.

**RESULTS**: B7H3 is highly expressed in a high proportion of human malignancies. OV-delivered BiTEs bind to T cells and target cells. We observed a series of phenomena reflecting T-cell activation induced by OAd-B7H3-BiTE, including cell clustering, cell size, activation markers, cytokine secretion, and proliferation. Furthermore, T-cell activation was mirrored by the corresponding cytotoxicity against B7H3+ tumor cells. In vivo, B7H3 BiTE was persistently expressed in tumors and enhanced the antitumor T-cell immune response.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Using an OV for the local expression of B7H3 BiTE maximizes the local concentration of BiTE while reducing systemic exposure. OV also provides a relatively "hot" T-cell immune environment for the function of BiTE. Because of its capacity to activate polyclonal T cells, BiTE has the potential to redirect virus-specific T cells to tumors. Our study provides new opportunities for the exploitation of B7H3-BiTE-armed OVs as therapeutic agents for the treatment of B7H3-positive malignancies.

Background

 A bispecific T-cell engager (BiTE) comprises two single-chain variable fragments (scFvs) that recognize a target antigen and the invariant component of the T-cell receptor (TCR) complex, that is, the CD3 chains with signaling capacity. Combining these two different specificities allows a BiTE to physically link a T cell to a tumor cell and induce TCR-mediated signaling, ultimately stimulating T-cell activation and, more importantly, inducing targeted T-cell cytotoxicity against recognized tumor cells. Owing to the independence of the recognition between TCR and major histocompatibility complex (MHC), BiTEs activate T cells in a polyclonal mode. However, these molecules have short biological half-lives and systemic exposure. Blinatumomab, a CD19-targeting BiTE, was the first BiTE approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for treating relapsed and refractory acute B-lymphoblastic leukemia. Clinical trials of blinatumomab have demonstrated various levels of cytokine release syndrome (CRS), neurotoxicity and on-target off-tumor toxicities caused by systemic exposure. Because of its rapid clearance by the kidney during circulation, blinatumomab has a short half-life of 2–3 hours and requires continuous intravenous dosing.

B7H3 is a type I transmembrane protein and a member of the B7 family with immunomodulatory functions. The expression of B7H3 is absent or low in normal human tissues, but is highly expressed in a high proportion of human malignancies. B7H3 expression contributes to tumorigenesis, metastasis, and malignant behavior through several mechanisms, and is correlated with poor prognosis. In recent years, B7H3 has been considered a promising target for future immunotherapies. Several therapeutic antibodies that target B7H3 have been developed. In 2023, a humanized B7H3-targeting antibody, enoblituzumab, which mediates antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, was shown to exert antitumor activity in a phase 2 clinical trial. Radioimmunotherapy with an 131I-conjugated B7H3-targeting antibody, 131I-omburtamab, has yielded promising results for different malignancies in clinical trials. In recent years, B7H3 has been regarded as a pan-cancer antigen to be targeted by chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy.

Regarding B7H3-targeting T-cell engagers, only obrindatamab (MGD009) were clinically tested in advanced B7H3-positive tumors (NCT02628535). In 2018, the FDA imposed a partial hold on this study because of hepatic adverse events, likely due to CRS secondary to increased T-cell activation. Although this hold was lifted in 2019, B7H3-targeting T-cell engagers still face the challenge of systemic toxicity.

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) represent a new class of cancer immunotherapeutics and serve as an appropriate platform for locoregional delivery of therapeutic genes. The combination of BiTEs and OVs provides bidirectional benefits. The efficacy of OVs is constrained by the antiviral T-cell immune response after intratumoral administration. Many T cells that cannot target the tumors (including virus-specific T cells) accumulate in the tumor microenvironment (TME). Furthermore, dominant anti-viral T-cell responses can inhibit subdominant antitumor T-cell responses. Due to the polyclonal mode of action, BiTEs can redirect these T cells to tumors. On the other hand, BiTE monotherapy suffers from limitations such as a short biological half-life and poor residence time in the TME. Furthermore, the efficacy of this therapeutic approach against solid tumors is limited by tumor barriers and immune-suppressive microenvironments. OVs have the potential to offer a simpler in situ vaccination approach to induce locoregional T-cell responses. This mode of delivery can maximize the local concentration of BiTE at the tumor site, facilitating its entry into solid tumors while reducing systemic exposure and increasing therapeutic efficacy.

In this study, we designed an oncolytic adenovirus (OAd) encoding a BiTE that targets the pan-cancer antigen, B7H3. To the best of our knowledge, there are no reports on the use of B7H3-targeting BiTE to arm OVs. We found that this engineered OV could induce polyclonal T-cell activation by delivering B7H3 BiTE. We demonstrated that B7H3 BiTE was persistently expressed in tumors and mediated a robust antitumor T-cell immune response. Herein, we describe a novel oncolytic immunotherapy for the treatment of B7H3-positive malignancies.

Methods

Cell culture

HEK293 (#SCSP-5209) and SGC-7901 (#TCHu46) cell lines were obtained from the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Shanghai Institutes for Biological Sciences. MGC-803 (#FH0269) cell line was purchased from Fuheng Biotechnology. U87 (#CL-0238), Jurkat (#CL-0129) and SH-SY5Y (#CL-0208) cell lines were purchased from Wuhan Procell Life Science & Technology. Raji (#CX0032) cell line was purchased from Boster. Cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (Gibco) or Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640 (Gibco) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (DearyTech), 1% penicillin-streptomycin (Biosharp). All cells were incubated at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2.

Immunohistochemical staining

Cancerous and peritumoral tissues from patients were examined using immunohistochemical staining. Deparaffinized and dehydrated sections were boiled for 10 min in 0.01 M citrate buffer and incubated with 3% hydrogen peroxide in methanol for 25 min to block endogenous peroxidase, incubated with B7H3 monoclonal antibody (1:3,000; #66481–1-Ig; Proteintech) and horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG (H+L) (1:200; #GB23301; Servicebio) sequentially, and visualized with 0.05% 3,3-diaminobenzidine tetrachloride. The sections were counterstained with hematoxylin, and observed, and representative images were captured using an inverted phase-contrast microscope. The proportion of positive cells was quantified using ImageJ V.1.46r (National Institutes of Health (NIH)).

Construction of recombinant OAd

Adenovirus type 5 plasmids were purchased from the MiaoLing Plasmid Platform. OAd was constructed as we previously described. The viral E1a gene is controlled by the human telomerase reverse transcriptase promoter, which enables OAd to replicate selectively in tumor cells and induce tumor-specific oncolytic cell death.

The sequences of the variable domains of the anti-B7H3 scFv or anti-CD3 scFv were respectively based on an anti-human B7H3 monoclonal antibody, 8H9 (omburtamab), or a CD19×CD3 BiTE, blinatumomab. The variable domains of the scFvs were connected by a (G4S)3 linker, respectively, and two scFvs were connected by a G4S flexible linker to construct a B7H3-targeting BiTE. The BiTE sequence was optimized for human codon usage. OAd-B7H3-BiTE was produced by inserting a BiTE cassette into the OAd plasmid under the control of the cytomegalovirus (CMV) promoter.

Homologous recombination of OAd-B7H3-BiTE was performed with the AdMax system. Ad-genomic and shuttle plasmids were transfected into HEK293 cells to produce OAd-B7H3-BiTE. Once an extensive cytopathic effect was observed, the virus was harvested from HEK293 cells using three freeze-thaw cycles. The amplified viruses were purified by cesium chloride density gradient centrifugation and concentrated using a Dialysis Cassette (#66380; Thermo).

Viral oncolysis and replication assay

For viral oncolysis assay, MGC-803 or U87 cells were seeded into a 96-well-plate at a density of 2×104 cells/well and infected with serial dilutions of OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE. Four days after infection, cell viability was examined with Cell Counting Kit-8 (#C6005; NCM Biotech).

For viral replication assay, MGC-803 or U87 cells were seeded into a 12-well-plate at a density of 3×105 cells/well. The cells were infected with OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE (multiplicity of infection (MOI)=0.5 for MGC-803 cells; MOI=5 for U87 cells) and harvested at different time points. Total DNA was extracted using a TIANamp Genomic DNA Kit (#DP304; TIANGEN). 20 ng total DNA was detected by quantitative PCR (qPCR) against the Ad5 E1a gene with specific primers (primer-F-5′-ACCGGAGGTGATCGATCTTA-3′ and primer-R-5′- GGGTGCTCCACATAATCTAACA-3′; GENEWIZ). Fold changes relative to the corresponding non-infected cells were calculated.

Western blot

HEK293 cells were seeded into a 12-well-plate at a density of 3×105 cells/well and infected with OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE at an MOI of 0.5. After a 48-hour incubation, the cell culture supernatants were harvested, and 10 µL were taken out to perform a standard sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) polyacryamide gel electrophoresis. Polyvinylidene fluoride membranes were blocked with 5% non-fat milk for 45 min at room temperature and then incubated with an anti–anti-CD3-scFv antibody (1:5,000; synthesized by Zoonbio Biotechnology) at 4°C overnight. After washing, the membranes were incubated with HRP-conjugated AffiniPure goat anti-rabbit IgG (H+L) (1:5,000; SA00001-2; Proteintech) for 1 hour at room temperature. The membrane was washed and imaged using a precooled chemiluminescence instrument.

Isolation of peripheral blood mononuclear cells

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated from the peripheral blood of healthy adults using a PBMC Isolation Solution Kit (P8680; Solarbio). PBMCs isolation was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Tumor and spleen tissue processing for flow cytometry

To obtain single-cell suspensions, tumor tissues were cut into small fragments and incubated in lysis buffer (1 mg/mL collagenase IV (#abs47048003; Absin) and 10 unit/mL DNase I (#M0303S; New England Biolabs) in Hank’s buffer (#abs9258; Absin)) for 2 hours at 37°C. Tumor and spleen tissues were filtered through a 70 µm cell strainer (#abs7232; Absin). For tumor tissues, fibrous tissue was removed by centrifugation using a 30% Percoll gradient (#abs9102; Absin). The cells were then subjected to red blood cell lysis using 2 mL of red blood cell lysis buffer (#abs9241; Absin) and washed for subsequent flow cytometric staining.

Flow cytometry

The following antibodies or fluorescent dyes were purchased from BioLegend: Zombie NIR Fixable Viability Kit (#423105), Pacific Blue anti-human CD45 (#304022), Brilliant Violet 605 anti-human CD3 (#317321), FITC anti-human CD4 (#300506), PE-Cy7 anti-human CD4 (#300511), PerCP/Cyanine5.5 anti-human CD8 (#344710), PE anti-human CD8a (#300908), PE anti-human CD366 (Tim-3) (#345005), and PE anti-human CD107a (#328608), APC anti-human CD69 (#310910), PE-Cy7 anti-human CD69 (#310911), PE anti-human B7H3 (#351003), PE Donkey anti-rabbit IgG antibody (#406421), PE Mouse IgG1 κ Isotype Ctrl Antibody (#400111), Brilliant Violet 605 Mouse IgG2a κ Isotype Ctrl Antibody (#400269), Purified Rabbit Polyclonal Isotype Ctrl Antibody (#910801), CFSE Cell Division Tracker Kit (#423801). Human Fc Block (#564219) and APC PD-1 (#558694) were purchased from BD Biosciences. Super Bright 600 CD3 (#63-0037-42) was purchased from eBioscience-Thermo Fisher Scientific.

For antigen expression detection, T or target cells were stained with the BV605 CD3, PE B7H3 or their corresponding isotype control antibody for 30 min at 4°C.

For binding assay, T or target cells were incubated with the supernatants of the OAd-B7H3-BiTE-infected HEK293 cells for 40 min at 4°C. Then cells were incubated with rabbit anti–anti-CD3-scFv antibody (1:40) for 30 min at 4°C or its corresponding isotype control followed by PE Donkey anti-rabbit IgG antibody for 30 min at 4°C.

For the cell-bridging assay, doublet engagement was assessed between PBMCs and target cells. 1×105 target cells were pre-stained with 0.5 µmol/L 5-(and 6)-carboxyfluorescein diacetate succinimidyl ester (CFSE) and mixed with 3×105 PBMCs, followed by incubation with the supernatants of OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE infected HEK293 cells at 4°C for 40 min. The cells were then washed and stained with PECy7 CD4 and PE CD8. The percentages of the CFSE/CD4 and CFSE/CD8 pairs were quantified using flow cytometry.

For T-cell phenotyping staining, cell suspensions were stained with the Fixable Viability Kit for 20 min to label dead cells, incubated for 15 min with the Fc block antibody, and subsequently stained with the corresponding anti-human antibodies for 30 min at 4°C.

The stained cells were analyzed using a BD FACSymphony A1 (BD Biosciences). Flow cytometry data were analyzed using the FlowJo software (Tree Star).

Co-culture assays

The indicated supernatants were obtained by infecting HEK293 cells with OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE at MOI=0.5 for 48 hours. 2×104 target cells were seeded into a 96-well-plate. After 24 hours, 2×105 PBMCs were added. Supernatants from OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE infected HEK293 cells were added at a dilution of 1:3,000 (or 1:30 for high concentrations). Images were captured at the indicated time points, and cells or supernatants were collected for further analysis.

For cell size and surface marker detection, the cells were stained for flow cytometry analysis after 48 hours of co-culture.

For cytokine secretion assay, supernatants were collected after 48 hours of co-culture to measure interleukin-2 (IL-2) or interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) with the human IL-2 (#KTE6014; Abbkine) or IFN-γ (#KTE6011; Abbkine) ELISA Kit.

For T-cell proliferation assay, PBMCs were pre-stained with 0.5 µmol/L CFSE, and co-cultured with MGC-803 cells as described above for 4 days. The cells were stained for flow cytometry to examine the CFSE dilution.

For flow cytometry-based lysis assays, target cells were pre-stained with 0.5 µmol/L CFSE and seeded into a 96-well-plate, followed by infected with OAd or OAd-B7H3-BiTE (MOI=0.5 for MGC-803 and Raji cells; MOI=5 for U87 cells) for 24 hours. PBMCs were seeded and co-cultured for 48 hours. Cells were collected and stained with a Fixable Viability Kit to label dead cells. The percentage of dead target cells was calculated as follows: CFSE+ Zombie-Dye+/CFSE+.

Animal experiments

The ARRIVE reporting guidelines were used. 6-week-old female NOD-PrkdcscidIl2rgem1Smoc (M-NSG; #NM-NSG-001) mice were purchased from the Shanghai Model Organisms Center and were fed in a specific pathogen-free grade environment. The M-NSG mice were intraperitoneally injected with 1×107 freshly isolated PBMCs from a healthy donor. After 3 days, 2×106 MGC-803 tumor cells were implanted subcutaneously into the right flank of the mice. When the tumor volume reached 50 mm³, the mice were randomized into three groups (n=5 for each group) with blinding and injected intratumorally with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), OAd, or OAd-B7H3-BiTE for three treatments at a dose of 1×108 plaque forming unit (pfu). Tumor volumes and body weights were recorded every 2 days. Tumor volume was calculated using the formula 0.5×length×width2. 16 days after the first treatment, the tumor tissues and other organs were collected for further analysis.

Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR

Total RNA was isolated from disrupted tumor samples using TRIzol reagents (#TG-DP424; TIANGEN). Total RNA (2 µg) was reversed transcribed into complementary DNA using HiScript III RT SuperMix (#R323; Vazyme). Reverse transcription-quantitative PCR was performed with Universal Blue qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix (#11 184ES08; YEASEN) using a LightCycler 480 Instrument II (Roche). The Ad5 E1a primers were used as previously described. B7H3 BiTE primers were primer-F-5′-CCCGCTACTACGATGACCAC-3′ and primer-R-5′-GGTCACTTTCTCTCCTGGGC-3′. All PCR primers were purchased from GENEWIZ. Corresponding messenger RNA copies per μg total RNA were calculated.

Mice tissue H&E staining

The human tumor xenograft mice were obtained as described above. The hearts, livers, spleens, lungs and kidneys were excised and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde. Fixed tissues were dehydrated using increasing concentrations of ethanol. We then wax-dipped and embedded the tissues to prepare paraffin slides. Slides were dewaxed and hydrated using dimethylbenzene and decreasing concentrations of ethanol. Standard H&E staining was performed using H&E HD Constant Dye Kit (#G1076; Servicebio). The tissues were observed under a microscope.

Statistical analyses

GraphPad Prism V.8 was used for all statistical analysis. All data are presented as mean±SD. P value<0.05 was considered statistically significant. In the figures, asterisks are used to indicate p values as follows: NS: not significant; *p<0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001, and ****p<0.0001. To calculate differences between groups, we used Student’s t-test or two-way analysis of variance.

Results

Generation and characterization of B7H3-BiTE-armed oncolytic adenovirus

TIMER2.0 database indicates that B7H3, as a pan-cancer antigen, is widely expressed in various tumors, and is expressed at low levels in corresponding normal tissues (figure 1A). We examined three highly expressed tumor types by immunohistochemical staining and found that B7H3 was highly expressed on the membranes of the tumor cells (figure 1B–C).

Given the high and homogeneous expression of B7H3 in a high proportion of human malignancies, we generated an OAd encoding a B7H3-targeting BiTE (OAd-B7H3-BiTE). B7H3 BiTE was constructed by linking an scFv that recognizes human B7H3 to an scFv that recognizes human CD3 via a flexible glycine-serine linker. To generate OAd-B7H3-BiTE, B7H3 BiTE expression cassette was inserted into the backbone of OAd, under the transcriptional control of the CMV promoter (figure 1D). The oncolytic potential (figure 1E) and viral replication (figure 1F) of the recombinant OAd-B7H3-BiTE were comparable to those of unarmed OAd, suggesting that the properties of the OAd were not impaired by the insertion of BiTE transgenes. To detect BiTE secretion, we synthesized an antibody against anti-CD3 scFv. The antibody was bound to various concentrations of anti-CD3 scFv (figure 1G). Using this anti–anti-CD3-scFv antibody, we detected the secretion of B7H3 BiTE, which is approximately twice the size of anti-CD3 scFv (figure 1H).

OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE mediates connection between T and target cells

To evaluate the clinical treatment potential of B7H3 as a specific target, the level of B7H3 on the cell surface of four B7H3+ cell lines (MGC-803, SGC-7901, SH-SY5Y, U87) and one B7H3− cell line (Raji) were detected by flow cytometry (figure 2A). To determine the specific binding of BiTE to target cells, the supernatants of OAd-B7H3-BiTE-infected cells were incubated with target or effector cells. Flow cytometry was performed to detect the binding of BiTE in supernatants. As shown in figure 2B, B7H3 BiTE is specifically bound to B7H3+ but not to B7H3− cancer cells. Next, binding to T cells was detected. B7H3 BiTE is bound to Jurkat cells, a T-cell line expressing CD3 (figure 2C–D). Additionally, B7H3 BiTE is bound to CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in human PBMCs, but not CD4− CD8− non-T cells (figure 2D). We also observed decreased fluorescence intensity of OKT3 (an anti-CD3 antibody) after incubation with OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants (figure 2E), indicating competitive binding between OKT3 and B7H3 BiTE. To directly detect the BiTE-mediated cell-to-cell binding, doublet engagement was assessed between PBMCs and target cells. B7H3 BiTE is simultaneously bound to its two targets and promoted T and B7H3+ cell conjugates (figure 2F and online supplemental figure 1).

OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE mediates T-cell activation

BiTE activates T cells in an MHC-independent and polyclonal mode. To evaluate the effects of OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE on polyclonal T cells, unstimulated human PBMCs, as effector cells, were co-cultured with cancer cells without the addition of any stimuli. T-cell activation in PBMCs was detected.

We first added different concentrations of the supernatants from OAd-B7H3-BiTE-infected cells to the co-cultures. High concentrations of OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants increased the expression of the activation marker CD69 and cytotoxicity indicator CD107a in T cells in the presence or absence of target cells, whereas low concentrations of BiTE increased the expression of CD69 and CD107a in T cells only in the presence of target cells (online supplemental figure 2A). To explore the role of target cells in BiTE-mediated T-cell activation, a relatively low concentration was selected for further analysis of T-cell activation.

Two B7H3+ cell lines (MGC-803, U87) were used to detect BiTE-mediated T-cell activation. After 48 hours of co-culture, we observed cell-clustering with OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants (figure 3A), indicating T-cell activation. Furthermore, increased T-cell size was observed (figure 3B), indicating a promotion of biomass production during T-cell activation. In the presence of two B7H3+ cell lines, OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants led to an increase in CD69 expression in CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, as well as an increase in CD107a expression in CD8+ T cells (figure 3C). However, in the absence of target cells or in the presence of a B7H3− cell line, OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants failed to increase the expression of CD69 and CD107a in T cells (figure 3C), suggesting that a single CD3 signal is not sufficient to induce T-cell activation. The participation of various second signals may be necessary. Although high concentrations of OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants slightly promoted the expression of CD69 and CD107a in the absence of target cells or in the presence of B7H3− cells, the extent of this promotion in expression was lower than that observed with the two B7H3+ cells (online supplemental figure 2B).

OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants led to an increase in IFN-γ and IL-2 production in the presence of B7H3+ cells (figure 3D), thus revealing the positive role of OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE in promoting T-cell activation. T cells proliferate on effective activation, which is essential for adaptive immune responses. Therefore, the proliferation of T cells was quantified through the analysis of the CFSE dilution using flow cytometry after 4 days of co-culture. We observed that OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants enhanced T-cell proliferation (figure 3E). Compared with CD4+ T cells, a more obvious proliferation was observed in CD8+ T cells, possibly reflecting the difference in the activation effect of BiTE. These findings suggest that, on activation, OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants promote clonal expansion of T cells.

OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE mediates T-cell cytotoxicity

Next, we analyzed whether T-cell activation and proliferation induced by OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE was mirrored by the corresponding effect on tumor cell lysis.

The OAd-B7H3-BiTE supernatants mediated T-cell cytotoxicity against B7H3+ target cells, but not B7H3− cells (online supplemental figure 3A).

To better mimic the process of viral infection, we replaced the BiTE-containing viral supernatants with a direct infection of the target cells, allowing the target cells to secrete B7H3 BiTE. The target cells were pre-stained with CFSE to be distinguished from the immune cells in the co-cultures. Flow cytometry-based lysis assays revealed significant BiTE-mediated T-cell cytotoxicity against B7H3+ tumor cells (figure 4A–B) but not B7H3− tumor cells (figure 4C). A small number of PBMCs died when co-cultured with pre-infected MGC-803 cells, probably due to the excessive activation of T cells (figure 4A and online supplemental figure 3B).

Therapeutic effects of OAd-B7H3-BiTE in tumor xenografts model

We further characterized OAd-B7H3-BiTE in a human tumor xenograft model using immunodeficient M-NSG mice. Because M-NSG mice lack T cells, freshly isolated human PBMCs were injected intraperitoneally. As shown in figure 5A, 3 days after tumor implantation, mice were injected intratumorally with PBS, OAd, or OAd-B7H3-BiTE for three treatments. 16 days after the first treatment, the tumor tissues and other organs were collected for further analysis. We found that the majority of tumor-infiltrating human immune cells and splenocytes were T cells (figure 5B), indicating the persistence of human T cells in mice.

Mice treated with unarmed OAd showed better tumor control than those treated with PBS, reflecting the therapeutic effect of OVs (figure 5C–D). Yet, mice treated with OAd-B7H3-BiTE had a statistically significant tumor control compared with that in all other groups (figure 5C–D). Furthermore, there was no significant change in body weights among the different groups (figure 5C). Subsequently, viral infection and replication in the tumors were examined. The viral E1a gene was highly expressed in OAd and OAd-B7H3-BiTE injected tumors, and was observed to be at a similar level in the two groups (figure 5E). B7H3 BiTE was highly expressed in OAd-B7H3-BiTE injected tumors, indicating the successful and consistent delivery of B7H3 BiTE (figure 5F). H&E staining was performed in the main organs (figure 5G). No obvious lesions and organic injuries were detected, indicating that OAd-B7H3-BiTE treatment had no obvious systemic toxicity in mice.

OAd-B7H3-BiTE increases T-cell infiltration and activation

To explore the mechanism underlying OAd-B7H3-BiTE treatment, tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and splenocytes were isolated and analyzed using flow cytometry (online supplemental figure 4). As shown in figure 6A, compared with the PBS or OAd groups, OAd-B7H3-BiTE led to an increase in the proportion of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. We observed increased expression of the activation marker CD69 in CD8+ T cells, but NS in CD4+ T cells (figure 6B). This may reflect differences in the cell activation caused by OAd-B7H3-BiTE in vivo. Subsequently, immune checkpoint molecules were examined to detect T-cell exhaustion. There was a concomitant decrease in T cell immunoglobulin mucin-3 (Tim-3) expression in CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in the OAd-B7H3-BiTE treated tumors (figure 6C). Compared with OAd, OAd-B7H3-BiTE treatment led to a decrease in programmed cell death-1 (PD-1) expression in CD4+ T cells (figure 6D). These results demonstrated that OAd-B7H3-BiTE increased T-cell infiltration and activation. Finally, we evaluated the effect of intratumoral OAd-B7H3-BiTE injection on systemic immunity and observed an elevated CD69 expression in CD8+ T cells in the spleen (figure 6E).

Discussion

This study demonstrated the therapeutic efficacy of B7H3-targeting BiTE in combination with OVs. OVs are a new class of cancer immunotherapeutics that offer the benefits of selective replication in tumor cells, delivery of multiple transgenes, and promotion of antitumor immunity. Since talimogene laherparepvec was approved by the FDA in 2015, an increasing number of oncolytic and non-OVs have been approved globally for cancer treatment. Here, OVs were used to locoregionally deliver B7H3 BiTE and provide an enhanced T-cell immune environment for B7H3 BiTE.

The OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE was confirmed to physically connect T cells and target cells. To demonstrate the function of OV-delivered BiTE, we detected the activation of polyclonal T cells in several aspects, including cell clustering, cell size, activation markers, cytokine secretion, and proliferation, ultimately causing cytotoxicity against B7H3+ cells. Previous attempts to arm OVs with BiTE have used purified BiTE or concentrated viral supernatants for functional validation. Here, we attempted directly to use supernatants of infected cells and did not observe T-cell death with the corresponding concentrations of supernatants. B7H3− cells were used as a control to demonstrate that BiTE-mediated T-cell activation is dependent on B7H3. Another strategy that we did not perform is to generate B7H3 knockout cell lines, which may help us understand the effects of B7H3 loss in tumors. We observed elevated levels of two cytokines. IL-2 is a potent lymphoid cell growth factor that exerts its biological activity primarily on T cells and promotes their proliferation and maturation. IFN-γ is mainly related to activation of CD8+ T cells and Th1 response. These results indicated that OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE has a powerful ability to activate polyclonal T cells. In addition, OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE failed to activate T cells in the absence of target cells (high concentrations resulted in slight activation), probably because of the lack of second signals on the surface of the target cells. In fact, CD28-containing trispecific T-cell engagers have been developed, but their molecular sizes are relatively large, making it difficult to arm OVs.

We have repeatedly demonstrated in our study that BiTE activates both CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells. The tumor therapeutic effect could be attributed to BiTE-mediated CD8+ T-cell cytotoxicity; however, the function of CD4+ T cells should also be considered. BiTE-mediated persistent “synthetic” immune synapses as well as activation of CD3 signaling pathway is able to induce CD4+ T cell-mediated antitumor immune responses. The elevated IFN-γ may represent an enhanced Th1 response, which plays an important role in antitumor immunity.

To mimic clinical applications, we constructed human targets of BiTE. However, this also limited our application in the immunodeficient mouse model. In this study, PBMCs were transferred to M-NSG mice in order to reconstruct the human immune system. We performed intratumoral injections, which remains the most efficient and safest way to administer OVs. After being armed with BiTE, the viral replication capacity was not impaired. After OAd-B7H3-BiTE treatment, tumor growth was evidently controlled, indicating that delivery of BiTE was more efficient compared with OVs monotherapy. Consistent with in vitro experiments, OAd-B7H3-BiTE led to an increase in the number of T cells and the activation of T cells within the tumor, suggesting that the BiTE delivered by the OVs functioned within the tumor. In addition, we noticed a reduction in the expression of two immune checkpoint molecules, Tim-3 and PD-1, which are known to be associated with T-cell exhaustion. Recent studies suggest that CD4+ T cells, like CD8+ T cells, also had limited effector functions in the TME. We observed that OAd-B7H3-BiTE abrogated T-cell exhaustion in cancer. We also demonstrated that OAd-B7H3-BiTE has the potential to induce systemic T-cell activation. Collectively, these findings demonstrated that OAd-B7H3-BiTE could mediate antitumor T-cell immune response in vivo.

In our in vivo models, OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE was locoregionally delivered within the tumor with no obvious damage to other organs. To our knowledge, this is the first report on the delivery of B7H3 BiTE using OVs to reduce systemic exposure and increase the concentration of BiTE at the tumor site. It must be pointed out that the toxicities of BiTE are partially attributed to unavoidable low-level expression of target antigen in non-malignant tissues. The anti-B7H3 part in the B7H3 BiTE does not cross-react with mouse B7H3 precluding exact assessment of potential toxicities caused by inappropriate targeting of normal cells in preclinical rodent models.

Current therapeutic studies on B7H3 have mainly focused on its expression rather than its function. Given the recent success of immune checkpoint blockade in advanced cancers, targeting the immunosuppressive function of B7H3 has been considered as one of the most promising strategies for next-generation cancer immunotherapy. Although B7H3 was used as a tumor-associated antigen here, the immunosuppressive effects of B7H3 as an immune checkpoint cannot be ignored. Therefore, it is reasonable to speculate that the tumor therapeutic effect of OAd-B7H3-BiTE can be partially attributed to the blockade of B7H3. In this study, OV-delivered B7H3 BiTE may enhance T-cell function in three aspects: (1) stimulation of CD3, (2) mediation of contact between T cells and tumor/dendritic cells (activated slightly without tumor cells), and (3) blockade of the immunosuppressive effects of B7H3.

Although OAd-B7H3-BiTE was delivered intratumorally in this study, systemic administration of OVs affords more opportunities to both the primary tumor and any overt or undiagnosed metastatic deposits simultaneously. However, pre-existing neutralizing antibodies in humans remain a major hurdle to the systemic administration of OAd. To address this, biological (eg, cells) and non-biological (eg, nanoparticles and polymers) carriers have been developed to shield OAds. Additionally, capsid modification allows partial overcoming of pre-existing neutralizing antibodies against OAd. These strategies may provide safe and effective ways for systemic administration of OAds. Thus, OAd-B7H3-BiTE could be combined with these strategies in future preclinical and clinical studies.

In summary, our data demonstrated that OAd-B7H3-BiTE mediates the antitumor T-cell immune response. This engineered OV has the potential to enhance the overall efficacy of both OVs and BiTEs therapy for the treatment of cancer and could be therapeutically used alone or in combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors, targeted therapy, and chemotherapy for patients with cancer. B7H3-BiTE-armed OVs may represent a new treatment option for B7H3-positive malignancies and should be tested in carefully designed clinical trials.
