# Enhancing the potency of 5T4 mRNA vaccine by CD70 mRNA-LNPs through ADCC and T cell boosting in prostate cancer therapy.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Prostate cancer remains a significant health challenge, as conventional treatments often fail to provide long-term benefits in the advanced stages. This study explored the potential of mRNA vaccines as novel therapeutic strategies, specifically focusing on the combination of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA delivered via lipid nanoparticles (LNPs). 5T4, which is highly expressed in various tumors but minimally expressed in normal tissues, is an ideal target for immunotherapy. CD70 was selected for its capacity to enhance T cell activation and augment the overall immune response.

**RESULTS**: Our findings demonstrated that the combination of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs significantly enhanced both humoral and cellular immunity, resulting in improved tumor suppression and prolonged survival in a prostate cancer mouse model. Mechanistically, these LNPs increased the immune responses, including elevated 5T4-specific antibodies, enhanced CD8 + T cell activity, and more active natural killer (NK) cells.

**CONCLUSIONS**: These results suggest that this combined mRNA vaccine strategy may offer more effective and durable treatments for prostate cancer and that CD70 mRNA, as an immune activator, has broader potential for cancer immunotherapy.

Background

Prostate cancer (PCa) is one of the most prevalent malignant tumors among men worldwide, and its incidence and mortality rates continue to rise, presenting a significant public health challenge. According to recent statistics, more than 1.4 million new cases are diagnosed globally each year, and this number is expected to increase in the coming decades. Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) can temporarily control disease progression, but almost all patients eventually develop castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC). Early diagnosis and radical surgery or radiotherapy can achieve long-term survival in some cases; however, traditional treatment remains largely unsatisfactory for patients with advanced metastatic disease. Therefore, developing new preventive and therapeutic strategies to improve patient outcomes are urgently required.

Recently, mRNA-based vaccine technology has garnered significant attention owing to advantages such as rapid production, ease of customization, and immune activation potential. Given the preliminary success of mRNA vaccines for other cancer types, accelerating the development of mRNA vaccines for prostate cancer is particularly promising and has great potential. The mechanism of action of cancer vaccines involves the introduction of tumor-related antigens, stimulation of the immune response, and utilization of the immune system to eliminate cancer cells. BNT122 and mRNA-4157 are two personalized neoantigen-specific cancer vaccines based on mRNA technology capable of encoding 20–34 patient-specific neoantigens. When combined with anti-PD-L1 antibodies, these vaccines demonstrate efficacy in pancreatic cancer and melanoma, achieving favorable clinical outcomes in various cancer types, including colon and lung cancers. Although personalized vaccines offer unique advantages in precisely targeting the tumor characteristics of individual patients, tumor-associated antigen (TAA) vaccines present greater appeal regarding universality and cost-effectiveness, making them suitable for large-scale implementation. Furthermore, TAA vaccines have been shown to effectively stimulate immune responses and multiple clinical trials have validated their safety and efficacy. Currently, CV9103, CV9104, and BNT112 are the primary mRNA vaccines for prostate cancer (PCa) in clinical trials. The TAAs included PSMA, PSA, PSCA, STEAP1, and PAP. Although these vaccines elicited a certain immune response in early trials, their clinical response rates have not met expectations. Within the tumor microenvironment, T cells frequently encounter inhibitory signals and fatigue. Relying solely on TAA vaccines may not sufficiently overcome these inhibitory factors, as the absence of costimulatory molecule signals can impair T cell function and diminish the anti-tumor effect. Therefore, combining mRNA drugs that encode immune regulatory factors to enhance the anti-tumor immune response of tumor-associated antigen (TAA) vaccines represents a crucial strategy for improving the efficacy of existing prostate cancer (PCa) TAA vaccines.

Encoding cytokines and costimulatory molecules through mRNA drugs to stimulate anti-tumor immune responses and reduce systemic toxicity is an effective means of treating tumors. Our team previously proposed the use of 4-1BBL, OX40L, and CCR7 as immune adjuvants to enhance the efficacy of mRNA vaccine efficacy. Moreover, in situ delivery of immunostimulatory molecules such as OX40L, CD80, and CD86 can transiently activate strong inflammatory responses. Recently, a strategy aimed at promoting tumor immunity has been to mitigate the immunosuppressive effects induced by chronic stimulation of the CD70-CD27 axis. However, the transient expression of CD70 in activated immune cells (including T cells, B cells, dendritic cells (DCs), and natural killer cells (NK cells) often leads to an overlooked role. For instance, CD70 engages CD27, which is predominantly expressed on T cells, delivering a crucial second signal for the activation of CD4 + and CD8 + T cells during tumor eradication. mRNA-based therapies have the potential to precisely extend the transient expression of CD70 while circumventing the immunosuppressive effects associated with prolonged chronic stimulation. A previous study by Brenda et al. demonstrated that electroporation-based delivery of mRNA encoding CD70, CD40L, and TLR4 into dendritic cells (DCs) significantly promotes T-cell activation in vivo. Building on this, we leveraged the advantages of a Lipid Nanoparticle (LNP) platform to deliver CD70 mRNA to enhance immune responses.

In the development of mRNA vaccines for prostate cancer, identifying effective tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) as targets for immunotherapy is essential. In recent years, with a deeper understanding of the biological characteristics of prostate cancer, several candidate TAAs (PSMA, PSCA, PSA, TGM4) have been identified and confirmed by our team to activate immune responses against prostate cancer. To address tumor heterogeneity and expand the clinical applicability of mRNA vaccines, we selected suitable TAAs. Among these, 5T4 has garnered significant attention because of its high expression in various solid tumors and its association with poor prognosis. Initially discovered in human placental tissue, the 5T4 protein (also known as placental membrane protein 5) is considered a promising target for immunotherapy because of its aberrant expression across multiple tumor types. Given its low expression in normal adult tissues and elevated levels in prostate cancer cells, 5T4 represents an ideal candidate for novel therapeutic approaches. Previous studies have demonstrated in vivo immune activation of 5T4 using viral vectors; however, the limitations of viral vector technology hinder its rapid clinical application. In contrast, mRNA vaccines based on the lipid nanoparticle (LNP) technology offer several advantages. Therefore, the key objective of this study was to explore the feasibility and mechanisms of 5T4-LNPs as potential candidates for prostate cancer immunotherapy, with the goal of expanding treatment options for future patients.

In this study, we developed 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs using an established LNP delivery system and demonstrated their capacity to transfect DCs in vitro. We observed that the combination of 5T4 and CD70 enhanced immune responses, inhibited tumor growth, and extended survival in a mouse model of prostate cancer. Mechanistically, CD70 promotes the activation of CD8 + T cells, enhances germinal center formation, stimulates B cells to produce tumor-specific antibodies, and facilitates NK cell-mediated antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), resulting in the destruction of tumor cells. Our findings suggest that 5T4 and CD70-LNPs hold considerable potential for prostate cancer therapy, and that CD70 may also function as an immune adjuvant in broader immunotherapies.

Materials and methods

Culture of cell lines

RM-1, Panc02, and B16-OVA cells were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium (Gibco. USA) supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum (Gibco. USA) and 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin Solution (Pricella. USA). All cell lines were obtained from the ATCC website. All cell lines were regularly checked for mycoplasma contamination (MycAwayTM Plus-Color One-Step Mycoplasma Detection Kit. YEASEN. China.).

To isolate bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs), bone marrow was harvested from femurs and tibias of C57BL/6J mice. After lysing red blood cells using ACK lysis buffer, the remaining bone marrow cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS), 1% penicillin–streptomycin, 20 ng/mL recombinant GM-CSF (rm-GM-CSF, Peprotech. USA), and 20 ng/mL recombinant IL-4 (rm-IL-4, Peprotech. USA) to promote dendritic cell differentiation. Every two days, 50% of the medium was replaced to maintain optimal growth conditions. On day 6, immature BMDCs were harvested from the culture dish and used in subsequent experiments.

mRNA synthesis

The 5T4 mRNA, CD70 mRNA, ovalbumin (OVA) mRNA, and CLDN18.2 mRNA were transcribed in vitro using T7 RNA polymerase (Megascript, Ambion. USA) and linearized plasmid templates. In the transcription reaction, methylated pseudouridine (m1Ψ)-5′-triphosphate (TriLink) was used to replace the conventional uridine (UTP), resulting in mRNA with nucleotide modifications. Subsequently, the synthesized mRNA was 5′-capped using 2′-O-methyltransferase (ScriptCap; CellScript. USA) and an m7G capping kit to enhance its stability and translation efficiency. The synthesized mRNA was purified using a cellulose column (Sigma-Aldrich. USA) to ensure removal of impurities. Quality control of the synthesized mRNA was performed using capillary electrophoresis (Agilent Technologies. USA) and an Agilent 5200 Fragment Analyzer (Agilent Technologies. USA). The integrity of the mRNA was assessed using the DNF-472-1000 kit and gel retardation method. The synthesized mRNA samples were stored at -80 °C until further use.

RNA-LNPs Preparation and characterization

The ionizable amino lipid (E12A1A3) synthesis and complete methodology for mRNA-LNP synthesis have been thoroughly described in our previous article [33]. The organic phase was dissolved in ethanol (ethanol phase) at a molar ratio of E12A1A3/ /DSPC (Avanti. China)/cholesterol (Avanti. China)/DMG-PEG 2000 (Avanti. China) of 50:10:38.5:1.5, while the mRNA was dissolved in sodium citrate solution (aqueous phase). The ethanol and aqueous phases were combined at a 1:3 volume ratio with a lipid-to-mRNA N/P ratio of 5:1. Lipids and mRNA were then mixed using a syringe pump within a microfluidic chip device INanoE (MicroNano. China). The final product was subjected to centrifugation at 1200 × g for 10 min through a 100 kDa ultrafiltration membrane, followed by washing and resuspension in PBS. The preparations were stored at 4 °C. The particle size distribution of the mRNA-LNPs was analyzed using dynamic light scattering (DLS) on a Zetasizer Nano (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK). The Quant-iT RiboGreen RNA Assay Kit (Invitrogen. USA) was used to assess concentration and encapsulation efficiency.

mRNA-LNP cytotoxicity test

The BMDCs were then treated with various concentrations of mRNA-LNPs or PBS for 24 h. After incubation, BMDCs were collected and cell viability was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK8) assay (Beyotime. China). Briefly, cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 104–1 × 105 cells/well in a 96-well plate and allowed to adhere overnight. After 24 h of incubation, cells were treated with various concentrations of mRNA-LNPs or PBS as a control. Following treatment, 10 µL of CCK8 solution was added to each well and the plate was incubated for an additional 2 h at 37 °C. The absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a microplate reader, and cell viability was calculated as the percentage of viable cells relative to the control group. Cell viability was determined using the following formula: Relative viability (%) = [(OD_treated − OD_blank) / (OD_control − OD_blank)] × 100. This assay provides a reliable measure of cell survival and proliferation in response to the treatment. Apoptosis was assessed using an Annexin V-FITC/Propidium Iodide (PI) Apoptosis Detection Kit (Life Technologies. USA). After treatment, the cells were harvested, stained with Annexin V-FITC and propidium iodide (PI), and analyzed by flow cytometry. Early apoptotic cells were identified as Annexin V-positive and PI-negative, while late apoptotic or necrotic cells were both Annexin V- and PI-positive. The percentage of apoptotic cells in each treatment group was calculated and compared with that in the control group. This assay provides a reliable method for assessing mRNA-LNP-induced apoptosis in BMDCs, enabling evaluation of its cytotoxic effects.

ELispot assay for 5T4 mRNA-LNP

To evaluate the immunogenicity of 5T4 mRNA, an ELISpot assay was performed to measure IFN-γ production by T cells upon stimulation with 5T4 mRNA-treated dendritic cells (DCs). Bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) were differentiated from C57BL/6J mice and treated with 5T4 mRNA-LNPs for 24 h. T cells were isolated from the spleens of C57BL/6J mice and co-cultured with mRNA-treated BMDCs at a ratio of 1:10 for 24–48 h. Following incubation, IFN-γ secretion by activated T cells was quantified using an IFN-γ ELISpot assay (Dakewe. China). The wells were pre-coated with an anti-IFN-γ capture antibody, and after incubation, spot formation was visualized using a biotinylated detection antibody and streptavidin-alkaline phosphatase conjugate. The number of spot-forming units (SFUs) was counted and the results were normalized to the number of T cells per well. This method provides a quantitative measure of T cell response to 5T4 mRNA, reflecting its immunogenic potential.

In vivo CTL assay

To assess the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells against 5T4-expressing tumor cells, an in vitro cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) killing assay was performed using RM-1 prostate cancer cells as target cells. RM-1 cells stably expressing the 5T4 antigen were labeled with carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE. ThermoFisher. USA) to track cell viability. CD8+ T cells were isolated from the spleens of C57BL/6J mice and activated using 5T4 mRNA-LNP-pulsed BMDCs for 48 h. After activation, CD8+ T cells were co-cultured with CFSE-labeled RM-1 cells at various effector-to-target ratios (10:1). After 4 h of coincubation, the level of target cell lysis was determined by flow cytometry using a live/dead staining kit to differentiate between dead and viable cells. The percentage of specific lysis was calculated as the proportion of CFSE-labeled RM-1 cells stained with a dead cell marker. This assay provides a quantitative measure of the cytotoxic activity of CD8+ T cells against 5T4-expressing tumor cells.

Animal experiment

Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee, Sun Yat-Sen University approved all the animal procedures (SYSU-IACUC-2024-001444). All animal experiments were performed under pathogen-free conditions, in compliance with the Laboratory Animal Care and Use Guidelines set by the National Institute of Health. C57BL/6 mice, aged 6–8 weeks, were purchased from Gemmatech. Subcutaneous tumor models, including RM-1, Panc02, and B16-OVA, with tumor cell inoculation doses of 2 × 105, 5 × 105, and 5 × 105 cells, respectively, were used for efficacy experiments. Each treatment group consisted of at least five mice. Immunization (0.5 mg/kg mRNA-LNP, intramuscular injection) commenced when tumors reached < 100 mm. Tumor size was measured with calipers every 2–3 days until the tumor volume reached an endpoint of 1500 mm³. The tumor volume was calculated using the following formula: (length × width²)/2. Mice were euthanized if they lost ≥ 20% of their body weight, were unable to eat, or had tumors exceeding 1.5 cm³.

Flow cytometry analysis

BMDCs analysis

BMDCs were cultured and stimulated under various conditions and their phenotypic characteristics were analyzed using flow cytometry. After harvesting, cells were washed with PBS and resuspended in PBS containing 5% FBS. The cells were then incubated with specific antibodies targeting surface markers including CD11c, MHC-II, CD86, and CD80 (BioLegend. USA). Following incubation, the cells were washed again and analyzed using a Beckman Coulter flow cytometer. Data acquisition was performed using the same flow cytometer and subsequent data analysis was conducted using FlowJo or CytExpert software. The expression levels of surface markers were used to quantitatively assess the activation and maturation of the dendritic cells.

Mouse tissue analysis

Spleen and lymph node samples from tumor-bearing mice were analyzed to evaluate immune cell populations and their functional characteristics using flow cytometry following various treatments. Tumor-bearing spleens and lymph nodes were dissected and single-cell suspensions were prepared through mechanical grinding and filtration. The resulting cell suspensions were washed with PBS and resuspended prior to staining with specific antibodies targeting the surface markers of T cells (CD3, CD4, CD8), B cells (B220), dendritic cells (CD11c), and natural killer (NK) cells (NK1.1). Following staining, cells were analyzed using flow cytometry to assess the frequency and activation status of distinct immune cell populations. Data were acquired using a Beckman Coulter flow cytometer and subsequent analyses were conducted using FlowJo or CytExpert software. The expression levels of surface markers were quantified to evaluate the contribution of different immune cell populations to immune response.

Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were conducted with GraphPad Prism (Version 8.0, CA, USA). A p value less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant (*p < 0.05; **p < 0.01; 426 ***p < 0.001; ns, not significant).

Results

Preparation, characterization and immunogenicity of mRNA-LNP

Initially, the open reading frame (ORF) sequences of the target genes 5T4 and CD70 were optimized, and the corresponding mRNAs were synthesized in vitro (Fig. 1A). The synthesized mRNAs were subsequently transfected into bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs), and western blotting and flow cytometry analyses confirmed the successful expression of 5T4 and CD70, thereby validating the efficient transfection and expression of the target genes (Fig. 1B-C). Next, the target mRNA was encapsulated in RNA-lipid nanoparticles (RNA-LNPs) using E12A1A3, an innovative ionizable lipid that has been previously developed and characterized. Dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis of the resulting LNPs revealed that the particle diameter ranged from 90 to 100 nm, with a polydispersity index (PDI) of less than 0.2, indicating a uniform size distribution (Fig. 1D). Additionally, the zeta potential of the LNPs stabilized at approximately + 5 mV, with the mRNA encapsulation efficiency exceeding 90% (Figure S1A-B). Further experiments confirmed that the mRNA encapsulated within the LNPs maintained excellent stability during storage, with minimal degradation and retained functionality over time (Figure S1C-D; Figure S2A-C). After validating the physicochemical properties of the LNPs, the biosafety of mRNA-LNPs was assessed. BMDCs were exposed to various concentrations of mRNA-LNPs in a concentration-dependent manner. The results indicated that BMDCs exhibited optimal growth conditions during co-transfection, with a maximum tolerable concentration of 15 µg/ml (Fig. 1E). Therefore, 1 µg/ml was chosen for subsequent experiments to prevent significant apoptosis of BMDCs (Fig. 1F-G). Safety assessments were performed across all treatment groups to ensure that mice demonstrated good tolerance to therapy. Blood biochemical analyses were performed to monitor potential systemic toxicity. The results showed that Key markers of liver and kidney function, including alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), and creatinine (CREAT), remained within normal physiological ranges across all groups, indicating no signs of hepatic or renal dysfunction (Fig. 1H). These findings suggested that the administration of LNP-based formulations, including CD70-LNPs and 5T4-LNPs, was well tolerated without inducing measurable biochemical toxicity. To further corroborate these observations, histological examinations of the major organs (the heart, liver, kidneys, spleen, and lungs) were conducted. Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining of tissue sections revealed no significant histopathological changes or tissue damage in any treatment group (Figure S3) After confirming both in vitro and in vivo safety of 5T4-LNPs, their immunogenicity was further assessed. In healthy C57BL/6 mice, three immunizations were performed, and T cells were isolated from the spleen and co-cultured with BMDCs pretreated with various formulations. IFN-γ production was measured using ELISpot assay (Fig. 1I). The results demonstrated that BMDCs pretreated with 5T4-LNPs had significantly enhanced IFN-γ production compared to control BMDCs, indicating that 5T4-LNPs effectively activated specific immune responses, highlighting their potential as tumor-associated antigen mRNA vaccines (Fig. 1J-K). This study demonstrated the successful optimization, synthesis, and delivery of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA via LNPs, confirming their efficient expression in dendritic cells, in vitro and in vivo safety, and ability to activate immune responses, highlighting their potential as tumor-associated antigen mRNA vaccines.

Maturation and activation of BMDCs and cytokine levels in T cells after in vitro treatment with different combinations of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs

After completing the safety validation of 5T4-LNP and CD70-LNP, and assessing the immunogenicity of 5T4-LNP, we further investigated the phenotypic modulation of BMDCs by these LNPs. Phenotypic changes in BMDCs were evaluated 24 h after transfection with 5T4-LNPs, CD70-LNPs, or their combination. The results showed that both 5T4-LNPs and CD70-LNPs significantly increased the expression of the proliferation marker Ki-67 (Fig. 2A), indicating enhanced cell proliferation. Additionally, both treatments upregulated the levels of the antigen-presenting molecule MHC-II, suggesting improved antigen-presenting capacity of the transfected BMDCs. Moreover, CD70-LNPs notably upregulated key co-stimulatory molecules, such as CD70, CD40, and the maturation markers CD80 and CD86 (Fig. 2B-F). When combined with 5T4-LNPs, the upregulation of these molecules was even more pronounced, suggesting a synergistic effect on the activation and maturation of dendritic cells. Further analysis revealed an increase in the expression of functional regulatory markers CD103, CD69, and OX40L, which enhanced the immunogenic characteristics of the transfected BMDCs (Fig. 2G-I). Subsequently, after 48 h of co-culture between T cells and BMDCs treated with different LNP formulations, only the combination group elicited robust secretion of TNF-α and IFN-γ by T cells, indicating that dual treatment is essential for fully activating the cytotoxic capacity of T cells (Fig. 2J-M). However, the expression of the T cell regulatory molecules 4-1BBL and B7-H2, as well as the DC migration marker CCR7, did not change significantly (Figure S4). These results suggested that although transfection promoted the activation and maturation of BMDCs, its impact on their migratory potential or specific regulatory pathways was not significant. These findings indicate that 5T4-LNPs and CD70-LNPs effectively enhanced the proliferation, antigen-presenting capacity, and co-stimulatory molecule expression of BMDCs, with a synergistic effect observed when both formulations were combined, indicating their potential to enhance dendritic cell activation and maturation for improved immunotherapeutic efficacy. Building upon these results, future studies will investigate the functional implications of these phenotypic changes in eliciting robust antitumor immune responses.

CD70 significantly enhanced the humoral and cellular immunity initiated by 5T4-LNPs in vivo

mRNA vaccines are recognized for their ability to induce robust immune responses through activation of both humoral and cellular immunity. Based on the phenotypic modulation of BMDCs by 5T4-LNPs and CD70-LNPs, the roles of these two mRNA-LNPs in in vivo immune response were investigated. To evaluate the specific role of CD70-LNPs in driving these immune responses, immunization experiments were conducted in tumor-free C57BL/6 mice. After three rounds of immunization, blood samples were collected for serum analysis and T cells were isolated from the spleen for functional assessment (Fig. 3A). Serum analysis revealed a significant increase in 5T4-specific antibodies in mice treated with CD70-LNPs, suggesting that CD70-LNPs play a key role in enhancing humoral immune response (Fig. 3B). To further assess the cellular immune response, the activation status of CD8 + T cells was analyzed. Compared to the group treated with only 5T4-LNPs, mice treated with a combination of 5T4-LNPs and CD70-LNPs showed a 15–20% increase in the secretion of IFN-γ and TNF-α from CD8 + T cells, indicating a synergistic effect of the combined treatment in enhancing the activation and function of cytotoxic T cells (Fig. 3C-F). Additionally, RM-1 cells and their transplanted tumors expressed the 5T4 protein (Figure S5A). To further evaluate the cytotoxic potential of these activated CD8 + T cells, they were co-cultured with the prostate cancer cell line RM-1. The combined treatment group exhibited the highest cytotoxic efficiency, significantly enhancing the killing of RM-1 target cells compared to the 5T4-LNPs-only group (Fig. 3G-H). However, after the second stimulation of BMDCs with 5T4-LNPs, no significant difference in splenic T cell proliferation was observed between the combined treatment group and the 5T4-LNPs-only group (Figure S5B). This enhanced cytotoxicity underscores the potential of CD70-LNPs to boost the antitumor activity of cytotoxic T lymphocytes, further demonstrating the dual role of CD70-LNPs in promoting both humoral and cellular immunity.

CD70 mRNA-LNPs facilitates anti-tumor efficacy in the B16-OVA and pancreatic cancer models

Based on the results presented above, the potential of CD70 as a target for tumor immunotherapy will be further validated and its role in promoting antitumor immune responses will be assessed. The B16-OVA model is a widely used murine model for evaluating antitumor immune responses and vaccine efficacy, particularly in the study of tumor immunotherapies and vaccines. This model provides an experimental platform for assessing immune system responses to OVA by combining the B16 melanoma cell line with recombinant ovalbumin (OVA). This study elucidated the mechanisms by which CD70-LNPs enhance immune responses in a B16-OVA classical tumor model. OVA-specific antibody titers were measured regularly throughout the treatment (Fig. 4A-B). The results showed that The total IgG levels in the combined treatment group (OVA + CD70-LNPs) were consistently higher than those in the OVA-LNP group, suggesting that CD70-LNPs play a pivotal role in enhancing the humoral immune response. Notably, no significant changes in body weight were observed in any treatment group, suggesting that all treatments were well tolerated with no systemic toxicity observed. Both OVA-LNPs and OVA + CD70-LNPs treatments significantly inhibited tumor growth, with the combined treatment group showing notably better results than the single-treatment groups (Fig. 4C-E). The tumor volume in the combined treatment group remained consistently below 500 mm³, with a continuous decline throughout the study, further demonstrating the enhanced antitumor activity of CD70-LNPs (Figure S6A-D). Immune cell infiltration analysis of the tumor microenvironment revealed a slight increase in CD8 + T cell infiltration in the OVA + CD70-LNPs group compared to that in the OVA-LNPs or CD70-LNPs groups (Fig. 4F-G). Although no significant change was observed in the proportion of tetramer-positive T cells (Fig. 4H), the NK cell activation markers CD69 and CD107a were significantly upregulated in the OVA + CD70-LNPs group (Fig. 4I-J). This upregulation was strongly correlated with the observed increase in IgG levels, suggesting that the combined treatment effectively enhanced NK cell activation. Additionally, an increased proportion of NK cells and CD69-positive cells in the spleen was observed, along with an increase in CD4 + and CD8 + T cell populations (Figure S7A-D). To further investigate the mechanism by which CD70-LNPs enhance specific IgG production, a detailed analysis of splenic immune response was conducted. The results showed that in mice treated with CD70-LNPs, there was a significant increase in the number of germinal center B cells (B220 + CD95 + GL-7+), plasma cells (B220 + CD138+) (Fig. 4K-N), and helper T cells (Figure S7E-F). These findings suggest that CD70 plays a key role in enhancing antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) through NK cell activation, significantly promoting the overall cellular immune response. Consequently, these enhanced immune responses translate into stronger tumor suppression and sustained immune activation. Furthermore, CLDN18.2 is considered an effective target for pancreatic cancer treatment, has been validated in CAR-T, ADC, and mRNA vaccines. To further assess the therapeutic effect of CD70-LNPs in non-classical models, a subcutaneous Panc02 tumor mouse model was established. The results showed that the combination of CLDN18.2-LNPs and CD70-LNPs yielded the most significant tumor suppression, significantly prolonging the survival of mice, with no noticeable adverse effects in any of the treatment groups (Fig. 4O-Q, Figure S8A-D). These observations suggested that CD70 plays a pivotal role in enhancing ADCC, likely through NK cell activation, and significantly contributes to the overall cellular immune response. Consequently, these enhancements translate into improved tumor suppression and robust immune activation.

CD70 mRNA-LNPs enhances the immunotherapeutic efficacy of the 5T4 mRNA-LNPs in the prostate cancer mouse model

The immunogenicity of 5T4 and its specific cytotoxicity against RM-1 cells in vitro have been confirmed, and CD70 has demonstrated potent immune-enhancing efficacy in various tumor models. Next, we evaluated the therapeutic efficacy of a combination of 5T4 and CD70-LNPs in a mouse model of prostate cancer. Following administration of the five LNPs, the combination group exhibited a significant advantage in both tumor volume reduction and overall survival. Tumor volumes consistently remained below 500 mm³ in the combination group mice, demonstrating superior antitumor ability compared to the other groups. Notably, compared to the control group, the combined therapy significantly increased the average survival time, indicating substantial therapeutic benefits. (Fig. 5A-H). Given the enhanced ADCC and cellular immune responses previously observed with CD70-LNPs, we further analyzed the effects of combination therapy on T and NK cell populations in both the spleen and tumor-infiltrating immune cells. The combination group showed a significant increase in the proportion of CD4 + and CD8 + T cells in the spleen, indicating systemic immune activation (Fig. 5I-O). However, the most pronounced increase was observed in tumor-infiltrating CD8 + T cells and NK cells, suggesting that the therapy effectively boosted effector cell infiltration into the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 5P-S). The combination group exhibited the highest levels of IFN-γ and TNF-α secretion by tumor-infiltrating CD8⁺ T cells (Fig. 5T-U). While no significant changes were noted in tumor-infiltrating dendritic cells, a slight reduction in splenic DCs was observed, possibly reflecting specific immune modulation (Fig. S9A-C). Immunofluorescence staining further supported these findings, revealing elevated expression of the effector cytokines IFN-γ and TNF-α at the tumor site in the combination group. This indicates that CD70 plays a dual role in enhancing both humoral and cellular immunity, contributing to the observed tumor suppression (Fig. S9D-E). To better understand the broader immune remodeling induced by the therapy, we analyzed splenic immune cell populations. CD8 + T cells from treated mice exhibited significantly increased expression of the stem cell marker TCF-7 and effector molecules granzyme B and perforin, indicating enhanced cellular immunity (Fig. 6A-C). Similarly, TCF-7 expression was significantly elevated in CD4 + T cells without a corresponding increase in regulatory T cell (Treg) population (Fig. S10A-B), suggesting that therapy did not promote an immunosuppressive state. This finding was further supported by the stable expression of CTLA-4 (Fig. S10C-D), indicating that CD70-LNPs selectively enhanced effector T-cell responses without driving immunosuppressive pathways. Moreover, combination therapy increased the proportion of central memory T cells (TCM) in the spleen, implying that CD70-LNPs promote the generation of long-lasting memory CD8 + T cells, which are crucial for sustained antitumor immunity (Fig. 6D-G). Beyond T cell responses, splenic DCs exhibited increased expression of the maturation markers CD86 and MHC-II, which is consistent with the activation of BMDCs observed in vitro, further supporting the conclusion that CD70-LNPs not only activated T cells but also enhanced antigen presentation by both macrophages and DCs (Fig. 6H-K). Additionally, CD70-LNPs significantly influenced macrophage polarization. M1 macrophages displayed elevated MHC-II expression, indicating an enhanced antigen presentation (Fig. 6L-M). The therapy promoted macrophage differentiation toward the pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype (F4/80+/CD206-) (Fig. 6N-P), as confirmed by immunofluorescence staining of tumor sections (Fig. 6Q). In conclusion, CD70-LNPs not only facilitate TAA-specific immunity, but also enhance macrophage inflammatory polarization to inhibit tumor progression, and the alliance of these effects results in the anti-tumor efficiency of CD70 and TAA-LNPs.

Bulk RNA sequencing results of dendritic cells in the spleen and T cells in the draining lymph nodes under different treatment groups

Bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) was performed on DCs isolated from the spleen and T cells isolated from the draining lymph nodes of mice administered with LNPs (Fig. 7A). Differential gene expression analysis of splenic DCs revealed a marked upregulation of genes associated with key DC subsets. Specifically, genes linked to conventional DC1 (cDC1) differentiation, such as Tlr3, Cadm1, Irf8, Strip2, and Clec9a, were elevated. These genes are essential for antigen cross-presentation and priming of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), emphasizing the critical role of cDC1 cells in antitumor immunity. Plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) are known for their ability to produce large quantities of type I interferons, which contribute to both antiviral and antitumor responses. Genes related to pDCs, including Spib, Ly6c2, Runx2, Siglech, and Cd209a, were significantly upregulated in the 5T4 + CD70 group, indicating an enhanced pDC differentiation. Furthermore, genes associated with the conventional DC2 (cDC2) lineage, including Cd22, Cd4, Usp18, and Lyz1, were upregulated. Given the crucial role of cDC2 in promoting the development of helper T cells and B cells, these genes may play a pivotal role in polarizing Th2 responses and facilitating B cell development, thereby mediating (ADCC) (Fig. 7B-D). These findings highlight that CD70-5T4 LNPs modulate DC differentiation, particularly favoring cDC1 and pDC lineage development, both instrumental in initiating and sustaining CTL responses that are vital for effective antitumor immunity. In parallel, RNA-seq analysis of T cells isolated from draining lymph nodes of the combination group mice revealed a significant increase in transcriptomic signatures associated with memory CD8 + T cells as well as Th1 and Th17 T cell populations, which are key drivers of cellular immunity (Fig. 7E). The upregulation of transcriptional regulators involved in T cell receptor (TCR) signaling, including Nfkb1, Nfatc2, and Fos, reflects robust activation of TCR-mediated pathways critical for T cell proliferation, activation, and differentiation into effector populations capable of anti-tumor activity. Moreover, increased expression of genes related to T-cell differentiation and function, such as Runx3, Cd44, and Bcl6, has been observed, promoting effector and memory T-cell development. Concurrently, effector molecules, including Ifng (encoding interferon gamma), Prf1 (encoding perforin), and Gzmb (encoding granzyme B), were upregulated, underscoring the role of T cell-mediated cytotoxicity in tumor clearance. Upregulation of co-stimulatory molecules and chemokine receptors, such as Cd27 and Cxcr6, further indicated enhanced T cell activation, migration, and interaction within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 7F-J). These results collectively demonstrate that 5T4-CD70 combination therapy promotes DC differentiation toward lineages essential for initiating CTL responses while simultaneously driving robust T cell activation and differentiation into effector cells. This dual activation of both DC and T cell compartments underscores the potent immunostimulatory effects of combination therapy, contributing to enhanced antitumor immunity.

Discussion

In this study, 5T4 and CD70 mRNA were synthesized, encapsulated in LNPs, and successfully transfected into DCs, enabling efficient antigen expression and delivery. Both 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs, whether used individually or in combination, enhanced DC maturation, antigen presentation, and T cell activation. Combination therapy significantly increased CD8 + T cell cytotoxicity, enhanced IgG production, and activated NK cells, ultimately leading to improved tumor suppression and prolonged survival in a prostate cancer model. These results demonstrated the feasibility of 5T4 as a TAA mRNA vaccine for the treatment of prostate cancer and highlighted the potential of CD70 as an mRNA vaccine, showing a promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy.

Current therapeutic strategies targeting the oncofetal antigen 5T4 encompass a range of modalities, including therapeutic vaccines, fusion proteins, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), and bispecific/trispecific antibodies. Among these, CV9202, an RNActive®-based immunotherapy incorporating 5T4 mRNA for non-small cell lung cancer, has demonstrated suboptimal clinical outcomes. This limited efficacy is primarily attributed to inefficient delivery of protamine, highlighting a critical challenge in mRNA-based therapies: the need for effective delivery systems to achieve robust immune activation and clinical success. To address this challenge, Tuthill et al. introduced a novel vaccine approach using two replication-deficient viral vectors, chimpanzee adenovirus (ChAdOx1) and modified vaccinia ankara (MVA), for 5T4 delivery. Although this system exhibited promising safety profiles and elicited 5T4-specific T cell responses, as evidenced by IFN-γ ELISpot assays on peripheral blood samples, it also had inherent drawbacks. These include the risks of genotoxicity, potential immune-related side effects, and high manufacturing costs, which significantly limit the scalability and broader clinical applicability of viral vector-based vaccines, despite their immunogenic potentialThese include the risks of genotoxicity, potential immune-related side effects, and high manufacturing costs, which significantly limit the scalability and broader clinical applicability of viral vector-based vaccines, despite their immunogenic potential. In response to these limitations, our study employed lipid nanoparticle (LNP) technology to deliver 5T4 mRNA for the first time. LNPs offer several advantages, including reduced genotoxicity associated with viral vectors, and a more streamlined and scalable production process that could facilitate clinical translation. Our in vitro and in vivo assessments revealed that 5T4-LNPs effectively activated dendritic cells (DCs), promoted antigen presentation, and induced robust cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) responses. Notably, treatment with 5T4-LNPs significantly slowed tumor progression and extended survival in preclinical models by 4–5 days. These findings underscore the potential of LNP-based mRNA delivery systems to target 5T4 and establish LNPs as versatile platforms for developing next-generation cancer immunotherapies with enhanced safety and efficacy profiles. Future efforts should focus on optimizing LNP formulations and exploring combination therapies, as these strategies could lead to more effective and durable cancer treatments, and ultimately improve patient outcomes in oncology.

Currently, researchers are focusing on immunotherapy strategies aimed at eliminating tumor cells by countering the immunosuppressive effects driven by chronic stimulation of the CD70-CD27 axis19. Notably, studies utilizing CD70 to enhance CD27 signaling, such as agonistic IgG1 anti-CD27 antibodies and monocyte-derived dendritic cell (DC) vaccines activated by electroporation using mRNA encoding CD40L, CD70, and active TLR4, have received limited attention. Therefore, we propose an innovative use of CD70 lipid nanoparticles (CD70-LNPs) as immunoadjuvants to enhance the efficacy of mRNA vaccines. A key finding was that CD70-LNPs significantly enhanced the antigen presentation capacity of dendritic cells. While CD70 is primarily known as a co-stimulatory molecule for T cell activation, our findings extend its role to include enhancing DC maturation and upregulating key co-stimulatory markers such as CD40, CD80, and CD86. This dual action suggests that CD70-LNPs not only boost T cell responses but also improve the antigen presentation environment within the immune system. This observation is critical because DC activation represents a bottleneck for vaccine efficacy, particularly in the context of low-immunogenic tumors. The enhanced expression of MHC-II and co-stimulatory molecules indicated that CD70 may have broader applications beyond T-cell activation, potentially serving as an adjuvant for mRNA vaccines to improve antigen presentation and overall immune engagement. Furthermore, the combination of 5T4 and CD70-LNPs demonstrates significant potential to drive CD8 + T cell infiltration and activation within the tumor microenvironment (TME). Tumor-associated antigens, such as 5T4, often encounter challenges owing to the immunosuppressive characteristics of the TME, which can inhibit effective T cell responses. However, our results indicate that combining CD70 with 5T4 not only enhances the cytotoxic potential of CD8 + T cells, but also promotes the activation of natural killer (NK) cells, as evidenced by the increased expression of CD69 and CD107a. This suggests that combination therapy may reshape the TME, creating a more immunogenic environment, which is crucial for overcoming the inherent resistance of tumors to immunotherapy. The observed increase in IFN-γ and TNF-α production further underscores the potential of this approach to elicit a robust Th1-biased immune response, which is essential for effective tumor clearance. Additionally, our findings are related to the role of CD70 in antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), particularly in NK cells. The observed increase in IgG levels and NK cell activation markers indicates that CD70 not only contributes to T cell-mediated cytotoxicity but also enhances the humoral immune response via ADCC. This dual action may be especially advantageous in tumors resistant to conventional cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) responses, which are sensitive to ADCC. Future studies could explore the combination of CD70-LNPs with monoclonal antibodies that leverage ADCC to further enhance the immune response against tumors. From a translational perspective, these findings provide a promising avenue for optimizing mRNA vaccines in the clinical setting. Although mRNA vaccines, such as those targeting SARS-CoV-2, have demonstrated the ability to induce rapid and robust immune responses, their efficacy against solid tumors remains inconsistent. Our data suggest that incorporating immune-stimulating molecules, such as CD70, which play a role in co-stimulation and immune modulation, could significantly enhance the therapeutic effects of mRNA vaccines in oncology. By boosting both innate and adaptive immune responses, this approach may yield durable immunity and improve tumor control, particularly when combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors or other immunomodulators. These findings represent a promising direction for future clinical trials. Further exploration of the molecular mechanisms underlying the enhanced immune response observed with CD70-LNPs is required. Our RNA sequencing data revealed the upregulation of genes associated with dendritic cell differentiation and T cell activation, indicating a complex network of signaling pathways that could be targeted for therapeutic benefits. For instance, increased expression of Tlr3, Irf8, and Runx3 suggests that CD70-LNPs may enhance TLR signaling pathways, which are crucial for initiating innate immune responses and subsequent adaptive immune activation, an area that warrants further investigation in our subsequent studies. Understanding the interactions of these pathways in different tumor contexts may provide valuable insights for designing more targeted mRNA vaccines.

This study has some limitations. First, there is a lack of assessment of the long-term immune memory. While the study demonstrated that the combination of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs effectively activated T cells and NK cells, enhancing tumor suppression, it lacked an in-depth evaluation of long-term immune memory. Although RNA sequencing data suggest an increase in the proportion of central memory T cells (TCM), it does not conclusively show that these memory cells can sustain an effective anti-tumor response over an extended period. Future studies should extend the observation period to assess the long-term protective effects of these vaccines. Second, although we demonstrated the immune-enhancing effects of CD70-LNPs, the precise molecular mechanisms underlying their role in promoting systemic immune remodeling remain to be elucidated. A deeper understanding of these mechanisms could offer valuable insights into how CD70-LNPs influence immune cells and reshape the tumor microenvironment. Finally, the study lacked validation in clinically relevant models. The experiments were primarily conducted in mouse models, and although their efficacy has been demonstrated, mouse models cannot fully replicate the human tumor microenvironment and immune responses. Therefore, clinical translatability of these findings remains unclear. Future research should consider testing preclinical tumor or primate models to evaluate their potential for clinical application in humans. Despite these limitations, we believe they do not undermine the integrity or validity of our conclusions.

Conclusions

In summary, the combination of 5T4 and CD70 mRNA-LNPs demonstrated robust immune activation and tumor suppression in mouse models, enhancing both humoral and cellular immunity, and offering promising insights for prostate cancer treatment. While the results are encouraging, further research is required to evaluate long-term immune memory, potential immune mechanisms, and clinical applicability in human models.
