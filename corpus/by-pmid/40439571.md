# Mathematical Modeling and Association Analysis Decipher the Impact of the Gut Microbiome on Cancer Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

**UNLABELLED**: The gut microbiome has emerged as a key regulator of response to cancer immunotherapy. However, a better understanding of the underlying mechanisms by which the microbiome influences immunotherapy is needed to identify strategies to optimize outcomes. To this end, we developed a mathematical model to obtain insights into the effect of the microbiome on the immune system and immunotherapy response. This model was based on (i) gut microbiome data derived from preclinical studies, (ii) mathematical modeling of the antitumor immune response, (iii) association analysis of microbiome profiles with model-predicted immune profiles, and (iv) statistical models that correlate model parameters with the microbiome. The model was used to investigate the complexity of murine and human studies on microbiome modulation. Comparison of model predictions with experimental observations of tumor response in the training and test datasets supported the hypothesis that two model parameters, the activation and killing rate constants of immune cells, are the most influential in tumor progression and are potentially affected by microbiome composition. Evaluation of the associations between the gut microbiome and immune profile indicated that the components and structure of the gut microbiome affect the activation and killing rate of adaptive and innate immune cells. Overall, this study contributes to a deeper understanding of microbiome-cancer interactions and offers a framework for understanding how microbiome interactions influence cancer treatment outcomes.

**SIGNIFICANCE**: Integration of mathematical modeling and microbiome data reveals how gut microbiome components impact immune response, providing insights to optimize immunotherapy strategies. Introduction

In recent years, immune checkpoint blockers (ICB) have revolutionized cancer treatment by harnessing the body’s immune system to combat malignancies. These therapies, which target regulatory pathways such as PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA4, have received approval for use across a broad range of cancer types and clinical scenarios. Despite their promise, the clinical response remains limited to a subset of patients, with objective response rates generally ranging from 20% to 40%. Furthermore, treatment with ICBs is often associated with immune-mediated toxicities, some of which may be severe or life-threatening. A major barrier to the broader efficacy of therapeutic agents, including ICBs, is the hostile tumor microenvironment, characterized by poor drug penetration and immunosuppressive cellular populations. Recently, strategies aimed at modulating the host microbiome have emerged as a novel means to enhance antitumor immunity and improve therapeutic outcomes.

The impact of the gut microbiota on immunotherapy outcome has been studied across several types of cancers, and an increasing number of clinical and preclinical studies have shown that the diversity, composition, and structure of the gut microbiome are associated with response and resistance to ICBs. Furthermore, recent experimental and clinical trials have demonstrated the benefit of fecal microbiota transplants (FMT) to overcome resistance to ICBs. Despite the strong evidence for the impact of the microbiota on tumor immunity and ICB efficacy, elucidating the underlying mechanisms remains an active area of investigation. The gut microbiota plays a major role in changing the intestinal immune system, which can be associated with the trafficking of immune cells (e.g., B cells, mononuclear phagocytes, and Th 17–like T cells) to peripheral organs. This immune trafficking may lead to protumor or antitumor alterations in the tumor-immune microenvironment. For instance, the abundance of Enterocloster species in the ileum can lead to the homing of retinoic acid receptor-related orphan receptor-γt+ (RORγt+) regulatory T cells to the tumor microenvironment, thus impairing the response to ICBs. The gut microbiota can also secrete soluble immunomodulatory factors such as metabolites. In this context, secondary bile acids, products of bacterial metabolization of primary bile acids, were shown to decrease the extravasation of CXCR6 + NK T cells across the liver endothelium, leading to an immunosuppressive environment in liver metastases. Additionally, microbiota-derived short-chain fatty acids can increase the antitumor activity of CD8+ T cells and modulate macrophage polarization. The inherent complexity of the dynamic host–microbiota interactions, along with the multifaceted interplay between tumors and the immune system, presents significant challenges in comprehensively elucidating the mechanisms by which the gut microbiota influences tumor immunity and response to immunotherapy.

Mathematical models have enhanced our understanding of not only tumor biology but also that of other diseases. For instance, mathematical models have been developed for the investigation of interactions among populations of microbes, determining which populations can prevail over others. There are also models focusing on interactions among a small number of bacteria and specific populations of immune cells or the interactions of the immune system with tumors. Other models have investigated how interactions between cancer cells and immune cells influence the efficacy of immunotherapy for cancer, as well as for patients with cancer who have contracted COVID-19. All these are mechanistic models, which explicitly incorporate the underlying biological and physical processes/mechanisms governing tumor or bacterial growth, progression, treatment, and immune response.

Machine learning (ML) models have also been developed in this field and provide complementary insights. Such models are data-driven, and with sufficient data, they can capture highly complex relationships without requiring the incorporation of the underlying mechanisms of the tumor, microbiome, and host immune system. For example, Hu and colleagues developed an ML-based approach to predict the impact of the gut microbiome on immune checkpoint blockade–associated adverse events. Similarly, Matson and colleagues used statistical models to investigate the gut microbiome’s impact on antitumor immunity in patients with metastatic melanoma. ML models are also able to predict response to treatment from CT images and ultrasound shear-wave elastography images. Although ML models excel in identifying correlations and predicting outcomes from high-dimensional datasets, they often lack the interpretability and mechanistic insights that mechanistic models provide. To our knowledge, none of the existing mechanistic modeling frameworks have analyzed the effect of the microbiome on cancer immunotherapy. Elucidating the role of the microbiome in cancer immunotherapy requires an in-depth investigation of (i) the mechanisms by which the microbiome affects the innate and adaptive immune systems and (ii) the collective and individual role of microbes on each different immune cell function. A fundamental understanding of these processes has the potential to inform new therapeutic strategies. This task is challenging, though, as it requires a detailed mathematical framework based on robust experimental data.

In this study, we developed a mathematical (mechanistic) model of immune checkpoint blockade therapy to investigate the possible mechanisms by which the microbiome influences the immune system. We used the model in combination with data from several independent murine and human studies to distill the complexity of microbiome modulation on ICB efficacy. By simulating these studies and comparing model predictions with the experimental and clinical data, we tested the hypothesis that two model parameters, namely the activation rate constant (Arc) and killing rate constant (Krc) of immune cells, are most influenced by the microbiome composition. We further used an association and regression analysis, including additional data to derive relations between specific bacterial families and immunotherapy efficacy, and found how the relative abundance of microbiome families affects the activation and killing rate of both adaptive and innate immune systems.

Materials and Methods

Description of modeling strategy

The model accounts explicitly for the interactions among cancer cells, adaptive and innate immune cells, cytokines, receptors, ligands, and antibodies (see Supplementary Table S1 for a list of the model variables and Supplementary Fig. S1 for the interactions among them; refs.). A comparison of the components incorporated in our modeling framework with previously published pertinent models is shown in Supplementary Table S2. To our knowledge, the current model uniquely includes tumor growth with comprehensive interactions of the immune system, the microbiome composition, and the effect of the microbiome on the tumor and immune system. The current limitations in our understanding of the specific roles played by different bacteria in cancer immunotherapy as well as the complexity of the underlying mechanisms that govern these interactions prevented us from developing a direct mathematical model for interactions between the microbiome and the immune system. Instead, based on the pertinent literature and our own experience on the effect of the microbiome on immunotherapy, we hypothesized that the gut microbiome indirectly affects immune cells in the tumor through two distinct processes: their activation effectiveness (AE) and killing effectiveness (KE). These processes are incorporated through an Arc and a Krc (Fig. 1); these parameters were changed only in treatment groups or mice with different microbiome compositions. The Krc represents the effectiveness of the tumor cells’ killing (cytolysis and phagocytosis) by the immune cells. As cells of the adaptive immune system, like CD8 effector T cells, have stronger killing potential than those of the innate system, their Krc value is assigned as  times higher, in which  is a proportionality constant. The Arc represents how easily a naïve cell can be activated against tumor antigens and become an effector cell, as shown in Fig. 1.

The values of most model parameters were taken from studies related to the immune system (Supplementary Table S3). For unknown parameters related to the growth of the tumor and the effects of immunotherapy, the baseline values (Supplementary Table S4) were determined by an optimization procedure to reproduce the average experimental tumor growth curves (Fig. 2A and B; Supplementary Fig. S2) from relevant mouse studies. Furthermore, a global sensitivity analysis was performed to assess the effect of model parameters (Fig. 2C). We then used the baseline values of these parameters and the model to simulate other data from an FMT clinical trial in patients with melanoma who progressed on immunotherapy prior to FMT and murine experiments developed to evaluate the impact of responder and nonresponder FMTs (Fig. 3A–C) on antitumor response. These simulations yield mechanistic insights about immune cell dynamics (Fig. 4), individual subjects’ microbiomes (Fig. 5A), and their Krc and Arc parameters (Fig. 3C), which can be directly compared. Additionally, we performed an association analysis between the immune profiles predicted by our model and the microbiome profile data (Fig. 5A–C) to discover potential positive and negative dependencies among specific microbial populations and components of the immune system. Finally, to test the hypothesis that the microbiome composition affects the immune system through the Arc and Krc constants, we performed a regression analysis to extract fitting regression models of Arc and Krc as a function of microbiome relative abundance from a training dataset (Fig. 6A and B) and then tested the hypothesis and the models (both regression models and our mathematical model) with preclinical and clinical test datasets (Fig. 6C and D).

The tumor volume was calculated based on the total change in the mass () and density of the tumor tissue, which was assumed to be constant through time and equal to the initial density of the cancer cells (). This assumption is justified by the observation that the density of the tumor tissue remains relatively stable over time; even during treatment, as tumor cells proliferate or die, no significant change in density was observed. In this framework, the density of tumor tissue is not influenced by variations in the relative proportions of tumor components. Even though the density of live tumor cells, dead cells, and immune cells varies through time, these changes redistribute mass within the tumor rather than alter its overall density, and when total mass increases or decreases, it finally affects the tumor volume. More specifically, the model includes the following components: tumor cells, immune cells (CD4+, CD8+ T cells, regulatory T cells, B cells, plasma cells, immature dendritic cells, antigen-presenting cells (APC), neutrophils, NK cells, and macrophages), cytokines (immunostimulatory and immunosuppressive), receptors and ligands (PD-1 and PD-L1), and antibodies (tumor antibodies, anti–PD-L1, and anti–PD-1) and their complexes (Fig. 1). The model considers the effects of immunostimulatory and immunosuppressive cytokines on immune cell functions, the activation of naïve immune cells, and the phagocytosis and cytolysis (killing) of cancer cells by immune cells. The model also includes cancer cells in three different states: live cancer cells, dead cancer cells that release free tumor antigens, and cancer cells targeted with antibodies. In addition, we account for the binding of PD-L1 to PD-1 on the immune cell plasma membrane, which reduces the killing and activation potential of immune cells. Injection of anti–PD-L1 or anti–PD-1 immunotherapy blocks PD-L1 or PD-1, respectively, on the cells, enhancing the killing rates of immune cells and the activation rates of the adaptive immune system.

The values of most model parameters related to the immune system were taken from the literature (Supplementary Table S2), and the remaining parameters related to the tumor cell lines and the effects of immunotherapy (Supplementary Table S4) were determined by an optimization procedure to reproduce the experimental tumor growth curves from published studies. Specifically, the net proliferation rate constant of tumor cells,  (proliferation minus natural death), and the amount of PD-L1 on the extracellular membrane of the tumor cell, , were defined by the optimization procedure for each murine melanoma cell line, B16 (RRID: CVCL_F936; ref.), BP, and HCmel1274 (RRID: CVCL_B0CF; ref.). In the optimization process, all the aforementioned model parameters related to immunotherapy were kept the same for all studies and treatment groups, and only the parameters related to the tumor cell line ( and ) changed for different cell lines among studies, whereas the parameters Arc and Krc varied to account for groups with different microbiome compositions.

The optimization process involved minimizing the sum of the squared differences between the logarithmic values of the measured experimental () and simulated () tumor volumes . The optimization algorithm accepts only positive values of model parameters that have a physical meaning by introducing a penalty into the optimization function for negative parameters. The Nelder–Mead simplex algorithm was used to find the minimum of the optimization function. After defining the baseline model parameters for the tumor cell lines and immunotherapy type (anti–PD-L1 and anti–PD-1) with the aforementioned process, the model was used with these baseline parameter values for the validation of tumor growth curves for each individual subject to simulate the effects of different microbiomes based on other experimental datasets by only varying the parameters Krc and Arc. This process assessed the predictive capabilities of the model by varying only two parameters that affect crucial mechanisms of the immune system, as well as the capability of the model to capture the different responses due to the variation of microbiome composition through these parameters.

Statistical analysis

We used statistical methods to study the connection between the microbiome and the immune system in experiments 3, 4, and 5. These experiments involved changing the microbiome only through FMTs. Description of murine and human studies used for validation of the model

Murine studies

The experimental design for these studies has been previously described in full detail. The studies were conducted at the MD Anderson Cancer Center under the approval of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. B6 germ-free mice were colonized by a complete responder microbiota through FMTs; the experiments 1 to 5 received FMTs from different donors. One week was allowed for engraftment, following which, mice received a subcutaneous injection of BP melanoma cells. Once tumors reached a size range of 250 to 500 mm3, mice were treated with three doses of intraperitoneal anti–PD-L1, and tumor size was measured using a caliper. More information about these experiments can be found in the original article.

Human studies

For further validation of our model, we incorporated data from a phase I clinical trial (NCT03353402) conducted by Baruch and colleagues that evaluated the safety of FMT in patients with PD-1–refractory melanoma. Ten patients were enrolled in this study and received a broad-spectrum antibiotic treatment to deplete the native microbiota, followed by FMT with stool samples from complete responders (2 donors). FMT was conducted via colonoscopy and continued via oral capsules on days 1 and 12 after colonoscopy. Subsequently, patients received anti–PD-1 treatment with additional FMT (oral capsules) administered every 14 days.

For the validation of the fitting regression model, the human microbiome data from Matson and colleagues were also used. The human microbiome data were from the analysis of stool samples of patients with melanoma who received immunotherapy, and the comparison was based on their response to treatment. Response to treatment was measured using the RECIST v1.1 criterion.

All clinical studies were approved by the Institutional Review Board and conducted under the Declaration of Helsinki guidelines. Written informed consent was obtained from all patients prior to enrollment in these studies.

Results

The gut microbiome affects the activation and killing potential of immune cells

We set out to define potential mechanisms by which the gut microbiome affects antitumor immune responses. To do so, we first established the values of the model parameters that are related to the tumor cells and immunotherapy (Supplementary Table S4) by optimizing the model based on experimental data from B16, BP, and HCmel1274 murine melanoma tumors. Parameters related to tumor cells varied by cancer cell lines (e.g., B16 and BP melanoma cell lines in Fig. 2A and B, respectively), whereas treatment- and immune-related parameters (Supplementary Tables S3 and S4) remained the same across datasets, including in the analysis of microbiome effects on immunotherapy.

In these studies, the gut microbiome was modulated in specific pathogen-free or germ-free mice via FMT from patients with melanoma, who were responders or nonresponders to ICBs. Subsequently, the mice received a subcutaneous injection of melanoma cells and were treated with anti–PD-L1 antibodies. The experimental data and model predictions using the baseline values after the optimization of tumor cells and immunotherapy parameters are shown in Fig. 2A and B and Supplementary Fig. S2. Among microbiome-related experimental groups, only the parameters Arc and Krc were varied to fit the experimental data. For instance, in Fig. 2A, the responder and responder + anti–PD-L1 groups (blue and dashed blue lines) share the same Krc and Arc, whereas nonresponders have different values for these parameters, reflecting hypothesized microbiome effects on the immune response.

A global sensitivity analysis of optimized parameters (Supplementary Table S4) was performed, as detailed in Materials and Methods. The full factorial design with these parameters across all treatment groups in Fig. 2A and B and Supplementary Fig. S2 generated approximately 4 million simulations. Tumor volume 1 day before and 1 day after immunotherapy was used as the response variable ( in Eq. C), and N-way ANOVA with constrained (type III) sums of squares identified significant predictors (P < 0.05) in Eq. C. Figure 2C displays the coefficients of significant predictors on the tumor volume after treatment, with negative values (left) indicating that an increase in this parameter will lead to a decrease in tumor volume (and vice versa) and positive values (right) indicating that an increase in these parameters will lead to an increase in tumor volume.

The results highlight that Krc has the most favorable (strongest negative) effect, followed by Arc, indicating these parameters’ significant role in immune response modulation with possible microbiome connection. Notably, the interaction between Krc and Arc has an even greater effect than Krc alone, suggesting a synergistic relationship: high Krc and low Arc lead to sufficient innate immune response, and low Krc and high Arc do not lead to any immune response, whereas high values of both parameters trigger a strong innate and adaptive immune response. Not surprisingly, the cancer cell net proliferation rate constant () has the most unfavorable effect, with the highest positive coefficient. Other parameters that have a significant effect are related to PD-1 and PD-L1, such as the unbinding rate constant () and the half-saturation concentration of the PD-1–PD-L1 complex (parameter ) that affects the killing and activation potential of immune cells. These have favorable effects because the former reduces the PD-1–PD-L1 complex and the latter reduces the effect of the complex. Finally, the level of PD-1 on immune cells ( and ) and the level of PD-L1 on tumor cells () have unfavorable effects because both reduce the overall killing and activation potential of immune cells. The results for the tumor volume before immunotherapy initiation followed a similar trend (Supplementary Fig. S4). A local sensitivity analysis was also conducted, and the results are presented in Supplementary Fig. S5.

Next, we used data from a small clinical study of patients with melanoma who progressed on ICBs and were subsequently treated with FMTs followed by ICBs in an attempt to overcome initial ICB resistance (Baruch and colleagues, ref.). In this dataset, the tumor growth after treatment was available and used for validation to demonstrate that the current model can be generalized and used in clinical applications when such data are available. In this study, we assigned the same baseline parameter values obtained for immunotherapy treatment from the murine dataset because of the lack of control group patients to extract proper parameters for humans. The results demonstrate that the model can be used to simulate tumor growth (Pearson rho, r = 0.7 with a P value = 0.0001, Supplementary Fig. S6A) and immune response (CD8+ T cells, Supplementary Fig. S6B). The validation of such a small clinical dataset has important limitations and does not allow firm conclusions about the effects of the microbiome in these human tumors. However, this analysis demonstrates the utility of the methodology in clinical settings when sufficient data are available.

We then used the model to probe new sets of pertinent experimental data with the same baseline values of the model parameters (Supplementary Tables S3 and S4) as they were estimated from previous independent studies in Fig. 2A and B and Supplementary Fig. S2. To reproduce these data, we only varied the parameters Arc and Krc, which we hypothesized are affected by gut microbiome composition (Fig. 3A). Specifically, we used data from 62 mice in which (i) 35 received FMT from a responder, a nonresponder patient with melanoma, or a healthy person (FMT, this study) and (ii) 27 received FMT from a responder patient (experiments 2–5 from Spencer and colleagues, ref.). After FMT, BP melanoma cells were implanted, and mice were treated with anti–PD-L1.

This validation procedure resulted in the calculation of the Krc and Arc for each mouse. The parameters Krc and Arc vary for each individual mouse due to the heterogeneity of the gut microbiome composition of each mouse, which induces the heterogeneity of the immune response. For example, although some mice received FMT from the same donor, the resulting microbiome compositions and immune responses might differ. Figure 3B presents the comparison of the experimental and the predicted tumor volume by the model (simulated volume) at all available time points (n = 369 points). The diagonal dashed line depicts the best-fitting case scenario. Points close to the diagonal line have the best agreement between the simulated and experimental values. By only varying Krc and Arc and keeping the rest of the parameters at their baseline values, the model was able to reproduce the measured experimental tumor volume for all mice and time points with good accuracy (coefficient of determination, R2 = 0.72; Pearson rho, r = 0.89 with a P value < 0.0001). This supports our hypothesis that the primary effects of the microbiome are through the modulation of the killing of tumor cells by immune cells and the activation of the adaptive immune system. Figure 3C shows the range of values of Krc and Arc that correspond to responders and nonresponders (blue and red points, respectively). Responders were defined as having complete response or partial response according to RECIST v1.1, and nonresponders were defined as having progressive disease or stable disease according to RECIST v1.1.

The modeling framework suggests antitumor immune responses

The advantage of mathematical modeling is the ability to estimate mechanistic parameters that are difficult to determine experimentally. From the analysis in Fig. 3B, our model predicted the immune profile of murine tumors for which the microbiome profile was available. Predictions of model variables for the antitumor immune response are shown in Fig. 4. The figure presents the time evolution of model variables, and the plots are divided into two categories representing the tumor response—responders and nonresponders (complete response or partial response for responders and progressive disease or stable disease for nonresponders according to RECIST v1.1; ref.). The nonresponder concentrations (red curves) of proinflammatory cytokines, NK cells, and APCs have lower values than the corresponding concentrations for the responders (blue curves), and they reach a plateau. The activation of the adaptive immune system is also insignificant in the nonresponders, and thus, the nonresponder concentrations of the effector CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, memory cells, and plasma B cells reach a plateau near zero, which results in a low amount of tumor antigen and tumor cells with antibodies. On the other hand, the responders’ concentrations of the proinflammatory cytokines, NK cells, and APCs are continuously increasing. There is also a significant activation of the adaptive immune system in responders, which results in high concentrations of effector CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, memory cells, and plasma B cells. Overall, the simulations predict that the responder mice have strong innate and adaptive immune responses, which results in tumor eradication. In responders, the high immune response and tumor eradication (very small tumor volume) finally result in an instant increase in all concentrations and density (blue curves in Fig. 4). Although the model results appear qualitative due to the lack of data in the literature for extensive validation, this process enabled us to make quantitative predictions for immune cells and parameters, which we used in our analysis for the effect of the microbiome on the immune system.

Association and regression analyses suggest how specific gut bacteria and their combinations correlate with immune cell responses

Next, we examined the relationships between specific microbes and components of the immune system by using a training dataset and a test dataset. The training dataset consists of tumor volume data (n = 134) and microbiome data (n = 32) from experiments 3, 4, and 5, which were previously validated in Fig. 3. The test dataset consists of tumor volume (n = 252) data and microbiome data (n = 36) from preclinical experiments 3, 4, and 5 that were not used in the validation and training process and microbiome data (n = 42) from a clinical study. To this end, we performed an association analysis between the model predictions of the immune system with the microbiome profiles (Fig. 5A and B) from the murine training dataset. To identify associations between components of the microbiome and the immune system, we evaluated the association of microbial families reported in the experimental studies with the immune profiles generated by our model. For the association analysis (Fig. 5C), the microbiome profiling data (Fig. 5A) were not separated into pre- and post-immunotherapy treatment because the underlying microbiome profiles are not significantly different, as shown in Fig. 5B for the P value of pre-/post-immunotherapy treatment (not pre-/post-FMT treatment, which is mentioned in the initial article of Spencer and colleagues, ref.). The three different experiments involve studies with three different FMT responder donors. The different FMT donors induce different microbiomes, as evidenced by the microbiome profiles in Fig. 5A and B (P value of the experiment 3, 4, and 5 is 0). For the association analysis in Fig. 5C, we used all the data from the three experiments and suitably adjusted the microbiome compositional data by taking the center log-ratio transformation [; ref. ] as described in Materials and Methods. In this way, we have the largest possible dataset that allowed us to extract the strongest signal from all experiments.

Our model identified bacterial families that have been the subject of previous studies, such as Lachnospiraceae, Prevotellaceae, and Ruminococcaceae. The analysis suggests that there are direct associations between immune cell killing effectiveness and activation effectiveness (KE and AE, which are related to Krc and Arc, respectively) and the bacterial families. These associations are consistent with our initial hypothesis that the microbiome affects the immune system primarily by enhancing immune cell AE and KE. The results also show that Bacteroidaceae, Lachnospiraceae, and Prevotellaceae correlate positively with the KE of the effector CD8+ T cells and with the AE of APCs and effector CD4+ T cells and negatively with the number of tumor cells. On the other hand, “other” families (families that had a very small relative abundance) are correlated negatively with the AE of APCs and effector CD4+ T cells and positively with tumor cells. In this study, the Ruminococcaceae family is correlated negatively with the AE of effector CD8+ T cells and the AE of APCs and effector CD4+ T cells, which results in a positive correlation with tumor cells. The Prevotellaceae family is also correlated positively with the KE of the innate immune cells.

Additionally, to test the initial hypothesis that the microbiome affects the parameters Krc and Arc, a regression analysis was performed incorporating these parameters (Fig. 3C) and the microbiome relative abundance of identified families (Fig. 5A) of the training dataset (experiments 3, 4, and 5; ref.). Krc and Arc were given by regression functions [] that depend on the microbiome relative abundances at the family level and the products of each pair after applying a center log-ratio transformation [; ref. ]. The fitting regression models of Krc and the natural logarithm of Arc [ln(Arc) was used because of its exponential variation] are presented in Fig. 6A and B, and the values of the model coefficient of each family  and product of families  for  and , respectively, as well as the intercept (), are shown in Table 1. The fitting regression model of Krc has a coefficient of regression R2 = 0.75, and the data are normally distributed around the fitted curve, which supports a strong correlation of the parameter Krc with the microbiome. The natural logarithm of Arc also correlates with microbiome relative abundance with R2 = 0.92, which also indicates a strong correlation and suggests that this parameter changes exponentially with the composition of the gut microbiome.

Subsequently, these fitting regression models were tested in additional preclinical and clinical datasets for which the microbiome profiling was known [experiments 3, 4, and 5, murine test dataset, tumor volume data (n = 252; ref.), and human microbiome test dataset (n = 42; ref.)]. We estimated the parameters Krc and Arc from the fitting regression models (Fig. 6A and B) and the microbiome relative abundance of the test datasets. For the murine test dataset, we were able to predict the experimental tumor volume with sufficient accuracy: the results in Fig. 6C indicate that the fitting regression models of Krc and Arc are able to capture the effect of the microbiome on the immune system. Furthermore, by using the human microbiome test dataset, we calculated the Krc and Arc parameters with the aforementioned fitting regression models, and the results are presented in Fig. 6D. Given the lack of tumor growth data for the clinical test dataset, the analysis was focused on responder vs. nonresponder patients. Figure 6D indicates that the effect of the microbiome on Krc and Arc is significantly different in the responders compared with the nonresponders, with the responders’ Krc and Arc being higher. Although the fitting regression models of Krc were extracted from preclinical experiments (with human FMT) that do not have the same variation in gut microbiome as the human gut microbiome, the statistical models were able to be generalized and captured the signal of the human gut microbiome.

Interestingly, our analysis unveils the complex interplay between bacterial families and their collective impact on immune responses, as evidenced by the fit of the regression models of Krc and Arc. For example, in the regression model of Krc, the Bacteroidaceae and Tannerellaceae families by themselves seem to have a significant (P < 0.05) negative effect (see the coefficients  of Bacteroidaceae alone and Tannerellaceae alone in Table 1). However, the synergistic effect of Bacteroidaceae and Tannerellaceae () when they coexist is positive and statistically significant (P < 0.05, see Table 1). The Desulfovibrionaceae family has a negative effect on Krc by itself but a positive effect when coexisting with “other” families. Desulfovibrionaceae and Ruminococcaceae families by themselves, and when coexisting, seem to have significant negative effects on Krc. For the fitting regression model of Arc, Prevotellaceae and Tannerellaceae families by themselves have the most positive effect, and Acidaminococcaceae and Sutterellaceae families have a positive synergistic effect. Sutterellaceae and Akkermansiaceae families by themselves have the most positive effect on Krc. Overall, the fitting regression models show that the effect of the microbiome is a collective contribution, and the same families in different environments can have completely different effects. For this reason, in future studies, the synergistic effect of two families at different population ratios needs to be tested.

As summarized in Table 2, in this study, the Prevotellaceae family has a clear positive effect, and the Ruminococcaceae family has a clear negative effect in both association and regression analyses. Regarding the regression analysis, Sutterellaceae, Akkermansiaceae, and Enterobacteriaceae have a clear positive effect on Krc or Arc, and Clostridia unknown (from unknown families) has a clear negative effect. The rest of the families were found to have dual roles or have been affected by other families, suggesting synergistic effects.

Discussion

In this study, we developed a systematic approach to elucidate the role of the microbiome in controlling immune responses and determining the efficacy of immunotherapy. Our approach is based on (i) microbiome profiles obtained from preclinical and clinical studies, (ii) a mathematical model specifically developed for the simulation of the immune profile during antitumor response, (iii) a train-test split framework validation of immune parameters that seems to be affected by the microbiome composition, and (iv) statistical analysis to correlate the experimental data with the model predictions and model parameters. Through the comparison of model predictions with experimental observation of tumor response in the training and test datasets, we validated and tested our hypothesis that the immune activation rate, Arc, and the killing rate, Krc, are key determinants of microbiome-mediated antitumor immunity. Using our mathematical model, we were able to distill the complexity of each experiment and microbiome modulation to two model parameters: Krc and Arc (Fig. 3). Furthermore, by extracting the fitting regression models of Krc and Arc parameters as a function of microbiome relative abundance data, we were able to test our hypotheses on preclinical and clinical datasets. The results indicate the significant impact of the microbiome on Krc, the Krc of both adaptive and innate immune cells, and a strong association with the natural logarithm of Arc, suggesting that the microbiome has an exponential impact on the activation of adaptive immune cells. Additionally, by evaluating the association of different bacterial families with the immune profiles predicted by our model and by extracting the fitting regression models for Krc and Arc, we identified patterns of association that provide additional information about the involvement of the microbiome in response and resistance to ICBs. Interestingly, the microbiome association and regression analysis indicate direct connections between the microbiome and the KE and AE of the immune cells. Our results can also help guide experimental investigations of these two crucial mechanisms and how they are affected by specific microbiome families and combinations of microbiome families. Of note, our model and methodology can be used as a tool to test and refine new mechanisms as soon as new experiments become available.

Regarding the association analysis of the microbiome families and the immune components, the Lachnospiraceae family is correlated positively with increased immune responses, which is in agreement with the literature. The Ruminococcaceae family was found to be associated with immunosuppression according to the association with less activated immune cells against tumor cells, like effector CD8+ T cells and APCs. This is consistent with previous reports demonstrating that this family has anti-inflammation effects on the colon and is related to fewer adverse effects during immunotherapy. The Ruminococcaceae family was also found to have a negative effect on Krc fitting regression model. In patients with melanoma, the Ruminococcaceae family was mostly found in responders to immunotherapy, which might correlate with fewer adverse effects. The models suggest that Desulfovibrionaceae and Ruminococcaceae, either individually or when they coexist, can induce a decrease in Krc. Also, the model predicts that Bacteroidaceae and Tannerellaceae families, by themselves, induce a decrease in Krc, but when they coexist, induce an increase in Krc. The synergistic effect of the different microbiome families is also highlighted with Acidaminococcaceae and Sutterellaceae families, which induce an exponential positive effect on Arc. Finally, it must be noted that the effect of the microbiome on the killing effectiveness of both innate and adaptive immune cells (Krc) and the activation effectiveness of adaptive immune cells (Arc) is proven in both training and test preclinical and clinical datasets.

Overall, this study highlights distinct roles for specific gut microbiota families, such as the Prevotellaceae family, which exhibits a clear positive effect, and the Ruminococcaceae family, which exhibits a clear negative effect. Sutterellaceae, Akkermansiaceae, and Enterobacteriaceae show positive effects on Krc or Arc, whereas Clostridia unknown (from an unidentified family) has a clear negative effect. The remaining families exhibit dual roles or are influenced by interactions with other families, leading to synergistic effects. These findings align with broader observations regarding the influence of these microbial families on ICB efficacy. Prevotellaceae, associated with plant-rich diets and fiber fermentation, play an ambiguous role in ICB response, whereas Ruminococcaceae produce anti-inflammatory short-chain fatty acids like butyrate, which are linked to fewer adverse events and, in some cases, improved ICB outcomes. In the Ruminococcaceae family, there are also strains that have a negative effect on ICB response. Sutterellaceae, particularly the genus Sutterella, are associated with mild proinflammatory effects and inflammatory bowel conditions that could influence immune responses. Akkermansiaceae, especially Akkermansia muciniphila, enhance gut barrier integrity and modulate immune responses, boosting ICB therapy efficacy. Enterobacteriaceae include both commensal and pathogenic strains, with complex and context-dependent roles in ICB response influenced by specific strains present in the gut microbiota. It should be noted that the association and regression analysis between microbiome and immune components was conducted at the family level, which inherently lacks the resolution to determine how individual species may directly affect immune responses. To address this limitation, future experiments should include in-depth microbiome profiling through metagenomic sequencing and directly assess the effects of specific bacterial species or communities on innate and adaptive immune responses. This could be achieved, for instance, by selectively ablating the adaptive immune response to provide clearer insights.

Although the analyses based on our model do not imply causal relationships between bacteria and the tumor-immune profile, the identified associations with immune components could help determine the focus and design of future mechanistic studies. In recent years, ML has emerged as a powerful tool for modeling complex systems such as the gut microbiome. An ML model could also perform as well as our mathematical model; however, it is important to note that any input–output connection in an ML model not only does not imply a causal relationship but also does not provide insights into the underlying mechanisms. Moreover, such a model cannot be generalized better than the current mathematical model, which explicitly incorporates biological principles. In addition, as with any mathematical model, our modeling framework is subject to certain assumptions and limitations. The most obvious limitations are that we focused on the gut microbiome and did not consider the local microbiome of the tumor. Also, our model was validated and trained in melanoma cancer, and due to these assumptions, the current model cannot be used for different types of cancer, such as colon cancer, in which the tumor and the gut microbiome are in direct contact. Furthermore, model predictions for the immune profile could be considered qualitative due to the lack of data in the literature for model validation. However, the model includes the most well-known mechanisms of immune cell interactions, which enable us to predict immune cell dynamics and use these predictions to provide insights into the behavior of immune cells without performing time-consuming and expensive experiments. In the future, well-designed studies with different relative abundances of the crucial microbiome families need to be performed to collect immune profile data and elucidate the underlying mechanisms. In addition, we assumed that the contributions of the microbiome to the activation and killing rates are the same for all cells of the innate and adaptive immune systems. We acknowledge that the Krc and Arc parameters can vary between different immune cells, but we keep them the same for all immune cells because of the lack of immune profile data to validate different Krc and Arc values for each immune cell. These parameters can be affected by factors other than the microbiome, such as genetic factors, environmental factors, and underlying medical conditions. Furthermore, we did not consider in the deterministic model the role of the PD-L2 ligand. Due to the complexity of the immune system, it would be intractable for all individual components to be incorporated into a model with parameters that can be estimated independently. These limitations notwithstanding, our systems approach combining state-of-the-art murine and human studies with mathematical modeling and statistical analysis paves the way for the elucidation of the role of microbiome modulation in improving cancer immunotherapy. Specifically, recent research efforts have focused on eliminating harmful bacteria and introducing beneficial microbial species or communities to enhance cancer response to immunotherapy. Given the complexity of studying microbial communities in animal models and clinical settings, mathematical models offer a valuable framework for identifying specific bacterial families, species, or communities that can be targeted for mechanistic analyses and clinical applications. Although this study specifically investigates the microbiome’s effect on melanoma treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors, the methodology is generalizable and can be applied to other cancers or noncancerous diseases influenced by the microbiome, such as colitis, provided appropriate training datasets are available.

In conclusion, the current study offers a framework for understanding how microbiome interactions influence cancer treatment outcomes, providing insights into broader biological mechanisms relevant to various tumor types. As additional experimental data emerge, particularly for other cancers, the model can be refined, revalidated, and extended to explore microbiome effects across different treatments and diseases. This approach has the potential to contribute to a deeper understanding of microbiome–cancer interactions beyond melanoma and inform future experimental and clinical applications.
