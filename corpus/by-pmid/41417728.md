# CRISPR screens define unified hallmarks of cancer cell-autonomous immune evasion.

## Abstract

Cancer immunotherapy has transformed cancer treatment, yet only a minority of patients achieve durable benefit. Although early efforts to enhance immunotherapy focused on boosting immune effector function, reversing T cell exhaustion, or altering the tumor microenvironment, it is now clear that cancer cell-autonomous mechanisms play a major role in immune escape. Such programs, driven by the cancer cell genome, transcriptome, and epigenome, include desensitization to cytokine signaling, such as interferon (IFN)γ and tumor necrosis factor (TNF); impaired antigen presentation; upregulation of suppressive ligands such as programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1); and epigenetic silencing of immunogenic pathways. The rise of high-throughput functional genomics, especially in vitro and in vivo CRISPR-based screening, has greatly expanded our ability to map these pathways and define how tumors evade CD8+ T cell-mediated pressure. A deeper understanding of these cancer cell-autonomous immune-evasion mechanisms will be essential for developing new therapeutic strategies that broaden the impact of immunotherapy across diverse cancers.

INTRODUCTION
The immune system serves as a crucial barrier to cancer 
development. 1 Cytotoxic CD8 + T cells play a central role in de-
tecting and eradicating transformed cells that express mutated 
or tumor-specific antigens. These antigens, presented via major 
histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I molecules, can be 
recognized by T cell receptors (TCRs), leading to targeted cell 
killing. Harnessing this inherent immune surveillance mechanism 
has become the foundation of cancer immunotherapy, a trans-
formative therapeutic modality that now complements conven-
tional treatments such as surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy. 
Immunotherapies have demonstrated durable responses in 
multiple cancer types and are increasingly adopted as first-line 
treatments in advanced disease settings. 2
Modern immunotherapy encompasses many divergent 
approaches but can be broadly grouped into two principal stra-
tegies: reactivating or invigorating endogenous anti-cancer im-
munity, through immune checkpoint inhibition; and redirecting 
or engineering immune effector cells through adoptive cell-
based therapies (ACTs). Immune-checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) 
function by blocking regulatory pathways that physiologically 
restrain T cell activity to prevent autoimmunity but are often 
co-opted by tumors to evade immune attack. Therapeutic 
blockade of checkpoints such as programmed death-1 (PD-1) 
and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4), or their ligands 
(e.g., PD-L1, B7-1/B7-2), can reinvigorate exhausted/dysfunc-
tional T cells and restore anti-tumor activity. 3 These agents 
have revolutionized treatment paradigms in melanoma, non-
small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), renal cell carcinoma, and other 
malignancies, but they demonstrated poor clinical responses 
in most solid tumors. Many patients exhibit primary resistance, 
while others develop acquired resistance, underscoring the 
need to better understand immune-evasion mechanisms and 
enhance therapeutic durability. 4
ACTs represent an approach whereby lymphocytes (often 
T cells or natural killer [NK] cells) are isolated, expanded, or 
genetically engineered to recognize and kill tumor cells. This 
includes therapies using TCR-engineered T cells, which are 
specific for intracellular tumor antigens presented on MHC 
molecules, and chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells, which 
recognize surface antigens in an MHC-independent manner. 
These strategies have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in 
hematologic malignancies, particularly B cell cancers, but their 
application in solid tumors remains challenging due to antigen 
heterogeneity, physical barriers to infiltration, and an immuno-
suppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). 5

often utilizing CRISPR-Cas systems, have been employed to 
systematically interrogate the genetic landscape of tumor im-
mune evasion. 6–8 Notably, high-throughput CRISPR-Cas9-

A growing body of research shows that cancer cell-autono-
mous features play a major role in shaping the immune response 
to cancer. These include disruptions in antigen-presentation 
pathways, such as loss of B2M or human leukocyte antigen 
(HLA) aberrations, 30,31 defects in IFN and TNF signaling, 9,32 
and activation of oncogenic pathways such as WNT-β-catenin, 
MYC, phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)-protein kinase B 
(AKT), and KRAS. 33–36 Tumors may also silence immune-stimu-
latory genes through epigenetic mechanisms, 10,37 reprogram 
their metabolism to suppress immune function such as 
through tryptophan degradation, 38 adenosine accumulation, 39 
or lactate production, 40 and undergo chromosomal instability 
that reshapes cytokine and chemokine expression. 41 Together, 
these mechanisms impair T cell infiltration, activation, and 
persistence in the TME. It is becoming realized that these cancer 
cell-autonomous features more accurately predict response 
to immune-checkpoint blockade (ICB) than single immune bio-
markers (i.e., PD-L1 expression), identifying patients likely to 
respond, relapse, or remain refractor, as exemplified by Miao 
and colleagues. 42
Cancer cell-autonomous immune evasion can operate either 
in a strictly cell-autonomous manner or through mechanisms 
that depend on interactions with neighboring cells. Cell-autono-
mous resistance includes complete loss of MHC class I or B2M 
or the upregulation of inhibitors such as SERPINB9, which 
directly protect individual tumor cells from T cell recognition 
and killing. In contrast, non-cell-autonomous mechanisms rely 
on secreted or surface factors that act within the TME. Examples 
include the downregulation of chemokines that normally recruit 
effector T cells or secretion of cytokines that polarize macro-
phages toward an immunosuppressive phenotype. CRISPR-
based screens are especially powerful for identifying cell-auton-
omous resistance mechanisms, as they reveal vulnerabilities at 
the level of single cells under immune pressure. However, these 
approaches are less suited to detecting collective, population-
level evasion strategies that require a critical mass of tumor cells 
cooperating together with other stromal or immune cells. Recog-
nizing this distinction helps clarify both the strengths and limita-
tions of CRISPR screening in dissecting the full spectrum of tu-
mor immune-evasion strategies.

approaches, including ICB, adoptive T cell transfer (both tumour

therapies), cytokine therapies, T cell engagers, vaccines (e.g.,

bladder cancer), and strategies to deplete immunosuppressive

CRISPR limitations and emerging discovery platforms 
High-throughput genomic technologies have enabled system-
atic mapping of cancer cell-autonomous immune resistance, 
with CRISPR-based screens proving particularly powerful. 
However, key limitations impede translation. Gene-deletion 
approaches miss essential genes, and whole-genome screens 
are often dominated by strong, well-known hits. In vivo screens 
better capture tumor complexity but are labor-intensive, costly, 
and require large cohorts to maintain single guide RNA (sgRNA) 
coverage. Even validated hits can be difficult to target clinically, 
as Cas delivery may trigger immune responses and perturbing 
key regulators can disrupt normal tissues or immune subsets. 
These challenges highlight the need for tissue-specific, precise 
delivery of CRISPR tools. Emerging bulk, single-cell, and spatial 
CRISPR platforms now help overcome these barriers by 
enabling more mechanistic, physiologically relevant insights.

formed how scientists study the ways in which cancer cells

ANTIGEN PRESENTATION DEFECTS: EVADING T CELL 
RECOGNITION
Effective CD8 + T cell-dependent anti-tumor immunity relies on 
antigen presentation to detect and eliminate tumor cells. CD8 + 
T cells monitor peptides presented by MHC class I molecules, 
which originate from intracellular proteins, including tumor-asso-
ciated antigens and neoantigens. Tumor-intrinsic defects in this 
pathway can impair antigen display, rendering cancer cells less 
visible to T cells, thus promoting immune evasion. Indeed, 
genome-wide CRISPR loss-of-function screens across multiple 
cancer models consistently identified components of the MHC 
class I antigen-processing pathway (including B2M, TAP1/2, 
TAPBP, PSMB8/9) as core cancer cell-autonomous regulators 
of T cell recognition and killing.
Mutations in key components of the antigen-processing and 
presentation pathway have been observed across tumor types 
and are enriched in immunotherapy-resistant cancers 30,31,50 
(Figure 2). Key examples include β2-microglobulin (B2M) and 
transporter associated with antigen-processing TAP1/2 proteins. 
Mutations in B2M result in loss of MHC class I surface expression. 
B2M is a non-polymorphic component of MHC class I molecules, 
and its inactivation leads to complete disruption of antigen display 
and subsequent immune evasion. 51 Similarly, TAP1/2 mutations 
or deletions impair peptide translocation into the endoplasmic re-
ticulum, reducing the pool of peptides available for MHC class I 
loading. Genetic loss of TAP proteins and B2M and other core 
components of the antigen-processing and presentation pathway 
(TAPBP, PSMB8/9) have been identified in many loss-of-function 
in vitro and in vivo CRISPR screens, highlighting the power of 
these screening approaches. 9,11,18,21,22,52 Tumors harboring 
such mutations may emerge under immune pressure in patients 
treated with checkpoint blockade, highlighting their role in ac-
quired resistance. For example, recurrent B2M 30 and JAK1/2 32 
mutations have been often identified in melanoma patients relaps-
ing after anti-PD-1 therapy.
Frequently, loss of antigen presentation is not due to irrevers-
ible mutation but rather through non-genomic mechanisms. Pol-
ycomb repressive complex 2 (PRC2)-mediated H3K27me3 and
DNA methylation at antigen-processing gene promoters can 
stably suppress their expression. One striking tumor-intrinsic 
mechanism of immune evasion involves the epigenetic silencing 
of MHC class I antigen presentation. Burr et al. demonstrated 
that PRC2, via its catalytic subunit EZH2, represses genes 
such as TAP1, TAP2, B2M, and HLA class I, thereby impairing tu-
mor recognition by CD8 + T cells. 12 This silencing reflects a 
conserved developmental program co-opted by tumors to 
establish an immune-invisible state. When we refer to pathways 
being co-opted by tumors, we mean that cancers exploit normal 
physiological checkpoints that have originally evolved to restrain 
excessive or autoreactive immunity to create an immunosup-
pressive environment that blunts anti-tumor T cell activity. 
Notably, EZH2 inhibition restored MHC class I expression and 
improved T cell-mediated killing, suggesting that targeting 
epigenetic regulators may overcome resistance to immuno-
therapy. 12 Similarly, through screening an epigenetics-focused 
sgRNA library, Djajawi et al. demonstrated that protein arginine 
methyltransferase (PRMT) 1 acts to suppress IFN-γ-induced 
MHC class I induction, which could be reversed using clinical 
type I PRMT inhibitors. 13 These findings underscore the impor-
tance of chromatin state in controlling tumor immunogenicity 
and offer a rationale for combining epigenetic therapy with ICB. 
Defects in cytokine-driven potentiation of the antigen-presen-
tation process (particularly by IFN-γ) has been well described 
and identified in multiple CRISPR screens as a mechanism of tu-
mor immune evasion. 11,14,15 Tumor cells with intact JAK-STAT 
signaling typically respond to IFN-γ by upregulating components 
of the antigen-presentation machinery, including MHC class I 
molecules. 53 As mentioned, and in support of these CRISPR 
screens, JAK1/2 mutations have been identified in patients 
that fail to respond to checkpoint-blockade therapy. 32 Further-
more, tumors can suppress NLRC5, a key transcriptional acti-
vator of MHC class I genes, 54 or epigenetically silence STAT1 
downstream targets to subvert IFN-driven antigen presenta-
tion. 55 In addition to epigenetic repression, oncogenic signaling 
pathways including MYC, KRAS, and WNT/β-catenin can sup-
press MHC class I gene expression by disrupting enhancer ac-
tivity and transcriptional regulation. For instance, in β-catenin-
exposed melanoma cells, MHC class I downregulation is 
associated with reduced CD8 + T cell infiltration and poor re-
sponses to ICB, highlighting a direct link between oncogenic 
programs and immune exclusion. 33 Importantly, defects in anti-
gen presentation are not uniform across all tumor cells. Instead, 
substantial intratumor heterogeneity exists, with distinct sub 
clonal populations exhibiting variable expression or functionality 
of key components such as B2M, TAP1/2, or MHC class I mole-
cules. This allows tumors to undergo immune editing, where 
immunogenic clones are preferentially eliminated while less 
visible or antigen-deficient subclones persist and expand. Under 
the selective pressure of T cell-based therapies or ICB, this can 
drive clonal evolution and immune escape, ultimately leading to 
therapeutic resistance and tumor progression. 56

IFN-γ signaling pathway disruption as an immune-
evasion mechanism
The IFN-γ-JAK-STAT signaling axis is central to effective anti-tu-
mor immunity, driving the transcriptional upregulation of genes 
involved in antigen presentation (including MHC class I and II); 
immune-checkpoint ligands such as CD274 (PD-L1); and pro-
apoptotic or immunostimulatory effectors including IRF1, 
members of the GBP family, and chemokines such as CXCL9 
and CXCL10. 60 Loss-of-function mutations in components of 
the IFN-γ signaling pathway, including IFNγR1, IFNγR2, JAK1, 
JAK2, and STAT1, are well documented in immunotherapy-
resistant cancers, particularly melanoma and mismatch-repair-
deficient colorectal cancers. 30,53 These genes have also 
emerged as repeatedly occurring ‘‘hits’’ in multiple genome-
wide CRISPR screens investigating tumor-intrinsic resistance 
to T cell-mediated killing and checkpoint blockade. 9,14,15,20 
In clinical cohorts of patients treated with ICIs, JAK1 or 
JAK2 loss-of-function mutations have been associated with 
primary resistance to anti-PD-1 therapy, and the absence 
of IFN-γ-induced gene signatures correlates with poor 
response. 30,32 While these findings are strongly supported by 
CRISPR and preclinical models, clinical validation remains 
more limited. Retrospective analyses have identified JAK1/2 or 
B2M mutations as correlates of resistance to PD-1 blockade, 
but these observations are based on small patient cohorts and 
not yet confirmed in prospective trials. Thus, these mechanisms 
should be viewed as translationally relevant hypotheses 
that require further clinical testing. Manguso et al. performed 
an in vivo CRISPR-Cas9 screen in B16 melanoma to identify 
tumor-intrinsic regulators of response to PD-1 blockade. The 
screen recovered known resistance genes (e.g., CD274, CD47, 
IFN-γ pathway components) and revealed that loss of PTPN2 
enhances antigen presentation and tumor sensitivity to 
immunotherapy via potentiated IFN-γ signaling. 15 Recently, 
through screening of genes frequently overexpressed in cancer, 
the UBA1-STUB1 axis has been demonstrated to promote im-
mune evasion and is predictive of poor treatment outcomes 
upon ICB. Depletion or inactivation of the UBA1-STUB1 axis 
stabilized JAK1, enhancing IFN signaling, and elevated key im-
mune modulators, including CXCL9, CXCL10, and MHC class 
I. 61 Similarly, STAT1 deficiency has been reported in tumors 
from non-responders in melanoma and lung cancer ICI trials, 
and its loss correlates with decreased intratumoral CD8 + T cell 
infiltration and poor survival outcomes. 62 Beyond genetic 
lesions, the pathway is frequently suppressed at the post-tran-
scriptional level. The negative regulator SOCS1, which attenu-
ates JAK-STAT signaling, is often upregulated in tumors that 
have acquired resistance to immunotherapy, including mela-
noma and NSCLC. 63 Indeed, SOCS1 emerged as a ‘‘sensitizer’’ 
hit in a genome-wide screen for regulators of tumor immune 
evasion. 14 Additionally, epigenetic silencing of STAT1 and key 
downstream targets such as IRF1 has been observed in multiple 
poorly immunogenic tumor types, including prostate and 
pancreatic cancers, enabling immune escape while preserving 
tumor cell viability. 55,64 Together, these findings underscore 
the importance of IFN-γ pathway integrity for clinical response 
to immunotherapy and highlight potential biomarkers of resis-
tance. While loss of IFN-γ-pathway signaling promotes immune
evasion from CD8 + T cells, it simultaneously increases their 
susceptibility to killing by NK cells. Indeed, Freeman et al. 
performed a genome-wide CRISPR screen and identified that 
loss of JAK1/2 sensitizes melanoma cells to killing by NK cells. 23 
Thus, NK-based immunotherapies may represent an effective 
strategy to overcome primary or acquired resistance to ICB 
therapies. 65
Although IFN-γ promotes antigen presentation and immune 
activation, Dubrot et al. demonstrated that tumor IFN-γ signaling 
can paradoxically drive immune escape. Genome-scale in vivo 
CRISPR screens revealed that IFN-γ-induced upregulation of 
classical MHC class I suppresses NK cell activity, while induction 
of non-classical MHC class I (Qa-1b/HLA-E) inhibits CD8 + T cells 
via NKG2A/CD94, particularly after ICB. Consistently, strong IFN 
signatures correlated with poor ICB response in patients. These 
findings highlight the dual, context-dependent role of IFN-γ in 
cancer immunity. 18
TNF and immunotherapy responses
While IFN-γ generally promotes anti-tumor immunity through 
enhanced antigenicity, the effects of TNF are complex, often 
acting as a double-edged sword, 66 capable of promoting either 
inflammation and survival signaling through NF-κB or, alterna-
tively, triggering cell death. 67 Indeed, loss of TNF-induced 
apoptosis pathway components including CASP8, FADD, and 
TNFR1 have now been identified in multiple CRISPR screens 
in vitro and in vivo, highlighting the importance of this pathway 
in controlling tumor cell survival. 14 Similarly, Kearney et al. 
used CRISPR screens to highlight TNF signaling as a central 
effector pathway in cytotoxic lymphocyte-mediated tumor con-
trol. They found that loss of key TNF signaling components 
(Tnfrsf1a, Tnfrsf1b, Casp8, Fadd, Tradd) rendered tumor cells 
resistant to CD8 + T cell and NK cell killing. Importantly, this resis-
tance persisted even in the absence of perforin, indicating that 
TNF alone can drive cytotoxicity independently of canonical 
lytic granule pathways. 11 In fact, it has been demonstrated that 
PD-L1 and inhibitors of apoptosis (IAPs) co-operate to protect 
tumors from both CD8 + T cell- and NK cell-derived TNF. 68
A recent pre-print by Djajawi et al. demonstrated that loss of 
TAK1 destabilizes cFLIP, leading to combined IFN-γ and TNF-
induced apoptosis. This mechanism suggests a tumor-intrinsic 
TAK1-dependent checkpoint that controls vulnerability to com-
bined CTL-derived TNF and IFN-γ that may be therapeutically 
exploited using TAK1 biologics. Similarly, through CRISPR-
Cas9 screening, two independent studies identified the E3 ligase 
HOIP, the catalytic subunit of the LUBAC complex, as a critical 
regulator of tumor resistance to immune attack. HOIP-deficient 
melanoma cells are highly sensitive to killing by both NK and 
CD8 + T cells due to enhanced vulnerability to the combined 
effects of TNF and IFN-γ. 24,25 There is growing evidence that 
TNF and IFN-γ co-operate to control tumor cells and mecha-
nisms to avoid this form of cell death arise. Furthermore, Vrede-
voogd et al. demonstrated that lowering the tumor cell threshold 
for TNF-induced cytotoxicity enhances the efficacy of immuno-
therapy. Through a CRISPR screen, the authors identified 
TRAF2 as a key regulator of TNF resistance; genetic deletion 
of TRAF2 sensitized tumor cells to RIPK1-dependent apoptosis.

Type I interferons and tumor resistance
Type I IFNs (IFN-α and IFN-β) signal through the interferon alpha/ 
beta receptor (IFNAR)1/2 receptors, triggering activation of a 
STAT1/STAT2-IRF9 axis. These events play critical roles in anti-
tumor immunity by promoting dendritic cell maturation, activating 
NK cells, and enhancing T cell-mediated responses. 71 There is 
now growing evidence that tumors can develop resistance mech-
anisms to evade type I IFN signaling. Indeed, tumor cells may 
evade immunity by downregulation of IFNAR receptors becoming 
refractory to type I IFN signaling 72 ; through transcriptional repres-
sion of key downstream effectors, such as IRF7 and IRF9 73 ; and 
mutations or dysregulation of negative regulators like USP18. 74–76 
Importantly, in vivo CRISPR screening identified that loss of 
ADAR1 in tumor cells led to a reduced A-to-I editing of IFN-induc-
ible RNA species, leading to double-stranded RNA ligand sensing 
by protein kinase R and MDA5 and production of type I IFN. 17 
Chronic exposure to type I IFNs can drive a paradoxical state of 
adaptive resistance within the TME. While acute type I IFN 
signaling promotes antigen presentation, dendritic cell matura-
tion, and cytotoxic T cell activation, prolonged exposure to IFN-
stimulated gene (ISG) products can paradoxically suppress 
immune effector function. 77 This adaptive program includes upre-
gulation of immunosuppressive molecules such as PD-L1, which 
inhibits T cell receptor signaling; IDO1, which depletes tryptophan 
and promotes regulatory T cell differentiation; and BST2, which 
can inhibit NK cell cytotoxicity. 78,79 Mechanistically, these effects 
are mediated through persistent activation of the JAK-STAT 
pathway, leading to transcriptional reprogramming that favors im-
mune evasion. This chronic IFN signaling signature has been 
observed in both preclinical models and human cancers, and is 
often associated with resistance to ICB. 77 Therapeutic strategies 
to enhance type 1 IFN responses include the use of stimulator of 
interferon genes (STING) agonists in combination with ICB. 
Indeed, preclinical models have demonstrated that STING activa-
tion can overcome resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade by re-
programming the TME to a more immunostimulatory state. 80
DIRECT RESISTANCE TO T CELL-MEDIATED 
CYTOTOXICITY
Even when tumor cells are properly recognized by the immune 
system and exposed to activating cytokine cues, their elimination
ultimately depends on intrinsic susceptibility to cytotoxic attack 
by CD8 + T cells and NK cells. These effector lymphocytes deploy 
two principal killing mechanisms: the perforin-granzyme pathway, 
which induces apoptotic death via granzyme-mediated cleavage 
of intracellular targets following perforin-facilitated entry; and the 
death receptor pathway, which triggers caspase activation 
through ligation of surface molecules such as FAS or TRAIL-R 
(Figure 2). Tumor-intrinsic impairments in either of these cytotoxic 
routes, including loss of perforin sensitivity, granzyme resistance, 
or defects in death receptor signaling, can confer substantial im-
mune evasion. Such resistance mechanisms reduce the effective-
ness of ICB and limit the success of cytokine-based therapies or 
ACTs, underscoring the importance of intact execution pathways 
for durable anti-tumor immunity.
Tumor cells can evade granzyme-mediated cytotoxicity 
through a range of intrinsic resistance mechanisms. One well-
characterized strategy involves the upregulation of serine 
protease inhibitors, such as SERPINB9, which directly bind 
and inactivate granzyme B within the cytosol. 81 In fact, Dervocic 
et al. identified Sepinb9 as a suppressor of immunotherapy re-
sponses in lung cancer through in vivo CRISPR screening. 26 In 
parallel, modifications to the tumor cell plasma membrane, 
including increased cholesterol content or disruption of lipid 
raft architecture, have been shown to impair perforin-mediated 
pore formation and subsequent granzyme entry. 82–84 Genome-
wide CRISPR screens aimed at identifying genes that mediate 
resistance to CTL-mediated killing have uncovered a range of 
autophagy-related genes whose loss sensitizes tumor cells to 
immune attack. 14 One proposed mechanism is that autophagy 
facilitates the degradation of cytotoxic effector molecules, 
such as granzyme B, after their uptake into target cells. Similarly, 
breast cancer cells exposed to hypoxic conditions that trigger 
autophagy were found to be resistant to NK cell-mediated cyto-
toxicity, with granzyme B being degraded within autophago-
somes. 85 Together, these multifaceted evasion strategies allow 
tumor cells to survive lethal cytotoxic lymphocyte attacks and 
represent important barriers to effective immunotherapy. 86,87 
Similarly, inhibition of endosomal sorting complex required for 
transport (ESCRT) components impairs membrane repair at 
granzyme entry sites, thereby amplifying cytotoxic damage. 88 
These findings highlight a conserved cellular defense network 
that tumor cells exploit to withstand cytotoxic lymphocyte attack 
and suggest that targeting intracellular degradation or repair 
pathways may enhance the efficacy of immunotherapy. 
CRISPR activation (CRISPRa) screening has emerged as a 
powerful approach to identify tumor-intrinsic genes that confer 
resistance to immune-mediated killing when overexpressed. A 
recent important study utilized a CRISPRa screen in human 
melanoma cells and identified multiple genes that, when overex-
pressed, cause immune evasion. Key hits included CD274 
(PD-L1), MCL1, JUNB, and B3GNT2. Indeed, overexpression 
conferred resistance to CTL-mediated killing across diverse 
cancer types and in vivo xenograft models. Mechanistically, 
MCL1 and JUNB enhanced survival through modulation of 
mitochondrial apoptosis, with JUNB also activating NF-κB 
signaling and suppressing death receptor expression (FAS, 
TRAIL-R). B3GNT2, a glycosyltransferase, disrupted tumor-T 
cell interactions by altering glycosylation of key surface ligands,

studied tumor-intrinsic immunoregulatory molecules. 92 While 
the expression of ligands such as PD-L1 (CD274) is often inter-

IFN-γ exposure, it is increasingly clear that intrinsic oncogenic 
and stress-related pathways can independently drive check-

establish an immunosuppressive microenvironment even in the 
absence of active immune engagement, blunting cytotoxic re-
sponses and promoting immune escape.

the CD274 promoter and enhancer regions, driving transcription 
across multiple tumor types. 34 Oncogenic RAS signaling further

TUMOR IMMUNE EVASION
Many cancers dampen anti-tumor immunity through altered 
transcriptional and epigenetic states. These non-genomic alter-
ations are indeed often plastic and reversible, representing 
potential opportunity for therapeutic intervention. Epigenetic 
silencing contributes to immune evasion through coordinated 
repression of key immune programs (Figure 3), including antigen 
presentation, cytokine signaling, and death receptor pathway 
component expression, thereby creating a ‘‘cold’’ TME that is 
poorly infiltrated by effector immune cells.

Polycomb-mediated repression and EZH2
It is now well established that EZH2 deposits repressive 
H3K27me3 marks at the promoters of immune-stimulating 
genes, leading to their stable transcriptional repression. EZH2 
is known to silence key molecules within the IFN signaling 
pathway, including IRF1, IRF8, and STAT1, thus dampening tu-
mor cell responsiveness to IFN-γ and subsequent upregulation 
of the antigen-processing and presentation machinery. Indeed, 
EZH2 can directly repress MHC class I genes, B2M, and 
TAP1/2, thereby reducing tumor visibility to CTLs, as mentioned 
earlier. 12 Furthermore, EZH2 promotes tumor immune evasion 
through suppression of chemokines, particularly CXCL9 and 
CXCL10, that normally serve to promote lymphocyte recruitment 
into solid tumors. This phenomenon is thought to be responsible 
for poor responses to ICB. 104
Importantly, the H3K27me3 suppressive mark deposited by 
EZH2 is reversible and the enzymatic activity of EZH2 is pharma-
cologically targetable. 105 Preclinical studies have shown that 
EZH2 inhibitors can restore the expression of silenced chemo-
kines and antigen-presentation genes, leading to increased 
T cell infiltration and improved sensitivity to ICIs. 104 Tazemeto-
stat has received US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) 
approval for EZH2-mutant follicular lymphoma and epithelioid 
sarcoma and is currently being evaluated in multiple clinical trials 
for use in combination with PD-1 blockade and other immuno-
therapies across a range of solid tumors (NCT04179864, 
NCT03854474). These findings underscore the therapeutic po-
tential of epigenetic reprogramming as a strategy to convert im-
mune-refractory tumors into immunologically responsive ones.
DNA methylation and immune gene silencing
DNA methylation is a chemical modification whereby a methyl 
group (–CH 3 ) is added to the to the 5-carbon of the cytosine 
ring within cytosine-phosphate-guanine (CpG) dinucleotides 
driving gene silencing. DNA methylation is orchestrated by 
DNMTs, particularly DNMT1 and DNMT3A/B, leading to chro-
matin compaction and repression of transcription. In immune-
cold tumors, aberrant hypermethylation affects a broad range 
of immune-regulatory genes, which can promote tumor immune 
evasion.
One critical consequence of DNA hypermethylation is the 
repression of ISGs, which impairs the tumor’s ability to respond 
to inflammatory cues and resist immune attack. Additionally, 
components of the extrinsic apoptosis machinery, such as FAS 
and TRAIL receptors (TNFRSF10A/B), are often silenced, limiting 
tumor susceptibility to T cell- and NK cell-mediated killing. 106 
A particularly important target of DNMT-mediated silencing is 
endogenous retroviral elements (ERVs), which are remains of 
ancient viral infections that have been incorporated into the 
genome. Such sequences are normally kept silent through 
methylation and repressive histone marks, preventing transcrip-
tion and sensing by pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), which 
represents a therapeutic vulnerability in tumors. When these 
elements become active, they mimic a viral infection inside the 
cell, triggering a type I IFN response. This makes the tumor 
more visible to the immune system, leads to infiltration of CD8 + 
T cells, drives tumor rejection, and builds long-lasting immune 
memory. 107 Upon treatment with DNMT inhibitors, ERVs can 
be transcriptionally de-repressed, leading to the accumulation 
of double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) within the cytosol. 108,109 This 
dsRNA is sensed by the pattern recognition receptor MDA5, 
which signals through mitochondrial antiviral signaling protein 
to activate IRF3 and IRF7, ultimately inducing type I IFN produc-
tion. Type I IFNs promote immune cell recruitment, enhance an-
tigen presentation, and can sensitize tumors to ICB. In preclinical 
models, ERV reactivation has been shown to upregulate key im-
mune-stimulatory genes such as CXCL10, B2M, and MHC class 
I, thereby enhancing responses to diverse immunotherapies. 
Similarly, PRMT7 suppresses anti-tumor immunity by epigenet-
ically silencing ERVs and RNA sensors (RIG-I, MDA5). Its inhibi-
tion restores innate immune signaling, enhances T cell infiltra-
tion, and sensitizes tumors to ICB. 16 Indeed, CRISPR screens 
have revealed that repression of ERVs is a key mechanism of tu-
mor immune evasion. CRISPR screens have identified that loss 
of epigenetic regulators such as SETDB1 28 or RNA-modifying 
enzymes such as ADAR1 17 serves as a negative regulator of 
anti-tumor immunity through ERVs. As a result, DNMT inhibitors 
are currently being trialed in combination with ICB in solid tumors 
(NCT02961101, NCT03822351).
Histone deacetylases and chromatin accessibility 
Histone deacetylases (HDACs) are epigenetic enzymes that re-
move acetyl groups from lysine residues on histone tails, leading 
to chromatin compaction and reduced transcriptional accessi-
bility. 110 In cancer, HDAC activity contributes to immune evasion 
by repressing the expression of immune-stimulatory genes. 111,112 
This includes downregulation of MHC class I and II molecules, 113 
costimulatory ligands such as CD80 and CD86, 114 and a broad 
array of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines necessary 
for immune cell recruitment and activation 115 (Figure 3).
HDAC inhibitors (HDACis), such as vorinostat (SAHA) and en-
tinostat, can reverse these effects and enhance tumor immuno-
genicity. These agents restore antigen presentation, boost 
expression of chemokines such as CXCL9 and CXCL10, and

Transcriptional repressors driving immune evasion 
Beyond classical epigenetic modifiers, several transcription 
factors and chromatin regulators repress immune-related gene 
programs, contributing to tumor immune evasion (Figure 3). In 
fact, the epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) drivers ZEB1 
and SNAI1 not only promote invasiveness and plasticity but 
also actively repress genes involved in antigen presentation 
and immune activation, contributing to a stem-like, immune-
evasive tumor phenotype. 117 YAP and TAZ, transcriptional 
co-activators downstream of the Hippo pathway suppress 
MHC class I and II expression while upregulating checkpoint 
ligands including CD155 (PVR) and PD-L1, creating an unfavor-
able environment for effective anti-tumor immunity. 118 In 
melanoma, the epigenetic enzyme KDM5B (also known as 
JARID1B) helps tumors hide from the immune system by shut-
ting off genes involved in IFN signaling and antigen presentation, 
both of which are necessary for immune surveillance. In mouse 
studies, removing KDM5B boosts the body’s anti-tumor immune 
response and improves the effectiveness of immune checkpoint 
therapy. KDM5B does this by working together with another 
enzyme, SETDB1, to silence ERVs. EMSY (C11orf30) plays a 
role in DNA repair and transcriptional regulation but also pro-
motes immune evasion. In lung cancer, EMSY represses ISGs, 
while, in breast cancer, it silences miR-31, a metastasis-sup-
pressor microRNA. 119 The zinc-finger transcriptional repressor 
BLIMP1 (PRDM1) shapes the immune landscape by binding pro-
moters of ISGs and components of the MHC signaling pathway, 
including antagonizing IRF1 to prevent premature activation of 
MHC class I expression. BLIMP1 also regulates the USP22-
SPI1-PD-L1 axis, thus modulating immune checkpoint path-
ways. 120 Notably, BLIMP1 has been shown to silence type III 
IFNs in mammary epithelial cells, positioning it as a broad 
repressor of the IFN cascade. 121 Moreover, the zinc-finger pro-
tein 652 (ZNF652) represses CD274 (PD-L1) transcription by re-
cruiting the nucleosome remodeling and deacetylation complex. 
During breast cancer progression, ZNF652 expression is 
frequently lost, particularly in triple-negative breast cancer, 
enabling PD-L1-mediated immune evasion. 122 Taken together, 
a range of transcriptional repressors (EMT drivers, chromatin 
modifiers, zinc-finger proteins) actively silence IFN signaling, an-
tigen presentation, and immune-activation pathways, thus pro-
moting tumor immune evasion across multiple cancer types.
EMERGING PLAYERS: METABOLISM AND AUTOPHAGY
Beyond classical genetic and epigenetic regulators of immune 
escape, tumors also evade immunity through emerging 
noncanonical mechanisms involving cell metabolism, vesicle
trafficking, and intracellular degradation systems. These pro-
cesses are deeply intertwined with immune regulation and are 
now being recognized as central to tumor-intrinsic immune 
evasion. CRISPR screens have highlighted that metabolic state 
and autophagy are not merely supportive processes but direct 
cancer cell-autonomous regulators of immune evasion. Meta-
bolic rewiring can alter a cell’s intrinsic sensitivity to inflamma-
tory cytokines such as TNF and IFN-γ, while autophagy can 
buffer cytotoxic stress and degrade immune effector molecules 
like granzyme B. These pathways therefore represent targetable 
determinants of whether cancer cells remain vulnerable or 
resistant to T cell-mediated elimination.
Metabolic reprogramming and immune evasion 
Tumor-intrinsic metabolic rewiring supports growth but also 
diminishes anti-tumor immunity. Aerobic glycolysis, a hallmark 
of cancer, increases lactate levels, acidifies the TME, and 
impairs T and NK cell function. 40 Depletion of arginine and 
tryptophan, via ARG1 and IDO1, suppresses T cell proliferation, 
while kynurenine, a tryptophan metabolite, activates the aryl 
hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) to drive T cell exhaustion. 123 
Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that hypoxia stabilizes 
HIF1α, which downregulates MHC class I and upregulates 
PD-L1. 124 Additionally, tumor-derived adenosine, generated 
via CD39/CD73, signals through A2A receptors to suppress 
T cell activity. 125
Recent CRISPR screens have identified metabolic regulators 
as key immune-evasion factors. Pan et al. demonstrated 
that aerobic glycolysis confers resistance to T cell-mediated 
bystander killing by dampening TNF-α-induced cell death. 
Tumor cells with high glycolytic flux were less sensitive to 
TNF-mediated cytotoxicity, revealing a direct link between meta-
bolic state and immune resistance. 126 Similarly, Kearney et al. 
identified Ado (2-aminoethanethiol dioxygenase) as a novel 
regulator of tumor sensitivity to TNF-mediated killing, linking it 
to immune evasion. ADO is a metabolic enzyme involved in 
cysteamine catabolism, and its loss disrupts cysteine and poly-
amine metabolism, impairing TNF-induced apoptosis without 
affecting upstream signaling. 11 These findings, along with prior 
CRISPR studies implicating AHR, IDO1, and CD73, 9,14 under-
score the immunosuppressive role of metabolic reprogramming 
and identify new targets for combination immunotherapy.
Autophagy and immune evasion
Autophagy is a stress-adaptive recycling process that enables 
tumor cells to survive under conditions of nutrient deprivation 
and metabolic stress. Beyond its role in supporting cell survival, 
recent genome-wide CRISPR screens have revealed that auto-
phagy also promotes tumor immune evasion. Specifically, tumor 
cells exploit autophagy to degrade key immune components 
such as MHC class I molecules and STING pathway proteins, 
thereby suppressing antigen presentation and dampening type 
I IFN signaling. 127 Autophagy also restricts inflammasome 
activation and cytokine secretion, limiting immune cell recruit-
ment. 128 Intriguingly, genome-wide CRISPR screen by Young 
et al. identified autophagy as a major tumor-intrinsic resistance 
mechanism to T cell-mediated killing through de-sensitization 
to the cytotoxic effects of TNF. Mechanistically, knockout of

Unified hallmarks of tumor-intrinsic immune-evasion 
mechanisms
Cancer cells evade immune attack through diverse autonomous 
mechanisms that impair antigen recognition, blunt cytokine re-
sponses, and resist cytotoxic killing. CRISPR-based functional 
genomic screens reveal these pathways by directly testing can-
cer cell survival under immune pressure. Across models, tumors 
frequently hijack physiological immune tolerance programs to 
persist under CD8 + T cell selection. 129,130 By identifying the 
genes and pathways that drive this shift, CRISPR approaches 
enable the rational development of therapies that restore im-
mune vulnerability in cancer cells. Ongoing advances in pooled, 
single-cell, in vivo, and spatial CRISPR technologies will further 
clarify cancer cell-autonomous immune escape and guide the 
next generation of immunotherapy combinations.
Similarly to the classical ‘‘hallmarks of cancer,’’ 131 cancer 
cell-autonomous immune evasion can be conceptualized as 
comprising six interconnected domains that collectively enable 
resistance to immune-mediated destruction. Loss of antigenicity 
refers to the diminished ability of tumor cells to generate, pro-
cess, and present antigens via MHC class I, thereby reducing 
recognition by cytotoxic lymphocytes. Impaired cytokine 
signaling encompasses defects in the ability to sense and 
respond to inflammatory cues such as IFNs and TNF. Resistance 
to cell death reflects the tumor’s capacity to withstand apoptosis 
and other cytotoxic mechanisms triggered by immune cells. 
Dysregulation of checkpoint ligands involves altered expression 
of immune-inhibitory or -stimulatory surface proteins that modu-
late T cell function and immune escape. Transcriptional and 
epigenetic suppression enables tumors to adopt immune-invis-
ible or therapy-resistant states through dynamic changes in 
gene expression and chromatin structure. Finally, unconven-
tional or emerging mechanisms capture diverse escape strate-
gies beyond canonical pathways, including metabolic rewiring
and autophagy-mediated resistance to immune effectors. This 
unified framework offers a conceptual foundation for studying 
immune resistance and informing the development of next-gen-
eration immunotherapies.
