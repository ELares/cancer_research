# Anti-CTGF/PD-1 bispecific antibody Y126S restrains desmoplastic and immunosuppressive microenvironment in pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is characterized by a desmoplastic and immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), limiting the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors such as anti-programmed cell death 1 (PD-1).

**METHODS**: This study aimed to evaluate the therapeutic potential of Y126S, a recombinant IgG1/IgG2 hybrid bispecific antibody (BsAb), in reshaping the immunotherapy-resistant TME in PDAC. Orthotopic PDAC and KPC (KrasLSL-G12D/+; Trp53LSL-R172H/+; Pdx1-Cre) mouse models were established and treated with Y126S, α-connective tissue growth factor (CTGF), α-PD-1, or a combination of α-CTGF and α-PD-1. TME remodeling, antibody distribution, and therapeutic efficacy were assessed using flow cytometry, immunohistochemical/Masson staining, atomic force microscopy, positron emission tomography (PET) imaging, distribution analysis, and other experimental techniques.

**RESULTS**: Here, Y126S was characterized in vitro and its antitumor efficacy was evaluated and validated in orthotopic PDAC mice and KPC mouse models. Notably, Y126S significantly remodeled the TME and demonstrated superior tumor-specific accumulation compared with single α-PD-1 treatment, leading to markedly enhanced antitumor efficacy relative to its parental antibodies or their combination. Mechanistically, Y126S suppressed cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) activation, reduced collagen deposition, and downregulated programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression on CAFs by targeting CTGF and enhanced the anti-PD-1-mediated reinvigoration of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells, thereby establishing a less desmoplastic and potent tumor-killing microenvironment.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our findings highlight the potential of Y126S as a promising BsAb-based immunotherapy strategy for PDAC by remodeling the desmoplastic and immunosuppressive TME.

Introduction

 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains one of the most lethal malignancies due to its late diagnosis, aggressive progression, and lack of effective treatment options. Unlike other cancers that have benefited from immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapies, PDAC exhibits intrinsic resistance to immunotherapy. This therapeutic resistance is largely attributed to its dense, desmoplastic, and immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), which acts as a physical and biochemical barrier to immune infiltration and therapeutic efficacy. Strategies to disrupt stromal components and enhance cytotoxic T cell responses have shown promise but remain insufficient for achieving significant tumor regression or prolonged survival.

Cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) are pivotal in shaping the fibrotic and immunosuppressive TME of PDAC. By promoting extracellular matrix (ECM) deposition and secreting immunosuppressive cytokines, CAFs hinder immune cell infiltration and function, thereby limiting the effectiveness of ICB therapies, including anti-programmed cell death 1 (PD-1)/programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) treatment. Recent studies focusing on physically disrupting the stromal component through selective CAF depletion, etc, have effectively reduced stromal content and tumor growth, whereas effective tumor regression and prolonged survival are not achieved. This underscores the need for novel strategies to overcome stromal and immune barriers in PDAC.

ICB treatment, including anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies, has transformed cancer treatment but benefits only a small subset of patients with PDAC (< 5 %). For non-response patients, the dense ECM and immunosuppressive TME, driven by CAFs, hinder antibody penetration and T cell activity, contributing to ICB resistance. Therefore, understanding how CAFs modulate ECM and immune responses is critical for improving immunotherapy outcomes. Instead of primarily focusing on tumor cells or immune cells as in conventional therapeutic strategies, we have chosen to target CAFs within the TME, leveraging the unique characteristics of pancreatic cancer to develop a more effective treatment approach.

Studies have demonstrated that overexpression of connective tissue growth factor (CTGF) is associated with fibrotic ECM remodeling, treatment resistance, and poor prognosis in PDAC. Furthermore, CTGF promotes CAF differentiation, ECM deposition, and immune suppression, highlighting its potential as a therapeutic target. FG-3019 (pamrevlumab), a monoclonal antibody targeting CTGF, has shown promising efficacy in improving survival outcomes in PDAC and other fibrotic diseases. Based on these findings, we hypothesized that targeting CTGF could serve as a novel therapeutic strategy to enhance the clinical outcomes of ICB treatment in PDAC. However, monotherapy against CTGF remains insufficient to fully overcome PDAC’s immunosuppressive barriers.

To address these challenges, bispecific antibodies (BsAbs), a class of engineered antibodies with two distinct antigen-binding sites, have garnered significant attention due to their unique advantages over traditional antibodies. Compared with monoclonal antibodies, BsAbs exhibit enhanced therapeutic outcomes, more stable pharmacokinetics, and lower susceptibility to drug resistance. Building on these advantages, we designed a novel CTGF/PD-1 BsAb Y126S targeting both CTGF and PD-1, the latter of which has been widely used in clinical immunotherapy for pancreatic cancer. By directly inhibiting CTGF, Y126S remodels the fibrotic stroma, allowing for improved immune infiltration. Simultaneously, PD-1 blockade reinvigorates cytotoxic CD8+T cells, restoring their tumor-killing capacity. This dual mechanism effectively converts the PDAC TME from an immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state, significantly enhancing antitumor efficacy. This innovative approach aims to explore a breakthrough treatment strategy for pancreatic cancer immunotherapy.

In this study, we demonstrate that Y126S accumulates specifically within tumors. By targeting CTGF, Y126S demonstrated the ability to accumulate specifically within tumors, counteract CTGF-mediated desmoplasia, and remodel the immunosuppressive environment driven by CAFs. Additionally, through its simultaneous targeting of PD-1, Y126S restored cytotoxic T cell infiltration and reduced the proportion of M2-like tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), thereby promoting an effective antitumor immune response. By overcoming stromal and immune barriers, Y126S represents a breakthrough strategy for PDAC immunotherapy, addressing the fundamental limitations of conventional ICB and offering a promising therapeutic advancement for patients with this devastating disease.

Materials and methods

Human PDAC tissue requirement

12 fresh samples of human PDAC tumor tissues were obtained from patients with PDAC who underwent curative resection operations from 2022 to 2024 at Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology (HUST, Wuhan, China).

Cell lines and culture

Pancreatic cancer cell lines PANC-1 and Panc-02 (ATCC, Manassas, Virginia, USA) were cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (KGL1211-500, KeyGEN) supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum (FBS-CS500, NEWZEREUM) at 37 ℃ and 5 % CO2. Primary human CAFs (hCAFs) derived from human PDAC tissues and mouse CAFs derived from tumor tissues in KrasLSL-G12D/+; Trp53LSL-R172H/+; Pdx1-Cre (KPC) mouse model (KPC-CAFs) were maintained in DMEM containing 15 % FBS and 1 % Penicillin-Streptomycin (B8929, Standard Reagents).

Expression and purification of antibodies

The therapeutic antibodies (Y126S, α-CTGF, and α-PD-1 antibody) used in this study were constructed using the Check-BODY platform. α-CTGF antibody was constructed based on CN103539858A, and α-PD-1 was designed based on an anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody (Patent No: US785874682B2). Y126S was generated with specific regions targeting both CTGF and PD-1. Briefly, pcDNA3.1 vectors (V79020, Invitrogen) encoding anti-CTGF and anti-PD-1 sequence were constructed and co-transfected into Chinese Hamster Ovary (CHO) cell lines (R80007, Invitrogen) using Fecto PRO Reagent (Polyplus). After culturing for 6 days, the supernatant was collected and purified serially by Sepharose Fast Flow Protein A affinity chromatography column (GE, Milwaukee, USA), Fab Affinity KBP Agarose High Flow Resin (ACROBiosystems, Newark, USA), and SP cation exchanged chromatography column (GE, Milwaukee, USA).

Sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) assay

Reduced and non-reduced SDS-PAGE was conducted to verify the purity and molecular weight of Y126S, as previously described. After Coomassie Brilliant Blue staining and decolorization, the ChemiDoc MP Imaging system (Bio-Rad) was used to capture the image of the SDS-PAGE gel.

Surface plasmon resonance assay

Surface plasmon resonance (SPR) experiments were used to detect the binding affinity of Y126, α-CTGF, and α-PD-1 to human CTGF antigen (hCTGF), mouse CTGF protein, and mouse PD-1 protein, as previously described. Briefly, antibodies were captured on a protein A biochip (Cytiva) regenerated with pH 1.5 Glycine solution. Then, antigens were injected over the surface. The data were analyzed by the Biacore T200 Evaluation software with a 1:1 binding fitting method to determine the kinetic data, including the binding rate constant (kon), the dissociation rate constant (koff), and the equilibrium dissociation constant (KD) of antibody-antigen binding.

Antibody ELISA assays

For indirect anti-hIgG ELISA, mouse CTGF antigen(CTF-M52Hc, ACROBiosystems) and mouse PD-1 antigen (Z03383, Genscript) (200 ng/well) were precoated on 96-well plates (N803096, Cellpro) at 4 ℃ overnight. 24 hours later, the plates were washed and blocked with 5 % bovine serum albumin (BSA) (9048–46, GENVIEW) in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) for 2 hours. Serially diluted Y126S, α-CTGF, α-PD-1, or IgG were added and incubated for 1 hour. Following washes, plates were incubated with anti-hIgG-HRP (A80-319P, Bethyl) for 20 min. Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) substrate (555214, BD Biosciences) was added, and the reaction was stopped after 10 min of incubation in the dark. Absorbance was measured at 450 nm using FLUOstar OPTIMA Microplate Reader (BMG LABTECH, Cary, North Carolina, USA).

For the double-antigen sandwich ELISA assay, CTGF (200 ng/well) was precoated on 96-well plates at 4 ℃ overnight. After washing and blocking, serially diluted Y126S was added into each well and incubated at 37 ℃ for 1 hour. Later, the plates were washed, incubated with anti-PD-L1-Biotin (200 ng/mL) for 1 hour, and then with peroxidase-conjugated streptavidin (SA00001-0, Proteintech). Absorbance was quantified at 450 nm.

Atomic force microscopy

Atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Jupiter XR, Oxford Instruments Asylum Research, London, UK) instrument was used to determine Young’s modulus of the samples. For cell stiffness, fibroblasts were seeded onto gelatin-coated silicone substrates for 72 hours in 2 % FBS PBS. For tumor tissue stiffness, tumor tissues were sectioned into 50-μm-thick slices and incubated in 2 % FBS PBS. A Cr-Au AFM cantilever (HQ: CSC38, MikroMasch, Portland, USA) with a spring constant of 0.03 N/m and resonating frequency of 10 kHz was used to measure the cellular and tissue stiffness. The tests were performed with a total indentation depth of 1 µm and an extension speed of 5 µm/s in constant. Hertz’s model was applied to calculate Young’s modulus.

Apoptosis assay

Cell apoptosis assay was measured by Annexin V-FITC/PI Apoptosis Detection Kit (KGA1102, KeyGEN) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The ratio of apoptotic cells was measured by flow cytometry.

Mice and tumor model establishment

Eight-week-old male C57BL/6J mice were purchased from Beijing Vital River Company. Experiments were carried out during the light cycle. All mouse experiments were performed following the National Institutes of Health (NIH) Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals.

For orthotopic PDAC models, 2×106 Panc-02-luc cells mixed with 2×105 KPC-CAFs (primary mouse cancer-associated fibroblasts derived from tumor tissues in the KPC mouse model) in a 50/50 PBS/Matrigel (356234, Corning) were injected to the tail of pancreas for each mouse. Antibody treatment initiated 12 days later (tumors approximately 100–200 mm3) with 6 mg/kg IgG, α-CTGF, α-PD-1 or the combination of α-CTGF and α-PD-1, or different dosages of Y126S according to the design (4, 8,16 mg/kg) every 3 days for six times. The generation of transgenic KPC (KrasLSL-G12D/+; Trp53LSL-R172H/+; Pdx1-Cre) mouse model was described previously and mice were kept in the C57BL/6J genetic background. Genotyping was conducted as previously described. All mice were fed ad libitum, challenged with tumors, and randomized into control and treatment groups, according to Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) guidelines. Littermate controls were used in all experiments. Mice were sacrificed if serious deterioration of health was observed.

IVIS imaging

Mice were anesthetized using inhaled isoflurane and injected with 200 µl of D-luciferin potassium (15 mg/mL) (HY-12591B, MedChemExpress) dissolved in PBS intraperitoneally (per animal). IVIS images were acquired using an In Vivo Imaging System (PerkinElmer). Regions of interest were drawn based on the location of the tumor using Living Image V.4.2 (Xenogen) to quantify average radiance (p/s/cm2/sr).

68Ga-DOTA-FAPI PET imaging

Positron emission tomography (PET) imaging was conducted at the Nuclear Medicine Department of Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, HUST as previously described. Briefly, mice were injected with ⁶⁸Ga-DOTA-FAPI (50 μCi) via tail vein and scanned 1-hour postinjection using bio-EXPLORER PET/CT (United Imaging, Shanghai, China). Tissue standard uptake values (SUV) were normalized to total injected radioactivity and body weight of each mouse. The maximum of SUVs of every volume of interest was presented as the SUVmax.

Flow cytometry analysis

For analyzing the immune profile in PDAC tumors, freshly dissected tumors were gently digested into single-cell suspensions by Tumor Dissociation Kit (130-096-730, Miltenyi Biotec). 1×106 Singlets were collected from each sample and were blocked with anti-mouse CD16/CD32 (553141, BD Biosciences) for 15 min and stained with FVS700 (564997, BD Biosciences) for 15 min at 4℃ in 2 % FBS PBS, followed by surface staining at 4 ℃ with fluorochrome-conjugated antibodies for 30 min. For analyzing the level of CD274 expression, 1×105 KPC-CAFs treated with mouse CTGF protein (mCTGF) (HY-P72154, MedChemExpress) and multiple antibodies were blocked and stained with surface markers for 30 min at 4 ℃. Detailed information on antibodies used in flow cytometry (Beckman CytoFLEX) was listed in online supplemental table 1. Data were analyzed using FlowJo V.10.6.2 (Ashland, Oregon, USA).

Cytokine secretion measurement

Cytokine levels in PDAC/KPC tumor lysates (100 mg tissues collected and homogenized in RIPA with Cocktail: HY-K0010, MedChemExpress and PMSF: G2008, Servicebio) were quantified by Mouse IL-2 and Mouse TNF-α ELISA Kit (NeoBioscience) after tissue grinding (5 mm beads, 60 Hz, 10 min×3), centrifugation (500×g, 4 °C) and total protein quantification.

Immunohistochemistry and correlation analysis

The procedure of immunohistochemistry analysis was conducted following the protocol as previously published. All antibodies used were listed in online supplemental table 1. Masson staining was conducted using Masson trichrome staining solution (BA4079A, Baso). Images were captured by an inverted fluorescence microscope (IX71, Olympus). The quantification was analyzed and normalized using ImageJ (NIH). The positive expression was measured by average optimal density (AOD) values. For anti-CD8 and anti-Granzyme B staining, the ratio of CD8+ and Granzyme B+ cells was measured by the number of positive cells in each field.

Correlation analysis of AOD of CTGF and PD-L1 in PDAC mice was analyzed by Pearson linear analysis using GraphPad Prism V.9.5.1.

Quantitative biodistribution assay

Orthotopic PDAC-bearing mice were administered a single dose of Y126S (200 µg) and α-PD-1 (150 µg). Serum, tumors, liver, and lung were collected at intervals (0.5 hour, 1 hour, 2 hours, 4 hours, 8 hours, 24 hours, and 48 hours). Antibody distribution in vitro was quantified by the human IgG1 ELISA Kit (500910, Cayman), normalized by molecular weight (Y126S: 200kD; α-PD-1: 150kD), and expressed as the percentage of molar concentration per gram of tissue.

T cell expansion ex vivo

T cells were isolated from the C57BL/6J mice-derived splenocytes using Mouse T Cells Kit (11413D, Invitrogen). CD8+ T cells were purified by CD8a+ T Cell Isolation Kit, mouse (130-104-075, Miltenyi Biotec). Isolated T cells were activated with Mouse T-Activator CD3/CD28 (11456D, Gibco) at a 1:1 ratio for 7–10 days in the presence of 100 U/mL IL-2 (HY-P7077, MedChemExpress).

T cell cytotoxicity and exhaustion assay

KPC-CAFs were seeded at 2×106 cells/mL in 24-well plates and were treated with mCTGF (20 ng/mL) and indicated antibodies (105 nM). 24 hours later, CD8+ T cells (effector/target ratio = 2:1) were added. After 18 hours incubation, half of CD8+ T cells were blocked and stained with fluorophore-conjugated anti-mouse antibodies purchased from BD Biosciences: CD3-BV421, CD8a-FITC, PD-1-PE-CF594, TIM-3-BV650 and analyzed by flow cytometry (Beckman CytoFLEX). Annexin V-FITC/PI Apoptosis Detection Kit (KGA1102, KeyGEN) was used to analyze apoptotic cell death of the other half of CD8+ T cells. Cellular cytotoxicity was quantified by Cytotoxicity LDH Assay Kit (L128329, Aladdin) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Cytokine secretion in supernatants was analyzed using Mouse IFN-γ, Mouse IL-2, and Mouse TNF-α ELISA Kit (NeoBioscience).

RNA-seq analysis

Three replicates of hCAFs isolated from patients with PDAC were treated with recombinant human CTGF (rhCTGF) (HY-P70106, MedChemExpress) for 2 days. Then, RNA extraction, preparation, and sequencing were performed by Apexbio (Beijing, China).

Statistical analyses

Unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-test was used for two-group comparison, whereas comparison analysis in multiple groups was performed with analysis of variance followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison test. Survival curves were generated using Kaplan-Meier estimates and tested using the log-rank test. Data were represented as mean ± SEM. Statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism V.9.5.1 (GraphPad Software, California, USA) software. The level of significance was indicated as *p < 0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, and ns = not significant. Additional detailed information is available in the online supplemental methods and materials.

Results

Generation and characterization of α-CTGF/PD-1 bispecific antibody Y126S

CTGF facilitates the activation of CAF, promotes ECM deposition, and induces immune suppression, making it a promising therapeutic target. Y126S is a recombinant IgG1/IgG2 hybrid BsAb designed to simultaneously target CTGF and PD-1 (figure 1A). Structurally, the short chain of Y126S comprises three domains: VLa, CL, and VHb, while the long chain consists of five domains: VHa, CH1, VLb, CH2, and CH3. The VLa and VHa regions are derived from an anti-PD-1 (α-PD-1) monoclonal antibody (Patent No: US785874682B2), and the VLb and VHb regions originate from an anti-CTGF (α-CTGF) single-chain variable fragment (Patent No: CN103539858A). The Fc region of Y126S is a hybrid fragment, with the CH1 and CH3 domains derived from human IgG1 and the CH2 domain from human IgG2, incorporating a double substitution of alanine for aspartic acid residues (D270A) on the Fc region. In the SDS-PAGE assay, a single band corresponding to non-reduced Y126S monomers and two bands corresponding to reduced Y126S were observed (figure 1B). CE-SDS assay confirmed the purity of Y126S to be over 95 % (online supplemental figure 1A), with a molecular weight of Y126S being about 200 kDa (37.5 kDa for the short chain; 63.7 kDa for the long chain (online supplemental figure 1B).

The binding affinity of Y126S and its parental antibodies to their respective antigens was evaluated using the SPR experiments (table 1). Compared with α-CTGF, Y126S exhibited higher binding affinity for hCTGF and mouse CTGF (hCTGF: KD=1.160×10−10 for Y126S, 2.081×10−9 for α-CTGF; mouse CTGF: KD=1.415×10−10 for Y126S, 1.912×10−10 for α-CTGF) (figure 1C–F). For the mouse PD-1 antigen, Y126S exhibited binding affinity comparable to α-PD-1 (KD=3.591×10−7 for Y126S, 3.367×10−7 for α-CTGF) (figure 1G,H). Additionally, the relationship between antibody concentration and binding ability to specific antigens for these antibodies was assessed by indirect anti-hIgG ELISA, with α-CTGF and α-PD-1 as binding controls, and hIgG1 as a negative control. The results confirmed that Y126S binds mouse CTGF and PD-1 with efficiency similar to its parental antibodies (figure 1I,J). Furthermore, double-antigen sandwich ELISA assays demonstrated Y126S’s unique ability to simultaneously bind both CTGF and PD-1, a feature not observed in its parental antibodies (figure 1K). These findings highlight Y126S’s dual-specificity binding and structural integrity.

Y126S reversed CTGF-mediated immunosuppressive TME

CTGF plays a pivotal role in modulating tumor-infiltrating immune cell responses in human disease. CTGF overexpression was positively correlated with increased PD-L1 expression, a higher proportion of exhausted CD8+ T cells (figure 2A), an elevated presence of M2-like TAMs, and a reduction in CD8+ T cell infiltration within the PDAC TME (online supplemental figure 2A). Furthermore, analysis of data from The Cancer Genome Atlas database demonstrated that CTGF promotes T cell differentiation, immune response regulation, and immune response-regulating cell surface receptor pathway (figure 2B).

To further elucidate CTGF’s role in modulating immune response, RNA-seq analysis was performed on hCAFs isolated from clinical specimens. CTGF stimulation resulted in the upregulation of PD-L1 (CD274) expression and disruption of T cell activation pathways (figure 2C,D). Notably, CTGF stimulation significantly increased PD-L1 expression on CAFs but did not affect other checkpoints like CD80 or MHC-II molecules (figure 2E,F and online supplemental figure 2B,C). Furthermore, CTGF-deficient CAFs exhibited lower PD-L1 expression compared with control cells (online supplemental figure 2D-G). These findings indicate that CTGF promotes PD-L1 expression in CAFs, contributing to immune evasion in PDAC. Given the strong association between CTGF and PD-L1 expression, PD-1 was selected as a complementary target for the development of the BsAb Y126S to enhance immunotherapeutic efficacy.

Building on the promising in vitro findings, we evaluated the impact of α-CTGF treatment in vivo using PDAC mouse models to investigate its ability to remodel the immunosuppressive TME (figure 2G). Administration of α-CTGF (200 µg) led to a moderate reduction in tumor burden (figure 2H,I and online supplemental figure 3), and inhibition of PD-L1 expression (figure 2J). Furthermore, α-CTGF treatment resulted in an increase in tumor-infiltrating CD3+ and CD8+ T cells, a specific decrease in exhausted CD8+ PD-1+ TIM-3+ T cells, a reduction in M2-like TAMs, and a lower proportion of Ki67+ proliferating tumor cells (figure 2K–P). These results indicated that CTGF inhibition can reverse the immunosuppressive TME in PDAC.

To further evaluate the impact of Y126S on PD-L1 expression in CAFs and its subsequent effects on T cell exhaustion and cytotoxicity, we co-cultured mouse CD8+ T cells with KPC-CAFs (CAFs isolated from tumors in KPC (KrasLSL-G12D/+; Trp53LSL-R172H/+; Pdx1-Cre) mice) pretreated with mCTGF and various antibodies. CTGF stimulation significantly upregulated PD-L1 expression on KPC-CAFs, an effect that was suppressed by Y126S, corroborating our previous findings. Additionally, unlike IgG and α-PD-1, Y126s effectively blocked CTGF-mediated CD8+ T cell exhaustion and apoptosis, leading to enhanced T cell cytotoxicity (figure 3A). As a result, elevated levels of IL-2, IFN-γ, and TNF-α secretion within the co-culture system were detected in the Y126S treated group (figure 3B). These results indicate that Y126S counteracts CTGF-caused immunosuppression by downregulating PD-L1 expression and reactivating T cell immune response.

Y126S inhibited desmoplasia induced by CAF activation through targeting CTGF

CTGF-mediated desmoplasia (online supplemental figure 4A), a hallmark feature of the TME, significantly impedes immune infiltration and drug delivery. Analysis of PDAC tumors from figure 2G using alpha-smooth muscle actin (α-SMA)/collagen I immunostaining and Masson’s trichrome staining revealed a significant reduction in CAF activation and tumor fibrosis in the α-CTGF group (figure 4A–D). Besides, α-CTGF could notably decrease the tissue stiffness in PDAC (figure 4E). These findings demonstrate that α-CTGF suppresses tumor progression by inhibiting CAF activation and reversing the desmoplastic TME in PDAC.

To investigate the role of CTGF in CAF activation and its impact on PDAC microenvironment, we treated CAFs with either exogenous CTGF protein or conditioned media (CM) from cancer cells, supplemented with α-CTGF or Y126S. CM containing CTGF from PANC-1 cells or rhCTGF protein significantly upregulated the expression of CAFs activation markers, including α-SMA and FAP, enhanced collagen deposition, and increased mesenchymal markers expression (vimentin) at both transcriptional and protein levels. These CTGF-mediated effects were effectively reversed by α-CTGF and Y126S (figure 4F,G). Immunofluorescence staining further validated these results, showing that rhCTGF treatment markedly increased α-SMA and collagen I expression, which was subsequently reduced on α-CTGF and Y126S treatment (figure 4H). Similar results were observed in KPC-CAFs treated with CM from Panc-02 cells or exogenous mCTGF, with α-CTGF or Y126S reversing these effects (figure 4I,J and online supplemental figure 4B). Additionally, Y126S and α-CTGF significantly attenuated CTGF-mediated CAF activation, as evidenced by reduced CAF stiffness measured via AFM (figure 4K–M) and decreased collagen gel contraction capacity (figure 4N and online supplemental figure 4C).

CTGF has also been implicated in promoting cell proliferation and inhibiting apoptosis. Consistently, Y126S and α-CTGF significantly reversed the CTGF-mediated reduction in apoptosis, restored cell cycle progression by reducing the G2/M phase ratio, and suppressed CAF proliferation (online supplemental figure 5).

Collectively, these results demonstrate that Y126S effectively inhibits CAF activation, proliferation, and collagen deposition in the PDAC tumor by targeting CTGF secreted by cancer cells, thereby mitigating desmoplasia and remodeling the TME.

Y126S demonstrates superior therapeutic efficiency compared with single or combination treatments of parental antibodies in an orthotopic PDAC model

To evaluate the in vivo antitumor effect of Y126S, we established an orthotopic PDAC tumor model and administered various antibody treatments. Initially, we investigated the dose-dependent antitumor effects of Y126S in PDAC-bearing mice in PDAC-bearing mice by randomizing them into four groups: IgG control, low-dose (4 mg/kg), medium-dose (8 mg/kg), and high-dose (16 mg/kg) of Y126S (figure 5A). IVIS imaging revealed a clear dose-dependent increase in antitumor activity (figure 5B and online supplemental figure 6A,B). Notably, both medium-dose (3/8, >80 days) and high-dose (4/8, > 80 days) Y126S treatments efficiently inhibited tumor progression and significantly prolonged overall survival (figure 5B,C). Importantly, no significant weight loss was observed across all treatment groups, indicating a favorable safety profile (online supplemental figure 6C). While we observed statistically significant difference in tumor weight between medium-dosage (8 mg/kg) and high-dosage (16 mg/kg) groups (p = 0.024), no significant differences were detected in either in vivo tumor activity by IVIS imaging or survival rates of tumor-bearing mice (figure 5B,C and online supplemental figure 6A,B). Considering the comparable efficacy profiles alongside potential increased drug burden and toxicity risks at higher doses, we selected 8 mg/kg as the optimal therapeutic dosage for subsequent in vivo experiments, with equivalent molar doses of control antibodies (α-CTGF at 6 mg/kg and α-PD-1 at 6 mg/kg) used for comparative analyses.

Next, we compared the therapeutic efficacy of Y126S with its parent antibodies in the PDAC model. Tumor-bearing mice were randomized into treatment groups receiving Y126S, α-CTGF, α-PD-1, or a combination of α-CTGF and α-PD-1 (figure 5D). No significant differences in weight change, abnormal serum biochemical indicators, or histological abnormalities in vital organs were observed across groups (online supplemental figure 7). Gratifyingly, IVIS imaging revealed that Y126S exhibited superior antitumor efficacy compared with individual or combined therapy with parental antibody (figure 5E,F). Tumor weights in the Y126S-treated group were significantly lower than those in IgG (p < 0.001), α-CTGF (p < 0.026), and α-PD-1 (p < 0.012), and marginally lower than the combination therapy (p = 0.06) (figure 5G). These findings highlight the potent therapeutic efficacy of Y126S in the orthotopic PDAC model, demonstrating its superiority over both single-agent and combination treatments with parental antibodies.

Y126S reduced collagen deposition and reshaped the immunosuppressive TME in the PDAC model

Given the critical role of CTGF in ECM remodeling through the regulation of the fibro-secretory capability of CAFs, we investigated the impact of Y126S on ECM dynamics by targeting CTGF. Masson and α-SMA/collagen I staining revealed that Y126S superiorly retained CAF activation, reduced collagen deposition, mitigated tumor fibrosis, and decreased Young’s modulus in PDAC-bearing mice compared with IgG, α-CTGF, and α-PD-1 treated group (figure 5H–L).

To assess whether Y126S restores antitumor immune response, we analyzed the tumor-infiltrating immune cells using flow cytometry (online supplemental figure 8). Y126S treatment facilitated the infiltration of CD3+ and CD8+ T lymphocytes into tumor centers, indicating robust T cell activation (figure 5M,N). Notably, the proportion of PD-1+ TIM-3+ exhausted CD8+ T cells was significantly lower in the Y126S-treated group than that in other treatment groups (figure 5O), suggesting that Y126S enhanced T cell cytotoxicity within tumor tissue. These findings were corroborated by immunohistochemical (IHC) staining for CD8 and Granzyme B, further supporting the activation of cytotoxic T cell responses (online supplemental figure 9A,B). Since the accumulation of M2-like TAMs in TME limits antitumor immunity, we also evaluated the effect of Y126S on TAM population. Y126S treatment significantly reduced M2-like TAMs proportion, further underscoring its immune-activating effects (figure 5P). Moreover, ELISA assays revealed higher levels of IFN-γ in Y126S-treated mice compared with other groups, indicating a highly activated antitumor immune response (figure 5Q). To further assess the impact of Y126S-restored immune response on immune cells, we performed IHC staining for Ki67 in tumor tissues. Y126S significantly reduced Ki67 expression compared with control groups, indicating that the tumor cell and KPC-CAF proliferation was suppressed by Y126S-enhanced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity (online supplemental figure 9C). Collectively, these results demonstrate that Y126S suppresses tumor progression by reducing tumor fibrosis, enhancing T cell immune response, and revising the immunosuppressive microenvironment in PDAC tumor.

Y126S yields a robust antitumor response and prolongs survival in the KPC model

To further validate the antitumor efficacy of Y126S, we used a transgenic KPC mouse model, administered with α-CTGF, α-PD-1, a combination of α-CTGF and α-PD-1, or Y126S (figure 6A). 68Ga-FAPI PET imaging system was used to monitor the tumor progression in KPC and found that Y126S dramatically decreased tumor burden (figure 6B), effectively inhibited cancer progression (figure 6C), and significantly prolonged survival compared with monoclonal antibody or combination therapies (figure 6D). Furthermore, histological analyses demonstrated that Y126S markedly suppressed CAF activation, decreased collagen deposition, reduced tumor fibrosis, and lowered Young’s modulus (figure 6E–I). In addition, flow cytometry and IHC assessments confirmed that Y126S substantially increased infiltration of CD3+ T cells and CD8+ T cells, while reducing the proportion of exhausted PD-1+ TIM-3+ CD8+ T cells and M2-like TAMs (figure 6J–M and online supplemental figure 10A). Additionally, Y126S enhanced the secretion of Granzyme B and IFN-γ within the TME (figure 6N and online supplemental figure 10B) and decreased Ki67+ cells in tumor tissue (online supplemental figure 10C). Together, these findings indicate that Y126S effectively reprograms the TME by reducing desmoplastic ECM remodeling, enriching tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells, and eliciting a potent antitumor immune response, ultimately leading to improved survival in the KPC model.

Y126S exhibits tumor-specific accumulation through targeting CTGF

CTGF overexpression has been reported in various cancers and is closely associated with tumor fibrosis, which hindered the penetration of conventional drugs. To validate these findings, we analyzed CTGF levels in tumor and adjacent normal tissues from patients with PDAC who underwent curative resection at Tongji Hospital. Both genetic and protein-level analyses revealed significant CTGF overexpression in tumor tissues compared with normal tissues (figure 7A–C). These results indicate that Y126S might achieve higher accumulation concentrations compared with normal ICB mono-antibodies at tumor sites through targeting CTGF.

To validate our hypothesis, we conducted quantitative biodistribution analysis in the PDAC mouse model to compare the concentration of Y126S and α-PD-1 in the tumor site and essential organs. After tumor establishment, Y126S (200 µg, 200 kD) and α-PD-1 (150 µg, 150 kD) were administered at equivalent molar doses (figure 7D). First, the Y126S concentration curve was determined by quantifying the hIgG concentration in serum samples, revealing a Y126S plasma peak at∼1 hour (figure 7E). Based on this plasma concentration curve and previous studies, we hypothesized maximum tumor accumulation would occur at 2–4 hours. Y126S demonstrated significantly higher tumor concentrations than α-PD-1 at all examined time points, likely due to its specific binding affinity for CTGF. Additionally, Y126S exhibited delayed peak accumulation (1–2 hours) and a longer clearance half-life (∼ 8–24 hours) compared with α-PD-1 (0.5–1 hour, ∼ 4–8 hours) (figure 7F). Importantly, Y126S achieved a higher peak concentration (at 2 hour) and total tumor concentration than α-PD-1 (figure 7G). This enhanced tumor accumulation likely contributes to the superior therapeutic efficacy of Y126S. The biodistribution profile of Y126S and α-PD-1 in the liver and lung mirrored those in the tumor. Notably, Y126S concentrations in the tumor exceeded those in the liver or lung at each time point, whereas α-PD-1 showed no significant difference between tumor and normal tissues (figure 7H-K). These findings suggest that Y126S’s dual-specificity structure and CTGF binding enable tumor-specific accumulation and stable pharmacokinetics, leading to enhanced therapeutic efficacy and reduced off-tumor toxicity. In addition to the potent antitumor effect, the lower accumulation in essential organs also explains the reduced side effects of Y126S. These results demonstrate that Y126S possesses tumor-specific targeting capabilities and supports CTGF as an effective carrier for delivering therapeutic antibodies to tumor sites.

Discussion

PDAC is notoriously resistant to immunotherapy due to its inherent tumor heterogeneity, highly desmoplastic, and immunosuppressed TME. Developing novel therapeutic strategies to improve treatment responsiveness remains an urgent clinical need. In this study, we developed a novel BsAb that simultaneously targets CTGF and PD-1. This anti-CTGF/PD-1 BiAb, termed Y126S, significantly improved antitumor response by inhibiting CTGF-mediated CAF activation, collagen deposition, and production of immunosuppressive cytokines, while simultaneously enhancing T cell infiltration and reinvigoration through PD-1 blockade.

One of the key findings in our study is that Y126S effectively reverses the immunosuppressive and desmoplastic TME of PDAC by targeting both CTGF and PD-1. CTGF plays a critical role in modulating the tumor stroma and immune response. It has been shown to promote CAF activation, collagen deposition, and ECM remodeling, all of which contribute to the desmoplastic features of PDAC. Furthermore, CTGF has been implicated in the regulation of immune checkpoint molecules like PD-L1 on CAFs, which helps create an immunosuppressive environment within the tumor. By targeting CTGF, Y126S not only inhibits CAFs activation and collagen deposition but also reduces the production of immunosuppressive cytokines, thereby creating a more favorable TME for immune cell infiltration and activity. This contrasts sharply with traditional therapies, which often focus on either ICB (eg, PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors) or stromal remodeling as separate therapeutic strategies.

Y126S administration effectively disrupted the critical role of activated CAFs in collagen deposition, tumor growth, and malignancy. Furthermore, Y126S restored CD8+ T cell infiltration and function while reducing the presence of immunosuppressive cells in PDAC and KPC models. Notably, the combination of anti-CTGF and anti-PD-1 monospecific antibodies (parental antibody with the same clone of Y126S) did not achieve the same degree of matrix remodeling and immune microenvironment modulation as Y126S, highlighting its superior therapeutic potential. In addition, in vivo experiments demonstrated that Y126S was well-tolerated at effective therapeutic doses in PDAC and KPC mice.

Traditional PDAC treatments, including chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors, have shown limited success, attributed to the dense stromal matrix in PDAC that impedes drug delivery and hinders immune cell infiltration. In contrast, Y126S uniquely addresses this challenge by targeting both the ECM and the immune components of the TME. The inhibition of CTGF-mediated desmoplasia softens the tumor matrix, facilitating improved immune cell infiltration, including the reinvigoration of exhausted CD8+ T cells. This dual action—targeting both the stromal and immune components—marks Y126S as a promising candidate for enhancing tumor responsiveness to immunotherapy.

Additionally, Y126S’s ability to simultaneously target PD-1 enhances its potential as an immunotherapeutic agent. PD-1 is a critical immune checkpoint receptor expressed on activated T cells, and its engagement with PD-L1 on tumor cells or immune cells results in T cell exhaustion and immune evasion. By blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction, Y126S rejuvenates tumor-infiltrating T cells, restoring their cytotoxic function and enhancing their ability to target and kill tumor cells. This action is particularly relevant in PDAC, where the TME is heavily immunosuppressive, and T cell exhaustion is a prominent feature. Traditional treatments, such as monotherapies with PD-1 or CTGF inhibitors, have not been able to achieve this level of synergy. Y126S, however, bridges these two pathways, providing a more comprehensive approach to modulating the immune response within the tumor.

The design of Y126S, featuring high affinity for both CTGF and PD-1, enhanced its selectivity for the PDAC TME over normal tissues, owing to the high expression of CTGF in PDAC tumor sites. Due to its bivalent binding to CTGF and PD-1 and a molecular mass similar to IgG, Y126S is expected to exhibit better tumor localization compared with previously generated monospecific antibodies and other BsAbs. This suggests that Y126S may more effectively deliver PD-1-expressing T cells to tumor sites with high CTGF expression, enabling targeted tumor cell or CAF elimination and matrix softening. The exact mechanisms by which Y126S restores exhausted CD8+ T cells remain an intriguing area for further investigation.

Developed using the Check-BODY platform, Y126S is a novel antibody that simultaneously targets two key aspects of PDAC: ECM deposition and immunosuppressive TME. Unlike conventional BsAbs that primarily focus on blocking immune checkpoints, Y126S innovatively addresses both ECM and immune components, marking a significant advancement in cancer therapy.

While the dual-targeting design of Y126S offers distinct advantages by simultaneously modulating both CTGF and PD-1 pathways, potentially obviating the need for combination therapies and their associated risks, further investigation of complementary treatment strategies remains crucial. Future studies could explore synergistic combinations with agents targeting additional TME components, particularly those involved in fibroblast activation or ECM remodeling. Such combination strategies could potentiate Y126S’s efficacy by creating a more permissive TME for immune-mediated destruction.

In conclusion, our study demonstrates that Y126S, which simultaneously blocks CTGF and PD-1/PD-L1 pathways, exhibits potent antitumor efficacy for PDAC. By reversing desmoplastic ECM remodeling, enhancing immune infiltration, and rejuvenating exhausted T cells, Y126S significantly improves the antitumor immune response in PDAC and KPC mouse models (figure 7L). This dual targeting approach, which addresses both the stromal and immune components of the TME, makes Y126S a highly promising candidate for clinical translation, offering a novel treatment option for patients with PDAC and potentially other solid tumors.
