# Bystander CAR-CD8+ T cells in a CAR-T cell product can expand and enhance the antitumor effects of a bispecific antibody.

## Abstract

Although bispecific antibody (BsAb) treatment is a valuable therapeutic option for post chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy against relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma, it remains to be clarified why it is still effective after intensive T-cell redirection therapy. Recently, the therapeutic potential of bystander CD8+ T cells in the field of cancer immunotherapy have been discussed. In this study, we have shown a clinical impact where bystander CAR-negative CD8+ T cells from a CAR-T cell product have a potential to augment immune responses of BsAb therapy through a case with relapsed diffuse large B-cell lymphoma after CD19 CAR-T cell therapy. T cells in a CAR-T-cell product dominantly showed central and effector memory T cells, and such T-cell phenotypes in peripheral blood significantly increased after CAR-T cell therapy. Furthermore, chronological T-cell receptor-β repertoire analyses for both CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells suggested that product-derived bystander CAR-CD8+ T cells successfully existed in a relapsed lymph node and expanded in the body after BsAb therapy. Further analyses are necessary, but our findings might help to explain the mechanisms and benefits of this sequential approach and strengthen the sequence of CAR-T cell therapy prior to BsAb therapy.

Insights

 This clinical case proposes an impact of bystander chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)−CD8+ T cells in a CAR-T cell product to augment antitumor effects of bispecific antibody therapy.

 Introduction 

Immunotherapy using T cells redirected with a chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) and bispecific antibody (BsAb) has shown promise for the treatment of relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma. It has been reported that BsAb therapy can induce overall responses in 30–50% of patients who suffer disease relapse after CAR-T cell therapy. Although this suggests that BsAb treatment is now a valuable therapeutic option for post CAR-T cell therapy, it remains to be clarified why it is still effective after intensive T-cell redirection therapy. Recently, the therapeutic potential of bystander CD8+ T cells in the field of immunotherapy have been discussed. In this study, we have proposed a clinical impact where bystander CAR-negative CD8+ T cells from a CAR-T cell product have the potential to augment immune responses of BsAb therapy.

 Results 

A patient in their 70s with diffuse large B-cell lymphoma was referred to our hospital. Although eight cycles of rituximab-CHOP chemoimmunotherapy had induced complete responses, the disease had relapsed after 2.5 years. Rituximab-GDP was selected as a secondary regimen, but the disease progressed within 3 months. At this point, our team carefully discussed the condition and decided to apply CD19 CAR-T cell therapy. Rituximab-EPOCH followed by ICE (ifosfamide, carboplatin, and etoposide) chemotherapy was chosen to reduce tumor burden. We then collected the peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), and the apheresis product was used to manufacture CAR-T cells (tisagenlecleucel). A regimen of polatuzumab vedotin, rituximab, and bendamustine was then started as a bridging chemoimmunotherapy. As partial responses had been achieved after three cycles (figure 1A) and CAR-T cell production had been completed, the patient underwent lymphodepletion with bendamustine, followed by administration of CAR-T cells. Grade 1 cytokine release syndrome (CRS) was easily managed using tocilizumab (8 mg/kg × one time) without corticosteroids, and close to complete responses were observed (online supplemental figure 1A). To identify CD19 CAR-T cells, we employed soluble CD19 (sCD19) dimer. As well as the CAR-T product, CAR-T cells were successfully detected in the peripheral blood until 3 weeks (figure 1B–C and online supplemental figure 2). The CAR-T cells showed a predominant effector memory (TEM) phenotype and became terminally differentiated T cells (TEMRA) after antigen-specific stimulation in vitro (figure 1C). Unfortunately, however, the patient suffered disease progression after 3 months (figure 1D). Analysis of relapse in a lymph node revealed that the tumor cells retained both CD19 and CD20 expression and that sCD19-negative CD3+ T cells with effector phenotypes were present (figure 1E).

On the basis of these findings, we selected epcoritamab (CD20×CD3 BsAb) as the next treatment. This resulted in grade 3 CRS with an elevation of the lactate dehydrogenase level, a high fever (40.0°C), and low blood pressure requiring continuous infusion of norepinephrine. Repetitive administration of tocilizumab (8 mg/kg four times) with dexamethasone (12 mg four times for 2 days and 6 mg four times for 4 days) led to a gradual improvement of the patient’s condition (figure 2A). Surprisingly, the relapse was completely resolved after 3 months, as demonstrated by positron emission tomography–computed tomography (PET-CT) scanning (figure 2B). Because the CAR-T cell product contained around 60% sCD19-negative bystander CD3+ T cells, we focused on the assessment of this population as possible responder cells in the presence of bispecifics. The patient’s sCD19-unstained T cells in the CAR-T product also showed a TEM phenotype (online supplemental figure 2A), and the peripheral blood CD3+ T-cell phenotype after CAR-T cell therapy was mainly central memory (TCM) and TEM unlike that observed before CAR-T cell therapy (online supplemental figure 2B). Interestingly, this population was preserved, and the frequency of TEM and the number of lymphocytes in the periphery was increased after administration of full-dose epcoritamab (online supplemental figure 1B and 2B). To investigate how T cells had expanded in the patient’s body, chronological T-cell receptor (TCR)-β repertoire analyses were performed. Product-derived bystander CAR−CD3+CD8+ T-cell clones appeared to have survived among CD3+ T cells in the peripheral blood and lymph nodes after CD19 CAR-T cell therapy (figure 2C, D). A unique CD3+CD8+ TCR-β repertoire of sCD19-positive CAR-T cells was hardly detectable, similarly to the results of flow cytometry (figure 2C, online supplemental figure 2B and online supplemental table 1). Among T-cell clones detectable during 20 weeks, 15 product-derived bystander CAR−CD3+CD8+ T-cell clones, but not 100 non-product CD3+CD8+ T-cell clones obtained at apheresis and 293 newly identified CD3+ T-cell clones after CAR-T cell therapy, preferentially proliferated between 12 and 20 weeks after epcoritamab therapy, supported by an increase of the TCR-β repertoire (figure 2E, online supplemental figure 1C and online supplemental table 2-3). As CD8+ T cells are more susceptible to cytokines, this might explain why the TCR-β repertoire of CD3+CD4+ T cells derived from the patient were not continuously detectable. From this viewpoint, it was considered that the patient’s CD3+CD4+-dominant CAR-T cells might support in vivo product-derived bystander CAR−CD3+CD8+ T-cell expansion.

 Discussion 

Since a series of BsAbs can engage mainly the CD3 molecule expressed on human T cells without any CD28 costimulatory signal, TCM/TEM can work preferentially to naïve T cells in the body. Manufacture of CAR-T cells for expansion using CD3/CD28 beads can also yield effector T cells, resulting in adoptive transfer of memory-formed bystander T cells along with CD19 CAR-T cells. These transferred T cells, as well as T cells remaining in the body, would be affected by cytokines released due to lymphodepletion and activation of CD19 CAR-T cells. Lymphodepletion has the potential to provide a homeostatic niche for the expansion of not only CAR-positive T cells, but also concomitantly transferred product-derived bystander T cells and non-product remaining/newly growing bystander T cells (figure 2C and online supplemental figure 1C). Since in this case the TCR-β repertoire used predominantly for CAR-T cells was derived from a small number of T-cell clones (online supplemental figure 1D), further differentiation of CAR-T cells with TEM/TEMRA as observed after antigen stimulation (figure 1C) might have led to the failure of long-lasting CAR-T cell responses. In contrast, bystander T cells with such supportive cytokine signals could have been advantageous for augmenting the antitumor response with BsAb, and product-derived bystander CAR−CD8+ T cells might have preferentially survived and worked as TCM cells as well as TEM cells, as supported by the patient’s TCR-β repertoire and clinical outcome (online supplemental figure 3). Some of the TCR-β sequences showed similarity (online supplemental table 2), suggesting that they might have recognized the specific HLA/peptide complex; however, this was unclear on the basis of an in silico search (https://vdjdb.cdr3.net/search).

We have previously performed side-by-side experiments to compare the antitumor effects of CAR-T cells with those of BsAb-mediated T cells, where pre-activated human T cells were used for both groups. Although these cells functioned comparably, it would be necessary to deliver both the BsAb and effector T cells properly in tumor microenvironments. In our present patient, product-derived bystander CAR-CD8+ T cells were still present at tumor sites (figures1E  2D), and might have been effective for killing relapsed tumor cells. Although the patient’s CD19 CAR-T cells were marginally expanded after BsAb therapy, the dual activation mediated by CD19 CAR plus CD20 BsAb might have resulted in sufficient signals for the CAR-T cells, which would also be beneficial when a proportion of target antigens on tumor cells were downregulated.

A limitation of our analysis was that it might have been difficult to completely segregate product from non-product CD8+ T cells even on the basis of the TCR-β repertoire. However, precise comparative analysis might provide a new insight into CAR-T cell therapy for adoptive transfer of pre-activated bystander CAR−CD8+ T cells to enhance the antitumor effects of BsAb therapy, thus strengthening the effectiveness of CAR-T cell therapy prior to BsAb therapy. Currently, a clinical trial of BsAb for patients showing partial responses at 30 days after CAR-T cell therapy is ongoing. Although further investigations will be necessary, our present results might help to explain the mechanisms and benefits of such sequential approaches.
