# A Phase II Study of Optimized Individualized Adaptive Radiotherapy for Hepatocellular Carcinoma.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: We hypothesized that optimizing the utility of stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) based on the individual patient's probability for tumor control and risk of liver injury would decrease toxicity without sacrificing local control in patients with impaired liver function or tumors not amenable to thermal ablation.

**PATIENTS AND METHODS**: Patients with Child-Pugh (CP) A to B7 liver function with aggregate tumor size >3.5 cm, or CP ≥ B8 with any size tumor were prospectively enrolled on an Institutional Review Board-approved phase II clinical trial to undergo SBRT with baseline and midtreatment dose optimization using a quantitative, individualized utility-based analysis. Primary endpoints were change in CP score of ≥2 points within 6 months and local control. Protocol-treated patients were compared with patients receiving conventional SBRT at another cancer center using overlap weighting.

**RESULTS**: A total of 56 patients with 80 treated tumors were analyzed with a median follow-up of 11.2 months. Two-year cumulative incidence of local progression was 6.4% [95% confidence interval (CI, 2.4-13.4)]. Twenty-one percent of patients experienced treatment-related toxicity within 6 months, which is similar to the rate for SBRT in patients with CP A liver function. An analysis using overlap weighting revealed similar local control [HR, 0.69; 95% CI (0.25-1.91); P = 0.48] and decreased toxicity [OR, 0.26; 95% CI (0.07-0.99); P = 0.048] compared with conventional SBRT.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Treatment of individuals with impaired liver function or tumors not amenable to thermal ablation with a treatment paradigm designed to optimize utility may decrease treatment-related toxicity while maintaining tumor control.

INTRODUCTION

While stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) is safe and effective for patients with small hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) tumors and good liver function, treatment of patients with large tumors or compromised liver function remains challenging. Previous studies assessing SBRT in Child-Pugh (CP) B or C patients demonstrated conflicting results, with some suggesting acceptable toxicity in carefully selected patients and others showing 35% or greater rates of functional hepatic decline.

Our prior work suggested that adapting treatment to liver function measured one-month after the first 3 treatments of a possible 5 treatment course maintained local control while reducing treatment related toxicity compared to other published studies. The prior strategy administered the highest possible dose with an acceptable risk of toxicity. However, this approach might not be optimal, because it may prescribe doses that increase the risk of toxicity more than increasing the probability of tumor control.

We hypothesized that outcomes could be improved by response adaptive treatment that optimizes the predicted utility of treatment, defined as a balance of the probability of local control and toxicity for individual patients, rather than by the standard method of giving the highest dose with an “acceptable” risk of toxicity. Here, we report the results of a prospective clinical trial assessing the safety and efficacy of optimized response adaptive SBRT. In addition, we compare our results to those obtained in similar patients treated with standard SBRT at another high-volume center.

METHODS

Patient Selection

Trial protocol and consent forms were approved by the University of Michigan Institutional Review Board (IRB) (NCT02460835). The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. Eligible patients were at least 18 years of age with HCC as determined either by biopsy or radiographic diagnosis. Patients had a Zubrod performance status of 0–2, life expectancy ≥12 weeks, platelet count ≥ 30,000, BUN ≤ 40 mg/dL, and creatinine ≤2.0 mg/dL. Any number of prior liver-directed therapies were permitted, provided the last procedure had been completed at least 4 weeks prior to protocol therapy and acute effects of the therapy had resolved based on clinical and laboratory based assessments. Patients with extrahepatic disease or those eligible for curative liver resection were excluded. All patients were reviewed at an HCC multidisciplinary tumor board. Informed written consent was obtained from all patients prior to enrollment. After interim analysis of the first 20 patients revealed excellent local control and minimal toxicity in patients with good liver function and small (<3.5 cm) tumors, the protocol was amended to enroll only patients with larger tumors or impaired liver function, where there was stronger rationale for improving the rate of local control or reducing the risk of toxicity. After this amendment, patients were required to have either a CP score ≥B8 with any size tumor, or CP A5-B7 with either a single or multiple tumors with aggregate size >3.5 cm, chosen as the standard maximum size for thermal ablation at our institution. At this analysis the decision was also made to proceed exclusively with a 5-fraction treatment regimen and the fractionated arm was closed.

Treatment Schema

At the start of the trial, initial treatment plans were developed in which tumor dose was selected as the lesser of either 11 Gy per fraction for 5 fractions, as this dose provides nearly 100% tumor control, or the dose resulting in a normal tissue complication probability (NTCP) of ≤10% predicted CP score change ≥2 points (“standard dosing”). After 20 patients were enrolled and the decision was made to exclude the most favorable patients, this approach was modified to select the initial dose to maximize the expected utility of the entire treatment course. Since mean liver dose (MLD) correlates with toxicity, an initial treatment plan was developed maximizing the ratio of tumor dose to mean liver dose. Patient-level probability estimates were then generated for treatment-related toxicity and lesion progression based on each patient’s baseline ICGR15 or ALBI, CP score, intended dose and fractionation, and mean liver dose. Expected utility of treatment was estimated as a weighted average of 4 binary toxicity and local control outcomes (tumor control vs. no tumor control, with or without treatment related toxicity), with weighting determined by utility scores assigned based on the desirability of each outcome. The ultimate treatment dose was selected with the goal of balancing the probability of local control against the probability of treatment-related toxicity. An example of this utility analysis is found in the supplementary materials.

All patients received 3 fractions every-other-day of this initially selected dose followed by reassessment of liver function at 1 month. The dose per fraction for the last 2 fractions was selected as the dose that maximized the expected utility of treatment; patients could either continue at the same dose, receive a lower dose, or receive no further radiotherapy. The final two fractions were also delivered every-other-day.

Treatment Planning

Radiation therapy was planned as described previously. Briefly, we used contrast-enhanced CT simulation with motion management, fused to a contrast-enhanced MRI for target delineation with standard definitions of GTV, ITV and PTV. Breath-hold technique was used for motion management when tolerated, otherwise 4DCT was used. Fiducial markers were not routinely used. Dynamic Contrast Enhanced MRI sequences were obtained, and portal venous perfusion or gadoxetic acid uptake rate was assessed for mapping of hepatic function as described previously. Functional avoidance treatment planning was enabled by dividing the liver into low, moderate, and high perfusion regions. We attempted to minimize dose to moderate and high perfusion or function areas. Standard organ-at-risk (OAR) limits in routine clinical practice were applied and were assigned priority over target coverage.

Evaluation

After completion of treatment, patients were followed with history and physical, laboratory assessment and liver MRI every 3 months for 2 years. Three pre-specified primary toxicity endpoints were analyzed: increase in CP score ≥ 2 points within 6 months of treatment, increase in ALBI by ≥ 0.5 within six months, and any treatment-related grade 3+ CTCAE ascites or GI bleeding. The threshold of change in ALBI of 0.5 was based on our prior work demonstrating this magnitude of change has a high correlation to hepatic toxicity. Lesion-specific local control was assessed using a modified version of the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST), as we have described previously. An increase in CP score ≥ 2 points in fewer than 25% of patients and a 1-year local control rate of at least 80% were pre-specified as sufficiently promising to proceed to a randomized trial.

Comparison to Conventional SBRT

Data from trial patients treated with optimized, response-adapted SBRT was compared with a cohort of 103 patients receiving standard SBRT in 3–5 fractions to 159 tumors from 2007–2017 at the University of Toronto/Princess Margaret Cancer Centre. The cohort of patients receiving standard SBRT included only patients with baseline CP A5-B8, so any trial patients receiving optimized adaptive RT with CP-B9 or greater disease were excluded from this analysis.

Statistical Analysis

Toxicity analyses were performed at the patient level while cumulative incidence of local progression and freedom from local progression (FFLP) were analyzed at the tumor level as we have reported previously. A patient level frailty term was included to account for potential correlation between lesions within a patient. The binary toxicity measures were summarized with binomial proportions and confidence intervals. In the primary analysis, freedom from local progression was evaluated using time-to-event analysis with censoring performed at the time of last follow up or death.

Comparison with patients receiving conventional SBRT was performed using overlap weighting of propensity scores. The Michigan Radiation Oncology Analytics Resource (MROAR) was used to process and curate both datasets. A propensity score model was generated using multivariable logistic regression with SBRT type (optimized vs conventional) as the treatment indicator, accounting for age, sex, race (white vs. non-white), baseline CP score, tumor size, target volume, number of active lesions, and baseline platelet count as predictor variables. Logistic regression was then performed with overlap weighting of propensity scores at the patient-level with toxicity as the outcome, as defined by change in CP score of ≥2 points within 6 months of completion of treatment. Multiple imputation was used to account for missing data and Rubin’s rule was used to pool estimates. A weighted Cox regression model was used for analysis of local control, with tumor-level local progression as the outcome. Analyses were performed using R software.

RESULTS

Patient Demographic and Tumor-Level Dosimetric Data

Between 2015 and 2021, 77 patients were enrolled; 56 were evaluable for this analysis (Figure 1). Two patients were re-enrolled for new lesions after successful treatment of their initial lesions. Patient demographics are summarized in Table 1.

A total of 80 tumors were treated to a median biologically effective dose (BED) of 69.3 Gy. Of these, 44 tumors were treated in patients with CP-A5 to B7 disease, with a median individual tumor size of 3.8 cm. Of these, 26 were solitary tumors ≥3.5 cm, while 18 tumors were <3.5 cm but were either enrolled prior to adjustment of enrollment criteria (n=5) or included based on aggregate size of ≥3.5 cm for multiple tumors in a single patient (n=13). Thirty-six tumors were treated in patients with CP ≥ B8 disease with a median tumor size of 2.1 cm. Median mean liver dose (MLD) was 6.6 Gy. Forty-one percent of patients were treated with 5 fractions. Initial planned dose, based on standard dosing, was reduced for 38% of patients after optimization of treatment utility. 68% of patients underwent mid-treatment adaptation with either omission or dose reduction of the final two treatments (Table 1). By CP class, 13/26 (50%) of CP-A patients, 17/22 (77%) of CP-B patients and 8/8 (100%) of CP-C patients underwent treatment adaptation. In patients for whom treatment was adapted at mid-treatment, omission of the final two fractions (87%) was more common than dose reduction (13%). Ten patients had portal venous thrombosis. The use of systemic therapy in these patients was at the discretion of the treating physicians. Of these 10 patients, 1 received systemic therapy prior to enrollment and 3 received systemic therapy at any point during follow-up. One patient received systemic therapy within 6 months of SBRT.

Toxicity

A total of 21% of patients experienced an increase in CP score of ≥ 2 within six months after completing treatment (Table 2). When assessed by baseline CP class, 15.4% of CP A, 31.8% of CP B, and 12.5% of CP C patients experienced an increase in CP score of ≥ 2.

Similarly, 26.8% of patients experienced worsening ALBI score by ≥ 0.5 points within 6 months of treatment. When assessed by baseline ALBI score, 26.7% of grade 1 patients, 28.1% of grade 2 patients and 22.2% of grade 3 patients experienced worsening of raw ALBI score by ≥ 0.5 points.

Ten patients (18.9%) experienced a grade 3 or higher toxicity judged by the investigators as possibly, probably, or definitely related to treatment. The most common grade 3+ toxicities were hepatic encephalopathy (4 patients, one of which was grade 5) and ascites (3 patients). No grade 3+ stomach, small bowel, colon or renal toxicities were observed.

While the median individual tumor size was 3.8 cm for CP-A5 to B7 patients and 2.1 cm for CP-B8+ patients, twenty-five patients (45%) had an individual tumor larger than 3.5 cm, accounting for 29/80 tumors (36%), with a median tumor size of 4.5 cm in this subset. Of these 29 tumors, 25 (86%) were in patients with baseline CP A5-B7 liver disease. Seven of the 25 patients with an individual tumor ≥ 3.5 cm (28%) experienced an increase in CP score ≥ 2 points within 6 months of treatment, compared to 5/31 (16%) of patients with tumors <3.5 cm. Similarly, eight patients (32%) with tumors ≥ 3.5 cm experienced an increase in ALBI of ≥ 0.5 points within six months, compared to 7/31 (22%) of patients with tumors < 3.5 cm (Table 2).

Of the 30 patients with prior liver directed therapy, 5 (17%) experienced a 2+ point increase in Child Pugh score within 6 months of SBRT, similar to the rate of 21% in the overall study population.

Local Control

The 1 and 2 year cumulative incidence of local progression (CILP), considering death as a competing risk, for all patients were 6.4% (95% CI [2.4 – 13.4]) and 6.4% (95% CI [2.4 – 13.4]), respectively (Figure 2). One-year CILP was 5.7% (95% CI [2.0 – 16.9]) in CP-A patients and 3.2% (95% CI [0.2 – 14.6]) in CP-B patients (Supplemental Figure 1). Among CP-C patients, no patients experienced local recurrence, though 10/12 experienced death within two years (Supplemental Figure 1).

The 1- and 2- year freedom from local progression were 91.6% (95% CI [82.0 – 100]) and 49.1% (95% CI [26.1 – 92.4]), respectively for tumors larger than 3.5 cm compared to 100% and 100%, respectively for smaller tumors (HR 4.96, p <0.001, 95% CI [2.06,12.0]; Supplemental Figure 2).

Comparison to Conventional SBRT

To compare the safety and efficacy of optimized, adapted SBRT to conventional liver SBRT we performed a propensity score analysis with overlap weighting to facilitate comparison of patients treated on our protocol with patients who underwent conventional liver SBRT at Princess Margaret Cancer Centre. Prior to overlap weighting, groups differed by age, race, and Child Pugh class, but not number of lesions, lesion size, or platelet count. (Supplemental Table 2). After weighting, groups were well balanced with respect to age, baseline CP score, number of active lesions, lesion size, target volume and baseline platelet levels, with all standardized mean differences <0.1 (Supplemental Figure 3). The standard SBRT cohort only included patients with CP A5-B8, so 17 trial patients with CP-B9 or greater disease were excluded from this analysis. We included 10 patients (17.9%) with portal vein invasion, a negative prognostic factor for survival, though no patients with portal vein invasion were present in the PMH group.

Patients undergoing optimized SBRT in this cohort received a median BED of 73.5 Gy compared to 64.9 Gy for those undergoing conventional SBRT. Local progression did not significantly differ between the two groups (HR 0.69, 95% CI [0.25–1.91], p = 0.48) (Figure 3). 1- and 2- year freedom from local progression was 98% and 61%, respectively, in tumors treated with optimized adapted SBRT compared to 88% and 70%, respectively, for patients treated with conventional SBRT. Overall survival did not differ based on treatment approach, with 1- and 2- year overall survival of 88% and 60%, respectively with optimized adapted SBRT vs. 82% and 73% for standard SBRT (HR 1.11, 95% CI [0.55–2.23], p = 0.79) (Figure 3), suggesting that the patient populations were probably reasonably similar. Comparison of treatment-related toxicity as defined by increase in CP score of ≥2 points revealed lower rates of toxicity with the use of optimized, adapted radiation therapy (OR 0.26, 95% CI [0.07 – 0.99], p = 0.048) (Figure 3C).

DISCUSSION

This prospective phase II clinical trial met its primary endpoint and suggests that, compared to standard SBRT, individualized optimized response adapted SBRT may decrease liver toxicity for patients with large tumors or poor liver function while maintaining tumor control.

Early work investigating the use of SBRT in localized HCC predominantly involved CP-A patients, with improvements in local control as treatment protocols have been refined over time. In contrast, data for treatment-related toxicity and efficacy in CP-B and C patients come from small prospective registries. For example, a recent analysis of 23 patients with Child Pugh scores of 7–10 undergoing SBRT to 35–40 Gy in 5 fractions revealed CP progression in 10/23 (43%) of patients at 6 months. Another prospective analysis of 29 patients with CP B and C disease undergoing SBRT for HCC to a mean dose of 34 Gy in 6 fractions demonstrated a 34% rate of CP progression of ≥2 points within a month of treatment. This rate is commensurate with a recent retrospective analysis of 146 patients undergoing SBRT to 50 Gy in 5 fractions or hypofractionated RT to 45 Gy in 18 fractions. Of the 51 patients with CP-B8-C disease, 18 (35%) experienced a ≥ 2 point worsening of CP score within 6 months of treatment. Our results compare favorably with these studies.

The concept of individualized, response adaptive SBRT for HCC has heretofore been based on maximizing delivered dose with a fixed probability of treatment-related toxicity. This trial prospectively assessed the feasibility of up-front and mid-treatment optimization of treatment utility by individualizing radiation dose to balance the likelihood of local control with the probability of toxicity. Leveraging this optimized, response-adapted treatment approach limited treatment-related toxicity, with 21% of patients experiencing a decline in Child Pugh score of ≥ 2 points within 6 months. This rate is in line with our prior retrospective studies and lower than previously reported rates in patients with impaired liver function. Additionally, local control was maintained with a 2-year cumulative incidence of local progression of 6.4% despite only 41% of patients receiving all 5 planned fractions.

Data from studies specifically assessing local control and treatment-related toxicity by tumor size are relatively sparse. While our prior work did not reveal tumor size as a predictor of local progression following SBRT, these studies did not emphasize enrollment of patients with large tumors. One recent study addressed this question by analyzing outcomes following SBRT for patients with tumors ≤ 4 cm, 4–10 cm and ≥10 cm in size. This study demonstrated reduction in 3-year local control from 98% for tumors ≤ 4 cm to 72% for tumors 4–10 cm and 82% for tumors >10 cm, with increased rates of transaminase elevation after SBRT in patients with larger tumors. In our study, although size criteria of tumor ≥3.5 cm for enrollment for CP-A5 to B7 patients was based on total diameter of involved lesions, 68% of patients in this group had at least one tumor larger than 3.5 cm. For the entire study, irrespective of Child Pugh class, 45% of patients had an individual tumor larger than 3.5 cm, with a median size of 4.5 cm and maximum size of 14.6 cm in this subgroup. These patients had a slightly higher toxicity rate of 28% within 6 months, compared to 16% for those with smaller tumors. Local control was significantly lower for patients with tumors ≥ 3.5 cm, highlighting the need for continued efforts to improve treatment for this subgroup.

Because this was a single-arm study, we sought to compare our toxicity and local control rates for patients enrolled on our study to patients treated at Princess Margaret Cancer Centre, a cancer center experienced in utilizing SBRT for HCC. This propensity weighted analysis revealed similar rates of local control between treatment approaches with less toxicity associated with optimized, adapted treatment. Patients with CP scores ≥B9 were included in our primary analysis but were not included in the propensity weighted analysis due to a lack of similar patients in the comparison cohort. Given the relatively low rates of toxicity in our primary analysis, it is possible that the magnitude of difference in toxicity compared to standard SBRT would be even greater if these patients were able to be included.

One limitation of our study is the low number of toxicity and progression events precluded statistical subgroup comparisons based on baseline Child Pugh score or lesion size in the primary analysis, though similar analyses have been performed previously. Another limitation is the lack of a randomized control arm to facilitate comparison to standard SBRT. This was partially addressed by using an overlap weighted analysis, which attempts to mimic attributes of a randomized controlled trial, for comparison with another large cohort of patients treated at a center of excellence in the treatment of HCC. While this approach accounts for known possible confounders, it is not able to account for unknown confounders and therefore should be interpreted with appropriate caution. Additionally, the patients on our study were enrolled from 2015–2021, while the patients in the comparison cohort underwent treatment from 2007–2017, raising the possibility of subtle treatment differences between these groups as techniques evolved over time.

Other approaches to limiting treatment-related toxicity are being investigated. For instance, proton therapy is being actively investigated with promising early results. Additionally, it is possible that a radioprotector such as amifostine, which increased liver tolerance during whole liver RT, provides meaningful radioprotection during SBRT. Additionally, we and others have found correlations between circulating inflammatory cytokines and radiation-induced liver injury. This opens the possibility that modulating the intrahepatic inflammatory state at the time of treatment could decrease treatment-related toxicity. With increasing efforts to optimize all aspects of radiation therapy to maximize treatment utility for each individual patient, the role of radiation therapy in the treatment of hepatocellular carcinoma continues to expand. Within this context, optimized, individualized adaptive SBRT appears to a safe and effective option for patients with diminished hepatic function classified as Child-Pugh B8 or higher, or those with less hepatic impairment but tumors larger than 3.5 cm. As with most interventions in this population, appropriate case selection is critical and highly individualized.
