# PSMA-based Therapies and Novel Therapies in Advanced Prostate Cancer: The Now and the Future.

## Abstract

The treatment landscape for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) is rapidly evolving with the advent of PSMA-targeted radioligand therapies (RLTs) and bispecific T-cell engagers (BiTEs). These novel approaches provide new hope for patients who have progressed on standard therapies. However, their full clinical potential will be realized only by addressing key challenges, including tumor heterogeneity, resistance mechanisms, immune-related toxicities, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Additionally, the optimal sequencing of these therapies at different stages of disease remains an open question. While most of these interventions are currently introduced in late-stage, heavily pretreated patients, ongoing clinical trials are exploring their role in earlier disease settings, where they may be more effective in altering the natural history of disease. PSMA-based RLTs, such as 177Lu-PSMA- 617, have demonstrated promising efficacy, particularly in patients with high PSMA expression. However, the presence of PSMA-negative or heterogeneous tumors necessitates the development of additional biomarkers and combination strategies. The ongoing PSMAddition trial may establish RLTs as an earlier-line treatment in hormone-sensitive metastatic prostate cancer, potentially shifting the standard of care. Moreover, mitigating toxicities through radioprotective agents may aid in expanding their clinical utility. BiTE therapies offer a different but complementary mechanism of action, leveraging T-cell engagement to drive tumor cell destruction. While cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immunogenicity remain significant hurdles, modifications such as low-affinity CD3 binding and optimized dosing regimens are showing promise. The potential synergy of BiTEs with immune checkpoint inhibitors and tumor microenvironment-modulating agents should be further explored to enhance therapeutic efficacy. Given these advancements, the future of mCRPC treatment likely lies in a personalized, multimodal approach that integrates PSMA-based RLTs, BiTEs, and complementary therapies at earlier disease stages. Strategic biomarker-driven patient selection and combination regimens will be essential in optimizing outcomes while minimizing resistance and toxicity.

PSMA-based Therapies and Novel Therapies in Advanced Prostate Cancer: The Now and the Future

Opinion Statement

The treatment landscape for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC) is rapidly evolving with the advent of PSMA-targeted radioligand therapies (RLTs) and bispecific T-cell engagers (BiTEs). These novel approaches provide new hope for patients who have progressed on standard therapies. However, their full clinical potential will be realized only by addressing key challenges, including tumor heterogeneity, resistance mechanisms, immune-related toxicities, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Additionally, the optimal sequencing of these therapies at different stages of disease remains an open question. While most of these interventions are currently introduced in late-stage, heavily pretreated patients, ongoing clinical trials are exploring their role in earlier disease settings, where they may be more effective in altering the natural history of disease. PSMA-based RLTs, such as 177Lu-PSMA- 617, have demonstrated promising efficacy, particularly in patients with high PSMA expression. However, the presence of PSMA-negative or heterogeneous tumors necessitates the development of additional biomarkers and combination strategies. The ongoing PSMAddition trial may establish RLTs as an earlier-line treatment in hormone-sensitive metastatic prostate cancer, potentially shifting the standard of care. Moreover, mitigating toxicities through radioprotective agents may aid in expanding their clinical utility. BiTE therapies offer a different but complementary mechanism of action, leveraging T-cell engagement to drive tumor cell destruction. While cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immunogenicity remain significant hurdles, modifications such as low-affinity CD3 binding and optimized dosing regimens are showing promise. The potential synergy of BiTEs with immune checkpoint inhibitors and tumor microenvironment-modulating agents should be further explored to enhance therapeutic efficacy. Given these advancements, the future of mCRPC treatment likely lies in a personalized, multimodal approach that integrates PSMA-based RLTs, BiTEs, and complementary therapies at earlier disease stages. Strategic biomarker-driven patient selection and combination regimens will be essential in optimizing outcomes while minimizing resistance and toxicity.

Introduction

Prostate cancer is one of the most frequently diagnosed malignancies in men and remains a significant cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide. With an estimated 1.4 million new cases annually, prostate cancer presents a considerable burden on global healthcare systems. In its early stages, prostate cancer is often localized and highly treatable, with curative options such as radical prostatectomy or radiation therapy offering excellent outcomes. The treatment landscape for advanced prostate cancer has undergone significant evolution over the past decade. Current treatment strategies still rely on a backbone of androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) with either gonadotrophin-releasing hormone (GnRH) agonists or antagonists. Androgen receptor pathway inhibitors (ARPIs) such as enzalutamide, apalutamide, and darolutamide, and the CYP17 inhibitor abiraterone acetate, as well as docetaxel chemotherapy, are commonly utilized in the frontline metastatic hormone sensitive setting prior to the development of castration resistance. However, the disease becomes increasingly challenging to manage when patients progress to metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC). Despite advances in systemic therapies, mCRPC continues to be associated with poor survival outcomes, with median survival rates hovering around 27 months after progression on standard treatments. As such, novel treatments beyond ARPIs and taxane chemotherapy are urgently needed, and substantial progress has been achieved in this regard over the last several years.

Prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA), a transmembrane glycoprotein highly expressed in prostate cancer cells and minimally present in normal tissues, has emerged as an attractive target for therapeutic innovation. The specificity of PSMA has enabled the development of targeted therapies, including radioligand therapy (RLT) and antibody-based therapies. Early successes with PSMA-based therapies, such as Lutetium- 177 PSMA- 617, have demonstrated significant clinical benefits, improving survival and quality of life in patients with advanced disease. However, challenges such as variability in PSMA expression, treatment resistance, and adverse effects like xerostomia and hematological toxicity remain barriers to broader application.

While immune checkpoint inhibitors have achieved success in other malignancies, their efficacy in prostate cancer has been limited due to the disease’s immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. This is characterized by low T-cell infiltration, an abundance of suppressive cells such as regulatory T-cells and myeloid-derived suppressor cells, low tumor mutational burden, and metabolic and hormonal factors that impair immune activity. Moreover, prostate cancer has a predilection for boney metastasis as compared to soft tissue spread, and this has been postulated as another barrier to immunotherapy response. This is at least in part due to osteoclast-mediated resorption of bone tumors and subsequent release of TGF-β, which then impairs T Helper 1 cells and prevents a robust immune mediated effect. However, parallel to the development of PSMA-targeted therapies, novel strategies to successfully engage the immune system are now emerging as promising treatment modalities for prostate cancer. Bispecific T-cell engagers (BiTEs), which direct T-cells to attack PSMA-expressing tumor cells, are providing one such an alternative immunotherapeutic approach. Early investigations into BiTE molecules, including AMG- 160 and pasotuxizumab, have shown the potential to induce antitumor responses in mCRPC. However, immune-related toxicities such as cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and challenges in maintaining effective T-cell activity may pose limitations to their ultimate widespread adoption.

Further new developments in PSMA-based therapies and BiTE constructs are underway to address limitations with previously developed compounds. Other PSMA-based therapeutics are exploring alternative pathways, enhanced delivery mechanisms, and combination strategies with other systemic therapies, such as ARPIs and immune modulators to improve their therapeutic index. For example, novel RLTs such as Actinium- 225 PSMA- 617 are being tested to overcome resistance to Lutetium-based therapies. In the realm of BiTE therapies, next-generation constructs are being engineered to enhance T-cell persistence, reduce CRS, and target additional antigens, such as the six-transmembrane epithelial antigen of the prostate 1 (STEAP1). These innovations aim to expand the applicability of BiTEs to a broader range of prostate cancer patients, particularly those with low or heterogeneous PSMA expression.

This review will explore the latest advancements in PSMA-based and BiTE therapies, focusing on recent clinical trials, emerging preclinical data, and their potential integration into existing treatment paradigms. By examining these novel approaches, this article seeks to provide insights into the evolving strategies to improve outcomes for patients with advanced prostate cancer.

PSMA-Based Therapies

PSMA is a transmembrane glycoprotein highly expressed in prostate cancer cells, with limited expression in normal tissues, making it an ideal candidate for precision-targeted therapies. Its extracellular domain enables ligand binding, followed by receptor-mediated endocytosis, which facilitates the internalization of therapeutic agents into cancer cells. The molecular properties of PSMA, particularly its high specificity and internalization capacity, have been harnessed for targeted delivery of RLTs and antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs), allowing for more specialized therapy in mCRPC. Radioligand therapy combines PSMA-specific small molecules or antibodies with either beta particles (such as lutetium- 177) or alpha particles (such as actinium- 225) to promote cell death via targeted DNA strand breakage. Antibodies allow for longer circulation time and marrow exposure, but decreased ability to reach salivary glands, intestine, and kidney due to their luminal expression, while small molecules diffuse to all sites of PSMA expression prior to being excreted.

Lutetium- 177 PSMA- 617 (177Lu-PSMA- 617) is a novel beta-emitting small molecule radioligand therapy that has demonstrated potential in the treatment of mCRPC. The VISION trial, a landmark phase III study, evaluated 177Lu-PSMA- 617 in patients with PSMA-positive mCRPC who had exhausted conventional therapies. Patients receiving 177Lu-PSMA- 617 alongside standard care experienced significant improvements in overall survival (OS; median 15.3 months vs. 11.3 months; hazard ratio [HR]: 0.62, P < 0.001) and radiographic progression-free survival (rPFS; median 8.7 months vs. 3.4 months; HR: 0.40, P < 0.001) compared to standard of care (SOC) alone. Of note, cabazitaxel was not allowed in the SOC arm. These promising findings led to the U.S. FDA approving Lutetium- 177 PSMA- 617 under the name Pluvicto in March 2022. This is the first and only agent of its class currently on the market and at this time, the label indication is for patients who have already been treated with ARPIs and taxane-based chemotherapy. Subsequent studies have further evaluated health-related quality of life and pain outcomes using 177Lu-PSMA- 617 and found that these metrics were both improved as compared to treatment with SOC, highlighting the importance of this new treatment in a more patient-centered lens. A subsequent safety analysis demonstrated that when adjusting for treatment exposure, the overall treatment-emergent adverse event (TEAE) rate was similar between the experimental and control arms, and that longer exposure to 177Lu-PSMA- 617 with 6 vs 4 cycles did not increase overall toxicity, further confirming a favorable risk–benefit profile.

Building on the success of VISION, the ongoing PSMAfore trial investigated 177Lu-PSMA- 617 as an earlier-line therapy before chemotherapy. This phase 3 randomized controlled trial evaluates the efficacy and safety of the of 177Lu-PSMA- 617 in taxane-naïve mCRPC patients who had disease progression on an ARPI as compared to changing to an alternate ARPI. The findings in the third data cutoff point thus far showed that those in the experimental group exhibited a statistically significant improvement in rPFS compared to the ARPI change group (median 11.6 months vs 5.59 months; HR:0.49). Preliminary safety analysis indicated that 177Lu-PSMA- 617 was generally well-tolerated with lower incidence of grade 3–5 adverse events as compared to control group, while OS data is still not fully mature. There has been concern pertaining to the trial designs of both VISION and PSMAfore: bias favoring the experimental arms, control arm treatments not reflecting accurate SOC, and stringent inclusion criteria. In addition, the requirement for PSMA-positivity on imaging could limit generalizability of trial results to the broader mCRPC patient population.

The potential for synergy between PSMA-based therapies and systemic treatments is also being actively explored. A phase 1 trial investigated the safety and efficacy of a single dose of 177Lu-PSMA- 617 followed by pembrolizumab in mCRPC patients who had disease progression on one or more ARPI, and at least three PSMA-avid lesions on PSMA PET scan. Among the 25 patients evaluated on this regimen, 14 (56%; 95 CI% 35–76) achieved confirmed objective response rate (ORR) with either a measurable decrease in tumor size or complete disappearance of tumors, as defined by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) guidelines. Secondarily, this combination had a favorable safety profile and demonstrated that this combination of treatments could lead to improved response outcomes in the mCRPC setting. The Enza-P trial was a phase 2 study evaluating the addition of 177Lu-PSMA- 617 to enzalutamide in mCRPC, demonstrating feasibility of this combination with higher rates of PSA response in the experimental arm as compared to control.

PSMA-I&T is another small molecule now used in combination with Lutetium- 177 to deliver RLT. The ECLIPSE trial is a Phase III clinical study evaluating the safety and efficacy of 177Lu-PSMA-I&T versus hormone therapy in patients with mCRPC. Thus far, it has been released that the study’s primary endpoint of statistically significant and clinically meaningful improvement in median rPFS has been met. Similarly, the SPLASH trial is a phase III study evaluating the efficacy of 177Lu-PSMA-I&T in PSMA-positive mCRPC following progression on an ARPI. Early results are showing that at a median follow-up time of 11.1 months for the RLT arm, and 12.9 months for the control ARPI arm, the median rPFS was 9.5 months (95% CI, 7.4–10.0) for 177Lu-PSMA-I&T compared with 6.0 months (95% CI, 4.7–7.9) in the alternate ARPI arm (HR, 0.71; 95% CI, 0.55–0.92; P = 0.0088). While Lutetium is a beta-emitting radioligand, optimizing alpha-emitting therapies has also been of significant interest in the field, due to their ability to deliver higher energy over shorter distances, thus minimizing damage to surrounding healthy tissues. Actinium- 225 (225 Ac-PSMA- 617) is one such alpha-emitting radioligand that has also been studied in the mCRPC arena. In a retrospective review study of 53 patients with mCRPC treated with ADT, subsequent treatment with 225 Ac-PSMA- 617 showed that 91% of participants had at least 50% decline in PSA (PSA50), with 96% of patients having any decline in PSA. In a larger international retrospective study, data was pooled among 488 mCRPC patients treated with 225 Ac-PSMA after previously being treated with ARPIs, chemotherapy, and Lutetium- 177. The median OS was 15.5 months (95% CI 13.4–18.3) and median PFS was 7.9 months, (6.8–8.9 mo) with xerostomia reported as a common treatment related side-effect. These studies suggest that alpha-emitting therapies might offer clinically meaningful benefits even for those who have progressed through other therapeutic options.

ADCs are an important emerging therapeutic class in the treatment of mCRPC, with multiple novel agents currently under investigation. These agents are designed to deliver cytotoxic therapies directly to cancer cells by targeting specific antigens including PSMA, STEAP1, TROP2, CD46, and B7-H3. PSMA has been the most explored target in this emerging drug class. ARX- 517 is a PSMA-targeted ADC with a tubulin inhibitor payload, amberstatin- 269, which has shown potent antitumor activity in the early phase APEX- 01 trial. Preliminary efficacy data (n = 24) indicated ARX- 517 led to significant PSA50 response in a dose-dependent manner. No dose limiting toxicities (DLT) or serious adverse events (SAE) were reported at any of the dosing levels to date, showing a highly favorable safety profile. 225 Ac-J591 is a novel alpha-emitting RLT conjugated to an anti-PSMA monoclonal antibody currently being explored. In a phase 1 study of 32 patients this therapy resulted in a 46.9% PSA50 response at any time in the trial with most-high grade adverse events being hematologic in nature and occurring in higher radioactivity dosages. Combining alpha and beta RLTs has been a more recent area of interest, aiming to exploit the complementary mechanisms of each mode of radiation. Early data from a phase 1 dose-escalation study of 18 patients with refractory mCRPC, combined 225 Ac-J591 and 177Lu-PSMA-I&T has shown to be a feasible treatment modality with about half of patients demonstrating a PSA50 response. While further efficacy testing is ongoing, this combined approach leverages the precision of ADCs to deliver targeted therapy while combined alpha and beta RLTs allow for a higher therapeutic index. Furthermore, this combination potentially reduces the cumulative toxicity associated with higher doses of each radionucleotide alone.

Bispecific T-Cell Engager (BiTE) Therapies

Bispecific T cell engager (BiTE) therapies are another emerging class of therapies in the mCRPC arena. BiTEs are engineered proteins designed to direct cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) towards tumor cells in a highly specific and targeted manner. By simultaneously binding to a tumor-associated antigen (TAA) on cancer cells and CD3 on T cells, BiTEs force an immunological synapse that facilitates T-cell activation, directed release of cytotoxic granules and cytokines, and ultimately targeted tumor cell lysis. In prostate cancer, PSMA is the most frequently targeted TAA due to its abundant expression on malignant cells, but other targets are currently being explored as well.

The first use of BiTE therapies in prostate cancer was pastoxizumab, an anti-CD3 and anti-PSMA protein. In subcutaneous administrations of this agent, patients developed treatment-emergent anti-drug antibodies (TE-ADAs), which led to significantly decreased levels of drug exposure and reversal of initially promising PSA response. To mitigate these issues, the route was switched to continuous IV infusion, which yielded PSA response in a dose dependent manner with a 54.9% reduction in the highest dose cohort. While ADAs did not develop in this mode of administration, the need for continuous infusion to maintain therapeutic drug levels was challenging and limited real-world feasibility, ultimately leading to early discontinuation. To overcome the need for continuous infusion, newer-generation BiTEs focused on improving pharmacokinetics for easier administration. Acapatamab, an alternate anti-CD3, anti-PSMA molecule achieved longer serum half-life due to being bound by an IgG crystallizable fragment. In a recent phase I study 133 patients received acapatamab at various dosing levels with preliminary efficacy data showing a 30.4% PSA50 response and rPFS of 3.7 months (95% CI:2.0–5.4). The most common TE-AE event was CRS, which occurred in 97.4% of patients in dose exploration and 98.2% in dose expansion, with grade ≥ 3 CRS observed in 23.4% and 16.1% of patients, respectively. Additionally, TE-ADAs were detected in 55% of patients, impacting serum exposure in 36% of patients in the dose expansion cohort. Due to the limitations in durable antitumor activity and significant immunogenicity, acapatamab development was discontinued. JNJ- 081, an alternate BiTE targeting PSMA, was studied in a 2023 phase 1 study of 39 patients and showed a PSA50 response of 7.7%. Every patient in the study experienced at least one TE-AE, with 19 patients developing TE-ADAs ultimately resulting in early discontinuation of the trial. The latest PSMA-targeting BiTE therapy to withdraw from further clinical research is LAVA- 1207, a gamma delta engager that was being evaluated in refractory mCRPC in a phase 1/2a study. Recently the drug manufacturers revealed that they will discontinue this study due to preliminary data points not reaching internal benchmarks. Of note, they commented that this decision was not secondary to safety concerns. JANX0007, a Tumor Activated T Cell Engager (TRACTr), remains as a promising PSMA-targeted therapy based on preliminary efficacy and safety data. This bispecific drug candidate employs a masked T cell-specific binding domain to help minimize the risk of CRS and has an albumin-binding domain to address issues with half-life. Early data from the phase 1a ENGAGER-PSMA- 01 trial (NCT05519449) showed that in 16 enrolled patients with heavily pre-treated mCRPC, 100% achieved a PSA50 decline with this study drug. Even more impressive is that 63% of patients achieved a PSA decline of at least 90% (PSA90), and 31% of patients achieved a PSA decline of at least 99%. While safety data analysis is still ongoing, CRS and CRS-related adverse events were primarily limited to cycle 1 and were grades 1 and 2. Phase 1b expansion trials are currently underway.

Other proteins have been explored as potential targets for BiTE therapy with the hopes of offering therapeutic synergy when combined with PSMA-directed treatment. One such target that has made it to clinical trials is STEAP1. STEAP1 is a cell-surface protein overexpressed in prostate cancer cells, particularly in advanced disease. It plays a role in intracellular iron and copper metabolism but it dispensable for normal tissue function, making it a promising TAA. Xaluritamig (AMG 509) is a STEAP- 1 targeting BiTE that has been evaluated in a phase 1 study of mCRPC. The study enrolled 97 heavily pretreated patients and administered increasing dosages of study drug to determine PSA response, safety, and optimal drug dosing. A PSA50 response was observed in 49% of patients, and a PSA90 response was observed in 30%, supporting the need for further development. Regarding this drug candidate’s safety profile, CRS occurred in 72% of patients, primarily during cycle 1, with other less common side effects including myalgia, myofascitis, and maculopapular rash. CRS events improved with premedication and step dosing, and only 3% of patients ultimately discontinued due to this. Another potential target being exploited for BiTE therapies is delta-like ligand 3 (DLL3). This protein is highly expressed in neuroendocrine prostate cancer (NEPC), which emerges in the setting of treatment resistance to prior therapies. Tarlatamab (AMG 757) is a half-life extended BiTE targeting DLL3 currently being evaluated in a phase 1b study for its safety and efficacy in patients with NEPC. Inclusion criteria is patients with de novo or treatment-emergent NEPC who have had disease progression on at least 1 line of prior systemic treatment. The objective response rate in the overall cohort was 10.5%, with mPFS 1.9 months. Of the 75% of patients experiencing CRS, only 1 patient had a grade ≥ 3 event. A phase 1b study by Janssen is currently investigating the early effects of a novel bispecific antibody targeting kallikrein-related peptidase 2 (KLK2) and CD3, combined with taxane chemotherapy or ARPi (NCT05818683). KLK2 promotes prostate cancer cell growth by enhancing androgen receptor transactivation, a crucial step in progression to mCRPC. Targeting this protein has been shown to suppress tumor growth and induce apoptosis in malignant cells making it an increasingly attractive drug target.

Current Limitations and Future Perspectives

PSMA-based therapies and BiTEs are rapidly challenging and advancing the landscape of mCRPC, with multiple key clinical trials recently completed and ongoing (summarized in Tables 1 and 2). With how novel these therapeutic agents are, several challenges have been identified with potential solutions already underway. While PSMA expression is strongly correlated with extent of disease, this varies significantly among patients and even within tumor sites in the same patient. This heterogeneity can lead to variable response rates to PSMA-targeted therapies and leave a treatment gap in patients with non-PSMA expressing prostate cancer, furthering the need for alternate biomarker investigation. These variations are also likely in part driving some of the resistance we are seeing in patients being treated with RLTs such as 177Lu-PSMA. The VISION trial and subsequent studies have demonstrated that higher baseline PSMA PET SUVmean is associated with improved clinical outcomes in patients treated with Pluvicto, specifically correlating with longer rPFS and OS. Some patients exhibit primary resistance to PSMA-targeted therapies, while others develop resistance over time as can occur with transformation of prostate adenocarcinoma to NEPC. To overcome resistance, combination therapies with agents that target complementary pathways are being explored. Combining PSMA-targeted RLT with other available therapies such as ARPIs, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and targeted therapy can enhance PSMA expression and improve therapeutic outcomes. The PSMAddition trial is an active international phase III study evaluating the addition of 177Lu-PSMA- 617 to SOC (ADT + ARPI) treatment in either treatment-naïve or minimally treated metastatic hormone sensitive prostate cancer (mHSPC) patients. Approximately 1126 patients will be randomized with the primary endpoint being rPFS, and secondary endpoints including OS, safety profile, health related quality of life, and other patient-reported outcomes. As 177Lu-PSMA- 617 is already approved in the mCRPC setting, if results of this trial are positive it could lead to new regulatory submissions, label expansions, and potentially a change in the standard treatment paradigm for early-stage metastatic disease. Toxicity is also a significant factor in PSMA-based therapies with long-term safety data still evolving. The most common toxicities of RLT include xerostomia, myelosuppression, fatigue, nausea, and pain. Radioprotective compounds such as amifostine are being investigated as tools to protect non-target tissues from radiation damage. Future studies are focusing on optimizing dosing regimens to balance efficacy with toxicity, and better stratify patients based on PSMA expression to identify those that are most likely to benefit from therapy.

Some early challenges identified by introducing BiTE therapies in mCRPC patients include high rates of CRS, immunogenicity, and overcoming the tumor microenvironment of this disease. CRS in BiTEs is primarily driven by the activation of T cells and subsequent release of pro-inflammatory cytokines including IL- 6, TNF-α, and IFN-γ. Activation of these cytokines can rapidly recruit additional immune cells including monocytes and macrophages, which further amplify cytokine production. High-affinity binding of the CD3 component in BiTEs can exacerbate this response, leading to severe CRS and early discontinuation of multiple clinical trials. One proposed mechanism to bypass this AE is by reducing the affinity of the CD3-binding domain to decrease T-cell activation, thereby mitigating CRS while maintaining antitumor efficacy. TNB- 585, a PSMAxCD3 BiTE incorporated a low-affinity anti-CD3 arm and demonstrated reduced cytokine release while effectively killing prostate tumor cells in an in vitro model. In a Phase 1 trial of AMG 340, a PSMA-targeting BiTE with a low-affinity CD3 binding domain, there was insufficient clinical activity to warrant further exploration, however the CRS toxicity observed was lower grade and more manageable as compared to standard-affinity CD3 binding agents. Immunogenicity with the development of TE-ADAs can neutralize the therapeutic effects of BiTEs, curtailing their efficacy over time. While switching from subcutaneous to continuous intravenous administration has been shown to reduce ADA formation, this mechanism of delivery is generally not clinically feasible. In addition to combining therapies and optimizing dosing regimens to help mitigate the immune response, stratifying patients based on biomarkers and immune profiles may identify patients less likely to develop ADAs. The tumor microenvironment (TME) is another obstacle that impacts the efficacy of BiTE therapy in prostate cancer. The TME is often characterized by various immunosuppressive elements such as TGF-β, IL- 10, and PD-L1, which can inhibit T-cell activation and function. These factors can reduce the cytotoxic activity of BiTE-engaged T cells, leading to diminished therapeutic efficacy. Prostate cancer is typically thought to be an immunologically “cold” malignancy with limited T-cell infiltration, creating a suboptimal pool of effector cells for BiTEs to engage with. Future directions to overcome these challenges include combining BiTEs with immune checkpoint inhibitors to enhance T-cell activity and counteract immunosuppressive signals within the TME. Additionally, strategies to increase T-cell infiltration into the tumor, such as using chemokines, can provide a larger pool of effector cells for BiTE engagement.

Another developing therapeutic class being investigated parallel to BiTEs are the tri-specific T-cell engagers (TriTEs). Like BiTEs these molecules target a TAA and the CD3 receptor on T cells, but they incorporate a third specificity often directed at a co-stimulatory molecule or an additional tumor antigen. This third binding domain can be used for dual targeting of TAA, dual targeting of T-cell receptors, or fusion to human serum albumin to extend half-life and allow for less frequent dosing. TriTEs in theory can induce a stronger, more sustained T-cell response and potentially improve the depth and duration of tumor cell killing. There have been two-tri specific T-cell engagers investigated in prostate cancer, one targeting PSMA (HPN424), the other DLL3 (HPN328). These agents demonstrated tolerable safety profiles and show early signs of promising efficacy. HPN424 has been discontinued after a phase 1 clinical trial, while HPN328 is underway in a phase 1/2 study of patients with DLL3 expression including those with refractory small cell lung cancer and NEPC.

At present most PSMA-based therapies and BiTEs are being investigated as treatment options for patients who have progressed on multiple other lines of therapy. Thought should be given into introducing these therapies in earlier stages of disease to improve patient performance status and potentially alter the TME while it is relatively more malleable. Currently there is a Phase 1b study evaluating the use of Xaluritamig in patients with newly diagnosed localized prostate cancer (NCT06613100). Combining PSMA-based therapies and BiTEs with other drugs exploiting complementary mechanisms of action is also a rapidly emerging area of interest with multiple studies looking into simultaneous ARPI and/or immunotherapy administration. Lastly, efforts should continue to focus on identifying additional non-PSMA biomarkers by implementing tumor molecular sequencing and circulating tumor DNA analysis. These findings can also be extrapolated to stratify which patients may be more likely to respond to certain therapies.

Conclusion

The treatment of advanced prostate cancer is undergoing a paradigm shift thanks to the emergence of precision-targeted PSMA-based therapies and immunotherapeutic strategies such as BiTEs. Radioligand therapies have demonstrated clinically meaningful survival benefits, spurring ongoing clinical trials that aim to refine dosing, reduce toxicities, and broaden eligibility criteria. PSMA-targeted antibody–drug conjugates and dual alpha- and beta-emitting regimens are similarly enhancing therapeutic selectivity and depth of response. In parallel, BiTE therapies—including next-generation constructs targeting additional antigens like STEAP1—are showing encouraging, albeit early, clinical efficacy. Yet, these novel agents face significant hurdles: tumor heterogeneity, immunogenicity, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment can limit efficacy and contribute to therapy resistance. Additionally, common adverse events highlight the ongoing need for more refined dosing strategies and mitigation protocols. Despite these challenges, the momentum in clinical trials and preclinical innovation is astounding. The future is likely to bring increasingly personalized therapeutic regimens, guided by biomarker-driven patient selection and adaptive treatment algorithms. Through continued collaborative research, meticulous trial design, and a commitment to addressing safety and patient quality of life, these next-generation PSMA-based and BiTE immunotherapies have the potential to redefine outcomes for patients with advanced prostate cancer.
