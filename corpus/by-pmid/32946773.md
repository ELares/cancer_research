# Challenges and Opportunities for Pancreatic Cancer Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDA) is among the most immune-resistant tumor types. Its unique genomic landscape shaped by oncogenic drivers promotes immune suppression from the earliest stages of tumor inception to subvert adaptive T cell immunity. Single-agent immune modulators have thus far proven clinically ineffective, and multi-modal therapies targeting mechanisms of immunotherapy resistance are likely needed. Here, we review novel immunotherapy strategies currently under investigation to (1) confer antigen specificity, (2) enhance T cell effector function, and (3) neutralize immunosuppressive elements within the tumor microenvironment that may be rationally combined to untangle the web of immune resistance in PDA and other tumors.

Challenges and Opportunities for Pancreatic Cancer Immunotherapy

Summary

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDA) is among the most immune-resistant tumor types. Its unique genomic landscape shaped by oncogenic drivers promotes immune suppression from the earliest stages of tumor inception to subvert adaptive T-cell immunity. Single-agent immune modulators have thus far proven clinically ineffective, and multi-modal therapies targeting mechanisms of immunotherapy resistance are likely needed. Herein, we review novel immunotherapy strategies currently under investigation to (1) confer antigen specificity, (2) enhance T-cell effector function and (3) neutralize immunosuppressive elements within the tumor microenvironment that may be rationally combined to untangle the web of immune resistance in PDA and other tumors.

Introduction

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDA) remains a significant clinical challenge. With a persistently increasing incidence and minimal change in mortality rates, PDA will become the 2nd leading cause of cancer-related mortality by 2030. Surgery and adjuvant chemotherapy can achieve long-term survival but only for a minority of patients with resectable disease. Combination chemotherapy developed over the past decade has provided an important option for patients with advanced and metastatic disease – enabling short-lived partial remissions or stable disease in newly diagnosed patients, but nearly all patients eventually relapse and second-line options are poor. Recent revelations deciphering PDA genomic and immune landscapes have fostered the development of targeted therapies, but only for small subsets of PDA patients. Ultimately, the 5-year overall survival (OS) of patients with PDA is only 9% in the United States, highlighting the need for alternative therapeutic options.

With the exception of the <1% of patients with microsatellite instability high (MSI-H) tumors, PDA is almost entirely refractory to FDA-approved immunotherapies that have transformed the outlook of advanced patients with solid tumors including melanoma and lung cancer among others. The near universal refractoriness of PDA to immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) is essentially unique across human cancer histologies. Classically, PDA exhibits an immunologically “cold” tumor microenvironment (TME) characterized by a prominent myeloid cell infiltration typically devoid of CD8+ T cells (Teffector) and low activation marker (e.g. GZMB and IFNG) expression – hallmarks of absent or dysfunctional adaptive T-cell immunity and refractoriness to checkpoint blockade. Tumor-intrinsic and -extrinsic factors, such as a low tumor mutational burden and a complex, immunosuppressive TME that thwarts T-cell priming, trafficking and function, further aggravate adaptive immunity in PDA. As such, PDA conforms to a model of both primary and adaptive immunotherapy resistance whereby cancer is unrecognized by or protected from adaptive immunity.

Given that immune monotherapies in microsatellite-stable PDA have little clinical activity, contemporary efforts to develop immunotherapy for PDA involve multi-modal immune modulatory strategies to (1) enhance endogenous T-cell function, (2) prime or adoptively transfer tumor-specific T-cell immunity, and (3) transform the TME from immunologically “cold” to “hot”. Early strategies seeking to combine standard chemotherapy with blocking antibodies to immune checkpoints, an approach with activity in both lung and breast cancer, have exhibited minimal activity to date in PDA. Dual checkpoint blockade using αCTLA-4 and αPD-L1 to target non-redundant pathways of T-cell inhibition have been similarly disappointing. Personalized immunotherapy strategies for individual patients or subgroups of patients based on PDA genotypic and phenotypic heterogeneity may provide novel insights. Herein, we will review promising immunotherapy strategies under investigation for the treatment of PDA and discuss how beyond checkpoint inhibition they may be rationally combined to reach an immunologic tipping point.

Tumor-intrinsic Mechanisms of Immune Resistance

PDA immunotherapy resistance is driven by its unique genetic landscape. Genetically engineered mouse models of PDA have greatly enhanced our understanding of PDA tumorigenesis and aided preclinical immunotherapy drug discovery. The KC (KRasLSL-G12D/+,Pdx1-Cre) and KPC (KRasLSL-G12D/+,Pdx1-Cre) mouse models have defined the pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PanIN) progression model of PDA neogenesis with PanIN formation initiated by mutant KRAS (mKRAS) and progression to invasive cancer following tumor suppressor gene inactivation. Exome studies of human PDA have validated near ubiquitous expression of activating KRAS mutations and inactivation of TP53, CDKN2A and/or SMAD4 in >50% of cases. Allelic imbalance leading to increased mKRAS gene dosage may be a key determinant of PDA progression and metastasis. Despite a common oncogenic pathway, PDA is a genetically heterogeneous disease with diverse clinical phenotypes. Genetic and epigenetic changes confer distinct molecular, cellular and clinical features that determine intertumoral heterogeneity which may be characterized by transcriptional or whole genome profiling.

Downstream signaling of mKRAS orchestrates a network of immunosuppression (Figure 1). Beyond its classical oncogenic role, there is growing evidence that mKRAS drives the establishment of immunosuppression within the TME. mKRAS directly prevents innate and adaptive anti-tumor immunity by enhancing autophagocytosis to modulate levels of cell surface MHC class I (MHC-I) and by regulating the expression of CD47 and PD-L1. Additionally, mKRAS coordinates a paracrine network to establish a TME composed of suppressive immune cells, activated stromal cells and desmoplasia. TME evolution precedes invasive cancer formation. In KC mice, PanIN lesions are infiltrated by immunosuppressive tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and regulatory T cells (Treg). In KPC mice, type I conventional dendritic cells (cDC1) important for Teffector priming become progressively decreased and dysregulated as PanINs evolve to invasive tumors, correlating with low levels of infiltrating DCs in human PDA samples. mKRAS drives tumor-intrinsic GM-CSF and CXCL1 expression promoting TME MDSC infiltration and T-cell exclusion. Furthermore, downregulated CCL4 expression via mKRAS-mediated WNT/β-catenin activation impedes DC recruitment. mKRAS also promotes Sonic Hedgehog signaling and the expression of COX2, IL-6, pSTAT3 and MMP7 leading to chronic inflammation and expansion of the fibroinflammatory stroma.

Intriguingly, targeting mKRAS signaling may promote anti-tumor immune responses. The pharmacologic inhibition of KRAS G12C in an engineered CT-26 mouse tumor model increases tumor cell MHC-I expression and promotes Teffector and cDC1 TME infiltration rendering tumors sensitive to immune modulation. KRAS G12C mutations are rare in PDA, and no targeted therapies exist for other, more prevalent mKRAS variants. However, alternative mKRAS-directed strategies, including engineered exosomes and mKRAS-specific T cells, have demonstrated promising results and may prove beneficial for PDA patients.

Primary Resistance to Immune Checkpoint Blockade in PDA

ICB therapy targeting CTLA-4 or PD-1/PD-L1, administered as monotherapy or in combination, has limited activity in advanced PDA. ICB therapeutic efficacy is felt to be contingent upon pre-existing T-cell immunity suppressed by immune checkpoints. Clinical responses to αPD-1/PD-L1 in melanoma and other tumors correlate with tumor mutational burden and the expression of CD8, PD-1 and PD-L1 as well as TCR clonality within the TME. This constellation of biomarkers implicate T-cell recognition of mutation-induced cancer neoantigens as a final common pathway of ICB activity. Recent evidence also suggests a role of innate immune cell activation within the TME in promoting ICB sensitivity. The lack of robust pre-existing T-cell immunity appears to underlie the ineffectiveness of ICB therapy in PDA. Naturally occurring T-cell responses are limited in treatment naïve PDA patients as evidenced by a lack of activated Teffector and TCR clonality in tumor samples. Factors that limit naturally occurring T-cell immunity in PDA include low MHC-I expression, low-moderate mutational burden limiting antigenic targets and TME-mediated suppression of T-cell priming and function.

Importantly, there exists a subset of PDA patients exhibiting moderate Teffector infiltration that correlates with cytolytic indices and OS suggesting pre-existing anti-tumor T-cell immunity. Additionally, clonal Teffector populations have been identified within tertiary lymphoid structures and positively correlate with clinical outcomes. Nevertheless, ICB therapy alone remains largely ineffective, presumably even for these patients. Responses to αPD-1 have been observed in the small subset (<1%) of PDA patients with hypermutated MSI-H tumors who demonstrate neoantigen-specific T-cell responses, but even in this setting, responses are not universal. Combinatorial strategies to confer antigen specificity, enhance T-cell function and enable tumor recognition – together with TME modulation – may sensitize PDA to ICB therapy (Figure 1). Indeed, non-redundant immune modulatory agents such as autophagy inhibition to restore tumor cell MHC-I expression or agonistic CD40 mAb (αCD40) to prime tumor-specific T-cell immunity enhance responses to ICB therapy in preclinical models.

Low Antigenic Strength in PDA

In gene-engineered mouse models of PDA, it is clear that tumor “antigenic strength” dictates immune reactivity, and the strength of immune responses to tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) may be limited due to central tolerance to self-proteins. The natural history of spontaneous tumor formation in the KPC model is not impacted by the presence or absence of T cells. In the absence of strong antigens, the salient features of immunosurveillance and immune editing are not observed in the KPC model. Tumor cell engineering to express a strong (foreign) antigen restores immunity and leads to immunoediting and escape, even without the use of ICB. The overall impression of PDA as modeled in KPC mice is that of immunological ignorance or privilege.

Non-synonymous gene mutations arise during tumorigenesis producing neoantigens uniquely expressed by tumor cells. Human PDA tumors express a moderate burden of non-synonymous neoantigenic mutations, and neoantigen quality metrics that predict immunogenicity have emerged and correlate with patient survival. Neoantigen-targeted strategies are generally limited by the fact that neoantigens are rarely shared among patients and require personalized treatment approaches which may be laborious and costly.

mKRAS as a Tumor Antigen

Ideal antigenic targets would be those exclusively expressed by tumor cells and critical to tumor pathogenesis. In PDA, somatic mutations within driver oncogenes fulfill each of these criteria. Non-synonymous mutations within KRAS occur in >90% of human PDA and almost exclusively involve the G12 codon position. TP53 inactivation occurs in ~70% of PDA cases, and recurrent non-synonymous mutations within the DNA-binding domain constitute 25–30% of these cases. Mutant KRAS and p53 have long been explored as cancer vaccine targets. Although T-cell responses to mKRAS and p53 may be induced in select patients, most studies are underpowered to demonstrate clinical benefit and fail to clearly define HLA-restricted immunogenic epitopes. Recent technological advances have facilitated the identification of MHC-I and MHC-II-restricted epitopes of mKRAS and p53 in cancer patients, and mKRAS-specific tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL) demonstrate anti-tumor activity in a patient with metastatic colorectal cancer validating oncogenic driver mutations as potential immunotherapeutic targets. Studies in HLA transgenic mouse models suggest immune targeting of mKRAS may be applicable for patients with select HLA types; however, a better understanding of mKRAS antigen processing and presentation as well as biochemical epitope-HLA binding properties is needed to advance broad clinical application of this approach.

Priming PDA-Specific Immunity

Chemotherapy, Radiation and Activation of Innate Immunity

Innate immune cells express pattern recognition receptors including toll-like receptors (TLRs) and cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) that detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) resulting in the expression of inflammatory cytokines and type I interferons (IFN) that activate adaptive anti-tumor immunity. Evidence suggests chemotherapy and radiation sensitize tumors to ICB therapy via the liberation of PAMPs and DAMPs that promote TLR- and stimulator of Interferon genes (STING)-dependent DC activation. In particular, activation of the cGAS-STING pathway by cytosolic tumor-derived DNA is critical to the priming of anti-tumor Teffector cells and the efficacy of ICB therapy in preclinical tumor models. Although clinical data are lacking, preclinical strategies to activate the cGAS-STING-IFN axis as an adjuvant to chemotherapy, radiation and immunotherapy are under investigation.

The tumoricidal activity of chemotherapy and radiation synergizes with immunotherapy by increasing MHC-I expression, antigen cross-presentation and T-cell priming resulting in a broadening of the TCR repertoire and enhancement of Teffector tumor infiltration. The dosage and sequencing of chemotherapy and radiation are likely to significantly impact anti-tumor immune responses. For example, metronomic dosing of chemotherapy is superior to maximum tolerated dosing in preclinical models of ovarian cancer due to preserved immune function and enhanced Teffector activity, and low-dose cyclophosphamide is commonly used to enhance cancer vaccine responses as it decreases Treg abundance and inhibitory function. Furthermore, in preclinical mouse models hypofractionated radiation is superior to single dose treatment regimens in the context of CTLA-4 blockade due to decreased induction of DNA exonuclease Trex1 which enhances cytoplasmic accumulation of dsDNA promoting STING-mediated type-I IFN secretion by tumor cells.

Although cytotoxic therapies may elicit immunogenic cell death, subsequent antitumor immunity is often constrained by suppressive TME components leading to therapeutic resistance. Indeed, radiation-induced abscopal effects have been documented in patients with metastatic solid tumors, but such observations have not been clearly observed in PDA patients. Hence, radiation therapy is neither fully immune stimulatory nor inhibitory, and combinatorial treatment strategies that further modulate the immune response or TME components are likely required to enhance therapeutic efficacy. Efforts combining chemotherapy or radiation with immunotherapy are actively being pursued in PDA clinical trials (Table 1).

Cancer Vaccines

Cancer vaccines effectively prime new or expand the TCR-repertoire of existing anti-tumor T-cell responses. Early phase vaccine studies targeting mKRAS, CEA and MUC-1, telomerase, gastrin and mesothelin demonstrate the induction of antigen-specific immunity and prolonged survival in immunologic responders. However, confirmatory vaccine trials using GVAX (GM-CSF-secreting allogeneic pancreatic tumor cells) or peptides targeting telomerase, survivin or VEGF receptor 2 fail to demonstrate clinical benefit in patients with advanced PDA despite the induction of T-cell immunity.

Vaccination as an adjuvant treatment, a setting in which tumor disease burden and TME immunosuppression are minimized, may improve clinical efficacy. Vaccine studies targeting mKRAS in KPC mice prolongs survival and reduces PanIN progression in young mice harboring early PanIN lesions, a phenotype which is lost in older mice bearing late-stage PanINs with more established TMEs. Adjuvant peptide vaccine studies targeting mKRAS in PDA patients elicits immune responses in the majority of patients and is associated with improved survival. We and others have initiated clinical adjuvant vaccine studies utilizing mKRAS HLA-allele specific short peptides (NCT03592888) or long peptides in combination with ICB therapy (NCT04117087). Unfortunately, a phase III adjuvant vaccine study investigating algenpantucel-L (irradiated allogenic PDA cells expressing murine α-galactosyltransferase) exhibited no clinical benefit in patients with resected PDA despite promising early phase results.

Nevertheless, vaccine studies highlight the safety and feasibility of inducing tumor-specific T-cell responses in this patient population immunosuppressed by chemotherapy and tumor-associated factors. Anti-tumor T-cell priming promotes TME remodeling, suggesting potential synergy with other immune modulatory agents (Table 1). Perioperative GVAX induces mesothelin-specific Teffector responses in the majority of subjects and promotes the formation of tertiary lymphoid aggregates that correlates with increased survival. TME changes associated with GVAX-induced tertiary lymphoid aggregate formation include IFNγ+ T-cell infiltration, Treg recruitment and PD-1/PD-L1 suppressive pathway activation. PD-1/PD-L1 upregulation associated with GVAX-induced T-cell infiltration suggests vaccination may sensitize tumors to ICB therapy. Similar TME changes are observed in mouse models of PDA following vaccination, and combination vaccine/ICB therapy promotes Teffector tumor infiltration and overcomes T-cell exhaustion to induce durable tumor regressions compared to either treatment alone. A phase I study in advanced PDA patients investigating GVAX and ipilimumab demonstrates increased survival in subsets of patients with vaccine-induced mesothelin-specific T-cell responses and broadening of the mesothelin-specific TCR repertoire.

CD40 Agonism

Antigen presenting cell (APC) agonists may be critical for priming and expanding T cells in vivo, especially given the early and systemic dysregulation of antigen presenting DCs in both human PDA and the mouse KPC model. αCD40 triggers T-cell dependent tumor rejection in experimental models via activation of cDC1. In KPC mice, αCD40 monotherapy activates macrophages resulting in T-cell independent tumor regression and TME involution, but fails to induce adaptive immunity resulting in only transient responses in mice and no survival benefit in an early phase clinical trial (, 2013). Recent studies demonstrate αCD40 restores cDC1 frequency and function and enhances anti-tumor T-cell responses when combined with further immune modulating therapies. When combined with tumoricidal chemotherapy to induce antigen spilling, αCD40 primes anti-tumor T-cell responses, and gemcitabine (Gem), nab-paclitaxel (nP) and αCD40 promotes cDC1-dependent T-cell immunity resulting in the regression of KPC tumors. In addition to T-cell priming, Gem/nP/αCD40 significantly remodels the TME as evidenced by DC activation, polyfunctional Teffector infiltration and reduced levels of suppressive cell types. T-cell responses that ensue following αCD40 upregulate PD-L1 expression within the TME suggesting αCD40 effects may be enhanced by ICB therapy. Indeed, combination Gem/nP/αCD40 plus αPD-1 and/or αCTLA4 extends the activity and durability of response compared to Gem/nP/αCD40 alone. These studies support a clinical trial investigating Gem/nP/αCD40 (APX005M) with or without nivolumab in untreated patients with metastatic PDA. Phase I results demonstrate impressive disease-control rates in 20/24 (83%) subjects. Immune profiling performed on peripheral blood from subjects treated on this study suggest treatment-related DC activation and remodeling of the myeloid compartment in the majority of patients. Phase II results evaluating the efficacy of this multimodal treatment strategy are anticipated (NCT03214250), and the combination of Gem/nP/αCD40 is being investigated in the neoadjuvant and adjuvant setting for patients with resectable PDA (Table 1).

The specific timing of chemotherapy administration in relation to αCD40 is of significance. As discussed, the administration of αCD40 after chemotherapy activates APCs and primes anti-tumor T cell responses. Independent of chemotherapy systemic αCD40 administration activates TAMs with antifibrotic activity resulting in significant stromal remodeling, and αCD40 administered prior to chemotherapy results in enhanced chemotherapy TME penetration and therapeutic efficacy in KPC mice. However, αCD40 administration <3 days in advance of chemotherapy results in significant toxicity in preclinical mouse models due to macrophage-induced hepatotoxicity, a phenotype that is lost if αCD40 is administered after chemotherapy or if chemotherapy is administered 5 days following αCD40 treatment. The PRINCE tTrial (NCT03214250) further investigates the clinical significance of chemotherapy and αCD40 sequencing as patients are administered αCD40 on day 3 between days 1 and 8 of chemotherapy.

Cytotoxic therapies may have detrimental effects on patient performance status and immune function that limit their use in combination with multimodal immunotherapies such as αCD40. Chemotherapy does not appear to permanently alter TCR diversity, but it may induce prolonged, irreversible changes to the CD4+ T-cell compartment that affect immune function and response to immunotherapy. We demonstrate in a recent report αCD40/ICB therapy, independent of chemotherapy or radiation, is sufficient to inhibit PDA tumor growth in orthotopic KPC mouse models, and the addition of chemotherapy is detrimental in this model. As opposed to rescuing exhausted T-cells, αCD40/ICB therapy serves to prime and expand polyfunctional T-cell responses. Treatment efficacy is dependent of CD40 and Batf3 expression and independent of TLR and STING pathways, suggesting TLR or STING agonists may further synergize with αCD40/ICB therapy. These data are provocative and suggest a potential role for immunotherapy alone in the absence of chemotherapy or radiation for subsets of PDA patients, such as those in remission after initial therapy for metastatic disease.

Adoptive T-cell Therapy (ATCT)

The correlation between T-cell infiltration with improved clinical outcomes in PDA suggests potential activity of tumor infiltrating lymphocyte (TIL) therapy. PDA tumors are infiltrated by a minimal to moderate degree of CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells which tend to reside within stromal areas excluded from the tumor core. Preclinical studies have demonstrated the feasibility of TIL isolation and ex vivo expansion from PDA tumor specimens. However, the lack of pre-existing T-cell immunity in the majority of PDA patients likely limits the applicability of TIL therapy in this patient population.

Rapid advances in the field of antigen identification, T-cell biology, gene therapy and gene editing have nurtured strategies to redirect T-cell antigen specificity and enhance T-cell function. These advances have resulted in unprecedented clinical outcomes for patients with hematologic malignancies treated with chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy and T-cell receptor T-cell (TCR-T) therapy. However, ATCT has been less successful for the treatment of solid tumors due to poor homing, proliferation and survival of transferred cells. Various strategies have been proposed to improve ATCT activity in solid tumors such as combinatorial treatment with immune modulating agents or the expression of transgenes to enhance T-cell homing, penetration and persistence. Furthermore, the advent of CRISPR/Cas9 technology has recently resulted in clinically translatable gene-editing strategies to enhance the antitumor activity of adoptively transferred T cells via increased transgenic receptor expression or deletion of inhibitory signaling molecules.

Antigen selection poses a major limitation to CAR-T and TCR-T strategies in PDA. Most studies to date target TAAs which may have variable or heterogeneous expression on tumor cells and may pose a greater risk of on-target, off-tumor toxicity. Antigenic targets under investigation for CAR-T therapy in PDA include mesothelin, prostate stem cell antigen, CEA, mucin 1, and HER2 among others. Serious adverse events have occurred in patients treated with T cell products engineered to target HER2 and CEA limiting the evaluation of these antigens as targets of CAR-T therapy in PDA. CAR-T directed against mesothelin have demonstrated efficacy in preclinical mouse models and safety in early phase clinical studies in patients with chemotherapy-refractory metastatic PDA which have warranted its continued clinical evaluation (Table 1). Stromnes et al employed a vaccination strategy in mesothelin KO mice to subvert central tolerance and identify high-avidity mesothelin-specific TCRs, and serial infusions of murine mesothelin TCR-T prolongs survival of KPC mice with established tumors.

TCRs targeting neoantigen epitopes are not subjected to thymic negative selection permitting the identification of high-avidity TCRs with enhanced anti-tumor activity and decreased toxicity. The high prevalence and conserved mutational profile of mKRAS in PDA affords a unique opportunity to develop a neoantigen-targeted TCR therapy with broad generalizability. Mutant KRAS-specific TCRs have been identified utilizing TIL and peripheral blood of patients with mKRAS epithelial cancers and can be induced in vaccinated HLA-transgenic mice. A case report highlights the therapeutic potential of targeting mKRAS neoantigens using ATCT, and clinical trials further investigating this strategy are under investigation (Table 1).

Targeting Myeloid Cells

Myeloid cells comprising TAMs, polymorphonuclear MDSCs and monocytic MDSCs are major components of the TME, especially in PDA. TAMs and MDSCs are critical to tumorigenesis as well as immune evasion via the suppression of Teffector function. Given their immunosuppressive role in the TME, targeting myeloid cells may complement immune-based strategies by removing inhibitory factors that constrain T-cell responses.

In PDA mouse models, CCR2 or CSF-1R inhibition depletes macrophages and promotes T-cell infiltration. CSF-1R blockade reprograms TAMs to promote antigen presentation and prime anti-tumor T-cell responses that are abrogated by the upregulation of immune checkpoints, and combination CSF-1R blockade and ICB therapy greatly enhances anti-tumor activity. CCR2 and CSF-1R inhibition have exhibited encouraging results in early phase clinical studies in patients with advanced PDA. Unfortunately, a phase II clinical study evaluating the CSF-1R blocking mAb cabiralizumab with nivolumab and chemotherapy in advanced PDA patients did not improve progression-free survival compared with chemotherapy alone (NCT03336216) (Table 1).

Mouse studies elucidating mechanisms of resistance to CSF-1R blockade demonstrate compensatory MDSC recruitment as a result of enhanced fibroblast expression of granulocytic chemokines. Therefore, concurrent inhibition of MDSCs may complement TAM-directed therapies. In KPC mice, CXCR2 inhibition prevents neutrophil accumulation, induces stromal remodeling and confers sensitivity to αPD1 therapy, and dual inhibition of CXCR2 and CCR2 to concurrently target MDSCs and TAMs, respectively, improves chemotherapy response and antitumor immunity in orthotopic mouse models of PDA. Additionally, the induction of antigen-specific immunity via αCD40 or vaccination may augment responses to ICB / myeloid-targeted combination therapy. Cytolytic activity correlates with the expression of multiple immune checkpoint genes in PDA, including CTLA4, TIGIT, TIM3 and VISTA. VISTA is expressed on TAMs and enriched in PDA. It contributes to adaptive immunotherapy resistance, and the inhibition of VISTA or additional immune checkpoints may improve responses to TAM-targeted therapies. Enhancing macrophage activity represents an alternative approach. Activation of innate immunity via αCD47 antibody-mediated phagocytosis may credential adaptive immunity via the priming of antitumor T-cell responses, and αCD47 used in combination with other immunotherapies that further enhance macrophage function and promote APC maturation (e.g. αCD40) or enhance T-cell priming and function (e.g. αCTLA4 and αPD1/PDL1) may act synergistically.

In KPC mice, neutralization of TME promoting tumor-intrinsic factors rapidly reverses myeloid to T cell ratios and transforms the TME from immunologically “cold” to “hot”, enhancing responsiveness to immunotherapy not otherwise achievable. In some cases, expression of these tumor-intrinsic factors are downstream of mKRAS, and thus, inhibition of KRAS signaling, if ever achievable with novel therapies, would likely abrogate production of these factors and render PDA sensitive to immunotherapy. In addition, non-oncogenic pathways intrinsic to PDA tumors also mediate myeloid infiltration, including COX2 which promotes the expression of CXCL1 and other factors that establish a “cold” TME in PDA. COX2 inhibition, already a clinically viable option, synergizes with immunotherapy in the KPC model, especially if combined with αCD40. Overall, we hypothesize that “myeloid immunosuppression” is a dominant immune checkpoint in PDA.

Targeting Stromal Elements

The desmoplastic stroma consists of heterogeneous cell populations including cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) as well as fibrotic stroma made up of collagen, fibronectin and hyaluronan. Pancreatic stellate cells (PSCs) represent the predominant fibroblastic cell type in the PDA TME and promote tumor progression and contribute to therapeutic resistance. The inhibition of the Hedgehog pathway decreases stromal fibroblasts and enhances tumor vascularity and chemotherapy responsiveness in KPC mice. However, in the absence of additional therapeutic intervention, studies in mice suggest stromal inhibition promotes tumor progression.

Hyaluronan is a major component of the PDA stroma, and enzymatic ablation of hyaluronan by the hyaluronidase PEGPH20 decreases interstitial fluid pressures and enhances chemotherapeutic efficacy in KPC mice. A phase III study investigating PEGPH20 in combination with Gem/nP in treatment naïve, advanced PDA patients with high hyaluronan levels did not improve OS compared to chemotherapy alone despite encouraging phase II data. A separate phase II study examining PEGPH20 in combination with mFOLFIRINOX in a similar patient population was prematurely terminated due to clinical futility and significant toxicity. The failure of PEGPH20 in combination with chemotherapy suggests that targeting desmoplasia alone is insufficient to overcome PDA immune resistance, and combinatorial strategies targeting non-redundant pathways (e.g. ICB therapy) are currently under investigation (Table 1).

Other potential stromal targets under investigation include tumor-intrinsic hyperactivation of focal adhesion kinase (FAK), the vitamin D receptor (VDR) and fibroblast activation protein (FAP). FAK in PDA promotes a fibrotic and immunosuppressive TME, and FAK inhibition in KPC mice reduces tumor fibrosis and immunosuppressive cell types and renders tumors sensitive chemotherapy or immunotherapy, including ATCT and ICB therapy. Early phase clinical trials evaluating FAK inhibitors in combination with αPD1 are under investigation (Table 1). The VDR has been identified as a transcriptional regulator to revert PSCs to a quiescent state, and the administration of vitamin D analogs induces stromal remodeling and enhances chemotherapy responsiveness in KPC mice. Vitamin D deficiency is associated with decreased survival in PDA, and clinical studies combining vitamin D analogs with ICB therapy are underway (Table 1). FAP is overexpressed by tumor cells and stromal fibroblasts and associated with poor outcomes in PDA. Ablation of FAP-expressing cells in mouse models of lung cancer and PDA improves vaccine and ICB responsiveness highlighting their immunosuppressive roles. FAP-directed CAR-T therapy demonstrates anti-tumor activity in mouse models of cancer including KPC mice. Furthermore, targeting CXCL12 produced by FAP+ fibroblasts via the pharmacologic inhibition of CXCR4 increases tumor sensitivity to ICB therapy in KPC mice. In a recent phase II study, CXCR4 inhibition in combination with pembrolizumab decreases suppressive cell types, promotes T-cell infiltration and yields encouraging clinical outcomes when combined with chemotherapy in patients with advanced, chemotherapy-refractory PDA, although a phase I study of the CXCR4 inhibitor LY2510924 and durvalumab showed only modest clinical activity.

Conclusions

PDA is among the most immune resistant tumor types, a feature dictated by its unique genomic landscape where oncogenic drivers fuel immunosuppression and T-cell exclusion at the earliest stages of tumor inception. Defective T-cell priming in part accounts for the clinical failure of αCTLA-4 and αPD-1/PD-L1 ICB therapy. Although rare PDA tumors with MSI-H status are important exceptions, non-MSI-H PDA tumors with relatively high mutational burdens remain resistant to ICB therapy. Strategies that promote T-cell priming used in combination with ICB therapy, including chemotherapy, radiation and vaccination have thus far been unsuccessful. Advances in our understanding of targetable PDA antigens, including mKRAS, have fostered the development of promising ATCT strategies, although the activity of CAR-T and TCR-T therapies remain unproven in PDA.

Although late phase clinical trials have yielded largely negative results, data from preclinical mouse models and early phase clinical studies offer important insights regarding criteria needed to induce PDA immunity. It is evident that single agent immunotherapies are unlikely to exhibit activity in PDA, and rational combinatorial treatments that target non-redundant mechanisms of immune-resistance are needed to reach an immunological tipping point. Rational combinatorial approaches should not only (1) confer T-cell antigen specificity, (2) enhance T-cell effector function and (3) neutralize immunosuppressive myeloid cells and stromal elements within the TME, but should also be selected based on PDA genotypic characteristics that drive phenotypic heterogeneity among subgroups of patients.

Toxicity and therapeutic sequencing pose challenges to the rapid clinical translation of immune modulatory combinations; however, multimodal immunotherapeutic approaches are currently under clinical investigation and have demonstrated safety. For example, the PRINCE trial (NCT03214250) utilizes chemotherapy to induce immunogenic cell death, αCD40 to promote T-cell priming and re-educate suppressive cells within the TME, and αPD-1 to prevent exhaustion of infiltrating tumor-specific Teffector. Alternative strategies may be further explored in clinical settings in which the TME has been minimized or reduced, such as in the adjuvant setting or as maintenance therapy following “induction” chemotherapy for patients with newly diagnosed PDA.

Finally, mKRAS remains an intriguing target that may be exploited as an immunologic bull’s eye. Although mKRAS as a “tumor antigen” may well be a tractable target for vaccines and ATCT one day, targeted therapies that block KRAS signaling (e.g. MEK inhibitors) or its phenotypic consequences (e.g. autophagy) that drive tumor-intrinsic mechanisms of immunosuppression already exist and may further synergize with immunotherapeutic strategies.
