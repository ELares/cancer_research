# Epigenetic suppression of Nrf2-Slc40a1 axis induces ferroptosis and enhances immunotherapy in pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Despite progress in immunotherapy for several solid tumors, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains largely unresponsive, primarily due to its profoundly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) characterized by limited CD8+ T cell infiltration. Novel strategies are needed to overcome this immune resistance and enhance the efficacy of checkpoint blockade.

**METHODS**: We established a patient-derived organoid (PDO)-autologous T cell co-culture platform using endoscopic ultrasound-guided fine-needle aspiration (EUS-FNA) specimens from patients with PDAC unresponsive to anti-programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) therapy. This high-throughput system was used to screen a focused library of epigenetic compounds. The effects of candidate drugs were validated in orthotopic PDAC models, integrating functional assays, sequencing analyses, and patient data.

**RESULTS**: Through screening, we identified the histone demethylase inhibitor JIB04, which synergized with anti-PD-1 therapy to enhance T cell cytotoxicity in PDO-T cell co-cultures. Mechanistically, JIB04 suppressed nuclear-factor-E2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) and reduced chromatin accessibility at distal regulatory regions of its downstream target solute carrier family 40 member 1 (Slc40a1), impairing iron efflux and promoting ferroptosis in tumor cells. This ferroptotic stress facilitated CD8+ T cell infiltration and activation, thereby converting the PDAC TME from "cold" to "hot." Patients with PDAC with lower Nrf2 and Slc40a1 expression exhibited higher CD8+ T cell infiltration and improved responses to anti-PD-1 therapy.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our findings establish a PDO-T cell platform for precision immunotherapy screening and identify JIB04 as a promising epigenetic agent that induces ferroptosis and sensitizes PDAC to immune checkpoint blockade. This ferroptosis-based reprogramming provides a potential strategy to overcome resistance and improve clinical outcomes in PDAC.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

We established a clinically relevant patient-derived organoid (PDO)-autologous T cell co-culture screening platform from endoscopic ultrasound-guided fine-needle aspiration samples of patients with PDAC unresponsive to programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) therapy.

Using this platform, we identified the histone demethylase inhibitor JIB04, which suppressed the nuclear-factor-E2-related factor 2 (Nrf2)–solute carrier family 40 member 1 (Slc40a1) axis, induced ferroptosis, and enhanced CD8+ T cell infiltration and cytotoxicity.

Patients with PDAC with lower Nrf2 and Slc40a1 expression exhibited greater CD8+ T cell infiltration and improved responses to PD-1 therapy.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

The PDO-T cell co-culture system provides a powerful translational platform for precision immunotherapy screening in PDAC.

Our findings highlight a ferroptosis-based epigenetic strategy and support JIB04 as a promising candidate for combinatorial immunotherapy.

Introduction

 Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), such as antibodies targeting programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) or cytotoxic T-lymphocytes-associated protein 4, has demonstrated remarkable therapeutic benefits across multiple cancer types, including melanoma, non-small cell lung cancer, and renal cell carcinoma. However, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains largely refractory to ICB-based therapies, with most patients exhibiting minimal or no clinical response. This limited efficacy is primarily attributed to the highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), low tumor mutational burden, and poor infiltration of effector T cells—hallmarks of “immune-cold” tumors. Therefore, identifying novel therapeutic strategies that can inflame tumors and enhance tumor-intrinsic immunogenicity, thereby enabling combination approaches that augment the efficacy of existing immunotherapies, is of critical importance. Epigenetic regulators play pivotal roles in tumor initiation, progression, and therapeutic resistance. Emerging evidence further highlights their central role in modulating the tumor immune microenvironment. These findings propose epigenetic intervention as a valuable strategy to boost the clinical benefits of ICB therapies.

Patient-derived organoid (PDO) cultures are three-dimensional cellular constructs generated from primary tumor tissues that serve as robust preclinical models. It provides a physiologically relevant and scalable platform for systematic drug screening and predictive biomarker identification, owing to its ability to preserve and recapitulate both the genetic landscape and histopathological features of the originating tumor. Since most advanced patients with PDAC receiving ICB therapy are ineligible for surgery, surgical specimens are often unavailable for organoid generation. Nevertheless, endoscopic ultrasound-guided fine-needle aspiration (EUS-FNA)—the current diagnostic standard—provides a minimally invasive and clinically feasible method for acquiring tumor tissue from these inoperable cases. Moreover, PDAC is characterized by a profoundly immunosuppressive TME, defined by an abundance of low tumor immunogenicity and limited infiltration of cytotoxic T cells. These features collectively contribute to the immunologically “cold” nature of PDAC and represent major barriers to the success of immunotherapy. These discussions highlight the importance of integrating PDOs with autologous T cells into co-culture assays to establish a drug screening platform that more faithfully recapitulates the in vivo TME.

In this study, we developed a robust and precise drug screening platform by establishing PDOs from EUS-FNA specimens of individuals unresponsive to anti-PD-1 therapy, enabling an unbiased evaluation of a focused epigenetic compound library. Autologous T cells were subsequently incorporated into co-culture assays to assess cytotoxic responses. Through high-throughput screening, we identified the histone demethylase inhibitor JIB04. JIB04 treatment markedly enhanced the cytotoxic activity of autologous T cells against PDOs in the organoid-T cell co-culture system, whereas no such effect was observed when T cells were treated alone, indicating that JIB04-induced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity relies on tumor cell-intrinsic alterations. Mechanistically, JIB04 downregulated Nuclear-factor-E2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) and reduced chromatin accessibility at distal regulatory regions of its downstream iron exporter gene, solute carrier family 40 member 1 (Slc40a1). This epigenetic reprogramming impaired iron efflux, promoted intracellular iron accumulation, and triggered ferroptosis in tumor cells. Ferroptotic stress was associated with increased CD8+ T cell infiltration and enhanced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity, effectively transforming the PDAC TME from “cold” to “hot”. Consistent with these findings, patients with PDAC who responded favorably to anti-PD-1 therapy exhibited lower expression of Nrf2 and Slc40a1 compared with non-responders, suggesting that these markers may serve as potential predictors of immunotherapeutic efficacy in PDAC.

Materials and methods

Patients and specimens

10 patients with PDAC were enrolled from the PDAC database of Nanjing Drum Tower Hospital, affiliated with Nanjing University Medical School. All patients received chemotherapy combined with anti-PD-1 therapy between January 2019 and December 2021. Initially, patients underwent six cycles of chemotherapy combined with PD-1 antibody therapy, followed by PD-1 antibody maintenance therapy. Bimonthly contrast-enhanced CT scans were performed to assess the therapeutic effects of the combination treatment. The response to therapy was evaluated using the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors.

In the cohort, biopsies of pancreatic cancer tissue were obtained through an EUS-FNA procedure. The procedure included rapid on-site evaluation (ROSE) with a cytopathologist present for each case. Once the preliminary diagnosis was confirmed via ROSE, one to two additional needle passes were performed for organoid establishment and paraffin embedding. Part of the FNA specimens was placed in basal organoid media and transported to the laboratory within 6 hours for further processing and organoid generation. Additionally, some tissue samples were fixed in 10% neutral buffered formalin for 48 hours and then embedded in paraffin. The study adhered to the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and the International Conference on Harmonization Guidelines for Good Clinical Practice.

Tissue samples were sectioned at a thickness of 4 µm following standard processing. H&E staining was performed according to a standard histological protocol. Immunofluorescence (IF) staining was conducted using primary antibodies against pan-cytokeratin (PanCK) (Invitrogen, 53-9003-82), interferon-γ (IFNγ) (Proteintech, 15365-1-AP), CD8 (Proteintech, 66868-1-Ig), Nrf2 (Invitrogen, PA5-27882) and Slc40a1 (Proteintech, 26601-1-AP). Fluorescence images were captured using a fluorescence microscope. Quantification of staining was performed by determining the percentage of positively stained cells using ImageJ software.

Organoid cultures, immunohistochemistry, and immunofluorescence staining

The isolation and culture of organoids were performed following Tuveson’s protocol. Briefly, tumor tissues were finely minced and enzymatically digested with collagenase II (5 mg/mL, Gibco) and DNase I (10 µg/mL, Roche) in Advanced Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM)/F12 supplemented with 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-piperazineethanesulfonic acid(HEPES) (1×, Invitrogen), GlutaMAX (1×, Invitrogen), and penicillin/streptomycin (1×, Invitrogen) at 37°C for 20 min. The resulting cell suspension was then embedded in growth factor-reduced (GFR) Matrigel. For passaging, organoids were dissociated using TrypLE (Gibco) at 37°C for 15 min before re-embedding in GFR Matrigel. The culture medium was refreshed every 3 days.

The culture medium was discarded, and organoids were collected by centrifugation. The pellets were fixed in paraformaldehyde for 1 hour and embedded in 2% agarose before being paraffin-embedded and sectioned. H&E staining was performed following standard histological protocols. Immunohistochemistry staining was conducted using anti-Ki67 (Invitrogen, MA5-14520), anti-Nrf2 (Invitrogen, PA5-27882), anti-Slc40a1 (Proteintech, 26601-1-AP), and anti-4HNE (Invitrogen, MA5-27570).

C11 staining was performed using the C11-BODIPY (581/591) probe (Dojindo, L267), while PI (Yeasen, 40 755ES64) and Calcein-AM (Yeasen, 40 719ES50) staining were conducted following the manufacturer’s protocols. IF staining was carried out using anti-PanCK (Invitrogen, 53-9003-82), anti-Nrf2 (Invitrogen, PA5-27882), anti-Slc40a1 (Proteintech, 26 601–1-AP) and anti-Ki67 (Abcam, ab16667).

Drug screening and cell viability assay

Pancreatic tumor organoids were derived from patients who had failed to respond to anti-PD-1 therapy. The organoids were harvested, digested into single cells, and counted using 0.4% Trypan blue solution. The cells were then resuspended in advanced DMEM/F12 and Matrigel at a 1:2 ratio and seeded into 96-well plates (250 cells per 5 µL suspension per well). After Matrigel solidification, 100 µL of complete medium was added, followed by incubation for 24 hours. The medium was then replaced with culture medium containing epigenetic compounds, with dimethyl sulfoxide serving as the negative control. Each drug concentration was tested in triplicate. Cell viability was evaluated using the CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay (Promega). After 5 days of drug incubation, the luminescence of each well was measured.

Organoid–PBMC Co-Culture

10 mL of peripheral blood was collected concurrently with tumor specimen collection. Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were isolated using Ficoll-Paque density gradient centrifugation and cryopreserved for later use.

PBMCs were thawed using a standard protocol, then plated in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% P/S at a density of 2×10⁶ cells/mL. The cells were stimulated with 1 µg/mL plate-bound anti-CD3 (OKT3) and 5 µg/mL soluble anti-CD28 antibodies for 48 hours. Subsequently, T cells were cultured in fresh medium containing 100 U/mL recombinant human interleukin-2 for an additional 3 days.

On the day of co-culture, organoids from one well were harvested, mechanically disrupted, and dissociated into single cells by incubation with Trypsin-EDTA for 15 min at 37°C. Immune cells were collected, counted, and combined with organoids at a T cell-to-organoid ratio of 20–30:1. Cells were seeded in 24-well glass-bottom high-content screening microplates (Corning) in 1 mL of medium, consisting of 500 µL T cell culture medium and 500 µL PDAC organoid culture medium. The co-cultures were then treated with αPD-1, 1 µM of individual epigenetic compounds, or a combination of compounds and αPD-1 for 48 hours. Tumor organoid area, representing tumor cell viability, was quantified from each image using the software integrated into the Incucyte device.

FFPE-embedding of co-cultures

For formalin-fixed paraffin embedding (FFPE) of co-cultures, wells were briefly rinsed with 1×Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline(DPBS) and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde for 20 min at room temperature. Following fixation, samples were washed three times with 1×DPBS and residual buffer was aspirated. In parallel, HistoGel (Thermo Scientific, 22-110-678) was liquefied according to the manufacturer’s instructions. A total of 400 µL of liquefied HistoGel was added to each well, and plates were transferred to 4°C for approximately 10 min to allow gel solidification. After polymerization, HistoGel-embedded co-cultures were carefully extracted using a thin metal spatula. Incomplete retrieval was considered indicative of poor matrix integrity. Samples were then transferred to biopsy cassettes and subjected to overnight dehydration using a vacuum tissue processor (TissueTek VIP5, Sakura). The next day, tissues were embedded in molten paraffin using metal molds, sealed with cassette lids, and allowed to solidify. Fully polymerized FFPE blocks were stored at −20°C until sectioning.

According to the manufacturer’s instructions, propidium iodide (PI) staining was performed using the PI kit (Yeasen, 40755ES64). IF staining was conducted with antibodies against PanCK (Invitrogen, 53-9003-82) and CD8 (Proteintech, 66868-1-Ig). Fluorescent images were acquired using a fluorescence microscope.

Flow cytometry

The organoid-T cell co-culture system was dissociated into single-cell suspensions using TrypLE Express (Gibco), followed by washing with staining buffer (BD Pharmingen, 554656). Cells were passed through 70 µm cell strainers to remove clumps and stained with Zombie-NIR fixable viability dye (BioLegend, 423106) to distinguish live and dead cells. For surface marker analysis, cells were incubated with fluorochrome-conjugated antibodies diluted in staining buffer (2% FBS in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)) at 4°C for 30 min in the dark. For intracellular cytokine detection, cells were stimulated immediately after isolation with 100 ng/mL phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (Yeasen, 50601ES03), 1 µg/mL ionomycin (Yeasen, 50401ES03), and 5 µg/mL brefeldin A (BioLegend, 420601) in RPMI-1640 medium containing 10% FBS at 37°C for 4 hours. Cells were then fixed, permeabilized, and stained with anti-CD8-PE (BioLegend, 344706), anti-IFN-γ-APC (BD Biosciences, 562017) and PI staining solution (Sigma, S7109) according to the manufacturers’ protocols.

To characterize immune cell subpopulations within the TME, orthotopic tumors were harvested at the study endpoint. Tumors were excised, minced into small fragments, and enzymatically digested in 2 mg/mL collagenase IV (Yeasen, 40510ES60) at 37°C for 25 min. The resulting cell suspensions were passed through 70 µm strainers to obtain single-cell suspensions. Cells were washed and stained with Zombie-NIR fixable viability dye (BioLegend, 423106) to discriminate live from dead cells. Cell surface and intracellular cytokine staining were performed as described above. Briefly, cells were fixed, permeabilized, and stained with the following antibodies: anti-CD45-PerCP-Cy5.5 (BD Biosciences, 550994), anti-CD3e-FITC (BD Biosciences, 553061), anti-IFN-γ-APC (BioLegend, 505809), anti-CD4-APC (BD Biosciences, 553051), and anti-NK1.1-PE (BD Biosciences, 561046), following the respective manufacturers’ instructions. Data were acquired using an LSRFortessa flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) and analyzed with FlowJo software.

Cell Lines

Murine PDAC cell lines KPC-1 and KPC-3 were derived from spontaneous pancreatic tumors in Pdx1-Cre; LSL-KrasG12D; Trp53R172H (KPC) transgenic mice. Early-passage cells were used for all experiments. Cells were maintained in DMEM Medium (GUMD-R115, Haixing Biosciences) supplemented with 10% FBS, 100 U/mL penicillin, and 100 µg/mL streptomycin. All cell lines were routinely tested and confirmed negative for Mycoplasma contamination.

Cell viability assay

To begin with, 5,000 cells were seeded into each well of a 96-well plate and incubated for 24 hours. Cell viability was assessed using the CCK-8 kit (TargetMol, USA, C0005). After treatment, 10 µL of CCK-8 solution was added to each well and incubated for 1 hour. The absorbance was then measured at 450 nm.

Orthotopic tumor challenges

Male immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice were purchased from GemPharmatech Co., Ltd. (Nanjing, China) and maintained under specific pathogen-free conditions. All animal procedures were conducted in accordance with protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Nanjing Drum Tower Hospital (approval no. 20231129). Orthotopic pancreatic tumors were established by injecting 5×10⁴ KPC-1-luciferase-expressing cells in 50 µL of sterile PBS directly into the pancreas, following a modified procedure based on the protocol described by Erstad et al. Tumor progression was monitored weekly using the IVIS Lumina LT in vivo imaging system (PerkinElmer, USA). For bioluminescence imaging, mice were anesthetized with isoflurane after intraperitoneal injection of D-luciferin (150 mg/kg; PerkinElmer), and signal intensities were quantified using Living Image software (PerkinElmer, USA).

For therapeutic interventions, mice received intraperitoneal injections of JIB04 (10 mg/kg, every other day) or deferoxamine (DFO, 50 mg/kg/day). On day 22 post-treatment initiation, mice were euthanized for subsequent analyses. Tumor tissues were either fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde or snap-frozen at –80°C for downstream applications. Throughout the treatment period, body weights were monitored every 3 days. At the time of euthanasia, blood was collected via orbital puncture for complete blood count and liver/kidney function analysis. Major organs were harvested and subjected to H&E staining to evaluate systemic toxicity. For mice enrolled in survival studies, observation continued until the endpoint was reached without predetermined sacrifice.

For CD8+ T cell depletion experiments, 200 µg of anti-mouse CD8α monoclonal antibody (clone 53–6.7, Bio X Cell, BE0004-1) or rat IgG2a isotype control antibody (clone 2A3, Bio X Cell, BE0089) was administered intraperitoneally starting 1 day prior to tumor implantation (day −1). Antibody injections were subsequently performed every 3 days until day 21.

For anti-PD-1 immunotherapy experiments, mice were administered 200 µg of anti-mouse PD-1 monoclonal antibody (clone RMP1-14, Bio X Cell, BE0146) or 200 µg of rat IgG2a isotype control (clone 2A3, Bio X Cell, BE0089) via intraperitoneal injection every 3 days, beginning on day 6 after tumor inoculation and continuing until the indicated endpoints.

Orthotopic tumors were excised from mice and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde at room temperature. After washing and dehydration in graded ethanol (70–100%), the neoplastic tissue was embedded in paraffin and sectioned into 5 µm thick slices. Antigen retrieval was performed by heating the tissue samples in a Tris-EDTA solution in a microwave for 10 min, followed by treatment with 3% hydrogen peroxide. The sections were then incubated overnight at 4°C with primary antibodies against Ki67 (Invitrogen, MA5-14520) and 4-HNE (Proteintech, 68 538–1-Ig), followed by incubation with a secondary antibody and DAB staining.

ATAC-sequencing and data analysis

KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells were treated with either dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) or JIB04 for 24 hours. Cells were first rinsed with PBS, then subjected to crosslinking using 1% paraformaldehyde for 10 min at room temperature. The reaction was quenched by glycine addition, and samples were snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen for storage. Approximately 5×10⁴ cells were used for subsequent steps. After thawing, cells were resuspended in 1 mL of chilled lysis buffer composed of 10 mM Tris-HCl (pH 7.4), 10 mM NaCl, and 3 mM MgCl2. The suspension was centrifuged at 4°C for 10 min at full speed in a fixed-angle rotor. Supernatant was discarded carefully, and the cell pellet was lysed by incubation in 50 µL of buffer containing 0.1% NP-40, 0.1% Tween-20, and 0.01% digitonin on ice for 3 min, with gentle pipetting to mix. Lysis was stopped by adding 1 mL of resuspension buffer supplemented with 0.1% Tween-20 (without NP-40 or digitonin), followed by gentle inversion. The nuclei were pelleted again at 4°C by centrifugation and resuspended in 50 µL of a transposition mix consisting of: 25 µL 2×transposition (TD) buffer, 2.5 µL Tn5 transposase (final concentration 100 nM), 16.5 µL PBS, 0.5 µL 1% digitonin, 0.5 µL 10% Tween-20, and 5 µL nuclease-free water. Transposition was carried out at 37°C for 30 min with shaking at 1000 rpm using a thermomixer. The DNA was purified using the MinElute PCR Purification Kit (Qiagen). Subsequent library amplification followed the protocol adapted from Buenrostro et al.

Raw Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq) reads were first trimmed using Trimmomatic (V.0.4.1) to remove sequencing adapters. The quality of the trimmed reads was assessed using FastQC. Clean reads were then aligned to the mm10 reference genome using Bowtie2 (V.2.2.6) with the parameters ‘-q --no-unal --threads 8 –sensitive’. Redundant and PCR duplicate reads were removed using Picard Tools (V.2.60; http://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/) and SAMtools (V.0.1.19) (http://broadinstitute.github.io/picard/). Peak calling was performed using MACS2 (V.2.1.0), with duplicated reads removed by setting the parameter ‘--keep-dup=1’ to ensure greater specificity.

Peak annotation was conducted using the ChIPseeker package (V.1.26.2) in R, with mm10 as the reference genome. Differential peak analysis was performed using the DiffBind package (V.3.0.15). Signal intensity visualizations were generated using DeepTools, and genome-wide signal tracks were visualized using the WashU Epigenome Browser.

For proximal and distal annotation, chromatin-accessible regions located within ±2 kb of the transcription start site (TSS) were designated as proximal, whereas regions located outside this window were classified as distal.

Transcription factor motif enrichment analysis of differential peaks associated with Slc40a1 was performed using HOMER (V.4.11.1). Differential ATAC-seq peak regions were compared with the mm10 reference genome. Each peak was resized to a uniform 200 bp window, and repetitive elements were masked using the mask option to minimize false positives from low-complexity sequences. HOMER automatically selected background sequences matched in both GC content and length to the input peaks. Motifs were selected based on enrichment p values, with only transcription factors having p<0.05 and those expressed in RNA sequencing included for further analysis (online supplemental table 1). The complete list of motif enrichment results is provided in online supplemental data file 1 to ensure reproducibility. For visualization, motif data were processed and plotted using the motifStack package (V.1.50.0; https://git.bioconductor.org/packages/motifStack) in R.

RNA sequencing of cells and tumor tissues

KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells were seeded in 6-well plates and treated with either DMSO or JIB04 for 24 hours. Total RNA was then extracted, and transcriptome sequencing was performed using the Illumina NovaSeq X-Plus platform by MajorBio (Shanghai, China).

Gene expression levels obtained from RNA-seq were used to rank genes in descending order. The genes were then evenly divided into five groups. For each group, chromatin accessibility surrounding the genes was quantified. Based on the differential changes in chromatin accessibility regions and the corresponding trends in gene expression alterations, a set of 3347 differentially expressed genes was identified. Subsequently, Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis was performed separately for the proximal and distal sets of differentially expressed genes.

Orthotopic pancreatic tumors were collected at the study endpoint for RNA analysis. Transcriptome sequencing was performed using the Illumina NovaSeq X-Plus platform by MajorBio (Shanghai, China). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) was subsequently conducted using the clusterProfiler package.

Construction of transcription factor regulatory modules

Motif enrichment analysis of the differential chromatin accessibility regions surrounding the proximal and distal sets of differentially expressed genes was performed using Homer software, with the mm10 reference genome. After obtaining the results, motifs with a sequence frequency greater than 5% and a significant enrichment (p value<0.05) were selected. Active transcription factors, which were expressed in tumor cells (based on RNA-seq results, with a read count>6), were then identified. Regulatory networks for these transcription factors and their downstream targets were constructed using the TRRUST database (https://www.grnpedia.org/trrust/).

Lipid peroxidation measurements

Malondialdehyde (MDA) concentration, BODIPY 581/591 C11 intensity, and CellROX flow cytometry assays were employed to assess lipid peroxidation in cell lines. Briefly, for MDA measurement (Abcam, Ab118970), cell samples were lysed using MDA lysis buffer as per the manufacturer’s instructions. Thiobarbituric acid (TBA) reagent was added to lysed samples or standards, followed by incubation at 95°C for 60 min, and absorbance was measured at OD 532 nm using a plate reader. For BODIPY 581/591 C11 (Dojindo, L267) and CellROX assays (Invitrogen, C10444), KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells treated with either DMSO or JIB04 (Selleck, S7281) were washed with PBS and stained with respective dyes for 30 min at 37°C. Subsequently, data were acquired using an LSRFortessa flow cytometer (BD Biosciences) and analyzed with FlowJo software.

Measurement of intracellular iron ions

Cells were seeded onto fluorescence culture dishes and treated with DMSO or JIB04 for 24 hours. The culture medium was then discarded, and cells were washed three times with Hank’s balanced salt solution (HBSS). Next, cells were incubated with FerroOrange (Dojindo, F374) working solution at a concentration of 1 µmol/L for 30 min in a 37°C, 5% CO2 incubator. Subsequently, images were captured using confocal microscopy.

Transmission electron microscopy

Following JIB04 treatment, KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells were washed with PBS, fixed overnight in 2.5% glutaraldehyde, and then in 1% osmium tetroxide for 2 hours. After dehydration in alcohol, the samples were embedded in Epon resin and observed and imaged using transmission electron microscopy.

Trace element analysis

Tissue samples underwent metal analysis using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (Perkin Elmer Nexion 2000) with 50 ppb Bismuth as an internal standard. Specifically, tissues were digested with 2 mL of nitric acid (Trace Metal Grade; Fisher) per gram of total wet weight for 24 hours, followed by an additional digestion with 1 mL of hydrogen peroxide (Trace Metal Grade; Fisher) per gram of total wet weight for another 24 hours at room temperature. Samples were stored at 4°C until metal quantification. Final sample dilutions were performed using ultrapure water (VWR Chemicals ARISTARULTRA).

Western blot analysis and antibodies

Whole-cell lysates were prepared using RIPA lysis buffer (Yeason, 20 101ES60) supplemented with 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF) and a protease inhibitor cocktail (MedChemExpress, HY-K0010). Protein concentrations were determined using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Primary antibodies used for immunoblotting included anti-GPX4 (Abcam, ab125066), anti-β-ACTIN (Proteintech, HRP-60008), anti-Slc40A1 (Proteintech, 26601-1-AP), and anti-Nrf2 (Cell Signaling Technology, #12721).

Plasmid construction

The coding sequences of Nrf2 and Slc40a1 were synthesized and subcloned into the pCDNA3.1-ZsGreen-puro expression vector (HANBIO, Shanghai, China). All constructs were confirmed by Sanger sequencing. KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells were subsequently transiently transfected with the respective plasmids using Lipofectamine 3000 (Invitrogen, L3000008) according to the manufacturer’s protocol.

RNA extraction and quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNA was extracted from cells using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, Life Technologies) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. cDNA synthesis was carried out using the PrimeScript Master Mix (Takara). Quantitative real-time PCR was performed on a 7500 Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) using SYBR Premix Ex Taq reagents (Takara). Mouse β-ACTIN was used as an internal control. The primer sequences used were as follows: Mouse Slc40a1: Forward 5′-GTGGAGTACTTCTTGCTCTGG-3′; Reverse 5′-CTGCTTCAGTTCTGACTCCTC-3′Mouse β-ACTIN: Forward 5′-GTCCCTCACCCTCCCAAAAG-3′; Reverse 5′-GCTGCCTCAACACCTCAACCC-3′.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation and PCR assay

Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) assays were conducted using a commercial ChIP Assay Kit (P2078, Beyotime, Beijing, China), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, chromatin was crosslinked, sheared, and immunoprecipitated using an anti-Nrf2 antibody (Cell Signaling Technology, #12721).

To identify potential Nrf2 binding regions within the mouse Slc40a1 gene, open chromatin regions were first determined based on ATAC-seq data. Candidate binding sites were then predicted using the JASPAR database (http://jaspar.genereg.net/) with the Scan tool. PCR primers were designed targeting the predicted Nrf2-binding region: forward 5′- TAACCTGCCTGCATACGTCCT′ and reverse 5′- CGCATTCACGGTACTGCTC −3′. ChIP-enriched DNA was amplified by PCR and resolved on a 2% agarose gel to visualize specific binding. Input and IgG controls were included in parallel to validate specificity.

Single-cell sequencing

Single-cell suspensions were loaded onto the DNBelab C4 system (MGI, China), targeting the capture of 5,000–10,000 cells per sample. Library preparation was performed using the DNBelab C Series Single-Cell Reagent Kit (MGI, China) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Libraries were sequenced on the DNBSEQ-T7 platform (MGI) with a paired-end configuration of 26 cycles for Read 1, 8 cycles for the i7 index, and 98 cycles for Read 2.

ScRNA-seq data processing, clustering, and integration

Raw single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) data generated from the DNBelab C4 system were processed using CellRanger (V.5.0.1) for read alignment, barcode assignment, and unique molecular identifier counting, with the genome reference mm10, after conversion to a 10x Genomics-compatible format. Filtered count matrices were converted to sparse matrices and analyzed using the Seurat package (V.4.2.0). Cells expressing fewer than 200 genes or with more than 5% mitochondrial gene content were excluded from downstream analysis.

To correct for potential batch effects across samples and experiments, data integration was performed using Seurat’s canonical correlation analysis-based integration workflow. Specifically, 2000 highly variable genes were selected for anchoring using the ‘FindIntegrationAnchors’ function, and 20 dimensions were used in the ‘IntegrateData’ function. Cell clustering was performed using the ‘FindClusters’ function at a resolution of 0.5, and cell types were annotated based on canonical marker genes reported.

Dimensionality reduction and visualization were conducted using the ‘RunUMAP’ function with uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP).

For T cell-specific analysis, T cells were subset from the integrated dataset and re-clustered using the same pipeline. Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using the top 20 principal components, and clustering was conducted with the ‘FindClusters’ function at a resolution of 0.5. Cell types were again annotated using established marker genes from previous studies. Dimensionality reduction was visualized via UMAP. Cluster resolutions were adjusted based on visual assessment of cluster separation.

Identification of signature genes

To identify marker genes for each cell cluster or subset, the ‘FindAllMarkers’ function from the Seurat package was used. Differential expression analysis was performed by comparing each cluster (or subset) against all other cells using the Wilcoxon rank-sum test (‘FindMarkers’ function with default parameters). Genes were ranked by log2 fold change, and those showing significant differential expression were considered as signature genes for further analysis.

Pathway analysis

Gene set variation analysis (GSVA) was performed using the GSVA package to evaluate pathway activity across cell populations. Differential enrichment scores between groups were calculated using the ‘FindMarkers’ function from Seurat.

DAMP release assays

The concentrations of high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), calreticulin (CRT), and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) in PDO culture supernatants were determined using commercial kits according to the manufacturers’ instructions. Human HMGB1 ELISA kit (JL47571, Jianglai), human CRT ELISA kit (JL10348, Jianglai), and ATP Assay Kit (S0026, Beyotime) were employed. PDOs derived from one representative non-responder were treated with DMSO, JIB04, or JIB04 plus DFO, and culture supernatants were collected for analysis. All experiments were performed in three independent replicates.

Statistical analysis

All experiments were repeated with three or more independent biological replicates. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism V.9.0. Data are expressed as mean±SD. Unless otherwise specified, statistical significance between multiple groups was determined using one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons test. For comparisons between two groups, unpaired Student’s t-test was used. Survival curves were generated using the Kaplan-Meier method, and statistical significance was assessed with the log-rank test. P values<0.05 were considered statistically significant.

Results

Epigenetic drug screening based on organoid platform

To identify effective drugs that enhance the efficacy of anti-PD-1 therapy in PDAC, we followed up with 10 patients who received anti-PD-1 therapy and collected their tissue samples. Our findings revealed that patients who responded positively to anti-PD-1 therapy had a prolonged overall survival (OS) compared with non-responders. Treatment efficacy was monitored bimonthly by contrast-enhanced CT (figure 1A,B). Considering that patients with significant T cell infiltration, specifically a sufficient density of CD8+T cells, respond to anti-PD-1 therapy, we evaluated the levels of infiltration of IFNγ+ CD8+ T cells in PDAC tissues from patients who responded positively or negatively to anti-PD-1 therapy using a multiplex IF (mIF) assay. Our findings revealed that responders exhibited a greater infiltration of IFNγ+ CD8+ T cells in PDAC tissues compared with non-responders (figure 1C).

To address the poor clinical outcomes of ICB-based therapies in PDAC, we established a high-throughput drug screening platform using pancreatic tumor organoids derived from patients who had failed to respond to anti-PD-1 therapy. Specifically, PDAC tumor samples were obtained from four non-responders, as shown in figure 1D. We first utilized ROSE to confirm the presence of pancreatic cancer cells in the tissue, followed by two additional passes using EUS-FNA to collect tissue for organoid establishment. Three organoids were successfully established, resulting in an overall success rate of 75% (3/4). To characterize the histological features of tumor tissues from the successful organoid cultures, we performed H&E staining and IF, which revealed that the organoids and their corresponding primary tumors exhibited similar histologic features (figure 1E).

Given that the combination of epigenetic therapies and immunotherapy is rapidly emerging as a novel paradigm for cancer treatment, we employed a comprehensive library of 177 commercially available epigenetic compounds for the screening process. Initially, all compounds were screened at a concentration of 1 µM. Based on preliminary results, six drugs that exceeded the half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) threshold in all three organoid models at 1 µM were selected for secondary screening (figure 1F,G). In the secondary screening, the organoid-T cell co-culture system was treated with these six compounds in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy, with anti-PD-1 monotherapy serving as a control. Interestingly, the histone demethylase inhibitor JIB04, in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy, demonstrated the greatest efficacy in PDAC organoids derived from patients resistant to prior anti-PD-1 treatment when co-cultured with autologous T cells (figure 1H). Additionally, we next applied the mIF method introduced by Harter et al to analyze epithelial damage and immune cell behavior within organoid-T cell co-culture system. Strikingly, we observed an increase in PI signaling within the organoids, accompanied by a higher presence of T cells surrounding the organoids following JIB04 treatment (figure 1I). Collectively, these results demonstrate that JIB04 is an effective epigenetic drug capable of modulating organoid growth and enhancing the response to anti-PD-1 therapy.

JIB04 treatment enhances T cell cytotoxicity in PDOs and regulates in vivo pancreatic tumor growth

To further evaluate the therapeutic effects of JIB04 in organoids derived from three non-responders, H&E, IF, and immunohistochemistry (IHC) staining were performed. JIB04 treatment led to a reduction in Ki67 levels and induced pronounced cell death compared with the DMSO control group (figure 2A). Further analysis revealed differential responses to JIB04 among organoids derived from three non-responders and three normal pancreatic organoids. Notably, the IC50 of JIB04 in normal pancreas organoids was more than dozens of times higher than that in non-responder organoids, indicating its selective toxicity (figure 2B–D). Given the potential role of JIB04 in enhancing antitumor immunity, we further investigated its impact on cell viability in non-responder-derived organoids co-cultured with autologous T cells. We observed a notable reduction in organoid size and increased dead cell numbers when JIB04 was combined with anti-PD-1 therapy (figure 2E and F; online supplemental figure 1A–D, Supporting Information). Moreover, JIB04 administration enhanced the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells in the organoid-T cell co-culture system (figure 2G). In contrast, treatment of CD8+ T cells alone with JIB04 at the same concentration did not increase their cytotoxicity (figure 2H). These findings suggest that JIB04-induced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity is dependent on changes in tumor cells.

To further elucidate the antitumor effects of JIB04 in PDAC, we employed the murine PDAC cell line KPC-1, derived from the KPC mouse model (Pdx1-Cre; LSL-KrasG12D; Trp53R172H), to establish an orthotopic pancreatic cancer mouse model (figure 2I). JIB04 treatment led to a significant therapeutic response, as evidenced by markedly reduced tumor growth (figure 2J) and decreased tumor weight (figure 2K,L) compared with the untreated group. Moreover, histopathological analysis revealed a reduction in Ki67-positive cells following JIB04 treatment (figure 2M). Notably, mice receiving JIB04 exhibited a significantly prolonged survival compared with the DMSO group (figure 2N). Most importantly, JIB04-treated mice exhibited no significant changes in body weight, major organ morphology, complete blood count, or biochemical parameters, suggesting a favorable safety profile in vivo (figure 2O; online supplemental figure 2A–C, Supporting Information). These findings suggest that JIB04 exerts potent antitumor effects, and its induction of T cell-mediated cytotoxicity relies on tumor cell-intrinsic alterations.

JIB04 treatment induces ferroptosis by modulating chromatin accessibility

Given that JIB04 functions as a histone demethylase inhibitor and induces T cell-mediated cytotoxicity through tumor cell-intrinsic alterations, we performed ATAC-seq analysis in KPC-1 and KPC-3 to assess changes in chromatin accessibility. Quality assessment of the ATAC-seq sequencing results revealed high biological reproducibility in chromatin accessibility data (online supplemental figure 3A–C, Supporting Information). Next, we identified two distinct peaks in the distribution of differential chromatin accessibility regions relative to TSS, which were categorized into proximal and distal regions (online supplemental figure 3D, Supporting Information). Of note, the differential open regions were annotated for their locations, revealing a significant downregulation of chromatin accessibility, accounting for 94.73% of the total differential regions (figure 3A–C). To further investigate the regulatory relationship between chromatin accessibility and gene expression, we examined the proportion of genes showing concordant changes (either upregulated or downregulated) in both chromatin accessibility and transcript levels. Among these differentially expressed genes, 45.23% were associated with changes in proximal regions, while 54.82% were associated with distal regions (figure 3D). Correlation analysis revealed a positive association between chromatin accessibility and gene expression levels, with PCA of the RNA-seq data confirming reproducible clustering of biological replicates, indicating that JIB04 modulates gene expression by altering chromatin accessibility (figure 3E; online supplemental figure 3E–J, Supporting Information). GO enrichment analysis of the 3347 differential genes revealed that the proximal genes were associated with the activity of DNA-binding transcription factors, which is consistent with the observed changes in chromatin accessibility. Intriguingly, pathway analysis indicated that the differentially expressed genes in the distal region were linked to leukocyte migration, leukocyte chemotaxis, reactive oxygen species metabolic processes, iron ion transport, and glutathione metabolic processes (figure 3F). These results suggest that the tumor cell alterations induced by JIB04, along with the subsequent T cell-mediated cytotoxicity, are primarily driven by changes in distal regulatory regions.

To elucidate the cell death pathway, we employed inhibitors specifically targeting apoptosis, necroptosis, and ferroptosis. The results indicated that each inhibitor blocked cell death to varying degrees induced by JIB04. Notably, the ferroptosis inhibitor, DFO, showed the most significant suppression of JIB04-induced cell death (online supplemental figure 4A,B, Supporting Information), which is consistent with the previous sequencing results (figure 3F). Furthermore, JIB04 treatment led to a dose-dependent increase in lipid radicals and MDA levels, accompanied by intracellular accumulation of ferrous ions (figure 3G–I, online supplemental figure 4C,D, Supporting Information). Concurrently, mitochondrial morphology was altered and intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) levels were elevated (figure 3J–K, online supplemental figure 4E,F, Supporting Information). In contrast, the expression of GPX4, a key enzyme that detoxifies lipid peroxides and protects against ferroptosis, was reduced following JIB04 treatment (online supplemental figure 4G, Supporting Information). IHC staining showed higher levels of 4-HNE, a lipid peroxidation byproduct commonly used as a ferroptosis marker, in organoids derived from three non-responders after JIB04 treatment (figure 3L). Further validation through IF confirmed that the fluorescence intensity of C11-BODIPY, a fluorescent probe for detecting lipid ROS, was significantly increased in PDOs (figure 3M). In addition, using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry, we detected an increase in total iron levels in orthotopic tumors treated with JIB04 (figure 3N). Meanwhile, orthotopic tumors treated with JIB04 exhibited increased expression of the lipid peroxidation marker 4-HNE (online supplemental figure 4H, Supporting Information). Together, these findings strongly suggest that the cell death observed following JIB04 treatment is ferroptosis driven by lipid peroxidation.

JIB04-mediated Nrf2 suppression induces ferroptosis through depletion of its target gene.

To elucidate the mechanism underlying JIB04-induced ferroptosis, we conducted a joint analysis of ATAC-seq and transcriptome data from DMSO-treated and JIB04-treated tumor cells. Considering that chromatin accessibility influences transcription factor-DNA interactions, and that tumor cell alterations induced by JIB04, along with the subsequent T cell-mediated cytotoxicity, are primarily driven by changes in distal regulatory regions, we performed motif enrichment analysis on the differential chromatin accessibility regions surrounding these distal differentially expressed gene sets and constructed a regulatory network for these transcription factors and their downstream targets based on the TRRUST database (https://www.grnpedia.org/trrust/). Strikingly, we identified three major transcription factor regulatory modules in the distal regions (figure 4A). Further functional analyses of these transcription factor regulatory modules revealed that Nrf2 is primarily involved in biological processes related to oxidative stress response, cellular response to metal ions, and response to iron ions, among others (figure 4B). Moreover, consistent with the decreased chromatin accessibility induced by JIB04, the expression levels of Nrf2 in tumor cells were also reduced following JIB04 treatment (figure 4C–D, online supplemental figure 5A, Supporting Information). Delving deeper, we observed that overexpression of Nrf2 resulted in a significant reduction in MDA levels in KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells following JIB04 treatment (figure 4E, online supplemental figure 5B,C, Supporting Information). Further validation using the fluorescent probe C11-BODIPY confirmed that Nrf2 overexpression significantly reversed the increase in lipid peroxidation levels in KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells after JIB04 treatment (figure 4F–G). These findings suggest that JIB04-induced ferroptosis in tumor cells is mediated by Nrf2.

To elucidate the potential mechanism by which JIB04 mediates ferroptosis through Nrf2 inhibition, we conducted further analysis of the aforementioned transcription factor regulatory network. On analyzing the distal regulatory pathways of Nrf2, we found that Slc40a1, also known as Ferroportin 1, appeared with the highest frequency in the iron homeostasis pathway (figure 4H). Next, we examined the local landscape of Slc40a1 and observed a decrease in chromatin accessibility surrounding the Slc40a1 region following treatment (figure 4I). We further validated that JIB04 treatment decreased the expression of Slc40a1 at both the mRNA and protein levels (figure 4J, online supplemental figure 5D–F, Supporting Information). Most importantly, overexpression of Slc40a1 led to a significant reduction in MDA and lipid peroxidation levels in KPC-1 and KPC-3 cells following JIB04 treatment (figure 4K–M, online supplemental figure 5,G,H, Supporting Information). Consistent with the above results, we observed a decrease in the expression levels of Nrf2 and Slc40a1 in organoids derived from three non-responders and orthotopic tumors following JIB04 treatment, with a corresponding correlation (figure 4N–P). We then investigated how Nrf2 activates Slc40a1 transcription. Considering the reduction in chromatin accessibility across three distal regions of Slc40a1 (figure 4I), we first used HOMER for motif enrichment analysis in these regions and found that two Nrf2 motifs were highly enriched (figure 4Q). Moreover, western blot analysis indicated that Nrf2 overexpression upregulated the protein levels of Slc40a1, suggesting that Slc40a1 serves as a potential downstream target (online supplemental figure 4R,S). ChIP-quantitative PCR analysis further validated the binding of Nrf2 to the region of Slc40a1 (figure 4T).

JIB04 promotes CD8+ T cell infiltration via tumor cell ferroptosis

Based on the aforementioned findings, the differentially expressed genes influenced by chromatin accessibility following JIB04 treatment were enriched in immune-related pathways (figure 3F, online supplemental figure 6A, Supporting Information). Given that its induction of T cell-mediated cytotoxicity relies on tumor cell-intrinsic alterations, we hypothesized that JIB04-induced ferroptosis in tumor cells might facilitate the activation of a cold TME. To investigate this, we performed bulk RNA sequencing on orthotopic tumors (online supplemental figure 6B, Supporting Information). PCA confirmed reproducible clustering of biological replicates and separation between treatment groups (online supplemental figure 6C, Supporting Information). Enrichment analysis (GSEA) of JIB04-treated orthotopic tumors revealed significant enrichment in pathways related to leukocyte-mediated immunity, particularly T cell activation. Moreover, ion transmembrane transport and ion homeostasis were also enriched (online supplemental figure 6D, Supporting Information). To further delineate the tumor-immune transcriptional landscape in an unbiased manner, we conducted scRNA-seq on orthotopic tumors. The UMAP algorithm was applied to visualize cellular cluster distributions, with cell identities annotated based on the expression of well-established marker genes (figure 5A, online supplemental figure 6E–H, Supporting Information). Notable shifts in immune cell infiltration were observed in JIB04-treated tumors, including a significant increase in total immune cells (CD45+) (figure 5B). To identify the key contributors through which JIB04 treatment modulates the cold TME, we analyzed the most divergent cell populations between the two groups using similarity metrics. Strikingly, natural killer (NK) cells and T cells exhibited the greatest divergence between the two conditions (figure 5C). Moreover, pathway enrichment analysis using GSVA revealed that immune activation-related pathways, including the positive regulation of leukocyte-mediated immunity, regulation of leukocyte-mediated immunity, and interferon-gamma production, were enriched in the NK/T cell group (online supplemental figure 6I, Supporting Information). These findings suggest that the NK and T cell group may play a crucial role in JIB04-induced modulation of the cold TME. We further investigated the interactions between NK cells, T cell subpopulations, and tumor cells using CellChat (V.2.1.2). Interaction network analysis revealed that JIB04 treatment enhanced the communication between tumor cells and CD8+ T cells (figure 5D and E). Next, we examined NK and T cell infiltration in orthotopic tumors using flow cytometry analysis. Specifically, JIB04-treated tumors exhibited a substantial increase in both the proportion and absolute number of CD8+ T cells compared with the DMSO group, as confirmed by IF staining (figure 5F–H, online supplemental figure 7, Supporting Information). Additionally, while a slight increase in the proportion of NK cells and CD4+ T cells within the tumor, their absolute numbers remained relatively unchanged (figure 5F–G). More importantly, JIB04 treatment enhanced CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity within tumors, as indicated by elevated IFNγ levels (figure 5I).

Given that ferroptotic tumor cells are capable of releasing immunostimulatory signals, we next examined the release of representative damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) in patient-derived organoids. JIB04 treatment significantly increased the extracellular levels of HMGB1, CRT, and ATP in organoid supernatants, and these effects were markedly reversed by co-treatment with DFO (online supplemental figure 7B–D, Supporting Information). These findings indicate that JIB04 promotes ferroptosis-associated DAMP signaling, thereby providing a mechanistic basis for enhanced immune activation. To further assess whether JIB04-induced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity depends on tumor cell ferroptosis, we treated non-responder-derived organoids co-cultured with autologous T cells with both DFO and JIB04. Notably, DFO significantly reversed the JIB04-induced increase in PI signaling within the organoids and was accompanied by a lower presence of T cells surrounding the organoids (figure 5J). In vivo, DFO treatment rescued tumor growth in JIB04-treated orthotopic tumors, accompanied by a significant reduction in intratumoral CD8+ T cell infiltration (figure 5K–Q) and a decrease in CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity within the tumors (figure 5R). Moreover, DFO treatment reversed the reduction in Ki67-positive cells observed following JIB04 treatment (online supplemental figure 7E, Supporting Information). Additionally, mice across all four treatment groups exhibited no significant changes in body weight, major organ morphology, complete blood count and biochemical parameters, indicating a favorable safety profile in vivo (figure 5S, online supplemental figure 7G–H, Supporting Information).

JIB04 synergizes with PD-1 blockade immunotherapy.

Subsequently, we depleted CD8+ T cells in tumor-bearing mice, which resulted in the rescue of tumor growth and reduction in Ki67 levels (figure 6A–D, online supplemental figure 8A,B, Supporting Information). Additionally, all treatment modalities exhibited a favorable safety profile in vivo (figure 6E, online supplemental figure 8C,D, Supporting Information). Notably, depletion of CD8+ T cells in tumor-bearing mice reversed the prolonged survival induced by JIB04 treatment (figure 6F). These findings highlight the critical role of CD8+ T cells in mediating the antitumoral effects of JIB04 treatment. Given the pivotal role of JIB04 treatment in antitumor immunity, we further explored its impact on modulating the tumor cell response to anti-PD-1 therapy (figure 6G–J). Moreover, all treatment modalities demonstrated a favorable safety profile in vivo (figure 6K, online supplemental figure 8E,F, Supporting Information). By the end of the observation period (50 days), no mouse deaths were observed in the combination treatment group (figure 6L). Next, we assessed CD8+ T cell infiltration in orthotopic tumors using flow cytometry and found that JIB04-treated tumors exhibited a significant increase in both the proportion and absolute number of CD8+ T cells compared with the DMSO group (figure 6M–N, online supplemental figure 8E, Supporting Information). Additionally, the combination treatment further reduced Ki67 levels compared with JIB04 treatment alone in orthotopic tumors, while both treatments demonstrated favorable safety profiles (online supplemental figure 1, Supporting Information). We also observed that JIB04 and the combination treatment significantly downregulated Nrf2 and Slc40a1 expression, accompanied by increased CD8+ T cell infiltration in orthotopic tumors, which is consistent with our previous findings (figure 6O). We next validated the correlation between Nrf2, Slc40a1, and CD8 expression and the efficacy of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy in patients with PDAC (figure 6P). Patients who responded positively to anti-PD-1 therapy exhibited reduced Nrf2 and Slc40a1 expression, as well as increased CD8 expression (figure 6Q–R). These results suggest that patients with PDAC with low Nrf2 and Slc40a1 expression may benefit more from anti-PD-1 therapy.

Discussion

PDAC is notoriously refractory to immunotherapy, and the limited efficacy of ICB has prompted clinical efforts to combine ICB with chemotherapy, based on the hypothesis that chemotherapy can be immunogenic. Nevertheless, to date, such combination regimens have not demonstrated superior clinical activity in PDAC compared with chemotherapy alone. Therefore, exploring novel therapeutic approaches that can enable patients with PDAC to benefit from ICB is crucial. Tumor organoid models, notably PDOs, have emerged as a key driver in the advancement of precision medicine. Their capacity to retain the genetic and physiological characteristics of the original tumor provides unprecedented opportunities for drug screening, preclinical validation, and clinical response prediction in oncology. Precision medicine approaches for advanced patients with PDAC receiving anti‐PD‐1 therapy are challenging because the majority of these patients are typically inoperable. Our study is the first to establish a PDO-based drug-screening platform using EUS-FNA specimens from patients unresponsive to anti-PD-1 therapy. We established PDOs from three of four EUS-FNA specimens, corresponding to a 75% success rate. In particular, PDOs faithfully retain the histopathological characteristics of their derived primary PDACs. Moreover, the entire process, from establishing PDOs to conducting drug screening, was completed in under 1 month. This rapid turnaround time undoubtedly enables timely testing in real clinical scenarios.

One biomarker generally predictive of response to immunotherapy is the degree of T cell infiltration into the TME. Moreover, higher densities of CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes surrounding tumor cells correlate with improved patient survival. Consistent with these, our finding revealed that patients who responded positively to anti-PD-1 therapy exhibited significantly greater CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocyte infiltration, which correlated with prolonged OS. Given the critical role of interactions between T cells within the TME and tumor cells in predicting responses to anti-PD-1 therapy, we conducted co-culture assays using autologous T cells and PDOs. Distinct from previous studies, our study is the first to establish co-culture assays using autologous T cells and PDOs generated from EUS-FNA specimens of patients unresponsive to anti-PD-1 therapy. This platform, which preserves defined tumor features and cell components, enables drug screening and prediction of clinical responses to immunotherapy.

Epigenetic regulators have recently been implicated in immune escape and sensitivity to immunotherapy, presenting a promising strategy for combining epigenetic therapies with immunotherapy in PDAC. Here, we aimed to identify an epigenetic inhibitor that could potentiate anti-PD-1 therapy by reprogramming the transcriptional program of pancreatic cancer cells, using a high-throughput drug screening platform based on the functional interaction between non-responder-derived organoids and autologous T cells. Consistent with the clinical treatment responses of the patients, all three co-culture assays established using autologous T cells and organoids derived from non-responders exhibited no response to anti-PD-1 therapy, underscoring the utility of this model as a reliable platform for identifying effective combinatorial immunotherapies. Here, we identified the epigenetic inhibitor JIB04, in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy, as demonstrating the greatest efficacy in co-culture assays. Moreover, JIB04 treatment induced greater organoid cell death and was accompanied by increased accumulation of T cells surrounding the organoids. JIB04 treatment markedly enhanced the cytotoxic activity of autologous T cells against PDOs in the organoid–T cell co-culture system, whereas it had no effect on T cell cytotoxicity in the absence of co-culture with non-responder-derived organoids, suggesting that JIB04-induced T cell-mediated cytotoxicity is contingent on tumor cell-intrinsic changes.

JIB04 has previously been shown to exert cancer-specific cytotoxicity. However, its role in adaptive immunity has not yet been defined. Previous studies have highlighted a critical role for ferroptosis in modulating immune cell infiltration and determining the efficacy of immunotherapy. Our analysis of high-throughput sequencing data revealed that JIB04 inhibited tumor growth in vivo in a ferroptosis-dependent manner and enhanced CD8+ T cell infiltration. Moreover, this ferroptosis-associated immune modulation was recapitulated in non-responder-derived organoids. Mechanistically, we constructed a regulatory network of these transcription factors and their downstream targets, identifying Nrf2 as a potential target of JIB04. As a central regulator of the antioxidant defense system, Nrf2 orchestrates the expression of genes that counteract oxidative stress and preserve redox balance within cells. Our study revealed that JIB04 treatment downregulated Nrf2 expression and reduced chromatin accessibility at distal regulatory regions of its downstream iron exporter gene, Slc40a1—the only known iron exporter responsible for releasing free iron from the labile iron pool (LIP) to prevent ferroptosis. Notably, we identified a novel Nrf2 binding site in the distal regulatory regions of the mouse Slc40a1 gene. Of note, in murine PDAC models, JIB04 treatment reshaped the tumor immune microenvironment, marked by a substantial infiltration of immune cells into tumor tissues—particularly an increased abundance and activity of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells. These effects were reversible on administration of the iron chelator DFO. Consistently, analysis of patients with PDAC who responded favorably to ICB therapy revealed lower expression levels of Nrf2 and Slc40a1 in tumor tissues compared with non-responders. Therefore, our study provides compelling evidence that targeting epigenetic regulators to modulate free iron levels in the LIP can reshape antitumor immune responses and enhance the efficacy of immunotherapy.

The ultimate goal of immuno-oncology is to develop treatment strategies with a favorable therapeutic window—approaches that selectively target tumor cells while sparing healthy tissues. A significant limitation of conventional chemotherapeutic agents lies in their lack of tumor specificity, often resulting in unintended toxicities due to their indiscriminate effects on both malignant and healthy cells. Previous studies have demonstrated that JIB04 induces cancer-specific cell death. Consistent with these findings, our data showed that JIB04-treated mice exhibited no significant changes in body weight, major organ histology, complete blood count and biochemical parameters. Furthermore, the IC50 of JIB04 in normal pancreatic organoids was over an order of magnitude higher than that observed in organoids derived from non-responders. Moreover, the concept that Nrf2 inhibition preferentially targets cancer cells is supported by prior findings: basal Nrf2 expression is low in normal cells and tissues, whereas elevated Nrf2 levels are commonly observed in tumors to adapt to the hostile microenvironment. This creates a therapeutic window by rendering tumors more vulnerable to ferroptosis. In addition, both in vivo and in vitro data demonstrated that JIB04 treatment did not impair CD8+ T-cell activity. Instead, it significantly promoted CD8+ T-cell infiltration and activation within tumors and the organoid-T cell co-culture system, confirming that JIB04 does not induce immune suppression and further supporting its safety for therapeutic use.

In this study, we established a standardized platform using autologous T cells to engage non-responder-derived organoids for high-throughput drug screening and precision therapy. Through this screening, we identified the histone demethylase inhibitor JIB04, which modulates the LIP by downregulating Nrf2 and reducing chromatin accessibility at distal regulatory regions of its downstream iron exporter gene, Slc40a1, in cancer cells. This epigenetic reprogramming impaired iron efflux, promoted intracellular iron accumulation, and triggered ferroptosis in tumor cells. Ferroptotic stress, in turn, promoted the formation of a T cell-inflamed TME. Overall, we discovered a novel epigenetic combination therapy that induces ferroptosis in tumor cells, thereby reshaping antitumor immune responses and enhancing the efficacy of immunotherapy.
