Advances and Strategies in Enhancing mRNA Cancer Vaccines

Abstract

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccine has emerged as a promising strategy in cancer immunotherapy, enabling the induction of precise and robust immune responses against tumor antigens. Nevertheless, enormous challenges remain, in particular its limitations in achieving optimal immunogenicity and therapeutic efficacy, result in modest clinical benefit. Here, the biological barriers are reviewed that impede the functionality of mRNA cancer vaccine and describe the design considerations, including structure engineering, chemical modification, and development of next‐generation delivery materials for enhancing cancer immunotherapy. Recent advances are highlighted aimed at improving vaccine efficacy through targeted delivery, modulation of immune cell interactions, and stimulation of innate immune responses. Finally, the challenges are examined in clinical translation and explore future directions for improving the therapeutic potential of mRNA cancer vaccines. By synthesizing current progress and identifying critical hurdles, this review provides a framework for advancing mRNA‐based cancer immunotherapy.

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines offer a powerful approach for cancer immunotherapy, but their clinical impact remains limited by delivery challenges and suboptimal immune activation. This review discusses key biological barriers and design strategies—including structural optimization, immunomodulation, organ targeting delivery, and advanced nanocarriers—to enhance vaccine efficacy. Emerging innovations and translational challenges are highlighted to guide next‐generation mRNA cancer vaccine development.  

Introduction

In recent years, mRNA vaccines have emerged as a transformative strategy in the field of cancer immunotherapy, garnering significant attention due to their potential to generate robust and specific immune responses against cancers.[  ] The ability of mRNA to serve as a template for protein synthesis in both proliferating and non‐proliferating cells, coupled with its lack of genomic integration risk, positions it as a highly favorable candidate for vaccine development.[  ] mRNA cancer vaccines operate by delivering synthetic mRNA encoding tumor‐associated antigens (TAAs) or tumor specific antigens (TSA) directly into host cells, enabling them to produce these antigens endogenously. This approach leverages the body's own machinery to prime an adaptive immune response, with the synthesized tumor antigens being presented on major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, leading to the activation of tumor‐specific cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs).[  ] 

The rise of mRNA technology, particularly following the success of mRNA‐based COVID‐19 vaccines, has further accelerated interest in exploring its potential for cancer treatment.[  ] While the therapeutic promise of mRNA vaccines has been demonstrated in various clinical trials, there is no mRNA cancer vaccine approved yet. This is largely due to several critical challenges: mRNA is prone to rapid enzymatic degradation,[  ] swift clearance in vivo,[  ] and ineffective targeting to appropriate immune cells.[  ] Moreover, achieving optimal immunogenicity and efficacy—especially the activation of CD8+ T cells—remains a key hurdle. Although exogenous mRNA is inherently immunostimulatory,[  ] modifications aimed at enhancing its translation and reducing inflammatory responses often result in lower innate immune activation.[  ] A well‐balanced activation of both innate and adaptive immunity is crucial for mounting effective anti‐tumor response and overcoming the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME).[  ] Therefore, appropriate strategies to improve delivery efficiency of antigen‐encoding mRNA to antigen‐presenting cells (APCs) and enhance its immunogenicity play critical roles in developing mRNA cancer vaccines. These strategies include encapsulating mRNA in appropriate delivery systems, directing mRNA vaccines to targeted immune organs, facilitating the endocytosis by immune cells, and stimulating the APCs with suitable adjuvants.

Well‐designed delivery system is essential in enhancing the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines. They enhance the stability of mRNA,[  ] facilitate targeted delivery to APCs,[  ] and can even function as adjuvants to boost immune responses.[  ] Lipid and lipid like nanoparticles, liposomes, and polymeric nanoparticles are among the various delivery systems currently being explored, each offering unique advantages in terms of biodistribution, cellular uptake, and immune activation. Additionally, the choice of administration route plays a pivotal role in determining vaccine efficacy, as different delivery routes expose the vaccine to distinct immune environments, such as different immune organs, influencing the interaction of mRNA vaccine with immune cells.[  ] Moreover, the combination of antigen‐encoding mRNA with adjuvants holds considerable potential for optimizing immunogenicity, as adjuvants can enhance the activation of innate immune pathways, which in turn strengthens the antigen‐specific adaptive immune response.

While existing reviews have highlighted advances in mRNA vaccines for cancer therapy, a comprehensive perspective summarizing key factors influencing the action of mRNA cancer vaccine remains lacking. Here, we aim to provide a systemic overview of current advancements in mRNA cancer vaccine and a conceptual framework to address challenges facing mRNA cancer vaccine development. We first explore various types of mRNA vaccines and their mechanisms of action in cancer immunotherapy. We then summarize the engineering strategies to enhance the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccine, including mRNA packaging and delivery via various types of nanoparticles, directing mRNA vaccines to lymphoid organs, altering the way mRNA engage with immune cells, accumulating mRNA in immune cells, and enhancing innate adaptive immune responses. Finally, we elucidate the critical elements required to unlock the full therapeutic potential of mRNA cancer vaccines and outline future directions for their development in the fight against cancer.

Challenges to the Success of mRNA Cancer Vaccines

Despite promising developments, the translations of mRNA cancer vaccines are mainly in early phase clinical studies (Table  1 ). In this section, we discuss the key challenges faced by mRNA cancer vaccines and outline strategies to enhance their immunogenicity, including the optimization of delivery vehicles, administration routes, lymphoid organ targeting, and adjuvant selection.

A major challenge in cancer immunotherapy is overcoming the inherent low immunogenicity, an essential factor restricting the immune system to recognize tumor antigens and elicit an effective antitumor response.[  ] Tumors with high immunogenicity, known as “hot tumors”, are distinguished by pro‐inflammatory cytokine production, high levels of T cell infiltration, elevated PD‐L1 expression, and a high mutational burden. These features render them more susceptible to immune attack and responsive to immunotherapy.[  ] Conversely, “cold tumors” display low immunogenicity, limited immune cell infiltration, and resistance to immune therapies. For effective cancer immunotherapy, robust activation of CD8+ T cells is essential, as these cells play a critical role in identifying and eliminating tumor cells.[  ] This activation requires two primary signals delivered by APCs: antigen presentation (signal 1) and co‐stimulatory signals (signal 2). Once administered, mRNA encapsulated by nanoparticles are taken up by APCs, such as dendritic cells (DCs), through endocytosis.[  ] Inside the cell, mRNAs are translated into the target antigen peptide or protein in the cytoplasm (Figure  1 ). The expressed antigens engage both B and T cells, enhancing antigen presentation (signal 1) for tumor immunotherapy. However, insufficient co‐stimulatory signals (signal 2) can lead to weak or ineffective immune responses and impaired T cell activation.[  ] Therefore, it is crucial to increase immunogenicity for mRNA vaccines. Numerous strategies have been employed to achieve this,[  ] including the selection of appropriate packaging materials, optimization of administration routes, targeting of secondary immune organs, and the use of suitable adjuvants.

Nanoparticles protect mRNA and immunostimulants from enzymatic degradation and rapid clearance, and facilitate their delivery to key immune sites, such as lymph nodes and spleen. Beyond protective function, some packaging materials act as self‐adjuvants, enhancing the immune response by mimicking pathogen‐associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). For instance, polyethyleneimine (PEI),[  ] chitosan,[  ] and LNPs[  ] effectively deliver mRNA while simultaneously triggering innate immune signaling, thereby enhancing the overall vaccine response. The strategic combination of delivery vehicles and immunostimulants maximizes the immune response by promoting sustained interaction with immune cells, leading to prolonged antigen presentation and co‐stimulation, thus significantly enhancing the potency of mRNA vaccine in cancer immunotherapy.

Beyond delivery vehicle, the selection of administration route significantly influences both the type and magnitude of the immune response elicited by mRNA vaccines.[  ] Different administration routes—such as intramuscular (IM), intravenous (IV), and subcutaneous (SC)—expose mRNA vaccines to distinct physiological environments, influencing their cellular uptake, clearance rates, and interactions with immune cells.[  ] Switching the vaccination route from IM to SC has turned out to reduce pro‐inflammatory reactions without compromising humoral immunity. However, the SC route elicited a stronger cellular immune response compared to IM administration.[  ] Optimal mRNA vaccine performance requires careful selection of the administration route to ensure its effective biodistribution and engagement with the immune system.

Targeting lymphoid organs, where antigen presentation and immune cell activation occur, is critical for effective vaccine design. Lymphoid organs such as spleen, lymph nodes, and mucosa‐associated lymphoid tissues (MALT) serve as crucial sites for initiating adaptive immune responses. The in vivo fate of nanoparticles is highly dependent on their inherent physical and chemical characteristics. Achieving effective drug delivery to target organs while minimizing off‐target accumulation requires careful consideration of factors such as protein corona formation, systemic clearance, vascular extravasation, cellular internalization, and intracellular transport.[  ] Modulating physicochemical properties—such as particle size, surface charge, and the inclusion of targeting ligands—allows researchers to improve mRNA vaccine delivery to immune sites, thereby enhancing immune activation and sustaining long‐term responses.[  ] 

Moreover, the strategic combination of adjuvants plays a crucial role in enhancing the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines by amplifying innate immune activation.[  ] Adjuvants exert their effects by engaging pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) on APCs through the detection of PAMPs or damage‐associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). This interaction initiates a cascade of immune responses, including the upregulation of co‐stimulatory molecules such as CD40, CD80, and CD86, as well as the secretion of key cytokines including tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF‐α), interleukin‐6 (IL‐6), IL‐10, and IL‐12. These responses are critical for APC maturation and the subsequent activation of naïve CD8⁺ T cells.[  ] 

Development of mRNA‐Based Cancer Vaccines

Types of mRNA Vaccine Payload

There are four major types of mRNA vaccines, including conventional linear mRNA, circular mRNA (circRNA), virally derived self‐amplifying mRNA (saRNA) and trans‐amplifying mRNA (taRNA) (Figure  2 ). Linear mRNA vaccines encode the antigen of interest and contain 5′ and 3′ untranslated regions (UTRs), whereas saRNA and taRNA encode both antigen and viral replication machinery that enables intracellular RNA amplification and increased protein expression. CircRNAs are single‐stranded RNAs with a covalently linked head‐to‐tail closed‐loop structure, and they lack both a 5′ cap and a 3′ polyadenylated tail (poly(A) tail).

Conventional linear mRNA is single‐stranded RNA molecule synthesized from a linear DNA template using T7, T3, or Sp6 phage RNA polymerase through in vitro transcription (IVT).[  ] Structurally, these IVT mRNA resembles endogenous mRNA, consisting of five regions from 5′ to 3′: a 5′ cap, a 5′ UTR, an open reading frame (ORF) encoding target protein sequence, a 3′ UTR, and a poly(A) tail (Figure  3A).[  ] 5′ cap is a modified structure at the 5′ end of mRNA, featuring a 7‐methylguanosine (m7G) nucleotide linked via a triphosphate bridge (ppp) and often methylated at the 2′ position of the first nucleotide.[  ] 5′ cap protects mRNA from exonuclease degradation, and ensures efficient protein production.[  ] 5′ and 3′ UTRs flanking the coding sequence are crucial for mRNA stability and translation. 5ʹ UTR impacts stability and translation initiation,[  ] while the 3ʹ UTR affects mRNA half‐life.[  ] ORF, beginning with a start codon and ending with a stop codon, contains the coding sequence translated into protein by the ribosome, and can vary in length from a few hundred to several thousand nucleotides.[  ] The poly(A) tail is essential for mRNA stability, export, and translation.[  ] Its length (typically 100–150 nucleotides) is crucial for interaction with poly(A) binding proteins (PAB), which facilitate translation initiation and protect the cap from decapping enzymes.[  ] 

CircRNA is non‐replicating RNA characterized by a covalently closed‐loop structure that lacks both 5′ cap and 3′ poly(A) tail (Figure 3A).[  ] These circRNAs are produced from linear mRNA precursors via an intramolecular ring‐forming reaction, achieved through chemical, enzymatic, or ribozymatic methods. Core elements of linear RNA precursors include an internal ribosome entry site (IRES) and an ORF. IRES elements, located upstream of the start codon, allowing for binding of the 40S ribosomal subunit and enabling translation initiation in a cap‐independent manner.[  ] Translation is initiated when the ribosome binds to the start codon within the ORF and continues until it reaches the stop codon.[  ] This process persists as long as the circRNA remains intact. Although circRNAs lack traditional 5′ and 3′ UTRs, the sequences flanking the IRES‐ORF cassette can be engineered to function as UTR analogs, inducing the recruitment of RNA‐binding proteins (RBPs) and promoting efficient translation.[  ] Compared to conventional linear mRNA vaccines—the application of which are often impeded by instability, suboptimal translation efficiency, and undesired immunogenicity—circRNA vaccines incorporating IRESs and ORFs present significant advantages, such as enhanced stability, resistance to exonucleases and prolonged protein expression, making it a promising alternative in terms of safety, stability, manufacturing simplicity, and scalability.[  ] 

SaRNA leverages RNA‐dependent RNA polymerases from RNA viruses to replicate the delivered RNA within cells, thereby enhancing antigen protein production (Figure 3A).[  ] Most saRNAs are derived from alphavirus genomes, such as Sindbis or Semliki Forest viruses. In saRNA vaccines, the genes encoding the virus's structural proteins are replaced with the gene encoding the target antigen, while the genes responsible for RNA replication are retained.[  ] An saRNA vaccine comprises three main components: a viral RNA‐dependent RNA polymerase, non‐structural proteins, and mRNA encoding the target antigen.[  ] The viral polymerase amplifies mRNA transcripts, enabling substantial antigen expression from minimal vaccine doses.[  ] Furthermore, saRNA vaccines inherently generate adjuvant‐like effects through double‐stranded RNA (dsRNA) structures, replication intermediates, and other motifs, contributing to their high potency. One limitation of saRNA vaccines is their large and complex structure. The sequence encoding viral proteins alone is approximately 7 kb, often bringing the total length of saRNA vaccines to over 9 kb. To address this challenge, recent strategies involve separating the sequences for non‐structural proteins and the antigen gene into separate RNA molecules, creating a trans‐amplifying or trans‐replicating system (Figure 3A).[  ] This trans‐amplifying approach offers potential advantages in versatility, safety, and scalability for large‐scale production. Comparative studies show that taRNA vaccines achieve translation efficiency similar to saRNA vaccines, highlighting the promise of taRNA for further development.[  ] 

Structure Design and Modification of mRNA Cancer Vaccine

Codon Optimization

Codon composition is known to influence translation efficiency. The current strategy to enhance translation efficiency involves replacing rare codons with synonymous, frequently used codons that correspond to abundant cognate transfer RNA (tRNA) in the cytosol, while encoding the same amino acids.[  ] Although replacing rare codons is a promising optimization strategy, it should be applied judiciously. This is because, for certain proteins, the slower translation rates associated with rare codons are crucial for proper protein folding and functional integrity.[  ] In addition, increasing secondary structure of the ORF lengthens mRNA half‐life, which also improves protein expression.[  ] Moreover, increasing the guanine plus cytosine (GC) content of the ORF in mRNA vaccine can help evade detection by PRRs, thereby enhancing mRNA stability and allowing for efficient translation for sufficient protein production.[  ] 

5′ Cap Optimization

Robust translation of mRNA requires a functional 5′ cap structure. The modification of the natural eukaryotic 5′ caps structure 7‐methylguanosine (m7G) is an essential approach to improve mRNA translation. At present, the anti‐reverse cap analog (ARCA) structure is most commonly used structure for capping mRNA vaccines.[  ] However, the ARCA capping approach suffers from relatively low capping efficiency (60–80%).[  ] The next generation co‐transcriptional cap analog, CleanCap™, has successfully addressed the limitations associated with ARCA and exhibited further improved translation efficiency.[  ] Additionally, several other enhanced 5′ caps are under development. It has previously been reported that the translation efficiency is significantly enhanced by replacing the m7G with 7‐benzylated guanosine, and this enhancement is further augmented 2‐fold by attaching the m7G to another m7G through a tetraphosphate bridge (m7Gppppm7G).[  ] In another study, a novel 5′ cap analog, N6,2’‐O‐dimethyladenosine‐capped 7‐methylguanosine triphosphate with benzyl group (m6Am‐cap‐m7GpppBn6AmpG) was developed. This innovation facilitated the separation of capped mRNA from uncapped mRNA by reversed‐phase high‐performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), while also extending mRNA half‐life and enhancing protein expression.[  ] 

5′ and 3′ UTR Optimization

Another strategy to improve mRNA translation efficiency involves incorporating regulatory sequence elements into 5′ and 3′ UTRs. These regulatory sequences can be derived from viral or eukaryotic genes and greatly increase the half‐life and expression of therapeutic mRNAs.[  ] UTR sequences from highly expressed genes, such as human α‐ and β‐ globin genes, are widely used for mRNA vaccine synthesis since mRNAs containing these UTRs normally show high levels of translation and stability.[  ] In addition, it is reported that the stabilization effect of human β‐globin 3′‐UTR sequences is further augmented by using two human β‐globin 3′‐UTRs arranged in a headtotail orientation.[  ] Although many 5′ and 3′ UTRs can independently enhance mRNA translation, a rational combination of 5′ and 3′ UTRs can maximize the translation efficiency.[  ] 

Poly(A) Tail Optimization

Poly(A) tail is a critical post‐transcriptional modification of mRNA that significantly contributes to its stability, export, and translation.[  ] The length of poly(A) tail indirectly regulates both mRNA half‐life and translation. In eukaryotic cells, the natural poly(A) tail length ranges from 50–250 nucleotides.[  ] The optimal length of the poly(A) tail for mRNA vaccine ranges between 100 and 150 nucleotides, as this size offers adequate binding sites for PAB while minimizing potential issues such as immune activation due to excessive length or reduced synthesis efficiency.[  ] Holtkamp et al. investigated the impact of poly(A) tail length on mRNA translation efficiency and found that mRNA with a 120‐nucleotide poly(A) tail exhibited greater intracellular stability compared to shorter variants (Figure 3A).[  ] However, the DNA sequences that encode these long poly(A) stretches can destabilize the DNA plasmids designed for transcription. One potential solution to address this stability issue is to incorporate a short UGC linker into the poly(A) tail.[  ] For instance, the Pfizer‐BioNTech vaccine BNT162b2 against SARS‐CoV‐2 uses this strategy and contains a 10 bp UGC linker to produce the sequence A30 (10 bp UGC linker) A70. In subsequent studies, Chen et al. designed and synthesized topologically and chemically modified mRNAs with multiple synthetic poly(A) tails.[  ] These multitailed mRNAs exhibited significantly enhanced translational efficiency, producing approximately 4.7‐ to 19.5‐fold higher luminescence signals than conventional mRNAs from 24 to 72 hours post‐transfection in vitro. Protein expression duration was extended to 14 days after multiple poly(A) tail modification, compared to less than 7 days observed with unmodified mRNA.

Nucleoside Modifications

Exogenous mRNA can be recognized by innate immune receptors, triggering degradation and thereby inhibiting antigen expression.[  ] To overcome this challenge, modified nucleosides are typically added during IVT of tumor antigen‐encoding mRNAs to reduce immunogenicity,[  ] thereby improving translation efficiency.[  ] Key nucleoside modifications (Figure 3B) that enhance mRNA stability and translation include pseudouridine (ψ), 5‐methylcytidine (m5C), N1‐methylpseudouridine (m1Ψ), 5‑methyluridine (m5U), 5‐methoxyuridine (mo5U), 2‐thiouracil (s2U), and N6‐methyladenosine (m6A).[  ] 

CircRNA

CircRNA does not contain a free 5′ cap or poly(A) tails and is, thus, unsusceptible to degradation by nucleases and more stable than linear RNA.[  ] Furthermore, circRNA without a stop codon reduces the frequency of ribosome detachment from the RNA, thereby enabling continuous translation and high protein expression. Therefore, circRNA can be used to improve mRNA translation efficiency and enhance mRNA cancer vaccine efficacy. For instance, Li et al. developed a circRNA vaccine platform that enhanced the stability of RNA vaccine and facilitated sustained antigen expression.[  ] The generated circRNA vaccines produced effectively triggered robust activation of innate and adaptive immune response, demonstrating superior anti‐tumor efficacy in multiple mouse tumor models. In addition, Zhang et al. recently developed antigen‐encoding small circRNAs, typically under 300 nucleotides, to reduce the activation of protein kinase R (PKR), which otherwise impede the translation of mRNA vaccine.[  ] Compared to its linear counterpart, small circRNA demonstrated significantly enhanced biostability (Figure 3C–E). In another study, researchers developed a non‐invasive intranasal cancer vaccine utilizing circRNA encapsulated by LNPs to induce localized mucosal immune responses.[  ] This strategy elicited potent antitumor T cell responses in preclinical lung cancer models while mitigating the systemic adverse effects commonly associated with intravenous RNA vaccination.

SaRNA and taRNA

SaRNA and taRNA include an alphavirus‐based replicon capable of amplifying mRNA transcripts within cells, which enables substantial protein production over an extended period from an exceptionally low‐dose injection.[  ] Encouraged by these characteristics, saRNA and taRNA can also be used to improve mRNA translation efficiency and enhance mRNA cancer vaccine efficacy. An early study reported that a single dose of saRNA vaccines encoding a chimeric protein derived from the fusion of the human papillomavirus type 16 (HPV‐16) E7 oncoprotein and the herpes simplex virus type 1 glycoprotein D, led to robust control of tumor burden in two orthotopic murine models.[  ] Further study revealed that this saRNA vaccine outperformed both the protein vaccine and the electroporated DNA vaccine containing or encoding the same antigen. Silva found that unmodified saRNA (U‐sa‐mRNA) achieved over 10‐fold higher protein expression than both unmodified and m1Ψ‐modified non‐replicating mRNAs (U‐nr‐mRNA and M1ψ‐nr‐mRNA) in HEK 293T cells, with sustained luciferase activity for up to five days (Figure 3F).[  ] To suppress the IFN response, McGee and colleagues substituted saRNA with modified nucleotides at 100% substitution, which confer innate immune evasion and robust long‐term protein expression.[  ] In a recent work by Chen et al., a saRNA named CARG‐2020, which carried a single‐chain IL‐12, short‐hairpin RNA (shRNA) targeting programmed death ligand 1 (PD‐L1), and a dominant‐negative form of IL‐17 receptor A (dn‐IL17RA), was developed and delivered by virus‐like vesicles.[  ] Intratumoral delivery of CARG‐2020 eradicated large established tumors (MC38 tumors with average size of 600 mm3 before treatment), prevented tumor recurrence and provided long‐term survival benefit to the tumor‐bearing mice. Beissert et al. (2020) developed a bipartite taRNA system in which the antigen‐encoding trans‐replicon is driven by a separately supplied replicase, achieving expression levels comparable to conventional saRNA.[  ] Subsequent optimization via promoter deletion, 5′UTR redesign, and directed evolution enhanced taRNA expression by accelerating replication.[  ] 

In summary, optimizing mRNA vaccine has evolved into a sophisticated engineering challenge that involves balancing multiple competing priorities—enhancing translational efficiency while ensuring proper protein folding, maximizing stability without compromising immunogenicity, and prolonging expression while preventing immune overactivation. Current strategies such as codon optimization, nucleoside modifications, and structural element engineering have achieved remarkable success, yet each approach presents inherent trade‐offs that demand more refined and nuanced solutions. Current advances on mRNA format alterations like circRNA and saRNA offer promising alternatives but introduce new complexities in manufacturing and immune regulation. The next frontier lies in developing integrated, AI‐driven design platforms that can simultaneously optimize multiple parameters while accounting for antigen‐specific requirements. Future breakthroughs will likely come from combining computational prediction models with high‐throughput experimental validation to create context‐aware mRNA architectures. These systems would feature dynamically regulated elements, smart stability controls, and precisely tuned immunogenic profiles tailored to specific therapeutic applications. These advanced mRNA cancer vaccines would represent a paradigm shift from empirical optimization to predictive design, enabling customized solutions that maintain the delicate balance between enhanced performance and biological fidelity across diverse clinical applications for cancer immunotherapy.

Delivery Materials of mRNA Cancer Vaccine

mRNA is large and negatively charged, which prevents it from passing through the anionic lipid bilayer of cell membranes.[  ] The single‐stranded nature of mRNA makes it highly susceptible to degradation, particularly in ribonuclease‐rich physiological environments.[  ] Consequently, effective delivery strategies are critical to protect mRNA from extracellular ribonucleases (RNase)‐mediated degradation and to facilitate its entry into cells and targeted tissues.[  ] Various delivery systems, including viral vector, LNPs, polymeric nanoparticles, and inorganic nanoparticles have been employed for mRNA delivery (Figure  4 ).

Viral Vector

Viral vectors have emerged as a potent tool for mRNA delivery due to their natural ability to efficiently infect cells and facilitate intracellular mRNA expression. Encapsulated within the protective viral capsid, mRNA can be delivered to target cells with high precision and effectiveness. Several viral vectors have been explored for in vivo mRNA delivery, including adenoviruses, lentiviruses, adeno‐associated viruses (AAVs), and Sendai viruses.[  ] Viral vectors are particularly notable for their high transduction efficiency, which ensures robust cellular uptake of mRNA, leading to potent and sustained protein expression. This makes them highly attractive for applications that require rapid and efficient mRNA expression. However, there are significant challenges associated with the use of viral vectors for mRNA delivery. One major concern is the potential induction of strong immune responses.[  ] The recognition of viral vectors by host immune system can lead to inflammatory reactions and, in some cases, rapid clearance of the vectors from circulation. This not only limits their therapeutic efficacy but also raises the possibility of adverse reactions and host rejection. Another safety concern is the risk of unintended genomic integration, especially with integrating vectors like lentiviruses, which could lead to oncogenic transformations or disruption of key genetic elements.[  ] Additionally, viral vectors are subject to size constraints, which can limit the delivery of larger mRNA sequences,[  ] thereby restricting their application for more complex therapeutic constructs.

LNP

Recent trends have increasingly favored the use of lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) for mRNA delivery.[  ] LNPs, typically range in size from 50 to 200 nm, are currently the most clinically advanced mRNA delivery vehicles. Aside from the RNA component, LNPs are typically composed of four components: an ionizable lipid, cholesterol, polyethylene glycol‐modified (PEGylated) lipid, and a helper lipid such as 1,2‐distearoyl‐sn‐glycero‐3 phosphocholine (DSPC).[  ] Among them, the ionizable lipids on the surface of LNPs exhibit cationic charges with their positively charged amine groups allow them to interact with the anionic RNA during particle formation at low pH (normally pH < 4).[  ] These cationic ionizable lipids have a net neutral surface charge at physiological pH during delivery, but they become positively charge at lower pH levels to facilitate membrane destabilization and endosome escape upon internalization.[  ] PEG‐lipids provide LNPs with a hydrophilic exterior that enhances their thermodynamic stability and functions as a steric barrier to prevent aggregation during storage and improve spatial stability,[  ] while cholesterol outside LNPs improves stability in vivo and promote membrane fusion.[  ] Neutral phospholipids such as DSPC provide bilayer structure and mediate mRNA encapsulation.[  ] A precise combination of these components in specific molar ratios allows the generation of LNPs with tailored functionalities for mRNA delivery.[  ] By incorporating either permanently cationic or anionic lipids into LNPs, selective organ targeting can be achieved—cationic lipids for lung targeting and anionic lipids for spleen targeting.[  ] Furthermore, adjusting the ratio of PEG‐lipids enables fine‐tuning of LNP size, with a 30 nm diameter being optimal for lymph node accumulation, as demonstrated by a study conducted by Harashima et al.[  ] Empty LNPs can act as adjuvants,[  ] with their composition and physicochemical properties influencing their immunogenicity.[  ] Ionizable lipids can trigger innate immune responses, such as IL‐1 release. SM‐102 based LNPs (Spikevax) have been reported to induce higher levels of IL‐1β than DLin‐MC3‐DMA LNPs (Onpattro).[  ] Another study by Moderna Inc. explored the effect of LNP size on immunogenicity, showing that 100 nm LNPs, administered IM, induced the highest IgG titers in mice.[  ] 

Lipoplex

mRNA lipoplex is formed by electrostatic complexation between mRNA and cationic lipids, such as 1,2‐dioleoyl‐3‐trimethylammonium‐propane (DOTAP) and N‐[1‐(2,3‐dioleyloxy)propyl]‐N,N,N‐trimethylammonium chloride (DOTMA). mRNA lipoplex attracts researchers’ attention due to its streamlined preparation process and high mRNA encapsulation efficiency. Kranz et al. developed intravenously injectable RNA‐lipoplex (RNA‐LPX) vaccines that selectively target DCs in lymphoid tissues by modulating nanoparticle charge, leading to efficient in situ antigen expression, robust type I IFN‐driven immune activation, potent cytotoxic T cell responses, and achieved complete tumor regression in multiple mouse models.[  ] Mendez‐Gomez et al. developed multi‐lamellar mRNA–lipid particle aggregates (RNA‐LPAs) that mimic pathogens to trigger strong innate immune activation through the retinoic acid‐inducible gene I/interferon‐alpha/beta receptor 1 (RIG‐I/IFNAR1) axis and reprogramed both systemic and intratumoral immunity and demonstrated potent therapeutic efficacy across murine, canine, and human glioma models by enhancing antigen presentation, T cell infiltration, and tumor microenvironment remodeling.[  ] 

Polymeric Nanoparticles

Polymeric nanoparticles, known for their structural versatility and ease of functional modification, have long been used for DNA and siRNA delivery.[  ] Recently, they have gained significant attention for mRNA delivery,[  ] with a variety of materials (e.g., PEI, polyesters, dendrimers, and polysaccharides) being widely employed. Cationic polymers can condense negatively charged mRNA into stable complexes, facilitating cellular uptake and protecting the mRNA from enzymatic degradation. For instance, Li et al. grafted PEI onto cyclodextrin (CP), which successfully condensed mRNA, enhanced mRNA expression at the N/P ratio of 16, and achieved intranasal mRNA delivery.[  ] Modification of PEI with benzamide, benzene sulfonamide, and sulfonamide derivatives has been discovered to stimulate the STING pathway and enable site‐specific delivery, especially Octyl sulfonamide–modified PEI, which was optimized for tumor‐targeted delivery of mRNA vaccines and STING activation, resulting in a potent tumor‐specific immune response and reversal of the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment.[  ] Biodegradable poly (β‐amino ester) (PBAE) was employed to deliver chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)‐expressing mRNA for in vivo T cell editing. By conjugating anti‐CD8 antibody, the polymeric nanoparticles successfully delivered mRNA payloads to primary T cells both in vitro and in vivo, and successfully reprogrammed them into CAR+ T cells, successfully eliminating leukemia.[  ] Chahal et al. designed an amphiphilic dendrimer‐based nanoparticle system for mRNA delivery. These nanoparticles efficiently delivered self‐replicating RNA and provided protective immunity for various pathogen challenges, including H1N1 influenza, Ebola virus, and toxoplasma gondii.[  ] As a polysaccharide, chitosan was conjugated with poly‐D,L‐lactide‐co‐glycolide (PLGA) to deliver therapeutic mRNA encoding surfactant protein B (SP‐B). Results showed that the system can successfully replace SP‐B deficiency and experimental asthma in mouse models.[  ] Polymeric nanoparticles demonstrate strong potential for mRNA delivery by offering high transfection efficiency, protecting mRNA from degradation, and promoting endosomal escape.[  ] However, their application remains limited due to safety concerns and the potential formation of excessively tight complexes with mRNA, which can impede the efficient release of mRNA from the nanoparticles into the cytoplasm.[  ] 

Lipid‐Polymer Hybrid Nanoparticles

Lipid‐polymer hybrid nanoparticles integrate the complementary advantages of both lipid and polymeric nanoparticles, providing a versatile and effective platform for mRNA delivery. Shi's group developed a hybrid delivery system in which mRNA was first complexed with the cationic lipid compound G0‐C14 within a PLGA core, then encapsulated by a lipid shell. This system exhibited low toxicity and significantly enhanced the translation of mRNA encoding the tumor suppressor phosphatase and tensin homolog (PTEN) in prostate tumor cells, resulting in marked tumor growth inhibition across multiple prostate cancer mouse models.[  ] In another study, Shi's group encapsulated mRNA encoding the tumor suppressor p53 using G0‐C14 in a redox‐responsive poly(disulfide amide) (PDSA) core, coated with lipid‐PEG. These nanoparticles increased p53 mRNA expression in both p53‐null hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and non‐small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cells. When combined with everolimus, a mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) inhibitor, the system significantly inhibited tumor growth in animal models of HCC and NSCLC.[  ] Cheng et al. adopted a distinct strategy by combining a dendrimer (5A2‐SC8) with LNPs to create dendrimer‐based LNPs (DLNPs) for mRNA delivery. In vivo studies demonstrated that DLNPs significantly enhanced the expression of fumarylacetoacetate hydrolase (FAH)‐encoding mRNA in FAH‐deficient mice, leading to a marked extension in survival following the induction of hepatorenal tyrosinemia.[  ] These hybrid nanoparticles demonstrate significant promise in improving mRNA delivery efficiency, offering enhanced stability, and reduced toxicity.

Inorganic Nanoparticles

Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs), mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs), and metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) present promising inorganic materials for mRNA delivery, owing to their exceptional stability, versatile properties, and relatively low production costs.[  ] For instance, AuNPs were functionalized with poly(thymine) to form AuNP‐poly(T)‐DNA, specifically designed to bind the poly(A) tail of mRNA via hybridization with poly(T). This system successfully facilitated the transfection of four different mRNAs across multiple cell lines.[  ] Wang et al. utilized dendritic mesoporous silica nanoparticles (DMSNs) for mRNA delivery, demonstrating that compared to conventional MSNs, their smaller particle size and larger pore structure resulted in markedly higher mRNA transfection efficiency.[  ] Similarly, Sun et al. developed a dendritic cationic zirconium (Zr)‐based poly(glycidyl methacrylate) MOF [MOF‐PGMA(EA)] nanoparticle for mRNA delivery. MOF‐PGMA(EA) provided superior colloidal stability, protected the encapsulated mRNA from enzymatic degradation, and improved mRNA transfection efficiency in targeted cells, compared to the PGMA(EA) nanocarrier.[  ] Despite these advancements, challenges remain regarding the potential toxicity and long‐term accumulation of inorganic nanoparticles in the body, which must be thoroughly addressed before their broader clinical application.[  ] 

Biomimetic Materials

Biomimetic materials have become a promising platform for mRNA vaccine delivery, providing enhanced biocompatibility, immune evasion, and targeted delivery capabilities. Leveraging naturally derived components such as cell membranes[  ] and extracellular vesicles (EVs),[  ] these biomimetic systems emulate the structural and functional characteristics of native biological entities. Specifically, bacterial outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) represent a promising strategy to enhance mRNA vaccine efficacy through intrinsic immunogenicity as potent TLR agonists. Li et al. demonstrated that OMVs effectively activated multiple TLR pathways—including TLR2, TLR4, TLR5, and TLR9. The authors further engineered OMVs as a multifunctional mRNA delivery platform by decorating the OMV surface with the RNA‐binding protein, L7Ae, and lysosomal escape protein, listeriolysin O (OMVs‐LL). Crucially, this engineered OMV‐LL platform synergistically leverages its intrinsic TLR activation and efficient delivery of mRNA tumor antigen into DCs, resulting in enhanced antigen processing and presentation and ultimately eliciting potent antitumor adaptive immunity.[  ] 

Efficient mRNA delivery remains a critical challenge in the development of mRNA‐based therapeutics. Various delivery systems—such as viral vectors, lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), polymeric nanoparticles, inorganic carriers, and biomimetic nanomaterials—each offer distinct advantages and limitations in terms of delivery efficiency, safety profile, and tissue targeting. However, when selecting an appropriate delivery platform, it is crucial not only to prioritize therapeutic efficacy but also to take into account practical considerations such as the scalability of manufacturing and unclear safey issues for clinical and commercial production. Challenges in large‐scale production include ensuring batch‐to‐batch consistency, maintaining nanoparticle quality and uniformity, and minimizing potential toxicity during long‐term administration. Additionally, regulatory requirements demand thorough evaluation of the physicochemical properties, stability, and immunogenicity of the delivery system. To date, LNPs remain the only clinically approved platform for mRNA delivery, primarily due to their favorable balance of efficacy, safety, and manufacturability. Nevertheless, continued innovation is needed to address the limitations of existing platforms and to develop next‐generation delivery systems that are both clinically effective and industrially viable.

mRNA‐based DC Vaccines

The activation of immature DCs into fully functional mature DCs is a pivotal step in initiating cytotoxic T‐cell‐mediated tumor immunotherapy. However, the substantial biological barriers to delivering mRNA vaccine to DCs and promoting their maturation in vivo have prompted researchers to develop alternative strategy—ex vivo DC vaccines (Figure  5 ). This approach involves isolating DC precursors from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) via leukapheresis, followed by their in vitro culture and differentiation using cytokines such as Granulocyte‐Macrophage Colony‐Stimulating Factor (GM‐CSF) and IL‐4. The immature DCs are then pulsed with mRNA encoding antigens, after which they are stimulated with a maturation cocktail, typically comprising agents like TNF‐α, IL‐6, and Toll‐like receptor (TLR) agonists. Finally, the matured DCs are re‐administered to the patient.[  ] 

Techniques for pulsing mRNA into DCs include electroporation,[  ] lipofection,[  ] and sonoporation.[  ] Ex vivo mRNA‐based DC vaccines offer several advantages, including strong antigen presentation, personalized therapeutic potential, and a favorable safety profile. However, their wider clinical use is restricted by challenges such as a complex and labor‐intensive manufacturing process, along with the limited lifespan of engineered DCs following reintroduction into patients.[  ] The FDA‐approved Sipuleucel‐T, an autologous DC vaccine, represents a significant milestone in this field. By targeting metastatic castration‐resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), Sipuleucel‐T showed notable clinical success with significantly increased 3‐year survival rate. Despite its potential, the widespread application of Sipuleucel‐T as well as other DC vaccines has been constrained by substantial production expenses and a complex manufacturing process. Future advancements in mRNA DC vaccines may focus on overcoming these challenges by streamlining production processes through automation and closed‐system manufacturing, engineering DCs with improved resistance to apoptosis and enhanced antigen‐presenting capacity, and integrating novel gene delivery tools such as mRNA‐loaded nanoparticles or in situ reprogramming technologies to eliminate the need for ex vivo manipulation. Additionally, advances in synthetic biology and biomaterials might enable the development of off‐the‐shelf, allogeneic DC substitutes or modular delivery platforms that mimic key functions of natural DCs. Together, these innovations could significantly reduce cost, improve scalability, and broaden the clinical applicability of DC‐based mRNA vaccines for cancer.

Strategies to Enhance mRNA Cancer Vaccines

Direct mRNA Vaccines to Lymphoid Organs

Intramuscular injection remains the predominant route for administering mRNA vaccines, as it induces localized inflammation which attracts immune cells to the injection site. These immune cells take up mRNA nanoparticles and travel to nearby lymph nodes, where they initiate T cell responses.[  ] However, this approach has limitations, such as significant uptake of mRNA nanoparticles by muscle cells and unintended accumulation in the liver and spleen, which can potentially cause adverse effects.[  ] While localized inflammation aids in immune activation, it may also lead to discomfort at the injection site and other reactions. To enhance efficacy and safety, emerging studies focus on directing mRNA vaccine to immune organs and APCs, such as DCs in lymph node and spleen—critical sites for initiating and regulating adaptive immunity. Concentrating mRNA vaccines in these immune‐rich environments enhances antigen presentation, resulting in stronger T cell priming, more potent immune activation, and longer‐lasting immunity.[  ] This targeted approach also reduces off‐target effects, enhances safety, and allows for lower dosing to achieve desired efficacy, thereby decreasing production costs. Targeting mRNA vaccines to these crucial immune organs offers significant promise for improving therapeutic outcomes, particularly in cancer immunotherapy, where robust and durable immune responses are essential.[  ] A summary of representative studies on targeted immune organ delivery is provided in Figure  6 , with further details to be discussed in the following sections.

Peripheral LN, a major component of immune system, is serving as a residence for immune cells and managing the drainage of cells and tissues via lymphatic vessels. This communication network makes them an ideal target for vaccine delivery and immunotherapy. Targeting DCs with engineered nanoparticles can greatly enhance delivery to LNs.[  ] Mannose, a ligand commonly used to target CD206—a receptor highly expressed on immature DCs—has been incorporated into nanoparticles to enable efficient mRNA delivery and enhance anti‐tumor immunity in vivo.[  ] García‐Fernández et al. developed a zwitterionic polymer‐based nanoparticle platform (ZION) for mRNA delivery, by integrating the targeting peptide to CLEC9A receptor, it enabled targeted delivery to medullary APCs within lymph nodes, effectively bypassing complement‐mediated opsonization and reducing off‐target inflammation.[  ] Key design principles for enhancing LN targeting include employing shorter lipid tails, favouring ester bonds over amide bonds, and substituting certain functional groups at the amine head with methyl groups.[  ] Wan et al. engineered mannose‐modified circRNA‐LNPs to target lymph nodes (Figure 6A,B), boosting antibody responses and durable immunity with a single dose.[  ] Another study conducted by Chen's group tackled the challenge of achieving targeted mRNA delivery to LNs for subcutaneously administered cancer vaccines. The researchers developed 113‐O12B LNP (Figure 6C), which exhibited enhanced and specific expression in LNs compared to ALC‐0315, a synthetic lipid used in COVID‐19 vaccine (Figure 6D). Targeted delivery of mRNA to the LN led to improved CD8+ T cell responses to the ovalbumin (OVA) model antigen, thereby enhancing protective effects against OVA‐expressing B16F10 melanoma. Additionally, the 113‐O12B LNP effectively delivered mRNA encoding the Tyrosinase related protein‐2 peptide (TRP2180–188), achieving significant tumor suppression and complete responses when combined with anti‐PD‐1 therapy. Mice treated with 113‐O12B LNP exhibited long‐lasting immune memory, preventing metastatic nodules development upon rechallenge. Overall, the 113‐O12B LNP system shows considerable promise as a universal platform for future mRNA cancer vaccines, significantly improving both antibody and CD8+ T cell responses and enhancing therapeutic efficacy against tumors.[  ] 

In the context of vaccination and immunotherapy, spleen plays a pivotal role in antigen presentation and immune response activation, primarily due to high density of immune cells. Effectively delivering mRNA vaccines to APCs, particularly DCs, is essential for stimulating B cell and T cell responses.[  ] Various strategies have been devised to enhance the targeting of mRNA to the spleen, which have been incorporated into packaging and delivery systems. A well‐recognized approach for increasing spleen accumulation involves using nanoparticles with negatively charged surfaces.[  ] Lena et al. developed luciferase mRNA‐encapsulated lipoplexes for organ‐targeted delivery and demonstrated that modulating the lipid‐to‐mRNA ratio, which adjusts the positive‐to‐negative charge balance, could shift the biodistribution of the lipoplexes from the liver to the spleen (Figure 6E).[  ] Additionally, altering the helper lipids can redirect LNPs from liver to spleen. For instance, LNPs containing anionic phosphatidylserine have demonstrated superior mRNA translation in spleen compared to LNPs with 1,2‐Dioleoyl‐sn‐glycero‐3‐phosphoethanolamine (DOPE) as helper lipid.[  ] To address the need for targeted mRNA delivery to specific cell populations within the spleen, Zhang's team developed a one‐component ionizable cationic lipid system (Figure 6F). This novel system is designed for efficient mRNA delivery to the spleen and T cells, contrasting with current four‐component LNPs that primarily target hepatocytes via intravenous administration. Unlike traditional tertiary amine‐based lipids, the secondary amine‐rich cationic lipids function as effective standalone carriers, offering superior mRNA binding and cellular uptake. This approach achieves high mRNA expression in the spleen and T cells, showing significant promise for cancer immunotherapy and vaccine development while expanding the potential of lipid systems for extrahepatic mRNA delivery. This methodology can be applied to various cationic lipids and polymers, paving the way for future advancements in mRNA delivery technologies.[  ] 

Lei et al. developed monosaccharides‐conjugated LNPs to enhance delivery efficiency to DCs and spleen, known as sweet LNPs with mannose conjugation (STLNP‐Man). STLNP‐Man could be specifically recognized by mannose receptor (CD206) and internalized by DCs (Figure 6G,H). Interestingly, STLNPs‐Man reduces the expression of cytotoxic T‐lymphocyte‐associated protein 4 (CTLA‐4) on cytotoxic T cells by disrupting the CD206/CD45 signalling pathway. The antitumor efficacy of STLNPs‐Man@mRNAOVA is significantly enhanced when combined with immune checkpoint blockade therapy, leading to complete tumor elimination.[  ] Meanwhile, Shimosakai et al.[  ] designed polymeric nanoparticles that can specifically target the spleen. These nanoparticles interacted with splenic macrophages and DCs to express tumor antigens encoded by mRNA. This study demonstrated that these targeted nanoparticles were efficiently captured by splenic immune cells, leading to enhanced antigen presentation and a stronger systemic immune response. This targeted delivery approach yielded higher levels of antigen‐specific antibodies and more robust T cell activation compared to non‐targeted nanocarriers. This research underscores the potential of spleen‐targeted nanoparticle delivery in enhancing systemic vaccine responses, thereby contributing to the development of more effective cancer vaccines.

Mucosa‐associated lymphoid tissues (MALT) serve as a crucial component in orchestrating mucosal and systemic immune responses. Therefore, mucosal delivery of mRNA vaccines has emerged as a promising strategy for eliciting robust immune responses at mucosal surfaces in a minimally invasive manner.[  ] Nevertheless, effective mucosal delivery remains technically challenging due to rapid enzymatic degradation, mucus‐associated physical barriers, limited residence time, and carrier instability. A recent study by Tang et al. introduced a dual‐targeted polymer–lipid hybrid mRNA nanoparticle system designed for efficient and selective delivery to lung tissues via inhalation. By incorporating hyaluronic acid for CD44 targeting and mannose‐modified DSPE‐PEG for glucose transporter targeting, these NPs achieved selective accumulation in lung tumor cells and inflammatory macrophages. The platform enabled robust in vivo expression of therapeutic (p53) and reporter (Luc, EGFP) proteins, demonstrating its potential as a versatile tool for inhaled mRNA‐based therapies and vaccines against lung‐associated diseases.[  ] To overcome the instability of LNPs during nebulization, Liu et al. developed a charge‐assisted stabilization (CAS) strategy by incorporating negatively charged peptide–lipid conjugates into conventional LNPs. The resulting CAS‐LNPs exhibited enhanced colloidal stability and efficient pulmonary mRNA delivery across mouse, dog, and pig models. Following inhalation, CAS‐LNPs primarily targeted lung DCs, inducing strong mucosal and systemic immune responses, demonstrating its potential as an inhaled therapeutic cancer vaccine.[  ] In parallel, oral mRNA vaccination has focused on targeting intestinal MALT, particularly Peyer's patches. Nanocarriers designed for gastrointestinal stability and epithelial uptake have shown success in delivering mRNA to gut‐associated lymphoid tissues, resulting in strong immune responses.[  ] Complementarily, Luo et al. reported a pH‐responsive β‐glucan–complexed mRNA‐LNP oral formulation that resisted enzymatic degradation and promoted antigen presentation by gut DCs, driving potent antitumor immunity in mice.[  ] Mucosal delivery offers the advantage of non‐invasive administration and the potential to elicit strong local immune responses at mucosal surfaces. However, several challenges must be overcome to enable the successful application of mRNA cancer vaccines. The mucus barrier and enzymatic components within mucosal secretions can compromise mRNA stability and hinder its access to epithelial cells, microfold cells, and especially APCs. Moreover, while the inflammatory response induced by mRNA vaccines is generally tolerable in intramuscular injections, the heightened sensitivity of mucosal tissues to inflammation raises concerns about local reactogenicity and safety.[  ] Nanoparticles, due to their tunable physicochemical properties and protective capabilities, hold great promise for facilitating the targeted delivery of mRNA to APCs within MALT, while minimizing off‐target effects and degradation.

For successful application of mucosal mRNA vaccine in vivo, ideal nanoparticles must meet several critical design criteria. Firstly, nanoparticles must be engineered to efficiently penetrate the dense, viscoelastic mucus layer that lines mucosal surfaces such as the respiratory, gastrointestinal, and vaginal tracts.[  ] This requires surface modifications, such as hydrophilic or neutrally charged coatings (e.g., PEG, zwitterionic polymers), to allow enhanced diffusion through mucus.[  ] Secondly, engineering mRNA with prolonged stability is necessary for generating therapeutic doses of antigen proteins. The mucosal environment is rich in extracellular RNases that can rapidly degrade unprotected mRNA.[  ] Thus, delivery systems must provide robust protection against enzymatic degradation, either through encapsulation within lipid, polymeric, or hybrid nanoparticles, or via chemical modification of the mRNA. Maintaining structural integrity until cellular uptake is essential for preserving antigen expression and immune activation. Additionally, nanoparticles must be capable of traversing the epithelial barrier, either through transcytosis across microfold cells (M cells), paracellular 758transport, or receptor‐mediated endocytosis.[  ] This includes avoiding entrapment at tight junctions and ensuring efficient uptake by epithelial and underlying immune cells such as DCs and macrophages. Finally, a successful mucosal mRNA vaccine should elicit strong local and systemic immune responses without causing excessive inflammation or tissue damage. This requires selective targeting of APCs, such as DCs and macrophages, within MALTs, to promote antigen processing and T cell activation.[  ] Minimizing non‐specific uptake by epithelial or non‐immune cells reduces off‐target effects and adverse reactions.

In summary, targeting mRNA vaccines to mucosal lymphoid organs holds great promise for enhancing cancer immunotherapy. By leveraging tailored nanocarriers and route‐specific delivery strategies, researchers can boost the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines while minimizing systemic toxicity. These advances not only overcome longstanding limitations of conventional vaccination but also open new avenues for localized and systemic immune modulation in cancer treatment.

Alter the Way mRNA Engage with Immune Cells

The administration routes for mRNA vaccines include IM, IV, SC, intradermal (ID), intranasal (IN), and intranodal administrations, with each route influencing the immunogenicity of vaccines in distinct ways (Figure  7 ). IM injection is the most common method, involving administration into deep tissue below the subcutaneous and dermal layers.[  ] The abundant blood vessels in muscular tissues can recruit various immune cells.[  ] Studies have reported that mRNA‐loaded LNPs can retard at the injection site and in draining lymph nodes for 28 hours.[  ] ID injection involves injecting mRNA vaccines into the dermal layer of the skin, which contains vascular and lymphatic vessels, assisting the transport of mRNA vaccines and APCs to lymph nodes.[  ] IN vaccination is another non‐invasive method where inhaled materials are taken up by DCs which will then be transferred to lymph nodes via lymphatic vessels for antigen presentation. Sufficient DCs reside in nasal cavity, making it a potential site for eliciting strong immune responses.[  ] SC injection is administered into the layer beneath the epidermis and dermis, which contains a loose network of immune cells. Subcutaneous tissues can accommodate a relatively larger injection volume, allowing for efficient drainage to the immune system. mRNA vaccines through IV administration are delivered directly to systemic circulation, allowing vaccines to access immune cells and lymphoid organs quickly, potentially enhancing vaccine potency.[  ] However, IV administration may also cause side effects, such as spleen injury and lymphocyte depletion.[  ] 

The physiological properties of the vaccination site impact the immunogenicity and efficacy of the vaccine. Kreiter et al. injected naked antigen‐encoding RNA via direct intranodal administration and found it drove robust Th1‐type CD4⁺ and CD8⁺ T cell responses, enabling potent prophylactic and therapeutic antitumor immunity in various mouse models.[  ] Anderluzzi et al. systemically investigated the immunogenicity of various format of saRNA‐loaded nanoparticles including solid lipid nanoparticles (SLNs), polymeric nanoparticles (PNPs), corosolic acid‐modified LNPs (cLNPs), and ionizable LNPs (iLNPs), administered via different routes (IM, ID, and IN), as shown in Figure  8A,B.[  ] Immune response analyses revealed that both IM and ID routes elicited higher antigen‐specific IgG titres compared to IN (Figure 8C). This diminished response from the IN route is likely due to the rapid mucociliary clearance and protease‐rich environment of the nasal cavity, which reduce vaccine retention and limit antigen expression.[  ] Among the four types of nanoparticles, iLNPs produced the highest IgG responses, indicating the strongest immunogenicity. Further studies on the cellular immune response also showed a similar trend (Figure 8D). Huang et al. prepared receptor‐binding domain (RBD)‐encoding mRNA encapsulated liposomes (Figure 8E) and investigated their immunogenicity via different routes (IV, IM, hypodermic (IH), ID, and IP). Their results showed that IH, ID, and IP treatments induced similar IgG2a response, which are higher than those induced by IV and IM administration (Figure 8F).[  ] It has also been reported that the effectiveness of mRNA vaccination via ID administration is more significant compared to IM administration.[  ] 

The administration routes also impact the levels of anti‐LNP antibodies (IgG and IgM against PEG). With a higher dose of mRNA‐LNPs (0.3 mg kg−1 as mRNA), SC administration has been reported to induce higher levels of IgG and IgM antibodies compared to IV and IM administration. Conversely, IV administration tends to generate higher antibody levels with a lower LNP dose (0.003 mg kg−1 as mRNA).[  ] Selecting the appropriate administration route for mRNA vaccines requires comprehensive consideration of multiple factors, including the duration of mRNA expression, the clearance rate of mRNA, and potential side effects. Normally, IM, ID, and SC routes achieve a longer duration of mRNA expression (≈10 days) compared to IV administration (1≈4 days).[  ] mRNA administrated via IN is generally cleared faster than through ID and IM routes.[  ] Additionally, IM administration may cause pain, swelling, and redness at the injection site.[  ] Collectively, variations in immunogenicity associated with different routes of administration may result from distinct initial interactions between antigens and the immune system, as well as differences in subsequent antigen processing and presentation pathways.[  ] 

Overall, the various physiological features of different administration routs may significantly influence the fate of mRNA nanoparticles, which underscore the need to balance immunization effect and safety of injected mRNA vaccines. Selecting appropriate administration route for mRNA vaccine development requires balancing factors such as targeting the desired immune response, ensuring efficient tissue delivery, maximing safety and optimizing practicality and scalability. Tailor designed mRNA nanoparticles to comprehensively consider these aspects can help achieve effective safe and accessible vaccine formulations for specific clinical objectives.

Accumulate mRNA in Immune Cells

DCs are pivotal target in cancer vaccines by orchestrating both innate and adaptive immune responses through efficient antigen presentation and promoting the activation of anti‐tumor T cell.[  ] Targeted mRNA delivery strategies have been explored to direct mRNA vaccines specifically to splenic DCs using biodegradable nanoparticles. Ben‐Akiva et al. showed that bio‐reducible lipophilic Poly(β‐amino ester) (PBAE) nanoparticles (R18D NPs) effectively co‐deliver mRNA with TLR agonist adjuvants (TLR3 agonist polyinosinic‐polycytidylic acid (poly(I:C)) or TLR9 agonist CpG oligodeoxynucleotide (CpG ODN)) at low dose to the splenic DCs without the need of surface modifications.[  ] This precise ligand free delivery system led to robust antitumor responses in murine melanoma and colon adenocarcinoma models with diminished systemic side effects (Figure  9 ). Building on this, Kranz's team demonstrated that DCs can be effectively targeted in vivo using intravenously administered RNA‐lipoplexes (RNA‐LPX)—lipid‐based carriers whose surface charge was optimized to enable efficient delivery without the need for molecular ligand functionalization. RNA‐LPX not only protects RNA from degradation but also facilitates its uptake and expression in DCs and macrophages across various lymphoid organs which are ideal for T‐cell priming and amplification. This delivery system triggers the release of IFN‐α by plasmacytoid DCs and macrophages, promoting in situ DC maturation and activating immune mechanisms akin to those observed during early systemic viral infections.[  ] 

Optimizing nanoparticle formulations offers another powerful approach to boosting mRNA delivery efficiency. Researchers have pioneered several strategies to refine LNPs, including tailoring lipid structures,[  ] adjusting cholesterol content,[  ] and fine‐tuning formulation composition.[  ] Chen et al. developed an ionizable lipid library using a Ugi‐based three‐component reaction (3CR), synthesizing 288 unique ionizable lipids with varying amine head groups and lipid tails. They found that a muscle‐targeted LNP (iso‐A11B5C1) achieved high specificity for myocytes with minimal off‐target delivery, yet still elicited potent cellular immune responses despite limited transfection of antigen‐presenting cells. This finding suggest that efficient immune cell transfection might not be indispensable for mRNA vaccine efficacy, highlighting an alternative pathway where antigen presentation by host cells suffices to trigger strong cytotoxic T cell responses.[  ] 

Natural‐Derived Adjuvants to Boost mRNA Cancer Vaccine

Natural TLR Agonists

Although cancer vaccines have demonstrated considerable potential,[  ] stimulating the immune response against tumor antigens poses greater challenges as compared to foreign pathogens, necessitating optimal antigen presentation and activation of APCs. IFN‐I‐associated adjuvants enhances adaptive immune responses, thereby improving the efficacy of mRNA vaccines.[  ] TLR and stimulator of interferon genes (STING) signalling pathways are two of the most prominent mechanisms responsible for triggering the secretion of IFNs.[  ] Recent advancements include the development of both natural and engineered adjuvants for co‐delivery with mRNA encoding antigens to boost the immunogenicity and therapeutic potential of mRNA cancer vaccines.

TLRs, a subclass of PRRs, play a central role in initiating innate immune responses by detecting PAMPs and DAMPs. Structurally, TLRs consist of extracellular leucine‐rich repeat (LRR) domains for ligand recognition and intracellular Toll/IL‐1 receptor (TIR) domains that activate downstream signaling cascades, such as NF‐κB and Mitogen‐Activated Protein Kinase (MAPK), leading to cytokine and chemokine production. Surface TLRs (e.g., TLR1, TLR2, TLR4) primarily detect microbial membrane components, while endosomal TLRs (e.g., TLR3, TLR7, TLR9) recognize nucleic acids. TLR agonists have emerged as promising immunostimulatory agents in cancer immunotherapy by enhancing innate immune activation and promoting robust adaptive immune responses.[  ] Natural TLR agonists are molecules that specifically interact with and activate TLRs, which are crucial components of the innate immune system. Typically derived from various pathogens, including bacteria, viruses, and fungi, these agonists play a vital role in enhancing immune responses. They are highly effective in stimulating immune activation and are widely employed as adjuvants in vaccines and as immunomodulatory agents in cancer therapy.[  ] For instance, lipopolysaccharides (LPS) from Gram‐negative bacterial cell walls specifically activate TLR4, while flagellin, a protein component of bacterial flagella, targets TLR5. Additionally, unmethylated CpG DNA sequences serve as agonists for activating TLR9.

When binding to their respective receptors, TLR agonists initiate a series of immune responses. This interaction triggers the release of inflammatory cytokines, promotes the expression of costimulatory molecules, and enhances the presentation of antigens. Such activation not only primes the immune system but also shapes the subsequent adaptive immune response. For example, TLR4 agonists, such as LPS, can activate transcription factors such as nuclear factor kappa B (NF‐κB), leading to a strong immune response. Similarly, TLR9 agonists stimulate the production of IFN‐I and improve T‐cell responses. Unlike conventional adjuvants (such as aluminum adjuvant), TLR agonists can also activate anti‐allergic T lymphocyte responses, making them valuable in allergen immunotherapy. Current research studies are investigating TLR agonists as vaccine adjuvants, with a focus on their potential to enhance vaccine efficacy.[  ] 

Natural Cyclic Dinucleotides (CDNs) STING Agonists

Natural cyclic dinucleotides (CDNs) act as STING agonists through binding to STING protein.[  ] The non‐canonical 2’3’‐cGAMP is a typical natural CDNs that is produced by mammalian cells via the cyclic GMP‐AMP synthase (cGAS)‐DNA recognition pathway. It can bind to all major human STING variants with varying degree of binding affinities.[  ] Besides 2’3’‐cGAMP, natural CDNs include the canonical 3’3’‐cGAMP and the non‐canonical 3’2’‐cGAMP, which respectively originated in bacteria and Drosophila melanogaster. Both 3’3’‐cGAMP and 3’2’‐cGAMP can only activate certain human STING variants with lower binding affinity than that of 2’3’‐cGAMP.[  ] Although these natural CNDs could intrinsically activate STING pathways, their applications as drug candidates were significantly limited due to the inefficient delivery into the cytosol and the vulnerability to hydrolytic degradation in the extracellular space by ectonucleotide pyrophosphatase/phosphodiesterase 1 (ENPP1).[  ] 

Metal Ions and Immune Modulation

Metal ions play multifaceted roles in regulating both innate and adaptive immune responses through their structural, catalytic, and signaling interactions with key immune components. Specific ions, such as Mn2⁺, Zn2⁺, Ca2⁺, K⁺, and Mg2⁺, directly influence immune pathways by modulating pattern recognition receptors and transcriptional regulators.[  ] For instance, Mn2⁺ enhances the sensitivity of the cGAS–STING pathway and promotes IFN‐I responses.[  ] In the innate immune system, metal ions such as Co2⁺ can activate Toll‐like receptor 4 (TLR4),[  ] whereas K⁺ efflux and Ca2⁺ influx serve as universal triggers for NOD‐like receptor family, pyrin domain containing 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome activation.[  ] In adaptive immunity, Ca2⁺ and Mg2⁺ are essential for T cell receptor (TCR) signaling and downstream transcriptional activation,[  ] while K⁺ impacts T cell metabolic reprogramming and the function of effector T cells.[  ] These highlight the potential of leveraging metal ions as therapeutic modulators of immune function, forming the foundation for emerging metalloimmunotherapy strategies in cancer and immune‐related diseases.

Co‐Delivery of Natural Adjuvants with mRNA Vaccine by Nanocarriers

In the realm of tumor vaccine development, achieving an efficient immune response requires optimal antigen presentation and the activation of APCs to prime naive T cells into CTLs. However, tumor antigen‐encoding mRNA, while crucial for vaccine potency, often has low immunogenicity to enhance translation efficiency. This underscores the importance of co‐delivery of adjuvants with mRNA cancer vaccines to bolster immune responses.

TLR agonists show great potential for augmenting the efficacy of mRNA cancer vaccines. However, there is ongoing debate regarding the possibility that activation of TLR pathways may suppress the expression of transfected mRNA. To address this challenge, Ben‐Akiva et al. pioneered the development of biodegradable polymeric nanoparticles capable of co‐delivering TLR agonists (such as CpG or poly(I:C)) alongside mRNA. In vitro studies revealed that encapsulated CpG had minimal impact on mRNA expression, while poly(I:C) caused a dose‐dependent reduction in mRNA expression. Despite this, in vivo experiments demonstrated that co‐delivering both CpG and poly(I:C) with mRNA significantly boosted CTL‐based immune responses and anti‐tumor therapeutic effects, outperforming nanoparticles containing mRNA alone.[  ] Pan et al. engineered a stearic acid‐modified LNP system for co‐encapsulating a TLR‐4 agonist (Monophosphoryl‐Lipid A, MPLA) and mRNA. MPLA improved DC activation and antigen presentation, and effectively inhibited tumor growth (Figure  10A–D).[  ] Similarly, the research conducted by Verbeke et al. also illustrated that MPLA facilitated DC activation and primed cytotoxic T cells.[  ] Gu et al. employed LNP formulations to co‐encapsulate TLR2 agonists, S‐[2,3‐Bis(palmitoyloxy)propyl]‐L‐cysteine (Pam2Cys), with mRNA antigens, leading to enhanced antigen presentation by both migratory and resident DCs (Figure 10E,F), along with increased secretion of proinflammatory cytokines. In vivo anti‐tumor studies with LNPs encapsulated with Pam2Cys and mRNA showed significant tumor growth inhibition, which rendered the surviving mice tumor‐resistant for an extended period, as evidenced by a rechallenge study.[  ] 

However, exogenous mRNA intrinsically binding to TLRs and RLRs can paradoxically enhance PKR activity, leading to suppressed antigen expression and a limited antigen‐specific immune response.[  ] Lokugamage et al. reported that co‐delivery of a TLR4 agonist decreased mRNA translation intracellularly. Their study elucidated that this inhibition stemmed from elevated levels of PKR protein, which actively impeded mRNA translation. Nonetheless, it is generally understood that co‐delivering mRNA with an appropriate amount of TLR agonist can facilitate both antigen mRNA translation for antigen presentation, and DC activation for priming cytotoxic T cells.[  ] 

The activation of STING pathway, which operates independently of TLRs and RLRs, presents a compelling combinational strategy to boost IFN‐I mediated host immunity and enhance the efficacy of mRNA vaccines.[  ] Since both mRNA and STING agonists necessitate efficient cytosolic delivery for optimal performance, innovative delivery methods capable of surmounting the existing delivery barriers are increasingly sought after to synergize STING activation with mRNA vaccination. Co‐delivery of mRNA and STING agonists is one of the most direct ways to introduce STING agonists for STING activation‐promoted mRNA immunization. Zhou et al. developed highly efficient nanovaccine formulations by complexing various cationic polymers phenylboronic acid‐grafted oligoethylenimine (PBA‐OEI) with mRNA and 2’3’‐cGAMP (Figure  11 ). The nanocomplexes effectively delivered mRNA antigens and 2’3’‐cGAMP to the cytoplasm of tumor cells.[  ] This innovative approach activates the STING pathway, facilitating the presentation of mRNA‐encoded antigens and enhancing antitumor immunity in vivo.

Recent studies have highlighted the potential of metal ions, particularly manganese (Mn2⁺), to enhance both the delivery efficiency and immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines. Fan et al. developed a Mn2⁺ coordinated lipid nanoparticle platform for mRNA vaccine delivery, integrating a novel ionizable lipid (IC8) with Mn2⁺ as a STING agonist and immune modulator. Mn2⁺ enhanced both mRNA expression and immunogenicity, promoting DCs’ maturation, robust IFN‐I responses, and durable humoral and cellular immunity in mice, highlighting Mn2⁺ coordination as a promising strategy to simultaneously enhance mRNA translation and adjuvanticity.[  ] Luo et al. developed a spleen‐targeted mRNA cancer vaccine that co‐delivers tumor antigen‐encoding mRNA and Mn2⁺ adjuvant using a 1,2‐dioctadecenoyl‐sn‐glycero‐3‐Phosphoserine (DOPS)‐modified LNPs platform. The formulation leveraged the selective organ‐targeting (SORT) strategy to enhance accumulation in the spleen, facilitating efficient uptake by DCs and robust activation of the cGAS–STING pathway. Mn2⁺ not only boosts mRNA transfection and DC maturation but also acts as a potent STING agonist, promoting IFN‐I production and strong antigen‐specific CD8⁺ T cell responses. In murine model of melanoma tumor, Mn@mRNA‐LNP induced effective tumor inhibition and prolonged survival rate (Figure 10J–M), presenting a promising platform for mRNA vaccines to enhance anticancer efficacy.[  ] 

In addition to commonly used adjuvants, other alternative approaches were also employed to synergize with mRNA vaccine. For example, IL‐12 can signal through its receptor on T and NK cells, promoting proliferation and IFN‐γ production, which may enhance CD8+ T cell responses by promoting proliferation and memory differentiation, thereby improving vaccine‐induced cellular immunity and long‐term protection.[  ] Aunins et al. explored the use of an IL‐12 mRNA‐LNP adjuvant to enhance CD8⁺ T cell responses elicited by mRNA vaccines. While endogenous IL‐12 was found to be non‐essential for CD8⁺ T cell activation following mRNA‐LNP vaccination, co‐administration of LNP‐encapsulated codon‐optimized IL‐12 mRNA with OVA mRNA significantly boosted antigen‐specific CD8⁺ T cell expansion, effector differentiation, and the generation of circulating and tissue‐resident memory subsets, resulting in significantly inhibited tumor growth.[  ] This study highlights the potential of incorporating cytokine‐encoding mRNA as an immunostimulatory component within mRNA vaccine formulations to strengthen cellular immunity and broaden protective efficacy.

Co‐delivery of natural adjuvants have demonstrated promising success in enhancing mRNA vaccine by eliciting efficient immune responses. However, these natural‐derived STING/TLR agonists present multiple challenges that ultimately limit their accessibility to maximize mRNA vaccine potency. Their susceptibility to degradation, insufficient lymph node accumulation, poor cytosolic bioavailability, and propensity to trigger nonspecific immune activation hinder antigen expression and presentation, undermine vaccine immunogenicity, and increase the risk of systemic toxicity. A further obstacle is the spatiotemporal mismatch in adjuvant release kinetics, where premature release can induce excessive inflammation before mRNA antigen translation, while delayed release may fail to provide timely innate activation necessary for optimal T‐cell priming.[  ] To overcome these limitations and fully harness the synergistic potential of mRNA‐adjuvant co‐delivery strategy, future advances should prioritize co‐encapsulation appraoch to improve lymphatic transport and cytosolic delivery, engineering stimuli‐responsive systems for coordinated release aligned with mRNA translation and antigen presentation, and developing cleavable prodrugs responsive to the tumor microenvironment or lymph node. Moreover, systematic investigations into the spatiotemporal distribution of adjuvants relative to mRNA expression kinetics, integration of real‐time release monitoring technologies, and comprehensive pharmacokinetic‐pharmacodynamic evaluations are essential to achieve potent immunostimulation while maintaining acceptable safety profiles in mRNA vaccine applications.

Adjuvants Engineering to Boost mRNA Cancer Vaccines

Engineered TLR Agonists

Synthetic TLR agonists represent a significant breakthrough in the development of adjuvants designed to enhance mRNA‐based cancer vaccines. Unlike natural TLR agonists, which originate from biological sources, synthetic TLR agonists are chemically engineered to target and activate specific TLRs with precision.[  ] This design allows for the development of adjuvants with properties tailored to boost immune responses against cancer effectively. The evolution of synthetic agonists addresses some limitations associated with traditional adjuvants, such as aluminium salts, which although effective, are facing challenges related to limited potency and potential adverse side effects. Synthetic TLR agonists are increasingly included in mRNA vaccine formulations to improve both antigen delivery and immune activation. Recent studies have demonstrated that these synthetic adjuvants can enhance the strength and durability of anti‐tumor responses when combined with mRNA vaccines.[  ] They also help address obstacles such as immune tolerance and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, thereby maximizing the therapeutic potential of mRNA‐based immunotherapies for cancer.[  ] 

TLR conjugates are ingeniously engineered molecules that combine the immune‐stimulating properties of TLR agonists with various molecular components, resulting in more targeted and potent adjuvants.[  ] This progress exemplifies a sophisticated understanding of immune modulation, aimed at improving outcomes in cancer immunotherapy. TLR conjugates are meticulously designed molecules to link TLR agonists with carriers or antigens to amplify their effectiveness. By attaching TLR agonists to specific targeting elements or immunological agents, these conjugates are optimized to enhance the stability, delivery, and overall immunological impact. For example, TLR agonists targeting receptors such as TLR3, TLR7, TLR8, and TLR9 agonists can be conjugated with tumor‐specific antigens or other immune‐modulating substances.[  ] This strategic combination not only improves the stability of the TLR agonists but also ensures their precise delivery to key immune cells like DCs. Activation of TLRs through these conjugates promotes the production of pro‐inflammatory cytokines, enhances antigen presentation, and strengthens the adaptive immune response. This integration promotes more robust and long‐lasting anti‐tumor responses, thereby improving the overall therapeutic potential of mRNA vaccines.

Engineered STING Agonists

Chemically modified CDNs and novel non‐nucleotide small molecules have been developed to overcome the poor drug‐like characteristics of natural CDNs. A significant portion of these advanced STING agonists consists of synthetic CDN analogues featuring phosphorothioate linkages instead of initial phosphodiester bonds, leading to a significantly increased resistance to ENPP1 hydrolysis as compared to natural occurring CDNs.[  ] For instance, ADU‐S100 is a cyclic diadenine molecule with mixed phosphorothioate linkages, designed for greater stability and lipophilicity to enhance STING pathway activation.[  ] The introduction of sulphur atoms in place of non‐bridging oxygen atoms on the phosphate backbone reduces vulnerability to enzymatic breakdown by ENPP1, potentially improving cellular uptake and cytosolic localization. CDNs modifications with thiourea, guanidinium, carbodiimide, urea, and triazole bonds have also been utilized to prevent the CDNs deactivation resulting from the enzymatic breakdown.[  ] Additionally, fluorination at 2′ or 3′ nucleotide sites not only enhances CDNs’ stability, but also improves the bioactivity by increasing lipophilicity‐mediated membrane permeability.[  ] The discovery of 5,6‐dimethylxanthenone‐4‐acetic acid (DMXAA), a flavonoid initially recognized for its potential anti‐tumor properties, served as an early validation for employing STING agonists to enhance tumor immunity,[  ] and paved the way for the exploration of non‐nucleotide small molecule STING agonists.[  ] Currently, various flavonoids and xanthones, including flavone‐8‐acetic acid (FAA) and α‐mangostin, have been certified as non‐CDN agonists.[  ] Unfortunately, most of these certified flavonoids and xanthones, except α‐mangostin, predominantly activate murine STING more effectively than human STING.

Polymers with unique structures have been reported to bind to STING protein and induce STING condensation via polyvalent interactions. Polyvalent polymer‐STING pairs possess much smaller dissociation constants than monovalent or divalent interactions between small molecules and STING, resulting in more stable binding and prolonged activation.[  ] Luo et al. initially screened a library of ultra‐pH‐sensitive polymers via T cell killing assays and they identified poly(2‐azepane ethyl methacrylate) (PC7A) as a polymer with cyclic seven‐membered amine ring in STING‐associated immune activation.[  ] To overcome the long‐lasting granulomatous inflammation at the injection site, Wang et al. developed a new STING‐activating polymer, PSC7A, with similar sidechains containing cyclic seven‐membered amine ring and a biodegradable polycarbonate backbone.[  ] PSC7A engages with the C‐terminal nucleotidyltransferase domain of STING protein, with in vitro hydrolysis assay revealed that PSC7A's degradation half‐life spans 12 days at pH 6.5 and extends to 27 days at pH 7.4. As compared to its nondegradable analogue, PSC7A led to a significant decrease in the duration of acute inflammatory response followed by complete tissue healing at the injection site.

Synthetic Adjuvants Potentiate mRNA Vaccine Based Cancer Immunotherapy

Nanotechnology has been applied to modify the pharmacokinetics, biodistribution, and cytosolic delivery of STING agonists, achieving a moderate level of success in preclinical studies.[  ] Recently, establishing TME‐cleavable covalent linkage between STING agonists and nanocarriers, including lipids and polymers, has emerged as a novel and efficient route to load and deliver STING agonists for cancer immunity.[  ] Dane at al. conjugated the STING‐activating CDNs to polyethylene glycol (PEG)‐modified lipids via the lysosomal enzyme‐cleavable linkers, referred to as CDN‐PEG‐lipids. Intravenous administration of LNDs loaded with CDN‐PEG‐lipids (referred to as LND‐CDNs) demonstrated superior tumor penetration capabilities, resulting in enhanced exposure of the majority of tumor cells to the STING agonist.[  ] 

Engineered adjuvants hold significant potential for advancing the performance and versatility of mRNA vaccines. The development in engineered adjuvants could allow for improved activation of DCs, enhanced cytotoxic T‐cell responses, and augmented antigen presentation efficiency. Furthermore, their ability to synergize with mRNA vaccines may provide new opportunities for customizing vaccine formulations, modulating the immune environment, promoting a sustained anti‐tumor response while minimizing off‐target effects. In this part, we focused on co‐delivery of engineered adjuvants and mRNA antigens to promote the maturation of DCs, thus facilitating the antigen presentation of their mRNA nanovaccines for synergic effect of cancer immunity activation.

Encapsulating mRNA within TLR activating nanocarriers facilitates endosomal escape and enhances mRNA immunization through TLR activation. Zhang et al. synthesized a diverse library of poly(amidoamine) (PAMAM)‐lipid polymers featuring varying lipid tail lengths (6, 8, 12, 14, and 16 carbons) for encapsulating mRNA alongside DSPE‐PEG 2000 (Figure  12 ). Their investigations revealed that PAMAM with a 12‐carbon tail (referred to as C1) exhibited potent stimulation of the TLR4 signalling pathway, leading to enhanced activation of DCs. Utilizing C1 to encapsulate OVA‐mRNA resulted in nanoparticles that significantly impeded tumor growth in mice bearing MC38‐OVA and B16‐OVA tumors.[  ] In a subsequent study, a new TLR7/8 agonist with two 12‐carbon lipids (designated as C12‐TLRa), demonstrated multiple interactions with the TLR7 receptor. Integration of C12‐TLRa into LNP formulations improved mRNA translation by enhancing endosome escape. Consequently, the mRNA/C12‐TLRa LNPs elicited robust cellular and humoral immune responses in vivo.[  ] 

STING activating materials developed for mRNA delivery can enable both tumor cytosolic delivery and STING activation‐promoted mRNA immunization. Recently, a series of nanomaterials, especially some cationic lipids or polymers containing amino groups, have been verified to activate STING pathway. Compared to co‐delivery of mRNA and STING agonists, delivery of mRNA by STING activating nanocarriers is more convenient and feasible, as these cationic nanocarriers readily encapsulate mRNA via electrostatic attraction.[  ] The activation of STING pathway and the subsequent promotion of mRNA immunization is highly dependent on the type of amine group and the molecular weight of amine‐functional polymers. For example, Miao et al. designed and synthesized a comprehensive library of ionizable lipid‐like materials to identify optimal mRNA delivery vehicles while eliciting potent and specific immune responses for in vivo mRNA delivery.[  ] Employing a three‐dimensional multi‐component reaction system, over 1,000 lipid formulations were synthesized and meticulously evaluated for their vaccine potential. The most promising formulations from this extensive screening elicited robust immune responses and demonstrated notable efficacy in inhibiting tumor growth and prolonging survival in melanoma and human papillomavirus E7 tumor models in vivo. Notably, the top‐performing lipids shared a common structural motif comprising an unsaturated lipid tail, a dihydroimidazole linker, and cyclic amine head groups. Further investigation revealed that these formulations induce maturation of antigen‐presenting cells and enhanced efficacy of mRNA immunization via the intracellular STING pathway rather than TLRs (Figure  13A–C).[  ] 

Recently, Zhang et al. synthesized a series of palmitoyl‐poly(2‐(dimethylamino) ethyl methacrylate) (PD) polymers, which were incorporated into LNPs to create PD LNPs. These nanoparticles were specifically designed to enhance mRNA delivery, improve lymph node targeting, and activate the antitumor immunity via STING pathways (Figure 13D–G). In vitro transfection study revealed that PD LNPs significantly enhanced mRNA expression compared to conventional LNP (CLNP). By incorporating PD polymers to LNP at the weight ratio of 2.5% (relative to the weight of ionizable lipid), PD18 LNP exhibited the optimal stimulation effect on DC2.4 cells and antigen presentation efficiency. In murine cancer models, PD18 LNPs showed remarkable tumor growth inhibition and prolonged survival compared to CLNPs, highlighting their potential as an advanced delivery platform for mRNA‐based cancer vaccines.[  ] 

Notably, polysaccharide nanoparticles with high molecular weights chitosan triggered STING‐mediated autophagy and NOD‐like receptor thermal protein domain associated protein 3 (NLRP3) inflammasome signalling. Wu et al. prepared four polysaccharide nanoparticles composed of chitosan with various molecular weights to encapsulate and deliver mRNA antigens.[  ] Consequently, these nanoparticles enhanced antigen cross‐presentation, DC maturation, CD4+/CD8+ T cell responses, and humoral immune responses, leading to superior mRNA antigen‐specific immune responses both in vitro and in vivo. Conversely, polysaccharide nanoparticles with low MW chitosan induced NLRP3 signalling without activating STING or autophagy, resulting in a less robust immune response. These studies not only reveal that STING‐activating nanocarriers can promote adaptive immunity of mRNA vaccines but also offer some insights for the design principles of nanoscale immunomodulators tailored for mRNA vaccines.

Co‐Delivery of mRNA‐Encoded Antigens and mRNA‐Encoded STING Adjuvants by Nanocarrier

As above‐mentioned, the activation of STING pathway by the extraneous STING agonists can improve the efficacy of mRNA vaccines by promoting advantageous adaptive immunity. Recent research investigated the co‐delivery of mRNA‐encoded antigens and mRNA‐encoded STING adjuvants using lipid materials, which constitutively activate the STING pathway via the upregulation of STING‐associated proteins.[  ] Tse et al. developed the co‐delivery of mRNA‐encoded STINGV155M and mRNA‐encoded antigen in a lipid nanoparticle for improved cancer immunotherapy, demonstrating the proof‐of‐concept utility of an mRNA‐encoded genetic adjuvant.[  ] The mRNA‐encoded STINGV155M significantly increased the secretion of IFN‐I, and thus promoted the adaptive immunity of mRNA‐encoded antigen, resulting in decreased tumor growth and prolonged survival of vaccinated mice with human papillomavirus tumors. Similarly, Zhivaki et al. reported the use of mRNA‐encoded a self‐DNA reactive variants of cGAS (cGASΔN) LNPs, which could induce robust activation of STING pathway and markedly enhanced antibody response to mRNA‐encoded antigen LNPs.[  ] In addition to STING activation, this mRNA‐encoded genetic mutation strategy avoids T cell cytotoxicity induced by traditional STING agonist, and effectively activate the STING pathway even in STING‐silenced cancers, where traditional STING agonists generally fail to achieve satisfactory STING activation.[  ] Liu et al. delivered mRNA‐encoded STINGR284S via mRNA‐encoded antigen LNPs, which reactivated the STING pathway and improved antigen presentation in STING‐silenced tumors such as pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma and Merkel cell carcinoma.[  ] Additionally, this approach hardly caused adverse effects on the proliferation of lymphocytic immune cells, and thus overcame the toxicity and limitations of traditional STING agonists.

Synthetic STING/TLR agonists and engineered delivery materials with inherent immunostimulatory properties have demonstrated remarkable potential in enhancing mRNA vaccine by synergistically promoting robust innate activation and antigen‐specific adaptive immune responses. However, current studies face significant challenges, including suboptimal spatiotemporal coordination between adjuvant activation and mRNA antigen expression, limited lymphatic organ targeting ability, and unexpected safety concerns such as excessive systemic inflammation or off‐target immune activation. Furthermore, the structural diversity and physicochemical complexity of these synthetic agonists impede rational design and optimization for tailored immunogenicity profiles. The emerging AI technologies may offer an robust tool to address these concerns. For example machine learning‐guided agonist structure optimization, with high‐throughput screening platforms can accelerate the development of next‐generation mRNA‐adjuvant formulations. Such approaches will enable precise tuning of adjuvant potency, timing, and biodistribution to maximize vaccine efficacy while minimizing adverse effects, ultimately facilitating the rational design of safer and more effective mRNA vaccine delivery systems.

Challenges and Perspectives

mRNA cancer vaccine has the potential for effective cancer immunotherapies, nevertheless, ensuring efficient mRNA delivery to target cells while achieving optimal immune activation remains a significant challenge. Strategies to improve mRNA stability, enhance its uptake by immune cells, and promote effective antigen presentation are essential for maximizing vaccine potency. As demonstrated by preclinical studies discussed here, current strategies in enhancing mRNA cancer vaccines provide clear motivation for clinical testing of many concepts. While advanced mRNA technologies offer tremendous therapeutic potential in cancer immunotherapy, they also carry certain risks and side effects that warrant careful consideration. Adverse effects often arise from the intrinsic immunogenicity of mRNA, which—despite chemical modifications—can still activate innate immune sensors such as Toll‐like receptors and RIG‐I‐like receptors, leading to unwanted inflammation and cytokine release. Moreover, delivery vehicles such as LNPs may trigger complement activation or cause local tissue toxicity.[  ] These safety challenges can restrict dosing strategies, reduce patient tolerability, and introduce regulatory complexities, ultimately hindering the clinical advancement of mRNA‐based therapeutics.

Emerging clinical and preclinical studies have reported adverse effects such as injection site inflammation, fever, pain, and transient systemic inflammation following LNP administration. These effects are partly attributed to the intrinsic adjuvant activity of certain ionizable lipids,[  ] which can activate innate immune pathways including TLRs and RIG‐I‐like receptors, leading to the release of inflammatory cytokines and chemokines, recruit neutrophils and trigger multiple pro‐inflammatory signaling cascades.[  ] Additionally, PEGylation, often employed to prolong circulation time and enhance nanoparticle stability, can induce the formation of anti‐PEG antibodies (IgG and IgM).[  ] These antibodies not only accelerate LNP clearance upon repeated administration but can also provoke complement activation‐related pseudoallergy (CARPA) and, in rare cases, severe anaphylactic‐like reactions.[  ] With the growing prevalence of PEG exposure in pharmaceuticals and consumer products, pre‐existing anti‐PEG immunity may further compromise vaccine safety and efficacy. Thus, there is increasing interest in developing next‐generation ionizable lipids and non‐immunogenic PEG alternatives, to mitigate these risks. Recent studies have demonstrated that LNPs with rationally designed compostions can reduce inflammatory response while maintaining efficient mRNA delivery and minimizing cytokine induction.[  ] The development of PEG substitutes like polysarcosine and zwitterionic coatings have shown promise in reducing complement activation and anti‐PEG antibody responses, improving both the safety and repeatability of mRNA LNPs. Futher research should focus on further refining these modifications and evaluating their long‐term effects to ensure the safety profile and effectiveness of these advanced mRNA delivery systems.

The discovery of antigens with high specificity for tumor cells and minimal cross‐reactivity with normal tissues is essential for maximizing therapeutic outcomes.[  ] Antigen identification is a critical determinant of the efficacy and safety of cancer vaccines, as targeting non‐tumor tissues can lead to off‐target immune responses and autoimmune toxicity. Ideally, tumor antigens should be highly expressed or uniquely presented on tumor cells, absent from healthy tissues, and capable of eliciting strong T cell responses. Recent advances in high‐throughput sequencing and bioinformatics have enabled the identification of tumor‐specific neoantigens,[  ] which are particularly attractive due to their exclusive expression in malignant cells.[  ] These technologies allow for whole‐exome and transcriptome sequencing of patient tumors, followed by computational prediction of MHC binding and TCR recognition.[  ] However, the immunogenicity of predicted neoantigens remains variable, and not all identified candidates elicit effective T cell responses. To improve antigen selection, several strategies have been developed. For example, current efforts focused on integrating multiomics data—such as genome, transcriptome, and proteome—to validate the expression and presentation of candidate antigens at the RNA, protein, and MHC‐bound peptide levels.[  ] Some machine learning algorithms are also being employed to better predict antigen processing, presentation, and immunogenicity.[  ] Moving forward, the development of more sophisticated AI models and high‐throughput validation techniques, as well as diverse animal models will be essential to overcome current hurdles and optimize antigen identification accurately across different species and populations.

While precise antigen selection plays a key role in ensuring vaccination specificity and minimizing autoimmune risks, effective delivery strategies are equally vital to prevent off‐target effects and enhance the safety profile of mRNA‐based immunotherapies. High doses of mRNA may lead to off‐target effects, unintended tissue transfection, or systemic inflammation.[  ] To mitigate these risks, nanocarriers equipped with targeting moieties are employed to enhance tissue specificity and reduce non‐specific distribution, while maintaining on‐target stability and efficacy.[  ] Besides selective delivery to lymphatic organs, targeting specific cellular subtypes is critical for enhancing the efficacy of cancer vaccine delivery. Among immune cells, dendritic cells (DCs) are the most potent antigen‐presenting cells, capable of initiating robust T cell‐mediated responses. Within this population, conventional type 1 dendritic cells (cDC1s), such as CD8⁺ DCs or CD103+ DCs in mice and CD141⁺ DCs in humans, are critical for cross‐presenting tumor antigens to CD8⁺ cytotoxic T lymphocytes and are therefore highly desirable targets for cancer vaccines.[  ] In contrast, plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs) and certain macrophage subsets may promote immunosuppressive responses depending on the tumor microenvironment.[  ] Thus, precise delivery system focuses on targeting immunostimulatory APC subsets—such as cDC1s—while avoiding tolerogenic or suppressive cell types, are anticipated to optimize antigen presentation, enhance immunogenicity, and reduce off‐target effects. Furthremore, advancing the understanding of mRNA nanoparticles with immune cells, and these cellular interactions within the tumor microenvironment will be essential for designing more effective and safe cancer vaccines with improved therapeutic outcomes.

mRNA cancer vaccine safety is also influenced by factors such as dose, administration route, and dosing frequency, all of which must be systematically optimized to balance efficacy with tolerability. Despite growing interest, comprehensive toxicity data across preclinical models remain limited. Observations such as complement activation or liver and spleen toxicity are frequently reported in animal studies but do not reliably predict clinical outcomes. Therefore, selecting appropriate animal models for evaluating toxicity, biodistribution, pharmacokinetics/pharmacodynamics, immunogenicity, and tumorigenicity is a critical challenge in nonclinical development. Regulatory agencies like the FDA and EMA recommend adopting a risk‐based approach for advanced therapies, allowing flexibility in study design to address product‐specific concerns. In parallel, in vitro assays should be used to define the biological activity and establish potency relevant to the intended clinical application, supporting a more predictive and translational safety assessment framework.

Emerging advances in computational RNA design, driven by artificial intelligence (AI), are rapidly surpassing traditional thermodynamics‐based approaches. These data‐driven tools are accelerating the optimization of RNA sequences, facilitating lipid nanoparticle discovery, and refining structure–activity relationship predictions with unprecedented speed and precision.[  ] By integrating ML‐powered design with cutting‐edge delivery technologies, the development of next‐generation mRNA vaccines is becoming increasingly streamlined and effective. With continued innovation and multidisciplinary collaboration, mRNA cancer vaccines are poised to become a cornerstone of precision oncology.
