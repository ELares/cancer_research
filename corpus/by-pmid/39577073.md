# Targeted therapeutic strategies for Nectin-4 in breast cancer: Recent advances and future prospects.

## Abstract

Nectin-4 is a cell adhesion molecule which has gained more and more attention as a therapeutic target in cancer recently. Overexpression of Nectin-4 has been observed in various tumors, including breast cancer, and is associated with tumor progression. Enfortumab vedotin(EV)is an antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) targeting Nectin-4, which has been approved by FDA for the treatment of urothelial carcinoma. Notably, Nectin-4 was also investigated as a target for breast cancer in preclinical and clinical settings. Nectin-4-targeted approaches, such as ADCs, oncolytic viruses, photothermal therapy and immunotherapy, have shown promising results in early-phase clinical trials. These therapies offer novel strategies for delivering targeted treatments to Nectin-4-expressing cancer cells, enhancing treatment efficacy and minimizing off-target effects. In conclusion, this review aims to provide an overview of the latest advances in understanding the role of Nectin-4 in breast cancer and discuss the future development prospects of Nectin-4 targeted agents.

Introduction

Breast cancer (BC) has become the most common malignant tumor among women worldwide. It was also the common cancer with highest incidence among Chinese women according to GLOBOCAN 2020. Despite mounting evidence that the mortality rate of breast cancer has been continuously dropping, it is presently declining at a slower rate than before. On the one hand, the popularization of early screening technology and improving clinical diagnosis and treatment have benefited many BC patients. On the other hand, it will be challenging for standard therapies such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy and endocrine therapy to be effective over the long term in locally advanced BC or metastatic breast cancer patients. Consequently, to further improve patient outcomes, exploring new targets and developing corresponding therapies are critically required.

Cell-cell adhesion is known as the process of adjacent cells adhering to one another through the interaction of cell adhesion molecules (CAM). Cell-cell adhesion regulates a wide range of biological activities, such as intercellular signaling, cell movement, tissue growth and development. In contrast, abnormalities in the adhesion process may lead to various diseases, including neurological diseases and cancer. Among the four family types of cell adhesion molecules (CAM), selectins and cadherins are calcium-dependent, while immunoglobulin superfamily (IgSF) and integrins are calcium-independent. Nectins, a subfamily of the IgSF, consists of four members (Nectin-1 to 4), each of which is encoded by a corresponding poliovirus receptor-related (PVRL) gene. Among them, the PVRL4 gene, localized on chromosome 1q23.3, encodes Nectin-4. According to the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) cohort, amplification of the Nectin-4 gene is prevalent in a majority of solid tumors. The highest rates of amplification are observed in bladder cancer (17 %), cholangiocarcinoma (14 %), hepatocellular carcinoma (12 %), breast cancer (9 %), and lung adenocarcinoma (7 %).

Nectin-4 is highly expressed in human tissues during the embryonic stage but is infrequently present in healthy adult tissues. Previous studies have confirmed that Nectin-4 has significant expression in a number of malignancies, including ovarian cancer, lung cancer, pancreatic cancer and breast cancer. Remarkably, Nectin-4 can be considered as a histological or serological biomarker and an independent factor of poor prognosis in breast cancer. Additionally, Nectin-4 is also closely related to tumor growth, angiogenesis, metastasis and recurrence. It is noteworthy that some clinical studies of antibody drug conjugates (ADCs) that target Nectin-4 in a range of malignancies, including breast cancer, have been widely conducted.

Despite mounting proof revealed that Nectin-4 is crucial in breast cancer, the latest findings have not yet been systematically summarized. Therefore, this review will discuss the biological functions, known molecular mechanisms and diagnostic and prognostic value of Nectin-4. We also reviewed the progress of using the Nectin-4 protein as a novel therapeutic target for breast cancer.

The function of Nectin-4 in biology

Molecular structure of Nectin-4

Nectin-4 is a 66 kDa single-pass type I transmembrane protein, which is a member of the Nectin family and shares similar extracellular, transmembrane and intracellular domains with the other three members. The extracellular domain, which has two C2-type and one V-type immunoglobulin-like (Ig-like) domains, is directly engaged in cell-cell adhesion. While the intracellular part can interact with the actin filament-binding protein afadin and involve in intracellular signaling.

Through the IgV domain of the extracellular portion, Nectin proteins can interact with each other in both homophilic and heterophilic forms, thereby regulating the adhesion association between cells. In addition to the interaction between Nectin family members, some molecules such as Nectin-2, were reported to bind to immune cell surface receptors CD226 and TIGIT, eventually exerting the immunomodulatory functions. The C-terminal of nectin-4 contains a conserved motif Gly-His-Leu-Val, which can bind to the PDZ domain of afadin.

The role of Nectin-4 in cell adhesion

Through the homophilic/heterophilic interaction, Nectin-4 creates Ca2+-independent adherens junctions (AJs) and tight junctions (TJs) for cell adhesion. As a protein binding with F-actin, afadin connects the interaction between Nectin-4 and actin cytoskeleton proteins, eventually activating multiple signaling pathways and affecting intercellular signal transduction. In addition to directly interacting with F-actin, afadin also binds to α-catenin through ponsin-vinculin and afadin DIL-domain-interacting protein (ADIP)-α-actinin structures, consequently indirectly regulating F-actin. In conclusion, during the initial stages of cell adhesion, a number of molecules, including afadin, cadherin, and F-actin, are recruited to Nectin-related cell adhesion sites. By interacting with Nectin-4 directly or indirectly, they take role in the regulation of intercellular adhesion.

Physiological and pathological function of Nectin-4

Nectin-4 can form homo-cis-dimers or hetero-trans-dimers to promote cell adhesion and aggregation, which is the process that underlies complex biology associated with multicellular organisms. In addition to being distributed on the cell membrane, soluble Nectin-4 is released into the peripheral circulation. Nectin-4, in contrast to Nectin-1 to 3, is uncommon in healthy adult tissues but is highly expressed in embryonic and placental tissues. Evidence suggests that Nectin-4 plays a vital role in the development of the ectoderm and the pregnancy process. For instance, the autosomal recessive disorder known as ectodermal dysplasia-syndactyly syndrome is attributed to mutations that lead to the abnormal expression and dysfunction of Nectin-4. Furthermore, Nectin-4 overexpression in the trophoblast cells can contribute to abnormal immunological reactions, leading to preeclamptic pregnancy.

Additionally, Nectin-4 is also involved in the development of the mammary gland development and transmission of prolactin signaling. The formation of the lactating region in the mammary gland during pregnancy is facilitated by a cell-adhesive transposition associated with both Nectin-1 and Nectin-4. Nectin-4 has also been verified to interact with prolactin receptors and promote the transcription of the alveolar development gene β-casein, resulting in the lactogenic differentiation of the mammary gland. Consequently, further researches are urgently needed to ascertain whether Nectin-4 has a pathogenic function in breast cancer, particularly considering the involvement of prolactin in breast cancer development.

Role of Nectin-4 in progression of malignancies

Nectin-4 promotes tumor proliferation and migration

Previous researches have demonstrated that knockdown of Nectin-4 can inhibit the proliferation of lung and pancreatic cancer cells. There is a significantly positive correlation between Nectin-4 expression and the proliferation marker Ki67. intriguingly, Rac1, a crucial GTPase for tumor growth and migration, facilitates the formation of lamellipodia. Nectin-4 is capable of interacting with afadin to activate Rac1 through the PI3K/AKT pathway, boosting the growth and metastasis of gallbladder cancer. The activation of Rac1 through this pathway also contributes to Nectin-4-mediated tumor cell proliferation and migration. In addition, Nectin-4 has also been reported to enhance tumor cell growth by mediating DNA repair. ADAM-17, a proteolytic enzyme activated under hypoxic conditions, specifically cleaves Nectin-4 into extracellular and intracellular domains. Remarkably, the intracellular domain of Nectin-4 can significantly participate in DNA repair, promoting tumor proliferation, while the extracellular domain may serve as a serological marker forbreast cancer metastasis.

Nectin-4 promotes tumor angiogenesis

In tumor progression, angiogenesis and lymphangiogenesis are regarded as essential processes. The key factor that stimulates angiogenesis within the tumor microenvironment is vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Nectin-4 expression has been found to be positively correlated with VEGF and the intratumoral microvessel density (IMD) in pancreatic cancer. The mechanism of Nectin-4 involved in tumor angiogenesis has been initially explored. When Nectin-4 interactes with integrin-4 in breast cancer cells, the iNOS/Src axis is activated, which in turn promotes angiogenesis. Nectin-4 may also promote angiogenesis through PI3K-AKT-mediated endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) pathway. Strikingly, knockdown of Nectin-4 reduces angiogenesis in angiosarcoma, indicating that Nectin-4 may represent as an attractive target for cancer therapy develpoment.

Nectin-4 promotes epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT)

EMT refers to the process by which epithelial cells transform into a mesenchymal phenotype under both physiological and pathological conditions. Tumor epithelial cells may lose their cell polarity and ability to adhere to neighboring cells when EMT is triggered, which may lead to invasion and migration. Therefore, EMT is a crucial factor in the development of a variety of malignancies, including lung, liver, pancreatic, and breast cancers. Overexpression of Nectin-4 contributes to the promotion of EMT by upregulating the expression of mesenchymal markers such as vimentin and N-cadherin and downregulating epithelial markers like E-cadherin. Through regulating the levels of proteins corelated with EMT via the PI3K/AKT pathway, Nectin-4 knockdown has been clarified to reduce EMT. Moreover, overexpression of Nectin-4 can influence the development of actin fibers by connecting with afadin and inducing the activation of PI3K/AKT pathway, which subsequently activates Rac1 to control EMT.Taken together, these findings suggest that Nectin-4 could be a significant regulator in the EMT process and a potential therapeutic target for cancers where EMT is a driving factor.

Nectin-4 is a biomarker for cancer stem cell

Among cancer cell populations, there exist a subset known as cancer stem cells (CSCs) that share characteristics with healthy stem or progenitor cells. All cell types observed in a particular cancer can be produced by CSCs since they have the capacity for self-renewal, differentiation, and tumorigenicity. CSCs are regarded as one of the main contributors to cancer metastasis, treatment resistance, and recurrence. It has been revealed that Nectin-4 is another biomarker of CSCs in addition to some well-known markers such as EpCAM, CD44, CD133 and SOX2. Nectin-4 expression was significantly elevated in breast cancer stem cells (BCSCs). Some studies have demonstrated that Nectin-4 regulates the ability of CSCs to self-renew and proliferate by triggering WNT/β-Catenin signaling transduction via PI3K/AKT pathway. Nectin-4 has also been confirmed to be highly expressed in cervical CSCs that are resistant to 5-FU, highlighting its potential as a biomarker for various CSCs and its implication in cancer therapy resistance.

Nectin-4 plays a role in the modulation of tumor immunity

Nectin-4 has the capacity to interact with receptors on specific immune cells, including NK and T cells, thereby modulating the immune response to tumor cells through signal transduction pathways. The inhibitory immune checkpoint receptor TIGIT is expressed on the surface of cytotoxic T cells and natural killer cells. Nectin-4 has been identified as a novel tumor-specific TIGIT ligand, distinguishing itself from other Nectin proteins by its exclusive binding to TIGIT. The immunological stimulation of cytotoxic T and NK cells can be reduced by the interaction between Nectin4 and TIGIT. Anti-Nectin-4 antibodies that block binding to TIGIT have been illustrated to increase tumor killing in vivo and in vitro, making immunotherapy more effective and targeted against cancer cells.

Nectin-4 induces drug resistance in tumor cells

Nectin-4 has been identified to engage with afadin, thereby activating the AKT-NF-κB pathway, which in turn fosters the growth, migration of colon tumor cells, and the development of 5-FU resistance. Additional research suggests that Nectin-4 may also influence the 5-FU resistance of cervical CSCs and promote the growth, invasion, and metastasis of these cells in a manner analogous to the NOTCH signaling cascade. In trastuzumab-resistant breast cancer cells, Nectin-4 can interact with the unique IV domain to ErbB2 variants, enhancing their dimerization and tyrosine phosphorylation, and effectively activating the PI3K-AKT signaling pathway to promote DNA synthesis.

In summary, Nectin-4 is crucial for the development and growth of malignant tumors and also a reliable biomarker because of its specific high expression. Moreover, Nectin-4 actively participates in various tumor-related processes such as angiogenesis, lymphangiogenesis, migration and proliferation, highlighting its multifaceted impact on cancer biology (Fig. 1).

Nectin-4 is a potential diagnostic and prognostic marker for BC

The predictive value of Nectin-4 in BC

Nectin-4 has been verified through extensive researches as both a diagnostic and prognostic marker for BC. Notably, while Nectin-4 is undetectable in the serum of healthy individuals, it has been found circulating in approximately half of the serum samples from patients with metastatic breast cancer (MBC). In vitro, soluble Nectin-4 can be found in breast cancer cells' supernatant, implying its potential utility as a serological marker. More than 200 serum samples from MBC and non-MBC patients as well as 57 BC tissues have demonstrated thatNectin-4 was significantly overexpressed in both tumor tissues and serum of MBC patients. Nectin-4 overexpression was connected with therapeutic outcomes, indicating that Nectin-4 could serve as a valuable tool for histological and serological diagnosis of breast cancer, as well as a predictor of therapeutic response. However, other studies have shown that Nectin-4 expression were elevated in recurrent tumors and were positively correlated with breast cancer aggressiveness, recurrence and lymph node metastasis. Taken together, these findings underscore the need for further research to precisely determine the roles and underlying mechanisms of Nectin-4 in the prediction of treatment efficacy and recurrence in breast cancer. Such studies are urgently needed to enhance our understanding and potentially improve clinical management strategies.

The role of Nectin-4 in promoting BC progression and its mechanism

Strikingly, cancer cells are distinguished by their ability to proliferate independently of attachment to the extracellular matrix. Nectin-4 has been shown to stimulate the autonomous proliferation of BC cells by activating the integrin β4/SHP-2/c-Src pathway, which subsequently contributes to the formation of cancer cell clusters through interactions with Nectin-1.Knockdown of Nectin-4 inhibited the capacity of BC cells to proliferate unattached and separate from the extracellular matrix, as well as the formation of cancer cell clusters. As previously mentioned, the enhancement of PI3K/AKT signaling pathway by Nectin-4 has also been identified to be implicated in the advancement of BC, facilitating tumor growth, EMT, metastasis and angiogenesis. Like angiogenesis, lymphangiogenesis is equally critical for the invasion and dissemination of breast cancer to distant organs. Nectin-4 was associated with CXCR4 expression, lymphatic vessel density, and lymph node angiogenesis. Under stressful circumstances, Nectin-4 stimulates the production of CXCR4 and CXCL12, thereby activating lymphatic endothelial receptor 1 (LYVE-1)-mediated lymphatic lymphangiogenesis.

The role of Nectin-4 in breast cancer subtypes

Lum A BC

Luminal-A BCs are typically characterized by the presence of a higher number of estrogen receptor (ER), high expression of progesterone receptor (PR), negativity for epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), and low expression of Ki-67. In a study with 57 BC samples, Nectin-4 expression was inversely connected with luminal-like biomarkers estrogen receptor (ER) and progesterone receptor (PR). Another study including 197 patients with early-stage BC showed that Nectin-4 was differentially distributed on the cell membrane (m-Nectin-4) and in the cytoplasm (c-Nectin-4) (respectively 75 % and 18 %). Patients with early luminal A BC who had positive m-Nectin-4 had a worse disease-free survival (DFS) and distant recurrence-free survival (DRFS). According to this study, m-Nectin-4 is a poor predictive factor for early-stage luminal A BC and may indicate the recurrence risk as well as be a prospective therapeutic target.

Lum B BC

Luminal-B BCs are typically ER positive and HER2 negative or positive. A retrospective investigation of 147 patients with luminal B BC evaluated the correlation between Nectin-4 and common clinicopathological variables and prognostic indicators. Except for a positive association with tumor size, there was no conclusive connection between Nectin-4 and other factors such as ER, PR and Ki-67 proliferation index. However, Nectin-4 mRNA level was negatively associated with survival outcomes such as overall survival (OS), DFS, and DRFS, implying that Nectin-4 is likely a critical negative prognostic parameter and potential treatment option of luminal B BC.

TNBC

A clinical study encompassing 1248 TNBC cases showed that Nectin-4 was highly expressed in tumor tissues. In addition, increased Nectin-4 mRNA levels was linked to a shorter metastasis-free survival (MFS), indicating a negative predictive value for recurrence. However, a separate study involving 148 TNBC samples patients with Nectin-4 overexpression experienced a prolonged OS. Furthermore, it has been reported that elevated Nectin-4 expression was associated with earlier tumor stages and an no lymph node metastases. Although these two studies revealed that Nectin-4 was significantly expressed in the majority of TNBC samples, the correlation between Nectin-4 expression and prognosis remains ambiguous. Notably, the survival outcomes may also be influenced by factors not accounted for in these studies, such as oncogene expression, immune infiltration and treatment modalities. Consequently, there is an urgent need for research that includes more comprehensive clinicopathological data to further explore the role of Nectin-4 in TNBC prognosis and to ultimately resolve the existing discrepancies.

The therapeutic potential of targeting Nectin-4 in BC

Overview of ADCs and their clinical applications in BC

ADCs are composed of toxic molecules that are covalently connected with antibodies through chemical linkers, enabling them to specifically target tumor antigens and eliminate tumor cells. These ADCs recognize and bind tumor-specific antigens, leading to endosome formation through endocytosis. Upon cellular internalization, endosomes merge with lysosomes, where enzymes mediate the release of cytotoxic molecules (Fig. 2A). This process triggers apoptosis by inhibiting tubulin or causing DNA damage. Similar to monoclonal antibody, the antibody component of ADC exerts anti-tumor effects by inhibiting the corresponding signaling pathways such as PI3K/AKT axis. Additionally, ADCs can also bind to immune cell surface antigens in the tumor microenvironment to stimulate anti-tumor immunity. Importantly, ADCs have the capacity to not only target antigen-positive (Ag+) cells within tumors, but also to eliminate antigen-negative (Ag-) cells. This particular antitumor effect is referred to as the "bystander effect". Internalized and degraded by Ag + cells, ADC releases cytotoxic drug molecules that diffuse to antigen-negative (Ag-) cells, inducing cell death. The presence of a "bystander effect" is frequently related to the structural design of the ADC linker and the biochemical properties of the payload. Remarkably, the "bystander effect" allows ADC drugs to bypass the heterogeneity of antigen expression while maintaining stable and efficient anti-tumor effects. However, it may cause ADC drugs to deviate from its intended target, resulting in additional off-target toxicity. Regardless, the "bystander effect" will continue to be a focal point in the future development and efficacy assessment of ADC drugs.

Identifying an appropriate tumor antigen as a target is the key to precise ADC therapy. The optimal target should be produced selectively and abundantly on the tumor cells and be capable of internalizing the ADC, enabling the cytotoxic molecule to exert its impact. Currently, the targets of approved ADCs for treating solid tumors include HER2, Trop-2, Nectin4, EGFR and folate receptor α (FRα). Remarkably, the ADCs approved for breast cancer treatment mainly include T-DM1 and T-DXd targeting HER2, and sacituzumab govitecan targeting Trop-2. Meanwhile, some new targets including Nectin-4, HER3, LIV-1and Claudin 18.2 have also gradually attracted more and more attention of researchers.

Enfortumab vedotin (EV) and its clinical progress

EV is a monoclonal antibody that targets Nectin-4 and is conjugated to monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) via a protease-cleavable linker. MMAE, a synthetic analogue of dolastatin 10, prevents efficient mitosis by inhibiting tubulin assembly. In vitro studies have shown that EV can prevent the interaction between Nectin-4 and Nectin-1, a known promoter of breast cancer cell cluster formation. In a preclinical study, EV could bind to Nectin-4 with high affinity and induce tumor apoptosis. Furthermore, in vivo studies have demonstrated that EVs can induce tumor regression in xenograft models of a variety of malignancies, including BC. The antitumor effect of EV is significantly correlated with the level of Nectin-4 amplification in the target organ and is dose-dependent. In a multicenter cohort of EV-treated mUC patients, those with Nectin-4 amplification demonstrated a markedly higher ORR compared to a non-amplified subgroup. The potential efficacy of EV in the patients suffering locally advanced or metastatic urothelial cancer (la/mUC) was further demonstrated in subsequent clinical studies, offering a promising therapeutic alternative for patients who have progressed following treatment with platinum, immunotherapy or erdafitinib.

The therapeutic potential of nectin-4-targeted ADCs in BC

Due to the high expression of Nectin-4 in BC, ADCs targeting Nectin-4 hold considerable therapeutic potential. Pavlova et al. have reported that a monoclonal antibody can significantly inhibit Nectin-4-driven activation of integrin β4/SHP-2/c-Src, thereby preventing the formation of cancer cell clusters facilitated by the interaction between Nectin-4 and Nectin-1. As an approved ADC, the therapeutic potential of EV in BC has also been explored. Preclinical studies have showed that EVs can effectively inhibit tumor growth in xenograft models of BC. Moreover, the efficacy of EV monotherapy was assessed in TNBC and HR+/HER2- BC in the EV-202 trial. The confirmed objective response rate (ORR) was 15.6 % in the HER2- BC cohort and 19 % in the TNBC cohort. Although EV demonstrated antitumor activity with tolerable side effects in both breast cancer cohorts, it did not meet the pre-established criteria for the overall response rate.

N41mab-vcMMAE, another ADC that targets Nectin-4, is created by coupling MMAE and anti-Nectin-4 antibody N41mab through a cleavable linker. It has a significant and sustained efficacy in both local primary tumors and metastatic lesions, and the therapeutic efficiency is correlated with dosage and the level of Nectin-4 expression of tumor tissues. A recent study has clarified that P-glycoprotein (P-gp), an efflux pump, was upregulated in tumors resistant to N41mab-vcMMAE. The combination of ADCs with P-gp inhibitors has been shown to reverse drug resistance in tumors, further underscoring the durable efficacy of N41mab-vcMMAE and its potential for long-term treatment of breast cancer. In conclusion, Nectin-4-targeted ADCs have displayed promising potential in preclinical researches on breast cancer, and additional clinical trials are required to accelerate their approval and clinical application. Oncolytic virus targeting Nectin-4

Oncolytic viruses are anti-tumor biological agents characterized by weakening the virulence of corresponding pathogens and increasing their specificity and lytic efficacy for tumor cells. Morbillivirus (MV) is frequently used to engineer oncolytic viruses. Its envelope contains glycoproteins hemagglutinin (H protein) and fusion protein (F protein), which guide the virus to attach to infected cells and fuse with the cell membrane respectively. As an oncolytic virus, MV has the advantages of low mutation risk and high safety, thus making treatment easily tolerated by frail tumor patients. Interestingly, Nectin-4, which is highly expressed explicitly in tumor tissues, was also illustrated as an epithelial MV receptor, making it an ideal target for oncolytic MVs.

The H protein binds to extracellular segment V domain of Nectin-4 and causes a structure alteration in the F protein, thereby mediating plasma membrane fusion for cell entry (Fig. 2B). When MV binds to Nectin-4, the Rac1/PAK1-dependent signaling cascade is activated, which causes actin-mediated membrane protrusion and ultimately completes endocytosis. MVs could easily infect breast cells with high expression of Nectin-4, thereby being internalized and exerting an oncolytic effect. Oncolytic MV can also enhance the anti-tumor efficacy by activating the immune system. For instance, MV was confirmed to interact with signaling lymphocyte activation molecules (SLAM/CD150) to enter immune cells. By enhancing costimulatory surface activation markers CD80 and CD86, MV could also stimulate dendritic cells and trigger a natural anti-tumor immune reaction.

Progress of oncolytic viruses targeting Nectin-4 in breast cancer

In recent years, the research on the oncolytic MV with Nectin-4 as the receptor in breast cancer treatment is constantly improving. Sugiyama et al. generated MVs that selectively did not recognize SLAM (rMV-SLAMblind) and evaluated their oncolytic effects in breast cancer. It showed that rMV-SLAMblind exhibited significant and sustained oncolytic activity using Nectin-4 as a specific receptor. Further studies suggested that rMV-SLAMblind effectively infected and suppressed TNBC tumors, and the oncolytic activity is related to the expression of Nectin-4. Repeated intravenous injections of the virus at higher doses into healthy cynomolgus monkeys did not cause any clinical symptoms of measles, such as rash, anorexia, diarrhea, weight loss, and leukopenia, thus providing a preliminary validation of its safety. In addition, some enhancers of oncolytic MV therapy have also been reported, further enriching oncolytic therapeutic strategies for breast cancer. For instance, combining ursolic acid (UA) and its nanoparticles (UA-NP) and MVs could inhibit the growth of Nectin-4-positive breast cancer cells. Interestingly, the combined use of baicalein (BAI) and cinnamaldehyde (CIN) could also enhance the killing effect of oncolytic MVs on Nectin-4-positive breast cancer cells. In summary, oncolytic MV targeting Nectin-4 has a significant curative effect on breast cancer and has the potential to become a candidate biological agent in future clinical treatment.

The role of Nectin-4 in immunotherapy of breast cancer

Tumor-specific antigens identified by T cells are critical to the three major approaches to cancer immunotherapy, including T cell-stimulating antibodies, adoptive antitumor T cells and tumor vaccines. Therefore, Nectin-4 can potentially be a candidate target for breast cancer immunotherapy. Lopez et al. generated HLA-A2-restricted cells that can recognize Nectin-4 peptides (VLVPPLPSL) cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) clones. In vitro, functional anti-Nectin-4 CTLs can successfully lyse Nectin-4-positive breast cancer and cause tumor regression. Nectin-4 is also a specific ligand of the immune checkpoint TIGIT, and blocking their combination can enhance the anti-tumor effect of CTL and NK cells. Taken together, combining anti-TIGIT antibody and ADC targeting Nectin-4 may exert a synergistic anti-tumor effect (Fig. 2C). In fact, there have been clinical applications of immune checkpoint inhibitors in combination with EV, which we will discuss further in the follow-up. Recently, the advancement of chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR-T) in malignant solid tumors has drawn attention due to its effective application in hematological malignancies. According to a recent study, CAR-T cells that targeted Nectin4 exhibited efficient cytotoxicity and exerted significant anti-tumor effects in metastatic tumor models. This indicated that Nectin-4 has the potential to serve as a CAR-T target in solid tumors, including breast cancer.

Imaging diagnosis and photothermal therapy of BC with mAb targeting Nectin-4

Single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) has demonstrated tremendous promise in precise diagnosis and individualized treatment of cancer. Antibody immuno-SPECT usually utilizes radioactive isotope-labeled ligands to bind to tumor-specific antigens to assess morphological imaging of tumors and detect changes in functional tumor markers. Photothermal therapy (PTT) is a minimally invasive treatment method that converts light energy into heat energy through photosensitizers such as nanoparticles to ablate tumor cells (Fig. 2D), and its extensive application value in breast cancer has been proven. Targeting PTT needs to be achieved by binding the corresponding tumor surface receptors through the conjugates of photothermal agents (PTAs) and specific ligands. Anti-Nectin-4 mAb is an ideal ligand for SPECT and targeted PTT. The radioactive probe designed with anti-Nectin-4 mAb successfully mediated SPECT imaging of TNBC, thus visualizing the Nectin-4 expression and providing a reference for subsequent targeted PTT. Furthermore, conjugates of indocyanine green (ICG), an already approved PTA, and an anti-Nectin-4 mAb successfully mediated targeted PTT which suppressed tumors in a TNBC xenograft model. In brief, SPECT imaging and PTT targeting Nectin-4 provide a new direction for diagnosis and treatment of TNBC.

Combination therapy of targeting Nectin-4

A combination therapy targeting multiple targets is frequently a superior strategy for enhancing therapeutic efficacy and overcoming malignancy resistance in comparison to monotherapy. A previous study has reported that ADC can activate multiple endoplasmic reticulum stress response pathways, leading to immunogenic tumor apoptosis and activating a therapeutic immune response involving T cells, which provided a foundation for further research into the strategy of combining ADC with immunotherapy. A Phase Ib/II study EV-103 is being conducted on patients with locally advanced or metastatic urothelial carcinoma (la/mUC) to evaluate the effectiveness of EV combined with antibody that target PD-1/PD-L1. Compared with monotherapy, EV + pembrolizumab (P) regimen resulted in higher objective response rate (ORR) and longer-lasting first-line therapy responses. The majority of treatment-related peripheral neuropathy and dermatologic toxicities observed in this study were less than Grade 2 in severity and resolved or improved with dose reduction or treatment interruption. This indicates that EV in combination with P has an acceptable safety profile. A subsequent phase III clinical study (EV-301) further compared the efficacy and safety of EV + P with the standard regimen of platinum-containing chemotherapy for advanced uroepithelial cancer. In comparison to the chemotherapy group, the EV + P group demonstrated significantly prolonged PFS (12.5 months vs. 6.3 months) and OS (31.5 months vs. 16.1 months), while exhibiting a reduced incidence of grade 3 or higher treatment-related adverse events. These findings indicate that the EV + P regimen has demonstrated superior efficacy and safety compared to platinum-containing chemotherapy in the treatment of advanced uroepithelial cancer. FDA has currently listed EV + P as the first-line therapy for la/mUC, which undoubtedly encourages the investigation of combined treatment strategies. Not only that, ongoing clinical studies such as EV-303, EV-304, and NCT05239624 have also assessed the antitumor activity and safety of EV + P as a neoadjuvant treatment for uroepithelial and bladder cancers (Table 2), thus promising to further expand the potential applications of the EV combination therapy strategy in urologic tumors.

In addition to immune checkpoint inhibitors, the combination of EV and TKI represents a primary area of investigation. Cabozantinib is a multi-targeted TKI that inhibits vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), mesenchymal epithelial transition factor (MET), and the receptor tyrosine kinase AXL. A phase I clinical trial (NCT04878029) investigated the combined use of cabozantinib and EV in mUC, and preliminary findings indicated that the combination strategy of EV and 20 mg of cabozantinib per day displayed early anti-tumor activity and was associated with manageable safety profiles. Moreover, Erdafitinib, a fibroblast growth factor receptor (FGFR) inhibitor, is recommended for the treatment of mUC with somatic FGFR2/3 mutations in cases of tumor progression following platinum-based chemotherapy. The preliminary findings of a phase Ib trial (NCT04963153) indicated that the combination of EV and Erdafitinib may have anti-tumor activity and was generally well-tolerated in mUC patients with FGFR2/3 mutations who had progressed after chemotherapy or immunotherapy.

Of note, the combination of EV and other ADCs represents an additional potential therapeutic strategy. The phase I clinical trial NCT04724018 constituted the initial investigation of the combination of EV and Sacituzumab Govitecan (SG), an ADC drug targeting Trop-2. At a safe dosage, this combination strategy achieved a noteworthy ORR of 70 % in patients with mUC who had progressed after treatment with platinum-based chemotherapy or immunotherapy. Subsequent multicenter phase II studies and even the exploration of a three-agent combination regimen of EV + SG + pembrolizumab are equally promising.

With the continuous enrichment and improvement of targeted Nectin-4 combination therapy strategies, its application in breast cancer is also foreseeable, especially considering that EV monotherapy in the EV-202 trial did not meet the expected effect in breast cancer. Furthermore, given the precedent set by ADC drugs such as T-DM1 in combination with chemotherapy in clinical practice, the potential of combining ADCs targeting Nectin-4 with traditional breast cancer treatments, including chemotherapy, endocrine therapy, and radiotherapy, merits further investigation in the future (Fig. 3).

Discussion and prospects

The specific high expression of Nectin-4 in a variety of tumors, including breast cancer, has generated considerable interest within the field of cancer research. At present, therapies targeting Nectin-4 in breast cancer, such as ADCs, typically utilize it as a target to accurately deliver payloads to kill tumors. However, it remains unclear whether the regulation of Nectin-4 itself by targeted drugs has an anti-tumor effect. For instance, some studies have indicated that the tumor-killing effect of EVs is solely attributable to the toxic payload. However, current studies have indicated that overexpressed Nectin-4 is involved in various aspects of breast cancer progression, including proliferation, metastasis, angiogenesis, EMT, and the immune microenvironment. The underlying pathways and mechanisms have been partially elucidated. The role of Nectin-4 in tumor progression may offer novel insights into the development of antitumor agents. Cyclin D1, for example, which promotes cell proliferation by directing the cell cycle switch from G1 to S phase, has excessive expression in a variety of tumor types. Nectin-4 silencing can increase Cyclin D1 expression, which leads to tumor cell apoptosis. Therefore, combining cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitors with anti-Nectin-4 agents could be a promising anti-tumor strategy. Moreover, Nectin-4 is involved in the regulation of angiogenesis, and its overexpression can cause VEGF downregulation and VEGFR2 upregulation. Preclinical studies may be considered to further elucidate the mechanisms underlying the association between anti-Nectin-4 and anti-angiogenic therapies. We believe that further exploration of the regulation of Nectin-4 function will help develop and optimize targeted therapies.

EV was the first Nectin-4-targeted drug to be approved for the treatment of malignant tumors, and thus ADC drugs have gradually become a major area of research focus for targeted Nectin-4 therapy. Overcoming drug resistance represents a persistent and inevitable challenge in the clinical application of ADC drugs. The prevailing opinion is that ADC resistance is primarily associated with drug stability, reduced target expression, intracellular transport, and alterations in signalling pathways. The down-regulation of the target is typically regarded as a key mechanism underlying ADC resistance. It has recently demonstrated that the majority of primary tumors (PRIM) of mUC with Nectin-4 amplification maintain high expression of membranous Nectin-4 protein during metastasis. In contrast, there is a significant reduction in membranous Nectin-4 protein expression in distant metastases (MET) of mUC without Nectin-4 amplification. Moreover, metastatic tumor samples with Nectin-4 downregulation were also associated with a shorter PFS. However, an alternative study demonstrated that the Nectin-4 membrane protein remained highly expressed in metastases from patients who experienced disease progression following EV treatment. This indicates that Nectin-4 downregulation may be associated with primary resistance in some patients, particularly in those without Nectin-4 amplification, but is not the sole mechanism of resistance. Consequently, the identification of Nectin-4 amplification status in PRIM and the dynamic assessment of Nectin-4 expression levels during disease progression may enhance the efficacy of targeted therapy. Rational combination therapy regimens may also improve drug resistance compared to individual drugs. In addition to the previously mentioned combinations with immunotherapy or TKIs, the exploration of mechanisms of resistance has provided new combination therapy strategies. For example, RNA sequencing demonstrated that the expression of ABCB1, which encodes the multidrug-resistant protein MDR-1/P-glycoprotein (P-gp), was upregulated in breast cancers that were resistant to EV-like ADC. Furthermore, the combination of ADC and a P-gp inhibitor was observed to induce rapid regression of drug-resistant tumors in vivo. Similar resistance mechanisms and corresponding response options need to be further explored.

As targeted Nectin-4 therapies continue to mature, discussions about future directions for drug development are critical. It is often the case that targeted therapies, such as ADCs, exhibit limited efficacy in tumors with low target expression. The development of dual-targeted drugs is expected to address this issue, as was first observed with anti-EGFR ADCs. In a recent multicenter Phase I clinical trial, BL-B01D1, an EGFR-HER3 bispecific ADC, was preliminarily validated for antitumor activity and safety in a variety of locally advanced or metastatic solid tumors. Of particular interest is the clinical activity demonstrated by BL-B01D1 in patients who progressed after prior TKI therapy. Furthermore, the previously mentioned bystander effect can also result in the apoptosis of antigen-negative cells adjacent to the target tumor cells. Recently, Guo et al. constructed a bystander effect scoring model for evaluating ADC efficacy and guiding ADC design by using a graph attention network (GAT) and identified a novel ADC, T-VEd9, which exhibited excellent antitumor activity in vivo and in vitro. Similar models and scoring systems can be used to screen ADCs with stronger effects, thus further improving the efficiency of drug development. Notably, there are a number of more specific problems with Nectn-4 drug development in breast cancer that need to be considered. For instance, considering the widespread use of anti-microtubule agents such as paclitaxel, vincristine, and eribulin in breast cancer, the efficacy of MMAE-loaded EVs in patients exhibiting resistance to these agents is uncertain and requires validation through further clinical trials. For resistance associated with the payload, several studies have demonstrated that replacing the appropriate payload significantly improves the efficacy of ADCs. This indicates that a greater variety of payloads will be required in the future to extend the use of ADCs targeting Nectin-4 in solid tumors including breast cancer, in order to overcome drug resistance.

Conclusion

Nectin-4 is a biomarker that is specifically expressed in tumors and serves as both a diagnostic and prognostic indicator for breast cancer. Nectin-4 is involved in several essential biological processes related to tumorigenesis and progression. Therefore, blocking Nectin-4 has the potential to induce tumor regression through multiple molecular mechanisms. Sufficient preclinical and clinical studies have proved that various strategies aimed at targeting Nectin-4, including ADCs, oncolytic viruses, photothermal therapy and combination therapies exert significant curative effects in breast cancer. It is with great interest that we anticipate the progression of these studies, and the exploration of additional therapeutic options targeting Nectin-4 in breast cancer warrants further investigation.
