# Killing cancer takes guts: lessons learned from the manipulation of gut microbiome and immunotherapy for the future of urothelial carcinoma.

## Abstract

Urothelial carcinoma (UC) remains a common cancer with significant morbidity and mortality worldwide. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have helped revolutionize the treatment of UC, and there is growing evidence suggesting the crucial role of the gut microbiome in immune system function influences immunotherapy outcomes in this disease. Herein, we review the preclinical basis for how manipulation of the gut microbiome may alter the efficacy of immunotherapy for patients with cancer, highlight interventions optimizing gut microbiome diversity currently in use, review recent and ongoing clinical trials supporting the role of the gut microbiome in improving immunotherapy outcomes, and discuss clinical implications to improve outcomes for UC patients with immunotherapy in the real world. There is growing evidence that suggests that specific gut microbiome compositions significantly modulate the host immune system and response to ICIs. Early studies have shown that certain microbial taxa enhance antitumor immunity by influencing T cell priming, dendritic cell activation, and cytokine production. Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), probiotic supplementation, and dietary modulation have emerged as promising methods to alter microbiomes to improve immunotherapy outcomes. Taxa from positive immunotherapy responders across a variety of cancers demonstrate beneficial effects when transplanted into both treatment-naive or prior nonresponders. Increasing evidence suggests that the gut microbiome plays a crucial role in cancer care, particularly when patients are treated with immunotherapy. Future studies are needed to better understand the underlying mechanisms. While some studies are currently underway to explore gut manipulation for patients with UC, more studies are needed to investigate the potential to convert nonresponders into responders through microbiome manipulation.

Introduction

Immunotherapy has dramatically changed the treatment landscape for cancer, including urothelial carcinoma, and is now the standard of care for multiple lines of treatment for nearly all stages of bladder cancer. The two most well-studied immunotherapy strategies in UC include monoclonal antibodies that inhibit programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4), which induce and enhance T cell-mediated immune responses against tumor-associated antigens. While some patients derive tremendous benefit, the majority of patients do not respond to immunotherapy. While several patient and tumor-specific factors contribute to responsiveness to ICI therapy, the relationship between the gut microbiome and anti-tumor has become an increased area of investigation.

Intestinal microbiota are a complex system of diverse microorganisms including bacteria, protozoa, and viruses that live within host systems in a symbiotic fashion, playing an important role in digestion, metabolism, and immune function. Current research has demonstrated the gut microbiome promotes antigen presentation, T cell activation and tumor infiltration, and direct cytotoxicity via enhancing molecular mimicry.

The purpose of this review is threefold: (1) to review the preclinical basis for how manipulation of gut microbiome may alter efficacy of immunotherapy for patients with cancer, (2) to highlight interventions augmenting the microbiome currently in use, and (3) to review recent and ongoing clinical trials exploring gut microbiome and immunotherapy outcomes with a focus on urothelial carcinoma (UC).

Antibiotic treated mice demonstrate impaired immunotherapy efficacy

Preclinical studies have investigated the immune responses of subjects lacking natural microbiomes, providing foundational insight into the immunomodulatory properties of gut microbiomes. In multiple murine studies, mice treated with antibiotics to sterilize their microbiomes, displayed significantly impaired responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). In a notable study by Routy et al., mice were given a combination of ampicillin, colistin, and streptomycin to sterilize the gut and then subsequently inoculated with MCA-205 sarcoma and RET melanoma. These mice had notably decreased anti-tumor response and worse survival compared to control when treated with anti-PD-1 therapy alone or in combination with anti-CTLA-4 therapy (median overall survival of 8.3 months in the antibiotic treated group compared to 15.3 months in the RET models (p-value = 0.001), and median overall survival of 11.5 months in the antibiotic treated group compared to 20.6 months in the MCA-205 models (p-value <0.001)). Routy et al. also showed that patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), renal cell carcinoma (RCC), and urothelial carcinoma who received antibiotics prior to PD-1 blockade had significantly worse responses. These studies support the microbiome’s involvement in systemic immune responses and promote the idea that an intact and fully optimized gut microbiome helps maximize the therapeutic efficacy of immunotherapy.

The underlying mechanisms for these impaired responses are thought to involve alterations and impaired development and function of both innate and adaptive immunities. The gut microbiome is involved in several aspects of physiologic immune regulation, including contributing to the priming and activation of T cells, the maturation of dendritic cells, and the production of cytokines essential for anti-tumor immunity, like interferon-y and interleukin-12. Disrupting these processes leads to less robust anti-tumor immune responses after administration of immunotherapy.

Evaluating specific gut microbiotas and influences on immunotherapy

Multiple groups have sought to identify specific bacteria that are associated with response to immunotherapy. Routy et al. sought to classify microbiome patterns to subsequently investigate the effects of direct bacterial replacement on immunotherapy efficacy. Using quantitative metagenomics by shotgun sequencing, they explored the composition of microbiomes between immunotherapy responders and nonresponders. They performed these examinations on a sample of 60 patients with NSCLC and 40 patients with RCC and compared bacterial gene richness at 6 months after the initiation of immunotherapy, noting statistically significant differences in genes and species corresponding to disease progression or improvement. Akkermansia muciniphila was highlighted for its particularly favorable association with positive clinical outcomes in both NSCLC and RCC patients and enrichment with A. muciniphila was associated with statistically significant differences in progression-free survival (PFS). They also highlighted a higher incidence of Enteroccocus hirae and Ruminococcus spp. in responders compared to non-responders. In their lung cancer samples, they also noted a higher frequency of Staphylococcus haemolyticus spp. and Corynebacterium aurimucosum spp. in nonresponders.

The effects of Enterococcus on immunotherapy efficacy have been investigated. Griffin et al investigated the mechanisms by which Enterococcus improve checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapy. They showed these bacteria express and secrete the peptidoglycan hydrolase SagA, which generates immune-active muropeptides; the expression of SagA has been shown to promote an immunotherapy response, and SagA-engineered probiotics or synthetic muropeptides also augmented anti-PD-L1 efficacy.

Gopalakrishnan et al. prospectively collected microbiome samples from 112 patients with metastatic melanoma prior to starting anti-PD-1 therapy and analyzed subsequent tumor biopsies, blood markers, and immune cell subsets between the groups at different time points. They then classified patients as either responders (those who had complete or partial response to anti-PD-L1 therapy at the 6-month mark) or nonresponders and performed microbiome, genomic, and immune profiling. They noted significant differences in the diversity and composition of the gut microbiomes between responders and nonresponders, with significantly higher alpha diversity (within-sample diversity) being significantly higher in responders compared to non-responders, which was correlated with PFS. Specifically, responders had a higher abundance of Faecalibacterium spp., Ruminococcaceae spp., and Clostridiales spp., while non-responders had an increased abundance of Bacteroidales, as well as Escherichia coli and Anaereotruncus spp. Gopalakrishnan et al. highlighted that Faecalibacterium spp. and Ruminococcaceae spp. were the dominant species of responder microbiomes and that the abundance of Faecalibacterium was specifically associated with prolonged PFS. Conversely, patients with a high abundance of Bacteroidales had a shortened PFS. Immune profiling revealed a higher density of CD8+ T cells in tumor samples from responders compared to non-responders, along with a statistically significant positive correlation between CD8+ T cell infiltration and the abundances of Faecalibacterium, Ruminococcaceae, and Clostridiales. These bacterial taxa were also associated with higher frequencies of CD4+​​ ​​and CD8+ T cells in the systemic circulation as well. They hypothesized that responder microbiomes exhibited functional differences in anabolic pathways resulting in increased amino acid synthesis, which thereby promoted host immunity. These findings suggest that specific species within the host gut microbiomes are crucial to their overall immune response.

Xie et al investigated Clostridium (specifically Clostridium butyricum) in colorectal cancer (CRC); while most patients with CRC do not respond to ICI, the group studied how C. butyricum could serve as a probiotic that potentiates anti-PD-1 efficacy in CRC. The group studied how C. butyricum activates cytotoxic CD8+ T cells and impairs tumor-associated macrophages when in conjunction with anti-PD-1 therapy. Like the above studies, they showed that downstream effects from the surface protein secD on C. butyricum leads to cross-linking on CRC cell receptors, overall leading to reduced secretion of IL-6. The Weis group also focused on the role of the microbiome in CRC but looked at Bacteroides uniformis. Using a series of microbiota transplantations from CRC and non-CRC patients into mice and examining outcomes, the group hailed B. uniformis as tumor protective and slows CRC growth and progression, with B. uniformis is reduced in patients with CRC. Mechanistically, the authors describe how the addition of B. uniformis. Mechanistically, the authors describe how B. uniformis is involved in a cascade between natural killer (NK) cells and Lag-3, leading to the overall induction of NK cells and reduced tumor growth secondary to an interaction with a B. uniformis capsular polysaccharide.

Other mouse models have corroborated the above findings and have highlighted that the efficacy of immunotherapy relative to that of specific microbiome taxa and that rescue or transplantation of these bacteria can restore immunotherapy efficacy. Sivan et al. conducted experiments in melanoma mouse models. They noted that differences in the gut microbiomes of mice obtained from different facilities corresponded with differences in melanoma growth after anti-PD-1 therapy: tumors grew more aggressively in mice obtained from Taconic farms (TAC mice) compared to mice obtained from the Jackson Laboratory (JAX mice). The JAX mice also showed higher tumor-specific T cell responses and intratumoral CD8+ T cell accumulation. Metagenomic analyses conducted by the group also identified a statistically significant association with the abundance of Bifidobacterium spp. and the accumulation of antigen-specific T cells within the tumor microenvironment in the JAX mice with improved tumor responses. The group also noted that cohousing the two groups prior to inoculation with melanoma and treatment with anti-PD-1 therapy eliminated statistically significant differences in tumor growth, suggesting that environmental influences may alter microbiome environments. The identification of Bifidobacterium in this context led the Sivan group to perform oral administration of Bifidobacterium in TAC mice inoculated with melanoma tumors. The subsequent results showed specimens demonstrated enhanced tumor-specific T cell responses, increased cytotoxic T-lymphocyte (CTL) infiltration into tumors, and increased IFN-γ production, particularly for dendritic cells. These experiments demonstrated how Bifidobacterium can enhance antitumor activity and improve the efficacy of immunotherapy, seemingly by augmenting dendritic cell function and improving the antitumor response within the TME.

Bredon et al investigated Faecalibacterium (specifically, Faecalibaterium prausnitzii) as well. MCA205 tumor-bearing mice who had administration of this bacterium showed improved antitumor response. Interestingly, they describe how this strain did not change fecal microbiota diversity or composition after administration, suggesting a direct effect on immune cells in the small intestine. The group is hoping to investigate this addition as an adjuvant to ICI in multiple cancers and is opening a trial investigating this role in metastatic gastric cancer (NCT06253611).

Investigating features of the gut microbiome in urothelial carcinoma (UC)

The association between the gut microbiome and specifically UC has been a subject of active investigation for some time. Aso et al. conducted a trial in 1992 investigating the effect of administering Lactobacillus supplementation for prophylaxis of the recurrence of superficial bladder cancer after transurethral resection of the bladder tumor in a cohort of 58 patients and demonstrated a 1.8 times longer recurrence-free interval in the treatment group compared to the control group. Aso et al. also published a subsequent double-blind trial of 138 patients, and their results highlighted improved outcomes in patients receiving supplementation compared to control, with Cox multivariate analysis demonstrating a p-value of 0.01 in favor of the supplementation groups. These studies led to similar investigations by other groups. In 2008, Naito et al. investigated Lactobacillus casei supplementation as a probiotic in patients receiving intravesical epirubicin after transurethral resection of their tumors. They studied a total of 100 patients receiving this combination compared to a control cohort of 102 patients, and reported that 3-y recurrence-free survival rate was significantly higher in the group receiving the L. casei supplementation compared to control (74.6% vs. 59.9%, p = 0.0234). Miyake et al showed that mouse models treated with gemcitabine and cisplatin and then oral probiotics, consisting of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium had significantly more tumor volume reduction and increased CD8+ T cell activation compared to control. Furthermore, Ohashi et al. demonstrated that diets with higher probiotic contents, via intake fermented mild products, were associated with a lower risk of UC, and a meta-analysis by Zhang et al also highlighted this association with fermented dairy products.

Pederzoli et al. sought to investigate microbiological taxa patterns in patients with muscle-invasive bladder cancer (MIBC) receiving pembrolizumab as a translational correlate in the PURE-01 clinical trial (NCT02373622). The PURE-01 study evaluated the use of single agent pembrolizumab as neoadjuvant therapy for patients with MIBC planned for cystectomy. Of 42 patients in the study who had pre-immunotherapy stool samples available for testing, 23 of these patients had pathological response and were considered good responders. Unique differences were noted in microbiological taxa between responders and non-responders. The genus Sutterella was enriched in responders while the species Ruminococcus bromii was enriched in nonresponders. Pederzoli et al. also used preclinical orthotopic animal models of bladder cancer to support these findings and reported a negative impact of R. bromii on anti-PD-1 antibody activity, as well as decreased survival in this preclinical model. At a physiologic level, enrichment of Sutterella in the gut promotes degradation of local immunoglobulin type A, theoretically favoring invasion of epithelial cells by local bacteria. It has also been hypothesized that there is an immunomodulatory role of Sutterella spp. given the ability of the species to adhere to the intestinal epithelium by binding to extracellular matrix proteins.

Wang et al. investigated bladder cancer immunotherapy in relation to a bacteria called Blautia coccoides. The group used 16S rDNA sequencing and multiomics to investigate the microbiota in patients undergoing treatment with ICIs and found how Blautia spp. produced metabolites that seemed to enhance antitumor immunity. They noticed high enrichment of Blautia spp. in good responders to immunotherapy. They described how oral supplementation of these bacteria led to elevated trigonelline levels, which, by suppressing beta-catenin expression, augmented antitumor activity in CD8+ T cells.

Modulation of gut microbiomes improves immunotherapy efficacy

While germ-free mice have demonstrated impaired tumor responses when given immunotherapy, the restoration of the gut microbiomes in mouse models has been shown to rescue immune responses. Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) refers to the process of transferring fecal matter and therefore the gut microbiota from a host to a recipient. Multiple groups have evaluated FMT and immunotherapy responses. Routy et al. performed FMT from human patients with NSCLC on 8 germ-free mice; 4 of these mice were given FMT from human patients who were considered responders to immunotherapy, while the other 4 mice were given FMT from human patients who did not respond to immunotherapy. All 8 mice were then inoculated with MCA-205 tumor cells and then treated with anti-PD1 immunotherapy. The mice that received the FMT from responders demonstrated significantly less tumor growth in comparison, as well as an improved survival, increased numbers of CXCR3+ CD4+ T cells, and increased PD-L1 expression in splenic T cells. The group then repeated the study in 7 mice inoculated with anti-PD-1-resistant renal cancer cells were then treated with anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapy. Interestingly, FMT of these mice from patients who responded well to immunotherapy for renal cancer restored anti-tumor activity in these mice.

The Routy group later performed oral supplementation of A. muciniphila and E. hirae into germ-free RET-melanoma mouse models that had exhibited resistance to immunotherapy and noted the restoration of antitumor activity after oral supplementation with these bacterial species. They further analyzed the immunologic mechanisms after oral supplementation and noted the recolonization of germ-free mice with A. muciniphila and E. hirae was followed by the emergence of CXCR3+ CCR9+ central memory T cells in tumor-draining lymph nodes and tumor cells. Similarly, A. muciniphila colonization was associated with increased IL-12 production and higher levels of gut-tropic CD4+ T cells expressing CCR9.17 The group noted E. hirae was associated with inducing TH17 cell levels and increasing the ratio of cytotoxic T cells to regulatory T cells in mouse models. The authors theorized that the introduction of these organisms induced dendritic cells to secrete IL-12, which plays a key role in the immunogenicity of PD-1 blockade through T lymphocyte recruitment into the tumor microenvironment, and that the antitumor effects of PD-1 blockade can be enhanced. Routy et al. were among the first to demonstrate that oral supplementation with A. muciniphila independently restored the efficacy of PD-1 blockade in mice that had been recolonized with nonresponding microbiota.

Gopalakrishnan et al. performed experiments similar to those of the Routy group with melanoma models. Gopalakrishnan et al. assigned germ-free mice to undergo FMT from human melanoma patients who were either responders (R-FMTs) or nonresponders (NR-FMTs) to anti-PD-1 therapy. Both groups of mice were then inoculated with melanoma cells to implant tumors in the mice. Both groups were subsequently given anti-PD-1 therapy, and had longitudinal correlative studies performed on their tumors, peripheral blood, and spleens to analyze immune responses and tumor growth. Interestingly, there was significantly reduced tumor size by day 14 in the R-FMT group compared to the NR-FMT group. There was also more CD8+ T cells in the tumors of the R-FMT group compared to the NR-FMT group, as well as increased CD45+ immune and CD8+ T cells in the guts of the R-FMT group compared to the NR-FMT group. Mass cytometry analysis of tumors from R-FMT mice also showed upregulation of PD-L1 in the respective tumor microenvironments compared to NR-FMT mice. These experiments highlight the importance of the gut microbiome in responders and suggest that FMT plays a role in restoring or improving immunotherapy efficacy. The proposed mechanisms responsible for these robust immune responses observed after FMT are thought to be related to the modulation of the tumor microenvironment, as there are increased level of CD8+ T cells in ICI-responders.

Vetizou et al. also demonstrated that the antitumor effects of CTLA-4 are dependent on Bacteroides species. Initially, their population of germ-free mice did not respond to CTLA-4 blockade, but this effect was overcome by gavage with Bacteriodes fragilis, immunization with B. fragilis polysaccharides or by adoptive transfer of B. fragilis-specific T cells. Mazmanian et al. investigated the mechanisms by which B. fragilis may be implicated in the host immune system. Studies have shown how bacterial polysaccharide (PSA) directs the cellular and physical maturation of the developing immune system, as it plays roles in lymphoid organogenesis, CD4+ T cell activation, and cytokine production. Researchers have also demonstrated how colonization with B. fragilis in germ-free mice led to the correction of prior T cell deficiencies. These findings support the argument that certain compositions of microbiomes influence the immune response and immunotherapy efficacy.

Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)

Several studies have utilized methods of manipulating the host gut microbiomes to observe subsequent responses to immunotherapy. This has included the use of antibiotics to sterilize germ mice and human subjects before receiving immunotherapy, as well as utilizing fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) to manipulate the gut microbiomes to observe immunotherapy efficacy. FMT refers to the process of transferring fecal matter—and thus, the gut microbiota—from a healthy donor to a recipient. While FMT is commonly used to treat severe Clostridioides difficile (C. difficile) infections, the application of FMT has been studied in several other contexts, including immunotherapy. FMT involves selecting healthy donors, excluding those with recent antibiotic usage, a history of gastrointestinal disorders or inflammatory bowel disease, autoimmune diseases, or malignancies to minimize the risk of adverse events and transmitting infections. Further, donors have blood and stool tests performed to ensure that the donor and samples are healthy and sterile from pathogens.

Collecting fecal matter for FMT is a several-step process. Preparation involves mixing with saline and filtration. After samples are processed and deemed adequate, delivery can occur. Delivery may include oral capsules and also direct delivery (i.e., esophagogastroduodenoscopy, colonoscopy, retention enemas, etc.). Often, bowel preparation is used to clear any contaminated fecal matter and ensure healthy grafts, and efforts are employed to avoid any antibiotic usage around the time of transplantation. Recipients also typically undergo bowel preparation prior to transplantation.

Stool banks are a centralized system for screening donors and processing, storing, and distributing high-quality fecal matter for FMT. While this process is burdensome, there are several recent technological advances that may expedite the process. One such process is utilizing frozen stool samples, which have been studied with good efficacy for FMT in C. difficile infection treatment. This is an area of future investigation in immuno-oncology and offers promise for scalability. Similarly, several studies utilize oral capsules as a delivery method for FMT, which greatly increases the accessibility of care by decreasing the procedure suite time. In the process of creating oral capsules for FMT, a single 30 g donation of fecal matter can yield multiple doses.

While FMT remains a promising avenue for medical advancement, its use is not yet standardized. The process, as briefly highlighted, can be burdensome, and generalizability remains one concern. The safety and efficacy of FMT are dependent on rigorous donor screening protocols, and concerns about the transmission of multidrug-resistant organisms (MDROs), which have also been highlighted by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), have been reported. FMT is not without risk. There have been documented adverse events associated with FMT, including invasive bacterial infections (even leading to death in one patient), which have brought attention to the potential transmission of MDROs secondary to FMT. These events even led to a statement put out by the FDA, highlighting these risks. The FDA recommends screening for extended-spectrum beta-lactamase (ESBL)-producing Enterobacteriaceae, vancomycin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), and carbapenem-resistant Enterobacteriaceae. Thorough screening to standardize safety measures, including both blood and stool testing, investigations of infectious agents, gastrointestinal comorbidities, general health parameters, and other systemic health conditions, has been proposed. However, FMT certainly can be conducted safely and effectively, but advancements in technology to make the process more scalable and streamlined will help increase its potential use in real-world settings.

FMT may help to overcome resistance to immunotherapy

There have been several therapeutic clinical studies have explored the influence of the gut microbiome in immuno-oncology. Baruch et al. investigated FMT via oral capsules harvested from 2 donors with a complete response to anti-PD-1 therapy for their melanoma, which was subsequently transplanted into a group of 10 patients with anti-PD-1 refractory metastatic melanoma. These authors reported that 3 of the 10 patients responded thereafter and noted an increased CD8+ cytotoxic T cell concentration and IFN-y gene expression in tumor cells. This study argues that FMT can promote a positive response in other immunotherapy-refractory melanoma patients and set the stage for further studies to follow in the future.

In a similar study, Davar et al. evaluated a population of patients with refractory melanoma and performed responder-derived FMT via colonoscopy to explore whether resistance to anti-PD-1 therapy can be overcome by altering the gut microbiota. The combination was well tolerated and provided clinical benefit in 6 of 15 patients, with similar increase in CD8+ cytotoxic cells and interleukin-8-expressing myeloid cells in the tumor microenvironment. Responders had distinct proteomic and metabolomic signatures that were confirmed by transkingdom network analyses to be regulated by gut microbiome alterations.

Routy et al. studied a sample of 20 patients with advanced melanoma in two treatment groups: 10 patients who were refractory to immunotherapy and 10 of who were treatment naive. They performed FMT via oral capsules harvested from healthy donors who responded to immunotherapy. They noted that 6 patients in a cohort of 10 patients refractory to PD-1 blockade responded to FMT from previous responders; they specifically noted 3 patients achieved partial response, and another 3 achieved stable disease at 12 weeks. In the treatment-naive group, they reported that 9 out of 10 patients achieved complete or partial responses. They also reported that good responders subsequently had increased circulating and intratumoral CD8+ T cells compared to non-responders both at baseline and after FMT.

Alternative methods to manipulate the gut microbiome

Probiotics are live microorganisms that are administered to either replace, alter, or enhance the innate gut flora and are another useful tool to manipulate the gut microbiome. In a small single-center study, Dizman et al. evaluated 30 patients with treatment-naïve advanced renal cell carcinoma (clear cell or sacromatoid histology) with intermediate- or poor-risk disease who were randomized 2:1 to receive ipilimumab and nivolumab with or without CBM588, a bifidogenic live bacterial product. PFS was significantly longer in patients receiving CBM588 (12 months vs. 2.5 months, HR 0.15, p = 0.001). This study paved the way for multiple trials involving probiotics to augment immunotherapy.

Additionally, dietary interventions can impact microbiome environments. Diets rich in fiber and complex carbohydrates tend to increase microbial diversity and promote the growth of potentially beneficial bacteria. Interestingly, in mouse models, high-salt diets were shown to negatively affect the response to immunotherapy, suggesting that salt intake plays a role in immune responses. The impact of specific diets and their subsequent effects on immunotherapy are being investigated and remain interesting concepts to explore. Dietary interventions, therefore, could independently be used as a method to foster a beneficial gut microbiome profile that is more conducive to generating a positive response to immunotherapy in patients with cancer. Finally, antibiotic therapies may be used to alter the microbiome, as discussed above. Antibiotic stewardship is an important concept in patients who may require immunotherapy and is an area of active investigation (Figure 1).

A recently published study by Scirgolea et al. in Nature Medicine described the role of sodium chloride (NaCl) in cancer immunology. The authors described the critical role of CD8-T cells in regulating tumor differentiation and describe their laboratory work that shows how NaCl supplementation can counteract T cell dysfunction and promote cancer regression. NaCl supplementation mechanistically induced IFN-γ production and effector cell differentiation; mice with high-salt diets were able to reduce the growth of experimental tumors by inhibiting terminal differentiation and having enhanced potency of CD8+ T cells. The authors describe how NaCl enhances glutamine consumption, which is normally critical for transcriptional reprogramming. The authors wrote how NaCl metabolism is a regulator of CD8+ T cell function and has potential implications in cancer immunotherapy, as T cells undergoing antigen recognition and tumors NaCl showed similar results to T cells undergoing antigen recognition and predicting favorable responses to checkpoint blockade therapy.

Gut microbiome and immune-related colitis

ICIs have revolutionized cancer therapy, but they are also associated with immune-related adverse events (irAEs). Immune-related colitis is common and severe, and current research has shown that baseline gut microbiome characteristics may be linked to the risk of colitis. Studies have investigated this relationship in metastatic melanoma patients, highlighting the importance of Bacteroidetes in the microbiome. In a prospective study led by Chaput et al., 26 patients with metastatic melanoma receiving the single agent ipilimumab had their microbiome compositions analyzed and compared statistically with the association and development of ICI-related colitis. Baseline gut microbiomes enriched with Faecalibacterium and other Firmicutes were associated with improved clinical benefit but also a high incidence of immune-related colitis. In a similar study, Dubin et al. evaluated the gut microbiome and colitis in 35 patients with metastatic melanoma patients receiving immunotherapy. They also noted that increased concentrations of Bacteroidetes were correlated with resistance to developing IO-related colitis. Additionally, certain genetic pathways involved in polyamine transport and B vitamin biosynthesis in the gut microbiome were associated with an increased risk of colitis. However, Andrews et al., in their cohort of 77 patients with metastatic melanoma receiving immunotherapy, noted a significantly higher abundance of Bacteroides in patients who developed toxicity. As we start to understand the microbiome more, we can ideally also better understand potential toxicity risks. Mapping the gut microbiomes can allow clinicians to better educate patients and create personalized treatment plans for patients.

Current relevant clinical trials and investigations

With mounting evidence connecting the gut microbiomes and immunotherapy responses, multiple clinical trials have been conducted or are ongoing investigating methods to manipulate the gut microbiome as a way to improve outcomes for patients with cancer treated with immunotherapy (Table 1).

Ongoing trials investigating FMT and immunotherapy in non-UC oncology

There are several ongoing trials investigating the role of FMT in combination with ICI for cancer therapy. In the genitourinary space, the MITRIC (NCT05286294) and TACITO (NCT04758507) trials are investigating FMT in combination with ICI for patients with advanced cancer. The MIRTIC (NCT05286294) trial plans to enroll 20 patients with progressive solid tumor malignancy on ICI therapy who will receive FMT from ICI-responders with a greater than 12-month response. Patients will receive up to 5 cycles of FMT and continue ICI treatment, subsequently analyzing safety, objective tumor response, and other outcomes. While this trial is ongoing, preliminary results have been reported for 9 patients: 6 with melanoma, 1 with RCC, 1 with pancreatic cancer, and 1 with head and neck cutaneous cancer. All the patients have received at least one FMT, with 8 of 9 receiving 2, and 5 patients currently have recorded stable disease as the best response. The FMT-related adverse events were all grade 1. Although these results are preliminary and the study is ongoing, current results show that FMT combined with continued ICI therapy can be feasible and safe.

The TACITO (NCT04758507) trial is investigating FMT for patients with metastatic renal cell carcinoma (mRCC) receiving immunotherapy. Patients with mRCC are randomized to axitinib plus pembrolizumab with FMT from a patient with complete and lasting response to immunotherapy or placebo. Patients received FMT administered via colonoscopy and subsequently FMT via oral capsules at days 90 and 180. The endpoints included PFS, overall survival (OS), and the objective response rate (ORR). Preliminary results presented at the European Society of Medical Oncology Congress 2024 demonstrated that patients treated with FMT had a 1-y PFS rate of 66.7% compared to 35.0% in the control group (n = 50 total patients). The study also noted an ORR of 54% in the FMT group compared to 28% in the placebo group. These preliminary results also highlighted the role of FMT increasing the activity and therapeutic response of immunotherapy in mRCC patients.

Similar studies evaluating the combination of FMT and immunotherapy are being conducted for prostate cancer (NCT04116775, an ongoing phase II trial) and lung cancer (NCT04924374, NCT04951583, and NCT05502913). Interestingly, NCT04924374 has no bowel preparation involved, whereas NCT04951583 employs bowel preparation via polyethylene glycol administration, and NCT05502913 is exploring administration of FMT via colonoscopy and oral capsules.

Additionally, multiple trials are ongoing in the GI cancer space exploring FMT and immunotherapy including NCT04130763, NCT04729322, and NCT04975217. NCT04729322 is investigating the efficacy of FMT for patients with colorectal cancer receiving anti-PD-1, using an antibiotic based bowel preparation with metronidazole, vancomycin, and neomycin. NCT04975217 is investigating FMT administered via oral capsules during colonoscopy to assess incidence of immune-related adverse events in patients with resectable pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. There are several other trials in the early stages investigating the role of FMT in immuno-oncology, and several more that are in the planning stages.

The PERFORM (NCT04163289) trial is assessing if FMT from healthy donors can impact and/or prevent immune related adverse effects. Preliminary results have been reported in a group of 10 patients with mRCC who received FMT from healthy donors, showing an associated safety profile and promising clinical efficacy data.

Relevant ongoing trials for UC

Multiple studies (Table 2) are ongoing that are exploring various microbiome interventions for patients with UC undergoing ICI as well as exploring microbiome signatures as possible predictive tools for ICI responders that will be fruitful and informative for UC patients.

NCT05220124 is a phase IV study evaluating both survival and response rates in patients with UC after receiving a probiotic capsule enriched with Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, and Enterococcus in addition to immunotherapy. UROBIOTA (NCT06675656) plans to enroll 400 patients with UC to establish the microbiota composition as a predictive tool for the response to the intravesical Bacilllus Calmette–Guerin (BCG) and two different chemotherapy schemes. Similarly, NCT04566029 is a prospective case-control study based in France seeking to identify microbiome signatures predictive of ICI response by comparing microbiomes of good responders to ICIs with non-responders.

NCT04601402 is a phase 1/1b, first-in-human, open-label, dose escalation and dose expansion study seeking to evaluate the safety, tolerability, biological and clinical activities of a live biotherapeutic product (GEN-001, lyophilized formulation of a strain of Lactoccocus lactis) and an avelumab in patients with locally advanced or metastatic solid tumors—including UC—who have progressed on at least 2 lines of approved therapy including anti-PD-1 therapy. Similarly, NCT06904573 (IMPROVE) is a multicenter, randomized controlled phase II study evaluating the efficacy and safety of immunotherapy combined with probiotics in patients with advanced UC.

The potential role of the gut microbiome in immunotherapy for urothelial carcinoma: future directions

While the role of the gut microbiome in immunotherapy has been explored in many cancers, its specific role in modulating the efficacy of immunotherapy for urothelial carcinoma warrants further investigation. Dedicated studies will be required to elucidate this relationship. Given the established influence that the gut microbiome has on the immunotherapy response in other malignancies that also utilize PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors, it is likely that there are similar influences applicable in urothelial carcinoma. It is reasonable to consider that the composition and characteristics of the host gut microbiomes also impact immunotherapy outcomes in UC. If there are also differences in immunotherapy efficacy for patients with unique gut microbiome characteristics receiving immunotherapy to treat UC, this is an area that deserves exploration, as findings could significantly impact clinical outcomes. Future research should focus on characterizing the gut microbiome signatures of all urothelial carcinoma patients before, during, and after treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors as a way to predict responses. Advanced sequencing techniques such as metagenomics can be used to identify and quantify the bacterial species present in patient stool samples given the clear relationship between certain bacterial species with immunotherapy response rates. It is plausible that this could be a feature of urothelial carcinoma, and this association we hope to elucidate. Moreover, as we learn more about the signatures of responders compared to non-responders, future research should focus on manipulating the gut microbiome and therefore the tumor microenvironment to help improve response rates to ICI. Whether, through targeted dietary interventions, the administration of prebiotics or probiotics to patients, or FMT, finding ways to make these interventions affordable and scalable in the clinic will be central to potentially dramatically improving outcomes for patients with UC (Table 3).

Conclusion

Further understanding the intricate relationship between the gut microbiome and immunotherapy, specifically in urothelial carcinoma, holds immense potential for improving patient outcomes and survival. UC is a challenging disease, but this is an area that could help clinicians in treating these patients. Understanding patients' unique microbiota signatures could help aid clinicians in their choice and treatment plans, while developing microbiome-based interventions could offer novel treatment strategies to increase the effectiveness of immune checkpoint blockade for these patients. This emerging area of research warrants attention and dedicated research efforts given the potential for significant improvements in patient outcomes.
