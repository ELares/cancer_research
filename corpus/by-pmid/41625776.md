# Recent advances in lipid nanoparticles for cancer vaccine delivery: Challenges and future perspectives.

## Abstract

Cancer remains a major global health burden, with 19.3 million new cases and 10.3 million deaths reported in 2020. Conventional modalities such as surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy often fail to prevent metastasis or recurrence. Cancer vaccination, which mobilizes durable, tumor-specific immunity, has gained traction, and LNPs have become pivotal to this approach. Originally optimized for siRNA, LNPs' core-shell architecture protects nucleic acids, enhances cellular uptake, and enables efficient cytosolic delivery. Their clinical validation in infectious-disease messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines has catalyzed rapid progress toward oncology applications. Preclinical and early-phase trials have indicated that mRNA-LNPs encoding tumor-associated antigens or patient-specific neoantigens can expand cytotoxic T cells and elicit preliminary antitumor activity. Key barriers remain. Manufacturing cost and batch consistency challenge scale-up. Physicochemical instability necessitates cold-chain logistics and complicates global deployment. Reactogenicity and anti-PEG antibodies increase safety and dosing concerns. Within tumors, immunosuppressive microenvironments, heterogeneous antigen expression, and suboptimal lymphoid targeting limit the efficacy of vaccines. This review describes the advances in LNP design (ionizable and biodegradable lipids, PEG alternatives, ligand-mediated targeting), formulation strategies (thermostable and lyophilized systems), and delivery routes (intranodal and intratumoral) to overcome these bottlenecks. It also highlights synergistic combinations with checkpoint blockade, radiotherapy, and innate agonists, and examines emerging pipelines leveraging AI-guided neoantigen discovery and quality control. By integrating material engineering, immunology, and translational evidence, we identified failure points and proposed a roadmap for next-generation LNP-based cancer vaccines. The goal is to accelerate progress from bench to clinic, while improving manufacturability, access, and durable patient benefits.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

Cancer is one of the leading causes of death worldwide. A staggering 19.3 million cases were reported in 2020 alone, and 10 million deaths have been documented globally. Traditional therapies comprising chemotherapy, radiation therapy and surgical interventions are often unable to prevent metastatic relapse of the disease. On the other hand, immunotherapy has had a significant impact in oncology as it harnesses the immune system of the body against cancer. Somatic mutations produce neoantigens and tumor specific antigens that are effectively targeted by cancer vaccines, with the potential to produce adaptive immunity. However, cancer vaccines have issues such as antigen delivery, effective immune activation, and stability, which compromise their clinical translation.

Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs) represent a promising platform to address these limitations. Originally developed for siRNA delivery, LNPs have gained international recognition as the primary delivery method for messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines (such as Pfizer-BioNTech's BNT162b2 and Moderna's mRNA-1273) during the COVID-19 pandemic. Their distinct structure, a core-shell assembly made up of ionizable lipids, cholesterol, phospholipids, and polyethylene glycol (PEG)-lipids, makes them effective in delivering mRNA. These components work together to effectively display antigens by preventing the breakdown of nucleic acids, facilitating cellular absorption, and permitting endosomal escape. Unlike viral vectors, which have immunological problems, or polymeric nanoparticles, which exhibit variable toxicity, LNPs have unparalleled biocompatibility and versatility.

Tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), neoantigens, and immunostimulatory adjuvants are delivered using LNPs in cancer vaccines. For instance, by producing potent CD8+ T cell responses, mRNA-LNPs encoding tailored neoantigens discovered using next-generation sequencing have shown remarkable efficacy in preclinical settings. Structural enhancements, such as surface functionalization with targeting ligands enhance dendritic cell (DC) selectivity, while co-delivery of adjuvants such as TLR agonists increases innate immune activation. Early-phase clinical trials that demonstrated a 44% reduction in the recurrence risk for melanoma patients when combined with pembrolizumab have demonstrated the translational potential of Moderna's mRNA-4157.

Despite these advancements, several major challenges remain. The high cost and complexity of scalable manufacturing under Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) standards limit accessibility. Stability problems hinder global distribution, necessitating cold-chain logistics. Immunogenicity risks, such as anti-PEG antibodies, which can occur in up to 40% of patients, could compromise efficacy and safety. Moreover, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), characterized by regulatory T cells (Tregs) and PD-L1 activation, often reduces vaccine-induced immunity. This necessitates the use of metabolic modulators or checkpoint inhibitors simultaneusly.

This review critically examines unresolved issues, assesses preclinical and clinical results, and summarizes the recent developments in LNP-based cancer vaccines. We also look at potential future paths, such as combinatorial methods using CAR-T cells or radiation therapy, biodegradable lipid formulations, and AI-guided neoantigen creation. This article aims to educate researchers, physicians, and policymakers on methods to expedite the development of next-generation cancer vaccines by bridging the gap between bench-scale innovation and clinical applications.

Overview of LNPs

Designed to encapsulate and transport therapeutic cargo like mRNA, DNA, proteins, or small-molecule medications, LNPs are self-assembling, spherical vesicles made of synthetic or natural lipids. They are now vital components of contemporary drug delivery systems, especially in cancer vaccination, owing to their modular architecture, biocompatibility, and capacity to shield payloads from enzymatic destruction. We go into detail about their makeup, mode of action, and benefits over rival distribution platforms.

Composition and structure

LNPs are crucial drug delivery systems, especially in the area of RNA-based vaccines. For their typical composition, they require at least four ingredients: solvents, stabilizers, lipids, and an active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) (Fig. 1). The complementary actions of these components ensure that the encapsulated medicinal substances are stable, functional, and bioavailable to the best of their capacity.

Ionizable Lipids:

The core lipid components of LNPs serve multiple functions, including forming the nanoparticle matrix, stabilizing the formulation, and promoting cellular uptake and endosomal escape. Ionizable lipids are a feature of modern LNPs, particularly in mRNA delivery systems, as they enhance the encapsulation efficiency and permit endosomal escape. Among other characteristics, lipid selection influences the size, charge, and capacity for interact with biological membranes. A recent study showed that novel ketal-ester ionizable lipids improved spleen targeting and reduced hepatotoxicity, making them promising mRNA vaccine delivery vehicles.

Owing to their positive charge at acidic pH, certain lipids (such ALC-0315 and DLin-MC3-DMA) can interact electrostatically with negatively charged nucleic acids (such as mRNA) during synthesis. They become neutral at physiological pH, reducing their toxicity. By buffering the acidic endosomal pH and facilitating endosomal escape through the "proton sponge effect," ionizable lipids can cause osmotic swelling and membrane rupture (Table 1).

PEG-Lipids:

Stabilizers such as PEGylated lipids or specific surfactants, are essential for maintaining the stability and integrity of LNPs throughout formulation and storage. Stabilizers assist the lipid matrix to remain stable under various of environmental condition and prevent aggregation. LNPs can be made more stable by adding poloxamers, which enhances their capacity to carry drugs. Developments, such as ligand-functionalized LNPs, which improve targeting capabilities and therapeutic efficiency, can enable the distribution of immune adjuvants with the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API).

Lipids conjugated with polyethylene glycol (PEG), such as DMG-PEG 2000, surround LNPs with a hydrophilic corona. These lipids can enhance the stability, tolerability, delivery effectiveness, and biodistribution of nanoparticles. Nevertheless, in certain people, PEGylation may result in anti-PEG antibodies, which can accelerates blood clearance (ABC phenomenon).

Phospholipids

The choice of solvent during nanoparticle manufacturing is important because it influences the final properties of LNPs. With the use of common solvents such as ethanol or water, lipids can self-assemble into nanoparticles. Drug release profiles may be affected by the development of distinct internal structures the LNPs. Its formation is affected by the process of combining the lipid mixtures with solvents. LNPs in with a range of forms can be produced using an evaporation process, which is helpful for enhancing drug administration. Structural lipids such as DSPC (1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine) improve cellular absorption.

Cholesterol

Cholesterol promotes LNP fusion with endosomal membranes, increases membrane stiffness, and prevent early cargo leakage. The LNP size (usually between 50 and 150 nm), charge, and biodistribution are all determined based on the molar ratio of these constituents. For example, the effectiveness of vaccines depends on the preferential accumulation of smaller LNPs (less than 100 nm) in the lymph nodes. As technology develops, ongoing research attempts to improve these four essential LNP components to increase treatment efficacy and decrease side effects. If innovative lipid design and state-of-the-art manufacturing techniques are combined, LNPs might move from being utilized in research to being employed on a large scale in cancer immunotherapy.

Mechanism of action

Cellular uptake, endosomal escape, antigen presentation, and immune activation are steps in the multi-stage procedure used to administer therapeutic medications, particularly RNA-based vaccines. Each stage is necessary for cargo delivery to elicit a robust immune response (Fig. 2).

In the realm of mRNA vaccines, the complexity of the process is influenced not only by the efficiency of the delivery system but also by the intrinsic interactions between mRNA and the innate immune system, as well as the immunological characteristics of the LNP matrix employed. Recent studies have corroborated that the process is affected not only by the delivery system's efficiency but also by the intrinsic interactions between mRNA and the LNP matrix with the innate immune system. It has been confirmed that mRNA itself is immunostimulatory, primarily through its interactions with Toll-like receptors (TLRs) such as TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8. The interaction between exogenous mRNA and TLRs elicits a robust immune response, characterized by increased cytokine production and activation of innate immune pathways, particularly the NF-κB and IRF (Interferon Regulatory Factors) signaling pathways. Furthermore, LNP lipid components, especially ionizable lipids, contribute to immunogenicity by activating TLR pathways independently of mRNA content, including TLR4 activation and dendritic cell maturation, even in LNPs devoid of nucleic acids.

Nevertheless, these immunostimulatory properties are also associated with increased reactogenicity, particularly in formulations with high ionizable lipid content, necessitating a balance between adjuvanticity and safety. Optimization of lipid composition, including the reduction of cationic lipids, as well as additional immunomodulatory strategies, have been reported to suppress inflammatory responses without compromising immunogenicity. These findings underscore that the biological effects of mRNA vaccines are largely determined by the mechanisms of interaction between LNP lipid components and the immune system, which form the basis for discussing immune activation mechanisms in this section.

Cellular uptake

APCs, including dendritic cells (DCs), initiate their actions by absorbing LNPs. Typical routes for this absorption include membrane fusion and endocytosis. APCs are essential for initiating an immune response because they process and deliver antigens to T cells. Several strategies have been used to increase the target specificity and uptake efficiency, including the introduction of customized ligands. The use of anti-CD40 antibodies as targeted ligands is an effective therapeutic strategy. These antibodies selectively enhance DCs' absorption of LNPs by DCs. The quantity of therapeutic RNA can be increased that reaches particular cells and ensure that the immune system is sufficiently stimulated by concentrating on them. LNPs are taken up by dendritic cells (DCs) and other antigen-presenting cells (APCs) via endocytosis or membrane fusion. One type of targeted ligand that can enhance APC-specific uptake is anti-CD40 antibody.

Endosomal escape

After cellular uptake (often via endocytosis), LNPs are trafficked into early endosomes and subsequently mature toward late endosomes/lysosomes, where progressive acidification and enzymatic activity can trap or degrade cargo if escape does not occur. Endosomal escape is therefore widely considered a rate-limiting step for functional mRNA delivery, because translation requires cytosolic access. A central design principle enabling escape is the pH-dependent ionization of ionizable lipids: as endosomal pH decreases, these lipids become protonated and acquire positive charge. Protonation strengthens electrostatic interactions with anionic endosomal membrane lipids and promotes formation of ion pairs, which can disrupt bilayer packing and lower the energetic barrier for membrane remodeling.

Several complementary mechanistic models have been proposed. In one widely cited framework, protonated ionizable lipids interact with anionic phospholipids to induce membrane destabilization via local thinning, increased curvature stress, and transient defects that permit release of nucleic acids into the cytosol. Another model emphasizes membrane fusion and non-bilayer phase behavior: the combination of protonated ionizable lipids with specific helper lipids can favor a transition toward inverted hexagonal (H_II) or other non-lamellar structures, which are associated with higher fusogenicity and bilayer disruption. Helper lipids such as DOPE-like phospholipids are often discussed in this context because of their intrinsic cone shape and propensity to adopt non-bilayer phases under acidic conditions, thereby supporting endosomal escape rather than stable bilayer formation. Cholesterol further modulates these processes by tuning membrane order and mechanical properties, influencing fusion propensity, LNP stability during trafficking, and interactions with endosomal membranes.

Despite these design principles, substantial uncertainties remain. Only a small fraction of internalized LNP cargo is typically thought to reach the cytosol, and the precise escape route likely depends on endosomal maturation state, cell type, lipid composition, and particle properties (size, PEG-lipid content, surface charge at relevant pH). Whether escape predominantly occurs via transient nanopores, “leaky” fusion intermediates, vesicle rupture, or back-fusion events is still debated, and direct, quantitative measurements in relevant immune cell populations remain limited. Accordingly, mechanistic understanding is improving but not complete, reinforcing the need to correlate formulation parameters (e.g., ionizable lipid pKa, helper lipid identity, cholesterol content) with functional readouts (translation potency, innate sensing, and toxicity) when optimizing LNP-based cancer vaccines.

Antigen presentation

Once messenger RNA (mRNA) is released into the cytoplasm, ribosomes are transformed into antigens. These antigens are then processed and presented by Major Histocompability Complex (MHC) molecules, specifically MHC I and MHC II. The presentation of MHC I molecules is essential for CD8+ T cell activation, which is necessary for the destruction of sick or cancerous cells. On the other hand, MHC II presentation stimulates CD4+ T cells, which help coordinate the immunological response. The effectiveness of LNPs in triggering the adaptive immune system is demonstrated by their use of two different methods to deliver antigens. The delivered mRNA is transformed into antigens by ribosomes, which then process and present antigens on MHC I and II molecules, activating CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, respectively.

Immune activation

Adding immunostimulatory adjuvants, such as Toll-like receptor (TLR) agonists, to LNPs as they are being made is one method to boost the immune response. These adjuvants improve DC maturation and encourage the generation of cytokines. The synergistic effects of RNA and adjuvants found in LNPs can significantly increase the immune response and provide enhanced and long-lasting immunity against infectious diseases and cancers. The intricate interaction between LNP constituents and the host immune system demonstrates how innovative engineering might increase the effectiveness of therapeutic vaccinations. LNPs can co-deliver adjuvants (such TLR agonists) to activate pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) and boost DC maturation and cytokine production.

Comparison with other delivery systems

Effective delivery is a primary determinant of efficacy for mRNA-based cancer vaccines, because the platform must protect labile nucleic acids, enable cytosolic delivery for translation, support repeat dosing, and maintain an acceptable safety profile. Several delivery strategies have been explored, each with distinct trade-offs (Table 2).

Viral vectors offer high transduction efficiency and, in some settings, strong immunogenicity that can act as an intrinsic adjuvant. However, their use in therapeutic vaccination is constrained by pre-existing anti-vector immunity, rapid development of neutralizing antibodies that limit repeat dosing, and platform-specific safety concerns (including insertional mutagenesis for integrating vectors and inflammatory/toxicities for non-integrating vectors). These liabilities are particularly relevant for cancer vaccination, which often requires multiple administrations to overcome tumor-mediated immunosuppression.

Polymeric nanoparticles provide tunable degradation and controlled release through polymer chemistry, which can be advantageous for sustained exposure. In practice, however, nucleic-acid encapsulation and endosomal escape are frequently less efficient than with LNPs, and polymer-dependent toxicity profiles can be variable, complicating translation and comparability across studies.

Liposomes have a long clinical track record and strong biocompatibility, exemplified by products such as liposomal doxorubicin (Doxil). Nevertheless, conventional liposomes often exhibit limited stability for large nucleic acids, less efficient cytosolic delivery, and relatively rapid clearance, which can restrict effective exposure and payload delivery for mRNA therapeutics 

In contrast, lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) have emerged as the leading non-viral platform for mRNA delivery because they combine high nucleic-acid encapsulation efficiency with ionizable-lipid–enabled endosomal escape, allowing robust intracellular translation after systemic or local administration. This balance of delivery efficiency and tolerability makes LNPs particularly suitable for personalized cancer vaccines that may require high mRNA dose density and repeat dosing schedules. Key limitations remain, including cold-chain dependence for many formulations and the potential for anti-PEG immune responses when PEG-lipids are used, which can affect pharmacokinetics upon repeat administration. Accordingly, current development trends focus on improving thermostability and exploring PEG alternatives or immunologically “stealth” surface chemistries while preserving delivery performance (Table 2).

Recent advances in LNP design for cancer vaccines

The rapid advancement of LNP technology has created new opportunities to enhance the efficacy, safety, and personalization of cancer vaccination. Recent developments in combinatorial delivery techniques, cargo diversity, and structural engineering have resolved long-standing problems with immune, antigen presentation, and tumor targeting.

Cargo innovations

Personalized neoantigen delivery

Using AI-driven prediction algorithms and tumor exome sequencing, LNPs release mRNA or DNA encoding patient-specific neoantigens. For example, BioNTech's FixVac Platform combines mRNA-LNPs with common tumor-associated antigens (such as NY-ESO-1 and MAGE-A3) to generate potent CD8+ T cell responses in prostate and melanoma cancer. Pembrolizumab with Moderna's mRNA-4157, a customized neoantigen vaccination, decreased the recurrence risk by 44% in patients with resected melanoma (Phase II trial NCT03897881) (Fig. 3).

Self-amplifying RNA (saRNA) and circular RNA

By enhancing immunogenicity, saRNA-LNPs enable longer antigen expression at lower dosages. Because circular RNA-LNPs are resistant to exonuclease degradation, they show ten-folden higher protein expression than linear mRNA in preclinical tests. Self-amplifying RNA (saRNA) has emerged as a revolutionary development in the field of RNA-based therapies, particularly in the delivery of cancer vaccines. Since saRNA- LNPs boost immunogenicity and enable extended antigen expression at lower dosages, effective vaccination uses less RNA. This characteristic can lower production costs as well as the potential side effects of high-dose administration. LNP encapsulation of saRNA helps to deliver it to target cells and inhibits its degradation, which is crucial for robust immune responses, as depicted in Fig. 4.

Circular RNAs (circRNAs) are another promising substitute for linear RNA (circRNA). Owing their unique structure, circular RNA-LNPs are resistant to exonuclease degradation, which is a significant stability advantage. Due to stability, preclinical research has demonstrated that circRNAs exhibits significantly higher protein expression than linear mRNA. Strong immune responses must be induced for successful cancer immunotherapy, and these increased expression levels aid in achieving this goal. Because the closed-loop structure of circRNAs allows for continuous translation, antigens are shown for longer periods of time and T-cell responses are more successfully triggered. An innovative approach to enhance the persistence of therapeutic effects is to incorporate circRNAs into LNP formulations. This could prolong anti-tumor immunity and boost the efficacy of cancer vaccines.

Given the promising results regarding saRNA and circRNA, the addition of these molecules to novel lipid nanoparticle formulations presents exciting opportunities for the development of cancer vaccination strategies. Since these RNA types can achieve higher levels of antigen expression while maintaining safety profiles, tudies are required to include them in future cancer immunotherapies. Although saRNA and circRNA research is still in progress, it may pave way for safer and more efficient immunization schedules that could significantly improve patient outcomes during cancer treatment.

Structural modifications

Targeted delivery

Dendritic cells (DCs) are the most potent subset of specialized APCs that control and trigger immunological responses. DCs can be broadly classified according to their ontogeny into plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs), monocyte-derived DCs (MoDCs), and conventional DCs (cDCs), which include two subsets (cDC1, cDC2). Every DC is different. Recent developments in high-throughput single-cell research have revealed new subsets and states of DCs, including DC3, allowing for a more precise understanding of DC activity and development. Because DCs initiate and modulate antigen-specific immunity and tolerance, their antigen-presenting capabilities and heterogeneity hold great potential for boosting the anticancer immune response elicited by therapeutic vaccinations.

DCs are triggered when they encounter altered cells, such as cancer cells, or invading pathogens. Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), including Toll-like receptors (TLRs), and external signals such as pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) initiate and guide DC functions. When DCs exhibit acquired exogenous antigens on MHC I molecules, a process known as cross-presentation takes place. This presentation takes place following DC migration to tumor-draining lymph nodes (TDLNs. Through T cell receptors (TCRs), active and mature antigen-loaded dendritic cells (DCs) transform naïve T cells into antigen-specific TEFF cells, such as CD4+ Th cells and CD8+ CTLs, depending on the cytokine milieu provided by DCs. Cross-presentation is necessary for CTL activation and cross-priming for tumor defense. However, the task of enhancing DCs' cross-presentation abilities to successfully cross-prime CTLs is often unmet in cancer immunotherapy (Fig. 6).

Size and surface engineering

T cell priming is increased by LNPs smaller than 100 nm because they preferentially drain to the lymph nodes. LNPs effectively enters the lymphatic system and interact with cells to deliver antigens. This interaction efficiently increases immune responses against target antigens by improving naive T cell activation and proliferation. Size-dependent targeting of lymph nodes is a significant development in vaccine and immunotherapy technologies. Zwitterionic lipid coatings decrease protein corona synthesis, which minimizes hepatic sequestration. Nonspecific protein adsorption is repelled by these lipid coatings owing to their balanced positive and negative charges. By limiting ability of the liver cells to recognize and absorb LNPs, this decrease in protein corona formation lengthens their half-life and increases the effectiveness of their distribution to the targeted regions.

Stimuli-responsive LNPs

pH-sensitive lipids react with the acidic environment found in endosomes or TME. They experience structural alterations when exposed to low pH values, which cause the lipid bilayer to become unstable and allow therapeutic cargo to enter the target cells directly as depicted in Fig. 7. By guaranteeing that active substances avoid degradation and successfully reach their molecular destinations, this mechanism greatly increases the efficiency of endosomal escape. In addition to improving drug delivery, pH-sensitive lipids reduce collateral harm to healthy tissues by taking advantage of the distinct acidity of the TME.

The purpose of ROS-responsive LNPs is to capture the concentrations of reactive oxygen species (ROS) that are commonly present in TME. LNPs experience structural deterioration when exposed to an environment high in ROS, which results in the precise and regulated release of their medicinal payload. In addition to guaranteeing that antigens are released precisely where and when required, spatiotemporal regulation also reduce systemic adverse effects. Utilizing the TME's oxidative stress features, ROS-responsive LNPs are a promising advancement in cancer immunotherapy and targeted drug delivery.

Co-delivery systems

Antigen + adjuvant combinations

LNPs that co-encapsulate mRNA antigens and STING agonists activate both the MHC-I and MHC-II pathways. A crucial stage in the immune response, dendritic cell antigen presentation is improved by dual activation. The MHC-I pathway primes cytotoxic CD8+ T lymphocytes to specifically target and destroy cancerous or infected cells. Helper CD4+ T cells simultaneously are stimulated by the MHC-II pathway at the same time. These cells coordinate and enhance the immune response. The potential of this strategy in cancer immunotherapy and vaccine development is demonstrated by the more robust and comprehensive adaptive immune response resulting from the synergy between these pathways (Fig. 8).

Checkpoint inhibitor integration

T-cell responses are regulated by inhibitory immune checkpoints that maintain peripheral tolerance but can be co-opted by tumors to suppress antitumor immunity. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) are monoclonal antibodies that restore antitumor T-cell function by blocking these inhibitory receptor–ligand interactions and have become a key partner strategy for therapeutic cancer vaccination.

Clinically used ICIs largely fall into three mechanistic classes. Anti-PD-1 antibodies (e.g., nivolumab, pembrolizumab, cemiplimab) bind PD-1 expressed primarily on activated/exhausted T cells and prevent inhibitory signaling triggered by PD-L1/PD-L2 engagement, thereby restoring T-cell effector function within the tumor microenvironment. Anti-PD-L1 antibodies (e.g., atezolizumab, durvalumab, avelumab) bind PD-L1 expressed on tumor cells and/or antigen-presenting cells, blocking PD-L1 interaction with PD-1 (and PD-L1–CD80 interaction), while PD-L2 is not directly targeted by anti-PD-L1 agents. Anti-CTLA-4 antibodies (e.g., ipilimumab) block CTLA-4, which normally competes with CD28 for CD80/CD86 binding, thereby enhancing T-cell priming and expansion, particularly in lymphoid tissues. Importantly, these agents do not “inhibit CD28 signaling”; rather, they relieve inhibitory pathways that limit T-cell activation.

Mechanistically, LNP-based cancer vaccines and ICIs are complementary. mRNA-LNP vaccines can increase tumor-specific T-cell priming by delivering antigen and promoting antigen presentation, whereas PD-1/PD-L1 blockade can prevent or reverse dysfunction of vaccine-expanded T cells within tumors, improving effector persistence and intratumoral activity. This rationale supports combination strategies across tumor types, while recognizing that ICI combinations and vaccine–ICI regimens may increase the frequency and earlier onset of immune-related adverse events compared with monotherapy.

Emerging approaches also explore checkpoint modulation at the delivery level, including nucleic-acid payloads designed to locally reduce immunosuppressive signaling (e.g., silencing checkpoint-related targets in the tumor microenvironment). While promising in preclinical settings, these strategies require careful assessment of on-target immune potentiation versus systemic immune toxicity and must be evaluated with clinically relevant endpoints and safety monitoring.

Multi-antigen cocktails

According to earlier research, tailored neoantigen-targeting tactics are crucial for maximizing immunotherapy. This is particularly true for colorectal cancer and other extremely diverse cancers, in which each tumor cell may have a unique combination of neoantigens that are not present in the surrounding healthy tissue. Enhancing the immunogenic responses elicited by these neoantigens is essential for achieving effective treatment outcomes. Neoantigen-rich cancer vaccines may increase therapeutic effectiveness and patient-specific immune responses by targeting clonal and subclonal neoantigens with differentiated immune responses. Understanding the exact relationship between LNP encapsulation of neoantigens and the activation of immunological responses is crucial because factors such as the quantity of neoantigens provided by each particle might influence the degree of T-cell activation and, in turn, antitumor immunity. In conclusion, research on LNP-based neoantigen systems may provide new treatment approaches that effectively regulate tumor heterogeneity in the treatment of colorectal cancer (Fig. 9).

LNP-based neoantigen administration for the treatment of colorectal cancer is currently being investigated in the BioNTech Phase I trial. BNT152-01C is a trial that examined the safety and effectiveness of BNT152+153, an mRNA-based immunotherapy that targets solid tumors. Although the goal of this study was to address tumor heterogeneity, the accessible sources did not specifically discuss particular data on the number of neoantigens per particle and their effect on overcoming heterogeneity. The ability of messenger RNA (mRNA) technology to guide the host cell creation of tumor-specific neoantigens, which can trigger a potent immune response, is the foundation for its therapeutic application. The main goal of this study is to address tumor heterogeneity, a significant barrier to the successful treatment of colorectal cancer, while also enhancing tumor diagnostics. The effectiveness of mRNA therapy against tumors will hopefully be clarified by this study, but little is known about how to quantify the number of neoantigens per lipid nanoparticle or how this metric may impact the ability of the treatment to overcome the inherent heterogeneity present in solid tumors.

Emerging formulations

Biodegradable and cleavable ionizable lipids

A growing strategy to improve the tolerability of mRNA-LNPs is the use of biodegradable (cleavable) ionizable lipids, designed to maintain delivery potency while reducing lipid persistence and associated hepatic burden (Table 3). In this context, ether-containing or otherwise cleavable lipids (e.g., LP01 and related structures) have attracted attention as next-generation components that can support efficient mRNA complexation and endosomal escape, yet undergo in vivo breakdown into less bioaccumulative by-products. Such designs aim to preserve transfection efficiency while mitigating dose-limiting toxicities that can arise from repeated administration or higher exposure, thereby improving the translational feasibility of mRNA-based therapies and vaccines.

PEG alternatives and stealth coatings: POx and beyond

Poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) remains a widely used “stealth” component for stabilizing LNPs and improving circulation behavior; however, repeated exposure may contribute to anti-PEG antibody formation, potentially accelerating clearance and reducing performance in subsequent dosing. To address this limitation, poly(2-oxazoline) (POx) has emerged as a promising alternative coating that can deliver PEG-like colloidal stabilization while potentially reducing immunological liabilities. Beyond POx, zwitterionic and other hydrophilic polymers are also being explored to maintain stability without triggering anti-PEG responses. In cancer vaccination, where iterative dosing and combination regimens are common, PEG-alternative strategies may be particularly valuable for sustaining exposure and consistency across treatment cycles

Lyophilized and thermostable LNPs

Cold-chain dependence remains a practical barrier to global deployment of mRNA-LNP products. Lyophilization (freeze-drying) is therefore being actively investigated to improve storage stability and enable distribution under less restrictive conditions. Importantly, successful lyophilization of LNPs is not a simple “drying step”; it requires careful control of formulation variables to prevent particle aggregation, leakage of encapsulated mRNA, or loss of functional potency upon reconstitution. In practice, lyophilized LNP development typically focuses on (i) selection of suitable cryo/lyoprotectants, (ii) optimization of freezing and drying cycles, and (iii) post-reconstitution assessment of critical quality attributes (e.g., size distribution, encapsulation efficiency, and transfection potency). If robustly optimized, lyophilized LNPs can extend shelf life and reduce reliance on ultra-low temperature infrastructure, which may improve feasibility for multi-site clinical studies and broader access in resource-limited settings.

Emerging RNA formats relevant to LNP cancer vaccination

Alongside lipid innovations, next-generation RNA payloads are being evaluated to improve the balance between immunostimulation, translation, and durability of antigen expression. Examples include self-amplifying RNA, circular RNA, and sequence or chemistry modifications intended to tune innate sensing and enhance protein production. These approaches may reduce the required dose, prolong antigen presentation, or reshape innate immune activation, but they also introduce new formulation and analytical considerations (e.g., integrity, size, and potency assays) for LNP-based delivery. As such, advances in RNA engineering and LNP design are increasingly interdependent, and future platforms will likely co-optimize both the carrier and payload to improve efficacy and tolerability in cancer vaccine settings.

Preclinical and clinical progress of LNP-based cancer vaccines

The development of LNP–based cancer vaccines, spanning preclinical models to clinical trials, has advanced through several important milestones, as illustrated in the timeline (Fig. 10), reflecting both substantial progress and ongoing challenges. The main conclusions from both human and animal trials are summarized below, with a focus on safety, effectiveness, and translational ideas. LNPs' ability to deliver mRNA-based cancer vaccines efficiently, guaranteeing strong antigen expression and immune response activation, has been convincingly demonstrated in preclinical research.

Early-phase clinical trials have been made possible by these discoveries, and encouraging efficacy has been noted. However, there are challenges in converting preclinical success into clinical applications, including establishing scalable production processes, minimizing potential immune-related side effects, and optimizing the dosage. To overcome these obstacles and enable safe and efficient administration of cancer vaccines in a variety of patient populations, LNP formulation refinement is still essential.

Preclinical advances

According to Sahin et al. (1), mRNA-LNPs that encode tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) cause B16F10 melanomas to completely recede in mice by increasing antigen-specific CD8+ T cells, with tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes reaching ≥60%. Additionally, the survival rates increased by 70% when these LNPs were combined with anti-PD-1 treatment. Furthermore, in BRAF-mutant melanoma models, LNPs that co-delivered neoantigens and STING agonists, such as cGAMP, showed exceptional potency, achieving 90% tumor eradication. These results demonstrate how mRNA-LNP-based cancer vaccines might boost immune responses and enhance treatment outcomes.

Fig. 11 highlights how LNP-based immunotherapies can drive robust antitumor activity in vivo when formulation and immunomodulation are combined. In an RM-1-PSMA prostate cancer model, combining the vaccine components (Tetra + ImmunER) with immune-checkpoint inhibition produced the strongest macroscopic response, including complete tumor regression in a subset of animals (Fig. 11A) and the most pronounced suppression of tumor growth relative to PBS, ICI alone, or Tetra + ImmunER without ICI (Fig. 11B). The ImmunER strategy further demonstrates that LNP-enabled immunostimulatory design can translate into improved tumor control and survival, outperforming comparator immunomodulatory constructs in the same RM-1-PSMA setting (Fig. 11C). Importantly, representative liver histology/fluorescence imaging suggests treatment-dependent differences in hepatic signal and tissue appearance, supporting the need to evaluate biodistribution and tolerability alongside efficacy for systemic LNP dosing (Fig. 11D). Finally, in a KPC-luc pancreatic cancer metastasis model established by hemisplenic inoculation, delivery of cGAMP-containing LNPs (including KRAS-encoded constructs) was associated with extended survival compared with controls, illustrating how LNP platforms can also co-deliver innate immune agonists to enhance therapeutic outcomes in aggressive disease models (Fig. 11E).

Studies on mRNA-LNPs that target E6/E7 oncoproteins have shown encouraging outcomes in cervical cancer models infected with HPV. According to previous studies, these vaccinations produced long-lasting memory T cell responses and effectively eliminated 80% of the mice. This implies that by sustaining a strong immune response, mRNA-LNP-based immunotherapy may offer long-term protection against tumor recurrence.

Combining LNPs, which are proven to be effective drug delivery vehicles, with Toll-like receptor (TLR) agonists, such as resistimod, appears to offer a novel strategy for reprogramming the tumor microenvironment. Given the resistance of glioblastoma tumors to traditional treatments, the ability to repolarize immunosuppressive microglia may revolutionize the treatment of this disease. Furthermore, the ability of TLR7/8 agonists to stimulate immune responses in malignancies has been studied. Research has demonstrated that by altering the immunosuppressive milieu, intravenous administration of nanoparticles loaded with a TLR7/8-agonist might cause tumor regression. This implies that attacking myeloid cells instead of depending only on T-cell-mediated immunity may be a viable strategy.

LNPs targeting at lymph nodes have been shown to efficiently stimulate dendritic cells (DCs) and enhance antigen-specific immune responses. The impressive tenfold increase in OVA-specific CD8+ T cells in lymph nodes demonstrates the effectiveness of this strategy in boosting adaptive immunity. Additionally, the manner in which LNP vaccines alter the tumor microenvironment (TME) is better understood using single-cell RNA sequencing. According to previous studies, these vaccinations can increase DC-T cell interactions and decrease regulatory T cells (Tregs) and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), creating an environment that is immunostimulatory. Improving the results of cancer immunotherapy may depend on TME modification (Table 4A).

Clinical trials and outcomes

As an adjuvant treatment for resected melanoma, mRNA-4157 (Moderna) in conjunction with pembrolizumab has demonstrated encouraging outcomes. Clinical studies have shown that after an 18-month follow-up period, the chance of recurrence was reduced by 44%. Pembrolizumab alone had a median recurrence-free survival (RFS) of 16.0 months, while the combined therapy had a median RFS of 25.7 months. In terms of safety, 14% of patients experienced grade 3 adverse events (AEs), with fatigue and injection-site responses being the most frequent side effects. Phase 3 trials are still being conducted to assess the treatment to confirm its effectiveness and long-term advantages.

Tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) such as NY-ESO-1, MAGE-A3, TPTE, and MUC1 are encoded by mRNA-lipid nanoparticles (mRNA-LNPs) in this method, which activate polyfunctional CD4+/CD8+ T cells. Promising outcomes were observed in melanoma RNA vaccine research, where patients had T cell responses against at least one TAA after immunization (Fig. 12a-b). The expression of these TAAs in both adult and pediatric melanoma has been demonstrated in another study, confirming the viability of vaccinations that target them. Furthermore, patients with anti-PD-1-refractory advanced melanoma showed a statistically significant improvement in the overall response rate (ORR) in BioNTech's Phase 2 trial of BNT111, an mRNA immunotherapy candidate.

CureVac's CV8102 is an experimental immunotherapy for solid tumors, such as head and neck cancer and melanoma. Patients with melanoma and head/neck cancer showed partial responses to CV8102 in a Phase I study, with sa 25% disease control rate (DCR) (Fig. 12c-d). The treatment uses TLR7/8 agonist co-delivery, which increases DC activation and may strengthen immune responses against cancer.

There are ongoing studies on the immunogenicity and safety profile of LNPs for mRNA-based treatments. In early trials, 30–40% of patients had anti-polyethylene glycol (PEG) antibodies, which were associated with a shorter LNP circulation duration and decreased efficacy. This implies that the pharmacokinetics of PEGylated treatments may be affected by generated or pre-existing anti-PEG antibodies. In terms of hepatic toxicity, 15% of patients were found to have temporary grade 1-2 in liver enzymes (ALT/AST), which usually go away on their own without treatment (Table 4B).

Challenges in LNP-based cancer vaccine development

Although LNPs have great potential to transform into cancer vaccines, there are many challenges in their path from the lab to the patient's bedside. The clinical translation of this innovative technology is frequently complicated by these issues, which cut across technical, biological, and logistical domains. It is essential to critically assess these obstacles using preclinical and clinical data in order to overcome them. We can begin outlining and putting into practice ways that open the door for LNPs to reach their full promise in revolutionizing cancer treatment by thoroughly comprehending these limits (Table 5)

Manufacturing and scalability

LNP manufacture is highly process-sensitive: small deviations in lipid composition, solvent–aqueous mixing ratio, flow conditions, or temperature during microfluidic assembly can shift particle size, polydispersity, and encapsulation efficiency, with downstream consequences for biodistribution and potency. Accordingly, scale-up requires tight control of critical material and process attributes, including the ionizable lipid:mRNA ratio and mixing parameters, to ensure batch-to-batch consistency.

Beyond technical control, cost and GMP compliance remain major barriers to broad deployment. Reported estimates place GMP-produced mRNA–LNP vaccines at approximately USD 10–20 per dose, with ionizable lipids (often proprietary chemistries such as ALC-0315) representing a dominant cost driver. Reducing dependence on proprietary components through expanded, well-characterized lipid libraries, coupled with transparent quality specifications, could improve affordability and supply resilience. On the manufacturing side, high-throughput microfluidic platforms can increase volumetric productivity while preserving mixing precision, and the transition from batch to continuous manufacturing offers a practical route to improved process robustness, in-line quality monitoring, and consistent product quality at scale.

Stability and storage

Most mRNA–LNP formulations require frozen storage (typically −20 to −80 °C) to minimize lipid aggregation and mRNA degradation, which complicates distribution and limits deployment in settings with constrained cold-chain capacity Lyophilization is a promising strategy to improve stability and logistics, but it can introduce risks during reconstitution, including changes in particle size, encapsulation efficiency, and potency. Accordingly, formulation work on cryo/lyoprotectants and standardized reconstitution conditions remains essential to preserve LNP integrity after drying. Relatedly, the reported refrigerated stability of CVnCoV (CureVac) highlights the feasibility of improving thermostability in mRNA-based products and provides a useful benchmark for next-generation, more deployable cancer vaccine formulations.

Immunogenicity and toxicity

PEGylated LNPs can trigger anti-PEG antibodies in a substantial fraction of recipients, contributing to the accelerated blood clearance (ABC) phenomenon and reduced exposure after repeat dosing. In clinical settings, anti-PEG IgM has been associated with weaker cellular immune readouts in some mRNA vaccine studies, underscoring that surface chemistry can meaningfully influence immunogenicity and efficacy (L). LNPs also exhibit a natural tendency to accumulate in the liver, in part through ApoE-mediated uptake by hepatocytes, which may contribute to transient hepatotoxicity signals (e.g., elevations in ALT/AST reported in a subset of patients). Particle size further modulates clearance and off-target exposure; very small LNPs can show increased splenic and renal filtration, complicating dose optimization and biodistribution control.

To mitigate these limitations, PEG alternatives such as poly(2-oxazoline) coatings and zwitterionic lipids are being explored to reduce immunogenicity while maintaining colloidal stability. In parallel, active-targeting strategies (e.g., anti-CD40 ligands for dendritic cells or RGD-based tumor targeting) aim to improve on-target delivery and potentially lower required doses, thereby widening the therapeutic window.

Tumor Microenvironment (TME) resistance

Multiple TME mechanisms can blunt responses to LNP-based cancer vaccines. Immunosuppressive populations such as myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and regulatory T cells (Tregs) can dampen effector T-cell priming and function, motivating combination strategies that deplete or reprogram these cells to improve vaccine responsiveness. In parallel, abnormal tumor vasculature and poor lymphatic drainage elevate interstitial fluid pressure and restrict nanoparticle penetration, limiting intratumoral delivery; vascular-normalization approaches have therefore been explored to improve drug distribution within solid tumors. Tumors may also evade immune recognition by downregulating antigen presentation pathways, including MHC-I, thereby reducing CD8+ T-cell activation and contributing to non-response in a subset of patients. Consequently, rational combinations that relieve immunosuppressive signaling (e.g., TGF-β inhibition) and/or restore T-cell function (e.g., PD-1/CTLA-4 blockade) are frequently proposed to overcome TME resistance and enhance vaccine efficacy.

Regulatory and commercial hurdles

Personalized mRNA–LNP cancer vaccines face operational and regulatory bottlenecks because they combine complex manufacturing with patient-specific antigen selection. End-to-end workflows spanning neoantigen identification, LNP production, and quality control can take several weeks, which is problematic for rapidly progressing malignancies. Ongoing efforts therefore focus on accelerating bioinformatic neoantigen prediction, increasing automation, and streamlining GMP-compliant manufacturing to shorten turnaround time. In parallel, regulatory pathways remain less standardized than for conventional “one-size-fits-all” vaccines, as individualized products require tailored approaches to lot release, comparability, and benefit–risk evaluation. Commercial translation is further limited by the lack of fully validated predictive biomarkers (e.g., tumor mutational burden and T-cell receptor clonality) with harmonized measurement and interpretation, which complicates patient stratification and trial design (Table 4).

However, the clinical utility of these biomarkers remains limited by the lack of standardized methods for quantification, interpretation, and threshold selection. Likewise, T-cell receptor (TCR) clonality, which captures the diversity and expansion of tumor-reactive T-cell populations, shows promise for predicting immune responses but still requires further validation and harmonization before routine clinical use. To address these gaps, current efforts focus on integrating multi-parameter biomarker panels with advanced sequencing and analytical pipelines to improve predictive accuracy and enable more reliable patient stratification, ultimately supporting more personalized and effective therapies.

Future perspectives in lipid nanoparticle (LNP)-based cancer vaccines

LNP platforms are accelerating therapeutic cancer vaccination by enabling efficient nucleic acid delivery, tunable immune activation, and rapid redesign for patient-specific targets. Continued progress will likely come from four converging directions: (i) personalization and AI-enabled design to improve neoantigen selection and optimize LNP structure–function (e.g., peptide–HLA binding, antigen processing, and immunogenicity prediction), (ii) next-generation formulations that reduce off-target toxicity and repeat-dose limitations (e.g., biodegradable/cleavable ionizable lipids, PEG alternatives, and stimulus-responsive release in acidic/oxidative or protease-rich TMEs), (iii) rational combinations to overcome TME resistance (e.g., checkpoint blockade, microenvironment reprogramming, or antigen-visibility enhancement), and (iv) manufacturing/regulatory modernization to shorten turnaround times and improve batch consistency through high-throughput microfluidics, continuous processing, and improved quality-by-design frameworks. Finally, global impact will depend on thermostable formats (e.g., lyophilized or excipient-stabilized LNPs) that reduce cold-chain dependence and support deployment beyond highly resourced settings. Together, these advances position LNP cancer vaccines to become more potent, safer for repeat dosing, faster to manufacture, and more broadly accessible (Table 6).

Conclusion

LNPs have emerged as a revolutionary platform for cancer vaccination, bridging the gap between cutting-edge nanotechnology and clinical immunotherapy. Their success with mRNA COVID-19 vaccines demonstrated their ability to transport nucleic acids safely and effectively, which opened the way for their rapid adoption in the cancer field. Co-delivery of immunostimulatory adjuvants, customized dendritic cell activation, and customized neoantigen administration are recent advancements that demonstrate versatility of LNPs in generating potent and durable anticancer immunity. Early clinical trials, such as those using Moderna's mRNA-4157 and BioNTech's BNT111, showed their capacity to reduce recurrence risks and improve survival in melanoma and other solid tumors.

However, significant challenges remain unaddressed. Significant barriers to widespread clinical use include the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME), cold-chain dependence, anti-PEG immunogenicity, and scalable manufacturing. Biodegradable lipid formulations, AI-driven neoantigen prediction, and combination therapy employing checkpoint inhibitors or CAR-T cells are novel strategies required to overcome these challenges. Stimuli-responsive LNPs and CRISPR-based genome editing increase their therapeutic potential by precisely regulating spatiotemporal activation of the immune system.

The development of LNP-based cancer vaccines requires cooperation between multiple disciplines. Together, chemists, immunologists, and doctors can enhance lipid compositions, speed up production, and confirm biomarkers for patient stratification. Even if decentralized production and global accessibility programs, such as lyophilized LNPs guarantee equitable distribution, regulatory frameworks need to adapt to permit customized medications. In summary, LNPs represent a paradigm shift in cancer immunotherapy by providing a dynamic platform for delivery of tailored, potent, and adaptable vaccines. Despite persistent challenges, immunological engineering, artificial intelligence, and nanotechnology work together to unlock their full potential. LNPs' unwavering innovation and collaboration may herald a new era of precision oncology, where each patient's immune system acts as a weapon against their illness, potentially transforming cancer vaccines from experimental instruments into widely used medications.

Beyond their current role as straightforward delivery vehicles, LNPs have the potential to evolve into intelligent systems that can perceive, adapt, and reprogram the tumor microenvironment as the field advances. Although the journey from bench to bedside will require patience, creativity, and international cooperation, the reward for a future in which cancer vaccines are ubiquitous and transformative as childhood vaccinations are within reach.
