# A First-in-Human Study of Givastomig, a CLDN18.2 and 4-1BB Bispecific Antibody, as Monotherapy in Patients with CLDN18.2-Positive Advanced or Metastatic Solid Tumors.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Givastomig is a bispecific antibody that targets CLDN18.2 and conditionally activates local 4-1BB-expressing T cells. This first-in-human study evaluated the safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and antitumor activity of givastomig in advanced solid tumors.

**PATIENTS AND METHODS**: A total of 75 patients were enrolled. Escalating givastomig doses of 0.1 to 18 mg/kg were evaluated in 36 patients with metastatic or advanced solid tumors. An additional 6 patients per cohort with CLDN18.2-positive (defined as membrane intensity score of ≥1+ on ≥1% of tumor cells) advanced or metastatic gastric cancer, gastroesophageal junction cancer, or gastroesophageal carcinoma (GEC) were treated with 5 to 15 mg/kg givastomig across four cohorts. Fifteen patients with CLDN18.2-positive GEC were then enrolled to the 12 mg/kg dose expansion cohort.

**RESULTS**: No dose-limiting toxicities were reported up to 18 mg/kg, and a maximum tolerated dose was not reached. The most common treatment-related adverse events (in ≥10% of patients) were nausea, anemia, fatigue, white blood cell count decrease, vomiting, and increased alanine aminotransferase. Givastomig exposure increased dose proportionally, and soluble 4-1BB approached a plateau above 5 mg/kg. The 12 mg/kg dose was selected for dose expansion. A 16% objective response rate was observed in CLDN18.2-positive GEC above 5 mg/kg (N = 43). CLDN18.2 expression in responders ranged from 11% to 100%.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Givastomig demonstrated manageable safety, dose-proportional exposure, and antitumor activity in patients with advanced solid tumors, particularly in CLDN18.2-positive GEC. A givastomig dose range of 5 to 12 mg/kg was chosen to combine with nivolumab and chemotherapy in part 2 of the study in frontline metastatic GEC. See related commentary by Pretelli and Garralda, p. 4866.

Translational Relevance

Givastomig (TJ033721 or ABL111) is a first-in-class Fc-silenced CLDN18.2 × 4-1BB bispecific antibody. Its antitumor activity depends on dual engagement of CLDN18.2 on tumor cells and 4-1BB on adjacent immune cells, thereby restricting 4-1BB agonism to the local tumor microenvironment to potentially limit systemic immune and hepatic toxicity. In this phase 1 study, givastomig at escalating doses was well-tolerated and exhibited encouraging antitumor activity in patients with gastroesophageal tumors expressing a wide range of CLDN18.2 levels, including expression as low as 1+ in 11% of tumor cells. These results support ongoing investigation of givastomig in combination with other agents for the treatment of gastroesophageal cancer.

Introduction

Claudins (CLDN) are members of the tetraspanin transmembrane protein family composing intercellular tight junctions present in different tissues. The isoforms CLDN18.1 and CLDN18.2 (product of CLDN18) show a differential expression pattern in normal tissues: whereas CLDN18.1 expression is restricted to the lung, CLDN18.2 is exclusively expressed in differentiated gastric epithelium. However, malignant transformation leads to disruptions in tight junctions, and alterations in cell polarity result in exposure of CLDN18.2 epitopes on the surface of tumor cells. CLDN18.2 protein is detected at various levels in 80% of ductal pancreatic adenocarcinoma, 75% of esophagus adenocarcinoma, roughly 70% of gastric/gastroesophageal junction (GEJ) cancers, 10% of ovarian cancer, and 6% of non–small cell lung cancer.

The high tumor-to-normal expression patterns have made CLDN18.2 an attractive therapeutic target with potential inherent tumor specificity. In the combined analysis of randomized phase 3 SPOTLIGHT and GLOW trials in treatment-naïve patients with locally advanced, unresectable, or metastatic gastroesophageal carcinoma (GEC) with high to moderate CLDN18.2 expression (defined as 2+/3+ in >75% of tumor cells), zolbetuximab, a chimeric mAb targeting CLDN18.2, combined with chemotherapy did not improve objective response rates (ORR), yet achieved median overall survival and progression-free survival (PFS) of 16.4 months versus 13.7 months and 9.2 months versus 8.2 months, respectively, compared with chemotherapy alone. However, in both SPOTLIGHT and GLOW, grade ≥3 nausea and vomiting occurred in 9% to 16% of patients, affecting its clinical use. Furthermore, due to the high CLDN18.2 expression cutoff, only a little more than a third of patients with GEC are eligible to receive zolbetuximab. Therapeutic approaches to broaden the patient population across a wider range of CLDN18.2 expression levels and improve the gastrointestinal (GI) toxicity profile remain an unmet clinical need.

4-1BB (product of TNFRSF9) is an established costimulatory molecule and member of the TNF superfamily. 4-1BB agonist antibodies have been attractive immunotherapy targets because of the expression of 4-1BB on activated T cells and the ability of 4-1BB to directly stimulate CD8+ T-cell proliferation and enhance cytotoxicity. In nonclinical tumor models, the 4-1BB agonist antibody 1D8 restored the function of exhausted CD8+ tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and inhibited tumor growth. The clinical development of systemically administered naked 4-1BB agonists has been limited by a high incidence of hepatotoxicity, despite some evidence of favorable pharmacodynamics (PD) modulation. More recently, strategies to restrict 4-1BB agonism to the local tumor microenvironment (TME) have shown early promise and represent an attractive approach.

Givastomig (TJ033721 or ABL111) is a first-in-class bispecific antibody that targets both CLDN18.2 and 4-1BB. Givastomig is engineered to contain a single Fc-domain mutation (asparagine to alanine) to eliminate Fc-effector function. In an in vitro coculture study, givastomig bound cell lines expressing a range of CLDN18.2 levels with a high affinity and induced 4-1BB activation only in the context of CLDN18.2 binding (Supplementary Fig. S1A and S1B; ref.). In humanized 4-1BB transgenic mice inoculated with human CLDN18.2-expressing tumor cells, givastomig induced a localized immune activation in tumor as evident by the increased ratio of CD8+/regulatory T cells, leading to superior antitumor activity and a long-lasting memory response against tumor rechallenge. Givastomig was well-tolerated, with no systemic immune response and hepatotoxicity in cynomolgus monkeys. In this study, we report the first-in-human (FIH) safety, tolerability, pharmacokinetics (PK), PD, and antitumor activity of givastomig monotherapy in patients with advanced solid tumors.

Patients and Methods

Study design

This phase 1 FIH study of givastomig comprises two parts. A total of 75 patients were treated in part 1, and part 2 is ongoing in combination with nivolumab and chemotherapy (Trial registration ID: NCT04900818). Escalating givastomig doses of 0.1 to 18 mg/kg were evaluated using the Bayesian optimal interval design in 38 patients with metastatic or locally advanced unresectable solid tumors regardless of CLDN18.2 expression. Dose levels 5, 8, 12, and 15 mg/kg were expanded to include an additional six patients per cohort with CLDN18.2-positive (defined as a plasma membrane intensity score of ≥1+ on ≥1% of tumor cells) advanced or metastatic gastric cancer, GEJ cancer, and gastroesophageal adenocarcinomas (GEC). The 12 mg/kg dose was selected for dose expansion to further explore activity in patients with CLDN18.2-positive GEC (n = 15). Part 2 was designed to study selected givastomig doses in combination with anti–PD-1 and standard 5-fluorouracil (5FU)/oxaliplatin chemotherapy in frontline CLDN18.2-positive patients with GEC. Part 2 is actively accruing and not reported here. The study was conducted in accordance with the protocol, Good Clinical Practice guidelines, and the Declaration of Helsinki and Council for International Organizations of Medical Sciences International Ethical Guidelines. Applicable local regulatory requirements were followed. The protocol, protocol amendments, informed consent form, and other relevant documents were reviewed and approved by each site’s Institutional Review Board before the study was initiated. All participants provided written informed consent.

Participants

Eligible patients were 18 years or older, with at least one histologically confirmed, unresectable, measurable lesion (as assessed by RECIST, version 1.1). The protocol was amended to restrict enrollment to patients with known PD-L1 status prior to enrollment of the expansion cohort at 12 mg/kg. In part 1, patients were required to have received prior therapy for their tumor type and patients with HER2-positive GEC must have received prior anti-HER2 therapy or been deemed unsuitable for such therapy. All patients were required to have an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status (PS) of 0 or 1, adequate organ function, and an estimated life expectancy >12 weeks. Patients enrolled to any expansion cohorts were required to have CLDN18.2-positive tumors, defined as a plasma membrane intensity score of ≥1+ on ≥1% of tumor cells, as determined by the Ventana CLDN18.2 SP455 IHC assay via central testing.

Treatment and dose escalation

In part 1 monotherapy dose escalation, dosing was initiated at 0.1 mg/kg and was sequentially escalated up to 15 mg/kg [0.1, 0.3, 1.0, 3.0, 5.0, 8.0, 12.0, and 15 mg/kg i.v. once every 2 weeks (Q2W; 28-day cycles) and 18 mg/kg i.v. once every 3 weeks (Q3W; 21-day cycles)]. The dose-limiting toxicity (DLT) window was the first 28 days of therapy in cycle 1 during dose escalation. Treatments continued until disease progression, intolerable adverse events (AE), or patient withdrawal of informed consent for up to 2 years. During cycle 1, AEs meeting the DLT criteria (Supplementary Table S1) were considered DLTs unless clearly unrelated to givastomig. Tumor response was assessed by CT or MRI every 8 weeks in the first year and every 12 weeks in the second year. Patients were followed for AEs for up to 90 days after the last dose of treatment, and survival follow-up was conducted every 16 weeks via telephone.

Study objectives

The primary objectives of part 1 were to determine the safety, tolerability, maximum tolerated dose or maximum administered dose, and the recommended phase 2 dose of givastomig in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors. Secondary objectives were to characterize the PK and PD profiles, evaluate immunogenicity, and assess the preliminary antitumor activity of givastomig.

Study assessments and endpoints

The primary safety endpoints were the occurrence of DLTs, incidence and severity of AEs evaluated by the investigator and rated according to Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events version 5.0, incidence of laboratory abnormalities, 12-lead ECG parameters, vital sign measurements, physical examinations, ECOG PS, and concomitant medication use. Secondary endpoints included characterization of the PK/PD profiles and preliminary antitumor activity of givastomig, including the ORR, duration of response (DOR), and PFS.

PK, PD, and biomarkers

Serum concentrations of givastomig were measured using ELISA. This method was validated to quantify givastomig concentrations with a nominal range of 10.0 to 2,000 ng/mL. PK parameters of givastomig were derived using a noncompartmental model by Phoenix version 8.5.1.3 (Certara, RRID: SCR_003163).

Serum samples for the evaluation of the PD effect of givastomig monotherapy on soluble 4-1BB (s4-1BB) were collected predose on day 1 of each cycle and on day 15 of cycles 1 and 2 for all patients; at 1 hour, 24 hours, and day 8 after the first dose in cycle 1; and 1 hour after dosing on cycle 2 day 1 from patients in dose escalation. Levels of total (including both free and drug-bound) s4-1BB in serum were determined using an electrochemiluminescence assay based on the Meso Scale Discovery platform. The method for quantification of s4-1BB in human serum was adapted from the manufacturer’s instructions and further modified to eliminate the drug interference caused by competing binding to s4-1BB by givastomig against the capture and detection of polyclonal antibodies used in the assay. Specifically, both the clinical samples and standard curve samples were preincubated with givastomig at a saturated concentration followed by assay detection. In this way, both free and drug-bound s4-1BB were converted into a drug-bound format for quantification.

A panel of cytokines, including IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-6, IL-8, and IL-10, were quantitated in serum samples taken at multiple pretreatment and on-treatment timepoints (C1D1_PRE, C1D2, C1D8, C1D15_PRE, C2D1_PRE, C2D15_PRE, C3D1_PRE, and C4D1_PRE) from patients enrolled in dose escalation in the United States using an electrochemiluminescence assay based on the Meso Scale Discovery platform (PPD, part of Thermo Fisher Scientific).

Immunophenotyping from patients enrolled in dose escalation in the United States was conducted at Covance Central Laboratory Services using BD FACSCanto II Clinical Flow Cytometer (Becton, Dickinson and Company: RRID: SCR_018056) to quantitate T-cell subsets and NK cells in pretreatment and on-treatment whole blood samples.

Tumor biopsy collection and processing

Archival formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tumor tissue samples submitted for study purposes needed to be obtained within 1 year prior to screening. A new biopsy was required if a patient had received immunotherapy between the time the archival tissue was obtained and screening, because checkpoint inhibitors have been shown to alter the presence of immune infiltrates in tumors even after such treatment has ended.

Statistical analysis

As a phase 1 study, the sample size was not based on formal statistical considerations. Rather, it was based on a need to obtain sufficient safety, tolerability, PK, PD, and preliminary efficacy data. The maximum cohort size was 12 for the Bayesian optimal interval design.

The safety population included patients who received at least 1 dose of study drug and represents the primary data set for all safety analyses. If a patient experienced repeat episodes of the same AE, the event with the highest severity grade was used for purposes of incidence tabulations. Treatment response was assessed in all patients with measurable disease at baseline who received at least 1 dose of study drug and had at least one on-study tumor scan.

The PK/PD analysis included all patients with at least 1 valid postdose sample. Noncompartmental PK analysis based on the actual time each sample was obtained was performed on individual serum concentration data. PK parameters for givastomig, including maximum observed concentration (Cmax), time to Cmax, area under the concentration–time curve (AUC) from time zero to the time of the last quantifiable concentration, terminal elimination half-life (t1/2), clearance, and volume of distribution associated with the terminal phase, were assessed, if feasible, from concentration–time data.

Induction of s4-1BB in individual patients at given time points after treatment were calculated as fold changes in reference to baseline values. Differences in s4-1BB induction between groups, such as CLDN18.2-high versus CLDN18.2-low or patients with disease control [partial response (PR) or stable disease (SD)] versus no disease control (progressive disease), were analyzed with log2-transformed fold changes using the unpaired Welch t test.

Results

Study population

As of July 30, 2024, a total of 75 patients had received givastomig across 9 sequential dose levels between 0.1 and 18 mg/kg [0.1 (n = 1), 0.3 (n = 1), 1 (n = 4), 3 (n = 4), 5 (n = 12), 8 (n = 10), 12 (n = 27), 15 (n = 12), and 18 mg/kg (n = 4)] at sites in the United States and China (Supplementary Fig. S2). The median age was 63 years (range, 27–82). More than half of patients were White (57%), male (56%), and had ECOG PS 1 (69%; Table 1). The study population was heavily pretreated, with a median of three prior lines of therapy. Fifty-three percent had received prior PD-(L)1 therapy. Approximately half of the patients (56%) had gastric/GEJ cancers, 19% had pancreatic cancer, and 9% had esophageal cancer. Across all dose levels, 36% had liver metastases, and 25% had peritoneal metastases; 75% of patients were CLDN18.2-positive by the trial definition (Table 1). Study representativeness of study participants is shown in Supplementary Table S2. Reasons for not meeting eligibility criteria reported in at least two patients included inadequate organ function (n = 7), CLDN18.2-negative status (expansion only, n = 6), other acute/chronic medical issue (n = 5), ECOG PS >1 (n = 4), congestive heart failure, unstable angina, or recent cerebrovascular accident/transient ischemic attack (n = 3), unable to comply with study procedures (n = 3), and no measurable disease (n = 2).

Safety

No DLTs were observed up to 15 mg/kg i.v. Q2W and 18 mg/kg i.v. Q3W, and the maximum tolerated dose was not reached. The most common all-grade treatment-related AEs (≥10%; TRAE) were nausea (21%), anemia (15%), fatigue (13%), white blood cell count decrease (13%), vomiting (12%), and alanine aminotransferase increase (11%; Table 2). Grade 3 nausea or vomiting was seen in 1% of patients.

Grade ≥3 TRAEs were reported in 21% of patients; those that occurred in at least two patients were anemia (5%), lymphocyte count decrease (5%), white blood cell count decrease (4%), aspartate aminotransferase increase (3%), neutropenia (3%), and upper GI hemorrhage (3%; Supplementary Table S3). Treatment-related nausea and vomiting were of grade 1 or 2 in most patients. Grade 3 nausea and vomiting leading to withdrawal of study drug was seen in 1 patient treated at givastomig 18 mg/kg.

Overall, TRAEs resulting in study drug withdrawal occurred in 5% of patients, including 1 grade 3 alanine aminotransferase increase (5 mg/kg), 1 grade 3 nausea (18 mg/kg), 1 grade 3 infusion-related reaction (5 mg/kg), and 1 grade 2 pulmonary embolism (12 mg/kg). In addition, 20 patients (27%) experienced TEAEs resulting in death. Of the fatal TEAEs, 18 (90%) were related to disease progression, and two patients (10%) experienced fatal sepsis (absolute neutrophil count nadirs 3.2 and 3.0 K/µL, respectively); neither of which were attributed to study drug (Supplementary Table S4). Serious adverse events (SAE) were reported in 53% of patients (Supplementary Table S5), and treatment-related SAEs were reported in 17% of patients (Supplementary Tables S5 and S6). The protocol does not permit dose reductions of givastomig. Nine patients experienced dose interruptions because of events attributed to givastomig. There were no interruptions or withdrawals of givastomig due to nausea and vomiting.

PK

Preliminary PK data were obtained from patients treated with givastomig 0.1 to 15 mg/kg Q2W and 18 mg/kg Q3W. The exposure of givastomig (both Cmax and AUC) in the blood of these patients increased with increasing dose. At dose levels ≥5 mg/kg, the exposure increased in an approximately dose-proportional manner, as evidenced by the mean concentration–time profiles (Supplementary Fig. S3). In patients receiving givastomig doses higher than 5 mg/kg, the mean half-life ranged from 108 to 160 hours. The mean PK parameters are shown in Supplementary Table S7.

PD and peripheral biomarkers

Among the 48 patients who received givastomig doses ranging from 0.1 to 18 mg/kg during dose escalation in the United States, a dose- and time-dependent increase in s4-1BB was observed. Induction in s4-1BB approached a plateau above 5 mg/kg, with a sustained increase after cycle 1 day 8 in patients dosed at ≥3 mg/kg (Supplementary Fig. S4A and S5B).

Among the 29 patients with GEC from both dose escalation and expansion cohorts dosed at 8 to 15 mg/kg Q2W, induction in s4-1BB at cycle 1 day 15 (after the first dose) was numerically higher in patients with PR and SD (geometric mean fold change of 63; n = 15) compared with patients with progressive disease (geometric mean fold change of 40; n = 14; P = 0.143; Supplementary Fig. S5A). Induction was also statistically higher in the CLDN18.2-high group (defined as CLDN18.2 at least 1+ in >50% of tumor cells; geometric mean fold change of 72; n = 18) compared with the CLDN18.2-low group (CLDN18.2 ≤50%; geometric mean fold change of 28; n = 11; P = 0.0005; Supplementary Fig. S5B).

Proinflammatory cytokines were quantified in serum samples from 31 patients in dose cohorts 0.1 to 12 mg/kg. In addition, immunophenotyping of T-cell subsets was performed on whole blood samples from 33 patients in dose cohorts 0.1 to 15 mg/kg. No discernible dose-related peripheral PD relationship was observed for any of the inflammatory cytokines and T-cell subsets tested (Supplementary Figs. S6A–S6C, S7A, and S7B), suggesting no systemic immune activation by givastomig.

Antitumor activity

Of 73 efficacy-evaluable patients (defined as all patients who received at least 1 dose of study drug), the confirmed ORR was 11% with 8 PRs [95% confidence interval (CI), 4.9–20.5], and the disease control rate was 37% with 19 SDs (95% CI, 26.0–49.1; Table 3). SD must be documented on at least one on-treatment scan occurring at least 8 weeks from the start of treatment or a prior on-treatment scan. Figure 1 depicts the maximum extent of tumor shrinkage (waterfall plot, panel A) and the duration of givastomig treatment (swimmer plot, panel B). Fifty-six patients had CLDN18.2-positive tumors that exhibited at least 1+ staining in 1% to 100% of tumor cells. Seven PRs were observed in patients with CLDN18.2-positive GEC, and one PR was observed in a patient with squamous cell head and neck carcinoma with unknown CLDN18.2 expression. Of 43 efficacy-evaluable patients with CLDN18.2+ GEC who received givastomig ranging from 5 to 18 mg/kg, seven PRs were observed (one at 5 mg/kg, one at 8 mg/kg, four at 12 mg/kg, and one at 18 mg/kg), with an ORR of 16%. The median time to response was 1.7 months (95% CI, 0.9–1.84), and the median DOR was 9.4 months (95% CI, 3.2–not available). The disease control rate was 49%. The median PFS was 1.7 months (95% CI, 1.71–2.83). Responses were observed across a broad range of CLDN18.2 tumor cell expression (11%–100%). Locally determined PD-L1 combined positive score (CPS) data were available for five of the seven patients with GEC who achieved a PR. A CPS of ≥5 was observed in two of the five patients, and a CPS <5 was observed in the remaining three patients, including one patient with a CPS of 0 (Supplementary Table S8).

Discussion

The results from the monotherapy part of this ongoing FIH study show that givastomig was well-tolerated in patients with advanced solid tumors up to 18 mg/kg. In line with the preclinical data, a low incidence of hepatotoxicity and immune-related AEs were observed, supporting the lack of systemic 4-1BB agonism. Givastomig is a conditional 4-1BB bispecific antibody that activates 4-1BB only upon sufficient binding to CLDN18.2-expressing tumor cells. Our prior work suggested that CLDN18.2-positive tumor cells exist in close spatial proximity with both 4-1BB+/ CD4+ and 4-1BB+/ CD8+ T cells in GC/GEJ tumors, providing a plausible local environment to deploy a bispecific antibody like givastomig. In addition to the preclinical rationale, the observation of a favorable safety profile of givastomig, which is consistent with the lack of any observable systemic immune response in any of the inflammatory cytokines and T-cell subsets examined, versus other 4-1BB mAbs suggests that combination approaches may be feasible. Specifically, we observed no DLTs and low rates of nausea (21% all grade, 1% grade 3, and no grade 4) or vomiting (12% all grade, 1% grade 3, and no grade 4), which are a barrier to clinical implementation of other CLDN18.2 therapies, despite the fact that prophylactic medications were not routinely administered in our study. Although preliminary, these rates of nausea and vomiting compare favorably with other reported CLDN18.2-directed agents in monotherapy or combination. We postulate that the reduced GI toxicities with givastomig compared with these other agents may be attributed to the fact that the Fc-effector function of givastomig is silenced. CLDN18.2-targeted antibodies or antibody–drug conjugates with intact Fc-effector function, such as zolbetuximab or CMG901 (also known as AZD0901), may result in Fc-γ receptor–mediated antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity against CLDN18.2-expressing tumor cells or normal gastric mucosa cells. Antibody–drug conjugate IBI343 also uses an Fc-silenced moiety; preliminary results from an ongoing phase 1 study in patients with advanced pancreatic or biliary tract tumors showed 26% treatment-related all-grade nausea and 26% treatment-related all-grade vomiting.

Defining the PK and PD parameters to select the optimal dose level of bispecific antibodies, particularly those with locally constrained biologic effects, can be difficult. However, based on the totality of available data, a dose range between 8 and 12 mg/kg givastomig has shown adequate PK, PD, tolerability, and consistent clinical activity. A dose-proportional increase in givastomig exposure was observed beginning at 5 mg/kg, and a previously conducted population PK model showing the target threshold concentration could be achieved in all patients receiving givastomig 12 mg/kg Q2W. The 12 mg/kg Q2W dose was chosen as the recommended dose for givastomig monotherapy based on the totality of available data, including exposure, 4-1BB activation, safety, and clinical activity. Although an imperfect surrogate, a higher tumoral CLDN18.2 expression may induce a more robust 4-1BB agonism and be reflected in a greater s4-1BB induction. In fact, in patients dosed at 8 to 15 mg/kg Q2W, induction of s4-1BB was significantly higher in the CLDN18.2-high group compared with the CLDN18.2-low group, which suggests that localized T-cell activation by givastomig was mediated by CLDN18.2 expression on tumor cells. Furthermore, the induction of s4-1BB in these dose cohorts was numerically higher in patients with PR and SD, compared with patients with progressive disease, supporting the hypothesis that a restricted activation of 4-1BB-positive T cells by givastomig in a TME leads to antitumor immunity. Although the combination of zolbetuximab with chemotherapies has been approved in frontline gastric cancer, its efficacy is limited to patients with high CLDN18.2 expression (2+/3+ in >75% tumor cells). In contrast, givastomig responses were observed across a broad range of CLDN18.2 expression, including CLDN18.2 low expressors. Having low expressors respond to givastomig does not exclude the possibility that CLDN18.2 expression may select for patient responses as more patients had responses at higher levels of CLDN18.2 cutoffs of 50% and 75%. Compared with zolbetuximab, givastomig was designed to have higher binding affinity to CLDN18.2-expressing cells at different levels of CLDN18.2, particularly in cell lines with low CLDN18.2 expression. These data suggest that givastomig might elicit 4-1BB activation in the TME of patients with low CLDN18.2 expression. The population explored in this study is limited by clinical heterogeneity and a wide range of CLDN18.2 expression, limiting our ability to explore the optimal CLDN18.2 cut point for givastomig activity.

Whereas clinical efficacy data in this study should be considered exploratory, several interesting observations can be made. First, based on our preclinical data, we allowed enrollment of patients with a broad range of CLDN18.2 expression (1+ or greater in ≥1% of tumor cells). We observed response and/or SD in CLDN18.2 low expressors, with a monotherapy ORR of 16% in patients with CLDN18.2-positive GEC (n = 43). Although tested in slightly different patient populations, and with all the caveats of cross-trial comparisons, the ORR in this study is numerically higher than the 9% ORR seen with single-agent zolbetuximab in previously treated GEC with a substantially higher cutoff (CLDN18.2 2+/3+ intensity in ≥50% tumor cells) in the MONO trial. In the current study, the median DOR was 9.4 months compared with 6.1 months for zolbetuximab in the MONO study, raising the possibility that the enhanced T-cell response with givastomig may result in more durable responses. None of our patients had prior CLDN18.2-directed therapies, and we cannot speculate on potential activity after zolbetuximab therapy. In fact, early observations indicate that systemic therapies may upregulate CLDN18.2 expression (medRxiv 2024.10.29.24316248). As cytotoxic chemotherapy can induce immunogenic cell death; coupled with anti–PD-1 therapy, givastomig may potentiate the antitumor immune response in this context. The hypothesis that givastomig may enhance the activity of standard 5FU/oxaliplatin and nivolumab is currently being tested in the ongoing part 2 of this trial and represents the major future direction of givastomig clinical development.

In summary, we provide the initial safety, PK/PD, and efficacy characterization of givastomig, a first-in-class bispecific antibody that targets both CLDN18.2 and 4-1BB. Much of the toxicity associated with 4-1BB activation, e.g., systemic immune response and hepatic toxicity, seems to be greatly reduced by the dual engagement of CLDN18.2 on tumor cells and 4-1BB on immune cells with localized T-cell activation in the TME. The mechanism underlying the reduced GI toxicities of givastomig will require further investigation but may be due to silenced Fc-effector function. This study also shows encouraging activity of monotherapy givastomig in patients with broad CLDN18.2 expression levels, particularly in patients with lower CLDN18.2 expression (those with IHC <75%) who would not be candidates for zolbetuximab. The favorable safety margin together with a dose-dependent PK profile, a PD profile in line with givastomig mechanism of action, and promising monotherapy efficacy support further development of givastomig as a combination with chemotherapy and nivolumab in patients with first-line metastatic HER2-negative GEC (NCT04900818).
