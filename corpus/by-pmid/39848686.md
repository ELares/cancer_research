# PD-1 cis-targeted IL-2v in combination with radiotherapy inhibits lung cancer growth and remodels the immune microenvironment.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: More efficient therapeutic options for non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) are needed as the survival at 5 years of metastatic disease is near zero. In this regard, we used a preclinical model of metastatic lung adenocarcinoma (SV2-OVA) to assess the safety and efficacy of novel radio-immunotherapy combining hypofractionated radiotherapy (HRT) with muPD1-IL2v immunocytokine and muFAP-CD40 bispecific antibody.

**METHODS**: We evaluated the changes in the lung immune microenvironment at multiple timepoints following combination therapies and investigated their underlying antitumor mechanisms. Additionally, we analyzed the tumor clonal heterogeneity upon the combination treatments to explore potential mechanisms associated with the lack of complete response.

**RESULTS**: The combination of HRT with muPD1-IL2v had a potent antitumor effect and increased survival in the SV2-OVA lung cancer model. Importantly, this combination therapy was devoid of measurable toxicity. It induced remodeling of the immune contexture through the increase of CD8+ T and natural killer (NK) cells. The addition of muFAP-CD40 to the combination treatment further increased infiltrating CD8+ T cells, expressing high levels of effector molecules, both in the periphery and core tumor regions. An accumulation of CD8+ PD-1+ TOX+ (exhausted) T cells, already at the 'early' timepoint, is consistent with the limited clinical benefits provided by the various combination treatments in this model. The study of the clonal dynamics of tumor cells during disease progression and therapy highlighted a clonal selection upon HRT+muPD1-IL2v therapy.

**CONCLUSIONS**: We demonstrated that HRT+muPD1-IL2v combination is a potent therapeutic strategy to delay tumor growth and increase survival in a metastatic lung cancer model, but additional studies are required to completely understand the resistance mechanisms associated with the lack of complete response in this model.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have been approved for the treatment of advanced non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), but the proportion of patients benefitting remains low.

Recent clinical research focuses on combining radiotherapy (RT) with immuno-oncology (IO) agents such as ICIs.

The novel programmed cell death protein 1-targeted agent providing interleukin-2 stimulation (PD1-IL2v), was recently shown to be active in combination with RT in pancreatic and head and neck tumors, emphasizing the importance of testing its efficacy in other tumor types in need of therapeutic improvement such as NSCLC.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Here, we used a preclinical model of metastatic lung adenocarcinoma to assess the safety, efficacy and immune pharmacodynamics of the novel IO agent PD1-IL2v in combination with RT, resulting in increased survival and remodeling of the lung immune landscape of treated animals.

The study of tumor clonal composition after this treatment provides clues to elucidate mechanisms of therapy resistance.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Our work highlights a novel potent therapeutic strategy to delay tumor growth and increase survival in NSCLC.

The development of combination treatments, designed specifically to counteract tumor escape mechanisms, appears as a necessary way forward to improve clinical management of NSCLC.

Introduction

 With more than 1.79 million deaths yearly, lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide (International Agency for Research on Cancer, WHO). Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) histological subtypes represent 85% of all cases. Of these, lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) is the most common subtype, accounting for 30% of all cases overall and 40% of all NSCLCs. Despite recent advances in low-dose CT screening, most patients with lung cancer are still diagnosed at advanced stages when treatment options are limited.

The first-line therapy for advanced NSCLC evolved from platinum-based chemotherapy to molecular-targeted therapy and immunotherapy. Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) have improved clinical outcomes of advanced NSCLC and are recommended for the treatment of tumors that are positive for EGFR or ALK oncogenic mutations, yet tumor relapses and acquired resistance are often observed and constitute a common limitation of TKIs. Recently, the clinical benefit of immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) compared with standard chemotherapy in patients with advanced NSCLC led to approval of programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) antibodies. ICB is currently the first-line therapy for advanced oncogenic driver negative NSCLC either alone or in combination with chemotherapy. However, most patients do not respond to ICB and severe immune-related adverse effects are commonly observed. The survival at 5 years of metastatic disease is around 4%, underlying the need for therapeutic improvement.

There is now growing evidence that radiation therapy (RT) targeted to the tumor can enhance priming and effector phases of antitumor immunity, making it a good combinatorial partner with immunotherapeutic agents. Recently, many efforts have been made to investigate the ability of hypofractionated radiation therapy (HRT) to boost the antitumor response when combined with ICB, and some HRT regimens have been described as highly immunogenic. In the context of NSCLC where ICB has an emerging therapeutic role, using HRT to modulate the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) and sensitize it to selected immunotherapies is clinically relevant.

With its ability to stimulate cytotoxic T cells, interleukin-2 (IL-2) has been approved for the treatment of metastatic renal cell carcinoma and melanoma. However, dose-limiting toxicities have limited its widespread application. Another tumor-targeted strategy was recently tested with the development of a PD-1 cis-targeted IL-2v immunocytokine. PD1-IL2v preferentially targets PD-1+ T cells and differentiates them towards ‘better effectors’ resulting in improved efficacy in several tumor models compared with ICB.

The costimulatory receptor CD40 is a member of the TNF receptor family, expressed on the surface of antigen-presenting cells, such as B cells, dendritic cells (DCs) and macrophages. Agonistic CD40 monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) have shown effective preclinical results in different cancer types, including LUAD and have also shown clinical efficacy. Yet, the use of CD40 mAbs in patients is limited due to serious toxicity associated with their systemic administration. A novel fibroblast activation protein (FAP)-targeted CD40 agonist (FAP-CD40) was developed to specifically enhance its local tumor retention and was tested in several murine tumor models. This bispecific FAP-CD40 showed therapeutic efficacy in combination with HRT in a FAP+-head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) model, increasing overall survival and inducing long-term antitumor immunity.

Here, we investigated the safety and efficacy of a new tumor-targeted therapeutic strategy in a metastatic LUAD preclinical model. We used HRT in combination with mouse-specific PD1-IL2v and FAP-CD40 immunomodulatory drugs. We evaluated the changes in the lung TIME by performing immune cell profiling at several timepoints. Finally, we studied the impact of this novel combination treatment on the tumor clonal composition to elucidate mechanisms of therapy resistance.

Methods

Tumor cell lines

The murine SV2 cell line was cultured in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM)+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Gibco). The murine SV2-OVA cell line was cultured in DMEM+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco) and 5µg/mL blasticidin (InvivoGen). The murine SV2-OVA-FAP cell line was generated using a previously described protocol by transfection with the Fap construct in the SV2-OVA cell line. SV2-OVA-FAP cell line was cultured in DMEM+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco), 5µg/mL blasticidin (InvivoGen) and 300µg/mL hygromycin B Gold (InvivoGen). The murine SV2-barcoded cell line was generated by lentiviral transduction of the SV2 cell line with a library of barcodes using CloneTracker XP-rLuc 10M Barcode-3’ Library in pScribe6-rLuc-Puro (BCXP10M3LP-V, Cellecta). SV2 cells were transduced with a gradient of four different viral doses and resistant cells were selected with 2µg/mL puromycin (InvivoGen) treatment. Droplet Digital PCR (Bio-Rad) technology, along with custom assays specific for rLuc coding sequence and Hprt1 gene, was used to select one of the four transduced SV2 cell lines showing single barcode integration per individual cell. SV2-barcoded cell line was cultured in DMEM+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco) and 2µg/mL puromycin (InvivoGen). The mEERL95 cell line was cultured in DMEM/F-12+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 5% heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco) and 1x Human Keratinocyte Growth Supplement (HKGS) (Gibco). All cell lines were cultured at 37°C in a 5% CO2 incubator and routinely tested in house to dismiss mycoplasma infection. Before injections, tumor cells were washed with Dulbecco's phosphate-buffered saline (DPBS) (Gibco) and detached with TrypLE Express Enzyme (Gibco) or with Cell Dissociation Solution Non-Enzymatic (Sigma-Aldrich). SV2-derived cell lines were resuspended in DPBS (Gibco) and mEERL95 cells were resuspended in 60% Hanks' Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS) (Gibco) and 40% Matrigel Matrix (Corning).

Mice

Female and male C57BL/6J mice aged 7–9 weeks were purchased from Janvier Labs and Charles River Laboratories, respectively. CD45.1 OT-1 mice were bred at the animal facility (Epalinges) of the University of Lausanne. The following housing conditions for the mice were used: 12-hour light cycle (07:00 on, 19:00 off), temperature between 19°C and 23°C, relative humidity between 40% and 60% and food and water were provided ad libitum.

Antibodies

For efficacy studies, the following mouse-specific surrogate molecules as previously described were applied: muPD1-IL2v and muFAP-CD40 based on a muIgG1 DAPG backbone without Fc effector functions. The antibodies were produced at the Roche Innovation Center Zurich (RICZ), Switzerland.

Tumor models and in vivo treatments

SV2 and SV2-OVA LUAD

Syngeneic metastatic lung cancer model was used to assess the in vivo efficacy of hypofractionated radiotherapy, muFAP-CD40 and muPD1-IL2v compared with the single agents or their combination in C57BL/6J mice. Tumor growth inhibition, survival rate and immunoPD were the readouts for this model. In brief, male C57BL/6J mice aged 9 weeks were inoculated with 1.5×106 SV2-OVA cells intravenously injected in the tail. Therapy started when evidence of tumor growth was visible in the lungs by μCT imaging using a MILabs instrument as described previously. Mice were randomized, 10 days after tumor inoculation, into different treatment groups, on the basis of tumor load. At day 12 after tumor inoculation, 105 OT-1 T cells were intravenously injected in the tail of all mice following a previously described protocol, as a mean to track antigen-specific CD8+ T cell response. Tumors were irradiated with a hypofractionated regimen consisting of two consecutive doses of 6Gy (2×6Gy) at days 13 and 14 after tumor inoculation. Doses were delivered with an X-Rad225C Precision X-Ray irradiator at a dose rate of 2.8Gy/min using a 20mm collimator that allows to locally irradiate the lungs. All treatments were administered intraperitoneally, and the following doses were investigated: muFAP-CD40 at 13.3mg/kg at days 14 and 18 after tumor inoculation and muPD1-IL2v at 0.5mg/kg every 5 days between days 21 and 41 after tumor inoculation or between days 21 and 62 after tumor inoculation (for continuous muPD1-IL2v study). For the standard-of-care chemotherapy study, cisplatin and pemetrexed were administered intraperitoneally at days 13, 20 and 34 after tumor inoculation, and the following doses were investigated: cisplatin (CAS 15663-27-1, Santa Cruz Biotechnology) at 5.2mg/kg and pemetrexed (CAS 357166-29-1, Sigma) at 12mg/kg. For survival rate curves, the termination criterion to sacrifice animals was either tumor load or body weight loss.

SV2-OVA-FAP LUAD

Subcutaneous syngeneic model was used to assess the in vivo efficacy of muFAP-CD40 in C57BL/6J mice. Tumor growth inhibition and survival rate were the readouts for this model. In brief, male C57BL/6J mice aged 7–8 weeks were inoculated subcutaneously in the flank with 3×106 SV2-OVA cells or with 5×106 SV2-OVA-FAP cells from a SV2-OVA cell line overexpressing the FAP molecule. Tumor volume was measured using a caliper and calculated with the formula: . Mice were randomized, 10 days after tumor inoculation, into the different treatment groups, on the basis of tumor size. Therapy started when tumors reached an average volume of 50mm3. muFAP-CD40 was administered intraperitoneally, and the following dose was investigated: 13.3mg/kg at day 11 after tumor inoculation. For survival rate curves, the termination criterion to sacrifice animals was either tumor size or tumor ulceration.

SV2-barcoded LUAD

Syngeneic metastatic lung cancer model was used to assess the tumor clonal heterogeneity after the different combination treatments in C57BL/6J mice. In brief, male C57BL/6J mice aged 9 weeks were inoculated with 1×106 SV2-barcoded cells intravenously injected in the tail. Tumor load quantification, animal randomization and treatment administration were performed as described for the SV2-OVA LUAD.

For the depletion studies, mice bearing SV2-OVA lung tumors were administered intraperitoneally with anti-CD8β mAb (clone H35-17-2, homemade) and anti-CD161 (clone PK136, homemade) or a Rat IgG2b or a Rat IgG2a used as Ig isotype controls. Depleting antibodies or isotype controls were given at 8mg/kg to SV2-OVA tumor-bearing mice, 2 days before the first administration of therapy and every 3 days after, for 5 weeks. The depletion efficiency of mouse CD8+ and CD161+ cells was evaluated in the blood by flow cytometry.

mEERL95 HNSCC

Orthotopic syngeneic model was used to assess the FAP expression levels compared with SV2-OVA metastatic lung cancer model. In brief, female C57BL/6J mice aged 7–8 weeks were inoculated subcutaneously with 1×105 mEERL95 cells injected in the submental space as described previously. Tumors from untreated animals were sampled at day 17 after tumor inoculation to quantify FAP expression levels.

Histology and immunofluorescence

For immunofluorescence analyses, lung tissue samples were collected in OCT cryo microtomy embedding medium (CellPath). 8µm cryosections were subsequently cut with a cryostat (CryoStar NX70, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and fixed with 2% paraformaldehyde (PFA) (EMS). Following fixation, the slides were washed three times in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (CHUV) and incubated in a 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) (Sigma), 0.1% Triton X-100 (PanReac AppliChem), 1% Donkey Serum (Bio-Rad) and/or 1% Normal Goat Serum (Reactolab) in PBS blocking solution for 1 hour at room temperature. Mouse FAP immunohistochemistry was performed with antimouse FAP (1:250; clone 28H1, RICZ) and antimouse E-cadherin (1:250; clone 24E10, Cell Signaling Technology). Mouse CD8, PD-1 and TOX immunohistochemistry was performed with antimouse CD8α (1:100; clone 53–6.7, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and antimouse TOX (1:100; clone E6G5O, Cell Signaling Technology). The slides were incubated overnight at 4°C in the primary antibody mix described above in blocking solution. After three additional washes in PBS, the slides were incubated 1 hour at room temperature in the secondary antibody mix including donkey antihuman IgG (H+L) DyLight488 (1:250, Invitrogen) for FAP, and donkey antirabbit IgG (H+L) AF647 (1:500, Invitrogen) for E-cadherin staining. For CD8, PD-1 and TOX immunohistochemistry, the slides were incubated 1 hour at room temperature in the secondary antibody mix including donkey antirat IgG (H+L) AF488 (1:500, Invitrogen) for CD8α, and donkey antirabbit IgG (H+L) AF647 (1:500, Invitrogen) for TOX staining. After secondary antibody staining, slides were washed in PBS. For CD8, PD-1 and TOX immunohistochemistry, the slides were stained for 30min at 4°C in PE antimouse PD-1 (1:100; clone 29F.1A12, BioLegend) in blocking solution. The slides were washed in PBS and mounting media was applied directly on the fluorescently labeled tissue sample (ProLong Gold Antifade Mountant with 4′, 6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI), Thermo Fisher Scientific). The slides were scanned using the NanoZoomer S60 Digital slide scanner (Hamamatsu). Visualization and analyses were performed using QuPath software (V.0.2.3). Quantification of FAP+ cells from scan images was performed with Fiji software (V.2.3.0, ImageJ2). For this, two thresholds were set to recognize AF488 and AF647 staining from FAP+ and E-cadherin+ cells, respectively and the algorithm for percentage of positive area was subsequently run. Automated quantification of multiple cell subsets such as CD8+ and CD8+ PD-1+ TOX+ T cells was performed using QuPath software, by running a DAPI-based cell detection, creating single measurement classifiers for each marker and creating a composite classifier including all markers (scripts are available on request). The output data were then transferred to GraphPad Prism software (V.9.4.0) for analysis of significance by unpaired Student’s t-test.

Cell isolation

Lungs and mediastinal lymph nodes (mLN) were harvested at days 17, 24 and 31 after tumor inoculation. Lung tissue was disrupted using manual scissors and digested in 500µl of an enzyme mix (Tumor Dissociation Kit, Miltenyi Biotec). After 30min of digestion at 37°C, the tissue mix was filtered through a 70µm cell strainer (Corning) and resuspended as a single-cell suspension with an appropriate volume for subsequent staining with fluorescently labeled antibodies. mLN were mechanically dissociated with a pestle, filtered through a 70µm cell strainer (Corning) and resuspended as a single-cell suspension with an appropriate volume for subsequent staining with fluorescently labeled antibodies.

For the depletion studies, blood was collected by tail vein blood sampling at day 31 after tumor inoculation. Blood was transferred to heparin tubes, and red blood cells were lysed with RBC Lysis Solution (QIAGEN). After red blood cell lysis, cells were resuspended as a single-cell suspension with an appropriate volume for subsequent staining with fluorescently labeled antibodies.

For the OT-1 T cell transfer at day 12 after tumor inoculation, OT-1 spleens were smashed on a 70µm cell strainer (Corning) and red blood cells were lysed for 5min at room temperature with RBC Lysis Solution (QIAGEN). After red blood cell lysis, cells were resuspended as a single-cell suspension with an appropriate volume for subsequent staining with fluorescently labeled antibodies.

Spatial proteomics of fresh frozen tumor sections

GeoMx DSP data acquisition

We followed published experimental methods (NanoString) with modifications as noted below. Briefly, lung tissue samples were collected in OCT cryo microtomy embedding medium (CellPath). 5µm cryosections were subsequently cut with a cryostat (CryoStar NX70, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and fixed with 10% Neutral Buffered Formalin (Sigma). Following fixation, the slides were washed in 1x Tris Buffered Saline-TWEEN (TBS-T) (Sigma), and antigen retrieval was performed. The slides were incubated in blocking buffer W (NanoString) for 1 hour at room temperature, followed by 4°C overnight incubation in a mix containing UV-photocleavable oligonucleotide-conjugated antibodies (53 protein targets; Mouse IO Protein Panels, NanoString) and fluorescently labeled morphological antibodies including anti-panCK (5.9µg/mL; clone AE-1/AE-3, Novus Biologicals), AF488 anti-CD3 (25µg/mL; clone CD3-12, Bio-Rad), AF647 anti-CD8α (10µg/mL; clone C8/144B, BioLegend) and SYTO 83 Orange Fluorescent Nucleic Acid Stain (500nM, Thermo Fisher Scientific) in blocking buffer W (NanoString). These four morphology markers allowed delineation of the epithelial, T lymphocytes, cytotoxic T lymphocytes and nuclear compartments, respectively. Immunofluorescence images, region of interest (ROI) selection, segmentation into marker-specific areas of illumination (AOI) and spatially indexed oligo cleavage and collection were performed on a GeoMx DSP instrument (NanoString). Approximately 9 ROIs and 18 AOIs were collected per treatment group. Protein profiling was performed with the nCounter platform (NanoString).

GeoMx DSP data analysis

The following mouse protein panels from NanoString were used: immune cell profiling (V.1.0), cell death (V.1.1), pan-tumor (V.1.0), IO drug target (V.1.0), immune activation status (V.1.0), immune cell typing (V.1.0) and PI3K/AKT signaling panels (V.1.0). Preprocessing and normalization of protein readout were performed on the GeoMx DSP instrument. Normalized expression data of endogenous proteins was imported into R software (V.4.2.0). Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed using the PCA function of the FactoMineR package (V.2.4,). PCA was performed either using all ROIs, or subsets of ROIs by selecting groups of treatments and/or timepoints. The contribution of each protein to PC1 and PC2 was determined using the get_pca_var function of the factoextra package (V.1.0.7). Similarly to PCA, heatmaps of ROIs were generated from subsets of treatments and/or timepoints. For each subset of ROIs, proteins that had a coefficient of variation (CV, ratio of the SD to the mean) above the median CV of all proteins were selected. The z-scores of variable protein expression across ROIs were visualized using the pheatmap package (V.1.0.12).

Tumor cell heterogeneity assessed by barcoding of the SV2 tumor cell line

DNA extraction and CloneTracker barcode retrieval

Lung nodules, sampled between days 51 and 86 after tumor cell inoculation, were collected in DMEM (Gibco) and cut in two. When nodules were big enough, one-half was mechanically dissociated with a pestle, filtered through a 70µm cell strainer (Corning) and put back in culture in DMEM+GlutaMAX (Gibco) supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS (Gibco), 2µg/mL puromycin (InvivoGen) and 1µg/mL amphotericin B (Thermo Fisher Scientific) to generate explant cell lines. The other half was stored in ATL Buffer (QIAamp DNA Mini Kit, QIAGEN) at −20°C. At the end of the survival experiment, the genomic DNA (gDNA) from all collected nodules was extracted using QIAamp DNA Mini Kit (QIAGEN) and was eluted in buffer AE (QIAGEN) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Starting with 200ng of the gDNA template, barcodes were retrieved by a two-step PCR using the Q5 High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase (New England Biolabs). In the first PCR step, Illumina adaptor sequences were added to the barcodes by 25 PCR cycles (5min 95°C, 25× (30s 95°C, 30s 65°C, 30s 72°C), 3min 72°C) with custom primers. The PCR products were purified with AMPure XP beads (Beckman Coulter) using a one-step protocol with a bead ratio of 1.5×, and 15pg of the purified DNA was subjected to additional 15 PCR cycles (5min 95°C, 15× (30s 95°C, 30s 65°C, 30s 72°C), 3min 72°C) using NEBNext Multiplex Oligos for Illumina (96 Unique Dual Index Primer Pairs (New England Biolabs)) to allow multiplexing of samples. The indexed amplicons were purified by AMPure XP beads (Beckman Coulter) using a two-step protocol with bead ratios of 0.6x and 1x successively. Library quality and fragment sizes were assessed on a Fragment Analyzer (Advanced Analytical) before high-throughput sequencing on an Illumina MiSeq system at the Lausanne Genomic Technologies Facility.

Bar-Seq data preprocessing and analysis

A custom R script was developed for processing barcode sequencing FASTQ files. Low-quality reads with an average Phred base quality score below 20 were filtered out, based on the 1–90bp range where the barcode is situated. Subsequently, the precise locations of the barcode flanking sequences (5’-ACCGAACGCAACGCACGCA-3’ and 5’-TGGTGCAGCTGGAGC-3’) were ascertained, and the intervening barcode sequences were extracted. To rectify potential sequencing errors within the extracted barcode sequences and to group them into distinct clusters, Starcode was used with defined parameters (-d 6 -s). Furthermore, to eliminate barcode clusters of inferior quality, representative sequences from each cluster were mapped using BLASTN with defined parameters (-word_size 6 -outfmt 6 -evalue 1E0 -max_hsps 1 -max_target_seqs 5 -num_threads 8) against the barcode reference supplied by Cellecta. The resultant mapped barcode clusters were then stored, and the barcodes from each sample were integrated to construct the barcode-sample table. Using the Bar-Seq data preprocessing pipeline, a sample of the SV2-barcoded population revealed 26 013 distinct barcodes, with 77% of the barcode from lung tumor samples overlapping with those in the original barcoded cell line. For the comparative analysis of barcode relative abundance among samples from distinct treatment groups, a dot plot was generated using the ggplot2 package (V.3.4.2) to depict the percentage distribution of the top five abundant barcodes in each sample.

Statistical analysis

Statistical significance was evaluated using GraphPad Prism software (V.9.4.0) with the statistical test indicated. Unpaired t-test was used for normally distributed data, and the Mann-Whitney U test or Wilcoxon test was used for non-normally distributed data. P values of or below 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Significance levels are indicated as follows: ns, not significant (p>0.05) or written entirely if p<0.05. Individual treatment groups were compared with the untreated group or with the group indicated.

Results

The combination of hypofractionated radiotherapy with PD1-IL2v immunocytokine is superior to single modalities in treating SV2-OVA lung tumors

To assess the therapeutic potential of HRT+PD1-IL2v combination therapy, we used the well-established murine SV2 metastatic LUAD model. First, we investigated the antitumor efficacy and safety of HRT (delivered in two fractions of 6Gy) and PD1-IL2v alone or combined according to the scheme shown in figure 1a. Both HRT and PD1-IL2v used in monotherapy delayed tumor growth by 10 days (figure 1b) and increased mouse survival by 16 and 22 days, respectively, compared with the untreated group (figure 1c). Interestingly, the combination of HRT+PD1-IL2v induced an enhanced tumor growth delay and further increased mouse survival by 10 and 4 days, respectively, compared with single agents (figure 1b and c). We previously showed that FAP-CD40 bispecific antibody demonstrated potent antitumor activity by boosting the CD8+ T cell-cDC1 axis in an aggressive head and neck tumor model, so here, we tested if its addition to the HRT+PD1-IL2v combination could potentiate the magnitude of the antitumor efficacy already observed. We administered FAP-CD40 sequentially after the HRT and before PD1-IL2v, to provide upstream co-stimulation (figure 1a). Neither the tumor growth delay (figure 1d) nor the mouse survival (figure 1e) was increased upon addition of FAP-CD40.

The combination of HRT with PD1-IL2v remodels the lung immune microenvironment by increasing CD8+ T and NK cells and the addition of FAP-CD40 further increases effector cells

We next explored the cellular immune components associated with the antitumor effect of the combinations. We first analyzed the lung immune infiltrate 3 days following HRT (day 17), and 3 and 5 days following PD1-IL2v (days 24 and 31, respectively) by using multispectral flow cytometry (figure 2a). At day 24, we observed major differences in the CD8+ T cell compartment between the treatment groups. Both PD1-IL2v monotherapy and the HRT added to PD1-IL2v led to increased CD8+ T cell frequency among total T cells by 10.5% and 9%, respectively, compared with the untreated group (figure 2b). Upon the addition of FAP-CD40 to the combination, the CD8+ T cell frequency was further enhanced by 18% and 19.5%, compared with PD1-IL2v monotherapy and HRT+PD1-IL2v, respectively. This increase was sustained at day 31, as shown in figure 2b. The frequency of CD4+ T cells among total T cells was decreased in all the treatment groups at days 24 and 31 (figure 2c). In addition, PD1-IL2v administration led to a similar range expansion of the NK cell compartment in all the treatment groups at day 24 (figure 2d). Upon addition of FAP-CD40 to the combination of HRT+PD1-IL2v, no increase of DC was observed in the lungs (figure 2a). Of note, we assessed the impact of FAP-CD40 administration on the lung immune microenvironment and observed that DCs were significantly more increased after two doses versus one dose of FAP-CD40 (online supplemental figure S1a). However, no DC activation marker upregulation was observed, except for PD-L1 (online supplemental figure S1b-e). When we analyzed the mLN immune cell composition using the same flow cytometry panel (figure 2e), a significant increase in CD8+ T cells was only observed upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v compared with the untreated group (figure 2f). CD4+ T cells in the mLN were decreased in all the treatment groups at days 24 and 31 (figure 2g). At day 24, all the treatment groups showed an increased proportion of DCs as compared with controls (figure 2h). We then examined the T cell phenotypic markers represented in the lung CD8+ T cell population. PD1-IL2v led to a notable increase in the frequency of effector CD8+ T cells (CD44+ CD62L−) infiltrating the lungs, which was even more pronounced upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v (figure 2i). In the mLNs, all treatments increased the frequency of central memory CD8+ T cells (CD44+ CD62L+) similarly (not shown). Considering the lung immune landscape remodeling with massive increase in CD8+ T and NK cell compartments, and to further validate whether these cells are required for the tumor control upon PD1-IL2v, we performed a depletion experiment. The antitumor efficacy observed with PD1-IL2v was lost upon CD8+ T cell depletion but maintained upon NK cell depletion, suggesting that PD1-IL2v therapeutic benefit critically depends on CD8+ T cells, but not NK cells (figure 2j). To spatially quantify the CD8+ T cell subsets upon the combination treatments, we performed immunofluorescence staining on the different groups for the ‘early’ (days 17–24) and ‘late’ (days 31–48) timepoints, using anti-CD8, anti-PD-1 and anti-TOX antibodies (online supplemental figure S2a). Immunofluorescence staining and quantification confirmed the data obtained by flow cytometry (figure 2a and b), as the net number of CD8+ T cells was increased in the tumor at the ‘early’ timepoint upon PD1-IL2v, HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v compared with the untreated group. At the ‘late’ timepoint, the numbers of CD8+ T cells remained high upon PD1-IL2v, HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v compared with the untreated group, with the highest CD8+ T cell increase observed in the response to HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v. The addition of HRT to PD1-IL2v did not change the CD8+ T cell counts, neither at the ‘early’ nor at the ‘late’ timepoints (online supplemental figure S2b). In the healthy lung region, HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v treatment led to increased CD8+ T cell counts compared with HRT+PD1-IL2v treatment at the ‘early’ timepoint. At the ‘late’ timepoint, HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v treatment led to increased CD8+ T cell counts compared with PD1-IL2v and HRT+PD1-IL2v treatments (online supplemental figure S2c). To track tumor-specific CD8+ T cell, we assessed the frequency of transferred OT-1xCD45.1+ cells in the lungs, mLNs and spleen of mice. In the lungs, we observed an increased frequency of CD45.1+ cells upon HRT+PD1-IL2v at day 24 and upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v at day 31, compared with the untreated group (online supplemental figure S2d). In the mLNs, CD45.1+ cell frequency was only increased upon PD1-IL2v at day 24, compared with the untreated group (online supplemental figure S2e). In the spleen, CD45.1+ cell frequency was increased upon all treatments except HRT, compared with the untreated group (online supplemental figure S2f). However, we could only recover a small fraction of transferred OT-1xCD45.1+ cells compared with endogenous CD8+ T cells, suggesting that the antitumor effect, in this model, was mainly driven by endogenous CD8+ T cells. Even though HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v combination therapies induced significant antitumor activity as well as cytotoxic cell infiltration in the lungs of treated mice, no complete response was achieved so far. Therefore, we pursued our investigations by studying the molecular components associated with both combination treatments.

The therapeutic benefit of HRT with PD1-IL2v and FAP-CD40 is associated with a functional CD8+ T cell signature both in the periphery and core tumor regions

To examine the functional profile of the CD8+ T cells upon the different treatments in the lungs, we measured the spatially resolved expression of 53 proteins included in several panels from NanoString, namely the immune cell profiling, cell death, pan-tumor, immuno-oncology (IO) drug target, immune activation status, immune cell typing and PI3K/AKT signaling panels. To capture the impact of the different treatment modalities, we sampled the lungs at days 17, 24 and 31 after tumor cell inoculation to assess TIME at days 4, 11 and 18 after treatment initiation. We defined ROIs in the lung tissue and used a segmentation mask to discriminate peripheral tumor versus tumor areas (figure 3a). We compared the differences in protein expression in HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v groups normalized based on expression levels found upon PD1-IL2v single agent, in the tumor periphery and tumor areas (figure 3b). We observed that many adaptive markers (CD8, CD4) and activation markers (VISTA, TIM-3) were shared across both groups in the tumor, suggesting similar activation pattern/remodeling of the intratumoral compartment and consistent with the similar survival results obtained in these two groups. We also found that both CD8 and PD-1 markers were upregulated in the tumor periphery only upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, confirming the high frequency of CD8+ T cells observed in this group by flow cytometry. Interestingly, the DC markers CD40 and MHC-II were also only upregulated in the tumor upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, suggesting a potential activation of DCs through FAP-CD40 antibody, that was not detected previously by flow cytometry in the total lungs. Additionally, we observed an upregulation of the vasculature signature (CD34, CD31) in the tumor tissue from both combination treatments (figure 3b). With an additional analysis of this experiment showing the most variable proteins across days and regions upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, we observed overexpression of Granzyme B and perforin effector molecules at day 24 (figure 3c), confirming that CD8+ T cells displayed a functional phenotype upon the combination therapy, in agreement with the flow cytometry profile (figure 3d). These results underline the requirement for CD8+ T cells in the HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v treatment antitumor efficacy. However, and strikingly at day 31, that functional profile is reverted (figure 3c and d), which may explain the limited tumor control achieved so far. To better understand this decline of functionality between the ‘early’ (day 24) and ‘late’ (day 31) timepoints, we investigated whether the CD8+ T cells presented an exhausted phenotype, as defined by co-expression of PD-1 and TOX markers (online supplemental figure S2a). Figure 3e shows that the numbers of CD8+ PD-1+ TOX+ T cells were increased in the tumor upon PD1-IL2v, HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v compared with the untreated group, and this was already the case at the ‘early’ timepoint. Similarly, CD8+ PD-1+ TOX+ T cells were increased in the healthy lung region upon PD1-IL2v, HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v compared with the untreated group, both at the ‘early’ and ‘late’ timepoints (figure 3f). In this context, we asked whether continuation of PD1-IL2v could sustain the functional effector profile. We administered PD1-IL2v at day 21 after tumor inoculation and every 5 days over the time course of the experiment. We observed that the continuation of PD1-IL2v treatment did not detectably increase the survival of mice (figure 3g). In view of the high expansion as well as impressive functional profile that CD8+ T cells exhibited upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, and regarding the upregulation of DC activation markers observed in the tumors of this group compared with HRT+PD1-IL2v, we postulated that the lack of FAP-CD40 additive effect in terms of tumor growth delay and survival could be due to heterogenous or too weak FAP expression levels in the SV2-OVA lung tumors to efficiently crosslink the bispecific antibody resulting in the activation of CD40+ cells.

The therapeutic potential of FAP-CD40 in the SV2-OVA lung tumors is dependent on endogenous FAP expression levels

FAP is expressed by fibroblasts in the microenvironment of several human solid tumors including lung cancer. To determine the FAP expression pattern in SV2-OVA lung nodules, we proceeded to immunofluorescence staining. We used mEERL95 head and neck tumors, which display high stromal FAP expression, as positive controls (figure 4a). We quantified the FAP-positive area per tumor and found that SV2-OVA tumors express an order of magnitude lower levels of FAP compared with mEERL95 tumors (figure 4b).

To determine whether those levels of FAP in the lung model matter in terms of FAP-CD40 activity, we transfected the SV2-OVA cell line with murine Fap gene to generate SV2-OVA-FAP cell line with recombinant FAP expression (figure 4c and d). In contrast to all other experiments where we used metastatic lung cancer model, here we inoculated SV2-OVA-FAP cells subcutaneously, as the immunogenicity of this cell line prevented metastatic tumor growth. Using subcutaneous SV2-OVA-FAP tumors as a proof of concept, we compared FAP-CD40 efficacy in tumors expressing endogenous FAP levels and in tumors overexpressing FAP (figure 4e). We observed that FAP-CD40 efficacy dropped from FAP overexpression to endogenous lower FAP levels, with 83% complete responses obtained in FAP overexpressing tumors vs 50% in SV2-OVA mock tumors (figure 4f and g), suggesting that higher FAP expression levels increase FAP-CD40 activity in the SV2-OVA tumors.

The combination of HRT with PD1-IL2v modifies tumor clonal heterogeneity by clonal selection

To better understand the mechanisms underlying the loss of therapeutic tumor control and the lack of complete response upon HRT+PD1-IL2v and HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, we used a cellular barcoding technology similar to that described by Roh et al, to dissect tumor heterogeneity under different therapeutic settings. The SV2 cell line was transduced with a library of barcodes to determine the clonal composition of tumors (figure 5a). We engrafted the barcoded cell line in mice and administered the combination treatments according to the scheme in figure 5b. We found that a small number of clones successfully establish tumors in the lungs (figure 5c). Moreover, distinct clones seem to be selected upon treatment, particularly upon HRT+PD1-IL2v, as shown in figure 5c. This novel approach opens the possibility to understand tumor intrinsic mechanisms of treatment resistance by combining NGS at the single cell level with the barcoding technology.

Cisplatin and pemetrexed doublet chemotherapy synergizes with the combination of HRT with PD1-IL2v and FAP-CD40 and increases survival in the SV2-OVA metastatic lung cancer model

Finally, to get closer to a clinical translation, we investigated whether HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v therapy, which appears to induce the most favorable TIME, could combine efficiently with the current standard-of-care for advanced metastatic NSCLC, the doublet chemotherapy cisplatin and pemetrexed. We administered three doses of a mix cisplatin/pemetrexed to mice receiving concomitant HRT, in addition to FAP-CD40 and PD1-IL2v (figure 6a). As shown in figure 6b, this new combination therapy showed a trend in delaying the tumor growth as compared with HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v. We observed in the survival an extension of 40 days as compared with the control and an extension of 20 days as compared with the HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v group (figure 6c). However, as expected, the addition of chemotherapy was toxic for the mice as shown by the body weight loss recording (figure 6d).

Discussion

Our study, focusing on developing an efficient radio-immunotherapy combination strategy for advanced LUAD, revealed that association of radiotherapy to the PD1-IL2v immunocytokine has a potent antitumor effect in the lung tumor model. From a clinical perspective, this HRT+PD1-IL2v combination adds to the therapeutic benefit of standard-of-care chemotherapy doublets.

Here, we report that PD1-IL2v in combination with HRT demonstrated a higher antitumor efficacy as compared with PD1-IL2v monotherapy. This is in accordance with recently published data from other groups, showing that PD1-IL2v, but not anti-PD-1, is active in combination with radiotherapy or ICB agents in pancreatic and head and neck tumors. High-dose IL-2 (aldesleukin) monotherapy has shown efficacy in metastatic renal cell carcinoma and melanoma, by stimulating T and NK cell antitumor activity, as well as in combination with ICB. However, its high toxicity rates, poor pharmacokinetics and pleiotropic effects represent major limitations for its clinical use. To bypass these limitations, several engineered IL-2 agents have been developed, including PD1-IL2v. By inducing cis-engagement of the IL-2Rβγ, this novel immunocytokine selectively activates T effector cells over regulatory T cells.

Different IL-2v targeted strategies such as CEA-specific antibody (CEA-IL2v) or FAP-IL2v potentiate the efficacy of different T and NK cell-based immunotherapies. In particular, FAP-IL2v strongly enhanced the activity of an agonistic CD40 antibody and ICB in a Panc02 model. However, recent data highlighted the different mechanisms of action of cis-IL2v as opposed to trans-IL2v stimulation, showing that cis-targeting induces more potent effector functions. Whether IL-2v is targeted at the tumor or not, it is able to potentiate the effect of other immunostimulatory agents, in line with our immuno-pharmacodynamic data obtained in the second result section showing an increase of CD8+ T cells and NK cells in the lungs. By binding to PD-1+ cells, PD1-IL2v acts as an immune checkpoint inhibitor, blocking PD-1/PD-L1 interaction. The affinity of the antibody region for PD-1 leads to preferential localization of PD1-IL2v at PD1+ cells, providing a more potent IL2v signal on PD-1 expressing cells. The exposure to stimulatory factors such as IL-2 enhances NK cell activation. Although it is known that NK cells may also express PD-1 receptor, a recent study showed that PD-1 expression level is minimal in mouse and human NK cells as compared with CD8+ T cells under diverse conditions including tumors. PD1-IL2v retains affinity for muIL-2Rβγ and therefore is still able to act on PD-1-negative NK and T cells. However, PD1-IL2v activity occurs at a 40-fold to 50-fold lower concentration on PD-1+ T cells. The treatment efficacy of HRT+PD1-IL2v relied in part on CD8+ T cells, with remarkable increase of effector cells upon the combination therapy. The expansion of T precursor exhausted (Tpex) cells by exploiting PD-1 expression was described in both chronic infection and cancer models in which PD1-IL2v preferentially targets antigen-specific stem-like PD-1+ TCF-1+ CD8+ T cells and differentiates them toward a novel population of ‘better effectors.’ The addition of FAP-CD40 to the HRT+PD1-IL2v combination further enhanced CD8+ effector T cells in the total lungs and led to an increased CD8+ T cell infiltration in the tumor and healthy lung regions as compared with HRT+PD1-IL2v, both at the ‘early’ and ‘late’ timepoints. However, the substantial increase of effector molecules perforin and Granzyme B observed upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v combination at the ‘early’ timepoint day 24 was not sustained at day 31. Indeed, more than the half of the T cells in the tumor were already expressing TOX at the ‘early’ timepoint and were also positive for this marker at the ‘late’ timepoint, suggesting exhaustion and reduced functionality prone to recurrence. This is consistent with the data reported in the DSP experiment, where we observed an upregulation of TIM-3 marker upon the combination treatments, suggesting the presence of exhausted T cells. This partially explains the limited clinical benefits provided by the various treatment regimens used in this study and underlines the need of a better mechanistic understanding of the causes leading to this late, poorly functional state.

The organization of cells in the tumor microenvironment (TME) into tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS) is a common feature of several cancer types and is associated with good prognosis. The density of mature DCs in intratumoral lymphoid structures is a predictor of clinical outcome in NSCLC. However, in this model, TLS were not detectable, probably due to the rapid tumor growth kinetics. Moreover, the cDC1 subtype was identified as key player in the antitumor mechanisms in mEERL95 tumors treated with HRT+FAP-CD40, through the CD40 agonism signal at the tumor site. Yet, in this lung study, no increase of the DC subtype was observed upon HRT+PD1-IL2v or HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v. A promising way to boost cDC1-mediated cross-presentation is to administer Flt3L, a growth factor promoting DC development. It was recently shown that the use of Flt3L in combination with anti-CD40 inhibited the growth of high tumor-mutational burden lung tumors, suggesting that Flt3L could efficiently combine with FAP-CD40 in our lung tumor settings.

We and others showed that FAP-mediated cross-linking at the tumor stroma is essential for the therapeutic activity of FAP-CD40 bispecific antibody. Along this line, our study in SV2-OVA lung tumors also suggests that the efficacy of FAP-CD40 is dependent on FAP expression levels at the tumor site, as subcutaneous SV2-OVA tumors overexpressing FAP were more efficiently cured compared with SV2-OVA tumors with endogenous levels of FAP. In the metastatic lung cancer model, the lack of FAP-CD40 efficacy is due to its low crosslinking in the TME. This hypothesis could be further investigated by monitoring and quantifying a fluorescently labeled FAP-CD40 antibody, as a mean to track the effective level of FAP-CD40 crosslinking in the nodules. Although human lung tumors are generally associated with high FAP expression, these results warrant establishing which levels of FAP are needed to elicit an efficient antitumor response.

Even though the HRT+PD1-IL2v combination treatment greatly enhanced mouse survival and participated in TME remodeling, this was not sufficient to obtain complete tumor eradication. Hence, we need to better understand the mechanisms associated with treatment resistance. In our tumor clonal composition study, certain clones were represented in almost all mice and treatment conditions, providing a new powerful tool to study aggressive lung cancer molecular features. This tool also allowed us to point out some clones enriched upon HRT+PD1-IL2v. These clones may be interesting to study further to identify features associated with treatment resistance, allowing to design new combination treatments that are specifically targeting the disrupted pathways. Recently, the TRACERx Consortium revealed that NSCLC intratumor heterogeneity mediated through chromosome instability was associated with an increased risk of recurrence or death, raising the need to further study tumor clonal heterogeneity and subclonal selection in NSCLC. Moreover, the monitoring of circulating tumor DNA to detect and profile residual tumor cells after therapy should provide insights into mechanisms of metastatic dissemination in patients with NSCLC.

To get closer to the standard clinical settings, we also tested the pemetrexed and cisplatin chemotherapy addition to our combination on the treatment efficacy. Strikingly, we observed the highest mouse survival obtained so far in this model upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v+chemotherapy. Platinum compounds including cisplatin, carboplatin and oxaliplatin are commonly used for cancer treatment. Of those, oxaliplatin was shown to induce robust immunogenic cell death (ICD) in tumor tissues and to suppress tumor growth by activating DCs and enhancing T cell infiltration in a lung tumor model. On the other hand, the platinum-based drugs are thought to be immunosuppressive by interfering with cell division. It is therefore important to understand the timing and sequencing of the immune-chemotherapy approach and compare its safety and efficacy with radio-immunotherapy and combined chemo-radio-immunotherapy approaches. Detailed immune pharmacodynamics should provide clues as to the best combinations and their eventual sequencing but were beyond the scope of this study. Indeed, the cytotoxic therapy was only tested here to see if complete responses could be achieved in this very aggressive lung tumor model. Moreover, it remains unknown whether some therapeutics might boost T cell responses in the context of chemotherapy.

Finally, it was shown that a distinct state of tumor-reactive lymphocytes with high PD-1 and TIM-3 expression, predicted response to ICB and correlated with increased overall survival in patients with NSCLC. Here, we clearly see an increase of these two markers upon HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v, suggesting that this combination could benefit from the addition of ICB. Other targeted-costimulatory receptor agonists/ICB might be needed in this model to achieve the full potential of PD1-IL2v. For instance, it was demonstrated that T cell immunoreceptor with Ig and ITIM domains (TIGIT) blockade promotes NK cell-dependent tumor immunity as well as tumor-specific T cell immunity and enhanced therapy with PD-L1 blockade in several tumor-bearing mouse models, suggesting that TIGIT may be a good target in combination therapies such as the one described here. Another aspect to consider is the impact of non-immune cells represented in the TME such as cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs). For instance, it was shown in human lung cancer that two distinct CAF populations promote T cell exclusion. Thus, in view of designing potent combination treatments and as a perspective in the study of HRT+PD1-IL2v combination, it is crucial to investigate further the complex immunosuppressive populations in the TME.

In conclusion, we demonstrated that HRT+PD1-IL2v combination is a potent therapeutic strategy to delay tumor growth and increase survival in a lung tumor model. It would be interesting to address the impact of radioimmunotherapy by modeling equivalent radiation doses of standard fractionated radiotherapy in future studies. The combination treatment was associated with an increase of CD8+ effector T and NK cell numbers in the lungs. The addition of FAP-CD40 to the combination treatment further enhanced effector cells and was associated with a functional CD8+ T cell signature both in the periphery and core tumor regions only at the ‘early’ timepoint. An accumulation of exhausted T cells, already at the ‘early’ timepoint, partially explains the limited clinical benefits obtained so far in this model. We also showed that FAP-CD40 efficacy is dependent on endogenous FAP expression levels. Additional studies are required to completely understand the resistance mechanisms associated with the lack of complete response in this model. Finally, and promisingly, we showed that HRT+FAP-CD40+PD1-IL2v combines well with the standard-of-care for advanced stages of NSCLC.
