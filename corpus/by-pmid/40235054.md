# Liquid metal nanoparticles for enhanced delivery of benzoporphyrin derivative in photodynamic cancer therapy.

## Abstract

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is a targeted cancer treatment offering precise tumor ablation with minimal systemic toxicity. However, its clinical application is constrained by poor solubility, rapid clearance, and inadequate tumor accumulation of photosensitizers (PS). This study presents an innovative liquid metal nanoparticle (LMNP) platform, composed of gallium-indium eutectic alloy (EGaIn), engineered to address these drug delivery challenges in PDT. Using a one-step sonication process, EGaIn nanoparticles are synthesized and functionalized with folic acid (FA) for tumor-specific targeting, beta cyclodextrin (β-CD) for enhanced drug encapsulation, and benzoporphyrin derivative (BPD) as a PS. The inclusion of β-CD significantly improves the BPD loading capacity, achieving a three-fold enhancement (52% vs. 18%) while ensuring nanoparticle stability and sustained drug release. Covalent binding of FA and β-CD to the gallium oxide surface enables effective targeting and biocompatibility. In vitro analyses demonstrate potent PDT efficacy, even with reduced cellular uptake, underscoring the platform's ability to overcome intracellular delivery barriers. This LMNP-based nanoplatform addresses critical PDT limitations, such as suboptimal drug delivery and systemic toxicity, leveraging the unique chemical and physical properties of EGaIn nanoparticles. Its multifunctional design integrates targeted delivery, controlled release, and precise therapeutic activation, representing a promising advancement in the development of effective, personalized cancer treatment strategies.

INTRODUCTION

In recent years, photodynamic therapy (PDT) has emerged as a promising alternative to traditional cancer treatments. PDT uses light‐activated photosensitizers to induce localized cytotoxicity in cancer cells, offering precise spatiotemporal control over tumor destruction while minimizing systemic toxicity. This advantage over conventional chemotherapy and radiation therapies makes PDT an attractive option. The process involves administering a photosensitizer that preferentially accumulates in tumor cells, followed by irradiation with light of an appropriate wavelength. This activation generates reactive oxygen species, causing cellular damage and tumor cell death. Despite its potential, the clinical efficacy of PDT is hindered by several challenges, such as poor solubility of photosensitizers in aqueous environments, rapid clearance from the bloodstream, and insufficient accumulation in tumor tissues. These limitations have driven the development of nanoplatforms to enhance drug delivery, achieve targeted release, and minimize off‐target effects in cancer therapy.

Nanotechnology, particularly nanoparticle‐based drug delivery systems, has revolutionized cancer therapy by improving the efficacy of treatments like PDT. The small size of nanoparticles (typically 10–200 nm) allows them to penetrate biological barriers and accumulate in tumors via the enhanced permeability and retention effect. Additionally, nanoparticles can be engineered to encapsulate multiple therapeutic agents, protect them from degradation, and enable controlled release at the target site. While liposomes and gold nanoparticles are widely studied for the purposes of drug delivery, liposomes suffer from low drug loading capacity and rapid clearance, while gold nanoparticles raise concerns regarding long‐term biocompatibility and biodegradability. These drawbacks highlight the need for alternative materials that combine the advantages of organic and inorganic nanoparticles while overcoming their limitations.

Liquid metal nanoparticles, particularly the eutectic alloy of gallium and indium (EGaIn, 75% Ga, 25% In, by weight), have garnered attention due to their unique physical and chemical properties and ease of preparation. EGaIn has a low melting point (15.3°C) and forms a stable, passivating gallium oxide (Ga2O3) layer upon exposure to oxidizing agents. This oxide layer can also degrade in acidic environments, such as those found in tumor tissues, releasing the encapsulated drugs. More importantly, EGaIn nanoparticle surface chemistry is highly versatile as the gallium oxide layer can be chemically and mechanically stabilized using single or multiple ligands, enabling functionalization with targeting ligands, therapeutic molecules, or imaging agents. 

In our previous study, we developed a fabrication methodology for EGaIn nanoparticles, EGaPs, which, when coated with capping agents such as hyaluronic acid (HA) and benzoporphyrin derivative (BPD), can be used for targeted drug delivery of pancreatic cancer treatment via PDT. This work also demonstrated that molecules with carboxylic acid anchoring groups effectively bind to the nanoparticle surface. Carboxylic acid groups react with the metal oxide surface, forming chemisorbed carboxylate species, which enable covalent attachment of both targeting ligands and anticancer drugs onto the EGaIn nanoparticle surface. This EGaIn‐based nanoplatform can be easily adapted for drug delivery in other cancer treatments by replacing HA with other targeting ligands.

This study leverages this unique property of EGaIn NPs and focuses on developing a novel nanoplatform to improve PS delivery with EGaIn NPs that are also functionalized with folic acid (FA) as the targeting ligand. FA targets folate receptors over‐expressed in breast and pancreatic cancer cells. FA binds to the gallium oxide surface via carboxylic acid (COOH) groups, forming strong carboxylate bonds and enhancing the stability of the nanoparticles. FA, with its tail groups, may cause steric hindrance on the nanoparticle surface due to van der Waals forces between adjacent ligands, which helps to control nanoparticle size distribution. The fabrication method, described in our previous study (a one‐pot sonication method), can be readily modified. Since FA contains two carboxylic acid (COOH) groups, FA‐coated nanoparticles can be successfully synthesized via sonication, where the passivating oxide layer reacts with FA to form a targeted drug delivery system for breast and pancreatic cancer PDT treatment.

While surface‐modified liquid metal nanoparticles typically exhibit moderate drug loading efficiency (17–20%), this study aims to enhance it by incorporating cyclodextrin (CD). CD, a water‐soluble, non‐toxic molecule, encapsulates drug molecules of certain sizes, improving drug loading capacity. Similar to FA, CD ligands will covalently bind to the EGaIn nanoparticle surface through carboxylation of the oxide layer. Succinyl‐β‐cyclodextrin (β‐CD), which contains seven carboxylic acid groups, reacts readily with the gallium oxide layer, further stabilizing the nanoparticles and enhancing drug entrapment. Furthermore, BPD, a hydrophobic photosensitizer, can be encapsulated within β‐CD via hydrophobic interactions. This approach ensures an adequate concentration of BPD reaches the target cancer cells.

The synthesized EGaIn nanoparticles, coated with β‐CD and FA and loaded with BPD, are characterized, and physicochemical properties, in vitro biocompatibility, drug delivery kinetics, and PDT efficacy in monolayer cancer models that have overexpression of folate receptors were evaluated. This work represents a novel integration of liquid metal nanoparticles with targeted PDT, addressing the limitation of low drug delivery efficiency in PDT. By incorporating cyclodextrin as an encapsulating agent, this study improves the drug loading efficiency, enabling controlled, slow‐release delivery to the tumor environment. Following this, the encapsulated BPD can be released to aggregate at the cancer site, where it will be activated by PDT to destroy cancer cells. This targeted approach, leveraging functionalized metal nanoparticles, enhances therapeutic efficacy and offers promising insights for the future of cancer treatment.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Materials

All of the chemicals, including gallium−indium eutectic (≥99.99% trace metal basis), benzoporphyrin derivative monoacid ring A (BPD; ≥94% purity), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO), folic acid (FA; ≥97%), sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3), phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS), and succinyl‐β‐cyclodextrin (CD; ≥97% purity), were purchased from Sigma‐Aldrich. Other materials, such as muscovite mica V‐2 (50‐949‐111), conical polypropylene centrifuge tubes (05‐539‐9LC), and disposable cuvettes (cuvette SQ 4 side 4 mL RPK), were purchased from Thermo Fisher Scientific. DMEM (Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium) was purchased from Corning, and FBS (Fetal Bovine Serum) was purchased from Gibco. Deionized (DI) water with a resistivity of 18.2 MΩ·cm was used throughout the experiments.

Synthesis of EGaIn‐CD‐FA (ECDF) and EGaIn‐CD‐FA‐BPD (ECDFB) nanoparticles

To synthesize FA and CD‐coated EGaIn (ECDF) NPs, 4 mg of β‐cyclodextrin was dissolved in 7.5 mL of DI water in a 50 mL centrifuge tube. Then, 30 μL of EGaIn was added to the tube containing the CD solution. The solution was then sonicated for 40 min using a probe sonicator (Qsonica Q125) at 65% amplitude to create ECD NPs in an ice bath. The sonication was set at a pulse of 10 s and a pause of 20 s to avoid the heating of NPs. Once the sonication was completed, 2.5 mL of 2 mg/mL FA was added dropwise to the ECD mixture and further sonicated for another 40 min to produce ECDF NPs. To synthesize FA, β‐CD, and BPD‐coated EGaIn (ECDFB) NPs, ECDF NPs were synthesized as described above, and 2.6 mL of BPD stock in DMSO at a concentration of 765 μM was added dropwise to the solution so that the final BPD concentration in the reaction mixture is 200 μM. The ECDFB solution was gently stirred overnight in a 37°C water bath to produce ECDFB NPs. Large particles and unattached ligands were removed by mild centrifugation (1000 rpm, 25°C, 10 min). The supernatant containing ECDFB was further purified by centrifugation at 4500 revolutions per minute (rpm) (25°C, 10 min) and washed with DI water twice. Finally, the ECDFB NPs, as shown in the schematic illustration in Figure 1, were resuspended in 10 mL of DI water and stored at 4°C.

Synthesis of folic acid‐coated EGaIn nanoparticles loaded with BPD (EFB)

For FA‐coated EGaIn (EF) NPs, 12 mg of FA was weighed and transferred into a 50 mL centrifuge tube. The sample was dissolved in 0.01 M of NaHCO3 solution, and 30 μL of EGaIn was added to the centrifuge tube containing 7.6 mL of solution. The tube was then placed in an ice water bath to prevent the heating of NPs. The tube was sonicated for 1 h at 65% amplitude using a probe sonicator (Qsonica Q125) to create EF NPs. After sonication, the large particles in the resulting suspension were removed, and the remaining solution, including EF NPs, was further centrifuged and washed with DI water three times. Finally, EF NPs were resuspended in the PBS buffer. To prepare FA and BPD‐coated NPs, EF NPs were prepared as described above, and 2.4 mL of BPD stock, in DMSO at a concentration of 300 μM, was added to that solution. This BPD‐containing EF NPs solution was again sonicated for 1 h in order to bind BPD to the surface of the nanoparticles. Large particles were removed, and the resulting suspension was washed for further purification via centrifugation. Finally, EGaIn‐FA‐BPD (EFB) NPs were resuspended in the PBS buffer. The synthesized EFB NPs were stored at 4°C.

Characterization of EFB and ECDFB nanoparticles

Scanning electron microscopy (SEM)

SEM images of EFB and ECDFB NPs were collected using a Zeiss Ultra Plus Field Emission SEM on a carbon tape substrate. Samples were imaged using an electron beam accelerating voltage of 5 kV and a working distance of 2.8–3.2 mm through an In‐lens detector. ImageJ free software was used to analyze the diameters of NPs from SEM images. Additionally, Energy Dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed to obtain elemental analysis maps. Elemental analysis mapping was conducted with a voltage of 25 kV at a working distance of 8.5 mm.

Atomic force microscopy (AFM)

AFM measurements were performed with Bruker Innova AFM under ambient conditions on an air table. Tapping mode AFM was used to obtain topographical images of EFB and ECDFB NPs at 512‐line resolution. Silicon probes (Bruker) with a 30 nm aluminum coating and a spring constant of 42 Nm−1 were used. Two hundred EFB and 200 ECDFB nanoparticles were individually analyzed using the NanoScope Analysis 3.0 software (Bruker) from AFM images.

Dynamic light scattering

The hydrodynamic diameter with polydispersity indices (PDI) of the EFB and ECDFB NPs was measured by Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS; Horiba SZ‐100‐HZ with a fixed angle dispersion of 90°) with an optical path length of 1 cm. EFB NPs were diluted 100 times with PBS in the polystyrene cuvettes, while the ECDFB NPs were diluted with DI water. The size data reported are an average of three separate measurements.

Raman spectroscopy

A Bruker SENTERRA confocal Raman microscope was used to collect Raman spectra using a 532 nm laser, 6.25 mW of laser power, and 9–15 cm−1 resolution with the wavenumber range of 45–4450 cm−1. The light was focused on an approximately 2 μm spot using a 50× objective and a slit aperture of 50 × 1000 μm. Raman spectra of FA, CD, BPD, EFB, and ECDFB nanoparticles were collected with parameters of 10 s integration time and 5 co‐additions at each selected spot to improve the signal‐to‐noise ratio.

Fluorescence spectroscopy

Fluorescence measurements of EFB and ECDFB NPs were performed with a SpectraMax i3X multimode plate reader (Molecular Devices) in top‐read mode. An excitation wavelength of 420 nm and an emission wavelength range of 600–800 nm were used. EFB and ECDFB nanoparticles were measured in a 96‐well opaque, black‐walled plate with three separate measurements to determine the stability of the NPs. The results reported are the average of three separate measurements. The concentration of BPD loaded in ECDFB NPs was calculated using a standard curve established using the above‐described fluorescence measurements.

 UV–Visible spectroscopy

UV–visible absorption measurements were performed using a Cary UV–visible spectrophotometer (Agilent Technologies). The samples were placed in quartz cuvettes, and the absorbance was measured from 175 to 800 nm. The parameters for absorbance measurement were set to a spectral bandwidth of 2 nm with a scan rate of 600 nm/min.

Photophysical characterization

Photobleaching measurements were performed in a 96‐well, black‐walled, transparent bottom plate. Solutions of 1 μM free BPD (stock in DMSO) or ECDFB were prepared either in PBS or DMSO. The solutions were irradiated with a 690 nm laser (Modulight) at an irradiance of 50 mW/cm2 with fluencies of 0, 5, 10, and 15 J/cm2. Therefore, the cumulative doses received by the wells are 0, 5, 15, and 30 J/cm2. Following each dose application, the fluorescence intensity of BPD was measured using a Varioskan LUX multimode microplate reader (Thermo Fisher Scientific) with an excitation of 420 nm and emission collected at 690 nm.

In vitro drug release

To mimic the in vitro cell culture environment, 45 mL of either DMEM or DMEM with 10% FBS media solutions was prepared separately in a 100 mL Erlenmeyer flask. Into each flask, 5 mL of ECDFB NPs solution was added. The flasks were gently shaken in a hot shaker (Bellco Glass) at 37°C. After the allotted time had passed, an aliquot of the solution was taken out of the flask and put into the centrifuge for 15 min at 4500 rpm. The supernatant of the solution was collected and decanted into a new vial. Absorption measurements of the supernatant were taken using UV–visible spectroscopy to calculate the cumulative drug release of ECDFB nanoparticles at specific times in different media, such as DMEM and DMEM+10% FBS solutions.

Cell culture

Human breast cancer cell line MDA‐MB‐231 and pancreatic cancer cell line MIA PaCa‐2 were obtained from American Type Culture Collection and cultured in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin and streptomycin. All cell lines were maintained at 37°C and 5% CO2. Cultures were tested mycoplasma‐free after resuscitation.

In vitro cellular uptake studies

To evaluate the delivery of BPD by ECDFB, MDA‐MB‐231 cells at 70–90% confluency were seeded in a black‐walled glass bottom 24‐well plate at a density of 50,000 cells/well. The following day, cells were incubated with either free BPD or ECDFB at a BPD eq. concentration of 0.25 μM for 1, 4, and 24 h. After the incubation, cells were washed twice with PBS and fixed in 10% formalin for 15 min. After fixation, formalin was replaced with PBS, and fluorescence imaging was performed using the EVOS M7000 imaging system (Invitrogen) using a 40× objective. Hoechst and BPD were imaged with a DAPI and Qdot 705 LED light cube. The fluorescence images were processed using ImageJ software.

In vitro cytotoxicity of ECDFB 

To evaluate the cytotoxicity (no light) of ECDFB, MDA‐MB‐231, and MIA PaCa‐2 cells were seeded in a 24‐well plate at a density of 50,000 cells per well. The following day, cells were incubated with varying concentrations of free CD or ECDF for either 1 or 24 h. Following the incubation, media were replaced with fresh media, and cells were incubated for a further 24 h before performing the viability assay using 3‐(4,5‐dimethylthiazol‐2‐yl)‐2,5‐diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT; Thermo Fisher Scientific) as previously reported. 

In vitro phototoxicity of ECDFB 

For the PDT study, MDA‐MB‐231 and MIA PaCa‐2 cells were seeded in 35 mm Petri dishes at a density of 1 × 105 cells per dish. The following day, cells were incubated with 0.25 μM free BPD or ECDFB for 1 h. Following incubation, media was replaced with fresh culture media, and cells were irradiated with a 690 nm laser at an irradiance of 50 mW/cm2 with fluencies of 0, 1, 2.5, 5, and 10 J/cm2. Cell viability was determined 24 hours after irradiation using the MTT assay as previously reported. 

Statistical analyses

Results were expressed as mean ± standard error unless mentioned in the figure caption. All experiments were carried out at least in triplicates. Statistical tests were carried out using GraphPad Prism. Specific tests and numbers of repeats are indicated in the figure captions. Reported p values <0.05 were considered significant. No exclusion criteria were used, and no data points were excluded from the analysis.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Characterization of ECDFB nanoparticles

The surface morphology of the fabricated ECDFB NPs was evaluated with SEM, including their shape, size, and stability. The SEM images in Figure 2A,B, show that ECDFB NPs are predominantly spherical in shape. To assess size distribution, the diameters of 2000 ECDFB NPs were measured from 10 SEM images taken over time. Figure 2C illustrates the size stability of the ECDFB NPs over a one‐month period. The size distribution histogram derived from the SEM images indicates that the average size of the ECDFB NPs is 78 ± 32 nm at Day 0 and 94 ± 32 nm at Day 28. Throughout the measurement period, the nanoparticles exhibited relatively stable sizes, though with notable variation, as indicated by the high standard deviation (SD) values. This variability is due to the range of sizes observed among the 2000 individual nanoparticles measured across the SEM images. Full histogram data and corresponding SEM images for Day 0 through Day 28 are provided in Figure S1. Given the critical role of nanoparticle size in drug delivery systems, maintaining NPs with a diameter range of 10–200 nm is essential as they have higher circulation time, optimal evasion from the reticuloendothelial system (RES), and low to moderate lymphatic accumulation. Particles smaller than 10 nm lead to rapid renal clearance, and particles larger than 200 nm have higher accumulation in the lymphatic system and rapid RES clearance resulting in reduced circulation time. Therefore, these ECDFB nanoparticles, with an average size of approximately 100 nm and demonstrating high stability over a month, were utilized for the subsequent biological evaluations.

DLS is a light scattering technique that provides the average size of nanoparticles in solutions. The size of ECDFB particles in suspension was measured by DLS, with diameters reported as 110 ± 8.39 nm for Day 0 and 107 ± 6.45 nm for Day 28, with the PDI values of 0.20 and 0.11, respectively (Figure 2D). The polydispersity index (PDI) is a key parameter for assessing sample homogeneity, with values below 0.3 representing an acceptable distribution for size uniformity for drug delivery applications. Additionally, the morphological properties of the EGaIn nanoparticles, such as the shape or surface smoothness, were analyzed using AFM, which allowed for individual nanoparticle analysis. AFM images (Figure S2) reveal that particles are spherical with smooth surfaces. Particle AFM height measurements were used to determine particle diameters, with the resulting size histogram presented over a month period in Figure S3. The average monthly particle size of ECDFB nanoparticles measured using SEM is 89.2 ± 31.2 nm, while the average monthly particle size measured using AFM is 78 ± 25 nm. Although both AFM and SEM provide information on particle size, the methods differ: SEM requires a vacuum environment, whereas AFM is conducted under ambient conditions. However, both techniques reveal that the ECDFB nanoparticles were spherical and with comparable size ranges. The size distribution data from AFM and SEM are based on the number of particles in the analyzed area, while DLS measurements depend on the intensity of light scattered by the particles in solution. These nanoparticles not only meet the desired size specifications, but also they maintain a stable size distribution over the course of a month, demonstrating their excellent stability in solution.

To better observe the surface features of ECDFB NPs, SEM imaging with EDS elemental mapping was performed, as shown in Figure S4. EDS analysis revealed the composition of ECDFB to be 77.0% Ga, 20.6% In, and 2.4% oxygen, which matches the Ga‐In composition of the starting bulk EGaIn materials. The oxygen peak observed in the EDS spectrum indicates that the particles are coated with a thin layer of gallium oxide. However, distinguishing the presence of FA and β‐CD on the nanoparticle surfaces is challenging, as both ligands bind to the oxide layer via their carboxyl (COOH) groups. While C and O were detected in the elemental analysis map of the ECDFB NPs, the mere presence of these elements is insufficient to confirm the attachment of FA and CD.

Since ligands like FA and CD cannot be specifically identified by EDS, other characterization techniques are needed to confirm their binding to the metal oxide surface. Raman spectroscopy was employed to verify covalent binding of both FA and CD to the gallium oxide layer of ECDFB through their carboxylic acid anchoring groups, which react with the oxide to form surface carboxylates. Raman scattering in the carboxylate regions (~1600 cm−1(n as) and ~ 1300 cm1(n νs)) was monitored to detect the presence of bound ligands, as shown in Figure 2E for cyclodextrin‐coated EGaIn NPs (ECD), CD and FA‐coated NPs (ECDF), and their comparison with the ECDFB NPs. The appearance of surface carboxylates at 1585 cm−1 and 1368 cm−1 confirms the covalent binding of FA and CD on the nanoparticle surface. The Raman spectra for pure CD, FA, and BPD samples are presented in Figure S9.

The developed EGaIn‐based nanoplatform (ECDFB) demonstrates effective fabrication, stability, and size control, with FA and CD ligands binding efficiently, as confirmed by SEM, AFM, DLS, and Raman spectroscopy. These characteristics highlight its potential for targeted drug delivery in cancer treatments.

The role of cyclodextrin in increasing the drug loading efficiency

One major limitation of drug delivery via surface‐modified liquid metal nanoparticles is insufficient drug entrapment capacity. To ensure an efficient amount of drug reach cancer cells, cyclodextrin was employed to enhance the drug loading efficiency of the nanoparticles. In this study, β‐cyclodextrin (β‐CD) was chosen for BPD drug encapsulation due to its optimal cavity size (6.0–6.5 Å) and superior encapsulation efficiency compared with α‐CD and γ‐CD. β‐CD's ability to encapsulate hydrophobic, moderately sized drugs is well documented, attributed to favorable intermolecular interactions. Its high water solubility and biocompatibility further support its suitability for drug delivery. Thus, β‐CD was identified as the optimal choice for stable and efficient BPD encapsulation. The concentration of BPD encapsulated in β‐CD on ECDFB NPs was quantified using fluorescence spectroscopy. Fluorescence measurements of ECDFB NPs (Figure 3A) confirm the presence of BPD on the nanoparticles, with a peak at 694 nm. The fluorescence intensity at 694 nm is directly proportional to the BPD concentration on the nanoparticles. Using a calibration curve of pure BPD in DMSO (Figure S5), the loading efficiency was determined to be 52%, representing a significant improvement over the 18–20% found in former studies, including our own. 

To confirm that this increase in drug loading efficiency was solely due to the presence of CD on the nanoparticle surface, we synthesized folic acid–BPD‐coated nanoparticles without CD (EFB NPs) via sonication, as described in our earlier work. EFB NPs exhibited an average hydrodynamic diameter of 102 ± 2 nm with a PDI of 0.026 (Figure S6). AFM analysis (Figure S6) revealed spherical particles with smooth surfaces and an average diameter of 71 ± 19 nm, while SEM images (Figure S7) indicated a similar spherical morphology with an average size of 77 nm (Figure S8). Fluorescence measurements of EFB NPs (Figure 3A) confirmed the presence of BPD, with a peak at 694 nm and a loading efficiency of 18–20%, consistent with our previous study.

ECDFB NPs incorporating CD exhibited a three‐fold increase in BPD concentration, achieving a loading efficiency of 52% (Figure 3A). This marked improvement underscores the critical role of CD in enhancing drug loading efficiency. Notably, the drug loading efficiency achieved here surpasses the typical range of 15–30% reported for metal nanoparticles. Furthermore, the stability of ECDFB NPs was evaluated by monitoring BPD fluorescence intensity over time (Figure 3B,C), revealing stable retention of 52% for up to 4 weeks. Overall, these findings highlight the significant advantage of incorporating β‐CD to improve drug loading efficiency for EGaIn NPs, positioning them as a promising platform for developing more effective drug delivery systems.

Photoactivity of ECDFB 

The photostability of PS is a critical factor in determining the efficacy of PDT. Rapid photobleaching can limit the therapeutic window and reduce overall treatment effectiveness. Therefore, we investigated the photobleaching characteristics of free BPD and ECDFB NPs under various physiologically relevant conditions (Figure 4). In PBS, free BPD exhibited rapid photobleaching, with its fluorescence intensity decreasing to near‐zero levels by 15 J/cm2 of light exposure. By contrast, ECDFB demonstrated significantly improved photostability, maintaining higher fluorescence levels throughout the exposure range up to 30 J/cm2, suggesting protective effects of the nanocarrier during PDT. When assessed in DMSO, both free BPD and ECDFB showed remarkable photostability, with minimal decrease in fluorescence intensity up to 30 J/cm2. This observation highlights the role of the surrounding medium in influencing PS stability. The organic environment appears to protect against photobleaching for both formulations. We observed distinct behaviors in PBS supplemented with 10% FBS, which more closely mimics physiological conditions. Free BPD underwent moderate photobleaching, with a gradual decrease in fluorescence intensity over the exposure range. However, ECDFB exhibited an unexpected increase in fluorescence intensity with increasing light exposure, reaching approximately 2.5 times the initial intensity at 30 J/cm2. This phenomenon could be attributed to complex interactions between the NPs and serum proteins, affecting either fluorescence properties or NP structure. The mechanisms underlying this enhancement require further investigation through protein corona analysis and photophysical characterization to understand their implications for in vivo applications. The superior photostability of ECDFB in PBS and their unique behavior in serum‐containing media underscore the potential advantages of ECDFB nanoformulation for PDT applications. These findings provide insights for optimizing photoactivation conditions while suggesting possible theranostic benefits through combined therapeutic and imaging capabilities.

Drug release of ECDFB nanoparticles

To evaluate the effect of media on BPD release from the nanoplatform, ECDFB NPs were placed in solutions of DMEM and a mixture of 90% DMEM and 10% FBS, mimicking the in vitro environment of cancer cells. These solutions were incubated at 37°C with mild constant shaking to simulate the real cell conditions. The amount of BPD released into the solutions was assessed at regular intervals.

Figure 5A demonstrates the cumulative drug release behavior of ECDFB NPs in DMEM and DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS over approximately 240 h. In DMEM, drug release exhibited a minimal increase during the first 100 h, reaching approximately 10%. A more pronounced release occurred between 150 and 200 h, with cumulative release surpassing 30%, ultimately peaking at around 40% by 240 h. This delayed release pattern suggests a controlled and sustained drug release mechanism, with the initial lag phase likely attributed to the entrapment of BPD within the CD matrix. The gradual release profile reflects a stable, prolonged delivery system, suitable for maintaining therapeutic drug levels over extended periods.

By contrast, the presence of FBS significantly altered drug release kinetics. Within the first 5 h, a rapid initial release of approximately 65% was observed, followed by a cumulative release of around 80% over 100 h. The disparity in release kinetics between DMEM and DMEM +10% FBS can be explained by the formation of a protein corona on the nanoparticle surface in the presence of serum proteins. The protein corona, a dynamic layer of adsorbed proteins, modifies the physicochemical properties of the nanoparticles, thereby affecting and further promoting drug release by disrupting the nanocarrier structure. This accelerated release profile highlights the influence of serum proteins on the release mechanism, suggesting a stronger initial therapeutic effect.

Figure 5B depicts DLS measurements of nanoparticle size in DMEM and DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS over 240 h, providing insight into the structural stability of nanoparticles and their interaction with serum proteins. In DMEM alone, the NPs size increased to approximately 175 nm within the first 6 h, likely due to initial hydration and aggregation. Over the subsequent 48 h, the size decreased slightly to around 150 nm, suggesting partial stabilization or rearrangement of nanoparticle surface ligands in the protein‐free medium. After this initial period, the nanoparticle size exhibited minimal fluctuations, remaining between 150 and 200 nm throughout the 240‐h observation period. This size stability correlates closely with the observed drug release kinetics. The minimal drug release during the first 100 h, while the nanoparticle size slightly increased indicates g that the nanoparticles retained their structural integrity and encapsulated the photosensitizer effectively. This suggests that the absence of serum proteins limited external interactions with the nanoparticles, resulting in a slower and more controlled release profile.

By contrast, in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS, the average nanoparticle size stabilized at approximately 180 nm throughout the experiment. The presence of serum proteins likely contributed to the formation of a protein corona, a dynamic layer of adsorbed proteins on the nanoparticle surface. The protein corona not only increased the apparent size of the nanoparticles but also played a significant role in modulating the release kinetics of the encapsulated drug. The rapid initial release observed in the DMEM + 10% FBS samples can be attributed to the interaction of serum proteins with the nanoparticle surface. These interactions likely disrupted the surface integrity, promoting the release of surface‐bound or weakly encapsulated drug molecules. The protein corona's influence extends beyond size stabilization; it alters the surface chemistry of nanoparticles, impacting their interactions with the biological environment. The adsorption of proteins such as albumin or other serum components can lead to structural rearrangements at the nanoparticle interface, enhancing drug release from the CD. This phenomenon is particularly evident in the faster initial release observed in the DMEM + 10% FBS condition compared with DMEM alone. Overall, the results underscore the critical role of the protein corona in influencing nanoparticle behavior, particularly size and release kinetics.

Furthermore, the degradation behavior of the Ga2O3 component in the nanoparticles was evaluated under acidic conditions, pH of 5 and 6, which are typically encountered in cancer cell environments. The oxide layer can be dissolved in solutions with different pH levels, leading to a pH‐dependent release of the drug (Figure S10). Upon exposure to acidic conditions, the Ga2O3 layer begins to dissolve, releasing the ligands as well as gallium ions (Ga3+), which are known to have anticancer properties and may contribute to the therapeutic effect alongside PDT.

Biocompatibility of ECDFB 

The biocompatibility of the ECDFB was evaluated in MDA‐MB‐231 breast cancer cells and MIA PaCa‐2 pancreatic cancer cells using cell viability assays and live/dead cell staining. First, we assessed the cytotoxicity of CD alone, a key component of our NPs formulation (Figure 6A). Both cell lines maintained high viability (>75%) across a wide range of CD concentrations up to 0.5 mg/mL for both 1‐h and 24‐h incubations. Minimal toxicity was observed at the highest concentration tested (1 mg/mL), indicating that CD itself has a favorable biocompatibility profile. Hence, we decided to coat the EGaIn for synthesizing the ECDFB with 0.4 mg/mL of CD to minimize any adverse toxic effects. Next, we evaluated the cytotoxicity of the complete ECDF formulation (Figure 6B). In both cell lines, ECDF exhibited concentration‐dependent and time‐dependent effects on cell viability. For 1‐h incubations, cell viability remained above 75% up to 150 μg/mL ECDF concentration for MDA‐MB‐231 cells, while for MIA PaCa‐2, it remained up to 100 μg/mL. However, a sharp decrease in viability was observed at concentrations greater than 150 μg/mL. The cytotoxic effects were more pronounced with 24‐h incubations, where significant reductions in cell viability were observed at concentrations beyond 50 μg/mL. To further visualize the cytotoxic effects, we performed live/dead cell staining on MDA‐MB‐231 cells after 1‐h incubation with various concentrations of ECDF (Figure 6C). The live cells are stained with calcein‐AM in green, while dead cells are stained with ethidium homodimer‐1 in red. Consistent with the viability assay results, minimal cell death was observed at concentrations up to 50 μg/mL, with cells maintaining their typical morphology. However, a noticeable cell density reduction was observed at 100 μg/mL. Substantial cell death was evident at 150 μg/mL, with numerous dead cells (red‐stained) and a dramatic decrease in the number of viable cells (green‐stained). These results demonstrate that while the CD component of our NPs is highly biocompatible, the complete ECDF formulation exhibits dose‐dependent cytotoxicity. Based on these findings, subsequent ECDF experiments were conducted at concentrations below 100 μg/mL to ensure minimal intrinsic cytotoxicity while maintaining therapeutic efficacy.

Quantification of BPD delivery by ECDFB 

To evaluate the efficacy of ECDFB in delivering BPD to cancer cells, we conducted cellular uptake studies in MDA‐MB‐231 breast cancer and MIA PaCa‐2 pancreatic cancer cell lines (Figure 7). This experiment is crucial for understanding the intracellular delivery dynamics of the ECDFB nanoformulation compared with free BPD, which directly impacts the potential therapeutic efficacy of PDT. Fluorescence microscopy images revealed successful internalization of BPD in both cell lines after 1 h of treatment, whether delivered via ECDFB or as free BPD (Figure 7A–D). The red fluorescence signal, indicative of BPD, was predominantly localized in the cytosol, while cell nuclei were counterstained with DAPI (blue). This visualization confirms that ECDFB can effectively deliver BPD into cancer cells, validating its potential as a drug delivery system for PDT. Interestingly, qualitative assessment of the images suggested that free BPD resulted in more intense intracellular fluorescence than ECDFB in both cell lines. This observation prompted us to conduct a quantitative, time‐dependent uptake study in both cell lines to understand the kinetics of BPD delivery better (Figure 7E,F). Further, understanding the uptake dynamics over time can help in determining the optimal time window for light activation in PDT. The cellular uptake analysis revealed distinct kinetics between free BPD and ECDFB as well as both cell lines over 24 h. In MDA‐MB‐231 cells, free BPD showed rapid initial uptake at 1 h, likely due to passive diffusion across cell membranes. The uptake peaked significantly at 4 h (p < 0.0001) compared with ECDFB, suggesting efficient passive diffusion for free BPD versus slower endocytic uptake for NPs. Both formulations showed decreased intracellular levels at 24 h, indicating possible drug efflux, cellular metabolism, or uptake pathway saturation. By contrast, MIA PaCa‐2 cells exhibited equal early uptake between formulations, suggesting different membrane characteristics or enhanced endocytic activity. Notably, ECDFB showed higher retention at 24 h (p < 0.05) in MIA PaCa‐2 cells, indicating reduced drug efflux or altered intracellular trafficking. The distinct uptake profiles highlight the need for cell‐specific optimization of both drug formulation and treatment timing to maximize therapeutic efficacy.

Photodynamic efficacy of ECDFB in vitro

The photodynamic efficacy of ECDFB was evaluated in comparison with free BPD in MDA‐MB‐231 breast cancer cells and MIA PaCa‐2 pancreatic cancer cells (Figure 8) following a 1‐hour drug incubation period, as at this time point, both formulations showed similar uptake. Both cell lines were treated with either free BPD or ECDFB at equivalent BPD concentrations, followed by exposure to various light fluences ranging from 0 to 10 J/cm2. Neither free BPD nor ECDFB exhibited significant cytotoxicity without light activation (0 J/cm2) in both cell lines, demonstrating the non‐toxic nature of these agents without photoactivation. The “light only” (LO) control, where cells were exposed to the highest light dose without photosensitizer, also showed no significant reduction in cell viability, confirming that light exposure alone does not induce cell death. Upon light activation, both free BPD and ECDFB demonstrated a dose‐dependent photodynamic effect in MDA‐MB‐231 and MIA PaCa‐2 cells. As the light fluence increased from 1 to 10 J/cm2, a progressive decrease in cell viability was observed for both formulations. This trend was consistent across both cell lines, indicating that the photodynamic efficacy of ECDFB was comparable to that of free BPD across all light fluences tested, and no statistical difference was observed. In MDA‐MB‐231 cells, both formulations reduced cell viability to approximately 50% at 2.5 J/cm2 and further to about 40% at 10 J/cm2. Similarly, in MIA PaCa‐2 cells, both free BPD and ECDFB showed equivalent photodynamic effects, with cell viability decreasing to around 30% at the highest light fluence of 10 J/cm2. The comparable efficacy between free BPD and ECDFB is particularly intriguing, given the lower cellular uptake observed for ECDFB in previous experiments. This discrepancy suggests that ECDFB may offer additional advantages, such as improved intracellular distribution or enhanced photodynamic activation, which compensate for the lower overall uptake. These findings highlight the complex nature of NP‐mediated drug delivery and underscore the importance of comprehensive characterization beyond simple uptake studies when developing novel nanotherapeutics. The absence of a significant difference in photodynamic efficacy between free BPD and ECDFB indicates that the NP formulation effectively delivered the PS to the cancer cells while maintaining its photodynamic properties. This comparable efficacy, combined with the potential benefits of NP delivery systems such as improved stability and potential for targeted delivery, suggests that ECDFB could serve as a promising alternative to free BPD for PDT applications. These results demonstrate that ECDFB possesses potent and controllable photodynamic activity against breast and pancreatic cancer cells in vitro. The ability of ECDFB to match the efficacy of free BPD, despite lower cellular uptake, warrants further investigation into their potential as a PDT agent for cancer treatment. Future studies should focus on elucidating the mechanisms behind this comparable efficacy and exploring the in vivo performance of ECDFB.

CONCLUSION

This study establishes EGaIn‐based liquid metal nanoparticles as a promising platform for PDT in cancer treatment. EGaIn NPs were successfully synthesized and characterized through a one‐step sonication methodology, incorporating FA as a targeting ligand, β‐CD as an encapsulating agent, and BPD as a photosensitizer. The inclusion of β‐CD significantly enhanced the drug loading capacity of BPD, achieving a three‐fold increase (52% vs. 18%) and improving the overall efficacy of the nanoplatform. The physicochemical properties of the nanoparticles were characterized through spectroscopic techniques, while fluorescence measurements confirmed the high BPD drug loading efficiency of the ECDFB nanoparticles. Covalent binding of FA and β‐CD to the gallium oxide surface was achieved via carboxylic acid groups present in the structures of FA and CD, ensuring nanoparticle stability and controlled drug release under physiological conditions. In vitro studies demonstrated good biocompatibility and comparable photodynamic efficacy to free BPD, despite lower cellular uptake, suggesting potential advantages in intracellular distribution or activation mechanisms. The controllable, light‐activated cytotoxicity of the system offers precise spatiotemporal control over cancer cell death, addressing critical limitations of conventional PDT approaches, including poor penetration depth of light, reduced efficacy in hypoxic tumor microenvironments, photosensitizer photobleaching, and systemic toxicity, while leveraging the unique properties of liquid metal nanoparticles. Additionally, the integration of drug delivery, PDT, and imaging capabilities within a single platform presents opportunities for more effective and personalized cancer therapies. Future research will focus on optimizing targeting efficiency and evaluating in vivo performance. This work makes a significant contribution to the field of nanomedicine, advancing the development of precise and effective cancer treatment strategies.
