# A moving target for drug discovery: Structure activity relationship and many genome (de)stabilizing functions of the RAD52 protein.

## Abstract

BRCA-ness phenotype, a signature of many breast and ovarian cancers, manifests as deficiency in homologous recombination, and as defects in protection and repair of damaged DNA replication forks. A dependence of such cancers on DNA repair factors less important for survival of BRCA-proficient cells, offers opportunities for development of novel chemotherapeutic interventions. The first drugs targeting BRCA-deficient cancers, poly-ADP-ribose polymerase (PARP) inhibitors have been approved for the treatment of advanced, chemotherapy resistant cancers in patients with BRCA1/2 germline mutations. Nine additional proteins that can be targeted to selectively kill BRCA-deficient cancer cells have been identified. Among them, a DNA repair protein RAD52 is an especially attractive target due to general tolerance of the RAD52 loss of function, and protective role of an inactivating mutation. Yet, the effective pharmacological inhibitors of RAD52 have not been forthcoming. In this review, we discuss advances in the state of our knowledge of the RAD52 structure, activities and cellular functions, with a specific focus on the features that make RAD52 an attractive, but difficult drug target.

SETTING THE STAGE: SYNTHETIC LETHALITY AND BRCAness

Defects in genome caretakers BRCA1 and BRCA2, or so-called “BRCAness”, underlies both pathogenesis and vulnerability of many cancers. This is because BRCA-deficient cancers depend on a narrower subset of DNA repair and genome maintenance factors compared to the BRCA-proficient cells (reviewed in). Originally BRCAness was viewed as a deficiency in DNA repair by homologous recombination (HR). The molecular machinery of HR is critical for the accurate repair of deleterious DNA lesions that affect both strands of the DNA duplex, including DNA double strand breaks (DSBs) and broken DNA replication forks. Along with Fanconi Anemia complex, the classically defined HR factors are also involved in the repair of interstrand DNA cross-links, and in protection of stalled or damaged DNA replication forks. Inherited and spontaneous mutations, and epigenetic alterations in key HR players, including tumor suppressors BRCA1, BRCA2 and PALB2 are believed to facilitate oncogenesis via accumulation of unrepaired or improperly repaired DNA breaks, and increased genetic instability. A newer view on BRCAness cancer phenotype is related to defect in restraining DNA replication in response to the replication stress leading to accumulation of ssDNA gaps and chemotherapy sensitivity.

The discovery of DNA repair proteins that facilitate survival, rapid proliferation and aggressive nature of BRCA-deficient cancers offers an insight into backup mechanisms that support fast replication, DNA repair and damage tolerance that persist in tumorigenic cells. Biochemical activities and interactions of these proteins constitute vulnerability of the BRCA-deficient cancers which can be exploited via synthetic lethality. Synthetic lethality is a phenomenon shared between two genes whose individual mutations or loss do not compromise cell viability, but mutation or loss of both genes results in cell death. Complex and agile DNA repair networks contain an abundance of “in pathway” genetic interactions. Among these interactions, synthetic lethality may arise in normally non-essential pathways when, for example, a genetic defect is blocking forward flow through the pathway while an additional genetic ablation, mutation or pharmacological inhibition is blocking the reverse reaction resulting in accumulation of an unrepairable intermediate and death by toxicity. A direct application of synthetic lethality in cancers involves using an inhibitor to target cancer cells that lack a functional tumor suppressor gene while sparing the functionality and lividity of surrounding ‘normal’ cells, usually referred to as pharmacologically induced synthetic lethality. Clinical application of this paradigm has led to identification of poly-ADP-ribose polymerase (PARP) as a potential target in BRCA1 and BRCA2-deficient cancers and subsequent development of the first drugs targeting BRCAness, PARP inhibitors (Figure 1A). To date, several PARP inhibitors have been approved for the treatment of advanced, chemotherapy resistant cancers in patients with BRCA1/2 germline mutations and are routinely indicated as maintenance treatment in advanced ovarian cancers responding to platinum-based chemotherapy, in germline mutated BRCA1/2 mutated metastatic breast, pancreatic and castration-resistant prostate cancers (see for review). There are two major challenges in using PARP inhibitors for pharmacologically induced synthetic lethality, namely, identification of cancers that have BRCAness vulnerabilities and emergence of the resistance to PARP inhibition. While ∼15% of ovarian cancer patients treated with olaparib remain disease free for more than 5 years, many develop resistance within the first year. Thus, identification of new synthetic lethal interactions that can lead to targeted chemotherapeutic schemes has become an active area of research.

RAD52 IS UNIQUE AMONG DNA REPAIR FACTORS THAT HAVE SYNTHETICALLY LETHAL RELATIONSHIPS WITH BRCAness.

To date, DNA repair protein RAD52, DNA polymerase POLθ, FANCD2, FANCM, APEX2 and FEN nucleases, PLK1 kinase, REV1-Polζ translesion DNA synthesis polymerase and sirtuins SIRT1 and SIRT6 have been shown to have synthetic lethal relationships with BRCA-deficiency (Figure 1B).

RAD52 DNA repair protein was identified as one of the first synthetic lethal relationships with BRCA deficiency. Pharmacologically induced synthetic lethality involving RAD52 was achieved by disruption of the protein oligomeric ring and by inhibition of ssDNA binding, or single strand annealing, which depends on the DNA binding. The mechanism(s) by which RAD52 promotes survival of BRCA-deficient cells are not fully understood (see discussion below), but is likely different from that of PARP1. A dual synthetic lethality observed between RAD52 depletion and PARP inhibition in BRCA-deficient chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) and B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia cells suggests that RAD52 and PARP1 are involved in different genome maintenance mechanisms important for the survival of BRCA-deficient cells. On the other hand, PARP and RAD52 inhibition may share at least one common outcome, namely persistence of the post-replicative ssDNA gaps, toxic to BRCA-deficient cells.

DNA polymerase theta, POLθ sometimes referred to as PolQ, a multifunctional enzyme that has polymerase and helicase domains, interacts with RAD51 recombinase and restricts its activities. POLθ’s main function is microhomology mediated end joining, MMEJ. Large scale genomics studies showed that nearly half of epithelial ovarian cancers have defects in HR and there is an inverse correlation between HR capacity and POLθ expression in these cancers. POLθ knockdown upregulates homology-dependent DSB repair in BRCA-proficient cells, but enhances cell death in BRCA-deficient cells.

ART558, a potent allosteric inhibitor (7.9 nM) of POLθ, selectively kills BRCA-deficient cells in a manner dependent on the functionality of the end resection nucleases. ART558 enhances the effect of PARP inhibition, suggesting that POLθ and PARP1 may contribute to the survival of BRCA-deficient cells through distinct mechanisms. Moreover, 53BP1 defective cells that are resistant to PARP inhibition, are sensitive to POLθ inhibition. Another POLθ inhibitor, antibiotic Novobiocin identified in a screen for the disruptors of the POLθ ATPase activity, selectively kills BRCA-deficient breast and ovarian cancer cells that acquired resistance to PARP inhibitors. Effectiveness of the POLθ pharmacological inhibition in cells that acquired PARP inhibitor resistance confirms that, similar to RAD52, POLθ promotes survival of BRCA-deficient cells through the mechanism different from that of PARP1. While promising due to its potency and specificity towards POLθ over other DNA polymerases, ART558 can be readily cleared by microsomes and is not stable enough to be effective in vivo. It will, therefore, require more medicinal chemistry work to become an effective alternative or addition to PARP inhibitors.

POLθ and RAD52 have a complicated relationship. Both proteins are involved in DSB repair, but have different preferences for the length of sequence homology near the break. Combined disruption of POLθ and RAD52 causes at least additive hypersensitivity to cisplatin. RAD52 also restricts POLθ-mediated end joining until mitosis. FANCD2:

Another synthetic lethal partner of BRCA1 and BRCA2 is FANCD2. FANCD2, is upregulated in BRCA-deficient cancers, promotes replication fork stability and restart and recruits POLθ to the sites of DNA breaks.

FANCM:

A Fanconi Anemia protein, FANCM, has been recently shown to mediate the DNA repair pathway choice at stalled replication forks, and its ATPase activity is important for survival of cells carrying BRCA1 hypomorphic mutations.

APEX2, FEN1 and PLK1:

Screens using shRNA and CRISPR-based libraries have identified two nucleases, APEX2 (Ape2) and FEN1 as synthetic lethal partners of BRCA2. These screens also confirmed a synthetic lethal interaction between BRCA deficiency and POLθ disruption. A screen using kinase inhibitors revealed that pharmacological inhibition of Polo-like kinase 1 (PLK1) is selectively toxic to BRCA1-deficient cells in cell cultures, chimeric spheroids, and mice xenografts.

REV1-Polζ interaction:

Recent studies revealed that the viability of BRCA1/2-deficient cells depends on the ability of the REV1-Polζ translesion synthesis polymerase to fill the gaps left behind due to HR deficiency. Pharmacological inhibition of the REV1-Polζ complex formation promotes synthetic lethality with BRCA-defects, which depends on the PRIMPOL and SMUG1 DNA glycosylase.

Sirtuins:

In addition to PARP1, but in a completely different mechanism, other enzymes implicated in NAD metabolism have a synthetically lethal relationships with BRCA-deficiency. CRISPR-mediated removal of SIRT1 or SIRT6, or small molecule sirtuin inhibitors induced synthetic lethality with BRCA-deficiency, replication stress and increased cellular protein PARylation. This effect was negated by genetic ablation of either PARP1 or the histone PARylation factor HPF1.

RAD52 is unique among pharmacological targets for inhibition in BRCA-deficient cancers:

An important feature that sets RAD52 apart from other factors involved in HR, homology directed DNA repair, and protection of DNA replication forks is that its depletion has a minimal effect on the BRCA positive cells. Moreover, in contrast to most DNA repair genes, RAD52 is tolerant to potential loss of function (LoF) mutations (LOEUF score of 0.64 at gnomAD database) with one of the inactivating mutations, S346X (loss of the nuclear localization sequence) shown to reduce risk of developing breast cancer in carrier of pathogenic germline BRCA2 mutations. This allele, 12–1023218-G-T is present in the human population at the frequency of 1.57×10−2 with a significant number of heterozygotes (gnomAD database). The non-essentiality of human RAD52 is further substantiated by the ease with which a biallelic disruption of RAD52 can be achieved in human cell lines. A specific inhibitor of RAD52, therefore, is likely to have lower toxicity than, for example, PARP inhibitors that have a range of adverse side effects.

Potential for RAD52 inhibition outside of the BRCA-deficient tumors.

While no synthetic lethal screens have been published with RAD52 depletion or inhibition, a synthetically lethal relationship has been reported between RAD52 and ATM (ataxia–telangiectasia mutated) serine/threonine kinase. RAD52 depletion partially suppressed tumorigenesis and rescued T-cell maturation in ATM deficient mice. Notably, this synthetic relationship is also shared with PARP1, as PARP inhibition with olaparib was toxic to ATM mutant lymphoblastoid cells both in cell lines and the in mouse xenograft model. ATM loss causes several severe genetic disorders, such as ataxia telangiectasia (A-T). ATM deficient mice often display several disease phenotypes including a predisposition to cancer, growth retardation and cell proliferation defects. A-T patients are at a higher risk for developing leukemia or lymphomas. ATM is rapidly activated by the DSB via auto-phosphorylation and dimer dissociation, and coordinates DSB repair by phosphorylating a large number of DNA repair proteins. Loss of ATM also drives signaling dysregulation of other repair proteins including p53 and CHK2, resulting in cell checkpoint defects and impaired DNA repair. In contrast to the ATM−/− mice that succumbed to aggressive T cell lymphoma within the first 6 months of their life, the ATM−/−,RAD52−/− mice rarely developed tumors, and RAD52 depletion was sufficient to suppress tumor formation in the mice. Immunodeficiency of the ATM−/− mice was partially rescued in a RAD52-deficient background, as indicated by the presence of mature T-cells in the mice’s spleen. Considering that RAD52 LoF is well tolerated and that the RAD52−/− mice are viable and proficient in homology directed DNA repair, RAD52 is an attractive target in ATM-deficient leukemias as well as in BRCA-deficient cancers.

Amplification of the genomic region 12p13.33, which contains the RAD52 gene, and somatic overexpression of RAD52 has been positively associated with increased lung cancer risk, and particularly with the development of lung squamous cell carcinomas (LUSC). The same study showed that RAD52 depletion in mouse bronchial epithelial cells increased DNA damage accumulation and tumor cell death. RAD52 inhibitors, therefore, may provide a new treatment option in LUSC.

RAD52 IS AN IMPORTANT, YET UNDERDEVELOPED, DRUG TARGET

While a validated and an attractive target, RAD52 presents several major challenges for drug discovery: (1) selection of the structural features or biochemical activities to be targeted, (2) the absence of a convenient active site, (3) challenge of developing a potent and selective inhibitor of the protein-nucleic acid interaction, and (2) determining which among cellular activities of RAD52 are important for survival of BRCA-deficient cells. We will first address the RAD52 structure and biochemical activities with respect to potential inhibitor development.

RAD52 is a 46 kDa, 418 amino acids long protein comprised by an evolutionarily conserved and well-structured N-terminal domain that forms oligomers, binds ssDNA, dsDNA, anneals complementary DNA strands (Figure 2A, red), and mostly disordered C-terminal domain that mediates several protein-protein interactions (Figure 2A). RAD52 forms a ring shaped oligomer, with a range of monomers in the oligomeric ring. Crystal structures of the conserved DNA annealing domain confirmed the ring shaped form of the RAD52 undecamer.

Several shorter RAD52 isoforms are expressed in different tissues as result of alternative splicing. The largest of these isoforms is 266 aa long and can be considered analogous to yeast Rad59, a Rad52 paralog which contains only the conserved DNA binding/self-association domain, but lacks protein-protein interaction regions. Notably, the two even shorter isoforms, 139 and 118 aa (the latter containing only 94 aa in common with the full-length RAD52) retain the DNA-binding activity.

The N-terminal domains of RAD52 proteins from different species share a high degree of homology and are presumed to share the structural and functional features. Amino acids important for various aspects of the S. cerevisiae Rad52 function have been identified through a molecular genetic analysis. Systematic replacement to alanine of the amino acids within the RAD52 N-terminal domain revealed four classes of mutations. Figures 2B and 2C show the identified residues mapped on the structure of the RAD52-ssDNA complex (PDB: 5XRZ from). Class A (red) included 4 mutants (N91A, F94A, R136A and F173A, corresponding to N73, F79, H121 and F158 in human RAD52). These mutants displayed severe Δrad52-like phenotype: these mutants were extremely sensitive to γ-ray radiation (reports on the DSB repair), deficient in mitotic recombination (HR capacity), as well as in recombination between direct repeats (reports on single-strand annealing). Mutations in this class are likely to interfere with structural integrity of the Rad52 protein as they are located deep in the protein core (F79 and F158), or at the interface between the RAD52 monomers (N73 and H121). In contrast to class A mutations, a single representative of class B, K159A substitution (in yeast nomenclature) resulted in mild γ-ray sensitivity and maintained recombination between direct repeats, while producing severe deficiency in mitotic recombination. In human RAD52, this mutation corresponds to the highly conserved K144 (Figure 2B and 2C, purple), which forms an ionic hydrogen bond with D149 from the adjacent subunit. Note that the analogous D164 in yeast Rad52 was not a part of the alanine scan. In a recent crystal structure (PDB: 5XRZ), K144 is in a close proximity to ssDNA though not in the “primary ssDNA binding site” (will be discussed below), while D164 forms an ion environment with E140, E145 and phosphodiester backbone of DNA. This is an interesting separation of function mutation as it likely disrupts the yeast Rad52 recombination mediator function, which is not fully retained in human RAD52. Yeast Rad52 mediates HR by assisting the Rad51 recombinase in formation of its active species, a nucleoprotein filament assembled on ssDNA previously occupied by the ssDNA binding protein, Replication protein A (RPA). An intermediate step in the Rad51 assembly involves formation of a Rad52-RPA-ssDNA ternary complex. Recent single-molecule studies revealed that when Rad52 forms a complex with RPA on ssDNA, it modifies the RPA conformational dynamics. RPA contains 4 DNA binding domain, DBD-A, B, C and D sequentially aligned on ssDNA with DBD-A bound to the 5’ end of the occluded sequence and DBD-D to the 3’ end (reviewed in). The RPA-DNA complex is dynamic with individual DBDs microscopically binding and dissociating from ssDNA while RPA as a whole is macroscopically bound to ssDNA. This multivalent arrangement allows RPA to bind ssDNA with high, sub-nanomolar affinity, while the microscopic dynamics promotes RPA replacement by downstream players in DNA replication, recombination and repair. Rad52 binding specifically modifies the dynamics of the DBD-D by removing it away from ssDNA. K144 (K159 in yeast Rad52) is positioned to direct the ssDNA in this ternary complex. While not a canonical recombination mediator per se, human RAD52 forms a stable complex with RPA-coated ssDNA, in which ssDNA is also shared between the two proteins. It will be interesting to see how K144A substitution affects the RAD52-RPA-ssDNA complex formation and dynamics.

Mutations in the most numerous class C (Figure 2B and 2C, light blue, Y66A, R70A, W 84A, R85A, Y96A, R156A, T163A, C180A and F186A in yeast corresponding to Y51, R55, H69, R70, Y81, K141, T148, C 165 and Y171 I human), resulted in increased radiation sensitivity of yeast cells, but have no effect on mitotic recombination or on the recombination between direct repeats. Most of the amino acids in this class donate or accept hydrogen bonds stabilizing the tertiary structure of the monomer. Exceptions include: R55 (corresponds to yeast R70), which faces the DNA-binding groove and is important for DNA binding, Y51, which donates hydrogen bond to D197 of the adjacent subunit, and Y81, which may stack with Y36 of the preceding subunit thus stabilizing subunit-subunit interaction.

The same subunit-subunit interface harboring class B 159A (K144) also contains another mutation affecting yeast Rad52 performance in mitotic recombination - K150A, which corresponds to K135 in human RAD51 (Figure 2B and 2C, salmon). Along with R127A (R112 in human RAD52) it was designated as a class D mutation. K135 forms an ionic hydrogen bond with D97 from the adjacent subunit likely contributing to the subunit-subunit interface in the RAD52 oligomer. K135 was also suggested to play a role in dsDNA binding in the secondary site of RAD52.

DNA binding by the RAD52 displays an extraordinary complexity and plasticity. In contrast to many ring-shaped DNA-binding proteins, which bind DNA in the central cavity, the DNA-binding sites of the RAD52 protein are around the oligomeric ring. Initially, residues that are most critical for ssDNA binding by human RAD52 were found in the groove that spans the circumference of the RAD52 ring. Notably, none of the individual substitutions among the DNA binding residues are sufficient to fully destroy the RAD52-ssDNA interaction and none of these residues appeared among Class A mutations suggesting a complex and agile nature of the RAD52-ssDNA interaction. Biochemical experiments using a Forster Resonance Energy Transfer (FRET)-based approach revealed that both ssDNA and dsDNA can be wrapped around the oligomeric ring of RAD52. The wrapping of the ssDNA around the oligomeric ring was confirmed by the recent crystal structure. Based on biochemical and recent structural studies, two distinct DNA binding sites have emerged in RAD52, the “inner” or “primary” binding site (a deep, narrow ssDNA binding groove with residues R55, K152, R153 and R156 playing the most critical role; Figure 2D deep purple) and “outer” or “secondary” DNA binding site, which includes residues K102 and K133 on the top of the ring and K169 and R173 on the ring’s outer leaflet (Figure 2D and 2E, cyan). This outer binding site can accommodate both ssDNA and dsDNA. Also, on the top of the ring located Y104 (Figure 2D and 2E, light purple), which is sandwiched between K102 and K133 of the adjacent subunits. Y104 is subject of phosphorylation by c-ABL kinase, which in turn is activated by ATM in response to ionizing radiation. Y104 phosphorylation reduces the dsDNA binding by RAD52 and facilitates single strand annealing. The architecture of the two DNA binding sites suggests protein’s adaptability to its many biochemical and cellular functions. Notably, all these functions involve ssDNA binding. Not surprisingly, most of the RAD52 inhibitors identified so far target the ssDNA binding groove, or the RAD52-mediated ssDNA annealing, possibly through interfering with DNA binding. The exception is the inhibitor that disrupts the RAD52 oligomeric ring and the peptide aptamer consisting of 13 amino acids V71 – G83, a polyglycine linker and sequence derived from the HIV-transactivator of transcription, which facilitates passage across cellular membrane.

Most of the mapped RAD52 protein-protein interactions are species specific and involve the C-terminal domain of RAD52 (Figure 2A). These include the RPA interacting motif, R260,Q261,K262, the RAD51 interacting region and the nuclear localization sequence. The C-terminal domain of RAD52 is also a site of interactions with the XPB and XPD subunits of transcription factor TFIIH and RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) Additional important interactions, which may involve either the N-terminal or the C-terminal domains include MUS81, DSS1, XPG nuclease, Cockayne Syndrome Protein B (CSB) and POLD3.

THE MANY CELLULAR FUNCTIONS OF RAD52

Rad52 was assigned its number in 1974 along with a large group of Saccharomyces cerevisiae genes whose function is important in repair of DSBs induced by ionizing radiation and in meiosis. Yeast Rad52 is a founding member of the Rad52 epistasis group, as Δrad52 displayed the most severe radiation sensitivity and meiosis defects. Yeast Rad52 plays several important functions: Rad52 is a mediator of HR, it restricts DSB resection, is a key player in Rad51-independent break induced replication and mutagenic single strand annealing; Rad52 annealing function is also used in the second end capture during DSB repair by HR.

Some, but not all of the cellular and biochemical functions of yeast Rad52 are conserved in the mammalian counterpart. Vertebrates display different dependencies on RAD52. In contrast to yeast, RAD52−/− murine cells do not exhibit severe DNA repair or recombination defects. RAD52 deficient mice created through disruption of exon three on MmRAD52 were viable, have unaltered resistance to DNA damaging drugs, such as mitomyosin C (MMC) and ionizing radiation. The RAD52−/− mouse cells show no decrease in DSB repair by homologous recombination in the reporter assays. Chicken DT40 RAD52−/− cells have reduced frequency of targeted integration, but no hypersensitivity to ionizing radiation, methyl methanesulfonate, or platinum agents. The RAD52−/− human cells exhibit a modest growth defect and are more sensitive to hydroxyurea, but not to ionizing radiation. RAD52 removal in human cells, however, affects DSB repair by both homology-directed repair and via single-strand annealing in plasmid-based reporter assays. Expression of human RAD52 in Δrad52 in s. cerevisiae restores DSB repair by a non-mutagenic mechanism, which does not depend on RAD52-Rad51 interaction.

Before killing BRCA2-deficient cells, RAD52 depletion reduces the residual HR 2 – 10 fold. This residual HR activity, however, is not sufficient to fully restore recombination capacity of BRCA-deficient cells. It is unlikely, therefore, that the HR function of RAD52 is responsible for the survival of BRCA-deficient cells.

Recent studies have shown that RAD52 is involved in multiple cellular events that support genome stability at all stages of the cell cycle. Figure 3 highlights these genome preserving activities of RAD52 in order of appearance through the cell cycle. While the relative importance of these activities to the BRCA/RAD52 synthetic lethality is debatable, all of them may contribute to survival of BRCA-deficient cells under different genetic and environmental circumstances.

Protection of DNA replication forks (Figure 3A):

RAD52 acts as a ‘gatekeeper’ during replication stress; it is recruited to ssDNA after replication fork stalling and stabilizes stalled forks by antagonizing fork reversal motor enzymes, such as SMARCAL1. First, RAD52 interacts with the parental ssDNA, i.e. with the gap at stalled fork and promotes a closed fork structure refractory to reversal (reviewed in). If the fork is reversed and the resulting four-way junction is digested in a MRE11-dependent manner, RAD52 interacts with the nascent ssDNA and prevents unregulated fork degradation, which in turn leads to fork restoration with large gaps. In the work by Malacaria and colleagues, replication stress was elicited by hydroxyurea. Similar dependence on RAD52, however, is expected with other types of replication stress, including oncogene overexpression. The exact structural/molecular mechanism(s) by which RAD52 reorganizes the fork to make it refractory to reversal, as well as what types of forks are protected and which are reversed remain to be determined. This function of RAD52 is likely to be at least in part responsible for BRCA/RAD52 synthetic lethality, but is not very important in the presence of functional machinery of HR that can protect stalled forks and restore broken forks, especially when HR-dependent fork protection is not overwhelmed due to replication stress. Notably, one of the consequences of RAD52 inhibition is generation of large ssDNA gaps upon recovery from replication stress, and it has been recently suggested that such replication gaps underlie pathology of BRCAness and the therapeutic response of BRCA-deficient cells.

Fork cleavage (Figure 3B):

If the replication stress persists, and the forks are stalled for prolonged period of time, RAD52 collaborates with MUS81 structure selective nuclease to cleave the fork producing single-ended DSBs. This process is a prerequisite of the fork repair by homologous recombination and is especially important when the G2/M cell cycle checkpoint is disabled. While collaborating with MUS81 in DNA replication forks cleavage, RAD52 seems to protect forks from EEPD1 nuclease which cleaves stalled DNA replication forks, as the toxicity of RAD52 depletion in BRCA-deficient cells is alleviated by EEPD1 depletion.

Single-strand annealing (Figure 3C):

While human RAD52 has only a minor function in HR, it plays a significant role in the potentially mutagenic single-strand annealing (SSA). In SSA, RAD52 binds to ssDNA or ssDNA-RPA complexes, and facilitates homology search and annealing of the complementary DNA strands. The RAD52-mediated annealing or the complementary or partially complementary sequences are likely to play an important role in survival of BRCA-deficient cells. Resected DSBs are committed to homology directed repair. These breaks can be repaired either by RAD52-mediated SSA or POLθ-mediated end joining (TMEJ) depending on the nature and location of the homology relative to the break, with RAD52 being effective in repairing breaks containing more than 50 nt of homology surrounded by non-homologous regions. The RAD52 annealing activity is enhanced by c-ABL mediated phosphorylation on Y104.

Antagonizing POLθ (Figure 3D):

Not only do RAD52 and POLθ work in parallel pathways repairing deleterious DSBs committed to homology-directed repair, along with BRCA2, RAD52 antagonizes POLθ and TMEJ in biochemical assays and in cells until the onset of mitosis. While this activity is unlikely to contribute to cell survival, it promotes genome stability by allowing more accurate mechanisms of DSB repair to act first.

Mitotic DNA synthesis (MIDAS) (Figure 3E):

RAD52 is crucial for promoting an early step in MiDAS. MiDAS, a pathway related to break-induced replication (BIR), repairs broken replication forks, under-replicated DNA or unprocessed common fragile sites (CFSs) that persist beyond S-phase. CFSs are difficult to replicate regions prone to breakage and chromosomal rearrangements under replication stress, a condition common in human cancers. High resolution mapping showed that sites where MiDAS takes place are common among various cell lines and encompass all known CFSs, which also map to large, highly transcribed, origin-poor genomic regions. When CFSs and their repair by MiDAS are triggered by a DNA polymerase inhibitor aphidicolin, RAD52 and not RAD51 or BRCA2 is retained on the chromatin; RAD52 foci co-localize with FANCD2 twin foci in early mitosis and recruits MUS81 and POLD3 to the CFCs. RAD52 depletion contributes to chromosomal instability observed as ultrafine DNA bridges and increased micronuclei in the daughter cells due to their inability to perform MiDAS. Notably, RAD52 function in initiating MiDAS may be more important in cancer cells than in non-cancerous counterparts, an observation that further enhances the RAD52 attractiveness as a drug target.

RAD52 interaction with RNA-DNA hybrids and RNA-templated DNA repair (Figure 3F):

One of the newest biochemical activities attributed to RAD52 is an inverse strand exchange whereby RAD52 binds to dsDNA and promotes strand exchange with homologous ssDNA or ssRNA molecule. This process contributed to the RNA-templated DNA repair which takes place in G0/G1 phase of the cell cycle, requires active transcription, R-loop sensing by CSB, and CSB-mediated recruitment of RAD52 to R-loops. RAD52 binding to the RNA-DNA hybrid within the R-loop is also important in S phase, where the R-loop bound RAD52 recruits XPG nuclease and BRCA1 to initiate transcription-associated homologous recombination repair, or when RAD52 is recruited by CSB to R-loops induced by oxidative damage, which are then repaired via transcription coupled but BRCA-independent homologous recombination. The CSB-RAD52 axis is also important for suppressing telomeric DNA breaks induced by reactive oxygen species. Similar to other types of R-loops, CSB senses the oxidative damage within R-loop and recruits RAD52, which in turn recruits POLD3 and initiates repair by BIR. This mechanism, however, is most important to ALT-positive cells experiencing oxidative stress. While involved in maintenance of genetic integrity, the processes described above are likely less important for survival of BRCA-deficient cells than the RAD52 functions in MiDAS and protection of damaged replication forks.

RAD52 EXPRESSION, PATHOGENESIS AND A PROGNOSTIC MARKER

Many cancer cells displayed enhanced DNA repair capacity, achieved though ATR-CHK1 control of E2F-dependent transcription. Along with many other DNA repair factors, RAD52 expression is induced by E2F1 activation. Its utility as a biomarker remains complex.

Ho and colleagues investigated if RAD52 expression in a cohort of rectal cancer patients could be used as a prognostic marker to indicate the rate of treatment success following surgical resection or pretreatment with a chemotherapeutic drug 5FU. A Kaplan-Meier curve demonstrated both worse overall survival and worse disease-free survival in patients that had high RAD52 expression in the tumor center compared to patients that had low RAD52 expression in the tumor center. However, they did not report any significant change in the overall and disease-free survival in the patients that had RAD52 expression localized to the tumor periphery. Notably, post receiving neoadjuvant therapy, patients with lower RAD52 expression had worse overall and disease-free survival scores.

Another study analyzed RAD52 expression in hepatocellular carcinomas (HCC) reported in the TCGA database. Among 413 tissue samples, RAD52 expression was significantly higher in tumor tissues in comparison to normal tissues. However, there was no significant impact on overall survival reported based on varying RAD52 expression levels.

RAD52 overexpression in a tumorigenic cell line Huh-7 significantly promoted cell proliferation and migration. Unlike RAD52 overexpression contributing to poor overall survival and relapse free survival in melanoma cells, the authors could not find any evidence to support the same claim in HCC. In a similar exploration of the TCGA database, Lieberman et al. showed a significant association between amplification of the genomic locus 12p13.33 containing the RAD52 gene, somatic RAD52 overexpression and development of lung squamous cell carcinoma Taken together these studies suggest that reliance on RAD52 may vary across different cancer subtypes and warrants further analysis.

RAD52 expression related SNP and miRNA analysis can also be used as a prognostic tool of the lifetime risk of developing cancer. Cao colleagues examined how potential regulatory variants could affect the expression of key DNA repair proteins, RAD51, RAD52 and XRCC2 in breast cancer patients. They found that nucleotide variations in the RAD52 SNPs (rs7963551 and rs1051669) impacted the individual’s risk for developing breast cancer. Indeed, individuals with variations AC (OR 0.684, 95%CI 0.492–0.951) and CC (OR 0.317, 95%CI 0.200–0.503) of rs7963551 in RAD52 had a significantly lower risk of breast cancer in codominant model; AC + CC (OR 0.568, 95%CI 0.415–0.778) and CC (OR 0.401, 95%CI 0.265–0.606) genotypes of rs7963551 in RAD52 showed decreased risk of breast cancer in dominant model and recessive model, respectively. The study also categorized patients based on binary variables such as age, menopausal status and number of pregnancies in order to further analyze the selected SNPs and breast cancer susceptibility. This analysis highlighted the protective effect of rs7963551 (AC+ CC genotype) (details of subgroups are provided in Table 1) as well as indicated that premenopausal women with AG+AA genotype of rs1051669 were at increased risk of breast cancer.

RAD52 AS A MARKER OF TREATMENT RESISTANCE

Although RAD52 expression has never consistently been shown to improve or reduce overall survival in patients, it can indicate treatment resistance. Shi et al., investigated the expression of RAD52 variants and their influence on platinum therapy resistance and treatment outcome in patients with cervical cancer. SNPs in the 5’ and 3’ UTR of RAD52 were assessed for correlation with platinum therapy resistance in patients with cervical cancer. Overall, rs1051669A and rs10774474T variant alleles were significantly associated with platinum resistance. It was also shown that cancer cells homozygous for these variants had lower responses to carboplatin and nedaplatin, respectively. Assuming a recessive genetic model, it was found that patients who had the rs10774474 TT genotype had a significantly increased resistance to nedaplatin in comparison to those who carried AA/AT genotypes (Table 2).

Although an independent association of a single variant, alone, with patients′ survival was not observed, the data showed that when all three of the RAD52 SNPs were considered together, patients with at least one variant allele had a worse overall survival as compared to patients who did not have any of the SNP variants.

PHARMACOLOGICAL INHIBITORS OF RAD52

Currently, there are no FDA approved small molecule inhibitors of RAD52 in use or in clinical trials. Initial steps in discovery of specific inhibitors can utilize in vitro biochemical, computational or cell based approaches. The detailed structure activity relationship in RAD52 along with the biomedical importance of various RAD52 biochemical and cellular activities is still being established. Several inhibitors identified to date suggested two biochemical activities, ssDNA binding and oligomerization interface can be targeted.

6-hydroxy-dopa:

In a screen of a diverse set of 18,304 drug-like compounds and the Sigma Lopac collection of 1,280 pharmacologically active compounds, Chandramouly et al. identified 6-hydroxy-dopa (6-OH-dopa; a precursor of the catecholaminergic neurotoxin 6-hydroxydopamine) as a RAD52 inhibitor targeting RAD52 oligomerization interface, preventing the RAD52-ssDNA interaction in vitro and SSA in a cell-based reporter assay. In addition, 6-OH-dopa selectively inhibited growth of human BRCA1 deficient HCC1937 and BRCA2 deficient Capan-1 and Chinese hamster VC8 cells. The IC50 for RAD52 inhibition in vitro was 1.1 µM, but much higher concentrations of the compound were needed to kill BRCA-deficient cells.

Epigallocatechin:

In a biophysical in vitro screen of MicroSource SPECTRUM collection (3168 compounds) Hengel et al. identified a naturally occurring polyphenol, Epigallocatechin (EGC; IC50 ∼1 µM), as an inhibitor of the RAD52-ssDNA interaction and DNA annealing. While several compounds were identified in this screen, EGC was the only one that inhibited RAD52 binding to ssDNA and ssDNA-RPA complex and annealing of RPA-coated ssDNA without disrupting the RPA-ssDNA interaction. EGC was able to stimulate cell death in BRCA2-depleted cells, thereby showcasing its ability to selectively target tumorigenic cells. Later studies showed that EGC inhibits RAD52 activity at stalled replication fork highlighting the importance of the RAD52-ssDNA interaction in replication fork protection. Along with two other RAD52 inhibitors discussed below, EGC was used in cell-based studies and mouse xenografts to establish that DNA repair helicase RECQ4 antagonizes RAD52 and SSA. While useful as a tool for understanding RAD52 biology, EGC and other polyphenolic compounds display poor drug like properties and are not amenable to optimization.

Quinolones:

Huang et al. employed a fluorescence-quenching assay that followed ssDNA annealing by RAD52, yielding two inhibitors (DI03 and D-G23; affinity values of 26 and 34 µM, respectively), structural analogs with quinolone cores as their central scaffold. These compounds inhibited growth of BRCA1 and BRCA2-deficient cells. Both compounds represent privileged scaffolds in drug discovery and are amenable to synthetic modification but lack desirable lead-like properties.

AICAR:

Sullivan et al., 2016, conducted a virtual screening study, examining approximately 140,000 compounds that focused on a small region of the large DNA binding groove of RAD52. Nine lead compounds were identified which could compete with DNA for binding to RAD52. The only compound that could effectively inhibit DNA binding and had cellular activity was the natural product: 5-aminoimidazole-4-carboxamide ribonucleotide (AICAR) 5’-phosphate (AMP). An attractive feature of AICAR is that it can permeate cells and becomes phosphorylated in the cytoplasm, enabling a selectivity in killing BRCA-deficient cells, while not impacting the growth of BRCA-proficient cells. The efficacy of AICAR or its specificity towards RAD52 was not reported.

CONCLUSION

Establishment of the synthetic lethal relationships for BRCA/RAD52 and ATM/RAD52 has brought RAD52 in spotlight resulting in identification of the new biochemical activities and cellular functions of RAD52 (Figure 2). The many cellular functions and structure-activity relationship in the RAD52 DNA repair protein, however, remain enigmatic. Full mechanistic understanding of these activities and the interconnecting genome maintenance pathways utilizing or surrounding RAD52 will be instrumental in its development as an attractive anticancer drug target. The discovery of several compounds that inhibit RAD52 in vitro, and selectively kill BRCA-deficient cells is an encouraging development which highlights the feasibility of small molecule therapeutics that target RAD52.
