# Current status and research progress of oncolytic virus.

## Abstract

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are natural or genetically recombinant viruses that selectively infect and kill tumor cells without affecting normal cell growth. As a novel type of immunotherapy, OVs have been shown to activate antitumor immune responses, regulate the tumor microenvironment, and enhance the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors. In this review article, we discuss the latest research on the characteristics, antitumor mechanisms, and status of OV research. In terms of mechanism of action, after targeting tumor cells, OVs not only directly lyse tumor cells but also exert antitumor effects through indirect approaches. As an emerging cancer treatment, OVs face challenges that need to be overcome. Finally, we summarize the challenges and prospects for the future application of OVs.

Introduction

Malignant tumors pose a serious threat to human health. Traditional clinical treatments for tumors primarily include surgical resection, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy. However, these methods are associated with certain intractable limitations owing to the complicated and diverse tumor microenvironment (TME). In addition, conventional therapies occasionally induce severe damage to normal tissues, resulting in treatment discontinuation and an increased rate of tumor recurrence. Therefore, it is crucial to develop better therapeutic strategies.

Immunotherapy, a revolutionary treatment strategy in the field of tumor therapy, utilizes the patient's immune system to kill tumors and prevent their recurrence, eliciting high specificity, high killing efficiency, and low toxicity. Owing to the complex immunosuppressive mechanisms of tumors, the progress of tumor immunotherapy has been excessively slow throughout the 1900s. More than 20 years ago, the discovery of the tumor immune escape mechanism mediated by programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) and programmed death 1 (PD-1) sparked a new era in this field. Currently, most immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) target T cells to reactivate or enhance the antitumor immune response. Adoptive cell therapy, represented by chimeric antigen receptor T-cell immunotherapy (CAR-T), has gradually become a major pillar of tumor immunotherapy in recent years, affording remarkable clinical efficacy in B-cell lymphoma and leukemia. With the evolution of tumor immunotherapy, there are increasingly diverse methods available for treating tumors, and additional treatment strategies are under investigation to identify optimal solutions.

As a novel tumor immunotherapeutic approach, oncolytic virotherapy utilizes dual mechanisms, i.e., direct and indirect, to achieve oncolytic effects. Most oncolytic viruses (OVs) used clinically are modified from attenuated or nonpathogenic strains that can selectively infect tumor cells and exert their oncolytic effects without inducing serious side effects. Despite minimal evidence suggesting the potential of oncolytic virotherapy to induce serious medical accidents, its limitations in terms of addressing urgent malignant tumors need to be resolved.

In this review article, we combined the classification and structure of common oncolytic drugs, both domestically and internationally, in recent years, emphasizing the dual oncolytic mechanisms of OVs and evaluating the challenges and prospects faced by existing oncolytic drug applications. Importantly, this review highlights the clinical value of OVs.

The characteristics and classification of OVs

OV are natural or artificially modified viruses that can utilize their own characteristics to achieve oncolytic effects. It is well-recognized that OVs exert their effect through two mechanisms: direct and indirect oncolysis. In direct oncolysis, OVs utilize tumor surface markers to target tumor cells and replicate within cells, directly mediating cell lysis or apoptosis. During indirect oncolysis, OVs activate the immune response by releasing tumor-related antigens, progeny viral particles, and other cytokines through tumor cell lysis. Based on differences in genetic material, OVs can be divided into DNA viruses (such as adenovirus [AdV], herpesvirus, poxvirus, and parvovirus) and RNA viruses (such as paramyxovirus, ortho-reovirus, and picornavirus RNA virus). To date, most clinical studies have focused on DNA OVs as experimental subjects owing to their stable double-helical structure, numerous conserved sequences, and large genomic manipulation spaces. Although RNA OVs have an unstable genome and are difficult to modify, their oncolytic efficiency and effects are stronger than those of DNA OVs, and the clinical research exploring RNA OVs is gradually increasing. This section provides a detailed introduction to the characteristics and applications of several commonly used DNA and RNA OVs in clinical research, and briefly explains their mechanisms of action based on their structural components.

DNA OVs

AdV

AdV is a non-enveloped virus containing a 26–48 kb double-stranded linear DNA genome, which includes four early genes (E1-E4) and five late genes (L1-L5). Early gene replication initiates viral DNA replication and transcription, whereas late genes encode the structural proteins of the virus. Mutation or deletion of E1A, the most important transcriptional activator of viral gene expression, frequently induces viral replication disorders in host cells and is often used as a point of modification.

VB-111 is a vascular-targeted therapeutic drug that can be used to treat recurrent tumors. Based on the type 5 AdV genome with E1A deficiency, VB-111 carries a modified mouse endothelin precursor promoter and Fas chimeric (Fas-c) genes. Notably, E1A deficiency allows VB-111 to specifically infect tumor cells, and the Fas-c gene enables VB-111 to mediate apoptosis of tumor angiogenic endothelial cells through the Fas pathway, thereby inhibiting tumor angiogenesis and destroying tumor blood vessels.

DNX-2401 (Delta-24-RGD) is a modified oncolytic AdV used to treat high-grade malignant gliomas. Based on the type 5 Adv genome, DNX-2401 was obtained by deleting a partial base in the E1A region responsible for binding to the Rb protein and inserting the RGD-4C sequence (ACDCRGDCFC) into the genome. Incomplete deletion of the E1A gene allows DNX-2401 to replicate only in Rb-deficient tumor cells. The RGD-4C gene enhances the infectivity of DNX-2401 and enables it to target integrins on the surface of tumor cell membranes, thus facilitating cell entry for replication and production of offspring particles. DNX-2401 lyses cells and promotes tumor immune cell infiltration to release tumor-related antigens.

H101 (oncoline) is a recombinant type 5 AdV injection used to treat refractory advanced nasopharyngeal carcinoma. H101 was obtained by knocking out an 827 bp sequence in the E1B–55K region and introducing several point mutations to produce a premature stop-coding region. Partial E1B deletion causes H101 to proliferate in tumor cells specifically. Relevant viral proteins can affect cell cycle progression and mediate apoptosis. In addition, H101 could mediate cellular autophagy to enhance tumor immune cell infiltration and promote an oncolytic effect.

CG0070 is a modified conditionally replicating type 5 AdV driven by the human E2F-1 (Early 2 transcription factor-1) promoter to express the E1 virus gene. In addition, the expression of granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) enables CG0070 to induce specific long-term antitumor responses. Lysis of infected tumor cells releases viral particles and the tumor-associated antigen GM-CSF, stimulating and enhancing antitumor immune response.

Herpesvirus

Herpes viruses are a large group of ubiquitous viruses that infect humans and animals, and herpes simplex virus (HSV) is the most studied herpes virus. HSV is an enveloped, double-stranded, linear DNA virus with a genome size of 152 kb, with most of its genes, including RL1, UL39, and US12, capable of endowing HSV with characteristics of immune escape. RL1 is the main neuroviral gene of HSV, and its product ICP34.5 can dephosphorylate eukaryotic initiation factor 2α (eIF-2α) to reverse the blockade of protein synthesis by inhibiting Beclin-1 or the interferon (IFN) signaling pathway. UL39 is responsible for encoding the main subunit of HSV ribonucleotide reductase (RR); its product ICP6 can inhibit apoptosis caused by tumor necrosis factor (TNF) α/TNF receptor 1 (TNFR1) and necroptosis induced by FasL (Fas ligand)/Fas by interacting with caspase 8. The product ICP47 of US12 can induce immune escape by downregulating the expression of major histocompatibility complex I (MHC I) and blocking transporters associated with antigen presentation (TAP) in host cells.

G207 is a modified HSV-1 therapeutic drug clinically used to treat malignant tumors of the central nervous system, including neuroblastoma or malignant glioma. Researchers deleted two copies of the RL1 gene and inserted the Escherichia coli lacZ gene into UL39 cells. Mutations in RL1 and UL39 allow G207 to specifically replicate in tumor cells, producing viral particles and tumor-associated antigens (TAA) that strongly enhance the body's immune response.

G47Δ is a modified oncolytic HSV-1 developed for treating glioblastoma or other neurocentral malignancies. Based on G207, researchers additionally knocked out the US12 gene to further weaken the replication ability of G47Δ in normal cells and the inhibitory effect on the synthesis of MHC Ⅰ in tumor cells.

Talimogene laherparepvec (T-Vec) is the first commercially available genetically modified HSV-1 approved by the US FDA to treat melanomas that cannot be comprehensively cured surgically. T-Vec was obtained by deleting RL1 and US12 genes, followed by the insertion of the GM-CSF gene. Deletion of the RL1 gene inhibits the neurotoxicity of T-Vec in normal cells, while deletion of the US12 gene and insertion of the GM-CSF gene promotes the antigen presentation ability of infected tumor cells and recruitment of antigen-presenting cells (APCs) to the TME.

VG2025 is a modified HSV-1 drug used to treat malignant tumors. VG2025 was obtained by combining a chemokine C-X-C motif receptor 4 (CXCR4) promoter with immunomodulatory cytokine genes (IL-12, IL-15, and IL-15Rα) to enhance the oncolytic efficacy and promote the immune response of the host. To enhance the selective expression of the IE gene in tumor cells, a tumor-specific carcinoembryonic antigen promoter was inserted at the transcription regulatory control. At the translational level, repeated binding sites for microRNA-124 and microRNA-143 were added into the 3’ untranslated region of the RL1 gene to confine virus replication in normal cells, eliciting good safety. Genetic modification of VG2025 is based on transcription and translation dual regulation, which can enhance its oncolytic effect and therapeutic level without damaging normal tissues. In addition, the glycoprotein B gene was deleted to promote intercellular fusion in tumor cells, thereby further improving the virulence, persistence, and tumor-killing abilities of VG2025.

Poxvirus

Poxviruses are enveloped viruses with a 130–330 kb double-stranded linear DNA. In the family of poxviruses, vaccinia virus (VV) is one of the most extensively investigated OVs owing to its unique extranuclear replication. VV has two antigenically different viral particles: an intracellular mature virus (IMV) and an extracellular enveloped virus (EEV). Infected cells generate only a small portion of EEV in progeny VV, and the envelope surface of the EEV is distributed with the host complement regulatory protein (CRP), which can evade the complement system and prolong survival. However, most progeny viruses are CRP-free IMVs, which are easily cleared by the host immune system, weakening their oncolytic effect.

JX-594 (Pexavec) is a replicable modified VV with three alterations in its genome. Jx-594 was obtained by inserting Lac-Z into the thymidine kinase (TK) gene to enable specific replication in tumor cells with high TK expression, along with the addition of GM-CSF genes to mediate a stronger immune response.

Lee et al. designed another oncolytic drug, SJ-607, based on JX-594, capable of exerting a better therapeutic effect on tumors than JX-594. SJ-607 was obtained by fusing human CD55 protein to the transmembrane domains of type I membrane proteins, which can produce IMV-containing human CD55 protein in infected cells to evade complement attacks and achieve prolonged survival, thereby eliciting superior oncolytic effects.

CF33hNIS (VAXINIA) is a novel chimeric smallpox virus developed for injection into solid tumors. CF33 was obtained via the homologous recombination of nine types of smallpox viruses and insertion of the human sodium iodide cotransporter (hNIS) gene into the J2R gene, enabling CF33 to image, track, and mediate targeted radiotherapy in vivo.

Parvovirus

Parvoviruses (PV) are single-stranded DNA non-enveloped viruses whose genome naturally undergoes small deletions and mutations to adapt to the physiological cellular environment. The rat PV, H–1PV (also known as ParvOryx), a type of OV, has been extensively studied. The H–1PV genome comprises two transcriptional components, NS and VP, regulated by the early P4 and late P38 promoters, respectively. Given that the natural host of H–1PV is rats, it is nonpathogenic to humans. Reportedly, H–1PV cannot replicate in normal human tissues, although it can effectively infect various human cancer cell lines and induce an oncolytic effect. Notably, the efficacy of H–1PV has been demonstrated in several preclinical animal experiments.

Although DNA OVs possess characteristics such as stability, safety, and ease of modification, their large genetic composition complicates the treatment of central nervous system tumors across the blood-brain barrier (BBB). RNA OVs have a smaller structure and are more likely to cross the BBB to treat central nervous system tumors, rendering them more suitable for systemic administration. In addition, RNA OVs have higher oncolytic efficiency than DNA OVs and can reduce the viral dose for treatment without impairing efficacy, given their smaller genome and cytoplasmic replication mechanism. Therefore, RNA OVs have great application value and are worth exploring as promising antitumor-specific drug candidates. Current clinical research on RNA OVs primarily focuses on Paramyxoviridae, orthoreoviruses, and picornavirus RNA viruses.

RNA OVs

Paramyxoviridae

Newcastle Disease virus (NDV) is a single-stranded negative RNA-enveloped avian paramyxovirus serotype 1 (APMV-1) whose genome encodes six types of structural proteins: large polymerase (L), hemagglutinin neuraminidase (HN), fusion (F), matrix (M), phosphoprotein (P), and nucleoprote@@ (NP). Among these, the F protein is the key factor present on the NDV surface that enables selective invasion. Upon binding of the F protein to the cell surface marker, the NDV can specifically infect target cells through membrane fusion or endocytosis. NDV reportedly exerts its immunostimulatory and oncolytic effects through the following methods: 1) specific targeting of tumor cells, 2) promoting the spread of the virus via exosomes and syncytia, and 3) suppressing glycolytic pathways and mediating autophagy in tumor cells.

Measles virus (MV) is a highly contagious single-stranded RNA-enveloped virus. Compared with other single-stranded RNA viruses, the MV genome is non-segmental, which makes its genes more stable, thereby resulting in a lower mutation rate. Most clinical studies on oncolytic MV currently employ an attenuated vaccine strain derived from the MV-EDM vaccine lineage as the object of renovation, demonstrating good safety and efficacy in both in vitro and in vivo experiments. MV utilizes hemagglutinin protein (H) on the envelope surface to recognize and bind to the host cell biomarkers CD46, CD150, or lectin-4R, entering cells through F to replicate in the cytoplasm. Although these targets are expressed at low levels in normal cells, they are overexpressed in tumor cells. In addition to directly using the original MV as an oncolytic drug, researchers have also inserted different therapeutic genes into the MV, such as the BNiP3 gene, to enhance its antitumor effect and apoptosis-inducing capacity.

Orthoreovirus

Reoviruses are double-stranded RNA-non-enveloped viruses with a genome encompassing 10 fragments. Mutations in the KRAS, BRAF, and EGFR genes allow reoviruses to proliferate preferentially in tumor cells with abnormal Ras signaling pathways. Currently, four types of reovirus have been identified, i.e., type 1 Lang, type 2 Jones, type 3 Dearing, and type 4 Ndelle, all of which exhibit distinct antisera capabilities.

Reolysin is an unmodified OV isolated from type 3 nonpathogenic reovirus strains and is suitable to treat advanced malignant tumors, such as glioma, melanoma, ovarian cancer, and pancreatic cancer.

Pelareorep is an intravenous reovirus injection based on reolysin, deemed suitable for most solid and hematological malignancies. Pelareorep selectively replicates in tumor cells, releasing viral particles and TAAs from lysed cells, potentially activating the innate and adaptive immune responses and reversing the immune escape induced by tumor cells.

Picornaviruses RNA virus

Rigvir is a wild-type ECHO-7 strain without an envelope used to treat melanoma. As an immunomodulatory agent, rigvir exhibits characteristics of tumor affinity and oncolysis and has been widely used in clinical treatment in Latvia.

Coxsackievirus (CV) is a nonenveloped, single-stranded, positive RNA (ss ​+ ​RNA) virus considered a promising oncolytic drug candidate. Cavatak (CVA21) is an OV isolated from the wild-type Kuykendall strain, which has shown efficacy in treating various malignant tumors. CVA21 typically interacts with intercellular adhesion molecule 1 and decay accelerating factor receptors that are overexpressed on the tumor cell surface, entering tumor cells to exert oncolytic effects.

In summary, these natural or modified OVs selectively proliferate in tumor cells and are involved in cell lysis through their unique gene products. Following tumor cell lysis, viral particles and TAAs are released to activate the immune system to further kill tumors. In addition, OVs do not induce serious damage to normal tissues and afford better efficacy than traditional methods employed to clinically treat malignant tumors. Targeting mechanisms of OVs

Intratumoral injection is the most commonly used method for OV administration. However, the low diffusion of OVs in tumors limits their area of action and results in uneven drug concentrations. For large and multiple tumors, multi-site injection is often required, increasing the number of operations, and direct intratumoral viral injection is inappropriate in these conditions. The use of viruses with high specificity to achieve systemic targeted drug delivery via intravenous channels can avoid the drawbacks of intratumoral injection and ensure the effective killing of tumor cells without damaging normal cells. Natural OVs lack the ability for accurate targeting; therefore, modified OVs are typically designed via genetic engineering to maximize their function. Natural or modified viruses can replicate in tumor cells specifically via the following pathways: (1) Gene mutations cause OVs to replicate in tumor cells. For example, the human AdV E1B gene encodes a 55 kDa protein (E1B55K) that binds and inactivates p53, following which the AdV proliferates in cells. Bischoff et al. remodeled mutant AdVs that do not express this viral protein owing to the loss of ability to inactivate p53, thereby suppressing their ability to replicate and lyse in functional p53 cells while selectively replicating and lysing in p53 deficient human tumor cells. (2) Tumor-specific promoters drive the expression of OV genes that are crucial for robust replication. For instance, Yue et al. constructed a conditionally replicative AdV (CRAd), in which the E1A promoter was replaced by another promoter, MUC16. MUC16 encodes CA-125, one of the largest, hyperglycosylated human proteins expressed in and shed from ovarian cancer cells, and is expressed at a low level in most other patients with cancer, as well as individuals without cancer. The modified virus has been shown to selectively inhibit CA-125+human ovarian cancer cells. (3) A deficiency in the antiviral response of tumor cells is conducive to OV infection and replication. In this context, the tumor specificity of several OVs largely depends on the inability of cancer cells to produce an effective antiviral response and abnormal conditions, such as the absence of the type I IFN signaling pathway or protein kinase R (PKR) activity, allowing the virus to infect cells. (4) Selective expression of certain molecules in the TME promotes OV gene expression. For instance, hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)-1 mediates transcriptional activation of target genes by binding to hypoxia response elements (HRE) and hypoxia response promoters (HRPs) located in its regulatory region under hypoxic conditions. The POST team constructed a hypoxia/HIF-dependent replicating AdV with viral replication genes regulated by HRE or HRP. This type of OV can replicate only in an environment where HIF exists (e.g., hypoxic environments). Unlike tumor cells, conventional cells cannot survive in hypoxic environments; therefore, AdVs are used to target tumor cells. (5) Tumor-targeting viral vectors deliver OV. For example, mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) exhibit several advantages as an OV vector. MSCs can specifically target tumor cells and are relatively easy to isolate, amplify, and modify in vitro. Mechanisms of OV-mediated killing

OVs can effectively kill infected cancer cells via multiple pathways, which can be divided into three categories: direct oncolysis of cancer cells, indirect killing of uninfected cancer cells, and others. Direct killing utilizes the characteristics of the OV and tumor cells and does not rely on extracellular factors to kill tumor cells. OVs selectively replicate in tumor cells and induce cell death through apoptosis, necrosis, or autophagy (Fig. 3). In addition to directly lysing tumor cells, OV can also exert an indirect antitumor action, which is manifested by activating the immune system of the body to fight against tumor cells. Long-term immunological memory can be generated through an indirect mechanism and is crucial for preventing tumor recurrence and metastasis (Fig. 4).

Induction of tumor cell apoptosis through multiple pathways

Apoptosis, also known as programmed cell death, is an initial cell death process controlled by genes and is mainly divided into the endogenous (mitochondrial pathway), exogenous (death receptor pathway), and endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress-induced apoptosis pathways. However, tumor cells can resist apoptosis at different levels, complicating the treatment of malignant tumors. The ability of OV to induce apoptosis can facilitate the elimination of tumor cells.

Upon stimulation by oxidative stress, calcium ions, and certain proteases, the mitochondria release Cyt C, which binds to Apaf-1 and ATP and subsequently to pro-caspase 9 to form the protein complex apoptosome. This is followed by the cleavage of pro-caspase 9, releasing active cleaved caspase 9. Subsequently, Caspase 3 is activated, leading to mitochondrial apoptosis. Tian et al. infected cancer cells with oncolytic AdV ZD55-VEGI-251 and revealed that ZD55-VEGI-251 induced the cleavage of caspase 9. By measuring the mitochondrial membrane potential, the authors reported that ZD55-VEGI-251 could substantially reduce the mitochondrial membrane potential, causing mitochondria to release Cyt C to the cytoplasm. These results indicate that ZD55-VEGI-251 enhances apoptosis in tumor cells via the mitochondria-mediated apoptotic pathway. Reportedly, NDV markedly upregulates the expression levels of Caspase-3 and -9 genes in infected breast cancer cells, thereby indicating that OV can induce apoptosis in tumor cells via the mitochondrial pathway.

Death receptors from the TNF receptor family, such as Fas and TRAIL, recruit Caspase 8 to form a death-induced signaling complex (DISC) upon activation. DISC activates Caspase 8, which directly activates Caspase 3 and 7 and ultimately induces cell apoptosis. A previous study found that ORFV can activate Caspase 3 and cleave poly ADP ribose polymerase (PARP), eventually leading to apoptosis. Elankumaran et al. found that NDV infection triggered the upregulation of soluble TRAIL in CF1 cells. These findings suggested that TRAIL-mediated apoptosis of the death receptor pathway occurred in tumor cells in the advanced stages of NDV infection. Additionally, the binding of the NDV strain AF2240 HN surface protein to the sialic acid receptor was found to trigger the extrinsic apoptotic pathway via the release of inflammatory TNF-α and TRAIL cytokines. The binding of these cytokines to their respective death receptors induces downstream apoptotic signaling through the Fas-associated death domain. In addition, recombinant oncolytic AdVs carrying TRAIL exhibited better efficacy than unmodified oncolytic AdVs in lung cancer models. Using recombinant oncolytic AdV infection of triple-negative breast cancer cells carrying the TRAIL gene, Zhu et al. demonstrated the same phenomenon of increased TRAIL and apoptosis in infected tumor cells. Accordingly, these results indicate that OV can induce apoptotic pathways triggered by death-inducible receptors, such as TRAIL, to exert an antitumor role, suggesting that TRAIL expression increases the potency of oncolytic AdV by enhancing apoptotic cell death.

During prolonged ER stress, IRE1, PERK, and ATF6 can induce pro-apoptotic signaling by activating C/EBP homologous proteins (CHOP), subsequently leading to the initiation of ER stress-mediated apoptosis. Simultaneously, the Caspase 12 proenzyme can be activated and transported to the cytoplasm, thus activating Caspase 9 and 3, leading to cell apoptosis. According to Jia et al. infection with the VV reduced the expression of anti-apoptotic proteins in hepatoma cells; activated caspase 12, 9, 7, and 3 and PARP; and dramatically downregulated the expression of ER stress-related proteins BiP and CHOP. These findings indicate that VV could induce tumor cell apoptosis via the ER pathway.

Tumor cell lysis via necrosis

Necrosis is characterized by cell lysis owing to the loss of cell membrane integrity, which can be attributed to pathological factors, resulting in morphological characteristics such as organelle swelling, plasma membrane rupture, and subsequent loss of intracellular substances. After the virus selectively replicates in tumor cells, OV inhibits protein synthesis and consumes energy from cancer cells, rendering tumor cells unable to meet their own growth needs and destroying the normal physiological balance of tumor cells. Numerous viruses are also capable of lysing cells, releasing more OV, and infecting more tumor cells, thereby resulting in a cascade reaction. Although the necrosis mechanism underlying the effects of OVs is yet to be comprehensively elucidated, it is clear that OVs can lyse tumor cells after infection. In theory, OVs can destroy all cancer cells unless the virus is cleared by the immune system prior to cancer cell elimination. In addition to directly eliminating viable tumors, necrosis is an immunogenic form of cell death that is beneficial for initiating a systemic immune response.

Autophagy-mediated inhibition of tumor cell growth

Autophagy-associated genes (ATG) are activated when cells are placed in a nutrient-deficient environment or stimulated by relevant stimuli. The expression level of p62, an auxiliary indicator of autophagy, is known to be inversely proportional to autophagy activity. Beciln1, an autophagy precursor factor involved in regulating autophagosome formation to induce autophagy, is not expressed in several tumor cells. In a study by Zhang et al., hepatocellular carcinoma infected with a novel oncolytic AdV demonstrated a marked tendency to downregulate P62 protein expression and upregulate Beclin 1 expression, whereas chloroquine-mediated inhibition of autophagy enhanced the survival rate of hepatocarcinoma stem cells; these findings indicated that OV inhibited cell growth by increasing autophagy levels. Atg 5 participates in autophagosome formation and is a marker of autophagy initiation. Jiang et al. observed that after adenoviral infection of brain cancer stem cells, the expression of the Atg12-Atg5 complex began increasing, leading to considerable autophagy and inhibition of tumor cell growth. ORFV reportedly downregulates AKT phosphorylation and mediates autophagy by inhibiting the AKT-mTOR pathway. More importantly, certain viruses can adapt to cellular environments and promote viral replication through autophagy. The addition of 3-methyladenine (3-MA) to inhibit autophagy reduces the synthesis of AdV structural proteins, whereas rapamycin can promote autophagy, resulting in increased AdV production, thereby reflecting a positive correlation between viral replication and AdV-induced autophagy, which may generate nutrients that can be used to construct viral progeny particles. An increase in the OV content can promote the further death of tumor cells.

Altering the immune status of the TME

Cancer cells secrete various factors, such as IL-10 and transforming growth factor-β, that inhibit the body's immune system to promote tumor development and metastasis. These cytokines recruit immunosuppressive cells to the tumor site, resulting in a complex TME, maintaining the body in an immunosuppressive state. OVs can reverse the immunosuppressive TME. OVs transform the immunosuppressive environment into an antitumor immune response environment via the local release of IFN, Toll-like receptor agonists, danger-associated molecular pattern (DAMP), and pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP) factors.

Induction of antitumor immune response

OVs lyse tumor cells to release tumor-specific antigens, which, in turn, induce immune cells to produce type I IFNs and immunostimulatory molecules and activate the antigen presentation signaling pathway, thereby enhancing the antigen presentation ability of APCs. Reportedly, VV can enhance the activity of dendritic cells and macrophages and induce macrophage polarization toward the M1 subtype, which is responsible for antigen presentation and immune surveillance and has antitumor ability. Moreover, VV has been shown to promote the expression of MHCI molecules and the recruitment of BATF3+ dendritic cells. BATF3+ dendritic cells can phagocytose soluble tumor antigens and migrate to regional lymph nodes, triggering adaptive T cell responses to tumors and promoting the infiltration of innate and cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) into tumor infection sites. CTL can recognize and kill tumor cells, as well as target distant tumor cells. This involves the “in situ vaccine” and “abscopal effect” in antitumor immunity. Additionally, some lysogenic viruses can exert antitumor effects against their own components. The HN protein of the NDV was found to remove sialic acid from the surface of tumor cells, exposing tumor cell surface antigens and promoting recognition by T cells. Long and short gene fragments of the reovirus dsRNA reportedly activate two pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), MDA5 and RIG-I. PRRs activate IFN-I secretion by immune cells, which contributes to the recruitment of dendritic and natural killer cells.

Destruction of tumor blood vessels

Tumor growth depends on the nutrients provided by tumor blood vessels; therefore, the destruction of tumor blood vessels could inhibit tumor growth. OVs can destroy tumor blood vessels. Studies have shown that intravenously administered vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV)-type OVs can directly infect and destroy tumor blood vessels in vivo without affecting normal blood vessels. After entering the body, VSV-type OVs specifically destroy tumor blood vessels, further facilitating extensive viral replication in tumor cells. Subsequently, the virus recruits neutrophils and other immune cells to the infected tumor site, initiating neutrophil-mediated inflammation in the body and eventually leading to microthrombi formation in tumor blood vessels. Microthrombi can affect the nutrient supply of tumor blood vessels, thereby killing tumor cells.

Challenge and prospects

Based on multiple iterative improvements, the design and development of OVs have advanced substantially, with great prospects. However, several challenges need to be addressed prior to the broad application of oncolytic virotherapy, mainly due to the following three reasons.

Limited administration approaches

Most currently approved oncolytic viral drugs are used to treat malignant gliomas, nasopharyngeal carcinomas, and melanomas. Moreover, intratumoral injection is the most common approach for OV administration. Previous preclinical and clinical studies have shown that intratumoral injection of OVs is effective, although the efficacy against disseminated and metastatic tumors remains insufficient. Systemic administration (e.g., intravenous injection), a notable method for second-generation oncolytic viral drug delivery, has obvious advantages for metastatic tumors. Upon injecting viruses intravenously, OVs must cross the endothelial cell and tumor envelope layers to exert their antitumor effects effectively. For intracranial tumors, intravenous administration must overcome the BBB to reach the tumor bed and exert its effects. As another method of local delivery, intracavity administration can be adapted for angiosarcomas and epithelioid sarcomas. However, most of these studies are in the preclinical animal experimental stage.

Therefore, exploring new, safe delivery methods to improve the effectiveness of OVs is urgently required to expand the clinical indications for oncolytic drugs. To address this issue, Srinivasan et al. employed selective intra-arterial administration technology with high-permeability fluid to cross the BBB, promoting the local aggregation of MSC-carrying OV Delta-24-RGD (MSC-D24) in gliomas. This method was found to effectively dissolve animal tumors. Cell-based delivery carriers are among the most promising carriers for OV delivery. When hidden in cell vectors, OVs can evade recognition by the host immune system. MSCs and neural stem cells have been shown to possess tumor-homing properties and have been exploited to deliver OVs to tumors. In the future, the intravascular delivery of OVs may be achieved via encapsulation within new materials, such as exosomes, liposomes, or other new materials that do not damage the blood vessel endothelium. A new drug delivery system will undoubtedly further expand the scope of OV applications.

Potential risk of viral infection

Although accumulated data s does not indicate substantial safety issues, the risk of OV infection remains inevitable. OVs rely on infections to activate the immune response of patients, thereby killing tumors. Given that most OV drugs are yet to be established as mainstream cancer therapies, patients receiving OV treatment may have already received multiple traditional cancer treatments. Therefore, patients with compromised immune systems are at higher risk of viral infection. In addition, most OV drugs are administered via intratumoral injection, which poses the risk of tissue damage and local infection. Therefore, the safety of OVs must be rigorously evaluated and further optimized before their clinical application.

The activation and secretion of cytokines by T cells are crucial for mediating immunotherapy. In clinical applications, emphasis should be placed on monitoring and recording changes in indicators related to T-cell activity and immune target organ pathology, such as lymphocyte phenotype detection and determination of the proportion of CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells. These indicators effectively reflect the immune status of patients with tumors, allowing physicians to better determine the OV timing and dosage. Based on clinical experience with OVs, immunosuppressed patients typically exhibit better responses than those with intact immune systems. However, this higher anticancer effect is frequently associated with unacceptable toxicity. Therefore, combining OVs with immunosuppressive drugs to delay viral immune clearance may elicit superior antitumor efficacy. The superior antitumor effects of OVs can be anticipated as long as the immune system of patients with tumors is strictly evaluated and immune support is well-provided during clinical applications.

Intratumoral antiviral response

Limited intratumoral infiltration of OVs

Tight junctions between epithelial cells in tumors have been implicated as a mechanism underlying drug resistance in tumor cells, especially in AdV 1. During tumor metastasis, tumor cells transit from the epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition to the mesenchymal-epithelial transition state, forming tight junctions between cells again, thus preventing drug penetration from damaging cells within the tumor and triggering the formation of cancer cells with stem cell characteristics, which is also an important factor leading to cancer recurrence. Furthermore, the extracellular matrix (ECM) composed of proteoglycans can hinder the spread of OVs in tumors. Therefore, achieving OV infiltration into tumors and the subsequent killing effects presents considerable challenges.

To address this issue, scientists have found that the combination of OVs and cytotoxic drugs can induce tumor cell apoptosis by activation of Caspase-8, leading to the formation of channel-like structures and spaces between tumor cells, which is beneficial for the transmission of OVs, especially HSVs.

Limited viral replication of OVs

To a certain extent, the antiviral immunity of tumor cells can limit virus replication in infected tumors. When OVs infect cells during early stages, certain gene products that help resist viral infection in tumor cells are cleared or inactivated, which may promote viral self-replication. However, once the OV activates the host immune system, in addition to killing tumor cells, the immune system may hinder OV replication. In addition, apoptosis induced by OVs infecting tumor cells is detrimental to OV replication. Early apoptosis in tumor cells lacking antiviral activity can be harmful to the subsequent development of the virus.

To resolve this issue, OVs were modified via the application of genetic technologies. Preventing OV-induced cell apoptosis during the early stages of infection was found to substantially promote virus replication, with cells ultimately “stretched to death” by the virus or “starved to death” owing to a large amount of intracellular virus “plundering” energy.

Second, the hypoxic effect is a characteristic of solid tumors, known to occur during the development and growth of tumors and can lead to cell cycle arrest, thereby inhibiting the replication of viruses within cells. To overcome the hypoxia-mediated inhibition of adenoviral replication, Zhang et al. successfully constructed a recombinant oncolytic AdV with a chimeric HRE sequence by gene editing, thereby targeting tumors under hypoxic conditions. The virus had a stronger replication ability under hypoxic conditions, thus exerting a stronger killing effect on renal cell carcinoma cells.

Maintenance of TME-mediated immune tolerance

Although OVs activate immune responses, such as cytokine release, lymphocyte recruitment, and activation, other factors also participate in immune tolerance in the TME, such as lack of co-stimulatory signals, negative regulation of immune checkpoints, and inflammatory and metabolic microenvironments. These factors are not conducive for immune cell activation. Therefore, TME-mediated immune tolerance greatly limits the OV-activated antitumor immunity in solid tumors.

This issue can be effectively addressed through combination therapy, for example, OVs combined with immunotherapy and OVs combined with chemotherapy. Two main options are available to combine OVs with immune therapy: OVs combined with ICIs and OVs combined with CAR-T. During ICI therapy, PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies alone fail to comprehensively overcome tumor-specific immune responses mediated by immune tolerance. However, the combination of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and ICIs (such as PD-1/PD-LLC and CTLA4 mAb) was found to elicit clinical efficacy in the treatment of various refractory tumors. Blake et al. reported that a patient with diffuse metastatic melanoma who received a combination of immunotherapy and OV therapy achieved a complete central nervous system response and partial systemic response, thereby highlighting the enormous potential of OVs in combination with immunotherapies. In addition, in patients with advanced melanoma, intratumoral injection of modified OVs was shown to enhance immune recognition and achieve a high response rate. To avoid the immunosuppressive effects of the TME, reverse T-cell failure, and prevent antigen escape, OV therapy has been proposed in combination with CAR-T cells. Considering OVs combined with chemotherapy, a phase II trial of ONYX-015 combined with cisplatin and 5-fluorouracil revealed that the effective rate of the combination therapy group increased by 50%, along with a reduction in tumor volume. The mechanism of action of ONYX-015 in combination with cisplatin and 5-fluorouracil suggests that following pretreatment with cisplatin and 5-fluorouracil, OVs are more likely to enter and kill tumor cells.

Therefore, in the future, apart from genetic engineering, combining OVs with traditional chemotherapy or ICIs can be explored to exert better synergistic antitumor effects for the application of OVs.

Immune memory

Pre-existing immune memory of OVs due to previous infections presents a major challenge in the clinical application of OVs. Following intravenous injection, OVs are neutralized by pre-stored antibodies, such as anti-reovirus and anti-measles virus antibodies, before reaching the target cancer cells and quickly activating the body's memory immune response. Accordingly, the half-life of OVs in the host is shortened, which makes it difficult to reach the “viral infection threshold”, thereby resulting in a modest therapeutic effect.

To facilitate the OV evasion of immune attacks, several delivery strategies have been developed, including stem cell-, cell membrane-, and serum albumin-mediated OVs. Membrane-mediated systemic OV drug delivery includes extracellular vehicle-wrapped OVs and tumor membrane-based hybrid vector systems. Rojas et al. discovered that the binding of serum albumin to the Adv surface, along with albumin aggregation on the OV surface, could effectively prevent the antibody-mediated destruction of AdV. In summary, these vehicles can deliver OVs intravenously to facilitate effective tumor enrichment with OVs through blood circulation, thereby improving the efficacy and safety of drug delivery. More importantly, these delivery systems can improve biocompatibility by ensuring the antitumor efficacy of OVs and avoiding rapid OV elimination by neutralizing antibodies, which is crucial for the clinical treatment of deep tumors and metastases.

Limited therapeutic targets and patient selection

The modest efficacy of wild-type OVs has been attributed to their low tumor specificity. Owing to the limited therapeutic and evaluative targets for tumors, OVs may fail to exert their anticipated effects. For clinical treatment, wild-type OVs have been genetically modified to improve the targeting of OVs to specific tumors. Undoubtedly, newly modified OVs are associated with prolonged timelines and higher economic costs, along with unknown risks, such as changes in virus virulence. Nevertheless, genetic engineering has resulted in breakthroughs in a growing number of oncolytic viral drugs. For example, T-VEC, an OV drug marketed in the United States, is a genetically modified HSV-1. Accordingly, identifying additional tumor-specific targets for the design and modification of OVs is an important future research direction.

Considering OV evaluation, the lack of reliable predictive biomarkers hinders the selection of appropriate patient categories that could benefit from OVs, as well as efficacy prediction. This reduces the desire of patients to receive oncolytic drug therapy. Given that most OVs are still in the experimental stage, patients undergoing treatment with OVs have already received various traditional cancer treatments, which results in alter tumor cells when compared with the initial state. This leads to substantial differences in the effectiveness of OVs among patients. Typically, patients with tumors who have received traditional antitumor treatment have an incomplete immune system and are less likely to produce an immune response to OVs.

Based on the life history traits of the virus, Yan et al. successively discovered four intracellular biomarkers capable of predicting the efficacy of the M1 virus; these biomarkers included zinc finger antiviral protein ZAP, cholesterol path-related protein RHOQ, ER stress pathway protein IRE1α, and KRAS mutant protein. In 2022, MXRA8 was identified as a receptor for the novel OV M1 (OVM), and a dual biomarker system was discovered to predict the efficacy of the M1 virus.

It must be acknowledged that as a widely used clinical anticancer drug, reliable biomarkers for therapy and therapeutic efficiency evaluation are still lacking. Currently, numerous studies are engaged in identifying therapeutic and evaluation targets for various tumors, which can provide directions for further modification of OVs.

Conclusions

With the development of biotechnology, our understanding of the molecular biology and mechanisms of OVs has improved dramatically. Genetic engineering can achieve OV modification with marked accuracy and diversification. To date, four oncolytic drugs have been launched globally and have achieved satisfactory clinical trial results. Moreover, combining OVs and other treatments was shown to elicit an antitumor phenomenon of “1 ​+ ​1>2″, which indicates that OV will gain momentum for tumor therapy and offer new hope to additional patients.
