# Targeted apoptotic immune modulator for the treatment of metastatic EGFR-positive solid tumors.

## Abstract

Aberrant activation and overexpression of the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) occurs in various solid cancers and often correlates with poor outcome. The clinical benefit from EGFR-targeted therapies is usually short-lived, with resistance being driven by tumor heterogeneity and an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). To address these limitations, we developed Targeted Apoptotic Immune Modulators (TAIM), a nonviral nanoparticle platform for the targeted delivery of polyinosine:polycytosine (polyIC), to simultaneously induce tumor cell death and activate antitumor immunity. The first TAIM compound, TAR001, was designed as a systemic treatment against metastatic EGFR-positive solid cancers. Here, we present TAR001's multifaceted mode of action. We demonstrate that TAR001 is selective toward EGFR-overexpressing cancers, provoking a pattern recognition response, apoptosis, cytokine secretion, and antitumor immunity. TAR001 modulates the TME, recruiting and activating both innate and adaptive immune cells. Systemic delivery of TAR001 markedly extends survival and inhibits tumor growth in multiple murine tumor models. TAR001 represents an innovative, safe, multimodal treatment approach with the potential to benefit patients with metastatic head and neck, non-small cell lung cancer, colorectal, renal, and triple-negative breast cancers. This unique modality utilizes a broad range of mechanisms to overcome the tumor's ability to escape apoptosis and immune cell activation.

## Full Text

Targeted apoptotic immune modulator for the treatment of metastatic EGFR-positive solid tumors

Significance

Epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) overexpression is associated with multiple aggressive solid tumors. Despite recent advances in cancer therapy, and specifically in EGFR-targeted therapy, there is an unmet need for effective treatment for patients with metastatic EGFR-driven disease. This paper presents the detailed mode of action of TAR001, a Targeted Apoptotic Immune Modulator (TAIM) nanoparticle. TAR001 directly kills EGFR-overexpressing cancer cells while simultaneously initiating a comprehensive antitumor immune response, involving both innate and adaptive immune components, and is efficacious in multiple in vivo models. This technology offers a potential treatment modality for patients with advanced refractory EGFR-positive solid tumors. Importantly, the nanoparticle platform can be designed to target different overexpressed internalizing receptors, broadening its therapeutic applications.

In recent years, immune checkpoint inhibition (ICI) has taken the center stage in cancer therapeutics. While significant improvements in solid tumor treatment have been made with the development and utilization of ICI alone or in combination, these treatments offer limited benefit for most patients with metastatic disease, including patients with Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR, also known as HER1/ErbB-1)-driven tumors, which tend to have a cold, immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME).

EGFR activating mutations, gene amplification, and/or protein overexpression are linked to many human cancers, including those of the head and neck (HN), breast, brain, lung, and colon, and typically correlate with more aggressive disease. Recent advances in the treatment of EGFR-driven cancers include tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKI), antibodies, antibody–drug conjugates (ADC), and bispecific antibodies, such as the recently approved Amivantamab, which targets both EGFR and Mesenchymal Epithelial Transition (MET). Nevertheless, there is an unmet need for novel therapies that can surmount both tumor heterogeneity and the suppressive TME.

To overcome these hurdles, we have developed a targeted nanoparticle platform, Targeted Apoptotic Immune Modulator (TAIM). Targeting, a critical aspect of nanoparticle drug delivery, enables selectivity and reduces nonspecific toxicity. TAIMs are designed to selectively kill cancer cells, while simultaneously activating both the innate and adaptive immune systems, generating a comprehensive antitumor immune response. The TAIM platform is based on a cutting-edge nanoparticle technology, Ta: RNA™, comprising a targeted nonviral vector that is electrostatically associated with the active ingredient, polyinosinic-polycytidylic acid (polyIC). The vector is a triconjugate composed of linear polyethylenimine (LPEI), polyethylene glycol (PEG), and a covalently bound targeting moiety that can be adapted to target any internalizing transmembrane receptor. PolyIC is a synthetic double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) and is selectively delivered by the nanoparticles to the targeted tumor cells.

Cellular uptake of polyIC mimics a viral infection and has been under investigation for decades as a potential cancer therapy. PolyIC, like viral dsRNA, binds to pattern recognition receptors (PRR), including TLR3, PKR, RIG-I, and MDA5. PRR activation leads to the production of proinflammatory cytokines and type-I interferons (IFN), apoptosis, and translational inhibition and harnesses innate and adaptive antiviral immune mechanisms to cause cell death and inflammation. In early clinical trials, polyIC was ineffective at tolerated doses. PolyICLC, a stabilized version of polyIC, has been tested as an immune adjuvant with vaccines and other agents. An intratumorally delivered PEI-polyIC nanoparticle (BO-112) was beneficial in phase 1/2 clinical trials, in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy. While this is encouraging, the approach is limited to accessible tumors.

TAR001, the first TAIM compound, has synthetic human EGF as the targeting moiety and was developed for the treatment of patients with advanced, refractory, EGFR-positive solid tumors. We previously demonstrated the efficacy of precursor EGFR-targeted nanoparticles in preclinical models of glioblastoma, triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). We showed profound effects on mouse survival and provided evidence that an antitumor immune response was generated. These data motivated us to develop a nanoparticle that would be suited to the clinic.

Here, we present a detailed analysis of the multifaceted mode of action (MoA) of TAR001 (SI Appendix, Fig. S1). TAR001, delivered systemically, is efficacious in multiple EGFR-positive cancer models, with minimal toxicity. We show that the internalization of TAR001 specifically into EGFR-positive cancer cells promotes the selective activation of PRR downstream signaling pathways, leading to cancer cell apoptosis and cytokine secretion. In turn, these cytokines recruit innate and adaptive immune cells, invoking a comprehensive immune response and generating a bystander effect.

Results

Selective Internalization of TAR001 by EGFR-Overexpressing Cells.

We examined EGFR endocytosis and the cellular internalization of TAR001 using confocal microscopy. For these experiments, TAR001 was prepared using labeled triconjugate and/or labeled polyIC. TAR001 treatment led to translocation of EGFR from the cell membrane to the cytoplasm (Fig. 1A). TAR001 was taken up efficiently by high-EGFR-expressing cells (hereafter “high-EGFR cells”), while minimal endocytosis into low-EGFR-expressing (“low-EGFR”) cells was observed (Fig. 1 A and B). Cy5-labeled polyIC trafficked through early and late endosomes, as shown by colocalization with EEA1 and Rab7, respectively (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). Colocalization with lysosomes (marked by LAMP1) was observed to a lesser extent (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A), consistent with endosomal escape due to protonation of the LPEI component of TAR001 in the endosomes. Indeed, a calcein release assay confirmed that TAR001 induces endosomal escape (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B). These studies showed that TAR001 is taken up selectively by high-EGFR cells.

Selective Cytotoxicity toward Cancer Cells Expressing High EGFR.

To study the potency and selectivity of TAR001 in vitro, multiple cell lines (cancer cell lines and immortalized noncancerous cell lines) with differential expression of EGFR were utilized (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A). Relative EGFR cell surface expression was determined by flow cytometry. TAR001 was highly selective: High-EGFR cancer cells (BT20, A431, MDA-MB-468, and HCC70) were sensitive to TAR001, with IC50 values in the ng/mL range, while low-EGFR cancer cells (MCF7 and U138) and noncancerous cells with moderate EGFR expression [WI-38 (immortalized lung fibroblasts)] were significantly less sensitive, with IC50 values at least 100-fold higher (Fig. 1 C and D). Furthermore, the cytotoxic effect on high-EGFR cells was mediated by TAR001 and not by its individual components: Neither polyIC alone, triconjugate alone, nor control nanoparticles carrying an inert cargo [polyglutamic acid (polyGlu)] induced significant cytotoxicity at the concentrations tested (Fig. 1 C and D and SI Appendix, Fig. S3B). These studies showed that TAR001 is potent against EGFR-positive cancer cells, in vitro.

Efficacy of the Polyplex against EGFR-Positive Tumors In Vivo.

We next examined the efficacy of TAR001 in vivo. To this end, we employed multiple EGFR-positive mouse tumor models, derived from adenocarcinoma, SCC, and melanoma.

Systemic administration of TAR001 led to profound growth inhibition of established subcutaneous tumors [105 to 180 mm3 (see SI Appendix, SI Extended Materials and Methods)] in nude mice. Models tested included tumors derived from vulvar SCC, TNBC, and HN cancer (A431, HCC70, and HSC-3 cell lines, respectively), all of which were strongly inhibited (Fig. 2 A–D). Moreover, TAR001 significantly inhibited the growth of a patient-derived xenograft (PDX; H22) representing HN cancer, from a patient with advanced laryngeal cancer (Fig. 2E). Dose dependence was demonstrated in A431 tumors, with a minimal efficacious dose of 0.625 mg/kg (Fig. 2A). TAR001 was more efficacious than free polyIC, showing the importance of targeting the delivery of polyIC (Fig. 2B). Furthermore, control nanoparticles with polyGlu in place of polyIC had no effect in vivo (Fig. 2B). Notably, in these models, TAR001 strongly inhibited tumor growth even in the absence of a T cell response.

To demonstrate that the in vivo efficacy of TAR001 is dependent on EGFR targeting rather than on the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect, we generated an isogenic cell model from the B16F10 mouse melanoma cell line by stable transfection with human EGFR cDNA (SI Methods). TAR001 treatment strongly inhibited the growth of tumors derived from B16F10-hEGFR cells (Fig. 2F), whereas tumors derived from B16F10 parental cells, which have little or no detectable EGFR expression (SI Appendix, Fig. S3C), were refractory to TAR001 treatment (Fig. 2G).

We further investigated the efficacy of TAR001 in the syngeneic Renca-hEGFR model, in which experimental lung metastases are evident 7 d after tumor cell injection into BALB/c mice. Immunohistochemical analysis of the tumors revealed heterogeneous expression of EGFR in the metastases (SI Appendix, Fig. S3D). TAR001 was administered systemically for 2 wk, starting from day 7 after cell injection, and led to a significant reduction in the number of lung metastases in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 2H).

Using the same immunocompetent experimental lung metastases model, we demonstrated a strong effect of TAR001 on mouse survival. Treatment of mice for only 2 wk, beginning at Day 10, led to significantly prolonged survival (Fig. 2I). Moreover, TAR001 had a more powerful effect on survival than cetuximab (CTX; Fig. 2I). CTX treatment of mice with Renca-hEGFR metastases led to a slight prolongation of survival compared to the vehicle control group, with a median survival of 50 d vs. 43.5 d respectively (CTX ~15% longer than vehicle). In contrast, treatment with TAR001 increased the median survival of the mice to 68.5 d (~57% longer than vehicle; 37% longer than CTX), showing a clear advantage of TAR001 over CTX in this model.

In all of these in vivo models, TAR001 was generally well-tolerated, with no significant weight loss.

These data demonstrate the strong efficacy of TAR001 in multiple in vivo models of EGFR-overexpressing cancer, both slow and fast growing, and highlight the advantages of TAR001 to target disseminated heterogeneous tumors.

Pharmacodynamic Effects of TAR001.

Activation of PRR signaling in response to TAR001.

The induction of multiple signaling pathways by TAR001 was demonstrated by RNA-Seq studies. We compared A431 tumors from vehicle- and TAR001-treated mice. Tumors were extracted at time points from 5 min to 8 h after treatment with a single dose of TAR001 (1.25 mg/kg). Differentially expressed genes were enriched for pathways involved in PRR response, apoptosis, cytokine/chemokine production, and innate and adaptive immune responses (Fig. 3A and SI Appendix, Fig. S4A). Similar data were obtained from RNA seq analysis of the HCC70 tumor model following multidose treatment with TAR001 (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). In vitro RNA-Seq data, comparing high-EGFR and low-EGFR cells treated with TAR001 or with control polyplexes carrying polyGlu instead of polyIC confirmed the specificity of the response and verified that polyIC, the active pharmaceutical ingredient in TAR001, drives the signaling (SI Appendix, Fig. S5 A–C). These effects on the transcriptomic profiles indicate that TAR001 treatment indeed mimics viral infection, leading to tumor cytotoxicity and changes in the TME that enhance antitumor immune responses.

The selective activation of known dsRNA-associated signaling pathways was verified by western blotting. TAR001 induced the phosphorylation of NFκB, interferon regulatory factor 3 (IRF3) and STAT1 in high-EGFR cells, but not in low-EGFR cells. Protein levels of interferon-stimulated genes such as MDA5, RIG-I, and interferon-stimulated gene 15 (ISG15) were induced following treatment (Fig. 3B). Furthermore, TAR001 (but not triconjugate alone) induced the cleavage of caspase-3 and polyadenosine diphosphate ([ADP]-ribose) polymerase-1 (PARP-1) in high-EGFR cells, but not in low-EGFR cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S6A), indicating that the direct cytotoxic effect of TAR001 is mediated via apoptosis.

Importantly, these pharmacodynamic (PD) effects were validated in vivo in two independent mouse models following single and multidose systemic administration of TAR001. Although TAR001 has a fast clearance of less than 2 h, administration of a single dose of TAR001 to mice bearing A431 tumors resulted in a prolonged PD effect, for at least 72 h. Fig. 3C shows immunoblot analysis of tumors from individual animals treated with TAR001 at the minimal efficacious dose (0.625 mg/kg) and killed at time points up to 72 h. The overall patterns show EGFR phosphorylation, indicating TAR001 target engagement, and the activation of PRR downstream signaling and apoptosis in the treated tumors, despite mouse-to-mouse variability. These effects were also seen in an additional model of subcutaneous HCC70 tumor-bearing athymic nude mice (SI Appendix, Fig. S6B). These data confirm that systemic administration of TAR001 results in activation of PRR signaling and tumor cell apoptosis in vivo.

Induction of cytokine secretion following TAR001 treatment.

The activation of IRF3 and NFκB (shown above) is known to induce the transcription of proinflammatory cytokines. Furthermore, the RNA-Seq analysis showed significant increases in transcription of multiple cytokines associated with chemoattraction and immune cell activation. We therefore evaluated the pharmacodynamic effects of TAR001 on cytokine levels, both in vivo and in vitro.

Analysis of the plasma from A431 tumor-bearing nude mice that were treated with a single dose of TAR001 (0.625 mg/kg) revealed increases in both human (i.e., tumor-derived) and mouse cytokines. Significant time-dependent increases in plasma levels of human CCL5 (RANTES) and CXCL10 (IP-10) were detected (Fig. 3D). Additionally, increases in multiple mouse cytokines were observed, including CCL5, CXCL10, CCL2, and TNFα (Fig. 3D).

In vitro, treatment with TAR001 led to selective increases in secretion of CXCL10, CCL5 (SI Appendix, Fig. S7A), IFN-λ (SI Appendix, Fig. S7B), and IFN-β (SI Appendix, Fig. S7C) from high-EGFR cells, but not from low-EGFR cells. No cytokine secretion was induced by control nanoparticles (SI Appendix, Fig. S7 A–C).

The induction of both human and mouse proinflammatory cytokines highlights the direct effect of TAR001 on the tumor as well as its ability to activate the host immune system.

TAR001 leads to immunogenic cell death (ICD).

PolyIC is known to induce ICD, which triggers simultaneous activation of innate and adaptive immunity against tumors. We therefore explored whether TAR001 can induce ICD. TAR001, but not control nanoparticles, induced significant increases in the secretion of high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) (SI Appendix, Fig. S8A), the release of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) (SI Appendix, Fig. S8B), and secretion of IFN-β (SI Appendix, Fig. S7C), all of which represent hallmarks of ICD, in high-EGFR cells only. Furthermore, TAR001 treatment led to an increase in major histocompatibility complex class I (MHC-I) cell surface expression specifically in high-EGFR cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S8C). These data demonstrate that TAR001 is not only a cytotoxic agent but also an ICD-inducing agent.

Immune cell infiltration and activation stimulated by TAR001.

Cytokines such as Type-I IFN, CXCL10 (IP-10), CCL5 (RANTES), and IFN-γ, as well as HMGB1 and ATP, have been shown to attract macrophages, T cells, and natural killer (NK) cells and to promote dendritic cell maturation and antitumor activity. Accordingly, we next investigated the effect of TAR001 on immune cell recruitment and activation.

In vitro, the migration of PBMCs toward supernatant from TAR001-treated cells was investigated using Boyden chambers. A significant increase in PBMC chemotaxis was observed only toward supernatant from TAR001-treated high-EGFR cancer cells, and not toward supernatant from treated low-EGFR cancer cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S9A).

We confirmed that TAR001 induces immune cell infiltration in vivo, using the immunocompetent Renca-hEGFR experimental lung metastases model. Tumors were harvested following treatment with TAR001 or vehicle (1, 3, or 6 doses). Multiplex immunofluorescence staining showed low density of CD8+ T cells, NK cells, and dendritic cells in tumors from vehicle-treated animals, suggesting a cold phenotype. In contrast, vigorous increases in the infiltration of CD8+ T cells and macrophages into the metastases were seen after 3 and 6 doses of TAR001 (Fig. 4A). Increases in the numbers of DC and NK cells were suggestive of an immune stimulatory effect but were not statistically significant (Fig. 4A). These studies confirmed that TAR001 promotes both innate and adaptive immune cell recruitment to the tumor.

To study the impact of TAR001 on immune cell activation, medium transfer experiments were conducted. PBMCs were incubated with conditioned medium from TAR001-treated high-EGFR and low-EGFR cells. Incubation of PBMCs with medium from TAR001-treated A431 cells led to increased IFN-γ secretion (Fig. 4B). Moreover CD8+ T cells showed significant increases in CD69 and Granzyme B (Fig. 4C) while NK cells showed significant increases in CD69 (SI Appendix, Fig. S9B). Medium from control nanoparticle-treated cells (Fig. 4C) or from TAR001-treated MCF7 cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S9C) did not induce a significant increase in IFN-γ secretion from PBMCs. These results demonstrate the selective immune cell activation by medium from TAR001-treated high-EGFR cancer cells.

Tumor-associated macrophages are among the most abundant immune cells infiltrating the TME. Macrophages internalize foreign materials through phagocytosis and play an important role in initiating and regulating the host inflammatory response. TAR001 treatment of nonpolarized M0 macrophages resulted in an increase in markers associated with the M1 phenotype (CD80, CD86, and secretion of IP-10; Fig. 4D), which is associated with an enhanced antitumor inflammatory response. Treatment of M2 macrophages also resulted in an increase in M1 markers and a decrease in the M2 marker CD206 (Fig. 4D).

Thus, TAR001 induced immune cell activation and proinflammatory macrophage repolarization. Taken together, our data show that TAR001 invokes both the innate and adaptive immune systems to promote a comprehensive antitumor immune attack.

Bystander effects of TAR001.

To further demonstrate that the activated immune cells, as well as the cytotoxic factors released from the tumor cells following TAR001 treatment, promote the demise of neighboring, non-EGFR-expressing cancer cells, we investigated bystander effects. To study direct bystander effects, TAR001-sensitive, high-EGFR cells were grown in coculture with low-EGFR cells that express luciferase (MCF7-Luc). MCF7-Luc cells alone were not affected by TAR001 treatment. In contrast, TAR001 treatment of the cocultured cells led to reduced MCF7-Luc cell viability (SI Appendix, Fig. S9D). The addition of PBMCs to the cocultured cells resulted in even stronger killing of the MCF7-Luc cells, demonstrating the induction of an indirect, PBMC-mediated bystander effect (SI Appendix, Fig. S9D). As tumor heterogeneity is an impediment to most cancer therapies, the bystander effect is a major advantage of TAR001.

TAR001 sensitizes tumors to anti-PD1.

Despite the significant clinical promise of anti-PD-1 therapies, only a subset of patients respond, due to primary or acquired resistance. The finding that TAR001 induces immune cell recruitment and activation suggested that it might sensitize “cold” tumors to ICI. As shown above, there was minimal infiltration of T cells into untreated Renca-hEGFR metastases (Fig. 4A). Therefore, it is not surprising that in this model treatment with anti-PD-1 antibody was not effective; the median survival of anti-PD-1 antibody-treated mice was the same as that of vehicle-treated mice (Fig. 4D). Administration of TAR001 as a single agent at suboptimal doses (0.31 mg/kg) provided a significant survival benefit to the mice (median survival of 57 d; 33% increase, compared to median survival of 44 d for vehicle group). An even more profound increase in survival was obtained when TAR001 and the anti-PD-1 antibody were combined (median survival of 66 d; 54% increase, compared to the vehicle group, Fig. 4D). The results obtained with this model suggest that combining TAR001 with anti-PD-1 could be beneficial.

Discussion

The pressing need for targeted cancer therapies has driven the development of diverse strategies, including TKI, monoclonal antibodies, and ADC. Even third-generation EGFR inhibitors, such as Osimertinib, have limited efficacy, as acquired resistance is common. The EGFR monoclonal antibodies, cetuximab and panitumumab, were approved for the treatment of metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC), and cetuximab for HN cancer, but their impact on progression-free survival or overall survival in the metastatic setting is limited. Recently, the 5-y survival rate for patients with advanced EGFR-mutated non–small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) was reported to be 24%. Additional EGFR-targeting therapies are being developed, including bispecific antibodies and ADC. Amivantamab, an EGFR-MET bispecific antibody, was approved recently for the treatment of patients with locally advanced or metastatic NSCLC with EGFR mutations. A bispecific antibody–drug conjugate targeting HER3/EGFR has shown promising results in early clinical trials. Innovative immunotherapeutic approaches, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cells, and bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs) have entered the clinic. Despite these advances, a significant unmet need remains for patients with EGFR-positive tumors, and novel therapies are needed to address on-target off-tumor toxicity, tumor heterogeneity, and the immunosuppressive TME.

To address these challenges, we developed the TAIM nanoparticle platform technology for the treatment of various solid cancers. Our lead compound, TAR001, conveys polyIC selectively to EGFR-overexpressing solid tumors. Our data characterize the multimodal MoA of TAR001, in vitro and in vivo. We show that targeting of TAR001 to the EGFR leads to high intracellular polyIC concentrations in high-EGFR cancer cells, triggering PRR signaling selectively in these cells. It seems that in cells with few receptors, polyIC levels do not reach a necessary threshold for PRR signaling. Thus, TAR001 selectively induces apoptotic and ICD in high-EGFR cells, promoting proinflammatory cytokine secretion, increased MHC-1 presentation, immune cell recruitment and activation, and bystander effects on neighboring, low-EGFR tumor cells. SI Appendix, Fig. S1 summarizes the MoA of TAR001, which results in tumor cell apoptosis and immune modulation.

The targeting of TAR001 differentiates it from other polyIC-based therapies under development, such as the intratumoral administration of BO-112. In order to treat patients with metastatic disease, we developed TAR001 for systemic administration. We demonstrated that IV administration of TAR001 was efficacious in multiple mouse models with moderate- to high-EGFR expression. These include SCC and adenocarcinoma models derived from human vulval and HN carcinomas, TNBC, and a renal carcinoma model with disseminated disease and heterogeneous expression of EGFR.

Whereas chronic inflammation is tumorigenic, a transient, acute increase in cytokines may favor an antitumor immune response. Comprehensive activation of both innate and adaptive antitumor immune responses should lead to an effective treatment. Haabeth et al. have argued that PAMPs, such as polyIC, promote an antitumor inflammatory environment in which even supposedly “tumorigenic” cytokines, such as IL-6, act to eradicate cancer. Our data confirm that TAR001 leads to immune cell chemotaxis, activation of T cells, and macrophage polarization toward an M1 phenotype. Utilizing the experimental lung metastases model, we showed that systemic administration of TAR001 led to an influx of CD8+ T cells and macrophages into the metastases and to prolonged survival of the mice. This result highlights the tumor-selective immune modulatory effect of TAR001, which resets the immune balance without activating a severe systemic immune response. Our results demonstrate that TAR001 induces prolonged and substantial pharmacodynamic effects, including induction of apoptosis and immune cell recruitment. These effects result in inhibition of tumor growth and prolonged survival in multiple mouse models.

TAR001 showed significant efficacy as a standalone treatment. Its immunomodulatory effect, converting cold tumors into hot tumors, suggests that it could also enhance ICIs. Indeed, the combination of anti-PD-1 with TAR001 led to a good response in the PD-1 refractory Renca-hEGFR model (Fig. 4). This approach may open treatment options for patients who currently do not respond to PD-1 therapies. Moreover, other treatment modalities, including CAR-T cells and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, which show limited efficacy against solid tumors, may benefit from combination with TAR001.

In summary, our studies elucidate the unique multimodal mechanism of action of TAR001. By targeting EGFR-overexpressing tumors while protecting polyIC within the nanoparticle, TAR001 achieves tumor-specific delivery and cellular internalization, enabling polyIC to reach the threshold concentration necessary for PRR activation. This mechanism results in selective cancer cell killing and localized immune activation at the tumor site, at doses at which naked polyIC is ineffective. By simultaneously generating a comprehensive immune response and targeted cancer cell killing, TAR001 addresses key challenges in cancer therapy. Given the intrinsic cytotoxic effect of TAR001, evident even in nude mouse models and in the immunologically cold Renca-hEGFR model, TAR001 may be therapeutically useful even in patients with compromised immunity.

GLP-compliant toxicity studies in rats and cynomolgus monkeys demonstrated that TAR001 was well tolerated in both species, with no evidence of toxicity in cynomolgus monkeys and only mild histopathological changes in rats. These results support the progression of TAR001 into first-in-human studies. The efficacy at low dose coupled with the positive safety profile suggests a broad therapeutic window. Taken together, our data show that TAR001 may provide meaningful clinical benefit to patients with EGFR-positive metastatic solid tumors.

Materials and Methods

Further details are supplied in the SI Appendix, SI Extended Materials and Methods.

Cell Lines.

High-EGFR cancer cell lines were A431, BT-20, HCC-70, MDA-MB-468, and Renca-EGFR, and control low-EGFR lines were MCF7 and U138 cancer cells or WI-38 fibroblasts with moderate EGFR levels. EGFR levels were confirmed by flow cytometry.

TAR001 Polyplex Formation.

The triconjugate component of TAR001 is composed of LPEI, PEG, and synthetic hEGF as the targeting moiety, and was prepared by a proprietary method. TAR001 nanoparticles were prepared by mixing triconjugate with polyIC in 5% glucose at a molar ratio of nitrogen (from triconjugate):phosphate (from polyIC) (N/P) of 4. The mixture was incubated at room temperature for 20 min, prior to further dilution. Control polyplexes were prepared in the same manner with polyGlu in place of polyIC. The concentration of TAR001 (or control polyplex) is defined as the concentration of polyIC (or polyGlu).

Cytotoxicity and Determination of IC50 Values.

Cells were plated in triplicate in 96-well plates. The next day, polyplexes were prepared and serially diluted. The diluted polyplexes were further diluted 1/10 into the medium of the cells. For standard testing, final treatment concentrations ranged from 0.075 to 625 ng/mL. After 72 h, viability was assayed using CellTiter-Glo (Promega). IC50 values were calculated by regression analysis with GraphPad Prism (sigmoidal dose response – variable slope).

Cell Internalization.

Cells were grown on 12 mm coverslips for 48 h. The cells were cooled on ice for 20 min. Then, 625 ng/mL TAR001 (Cy5-polyIC/ unlabeled triconjugate) was added on ice for 30 min. The cells were transferred to 37 °C for incubation times ranging from 10 min to 4 h. Primary antibody incubation was overnight at 4 °C followed by secondary antibody incubation for 4 h.

Primary antibodies (at 1/100 dilution) were anti-EEA1 antibody to mark early endosomes, anti-Rab7 antibody to mark late endosomes, anti-LAMP1 antibody to mark lysosomes, anti-Cy5 antibody to identify Cy5-labeled polyIC. After staining with secondary antibodies, coverslips were mounted with Fluoroshield solution containing DAPI (Sigma, catalog no. F6057) and cells were imaged using an LSM 880 Indimo, AxioObserver Zeiss Microscope. Three independent biological repeats were performed. Antibodies are listed in SI Appendix, Table S1.

Live Cell Microscopy.

Cells were treated with 625 ng/mL of fluorescently labeled TAR001 [Cy5-polyIC (red) and Alexafluor 488-triconjugate(green)] and subjected to live imaging over the course of 8 h at 20× magnification on a confocal microscope. Nuclei were counterstained with Hoechst 33342 (blue).

Mouse Models.

TAR001 was administered IV as a bolus injection via the tail vein after tumors were established. TAR001 was generally well tolerated.

Subcutaneous tumor models.

The effects of TAR001 on the growth of A431 (vulvar carcinoma SCC), HCC70 (TNBC), HSC-3 (HN), and H22 (HN PDX) subcutaneous xenografts in athymic nude mice and on B16F10 (melanoma) and B16F10-hEGFR subcutaneous tumors in syngeneic C57BL/6JRccHsd mice were measured.

Renca-hEGFR (mouse renal carcinoma) experimental lung metastasis model.

An experimental lung metastasis model was generated by IV injection of cells derived from a mouse renal carcinoma cell line overexpressing human EGFR (Renca-hEGFR) into immunocompetent 8 to 10-wk-old BALB/c mice (250,000 cells per mouse). In this model, lung micrometastases develop 7 d after injection of the cells. To test the effect of TAR001 on mouse survival, mice were randomized after 10 d. The presence of tumors in the lungs was verified upon killing of animals.

Analysis of experimental lung metastases.

TAR001 treatment was initiated 7 d after injection of Renca-hEGFR cells. Mice were killed 22 d after tumor cell injection, and the lungs were collected and embedded in paraffin blocks. Lungs were stained with anti-EGFR and counterstained with hematoxylin.

Characterization of immune cell infiltration into the metastases.

Mice were randomized and IV treatment [TAR001 (1.25 mg/kg) or vehicle (5% glucose, 10 μL/g); n = 3 for each time point] was initiated 19 d after injection of the Renca-hEGFR cells. Mice were treated with a single injection, three injections (3×/wk, 1 wk), or six injections (3×/wk, 2 wk), and lungs were harvested 24 h after the final injection. After fixation and embedding in paraffin, tissue samples were subjected to Tyramide Signal Amplification (TSA)-based Multiplex Fluorescence staining to detect innate and adaptive immune cell infiltration into the tumors. P-values were calculated using the unpaired Student’s t test with two tails and unequal variance. Antibodies are listed in SI Appendix, Table S1. (Staining and analysis by AnaPath Services GmbH).

Luminex Platform.

Mouse and human cytokines were analyzed using Luminex panels (performed by Firalis CRO Services Provider). Luminex Discovery assays (Bio-techne, USA) were used according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The statistical analysis of these data was performed using the One-way ANOVA with Dunnett’s multiple comparison test.

Macrophage Reprogramming.

Monocytes were isolated from PBMCs from three different donors using immunomagnetic negative selection for CD14+ cells (EasySep™ Human Monocyte Isolation Kit #19359) and were differentiated into nonpolarized (M0) macrophages in vitro according to standard protocols.

M0 to M1 reprogramming.

M0 macrophages were incubated with TAR001 for 48 h and the change in the expression of M1 (CD80, CD86) and M2 (CD206) markers upon TAR001 treatment was evaluated by flow cytometry. Secretion of IP-10 was evaluated by the ELISA.

M2 to M1 reprogramming.

M0 macrophages were polarized to M2 phenotype with IL-4 (BioLegend, Cat no. 574004) and IL-13 (BioLegend, Cat no. 571102) for 24 h. The medium was then replaced by fresh medium lacking the M2 polarization factors and the M2 macrophages were incubated with TAR001 for 48 h. As a positive control, M1 polarization medium containing IFNγ (BioLegend, Cat no. 570204) and LPS (Sigma-Aldrich/Merck, Cat no. L2880) was used. The change in the expression of M1 (CD80, CD86) and M2 (CD206) markers upon TAR001 treatment was evaluated by flow cytometry, and the secretion of IP-10 was evaluated by the ELISA. Antibodies for flow cytometry are listed in SI Appendix, Table S3.
