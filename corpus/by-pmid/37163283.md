# Preclinical Development and Validation of Translational Temperature Sensitive Iodized Oil Emulsion Mediated Transcatheter Arterial Chemo-Immuno-Embolization for the Treatment of Hepatocellular Carcinoma.

## Abstract

Herein a practical strategy for augmenting immune activation in transcatheter arterial chemoembolization (TACE) of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is presented. Pluronic F127 (PF127) is incorporated with Lipiodol (LPD) to achieve safe and effective delivery of therapeutic agents during transcatheter intra-arterial (IA) local delivery. Enhanced emulsion stability, IA infusion, embolic effect, safety, pharmacokinetics, and tumor response of Doxorubicin loaded PF127-LPD (Dox-PF127-LPD) for TACE in both in vitro and in vivo preclinical VX2 liver cancer rabbit model and N1S1 HCC rat model are demonstrated. Then, transcatheter arterial chemo-immuno-embolization (TACIE) combining TACE and local delivery of immune adjuvant (TLR9 agonist CpG oligodeoxynucleotide) is successfully performed using CpG-loaded Dox-PF127-LPD. Concurrent and safe local delivery of CpG and TACE during TACIE demonstrate leveraged TACE-induced immunogenic tumor microenvironment and augment systemic anti-tumor immunity in syngeneic N1S1 HCC rat model. Finally, the broad utility and enhanced therapeutic efficacy of TACIE are validated in the diethylnitrosamine-induced rat HCC model. TACIE using clinically established protocols and materials shall be a convenient and powerful therapeutic approach that can be translated to patients with HCC. The robust anti-cancer immunity and tumor regression of TACIE, along with its favorable safety profile, indicate its potential as a novel localized combination immunotherapy for HCC treatment.

Introduction

The systemic administration of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), cancer vaccines, or immune modulators (cytokines, agonists, and adjuvants) is one of the mainstays in current cancer therapy. However, while monotherapy has shown promising results, the development of various combination immunotherapies that can enhance response rates has been a focus of recent preclinical studies and clinical trials. The search for optimal and synergistic combination immunotherapies with standard immunogenic cancer therapies remains of great interest in cancer medicine. Despite higher response rates of combination immunotherapy compared to mono‐immunotherapy, most patients still fail to achieve long‐term tumor control. Therefore, the identification of measures that can amplify the immune system and counteract tumor escape mechanisms without causing unacceptable rates of immune‐related adverse events (irAEs) is crucial to improve patient outcomes.[  ] Recent studies revealed various promising potential of locoregional immunotherapies and immunotherapeutic carriers that can modulate targeted local tumor microenvironment and induce systemic antitumor immunity while minimizing systemic toxicity.[  ] These practical local immunotherapeutic strategies have the potential to execute effective and safe combinational immunotherapies in clinical setups, addressing the urgent need for novel approaches in cancer immunotherapy.

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) therapeutic options have included surgical intervention (resection or transplant), locoregional, and systemic therapies. However, most systemic and regional therapies offer palliation rather than cure. Recently, randomized clinical trials (RCTs) have led to the approval of systemic ICIs (CTLA‐4, PD‐1, and PD‐L1 combined with anti‐VEGF). Despite these advances, a considerable proportion of HCC patients exhibit a lack of response to ICIs immunotherapy. In response to this challenge, immune modulators such as toll‐like receptor (TLR) agonists, STING agonists, RIGI or MDA5 agonists, and inflammasome agonists, have emerged as another potential immunotherapeutic option for HCC. These agents are capable of activating local and adaptive immunity effectively. However, when administered systemically, these agents can induce toxicity and trigger sepsis‐like cytokine release syndromes, leading to systemic inflammation.[  ] Therefore, strategies to administer these agents locally or regionally while minimizing systemic toxicity are of significant interest for the development of effective and safe combinational immunotherapies for HCC.

Image‐guided interventional local therapies such as transarterial chemoembolization (TACE), radioembolization, and ablations demonstrated strong immunogenicity and immune modulation. The combination of interventional local therapy and immunotherapy is an emerging strategy that shows promise in overcoming the current challenges of immunotherapies. Recent encouraging data has sparked the initiation of clinical trials exploring the combination ICIs immunotherapy with chemoembolization, radioembolization, or percutaneous ablation.[  ] The development of image‐guided interventions combined with immunotherapy is an evolving field with the potential to improve response rates while maintaining an acceptable safety profile.[  ] TACE is the standard of care for patients with intermediate‐stage HCC. Clinical trials investigating the combination of systemic ICIs with locoregional TACE are underway (CHECKMATE‐74 W (NCT04340193) and EMERALD‐1 (NCT03778957)). However, considering the limited efficacy of systemic ICIs immunotherapy in HCC,[  ] further development of synergistic combination immunotherapies is required to promote the therapeutic efficacy. Based on the fact that TACE‐mediated cell death induces the release of tumor antigens,[  ] incorporating immunomodulatory agents into existing TACE procedures can potentially facilitate in situ vaccination, promoting systemic antitumor immunity and response to immunotherapies.[  ] Lipiodol (LPD) composed of iodinated ethyl esters of fatty acids from poppy seed oil exhibits transient and plastic embolic effects and facilitates localized delivery of Doxorubicin (Dox) to HCC during conventional TACE (cTACE). However, substantial instability and heterogeneity of the LPD‐based emulsions of therapeutic agents can induce the potential risk for systemic adverse events due to the inevitable systemic leakage of loaded therapeutics.[  ] Therefore, the development of LPD‐based formulations that can enhance stability and minimize systemic leakage may not only improve the current standard of care using chemotherapy drugs but also allows an opportunity for the safe incorporation of potent immunomodulatory agents into the procedure.

Here, we incorporated an FDA‐approved copolymer surfactant PF127 (poly(ethylene oxide)‐poly(propylene oxide)‐poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO‐PPO‐PEO)) in the LPD‐based emulsion to investigate whether it can improve physicochemical properties of the emulsion.[  ] We presented the preparation of PF127‐incorporated LPD emulsions and investigated the safety and efficacy of PF127‐LPD emulsion for delivering toxic therapeutics and TACE. In vivo feasibility of transcatheter intra‐arterial (IA) infusion of PF127‐LPD was tested with digital subtraction angiography (DSA)‐guided arterial embolization in VX2 rabbits. Then, Dox and CpG oligodeoxynucleotide TLR9 agonist, which develops an effective innate and adaptive immune response with TACE‐mediated antigens,[  ] were added for performing an effective and safe transarterial chemo‐immuno‐embolization (TACIE) of HCC. The therapeutic response and activation of anti‐cancer immunity of TACIE were evaluated in an orthotopic syngeneic N1S1 Novikoff hepatoma model.[  ] The therapeutic efficacy was further confirmed with a chemically induced diethylnitrosamine (DEN)‐HCC rat model that is largely recapitulating the clinical HCC.[  ] 

Results

PF127 Improves Physicochemical Properties of Dox‐Lipiodol Emulsion

For the incorporation of PF127 into Dox‐LPD, PF127 was mixed with Dox in the aqueous phase prior to form with LPD emulsion. A PF127/Dox solution at 20% PF127 exhibiting a temperature‐responsive gelation property at 37 °C was selected (Figure S1A,B, Supporting Information). Cellular uptake and cytotoxicity of Dox were not affected by the addition of PF127 (Figure S1C–E, Supporting Information). Then, Dox‐PF127‐LPD emulsions were prepared by an established clinical cTACE protocol.[  ] PF127/Dox (aqueous phase) and LPD (oily phase) were mixed through a three‐way stopcock between two syringes with 30 pushes and pulls (Figure  1A). Dox with or without PF127 could be emulsified with LPD in clinically relevant aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratios of 1:1–1:4 (Figure 1A,B).[  ] The Dox‐LPD emulsion without PF127 showed rapid phase separation within 5 min post‐emulsification (Figure 1C), as observed in previous reports.[  ] However, Dox‐LPD with PF127 (Dox‐PF127‐LPD) maintained a stable emulsion with well‐distributed aqueous droplets in the oil phase at room temperature (Figure 1C). The enhanced stability of Dox‐PF127‐LPD was observed with no significant phase separation in a capillary tube during a 24 h incubation at 37 °C (Figure 1D). Subsequently, the addition of PF127 in LPD emulsion allowed superior retention of Dox compared to Dox‐LPD (Figure 1E; Figure S2, Supporting Information). The secure retention of therapeutic release from the aqueous phase in PF127‐LPD could be used for various potential therapeutic molecules such as dextran[  ] and IgG antibodies (Figure S2, Supporting Information).[  ] Next, in vivo feasibility of using Dox‐PF127‐LPD (Figure 1F) and its embolization effect were demonstrated by transcatheter IA embolization of VX2 rabbit arteries (Figure 1G). The selected VX2 rabbit animal model has been commonly utilized for evaluating translational interventional procedures.[  ] Hepatic artery catheterization and embolization using Dox‐PF127‐LPD were performed with DSA guidance (Figure 1F). Under DSA images from X‐ray fluoroscopy, Dox‐PF127‐LPD could be successfully infused into the upstream at the proper hepatic artery (PHA) bifurcation into the right and left hepatic arteries without any complications (Figure 1G). The occlusion of the hepatic artery including tumor vessels with IA‐infused Dox‐PF127‐LPD was demonstrated with Omnipaque contrast‐enhanced angiographic images before and after the infusion (Figure 1G). Taken together, the data suggest that PF127 can be successfully incorporated into Dox‐LPD emulsion, resulting in increased stability, drug retention, and in vivo transcatheter IA infusion of Dox‐PF127‐LPD.

PF127 Improves the Safety and Efficacy of TACE

We further investigated whether the improved physicochemical properties of Dox‐PF127‐LPD emulsion in vivo can lead to enhanced in vivo safety and efficacy in a syngeneic HCC model (Figure  2A). An orthotopic N1S1 rat hepatoma model was established by implanting N1S1 HCC cells into the left hepatic lobe of Sprague Dawley rats.[  ] A baseline MRI was performed one week after implantation, and the results showed an average tumor volume of ≈100 mm3. Then, TACE using Dox‐LPD or Dox‐PF127‐LPD (Dox: 200 µg) was performed within 2 days after the baseline MRI scans. Successful transcatheter arterial delivery of samples was confirmed with DSA. A Pharmacokinetics study showed that Dox‐PF127‐LPD significantly decreased the Cmax and AUC of Dox compared to Dox‐LPD (Figure 2B,C, Table S1, Supporting Information). Respective Cmax and AUC of Dox‐PF127‐LPD were 0.11 ± 0.07 µg ml−1 and 2.34 ± 1.52 µg h−1 ml−1, which were ≈eight‐ to twelvefolds lower than the results of Dox‐LPD. Observation of body weight change revealed that both experimental groups exhibited post‐treatment‐related body weight loss (Figure 2D). However, the group treated with TACE using Dox‐PF127‐LPD exhibited less body weight loss and faster body weight recovery (Figure 2D), due to the minimized systemic exposure of Dox (Figure 2B,C, Table S1, Supporting Information). Subsequent weekly MRI and MRI‐based tumor volume measurements showed that Dox‐PF127‐LPD led to superior tumor growth inhibition (Figure 2E,F; Figures S3–S5, Supporting Information). Both groups induced comparable tumor growth inhibition up to 1‐week post‐TACE. At 2‐week post‐TACE, the tumor growth of the Dox‐PF127‐LPD TACE group was significantly suppressed while the Dox‐LPD TACE group showed exponential tumor growth during 2‐week post‐TACE. Histological quantification analysis using TUNEL assay also revealed significantly enhanced cell death in the Dox‐PF127‐LPD group (Figure 2G; Figure S6, Supporting Information). The data imply that the altered physicochemical properties of the Dox‐LPD emulsion resulting from the incorporation of PF127 appear to play a crucial role in the improved therapeutic efficacy of TACE with reduced systemic toxicity.

TACIE Using CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD Activates Innate and Adaptive Immunity in HCC

After observing the ability of PF127‐LPD to facilitate the safe delivery of drugs through transcatheter IA infusion, we aimed to utilize this formulation to co‐deliver both doxorubicin and immunotherapeutic agents for the purpose of TACIE. TLRs signals can activate a wide range of cells within both the innate and adaptive immune systems. Their local administration has been shown to effectively induce intratumoral immune activation and systemic anti‐tumor T cell response.[  ] To investigate whether transcatheter IA co‐delivery of TLRs and Dox induces similar immunological benefits, we incorporated CpG oligonucleotide‐a ligand for TLR9 into the Dox‐PF127‐LPD by simply adding it in the aqueous phase with Dox. The intended doses of Dox and CpG in CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD were 200 and 20 µg, respectively. To assess early immune responses, we treated the rats with Dox‐PF127‐LPD (TACE group) or CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD (TACIE group) and then collected the tumors at 4 days post treatments (Figure  3A). IFN‐γ is a key cytokine in the development of T cell‐mediated immunity and adaptive immune responses against tumors.[  ] The activation of the immune system was manifested in the TACIE group with a significant increase in interferon‐γ (IFN‐γ) secretion from antigen‐presenting cells (CD11b/c+), NK cells (CD161α +), and T cells (CD3+CD4− and CD3+CD8+) (Figure 3B; Figures S7,S8, Supporting Information). Non‐significant increase of the IFN‐γ secretion in the TACE alone group implied its moderate activation of the immune system.[  ] TACIE group also demonstrated significantly increased tumor‐infiltrated CD8+ T cells, as shown in flow cytometry data and immunohistology images (Figure 3C,D). At the same time, TACIE treatment significantly upregulated co‐stimulatory molecule expression (CD86+) in antigen‐presenting cells (Figure S9, Supporting Information). According to the results, it is suggested that TACIE with CpG effectively induced substantial tumoral anti‐cancer immune activation.

TACIE Regresses Established HCC and Induces Systemic Tumor‐Specific T cell Immunity

Next, the therapeutic outcome and systemic anti‐tumor immunity of TACIE were further evaluated (Figure  4A). Radiographic assessment by MRI showed that the TACIE group exhibited comparable tumor size with the TACE group at 1‐week post‐treatment (Figure 4B,C). However, at 2‐week post‐treatment, the TACIE group showed superior tumor growth inhibition and regression compared to the control or TACE group. The relative tumor volume compared to baseline at 2‐week post‐treatment was 341.8 ± 115.4% and 79.4 ± 49.5% in the TACE and TACIE groups, respectively (p < 0.01). All the rats in the TACE‐only group (7/7) demonstrated disease progression, while 75% of the rats in the TACIE group (3/4) showed tumor regression (Figures S5,S10, Supporting Information). To investigate the induction of systemic anti‐tumor immunity, we isolated splenocytes from each treated and untreated groups and then incubated them with antigens generated by heat‐killed tumor cells.[  ] We then investigated the activation of tumor antigen‐specific CD8+ T cells after each treatment (Figure 4D). Antigen stimulation resulted in increased expression of CD8+ T cells and proinflammatory cytokine IFN‐γ specifically in the TACIE group (Figure 4E,F), implying tumor‐specific immunity. However, CD8+ T cells without antigen stimulation expressed a low level of IFN‐γ in all groups. These data suggest that TACIE can effectively induce systemic anti‐tumor immunity and significantly improve the therapeutic efficacy of HCC.

TACIE Does Not Induce Systemic or Local Toxicity

CpG is a potent immune adjuvant, but its systemic exposure is typically associated with toxicity and is therefore not considered clinically acceptable in the systemic administration route. Observation of body weight change revealed that TACIE including CpG treatment did not induce additional body weight loss and showed no significant difference in body weight change compared to the TACE‐only group throughout the 2‐week post‐treatment (Figure  5A). We also obtained blood serum specimens of treated and untreated rats at 3 and 14 days post‐treatment. Examination of serum proinflammatory cytokines TNF‐α and IFN‐γ revealed no significant difference across all groups (Figure 5B). Furthermore, serum concentrations of aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), creatine kinase, and total bilirubin also showed no significant difference across all groups (Figure 5C). These results show that TACIE does not cause systemic or local toxicity.

Efficacy of TACIE Is Confirmed in DEN‐Induced HCC Model

Finally, the broad utility and enhanced therapeutic efficacy of TACIE were validated in the DEN‐induced rat HCC model. DEN causes liver tumors with a sequential progression of hepatitis, cirrhosis, and tumor formation, which closely mimics the development of human liver cancer.[  ] At 10 weeks following DEN treatment, rats were treated, and then laparotomy was performed to identify those with visible HCC nodules (Figure  6A). Macroscopic observation revealed a significant amount of tumor nodule development, especially in the non‐treated control group, which was confirmed through histological analysis (Figure 6B). The number of HCC nodules larger than 1 mm in diameter was 17 ± 6 in the no‐treatment control group, 11 ± 3 in the TACE group, and 3 ± 2 in the TACIE group (Figure 6C). The largest tumor size was 5.8 ± 2.3 mm in the non‐treatment control group, 4.8 ± 1.2 mm in the TACE group, and 1.8 ± 0.9 mm in the TACIE group (Figure 6D). Also, the fibrotic area measured by Sirius Red staining was 31.3 ± 10.4% in the non‐treatment control group, 22.8 ± 8.6% in the TACE group, and 3.5 ± 1.3% in the TACIE group (Figure 6E,F). These results show that TACIE local combination immunotherapy can effectively inhibit the development of HCC in the DEN‐induced model. Concurrent local CpG delivery with TACE as a form of TACIE was able to further enhance its therapeutic effect of TACE significantly.

Discussion

TACE remains the standard of care for patients with intermediate‐stage HCC. Especially, cTACE using Dox‐LPD emulsion has been routinely utilized in clinical practice due to its validated safety, long retention time, and transcatheter IA infusion procedures of LPD agents allow versatile uses of LPD as a therapeutic carrier.[  ] Although there is early leakage of therapeutics in the aqueous phase of LPD emulsion, well‐established LPD carriers and cTACE procedure can be a promising translational approach that can achieve practical local combinational immunotherapy. Recent data on the immunogenicity of cTACE and clinical trials evaluating the combination of cTACE and systemic immunotherapy encourage the incorporation of cTACE and local immunotherapy. Herein, we observed increased immune activation in the TACE group compared to the no‐treatment control group as assessed by IFN‐γ secretion from various immune cells, CD8+ T cell recruitment, and expression of co‐stimulatory receptors by antigen‐presenting cells. The tendency of enhancement of immune activation was consistent with previous studies.[  ] The dox agent used in TACE is known to induce immunogenic cell death (ICD).[  ] Dox‐TACE‐treated HCC patients have shown a significant increase of ICD markers[  ] and decreases of Tregs in the peripheral blood[  ] with upregulated pro‐inflammatory cytokine secretion.[  ] We hypothesized that the superior therapeutic effect of TACIE (TACE + CpG‐combination regimen) was likely attributed to a synergistic effect resulting from the immunomodulatory effects of TACE as opposed to the limited therapeutic efficacy of CpG or TACE when used alone.[  ] 

In many clinical and preclinical studies, in situ vaccination has been performed via percutaneous intratumoral injection, which is often requiring multiple injections throughout the treatment period.[  ] This poses drawbacks in terms of patient compliance, availability of a suitable injection site in the tumor, and requirements for additional imaging modalities. Transcatheter tumor arterial local delivery of immune adjuvants can be a promising option for local immunotherapy, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing systemic irAEs.[  ] Various stimuli‐responsive hydrogel, nanoparticles, and microsphere embolic agents have been proposed for combinational TACE and immunotherapy.[  ] In our study, we utilized modified LPD TACE agents for the combinational TACE and in situ vaccination using CpG immunotherapy. Our one‐time local delivery of CpG using our Dox‐PF127‐LPD during TACIE demonstrated a significant anti‐cancer effect. The utilization of PF127‐LPD emulsion is a promising and translatable approach, as it enables the retention and sustained release of both Dox and CpG over an extended period of several weeks, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of the treatment.[  ] TACE procedures have been established clinically for targeting only the tumor vascular supply. Our TACIE, based on the clinical TACE protocols, is not limited by conventional percutaneous intra‐tumoral delivery parameters. Further, the interventional oncology imaging guidance of TACIE enables enhanced targeting and controlled delivery of the combination of chemo and immunotherapy. It is anticipated that TACIE incorporating CpG into TACE can be readily translated without significant changes in the current clinical procedures.

Risk: benefit considerations are of significant importance when combining immunotherapies with conventional therapies. TACE shows an objective response of ≈50% based on RECIST and median survival with TACE ranges from 26 to 40 months, depending on the stage of disease.[  ] Combining TACE with systemic anti‐angiogenic agents (e.g., sorafenib) has not improved survival outcomes.[  ] Although clinical trials investigating the combination of ICIs with TACE are underway (NCT034143270 and NCT03778957), the limited efficacy of ICI monotherapy in various cancers, including advanced HCC,[  ] indicates that further optimization of therapy is necessary. This is exemplified by the recent discontinuation of Nivolumab monotherapy for the treatment of HCC. While the combinations of ICIs can lead to higher response rates, the significantly higher incidence of irAEs associated with such regimens requires careful risk: benefit considerations for the patient population. IA locoregional immunotherapy performed in this study did not result in any local or systemic adverse events due to its local distribution within the tumor and low‐dose requirement. Our TACIE approach, which demonstrated robust local anti‐cancer immunity and effective tumor response with safety, is suggesting a new form of local immunotherapy for the treatment of HCC. Additionally, the development of PF‐127‐LPD formulation offers great potential for various combinations of local immunotherapy with immune adjuvants, ICIs, and chemotherapies.[  ] This safe integration of immunotherapy with conventional modalities will be of critical importance in designing clinical trials.

Conclusion

Herein we have developed a practical strategy for augmenting immune activation in the cTACE of HCC. As a first step, PF127 was added to LPD for effectively securing and releasing therapeutics dissolved in the aqueous phase of LPD emulsion. Our optimized PF127 components with Dox‐LPD improved the tumor response and enhanced the immune reaction of HCC. Given that the stabilized aqueous phase in the LPD emulsion has the capacity to accommodate a diverse range of hydrophilic therapeutic agents, including chemotherapy molecules and IgG‐based antibody therapeutics, it should be an effective promising local therapeutic delivery platform. Herein incorporation of CpG molecules into our developed Dox‐PF127‐LPD‐based emulsion and its transarterial delivery performing TACIE successfully induced robust local immune responses, leading to the regression of HCC and formation of systemic anti‐tumor immunity. Intratumoral CpG delivery, which can be considered an in situ vaccination, targets the universal innate immune system without requiring information of tumor antigens.[  ] Our approach using all clinically usable materials should be a convenient and powerful therapeutic approach that can be translated to patients with HCC. Moreover, the tumor‐specific anti‐cancer immunity induced by local immunotherapy may enhance the response to potential combination with other conventional ICIs immunotherapies.

Experimental Section

Study Design

The two main objectives of this study were to develop a modified LPD emulsion for enhanced therapeutic delivery during embolization and assess the therapeutic efficacy of TACIE in rat models of HCC. The strategy for the first objective was to exploit PF127, an FDA‐approved amphiphilic block co‐polymer, as a stabilizers of LPD emulsion. The authors found PF127 not only stabilized the emulsion as it prevented rapid phase separation but also allowed sustained release of incorporated drugs such as doxorubicin. The feasibility of using the formulation in rabbits was confirmed as it showed good injectability and embolization effect. The improved safety and efficacy of the emulsion was demonstrated in a rat N1S1 HCC model. Strategy for the second objective was to incorporate CpG, a widely known and used TLR9 agonist to activate the innate immune system, into the emulsion. It was hypothesized that TACE‐induced antigen release could be leveraged by concurrent delivery of CpG and it could lead to effective local priming of immune cells. Two rat HCC models induced by implantation of N1S1 hepatoma cells were used and DEN was used to study the therapeutic efficacy of TACIE. The general strategy for the evaluation of immune response was to thorough assessment of IFN‐γ secretion using flow cytometry in immune populations as CpG directly impacted the secretion of IFN‐γ in dendritic cells and was a critical mediator of both innate and adaptive immunity. Formation of systemic anti‐tumor immunity was assessed via splenocyte restimulation using heat‐killed N1‐S1 tumor cells. The sample sizes were selected on the basis of the results of pilot experiments so that relevant statistical tests could reveal significant differences between experimental groups. Animals that did not develop tumors or died because of surgery were excluded from the experiments. In therapy experiments using the N1S1 HCC model, baseline tumor sizes were measured using MRI at 2 weeks post‐implantation and then those with an established tumor were randomized into groups. The rats were given treatments within 2 days after baseline MRI. In therapy experiments using the DEN‐induced model, laparotomy was performed after 10 weeks of DEN feeding to identify the development of HCC. Those with established HCC were randomized into groups and given treatments. For each experiment, animal numbers, statistical tests, and numbers of experimental replicates were described in the figure legends. Investigators were not blinded during the evaluation of the in vivo experiments.

Reagents

CpG (ODN D‐SL03) was purchased from Invivogen. Dox was purchased from LC Laboratories. PF127 was purchased from Sigma Aldrich. The following antibodies were used for flow cytometry: CD11b/c‐BV421 (BD Biosciences; 743 977), CD86‐BV510 (BD Biosciences; 743 212), PD‐L1‐FITC (Cell Signaling Technologies; 250 485); CD3‐BV605 (BD Biosciences; 563 949), CD4‐APC‐Cy7 (BD Biosciences; 565 432), CD8α‐BV510 (BD Biosciences; 740 139), CD161α‐BV421 (BD Biosciences; 555 008); IFN‐γ‐AF647 (BD Biosciences; 562 213).

Emulsion Preparation and Characterization

Water‐in‐oil emulsions were prepared with LPD for the lipid phase and water for the aqueous phase with clinically relevant aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratios of 1:1‐1:4. Stock solutions of PF127 (30% w/v), Dox (45 mg ml−1), and Dox plus CpG (45 mg ml−1 Dox and 3 mg ml−1 CpG) were prepared in distilled water. To prepare PF127‐containing LPD emulsion, the PF127 stock solution was first mixed with a drug solution at a 2:1 volume ratio. For example, the PF127 and Dox plus CpG stock solutions were mixed to obtain a drug solution containing 20% w/v PF127, 15 mg ml−1 Dox, and 1 mg ml−1 CpG. For the conventional formulation, distilled water without PF127 was used. Then, LPD and the drug solution were mixed using 30 back‐and‐forth pump exchanges through a 3‐way stopcock. The formulations were loaded into a 1 ml syringe (Becton Dickinson) and injected through a 24‐gauge, 3/4‐inch infusion catheter (Terumo) at a flow rate of 50 µl s−1.

Drug Release

LPD‐based emulsions at a 1:3 aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratio were prepared using solutions of Dox (45 mg ml−1), FITC‐IgG (400 µg ml−1), and FITC‐dextran (10 kDa) (1 mg ml−1). Emulsion (200 µl of each) in a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube was incubated at 37 °C for 5 min before gently adding 1 ml of PBS containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) pre‐warmed to 37 °C. The samples were maintained at 37 °C throughout. 20 µl of the supernatant was collected at varying time points to measure the release of the molecules from the emulsion. To assess the loading efficacy of LC beads, LC beads in the range of 300–500 µm (Biocompatibles, VE420GS) were used. Beads (250 µl) were drawn out of the vial and the packing solution was removed. Then, the beads were incubated with Dox (500 µg), FITC‐IgG (10 µg), and FITC‐dextran (100 µg) dissolved in PBS at 4 °C for 4 h. The supernatant was used to measure unloaded molecules and calculate loading efficacy.

Cell Line

The N1S1 rat hepatoma cell line (ATCC, CRL‐1604) was cultured in complete Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM) (Hyclone) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin.

Tumor Model and Intra‐Arterial Therapy

All animal procedures were performed in accordance with protocols (IS00014738) approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. All rats were purchased from Envigo. For the N1S1 rat HCC tumor model, 300–350 g male Sprague Dawley rats were used and implanted 5 × 106 N1S1 hepatoma cells in the left lateral lobe of the liver during mini‐laparotomy procedures in Sprague Dawley rats. At 7 days after tumor implantation when the tumor size reached ≈5 mm in size as measured by MR imaging, TACE was performed by infusing ≈100 µl of LPD emulsion into the left hepatic artery. The safety and efficacy of LPD emulsions with or without PF127 were evaluated. TACE was performed using LPD emulsion with (20%) or without (0%) PF127 and the corresponding dose for Dox was 200 µg. The Pharmacokinetics of Dox and body weight changes were measured for toxicity evaluation. MR imaging and immunohistochemistry were performed for efficacy evaluation. The effect of CpG incorporation into TACE was evaluated. TACE was performed using LPD emulsion with or without CpG and the corresponding doses for Dox and CpG were 200 and 20 µg, respectively. Immune responses in the tumor were analyzed at 4 and 14 days post‐TACE using flow cytometry. Immune‐related adverse events were evaluated by measuring blood cytokine and blood tests at 4 and 14 days post‐TACE. Tumor‐specific immunity was analyzed using a splenocytes restimulation assay at 14 days post‐TACE. For the diethylnitrosamine (DEN)‐induced HCC model, Wistar rats were used, and they were given 0.01% DEN water bottles for a period of 10 weeks. Laparotomy was performed to identify liver tumors and those with liver tumors were randomly grouped. Rats were sacrificed at 2 weeks post‐TACE and immediately after sacrifice, externally visible tumors larger than 1 mm were counted by stereo microscopy.

MR Imaging

MR imaging studies were performed using a 7.0‐T ClinScan high‐field small animal MR imaging system with a commercial rat coil (Bruker Biospin, Billerica, Massachusetts). Tumor volume was calculated as length × width2/2, where length represented the largest tumor diameter and width represented the perpendicular tumor diameter.

Blood Collection

Blood was collected from the tail vein using a 23 G butterfly needle (Becton Dickinson). The tip of the needle was inserted into one of the lateral tail veins ≈2 inches away from the tip of the tail. The rubber end of the butterfly needle was inserted into the vacuum blood collection tube coated with EDTA (Becton Dickinson; 367 841). After collection, the needle was removed, and gentle pressure was applied to stop the bleeding. The blood samples were centrifuged at 2000 × g for 5 min to separate plasma.

FACS Analysis

Surface staining of cells was performed in phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS) containing 1% FBS for 30 min at 4 °C. For intracellular staining, the cells were resuspended in fixation/permeabilization (BD Bioscience) solution for 20 min at 4 °C before intracellular staining in permeabilization buffer for 30 min at 4 °C. The stained cells were washed and resuspended in PBS for flow cytometric analysis. Flow cytometry was performed on a BD LSRFortessa (BD Biosciences).

Splenocyte Restimulation

The spleen was collected in ice‐cold complete RPMI medium, placed on a 70 µm cell strainer, and mashed using a syringe plunger through the strainer. Splenocytes were centrifuged at 350 g for 10 min at 4 °C. The cells were resuspended in ACK lysis buffer for the lysis of red blood cells. After resuspension in RPMI medium, cells were counted, diluted, and seeded onto a 24‐well plate at a cell density of 5 × 106 cells ml−1. For splenocytes restimulation, 5 × 105 tumor cells heated at 45 °C for 1 h or irradiated under a UVB lamp for 1 h were added. For IFN‐γ detection, the cells were incubated with the tumor cells for 6 h in the presence of 5 µg ml−1 Brefeldin A and flow cytometry was performed.

Statistical Analysis

Prism 8.0 software was used for statistical analyses. Significant differences between the two groups were analyzed using an unpaired Student's t‐test. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for greater than two groups with Tukey's test for multiple comparisons between all experimental groups. P values <0.05 were considered significant.

Preclinical Development and Validation of Translational Temperature Sensitive Iodized Oil Emulsion Mediated Transcatheter Arterial Chemo‐Immuno‐Embolization for the Treatment of Hepatocellular Carcinoma

Abstract

Herein a practical strategy for augmenting immune activation in transcatheter arterial chemoembolization (TACE) of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is presented. Pluronic F127 (PF127) is incorporated with Lipiodol (LPD) to achieve safe and effective delivery of therapeutic agents during transcatheter intra‐arterial (IA) local delivery. Enhanced emulsion stability, IA infusion, embolic effect, safety, pharmacokinetics, and tumor response of Doxorubicin loaded PF127‐LPD (Dox‐PF127‐LPD) for TACE in both in vitro and in vivo preclinical VX2 liver cancer rabbit model and N1S1 HCC rat model are demonstrated. Then, transcatheter arterial chemo‐immuno‐embolization (TACIE) combining TACE and local delivery of immune adjuvant (TLR9 agonist CpG oligodeoxynucleotide) is successfully performed using CpG‐loaded Dox‐PF127‐LPD. Concurrent and safe local delivery of CpG and TACE during TACIE demonstrate leveraged TACE‐induced immunogenic tumor microenvironment and augment systemic anti‐tumor immunity in syngeneic N1S1 HCC rat model. Finally, the broad utility and enhanced therapeutic efficacy of TACIE are validated in the diethylnitrosamine‐induced rat HCC model. TACIE using clinically established protocols and materials shall be a convenient and powerful therapeutic approach that can be translated to patients with HCC. The robust anti‐cancer immunity and tumor regression of TACIE, along with its favorable safety profile, indicate its potential as a novel localized combination immunotherapy for HCC treatment.

Here transcatheter arterial chemo‐immuno‐embolization (TACIE) combining transcatheter arterial chemoembolization (TACE) and local delivery of immune adjuvant (TLR9 agonist CpG oligodeoxynucleotide) is successfully performed using CpG and Doxorubicin loaded temperature sensitive iodized oil emulsion. Introduction

The systemic administration of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), cancer vaccines, or immune modulators (cytokines, agonists, and adjuvants) is one of the mainstays in current cancer therapy. However, while monotherapy has shown promising results, the development of various combination immunotherapies that can enhance response rates has been a focus of recent preclinical studies and clinical trials. The search for optimal and synergistic combination immunotherapies with standard immunogenic cancer therapies remains of great interest in cancer medicine. Despite higher response rates of combination immunotherapy compared to mono‐immunotherapy, most patients still fail to achieve long‐term tumor control. Therefore, the identification of measures that can amplify the immune system and counteract tumor escape mechanisms without causing unacceptable rates of immune‐related adverse events (irAEs) is crucial to improve patient outcomes.[  ] Recent studies revealed various promising potential of locoregional immunotherapies and immunotherapeutic carriers that can modulate targeted local tumor microenvironment and induce systemic antitumor immunity while minimizing systemic toxicity.[  ] These practical local immunotherapeutic strategies have the potential to execute effective and safe combinational immunotherapies in clinical setups, addressing the urgent need for novel approaches in cancer immunotherapy.

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) therapeutic options have included surgical intervention (resection or transplant), locoregional, and systemic therapies. However, most systemic and regional therapies offer palliation rather than cure. Recently, randomized clinical trials (RCTs) have led to the approval of systemic ICIs (CTLA‐4, PD‐1, and PD‐L1 combined with anti‐VEGF). Despite these advances, a considerable proportion of HCC patients exhibit a lack of response to ICIs immunotherapy. In response to this challenge, immune modulators such as toll‐like receptor (TLR) agonists, STING agonists, RIGI or MDA5 agonists, and inflammasome agonists, have emerged as another potential immunotherapeutic option for HCC. These agents are capable of activating local and adaptive immunity effectively. However, when administered systemically, these agents can induce toxicity and trigger sepsis‐like cytokine release syndromes, leading to systemic inflammation.[  ] Therefore, strategies to administer these agents locally or regionally while minimizing systemic toxicity are of significant interest for the development of effective and safe combinational immunotherapies for HCC.

Image‐guided interventional local therapies such as transarterial chemoembolization (TACE), radioembolization, and ablations demonstrated strong immunogenicity and immune modulation. The combination of interventional local therapy and immunotherapy is an emerging strategy that shows promise in overcoming the current challenges of immunotherapies. Recent encouraging data has sparked the initiation of clinical trials exploring the combination ICIs immunotherapy with chemoembolization, radioembolization, or percutaneous ablation.[  ] The development of image‐guided interventions combined with immunotherapy is an evolving field with the potential to improve response rates while maintaining an acceptable safety profile.[  ] TACE is the standard of care for patients with intermediate‐stage HCC. Clinical trials investigating the combination of systemic ICIs with locoregional TACE are underway (CHECKMATE‐74 W (NCT04340193) and EMERALD‐1 (NCT03778957)). However, considering the limited efficacy of systemic ICIs immunotherapy in HCC,[  ] further development of synergistic combination immunotherapies is required to promote the therapeutic efficacy. Based on the fact that TACE‐mediated cell death induces the release of tumor antigens,[  ] incorporating immunomodulatory agents into existing TACE procedures can potentially facilitate in situ vaccination, promoting systemic antitumor immunity and response to immunotherapies.[  ] Lipiodol (LPD) composed of iodinated ethyl esters of fatty acids from poppy seed oil exhibits transient and plastic embolic effects and facilitates localized delivery of Doxorubicin (Dox) to HCC during conventional TACE (cTACE). However, substantial instability and heterogeneity of the LPD‐based emulsions of therapeutic agents can induce the potential risk for systemic adverse events due to the inevitable systemic leakage of loaded therapeutics.[  ] Therefore, the development of LPD‐based formulations that can enhance stability and minimize systemic leakage may not only improve the current standard of care using chemotherapy drugs but also allows an opportunity for the safe incorporation of potent immunomodulatory agents into the procedure.

Here, we incorporated an FDA‐approved copolymer surfactant PF127 (poly(ethylene oxide)‐poly(propylene oxide)‐poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO‐PPO‐PEO)) in the LPD‐based emulsion to investigate whether it can improve physicochemical properties of the emulsion.[  ] We presented the preparation of PF127‐incorporated LPD emulsions and investigated the safety and efficacy of PF127‐LPD emulsion for delivering toxic therapeutics and TACE. In vivo feasibility of transcatheter intra‐arterial (IA) infusion of PF127‐LPD was tested with digital subtraction angiography (DSA)‐guided arterial embolization in VX2 rabbits. Then, Dox and CpG oligodeoxynucleotide TLR9 agonist, which develops an effective innate and adaptive immune response with TACE‐mediated antigens,[  ] were added for performing an effective and safe transarterial chemo‐immuno‐embolization (TACIE) of HCC. The therapeutic response and activation of anti‐cancer immunity of TACIE were evaluated in an orthotopic syngeneic N1S1 Novikoff hepatoma model.[  ] The therapeutic efficacy was further confirmed with a chemically induced diethylnitrosamine (DEN)‐HCC rat model that is largely recapitulating the clinical HCC.[  ] 

Results

PF127 Improves Physicochemical Properties of Dox‐Lipiodol Emulsion

For the incorporation of PF127 into Dox‐LPD, PF127 was mixed with Dox in the aqueous phase prior to form with LPD emulsion. A PF127/Dox solution at 20% PF127 exhibiting a temperature‐responsive gelation property at 37 °C was selected (Figure S1A,B, Supporting Information). Cellular uptake and cytotoxicity of Dox were not affected by the addition of PF127 (Figure S1C–E, Supporting Information). Then, Dox‐PF127‐LPD emulsions were prepared by an established clinical cTACE protocol.[  ] PF127/Dox (aqueous phase) and LPD (oily phase) were mixed through a three‐way stopcock between two syringes with 30 pushes and pulls (Figure  1A). Dox with or without PF127 could be emulsified with LPD in clinically relevant aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratios of 1:1–1:4 (Figure 1A,B).[  ] The Dox‐LPD emulsion without PF127 showed rapid phase separation within 5 min post‐emulsification (Figure 1C), as observed in previous reports.[  ] However, Dox‐LPD with PF127 (Dox‐PF127‐LPD) maintained a stable emulsion with well‐distributed aqueous droplets in the oil phase at room temperature (Figure 1C). The enhanced stability of Dox‐PF127‐LPD was observed with no significant phase separation in a capillary tube during a 24 h incubation at 37 °C (Figure 1D). Subsequently, the addition of PF127 in LPD emulsion allowed superior retention of Dox compared to Dox‐LPD (Figure 1E; Figure S2, Supporting Information). The secure retention of therapeutic release from the aqueous phase in PF127‐LPD could be used for various potential therapeutic molecules such as dextran[  ] and IgG antibodies (Figure S2, Supporting Information).[  ] Next, in vivo feasibility of using Dox‐PF127‐LPD (Figure 1F) and its embolization effect were demonstrated by transcatheter IA embolization of VX2 rabbit arteries (Figure 1G). The selected VX2 rabbit animal model has been commonly utilized for evaluating translational interventional procedures.[  ] Hepatic artery catheterization and embolization using Dox‐PF127‐LPD were performed with DSA guidance (Figure 1F). Under DSA images from X‐ray fluoroscopy, Dox‐PF127‐LPD could be successfully infused into the upstream at the proper hepatic artery (PHA) bifurcation into the right and left hepatic arteries without any complications (Figure 1G). The occlusion of the hepatic artery including tumor vessels with IA‐infused Dox‐PF127‐LPD was demonstrated with Omnipaque contrast‐enhanced angiographic images before and after the infusion (Figure 1G). Taken together, the data suggest that PF127 can be successfully incorporated into Dox‐LPD emulsion, resulting in increased stability, drug retention, and in vivo transcatheter IA infusion of Dox‐PF127‐LPD.

PF127 Improves the Safety and Efficacy of TACE

We further investigated whether the improved physicochemical properties of Dox‐PF127‐LPD emulsion in vivo can lead to enhanced in vivo safety and efficacy in a syngeneic HCC model (Figure  2A). An orthotopic N1S1 rat hepatoma model was established by implanting N1S1 HCC cells into the left hepatic lobe of Sprague Dawley rats.[  ] A baseline MRI was performed one week after implantation, and the results showed an average tumor volume of ≈100 mm3. Then, TACE using Dox‐LPD or Dox‐PF127‐LPD (Dox: 200 µg) was performed within 2 days after the baseline MRI scans. Successful transcatheter arterial delivery of samples was confirmed with DSA. A Pharmacokinetics study showed that Dox‐PF127‐LPD significantly decreased the Cmax and AUC of Dox compared to Dox‐LPD (Figure 2B,C, Table S1, Supporting Information). Respective Cmax and AUC of Dox‐PF127‐LPD were 0.11 ± 0.07 µg ml−1 and 2.34 ± 1.52 µg h−1 ml−1, which were ≈eight‐ to twelvefolds lower than the results of Dox‐LPD. Observation of body weight change revealed that both experimental groups exhibited post‐treatment‐related body weight loss (Figure 2D). However, the group treated with TACE using Dox‐PF127‐LPD exhibited less body weight loss and faster body weight recovery (Figure 2D), due to the minimized systemic exposure of Dox (Figure 2B,C, Table S1, Supporting Information). Subsequent weekly MRI and MRI‐based tumor volume measurements showed that Dox‐PF127‐LPD led to superior tumor growth inhibition (Figure 2E,F; Figures S3–S5, Supporting Information). Both groups induced comparable tumor growth inhibition up to 1‐week post‐TACE. At 2‐week post‐TACE, the tumor growth of the Dox‐PF127‐LPD TACE group was significantly suppressed while the Dox‐LPD TACE group showed exponential tumor growth during 2‐week post‐TACE. Histological quantification analysis using TUNEL assay also revealed significantly enhanced cell death in the Dox‐PF127‐LPD group (Figure 2G; Figure S6, Supporting Information). The data imply that the altered physicochemical properties of the Dox‐LPD emulsion resulting from the incorporation of PF127 appear to play a crucial role in the improved therapeutic efficacy of TACE with reduced systemic toxicity.

TACIE Using CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD Activates Innate and Adaptive Immunity in HCC

After observing the ability of PF127‐LPD to facilitate the safe delivery of drugs through transcatheter IA infusion, we aimed to utilize this formulation to co‐deliver both doxorubicin and immunotherapeutic agents for the purpose of TACIE. TLRs signals can activate a wide range of cells within both the innate and adaptive immune systems. Their local administration has been shown to effectively induce intratumoral immune activation and systemic anti‐tumor T cell response.[  ] To investigate whether transcatheter IA co‐delivery of TLRs and Dox induces similar immunological benefits, we incorporated CpG oligonucleotide‐a ligand for TLR9 into the Dox‐PF127‐LPD by simply adding it in the aqueous phase with Dox. The intended doses of Dox and CpG in CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD were 200 and 20 µg, respectively. To assess early immune responses, we treated the rats with Dox‐PF127‐LPD (TACE group) or CpG‐Dox‐PF127‐LPD (TACIE group) and then collected the tumors at 4 days post treatments (Figure  3A). IFN‐γ is a key cytokine in the development of T cell‐mediated immunity and adaptive immune responses against tumors.[  ] The activation of the immune system was manifested in the TACIE group with a significant increase in interferon‐γ (IFN‐γ) secretion from antigen‐presenting cells (CD11b/c+), NK cells (CD161α +), and T cells (CD3+CD4− and CD3+CD8+) (Figure 3B; Figures S7,S8, Supporting Information). Non‐significant increase of the IFN‐γ secretion in the TACE alone group implied its moderate activation of the immune system.[  ] TACIE group also demonstrated significantly increased tumor‐infiltrated CD8+ T cells, as shown in flow cytometry data and immunohistology images (Figure 3C,D). At the same time, TACIE treatment significantly upregulated co‐stimulatory molecule expression (CD86+) in antigen‐presenting cells (Figure S9, Supporting Information). According to the results, it is suggested that TACIE with CpG effectively induced substantial tumoral anti‐cancer immune activation.

TACIE Regresses Established HCC and Induces Systemic Tumor‐Specific T cell Immunity

Next, the therapeutic outcome and systemic anti‐tumor immunity of TACIE were further evaluated (Figure  4A). Radiographic assessment by MRI showed that the TACIE group exhibited comparable tumor size with the TACE group at 1‐week post‐treatment (Figure 4B,C). However, at 2‐week post‐treatment, the TACIE group showed superior tumor growth inhibition and regression compared to the control or TACE group. The relative tumor volume compared to baseline at 2‐week post‐treatment was 341.8 ± 115.4% and 79.4 ± 49.5% in the TACE and TACIE groups, respectively (p < 0.01). All the rats in the TACE‐only group (7/7) demonstrated disease progression, while 75% of the rats in the TACIE group (3/4) showed tumor regression (Figures S5,S10, Supporting Information). To investigate the induction of systemic anti‐tumor immunity, we isolated splenocytes from each treated and untreated groups and then incubated them with antigens generated by heat‐killed tumor cells.[  ] We then investigated the activation of tumor antigen‐specific CD8+ T cells after each treatment (Figure 4D). Antigen stimulation resulted in increased expression of CD8+ T cells and proinflammatory cytokine IFN‐γ specifically in the TACIE group (Figure 4E,F), implying tumor‐specific immunity. However, CD8+ T cells without antigen stimulation expressed a low level of IFN‐γ in all groups. These data suggest that TACIE can effectively induce systemic anti‐tumor immunity and significantly improve the therapeutic efficacy of HCC.

TACIE Does Not Induce Systemic or Local Toxicity

CpG is a potent immune adjuvant, but its systemic exposure is typically associated with toxicity and is therefore not considered clinically acceptable in the systemic administration route. Observation of body weight change revealed that TACIE including CpG treatment did not induce additional body weight loss and showed no significant difference in body weight change compared to the TACE‐only group throughout the 2‐week post‐treatment (Figure  5A). We also obtained blood serum specimens of treated and untreated rats at 3 and 14 days post‐treatment. Examination of serum proinflammatory cytokines TNF‐α and IFN‐γ revealed no significant difference across all groups (Figure 5B). Furthermore, serum concentrations of aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), creatine kinase, and total bilirubin also showed no significant difference across all groups (Figure 5C). These results show that TACIE does not cause systemic or local toxicity.

Efficacy of TACIE Is Confirmed in DEN‐Induced HCC Model

Finally, the broad utility and enhanced therapeutic efficacy of TACIE were validated in the DEN‐induced rat HCC model. DEN causes liver tumors with a sequential progression of hepatitis, cirrhosis, and tumor formation, which closely mimics the development of human liver cancer.[  ] At 10 weeks following DEN treatment, rats were treated, and then laparotomy was performed to identify those with visible HCC nodules (Figure  6A). Macroscopic observation revealed a significant amount of tumor nodule development, especially in the non‐treated control group, which was confirmed through histological analysis (Figure 6B). The number of HCC nodules larger than 1 mm in diameter was 17 ± 6 in the no‐treatment control group, 11 ± 3 in the TACE group, and 3 ± 2 in the TACIE group (Figure 6C). The largest tumor size was 5.8 ± 2.3 mm in the non‐treatment control group, 4.8 ± 1.2 mm in the TACE group, and 1.8 ± 0.9 mm in the TACIE group (Figure 6D). Also, the fibrotic area measured by Sirius Red staining was 31.3 ± 10.4% in the non‐treatment control group, 22.8 ± 8.6% in the TACE group, and 3.5 ± 1.3% in the TACIE group (Figure 6E,F). These results show that TACIE local combination immunotherapy can effectively inhibit the development of HCC in the DEN‐induced model. Concurrent local CpG delivery with TACE as a form of TACIE was able to further enhance its therapeutic effect of TACE significantly.

Discussion

TACE remains the standard of care for patients with intermediate‐stage HCC. Especially, cTACE using Dox‐LPD emulsion has been routinely utilized in clinical practice due to its validated safety, long retention time, and transcatheter IA infusion procedures of LPD agents allow versatile uses of LPD as a therapeutic carrier.[  ] Although there is early leakage of therapeutics in the aqueous phase of LPD emulsion, well‐established LPD carriers and cTACE procedure can be a promising translational approach that can achieve practical local combinational immunotherapy. Recent data on the immunogenicity of cTACE and clinical trials evaluating the combination of cTACE and systemic immunotherapy encourage the incorporation of cTACE and local immunotherapy. Herein, we observed increased immune activation in the TACE group compared to the no‐treatment control group as assessed by IFN‐γ secretion from various immune cells, CD8+ T cell recruitment, and expression of co‐stimulatory receptors by antigen‐presenting cells. The tendency of enhancement of immune activation was consistent with previous studies.[  ] The dox agent used in TACE is known to induce immunogenic cell death (ICD).[  ] Dox‐TACE‐treated HCC patients have shown a significant increase of ICD markers[  ] and decreases of Tregs in the peripheral blood[  ] with upregulated pro‐inflammatory cytokine secretion.[  ] We hypothesized that the superior therapeutic effect of TACIE (TACE + CpG‐combination regimen) was likely attributed to a synergistic effect resulting from the immunomodulatory effects of TACE as opposed to the limited therapeutic efficacy of CpG or TACE when used alone.[  ] 

In many clinical and preclinical studies, in situ vaccination has been performed via percutaneous intratumoral injection, which is often requiring multiple injections throughout the treatment period.[  ] This poses drawbacks in terms of patient compliance, availability of a suitable injection site in the tumor, and requirements for additional imaging modalities. Transcatheter tumor arterial local delivery of immune adjuvants can be a promising option for local immunotherapy, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing systemic irAEs.[  ] Various stimuli‐responsive hydrogel, nanoparticles, and microsphere embolic agents have been proposed for combinational TACE and immunotherapy.[  ] In our study, we utilized modified LPD TACE agents for the combinational TACE and in situ vaccination using CpG immunotherapy. Our one‐time local delivery of CpG using our Dox‐PF127‐LPD during TACIE demonstrated a significant anti‐cancer effect. The utilization of PF127‐LPD emulsion is a promising and translatable approach, as it enables the retention and sustained release of both Dox and CpG over an extended period of several weeks, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of the treatment.[  ] TACE procedures have been established clinically for targeting only the tumor vascular supply. Our TACIE, based on the clinical TACE protocols, is not limited by conventional percutaneous intra‐tumoral delivery parameters. Further, the interventional oncology imaging guidance of TACIE enables enhanced targeting and controlled delivery of the combination of chemo and immunotherapy. It is anticipated that TACIE incorporating CpG into TACE can be readily translated without significant changes in the current clinical procedures.

Risk: benefit considerations are of significant importance when combining immunotherapies with conventional therapies. TACE shows an objective response of ≈50% based on RECIST and median survival with TACE ranges from 26 to 40 months, depending on the stage of disease.[  ] Combining TACE with systemic anti‐angiogenic agents (e.g., sorafenib) has not improved survival outcomes.[  ] Although clinical trials investigating the combination of ICIs with TACE are underway (NCT034143270 and NCT03778957), the limited efficacy of ICI monotherapy in various cancers, including advanced HCC,[  ] indicates that further optimization of therapy is necessary. This is exemplified by the recent discontinuation of Nivolumab monotherapy for the treatment of HCC. While the combinations of ICIs can lead to higher response rates, the significantly higher incidence of irAEs associated with such regimens requires careful risk: benefit considerations for the patient population. IA locoregional immunotherapy performed in this study did not result in any local or systemic adverse events due to its local distribution within the tumor and low‐dose requirement. Our TACIE approach, which demonstrated robust local anti‐cancer immunity and effective tumor response with safety, is suggesting a new form of local immunotherapy for the treatment of HCC. Additionally, the development of PF‐127‐LPD formulation offers great potential for various combinations of local immunotherapy with immune adjuvants, ICIs, and chemotherapies.[  ] This safe integration of immunotherapy with conventional modalities will be of critical importance in designing clinical trials.

Conclusion

Herein we have developed a practical strategy for augmenting immune activation in the cTACE of HCC. As a first step, PF127 was added to LPD for effectively securing and releasing therapeutics dissolved in the aqueous phase of LPD emulsion. Our optimized PF127 components with Dox‐LPD improved the tumor response and enhanced the immune reaction of HCC. Given that the stabilized aqueous phase in the LPD emulsion has the capacity to accommodate a diverse range of hydrophilic therapeutic agents, including chemotherapy molecules and IgG‐based antibody therapeutics, it should be an effective promising local therapeutic delivery platform. Herein incorporation of CpG molecules into our developed Dox‐PF127‐LPD‐based emulsion and its transarterial delivery performing TACIE successfully induced robust local immune responses, leading to the regression of HCC and formation of systemic anti‐tumor immunity. Intratumoral CpG delivery, which can be considered an in situ vaccination, targets the universal innate immune system without requiring information of tumor antigens.[  ] Our approach using all clinically usable materials should be a convenient and powerful therapeutic approach that can be translated to patients with HCC. Moreover, the tumor‐specific anti‐cancer immunity induced by local immunotherapy may enhance the response to potential combination with other conventional ICIs immunotherapies.

Experimental Section

Study Design

The two main objectives of this study were to develop a modified LPD emulsion for enhanced therapeutic delivery during embolization and assess the therapeutic efficacy of TACIE in rat models of HCC. The strategy for the first objective was to exploit PF127, an FDA‐approved amphiphilic block co‐polymer, as a stabilizers of LPD emulsion. The authors found PF127 not only stabilized the emulsion as it prevented rapid phase separation but also allowed sustained release of incorporated drugs such as doxorubicin. The feasibility of using the formulation in rabbits was confirmed as it showed good injectability and embolization effect. The improved safety and efficacy of the emulsion was demonstrated in a rat N1S1 HCC model. Strategy for the second objective was to incorporate CpG, a widely known and used TLR9 agonist to activate the innate immune system, into the emulsion. It was hypothesized that TACE‐induced antigen release could be leveraged by concurrent delivery of CpG and it could lead to effective local priming of immune cells. Two rat HCC models induced by implantation of N1S1 hepatoma cells were used and DEN was used to study the therapeutic efficacy of TACIE. The general strategy for the evaluation of immune response was to thorough assessment of IFN‐γ secretion using flow cytometry in immune populations as CpG directly impacted the secretion of IFN‐γ in dendritic cells and was a critical mediator of both innate and adaptive immunity. Formation of systemic anti‐tumor immunity was assessed via splenocyte restimulation using heat‐killed N1‐S1 tumor cells. The sample sizes were selected on the basis of the results of pilot experiments so that relevant statistical tests could reveal significant differences between experimental groups. Animals that did not develop tumors or died because of surgery were excluded from the experiments. In therapy experiments using the N1S1 HCC model, baseline tumor sizes were measured using MRI at 2 weeks post‐implantation and then those with an established tumor were randomized into groups. The rats were given treatments within 2 days after baseline MRI. In therapy experiments using the DEN‐induced model, laparotomy was performed after 10 weeks of DEN feeding to identify the development of HCC. Those with established HCC were randomized into groups and given treatments. For each experiment, animal numbers, statistical tests, and numbers of experimental replicates were described in the figure legends. Investigators were not blinded during the evaluation of the in vivo experiments.

Reagents

CpG (ODN D‐SL03) was purchased from Invivogen. Dox was purchased from LC Laboratories. PF127 was purchased from Sigma Aldrich. The following antibodies were used for flow cytometry: CD11b/c‐BV421 (BD Biosciences; 743 977), CD86‐BV510 (BD Biosciences; 743 212), PD‐L1‐FITC (Cell Signaling Technologies; 250 485); CD3‐BV605 (BD Biosciences; 563 949), CD4‐APC‐Cy7 (BD Biosciences; 565 432), CD8α‐BV510 (BD Biosciences; 740 139), CD161α‐BV421 (BD Biosciences; 555 008); IFN‐γ‐AF647 (BD Biosciences; 562 213).

Emulsion Preparation and Characterization

Water‐in‐oil emulsions were prepared with LPD for the lipid phase and water for the aqueous phase with clinically relevant aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratios of 1:1‐1:4. Stock solutions of PF127 (30% w/v), Dox (45 mg ml−1), and Dox plus CpG (45 mg ml−1 Dox and 3 mg ml−1 CpG) were prepared in distilled water. To prepare PF127‐containing LPD emulsion, the PF127 stock solution was first mixed with a drug solution at a 2:1 volume ratio. For example, the PF127 and Dox plus CpG stock solutions were mixed to obtain a drug solution containing 20% w/v PF127, 15 mg ml−1 Dox, and 1 mg ml−1 CpG. For the conventional formulation, distilled water without PF127 was used. Then, LPD and the drug solution were mixed using 30 back‐and‐forth pump exchanges through a 3‐way stopcock. The formulations were loaded into a 1 ml syringe (Becton Dickinson) and injected through a 24‐gauge, 3/4‐inch infusion catheter (Terumo) at a flow rate of 50 µl s−1.

Drug Release

LPD‐based emulsions at a 1:3 aqueous‐to‐lipid phase ratio were prepared using solutions of Dox (45 mg ml−1), FITC‐IgG (400 µg ml−1), and FITC‐dextran (10 kDa) (1 mg ml−1). Emulsion (200 µl of each) in a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube was incubated at 37 °C for 5 min before gently adding 1 ml of PBS containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) pre‐warmed to 37 °C. The samples were maintained at 37 °C throughout. 20 µl of the supernatant was collected at varying time points to measure the release of the molecules from the emulsion. To assess the loading efficacy of LC beads, LC beads in the range of 300–500 µm (Biocompatibles, VE420GS) were used. Beads (250 µl) were drawn out of the vial and the packing solution was removed. Then, the beads were incubated with Dox (500 µg), FITC‐IgG (10 µg), and FITC‐dextran (100 µg) dissolved in PBS at 4 °C for 4 h. The supernatant was used to measure unloaded molecules and calculate loading efficacy.

Cell Line

The N1S1 rat hepatoma cell line (ATCC, CRL‐1604) was cultured in complete Dulbecco's modified Eagle's medium (DMEM) (Hyclone) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin.

Tumor Model and Intra‐Arterial Therapy

All animal procedures were performed in accordance with protocols (IS00014738) approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. All rats were purchased from Envigo. For the N1S1 rat HCC tumor model, 300–350 g male Sprague Dawley rats were used and implanted 5 × 106 N1S1 hepatoma cells in the left lateral lobe of the liver during mini‐laparotomy procedures in Sprague Dawley rats. At 7 days after tumor implantation when the tumor size reached ≈5 mm in size as measured by MR imaging, TACE was performed by infusing ≈100 µl of LPD emulsion into the left hepatic artery. The safety and efficacy of LPD emulsions with or without PF127 were evaluated. TACE was performed using LPD emulsion with (20%) or without (0%) PF127 and the corresponding dose for Dox was 200 µg. The Pharmacokinetics of Dox and body weight changes were measured for toxicity evaluation. MR imaging and immunohistochemistry were performed for efficacy evaluation. The effect of CpG incorporation into TACE was evaluated. TACE was performed using LPD emulsion with or without CpG and the corresponding doses for Dox and CpG were 200 and 20 µg, respectively. Immune responses in the tumor were analyzed at 4 and 14 days post‐TACE using flow cytometry. Immune‐related adverse events were evaluated by measuring blood cytokine and blood tests at 4 and 14 days post‐TACE. Tumor‐specific immunity was analyzed using a splenocytes restimulation assay at 14 days post‐TACE. For the diethylnitrosamine (DEN)‐induced HCC model, Wistar rats were used, and they were given 0.01% DEN water bottles for a period of 10 weeks. Laparotomy was performed to identify liver tumors and those with liver tumors were randomly grouped. Rats were sacrificed at 2 weeks post‐TACE and immediately after sacrifice, externally visible tumors larger than 1 mm were counted by stereo microscopy.

MR Imaging

MR imaging studies were performed using a 7.0‐T ClinScan high‐field small animal MR imaging system with a commercial rat coil (Bruker Biospin, Billerica, Massachusetts). Tumor volume was calculated as length × width2/2, where length represented the largest tumor diameter and width represented the perpendicular tumor diameter.

Blood Collection

Blood was collected from the tail vein using a 23 G butterfly needle (Becton Dickinson). The tip of the needle was inserted into one of the lateral tail veins ≈2 inches away from the tip of the tail. The rubber end of the butterfly needle was inserted into the vacuum blood collection tube coated with EDTA (Becton Dickinson; 367 841). After collection, the needle was removed, and gentle pressure was applied to stop the bleeding. The blood samples were centrifuged at 2000 × g for 5 min to separate plasma.

FACS Analysis

Surface staining of cells was performed in phosphate‐buffered saline (PBS) containing 1% FBS for 30 min at 4 °C. For intracellular staining, the cells were resuspended in fixation/permeabilization (BD Bioscience) solution for 20 min at 4 °C before intracellular staining in permeabilization buffer for 30 min at 4 °C. The stained cells were washed and resuspended in PBS for flow cytometric analysis. Flow cytometry was performed on a BD LSRFortessa (BD Biosciences).

Splenocyte Restimulation

The spleen was collected in ice‐cold complete RPMI medium, placed on a 70 µm cell strainer, and mashed using a syringe plunger through the strainer. Splenocytes were centrifuged at 350 g for 10 min at 4 °C. The cells were resuspended in ACK lysis buffer for the lysis of red blood cells. After resuspension in RPMI medium, cells were counted, diluted, and seeded onto a 24‐well plate at a cell density of 5 × 106 cells ml−1. For splenocytes restimulation, 5 × 105 tumor cells heated at 45 °C for 1 h or irradiated under a UVB lamp for 1 h were added. For IFN‐γ detection, the cells were incubated with the tumor cells for 6 h in the presence of 5 µg ml−1 Brefeldin A and flow cytometry was performed.

Statistical Analysis

Prism 8.0 software was used for statistical analyses. Significant differences between the two groups were analyzed using an unpaired Student's t‐test. Analysis of variance (ANOVA) was used for greater than two groups with Tukey's test for multiple comparisons between all experimental groups. P values <0.05 were considered significant.
