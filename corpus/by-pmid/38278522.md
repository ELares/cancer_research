# Synergistic Viro-chemoimmunotherapy in Breast Cancer Enabled by Bioengineered Immunostimulatory Exosomes and Dual-Targeted Coxsackievirus B3.

## Abstract

Breast cancer's immunosuppressive environment hinders effective immunotherapy, but oncolytic viruses hold promise for addressing this challenge by targeting tumor cells and altering the microenvironment. Yet, neutralizing antibodies and immune clearance impede their clinical utility. This study explored microRNA-modified coxsackievirus B3 (miR-CVB3), an innovative oncolytic virus, and its potential in breast cancer treatment. It investigated miR-CVB3's impact on immune-related proteins and utilized exosomes as both protective shields and delivery carriers. Results demonstrated miR-CVB3's capacity to reshape immune-related protein profiles toward a more immunostimulatory state and enhance exosome-mediated immune cell activation. Notably, cancer cell-released exosomes encapsulating miR-CVB3 (ExomiR-CVB3) maintained its antitumor cytotoxicity and bolstered its immunostimulatory effects. Moreover, ExomiR-CVB3 shielded miR-CVB3 from neutralizing antibodies and rapid immune clearance when it was systemically administered. Building on these findings, ExomiR-CVB3 was engineered with the AS1411 aptamer and doxorubicin (ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt), enhancing therapeutic efficacy. This notable approach, combining genomic modification, aptamer surface decoration, and doxorubicin addition, demonstrated safe delivery of CVB3 to cancer cells. Comprehensive in vitro and in vivo analyses revealed selective breast cancer cell targeting, cell death induction, and significant immune cell infiltration within the tumor microenvironment while sparing healthy organs. In summary, this study highlights ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt as a pioneering breast cancer treatment strategy adaptable for diverse cancer types, offering a potent and versatile approach to reshaping cancer immunotherapy.

Introduction

Breast cancer stands as the most commonly diagnosed cancer and the second-leading cause of cancer-associated fatalities in women worldwide. The available treatment options for breast cancer encompass radiation therapy, surgical procedures, and chemotherapy. Despite recent advancements in treatments, the five-year survival rate among patients with breast cancer remains around 25%. Current anticancer drugs lack precise discrimination between malignant and normal cells, causing widespread toxicity and adverse effects. Hence it is imperative to explore effective therapeutic approaches to improve patient outcomes.

An oncolytic virus (OV) is a type of virus targeting and replicating within a range of tumor cells while sparing healthy tissues from damage. Mechanistically, OVs induce the direct lysis of tumor cells while also triggering the release of tumor-associated antigens. Additionally, they facilitate the exposure/secretion of pathogen-associated molecular patterns and cellular danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), thereby prompting the initiation of innate and adaptive immune responses that collectively combat and eliminate tumor cells. Coxsackievirus B3 (CVB3) is a single-stranded RNA virus belonging to the enterovirus family. Studies have demonstrated the oncolytic potential of CVB3 against various cancer cells, including breast cancer cells, colon cancer cells, and lung cancer cells. CVB3 displays a broad tropism for several internal organs, potentially resulting in the development of severe systemic conditions such as myocarditis and pancreatitis, which limit its clinical application. To minimize unintended effects of CVB3 and enhance its selectivity toward cancer cells, we previously generated a modified CVB3 variant (designated as miR-CVB3). In this recombinant construct, we introduced the target sequences of tumor-suppressing microRNAs (miR-145 and miR-143) that are downregulated in tumors, along with organ-specific microRNAs (such as muscle-specific miR-1 and pancreas-selective miR-216) into the 5′ untranslated region (5′-UTR) of the CVB3 genome. Our study revealed that administering miR-CVB3 led to a significant mitigation of the toxicities typically associated with unmodified CVB3. While exhibiting notable antitumor efficacy through intratumoral injection, challenges arise when treating deep-seated and metastatic tumors, diminishing the clinical viability of miR-CVB3. A rational approach to address this is systemic delivery. However, the effectiveness of this delivery method is hampered by the presence of neutralizing antibodies and rapid clearance of OVs after intravenous or intraperitoneal injection. To tackle these challenges, scientists have endeavored to create innovative carriers for OVs. These include cellular carriers like mesenchymal stromal cells and osteosarcoma cells, alongside nanomaterials such as nanogold, graphene oxides, and liposomes.

Exosomes (Exos) are extracellular nanovesicles surrounded by a lipid bilayer approximately 30–100 nm in size. They hold significant potential as drug carriers. These naturally occurring structures are released by all cell types and play a crucial role in intercellular communication by transporting peptides, metabolites, and nucleic acids between both nearby and distant cells. Exos can be genetically engineered to enhance their capacity for targeted delivery and efficient uptake. Several tumor penetration and targeting agents have been successfully incorporated onto Exo surfaces to enable precise delivery of drugs into the cancer cells. Despite demonstrated efficacy of Exos as drug carriers, recent studies indicate that Exos released by cancer cells might carry cargo molecules, such as epidermal growth factor receptor, programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), transforming growth factor-β, and other tumor-promoting molecules.− These cargos play a role in promoting immune evasion, triggering epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and ultimately contributing to tumor metastasis. The AS1411 aptamer is a nonimmunogenic and thermally stable DNA aptamer, comprising 26 guanine-rich nucleotides with a quadruplex structure. The AS1411 aptamer can serve not only as a detection tool to identify nucleolin, a cell surface protein that is overexpressed in many cancer cells, but also as a targeting agent on the surface of various drug delivery systems.

In the current study, we aimed to improve tumor delivery and therapeutic efficacy by encapsulating oncolytic CVB3 within Exos, through modifying Exos with the AS1411 aptamer, and by combining CVB3 with a chemotherapy drug. We showed that miR-CVB3 treatment of breast cancer cells resulted in a shift from an immunosuppressive to immunostimulatory profile. Our analysis demonstrated increased release of Exos with enhanced immunostimulatory capabilities after miR-CVB3 treatment. Interestingly, Exos secreted from cancer cells after miR-CVB3 infection contain the virus (ExomiR-CVB3) and can be used as a potential carrier for miR-CVB3 delivery. Moreover, modification of ExomiR-CVB3 with AS1411 significantly enhanced miR-CVB3 internalization, and incorporation of doxorubicin (Dox) further increased cytotoxicity in cancer cells, improving survival in mice carrying 4T1 breast tumors with a high safety profile.

Results and Discussion

Infection with miR-CVB3 Leads to a Reduction in Immune Checkpoints and “Do Not Eat Me” Signals, While Concurrently Boosting “Eat Me” and “Find Me” Signals

To study the impact of miR-CVB3 on different immunological proteins, 4T1 cells were infected with miR-CVB3 (MOI = 1). Western blot analysis revealed a significant decrease in the levels of PD-L1 and B7-H3, two well-known immune checkpoints, as well as CD47, a “do not eat me” signal (Figure 1A). Moreover, flow cytometry analysis corroborated these findings, showing reduced surface expression of these proteins upon miR-CVB3 treatment (Figure 1B). Although Western blot analysis indicated no substantial alterations in the levels of proteins that function as “eat me” or “find me” signals, such as calreticulin, heat shock protein 70 (HSP70), and high mobility group box 1 protein (HMGB1) (Figure 1A), intriguingly, flow cytometry analysis demonstrated the translocation of calreticulin to the surface of cancer cells following infection (Figure 1B). Furthermore, miR-CVB3 infection significantly enhanced the release of ATP, a potent “find me” signal, from cancer cells (Figure 1C). Consistently, in vivo studies revealed that infection of 4T1 tumor-bearing mice with miR-CVB3 resulted in decreased levels of PD-L1, B7-H3, and CD47 in the tumor microenvironment (Figure 1D). These results provide compelling evidence of the ability of miR-CVB3 to remodel the landscape of immunological proteins in both cancer cells and the tumor microenvironment.

miR-CVB3 Infection Not Only Amplifies the Production and Release of Exos from Cancer Cells but Also Augments Their Immunostimulatory Potential

Exos released from cancer cells often play a role in dampening or evading the immune system. These exosomes encompass a variety of bioactive molecules, including proteins, nucleic acids, and lipids, which can mediate immunosuppressive effects. For instance, they might carry immune checkpoint molecules, like PD-L1, that engage with immune cells, such as T cells, and inhibit their activity. We next examined the impact of miR-CVB3 infection on the generation and release of Exos, along with their cargo, from cancer cells. As shown in Figure 1E, miR-CVB3 infection significantly enhanced Exo production and release from both MDA-MB-231 and 4T1 cell lines compared with uninfected cancer cells with an equal number of the cells, as indicated by the elevated protein levels of exosome markers CD9 and Alix. The observed increase in exosome production and secretion in infected cells may be attributed to the ability of CVB3 to induce the autophagy process and enhance autophagosome production. As shown in Figure 1F, exosomes released by cancer cells after infection contain miR-CVB3. Characterization of ExomiR-CVB3 using DLS revealed that ExomiR-CVB3 has an average size of 90.53 ± 8.7 μm and zeta potential of −21.6 ± 4.8. Moreover, Western blot analysis revealed that Exos originating from miR-CVB3-infected cells exhibited reduced expression of PD-L1 but higher levels of HSP-70, calreticulin, CD47, and HMGB-1 (Figure 1G and Supplemental Figure 1). Notably, the detection of VP1 within the Exos released by infected cells serves as tangible evidence of the presence of miR-CVB3 in the exosomal cargo, underscoring the connection between the infection and the exosomal content (Figure 1E,G). Furthermore, we extended our research to an in vivo context, where we examined the protein levels within Exo released by tumor cells following the administration of miR-CVB3 to 4T1 tumor-bearing mice. Strikingly consistent with our in vitro findings, a congruent pattern emerged, highlighting the pivotal role of miR-CVB3 in orchestrating the production of Exos characterized by an enriched immunostimulatory cargo (Figure 1H). Together, these findings elucidate the multifaceted impact of miR-CVB3, showcasing its dual role as an oncolytic virus that induces cancer cell lysis, while simultaneously reshaping the protein landscape within cancer cells and influencing the composition of exosomal cargo. Importantly, this orchestrated transformation aligns favorably with immune system dynamics, suggesting the potential to enhance immune responses (Supplemental Figure 2).

Exos Released by Cancer Cells after miR-CVB3 Infection Can Serve as a Potential Carrier for miR-CVB3 (ExomiR-CVB3)

Currently, the widely accepted viewpoint maintains that pre-existing immunity attenuates the efficacy of OVs upon systematic administration, primarily due to the acceleration of viral clearance. Although OVs are unaffected by pre-existing antibodies after intratumoral injection, from a clinical perspective, administration of OVs systematically is more favorable as it not only alleviates patient discomfort but also can be used for tumors that are inaccessible for intratumoral injection.

Building on previous discussions, Exos secreted by cancer cells subsequent to miR-CVB3 infection encapsulate miR-CVB3 and are enriched with robust immunostimulatory properties that distinguish them from the typical Exos released by cancer cells (Figure 1F). Moreover, these Exos, referred to as ExomiR-CVB3, exhibit increased levels of CD47 that acts as a “do not eat me” signal. By strategically incorporating CD47, ExomiR-CVB3 is expected to gain an extended circulation period postadministration, effectively shielding them from prompt recognition and elimination by immune cells. To corroborate the efficacy of ExomiR-CVB3 in comparison to free miR-CVB3, we conducted a comprehensive array of experiments as below.

ExomiR-CVB3 Is Able to Infect Cells That Lack the Coxsackievirus and Adenovirus Receptor (CAR) and Decay-Accelerating Factor (DAF)

CVB3 predominantly relies on two key receptors, CAR and DAF (also known as CD55), to gain access to host cells. We have previously shown that CVB3 has low efficacy in targeting and killing cells with little to no CAR receptors. Western blot analysis demonstrated the successful cellular entry and replication of both ExomiR-CVB3 and free miR-CVB3 within 4T1 cells that are known to express both CAR and DAF receptors (Figure 2A). Moreover, the MTS assay demonstrated a comparable reduction in cell viability with both treatment approaches (Figure 2B). To determine the impact of CAR and DAF receptors on ExomiR-CVB3 entry, we utilized CAR- and DAF-double knock-out (KO) HeLa cells. We observed that ExomiR-CVB3 induced significantly higher VP1 expression compared to free miR-CVB3 (Figure 2C), highlighting the infection efficiency of ExomiR-CVB3 in cells expressing low/no levels of CAR and DAF receptors. We conducted additional experiments to assess the cytotoxicity of ExomiR-CVB3 in MCF-7 cells, known for their low CAR expression. Our findings indicate that while miR-CVB3 has a limited impact on MCF-7 cell viability, ExomiR-CVB3 significantly reduces cell viability. This implies the capacity of ExomiR-CVB3 to effectively infect and disrupt cancer cells characterized by low CAR expression (Figure 2D). Furthermore, MTS assays conducted on RAW 264.7 cells, also characterized by restricted CAR expression, revealed that ExomiR-CVB3 does not induce any toxicity in normal cells. This outcome is attributed to the genome modification of CVB3 using the miRNA modification approach, substantiating the safety profile of ExomiR-CVB3 (Figure 2D). Collectively, these findings emphasize that encapsulation of miR-CVB3 in Exos preserves its efficacy in cells expressing CAR and DAF, while concurrently offering the advantage of precisely targeting cancer cells characterized by limited CAR and DAF expression.

ExomiR-CVB3 Triggers a More Pronounced Activation of Immune Cells in Comparison to Unbound miR-CVB3

We next investigated the immune-stimulating capabilities of ExomiR-CVB3 by exposing RAW 264.7 murine macrophage cells to ExomiR-CVB3 and free miR-CVB3. Macrophages, which function as antigen-presenting cells, play a crucial role in orchestrating immune responses by releasing a range of pro-inflammatory cytokines. We assessed the expression levels of various proinflammatory genes including TNF-α, CXCL10, CCL5, and IFNB1, following each treatment. While free miR-CVB3 led to a significant increase in pro-inflammatory gene expression compared to the control group, the most substantial enhancement was observed in cells treated with ExomiR-CVB3 (Figure 2E). Furthermore, we measured the levels of co-stimulatory molecules, CD80, major histocompatibility complex (MHC) II, CD86, and CD206 on the surface RAW 264.7 cells following each treatment using flow cytometry. We showed that ExomiR-CVB3 treatment elevated the percentages of CD80+, MHC II+, and CD86 cells by more than 10%, 15%, and 25%, respectively, compared to miR-CVB3 treatment (Figure 2F and Supplemental Figure 3). However, no significant change was observed in the level of CD206 on the surface of macrophages (Supplemental Figure 3). Notably, in addition to miR-CVB3, the Exos released by cancer cells after miR-CVB3 infection contained a diverse array of immunostimulatory molecules, including HSP70 and HMGB1. Importantly, these Exos harbor fewer immune checkpoint molecules, allowing them to synergistically activate immune cells to a higher extent when combined with miR-CVB3, in contrast to the administration of free miR-CVB3. Collectively, these findings underscore the potential of ExomiR-CVB3 as a promising candidate for cancer therapy, presenting noticeable immunostimulatory properties.

ExomiR-CVB3 Exhibits Noticeable Antitumor Efficacy and Effectively Mobilizes Immune Cells to Infiltrate the Tumor Microenvironment

Having established the potent cytotoxicity of ExomiR-CVB3 toward cancer cells and its impressive immunostimulatory properties, we proceeded to validate its effectiveness in vivo. To achieve this, we administered treatments to mice bearing 4T1 tumors according to the therapeutic schedule outlined in Figure 3A. Results presented in Figure 3B,C revealed that both ExomiR-CVB3 and miR-CVB3 effectively delayed tumor growth in comparison to sham-treated groups. While the average tumor size in the sham group reached 1203.41 ± 231.19 mm3 on day 21 post-treatment, the tumor sizes in the miR-CVB3 treated group and ExomiR-CVB3 group were only 699.15 ± 117.88 mm3 and 466.33 ± 51.57 mm3, respectively (Figure 3B). Notably, the highest rate of tumor inhibition was achieved through ExomiR-CVB3 treatment, resulting in a TSR of 62.9 ± 4.2%, whereas the TSR for miR-CVB3 was 41.9 ± 9.7% (Figure 3D). It was also observed that ExomiR-CVB3 significantly prolonged survival, demonstrating an impressive overall survival rate of 83.3% after 40 days of treatment (Figure 3E). The efficacy was further evidenced by TUNEL staining, which revealed maximal tumor cell apoptosis in mice treated with ExomiR-CVB3 (Figure 3F).

Importantly, the body weight of mice across various treatment groups remained relatively stable throughout the treatment period (Figure 3G). Histopathological examination through H&E staining also indicated no discernible pathological changes in major organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney) in both ExomiR-CVB3 and miR-CVB3 treatment groups compared to the control group (Figure 3H), suggesting excellent biocompatibility and biosafety.

Furthermore, we evaluated the ability of ExomiR-CVB3 and miR-CVB3 to enhance immune cell infiltration within the tumor microenvironment. We observed that ExomiR-CVB3 treatment led to a significant increase in CD8+ immune cells and F4/80 immune cells within tumor tissues compared with miR-CVB3 treatment (Figure 3I). These results collectively highlight that Exos not only successfully deliver miR-CVB3 to tumor cells but also recruit a greater number of immune cells to the tumor microenvironment, ultimately leading to superior antitumor effects.

Exos Provide a Protective Shield for miR-CVB3, Mitigating Its Swift Clearance by Immune Cells Following Systemic Administration

Pre-existing immunity is a key challenge for systemic oncolytic virus use. Existing antibodies from previous infections or vaccinations can rapidly neutralize the virus upon introduction, hindering its ability to reach the targeted tumor site. This scenario can limit the therapeutic effectiveness of the virus, necessitating strategies to bypass or counteract this immune response. To assess the ability of Exos to shield miR-CVB3 from neutralizing antibody binding, ExomiR-CVB3 and miR-CVB3 were incubated with serum containing neutralizing antibodies against CVB3. Subsequently, 4T1 cells were exposed to ExomiR-CVB3 and miR-CVB3. MTS assay demonstrated that in the presence of neutralizing antibodies, miR-CVB3 encapsulated in Exos exhibited a 2-fold increase in cytotoxicity compared to free miR-CVB3 (Figure 4A). This finding implies that neutralizing antibodies can bind to free miR-CVB3, thereby impeding its infectivity.

We then extended our investigation to animal models to assess the protective effect of Exos on miR-CVB3. To achieve this, we preimmunized Balb/c mice with heat-deactivated CVB3 before implanting tumor cells. The schedule of treatments is outlined in Figure 4B. The immunized 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were divided into three groups, receiving two intraperitoneal injections of PBS (sham), miR-CVB3, or ExomiR-CVB3. It is expected that Exos will counteract the attachment of neutralizing antibodies to miR-CVB3, consequently enhancing their ability to reach the tumor microenvironment with greater efficiency compared to that of free miR-CVB3 (Figure 4C). The results, shown in Figure 4D,E, indicated minimal changes in tumor size and weight in mice receiving free miR-CVB3 compared to the control groups. In contrast, mice treated with ExomiR-CVB3 exhibited a significantly reduced tumor size and weight compared with the other two groups. Notably, ExomiR-CVB3 achieved a TSR of 45.8 ± 9.7%, while miR-CVB3 treatment resulted in a TSR of only 9.3 ± 7.6% (Figure 4F). Moreover, ExomiR-CVB3 contributed to a significant extension of survival rates (Figure 4G). No significant alteration was observed in body weight following each treatment (Figure 4H). Additionally, tumor tissues from mice treated with ExomiR-CVB3 showed notably higher levels of viral protein VP1 compared with those receiving miR-CVB3 (Figure 4I), suggesting an elevated accumulation and replication of miR-CVB3 within the tumor microenvironment upon encapsulation in Exo. These cumulative results imply that Exos effectively shield miR-CVB3 from immune cells during systemic administration, making them excellent carriers to target cancer cells.

Further Modification of ExomiR-CVB3 Increases Its Specificity and Potency against Cancer Cells

After confirming the potent anticancer and immunostimulatory effects of ExomiR-CVB3, we sought to enhance its therapeutic potential through additional modifications. Our strategy involved the surface modification of ExomiR-CVB3 with the AS1411 aptamer, a tumor-targeting molecule, alongside the incorporation of Dox into the ExomiR-CVB3. The selection of the AS1411 aptamer was based on its specific binding affinity for nucleolin, a protein that is prominently overexpressed on the surface of cancer cells as compared to normal cells (Figure 5A). To confirm the successful coupling of the AS1411 aptamer onto ExomiR-CVB3, agarose gel electrophoresis was performed. Figure 5B demonstrated that the mobility of the AS1411 aptamer was impeded upon the introduction of ExomiR-CVB3, validating its attachment to the Exo surface (the complex was referred to as to ExomiR-CVB3/Apt). Subsequent fluorometric analysis verified the successful loading of Dox, with an entrapment efficiency (EE) of approximately 33% (the new complex was designated as ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt). The schematic illustration of the ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt complex formation is presented in Figure 5C. The Dox release profile from ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt was evaluated under two distinct pH conditions (7.4 and 5.5). As illustrated in Supplemental Figure 4, within an acidic environment such as the tumor microenvironment, the release of Dox from ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt reached approximately 70%, contrasting with a 22% release at physiological pH (7.4) after 36 h. These findings highlight a notably enhanced Dox release within the tumor microenvironment, suggesting a potential strategy to reduce off-target effects associated with conventional free drug administration.

For the evaluation of cellular uptake, confocal microscopy was employed, involving the treatment of 4T1 cells with Dox, ExomiR-CVB3/Dox, or ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt. The confocal images revealed enhanced cellular internalization of Dox when encapsulated within ExomiR-CVB3 (Figure 5D). Significantly, the additional modification of ExomiR-CVB3 with the AS1411 aptamer markedly augmented its uptake by cancer cells (Figure 5D). This finding was further supported by flow cytometry analysis (Figure 5E), which showed a 2-fold increase in internalization for ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt compared to ExomiR-CVB3/Dox. In addition, in vitro evaluation of cytotoxicity revealed a 20% greater reduction in cell viability for ExomiR-CVB3/Apt compared to ExomiR-CVB3 in 4T1 cells (Figure 5F). Notably, ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt exhibited the highest cytotoxicity against 4T1 cells, suggesting the synergistic effect of miR-CVB3 and Dox on cancer cell elimination. Taken together, these findings highlight that the additional modification of ExomiR-CVB3 with the AS1411 aptamer and Dox not only amplifies its cancer cell selectivity but also enhances its therapeutic efficacy.

ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt Exhibits Potent Tumor-Targeting Efficacy in Mice Bearing Breast Tumors

To evaluate the antitumor efficacy, systemic safety, and immunostimulatory properties of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt, we performed an in-depth in vivo study using Balb/c mice bearing 4T1 tumors. When the implanted tumors reached a palpable size, we stratified the Balb/c mice into four distinct groups, with each group receiving two intraperitoneal injections of PBS, ExomiR-CVB3, ExomiR-CVB3/Apt, or ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt. The experimental workflow is depicted in Figure 6A. As shown in Figure 6B,C, tumor growth was variably suppressed across the treatment groups when compared with the PBS control. Specifically, mice administered ExomiR-CVB3 displayed an average tumor size of 936.2 ± 97.7 mm3 on day 21, whereas the ExomiR-CVB3/Apt-treated group exhibited an average tumor size of 639.1 ± 49.7 mm3 on the same day, indicating that the AS1411 aptamer enhances the accumulation of ExomiR-CVB3 within the tumor microenvironment, consequently yielding enhanced antitumor effects. Significantly, treatment with ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt resulted in the most pronounced inhibition of tumor growth, as evidenced by an average tumor size of 325.7 ± 102.3 mm3 (Figure 6B). It was also observed that TSR% in the ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt group reached an impressive 75.1 ± 7.7%, markedly surpassing the respective 28.7 ± 7.4% for ExomiR-CVB3 and 51.3 ± 3.7% for ExomiR-CVB3/Apt (Figure 6D). Moreover, survival analysis demonstrated a higher survival rate (100% up to 40 days) among mice receiving the ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt treatment (Figure 6E). Further insights were gained from TUNEL and Ki67 staining, showing that ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt induced the highest apoptosis rate and significantly inhibited tumor cell proliferation (Figure 6F). Importantly, body weight monitoring throughout the treatment period indicated no notable weight loss within the treated groups (Figure 6G). Treatment safety was validated by histopathological assessment of collected tissues through H&E staining, revealing no significant pathological issues (Figure 6G). This observation suggests the absence of drug leakage and systemic toxicity during treatment. Additionally, we examined the immune responses elicited by each treatment. The immunofluorescence images showed elevated levels of granzyme B in the groups treated with ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt (Figure 6H). IHC revealed a substantial increase in the population of CD8+ T cells, coupled with an increase in the number of macrophages, following ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt administration (Figure 6H). Furthermore, analysis of TNF-α and IFN-γ levels in the mouse serum demonstrated that ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt significantly elevated proinflammatory cytokine levels compared to those of other treatments (Supplemental Figure 5). Together, these findings indicate the potent antitumor effects of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt. This treatment not only hinders tumor growth but also stimulates a robust immune response within the in vivo setting, as evidenced by enhanced granzyme B levels and increased infiltration of immune cells. These results collectively validate the potential of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt as a promising therapeutic approach.

Conclusion

In conclusion, our study has revealed the transformative potential of miR-CVB3 in reshaping the landscape of immune-related proteins toward a more immunostimulatory profile. Beyond this, miR-CVB3 exhibits the capacity to modulate the cargo of exosomes, thereby enhancing their ability to activate immune cells. Importantly, our observations reveal that the Exos released from cancer cells following miR-CVB3 infection also harbor the virus, presenting a distinct avenue for virus-mediated delivery. Moreover, our investigations have demonstrated the capacity of Exos to effectively shield miR-CVB3 from neutralizing antibodies and rapid immune cell clearance, thus positioning them as promising carriers for the systemic delivery of OVs. Expanding on our findings, we engineered ExomiR-CVB3 to encompass the AS1411 aptamer and Dox, a strategic modification aimed at enhancing therapeutic efficacy. Our comprehensive in vitro and in vivo analyses have substantiated the multifaceted potential of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt. Beyond its ability to selectively target breast cancer cells and induce cell death, this treatment regime demonstrates a significant ability to augment immune cell presence within the tumor microenvironment.

Taken together, our findings point to the significant potential of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt as a leading candidate in breast cancer treatment. The versatility demonstrated by this approach also suggests its potential applicability to diverse cancer types.

Methods

Cell Culture

The 4T1 cells (CRL-2539, a murine triple-negative mammary tumor cell line isolated from Balb/c mice), RAW 264.7 cells (TIB-71, a macrophage-like cell line derived from Balb/c mice), MDA-MB-231 (HTB-26) and SUM149 (CRL-2316) (human triple negative mammary tumor cell lines), HeLa cells (CCL-2, human cervical cancer cells), and MCF-7 cells (HTB-22, luminal A mammary tumor cell line) were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection. The 4T1 and RAW 264.7 cells were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute medium supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% antibiotics (100 μg/mL streptomycin and 100 U/mL penicillin). MDA-MB-231, SUM149, and HeLa cells were maintained in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium with 10% FBS and 1% antibiotics (100 μg/mL streptomycin, 100 U/mL penicillin).

Animals

Female Balb/c mice (000651, The Jackson Laboratory), aged between 6 and 8 weeks, were employed for the in vivo investigations. All animal protocols adhered to rigorous standards for the ethical treatment and usage of laboratory animals, as approved by the Animal Care Committee at the University of British Columbia (A22-0237).

Generation of Recombinant CVB3

The construction of miR-CVB3 was conducted according to the established protocol as described previously. In brief, the 5′ UTR of the CVB3 genome was modified by inserting 4 copies of the miRNA-145 target sequence (TS), 4 copies of miRNA-216 TS, 2 copies of miRNA-1 TS, and 2 copies of miRNA-143 TS. The produced miR-CVB3 was propagated in HeLa cells and stored at −80 °C for subsequent use.

Isolation of Exo and ExomiR-CVB3

To generate Exos under sham infection (Exosham), 4T1 cells were seeded in a T-75 flask. Upon reaching 80% confluency, the old medium was substituted with fresh medium, and the cells were incubated overnight. On the subsequent day, the medium was harvested. The collected media underwent centrifugation at 2000 × g for 30 min to remove cell debris and apoptotic bodies. Subsequently, Exos in the supernatant were extracted using a commercially available kit (Thermo Fisher, 4478359). Briefly, the supernatant was mixed thoroughly with exosome isolation reagent at a 1:2 ratio and incubated for 24 h at 4 °C. On the following day, the samples were subjected to centrifugation at 10000 × g for 1 h at 4 °C. Finally, the supernatant was discarded, and the Exos present in the pellet were resuspended in PBS for subsequent applications. To generate Exos under miR-CVB3 infection (ExomiR-CVB3), 4T1 cells were seeded in a T-75 flask. Upon reaching 80% confluency, cells were infected with miR-CVB3 at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 1. After overnight infection, the medium was harvested, and ExomiR-CVB3 was isolated as described above. To determine virus titers, exosomes underwent serial dilution and were then introduced to 4T1 cells seeded in 60-well Terasaki plates. The 50% tissue culture infective dose titer (TCID50) was determined using the statistical method of Reed and Muench following 48 h of incubation. Subsequently, virus titers were recalculated as PFU per milliliter, employing the conversion factor of 1 infectious unit = 0.7 TCID50, as outlined in previous study. To produce miR-CVB3 independently of Exos, cells underwent a 24 h treatment with GW4869 (10 μM, Selleck Chemicals, S7609), an exosome-release inhibitor. Following this, the cells were exposed to miR-CVB3 infection overnight. The next day, miR-CVB3 present in the medium was extracted for subsequent comparison with ExomiR-CVB3. Cryogenic transmission electron microscopy (CryoTEM) was used to visualize the structure of ExomiR-CVB3. Dynamic light scattering (DLS, Malvern Zetasizer) was used to determine the size and zeta potential of ExomiR-CVB3.

The next day, the mixture was subjected to centrifugation at 10000 × g for 30 min at 4 °C to remove residual free Dox. Generation of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt and Drug Release

To achieve target-specific delivery, the surface of the Exos was covalently modified with the 5′-COOH modified AS1411 aptamer (GGT GGT GGT GGT TGT GGT GGT GGT GG, AS1411 Apt, Bio Basic Inc.) employing 1-ethyl-3-(3-(dimethylamino)propyl) carbodiimide (EDC)–N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) coupling. Briefly, aptamer carboxyl groups were activated by introducing aptamer (10 μM, 50 μL) to EDC (46 mg, 0.3 mmol) and NHS (35 mg, 0.3 mmol) for 1 h. Subsequently, the mixture was incubated with 1 mL of suspension of increasing concentrations of ExomiR-CVB3 (20–80 mg/mL) or ExomiR-CVB3/Dox to generate ExomiR-CVB3/Apt or ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt, respectively. The stable NHS ester promptly reacted with the amino groups on the surface of the Exos. Finally, 2.5% (w/v) agarose gel electrophoresis was performed to obtain the optimal concentration of ExomiR-CVB3 that can absorb all aptamers.

To evaluate the pH-dependent release kinetics of Dox, ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt was immersed in both phosphate buffered saline and sodium acetate buffer (with pH values of 7.4 and 5.5, respectively). The release of Dox into the buffer from ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt was measured at different time intervals (1, 3, 6, 12, 18, 24, and 36 h) using a SpectraMax iD3 microplate reader. Calculations for the cumulative release percentage (AR %) of Dox were determined using the equation AR % = D1/D0 × 100, (D1 = concentration of released Dox and D0 = the initial concentration of Dox loaded onto ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt).

Assessment of the Impact of miR-CVB3 on Immunological Proteins in Breast Cancer Cells and Exosomes Released by Breast Cancer Cells

To evaluate the effect of miR-CVB3 on immunological proteins in breast cancer cells and released Exo cargo, 4T1 cells were seeded in 12-well plates (5 × 105 cells per well). On the subsequent day, cells were subjected to an exposure to miR-CVB3 (MOI = 1). After overnight incubation, Exos released in the media were isolated as mentioned above. Then, both cells and isolated Exos were thoroughly washed with PBS and lysed in buffer (10 mM HEPES, pH 7.4, 50 mM Na pyrophosphate, 50 mM NaF, 50 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 5 mM EGTA, 100 μM Na3VO4, and 0.1% Triton X-100). Proteins were subsequently subjected to separation using 12% sodium dodecyl sulfate–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis. The isolated protein bands were then transferred onto a poly(vinylidene difluoride) (PVDF) membrane. To minimize nonspecific binding, the PVDF membranes were blocked using a 5% skimmed milk solution for 1 h. Following this, the membranes underwent an overnight incubation with the primary antibody at 4 °C. The next day, after washing with TBST three times, membranes were incubated with secondary antibody for 1 h at room temperature. Finally, chemiluminescent imaging and ImageJ analysis were conducted to visualize and quantify protein expression, respectively. The antibodies used are as follows: anti-PD-L1 (A1645, ABclonal), anti-B7-H3 (A17216, ABclonal), anti-HSP70 (A4777, ABclonal), anti-calreticulin (A11563, ABclonal), anti-CD63 (A21923, ABclonal), anti-HMGB1 (A19529, ABclonal), anti-CD9 (A19027, ABclonal), anti-Alix (A2215, ABclonal), anti-CD47 (A21904, ABclonal), and anti-VP1 (M47, Mediagnost, Germany).

To investigate Exo production following miR-CVB3 infection, T75 flasks were employed to culture 4T1 cells and MDA-MB-231 cells. On the subsequent day, the cells were subjected to either PBS (sham) or miR-CVB3 treatment (MOI = 1) overnight. On the next day, Exos present in the supernatant were harvested following the aforementioned procedure. The obtained Exos from both samples were washed using PBS and subsequently lysed using protein lysis buffer. The resulting proteins then underwent Western blot analysis to assess the expression of the exosomal marker proteins (Alix and CD9).

Moreover, flow cytometry was applied to measure the level of calreticulin, PD-L1, B7-H3 and CD47 on the surface of breast cancer cells after miR-CVB3 infection. Briefly, 4T1 cells were seeded in 12-well plates (5 × 105 cells per well). The next day, cells were infected with miR-CVB3 (MOI = 1). After 15 h, cells were washed with PBS, collected, and incubated with Alexa Fluor 647-calreticulin antibody (ab196159, Abcam) or PD-L1, B7-H3, and CD47 antibodies at 4 °C in the dark for 1 h. Following an additional 1 h incubation with Alexa Fluor 488 antibodies (A11034, Thermo Fisher Scientific), the cells underwent washing steps and were then subjected to flow cytometry analysis.

Furthermore, the release of ATP from cells after miR-CVB3 infection as a DAMP marker was measured. In this regard, 4T1 cells were subjected to either PBS or miR-CVB3 treatment (MOI = 1), followed by an overnight incubation. The amount of ATP released into the supernatant was assessed utilizing the RealTime-Glo extracellular ATP assay kit (GA5010, Promega), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Specifically, 20 μL of the 4× RealTime-Glo extracellular ATP assay reagent was introduced to each culture. Subsequently, luminescence measurements were obtained by using a microplate reader (BioTek Synergy H1).

Analysis of the Effect of miR-CVB3 on Immune-Related Proteins in the Tumor Microenvironment and Exosomes Secreted by Tumor Cells in Mice

Female Balb/c mice were subcutaneously injected with 4T1 cells (5 × 105 cells) suspended in 100 μL of cold PBS as previously described. After a period of about 10 days, during which the tumors reached a noticeable size of approximately 80 mm3, Balb/c mice harboring 4T1 tumors were randomly allocated into two groups (n = 5 for each group). These groups were subsequently subjected to intratumoral interventions of PBS (sham) or miR-CVB3 (at a dosage of 106 Plaque-Forming Units (pfu) per mouse) twice on days 0 and 5, respectively. Fourteen days following the treatments, the mice were sacrificed, and the tumor tissues were collected and preserved by fixation in 4% paraformaldehyde. The fixed samples were then treated with 0.5% Triton X-100-PBS at 4 °C for 10 min. Tissues were blocked with 10% FBS, then incubated overnight at 4 °C with primary rabbit antibodies (PD-L1, B7-H3, and CD47 antibodies). Following PBS washes, sections were stained with secondary Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated antibody (A11029, Invitrogen) for 30 min at room temperature. Eventually, tumor samples were stained with Fluoroshield mounting medium containing 4,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI; F6057, Sigma-Aldrich) and subsequently imaged using Zeiss LSM 880 inverted confocal microscopy.

In parallel, blood samples (0.5 mL) were collected from the mice and then processed by centrifugation at 3000 rpm for 15 min to facilitate the separation of the serum. The Exos present within the serum were isolated utilizing a commercially available kit (4478360, Thermo Fisher). Briefly, the serum underwent centrifugation at 2000 × g for 30 min to remove cellular elements and debris. Subsequently, the supernatant was mixed with an exosome isolation reagent (1:5 ratio) and incubated for 30 min at 4 °C. Following the incubation, the samples were subjected to centrifugation at 10000 × g for 10 min at room temperature. The resulting pellet, containing the isolated Exos, was resuspended in PBS. Western blot analysis was performed to compare the level of proteins in the Exos.

MiR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3 Comparison

Cell Internalization

Western blot analysis was conducted to evaluate the ability of miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3 to enter and replicate in cells. For this aim, 4T1 cells and coxsackievirus and adenovirus receptor (CAR)- and decay accelerating factor (DAF)-double knockout (KO) HeLa cells generated through the CRISPR-Cas 9 knockout system were seeded in 12-well plates (5 × 105 cells per well). The next day, cells were exposed to miR-CVB3 (MOI = 1) or ExomiR-CVB3 (MOI = 1). After overnight incubation, cells were subjected to Western blot analysis for viral protein VP1 detection.

Cell Viability

4T1 cells, MCF-7 cells, and RAW 264.7 cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at 5000 cells per well and cultured for 24 h. miR-CVB3 (MOI = 1) or ExomiR-CVB3 (MOI = 1) was introduced into the cells and incubated for 24 or 48 h. Following the respective incubation times, MTS (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-5-(3-carboxymethoxyphenyl)-2-(4-sulfophenyl)-2H-tetrazolium) solution was added to the cell cultures, followed by another 3 h incubation. A microplate reader (BioTek Synergy H1) was used to measure the absorbance of the solutions at a wavelength of 490 nm. The absorbance of untreated cells served as a baseline, representing 100% cell viability. The percentage of inhibition was then calculated based on this baseline.

In a separate experiment, miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3 were incubated with mouse serum containing CVB3-specific neutralizing antibodies, which was obtained from mice that were immunized with head-deactivated CVB3, at room temperature for 3 h before being introduced to 4T1 cells. MTS assay was performed to evaluate the cytotoxicity of miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3 in the presence of these neutralizing antibodies.

Stimulation of Immune Cells

To assess the immune-stimulating properties of miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3, reverse transcription quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) was employed to investigate the gene expression levels of TNF-α, CXCL10, CCL5, and IFNB1 in RAW 264.7 macrophages following a 2 h infection with miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3. For the RT-qPCR analysis, primers were synthesized by Integrated DNA Technologies and are outlined in Table 1. Following the incubation, total RNA was isolated by utilizing the RNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, 74104, Qiagen). The qPCR reaction, which contained 1 μg of RNA, was conducted using the TaqMan RNA-to-CTTM 1-Step Kit (4392653, Thermo Fisher Scientific) on a ViiA 7 Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems). The resultant data were normalized to β-actin mRNA levels. Samples were analyzed in triplicate, and the comparative CT (2 – ΔΔCT) method was applied using control samples, with the results presented as relative fold changes.

In addition, following a 12 h incubation of RAW 264.7 cells with miR-CVB3 and ExomiR-CVB3, the cell surface expression of CD80, MHC II, CD86, and CD206 was assessed via flow cytometry. Briefly, after incubation, RAW 264.7 cells were collected and subjected to staining using CD80–PE (B340153, Biolegend), MHC-II–Alexa Fluor 647 (B346505, Biolegend), CD86–Alexa Fluor 488 (B264188, Biolegend), and CD206–Alexa Fluor 647 (B266867, Biolegend) antibodies for 30 min. Subsequently, the activation status of the macrophages was determined through flow cytometric analysis. The acquired data were analyzed by using FlowJo version 10 software.

Antitumor Activity

A total of 5 × 105 4T1 cells suspended in 100 μL of PBS buffer were subcutaneously injected into the right flank of female Balb/c mice. Upon the tumors reaching a size of ∼80 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into three groups (n = 6 for each group): Sham (PBS), miR-CVB3, and ExomiR-CVB3 groups. On day 0 and 5, mice were intratumorally injected with the same amount of virus (106 PFU per mouse). Following the treatment, tumor dimensions were measured using a digital caliper and calculated using the formula: volume = length × width × 0.52. The tumor suppression rate (TSR) was quantified as follows: TSR (%) = [1 – (tumor volume of treated group)/(tumor volume of control group)] × 100%. The mouse body weights were recorded every 3 days throughout the experiment. In accordance with our protocol, mice were euthanized if any of the following indicators were met: ≥20% reduction in body weight, ulceration covering ≥10% of the tumor area, tumor diameter ≥1.7 cm, or tumor weight exceeding 10% of body weight. Furthermore, to assess the treatment effectiveness, survival rates were documented for each group. At the last day of observation, the mice were sacrificed, and tumor tissues were collected to assess apoptosis rates using the terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase dUTP nick end labeling (TUNEL) assay as per the manufacturer’s instructions (G3250, Promega).

Moreover, to study the immune stimulatory effect of various treatments, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into three groups (n = 5 for each group) and received the same treatments as described above. At 14 days post-treatment, tumors were harvested and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde. The subsequent steps involved deparaffinization, rehydration of tumor sections, and staining with antibodies against CD8 (98941, Cell Signaling Technology) and F4/80 (70076, Cell Signaling Technology). Immunohistochemistry (IHC) was carried out using the MACH4 Universal HRP-Polymer Detection System (BRI4012H, Biocare Medical) and Gill II hematoxylin solution (GHS232, Sigma-Aldrich) as previously documented. Finally, digital images were obtained using the Aperio ScanScope AT (Digital slide scanner, Leica Biosystems Inc.), and quantification analysis was executed via NIH ImageJ (version 1.52p), with results presented as relative optical density. Additionally, on day 14, tissues were collected and stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) for pathological assessment.

For the immunization study, prior to tumor cell injection, mice were immunized with heat-deactivated CVB3 (PFU = 106) three times every 10 days. Following this, immunized-4T1 tumor-bearing Balb/c mice were divided into three groups (n = 6 for each group) receiving PBS (sham), miR-CVB3 (PFU = 106), or ExomiR-CVB3 (PFU = 106) intraperitoneally twice with a 5-day interval between doses. Antitumor activity of each treatment was monitored as described earlier.

Examination of Cellular Uptake and In Vitro Cytotoxicity of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt

The level of nucleolin was evaluated in 4T1, SUM149, and RAW 264.7 cells by Western blot analysis with nucleolin antibody (20910, ABclonal). To assess cellular internalization, 4T1 cells were cultured onto 8-well chamber slides at a density of 104 cells per well. On the subsequent day, the cells were exposed to Dox, ExomiR-CVB3/Dox, or ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt, all at the same concentration of Dox (3 μg/mL). Following 6 h incubation, the cells were washed with PBS, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100, and then blocked using 3% FBS. Finally, the cells were mounted with DAPI and imaged using Zeiss LSM 880 inverted confocal microscopy. Additionally, the cellular uptake of each formulation in 4T1 cells was quantified by using flow cytometry. Briefly, seeded 4T1 cells were exposed to each treatment for 6 h, and the fluorescence signal of Dox in the cells was evaluated by flow cytometry (Gallios Flow Cytometer). Flow cytometry data were analyzed with FlowJo version 10 software. The potential cytotoxic impact of each formulation on 4T1 cells was assessed through the MTS assay. Cells were initially cultured at a density of 5000 cells per well for 24 h. Subsequently, the cells were exposed to Dox, ExomiR-CVB3/Apt, or ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt (MOI = 1 for all treatments). After 24 h, cell viability was measured as described above.

Assessment of the Performance of ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt

A total of 5 × 105 4T1 cells, suspended in 100 μL of PBS buffer, were subcutaneously implanted into the right flank of female Balb/c mice. Once the tumors reached a volume of 80 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into four groups (n = 6 in each group): sham (PBS), ExomiR-CVB3, ExomiR-CVB3/Apt, and ExomiR-CVB3/DoxApt. Mice received two intraperitoneal injections of the above treatments on days 0 and 5, respectively. The concentration of miR-CVB3 was 105 PFU and concentration of Dox was 3 mg/kg. Antitumor activity of each treatment was evaluated as described earlier. At the end of the experiment, the mice were euthanized, and tumor tissues were collected to assess apoptosis rates using the TUNEL assay. The level of Ki67 in the tumor tissues was also evaluated using an immunofluorescence technique with the Ki67 antibody (AB16667, ABclonal). In a separate experiment, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice that received the treatment as above were sacrificed on day 14 (n = 5 for each group). Tumor tissues were collected to evaluate the immune cell infiltration in the tumor microenvironment, and different organs were obtained for safety analysis. Moreover, the levels of TNF-α and IFN-γ in the serum were measured using mouse TNF-α ELISA Kit (RK00027, ABclonal) and mouse IFN-γ ELISA Kit (RK00019, ABclonal).

Statistical Analysis

Statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism V8.0.1 software. All data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD) with a minimum sample size of n ≥ 3. The outcomes were analyzed through unpaired Student’s t test or one-way ANOVA, followed by Tukey’s test to identify variations. Differences in survival rates were evaluated using the log-rank test. A p-value of less than 0.05 denoted statistical significance (*p < 0.05; **p < 0.01; ***p < 0.001, ****p < 0.0001).
