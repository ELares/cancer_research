Combined immune checkpoint inhibitor–induced type 1 diabetes: a rare but serious endocrinopathy

Abstract

Cancer immunotherapies have a class of medications called checkpoint inhibitors that can induce endocrinopathies including hypothyroidism, hypophysitis, and adrenalitis. This case illustrates a 72-year-old woman with recently diagnosed non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) who developed immune-mediated hypothyroidism and subsequent type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) presenting as diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) after 3 months of combination immunotherapy with nivolumab and ipilimumab. After her hospitalization, she was discharged on insulin. Our clinical case not only provides more literature on the risks involved with immunotherapy but also shows a rare outcome with checkpoint inhibitor–induced T1DM.

Introduction

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) are a class of cancer immunotherapies that enhance T-cell activation and antitumor immune response by targeting key inhibitory receptors on T-cells, specifically cytotoxic T-lymphocyte–associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4) and programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and its ligands PD-L1 and PD-L2. Nivolumab is a human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that blocks the PD-1 receptor on T-cells, while ipilimumab is a human monoclonal antibody directed against CTLA-4. When used as monotherapy or in combination, ICI have become an essential treatment option for various advanced malignancies, but they also come with risks that can lead to endocrinopathies.

Endocrinopathies that occur because of ICI are called immune-related adverse events (irAE). Among these adverse events, thyroid dysfunctions, hypophysitis, and adrenal insufficiency are the most common. Several cases have reported thyroid irAE, with ICI-induced thyroiditis occurring in approximately 6% to 8% of treated patients. Although the mechanism remains unclear, thyroid peroxidase (TPO) antibody and T-cell mediated thyroid destruction have been seen. New-onset type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is a rare but increasingly recognized irAE with an estimated incidence of 0.6% to 1.4%. Contributing risk factors to this complication include receiving combination therapies involving anti-CTLA4 and anti-PD-1 agents, age, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, genetic susceptibility, presence of islet autoantibodies and preexisting non-type 1 diabetes.

Given the growing use of ICI and the expanding spectrum of associated endocrinopathies, prompt recognition and management of these adverse events are essential. Several case reports and systematic reviews have documented the association between ICI and diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) as a presenting feature of new-onset T1DM. This case report describes a patient recently diagnosed with immune-mediated hypothyroidism who developed DKA secondary to new-onset T1DM approximately 3 months after initiating combination immunotherapy with ipilimumab and nivolumab for non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC).

Case presentation

A 72-year-old female patient with a history of NSCLC, recently diagnosed immune-mediated hypothyroidism, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, and osteoporosis presented to the emergency department (ED) after being found by her neighbors at home with altered mental status, slurred speech, and shortness of breath. While in the ED, she was noted to have labored breathing as well as endorsed decreased appetite and fatigue.

Three months prior, she had initiated combination immunotherapy with ipilimumab and nivolumab for NSCLC and had completed 3 cycles. She had developed immune-mediated hypothyroidism after her second treatment cycle and started on oral levothyroxine 125 mcg daily. She had no known history of diabetes prior to this admission and no prior glycated hemoglobin (HbA1c) result available on record. Additional outpatient medications that she was taking included apixaban 2.5 mg twice a day for thrombus prevention while on immunotherapy, diltiazem extended-release 120 mg daily, hydrochlorothiazide 12.5 mg daily, and rosuvastatin 5 mg daily.

Diagnostic assessment

On presentation, the patient was hemodynamically stable, afebrile, and normotensive, with a weight of 31.8 kg, height of 1.626 m, and a body mass index (BMI) of 12 kg/m2. She was alert and oriented to place, with dysarthria, but no focal neurological deficits. Laboratory studies revealed metabolic acidosis with a pH of 7.09 (reference range, 7.320-7.430), high anion gap of 28 mmol/L (reference range, 3-12 mmol/L), bicarbonate <5 mmol/L (reference range, 22-29 mmol/L), and a serum glucose of 782 mg/dL (SI: 43.40 mmol/L) (reference range, 70-99 mg/dL [SI: 3.89-5.49 mmol/L]). Urinalysis on admission was significant for ketonuria, with levels exceeding 1000 mg/dL (SI: >98 mmol/L) (reference range, negative). Her HbA1c was elevated at 9.6% (reference range, 4.3%-6.0%). A non-contrast computed tomography scan of the head was unremarkable. There were no corticosteroids used prior to or during her hospitalization. She was admitted to the intensive care unit for DKA.

Given her recently diagnosed immune-mediated hypothyroidism, there was concern for a new immune-related endocrinopathy. A T1DM autoimmune panel and C-peptide level were obtained. Results revealed a low C-peptide level of 0.1 ng/mL (SI: 0.03 nmol/L) (reference range, 0.8-3.85 ng/mL [SI: 0.26-1.27 nmol/L]) and elevated zinc transporter 8 (ZnT8) antibodies at 17 U/mL (SI: 17 kU/L) (reference range, <15 U/mL [SI: <15 kU/L]). The patient's glucose reading at the time of the C-peptide level check was 329 mg/dL (SI: 18.26 mmol/L), a repeat HbA1c was not obtained since admission. Other autoimmune markers, including islet antigen-2 (IA-2), insulin autoantibodies, and glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD) antibodies were negative. With the low C-peptide, elevated HbA1c on admission, and positive ZnT8 antibodies, a diagnosis of T1DM was made, likely secondary to combined ICI.

Treatment

The patient was started on a standard DKA protocol, which included intravenous fluids at 1 L/hour for 4 hours, followed by 250 to 500 mL/hour for the next 4 hours, and then 100 to 250 mL/hour. Additionally, a continuous intravenous regular insulin infusion was initiated at 0.1 U/kg/hour. Her immunotherapy was held during the hospitalization. After 24 hours, 2 basic metabolic panels showed that her anion gap had closed, with normalized bicarbonate levels, indicating DKA resolution. She was then able to tolerate an oral diet, after which subsequent basic metabolic panels continued to show DKA resolution. She was transitioned to a subcutaneous insulin regimen and after 2 hours, the intravenous insulin infusion was discontinued.

Outcome and follow-up

Her total insulin requirement over a 24-hour period was 124 units while on the insulin infusion. The patient's weight-based dosing for subcutaneous insulin requirements was approximately 17 units of glargine with 5 units of prandial Humalog.

Upon transition, she was started on 15 units of glargine but became hypoglycemic at 42 mg/dL (SI: 2.33 mmol/L). Her regimen was reduced to 10 units of glargine with a Humalog sliding scale. Despite this adjustment, hypoglycemia persisted, likely due to her low BMI contributing to the insulin sensitivity. Contributing factors to her weight included increased metabolic demand from her cancer and immunotherapy, inconsistent eating habits entailing low-nutrient food, and being insulin deficient limiting fat storage. Her regimen was finally reduced to 8 units of glargine daily with prandial Humalog sliding scale, which she was discharged on.

A diabetes educator evaluated her before discharge, and she demonstrated accurate mock insulin injections with assistance. She was discharged to a skilled nursing facility (SNF) for 2 weeks with a Libre 3 continuous glucose monitor (CGM) and instructed to follow up closely with oncology and endocrinology for continued diabetes management and reassessment of her immunotherapy.

A day after she got discharged from SNF, she was readmitted for DKA again and then once more 1 week later, all within 1 month of the initial DKA admission. During these readmissions, she endorsed difficulty with reading and having low numeracy skills, leading to poor insulin adherence and inability to use her CGM device. Since the CGM was not used, there was no glucose data available for interpretation during these readmissions. Additionally, after being discharged from her SNF facility, she lacked financial and social support, further limiting her insulin use. Her oncologist discontinued immunotherapy because of readmissions. Unfortunately, she did not follow up with endocrinology outpatient, so discussions regarding insulin pump use and goals in diabetes management were not able to happen.

Discussion

This case illustrates the broad spectrum of irAE associated with ICI, particularly endocrinopathies. The diagnosis of immune-related hypothyroidism raised strong suspicion for immune-related T1DM (ICI-T1DM). Our patient was already at an increased risk for hypothyroidism by being on nivolumab, a PD-1 inhibitor. What makes our case different is that not only would she have been at an increased risk of developing another rare endocrinopathy, but also her risk was exceptionally higher by being on combination ICI.

The patient had a markedly low C-peptide level and hyperglycemia, consistent with insulin deficiency in T1DM. However, it is worth mentioning that glucose toxicity can also result in a transient suppression in C-peptide levels due to reversible impairment of beta cell function. Differentiating these 2 relies on time to allow for metabolic stabilization; typically at least 2 weeks is needed after a hyperglycemic emergency to do retesting. However, if the C-peptide level is severely low, as with our patient, retesting is not indicated, as this is definitive for insulin deficiency.

Although rare, ICI-T1DM is increasingly recognized and typically presents as new-onset DKA. The relationship between ICI and diabetes remains poorly understood, and the incidence is much lower compared to other irAE. Mechanistically, PD-1 binds to its ligands PD-L1 and PD-L2 to suppress T-cell activation, thereby promoting self-tolerance and protecting against autoimmunity. PD-L1 is expressed on various parenchymal cells, including pancreatic islet cells, making them susceptible to immune-mediated destruction with PD-1 inhibitors. In contrast, CTLA-4 functions primarily on activated T-cells to prevent overstimulation but has not been directly linked to beta cell autoimmunity.

It is believed that ICI can unmask preexisting islet autoimmunity in genetically susceptible patients. Among reported cases of ICI-T1DM, GAD antibodies are frequently identified in about 50% of cases and are associated with adult-onset autoimmunity, whereas ZnT8 autoantibodies are less common and are usually associated with childhood autoimmunity, with both generally not occurring simultaneously. GAD antibodies are dominant autoantigens primarily associated with accelerated onset of disease, with severe presentation such as DKA. Our patient's presentation does not align with typical features of ICI-T1DM as described above, given how she presented with DKA with only positive ZnT8 autoantibodies as an adult. Currently, there is limited evidence to suggest whether ZnT8 provides a stronger adverse prognostic indication. One cohort study was able to identify certain risk factors that can increase the risk of ICI diabetes, which includes prior diagnosis of type 2 diabetes, being on combination immunotherapy, and melanoma with only combination immunotherapy applicable to our patient.

Combination ICI therapies are known to significantly increase the risk of irAE compared to monotherapy. A meta-analysis reported that patients receiving PD-1 monotherapy had an odds ratio of 1.89 (P = .03) for developing hypothyroidism, while those receiving combination therapy with nivolumab and ipilimumab had a much higher odds ratio of 3.81 (P < .001). In ICI-T1DM, the incidence is approximately 1% with most cases occurring under PD-1 monotherapy. Monotherapy with CTLA-4 inhibitors has not been strongly linked to T1DM, but when used in combination with PD-1 inhibitors, the risk increases significantly. One review demonstrated a hazard ratio of 1.61 (P = .0055) for T1DM with combination therapy compared to a hazard ratio of 1.40 (P = .74) with anti-CTLA-4 monotherapy alone.

Learning points

Careful patient selection and shared decision-making are essential before initiating ICI therapy, particularly in combination regimens.

There should be a high index of suspicion of endocrinopathies for patients on ICI therapy with new vague symptoms.

Routine screening for endocrine dysfunction should be integrated into ICI monitoring protocols to enable early detection and timely intervention, with prompt referral to an endocrinologist as warranted to ensure appropriate therapy and close follow-up.
