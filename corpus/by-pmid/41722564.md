# Dual PD-1/IL-2Rα targeting restores CD8+ T cell fitness via STAT5/CD47 axis in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC.

## Abstract

SMARCA4-deficient non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is a genomically distinct and clinically aggressive subtype characterized by primary resistance to immune checkpoint inhibitors. This study identifies that SMARCA4 deficiency profoundly disrupts the interleukin (IL)-2-STAT5 signaling pathway in tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells by suppressing IL-2 receptor alpha (IL-2Rα) (CD25) expression, leading to severe T cell exhaustion and resistance to PD-1 inhibition. An engineered PD-1/IL-2 bispecific antibody (bsAb) with α-receptor-targeting activity reverses this defect across multiple preclinical models by co-engaging PD-1 and delivering a CD25-targeted IL-2 signal, thereby restoring STAT5 activation and effector function in exhausted CD8+ T cells. Mechanistically, PD-1/IL-2 bsAb-driven STAT5 activation transcriptionally upregulates CD47 on CD8+ T cells, which shields them from macrophage-mediated phagocytosis and enhances T cell survival in the tumor microenvironment. These findings delineate a role for the IL-2-STAT5-CD47 axis in immune evasion and suggest reactivating this pathway with PD-1/IL-2 bsAb may represent a therapeutic strategy to overcome resistance in this subtype.

Introduction

Lung cancer remains the leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, with an estimated 2.2 million new cases and 1.8 million deaths annually, making it the most prevalent and lethal cancer worldwide. The immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), such as programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1)/programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) inhibitors, have become the standard of care for patients lacking actionable mutation. However, a subset of NSCLC remains highly refractory to both targeted therapy and immunotherapy, posing a major clinical challenge. Among these, tumors exhibiting functional loss of the chromatin remodeler SMARCA4 (SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC) constitute a distinct, aggressive subtype frequently associated with primary resistance to immune checkpoint blockade.

SMARCA4 (SWI/SNF-related, matrix-associated, actin-dependent regulator of chromatin subfamily A member 4) is a tumor suppressor gene that encodes BRG1 (Brahma-related gene 1), the ATPase catalytic subunit of the SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex. This complex plays a crucial role in regulating chromatin accessibility and gene transcription, thereby influencing cell differentiation, proliferation, and DNA repair. It was reported that BRG1 might act as a tumor suppressor and represent a target for tumor cell destruction. BRG1, one of the two mutually exclusive ATPase subunits of the SWI/SNF complex, is encoded by SMARCA4. Loss of BRG1 is associated with class I mutations in the SMARCA4 gene.

SMARCA4 mutation, divided into class I and class II mutations, occurs in about 10% of patients with NSCLC. It has been reported that nearly one-half of these mutations would result in BRG1 deficiency. This SMARCA4/BRG1 deficiency is associated with poor prognosis and resistance to conventional chemotherapy. Besides, the efficacy of targeted therapies is notably limited in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC because of the absence of specific molecular targets that are often present in other NSCLC subtypes. SMARCA4 deficiency remains an adverse prognostic factor for first-line immunotherapy outcomes in metastatic NSCLC and correlates with a suppressive immune microenvironment. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop effective treatments to improve survival outcomes and reduce mortality in this particular cohort of patients.

Our preliminary study revealed that in the tumor microenvironment (TME) of SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC, CD8+ T cells exhibit a profoundly exhausted phenotype, characterized by upregulation of multiple inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, TIM-3, and LAG-3, which is strongly correlated with poor outcomes in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC. Furthermore, we observed a significant suppression of the IL-2 signaling pathway, notably through the downregulation of IL-2 receptor alpha (IL-2RA) (encoding IL-2Rα), suggesting that impaired IL-2 signaling may underpin the dysfunctional anti-tumor immune response in these tumors.

IL-2 is a γ-chain cytokine (γc cytokine) that exerts its effects by binding to the IL-2R, which consists of IL-2Rα, IL-2Rβ, and IL-2Rγ subunits. This interaction triggers JAK-STAT signaling, leading to the activation of transcriptional programs that regulate T cell expansion, survival, and effector function. However, the clinical application of recombinant IL-2 has been limited by severe systemic toxicity, including life-threatening capillary leak syndrome, and its propensity to preferentially expand immunosuppressive regulatory T cells (Tregs). These limitations have spurred the development of engineered IL-2 variants and delivery strategies designed to achieve precise immunomodulation within the TME.

Among these, bispecific antibodies (bsAbs) targeting both PD-1 and the IL-2 pathway represent a promising strategy to co-localize checkpoint blockade with cytokine signaling in the TME. Accordingly, we evaluated a PD-1/IL-2 bsAb engineered with an α-biased IL-2 arm. Its design, which retains binding affinity for IL-2Rα (CD25) while attenuating engagement with IL-2Rβγ, enables PD-1-directed delivery of IL-2 signals preferentially to activated, CD25-expressing CD8+ T cell. This precise targeting aligns closely with the need to restore productive IL-2 activity specifically within SMARCA4-deficient tumors. We therefore hypothesized that this bispecific agent would effectively reverse T cell dysfunction and inhibit tumor growth in this resistant subtype.

In the present study, we aimed to elucidate the key mechanism of immune evasion driven by SMARCA4 deficiency and to systematically evaluate the therapeutic potential of a PD-1/IL-2 bsAb targeting this pathway in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC models. This strategy, through coordinated PD-1 blockade and targeted delivery of IL-2 signals, effectively restored CD8+ T cell effector function and was found to enhance T cell persistence via the induction of a key protective signal. These findings highlight a potential immunosuppressive mechanism in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC and offer therapeutic insights that may be relevant to this particular patient cohort.

Results

SMARCA4 deficiency drives CD8+ T cell dysfunction and confers resistance to PD-1 blockade

To define the clinical relevance of SMARCA4 deficiency, we first analyzed a retrospective cohort from our institution. Notably, patients with SMARCA4-deficient (BRG1-deficient) NSCLC derived minimal benefit from ICIs, exhibiting a median progression-free survival of only 1.6 months (Figure 1A) and demonstrating early radiographic progression (Figures 1B and 1C). This observation highlighted a pressing need to elucidate the mechanisms underlying immunotherapy resistance in this subset.

To model the therapeutic context of BRG1 protein depletion, we established an immunocompetent, orthotopic lung cancer model by implanting CMT167 cells with stable SMARCA4 knockdown (KD, achieved via lentiviral shRNA) into syngeneic mice (Figures S1A–S1C). Mice-bearing isogenic SMARCA4-wild-type (WT) or -KD tumors were treated with anti-PD-1 or isotype control antibody (Figure 1D). While PD-1 blockade profoundly inhibited tumor growth and extended survival in WT tumor-bearing mice, it failed to elicit a therapeutic response in SMARCA4-KD counterparts, confirming a state of primary immunotherapy resistance (Figures 1E–1H). Comprehensive flow cytometric immunoprofiling of the TME revealed no significant differences in the overall abundance of major immune lineages, including T cells, B cells, natural killer (NK), and myeloid cells (dendritic cells, macrophages, and myeloid-derived suppressor cells) between WT and SMARCA4-KD tumors (Figures 1I–1J and S1D). This finding was corroborated by in silico deconvolution analysis of public lung cancer dataset (Figure S1E), suggesting that gross immune exclusion was not the primary mechanism of resistance.

The absence of major differences in overall immune cell infiltration prompted us to investigate a more subtle mechanism of immune resistance under SMARCA4 deficiency. We hypothesized that the defect might lie not in the exclusion of immune cells, but in the functional impairment of the key effector population. Consequently, we focused the analysis on CD8+ T cells, the central executors of anti-tumor immunity, to determine if their functional capacity was compromised in a context where PD-1 blockade proved ineffective. The results showed that CD8+ T cells from SMARCA4-KD tumors exhibited a markedly reduced capacity to produce the effector cytokines tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) and interferon gamma (IFN-γ) compared to those from WT tumors (Figures 1K–1M and S1F). This finding indicates that the SMARCA4-deficient microenvironment cripples the functional arsenal of CD8+ T cells, effectively disarming these critical immune soldiers upon arrival rather than preventing their recruitment.

IL-2/STAT5 pathway attenuation coupled with T cell exhaustion in SMARCA4-deficient microenvironment

To delineate the molecular underpinnings of the observed CD8+ T cell dysfunction, we performed unbiased RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) on CD8+ T cells sorted from SMARCA4-WT and -KD orthotopic tumors (Figure 2A). Principal-component analysis (PCA) and hierarchical clustering confirmed distinct transcriptional landscapes between the two groups (Figures S2A and S2B). Differential expression analysis identified 4,394 upregulated and 487 downregulated genes in KD-derived CD8+ T cells (Figure 2B). Gene Ontology (GO) and KEGG pathway analyses of downregulated genes converged on a profound suppression of critical T cell functional programs, including T cell receptor (TCR) signaling, positive regulation of T cell proliferation, and IL-2 production (Figures 2C and 2D). Reactome analysis further highlighted the specific attenuation of IL-2 family signaling and IL-2 signaling pathways (Figure 2E). Guided by these enrichment results, gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) provided direct evidence for a significant downregulation of the IL-2/STAT5 signaling signature in CD8+ T cells from SMARCA4-KD tumors (Figure 2F).

Since IL-2/STAT5 signaling is crucial for maintaining T cell fitness and preventing exhaustion, we next examined its relationship with exhaustion markers. Correlation analysis and radar plot visualization revealed a distinct molecular pattern in CD8+ T cells from KD tumors, characterized by a significant downregulation of IL-2Rα (CD25) with a concurrent upregulation of the exhaustion markers TIGIT, HAVCR2 (TIM-3), LAYN, and CXCL13, while the expression of IL-2Rβ and IL-2Rγ remained largely unaltered (Figures 2G, 2H, and S2C). This transcriptomic evidence for a dysfunctional state was validated at the protein level. Flow cytometry and multiplex immunofluorescence confirmed a higher frequency of CD8+ T cells expressing the exhaustion-associated proteins PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3 within SMARCA4-KD tumors (Figures 2I–2K and S2D). Collectively, these findings delineate a specific molecular rewiring in CD8+ T cells within the SMARCA4-deficient niche, characterized by a crippled IL-2/STAT5 pathway and an acquired exhaustion signature, providing a mechanistic link to their functional impairment.

SMARCA4 deficiency attenuates CD8+ T cell function via NF-κB-mediated suppression of ICAM1

To investigate whether SMARCA4-deficient tumor cells contribute to CD8+ T cell dysfunction, we employed a co-culture system in vitro. Activated human CD8+ T cells co-cultured with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumor cells exhibited a significant increase in surface expression of the exhaustion markers PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3, compared to those cultured with isogenic WT cells (Figure 3A). This was accompanied by a functional impairment, evidenced by a reduced capacity to produce the effector cytokines IFN-γ and TNF-α (Figures 3B and 3D). At a mechanistic level, the interaction led to a specific downregulation of the high-affinity IL-2 receptor subunit, IL-2RA, on the CD8+ T cells (Figures 3C and 3D). To confirm the translational relevance, we performed an integrated single-nucleus RNA sequencing (snRNA-seq) analysis. This revealed a significant suppression of IL-2/STAT5 pathway activity specifically in CD8+ T cells from our internal cohort of SMARCA4-deficient patient tumors (Figure 3E), as compared to SMARCA4-WT tumors from a public database.

We next sought the tumor-intrinsic mechanism responsible for this T-cell-instructive effect. Cell-cell communication analysis of the snRNA-seq data revealed a notable disruption, namely a marked attenuation of the ICAM signaling pathway from tumor cells to T cells in SMARCA4-deficient samples (Figures 3F–3H). This was highly relevant, as ICAM1 is a critical adhesion molecule involved in immune synapse formation and T cell co-stimulation. Supporting this bioinformatic discovery, a significant positive correlation between ICAM1 and SMARCA4 expression was observed in TCGA cohorts (Figure 3I). Consistently, ICAM1 expression correlated positively with IL2RA (Figure S3A). This relationship was also evident at the protein level, as immunohistochemistry of patients' tissues, together with immunoblotting and RT-qPCR of cell lines, confirmed substantially lower ICAM1 expression in SMARCA4-deficient contexts (Figures 3J, S3B, and S3C).

We hypothesized that restoring ICAM1 expression would rescue T cell function. To test this, we overexpressed ICAM1 in SMARCA4-KD tumor cells (Figures S3D and S3E). Strikingly, ICAM1 overexpression reversed the phenotypic and functional impairment of co-cultured CD8+ T cells, restoring lower exhaustion marker expression (PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3) and rescuing the production of IFN-γ and TNF-α (Figures S3F–S3J). This established ICAM1 downregulation as a key mechanistic step. To delineate the upstream mechanism responsible for ICAM1 downregulation, we focused on the transcription factor nuclear factor κB (NF-κB) (Figure 3K). Its activity has been reported to be compromised following SMARCA4 loss. Based on in silico promoter analysis predicting potential NF-κB binding sites, we hypothesized that NF-κB might directly regulate ICAM1 transcription. Treatment of SMARCA4-WT tumor cells with the NF-κB inhibitor PTDC dose-dependently reduced both NF-κB activation and ICAM1 expression (Figures 3L and S3K), phenocopying the SMARCA4-deficient state. In silico analysis predicted NF-κB binding sites in the ICAM1 promoter. Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-qPCR and dual-luciferase reporter assays pinpointed a specific binding site (site 2, −187 to −178) through which NF-κB directly activates ICAM1 transcription (Figures 3M–3N). Taken together, these findings support a model wherein the loss of ICAM1 on SMARCA4-deficient tumor cells compromises a key cell-contact signal, leading to diminished IL-2 responsiveness, an exacerbated exhausted state, and impaired effector function in CD8+ T cells.

PD-1/IL2 bsAb restores IL-2 signaling and reverses SMARCA4 deficiency-induced CD8+ T cell exhaustion in NSCLC

Based on the specific downregulation of IL-2Rα in CD8+ T cells, we reasoned that an α-receptor-biased IL-2 pathway agonist might restore signaling without inducing broad, non-specific immune activation. We therefore evaluated a PD-1/IL-2 bsAb engineered to preferentially deliver IL-2 signals to PD-1-expressing T cells via its α-biased moiety (Figure S4A). Reflecting its designed selectivity, the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb preferentially expanded NK cells and CD8+ T cells over Tregs in in vitro expansion assays, in stark contrast to recombinant IL-2, which potently promoted the proliferation of all IL-2R-expressing cell types, including Tregs (Figures S4B–S4F), confirming its desired effector-biased profile. We next tested whether this targeted strategy could reverse the dysfunction imposed by SMARCA4-deficient tumor cells. In the co-culture system, activated CD8+ T cells exposed to SMARCA4-KD tumor cells were treated with isotype (contrl), anti-PD-1, IL-2, or the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb. The bsAb most effectively reduced the surface expression of the exhaustion markers PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3 on CD8+ T cells, outperforming both anti-PD-1 and IL-2 monotherapies (Figures S4G and S4H). Functional analysis correlated with this phenotypic rescue and the bsAb significantly enhanced the production of IFN-γ and TNF-α by CD8+ T cells, showing a more pronounced effect than IL-2 alone (Figure S4I). Collectively, these data demonstrate that the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb, through its α-biased and PD-1-targeted design, selectively expands effector lymphocyte populations and, within the SMARCA4-KD context, potently reverses CD8+ T cell exhaustion and restores effector function.

PD-1/IL-2 bispecific antibody shows efficacy in SMARCA4-deficient tumors

Building on the promising in vitro activity, we next evaluated the therapeutic potential of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb in vivo using a humanized mouse model. “NOD-SCID” mice engrafted with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumors and reconstituted with human PBMCs were randomized to receive isotype control, anti-PD-1, IL-2, or PD-1/IL-2 bsAb (Figure 4A). PD-1/IL-2 bsAb treatment resulted in significant tumor growth suppression and a marked extension of overall survival compared to all other groups, highlighting the therapeutic synergy of combined PD-1 blockade and IL-2 pathway engagement in this setting (Figures 4B–4D). Immunophenotypic analysis of treated tumors revealed the mechanistic underpinnings of this efficacy. Tumors from the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb group exhibited a significant increase in total CD8+ T cell infiltration (Figures 5E, S5A, and S5B). More importantly, a lower frequency of CD8+ T cells expressing the exhaustion markers PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3 and a higher frequency of polyfunctional cells capable of producing both TNF-α and IFN-γ were observed in PD-1/IL2 bsAb treatment group (Figures 5F, 5G, S5C, and S5D).

We next assessed the dependency of this efficacy on SMARCA4 status. In mice bearing isogenic SMARCA4-WT tumors, anti-PD-1 monotherapy was effective. In contrast, its activity was markedly diminished in the SMARCA4-KD setting. The PD-1/IL-2 bsAb, however, demonstrated significant anti-tumor activity specifically in SMARCA4-KD tumors, indicating its potential to overcome this form of immune resistance (Figures S6A–S6C). To establish the cellular mechanism, we performed CD8+ T cell depletion experiments in the SMARCA4-KD model. Depletion of CD8+ T cells, confirmed by flow cytometry and immunofluorescence (Figures S6G and S6H), substantially abrogated the anti-tumor efficacy of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb. This intervention resulted in accelerated tumor growth, indicating that the therapeutic activity of the bsAb is critically dependent on CD8+ T cells (Figures S6D–S6F).

We extended these findings to a clinically relevant model using a patient-derived xenograft (PDX) from a treatment-naïve SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC patient (Figures 4H, S7A, and S7B). Consistent with the cell-line-derived models, treatment with the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb resulted in potent tumor growth inhibition and survival benefit compared to control and anti-PD-1 monotherapy (Figures 4I–4K). Immune profiling of these PDX tumors showed that bsAb treatment effectively remodeled the CD8+ T cell compartment, reducing exhaustion markers and increasing the frequency of cytokine-producing effector cells, while leaving the total CD8+ T cell count unchanged (Figures 4L–4O and S7C–S7F).

Finally, we evaluated the bsAb in humanized mice bearing SMARCA4-KD variants of the immunologically responsive A375 melanoma and SW480 colorectal carcinoma lines. In both models, the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb selectively and significantly inhibited the growth of SMARCA4-KD tumors, supporting the applicability of this strategy across cancer types harboring this alteration (Figures S8A–S8F). These findings suggest that the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb exerts robust anti-tumor activity in SMARCA4-KD models in a CD8+ T-cell-dependent manner.

PD-1/IL2 bsAb reverses CD8+ T cell exhaustion via IL-2-STAT5 signaling in SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC

IL-2 signaling is principally transduced through the JAK-STAT pathway. To identify the specific STAT protein responsible for mediating the restorative effects of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb on CD8+ T cells, we employed selective pharmacological inhibitors against STAT1, STAT3, and STAT5 during in vitro co-culture assays. Inhibition of STAT5, but not STAT1 or STAT3, significantly increased the expression of the exhaustion markers PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3 on CD8+ T cells exposed to SMARCA4-KD tumor cells (Figures S9A and S9B). Concomitantly, STAT5 inhibition markedly impaired CD8+ T cell effector function, as evidenced by reduced production of TNF-α and IFN-γ (Figures S9C and S9D).

To genetically validate this finding within the relevant tumor context, we performed STAT5 knockdown in primary human CD8+ T cells prior to their co-culture with SMARCA4-KD tumor cells (Figures S9E and S10). Loss of STAT5 completely abolished the beneficial effects of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb in this setting, resulting in elevated exhaustion marker expression and a significant reduction in cytokine secretion (Figures 5A–5D). These data establish STAT5 activation as a necessary component in the bsAb-induced rescue of CD8+ T cell fitness within the SMARCA4-deficient microenvironment.

We next assessed the functional relevance of the IL-2-STAT5 axis in vivo using an adoptive T cell transfer model. OT-I CD8+ T cells engineered to express a constitutively active form of STAT5 (STAT5CA) or an empty vector were adoptively transferred into CD45.1+ hosts bearing orthotopic SMARCA4-KD-OVA CMT167 lung tumors (Figure 5E). Mice receiving STAT5-CA OT-I cells exhibited significantly delayed tumor progression and improved overall survival compared to those receiving control cells (Figures 5F–5H). This therapeutic benefit was abrogated when a STAT5 inhibitor (STAT5 in) was co-administered, confirming the specific dependency on STAT5 signaling in vivo. Immunophenotypic analysis showed that tumors from mice treated with STAT5-CA OT-I cells showed a significant increase in CD8+ T cell infiltration (Figure 5I). Furthermore, this expanded T cell pool displayed a less exhausted phenotype, with lower expression of PD-1, TIGIT, and TIM-3 (Figure 5J) and a greater frequency of functional IFN-γ+ or TNF-α+ cells (Figures 5K–5M). These findings highlight IL-2-STAT5 signaling as a critical pathway enhancing CD8+ T cell effector function in the immunosuppressive microenvironment of SMARCA4-deficient lung tumors.

PD-1/IL2 bsAb enhances CD8+ T cell persistence via CD47-mediated evasion from macrophage phagocytosis

To elucidate the transcriptional network downstream of STAT5 that mediates the functional rescue of CD8+ T cells, we performed Cleavage Under Targets and Tagmentation (CUT&Tag) sequencing for STAT5 in CD8+ T cells co-cultured with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumor cells, with or without PD-1/IL-2 bsAb treatment (Figure 6A). STAT5 binding was preferentially enriched near transcription start sites (TSSs), with a significantly higher peak intensity observed in the bsAb-treated group compared to the untreated control (Figure 6B). Peak distribution analysis revealed that 63.87% of the differentially enriched STAT5 binding sites were located in promoter regions (Figure 6C), underscoring its primary role in direct transcriptional regulation. KEGG pathway analysis of genes associated with STAT5 binding sites identified several immune-relevant pathways, including PI3K-AKT signaling, Th17 cell differentiation, and NF-κB signaling (Figure 6D). To pinpoint the critical STAT5-regulated genes responsible for the bsAb’s effect, we integrated the CUT&Tag data with RNA-seq performed on the same experimental groups. This integrated analysis identified 12 candidate genes that were both bound by STAT5 and significantly upregulated upon bsAb treatment (Figures 6E and 6F). Subsequent scrutiny of STAT5 binding within promoter regions narrowed this list to six high-confidence direct transcriptional targets: CD47, KCNJ2, MITF, SIGLEC1, STAC, and UNC5C. Among these, CD47 emerged as a top candidate. The ChIP-qPCR analysis confirmed that PD-1/IL-2 bsAb treatment significantly enhanced STAT5 binding to the promoter region of CD47 (Figure 6G). Visualization of the sequencing tracks further validated a pronounced increase in STAT5 occupancy at the CD47 promoter and its regulatory elements following bsAb treatment (Figure 6H). CD47 is a canonical “don’t-eat-me” signal that protects cells from phagocytosis. We therefore hypothesized that the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb, by upregulating CD47 on CD8+ T cells, could shield them from macrophage-mediated clearance. To test this, we primed WT or CD47-knockdown primary human CD8+ T cells by co-culture with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumor cells in the presence or absence of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb (Figures S9F and S10). These primed CD8+ T cells were then challenged with primary human macrophages for 72 h, and phagocytosis was quantified (Figure 6I). CD8+ T cells that had been pre-treated with the bsAb in the context of SMARCA4-KD co-culture successfully resisted macrophage engulfment. Conversely, genetic knockdown of CD47 in CD8+ T cells rendered them highly susceptible to phagocytosis, even in the presence of the bsAb (Figures 6J and 6K). This result demonstrates that CD47 is essential for the bsAb-conferred protection, directly linking STAT5-mediated CD47 upregulation to enhanced CD8+ T cell survival within the immunosuppressive niche.

CD47-mediated protection of CD8+ T cells from macrophage clearance enhances antitumor immunity in SMARCA4-deficient lung cancer

To define the functional contribution of CD47 in vivo, we utilized an adoptive T cell therapy model. CD47-overexpressing (CD47-OE) or control OT-I CD8+ T cells were transferred into mice bearing orthotopic SMARCA4-KO-OVA-CMT167 lung tumors. To dissect the role of tumor-associated macrophages, recipient mice received clodronate liposomes to deplete macrophages beginning 3 days after tumor inoculation (Figure 7A). The combined approach of transferring CD47-OE T cells into a macrophage-depleted microenvironment produced the most potent therapeutic effect, resulting in the greatest delay in tumor progression and the most significant extension of overall survival across all experimental groups (Figures 7B–7E). Analysis of the tumor immune landscape revealed the basis for this synergy. Mice treated with both CD47-OE T cells and macrophage depletion exhibited a substantial increase in total tumor-infiltrating CD45.2+CD8+ T cells (Figure 7F). This expanded T cell population displayed a markedly improved functional profile, characterized by a lower frequency of exhausted PD-1+, TIGIT+, or TIM-3+ cells (Figures 7G and 7H) and a higher proportion of functional TNF-α+ or IFN-γ+ effector cells (Figures 7I and 7J). Immunofluorescence staining confirmed CD47 overexpression on tumor-infiltrating T cells and validated the efficacy of the macrophage depletion strategy, demonstrating a marked reduction in intratumoral macrophage density following treatment (Figure 7K). These results provide direct in vivo evidence that CD47 upregulation on CD8+ T cells, by conferring protection against phagocytic clearance, acts in concert with macrophage depletion to enhance T cell persistence, sustain effector function, and amplify antitumor immunity in SMARCA4-KD tumors.

Discussion

The present study delineates that SMARCA4 deficiency drives primary resistance to PD-1 blockade by disrupting IL-2 signaling in CD8+ T cells, specifically through the downregulation of IL2RA (CD25), which leads to impaired STAT5 activation and a profoundly exhausted phenotype. The PD-1/IL-2 bsAb addresses this defect by co-engaging PD-1 and leveraging its engineered IL-2 arm to preferentially deliver activation signals to intratumoral, PD-1+CD25+ CD8+ T cells. Mechanistically, this targeted intervention restores STAT5 activity, which in turn transcriptionally upregulates the “don’t eat me” signal CD47 on CD8+ T cells, thereby shielding them from macrophage-mediated phagocytosis and potentiating antitumor immunity (Figure 7L).

These insights shift the prevailing paradigm of SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC from a passively “cold” tumor to one that actively engineers T cell dysfunction. This active instruction is executed through a defined tumor-intrinsic pathway initiated by SMARCA4 loss. Impairment of NF-κB activity, a consequence of this deficiency, directly suppresses transcription of the critical adhesion molecule ICAM1. The resulting attenuation of ICAM1-mediated cell-contact signaling to CD8+ T cells constitutes the pivotal instructive event, leading to the specific loss of IL-2Rα (CD25) expression. This disruption dismantles the essential IL-2/STAT5-positive feedback loop, thereby entrenching T cell hyporesponsiveness. Therefore, the collapse of the NF-κB-ICAM1 co-stimulatory axis provides the precise molecular basis for the tumor-instructed immune suppression characteristic of this aggressive subtype, moving beyond mere correlative association to establish a causal mechanistic chain. While this axis is central and sufficient, the potential contribution of ancillary signals within the complex tumor stroma warrants further investigation.

Importantly, these findings significantly extend prior observations linking SMARCA4 alterations to a non-inflamed TME and poor immunotherapy outcome. Our work bridges a gap by defining a sequential pathway from epigenetic dysregulation in the tumor cell to functional paralysis in the lymphocyte. The identification of ICAM1 downregulation as the key missing link reveals how SMARCA4 deficiency exerts a non-cell-autonomous effect, actively dismantling the immunological synapse rather than merely preventing T cell recruitment. This provides a mechanistic foundation for the paradoxical presence of CD8+ T cells without clinical benefit in this disease subset.

The canonical role of the IL-2/STAT5 pathway in promoting T cell activation, proliferation, and survival is well established. Our findings do not contradict this fundamental understanding but rather reveal that within the specific immunosuppressive, macrophage-rich microenvironment of SMARCA4-deficient tumors, a decisive bottleneck exists. The pathway-derived “don’t-eat-me” signal, CD47, emerges as critical for determining whether the functionally restored T cell population can successfully colonize and maintain its activity. Thus, CD47 upregulation can be viewed as an important functional arm of the IL-2/STAT5 axis, evolved to counter this particular microenvironmental threat. It acts in concert with the core proliferative and survival functions, thereby collectively ensuring the establishment of an effective antitumor immune response.

The data indicate that the loss of IL-2 signaling is not merely a downstream marker of exhaustion but a critical upstream lesion initiated by defective co-stimulation. The therapeutic restoration of this axis via PD-1/IL2 bsAb yields a dual benefit, not only directly repressing the transcriptional program of exhaustion through STAT5 activation but also, more innovatively, initiating a pro-survival program via the upregulation of CD47 on T cells themselves. While this pro-survival characteristics of CD47 is proven, its multifaceted functions, which includes roles in cell adhesion and signal transduction, suggests that its upregulation may fine-tune T cell activity through ancillary pathways, a possibility warranting future study. It addresses a fundamental barrier in solid tumors, particularly those rich in immunosuppressive macrophages, by ensuring that reactivated T cells can persist within the hostile TME long enough to execute their cytotoxic function.

The protection conferred by CD47 upregulation, while crucial, operates within a complex suppressive landscape. Although CD47 overexpression reduces macrophage-mediated clearance of CD8+ T cells, this engagement is not absolute, and residual phagocytic activity may persist. Moreover, TAMs exert multifaceted immunosuppression beyond phagocytosis, including the secretion of inhibitory cytokines (e.g., IL-10 and TGF-β) and expression of checkpoint molecules like PD-L1. This broader suppressive context explains why combining CD47 overexpression on T cells with macrophage depletion yielded the most potent therapeutic effect in our models, as it simultaneously mitigates both direct phagocytic clearance and the broader immunosuppressive signals from the TAM repertoire.

The translational potential of this strategy underscores the need for precise patient stratification. Notably, SMARCA4 mutation status and BRG1 protein loss are not completely the same. Not all tumors with SMARCA4 mutations exhibit complete BRG1 protein loss, and conversely, protein deficiency can occur in the absence of detectable mutations, possibly due to epigenetic silencing or post-translational regulation. This discordance suggests that BRG1 protein loss, by directly reflecting functional deficiency, may constitute a more robust and integrative biomarker for predicting intrinsic immune resistance and identifying patients most likely to benefit from IL-2 pathway restoration strategies than mutation status alone. Future studies validating this protein-based biomarker in clinical cohorts are therefore warranted.

The mechanistic dissection focuses on the consequence of isolated SMARCA4 deficiency, yet clinically, this alteration often co-occurs with mutations in other key regulators such as STK11 or KEAP. These co-mutational patterns may jointly sculpt a profoundly immunosuppressive TME. For instance, STK11 loss has been linked to reduced availability of IL-2, which could further constrain the STAT5 signaling that is central to our model. Similarly, KEAP1 mutation in the context of SMARCA4 deficiency might influence the regulation of pathways involving CD4. While the precise interplay between these genomic events and the IL-2/STAT5/CD47 axis defined here remains to be fully elucidated, their potential synergistic role in modulating T cell fitness represents a critical and compelling avenue for future investigation.

Collectively, this work delineates a coherent pathway from SMARCA4 loss to CD8+ T cell dysfunction via the NF-κB-ICAM1 axis and the subsequent collapse of the IL-2-STAT5-CD47 circuit. It redefines this NSCLC subtype as an active engineer of immune suppression and provides a mechanistically grounded, effective therapeutic strategy using a bispecific PD-1/IL-2 antibody. These findings offer a promising targeted approach for a patient population with limited options and illustrate how deep mechanistic dissection can guide the development of precise immunotherapies.

Limitations of the study

A potential limitation of our study is that the mechanistic and therapeutic findings are largely derived from preclinical models, which cannot fully recapitulate the complexity and long-term dynamics of the human tumor immune microenvironment. The translational potential of the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb requires validation in clinical trials. Another is that our investigation centered on isolated SMARCA4 loss, while in clinical practice, it often co-occurs with alterations in genes such as STK11 or KEAP1. The influence of these co-mutations on the described signaling axis remains to be determined. Furthermore, the conclusion that CD47 upregulation is a key mediator of T cell protection relies substantially on engineered in vitro and adoptive transfer models. The relative contribution of this mechanism in vivo under polyclonal settings merits further quantitative analysis.

Experimental model and study participant details

Human subjects and patient-derived materials

All research involving human samples was approved by the Ethics Committee of Shandong Cancer Hospital and Institute (Approval No. SDZLEC2024-368-02) and was conducted in accordance with the relevant ethical guidelines. The need for informed consent was waived for the retrospective analysis of clinical data and archival specimens. For prospectively collected samples, written informed consent was obtained. This study utilized several types of patient-derived materials. Clinical outcomes were first evaluated in a retrospective cohort of 8 patients with SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC who received first-line ICIs at our center between September 2023 and November 2024, with tumor response in all available cases assessed quantitatively according to RECIST v1.1 criteria (8 males; age range, 50–79 years); SMARCA4 deficiency was confirmed by immunohistochemistry (IHC), and the Ethics Committee waived the requirement for individual patient consent for this retrospective analysis. Patient 6 (P6) provided specific authorization for the publication of CT images in this study.

To investigate molecular mechanisms, FFPE specimens from 3 SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC patients were retrospectively collected from our institution’s archives for single-nucleus RNA sequencing analysis (3 males; age range, 58–73 years). For IHC validation of ICAM1 expression, we collected formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor tissues from an independent set of 10 SMARCA4-deficient and 10 SMARCA4 WT NSCLC cases (13 males and 7 females; age range, 37–79 years). A patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model was established after obtaining written informed consent using needle biopsy tissue from one treatment-naïve patient with SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC for in vivo efficacy studies (male, 76 years).

Mice

All animal experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Shandong Cancer Hospital and Institute (Approval No. SDTHEC2024006145). The mice used in this study, including C57BL/6J mice, CD45.1+ C57BL/6J mice, NOD-SCID mice, and OT-I TCR transgenic mice (C57BL/6J, CD45.2+) were obtained from GemPharmatech Co., Ltd. All mice were 6-8-week-old, healthy, and housed under specific pathogen-free conditions with a 12-h light/dark cycle, and provided with autoclaved food and water ad libitum.

Mice used in experiments were randomly assigned to experimental groups at the indicated age. Female mice were used for all orthotopic and subcutaneous tumor models unless otherwise specified. The NOD-SCID mice are immunodeficient (lacking functional T and B cells, with reduced NK cell activity). OT-I TCR transgenic mice possess CD8+ T cells with a transgenic T cell receptor specific for ovalbumin peptide SIINFEKL. Wild-type C57BL/6J and congenic CD45.1+ C57BL/6J mice are immunocompetent. All animals were confirmed healthy prior to inclusion in experiments and monitored regularly throughout the study.

Cell line culture

The human lung cancer cell lines A549, H1299, H23, PC9, H460, and H2122, the melanoma cell line A375, the colorectal carcinoma cell line SW480 and the murine lung adenocarcinoma cell line CMT167 were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium or DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin in a 37°C incubator with 5% CO2. SMARCA4-KD variants of H2122, A375, SW480, and CMT167 were generated via stable lentiviral shRNA knockdown for specific experimental applications. All cell lines were regularly tested for mycoplasma contamination, and preserved at the laboratory of Shandong Cancer Hospital and Institute.

Method details

Transplant tumor models

Female C57BL/6J mice aged 6–8 weeks were used for orthotopic implantation. Luciferase-expressing SMARCA4 WT and SMARCA4-KD CMT167 murine lung adenocarcinoma cell lines were used to establish tumors via intrathoracic injection of 5 × 105 cells in sterile PBS. Once tumors were established, mice were randomly allocated into four treatment groups: WT tumor-bearing mice receiving either anti-PD-1 antibody (BioXcell, 200 μg/mouse) or isotype control (BioXcell, 200 μg/mouse), and SMARCA4-KD tumor-bearing mice receiving the same regimens. Treatments were administered intraperitoneally every other day for a total of 10 days. Tumor progression was monitored by in vivo bioluminescence imaging at baseline (prior to treatment initiation), after one complete treatment cycle, and after two treatment cycles. For endpoint analyses, mice were euthanized for tumor resection upon completion of the treatment course. A parallel survival study employed identical treatment and monitoring protocols, with mice followed until they reached predefined humane endpoints.

For humanized immunotherapy studies, 1 × 106 SMARCA4- WT or -KD H2122 human lung cancer cells were subcutaneously implanted into the right flank of NOD-SCID mice, followed by intravenous reconstitution with 2 × 107 freshly isolated human PBMCs one week later. A series of therapeutic efficacy experiments were then conducted. In mice bearing SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumors, treatment groups received isotype control, anti-human PD-1 antibody (Sintilimab, 200 μg/mouse), recombinant human IL-2 (5 × 104 U/mouse), or the PD-1/IL-2 bsAb (200 μg/mouse). To compare the efficacy of immunotherapies between SMARCA4 genotypes, mice bearing either SMARCA4-WT or -KD tumors were treated with isotype control, anti-PD-1, or PD-1/IL-2 bsAb. CD8+ T cell dependency was assessed by co-administering a depleting anti-human CD8α antibody (BioXcell, 15 μg/mouse) with PD-1/IL-2 bsAb in the SMARCA4-KD model. Depletion (initiated one day before PD-1/IL-2 bsAb and maintained twice weekly) was confirmed by flow cytometry (>90% reduction in circulating human CD3+CD8+ T cells). To assess broader applicability, the efficacy of PD-1/IL-2 bsAb was also evaluated in humanized mice bearing SMARCA4-KD variants of the A375 melanoma and SW480 colorectal carcinoma cell lines. In all studies, tumor growth was monitored by caliper measurements, and mice were euthanized at defined endpoints for analysis.

A PDX model was established from a treatment-naïve NSCLC patient. The tumor harbored a SMARCA4 mutation, as confirmed by whole-exome sequencing (WES), and exhibited loss of SMARCA4 protein expression by IHC. Fresh tumor tissue was implanted subcutaneously into female NOD-SCID mice. After stable engraftment, tumor fragments from the second passage were used for experiments. When tumors reached a palpable volume, mice were randomized into treatment groups. On day 0 of treatment, each mouse received an intravenous injection of 2 × 107 human PBMCs, followed immediately by an intraperitoneal injection of either anti-human PD-1 antibody (Sintilimab, 200 μg/mouse), isotype control IgG (200 μg/mouse), or PD-1/IL2 bsAb (200 μg/mouse). Tumor size was measured with calipers every three days. Mice were euthanized at ethical endpoint, and tumors were excised for downstream analyses.

Adoptive T cell transfer

For adoptive transfer, OVA-specific CD8+ T cells were isolated from spleens of OT-I transgenic (CD45.2+) mice and activated in vitro with anti-CD3/CD28 beads. T cells were activated in vitro and, followed by lentiviral transduction, engineered to overexpress distinct functional modules-a constitutively active STAT5 (STAT5CA) for sustained signaling, CD47 for enhanced ‘don’t eat me’ signaling, or an empty vector as a control. The transduced CD8+ T cells were then intravenously injected into CD45.1+ C57BL/6J mice bearing established orthotopic CMT167-Luc with SMARCA4-KD-OVA lung tumors (5 × 105 cells per mouse). Transfers were repeated every five days for a total of three administrations. To genetically validate the requirement of STAT5 signaling in vivo, a subset of mice receiving STAT5CA T cells was concurrently treated with a STAT5 inhibitor (100 μg/mouse) every 2 days for a total of 3 times. Tumor growth was monitored, and survival was recorded.

Macrophage depletion in vivo

To deplete TAMs, CD45.1+ C57BL/6J mice bearing established orthotopic SMARCA4-KD-OVA CMT167 lung tumors received intravenous injections of clodronate-loaded liposomes (200 μL/mouse). Injections were administered every other day for a total of 6 days, starting on day 3 post-tumor inoculation. Control mice received PBS-loaded liposomes. The efficacy of macrophage depletion within the TME was confirmed by flow cytometric analysis.

Generation of SMARCA4-KD cells via lentiviral shRNA

Stable SMARCA4-knockdown (KD) cell lines were established in human H2122, A375, SW480 and murine CMT167 lung adenocarcinoma cells through lentivirus-mediated delivery of specific short hairpin RNAs (shRNAs), with technical assistance provided by Genomeditech Co., Ltd. (Shanghai, China). The independent shRNA constructs targeting distinct regions of SMARCA4 mRNA, along with a non-targeting control, were packaged into lentiviral particles. Following transduction, polyclonal KD cell populations were selected under puromycin pressure. Knockdown efficiency was rigorously validated at both transcriptional (quantitative PCR) and translational (Western blot) levels. All subsequent experiments utilized only cell populations exhibiting consistent and significant SMARCA4 depletion mediated by at least two independent shRNAs, ensuring phenotype specificity and reproducibility.

Isolation of CD8+ T cells from tumors

CD8+ T cells were isolated from lung tumors of C57BL/6J mice bearing orthotopic tumors derived from SMARCA4-KD or WT CMT167 cells. Tumor tissues were enzymatically dissociated into single-cell suspensions, followed by magnetic-activated cell sorting using a Mouse CD8+ T cell Isolation Kit (Miltenyi Biotec) for negative selection. To further enrich purity, isolated cells were subjected to fluorescence-activated cell sorting, and CD8+ T cells with a purity >95% were collected. Sorted CD8+ T cells were immediately lysed in TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and stored at −80°C for subsequent RNA sequencing analysis.

Source of primary human T cells and macrophages

Primary human CD8+ T cells were isolated from PBMCs by density gradient centrifugation using Ficoll-Paque (Sigma-Aldrich), followed by negative selection using the Human T cell Enrichment Kit (Miltenyi Biotec). Purified CD8+ T cells were cryopreserved at 2×107 cells/mL in vapor phase [90% FBS supplied with 10% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)] until thawed for subsequent experiments. For macrophage differentiation, CD14+ monocytes were positively selected from PBMCs using magnetic bead-based isolation (Miltenyi Biotec), then cultured in RPMI 1640 complete medium (supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin) at 1×106 cells/mL with 50 ng/mL human M-CSF (PeproTech). Cells were maintained in 24-well plates with half-medium changes every 48 h, achieving full differentiation into macrophages after 6–7 days of culture. All cellular preparations were maintained at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO2 atmosphere.

Quantitative real-time PCR (qRT-PCR)

Total RNA was extracted from cultured cells using TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Reverse transcription was performed using the PrimeScript RT Reagent Kit (Takara). Quantitative PCR was carried out on a StepOnePlus Real-Time PCR System (Applied Biosystems) using TB Green Premix Ex Taq II (Takara) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Gene expression was normalized to β-Actin by the comparative ΔCt method for analysis of relative gene expression. Primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

Western blot

Protein lysates were prepared from indicated cell lines or isolated CD8+ T cells using RIPA lysis buffer (Beyotime). Equal amounts of protein were separated by SDS-PAGE, transferred to nitrocellulose membranes, and blocked with 5% BSA. Membranes were probed with specific primary antibodies overnight at 4°C, followed by incubation with HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies. Protein bands were detected using enhanced chemiluminescence substrate (Thermo Fisher Scientific). All primary antibodies used are listed in the key resources table.

CUT&tag assay

The CUT&Tag assay was performed using the CUT&Tag Assay Kit (Active Motif) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, human activated CD8+ T cells, after co-culture with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumor cells in the presence or absence of PD-1/IL2 bsAb, were harvested and bound to Concanavalin A–coated magnetic beads. Cells were then sequentially incubated with a primary antibody against STAT5 (Cell Signaling Technology) and a secondary antibody, followed by incubation with the pA-Tn5 transposome. After tagmentation, DNA was extracted and purified. Sequencing libraries were constructed using the provided reagents, quantified, and paired-end sequencing was performed on an Illumina NovaSeq platform. Bioinformatic analysis was conducted by Guangzhou Chengqi BioTech Co., Ltd.

Preparation and analysis of bulk RNA sequencing (RNA-seq)

Total RNA was extracted using TRIzol reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific) from CD8+ T cells sorted from SMARCA4-KD and WT CMT167-derived murine lung tumors, as previously described. The quality and quantity of RNA were assessed using NanoDrop spectrophotometry and Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer, ensuring RNA integrity number (RIN) > 7. Qualified RNA samples were submitted for high-throughput RNA sequencing using the Illumina platform. In parallel, CD8+ T cells isolated from PBMCs of healthy human donors were activated with anti-CD3/CD28 magnetic beads (Invitrogen) and co-cultured directly with SMARCA4-KD H2122, with or without PD-1/IL2 bsAb treatment for 48 h. Following treatment, total RNA was extracted using TRIzol and subjected to RNA-seq as above.

Differential gene expression analysis was performed using the DESeq2 package in R. Genes with adjusted p-values <0.05 and |log2 (fold change)| > 1.2 were considered significantly differentially expressed. Heatmaps were generated using the ComplexHeatmap package in R to visualize expression patterns. Functional annotation and pathway enrichment analysis, including Gene Ontology (GO) terms and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways, were conducted using the KOBAS-i online platform. Additionally, Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) was performed to identify enriched biological signatures and pathways associated with the observed transcriptomic changes.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP)-quantitative PCR (qPCR)

The ChIP-qPCR was performed using the SimpleChIP Enzymatic Chromatin IP Kit (Cell Signaling Technology) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, human CD8+ T cells or H2122 cells were fixed with formaldehyde, and chromatin was fragmented using enzymatic digestion. The sheared chromatin was incubated overnight at 4°C with an anti-STAT5 antibody or anti-NF-κB antibody (Cell Signaling Technology). Immune complexes were captured using Protein G Magnetic Beads, and after thorough washing, bound DNA was eluted and purified. Purified DNA was analyzed by qPCR with promoter-specific primers (sequences listed in Table S2).

Dual-luciferase reporter assay

To assess NF-κB-mediated transcriptional regulation of the ICAM1 promoter, dual-luciferase reporter assays were performed in H2122 cells. Fragments of the human ICAM1 promoter (−2000 to +1, −500 to +1, or −2000 to −501) were cloned into the pGL3 vector. Site-directed mutagenesis was used to delete predicted NF-κB binding sites (site 2 or site 3). Cells were co-transfected with the reporter constructs and a Renilla luciferase control plasmid (pRL-TK) using Lipofectamine 2000 (Invitrogen). Where indicated, cells were treated with the NF-κB inhibitor PDTC (50 μM) or vehicle (DMSO) for 24 h after transfection. Luciferase activity was measured 48 h post-transfection using the Dual-Luciferase Reporter Assay System (Promega) on a microplate reader. Firefly luciferase signals were normalized to Renilla luciferase for each sample.

Preparation of single-cell suspension and flow cytometry

Murine tumor tissues were finely minced and digested in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, 0.05 mg/mL DNase I (Roche), and 0.5 mg/mL collagenase type IV (Worthington) at 37°C with gentle agitation for 45 m. Following enzymatic digestion, the cell suspension was passed through a 70 μm cell strainer and centrifuged at 300 × g for 5 m to collect single cells. After red blood cell lysis, the cells were resuspended in flow cytometry staining buffer for subsequent analyses.

For flow cytometric analysis, cells were first incubated with anti-mouse CD16/CD32 antibody (Fc block) to minimize non-specific binding. For viability assessment and surface staining, cells were first incubated with Live/Dead dye (biogems) for 15 m at room temperature in the dark, followed by surface marker staining using fluorochrome-conjugated antibodies diluted 1:100 in staining buffer. For intracellular cytokine staining, cells were stimulated with Cell Stimulation Cocktail (Invitrogen) at 37°C for 4 h, then fixed and permeabilized using the Intracellular Staining Kit, and stained with anti-IFN-γ or anti-TNF antibodies. For transcription factor staining (FOXP3), cells were fixed and permeabilized using a specific nuclear permeabilization buffer according to the manufacturer’s instructions, prior to intracellular antibody incubation. Flow cytometry data were acquired using a BD Celesta flow cytometer and analyzed with FlowJo software. Detailed information on the antibodies used can be found in key resources table.

Hematoxylin and eosin (H&E), IHC and immunofluorescence assays

FFPE tumor tissue sections from patients or xenograft models were used. For H&E staining, sections were deparaffinized, rehydrated, stained with hematoxylin and eosin, dehydrated, and mounted. For IHC, after deparaffinization, rehydration, and antigen retrieval, slides were incubated with specific primary antibodies overnight at 4°C. Immunoreactivity was detected using an HRP-conjugated secondary antibody and visualized with DAB substrate. For immunofluorescence, fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies were used. All primary antibodies used are listed in the key resources table.

Multiplex immunofluorescence (mIF) staining

FFPE tumor tissue sections were stained using the Opal 7-Color IHC Kit (Akoya Biosciences) following the manufacturer’s protocol. Sequential staining rounds involving antigen retrieval, primary antibody incubation, and tyramide signal amplification (TSA) were performed for each marker. Antibody-TSA complexes were removed by microwave treatment between rounds. Multispectral images were acquired using a Vectra 3.0 automated imaging system at 20× magnification, followed by spectral unmixing. Antibodies are listed in the key resources table.

Whole-exome sequencing (WES)

Genomic DNA was extracted from FFPE tumor tissues using a commercial DNA extraction kit. WES was performed on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform after library preparation and exome enrichment. Sequencing data were aligned to the human reference genome (hg38), and somatic variants were identified using standard bioinformatics pipelines. Mutations in SMARCA4 were specifically analyzed to determine the mutation status of patient sample.

Phagocytosis assay

The activated CD8+ T cells were first co-cultured directly with SMARCA4-KD H2122 tumor cells for 48 h in the presence of PBS (control), PD-1/IL2 bsAb, or PD-1/IL2 bsAb combined with shRNA-mediated CD47 knockdown. After co-culture 48h, CD8+ T cells were collected, labeled with 5 μM CFSE for 20 min at 37°C, and washed. Human monocyte-derived macrophages, generated by M-CSF differentiation, were labeled with CellTracker Deep Red for 20 min at 37°C, and washed. For the phagocytosis assay, CFSE-labeled CD8+ T cells and Deep Red-labeled macrophages were co-cultured at a 2:1 ratio in 96-well plates for 72 h. Phagocytosis was quantified using both confocal microscopy (counting CFSE+ macrophages) and flow cytometry (detecting CFSE+Deep Red+ double-positive events).

Single-nucleus RNA sequencing from FFPE tissues

FFPE tumor tissues from three SMARCA4-deficient NSCLC patients were used. Sections were deparaffinized, rehydrated, and homogenized in lysis buffer. Nuclei were isolated, filtered, and counted. Single-nucleus RNA-seq libraries were prepared using the VITApilote FFPE kit (M20 Genomics) on the VITAcruizer DP400 instrument (M20 Genomics), following the manufacturer’s protocol for reverse transcription, barcoding, and library construction. Libraries were sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with 150-bp paired-end reads. Raw sequencing data were processed using the VITAseer v1.0 pipeline for demultiplexing, barcode/UMI correction, and alignment (STAR). Downstream analysis was performed in Seurat (v4.1.1). Low-quality nuclei were filtered based on gene counts, UMIs, and mitochondrial percentage. These data processing and analysis were performed with the assistance of Shandong PhenoScience Biotechnology Ltd.

A publicly available single-nucleus RNA-seq dataset of treatment-naïve, SMARCA4 WT lung adenocarcinomas was downloaded from the Genome Sequence Archive in the National Genomics Data Center (GSA; accession number: HRA003939). Data processing, normalization, and cell type annotation were performed consistent with the original publication. This dataset was used as a reference for comparative analysis of the tumor immune microenvironment.

Bioinformatic analyses of public datasets

Analyses of immune cell infiltration in lung adenocarcinoma were based on data from a published pan-cancer immune landscape study. Correlation analyses between SMARCA4, ICAM1, and IL2Rα gene expression were performed using the TIMER 2.0 web server within the TCGA-LUAD cohort (http://timer.cistrome.org/).

Quantification and statistical analysis

Statistical comparisons between two groups were performed using two-tailed Student’s t test, while multiple group comparisons were analyzed by one-way ANOVA or two-way ANOVA. Correlation analyses were conducted using Pearson’s correlation coefficient. Survival curves were compared using the log rank test. A p-value <0.05 was considered statistically significant. All experiments were independently repeated at least three times. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism software (version 9.0) and R (version 4.2). All schematic diagrams were created using BioRender (BioRender.com).
