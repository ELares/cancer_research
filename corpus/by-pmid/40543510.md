# Immune targeting of triple-negative breast cancer through a clinically actionable STING agonist-CAR T cell platform.

## Abstract

Stimulator of interferon genes (STING) has emerged as a critical cancer immunotherapy target. However, no STING agonist has advanced beyond phase I/II clinical trials, as obstacles center around applying STING agonism to the appropriate clinical context, retaining it in the tumor microenvironment (TME), and limiting its T cell toxicity. Using triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), we identify defective STING turnover as a cancer state promoting hypersensitivity to STING agonism. We also repurpose a US Food and Drug Administration (FDA)-approved polyethylene glycol (PEG) biopsy marker to deliver STING agonists in a controlled release fashion into the TME. However, STING agonist-induced T cell toxicity limits robust endogenous clonal T cell response, which can be overcome by sequential co-delivery of the STING agonists with CAR T cell therapy using the same PEG marker, eradicating orthotopic TNBC in mouse models while also controlling distant disease. These findings identify a highly translatable platform to combine STING agonists with CAR T cell therapy locally for TNBC and potentially other solid cancers.

Introduction

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is highly aggressive, resulting in a disproportionate number of metastatic cases and breast cancer deaths. While the KEYNOTE-355 and KEYNOTE-522 clinical trials have established anti-PD-1 immunotherapy as standard of care with chemotherapy for patients with metastatic and early-stage TNBC, durable clinical activity is rare.

STING (stimulator of interferon genes) is a critical regulator of the innate immune response to cytoplasmic double-stranded DNA in cancer, and STING agonists are actively being pursued for cancer immunotherapy. A phase 1b clinical trial (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT03172936) of intratumoral injection of the STING agonist ADU-S100 with anti-PD-1 therapy in patients with advanced/metastatic solid tumors or lymphomas found that objective responses were mostly limited to patients with TNBC. TNBC patients achieved a 17.3% objective response, higher than what would be predicted (∼5%) from prior trials investigating singular anti-PD-(L)1 therapy when not accounting for baseline PD-L1 tumor expression. Despite these encouraging results and a favorable safety profile, the study was terminated due to minimal efficacy and limitations of direct injection. Furthermore, the rapid clearance of ADU-S100 in plasma highlighted that pharmacologic optimization could improve delivery within the tumor microenvironment (TME) to promote sustained STING signaling. Thus, the potential for biomarker-driven treatment of TNBC remains, especially when combined with optimal administration of STING agonists.

STING levels are tightly regulated in cancer cells, and specific cell states are associated with STING upregulation or lack thereof. Following its activation, STING protein undergoes Ras-related protein Rab-7a (Rab7)-mediated lysosomal turnover. In phosphatase and tensin homolog (PTEN)-null TNBC, physiological STING degradation is disrupted by maintenance of inactive S72 Rab7 phosphorylation by Tank-binding kinase 1 (TBK1)/IKKε, resulting in STING agonist sensitivity. Treatment of STING positive PTEN-null TNBC cell lines with the STING agonist ADU-S100 preferentially results in increased cytokine levels, cell-intrinsic toxicity, and recruitment of T cells. We therefore explored this biology in greater depth to develop a novel approach to sustain STING agonism in TNBC. To overcome limitations related to STING agonist-induced T cell toxicity, we further adapted a readily translatable clinical platform to facilitate local delivery of STING agonism followed by CAR T cells into the TME, observing robust preclinical activity in TNBC animal models.

Results

Sustained tumor cell STING signaling promotes T cell migration

Given the importance of understanding baseline expression of tumor cell STING levels in TNBC to help direct therapies that prime innate immunity and to potentially serve as a biomarker for STING agonist use in the clinical setting, we first performed STING immunohistochemistry (IHC) on a TNBC tissue microarray (TMA) derived from treatment-naive patients with clinicopathologic characteristics (described in Table S1). A breast pathologist visually scored each of the 63 patient samples using an intensity scale of tumor cell STING positivity (Figure 1A). Among TNBCs, 61.9% showed STING expression with intensity ranging from 1+ to 3+. PTEN IHC revealed PTEN loss in 64% (32/50) of evaluable samples, similar to prior TNBC studies. Among the STING 3+ TNBCs, 5 of 6 specimens evaluable for PTEN staining were either PTEN IHC negative or low (Figure 1B). Indeed, analysis of the larger TMA showed that PTEN null status trended toward elevated tumor cell STING levels compared with PTEN intact status, with most PTEN-null cases being STING positive (Figure S1A). Similarly, using a TNBC cell line panel, PTEN-null lines (MDAMB468, HCC1937, and HCC70) showed higher STING protein expression than PTEN wild type (WT) lines (HCC1143, HCC1806, and MDAMB231) (Figure 1C). Furthermore, CRISPR-Cas9-mediated knockout of PTEN in MDAMB231 cells upregulated STING protein levels (Figure S1B) and sensitized to ADU-S100, inducing the interferon regulatory factor 3 (IRF3)-regulated chemokine CXCL10 (Figure S1C). Rab7 knockout in PTEN WT MDAMB231 cells also resulted in a marked increase in STING protein levels (Figure 1D) and sensitized to ADU-S100 (Figure 1E). In contrast, Rab7 knockout in PTEN-null MDAMB468 cells, which already exhibit defective Rab7 function due to inactivating S72 phosphorylation, did not further alter STING protein levels (Figure S1D). As expected, PTEN-null HCC70 cells treated with ADU-S100 also exhibited increased phosphorylation of STING, IRF3, TBK1, and STAT1, while deletion of Rab7 was required to induce this degree of induction in MDAMB231 cells (Figures 1F and 1G, and 1H). We further validated these results in a second PTEN WT TNBC model (HCC1806 cells) following Rab7 knockout (Figures S1E and S1F). These findings confirm the relationship between PTEN loss and defective Rab7-mediated STING trafficking, providing models to understand how sustained STING signaling in TNBC cells might interact with the TME.

We therefore next collected conditioned medium from ADU-S100-treated cells from MDAMB468, MDAMB231, and HCC1806 cells with or without Rab7 knockout and performed multiplexed cytokine/chemokine profiling (Figures 1I, 1J, and S1G). As expected, CXCL10 was strongly induced in PTEN-null MDAMB468 cells following STING agonism, regardless of Rab7 status, whereas Rab7 knockout in MDAMB231 and HCC1806 cells induced CXCL10 in response to ADU-S100 treatment. Induction of CCL5 tracked closely together with CXCL10, while interleukin-6 (IL-6) and IL-8, implicated in pro-tumorigenic effects of STING activation, were also variably elevated. Since both CXCL10 and CCL5 promote T cell trafficking, we examined whether STING agonism could still induce T cell recruitment, utilizing a 3D microfluidic T cell migration assay (Figure 1K). MDAMB231 scramble and Rab7 knockout spheroids were pretreated with 50 μM ADU-S100 for 24 h and washed with PBS before loading into the central collagen chamber of the device. Primary human CD8+ T cells were introduced into the fluid side channel, and T cell trafficking into the collagen migration zone was evaluated after 48 h. Significant T cell migration was induced toward MDAMB231 spheroids by Rab7 knockout, and ADU-S100 treatment further increased T cell trafficking into the migration zone (Figure 1L). These findings were also validated in HCC1806 spheroids (Figure S1H). We further confirmed the role of CXCL10 and CCL5 in STING-mediated T cell trafficking, since neutralizing antibodies against these chemokines blocked T cell migration toward MDAMB231 Rab7 knockout spheroids treated with ADU-S100 (Figure 1M).

Finally, we compared these findings with pharmacologic inhibition of Rab7 using the Rab7 inhibitor CID1067700. To establish on-target activity of CID1067700, we examined STING colocalization with lysosomes in ADU-S100-stimulated MDAMB231 cells, which was disrupted by Rab7 inhibition (Figures S2A–S2D; two independent experiments). Addition of CID1067700 thus sensitized MDAMB231 and HCC1806 PTEN WT cell lines to ADU-S100-mediated cytotoxicity (Figures S2E–S2G). PTEN knockout and Rab7 inhibition in MDAMB231 further sensitized cells to ADU-S100 (Figures S2H and S2I). Thus, pharmacologic disruption of STING lysosomal trafficking in MDAMB231 spheroids following ADU-S100 treatment also induced significant migration of Jurkat cells (Figures S2J–S2L; two independent experiments) in a CXCR3-dependent manner (Figures S2M and S2N). Taken together, these data reveal that sustaining tumor cell STING signaling in TNBC cells enhances STING agonist response and facilitates T cell migration.

A clinical PEG hydrogel biopsy marker can be repurposed to deliver the STING agonist locally into TNBC

We next examined sustained STING agonist delivery as an alternative to co-opt this biology, repurposing implantable devices already utilized in breast cancer management. In standard breast radiology practice, after a patient undergoes a biopsy, a metal marker is deployed. More recently, to optimize visualization on ultrasound, metal markers are embedded in hydrogels that expand within the breast. These markers are US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved and consist of polyethylene glycol (PEG), collagen, or beta-glucan. Once hydrated, they fully expand within 24 h, with the PEG hydrogel increasing proportionately the most in volume (Figures 2A and S3A), absorbing at least 250–300 μL of double distilled water (ddH2O), which is more than adequate to deliver a desired drug. PEGs promote sustained-release drug delivery with a well-established safety profile and non-immunogenic degradation, and we confirmed the ability of this PEG marker to expand in mice for in vivo testing of drug delivery (Figures 2B and S3B). We also noted that the hydrophilic nature of this PEG marker is ideal for retaining water-soluble STING agonists such as ADU-S100.

We therefore developed a THP-1 macrophage CXCL10 reporter assay to quantify slow release of ADU-S100 over time, stimulating the macrophages with known ADU-S100 concentrations as a reference (Figure 2C). We reconstituted ADU-S100 (100 μg) into 20 μL of ddH2O, allowed the PEG hydrogel to absorb the drug, embedded PEG containing ADU-S100 in collagen, and collected 1 mL of fresh conditioned medium every 24 h for THP-1 macrophage treatment (Figures 2D and S3C). The PEG hydrogel was able to control slow release of ADU-S100 over 5 days, releasing a total of 70.8 μg of the embedded 100 μg by day 5. Unlike ADU-S100, 2′3′ cyclic guanosine monophosphate-adenosine monophosphate (cGAMP) release was limited, likely due to ectonucleotide pyrophosphatase/phosphodiesterase 1-mediated degradation (Figure S3C). Moreover, collagen and beta-glucan breast markers were also less effective at releasing ADU-S100 compared with PEG (Figures 2E and S3D).

Based on these promising data, we next tested PEG marker ADU-S100 delivery in vivo using the well-established TNBC 4T1 syngeneic model. The 4T1 model was also used to test ADU-S100 delivery via PEG lipid nanodiscs, exhibiting limited response compared to MC38 tumors. Given the aggressiveness of 4T1 tumors, we first implanted PEG markers containing PBS versus 100 μg of ADU-S100 in a low-tumor-burden model on day 4 after injecting 10,000 4T1 cells orthotopically into the fourth mammary fat pad. PEG+ADU-S100 prevented 4T1 tumor establishment, whereas PEG+PBS showed robust tumor growth (Figure 2F). However, when PEG+ADU-S100 was implanted adjacent to an established 4T1 tumor, growth was inhibited initially, but outgrowth was observed by day 20, similar to the prior nanodisc study (Figure 2G). We also compared these results with direct intratumoral injection of ADU-S100 and observed minimal response compared to PEG delivery (Figure 2H).

To examine on-target tumor-intrinsic effects of PEG-delivered ADU-S100, established 4T1 mouse tumors were first stained for pTBK1 and pSTAT1 on day 4, which confirmed ADU-S100-induced STING pathway activation (Figures 2I and S3E). Next, we performed single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) on CD45+ immune cells from 4T1 tumors 14 days after treatment (Figure 2J). Clustering identified 5 different immune cell types (Figure 2K). Antigen-presenting cell (APC) and macrophage clusters exhibited robust upregulation of interferon (IFN)-induced genes by PEG-delivered ADU-S100, including lfitm3, B2m, Isg15, Irf7, Cxcl2, and Cxcl10 (Figure 2L) and hallmark IFN gene signatures (Figures 2M and 2N). ADU-S100 treatment also increased BATF3-expressing dendritic cells in the APC cluster specifically, consistent with prior literature (Figures S3F and S3G; Table S3). Taken together, these findings confirmed effective PEG marker delivery of ADU-S100 into the TME despite the lack of durable tumor control.

STING agonist delivery to TNBC is toxic to endogenous T cells but can be mitigated through PEG delivery

Prior studies have shown that excessive STING agonism is toxic to T cells. We therefore investigated this as a potential contributor to the limited 4T1 antitumor activity. Since we recently demonstrated ADU-S100-induced T cell toxicity in human explanted tumors, we examined whether this finding extends to human TNBC. Indeed, we observed a marked decrease in CD3+, CD4+, and CD8+ T cells induced by ADU-S100 treatment of a primary human TNBC explanted tumor (Figure 3A). We also treated primary peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC)-derived human CD8+ T cells with increasing concentrations of ADU-S100 and observed T cell death (Figure 3B), similar to mouse splenocyte-derived T cells (Figure 3C).

We thus examined whether slow-release PEG marker ADU-S100 delivery could mitigate T cell toxicity. We first isolated human CD8+ T cells and treated them with conditioned medium collected every 24 h from the PEG marker embedded with 100 μg of ADU-S100 as in Figure 2C. Despite T cell toxicity over the initial 24- to 48-h period of ADU-S100 release from the PEG, toxicity was less when compared with free STING agonist and reduced with time (Figure 3D). We also examined the effect of cumulative ADU-S100 release on T cells. Human CD8+ T cells were plated in triplicate and then treated directly with PBS or 100 μg ADU-S100 as compared with PEG-delivered PBS or 100 μg ADU-S100. T cell toxicity on day 5 was significantly higher with the direct ADU-S100 treatment versus embedding the drug in the PEG, which was also evident by impairment of subsequent T cell expansion in IL-2, IL-7, and IL-15 (Figure 3E). Indeed, we confirmed the long-term rebound of PEG+ADU-S100-exposed T cells in contrast to direct ADU-S100 treatment (Figure 3F).

Despite mitigating T cell toxicity by PEG-mediated ADU-S100 delivery, we still wondered whether immune escape in the 4T1 model could be related to the failure of endogenous T cell clonal expansion. We therefore performed T cell receptor sequencing (TCR-seq) on CD45+ immune cells isolated from animals treated in Figure 2J. We identified numerous clonotype-positive T cells (Figure 3G) but found no evidence of unique clonotype expansion in either group of mice. We also performed scRNA-seq and TCR-seq on PBMCs obtained from mice that had received PEG+PBS or PEG+ADU-S100 on day 4 and had a second marker placed on day 30 to maximize STING agonist delivery, collecting peripheral blood on day 50 (Figures S4A–S4C). This also confirmed no circulating clonotypic T cell expansion in either condition (Figure 3H). Tumor-infiltrating T cells exposed to PEG+ADU-S100 still exhibited IFN signature activation (Figures S4D–S4F), associated with depletion of CD4+ and CD8+ T cell subpopulations as well as memory T cells (Figures S4G–S4J), likely responsible for impairing antigen-specific T cell expansion.

TNBC tumor cell STING signaling primes response to CAR T cell therapy

Given the emerging evidence for anti-tumor efficacy of local CAR T cell delivery, we considered whether a multimodal delivery system of STING agonists with CAR T cells could overcome this limitation. Furthermore, addition of STING agonism to CAR T therapy in an aggressive breast cancer murine model primed the TME to increase recruitment of CAR T cells. We focused on mesothelin (MSLN) targeting CAR T cells as a well-established model, since 67% of TNBCs have high expression of MSLN, and MSLN expression in TNBC is associated with aggressive biology. Positive cell-surface MSLN expression was identified in PTEN WT HCC1806 cells and PTEN-null HCC70 cells by immunoblot and flow cytometry, in contrast to HCC1143 cells (PTEN WT/MSLN negative) (Figures 4A and 4B). We therefore generated multiple labeled versions of MSLN CAR T and control CAR T cells (Figure 4C) and confirmed high-level CAR expression (Figures S5A and S5B). MSLN-negative HCC1143 cells exhibited negligible cell killing by MSLN CAR T cells in 2D co-culture across different effector:target (E:T) cell ratios (Figure 4D). In contrast, MSLN CAR T cells effectively killed both HCC1806 and HCC70 cell lines across E:T ratios, relative to control CAR T cells, which only showed alloreactive cytotoxicity at high doses (Figure 4E).

We then utilized the 3D microfluidics assay to investigate cytotoxic killing of tumor spheroids by CAR T cells. Co-culturing MSLN CAR T cells directly with HCC1806 spheroids in collagen yielded effective tumor cell killing as compared with control CAR T cells (Figure 4F). Results were also confirmed in HCC70 spheroids (Figure 4G), and quantification of cell death showed that MSLN CAR T cells achieved nearly 100% cell death when co-cultured with both HCC1806 and HCC70 but not with control HCC1143 spheroids (Figure 4H). Conditioned medium was also analyzed for IFNγ, granzyme B, and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNFα), which confirmed on-target MSLN CAR T cell activation (Figure 4I).

We next measured CAR T cell migration into the central collagen chamber from the medium-filled side channel (Figure 1K). As expected, untreated PTEN-null HCC70 spheroids, which basally activate STING, recruited MSLN CAR T cells to a significantly greater degree than PTEN WT HCC1806 spheroids, correlating with differential tumor spheroid killing (Figure 4J). HCC1806 Rab7 knockout (KO) spheroids, especially when pretreated with ADU-S100, recruited and activated MSLN CAR T cells, resulting in cell death and production of effector cytokines (Figure 4K). Together, these data further establish the role of sustained STING activation in promoting CAR T cell recruitment while credentialing the PTEN-null HCC70 model for in vivo testing.

PEG biopsy marker-mediated local delivery of CAR T cells into TNBC

To evaluate PEG marker delivery of CAR T cells, we soaked the PEG with NanoLuc-MSLN CAR T cells (4 × 106 cells) or PBS and imaged markers using the Xenogen bioluminescence system. Whereas the control PEG showed a background signal, PEG+NanoLuc-MSLN CAR T cells displayed a strong luciferase signal, confirming effective CAR T cell absorption (Figure 5A). Since concurrent STING agonist/CAR T cell loading could be toxic to T cells, however, we also explored CAR T cell injection into a previously hydrated PEG marker. PEG markers were hydrated and then injected with either PBS or NanoLuc-MSLN CAR T cells (4 × 106 cells), confirming robust CAR T cell retention (Figure 5B). To ensure that the PEG hydrogel was not selectively toxic to the CAR T cells, we also soaked or injected NanoLuc-tagged CAR T cells into the PEG marker and compared the luciferase signal of increasing amounts of CAR T cells in a comparable volume of collagen under bioluminescence imaging. CAR T cells showed significantly higher signal when embedded in the PEG versus collagen (Figure S5C).

To determine whether soaked or injected CAR T cells can be released by the PEG marker and result in killing, we generated MSLN-targeting versus control CAR T cells using lentiviral vectors containing Vex-GFP for facile CAR T detection (Figure S5B). Consistent with previous results, direct co-culture of MSLN CAR T cells with firefly luciferase-tagged HCC70 (ffLUC-HCC70) spheroids resulted in significant cell death compared to control CAR T cells (Figure S5D). ffLUC-HCC70 spheroids were thus treated with either MSLN or control CAR T cells from biopsy markers (PEG, collagen, or beta-glucan) at different E:T ratios. After 24 h, ffLUC-HCC70 spheroids were harvested for a luciferase assay to analyze viability (Figure 5C). MSLN CAR T cells delivered by the PEG marker, especially when injected, resulted in potent and specific targeting compared to control CAR T cells (Figures 5D and 5E). Both the collagen and beta-glucan markers showed some efficacy in delivering CAR T cells (Figures S5E and S5F), but because of their suboptimal expansion properties and ADU-S100 release (Figures 2A and 2E), we chose the PEG marker for subsequent in vivo studies.

To test CAR T cell delivery in vivo, we next orthotopically established HCC70 tumor xenografts in the fourth mammary fat pad of NOD SCID gamma (NSG) mice and implanted PEG+PBS or PEG+MSLN CAR T cells immediately adjacent to the cancer. We first performed a short-term in vivo experiment, performing human CD3 IHC on day 14 tumors to ensure that CAR T cells could migrate intratumorally from the PEG. Indeed, CAR T cells trafficked effectively into both the tumor periphery and interior (Figure 5F). Repeating this experiment to examine longer-term durability revealed that MSLN CAR T cells controlled growth of HCC70 tumor xenografts by day 20 (Figure S5G), with persistent CAR T cell infiltration on day 42 (Figure 5G). Given these promising results, we treated n = 6 per group of orthotopically established HCC70 tumor xenografts (size 150 mm3) with PEGs soaked with NanoLuc-tagged MSLN or control CD19 CAR T cells delivered in equal numbers (4 × 106 cells), using in vivo imaging to detect CAR T cell expansion. MSLN CAR T cells robustly expanded intratumorally, in contrast to control CD19 CAR T cells (Figure S5H). Consistent with this finding, all control CAR T cell-treated mice were euthanized by 9 weeks due to tumor progression, whereas 3 mice were still alive in the MSLN-treated group at the 12-weeks study endpoint, with decreased tumor burden (Figure S5I) and overall improved survival (Figure S5J). MSLN CAR T cell-treated mice (#3 and #4) with strong luciferase signals at week 2 had tumors that were no longer palpable by week 3. While mouse #4 demonstrated continued CAR T cell expansion and tumor-free survival in the subsequent weeks, mouse #3 exhibited recurrent tumor growth at week 7 with subsequent reappearance of luciferase signal. Interestingly, IHC staining of this tumor outgrowth showed a specific absence of MSLN (Figure S5K), confirming escape due to loss of antigen and, thus, on-target activity of MSLN CAR T cells.

Given this suboptimal durable response of tumors with CAR T monotherapy, we next explored sequential delivery of ADU-S100 followed by CAR T cells. Unlike other polymers, which degrade within days of implantation, the PEG marker remained intact and palpable well beyond 35 days (Figures 5H and S5L). To ensure that the majority of the ADU-S100 was released from the PEG marker by day 7, PEGs hydrated with ADU-S100 or PBS were implanted into mice, and after 7 days, these PEGs were retrieved and co-cultured with MSLN CAR T cells in vitro, revealing no significant CAR T cell cytotoxicity (Figures 5I and 5J). We therefore tested implantation of PEG markers hydrated with PBS or ADU-S100 adjacent to orthotopic HCC70 tumors 7 days before injecting MSLN CAR T cells into the PEG, henceforth referred to as the sequential delivery PEG method or the sd-PEG method (Figure 5K). ADU-S100 pre-release induced a significant increase in CAR T cell infiltration within 2 days, as evidenced by increased human CD3 IHC staining of HCC70 tumors (Figure 5L) and flow cytometry (Figures 5M and 5N). These data confirm the extended capability of this PEG marker platform to prime tumors with STING agonism slow release and temporally administer CAR T cells afterward to mitigate T cell toxicity.

Sequential local delivery of STING agonist followed by CAR T cells through a PEG hydrogel biopsy marker results in long-term durable control of TNBC growth

We therefore proceeded to test combination therapy in the same HCC70 xenograft model. We compared multiple potential co-delivery methods with the sd-PEG method (Figure 5K) to ensure superiority to (1) directly injecting ADU-S100 into the tumor and then, 3 days later, directly injecting CAR T cells without a PEG and (2) implanting two separate PEG markers containing ADU-S100 or CAR T cells. Within 4 weeks, it was evident that the sd-PEG method resulted in the most robust expansion of intratumoral CAR T cells (Figures 6A and 6B). We therefore expanded its study in orthotopically established tumors using different MSLN CAR T cell numbers (Figure 6C), keeping ADU-S100 constant at 100 μg. The relative impact of ADU-S100 on tumor growth was especially apparent following downtitration of MSLN CAR T cells to as low as 0.5 × 106 T cells per PEG (Figures 6C and S6A, second independent experiment), exhibiting greater intratumoral CAR T cell expansion (Figure 6D) and higher levels of IFNγ in peripheral blood (Figure S6B).

Given these promising results using the sd-PEG method, we performed a large-scale study using two different MSLN CAR T cell concentrations (2 × 106 and 4 × 106) with ADU-S100 priming (100 μg) to evaluate durable control of orthotopic HCC70 tumor growth (Figure 6E). By week 5, 3 mice of 5 from the control PBS+CD19 group had to be sacrificed, given marked tumor growth. Comparatively, all PBS- or ADU-S100-primed tumors responded to MSLN CAR T cells at the 4 × 106 cell dose (MSLN4) and the 2 × 106 cell dose (MSLN2). Furthermore, while the higher dose of CAR T cells (MSLN4) itself led to durable tumor control, downtitrating the injected MSLN CAR T dose to 2 × 106 cells (MSLN2) unveiled the distinct ability of ADU-S100 priming to prevent tumor escape and promote durable eradication of these TNBC xenografts up to 120 days (Figures 6F and S6C, second independent study; Figure S6D, third independent study; and Figures S6E and S6F, study repeated in another TNBC (HCC1806) model). Relapse-free survival (RFS) at 120 days was markedly improved among all MSLN CAR T cell-treated groups versus the CD19 CAR T cell group (Figure 6G). Furthermore, with the lower dosage (2 × 106) of MSLN CAR T cells, ADU-S100 priming achieved 100% RFS at 120 days versus 40% with MSLN CAR T cell monotherapy, further supporting the additive effects of this combination therapy. We also obtained peripheral blood from all five groups on days 7, 14, and 21 after CAR T cell treatment to measure IFNγ levels (Figure 6H). By day 7, there was a trend toward higher levels of IFNγ among all treatment groups, which was enhanced by ADU-S100 priming. Furthermore, MSLN IHC staining of the three tumors that regrew with PBS+MSLN (2 × 106) monotherapy revealed weak or absent MSLN staining relative to the control, again demonstrating loss of tumor antigen as a mechanism of escape and on-target effects of the MSLN CAR T cells (Figure 6I).

Local delivery of STING agonist followed by CAR T cells with the sd-PEG method results in effective control of distant TNBC growth

Since STING agonism can promote vascular remodeling and T cell adhesion, we evaluated whether priming of tumors with ADU-S100 in the xenografted TME could enhance CAR T cell extravasation. Regardless of the dosage of MSLN CAR T cells (0.25 × 106, 0.5 × 106, 2 × 106, and 4 × 106), combination therapy with ADU-S100 resulted in a striking elevation of circulating CD3+ T cells by flow cytometry versus MSLN CAR T monotherapy (Figures 7A, S7A, and S7B, additional TNBC cell line HCC1806).

We therefore evaluated the potential of this platform to treat distant disease sites. We first tested a dual tumor model by establishing HCC70 tumor xenografts (150 mm3) in the fourth mammary fat pad and ipsilateral flank (Figure 7B). Using the sd-PEG method with ADU-S100 or PBS, followed 7 days later by MSLN CAR T cells (2 × 106 and 4 × 106) or CD19 CAR T cells, treatment of the primary site with ADU-S100+MSLN CAR T cells not only eradicated disease locally but also contained distal flank tumor growth (Figure 7C, independent experiment #1 and 7D, independent experiment #2). Although some mice treated with PBS+MSLN CAR T cells showed growth-inhibitory effects of both the primary and flank tumors, this was less consistent than with ADU-S100 priming, which also resulted in more robust intratumoral CAR T cell expansion in the ipsilateral flank (Figure 7E). Furthermore, there was a marked difference in probability of survival at 120 days that favored MSLN CAR T groups that had received ADU-S100 priming (Figures 7F and 7G), with euthanasia due to signs of systemic compromise conducted by a veterinarian blinded to treatment groups.

We next evaluated whether primary tumor control could prevent distant disease from forming at a later time point (metastasis prevention model; Figure 7H). HCC70 tumor xenografts (150 mm3) were treated orthotopically (fourth mammary fat pad) as per the sd-PEG method with ADU-S100 versus PBS, followed by MSLN (2 × 106) versus CD19 CAR T cell therapy. Seven days following CAR T cell injection into the PEG, the ipsilateral flank was injected with 3 × 106 HCC70 cells. Whereas the PBS+CD19 animals exhibited significant growth of both primary and flank tumors, the ADU+MSLN2-treated animals exhibited minimal flank tumor growth in addition to eradication of the primary tumor (Figure 7I). The probability of survival was also much greater among the ADU+MSLN2 group than the PBS+CD19 group (Figure 7J). Taken together, these data show that local delivery of STING agonism followed by CAR T cell therapy to primary TNBC could potentially treat and prevent distant disease.

Discussion

Here we identified a TNBC cell state and immunotherapy delivery platform with translational potential to prime the TME for local delivery of CAR T cells. Building upon prior work, we find that PTEN-null TNBC can recruit T cells by sustaining STING signaling due to defective Rab7-mediated trafficking, which can be broadened to include PTEN WT TNBC by Rab7 KO. To co-opt this biology and overcome the challenge of STING agonist-induced T cell toxicity, we demonstrate that PEG marker local delivery can release intratumoral STING agonism over the course of 1 week, and then CAR T cells can be injected into the hydrogel and distributed into an optimized TME. Importantly, other biomaterials studies typically utilize a resected tumor bed or low tumor burden to show efficacy, while this platform eradicated established primary TNBC while also targeting distant disease.

The ability to deliver STING agonism and CAR T cells using an established radiographic marker provides a practical and readily translatable method to develop effective anti-tumor immunity against TNBCs. Our platform addresses three critical hurdles to successfully deliver CAR T cells into the TME of solid tumors. First, when CAR T cells are delivered via intravenous infusion, CAR T cell homing into solid tumors remains challenging. This is readily overcome by sd-PEG local CAR T delivery. Second, even if CAR T cells traffic into the TME, activity is impaired by the hostile immunosuppressive environment. Here, we show that pretreatment with ADU-S100 induces a more favorable environment for CAR T cells and enhances circulation to target distant disease. Third, solid tumors are heterogeneous, and therefore treatment with one CAR is unlikely to eradicate disease, as evidenced by MSLN antigen downregulation in some escaping tumors. Since the PEG hydrogel remains in human breast tissue for at least 3 months and upward of 11 months, additional immune agonists and/or CAR T cells targeting different antigens can be introduced through injection into the PEG marker embedded adjacent to the primary tumor. Given the ease of implantation, multiple CAR T PEG markers can also be placed simultaneously or sequentially.

While other groups have proposed delivering CAR T cells through biomaterials, the PEG hydrogel marker identified here has multiple advantages. This marker is part of a biopsy method that is FDA approved and used internationally to mark breast biopsy sites, allowing for immediate translation into clinical practice, with established longevity in human tissue. Additionally, in a histologic study using 88 breast tissue and 36 lymph node samples to analyze the clip site of the same PEG hydrogel marker, layers of epithelioid histiocytes without lymphocytes or fibrosis have been observed. Unlike other breast biopsy clips commonly associated with a foreign body reaction, this PEG marker lacks a non-specific lymphocytic infiltrate that can crowd out CAR T cells. Moreover, histocytes are a prime target of IFN and chemokine signaling induced by STING agonists.

STING is also highly expressed in the endothelial cells of both normal and tumor tissues. STING vascular activation has been implicated in autoimmune disorders such as STING-associated vasculopathy with onset in infancy. Furthermore, in lymphoid organs, STING expression is observed in venules involved in lymphocyte migration. STING activation in both human and murine models results in increased production of type I IFNs, notably IFN-β, by endothelial cells to initiate antitumor responses even before the infiltration of dendritic cells and CD8+ T cells into the TME. Emerging data also demonstrate that STING activation can indeed normalize tumor vasculature with upregulation of endothelial-leukocyte adhesion molecules and enhanced pericyte coverage, facilitating trafficking of effector T cells across the endothelial barrier to enhance antitumor immunity. This biology likely accounts for the capacity of ADU-S100 priming to enhance MSLN CAR T cell efficacy and induce systemic trafficking into the circulation.

In sum, the ability to co-deliver STING agonist and CAR T cells directly into TNBCs using an established radiographic marker provides a versatile immunotherapy platform that is readily translatable into the clinic. Furthermore, although we have utilized TNBC and MSLN CAR T cells as an initial model system, this therapeutic platform may be widely applicable to other types of solid cancers and engineered cell therapies.

Limitations of the study

We also acknowledge that our study has several limitations. First, although we demonstrate the importance of CXCL10 and CCL5 in the T cell migration phenotype ex vivo, the relative role of these and other chemokines in recruiting CAR T cells in vivo remains to be determined. Understanding these factors with greater precision could enable chemokine receptor engineering of CAR T cells to enhance activity even further. Developing additional preclinical models that better recapitulate human tumor metastasis will facilitate more precise testing of future approaches as well. Testing mouse CAR T cells in immunocompetent mouse models would also better clarify the broader impact of the immune TME on this platform. In addition, while we establish the ability of sequentially dosed STING agonism to sensitize primary and distant tumor sites to low doses of CAR T cells in mouse models, engineering CAR T cells to resist STING agonist toxicity as well as T cell exhaustion could further strengthen this combination and even enable concurrent delivery.

Animal studies

All mouse experiments were conducted according to a DFCI-approved protocol. Mice were maintained under pathogen-free conditions. Six-to eight-week-old female C57BL/6J, BALB/cJ, and NSG (NOD.Cg-Prkdcscid Il2rgtm1Wjl/SzJ) mice were obtained from the Jackson Laboratory. For the low tumor burden 4T1 model, 10,000 4T1 cells in 100 μL PBS with 30% Matrigel (Corning, Cat.# 356231) were injected into the 4th mammary fat pad of BALB/cJ mice on Day 0. The PEG biopsy markers (Mammotome) were soaked with 20 μL PBS or 100 μg ADU-S100 in 20 μL PBS and implanted subcutaneously near the mammary fat pad on Day 4 (or Day 30 for a second PEG marker) (n = 5–10 mice per group). Tumor volume and size of the PEG marker were determined from digital caliper measurements of length (L) and width (W) according to the formula (L × W2)/2. For the established 4T1 tumor model, 100,000 4T1 cells were injected as previously described. Animals were randomized into various treatment groups when tumor volumes reached 75 mm3 (n = 7 mice per group). The PEG markers were implanted adjacent to the tumor on Day 6. For single-cell RNA and TCR sequencing, tumors were harvested on Day 14 after PEG implantation. For intratumoral injection, 100 μg free ADU-S100 or PBS was injected intratumorally into the established 4T1 tumor on Day 6 (n = 4–5 mice per group).

For NSG animal experiments, five million HCC70 cells or 2 million HCC1806 cells in PBS with 30% Matrigel were injected into the 4th mammary fat pad or ipsilateral flank of NSG mice. When primary tumor volumes reached about 150 mm3 (or as indicated in the figure legend), animals were randomized into various treatment groups with equal average tumor sizes. The PEG markers were soaked with 20 μL PBS, 100 μg ADU-S100 in 20 μL PBS, or 4×106 NanoLuc-labeled CD19/MSLN CAR T cells in 20 μL T cell media and implanted adjacent to the tumor. For combination treatment or sequential delivery PEG method, 7 days after PEG marker implantation (= Day 0), 4x106 or 2x106, 0.5x106 CD19 CAR T cells, 4x106, 2x106, 1x106, 0.5x106 or 0.25x106 MSLN CAR T cells (in 50 μL T cell media) were injected into the PEG marker previously soaked with PBS (PBS+CD19; PBS+MSLN4; PBS+MSLN2; PBS+MSLN1; PBS+MSLN0.5; PBS+MSLN0.25) or 100 μg ADU-S100 (ADU+CD19; ADU+MSLN4; ADU+MSLN2; ADU+MSLN1; ADU+MSLN0.5; ADU+MSLN0.25) (n = 3–6 mice per group). Bioluminescence imaging (BLI) using Nano-Glo Fluorofurimazine In Vivo Substrate (FFz) (Promega, Cat.#N4100) to track NanoLuc-labeled CAR T cells was performed using Xenogen System once weekly after CAR T cell administration for the first 4 weeks or continued every 2 weeks for a total of 12 weeks. BLI images were analyzed with the Living Image software (Caliper Life Sciences).

For the direct intratumoral injections, PBS or 100 μg ADU-S100 and 4x106 CD19/MSLN CAR T cells were injected intratumorally at a 3-day interval. For the dual PEG delivery study, the markers were soaked with PBS/100 μg ADU-S100 or 4x106 CD19/MSLN CAR T cells and were then implanted adjacent to the tumor simultaneously (n = 3 mice per group).

For the metastasis prevention model, five million HCC70 cells were injected into the 4th mammary fat pad of NSG mice, and established primary HCC70 tumors were implanted with PEG+PBS or PEG+100 μg ADU-S100 (Day −7) as described above. On Day 0, CAR T cells of the indicated dose were injected. 7 days later, 3 million HCC70 cells were injected into the ipsilateral flank of NSG mice.

Caliper measurements were performed twice weekly until the mice reached the criteria for euthanasia. Tumors were harvested for IHC analysis on Day 2, Day 14, Day 42, or at the end of the study. Staining for human CD3+ T cells in the peripheral blood and cytokine analysis from mouse serum were performed at indicated time points. Mice were subjected to routine veterinary assessment for signs of overt illness and were euthanized at experimental termination or when tumor size reached the endpoint of tumor volume exceeding 2000 mm3 or by recommendation of the veterinarian.

Method details

Immunohistochemistry

STING and PTEN staining of the TNBC TMA was performed with a BOND III automated staining platform (Leica Biosystems) at the BWH Pathology Core, as were pSTAT1, pTBK1 staining of 4T1 murine tumors and human CD3, mesothelin staining of TNBC xenograft tumors. FFPE tissue sections were processed per standard protocol as previously described. The antibodies for STING (Cell Signaling, Cat.# 13647S), PTEN (Cell Signaling, Cat.# 9559S), pSTAT1 (Cell Signaling, Cat.# 9167S) and pTBK1 (Cell Signaling, Cat.# 5483S) were run at 1:50 dilution. The antibody for CD3 (Dako/Agilent, Cat.# A0452) was run at 1:250 dilution and the antibody for mesothelin (Leica Biosystems, Cat.# PA0373) was ready to use. Pathologists D.A. Dillion and N.R. Mahadevan assigned the intensity of staining (0–3+) of the tumor cells to each sample. PTEN immunohistochemical staining was considered positive if any tumor cells displayed cytoplasmic staining of any intensity. Pathologist N.R. Mahadevan assigned an H-score (range: 0 to 300) of STING expression to each sample by multiplying the percentage of tumor cells positive for STING immunohistochemical staining (0%–100%) by the average intensity of staining (0–3+) of the tumor cells. Low expression was determined if the H-score was less than or equal to 30. Area fractions of total pSTAT1 and pTBK1 in 4T1 tumors were quantified by ImageJ.

Generation of human CAR T cells

For the generation of NanoLuc-labeled CAR T cells, all constructs are in an SFG retroviral plasmid. The variable heavy chain/variable light chain (VH/VL) sequences (linked via [G4S]3 flexible linker) from a non-functional ScFv for CD19 (FMC63 clone) and the mesothelin-specific ScFv derived from YP218 antibody as previously described were cloned into 4-1BBz second-generation CARs. DNA was custom synthesized by Integrated DNA Technologies. All cloning was performed by restriction enzyme digest or Gibson Assembly (New England Biolabs). The construct also contains the LssmOrange-NanoLuc fusion fragment separated from the CAR fragment by self-cleaving T2A. H29D human retroviral packaging cells were transfected with the gamma retro CAR vector of interest, which was packaged transiently in vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV)-G pseudotyped particles. These particles were used to deliver the vector to RD114 cells, containing the RD114 envelope from the feline endogenous virus. Single cell clones were isolated to achieve stable packaging of RD114-pseudotyped particles by flow cytometry. Healthy donor PBMCs were activated with TransAct (1:100; Miltenyi Biotec, Cat.# 130-111-160) in complete medium (RPMI-1640 supplemented with 10% FBS) in the presence of IL-2 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat.# PHC0021) at 100 IU/mL. Two days after activation, the T cells were transduced by spinfection + retrovirus supernatants on the RetroNectin (Takara Bio, Cat.#T100B)-coated plate. A multiplicity of infection of 2 was used for T cell transduction. After transduction, T cells were expanded with cytokines, IL-2 (100 IU/mL), IL-7 (25 ng/mL; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat.# PHC0075), and IL-15 (25 ng/mL; Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat.# PHC9154) in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 10% FBS. Transduction efficiency was determined by FACS analysis of LssmOrange 72-96 h after transduction.

For the generation of CAR T cells with lentiviral vectors, an anti-MSLN CAR was custom synthesized in a lentivirus backbone with the same ScFv. The BCMA CAR sequence has been previously described and was cloned into the same lentiviral vector as a control. The constructs also contain vexGFP fragment separated from the CAR fragment by self-cleaving T2A. The CAR gene constructs were packaged with psPAX2 and pMD2.G in HEK293T cells using X-tremeGENE HP DNA transfection reagent (Roche, Cat.# 06366236001). Activated T cells were lentivirally transduced by spinoculation with the BCMA and MSLN CAR virus on RetroNectin-coated plate in the presence of Lentiboost (1:100; Sirion Biotech). After transduction, T cells were expanded with cytokines as described above. The percentage of T cells expressing CAR was determined via flow cytometric analysis of vexGFP 72–96 h after transduction.

Three-dimensional T cell migration and co-culture assay

Human cell line-derived tumor spheroids were generated from MDAMB231, MDAMB231 Rab7 knockout, HCC1806, HCC1806 Rab7 knockout, HCC1143 and HCC70 cell lines by seeding 5x105 cells per well into a 6-well ultra-low attachment (ULA) plate (Corning, Cat.# 3471) and incubating at 37°C, 5% CO2 for 24 h. Collagen hydrogel was formulated to a concentration of 2.5 mg/mL using type I rat tail collagen (Corning, Cat.# 354236) as previously described. Tumor spheroids were then resuspended in the collagen hydrogel and embedded into the central gel region of an AIM chip microfluidic device (AIM Biotech, Cat.# DAX-1) as previously described. Devices were incubated for 40 min at 37°C in humidity chambers to allow ample collagen polymerization.

For the parental and CXCR3-overexpressing Jurkat cell migration assay, spheroids were either left untreated or treated with 100 nM CID1067700, 50 μM ADU-S100, or a combination. The following day, Jurkat T-cells were stained with CellTrace Violet (Invitrogen, Cat.#C34557) following the manufacturer’s instructions and added to one exterior chamber of the device for a culture of 48 h.

For human primary CD8+ T cell and CAR T cell migration assay, tumor spheroids were pre-treated with 50 μM ADU-S100 in the ULA plate for 24 h, washed with PBS to remove residual ADU, and then loaded into the 3D microfluidic device. After collagen polymerization, T cells were stained with CellTrace Yellow (Invitrogen, Cat.#C34567) and added to one exterior chamber of the device at a 2:1 ratio of tumor spheroids (1:1 ratio for CAR T migration) in complete media with IL-2 (100 IU/mL) for a culture of 48 h. Antibodies blocking CXCL10 (R&D Systems, Cat.# MAB266-SP) and CCL5 (R&D Systems, Cat.# MAB678-SP) or isotype control (R&D Systems, Cat.# MAB002) were also added to the exterior chamber at 1 μg/mL for neutralization.

For the co-culture assay, tumor spheroids and CellTrace Yellow stained CAR T cells were resuspended in collagen hydrogel respectively, mixed at a 1:1 ratio, and embedded into the central gel region of each device for a co-culture of 72 h. Conditioned media were harvested from the exterior channels of the device to analyze cytokines secretion. The central channel was then counterstained with DAPI or Hoechst 33342 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat.#H3570) or Hoechst 33342, Calcein AM (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat.#C3099) and DRAQ7 (Biolegend, Cat.# 424001). Whole devices were imaged using a Nikon Eclipse 80i fluorescence microscope equipped with a z-stack-capable stage (Prior) and an Andor Zyla camera (Andor). Image capture and analysis were performed using the NIS-Elements AR software package as previously described. Live/dead cell quantification was performed by measuring the total fraction area of each dye. The spheroid’s viability was determined by calculating the ratio of dead cells as equal to 100% x DRAQ7/Hoechst.

In vitro ADU-S100 and 2′3′ cGAMP drug release assay from biopsy markers

5x10 THP-1 cells per well were plated in a 24-well plate and were treated with PMA for 24 h. Differentiated THP-1 cells were treated with increasing amounts of ADU-S100 or 2′3′ cGAMP (Invivogen, Cat.# tlrl-nacga23-1) in 1 mL media or serum-free media. Conditioned media were harvested after 24 h for CXCL10 ELISA to generate standard curves. All conditions were tested in triplicate. Biopsy markers (PEG Hydrogel, Collagen, and Beta-glucan; Mammotome) soaked with 100 μg ADU-S100 or 100 μg 2′3' cGAMP in 20 μL PBS were plated in a 24-well plate (n = 3 per condition). Collagen hydrogel was formulated as described above and 500 μL collagen solution was added to form the collagen barrier. After collagen polymerization, 1 mL RPMI complete media or serum-free RPMI media were added to each well and were harvested and replaced with 1 mL fresh media every 24 h for a total of 5 days. Conditioned media harvested from each biopsy marker were added to another 24-well plate of differentiated THP-1 cells. CXCL10 ELISA was performed after 24 h. Values were averaged from 2 to 3 biopsy markers per time point. Conditioned media harvested from the biopsy marker after 24h were used as a control. The amounts of drugs released from biopsy markers over time were identified by referring to the x axis value on the standard curve based on averaged CXCL10 production on the y axis.

After 24 h of treatment with 50 μM ADU-S100 in the ULA plate, tumor spheroids were digested with trypsin in a 37°C incubator for 5 min to obtain single-cell suspensions. Flow cytometry was performed to analyze immune cell components. Cells were incubated with Live/Dead Fixable Near-IR Dead Cell Stain Kit (Invitrogen, Cat#. L34975) for 4 min in the dark before staining with pre-conjugated antibodies. Antibodies were specific for the following human markers: CD45 (Biolegend, Cat.# 304050), CD3 (Biolegend, Cat.# 300424), CD4 (Biolegend, Cat.# 300554) and CD8 (BD Biosciences, Cat.# 560662).

In vitro cytotoxicity assay using CellTiter-Glo

For cell toxicity assay of primary CD8+ T cells, 40,000 cells per well were seeded in a 96-well plate (Corning, Cat.# 3904) and treated with ADU-S100 at the indicated concentrations or conditioned media harvested from PEG biopsy markers for 24 h. PBS or conditioned media harvested from biopsy markers only were used as control. Cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability assay (Promega, Cat.#G7571) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. All conditions were tested in triplicate and values were normalized to control.

For the primary CD8+ T cell toxicity assay comparing direct addition of ADU-S100 versus ADU-S100 delivered by PEG marker, 500,000 cells per well were seeded in a 24-well plate in 1 mL RPMI-1640 complete medium. Cells were treated with direct addition of PBS, 100 μg ADU-S100 or PEG+PBS, PEG+100 μg ADU-S100 for 5 days. Cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter-Glo assay. All conditions were tested in triplicate and values were normalized to the PBS group. ADU-S100 direct addition and PEG+100 μg ADU-S100 treated cells were then washed and supplemented with RPMI-1640 complete medium with IL-2, IL-7, and IL-15 for another 5 days, followed by CellTiter-Glo assay. Cell viability was also assessed by continuous cell counting until Day 18 in a separate experiment.

In vitro analysis of CAR T cell killing was performed by co-culture of CAR T cells and target tumor cells. 10,000 tumor cells per well were seeded in a 96-well plate and CAR T cells were added based on different E:T ratios. Tumor cells only and medium only were used for controls. 24 h later, cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter-Glo assay. The percentage of specific target cell death was calculated using the following formula: % target cell death = 100–100 x [(viability of co-culture – viability of CAR T cell only)/(viability of tumor cell only – medium only)]. All conditions were tested in triplicate and plates were read on a Tecan Infinite Mplex Microplate Reader.

For in vitro analysis of CAR T cell viability co-cultured with the PEG markers, PEG markers were soaked with PBS or 100 μg ADU-S100 in 20 μL PBS and implanted adjacent to established HCC70 xenografts as described above. 7 days later, the PEG markers were extracted and cocultured with 500,000 MSLN CAR T cells in a 24-well plate for 24 h. Cell viability was assessed using the CellTiter-Glo assay. All conditions were tested in duplicate or triplicate and values were normalized to CAR T cells only without PEG markers.

Single-cell RNA and T cell receptor (TCR) sequencing

Fresh 4T1 tumors treated with PEG+PBS or PEG+ADU-S100 were pooled (n = 4 per group) and mechanically and enzymatically disaggregated in dissociation buffer consisting of DMEM full media +100 U/mL collagenase type IV and 50 μg/mL DNase I. Suspension cells were incubated at 37°C for 45 min and then further mechanically dissociated. Red blood cells were removed before Live/Dead staining (Biolegend, Cat.# 423111) and Fc blocking. Cells were then stained with antibodies specific for mouse CD45 (Biolegend, Cat.# 103106) and mouse CD3 (Biolegend, Cat.# 100219). Samples were washed and sorted using a BD FACS Melody instrument to isolate viable CD45+ and CD3+ cells. For analysis of PBMCs, whole blood was spun at 1000 g for 10 min to collect the buffy coat followed by red blood cell lysis. Cells were then washed and assessed for viability. 16,500 cells per condition were loaded onto a 10X Chromium instrument (10X Genomics) per the manufacturer’s instructions. Single-cell RNA and TCR libraries were generated using the Chromium Single Cell 5′ Library Construction (10X Genomics, Cat.# PN-1000190) and Chromium Single Cell Mouse TCR Amplification kits (10X Genomics, Cat.# PN-1000254) per user guide. Quality control of the completed libraries was performed using the Bioanalyzer High Sensitivity DNA Kit (Agilent, Cat.# 5067–4626) and then sequenced using the Illumina NovaSeq platform.

Single-cell RNA-seq data were aligned to the mouse genome (mm10) and subjected to initial filtering using Cell Ranger v 6.0.1 with default parameters. The feature matrix generated by Cell Ranger was used to perform downstream analysis using R toolkit Seurat (v.4.3.0.1). Cells containing fewer than 500 (1000 for PBMCs) or more than 40,000 unique molecular identifiers, or more than 25% (15% for PBMCs) of reads mapping to mitochondrial genes were removed from consideration. Cell doublets were removed by Scrublet (v.0.2.3). The filtered cell-gene matrix was log-normalized and scaled with default Seurat parameters. 2,000 (3000 for PBMCs) most variable features (genes) were found using FindVariableFeatures with ‘vst’ option for selection.method. Then, dimensionality reduction using principal component analysis (PCA) was performed for these genes. Clustering was performed on the chosen principal components using the shared nearest neighbor algorithm as implemented in Seurat, with top 10 (30 for T cell sub-clustering) dimensions and resolution of 0.2. The clusters were visualized using the Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) algorithm. Marker genes for each cluster were identified with Seurat’s function FindAllMarkers (with default parameters and expression fold-change threshold of log2(1.2)(log2(0.5) for T cell sub-clustering). The dominant cell types were assigned by known cell type marker genes listed in Table S3 and Table S4. TCR chain assembly was performed using the Cell Ranger pipeline, with default parameters. Cells with low confidence or nonproductive TCR clonotypes were filtered out, specifically only the clonotypes exhibiting both a single alpha chain and a single beta chain were retained for cell assignment.

Differentially expressed genes between ADU-S100 and PBS in the clusters of APC/Macrophage and T cell were identified using Seurat’s FindMarkers function with the parameter ‘thresh.use’ set to zero and the default values of other parameters. Gene expression changes were considered significant if they had a fold change of 1.2 or greater and an adjusted p-value of 0.05 or less. Up-regulated and down-regulated genes were marked as red and blue dots respectively in the volcano plot. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) was performed using the clusterProfiler package (v4.12.0). The normalized enrichment scores for selected HALLMARK pathways were plotted, with the colors indicating the adjusted p-values. The leading edge plots for the selected pathways were generated using the gseaplot2 function from the enrichplot package (v1.24.0) (https://www.bioconductor.org).

In vitro bioluminescent imaging

PEG biopsy markers were soaked with PBS or 2x106/4x106 NanoLuc-MSLN CAR T cells (in 20 μL) or hydrated with 150 μL PBS for 30 min followed by PBS or 2x106/4x106 MSLN CAR T cells injection (in 50 μL) and then transferred to the 60mm dish or 48-well plate. Increasing numbers of MSLN CAR T cells were embedded in 200 μL type I rat tail collagen and plated in the 48-well plate. Nano-Glo Fluorofurimazine In Vivo Substrate (FFz) was directly added to the PEG markers or wells with collagen and BLI imaging was performed using Xenogen System after 4–5 min. BLI images were analyzed with the Living Image software (Caliper Life Sciences).

In vitro luciferase assay

5×105 per well ffLUC-HCC70 cells were plated in the 24-well ULA plate. BCMA/MSLN CAR T cells were added to (or injected into) biopsy markers at indicated E:T ratios. After impregnation, the biopsy marker was added to the well of ffLUC-HCC70 spheroids in the ULA plate. ffLUC-HCC70 spheroids with biopsy marker only were used for 0:1 E:T ratio. 24 h later, 100 μL ffLUC-HCC70 cells per well were pipetted into a 96-well assay plate for analysis with ONE-Glo luciferase assay system (Promega, Cat.#E6110) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In the co-culture of CAR T cells with ffLUC-HCC70 cells without biopsy markers, 10,000 tumor cells per well were seeded in a 96-well plate and CAR T cells were added based on different E:T ratios. 24 h later, cell viability was assessed with the ONE-Glo luciferase assay system. ffLUC-HCC70 cells only were used for control. All conditions were tested in duplicate or triplicate and plates were read on a FLUOstar Omega Microplate Reader (BMG LABTECH). The percentage of specific lysis was calculated using the following formula: % ffLUC-HCC70 death = 100–100 x (experimental data/viability of tumor cell only).

Quantification and statistical analysis

GraphPad Prism 10.3.1 was used for statistical analysis, data processing, and graph generation. The values reported are the mean and SEM. All in vitro experiments were performed with at least 2 biological replicates as indicated in the figure legends. Statistical significance was assessed using unpaired two-tailed Welch’s or Student’s t test, one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s post-hoc test, or two-way ANOVA followed by Tukey’s or Sidak’s post-hoc test as described in the figure legends. Colocalization was determined by ImageJ plugin CoLoc2 and reported as a Pearson’s correlation. Log rank Mantel-Cox test was used for mouse survival analysis. Statistical significance was determined as p < 0.05. Asterisks used to indicate significance correspond with: ∗, p < 0.05; ∗∗, p < 0.01; ∗∗∗, p < 0.001; ∗∗∗∗, p < 0.0001. Illustrations were created with BioRender.com.
