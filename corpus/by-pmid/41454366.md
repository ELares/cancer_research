# A new strategy for melanoma treatment: an investigation of the clinical value of the combination of immune checkpoint inhibitors and oncolytic viruses.

## Abstract

Melanoma is a highly malignant tumor. In the past, treatment options like chemotherapy were of limited benefit, and its prognosis, particularly in the advanced stage, was poor. The introduction of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) has revolutionized the therapeutic landscape for melanoma and significantly improved patient survival. However, the efficacy of ICIs monotherapy remains limited, with only a small proportion of cancer patients benefiting. It has been proposed that oncolytic viruses (OVs) can significantly increase the density of CD8 + T-cells in tumors by modulating the tumor microenvironment from "cold" to "hot", and this change can synergistically activate anti-tumor immunity in conjunction with ICIs, thus improving the response rate of ICIs therapy. This change can synergize with ICIs to activate the body's anti-tumor immunity, thus improving the response rate of ICIs. However, there are still some unanswered questions, such as whether the combination will increase the incidence of adverse reactions. This review systematically combed the available clinical trial data to assess the feasibility and clinical application value of combining ICIs with OVs in the treatment of melanoma, as well as summarized the occurrence of adverse reactions in the clinical studies, analyzed their characteristics, and made management recommendations. Our review constructs a new theoretical framework for melanoma treatment, focusing on directions such as efficacy and adverse effects to provide practical guidance for clinical practice.

Introduction

Melanoma is considered a highly metastatic, immunogenic malignant tumor. It commonly occurs on the skin but can also develop in other body areas such as mucous membranes, extremities, and internal organs. When it arises in mucous membranes, it is termed mucosal melanoma; when it occurs on the extremities, it is known as acral melanoma. According to the 2022 global cancer statistics report, there were approximately 331,647 new cases and 58,645 deaths worldwide, indicating that the prevention and treatment of melanoma still face severe challenges. Although surgical resection is the mainstay of treatment for early-stage melanoma, its effectiveness is often limited as the disease progresses to stage III-IV or involves vital organs, with a higher risk of postoperative recurrence and relatively poor survival outcomes and prognosis for patient s. Prior studies have reported that the five-year survival rate for melanoma is generally higher in Europe and the United States, with the overall five-year survival rate in the United States exceeding 90%. In contrast, the five-year survival rate for melanoma patients in China is only around 49.6%. This discrepancy may be related to factors such as subtype distribution and diagnostic stage. Unlike cutaneous melanoma, which is predominantly ultraviolet (UV)-driven in European and American countries, a much higher proportion of melanomas in Chinese patients are acral melanoma and mucosal melanoma—subtypes with more aggressive biological behaviors—compared to Western populations. Additionally, the majority of Chinese melanoma patients are diagnosed at stage Ⅲ-Ⅳ.

Notably, approximately 50% of melanoma patients carry the BRAF V600E/K mutation. The BRAF protein is a key molecule in the MAPK signaling pathway. Its mutation leads to sustained activation of this pathway, promoting tumor cell proliferation and survival. Targeting this mechanism, multiple BRAF inhibitors (such as vemurafenib and dabrafenib) and MEK inhibitors (such as trametinib, cobimetinib, and binimetinib) have been developed. BRAF mutations also exhibit multiple subtypes, with V600E and V600K being the most common. Other rare mutations (such as V600R, V600D, L597P/Q/R/S, K601E, etc.) show significant variability in treatment efficacy. Studies indicate that patients with BRAF V600-mutated melanoma derive significant benefit from combination therapy with BRAF inhibitors and MEK inhibitors. However, most patients experience tumor progression or resistance within 12 to 15 months. Immunotherapy, in contrast, achieves response rates of 35% to 40% and is less prone to developing resistance. Therefore, among many tumor treatments, the treatment plan for melanoma will tend to consider immunotherapy.

In recent years, a variety of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have been approved for the treatment of tumors such as melanoma, lung cancer, and gastric cancer. Multiple authoritative guidelines including NCCN, CSCO, and ESMO also recommend their use as first-line, second-line, and even subsequent-line therapies for various cancer types. Currently, the primary immunotherapies for melanoma are immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), including anti-CTLA-4 monoclonal antibody (ipilimumab), anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody (nivolumab, pembrolizumab), and anti-PD-L1 monoclonal antibody (durvalumab). By February 2025, 33 kinds of ICIs have been approved globally, and 24 have been approved in China, including 16 PD-1 inhibitors, 11 PD-L1 inhibitors, 2 CTLA-4 inhibitors, 2 PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific inhibitors, 1 LAG3/PD-1 inhibitor and 1 PD-1/VEGF bispecific inhibitor. ICIs have shown promising results in prolonging survival and reducing mortality in melanoma patients, especially in advanced stage patients. The efficacy of ICIs therapy is closely linked to the immune status of the tumor microenvironment (TME). Immune status is a multidimensional concept, primarily encompassing the density, spatial distribution, and functional state of various immune cells. These dimensions not only determine the “hot” or “cold” phenotype of the TME but also significantly predict a tumor’s potential response to ICIs treatment. The stronger immunosuppressive effect in TME can lead to nonresponse or resistance to single ICIs in many melanoma patients. Therefore, there is an urgent need to find combination therapies that can improve the TME to enhance the response rate to ICIs.

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are a class of naturally occurring or genetically modified tumor-killing viruses with high replication capacity, which can act by recognizing specific receptors or by means of diverse anti-tumor mechanisms. First, after infecting tumor cells, oncolytic viruses (OVs) leverage the metabolic mechanisms of tumor cells to replicate and proliferate, ultimately leading to the lysis and death of tumor cells. This constitutes the direct oncolytic effect of OVs. Second, tumor cell lysis releases tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) and danger signals (such as high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) and adenosine triphosphate (ATP)), activating dendritic cells (DCs). DCs present TAAs to T cells, inducing specific anti-tumor immune responses that generate cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) capable of killing virus-uninfected distant tumor cells. Additionally, certain OVs can be engineered to produce “armed” OVs, thereby enhancing immune stimulation. Finally, OVs infection can downregulate angiogenesis factors such as VEGF, disrupting tumor blood supply and leading to ischemic necrosis of tumor cells. Importantly, OVs can reverse tumor states within the TME to achieve comprehensive regulation of the immune system. By promoting the formation of a “hot” tumor microenvironment, they overcome the limitation of low response rates seen with monotherapy using ICIs. OVs can be classified into two main categories, DNA viruses and RNA viruses, depending on their genetic material. Currently, the most commonly used types of OVs in cancer therapeutic research include herpes simplex virus (HSV), adenovirus, poxvirus, and coxsackievirus, etc.. Since 2000 to date, five OVs drugs have been approved for marketing at home and abroad. Since 2003, when the State Food and Drug Administration (SFDA) approved Gendicine, the world’s first gene therapy drug, the number of studies on OVs has been increasing. In 2015, Imlygic (T-VEC) was approved for clinical use in the treatment of melanoma, becoming the first widely recognized OVs product. This approval marked an important breakthrough in the clinical application of OVs therapies. It has been found that OVs can induce immunogenic cell death, strongly recruit and activate tumor-specific T cells. Within the inflammatory microenvironment they induce, these viruses can functionally overcome existing PD-L1-mediated immune suppression. In addition, documents such as the Chinese Expert Consensus on the Clinical Application of Genetic Recombinant Oncolytic adenovirus in the Treatment of Malignant Tumors (2022) [in Chinese] and SITC clinical practice guideline on immunotherapy for the treatment of melanoma (2023) have been published, which provide important guidance and references for the treatment of malignant tumors by OVs.

A number of clinical trials combining ICIs with OVs are currently underway, some of which have yielded preliminary positive results, demonstrating the potential of combination therapy to improve antitumor efficacy. With the deepening of clinical practice and accumulation of data, this combination therapy is expected to broaden the scope of immunotherapy. In this paper, we will systematically analyze the feasibility of combining ICIs with OVs in the treatment of melanoma from the basis of mechanism and efficacy data, and put forward practical suggestions for the management of adverse reactions, so as to provide a basis for the clinical treatment of melanoma.

Mechanistic basis of immune checkpoint inhibitors in combination with oncolytic virus

The tumor microenvironment (TME) is a highly heterogeneous multicellular environment in which tumor cells communicate with non-tumor cells within the host, and its immune properties are defined by dynamic processes such as infiltration of inflammatory cells and secretion of pro-inflammatory factors, which have led to the differentiation of tumors into "hot" and "cold" types. “Hot” tumors are characterized by high levels of T-cell infiltration, enhanced interferon-gamma signaling pathway, and upregulation of immune checkpoint proteins such as PD-L1, which make them responsive to ICIs. “Cold” tumors, on the other hand, are weakly immunogenic and escape immune surveillance due to a paucity of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), impaired antigen presentation, and low PD-L1 expression. Although melanoma is mostly classified as a “hot” tumor, up to 40–50% of patients present a “cold” tumor phenotype, which constitutes a major bottleneck in the clinical application of ICIs. The mechanism of action of ICIs relies on the activation of T cells (especially CD8 + T cells) (Fig. 1)and the restoration of their anti-tumor function, which is difficult to be effective in “cold” TME that lacks T cell infiltration and insufficient neoantigen presentation.

It is worth noting that different subtypes of melanoma exhibit significant differences in molecular characteristics, tumor microenvironment, and response to immunotherapy. In terms of mutation profiles, cutaneous melanoma commonly carries driver mutations such as BRAF and NRAS, while acral melanoma is primarily characterized by KIT mutations. Mucosal melanoma frequently exhibits mutations in the NRAS, C-KIT, and NF1 genes. In terms of immune infiltration characteristics, the tumor microenvironment of cutaneous melanoma exhibits a relatively high level of CD8 + T cell infiltration and a significantly higher expression rate of immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1. In contrast, acral and mucosal melanomas predominantly present with an immune desert or immune exclusion phenotype, characterized by lower levels of immune cell infiltration. These differences result in distinct therapeutic responses to ICIs among the three melanoma subtypes. Patients with cutaneous melanoma demonstrate significantly higher response rates to ICIs therapy compared to those with acral or mucosal melanoma, which often exhibit primary resistance.

OVs release tumor antigens and damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) by selectively infecting and lysing tumor cells, and their own pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) simultaneously activate antigen-presenting cells such as dendritic cells, initiating anti-tumor immune responses(Fig. 2). It also significantly reverses immunosuppressive TME, including reduction of regulatory T cells (Treg) and suppressor myeloid cells, downregulation of immunosuppressive factors such as TGF-β and IL-10, improvement of aberrant angiogenesis and promotion of pro-inflammatory factor secretion. The strong inflammatory response induced by OVs often upregulates the expression of immune checkpoint molecules, such as PD-L1, on tumor cells and immune cells. This makes “cold” tumors with low PD-L1 expression eligible for targeting by ICIs (e.g., anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies), and provides a “target” for subsequent ICIs intervention.

Additionally, tumor vasculature disruption induced by OV-based therapies may temporarily impair the recruitment of immune cells to tumor sites. To mitigate this potential impact, OVs can be “armed” through genetic engineering techniques. Available evidence indicates that OVs serve as ideal gene therapy vectors, capable of inserting exogenous genes including not only cytokines and chemokines, but also immunomodulatory molecules and bispecific antibodies. These “armed” OVs, into which cytokine and chemokine genes have been inserted, secrete corresponding products upon infecting tumor cells. Through “chemical chemotactic signals,” they actively recruit immune cells, effectively enhancing the efficiency of local immune cell infiltration in tumors. Additionally, intra-tumoral injection of “armed” OVs enables the localized and precise expression of functional protein molecules, such as anti-PD-1/PD-L1 single-chain antibody fragments, and may also reduce adverse reactions triggered by systemic administration of immune checkpoint inhibitors. Optimizing “armed” OVs into intravenously infused systemic formulations could offer a novel therapeutic option for metastatic cancer patients. This strategy overcomes the limitations of conventional intratumoral injections in reaching deep or multiple metastatic sites, but still faces new challenges such as neutralizing antibodies, hepatic and splenic clearance, and off-target toxicity.

Therefore, the conversion of TME from the “cold” state of immunosuppression to the “hot” state of immune activation will help ICIs to perform better. OVs have the unique ability to reverse the “hot–cold” tumor microenvironment, transforming tumors into immune “hot spots” (Fig. 3). Another key advantage of this combination therapy is the induction of differentiation of activated T cells into memory T cells. These memory T cells not only enhance the synergistic local anti-tumor effect, but more critically, they mediate systemic immune surveillance and attack against tumors at distal non-injection sites, the “distal effect”. This is an outcome that neither OVs nor ICIs alone can readily achieve. Some in vitro studies indicate that mice treated with a combination of OVs and ICIs exhibit a robust IFN-γ memory response, a hallmark functional manifestation of activated memory T cells. Higher IFN-γ secretion correlates with increased numbers of memory T cells, enhanced functional stability, and stronger long-term antitumor protection. In this TME pre-modified by OVs, ICIs are able to act more efficiently on infiltrated T cells to release their inhibitory state, resulting in an anti-tumor immune response.

Efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors combined with oncolytic viruses

According to data from ClinicalTrials.gov as of May 2025, OVs have been conducting clinical trials across multiple solid tumor indications. A total of 274 clinical trials have been registered, with 196 trials having clearly defined phases: Phase I (105 trials, 53.6%), Phase II (76 trials, 38.8%), Phase III (10 trials, 5.1%), and Phase IV (5 trials, 2.6%). Focusing on melanoma, there were 22 special studies of OVs in combination with ICIs, of which 8 (36.4%) have completed trials and published results.

CTLA-4 inhibitors + herpes simplex virus

Ipilimumab + T-VEC

Talimogene Laherparepvec (T-VEC) is a genetically modified herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) that is unique in that the ICP34.5 and ICP47 genes have been deleted from the genome, preventing it from replicating in normal tissues in order to retain selectivity for tumor cells. Specifically, following deletion of the ICP34.5 gene, T-VEC struggles to replicate within normal cells due to its inability to antagonize the interferon antiviral pathway. However, this pathway is often defective in tumor cells, allowing the virus to replicate normally. After deletion of the ICP47 gene, T-VEC is readily cleared by the immune systems of normal cells. Tumor cells, with their low immunogenicity and weak antigen presentation, cannot effectively clear the virus, enabling sustained viral replication within them. The positive progress achieved in T-VEC monotherapy provides strong support for subsequent clinical studies in combination with ICIs.

Puzanov et al. initially evaluated the safety of T-VEC combined with ipilimumab in a phase Ib study, enrolling patients with unresectable stage IIIB-IVM1c melanoma. The study was observed in 18 evaluable patients and showed that the ORR and DRR of the combination therapy were 50% and 44%, respectively, with no dose-limiting toxicities compared to the ipilimumab monotherapy arm, and the overall adverse effects were safe and manageable. Based on these preliminary results, Chesney et al. subsequently conducted a pivotal phase II study to further validate the effectiveness of this combination therapy. The study found that patients treated with ipilimumab in combination with the T-VEC regimen had an ORR of 39%, compared to an ORR of 18% for patients treated with ipilimumab alone. In addition, further subgroup analyses revealed that patients with low tumor stage and BRAF wild type may benefit more from combination therapy. According to the results of a 5-year-long follow-up, the combination of T-VEC and ipilimumab appears to effectively enhance treatment outcomes and improve overall prognosis in patients with unresectable stage IIIB-IV melanoma.

Interestingly, a first-in-human Phase I/II translational trial demonstrated the feasibility of combining T-VEC with isolated limb perfusion (ILP) technology for treating patients with locally advanced sarcoma or melanoma. Results showed an overall response rate (ORR) of 53% for this combination regimen, comparable to previously reported efficacy of ILP monotherapy. Concurrently, the combination therapy was well tolerated, with no elevated incidence of adverse events (AEs) observed.

Ipilimumab + HF10

HF10 (also known as C-REV) is a naturally mutated HSV-1. In experimental studies in a mouse model of melanoma, HF10 alone significantly inhibited tumor growth and improved mouse survival. Andtbacka et al. reported treatment data from a Phase II clinical trial evaluating HF10 in combination with ipilimumab. The study enrolled patients with stage IIIb-IV unresectable melanoma who were either treatment-naïve or had not previously received ICIs therapy. At the 12-, 18-, 24-, 36-, and 48-week evaluations, the combination therapy group achieved an ORR of 41% and a DCR of 68% at week 24, which was significantly higher than the trial data in Japanese melanoma patients. Notably, both primary and treated (≥ 1 prior therapy) patients showed a positive trend in outcome and survival prognosis. The final results of the study suggest that HF10 in combination with ipilimumab may serve as a viable option for second-line and beyond treatment of melanoma, particularly in patient populations who have failed first-line therapy.

CTLA-4 inhibitors + coxsackievirus

Ipilimumab + CVA21

Coxsackievirus A21 (CVA21, also known as V937) has been shown to have potential as an effective therapy for melanoma. Shafren et al. found that intercellular adhesion molecule-1 (ICAM-1), acting as a CVA21 receptor, is upregulated in metastatic melanoma cells. This upregulation promotes immune activation against tumor cells within the TME by facilitating more efficient intercellular contacts and signaling. This finding is consistent with the studies of Au et al. and Robert et al.. Several studies have demonstrated that the combination of CVA21 and ipilimumab has shown significant synergistic effects in patients with advanced melanoma. For example, the MITCI study showed an ORR of 30%, mOS of 45.1 months, and mPFS of 6.2 months in 50 evaluable patients. Particularly, in 17 patients who had not received anti-PD-1 therapy, combination therapy resulted in an ORR of 47%, a figure superior to the efficacy of treatment with ipilimumab monotherapy (12%) in the same group. In addition, the adverse effects reported by the investigators were consistent with previous monotherapy regimens.

PD-1 inhibitors + herpes simplex virus

Pembrolizumab + T-VEC

Given the potentially divergent mechanisms of action of different checkpoint inhibitors, the researchers drew on the combination strategy of CTLA-4 inhibitors and went on to explore the differences in efficacy when combining different checkpoint inhibitors with OVs. In the phase Ib clinical study, the Ribas team achieved encouraging results using the “intratumoral injection of T-VEC combined with pembrolizumab” regimen in patients with melanoma lesions in the dermal, subcutaneous, or lymph nodal regions. The ORR reached 62%, with 33.3% of patients achieving complete remission. Subsequently, a Phase II clinical trial conducted by Robert et al. demonstrated that for patients with resectable melanoma who had progressed on prior anti-PD-1 therapy, the ORR following treatment with T-VEC combined with pembrolizumab reached 40.0%-46.7%. In contrast, the ORR for this combination regimen in patients with unresectable/metastatic disease was only 0%-6.7%. These findings are highly consistent with the latest results from the IGNYTE study. Given the similar patient characteristics across both studies, this further validates the differential efficacy of the combination regimen across different disease states. Furthermore, in the phase III trial conducted by the team, although the combination of T-VEC + pembrolizumab did not improve OS in patients, it showed a small increase in ORR, and both CRR and DRR were numerically higher in the combination group than in the monotherapy group.

Nivolumab + RP-1

RP-1 is a genetically modified variant of HSV-1, which gains a unique therapeutic advantage through the expression of a fusion protein (GALV-GP-R), a property that significantly distinguishes it from other OVs (e.g., T-VEC). In the phase 1/2 IGNYTE trial conducted by Robert et al., enrolled melanoma patients had progressed after prior anti-PD-1 therapy, with 48.6% being Stage IV. The trial employed a combination regimen of RP-1 and nivolumab, yielding an ORR of 33.6%, with all patients demonstrating a baseline duration of remission (DOR) of ≥ 6 months. In addition, the incidence of grade 3–4 adverse reactions was low and most of them were reversible events.

Toripalimab + OrienX 010

OrienX 010 is another OVs based on the modification of HSV-1. Liu’s team recently published the results of a phase I clinical trial of OrienX 010 in combination with toripalimab for the neoadjuvant treatment of melanoma of the extremities in a Nature subissue. In this study, 30 patients treated with this combination not only excelled in pathologic response, but also demonstrated a favorable short-term survival prognosis. The imaging and pathologic remission rates reached 36.6% and 77.8%, respectively. These data also provide treatment ideas for patients with different subtypes of melanoma.

PD-1 inhibitors + adenoviruses

Pembrolizumab + ONCOS-102

ONCOS-102 is a chimeric oncolytic adenovirus with a chimeric structure integrating human adenovirus types 5 (Ad5) and 3 (Ad3) in the fibronectin junction region. ONCOS-102 was systematically validated in preclinical studies and has advanced to a Phase I clinical trial in combination with ICIs. This Phase I clinical trial is divided into two parts. Part 1 consisted of patients receiving ONCOS-102 and pembrolizumab sequentially; Part 2 was the combination therapy phase. The results showed a favorable safety profile and potential clinical efficacy (ORR of 35%) in patients with anti-PD-1 refractory, unresectable advanced melanoma who were treated with ONCOS-102 combined with pembrolizumab.

PD-1 inhibitors + coxsackievirus

Pembrolizumab + CVA21

The Silk research group conducted a preliminary assessment of the efficacy of pembrolizumab in combination with CVA21 in the treatment of 14 patients with advanced melanoma by means of a pre-specified futility analysis. The results of the study showed an ORR of 73% and a 91% disease control rate. After the sample size was expanded to 36 patients, nearly half of the patients experienced tumor shrinkage (ORR of 47%), and eight of them achieved complete remission with mOS and mPFS of 30.9 and 11.9 months, respectively. The latest KEYMAKER-U02 study (substudy 02C) employed an “umbrella plus rolling arm” design to systematically evaluate the short-term efficacy of pembrolizumab in combination with multiple novel agents for neoadjuvant treatment of stage III melanoma. Results demonstrated that all three neoadjuvant regimens—pembrolizumab plus vibostolimab, pembrolizumab plus gebasaxterv, and pembrolizumab monotherapy—exhibited manageable safety profiles and favorable antitumor activity in stage III melanoma patients. Pathological complete response (pCR) rates were 38%, 28%, and 40% for the three regimens, respectively. Despite the encouraging preliminary findings, the long-term efficacy and safety of Pembrolizumab in combination with CVA21 still need to be further validated.

Adverse reactions of immune checkpoint inhibitors combined with oncolytic viruses

We conducted a systematic meta-analysis of the incidence of AEs in a number of the previously mentioned clinical studies, and the relevant detailed data have been compiled in Table 2. Overall, the incidence of AEs at any level was typically around 50%, a rate that is broadly consistent with the meta-analysis by Liu et al.. Of these AEs, the most common included pruritus, fatigue, diarrhea, pyrexia, rash, and chills, and most were mild to moderate (grade 1/2). However, it is worth noting that after the investigators’ evaluation, it was found that the occurrence of grade 3 and higher AEs was mostly closely related to the use of ICIs. For example, serious adverse events such as grade 3 autoimmune hepatitis, aseptic meningitis, keratoacanthoma, and grade 4 pneumonia occurred when PD-1 inhibitors were combined with T-VEC or V937.

From the data in Table 2, the combination of pembrolizumab with ONCOS-102 showed relatively low rates of overall AEs and grade 3/4 adverse events. In contrast, two combination regimens, T-VEC with ipilimumab and CVA21 with ipilimumab/pembrolizumab, had higher rates of overall AEs and some risk of fatality. In a meta-analysis conducted by Zou et al., the incidence of serious immune-related adverse events (irAEs, grade ≥ 3) induced by OVs monotherapy or in combination with ICIs was reported, and the results showed a significant increase in the value to 39% after combination with ICIs, compared to the 12% incidence of AEs with OVs monotherapy. This result differs slightly from the findings of a large network meta-analysis. The meta-analysis, which covered 13 randomized controlled trials involving 1,939 patients, showed that the combination of OVs with ICIs was expected to provide a better ORR compared with other agents such as radiotherapy or targeted therapies, while not significantly increasing the incidence of grade ≥ 3 AEs.

In addition, a systematic analysis of 34 clinical studies by Wang et al. reported a correlation between an increase in adverse effects and efficacy. In the combination of T-VEC with ICIs, the occurrence of chills is considered a positive sign of complete tumor remission, but this does not mean that sustained disease control can be achieved. Taking the clinical study of pembrolizumab combined with T-VEC as an example, in terms of efficacy, the complete response rate (CRR) in the T-VEC plus pembrolizumab combination group showed a significant advantage over the placebo plus pembrolizumab monotherapy group (17.9% vs 11.6%); Regarding safety, two key characteristics of adverse reactions emerged: First, the overall incidence of treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) was slightly higher in the combination group (88.4% vs. 74.6% in the monotherapy group). Second, the proportion of Grade 3 or higher treatment-related adverse events (AEs) in the combination group was 20.3%, significantly higher than the 15.7% in the monotherapy group. All adverse reactions were manageable with symptomatic treatment. The study also reported a small number of fatal adverse reactions, which investigators considered to fall within the known adverse reaction profiles of both T-VEC and pembrolizumab. Nonetheless, caution is warranted regarding the potential for adverse reactions during combination therapy.

Recommendations for the management of adverse reactions to immune checkpoint inhibitors combined with oncolytic viruses

Due to the lack of a unified and systematic program for the management of adverse reactions to ICIs + OVs combination therapy, there is no clear reference standard in clinical practice. In view of this, this review proposes the following management recommendations based on the available data on AEs as well as clinical practice experience, and taking into account the characteristics of the occurrence of adverse reactions (Fig. 4):

Pre-treatment: baseline examination, risk assessment

A thorough assessment of a patient’s underlying health status is required before a decision can be made as to whether he or she is a suitable candidate for ICIs + OVs combination therapy. This includes a detailed review of the patient’s medical history, with special attention paid to those conditions that may affect the effectiveness of treatment or increase the risk of adverse effects, such as high-risk patients with a history of cardiovascular disease or advanced age should routinely undergo cardiac function assessments during combined ICIs + OVs therapy. This includes regular monitoring of cardiac markers such as cardiac troponin I/troponin T (cTnI/cTnT) and brain natriuretic peptide (BNP/NT-proBNP) before and during treatment, supplemented with echocardiography when necessary. This approach facilitates early identification of myocardial injury or heart failure risk. At the same time, the patient’s physical status is assessed using the Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) score or the Kahn Score (KPS) to determine whether they are able to tolerate the possible adverse effects of combination therapy. In addition, laboratory tests and imaging tests can be used to further understand patients’ organ function and immune system status to assess their susceptibility to adverse effects.

During treatment: full monitoring, hierarchical management

Full dynamic monitoring needs to cover the full treatment cycle and months after completion, focusing on the potential for superimposed toxicity (e.g., severe hepatitis/pneumonia, CRS-like reactions) or unique adverse reactions (OVs-associated influenza-like symptoms, localized inflammation) that may be triggered by synergism between ICIs and OVs. For example, regarding “local necrosis at the injection site” associated with OVs, add: “avoid routine debridement; prioritize topical antiviral gel combined with sterile dressing care while monitoring for concurrent infection.” For “immune myocarditis” associated with ICIs (linked to fatal heart failure risk), clarify that “treatment must be immediately suspended upon elevated troponin levels or chest tightness symptoms, initiating glucocorticoid pulse therapy (e.g., methylprednisolone 1–2 mg/kg/day) alongside cardiovascular specialist evaluation”; T-VEC should be administered based on body surface area. When combined with pembrolizumab, avoid additive ICI-related toxicity due to OVs activating immune responses. Close monitoring for 48 h is recommended after the first combined administration. In principle, combination therapy should be monitored more frequently than monotherapy, especially during the initial first 1–3 months, which is the period of highest risk for adverse reactions. The monitoring program includes a daily patient symptom diary, healthcare system checkups, screening of laboratory indicators, and targeted imaging/organ function tests, such as body temperature, lymphocyte subset ratios, and cytokine levels. For high-risk groups with previous autoimmune diseases and history of chronic infections, symptom monitoring and clinical review should be strengthened, and weekly to biweekly comprehensive assessments are recommended. In addition, synchronization with steroid hormones or antiviral drugs should be avoided during ICIs + OVs combination therapy because potential drug interactions may weaken the overall efficacy of the combination therapy, exacerbate the immune response, and increase the risk of multi-organ immunotoxicity.

Clinicians can implement individualized stratified management of patients based on the risk and severity of adverse reactions. To the extent possible, distinguishing whether a reaction originates from ICIs, OVs, or both synergistically contributes to the patient’s subsequent management. To determine whether an adverse reaction is caused by ICIs, the severity of irAEs should be graded by referring to the Common Terminology Criteria for the Evaluation of Adverse Events (CTCAE) version 5.0, and appropriate management measures should be selected accordingly. For irAEs ≥ grade 2, intervention with a low, medium, or high dose gradient of glucocorticoid analogs is usually required in addition to symptomatic treatment. Additionally, in cases of grade 3–4 severe adverse reactions, ICIs therapy should be delayed/interrupted or permanently discontinued rather than undergoing conventional dose adjustments. For OVs therapy, dose/administration regimens may be adjusted as appropriate to reduce the risk of severe toxicity. Under standardized diagnosis and treatment, the vast majority of adverse reactions can be effectively controlled, and related symptoms can be significantly reduced or even disappear. For example, the thyroid disorders such as hypothyroidism reported by Chesney et al. and Silk et al. in combination therapy studies were effectively controlled through hormone replacement therapy and did not result in treatment discontinuation.

Post-treatment: regular follow-up, patient education

Long-term systematic follow-up (≥ 1 year) is still needed after the end of combination therapy, focusing on monitoring delayed or potential adverse reactions. The frequency of follow-up should be adjusted individually according to the previous toxicity grade. At the initial stage of follow-up, it is recommended that the patient’s symptoms, organ function and tumor status should be comprehensively evaluated every 1–3 months, and then the follow-up interval can be gradually extended according to the patient’s specific situation. At the level of patient education, patients should be provided with comprehensive education on treatment-related knowledge, explaining in detail the possible adverse effects of combination therapy and its management measures, such as the care of rashes, dietary adjustments for diarrhea, and symptom recognition of abnormal thyroid function. Concurrently, proactive one-on-one psychological counseling should be provided at each stage of the patient’s ICIs plus OVs combination therapy (≥ 30 min weekly for the first four cycles) to minimize baseline anxiety, enhance self-efficacy, and improve subsequent treatment adherence.

Conclusions

The immunotherapy revolution in solid tumors began in melanoma, and it can be assumed that melanoma patients are the beneficiaries of the ICIs treatment era. Nevertheless, a significant proportion of patients still face bottlenecks of treatment non-response or resistance, management of treatment-related toxicity, and efficacy in specific subtypes (e.g., liver metastases, mucosal melanoma) and special populations.The anti-tumor mechanism of OVs complements ICIs therapy well and compensates for its ineffectiveness in “cold” tumors due to immunosuppression of the tumor microenvironment. OVs can complement ICIs therapy and compensate for their ineffectiveness against “cold” tumors due to immunosuppression of the tumor microenvironment. Adverse effects are an unavoidable consideration in ICIs monotherapy or combination therapy, but most clinical trials of OVs have not reported adverse effects other than influenza-like symptoms and local injection reactions, and the rate of treatment discontinuation due to toxicity is low, which is a safety feature that has certain advantages in combination therapy. Many of the available trial results have shown preliminary evidence that combination regimens of ICIs and OVs show potential for reversing drug resistance compared to monotherapy with either ICIs or OVs. Safety-wise, adverse effects of ICIs have not been exacerbated and are overall well tolerated.

In conclusion, the application of this combination therapy still faces many challenges: at this stage, the clinical trials of ICIs in combination with OVs mainly focus on phase I/II, with limitations such as small sample size and short follow-up time; how to explain the difference in efficacy produced by T-VEC in combination with CTLA-4 inhibitors or PD-1 inhibitors for melanoma? How to choose the optimal combination regimen of ICIs according to different cancer types, and does the administration of OVs mask the adverse effects that ICIs may cause in some patients? These questions still need to be further validated in larger and longer-term clinical studies. However, this will not mask the bright future of ICIs combined with OVs. With the deepening of basic mechanism research and the iterative optimization of clinical protocols, this combination strategy is expected to be extended to the treatment modalities of different cancer types!.
