# Application and Progress of Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) in the Treatment of Metastatic Triple-Negative Breast Cancer.

## Abstract

Metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) lacks actionable targets, and chemotherapy yields median progression-free survival (mPFS) of 4.6-9.7 months and median overall survival (mOS) of 12.6-26.3 months. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors only help subsets (objective response rate [ORR] of ~ 5% and ~ 12%, respectively). Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have emerged as a leading therapeutic strategy: sacituzumab govitecan extended mPFS to 5.6 months (ASCENT trial), SKB264 to 6.7 months, and datopotamab deruxtecan achieved an ORR of 79%. In Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-low TNBC, trastuzumab deruxtecan prolonged OS to 18.2 months, while disitamab vedotin and SHR-A1811 achieved ORRs of 26% and 60%, respectively. ADCs targeting Human epidermal growth factor receptor 3 (HER3)-, Nectin-4-, LIV-1- and folate receptor α (FRα) showed responses in 22%-54% of cases. Resistance can arise via antigen loss, endocytic defects, lysosomal failure, and efflux pumps. Bispecific ADCs, linker optimization, and combination regimens (ICI, PARPi) are under investigation. Future efforts will focus on targeting epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and FRα and on developing multimodal immunovascular strategies to sustain clinical benefit.

Key Summary Points

Introduction

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) constitutes 10%–15% of all breast cancer (BC) cases. It is defined by immunohistochemistry showing < 1% expression of estrogen and progesterone receptors, along with no Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) amplification. TNBC is characterized by its aggressive behavior and poor prognosis, often presenting at a young age with a high likelihood of metastasis and low survival rates. Treatment for recurrent metastatic TNBC primarily involves chemotherapy, although new therapeutic options have emerged, including antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase inhibitors (PARPIs), immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), angiogenesis inhibitors, and other agents. The introduction of new therapeutic agents has transformed the treatment of patients with metastatic TNBC, with ADCs marking a major technological advancement in tumor therapy. Developing new therapeutic options for BC is crucial because of the significant limitations of traditional chemotherapy, such as high toxicity and limited efficacy. ADCs offer a promising solution to this challenge by providing a novel therapeutic option. The antibody component of ADCs is designed to recognize the surface antigens of tumor cells and bind specifically to them. The antigen-antibody complex formed following internalization into the tumor cells via endocytosis and cytosolization is then phagocytosed by intracellular lysosomes. After this, lysosomal proteases cleave the ADCs, and the loads are removed from the cells by endocytosis. The process of cleaving ADC results in the release of the load into the cytoplasm in an active form. This effect has been demonstrated to trigger tumor cell death while minimizing harm to normal tissues. This paper systematically reviews recent advancements in ADC therapy for metastatic TNBC (mTNBC). This study offers a detailed overview of the clinical applications, efficacy, safety, and future development trends of ADC therapy for the specified condition. The objective of this study is to establish a robust foundation for the optimization of ADC therapy in the management of mTNBC.

Treatment Beyond Antibody-drug Conjugate in Metastatic TNBC

At present, chemotherapy remains the primary treatment for metastatic TNBC. The study concludes that the effectiveness of anthracyclines and paclitaxel as initial treatments, as well as the addition of platinum, capecitabine, or gemcitabine following their failure, is inadequate. For patients with mTNBC undergoing first-line chemotherapy, the median progression-free survival (mPFS) ranged from 4.6 to 9.0 months, while the median overall survival (mOS) ranged from 12.6 to 30.05 months. These findings led to the exploration of rescue therapy for TNBC. Consequently, TNBC rescue therapy has been the focus of continuous exploration to enhance its therapeutic efficacy.

The study’s findings suggest that TNBC exhibits increased vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) protein expression and gene amplification frequency, indicating the potential effectiveness of anti-angiogenic agents for TNBC treatment. In the context of advanced TNBC (aTNBC), the combination of bevacizumab and chemotherapy demonstrated an enhanced response rate and prolonged PFS. A comprehensive review of the available Literature revealed no statistically significant improvement in overall survival. In 2011, the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) withdrew its approval of bevacizumab for BC treatment based on these findings. Most guidelines in Europe, the US, and China still advocate for bevacizumab because of its PFS benefit. The evidence for utilizing large-molecule anti-angiogenic agents, excluding bevacizumab, in metastatic TNBC is limited. Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs), as small molecules, have shown promise as anti-angiogenic agents in treating metastatic TNBC. The Expert Consensus on the Over-the-Counter Use of Small Molecule Anti-Angiogenic Drugs for Advanced Breast Cancer offers guidance on using vascular epidermal growth factor receptor (VEGFR)-TKIs like apatinib in BC treatment.

ICIs are a major advancement in treating aTNBC, characterized by elevated Programmed Cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression, increased immune cell infiltration, and a higher mutational load compared to other BC subtypes. These findings indicate that TNBC could be responsive to Programmed Cell Death Protein 1 (PD-1)/Programmed Cell Death Ligand 1 (PD-L1) inhibitor immunotherapy. The response rate of single-agent ICIs for TNBC is generally low, with approximately 5% in an unscreened population. In the KEYNOTE-012 (NCT01848834) trial, pembrolizumab monotherapy achieved an objective response rate (ORR) of 18.5% and a disease control rate (DCR) of 25.9% in 27 cases of PD-1/PD-L1 positive aTNBC. In the phase I study (NCT01375842) of atezolizumab monotherapy in 116 patients with metastatic TNBC, those with PD-L1-positive status showed improved outcomes, including a higher ORR (12% vs 0%), DCR (15% vs 5%), and longer mOS (10.1 months vs 6.0 months). The study did not show significant efficacy or safety benefits. Nonetheless, specific PD-1 antibodies, such as camrelizumab, pembrolizumab, and nivolumab, along with PD-L1 antibodies, like durvalumab, atezolizumab, and avelumab, have been approved and marketed, establishing the use of ICI monotherapy for metastatic TNBC. The ORR and DCR are unsatisfactory, accompanied by a notable rise in adverse reactions. Therefore, interest in the enhanced therapeutic efficacy of ICIs is increasing. In addition to identifying effective biomarkers of ICIs, such as PD-L1 expression and tumor mutation load, which are aimed at enhancing the therapeutic efficacy of ICIs, there is also an exploration of combination therapy modes of ICIs. This research aims to improve the efficacy of PD-1/PD-L1 blockade therapy and convert non-responders into responders. The research landscape of combining chemotherapy with ICIs has been extensively examined. However, the efficacy of different chemotherapeutic agents in anti-tumor effects is inconsistent, and the enhancement of immunotherapy by affecting the tumor microenvironment is also inconsistent. Consequently, the research on “chemotherapy combined with ICIs” has produced similar methods but inconsistent conclusions. KEYNOTE-355 demonstrated the efficacy of the PD-1 inhibitor, pembrolizumab, in combination with chemotherapy, compared with a placebo and chemotherapy, in untreated patients with locally recurrent, inoperable, or metastatic TNBC. The combination of pembrolizumab and chemotherapy significantly improved PFS and OS in first-line treatment of metastatic TNBC with a combined positive score (CPS) of ≥ 10. Similarly, the IMpassion130 study showed that atezolizumab with nab-paclitaxel significantly enhanced PFS in the intention-to-treat (ITT) population compared to placebo with nab-paclitaxel for metastatic TNBC (7.2 vs 5.5 months, HR = 0.80, P = 0.0025). The population with PD-L1 positivity exhibited significantly higher mPFS and mOS in both groups. While PFS and OS showed significant benefits with manageable adverse events, the IMpassion131 study revealed that for first-line treatment of metastatic TNBC in PD-L1-positive patients, atezolizumab combined with paclitaxel did not significantly lower the risk of progression and death compared to the placebo plus paclitaxel group. In contrast to the favorable outcomes observed in the KEYNOTE-355 and IMpassion130 studies, which examined the initial treatment of metastatic TNBC, the IMpassion131 study yielded adverse results. The following factors may contribute to this discrepancy. First, nab-paclitaxel exhibits a high degree of specificity for tumor tissues, enhancing its anti-tumor properties. Second, the administration of solvent-based paclitaxel necessitates the implementation of glucocorticoid prophylaxis. The presence of active glucocorticoid signaling has been observed to impede the immune sites of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, thereby diminishing the efficacy of the immune drug. Research indicates that nab-paclitaxel does not necessitate glucocorticoids for allergy prevention, thereby not hindering the immune activation of atezolizumab. The increase in activated and proliferated CD8 + T cells is, of course, effective for an ICI.

The ‘anti-angiogenic drug-chemotherapy-enhanced ICIs treatment model’ has the potential to address the conundrum that ‘there are differences in the efficacy of different chemotherapeutic agents to modulate the tumor microenvironment in order to enhance immunotherapy.’ The FUTURE series of studies examined the effectiveness of combining camrelizumab with famitinib and nab-paclitaxel for treating patients with immunomodulatory (IM) subtypes of aTNBC. The FUTURE-SUPER study suggests that a targeted treatment approach based on molecular subtypes and genomic markers, specifically using “antiangiogenic drugs combined with chemotherapy and ICIs,” significantly extended the PFS for first-line treatment of mTNBC compared to standard chemotherapy regimens. The study comprised 139 patients with either metastatic or locally advanced inoperable mTNBC. The patients were divided into five treatment arms based on TNBC subtypes and genomic biomarkers and randomized 1:1 to receive either standard chemotherapy with nab-paclitaxel (Nab-P) (control arm) or precision therapy with Nab-P in combination with targeted/immunological agents (precision therapy arm). The IM subtype (Nab-P + famitinib + camrelizumab vs Nab-P) demonstrated the most substantial mPFS improvement, with an Absolute benefit of 8.6 months (15.1 vs 6.5 months), marking the most significant survival advantage. The FUTURE-SUPER study supports the future use of a regimen combining antiangiogenic drugs, chemotherapy, and ICIs for aTNBC.

PARPi, by inhibiting the defective homologous recombination DNA repair pathway, has made significant advancements in treating aTNBC. In the OlympiAD study, 50% of patients with advanced HER2-negative BC and BRCA mutations were diagnosed with TNBC. The analysis showed that olaparib significantly improved mPFS compared to chemotherapy (7.0 months vs 4.2 months, HR = 0.58, P < 0.01). In the TNBC subgroup, olaparib showed an extended mOS compared to chemotherapy, but the difference was not statistically significant (P > 0.05). The EMBRACA study evaluated the efficacy of talazoparib versus chemotherapy in advanced BC with BRCA1/2 germline mutations. Talazoparib significantly enhanced the ORR and mPFS in patients, with a median of 8.6 months compared to 5.6 months (HR = 0.54, P < 0.01). The mOS difference between the two groups was not statistically significant (P > 0.05). It is estimated that approximately 12% of patients with TNBC who have a pathogenic BRCA mutation could benefit from PARPi therapy. In contrast, most patients with TNBC who do not carry a BRCA mutation do not respond positively to this form of therapy. In view of the lack of precision and resistance of PARP inhibitors in the TNBC population, there is a need to screen for more potent suggestive markers for DNA damage repair therapy as well as to find new targets to ameliorate resistance.

Existing therapies (chemotherapy, anti-angiogenics, ICIs, and PARP inhibitors) remain limited in terms of efficacy, precision, durability, and broad patient applicability. These challenges underscore the urgent need for novel therapeutic strategies in mTNBC. ADCs have emerged as a promising approach, capable of selectively delivering potent cytotoxic agents directly to tumor cells, thereby potentially overcoming the limitations of existing therapies.

Antibody-drug Conjugate and Metastatic TNBC

The precise targeting capability of antibodies combined with the potent cytotoxic effects of payloads in ADCs enables the effective and targeted destruction of tumor cells, making it a key area in antitumor drug research and development. By 2024, 16 ADC products had received global marketing approval, with 8 approved in China, and over 200 clinical trials are ongoing. Currently, ADCs for TNBC mainly target trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (Trop-2) and HER2 in tumors with low HER2 expression. The investigation of ADC drugs directed toward novel targets Like Human epidermal growth factor receptor 3 (HER3) and the cell adhesion molecule Nectin-4 has shown initial promise in treating TNBC. Antibody-drug Conjugates Targeting Trop-2

Trop-2, a transmembrane glycoprotein encoded by the TACSTD2 gene, has been Linked to aggressive behavior and poor prognosis. It is expressed in up to 90% of TNBC cases, while its presence in normal tissues is limited. This establishes it as a promising therapeutic target. Notable drugs such as sacituzumab govitecan (SG), sacituzumab tirumotecan (SKB264/Sac-TMT), and datopotamab deruxtecan (Dato-DXd/DS-1062) have shown promising antitumor efficacy. The antibody component of both SG and SKB264 are humanized immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1hRS7), with dissociation constants of 0.2939 and 0.3083 nmol/l, respectively. The SKB264 antibody, homologous to SG, was optimized for a more stable binding site and reduced off-target risk through an irreversible coupling technique. The linkers of SG and SKB264 are found to be pH-dependent hydrolyzable CL2A-type linkers. Furthermore, SG uses a reversible maleimide-thiol linker, while SKB264 uses an irreversible methylthionylpyrimidine-thiol-coupled CL2A linker, with improved plasma stability. In antibody-drug conjugates, the coupling ratio is crucial for assessing the drug’s efficacy and safety. The drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) of 7.6 and 7.4 for SG and SKB264, respectively, indicates that each antibody molecule is connected to an average of 7.6 or 7.4 drug molecules. These coupling ratios can influence the drug’s pharmacokinetics and distribution. A study showed that SKB264 exhibited significant in vivo antitumor activity, with a 4.6-fold greater tumor tissue exposure compared to SG (IMMU-132). This finding suggests a more pronounced targeting effect and enhanced antitumor activity. The antibody for Dato-DXd is humanized IgG1MAAP-9001a with a dissociation constant of 27 nmol/l. The Linker is a lysosomal protease cleaved GGFG tetrapeptide Linker with a DAR of 4.0. The SG, SKB264, and Dato-DXd payloads are SN38, T-030, and DXd (topoisomerase I inhibitors), respectively, and thus possess divergent pharmacological properties.

SG

SG is the first approved ADC targeting the Trop-2 protein, marking a significant advancement in anticancer therapy. This Milestone was achieved through the phase III ASCENT study, involving 529 patients with unresectable, locally advanced, or metastatic TNBC. These patients had received at least two chemotherapy regimens, including one for metastatic disease. The study design randomized patients into two groups: the SG treatment arm and the chemotherapy arm, with a 2:1 ratio favoring the former. The chemotherapy arm encompassed various agents, including eribulin, vinorelbine, gemcitabine, and capecitabine, among others. The study revealed that the treatment group (267 cases) had a significantly longer mPFS of 5.6 months (95% CI 4.3–6.3) compared to 1.7 months (95% CI 1.5–2.6) in the chemotherapy group, with a HR of 0.41 (95% CI 0.32–0.52, P < 0.001). The treatment group showed a 59% reduction in disease progression risk, as indicated by the study’s primary outcome. For secondary endpoints, SG significantly improved patient OS, with a mOS of 12.1 months compared to 6.7 months in the control group. The analysis indicated a 52% decrease in mortality risk (HR = 0.48, 95% CI 0.38–0.59, P < 0.001). The treatment group exhibited an ORR of 35% (82/235), in contrast to 5% (11/233) in the chemotherapy control group. Regarding safety, grade 3 or higher treatment-related adverse events in the SG group versus the control group primarily included neutrophil deficiency (51% vs 33%), diarrhea (10% vs 1%), leukopenia (10% vs 5%), anemia (8% vs 5%), and granulocytopenic fever (6% vs 2%). The management of these side effects was achieved through dose adjustments and symptomatic treatment. The phase II clinical trial EVER-132-001, conducted in the Chinese population, demonstrated comparable efficacy outcomes, exhibiting an mPFS of 5.6 months and an ORR of 38.8% in patients undergoing treatment with SG. In 2022, China authorized the use of SG for the treatment of patients with unresectable locally advanced or metastatic TNBC who have received at least two systemic therapies, based on favorable study data. Current research is investigating the use of SG for TNBC (NCT05675579, NCT04595565, NCT05382299, NCT05382286). These studies primarily examine the role of SG in first-line treatment, neoadjuvant therapy, and combination with immunotherapy for aTNBC.

SKB264/Sac-TMT

SKB264/Sac-TMT represents the first original Chinese ADC for Trop-2. A phase III clinical study aimed to evaluate the efficacy of SKB264 in patients with locally relapsed or metastatic TNBC. The OptiTROP-Breast01 study enrolled 263 patients with locally relapsed or metastatic TNBC, all of whom had undergone at least two prior treatments, including at least one for metastatic disease. Patients were randomly allocated in equal proportions to either the SKB264 group or the chemotherapy group, which included treatments such as eribulin, vinorelbine, capecitabine, or gemcitabine. The study found that SKB264 significantly improved PFS to 6.7 months compared to 2.5 months with chemotherapy, with a HR of 0.32 (95% CI 0.24–0.44, P < 0.00001), indicating a 68% reduction in the risk of disease progression or death. The OS was 9.4 months for the chemotherapy group, while it was not reached for the SKB264 group, with a HR of 0.53 (95% confidence interval (CI) 0.36–0.78, P = 0.0005). The OptiTROP-Breast01 study’s subgroup analysis consistently showed benefits in PFS and OS. Notably, more than half of the patients treated with SKB264 survived beyond 1 year, with a 12-month overall survival rate of 45.4%. Regarding safety, 63.1% of patients experienced grade 3 or higher TRAEs, with neutropenia (35%), anemia (29%), leukopenia (28%), oral mucositis (10%), and thrombocytopenia (13%) being the most common. Preliminary findings from the 2024 ESMO conference revealed that patients with mTNBC who had previously been treated with PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors had a significantly longer mPFS with SKB264 compared to chemotherapy (5.6 months vs 2.7 months, HR 0.31, 95% CI 0.17–0.54). In patients who had not received PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors, mPFS was 7.2 months for the SKB264 group versus 2.3 months for the chemotherapy group (HR 0.34, 95% CI 0.23–0.48). Patients with mTNBC can benefit from SKB264 treatment regardless of prior PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor therapy. Furthermore, a phase III clinical trial (NCT06279364) is currently underway to investigate the comparative efficacy of SKB264 and standard chemotherapy (e.g., paclitaxel) in patients with mTNBC, regardless of PD-L1 status, who have not responded to previous immunotherapy.

Dato-DXd/DS-1062

The TROPION-PanTumor01 study was a Phase I, multicentre, open-label, multi-dose investigation of Dato-DXd in patients with advanced solid tumors, such as HR +/HER2-BC and TNBC, conducted at 17 centres in Japan and the US. The study comprised 44 patients with TNBC who had undergone multiple lines of treatment (average of 3, ranging from 1 to 10) and were treated with Dato-DXd. The results showed an ORR of 31.8%, DCR of 79.5%, mPFS of 4.4 months, and mOS of 13.5 months. Stomatitis was the most common toxic epidermal antigen-related adverse event in both HER2-negative BC (any grade: 82.9%, ≥ grade III: 9.8%) and TNBC cohorts (any grade: 72.7%, ≥ grade III: 11.4%). The occurrence of hematological toxicity and diarrhea was minimal, with no cases of drug-related interstitial pneumonia reported. Numerous clinical trials are ongoing to evaluate the effectiveness of Dato-DXd, both alone and in combination, for treating TNBC across various lines of therapy. TROPION-Breast02 (NCT05374512) is a phase III trial aimed at comparing the efficacy of Dato-DXd against chemotherapy for first-line treatment of metastatic TNBC. Future studies, TROPION-Breast03, 04, and 05 (NCT05629585, NCT06112379, NCT06103864), will examine the impact of Dato-DXd combined with ICIs. The Phase 1/2 TROPION PanTumor02 study (NCT05460273) is examining the efficacy of Dato-DXd monotherapy for aTNBC in the Chinese population. Concurrently, the BEGONIA Phase Ib/II trial is assessing a first-line treatment for unresectable locally advanced or metastatic TNBC, with Cohort 7 exploring a combination of Dato-DXd and ICIs (durvalumab). The findings of Cohort 7 demonstrated that 87% of the 62 patients enrolled in the treatment were PD-L1-negative, and the combination therapy exhibited an ORR of 79% in first-line treatment, with a mPFS of 13.8 months and a mDoR of 15.5 months. The remission rate from treatment was independent of PD-L1 expression, with ongoing studies focusing on PD-L1-positive patients with TNBC.

ADCs targeting HER2

Tarantino et al. were the first to introduce the concept of "HER2 low-expression BC" in 2020. HER2 low expression is defined as an immunohistochemistry (IHC) score of 1 + or 2 + with negative in situ hybridization (ISH). Approximately 35% of patients with TNBC show low HER2 expression. Initial attempts to prove the effectiveness of early HER2-targeting ADCs, like trastuzumab emtansine (TDM1), in these patients were unsuccessful. Recent advancements in the field of antibody-coupled drug development, exemplified by T-DXd, have led to significant therapeutic breakthroughs. These innovations encompass structural changes such as a high drug-antibody ratio (DAR = 8), the use of a cleavable linker, and the induction of a potent bystander effect. The drug’s ability to penetrate the cell membrane leads to the elimination of adjacent tumor cells with low or negative HER2 expression. The phase III DESTINY-Breast04 study showed that T-DXd significantly extended mPFS and mOS while improving the objective remission rate in patients with HER2 low-expressing TNBC. Studies have shown that other HER2-targeting ADCs, such as disitamab vedotin (RC48) and trastuzumab-rezetecan (SHR-A1811), also achieve high remission rates. These advances have transformed the therapeutic landscape for TNBC, establishing low HER2 expression as a distinct therapeutic target and offering novel precision therapy options for patients with TNBC who have limited effective treatment options.

T-DXd

The efficacy of T-DXd in the treatment of TNBC, particularly in cases exhibiting low HER2 expression, can be attributed to its innovative overall structural design. T-DXd is composed of a novel DNA topoisomerase I inhibitor, DXd, linked to a humanized anti-HER2 immunoglobulin G1 antibody via a cleavable tetrapeptide Linker. It features a high drug-antibody ratio of 8:1, maintains a stable count of antibody-conjugated cytotoxic drugs, and demonstrates significant homogeneity. Additionally, it has shown a strong bystander effect, capable of destroying tumor cells with low or no HER2 expression near the target cells. In the phase Ib clinical trial, T-DXd demonstrated an ORR of 37% for treating HER2 low-expressing metastatic BC. In the phase II DAISY trial, T-DXd achieved a 37.5% ORR in the treatment of HER2 low-expressing metastatic BC. DESTINY-Breast04 is the first phase III trial for HER2-low-expressing BC, aiming to compare the efficacy of T-DXd against chemotherapy in patients with unresectable and/or metastatic BC who have undergone one or two chemotherapy treatments. The study revealed that T-DXd significantly improved mPFS (9.9 vs 5.1 months, HR = 0.50, P < 0.001) and mOS (23.4 vs 16.8 months, HR = 0.64, P = 0.001) in the overall population. A total of 213 patients from East Asian regions were randomized to receive T-DXd (n = 147) or treatment of physician’s choice (TPC) (n = 66). In hormone receptor-positive East Asian patients with mBC, the mPFS was 10.9 months for T-DXd and 5.3 months for TPC. In the East Asian population, the mPFS was 10.9 months for T-DXd and 4.6 months for TPC. In both populations, the mOS for T-DXd was not reached, while the mOS for TPC was 19.9 months. Among East Asian patients, T-DXd demonstrated a higher ORR compared to TPC. The median therapy duration was 8.4 months for T-DXd and 3.5 months for TPC. In East Asian patients receiving T-DXd, the most common grade ≥ 3 drug-related adverse events were neutropenia (16.3%), anemia (12.9%), and leukopenia (11.6%), with higher rates of neutropenia and leukopenia observed in TPC treatment. T-DXd was associated with a 14.3% incidence of drug-related interstitial Lung disease or pneumonia, mostly grade 1–2 cases. This study suggests that T-DXd enhances PFS and OS in patients with low HER2 expression, regardless of HR status or East Asian ethnicity. Based on strong evidence from DESTINY-Breast 04, T-DXd was approved in China in July 2023 for treating adult patients with unresectable or metastatic HER2 low-expression BC who have undergone at least one systemic therapy in the metastatic phase and experience relapse within 6 months post-adjuvant chemotherapy or during/after chemotherapy. Several key clinical studies are investigating T-DXd in HER2-low-expressing BC. DESTINY-Breast15 (NCT05950945) specifically examines the efficacy and safety of T-DXd in the TNBC subpopulation with HER2-low-expressing or negative status. The primary endpoint of the study is “time to next line of therapy.” The study NCT04042701 explored the novel therapeutic combination of T-DXd and pembrolizumab for patients with low HER2 expression. A plethora of registry studies have consistently corroborated the efficacy assessment of T-DXd in diverse patient populations, encompassing varying numbers of treatment lines and special populations, such as those with brain metastases. These studies will broaden the scope of T-DXd’s application in patients with HER2 low- and ultra-low-expressing BC.

RC48

RC48 is an anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody conjugated to monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) via a protease-cleavable linker, resulting in an ADC with a safer profile than high DAR drugs. The phase Ib C003CANCER trial evaluated the efficacy and safety of RC48 in patients with low-HER2-expression advanced BC. The study enrolled 66 patients with advanced HER2-low-expressing BC, who received a biweekly RC48 regimen. The study found a 33.3% ORR and a mPFS of 5.1 months at a 2.0-mg/kg dose in patients with low HER2 expression. Grade 3 or higher treatment-related adverse events comprised neutropenia, elevated glutamyl transpeptidase, malaise, reduced white blood cell count, and sensory abnormalities. Additionally, a study investigated RC48’s application in HER2-positive and HER2-low-expressing metastatic BC cases. This multicenter, real-world study enrolled 154 patients, including 50 with low HER2 expression. The study revealed that patients with low HER2 expression had a mPFS of 4.28 months and an ORR of 26.0%. The findings reinforce the effectiveness of RC48 in treating patients with HER2 low-expression BC.

SHR-A1811

The SHR-A1811 ADC represents a novel therapeutic agent that exhibits favorable therapeutic potential in BC, exhibiting varying levels of HER2 expression. In a phase II clinical trial, SHR-A1811 also demonstrated efficacy and safety in the preoperative treatment of HER2-positive BC, with a pathological complete remission rate (pCR) of 63.2% with SHR-A1811 monotherapy and 62.5% when combined with pyrotinib. This study explores SHR-A1811 as a treatment for recurrent metastatic HER2-positive BC, a topic under current investigation. Additionally, the application of SHR-A1811 in TNBC with low HER2 expression has attracted considerable attention. In a global multicentre phase I clinical trial, SHR-A1811 achieved a 60.4% ORR in patients with HER2-low-expressing BC, indicating its potential therapeutic efficacy.

ADCs Targeting Other Targets

The clinical bottleneck posed by primary or acquired resistance to Trop-2- and HER2-directed ADCs has accelerated the systematic exploration of alternative surface antigens in TNBC. Increasing pre-clinical and early-phase data now support a diversified portfolio of next-generation ADCs, whose efficacy depends not only on target prevalence but also on the unique biology of each antigen, biology that may help bypass classical resistance mechanisms. The advancement of ADCs targeting novel antigens has marked significant progress in addressing the limitations of traditional therapies for TNBC. HER3-DXd targets 28% of HER3-overexpressed TNBC cases, achieving a single-agent ORR of 22.6%. Recent studies show an improved ORR of 54% when patients are treated with the Estrogen-regulated Protein LIV-1 (LIV-1)-targeted ADC ladiratuzumab vedotin (LV) combined with immunotherapy. Nectin-4 is overexpressed in 62% of TNBC cases, and drugs like enfortumab vedotin effectively induce cell death via the bystander effect. IMGN853, targeting folate receptor α (FRα), has shown efficacy in preclinical models, while combination strategies involving epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and orphan receptor 2 (ROR2) targets are under investigation to address resistance. The ADCs mentioned above provide diversified therapeutic options for TNBC, especially in cases where conventional therapy has limited efficacy. ADC drugs enhance cell killing by specifically targeting tumor cells with cytotoxic agents, minimizing harm to normal cells. These advances have engendered novel prospects and methodologies for the management of TNBC.

ADCs Targeting HER3

HER3, a member of the HER family, is distinguished by its limited intracellular tyrosine kinase activity. The process of downstream signal transduction in HER3 is dependent on its Ability to form a heterodimer, and it has been observed that approximately 28% of TNBC cases exhibit elevated levels of HER3 expression. Patritumab deruxtecan (HER3-DXd/U3-1402) is the inaugural ADC targeting HER3. It comprises patritumab, a monoclonal antibody against HER3’s extracellular domain, and DXd, connected to patritumab by a cleavable Linker with a DAR of 8. The phase I/II clinical trial results showed a 22.6% objective ORR in 53 patients with TNBC, with a mPFS of 5.5 months and a mOS of 14.6 months. The study’s findings suggest that HER3-DXd is as effective as SG for the TNBC subgroup and that HER3-DXd shows significant potential as a HER3-targeted therapy in HER3-overexpressing BC.

ADCs Targeting LIV-1

LIV-1 (ZIP6), part of the zinc ion transmembrane transporter family, is prevalent in hormonally regulated tissues. LIV-1 expression has been identified as a common feature in metastatic BC. LV is an ADC consisting of a monoclonal antibody directed against LIV-1. This is connected to the powerful microtubule inhibitor, MMAE, through a cleavable dipeptide linker. The compound has been demonstrated to bind to the extracellular structural domain of LIV-1, undergo internalization, and be transported to the lysosome. It has been shown that in this location, the compound exerts its cytotoxic effect by hydrolyzing the chemotherapeutic agent MMAE. LV has been shown to trigger an immune response by inducing immunogenic cell death, creating a tumor microenvironment that enhances the effectiveness of immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy. In a phase I clinical trial involving 44 patients with TNBC, the combined dose escalation and expansion cohort achieved an ORR of 32% and a mPFS of 11.3 weeks. A phase Ib/II clinical study assessed the efficacy and safety of combining ladiratuzumab vedotin with pembrolizumab as a first-line treatment for patients with metastatic TNBC. The study demonstrated a 54% ORR in the 26 patients treated with the combination therapy, indicating its potential clinical benefit. Additionally, the study noted that the combination therapy exhibited a manageable safety profile, indicating that it could be well tolerated by patients without significant adverse effects.

ADC Targeting Nectin-4

Nectin-4, a transmembrane cell adhesion molecule, is minimally expressed in normal human tissues but shows high expression in various solid tumors. Nectin-4 expression has been detected in approximately 62% of patients with TNBC. Nectin-4 has been identified as a promising prognostic biomarker and a viable target for ADC therapies in TNBC. Various in vivo and in vitro studies have shown the notable therapeutic efficacy of Nectin-4-targeted ADCs. Enfortumab vedotin, an ADC drug targeting Nectin-4, is FDA-approved for uroepithelial carcinoma and has demonstrated potential in various clinical and preclinical studies.

ADC Targeting FRα

The membrane protein FRα is expressed in 70% to 86% of metastatic TNBC cases and is closely linked to poor prognosis. The elevated expression of FRα makes it a viable prognostic and therapeutic target. IMGN853, an ADC drug, targets FRα to inhibit tumor growth by binding to FRα-positive cells and releasing cytotoxic agents. A plethora of studies have shown that IMGN853 demonstrates potent antitumor activity in a variety of FRα-expressing xenograft tumor models. This introduces a novel therapeutic approach specifically for patients with FRα-positive tumors.

ADCs Targeting EGFR

EGFR expression in TNBC exhibits significant inter-patient variability, ranging from 13 to 76%. Clinical trials of ADCs targeting the EGFR show that only a small percentage of patients with TNBC respond to EGFR-targeted therapies, suggesting possible resistance mechanisms during treatment. Alternating between EGFR and Src family tyrosine kinases has been shown to increase cellular sensitivity to EGFR TKIs. This discovery suggests a new direction for developing innovative combinatorial therapies. A thorough grasp of EGFR’s role and its interactions with other signaling pathways in TNBC is crucial for improving the effectiveness of EGFR-targeted therapies.

Furthermore, research is exploring new targets for TNBC, such as receptor tyrosine kinase like ROR2 and so on. ADCs targeting these novel sites are in clinical trials, potentially providing more precise treatment options for patients with TNBC. Subsequent studies will continue to explore new targets and more effective drug combinations, aiming to improve treatment efficacy and minimize side effects.

Taken together, these emerging ADCs, targeting HER3, Nectin-4, FRα, EGFR, and ROR2, demonstrate promising efficacy in mTNBC, with ORRs ranging from 22 to 54%. Developed partly in response to resistance to Trop-2 and HER2-directed agents, they represent the next wave of ADC strategies. However, accumulating evidence indicates that even these novel approaches may face similar resistance mechanisms, underscoring the importance of investigating the biological basis of ADC resistance and developing strategies to overcome it.

Mechanisms and Countermeasures of ADC Drug Resistance in TNBC Application

The mechanisms of ADC drug resistance are complex and varied, including antibody-mediated resistance, blocked endocytosis, impaired lysosomal function, and chemotherapy-associated resistance. First, antibody-mediated resistance may be associated with downregulation or mutation of antigens on the surface of target cells. This can result in the inability of ADCs to bind and function effectively. For instance, mutations in TROP2 have been demonstrated to result in ADC resistance, and the T256R missense mutation in TROP2 has been demonstrated to induce ADC resistance by affecting cell membrane localization and antibody binding. Furthermore, it is widely acknowledged that HER2 gene mutations play a pivotal role in the development of ADC resistance. For instance, the L755S mutation in HER2 has been demonstrated to induce acquired resistance to HER2-targeted therapy by reactivating the HER signaling network. Second, impaired endocytosis of ADCs is also an important mechanism of drug resistance. Endocytosis represents a pivotal step in the internalization process of ADCs, enabling the release of their cytotoxic drugs. The effectiveness of ADCs is notably reduced when endocytosis is obstructed. Third, ADC resistance can arise from impaired lysosomal function, as the degradation of ADCs in lysosomes is essential for releasing their active components. An increase in lysosomal pH or compromised protein hydrolyzing activity can hinder this process, resulting in drug resistance. Fourth, the role of chemotherapy-associated resistance in ADC resistance is also of significance. TNBC cells may resist the cytotoxic effects of ADCs by upregulating drug efflux pumps or anti-apoptotic proteins. In summary, the phenomenon of ADC resistance in TNBC therapy involves multiple mechanisms, including antibody-mediated resistance, impaired endocytosis, impaired lysosomal function, and chemotherapy-associated resistance (Fig. 1). Investigating these resistance mechanisms is crucial for developing effective therapeutic strategies to overcome ADC resistance in TNBC treatment.

To overcome ADC resistance, researchers have been known to employ active measures as follows. First, the development of novel ADCs, or the improvement of the structure and function of existing ADCs, has the potential to overcome these resistance mechanisms to a certain extent. When designing the next generation of ADCs, it is equally important to select highly specific antigens that are stably expressed on tumor cells as well as payload drugs that do not cause cross-resistance. Concurrently, researchers are refining carrier-linker architectures to increase systemic stability and ensure precise, efficient drug release, thereby maximizing the therapeutic index. Linker optimization and multi-antigen targeting further enhance ADC potency and reduce resistance. Second, the combination of ADCs with other drugs has been identified as a highly effective strategy to overcome drug resistance. Numerous studies have shown that combining ADCs with ICIs can improve anti-tumor efficacy and address drug resistance. ADCs exert antitumor effects not only through direct cytotoxicity but also by modulating the immune microenvironment. They can induce immunogenic cell death (ICD), antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC), and dendritic cell activation, thereby potentially enhancing the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade. Based on these synergistic mechanisms, the combination of SG (sacituzumab govitecan) and pembrolizumab has demonstrated promising efficacy in aTNBC. The BEGONIA study also demonstrated the efficacy of the ADC drug Dato-DXd in combination with durvalumab as a first-line treatment for TNBC, independent of PD-L1 expression. Studies have shown that ADC drugs can synergize with PARPi. The combination of the SG's topoisomerase I inhibition and PARPi’s disruption of DNA repair enhances DNA damage, resulting in cell cycle arrest. This leads to synergistic growth inhibition, effectively overcoming resistance in both in vitro and in vivo models. A phase I/II clinical trial (NCT04039230) is currently assessing the dose-limiting toxicity of this regimen for metastatic TNBC, aiming to expand therapeutic options. This combination therapy strategy may improve ADC efficacy and simultaneously prevent or delay drug resistance through multiple mechanisms of action. Third, researchers are investigating biomarkers to predict patient sensitivity to ADCs, aiming to identify primary resistance early in treatment. Trop-2, a cell surface protein overexpressed in various cancers, has been studied as a potential biomarker for ADC therapy. By detecting the expression level of Trop-2, it can help predict the patient’s response to ADCs, thus optimizing the treatment regimen and avoiding unnecessary treatment delays and resource wastage. The application of liquid biopsy technology in TNBC is expected to enable precision medicine by providing a real-time assessment of tumor biology, identifying therapeutic targets, detecting minimal residual disease, and predicting response to treatment. Advances in spatial omics technologies have provided high-resolution insights into the tumor microenvironment, facilitating the discovery of novel therapeutic targets and spatially distinct biomarker patterns. In addition, recent studies have revealed that the regulation of iron metabolism, lipid peroxidation, and redox homeostasis plays a crucial role in reversing ADC resistance. For instance, the inhibition of crVDAC3 by means of reducing HSPB1 expression has been demonstrated to induce ferroptosis in breast cancer cells, thereby effectively reversing TDXT resistance.

In conclusion, the above strategies can be expected to overcome ADC resistance in TNBC treatment, which not only helps to improve the therapeutic efficacy but also provides more therapeutic options and hope for patients.

Summary and Outlook

Metastatic TNBC remains lethal because of the lack of actionable drivers. ADCs now represent a superior option over chemotherapy. The ASCENT trial demonstrated that sacituzumab govitecan doubled mPFS and prolonged mOS. SKB264 achieved a mPFS of 6.7 months with manageable toxicity, and datopotamab deruxtecan produced an ORR of 79%, particularly when combined with ICIs. Trastuzumab deruxtecan has extended OS to 18.2 months in HER2-low disease. Meanwhile, phase I/II ADCs targeting HER3, Nectin-4, LIV-1, and FRα have generally yielded responses in the range of 22%–54%. However, therapeutic resistance remains a key challenge, driven by factors such as antigen loss, endocytic defects, lysosomal failure, and efflux pumps. Countermeasures, such as bispecific constructs, linker optimization, ADC-plus-ICI/PARP combinations, and biomarker-guided selection, aim to overcome these limitations. Continued structural innovation and precision stratification are essential to translate these advances into durable survival gains.
