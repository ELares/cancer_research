# Poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase inhibitors in cancer therapy.

## Abstract

Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors (PARPis) have emerged as critical agents for cancer therapy. By inhibiting the catalytic activity of PARP enzymes and trapping them in the DNA, PARPis disrupt DNA repair, ultimately leading to cell death, particularly in cancer cells with homologous recombination repair deficiencies, such as those harboring BRCA mutations. This review delves into the mechanisms of action of PARPis in anticancer treatments, including the inhibition of DNA repair, synthetic lethality, and replication stress. Furthermore, the clinical applications of PARPis in various cancers and their adverse effects as well as their combinations with other therapies and the mechanisms underlying resistance are summarized. This review provides comprehensive insights into the role and mechanisms of PARP and PARPis in DNA repair, with a particular focus on the potential of PARPi-based therapies in precision medicine for cancer treatment.

Introduction

Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerases (PARPs) are vital enzymes involved in essential biological processes, such as DNA repair, maintenance of genomic stability, and apoptosis. The PARP family comprises several members, including PARP-1, PARP-2, and PARP-3, each of which has distinct yet occasionally overlapping functions in DNA repair. Notably, PARP-1 and PARP-2 play pivotal roles in DNA damage response (DDR). PARP-1, the most prominent member, plays a crucial role in the repair of single-strand breaks (SSBs), whereas PARP-2 and PARP-3 contribute to DDR and repair processes. In healthy cells, PARPs promptly recognize and repair DNA damage, thereby safeguarding genomic integrity and cell survival. Conversely, in cancer cells, particularly those harboring defective DNA repair mechanisms, PARP activity assumes increased importance, underscoring its role in tumor biology.

PARP inhibitors (PARPis) represent a class of medications that inhibit PARP activity, thereby disrupting DNA repair mechanisms. Originally developed to augment the effectiveness of chemotherapy, these drugs leverage the ability of chemotherapy to induce DNA damage and depend on the intrinsic repair capabilities of cancer cells. Subsequent research has revealed the remarkable efficacy of PARPis in treating cancers harboring BRCA1/2 mutations. Mutations in BRCA1/2 lead to the loss of homologous recombination repair (HRR) functionality, severely compromising the ability of cancer cells to repair DNA damage. Consequently, these cancer cells rely on alternative repair pathways, such as base excision repair (BER), to preserve their genomic integrity. PARPis block the BER pathway, preventing DNA repair in HRR-deficient cancer cells and leading to cell death. This phenomenon, called “synthetic lethality,” underpins the rationale for employing PARPis in cancer therapy. Not only do PARPis directly eliminate cancer cells through this mechanism but also amplify the potency of conventional anticancer treatments, significantly boosting cancer treatment success rates. Thus, PARPis have emerged as indispensable drugs in contemporary cancer management, particularly for addressing recalcitrant cancers that are resistant to standard therapies.

Since the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved the pioneering PARPi olaparib for the treatment of breast cancer susceptibility gene (BRCA)-mutated ovarian cancer in 2014, the landscape of PARPis has undergone significant expansion, with numerous novel agents being introduced to clinical practice. Drugs, such as olaparib, rucaparib, niraparib, and talazoparib, have shown diverse levels of efficacy across a broad spectrum of cancer types, particularly in patients with BRCA-mutated ovarian, breast, and prostate cancers, where these drugs have achieved impressive outcomes.

This comprehensive review delves into the current status of PARPis, examining its mechanisms of action, clinical research endeavors, resistance mechanisms, and combination therapy approaches. Furthermore, it is important to assess the prospects of these inhibitors for cancer treatment. Therefore, this review aims to provide a clear understanding of the mechanisms, clinical applications, and resistance challenges associated with the use of PARPis in cancer therapy. By synthesizing the current research across different cancer types, this study highlights the unique therapeutic potential of PARPis in precision oncology. Additionally, it offers new insights by exploring strategies to overcome resistance, particularly through combination therapies, thus contributing to a deeper understanding of how to optimize clinical practices and advance future research.

Role of PARPs in DNA Repair

The pivotal role of PARPs in DNA repair is undeniable as they swiftly discern damage and initiate repair mechanisms to safeguard genomic integrity. Upon the occurrence of a DNA SSB, the DNA-binding domain of PARP binds to the affected site, leveraging its catalytic domain and utilizing nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD)+ as a substrate to synthesize poly(ADP-ribose) (PAR) chains. These PAR chains subsequently recruit vital repair proteins, such as X-ray repair cross-complementing protein 1 (XRCC1), DNA polymerase β (polβ), and DNA ligase III, to the damaged area. Collaboratively, these proteins orchestrate the eradication and restoration of damage via the BER pathway, thereby preserving the integrity and stability of the DNA. Once the SSB has been repaired, PAR glycohydrolase degrades the PAR chains, prompting the dissociation of PARP and the repair complex from the DNA and rendering them receptive to impending repair endeavors.

Although PARP primarily plays roles on SSB repair, it also provides auxiliary support for the repair of double-strand breaks (DSBs). Six primary DNA damage repair pathways have been identified, and each pathway participates in repairing DSBs and SSBs caused by different mechanisms of injury. HRR and nonhomologous end-joining (NHEJ) are the two major pathways responsible for DSB repair. HRR becomes active during the S/G2 phase owing to the availability of a sister chromatid, whereas NHEJ functions throughout all phases of the cell cycle, except the M phase, with primary activity in the G1 phase. NHEJ is generally faster but less accurate than HRR. In addition to known proteins, such as Ku70/80, DNA-dependent protein kinase catalytic subunit (DNA-PKcs), Artemis, DNA pol λ/μ, DNA ligase IV, X-ray repair cross-complementing protein 4 (XRCC4), and XRCC4-like factor (XLF), new proteins have been identified in the NHEJ pathway, such as PAralog of XRCC4 and XLF (PAXX), MRI/CYREN, TARDBP (TDP-43), IFFO1, ERCC6L2, and RNase H2. Notably, MRI/CYREN exhibits a dual role, promoting NHEJ during the G1 phase and inhibiting it during the S and G2 phases. This pivotal step necessitates the adept execution of precise single-strand repair mechanisms. At this juncture, PARP safeguards the integrity of the DNA template by remediating SSBs, thereby facilitating the seamless progression of HRR. Conversely, PARPis impede the functionality of PARP, resulting in the accumulation of SSBs that transform into DSBs. Cancer cells harboring BRCA1/2 mutations are incapable of restoring these DSBs, ultimately resulting in cell death. This mechanism selectively eliminates cancerous cells while minimally affecting healthy cells, underscoring its substantial therapeutic potential.

Furthermore, PARP play a pivotal role in addressing replication stress by safeguarding the stability and orderly progression of replication forks. PARPs function in conjunction with repair proteins, such as ataxia-telangiectasia mutated (ATM), ataxia telangiectasia-mutated and Rad3-related (ATR), and checkpoint kinase 1 (CHK1), to control cell cycle checkpoints, ensuring that DNA damage is repaired before cell division to protect genomic stability.

Mechanism of PARPis in Anticancer Treatment

The emergence of PARPis as a class of anticancer drugs has been groundbreaking because they disrupt the DNA repair mechanisms of cancerous cells by inhibiting PARP enzyme activity. This strategy is highly effective, especially in patients with cancer with BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations, thereby providing novel avenues for cancer treatment.

DNA repair inhibition

PARP1 is a pivotal enzyme essential for DNA repair. In normal physiological conditions, upon DNA damage, PARP1 swiftly attaches to the affected sites and harnesses NAD+ as a substrate to catalyze the poly(ADP-ribosyl)ation (PARylation) reaction, which is fundamental for DNA repair. Intriguing findings from single-molecule assays have revealed that, in the presence of inhibitors, the capacity of PARP1 to bind to DNA damage sites is markedly diminished. This observation highlights the efficacy of these inhibitors in hindering NAD+ binding, thereby inhibiting PARP1 activity. By thwarting NAD+ binding, PARPis effectively impede the PARylation function of PARP1, ultimately impeding DNA repair and promoting the accumulation of DNA damage, leading to elevated rates of cell death.

These inhibitors commonly incorporate pharmacophores, such as nicotinamide or benzamide, which competitively engage with the catalytic site of PARP1, thereby preventing NAD+ binding. The latest generation of inhibitors has evolved diverse scaffold structures, including benzo [thiazole] and tetrahydroquinazoline (e.g., olaparib and talazoparib), benzo [imidazole] and indole [carboxamide] (e.g., veliparib and niraparib), and tricyclic indole [lactone] (e.g., rucaparib). These structures form robust interactions with the active site of PARP1, augmenting their selectivity and potency. Furthermore, the inclusion of halogen, sulfonamide, and amine groups fosters additional interactions, leading to an even greater refinement in selectivity and potency.

PARPis induce cancer cell death through “PARP trapping.” Typically, PARP1 binds to damaged DNA and modifies itself (PARylation), which helps it detach itself from the DNA. Some PARPis prevent this detachment, trapping PARP1 on the DNA and blocking repair. This phenomenon traps PARP1 at the damage sites, disrupting normal DNA repair processes and exacerbating DNA damage. Accumulated SSBs may transform into DSBs during DNA replication, especially in cells with defective HRR (such as BRCA-mutated cells), in which DSBs cannot be repaired. Different PARPis exhibit varying degrees of PARP1-trapping ability. Studies have revealed that talazoparib demonstrates the strongest trapping ability, whereas olaparib and veliparib exhibit weaker trapping abilities. Notably, veliparib, despite having a similar affinity for PARP1, possesses a higher dissociation rate constant, resulting in inferior trapping efficiency. Synthetic lethality

Synthetic lethality refers to a genetic interaction in which the combination of defects in two genes leads to cell death, whereas a single gene defect does not affect cell survival, as illustrated in Figure 2. Taking advantage of this effect, PARPis have emerged as a significant strategy for cancer treatment, particularly for cancers harboring BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations.

HRR is the primary mechanism for repairing DNA DSBs, with the BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes playing pivotal roles. In normal cells, DSBs can be repaired through HRR. However, cancer cells with BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations lack effective HRR mechanisms, which renders them unable to repair DSBs.

In addition to BRCA1 and BRCA2, other genes that cause homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) include ATM,CHEK2,PALB2, BARD1,BRIP1,RAD51C, and RAD51D. These genes are closely involved in DDR, and mutations in these genes are associated with a significantly increased risk of breast and ovarian cancers. Multiple studies have demonstrated that the loss of these genes enhances the sensitivity of cells to PARPis. For example, ATM encodes a serine/threonine kinase that plays a critical role in DDR.CHEK2 is a key mediator of DDR and is involved in DSB-induced apoptosis and cell cycle arrest.ATM activates and recruits repair proteins, such as C-terminal binding protein interacting protein (CtIP) and BRCA1, initiating DNA end resection and generating single-stranded DNAs (ssDNAs), which is a crucial step in HRR. Individuals with ATM or CHEK2 mutations have a significantly increased risk of developing breast cancer. Clinical trials have shown that patients with ATM or CHEK2 mutations exhibit good efficacy when treated with olaparib.

Replication stress

Replication stress encompasses the slowing or stalling of DNA replication processes owing to various factors, ultimately contributing to genomic instability, which is the primary driver of cancer development. Safeguarding the replication forks is important for maintaining genomic stability and cellular survival. During DNA replication, forks may encounter obstacles that lead to stalling, which exposes and renders nascent DNA strands vulnerable to degradation. PARPs play a pivotal role not only in DNA repair but also in safeguarding replication forks. Specifically, PARPs mark reversed replication forks, thereby inhibiting RECQ-mediated fork regression and protecting nascent DNA from MRE11- and DNA2-mediated degradation. Stalled and reversed forks are highly susceptible to nucleolytic degradation; however, PARPs prevent this degradation through their marking function, ensuring the preservation of newly synthesized DNA strands. BRCA1 and BRCA2 play central roles in this process. They are indispensable for HRR and protect nascent DNA at stalled and reversed forks, thereby preventing genomic instability. Collectively, PARPs and their associated proteins promote genomic integrity, mitigate mutations and genomic rearrangements, and sustain normal cellular functions and survival. PARPis can induce cancer cell death by disrupting the replication fork protection function of PARP. In BRCA1/2-mutated cancer cells, PARPis not only impede PARP-mediated DNA repair but also exacerbate nascent DNA degradation at forks. This dual assault renders these cells incapable of repairing replication fork damage, ultimately leading to cell death.

The expression of specific genes, such as elevated Mycn proto-oncogene, bHLH transcription factor (MYCN) gene expression in neuroblastoma, triggers replication stress, leading to replication fork stalling and subsequent DNA damage. PARPis augment replication stress and fork stalling, causing extensive DNA damage beyond the repair capacity of a cell, resulting in significant cytotoxic effects, even without HRR deficiency. Consequently, even in scenarios without HRD, PARPis can exert substantial cytotoxic effects on cancer cells by intensifying replication stress.

Research has established that the proteins Schlafen 11 (SLFN11) and MRE11 contribute to enhancing the sensitivity of cancer cells to PARPis by fostering the creation and accumulation of ssDNA gaps. In the presence of PARPis, SLFN11 selectively binds to these ssDNA gaps located behind replication forks without impeding the replication process, thereby exacerbating their accumulation. MRE11, a component of the MRE11-RAD50-NBS1 (MRN) complex, facilitates the generation of ssDNA gaps through the resection of DNA ends and subsequently recruits SLFN11. This concerted action of SLFN11 and MRE11 markedly amplifies the therapeutic efficacy of PARPis, underscoring its potential in cancer treatment.

POLE3 and POLE4 are auxiliary subunits of DNA polymerase ε (POL ε). Their absence leads to a reduction in replication speed and the accumulation of ssDNA gaps behind replication forks, ultimately triggering robust replication stress responses. In cells deficient in POLE3 or POLE4, treatment with PARPis accelerates the replication fork speed, resulting in an increased number of ssDNA gaps that cannot be promptly repaired. This, in turn, induces DNA damage and repairs defects. These findings indicate that the loss of POLE3 or POLE4, independent of HRR deficiency, can induce replication stress, rendering PARPis highly effective at eliminating cancer cells.

Clinical Applications of PARPis

In recent years, remarkable progress has been achieved in the clinical applications of PARPis, showing promising efficacy in the treatment of various cancer types, including ovarian, breast, prostate, and pancreatic cancers. Several PARPis, notably olaparib, niraparib, and rucaparib, have garnered FDA approval for use as standalone therapies or in combination with chemotherapy regimens, highlighting their significant potential in cancer management.

Olaparib (Lynparza) has been approved by the FDA for the treatment of various cancers across different timelines. Initially approved in December 2014 for patients with BRCA1/2-mutated ovarian cancer who had received three or more lines of chemotherapy, olaparib was subsequently approved in January 2018 for the treatment of patients with metastatic breast cancer with germline BRCA mutations who had previously undergone chemotherapy. In December 2019, olaparib was approved for the treatment of BRCA-mutated metastatic pancreatic cancer. In May 2020, it was approved for the treatment of patients with metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer harboring BRCA1/2 or other HRR gene mutations. Niraparib, a PARPi, was approved by the FDA in 2017 for the treatment of recurrent ovarian cancer, particularly in patients sensitive to platinum-based chemotherapy. This was the first PARPi that does not require BRCA mutation screening. Rucaparib was approved by the FDA in May 2020 for the treatment of patients with metastatic prostate cancer harboring BRCA mutations (germline and somatic) who received androgen receptor–targeted therapy and taxane-based chemotherapy. Talazoparib, a highly potent PARPi, was approved by the FDA in 2018 for the treatment of patients with breast cancer harboring BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations. Veliparib is currently undergoing clinical trials for the treatment of ovarian cancer, breast cancer, and other solid tumors but has not yet received FDA approval. Iniparib was initially used to treat triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC); however, subsequent studies have shown limited clinical efficacy. Nowadays, it is rarely used in clinical practice and has not been approved by the FDA. Moreover, preclinical and clinical studies have highlighted the potential applications of PARPis in the treatment of additional cancer types, such as gastric and lung cancers. Research has revealed that, in patients with gastric cancer, tumor cells frequently harbor mutations in genes involved in homologous recombination (HR) DNA repair, the WNT signaling pathway, and the PI3K-ERBB signaling pathway. These gene mutations are closely linked to HRD in tumor cells. A clinical trial demonstrated that the combination of a PARPi and paclitaxel significantly enhanced the survival rates of patients. Although precision screening has improved the overall survival of patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), gains in progression-free survival (PFS) remain limited. However, small cell lung cancer (SCLC) has shown promising outcomes when a PARPi is used in combination with immunotherapy, radiotherapy, or WEE1 inhibitors. SLFN11 has emerged as a critical biomarker for guiding precision medicine in the treatment of SCLC. In hepatocellular carcinoma, PARPis, such as veliparib and olaparib, show enhanced radiotherapy efficacy by disrupting DNA repair pathways and overcoming resistance to sorafenib. Additionally, targeting AXL and DDX11 has further improved treatment sensitivity, even in patients without BRCA mutations. Research on colorectal cancer has shown that PARPis induce synthetic lethality through defects in ATM and XRCC2, demonstrating good tolerability and partial efficacy in refractory cases. In melanoma, patients with BRCA1/2 mutations benefit from PARPis, and combining these with BRAF/MEK inhibitors delays the development of resistance. Additionally, high PARP1 expression has been associated with a better treatment response in patients with melanoma. In endometrial cancer, olaparib has achieved tumor shrinkage and disease stabilization in patients with BRCA2 mutations, and the addition of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and PI3K inhibitors has further enhanced therapeutic outcomes. In summary, precise patient screening, biomarker identification, and optimized combination therapies are essential for maximizing the therapeutic potential of PARPis. A key strategy is to target DNA repair defects and leverage multimodal therapies to improve the efficacy and overcome resistance across various cancer types.

Adverse Effects

PARPis can induce a spectrum of adverse effects during treatment that have been extensively reported in various clinical trials, as summarized in Table 1. Common adverse effects include anemia, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, and neutropenia. Although most of these effects are of low to moderate severity, some patients experience severe grade 3 or higher reactions. For example, among patients treated with olaparib, 40% developed anemia, 16.1% experienced grade 3 anemia, 27.3% developed neutropenia, and 9.3% experienced grade 3 neutropenia. Similarly, PARPis, such as talazoparib, cause high-grade adverse events, such as anemia, decreased appetite, and asthenia. Among patients treated with talazoparib, 68.4% developed anemia, 47.4% experienced grade 3 anemia, 63.2% developed neutropenia, and 21.1% experienced grade 3 neutropenia.

Although combination therapies may result in a higher incidence of adverse events, studies have indicated that they significantly improve radiographic PFS compared with monotherapy, potentially offsetting the risk of treatment interruption due to adverse events. Some adverse reactions necessitate treatment interruptions or dose adjustments; for instance, 25.4% of patients required dose reductions and 35.1% needed to interrupt or delay treatment. Furthermore, some trials have observed a higher rate of severe adverse events, such as myelodysplastic syndrome and acute myeloid leukemia. Among patients treated with olaparib and gemcitabine, 40% experienced grade 3 or higher adverse events.

Resistance Mechanisms to PARPis

Drug efflux

ABCB1 (MDR1) encodes P-glycoprotein (ABCB1), an ATP-dependent efflux pump that actively transports drugs out of cells, thereby reducing intracellular drug concentrations. Rucaparib is a substrate for ABC efflux transporters Abcg2 and Abcb1a/1b, which influence the disposition of rucaparib in mice. The use of ABCB1/ABCG2 inhibitors, such as verapamil and elacridar, can reverse this phenomenon.BRCA1-deficient mammary tumors in mice may acquire resistance to olaparib by activating the P-glycoprotein drug efflux transporter driven by the upregulation of the Abcb1a/b gene. In paclitaxel-resistant ovarian cancer cells, ABCB1 expression is significantly increased, associated with gene amplification. These resistant cells exhibit marked resistance to olaparib and rucaparib but not to other PARPis, such as veliparib and AZD2461 (a structural variant of olaparib), as these drugs are not effective substrates of P-glycoprotein. Therefore, when selecting a PARPi in clinical settings, the expression level of ABCB1 and its impact on drug efflux should be considered.

PARP1 mutations

There is no doubt that mutations in PARP1 can lead to PARPi resistance, considering that PARP1 plays a dominant role in DNA damage repair. Mutations that result in the ablation of PARP1 expression impair its trapping ability, preventing its binding to chromatin and causing resistance to PARPis. Zinc finger (Znf) domains are protein structural motifs coordinated by zinc ions and amino acid residues. PARP1 contains multiple Znf domains that are crucial for DNA damage recognition and repair. PARP1 mutations typically affect these Znf domains, hindering the proper recognition and binding of DNA, thus compromising DNA repair and leading to PARPi resistance.

Mutations in PARP1 and BRCA1 are synthetically lethal. Nonetheless, in cells harboring partially functional BRCA1 mutations, such as SUM149 (a human TNBC cell line), PARP1 mutations can confer resistance to PARPis. Experimental evidence demonstrates that, although SUM149 cells lack full-length BRCA1 protein expression due to mutations, they retain partially functional BRCA1 variants. These variants enable the cells to tolerate PARP1 mutations, thereby conferring resistance to PARPis. This underscores the fact that PARP1 mutations can induce PARPi resistance in cells with residual BRCA1 activity.

Restoration of homologous recombination

HRD represents a critical vulnerability that renders tumor cells sensitive to PARPis. Conversely, the restoration of HR is recognized as a primary factor in the emergence of resistance to PARPis in tumor cells. This restoration of HR can be accomplished through intronic deletions within the BRCA2 gene, which repair the BRCA2 mutation and restore the open reading frame (ORF), ultimately leading to the production of novel BRCA2 isoforms. These new isoforms form RAD51 nuclear foci and effectively repair DNA damage, thereby reinstating HR functionality. When HR is restored, cells can effectively repair DNA damage and maintain genomic stability, ultimately resulting in resistance to PARPis.

Secondary mutations that restore the ORFs of HR genes, including BRCA1, RAD51C, and RAD51D, have been identified in PARP-resistant tumors. This restoration of HR functionality enables cancer cells to repair the DNA damage induced by PARPis, resulting in acquired resistance to these inhibitors.

Methylation of the BRCA1 promoter leads to gene silencing and reduced BRCA1 protein expression, ultimately causing defects in HR repair and sensitivity to PARPis. However, under therapeutic pressure, the methylation status of the BRCA1 promoter may transition from fully methylated to partially or fully demethylated, leading to the re-expression of BRCA1, restoration of HR repair function, and, consequently, resistance to PARPis.

In BRCA1-deficient cells, p53 binding protein 1 (53BP1) inhibits HR repair by preventing DNA end-resection. The deletion of 53BP1 promotes ATM-dependent DNA end processing, generating ssDNAs suitable for HR repair. This mechanism has been validated using RAD51 focus formation, sister chromatid exchange, and recombination reporter assays. Therefore, 53BP1 deletion enhances HR repair capability, improves genomic stability in BRCA1-deficient cells, and reduces their sensitivity to PARPis, ultimately leading to resistance.

Replication fork protection

Replication fork protection is a key mechanism for maintaining cellular genetic stability, especially in cancer cells that frequently experience replication stress. Replication fork protection reduces fork instability, thereby lowering the risk of DNA damage and cell death. This protection also contributes to the development of PARPi resistance. Several mechanisms explain how replication fork protection leads to PARPi resistance: The loss of Pax2 transactivation domain interacting protein (PTIP) protects nascent DNA strands from extensive degradation by inhibiting the recruitment of MRE11 nuclease at stalled replication forks. This protection mechanism allows BRCA1/2-deficient cells to evade DNA damage, resulting in PARPi resistance. When DNA replication forks are stalled, a complex series of protein response mechanisms are activated to protect and stabilize these fork structures. For example, proteins of the sucrose nonfermenting (SNF2) family, such as SMARCAL1, ZRANB3, and helicase-like transcription factor (HLTF), remodel stalled replication forks through their translocase activity, thereby preventing DNA degradation. These proteins play crucial roles in protecting replication fork protection and maintaining genomic integrity. The Fanconi anemia group D2 protein (FANCD2) protein also plays a critical role in replication fork protection. It inhibits MRE11-mediated fork degradation, thus maintaining fork stability in BRCA1/2 mutant cells. When PARPis trap PARP on the DNA, blocking DNA replication, cells rely on FANCD2 to stabilize the stalled forks and prevent their excessive degradation by nucleases, such as MRE11, DNA2, and MUS81. RADX regulates RAD51 activity, preventing its overactivity and maintaining fork stability, thereby promoting PARPi resistance in BRCA2 mutant cancer cells. Combination Therapies with PARPis

Cytotoxic chemotherapeutic drugs

In the field of cancer treatment, the combination of PARPis with chemotherapeutic drugs has been shown to enhance therapeutic efficacy. Notably, the use of olaparib in conjunction with platinum-based drugs, such as cisplatin or carboplatin, has demonstrated significant efficacy in the treatment of ovarian and breast cancers. This combination strategy increases DNA damage and blocks the repair process, thereby improving treatment response rates and extending PFS. Notably, although olaparib alone is insufficient to cause DNA DSBs, its combination with platinum-based drugs significantly enhances the DNA-damaging effect. Additionally, this treatment combination effectively inhibits cancer cell proliferation by arresting more cells in the G2/M phase.

The combination of olaparib and paclitaxel has also been shown to exert synergistic effects in the treatment of ovarian cancer. In cancer cells, paclitaxel induces cell stress and DNA damage, whereas olaparib inhibits DNA repair. This combination therapy not only improves disease control rates but also reduces tumor marker levels and the likelihood of tumor metastasis after treatment. However, although this treatment combination shows promising efficacy, it also carries the risk of increased adverse effects, necessitating careful dose management.

The combination of PARPis with gemcitabine has also demonstrated synergistic effects in the treatment of TNBC and pancreatic cancer. Gemcitabine is incorporated into DNA strands to block replication, whereas the PARPi blocks subsequent DNA repair, thereby promoting the accumulation of DNA damage. This activates cell cycle checkpoints, preventing cells from entering mitosis and ultimately leading to cell death. Additionally, PARPis reduce the expression of ΔNp63α (p63), releasing the inhibition on p73, enhancing its pro-apoptotic function, and further promoting cell cycle arrest and apoptosis through p21. This combination therapy not only increased apoptosis rates and decreased cell proliferation but also significantly extended the survival time in animal models.

Targeted therapy

PARPis can act synergistically with targeted therapies for cancer treatment. For example, the combination of PARPis with the anti-EGFR monoclonal antibody cetuximab (C225) inhibits cancer cell proliferation and survival as well as enhances the sensitivity of cancer cells to radiation and chemotherapy. C225 blocks EGFR signaling and inhibits HR and NHEJ DNA repair mechanisms. This, combined with the cytotoxic effects of PARPis, significantly increases cancer cell death through intrinsic apoptotic pathways.

Furthermore, the combination of olaparib with the PI3K/mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR) pathway inhibitor dactolisib acts via dual mechanisms. Dactolisib inhibits the PI3K and mTOR pathways and reduces the cellular response to olaparib-induced DNA damage. Simultaneously, dactolisib reduces the phosphorylation and aggregation of 53BP1, enhancing the effects of olaparib and inhibiting tumor growth.

The combination of PARPis with a vascular endothelial growth factor receptor (VEGFR) inhibitors works through two main mechanisms: VEGFR inhibitors block the VEGF pathway, limiting the tumor blood supply, while the PARPi prevents DNA damage repair, leading to DNA damage accumulation and cancer cell death. A recent study suggested that this combination therapy may be effective in a broad range of patients, including those without BRCA mutations.

Another combined strategy, that is, PARPis used with ATR inhibitors, increases the accumulation of DSBs and disrupts RAD51 function at DNA damage sites, thereby impairing the DNA repair capabilities of cancer cells. This combination is particularly effective against BRCA-mutated cancer cells. Inhibiting ATR disrupts not only DNA replication fork stability but also rewired homologous recombination pathways, increasing its sensitivity to DNA damage, particularly in BRCA-deficient cells.

Combining PARPis with histone deacetylase inhibitors enhances tumor immunogenicity and responsiveness to immunotherapy by activating the Cyclic GMP–AMP synthase-stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS-STING) pathway and boosting immune cell activity. This helps reshape the tumor microenvironment and inhibit tumor growth.

Finally, the combination of PARPis and CDK4/6 inhibitors produces a synergistic anticancer effect by increasing DNA damage and blocking cell cycle progression. This combination not only exacerbates DNA damage and genomic instability but also enhances apoptosis by preventing cells from entering the S phase.

Immunotherapy

The combined use of PARPis and ICIs, such as cytotoxic T lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) and programmed death (PD)-1 blockers, has demonstrated synergistic effects in cancer treatment. CTLA-4 blockade can activate T cells and promote the production of interferon gamma, whereas PARPis increase the immunogenicity of BRCA1-deficient cancer cells by interfering with their DNA repair mechanisms. This combined approach not only produces significant cytotoxicity within the tumor microenvironment but also induces durable immune memory, providing robust clinical benefits for patients with BRCA1-deficient ovarian cancer.

PD-1 inhibitors function by blocking the interaction between PD-1 and PD-L1, restoring T-cell function and enhancing the ability of the immune system to attack tumors. This synergistic effect is further enhanced when used in combination with a PARPi. DNA damage induced by PARPis activates the STING pathway, which increases the production of type I interferons and strengthens the immune response as well as elevates PD-L1 expression on the surface of tumor cells, making them more susceptible to PD-1 inhibitors. Clinical models have also demonstrated that PARP inhibition inactivates Glycogen synthase kinase 3 (GSK3) and upregulates PD-L1 in a dose-dependent manner. Consequently, T-cell activation is suppressed, resulting in enhanced apoptosis in cancer cells. Single-cell spatial analysis showed that the combination therapy enhanced the interaction between exhausted CD8+ T cells and PD-L1-positive cells within the tumor, confirming the synergistic mechanism of the combined treatment.

Challenges in the Development of PARPis

Significant challenges are encountered in the development of PARPis, including tumor resistance through restored HRR, side effects, such as nausea and anemia, and difficulty in identifying reliable biomarkers. Although BRCA mutations are key indicators, not all patients with these mutations respond to PARPis, while some without them do. Combining PARPis with other treatments can increase toxicity and complicate regimens. Tumor heterogeneity further complicates treatment.

Future Directions

1. Addressing resistance through combination therapies: Combining PARPis with other drugs targeting different pathways to overcome resistance.

2. Advancing personalized treatments: Leveraging genomic profiling to tailor treatments, enhancing efficacy, and reducing adverse effects.

3. Utilizing proteomic analysis: Analyzing ovarian cancer proteomics and adaptive therapy responses to identify new therapeutic options, reducing PARPi resistance, and improving outcomes.

4. Developing next-generation PARPis: Focusing on creating PARPis with higher efficacy and lower toxicity.

5. Exploring effective combination therapies: Identifying optimal drug combinations to improve treatment outcomes.

6. Expanding PARPi applications: Extending the use of PARPis to non-BRCA cancers, additional cancer types, and earlier disease stages.

7. Improving clinical trial designs: Incorporating adaptive elements and real-time biomarker monitoring into trials to better evaluate efficacy and identify responsive patients.

Conclusion

PARPis represent a significant advancement in cancer therapy, particularly for BRCA-mutated cancers. They induce cancer cell death by disrupting DNA repair mechanisms. Although effective, challenges, such as resistance and side effects remain. Future research should focus on overcoming these challenges, developing better PARPis, and combining them with other therapies to improve cancer treatments.
