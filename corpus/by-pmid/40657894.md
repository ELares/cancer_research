# Sacituzumab Govitecan Population Pharmacokinetics: Updated Analyses Using HR+/HER2- Metastatic Breast Cancer Data From the Phase 3 TROPiCS-02 Trial.

## Abstract

Sacituzumab govitecan (SG) is an antibody-drug conjugate composed of a Trop-2-directed antibody coupled to SN-38. SG is approved in multiple countries for pretreated metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (mTNBC) and hormone receptor-positive/human epidermal growth factor receptor 2-negative (HR+/HER2-) mBC. Three previously developed population pharmacokinetic (PopPK) models for SG, free SN-38, and total antibody (tAB) in patients with mTNBC or other solid tumors were externally validated using data from 260 patients with HR+/HER2- mBC from TROPiCS-02 (NCT03901339). Pharmacokinetic parameters were re-estimated using data from 789 patients with HR+/HER2- mBC, mTNBC, or other solid tumors from three studies-TROPiCS-02, ASCENT (NCT02574455), and IMMU-132-01 (NCT01631552). Previously developed PopPK models adequately described the data from TROPiCS-02. Typical parameter estimates based on combined dataset for clearance and steady-state volume of distribution were 0.128 L/h and 3.58 L for SG and 0.0155 L/h and 4.29 L for tAB, respectively. The pharmacokinetics of the three analytes (SG, free SN-38, and tAB) in participants with HR+/HER2- mBC were consistent with those observed in mTNBC and other tumor types. The analyses confirmed mild-to-moderate renal impairment, mild hepatic impairment, age, tumor type (based on limited data in non-breast cancer tumor types), baseline albumin level, UGT1A1 genotype, or Trop-2 expression did not have a clinically relevant impact on the exposure of the three analytes across populations. These findings support that the SG dosing regimen of 10 mg/kg on Days 1 and 8 of 21-day cycles is adequate for patients with HR+/HER2- mBC.

Introduction

Breast cancer is a heterogenous disease, characterized by molecular subtyping and clinical and pathological features. The most common breast cancer subtype is hormone receptor‐positive (HR+)/human epidermal growth factor receptor 2‐negative (HER2−), representing approximately 65% of all new breast cancer cases . Patients who present with early‐stage HR+/HER2− breast cancer have a favorable prognosis versus early‐stage triple‐negative breast cancer (TNBC). However, in advanced disease, HR+/HER2− has the second worst prognosis after metastatic TNBC (mTNBC) with a 5‐year survival rate of ~35%.

Guidelines for first‐line treatment of HR+/HER2– mBC in postmenopausal or premenopausal patients receiving ovarian ablation or suppression consist of endocrine therapy in combination with cyclin‐dependent kinase 4/6 inhibitors (CDK4/6i). Subsequent treatment includes additional lines of endocrine therapy, alone or combined with other targeted agents including mammalian target of rapamycin inhibitors and phosphatidylinositol‐3‐kinase inhibitors, or poly (ADP‐ribose) polymerase inhibitors for patients with breast cancer gene 1 or 2 (BRCA1/2) mutations. Following the development of endocrine resistance, remaining treatment options include sequential single‐agent chemotherapy, which is associated with low response rates, poor quality of life, and substantial toxicity. Despite advances in treatment for patients with endocrine‐resistant disease, including the introduction of trastuzumab deruxtecan in previously treated HR+/HER2−low mBC, there remains a high unmet need for novel effective treatments for HR+/HER2– mBC.

Sacituzumab govitecan (SG) is an antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) composed of a trophoblast cell‐surface antigen‐2– (Trop‐2) directed monoclonal antibody coupled to SN‐38, the active metabolite of the topoisomerase inhibitor irinotecan, via a hydrolysable linker. The linker facilitates the release of the payload into Trop‐2 expressing cancer cells and subsequently to neighboring cancer cells, which also allows for localized bystander killing of neighboring Trop‐2 low/negative cells.

SG is approved in multiple countries for patients with pretreated mTNBC and for patients with HR+/HER2– mBC who have received endocrine therapy and at least two systemic therapies in the metastatic setting. Safety and efficacy of SG in patients with endocrine‐resistant and chemotherapy‐resistant HR+/HER2– mBC were evaluated in the phase III TROPiCS‐02 study (NCT03901339). Compared with single‐agent chemotherapy, SG demonstrated statistically significant improvement in progression‐free survival (PFS; median 5.5 months [95% confidence interval (CI) 4.2–7.0 months] vs. 4.0 months [95% CI 3.1–4.4 months], hazard ratio [HR] 0.66, 95% CI 0.53–0.83, p = 0.0003) and in overall survival (OS; median 14.4 months [95% CI 13–15.7 months] vs. 11.2 months [10.1–12.7], HR 0.79, 95% CI 0.65–0.96, p = 0.020).

Serum samples were collected from all patients from TROPiCS‐02 for pharmacokinetic assessment of SG and its components. Previously, we described the population pharmacokinetics (PopPK) and exposure‐response analyses of SG in patients with mTNBC. Separate PopPK models were developed for the three analytes, SG, free SN‐38, and total antibody (tAB, hRS7 unconjugated or conjugated with SN‐38), utilizing data from patients with mTNBC and other solid tumors enrolled in two clinical studies: IMMU‐132‐01 (phase I/II basket study in patients with solid tumors; NCT01631552) and ASCENT (phase III in patients with mTNBC; NCT02574455). In the present analysis, we extend this work to patients with HR+/HER2– mBC enrolled in TROPiCS‐02 with an analysis of the combined dataset from the three studies.

Methods

Clinical Study Design and Pharmacokinetic Assessment

The study designs for IMMU‐132‐01, ASCENT, and TROPiCS‐02 have been described previously. In each study, SG was administered intravenously on Days 1 and 8 of a 21‐day cycle. Concentration‐time data from patients enrolled in IMMU‐132‐01, ASCENT, and TROPiCS‐02 who received at least one dose of SG and had at least one measurable concentration of SG or any other analyte were used. Table shows the serum sampling schemes for pharmacokinetic and anti‐drug antibodies (ADA) assessment by study.

Bioanalytical Method

Detailed bioanalytical methods have been described previously. Briefly, validated liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry methods were developed and utilized to measure the concentrations of total SN‐38 (lower limit of quantitation [LLOQ] 5 ng/mL and upper limit of quantitation [ULOQ] 2500 ng/mL) and free SN‐38 (LLOQ 1 ng/mL and ULOQ 500 ng/mL). Concentrations of tAB were quantified using a validated sandwich electrochemiluminescence immunoassay (LLOQ 374 ng/mL and ULOQ 3049 ng/mL). Concentrations of SG were calculated assuming a drug‐to‐antibody ratio (DAR) of 8. Serum ADA and neutralizing ADAs were detected using separate validated electrochemiluminescence immunoassays as described previously.

Starting Models

PopPK models were previously developed for SG, free SN‐38, and tAB using data from IMMU‐132‐01 and ASCENT. Briefly, SG and tAB models were independent whereas the free SN‐38 model depended on the SG model such that free SN‐38 formation was driven by the total amount of SG following first‐order kinetics based on SG pharmacokinetic parameters estimated with the SG model. The M6 method was used to handle samples with concentrations below the limit of quantitation (BLQ) for SG and tAB whereas the M3 method was used for free SN‐38 (Supporting Information). Pharmacokinetics of the three analytes were described by two‐compartment models and included allometric scaling exponents for clearance and volume. These models included body weight as a covariate on SG and tAB clearance (CL), apparent CL of free SN‐38, SG and tAB volume of distribution (Vd) and free SN‐38 apparent Vd; serum albumin as a covariate on CL for SG and tAB; tumor type and time on tAB CL; sex on tAB Vd, study on free SN‐38 and tAB residual unexplained variability (RUV); and time after last dose on SG and free SN‐38 RUV.

External Validation and Model Re‐Estimation

A two‐step process was employed to evaluate the appropriateness of the previously developed models for describing the new data from TROPiCS‐02. First, the previously developed models were used to describe the concentration‐time data from TROPiCS‐02, estimating only random effects (MAXEVAL = 0). Graphical and simulation‐based evaluations were employed to assess the goodness‐of‐fit (GOF). In the second step, the previously developed models were re‐fitted, and parameters were re‐estimated using available data from all three studies.

Additional information regarding covariate analyses, model evaluation, model predictions, ethics approval, and data and code availability are included in the Supporting Information.

Results

Patient Demographics and Baseline Characteristics

The combined dataset from three studies included 789 patients treated with SG: 276 from IMMU‐132‐01 (mTNBC, n = 24; metastatic urothelial cancer [mUC], n = 36; HR+/HER2– mBC, n = 32), 253 from ASCENT (all mTNBC), and 260 from TROPiCS‐02 (all HR+/HER2– mBC). Table 1 shows patient demographics and baseline characteristics including body weight, sex, age, race, prior treatment, number of prior lines of chemotherapy, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status, tumor type, uridine diphosphate glucuronosyltransferase 1A1 (UGT1A1) genotype, biomarkers for hepatic function and renal function, and Trop‐2 expression. The majority of patients were female (85%), white (79%) and had a median age of 58 years (range, 27–88) and a median body weight of 69 kg (range, 31–140). Trop‐2 expression data were available as weak, moderate, or strong staining by immunohistochemistry for study IMMU‐132‐01, whereas for the ASCENT and TROPiCS‐02 studies, Trop‐2 expression was characterized as a numerical histochemical score (range, 0–300).

External Validation and Final PopPK Model for SG 

The previously developed PopPK model of SG based on IMMU‐132‐01 and ASCENT data showed that SG pharmacokinetics were best described by a two‐compartment model with first‐order linear elimination. The model also included estimated body weight‐based allometric scaling exponents on clearance (CLSG), intercompartmental clearance (QSG), central volume of distribution (V1SG), and peripheral volume of distribution (V2SG), and inter‐individual variability (IIV) on CLSG. An additive residual error model was utilized for SG. This model was able to well‐describe the SG concentration‐time data from TROPiCS‐02 as shown in the prediction‐corrected visual predictive check (pcVPC) for external validation (Figure S1a). Subsequently, the previously developed model parameters were re‐estimated using 6214 SG concentrations from a combined dataset across the three studies. Following re‐estimation, backward elimination for covariate assessment confirmed that all covariates in the previous SG model remained statistically significant (α = 0.001). As such, the structure of the final PopPK model of SG was unchanged compared with the previous analysis. Table 2 shows that the final parameter estimates and relative standard errors (%RSE) of the final model of SG after the addition of TROPiCS‐02 data were in good agreement with estimates from the prior analysis. The updated typical parameter estimates for CLSG, V1SG, and V2SG were 0.128 L/h, 2.65 L, and 0.929 L, respectively. Final SG model parameters were estimated with good precision with RSE < 15% and had acceptable shrinkage for ETA on CLSG and residual error (26% and 3.6%, respectively). Parameter estimates and 95% CI based on the final SG model and bootstrap analysis also showed good concordance (Table S2).

Body weight effects on the two CL and two volume parameters were modeled as a power function, estimating a 5.1% increase in CLSG and QSG and a 5.3% increase in V1SG and V2SG with a 10% increase in body weight. Similarly, the effect of baseline albumin was modeled using a power function estimating a 3.7% decrease in CLSG with a 10% increase in albumin level. GOF plots for the final updated SG model (Figure S2a) showed that individual and population predictions showed good correlation with the observed data, and weighted residuals were evenly distributed around 0 and showed no trends over time or by concentration, indicating no meaningful bias in the model. The pcVPC plot (Figure 1a) for the final updated SG model showed good concordance between the observed and simulated percentiles.

External Validation and Final PopPK Model for Free SN‐38

The previously developed PopPK model of free SN‐38 was a sequential model where free SN‐38 was generated via a first‐order release process from SG driven by individual model‐predicted SG concentrations. The pharmacokinetics of free SN‐38 were adequately described by a two‐compartment model and included body weight–based allometric scaling exponents for apparent clearance (CLSN38/F) and intercompartmental clearance (QSN38/F), and apparent central and peripheral volumes of distribution (V1SN38/F and V2SN38/F, respectively). V1SN38/F and V2SN38/F were fixed to the values reported in the literature (49 L and 2177 L, respectively) to ensure identifiability of the model. An additive residual error model was utilized for free SN‐38. The pcVPC for external validation (Figure S1b) shows that the previous model of free SN‐38 was able to describe the free SN‐38 concentration‐time data from TROPiCS‐02 reasonably well. Subsequently, the model parameters for free SN‐38 were re‐estimated using a combined dataset with 6118 concentrations from the three studies. In the backward elimination step for covariate assessment, all the previously included covariates were found to be statistically significant (α = 0.001) and hence the structure of the final PopPK model for free SN‐38 was unchanged. Table 3 shows that the estimates for the final free SN‐38 model parameters and their %RSE after the addition of TROPiCS‐02 data were closely aligned with estimates from the previous analysis. All parameters for the final free SN‐38 model were estimated with good precision with %RSE < 25%. Table S3 shows the parameter estimates and 95% CI based on the final free SN‐38 model and bootstrap analysis. Precision of the final model parameter estimates was assessed utilizing a stratified bootstrap analysis by study as a reflection of the study effect included in the model. Of the 250 bootstrap replicates, 153 (61.2%) converged successfully; however, most of the replicates that did not converge successfully terminated due to rounding errors, mainly due to the study effect. Therefore, the bootstrap results were summarized based on both only successful bootstrap runs and all bootstrap runs. Overall, parameter estimates showed good consistency between the final model and the bootstrap runs.

Given that the likelihood‐based estimation method (M3) was used to account for BLQ samples in the free SN‐38 model, diagnostic plots based on normalized prediction distribution error (NPDE) were considered rather than conditional weighted residuals for the final free SN‐38 model (Figure S2b). NPDE assessment was done to ensure a lack of bias in the distribution of both the measured (concentrations above LLOQ) and estimated BLQ concentrations. Individual and population predictions showed adequate correlation with the observed data. The NPDEs were evenly distributed around 0 and showed no trends over time or by concentration. The pcVPC plot (Figure 1b) showed that the final free SN‐38 model adequately predicted the central tendency while slightly underpredicting the 5th percentile of the observed free SN‐38 concentrations.

External Validation and Final PopPK Model for tAB 

Previously, the pharmacokinetics of tAB were best described by a two‐compartment model with first‐order time‐dependent elimination. Estimated body weight–based allometric scaling for CLtAB and QtAB and for V1tAB and V2tAB were included in this model. IIV on CLtAB and V1tAB was estimated, and a combined additive and proportional residual error model was utilized. Also, the correlation of IIV on CLtAB and V1tAB as a covariance term was included in the model. An evaluation of tAB pharmacokinetic data from TROPiCS‐02 based on the previously developed model is shown in the pcVPC for external validation (Figure S1c). Subsequently, the previously developed model was re‐estimated based on the combined dataset containing 6257 tAB concentrations from the three studies. Backward elimination step confirmed that all previously included covariates were statistically significant at α = 0.001. Table 4 shows that the parameter estimates and their RSE for the final model of tAB after the addition of TROPiCS‐02 data were in good agreement with the estimates from the previous analysis. Typical parameter estimates for CLtAB, V1tAB, and V2tAB were 0.0155 L/h, 2.97 L, and 1.32 L, respectively. The model also included an effect of time on systemic CLtAB as a relative exponential onset model. The maximum reduction of systemic clearance was estimated to be 17% with a time to half‐maximal reduction of approximately 74 days. All parameters of the final tAB model were estimated with good precision. The final model parameters and 95% CI were also closely aligned with the results from bootstrap analysis (Table S4).

Body weight effects on the two CL and two volume parameters were modeled as power functions predicting a 4.1% increase in CLtAB and QtAB and a 4.5% increase in V1tAB and V2tAB with a 10% increase in body weight. The effect of baseline serum albumin was also modeled as a power function predicting a 6.8% decrease in CLtAB with a 10% increase in serum albumin. Other significant covariates included in the final model were a small effect for other cancer types on CLtAB (11% lower CLtAB for patients with other tumor types including small‐cell lung cancer, non‐small‐cell lung cancer, colorectal cancer, esophageal cancer, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, etc. as compared to those with mTNBC, mUC, and HR+/HER2– mBC), sex on V1tAB (15% higher V1tAB in males), and study on RUV (14% lower standard deviation for study IMMU‐132‐01 vs. ASCENT and TROPiCS‐02). The effect of other cancer types on CLtAB should be interpreted with caution given the very limited data in these other tumor types from the phase I study. The GOF plots (Figure S2c) and pcVPC plot (Figure 1c) confirmed that the final tAB PopPK model described the observed concentrations across the three studies adequately well.

Impact of Statistically Significant Covariates on Exposure

As described previously, the impact of statistically significant covariates in the final models on predicted first‐cycle maximum concentration and exposure (C max, AUC) of SG, free SN‐38, and tAB were visualized using tornado plots. As shown in Figure 2, for SG the impact of statistically significant covariates of body weight and albumin resulted in up to 16% lower and 21% higher AUC as compared to a typical patient. Similar results were observed for the impact of body weight on free SN‐38 AUC (Figure S3a,b). For tAB, the 95th percentile of body weight (105 kg) resulted in 26% higher AUC as compared to a typical patient (Figure S3c,d). This is attributed to the effect of body weight on CLtAB and the weight‐based dosing regimen of SG. Other significant covariates included in the final tAB model resulted in predicted relative exposures within 80%–125%.

None of the patients from TROPiCS‐02 had treatment‐emergent ADAs to SG. As such, the combined pharmacokinetic analysis dataset of 789 patients from the three studies included only four patients (all from ASCENT) with treatment‐emergent ADAs. All four patients had low ADA titer levels, and three of these were positive for neutralizing ADAs to SG, as reported previously.

Correlation Between All Tested Covariates and Individual Predicted Exposure

The correlations between individual predicted AUC and C max over the first treatment cycle and all covariates were also evaluated in the covariate assessment and visualized using forest plots for the three analytes. Figure 3 shows the correlation of SG exposure metrics (AUC and C max) with the assessed covariates and found that tumor type, mild or moderate renal impairment, mild hepatic impairment, age, sex, baseline albumin level, race, ECOG status, UGT1A1 genotype, use of UGT1A1 inducers or inhibitors, or Trop‐2 expression did not have clinically relevant correlation. Similar results were observed for free SN‐38 and tAB models (Figures S4 and S5).

Discussion

Three separate PopPK models describing pharmacokinetics of the three analytes SG, free SN‐38, and tAB following SG administration were previously developed using data from two studies (IMMU‐132‐01 and ASCENT). In the current analyses, the dataset was augmented with concentration‐time data from a new study: TROPiCS‐02 (phase III study in patients with HR+/HER2– mBC). The previous models were found to describe the data from TROPiCS‐02 as confirmed by external validation. The pharmacokinetic parameters for all three PopPK models were re‐estimated using the combined dataset from three studies (IMMU‐132‐01, ASCENT, and TROPiCS‐02). The statistical significance of the previously selected covariates in these updated analyses was confirmed by backward elimination.

After the addition of data from TROPiCS‐02, the pharmacokinetics of the three analytes continued to be well‐characterized by two‐compartment models using allometric scaling exponents for CL and volume. Updated final model parameters for SG, free SN‐38, and tAB were estimated with good precision. The pcVPC plots for all three analytes confirmed the adequate description of average and variability of the observed data. The final model parameters and 95% CI were confirmed by bootstrap analysis. Out of the total 250 bootstrap replicates, all 250 replicates for SG and 235 replicates for tAB converged successfully, whereas for free SN‐38, 153 replicates converged successfully. For free SN‐38, replicates were terminated mainly due to rounding errors. However, including or excluding the terminated replicates for free SN‐38 had limited impact, as the parameter estimates and 95% CI based on all replicates (250) and only successful replicates (153) were highly consistent with those of the final free SN‐38 model, thus confirming the robustness of the final model estimates.

As observed with the TNBC analyses, body weight was found to be a statistically significant covariate for SG, free SN‐38, and tAB in these updated analyses. In this population of patients, predominantly with mBC (mTNBC or HR+/HER2− mBC), the effect of body weight on exposure is adequately accounted for by a body weight–based dosing regimen of SG. Given the overall variability of exposure, any residual impact of body weight is considered to be not clinically relevant. Furthermore, any impact of exposure on efficacy or safety is likely attributed to dose interruptions or dose reductions due to adverse events instead of intrinsic exposure differences within the mBC population.

Consistent with previous findings, a slight reduction in CLtAB over time was estimated with a maximum reduction of 17%. The reduction in CLtAB after 6 months on treatment was estimated to be 14%. Such time‐dependent decrease in CL is not considered to be clinically meaningful and is potentially attributed to reduced tumor burden and cachexia with treatment, as observed previously after treatment with immune checkpoint inhibitors in patients with cancer. It is also well documented that serum albumin levels could reflect the IgG catabolic rate that directly impacts the clearance of therapeutic IgG antibodies. The impact of serum albumin is low and is consistent with the value reflected in the literature.

Results from covariate evaluation showed that no dose adjustment is required based on race or age. Trop‐2 expression was evaluated as a continuous and categorical covariate based on available data but did not have a statistically significant impact on clearance. Organ impairment status including mild‐to‐moderate renal impairment or mild hepatic impairment did not have a clinically relevant impact on the exposure of any of the three analytes. The potential impact of severe renal impairment and moderate/severe hepatic impairment could not be evaluated due to data limitations. UGT1A1 genotype was not found to be a statistically significant covariate of either SG or free SN‐38 clearance, and the model‐predicted exposures for patients with UGT1A1 *1/*1, *1/*28, and *28/*28 genotypes were comparable, which is consistent with our prior findings. While the number of participants in the SG arm who received UGT1A1 inhibitors (n = 16) or inducers (n = 5) were small and the impact of these drugs on exposure could not be assessed during covariate evaluation, the model‐predicted exposures for SG or free SN‐38 in patients who received UGT1A1 inhibitors/inducers versus those who did not receive these drugs were comparable, indicating a lack of impact.

Tumor type continued to be a significant covariate for CLtAB, predicting 11% lower CLtAB in patients with “other” cancer types (including small‐cell and non‐small‐cell lung, colorectal, esophageal, endometrial, etc.) as compared to mTNBC, mUC, and HR+/HER2– mBC. Since the difference in estimated CLtAB between the tumor types was small, predicted tAB exposure was comparable across tumor types. Given the limited data on tumor types other than mTNBC or HR+/HER2– mBC, primarily from IMMU‐132‐01, the impact of tumor type on CLtAB should be interpreted with caution. Multiple phase III studies of SG to evaluate its efficacy and safety across different tumor types including SG as first‐line treatment of mTNBC, early‐stage TNBC, and as treatment of HR+/HER2– mBC after endocrine therapy are ongoing. Data from these ongoing studies will further inform the characterization of SG pharmacokinetics in both monotherapy and combination settings across additional tumor types. Exposure‐response analysis in the TNBC population showed that SG‐related serum exposures over the duration of treatment were significantly correlated with the assessed efficacy and safety endpoints. Using data from these ongoing studies, additional analyses including tumor growth inhibition modeling and exposure‐efficacy analyses (including parametric time‐to‐event models) could help predict efficacy outcomes, whereas neutropenia modeling and exposure‐safety analyses could provide useful insights into the observed safety outcomes across tumor types and lines of treatment.

The number of ADA‐positive patients was small as none of the patients from TROPiCS‐02 were ADA positive. Hence, it was decided not to formally quantify the impact of immunogenicity on exposure.

Some of the limitations of these analyses include that SG (ADC) concentrations were calculated using bound SN‐38 concentrations, which were derived based on the measured total and free SN‐38 concentrations in serum as described previously. The calculation of SG concentrations assumed a constant DAR of eight given the limitation of the bioanalytical assays to robustly resolve the decreasing DARs over time. Structural models were not re‐assessed, and only backward elimination was used for covariate assessment as the external validation results showed that the data from patients with HR+/HER2– mBC from TROPiCS‐02 were described adequately well using the previous models and did not identify a need for a full covariate re‐assessment.

Overall, the pharmacokinetics of SG, free SN‐38, and tAB in patients with HR+/HER2– mBC were consistent with those in patients with mTNBC and other tumor types. These analyses confirm that the SG treatment regimen of 10 mg/kg on Days 1 and 8 of a 21‐day cycle is adequate and that no dose adjustments are recommended for special populations such as patients with mild or moderate renal impairment or mild hepatic impairment, or elderly patients, or to account for race or tumor type. These models were used to estimate individual exposure metrics that were correlated with key efficacy and safety outcomes in patients with mBC as a part of exposure‐response analyses that will be presented in forthcoming reports.
