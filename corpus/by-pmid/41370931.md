# Targeting Genome Maintenance Defects of Cancers Using Chain-Terminating Nucleoside Analogs.

## Abstract

Conventional cancer therapies, including radiation therapy and chemotherapy, rely on inflicting DNA damage, yet they inevitably affect normal cells, leading to severe adverse effects. The advent of precision chemotherapy exploiting tumor-specific DNA repair defects has validated the effectiveness of this approach. The first successful example is PARP inhibitors, which selectively kill homologous recombination (HR) defective cancers, such as familial breast cancer possessing HR deficiency due to BRCA gene mutations. However, the broader landscape of DNA maintenance-including DNA replication, repair, and checkpoint pathways-harbors numerous mutations in tumors that remain untargeted. Here, we propose repurposing chain-terminating nucleoside analogs (CTNAs) to target such cancers' vulnerabilities. CTNAs, long utilized as anti-cancers and anti-viral drugs, inhibit replication and thereby suppress growth, but their activity has never been systematically aligned with specific cancer mutations associated with DNA maintenance defects. Based on our recent studies, we demonstrate that CTNAs elicit synthetic lethality in cells deficient for distinct DNA maintenance systems, amplifying replication stress, leading to cell death. We highlight the spectrum of CTNA-induced lesions and repair pathways required for cellular tolerance. This framework presents a versatile "repair-defect-guided" chemotherapy that expands the clinical utility of CTNAs and improves therapeutic effect by reducing side effects.

Targeting Genome Maintenance Defects of Cancers Using Chain‐Terminating Nucleoside Analogs

Introduction

Current cancer therapies, including radiation therapy and conventional chemotherapy, primarily rely on the principle that DNA damage caused by the treatment limits cancer proliferation. This strategy exploits the fact that cancer cells, due to their rapid cell proliferation, generally replicate their genome more frequently than normal healthy cells, making them vulnerable to DNA damage. A hallmark of many cancers is genome instability associated with mutations, which is gained during tumorigenesis. As a result of cancer mutations, genome maintenance systems, including DNA repair, checkpoint, and replication, are frequently compromised in cancer cells. Recent therapeutic advances have begun to exploit these intrinsic weaknesses. The most prominent example of the therapies targeting weakened genome maintenance systems is the use of poly ADP‐ribose polymerase (PARP) inhibitors to selectively target HR‐deficient tumors such as BRCA‐mutated breast cancer. Despite the success of PARP inhibitors, the broader concept of “targeting cancers' weakened genome maintenance system” has yet to be fully explored in cancer chemotherapy. This review aims to explore the possibility of the novel cancer chemotherapy based on the concept of “targeting cancers' weakened genome maintenance system” using nucleoside analogs.

Nucleoside Analogs

Nucleoside analogs are chemical compounds structurally resembling natural nucleosides that compose DNA. They have long been used as anti‐cancer and anti‐viral agents, since the incorporation of these drugs into genomic DNA interferes with DNA replication. This phenomenon effectively restricts the proliferation of cancers and viruses. So far, nucleoside analogs are known to interfere with DNA replication in the following three ways. First, these are incorporated at the growing end of nascent DNA and subsequently interfere with polymerization reactions by acting as chain‐terminators. Thus, nucleoside analogs exerting such effects are referred to as chain‐terminating nucleoside analogs (CTNAs). Second, they can be embedded within DNA templates and subsequently impede replication fork progression. Third, certain analogs inhibit key enzymes in nucleotide biosynthesis, thereby limiting the deoxynucleotide pool, indirectly affecting DNA synthesis.

Emerging evidence suggests that cellular tolerance to nucleoside analogs depends on various genome maintenance systems. For example, factors involved in the checkpoint system, such as Rad17, ATR, and Chk1, are required for the cellular tolerance to gemcitabine. Additionally, the exonuclease activity of polymerase epsilon and tyrosyl‐DNA‐phosphodiesterase 1 (TDP1) has been shown to remove mis‐incorporated nucleoside analogs from the 3′‐end of elongating DNA, thereby mitigating chain‐terminating effects. Despite these insights, the potential for exploiting “synergistic effects” between specific gene mutations (e.g., RAD17 mutation) and nucleoside analogs (e.g., Gemcitabine) remains underexplored. This situation is likely due to a lack of systematic understanding regarding how the effectiveness of each nucleoside analog is affected by defects in genome maintenance systems, including DNA repair, replication, and the DNA damage checkpoint—commonly identified in cancers.

Genome Maintenance Systems

Nucleoside analogs affect DNA replication, implying that the genome surveillance pathway is pivotal for cellular tolerance to these drugs. As described above, nucleoside analogs directly interfere with DNA replication in two major ways. The exonuclease activity of polymerase epsilon and TDP1's nuclease activity  remove incorporated nucleoside analogs from the 3′ end of nascent DNA, thereby mitigating chain‐terminating effects. In addition, CtIP and Mre11, key nucleases in the initial step of HR, also participate in removing CTNAs to mitigate the chain‐terminating effects. Thus, these mechanisms counteract CTNAs' first interference effects. To alleviate replication fork arrest on damaged templates containing nucleoside analogs, cells rely on the DNA damage tolerance (DDT) system, which includes translesion DNA synthesis (TLS), HR‐mediated template switching, and repriming. TLS allows bypass of DNA lesions employing specialized polymerases that enable the insertion of a nucleotide opposite the damaged template strand. This reaction is promoted in two steps: the insertion of a nucleotide opposite the damaged base conducted by Y‐family polymerases, such as Polymerase η (Polη), followed by the extension from the mismatched primer end by Polymerase ζ (Polζ) to complete TLS. HR‐mediated template switching enables replication to bypass lesions by using an undamaged nascent strand from the sister chromatid as an alternative template . In repriming, PrimPol reinitiates replication downstream from the lesion, leaving an unreplicated gap. Thus, these mechanisms counteract the inhibitory effect of nucleoside analogs inserted into the template strand.

In addition to these systems, including nucleases for mitigating the chain‐terminating effect and the DDT systems for resuming arrested forks on damaged templates, the DNA damage checkpoint plays a critical role in cellular tolerance to nucleoside analogs. The accumulation of single‐stranded DNA gaps activates DNA damage checkpoint signaling. Rad17, one of the components in the alternative clamp loader Rad17‐RFC, loads the 9–1‐1 checkpoint clamp at the damaged region to initiate the DNA damage checkpoint through Chk1 phosphorylation. ATR kinase also senses replication stress and mediates DNA damage checkpoint signals via Chk1 phosphorylation. Prolonged replication fork arrest results in fork collapse, leading to DNA double‐strand break (DSB) formation, which is repaired by HR and non‐homologous end joining (NHEJ), in which HR repairs DSBs in an error‐free manner, whereas NHEJ introduces insertions and deletions.

Additional repair pathways such as BER and nucleotide excision repair (NER) also contribute to the repair of damaged DNA in an error‐free manner. Importantly, the specific repair mechanisms required for cellular tolerance to individual nucleoside analogs vary distinctly depending on the nucleoside analog, and comprehensive information on “gene‐drug synergy” remains limited. To address this gap, we have initiated a systematic study of the genetic factors underlying sensitivity to various nucleoside analogs to expand our knowledge on the “gene‐drug synergy”. The following sections summarize our recent findings on their cellular effects and gene‐specific synthetic interactions.

Alovudine

Alovudine is a thymidine analog that potently inhibits reverse transcriptase. It was used in clinical trials but is no longer used due to its strong cytotoxicity. It also interferes with the activity of the mitochondrial (mt) DNA polymerase γ, thereby restricting mtDNA synthesis. This inhibitory effect contributes to alovudine's capability to restrict the proliferation of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) cells. Furthermore, radioactive molecule labeled alovudine (18F‐FLT) is also used in Positron Emission Tomography due to its chemical stability and high tumor affinity.

In our recent studies using 24 kinds of mutant cell lines derived from chicken DT40 defective in the genes involved in genome maintenance pathways, we identified BRCA1 and FEN1 as critical factors required for cellular tolerance to alovudine. BRCA1 plays a dual role: first, BRCA1 removes alovudine from the growing end of DNA, thereby diminishing the chain‐terminating effect of alovudine. Second, BRCA1 contributes to resuming replication stalling at alovudine‐incorporated damaged template by promoting HR. BRCA1 promotes HR by suppressing the inhibitory action of 53BP1 on HR, and consistently, the alovudine‐hypersensitive phenotype of BRCA1 −/− cells is restored by the loss of 53BP1. Notably, while the loss of 53BP1 in BRCA1 −/− cells restores the slower replication on alovudine‐incorporated damaged template, the enhanced chain‐terminating effect of alovudine in BRCA1 −/− cells is not restored. These findings indicate that BRCA1's role in HR‐mediated fork recovery is essential for cellular survival upon alovudine, while BRCA1's role in mitigating the chain‐terminating effect of alovudine is not critical for alovudine tolerance (Figure 1A).

FEN1 is involved in the maturation of the Okazaki fragments during lagging strand synthesis. The loss of FEN1 results in the increased chain‐terminating effect of alovudine without affecting replication fork progression speed on the alovudine‐damaged template, indicating that FEN1 plays a role in nascent DNA synthesis in the presence of alovudine. Consistently, Okazaki fragment maturation kinetics is severely impaired in FEN1 −/− cells upon alovudine treatment. Strikingly, the loss of 53BP1 restores all alovudine‐associated mutant phenotypes of FEN1 −/− cells, indicating that the 53BP1 protein serves as a poison in the alovudine‐treated FEN1 −/− cells. Indeed, strong 53BP1 foci accumulate at nascent DNA regions during DNA synthesis, and subsequently, γH2AX (DNA damage marker) foci appear at regions superimposing the 53BP1 foci. Considering that 53BP1 interacts with RNA primers in Okazaki fragments, which is enhanced in FEN1 −/− cells due to the accumulation of unsolved long flapped DNA, we hypothesize that the aberrant accumulation of 53BP1 at unprocessed flaps leads to replication‐associated DNA damage in the presence of alovudine, leading to alovudine hypersensitivity (Figure 1B). Further studies are needed to elucidate the precise molecular mechanisms underlying the counteractive relationship between FEN1 and 53BP1 in alovudine tolerance.

Ara‐C and Other Arabinosides (Ara‐A, Ara‐G, and Ara‐T)

Ara‐C (a.k.a. Cytarabine) is a cytidine analog widely used in the treatment of acute lymphocytic and myeloid leukemia. It belongs to the arabinoside family, a class of nucleoside analogs with arabinose as the sugar moiety. Other arabinosides, Ara‐A, Ara‐G, and Ara‐T, are clinically used for the treatment of human herpes virus infections, T‐cell malignancies, and herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections, respectively.

Our sensitivity profiling revealed that the POLE1 exo−/− (the loss mutation of proofreading exonuclease activity in Polε) and CTF18 −/− mutations show strong synthetic lethality with Ara‐C treatment. The proofreading exonuclease activity of Polε removes Ara‐C from the 3′ end of growing DNA strands, and thereby mitigates the chain‐terminating effect, while CTF18 promotes the function of Polε by preventing the dissociation of Polε (run‐off) from the replication fork (Figure 1C). Interestingly, Ara‐A and Ara‐G only partially require CTF18 in the removal of these arabinosides from the primer end by Polε exonuclease. In the case of Ara‐T, Polε exonuclease plays a role independently of CTF18, and CTF18 is dispensable for the removal of incorporated Ara‐T. The mechanism underlying this bias among the 4 arabinosides in the requirement of CTF18 is not elucidated. Further studies might clarify these differences in the CTF18 requirement.

Cidofovir

Cidofovir is an acyclic cytidine analog, and its diphosphate form exhibits higher affinity to viral DNA polymerases than to host cell replicative polymerases, thereby preferentially targeting viral DNA polymerases over host replicative polymerases. Cidofovir diphosphate competitively inhibits the incorporation of deoxycytidine triphosphate (dCTP) into viral DNA, thereby suppressing viral DNA replication.

Our sensitivity profile analysis identified RAD17 /−, PRIMPOL −/−, BRCA1 −/−, BRCA2 −/−, RAD18 −/−, and CTF18 −/− DT40 cells as significantly more sensitive to cidofovir than wild‐type cells, with RAD17 /− and PRIMPOL −/− cells exhibiting the strongest sensitivity to cidofovir. Rad17 functions downstream of cidofovir incorporation by activating the intra‐S‐phase checkpoint through phosphorylation of Chk1, maintaining cellular survival upon cidofovir. In contrast, PrimPol promotes repriming DNA synthesis downstream of the cidofovir‐stalled site on cidofovir‐incorporated template, generating gaps that can be filled by HR‐mediated template switch. Thus, Rad17 and PrimPol play in different timing after cidofovir incorporation: Rad17 participates after cidofovir incorporation in nascent DNA and thereby activates the intra‐S‐phase checkpoint to maintain cellular viability upon cidofovir, while PrimPol promotes continuous replication on cidofovir‐incorporated damaged template (Figure 1D). This view is consistent with the observation that BRCA1, BRCA2 (HR factors), and Rad18 (TLS factor) are also required for cellular viability upon cidofovir.

 FUdR 

FUdR (a.k.a., Floxuridine or 5‐fluorodeoxyuridine) is an active metabolite of 5‐fluorouracil (5‐FU) and is a uridine analog. FUdR is FDA‐approved for the treatment of hepatic metastases from colorectal cancer and other gastrointestinal tract tumors. It is metabolized intracellularly into 5‐FdUTP, primarily by thymidine kinase and other kinases, and inhibits replication through two mechanisms: either it inhibits thymidine synthase, which in turn depletes the cellular dTTP pool and promotes dUTP accumulation, or it misincorporates dUMP and 5‐FdUMP into genome DNA, thereby suppressing replication. Both outcomes cause replication arrests, leading to the activation of ATR‐dependent intra‐S checkpoint signaling.

Our sensitivity profiling revealed that mutant cells deficient in HR (BRCA1 −/− and BRCA2 −/−), BER (PARP1 −/−), and checkpoint signaling (RAD17 /−) show hypersensitivity to FUdR. The involvement of these pathways in FUdR tolerance has been clarified. Notably, our screening also identified REV3 −/− cells, deficient in Polζ (a TLS polymerase), as the mutant with the highest FUdR sensitivity. REV3 promotes cellular survival in the following two ways: first, REV3 activates TLS to promote continuous replication on FUdR‐incorporated damaged template (Figure 1E); second, REV3 promotes DNA damage checkpoint activation and induces cell‐cycle arrest in the early S‐phase, which is required for the suppression of FUdR incorporation in the genome, thereby contributing to the suppression of chromosome instability (Figure 1E). The second function is particularly unexpected, as REV3 has not previously been implicated in cell cycle control. These findings suggest a novel function of Polζ beyond its canonical role in TLS.

Ganciclovir

Ganciclovir is a clinically important guanine nucleoside analog used for treating CMV infections. After being incorporated into virally infected cells, ganciclovir is initially phosphorylated by UL97 viral kinase. Subsequent phosphorylation by host kinases produces ganciclovir‐triphosphate, which marginally competes with dGTP for incorporation by DNA polymerases.

Our sensitivity profile analysis revealed that mutant cells deficient in HR (BRCA1 −/−), TLS (RAD18 −/−), and checkpoint signaling (RAD17 /−) exhibit higher sensitivity to ganciclovir than wild‐type cells. Rad17 activates the intra‐S checkpoint upon ganciclovir treatment to enhance ganciclovir tolerance. Rad18 is critical to ganciclovir resistance by suppressing deleterious NHEJ, which causes abnormal chromosomal fusion, such as radial chromosome, independently of its ability to activate TLS. On the other hand, BRCA1 suppresses DSBs during DNA replication on the ganciclovir‐incorporated damaged templates by promoting HR‐mediated DSB repair and template switching. The strong ganciclovir sensitivity of BRCA1 −/− is rescued by the 53BP1 loss. BRCA1 and Rad18 independently contribute to ganciclovir tolerance: BRCA1 suppresses DSBs, and Rad18 suppresses toxic chromosomal fusion (Figure 1F).

Remdesivir

Remdesivir is a prodrug of a 1′‐cyano modified adenosine nucleoside. This drug is used for the treatment of SARS‐CoV‐2 infections. This drug was initially developed for the treatment of hepatitis‐C virus infection, and is also effective against Ebola virus, Nipah virus, and Respiratory Syncytial virus infections. Remdesivir inhibits viral RNA‐dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) and thus restricts viral proliferation. Moreover, remdesivir has been reported to exhibit anti‐carcinogenic effects. In cells, remdesivir is metabolized to its monophosphate form, bypassing the initial phosphorylation step. It is subsequently converted into the active triphosphate form by cellular kinases, which act as a chain‐terminator during RNA synthesis.

Our sensitivity profiling analysis revealed that mutant cells deficient in FEN1 (FEN1 −/−) exhibit higher sensitivity to remdesivir than wild‐type cells. Remdesivir serves as a chain‐terminator by interfering with Okazaki fragment maturation. FEN1 alleviates the inhibitory effects of remdesivir by facilitating Okazaki fragment processing and thereby suppressing remdesivir‐induced DNA damage (Figure 1G). Hence, replication fork progression is significantly slower in remdesivir‐treated FEN1 −/− cells. Unlike alovudine, the loss of 53BP1 does not rescue the remdesivir‐associated mutant phenotypes of FEN1 −/− cells. The precise mechanisms underlying this difference might be clarified in future studies.

Gemcitabine

Gemcitabine, a cytidine analog, is clinically relevant and is employed in the treatment of pancreatic cancer. Gemcitabine halts continuous replication by serving as a chain‐terminator, and Mre11 is known to be responsible for the removal of incorporated gemcitabine from the growing end of nascent DNA (Figure 1F). Moreover, a previous study demonstrated that the inhibition of Rad17 potentiated gemcitabine cytotoxicity in the pancreatic cancer cell lines BxPC‐3 and MiaPaca‐2 and in the primary cell line JoPaca‐1 that closely resembles primary tumor tissue, indicating an important role of the Rad17‐mediated checkpoint axis for maintaining cellular survival upon gemcitabine challenge (Figure 1F).

Comparison of Cellular Effects of Nucleoside Analogs

We compared the sensitivity profiles of 12 nucleoside analogs, including the above six nucleoside analogs and six additional compounds that were preliminarily tested using 24 mutant DT40 cell lines deficient in various DNA repair mechanisms. The cell lines include mutants deficient in HR (BRCA1∆, BRCA2∆), NHEJ (KU70∆, POLQ∆), DSB repair (RAD54∆/KU70∆), BER (POLB∆, PARP1∆, FEN1∆), NER (XPA∆), DNA Interstrand Crosslink Repair (FANCC∆, FANCJ∆), TLS (REV3∆, RAD18∆, POLH∆, PrimPol∆), Proofreading (POLE1 −exo∆ ), DNA‐Protein Crosslink Repair (TDP1∆/TDP2∆, SPRTAN∆), Checkpoint (RAD17∆, ATM∆), or Chromosome Cohesion (CTF18∆, DDX11∆, SA2∆). To quantitatively evaluate the sensitivity of mutant cells to these drugs, we employed the LD50 value, which represents the drug dose required to kill 50% of the total cell population (Figure 2A). Furthermore, the relative sensitivity of the mutants was assessed using the following scores: log2 (LD50 in mutant cells)/(LD50 in wild‐type cells) (Figure 2B). As shown in Figure 3, each nucleoside analog exhibits a distinctly different sensitivity pattern (Figure 3, red represents highly sensitive). For example, proofreading exonuclease activity of Polε is required solely for the cellular tolerance to Ara‐A, whereas TDP1/TDP2 is specifically necessary for viability in response to zebularine.

We next analyzed the similarity of sensitivity profiles across all tested compounds (Figure 4). In this analysis, a similarity score of 1.0 (green) indicates an identical sensitivity pattern. Among the tested compounds, cidofovir and ganciclovir exhibit the highest similarity score (Figure 4, > 0.75). Consistently, phylogenetic clustering based on the sensitivity profiles revealed that cidofovir and ganciclovir are most closely related (Figure 5). This might be attributable to their similar chemical structure, both of which contain an acyclic sugar moiety. However, notable differences are detected in specific mutant strains. For instance, cidofovir critically requires PrimPol for cell viability, whilst ganciclovir does not. To accurately predict the cellular effects of nucleoside analogs and guide their rational structural design, more experimental data on the sensitivity profile of nucleoside analogs would be needed. In these comprehensive assessments of the genetic synergies between nucleoside analogs and specific gene mutations, we used chicken DT40 cells. The findings presented here might be extrapolated to human cells, since, in all cases tested, the similarities between human TK6 and DT40 cells were confirmed for the mutant exhibiting hypersensitivity to nucleoside analogs.

Future Prospects on the Use of Nucleoside Analogs in Cancer Chemotherapy

The success of chemotherapy targeting genome surveillance defects in cancers, such as PARP inhibitor‐based chemotherapy, highlights the potential of strategies that exploit specific defects (weakened genome maintenance systems) in individual cancer cells. The PARP inhibitor‐based chemotherapy targets HR‐deficient cancers such as BRCA gene mutated cancers. This concept of “genetic synergy”, exemplified by BRCA gene mutations and PARP inhibitors, offers a blueprint for designing novel targeted chemotherapy. In this review, we summarized the genetic synergies between nucleoside analogs and specific gene mutations (Figure 3). Our comprehensive analyses identified several nucleoside analogs that act synergistically with gene mutations in addition to HR defects, suggesting broader applicability of nucleoside analogs in such targeted chemotherapy based on the knowledge of genetic synergy. Therefore, patients with cancer mutations in other genome maintenance pathways would also benefit from targeted chemotherapy using nucleoside analogs.

Challenges in the Medical Application of Nucleoside Analogs

To utilize CTNAs effectively in cancer chemotherapy, broader consideration of biological and pharmacological aspects—beyond the experimental gene–drug sensitivity profile presented here—is required. These include their actual clinical efficacy, interplay with nucleic acids metabolism, and their metabolic stability. In general, cellular effects of nucleoside analogs depend on their incorporation efficiency, stability, and the efficiency of tri‐phosphorylation, which are determined by the status of expression levels of the transporters, metabolic enzymes affecting stability of nucleotides such as TYMP, SAMHD1, and ADA, and cellular kinases. Therefore, evaluating these comprehensive factors in individual tumors would be essential for improving the clinical relevance and translational potential of CTNA‐based agents.

Previous studies have shown that several tumors rely on the checkpoint axes such as the ATM‐Chk2 and Rad17‐ATR‐Chk1 pathways for maintaining cellular survival following treatment with Ara‐C or gemcitabine. In contrast, our sensitivity profiles using mutant cell lines identified synthetic relationships with RAD17∆ but not with ATM∆ mutants for Ara‐C or gemcitabine (Figure 3). These discrepancies might arise from tumor type‐specific differences in the utilization of checkpoint pathways. Thus, a comprehensive understanding of tumor type specificity and differential usage of DNA repair pathways among cancers will be critical for developing effective therapeutic strategies.

In our DT40 mutant cell line panel, various genome maintenance‐defective mutants were examined (Figure 3). Comparison with the gene panels used for cancer genetic diagnosis, such as NCC oncopanel, FoundationOne, GenMineTOP, and Guardant360, reveals that FoundationOne and GenMineTOP cancer profiling systems include several genes related to DNA repair. However, most of the genes analyzed in this study are not represented in current cancer genome panels, which largely focus on oncogenes and proto‐oncogenes. Expanding these panels to include DNA repair‐related genes would enhance their utility for predicting nucleoside analog sensitivity. Similarly, incorporating mutants involving major oncogenes such as KRAS and TP53 into the experimental mutant panel would further refine our understanding of gene‐drug interaction.

Bridging Traditional Cytotoxicity and Precision Oncology: Nucleoside Analogs as Promising Agents of Next‐Generation Cancer Chemotherapy

Traditional cancer chemotherapies have largely relied on non‐specific cytotoxic agents that damage DNA, targeting rapidly proliferating cells. In contrast, the approach proposed in this review utilizes the concept of synthetic lethality between nucleoside analogs and specific defects in genome maintenance pathways found in cancer cells. By systematically profiling the genetic dependence associated with each nucleoside analog, we have proposed a novel approach that targets tumor‐specific vulnerabilities. This strategy not only enhances treatment specificity but also expands the scope of precision oncology to include HR‐deficient tumors and other cancers possessing defects in other genome maintenance systems. By accumulating deep knowledge of genome maintenance networks and refining the sensitivity maps of nucleoside analogs, we anticipate the emergence of a new class of rationally designed mutation‐targeted chemotherapy regimens. In this regard, nucleoside analogs serve as a powerful bridge linking the foundational principles of cytotoxic therapy with the new era of “genome maintenance defect‐guided” cancer treatment. We believe that our current review provides significant information on the genetic synergy effects of gene mutations and nucleoside analogs which can be utilized to establish novel cancer chemotherapy targeting cancer mutations. Nucleoside analogs are truly the promising agents and might serve as an important bridge between the history and future of cancer treatment.
