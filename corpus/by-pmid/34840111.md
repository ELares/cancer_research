# Mechanistic roles of mutant p53 governing lipid metabolism.

## Abstract

Metabolic reprogramming of cancer cells by various acquired mutations provides support for rapid proliferation and growth in the tumor microenvironment. Mutations in the TP53 gene are the most common mutation found across all human cancers. Commonly referred to as "the guardian of the genome", p53 has a well-established role as a tumor suppressor by mediating checkpoint integrity and protecting cells from DNA damage. To date, the many functional roles of p53 extending beyond its classical function and exerting control over metabolic processes continues to confound the field. Recently, emerging roles for p53 in mediating lipid metabolism have come to light with intriguing metabolic roles in regulating cholesterol homeostasis and lipid droplet formation. Herein, we will seek to unify the mechanisms by which absence of functional p53, as well as stable mutant forms of p53, exert control over these lipid metabolism programs. Of equal importance, synthetic lethal phenotypes in the context of mutant p53 and aberrant lipid homeostasis offer new possible targets in the therapeutic landscape. This review aims to characterize the mechanisms by which p53 exerts control over these pathways and examine how precision medicine may benefit from tumor subtyping of p53 mutations.

Introduction

From the time that TP53 was discovered to act as a tumor suppressor, characterizing the consequence of loss of p53 function has been a major focus in the cancer field. The tumor suppressor protein p53 is a tetrameric protein that functions as a transcription factor, directly binding to target genes in a sequence-specific manner. Missense mutations in the DNA binding domain (DBD) of p53 comprise the vast majority of all p53 mutations found in human cancers (~80%). Intriguingly, eight missense mutations within the DBD (R175H, R248Q, R273H, R248W, R273C, R282W, G245S, and R249S, termed “hot spot” mutants) comprise ~28% of mutant p53 proteins across human cancers. The prevalence of these mutants extending across different cancer types strongly suggests a selective pressure exists, whereby these mutations confer a survival advantage over other loss-of-function (LOF) p53 mutations. At a structural level, the DBD mutants have been classified into structural mutants that greatly alter the conformation of p53, and contact mutants, which affect the recognition of target gene sequences. Along these lines, a large amount of evidence supports the notion that mutant versions of p53 have functional relevance extending beyond the loss of normal p53 function. These acquired novel functions they impart on cancer cells are aptly named p53 gain-of-function (GOF) activities. Perhaps one of the strongest pieces of evidence that p53 hot spot mutations differ from conventional loss of p53 function was gleaned from genetically engineered mouse (GEM) models of Li-Fraumeni syndrome, which in humans is often characterized by an inherited mutant p53 allele. Humans with Li-Fraumeni syndrome are predisposed to developing a host of cancers throughout their lifetime. Mice engineered to harbor mutant p53 alleles in the Li-Fraumeni syndrome model develop a distinct, allele-specific tumor spectra from p53 knock-out mice. The functions of mutant p53 on altering metabolism have been detailed in excellent reviews. Indeed, a pan-cancer analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) demonstrates a significant decrease in overall survival when comparing patients with TP53 mutations compared to wild-type TP53 across all cancers (Figure 1A). In this review we will focus specifically on functional roles of p53 in mediating lipid metabolism, particularly in facilitating cellular sterol biosynthesis, storage, and uptake.

In mammalian cells, the mechanisms governing cholesterol homeostasis are tightly controlled to maintain levels of cellular cholesterol that support steroidogenic growth signaling and membrane maintenance, while preventing lipotoxicity. All mammalian cells harbor the ability to synthesize de novo cholesterol, however synthesis mainly occurs in liver hepatocytes. The biosynthesis of cholesterol is mediated through a regulatory feedback by intracellular cholesterol content. Controlling this process is the sterol regulatory binding protein 2 (SREBP-2), which acts as a master regulator of cholesterol biosynthesis in cells through transcriptional regulation of the mevalonate pathway. Elegant experiments performed by Brown and Goldstein have characterized the mechanistic process by which SREBP-2, an ER-resident protein, undergoes proteolytic cleavage to release its mature form to the nucleus under conditions of low cellular cholesterol (Figure 2). Upon nuclear translocation, SREBP-2 binds to sterol regulatory elements (SREs) in the promoter regions of mevalonate pathway genes to activate their transcription (Figure 2). The main function of the mevalonate pathway is to convert Acetyl-CoA to mevalonate through a series of biochemical reactions. Importantly, the process of irreversible conversion of Acetyl-CoA to mevalonate is controlled by the rate-limiting enzymes HMG-CoA synthase (HMGCS1) and HMG-CoA reductase (HMGCR). Once mevalonate has been generated, the pathway bifurcates to committed synthesis of either (1) isoprenoid precursors or (2) cholesterol. As sufficient levels of cholesterol are restored through synthesis and low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR) mediated uptake, sterols bind to conserved sterol sensing domains (SSDs) to prevent further SREBP-2 cleavage and degrade the rate-limiting HMGCR through ubiquitination.

In contrast, cancer cells often benefit from dysregulation of lipid metabolism by upregulating biosynthesis and storage mechanisms to promote rapid cell proliferation and enhanced fitness in stressful microenvironments. Here we seek to gain a more complete understanding of the consequence of p53 GOF and LOF mutations on perturbances of cellular cholesterol regulation and provide insight into acquired vulnerabilities and targeting strategies. Further, we will attempt to define new and emerging roles for these mechanisms identified in cancers as well as explore the developing landscape of tumor cholesterol metabolism and treatment strategies.

Consequence of p53 mutations on SREBP-2/mevalonate axis

As stated previously, the role of p53 is now believed to extend beyond the role of maintaining genomic integrity. Recent reports suggest that loss of p53 function, as well as mutant p53 GOF activity play a critical role in modulating the mevalonate pathway. Interestingly, human patient data from the TCGA breast cancer dataset exemplifies an enhanced mevalonate gene signature in TP53 mutant breast tumors (Figure 1B). By examining the role of mutant p53 in disrupting acinar morphogenesis, Freed-Pastor et. al. uncovered a key association of p53 GOF mutants (p53R175H and p53273H) interacting with the mature form of SREBP-2. In addition, quantitative chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) in p53-mutant breast cancer cell lines revealed binding of mutant p53 to SREs at the promoter regions of mevalonate pathway genes in an SREBP-2 dependent complex. This stabilization results in upregulation of several genes of the mevalonate pathway, suggesting an adaptive mechanism by which mutant p53 co-opts isoprenoid and cholesterol biosynthesis for enhanced fitness in the tumor microenvironment (Figure 2). Indeed, breast cancer patient data stratified by mevalonate gene expression signature reveals a striking positive correlation between enhanced expression and p53 mutational status, in which these patients have a significant decrease in overall survival. Furthermore, mutant p53 has now been associated with the upregulation of isoprenyl cysteine carboxyl methyltransferase (ICMT), a key regulator in the post-translational processing of isoprenoid intermediates generated by the mevalonate pathway. Although the roles of ICMT in cancer progression are under active investigation, previous reports suggest ablation of ICMT hinders the growth of Ras-driven breast cancer cell lines in a xenograft model. By this logic, p53 mutation enhances isoprenoid processing mechanisms beyond the regulation of the SREBP-2 transcriptional program. Therefore, targeting the isoprenoid biosynthetic branch of the mevalonate pathway may prove efficacious for treatment of patients harboring mutations in TP53.

In a follow-up study utilizing a luciferase based chemical compound screen comparing p53-null (LOF) and p53R175H (GOF), three statin compounds (lovastatin, atorvastatin, and mevastatin) were found to be the most effective in reducing proliferation of p53 GOF mutant cells. Here it was discovered that in addition to stabilizing the transcriptional activity of SREBP-2, conformational mutants of p53 (i.e., p53R175H) are protected from ubiquitylation by CHIP-mediated degradation of DNAJA1 by binding to phosphomevalonate kinase. This in turn creates a positive feedback loop of enhanced mevalonate pathway upregulation and mutant p53 abundance and activity. Interestingly, this study was extended to include tumor types from multiple tissues (breast, colon, lung, pancreatic) and colony formation assays demonstrated a conserved reduction in growth, however this was limited only to the conformational mutants of p53, whereas structural mutants (i.e., p53R273H) and wild-type p53 were largely unaffected. Nevertheless, these findings present an intriguing rationale for the inconclusive benefit of targeting the mevalonate pathway in cancer.

Further exploration of the mechanism by which p53 interacts with SREBP-2 mediated activation of the mevalonate pathway has elucidated a novel, direct mechanism. Comparative transcriptional profiles of TP53WT vs. TP53−/− HCT116 colon cancer cell lines reinforces previous findings of mevalonate pathway gene upregulation upon loss of p53 function. Additionally, lipidomic analysis reveals a marked enhancement of free cholesterol in the TP53−/− compared to TP53WT cells. Treatment with Nutlin-3a, which stabilizes p53 by disruption of MDM2-mediated ubiquitination and enhances transcription of target genes, results in broad transcriptional repression of the mevalonate pathway. This suppressive mechanism is attributed to bolstering p53-mediated transcription of ABCA1, a member of the ATP-binding cassette family involved in cellular cholesterol efflux. Thereby, when p53 transcriptional activity is lost through mutation or deletion, ABCA1 downregulation results in enhanced SREBP-2 maturation (Figure 2). Previous reports present conflicting results on the SREBP-2/ABCA1 relationship. For example, the Liver X Receptors (LXRs) have been shown to promote ABCA1 upregulation through ligand-induced activation. Also, statin-induced activation of SREBP-2 in Chinese-hamster ovary cells (p53 mutant) results in upregulation of ABCA1 gene expression and this mechanism was determined to occur through the sterol byproduct 24(S),25-epoxycholesterol, an established LXR ligand that is supplied through mevalonate pathway turnover. Indeed, it is an interesting paradox to consider that downregulation of a bona fide cholesterol efflux transporter, which functions to lower cholesterol content, promotes SREBP-2 maturation. However, analysis of the cancer cell line encyclopedia does confirm an inverse correlation of ABCA1 expression with mevalonate gene signature. Importantly, these findings substantiate the claim that p53 LOF and GOF mutations directly impact the mevalonate pathway through regulation of SREBP-2. What remains to be confirmed is the relevance of cellular cholesterol turnover in this process.

Promotion of lipid droplet formation in the context of p53 mutations

Excessive accumulation of lipids and cholesterol in cancer cells promotes the accumulation of lipid droplets (LDs) through conversion to triglycerides and cholesteryl esters, respectively. Accumulation of LDs has been observed across many cancer types, suggesting they have a role in supporting tumor cell survival. To perform this role, LDs sequester abundant lipids (i.e. fatty acids, cholesterol, ceramide) and store them in inert forms In tumor cells, specific attention has been directed towards examining the role of Sterol O-acyltransferase (SOAT1/ACAT1, hereafter referred to as SOAT1), in preventing cellular lipotoxicity when cholesterol levels are elevated. In brief, SOAT1 performs the enzymatic conversion of free cholesterol to cholesteryl esters from both de novo synthesis and imported cholesterol upon reaching the ER for storage in LDs (Figure 2). As cholesterol biosynthesis is regulated by its end-product, it has been postulated that SOAT 1 activity alleviates feedback regulation to promote mevalonate pathway activation. A recent finding was made in support of this notion by comparing pancreatic ductal carcinoma (PDAC) mouse tumor organoids harboring mutant p53 with wild-type pancreata organoids. SOAT 1 expression was found to be dependent on p53 status, whereas both loss of p53 activity and mutant p53 had elevated SOAT1 expression relative to functional p53 controls. Further, CRISPR-mediated deletion and shRNA knockdown of SOAT1 significantly impaired proliferation in vitro and in orthotopically implanted organoids. Interestingly, the proliferation defect observed upon SOAT1 knockdown was associated with increased cholesterol accumulation dependent on p53 mutation or loss of function. Moreover, culturing of p53 mutant organoids in cholesterol replete conditions further enhanced the proliferation defect. This observation could be explained by cholesterol-induced lipotoxicity caused by enhanced mevalonate pathway activity and deficient alleviation through esterification of free cholesterol. Excess free cholesterol has been implicated in promoting the ER stress and activation of the unfolded protein response. Targeting SOAT1 in SW480 colon cancer cells, a p53 double-mutant (p53R273H/P309S), revealed a similar downregulation of mevalonate pathway, however a simultaneous activation of YAP signaling, promoted through increased free cholesterol accumulation, mediated resistance to therapy. Accordingly, combination therapy with the cholesterol sequestering agent Nystatin synergized with SOAT 1 inhibition to circumvent YAP activation. Nonetheless, the consequence of increasing intracellular free cholesterol through SOAT1 inhibition must be carefully considered, to prevent unforeseen mechanisms of resistance driven through increased membrane cholesterol on signaling programs.

Disrupting cholesterol trafficking in the p53 mutant context

An additional aspect of cellular cholesterol concentration is receptor-mediated uptake of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) by LDLR. Circulating LDL containing cholesterol is brought into cells through receptor-mediated endocytosis and delivered to the lysosome for subsequent degradation of LDL by lysosomal acid lipases and free cholesterol is released within the lysosome (Figure 2). The LDL-derived cholesterol is then fractionally distributed to either the PM or ER. Although a definitive link between LDLR upregulation and p53 mutational status has yet to be established, dysregulation of SREBP-2 mediated feedback to downregulate LDLR suggests an understudied layer of generating synthetic lethality by targeting lysosomal cholesterol transport of free cholesterol. Indeed, the lysosomotropic compound leelamine has been shown to mediate cancer cell death through preventing lysosomal cholesterol clearance. Niemann-Pick disease, characterized by mutation of the lysosomal resident proteins NPC1 and NPC2, exemplify the consequence of failed intracellular cholesterol transport resultant of acid sphingomyelinase (ASM) deficiency. Further, a screen of ASM inhibitors exhibited cell death characteristic of inhibition of lysosomal cholesterol clearance, impaired vesicular transport, and impairment of autophagic flux. Along these lines, the phosphatidylinositol-4-phosphate-5-kinases (PI5P4Ks), which perform the conversion of phosphatidylinositol 5-monophosphate (PI-5-P) to phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PI-4,5-P2) largely at intracellular membranes, were found to facilitate autophagosome-lysosome fusion and lysosomal cholesterol transfer to peroxisomes (Figure 2). Interestingly, the PI5P4Ks have been implicated in a synthetic lethal interaction with mutant p53 and are highly expressed in mutant p53 breast cancers. Although these mechanisms are still not fully understood, they provide a logical next step to uncovering novel, synergistic treatments in the p53 mutant context.

Targeting cholesterol metabolism in preclinical models

Historically, statins have received the most attention in regard to targeting dysregulated cholesterol metabolism in the p53 mutant context. Statins are competitive inhibitors of HMG-CoA reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme of the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway, thus limiting mevalonate pathway activation and de novo synthesis. As previously described, p53 LOF and GOF mutations drive aberrant cholesterol metabolism by increased activation of the mevalonate pathway which both stabilizes mutant p53 protein and provides the biosynthetic intermediates required for enhanced survival and growth in the tumor microenvironment. Statins can be classified into two groups: the naturally derived type-I derivatives (simvastatin, lovastatin, pravastatin, and mevastatin) and synthetic type-II derivatives (fluvastatin, atorvastatin, cerivastatin, pitavastatin, and rosuvastatin). Intriguingly, statins have been implicated to have both apoptotic and anti-proliferative roles in preclinical cancer models, suggesting their utility as a cancer therapeutic. In lung adenocarcinoma cells, an enhanced sensitivity to simvastatin was observed in p53 GOF cells compared to p53 LOF and wild-type counterparts. For a comprehensive review detailing mutant p53 and mevalonate pathway targeting by statins see Parrales et al. Although preclinical models have demonstrated considerable efficacy of statins as an anticancer agent, clinical data has exemplified an inverse association of survival with statin use. In addition, consistent exposure to statins can promote enhanced activation of the mevalonate pathway as a single agent through adaptive activation of HMG-CoA reductase inhibition. A suspected reason for this discrepancy is poor oral bioavailability of statins. To this point, current advances in statin delivery through nanotechnology are underway to enhance bioavailability and controlled systemic release. Recently, adjuvant administration of isoprenoids has been suggested as an adjuvant therapy for blocking the processing and localization of SREBP-2, independently of statin mechanistic action.

Apart from statins, advances in our understanding of vulnerabilities poised through dysregulation of cholesterol feedback mechanisms has also revealed new targets of p53 mutant cancers. The emerging role of SOAT1 in modulating cancer cell survival by prevention of free cholesterol accumulation provides rationale for novel therapies targeting cholesterol trafficking mechanisms in this context. Indeed, the structure of human SOAT1 protein has recently been elucidated and several SOAT1 inhibitors are commercially available for preclinical studies. Additionally, cholesterol clearance from lysosomes represents an intriguing therapeutic target, as lysosomotropic drugs such as leelamine and ASM inhibitors decrease lysosomal cholesterol clearance and disrupt autophagic flux, however the precise mechanism by which this is achieved is still unexplored. Further, an intriguing new target, which has been specifically implicated in p53 mutant cancers, are the PI5P4Ks. With promising preclinical studies using shRNA mediated knockdown and cancer mouse models demonstrating that targeting the PI5P4Ks in mutant p53 cancer cells is an attractive therapeutic option, potent and selective chemical inhibitors are currently being developed by several groups. In fact, recently a noncovalent dual PI5P4Kα/β inhibitor, CC260, was identified using a high throughput screen to selectively kill p53 mutant breast cancer cells. In the end, with PI5P4Ks regulating autophagosome-lysosome fusion, their newly discovered role in modulating lysosomal cholesterol transport and notably their synthetic lethality in conjunction with mutant p53 render them exciting targets for p53 mutant cancers.

Discussion

Over the past decade, significant advancements have been made in understanding the complex regulatory mechanisms of p53 in lipid metabolic processes. Although these processes promote substrate availability for rapidly proliferating tumor cells, they pose a unique therapeutic opportunity for targeted therapy by interfering with adaptive growth pathways co-opted by mutant p53. The role of mutant p53 in modulating the mevalonate pathway through enhanced activation of SREBP-2 signaling demonstrates a critical requirement for isoprenoid and cholesterol biosynthesis. As the regulation of cholesterol homeostasis through biosynthesis, storage, and efflux is exceedingly complex, the recent studies mentioned herein provide a wealth of novel treatment options for current research. Systematic testing of p53 GOF mutants has begun to elucidate key differences from both p53 LOF and p53 wild-type in response to statin treatment, which may explain previous failings in identifying statins as a viable therapeutic option in cancer. In addition, the role of cholesterol trafficking and storage in the p53 mutant context is a developing field warranting further study on diverse cancer types. Targeting cholesterol clearance from the lysosome is an intriguing concept, divergent from statin therapy, as the focus is shifted from preventing biosynthesis to taking advantage of rewired p53 metabolism. This could ultimately prove to be a more selective treatment, as normal cells maintain the ability to prevent cholesterol biosynthesis through conserved feedback mechanisms, whereas mutant p53 cells cannot. In closing, the determinant factor on the successes of modulating lipid metabolism as a viable therapeutic option may reside with more careful selection of cancer patient cohorts that harbor TP53 mutations.
