# CDC6 as a pan-cancer immunological and prognostic biomarker and its role in suppressing melanoma malignancy.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Cell division cycle 6 (CDC6) is a key licensing factor for DNA replication in the G1 and S phases. Besides initiating replication, CDC6 also helps establish and maintain the S-M checkpoint, ensuring genomic stability. Emerging evidence highlights its dysregulation in various cancers, implicating CDC6 in tumor progression and therapy resistance. However, a comprehensive pan-cancer analysis evaluating its diagnostic, prognostic and immunomodulatory potential remains lacking, underscoring the need for further investigation.

**METHODS**: By integrating multi-omics datasets from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), Genotype-Tissue Expression Project (GTEx), cBioPortal, Human Protein Atlas (HPA), UALCAN and SangerBox, we employed systematic bioinformatics approaches to investigate the oncogenic role of CDC6 across multiple cancer types. Our analysis encompassed prognostic associations, mutational landscapes, tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) infiltration patterns and epigenetic regulation via DNA methylation, providing a pan-cancer perspective on CDC6’s potential role in tumorigenesis. In addition, CDC6’s role in melanoma cell proliferation, invasion and migration was experimentally assessed.

**RESULTS**: Pan-cancer analysis demonstrated CDC6 as a consistently upregulated oncogene across multiple malignancies, exhibiting significantly elevated expression compared to normal tissues. Notably, CDC6 is closely associated with prognosis across various cancer types. Our investigation further revealed robust correlations between CDC6 expression and immune cell infiltration patterns. Epigenetic profiling identified significant associations between CDC6 expression and DNA methylation alterations in nine cancer types. Functional studies validated CDC6’s oncogenic role, where its overexpression significantly promoted cellular proliferation, migration and invasion in melanoma.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our study demonstrates that CDC6 serves as a crucial oncogenic driver across diverse tumor types, establishing its dual utility as a diagnostic biomarker and independent prognostic indicator. Importantly, we identified a significant correlation between elevated CDC6 expression and specific immune microenvironment alterations, suggesting its potential as a predictive biomarker for immunotherapy response. These findings demonstrate CDC6’s dual role in cancer development and immune regulation, warranting further investigation into its mechanisms and therapeutic potential.

Background

Cancer represents a major global health challenge, with its escalating incidence and mortality rates rising at an alarming pace, placing an increasingly severe strain on public health systems worldwide. Despite advances in oncology, the intricate mechanisms underlying tumorigenesis and the persistent issue of poor prognosis continue to hinder effective cancer management. In this context, pan-cancer analysis has emerged as a powerful tool, enabling researchers to uncover shared molecular and biological features across diverse cancer types.

The tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) serves as a crucial determinant in tumorigenesis, disease progression and therapeutic response to immunotherapy. Emerging evidences underscore the complex bidirectional interactions between malignant cells and host immune components, encompassing sophisticated immune evasion strategies, recruitment of immunosuppressive cell populations and aberrant immune checkpoint signaling. These dynamically evolving TIME characteristics have increasingly demonstrated clinical utility as predictive biomarkers for immunotherapy efficacy and resistance. CDC6 (Cell Division Cycle 6) is a critical cell cycle regulatory protein primarily involved in the initiation of DNA replication and the regulation of the cell cycle. Emerging evidences under CDC6’s dual functionality in maintaining cellular homeostasis and driving oncogenic processes. Beyond its essential functions in maintaining normal cell cycle progression, CDC6 has been implicated in the initiation, progression and metastasis of various cancers. Moreover, emerging evidence suggests that CDC6 modulates the tumor immune microenvironment, influencing immune evasion mechanisms and shaping responses to immunotherapy. CDC6 plays a critical role in cancer development and the complex interplay between tumors and the immune system. Therefore, a pan-cancer analysis of CDC6’s biological characteristics across different cancer types is of great importance.

Melanoma, an increasingly prevalent and aggressive skin cancer, although immunotherapies have significantly improved treatment outcomes, their clinical benefits are often limited by both intrinsic and acquired resistance mechanisms.

Although CDC6 overexpression is increasingly linked to tumor progression and adverse prognosis in multiple cancers, its functional role in melanoma development and progression remains elusive. Elucidating the molecular mechanisms underlying CDC6’s role in melanoma progression, particularly its dynamic interactions with the tumor immune microenvironment, may identify novel prognostic biomarkers and reveal actionable therapeutic targets to address existing treatment limitations.

Through the integration of multi-omics approaches and functional validation studies, this investigation seeks to comprehensively delineate CDC6’s oncogenic expression patterns across diverse malignancies, decipher its immunoregulatory mechanisms within the tumor ecosystem, and establish its clinical prognostic value. Furthermore, we will evaluate CDC6’s functional contributions to melanoma pathogenesis and assess its potential as a molecular target for therapeutic intervention.

Methods

Data collection

Gene expression profiles and corresponding clinical data were acquired from the UCSC Xena platform (https://xenabrowser.net/datapages/), incorporating datasets from both The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and Genotype-Tissue Expression (GTEx) projects. A comprehensive list of TCGA cancer-type abbreviations alongside their complete nomenclature is presented in Table 1. For comparative analysis of CDC6 expression patterns between tumor specimens and their histologically normal counterparts, we employed the TIMER analytical tool (https://cistrome.shinyapps.io/timer/).

Protein expression analysis

We systematically analyzed CDC6 protein expression profiles in both neoplastic and normal human tissues using The Human Protein Atlas (HPA; https://www.proteinatlas.org). Protein-protein interaction networks were constructed using the STRING database (https://string-db.org), and subcellular localization patterns were determined via GeneCards (https://www.genecards.org).

CDC6 expression correlates with immune checkpoint marker levels

We utilized the Sangerbox database to systematically analyze the correlation between CDC6 expression levels and key tumor immune biomarkers, encompassing both inhibitory and stimulatory immune checkpoint (ICP) genes.

Immune cell infiltration

 Standardized pan-cancer data were acquired from the UCSC TCGA repository through the Sanger Box database. For each tumor sample, CDC6 gene expression values were extracted, log2-transformed (log2[X + 0.001]), and analyzed by cancer type with corresponding GeneSymbol annotation. Tumor microenvironment characterization was performed using the ESTIMATE algorithm (v1.0.13) to quantify stromal content, immune cell infiltration, and generate composite ESTIMATE scores. To comprehensively assess immune cell infiltration patterns, we employed methods (TIMER and CIBERSORT) implemented in the IOBR package (v0.99.9), enabling systematic evaluation of correlations between CDC6 expression levels and tumor immune profiles across multiple cancer types.

Prognostic value assessment

Survival outcomes were evaluated through comprehensive statistical analyses, we conducted systematic univariate Cox proportional hazards regression analyses to rigorously examine the prognostic significance of CDC6 expression across four key clinical endpoints: overall survival (OS), disease-specific survival (DSS), disease-free interval (DFI), and progression-free interval (PFI) in pan-cancer datasets.

Gene set enrichment analysis

To systematically investigate CDC6-associated gene networks, we conducted genome-wide correlation analyses based on TCGA expression profiles. Significant gene correlates (Pearson’s |r| > 0.3, p < 0.05) were subjected to pathway enrichment analysis using the clusterProfiler package (version 4.0.5) in R. The GSEA parameters were carefully optimized as follows: permutation number = 1000, minimum gene set size = 10, maximum gene set size = 1000 and significance cutoff = 0.05. Reactome pathways were employed as the curated gene set collection to identify functionally enriched pathways.

Epigenetic and genomic characterization of cdc6 in pan-cancer analysis

To investigate the epigenetic regulation of CDC6, we employed UALCAN (http://ualcan.path.uab.edu/), an interactive web-based platform for TCGA gene expression and methylation analysis, to assess CDC6 promoter methylation levels across multiple cancer types. Additionally, we utilized cBioPortal (http://www.cbioportal.org/), an integrated repository of TCGA-derived genomic data, to analyze CDC6 mutations and alterations. Our dataset comprised 10,953 tumor samples from 32 cancer types. Using cBioPortal’s “OncoPrint” module, we visualized CDC6 alterations (including mutations, copy number variations, and expression changes) in a heatmap format. The “Cancer Types Summary” module was further employed to quantify mutation frequencies across different malignancies, presented as a bar chart.

Clinical specimens

Study eligibility required histologically confirmed melanoma diagnosis without concurrent comorbidities. Primary melanoma tissues, matched para-tumor tissues (≥ 2 cm from lesion margin), and normal control tissues were aseptically collected from seven treatment-naive patients undergoing surgical resection at the Dermatology Hospital of Southern Medical University. All specimens were immediately flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at −80℃ until analysis.

5-ethynyl-2'-deoxyuridine (EdU) proliferation assay

Cell proliferation was evaluated using a 5-ethynyl-2’-deoxyuridine (EdU) incorporation assay (Ribobio, China). Cells were seeded in 24-well plates (1 × 10⁵ cells/well) and pulsed with 50µM EdU for 2 h at 37 °C. Following fixation, EdU detection was performed according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Nuclei were counterstained with Hoechst 33,342 for total cell enumeration. Proliferation rates were determined by calculating the percentage of EdU-positive cells from five random fields per well.

Cell migration assay

A wound healing assay was performed to evaluate cell migration capacity. A375 cells (both wild-type and CDC6-knockdown) were seeded in 24-well plates (2 × 10⁴ cells/well) and cultured to 90–100% confluency. Uniform scratches were created in the monolayer using a sterile 10 µl pipette tip. Initial wound images (0 h) were acquired using phase-contrast microscopy (10× objective), followed by imaging at identical locations after 24 h of incubation under standard culture conditions.

Colony formation assay

A colony formation assay was performed to assess clonogenic potential. A375 parental cells and CDC6-knockdown counterparts were seeded in 6-well plates at a density of 400 cells per well (200 cells/mL, 2 mL/well) in complete medium. After 10 days of culture under standard conditions (37 °C, 5% CO₂), colonies were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 15 min at room temperature. Cells were then stained with 0.1% crystal violet for 30 min, washed gently with PBS and air-dried. Colony images were captured using a digital camera under consistent lighting conditions.

Transwell invasion assay

To assess the impact of CDC6 knockdown on invasive potential, we performed a standardized Matrigel invasion assay using 24-well Transwell chambers (8 μm pore size, Corning). The inserts were pre-coated with growth factor-reduced Matrigel (50 µg/cm², BD Biosciences) and allowed to polymerize for 2 h at 37℃. Briefly, serum-starved A375 cells (parental and CDC6 knockdown) were seeded in the upper chambers (5 × 10⁴cells/well in serum-free medium), while complete medium containing 10% FBS served as chemoattractant in the lower chamber. After 24 h incubation (37℃, 5% CO₂), non-invading cells were mechanically removed from the upper membrane surface using cotton swabs.Invaded cells on the lower membrane surface were fixed with ice-cold 100% methanol (10 min), stained with 0.1% crystal violet for 30 min and quantified under a light microscope.

Real-time quantitative PCR

Total RNA was isolated from A375 parental and CDC6-knockdown cells using RNAiso Plus reagent (Takara, China) following the manufacturer’s protocol, with RNA integrity verified by agarose gel electrophoresis. Genomic DNA contamination was eliminated using the PrimeScript RT reagent Kit with gDNA Eraser (Takara, China), followed by first-strand cDNA synthesis under optimized conditions (37 °C for 15 min, 85 °C for 5 s). The qPCR protocol included an initial pre-denaturation step at 95 ℃ for 5 min, followed by 40 amplification cycles (95 ℃ for 10 s and 60 ℃ for 34 s) and a final melt curve analysis (95 ℃ for 15 s, 60 ℃ for 1 min, 95 ℃ for 15 s). The relative mRNA expression levels were calculated using the 2^(-ΔΔCt) method.

Statistical analyses

Quantitative data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Intergroup comparisons were performed using Student’s t-test for pairwise comparisons or one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) for multiple group comparisons, following assessment of data distribution and variance homogeneity. All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 8 software. A P-value < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

CDC6 expression profiles across cancers: tumor versus normal tissue comparison

To comprehensively evaluate CDC6 expression across diverse cancer types, we initially analyzed the TCGA dataset using the TIMER 2.0 database. Our findings revealed significant upregulation of CDC6 expression in a wide range of malignancies, including BLCA, BRCA, CHOL, COAD, ESCA, HNSC, KIRC, KIRP, LIHC, LUAD, LUSC, PRAD, READ, STAD, THCA and UCEC. Notably, our analysis identified a statistically significant differential expression pattern of CDC6 in melanoma metastasis (Fig. 1A), suggesting its potential involvement in metastatic progression. To address the absence of normal control data in the TIMER 2.0 database for ACC, CESC, DLBC, GBM, LAML, LGG, OV, PAAD, PCPG, SARC, TGCT, THYM and UCS tumor types, we performed a complementary analysis using the GEPIA database, which integrates comprehensive datasets from both TCGA and GTEx. This analysis demonstrated marked upregulation of CDC6 expression in CESC, DLBC, GBM, OV, PAAD, SARC, TGCT, THYM and UCS, while revealing significant downregulation in LAML (Fig. 1B).

To further validate and expand these findings, we conducted a comprehensive pan-cancer analysis of CDC6 expression across 33 distinct cancer types and their corresponding adjacent normal tissues using the Sangerbox database. This analysis confirmed significant CDC6 overexpression in GBM, GBMLGG, LGG, UCEC, BRCA, CESC, LUAD, ESCA, STES, KIRP, KIPAN, COAD, COADREAD, PRAD, STAD, HNSC, KIRC, LUSC, LIHC, WT, SKCM, BLCA, THCA, READ, OV, PAAD, TGCT, UCS, ALL, LAML, ACC and CHOL (Fig. 1C). Importantly, the CDC6 expression profiles obtained from both Sangerbox and TIMER2.0 databases exhibited remarkable consistency across various tumor types, reinforcing the robustness of our findings.

To extend our investigation to the protein level, we systematically analyzed CDC6 expression in BRCA, COAD, LIHC, LUAD and PRAD compared to their corresponding normal tissues using the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) database (Fig. 2A and E). This analysis revealed distinct protein expression patterns across these cancer types, consistent with our transcriptomic findings. Furthermore, to elucidate the functional implications of CDC6 dysregulation, we investigated its subcellular localization through the HPA database (Figs. 2F-I). The results demonstrated that CDC6 predominantly localizes to the nucleoplasm and nucleolus, a finding that aligns with previous reports and suggests its potential role in nuclear processes. Collectively, these multi-level analyses provide compelling evidence that CDC6 expression is dysregulated across multiple tumor types, with the most of cancers exhibiting significantly higher expression levels compared to normal tissues.

Building on these findings, we further explored the association between CDC6 expression and tumor stages across various cancer types. Our analysis revealed a significant correlation between CDC6 expression levels and the tumor stages of ACC, BRCA, ESCA, HNSC, KICH, KIRC, KIRP, LIHC, LUAD, LUSC, MESO, STAD and TGCT (Fig. 3A-M). To validate these observations, we utilized the GEPIA2 database to analyze CDC6 expression in relation to major pathological stages. The results confirmed a consistent association between CDC6 expression and the stages of ACC, BRCA, KICH, KIRP, LIHC, LUAD, OV and UCS (Fig. 4A-H). These findings collectively suggest that CDC6 expression levels are closely linked to the clinical progression of these cancers and may serve as a potential biomarker for determining pathological staging in these malignancies.

Prognostic implications of CDC6 expression across diverse cancer types

To comprehensively evaluate the prognostic significance of CDC6 in cancer patients, we performed a detailed survival analysis using the Sangerbox platform, examining its association with multiple survival metrics, including overall survival (OS), disease-specific survival (DSS), disease-free interval (DFI) and progression-free interval (PFI). Cox regression analysis of OS demonstrated that elevated CDC6 expression was significantly correlated with poorer prognosis in GBMLGG, LGG, KIPAN, KIRP, ACC, LIHC, KICH, PAAD, LUAD, MESO, LAML, PRAD, SKCM.

and ALL, highlighting its role as a potential risk factor in these cancers. Interestingly, high CDC6 expression exhibited a protective effect in THYM and READ (Fig. 5A).

Further analysis revealed that high CDC6 expression was associated with worse DSS in GBMLGG, LGG, KIRP, KIPAN, ACC, KICH, PAAD, LIHC, MESO, LUAD,

PRAD and SKCM-P (Fig. 5B). Similarly, elevated CDC6 expression was identified as a risk factor for DFI in KIRP, KIPAN, PAAD, THCA, LIHC, LUSC and MESO (Fig. 5C). In the analysis of PFI, higher CDC6 expression was linked to poor prognosis in GBMLGG, LGG, KIPAN, ACC, KIRP, KICH, PAAD, LIHC, UVM, MESO, BLCA, PRAD, LUAD, SKCM-P and PCPG, while it appeared to be a protective factor in OV (Fig. 5D). These findings collectively underscore the tumor-specific prognostic implications of CDC6 expression, with its role varying significantly across different cancer types.

In conclusion, our results demonstrate that CDC6 serves as a potential prognostic biomarker in multiple cancers, with its expression levels exhibiting distinct associations with patient outcomes depending on the tumor type.

CDC6 expression correlates with immune infiltration levels

To explore the relationship between CDC6 expression and immune cell infiltration in the tumor microenvironment, we analyzed the association between CDC6 levels and tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), which are known to independently predict sentinel lymph node status and cancer survival. Using the CIBERSORT algorithm, we assessed the correlation between CDC6 expression and immune cell infiltration across multiple cancer types. Our analysis revealed that CDC6 expression was significantly associated with immune cell infiltration in most cancer types (Fig. 6A). Specifically, CDC6 levels showed a positive correlation with the infiltration of B cells, macrophages, T cells, neutrophils, and dendritic cells (Fig. 6B). These findings suggest that CDC6 may play a multifaceted role in modulating the immune response within the tumor microenvironment, potentially influencing tumor immunity in a cancer type-specific manner.

CDC6 expression demonstrates significant associations with ICP genes, TMB, MSI and neoantigens in pan-cancer analysis

Immune checkpoint (ICP) genes play a significant role in the tumor infiltration of immune cells and immunotherapy responses. Immune checkpoint proteins are key regulators of immunity by activating over suppressing critical immune regulatory signaling pathways. Thus, ICP proteins are critical for the maintenance of self-tolerance and immune responses. Furthermore, immune checkpoint-related genes play a key role in the immune escape mechanisms of tumors. Therefore, we analyzed the correlation between expression levels of CDC6 and the ICP genes to determine the role of CDC6 in immunotherapy. The data demonstrated that CDC6 expression correlated with immune-related genes in most tumors such as DLBC, UVM, KIPAN, KIRC, LIHC, OV, GBMLGG, LGG, KICH, MESO, PRAD, THCA, PAAD, READ, LAML, STAD, STES, ESCA, LUSC, ALL, GBM, ACC, WT, COAD, COADREAD, LUAD, BLCA, BRCA, UCS, HNSC, PCPG, SKCM, KIRP, UCEC, CHOL, CESC, SARC, THYM, NB and TGCT (Fig. 7A). Therefore, CDC6 could be a promising target for tumor therapy.

Tumor mutational burden (TMB), microsatellite instability (MSI), and neoantigen load have been widely recognized as biomarkers predictive of immunotherapy response in cancer. To explore whether CDC6 expression may serve a similar role, we systematically analyzed its association with TMB and MSI across multiple tumor types using TCGA data. Our analysis revealed significant positive correlations between CDC6 expression and TMB in diverse malignancies, including BRCA, BLCA, ACC, UCS, UCEC, TGCT, STAD, SARC, READ, PRAD, PAAD, OV, MESO, LUSC, LUAD, LGG, KIRC, KICH, HNSC and COAD (Fig. 7B). Conversely, a significant negative correlation was observed between CDC6 expression levels and TMB in THYM. Furthermore, CDC6 levels showed a positive association with MSI status in eight cancer types: COAD, HNSC, LIHC, LUSC, SARC, STAD, UCEC and UCS (Fig. 7C). These findings suggest that elevated CDC6 expression may serve as a novel predictive biomarker for immunotherapy efficacy in these specific cancer types, potentially reflecting enhanced tumor immunogenicity through its association with established markers of immunotherapy response.

Epigenetic regulation and genomic alterations of CDC6

Using the UALCAN platform, we systematically analyzed CDC6 promoter methylation levels across multiple cancer types. DNA methylation status was quantified by beta values (0: unmethylated; 1: fully methylated), with hypermethylation (β = 0.5–0.7) and hypomethylation (β = 0.25–0.3) defined by established cutoffs. Notably, tumor tissues exhibited significantly elevated CDC6 promoter methylation in COAD, ESCA, HNSC, KIRC, LUSC, PAAD and SARC compared to normal controls. Conversely, BLCA and PRAD demonstrated marked CDC6 hypomethylation in tumor versus normal tissues (Fig. 8).

Analysis of CDC6 mutation spectra in 10,953 TCGA tumors through cBioPortal revealed distinct patterns of genomic alterations across cancer types. Gene amplification emerged as the predominant alteration type, with particularly high frequency in: Esophageal adenocarcinoma (8.24%), Stomach adenocarcinoma (7.95%), Breast invasive carcinoma (5.26%) and Uterine carcinosarcoma (3.51%) (Fig. 9). These findings highlight the potential oncogenic role of CDC6 amplification in these aggressive cancers.

Functional association between CDC6 and cutaneous melanoma (SKCM)

Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) revealed significant functional associations between CDC6 and SKCM pathogenesis. KEGG pathway analysis identified the top five CDC6-associated pathways: allograft rejection, arachidonic acid metabolism, complement and coagulation cascades, graft-versus-host disease and linoleic acid metabolism (Fig. 10A). Complementary gene ontology (GO) analysis demonstrated striking enrichment in skin biology processes, with the most significant terms being: cornification, keratin filament, keratinization, keratinocyte differentiation and skin development (Fig. 10B). These results implicate CDC6 in both immunomodulatory pathways and epidermal differentiation mechanisms in cutaneous melanoma.

CDC6 drives melanoma progression via proliferation, migration and invasion

To evaluate the role of CDC6 in melanoma, we first examined its expression in clinical samples. Immunohistochemical analysis revealed significantly elevated levels of CDC6, MelanA and Ki67 in melanoma tumor tissues compared to adjacent normal or paracancerous tissues (Fig. 11A). To further investigate its functional impact, we performed loss-of-function studies in A375 melanoma cells. qPCR confirmed efficient knockdown of CDC6 (Fig. 11B). Functional assays demonstrated that CDC6 depletion markedly impaired melanoma cell proliferation, as evidenced by reduced colony formation (Fig. 11C) and diminished EdU incorporation (Fig. 11D). Additionally, CDC6 knockdown significantly attenuated invasive and migratory capacities in Transwell assays (Fig. 11E) and wound healing (Fig. 11F). Collectively, these findings establish CDC6 as a critical regulator of melanoma progression, promoting proliferative, migratory, and invasive behaviors.

Discussion

The comprehensive pan-cancer analysis of CDC6 presented in this study reveals its multifaceted role in tumorigenesis, prognosis and immune modulation across diverse malignancies. Our findings not only highlight the consistent overexpression of CDC6 in tumor tissues compared to normal counterparts but also underscore its clinical relevance as a potential prognostic biomarker and therapeutic target.

CDC6 is a key DNA replication factor that binds to replication origins and is essential for replication initiation. Given its fundamental role in cell cycle progression, dysregulation of CDC6 has been closely linked to tumorigenesis and cancer development. Studies demonstrate that CDC6 is frequently overexpressed in lung cancer tissues, where elevated expression levels are strongly correlated with poorer overall survival (OS) in patients. Similarly, Steven Buechle reported that high CDC6 expression serves as a negative prognostic marker in estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer, with patients exhibiting significantly shorter survival times. Further supporting these findings, Karanika S et al. observed that CDC6 mRNA levels are substantially upregulated in prostate cancer tissues compared to adjacent normal controls, and increased expression independently predicts an unfavorable prognosis in prostate cancer patients. Our findings corroborate previous studies, demonstrating that CDC6 is consistently upregulated across diverse cancer types, further supporting its pivotal role in tumorigenesis. This dysregulated overexpression aligns mechanistically with CDC6’s well-established function in DNA replication initiation and cell cycle progression. Importantly, elevated CDC6 expression is strongly associated with poorer clinical outcomes, suggesting it actively contributes to aggressive tumor behavior. Notably, our functional validation in cutaneous melanoma (SKCM) demonstrates that CDC6 directly promotes three fundamental hallmarks of malignant progression: proliferation, migration and invasion. While these findings clearly establish CDC6’s oncogenic functions, the molecular mechanisms underlying its regulation of cellular motility - particularly its potential involvement in EMT pathways - remain to be fully elucidated and warrant further investigation. Systematic analysis of key EMT markers (including E-cadherin, N-cadherin and vimentin) in future studies will be crucial to determine whether CDC6 mediates migration through EMT-dependent mechanisms, and fully elucidate its regulatory role in melanoma cell motility. Given its widespread dysregulation and robust correlation with tumor aggressiveness across cancers, CDC6 not only serves as a universal prognostic biomarker but also represents a promising therapeutic target for precision oncology strategies.

Beyond its canonical role in DNA replication and cell cycle control, CDC6 exhibits emerging functions in modulating the tumor immune microenvironment (TME). Our analyses revealed that elevated CDC6 expression correlates strongly with immunosuppressive tumor microenvironments (TMEs) across multiple cancer types. This association may stem from CDC6’s potential regulatory crosstalk with immune checkpoint pathways—a plausible mechanistic link to tumor immune evasion. However, further experimental validation is essential to confirm CDC6’s immune-regulatory role, particularly its interactions with tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) and other key immune cell populations. Our pan-cancer analysis further reveals that CDC6 expression significantly correlates with immune-related gene signatures, as well as established immunomodulatory biomarkers, including tumor mutational burden (TMB), microsatellite instability (MSI) and neoantigen load, all of which critically influence tumor immunogenicity and immunotherapy response.

Moreover, comprehensive profiling of CDC6’s genomic alterations—including mutational patterns, copy number variations and epigenetic modifications—highlights its potential as a multifaceted immunological regulator. The convergence of these genomic aberrations with CDC6’s immunomodulatory effects suggest a dual mechanism of action: driving tumor proliferation while simultaneously shaping immune resistance. Collectively, these findings position CDC6 not only as a prognostic biomarker but also as a promising predictive indicator and therapeutic target in the context of cancer immunotherapy.

Given its widespread dysregulation across cancers and dual role in tumor progression and immune modulation, CDC6 represents a compelling therapeutic target, particularly for immunotherapy-resistant malignancies. Mechanistic studies are urgently needed to dissect its precise contributions to immune suppression—such as checkpoint pathway regulation or TME remodeling—which could unlock novel combinatorial strategies. Therapeutically, targeting CDC6 through small-molecule inhibitors or epigenetic modulators may synergize with existing immunotherapies by simultaneously disrupting its oncogenic functions and reversing immune evasion. This approach holds strong potential for CDC6-driven cancers, particularly aggressive subtypes like cutaneous melanoma (SKCM). Clinically, CDC6’s strong association with key immune biomarkers (TMB, MSI, neoantigens) further positions it as a potential predictive biomarker for patient stratification, enabling precision immunotherapy selection. Together, our results highlight the potential of CDC6 as a key regulator of immune cell infiltration and its possible involvement in mediating tumor immune responses.

While our study establishes CDC6 as a pan-cancer oncogene with significant prognostic and functional relevance, certain mechanistic details remain unresolved. Notably, evidence suggests CDC6 may engage with chromatin remodeling complexes or modulate histone methylation marks to drive tumorigenesis. Additionally, CDC6 appears to influence tumorigenesis through epigenetic regulation, including potential effects on histone acetylation. Although CDC6 is primarily recognized for its role in DNA replication licensing, recent studies demonstrate that replication factors can interact with chromatin-modifying enzymes. For instance, Cdc6 exhibits oncogenic activity by silencing the INK4/ARF tumor suppressor locus via HDAC1-mediated histone deacetylation and DNMT1-dependent DNA methylation, thereby promoting cell cycle dysregulation and tumorigenesis. While our current study has validated CDC6’s role in melanoma cell proliferation and migration, future investigations should employ epigenetic profiling in CDC6-depleted models to comprehensively examine its chromatin remodeling interactions and dual regulation of histone acetylation/methylation across malignancies.

Conclusion

In conclusion, our integrative analysis establishes CDC6 as a critical oncogenic driver with prognostic, immunological, and therapeutic significance across cancers. Future preclinical and clinical studies should validate its utility as a biomarker and explore targeted interventions to improve patient outcomes.
