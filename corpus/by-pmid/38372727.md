# Cadonilimab Combined with Chemotherapy with or without Bevacizumab as First-Line Treatment in Recurrent or Metastatic Cervical Cancer (COMPASSION-13): A Phase 2 Study.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) have been a potential treatment option for patients with cervical cancer in several clinical studies. We investigated the safety and efficacy of cadonilimab, a bispecific antibody targeting PD-1 and CTLA-4, plus standard therapy for the first-line treatment of R/M CC (recurrent and/or metastatic cervical cancer).

**PATIENTS AND METHODS**: Eligible patients were assigned to 3 cohorts: cohort A-15 (cadonilimab 15 mg/kg every 3 weeks (Q3W) plus chemotherapy), cohort A-10 (cadonilimb 10 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy), and cohort B-10 (cadonilimab 10 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy and bevacizumab). They received the corresponding treatments until disease progression, unacceptable toxicity, withdrawal of consent, or investigator decision. The primary objective was safety; the secondary endpoints included objective overall response (ORR), duration of response, disease control rate, progression-free survival, and overall survival. This study is registered with ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT04868708).

**RESULTS**: As of February 13, 2023, treatment-related adverse events (TRAE) occurred in 45 (100.0%) patients. Grade ≥3 TRAEs were reported in 33 (73.3%) patients. Immune-related adverse events (irAE) occurred in 29 (64.4%) patients and grade ≥3 irAEs were observed in 9 (20.0%) patients. Seven (15.6%) of 45 patients permanently discontinued cadonilimab treatment due to TRAEs. One death due to hemorrhagic shock occurred in cohort B-10. Among 44 patients who underwent at least one post-baseline tumor assessment, the ORR was 66.7% in cohort A-15, 68.8% in cohort A-10, 92.3% in cohort B-10, and 79.3% in cohorts A-10 and B-10 combined.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Cadonilimab combined with standard therapy was acceptable, with encouraging antitumor activity in patients with R/M CC.

Translational Relevance

The standard first-line therapy for cervical cancer is a combination of chemotherapy (cisplatin or carboplatin plus paclitaxel) and bevacizumab, and based on the KEYNOTE-826 study, pembrolizumab has been included in the standard first-line treatment in patients with PD-L1–positive cervical cancer. Introduction

Cervical cancer is a prevalent disease that is ranked as the fourth most frequently diagnosed cancer and the fourth leading cause of cancer-related deaths in women worldwide, with an estimated 604,000 new cases and 342,000 deaths reported in 2020. Of cervical cancer patients, 15%–20% will experience recurrence with a five-year overall survival (OS) rate of only 5%. In 2014, the FDA approved bevacizumab for advanced cervical cancer on the basis of improved OS in the Gynecologic Oncology Group (GOG) 240 trial. According to the National Comprehensive Cancer Network Categories of Preference, the standard first-line therapy for cervical cancer is a combination of chemotherapy (cisplatin or carboplatin plus paclitaxel) and bevacizumab. Recently, pembrolizumab combined with chemotherapy, with or without bevacizumab, is the recommended first-line standard therapy for patients with programmed death ligand 1 (PD-L1)–positive cervical cancer, defined as a combined positive score (CPS) of ≥1 based on the KEYNOTE-826 study.

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) have been a potential treatment option for patients with cervical cancer in several clinical studies. The KEYNOTE-158 trial of pembrolizumab monotherapy showed an objective response rate (ORR) of 12.2% and 14.6% in all patients and in PD-L1–positive patients, respectively, whereas no response was observed in patients with PD-L1–negative tumors. In a phase 3 study of cemiplimab monotherapy, the ORR was 18.3% and mOS was 13.9 months in patients with PD-L1 expression greater than 1%, whereas the ORR was 11.4% and median OS (mOS) was 7.7 months in patients with PD-L1 expression of less than 1%. These results highlight the potential benefits of anti–PD-1 monotherapy in the treatment of cervical cancer, particularly in patients with PD-L1–positive cervical cancer. However, these benefits were limited for patients with PD-L1–negative cervical cancer. The combination of ICIs targeting programmed death-1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4) has exhibited synergistic antitumor activity in preclinical and clinical studies. In a phase 2 clinical trial of balstilimab (anti–PD-1) and zalifrelimab (anti–CTLA-4) as a second-line treatment in patients with recurrent and/or metastatic cervical cancer (R/M CC), the confirmed ORR was 25.6%, which comprised 10 complete responders and 22 partial responders. In addition, CheckMate-358 demonstrated that nivolumab in combination with ipilimumab produced clinically meaningful and durable responses in patients with R/M CC. These data exhibited the promising clinical activity of dual anti–PD-1/CTLA-4 therapy in patients with R/M CC. However, the combination has also been associated with an increased incidence of adverse events (AE) compared with monotherapy as reported in previous studies. New strategies of combined ICIs with mitigated AEs in R/M CC are worth exploring, especially in patients with PD-L1–negative cervical cancer.

Cadonilimab (AK104) is a bi-specific antibody targeting PD-1 and CTLA-4 with crystalline fragments; it mutates to eliminate Fc receptor and complement-mediated cytotoxic effects. In a phase 2 study (NCT04380805) cadonilimab had demonstrated promising efficacy and safety as a monotherapy in patients with R/M CC who had previously failed first-line or subsequent treatments. The ORR was 33.0% and median progression-free survival (PFS) was 3.75 months, and based on these results cadonilimab was approved by China's National Medical Products Administration (NMPA) for R/M CC as a second-line treatment in June 2022.

In this phase 2 study, we aimed to assess the safety and preliminary efficacy of cadonilimab plus chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab as a first-line treatment for patients with R/M CC.

Patients and Methods

Study design and participants

This multicenter, open-label, phase 2 trial enrolled patients from 6 clinical sites in China. This study included 3 cohorts: cohort A (cadonilimab plus chemotherapy as first-line therapy), including cohort A-15 [cadonilimab 15 mg/kg every 3 weeks (Q3W) plus chemotherapy], cohort A-10 (cadonilimab 10 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy); cohort B (cadonilimab 10 mg/kg plus chemotherapy and bevacizumab as a first-line therapy); and cohort C (cadonilimab monotherapy as a second-line or multi-line treatment). As cadonilimab was approved by NMPA for R/M CC as second-line treatment in June 2022, the enrollment of cohort C preset was prematurely terminated; in this article, we only reported the results from cohorts A and B.

Patients were eligible for enrollment if they met the following criteria: females of ages 18 to 75 years at the time of signing the informed consent form (ICF); Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status (PS) score of 0 or 1; expected survival of ≥3 months; recurrent or metastatic cervical cancer with squamous cell, adenocarcinoma, or adenosquamous histology, and not appropriate for radical surgical resection and/or radical chemoradiotherapy; at least one measurable lesion per RECIST (version1.1); and acceptable renal and liver function. The main exclusion criteria included: They had received ICIs (i.e., anti–PD-1 antibodies, anti–PD-L1 antibodies, and anti–CTLA-4 antibodies.), immune checkpoint agonists (i.e., ICOS, CD40, CD137, GITR, and Ox40), and immune cell therapy. For cohort B, subjects should be excluded if they are receiving or have received long-term treatment with nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, antiplatelet drugs or anticoagulants within 2 weeks before the first dose of investigational product; have uncontrolled hypertension; have any arteriovenous thromboembolic events within 6 months before the first dose; have received major surgery within 4 weeks before the first dose; and have urine protein ≥2 + and 24-hour urine protein >1 g/24 hours.

The protocol was approved by an independent ethics committee or Institutional Review Board before initiation. The study was done in accordance with the protocol, the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practice. All patients provided written informed consent before enrollment.

Procedures

Patients were assigned to receive 15 or 10 mg/kg cadonilimab intravenously Q3W in combination with chemotherapy, consisting of paclitaxel (175 mg/m2 intravenously Q3W) and cisplatin (50 mg/m2 intravenously Q3W) or carboplatin (area under the curve 5 mg/mL/min intravenously Q3W), with or without bevacizumab (15 mg/kg intravenously Q3W). Patients were allocated to 3 cohorts based on the physician's discretion: cohort A-15 (cadonilimab 15 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy), cohort A-10 (cadonilimab 10 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy), and cohort B-10 (cadonilimab 10 mg/kg Q3W plus chemotherapy and bevacizumab). Dose modifications were not permitted for cadonilimab or bevacizumab. Patients were permitted to continue cadonilimab ± bevacizumab as maintenance treatment if appropriate, until disease progression, unacceptable AEs, or withdrawal of consent. The allowed longest duration of cadonilimab treatment was 24 months. There was no limit to the maximum duration of chemotherapy, until disease progression, unacceptable AEs or physician's discretion.

Tumor assessment by enhanced CT or MRI scan was done at screening (within 28 days before cycle 1) and every 6 weeks for the first 54 weeks, and every 12 weeks thereafter, until disease progression, a new anticancer therapy, withdrawal of consent, death or study closure, whichever occurred first. Tumor responses were assessed by investigators according to RECIST1.1. Once patients achieved complete response, they were allowed to receive maintenance treatment, if appropriate, until disease progression, unacceptable toxicity or withdrawal of informed consent. After treatment discontinuation, patients were contacted at 30 and 90 days and every 3 months to assess survival during the follow-up period.

AEs were monitored throughout the study and for 30 days after the last dose of study treatment, and 90 days for serious adverse events (SAE) and immune-related adverse events (irAE). All AEs were assessed and graded according to the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for AEs, version 5·0 (CTCAE v5·0). IrAEs were programmatically established according to The International Dictionary of Medical Terms (MedDRA).

The expression of PD-L1 in tumor tissues was assessed by IHC using Monoclonal Mouse Anti-Human PD-L1 Clone 22C3 (Dako Denmark ApS) at the central laboratory, and measured by CPS, defined as the number of PD-L1–positive cells (tumor cells, lymphocytes, and macrophages) divided by the total number of tumor cells×100; a minimum of 100 viable tumor cells must have been present for the specimen to be considered evaluable.

Outcomes

The primary objective of this study was to evaluate the safety, including the incidence and severity of AEs, as well as abnormal laboratory results of clinical significance. Secondary endpoints included ORR, duration of response (DoR), disease control rate (DCR), PFS, and OS. The ORR was defined as the proportion of confirmed complete response (CR) and partial response (PR). DoR was defined as the duration between the first-documented confirmed CR or PR and the first-documented disease progression by the investigators or death from any cause, whichever occurred first. DCR was defined as the proportion of patients with the best overall response (BOR) of confirmed CR, PR, and stable disease. PFS was defined as the duration between the first dose of study drug and the first-documented disease progression by the investigators or death from any cause, whichever occurred first on the basis of the RECIST1.1. OS was defined as the duration between the first dose of study drug and death from any cause. Other secondary endpoints of immunogenicity and pharmacokinetics were not reported here.

Statistical analysis

Safety analyses were performed using the safety analysis set according to the actual treatment received by the subjects. AEs were coded using the MedDRA Version 23.0 (or higher) and graded according to the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for AEs (NCI CTCAE) Version 5.0. Treatment-emergent adverse events (TEAE) were defined as AEs that occurred during treatment [during or after the first dose of study drugs up to 30 days (for non-SAEs) or 90 days (for SAEs) after the discontinuation of the study drugs or initiation of other antitumor therapy, whichever occurred first]; or AEs that occurred at baseline but had recovered before treatment and re-emerged during the treatment period; or persistent AEs with severe deterioration after treatment relative to that before treatment. An irAE was defined as an AE that was associated with drug exposure and consistent with an immune-mediated mechanism of action, without a clear alternate etiology. Treatment-emergent irAEs, regardless of serious or non-serious irAEs, would be reported until 90 days after the last dose date (whether other antitumor therapy has been initiated). Treatment-related TEAEs (TRAEs) were defined as TEAEs that are “definitely related,” “probably related” or “possibly related” to treatment. TEAEs with missing relationship would be summarized as drug-related.

The ORR, DCR, and DoR were assessed in all patients who had at least one tumor assessment result. PFS and OS were assessed on the basis of the safety analysis set, defined as all patients who received any dose of cadonilimab. Full analysis set was defined as all patients who received at least one dose of cadonilimab and had a measurable lesion at baseline per RECIST version1.1. All efficacy endpoints were analyzed on the basis of PD-L1 expression subgroups, CPS < 1, CPS ≥ 1, and CPS ≥ 10 population were summarized, respectively. Safety was assessed on the basis of safety analysis set, according to the actual treatment received by the patients. The 95% exact two-sided confidence interval of the objective response rate was estimated using a Clopper–Pearson method. Median duration of time-to-event (DoR, PFS, and OS) will be estimated by Kaplan–Meier and Brookmeyer–Crowley methods for 95% confidence interval (CI). On the basis of the patients who had at least one tumor assessment result, the percentage change of target lesions from baseline to the end of the study was presented graphically using a spider plot; the best percentage change of target lesions was presented graphically using a waterfall plot; the tumor assessment status for individual subjects was presented graphically using a swimmer plot. All statistical analyses were done with SAS (version 9.4). The trial is registered with ClinicalTrials.gov, number NCT04868708.

Data availability

The data generated in this study are available upon reasonable request from the corresponding author.

Results

Patients

Between April 26, 2021, and November 5, 2021, a total of 45 patients were enrolled and received at least one dose of cadonilimab, 15 in cohort A-15, 16 in cohort A-10, and 14 in cohort B-10. On the basis of the safety and preliminary efficacy of this study, a phase 3 study began enrollment in September 2021. Given that cohort B-10 had the same population and same treatment regimen as the phase 3 study, and a sample size of 14 in cohort B was acceptable for safety exploration, the enrollment in cohort B was discontinued with the last patient enrolled on November 5, 2021.

At data cutoff (February 13, 2023), the median duration of follow-up was 18.30 months (95% CI, 15.9–19.4) in cohort A-10, 20.24 months (95% CI, 18.7–20.8) in cohort A-15, and 15.01 months (95% CI, 12.7–17.7) in cohort B-10. For all patients, the median age was 52.9 years (range, 33–71 years); 26 (57.8%) patients had ECOG PS of 1, and 40 (88.9%) patients were diagnosed with squamous cell carcinoma. Seventeen (37.8%) patients had PD-L1 CPS < 1, 15 (33.3%) had PD-L1 CPS ≥ 1 and <10, and 13 (28.9%) had PD-L1 CPS ≥ 10. The baseline characteristics of the patients are listed in Table 1.

Safety and AEs

All 45 patients were included in the safety analysis. The median drug-exposure time of cadonilimab was 10.1 cycles (7.0 months) in cohort A-15, 10.5 cycles (7.2 months) in cohort A-10, and 10.3 cycles (7.1 months) in cohort B-10. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAE) occurred in 45 (100.0%) patients, with anemia (31, 68.9%), decreased white blood cell count (30, 66.7%), decreased neutrophil count (17, 37.8%), and decreased appetite (17, 37.8%) being the most common. Grade ≥3 TRAEs were reported in 33 (73.3%) patients, with anemia (10, 22.2%), decreased neutrophil count (9, 20.0%), and decreased white blood cell count (7, 15.6%) being the most common. Cadonilimab-related grade ≥3 TRAEs were 55.6% and 50.0% in all populations and in cohort B-10. Overview of all AEs is presented in Table 2.

IrAEs occurred in 29 (64.4%) patients, the most common of which were rash (8, 17.8%), hypothyroidism (7, 15.6%), and increased alanine aminotransferase (5, 11.1%). Grade ≥3 irAEs were observed in 9 (20.0%) patients, including rash (2, 4.4%), drug eruption (1, 2.2%), immune-mediated dermatitis (1, 2.2%), increased blood creatinine (1, 2.2%), decreased platelet count (1, 2.2%), hypothyroidism (1, 2.2%), hypersensitivity (1, 2.2%), and immune system disorder (1, 2.2%). Most grade ≥3 irAEs resolved, with skin toxicity in 3 patients and hypersensitivity in 1 patient resolving within 1 week, rash in 1 patient resolving at the 8th week, immune system disorder in 1 patient resolving after 6 weeks, and decreased platelet count in 1 patient persisting for 4 months before resolving. Elevated blood creatinine in 1 patient was resolved, and hypothyroidism in 1 patient was alleviated after receiving levothyroxine sodium. Seven (15.6%) of 45 patients permanently discontinued cadonilimab treatment due to TRAEs, including hypoesthesia, hypersensitivity and increased blood creatinine, radiotherapy proctitis, immune system disorder, enteritis and drug eruption. Treatment-related serious adverse events (TRSAE) occurred in 23 (51.1%) patients. Most TRSAEs were resolved, with the exception of one death due to hemorrhagic shock that occurred in cohort B-10, which was deemed to be associated with bevacizumab by investigators. A summary of AEs is listed in Table 3, including TRAEs with an incidence of ≥20% and irAEs that occurred in ≥2 cases. A summary of TRSAEs is provided in Supplementary Table S1.

Efficacy

A total of 44 patients had at least one post-baseline tumor assessment; the ORR was 66.7% (10/15; 95% CI, 38.4–88.2) with 3 CR and 7 PR in cohort A-15, 68.8% (11/16; 95% CI, 41.3–89.0) with 1 CR and 10 PR in cohort A-10, 92.3% (12/13; 95% CI, 64.0–99.8) with 1 CR and 11 PR in cohort B-10, and 79.3% (23/29; 95% CI, 60.3–92.0) with 2 CR and 21 PR in cohort A-10 + cohort B-10, and it appeared that the ORR in cohort B-10 was higher than in cohorts A-15 and A-10. A total of 28 (63.6%) patients achieved tumor burden reduction of more than 50.0%. The DoR was 7.62 months (95% CI, 2.86–NE) in cohort A-10, and it was not reached in cohorts A-15 and B-10. The DCR was 100.0% in all 3 cohorts. On the basis of all 44 patients, the ORR was 66.7% (8/12), 86.7% (13/15), and 70.6% (12/17) in patients with PD-L1 CPS ≥10, 1 ≤ CPS < 10, and CPS < 1, respectively, which may suggest that patients with PD-L1 CPS < 1 could also benefit from cadonilimab plus standard therapy. The summary of confirmed BOR is shown in Supplementary Table S2. Representativeness of study participants is shown in Supplementary Table S3. A waterfall plot of best percentage change from baseline in sum of diameters with best overall confirmed response (N = 44) is presented in Fig. 1; and a swim-lane plot and spider plot of cohorts A-15 and A-10 are presented in Fig. 2A–D. The typical CT scans of patients are presented in Supplementary Fig. S1A–S1C.

At the cutoff date, in cohort A-15, 53.3% of patients experienced disease progression or death, the mPFS was 11.10 months (95% CI, 5.5–NE), and the estimated 12-month PFS rate was 49.0% (95% CI, 21.6–71.7). The mOS was not reached in cohort A-15, and the estimated 12-month OS rate was 93.3% (95% CI, 61.3–99.0). In cohort A-10, 68.8% of patients experienced PFS events, the mPFS was 7.06 months (95% CI, 4.1–NE), and the estimated 12-month PFS rate was 37.5% (95% CI, 15.4–59.8). The mOS was not reached in cohort A-10, and the estimated 12-month OS rate was 87.5% (95% CI, 58.6–96.7). In cohort B-10, the mPFS and mOS were all not reached. The Kaplan–Meier curves of PFS in cohorts A-15 and A-10 are presented in Supplementary Fig. S2A and S2B, respectively.

Discussion

This article reported the preliminary findings of a phase 2 clinical trial, evaluating cadonilimab in combination with chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab in patients with R/M CC. The safety profile of cadonilimab was acceptable and manageable, and no new safety signals were observed. And 63.6% patients achieved tumor shrinkage of more than 50.0%, suggesting the promising efficacy of this regimen in first-line treatment of cervical cancer.

The safety profile of cadonilimab plus chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab was generally consistent with the known toxic effects of each single agent. In our study, the incidence of grade ≥3 TRAEs was 73.3% in all populations, which was similar to that in the KEYNOTE-826 study; grade 3 to 5 TRAEs occurred in 68.4% of patients in the pembrolizumab group. In addition, the incidence of grade ≥3 TRAEs was the highest in cohort B-10 (12, 85.7%), which may be attributed to the combination involving multiple agents. It has been noted that the combination of anti–PD-1 and anti–CTLA-4 can increase immune-related toxicity compared with the anti–PD-1 monotherapy. In previous studies, irAEs occurred in 44.5% and 32.9% of patients with cervical cancer treated with balstilimab plus zalifrelimab and balstilimab monotherapy, respectively. In the KEYNOTE-826 study in the pembrolizumab group, the incidence of irAEs was 33.9% and grade ≥3 irAEs was 11.4%. In our study, the incidences of irAEs (29, 64.4%) were high; most of them were mild and moderate, and 9 patients (20.0%) experienced grade ≥3 irAEs and most of them recovered. It appeared that the incidences of irAEs (11, 73.3%) and grade ≥3 irAEs (4, 26.7%) were the highest in cohort A-15, whereas in cohorts A-10 and B-10, the incidences of grade ≥3 irAEs were 18.8% and 14.3%, respectively. The high incidence of irAEs may be attributed to the high dose of cadonilimab used in cohort A-15; attention should be paid to safety when a high dose is administered. TRSAEs occurred in 23 (51.1%) patients, which was comparable with 49.8% in the KEYNOTE-826 study. One death due to hemorrhagic shock occurred in cohort B-10, which was deemed to be associated with bevacizumab by investigators. The patient has not received prior radiotherapy or chemotherapy treatment. Death occurred 9 days after one cycle of combination treatment.

Platinum–paclitaxel in combination with bevacizumab has been established as the standard treatment regimen for R/M CC, yielding an ORR of 55.8% to 64.5% and mPFS ranging from 9.2 to 10.8 months, and an mOS ranging from 16.4 to 19.1 months. However, in patients who received platinum–paclitaxel regimens without bevacizumab, ORR ranged from 58.8% to 62.6% and mPFS ranged from 4.8 to 7.9 months in the JCOG0505 study, and mOS was 9.7 in the GOG-169 study and 13.3 months in the GOG-240 study, as previously reported. In the KEYNOTE-826 study, in the subgroup analysis patients who received pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy with bevacizumab achieved an ORR of 75.5%, whereas in patients who only received pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy, the ORR was 49.1%. In our study, the combination of cadonilimab with chemotherapy and bevacizuamb achieved an ORR of 92.3% in cohort B-10, which was much higher (92.3% vs. 75.5%) than that in the KEYNOTE-826 study; the ORRs were also higher (66.7% and 68.8% vs. 49.1%) even without bevacizumab in cohorts A-15 and A-10. Moreover, the mPFS of patients who received cadonilimab (15 and 10 mg/kg) plus chemotherapy without bevacizumab was 11.10 and 7.06 months. In the KEYNOTE-826 study, the mPFS was 6.3 months in patients who received pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy without bevacizumab, which was not significantly different from chemotherapy alone. These findings suggest that the addition of cadonilimab to standard therapy could improve the efficacy, even without bevacizumab; cadonilimab plus chemotherapy could provide promising efficacy to patients with R/M CC. Notably, patients with PD-L1 CPS < 1 also demonstrated an impressive response rate of 60.0% (3/5) in cohort A-15, 62.5% (5/8) in cohort A-10, and 100.0% (4/4) in cohort B-10, suggesting that cadonilimab may provide clinical benefits for patients with PD-L1–negative cervical cancer.

Our study is limited by non-randomized design and small sample size. Nonetheless, cadonimilab combined with chemotherapy with or without bevacizumab showed a manageable safety profile and encouraging antitumor activity. A phase 3 confirmatory clinical trial is ongoing (NCT04982237).
