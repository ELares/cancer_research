Toward a better pan-tumor predictive signature for unleashing precision immuno-oncology

Abstract

While immune checkpoint inhibition (ICI) has revolutionized therapy across solid tumors, resistance remains an issue. Programmed death ligand-1 immunohistochemistry has limited clinical utility, whereas tumor mutation burden and microsatellite instability are only valuable for a minority of patients and leave room for improvement. Multiomic gene signatures have enhanced prediction of immune response by incorporating interferon-gamma signaling, T-cell dysfunction and exhaustion genes, and myeloid signatures.  Undoubtedly, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) have made an indelible impact on how we treat many cancers. Some patients with metastatic cancers, such as melanoma or lung cancer, that had poor prognosis and limited treatment options can now hope for long-term disease control or even remission. Despite the promise of immune checkpoint inhibition, many patients do not respond to treatment and/or suffer severe immune adverse events. One analysis found that in the USA found that while more than half of the patients with advanced cancer were eligible for ICIs, only approximately 20% responded to this treatment. Therefore, predictive and prognostic biomarkers are necessary to enhance the therapeutic index and fully unlock the potential of ICIs, ensuring that these treatments are given to the patients most likely to benefit.

The first biomarker that attempted to predict ICI response was programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) immunohistochemistry (IHC), given that clinically used ICI regimens target the programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1)/PD-L1 interaction. Despite approval of ICIs in a few specific malignancies and settings based on PD-L1 IHC, PD-1/L1 inhibitors have generally demonstrated activity regardless of PD-L1 protein expression. Indeed, this biomarker has ultimately been disappointing as a predictive biomarker across most malignancies, with one analysis estimating that it was only predictive in 28.9% of cases where approved for clinical use. This test was fraught with pathologic conundrums, including differing results based on antibody used and whether to count PD-L1 expression in the stroma versus restricting evaluation to tumor cells. Microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) and tumor mutation burden (TMB)-high cancers guided by genomics assays have tissue-agnostic approvals for specific PD-1 inhibitors (pembrolizumab, dostarlimab); biomarker-guided combination ipilimumab plus nivolumab is approved for MSI-H colorectal cancer. Nonetheless, determining an optimal cutpoint between responders and non-responders appears to vary between different malignancies. Furthermore, the existence of immunoresponsive cancers that typically harbor low TMB, such as renal cell carcinoma and Merkel cell carcinoma, illustrates the importance of tumor microenvironmental (TME) factors and host genetics in determining sensitivity to ICIs. Currently, there are no approvals tied to transcriptomic profiling.

Therefore, novel and robust biomarkers are necessary. In Zander et al, one such novel biomarker, a pan-tumor Immune Profile Score (IPS), is introduced. This is a “multi-omic” assay developed from DNA and RNA sequencing. A machine learning model selected and weighted the 12 features that were most predictive of ICI response on a training set of more than 1,000 patients across 16 malignancies. These features include familiar markers, such as TMB and expression of eight genes, namely CD74, CD274, CD276, CXCL9, IDO1, PDCD1LG2, SPP1, and TNFRSF5. Three gene signatures were included as well, a granulocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cell signature, a tumor-intrinsic immune resistance signature derived from single-cell RNA sequencing, and a meta-analysis-based signature incorporating 105 genes. The latter two signatures were developed specifically for this study. Biological processes covered by this model include immune inflammation, T-cell exhaustion/inhibition, neoantigen burden, and tumor immune resistance.

Using a cut point of below the 55th percentile of scores in the training cohort as IPS-low (IPS-L) and above the 60th percentile as IPS-high (IPS-H), this model was validated on a cohort of 1,600 patients from a real-world database of patients with solid tumor cancers who were treated with ICI. Overall, the prevalence of IPS-H was 28.64%, with the greatest prevalence found in lung adenocarcinoma, clear cell renal cell carcinoma, and melanoma. IPS was found to be prognostic for overall survival including non-ICI settings or from initiation of ICI in multiple settings, including frontline use, refractory tumors, and in combinations with an overall HR of 0.45 (95% CI 0.4 to 0.52) between IPS-H and IPS-L. Therefore, the true predictive versus prognostic utility requires further evaluation. Furthermore, the data were derived from a retrospective, although large, study of patients with various malignancies receiving ICI alone or combined with other agents in the first or salvage line of therapy. IPS was shown to enhance prognostic value of preexisting tests, including PD-L1 score, MSI, and TMB, for overall survival, with patients who were IPS-H/TMB-high and IPS-H/MSI-H exhibiting the best outcomes. A total of 14.1% of patients were IPS-H/TMB-low, a population that would be predicted to derive benefit from ICI based on this biomarker.

Other previously reported studies have employed gene expression alone or in combination with genomics. An interferon-gamma (IFN-γ)-based messenger RNA (mRNA) signature using NanoString technology consisting of an 18-gene signature to define a T cell-inflamed gene expression profile was found to be more predictive of response across solid tumors than PD-L1 expression. TMB, PD-L1 protein expression and immune gene expression profiling were independently predictive of response to pembrolizumab across multiple solid tumors in another study and these demonstrated low correlation, suggesting that they capture orthogonal determinants of response. These data underscore the complexity of tumor biology impacting outcomes with ICIs.

Similarly, the tumor immune dysfunction and exclusion (TIDE) signatures were developed to model the TME and suppression of immune response. These two genome-wide expression signatures model T-cell dysfunction and T-cell exclusion, predicting immune evasion. These signatures predicted response across malignancies to anti-PD-1 and anti-cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 therapies and were superior to PD-L1, IFN-γ, and other available immune predictors.

Prediction of ICI response can model the balance between adaptive immunity and protumorigenic inflammation. The adaptive Immune:pro-tumorigenic Inflammation Ratio (2IR) immune response score, based off bulk mRNA sequencing from a landmark bladder cancer clinical trial, is a weighted ratio between expression of adaptive immune genes associated with response to ICI and myeloid inflammatory immune genes associated with ICI resistance. High 2IR scores were associated with ICI response and correlated with presence of tertiary lymphoid structures. This score was independently found to be prognostic for overall survival and predictive for time on treatment with pembrolizumab in a real-world cohort of patients with bladder cancer treated with ICI.

Single-cell RNA sequencing can further define the TME, evaluating the role of individual cells and enabling detection of rare populations. A myeloid single cell Immune: protumorigenic Inflammation Ratio (Msc2IR score) further found that T cells and B cells drove adaptive immune response, while macrophages had prominent protumorigenic inflammation. Monocyte populations with low Msc2IR score were also detected in the peripheral blood of patients with ICI-resistant metastatic urothelial carcinoma. Another model based on single-cell RNA sequencing nominated a pan-cancer signature of cancer stem cells (Stem.sig) to predict immunotherapy response. The Stem.sig model was prognostic for overall survival from the start of ICI and had better performance than other established predictive models across multiple cancers. Single-cell RNA sequencing can also be taken one step further to analyze changes in cells during ICI treatment. The scRNA-seq-based Changed and Unchanged cell Recognition during immunotherapy (scCURE) assay uses this methodology to not only establish baseline characteristics that predict ICI response but also to differentiate change and unchanged cells in ICI responders and non-responders to better understand the underlying mechanisms of response. For example, depolarization of M2 macrophages during treatment led to CD8+ T-cell activation in patients who responded. These assays underlie the novel technologies that are enhancing understanding of the biologic mechanisms underpinning ICI resistance.

The IPS score introduced in Zander et al builds on the foundations of prior ICI response prediction scores (figure 1). This score incorporates immune response genes from the IFN-γ signaling, tumor-intrinsic immune resistance signatures, similar to TIDE, and myeloid signature, similar to the 2IR and Msc2IR. Moreover, this adopts single-cell RNA sequencing, similar to Stem.sig and scCURE assays. The IPS score is reproducible and will be available from Tempus, allowing for ease of distribution. Therefore, it has considerable potential for developing precision immuno-oncology but will require further prospective validation of its prognostic and/or predictive impact.

Nonetheless, given durable survival of approximately 25% IPS-L patients, the performance leaves substantial room for improvement. While a pan-tumor predictive panel is highly desirable, calibration of such pan-tumor scores to optimally adapt to specific malignancies is likely warranted. The incorporation of readily available clinical factors (eg, sites of metastasis) and host genetics, which may not only impact efficacy but also predict immune adverse events, is warranted to optimize the therapeutic index. Additionally, biomarkers to predict primary refractory disease and longitudinally assess tumor response are a huge unmet need, which will allow investigating novel strategies from the outset in such patients. Indeed, the avoidance of futile immunotherapy associated with primary refractory disease and/or life-threatening immune adverse events is essential. Utilization of matched or tumor-agnostic non-invasive assessments, such as liquid biopsies, circulating tumor cell assays, or organoid models, could further enhance these immune prediction models and predict early resistance. One assay, Liquid Biomarker of Immunotherapy Outcomes (LiBIO), incorporated serial single-cell transcriptomics and T/B-cell receptor analyses on circulating lymphocytes to successfully identify early on-treatment dynamics to differentiate ICI responders from non-responders in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma.

In conclusion, the incorporation of orthogonal information from non-invasive biomarkers, microbiome, computational pathology, spatial transcriptomics, epigenetics, proteomics and radiomics may further facilitate precision medicine. Moreover, as additional novel immunotherapies are adopted, including T-cell engagers, chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy, and neoantigen-based vaccines, new prognostic and predictive scores may be needed.
