# Immunotherapy in clear cell renal cell carcinoma: current Status, novel Strategies, and future perspectives.

## Abstract

Clear cell renal cell carcinoma (ccRCC) remains a major clinical challenge, with high rates of recurrence and limited long-term survival despite surgical resection and VEGF-targeted therapy. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs)-targeting PD-1, PD-L1, and CTLA-4-have revolutionized first-line systemic treatment, particularly in combination with VEGF tyrosine kinase inhibitors or as dual ICI regimens. However, primary and acquired resistance, immune-related adverse events (irAEs), and heterogeneous treatment responses limit the durability of benefit in many patients. This review aims to address a central question: how can immunotherapy for ccRCC evolve from incremental survival extension to durable, potentially curative control? We highlight emerging strategies-including next-generation checkpoint inhibitors (LAG-3, TIM-3, TIGIT), bispecific T cell engagers, cytokine-based agents, CAR-T and TCR-T therapies, and cancer vaccines-designed to enhance and sustain anti-tumor immunity. In parallel, we examine the role of multi-omic and spatial biomarkers, such as PBRM1 mutations, interferon-γ signatures, single-cell spatial atlases, and gut microbiome profiles, in refining patient selection and predicting therapeutic outcomes. This review uniquely integrates mechanistic insights with translational advances, providing a forward-looking synthesis of precision immunotherapy in ccRCC. We also emphasize rational combination strategies, biomarker-guided personalization, and irAE management as key priorities to overcome resistance and improve long-term outcomes.

Introduction

Clear cell renal cell carcinoma is the most prevalent histologic subtype of renal cell carcinoma (RCC), accounting for approximately 70–80% of all RCC cases. In recent years, the global incidence of ccRCC has continued to rise, posing a significant public health challenge. According to epidemiological data, an estimated 81,800 new RCC cases and 14,890 RCC-related deaths occurred in the United States in 2023. Despite advances in surgical techniques, approximately 20–30% of patients experience disease recurrence or metastasis after nephrectomy, highlighting the urgent need for effective systemic therapies.

At the molecular level, ccRCC is frequently driven by inactivation of the von Hippel–Lindau (VHL) tumor suppressor gene, resulting in the accumulation of hypoxia-inducible factors (HIFs) and subsequent activation of pro-angiogenic pathways, most notably those involving vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Targeted therapies such as VEGF receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitors (VEGFR-TKIs) have long constituted the standard of care, demonstrating moderate survival benefits. Nonetheless, their clinical utility is limited by the development of therapeutic resistance and significant treatment-related toxicities. Over the past decade, ICIs have substantially reshaped the therapeutic landscape for advanced ccRCC. Inhibitors targeting programmed death-1 (PD-1), programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte–associated protein 4 (CTLA-4), whether administered as monotherapy or in combination with VEGFR-TKIs, have shown significant improvements in overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS) in multiple phase III clinical trials, establishing ICIs as a foundational component of first-line therapy for metastatic ccRCC. Despite these advances, several challenges remain. Primary and acquired resistance to ICIs is common, a substantial proportion of patients fail to respond to treatment, and the absence of reliable predictive biomarkers continues to hinder the development of personalized treatment approaches. These challenges are particularly pronounced in patients with favorable-risk disease, where achieving a balance between efficacy and toxicity remains a key clinical concern.

In response to these limitations, the field of immuno-oncology is rapidly evolving toward more precise and durable therapeutic strategies. A broad array of novel approaches is currently under investigation, including optimized ICI-based combination regimens, next-generation checkpoint inhibitors, bispecific antibodies, cytokine therapies, therapeutic cancer vaccines, adoptive T-cell transfer, CAR-T cell therapy, and interventions targeting the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. These strategies aim to enhance T-cell activation, improve tumor specificity, and overcome mechanisms of immune escape, with the overarching goal of achieving more durable clinical responses.

In this review, we adopt a problem-driven approach to examine the evolving landscape of immunotherapy in ccRCC, with particular focus on three distinct unresolved challenges: increasing the proportion of patients who achieve a meaningful response by overcoming primary and acquired resistance; improving the depth and durability of responses among those who do respond, as current median durations of benefit remain limited even in pivotal first-line trials; and developing robust, clinically applicable biomarkers to enable mechanism-based and truly personalized treatment. We first summarize the status of immune checkpoint inhibitor monotherapy, combination regimens, and adjuvant applications, critically appraising their efficacy, toxicity, and durability. We then explore emerging strategies—including novel immune checkpoints, bispecific antibodies, cytokine therapies, adoptive cell transfer, and immunometabolic modulation—framed around their potential to address these core challenges. Next, we analyze the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment and dissect mechanisms of resistance, integrating insights from spatial and single-cell technologies. Finally, we review advances in biomarker development—ranging from genomic alterations such as PBRM1 to transcriptional signatures and microbiome composition—and present our perspective on how these may guide a shift toward mechanism-based, individualized immunotherapy. By synthesizing current evidence through this lens, we aim to provide a thematic and analytical framework that informs both clinical decision-making and future research priorities.

Current status of immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy in CcRCC

In recent years, the widespread application of ICIs has led to a fundamental shift in the systemic treatment landscape of RCC. Monoclonal antibodies targeting PD-1, PD-L1 and CTLA-4 have demonstrated substantial clinical activity across various treatment settings, ranging from monotherapy and combination regimens to adjuvant therapy following nephrectomy. However, these clinical gains are often accompanied by significant challenges, including irAEs, limited durability of response in many patients, and complex mechanisms of resistance that continue to hinder treatment efficacy and long-term disease control.

Monotherapy

ICI monotherapy is primarily reserved for patients who have failed prior systemic therapy or those ineligible for combination regimens due to comorbidities or toxicity concerns. The phase III CheckMate 025 trial was a landmark study that demonstrated a significant OS benefit with nivolumab compared to everolimus in patients with previously treated metastatic RCC. Nivolumab significantly improved OS (HR 0.73; 98.5% CI, 0.57–0.93; P = 0.002) and showed a higher objective response rate (ORR) (25% vs. 5%; odds ratio (OR), 5.98; 95% CI, 3.68–9.72; P < 0.001). Common treatment-related adverse events included fatigue (33%), nausea (14%), and pruritus (14%). These results led to the approval of nivolumab by both the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) for second-line treatment of metastatic RCC.

The KEYNOTE-427 study evaluated pembrolizumab as first-line monotherapy in treatment-naïve patients. In Cohort A (ccRCC), pembrolizumab achieved an ORR of 36.4%, with a median duration of response (mDOR) of 18.9 months and an 18-month OS rate approaching 80%. Cohort B provided early evidence supporting the use of ICIs in non–ccRCC, with an ORR of 24.8%. Collectively, while monotherapy can induce durable responses in a subset of patients, overall response rates and depth of response remain inferior to those achieved with combination strategies. These findings suggest that while ICI monotherapy remains a valuable option for specific patient populations, particularly those who are frail or treatment-intolerant, it may be insufficient to achieve robust disease control in the broader metastatic ccRCC population.

Combination therapy

To overcome the limitations of monotherapy, combination approaches have been developed to synergistically enhance anti-tumor activity and broaden therapeutic benefit. Combination immunotherapy has become the mainstay for managing advanced RCC and is broadly categorized into two major approaches: dual ICI regimens and PD-1 inhibitor plus VEGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) combinations. Biologically, these combinations act through complementary mechanisms: dual immune checkpoint blockade (e.g., nivolumab plus ipilimumab) enhances T-cell priming and expands tumor-reactive clones, whereas PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor plus VEGFR-TKI regimens leverage VEGF-pathway inhibition to normalize tumor vasculature, improve T-cell trafficking, and reduce immunosuppressive myeloid recruitment. These differences also help explain the distinct efficacy patterns observed across International Metastatic RCC Database Consortium (IMDC) risk groups.

Dual immune checkpoint blockade with nivolumab plus ipilimumab enhances the priming and expansion of tumor-specific T cells, broadens epitope spread, and can re-invigorate exhausted clones within an already inflamed tumor microenvironment, thereby increasing the likelihood of deep and sometimes complete remissions. Consistent with these biologic features, the CheckMate 214 trial demonstrated that dual ICI therapy confers the greatest long-term benefit in intermediate- and poor-risk patients defined by IMDC criteria, with a median OS of 52.7 months after 8 years of follow-up, an ORR of 42%, a complete response (CR) rate of 12%, and an mDOR of 76.2 months. In contrast, favorable-risk patients did not derive significant advantage from this regimen, underscoring the importance of risk stratification.

An important contribution to personalized treatment selection comes from the biomarker-driven BIONIKK trial (NCT02960906), the first prospective study to allocate therapy based on transcriptomic subgroups of ccRCC. Using a validated 35-gene classifier, tumors were categorized into four molecular phenotypes (ccrcc1–4) with distinct immune and angiogenic characteristics. Objective response rates varied markedly across subgroups. In the immune-poor ccrcc1 group, nivolumab monotherapy showed limited activity (29%), whereas dual checkpoint blockade achieved improved responses (39%). The immune-inflamed ccrcc4 subgroup demonstrated the highest sensitivity to immunotherapy (nivolumab 44%; nivolumab–ipilimumab 50%). Conversely, the angiogenic ccrcc2 subgroup derived comparable benefit from VEGFR-TKI therapy (50%) and dual ICI (51%), highlighting the biological rationale for VEGF-targeted combinations in this setting. These findings support a future framework in which molecular phenotyping—rather than IMDC risk alone—guides the optimal choice between dual ICI and ICI–TKI regimens in metastatic ccRCC.

PD-1 + TKI combinations have also yielded robust clinical outcomes across multiple phase III trials. In the KEYNOTE-426 trial, pembrolizumab plus axitinib demonstrated sustained survival benefits after a median follow-up of 67.2 months, with a 5-year OS rate of 41.9%, PFS rate of 18.3%, and an mDOR of 23.6 months. The CheckMate 9ER study evaluated nivolumab plus cabozantinib, showing a median OS of 46.5 months and an ORR of 55.7%. In the CLEAR trial, pembrolizumab plus lenvatinib achieved an ORR as high as 71%, with an mDOR of 26.7 months, albeit with a higher incidence of grade ≥ 3 adverse events (82%). These outcomes align with the biologic rationale that PD-1 + TKI combinations produce rapid tumor shrinkage and are particularly useful for favorable-risk or symptomatic patients with high disease burden. In clinical decision-making, PD-1 + TKI combinations are generally preferred for patients requiring rapid tumor reduction or symptomatic relief, whereas dual ICI regimens may be considered for those aiming for long-term disease control and durable remission. These complementary strategies highlight the importance of individualized treatment selection based on risk stratification, disease burden, and therapeutic goals.

Triplet intensification has also been explored in intermediate- and poor-risk ccRCC. In the phase III COSMIC-313 trial, the addition of cabozantinib to nivolumab–ipilimumab significantly improved progression-free survival but was associated with a substantially higher incidence of grade ≥ 3 adverse events. Subsequent analyses did not demonstrate an overall survival benefit over nivolumab–ipilimumab alone, underscoring that empiric treatment intensification may increase toxicity without translating into longer survival and highlighting the need for biomarker-guided patient selection. However, optimizing the choice and sequence of combinations remains an area of active clinical investigation.

Adjuvant therapy

Beyond metastatic disease, the potential of ICIs in the curative setting has also been explored through adjuvant strategies following nephrectomy. To date, pembrolizumab in the KEYNOTE-564 trial remains the only immune checkpoint inhibitor approved by the U.S. FDA for adjuvant therapy in renal cell carcinoma. This phase III study enrolled patients with high-risk localized ccRCC (with or without sarcomatoid features) following nephrectomy. At the 48-month follow-up, pembrolizumab demonstrated a HR for disease-free survival (DFS) of 0.72 and an OS HR of 0.62, solidifying its role in the postoperative setting. Subgroup analyses from KEYNOTE-564 provide additional granularity on which patients benefit most from adjuvant pembrolizumab. In the updated OS analysis, patients with M0 intermediate-to-high–risk disease experienced a consistent improvement (48-month OS: 92.6% vs. 87.7%; HR 0.59). Those with M0 high-risk features showed a numerically smaller but directionally favorable effect (48-month OS: 80.0% vs. 73.0%; HR 0.78). Notably, the resected M1–no-evidence-of-disease (M1-NED) subgroup achieved the greatest relative benefit, with a 48-month OS of 89.7% versus 78.0% and a HR of 0.51, despite the limited sample size. These findings indicate that patients with M1-NED status—representing minimal residual disease (MRD) after metastasectomy or metastasis-directed therapy—may be particularly strong candidates for adjuvant pembrolizumab. These findings support the use of adjuvant pembrolizumab in a broad spectrum of high-risk ccRCC following nephrectomy, while ongoing analyses seek to refine indications using molecular and MRD biomarkers. Other randomized trials evaluating adjuvant immunotherapy have not met their primary endpoints. These include IMmotion010 (atezolizumab), CheckMate 914 (nivolumab plus ipilimumab), and PROSPER (perioperative nivolumab), none of which showed statistically significant improvements in DFS or OS. These findings highlight the complex interplay between patient selection, treatment intensity, and duration of exposure in determining adjuvant benefit. Looking forward, novel therapeutic combinations such as those incorporating belzutifan (a HIF-2α inhibitor) and the integration of MRD biomarkers, including circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) and kidney injury molecule-1 (KIM-1), may improve risk stratification and guide adjuvant therapy more effectively. Given the inconsistent results across trials, future success in the adjuvant setting will likely depend on improved molecular risk stratification and biomarker-guided patient selection to avoid overtreatment and maximize benefit.

Toxicities and ongoing challenges

In dual ICI regimens, irAEs are frequent, occurring in approximately 60% of patients, with grade ≥ 3 irAEs observed in about 30%, and treatment discontinuation occurring in 22%, as reported in the CheckMate 214 trial.

In contrast, PD-1 plus TKI combinations are associated with even higher toxicity rates, with grade ≥ 3 TRAEs ranging from 62% to 82% across KEYNOTE-426, CLEAR, and CheckMate 9ER. Frequent toxicities in this setting include hypertension, elevated liver enzymes, and proteinuria, necessitating close monitoring and multidisciplinary management. Moreover, aside from nivolumab plus ipilimumab (which demonstrated a mDOR of 76 months), most combination regimens offer limited durability, with mDORs ranging from 22 to 27 months, and more than half of patients failing to maintain remission beyond three years. The mechanisms underlying both primary and acquired resistance remain incompletely understood. Data from studies such as CONTACT-03 and TiNivo-2 suggest that rechallenging with similar agents yields minimal benefit, underscoring the urgent need for alternative strategies. ICIs have become a cornerstone in the management of renal cell carcinoma. While ICI monotherapy retains value in second-line settings and for select patient populations, combination strategies—either dual ICI or PD-1 plus TKI—offer complementary strengths and should be tailored according to individual disease characteristics and treatment goals. In the adjuvant setting, pembrolizumab has shown an OS benefit and is currently the only approved ICI, whereas other regimens remain investigational. Key challenges moving forward include managing toxicity, improving response durability, and overcoming resistance. Addressing these limitations will require continued mechanistic insights and a shift toward biomarker-driven, individualized treatment paradigms, which are further discussed in the future directions Sect. Novel immune strategies for enhancing and sustaining Anti-Tumor immunity

Although ICIs have significantly transformed the treatment paradigm for advanced ccRCC, durable survival benefits remain limited to a subset of patients. Consequently, ongoing research is focused on expanding beyond the classical PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 axes to identify novel immune checkpoints and develop innovative combinatorial immunotherapeutic strategies.

Reinforcing T cell activation

Emerging immune checkpoints: LAG-3, TIM-3, and TIGIT

A number of emerging inhibitory immune checkpoints, including lymphocyte activation gene-3 (LAG-3), T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin-domain containing-3 (TIM-3), and T-cell immunoreceptor with Ig and ITIM domains (TIGIT), have been found to be upregulated in the RCC tumor microenvironment and are closely associated with progressive T-cell exhaustion. Single-cell transcriptomic studies in ccRCC consistently demonstrate that exhausted CD8⁺ T-cell clusters co-express PD-1 together with LAG-3, TIM-3, and TIGIT, supporting the biological rationale for targeting multiple inhibitory pathways concurrently. Clinically, LAG-3–directed strategies are the most advanced. A pivotal phase II trial (NCT05805501) is currently evaluating RO7247669, a bispecific antibody targeting both PD-1 and LAG-3, in combination with axitinib in treatment-naïve patients with locally advanced or metastatic ccRCC. An additional cohort in this study incorporates the TIGIT inhibitor tiragolumab to intensify multi-checkpoint blockade. Although RCC-specific efficacy data are not yet available, early findings from other solid tumors suggest that PD-1×LAG-3 bispecifics may broaden anti-tumor responses beyond PD-1 monotherapy.

Development of TIM-3 and TIGIT inhibitors in ccRCC remains at an earlier stage, but their mechanistic relevance is supported by high expression of these molecules on dysfunctional intratumoral T cells and by preclinical evidence that dual or triple blockade can partially reverse exhaustion phenotypes. Together, these emerging immune checkpoints may be most beneficial in tumors with strong exhaustion signatures characterized by high LAG-3/TIM-3/TIGIT co-expression. Key challenges include avoiding overlapping immune-related toxicities and determining optimal combinations with VEGF-targeted or metabolic therapies. Biomarker-guided patient selection will likely be essential for maximizing therapeutic benefit.

Cytokine-Based therapies

Historically, high-dose interleukin-2 (IL-2) represented one of the earliest immunotherapies capable of inducing durable complete responses in a small subset of patients with metastatic RCC, but its use was limited by severe systemic toxicities such as capillary leak syndrome and multiorgan dysfunction. Modern cytokine-based strategies aim to preserve or enhance the immunostimulatory properties of IL-2 and related cytokines while minimizing off-tumor toxicity. Many of these agents bias signalling toward the intermediate-affinity IL-2 receptor (CD122/CD132) expressed on effector CD8⁺ T cells and NK cells, or locally concentrate cytokine activity within the tumor microenvironment. The overarching goal is to expand and activate cytotoxic lymphocyte populations in a more controlled and tumor-focused manner, thereby complementing checkpoint blockade. Cytokine-based immunotherapy has re-emerged as a promising avenue in renal cell carcinoma, with recent innovations aimed at enhancing efficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity. One notable agent is bempegaldesleukin (NKTR-214), a CD122-biased IL-2 pathway agonist, which has been evaluated in combination with nivolumab in a single-arm phase I dose-escalation trial (NCT02983045) in 38 immunotherapy-naïve patients with advanced melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, and non–small cell lung cancer. Across all tumor types and dose cohorts, the combination achieved an objective response rate of 59.5% (22/37) with 18.9% complete responses, and was generally tolerated: grade 3/4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 21.1% of patients, with no treatment-related deaths reported. Although the RCC subgroup was small, the numerical activity in these patients suggests that IL-2 pathway agonism can meaningfully synergize with PD-1 blockade in selected cases.

Another cytokine-based approach involves NHS-IL12 (M9241), an immunocytokine that delivers interleukin-12 (IL-12) directly to the tumor microenvironment. In phase I trials (NCT01417546 and NCT02994953) evaluating NHS-IL12 in combination with avelumab for patients with advanced solid tumors—including ccRCC—the therapy demonstrated an acceptable safety profile, with grade 3/4 TRAEs reported in approximately 20% of patients. Engineered IL-2 agonists restore CD8⁺ proliferation without the vascular leak seen with high-dose aldesleukin, yet their single-agent durability remains modest. Biomarker-driven combination—e.g., pairing with PD-1 blockade in cytokine-responsive IFN-γ–high tumours— may enhance efficacy.

Redirecting cytotoxicity to tumors

Bispecific T cell engagers in CcRCC

Bispecific T cell engagers harness the existing polyclonal T-cell repertoire by physically bridging CD3 on T cells with a tumor-associated surface antigen, thereby forming an artificial immune synapse and triggering T-cell activation and cytotoxicity in an MHC-independent manner. This approach circumvents defects in antigen processing and presentation and does not require patient-specific T-cell receptor (TCR) engineering, offering an “off-the-shelf” means of redirecting T cells toward ccRCC cells. In principle, such agents can rapidly recruit and activate bystander T cells within the tumor microenvironment, even when endogenous tumor-reactive T cells are scarce or functionally exhausted. Bispecific antibodies, which simultaneously bind tumor-associated antigens and T cell receptors such as CD3, offer a unique strategy to redirect T cells toward malignant cells and enhance anti-tumor immunity. One notable example is XmAb819, developed by Xencor, which utilizes a “2 + 1” configuration—two binding arms targeting ENPP3, a ccRCC-associated surface antigen, and one arm targeting CD3. In the ongoing phase I XmAb819-01 trial (NCT05433142), the ENPP3×CD3 bispecific antibody XmAb819 has demonstrated preliminary antitumor activity in heavily pretreated patients with advanced ccRCC, including confirmed partial responses, with a manageable safety profile. Another investigational bispecific antibody targeting G250/CD3 has shown promising preclinical activity by bridging RCC cells and T cells to potentiate cytotoxic responses. However, this agent has not yet progressed into late-stage clinical development. Compared with CAR-T, off-the-shelf bispecifics offer lower manufacturing cost and immediate availability, but their serum half-life may require continuous infusion pumps. Long-acting formats (e.g., IgG-like Fc) are therefore a critical next step for real-world adoption.

Bispecific antibodies offer immediate “off-the-shelf” redirection of T cell cytotoxicity toward tumor cells and may be most suitable for patients with accessible surface antigens and rapid disease progression requiring urgent tumor control. Their strengths include bypassing MHC restriction and scalable manufacturing, but their short half-life and potential for cytokine release limit single-agent durability. Long-acting Fc-engineered formats and combinations with immune checkpoint blockade may expand their clinical utility.

CAR-T and TCR-T cell therapies

Adoptive T-cell therapies such as chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T) and engineered TCR-T provide a way to supply patients with large numbers of highly tumor-reactive lymphocytes. CAR-T cells recognize surface antigens in an HLA-independent manner through an antibody-derived binding domain, whereas TCR-engineered T cells recognize intracellular antigens presented on specific HLA molecules, enabling targeting of neoantigens or cancer-testis antigens. Both platforms offer highly specific and potent cytotoxicity, but in solid tumors like ccRCC they face common challenges, including efficient trafficking into the tumor bed, persistence in a hostile and immunosuppressive microenvironment, and maintenance of effector function over time. Rational combinations that modulate the tumor vasculature, reprogram myeloid cells, or alleviate metabolic suppression may therefore be required to unlock their full potential. CAR-T cell therapy, while facing significant barriers in solid tumors, has shown initial signs of efficacy in ccRCC. The TRAVERSE trial (NCT04696731) is an ongoing phase I study evaluating ALLO-316, an allogeneic CAR-T product targeting CD70, in patients with metastatic ccRCC who have progressed after prior ICI and TKI therapies. In the overall treated cohort, the ORR was 12%, and the DCR was 71%. In patients with CD70-positive tumors, confirmed ORR reached 22%, and DCR was 100%. ALLO-316 was engineered using TALEN® technology to disrupt TCRα and CD52 loci and is administered alongside ALLO-647, a lymphodepletion agent, to prevent graft-versus-host disease (GvHD) and minimize immune-related toxicities. No cases of GvHD or immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) were reported, and the overall safety profile was favorable.

In parallel, TCR-based therapies targeting tumor-associated antigens have also entered clinical testing. In the NCT03354390 trial, investigators introduced a TCR specific for HERV-E, an endogenous retroviral antigen selectively expressed in ccRCC, into autologous T cells of HLA-A*11:01-positive patients. This phase I study demonstrated feasibility and safety, with no dose-limiting toxicities observed. In some patients, in vivo expansion and infiltration of HERV-E–specific TCR-T cells were detected, along with reductions in tumor burden; one patient achieved a partial response, supporting the therapeutic potential of neoantigen-specific TCR strategies in selected populations.

CAR-T and TCR-T therapies are ideally suited for biomarker-defined populations with homogeneous and high antigen expression (e.g., CD70 + or HERV-E+/HLA-A*11:01+). These approaches deliver highly specific, potent cytotoxicity, yet face challenges in trafficking to the tumor bed and overcoming the suppressive tumor microenvironment. Incorporating TME-modulating agents—such as VEGF blockade, metabolic reprogramming, or oncolytic viruses—may improve infiltration and persistence.

Activating innate immunity and tumor immunogenicity

In parallel with targeting adaptive immune resistance mechanisms, investigators have also sought to activate innate immune signaling pathways. One approach involves the use of Toll-like receptor 7/8 (TLR7/8) agonists. For instance, MEDI9197, an intratumorally administered TLR7/8 agonist, has been combined with the PD-L1 inhibitor durvalumab in a phase I/II trial (NCT02556463) involving patients with advanced solid tumors. Preliminary data from this study suggest favorable tolerability, with some patients achieving stable disease as their best response.

Although no therapeutic cancer vaccine has been approved for ccRCC to date, early-phase studies have provided a conceptual and technical foundation for future development. IMA901, a multipeptide vaccine, was evaluated in the phase III IMPRINT trial (NCT01265901) in combination with sunitinib for HLA-A*02-positive patients with metastatic ccRCC. The study failed to demonstrate a significant OS benefit (median OS of 33.17 months in the combination arm vs. not reached in the control; HR = 1.34, P = 0.087), and PFS outcomes were not explicitly reported. While earlier studies linked multivalent T cell responses induced by IMA901 to improved outcomes, these findings were not confirmed in phase III, indicating that enhancing vaccine immunogenicity remains a critical barrier. Rocapuldencel-T (CMN-001), a dendritic cell–based vaccine, similarly failed to improve OS in the ADAPT trial (NCT01582672), though it is now being re-evaluated with nivolumab and ipilimumab (NCT04203901). A personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccine (RO7198457) combined with atezolizumab achieved an 8% objective response rate in a phase Ib solid-tumor study that included a small number of ccRCC patients. However, the responses were not clearly driven by RCC cases, and durability remains uncertain.

Collectively, current vaccine strategies—including multipeptide, dendritic cell–based, and mRNA neoantigen platforms—have shown biological activity without consistent clinical benefit in ccRCC. Enhancing antigen selection, improving dendritic-cell priming, and overcoming the profoundly immunosuppressive RCC microenvironment will be essential for translating these approaches into clinically meaningful therapy.

Reprogramming immunometabolic suppression

Adenosine axis

Immunometabolic dysregulation is a key contributor to immune evasion in ccRCC. The adenosine pathway (CD39–CD73–A2AR) facilitates the accumulation of immunosuppressive adenosine in the tumor microenvironment, impairing effector T cell function. Multiple translational studies have shown that CD73 and A2AR are overexpressed in ccRCC and that high CD73 expression correlates with more aggressive disease, an immunosuppressive infiltrate, and poorer survival, supporting this axis as a rational therapeutic target in renal cell carcinoma.

The A2AR antagonist ciforadenant has been evaluated in patients with treatment-refractory RCC. In a phase I trial of 68 patients with advanced RCC, ciforadenant was administered either as monotherapy or in combination with the anti–PD-L1 antibody atezolizumab. The regimen was generally well tolerated, and both single-agent and combination therapy produced objective responses and durable disease control, including in patients who had previously progressed on PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors. Importantly, clinical benefit was associated with increased intratumoral CD8⁺ T-cell infiltration and an adenosine-regulated gene-expression signature in pretreatment biopsies, providing mechanistic proof-of-concept that A2AR blockade can partially reverse adenosine-mediated immunosuppression in RCC.

IDO–Kyn–AHR pathway

Another critical immunometabolic pathway involves indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) and the downstream kynurenine–aryl hydrocarbon receptor (Kyn–AHR) axis. This pathway mediates tryptophan catabolism into immunosuppressive kynurenine, leading to T cell dysfunction. In a phase I trial (NCT02327078), the IDO1 inhibitor epacadostat combined with pembrolizumab was tested in 11 RCC patients, achieving a disease control rate of 63.6%, including partial responses (PRs) and stable disease (SD) in 7 patients. However, enthusiasm for IDO inhibition was tempered by the failure of the ECHO-302 phase III trial in melanoma to meet its primary endpoint, resulting in early termination of the development program. Despite this setback, the IDO pathway remains a compelling immunosuppressive mechanism, and next-generation IDO inhibitors, such as linrodostat, continue to undergo evaluation across multiple malignancies, including RCC. Early failure of epacadostat highlights the need for potent, irreversible IDO/TDO dual inhibitors and rational triplets (IDO + PD-1 + oncolytic virus) to remodel a profoundly tryptophan-depleted micro-niche.

In summary, next-generation checkpoints and cytokine agonists (activation arm), bispecifics and adoptive cells (directed-cytotoxic arm), innate-sensing agonists and vaccines (priming arm), and metabolic rewiring agents (release-the-brakes arm) map onto complementary, non-overlapping immunologic axes. The most promising translational path appears to be biomarker-informed, mechanism-oriented doublets or triplets—for example, PD-1×LAG-3 bispecifics + A2AR blockade in adenosine-rich tumours, or CD70 CAR-T following IDO-inhibitor priming to increase antigen expression. Prospective trials embedding spatial transcriptomics and ctDNA MRD readouts will be essential to match patients with the most rational combination and monitor on-treatment evolution.

Immunometabolic interventions are particularly relevant for adenosine-rich or tryptophan-depleted tumor microenvironments, where they can restore effector function and counter metabolic exhaustion. Their advantage is targeting non-redundant resistance pathways; however, systemic toxicity and context-specific efficacy require careful patient selection. Combining metabolic modulators with checkpoint inhibitors or adoptive T cell therapies offers a promising route to durable immune activation.

Integrative strategy outlook

The four immunological axes outlined in this section—T cell activation, redirected cytotoxicity, innate immune activation, and metabolic reprogramming—are mechanistically distinct yet synergistic. T cell activation strategies expand the effector pool, redirected cytotoxicity channels these effectors toward tumor cells, innate activation broadens antigenic targets and primes immune responses, and metabolic reprogramming sustains T cell function in hostile microenvironments. Rational integration of these modalities—guided by spatial immune architecture, antigen expression profiles, and metabolic signatures—can maximize therapeutic synergy while minimizing redundant toxicity. Embedding such biomarker-informed doublets or triplets into early-phase trials, with real-time monitoring via ctDNA and spatial transcriptomics, will be critical to translating these complementary strategies into durable clinical benefit for patients with ccRCC. Biomarkers and mechanisms of resistance

Bulk-Tissue biomarkers

The advent of ICIs has revolutionized the treatment landscape of metastatic renal cell carcinoma. Despite these advances, therapeutic responses remain highly heterogeneous—while some patients experience rapid disease progression, others achieve deep and durable remissions. As such, identifying reliable predictive biomarkers and elucidating the mechanisms of resistance have become central to both clinical practice and translational research in RCC.

The most commonly investigated biomarkers include PD-L1 expression, tumor mutational burden (TMB), and PBRM1 gene alterations. Although PD-L1 protein expression has shown predictive value in several malignancies, its role in RCC is inconsistent. In a phase II trial of nivolumab, higher ORR were observed in the subgroup with PD-L1 expression ≥ 5%; however, in large phase III studies such as CheckMate 025 and JAVELIN Renal 101, PD-L1 expression was not significantly associated with OS. This discrepancy likely stems from multiple factors, including intratumoral heterogeneity, spatial and temporal variability between primary and metastatic sites, and inconsistencies in assay platforms and cutoff thresholds. Moreover, tumors that are PD-L1–negative but PD-L2–positive may be overlooked, further limiting the predictive utility of PD-L1 testing in RCC.

Similarly, the clinical relevance of TMB and neoantigen load remains controversial in RCC. Although RCC exhibits a relatively high frequency of clonal insertion-deletion mutations, which theoretically enhances immunogenicity, these metrics failed to predict response to atezolizumab in the IMmotion150 trial. On the other hand, PBRM1 loss-of-function mutations, present in over 50% of patients who respond to nivolumab, have emerged as a potentially actionable biomarker. PBRM1 mutations have also been associated with improved response to VEGFR-TKIs and prolonged survival. Nonetheless, the clinical utility of PBRM1 status is hindered by the lack of prospective validation and the absence of standardized detection protocols.

The integration of multi-dimensional gene expression profiling has introduced new avenues for response prediction. In IMmotion150, RNA sequencing–based molecular classification stratified RCC into three subtypes—angiogenic, T-effector, and myeloid inflammatory—which correspond to sensitivity to VEGF blockade, PD-L1 monotherapy, and combination regimens with bevacizumab, respectively. In JAVELIN Renal 101, a 26-gene immune-related gene expression signature was developed to predict response to avelumab plus axitinib; this signature showed potential in identifying patients more likely to benefit from the combination.

Another emerging biomarker is CD8⁺ T cell infiltration. While high CD8⁺ T cell density is typically associated with poor prognosis in RCC, it may paradoxically predict longer PFS in patients receiving certain immunotherapy combinations. Additionally, the interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) gene signature, which has shown predictive value across multiple solid tumors, warrants further investigation in RCC to determine its clinical applicability.

Among bulk-tissue biomarkers, PBRM1 mutation currently holds the greatest translational promise due to its relatively high prevalence in ccRCC, reproducible association with immunotherapy responsiveness, and ease of genomic detection. However, lack of prospective validation and standardized testing platforms still limits its clinical adoption. Future progress will likely come from integrated molecular classifiers that combine genomic, transcriptomic, and immune phenotyping data to outperform single-analyte biomarkers in predictive accuracy. This section primarily focuses on biomarkers identified through conventional bulk-level analyses (e.g., IHC, bulk RNA-seq, genomic profiling), while insights from single-cell or spatial technologies are discussed in Sect. 4.2 and 4.3.

While bulk-tissue approaches have yielded important biomarker candidates such as PBRM1 mutation and immune-related gene expression signatures, these methods inherently average molecular signals across heterogeneous cell populations, masking cell-type–specific programs and spatial immune organization. The emergence of single-cell and spatial omics overcomes these limitations by enabling high-resolution mapping of tumor–immune ecosystems, revealing functional states, clonal dynamics, and local signaling networks that cannot be resolved by bulk profiling. This methodological progression directly supports precision immunoprofiling in RCC and informs rational patient stratification for targeted therapies.

Spatial and Single-Cell biomarkers

The advent of single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) and spatial transcriptomics has enabled unprecedented resolution in dissecting the spatiotemporal immune heterogeneity of RCC. scRNA-seq has elucidated the evolutionary trajectory of CD8⁺ T cells from a progenitor-exhausted state (Tpex) toward a terminally exhausted phenotype. Tpex cells retain self-renewal potential and serve as the proliferative “seeds” upon PD-1 blockade, whereas Ttex cells, particularly those expressing high levels of TIGIT, exhibit limited responsiveness to PD-1/CTLA-4 inhibitors but may be more susceptible to TIGIT-targeted therapy.

Spatial transcriptomic analyses further reveal distinct immunosuppressive niches within the tumor. As previously discussed in Sect. 4.1, PBRM1-deficient tumor cells, in addition to their prognostic relevance, are frequently co-localized with CD163⁺ M2-like tumor-associated macrophages, accompanied by elevated local expression of VEGF-A and IL-10. This configuration establishes a highly suppressive microenvironment that is generally resistant to PD-1 monotherapy but may respond favorably to combinatorial VEGF and PD-L1 blockade. Additionally, the presence of tertiary lymphoid structures (TLSs) has been associated with enhanced immune activation and improved treatment response in RCC. These high-resolution spatial and cellular maps are increasingly being incorporated into prospective clinical trials, supporting the translational potential of composite biomarkers.

Spatial and single-cell approaches provide unmatched resolution in dissecting intratumoral heterogeneity, mapping immunosuppressive niches, and linking molecular alterations to functional immune states. These strengths position them as powerful tools for patient stratification and rational design of combination therapies. Current barriers include high cost, technical complexity, and lack of consensus analytical frameworks. Their broader clinical integration will depend on harmonized protocols, cost reduction, and incorporation into prospective biomarker–driven trials.

Mechanisms of resistance and immunosuppression

Resistance to ICIs in ccRCC can be broadly categorized into primary and acquired forms. Primary resistance refers to the absence of any meaningful clinical response despite adequate drug exposure, typically driven by pre-existing immune exclusion, insufficient neoantigen presentation, metabolic dysfunction, or constitutive inhibitory signaling. Acquired resistance, by contrast, occurs after an initial period of tumor control and arises through adaptive remodeling of the tumor–immune ecosystem—including progressive T-cell exhaustion, loss of antigenicity, activation of compensatory checkpoints, and expansion of suppressive myeloid populations. Poor or acquired resistance to ICIs in RCC is often driven by intrinsic T cell dysfunction and the development of a deeply immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Chronic antigen stimulation induces sustained expression of inhibitory receptors such as PD-1, TIGIT, and LAG-3 on CD8⁺ T cells, along with mitochondrial dysfunction and metabolic exhaustion, culminating in irreversible terminal exhaustion. In this context, monotherapy with PD-1 or PD-L1 blockade is often insufficient to restore effector function. Thus, multi-target immunotherapies (e.g., PD-1 + TIGIT/LAG-3) and interventions targeting metabolic and epigenetic reprogramming have become areas of active investigation (Fig. 1).

TME in RCC is suppressive. VEGF contributes to immune evasion by promoting abnormal angiogenesis, recruiting myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and regulatory T cells (Tregs), and impairing the formation of high endothelial venules, thereby restricting T cell trafficking and activation. While VEGFR-TKIs may transiently “normalize” vasculature and sensitize tumors to immunotherapy, prolonged use can lead to hypoxia-induced M2 TAM accumulation, reinforcing immune escape mechanisms. Furthermore, myeloid inflammatory pathways such as IL-6/JAK-STAT, A2AR, and IDO, along with emerging immune checkpoints like LAG-3, TIM-3, and TIGIT, act synergistically to construct complex immune resistance networks, limiting the efficacy of PD-L1 monotherapy.

Resistance to immunotherapy in ccRCC is multifactorial, but three mechanisms consistently emerge as high-priority therapeutic targets: CD8⁺ T cell exhaustion with multi-checkpoint co-expression, VEGF-driven vascular and immune suppression, and myeloid inflammatory networks mediated by TAMs and MDSCs. These are prioritized due to their high prevalence, strong preclinical–clinical concordance, and availability of targeted agents in advanced clinical testing. Mechanistic overlaps suggest rational combinations—for example, VEGF/VEGFR blockade to normalize vasculature and enhance T cell infiltration, coupled with PD-1 plus LAG-3/TIGIT inhibition to reinvigorate exhausted T cells; or TAM reprogramming alongside metabolic pathway blockade to dismantle suppressive myeloid niches. Translation into clinical benefit will require biomarker-guided patient selection, longitudinal monitoring with spatial transcriptomics or ctDNA, and adaptive treatment algorithms to counter evolving resistance patterns.

The immune resistance mechanisms described above are not solely determined by intrinsic tumor genetics and local microenvironmental factors. Distal regulatory influences—particularly the gut microbiome—can modulate systemic immune tone, shape myeloid and lymphoid cell function, and reprogram metabolic pathways relevant to tumor immunity. Recognizing these extrinsic modulators creates opportunities to pair mechanism-targeted interventions with microbiome-directed strategies, potentially enhancing both the magnitude and durability of immunotherapy responses.

The role of the gut Microbiome in immunotherapy resistance

Remote regulatory factors, particularly the gut microbiota, are increasingly recognized as modulators of immunotherapy outcomes. Clinical evidence has shown that antibiotic exposure significantly compromises the efficacy of ICIs, while probiotic supplementation—notably with CBM588—has been associated with favorable modulation of the gut microbiome and significantly prolonged PFS when added to ipilimumab/nivolumab regimens.

The gut microbiome represents a promising and potentially modifiable biomarker, with the advantage of non-invasive sampling and feasibility of therapeutic modulation through diet, probiotics, or fecal microbiota transplantation. Its translational potential is high, but significant inter-patient variability, dietary confounders, and inconsistent results across studies remain barriers to clinical application. Controlled interventional trials and mechanistic mapping of host–microbe–immune interactions will be essential to realize its role in precision immuno-oncology.

Biomarker research in RCC has progressed from bulk-level analyses, which provide broad molecular correlates of response, to high-resolution single-cell and spatial technologies capable of capturing immune heterogeneity and microenvironmental context. These advances, combined with mechanistic insights into T cell exhaustion, VEGF-driven immune suppression, and myeloid-mediated resistance, lay the groundwork for more precise therapeutic targeting. Distal modulators such as the gut microbiome further expand this framework by influencing both immune composition and functional states. Integrating biomarker discovery with mechanistic prioritization and regulatory modulation offers a cohesive pathway toward personalized immunotherapy in RCC, and will require prospective, biomarker-guided trials with longitudinal monitoring to translate these insights into durable patient benefit.

Conclusions and future perspectives

Immunotherapy has transformed the therapeutic paradigm of advanced ccRCC, yet durable benefit remains restricted to a subset of patients. Overcoming this limitation requires a strategic shift toward mechanism-driven combination strategies, dynamic disease monitoring, and biomarker-guided personalized intervention.

Mechanism-driven strategies should target the dominant resistance pathways in ccRCC—such as CD8⁺ T cell exhaustion, VEGF-mediated vascular and immune suppression, and TAM-driven immunosuppression—through rational doublet or triplet regimens (e.g., PD-1 plus LAG-3/TIGIT blockade, VEGFR-TKI plus ICI, or TAM reprogramming combined with metabolic modulation).

Dynamic monitoring using single-cell transcriptomics, spatial profiling, and ctDNA-based MRD detection can provide real-time insight into tumor–immune evolution, enabling early detection of resistance and timely therapeutic adaptation.

Personalized intervention integrates these mechanistic and monitoring insights to match patients with the most appropriate therapy at each disease stage, minimizing unnecessary toxicity and maximizing durable benefit. In this precision framework, the gut microbiome emerges as a modifiable systemic regulator, offering opportunities for adjunctive strategies to reinforce local and systemic immune function.

The path forward will depend on embedding such integrated approaches into prospective clinical trials, with predefined biomarker endpoints and adaptive designs. By coupling deep mechanistic understanding with high-resolution monitoring and individualized therapeutic algorithms, the field can move closer to delivering long-term, durable immunotherapy benefit for patients with ccRCC.
