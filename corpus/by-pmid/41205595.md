# Non-orthosteric inhibition of enolase 1 impedes growth of triple-negative breast cancer.

## Abstract

Cancer cells exploit and overexpress glycolytic enzymes such as enolase 1 (ENO1) to sustain the Warburg effect, a hallmark of cancer metabolism, which makes ENO1 a compelling therapeutic target. Here, we demonstrate that SU212, a small molecule inhibitor, binds ENO1 and induces its degradation, restricting its intracellular localization. Through these effects, SU212 reduces tumor cell glycolytic activity and glucose uptake and ultimately suppresses tumor growth and metastasis in syngeneic, genetic, and patient-derived xenograft models of triple-negative breast cancer. Further, in a diabetic mouse model, SU212 demonstrated robust anti-tumor efficacy while improving fatty liver conditions and lowering blood glucose. Other glycolytic enzyme inhibitors have been limited by toxicity; thorough pharmacokinetic and toxicity testing of SU212 revealed a favorable drug-like profile with minimal toxicity and no interference with key biological systems. These findings highlight SU212's dual-action potential to disrupt cancer metabolism and address metabolic disorders, offering a transformative approach to cancer therapy.

Introduction

Altered energy metabolism is a defining feature of cancer cells. While healthy cells primarily rely on oxidative phosphorylation for energy production, in contrast, cancer cells rely on aerobic glycolysis, a phenomenon known as the Warburg effect. This metabolic state increases cellular glucose consumption and lactate production, even in aerobic settings. The cancer cell’s increased glucose consumption fuels the production of macromolecules necessary for a high rate of proliferation, and lactate accumulation fosters a tumor microenvironment that encourages invasion and metastasis. This metabolic state is enabled by glucose metabolism-related enzymes, like glucose transferase, hexokinase, and enolase 1 (ENO1), which are overexpressed in a wide range of cancers. Thus, targeting these enzymes, and other highly expressed glucose metabolism enzymes, has long been considered a potentially potent strategy for anticancer drug development. However, thus far, toxicity and inadequate anticancer efficacy have limited clinical translation.

ENO1, a central enzyme in the glycolytic pathway, catalyzes the dehydration of 2-phospho-D-glycerate (2PG) to phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). ENO1 overexpression across various cancers, such as breast, lung, and colorectal cancers, correlates with increased glycolytic flux, tumor growth, and worse overall survival. Inhibiting ENO1 hinders the glycolytic pathway, depriving cancer cells of the ATP and biosynthetic intermediates necessary for rapid growth. This can result in energy depletion and accumulation of toxic intermediates, which induces metabolic stress and sensitizes cancer cells to apoptosis. In addition to its role in glycolysis, ENO1 has non-metabolic, “moonlighting” functions, such as promoting cell adhesion, which supports cell migration and invasion—essential processes for cancer metastasis. Thus, inhibiting ENO1 could diminish cancer cell invasive and migratory abilities, potentially restricting tumor dissemination.

Both cellular and whole-organism factors influence cancer cell metabolism. In conditions like obesity and type 2 diabetes (T2D), high blood glucose and insulin stimulate signaling pathways that promote cell growth, increase tumor glucose uptake, and fuel rapid tumor growth. For example, in hyperglycemia, tumor cells increase ENO1 activity to meet energy needs; hyperinsulinemia activates insulin/insulin-like growth factor signaling, boosting ENO1 and glycolysis, which encourages cancer reprogramming. This link between systemic metabolism and tumor growth may explain why obesity and T2D are associated with higher cancer risk, especially for triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC). TNBC, which lacks the estrogen receptor, progesterone receptor, and the human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), is unresponsive to hormone or HER2-targeted therapies and relies heavily on glycolysis for energy, making it potentially vulnerable to therapies targeting glucose metabolism.

Our laboratory developed a podophyllotoxin derivative, SU212, which has a remarkable ability to inhibit TNBC tumor growth in vitro and in vivo. We previously demonstrated that SU212 treatment results in energy-independent AMPK activation, shifting tumor cells toward oxidative phosphorylation. These data suggested that SU212 might target an enzyme in the glycolytic pathway, and here, we identify the glycolytic enzyme ENO1 as the target of SU212. SU212 induces ENO1 degradation, reduces cellular glycolysis, and limits TNBC tumor growth and metastasis, as validated across numerous TNBC experimental models. Importantly, SU212 limited tumor growth under hyperglycemic and hyperinsulinemia conditions and demonstrated a favorable safety profile. These data reveal SU212 as a highly promising compound with minimal toxicity and strong efficacy; thus, SU212 has potential to overcome two factors that have limited clinical translation of compounds targeting enzymes driving glucose metabolism.

Results

SU212 interacts with and inhibits ENO1

Podophyllotoxin is a versatile compound recognized for its significant medicinal properties. For example, podophyllotoxin is a treatment for genital warts, effectively curing these infections. Furthermore, podophyllotoxin derivatives, such as the powerful anticancer agents etoposide and teniposide, demonstrated efficacy against various malignancies. These derivatives function by inhibiting DNA topoisomerase II, leading to cell-cycle arrest and tumor cell death. However, both etoposide and teniposide can induce severe side effects, limiting their use. Building on our knowledge of these compounds, we developed a podophyllotoxin derivative, SU212 (Figure 1A), and demonstrated it is less toxic and more potent against TNBC cells (MDA-MB-231) than etoposide in vitro (Figure S1A). Etoposide targets DNA topoisomerase II by blocking its DNA binding. To determine whether the molecular mechanism of SU212 is similar to etoposide, we treated MDA-MB-231 cells with etoposide (5 μM) or SU212 (0.5 μM) for 6 h and then analyzed cell lysates for DNA topoisomerase II alpha (TOP2A) stabilization. The results suggest that etoposide stabilizes TOP2A, whereas SU212 does not (Figure S1B), indicating that SU212 has a different target than etoposide. To identify SU212’s molecular target, we employed the cellular thermal shift assay (CETSA). CETSA identifies protein-ligand interactions based on a change in the protein’s thermal stability. For these experiments, MDA-MB-231 cells were treated with either DMSO or SU212 (0.5 μM) for 1.5 h; then cells were harvested and exposed to a range of temperatures (37°C–67°C). The soluble fraction of proteins was labeled with tandem mass tag (TMT) and analyzed using mass spectrometry (Figure 1B). Of 1,074 soluble proteins, SU212 treatment significantly increased the thermal stability of two proteins, ENO1 and enolase 3 (ENO3) (p < 0.01 and R2 > 0.8, Figure 1C). SU212 treatment increases the stability of ENO1 and ENO3 by 7.9°C ± 1.36°C (Figure 1D). ENO1 and ENO3 are isoforms of the enolase enzyme and have structural similarities, making it difficult to differentiate between the two overlapping isoforms using this method. To determine whether SU212 interacts with both proteins, we performed a protein-ligand interaction analysis using biolayer interferometry (BLI). We used His-tagged ENO1 and ENO3 proteins, immobilized them with Ni-NTA sensors, and used BLI to measure their interaction with SU212. BLI results indicate that SU212 interacts more strongly with ENO1 compared to ENO3 (Figure 1E). We subsequently performed a dose-response study using BLI for the SU212:ENO1 interaction and found that SU212 interacts with ENO1 in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 1F). To further validate SU212-ENO1 binding, we conducted a binding assay using a reverse approach with biotinylated-SU212 and ENO1, utilizing surface plasmon resonance ([SPR] Biacore). In this assay, biotinylated SU212 was immobilized on a streptavidin sensor chip, and ENO1 (0.125–4 μM) was tested in 1X PBS buffer. The SPR results demonstrate that ENO1 binds strongly to SU212, providing additional validation for the direct SU212-ENO1 interaction (Figure S1C). Furthermore, immunoblotting suggests that SU212 strongly inhibits ENO1 protein expression in multiple TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, and EMT6) in a dose-dependent manner (Figure 1G, left). Consistent with the BLI results, SU212 treatment does not change ENO3 protein expression (Figure 1G, right) in all TNBC cell lines. To evaluate the potential pathway of ENO1 degradation caused by SU212 treatment, we co-treated TNBC cells with MG132 (proteasomal inhibitor), 3MA (autophagic inhibitor), and NH4Cl (lysosomal inhibitor), along with cycloheximide (CHX, protein synthesis inhibitor) and SU212. Protein expression analysis showed that co-treatment with either MG132 or 3MA partially rescued ENO1 degradation induced by SU212 (Figure 1H), suggesting that SU212 promotes ENO1 degradation through both proteasomal and autophagic pathways. This ENO1 degradation is followed by apoptotic cell death (Figure 1I), which occurs in a time- and dose-dependent manner. Because toxicity is one common issue for compounds that target glycolytic enzymes, we asked whether SU212 disrupts ENO1’s enzymatic activity, indicating orthosteric (at the active site) or allosteric (not at the active site) binding. We performed an enzymatic assay using purified ENO1 protein (cell free) in the presence of increasing concentrations of SU212 (0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.25, 0.5 μM). The results suggest that SU212 does not inhibit ENO1’s enzymatic activity to convert 2PG to PEP (Figure 1J). Generally, compounds that bind to non-orthosteric sites are less likely to have off-target effects. Overall, these results suggest that SU212 binds ENO1 specifically, does not directly inhibit ENO1 enzymatic activity, and the interaction results in inhibition of ENO1 protein expression in TNBC cells.

SU212 inhibits cellular glycolysis and the mTOR signaling pathway

Because SU212 binds ENO1 non-orthosterically, it does not directly affect ENO1 enzymatic activity; however, because SU212 binding results in ENO1 degradation, we hypothesized that SU212 would affect the cellular glycolytic rate. To assess the effects of SU212 on glycolytic rate in vitro, we performed a Seahorse XF glycolytic rate assay on the TNBC cell line MDA-MB-231 after treatment with SU212 or vehicle control for 1.5 h. We found that SU212 treatment (0.25 μM) inhibited the overall oxygen consumption rate and extracellular acidification rate along with the glycolytic rate (Figure 2A). This effect was specific to cancer cells, as SU212 treatment (0.25 and 0.5 μM) of normal breast and kidney cell lines (MCF-12A and HEK293, respectively) did not affect their glycolytic rate (Figure 2A). In support of this, SU212 also did not affect the viability of normal cells at concentrations that are cytotoxic to cancer cells (Figure S1D). Thereafter, we tested the effect of SU212 on glucose uptake in vivo using an F18-fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) uptake assay and positron emission tomography (PET) scanning. FDG is a glucose analog that accumulates in high glucose-demanding cells and can be used to measure glucose uptake by tumor cells in vivo with and without SU212 treatment. In this experiment, MDA-MB-231 cells were subcutaneously implanted in female NSG mice and allowed to grow up to 250–300 mm3 in diameter. Mice were randomized and treated with vehicle only or SU212 (30 mg/kg) intraperitoneally (i.p.) for 3 days. At the time of the PET scan, the tumor size of the SU212 treatment group was not significantly different from control (Figures 2B and 2C). On the day of analysis, mice were injected with F18-FDG intravenously and imaged using a microPET/computed tomography system after 30 min. The PET scan data suggested that SU212 treatment significantly reduces FDG uptake by tumor cells (Figures 2B and 2D). Thus, in vitro and in vivo data indicate that SU212 reduces cellular glycolytic rate, lowering the overall glucose demand of tumor cells. Because SU212 does not directly affect ENO1 enzymatic activity, the SU212 effects on glycolysis are likely due to ENO1 degradation (Figure 1H).

These data are in line with our previous global proteome profiling, which showed that treatment with 0.5 μM SU212 for 12 h inhibited the glycolysis and pentose phosphate pathways and pushed the cells toward the oxidative phosphorylation pathway. To evaluate the effects of SU212 earlier after exposure, we treated the human TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, and BT549) with SU212 for 6 h and analyzed SU212’s effect on the global proteome profile using TMT labeling and mass spectrometry analysis. These proteomics data suggested that SU212 treatment for 6 h significantly downregulated the mTORC1 signaling pathway and upregulated various ribosomal pathways (Figures 2E and 2F), providing further supporting evidence that treatment with SU212 dysregulates glucose metabolism through mTORC1.

SU212 treatment inhibits the tumor progression of TNBC models

Having observed SU212’s impact on cellular glycolysis, next, we explored SU212’s efficacy against TNBC cell lines. Initially, we performed viability screening on several human and mouse cell lines to confirm the broad effect of SU212. Supporting our previous data, these data indicated that a 48-h treatment with SU212 inhibited 50% cell viability within a range of 37–250 nM for human TNBC cells and from 39 to 850 nM for mouse TNBC cell lines (Figures S2A and S2B). On the other hand, normal cells MCF12A and HEK293 exhibited resistance, requiring a higher concentration of SU212 to inhibit 50% of cell viability, with effective IC50 concentrations ranging from 1.5 to 5.5 μM (Figure S1D). In addition, we assessed the impact of SU212 on the clonogenic potential of human (Figure S2C) and mouse (Figure S2D) TNBC cells. Experimental cells were treated with either vehicle (DMSO) or SU212 (0.1–0.5 μM) and allowed to form colonies for 6–10 days. By monitoring percent confluency, we found that SU212 treatment significantly inhibited TNBC cells’ clonogenic potential (Figures S2C and S2D). Furthermore, cell viability screening on the NCI60 cancer cell line panel indicated that in addition to TNBC, SU212 treatment also significantly inhibits the proliferation of eight other cancer types (Figure S2E).

Given this promising in vitro efficacy data, our previously published in vivo efficacy data, and because compounds targeting glycolysis have been limited by toxicity, we wanted to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and assess liver toxicity parameters in vivo. We performed an MTD assay in female C57BL/6 mice and SD rats. We administered a single dose of SU212 at 100, 200, and 400 mg/kg and observed animals for 72 h. We did not observe any significant weight loss (Figures S3A and S3B) or mortality (Figures 3A and 3B) in either mice or rats at any dose tested. Furthermore, we closely observed the rats for any signs of stress, including behavioral, neurological, and autonomic stresses. No signs of stress were observed in any animal (Figures S4A and S4B), suggesting that SU212 treatment up to 400 mg/kg was well tolerated in both mice and rats.

To assess liver toxicity and anti-tumor activity, we used two syngeneic orthotopic TNBC models, EMT6 and PY8119. TNBC cells were implanted in the mammary fat pad of female mice (Balb/c for EMT6 and C57BL/6 for PY8119) and treated with vehicle only or SU212 (30 mg/kg) i.p. for 5 days/week for 21 days (EMT6) or 24 days (PY8119) (Figure 3C). Tumor progression was measured at regular intervals, and results demonstrated that SU212 treatment significantly delayed tumor growth in both TNBC models, resulting in significantly lower tumor weight at experiment end (Figures 3D and 3E). At experiment end, to assess SU212 toxicity, we collected blood from the C57BL/6 mice bearing PY8119 tumors and analyzed blood serum for biochemical markers of liver (aspartate aminotransferase [AST], alanine aminotransferase [ALT], and alkaline phosphatase [ALP]) and nephron (blood urea nitrogen [BUN] and creatinine) toxicity. SU212 treatment for 24 days did not cause any significant changes in these markers of liver (Figure 3F) or nephrotoxicity (Figure 3G), indicating that SU212 treatment does not induce liver or kidney toxicity in C56BL/6 mice.

To evaluate the effect of SU212 treatment on tumor development and progression, we used the hemizygous MMTV-PyMT transgenic female mouse model. These mice develop palpable mammary tumors between 6 and 10 weeks of age. At age 6–7 weeks, female mice were randomized into two groups and treated with either SU212 (20 mg/kg, i.p.) or vehicle only, for 5 days a week. Overall survival, tumor weight, and the number of tumors were recorded (Figures 3H–3L). We found that SU212 treatment significantly improved overall survival (Figures 3H and 3I) and reduced tumor burden (Figures 3J and 3K) and incidence (Figure 3L) compared to the vehicle group.

To validate the in vivo efficacy of SU212 in a more clinically relevant model, we employed three orthotopic patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models. PDX models MA2821, CA1262, and CA1616 were obtained from the NCI-PDMR repository and were expanded in female NSG mice (Figure 4A). Each model is from a patient with a different ethnicity that went through various chemotherapies (Figures 4B–4D). NSG female mice were implanted with PDX tissue samples orthotopically, which grew to 60–70 mm3 prior to treatment. After randomization into two groups, mice were treated with either vehicle only or SU212 (30 mg/kg, i.p.) for 5 days/week for 21 days. Indeed, as in the syngeneic and transgenic models, SU212 treatment limited tumor growth of all three PDX models, resulting in a lower tumor burden at experiment end (Figures 4B–4D). Overall, in vivo data suggest that SU212 treatment inhibits tumor growth in all tested mouse- and human-origin TNBC mice models without significant toxicity.

SU212 treatment inhibits ENO1 subcellular localization and metastasis

In addition to its role in glycolysis, ENO1 participates in moonlight functions in cancer cells. For example, ENO1 in the cell membrane acts as a plasminogen receptor, converting plasminogen into plasmin and promoting extracellular matrix degradation, cancer cell invasion, migration, and metastasis. To evaluate the effect of SU212 treatment on ENO1’s moonlighting functions, we examined whether SU212 affected ENO1 subcellular localization. We treated TNBC cell lines MDA-MB-231 and EMT6 with a sublethal concentration of SU212 (0.1 μM) for 6 h. Cellular fragments were collected and analyzed for ENO1 protein expression. The cellular fractionation results suggested that SU212 significantly altered ENO1 subcellular localization, most significantly limiting membrane-bound ENO1, with more limited effects on nuclear and mitochondrial ENO1 (Figures 5A–5C). Furthermore, we found that SU212-induced inhibition of ENO1 cell membrane localization was partially reversed when TNBC cells were co-treated with MG132 and 3MA (Figure 5D).

Because SU212 limits cell membrane-localized ENO1, which facilitates cell migration and metastasis, we asked whether SU212 affects cancer cell migration and metastasis in vitro and in vivo. To examine the effects of SU212 on cell migration in vitro, we performed a Matrigel dot assay where TNBC cells are mixed with Matrigel (1:1), treated with low doses of SU212 and permitted to migrate and invade through the Matrigel. Across four TNBC cell lines, SU212 treatment strongly inhibited cell migration (Figure 5E). To evaluate the effect of SU212 on cancer cell migration and metastasis in vivo, we used the orthotopic EMT6 mouse model of TNBC, which leads to severe lung metastasis in immune-compromised mice. We implanted EMT6 cells in a mammary fat pad of female NSG mice, and mice were treated with vehicle only or SU212 (30 mg/kg) i.p. for 5 days/week for 21 days. At the end of treatment, mice were euthanized, lungs were collected, and lung metastasis was analyzed (Figures 5F–5H). SU212 treatment reduced lung metastasis ∼66% compared to the vehicle-only group (Figures 5G and 5H). Overall, these findings indicate that SU212-induced ENO1 degradation (Figure 1) influences its subcellular localization and associated moonlight functions.

SU212 treatment restrains tumor growth in hyperglycemic and hyperinsulinemic conditions

ENO1 is a significant player in cancer metabolism, particularly under hyperglycemic and hyperinsulinemic conditions. These conditions characterize the tumor microenvironment in patients with T2D and metabolic syndromes, which influences both cancer progression and therapeutic responses, making these patients particularly difficult to treat. To evaluate the effect of SU212 on cancer progression in conditions of hyperglycemia and hyperinsulinemia, we used the Leprdb (Db/Db) mouse model, which are obese and have elevated plasma insulin and blood sugar by age 8–9 weeks. These Db/Db mice were orthotopically implanted with PY8119 TNBC cells and treated with vehicle only or SU212 (10 mg/kg), i.p. for 5 days/week for 32 days. At the end of treatment, mice were euthanized, and tumors and other organs were collected. SU212 treatment significantly reduced tumor growth (p < 0.0001) (Figures 6A and 6B) and reduced overall tumor burden (p = 0.0033) (Figure 6C). Immunohistochemical staining for ENO1 of tumor samples suggests that SU212 treatment inhibits ENO1 expression (Figure 6D), as we observed in vitro (Figure 1). As Db/Db mice are identifiably obese and a commonly used model for fatty liver disease, we asked whether SU212 influenced these comorbidities. SU212 did not significantly affect overall mouse body weight (Figure 6E), but SU212 did cause a significant drop in blood glucose level (p = 0.0425) (Figure 6F), which occurred after only a single dose of SU212 (Figure 6G). Furthermore, at the end of treatment, we collected blood serum to analyze for markers of liver health and toxicity. These assays indicate that SU212 significantly reduces the level of AST) (p = 0.0278), ALT (p = 0.0105), alkaline phosphatase (p = 0.0034), and glutamate dehydrogenase (GLDH) (p = 0.0484) (Figure 6H), which are elevated in fatty liver conditions. The reduction of these markers after SU212 treatment suggests an improvement in the fatty liver conditions of Db/Db mice. To evaluate this, we weighed and performed H&E staining on livers obtained from vehicle-only and SU212-treated mice and scored them for fat-associated space. Consistent with the reduction of liver toxicity markers, SU212-treated mice had significantly reduced liver weight (p = 0.0019) and reduced fat-associated space by 80%–90% (p < 0.0001) compared to control mice (Figures 6I–6K). In hyperinsulinemic states, ENO1 exhibits a complex interaction with insulin signaling pathways, which typically influences metabolic processes and cell growth. Studies have demonstrated that elevated insulin levels can lead to increased ENO1 activity, further driving the glycolytic flux in cancer cells. To evaluate the effect of SU212 on metabolism-associated genes and pathways in the Db/Db model, we profiled the tumor samples from these mice for the mRNA expression of metabolism-associated genes. mRNA profiling clearly indicated that in comparison to SU212-treated tumors, vehicle-treated tumors only upregulated genes associated with the PI3K pathway and downregulated the mitochondrial respiration pathway (Figure 6L). On the other hand, SU212 treatment significantly downregulated the PI3K pathway (p = 0.0029) and upregulated the mitochondrial respiration pathway (p = 0.0012) in comparison to untreated tumors (Figure 6L). This is in line with our previous observation in TNBC cell lines that SU212 treatment pushes the cells toward oxidative phosphorylation. Overall, in vivo studies in a diabetic mouse model suggest that SU212 restrains tumor growth in hyperglycemic and hyperinsulinemic conditions and may help improve diabetic and fatty liver conditions.

Pharmacokinetics and toxicological analysis of SU212

Pharmacokinetic (pK) properties and toxicity testing are crucial components in the development of small molecule drugs. These assessments inform the efficacy, safety, and viability of pharmaceutical compounds. Given the therapeutic potential of SU212 that we observed in numerous in vitro and in vivo TNBC models, to determine whether SU212 is a candidate for clinical translation, we performed pK and non-specific toxicity-associated experiments (Figure 7). First, we determined the pK profile of SU212 in female C57BL/6 mice and SD rats. A single dose of SU212 (50 mg/kg) was given at time 0, blood was collected at different time points, and SU212 concentration was determined using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS). We observed concentration maxima (Cmax = 1,043 ng/mL) for SU212 in mice at 1 h (Tmax) (Figures 7A and 7B) and in rats at 15 min (Cmax = 915 ng/mL) (Figures 7C and 7D). SU212 showed a sustained clearance rate with 5.2 h T1/2 in mice and 8.61 h T1/2 in rats (Figures 7B and 7D). Next, to determine SU212’s distribution, mice and rats were administered a single dose of SU212 and euthanized 2, 4, and 24 h after treatment, followed by organ (brain, lung, liver, kidney, ovary, spleen, and mammary fat pad) collection. Tissue pieces of equal weight were lysed and processed to determine SU212 concentration using LC-MS/MS. LC-MS/MS data indicate that SU212 was available and distributed to all the analyzed organs, including the brain, in both mice and rats (Figures 7E and 7F).

Drug metabolism plays a significant role in toxicity because metabolic products resulting from the breakdown of drugs can lead to toxicity. To assess SU212’s metabolic by-products, we performed metabolite identification in human liver microsomes. We mixed SU212 with human liver microsomes and collected fractions at different time intervals, followed by LC-MS/MS identification. LC-MS/MS data indicate that metabolism of SU212 produces no toxic metabolites (Figure S5). Furthermore, to assess SU212’s specificity, we performed a kinase profiling and binding assay. For these assays, we used ∼20-fold higher concentration (10 μM) of SU212 compared to average IC50 values to identify any dose-dependent non-specific targets. First, we performed the activity screening of SU212 on 450 human kinases and disease-relevant mutant variants. Many kinases play a crucial role in the normal functioning of the human body, and non-specific kinase inhibition often leads to adverse effects on patient health. The screening profile indicates that SU212 does not interfere with the activity of any kinases at 10 μM concentration (Figure 7G). In addition to kinases, other molecular targets such as G-protein-coupled receptors, ionic channels, enzymes, transporters, and nuclear receptors are present in primary battery systems (central nervous, cardiovascular, and respiratory) as well as in secondary organ systems (gastrointestinal and renal). Inhibiting these molecular targets could result in unwanted adverse effects. To test the effect of SU212 on these molecular targets, we used the SafetyScreen 44 panel (Eurofins, France). The screening results suggest that SU212 (10 μM) does not interact with or inhibit most molecular targets, including enzymes (50% inhibition and above was considered significant effects of the test compounds) (Figures 7H and 7I). Overall, pK and non-specific toxicity assays suggest that SU212 has a drug-like profile.

Discussion

Podophyllotoxin, a naturally occurring plant compound derived predominantly from the roots and rhizomes of Podophyllum peltatum and Sinopodophyllum hexandrum, belongs to a class of phytochemicals known as aryltetralin lignans. Podophyllotoxin and its derivatives, notably etoposide and teniposide, are utilized in clinical oncology for their anti-tumor efficacy. Etoposide and teniposide remain important chemotherapeutic agents in the management of certain malignancies; however, their limitations must be recognized and addressed. Etoposide, for instance, is associated with severe side effects, such as myelosuppression, which restrict its use. Our podophyllotoxin derivative, SU212, has significant anticancer activity, with better efficacy and less toxicity than etoposide. These properties position SU212 as a promising next-generation compound within this chemical family.

SU212 inhibits the glycolytic enzyme, ENO1. By initiating ENO1 degradation, SU212 prevents its participation in glycolytic metabolism, leading to decreased glycolytic activity, glucose uptake, and metastatic processes in TNBC tumor cells. By focusing on ENO1, it may be feasible to undermine the metabolic adaptability of cancer cells, thereby improving the efficacy of existing therapies and potentially enhancing patient outcomes, especially in aggressive cancers like TNBC, where metabolic reprogramming is vital to tumor aggression and drug resistance. For example, chemotherapy and radiation rely on inducing cellular stress and DNA damage, processes that are amplified in metabolically stressed environments created by ENO1 inhibition. Additionally, targeting ENO1 could help mitigate resistance to chemotherapy and radiation, which often exploit metabolic weaknesses of tumor cells. Capello et al. (2015) investigated the effects of ENO1 silencing. They found that cancer cells, including MDA-MB-231, activate catabolic processes to restore pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, and support oxidative phosphorylation. ENO1-silenced cells induce cell-cycle arrest and enter senescence. Consistent with this, inhibiting ENO1 with SU212 treatment in TNBC cells leads to mitotic arrest, promotes a shift toward oxidative phosphorylation, and suppresses glycolysis. Finally, metabolic modulation by SU212 may counteract the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment by enhancing oxidative metabolism in immune cells or reducing lactate-driven immunosuppression, suggesting that SU212 could modulate immunotherapy effectiveness. Exploring these combinations in future studies could reveal additional therapeutic benefits and inform clinical strategies.

TNBC is an aggressive breast cancer subtype, which is known for rapid growth, metastasis, and poor prognosis. TNBC lacks targeted therapies, thus patients rely on aggressive approaches like systemic and neoadjuvant chemotherapy and therapy. New treatments, such as immunotherapy and targeted options, are being explored to improve outcomes for this challenging, heterogeneous disease. TNBC is highly associated with increased aerobic glycolysis, relying heavily on glycolysis for energy to support rapid proliferation and survival. Thus, the capacity of SU212 to diminish the Warburg effect and shift metabolism toward oxidative phosphorylation suggests that SU212 might be particularly effective in TNBC. Our data demonstrate that SU212 decreases TNBC glucose uptake and significantly reduces tumor growth, as demonstrated across multiple syngeneic TNBC models and three PDX TNBC models, underscoring SU212’s potential to disrupt TNBC’s metabolic dependencies in cells.

T2D and obesity significantly influence TNBC progression by promoting inflammation, insulin resistance, and adipokine imbalances that boost tumor growth and treatment resistance. Indeed, T2D is linked to higher risk and poorer prognosis in TNBC, with studies showing a 20%–30% increased risk. These metabolic issues lead to the release of tumor-promoting exosomes and increased expression of glycolytic enzymes like ENO1, supporting cancer cell growth, invasion, and survival via the Warburg effect and PI3K/AKT pathways. Additionally, the diabetic environment, with elevated inflammatory cytokines like interleukin-6 and tumor necrosis factor alpha, can alter the tumor microenvironment, helping cancer cells survive chemotherapy and develop resistance. Thus, managing metabolic disorders alongside cancer therapies may improve TNBC outcomes. Research indicates that ENO1 may enhance insulin-mediated signaling, thereby promoting cancer cell survival and proliferation in an insulin-rich environment. In a Db/Db mouse model, SU212 treatment significantly inhibited tumor progression and reduced tumor burden, while also decreasing liver weight and blood glucose levels. SU212 improved fatty liver conditions by reducing liver damage markers and restoring fat-associated liver space. Additionally, SU212 treatment altered metabolism-associated gene expression, downregulating the PI3K pathway and upregulating mitochondrial respiration, aligning with a shift toward oxidative phosphorylation. Overall, SU212 may suppress tumor progression in diabetic conditions and improve fatty liver health.

Finally, toxicity is a concern for compounds that target the glycolytic pathway. We performed pK and toxicity testing of SU212 in mice and rats, which showed favorable drug properties, including sustained clearance, distribution across organs, and no toxic metabolites. Additionally, kinase profiling and screening for molecular target interactions revealed that SU212 does not interfere with key biological systems, suggesting a drug-like profile with minimal toxicity.

Current ENO1 inhibitors include an anti-ENO1 antibody and ciwujianoside E, which target surface ENO1 to decrease invasion and metastasis. However, these agents do not kill cancer cells, limiting their anticancer potential. Granulin A binds to ENO1 and inhibits migration, but its exact mechanism is unknown. ENOblock affects non-glycolytic functions of ENO1 but lacks specificity and may cause off-target effects. Thus, a highly selective ENO1 inhibitor that targets cancer cells specifically, sparing normal cells, is still needed and urgently required. In summary, targeting ENO1 with SU212 offers an effective strategy to manage TNBC, and other T2D-linked cancers, by exploiting the cancer cell’s metabolic vulnerabilities. This strategic inhibition opens avenues for further research into metabolic therapies as adjuncts or alternatives to conventional chemotherapeutics, thereby improving outcomes for patients afflicted by this challenging form of breast cancer. Integrating metabolic modulators like SU212 into the treatment paradigm represents a promising direction in enhancing therapeutic efficacy for tumors that traditionally have limited responses to existing treatments.

Limitations of the study

The current study identified ENO1 as the primary target of SU212 in the CETSA assay. Further investigation is required to explore the effects of SU212 on other targets and their contribution to SU212 effects and potential toxicity. In this study, we used TNBC models to assess compound target engagement and efficacy; however, these findings need to be validated in other cancer histology models to establish the full spectrum of its efficacy and target specificity.

Method details

SU212 synthesis

2-((2,3-Dihydro-1H-inden-5-yl)amino)ethan-1-ol

To a solution of 2,3-dihydro-1H-inden-5-amine (22.0 g, 165 mmol, 1.00 equiv.) and pyridine (17.0 g, 17.3 mL, 215 mmol, 1.30 equiv.) in 300mL CH2Cl2 under nitrogen atmosphere was slowly added 2-chloroethyl carbonochloridate (24.8 g, 17.9 mL, 173 mmol, 1.05 equiv.) at 0°C. The solution turned brownish to orange and a participate was forming during the addition. After complete addition, the ice bath was removed and the reaction mixture was stirred for 4 h at room temperature, after which water was added, and the resulting two-phase mixture was separated. The organic phase was washed three times with water, dried over Na2SO4 and concentrated. The oily residue was dissolved in 250 mL EtOH, KOH (37.1 g, 661 mmol, 4.00 equiv.) was added and the resulting mixture was heated to 90°C overnight. On the next day, the mixture was allowed to cool to room temperature, and EtOH was evaporated under reduced pressure. The residue was purified via column chromatography to obtain the desired product in 65% yield. (19.0 g, 107 mmol). 1H NMR was in accordance with the literature.

CETSA analysis

CETSA followed by LC/MS-based proteomic analysis was conducted on MDA-MB-231 cells treated with or without SU212 (0.5 μM), as previously described. In brief, MDA-MB-231 cells were plated on 15 cm cell culture plates. When the cells reached 60–70% confluency, they were treated with either the vehicle (DMSO) or SU212 (0.5 μM) for 1.5 h. After treatment, the cells were collected, washed, and resuspended in 1X PBS at a concentration of 1x106 cells per 100 μL. A 100 μL cell suspension was then dispensed into ten separate PCR tubes. Tubes were exposed to temperatures of 37, 41, 44, 47, 50, 53, 56, 59, 63, and 67°C for 3 min using a thermal cycler (Veriti Thermal Cycler, Applied Biosystems, USA), followed by a 2-min incubation at room temperature. Each tube was snap frozen using liquid nitrogen. Cells were lysed using freezing and thawing, followed by a collection of soluble fractions. Soluble proteins were quantified and equal volume equivalent to 100 μg protein in a control sample exposed to 37°C temperature. Wach samples were labeled with Tandem Mass Tag (TMT) using the manufacturer’s protocol (TMT10plex Isobaric Label Reagent Set, # 90110, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). TMT-labeled samples were analyzed using LC-MS/MS.

BLI analysis

Physical interaction between enolase proteins (ENO1&3) and SU212 using Biolayer Interferometry analysis by following the Octet User Manual. The assay consisted of six steps: an initial baseline measurement (45 s), loading (600 s), washing (60 s), a second baseline measurement (240 s), association (180 s), and dissociation (180 s). During the loading step, recombinant His Tag-Enolases were immobilized on NTA Biosensors using a kinetic buffer (1X PBS) with a final reagent volume of 50 μL, which contained 50 μg/mL of ENO1 (#11554-H07E−100, Sino Biological) and ENO3 (#14270-H07E, Sino Biological), all placed in a black 384-well microplate. This process generated a wavelength shift response signal in the range of 1–1.5 nm. Etoposide was used as a control analog to assess specificity. The concentrations of SU212 (10, 5, and 1μM) were prepared in PBS at a final volume of 50 μL in a black 384-well microplate for the association and dissociation steps. The response signal from the negative control (biosensors with no Enolase protein loaded) was subtracted from the experimental values at each step before further data processing.

Surface plasmon resonance (SPR)

Experiments were conducted using a Biacore T200 (GE Healthcare) instrument at 25°C. Biotinylated-SU212 was captured via the biotin on a Streptavidin Chip (GE Healthcare). ENO1 binding studies were performed in 1X PBS (10 mM phosphate buffer, 2.7 mM KCl, 0.137 M NaCl) at pH 7.4. To examine ENO1 binding at six concentrations (0.125–4 μM), injections were made over immobilized ligands at 30 μL/min for 120 s, with a regeneration step of 600 s. After each injection, the system was washed with 50% DMSO. DMSO correction curves were created by injecting running buffers with serial DMSO concentrations from 4.5% to 5.8%. All data were adjusted for non-specific binding by subtracting signals from a control cell without immobilized ligand.

Cell viability assay

Cell invasion assay

50,000 cells were suspended in 25 μL of their respective media and mixed with an equal volume of Matrigel (#356237, Corning). 50 μL of this cell suspension was dispensed in the center of a well in a 12-well cell culture plate (#3598, Corning-Costar). After solidification, Matrigel dots were covered with media containing either vehicle or respective concentrations of SU212. Cells were incubated for 5–7 days and allowed to invade and migrate from the Matrigel dot. Media was replaced with fresh media every alternate day. The percent confluence was measured using a Tecan multimode plate reader on days 0, 3, and the last day.
