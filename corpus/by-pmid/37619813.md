# Photodynamic augmentation of oncolytic virus therapy for central nervous system malignancies.

## Abstract

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) have emerged as a clinical therapeutic modality potentially effective for cancers that evade conventional therapies, including central nervous system malignancies. Rationally designed combinatorial strategies can augment the efficacy of OVs by boosting tumor-selective cytotoxicity and modulating the tumor microenvironment (TME). Photodynamic therapy (PDT) of cancer not only mediates direct neoplastic cell death but also primes the TME to sensitize the tumor to secondary therapies, allowing for the combination of two potentially synergistic therapies with broader targets. Here, we created G47Δ-KR, clinical oncolytic herpes simplex virus G47Δ that expresses photosensitizer protein KillerRed (KR). Optical properties and cytotoxic effects of G47Δ-KR infection followed by amber LED illumination (peak wavelength: 585-595 nm) were examined in human glioblastoma (GBM) and malignant meningioma (MM) models in vitro. G47Δ-KR infection of tumor cells mediated KR expression that was activated by LED and produced reactive oxygen species, leading to cell death that was more robust than G47Δ-KR without light. In vivo, we tested photodynamic-oncolytic virus (PD-OV) therapy employing intratumoral injection of G47Δ-KR followed by laser light tumor irradiation (wavelength: 585 nm) in GBM and MM xenografts. PD-OV therapy was feasible in these models and resulted in potent anti-tumor effects that were superior to G47Δ-KR alone (without laser light) or laser light alone. RNA sequencing analysis of post-treatment tumor samples revealed PD-OV therapy-induced increases in TME infiltration of variable immune cell types. This study thus demonstrated the proof-of-concept that G47Δ-KR enables PD-OV therapy for neuro-oncological malignancies and warrants further research to advance potential clinical translation.

Introduction

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are either genetically engineered or naturally occurring replication-competent viruses that are able to replicate selectively in cancer cells and destroy them. OVs, in general, have unique, dual mechanisms of action (MOA) characterized by direct cell killing and elicitation of anti-tumor immune responses secondary to OV-mediated cancer cell death. Preclinical and clinical studies have been extensively investigating the potential of OVs as a therapeutic modality for the malignant primary brain tumor glioblastoma (GBM) that exhibits a notorious resistance to conventional therapies such as radiation and chemotherapy. The recent conditional approval of oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 1 (oHSV) G47Δ (Teserpaturev, Delytact®) for progressive or recurrent malignant gliomas represents a significant milestone and provided a boost in clinical translation of a variety of OV platforms in neuro-oncology. We have reported the capacity of G47Δ to replicate in and kill patient-derived GBM stem-like cells (GSCs) and to increase survival in mice bearing orthotopic GSC-derived GBM, which may underlie its activity against GBM demonstrated in clinical trials. In neuro-oncology, therapeutic utility of G47Δ is not limited to malignant gliomas. We have shown the therapeutic efficacy of intratumoral injection of G47Δ in patient-derived orthotopic models of malignant meningioma (MM), a rare subset of meningioma that is often lethal. G47Δ is thus a clinical therapeutic candidate for GBM and MM, two of the deadly malignancies in the central nervous system.

Combination approaches have been successfully applied both preclinically and clinically to overcome cancer evasion of monotherapy and enhance overall efficacy. There is mounting evidence that combination strategies centered around OVs are safe and effective for cancers such as GBM. We have shown that targeted agents like small molecule inhibitors of poly (ADP)ribose polymerases (PARP) and TGF-β receptor could augment the potency of oHSVs, resulting in superior efficacy in human GSC GBM models in vivo. On the other hand, therapeutic transgenes can be incorporated into the oHSV genome to be expressed in infected cancer cells. oHSV-mediated secretion of soluble tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) exerted potent anti-tumor effects via induction of apoptotic cell death in GBM cells. Treatment of GBM with oHSV armed with immune stimulatory cytokine interleukin-12 (IL-12) modulated the tumor microenvironment (TME) by activating effector T cells and downregulating tumor-associated vasculature. These examples represent a variety of mechanisms by which oHSV combinatorial strategies can become impactful. Simultaneous induction of robust tumor cell killing and beneficial modification of the TME is a rational approach to maximizing overall anti-tumor efficacy and achieving durable tumor control.

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) of cancer uses photochemical reactions of a photo-activatable molecule, photosensitizer (PS), to light of a specific wavelength to induce cyto-ablative effects. PDT is driven by light excitation of PS that generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), damaging cell membranes and organelles, leading to cell death. PDT using chemical PSs such as 5-aminolevulic acid-induced protopor-phyrin IX (PpIX) and talaporfin sodium has been actively tested clinically for CNS malignancies including GBM and MM. Of note, recent research has elucidated that PDT not only exerts powerful local cytotoxicity but also transiently primes the TME to sensitize the tumor to enhanced secondary therapies such as chemo- and immunotherapy, a process termed “photodynamic priming”. Analogous to OV, PDT can trigger inflammation and anti-tumor immune responses in the TME. Thus, despite stark differences in the outcome mediators, ROS in PDT and virus replication in OV, OV and PDT are driven by dual MOA: potent killing of cancer cells and elicitation of anti-tumor immune responses. Interestingly, both OV and PDT are capable of induction of multiple cell death pathways; apoptosis, necroptosis, autophagy and pyroptosis for OV, while apoptosis and necrosis for PDT, with all of these potentially contributing to treatment-triggered immunogenic cell death.

We hypothesize that the unique priming features of PDT enable the combination of PDT and OV to mechanistically interact to mediate potent cancer-selective death and drastically alter the TME. We term this new anti-cancer strategy PD-OV therapy. To this end, we have created a new recombinant oHSV, G47Δ-KR. G47Δ-KR is based on clinical oHSV G47Δ and armed with genetically-encoded, fluorescent photosensitizer KillerRed (KR) that is a well-documented, potently phototoxic protein capable of producing superoxide (O2•–) upon light excitation (Fig. 1A). We set out to characterize the therapeutic activity of PD-OV that takes advantage of oHSV G47Δ-KR infection of cancer cells, followed by light activation of KR expressed by infected cancer cells. After confirming enhanced potency of PD-OV therapy in human GBM and MM models in vitro, we advanced to proof-of-concept in vivo investigations of PD-OV using intratumoral G47Δ-KR injection and laser light irradiation in xenograft models.

Materials and methods

Cell lines

U87MG (U87) human GBM cells were from ATCC (Manassas, VA). MGG4 cells were established from a patient with GBM under an IRB approved protocol. Human MM cells IOMM-Lee (NF2 intact) and CH157 (NF2 loss) were obtained from Dr. Randy Jensen (University of Utah) and Dr. Wenya Bi (Brigham and Women’s Hospital), respectively. These tumor cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal calf serum. African green monkey Vero cells were from ATCC and grown in DMEM supplemented with 10% calf serum. Short tandem repeat analysis was performed to authenticate the human cell lines (IDEXX, Westbrook, ME). All experiments were performed with mycoplasma-free cells.

Generation of U87-KR cells

Mammalian expression vector pKillerRed-mem (#FP966) was purchased from Evrogen (Moscow, Russia). Plasmid DNA was transfected to U87 cells using Lipofectamine 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA), and stable transfectants were derived by 4-week exposure to G418 (InvivoGen, San Diego, CA) at 400 μg/ml. Resulting cells were further subjected to flow cytometric sorting of KR-positive cells, establishing U87-KR cells.

Construction of G47Δ-KR

G47Δ is a multi-mutated oHSV derived from strain F. G47Δ-KR was constructed using the bacterial artificial chromosome (BAC)-based flip-flop recombination method, as previously described. Briefly, cDNA of the membrane-targeted KR from pKillerRed-mem was subcloned into the shuttle plasmid pFLS-IE4/5 under an HSV IE4/5 promoter. The resulting plasmid DNA was recombined with G47Δ-BAC DNA using Cre recombinase (New England Biolabs, Ipswich, MA), followed by transformation of DH10B (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and clone characterization by restriction enzyme analysis. Properly recombined clones were co-transfected with Flp-expressing pCAGS-FLPe (Addgene, Watertown, MA) into Vero cells, to excise BAC and EGFP-derived sequences flanked by FRT sites. Resulting plaques were harvested, isolated, purified and amplified in Vero cells. Plaques were stained with X-gal using 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-β-D-galactoside (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

Assay for the optical property of KillerRed

The excitation and emission spectrum of KR was determined by scanning cells using the Optimization software of the SpectraMax iD3 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices, San Jose, CA), according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

G47Δ-KR-mediated photodynamic therapy in vitro

Tumor cells were seeded at 5000–10,000 cells per well in black-walled 96-well plates with clear-bottom (Corning, Corning, NY). The next day, cells were infected with G47Δ-KR at MOI = 0.2–5, as indicated in figure legends. Forty-eight hours post infection, when KR fluorescence was detected in more than 80% of cells, amber LED light (590 nm ± 80 nm, where 80 nm is the half-bandwidth, Luxeon C PC-A, Alberta, Canada) was given to cells by a stable direct current (0.7 amp, 1.16 Watt/cm2) from a DC power supply (model 1762, B&K precision, Yorba Linda, CA) for 3 h at room temperature. During light illumination, the LEDs were fixed on a metal heat-sink and two small fans continuously provided an air current to minimize thermal effects (Supplementary Fig. 1). The plates that did not receive light were also removed from tissue culture incubators and kept at room temperature for 3 h. At the completion of the light treatment, photobleach of KR fluorescence was confirmed under a fluorescent microscope. The plates were returned to tissue culture incubators, and 2 h after the completion of lighting, subjected to ATP-based cytotoxicity and cell death assays. Each experiment was done in quadruplicates (i.e., 4. wells per experimental group), with the 4 wells for lighting laid-out separately from each other in the plate (Supplementary Fig. 1, Right). In vitro experiments were repeated at least twice.

ROS assay

Intracellular ROS, especially superoxide and hydroxyl radicals, in cells was quantified by the Fluorometric Intracellular ROS Assay kit from Millipore-Sigma (Burlington, MA). Cells in 96-well white plates were infected with G47Δ-KR. About 30 h later when KR fluorescence was detected in more than 80% of cells, the cells were given the ROS reagent and LED light as above for 15 min. U87 and U87-KR cells were subjected to the same protocol without G47Δ-KR infection. A fluorescent product derived from ROS reaction with a ROS sensor was captured by fluorescent microscope and the green fluorescence intensity was measured by FIJI.

ATP-based cytotoxicity assay

Treatment-induced cytotoxic effects were quantified by ATP-based CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay from Promega (Madison, WI), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Luminescence was quantified with the SpectraMax iD3 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader.

Cell death assay

To quantify the degree of cell death associated with disintegration of the cell membrane, CytoTox-Glo Cytotoxicity Assay (Promega) was used, which measures extracellular activity of dead-cell protease using a cell impermeant peptide substrate, AAF-aminoluciferin. Luciferase-generated luminescence was measured with the SpectraMax iD3 Multi-Mode Microplate Reader.

G47Δ-KR injection to xenografts and histopathology

U87 cells (2 million cells suspended in 100 μl serum-free DMEM) were implanted into the right flank subcutaneous area in 8–9-week-old female athymic mice (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA), and allowed to form tumors. When the tumor size reached 7–8 mm in the longest diameter, concentrated G47Δ-KR at a dose of 3 × 107 pfu (in 10 μl of PBS) was intratumorally injected. Two days later, the tumors were harvested and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde overnight. Frozen sections (7 μm-thick) were prepared and subjected to standard hematoxylin and eosin staining, X-gal staining as previously described, and fluorescent microscopy.

Efficacy studies in xenografts

U87 and IOMM-Lee xenograft models were generated in 8–9-week-old female athymic mice by implanting 2 million cells in the right flank. When the tumor volume reached 50 mm3, the mice were randomized to 4 groups: control group (untreated or PBS injection), laser light group, G47Δ-KR group, and PD-OV group (n = 5/group), and G47Δ-KR intratumorally injected as described above in the G47Δ-KR and PD-OV groups. Two days later, fiber-coupled semiconductor laser light (wavelength 585 nm, power = 100 mW, 10 mm collimated beam, MGL-U-585, CNI laser, Changchun, P. R. China) was illuminated to the tumors (0.13 Watt/cm2) for 30 min thorough the skin. Tumor size was measured daily using a caliper, and the tumor volume calculated using the formula: Volume (mm3) = 1/2xLxS2, where L is the longest and S the perpendicular diameters of the tumor. Follow-up was terminated and the animals euthanized when tumor size reached or exceeded the volume of 700 mm3. All animal procedures used in this work were approved by IACUC at Massachusetts General Hospital.

RNA sequencing

U87 and IOMM-Lee xenografts in athymic mice were treated as described above with intratumoral PBS, laser light, intratumoral G47Δ-KR, and PD-OV (intratumoral G47Δ-KR, followed 2 days later by laser light) (N = 3/group). All the tumors were harvested 48 h after laser light irradiation, which is 96 h after G47Δ-KR or PBS, and subjected to total RNA extraction using Trizol (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to manufacturer’s instructions. Total RNA (1 μg) underwent two rounds of mRNA purification (polyA-selection) using Dynabeads mRNA DIRECT Kit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). Double-stranded cDNA was generated using the Superscript III First-Strand Synthesis System (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA). The cDNA products were used to construct libraries with the Nextera XT DNA Sample Preparation Kit (Illumina, Inc., San Diego, CA). Libraries were sequenced on the Illumina NextSeq 500. Reads were aligned to the hg38 human genome and mm10 mouse genome using Spliced Transcripts Alignment to a Reference (STAR). Data were analyzed as previously described, and normalized to the total number of reads per kilobase of exon per million (RPKM). RNA sequencing data has been deposited to NCBI and publicly available as GSE222895.

Differential gene expression analysis

Differential gene expression (DGE) analysis was performed using the noiseqbio function with the cpm-based filter in the NOISeq package in R. The cpm-based filter was used to filter genes with very low counts in all samples and therefore low likelihood of a significant difference in expression being detected. DGE analysis was then performed on the remaining samples. Genes were considered differentially expressed if the fold change was at least 1.5 in either direction and false discovery rate less than 0.05. Based on this list of differentially expressed genes, gene ontology analysis was performed using the GOSeq package in R. Gene ontologies were considered over-represented if the adjusted p-values were less than 0.05.

Tumor microenvironment immune cell characterization

A deconvolution algorithm, murine Microenvironment Cell Population counter (mMCP-counter), was applied to the bulk RNA-seq data for each of the mice. Populations of 14 different cell types were calculated in terms of mMCP-counter scores, an arbitrary unit, which can be compared between different mice but not different cell types. The values obtained were then plotted as heat maps with accompanying dendrograms using the heatmap function in R. Another set of mice with U87 xenografts were treated with intratumoral PBS, laser light, intratumoral G47Δ-KR, and PD-OV (intratumoral G47Δ-KR, followed 2 days later by laser light) (N = 3/group). All the tumors were harvested 48 h after laser light irradiation, which is 96 h after G47Δ-KR or PBS. Formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded sections of the tumors were subjected to immunohistochemistry with anti-mouse CD68 antibody (ab125212, Abcam) as previously described. The number of positively-stained cells were counted in at least 3 high-power fields from each tumor to yield the average number of CD68+ cells per field.

Statistical analysis

Comparisons of data in viral replication and cytotoxicity assays were performed using a two-tailed Student t-test (unpaired) or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Bonferroni multiple comparisons. All statistical analyses were performed using Prism 9 Software (Graph-Pad, v9.3.1). For the differential gene expression, statistical analysis was conducted using the NOISeq and GOSeq packages in R, with Bonferroni multiple comparisons. P values of less than 0.05 were considered significant.

Results

KillerRed is activated by 585–595 nm light, generates ROS and kills cancer cells in vitro

We first characterized the optical and biological properties of KR using U87 GBM cells that are stably transduced with KR cDNA (U87-KR). Scanning U87-KR cells with a multi-mode microplate reader showed the excitation and emission spectrum of KR displaying peaks at 570–590 nm (amber) and 620–640 nm (red), respectively, which was absent in nontransduced U87 (U87-WT) cells (Fig. 1B). Light exposure by LED (peak wavelength around 590 nm, as shown in Supplemental Fig. 1) induced robust photobleaching in U87-KR cells (Fig. 1C). Fluorometric intracellular ROS assay demonstrated a marked increase in light-induced production of ROS in U87-KR cells compared with U87-WT cells (Fig. 1D), confirmed by quantification of green fluorescence intensity (Fig. 1E). We next determined the cytotoxic effects of KR-driven PDT in vitro. ATP-based cell cytotoxicity assay showed that LED illumination (3 h) slightly induced cytotoxicity in U87-WT cells, while greater reduction in cellular ATP levels was observed in U87-KR cells (Fig. 1F). To assess cell death and impaired cell membrane integrity, we measured dead-cell protease activity, revealing a potent induction of cell death in U87-KR cells after exposure to LED, in contrast with a modest effect found in control U87-WT cells (Fig. 1G). These results showed that KR is activated by appropriate light, generates ROS and kills cancer cells in vitro.

585–595 nm light treatment activates KillerRed and enhances G47Δ-KR virotherapy against CNS tumor cells in vitro

Confirmation of expected activity of KR in GBM cells led us to generate an oHSV incorporating KR to begin investigation of PD-OV therapy. We used the BAC-based flip-flop recombination method to construct G47Δ armed with a KR cDNA driven by an HSV immediate early promoter, named G47Δ-KR (Fig. 2A). This KR, also used for U87-KR, is a membrane localization version of KR (Fig. 2B), which was shown to induce more effective cell death, presumably because of lipid oxidation. Reporter lacZ in G47Δ-KR allows convenient chemical detection of infected cells (Fig. 2C).

We next determined the excitation/emission spectrum in human GBM (U87) and MM (IOMM-Lee) cells after infection with G47Δ-KR. Both cell models were infectable with G47Δ-KR, fluorescing red, but U87 needed higher MOI to achieve 100% infectability, indicating relatively poor permissiveness to oHSV. Following G47Δ-KR infection, both U87 and IOMM-Lee displayed the same optical properties as shown with KR cDNA transfection (Figs. 2D and 1B). Amber LED photobleached KR fluorescence in G47Δ-KR-infected cells in a time-dependent manner, and complete photobleach took 2–3 h (Supplemental Figs. 2A and B). G47Δ-KR infection followed by light illumination, i.e., PD-OV therapy, mediated the greatest cytotoxic effects and increase of cell death in U87 cells, as compared with light alone and G47Δ-KR without light (Fig. 2E and F). PD-OV therapy induced ROS production, but light alone and G47Δ-KR alone did not (Fig. 2G, Supplementary Fig. 2C). In IOMM-Lee MM cells, we found that PD-OV therapy caused more robust decrease of cellular ATP levels as well as increased cell death, while G47Δ-KR alone had milder but significant cytotoxicity (Fig. 2H and I). PD-OV therapy, but not G47Δ-KR alone, induced ROS production as was seen with U87 GBM cells (Fig. 2J, Supplementary Fig. 2D).

We continued to test PD-OV therapy in additional GBM and MM models to validate findings obtained with U87 and IOMM-Lee cells. We chose a patient-derived GBM cell line model MGG4 since U87 does not faithfully represent patient GBM. MGG4 cells were infectable with G47Δ-KR and the optical properties of infected cells were very similar to what was observed with U87 and IOMM-Lee (Fig. 3A). LED illumination was able to photobleach KR fluorescence in G47Δ-KR-infected MGG4 (Fig. 3B), which was accompanied by the greatest cytotoxic impact, indicating the superior efficacy of PD-OV therapy (Fig. 3C). Due to limited availability of cell culture models of MM, we chose NF2-mutant, CH157 cells as the second MM model. We confirmed the expected optical properties of G47Δ-KR and potent cytotoxic effects of G47Δ-KR-driven PD-OV therapy in the CH157 MM model (Supplementary Figs. 3A–C).

Collectively, G47Δ-KR enabled PD-OV therapy that is more effective than OV monotherapy in vitro models of neuro-oncological malignancies.

G47Δ-KR can infect and spread in a GBM xenograft model in vivo

We next advanced to test G47Δ-KR in human xenografts in vivo. U87 GBM xenografts were generated (Fig. 4A) and injected with G47Δ-KR (at 3 × 107 pfu) intratumorally, followed two days later by tumor harvest. Pathological examination on hematoxylin and eosin-stained tissue sections showed an area of necrosis and reduced cell density at the center of the tumor that received G47Δ-KR (Fig. 4B). This necrotic area was matched with the tumor area infected with G47Δ-KR on the adjacent slide, extending over 5.0 mm in diameter, as visualized by X-gal staining of the LacZ reporter of G47Δ-KR (Fig. 4C). Fluorescent microscope analysis on an adjacent section confirmed red KR expression in the area corresponding to the LacZ + area (Fig. 4D).

Injection of G47Δ-KR into IOMM-Lee meningioma led 48 h later to extensive necrosis in the tumor, consistent with the susceptibility of this tumor model to G47Δ-KR (Fig. 4E). LacZ-positive area was observed within this necrotic area impacted by G47Δ-KR treatment (Fig. 4F). We found extensive spread of KR expression that colocalized with the necrotic tumor area (Fig. 4G).

Therefore, direct intratumoral injection of G47Δ-KR led to infection and spread in GBM and MM xenograft models in vivo.

Photodynamic oncolytic virus therapy suppresses tumor growth in GBM and MM xenograft mouse models

A key component of PDT is the delivery of light. To assess the feasibility and efficacy of our PD-OV therapy in mouse models of GBM and MM, we employed a fiber-coupled laser diode (585 nm) with a laser light collimator (total power = 100 mW at the tip) as the light source for PD-OV (Fig. 5A). A proof-of-concept therapeutic study was then designed to directly irradiate flank subcutaneous tumors to elicit PDT effects two days after G47Δ-KR treatment of the tumor (Fig. 5A). When the tumor volume of U87 xenografts reached 50 mm3, the mice were randomized into four treatment arms: control, laser light alone (30 min), G47Δ-KR alone and PD-OV. We noted that laser light irradiation evoked brighter red color visible through the skin in tumors that had G47Δ-KR injections, compared with tumors without G47Δ-KR (Fig. 5B). Treatment effects were assessed by monitoring tumor growth. In this tumor model, laser light alone and G47Δ-KR alone did not induce significant anti-tumor effects, although 1 of 5 tumors receiving G47Δ-KR showed retarded growth (Fig. 5C and D). In contrast, PD-OV demonstrated significant inhibition of tumor growth including durable tumor control in 1 animal (Fig. 5C and D), which translated into a significant extension of animal survival (Supplementary Fig. 4A).

We next performed a similar therapeutic experiment in the IOMM-Lee MM model. In this model, laser light alone had no effect on tumor growth, consistent with the U87 model (Fig. 5E and F), indicating the absence of laser light-induced heat mediating anti-tumor effects. G47Δ-KR alone without laser light, however, mediated significant inhibition of tumor growth, compared with the control, reflecting susceptibility of this model to G47Δ-KR oHSV (Fig. 5E and F). PD-OV demonstrated a potent anti-tumor efficacy as evidenced by progressive regression of 4 of 5 treated tumors, although not significantly different statistically from the G47Δ-KR alone group (Fig. 5E and F). Kaplan-Meier analysis showed a survival extension in the two groups involving G47Δ-KR compared with PBS control, and a strong trend towards improved survival in the PD-OV group over the G47Δ-KR alone (Supplementary Fig. 4B).

Taken together, our in vivo studies using both GBM and MM xenografts provided proof-of-concept evidence that G47Δ-KR-driven PD-OV therapy is more efficacious than OV monotherapy.

Photodynamic oncolytic virus therapy modulated the tumor microenvironment

Finally, we used RNA sequencing and transcriptomics analysis to understand molecular mechanisms that underlie PD-OV therapy in vivo. Tumors were harvested for RNA extraction 48 h after laser light illumination to capture transcriptomic changes that occur acutely during PD-OV therapy (Fig. 6A). RNA reads were aligned to both human (hg38) and mouse (mm10) genomes to analyze transcriptomics in human tumor cells and in the TME, respectively.

We performed differential gene expression analysis, focused on transcriptomic differences between G47Δ-KR alone (OV group) and PD-OV therapy group (N = 3/group) (Supplementary Figs. 5A and B). For the human genome (i.e., in tumor cells), 11 and 50 gene ontologies were over-represented within the list of differentially expressed genes in the U87 and IOMM-Lee model, respectively (Supplementary Fig. 6A). These ontologies suggest miRNA-mediated gene silencing in the U87 model, while some form of reorganisation of the tumour cells in the IOMM-Lee model, in response to the PD effects in conjunction with OV therapy (Fig. 6B). Regarding the differential impact on the TME represented by mouse host cells, 51 ontologies were overrepresented in the U87 model and none in the IOMM-Lee model (Supplementary Fig. 6B). This analysis suggested the abilities of PD-OV therapy to modulate the TME, in terms of blood vessel development, inflammatory response and immune response of certain cells such as neutrophils in the U87 model (Fig. 6C). We further analyzed differentially expressed genes for OV compared to PBS and PD-OV compared to PBS (Fig. 6D). The resulting gene ontology analysis showed that the majority of effects caused by OV were also seen in the PD-OV group, indicating overlapping effects. However, we also noted the genes that were differentially expressed in the PD-OV but not OV groups. Those were represented by a number of ontologies, suggesting transcriptomic changes uniquely induced by PD-OV therapy.

Lastly, we applied a deconvolution algorithm to our RNA-seq data to infer immune cell profiles in the TME. The heatmap and dendrogram analysis revealed that tumors from the four treatment cohorts, in both the MM and GBM models, were generally split into two TME groups: one with greater immune cell infiltration and the other with markedly lower immune infiltration (Fig. 6E). In both tumor models, all the PD-OV-treated tumors and some of the OV-only tumors were in the higher-infiltration TME group. The mMCP-counter values were not significantly different between OV alone vs PD-OV. However, the consistent clustering of PD-OV-treated tumors in the higher infiltration group (three in both cases), compared with the OV alone tumors (one and two for the U87 and IOMM-Lee model, respectively), suggested that PD-OV therapy may have a greater capacity to modulate the immune TME. Indeed, immunohistochemical detection of CD68 showed that the tumors treated with PD-OV therapy had a higher number of CD68+ macrophages infiltrating in the tumor compared with the other groups (Fig. 6F), suggesting a greater stimulation of the host immunity.

Discussion

Here, we demonstrate the feasibility and efficacy of PD-OV therapy in preclinical models of adult neuro-oncological malignancies, GBM and MM. The PD-OV therapy involves a newly constructed oncolytic oHSV, G47Δ-KR, that carries cDNA encoding the genetically encoded PS KR. As a single agent, G47Δ-KR mediated dual anti-tumor actions: viral oncolysis and photodynamic cytotoxicity, which was elicited by 585–590 nm (amber-color) light illumination. As expected from prior research on KR, virally encoded KR produced ROS upon exposure to the light, and resulted in augmented cytotoxicity in infected neoplastic cells, as compared with G47Δ-KR without light activation of KR. PD-OV therapy was more efficacious than OV alone in both GBM and MM xenograft models in vivo. Our data revealed a new activity of photodynamic priming of PDT capable of augmenting OV therapy. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of a recombinant oHSV enabling PD-OV therapy for cancer and demonstrating PD-OV therapy for neuro-oncological malignancies. The only other analogous therapeutic approaches utilized an oncolytic adenovirus expressing KR that provided anti-tumor effects in preclinical models of colon cancer and melanoma.

We chose KR to be incorporated in our oHSV because of its well documented potency as a PS. Currently, options for genetically encoded PSs are rather limited, and the excitation wavelength 585 nm for KR is the longest among currently available protein PSs. We showed that both plasmid transfection and oHSV-mediated KR expression resulted in the emission spectrum peaking at 620–640 nm. This measured peak wavelength was slightly shifted from the original report of a peak at 610 nm and a relatively gradual decline of fluorescence at the higher wavelength range, with 50% intense fluorescence at 640 nm. This difference in KR optical properties may have been caused by the particular optical scanning method we employed that was only able to use 570 nm as the maximum excitation wavelength to detect 610 nm. Other factors that might have affected the emission spectrum in our work include protein concentrations and pH in the cellular environment.

G47Δ-KR-driven PD-OV therapy involving LED light irradiation triggered a rapid production of intracellular ROS and induced subsequent death in infected cells in vitro. Although HSV1 infection has been shown to induce ROS in neural cells and microglia, we observed only negligible induction of ROS by G47Δ-KR without light. PD-OV production of intracellular ROS was followed by disruption of the cell membrane and cell death, as measured by dead cell-derived protease. Both activation of the membrane-targeted KR and oHSV replication can contribute to damage to and disruption of cell membrane integrity and, in conjunction with other impacts such as DNA damage, drive potent target cell death. Importantly, genetic modifications present in G47Δ ensure tumor-selective infection and replication of G47Δ-KR, upon intratumoral injections. This feature of PD-OV therapy addresses the phototoxicity issues due to photosensitizer accumulation in normal tissues, one of the major challenges of current PDT involving systemic administration of PSs. Indeed, we showed that G47Δ-KR mediated highly efficient delivery of KR to GBM and MM xenografts, which, following laser light irradiation to G47Δ-KR-treated tumors, enabled the production of anti-tumor effects that were more potent than G47Δ-KR alone. PD-OV therapy was safe in our mouse models as no treatment-associated adverse events were noted.

Both oHSV (or OV in general) and PDT have been extensively shown to be able to induce immunogenic cell death, alter the TME, and elicit anti-tumor immune responses. We used bulk RNA sequencing and differentially expressed gene analysis to show that PD-OV therapy drove transcriptomic changes in tumors, exemplified by inflammatory responses, migration of innate immune cells (e.g., neutrophils and myeloid leukocytes) and angiogenesis: significantly enriched GOs, as compared with the tumors receiving G47Δ-KR without laser light. Furthermore, by applying the mMCP counter deconvolution algorithm to the RNA-seq data, we found a trend towards increased infiltration of a variety of immune cell types, such as neutrophils, monocytes/macrophages and NK cells, in PD-OV-treated GBM and MM. Despite the experiments using a small cohort of animals (N = 3/group), our results are consistent in the two tumor models and suggested a capacity of PD-OV therapy to modulate the immune TME in a manner potentially distinct from oHSV alone. This was corroborated by immunohistochemistry, which validated the ability of PD-OV therapy to increase tumor infiltration of CD68+ macrophages. Although use of human xenografts was a logical extension of in vitro studies and can support oHSV replication, as compared with murine tumors, our experimental setting has its inherent limitations as tumors are ectopically located in animals that lack adaptive, T lymphocyte-mediated immunity. Nevertheless, these results form a scientific basis that warrants further studies to determine if PD-OV therapy induces anti-tumor immune responses more potently than oHSV or PDT monotherapy through more detailed examinations in a time course pre and post therapies. To this end, we have begun testing PD-OV therapy in immunocompetent settings and in orthotopic neuro-oncology models.

The current work is also significant in establishing proof-of-concept that PD-OV therapy is feasible and effective in preclinical neurooncology models. This can be translated to a clinical setting where therapy commences with stereotactic injections of G47Δ-KR followed by craniotomy, tumor resection, and intraoperative laser light irradiation to the residual tumor days later, when the spread of G47Δ-KR is near maximum. This proposed therapeutic model does however come with some challenges that would require further research to overcome. The most notable is light penetration into the tumor tissue, due to the relatively short wavelength of the excitation light (<600 nm). Intraoperative PDT to GBM has been clinically investigated and is already approved in Japan. The balloon-assisted illumination device fit in the tumor resection cavity enabled homogenous light diffusion, representing a technological refinement that will be helpful and necessary to optimize light delivery to residual tumors left behind post-resection. Preclinically, upconversion nanoparticles have been successfully used to convert deep-penetrating near infrared light to green light, showing the potential to activate KR in deep-seated tissues. Stereotactic inoculation of oHSV G47Δ has been approved in Japan for malignant glioma and can be applied with G47Δ-KR to target tumors including those difficult to access for resection. We are currently exploring the feasibility of PD-OV therapy using clinically representative orthotopic tumor models. Ultimately, though, tumor cells invading deeply in the brain parenchyma, most notably in GBM, will be outside the direct target of PD-OV therapy. Successful tumor control in such cases should require the induction of potent anti-tumor immune responses and this aspect of PD-OV therapy is one of our ongoing research focuses. Additionally, PD-OV therapy may be rationally combined with approaches like immune checkpoint blockade, anti-angiogenic agents, or chemotherapies, and future research should aim to identify mechanistic combinatorial approaches that can advance to clinical trials.

In conclusion, the current work presents a proof-of-principle study showing that G47Δ-KR and laser light drives PD-OV therapy that is more potent than G47Δ-KR both in vitro and in vivo. Ongoing and future investigations involve testing this strategy in animal models that are more clinically relevant, such as orthotopic tumor models in an immunocompetent setting. Such studies should shed light on the immune tumor microenvironmental impact of PD-OV therapy as well as technical feasibility of the methodology, providing data and knowledge that is critical for clinical translation of the modality.
