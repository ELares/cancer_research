# Nanofilament immunotherapy induces potent antitumor vaccine responses.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Checkpoint inhibitors revolutionized cancer treatment by potentiating antitumor immune responses. However, many patients do not respond to these therapies, often due to the lack of a pre-existing immune response against cancer cells. Developing immunotherapies that promote cancer-cell antigen recognition, and the initiation of antitumor immune responses could thus improve response rates.

**METHODS**: We established multimodal nanofilament immunotherapy as an antigen-agnostic in situ cancer vaccine modality. Through genetic engineering of the M13 bacteriophage, nanofilaments displaying combinations of therapeutic agents were generated to guide immune recognition and response against cancer cells. TAT003 is a multimodal nanofilament combining the natural adjuvant properties of M13 with the display of both anti-PD-L1 single-chain antibody fragments (scFvs) and interleukin-2 (IL-2) molecules. It was developed to bind to the surface of cancer cells and transform them into immunological targets. After validation of TAT003's biological activities in vitro and assessment of its biodistribution, its potency was evaluated after intratumoral administration in murine syngeneic tumor models, both as a single agent and in combination with Programmed Death protein 1 (PD-1) blockade therapy. In addition, the mechanism of action of TAT003 was characterized using cytokine and immune profiling and T-cell activation assays.

**RESULTS**: TAT003 nanofilaments displayed several copies of biologically active anti-Programmed Death ligand 1 (PD-L1) and IL-2 molecules. On intratumoral injection, TAT003 attached durably to the tumor, thus limiting systemic exposure to the drug. TAT003 profoundly remodeled the tumor microenvironment of injected lesions, where it initiated a robust myeloid-cell infiltrate, and promoted the invasion of non-injected, contralateral lesions by T cells. This translated into potent regression of both injected and non-injected tumors in several cancer models, and potentiated PD-1 blockade therapy. TAT003 treatment induced the expansion of cancer-cell specific effector T cells systemically, providing a long-lasting antitumor vaccine response.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Multimodal nanofilament immunotherapy is a novel approach to mounting systemic antitumor immune responses in situ by physically attaching large immunostimulatory molecules to cancer cells. TAT003 induced marked tumor regression by leveraging synergies between therapeutic agents displayed on its surface while offering a favorable tolerability profile. The results presented here establish multimodal nanofilaments as an innovative and versatile immunotherapy platform for developing in situ cancer vaccines.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Current immunotherapies focus on activating T cells and have limited efficacy in most cancer types. Recent studies have highlighted the need to engage other immune pathways to reprogram the tumor microenvironment and spark an antitumor immune response. We developed a nanofilament immunotherapy to close this gap and engage both lymphoid and myeloid immune cells in the fight against cancer.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Here, we demonstrate that bacteriophages can be engineered as an effective nanofilament immunotherapy for the treatment of cancer and show how nanofilaments stimulate myeloid cells to change the tumor microenvironment and promote the establishment of a strong T-cell-driven antitumor immune response.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Our study highlights the need for immunotherapies that stimulate multiple immune cell types to mount an antitumor immune response, and supports using nanofilaments as an innovative new modality to treat cancer.

Introduction

 Immunotherapy has revolutionized cancer treatment by leveraging the immune system’s intrinsic ability to recognize and attack cancer cells. For example, Programmed Death protein 1 (PD-1)/Programmed Death ligand 1 (PD-L1) immune checkpoint inhibitors block interactions between immune cell receptor (PD-1) and immune checkpoint displayed on cancer cells (PD-L1). This blockade potentiates the killing activity of T cells directed against neoantigens expressed by cancer cells. While immune checkpoint inhibitors demonstrate impressive efficacy in some cancer types, 55–87% of patients still fail to respond to these immunotherapies, with especially high failure rates for solid tumors. This low response rate is attributed, in part, to the absence of pre-existing activated T-cell effectors that recognize cancer-cell neoantigens. The ability of the immune system to detect neoantigens can be impaired by several factors such as low mutational burden or major histocompatibility complex expression biases. The tumor microenvironment can also suppress the immune system in several ways, leading to immune evasion and failure to initiate an antitumor response.

Vaccination is a well-known strategy for eliciting immune responses against selected antigens. However, this approach is difficult to implement in cancer therapy since the tumor neoantigen repertoire arises from random mutational events and thus can (1) vary greatly among patients and (2) continue to evolve, with primary tumor and metastases acquiring and losing antigens. To address this issue, personalized cancer vaccines are being developed, showing promising results in the clinic. Unfortunately, the manufacturing of personalized vaccines currently requires tumor sampling, sequencing, and neoantigen predictions for each patient, which delays treatments, is expensive, and limits scalability. Instead of injecting selected antigens into a patient, directly exploiting the tumor as a source of neoantigens could provide a more robust alternative to conventional cancer vaccination. Therapeutic agents designed to enhance the immunogenicity of cancer cells could thus be used to stimulate an immune response against the diverse neoantigen repertoires of tumors in situ.

Recent studies described the self-adjuvanting properties of nanofilaments and their preferential uptake by immune cells. This sparked the development of “nanovaccines” using these large structures as antigen carriers. Nanofilaments can be formed from self-assembling peptides; however, this strategy presents manufacturing challenges to ensure nanofilament uniformity. An attractive alternative approach to producing nanofilaments is the use of filamentous bacteriophages, whose size can be controlled precisely by adjusting the length of the encapsidated DNA. Filamentous bacteriophage-derived nanofilaments designed to bind cancer cells and enhance their immunogenicity could thus be used to stimulate an immune response against the diverse neoantigen repertoires of tumors in situ.

Here, we describe TAT003, a multimodal nanofilament that binds PD-L1 via a displayed single-chain antibody fragment (scFv) and displays interleukin-2 (IL-2). By flagging cancer cells as immunological targets, TAT003 initiates local antitumor immune responses that disseminate to produce systemic protective immunity. We demonstrate that intratumoral administration of TAT003 induces strong therapeutic responses in several cancer models, both as a monotherapy and in combination with immune-checkpoint inhibitors, while also displaying a low toxicity profile due to its high retention in tumor tissues.

Results

Engineering of TAT003 multimodal nanofilament

Filamentous bacteriophages are well-known for their use in phage display, a genetic method that fuses proteins of interest to bacteriophage coat proteins. This plug-and-play strategy can be used to engineer bacteriophages into multimodal nanofilaments by fusing their coat proteins with therapeutic agents that stimulate the immune system in innovative ways (online supplemental extended data figure 1). To ensure uniformity of protein display on bacteriophage particles, the native coat protein genes are first inactivated in the M13 genome. Then, the coat proteins are complemented by the expression of a fusion gene in trans encoding the therapeutic protein fused to the coat protein (online supplemental extended data figure 2a). Since all coat proteins are essential for bacteriophage assembly, it ensures all particles display therapeutic agents. Furthermore, displaying therapeutic agents on all copies of a truncated pIII coat protein renders bacteriophage-derived nanofilaments unable to infect bacteria, which strengthens bacteriophage DNA biocontainment (online supplemental extended data figure 2b).

Of particular interest for the development of immunotherapies, filamentous bacteriophages are naturally immunogenic. For example, their genomes have toll-like receptor (TLR)9 agonistic activity, and filamentous bacteriophages elicit high antibody titers on injection in the absence of adjuvant. Importantly, exposure of tumor-associated macrophages to M13 filamentous bacteriophages induces activation and antigen-presentation markers (figure 1a). Filamentous bacteriophages are thus an attractive platform to engineer immunotherapies.

We designed the TAT003 nanofilament to be administered intratumorally, harnessing M13’s immunogenic properties to convert tumors into antigen sources and trigger a systemic antitumor immune response. To achieve this, we combined the multicopy display of anti-PD-L1 scFvs at one tip of the bacteriophage particle with that of murine IL-2 molecules (mIL-2s) at the opposite extremity. Through PD-L1 blockade and IL-2 stimulation, TAT003 is designed to potentiate CD8+ T-cell activity to eliminate cancer cells (figure 1b).

With this design, TAT003 binds to cancer cells via interactions between five anti-PD-L1 scFvs on the bacteriophage tip and PD-L1s on cell surfaces. Through this binding, TAT003 nanofilaments decorate cancer cells and transform them into targets for the immune system. The binding of TAT003 to PD-L1 checkpoint proteins also advantageously impedes their interactions with PD-1, which reduces cancer-cell resistance to phagocytosis by macrophages and potentiates attack by T cells. We selected the anti-PD-L1 atezolizumab (Roche) for fusion to TAT003 because it is mouse/human cross-reactive. Atezolizumab was reverse-engineered into a scFv and fused to the C-terminal N3 domain of the pIII coat protein (online supplemental data 1).

Next, to polarize the immune response towards the stimulation of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), TAT003 was also engineered to display multiple copies of mIL-2 via fusion to the N-terminus of the pIX coat protein. While low doses of IL-2 have been associated with the induction of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and immune tolerance, the efficacy of high doses of IL-2 to treat cancers is validated in the clinic, where it stimulates CTL activity, but also results in hard-to-manage toxicity. Being administered intratumorally, TAT003 circumvents this problem by anchoring IL-2 to cancer cells through the binding to PD-L1; this enables IL-2 to reach high local concentrations while limiting systemic exposure and its associated side effects. A control nanofilament TAT003ΔIL2, identical to TAT003 but lacking IL-2, was also generated to evaluate the contribution of IL-2 to TAT003-induced antitumor activity.

TAT003 displays biologically active anti-PD-L1 scFv, mIL-2 and has TLR9 agonistic activity

The biological activities of TAT003-displayed anti-PD-L1 scFv and mIL-2 were first tested. The binding of TAT003 to human and mouse PD-L1 was confirmed by ELISA using immobilized recombinant proteins (online supplemental extended data figure 3a). In addition, the binding of TAT003 to native PD-L1 expressed on the surface of mouse and human cells was established in a competitive flow cytometry assay (figure 1c and online supplemental extended data figure 3b). We confirmed that the display of anti-PD-L1 scFvs on TAT003 effectively decorates cancer cells with nanofilaments (figure 1c,d). Next, to confirm that the atezolizumab scFv we generated retained its capacity to inhibit PD-1/PD-L1 interaction, we performed a bioassay to quantify their blockade ability (figure 1e). Atezolizumab scFv had very similar EC50 when displayed on TAT003∆IL2 (EC50=3.55×1011 molecule/mL), TAT003 (EC50=5.02×1011 molecule/mL) or in its native commercially available IgG format (EC50=5.43×1011 molecule/mL), confirming that display on the nanofilament caused no loss of function.

The presence of mIL-2 on TAT003 was confirmed in a sandwich ELISA using immobilized anti-mIL-2 antibody to capture nanofilaments and labeled anti-M13 antibody for detection (online supplemental extended data figure 3c). The mIL-2 displayed by TAT003 induced the SEAP (secreted embryonic alkaline phosphatase) reporter gene in human-derived HEK-Blue IL-2R cells, confirming that mIL-2 is biologically active and cross-reactive, whereas this induction was not observed with TAT003ΔIL2 nanofilaments (online supplemental extended data figure 3d). Surprisingly, mIL-2 activity on TAT003 was 22.4-fold higher than recombinant mIL-2 at equimolar concentration, suggesting that the proximity of multicopy mIL-2 on TAT003 enhances activation of the IL-2 receptor (figure 1f).

Indirect ELISA confirmed that TAT003 displays both anti-PD-L1 and mIL-2 molecules on the same particle. Nanofilaments were captured with immobilized PD-L1, and an anti-mIL-2 antibody was used to detect bound particles displaying both therapeutic agents simultaneously (figure 1g). Only TAT003 produced a signal in this assay, confirming it simultaneously displays anti-PD-L1 scFv and mIL-2. Taken together, these results confirm the simultaneous display of biologically active anti-PD-L1 scFv and mIL-2 on TAT003.

Finally, the natural properties of nanofilaments to stimulate innate immunity were investigated. As previously reported for M13, TAT003 was able to stimulate TLR9 receptors of murine and human origins in a similar manner, suggesting its innate properties could be translated from mice to humans (figure 1h,i). Interestingly, nanofilaments appear to be easily detected and uptaken by phagocytic cells, suggesting that nanofilament-coated cancer cells could promote attacks by phagocytes such as macrophages (online supplemental extended data figure 3e; see insets to observe ingested single cells). We thus quantified the phagocytotic activity of bone marrow-derived macrophages when exposed to A20 cancer cells or TAT003-covered A20 cancer cells (figure 1j). A drastic increase in phagocytotic activity was observed in the TAT003 treated condition, indicating that covering cancer cells in immunogenic nanofilament is a good strategy to induce cancer cell recognition by the innate immune system.

TAT003 anchors in tumors and triggers limited toxicity

Two characteristics affecting TAT003’s biodistribution are the large size of the nanofilament (~440 nm in length) and its ability to bind to cancer cells, both of which theoretically limit its diffusion from tumor tissues following intratumoral injection (figure 1b). The biodistribution of TAT003 on intratumoral injection in PD-L1+ A20 tumors was thus assessed in mice. Mice received a single injection of TAT003, and the presence of bacteriophage DNA (proxy for bacteriophage particles) was monitored by quantitative PCR (qPCR) in the tumor, blood, and organs (figure 2a). As a control for the qPCR method, the presence of M13 in tissues and blood was also quantified by infection assay and qPCR in parallel; since, unlike TAT003, M13 can infect bacteria. Infection and qPCR assay results correlated strongly, which validated the qPCR quantifications (online supplemental extended data figure 4). The biodistribution data indicate that TAT003 behaved similarly to M13, with the highest tissue concentration being in the tumor, followed by draining lymph nodes. TAT003 limited diffusion rates, combined with its ability to bind cancer cells via PD-L1, resulted in a maximal leakage of<0.01% in distant tissues. The distribution of TAT003 within tumors was next investigated (figure 2b). After three intratumoral injections of TAT003, histologic analysis of tumor tissue showed that only a minor area of the tumor contained nanofilaments, revealing limited TAT003 diffusion in tumor tissues.

Importantly, no behavioral signs of toxicity were observed throughout mouse experiments, possibly due to the advantageous biodistribution profile of TAT003. To further evaluate TAT003’s toxicity profile, mice received three intratumoral injections of either PBS or 1012 particles of TAT003 (the highest TAT003 administrable dose). The day after the last treatment, sera were collected to evaluate levels of cytokines associated with cytokine release syndrome (figure 2c). Among the 12 cytokines tested, only IL-6 levels were found to be significantly increased, with a 17.6-fold induction in the serum of mice after TAT003 treatment. IL-2 and Keratinocyte Chemoattractant (KC) (also known as CXCL1) had slightly elevated concentrations in serum (twofold or less), which could possibly result from local secretion at the tumor site.

Intratumoral treatment with TAT003 leads to tumor regression and clearance

TAT003’s efficacy was first assessed in a PD-L1+ CT26 colon carcinoma model, which is known to moderately resist PD-1-blockade treatments (figure 3a). Mice bearing CT26 tumors received three intratumoral injections of either PBS, atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1 antibody), or mIL-2 to confirm the model’s resistance to immunotherapy and set an efficacy benchmark for nanofilament variants (figure 3a–c and online supplemental extended data figure 5a–c). Next, CT26 tumors were treated with M13, TAT003ΔIL2, or TAT003 nanofilaments to evaluate the contribution of each to the antitumor response (figure 3a–c and online supplemental extended data figure 5d–f). Treatment of CT26 tumors with TAT003 inhibited tumor growth by 82±21% on average within the treatment window, as opposed to 35±21% for atezolizumab and 5±45% for mIL-2. Treatment with TAT003 also increased average survival post-treatment by 59%. Furthermore, nanofilaments TAT003ΔIL2 and TAT003 were the only treatments to induce cases of complete remission in this model. Importantly, a beneficial effect was produced by the addition, first, of anti-PD-L1 scFv (TAT003ΔIL2), then of mIL-2 (TAT003), to the M13 bacteriophage particle; the three elements coupled together produced the strongest effect on all parameters studied.

The potency of TAT003 and each of its precursor variants was next evaluated in the PD-L1+ A20 B-cell lymphoma mouse model (figure 3a), which is known to be resistant to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapies once established. A20 tumors were treated by intratumoral injections of either PBS, atezolizumab, M13, TAT003ΔIL2, or TAT003 (figure 3d,e and online supplemental extended data figure 6a–e). Similar to the CT26 tumor model, atezolizumab had a very limited effect in the A20 model with a tumor growth inhibition of 37±43%. Yet, whereas M13 produced limited efficacy with CT26 tumors, it had a more pronounced effect on both tumor growth (83±26%) and survival in the A20 model. The additive effects of anti-PD-L1 scFv and IL-2 were again observed, as TAT003ΔIL2 and TAT003 both reduced tumor growth (95±4% and 97±3%, respectively) and improved clearance rates, with TAT003 being the only treatment to achieve complete remission in all treated mice.

Cured mice, whose A20 tumors had cleared, were kept under observation for 178 days to assess long-term protection against tumor recurrence. During that period, none of the cured mice developed secondary tumors or any signs of recurrence of the treated tumor. After 179 days, these mice received a new injection of A20 cells, this time on the contralateral flank from the original tumor (figure 3f). Naïve mice of a similar age also received an injection of A20 cells as a control for tumor-cell fitness and engraftment rates. While all naïve mice developed tumors exhibiting normal growth rates, none of the previously cured mice developed tumors on re-challenge, supporting that TAT003-treated mice acquired immune protection against A20 cells.

TAT003 was next tested in the B16 F10 melanoma model. This model forms cold aggressive tumors when injected in C57BL6J mice and is notoriously difficult to treat. Similarly to other models, C57BL6J mice bearing B16 F10 tumors on their right flank were treated by three intratumoral injections of either PBS, M13, TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003 (figure 1g,h and online supplemental extended data figure 7a–d). Injecting M13 particles had a meaningful effect on both B16 F10 tumor growth and mouse survival but was highly variable between mice, with an overall tumor growth inhibition of 24±60% still resulting in a 63% improvement in median survival time post treatment. Unlike in the CT26 and A20 models, adding anti-PD-L1 on M13 did not produce any benefit for B16 F10 tumor growth inhibition. However, the addition of IL-2 further improved mouse survival and tumor growth inhibition (42±64%), with most variability being driven by only two non-responder mice. As previously reported, these results confirm that the B16 F10 model does not respond well to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. Nonetheless, the combined therapeutic activities of TAT003 still produced significant tumor growth inhibition in this hard-to-treat model.

TAT003 treatment triggers a systemic antitumor immune response

The ability of TAT003 to promote a systemic antitumor response on intratumoral injection was next evaluated in mice bearing two tumors simultaneously (figure 4a). Tumors on the right flank were treated with either PBS or TAT003 and tumor volumes of both injected tumors and non-injected tumors (left flanks) were measured (figure 4b). Treatment with TAT003 resulted in complete remission of 16 out of 18 injected tumors and 15 out of 18 non-injected tumors, indicating that local treatment with TAT003 induced a systemic antitumor immune response in most of the treated mice. Interestingly, the 15 mice that successfully eliminated the non-injected tumors also eliminated their injected tumor, suggesting that complete response in the injected tumor might promote complete response in non-injected tumors. To confirm that the therapeutic activity of TAT003 in the non-injected tumors was not due to leakage of TAT003 from the injected tumor to the non-injected tumor, we extracted tumors the day after the full treatment routine and quantified the amount of TAT003 in injected and non-injected lesions by qPCR (figure 4c). In accordance with the biodistribution data, no TAT003 particles were detected in the non-injected lesions, indicating that the observed tumor-growth inhibition cannot be attributed to treatment leakage.

To characterize the immune response triggered by TAT003, cytokine profiling and immunotyping were performed on injected and non-injected A20 tumors. Mice bearing tumors on both flanks received the complete treatment routine of either PBS or TAT003 in one tumor, ending on Day 18, and were euthanized the following day, a time point when tumor regression begins (online supplemental extended data figure 8a). Next, both the injected and non-injected tumors were extracted, split in two and one half was embedded in paraffin for immunofluorescence microscopy while the other half was homogenized and stained to identify the major families of immune cells by flow cytometry (figure 4d and online supplemental extended data figure 8b–l). In parallel, cytokine profiling experiments were performed on sera and tumor-homogenate supernatants to measure the activity of immune cells (figure 4e and online supplemental extended data figure 9). TAT003 strongly recruited M1-polarized macrophages (6.7-fold increase from placebo group) in tumor tissue (online supplemental extended data figure 8k), and granulocytes (>60 fold increase from placebo group) in the injected tumor (online supplemental extended data figure 8l). The high infiltration of granulocytes was confirmed to be composed of neutrophils by fluorescence microscopy (online supplemental extended data figure 8m) and which expressed activation markers linked to an N1 phenotype (online supplemental extended data figure 8n). This expansion of neutrophils can also be observed systemically, where neutrophil populations increase by 4.75-fold in the blood of TAT003-treated mice (online supplemental extended data figure 10a,b). Additionally, a broad induction of cytokines can be observed after treatment with TAT003. Among those cytokines, an induction of T helper cell type 1 (Th1 T-cell)cytokines (C-C motif chemokine ligand (CCL)5, CCL4, CCL3, Inteferon (IFN)-γ, IL-12, IL-2) was observed in both the injected and non-injected tumors, which correlates with infiltration by CTLs and Th cells in the non-injected tumor (3.6-fold and 3.4-fold increase against the placebo group, respectively) (figure 4e and online supplemental extended data figure 8e,f). We next wanted to interrogate the activation status of CTLs specifically and looked at their single-cell IFN- γ expression profile in both the non-injected and the injected tumors (figure 4f). While the vast majority of the CTLs expressed low levels of IFN-γ, the TAT003 treatment induced a subpopulation of infiltrating CTLs secreting high levels of IFN-γ in non-injected lesions, which are likely responsible for the antitumor immune response. Interestingly, Th17-associated cytokines (C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 1 (CXCL1), Lipopolysaccharide-induced CXC chemokine (LIX), Macrophage Inflammatory Protein 2 (MIP-2), IL-17) were found to be strongly upregulated in the injected tumors, and Th17 induction is known to promote the infiltration and activation of neutrophils. Despite displaying unmodified IL-2 molecules, which are known to stimulate Treg activity, TAT003 treatment did not seem to have any significant effect on their recruitment to tumor sites (online supplemental extended data figure 8g). However, the IL-2 molecules displayed on TAT003 likely contributed to the overall IL-2 induction levels in the injected tumors, where TAT003 concentrations were sufficiently high to be detected in our cytokine paneling experiment. Unfortunately, our cytokine paneling experiment did not allow us to measure Treg activity since it only included IL-10 as a Treg-associated cytokine, which was slightly upregulated in injected tumors but is also secreted by many other cell types (online supplemental extended data figure 9a).

To confirm the induction of a systemic, T-cell driven immune response, Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs) derived from the same mice were isolated and co-cultured with A20 cells to detect tumor-cell-specific T-cells in an antigen-agnostic manner. On co-culturing, activation of CD8+ cells was surveyed by staining for intracellular IFN-γ. As suspected, TAT003 treatment resulted in the expansion of circulating, cancer-cell specific CD8+ T-cells resulting in a 4.3-fold increase, supporting its mechanism of action as an antigen agnostic cancer vaccine modality (online supplemental extended data figure 10c).

Given this apparent systemic T-cell induction, we sought to evaluate T-cell infiltration in tumors following TAT003 treatment. Immunohistochemistry staining of CD3 cells was performed on both injected and non-injected A20 and CT26 tumor samples, collected 8 days post-treatment (online supplemental extended data figure 10d). While no significant increase in T cells was observed in A20 tumors, a substantial infiltration of T cells was detected in CT26 tumors. The difference in T cell recruitment in these models might derive from their initial Tumor Microenvironment (TME) immune profile. Our data indicate that, as opposed to CT26 tumors, A20 tumors are already highly infiltrated with T cells at baseline, which may explain the limited ability of TAT003 treatment to recruit additional T cells in this context.

TAT003 treatment elicits the production of anti-TAT003 antibodies that do not severely impede treatment efficacy

Since TAT003 treatment appears to spark a broad immune response and is derived from M13, a bacteriophage known to elicit the production of antibodies on injection in mice, we next investigated the potential impact of neutralizing antibodies on treatment efficacy. The kinetics of anti-TAT003 antibody production was initially studied in B16-F10 tumor-bearing C57BL/6 mice. Following the complete TAT003 treatment regimen, the mice were sacrificed at various time points to evaluate antibody production against TAT003 using ELISA (online supplemental extended data figure 11a). Surprisingly, anti-TAT003 antibodies were detected as early as 5 days following the initial treatment, suggesting their presence during the final TAT003 injection on day 7. Anti-TAT003 antibody production peaked at day 14 and remained stable through day 35 post-treatment, demonstrating sustained immune response against TAT003. The A20 tumor model was next selected to evaluate the impact of anti-TAT003 antibodies on treatment efficacy, as it demonstrates high responsiveness to TAT003, making any reductions in efficacy readily detectable. BALB/c mice received a subcutaneous injection of 1×1011 TAT003 particles 6 days before tumor cell injection. Tumors were engrafted on both flanks of the mice following the same study design as described in figure 4a. Mice were subsequently treated with either PBS or TAT003 on days 16, 20, and 23 following the pretreatment, when anti-TAT003 antibody concentrations were expected to be at their peak (online supplemental extended data figure 11b). The presence of anti-TAT003 antibodies did not significantly interfere with efficacy in injected tumors, where all mice responded to treatment and 71% of the mice achieved complete remission, as opposed to 88% with no pretreatment (figure 4b). However, a small reduction in efficacy was observed in the non-injected tumor, suggesting that pre-existing immune responses against TAT003 may slightly impair its ability to elicit an adaptive immune response against tumor cell antigens. Nonetheless, a complete remission was achieved in 57% of the mice, as opposed to 83% in the absence of the TAT003 pre-treatment.

TAT003 antitumor activity can be potentiated by PD-1 blockade

Mice bearing CT26 tumors on both flanks were treated intratumorally with either PBS, M13, TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003 before systemic treatment with an anti-PD-1 antibody or an isotype control (figure 5a). Pretreatment with either TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003 improved mouse survival rates, as well as tumor-growth inhibition, when compared with anti-PD-1 treatment alone (figure 5b–d and online supplemental extended data figure 12). Interestingly, whereas TAT003ΔIL2 and TAT003 produced similar outcomes when combined with anti-PD-1, TAT003 was far superior over TAT003ΔIL2 as a monotherapy.

The effect of TAT003 treatment on metastases was next investigated using the 4T1 triple-negative breast cancer model, which is known to quickly spur lung metastasis. For this experiment, 4T1 cells were engrafted orthotopically and left to grow for 3 days. Then, mice were treated three times with either PBS, M13, TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003 by intratumoral injection on Days 3, 5, and 7. On Day 9, primary tumors were resected, leaving only lung metastases to grow (figure 5e). On Days 10 and 13, mice received a systemic treatment with either anti-PD-1 or an isotype control. Survival was next monitored up until Day 100 (figure 5f–h). Median survival times of mice treated with anti-PD-1 antibody alone were 36 days, which is identical to the PBS-treated group, indicating a complete lack of response to anti-PD-1 in this context. Interestingly, when combined with nanofilaments, anti-PD-1 improved mice survival from 45 to 58 days for M13, from 37 to 124 days for TAT003ΔIL2 and from 90 to 120 days for TAT003. Intriguingly, both anti-PD-1 and TAT003ΔIL2 displayed almost no therapeutic benefit when used as monotherapy but led to the survival of a majority of mice when combined. Nonetheless, TAT003 proved far superior to TAT003ΔIL2 in monotherapy and displayed similar efficacy as TAT003ΔIL2 when combined with a systemic anti-PD-1 treatment, as previously observed in the CT26 tumor model.

To understand how the infiltrating immune system interacts with TAT003 to produce a systemic protection against metastases in the 4T1 model, resected tumors from four animals per treatment group were processed for immune profiling by flow cytometry (online supplemental extended data figure 13). As anti-PD-1 treatment alone failed to produce a significant effect on mice survival, we first investigated PD-1 and PD-L1 levels (online supplemental extended data figure 13a,b). PD-1 expression levels remained consistently low, whether in the entire cell population (online supplemental extended data figure 13a) or specifically in effector T cells (online supplemental extended data figure 13h). These results suggest that T cell repression in the 4T1 model may occur through an alternative immune evasion pathway. In contrast, PD-L1 expression levels were high, enabling TAT003 to bind to the surface of cancer cells and exert therapeutic effects through mechanisms other than PD-1/PD-L1 axis inhibition. Immune infiltration of 4T1 tumors, as early as 9 days post-engraftment, exhibited a trend of reduced T cell presence and increased myeloid cell presence in TAT003-treated mice (online supplemental extended data figure 13c–l). Among the statistically significant changes, TAT003 tumor exhibited increased infiltration by B cells and granulocytes (identified as other myeloid cells) and a decrease in macrophages. These data further support the importance of granulocytes in the mechanism of action of TAT003.

Discussion

The data presented in this article support the use of bacteriophage-derived multimodal nanofilaments as a tumor-antigen agnostic, in situ cancer vaccine. This innovative modality leverages the tumor as a source of antigens to locally initiate a systemic antitumor immune response by combining the self-adjuvanting properties of nanofilaments with engineered therapeutic activities.

The TAT003 candidate is a multimodal nanofilament combining the natural immunogenicity of the M13 bacteriophage with the display of anti-PD-L1 scFvs and IL-2 molecules. On intratumoral injection, TAT003 anchors to cancer cells and transforms them into immunological targets to mount an antitumor response. TAT003 sharply inhibited growth of local and distal tumors, despite marginal leakage from the injected tumors. Following TAT003 treatment, the microenvironment of local and distal tumors was profoundly remodeled. TAT003-injected tumors were consistently infiltrated by granulocytes across tumor models, with distal non-injected lesions exhibiting a Th1 response. This difference in immune-cell populations highlights the involvement of innate immunity at the injected lesion, which serves as a neoantigen source to subsequently trigger an adaptive, systemic T-cell-mediated immune response driving the observed abscopal effect. Additional experiments will be required to elucidate the specific roles of each immune cell type in the observed antitumor effect. Exploring the contribution of myeloid cells to the initiation of a T-cell adaptive response through depletion assays would be especially interesting. Our current immune profiling data represents an initial assessment of how major immune cell types are recruited following TAT003 treatment. Investigating the activation status of these infiltrating immune cell types and further dissecting the immune response induced by TAT003 will be crucial to elucidating the significance and role of each immune cell type in the antitumor immune response triggered by TAT003. One compelling area for future research is the activation statuses and cell states of CD8+T cells, Th cells, and Tregs across various tumor contexts, as these cell types are likely to interact with the IL-2 displayed on TAT003.

One striking feature of our immune-profiling experiment is the intense and consistent infiltration of neutrophils in injected lesions, which is observed across tumor models. Neutrophils have long been studied for their pro-tumoral role and are thought to promote both tumor progression through inflammation and metastasis by degrading the extracellular matrix. However, recent studies have highlighted the dual role of neutrophils in cancer, recognizing the potential contribution of N1 neutrophils to the antitumor immune response. Immune profiling experiments revealed that injected lesions that responded well to treatment were heavily infiltrated by N1 neutrophils. Neutrophils were likely attracted directly or indirectly to the tumor by the bacteriophage nature of TAT003 and were possibly stimulated by Th17 cells, as a drastic induction of IL-17 levels was found in the tumor. In this context, the inflammation induced by TAT003 may have triggered neutrophil effector functions, promoting an antitumor neutrophil profile and the capture of cancer-cell antigens. Likewise, neutrophils can secrete an array of chemokines, which facilitate the recruitment of other immune cells to the tumor. No metastases were observed in mice treated with TAT003, supporting that neutrophil activity did not promote metastasis in our animal models. In fact, granulocytes were found to infiltrate 4T1 tumors on treatment with TAT003, which appeared to protect against metastasis. Fully understanding the contribution of neutrophils to TAT003 antitumor activity will require further investigation, but our current data suggests the neutrophils had either a neutral or positive impact on treatment response. It would also be valuable to investigate the role of Th17 cells in the early response to treatment, as our data suggests they are likely recruited to injected tumor sites and are potentially active. Th17 cells have a controversial role in antitumor immune responses, functioning either as regulatory T cells or as potent mediators of antitumor immunity. Thus, their involvement could be pivotal to TAT003’s mode of action, warranting further study.

The natural immunogenicity of TAT003 appears to be a key contributor to its therapeutic activity. The bacteriophage-like properties of TAT003, such as its TLR9 agonistic activity appears to spark a very broad immune response. Investigating the importance of TLR9 activation in the induction of TAT003-driven antitumor response in TLR9 knockout mice could provide important insights on the contribution of innate immunity in cancer cell immunization. Similarly to oncolytic viruses, the production of antibodies against TAT003 does not seem to severely hinder its therapeutic activity. This may be attributed to the intratumoral route of administration, which enables a high concentration of TAT003 molecules to be delivered to a single lesion. Consequently, the local concentration of TAT003 might be too high for anti-TAT003 antibodies to drastically alter its activity.

Interestingly, whereas TAT003 monotherapy had a clear advantage over TAT003ΔIL2, the outcome of treatments with both agents was similar when combined with the systemic administration of an anti-PD-1 antibody. TAT003 also induced a stronger systemic expansion of cancer-cell specific T cells, suggesting that the presence of IL-2 on TAT003 promotes a stronger T-cell antitumor response capable of substantially overriding PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition. Furthermore, despite the known role of IL-2 in the induction of Tregs, no clear signs of Tregs induction were observed in mice treated with TAT003, which displays unmodified IL-2 molecules. The observed enhanced immune response could be explained by TAT003-coated cancer cells creating a locally high IL-2 concentration that stimulates antitumor effector T cells in their vicinity. Also, the close proximity of the bundled IL-2 molecules at one end of the TAT003 particle could strongly stimulate effector T cells in close vicinity of cancer cells.

Providing the therapeutic activity of TAT003 in mice is conserved in humans, nanofilaments could become an important therapy in the neo-adjuvant and perioperative settings. Preoperative treatment with TAT003 also conferred protection against metastasis in mice bearing triple-negative breast-cancer cells implanted orthotopically. Treatment of 4T1 primary tumors with TAT003 before resection enhanced survival rates and potentiated the therapeutic efficacy of anti-PD-1, supporting the use of TAT003 in neoadjuvant and perioperative settings.

The main potential toxicity marker induced by TAT003 treatment was IL-6, which likely resulted from the TLR9 activity of TAT003. The induction of IL-6 has been shown to be important for the therapeutic activity of TLR9 agonists in the clinic, indicating that low levels of IL-6 induction may contribute to TAT003 efficacy. Similarly, IL-2 levels were found to be elevated in blood samples from half of the treated mice. This increase in IL-2 cannot be attributed to TAT003 molecule leakage in the blood since biodistribution data suggest that TAT003 bound IL-2 should fall below detection levels of our cytokine paneling assay. Nonetheless, the absolute levels of IL-2 (1.78 pg/mL) found in those animals were still very close to the detection limit and did not appear to be a concern for animal well-being. The overexpression of cytokines in tumor tissues, combined with the favorable low systemic cytokine induction profile, correlates with TAT003’s advantageous biodistribution.

On intratumoral injection, TAT003 molecules mostly stay within tumor tissue, with a maximal leakage of 0.01% to other organs or in the blood. The small accumulation of TAT003 observed in the draining lymph node, combined with findings from our phagocytosis assay, suggests that TAT003 might be eliminated through the mononuclear phagocytic system, which is consistent with the route of elimination reported for other bacteriophages. TAT003 could thus have a good safety profile, allowing the treatment of patients with resectable/locally advanced tumors. Early clinical data has also demonstrated the safety of bacteriophage in humans, further supporting their use as innovative drugs. Bacteriophage-derived multimodal nanofilaments thus offer an attractive alternative to personalized cancer vaccines, by providing an off-the-shelf option to immunize patients against tumor antigens and improve response rates.

Online methods

Reagents, strains, medium and cell culture

All DNA cloning and assembly steps were performed on the filamentous bacteriophage, M13K07 (NEB) using Escherichia coli MM294 (DSMZ) before transferring to E. coli MG1655 (E. coli Genetic Stock Center) for bacteriophage production. Except for bacteriophage batch production, bacteria were grown at 37°C overnight in Luria broth Miller (LB) or on LB agar medium supplemented with appropriate antibiotics. Antibiotics were used at the following working concentrations: ampicillin 100 µg/mL, kanamycin 50 µg/mL, spectinomycin 100 µg/mL (all from Bio Basic).

The cell lines A20 (American Type Culture Collection (ATCC), TIB-208), B16-F10 (ATCC, CRL-6475), CT26 (ATCC, CRL-2638), 4T1 (ATCC, CRL-2539) and SU-DHL-1 (ATCC, CRL-2955) were obtained from ATCC and cultured according to their recommendations. Briefly, the A20, CT26, 4T1 and SU-DHL-1 cell lines were grown in Rosewell Park Memorial Institute medium (RPMI)-1640 medium (Cytiva, SH30027.01) supplemented with 10% v/v fetal bovine serum (FBS) (Fisher Scientific, FB12999102), and 1X penicillin-streptomycin (Cytiva, SV30010) at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2. The B16-F10 cell line was grown in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (Corning, 10–017-CV) supplemented with 10% FBS, and 1X of penicillin-streptomycin at 37°C in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2. Routine subculture and medium renewal were performed according to ATCC specifications for each cell line.

For the PD-1 blockade assay, we used Promega’s thaw-and-use kit (J1250) and strictly followed the manufacturer’s protocol. Luminescence readings were obtained using a Tecan Spark plate reader.

Plasmid construction

DNA minipreps were prepared using the EZ10-Spin Column Plasmid Miniprep kit (Bio Basic, BS614) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. PCR amplifications were performed using TransStart FastPfu Fly DNA polymerase (TransGen Biotech, AP231), whereas qPCR reactions were done with 2X Supermix prepared by the Plateforme de purification de protéine of the Université de Sherbrooke (#S-mix). Before assembling PCR products into plasmids using NEBuilder Ultra DNA assembly master mix (NEB, E2621L), PCR reactions were purified by Solid Phase Reversible Immobilization using Agencourt Ampure DNA XP DNA binding beads (Beckman Coulter, A63881) following the manufacturer’s guidelines. DNA concentration and purity were routinely assessed using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Competent E. coli MM294 cells were transformed by plasmid DNA following standard procedures or by electroporation. Briefly, to prepare electrocompetent E. coli strains, an overnight culture of the desired host strain was diluted 1/50 in 20 mL LB and grown to an optical density (OD600nm) of 0.6. Cells were then washed three times in sterile 10% glycerol solution and resuspended in 200 µL 10% glycerol. DNA was then added to 40 µL electrocompetent cells, and the mixture was transferred in a 1 mm electroporation cuvette. An electroporation pulse of 1.8 kV, 25 µF, and 200 Ω for 50 ms was applied to the cells, which were then resuspended in 1 mL LB and incubated 1 hour at 37°C for recovery before plating on selective media.

Bacteriophage batch production and purification

A 15 mm culture tube containing 10 mL 2xYT broth (Bio Basic, SD7019) supplemented with the appropriate antibiotics was inoculated with a frozen stock of E. coli MG1655 containing one of the nanofilament expression system and incubated at 37°C with 180 rpm for 18 hours. The overnight culture was next transferred to a 2-L Erlenmeyer flask containing 500 mL 2xYT broth supplemented with antibiotics, and incubated at 37°C, rotating at 180 rpm until reaching 0.4–0.6 OD600nm. The culture was then cooled on ice for at least 15 min and incubated at 23°C, 220 rpm shaking for 20 hours. The next day, the culture was centrifuged at 13,000×g for 20 min at 4°C. The 500 mL supernatant was then clarified on a 0.2 µm Polyethersulfone (PES) membrane filter unit (Fisherbrand, FB12566504). Next, 450 mL clarified supernatant was distributed equally between 500 mL clear centrifugal bottles and 25 mL ice-cold Polyethylene glycol-Sodium chloride (PEG-NaCl) solution (20 % w/v PEG 8000, 2.5 M NaCl) was added to each bottle. The bottles were inverted 15 times and incubated on ice for 1 hour before being centrifuged at 13,000×g for 20 min at 4°C. The bulk supernatant was decantated, and the bottles were centrifuged once more at 13,000×g for 10 min at 4°C. The remaining supernatant was aspirated, and the phage pellets dried for 10 min by inverting the bottles on absorbent paper. Phage pellets were carefully washed without being dislodged with 1 mL PBS containing 0.1 mM CaCl2. The washing solution was aspirated, and phage pellets resuspended and pooled in 2–5 mL PBS containing 0.1 mM CaCl2. Resuspended phage were incubated on ice for 1 hour, vortexed for 30 s, then endotoxins were removed using 0.5 mL to 0.75 mL EndoTrap HD resin (Lionex, LET0023) according to the manufacturer’s batch-mode protocol. The final endotoxin level of the preparation was determined using the PyroGene Recombinant Factor C Endotoxin Detection System (Lonza, 50–658U). Only bacteriophage preparations with an endotoxin level below 350 EU per dose were included in in vivo mouse studies.

Sandwich ELISA for measuring phage concentrations and evaluating engineered phage

The overall procedure for three different ELISAs is described in this section, followed by details specific to each assay in the paragraphs below. A Nunc MaxiSorp Flat-Bottom 96-wells plate (Invitrogen, 44-2404-21) was coated with 100 µL containing a “capture protein” (anti-M13/fd/f1, recombinant human or murine PD-L1, or murine IL-2 receptor, see below) diluted at 2 µg/mL in coating buffer (0.05 M carbonate-bicarbonate; pH 9.6) for 18 hours at 4°C. Wells were then washed three times with 200 µL Tris Buffered Saline-Tween (TBS-T) (20 mM Tris-Base, 150 mM NaCl, 0.05% Tween-20; pH 7.5). Residual protein binding sites were blocked with 200 µL blocking buffer (TBS-T, 1 % w/v non-fat powdered milk, 3 % w/v Bovine Serum Albumine (BSA)) for 1 hour at room temperature, then wells were washed once with 200 µL TBS-T. Samples (M13, TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003) or standard curve solution (M13K07 phage) were then diluted in PBS (137 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl in 10 mM phosphate buffer; pH 7.3–7.5). 100 µl were transferred to blocked wells. The plate was incubated for 1 hour at room temperature, washed three times with 200 µL TBS-T, then bound phage were detected either directly by an anti-M13/fd/f1 antibody-HRP conjugate or indirectly by a rabbit primary anti-mIL-2 antibody followed by a secondary, goat-(anti-rabbit IgG) antibody-HRP conjugate (described below). In either case, 100 µL detection antibody-HRP conjugate was diluted in blocking buffer, added to each well, and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature in the dark. Wells were then washed five times with 200 µL TBS-T, 100 µL 3,3'5,5'-Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) substrate solution (BioLegend, 421501) was added to each well, and plates were incubated at room temperature. Reactions were stopped after 3–5 min by adding 100 µL 1 M H2SO4. The OD450nm was measured using a plate reader (Multiskan GO, Thermo Fisher), and the data was plotted using GraphPad Prism V.10.0.0. All experiments were performed in triplicate.

To evaluate phage titers, wells were coated with 2 µg/mL containing a capture-protein anti-M13/fd/f1 antibody (ProGen, 61097). Samples were diluted in PBS, and a standard curve ranging from 1×107 to 5×109 bacteriophage particles/mL was prepared using M13K07 helper phage (NEB, N0315S) diluted in PBS. Bound phages were detected using an anti-M13/fd/f1-HRP antibody conjugate (Progen, 61097-HRP) diluted 1:500. The concentration of phage in each sample was calculated using the standard curve data, by fitting a four parameters sigmoidal function where X is the concentration using GraphPad Prism V.10.0.0.

To assay anti-PD-L1 scFv displayed by phage, wells were coated with either 2 µg/mL of human PD-L1 recombinant protein (Sino Biological, 10084-HNAH) or mouse PD-L1 recombinant protein (Sino Biological, 50010-M08H). Nanofilament variants were diluted in PBS to a standard concentration of 5×1011 particles/mL and detected by the anti-M13/fd/f1-HRP antibody-HRP conjugate diluted 1:500.

To assay mIL-2 displayed by phage, wells were coated with 2 µg/mL anti-mIL-2 antibody (Sino Biological, 51061-R257). Nanofilament variants’ concentrations were equilibrated at 5×1011 particles/mL, and detected with the anti-M13/fd/f1-HRP antibody conjugate diluted 1:500.

For the confirmation of dual display of anti-PD-L1 scFv and mIL-2, wells were coated with 2 µg/mL human PD-L1 and blocked before adding 5×1011 particles/mL of each nanofilament variant. Only nanofilaments able to bind PD-L1 will bind to the wells. Then, displayed mIL-2 was detected using a primary rabbit anti-mIL-2 IgG (Sino Biological, 11848-R365) diluted 1:500 in blocking buffer. For detection, an anti-rabbit IgG-HRP antibody conjugate (Cytiva, NA934-100UL) diluted 1:2000 in blocking buffer was added to each well.

Stimulation of tumor-associated macrophages assay

Macrophages were generated from the bone marrow of C57BL/6 mice as described previously. Briefly, the tibias and femurs of euthanized mice were collected, and a 25 G needle attached to a 10 mL syringe was used to flush the bones with sterile, cold PBS. The marrow was collected and passed through a 70 µm strainer and cells were spun at 500 g for 5 min. The cell pellet was resuspended in ACK lysis buffer (Gibco) for 5 min at room temperature and spun at 500 g for 5 min. The final pellet was resuspended in complete medium (DMEM/F12 containing 10% v/v FBS, 100 U/mL penicillin, 100 µg/mL streptomycin and 2 mM L-Glutamine (all from Wisent Bioproducts), and 100 ng/mL murine Macrophage Colony-Stimulating Factor (M-CSF) (Peprotech #315–02)) and plated at a density of 2×106 cells/well in a 6-well plate. Fresh medium containing M-CSF was added on Day 3. On Day 7, macrophages were cultured in complete medium containing 30% supernatant from B16-F10 melanoma confluent culture (grown according to the procedures described in the Reagents, strains, medium and cell culture section) for 48 hours to induce TAM phenotype. On Day 9, PBS or 1×1011 particles of M13 bacteriophage were added for 24 hours, then collected, washed with Fluorescence-Assited Cell Sorting (FACS) buffer, and transferred to a 96-well plate and 2.5 µL Fc Block (BD Biosciences #553142) was added to each well and incubated for 10 min at room temperature. Then, 1 µL of each of the following antibodies was added to each well: anti-CD80-BV510 (BD Biosciences, #740130), anti-CD86-PE-Cy7 (BD Biosciences, #560582), anti-H2Db-PE (BD Biosciences, #553574) and anti-H2Kb-BV421 (BD Biosciences, #566240). Cells were incubated for 30 min at 4°C and washed twice in FACS buffer before resuspension in 50 µL FACS buffer. Marker expression levels were next assessed by flow cytometry (Cytoflex 20, Beckman Coulter) and analyzed in CytExpert 2.5 (Beckman Coulter).

PD-L1 competitive binding assay by flow cytometry

A20 or SU-DHL-1 cells (grown according to the procedures described in the Reagents, strains, medium and cell culture section) were harvested and transferred in a 15 mL Falcon tube on ice. Tumor cell phagocytosis assay

The quantification of phagocytosis events using bone marrow-derived macrophages was performed as described previously. Briefly, BMDMs were generated from the femurs of BALB/c mice. A 25-G needle attached to a 10 mL syringe was used to flush marrow from femurs with cold RPMI medium containing 10% v/v FBS, 50 µM β-mercaptoethanol and 1X penicillin-streptomycin. The resulting marrow in suspension was passed through a 70 µm strainer and spun at 400 g for 5 min at 4°C. The cell pellet was resuspended in complete RPMI containing 50 ng/mL murine GM-CSF (BioLegend, 576302) and plated at a density of 2×105 cells/mL in a 100 mm cell culture petri dish. Fresh medium containing GM-CSF was added on Day 3, then on Day 7, the culture medium was removed, and the adherent cells were detached from the petri using cold Versene 1X and the differentiation of bone-marrow cells into macrophages was assessed by flow cytometer using a PE-labeled anti-F4/80 antibody (BioLegend, 111603) (data not shown). 2×107 A20 cells were stained in 5 mL of Carboxylfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE) (Thermo Fisher Scientific # C34554) diluted to a final concentration of 5 µM for 10 min at 37°C+5% CO2. Cells were washed in complete medium and 1×1011 particles of TAT003 were added to 5×105 CFSE-stained A20 cells in relevant conditions and incubated for an hour at 37°C+5% CO2. A20 cells stained or not and coated with TAT003 or not were next mixed in equal parts with macrophages (5×105 cell for each cell type) and incubated for 24 hours at 37°C+5% CO2. Cells were next harvested using cold Versene and stained using a PE-labeled anti-F4/80 antibody (BioLegend, 111603) by acquiring phagocytosis event using a FACS Accuri C6 Plus system.

Biodistribution of TAT003 after intratumoral administration in mice

BALB/c mice received a single subcutaneous injection of 5×106 A20 cells in their right flank. On reaching a tumor volume of 75–250 mm3, four untreated mice served as controls to isolate organs and evaluate the level of non-specific amplification in each tissue type (data not shown but was used to set a detection limit). Other mice either received an intratumoral injection of 1×1011 particles of M13 or TAT003. Mice from both treatment groups were next euthanized at different time points (four mice per time point); 2 hours, 24 hours, and 72 hours to evaluate the distribution of nanofilament particles in blood and different organs. For each mouse, the brain, lung, spleen, liver, right kidney, tumor draining lymph node, tumor, and blood were harvested. For blood specifically, it was retrieved from mice under anesthesia with isoflurane by cardiac puncture and collected in a microtube supplemented with 5 µL EDTA 0.5 M to prevent clotting. Organs and blood samples were weighed and homogenized in 500 µL PBS using a glass bead in a FastPrep-24 instrument (MP Biomedicals) at maximal speed for 2 min. For each sample, the presence of M13 and TAT003 was evaluated by qPCR. Additionally, the biodistribution of M13 in mice treated with M13 treated mice biodistribution was evaluated by infection assays to validate the accuracy of the qPCR method. Both detection methods are described in the following two paragraphs.

For infection assays, freshly homogenized organs were used. OD600nm of an overnight culture of E. coli ER2738 (F+) was measured. E. coli cells were washed in LB and resuspended to a density of 1.0 OD600nm. Then, the E. coli ER2738 culture was aliquoted 1 mL per tube for infection assay. For each tissue or blood sample, 10 µL homogenate was added to 1 mL E. coli ER2738 cells. The infection reactions were next incubated for 2 hours at 37°C. After incubation, infections were serially diluted and plated on selective media for colony-forming units (CFU). This was made possible as the bacteriophage M13 in our system is expressed from a helper plasmid and a phagemid, which respectively encodes kanamycin and spectinomycin resistance, allowing their detection by CFU. Plates were incubated overnight at 37°C then CFU were counted. The number of infection events was then normalized to tissue weight. Infection assays are a robust means of evaluating M13 biodistribution as they detect intact bacteriophage particles (online supplemental extended data figure 4a).

For the qPCR assay, tissue homogenates were aliquoted 100 µL per tube and flash frozen. Once all samples were obtained, each tissue type was processed serially. Briefly, tissue homogenates were thawed rapidly by adding 900 µL PBS to the aliquot to dilute PCR inhibitors. For each organ type, a standard curve was also performed in tissues diluted 1:10 in PBS by spiking serial dilutions of M13K07 ranging from 1×1010 to 1×104 bacteriophage. Also, a non-spiked tissue was analyzed for background amplification. For all controls and samples, 10 µL tissue homogenate was added to 100 µL Chelex 100 5% w/v (Bio-Rad) and incubated for 25 min at 55°C and then for 10 min at 100°C to extract DNA. The qPCR reaction was performed using the 2X Supermix from the Plateforme de purification de protéine of the Université de Sherbrooke with primers binding in aad7 within the phagemid (5’-GGCGATTCTGCTAACAAAGG-3’ and 5’-GGCGAGTTCCATAGCTTCAG-3’). A standard curve for each organ type was generated from the data and used to convert sample amplification thresholds into bacteriophage particles. Then, data were normalized by tissue weight or blood volume. Since TAT003 particles are not infectious, the qPCR quantification method was first validated by comparing M13 biodistribution using an infection assay and qPCR (online supplemental extended data figure 4).

Efficacy animal models

For all intratumoral injections, treatments were diluted in PBS and administered in a volume of 50 µL. All mouse-related protocols were strictly evaluated to avoid animal suffering by the Université de Sherbrooke Animal Care Committee. Water and standard chow (Charles River) were provided ad libitum to the animals during the experiments. No more than five animals were housed in the same ventilated cage. All experiments used a 1:1 mix of male and female, 6–8 weeks old BALB/c mice (Charles River), who were given at least a 3 days rest on arrival. Daily evaluation of mouse health and weight revealed no significant health or weight loss for all mice throughout the experiments. All experiments were performed using a blind study design, where the person responsible for tumor measurement, treatment administration, and compilation of tumor volume was unaware of the treatment administered. Treatments were prepared by another contributor, and all treatments had the same aspect as the placebo PBS treatment. Mice were randomized by the tumor volume before treatment, maintaining the same sex ratio in all treatment groups. Tumor volumes were always measured twice a week and always before treatment injection to avoid the confounding effect of the injection on tumor volume. The Université de Sherbrooke Animal Care Committee required animals to be euthanized once tumor volume reached 1500 mm3. Whenever tumors created skin rash at the tumor site, clay was applied to the skin of the animal for their comfort. In a very limited number of cases, some mice were euthanized to avoid unnecessary suffering during the experiment due to ulcerous lesions attributable to the CT26 model. Those mice were excluded from analysis. Also, mice whose tumors did not fit the required volume range at the time of first treatment were excluded from studies. For experiments requiring the collection of tumor tissue, mice that did not have any tumor left on the day of collection were excluded from the study. Similarly, for experiments that require primary cells, samples that did not have enough cells to generate quality data were excluded from analysis. All other mice were included in the studies.

For primary efficacy studies, 1×106 CT26 cells or 5×106 A20 cells were injected subcutaneously in the right flank of BALB/c mice. Alternatively, 1×106 B16 F10 cells were injected subcutaneously in the right flank of C57BL6J mice. Tumor width and lengths were measured with an electronic caliper, where volume=length×width2×0.5 as previously calculated. Tumors were grown to a volume of 25–100 mm3 (CT26 model) or 75–250 mm3 (A20 model) or 15–75 mm3 (B16 F10 model) before administering treatments at Days 0, 3 or 4 and 7. Tumor volume, mouse weight and general health were next recorded twice a week until a maximal tumor volume of 1500 mm3 was reached. For re-challenge experiments, mice that received a treatment and achieved complete remission were kept under surveillance for 178 days before receiving a second injection of cancer cells on Day 179, this time subcutaneously in the left flank.

For primary efficacy studies in A20, CT26, and B16 F10 models, nanofilament variants M13, TAT003ΔIL2, and TAT003 were administered at a dose of 1×1011 molecules/injection. When used, atezolizumab and mIL-2 treatments were administered at a dose of 5×1011 molecules/injection. As each nanofilament displayed 4–5 molecules of anti-PD-L1 scFv and mIL-2, these doses allowed the direct comparison of equimolar doses between the products and the nanofilaments to adequately assess the contribution of each to the therapeutic effect. For the abscopal effect models, nanofilaments M13, TAT003ΔIL2 and TAT003 doses were increased to 1×1012 particles/injection as this dosage was found not to induce any additional toxicity in mice and improved systemic response rates. Finally, for the metastatic 4T1 model, nanofilaments were administered at 5×1011 particles/injection.

For the investigation of the abscopal effect, tumors were engrafted in both flanks of mice with a 4-day delay between engraftment in the right and left flank. Intratumoral treatments were administered in the right flank tumor once it had grown to a volume of 25–100 mm3 (CT26 model) or 75–250 mm3 (A20 model). Intratumoral treatments were repeated three times on Days 0, 3 or 4 and 7 for both tumor models. Additionally, for the CT26 model, mice received an intraperitoneal injection containing 200 µg in 100 µL PBS of either an anti-PD-1 (Bio X Cell, BP0146) or an isotype control antibody (Bio X Cell, BP0089) on Days 10 and 14. Mice were euthanized at the end of the experiment or once the combined volume of tumors reached 2000 mm3. For the investigation of the effect of anti-TAT003 antibodies on treatment efficacy, the A20 model was performed as described above, but mice received a subcutaneous injection of 1×1011 particles of TAT003 in 50 µL 6 days prior to the engraftment of the first A20 tumor.

For the 4T1 triple-negative breast cancer model, BALB/c mice first received an injection of 1×105 4T1 cells in their second thoracic mammary gland. On Days 3, 5, and 7 post-engraftments, mice received an intratumoral injection of either PBS, or 5×1011 particles of M13, TAT003ΔIL2 or TAT003. Primary tumors were next resected on Day 9 and mice received an intraperitoneal injection containing 200 µg in 100 µL PBS of either an anti-PD-1 (Bio X Cell, BP0146) or an isotype control (Bio X Cell, BP0089) on Days 10 and 13. Since the 4T1 model is known to produce lung metastases quickly after engraftment, mice were kept under observation for signs of respiratory distress. Mice displaying severe respiratory distress were euthanized.

Immunohistochemical study of resected tumors

A single A20 tumor was engrafted in the right flank of eight mice and treated as described in the Efficacy animal models section. On reaching a volume of 75–250 mm3, tumors received three injections of either TAT003 (n=4) or PBS (n=4) on Days 0, 3 and 7. On Day 8, tumors were resected, placed in a TRU-FLOW tissue cassette, and submerged in 10% NBF. After an overnight incubation at room temperature, the cassettes were washed twice in 70% Ethanol (EtOH) for 5 min, then incubated in fresh 70% EtOH overnight. The next day, cassettes were transferred to another container with 70% EtOH and delivered at the Plateforme d’histologie de the Université de Sherbrooke for paraffin embedding. Paraffin blocks were sectioned in 4 µm thick slices with a microtome before immunohistologic staining. Antigen retrieval was performed using 10 mM citric acid pH 6.00 for 12 min. Tumor slices were blocked with 2% BSA in PBS for 30 min and incubated overnight at 4°C with anti-M13-HRP antibody conjugate diluted 1:100 in blocking buffer. The bound antibody was detected using the 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (Sigma-Aldrich, D12384) chromogenic substrate solution. The slices were counter-stained using the Hematoxylin Gill III (Sigma-Aldrich, 65067). Image acquisition of the slices was performed on a NanoZoomer and was analyzed for phage-positive cell quantification using QuPath-V.0.4.4. The same procedure was performed with A20 and CT26 tumor samples at Day 8 for CD3+staining using an anti-CD3-HRP conjugate diluted 1:100 in blocking buffer.

Detection of tumor cell-specific T-cells in blood samples

The identification of tumor-cell specific T-cells was performed as previously described with minor adaptations. A20 tumors were engrafted in the right flank of mice and on the left flank of mice 4 days later. On reaching 100–200 mm3, right flank tumors were next treated as described in the Efficacy animal models section by intratumoral injection of either PBS, 1×1011 molecules of TAT003ΔIL2, or 1×1011 molecules of TAT003, on Days 0, 4 and 7 (four mice per treatment group, one mouse from the TAT003 treatment group was excluded from the study due to an experimental mistake that led to a drastically lower cell viability in culturing steps). On Day 8, mouse blood was collected through cardiac puncture and transferred to a 1.5 mL microtube containing 5 µL 0.5 M EDTA at pH 8.0. The blood was next mixed to an equal amount of PBS 1X and layered on top of Lymphoprep (StemCell Technologies, 1856) at a 1:8 ratio. Tubes were centrifuged at 800 g for 20 min at room temperature with brakes turned off. Using a P200 micropipette, the buffy coat containing mouse PBMC was collected and transferred to a new 15 mL tube containing 5 mL complete RPMI-1640 media (RPMI+10% FBS + 1% penicillin-streptomycin) + 0.05 mM of β-mercaptoethanol. Tubes were centrifuged at 400 g for 5 min and the supernatant was removed. PBMC pellet was resuspended in 3 mL complete RPMI-1640+0.05 mM β-mercaptoethanol, transferred into an untreated 6-well plate, and incubated at 37°C 5% CO2 overnight.

The day prior to PBMC isolation, A20 cells were treated with 7 ng/mL IFNγ (Thermo Fisher 14-8311-63) for 48 hours to increase antigen presentation. On the day of the experiment, 200,000 IFNγ-induced A20 cells were added to each PBMC well. As a positive control of T-cell activation leading to IFNγ expression, one well was additionally treated with 10 ng/mL of Phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA, Fisher Scientific, AC356150010) and 1.5 µg/mL of ionomycin (Fisher Scientific, 4 079 511 MG). After 1 hour of incubation at 37°C 5% CO2, all wells were then treated with 1X Brefeldin A (Thermo Fisher, 00–4506) for 5 hours at 37°C 5% CO2 to inhibit IFNγ secretion in the extracellular medium. Media and cells were next transferred to a 15 mL tube and centrifuged at 500 g for 5 min. Cells were resuspended in 100 µL Anti-Mo CD16/32 (Invitrogen 14-0161-82) diluted 1/200 in FACS buffer (PBS+1% BSA) and incubated for 20 min at 4°C. Cells were washed once in FACS buffer and centrifuged at 500 g for 5 min. Anti-CD8-PerCP/Cy5.5 (BioLegend, 100733) and anti-CD4-FITC (BioLegend, 100509) were diluted 1/100 in FACS buffer, and cells were stained in 100 µL of appropriate antibody cocktail for 20 min at room temperature in the dark. Cells were rinsed once with FACS buffer and fixed using 4% paraformaldehyde (Thermo Fisher, A11313 36) for 10 min at 4°C in the dark. After one rinse with FACS buffer, cells were incubated in permeabilization buffer (PBS+1% BSA + 0.03% Triton X-100) for 15 min at room temperature in the dark. Anti-IFNγ-APC (BioLegend, 505810) was diluted 1/100 in permeabilization buffer and cells were stained for 20 min at room temperature in the dark. Lastly, cells were rinsed once using FACS buffer and resuspended in 500 µL FACS buffer. Analysis was performed using the BD Accuri C6 Plus flow cytometer system.

Detection of TAT003 specific antibodies

1×106 B16 F10 cells were injected subcutaneously in the right flank of C57BL6J mice. On reaching a volume of 15–75 mm3, mice received 1×1011 TAT003 particles by intratumoral injection on Day 0, 4 and 7. On Days 0, 1, 5, 8, 14, 21, 28 and 35, three mice were anesthetized with 5% isoflurane and blood was collected by intracardiac puncture followed by euthanasia. Serum was isolated by centrifugation at 2,000 g for 10 min and flash frozen.

Nunc Maxisorp ELISA plates were next coated with 1×1011 particles of TAT003 in 100 µL of coating buffer (0.05 M carbonate-bicarbonate; pH 9.6) per well for 18 hours at 4°C. Wells were then washed three times with 200 µL TBS-T (20 mM Tris-Base, 150 mM NaCl, 0.05% Tween-20; pH 7.5). Residual protein binding sites were blocked with 200 µL blocking buffer (TBS-T, 1 % w/v non-fat powdered milk, 3 % w/v BSA) for 1 hour at room temperature, then wells were washed once with 200 µL TBS-T. Serum samples were thawed on ice and diluted 1/500 in PBS (137 mM NaCl, 2.7 mM KCl in 10 mM phosphate buffer; pH 7.3–7.5). 100 µl of the diluted serum were transferred to blocked wells. The plate was incubated for 1 hour at room temperature, washed three times with 200 µL TBS-T, then bound antibodies were detected directly by an anti-mouse IgG-HRP conjugate (Cayman, Cat#10004302) diluted 1/5000 in blocking buffer. 100 µL of this mixture was next added to each well, and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature in the dark. Wells were then washed five times with 200 µL TBS-T, 100 µL TMB substrate solution (BioLegend, 421501) was added to each well, and plates were incubated at room temperature. Reactions were stopped after 3–5 min by adding 100 µL 1 M H2SO4. The OD450nm was measured using a plate reader (Multiskan GO, Thermo Fisher), and the data was plotted using GraphPad Prism V.10.0.0. All experiments were performed in technical duplicates, data are representative of three different animals.
