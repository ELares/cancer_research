# Preclinical evaluation of antitumor activity and toxicity of TROP2-specific CAR-T cells for treatment of triple-negative breast cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) represents a subtype of breast cancer with poorest prognosis due to limited effective targeted therapies. Chimeric antigen receptor T cell (CAR-T) therapy has shown remarkable efficacy in treating hematological cancers, but its application in TNBC requires further development. One major obstacle is the lack of suitable tumor-specific target in TNBC. Inspired by recent success of trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP2) antibody-drug conjugate in TNBC, we developed a second-generation CAR that specifically targets TROP2 and formally evaluated its antitumor activity and safety profile using in vitro and in vivo models.

**METHODS**: A CAR molecule targeting TROP2 was constructed based on the clinically-validated humanized antibody Sacituzumab and expressed in primary human T cells using a retroviral vector. Tumor cytotoxicity, cytokine production and T-cell proliferation of TROP2 CAR-T cells were tested against multiple TNBC cell lines in vitro. Antitumor efficacy was evaluated using orthotopic and metastatic models of cell line-derived xenograft in NSG mice and in patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model. The safety profile of TROP2 CAR-T cells was assessed using TROP2-humanized immunocompetent mice and an "AND"-logic gated SynNotch CAR targeting B7-H3 and TROP2 was engineered to minimize off-tumor, on-target toxicity of TROP2 CAR-T cells.

**RESULTS**: Human TROP2 CAR-T cells demonstrated robust antitumor activity in vitro and in orthotopic/metastatic/PDX xenograft mouse models. TROP2 CAR-T cells caused lethal on-target, off-tumor toxicity in TROP2-humanized immunocompetent mice, causing severe tissue damage in lungs and systemic inflammation. The B7-H3/TROP2 "AND"-logic gated SynNotch CAR-T cells showed comparable antitumor efficacy without causing apparent adverse effects as in TROP2 CAR-T cells.

**CONCLUSIONS**: These data indicate that while CAR-T therapy targeting TROP2 possesses potent antitumor activity against TNBC cell lines and PDX, its potential side effects could be lethal due to TROP2 expression in vital organs such as the lung. Using an "AND"-logic gated CAR is a viable solution to overcome its in vivo toxicity. Our study lays the groundwork for future development of TROP2 CAR-T cell therapy for TNBC.

Preclinical evaluation of antitumor activity and toxicity of TROP2-specific CAR-T cells for treatment of triple-negative breast cancer

Abstract

Background

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) represents a subtype of breast cancer with poorest prognosis due to limited effective targeted therapies. Chimeric antigen receptor T cell (CAR-T) therapy has shown remarkable efficacy in treating hematological cancers, but its application in TNBC requires further development. One major obstacle is the lack of suitable tumor-specific target in TNBC. Inspired by recent success of trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP2) antibody-drug conjugate in TNBC, we developed a second-generation CAR that specifically targets TROP2 and formally evaluated its antitumor activity and safety profile using in vitro and in vivo models.

Methods

A CAR molecule targeting TROP2 was constructed based on the clinically-validated humanized antibody Sacituzumab and expressed in primary human T cells using a retroviral vector. Tumor cytotoxicity, cytokine production and T-cell proliferation of TROP2 CAR-T cells were tested against multiple TNBC cell lines in vitro. Antitumor efficacy was evaluated using orthotopic and metastatic models of cell line-derived xenograft in NSG mice and in patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model. The safety profile of TROP2 CAR-T cells was assessed using TROP2-humanized immunocompetent mice and an “AND”-logic gated SynNotch CAR targeting B7-H3 and TROP2 was engineered to minimize off-tumor, on-target toxicity of TROP2 CAR-T cells.

Results

Human TROP2 CAR-T cells demonstrated robust antitumor activity in vitro and in orthotopic/metastatic/PDX xenograft mouse models. TROP2 CAR-T cells caused lethal on-target, off-tumor toxicity in TROP2-humanized immunocompetent mice, causing severe tissue damage in lungs and systemic inflammation. The B7-H3/TROP2 “AND”-logic gated SynNotch CAR-T cells showed comparable antitumor efficacy without causing apparent adverse effects as in TROP2 CAR-T cells.

Conclusions

These data indicate that while CAR-T therapy targeting TROP2 possesses potent antitumor activity against TNBC cell lines and PDX, its potential side effects could be lethal due to TROP2 expression in vital organs such as the lung. Using an “AND”-logic gated CAR is a viable solution to overcome its in vivo toxicity. Our study lays the groundwork for future development of TROP2 CAR-T cell therapy for TNBC.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP2) is a potential target antigen of triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), and TROP2 targeting chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cells had been developed for various solid tumors, but its safety profile had not been formally evaluated.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Using a TROP2-humanized mouse model that fully recapitulates the TROP2 expression pattern in human, we showed that TROP2 CAR-T cells caused lethal toxicity in lungs that express TROP2 antigen. We developed B7-H3/TROP2 “AND”-logic gated SynNotch CAR-T cells that fully retained the antitumor activity of TROP2 CAR-T cells without in vivo adverse effects.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Considering TROP2 CAR-T cells have recently been granted permission for clinical trials, our study warns against direct targeting of TROP2 due to its potential lethal toxicity and provides a viable solution to overcome such adverse effects.

Introduction

 Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is an aggressive type of breast cancer that accounts for 15%–20% of all breast cancer cases. TNBC is characterized by negative staining for HER2, estrogen receptor and progesterone receptor, hence its treatment options are limited while having the worst prognosis among all types of breast cancer. Neoadjuvant therapy and post-surgery adjuvant therapy that combines chemotherapy and immune checkpoint inhibitors showed promise in early phases of TNBC. However, patients still suffer from high relapse rate. Hence, novel therapeutic targets and strategies are in dire need for patients with TNBC. Trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP2, encoded by the TACSTD2 gene) is one of the cell surface targets being explored in TNBC. TROP2 protein was reported to be moderately or strongly expressed in >80% of patients with TNBC from three cohorts. Notably, clinical trials (phase I/II IMMU-132–01 and phase III ASCENT trial) of the antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) targeting TROP2 (Sacituzumab Govitecan, SG) showed significant improvement in objective responses and survival rate in patients with heavily pretreated metastatic TNBC (mTNBC) receiving this ADC compared with single-agent chemotherapy. In 2020, SG received accelerated approval by the US Food and Drug Administration for the treatment of adult patients with mTNBC who received at least two prior therapies and was subsequently recommended by major guidelines worldwide. Recently, data from various clinical studies has provided strong evidence for the efficacy and safety of SG as first or second-line therapy for advanced TNBC. The success of SG in treating TNBC demonstrated that TROP2 as a reliable surface protein targetable by antibody-based therapies for TNBC.

Chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-Ts) are an emerging immunotherapy strategy that relies on an antibody-T cell receptor (TCR) hybrid surface receptor, that is, CAR to directly recognize surface protein targets in a major histocompatibility complex-independent manner. While CAR-T therapy showed remarkable efficacy in the treatment of hematological cancers, its application in solid tumors is still limited. One of the obstacles in the development of solid tumor CAR-T therapy is the lack of appropriate surface antigens that are homogenously and exclusively expressed in tumor tissues. Leveraging the success of TROP2 ADC in TNBC, it is likely that TROP2-specific CAR-Ts could also be effective in eradicating TROP2+ TNBC cells while maintaining long-term protective memory against potential relapses. The development of TROP2-specific CAR-T therapy has been reported by others, and it showed robust antitumor activity against multiple TROP2-positive tumor models. However, the safety profile of TROP2-specific CAR-Ts has not been formally investigated in animal models where the cognate hTROP2 antigen is expressed in physiological locations. Despite TROP2-ADC being well-tolerated in clinical trials, CAR-T therapy functions as a living drug that could elicit stronger or unique adverse effects. Hence, such toxicity should be more carefully evaluated, considering TROP2 is expressed in multiple healthy organs (eg, skin, lung and stomach) and TROP2-specific CAR-T therapy is entering Phase I clinical trials (NCT06082557).

Here, we aimed to comprehensively evaluate the antitumor activity and toxicity of a second-generation TROP2-specific CAR molecule consisting of the antigen-binding moiety of clinically proven sacituzumab with CD28 and CD3ζ endodomains. TROP2-specific CAR-Ts (CAR.TROP2) specifically targeted and eradicated TROP2-expressing TNBC cell lines. In addition, CAR.TROP2 showed robust antitumor activity in multiple orthotopic and metastatic xenograft TNBC models and in one patient-derived xenograft (PDX) model. Importantly, we assessed the potential adverse effects of CAR.TROP2 in a TROP2-humanized mouse model and found that CAR.TROP2 caused severe lung toxicity and systemic inflammation, suggesting direct TROP2 targeting by CAR-Ts could be detrimental. To minimize the on-target, off-tumor effects of CAR.TROP2, we developed an “AND” logic-gated SynNotch system that restricts TROP2 CAR molecule expression in non-cancerous areas and confirmed its selective antitumor activity against dual antigen-expressing cells and the lack of in vivo toxicity. In conclusion, our study showed that CAR.TROP2 possesses potent antitumor activity against TNBC but also has potential safety concerns that warrant further investigation in future development and clinical trials.

Methods

Cell culture and medium

Human TNBC cell lines, MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, MDA-MB-436, HCC1806, HCC1937, BT-549, HS-578T and human Burkitt’s lymphoma cell line Daudi were purchased from Procell (Wuhan, China). MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, MDA-MB-436, and HS-578T were cultured in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM, Gibco) supplemented with 10% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS, Transgen, Beijing, China), 2 mM GlutaMAX (Gibco) and 100 U/mL penicillin and 100 µg/mL streptomycin (Gibco). HCC1806, HCC1937, BT-549 and Daudi were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640 (RPMI-1640, Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS (TransGen), 2 mM GlutaMAX and 100 U/mL penicillin and 100 µg/mL streptomycin. Human T cells were maintained in complete human T-cell medium (1/2 RPMI-1640, 1/2 Click’s Media (Mesgen), supplemented with 10% FBS (HyClone), 2 mM GlutaMAX, 100 U/mL of penicillin and 100 µg/mL of streptomycin). Murine T cells were maintained in complete murine T-cell medium (RPMI-1640, 10% FBS (TransGen), 2 mM GlutaMAX, 100 U/mL of penicillin, 100 µg/mL of streptomycin, 50 µM β-mercaptoethanol (Gibco), 1× MEM Non-Essential Amino Acids Solution (Beyotime, Shanghai, China) and 1 mM Sodium Pyruvate (Beyotime)). Cells were maintained in a humidified atmosphere containing 5% CO2 at 37°C.

MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, HCC1806, HCC1937 and Daudi cells were transduced with a retroviral vector encoding the Green Fluorescent Protein (GFP) and positive cells were sorted by Fluorescence-Activated Cell Sorting (FACS). Daudi cells were transduced with retroviral vectors encoding human TROP2 and/or B7-H3 and positive cells were sorted by FACS. To generate firefly luciferase (Ffluc) expressing cell line for in vivo imaging, cells were transduced with a bicistronic retroviral vector expressing Ffluc and puromycin resistant gene and subsequently selected with 5 µg/mL puromycin for 1 week. B7-H3 knockout was achieved by transducing MDA-MB-231 cells with retroviral vectors encoding Cas9 and B7-H3 targeting sgRNA (CTGGTGCACAGTTTCACCGA) driven by a U6 promoter. B7-H3 negative cells were then sorted by FACS. All cell lines were validated by the Short Tandem Repeat (STR) authentication and routinely screened for Mycoplasma contamination. Expression of surface markers and functional readouts were validated by FACS.

Human tissue samples

Paraffin-embedded normal human tissue microarrays (TMA) and TNBC TMA were purchased from Bioaitech (Xi’an, China, MuFDA1021a for normal tissue array and F551101 for TNBC array). Tumor tissues to generate the TNBC-PDX model were obtained from a patient with TNBC under protocols approved byInstitutional Review Board (IRB) at Biocytogen with obtained informed consent.

Plasmid construction

The full-length human TROP2 (encoded by TACSTD2, GenBank: NM_002353.3) gene was amplified by PCR from complementary DNA (cDNA) of MDA-MB-231 cell line, and cloned into the SFG γ-retroviral vector. The extracellular domain and transmembrane region fragment of CD276 (B7-H3) (GenBank: NM_001024736) gene was amplified by PCR from cDNA of BXPC3 cell line, and cloned into the SFG γ-retroviral vector. The variable regions of the heavy and light chains of the TROP2 monoclonal antibody (mAb) were derived from the humanized sacituzumab antibody then cloned as a single-chain variable fragment (scFv) fragment into previously validated CAR formats that include the human CD8α hinge and transmembrane domain, intracellular CD28 costimulatory domain and CD3ζ signaling domain. The scFv fragment targeting human B7-H3 (clone 376.9616) was cloned into the same CAR formats. For the murine version of the TROP2.CAR (mTROP2.CAR), human CD8α, CD28 and CD3ζ were replaced by murine CD28 and CD3ζ. The SynNotch system backbone pHR_PGK_antiCD19_synNotch_Gal4VP64 and pHR_Gal4UAS_tBFP_PGK_mCherry backbone were gifts from Wendell Lim (Addgene plasmid #79125 and #79130; http://n2t.net/addgene: 79125; RRID:Addgene_79125). SynNotch gene cassettes were PCR amplified and cloned into SFG and MSCV retroviral vector for optimal expression in human and murine T cells, respectively.

Retrovirus preparation

Retroviral supernatant used for the transduction of human T cells was prepared based on the described protocol. Briefly, 293T cells were seeded in a 10 cm cell culture dish and transfected with transfer vector, the PegPam plasmid encoding MoMLV gag-pol, and the RDF plasmid encoding the RD114 envelope, using the TransIT-LT1 Transfection Reagent (Mirus) according to the manufacturer’s instruction. Retroviral supernatant to transduce murine T cells were prepared similarly except that pCL-Eco ecotropic packaging vector was used instead of PegPam/RDF. Supernatant containing the retrovirus was collected 48 and 72 hours after transfection and filtered with 0.45 µm filters (Millipore).

Transduction and expansion of CAR-Ts

Human and murine CAR-Ts were manufactured as previously described.

For human CAR-Ts, frozen peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from healthy donors were purchased from STEMCELL Technologies. Thawed PBMCs were activated by plate-bound α-CD3 (1 µg/mL, BioLegend) and α-CD28 (1 µg/mL, BD Biosciences) mAbs for 2 days. Activated T lymphocytes were then transduced with retroviral supernatants using retronectin-coated plates (Takara). 3 days post transduction, T cells are harvested and cultured in complete human T-cell media medium containing interleukin (IL)-7 (10 ng/mL; PeproTech) and IL-15 (5 ng/mL; PeproTech) for an additional 7–9 days. CAR-Ts were washed and cultured in complete T-cell medium without IL-7/IL-15 cytokines for 24 hours before functional assays.

For murine CAR-Ts, splenocytes were harvested from 6 to 8 weeks old C57BL/6 mice. Purified T cells were obtained using the MojoSort Mouse CD3 T Cell Isolation Kit (BioLegend) by negative selection and subsequently activated by plate-bound α-mCD3 (1 µg/mL, Thermo Fisher) and α-mCD28 (1 µg/mL, Thermo Fisher) mAbs for 2 days. Activated murine T lymphocytes were transduced with retroviral supernatants using retronectin-coated plates. 2 days post-transduction, murine T cells are harvested and cultured in complete T-cell media medium containing IL-7 (10 ng/mL) and IL-15 (5 ng/mL) for an additional 1–2 days.

Binding affinity and stimulation of the CAR.TROP2 by recombinant TROP2 protein

Recombinant human TROP2-Fc protein (rhTROP2-Fc) was obtained from ACRO Biosystem. To measure the binding affinity of CAR molecules to TROP2, CAR.TROP2 were incubated with rhTROP2-Fc at different concentrations (16–10,000 ng/mL) and then stained with a secondary PE-conjugated anti-Fc antibody at 4°C for 30 min. CAR.TROP2 were stimulated in non-tissue culture treated plate coated with rhTROP2-Fc at different concentrations (1.6–1,000 ng/mL) for 24 hours and the expression of activation marker was measured by FACS.

Immunohistochemistry and tissue histopathology

Immunohistochemistry staining of TROP2 and B7-H3 on tissue arrays was performed using α-TROP2 (clone EPR20043, abcam) and α-B7-H3 (clone SP206, Abcam) antibodies. The TROP2 and B7-H3 staining were evaluated in cells showing membranous expression and scored by the Immunoreactive Score method. Briefly, according to the intensity of cell staining, the staining score was graded from 0 to 3 as follows: 0 for no positive staining (−), 1 for light yellow (weak positive), 2 for brown yellow (moderate positive), and 3 for brown (strong positive). The positive staining percentage score was graded as follows: 1=<25% cells showed positive staining, 2=26–50% positive staining, 3=51–75% positive staining and 4=76–100% positive staining. The final Immunoreactive Score was obtained by multiplying staining score with the positive staining percentage score, which ranged from 0 to 12.

To evaluate histopathology of tumor and normal tissues, parafilm-embedded tissues were stained with H&E. All the pathological slides were evaluated by a qualified pathologist from the Department of Pathology, the First Affiliated Hospital of Air Force Medical University.

ELISA

Cytotoxic cytokines produced by CAR-Ts on tumor stimulation, such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-α, interferon (IFN)-γ and IL-2 were detected using specific ELISA kits (R&D systems) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Standard curves were performed for each ELISA assay.

Inflammatory cytokines, such as TNF-α, IFN-γ and IL-6, in mouse serum on CAR-Ts infusion were measured using specific ELISA kits (Invitrogen systems) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Standard curves were performed for each ELISA assay.

Flow cytometry

For surface staining, cells were incubated with antibodies (1:100 dilution in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)) at room temperature for 15 min or at 4°C for 30 min. For staining of TROP2-specific scFv molecules on T-cell surface, CAR-Ts were incubated with 10 µg/mL recombinant human TROP2-Fc (ARCOBiosystem) protein followed by Alexa Fluor 647 or PE-conjugated anti-Fc antibody (Clone M1310G05, BioLegend). For intracellular staining, cells were fixed and permeabilized using Cytofix/CytoPerm (BD Biosciences) for 30 min at room temperature and washed with 1× PermWash (BD Biosciences). Subsequent staining was performed using 1× PermWash. In some cases, cells were stained with Zombie Aqua Live/Dead Viability dye (BioLegend, 1:300 dilution in PBS) to gate out dead cells for analysis.

The following antibodies used for the flow cytometry analysis were obtained from BioLegend: PE-conjugated anti-TROP2 (Clone NY18), PE/Dazzle 594-conjugated anti-CD3 (Clone OKT3), APC-conjugated anti-CD3 (Clone OKT3), BV711-conjugated anti-CD4 (Clone OKT4), Alexa Fluor 700-conjugated anti-CD8 (Clone SK1), APC-conjugated anti-CD25 (Clone BC96), BV650-conjugated anti-CD137 (Clone 4B4-1), PE-conjugated anti-CD45RA (Clone H100), PE-conjugated anti-human IgG Fc (Clone M1310G05).

The following antibodies used for the flow cytometry analysis were obtained from BD Biosciences: APC-Cy7-conjugated anti-CD69 (Clone FN50), V450-conjugated anti-granzyme B (Clone GB11), FITC-conjugated anti-CCR7 (Clone 150503).

The following antibodies used for the flow cytometry analysis were obtained from Thermo Fisher: PE-Cyanine7-conjugated CD276 (Clone 7–517).

Flow cytometry data were collected on a NovoCyte Quanteon flow cytometer (Agilent) using NovoExpress software and the flow data were analyzed by FlowJo software (V.10.7, TreeStar).

Co-culture experiments

Adherent tumor cells were seeded in 12-well plates at a concentration of 1×105 cells/well 10–12 hours prior to addition of T cells. Daudi cells were seeded in 24-well plates at a concentration of 2×105 cells/well the same day of T-cell addition.

To assess the cytotoxic functions of CAR-Ts, T cells were added to the wells at different effector-to-target ratio (E:T) ratios of 1:1; 1:2, or 1:4 without the addition of exogenous cytokines for 3 days. Cells were then stained and analyzed/numerated by FACS. In some experiments, co-culture was conducted repetitively as previously described and FACS analysis were carried out after each round of co-culture.

To detect cytokine production and activation markers of CAR-Ts, a 5:1 E:T ratio was adopted in the co-culture system. After 24 hours of co-culture, supernatant and cells were separately collected for ELISA assay or FACS evaluation.

Proliferation assay

T cells were labeled with 5 µM CellTrace Violet (CTV, Thermo Fisher) following manufacturer’s instruction and co-cultured with tumor cells at an E:T ratio of 1:1. CTV dilution of T cells was measured on 3 and 5 days after co-culture by FACS.

Xenogeneic mouse models

Severe immunodeficient NOD/SCID/IL2Rγ−/− (NSG) mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratory and raised in specific pathogen-free environment by Laboratory Animal Center of Southern University of Science and Technology. Severe immunodeficient B-NDG mice used for TNBC-PDX model and TROP2 humanized B-hTROP2 mice were purchased from Biocytogen. C57BL/6 mice were purchased from GemPharmatech.

For orthotopic TNBC models, Ffluc-labeled MDA-MB-231 (1.5×105/mouse), MDA-MB-468 (5×105/mouse) or HCC1937 (5×105/mouse) cells were resuspended in PBS, mixed 1:1 with matrigel (Corning) and engrafted into the fourth pair of mammary fat pads of 6–10 weeks old female NSG mice. Tumor growth was monitored weekly by bioluminescence imaging using the IVIS Lumina II in vivo imaging system. Tumor diameter was also recorded using a caliper. Tumor volume was calculated by the formula: volume=0.5×length×width2. For the metastatic TNBC model, Ffluc-labeled MDA-MB-231 (1×106/mouse) were resuspended in PBS and infused into 6–10 weeks old female NSG mice via tail vein injection. Tumor growth was monitored weekly by IVIS imaging. For the TNBC-PDX model, tumor tissues were engrafted subcutaneously into the right flank of B-NDG mice. Tumor growth was monitored biweekly with a caliper. Mice were euthanized at the end point of each experiment or after showing signs of suffering from heavy tumor burden or any related side effects. No randomization or blinding was used, and no confounders were specifically controlled. The experiment handlers and principal investigators (PIs) were aware of group allocation at all stages of the experiment.

Mouse experiments were performed in accordance with Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee guidelines and were approved by the IRB of SUSTech under protocol number (SUSTech-JY2021002 and SUSTech-JY202205003). TNBC-PDX experiments were conducted at the animal facility of Biocytogen and B-hTROP2 experiments were conducted at the animal facility of Topbiotec. No inclusion and exclusion criteria were set, and data from all tested animals were shown.

TROP2-humanized mouse model

TROP2 humanized mice (B-hTROP2) were generated by inserting human TACSTD2 gene into the mouse native Tacstd2 genome locus. 6 weeks old female B-hTROP2 mice were infused intravenously with untransduced (Ctrl) T cells, mCAR.TROP2 and mCAR.SynNotch-Ts and were monitored for their mental state, food intake and body weight changes. Mice were then euthanized 8 days post T-cell infusion. Immune cell composition of the peripheral blood was analyzed by FACS. Cytotoxic cytokines in serum were detected by ELISA. Tissues from major organs were collected, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) and parafilm embedded for H&E staining and evaluation for tissue damage.

Statistical analysis

Student’s t-tests were used to determine statistically significant differences between two samples. When multiple-comparison analyses were required, statistical significance was evaluated by analysis of variance (ANOVA) (one or two-way), followed by post hoc analysis. If the data reflected measurement of one sample over time or under different conditions, repeated-measures ANOVA was used, followed by Sidak’s post hoc analysis. All statistical analyses were performed with two-tailed tests. Graph generation and statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism, V.8 (GraphPad Software). A p value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

TROP2 is broadly expressed in clinical TNBC samples and TNBC cell lines

While TROP2 was reported to be highly expressed in TNBCs, how its expression compared with other potential TNBC targets (eg, B7-H3, encoded by CD276 gene) remained unclear. Thus, we performed immunohistochemistry (IHC) staining on TNBC tissue microarray using validated commercial TROP2 and B7-H3 specific antibodies. TROP2 and B7-H3 expressions were evaluated by an independent pathologist and only membranous expressions were considered and scored (figure 1A). Using the Immunoreactive Score method, TROP2 expression was detected in 48/50 cases and highly expressed in most cases (figure 1B–D). However, the expression of B7-H3 is relatively narrow with more than half of the cases being negative and 22/50 cases were positive for both B7-H3 and TROP2 (figure 1B–D). These results suggest TROP2 could be a superior target in TNBC compared with B7-H3 regarding patient coverage and antigen expression level. Next, we evaluated the expression of TROP2 on seven human TNBC cell lines by flow cytometry. TROP2 is abundantly expressed in four of seven cell lines (MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, HCC1806, and HCC1937) but negative in BT-549, MDA-MB-436 and HS-578T (figure 1E,F), which is further confirmed with clinically validated sacituzumab antibodies (figure 1G,H). Notably, all seven cell lines were positive for pan-cancer target B7-H3 (figure 1I,J), demonstrating the discrepancies of antigen expression in cell lines and in patient samples. Overall, our data indicate TROP2 is highly expressed in clinical TNBC samples and TNBC cell lines.

Development of TROP2-specific chimeric antigen receptor

Next, we generated TROP2-specific CAR molecule using scFv derived from the humanized sacituzumab antibody and the second generation CAR backbone containing the CD28 co-stimulation and CD3ζ endodomains. Two versions of scFv were used to generate the CAR molecule, varying in the orientation of variable heavy and light chain segment (VH-VL or VL-VH, online supplemental figure S1A), and their expression and functionality were compared. The transduction efficiencies in activated human T cells were comparable, and both CAR molecules were stably expressed during a 12-day ex vivo culture (online supplemental figure S1B and C). Moreover, both TROP2-CAR transduced T cells expanded similarly during IL-7/IL-15 culture and had similar CD4/8 ratio and memory differentiation status (online supplemental figure S1D-F). To evaluate the antigen binding ability of VH-VL and VL-VH CARs, transduced T cells were incubated with serially diluted recombinant TROP2-Fc protein and stained with a secondary anti-Fc antibody. The staining curves overlap between two CAR molecules, suggesting equal antigen binding ability (online supplemental figure S1G and H). Lastly, the functionality of CAR molecules was assessed based on T-cell activation and target cell lysis experiments. Both CAR-Ts were equally activated by plate-bound recombinant TROP2-Fc protein, measured by CD69 expression (online supplemental figure S1I-K). When co-cultured with TROP2+ cell lines at different E:T ratios, both CAR molecules mediated robust cytotoxic ability (online supplemental figure S1L). Since VH-VL and VL-VH TROP2 CAR molecules showed comparable expression and functionality, we chose the VH-VL version for further development.

TROP2-specific CAR-Ts specifically eradicate TROP2-expressing target cells in vitro

To confirm the specificity of TROP2 CAR-Ts (CAR.TROP2), we over-expressed human TROP2 cDNA in lymphoma cell line Daudi, which is naturally TROP2 negative (DaudiTROP2, online supplemental figure S2A) and co-cultured WT Daudi (DaudiCtrl) and DaudiTROP2 with CAR.TROP2 or control T cells (Ctrl) at different E:T ratios. CAR.TROP2 effectively eradicated DaudiTROP2 cells but spared DaudiCtrl lacking TROP2 expression (online supplemental figure S2B-D). In addition, we found CAR.TROP2 progressively proliferated on co-culture with DaudiTROP2 but not with DaudiCtrl using the CTV dilution assay (online supplemental figure S2E), confirming that our TROP2 CAR molecule was indeed specific towards TROP2 molecules but not otherwise.

Next, we comprehensively evaluated the functionality of CAR.TROP2 against human TNBC cell lines. On co-culture with TROP2+ MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 cell lines, CAR.TROP2 was robustly activated, shown by the release of cytokines (TNF-α, IFN-γ and IL-2) in the supernatant (figure 2A) and the expression of activation markers CD69, CD25, CD137 and granzyme B (figure 2B, online supplemental figure S2F) while untransduced (Ctrl) T cells and unstimulated CAR.TROP2 remained quiescent. To assess the antitumor activity of TROP2-specific CAR Ts, we co-cultured four TROP2+TNBC cell lines (MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468, HCC1806, and HCC1937) with either CAR.TROP2 or Ctrl T cells for 3 days at different E:T ratios, and the residual tumor cells and T cells were quantified using flow cytometry. CAR.TROP2 was able to kill most tumor cells even at a 1:4 E:T ratio, while Ctrl T cells were functionally inert at high E:T ratio (figure 2C,D). Notably, the MDA-MB-231 cell line showed most resistance towards CAR.TROP2, possibly due to its relatively low TROP2 expression (figure 1F) and rapid tumor growth in vitro. Furthermore, the proliferation of CAR.TROP2 on co-culture with MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 cell lines were also confirmed with CTV dilution assay (figure 2E). Lastly, to mimic chronic antigen exposure CAR-Ts faced in tumor microenvironment, we designed a repetitive co-culture assay and tested CAR.TROP2’s ability to control tumor growth on repeated tumor challenges in vitro (figure 2F). Since MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-468 cell lines also express B7-H3, we added B7-H3-specific CAR-Ts (CAR.B7-H316) in this stressed experiment to compare their antitumor activity in parallel. While both CAR.TROP2 and CAR.B7-H3 effectively controlled tumor growth in the first two rounds of co-culture, CAR.TROP2 were modestly superior to CAR.B7-H3 at later rounds, both in terms of tumor control and T-cell expansion (figure 2G). Overall, these data suggest that CAR.TROP2 is specific and possesses potent antitumor activity against TROP2+ human TNBC cell lines in vitro.

TROP2-specific CAR-Ts showed robust antitumor activity in TNBC xenograft mouse model

To evaluate the antitumor effects of TROP2.CAR-Ts in vivo, we generated orthotopic xenograft models using human TNBC cell lines. Luciferase-labeled MDA-MB-231, MDA-MB-468 or HCC1937 cells were injected into the fourth pair of mammary fat pads of female NOD/SCID/γc−/− (NSG) mice. 7 days post inoculation, as tumors became palpable, mice were treated with CAR.TROP2 or untransduced Ctrl T cells via intravenous injection and tumor growth was monitored and measured weekly by IVIS imaging and caliper (figure 3A). The antitumor activity of CAR.TROP2 was apparent in all three models as early as day 14 post T-cell injection, as IVIS signals were mostly absent on treatment (figure 3B–D). Measurement by caliper at a later time point indicated that this antitumor activity could be durable up to 48 days post-infusion, as all mice survived and no relapse was observed in CAR.TROP2 treatment group for all models (figure 3E–J). Importantly, the lung is one of the most common metastatic sites of patients with TNBC and TNBC that established lung metastasis had the shortest median survival compared with other common sites such as brain and liver. Hence, we generated a lung metastatic TNBC model by intravenously infusing the MDA-MB-231 cell line into NSG mice (figure 4A). 5 days post-tumor infusion as tumor signal could be readily detected using IVIS (figure 4B–D), mice were treated with CAR.TROP2 and Ctrl T cells via intravenous injection and tumor signal were measured weekly by IVIS. We observed effective tumor control in the CAR.TROP2 group compared with Ctrl group up to 21 days post T-cell injection. However, tumor relapses were detected at later time points, mainly in lung and in peritoneal space where CAR-T infiltration is limited. To confirm early tumor control in the lungs by CAR.TROP2, we collected lung tissues from four Ctrl and three CAR.TROP2 mice and performed H&E and CD3 IHC staining to assess the presence of tumor lesion and CAR-Ts, respectively (online supplemental figure S3). Tumor lesions are histologically visible in Ctrl but not CAR.TROP2 group (online supplemental figure S3A and B). Moreover, we detect human CD3 positive cells by IHC staining in CAR.TROP2 but not in the Ctrl group (online supplemental figure S3C). These data suggest that CAR.TROP2 could infiltrate and persist at tumor site 21 days post injection but could be functionally exhausted or physically restricted to prevent relapses. Lastly, we assessed the antitumor activity of CAR.TROP2 in a TNBC PDX tumor model, which better reflects clinical characteristics of human tumors. TROP2+ patient-derived BP1935 tumor tissues were subcutaneously implanted into the right flank of NSG mice. 2–3 weeks post-tumor implantation as tumor size reached~100 mm3, mice were treated with CAR.TROP2 or Ctrl T cells intravenously (figure 4E). PDX-BP1935 retained the expression of TROP2 in vivo on engraftment in NSG mice (figure 4F). CAR.TROP2 generated from two healthy donors showed dramatic antitumor responses against PDX, completely eradicated tumor without measurable relapse or death up to 60 days post T-cell injection (figure 4G–H). In conclusion, we demonstrated the in vivo antitumor activity of CAR.TROP2 in multiple human cell lines and patient-derived tissue established xenograft models.

Preclinical safety evaluation of TROP2-specific CAR-Ts using TROP2-humanized mouse model

While TROP2 is highly expressed in TNBCs and is an attractive target for ADC and immunotherapy, previous studies showed that its expression is not restricted to tumor tissues, raising safety concerns despite limited adverse effects being reported in TROP2-ADC clinical trials. However, when being targeted by a “live drug” such as CAR-Ts, on-target, off-tumor effect could be amplified and potentially cause severe toxicity. Hence, we performed TROP2 IHC staining on human tissue array and stained slides were analyzed blindly by an independent pathologist. Major organs such as heart, brain, liver, and muscle were negative for TROP2 staining, but different degrees of TROP2 membrane expression were observed in some normal tissues (figure 5A,B). Notably, TROP2 is stained strongly/moderately positive in mammary gland and skin, while weakly in kidney, consistent with previous reports regarding the expression of TROP2 in normal tissues.

To formally evaluate the on-target, off-tumor effect and potential toxicity of our CAR.TROP2, we leveraged a commercially available TROP2 humanized mouse model where murine TROP2 gene locus was replaced with human TROP2 cDNA (B-hTROP2). In B-hTROP2, human TROP2 is expressed in skin, lung, mammary gland, kidney and other organs in B-hTROP2 mice, consistent with what was observed in normal tissue array (figure 5C). Next, we generated a murine version of the TROP2-specific CAR by swapping the endodomains of the human TROP2.CAR with murine CD28 and CD3ζ endodomains (online supplemental figure S4A). This CAR (mCAR.TROP2) will recognize human TROP2 as its human counterpart but instead activate murine T cells. mCAR.TROP2 was efficiently transduced in murine T cells and mediated effective cytotoxicity against human TROP2+ TNBC cell lines (online supplemental figure S4B and C). We then injected mCAR.TROP2 and untransduced (Ctrl) murine T cells intravenously into B-hTROP2 mice and monitored their behavioral activity, food intake and body weight changes (figure 5D). No apparent toxicity was observed during the first 4 hours, even though mCAR-Ts were injected intravenously and first accumulated in the lung that expresses TROP2 antigen. However, we observed gradual weight loss in the mCAR.TROP2 group (figure 5E) and mice appeared crouched and listless at day 8 post mCAR-Ts injection. We ended the experiment and performed H&E staining on various organs to assess tissue pathology. No evident lesions were observed in most organs, such as the kidney, skin, mammary gland, even though they are TROP2 positive (figure 5F). Other vital organs, such as the heart, liver and brain, also appeared normal. However, we observed strong inflammatory infiltrate in the lung, with large numbers of lymphocytes and plasma cells surrounding the bronchi and small blood vessels and alveolar epithelial hyperplasia (figure 5G). Furthermore, we performed FACS analysis on peripheral blood and found a reduction in CD45+ and CD19+ cells but elevated CD11b+myeloid cells in the mCAR.TROP2 group compared with Ctrl group, confirming a systemic inflammatory phenotype (figure 5H). Overall, these data show that CAR.TROP2 could cause major toxicity by inducing strong inflammation in TROP2 positive lung tissues.

“AND” logic-gated SynNotch system improves safety of TROP2.CAR-Ts

Next, we sought to incorporate safety mechanisms into TROP2 CAR to minimize its on-target, off-tumor effects in normal tissues. The “AND” logic-gated CAR design requires the expression of two tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) to fully activate T cells, and thus reduce toxicity of conventional CAR-Ts that recognize certain antigens expressed in normal tissues. SynNotch design is one of the most advanced “AND” logic-gated systems that leverages the presence of one TAA to drive the expression of CAR molecules targeting another TAA, hence restricting the CAR expression solely to tumor microenvironment. Its application in CAR-Ts had been reported in various tumor models, such as metastatic neuroblastoma and liver cancer. To design a SynNotch system that targets TNBCs, we considered B7-H3 to be a reasonable antigen to pair with TROP2 for two reasons: first, B7-H3 is expressed in >45% of the TNBC samples that is highly overlapped with TROP2; second, B7-H3 is only expressed in limited normal tissues and does not overlap with that of TROP2. Hence, we constructed a vector that encodes a SynNotch receptor that recognizes B7-H3 antigen with a cytoplasmic VP64 gene induction domain and an Inducer vector expressing TROP2.CAR under the UAS promoter (figure 5A). Truncated NGFR and mCherry were used as reporters for transduction, respectively. Primary human T cells were co-transduced with SynNotch and Inducer vectors and we achieved a double-transduction efficiency >90% (figure 5B). scFv against B7-H3 but not TROP2 could be detected on un-induced SynNotch T cells, confirming that our SynNotch design has minimal leakage (figure 5C). To test the specificity and functions of this B7-H3/TROP2 SynNotch system, we over-expressed B7-H3 and/or TROP2 in Daudi cell line, which naturally lacks expression of either antigen (DaudiB7-H3, DaudiTROP2, DaudiB7-H3/TROP2). Single and double expression of B7-H3 and/or TROP2 were confirmed by flow cytometry (online supplemental figure S5D). Then, we co-cultured these Daudi variant cell lines with Ctrl, CAR.B7-H3, CAR.TROP2 and CAR.SynNotch T cells. While CAR.B7-H3 and CAR.TROP2 eradicated Daudi cells expressing respective antigen, CAR.SynNotch was only effective against DaudiB7-H3/TROP2 but not ones expressing either single antigen (online supplemental figure S6A and B). Furthermore, the induction of TROP2-specific CAR molecule was restricted to conditions where CAR.SynNotch is co-cultured with B7-H3 expressing Daudi cells (figure 6C,D). Notably, the induction was more efficient when TROP2 antigen is also present, possibly due to the elevated promoter activity on T-cell activation. These data indicate that our B7-H3/TROP2 SynNotch system facilitates the induction of TROP2 targeting CAR molecules, exclusively on recognition of B7-H3, thus its cytotoxic functions are restricted to cells expressing both B7-H3 and TROP2 antigens. Moreover, to better model normal epithelial tissues that express TROP2 but not B7-H3, we knocked out the B7-H3 gene from MDA-MB-231 using CRISPR/Cas9 technology (figure 5E). Co-culture MDA-MB-231WT and MDA-MB-231B7-H3KO with Ctrl, CAR.B7-H3, CAR.TROP2 and CAR.SynNotch again proves the requirement of two antigens for CAR.SynNotch to exert its cytotoxic functions (online supplemental figure S6E and F). This is further confirmed by an in vivo experiment as CAR.SynNotch completely lost its antitumor activity against an orthotopic engrafted MDA-MB-231 cell line lacking the B7-H3 antigen (figure 6A–B, online supplemental figure S6G). Notably, the in vivo antitumor activity against MDA-MB-231WT cells expressing both antigens remain intact for CAR.SynNotch (figure 6C–D, online supplemental figure S6H).

To further validate the safety profile of this TROP2 CAR.SynNotch system, we developed a murine version of the human counterpart (online supplemental figure S7A-C). mCAR.SynNotch showed similar selective cytotoxicity against tumor cells expressing both TROP2 and B7-H3 antigens in vitro, to the level comparable to mCAR.TROP2 (online supplemental figure S7D and E). Next, we formally evaluated the in vivo adverse effects of mCAR.SynNotch in the same TROP2 humanized mouse model (B-hTROP2) as in figure 5. While the infusion of mCAR.TROP2 into B-hTROP2 mice again caused rapid weight loss, severe lung damage and inflammation, accompanied by alteration of peripheral blood immune cell composition and systemic production of inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and IFN-γ, mCAR.SynNotch were well tolerated, showing no weight loss or any inflammatory symptoms (figure 6F–I). Collectively, these data highlight the safety profile of our B7-H3/TROP2 SynNotch “AND” logic-gated design, which could foster the clinical translation of CAR-T therapy against TROP2 antigen in TNBCs.

Discussion

In this study, we investigated the efficacy and safety of TROP2 as a potential target in TNBC for CAR-T cell therapy. TROP2 is a surface glycoprotein that is overexpressed in the majority of TNBC tissues. We developed a TROP2-targeting CAR molecule based on a clinically validated fully human Sacituzumab and tested the in vitro and in vivo antitumor activity of CAR.TROP2. In addition, we formally investigated the potential toxicity of CAR.TROP2 in vivo and proposed an “AND” logic-gated SynNotch design to minimize its adverse effects.

Antigen expression heterogeneity in solid tumors represents a major hurdle in selecting the appropriate target antigen for CAR-T therapy. While it could be challenging to identify antigens with a broad expression spectrum, ensuring CAR-Ts can target and eradicate tumor cells with low antigen expression adds another layer of complexity to the task. Consistent with previous reports, TROP2 antigen is broadly expressed in nearly all TNBC tissues with a relatively higher expression score compared with another potential target B7-H3. TROP2 is also highly expressed in multiple TNBC cell lines but not on BT-549, MDA-MB-436 or HS-578T. Interestingly, these TROP2-negative cell lines do not express the epithelial marker EPCAM but instead express fibroblast-related genes such as FAP and POSTN according to the CCLE database, suggesting that the lack of expression could be due to their origin. Pre-conditioning is one of the solutions to antigen expression heterogeneity. Reppel et al showed that EZH2 inhibitor tazemetostat could elevate GD2 expression in lung cancer cell lines, potentiating cytotoxicity of GD2-CAR-Ts. A study showed that TROP2 expression in TNBC cells could indeed be enhanced by epigenetic modulation (eg, decitabine), sensitizing them to TROP2-ADC. Furthermore, performing genetic/pharmacological screening for enhancers of TROP2 expression could foster the development of novel pre-conditioning approaches, broadening the application of TROP2 CAR-T therapy, in patients with relatively low intratumoral TROP2 expression.

CAR-Ts targeting TROP2 antigen have been previously developed by others, but none of these studies formally evaluated the in vivo safety profile of this strategy in a setting where the cognate antigen is expressed naturally within a fully immunocompetent mouse. We and others found TROP2 being expressed in several normal tissues, especially in vital organs such as the lung, kidney and skin, suggesting potential on-target, off-tumor effects of CAR-Ts targeting TROP2 antigen. Leveraging a mouse model with humanized TROP2 gene locus on a C57BL/6 background, we monitored potential adverse effects after infusion of CAR.TROP2 over a period of 7 days. Mice gradually lost weight and showed signs of discomfort due to inflammatory infiltration in the lungs and systemic alteration of immune cell composition. Other organs, such as the kidney, skin and stomach, although expressing the target antigen, did not show any signs of histopathology, possibly due to limited CAR-T cell infiltration in the absence of an inflammatory signal. Notably, mice infused with CAR.TROP2 did not develop symptoms until day 4–5 post-treatment, suggesting that the inflammation was not caused by an acute response of CAR-T cell activation, but possibly due to a secondary response after significant accumulation of CAR-Ts in situ. While these are not entirely consistent with clinical observations in patients with TNBC treated with TROP2-ADC, showing minor adverse effects that are consistent with other ADC therapy (such as neutropenia and anemia). This could be attributed to the differential accessibility, mode of cytotoxicity and half-life of the two treatment modalities. Despite different scFv being used in this study and others, we anticipate these adverse effects to be commonly present, that require careful monitoring, especially for one TROP2 CAR-T therapy that is entering clinical trial (NCT06082557). Alternatively, local administration of CAR-T cells could be a viable option for minimizing toxicity against other TROP2-expressing tissues.

To overcome the on-target, off-tumor effect of CAR.TROP2, we used a SynNotch design that restricts CAR-T activation on recognition of two TAAs. The SynNotch receptor recognizes B7-H3 antigen and drives the expression of a fully functional TROP2 CAR molecule, allowing the eradication of TROP2 positive TNBC cells. Our B7-H3/TROP2 SynNotch design had minimal leakage before induction and showed robust antitumor activity towards tumor cell lines expressing both antigens. Notably, the observed in vivo toxicity of CAR.TROP2 in humanized TROP2 mice was completely avoided when the SynNotch system was applied. These data demonstrate the utility of AND logic-gated CAR system in overcoming the on-target, off-tumor effect and other antigen combinations in the context of TNBC shall be further explored. Targeting mammary gland-specific surface proteins by SynNotch receptor could potentially restrict the expression of TROP2 CAR molecule within tumor tissue, further improving its specificity and safety. Furthermore, other approaches, such as the utilization of co-opting signaling molecules or regional pharmacological/optogenetic control of CAR expression shall also be investigated to determine the optimal strategy to curb unwanted CAR-T cell activity outside of tumor microenvironment.

In summary, we conducted preclinical efficacy and safety evaluation of TROP2-targeting CAR-T therapy for treatment of TNBC, highlighting its clinical translation prospect and potential adverse effects due to TROP2 expression in normal tissues. We believe this study can foster future development and optimization of CAR-T therapy against TROP2 antigen in various solid tumors.
