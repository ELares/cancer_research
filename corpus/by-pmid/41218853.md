# Systemic mRNA vaccines elicit rapid immune activation in canine brain tumors.

## Abstract

Malignant glioma is considered invariably recalcitrant, thus necessitating development of new therapeutic approaches. Spontaneously arising canine glioma exhibits important molecular and pathological similarities to malignant pediatric glioma, making it an invaluable model for testing novel therapies. We evaluated safety and activity of RNA lipid particle aggregates (LPAs) designed to provoke cancer immunogenicity in 10 dogs with histologically confirmed glioma. Vaccine manufacturing was feasible in all subjects and generally well tolerated. Median survival time was 123 and 155 days in dogs receiving three (Group A) or four (Group B) vaccines, respectively; this was increased from historical reporting of subjects receiving palliative care. Responding animals had a robust cytokine response following infusions. In animals receiving non-specific RNA-LPAs, there was localization of messenger RNA (mRNA) payloads to the brain tumor microenvironment and a rapid shift in immunologic gene signatures consistent with a viral response. These data demonstrate the ability to induce rapid viremic response in the tumor microenvironment following a single peripherally administered mRNA vaccine. Since RNA-LPAs are amenable to repeat boosts, these formulations may overcome challenges of tumor immunosuppression and therapeutic access and are currently under evaluation in first-in-human trials (NCT04573140, NCT06389591).

Introduction

 Canine glioma is considered to be a uniformly fatal condition and has been shown to have many features of pediatric gliomas, making it an invaluable model to rapidly catalyze development of novel therapeutics. Canine brain tumors have faithful biology to pediatric central nervous system (CNS) malignancies and are spontaneously occurring, allowing meaningful safety and activity data to be gleaned with direct longitudinal relevance. Since drug development for orphan diseases often requires large animal testing, the canine model can serve as an important bridge to more rapidly validate promising therapies.

Challenges to glioma therapy include limited permeability of systemic therapies across the blood-brain barrier. Given these challenges, many groups have prioritized the development of oncolytic viral therapy with promising results against both adult and pediatric glioma. Since oncolytic viral therapy requires intratumoral administration, repeated boosts are challenging to dispense. To overcome these challenges, our group has developed a novel treatment approach that leverages messenger RNA (mRNA) in lipid particle aggregates (RNA-LPA) to elicit tumor-specific immunity against solid tumors with promising results in murine, large animal and first-in-human trials. mRNA serves as a pathogen-associated molecular pattern that can stimulate several pattern recognition receptors including toll-like receptors (TLR3, TLR7, TLR8) and intracellular sensors (RIG-I, MDA-5). By loading mRNA into LPAs, mRNA packaging per payload increases, thereby enhancing innate immune responses necessary for overcoming intratumoral immunosuppression. If mRNA can be designed to reprogram intratumoral immunosuppression, it can be adapted for a host of human malignancies currently resistant to immunotherapy. Herein, we provide comprehensive results of our first-in-canine glioma trial. Remarkably, we find that even a single administration of non-specific RNA-LPA is sufficient to reprogram the tumor microenvironment (TME) in a manner that resembles oncolytic viral immunotherapy. Since RNA-LPAs are amenable to repeat boosts, this novel formulation may overcome challenges of tumor immunosuppression and therapeutic access.

Materials and methods

Study design

A sequential-design, two-group study (online supplemental figure 1) for canine glioma was conducted following approval by the University of Florida Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (Protocol #201609430, 201910811) and the College of Veterinary Medicine Hospital Research Review Committee. Eligibility was restricted to pet dogs presumptively diagnosed with glioma forebrain lesions by MRI. Dogs were required to be neurologically stable with minimal neurological deficits. Other inclusion criteria included no significant systemic disease as evidenced by thoracic radiographs, complete blood counts, and serum chemistry profile. Primary outcome measures were feasibility of vaccine generation, safety, and tolerability of repeated vaccine administrations. The population consisted of eight boxers and two French bulldogs, with mean age 7.05 years (range 4–9.5 years), mean weight 28.2 kg (range 10.5–44.6 kg), and equal numbers of castrated males and spayed females. There was an even distribution of spayed female to castrated male dogs; Group B had two French bulldogs by chance (table 1). The first five dogs (Group A) received three doses of RNA-LPA (0.05–0.1 mg/kg/dose) administered intravenously 1 week apart beginning 2 weeks from a tumor biopsy (figure 1A). The second five dogs (Group B) received a non-specific RNA payload, pp65 mRNA derived from Cytomegalovirus, within 48 hours of a biopsy, as an intravenous adjuvant to bolster immune responses, followed by three vaccines 1 week apart, consisting of personalized mRNA mixed 1:1 with pp65 (figure 1B).

Tissue procurement and vaccine manufacturing

Group A subjects were hospitalized for CT-guided biopsy of the tumor until the following day. Group B subjects were sequentially enrolled (following group A subjects) and received non-specific mRNA vaccines encoding for pp65 before being hospitalized for CT-guided biopsy of the tumor the following day. All patients were placed under general anesthesia following a protocol determined by the board-certified attending veterinary anesthesiologist. A CT (Toshiba Aquilion) of the head was performed, and the tumor was identified. A minimal surgical approach to the skull was performed. A burr hole was made over the tumor using a pneumatic drill. The dura was incised using an #11 blade. A Nashold biopsy needle was advanced to an individualized predetermined depth, and the patient was again scanned to ensure accurate placement of the needle. Multiple samples were collected with subsequent saline lavage of the surgical site. A section of GelFoam (Ethicon Johnson & Johnson, New Brunswick, New Jersey, USA) was placed over the craniectomy defect. The incision was closed, and the patient was scanned again to confirm a lack of hemorrhage at the biopsy site. The patient was hospitalized in the intensive care unit overnight and discharged the following day. All dogs were diagnosed with glioma based on histopathology of surgical biopsy. Six dogs were diagnosed with glioma without further characterization. Two dogs received further characterization of the lesion as astrocytoma, and two as oligodendroglioma. Following tumor biopsy, without resection, each glioma’s total RNA was extracted, and made into a complementary DNA library by reverse transcription-polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR) from which mRNA was amplified and loaded into RNA-LPA (figure 1C), using methods previously described. Briefly, mRNA was used to cluster positively charged lipid particles made following rotary vacuum evaporation of organic solvents before aqueous reformulation, heating, sonication and extrusion. Multilamellar particles formed following undisturbed culture in the presence of mRNA at 15:1 (lipid:mRNA) ratios. Vaccines were administered at a constant-rate infusion initially over 5 min, and after the second Group A dog, over 30 min and continuously monitored. Animals were monitored closely for 6 hours after completion of the vaccine. Adverse reactions to vaccine administration were classified according to the Veterinary Cooperative Oncology Group-Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (VCOG-CTCAE) V.2.

Tissue sample analysis

For three dogs in each group, tissue samples were obtained from the biopsies. Damaged tissue was removed to ensure the resulting RNA was from healthy tumor tissue. Tissue was finely chopped under low-temperature conditions to reduce RNA breakdown and stored in RNAlater (Thermo Fisher Scientific, catalog no. AM7020) at 4°C overnight to maintain RNA integrity. The following day, the RNAlater solution was discarded, and the tissues were sectioned and kept at −80°C until RNA isolation. For each sample, around 50 mg of tissue was readied for RNA processing. RNA extraction was done using the TRIzol Reagent (Invitrogen, catalog no. 15596026) as per the provided instructions. The volume of reagents was adapted based on tissue weight as suggested by the manufacturer to achieve the best RNA quality and yield. The isolated RNA underwent further cleaning and purification with the PureLink RNA Mini Kit (Invitrogen, catalog no. 12183018A).

RNA concentration and quality were assessed with a NanoDrop OneC (Thermo Fisher Scientific), with RNA amounts standardized and prepared for NanoString’s protocol processing. To analyze the expression of about 800 genes linked to immune responses and cancer pathways in canines, the nCounter Canine IO Panel (NanoString, catalog no. 115000465) was used. Following the manufacturer’s guidance, samples were tagged and hybridized to produce gene expression data. The derived data were normalized with the manufacturer’s chosen reference housekeeping genes through the nSolver Analysis Software, for consistency across samples.

To examine the reliability of our sample values, we conducted gene expression analysis for each subject two times using separate tissue sections, creating two technical replicates for each subject; however, one replicate in Group B failed quality control and was discarded. We calculated mean RNA expression values for each subject and used the resulting values for all subsequent analysis.

Differential gene expression and analysis

Principal component analysis (PCA) was performed to test for differences between sample replicates and between groups using iDEP.96, (https://bioinformatics.sdstate.edu/idep96/). For detailed comparison across groups, gene expression read counts were imported into the iDEP web application and pre-processed. We used DESeq2 in iDEP for differential gene expression analysis. Throughout this work, we define differentially expressed genes (DEGs) as those with fold change >2 and Benjamini-Hochberg adjusted p values<0.05. We ranked these using standard deviation across all samples, establishing a hierarchy of clusters based on gene expression patterns. Default settings in iDEP were used for generating the heatmap with these results, which included using “correlation” distance metric and “average” linkage method. A z-score cut-off of 2 was used to highlight data with significant deviations from the mean.

After identifying DEGs, we performed Gene Ontology (GO) analysis (iDEP.96 with adjusted p values<0.05 considered statistically significant), to identify biological processes, cellular components, and molecular functions enriched in DEGs. We used IPA software (V.23.0; Qiagen, USA) with expression analysis and log ratio settings, to analyze functional enrichment of target genes and their role in canonical pathways, molecular networks, and disease pathways.

Immune cell signatures

Immune cell deconvolution was conducted through the original CIBERSORT method contained in the CIBERSORTx deconvolution algorithm. B-mode batch correction was used and the LM22 signature was applied as the immune reference. CIBERSORTx was run using both relative fraction and absolute fraction output modes with quantile normalization disabled. Under these conditions, deconvolution was approximated with 1,000 permutations. We tested for differences in absolute immune scores, which include all 22 immune cell types in LM22.

Results

Primary outcome measures

Feasibility

Vaccines were successfully generated on all canine subjects confirming feasibility of this approach for large scale manufacturing with rapid turnaround time (~2 weeks) for personalized therapy following biopsy.

Safety

The initial presenting complaint for all dogs was seizure activity. The mean time from initial seizure onset to MRI diagnosis was 26.7 days (range 0–98 days). All enrolled dogs were already receiving at least one anti-epileptic drug. Five dogs were receiving phenobarbital, four dogs were receiving regular release levetiracetam, three dogs were receiving extended-release levetiracetam, and two dogs were receiving a combination of phenobarbital and extended-release levetiracetam. One dog on combination therapy was also receiving zonisamide. Seven of the dogs were receiving prednisone. The mean prednisone dose administered was 0.54 mg/kg per day (range 0.17–0.85 mg/kg).

Dog 1 developed salivation and smacking of the lips with mRNA infusion, which was assumed to be a manifestation of nausea, considered a Grade 1–2 adverse event (AE). Anti-nausea medicine, Maropitant (Cerenia, Zoetis, Kalamazoo, Michigan, USA) 1 mg/kg subcutaneously, was added to the protocol as a vaccine premedication. One AE was characterized by bradycardia as noted during vaccination in one dog from Group A; this was considered a Grade 2 AE. To mitigate future reactions, RNA-LPA infusion time was increased; however, after a another reaction of bradycardia following the second vaccine, this dog did not continue receiving vaccines and subsequently found to have an arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy on necropsy. No other dog had an immediate or delayed cardiovascular reaction during vaccination. Maintenance intravenous fluids (LRS, Dechra Veterinary Products, Overland Park, Kansas, USA) at 2 mL/kg/hour for 2 hours post-infusion was added to the protocol post-vaccine after Dog 2.

One dog in Group A had a seizure associated with the first and third vaccinations; this was considered a Grade 2 AE; however, the seizure activity in this dog had been poorly controlled prior to drug delivery and anticonvulsant medications had to be adjusted throughout his time in the study protocol. One dog in Group B received four doses of lomustine (7–21 mg) prior to enrollment, with the most recent dose 1-month prior to presentation. This dog developed transient thrombocytopenia after administration of all mRNA vaccines, with a nadir observed 6 hours after vaccine, and resolution by 24 hours. This thrombocytopenia was considered a Grade 2 and 3 AE. No other dog in the study had received any adjunctive antitumor therapy prior to inclusion. One dog had a seizure likely due to progressive disease and developed aspiration pneumonia, which precipitated multiorgan failure, and euthanasia was elected.

9 of 10 dogs received all three vaccines. One dog (long-term survivor) was lost to follow-up; no other dogs were alive at the time of writing. Two other dogs spontaneously died at home with no noted precipitating event; the remaining seven dogs were euthanized due to progressive neurologic deficits. 3 of the 10 dogs underwent necropsy. The diagnosis of glioma was confirmed on necropsy in those dogs. Aside from the one described above with breed-associated arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy, no other significant abnormalities were noted in any dog.

Early drops in white blood cell counts and platelets rapidly recovered and several canines had an elevated alkaline phosphatase value prior to the first vaccine (online supplemental figure 2), but this would not be considered unexpected for a dog chronically receiving glucocorticoids and/or phenobarbital. These are considered reversible, time-dependent and dose-dependent, drug-induced elevations and are not indicative of hepatopathy.

Antitumor activity

Although there was no significant difference in survival between Group A and B (figure 1D), the initial mean tumor volume was statistically greater for Group B (figure 1E). Median survival time from diagnostic MRI to death was 123 days in Group A and 155 days in Group B, significantly greater than historical survivorship of 26 days with palliation alone for symptom management of seizures and peritumoral edema and 84 days with definitive treatment that could include surgery, radiation or chemotherapy.

Secondary outcome measures

RNA gene expression and cytokine response

To discern mRNA localization to the brain TME, we performed PCR for pp65 in Group B canines treated with a single vaccine prior to tumor biopsy. In four out of five subjects, we detected pp65 expression based on detection of bands within the size range expected for pp65 (figure 2A). We then analyzed the periphery in Group B patients for cytokine response signatures. In the two most responsive subjects (median survival >150 days) there were increases detected in inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin-2, tumor necrosis factor-α, and interferon (IFN)-γ; however, in non-responders, we did not observe increases in inflammatory cytokines with subsequent vaccination cycles (figure 2B and C).

Differential gene expression and analysis

We found notable differences in gene expression levels between Groups A and B for the tissue samples analyzed (analysis schema in figure 3A). The PCA plot of DEGs in figure 3B shows separation between groups, and concordance between technical replicates. Differential expression analysis identified 38 DEGs that were upregulated and 6 DEGs that were downregulated (heat map in online supplemental fig. 3A, list in online supplemental table 1). We subsequently analyzed the pathways involved in the DEGs between Groups A and B and calculated p values (online supplemental fig. 3B). The network tree of these enriched pathways (online supplemental figure 3C) includes hierarchical cluster analysis results. Gene sets closer in the tree share more genes, and larger dots correspond to smaller adjusted p values. Enrichment analysis of DEGs identified enriched GO biological process terms (online supplemental figure 3D). Nodes represent biological processes, and edges connect processes sharing at least 30% genes, revealing potential interactions and functional relationships between them. Key nodes include response to virus, innate immune response, biological process involved in interspecies interaction between organisms, immune system process, response to external biotic stimulus, defense response to virus, and response to other organism. These processes were all highly significant, demonstrating strong immune system upregulation in response to the non-specific mRNA vaccine. Additionally, type I IFN signaling pathway and cellular response to type I IFN were each upregulated. Online supplemental table 2 lists the DEGs in the top five GO terms upregulated or downregulated (with adjusted p values) in biological processes (2A), cellular components (2B), and molecular functions (2C). The top five significant upregulated GO cellular components were major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I peptide loading complex, proteasome core complex, MHC protein complex, cell surface, and endopeptidase complex; the top five upregulated GO molecular functions were threonine-type endopeptidase activity, TAP binding, TAP1 binding, TAP2 binding, and threonine-type peptidase activity. These results suggest signal initiation to activate CD8+T cells.

Pathway analysis with IPA enabled us to predict potential upregulation or downregulation of downstream biological activities and functions, shedding light on contributions of DEGs (online supplemental figure 4). Mechanistic network analysis with IPA revealed DEGs in mechanistic networks in glioma, including RIG-I, TREX1, STAT1, and STAT2 (online supplemental figure 5). Since genes implicated in STAT1/2 activation were significantly altered following RNA-LPAs, we theorized that non-specific RNA-LPAs elicit intratumoral IFN responses, which correlate with significant shifts in immune gene signatures in the glioma TME. Together, these data suggest rapid remodeling of the TME following an intratumoral damage response mediated by IFN-response signaling, resulting in significant mobilization of immune cells.

Immune cell signatures

Given the apparent increase in expression of key immune signaling molecules in pp65-treated tumors, we tested the prevalence of immune cell infiltration in tumors using CIBERSORTx immune cell deconvolution. For a subset of nine immune cells of interest, we compared scores at the sample level (figure 3C). Group B exhibited increased absolute immune scores compared with Group A (figure 3D).

Discussion

Herein, we report results of our primary (feasibility, safety, efficacy) and secondary objectives (tumor sample gene signatures before and after vaccine treatment) in canines with glioma. Although the number of subjects was small, there was a remarkable increase in median survivorship (compared with historical cohorts of subjects receiving definitive therapy). Repeated administration of RNA-LPAs was feasible to generate in all patients and largely well tolerated, and appears to have a positive effect on survival of canines with glioma.

Non-specific vaccines with pp65 were used in this study to prime immunity for recall responses to tumor-specific vaccines that were “spiked” with pp65. We found that many immune-related genes were significantly upregulated while others were downregulated. While this might suggest a “mixed response”, these results are likely more consistent with a negative feedback loop following intratumoral immune mobilization and IFN response activity. Interestingly, many animals developed an initial increase in tumor size (table 1) despite stable symptoms and enhanced survivorship (compared with historical controls), suggesting pseudoprogression. Although well described in immunotherapy for human glioma, pseudoprogression is poorly characterized in canine glioma. Future studies should consider the incorporation of diffusion and perfusion-weighted MRI to refine assessments.

There were several limitations which include: (1) inability to perform serial biopsies in each canine subject (due to reluctance of pet owners); (2) small sample size of nanostring analyzed post-treatment samples (n=3/group), which could introduce a study bias; (3) and a lack of longitudinal functional analysis on canine peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) to discern magnitude and persistence of antigen-specific immune response. AEs (seizures, bradycardia), while more likely attributable to other etiologies, could possibly be induced or exacerbated following RNA-LPAs and small sample size may not discern whether the AEs herein are definitively related. Overall, this study demonstrates feasibility, safety and activity of a systemic mRNA vaccine in canines, which provided the foundation for Food and Drug Administration Investigational New Drug (FDA-IND) application for multiple human glioblastoma studies (NCT04573140, NCT06389591). Our results highlight the ability to induce rapid activation of immune responses in the TME, potentially overcoming challenges of local oncolytic delivery and systemic challenges from translocation across the blood-brain barrier.
