## Full Text

Exploiting Replication Gaps for Cancer Therapy

Defects in DNA double strand break repair are thought to render BRCA1 or BRCA2 (BRCA) mutant tumors selectively sensitive to PARP inhibitors (PARPi). Challenging this framework, BRCA and PARP1 share functions in DNA synthesis on the lagging strand. Thus, BRCA-deficiency or “BRCAness” could reflect an inherent lagging strand problem that is vulnerable to drugs such as PARPi that also target the lagging strand, a combination that generates a toxic accumulation of replication gaps.

Overview

Features that distinguish cancer from non-cancer cells enable selective targeting. As compared to normal cells, tumors with mutations in the hereditary breast/ovarian cancer genes, BRCA1 or BRCA2 (BRCA) are selectively sensitive to PARP inhibitors (PARPi). Conventionally, PARPi sensitivity is attributed to BRCA-deficient cells being unable to prevent and repair DNA double strand breaks (DSBs). This framework is based on the well-characterized role of the BRCA proteins in repairing DSBs by homologous recombination (HR). Moreover, BRCA proteins are expected to limit the formation of DSBs by promoting replication fork protection (FP). Accordingly, HR and FP defects or “BRCAness” guides the clinical use of PARPi. Here we propose a distinct model in which PARPi toxicity stems from DNA replication associated single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) gaps. In support of this model, the parameters of HR and FP deficiency fail to fully explain tumor response. Moreover, gaps, unlike DSBs, form in the immediate aftermath of PARPi treatment, are exacerbated in BRCA-deficient cells, and are suppressed upon PARPi resistance. We further propose that in addition to loss of BRCA and PARP1, other toxic interactions could derive from lagging strand synthesis problems, fork repriming, and/or other events that expand gap formation. To leverage these insights for precision medicine, rather than an HR-deficiency (HRD) score, a more precise and clinically significant measure will be a “gap” score. Ideally, the gap model of PARPi toxicity will broaden the range of treatable tumors and provide opportunities to develop robust drug combinations that maximize gaps and limit resistance.

PARP and BRCA loss: a synthetic lethal interaction

Genotoxic chemotherapy that exploits cancer cell defects in DNA repair has been the cornerstone of targeted therapy and applied clinically to tumors with mutations in the hereditary breast and ovarian cancer genes, BRCA1 or BRCA2 (BRCA). The promise of this strategy was revealed by the extreme sensitivity of BRCA mutant cancers upon inhibition of poly(ADP-ribose) (PAR) polymerase (PARP). PARP1, the major protein in PARP family, binds damaged DNA activating its poly(ADP-ribosyl)ation (PARylation) activity of itself and other proteins to facilitate DNA repair. PARP inhibitors (PARPi) can “trap” PARP1 on damaged DNA and/or inactivate its PARylation activity, thereby generating a protein/DNA complex that can either block DNA replication and/or interfere with DNA damage repair. PARPi toxicity in BRCA-deficient cells is often referred to as a synthetic lethal interaction, a situation in which concurrent loss of two genes is cytotoxic but their single loss is tolerated. While cell killing due to loss of both PARP1 and BRCA genes is consistent with a synthetic lethal interaction, this designation falls short when toxicity instead derives from PARP trapping given that this is not loss of a gene. Nevertheless, this so called synthetic lethal interaction has propelled a revolution in cancer therapy.

The sensitizing lesions following PARPi is generally thought to be DNA double strand breaks (DSBs). Logically, DSBs necessitate BRCA function in DSB repair by homologous recombination (HR). Moreover, given that PARPi is thought to trap PARP on DNA and/or block DNA replication, this further necessitates BRCA function in fork protection (FP) that limits the degradation and collapse of stalled replication forks into DSBs. Thus, PARPi-induced DSBs and fork collapse are anticipated to be lethal in BRCA-deficient cells. To identify and predict tumors that will be sensitive to PARPi, much effort has been applied to scoring HRD tumors by mutational signatures, protein expression, and/or genome scaring events. The emergence of chemotherapy resistance in BRCA-deficient cancer cells also often correlated with restoration of HR and/or FP, furthering the model that a DSB is the sensitizing lesion.

Here, we diverge and propose instead that the common vulnerability of cancer cells sensitive to PARPi is aberrant single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) gap formation occurring in the wake of DNA replication (also called daughter-strand gaps). This premise is based on the finding that defects in gap suppression, as opposed to DSB repair of FP, more accurately align with therapy sensitivity and patient response in BRCA mutant cancer. Replication gaps as opposed to DSBs also align more with PARPi response. Accordingly, limiting replication gaps is a function enacted by the BRCA-RAD51 pathway that is distinct from its roles in HR and FP. The source of gaps has been linked to defects in both lagging strand synthesis and repriming reactions occurring on the leading strand. We further predict that tumors with mutations in BRCA and other genes that share the phenotype of BRCAness are vulnerable to gap inducing genotoxic therapies such as PARPi. Lastly, we expect that gaps are relevant to a range of synthetic lethal or toxic interactions.

Rethinking synthetic lethality between PARPi and BRCA loss

The analysis of DNA replication-associated ssDNA gaps in the immediate aftermath of PARPi treatment revealed fundamental differences between sensitive and resistant lines. In resistant BRCA-proficient control cells, gaps were restricted, whereas in sensitive BRCA-deficient cells, gaps were enhanced. Moreover, gaps were suppressed in a series of genetic and acquired models in which PARPi resistance was thought to derive from restored HR and/or FP. Gaps were also suppressed in cancer cell lines and patient derived tumors with de novo PARPi resistance in which the mechanism was undefined. More strikingly, PARPi resistance was observed in cells deficient in HR and FP. In particular, cells null for the BRCA1 associated helicase, FANCJ displayed PARPi insensitivity despite defects in HR and FP. Another important observation is that PARPi induced gaps were avoided in the FANCJ null cells. By comparison, gaps were elevated in Fanconi anemia (FA) patient cells that maintain a single allele mutation in the recombination gene RAD51. The FA cells were PARPi sensitive despite proficiency in HR and the additional restoration FP. Thus, PARPi response can be uncoupled from HR and FP, a finding implicating gaps alone could be fundamental to therapy response. Gaps were also rapidly induced (minutes to hours) whereas DSBs formed later (hours to days). This latency could reflect that unrepaired ssDNA breaks or PARP-trapped complexes eventually block DNA replication to induce DSBs. Inconsistent with a fork arrest model, however, PARPi-treated cells display replication tracts that appear longer. Notably, these “lengthened” tracts are sensitive to S1 nuclease cutting implicating discontinuous DNA replication. While these gaps could seed DSBs in a subsequent S-phase, there are other potential sources for DSB formation. PARPi also induces apoptosis, a cell death process involving enzymes that cut DNA. Inhibition of apoptosis reduced DSBs, indicating that breaks derive in large part from apoptosis. Gaps are also not necessarily precursors to stalled/collapsed forks. Gaps appear as separate entities and degrade/extend under distinct conditions. Taken as a whole, the relevance of DSBs remains unclear whereas gaps are consistently linked with PARPi response (Figure 1).

What is the source of gaps?

When canonical Okazaki fragment processing (OFP) is incomplete and ssDNA gaps remain in the lagging strand, PARP1 is activated. PARP1’s auto-PARylation recruits XRCC1, a scaffold protein that interacts with Ligase 3 (LIG3) and DNA polymerase β (POLβ) to process and fill gaps in a mechanism termed backup OFP that is fundamental to the completion of lagging strand synthesis in unperturbed cells Unexpectantly, this PARP1-backup OFP mechanism requires the BRCA-RAD51 pathway. In BRCA1-deficient cells, lagging strand dependent PARP1 activation is abnormally elevated. However, the hyperactivated PARP1 fails to effectively recruit XRCC1 and LIG3. Consistent with a defect in the backup pathway, BRCA1-deficient cells demonstrate a greater reliance on canonical lagging strand synthesis. They have a heightened sensitivity to loss of the canonical flap nuclease FEN1 that generates ligatable nicks. Given that PARPi also hinders the maturation of nascent DNA strands during DNA replication, these findings implicate that lagging strand synthesis is a vulnerability in BRCA-deficient cells underlying the cytotoxicity of PARPi.

Intriguingly, backup OFP improves substantially in BRCA-RAD51-deficient cells that gain PARPi resistance. Along with gap suppression, the aberrant S-phase PARylation is significantly reduced. Even in the well-characterized genetic model in which BRCA1-deficient cells gain PARPi resistance by loss of the DNA repair protein, 53BP1, gaps and PAR are dramatically suppressed. Consistent with this rescue occurring via restored OFP, the chromatin association of XRCC1 and LIG3 are also re-established at levels above those in control cells. Moreover, sensitivity to FEN1 loss is mitigated indicating reduced reliance on canonical OFP. Yet, a new vulnerability arises. In this dual BRCA1 and 53BP1 deficiency background, the top target from a genetic screen to revert the PARPi resistance is LIG3. Remarkably, this screen did not predominantly identify HR genes which one would expect if loss of 53BP1 conferred PARPi resistance via HR. Other LIG3 functions (i.e., base excision repair) cannot be ruled out. However, the relevance of backup OFP is supported. BRCA1 and 53BP1 dual-deficient cells have enhanced PAR upon depletion of LIG3 as compared to depletion of FEN1 or LIG1, the major enzyme joining Okazaki fragments, findings that are reversed in control cells. Thus, BRCA1 and 53BP1 dual-deficient cells appear to require LIG3 not only for viability but also for normal S phase PAR levels. These new insights implicate that an acquired vulnerability in cells dually deficient in BRCA1 and 53BP1 includes backup OFP that also could be the target of drugs re-sensitizing to PARPi.

A source of gaps in BRCA-deficient cells is also linked to repriming reactions on the leading strand by the primase/polymerase PRIMPOL (Figure 2A). PRIMPOL enables the repriming of replication past obstacles when fork reversal is compromised. For example, repriming is linked to PRIMPOL during PARPi treatment. Without PARylated PARP1, fork reversal is counteracted by the RECQ1 helicase. Similarly, loss of the fork remodeler, HLTF reduces fork reversal and gaps form in a manner dependent on PRIMPOL as well as FANCJ. Based on these findings, it appears that the lack of fork reversal propels repriming of the forward fork. Counterintuitively, a process known as fork traverse that enables replication past crosslinks not only engages PRIMPOL but also requires fork reversal. Perhaps to engage repriming, fork reversal is required even if transient as might occur with PARPi or loss of HLTF.

Another interesting possibility is that defects in OFP are coupled to repriming reactions. OFP defects could confer topological stress that triggers fork reversal but in turn limits end processing required for restart, necessitating fork repriming for replication to continue (Figure 2B). Indeed, defects in OFP through loss of FEN1 or RNase H2 in a yeast model impairs nascent strand resection at reversed forks. Conceivably, a reversed fork could be rescued by repriming on the leading strand via PRIMPOL. A rapid interchange between OFP defects, reversal, and repriming in a hyper-dynamic fork could reveal only gaps in the wake of DNA replication. In this context, it is also interesting that gap suppression by 53BP1 loss in BRCA1-deficient cells is associated with not only restored backup OFP but also template switch (TS), a gap filling mechanism that can operate at reversed forks. TS can also initiate from a gap in post-replication and possibly require RAD51-dependent recombination also restored by 53BP1 loss. Collectively, restored backup OFP and/or TS could eliminate gaps on both the leading and lagging strands.

Alternatively, defects in OFP could indirectly cause repriming. Lagging strand gaps could be coupled to disrupted chromatin assembly and cohesion establishment and/or sequestered histone chaperones behind replication forks and therefore derail the processing and restart of upstream stalled forks to indirectly trigger repriming reactions (Figure 2C). In either scenario, PARPi in BRCA-deficient cells could generate both leading and lagging strand gaps. This combination could explain why PARPi synthetic lethal interactions share the phenotype of “lengthened” discontinuous replication (Figure 2). Linking lagging strand defects to leading strand repriming unifies the seemingly distinct sources of gaps in BRCA-deficient cells. However, as described above, repriming induced gaps could develop on either strand and be independent of OFP defects.

The toxicity of gaps following PARPi or other agents are likely to be more profound in BRCA-deficient cells. For one, repriming is countered by BRCA2 and its interaction with MCM10, a component of the replisome. Limiting gap formation by repriming or other means could be indirect as both BRCA proteins are critical for restraining replication during stress. Moreover, BRCA proteins limit the extension of gaps by MRE11 or other nucleases and engage in post-replication gap repair mechanisms along with PARP1. Consequently, combined PARPi and BRCA deficiency would amplify the post-replicative gap burden. Indeed, BRCA2 is predicted to limit gaps by promoting a RAD51-Polymerase α interaction that could activate the polymerase to ensure efficient lagging strand synthesis. Collectively, BRCA proteins are emerging to have multiple routes to limit gaps via functions in OFP, restraining replication and PRIMPOL activity, along with post-replication repair/recombination gap filling roles. Thus, while some gaps could provide an adaptive response to chemotherapy, therapy-induced gaps could readily pass a toxicity threshold in BRCA-deficient cells. This could explain why targeting PRIMPOL can elevate resistance in BRCA-deficient cells. However, the individual gap suppression mechanism compromised in BRCA1 vs BRCA2 genetic backgrounds is likely distinct. Gaps are suppressed in BRCA2-deficient cells by loss of CHD4, whereas gaps are suppressed in BRCA1-deficient cells by loss of 53BP1. Understanding the distinct roles of the BRCA proteins in gap suppression and how circumvented in resistant tumors will be critical future directions.

Gaps, a universal mediator of PARPi synthetic lethal interactions?

In any model in which PARPi is synthetic lethal, it is worth considering that the mechanism involves combined lagging strand dysfunction and toxic gap induction. Not only does PARPi sensitize cells deficient, mutated, or disrupted in canonical OFP factors including EXO1, DNA2, LIG1, or PCNA, but also cells deficient in backup OFP factors including XRCC1, LIG3, POLβ. Moreover, PARPi is sensitizing to cells with loss of ribonuclease H2 (RNase H2), an enzyme that has the capacity to remove RNA primers that are generated during lagging strand synthesis, a process critical for Okazaki fragment maturation. Furthermore, PARPi is synthetic lethal with loss of the histone PARylation factor 1 (HPF1) that modulates PARP1 catalytic activity and promotes backup OFP. It is also noteworthy, that LIG3, POLβ, and HPF1 were identified as targets to augment PARPi sensitivity. Restoration of PARPi sensitivity could also involve reestablishing gaps. Guanine-quadruplex (G4s) ligands restore PARPi sensitivity in cells dually deficient in BRCA1 and 53BP1 and interestingly G4s have the propensity to form on lagging strands. Conceivably, G4 ligands may derail backup OFP and induce gap formation.

Lastly, it is foreseeable that gaps provide a range of additional synthetic lethal interactions (Figure 3). For example, combined lagging strand dysfunction could cause synthetic lethality between loss of BRCA2 or XRCC1 and LIG1. It is also reasonable to consider a similar scenario in the synthetic lethal interaction between loss of BRCA and RAD52 given that RAD52 is predicted to function in lagging strand synthesis. Gaps could also be operational in the synthetic lethal interaction between BRCA and CIP2A, a protein that surveys genome integrity . To consider if gaps contribute to cell killing in any of these models, it will be vital to determine if cell killing is augmented by enhancing or exposing gaps. Indeed, in BRCA-deficient cells, there is a heightened requirement for ssDNA binding protein, RPA with its loss synergizing with PARPi. Moreover, there is greater reliance on translesion synthesis (TLS), a gap filling mechanism. In BRCA-deficient cells, up-regulated TLS provides a route to chemoresistance and mutagenesis, and its targeting re-sensitizes chemotherapy-resistant cells. Foreseeably, gap prevention by TLS “on the fly” during replication is critical for chemoresistance whereas TLS gap filling in post-replication confers basic cell fitness. This distinction could explain why TLS inhibition reduces the viability of BRCA-deficient cells whereas TLS hyper-activation is associated with chemoresistance. In summary, cells with a gap problem could be vulnerable to loss of mechanisms that prevent gaps in S phase, fill gaps in post-replication, or survey genome integrity prior to cell division (Figure 3).

Minding the gap for clinical design and treatment

Given the clinical promise of PARPi, augmenting response and preventing resistance are key goals. Based on the conventional dogma, PARPi efficacy would be expected to be improved when combined with drugs disrupting modes of resistance such as HR and FP. Accordingly, the destabilization of replication forks has been proposed as the mechanism for re-sensitization to PARPi by inhibitors of ATR (ATRi) or WEE1(WEE1i). It is notable, however that ATRi or WEE1i are less effective when cancer cells engage in TLS. What’s more, TLS inhibitors restore gaps and synergize with ATRi or WEE1i to kill cancer cells, consistent with gaps conferring sensitization. Furthermore, the anti-cancer barrier that limits cell fitness that is induced by expression of the oncogene cyclin E also elevates gap induction. Importantly, TLS counters these gaps and improves cell fitness. The finding that many cancer cells routinely engage TLS suggests that cancer evolution requires TLS and its targeting could selectively impact cancer cells without harming non-cancer cells.

The number of converging cellular functions that overlap with lagging strand synthesis confounds the understanding as to whether this is a key vulnerability in cancer. Moreover, it remains unclear how gaps from any source could propel cell death. However, a framework that implicates gaps in PARPi sensitivity provides a rationale for why PARPi has clinical benefit regardless of HR status. If “BRCAness” relates to a gap vulnerability as opposed to a defect in HR and FP, the potential of PARPi could be expanded to a range of vulnerable tumors not currently considered based on the DSB dogma. Moreover, it would provide a blueprint for more accurate biomarkers predicting PARPi response. Rather than DSBs or HRD scores, gaps and/or deficiency in lagging strand synthesis factors would be assessed. Furthermore, instead of drug combinations seeking to maximize DSBs, effective and durable therapies will boost gap formation. These drugs could elevate the gap threshold not only by inhibiting OFP, but also by targeting gap repair mechanisms. Alternatively, unresolved OFP gaps could be harnessed for cell killing by targeting responses to under-replicated DNA that check cell division and enable gap repair. Counteracting lagging strand synthesis mechanisms employed by cancer cells could limit not only viability but also mutagenesis that propels cancer evolution. Overall, rethinking and retesting the basis for synthetic lethality will be informative and potentially lead to finding more selective cancer therapies.
