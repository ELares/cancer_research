# Expanding the horizons of cancer therapy with next-generation 4-1BB agonists: a review of molecular and clinical strategies to maximize efficacy and ensure safety.

## Abstract

Driven by the substantial limitations of first generation 4-1BB agonists urelumab and utomilumab, the field has shifted toward engineering next-generation molecules with improved therapeutic windows. This review provides a comprehensive analysis of this evolution, detailing how key molecular design strategies are used to restrict 4-1BB activation to the tumor microenvironment. We summarize available clinical data, highlighting that 4-1BB bispecific antibodies exhibit superior antitumor efficacy and more favorable safety profiles compared with their monospecific predecessors. Furthermore, we discuss strong rationale for combination strategies, emphasizing how 4-1BB signaling provides the crucial costimulatory signal necessary to sustain durable anti-tumor responses. In summary, this review elucidates the scientific basis of antibody engineering aimed at improving safety and tumor-selective activation of 4-1BB agonists and outlines future directions for optimizing their clinical application in cancer immunotherapy.

Expanding the horizons of cancer therapy with next-generation 4-1BB agonists: a review of molecular and clinical strategies to maximize efficacy and ensure safety

ABSTRACT

Driven by the substantial limitations of first generation 4–1BB agonists urelumab and utomilumab, the field has shifted toward engineering next-generation molecules with improved therapeutic windows. This review provides a comprehensive analysis of this evolution, detailing how key molecular design strategies are used to restrict 4–1BB activation to the tumor microenvironment. We summarize available clinical data, highlighting that 4–1BB bispecific antibodies exhibit superior antitumor efficacy and more favorable safety profiles compared with their monospecific predecessors. Furthermore, we discuss strong rationale for combination strategies, emphasizing how 4–1BB signaling provides the crucial costimulatory signal necessary to sustain durable anti-tumor responses. In summary, this review elucidates the scientific basis of antibody engineering aimed at improving safety and tumor-selective activation of 4–1BB agonists and outlines future directions for optimizing their clinical application in cancer immunotherapy.

Introduction

4-1BB (CD137) is a crucial costimulatory receptor belonging to the tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily. It is primarily expressed on antigen-primed, activated T cells such as cytotoxic CD8+ and helper CD4+ T cells, but also present on a variety of non-T cells, including B cells, natural killer cells, monocytes, and activated dendritic cells (Figure 1). Its expression is rapidly upregulated upon immune cell activation, making it a key mediator of effective anti-tumor responses. When engaged by its natural ligand, 4–1BB ligand (4-1BBL), or agonistic antibodies, 4–1BB delivers a vital “second signal” that synergizes with the initial T cell receptor signaling. In contrast to other costimulatory molecules, most notably CD28, which is instrumental in initial priming and rapid expansion, 4–1BB is distinguished by its specialized role in sustaining T cell effector functions and survival beyond the transient activation phase. This costimulatory signal prevents T cell anergy and enhances T cell survival, proliferation, clonal expansion, and memory differentiation, driving robust effector functions, such as the production of cytokines (e.g., interferon-γ) and cytolytic molecules (e.g., perforin, granzymes). 

In the context of cancer immunotherapy, 4–1BB signaling is particularly important for preventing T cell exhaustion, a dysfunctional state arising from chronic antigen exposure, within the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). By counteracting these inhibitory signals, 4–1BB helps maintain T cell function and promotes the development of long-lived memory T cells, which are essential for durable anti-tumor immunity. The first generation 4–1BB agonists urelumab (BMS-663513) and utomilumab (PF-05082566) showed initial promise, but had adverse safety profiles (e.g., fatal hepatotoxicity) in clinical studies due to widespread immune activation by urelumab and insufficient therapeutic benefit by utomilumab. These limitations have driven the development of next-generation 4–1BB agonists engineered for enhanced safety and efficacy. These innovative molecules are designed to selectively activate 4–1BB within the TME by modulating 4–1BB and/or Fc gamma receptor (FcγR) binding, or by simultaneously binding to a tumor-associated antigen (TAA) and 4–1BB, thereby maximizing anti-tumor activity while minimizing systemic immune-related adverse events. This strategy highlights the potential of 4–1BB as a key component of modern cancer immunotherapy, particularly in the context of combination strategies, as evidenced by a broad range of therapeutic candidates under investigation.

This review discusses the molecular characteristics and available clinical data for both monospecific and bispecific 4–1BB agonist antibodies, as well as their combination strategies, with a focus on actively progressing clinical candidates. The information in this review was collected from the Clarivate Cortellis Drug Discovery Intelligence™ platform (www.cortellis.com/drugdiscovery/home), publicly available abstracts/posters, scientific literature, and clinical trials (ClinicalTrials.gov). Here, we focus on the molecular characteristics of 4–1BB agonist antibodies, their clinical applications, and future directions in cancer immunotherapy.

Molecular characteristics of 4–1BB agonists

Among the 60 identified 4–1BB agonists in clinical development, 15 are monospecific IgG-based antibodies, and 44 are bi- or multi-specific antibodies or fusion proteins, with BT7480 being the only Bicycle® peptide. Monospecific 4–1BB agonists possess a variety of features, including their antibody backbone, FcγR cross-linking properties, epitope specificity, binding affinity, and 4–1BB/4-1BBL blocking activity. Furthermore, bispecific 4–1BB agonist antibodies or fusion proteins (hereafter, 4–1BB BsAb) exhibit even more diverse features than monospecific agonists, including multiple structural features (e.g., Fc domain presence, valency, number of binding sites, molecular weight) and different affinity for each target. Based on publicly available data, this section provides a comprehensive overview of the molecular characteristics of 4–1BB agonists (Figure 2), as summarized in Tables 1, 2 and Supplemental Tables S1, S2. 

Monospecific 4–1BB agonists

Following the failure of the first generation of 4–1BB agonist antibodies including urelumab (BMS-663513, targeting cysteine-rich domain 1 (CRD1) of 4–1BB, 4–1BBL non-blocking, IgG4) and utomilumab (PF-05082566 targeting CRD3-4 of 4–1BB, 4–1BBL blocking, IgG2), the next generation of monospecific 4–1BB agonist antibodies has been designed to target different epitopes in either an IgG1 or IgG4 backbone. Most of these molecules have cross-linking-dependent 4–1BB agonist activity and subnanomolar to double-digit nanomolar affinity for 4–1BB (Table 1 and Supplemental Table S1).

The 4–1BB agonist antibodies, such as LVGN6051, AGEN2373, ADG206, STA551, and YH004, are IgG1, which generally have a higher affinity for FcγRs than IgG4. LVGN6051, ADG206, and STA551 contain mutations in the Fc region to enhance selective binding to Fc gamma receptor IIB (FcγRIIB), resulting in FcγRIIB-dependent 4–1BB clustering and reducing FcγRI or FcγRIII-mediated antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) and antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP) compared to wild-type (WT) IgG1. In addition to the FcγRIIB-selective binding, these conditional 4–1BB agonistic molecules are designed to improve safety profiles while maintaining anti-tumor activity by selecting clones with weak agonist function (LVGN6051), by ensuring binding only in tissues with high adenosine triphosphate concentration (STA551), or by masking the paratope with a protease-activatable linker (ADG206) for localized activation in the protease-rich TME. Interestingly, AGEN2373 and YH004, which have an IgG1 WT backbone, have ADCC/ADCP-mediated Treg suppression as one of their mechanisms of action (MoA). In contrast, ADG106, PE0116, CTX-471, and ATOR-1017 are FcγR conditional 4–1BB agonists in IgG4 format. While they also exhibit cross-linking-dependent 4–1BB agonist function, their 4–1BB binding epitopes and 4–1BBL blocking abilities differ from those of urelumab. Based on the available information regarding their 4–1BBL blocking characteristics, ADG106, ADG206, PE0116, and ATOR-1017 block 4–1BBL binding to 4–1BB, and AGEN2373 and CTX-471 are 4–1BBL non-blocking antibodies. This distinction may affect their activities when soluble or membrane 4–1BBL is expressed, although it is not clear whether 4–1BBL can enhance the activity of non-blocking binders or reduce the activity of blocking binders under physiological conditions.

Bi- or multi-specific 4–1BB agonists

The 4–1BB BsAb represents a promising approach by enabling conditional 4–1BB activation in the TME. A wide range of 4–1BB BsAbs have been developed by engaging with multiple TAAs, such as claudin 18.2 (CLDN18.2), epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), B7 homolog 4 (B7-H4), epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM), carcinoembryonic antigen-related cell adhesion molecule 5 (CEACAM5), glypican-3 (GPC3), melanotransferrin (CD228), trophoblast glycoprotein (5T4), prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA), and CD19, a tumor stroma-associated antigen (fibroblast activation protein alpha (FAPα)), antigens expressed on both tumor and immune cells (programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) and CD47), and immune cell-specific targets (CD40, OX40, and programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1)). By employing target-binding arm(s), 4–1BB BsAbs strongly induce target-dependent 4–1BB cross-linking within the TME. In addition, target-binding moieties confer additional characteristics to 4–1BB BsAbs such as immune inhibitory pathway blockade (for PD-(L)1, B7-H4, or CD47), growth signaling blockade (for EGFR or HER2), or activation of additional stimulatory pathways (for CD40 or OX40).

IgG-based 4–1BB BsAbs typically have Fc-silenced IgG1 or IgG4 to abrogate FcγR binding and subsequently FcγR-mediated 4–1BB cross-linking to prevent unwanted 4–1BB activation in vivo (Table 2 and Supplemental Table S2), while maintaining neonatal Fc receptor binding for their half-life in the circulation, except for two molecules (YH32367/ABL105 and AP402), which have IgG1 WT backbone and ADCC as one of their MoAs. Some molecules, such as NM21-1480, CB307, and MP0310, have human serum albumin (HSA) binding domain(s) rather than the Fc region to achieve antibody-like half-life. DSP107 is a trimeric fusion protein comprising the extracellular domains of signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRPα) and 4–1BBL and does not possess pharmacokinetics-improving properties.

While 4–1BB binding epitope and 4–1BBL blocking ability have not been generally disclosed for most 4–1BB BsAbs, some are known to block 4–1BBL binding (Figure 1). For instance, 4–1BBL-fusion proteins (englumafusp alfa, RG7827, and DSP107) bind to CRD2-3 of 4–1BB and compete with endogenous 4–1BBL, as they contain 4–1BBL itself. Additionally, some 4–1BB BsAbs, such as GEN1046, MCLA-145, FTL008.16, and MP0310, are also known to be 4–1BBL blocking antibodies, even though their specific binding epitopes are not known. Conversely, several 4–1BBL non-blocking 4–1BB BsAbs have also been developed with distinct binding epitopes. ABL’s Grabody-T programs (ABL503, ABL111, ABL103, YH32364/ABL104, and YH32367/ABL105) and PM1003 bind to membrane-proximal CRD4 of 4–1BB, and NM21-1480 and ALG.APV-527 binds to the membrane-distal CRD1-2 of 4–1BB. All these 4–1BB BsAbs are known not to block 4–1BBL binding to 4–1BB. The potential for competition with 4–1BBL for 4–1BB binding may influence agonist activity in the environments where endogenous 4–1BBL is present.

The optimal 4–1BB activation by 4–1BB BsAbs seems to be modulated by binding epitopes for 4–1BB and TAA, and the distance between the TAA and 4–1BB, which is determined by the specific design of formats. In the ABL’s Grabody-T platform and PRS-343 cases, four fusion sites of anti-4-1BB scFv or anticalin were tested, and the differences in 4–1BB or T cell activation profile were observed depending on the BsAb format. Both studies demonstrated that conjugating anti-4-1BB scFv or anticalin to the C-terminus of the heavy chain results in the highest agonist activity. Therapeutic 4–1BB BsAbs with different epitopes, binding affinities, and binding valencies also determine 4–1BB activation. Except for two 4–1BBL-fusion proteins, englumafusp alfa (CD19 and 4–1BBL) and RG7827 (FAPα and 4–1BBL), which have a 3 + 1 ratio (3 4–1BB binding sites and 1 target binding site), the 1 + 1 or 2 + 2 ratio of 4–1BB to the target binding site is typical format of 4–1BB BsAb. Given that 4–1BB activation is initiated by its hyper clustering on immune cells, the expression level of TAA and the stoichiometry of 4–1BB and TAA determine 4–1BB BsAbs activity within the TME. Currently, 4–1BB BsAbs in clinical development exhibit a variety of target and 4–1BB binding affinities (subnanomolar to triple-digit nanomolar), as well as KD ratios (Table 2 and Supplemental Table S2). For LBL-024 (PD-L1 × 4-1BB), it was reported that the difference (about 500-fold) between PD-L1 binding affinity (0.289 nM) and 4–1BB binding affinity (146 nM) may be advantageous, as it confines 4–1BB activity on T cells to the PD-L1-expressing tumor cells within the TME. In contrast, GEN1046 (PD-L1 × 4-1BB), the most advanced 4–1BB BsAb, exhibits balanced binding affinities to its targets within the subnanomolar range (PD-L1: 0.16 nM, 4–1BB: 0.15 nM), resulting in a KD ratio (4-1BB/PD-L1) of approximately 0.94. However, it remains an open question whether different KD ratios impact pharmacological activity in human clinical trials. Overall, additional engineering efforts to generate the next generation of 4–1BB BsAb with enhanced efficacy and safety will continue.

To address the continuing need to improve the efficacy of T cell engagers (TCEs), multiple trispecific antibodies targeting CD3, 4–1BB, and TAA are under development. Chugai has developed a Dual-Ig® that can bind to both CD3 and 4–1BB, but not simultaneously, and is using this technology to develop SAIL66 (CLDN6×CD3/4-1BB) and RO7616789 (DLL3×CD3/4-1BB). SystImmune has developed the octavalent, tetra-specific Guidance and Navigation Control (GNC) antibodies for the treatment of receptor tyrosine kinase-like orphan receptor 1 (ROR1), EGFRvIII, and CD19-positive cancers by combining T cell stimulation (CD3 and 4–1BB) and inhibiting T cell suppression (PD-L1). Even though these molecules showed preclinical anti-tumor efficacy, no publicly available clinical data exists, as they are still in early-stage clinical trials for solid tumors, leukemia, and lymphoma. Overall, trispecific antibodies represent a highly promising next-generation therapeutic option currently under clinical development.

Clinical development of 4–1BB agonists

Although urelumab, a humanized anti-human 4–1BB antibody, demonstrated potent immune activation in early-phase trials (NCT00309023, NCT00612664, NCT01471210), its clinical development was limited by severe treatment-related adverse events, most notably hepatotoxicity. Two fatal cases were reported at 1 mg/kg and 5 mg/kg, respectively. Overall, elevation in alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) were observed in 52.6% (182/346) and 54.3% (188/346) of patients, respectively. Unlike urelumab, another monoclonal 4–1BB agonist antibody, utomilumab did not induce major liver toxicity but showed very limited efficacy in clinical trials as a single agent (objective response rate (ORR): 3.8%, disease control rate (DCR): 28.3%) and in combination with pembrolizumab (ORR: 26.1%, DCR: 69.6%).

Clinical evaluation of monospecific 4–1BB agonists

Several monospecific 4–1BB agonist antibodies are in clinical evaluation, employing distinct molecular strategies to mitigate systemic toxicity while enhancing immune costimulation. In contrast to urelumab, which binds to CRD1, most next generation monospecific 4–1BB agonist IgG4 antibodies (ADG-106, ATOR-1017, and CTX-471) have been designed to target alternative epitopes of 4–1BB (CRD2 to CRD4), which may potentially reduce hepatic toxicity. ADG-106, which targets the CRD2-3, DCR of 47.1% and an ORR of 0% in patients with solid tumors, with ALT and AST elevations in 21.6% and 17.6% of patients, respectively. ATOR-1017, which also targets CRD2-3, showed a DCR of 52% with no objective responses and lower hepatic enzyme elevations (8% each for ALT and AST). CTX-471, which binds to membrane proximal CRD3-4, similar to utomilumab, showed a DCR of 47.7% and an ORR of 7.7%, with relatively low AST elevation (6.3%) and no reported ALT increase. These preliminary interim data from early clinical trials suggest that next-generation 4–1BB agonists, even with the same isotype as urelumab (IgG4), can have different clinical toxicity profiles depending on the 4–1BB binding epitope. Multiple IgG1-based 4–1BB agonist antibodies, such as AGEN2373 and LVGN6051, are in various phases of development, and their interim clinical data have been reported. AGEN2373, a conditionally active 4–1BBL non-blocking, 4–1BB agonist antibody, with Fc-competent WT backbone harboring Fc effector functions, achieved an ORR of 10.5% and a DCR of 47.4% with no treatment-related hepatitis reported. LVGN6051, designed for FcγRIIB-mediated conditional activation in the TME, reported an ORR of 7.0% and a DCR of 41.7%, although ALT and AST elevations occurred in 22.0% and 20.0% of patients, respectively. Collectively, these findings illustrate that varying degrees of clinical activity and hepatic safety of monospecific 4–1BB agonists are dictated by antibody isotype, epitopes, and MoA (summarized in Table 3 and Supplemental Table S3). It will be interesting to monitor the results of multiple approaches of 4–1BB immunotherapy in various clinical trials.

Clinical evaluation of bispecific 4–1BB agonists

To enhance tumor selectivity and mitigate off-target immune-related toxicity, 4–1BB BsAbs were engineered to be devoid of Fc receptor binding and showed better safety and potent 4–1BB activation in clinical development (Table 3 and Supplemental Table S3). Among many of these 4–1BB BsAbs, the most advanced PD-L1 × 4-1BB BsAbs in clinical development are GEN1046 and ABL503. Several clinical-stage PD-L1 × 4-1BB BsAbs have reported promising preliminary efficacy and manageable hepatic safety profiles. Across multiple trials, these BsAbs have demonstrated an ORR ranging from 4.4% to 33.3% and a DCR ranging from 33.3% to 65.6%. ALT or AST elevations were observed in approximately 15.6% to 34.2% of patients, which is notably lower than that reported with urelumab (ALT 52.6%, AST 54.3%), indicating a more favorable hepatic safety profile. Early clinical data suggest that these 4–1BB BsAbs can induce promising and durable responses, even in patients refractory to PD-(L)1 inhibitors, particularly with optimized dosing and combination strategies. Consistent with these observations, ABL503 has demonstrated antitumor activity with ORR of 26.9% and DCR of 69.2% at the potentially efficacious dose level (3 and 5 mg/kg), and the responders included patients refractory to prior PD-(L)1 inhibitor treatment. Although ALT (32.1%) and AST (30.2%) elevations were observed in patients following ABL503 treatment, they were generally reversible and manageable with corticosteroids or treatment interruption. Of note, ABL503 treatment was associated with an increase in the number of effector memory T cells and resulted in sustained tumor shrinkage for more than six months even after treatment discontinuation. Although all the PD-L1 × 4-1BB BsAbs have demonstrated a more favorable safety profile compared to the first generation agonist Urelumab, a recurring class effect of manageable hepatic enzyme elevations has been observed, potentially attributable to basal PD-L1 expression in liver tissue (Table 3). Importantly, recent clinical data indicate that hepatic enzyme elevation may be further mitigated by optimizing the dosing schedule without compromising efficacy. For instance, in the case of GEN1046, a dosing modification from every 3 weeks (Q3W) to every 6 weeks (Q6W) resulted in a lower incidence of liver-related adverse events and treatment discontinuations, while preserving encouraging antitumor activity and overall survival (OS) outcomes. Interestingly, GEN1046 showed noteworthy clinical data when combined with pembrolizumab to block PD-1. In previously treated PD-L1-positive metastatic non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), GEN1046 with pembrolizumab (Q6W dosing) achieved a median OS of 17.5 months and a 12-month OS rate of 69%, substantially exceeding historical clinical data for pembrolizumab monotherapy in the second-line setting (median OS ~ 8–9 months, 12-month OS rate ~35–40%). These findings suggest that rational adjustment of dose schedules could provide a strategy to broaden the therapeutic window of PD-L1 × 4-1BB BsAbs by enhancing clinical efficacy while mitigating safety concerns.

Meanwhile, BsAbs targeting other TAAs have also shown promising results. ABL111, a CLDN18.2 × 4-1BB BsAb, reported an ORR of 11% and a DCR of 37%, with a favorable hepatic safety profile (ALT elevation 11%, AST elevation 6%) in the Phase 1 dose escalation study. In addition, the Phase 1b study evaluating ABL111 as a first-line treatment for gastric cancer in combination with nivolumab and mFOLFOX6 chemotherapy reported an ORR of 71% (12/17) and a 100% DCR across all dose levels. An ORR of 83% (10/12) was observed at dose levels selected for the ongoing dose expansion study (8 and 12 mg/kg). PM1032, another CLDN18.2 × 4-1BB BsAb, reported an ORR of 12.5% and a DCR of 43.8%, with a favorable hepatic safety profile (ALT, AST elevation 13.3%) in Phase 1 dose escalation and expansion study. YH32367/ABL105, a HER2 × 4-1BB BsAb with Fc-competent backbone, demonstrated an ORR of 23% and DCR of 55% with only 3% elevation in hepatic enzyme. Notably, despite retaining an unmodified Fc region often attributed to FcγR-mediated systemic 4–1BB activation and hepatotoxicity, YH32367/ABL105 demonstrated a favorable hepatic and efficacy profile in early clinical evaluation. PRS-343, another HER2 × 4-1BB BsAb, demonstrated an ORR of 12.5% and DCR of 52.5% with no hepatic enzyme elevation. While more studies of such molecules are required, this suggests that 4–1BB epitope selection and structural configuration may effectively confine 4–1BB activation to HER2+ tumor sites, mitigating off-target immune stimulation. Collectively, these findings illustrate the therapeutic potential of TME-restricted 4–1BB BsAbs, offering a promising approach for enhancing anti-tumor immunity (e.g., by promoting CD8+ T cell activity), while maintaining hepatic tolerability, and potentially overcoming resistance to prior immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). Another target of 4–1BB BsAb is EGFR, a key regulator of cell proliferation, differentiation, and survival, and an important therapeutic target in cancer. While EGFR-targeted therapies demonstrate efficacy in EGFR-overexpressing cancers, the frequent emergence of drug resistance highlights the need for novel strategies. YH32364/ABL104, an EGFR × 4-1BB BsAb, was designed with a dual mechanism to address this challenge: inhibiting EGFR signaling while simultaneously inducing tumor-specific 4–1BB stimulation. This approach enables localized activation of 4–1BB within tumors while minimizing the risk of systemic overstimulation. YH32364/ABL104 received clinical trial authorization from South Korea’s Ministry of Food and Drug Safety in 2025 and entered clinical trial study.

Combination partners for 4–1BB agonists

Building on their improved safety profiles as a monotherapy, 4–1BB agonists are being actively evaluated in combination with a wide range of therapeutic agents, as detailed in Table 4 and Supplemental Table S4. Early findings from clinical trials suggest that, when paired with appropriate combination partners, 4–1BB agonists have the potential to improve established efficacy endpoints such as OS, progression-free survival (PFS), and ORR over current standards of care.

Immune checkpoint inhibitors

The advent of ICIs, targeting coinhibitory receptors cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) and PD-(L)1 on immune cells, has revolutionized cancer treatment. By “releasing the brakes” on anti-tumor T cells, ICIs offer durable responses in a subset of patients across various tumor types. However, despite their groundbreaking success, a significant portion of patients still do not respond to ICIs or develop resistance over time. This highlights the need for combination strategies to amplify and sustain robust anti-tumor immunity. A compelling avenue is to pair ICIs with costimulatory agonists, particularly those targeting 4–1BB, as shown in multiple preclinical studies. This strategy is based on a strong complementary MoA: while ICIs reinvigorate exhausted T cells, 4–1BB agonists provide a powerful secondary signal that promotes T cell proliferation and effector function. Additionally, 4–1BB agonism itself upregulates PD-1 expression on T cells, making them more responsive to PD-1 blockade. This approach aims to overcome ICI resistance and establish robust, sustained anti-tumor immunity, thereby providing more effective treatment strategies for cancer patients. This synergistic combination strategy is now a major focus of clinical investigation.

An advanced strategy to deliver this constructive collaboration is through bi- or multi-specific 4–1BB agonists that incorporate PD-(L)1 blockade into a single molecule. As of 2025, multiple PD-(L)1 × 4-1BB BsAbs have entered clinical trials (Table 2, Supplemental Table S2). However, these molecules may face a pharmacological challenge due to their distinct pharmacological requirements for inhibiting/activating two targets. Effective PD-(L)1 blockade requires high, saturating receptor occupancy (approaching 100%) to fully inhibit the negative signaling. In contrast, 4–1BB agonism is dependent on receptor cross-linking or multimerization to trigger a productive downstream signaling, excessive 4–1BB receptor occupancy by 4–1BB BsAb can lead to monovalent binding (singly saturated 4–1BB), which fails to induce clustering and results in suboptimal or even diminished activation. This creates a therapeutic paradox: the optimal concentration for maximal 4–1BB activation might be too low to achieve the saturating occupancy required for effective PD-(L)1 blockade (Figure 3). Therefore, the expression of PD-(L)1 and 4–1BB within the TME, along with their binding kinetics and stoichiometry, must be carefully considered. To compensate for this potentially incomplete PD-(L)1 inhibition by the PD-(L)1 × 4-1BB BsAb, combining it with separate, conventional anti-PD-(L)1 inhibitors could be a rational strategy to maximize anti-tumor efficacy and promising clinical applications in the future. 

T cell engagers

T cell-based immunotherapies have emerged as a major clinical practice in cancer treatment. Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells and TCEs are the key immunotherapies. TCEs are innovative BsAbs designed to bridge tumor cells with T cells (typically, TAA×CD3), thereby redirecting T cell cytotoxicity in an major histocompatibility complex (MHC)-independent manner, allowing them to activate even naive or weakly activated T cells and effectively target tumors with low immunogenicity or MHC downregulation. Upon binding, TCEs effectively create an immunological synapse that initiates proximal T cell activation. This leads to rapid polarization of the T cell and the release of cytolytic granules and proinflammatory cytokines, amplifying both direct tumor killing and local anti-tumor immune response. Clinically, TCEs have demonstrated significant clinical benefit and have been approved for the treatment of hematologic malignancies. However, low efficacy in solid tumors, the risk of systemic cytokine release syndrome (CRS), and on-target off-tumor toxicity remain as major challenges. Moreover, their short half-lives and poor biodistribution often necessitate continuous infusion. Sustained activation by TCEs without adequate costimulation can lead to T cell exhaustion, thereby limiting durable responses and losing their ability to kill cancer cells.

The combination of TCEs with 4–1BB agonists or the use of TAA×CD3 × 4-1BB trispecific antibodies offers a compelling synergistic strategy, capitalizing on their complementary roles in T cell activation and modulation. While TCE monotherapy excels at initiating primary T cell activation, it often falls short of eliciting sustained anti-tumor responses in the absence of robust costimulatory signals, often leading to T cell exhaustion. This is where 4–1BB agonists become crucial: CD3 stimulation upregulates 4–1BB expression on T cells, making them more responsive to 4–1BB agonists. By providing a vital costimulatory signal by promoting anti-apoptotic pathways and metabolic fitness, 4–1BB agonists can transform a fleeting T cell activation into a potent, sustained, and durable anti-tumor response, thereby inducing T cell reinvigoration. Furthermore, dual targeting of 4–1BB and CD3 amplifies local anti-tumor immunity by restricting 4–1BB-mediated costimulation to the tumor sites enriched with T cells by TCEs. This strategy maximizes anti-tumor effects while minimizing systemic toxicity. Indeed, preclinical studies consistently demonstrate the synergistic anti-tumor efficacy of combining TCEs with 4–1BB agonists, resulting in greater tumor regression, improved survival, and more robust immune responses, including increased numbers of functional tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and higher cytokine production.

Although the clinical evaluation of combining TCEs with 4–1BB agonists is still in its early stages, this strategy represents a promising therapeutic approach characterized by unique costimulatory properties distinct from other signals, such as CD28. Recently, two Phase 1 studies investigated CD19-targeted 4–1BB BsAb (englumafusp alfa) or CD28 BsAb (RO7443904) in combination with CD20-targeted TCE (glofitamab) for patients with relapsed/refractory non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL, NCT04077723, NCT05219513). In these trials, 4–1BB BsAb combined with TCE promoted complete metabolic responses that began at the first response assessment and persisted through subsequent assessments. This indicated a gradual yet persistent anti-tumor response, coinciding with the upregulation of activated effector/central memory CD8+ T cells. In contrast, CD28 BsAb combined with TCE achieved complete metabolic responses rapidly and robustly at the first response assessment, demonstrating an immediate and potent response, accompanied by an increase in effector-to-central memory cell ratio and proliferation signatures (Ki-67+). These results collectively underscore that the CD3/4-1BB costimulation strategy uniquely bridges immediate T cell activation with long-term durability, ensuring sustained tumor control through a robust memory response. Consistently, this finding aligns with the rationale underpinning several other ongoing clinical trials evaluating the TCE and 4–1BB agonist combination (Table 4, Supplemental Table S4).

Clinical data from CAR-T cell therapies have provided critical insights into the distinctive properties and remarkable clinical efficacy of simultaneous activation of 4–1BB and CD3. The success of 4–1BB costimulation in CAR-T cell therapy is driven by its ability to generate a progressive and sustained T cell response, characterized by the robust induction of a long-lasting memory phenotype. This is exemplified by prolonged median OS and PFS, alongside a higher proportion of minimal residual disease-negative complete remission. This sustained performance is correlated with the divergent metabolic profiles of 4–1BB CAR-T cells, demonstrating that 4–1BB costimulation preserves mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation and promotes memory-like differentiation marked by CD62L expression. A low incidence of severe CRS was observed in patients receiving 4–1BB CAR-T cells, which typically had a slow onset (3–5 days post infusion) and lacked rapid systemic immune activation. These attributes diverge from the functional profile of CD28 costimulation, which favors robust and immediate immune activation. CD28 CAR-T cells preferentially utilize glycolytic metabolism to support fast proliferation and effector phenotypes characterized by CD69 and CD57 expressions, and they typically exhibit an earlier onset of CRS (2 days post infusion), indicating more rapid and intense systemic immune activation. Collectively, the insights from CAR-T therapies highlight that concurrent stimulation of 4–1BB and CD3 enhances clinical performance and confers a more favorable safety and durability profile, a benefit that extends to combination or trispecific antibody strategies.

Antibody-drug conjugates

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have emerged as a transformative approach in cancer therapy by enhancing tumor-specific delivery of potent cytotoxic drugs directly to cancer cells while sparing healthy cells. This process not only reduces tumor burden, but also induces immunogenic cell death (ICD), leading to the release of tumor antigens and soluble mediators that can reshape the immune landscape within the TME. ICD and associated immunomodulatory effects upregulate MHC class I/II expression on antigen-presenting cells, thereby promoting T cell priming against exposed TAAs. These effects position ADCs as potential immunomodulatory enhancers in cancer therapy. Given these immunogenic properties, combining ADCs with immunotherapies has demonstrated promising synergistic effects in both preclinical models and clinical studies, leading to enhanced innate and adaptive anti-tumor immunity and durable therapeutic response by prompting immune memory formation.

While ADC-ICI combinations have been extensively explored, the synergy between ADCs and 4–1BB agonists remains an emerging, yet highly promising, area of investigation. However, the mechanistic rationale is compelling, and ongoing research is expected to provide crucial insights into its feasibility. Preclinical evidence suggests that 4–1BB activation enhances T cell persistence and effector functions, potentially complementing the immune-stimulatory effects of ADCs. By leveraging ADC-induced immunogenic changes in the TME, 4–1BB agonists could further potentiate adaptive immune responses, leading to a more durable and robust anti-tumor effect. In this context, early clinical data are beginning to support this concept of co-administering these molecules with chemotherapeutic agents; for instance, in treatment-naïve advanced extrapulmonary neuroendocrine carcinoma (EP-NEC), LBL-024 (PD-L1 × 4-1BB) combined with etoposide/platinum-based chemotherapy achieved a median OS of 12.2 months and a median PFS of 6.4 months, exceeding historical benchmarks for chemotherapy alone (median OS ~ 8–9 months, median PFS ~4.0–5.0 months). LBL-024‘s clinical activity extends beyond EP-NEC, with promising efficacy observed in small cell lung cancer (SCLC), NSCLC, biliary tract cancer and other malignancies.

Concluding remarks

The clinical landscape for 4–1BB agonists has evolved significantly from the first-generation molecules, which were constrained by dose-limiting toxicities or suboptimal efficacy. The development of next-generation 4–1BB agonists, as detailed in this review, has been driven by a sophisticated understanding of their molecular characteristics, aiming to improve the therapeutic index by selectively activating 4–1BB within the TME. These efforts include Fc engineering, epitope-specific targeting, and the creation of bi- or multi-specific formats.

Our review highlights key insights from the clinical evaluation of these next-generation 4–1BB agonists. Monospecific agonists, while facing challenges, have shown that molecular design, including isotype, epitope, and mechanism of action, can critically impact their clinical activity and safety, particularly regarding hepatic tolerability. However, the most promising approach to date involves BsAbs that co-target 4–1BB and TAAs, thus activating T cells specifically within the TME. These molecules demonstrate the potential to enhance tumor-selective T cell activation while minimizing systemic toxicity. Early clinical data for these 4–1BB BsAbs, such as the PD-L1 × 4-1BB, Claudin18.2 × 4-1BB, and HER2 × 4-1BB BsAbs, suggest they can induce promising and durable responses, even in patients who were previously resistant to PD-(L)1 inhibitors.

Furthermore, the strategic combination of 4–1BB agonists with other therapeutic modalities, including ICIs and TCEs, represents a key advancement. This synergistic approach leverages the ability of 4–1BB to provide a crucial costimulatory signal, which is essential for sustaining T cell activation and preventing exhaustion. This principle has been validated by preclinical and early-phase clinical data and is further supported by the remarkable success of 4–1BB costimulation in CAR-T cell therapies, which demonstrate that 4–1BB enhances clinical performance and confers a more favorable safety and durability profile, providing a compelling rationale for the development of 4–1BB agonists. Additionally, the ability of ADCs to induce ICD and activate the immune system provides a strong mechanistic rationale for combination with 4–1BB agonists, as these agents can enhance the overall anti-tumor immune response.

Although the field remains an active area of investigation, as the landscape of immuno-oncology continues to advance, 4–1BB agonists have the potential to become an integral component of future combination regimens, designed to overcome resistance and prolong therapeutic efficacy by enhancing immune memory and sustaining T cell activation, as has been observed in other combination strategies. Future research on dosing regimens, including rational dose scheduling to further expand the therapeutic window, and on identifying biomarkers that predict patient responses, is critical and will ultimately pave the way for the successful clinical translation of this promising therapeutic construct.
