# Immune checkpoint inhibitor-induced hepatitis injury: risk factors, outcomes, and impact on survival.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) are associated with a unique set of immune-related adverse events (irAEs). Few studies have evaluated the risk factors and outcomes of patients who develop ICI-induced hepatitis (ICIH).

**METHODS**: We utilized an institutional database of patients with advanced cancers treated with ICI to identify patients with ICIH. irAEs were graded using the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events v4. Overall survival (OS) was calculated from the date of ICI to death from any cause or the date of the last follow-up. OS with 95% confidence intervals were estimated using the Kaplan-Meier method and stratified by the occurrence of ICIH.

**RESULTS**: We identified 1096 patients treated with ICI. The most common ICIs were PD1/L1 (n = 774) and CTLA-4 inhibitors (n = 195). ICIH occurred among 64 (6%) patients: severity was < grade 3 in 30 and ≥ grade 3 in 24 patients (3.1% overall). Median time to ICIH was 63 days. ICIH was more frequent in women (p = 0.038), in patients treated with combination ICIs (p < 0.001), and when given as first-line therapy (p = 0.018). Occurrence of ICIH was associated with significantly longer OS, median 37.0 months (95% CI 21.4, NR) compared to 11.3 months (95% CI 10, 13, p < 0.001); there was no difference in OS between patients with ≥ grade 3 ICIH vs grade 1-2.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Female sex, combination immunotherapy, and the first line of immunotherapy were associated with ICIH. Patients with ICIH had improved clinical survival compared to those that did not develop ICIH. There is a need for prospective further studies to confirm our findings.

Introduction

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), such as antibodies directed at programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) and its ligand programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), have revolutionized cancer treatment. First approved in melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), ICIs are now approved for many types of cancer and are often standard first line treatment. ICIs have significantly improved response rates and overall survival compared to chemotherapy alone in multiple solid tumors and are often better tolerated. In contrast to the toxicities of cytotoxic chemotherapy, ICI’s have been associated with immune-related adverse events (irAEs) due to immune targeting of normal tissues. irAEs can involve any organ system but the most commonly affect the lung, skin, gastrointestinal tract, and liver.

Although the majority of irAEs are mild to moderate in severity, serious or even life-threatening irAEs (e.g., severe colitis, pneumonitis, encephalitis) have been reported. Reassuringly, the reported incidence of fatal adverse side effects is low. irAEs such as pneumonitis and colitis have been explored in the literature, however few studies have evaluated the risk factors of ICI hepatitis (ICIH) as well as impact on overall survival. Hepatitis due to ICI has been reported to occur in up to 10% of patients in large phase 3 trials and more frequently has been noted in combination checkpoint inhibitor therapy. As ICI therapy becomes part of more complex multi-modality treatment regimens including with chemotherapy, targeted therapy, and other immune agents each of which may also be potentially hepatotoxic, a better understanding of patterns of toxicities from ICI will improve clinical decision making. We therefore conducted a study to evaluate the risk factors and OS among patients with ICIH.

Methods

We utilized a single institution database of patients with advanced cancers treated with ICI between 2011 and 2017 at The Ohio State University Comprehensive Cancer Center to identify patients with ICIH. This study was approved by the institutional review board at the Ohio State University (Institutional Review Board approval # 2016C0070). Patient with advanced cancer who received at least one dose of ICI, alone or in combination with other systemic therapies were included in this analysis. Patients on clinical trials (investigational ICI) and standard of care were included. Clinical data were extracted through chart review via electronic medical record system. Research Electronic Data Capture (REDCap) was used for data collection. The baseline patient characteristics were assessed from the clinical documentation. irAEs were identified by diagnosis and clinical intervention. We screened and identified patients with ICIH using transaminitis because that was the most common cause presentation for ICIH. We evaluated for competing causes for hepatitis including viral hepatitis, significant alcohol consumption or cirrhosis, concomitant medications, including herbals. ICIH was defined as an increase in AST and ALT. Grade 1 was defined between upper limit of normal (ULN) and 3.0 xULN. Grade 2 was defined as 3.0xULN-5.0xULN. Grade 3 was defined at 5.0xULN-20.0xULN, and Grade 4 was identified as greater than 20xULN. All irAE were graded using the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events, version 4.0 (CTCAE). Attribution of ICIH was dependent on both lack of other likely etiology as documented by clinical notes and confirmed through manual chart review. Overall survival (OS) was calculated from the date of the initiation of the ICI to death from any cause. If date of death was unknown, survival was censored by the date of the last follow-up examination.

Patient characteristics were summarized using descriptive statistics including medians, interquartile range (IQR), and ranges for the continuous variables and frequencies for the categorical variables for all the patients, stratified by ICIH. Patient characteristics were compared by ICIH status using Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test for the categorical variables and Wilcoxon rank-sum test for the continuous variables as appropriate. Kaplan–Meier (KM) survival curves were stratified by ICIH status (Yes vs No; or No, grade 1–2, vs grade 3 or higher). These survival curves were compared using log rank test. Cox regression model was used for overall survival analysis with ICIH status adjusted for age, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) and line of therapy. Hazard ratio (HR) with 95% confidence interval was reported. A p-value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant. All analyses were conducted using SAS version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, NC).

Results

Patient characteristics

We identified 1,096 patients treated with at least one dose of ICI. The most common cancer types were melanoma (n=342, 31%) and lung cancer (n=224, 20%), and the most common ICIs were PD1/L1 (n=774, 70.6%) and CTLA-4 inhibitors (n=195, 17.8%). Eighty-one patients received combination immunotherapy with anti-PD-1/L1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapy. Patient demographics are summarized in Table 1.

Incidence of ICIH:

Among 1096 patients, 64 (5.8%) patients developed ICIH of any grade. The incidence of ≥grade 3 ICIH in the entire cohort was 3.1%. Severity of those with ICIH was grade 1–2 in 30 and ≥grade 3 among 34 patients. No grade 5 ICIH was noted in this study cohort. Nearly all patients presented with transaminitis, with only 4 patients having grade 3 or above elevations in bilirubin alone. Of patients with ICIH, 30 (47%) were male and the median age was 60 years. The median time to the diagnosis of ICIH was 63 days (range 11–448 days). ICIH was the only irAE in 24 patients and co-occurred with other irAE in 40 patients. The most common co-occurring irAEs included pneumonitis (n=7), colitis (n=15), thyroid dysfunction (n=14), and dermatological toxicity (n=15).

Risk factors for ICIH:

Sex, type of immunotherapy, and line of immunotherapy were all significantly associated with ICIH. ICIH of any grade was more common in women (p=0.038), in patients treated with combination (CTLA4 and PD1/PD-L1) as opposed to ICI monotherapy (p<0.001), and in patients treated with immunotherapy as first line (p=0.018) (Table 1). There was no significant association between risk of ICIH and age, race, history of diabetes, concomitant medications (statins, proton pump inhibitors (PPI), smoking history, cancer type, body mass index (BMI) or performance status. Absolute lymphocyte count (ALC) (p=0.040) and neutrophil to lymphocyte ratio (NLR) (p=0.048) at baseline were both associated with risk for ICIH; however, no association was identified with absolute neutrophils, monocytes, or eosinophils at baseline. Among the 64 patients who developed ICIH, four (6.3%) had prior known history of liver cirrhosis, 13 patients (20.3%) had documented alcohol use, one (1.6%) had a known history of chronic alcohol abuse, two patients (3.1%) had history of hepatitis virus C and one patient with history of hepatitis B infection (1.6%). One of the hepatitis C patient received active treatment and viral load was 289 IU/mL at the time of diagnosis the other patient with hepatitis C had completed treatment and had no viral load. For the patient with hepatitis B, the viral load was 27,7162 IU/mL and was not receiving treatment at the time of ICIH treatment. Seven patients (11.0%) had imaging evidence of liver metastases at the time of ICI initiation.

Treatment of patients with ICIH:

Nearly all (33 out of 34) patients with grade 3 or higher ICIH were treated with steroids. with oral prednisone as the most common initial treatment: prednisone n=23 (70%), dexamethasone n=6 (18%), methylprednisolone n=4 (12%). In patients with grade 1–2 hepatitis, only 13 patients (43%) received steroids. Median duration of steroids was 45 days (range 21–120 days). Three patients received additional treatment for ICIH after not responding to steroids initially within two weeks including mycophenolate; one patient also received infliximab after no improvement in liver function test after 30 days. The main reason that these three patients were started on additional immune suppressant was due to persistent elevation in liver enzymes. Three patients underwent liver biopsy, however findings may have been influenced by use of steroids (Supplemental Table 2). Of 34 patients with grade ≥ 3 ICIH, 11 resumed ICI therapy and none of these patients developed recurrent hepatitis.

Overall survival

Patients who developed ICIH had significantly longer OS than patients who did not develop ICIH with median OS 37.0 months (95% CI: 21.4, not reached [NR]) compared to median OS 11.3 months (95% CI: 10, 13, p<0.001). There was no significant difference in OS for patients who developed grades 1–2 ICIH (median OS NR, 95% CI: 20.9, NR) vs grades 3 or 4 ICIH (median OS 27.6 months, 95% CI: 17.9, NR, p=0.601). The hazard ratio for death among patients with hepatitis was 47% lower than for those patients without hepatitis after adjusting for gender, cancer type, ECOG performance status and line of therapy (HR 0.53, 95% CI: 0.37, 0.76, p<.001, Table 3).

Discussion

In this retrospective single center study of patients with advanced cancer treated with ICI, we found that female sex, type of immunotherapy, line of therapy, ALC, and NLR were all associated with the development of ICIH. In addition, ICIH was associated with improved OS regardless of severity of toxicity. We also observed a low incidence of recurrence of ICIH even among patients with grade 3 or 4 toxicity, which differs from prior studies of other irAEs such as colitis where the recurrence rate is higher.

We found an association with longer overall survival in patients who developed ICIH regardless of grade of toxicity. Our study confirms reports in other studies regarding the incidence of irAE broadly and the association with survival in patients with solid tumors treated with ICI. However, these prior studies differed from our study in several important ways. Here we focused on ICIH, observed the association with longer survival regardless of grade of hepatoxicity, and identified several risk factors for ICIH. We previously reported that median survival was significantly longer for patients who experienced grade 3 or higher irAE of any organ, although this did not hold true for older patients. Our finding of an association with improved survival in patients with ICIH is consistent with prior studies showing that patients with irAE seem to derive improved clinical benefit from therapy. However irAE are also more likely to occur with longer duration of therapy and studies utilizing landmark analyses have not consistently found an association with survival. In addition, meta-analyses have suggested that grade 3 or higher irAE lead to worse survival, although this study did not evaluate association between specific types of irAE and survival. In our study, we found no statistical difference in survival in patients with grade 3 or higher ICIH compared to grade 1–2 ICIH. About a third of patients who develop ICIH also developed other irAEs, which is consistent with prior studies. Takanori et al found that those with liver injury induced from ICI also occurred with other irAE and usually occurred later during treatment. A recent meta-analysis reported that patients who develop multi-system irAE have improved survival than patients without irAE or with only 1 irAE. The high occurrence of multi-system irAE in our study may partially explain the improvement in survival.

Similar to other studies, we found that there is an association between female sex and ICIH. Kitagaya et al. found that incidence of grade 3 or higher ICIH was associated with female sex and history of ICI treatment.Prior studies in NSCLC and melanoma have found that women had higher rates of irAE overall. It is established that autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) in general population is reported to be more common in younger females. Because AIH is more common in younger females, and women have been shown to have more robust immune responses to infections and vaccinations as well as higher rates of autoimmune disease, which may indicate an immune phenotype that is more likely to develop irAE. In a study by Duma et al, pre-menopausal women had higher rates of irAE compared to post-menopausal women and men. In our study, we were unable to show any association between irAE with menopausal status to further evaluate this association as these data were not available, but this could be an important consideration for future studies.

Our findings that ICIH is more common in patients receiving dual ICI are consistent those in other reports, including a study by Cheung et al where hepatitis was most found to occur most commonly in those receiving dual ICIs (PD/PDL1 + CTLA inhibitors). Out of 453 patients from the Chueng study, hepatitis was most common in those receiving dual therapy (incidence 20%), with 75% of Grade 4 hepatitis cases occurring with ipilimumab-containing regimens.

Emerging data suggest that resumption ICI can be safe option with a low risk of recurrent high grade irAE. Li et al performed a retrospective study of patients with melanoma treated with ICIs and who had recovered from grade 3–4 ICIH. Of the 102 patients who developed high-grade ICIH, 31 were re-challenged with ICI and only 4 patients developed recurrent grade 2 or higher hepatitis requiring ICI discontinuation. There was no difference noted regarding best overall response or time to death between patients who resumed ICI and those who did not. The optimal strategy for patient selection and timing of ICI resumption in patients who develop ICIH remains uncertain.

The rate of grade ≥ 3 ICIH in our trial (3.1%) is somewhat lower than in prior studies. For instance, Li et al found hepatic adverse events were observed in 27% (30 patients out of 112 patients) with 7% (8/112) grade 3 or higher. The median time to ICIH was 3 weeks after initiation of ICI. There are several possible reasons for our finding a lower incidence rate compared to prior studies, including a larger and more heterogeneous cohort, high incidence of second line of therapy or beyond, or potential difference in as yet unknown risk factors for ICIH. The reason several studies (and trials) report higher rates of liver injury is that they do not distinguish between ICIH and liver enzyme elevations from other causes 

We acknowledge limitations to our study including the retrospective nature of the study, single center population, and a heterogeneous cohort. The diagnosis of irAE is fraught with challenges and irAE are known to occur even after treatment discontinuation. We were not able to fully capture other potential confounding risk factors including serum protein, portal hypertension, and ascites. Finally, there are limitations to drawing conclusions regarding different grades of ICIH given small numbers of cases. Prospective studies are needed to delineate the relationship between ICI and ICIH as well as to define the optimal treatment of ICIH and methods of predicting or mitigating toxicities, as well as the optimal strategies for identifying patients that may be safely re-treated with ICI.

Conclusion

We identified risk factors for immune checkpoint inhibitor hepatitis including sex, type and line of immunotherapy. We found that patients who developed immune checkpoint inhibitor hepatitis of any grade had longer survival than patients who did not.
