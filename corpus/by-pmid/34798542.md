## Full Text

Better living through chemistry: CRISPR/Cas engineered T cells for cancer immunotherapy

T cells engineered to express transgenes such as chimeric antigen receptors (CAR) or modified T cell receptors (TCR) represent a new pillar of cancer therapy. Use of CRISPR/Cas gene–editing tools now allows even stronger and more precise control over the fate and function of engineered T cell therapies, including multiplex genome editing to facilitate use of off-the-shelf allogeneic T cells and novel approaches which have the potential to overcome some of the limitations of canonical Cas9-mediated DNA cleavage. This review summarizes the CRISPR/Cas techniques that have been used in preclinical research and outlines those that currently being tested in clinical trials.

Introduction

Adoptive cell therapy (ACT) with genetically engineered T cells expressing a transgenic TCR or CAR has emerged as a promising treatment strategy for patients with advanced cancer. While particularly effective against some hematologic malignancies, there have been some reports of responses among patients with epithelial malignancies. However, despite the strong antigen-specific activity conferred by expression of the CAR, it has become clear that engineered T cells are still subject to dysfunction or exhaustion, impaired trafficking, and to the dictates of the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). From the clinical and logistical perspective, current autologous T-cell therapies must be manufactured patient-by-patient in a time consuming, labor- and resource-intensive process. The search for tools to further refine T cell engineering was boosted by the discovery and rapid evolution of the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated (Cas) proteins. Here we review recent advances in using CRISPR/Cas and its derived technologies such as base- and prime-editing to increase the safety, efficacy, and accessibility of T-cell therapies.

Brief overview of CRISPR technologies: CRISPR, base-editing, prime-editing

The goal of gene editing is to convert DNA sequences in living cells into new, desired sequences with precision and efficiency. DNA can be cut using a variety of endonucleases, and early (and successful) attempts to engineer the human genome used engineered endonucleases such as transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALEN), zinc-finger nucleases (ZNF), and more recently, clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) combined with CRISPR-associated proteins (Cas). Ease of use, cost effectiveness, and the ability for multiplex genome-editing, paired with the rapid development of a continuously growing CRISPR/Cas genome-editing tool-kit has sparked a gene-editing revolution centered around CRISPR/Cas that holds great promise for enhancing the efficacy of T cell immunotherapy. CRISPR/Cas nucleases and derived technologies such as base-editors and prime-editors have greatly expanded the scope of possible genome modifications, allowing for targeted gene insertion, deletion, base-pair conversions or a combination of those.

CRISPR/Cas is a programmable two-component system consisting of a guide RNA (gRNA) and an RNA-guided endonuclease (Cas) capable of binding and cleaving a specific target DNA sequence in various cell types, including T cells. Cas9, from Streptococcus Pyogenes, the most widely used CRISPR-associated endonuclease, makes a blunt-end DNA double-strand break (DSB) 3 bp upstream of a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) as shown in Figure 1a. CRISPR/Cas-induced DSBs are often repaired by the error-prone nonhomologous end-joining pathway (NHEJ), resulting in uncontrolled, but predictable insertion and deletion mutations (INDELs). These INDELs, given they are not of triplicate nucleotides, eventually cause a functional knockout of the target-gene by inducing premature stop-codons or frameshift mutations that eliminate effective protein production through nonsense-mediated decay (NMD). As discussed later, several genetic screening platforms have been developed in T cells based on this approach. Alternatively, homology directed repair (HDR) pathways can be used to knock in DNA sequences or install targeted mutations by adding a DNA donor template (single or double stranded DNA) with the desired DNA sequence. The use of CRISPR/Cas in T cells has been widely adopted in preclinical research and in the clinic with various groups demonstrating knockout efficiencies approaching 100% in primary human T cells. The CRISPR/Cas system can also be used to modulate target gene-expression by fusing a catalytically dead Cas9 to either a gene repressor protein (CRISPRi) or a transcription activator protein (CRISPRa). Since catalytically dead Cas9 can still bind sgRNA, the ribonucleoprotein complex can be targeted to a specific site in the genome to allow for the reversible induction or repression of gene activity.

Limitations of genome editing with CRISPR/Cas nucleases include the heterogeneity in editing outcomes through competing repair pathways, the p53-mediated cell toxicities associated with DSBs, and the preference for NHEJ over HDR in the presence of DNA double strand breaks. To address the need for a programmable system to change (edit) single nucleotides, Liu and colleagues developed CRISPR-derived editing technologies such as base- and prime-editing, that do not require DSBs (by using a dead Cas9 or Cas9 nickase) or donor DNA templates.

Base-editors have two main components as shown in Figure 1b: a catalytically impaired Cas-nuclease that binds to a specific target sequence, and an engineered deaminase enzyme that can convert substrate nucleotides in a defined editing-window (typically protospacer positions 4–8). Two types of base-editors are commonly used: cytosine base-editors (CBEs), which can induce C->T edits, thereby enabling C:G to T:A transition mutations, and adenine base-editors that induce A->G edits, resulting in A:T to G:C transition mutations. Base-editors can be used for targeted point mutations that may result in an altered polypeptide sequence with novel or enhanced functions. Alternatively, base-editing can be used to inactivate genes by disrupting splice donors or splice acceptors at exon-intron boundaries (Figure 1b). Like canonical CRISPR/Cas nucleases, this results in a functional protein knockout, but without causing double-strand breaks, thereby mitigating the risk of chromosomal translocations and p53-dependent toxic responses. Various groups have reported high base-editing efficiencies for various genes in primary human T cells, in both single and multiplex settings (80–90% for TRAC, PDCD1 and B2M). Early on, base-editing was restricted to transition mutations and relied on the availability of NGG-PAM sites that place the target nucleotide into the editing-window. However, recent efforts drastically improved the targeting capabilities of base-editors by developing Cas-proteins that utilize non-NGG PAMs as well as deaminases with more flexible editing windows. Yet, base editing still cannot install all eight transversion mutations or small insertions and deletions. Therefore, a more recent technology called prime editing was invented that can introduce all types of point mutations as well as programmable small insertions and deletions, broadening the scope of possible DNA-modifications.

Prime-editors use an engineered Cas9 nickase fused to a reverse transcriptase (RT) enzyme and a gRNA that has an extended 3’ end that serves as a primer and template for reverse transcription as depicted in Figure 1c. The Cas9 nickase nicks the PAM-containing DNA strand, allowing it to hybridize with the 3’ extension of the gRNA. Following reverse transcription, the newly synthesized 3’flap containing the edit can be stably incorporated into the DNA by removing the redundant, unedited 5’flap through the cellular DNA repair machinery. While prime-editing promises to be a versatile tool to install all possible mutations (and therefore amino acid changes) as well as small insertions and deletions with greater PAM flexibility, it is generally less efficient than base-editing. However, the feasibility of using prime editing in primary human T cells has recently been demonstrated, with reported editing efficiencies of 7.5%.

Enhancing T-cell therapy efficacy through CRISPR/Cas gene-editing

The early, reproducible, and robust success of autologous CAR T cell therapy for B-cell malignancies such as ALL, lymphomas and multiple myeloma has highlighted an increasing appreciation of the multiple challenges that limit broader implementation in other types of cancer. There are multiple ways in which the efficacy of CAR T cell therapy can be increased to overcome T cell dysfunction, poor tumor penetration and resistance in the TME depicted in Figure 2. Here we will focus on modifications through CRISPR/Cas mediated gene editing in primary human T cells.

T cell dysfunction arises in the context of chronic antigen stimulation. Dysfunctional T cells upregulate expression of inhibitory immune receptors (colloquially, checkpoint molecules) but the functional consequences of T cell exhaustion include inability to mediate tumor killing, low levels of cytokine production, augmented signaling of inhibitory factors, accelerated senescence, and impaired proliferation potential and survival. T cells also face resistance in the TME by other cells as they secrete immunsuppressive factors such as VEGF, TGFb, IL-4, and IL-10. These cells also upregulate inhibitory ligands such as PDL1 that can dampen proper T cell activation.

Knocking out immunosuppressive cytokine receptors such as TGFb receptor, IL-4R and IL-10R on T cells can be another strategy to armor CAR T cells against the suppressive TME. Targeting checkpoint molecules such as PD-1, LAG3, TIM-3, TIGIT and LAG3 etc by CRISPR/Cas could increase anti-tumor efficacy of CAR T cells. Pre-clinical models have shown that PD-1 can be targeted with a high efficiency knockout. Seki and Rutz have also shown that genes such as CXCR4, PD-1, CTLA-4 and TIGIT can be edited with an efficiency of 80–90%. Other groups have knocked-out genes such as DGKa/b, SHP1/2, and Regnase to augment T cell signaling. Jung et al showed that knocking out diacylglycerol kinase alpha (DGK alpha) increased the TCR signaling and in turn enhanced the effector function of the T cells (Jung et al, 2018). Transcription factors such as TOX and NR4a also play a major role in T cell exhaustion, but their disruption is yet to be explored in primary human T cells.

Gene disruption in CAR T cells has also been used to avoid self-targeting of CAR T cells and edit genes such as CD7, CD5, and CD3. This prevents CAR T cell fratricide and maintains proliferation during the manufacturing process and persistence in the patients. Above strategies can be combined by targeting multiple genes simultaneously to further enhance the efficacy of CAR T cells.

Genome-wide CRISPR screens in immune cells can be used to identify and/or prioritize genes that impact T cell function. Shifrut et al performed a genome-wide CRISPR Screen in primary human T cells showing that ablating SOCS1, TCEB2, RASA2 and CBLB enhanced T cell proliferation and anti-tumor function.

Apart from increasing the efficacy of T cell immunotherapy, genome editing can also increase the safety and accessibility of CAR T cells. Allogeneic T cells have multiple advantages over autologous T cells but could lead to graft versus host disease and host allorejection. Multiplex genome editing can overcome the limitations of allogeneic CAR T cells. Stadtmauer et al recently conducted the first in human clinical trial of CRISPR-engineered T cells in patients with refractory cancer. They edited the TCR alpha, TCR beta and the PDCD1 loci, demonstrating that multiplex gene editing can be performed at clinical scale in T cells. Removing the endogenous TCR and PD-1 could improve the function and persistence of engineered T cells.

Furthermore, knocking in the TCR or CAR transgenes into the TCR locus has shown to be achievable with high efficiency. This allows single gene-knockout and TCR/CAR integration to be performed in the same step rather than in a two-step protocol. Viral vectors are used to deliver the desired transgene. Viral vector production is expensive and time consuming and can cause variability in transduction efficiency in the clinic. This has been recently mitigated by using non-viral approaches to deliver the transgene of interest. The knock-in places the transgene under the control of the endogenous transcriptional regulatory elements, with important implications for dynamic, temporal, or spatial gene expression. Knocking out the TCR has also been extremely helpful in TCR clinical trials since it prevents mispairing with the endogenous TCR thereby increasing the efficiency of the transduction of the transgenic TCR and improving safety by reducing the potential for off-target effects.

Most of the above strategies have shown promising anti-tumor efficacy in preclinical models and multiple clinical trials are underway to determine their efficacy in humans. However, the long-term safety and efficacy of these approaches is yet to be determined.

Base and prime editing

Base- and prime-editors have the potential to enhance safety and expand the scope of genome-editing in T cells. Like conventional Cas nucleases, base- and prime-editors can be used to disrupt target genes such as immune checkpoints to enhance T cell function or to inactivate genes relevant for the generation of allogeneic T cell therapies such as TRAC or B2M. This can be achieved by either inducing premature stop codons, by removing start codons or by disruption of splice donor/acceptor sites (Figure 1b). For example, Webber et al. demonstrated that base-editors can disrupt the genes encoding for PD1, B2M and the TCR alpha chain in a multiplex setting with comparable knockout efficiencies to conventional Cas9 nucleases. Importantly, the use of base-editors resulted in improved T cell expansion and lack of DSB-induced genomic translocations compared to T cells that have been edited with Cas9 nuclease. Similarly, base-editors have also been used to remove CD3 and CD7 from CD3/CD7-targeting CAR-T cells by inducing premature stop-codons, thereby minimizing fratricide during manufacturing with reduced chromosomal translocations compared to conventional Cas9 nuclease. Alternatively, base-editors can also be used to modulate protein levels post-translationally instead of completely ablating gene expression. For example, mutating the N-glycosylation site (N74) in PD-1 with an adenine base-editor resulted in destabilization of the protein and overall reduced expression, reducing PD-1 mediated suppression of CAR-T cell function. This allows for a more subtle control over T cell behavior instead of bifurcation caused by either having or not having the functional target protein as a result of gene knockout.

In addition to gene-silencing, base- and prime-editors can expand the scope of T cell gene-editing to situations in which gene-modifications, rather than gene-disruptions are desired. For example, base- and prime-editors could be used to install gain of function mutations in genes that are known to promote T cell function such as cytokine receptors and their downstream effectors Jak’s or STAT’s. Alternatively, base- and prime-editors could be used to install mutations that change cytokine receptor selectivity or promote biased signaling. This could facilitate the translation of orthogonal ligand-receptor pairs such as IL2/IL2R-b for T cells without the requirement for full-length transgene insertions or skew the effects of pleotropic cytokines towards a desired effect in the engineered cell population. Thus, base and prime editing facilitates more sophisticated genome modifications with the potential for more refined control of gene expression and function.

Clinical Trials

Clinical studies investigating CRISPR/Cas-engineered T cells for cancer immunotherapy are in their infancy but have yielded encouraging results. Out of 20 clinical trials, 4 have been completed or terminated and 2 corresponding studies were published (clinicaltrials.gov accessed on 07/31/2021; Table 1).

The first report of multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing in patients was conducted at the University of Pennsylvania. Stadtmauer et al. investigated the safety and feasibility of multiplex CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing of autologous T cells from patients with refractory advanced myeloma and sarcoma (NCT03399448). In this landmark phase 1 clinical trial, T cells were genetically engineered with CRISPR/Cas9 to knockout endogenous TCR and PD-1 genes (TRAC, TRBC, and PDCD1, respectively) and transduced with a lentiviral vector to express a transgenic TCR targeting the cancer-testis antigen NY-Eso1 1 (NYCE T Cells). We reported efficient editing of all genomic target loci, persistence of engineered T cells from 3 to 9 months and low (0.2–3%) off-target editing. Although chromosomal translocations were detected in all manufactured NYCE T Cells, no cellular transformation or gene-editing-related severe adverse events were observed. Multiplex editing at all three loci was demonstrated at the level of the genome in single cells, however, contrary to expectations, these cells did not have preferential survival in blood. It is possible that this reflects preferred trafficking of engineered T cells from the blood to sites of tumor where antigen was expressed. Alternatively, the fitness of cells may have been adversely affected by multiplex editing and/or that that PD-1 must be expressed at some level for edited T cells to differentiate into memory T cells. This later hypothesis is supported by studies in mouse transgenic T cells.

The second study by Lu et al. examined the safety and feasibility of CRISPR/Cas9-mediated PD-1-editing of autologous T cells from patients with refractory non-small-cell lung cancer (NCT02i793856). The first-in-human phase I clinical trial demonstrated very low off-target editing rates and no severe treatment-related adverse events. However, edited T cells did not persist long term in most patients, presumably due to low levels of editing efficiency or a requirement for PD-1 expression as mentioned above.

These studies preliminarily indicate that the application of CRISPR-edited T cells for cancer immunotherapy is generally safe and feasible. However, thus far clinical trials were largely focused on the genetic knockout of PD-1 and the therapeutic efficacy of CRISPR-edited T cells was limited by low gene editing efficiencies. Therefore, further clinical trials investigating additional target sites for CRISPR-engineering in T cells and enhanced gene editing efficiencies are required to transform CRISPR-engineered T cells from a proof-of-concept to a potent cancer therapeutic.

Perspectives and Conclusions

T cells expressing either CAR or engineered TCR have the potential to provide tumor eradication in cancer patients. However, the next generation of T cell immunotherapies will likely include complex genome modifications such as targeted transgene-insertion, gene-inhibition/activation, or targeted mutations that confer an enhanced or novel function to overcome current challenges faced in the field of cellular immunotherapy. With continuous efforts in synthetic biology and the evolving landscape of CRISPR/Cas gene-editing modalities, the field of cellular immunotherapy is expected to achieve substantial improvements with the goal to move from an individualized “designer therapy” to a mainstay in cancer treatment.
