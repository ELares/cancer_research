# Applications and enhancement strategies of ROS-based non-invasive therapies in cancer treatment.

## Abstract

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) play a crucial role in the pathogenesis of cancer. Non-invasive therapies that promote intracellular ROS generation, including photodynamic therapy (PDT), sonodynamic therapy (SDT), and chemodynamic therapy (CDT), have emerged as novel approaches for cancer treatment. These therapies directly kill tumor cells by generating ROS, and although they show great promise in tumor treatment, many challenges remain to be addressed in practical applications. Firstly, the inherent complexity of the tumor microenvironment (TME), such as hypoxia and elevated glutathione (GSH) levels, hinders ROS generation, thereby significantly diminishing the efficacy of ROS-based therapies. In addition, these therapies are influenced by their intrinsic mechanisms. To overcome these limitations, various nanoparticle (NP) systems have been developed to improve the therapeutic efficacy of non-invasive therapies against tumors. This review first summarizes the mechanisms of ROS generation for each non-invasive therapy and their current limitations, with a particular focus on the enhancement strategies for each therapy based on NP systems. Additionally, various strategies to modulate the TME are highlighted. These strategies aim to amplify ROS generation in non-invasive therapies and enhance their anti-tumor efficiency. Finally, the current challenges and possible solutions for the clinical translation of ROS-based non-invasive therapies are also discussed.

Introduction

Cancer is one of the leading causes of death worldwide and poses a significant threat to human health. According to the Journal of Cancer for Clinicians, it is projected that the incidence of new cancer cases will surge to 35 million by 2050, indicating a persistent growth in the cancer burden. Conventional cancer treatment strategies include chemotherapy, surgery, and radiotherapy. However, traditional chemotherapy and radiotherapy suffer from poor tumor targeting, leading to inevitable side effects and the development of drug/radiation resistance. Surgical intervention is associated with significant trauma to the body while also exhibiting a propensity for recurrence and metastasis. These conventional approaches have not significantly improved the quality of life or survival rates of cancer patients. Therefore, there is an urgent need for an effective anti-cancer strategy with minimal side effects and low resistance potential. In the complex tumor microenvironment (TME), reactive oxygen species (ROS) have been highlighted as crucial signaling molecules influencing tumor progression. ROS are byproducts of oxygen consumption and cellular metabolism, including hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), singlet oxygen (1O2), superoxide ions (O2•−), and hydroxyl radicals (•OH). Mitochondrial metabolism is the primary source of endogenous ROS. Under normal physiological conditions, cells maintain ROS at low steady-state levels by balancing their production and elimination through various antioxidant defense systems, such as superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT), glutathione (GSH), and glutathione peroxidases (GPXs). This balance is crucial as ROS serve as signaling molecules that are essential for regulating normal physiological functions, including cellular differentiation and proliferation. However, when an imbalance between ROS and the antioxidant defense systems leads to oxidative stress, elevated ROS levels can cause damage to cellular components such as proteins, lipids, and DNA, and promote the transformation of normal cells into cancerous ones. In general, cancer cells exhibit significantly elevated levels of ROS (∼100 μM) compared to normal cells (∼20 nM). These elevated ROS levels contribute to oncogenic signaling, promoting tumor proliferation, metastasis, and drug resistance. However, it is noteworthy that, similar to normal cells, when ROS levels in cancer cells continue to rise beyond a critical threshold, it can lead to cellular damage. The fact is that numerous chemotherapy drugs, such as paclitaxel (PTX) and doxorubicin (DOX) have been found to enhance the production of ROS in cancer cells. Therefore, inducing an increase in levels of ROS levels is considered a viable strategy for combating cancer.

Based on this, numerous tumor treatment methods have emerged that leverage the excessive ROS production within tumor tissues. Among these, photodynamic therapy (PDT), sonodynamic therapy (SDT), and chemodynamic therapy (CDT) are gaining increasing attention in tumor treatment as non-invasive therapies. Compared to chemotherapy-activated ROS, these therapeutic strategies offer several advantages: PDT and SDT utilize excitation light or ultrasound (US) as the activation source, stimulating photosensitizers (PS) or sound sensitizers to generate ROS. CDT relies on the in situ generation of ROS through Fenton and Fenton-like reactions between iron or other metal ions and the overexpressed H2O2 in the mildly acidic TME. Compared to ROS activated by chemotherapeutic drugs, these non-invasive therapies have become a novel anti-tumor approach because their efficacy is confined to tumor regions containing sensitizers and catalysts, thereby minimizing damage to healthy tissues. Despite the significant progress these therapies have made in cancer treatment, it is important to note that sensitizers or catalysts must be precisely delivered to the tumor site to ensure that the ROS levels generated within tumor cells remain above the toxic threshold. This remains a key challenge in further enhancing the anti-tumor efficacy of ROS-based therapies. On the other hand, the effectiveness of ROS-based therapies is also influenced by the TME. Firstly, the generation of ROS is oxygen-dependent, and hypoxia is a hallmark of the tumor microenvironment. In particular, the O2 consumption during PDT and SDT may exacerbate tumor hypoxia, thereby diminishing the therapeutic efficacy. Additionally, GSH is abundantly expressed within the tumor and functions as a potent reducing agent capable of counteracting the ROS generated, thereby attenuating the anti-tumor efficacy. While CDT is directly triggered by the endogenous chemical energy of the TME, the acidity of the TME is significantly higher than the optimal pH for the Fenton reaction, leading to a reduced reaction rate. Additionally, the endogenous H2O2 levels in tumors are insufficient to sustain continuous •OH generation. In addition to the TME, various non-invasive therapies are also influenced by their inherent mechanisms of action. For instance, conventional PDT relies on ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) light as the excitation source. However, the limited penetration of UV–Vis light into deep tissues poses a challenge. Therefore, selecting a light source with deeper tissue penetration is crucial for enhancing the efficacy of PDT. Sonosensitizers play a crucial role in the process of SDT, however, the current sonosensitizers produce insufficient ROS, which limits the antitumor efficacy of SDT. In Fenton or Fenton-like reactions, •OH is generated through the catalytic reaction of H2O2 with low-valent metal ions, which are easily oxidized to high-valent states. However, the conversion rate from high-valent to low-valent metal ions is slow, significantly limiting the performance of the catalyst and thus hindering the efficiency of ROS generation in CDT.

In recent years, with the advancement of nanomedicine and nanobiotechnology, combining these technologies with traditional ROS-based non-invasive therapies has provided unprecedented opportunities to address the challenges currently faced by ROS-based non-invasive treatments. Nanocarriers can deliver sonosensitizers or photosensitizers specifically to tumor tissues, precisely regulating ROS generation, thereby enabling tumor-specific therapy. Notably, to overcome the challenges posed by the TME, O2 carrying nanoparticles (NPs) and O2-generating NPs have been designed to address hypoxia, while redox-sensitive NPs have been developed to deplete the high levels of GSH in tumor cells. Additionally, through rational design, NIR light or X-ray responsive nanoplatforms have been developed to enhance PDT for deep-seated tumors, novel inorganic sonosensitizers have been created to improve SDT efficiency, and the development of nano-catalysts for increasing H2O2 level, or pH independent or with high catalytic efficiency to enhance CDT efficiency efficacy. In summary, the functionality of these various NPs is aimed at increasing ROS production within tumor cells to enhance the cytotoxic effects of these non-invasive therapies.

Although numerous reviews have examined the regulation of ROS production by nanomaterials in cancer therapy, most of them are about one therapy, or based on one particular nanomaterial, and a comprehensive analyses focusing on the underlying mechanisms, current challenges, and enhancement strategies for each therapeutic approach is lacking. Given the importance of strengthening ROS-based therapies in cancer treatment, this review summarizes the ROS generation mechanisms for each non-invasive therapy, as well as the current obstacles to their therapeutic efficacy, and then focuses on the enhancement strategies for these therapies based on NP systems (Fig. 1). Additionally, the importance of modulating the TME to enhance these therapies is summarized (Fig. 2), including overcoming tumor hypoxia, adjusting GSH levels, increasing H2O2 levels, and lowering tumor pH, among other strategies. Finally, the current challenges and possible solutions for the clinical translation of ROS-based non-invasive therapies are discussed.

ROS enhancement in cancer therapy

In tumors, low levels of ROS are involved in multiple signaling pathways promoting tumor development. However, dysregulation of the balance between excessive ROS production and cellular antioxidant defenses leads to oxidative stress. This stress can damage essential cellular components, including DNA, proteins, and lipids, thereby exerting cytotoxic effects. Importantly, due to the high basic ROS levels in cancer cells, they are more susceptible than normal cells to further increases in intracellular ROS levels. Therefore, the anti-tumor potential generated by inducing ROS has attracted significant interest. Despite the potential of chemotherapy drugs to induce ROS, their application has been constrained by suboptimal patient tolerance and relatively insufficient levels of ROS induction. Novel approaches including non-invasive therapies such as PDT, SDT, and CDT have been developed to improve anti-tumor efficiency by generating large amounts of ROS as death inducers to induce tumor cell death, thus showing considerable promise in anti-tumor. This section provides an overview of the relationship between ROS enhancement and tumor therapy using the common modes of death in PDT, SDT, and CDT that are closely related to ROS, including apoptosis, ferroptosis, pyroptosis, and immunogenic cell death (ICD) as example (Fig. 3).

Apoptosis

Apoptosis is an autonomous, orderly process of death that occurs in cells. Morphologically, apoptosis is characterized by cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation, nuclear fragmentation, and the formation of plasma membrane blebs. Elevated ROS can mediate apoptosis via the intrinsic mitochondrial pathway or the extrinsic death receptor pathway. Exogenous apoptosis is triggered by the binding of death ligands such as FasL, TRAIL to death receptors. Ligand binding leads to trimerization and aggregation of the death receptor, which then recruits Fas-associated death domains (FADDs) via death domains (DDs). FADDs, through their death effector domains (DEDs), recruit initiator caspase proteins such as procaspase 8 to form the death-inducible signaling complex (DISC). Formation of DISC activates caspase-8, which in turn triggers a downstream caspase cascade leading to apoptosis. Furthermore, caspase-8 also cleaves the pro-apoptotic B-cell lymphoma protein 2 (Bcl-2) family member Bid into tBid, thereby activating the mitochondria-mediated intrinsic apoptotic pathway. Initiator caspase activation in the exogenous pathway can be negatively regulated by the FADD like intedeukin-1-β converting enzyme inhibitory protein (c-FLIP). c-FLIP expression is up-regulated in a variety of tumors and leads to resistance to apoptosis in cancer cells by competing with procaspase-8 for binding to the adaptor protein FADD. And several studies have confirmed that ROS can downregulate the expression of c-FLIP, which enhances tumor cell apoptosis. Pretreatment with the ROS scavenger N-acetylcysteine (NAC) effectively prevented c-FLIP protein loss and inhibited cells from undergoing apoptosis. These results indicate the important role of ROS in inducing extrinsic apoptosis.

Currently, ROS-dependent mitochondrial apoptotic pathways are more commonly observed in PDT, SDT, and CDT. Excess ROS cause oxidative damage to mitochondrial proteins and DNA, leading to mitochondrial outer membrane permeability (MOMP) and cytochrome C release. Subsequently, cytochrome C binds to the bridging molecule apoptotic peptidase-activating factor 1 (APAF1) to form the apoptosome, which promotes caspase 9 activation, and then activates the caspase cascade reaction, leading to apoptosis. In addition, ROS modulate members of the Bcl-2 family, which play a key role in enhancing MOMP.

Ferroptosis

Ferroptosis is a form of regulated cell death characterized by iron-dependent lipid ROS accumulation. Morphologically, ferroptosis is typified by pronounced mitochondrial shrinkage, increased membrane density, and the reduction or loss of mitochondrial cristae. It is generally accepted that the upstream signaling pathways leading to ROS accumulation in ferroptosis are mainly divided into the cystine glutamate transporter (System Xc-)/Glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) axis and the iron axis.

The presence of Fe2+ ions plays a crucial role in ferroptosis, and Fe2+ can trigger a Fenton reaction with endogenous H2O2: Fe2+ + H2O2→ Fe3+ + OH− + •OH. The resulting •OH triggers a chain reaction of free radicals leading to oxidative modifications of cell membrane phospholipids that leading to cell membrane rupture and subsequent ferroptosis. Various iron-based nanomaterials, based on the Fenton reaction and ferroptosis, have been used in studies of cancer therapy, demonstrating great potential in this field. System Xc- is a cystine/glutamate exchange transporter composed of a light chain subunit (SLC7A11) and a heavy chain subunit (SLC3A2). System Xc- exchanges glutamate for cystine, which can be reduced to cysteine for GSH biosynthesis. GPX4 is a glutathione peroxidase that attenuates lipid peroxidation and inhibits the occurrence of ferroptosis in cells by depleting GSH. Consequently, by inhibiting the System Xc-, blocking GSH synthesis, and suppressing GPX4 activity, ROS accumulation can be induced, thereby promoting ferroptosis and eradication of tumors in vivo.

Pyroptosis

Pyroptosis is a form of programmed cell death triggered by the activation of inflammasomes. It is characterized by cell swelling to the point of rupture of the plasma membrane and release of contents. Caspase-mediated cleavage of gasdermin (GSDM) family members, specifically GSDMD and GSDME, plays a crucial role in the initiation of pyroptosis. In the canonical inflammasome pathway, caspase-1 is activated by various classical inflammasomes, such as NLRP3, NLRP1, and NAIP-NLRC4. Upon activation, caspase-1 cleaves GSDMD, leading to the oligomerization of its N-terminal domain, which forms pores in the cell membrane and ultimately results in cell membrane rupture. The non-canonical inflammasome pathway is not dependent on the classical inflammasome, caspase 4/5/11 is directly activated by lipopolysaccharide and cleaves GSDMD to promote cellular pyroptosis. Chemotherapy drugs can induce apoptosis mediated by caspase-3. If the target cells highly express GSDME, GSDME can be cleaved by caspase3, thereby transforming non-inflammatory apoptosis into pyroptosis. In addition, Granzyme B (GZMB) can directly cleave GSDME and activate non-caspase-dependent pyroptosis in target cells.

In this regard, it has been shown that ROS can mediate NLRP3 activation, which further activates the Caspase1/GSDM axis to induce pyroptosis. In addition, Peng et al. developed a photosensitizer with aggregation-induced emission properties. Under white light irradiation, it can generate a large amount of ROS in mitochondria, activate the expression of caspase-3, and subsequently cleave GSDME to produce the GSDME-N domain, leading to membrane perforation and the pyroptotic cell death. In vivo studies have shown that this ROS-mediated pyroptosis has the ability to eradicate tumors, with tumor suppression reaching approximately 90 %.

Immunogenic cell death (ICD) is a type of cell death that can trigger an immune response. The tumor cells undergoing ICD could release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and tumor-associated antigens (TAAs). Calreticulin (CRT), high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) are the most characteristic hallmarks of DAMPs. These DAMPs initiate host anti-tumor adaptive immune responses by stimulating antigen presentation by dendritic cells (DCs) and the proliferation of cytotoxic T lymphocytes.

ROS and endoplasmic reticulum stress (ERS) are thought to be strongly associated with ICD. Endoplasmic reticulum is a central organelle for the synthesis, folding, and modification of secreted and transmembrane proteins. In a variety of cellular events, such as hypoxia, inadequate glucose supply, dysregulation of calcium homeostasis, and oxidative stress, these factors interfere with the protein folding capacity of the endoplasmic reticulum, which can lead to endoplasmic reticulum stress. Depending on whether they act directly on the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), ICD inducers are categorized into type I and type II. Type I ICD inducers do not directly correlate with ER stress and cause endoplasmic reticulum stress effects through secondary or indirect mechanisms. Type II inducers selectively target the endoplasmic reticulum and trigger ICD via the ROS-mediated ERS signaling pathway. ROS-mediated endoplasmic reticulum stress is thought to be more efficient due to the ability of ROS-mediated ERS-triggered ICD to increase the release of DAMPs, reduce the transport of DAMP molecules complexed with non-DAMP molecules, and simplify DAMP transport. In recent years, an increasing body of research has demonstrated that PDT, SDT, and CDT elicit ICD through the generation of ROS, consequently inhibiting tumor progression.

In summary, when ROS is elevated acts as an inducer of cell death and triggers tumor cell death through various pathways. In this regard, ROS-based non-invasive therapies, which cause cancer-specific cell death by generating ROS, have shown promising results. The type of cell death may vary depending on the treatment, cell type, and environment. In conclusion, inducing ROS generation in tumor tissues to promote tumor cell death can significantly enhance the therapeutic effect of solid tumors, showing considerable potential in the field of tumor therapy.

Photodynamic therapy (PDT)

Mechanism of PDT

PDT is a light-based treatment method that has been approved for clinical use in the treatment of various malignant tumors. PS, light and molecular oxygen are the three necessary conditions for PDT. When PSs are exposed to light of a specific wavelength, they transition from the ground state to an excited singlet state (1PS∗). The 1PS∗ is inherently unstable and can revert to the ground state via the dissipation of energy either as light (fluorescence) or heat. Alternatively, it may undergo intersystem crossing to form a more stable triplet excited state (3PS∗). On the one hand, 3PS∗ can return to the ground state through a slow phosphorescence process. Importantly, the 3PS∗ can transfer energy to oxygen molecules to generate ROS through two reaction processes (Type I and Type II). In type I reactions, 3PS∗ participates in a hydrogen or electron transfer process that reacts with biological substrates to form free radicals, which can then interact with oxygen and generate ROS, such as O2•−, •OH, and H2O2. In Type II reactions, the 3PS∗ directly transfers energy to triplet oxygen molecules, generating highly toxic 1O2. Typically, both Type I and Type II reactions can occur simultaneously, with their relative proportions primarily determined by the type of PS, substrate concentration, oxygen availability, and the binding affinity between the sensitizer and the substrate. However, most experimental studies have shown that type II reactions play a dominant role in PDT.

Development of photosensitizers

The photochemical properties of PS are crucial for PDT efficacy. Based on their chemical structures and characteristics, PS can typically be classified into three generations. Hematoporphyrin derivatives (HpD) represent the first generation PSs. In 1976, Kelly et al. first applied HpD to the PDT treatment of human bladder cancer, which laid the foundation for extensive research on PDT. HpD has been commercialized as Photofrin®, the first clinically approved PS for PDT. However, the poor chemical purity and significant skin photosensitivity of Photofrin® limit its clinical application. These shortcomings of the first generation PS prompted people to explore new PS, leading to the development of second-generation PS. Currently, the second generation PSs mainly include phthalocyanines, phenothiazines, chlorins, xanthines, and some indocyanine dyes. Compared to the first generation PS, the second generation PS exhibits significant improvements in purity, photosensitivity, and tissue selectivity. However, their main shortcomings are poor water solubility and inadequate tumor targeting. In addition, ROS exhibit a short half-life (<0.04 μs) and a limited action radius (<0.02 μm), meaning that only cells in close proximity to the activated PS can be affected. Consequently, to improve the accumulation of PS in tumor, researchers are focusing on the development of third generation PS utilizing nanotechnology, including organic nanocarrier and inorganic nanocarrier. Organic nanocarrier primarily consist of organic compounds, including liposomes and polymer nanoparticles. These carriers are capable of delivering multiple types of PS and exhibit excellent biocompatibility and biosafety. Inorganic nanocarriers primarily consist of inorganic materials, such as silica nanoparticles, gold nanoparticles, metal oxides and carbon nanomaterials. These materials exhibit distinctive optical properties, a porous structure, and high stability. Several inorganic nanomaterials, such as TiO2, ZnO are also employed directly as PS due to their special light absorption properties.

Despite the demonstrated effectiveness of NPs-based PDT in both in vitro and in vivo experiments, several challenges remain. Notably, these include: 1) The limited penetration depth of light into tissues, which compromises the therapeutic efficacy of PDT for deep tumors. 2) PDT exhibits a high dependence on oxygen. However, the hypoxic conditions at the tumor site, coupled with oxygen consumption during PDT, result in inadequate PS activation. These limitations significantly restrict the efficacy and application of PDT. Consequently, various strategies have been devised to enhance deep tumor therapy and improve oxygen delivery. Strategies for overcoming hypoxia will be elaborated in detail in the subsequent sections. This section specifically focuses on enhancing penetration for PDT. Enhancement strategies of PDT

In conventional PDT, PS typically utilize ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) light with short wavelengths, which falls outside the first biological transparency window (650–950 nm),resulting in a very low penetration depth of visible light (<3 mm). Therefore, its limited tissue penetration has become a major weakness that restricts the effectiveness of PDT in treating deep tumors below the skin. To overcome the problem of weak light penetration in PDT, researchers have tried various methods, including near-infrared fluorescence (NIR) and X-ray radiation. NIR light-excited PDT

The wavelength of NIR laser light typically ranges from 700 to 1000 nm, which is commonly regarded as the optical transparency window for biological tissues. This allows for deeper tissue penetration, reaching depths of 10–15 mm. In recent years, significant progress has been made in NIR-mediated PDT research, including the application of NIR-activated PSs and the development of NIR-excited upconversion NPs (UCNPs) and two-photon excitation photodynamic nanosystems.

PS directly excited by NIR light

NIR-responsive PSs can directly absorb low-energy NIR photons and generate singlet oxygen through photochemical reactions. Anthocyanin dye indocyanine green (ICG) exhibits strong absorbance at approximately 800 nm and is an FDA-approved NIR fluorescent dye. Furthermore, in the presence of NIR, ICG is capable of converting light energy into heat. Therefore, ICG is frequently used in the integration of photothermal therapy (PTT) and PDT for tumor treatment. To enhance the blood stability of ICG, Wang et al. loaded ICG into a tumor-targeting peptide-modified polyethylene glycol-polylactic acid-glycolic acid (PEG-PLGA) nanomaterial. This TMVP1-ICG-NPs improved both the blood stability and targeting ability of ICG towards tumors. The tumor growth was significantly inhibited under the irradiation of a near-infrared laser, effectively realizing PTT and PDT to destroy the tumor. Furthermore, several other organic PS, such as Ce6 and BODIPY, are also capable of absorbing infrared light for PDT therapy. In a recent study, Bonelli et al. developed a novel PS with NIR absorption based on the conjugation of zinc phthalocyanine (ZnPc) to a cyclo metataled Ir(III) complex,and then encapsulated this conjugate (Ir-ZnPc) into polyurethane-polyurea hybrid nanocapsules (NCs). Under NIR irradiation, Ir-ZnPc exhibited enhanced ROS generation compared to ZnPc alone, which might be attributed to the interaction involving excited-state electron transfer between the active iridium complex and ZnPc. In addition, the encapsulation of the nanocarriers enhanced the photostability and uptake of the conjugates in tumor cells. In vitro experiments on 3D cell models confirmed that the nanocarrier-wrapped conjugates induced ROS-mediated cancer cell death under red light irradiation. The strategy of conjugating organic PS with transition metal coordination provides a new idea for the development of novel NIR PS.

Currently, more research is focused on the development of inorganic PS that exhibit NIR absorption. In 2014, Hwang et al. discovered for the first time that gold nanorods (Au NRs) can be photosensitized by very low power density single-photon near-infrared laser irradiation without the addition of organic photosensitizers, initiating significant PDT effects. They demonstrated that Au NRs only excite the PDT effect when exposed to near-infrared light (875–1100 nm), but do not respond to Vis light or short NIR light. When HeLa cells were treated with Au NRs, the number of cell deaths under 940 nm infrared light irradiation was about 10 times higher than the number of cell deaths induced by 550 nm light. This is because Au NRs mediates the PDT effect at 940 nm excitation, producing a large amount of 1O2 leading to cell death, while cell death at 550 nm light irradiation was only caused by the PTT effect. The same results were also confirmed in vivo when Au NRs were injected into tumor sites in mice. The PDT effects mediated by Au NRs completely destroyed tumors, which was significantly superior to the effects of conventional anti-cancer drugs (doxorubicin).

In recent years, some other emerging NIR semiconductor materials have shown promising application prospects in the field of PDT due to their superior optical properties. Zhao et al. reported the use of spherical BiAgOS-PEG NPs for NIR light-triggered combined PDT and PTT (Fig. 4A). Under 808 nm near-infrared laser irradiation, BiAgOS-PEG successfully generated heat and induced ROS production within tumor cells. As expected, the BiAgOS-PEG + 808 nm group significantly inhibited tumor growth in vivo. In another study, Sheng et al. developed 65 nm dopamine-coated tungsten oxide nanodots (WO₃-x/Dpa-Mel NPs). The dopamine coating enhanced the stability and biocompatibility of WO₃-x/Dpa-Mel NPs in vivo. Under 808 nm laser irradiation, WO₃-x/Dpa-Mel NPs exhibited photothermal stability and ROS generation. In 4T1 mouse breast cancer tumor model, compared to the groups treated with either the 808 nm laser or WO₃-x/Dpa-Mel NPs alone, the WO₃-x/Dpa-Mel NPs + 808 nm laser treatment group showed significant tumor ablation within 14 days. In addition, Veeranarayanan et al. developed a copper bismuth sulfide (Cu3BiS3) NCs, which exhibited significant activity against human breast cancer cells (MCF-7) at ultra-low dose NIR laser power, demonstrating their potential as NIR-PDT responsive nanomaterials.

NIR light excited upconversion NPs (UCNPs)

To date, only a small fraction of PSs have demonstrated PDT effects under NIR light excitation. To overcome this limitation, phototransducers have been employed as energy amplifiers. These transducers can absorb the energy of NIR light and emit UV–Vis light, thereby activating nearby PSs and generating ROS through a fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) mechanism. Among these, UCNPs have emerged as a promising class of NIR phototransducers. By conjugating with PSs, these PSs can be indirectly activated by NIR light, enabling NIR-excited PDT nanoplatforms that address the issue of limited tissue penetration depth.

UCNPs are typically lanthanide-doped nanocrystals, and physical adsorption is the main strategy for UCNP binding to PS. In 2007, Zhang and co-authors first applied UCNPs to PDT excited by NIR light. The system was constructed by coating the UCNPs with a silica layer and doping the photosensitive molecule Merocyanine 540 (M-540) onto the silica-coated surface. Photoluminescence spectra confirmed that the silica coating will not influence the performance of UCNPs' photon conversion. M-540 could be activated by the red luminescent emission of UCNP to produce 1O2, which exhibited a strong photodynamic cell killing effect on MCF-7/AZ breast cancer cells. In another report, Jin et al. used an amphiphilic polymer to encapsulse a luminogen (2-(2,6-bis((E)-4-(phenyl(40-(1,2,2-triphenylvinyl)-[1,10-biphenyl]-4-yl)amino)styryl)-4H-pyran-4-ylidene)malononitrile, TTD) with aggregation-induced emission (AIEgen) characteristics and UCNPs, and further modified with cycloarginine-glycine-aspartic acid (cRGD) to obtain a nanoplatform (UCNP@TTD-cRGD). The emission from the UCNPs under NIR excitation was well-matched with the absorption of TTD, ensuring efficient upconversion energy transfer. Under 980 nm laser irradiation, the 1O2 quantum yield of UCNP@TTD-cRGD NPs reached 36.4 %. Although the 1O2 quantum yield under white light illumination was as high as 63.7 %, placing a 6 mm thick chicken tissue between the laser source and the UCNP@TTD-cRGD NPs resulted in negligible 1O2 generation under white light. In contrast, 1O2 production was still detectable under 980 nm irradiation. These findings suggest that 980 nm excitation offers superior tissue penetration compared to white light. Additionally, the PEG coating and cRGD conferred prolonged circulation time and active tumor targeting capabilities to the UCNP@TTD-cRGD NPs. The in vivo PDT result showed that the UCNP@TTD-cRGD NPs exhibited significant tumor regression in the breast cancer mouse model under 980 nm light irradiation. The above studies clearly demonstrated the great potential of UCNP-PS nanocomposites as deep PDT reagents.

Compared to the physical adsorption, the covalent conjugation of PS to UCNPs can reduce the distance between PS and UCNPs. This enables direct transfer of red light emission from UCNP to PS through FRET, resulting in a considerable efficiency. For instance, Liang et al. covalently linked a biodegradable photosensitive protein (KillerRed) to UCNP, resulting in the formation of nanocomposites (KillerRed-UCNPs). The researchers initially compared KillerRed with UCNP using physical adsorption and covalent coupling, and discovered that covalent coupling exhibited superior binding efficiency and stability. Interestingly, the researchers also observed the tissue penetration capacity of 980 nm light. When a 5 mm or 10 mm thick pork tissue was placed on top of the cells, it was observed that ROS generation under the 586 nm laser source decreased by more than 80 %. In contrast, ROS induced by 980 nm near-infrared irradiation decreased by only 13 % and 21 %, respectively. Cytotoxicity assays revealed that, under 980 nm near-infrared excitation, KillerRed-UCNPs retained over 60 % of their therapeutic efficacy even with a 10 mm tissue barrier, whereas the cytotoxicity under the 586 nm laser was significantly reduced to 7 % due to the blocking effect of the tissue. Recently, Guo et al. developed composite NPs (UCNPs-CPs) by coordinating a copolymer (PPVBT-COOH) with PDT effects and a conjugated polymer with PTT effects with UCNPs (Fig. 4B). In this system, UCNPs act as energy transducers. Under 980 nm laser irradiation, UCNPs facilitated energy transfer to adjacent PPVBT-COOH, thereby generating ROS that induced PDT. Moreover, under 808 nm laser irradiation, the UCNPs-CPs generated heat, triggering the PTT effect. In vivo studies demonstrated that under simultaneous irradiation with both NIR light, the UCNPs-CPs achieved a 100 % tumor inhibition rate through the synergistic effects of PDT and PTT.

Additionally, several inorganic PSs have also been combined with UCNPs for deep PDT triggered by NIR light. A common approach is to coat these nanomaterials on the surface of UCNPs, which ensures maximum energy transfer while also addressing issues related to PS leakage and low loading efficiency. For instance, a TiO2-coated core/shell nanocomposite of UCNPs (UCNPs@TiO2 NCs) was developed as a photosensitizer activated by NIR light. In this system, the TiO2 shell was directly coated onto the surface of the UCNPs, ensuring maximum transfer efficiency of fluorescence resonance energy from the UCNPs to TiO2. The effect of PDT was observed using HeLa cells as the research object. Intracellular ROS increased with the increase in irradiation time under 980 nm light. The in vivo PDT results showed that the injection group UCNPs@TiO2 under 980 nm laser irradiation exhibited better tumor inhibition compared to the group irradiated with 365 nm light, which was attributed to the deeper tissue penetration of NIR light. Recently, Chu et al. prepared AgBiS2-coated Nd3+ sensitized upconversion NPs (UCNPs@AgBiS2) (Fig. 5A). Due to the efficient cross-relaxation pathway between Nd3+ ions and AgBiS2, UCNPs@AgBiS2 exhibited excellent photothermal properties, which could be tuned between 14.7 % and 45.0 % by adjusting the concentration of Nd3+ ions. Furthermore, when the doping concentration of Nd3+ ions in the core was 1 %, the UCNPs displayed enhanced upconversion emission. Under 808 nm NIR excitation, the UCNPs and AgBiS2 achieved high FRET efficiency. In 4T1 cells, UCNPs@AgBiS2 effectively converted 808 nm light into higher-energy Vis light to excite AgBiS2, thereby increasing ROS production. Thus, UCNPs@AgBiS2 effectively suppressed tumor development by realizing the combination of PTT and PDT.

The remarkable optical properties of UCNPs exhibit significant potential for deep-PDT. However, their transfer to aqueous media frequently results in substantial quenching. Thermal bursting is one of the main factors in upconversion quenching, which occurs because elevated temperatures promote accelerated non-radiative decay of phononactivity, leading to the quenching of UCNPs.The strategy to enhance the luminescence of UCNPs by inducing a low-temperature environment to suppress thermal quenching has been proposed recently. Molkenova et al. prepared UCNPs coated with silica shells, subsequently loaded with Ce6, finally functionalized with HA, and named UCNPs@SiO2–Ce6-HA. Compared with room temperature, low-temperature treatment enhanced the luminescence of UCNPs@SiO2 by 2.45 times and increased the 1O2 generation by UCNPs@SiO2–Ce6-HA under NIR excitation by 3.15 times. Subsequent in vivo anti-tumor results in mice further validated the enhanced effect of the low-temperature condition. In this condition, the tumor inhibition rate in the Cryo + Laser + UCNPs@SiO2–Ce6-HA group was 79 %, which was higher than that in the Laser + UCNPs@SiO2–Ce6-HA group (55 %) and the Cryo + UCNPs@SiO2–Ce6-HA group (17 %).

Two-photon-excited PDT (TPE-PDT)

Another strategy for NIR light as an excitation source in PDT is two-photon excitation photodynamic therapy (TPE-PDT). TPE is a nonlinear optical process in which two photons, either of the same or different frequencies, are simultaneously absorbed, resulting in the excitation of a molecule from its ground state to a higher-energy electronic state. Due to the quadratic dependence of TPE on laser intensity, PS can be effectively excited only at the focal point of the laser beam. This allows TPE-PDT to achieve higher spatial precision, thereby reducing damage to surrounding healthy tissues. It is important to note that this specialized process requires the use of short-pulsed femtosecond NIR lasers to initiate the excitation. A high two-photon absorption (TPA) cross-section is a critical requirement for effective TPE-PDT. However, traditional PS typically exhibit low TPA cross-sections. For example, the two-photon absorption cross-sections of tetraphenylporphyrin and porphyrins are generally below 20 GM. Currently, strategies to achieve large intrinsic TPA cross-sections in the NIR region include the design of novel TPA PS. Another approach involves the use of TPA nanomaterials that either complex with the surface of existing PSs or encapsulate PSs within their internal cavities. In this case, the TPA nanomaterials act as energy donors, indirectly exciting the PSs for TPE-PDT.

The study by Wang et al. provides an excellent example of TPE-PDT. They synthesized gold nanobipyramids (GBPs) for TPA and further conjugated sulfonated Al-phthalocyanine (AlPcS) on their surface as the PS, creating nanoconjugates (GBP-AlPcS) for TPE-PDT. Upon two-photon irradiation with femtosecond laser pulses at 800 nm, the strong plasmon resonance of the GBP core amplified the local electric field, enhancing the two-photon fluorescence emission of the attached AlPcS by 16-fold, which subsequently increased the production of 1O2 in TPE. In vitro TPE-PDT experiments conducted on cultured HeLa cells showed that GBP-AlPcS achieved an approximately 80 % cancer cell death rate under two-photon irradiation, which was significantly higher than the cell-killing rate of free AlPcS under the same conditions (less than 10 %). Following this, in vivo TPE-PDT experiments were performed on HeLa tumor-bearing nude mice, revealing that tumor growth was unaffected by PBS or free AlPcS under 800 nm two-photon laser irradiation, whereas GBP-AlPcS significantly inhibited tumor growth. Additionally, the authors compared gold nanospheres (GNS) and nanorods (GNR) for enhancing the two-photon excited fluorescence of PS, but the effects were inferior to those of GBPs, likely due to differences in shape. Similarly, Soleimany et al. utilized riboflavin-conjugated N,S-doped graphene quantum dot (Rf–N,S-GQD) as a two-photon photosensitizer and subsequently electrostatically adsorbed them onto the surface of thiolated chitosan-coated gold nanostars (AuNS-TCS) to develop a nanohybrid system (AuNS@GQDs). Given two-photon laser irradiation, an enhanced ROS-generating effect of AuNS@GQDs was observed in both 2D and 3D cell models compared to the individual Rf N,S-GQDs. This is attributed to the plasmonic effect of AuNS, which enhances the two-photon absorption cross section of the photosensitizer. In addition, due to the photothermal effect of AuNS, AuNS@GQDs achieved combined PDT/PTT treatment, and effectively disrupted the structure of 3D cell cultures.

In addition, some PS-based self-assembled NPs showed a larger TPA cross-section. In a recent study, a natural two-photon-excited photosensitizer, emodin, was developed and co-assembled with human serum albumin (HSA) to form nanoplatforms (E/H NPs). This system exhibited a TPA cross-section of 4.02 × 107 GM. Additionally, E/H NPs achieved a two-photon imaging depth exceeding 1500 μm in simulated tissue, significantly surpassing the imaging depth of approximately 200 μm under 488 nm single-photon excitation. To demonstrate the reliability of E/H NPs as two-photon energy donors for inducing PDT effects, MCF-7 cells were used as a model. After two-photon laser irradiation, cells treated with E/H NPs displayed intense ROS fluorescence. When the NPs were intravenously injected into MCF-7 tumor-bearing nude mice, the tumors in the laser-irradiated group were completely ablated within 14 days, compared to the non-irradiated group.

X-ray-excited PDT (X-PDT)

The high permeability of X-rays is able to reach internal tumors without depth limitation, making it an ideal excitation source for PDT to treat deep tumors. However, the energy of X-rays is too high, ranging from hundreds to kiloelectron volts, to activate most conventional PSs. Therefore, X-PDT requires an energy converter to convert the absorbed X-ray energy into photons of appropriate wavelengths, which are absorbed by well-matched photosensitizers, which are instantaneously activated by a FRET process to produce ROS. These sensors, commonly known as scintillators, exhibit X-ray excited optical luminescence. Since Chen et al. first proposed the concept of NPs-mediated X-PDT in 2006, researchers have combined numerous nanoscintillation materials with photosensitizers and confirmed the feasibility of X-PDT in tumor treatment. High doses of X-rays (>5.0 Gy) inevitably cause radioactive damage to normal tissues. Therefore the use of low radiation doses while maintaining high therapeutic efficiency aspects seems to be the focus of X-PDT research at this stage. High-Z elements strongly absorb or scatter X-ray photons as a radiation reduction strategy and are widely used as dopants or host ions in nanoscintillator.

In one study, Sun et al. doped high-Z elements Zn and Mn into a silicate scintillator, which was then conjugated with the photosensitizer Rose Bengal (RB) and further modified with RGD peptides, resulting in tumor-targeting nanosensitizers (RGD-ZSM-RB NSs) (Fig. 5B). These nanosensitizers were used as transducers to convert low-dose X-rays into visible light (peaking at 570 nm), which overlaps well with the absorption spectrum of RB (480–550 nm). As a result, under low-dose X-ray irradiation (1.0 Gy), the RGD-ZSM-RB NSs generated a strong ROS fluorescence signal, significantly inhibiting tumor cell viability compared to the RGD-ZSM-RB NSs group without X-ray exposure. Similarly, when intravenously injected into U87MG tumor-bearing mice, the RGD-ZSM-RB NSs combined with X-ray treatment achieved a tumor inhibition rate of 98.1 % after 14 days, compared to the control group. Notably, even when a 1 cm piece of pork tissue was placed between the tumor and the X-ray source, significant antitumor effects were still observed. Throughout the treatment period, no changes in body weight or signs of systemic toxicity were detected, indicating good biocompatibility.

Persistent luminescent nanoscintillators (PLNPs) have attracted attention for their ability to emit light persistently from minutes to days after discontinuation of X-ray irradiation, making them promising for the development of low-dose X-rays. Song et al. doped tungsten atoms W atoms (VI) into ZnGa2O4, which was then conjugated with the photosensitizer Zn(II) phthalocyanine tetrasulfonic acid (ZnPcS4), resulting in the PDT treatment platform ZGO:Cr/W-ZnPcS4 PLNPs. The co-doping with W atoms modulated the trap density and depth of ZGO:Cr/W and enhanced the X-ray photon absorption efficiency, enabling ZGO:Cr/W PLNPs to exhibit persistent luminescence for more than 6 h. Under X-ray irradiation, ZGO/W-ZnPcS4 generated a substantial amount of ROS in HeLa cells, leading to a decrease in cell viability. Similarly, in an S180 tumor-bearing mouse model, an ultra-low radiation dose of 0.18 Gy effectively activated X-PDT and significantly inhibited tumor progression.

In the traditional process of X-ray induced photodynamic therapy, the energy transfer between the scintillator and the photosensitizer inevitably leads to energy loss from the X-ray irradiation, thereby reducing the performance of X-PDT. Therefore, it is particularly important to avoid or reduce the energy transfer loss between the scintillator and the photosensitizer. Maiti et al. developed a terbium (Tb)-RB coordination nanocrystals (T-RBNs) for low-dose X-PDT. The researchers chosen the [RB]/[Tb] molar ratio of 3 as the optimal formulation for the preparation of T-RBNs because it ensured more efficient energy transfer from Tb3+ to RB. The results indicated that under the irradiation of 0.5 Gy X-rays, T-RBNs induced the death of 4T1-luc cells by generating ROS, demonstrating excellent X-PDT effect.

At the same time, nanomaterials that serve as both nanoscintillators and photosensitizers have increased the potential to improve the efficiency of X-ray activated PDT. For instance, Wang et al. reported that based on pure organic phosphorescent nanoscintillators can be directly excited by X-rays to produce large amounts of 1O2. They validated the PDT effect of scintillator nanoparticles in vitro using 4T1 mouse breast cancer cells, and when co-incubated with 4T1 cells, only X-ray irradiated scintillator nanoparticles induced cytotoxicity. Even with a 3.5 cm thick layer of chicken breast covering the cells, good therapeutic effects were achieved. In contrast, the traditional PDT effect of the C6, when excited under white light, was weakened even with just 1 cm of chicken breast. In vivo, the NPs effectively inhibited tumor growth at a very low X-ray dose (0.4 Gy). In addition, Chuang et al. used a yttrium oxide nanoscintillator core and a silica shell to form an efficient nanoscintillator and photosensitizer (Y2O3:Eu@SiO2) that can be directly excited by X-rays for in vivo use in simultaneous imaging and PDT.

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT)

Mechanism of SDT

In 1989, Yumita et al. first discovered that hematoporphyrin could induce tumor cell death under ultrasound and named this treatment modality SDT. SDT utilizes US as an external trigger source capable of generating ROS to induce cell death when they activate acoustic sensitizers. US is a mechanical wave that vibrates periodically at a frequency greater than 20 kHz in a continuous medium, allowing for deep tissue penetration capabilities of approximately 10 cm. Therefore, SDT has higher penetration compared to PDT. While the precise mechanism of ROS generation through SDT remains to be fully elucidated, it is currently acknowledged that the mechanism is based on the cavitation effect, involving pyrolysis and sonoluminescence. Cavitation is a complex phenomenon that arises when US interacts with the aqueous environment. This process encompasses three stages: nucleation of bubbles, growth of bubbles, and bubble collapse. Cavitation can be categorized into inertial cavitation and stable cavitation. Notably, inertial cavitation encompasses the processes of pyrolysis and sonoluminescence. Inertial cavitation is initiated when US intensity surpasses a specific pressure threshold, causing bubbles to expand beyond their resonant size. This expansion leads to bubble instability, culminating in collapse and implosion. Consequently, an extreme microenvironment characterized by temperatures exceeding 10,000 K and pressures surpassing 81 MPa is generated. This extreme temperature and pressure are considered to be an sono-chemical reactor that can explain ROS production through two mechanisms (Fig. 6).

The first mechanism is pyrolysis, and it has been suggested that during inertial cavitation, such extreme environments may decompose the sonosensitizer to produce •OH through water pyrolysis, which can further react with endogenous substances to generate other ROS. The second mechanism is sonoluminescence, which is also considered to be the most important mechanism in SDT. Sonoluminescence is a transient flash phenomenon that occurs during the rapid collapse of cavitation bubbles. The light emitted from sonoluminescence can excite sonosensitizers, initiating a ROS pruduction mechanism akin to PDT. While the SDT effect is highly dependent on ROS production, mechanical damage induced by cavitation is not negligible. In the cavitation phenomenon, although stable cavitation does not produce ROS, the bubbles in stable cavitation continuously oscillate, exerting shear forces and microjets on the surrounding cells. In addition, during inertial cavitation, the shock waves generated by bubble collapse can also induce physical damage to cells and tissues. Therefore, the therapeutic efficacy of SDT is not solely dependent on ROS-mediated cytotoxicity but is also attributed to cavitation-induced mechanical damage.

Development of sonosensitizer

A sonosensitizer is the key determinants in SDT. Currently, sonosensitizers are primarily categorized into organic and inorganic types. Most organic sonosensitizer are inspired by photosensitizers and include compounds such as porphyrins, phthalocyanines (Pcs), phenothiazine derivatives, and small-molecule fluorescent dyes like IR780 and ICG, which have been demonstrated to exhibit properties characteristic of SDT sensitizers. However, organic sonosensitizers have problems such as poor water solubility, strong phototoxicity, and low tumor tissue targeting. Inorganic sonosensitizers including composites such as titanium dioxide (TiO2), zinc oxide (ZnO), molybdenum disulfide (MoS2), black phosphorus, and other composite materials. Due to their high stability, unique band structure and low phototoxicity, inorganic sonosensitizers have been widely studied in tumor SDT. Furthermore, the nanostructure of inorganic sonosensitizers can offer an increased number of nucleation sites for cavitation bubble formation, thereby enhancing the generation of ROS.

Inorganic sonosensitizers are energy-matched sonosensitizers. When they are excited by the light of sonoluminescence, electrons (e−) transfer from the valence band (VB) to the conduction band (CB), and holes (h+) will form in the VB. Then, the highly active e− and h+ react with the surrounding O2 or H2O to generate ROS. Therefore, the separation of e−-h+ in the energy band structure of inorganic sonosensitizers would contribute to the production of ROS. However, the separated electrons e− and h+ could easily recombine leading to low yields of ROS production. Thus, facilitating e−-h+ separation or inhibiting e−-h+ recombination is a challenge to be overcome to improve SDT. Optimizing the structure of inorganic photosensitizers holds significant importance for addressing this challenge, encompassing metal ion-doped sonosensitizers, defect-rich engineering, construction of heterojunctions, and the application of piezoelectric materials. Structure optimization of inorganic sonosensitizers

Metal ion doped sonosensitizers

Metal ions exhibit a localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) effect, and their incorporation into the framework of semiconductor-based nanosonosensitizers can effectively narrow the band gap, thereby facilitating the separation of e−-h+ pairs under external US irradiation. In addition, the metal can act as an electron trap to capture electrons generated by semiconductor charge separation, facilitating electron transfer at the composite interface and preventing recombination of e−-h+ pairs. For instance, Deepagan et al. deposited gold on titanium dioxide NPs (TiO2 NPs) and anchored carboxymethyl glucan onto their surface to form hydrophilic nanocomposites (HAu–TiO2 NCs). The introduction of Au NPs acted as an electron receiver to facilitate the transfer of electrons and effectively prevented the complexation of e−-h+ pairs. The calculated rate constants of ROS generation for HAU-TiO2 NCs and undeposited gold nanomaterials (HTiO2) NPs under US irradiation were 0.0605s−1 and 0.0781s−1, respectively. As expected, HAu–TiO2 NCs produced higher levels of ROS both in vitro and in vivo. Furthermore, the modification of hydrophilic carboxymethyl dextran resulted in an extended circulation time of the nanoparticles in vivo. Using SCC7 tumor-bearing mice as a model, the tumor regression rate of HAu–TiO2 NCs at the end of treatment was 3.11 times higher than that of HTiO2 NPs under US irradiation, strongly confirming the effectiveness of Au doping in enhancing the therapeutic effect of TiO2-based SDT tumors.

Bai et al. synthesized Fe-doped TiO2 nanodots (Fe–TiO2 NDs) to enhance SDT effect, and then modified them with PEG to improve their stability and biocompatibility. The doping of Fe effectively reduced the bandgap, resulting in a decrease in the bandgap value of pure TiO2 nanodots from 3.2 eV to approximately 2.3 eV (Fe–TiO2), which facilitated e−-h+ separation. This enhancement in e−-h+ separation ensured that the ROS generation efficiency of Fe–TiO2 NDs was significantly higher compared to that of pure TiO2 nanodots. Furthermore, the presence of Fe ions in the Fe–TiO2 NDs triggered the Fenton reaction, thereby accelerating the generation of ROS. In vivo, the Fe–TiO2 NDs + US group significantly inhibited tumor growth and exhibited the strongest ROS fluorescence signal. In another study, Hu et al. designed the iron Fe and manganese (Mn) co-doped zinc oxide nanosonosensitizers (D-ZnO NPs). The doping of Mn and Fe made the band gap of D-ZnO NPs (2.02 eV) significantly lower than that of pure ZnO NPs (3.37 eV). On the other hand, doping with Mn and Fe was able to deplete the highly expressed GSH in the cells, thereby reducing the consumption of ROS by GSH. At the same time, the metal ions provided a Fenton-like reaction function to catalyze the formation of •OH from H2O2. The tumor inhibition rate of D-ZnO NPs finally reached 92.8 %, effectively synergizing SDT and CDT treatments. In addition, doping of other metal ions such as W and Cu also resulted in extended recombination time of e−-h+, and the CDT effect was endowed concurrently for synergistic SDT/CDT against tumor.

Defect rich engineering

Defect engineering has been proposed as a promising strategy for improving ROS production. By creating vacancies in the framework of the nanosonosensitizer, defects can act as positive charge centers that trap electrons to limit rapid recombination of e−-h+. For instance, Zhang et al. synthesized TiO2 nanoparticles in situ on Ti3C2 nanosheets using the hydrothermal method. Subsequently, they induced multiple oxygen defects inside the TiO2 NPs by employing the hydrazine hydrate reduction method to form nanohybrids (Ti3C2@TiO2-x) (Fig. 7A). The Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS) revealed the presence of titanium, oxygen, and carbon elements in Ti3C2@TiO2-x, indicating successful in situ growth of TiO2 on Ti3C2. Meanwhile, the XPS spectroscopy revealed that the O1s peak in Ti3C2@TiO2-x shifted from 529.9 eV to 529.2 eV compared to Ti3C2@TiO2. In addition, the quantitative analysis showed a significant decrease in the O/Ti ratio from 2 to 1.6 in Ti3C2@TiO2-x compared to Ti3C2@TiO2, and these results illustrate the formation of oxygen vacancies in Ti3C2@TiO2-x. To gain deeper insights into the acoustodynamic mechanism of Ti3C2@TiO2-x, researchers employed Kubelka-Munk functions to calculate the band gaps. The results revealed band gaps of 3.32 eV and 2.65 eV for TiO2 and Ti3C2@TiO2-x, respectively. It is evident that oxygen vacancies decreased the band gap, thereby serving as charge traps to impede e−-h+ recombination under US activation. Compared to Ti3C2@TiO2, Ti3C2@TiO2-x exhibited enhanced capability for ROS generation. In addition, the Ti3C2 nanosheets also possessed photothermal effect. Upon US/NIR laser dual-stimuli, Ti3C2@TiO2-x triggered massive cell killing in vitro and complete tumor eradication in vivo.

In addition to TiO2, Liu et al. prepared defect-rich gadolinium (Gd) doped zinc oxide nanobombs (D-ZnOx:Gd) through deoxidation method. The presence of oxygen defects in D-ZnOx:Gd was confirmed by electron spin resonance (ESR) spectra. Compared to ZnO, D-ZnOx:Gd could provide sites for trapping electrons, which was beneficial for promoting the separation of e−-h+ from the band structure after ultrasonic irradiation and improving the efficiency of ROS generation. Furthermore, D-ZnOx:Gd was more prone to adsorbing water and oxygen molecules due to its abundant hypoxic properties, thereby significantly enhancing the capacity for ROS generation. In addition, D-ZnOx:Gd exhibited a good photothermal conversion efficiency under NIR irradiation. In the 4T1 mouse breast cancer model, the D-ZnOx:Gd + US + laser group completely cured the tumor, effectively achieving combined PTT and SDT against cancer. Gong et al. prepared ultrasmall oxygen-deficient bimetallic oxide (MnWOX-PEG). Due to the oxygen-deficient structure of MnWOX serving as electron trap sites, it prevented e−-h+ recombination, thereby enabling MnWOX-PEG NPS to exhibit more efficient ROS generation compared to traditional sonosensitizers protoporphyrin-IX and TiO2 under US irradiation. Furthermore, the presence of Mn2+ and W elements endowed MnWOX-PEG NPs with magnetic resonance (MR) and computed tomography (CT) imaging capabilities, enabling precise SDT for tumors.

Heterojunction structure

Introducing another junction at the interface of an inorganic acoustic sensitizer to form a heterojunction is a promising strategy for enhancing SDT by suppressing the recombination of e−-h+pairs. The current types of heterojunctions include semiconductor-semiconductor (S–S) heterojunctions and semiconductor-metal (S-M) heterojunctions.

Among S–S heterojunctions, Z-type heterojunctions have been shown to enhance the production of ROS and improve the performance of SDT for cancer treatment. For instance, He et al. precipitated graphitic carbon nitride quantum dots (g-C3N4 QDs) on hollow mesoporous TiO2 spheres to form Z-type heterojunctions (TiO2@g-C3N4). In comparison to pure TiO2, TiO2@g-C3N4 exhibited a reduced band gap, which facilitated the rapid separation of e−-h+. Moreover, the Z-scheme heterojunction structure allowed for the retention of more photoexcited electrons in the conduction band of g-C3N4 and more photoexcited holes in the valence band of TiO2, thereby inhibiting the recombination of e−-h+ pairs. Under US irradiation, TiO2@g-C3N4 induced a higher production of ROS in 4T1 tumor cells compared to TiO2. Recently, Feng et al. incorporated iron-doped carbon dots (CDs) into cubic crystal Cu2O to form a type Z heterojunction (Fe-CDs@Cu2O). Due to the positive charge of Cu2O and the negative charge of Fe-CDs, they came into direct contact to form a heterojunction, generating an internal electric field. The formation of the embedded electric field selectively facilitated electron transfer from Fe-CDs to Cu2O. Conversely, electron back-transfer at the heterojunction was inhibited, thereby suppressing e−-h+ recombination. The ROS generation rate of Fe-CDs@Cu2O was 3.5-fold higher than that of pure Cu2O. Meanwhile, the presence of Cu+ and Fe2+ further induced CDT in TME. In summary, the enhanced SDT effect of the Z-scheme heterojunction and the CDT effect mediated by Cu+/Fe2+ enabled Fe-CDs@Cu2O to achieve nearly complete tumor eradication under ultrasound irradiation.

Another strategy is to form a Schottky heterojunction, which is created by connecting a high work function metal with a low work function semiconductor. Specifically, when a metal is in close contact with a semiconductor, electrons flow from the semiconductor with a higher Fermi level to the metal with a lower fermi level. The transfer of electrons in the semiconductor generates positively charged holes, which result in an upward bending of the energy band and the formation of a Schottky barrier. The Schottky junction can act as an electron trap, effectively promoting the separation of e−-h+. Li et al. deposited ruthenium (Ru) onto the shell of TiO2 to prepare a Schottky heterojunction sonosensitizer (TiO2–Ru-PEG) (Fig. 7B). By calculation, the work functions of TiO2 and Ru were determined to be 3.11 eV and 4.95 eV, respectively. The substantial difference in work functions drived electron transfer from TiO2 to Ru, creating an internal electric field and a Schottky barrier, which promoted e−-h+ separation under ultrasound irradiation. To verify the SDT effect of TiO2–Ru-PEG, MB49 bladder tumor cells were used as an in vitro model. Under US irradiation, TiO2–Ru-PEG significantly induced ROS generation in MB49 cells, resulting in a highly efficient SDT effect. Importantly, the heteronode structure enhanced the anti-tumor effect of SDT in both subcutaneous and orthotopic bladder tumor models in vivo. In addition to TiO2-based sonosensitizers, this composite strategy of suppressing e−-h+ pairs through heterojunctions is also applied to other types of sonosensitizers. Ouyang et al. prepared two-dimensional sonosensitizer (Au@BP nanohybrids) by in-situ growth of Au NPs on black phosphorus (BP) nanosheets. In this system, the fermi level of Au (−5.1 eV) was lower than that of BP (−3.9 eV), which promoted electron accumulation on Au NPs, and subsequently formed a Schottky barrier. When intravenously injected into 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, Au@BP nanohybrids exhibited significant inhibition of tumor growth under ultrasound irradiation, with no apparent side effects.

Piezoelectric materials

Piezoelectric materials are a type of material with a non-centrosymmetric crystal structure, which can be easily polarized under ultrasonic stimulation and generate an inherent electric field. This electric field enables continuous separation of electrons and holes, thereby enhancing ROS generation activity. In one study, Zhu et al. verified the piezoelectric catalytic mode of ROS generation in an uncentrosymmetric quadrilateral barium titanate (T-BTO) piezoelectric material under ultrasound. Under ultrasound irradiation, non-centrosymmetric T-BTO NPs generated a strong built-in electric field due to their piezoelectric properties. This field allowed for continuous separation of electrons and holes, effectively catalyzing the generation of ROS. To investigate its in vivo efficacy, researchers coated T-BTO NPs in a thermosensitive hydrogel and, by intratumoral delivery, effectively eradicated tumors under US irradiation.

In recent years, some new piezoelectric materials have been developed and used to obtain higher catalytic activity. Cai et al. the Au NPs deposited in UIO-66 piezoelectric material surface, form a composite materials (UIO-66-Au NPs) (Fig. 8A). The introduction of Au NPs significantly enhanced the electron transfer efficiency and piezoelectric coefficient of UIO-66, while also reducing its bandgap, thereby suppressing the recombination of e−-h+. Under US irradiation, e−-h+ pairs migrated to different sides of the composite material. Simultaneously, internal polarization induced band bending, reducing the distance between the band edges and the redox potential of ROS, thereby enhancing the SDT efficiency of UIO-66-Au NPs. With increased US irradiation, the ROS generation rate of UIO-66-Au NPs reaches 70 %, compared to only 30 % for pure UIO-66. In vivo, UIO-66-Au NPs demonstrated a tumor inhibition rate of up to 93.2 % under US irradiation. In another study, Wu et al. introduced sulfur (S)-vacancies into molybdenum disulfide (MoS2) nanoflowers to enhance the piezoelectric performance of MoS2. Under US irradiation, sulfur vacancies in MoS2 nanoflowers can regulate effective e−-h+ pair separation and rapid charge transfer, thereby enhancing the efficiency of ROS generation. This vacancy-engineering strategy significantly improves the SDT effect of piezoelectric sonosensitizers, resulting in a tumor suppression rate of up to 85.4 % after 18 days of treatment.

Enhancing cavitation effect

In the mechanism of SDT, the formation and collapse of cavitation bubbles are crucial to the SDT effect. On one hand, the cavitation effect physically damages cells and enhances the diffusion of sonosensitizers. On the other hand, inertial cavitation promotes the production of ROS, which induces cell death. Therefore, enhancing the cavitation effect is an effective means to increase the rate of ROS generation.

Currently, microbubbles (MBs) are commonly used for enhancing cavitation. They consist mainly of a shell and a gas core, with the shell materials including lipids, proteins, or biodegradable polymers. In a recent study, Fan et al. integrated carbon dots (C-dots) into the lipid shell of gas microbubbles (MBs). Under US irradiation, the cavitation produced by MBs generated extremely high energy, which decomposed on the C-dots and thereby increased ROS production. Compared to conventional lipid MBs, the ROS generated by C-dots MBs was sufficient to induce apoptosis in tumor cells. Beyond MBs, incorporating a large amount of gas into nanoparticles has been used to amplify the cavitation effect. For example, Lee et al. utilized perfluorohexane (PFH) as a gas precursor, encapsulated in polyethylene glycolated mesoporous titanium dioxide nanoparticles, to develop a cavitation nanobooster (PFH@P-MSTNs) (Fig. 8B). Under US irradiation, the PFH bubbles rupture, generating intense sonoluminescence energy, which was transferred to TiO2, thereby enhancing ROS production. Compared to NPs without PFH loading (P-MSTNs), PFH@P-MSTNs induced higher ROS production in SCC7 tumors under low-frequency US, effectively inhibiting tumor growth. Additionally, due to the vaporization of PFH, PFH@P-MSTNs exhibited photoacoustic imaging capabilities in vivo, enabling the monitoring of cavitation-induced cell death.

Chemodynamic therapy (CDT)

Mechanism of CDT

CDT is a novel tumor treatment strategy based on in situ Fenton or Fenton-like reactions, where Fe2+ acts as a catalyst to react with H2O2 highly expressed in TME, producing highly cytotoxic •OH. In 2016, Bu's research group synthesized amorphous iron NPs (AFeNPs), and first proposed the concept of CDT. Currently, various iron-based nanomaterials have been developed for CDT applications. To advance CDT, other transition metals, including Cu, Mn, Co, Pt have also been introduced into CDT for cancer treatment. These metal ion-mediated reactions are referred to as Fenton-like reactions.

Main conclusions from CDT

CDT utilizes metal ions as catalysts to generate ROS via Fenton or Fenton-like reactions with endogenous H2O2 from tumors

CDT utilizes endogenous chemical energy for triggered therapy and is not limited by tissue penetration issues

Various metal-based nanomaterials with TME responsiveness have been developed as catalysts for CDT

Limitations and enhancement strategies of CDT in tumor therapy

In general, CDT utilizes endogenous chemical energy for triggering and therefore does not require additional energy input compared to PDT and SDT strategies that rely on exogenous energy such as light or US to trigger treatment, thereby avoiding the limitation of tissue penetration. Despite its promising potential, CDT strategy still faces several key challenges. Firstly, although the level of H2O2 in tumor tissues is higher than in normal tissues, the absolute concentration of endogenous H2O2 is only 50–100 μM, which is generally insufficient to sustain continuous ROS generation. Meanwhile, the pH of weakly acidic TME is not the optimal pH for Fenton or Fenton-like reactions (pH 2–4). Furthermore, the efficiency of CDT is constrained by the low catalytic activity of metal ions and the limited recyclability of highly active metal ions. To improve CDT efficacy, various strategies have been proposed, including pH modulation, increasing H2O2 levels, and enhancing the efficiency of metal ion catalysts. While strategies focused on pH modulation and increasing H2O2 levels will be discussed in Section 6, this section will concentrate on strategies aimed at improving the catalytic efficiency of metal ions. Enhancement strategies of CDT

Accelerating metal ions valence conversion

In Fenton or Fenton-like reaction, low-valence metal ions with high catalytic properties will be oxidized to high-valence with low catalytic properties (e.g., Fe2⁺ to Fe³⁺). However, the conversion rate from Fe(III) to Fe(II) is very slow (0.002–0.01 M−1 s−1), which significantly reduces the efficiency of the Fenton reaction. Therefore, developing regeneration strategies to promote the conversion of low-activity high-valent metal ions back to highly active low-valent metal ions is crucial for enhancing the catalytic efficiency of the Fenton reaction.

Introducing reductants into nanomaterials to catalyze the regeneration of low valent metal ions is a commonly used strategy. For instance, Chen's team developed DOX-loaded Fe(III) species-WS2-polyvinylpyrrolidone nanocapsules (DOX-Fe(III)@WS2-PVP) (Fig. 9). In this nanosystem, an endogenous redox reaction occurred between Fe(III) and WS2 nanosheets, generating Fe2+ and WO42−. The Fe2+ then reacted with intracellular H2O2 in a Fenton reaction, producing Fe3+ and hydroxyl radicals •OH. Subsequently, Fe3+ reacted again with WS2 to regenerate Fe2+, creating a self-cycling endogenous redox and Fenton reaction that enhanced •OH generation. Additionally, the repeated reactions facilitate the biodegradation of Fe(III)@WS2-PVP nanocapsules, leading to the release of DOX. This nanoplatform, which integrates chemotherapy, photothermal, and CDT effects, completely inhibited tumors in vivo. In another study, Zhang et al. developed a self-catalytic Fenton nanosystem (GOx@ZIF@MPN) by encapsulating GOx within a zeolitic imidazolate framework (ZIF) and coating it with a metal-phenolic network (MPN) (Fig. 10A). Upon entry into ATP-overexpressing tumor tissue, the MPN shell degraded into Fe3+ and tannic acid (TA). The TA reduced Fe(III) to Fe(II), providing a rapid Fe(III)/Fe(II) conversion system, thereby achieving enhanced CDT for tumor ablation.

Compared to NP catalyst-triggered models, TME-triggered valence state transitions exhibit inherent specificity. Many types of cancer cells overexpress surface transferrin receptors, leading to elevated levels of endogenous labile Fe(II). Based on this, Chen et al. designed copper hexacyanoferrate NPs (CuHCF NPs) activated by endogenous labile Fe(II). Upon entering tumor cells, endogenous Fe2+ altered the surface valence state of CuHCF NPs, initiating a redox cycle between Fe3+/Fe2+ and Cu2+/Cu+. This cycle increased the levels of Cu+ and Fe2+, thereby inducing CDT. In vitro experiments showed that CuHCF enhanced ROS generation within treated 4T1 cells, exhibiting a concentration-dependent cytotoxicity. When combined with the small molecule ERA, which increases intracellular labile iron (II), the cytotoxic effect on 4T1 cells was further amplified. Additionally, CuHCF NPs demonstrated excellent NIR-II photothermal properties under the influence of Fe2+. In the vivo experiment, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were divided into 5 groups, and the group treated with CuHCF NPs + ERA + laser showed the highest efficiency in inhibiting tumors, reaching an inhibition rate of 83.9 %. In another study, Tian et al. constructed ellagic acid–Fe–bovine serum albumin NPs (EA-Fe@BSA) by leveraging the high expression of H2S in colon cancer tissues (Fig. 10B). H2S has a much stronger reducing ability, upon entering colon tumors, Fe(III) in EA-Fe@BSA was reduced to Fe(II) by H2S, providing a rapid Fe(III)/Fe(II) transformation. Concurrently, the photothermal effect of EA-Fe@BSA NPs also accelerated •OH production. In vivo, using an HCT116 tumor-bearing mouse model, the combined H2S and photothermal-enhanced CDT effect led to the complete elimination of tumors within 16 days of treatment with EA-Fe@BSA.

Using exogenous assistances

Typically, an increase in temperature accelerates the reaction rate of chemical reactions. It has been demonstrated that increasing the temperature from 20 °C to 50 °C can quadruple the rate of the Fenton reaction. Hence, raising the temperature at the tumor site is an effective method to enhance the Fenton reaction in CDT. Liu et al. constructed a mild hyperthermia-enhanced enzyme-mediated CDT nanocomposite system (PMFG) (Fig. 11A). This system comprises glucose oxidase (GOx), multi-walled carbon nanotubes (MWCNTs), and Fe3O4 NPs. In this setup, Fe3O4 acted as a Fenton agent, generating •OH radicals to induce cell death. GOx converted glucose into a substantial amount of H2O2, which further promoted the Fe3O4-based Fenton reaction. Additionally, MWCNTs generated localized heat under 808 nm laser irradiation, exhibiting a PTT effect, and the heat generated by photothermal conversion further enhances the Fenton reaction. As expected, in vitro treatment of MCF-7 cells with different groups showed that the PMFG + laser irradiation group produced more •OH and induced a higher cell death rate compared to the PMFG group without laser irradiation, ultimately achieving synergistic PTT and CDT.

Fe3+ ions generate Fe2+ ions under light irradiation, making photo-assisted Fenton reaction more effective at producing •OH. However, the radiation used to enhance the Fenton reaction is primarily UV/Vis light, which has limited penetration ability, restricting its application in CDT. Therefore, using NIR or X-ray, which have stronger penetration capabilities, offers the potential for designing more efficient photo-Fenton reaction nanocatalysts. In one study, Zhou et al. developed a nanoplatform by using carboxyl-functionalized ferrocene derivatives (Fc) as Fenton-like reagents, which was loaded onto NaYF4,Tm@NaYF4 UCNPs and then encapsulated within liposomes to form the final nanocomposite (Lipo-FNPs) (Fig. 11B). Under NIR irradiation, the FRET between Fc and UCNPs enhanced the Fenton reaction, producing more •OH. Additionally, the oxidized intermediate Fc+ from the Fenton reaction was reduced back to Fc by GSH, allowing Lipo-FNPs to provide sufficient Fc for a cyclically enhanced Fenton reaction. Furthermore, FNPs exhibit strong upconversion emissions at 647 nm and 696 nm, enabling upconversion luminescence imaging for precise tumor therapy. Both in vitro and in vivo experiments demonstrated that NIR irradiation significantly enhanced the effects of Fenton reaction. In another study, Zhang et al. synthesized sodium polyacrylate-coated copper hydroxyphosphate nanocrystals (Cu2(OH)PO4@PAAS NCs) (Fig. 12A). X-ray irradiation induced the production of low-energy electrons, accelerating the conversion of Cu(II) to Cu(I), which effectively promoted the Fenton-like reaction of Cu(I). When Cu2(OH)PO4@PAAS NCs were co-incubated with HeLa cells, X-ray irradiation significantly enhanced the production of •OH and markedly reduced the viability of HeLa cells. However, Cu2(OH)PO4@PAAS NCs without X-ray irradiation had negligible effects on cell viability. This X-ray-enhanced CDT effect was also confirmed in vivo, where Cu2(OH)PO4@PAAS NCs under X-ray irradiation exhibited the highest tumor inhibition rate compared to groups treated with either the nanomaterials or X-ray alone.

During the US process, the cavitation bubbles generated can induce severe turbulence, significantly enhancing the transfer rate of Fenton reagents. Inspired by this, Gao and his colleagues constructed a pH-responsive nanoplatform (GOD@CaCO3–Fe3O4) with US-enhanced CDT capability (Fig. 12B). GOD@CaCO3–Fe3O4 decomposes glucose in situ to increase H2O2 content. Assisted by US, the cavitation effect of GOD@CaCO3–Fe3O4 generates more •OH. Consequently, GOD@ CaCO3–Fe3O4 exhibited optimal cancer treatment efficacy both in vitro and in vivo. In a recent study, Feng et al. developed copper nanodots (Cu NDs). Under US stimulation, free charge carriers formed on the Cu NDs, inducing the conversion of Cu(III) to Cu(I), producing more •OH and effectively achieving synergistic CDT and SDT.

Modulating the TME

Although the aforementioned strategies can enhance ROS production efficiency for each treatment modality, the complex factors within the TME, such as hypoxia, pH value, H2O2, and GSH levels, pose significant limitations on ROS generation. Therefore, effectively reprogramming the TME remains a pressing issue. Currently, common strategies include ameliorating hypoxia, regulating pH levels, reducing GSH levels, and increasing H2O2 levels. A more comprehensive strategies summary is shown in Table 4.

Alleviating tumor hypoxia

It has been reported that hypoxic cells in advanced solid tumors are 2.8–3 times higher resistance to ROS than oxygenated cells. Consequently, researchers have developed various strategies to alleviate tumor hypoxia, which can be broadly categorized into delivering O2 to the tumor, generating oxygen within the tumor in situ, reducing O2 consumption, inhibiting angiogenesis, and suppressing the expression of hypoxia-inducible factors.

Delivering O2 to tumor site

Researchers have effectively alleviated tumor hypoxia by delivering artificial oxygen sources such as hemoglobin (Hb) or perfluorocarbons (PFCs) to tumor sites using nanocarriers. Hb is a specialized protein in red blood cells responsible for oxygen transport. In a recent study, Ding et al. developed biomimetic nanovesicles coated with red blood cell membranes (SPN-Hb@RBCM) (Fig. 13A). In this system, semiconductor polymer NPs (SPNs) serve as PS, and under 808 nm laser irradiation, which can generate substantial ROS in the presence of adequate O2. The red blood cell membrane coating endowed the NPs with prolonged circulation, allowing for more effective accumulation of PS in the tumor. Hemoglobin was covalently linked to SPNs as O2 carriers with 5.7 % grafting. The dissolved oxygen meter showed that 1 mg of SPN-Hb@RBCM carried 0.142 μg of O2, demonstrating good oxygen-carrying capacity. Thus, SPN-Hb@RBCM effectively generated ROS even under hypoxic conditions, compared to SPNs. Additionally, Hb catalyzed the production of •OH from endogenous high concentrations of H2O2 for CDT. In vivo results showed that intravenously administered SPN-Hb@RBCM accumulated in the tumor tissue of SMMC-7721 tumor-bearing nude mice. Due to the synergistic effects of PDT and CDT, this self-oxygenating nanoplatform combined with irradiation nearly completely ablated the tumor. In contrast to the low loading efficiency of hemoglobin, PFCs, a colorless liquid capable of physically dissolving large amounts of oxygen, have been investigated as artificial oxygen carriers. For example, Zhang et al. constructed self-oxygenating NPs (CS-Rh-PFC NPs). This system was composed of chondroitin sulfate, the sonosensitizer Rhein, and perfluorocarbon, which self-assemble into CS-Rh-PFC NPs. Chondroitin sulfate can effectively target tumor cells expressing CD44 on their surface, enhancing the uptake of the nanoplatform by tumor cells. Due to the high oxygen-carrying capacity of perfluorocarbon, CS-Rh-PFC NPs produced higher levels of ROS in vitro and in vivo under ultrasound treatment compared to NPs without PFC, and significantly reduced the hypoxic area in B16F10 solid tumor tissues.

Hyperbaric oxygen (HBO), with its rapid oxygen diffusion rate and extended diffusion distance, has shown potential in overcoming tumor hypoxia. For instance, Li et al. employed HBO to assist UCNPs loaded with RB to enhance PDT for cancer. In this system, the UCNP core acted as a light converter, activating RB to generate ROS via FRET under NIR irradiation. To verify the hypoxia-relieving capability of HBO, DCFH-DA was utilized as a probe to measure ROS generation in 4T1 cells treated with UCNPs under both normoxic and hypoxic conditions. The results demonstrated that under normoxic conditions, the intracellular ROS level in the UCNPs + NIR group was 1.74 times higher than that under hypoxic conditions. Notably, when HBO was introduced under hypoxic conditions, the ROS fluorescence signal within cells was 3.5 times stronger compared to the normoxic condition. These results suggest that HBO effectively mitigates tumor hypoxia and enhances the PDT process. When UCNPs were injected via the tail vein into 4T1 tumor-bearing BALB/c mice, the UCNPs + NIR + HBO group exhibited tumor growth of only 0.6 times compared to the control group, indicating higher efficiency. Mechanistic studies revealed that the enhanced PDT effect was due to HBO not only effectively alleviating hypoxia but also improving the penetration of nanoparticles into deeper regions of the tumor, away from blood vessels.

In addition, calcium peroxide (CaO2), known for its high bioavailability and oxygen-generating capacity, is a potential O2 carrier. Liu et al. developed a liposome-based co-delivery system (LipoMB/CaO2) containing methylene blue (MB) and CaO2 NPs. Once the LipoMB/CaO2 entered the tumor, the CaO2 reacted with H2O or weak acids to release O2. To assess the oxygen-generating performance of LipoMB/CaO2, the dispersion of LipoMB/CaO2 in deoxygenated water was measured using a dissolved oxygen meter. The results indicated that the dissolved O2 concentration in the LipoMB/CaO2 group consistently exceeded that of the NPs without encapsulated CaO2 (LipoMB). Upon irradiation, the liposomes was disrupted, increasing the contact area of CaO2 to H2O, thereby generating more O2. Crucially, the 1O2 producing efficiency of MB under irradiation was severely limited under hypoxic conditions, whereas the 1O2 producing efficiency of MB was greatly improved when CO2 was added. When 4T1 cells were co-incubated with NPs, the intracellular ROS fluorescence intensity in cells treated with LipoMB/CaO2 was markedly higher than that in cells treated with LipoMB alone. This indicated that elevated O2 levels lead to increased ROS production. Furthermore, after a second irradiation, ROS generation within the LipoMB/CaO2 group further increased, indicating an enhanced PDT effect. This self-supplying O2 platform exhibited enhanced PDT anti-tumor effects both in vitro and in vivo.

In situ oxygen generation

It is well known that cancer cells with higher H2O2 levels compared to normal cells. Utilizing active compounds to generate oxygen from endogenous H2O2 has become a research hotspot. Among these compounds, catalase, a unique catalytic enzyme, efficiently decomposes H2O2 into O2 and has been explored for constructing various types of nanoplatforms to alleviate tumor hypoxia. For instance, Sim et al. designed a MOF-based catalase delivery nanosystem (IR780@MOF-CAT) to mitigate intracellular hypoxia and achieve effective PDT (Fig. 13B). The researchers first validated the oxygen generation capability of CAT in a PBS solution containing H2O2. The results indicated that bare MOFs did not produce any O2, whereas MOF-CAT at a concentration of 60 μg/mL generated approximately 4 mg/L of O2 within 2 min, demonstrating that catalase can overcome the hypoxic tumor environment. After cellular uptake, the acidic tumor microenvironment weakened the supramolecular interactions between CAT and MOFs, leading to the desorption of CAT from the surface of MOF NPs. Subsequently, CAT reacted with H2O2 to generate O2, enhancing the PDT process of IR780 under irradiation. Compared to IR780@MOF, IR780@MOF-CAT significantly reduced the H2O2 levels and the viability of HeLa cells.

Natural oxidases suffer from high preparation costs and low purification efficiency, making artificial nanozymes with properties akin to natural enzymes a promising alternative. These nanozymes are primarily composed of metal oxides and noble metals, such as MnO2, Fe3O4, and platinum (Pt) nanozymes. For instance, a polymer-modified hollow structured Pt–CuS Janus nanoparticle has been developed to enhance the SDT effect. In this Janus, Pt reacted with H2O2 to generate highly reactive O2, providing an O2 source for ROS production induced by SDT. Importantly, this nanoplatform also exhibited photothermal efficacy. The heat generated under 808 nm laser irradiation enhanced the catalase-like activity of Pt, thereby inducing the production of more O2. Tumor sections from mice 24 h post intravenous injection of this nanoplatform showed very weak fluorescence signals of hypoxia-inducible factors, indicating that the Janus effectively alleviate tumor hypoxia. Complete tumor eradication was achieved under 808 nm laser and ultrasound irradiation, with no significant recurrence observed. In a recent study, Wu et al. developed a two-dimensional composite (GO-MnO2@tLyP-1/Ce6) (Fig. 14A) by integrating MnO2 colloid, tumor-homing and penetrating peptide tLyP-1, and PS Ce6. The tLyP-1 peptide specifically recognized and penetrated 4T1 cells with high expression of the NRP-1 receptor, targeting the hypoxic core of the tumor. Subsequently, the MnO2 colloid reacted with H2O2 to generate O2, enhancing the ROS production of the Ce6 under laser irradiation. In vivo studies demonstrated that tumors treated with GO-MnO2@tLyP-1/Ce6 exhibited significantly reduced hypoxia distribution. Following laser irradiation, there was a complete inhibition of tumor growth, with an inhibition rate of 88.5 %. These findings offer new insights into improving hypoxic conditions for more effective cancer therapy.

Reducing O2 consumption

Current research primarily focuses on increasing O2 levels to overcome tumor hypoxia. However, some scholars have proposed that reducing O2 consumption is also a viable strategy. It is well known that mitochondria are the primary site of aerobic respiration in eukaryotic cells. Therefore, inhibiting mitochondrial respiration may be a feasible approach to reducing O2 consumption and alleviating tumor hypoxia.

For instance, Yuan et al. reported an O2-conserving nanoplatform (Mito-OxE) designed to enhance PDT treatment (Fig. 14B). Specifically, this NP was synthesized by solid-phase peptide synthesis, combining the PS molecule PpIX with hydrophilic polyethylene glycol and the mitochondria-targeting molecule triphenylphosphine (TPP) to form mitochondria-targeting molecules (Mito-PSs). These Mito-PSs were then self-assembled with the mitochondrial respiration inhibitor atovaquone (ATO). When co-incubated with 4T1 tumor cells, Mito-OxE actively accumulated in the mitochondria due to the mitochondrial targeting effects of Mito-PSs. Simultaneously, ATO was released from Mito-OxE in the acidic TME. Compared to the control group and Mito-PSs, cells treated with Mito-OxE showed a more than 10-fold reduction in mitochondrial O2 consumption rate. As expected, under light irradiation and even in hypoxic conditions, Mito-OxE induced more effective ROS generation and tumor growth inhibition than Mito-PSs. This demonstrated that inhibiting mitochondrial respiration can improve the hypoxic environment within tumors, thereby enhancing PDT efficiency. This effect was also confirmed in vivo, where tail vein injection of Mito-OxE into 4T1-bearing mice alleviated hypoxia in tumor tissues, and Mito-OxE inhibited tumor growth more effectively than the other groups under light irradiation. In another study, the PS IR780 and metformin were loaded into PEG-PCL liposomes. Metformin directly inhibited the activity of complex I in the mitochondrial electron transport chain, thereby reducing endogenous oxygen consumption. Under 808 nm laser irradiation, this nanoplatform, which targeted mitochondria and overcomed hypoxia limitations, enhanced the antitumor efficiency of IR780 through both PDT and PTT.

In addition to the use of exogenous drugs to inhibit mitochondrial respiration, NO, as a gas transmitter, is an ideal choice to alleviate hypoxia by competing with O2 for binding of mitochondrial oxygen-binding sites and thus inhibiting cellular respiration. Xiang et al. coloaded Ce6 and l-arginine (L-Arg) into PLGA NPs to develop a nanosystem (L-Arg@Ce6@P NPs). When L-Arg@Ce6@P NPs entered the tumor, the released L-Arg reacted with the overexpressed H2O2 in tumor cells, producing NO at non-cytotoxic concentrations. NO diffused within the tumor cells, inhibiting mitochondrial complex IV and thus reducing O2 consumption. After 90 min of co-incubation with MDA-MB-231 cells, the O2 levels in cells treated with PBS or Ce6@P NPs (without L-Arg) were 46.9 % and 49.7 % of the control group, respectively. In contrast, cells treated with L-Arg@Ce6@P NPs retained 70.8 % of the control group's O2 level. Intravenous injection of L-Arg@Ce6@P NPs into MDA-MB-231 tumor-bearing mice effectively alleviated hypoxic regions in the tumor tissue. Under laser irradiation, L-Arg@Ce6@P NPs significantly inhibited tumor growth, whereas mice injected with Ce6@P NPs exhibited a weaker antitumor effect upon laser irradiation. These results demonstrated the importance of reducing O2 consumption for enhancing PDT efficacy.

Other strategy of alleviating tumor hypoxia

The origin of hypoxia can largely be traced back to abnormal vascularization, which reduces the supply of oxygen and nutrients, consequently adversely affecting ROS generation. Therefore, numerous anti-angiogenic drugs are employed to regulate hypoxia within tumors. For example, Zhu et al. developed EXO/AIEgen hybrid nanovesicles (DES) by electroporation method using tumor-derived exosomes (EXO) and AIE PS (DCPy). In this study, EXO were capable of recognizing specific proteins on the surface of tumor cells, thereby actively targeting DES to the tumor cells. Dexamethasone (DEX), functioning as an anti-angiogenic agent, was administered intravenously, resulting in a significant increase in microvessel density and α-smooth muscle actin (αSMA+) pericyte marker-positive vessel density. Under laser irradiation, the combination of DES and DEX enhanced ROS generation within the tumor, resulting in more effective tumor growth inhibition compared to the PDT treatment group alone.

In a previous study, Zhou et al. reported the development of a nanoplatform (CAM NPs) self-assembled from Ce6, axitinib (AXT), D-1-methyltryptophan (1 MT), and HSA. The results indicated that HSA enhanced the accumulation of CAM NPs at the tumor site by binding to the albumin-binding proteins overexpressed in B16F10 tumor cells. Subsequently, CAM NPs disassembled in the acidic tumor microenvironment, releasing the drugs. AXT inhibited the activity of vascular endothelial growth factor receptors (VEGFR), promoting vascular normalization. In vivo experiments demonstrated that treatment with CAM NPs resulted in more perfused tumor vasculature in B16F10 tumors, aiming to improve tumor hypoxia and thereby enhance PDT efficacy. Additionally, strategies that increase blood flow through thermal effects have been employed to improve tissue hypoxia. For example, Gong et al. utilized polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)-modified TiH1.924 nanodots, prepared by liquid phase exfoliation, as photothermal agents under near-infrared irradiation. These nanodots significantly enhanced oxygen content and promoted ultrasound-induced ROS generation by accelerating intratumoral blood flow through mild thermal effects.

In the tumor microenvironment, the overexpression of hypoxia-inducible factors (such as HIF-1α) has been targeted to regulate tumor hypoxia. Broekgaarden et al. co-encapsulated a HIF-1 inhibitor (acriflavine) and a PS (zinc phthalocyanine) in cationic liposomes. In vitro studies revealed that these liposomes could inhibit HIF-1, thereby increasing the extent of PDT-induced cell death under hypoxic conditions. In another study, Li et al. developed a biomimetic membrane-coated nanoplatform (ISZ@JUM) loaded with HIF-1α small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) and ICG to enhance SDT against glioblastoma. The tumor cell membrane coating endowed the nanoplatform with homotypic targeting capabilities, allowing ISZ@JUM to effectively enter GBM cells. Under acidic conditions, ICG and HIF-1α siRNA were released from ISZ@JUM. HIF-1α siRNA silenced the HIF-1α gene, effectively inhibiting HIF-1α expression. Upon US irradiation, ISZ@JUM-treated cells exhibited efficient ROS generation. In vivo results showed that the ISZ@JUM + US group significantly inhibited tumor growth compared to US irradiation alone, ISZ@JUM treatment alone, or ICG + US treatment.

Reducing GSH levels

GSH is a naturally occurring tripeptide composed of glutamate, cysteine, and glycine, and serves as the primary antioxidant within cells. In normal cells, the concentration of GSH is approximately 1–2 mM. However, in tumor cells, the concentration can reach up to 10 mM, which is about four times higher than in normal tissues. Typically, GSH can react with oxidizing agents such as ROS, thereby reducing ROS levels. Therefore, reducing GSH levels presents a feasible strategy to enhance the efficacy of ROS-based therapies.

Depleting GSH

Many current studies focus on depleting already synthesized GSH, as this approach can rapidly achieve a significant reduction in intracellular GSH levels. Several high-valent metal ions, such as Mn, Cu, and Fe, have been developed as antioxidant depletion agents. These ions can react with GSH to produce oxidized glutathione (GSSG), thereby reducing GSH levels. In nanotechnology-based systems, these metal ions are typically incorporated as coatings, nanocores, or linkers within nanostructures.

Chen's team developed mesoporous silica NPs coated with MnO2 (MS@MnO2 NPs), demonstrating for the first time that MnO2 possesses both Fenton-like Mn2+ delivery properties and GSH depletion capabilities (Fig. 15A). Specifically, GSH reduced the MnO2 shell to Mn2+, which was utilized in CDT. The anticancer drug camptothecin (CPT) was loaded into the MS@MnO2 NPs, and the GSH-responsive dissociation of the MnO2 shell facilitated the release of CPT. Additionally, Mn2+ served as an advanced contrast agent for cancer magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Thus, along with the modulation of TME, MS@MnO2 NPs could enable the chemical-CDT combination of MRI monitoring and GSH depletion for cancer treatment. In a recent study, Zhao et al. designed a copper-containing CaCO3 NPs loaded with Ce6 (Cu/CaCO3@Ce6) for comprehensive tumor therapy. Within the TME, Cu/CaCO3@Ce6 released Ca2+, Cu2+, and Ce6. The Cu2+ ions generated •OH through a Fenton-like reaction while simultaneously depleting GSH to prevent it from neutralizing intracellular ROS. Under US irradiation, Ce6 was activated to induce a SDT effect, producing a significant amount of ROS within the cells. Additionally, the influx of Ca2+ exacerbated mitochondrial damage, further enhancing oxidative stress. This multifunctional nanosystem, which induced both CDT and SDT, demonstrated excellent antitumor efficacy in a 4T1 tumor.

In addition to utilizing the redox properties of metal ions, certain carriers containing disulfide bridges can deplete glutathione by breaking disulfide bonds, resulting in the formation of thiols and GSSG. For example, Cheng et al. reacted a disulfide bond-containing crosslinker (SSPS) with a polymer loaded with Ce6 and the hypoxia-activated prodrug tirapazamine (TPZ) to obtain a disulfide-bond crosslinked multifunctional polymer (TPZ/Ce6@SSPS). The disulfide bonds in TPZ/Ce6@SSPS reacted with GSH, leading to GSH depletion. Additionally, the generated highly reactive quinone methide could further deplete GSH. In both in vivo and in vitro experiments, TPZ/Ce6@SSPS significantly reduced GSH levels in 4T1 tumor cells. Compared to mice treated with non-disulfide-crosslinked polymersome-loaded Ce6 (Ce6@CCPS), Ce6@SSPS exhibited more pronounced tumor inhibition, indicating that the sustained depletion of GSH enhanced the PDT effect of Ce6. Furthermore, the hypoxia induced by PDT exacerbated the activation of the prodrug TPZ, achieving enhanced PDT combined with hypoxia-activated chemotherapy. After 14 days of treatment, tumors in the TPZ/Ce6@SSPS group treated mice were almost eliminated. In another research, Hao et al. designed a dual GSH-responsive nanosystem (–SS–HMONs@MB@MnO2 NPs), featuring a disulfide-doped hollow mesoporous organosilica NPs (–SS–HMONs) core loaded with the PS MB, and then coated with a MnO2 layer, which demonstrated tumor-specific PDT capabilities (Fig. 15B). These therapeutic functions were triggered by sequential redox reactions between the MnO2 shell and disulfide bonds with GSH, leading to the degradation of the carrier and the release of MB. Due to GSH depletion, –SS–HMONs@MB@MnO2 NPs generated an effective PDT effect within tumor cells upon laser irradiation.

Inhibiting GSH regeneration

Although numerous studies focus on depleting the already synthesized GSH within tumors, the depletion of GSH can activate multiple protective mechanisms that attempt to restore GSH levels through various pathways. These include the enzyme-mediated GSH synthesis pathway, which relies on precursors such as glutamate, cysteine, and glycine as well as the intracellular recycling pathway of GSH and GSSG controlled by glutathione reductase and glutathione peroxidase. Additionally, under conditions of elevated ROS, the endogenous production of l-cysteine is insufficient for the synthesis of GSH. The primary pathway for extracellular acquisition of l-cysteine involves the transport of its disulfide form (CSSC) via the CSSC/l-glutamate antiporter. Therefore, preventing the regeneration of GSH during its depletion is of critical importance.

Yu et al. reported a multifunctional GSH-inhibiting nanosystem (PCNPs) composed of porous coordination network (PCN-224) NPs as the core, loaded with oxaliplatin and BSO, and coated with an RGD-modified lipid layer (Fig. 16A). BSO is a γ-glutamylcysteine synthetase inhibitor that can block the replenishment of GSH by inhibiting its biosynthesis pathway. This nanosystem actively targeted 4T1 breast cancer cells with high αvβ3 expression through the action of RGD. Co-incubation of PCNPs with cells resulted in a significant reduction in intracellular GSH levels. In contrast, cells treated with NPs lacking BSO showed no change in GSH levels. Under laser irradiation, PCNPs induced intracellular ROS levels 1.5 times higher than those in the BSO-free NPs group, leading to higher cell mortality. Additionally, the reduction in GSH inhibited the GSH-catalyzed inactivation of oxaliplatin, thereby enhancing the synergistic antitumor effects of both PDT and chemotherapy. In another study, Tang et al. constructed manganese-doped silica NPs loaded with sorafenib (FaPEG-MnMSN@SFB). In this system, the chemotherapeutic agent sorafenib inhibited GSH biosynthesis by blocking the CSSC/l-glutamate antiporter, while MnMSN depleted the overexpressed GSH within the cells. This dual depletion of GSH resulted in FaPEG-MnMSN@SFB exhibiting enhanced antitumor efficacy in CDT.

Another approach to inhibiting glutathione biosynthesis is by blocking nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH). During GSH regeneration, NADPH acts as a cofactor for glutathione reductase, facilitating the conversion of GSSG back to its reduced form. Fortunately, some nanozymes, such as Prussian blue NPs and gold NPs, have demonstrated NADPH oxidase activity, offering a novel perspective for targeting NADPH to inhibit GSH regeneration. For example, Han et al. developed MnOx-coated Au NPs (AMO) for blocking glutathione regeneration (Fig. 16B). Specifically, in the mildly acidic and GSH-elevated TME, MnOx undergoes degradation, which not only facilitated the depletion of GSH but also released Mn2⁺ ions to exert a CDT effect. Simultaneously, the Au NPs exhibited the ability to catalyze NADPH oxidation. After 10 h of AMO treatment, NADPH levels in 4T1 cells decreased, leading to a sharp decline in GSH levels, thereby enhancing the therapeutic efficacy of CDT.

Increasing H2O2 levels

In the previous discussion, we explored various natural enzymes or nanozymes that overcome hypoxia in ROS-generating strategies by catalyzing the in situ conversion of endogenous H2O2 into O2. Beyond this, H2O2 serves as a crucial substrate for the Fenton reaction in CDT. However, despite the fact that the concentration of H2O2 in cancer cells is five times higher than in normal cells, the intracellular levels of H2O2 remain insufficient to achieve optimal anticancer effects. Therefore, strategies to increase the levels of H2O2 within tumors are essential. Current approaches mainly focus on either the delivery of exogenous H2O2 or the in situ generation of H2O2 at the tumor site.

Providing exogenous H2O2

H2O2 is a viscous liquid that is miscible with water in any proportion. In one study, Li et al. encapsulated liquid H2O2 and Fe3O4 NPs separately within the hydrophilic core and shell of a PLGA polymer. Upon exposure to US, the PLGA polymer was disrupted, releasing H2O2, which then underwent a Fenton reaction with the concurrently released Fe3O4. Notably, this H2O2-loaded nanomaterial provided an additional source of H2O2, demonstrating controllable ROS generation by modulating the amount of encapsulated H2O2. To avoid relying on external energy sources, Lin et al. developed copper peroxide (CP) nanodots (Fig. 17A) by coordinating H2O2 with Cu2+ ions in the presence of hydroxide ions, using polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) as a stabilizer. These CP nanodots, with their small particle size, accumulate within tumor cells and dissociate in the acidic tumor microenvironment, releasing Cu2+ and H2O2. This dissociation trigged a Fenton reaction, providing a novel strategy to enhance the efficacy of CDT.

Directly delivering H2O2 offers a favorable approach for inhibiting tumor growth. However, encapsulating H2O2 within nanoparticles may face challenges such as premature H2O2 loss and limitations in encapsulation efficiency. Therefore, developing methods for the specific generation of H2O2 is essential, with metal peroxide-based nanoplatforms serving as a representative example. In one study, copper peroxide (CuO2) nanodots were assembled with DOX-loaded mesoporous silica NPs to form a nanocatalyst (DOX@MSN@CuO2). In the mildly acidic TME, DOX@MSN@CuO2 decomposed to release Cu2+ and H2O2. The Cu2+ was then reduced to Cu+ by reacting with GSH, and the generated Cu+ catalyzed a Fenton-like reaction with H2O2, accelerating ROS production. Consequently, CuO2 served as a continuous H2O2 precursor, providing a sustained supply of H2O2 for CDT. Additionally, the release of DOX further contributed to antitumor activity. In 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, this combination exhibited superior antitumor effects, achieving a synergistic therapeutic outcome through the integration of chemotherapy and CDT.

Additionally, CaO2 decomposes upon contact with water, releasing both O2 and H2O2. Beyond its role in alleviating hypoxia, CaO2 is also valuable for enhancing H2O2 production. For instance, Liu et al. developed a self-sustaining H2O2/O2 nanoplatform ((MSNs@CaO2-ICG)@LA) by co-loading CaO2 NPs and ICG into manganese silicate NPs (MSNs) with a lauric acid (LA) coating (Fig. 17B). Upon laser irradiation, LA was melted by the PTT effect generated by ICG, exposing CaO2. Subsequently, CaO2 reacted with H2O, rapidly producing H2O2 and O2. Furthermore, the MSN component consumed GSH to generate Mn2+, initiating CDT. This self-sustaining H2O2 and O2 supply system significantly enhanced ROS generation in CDT/PDT. In a 14-day treatment study using an MCF-7 tumor-bearing nude mouse model, (MSNs@CaO2-ICG)@LA completely eradicated the tumors under laser irradiation.

Amplifing endogenous H2O2

In current research, converting naturally occurring endogenous substances within the TME into H2O2 has gained increasing popularity. Strategies for in situ H2O2 generation typically involve the use of in situ enzymes such as GOx and lactate oxidase (LOD). Compared to normal cells, tumor cells are more prone to undergo anaerobic glycolysis, and those undergoing aerobic glycolysis require large amounts of glucose while producing excessive lactate in the TME. GOx and LOD can catalyze the production of H2O2 from glucose and lactate respectively, making this approach a promising strategy for enhancing tumor therapy. For example, Yao et al. developed a nanoplatform (FePBG@GOX) for multi-cascade catalytic reactions and efficient CDT by self-assembling GOx onto iron-doped phosphate-based glass (FePBG) NPs, with polyethyleneimine as a surface coating. When FePBG@GOX entered tumor cells, GOx acted as the initial catalytic agent, consuming glucose within the tumor tissue to generate large amounts of H2O2. Subsequently, the FePBG NPs mediate a Fenton reaction to produce a significant quantity of •OH. FePBG@GOX was capable of generating sufficient H2O2, achieving a tumor inhibition rate of 94.65 % while also demonstrating high in vivo safety. For the same purpose, Zhang et al. developed Nb2C quantum dots loaded with LOD and coated with a red blood cell membrane (Nb2C QDs/LOD@RBC) (Fig. 18A). The Nb2C quantum dots acted as catalysts, converting H2O2 into •OH through a Fenton-like reaction. LOD consumed lactate in the TME to produce H2O2, replenishing the consumed H2O2. The RBC membrane coating enhanced the accumulation of Nb2C QDs/LOD@RBC at the tumor site. Among 4T1 cells treated with Nb2C QDs/LOD@RBC, the increase in •OH production was the most significant. After intravenous injection, Nb2C QDs/LOD@RBC exhibited the most pronounced antitumor effect, stronger than nanocatalysts not coated with membrane (Nb2C QDs/LOD) and nanocatalysts not loaded with LOD (Nb2C QDs), due to the active targeting by RBC membrane and the H2O2-generating function of LOD. Additionally, lactate consumption facilitated the repolarization of M2 macrophages to the M1 phenotype, alleviating the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment and further enhancing CDT.

In addition to natural enzymes, nanozymes are also utilized to enhance H₂O₂ generation. Shi and his team were the first to report a dual inorganic nanozyme-triggered cascade catalytic system (DMSN-Au-Fe₃O₄ NPs). This system was composed of ultrasmall and highly dispersed Au NPs and Fe₃O₄ NPs sequentially loaded into the large mesopores of dendritic mesoporous silica NPs (DMSNs). Au NPs acted as a GOx-mimicking inorganic nanozyme. Upon entry into tumor cells, the Au NPs initially catalyzed the oxidation of glucose to H₂O₂. Subsequently, the Fe₃O₄ NPs catalyzed the conversion of H₂O₂ to •OH via the Fenton reaction. This nanozyme system enhanced ROS production in 4T1 cells and achieved a tumor inhibition rate of 69.08 %. Superoxide dismutase (SOD) is a class of metalloenzymes that catalyze the dismutation of O2•−into H₂O₂. Therefore, activating SOD or mimicking SOD activity can also increase H₂O₂ levels. In one study, Xiao et al. synthesized nanoselenium (nano-Se)-coated manganese carbonate-deposited iron oxide nanoparticle (MCDION-Se). In the acidic TME, manganese carbonate dissolved, releasing Mn2⁺ ions that induce tumor cell death via the Fenton reaction. Simultaneously, the nano-Se on MCDION-Se promoted the generation of O2•− and activated SOD in the tumor, which subsequently catalyzed the conversion of O2•− to H₂O₂. When co-incubated with HeLa tumor cells, the cells treated with MCDION-Se exhibited significantly increased levels of SOD, ROS fluorescence, and cytotoxicity compared to NPs without nano-Se (MCDION). This demonstrated that nano-Se effectively activates SOD, catalyzing the conversion of O2•− to H₂O₂, thereby further enhancing the CDT effect. Additionally, nano-Se could serve as an ATP inhibitor, blocking the energy supply to cancer cells, further leading to cancer cell apoptosis. Recently, Sang et al. modified the small molecule inhibitor 3-amino-1,2,4-triazole (3-AT) and PEG on a zeolitic imidazolate framework-67 (ZIF-67) to construct an H₂O₂ homeostasis disruptor (PZIF67-AT) (Fig. 18B). PZIF67-AT exhibited SOD activity, converting O₂•− into H₂O₂, thereby promoting H₂O₂ generation. Moreover, 3-AT, as a CAT inhibitor, was released in a mildly acidic environment, inhibiting the decomposition of H₂O₂ by CAT. This strategy of elevating H₂O₂ levels significantly enhanced the efficacy of CDT, markedly inhibiting tumor growth in mice.

Additionally, certain chemotherapeutic drugs, such as cisplatin, β-lapachone, and DOX can activate NADPH oxidase (NOX), converting endogenous O₂ into O₂•−. Based on this mechanism, Chen's research group developed a self-assembled nanosystem (DPPF NPs) comprising DOX, a platinum prodrug-polyphenol, polyethylene glycol-polyphenol, and Fe³⁺. In the tumor microenvironment, the platinum prodrug-polyphenol was reduced to cisplatin, which, together with DOX, co-activated NOXs, promoting the generation of O₂•−. Subsequently, O₂•− was catalyzed by the SOD-like activity of polyphenols to produce H₂O₂. This process enhanced the CDT effect mediated by Fe³⁺ and further amplified chemotherapy through the Fenton reaction. Compared to chemotherapy or CDT alone, DPPF NPs demonstrated the highest tumor inhibition efficacy.

Adjusting the pH

pH is a crucial factor for CDT, with a pH of 2–4 being the optimal range for Fenton/Fenton like reaction. As the pH increases to between 5 and 8, the reaction rate decreases. As we all know, the pH of the TME in solid tumors typically ranges from 6.5 to 7, which restricts the efficiency of the Fenton /Fenton like reaction. Therefore, strategies aimed at lowering the intracellular pH of tumor cells represent an effective approach to enhancing the therapeutic efficacy of CDT.

CO₂ is a major source of acidity within tumors, and its regulation is closely associated with carbonic anhydrase IX (CA IX). CA IX, a transmembrane protein, catalyzes the conversion of CO₂ into bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and protons (H⁺). Under hypoxic conditions, HIF-1α further induces the overexpression of CA IX, leading to extracellular acidification. Therefore, inhibiting CAIX expression may reduce the formation of extracellular HCO₃⁻/H⁺, thereby enhancing intracellular acidification. Bu's team developed amorphous iron NPs (AFeNPs) loaded with a carbonic anhydrase IX inhibitor (CAI) (Fig. 19A). Theoretically, AFeNPs suppress CA IX expression, thereby increasing intracellular H⁺ concentration, which is favorable for the Fenton reaction. To validate this hypothesis, the intracellular pH was measured using the pH-sensitive probe BCECF-AM. Fluorescence intensity revealed that co-incubation of AFeNPs@CAI with cells under hypoxic conditions significantly lowered intracellular pH in a time-dependent manner. Additionally, the acidic microenvironment facilitated the release of Fe2⁺ from AFeNPs, accelerating the generation of •OH. In vitro experiments confirmed that this strategy, through CA IX inhibition, effectively enhanced •OH production, resulting in increased cancer cell death.

Additionally, the pH within lysosomes of tumor cells is approximately 4.5. Disruption of lysosomal function can release acidic contents into the cytoplasm, leading to cytoplasmic acidification. For example, Li et al. functionalized a near-infrared (NIR) photothermal oxazine molecule with hydrophilic biotin-polyethylene glycol (PEG) chains, forming self-assembled nanostructures (PTO-Biotin NPs) (Fig. 19B). These PTO-Biotin NPs specifically accumulated in the lysosomes of tumor cells. Upon NIR irradiation, PTO-Biotin NPs induced lysosomal dysfunction, causing the release of protons into the cytoplasm and triggering intracellular acidification. After treatment with PTO-Biotin NPs, the intracellular pH decreased from 7.4 to 6.24. Concurrently, lysosomal dysfunction led to impaired autophagy. Importantly, the lysosomal dysfunction induced by light-activated PTO-Biotin NPs significantly enhanced the Fenton reaction and triggered ferroptosis. In an in vivo tumor model, the combination of PTO- Biotin NPs and laser irradiation resulted in substantial damage to tumor tissues.

In addition to remodeling the tumor microenvironment by lowering pH, developing Fenton-like drug delivery systems that are independent of pH holds significant importance. To date, several metal-based nanoparticles have been reported to exhibit Fenton-like activity under mildly acidic or even neutral pH conditions. For example, Chen et al. synthesized an ultra-small bovine serum albumin-modified chalcopyrite nanoparticle (BSA-CuFeS2 NPs) with high dispersivity and biocompatibility. These BSA-CuFeS₂ NPs were able to induce a blue reaction and generate substantial amounts of •OH radicals across a range of pH conditions (7.4, 6.5, 5.4, 4, and 3). This phenomenon is attributed to the formation of copper-based Fenton-like hydrolytic complexes, which are water-soluble and exhibit higher catalytic activity under neutral conditions compared to traditional iron-based catalysts, thereby enabling effective CDT cancer therapy.

Other strategies to enhance ROS-based non-invasive therapies

Synergetic PDT, SDT and CDT

Due to the complexity of the TME, a single therapeutic strategy may be difficult to achieve the desired therapeutic effect. Theoretically, multimodal therapy overcomes the inherent limitations and demonstrates higher efficacy compared to monotherapy. To date, researchers have developed multiple synergistic strategies to increase ROS production and then enhance anti-tumor effects. For example, PDT generate 1O2 and O2•− under NIR light, and O2•− radicals react with SOD to form H2O2, thereby supplying additional H2O2 for CDT. Ultimately, PDT and CDT work synergistically to maximize the production of ROS. Using sensitizers which are appropriate for both PDT and SDT, it not only generates excellent ROS but also inhibits the growth of deep-seated tumors through SDT. In recent years, researchers have engineered a diverse array of nano-platform systems for anti-tumor therapy by integrating PDT, SDT, and CDT. Currently, there are limited examples of simultaneous combination of SDT, CDT and PDT, However, it is hoped that research on the integrated application of these three therapies will inspire researchers to develop more efficient and comprehensive treatment solutions.

For instance, Wang et al. constructed a Janus nanostructure (UPFB) composed of UCNPs and porphyrin-based MOFs. The UPFB was activated by NIR laser and US simultaneously to generate a large amount of ROS for PDT and SDT (Fig. 20 A). In this process US was able to enhance the efficiency of energy transfer between UCNPs and photosensitizers, therefore enabling a further increase in ROS generation. In addition, Fe3+ coordinated in UPFB could catalyze the generation of O2 from endogenous H2O2 to overcome tumor hypoxia. Furthermore, under acidic pH, Fe3+ was reduced to Fe2+, and the subsequent Fenton reaction between Fe2+ and H2O2 facilitated the CDT performance. Therefore,the nanostructure UPFB could simultaneously achieve multimodal treatment (PDT/SDT/CDT). U14 tumor bearing mouse model was used for vivo experiment, On day 14 after treatment, mice treated with UPFB upon 808 nm laser irradiation and US irradiation exhibited a high antitumor efficiency.

Du et al. developed Pd-single-atom coordinated porphyrin-based polymeric networks (Pd-Pta/Por) as biocatalysts for the synergistic tumor therapy of PDT/SDT/CDT. Pd-Pta/Por exhibited peroxidase-like catalytic activity and generated a substantial amount of •OH for CDT through the Fenton reaction. As photo/sonosensitizers, the excellent 1O2 production ability of porphyrin polymers leaded to considerable PDT and SDT results. In comparison to Pd-Pta/Por triggered CDT/PDT and CDT/SDT dual-modality therapies, the integration of PDT/SDT/CDT modalities has demonstrated a significantly enhanced generation of ROS in tumor cells, leading to the highest observed level of apoptosis. This effect was also validated in vivo, where tumor growth was markedly suppressed in the PDT/SDT/CDT group in comparison to the other groups. Similarly, the nanoplatform Pd@MOF-525@HA prepared by Duan et al. also showed a synergistic effect. In this nanosystem, porphyrin MOF-525 was used as a sono/photosensitizer for PDT and SDT. In addition, the well-dispersed Pd nanocubes within MOF-525 reacted with intracellular H2O2 in a Fenton-like reaction. It was demonstrated that the nanoplatform achieved effective ROS generation and apoptosis induction through PDT/SDT/CDT mediation.

Currently, research concerning the integration of CDT, SDT, and PDT on a nano-platform remains in its investigational phase. Nevertheless, the development of rational, secure, and efficacious multimodal therapeutic strategies represents an emerging trend.

Medical gas plasma

Although a large number of in vivo and in vitro experiments have confirmed that nanoplatform-based multimodality significantly improves the anti-tumor effects of ROS-based non-invasive therapies, tumors remain lethal. Moreover, multimodal therapeutic platforms have more complex synthesis steps and are limited in their manipulability. Therefore, new and easy-to-use methods to improve the efficacy of non-invasive therapies should be continuously sought. Medical gas plasma technology has received attention in recent years for its antitumor activity against a variety of cancers.

Plasma is a partially ionized gas containing many active components including, photons (as UV or visible radiation), electric fields, electrons, ions, thermal radiation and neutral radicals such as reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (ROS/RNS). Cold atmospheric plasma (CAP) is a plasma that operates at body temperature. When used with medical gas plasma devices, this highly reactive gas can be efficiently delivered to target cells and tissues without causing thermal damage. It has been developed as a medical gas plasma technique for the treatment of a wide range of diseases. Fig. 20B shows the characteristics of medical gas plasma. Since in vitro killing effect of plasma on human melanoma skin cancer cells was first reported in 2007, gas plasma has been shown to reduce tumor cell proliferation and induce apoptosis or immunogenic cell death, leading to an effective anticancer effect in many types of tumors. Clinically, medical gas plasma has been used in the palliative care of patients with head and neck cancers. ROS are believed to play a central role in the effects of gas plasma on living organisms. Unlike other ROS-based non-invasive therapies, gas plasma treatment introduces large amounts of ROS from outside the cells. Cancer cells contain less cholesterol in their cell membranes than healthy cells, increasing the permeability of the cell membrane, so ROS may penetrate more easily into tumor cells to induce cell death.

Currently, gas plasma has been used to treat tumors in conjunction with a variety of therapies, including chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and immunotherapy. In particular, the association with a variety of nanoparticles demonstrates potential in improving antitumor efficacy, which can be achieved by increasing ROS production and enhancing the uptake of nanoparticles into tumor cells. In addition, gas plasma can rapidly and reversibly lower tissue pH. In theory, CAP in conjunction with CDT might create the required reaction conditions for the Fenton reaction. Although there are relatively few reports on the use of CAP in combination with other ROS-based non-invasive therapies, some investigators have used CAP as a novel light source in conjunction with PDT. The results of this study showed that CAP enhanced the ability of PDT to induce tumor cell death by enhancing intracellular ROS production in tumor cells and promoting the accumulation of photosensitizers in tumor cells. In summary, medical gas plasma is expected to play a unique role in anti-tumor therapy as an ideal ROS promoter in synergy with other non-invasive treatments.

Summary and outlook

In conclusion, ROS-based non-invasive therapies, including PDT, SDT, and CDT, generate ROS through different mechanisms to kill tumors and have been proven to be powerful tools for cancer diagnosis and treatment. We have summarized various strategies for enhancing these non-invasive therapies through nanoplatforms. Despite significant progress, the field remains in its early stages, and substantial challenges persist in translating these strategies into clinical practice.

First and foremost, ensuring the biosafety of nanomedicines is crucial to prevent unnecessary toxic side effects. In the design of nanoplatforms based on PDT, SDT, and CDT, researchers have developed various multifunctional nanosystems that have demonstrated satisfactory tumor eradication in vivo. However, the preparation of many ROS-driven nanosystems, particularly those combining multiple therapeutic approaches, involves complex synthesis procedures and formulations. Achieving the industrial-scale production of these nanosystems remains a significant challenge. Additionally, the low degradability and potential toxicity, including both acute and chronic toxicities, of transition metal-based Fenton reaction catalysts and inorganic nanomedicines containing high-Z elements in vivo are major concerns and significant barriers to clinical translation. Balancing biosafety with therapeutic efficacy thus remains a highly challenging issue. Although many studies have employed conventional methods such as hemolysis tests and histological analysis to assess the safety of various nanomedicines, the issue of long-term safety, particularly concerning prolonged in vivo retention, remains unresolved. Therefore, future research should comprehensively evaluate the long-term biosafety, biodistribution, and biodegradation of nanomedicines from multiple perspectives, as these factors are prerequisites for clinical translation.

High levels of ROS in tumor cells can damage proteins, nucleic acids, and organelles, ultimately leading to cell death. However, if the nanosytem accumulates in normal tissues, the highly toxic ROS can also cause non-selective damage to healthy cells. In addition, the extremely short half-life of ROS and their severely limited diffusion distance may constrain their efficacy. Therefore, the precise localization of NPs within tumor cells is crucial for the effectiveness of ROS-based strategies. To enhance antitumor efficacy, designing NPs with precise organelle-targeting capabilities, such as targeting the nucleus, lysosomes, or mitochondria can increase their internalization within tumor cells, thereby maximizing the cytotoxic effects of ROS on cancer cells while minimizing damage to normal cells.

Current research clearly indicates that increasing ROS levels can effectively enhance anticancer efficacy. However, in current studies, the toxicity threshold for ROS induction in different types of tumor cells has not been specifically quantified. Additionally, due to variations in experimental conditions, it is challenging to directly compare the efficiency and types of ROS generated by different agents. For various treatment modalities, such as PDT, the efficacy is closely related to the dosage of photosensitizers, as well as the intensity and duration of light exposure. Similarly, the efficacy of SDT depends on the dosage of sonosensitizers and the frequency and intensity of ultrasound. In CDT, the amount of catalyst also plays a critical role in ROS generation. Increasing evidence suggests that ROS is a double-edged sword: at low doses, it can promote tumor initiation and progression, while at high doses, it can induce cell death. Therefore, a better understanding of the complex roles of ROS modulators in different cancers is necessary. The development of novel molecular probes capable of specifically monitoring ROS is crucial for determining the appropriate ROS dosage, timing, and intensity. Before ROS-related therapies can be established as adjuncts or alternatives to traditional cancer treatments, more experimental work and discussions are needed to establish standardized protocols.

In current antitumor research involving ROS-based non-invasive therapies, almost all pharmacodynamic evaluations are conducted using in vitro 2D cell cultures and mouse subcutaneous tumor models. However, these models struggle to replicate the physicochemical properties of the human TME. Additionally, immunocompromised mice used in human-derived xenograft models lack a tumor immune microenvironment, making them inadequate representations of cancer patients. Specifically, in experiments simulating the efficacy of PDT for deep-seated tumors, it is common to use materials like pig or chicken tissue of equivalent thickness to model deep tumors. However, such in vivo treatment effects often fail to meet the requirements for preclinical trials. Therefore, future research should focus on improving animal models, such as developing in vitro 3D models, conducting in situ tumor studies in mice or even larger animals, or utilizing organ-on-a-chip technology to capture the 3D structure and physiological characteristics of human organs and the TME. These advancements would enable a more accurate assessment of the effectiveness of NPs in human tumors during preclinical studies.

In summary, ROS-based non-invasive therapies represent a promising strategy for cancer treatment, especially when combined with advanced nanodelivery materials and technologies, showcasing a higher potential for clinical application. Although the journey from the laboratory to the clinic will be slow and challenging, we remain confident that precise and personalized ROS-based non-invasive therapies will eventually make this transition.
