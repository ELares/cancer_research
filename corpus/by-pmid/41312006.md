# DNA methylation of CA10 regulates the proliferation and metastasis of bladder cancer.

## Abstract

This study investigated the role of CA10 gene DNA methylation in bladder cancer progression, revealing that hypermethylation significantly reduces CA10 expression in tumor tissues compared to adjacent tissues. Utilizing TCGA data analysis with the ChAMP package for DMPs/DMRs, alongside qRT-PCR/semiquantitative RT-PCR for methylation and expression validation, researchers found that treatment with the demethylating agent 5-Aza-dC restored CA10 expression. Functional assays (flow cytometry, MTT, transwell) demonstrated that this restoration inhibited cancer cell proliferation, migration, and cell cycle progression. Furthermore, in vivo tumor xenograft models confirmed CA10's tumor-suppressive role and the efficacy of epigenetic therapy in inhibiting tumor growth. The study identifies DNA methylation-driven silencing of CA10 as a key mechanism in bladder carcinogenesis and highlights its broader diagnostic potential across cancers.

Introduction

Bladder cancer is a urinary tract malignancy that is becoming increasingly prevalent worldwide due to exposure to industrial substances and an aging population. The global incidence of bladder cancer is steadily increasing, with an estimated annual growth rate of approximately 2%. By 2025, projections indicate that the number of new cases will approach 900,000. The pathology of bladder cancer is diverse, encompassing transitional cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and adenocarcinoma of the urinary tract. Transitional cell carcinoma accounts for over 90% of all cases, making it one of the predominant subtypes. In certain regions, such as the United States and Western Europe, the incidence of urothelial carcinoma of the bladder is higher than that of all other cancer types combined. The incidence of malignant tumors of the urinary system in China has been steadily increasing over the years, with bladder cancer also exhibiting an upward trend. This phenomenon may be attributed to factors such as population aging, smoking habits, occupational exposure to toxic chemicals, and other related variables. Given this epidemiological situation, it is imperative to enhance prevention and treatment strategies to mitigate the impact of bladder cancer on public health.

DNA methylation is becoming more widely recognized as a pivotal technique for the early detection of cancer. It is particularly valuable due to its early occurrence in cancer development, consistent presence, and detectability using techniques. Additionally, numerous investigations have documented distinct methylation patterns in the development of cellular proliferation and differentiation. Evidence from studies on DNA methylation in bladder cancer highlights its significant potential and practical value in tumor diagnosis. Specifically, the characteristic alterations observed in urine cell-free DNA methylation have been validated as an ideal biomarker for non-invasive diagnosis, prognostication, and monitoring of bladder cancer. For instance, an elevated prevalence of Twist1 gene methylation has been identified in both bladder cancer tumor tissues and urine samples, indicating a novel candidate marker for diagnosing bladder cancer. Current evidence substantiates that abnormal alterations in DNA methylation, such as CpG island hypermethylation and genome-wide aberrant methylation patterns, play a critical role in the development and progression of bladder cancer. Therefore, conducting comprehensive investigations to identify accurate DNA methylation biomarkers may represent a crucial avenue for screening bladder cancer and developing targeted therapies.

5-Aza-dC, also known as 5-Aza-2-deoxycytidine, induces demethylation and initiates phen associated with the reactivation of genes that have been epigenetically silenced. Wang et al. observed a methylation level at the CpG1 site in DAB2-Aza-dC. CA10, a zinc metalloenzyme belonging to the carbonic anhydrase family, is believed to play a role in central nervous system development during brain ontogenesis. Although there is limited current research on CA10, a study conducted by Chung et al. and published in Cancer Epidemiology suggested that CA10 has the potential to serve as a precise biomarker for early detection of bladder tumors in urine sediments. In our study, we investigated differentially methylated genes in patients with bladder cancer through methylome analysis using paired tumor and surrounding tissues. Notably, CA10 emerged as one of the genes displaying significant differential methylation, thereby indicating a potential regulatory role of CA10 in bladder cancer. By focusing on CA10 and its association with bladder cancer, we endeavor to prognosticate the condition.

We utilized DNA methylation data from the TCGA to investigate potential tumor suppressor genes in bladder cancer. The analysis unveiled CA10 as a promising candidate, which was confirmed by cell assays and a tumor xenograft model. While our preliminary results provide support for the role of CA10 in suppressing bladder cancer, additional research is warranted to confirm and elaborate upon these findings. This study contributes to enhancing our understanding of the significance of CA10 as a diagnostic marker or therapeutic target for bladder cancer.

Materials and Methods

Methylome analysis

To acquire DNA methylation datasets for bladder cancer, we downloaded them from the The cancer genome atlas (TCGA) database (). We performed correlation analysis using a total of 33 paired tumor and adjacent samples out of 414 cases, based on the available data on clinical characteristics and DNA methylation. For our research purposes, we employed the AMP package, which encompasses limma-based differential methylation probe analysis as well as association-based differential methylated region (DMR) analysis functions.

Cell cultures

We also acquired the SV-HUC-1 normal cell line from the same source. All cells were cultured at a temperature of 37°C in a humidified environment with 5% CO2. SV-HUC-1 cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). The 5,637 cell line was cultured using the RPM1640 medium containing 10% FBS (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), while the J82 cells were maintained using the minimum essential medium supplemented with% FBS also provided by Sigma. The UMUC3 cell line was cultured using the MEM medium containing 10% FBS from Sigma. The cells used in this study were procured from Wuhan Procell Biology (Wuhan, China). In this study, the involvement of mechanistic molecules in bladder cancer was demonstrated using various sources of bladder cancer cell lines.

DNA extraction and bisulfite conversion

We performed the extraction of fresh tissues according to the manufacturer’s protocol using the DNA Mini Kit (Qiagen, Frankfurt, Germany). To induce changes in gene expression without altering the DNA sequence, we employed the EZ DNA Methylation-Gold Kit (ZYMO Research, Irvine, CA) for the modification of DNA obtained from each sample.

Real-time fluorescent quantitative PCR (qRT-PCR)

The TRIzol reagent was utilized for extracting total mRNA from the cells, which was subsequently reverse transcribed into complementary DNA. The reverse transcription process entailed an incubation period of 10 ‍min at 25°C, followed by 30 ‍min at 50°C, and finally 5 ‍min at 85°C. For detection purposes, a fluorescence quantitative PCR kit was employed. The quantitative fluorescence process was conducted under the following conditions: initial denaturation at 95°C for 5 ‍min, followed by denaturation at 95°C for 15 ‍s, and annealing/extension at 60°C for one min; this cycle was repeated a total of 40 times. The specificity of each primer was confirmed using melting curve analysis, and β-actin was utilized as an internal reference gene for relative quantification in this study.

Methylation-specific PCR (MS-PCR)

The primers for methylation-specific PCR and unmethylation-specific PCR (Supplemental Table 1*) were designed using the Express software. Bisulphite-treated DNA was amplified separately using CA10 meth unmethylation-specific primers and AmpliTaq-Gold DNA Polymerase from Thermo Fisher Scientific (Waltham).

The PCR protocol included an initial saturation step at 95°C for 5 ‍min; followed by 31 cycles of denaturation at 95°C for 45 ‍s, annealing at 59°C, and extension at 72°C for 30 ‍s; concluding with a final extension step at 72°C min. In the subsequent step, 20 PCR products were separated on a 2% agarose gel, and ethidium bromide was used to stain.

Demethylation with 5-Aza-dC

The cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 106 cells/ml. After 24 ‍h, the cells were treated with 1 ‍μM of the DNA demethylating agent 5-Aza-dC (Sigma-Aldrich) and an equivalent concentration of vehicle (dimethyl sulfoxide) for 5 days. The ells were then harvested for DNA and RNA extractions.

Analysis of CA10 mRNA

The mRNA expression of CA10 was quantified using quantitative real-time PCR. The total RNA from cell lines was extracted using TRIzol (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) and evaluated based on the A260/A280 ratio. To generate cDNA, 1 ‍μg of total RNA was reverse transcribed using the TM III First-Strand Synthesis Kit (Invitrogen). QPCR amplification was performed on the LightCycler 480 Real-Time PCR System (Roche, Basel, Switzerland) using SYBR Realtime PCR Master Mixers used are listed in Supplemental Table 2*. The designed primers were strategically positioned to cover the exon-ex, and conducted in triplicate, with β-actin serving as an internal control.

MTT assay

A density of one well per cell was used to seed the bladder cancer cells that were showing logarithmic growth. Following a 24-h incubation at a controlled temperature without serum, the treatment with 1 ‍μM 5-Aza-dC was initiated at different time points (24, 48, 72, and 96 ‍h). The cell proliferation activity was assessed using a reader that measured absorbance at a wavelength of 490 ‍nm.

Transwell assay

For cell migration assays, a migration insert (8-μm pore) (Corning Inc., Corning, NY) was placed into a 24-well the lower chamber, RPMI1640 medium with 10% FBS was added as chemoattractant. A total of 2 × 105 bladder cancer cells with or without 5-Aza-dC treatment were seeded on the upper insert. After incubation for 24 ‍h, the insert was fixed, stained, and counted.

Flow cytometry (FCM) assay

The cell cycle status was determined using propidium iodide. Briefly, bladder cancer cells were cultured in serum for 24 ‍h. Following three wash PBS, the 4°C in ethanol. Afterward, they were incubated with a mixture containing the Aldrich reagent at a concentration of 50 ‍mg/ml and RNase A (Sigma-Aldrich) at a concentration of 25 ‍mg/ml for 30 ‍min at 25°C. The fluorescence intensity emitted by a FACScan instrument (BD Biosciences, San Diego, CA) enabled measurement of cells across the Go/G1 and G2/M phases of the cell cycle.

Tumor xenograft

Female BALB/c nude mice, aged six-weeks, were purchased from Beijing Weitong Lihua Laboratory Animal Co., Ltd. (Beijing, China) and maintained in a controlled environment from specific-pathogens. To establish the tumor model, J82 cells (1 × 106/200 ‍μl PBS) were subcutaneously injected until an average tumor size of approximately 100 ‍mm3 was obtained. Subsequently, the mice were randomly divided into two groups. The experimental group received epigenetic therapy using 5-Aza-dC (5 ‍mg/kg) via intraperitoneal injection the control group. Finally, sodium pentobarbital was used to kill the animals and subsequent analysis was performed. Tumor dimensions were measured using a caliper to determine the longest long diameter (a) and the longest short diameter (b), allowing calculation of the tumor volume according to the formula: V = 1/2 ab2 (mm3). Mice were monitored every three days. In this study, the maximum long diameter of the tumor was 14.54 ‍mm, the maximum short diameter was 9.62 ‍mm, and the calculated maximum tumor volume was 725.42 ‍mm3. The procedures involving animals were performed with the approval from the Animal Care and Use Committee of the Medical Institution of Shengzhou People’s Hospital (No: SYDW202206071).

Immunohistochemistry

The tumors were obtained intraoperatively and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA). Subsequently, they were embedded in paraffin to achieve a thickness of approximately 6 ‍μm. Formalin was used for fixation, which was subsequently followed by a series of solutions. The sections were then incubated overnight with Anti-Ki67 (Abcam, Cambridge, UK) at a dilution of 1:250. After a thorough washing of the sections anti-Rabbit secondary antibody (ab) concentration of 1:500. The number of positive cells was quantified using the ImageJ software.

Statistical analysis

The statistical analysis was performed using the GraphPad Prism 6.0 software (GraphPad Inc., La Jolla, CA). The data were presented as the mean value. A t test was used to compare the two groups, while analysis of variance was used for comparing expression considered 0.05.

Results

Genome-wide methylation data was from TCGA for tumor/adjacent tissues

In our study, we obtained comprehensive genome-wide methylation data from the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database for a total of 33 pairs of bladder cancer tumor and adjacent tissue samples. The clustering of 395,735 probes in normal samples was visually represented by constructing a dendrogram, providing valuable insights into the overall methylation patterns (Fig. 1A). To highlight the disparities in DNA methylation patterns between tumor and non-tumor samples, we created a heatmap illustrating the differential methylation of the top 1,000 imprinted CpG sites (Fig. 1B). The heatmap confirmed the existence of distinct methylation patterns by presenting compelling evidence of significant differences in DNA methylation profiles between tumor and non-tumor samples. Furthermore, these findings revealed a clear clustering of samples according to their respective phenotypes. In the distribution of all probes, there is a predominant enrichment of differentially methylated probes.

CA10 hypermethylation and lower mRNA expression

Differential analysis of methylation patterns in tumors and normal tissues revealed 40 genes that exhibited significant differences in methylation. Particularly noteworthy was the observation that CA10 displayed distinct methylation patterns between tumor tissues and normal tissues (Fig. 2A). The provided heatmap illustrates the methylation data by employing a blue-red color scheme to represent the varying levels of methylation between low and high. Importantly, the CA10’s differentially methylated exhibited a significant value within the bladder cancer tumor group (Fig. 2B). Kaplan–Meier analysis revealed that the overall survival of patients with increased CA10 expression was significantly reduced in the high-methylation group (Fig. 2C). Tumor tissues exhibited increased CA10 expression in comparison to their corresponding normal tissues, as revealed by Gene Expression Profiling Interactive Analysis (GEPIA) () (Fig. 2D). Collectively, these findings provide compelling evidence that a strong association exists between hypermethylation of CA10 and downregulation of mRNA expression in bladder cancer. This implies that aberrant DNA methylation may exert regulatory control over CA10 expression, potentially influencing the pathogenesis of bladder cancer.

5-Aza-dC induced demethylation of CA10

In this study, we detected the expression of CA10 in tumor cells and bladder epithelial cells using quantitative methylation-specific PCR (qMSP) and found that its methylation level was significantly increased in tumor cells (Fig. 3A). The minimum effective concentration of 5-Aza-dC was observed in the MTT assay, and a distinct response was observed at a concentration of 1 ‍μM. Hence, subsequent experiments were conducted employing this selected concentration (Fig. 3B). The demethylation of DNA can be effectively induced by utilizing inhibitors targeting DNA methyltransferase. It has been observed that the activity of DNMT is inhibited by 5-Aza-dC (Fig. 3C). Subsequently, we investigated the demethylation of CA10-rich cancer cells treated with 1 ‍μM of 5-Aza-dC. Quantitative PCR analysis revealed minimal demethylation of CA10 in SV-HUC-1 cell lines, while significant demethylation was observed in the 5,637 and J82 cell lines (Fig. 3D).

CA10 arrested the cell cycle at the G0/G1 phase and inhibited cell proliferation and migration

To investigate the functional role of CA10 in tumorigenesis, the impact of 5-Aza-dC treatment on cell proliferation, cell cycle distribution, and cell migration in bladder cancer cells was examined. The results demonstrated a significant upregulation of CA10 in the bladder cells following exposure to 5-Aza-dC treatment (Fig. 4A). This observation suggests that the restoration of CA-rich cells resulted from 5-Aza-dC-induced demethylation. To assess CA10 re-expression induced proliferation, MTT assays were conducted. The findings demonstrate that demethylation of CA10 significantly suppressed the cell proliferation group (Fig. 4B). The distribution of cells in different phases of the cell cycle was evaluated using flow cytometry analysis. The 5-Aza-dC treatment resulted in a significant increase in the number of cells in the G0/G1 phase, rising from 48.4% in the control group to 62.3% in the 5-Aza-dC-treated group (Fig. 4C). Furthermore, we investigated the impact of 5-Aza on the migratory bladder cancer cells, which demonstrated a significant decrease after the administration of 5-Aza-dC (Fig. 4D). Based on the observations, it is plausible that CA10 may play a role in the pathogenesis of tumors.

Overexpression of CA10 could inhibit the proliferation and metastasis of bladder cancer cells

To elucidate the role of CA10 in regulating bladder cancer progression, we initially established a cell line overexpressing CA10 (Supplemental Fig. 1*) and observed a significant inhibition of proliferation in 5,637 cells (Fig. 5A). This finding was further confirmed through EdU staining (Fig. 5B), while transwell assay validated the inhibitory effect of CA10 on migration in 5,637 cells (Fig. 5C). Further we adopt J82 cells the same experiment found that their results are similar trend (Supplemental Fig. 2*). Collectively, these results demonstrate that overexpression of CA10 effectively suppresses tumor cell progression.

5-Aza-dC (5 ‍mg/kg) treatment group suppressed tumor growth in vivo

To further validate the impact of CA10 on tumor development, we evaluated the influence of epigenetic intervention. As illustrated in Fig. 6A and B, a significant suppression of tumor growth was observed upon administration of 5-Aza-dC compared to the NC group. The administration of 5-Aza-dC at a dosage of 5 ‍mg/kg resulted in a significant reduction in the number of cancer cells, as evidenced by the measurement of Ki67 expression (Fig. 6C). The average Ki-67 proliferation index demonstrated a decrease in tumors and those subjected to 5-Aza-dC treatment (Fig. 6D). Furthermore, we examined the expression profiles of CA10 in each experimental group. As expected, the treatment group administered with 5-Aza-dC exhibited a significant increase in mRNA compared to the control group (Fig. 6E). IHC assays show that the CA10 protein expression level significantly increased in the 5-Aza-dC group (Fig. 6F).

Discussion

In the present research, we made several key findings regarding CA10 hypermethylation and its potential role as a tumor suppress in bladder cancer. Using the ChAMP R software package, we observed that bladder cancer tissues exhibited higher levels of methylation and lower expression of CA10 compared to adjacent tissues. Furthermore, patients in the high-methylation group of CA10 exhibited inferior survival outcomes. To investigate the potential therapeutic implications of targeting CA and in vivo experiments using 5-Aza-dC treatment. Our findings demonstrated significant growth inhibition following 5-Aza-dC treatment in demethylation of CA10 and restoration of its expression. Our hypothesis that CA10 functions as a tumor suppressor gene in bladder cancer is strongly supported by these results.

DNA methylation is a pivotal epigenetic process that is expressed at the transcriptional level. While playing a crucial role in normal development, aberrant methylation is implicated in the process of cancer formation. The characteristic alterations in urine cell-free DNA methylation have been established as an optimal biomarker for non-invasive diagnosis, prognosis, and monitoring of bladder cancer. A study devised a machine-learning classification-based approach to identify bladder cancer-specific DNA methylation signatures in urine samples by constructing a statistical classification model for tumor-specific methylation signatures, which was subsequently validated in a multicenter prospective cohort. In another investigation, quantitative methylation-specific PCR (qMSP) and methylation-sensitive restriction enzyme qPCR were employed to analyze urine sediments from 474 patients, revealing that ECRG4-ITIH5 exhibited a sensitivity of 64–70% in discriminating healthy individuals or those with benign and/or inflammatory diseases from those with bladder cancer. The specificity ranged between 80% and 92%. DNA methylation, as an important biomarker, has potential value in the diagnosis, treatment, and prognostic evaluation of bladder cancer. The regulatory association between bladder cancer and the CA10 gene was further substantiated by the use of bioinformatics analysis to search for aberrant DNA methylation in tumor cells.

5-Aza-dC was initially developed as a nucleoside antimetabolite for cancer therapy. It has been shown to be a potent inhibitor of DNA methyltransferase (DNMTi) and has demonstrated efficacy. The previous studies have underscored the potential variations in impact on cytotoxicity, ant activity, and natural killer (NK) cell function. Venturelli et al. demonstrated that these closely related DNMTi nucleoside analogs induce substantially different reaction patterns in tumor cells. In our study, we investigated the impact of 5-Aza-dC on cell cycle regulation and migration in bladder cancer. Further experimental validation is necessary to ascertain the therapeutic potential of 5-Aza-dC in treating this malignancy.

According to the MethHC database, CA10 has been found to be inactivated in 18 distinct types of human cancer tumors compared to the adjacent tissues (). However, the precise functional role of CA10 as a tumor suppressor gene remains to be elucidated. conducted a study demonstrating that CA10, in combination with other biomarkers, has the potential to significantly improve the early detection of bladder carcinoma through urine sediments. However, there are no comparable reports or validations concerning the function of CA10 in other types of tumors. In the present study, we performed a comprehensive analysis of genome-wide methylation data using the AMP R package, leveraging information obtained from the TCGA database. Among the top 40 differentially methylated genes, CA10 was the most distinct. Moreover, the demethylation-induced restoration of CA10 expression led to a significant inhibition of tumor growth both in vitro and in vivo. These findings strongly indicate that CA10 may function as a suppressor gene during tumorigenesis. While our study focused on CA10, prior work identified nine additional genes (A2BP1, NPTX2, SOX11, etc.) as hypermethylated in bladder cancer. Future studies should investigate whether 5-Aza-dC concurrently reactivates these genes and whether their combined silencing contributes to bladder carcinogenesis. Such analyses will clarify whether CA10 operates independently or synergistically within a broader epigenetic network. In summary, our research indicates that CA10 is frequently deactivated in various cancers and could potentially serve as a biomarker for early cancer detection. Methylation data specific to CA10 reveal it to be one of the most differentially methylated genes. However, further studies with larger sample sizes are necessary to establish the clinical significance of CA10. The use of methylation-specific primers and confirmation of hypermethylation, along with observed tumor suppression upon demethylation, provide compelling evidence that CA10 functions as a tumor suppressor gene in bladder cancer.

In summary, our study demonstrated that the expression of CA10 regulated by DNA methylation offers robust evidence supporting its role in bladder cancer. Moreover, our findings have substantially advanced the understanding of CA10’s involvement in bladder cancer development and highlighted its potential clinical significance as a diagnostic marker for various cancers. Nevertheless, it is crucial to carry out prospective studies specifically investigating CA10 methylation in bladder cancer, which could play a critical role in establishing CA10 as both a biomarker and a therapeutic target for treatment.
