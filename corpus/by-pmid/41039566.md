# ​​The immune microenvironment of pathogen-associated cancers and current clinical therapeutics.

## Abstract

Pathogen-associated cancers account roughly 15-20% of all malignancies worldwide and arise from chronic infections by oncogenic viruses, bacteria and parasites. Pathogens can alter the tumor microenvironment (TME) into an immune-suppressive niche using multiple mechanisms, including immune checkpoint manipulation, chronic inflammation, metabolic reprogramming, and direct epigenetic modifications. Immune dysfunctions related to infections linked to cancers can include M2-polarized macrophages, expansion of MDSCs, exhausted T/NK cells, and tolerogenic dendritic cells, all progressing immune evasion and resistance to cancer therapy. Advances in therapeutics exist in prophylactic vaccines, immune checkpoint inhibitors, and next-generation methods such as the engineered Salmonella to deliver to hypoxic tumors and the targeting the microbiome. The advancement of pathogen-, cancer-, and patient-specific therapeutics is not without its challenges, including heterogeneity of the pathogens, absence of clinically useful biomarkers, concerns about the safety of the restoring anti-pathogen immunity and challenges of scale for manifold interventions. This review will provide a synthesis of the mechanistic aspects of pathogen-mediated TME remodeling, contemporary clinical therapeutics, and ongoing investigations into using pathogen/host dynamics to enhance measurable precision immunotherapy strategies.

Introduction​​

Cancer is a significant global health threat, and a large percentage of tumors are attributable to chronic infections by oncogenic pathogens, including viruses, bacteria and parasites. These pathogens use host immunity to create chronic infections, in a tumor-permissive microenvironment that is characterized by immune evasion, chronic inflammation and stromal remodeling.

Tumor microenvironments (TMEs) in pathogen-associated cancers are defined by a reciprocal relationship between the host’s immune cells and microbial factors, causing carcinogenesis due to immune evasion that leads to genomic instability, inappropriate cytokine signaling and recruitment of immune suppressive cells. For instance, TME of EBV-associated cancers lead to resistance to PD-1 blockade due to immune tolerance caused by latent infection whereas hepatocellular carcinomas associated with HBV have an infiltrate characterized by exhausted T-cells. Furthermore, certain pathogen-associated tumors exhibit enhanced responsiveness to immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) compared to pathogen-negative counterparts of the same cancer type, likely due to higher tumor mutational burden, increased PD-L1 expression, and pre-existing immune infiltration. Even some chronic viruses, such as Cytomegalovirus (CMV), confers beneficial effects on the TME but most viruses are immune suppressive. For example, in the context of HPV, its oncoproteins degrade tumor suppressor proteins, while also blocking antigen presentation to the immune system. In the context of H. pylori, its virulence factors, including the CagA protein, activate NF-κB and COX-2 to facilitate inflammation.

Pathogens have the ability not only to initiate tumors but modulate TME to changes in anti-tumor therapies, conventional and immunotherapy. The field of clinical therapeutics has made many exciting advances in targeting aspects of pathogen-interference with the immune system. Prophylactic vaccines, e.g. HPV, HBV have successfully prevented malignancies linked to infections. Pathogen directed therapies, e.g. antivirals, antibiotics have been tested as adjunct therapy on in conjunction with immune checkpoint inhibitors to restore immune surveillance. These include oncolytic viruses, engineered bacteria and microbiota-directed therapies to reprogram the TME and for improving antitumor immunity. It is important to mention that not all aspects of microbial associations with tumors will lead to improved therapeutic efficacy. There are still limitations related to pathogen-related cancer, including pathogen heterogeneity, pre-existing immunity to viral vectors, and biomarkers to predict response invoked after therapy. This review summarizes the current understanding of the immune microenvironment in pathogen-associated cancers, with mechanistic insight, clinical, and unresolved challenges. With further bridges to remain between microbiology, immunology and oncology, we hope to identify future therapeutic vulnerabilities and ways to exploit or counterbalance the influences of pathogens on cancer progression or treatment.

Tumor microenvironment in pathogen-associated cancers​​

The TME in pathogen-associated malignancies involves a delicate interplay of one or more microbial pathogens, immune responses of the host, and tumor cells (Fig. 1). Oncogenic viruses, bacteria and parasitic pathogens induce chronic infections and contribute to the development of a persistent immunosuppressive & pro-inflammatory context that promotes tumor progression, immune evasion, and resistance to therapeutic intervention.

Innate immune cells​​

Tumor-Associated Macrophages (TAMs)

The polarization of macrophages in the tumor microenvironment is nuanced; hence, the immune outcome is determined in pathogen-associated cancers by the functional polarization of macrophages. Numerous microbial pathogens force the differentiation of macrophages toward an M2-like immunosuppressive phenotype through numerous mechanisms. When the infectious process is chronic, as with Helicobacter pylori and human papillomavirus (HPV) infection, the pathogens remain active and produce constant levels of immunoregulatory cytokines to induce M2 polarization, including IL-10 and TGF-β. M2-like TAMs down-regulate many systemic pro-inflammatory signals responsible for initiating antitumor immunity and angiogenesis and tissue remodeling.

Pathogens can induce macrophage dysfunction, allowing persistent survival beyond host immune clearance. This process confers biological advantages by promoting a progressive shift toward M2-like polarization. Sustained pathogen activity generates a permissive microenvironment characterized by ineffective immune surveillance, which facilitates tumor progression and drives neoplastic evolution. Moreover, there are specific malignancies that are driven by viruses. These viral infections identify specific events where oncogenes are responsible for induction of similar transition of immunosuppressive neighborhood M2 TAM macrophages through viral-specific mechanisms. The human oncoproteins of the Epstein-Barr virus (LMP1) and Kaposi’s sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (vIL-6) have increased recruitment and activation of M2-like TAMs in nasopharyngeal carcinoma and Kaposi’s Sarcoma. These viral proteins induce specific signal transduction pathways that are uniquely propagating immunosuppressive functions for macrophages, which subsequently suppress anti-tumor immune responses through targeting antigen-presenting macrophages functions. Shedding the weight of tumor-associated macrophages as other macrophages were recruited and replaced by similar M2-like macrophages, specifically M2-like macrophages imprinted with scavenger receptors, arginase-1, and other protumor genic factors supported tumor growth and metastasis of malignant disease associated with viruses.

Myeloid-Derived Suppressor Cells (MDSCs)​​

The expansion and activation of myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) is an important immunosuppressive mechanism in pathogen-associated malignancies. For example, bacterial pathogens Fusobacterium nucleatum and Helicobacter pylori have been shown to systemically promote the recruitment and expansion of MDSCs within the tumor microenvironment. Expanded numbers of MDSCs have potent immunosuppressive effects, through several mechanisms including the production of arginase-1 and reactive oxygen species (ROS) that inhibit T-cell proliferation and effector functions. The combined effects of MDSC expansion and resultant immunosuppression establish an immunosuppressive milieu that drives tumor immune evasion and progression in these infection-associated cancers. In the context of viral-associated malignancies, MDSC contribute just as equally to altered tumor microenvironment. For example, hepatitis B virus (HBV) associated hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is also associated with an increase in the accumulation of MDSCs which in turn associates with poor clinical outcome and resistance to PD-1 blockade immunotherapy. The mechanistic relationship between MDSC infiltration and resistance to therapy relates to their ability to create an immunosuppressive and non-inflamed niche. It may be possible to engage novel clinical strategies that promote targeting MDSCs to overcome resistance to immunotherapy, with the goal of eliminating or reversing MDSCs to improve responses to immune checkpoint blockade for HBV associated HCC and other pathogen-associated cancers.

Tumor-Associated Neutrophils (TANs)

The contribution of neutrophils in the tumor microenvironment has come to light as a key component in defining the pathogenesis of infection-associated cancers. In the case of pathogen-driven tumors, the sustained inflammatory response induced by chronic microbial infections results in persistent neutrophil recruitment and polarization toward an N2 phenotype, most extensively characterized in Fusobacterium nucleatum-mediated colorectal cancer. Following bacterial colonization of the colon, cardiorespiratory stress and immune inflammatory responses are rapidly induced, resulting in the production and release of multiple inflammatory mediators that ultimately drive neutrophils to invade and be skewed toward an N2 phenotype. These N2-skewed neutrophils express unique functional characteristics, including increased production of MMP-9 and NETs contributing to tumor development and metastatic spread. The underlying molecular mechanisms by which N2 neutrophils contribute to tumor progression involve several routes of exposure. As the N2 neutrophil secretes MMP-9, the matrix surrounding tumors is degraded and remodeled, allowing the movement and intrusion of tumor cells within surrounding spaces through complete invasion of blood vessels. Additionally, NETs form a supportive environment, that is conducive to metastasis potentiating the survivability of circulating tumor cells and subsequent extravasation. The secreted products from N2 neutrophils facilitate a feed-forward loop of tumor progression, in which pathogen-specific inflammation instigates the mechanism for initiation and yet provides the continuous drive for N2 associated exacerbation of disease. Strongly related to these discoveries, clinical studies have found evidence of N2 neutrophils in clinical samples from pathogen-associated cancers, with infiltration positively related with disease stage and poor prognosis across various pathogen-associated cancers.

Dendritic Cells (DCs)​​

Dendritic cell (DC) dysfunction is a significant immunological defect related to pathogen-associated cancers. DCs, referred to as antigen presenting cells, are important in neoplasia, related to their role in priming and activating T cells. Pathogens such as Epstein-Barr (EBV) virus and Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori), infecting mucosal epithelium and then relating to CD4 + T cell activation, induce severe dysfunction in primary DC maturation and activation, and developing tolerogenic DCs. These pathogenically altered DCs will not only serve as poor antigen presenting cells by not expressing corresponding co-stimulatory molecules, thus not expanding T effector cells, nor also stimulating pathogenic impressions of Th1-type responses; coupled with exposure to non-pathogenic microbes and H. pylori, establishes tolerogenic responses to microbes. This DC dysfunction otherwise alters Th1-like immune responses and induces a unique immunologic-local environment promoting tumor immune evasion and persistence of chronic infection. The cellular and molecular factors causing this dysfunction are multiple and complex, such as pathogen-specific virulence factors affecting normal intracellular signaling pathways. Encoded proteins with EBV are involved in dereferencing the normal NF-κB signaling pathway associated with maturation of DCs. Whereas H. pylori have virulence factors that either modify TLR signaling pathways, or disregard TLR pathways entirely. Regardless, pathologies such as H. pylori prefer to develop DC pathways favoring Treg populations in option to effective anti-tumor immune response. Additionally, DC dysfunction contributes significantly to the clinic from their effects to human subjects, establishing chronic infection and continuing their illness associated with cancer. They produce immune-depressive tumor microenvironments that allow for the escape of transformed cells.

NK (natural killer) cell

NK cell cytotoxicity is a crucial aspect of antitumor immunity; however, NK cell function is often dysfunctional in pathogen-associated cancers. Persistent viral infections (e.g. HCV and EBV) induce NK cell immune surveillance dysfunction through a range of mechanisms, including the downregulation of various activating ligands on the cells of interest. Specifically, these viruses can both downregulate the expression of the key activating recognition signal that is normally expressed by infected cells which also inhibits NK cell cytotoxicity but also promotes immune evasion by tumor cells and thereby escape from NK detection or removal. The consequences of dysfunctional NK cell activity extend beyond impaired NK suppression of direct tumor cell killing due to the inhibition of a cytokine signal or interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) signal, whereby NK cells shape adaptive immune signaling by promoting Th1 polarizing cytokines and enhancing dendritic cell antigen presentation. Ultimately, in HCV- and EBV-associated cancers, the NK cell-derived inhibited capacity for tumor cell cytotoxicity is also present in the context of dysfunctional cytokine signaling. In addition to infection-induced factors limiting NK cell function, recent evidence suggests that tumor-associated stressors in the local microenvironment may correspondingly impair NK cell activity by recruiting immunosuppressive cell types such as Tregs and MDSCs that also inhibit NK cell proliferation and effector activities. From a clinical standpoint, the dysfunction of NK cell cytotoxicity has implications for cancer progression and treatment indication levels. Specifically, in HCV-associated hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and EBV-induced lymphomas, effective cellular infiltration and function by NK cells is typically associated with low progression stage or effective treatment. Therefore, effective recovery from HCV-associated HCC, or EBV-induced lymphomas are often accompanied by NK cell deficiencies in real clinical time following viral infections and therefore symptoms of poor overall prognosis of associated cancers. Therapeutic strategies reestablishing NK cell function (e.g. blockade of inhibitory receptors such as KIR or NKG2A by checkpoint blockade or adoptive NK cell therapy) are being assessed as cancer therapies in pathogen-related cancers. It is vital to understand the factors that contribute to the dysfunction of NK cells in pathogen-associated cancers to facilitate successful immunotherapeutic strategies that enable effective NK cell-mediated antitumor immune responses.

Adaptive immune cells​​

T cells

The dysregulation of T cell responses is one of the key signatures of the tumor microenvironment in pathogen-associated cancers. Chronic infection with oncogenic viruses, such as HTLV-1, has been shown to alter the function of CD8 + T cells through persistent antigen exposure and upregulation of immune checkpoints receptors, including PD-1 (present on cytotoxic T lymphocytes) and its ligand PD-L1 (expressed on infected cells), developing an immunosuppressive axis that exhausts any antitumor T cell response. The exhausted phenotype of T cells is characterized by significantly impaired cytokine production, reduced proliferative capacity, and dampened cytotoxic capacity, allowing tumor immune evasion and disease progression.

In addition to CD8 + T cell exhaustion, we also observe that pathogen-associated malignancies have an expansion of regulatory T cell (Treg) populations. Previous work has mechanistically linked EBV and HPV infection with additional FoxP3 + Treg infiltration of the tumor microenvironment. It seems that both viruses can use multiple pathways to drive Tregs, both by virally-mediated interactions with host immune cells and indirect mechanisms through the accumulation of inflammatory cytokines. In a Treg hyperplastic environment, the Tregs can exert robust immunosuppressive activity through multiple mechanisms, including production of IL-10 and TGF-β, competition for essential nutrients required for leukocyte metabolic activity, and directly through inhibition of effector T cell function and activity. This combination of compromised T cell response and increased regulatory suppression creates an overwhelming immunosuppressive environment with significant implications for antitumor immunity in virus-associated malignancies.

The pathogen-associated disruptions in T cell populations have significant clinical implications for their use in cancer immunotherapy. For example, there was an enormous overexpression of PD-1/PD-L1 by HPV and HTLV-1, representing a potential actionable weakness for immune checkpoint blockade strategies by targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis. On the other hand, the regulation of Tregs may require more combination approaches, such as CTLA-4 blockades or Treg-depleting therapies. Knowing that T cell dysfunction in pathogen-associated malignancies exhibits specific patterns as a consequence of distinct pathogens, is valuable for designing stratified immunotherapeutic approaches that have the potential to restore antitumor immunity in infection-associated cancers.

Pathogen-Specific immune microenvironment remodeling​​

The immune microenvironment in pathogen-associated cancers is dynamically reshaped by microbial pathogens to foster immune evasion, chronic inflammation, and malignant progression. Viruses, bacteria, and parasites employ sophisticated strategies to subvert host immunity, creating immunosuppressive niches that support tumor survival. These pathogens modulate immune checkpoints, alter antigen presentation, recruit regulatory immune cells, and induce genotoxic stress, collectively driving oncogenesis through ​​immune exhaustion​​, ​​chronic inflammation​​, and ​​epigenetic reprogramming​​ (Figs. 2 and 3).

Viral pathogens and their oncogenic mechanisms within immune microenvironment

Human T-cell Lymphotropic Virus-1 (HTLV-1)

​​The Human T-cell Lymphotropic Virus-1 (HTLV-1), a retrovirus, is most commonly associated with the development of adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma (ATLL). This malignancy is characterized by an aggressive disease of CD4 + T lymphocytes. HTLV-1 has been linked to oncogenesis through several immunomodulatory mechanisms that promote malignant transformation and tumor progression. An important mechanism involves the expansion of FOXP3 + Tregs, which exert profound immunosuppression by inhibiting cytotoxic T-cell activity through cytokine-mediated inhibition and direct cell-to-cell contact, creating a local immune-privileged microenvironment that supports malignant cell survival and expansion. In addition to Treg expansion, HTLV-1 can downregulate both MHC class I and II molecules, a coordinated mechanism whereby antigen presentation and therefore recognition by CD8 + and CD4 + T cells are diminished. In ATLL patient tissue, clinical studies have revealed that 27% of cases showed high levels of PD-L1 + marked upregulation of PD-L1 on tumor cells along with a functional GAL-9/Tim-3 activation, potentially contributing to T-cell exhaustion and T-cell dysfunction as critical mechanisms of immune evasion in malignancies associated with HTLV-165.

HTLV-1 infection causes tumor immune microenvironment remodeling on multiple fronts. HTLV-1 infection is associated with chronic T-cell exhaustion, which is typified by a progressive loss of effector function and proliferation. These exhausted T-cells express multiple inhibitory receptors and may have greatly reduced capacity for cytokine production. In parallel with this chronic T-cell exhaustion and regulatory T-cell proliferation, HTLV-1 infection also activates macrophages inducing a M2-like tumor-associated macrophage phenotype that produces suppressive products and CCL1867. M2 macrophages are involved in tumor angiogenesis, tissue remodeling, and reduced antitumor immune response. The overall effect of these immunomodulatory mechanisms generates an extensively and moderately immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, supporting viral persistence and tumor advancement. HTLV-1-associated ATLL tumor microenvironment is unique and continues to clarify the path toward targeted immunotherapies that reverse virus-induced immune suppression and promote effective antitumor immunity.

Human Papillomavirus (HPV)

Human papillomavirus (HPV) is an important oncogenic DNA virus that is responsible for an estimated 5% of all human cancers. High-risk genotypes of HPV (most notably HPV-16 and HPV-18) are etiologically associated with cervical carcinoma, oropharyngeal carcinoma, anal carcinoma, vulvar carcinoma, vaginal carcinoma, and penile carcinoma. HPV utilizes elaborate immune evasion mechanisms employing multiple methods. The HPV E5 protein significantly downregulates MHC class I expression on infected epithelial cells and diminishes CD8 + T cell recognition as well as NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity. Additionally, HPV infection creates a polarized Th1/Th2 immune response through the production of elevated interleukin-4 (IL-4), which reduces type I interferon antiviral responses (stimulates cytotoxic T cell responses) and creates a tumor-promoting microenvironment, providing a favorable environment for viral persistence and malignant progression. At the molecular level the HPV-18 E7 protein targets and degrades the stimulator of interferon genes (STING) tumor suppressor, eliminating the host’s innate immune recognition process and ultimately type I interferon production. In cervical cancer samples, HPV infection correlates with PD-L1 expression on the tumor cells as well as infiltrating immune cells providing an immune checkpoint barrier to T cell activity and exhaustion. The combined effects of reduced MHC class I expression, inhibited antigen presentation and enhanced expression of diverse checkpoint molecules form a highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment that enables immune evasion and malignant progression. These intricate immunomodulatory processes pose distinct challenges for effective therapeutic strategies but also provide targets for the development of Immunotherapeutics in HPV-associated malignancies in the future.

Hepatitis B/C Viruses (HBV/HCV)

Hepatitis B and C viruses (HBV/HCV) are hepatotropic pathogens capable of establishing chronic infections which lead to hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), the most common primary liver cancer accounting for around 80% of cases globally. HBV exhibits oncogenic potential via mechanisms that modify the immune system, resulting in chronic infection stimulating the expansion of Tregs and creating an immunosuppressive environment favorable for tumor development. The presence of hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) induces B-cell dysfunction and impairs adaptive humoral immunity, with the concurrent upregulation of the PD1/PDL1 checkpoint pathway impairing T-cell anti-tumor activity by driving exhaustion and viral persistence. Subsequently, these immune mechanisms induce a stepwise transition to cirrhosis and ultimately HCC. Meanwhile, HCV employs different, but equally robust, tactics to evade host immunity and support hepatocarcinogenesis. The NS5A protein by HCV induces the degradation of STAT1 eliminating interferon-mediated antiviral responses and, therefore, promoting viral persistence. Combined with the synergistic effects of transforming growth factor-beta (TGF-β) and PDL1, the outcome of CD8 + T-cell dysfunction creates an immuno-privileged niche within the liver microenvironment. Ultimately, the functionality of both viruses infers verification of the IL-6/STAT3 signaling pathway, promoting inflammation and inducing cellular proliferation while inhibiting apoptosis within premalignant hepatocytes. HBV also aids the virus by promoting glycolysis inducing lactic acid, and inhibiting RLR signaling, by interfering with the RIG-I/MAVS signaling. Dendritic cells in this environment also exhibit a maturation defect and functional impairment further curtailing antigen presentation and T-cell priming. Collectively, broader immune suppression created by HBV and HCV collectively promotes a favorable environment for malignancy transformation while providing major challenges for immunotherapy when administered targeting HCC. These distinct immunological profiles of virus-related HCC will continue to provide insights into developing targeted therapeutics promoting efficient antitumor immunity in this notoriously difficult-to-treat cancer.

Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV)

The Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), a gamma herpesvirus with demonstrated potential for oncogenesis, has been linked to a variety of cancers, including Burkitt lymphoma, Hodgkin lymphoma, nasopharyngeal carcinoma, gastric adenocarcinoma, and post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorders. EBV uses elaborate molecular mechanisms to induce oncogenesis within microenvironment. The main function of latent membrane proteins (LMP1) is to constitutively activate important oncogenic signaling pathways including PI3K/AKT and MAPK, thus causing cell survival, proliferation and metabolic reprogramming of the infected cell. EBV nuclear antigen 2 (EBNA2) is essential for lymphomagenesis, imparting activated NOTCH signaling function, primarily involved in B-cell malignancies, thus allowing uncontrolled proliferation of infected B-cells by transcriptional activation of oncogenes. EBV employs remarkably sophisticated immune evasion strategies. Interestingly, EBV encodes small non-coding EBER RNAs that contribute to malignant transformation while also contributing to immune evasion through modulation of host interferon responses. At the molecular level, EBV proteins BGLF5 and BILF1 efficiently block antigen presentation through direct interference with both MHC class I and MHC class II pathways, rendering EBV-infected cells invisible to cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs). EBV LMP1 protein upregulates PD-L1 expression through activation of the NF-κB pathway, thus inducing an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment inducing T cell exhaustion. The immune remodeling caused by EBV infection creates serious hurdles for adoption of immunotherapy strategies. Tumors with latency II, expressing EBNA1 along with LMP1 and LMP2, exhibit extreme resistance toward CAR T-cell therapy due to multi-layered mechanisms used for immune evasion. LMP1 induces a profoundly immunosuppressive niche by coordinated modulation of antigen presentation machinery, prohibiting inhibitory ligands and recruiting regulatory immune cell populations. The complex interplay of viral oncoproteins and host immune defences in EBV-associated malignancies remains an area of active investigation relevant to the development of targeted immunotherapies and treatment combinations.

Kaposi’s Sarcoma Herpesvirus (KSHV)

Kaposi’s Sarcoma Herpesvirus (KSHV), or human herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), is an oncogenic gamma herpesvirus with numerous associated neoplasms, including Kaposi’s sarcoma (KS), primary effusion lymphoma (PEL), and multicentric Castleman’s disease. KSHV uses a variety of complex molecular mechanisms to drive tumorigenesis within microenvironment, utilizing the unique collection of viral oncogenes encoded by the virus. For example, the viral GPCR (vGPCR) is a potent and unique chemokine receptor that is constitutively active. vGPCR promotes angiogenesis by inducing vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). KSHV encodes a functional interleukin-6 (IL-6, vIL-6) protein that exerts activity that is similar to human IL-6 and stimulates endothelial cells to proliferate and survive. The KSHV FLICE inhibitory protein (vFLIP) affects tumor development by inhibiting apoptosis by blocking caspase-8 activity and maintaining constitutive NF-κB signaling, which allows for cell survival and signaling for additional inflammatory responses in the affected cells. The immune remodeling induced by KSHV infection creates a virally-induced profoundly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment to enable KSHV persistence and tumor progression. KSHV infection targets MHC Class II for degradation by RTA. This limits antigen presentation to CD4 + T cells, and as a result limits the activation of adaptive immune responses. KSHV infection also causes extensive upregulation of programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression in monocyte-derived cells. This leads to an immune checkpoint barrier to T cell activation resulting in T cell exhaustion and functional impairment. KSHV infection also shifts tumor-associated macrophages toward an M2 polarization and promotes expression of immunosuppressive cytokines and pro-angiogenic factors to promote continued tumor growth and vascularization. These complex immune modulatory mechanisms collectively create an immune permissive environment for KSHV-associated malignancies to develop and progress. KSHV mounts a concerted assault on the host immune response, by promoting angiogenesis while inhibiting innate and adaptive immune responses. The consequences of the immunology of KSHV-associated tumors create challenges for treating cancer but at the same time reveals possibilities to catalogue immunotherapy targets for development. By learning about virus-host interactions, we can inform new ways to treat KSHV-associated malignancies, especially in the case of patients who are immunocompromised, where these tumors are most frequently found.

Bacterial pathogens and their oncogenic mechanisms within immune microenvironment

Helicobacter pylori

Helicobacter pylori is a gram-negative bacterium that chronically colonizes the gastric mucosa and has been recognized by the World Health Organization as a Class I carcinogen. Helicobacter pylori has an etiologic association with gastric adenocarcinoma and gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) lymphoma and accounts for about 75–90% of the gastric cancer cases worldwide. The putative carcinogenic properties of H. pylori within microenvironment are likely related to its virulence factors that can modulate important cellular pathways within gastric epithelial cells. For example, the cytotoxin-associated gene A (CagA) protein uses a type IV secretion system to be delivered into gastric epithelial cells to block E-cadherin/β-catenin signaling, which induces complete loss of cell-cell adhesion, inducing pro-proliferative signals from adheres, junctions. Simultaneously, one of H. pylori’s virulence factors, vacuolating cytotoxin (VacA), induces mitochondrial disfunction in T cells that likely osmotically disable T cell activation and effector functions. Because of the unique virulence factors of the bacterium, it can in addition activate Hedgehog signaling pathway, which is a necessary pathway for maintaining gastric stem cells and can promote carcinogenesis. The immune microenvironment in H. pylori-associated gastric tumors undergo significant remodeling which can facilitate tumor immune evasion and tumor-intrinsic progression. For example, in the gastric tumor microenvironment, the bacterium modulates expression of PD-L1 on both tumor and immune cells to create an immune checkpoint barrier that leads to T cell exhaustion. H. pylori recruit Scholafen4 + MDSCs that polarize and selectively expand the cereal variety. This expansion of MDSCs likely potently suppresses T cell response by L-arginine mediated mechanisms. The bacterium could induce the activation of fibroblast activation protein (FAP) + cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAF) which can induce an epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) that remodels the extracellular matrix and thus creates a favorable niche for distant metastasis. Taken together, these coordinated mechanisms create an immunosuppressive TME that not only supports bacterial persistence but also promotes the malignant transformation and progression of gastric epithelium into cancer.

Fusobacterium nucleatum

Fusobacterium nucleatum is an anaerobic, gram-negative bacterium that has been identified as an emerging onco-pathogen, particularly in association with colorectal cancer (CRC). F. nucleatum is identified in approximately 30% of CRC tumor tissue. F. nucleatum can foster tumorigenesis within microenvironment through several different molecular mechanisms. One of the main mechanisms by which F. nucleatum develops a pro-tumor environment is through its unique FadA adhesion protein. FadA bind E-cadherin, a protein responsible for cell-cell adhesion, on the surface of host epithelial cells and interrupts cellular adhesion to activate oncogenic pathways, Molecular signals that are activated by bacterial adhesion include the NF-κB pathway and the β-catenin pathway to drive chronic inflammation and cellular proliferation, which are major steps in malignant transformation. F. nucleatum produces succinate as a byproduct of metabolism, which promotes chemoresistance in tumor cells by stabilizing HIF-1α122. The immune microenvironment in tumors associated with F. nucleatum undergoes significant remodeling to allow for immune evasion and tumor survival. The surface protein Fap2 on F. nucleatum binds the TIGIT receptor on cells with NK cell activity to inhibit cell function and prevent tumor immune surveillance. F. nucleatum can also drive the upregulation of PD-L1 by activating TLR4/NF-κB signaling, creating an immunosuppressive checkpoint barrier that inhibits T-cell function. F. nucleatum also manipulates the immune microenvironment by recruiting pro-tumor disease-associated M2 macrophages and MDSCs that will further generate an immunosuppressive niche by secreting anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-10 and TGF-β. Through these coordinated mechanisms, F. nucleatum establishes a tumor permissive microenvironment that supports bacterial persistence, as well as tumor immune escape and resistance to therapy. Importantly, F. nucleatum was identified in CRC and is associated with poorer prognosis and advanced disease, showing that F. nucleatum is both a possible biomarker and a response target. Current research continues to explore the use of microbiome-modulating strategies relevant to F. nucleatum, such as phage therapy and probiotic-based therapy, to overcome bacterial immune suppression and effectively use immunotherapy in colorectal cancer cases. Studying the interaction of F. nucleatum and the host can give key insight into developing alternative treatment options for microbiome-associated malignancies.

Salmonella typhimurium

Due to its selective tumor targeting and colonization of hypoxic regions surrounding the tumor, Salmonella typhimurium has emerged as a potential therapeutic agent in cancer immunotherapy. The characteristics associated with hypoxic tumor regions in the tumor microenvironment are sometimes indicative of treatment resistance. As a facultative anaerobic bacterium, S. typhimurium shows remarkable tumor tropism because it accumulates in necrotic, poorly vascularized regions of solid tumors that are often difficult for conventional therapy to penetrate effectively. While S. typhimurium colonizes tumor tissue, pro-inflammatory cytokines, including tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), and C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 10 (CXCL-10) are induced, leading to a rich cytokine landscape that promotes immune cell recruitment and activation. What is particularly interesting about the immune remodeling associated with S. typhimurium in the tumor microenvironment is its ability to switch the axis from immunosuppression towards immunostimulation. S. typhimurium subjects tumor cells to immunogenic cell death (ICD) and releases damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), including calreticulin, ATP, and high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), which are seen as danger signals and activate dendritic cells and subsequently initiate tumor-specific T cell responses. Major recruitment of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), particularly CD8 + cytotoxic T cells and Th1-polarized CD4 + T cells is also seen. TILs are required for effective anti-tumor immunity. The action of S. typhimurium also leads to decreased numbers of MDSCs considered one of the most important immunosuppressive cell types that reduce T cell function and drive tumor immune escape. Using genetically engineered bacteria represented by VNP20009 and A1-R offer further therapeutic potential for S. typhimurium for cancer treatment by improving tumor specificity and compliancy and have properties that stimulate immunity. Genetically engineered strains not only promote tumor-specific immunity better than the wild type but also minimize systemic damage. Agent S. typhimurium is unique in its ability to exploit multiple effects on the biology of a tumor: on the tumor itself for killing, on activating immune responses, and modifying the corresponding tumor microenvironment leading to the possibility of employing S. typhimurium in combinations with traditional therapies such as chemotherapy and radiation, as well as newer immunotherapies like immune checkpoint inhibitors. Continued clinical development supports further treatment options based on S. typhimurium approaches in the treatment of colorectal, pancreatic, and breast cancers, representing a timely opportunity to influence an important aspect of many hypoxic tumor microenvironments.

Parasitic pathogens and their oncogenic mechanisms within immune microenvironment

Schistosoma haematobium

Schistosoma haematobium, a parasitic trematode, is classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as a Group 1 carcinogen as it is associated with the development of bladder squamous cell carcinoma, the predominant histological subtype across endemic regions of schistosomiasis. S. haematobium’s oncogenic effects targeting microenvironment stem from a chronic infection characterized by continuous egg deposition within the wall of the bladder, resulting in a cascade of biological events leading to malignant transformation. The eggs become entrapped within the bladder wall leading to extensive fibrosis induced by proteolytic enzymes and reactive oxygen species released from the trapping eggs. Concurrently, S. haematobium is able to incite a polarized Th2 response characterized by producer interleukin-4 (IL-4), establishing tumor permissive conditions. They do this by driving alternatively activated macrophages and inducing suppressive anti-tumor immune response. The immune microenvironment underlying S. haematobium-associated bladder cancer undergoes significant remodeling. The immune microenvironment actively promotes malignant progression. The parasite egg incites polarization to M2-skewed tumor-associated macrophages that support the progression of cancer through the secretion of increased pro-angiogenic factors, at which VEGF is one example and increased immunosuppressive cytokines including IL-10 and TGF-β. The malevolent macrophage programming creates a feed-forward loop of processes that increases the Th2 response while attenuating the protection of Th-1 immunity. Furthermore, chronic infection drives the expansion of Tregs and Tregs cause amply regulatory effects on T effector and T memory by cytokine secretion and/or direct cell to cell contact. These immunomodulatory effects together impact establishment of chronic immunomodulatory cytokine milieu of IL-4, IL-10 and TGF-β sufficient to allow S. haematobium persist and co-opt the immune response to create a tumor immune-privileged niche. The genotoxicity of S. haematobium infection on bladder epithelium is another oncogenic mechanism. The reactive nitrogen and oxygen species produced by the chronic inflammatory microenvironment leads to DNA modification and epigenetic changes in epithelial cells, while parasite-derived compounds interfere with rate of progression of DNA repair response. Genotoxic stress in combination with the impaired DNA damage response leads to accumulation of mutations in tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes driving the multilayer process of carcinogenesis. The unique immunologic and molecular features of S. haematobium-associated bladder cancer that distinguish it from non-parasitic malignancies of the bladder present challenges and opportunities for therapeutic intervention, with reports in the literature currently building on efforts to reverse parasite-induced immune suppression while targeting the pathways molecularly resulting from this infection-associated cancer.

Opisthorchis viverrini

Opisthorchis viverrini is a liver fluke that is endemic to Southeast Asia because of its correlation with cholangiocarcinoma, a malignant bile duct lesion that is particularly prevalent in Thailand, Laos, and Cambodia. The oncogenic potential of the parasite within microenvironment is mediated through several mechanisms beginning with potential secretion of Ov-GRN-1, a growth factor-like protein that induces cholangiocyte hyperplasia by activating cellular survival and proliferation pathways. Infection with O. viverrini leads to chronic biliary inflammation characterized by infiltration of inflammatory cells skilled in producing reactive oxygen and nitrogen species (ROS/RNS) that lead to DNA damage and subsequent genomic instability in bile duct epithelial cells. The immune microenvironment of O. viverrini-associated cholangiocarcinoma undergoes huge remodeling that enables tumor immune evasion. For example, infection with the parasite leads to activation of the IL-6/STAT3 signaling pathway that not only promotes cholangiocyte proliferation but also creates a pro-inflammatory environment that supports tumor progression. This chronic inflammatory state supports the infiltration of immunosuppressive myeloid cells such as MDSCs and TAMs, producing anti-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-10 and TGF-β, all of which impede antitumor immune responses in patients with cholangiocarcinoma. Additionally, the parasite itself can induce immune checkpoints such as PD-1/PD-L1 and cause other mechanisms of T cell dysfunction like T cell exhaustion to impair cytotoxic T cell function. Collectively, these immunomodulatory mechanisms establish an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment that allows for parasite persistence and fosters the development and progression of cholangiocarcinoma. The specific molecular and immunological characteristics of O. viverrini associated cholangiocarcinoma create unique obstacles to the organizations of diagnosis and treatment. The chronic inflammatory milieu along with genotoxic stresses, provides a tumor microenvironment rich in pro-fibrotic factors that contribute to the desmoplastic reaction that characterizes these tumors. Ongoing research is centered on targeting parasite-specific pathways, such as Ov-GRN-1 signaling, and reversing immune suppression pathways using checkpoint inhibition and cytokine modulation. Continued elucidation of the interactions between the host and O. viverrini will provide new ideas for treatment opportunities for this morbid cancer that occurred from an associated infection, which remains a public health issue in regions with endemicity.

Therapeutic strategies targeting the immune microenvironment​​

The immune microenvironment in pathogen-associated cancers presents unique challenges and opportunities for therapeutic intervention, as microbial pathogens actively reshape immune evasion mechanisms to promote tumor survival. These cancers driven by viruses, bacteria, and other pathogens exploit immune checkpoint pathways, alter antigen presentation, and induce chronic inflammation to create an immunosuppressive niche. Emerging strategies aim to counteract these mechanisms through ​​immune checkpoint inhibition​​, ​​cellular therapies​​, ​​microbiome modulation​​, ​​vaccine development​​, and ​​combination therapies​​, each tailored to disrupt pathogen-mediated immune suppression while enhancing antitumor responses (Table 1).

Immune checkpoint modulation strategies​​

Pathogen-associated checkpoint molecule modifications induce novel challenges and opportunities for therapeutic development. While the clinical translatability of these changes has resulted in several permissible checkpoint inhibitors with clinical efficacy in pathogen-associated malignancies, therapeutic strategies targeting PD-1 with inhibitors like pembrolizumab and nivolumab are showing clinical relevance in EBV-positive gastric cancer and HBV/HCV-related HCC respectively. Among the PD-L1 inhibitors, atezolizumab has garnered interest amongst patients with KSHV-associated Kaposi Sarcoma. The CTLA-4 inhibitor ipilimumab has been studied in HTLV-1 associated ATLL to block Treg-induced suppression and relatlimab (LAG-3 inhibitor) mechanism has showed early promise in patients with EBV-associated cancers. These avenues of targeted immunotherapy emphasize the need for understanding the different mechanisms by which pathogen-specific immune evasion occurs to better target methods of treatment in the case of infection-related cancers.

Cellular therapy approaches​​

The concept of cancer therapies leveraging the cellular response to pathogens has been given a new lease of life with the advent of cellular therapy. Cellular therapies can now be used in conjunction with other novel therapeutic approaches for pathogen-associated malignancies. Theoretically, a direct example of this would be the EBV associated tumors wherein a T cell therapy developed against T lymphocytes infected with WBV has therapeutic efficacy. In more elaborate approaches, CAR-T cells have been designed specifically to treat EBV-associated malignancies using EBV target antigens (e.g., EBNA1, LMP1, and LMP2), and resultingly, could yield unprecedented outcomes such as preventing the multiple post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorders because of pre-emption with CAR-T EBV targeted therapy. The most problematic EBV-associated malignancy must be the one with a gene expression pattern indicative of latency II, where there has been a level of success with previously mentioned T-cell based therapies, but with more complex biological constraints of limited expression of dominant EBV antigens. For example, HTLV-1-associated ATLL recent therapeutic strategies targeting the immunosuppressive tumor of ATLL utilize biologic immunotherapy. The monoclonal antibody mogamulizumab has been clinically effective at targeting and depleting CCR4 + malignant T cells, and although not necessarily clinically tested yet, targeted modulation of the OX40/OX40L axis could hold promise for remediating the Treg-mediated immune suppression associated with ATLL.

There will also be future opportunities with bacterial pathogens, in cellular modalities. As an example, targeting gastrointestinal cancers with a modified Salmonella engineered T cell was an innovative technique seeking to draw upon the natural affinity of bacteria to preferentially target solid malignancies and T cell based immunotherapy. Furthermore, with Listeria vaccines, Listeria utilizes listeriolysin O (LLO), a virulence factor of Listeria, that activates cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) by exposing CTL populations to more antigen at once and homeostatically priming T cells to produce CTLs in greater numbers. The commercial development of bacterial mediated therapies illustrates how biologically inherent properties of pathogens can be exploited for greater usage in current and newer designs of cancer immunotherapy.

Microbiome-targeted therapies​​

While the use of intentional microbiome targeting as a treatment strategy is a nascent approach, there is already some interesting options when it comes to treatment malignancies linked to pathogenic infections. In the broader context of targeting cancer causation, new methodologies for microbiome modification continue to be developed with an aim to do the same with respect to the oncogenic microbiome associated with cancer associated pathogens. For example, supplementation with the probiotic Akkermansia muciniphila was found to improve the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade therapy possibly due to its ability to improve gut barrier function as well as alter immune responses systemically. These microbiome-targeted methods represent important new paradigms for cancer treatment as they shift the treatment focus from targeting the tumor in isolation towards targeting the microbiome as an aspect of oncogenesis, and resistance to therapy.

Vaccine development strategies​​

The development of vaccines targeting oncogenic pathogens has followed two different, but complementary, pathways: preventive and therapeutic. Vaccination programs in the preventive space have aimed to stop initial infections with established oncogenic pathogens such as the virus-like particle (VLP) vaccines. Vaccine development in the therapeutic area has focused on recruiting adaptive immunity to eliminate problematic infections and their related malignancies. A current exploratory approach is testing a messenger RNA vaccine directed against the glycoproteins gp350, gB, gH, and gL of EBV. EBV vaccine studies could lead to adequate and robust immune responses against EBV as a non-immediate infection. For merkel cell carcinoma driven by Merkel cell polyomavirus (MCPyV), they are developing vaccines with a focus on the viral T-antigen, as it is a crucial biological driver of malignant replication. These therapeutic vaccines may work in two ways: the vaccines may train immune systems to overcome persistent viral infection’s immune evasion, and, at the same time provide a way to selectively eliminate transformed cells of the original infection expressing viral oncoproteins. We expect that vaccine therapy will complement immunotherapies used in advanced disease.

Combination therapy paradigms​​

Pathogen-associated cancers are complex, so it is understandable that newer combination therapies targeting different aspects of the tumor microenvironment are being developed. One interesting approach is the combination of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) with oncolytic viruses, such as the demonstrated synergy of herpes simplex virus (HSV) with anti-PD-1 therapy in Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV)-associated sarcomas. This strategy benefits from the immunogenic cell death by the virus, as well as the immune checkpoint blockade negating tumor immune evasion producing a strong anti-tumor immune response. Another combining strategy is correlated with CAR T-cells, where patients are specifically targeted for pathogen antigen with immune checkpoint blockade. Trial data has shown promise for EBV specific CAR-T cells toward latent membrane proteins with PD-1 blockade; it has a dual-action approach toward EBV and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment of EBV-associated lymphomas in patients. This combination could enhance T-cell recognition toward viral antigens together with immune checkpoint blockade to prolong T-cell engagement and thus continued attack of the viral tumor microenvironment. The third main combination strategy encompasses epigenetic reprogramming; the trials to date are promising demarcating immunotherapy augmentation. Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors have upregulated major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I expression in tumor cells, therefore escaping a mechanism of immune evasion in virus-related cancers. This strategy alongside IFN/IL-6 will initiate the immunogenic tumor phenotype and help with T-cell engagement while targeting the virus; it is a helpful avenue particularly in malignancies where the viral oncoproteins down-regulate the antigen presentation pathway. These combination treatment paradigms exhibit an exciting growth in complexity from a developmental perspective, which provides the opportunity to gradually engage broader pathogen-specific mechanisms of susceptibility and immune resistance in cancers.

Future directions and clinical challenges in pathogen-associated cancer immunotherapy

The clinical development of immunotherapy for pathogen-associated cancer consists of multiple vital safety issues to navigate. Potential reactivation of pathogens, especially regarding patients with chronic HBV infection undergoing PD-1/PD-L1 therapy is paramount, as the recommended guidelines for clinical practice endorse using prophylactic antivirals (tenofovir) to impede viral rebound and liver-related adverse events although fellows can become cavalier about HBV therapy in these situations. Another pressing clinical challenge is cytokine release syndrome associated with EBV specific CAR-T cell therapy. The profound activation of the immune system directed at a large number of viral antigens without specificity, likely increases the risk of cytokine release syndrome and mandates increased monitoring and therapeutic interventions for symptom management relative to the specific cytokines. Future therapeutic strategies for pathogen-associated cancers will rely on biomarker-guided personalization, nanomedicine, and synthetic biology innovations, while addressing mechanisms of hypoxia-driven resistance, and immune evasion. This field is accelerating into exciting new directions that will converge with new technologies. Synthetic biology strategies using CRISPR gene editing are being developed to create CAR-T cells that directly target latent membrane protein 2 (LMP2), allowing for more specific targeting of EBV-associated malignancies versus off-tumor toxicities. This is an important strategy, as our future gains could involve temporal targeting where we target pathogen-associated malignancies at specific times in the pathogen’s life cycle, such as lytic EBV-infection versus latent EBV-infection. We would expect precision timing to corroborate efficacy improvement going forward, which will be very informative as we develop a better understanding of the pathogen biology and host host-interactions at various times in the tumor microenvironment. Future success hinges on biomarker-guided personalization and synthetic biology innovations.

Conclusion​​

Pathogen-specific immune microenvironment remodeling represents a critical mechanism linking chronic infections to cancer development. Understanding these complex interactions provides opportunities for both cancer prevention through pathogen eradication and novel therapeutic approaches targeting the infection-associated tumor microenvironment.
