# Editing of Endogenous Genes in Cellular Immunotherapies.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE OF REVIEW**: T cell-based cellular and antibody immunotherapies have dramatically altered the landscape of cancer treatment over the past decade. Over the same time span, gene editing technologies have enabled unprecedented degrees of genetic control.

**RECENT FINDINGS**: Knock-outs of endogenous genes, especially based on electroporation of targetable nucleases such as CRISPR/Cas9, have rapidly proliferated. Simultaneous introduction of large DNA sequences can integrate new synthetic genetic instructions with specific endogenous loci to alter T cell function and specificity. Recently developed discovery technologies to perform genome-wide knock-out and large-scale knock-in screens in T cells can rapidly identify endogenous gene targets and therapeutic knock-in programs. Endogenous gene knock-outs and targeted knock-ins may offer the chance to expand beyond the current limitations of randomly integrating viral vector-based T cell therapies, and extend immunotherapies' therapeutic advances to wider hematologic and solid tumor indications.

Purpose of review

INTRODUCTION

The role of the immune system in cancer therapy has risen, fallen out of favor, and re-emerged as a curative therapeutic modality over the past century. In the 1890s and early 20th century, William Cooley administered a serum containing B. prodigiosus endotoxins to malignant cancer patients, noting sustained immunologic activation and remissions in minorities of patients. Despite these early glimpses of efficacy, surgical, chemotherapeutic, and radiation therapies dominated cancer therapeutics throughout the 20th century. Continuous advances in the understanding of the immune system in the 1990s led to a growing reevaluation of the role of immune cells, adaptive T cells in particular. The stunning recent clinical successes of monoclonal antibody therapies against the T cell checkpoint molecules CTLA4 and PD-1 has emphasized the therapeutic power of modulating the immune system. Whether through adjuvants, vaccines, checkpoint inhibitors, or direct cell transfers, the adaptive and specific T cell response to tumors is central to the efficacy of immune-modulating therapies in cancer.

The prospect of directly using T cells themselves as the basis of cancer therapies inspired the very first cell-based therapeutics in the late 1980s and early 1990s. The discovery that T cells could overcome regulatory pathways preventing self-recognition and bear T cell receptors (TCRs) specific for tumor antigens encouraged the first Tumor Infiltrating Lymphocyte (TIL) trials. Advances in the ex vivo culture of T cells, such as the discovery and efficient production of IL-2, enabled large quantities of T cells to be grown outside the body and returned to a patient. Improvements to the isolation, expansion, and re-infusion of TILs have now progressed for over three decades.

However, increasing molecular characterization of tumor genetics and immune responses have revealed that many tumors, such as prostate and ovarian cancers, possess few neoantigens distinguishable by TCRs, and many tumors possess few if any TILs. Advances in recombinant DNA engineering, DNA synthesis, and subsequently synthetic biology have opened the door for T cell-based therapies to overcome these nature limitations. Synthetic T cell engineering can reprogram T cell specificity to target extracellular antigens not presented by Major Histocompatibility Complexes (MHC) or target antigens present in healthy adult tissues. Most prominently, Chimeric Antigen Receptors (CARs) combine the specificity domains of antibodies with the signaling domains of the TCR and various co-stimulatory receptors to enable T cells to recognize any surface antigen that an antibody or other binding domain can be generated against. CAR T therapies have shown dramatic clinical successes in B cell malignancies, culminating in the FDA approvals of two cellular CAR T therapies in 2017.

Effective ex vivo immune cell culture, as well as the development of retroviral and lentiviral vectors that can randomly integrate new genetic material into the genomes of T cells, has driven the historical development of TIL and CAR T therapies. The advent of targetable gene editing technologies, which was first applied to T cells with Zinc Finger Nucleases (ZFNs) in the 2000s and whose applications exploded in the 2010s with the ease of use and adaptability of CRISPR/Cas9 systems, allows a new generation of technologies to be applied towards therapeutic engineering of human T cells. These targetable nucleases first have enabled the specific and heritable disruption of endogenous genes, resulting in complete knock-out of desired proteins in T cells. Further, combining targetable nucleases with DNA donor templates has allowed even large new DNA segments to be integrated at almost any site in the T cell genome, with total control of the final DNA sequence of these knock-ins. These two abilities, to knock-out and knock-in at endogenous genetic loci, offer unique advantages to previous generations of T-cell modulating technologies based on random viral integrations, and may catalyze future expanded applications of human T cells in cancer therapies.

Targeted Knock-outs in T Cell Therapies

While various RNA interference-based gene knock-down strategies have been employed in human T cells, heritable knock-outs in any cell type traditionally begin with a double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) break. To maintain chromosomal integrity, cellular DNA repair pathways rapidly re-ligate these two exposed ends. Depending on the repair pathway used, this can result in the introduction of small indels and mutations, and less commonly larger deletions and translocations. The location of this dsDNA break can be controlled when using targetable nucleases. Unlike general exonucleases, or the restriction endonucleases targetable to 6–8 bp palindromic sequences, targetable nucleases cut DNA only at specific longer 16–24 bp DNA sequences, long enough in many cases to target only a single site in the 3 billion base pair human genome. Zinc Finger Nucleases (ZFNs)were the first targetable nucleases applied to human T cells. ZFN’s specificity is confirmed by its modular protein structure, and CCR5 targeted ZFNs were introduced into the T cells of HIV positive patients in late 2000s and 2010s. Ongoing CCR5 knock-out clinical trials have seen hundreds of patients dosed with these modified HIV resistant T cells, although CCR5 knock-out has not proven to be curative.

Delivery of gene editing reagents to the nucleus of human T cells with high editing efficiency and minimal disruption to cell state and viability remains a crucial challenge for all gene knock-out therapies. The limited DNA capacity of commonly-used lentiviral and AAV vectors to introduce new DNA to T cells hinders the direct expression of large targetable nuclease proteins in human T cells. More commonly, targetable nucleases are recombinantly produced, and the protein effector delivered directly into the cell. Because the knock-out inducing DNA changes caused by the targetable nuclease are heritable, transient protein delivery may even be therapeutically advantageous due to reductions in off-target gene editing effects. Various systems can deliver protein nucleases into the cell, ranging from early attempts at direct micro-injection in T cells, to osmotic pressure gradients, to lipid, virus-like, and nanoparticles. However, the formation of pores in the plasma and potentially nuclear membranes using electroporation have shown the greatest delivery efficiencies in human T cells. Ineffective membrane repair in many cells causes viability losses following electroporation, but the efficiency of delivery coupled with the robust expansion of T cells ex vivo has seen electroporation-based systems become the most widely-applied clinical delivery technology for gene editing.

CRISPR-based targetable nucleases identify their cut site through complementary base pairing of a guide RNA in complex with the protein nuclease. This drastically reduces the cost and complexity of generating a nuclease to target dsDNA breaks to a sequence of interest. The rapid development of CRISPR technologies in the early 2010s quickly saw the application of traditional DNA and protein delivery methods to ferry these new nucleases into T cells. With the toxicity of direct DNA delivery and the limited capacity of viral vectors, the first efficient CRISPR-based knock-out strategy employed in human T cells was based on complexing a recombinantly produced Cas9 nuclease with a guide RNA (gRNA) to form functional ribonucleoproteins (RNP), easily deliverable by electroporation. Gene knock-outs in human T cells have also been based on delivering mRNA templates for Cas9 along with a guide RNA, followed by RNP formation with newly translated Cas9 inside the cell.

Knock-out targeting and efficiencies in T cells have rapidly improved due to improved Cas9 protein variants, gRNA synthesis and stability improvements, increased ratios of gRNA to Cas9, and optimized electroporation parameters and additives. Small molecule inhibitors of DNA repair pathways have also been used, although many are hindered by toxicity issues in primary cells. The simplicity and efficiency of RNP based systems has even allowed for scalable array-based knock-out screens of hundreds of gene targets. Overall, efficiency improvements have enabled close to 100% single and even multiplexed knock-outs in primary human T cells. Additionally, similar electroporation-based RNP delivery has demonstrated efficient gene knock-outs in primary mouse T cells, facilitating pre-clinical development of human T cell therapies.

The power to heritably knock-out genes in human T cells destined for therapeutic use opens the central question of which specific gene targets in human T cells, when knocked-out, would improve anti-tumor function. Initial applications focused on well-known T cell immunotherapy targets, such as the immune checkpoints PD-1 and CTLA4, already targeted by monoclonal antibody-based therapies. Knock-out of PD-1 in CD19 CAR T cells showed improved clearance of mouse xenograft liquid tumors compared to control edited T cells. With virally integrated CAR and TCRs, knock-out of the endogenous TCR by targeting the endogenous TCRα or TCRβ chains may reduce TCR mispairing or off-target binding. Critically, the development of large-scale pooled knock-out screening technologies to human T cells has enabled genome-wide screens for desired phenotypic alterations, such as improved proliferation and resistance to exhaustion. The first published genome-wide knock-out screens in human T cells identified a novel regulator of adenosine suppression, and additional recent in vivo pooled knock-out screens have identified novel cellular immunotherapy targets such as REGNASE-1.

Human clinical trials of knock-out T cells are now underway. The first generation of gene knock-out therapies has aimed to improve the safety and efficacy of existing CAR and TCR viral-based cell therapies. Ongoing clinical trials have coupled knock-out of the endogenous TCR with viral delivery of a new CAR or TCR specificity. Most prominently, the first US phase 1 clinical trial of gene knock-outs in T cells aimed to simultaneously knock-out the TCRα and TCRβ chains as well as PD-1 using co-delivery of three separate gRNAs. A new TCR specificity for the melanoma antigen NY-ESO-1 was virally integrated as well during the manufacturing process. Although the need to define clinical trial parameters years in advance prevented the use of newer higher-efficiency knock-out methods, NY-ESO-1 TCR transduced and CRISPR modified, triple knock-out cells were found to be safe, and could be identified in peripheral blood samples for further phenotypic analysis. The variable efficiency of the three separate knock-outs resulted in the transfer of 16 genetically distinct T cell products (+/− NY-ESO-1 TCR, +/− Endogenous TCRα KO, +/− Endogenous TCRβ KO, +/− PD1 KO), even without taking into account mono-allelic versus biallelic knock-outs. The in vivo phenotypic information associated with these variable genotypes mirrors in vitro pooled screening approaches, and may offer a unique opportunity to accelerate discovery and therapeutic translation of T cell cellular immunotherapies based on endogenous gene knock-outs.

Targeted Knock-ins in T Cell Therapies

Genetic knock-outs can remove endogenous genes, but disruption of only so many endogenous gene products can be expected to have therapeutic benefit in T cell immunotherapies, and there is no guarantee that a desired T cell phenotype can be reached through knock-outs alone. Random integration of new genetic material into the genome of human T cells has been possible since the late 1980s, and continued improvements in retroviral and lentiviral vectors has made random addition of CARs and TCRs a mainstay of clinical T cell therapies. However, the ease with which dsDNA breaks can be created in T cells using ZFNs, CRISPR/Cas9, and other nucleases offers the opportunity to extend targeted genetic modifications beyond knock-outs to the seamless integration of new DNA sequences at a defined genomic locus. In dividing cells, dsDNA breaks can be repaired by non-homologous end joining, but also through templated repair off of the opposite chromosome, termed homology directed repair (HDR). While there are crucial molecular differences between different types of templated repair pathways, in general an exogenous DNA template containing a new DNA sequence flanked on each side by regions homologous to the site of dsDNA break is introduced along with the targetable nuclease. Complementary base pairing with the newly exposed cut DNA ends enables crossing over and templated repair, with the byproduct of adding the DNA insert between the homology arms into the genome with exact control over the final DNA sequence.

Importantly, homology directed repair is a stochastic process and the greater the concentration of DNA template in the nucleus, the more likely a given dsDNA break will be repaired by HDR instead of alternative pathways. The DNA repair template (also termed HDR template) can be introduced into T cells in a variety of formats, most commonly. Viral vectors are highly evolved to efficiently deliver their DNA genomes into the nucleus, and cloning an HDR template into the genome of an integrase-deficient virus can result in robust nuclear delivery of HDR templates. In conjunction with electroporation of a CRISPR/Cas9 RNP or other recombinant protein nuclease, viral HDR template delivery can result in efficient HDR-mediated knock-ins of large DNA sequences. Most prominently in T cells, integrase-deficient AAV6 vectors have shown extremely high efficiencies of targeted knock-in of multiple kB DNA templates. AAV approaches are however limited by the approximately 4.5 kb DNA carrying capacity of an AAV genome, limiting the size of inserted templates to 3–4 kb when subtracting the length of the necessary homology arms. Other viral vectors, such as integrase-deficient lentiviruses, have also shown successful knock-ins at variable efficiencies.

The electroporation necessary for RNP delivery, however, can also be used for delivery of non-viral DNA templates in a simplified editing pipeline. Short chemically-synthesizable single-stranded HDR templates (“ssODNs”) can be co-electroporated with RNPs to allow for integration of a handful of DNA base pairs, useful for the correction of individual mutations. Therapeutic engineering of T cells requires much larger integrations though, and recently large, targeted non-viral knock-ins in primary T cells using significantly larger ssDNA or dsDNA templates was successfully demonstrated. Co-electroporation of RNP and these large non-viral DNA templates resulted in efficient knock-in of large multiple kB DNA sequences with minimal impact on T cell viability or function, although published efficiencies of non-viral knock-ins remain lower than using more complex viral HDR templates. Recent improvements to non-viral template design and delivery have further raised the efficiencies of non-viral targeted knock-ins.

To warrant targeted integration of DNA sequences rather than more traditional and clinically-proven randomly-integrating viral vectors, knock-ins to a defined endogenous genetic locus must offer therapeutic benefits. The endogenous TCR locus represents a prominent example of where gene targeting may be uniquely suited. Knock-in strategies have been designed to replace the endogenous TCR with a CAR or new TCR specificity. By targeting the constant exon of TCR-α (TRAC exon 1), every cell in a polyclonal T cell population can be modified to express a desired CAR or TCR. Similar to targeted knock-out of the endogenous TCR with viral addition of a new TCR or CAR, this allows for the simultaneous removal of a T cell’s endogenous antigen specificity and replacement with a new desired antigen receptor. Further, dual knock-in of a new TCR-α specificity to TRAC exon 1, and a new TCR-β specificity to TRBC1 or TRBC2 can almost entirely eliminate mispairing of a new therapeutic TCR specificity with an endogenous sequence.

Beyond replacing the endogenous TCR though, targeted integration at the TRAC locus has the advantage of imparting the TCR’s endogenous regulatory control and expression circuits to the newly integrated antigen receptor. In mouse models of B cell leukemia, a CAR expressed by the endogenous TCR-α promoter cleared tumors at lower cell doses than the same CAR driven by a viral promoter. Future work will be necessary to fully characterize T cell’s functional differences when their new, cancer-targeting antigen receptors are driven by endogenous regulatory control, compared to high-expression, high-variability viral promoters.

The technology to perform large targeted gene knock-ins in human T cells has been in development for only a handful of years, compared to over three decades of progress with randomly integrating viral vectors. Continued technical improvements in both viral and non-viral knock-in efficiency, the size of knock-in templates, and the clinical translation to Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) compatible knock-in T cell production will drive greater clinical utility and translation. Screening platforms to assay large numbers of therapeutic knock-in constructs similarly will accelerate therapeutic discovery, and the technology to perform large-scale pooled knock-in screens of multiple kB DNA templates in primary human T cells has recently been developed. With large-scale screening approaches rapidly discovering new therapeutic knock-in sequences in T cells, and the first clinical trials of large knock-ins underway, the greater genetic control and synthetic engineering enabled by targeted gene knock-ins to endogenous loci will continue to expand the functional capacity of T cell immunotherapies.

CONCLUSIONS

Gene editing technologies have found their place alongside ex vivo cell culture, viral vectors, and synthetic biology as key pillars for the production of T cell immunotherapies. The greater control over cellular function that both heritable knock-outs of endogenous genes and even more so, the targeted integration of large new DNA sequences at defined genetic loci, offer will support the next generation of cellular therapies. Discovery technologies to perform genome-wide knock-out screens will rapidly expand the sets of therapeutic knock-out targets in T cells, and large-scale knock-in screens will allow for direct, head-to-head comparisons of numerous therapeutic modifications in primary T cells in the most physiologically-relevant model systems available. Targeted knock-outs and knock-ins must demonstrate clinical potential beyond what is possible with traditional randomly integrating viral vectors, but the continued failures of cellular therapies targeted against certain hematologic tumors and the majority of solid tumors offers a large unmet need. Synthetic manipulation of endogenous T cell genetics may drive this future expansion of curative cellular immunotherapies against poorly-treated hematologic and solid malignancies.
