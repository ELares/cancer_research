# Phase II trial of enfortumab vedotin in patients with previously treated gastric and esophageal cancers.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Enfortumab vedotin (EV), a Nectin-4-directed antibody-drug conjugate, was evaluated in patients with solid tumors in EV-202 (NCT04225117), a multicohort, open-label, phase II study.

**MATERIALS AND METHODS**: The gastroesophageal adenocarcinoma (GEA) or esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) cohorts from EV-202 included adults with predominantly heavily pretreated locally advanced or metastatic (la/m) GEA or ESCC, respectively. Patients received EV 1.25 mg/kg intravenously on days 1, 8, and 15 of each 28-day cycle. The primary endpoint was investigator-assessed confirmed objective response rate (ORR) per RECIST v1.1. Secondary endpoints included duration of response (DOR), disease control rate (DCR), progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), and safety. Of 40 response-assessable patients, ≥7 (≥17.5%) per cohort were required to declare promising antitumor activity.

**RESULTS**: In the GEA cohort, 42 patients (71.4% male; median age 62 years) received EV (median follow-up 11.1 months). Confirmed ORR and DCR were 9.5% and 47.6%, respectively. Median DOR, PFS, and OS were 10.3, 3.1, and 8.3 months, respectively. The most common all-grade treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) were pruritus (n = 16, 38.1%), alopecia (n = 15, 35.7%), and dysgeusia (n = 13, 31.0%). Twelve patients (28.6%) experienced grade ≥3 TRAEs. In the ESCC cohort, 44 patients (81.8% male; median age 66 years) received EV (median follow-up not estimable). Confirmed ORR and DCR were 18.2% and 45.5%, respectively. Median DOR, PFS, and OS were 3.9, 2.1, and 7.4 months, respectively. The most common all-grade TRAEs were pruritus (n = 12, 27.3%), rash maculopapular (n = 12, 27.3%), and dysgeusia (n = 11, 25.0%). Twelve patients (27.3%) experienced grade ≥3 TRAEs.

**CONCLUSIONS**: EV demonstrated antitumor activity in patients with predominantly heavily pretreated la/m GEA or ESCC. The predefined threshold for promising antitumor activity was met in patients with ESCC but not in those with GEA. Safety was manageable and consistent with previous reports.

Introduction

Gastric and esophageal cancers are a leading cause of cancer deaths worldwide. These cancers include proximal esophageal squamous cell carcinomas (ESCCs) of the upper and middle esophagus, and adenocarcinomas of the distal esophagus, gastroesophageal junction, and stomach, known as gastroesophageal adenocarcinomas (GEAs). The 5-year survival for patients with metastatic gastric or esophageal cancers is <10%, and second-line and later (2L+) treatments represent an unmet need. In patients receiving 2L treatment for GEA or ESCC, median overall survival (OS) ranged from 4 to 7 months. In patients with locally advanced or metastatic (la/m) gastric, esophageal, or gastroesophageal junction adenocarcinoma, first-line (1L) treatment is often guided by biomarker assessment, including programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) combined positive score (CPS), human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), and claudin 18 isoform 2 (CLDN18.2) expression. In patients with PD-L1 CPS ≥1, fluoropyrimidine and platinum doublet chemotherapy in combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) is recommended. In patients with HER2-positive or HER2-amplified disease, trastuzumab combined with chemotherapy with or without ICIs is the recommended treatment. In patients with CLDN18.2-positive, HER2-negative disease, zolbetuximab plus chemotherapy is recommended. In 2L+ treatment, patients with GEA typically receive ramucirumab with or without paclitaxel, or cytotoxic chemotherapy alone, such as irinotecan, while trastuzumab deruxtecan is recommended for those with HER2-positive disease. For patients with ESCC, the preferred 1L treatment is cytotoxic chemotherapy in combination with ICIs or dual ICIs for patients with tumors expressing PD-L1. In 2L, ICIs (if not used in the 1L setting) or cytotoxic chemotherapy can be considered.

Nectin-4 is a cell-adhesion molecule highly expressed in urothelial carcinoma that is also expressed in gastric and esophageal cancers. Enfortumab vedotin (EV) is an antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) consisting of a Nectin-4–directed, fully human monoclonal antibody linked to the microtubule-disrupting agent monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE). EV monotherapy has demonstrated significant clinical benefit over standard of care in patients with previously treated locally advanced or metastatic urothelial carcinoma (la/mUC; EV-301, NCT03474107) and in combination with pembrolizumab as 1L treatment for patients with la/mUC (EV-302, NCT04223856) and has been approved in several countries for these indications.

EV-202 (NCT04225117) is a multicenter, multicohort, open-label, phase II trial evaluating the antitumor activity and safety of EV in patients with solid tumors that are known to express Nectin-4. Here, we report the antitumor activity and safety of EV in adults with previously treated la/m GEA or ESCC.

Materials and methods

Study design

The study design of EV-202, including GEA and ESCC cohorts, and the design of the global trial was previously described by Swiecicki and colleagues.

The trial used a Bayesian optimal design for phase II. An interim analysis of antitumor activity was conducted when 20 patients treated with EV were evaluable for investigator-assessed response (stage 1). The minimum number of responders needed to proceed to stage 2, based on the target and reference objective response rates (ORRs), was n = 2 each for the GEA and ESCC cohorts. Of 40 response-assessable patients (stage 2), a minimum of 7 patients (17.5%) per cohort at final analysis were required to declare promising antitumor activity.

Study protocol/amendments were approved by institutional review boards or independent ethics committees and conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and the Good Clinical Practice Guidelines of the International Council for Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use. Written informed consent was obtained from each patient before any study-related screening procedures were performed.

Patients

Adults with histologically/cytologically confirmed gastric/gastroesophageal junction or esophageal adenocarcinoma were enrolled into the GEA cohort. Adults with histologically/cytologically confirmed ESCC were enrolled into the ESCC cohort.

Patients were required to have measurable disease by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) version 1.1 and an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0 or 1. Eligible patients had la/m disease that was not amenable to curative-intent treatment and evidence of radiographic progression during or after the previous treatment. Patients had experienced tumor progression or had discontinued treatment owing to toxicity after a platinum-based standard-of-care regimen, with two or fewer prior lines of cytotoxic anticancer therapy in the la/m setting; neoadjuvant or adjuvant cytotoxic regimens were considered to be prior lines of therapy if the tumor progressed in the 6 months after completion of the treatment. Eligible patients had received a HER2-targeted therapy if they were known to have HER2-positive cancer. They had also received a programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1)/PD-L1 inhibitor therapy unless contraindicated and had experienced tumor progression or had discontinued treatment owing to toxicity. Nectin-4 expression was not an inclusion criterion but was retrospectively assessed. See Supplementary Methods, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806, for details on Nectin-4 expression.

Major exclusion criteria included active central nervous system metastases, ongoing clinically significant toxicity (grade ≥2, except alopecia, including pre-existing grade ≥2 sensory or motor neuropathy) associated with prior treatment, uncontrolled diabetes mellitus [hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) ≥8%, or HbA1c between 7% and <8% with associated diabetes symptoms (polyuria or polydipsia) not otherwise explained] in the 3 months before the first dose of study treatment, and prior treatment with an MMAE-based ADC.

Intervention

Patients received EV 1.25 mg/kg intravenously on days 1, 8, and 15 of each 28-day cycle until disease progression or treatment discontinuation due to unacceptable toxicity, initiation of a new anticancer therapy, continuous dose interruption of >6 months, or meeting another discontinuation criterion. Dose modifications were allowed depending on the type and severity of toxicity. Dose interruptions were permitted at the discretion of the investigator and could last for ≤8 weeks (two cycles).

Endpoints

The primary endpoint was confirmed ORR [complete response (CR) + partial response (PR)] per RECIST version 1.1 based on investigator assessment. Secondary endpoints included: duration of response (DOR), disease control rate (DCR), and progression-free survival (PFS), all per RECIST version 1.1 by investigator assessment; OS; and safety. Key exploratory endpoints included: antitumor activity by blinded independent central review; biomarker analyses (including Nectin-4); selected pharmacokinetic parameters for EV and MMAE; incidence of antitherapeutic antibodies to EV; and patient-reported outcomes of the 5-level EQ-5D version (EQ-5D-5L) and global pain assessment.

Efficacy was assessed by computed tomography/magnetic resonance imaging at screening/baseline and every 8 weeks (±1 week) from the first dose of study treatment through post-treatment follow-up until whichever of the following occurred first: radiologically confirmed disease progression, initiation of new anticancer therapy, death, consent withdrawal, loss to follow-up, or study closure. After patients had received study treatment for 1 year, response assessment frequency was reduced to every 12 weeks (±1 week). If response was assessed as CR or PR by the investigator, confirmatory imaging was required 4 weeks (+1 week) after the first response. Confirmed best overall response was determined per RECIST version 1.1. If a patient did not have a confirmed CR or PR but had one or more tumor assessment of CR, PR, or stable disease ≥49 days after the first dose, they were considered to have best overall response of stable disease. PFS and OS were assessed in the full analysis set, comprising all participants who received any amount of study drug. Response-related efficacy was assessed in the response-assessable set, consisting of those patients in the full analysis set with measurable disease at baseline by investigator assessment who had two or more postbaseline scans or were no longer in response follow-up at the time of analysis.

A safety follow-up was completed 30 days from the last dose of EV and before initiation of subsequent anticancer therapy. Adverse events (AEs) were graded per the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events version 4.03. Prespecified AEs of special interest for EV were classified by Sponsor-Specific Query/Customized Medical Query or Standard Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities Query and preferred term, and were reported as composite terms [hyperglycemia, peripheral neuropathy, ocular events (dry eye, corneal disorders, and blurred vision), skin reactions, and infusion-related reactions] by treatment group. Safety was assessed in the safety analysis set, comprising all participants who received one or more dose of the study drug.

Statistical analysis

Data were summarized descriptively for continuous data and with frequencies/percentages for categorical data. Confirmed ORR was calculated with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) constructed by the Clopper–Pearson method. PFS, OS, and DOR were analyzed using Kaplan–Meier methodology, and estimates were provided with medians and two-sided 95% CIs.

Results

Patient demographics

The data cutoff was 3 March 2023 for the GEA cohort and 2 June 2023 for the ESCC cohort. In total, 42 and 44 patients were enrolled into the GEA and ESCC cohorts, respectively (Supplementary Figure S1, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806). Baseline demographics and clinical characteristics are shown in Table 1.

In the GEA cohort, the median age of patients was 62 (range, 33-79) years, and 30 patients (71.4%) were male. Thirty patients (71.4%) had prior treatment with a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor; as per the inclusion criteria, all patients had received platinum-based chemotherapy. Twenty-one patients (50.0%) had received three or more prior lines of systemic therapy, and 14 patients (33.3%) had received two prior lines of systemic therapy. Tumor tissue was available for baseline Nectin-4 testing in 33 of 42 patients; Nectin-4 was expressed in 31 (93.9%) of these patients. Median Nectin-4 H-score was 90 (range 0-290).

In the ESCC cohort, the median age of patients was 66 (range, 47-84) years, and 36 patients (81.8%) were male. Forty patients (90.9%) had prior treatment with a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor, and 43 patients (97.7%) had previously received platinum-based chemotherapy. Twenty-one patients (47.7%) had received three or more prior lines of systemic therapy, and 19 patients (43.2%) had received two prior lines of systemic therapy. Tumor tissue was available for baseline Nectin-4 testing in 41 of 44 patients; Nectin-4 was expressed in 40 (97.6%) of these patients. Median Nectin-4 H-score was 240 (range 0-300).

Antitumor activity

In the GEA cohort, among 42 response-assessable patients, investigator-assessed confirmed ORR was 9.5% (95% CI 2.7%-22.6%) (Table 2). Investigator-assessed confirmed DCR was 47.6%, with best overall response of PR in four patients (9.5%) and stable disease in 16 patients (38.1%). Postbaseline tumor assessments were conducted in 36 of 42 patients. Of these, 13 (36.1%) had reductions in target lesions from baseline and 7 (19.4%) had reductions of ≥30% in target lesions from baseline (Figure 1A). Among four patients with a confirmed response (Figure 1B), median time to response was 1.8 (range, 1.7-3.3) months. Median DOR was 10.3 (95% CI 1.6-10.3) months (Figure 2A). Among all 42 patients, median PFS was 3.1 (95% CI 1.8-3.7) months and median OS was 8.3 (95% CI 5.6-15.4) months (Figure 2B and C). Response assessments by blinded independent central review were generally consistent with investigator assessments (Supplementary Table S1, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806). The concordance rate of best overall response between investigator assessment and blinded independent central review was 100% (42/42). No clear trend between Nectin-4 H-score and best overall response was observed in the dataset (Supplementary Figure S2, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806).

Among 44 response-assessable patients in the ESCC cohort, investigator-assessed confirmed ORR was 18.2% (95% CI 8.2%-32.7%) (Table 2). Investigator-assessed confirmed DCR was 45.5%, with best overall response of PR in 8 patients (18.2%) and of stable disease in 12 patients (27.3%). Postbaseline tumor assessments were conducted in 42 of 44 patients. Of these, 15 (35.7%) had reductions in target lesions from baseline and 8 (19.0%) had reductions of ≥30% in target lesions from baseline (Figure 1C). Among eight patients with confirmed response (PR; Figure 1D), median time to response was 1.7 (range, 1.6-3.5) months. Median DOR was 3.9 (95% CI 2.3-7.4) months (Figure 2D). Among all 44 patients, median PFS was 2.1 (95% CI 1.9-3.7) months and median OS was 7.4 (95% CI 6.1-9.5) months (Figure 2E and F). Response assessments by blinded independent central review were generally consistent with investigator assessments (Supplementary Table S1, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806). The concordance rate of best overall response between investigator assessment and blinded independent central review was 97.7% (43/44). In prespecified subgroup analyses, among patients who had received less than three prior lines of therapy (n = 22), the confirmed ORR was 22.7%; median PFS was 3.0 (95% CI 1.7-5.5) months, and median OS was 7.4 (95% CI 4.5-18.7) months (Supplementary Table S2, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806). Among patients who had received three or more prior lines of therapy (n = 21), the confirmed ORR was 14.3%; median PFS was 1.9 (95% CI 1.6-3.7) months, and median OS was 6.9 (95% CI 5.5-9.5) months (Supplementary Table S2, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806). No clear trend was observed between Nectin-4 H-score and best overall response (Supplementary Figure S2, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806).

Safety

In the GEA cohort, 36 patients (85.7%) experienced treatment-related AEs (TRAEs; Table 3). The most common TRAEs of any grade occurring in ≥20% of patients were pruritus, alopecia, dysgeusia, rash maculopapular, fatigue, and diarrhea. Twelve patients (28.6%) experienced grade ≥3 TRAEs; the most common grade ≥3 TRAEs (occurring in two or more patients) were rash maculopapular, hyperglycemia, and neutrophil count decreased. TRAEs led to dose reduction in 9 patients (21.4%), dose interruption in 14 patients (33.3%), and treatment withdrawal in 1 patient (2.4%). No patients died owing to TRAEs. The most frequent TRAEs of special interest were skin reaction, peripheral neuropathy, and dry eye.

In the ESCC cohort, 42 patients (95.5%) experienced TRAEs (Table 3). The most common TRAEs of any grade occurring in ≥20% of patients were pruritus, rash maculopapular, dysgeusia, decreased appetite, and malaise. Twelve patients (27.3%) experienced grade ≥3 TRAEs; the most common grade ≥3 TRAEs (occurring in two or more patients) were rash maculopapular, hyperglycemia, decreased neutrophil count, and hypercalcemia. TRAEs led to dose reduction in 13 patients (29.5%), dose interruption in 14 patients (31.8%), and treatment withdrawal in 2 patients (4.5%). No patients died owing to TRAEs. The most frequent TRAEs of special interest were skin reaction, peripheral neuropathy, and hyperglycemia.

Pharmacokinetics (Supplementary Table S3, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806), immunogenicity, and patient-reported outcomes data (Supplementary Figures S3 and S4) are reported in the Supplementary Results, available at https://doi.org/10.1016/j.esmoop.2025.105806.

Discussion

Despite recent advances in immunotherapy and targeted therapy, effective treatment options remain limited for patients with la/m GEA and ESCC. In the current phase II trial, the predefined threshold for promising antitumor activity of EV was met in patients with ESCC with a confirmed ORR of 18.2%. Of 44 patients in the ESCC cohort, 43.2% had received two prior lines of therapy and 47.7% had received three or more prior lines of therapy; 90.9% of patients had received a PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor. In prespecified subgroup analysis, the ORR was 22.7% in patients who had received two or fewer prior lines of therapy. In a real-world study, ORR was 8.4% in patients receiving anlotinib monotherapy as a 2L+ therapy. Of these patients, 89.2% received anlotinib as a third- or further line therapy and 24.1% had received a prior PD-1 inhibitor. Owing to the scarcity of trials that enrolled similar patient populations to those enrolled in EV-202 (i.e. those who were predominantly heavily pretreated with chemotherapy and immunotherapy), only limited data were available to provide a historical/contemporary benchmark on the performance of a similar patient population.

AEs in this study were consistent with the known safety profile of EV monotherapy in patients with la/mUC. The type and incidence of TRAEs of special interest were also similar to previous reports. No new safety signals were identified. Treatment discontinuation due to TRAEs was low; TRAEs were managed with dose interruptions and/or dose reductions.

Nectin-4 was expressed in the majority of tumor samples; most patients with GEA had low to moderate expression, whereas patients with ESCC had higher levels of expression. In both the GEA and the ESCC cohorts, no apparent trend between Nectin-4 expression and best overall response was observed. In both cohorts, serum concentrations of EV and plasma concentrations of free MMAE were generally consistent with existing literature. Throughout the study period, antibodies against EV were either not detectable or detected only transiently.

The current study was a nonrandomized, single-arm study with a small sample size, which limits interpretability. Results can only be compared with previous data, which leads to further limitation because of the scarcity of trials involving patients with la/m GEA or ESCC who were predominantly heavily pretreated with chemotherapy and immunotherapy. In both cohorts, data interpretation of patient-reported outcomes (PRO) was challenging owing to limitations associated with study design, risk of bias, reducing levels of completion and compliance, and the small sample size. However, EQ-5D-5L and the global pain assessment data suggested that stability or improvement in quality of life/PROs could be further explored.

Conclusions

Antitumor activity and safety of EV were assessed in adults with la/m GEA or ESCC whose disease progressed following their previous treatment regimen. The predefined threshold for promising antitumor activity was met in patients with ESCC but not in those with GEA. The antitumor activity and manageable safety profile observed in the ESCC cohort may warrant further exploration of EV in this setting either as monotherapy or in combination with other anticancer agents.
