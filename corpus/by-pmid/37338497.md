The East Asian gut microbiome and its role in oncology: a narrative review

The field of onco-microbiome is rapidly expanding. Multiple studies have shown the crucial role of gut microbiota in the regulation of nutrient metabolism, immunomodulation and protection against pathogens. Tools for manipulating the gut microbiota include dietary modification and faecal microbiota transfer. Accumulating evidence has also documented the application of specific intestinal microbiome in cancer immunotherapy, notably in enhancing the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors. The aim of this review is to focus on the East Asian microbiome and to provide a current overview of microbiome science and its clinical application in cancer biology and immunotherapy.

‘All disease begins in the gut’

More than 2500 years later, the idea of gut microbiome and its modification has captured the attention of many through the presence of rapidly expanding scientific literature and increasing media coverage.

INTRODUCTION

The term ‘microbiome’ was coined by Nobel laureate Joshua Lederberg, who described it as “the ecological community of commensal, symbiotic, and pathogenic microorganisms that literally share our body space and have been all but ignored as determinants of health and disease”. The community of microbes that live in a human gut varies based on where a person resides, largely due to interindividual differences in diets, lifestyle and other environmental factors, which are potentially modifiable.

There are abundant published studies enforcing the association between the human microbiome and diverse medical conditions, such as inflammatory bowel disorders, depression and cancer. Specific to oncology, numerous landmark studies have reported the role of gut microbiota in the regulation of cancer molecular pathways, thereby affecting the host’s genetic stability as well as immune response. This, in turn, translates into clinical outcomes such as patients’ toxicity and tolerance to therapy, as well as their clinical response. There is already emerging data that gut microbiome modulation through faecal microbiota transplant can modulate and augment the response to immunotherapy. As the sequencing technology and bioinformatic tools continue to advance, they allow us to gain insight into the distinct microbial features associated with antitumour activity, and thereby exploit the microbiome for personalised medicine, to treat cancer better. This review showcases the unique role of gut microbiome in oncology and compels more research to profile the Asian microbiome that is present in the majority of the world’s cancer population.

DIET, LIFESTYLE AND ENVIRONMENT

Although the microbiome is a complex ecological community, comprising 100 trillion microbes and 10–100 times the number of genes compared to the human genome, with greater interindividual variability, the gut microbiome is also easily influenced by environmental exposure to potentially modifiable factors, namely diet, lifestyle, medications and supplements.

Dietary changes are the most nonintrusive and easily modifiable intervention. Studies have shown that dietary interventions in humans can alter microbial composition within just 24 hours of initiation, with reversion to baseline within 48 hours of diet discontinuation. Microbiome-modulating diets may contain both probiotic foods (e.g., kimchi, yoghurt) and prebiotic foods (e.g., legumes, banana, asparagus). Probiotics are live microorganisms with health benefits and prebiotics are food ingredients that selectively stimulate the growth of beneficial microbes. Beneficial commensal bacteria such as Firmicutes species, Clostridia species, Ruminococcaceae and Lachnospiraceae have been found to exert a marked effect on cancer prevention and treatment through microbial fermentation of carbohydrate to produce short-chain fatty acids. Among the short-chain fatty acids, butyrate has been shown to induce tumour inhibition and programme cell death in colorectal cancer cell lines. Proteobacteria and Bacteroidetes have been associated with pancreatic cancer through upregulation of toll-like receptors resulting in cell proliferation. Pseudomonas aeruginosa has been implicated in breast cancer due to immunosuppression and hence compromised immunosurveillance through activation of nuclear factor kappa B, and Bacteroides fragilis has been associated with bladder cancer as it promotes epithelial cell proliferation through interleukin-6 signalling pathway. The current evidence for a generally healthy gut microbiome advocates a diet of high diversity, including fermented foods, plenty of vegetables, consumption of ‘whole food’ and avoidance of excess red meat and processed items, which is related to positive health outcomes and incidence reduction of many diseases.

Smoking, an established risk factor for various cancers, has been shown to have a significant influence on the composition of gut microbiota and to increase Bacteroides–Prevotella in patients with Crohn’s disease and in healthy individuals. Other lifestyle factors like stress have also been found to impact the colonic motor activity via the gut–brain axis, increasing the incidence of colonic cancer through altering the gut microbiota profiles by causing a reduction in the number of Lactobacillus and its potential benefits, or secreting enteric-based corticotrophin releasing hormone that has been associated with increased inflammation and cellular proliferation.

In this increasingly interconnected world, there is frequent migration of people across national borders. A recent study of Asian immigrants has suggested that with urban migration, an individual’s microbiota rapidly assumes the structure of the country of migration. In addition, the links between ethnicity and metabolic disease have been well reported. Patients of East Asian descent are deemed more likely to develop health-related metabolic conditions at lower body mass index compared to their western counterparts. Host genetics and gut microbiome may potentially explain some of the differences across ethnic groups.

ROLE OF MICROBIOME IN ONCOLOGY

The discovery of gut microbiome’s impact on immunotherapy in cancer is considered one of the top five recent greatest discoveries. The potential of gut microbiome includes increasing treatment efficacy, providing innovative approaches for point of diagnostics, as well as risk stratification of disease. Identification of important microbial metabolic pathways is key to further oncology research, as these small microbial molecules (metabolites) may offer novel microbial-based biomarkers to aid in early detection or may be exploited in precision medicine to treat cancer patients better.

Gut microbiome continues to play a role in modulating a patient’s immune response, and there is mounting evidence suggesting that gut dysbiosis can be responsible for the outcome of inflammatory conditions including that related to infection by severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2. A recent East Asian microbiome-focused study by the Chinese University of Hong Kong studied and characterised the gut microbiome composition of coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) patients and showed that specific gut commensals with known immunomodulatory potential, such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Eubacterium rectale and Bifidobacteria, were underrepresented in COVID-19 patients. The study suggested that the gut microbiome potentially modulates host immune responses and is postulated to be a factor in determining COVID-19 severity.

Gut microbiota and immunotherapy

Immunotherapy, a treatment that takes advantage of our immune system to recognise and attack cancer cells, is already a standard therapy for some types of cancers. There are many different types of immunotherapy. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI), whereby monoclonal antibodies target the programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1), PD-1 ligand (PD-L1) or cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4), are currently the mainstay standard-of-care therapeutic options for various cancers. Furthermore, in multiple studies, the gut microbiome has been shown to regulate the cancer immune response, whereby modulating the gut microbes through probiotics or faecal microbiota transplant has been demonstrated in humans to significantly increase the efficacy and minimise toxicity of cancer immunotherapy.

Animal studies have suggested the potential role played by gut microbiome modulation in dictating the response to immunotherapy. Vetizou et al. demonstrated that when the gut microbiomes of mice were enriched with B. fragilis and Burkholderia cepacia, there was a significant increase in the efficacy of anti-CTLA-4 antibodies in reducing sarcoma tumour growth. This was also demonstrated in the report of Sivan et al., where the researchers proved that in murine models, the efficacy of anti-PD-L1 antibodies in melanoma was improved when the gut microbiome was enriched with oral lavage of Bifidobacterium spp.

A recent opinion piece by Routy et al. highlighted specific bacterial species, such as Akkermansia muciniphila, B. fragilis, Bifidobacterium spp. and Faecalibacterium spp., that have been associated with favourable anticancer immune responses in both preclinical tumour models and in patients with cancer. However, there is a need for future studies to refine the associations between cancer immunosurveillance and intestinal microbial communities within the context of organismal physiology.

Microbiome modification through prebiotics, probiotics and postbiotics

In recent decades, bioactive components like probiotics and microbial-derived biomolecules (postbiotics) have been used to modulate the composition of intestinal microbiota. Clinical human-based studies have demonstrated the usefulness of probiotics in improving chemotherapy-related toxicities, in particular, in minimising chemotherapy-related diarrhoea for colorectal cancer. International organisations, including the Multinational Association of Supportive Care in Cancer and International Society of Oral Oncology and the European Society for Medical Oncology, now recommend the use of probiotics integrated with Lactobacillus spp. in patients receiving chemotherapy and/or radiation therapy for pelvic malignancies.

Microbiome modification through antibiotics

Antibiotics and medications like proton pump inhibitors have been studied in multiple clinical studies for their potential to affect the diversity of gut microbiota. One study by Derosa et al. prospectively collected faecal samples of 69 patients with advanced renal cell carcinoma who were treated with ICI nivolumab and found that recent antibiotic usage reduced the objective response rates (from 28% to 9%, P < 0.03), and this correlated with reduced diversity in the composition of their microbiome. Derosa et al. also studied the impact of antibiotics in another study with 370 non-small cell lung cancer patients and found that the clinical efficacy of ICI was reduced when patients were given antibiotics 4–8 weeks before ICI commencement, as compared to patients who were not given antibiotics within 30 days from initiation.

The field of onco-microbiome is maturing rapidly, and earlier pioneer discovery studies need more prospective studies to validate and elucidate the precise microbial–immune mechanisms leading to patient response and toxicity to ICI. In addition, there is a further need to validate the in silico metagenomic microbiome findings with microbial multi-omics analysis, in particular, microbial metabolomic readouts. Furthermore, microbiome studies should also include host genome and immune phenotyping, so as to gain better insight into the host–microbial interactions in tumour biology and immunotherapy response. Lastly, most of the current datasets have been western centric, with a lack of representation of microbiota datasets from Asian populations.

THE ASIAN MICROBIOME

Studies have emphasised differences in gut microbial community structure among countries, and this is attributed to the unique ethnic diets and lifestyle practices, as elucidated previously. To further illustrate the cultural and geographical influence on microbiome composition, a migratory study published in the journal Cell in 2017 reported how the process of US immigration westernised the human gut microbiome. Currently, the existing public microbiota datasets are dominated mainly by Caucasians in western countries, thereby lacking Asian representation.

Likewise, microbial-based interventional studies are also less prevalent in the East. As of February 2022, there were about 1,400 clinical trials on gut microbiome, of which only 21% were based in East and South Asia. Compared to 452 clinical trials in North America and 468 clinical trials in Europe, Southeast Asia has only 29 clinical trials on gut microbiome. Abdill et al. analysed over 440,000 publicly available microbial DNA sequences from the past 11 years. Their study showed that more than 40% of samples were from the USA, which is five times the number from any other country. They found that Europe and North America provided more than 71% of samples, even though they comprise about 15% of the world’s population, whereas central and southern Asia contributed only 1.5% of samples from almost 26% of the world’s population. A recent commentary in Science highlighted the disparity of data from developing countries and suggested that the reasons for this inequity were low levels of research funding, lack of state-of-the-art technology and few people trained to analyse samples.

Research representing the East Asian microbiome is important for a multiracial country like Singapore, which comprises predominantly three ethnic groups — Chinese, Malay and Indian. Locally, there is a wide variety of unique dietary and other environmental exposures affecting the local microbiome composition. As such, Singapore is poised at a prime position to establish the East Asia microbiome database. Thus, more research within the region of Singapore is important to gain better insight into unique Asian microbial elements that can result from the culturally diverse influences, which may in turn, have unique microbial genes. The overexpression or underrepresentation of these genes can lead to disease and potentially affect cancer biology.

In today’s digital era, there already exist multiple applications of machine learning (ML) in oncology clinical practice. CURATE.AI is a joint automatic learning application developed by the National University of Singapore, Singapore and the University of California, USA. This application helps doctors to choose the optimal dosage and therapeutic combination for a patient. In the field of digital pathology, Penson et al. developed an ML approach to predict tumour type from targeted panel DNA sequence data obtained at the point of care, which were able to predict the tissue of origin in oncological practice and integrate the pathological diagnoses. Such artificial intelligence (AI) approaches can further be applied to facilitate microbiome-based prediction of clinical outcomes. AI technology is best positioned to input large amounts of complex healthcare information with microbiome readouts to formulate algorithms that would help to better predict an individual’s cancer risk, detect cancers early, prognosticate the patients and suggest potential treatment options. An example of ML in microbiome is ML4Microbiome COST Action, which was launched in Europe in 2019. This European net-based ML network encourages open collaboration among scientists all over the world to input their microbiome datasets, allowing for fully annotated microbiome data to be publicly available. A similar effort stemming from the US NIH Human Microbiome Project provides all its microbiome multi-omics, accumulated over the last ten years, through public libraries for everyone to use. Both networks provide the basis for a similar Asian-dominant dataset to be made publicly available, so as to open novel and exciting avenues to advance onco-microbiome discoveries, particularly for local patients.

CONCLUSION

In the last decade, the role of gut microbiome has been demonstrated across an increasing range of diseases, including cancer, particularly its role in cancer pathogenesis and progression, and its positive effect on treatment outcomes. However, the distribution of samples also sheds light on the deep inequities found in funding and the conduct of research. As Asia is a very diverse continent, having representation from Asian populations will paint a more complete picture of how different microbiomes impact health and could push the development of diagnostics and therapeutics to focus more on Asian populations.
