# Rational payload selection enables high antitumoral efficacy of an anti-EGFR antibody-drug conjugate against ovarian tumors.

## Abstract

The epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) is frequently expressed in ovarian cancer, yet its potential as a therapeutic target remains underexplored. We investigated EGFR expression and its therapeutic exploitation using antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) based on the clinical anti-EGFR antibody cetuximab. EGFR protein and mRNA levels were evaluated in patient-derived tumors and ovarian cancer cell lines by western blotting and qPCR, and cell surface localization was analyzed by flow cytometry. Several cetuximab-based ADCs were generated using clinically validated cytotoxics: DM1, deruxtecan (DXd), and monomethyl auristatin F (MMAF), with cleavable or non-cleavable linkers. Their structural integrity, antiproliferative activity, internalization dynamics, and mechanisms of action were examined in vitro, and in vivo efficacy and pharmacokinetics were assessed in SKOV3 and OVCAR8 xenograft models. All ADCs showed successful conjugation and preserved antibody integrity. MMAF-conjugated ADCs displayed superior antiproliferative effects, particularly the cleavable cetuximab-vc-MMAF, while A2780 and PEO4, which showed low EGFR expression, were less responsive. Cetuximab alone or free MMAF were markedly less active. Mechanistic studies revealed rapid internalization and lysosomal trafficking of cetuximab-vc-MMAF, leading to G2/M arrest, increased mitotic markers, spindle defects, DNA damage, and apoptosis. In vivo, cetuximab-vc-MMAF significantly inhibited tumor growth and prolonged survival without systemic toxicity. Pharmacokinetic analyses confirmed selective intratumoral accumulation of the ADC and its payload with minimal off-target distribution. Treated tumors exhibited reduced proliferation and increased markers of mitotic arrest, DNA damage, and apoptosis. These results demonstrate that cetuximab-based EGFR-targeting ADCs exert potent and selective antitumor activity in ovarian cancer, supporting their further preclinical and clinical development.

Introduction

Ovarian cancer remains one of the most lethal gynecologic malignancies, with a prediction of 314,000 new cases and over 200,000 deaths worldwide annually. This high mortality rate is largely attributable to late-stage diagnosis, resistance to chemotherapy, and the absence of robust early detection methods. High-grade serous ovarian carcinoma, which accounts for 75% of ovarian tumors, typically presents with widespread peritoneal dissemination, which limits the curative potential of surgical resection and complicates systemic treatment. In those cases, cytoreductive surgery is usually followed by platinum-based chemotherapy, often combined with a taxane. While initial responses are frequently observed, approximately 70% of patients relapse within two years, eventually developing platinum-resistant disease. The introduction of PARP inhibitors, anti-angiogenic agents like bevacizumab, and immune checkpoint inhibitors have modestly improved outcomes. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop novel therapeutic approaches that can overcome chemoresistance, and provide effective treatment options for patients with recurrent disease.

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) represent a novel class of targeted cancer therapeutics that combine the specificity of monoclonal antibodies with the cytotoxic potency of chemotherapeutic agents. By selectively delivering a toxic payload to antigen-expressing tumor cells, ADCs are designed to maximize antitumor efficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity. ADCs typically consist of three components: (i) a monoclonal antibody directed against a cell surface tumor-associated antigen; (ii) a cytotoxic payload, often a microtubule inhibitor (e.g., auristatins or maytansinoids) or a DNA-damaging agent (e.g., calicheamicins or topoisomerase inhibitors); and (iii) a chemical linker that joins the two molecules and influences payload release. Upon binding to the target antigen, the ADC-antigen complex is internalized and trafficked to the lysosome, where the ADC is cleaved. Upon cleavage, the cytotoxic payload can be released from the lysosomes and travels to the cytosolic or nuclear compartments, where its final target is located. In some cases, payloads are sufficiently permeable to diffuse across membranes, exerting a bystander effect, killing adjacent antigen-negative cells in the tumor microenvironment.

The human epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) appears as an attractive ADC target due to its expression in tumor tissues, capacity for efficient internalization, and established role in oncogenesis. The EGFR is a transmembrane protein that belongs to the ErbB/HER family of receptor tyrosine kinases, which also includes HER2, HER3 and HER4. In ovarian cancer, EGFR overexpression has been reported in 30-60% of cases, depending on the detection method and tumor subtype. High EGFR expression has been associated with advanced stage, high grade, and poor prognosis in several studies, although its role as a predictive or prognostic biomarker remains controversial. Recent efforts have been made on developing EGFR-targeting ADCs that leverage these features. Moreover, data from a phase I clinical trial using in head and neck and lung carcinomas, using an EGFR-targeted ADC have been published, showing encouraging results.

In the case of ovarian cancer, preclinical evidence that may support the clinical use of EGFR-targeting ADCs is lacking. Considering that EGFR-targeting ADCs could offer a potent and specific strategy for treating EGFR-positive ovarian cancers, it is relevant to explore the value of EGFR-directed ADCs in this disease. Here we report the efficacy and mechanism of action of EGFR-targeted ADCs in preclinical models of ovarian cancer, with the goal of advancing new therapeutic options for this high-risk patient population.

Methods

The Immobilon®-P transfer membranes, Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA), Hoechst 33342, and Matrigel were obtained from Merck Millipore Corp. (Darmstadt, Germany). The SMCC-DM1 cytotoxin (catalog reference: SET0101) was obtained from Levena (Tokyo, Japan). Cetuximab (Erbitux®, Merck Europe BV, Amsterdam, The Netherlands), T-DM1 (Kadcyla®, Roche, Madrid, Spain) and T-DXd (Enhertu®, Daiichi-Sankyo, Tokyo, Japan) were purchased from a local pharmacy. Propidium iodide and Annexin V were obtained from BD Biosciences (San Jose, CA, USA). Other general reagents used in the experiments were purchased from Sigma (St. Louis, MO, USA), Roche Molecular Systems (Madrid, Spain), Bio-Rad (Hercules, CA, USA), or Merck (Darmstadt, Germany).

The anti-human IgG ELISA kit and TMB were from Invitrogen (Gaithersburg, MD, USA). The anti-DM1, anti-DXd, and anti-EGFR endodomain antibodies were prepared in our laboratory. The antibodies directed to EEA1, RAB7, LAMP1, β-tubulin, cleaved caspase-3, pRb (Ser780), and pH2AX were from Cell Signaling Technology (Danvers, MA, USA). The anti-MMAF antibody was from ThermoFisher (Madrid, Spain). The total caspase-3 and BUBR1 antibodies were obtained from BD Biosciences (Madrid, Spain). The anti-Ki67 antibody was supplied by Origene Technologies (Rockville, MD, USA). The anti-tubulin and pHH3 antibodies were obtained from Merck Millipore. Antibodies against Cyclin B1, pCDK1, PARP1, and actin were from Santa Cruz Biotechnology (Dallas, TX, USA). HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies recognizing mouse IgG were from Cytiva, those interacting with rabbit IgG from Bio-Rad, and those recognizing human IgG from ThermoFisher. The anti-human IgG Fc-specific antibody used for coating ELISA plates, as well as the fluorescent secondary antibodies anti-human Cy3 and anti-Rabbit Cy2, were supplied by Jackson ImmunoResearch (West Grove, PA, USA). The anti-mouse Cy2 antibody was from GE Healthcare Life Sciences (Piscataway, NJ, USA).

Generation of ADCs

ADCs were prepared using cetuximab as a backbone, conjugated to various cytotoxic agents through different linker types. In each conjugation reaction, 2.5 mg of cetuximab were buffer-exchanged into PBS using a PD10 column (Cytiva, Madrid, Spain). For MMAF and DXd conjugates, the antibody was reduced using 8 molar equivalents of the freshly prepared reducing agent tris (2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP, 50 mM in water) (Merck), and incubated for 2 hours at 37°C. After reduction, the antibody was allowed to cool for 10 minutes at room temperature. Subsequently, 20 molar equivalents of the linker-payload (diluted in DMSO at a concentration of 12 mM), were added. The conjugation reaction was allowed to proceed for one hour at room temperature before quenching with 0.1 mM of N-Ethylmaleimide (NEM, freshly prepared in DMSO, at a concentration of 10 mM) (Merck) for 15 minutes. Finally, the resulting ADC was cleaned from the components used in the coupling reaction by passage through a PD10 column, and the eluate containing the ADC was sterilized by filtration, using a 0.2 μm low protein binding filter (Merck-Millipore).

Cell culture and proliferation assays

OVCAR8, SKOV3, OVCAR3, PEO4, and PEO6 cells were cultured in DMEM containing high glucose (4500 mg/L), while A2780 and IGROV1 cells were maintained in RPMI-1640 medium. Both media included antibiotics (100 U/mL penicillin, 100 μg/mL streptomycin) and were supplemented with 10% FBS. Cells were maintained in a humidified atmosphere at 37°C with 5% CO2. The cell lines were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA), and their authenticity was verified by STR analyses.

Cell proliferation was assessed using cell counting. Cells were plated in 6-well plates at a density of 20,000 to 100,000 cells, depending on the cell line. Cells were then incubated overnight at 37°C in culture media and the following day fresh media containing different drug treatments added. Cells were counted 5 days after treatment using a Z1 Coulter Particle Counter (Beckman Coulter, Pasadena, CA, USA).

Protein extraction and western blot

Cells were cultured in 100 mm dishes and treated 24 hours after seeding. At the indicated times, monolayers were washed with cold PBS and lysed in 140 mM NaCl, 50 mM Ethylene-diaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA), 10% glycerol, 1% Nonidet P-40, 20 mM Tris (pH 7.0), 1 μM pepstatin, 1 μg/ml aprotinin, 1 μg/ml leupeptin, 1 mM phenylmethylsulfonyl fluoride (PMSF), 25 mM β-glycerophosphate, 50 mM sodium fluoride, and 1 mM sodium orthovanadate. Samples were then centrifuged at 13,000 rpm for 10 minutes at 4°C to obtain the clarified extracts. Protein concentration was measured using the BCA assay (ThermoFisher, Madrid, Spain). In general, 50 micrograms of protein from each sample were loaded in SDS-PAGE gels, which contained 2,2,2-trichloroethanol (50 μl/10 ml of final gel solution). Stain free gel images were taken using the ChemiDoc Detection System (Bio-Rad Laboratories). For western blotting, gels were transferred to Immobilon-P membranes, which were then blocked for 1 h with Tris-buffered saline with Tween (TBST) (100 mM Tris [pH 7.5], 150 mM NaCl, 0.05% Tween 20) containing 1% BSA and 0.05% sodium azide. Membranes were incubated with the corresponding primary antibody (2-16 hours), washed three times, 7 minutes each, with TBST and then incubated with HRP-conjugated anti-mouse or anti-rabbit secondary antibodies for 30 minutes. Chemiluminescent detection was performed using the ChemiDoc apparatus, and band quantitation analyzed with Image Lab™ Software Version 6.0.1 (Bio-Rad Laboratories). Tubulin or actin were used as loading controls.

Quantitative RT-PCR

RNA from ovarian cancer cell lines was extracted using the Ambion PureLink RNA MiniKit (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA, USA), and cDNA was synthesized with the M-MLV reverse transcriptase kit (Invitrogen). Quantitative RT-PCR was performed in 96-well optical plates. Samples were prepared by adding 6.35 µL of Sybr Green (ThermoFisher), containing 0.2 µL of each forward and reverse primers (resuspended in water at a concentration of 10 µM), and 10 ng of each cDNA sample. The final reaction volume was adjusted to 12.5 µL with RNase-Free Water. For negative controls, water was added instead of cDNA. The plate was centrifuged at 3,700 rpm for 3 minutes to remove any air bubbles. RT-PCR was carried out on a Quant Studio 1 System (ThermoFisher). All samples were analyzed in duplicates and normalized to the endogenous control GAPDH. mRNA levels were calculated based on the RQ values and are represented as the mean ± SD of duplicates from a representative experiment repeated twice. The primers used for amplification of the EGFR gene and the endogenous control GAPDH were as follows: EGFR forward 5′–AGGTGAAAACAGCTGCAAGG–3′; EGFR reverse 5′–CCAGAAGGTTGCACTTGTCC–3′; GAPDH forward 5′–GAGTCAACGGATTTGGTCGT–3′; GAPDH reverse 5′–GATCTCGCTCCTGGAAGATG–3′.

Immunofluorescence assays

OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cell lines were seeded on glass coverslips in 35 mm dishes at a density of 100,000 cells per dish. Twenty-four hours post-plating, the cells were treated with 10 nM cetuximab-vc-MMAF. The immunofluorescence protocol has been described. Samples were visualized using a Leica SP8 confocal microscope (Leica, Wetzlar, Germany) and then analyzed using Leica LAS X software. Quantitation of colocalization in the immunofluorescence experiments between the cetuximab-vc-MMAF and the different endocytic vesicles was performed using ImageJ software (Coloc2 plugin). For each image, automatic thresholding was applied to minimize background noise, and the Manders’ coefficients (M1 and M2) were calculated as measures of the degree of overlap between fluorescent signals. The results were expressed as the percentage of colocalization. For each vesicle type and time point, 10 randomly selected fields were analyzed, and the mean ± SD of the colocalization coefficients was calculated and represented.

Flow cytometry experiments

For the detection of cell surface EGFR by flow cytometry, cetuximab was directly labelled with the fluorescent dye DyLight 488. Cetuximab was prepared at a concentration of 2.5 mg/mL in 0.05 M sodium borate buffer (pH 8.5), in a final volume of 200 µL. The antibody was then added to one vial (1 mg) of the DyLight Amine-Reactive Dyes Kit (ThermoFisher) and incubated at room temperature for 1 h. The excess dye was removed using Dye Removal Columns (ThermoFisher).

Ovarian cancer cell lines were plated in 60 mm plates and, upon reaching 70% confluence, were trypsinized, and then trypsin removed by a short centrifugation. Cells were resuspended in PBS+1% BSA and labeled for 15 minutes in the dark, using 10 nM cetuximab-DyLight 488. Fluorescence acquisition was performed using an Accuri C6 flow cytometer (BD-biosciences). A minimum of 50,000 events were analyzed for each condition. Results are presented as graphs of a representative experiment repeated at least three times. Quantification of these assays is presented as the mean ± SD of all experiments performed. Cell cycle and apoptosis evaluation by flow cytometry were carried out as described.

Fluorometric detection of caspase activity

OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cell lines were treated for different times (24 h and 48 h) with 10 nM cetuximab-vc-MMAF and lysed according to the protein extraction protocol described above under “Protein extraction” section. Fifty µg of extract were incubated in the caspase reaction buffer (25 mM HEPES pH 7.4, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 0.1% CHAPS, 10% sucrose, and 10 mM DTT) along with the fluorescent detection substrate Ac-DEVD-AFC (BD Biosystems), at a concentration of 5 µM. Lysates were incubated at 37°C for 1 h, and the fluorescent signal measured at 400/505 nm using a Synergy4 multiwell plate reader (BioTek Instruments, Winooski, VT, USA).

In vivo experiments

Animal experiments were performed in accordance with institutional guidelines and protocol approved by the Ethics Committee of the University of Salamanca (Salamanca, Spain, approval code: CBE-2019-325). Female BALB/C nu/nu athymic mice, aged six weeks and weighing 18–20 g (Charles River Laboratories, Wilmington, MA, USA), were kept in pathogen-free facilities at the University of Salamanca’s Animal Care Facility (Salamanca, Spain). Mice were injected one week after the quarantine period with 1 × 10⁶ OVCAR8 cells or 2 × 10⁶ SKOV3 cells, resuspended in a 1:1 mixture of DMEM and Matrigel (ThermoFisher). Tumor cells were injected into the caudal region of the dorsal flank. Once tumors reached a volume of 100-150 mm³, mice were randomized into two groups (six animals per group). One group received the vehicle (PBS), and the other group was treated with cetuximab-vc-MMAF. Treatments were administered by intraperitoneal injection once per week. To assess tolerability, SKOV3-bearing mice received weekly escalating doses, consisting of 3 mg/kg in weeks 1 and 2; 5 mg/kg in week 3; and 10 mg/kg in week 4. Mice bearing OVCAR8 tumors received a fixed weekly dose of 5 mg/kg throughout the treatment period. Mice were euthanized with isoflurane once tumor volume reached 1500 mm³. Tumor size and body weight were monitored three times per week. Tumor volume was calculated using the formula: V = (L/2) × (W/2)² × (4/3) × π where V represents tumor volume (mm³), L is tumor length (mm), and W is tumor width (mm).

Blood samples were collected via maxillary vein puncture on days 1, 2, 5, 9, 14, and 19 post-treatment. Samples were collected in EDTA tubes to prevent coagulation and centrifuged for 10 minutes at 3000 rpm at 4°C to obtain plasma. At the study endpoint, tumors and selected organs were harvested and either stored at −80°C for biochemical or fixed for immunohistochemical analyses.

Sandwich ELISA analysis

Analysis of cetuximab-vc-MMAF in plasma and tumor samples was performed using ELISA or western. To distinguish between total antibody levels (human IgG detection) and MMAF-associated antibody species, two complementary immunoassays were employed. Total human IgG (representing the cetuximab antibody backbone) was quantified using a commercial Human IgG ELISA Kit (ThermoFisher). Plasma samples obtained from mice were diluted 1:400 in PBS, and 100 µL of each sample was analyzed according to the manufacturer’s instructions. MMAF bound to the antibody was detected using a custom sandwich ELISA. Plates were coated with 50 μL per well of anti-human IgG Fc-specific antibody (10 μg/mL) and incubated overnight. After incubation, the antibody solution was discarded and wells were blocked with PBS containing 3% BSA for at least one hour. Then the blocking solution was discarded, and 50 µl of 1:100 diluted plasma was added and incubated for at least 2 hours. Plates were then washed four times with PBS, and the primary anti-MMAF antibody was applied at a 1:1000 dilution. After four washes with 300 µL of PBS each, the secondary antibody for MMAF detection (anti-mouse HRP, 1:1000) was incubated for 1 h. After washing again four times with 300 µL of PBS, the ELISA was developed by adding 50 µL of TMB substrate for 15 minutes, and the reaction was stopped with 50 µL of 1 M ortho-phosphoric acid. Absorbance was measured at 450 nm, with wavelength correction at 570 nm, using a Tecan microplate reader (Tecan, Männedorf, Switzerland). To generate the standard curve, we used a commercial antibody conjugated to MMAF (Belantamab Mafodotin, MedChem Express), allowing detection within a concentration range of 1.3-100 ng/ml. All samples were analyzed in duplicate, and results are presented as the mean ± SD of a representative experiment, with a minimum of two independent experiments.

Immunohistochemical and biochemical analyses of tumor samples

Immunohistochemical analyses were performed as described. The antibodies were used at a 1:100 dilution. The results were evaluated by expert pathologists from the Comparative Molecular Pathology Service of our center. For quantitation, positive cells were counted in 10 randomly selected fields (40× magnification) using ImageJ 1.44p software. Three independent samples from each condition were analyzed for each antibody. For biochemical analyses, tumors preserved at −80°C were lysed using a gentleMACS™ Octo Dissociator (Miltenyi Biotec), and the extracts were clarified for subsequent analysis by western blot or ELISA.

Statistical analyses

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA, USA). For Western blot analyses of patient samples, data were represented as box plots showing the median and the interquartile range (IQR) for each group. In the in vivo experiments, t-tests were used to analyze differences in tumor volume between control and treated groups, yielding an overall p-value for the experiment. Similarly, t-tests were used to determine significant differences in the western blot and IHC experiments derived from the in vivo studies. A p-value <0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

EGFR expression in ovarian cancer

To explore the possibility that the EGFR may represent a target of antibody-drug conjugates in ovarian cancer, the expression of the protein was assessed by western blotting in a set of 58 samples from tumors of diverse histological origin, including high grade serous ovarian carcinoma (HGSOC, n = 32), low grade serous ovarian carcinoma (LGSOC, n = 8), borderline tumors (n = 11), and endometrioid ovarian tumors (n = 7). As shown in Fig. 1A, EGFR expression was found in all four analyzed subtypes. Quantitative analysis (Fig. 1B) relativizing to the stain free gels (Supplementary figure 1), showed that the amount of EGFR was variable among the different samples. Of note, normal ovarian tissues also expressed the EGFR. The expression of the EGFR was also analyzed in seven ovarian cancer cell lines representative of HGSOC (OVCAR3, OVCAR8, PEO4 and PEO6), LGSOC (SKOV3), and endometrioid ovarian cancer (IGROV1 and A2780). Western blotting (Fig. 1C) and qPCR (Fig. 1D) showed variable expression of the EGFR in the cell lines analyzed.

To represent an ADC target, the domain of the protein recognized by the ADC should be exposed to the extracellular space. To analyze the surface expression of the EGFR in the ovarian cancer cell lines, cytometric analyses were carried out using cetuximab labelled with DyLight 488. Cetuximab is a clinically-approved humanized monoclonal antibody that binds to the ectodomain of the EGFR. Preliminary experiments showed that the coupling of DyLight 488 to cetuximab did not alter its ability to interact with the EGFR (data not shown). All the cell lines bound cetuximab-DyLight 488 (Fig. 1E). Quantitative analysis showed correlation between the amount of cell surface EGFR (Fig. 1F) and the total levels of the receptor, suggesting that cells expressing high amounts of the EGFR will also expose the receptor at the cell surface. Taken together, the quantitative analyses of the expression of the EGFR in the different cell lines indicated that OVCAR8 and SKOV3 expressed higher amounts than IGROV1 or OVCAR3 cells, which in turn expressed higher amounts than PEO4 and PEO6. The cell line with the lowest EGFR expression was A2780.

Biological effect of ADCs targeting the EGFR

The expression of the EGFR in patient samples confirmed that this membrane receptor could serve as a docking site for ADCs in ovarian cancer. Moreover, the expression of EGFR in human cell lines allowed their use to explore the potential antiproliferative action of EGFR targeting with ADCs. Taking these circumstances into consideration, and also the availability of cetuximab, we decided to use that antibody as a backbone for the construction of ADCs containing different cytotoxic payloads (schematically represented in Fig. 2A). Three cytotoxics were used, namely DM1, deruxtecan (DXd) and monomethyl auristatin F (MMAF), because of their already demonstrated clinical value. In the case of MMAF, two versions were prepared, one including a cleavable valine-citrulline (vc) linker and the other of a non-cleavable MC-alkyl-hydrazine linker (Supplementary figure 2). Gel electrophoresis followed by stain-free analysis showed an increase in the molecular weight of the heavy and light chains of the ADCs prepared, when compared to the nude antibody used for the coupling (Fig. 2B, stain free gel). The gel containing the different coupled antibodies as well as uncoupled cetuximab, was then transferred to a PVDF membrane that was sliced to be incubated with antibodies that recognized the different payloads. These studies showed that the anti-payload antibodies recognized the coupled cytotoxic, failing to interact with the nude antibody backbone. DXd and MMAF were coupled to the heavy and light chains of cetuximab, while DM1 preferentially coupled to the heavy Ig chain (Fig. 2B). The average drug-antibody ratio (DAR) of the cetuximab-based ADCs was determined by UV-visible spectrophotometry (Supplementary figure 3A). The prepared ADCs exhibited average DAR values within the range expected for ADCs generated using their respective conjugation chemistries. DAR values were also estimated using a Western blot-based approach. In these experiments, clinically approved ADCs with known DARs were included as internal reference standards on the same gels. Specifically, belantamab mafodotin, trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1), and trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd) were run in parallel with the cetuximab-based ADCs and blots probed with payload-specific antibodies. As shown in supplementary Figures 3B-D, the relative signal intensities of the cetuximab-based ADCs closely matched those of the corresponding reference ADCs, allowing estimation of average DAR values based on direct comparison under identical experimental conditions. Stain-free gel imaging demonstrated comparable antibody integrity across all ADC preparations.

The effect of the different cetuximab-based ADCs was then evaluated in the seven ovarian cancer cell lines. Dose-response studies were performed by cell counting with the four cetuximab-based ADCs, and results obtained in those cell lines plotted (Fig. 2C). MMAF-coupled cetuximab ADCs exerted a strong antiproliferative effect on most cell lines, except in the case of A2780 and PEO4. In the latter two cell lines, the poor effect (see also supplementary figure 4) could be related to the low level of EGFR expression as compared to that present in the other ovarian cancer cell lines. Cetuximab-DM1 had a minimal antiproliferative effect on any of the cell lines. Similarly, cetuximab-DXd was found to be less potent and efficient than the MMAF-coupled cetuximab ADCs. Together, these experiments indicated that MMAF-coupled cetuximab was more potent than DM1- or DXd-coupled cetuximab-based ADCs. Because of that, we developed further studies using MMAF-coupled cetuximab.

To explore whether attachment of the payload favored the action of the ADC, experiments were designed to measure the antiproliferative activity of the payload (MMAF) or the nude antibody (cetuximab) alone, comparing their effectiveness to that of the ADCs (cetuximab-vc-MMAF, cetuximab-mc-MMAF). As shown in Fig. 3A, the different cell lines responded similarly to free MMAF, which reached a maximal effect at concentrations between 0.5-1 μM. The antiproliferative effect of cetuximab-vc-MMAF and cetuximab-mc-MMAF was more potent, reaching a maximal effect at concentrations of 500 pM, except in the case of A2780, who was largely resistant to the ADCs. The nude antibody, at concentrations up to 10 nM, did not have a major effect on cell proliferation (Fig. 3B).

Internalization of cetuximab-vc-MMAF

The mechanisms of the antiproliferative action of MMAF-coupled cetuximab-based ADCs in ovarian cancer cells was then explored. Since internalization of ADC-target complexes represents an initial critical step in the mechanism of action of ADCs, the internalization of cetuximab-vc-MMAF over time was then explored. To that end, OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells were treated with a saturating dose of the antibody (10 nM) for different times. Immunofluorescent studies showed that at early incubation times, the ADC presented cell membrane staining (Fig. 4A and supplementary figure 5A). Extending the incubation times showed that in addition to the cell surface staining pattern, accumulation of the ADC was also present, following a dotted pattern. That dotted pattern progressively increased at times of 3 hours and beyond, reaching larger dots after 24 hours of incubation with cetuximab-vc-MMAF.

To analyze the intracellular trafficking of cetuximab-vc-MMAF, colocalization experiments with the EGFR as well as with markers of intracellular endocytic compartments were performed in OVCAR8 (Fig.s 4B and C) and SKOV3 (Supplementary figure 6). The ADC colocalized with the EGFR, as indicated by co-staining experiments using an anti-endodomain anti-EGFR antibody (Supplementary figure 5B and C). Moreover, treatment with the ADC provoked a time-dependent decrease in the total amount of the EGFR in both cell lines (Supplementary figure 5D). Cetuximab-vc-MMAF colocalized with EEA1, a marker of early endosomes, at the first time point analyzed (15 minutes) indicating that endocytosis of the ADC was fast. Colocalization of cetuximab-vc-MMAF with EEA1 peaked at one hour, decreasing thereafter (Fig. 4C). In the case of RAB7, a marker of late endosomes and lysosomes, colocalization started to be observed after one hour of treatment with the ADC, and progressively increased up to 24 hours. Similar kinetics of colocalization to those with RAB7 were observed in the case of the lysosomal marker LAMP1 (Fig. 4C and supplementary figure 6).

Cetuximab-vc-MMAF Induces mitotic arrest, DNA damage and apoptotic cell death

To explore the mechanism underlying the antiproliferative activity of cetuximab-vc-MMAF in ovarian cancer, we first evaluated its impact on cell morphology and cell cycle dynamics. Phase contrast microscopy of OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells showed that the ADC provoked rounding of cells (Fig. 5A). Flow cytometric analyses of DNA content in OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells showed that the ADC induced accumulation of cells in the G2/M phase compared to untreated controls, accompanied by a reduction of cells in the G0/G1 cell cycle phases (Fig. 5B and C).

Biochemically, treatment of OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells with cetuximab-vc-MMAF led to increased levels of phosphorylated histone H3 (pHH3), a canonical marker of mitotic chromatin condensation, and phospho-BUBR1, a spindle assembly checkpoint kinase (Fig. 5D). Cyclin B1, which partners with CDK1 to drive mitosis, was upregulated, especially in SKOV3 cells. These biochemical signatures suggested that cetuximab-vc-MMAF impaired mitotic progression. Moreover, increased phosphorylation of the retinoblastoma protein (pRb Ser780) was observed, further reflecting sustained CDK activity during mitosis, and suggesting cell cycle deregulation and incomplete mitotic exit.

Since MMAF acts by disrupting tubulin polymerization, a critical step for mitotic spindle assembly during mitosis, the effect of cetuximab-vc-MMAF on mitotic spindles was explored. Staining of mitotic cells with β-tubulin and DAPI revealed that ADC-treated OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells developed aberrant mitotic figures, including multipolar and disorganized spindle structures (Fig. 5E and F). These abnormalities suggest that cetuximab-vc-MMAF disrupts proper spindle formation, which could interfere with accurate chromosome segregation and delay cell cycle progression.

Prolonged mitotic arrest can trigger mitotic catastrophe, a cell death process associated with defective mitosis. To determine whether cetuximab-vc-MMAF induces cell death following mitotic arrest, markers of apoptosis were examined. Flow cytometry analyses using Annexin V-FITC and propidium iodide staining demonstrated a time-dependent increase in non-viable cells upon ADC treatment (Fig.s 6A and B). In both OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cell lines, the percentage of dead cells increased progressively at 24 and 48 hours post-treatment (Fig.s 6A and B).

To explore the potential involvement of DNA damage as part of the cascade of events triggered by the ADC, we analyzed apoptotic and DNA damage markers by western blot. Treatment with cetuximab-vc-MMAF resulted in a time-dependent decrease in PARP and an increase in cleaved caspase-3 in both cell lines (Fig. 6C). Enzymatic activity studies confirmed the activation of Caspase-3 by the ADC (Fig. 6D). Notably, increased levels of phosphorylated H2AX (γH2AX), a sensitive marker of DNA double-strand breaks, were also observed after 24 and 48 hours of ADC exposure.

In vivo action of cetuximab-vc-MMAF

To evaluate the therapeutic efficacy of cetuximab-vc-MMAF in vivo, OVCAR8 and SKOV3 cells were xenografted into immunodeficient female mice. Treatment with the ADC was initiated once tumors reached 150 mm3 (OVCAR8) or 200 mm3 (SKOV3), following the schedule outlined in Fig. 7A. In addition to measurements of tumor growth, blood samples were taken at the indicated days for pharmacokinetic analyses of ADC blood concentrations. As also indicated in Fig. 7A, the experiment using SKOV3 cells terminated 26 days after the first ADC injection, due to the fast-growing kinetics of tumors in untreated animals. In the case of animals injected with OVCAR8 cells, and due to the slower growth rate of the tumors, the experiment was extended for 107 days after the first injection of the ADC. At the termination of the experiments, several organs and the tumors (if present) were collected for the analysis of ADC accumulation in these tissues. In addition, several markers of cell cycle progression/apoptosis were analyzed in tumors, both biochemically and/or immunohistochemically.

Administration of cetuximab-vc-MMAF to mice bearing tumors derived from the injection of SKOV3 (Fig. 7B) or OVCAR8 (Fig. 7C) cells, resulted in a pronounced inhibition of tumor growth compared to the vehicle (PBS)-treated groups. Tumor volume in mice treated with the conjugate remained significantly lower throughout the study period, with P values of 0.0081 or < 0.0001 in SKOV3 and OVCAR8 tumors, compared to their respective control arms. Treatment with the ADC did not significantly affect the weight of the mice (Fig.s 7D and E) suggesting that the dose of the ADC used did not exert major toxicity. Kaplan-Meier analyses revealed prolonged survival in mice receiving the conjugate compared to control groups (Fig.s 7F and G). Individualized analysis of the tumors in mice treated with the ADC showed that some of the tumors reduced their size with respect to the values at treatment initiation (Fig.s 7H and I). Together, these results indicated that cetuximab-vc-MMAF exerted antitumor activity in vivo, effectively suppressing tumor growth, and improving overall survival in the two ovarian cancer xenograft models tested.

To analyze the pharmacokinetics of cetuximab-vc-MMAF, plasma concentrations of both human IgG (cetuximab) and MMAF coupled to cetuximab were monitored by ELISA, in animals xenografted with SKOV3 (Fig. 8A) or OVCAR8 (Fig. 8B) cells. Of note, samples from untreated animals did not give any signal in these experiments (data not shown), as neither did the first serum time point analyzed, which was taken one day before treatment initiation (Fig.s 8A and B). Injection of cetuximab-vc-MMAF caused an increase in the plasma levels of human IgG, which represents a readout of the cetuximab backbone, peaking at one day after the initial dose of the ADC (Fig.s 8A and B, left graphics). The plasma concentration of the ADC decreased thereafter until a second dose was administered, nine days after the initial one. Of note, the plasma levels achieved were substantially higher than the dose of 10 nM (indicated in the figure), that caused maximal antiproliferative effect in vitro. Similar pharmacokinetic curves were obtained when analyzing the levels of MMAF coupled to cetuximab (Fig.s 8A and B, right panels).

ELISA analyses demonstrated accumulation of both human IgG and MMAF within tumor tissues of mice treated with cetuximab-vc-MMAF, with no detectable levels in non-treated mice (Fig. 8C). Such accumulation was further assessed by western of tumors from untreated as well as cetuximab-vc-MMAF treated mice, using the anti-MMAF antibody (Fig. 8D). Immunohistochemical studies detected cetuximab-vc-MMAF within parenchymatous tumoral cells as well as in the interstices between them (Fig. 8E). The latter pattern was compatible with membrane reinforcement, characteristic of staining of membrane proteins. In addition, in animals treated with the ADC, staining was also present in the tumor-associated vasculature, with clear distinction from control treatments (Fig. 8E). Accumulation of cetuximab-vc-MMAF in other organs (lung, liver, kidney, brain, spleen, and heart) remained low, with respect to accumulation in the tumoral tissues of treated mice (Fig.s 8F and G and supplementary figure 7). Collectively, these findings indicate that cetuximab-vc-MMAF exhibits selective tumor targeting, prolonged systemic and intratumoral exposure, and minimal off-target distribution.

Additional studies were performed to explore biochemical changes related to the action of cetuximab-vc-MMAF in vivo. Western blotting (Fig.s 9A and B) as well as immunohistochemical (Fig. 9C) studies showed that treatment with the ADC caused a decrease in the amount of the proliferation marker Ki67. In addition, an increase in the mitotic marker pHH3 was detected both in the western blotting and the immunohistochemical studies (Fig.s 9A–C). The immunohistochemical analyses also revealed that the ADC augmented the amount of cleaved caspase 3 and γH2AX (Fig. 9C). EGFR expression decreased in the tumor samples treated with the ADC (Fig.s 9A and B).

Discussion

The present study demonstrates the preclinical activity of cetuximab-based antibody-drug conjugates in EGFR-expressing ovarian cancer models. Our data show that cetuximab conjugated to the microtubule-disrupting agent MMAF exerted potent antitumor effects both in vitro and in vivo. These results support the potential use of cetuximab, a clinically approved anti-EGFR antibody, as a targeted delivery vehicle for cytotoxic agents in ovarian cancer.

EGFR overexpression is frequently observed in epithelial ovarian carcinomas and is associated with poor prognosis, chemoresistance, and enhanced tumor aggressiveness. Despite this, EGFR-targeted therapies such as cetuximab and tyrosine kinase inhibitors have shown limited clinical benefit in ovarian cancer, likely due to low EGFR dependency in many tumors. However, the exploitation of EGFR as a target for ADCs does not solely rely on functional EGFR inhibition but rather on its ability to mediate efficient internalization of the payload-conjugated antibody. This paradigm shift opens new therapeutic avenues in tumors where EGFR is expressed but not necessarily oncogenic. Our data demonstrate that the expression of EGFR in ovarian cancer cells is sufficient to enable internalization and payload delivery, as evidenced by the potent cytotoxicity of cetuximab-MMAF in EGFR-expressing models. In fact, the internalization studies using cetuximab-vc-MMAF further confirm that EGFR effectively mediates intracellular trafficking of the ADCs in ovarian cancer cells. These findings reinforce the notion that EGFR is not only a valid surface target but also an efficient conduit for payload delivery, even in tumors that may not be driven by EGFR signaling.

One of the key observations from our study is the differential activity of the four cetuximab-based ADCs tested. Cetuximab-MMAF consistently showed greater potency than cetuximab-DM1 or cetuximab-DXd across a panel of ovarian cancer cell lines. This is consistent with reports showing that MMAF exhibits more efficient killing in certain cell types, by virtue of its ability to persist intracellularly and induce mitotic catastrophe. Although MMAE is a clinically established auristatin ADC payload, similar to MMAF, with membrane permeability and a documented bystander effect, MMAF was intentionally selected in this study to ensure that cytotoxic activity was strictly dependent on EGFR-mediated internalization and intracellular processing, allowing a more controlled assessment of target-specific delivery. In contrast, DM1, a maytansinoid payload used in the FDA-approved ADC trastuzumab-emtansine (T-DM1), and DXd, used in trastuzumab-deruxtecan, appear to be less potent in the in vitro ovarian cancer models tested, suggesting that the choice of payload significantly impacts ADC efficacy in this tumor type. Although membrane-permeable payloads such as deruxtecan can potentially enhance antitumor activity in vivo, cetuximab-DXd displayed minimal cytotoxicity in several ovarian cancer cell models in vitro, despite adequate EGFR expression. Consequently, the in vivo studies were prioritized for ADCs that demonstrated strong preclinical efficacy in vitro. Further investigations will be required to determine whether cetuximab-deruxtecan shows therapeutic activity in vivo. Moreover, in OVCAR3 and OVCAR8 cells, the cleavable form of cetuximab-MMAF was more potent than the uncleavable form, suggesting that linker chemistry represents another factor that influences ADC potency.

Importantly, our study demonstrates that cetuximab-based ADCs retain selectivity for EGFR-expressing cells. This was evidenced by the minimal cytotoxic effect of either ADC in EGFR-low models, such as A2780, and by the correlation between surface EGFR levels and sensitivity. These results suggest that EGFR expression could serve as a predictive biomarker for ADC activity in ovarian cancer. The possibility of using existing clinical diagnostics to identify patients with EGFR-positive tumors enhances the translational potential of these findings.

The in vivo efficacy studies in OVCAR8 and SKOV3 xenografts provide compelling evidence of the therapeutic potential of cetuximab-vc-MMAF. This ADC significantly reduced tumor growth, without causing significant weight loss or apparent toxicity in mice. The pharmacokinetic studies showed that the serum levels of the ADC remained substantially above the dose (10 nM) that exerted maximal in vitro antiproliferative effect. Therefore, under the experimental conditions established, the ADC should be available at maximal effective concentrations to act on tumoral cells at least for three weeks. Moreover, both ELISA and western studies confirmed accumulation of the ADC in tumors, while its presence in normal tissues was marginal. This is relevant as it indicates that the ADC has preference for homing to the tissues with high antigen expression. However, it is relevant to mention that cetuximab does not interact with the murine version of the EGFR, impeding accurate assessment of on-target, off-tumor effects of the ADC. That represents a limitation of the present study. Immunohistochemical and biochemical studies of tumor samples offered results compatible with the in vitro mechanistic studies. The ADC provoked a profound decrease in the amount of the proliferation marker Ki67, and augmented the levels of pHH3. These data are in line with the antiproliferative effects of the ADC. In addition, cetuximab-vc-MMAF caused an increase in γH2AX and cleaved caspase 3, indicating that the compound was able to promote DNA damage and cell death also in vivo. These results validate the antitumor activity observed in vitro and highlight the translational relevance of cetuximab-vc-MMAF.

It is worth noting that although cetuximab has been previously tested in ovarian cancer as a single agent or in combination with chemotherapy, its clinical efficacy has been modest. Our findings suggest that the therapeutic utility of cetuximab could be significantly enhanced by converting it into an ADC. This approach takes advantage of the well-characterized pharmacokinetics and safety profile of cetuximab, while expanding its antitumor activity beyond EGFR blockade. Furthermore, the clinical experience with cetuximab could facilitate the clinical development of cetuximab-based ADCs by leveraging existing regulatory and manufacturing pathways. The feasibility of EGFR-targeted ADCs is further supported by ongoing clinical development in other tumor types, as well as by the approval in Japan of an anti-EGFR ADC. Nevertheless, clinical translation of EGFR-targeted ADCs has proven challenging. Several EGFR-directed ADCs have entered clinical development, with depatuxizumab mafodotin (ABT-414) representing the most advanced example to date. Although this EGFR-directed MMAF-based ADC showed encouraging preclinical activity, clinical studies revealed limited efficacy at tolerated doses together with dose-limiting ocular toxicity. In ovarian cancer, the clinical approval of the folate receptor α-directed ADC mirvetuximab soravtansine, carrying the maytansinoid DM4, provides a relevant benchmark for the therapeutic potential of microtubule-disrupting ADCs in this disease. In this framework, our results demonstrate that EGFR can function as an efficient delivery route in ovarian cancer models and that ADC design, including the use of MMAF with sustained intracellular persistence and mitotic arrest activity, may help overcome limitations observed with earlier EGFR-ADC approaches.

An important strength of our study is the use of both in vitro and in vivo models to evaluate ADC efficacy. However, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, while our in vivo experiments demonstrate significant antitumor activity, they were performed in immunodeficient mice, which do not recapitulate the immune microenvironment of human ovarian tumors. Given that cetuximab has known immune effector functions via FcγR binding, future studies in immunocompetent or humanized models may be necessary to assess the contribution of antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) to the overall efficacy of the ADCs. Second, although we focused on surface EGFR levels as a biomarker of sensitivity, additional factors such as internalization rate, trafficking efficiency, lysosomal function, and expression of drug efflux pumps could modulate ADC response. These parameters warrant further investigation to refine patient selection strategies. Finally, as mentioned above, while cetuximab-vc-MMAF did not show overt off-target cytotoxic activity, it is worth mentioning that cetuximab does not recognize the murine version of the EGFR, therefore preventing evaluation of on-target effects of the ADC in normal tissues. This represents a limitation of the present study, as it is the lack of evaluation of cetuximab-deruxtecan in an in vivo setting.

Conclusion

In this study, we developed and characterized cetuximab-based antibody-drug conjugates directed against EGFR, which exhibited strong and selective antitumor effects in ovarian cancer models. These ADCs effectively inhibited cell proliferation and induced mitotic arrest and apoptosis both in vitro and in vivo. Our findings provide compelling preclinical evidence supporting the development of EGFR-targeted ADCs as a promising therapeutic strategy for patients with EGFR-expressing ovarian tumors.
