# Cancer genetics and response to oncolytic virus treatment for ovarian cancer.

## Abstract

Ovarian cancers (OCs) are often defined as poorly immunogenic tumors that have low response rates to current immunotherapies and frequently develop resistance to chemotherapies. Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are an emerging therapeutic approach that is favored due to its multifactorial mechanism of action; OVs aim to enhance immune cell recovery and infiltration into the tumor, in addition to assisting the immune system to identify and target evasive tumors. While many different OVs have been studied, this review focuses on the four that have been extensively tested in preclinical models and clinical trials with OC patients: vaccinia viruses, vesicular stomatitis virus, herpes simplex 1, and adenoviruses. We will first explore how these viruses have been developed, modified and tested as monotherapies in OCs, with limited success. The various combinatorial approaches involving OVs that are currently being investigated to improve the outcomes for OC patients will then be addressed. Attention will be given to how the genetics of OC cells may influence response to OVs and how that has led to genetic modifications of OVs that improve the cancer specificity and efficacy of these therapies.

Plain Language Summary

This review explores the impact of both viral and cancer cell genetics on the success of oncolytic virus (OV) therapy in ovarian cancers. Herein, we evaluate published studies on the four most commonly used viruses in ovarian cancer, which are vaccinia virus, adenovirus, herpes simplex virus 1, and vesicular stomatitis virus. While each of these viruses has been tested in clinical trials, efficacy in patients has been somewhat limited. This may be due in part to the lack of consideration of cancer genetics, since cancers with certain mutations may be better candidates for viral infection. Additionally, many OV therapies have focused on delivery as monotherapies. However, combinational approaches, including pairing OVs with chemotherapies, other drugs, or even other OVs, is becoming a new focus for the field and may substantially improve patient outcomes.

Introduction

Despite many advancements in the treatment of other cancers, the long-term survival rate for ovarian cancer (OC) patients has not improved significantly in the past 30 years, with 5-year survival remaining below 45%. There are two main factors that contribute to this poor survival rate: delays in the detection and diagnosis of OC, and development of tumor resistance to current treatments. There are currently no effective screening programs for OCs. Instead, transvaginal ultrasounds are used to detect tumors once a patient starts to exhibit symptoms; however, due to the vagueness of these symptoms (i.e., bloating, loss of appetite, pelvic or abdominal pain, feeling full quickly after eating, and urinary changes), they are often overlooked. The result is that most women are not tested until tumors have progressed into advanced stages with poor prognosis. Once tumors are identified, the current standard of care includes surgical debulking to remove the tumor mass and platinum-based chemotherapy. However, while most patients will initially respond to chemotherapy, up to 80% will experience a recurrence depending on their stage.

OCs are divided into different types depending on their histological features, molecular characteristics, and the cell of origin. Epithelial ovarian cancers (EOCs) account for roughly 90% of all OCs and are further classified into four subtypes: serous, mucinous, clear-cell carcinoma, and endometrioid. Serous EOCs are further divided based on the grade of the tumor into either high- or low-grade cancers, with the majority (70%) of all diagnosed OCs and 80% of OC-related deaths being due to high-grade serous cancers (HGSC). Consequently, much research has focused on developments to treat HGSC specifically. Other, less common types of ovarian tumors include stromal, germ cell, primary peritoneal serous carcinomas, and borderline ovarian tumors.

The genetic mutation profile of HGSC is one factor that can alter therapeutic efficacy. Mutations in the TP53 tumor suppressor gene occur in nearly all HGSC, which is unique compared to other OC subtypes. Although many p53-based therapies have been explored, there has been limited translational success. The incidence of HGSC is also heavily influenced by inherited genetic mutations, most commonly in BRCA1 or BRCA2, which account for up to 20% of cases. Mutations in RAD51C, RAD51D, BRIP1, PALB2 and mismatch repair genes (Lynch Syndrome) are also known to increase the risk of ovarian cancer.

Current treatments

In addition to platinum-based chemotherapy, one class of novel therapies used to treat HGSC is poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors (PARPi), which function by blocking the PARP family of enzymes that repair single-stranded DNA breaks. PARPi are most effective in tumors with mutation or defects of expression in BRCA1 or BRCA2 genes that code for enzymes involved in double-stranded DNA repair. PARPi thus induce synthetic lethality, where cancer cells have two dysfunctional DNA repair mechanisms and undergo increased cell death. To date, the FDA has approved three PARPi for use in OCs, which include olaparib, niraparib, and rucaparib. However, 40–70% of patients who receive PARPi develop resistance through either BRCA reversion or mutations in RAD51C and RAD51D, which have also been linked to chemotherapy resistance.

Another commonly used treatment for OCs is bevacizumab, an inhibitor of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-A inhibitor. Bevacizumab blocks VEGF-induced angiogenesis and ultimately inhibits tumor growth. A phase III double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial found the maximum separation in progression-free survival (PFS) curves of EOC, primary peritoneal or fallopian tube cancer patients who received drug versus placebo occurred at 15 months. However, the PFS for both groups converged around 22 months, indicating no further benefit.

Testing of immunotherapies

There has been a strong push to explore immunotherapies designed to enhance and re-activate the immune system to kill the tumor cells. Two of the most common are immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapies. ICIs function by blocking immune checkpoints, which are receptors found on immune cells and/or cancer cells that block the immune system from recognizing and killing affected cells. These ICIs often target PD-1 and LAG-3, which are expressed on T cells and natural killer (NK) cells, or PD-L1, which is often expressed on cancer cells. Unfortunately, due to the highly immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) found in OCs, only 10–15% of patients are responsive to ICI monotherapies. CAR-T cell therapy, on the other hand, involves collecting T cells from the patient and modifying them ex vivo to express synthetic receptors that will recognize tumor antigens, allowing these T cells to attack the tumor once they are re-introduced into the patient. While CAR-T therapy has been a promising avenue for hematologic cancers, it has been largely ineffective in OCs due to the heterogeneity of the tumors, loss or downregulation of antigen expression, off-target effects, and the immunosuppressive nature of the TME.

To improve therapeutic outcomes for patients with OC, new treatment options are needed to reverse the immunosuppressive nature of the TME for current immunotherapies to be effective. One promising approach is the use of OVs.

Oncolytic viruses

OVs kill cancer cells either directly through cell lysis or indirectly by the delivery of a transgene that can code for various cytokines, transcription factors, or inhibitors. A critical feature of these viruses is induction of immune response via both innate and adaptive immune pathways that can also lead to immunogenic cell death (as shown in Figure 1). OV-induced tumor cell lysis helps to reverse immunosuppression in the TME via release of tumor-associated antigens, danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), and pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) that were previously hidden. Released PAMPs and DAMPs can drive innate production of type I interferons (IFNs) (α and β) that lead to enhanced Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) I/II antigen presentation by antigen presenting cells (APCs), induction of APC maturation, and the stimulation of immunogenic cytokine release (ex: IL1β, TNF-α, and IL-12). This leads to the recruitment of additional immune cells to the TME, where T lymphocytes and NK cells can be primed and activated by APCs to attack tumor cells, including those at metastatic sites.OVs can be used effectively for cancer cell lysis without affecting healthy cells due the dysregulation of immune pathways that occur in cancer. Typically, when a virus enters the body, pattern recognition receptors located on or within cells detect these foreign molecules and signal downstream effectors to cause gene expression changes that alert the immune system to respond to clear the infection before disease can spread. In OCs specifically, important immune pathways such as IFN, MHC I presentation, and cyclic GMP-AMP synthase – stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS-STING) pathways are all downregulated, facilitating cancer progression. As a result of modified cancer genetics, cancer cells are unable to clear OV infections while healthy cells can, making cancer cells more likely to succumb to the lytic effects. As previously discussed, the TME of OCs has increased expression of many immunosuppressive factors (such as IL-10, TGF-β), which can impede recruitment of immune cells to the tumor. However, the use of OVs has been shown to significantly alter the TME by inducing Th1 cells and depleting immunosuppressive cells, such as M2 macrophages, to help overcome this immune suppression. 

To date, five OVs have been approved for clinical use in humans. However, only two are FDA-approved. In 2004, Rigvir® was the first oncolytic virus to be approved for use in melanoma patients in Latvia. Since then, Oncorine (H101), a modified adenovirus, has been approved for use in head, neck, and esophageal cancers in China. Talimogene laherparepvec (aka T-VEC or IMLYGIC®), a modified herpes simplex 1 virus (HSV-1), was approved by the FDA in 2016 for use in melanomas. More recently, Japan has approved a HSV-1 virus called Teserpaturev/G47Δ (Delytact®) for use in glioblastomas. The FDA has also approved nadofaragene firadenovec, a non-replicating adenovirus for gene therapy in non-muscle invasive bladder cancer. While no OV has been approved for clinical use in OCs to date, many groups are investigating strategies that use similar viral backbones to target OCs.

Designing OVs for cancer treatment

There are several design aspects that must be addressed in the development of OVs. First, the natural prevalence of the virus in the community must be considered. When a patient has prior exposure to a virus strain, there may be preexisting immunity and antibodies against the OV that may destroy it before it reaches the tumor. Viral tropism is another factor to consider since an effective OC therapy must have natural or genetically engineered affinity to replicate within ovarian tumors. Tropism can also influence off target effects, which can be limited by selecting OVs lacking tropism for other organs.

The genomes of OVs are also commonly edited to prevent replication in non-cancerous cells. This is a safety modification that leverages common molecular pathways that are defective in tumor cells but remain functional in healthy cells. For instance, IFN signaling is commonly defective in tumor cells to allow for rapid replication; however, this adaptation lowers the cells’ defenses to intracellular pathogens such as OVs. In contrast, a healthy cell can effectively clear OV infection via IFN signaling. Furthermore, the possibility of viral genome integration into the patient’s genome is a possibility for some OV strains that could lead to off-target effects when pathogenic components of the virus are permanently integrated into the genome (i.e., HIV). Finally, virus selection should be taken into account since the size of an OV genome can dictate the maximum size of compatible transgene and how easily the genome can be manipulated.

In the remainder of this review, we will explore different OV strategies that have been designed to target OCs. While many different types of viruses have been explored for OCs, we will focus on the four most commonly used, first providing an overview of their testing as monotherapies, and then elaborating on their use in combinational approaches to improve therapeutic outcomes.

OVs as Monotherapies

There are primarily four OVs that have been used for OC research: vaccinia, HSV-1, adenoviruses, and vesicular stomatitis viruses (VSV). The following describes how these viruses have been modified for anti-cancer use and some of the studies that have tested their use as monotherapies. Their key features are summarized in Table 1.

Vaccinia virus

Vaccinia virus is a member of the Poxviridae family, a group of double-stranded DNA viruses best known for its use against smallpox. Vaccinia viruses replicate exclusively in the cytoplasm and therefore must encode their own DNA and RNA polymerases, resulting in larger genomes (190 kb with 200–250 protein coding genes) that contain many non-essential genes which can be disrupted or deleted to accommodate large sequences of foreign DNA (25–30 kb). Vaccinia virus has a natural tropism for the ovary and enters host cells via membrane fusion. Early gene products produced include proteins required for DNA replication and transcription of intermediate/late genes. Once the virus genome has been replicated and translated, the viral components are assembled into new viral particles, a process termed morphogenesis. The newly formed particles are then released, either through viral budding or cell lysis.

One of the most common genetic alterations to vaccinia viruses is the deletion of the viral thymidine kinase (TK) gene. As a result, the virus relies upon the host cells to provide TK, which is most abundant in rapidly dividing cells such as tumors (Figure 2). An example of this type of modified virus is JX-594, an edited Wyeth strain of vaccinia in which the viral TK gene has been replaced with the human gene that codes for granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), an immunostimulatory cytokine. While in vitro data showed that low concentrations of JX-594 [ < 1 plaque-forming units (pfu)/cell] were sufficient to infect OC cell lines and that 5/6 ex vivo tumor explants were susceptible to the virus, there is limited information about its use in clinical trials. A published abstract for a phase Ib trial in 10 patients with advanced solid tissues reported stable disease in an OC patient, but a registered phase II trial to test this virus in patients with peritoneal carcinomatosis of OC origin (NCT02017678) was withdrawn. 

While the Wyeth strain used to make JX-594 is a replicating strain, some groups have focused on using a non-replicating vaccinia called Modified Vaccinia Ankara virus (MVA). This strain is unique because it is unable to undergo morphogenesis and has a smaller genome (178 kb). As a result, MVA viruses complete all stages of gene expression, thus allowing for transgene expression, but stops the replication process before producing viral particles that directly lyse cells. MVA has been used to produce MVA-5T4 (TroVax®) that encodes the human tumor antigen 5T4, a cell surface antigen that makes tumors more recognizable to the immune system as foreign. Unfortunately, a phase II clinical trial conducted with 94 OC patients with stage III – IV disease found that MVA-5T4 had no survival benefit.

GL-ONC1 (Olvi-Vec) is a more promising candidate consisting of a Lister strain of vaccinia virus with three transgenes – Ruc-GFP, B-glucuronidase, and B-galactosidase. In a phase I clinical trial on patients with advanced peritoneal carcinomatosis, 3 of whom had OC, patients received three doses of 10^7–10^9 PFU per cycle by intraperitoneal (i.p.) infusions, for up to 4 cycles. The virus was shown to be safe and well-tolerated, with no maximum-tolerated dose (MTD) being reached and no grade 4 adverse events observed. Additionally, there was no observed difference in the number of side effects experienced by patients who had been previously vaccinated with vaccinia virus compared to those who had not. The study showed that the virus had infected, replicated in, and killed tumor cells in 8/9 patients, and highlighted how reporters (Ruc-GFP and B-glucuronidase) could be used to monitor infection and oncolysis in real-time. However, a key limitation noted by the authors was that the patients studied were at advanced stages of disease, therefore tumors were less likely to show significant regression. There is a need to test GL-ONC1 in early-stage disease. In a phase II trial, 14 patients with platinum-resistant OC and 12 patients with platinum-refractory OC were given GL-ONC1, followed 6 weeks later with platinum-based chemotherapy. They reported that 54% of patients had an objective response rate with a median PFS of 11 months. GL-ONC1 has now moved into a phase III trial (NCT05281471).

Herpes simplex virus 1

Another OV extensively studied in OC is herpes simplex viruses (HSV) 1, a large (152 kb genome), double-stranded DNA, Herpesviridae virus that replicates in the nucleus but does not cause genome integration.

Many studies using HSV-1 for OCs have used genetically altered virus to increase cancer cell death while decreasing neurotropism and off-target effects. One of the most common genetic alterations is the deletion of both copies of the γ34.5 gene, which encodes the γ34.5 protein (also called ICP34.5) that blocks IFN I signaling (Figure 3). When a cell is infected with a virus, IFN-I signaling is typically triggered to mediate anti-viral responses and the host cell is directed to shut off protein synthesis via protein kinase R (PKR) activation. PKR disables the translation initiation factor eIF2α via phosphorylation and, as a result, both host and viral protein synthesis are inhibited. To prevent this from occurring, γ34.5 protein encoded by HSV interacts with host phosphatase PP1α, causing the enzyme to dephosphorylate eIF2α and allow for protein synthesis to continue. By deleting the gene encoding γ34.5, HSV-1 becomes more selective for cancer cells with defective IFN I pathways. Additionally, due to the role of γ34.5 in neurovirulence, deleting this gene also reduces potential off-target effects and improves the safety profile. 

Intense investigation of the use of γ34.5-deleted mutants in OCs took place in the 1990’s and early 2000’s. Coukos et al. reported that HSV-1716, a γ34.5 deleted strain, is able to infect and cause cytopathic effects in various EOC cell lines and primary cultures, with primary cultures being 10-to-20 fold more sensitive to the virus than cell lines. Additionally, they found that PA-1 and A2780 cell lines were the most sensitive to this strain while SKOV3 was the least sensitive. When using SKOV3 and A2780 cells as xenografts into SCID mice, they found that one dose of HSV-1716 three weeks after tumor cell injection was able to significantly reduce tumor size and prolong survival compared to controls. They also determined that PA-1 cells could be used as carrier cells for the virus, meaning cells that are infected in vitro, irradiated, and injected into the mice could be used for viral delivery to prevent the virus from being exposed to anti-herpes antibodies that may exist in patients that have been previously exposed to wildtype HSV strains. Using mice xenografted with SKOV3 and A2780, they discovered that PA-1 cells 1) preferentially bound to tumor peritoneal tissues over healthy tissue and that 2) the use of these carrier cells could significantly prolong survival and decrease tumor size in treated mice compared to uninfected irradiated controls. In other studies, HSV-1716 has been shown to effectively kill tumor cells in an immunocompetent model previously exposed to a wildtype HSV strain. Although HSV-1716 has not yet been tested in OC patients in clinical trials, recent Phase I trials in pediatric patients with other types of solid tumors have shown intratumoral and intravenous injections are safe.

Other groups have used HSV-1 viruses to deliver cytokines, such as IL-12, or GM-CSF. Given the success of T-VEC in melanoma models, an open phase I clinical trial was recently performed where patients with peritoneal dissemination from gastrointestinal or recurrent, platinum-resistant ovarian tumors received T-VEC every 2 weeks for up to four doses. The trial (NCT03663712) has been completed, but the results posted (not yet published) suggest that the incidence of serious adverse events might limit its potential.

Adenovirus

Adenoviruses are non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA viruses that replicate in the nucleus. Among > 50 serotypes that exist, 5 (Ad5) is one of the most commonly used for oncolytic therapies. Given their ability to infect a wide variety of cell types, adenoviruses for cancer therapy must be edited to ensure tropism specific for cancer cells, which has been achieved by deletion of the genes coding for E1A and E1B. E1B inhibits the tumor suppressor p53, aiding viral replication by preventing host cells from halting the cell cycle and undergoing apoptosis (Figure 4). Without E1B, adenoviruses prefer cancer cells with mutated TP53 genes because healthy cells with functional p53 pathways will undergo rapid apoptosis upon infection. E1A protein causes growth-arrested cells to enter S phase by binding to the retinoblastoma protein (Rb), resulting in the release of transcription factor eIF2. Therefore, removing E1A allows the virus to replicate selectively in cancer cells due to common defects in Rb pathways, while healthy cells remain unaffected. 

One of the most well-studied oncolytic adenoviruses is ONYX-015, a E1B-deleted adenovirus that allows viral replication only in cells with a dysfunctional p53. While the virus can infect and replicate in OC cells with TP53 mutations, limited benefit has been seen in clinical trials. This lack of beneficial effect may be due to the fact that adenoviruses preferentially enter cells using the coxsackievirus and adenovirus receptor (CAR), which is expressed at low levels in primary OC cells and inversely related to tumor grade. Therefore, there is a need to focus on approaches that would allow adenoviruses to infect in a CAR-independent manner.

One method to enable CAR-independent infection by adenoviruses is to create a serotype-chimeric 5/3 adenovirus. In this case, the serotype 5 adenovirus knob is replaced with a serotype-3 knob, which uses cell receptors that are more abundant on OC cells. Kim et al. used this approach to create a chimeric virus that contained a 24 bp deletion in the E1A gene (referred to as Ad5/3-Δ24). In this phase I clinical trial, 10 patients with recurrent EOC, primary peritoneal, fallopian tube, or endometrial cancers were given i.p. injections of the virus for three consecutive days. While some patients did experience symptoms attributable to the virus, such as fever, chills, nausea, fatigue and/or myalgia, no grade 3 or 4 clinical adverse events were reported. This study also reported that six patients had stable disease according to RECIST criteria and three had decreases in CA-125 levels; however, five patients had increased CA-125 levels.

Koski et al. took this approach a step further by adding GM-CSF to Ad5/3-Δ24. When patients with refractory advanced solid tumors were treated with the virus, 8/12 treated patients had disease control or better, including one patient with OC. Additionally, 2 patients had reduction in tumor size, one of which had OC, and no patient experienced grade 4 or 5 adverse effects. Interestingly, GM-CSF was restricted to local sites of viral replication whereas vaccinia viruses coding GM-CSF (such as JX-594) showed elevated levels of GM-CSF in the serum and the white blood cells. The authors hypothesized that GM-CSF was only found locally because the virus can only replicate in specific cells, which further indicated that adenoviruses could be a safe viral vector.

Serotype 5 adenovirus infection can be influenced by BRCA2 and RAD51, members of the homologous recombination pathway of double-stranded DNA repair, that play a role the nuclear localization of viral replication centers. While both were associated with increased cytotoxicity and DNA replication, RAD51 co-localization with viral replication centers was found to occur independently of BRCA2. The observation that BRCA2-deficient cells were less sensitive to cytotoxicity induced by the virus suggests that there may be differences in OC responsiveness based on BRCA status.

Vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV)

Vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) has a single-stranded RNA genome and is a member of the Rhabdoviridae family of viruses. Compared to other OVs, VSV has a smaller genome (11 kb) with only 5 genes that code for the nucleocapsid (N) protein, phosphoprotein (P), matrix (M) protein, glycoprotein (G) protein, and large polymerase (L). VSV replicates entirely within the cytoplasm and poses no risk of genome integration.

VSV is a commonly used virus for testing oncolytic therapies for a variety of reasons, including its strong sensitivity to IFN responses. The methionine at amino acid position 51 in the matrix protein (VSV-ΔM51) is commonly deleted since it is involved in shutting down host mRNA export by binding to nucleoporin Nup98 and nuclear export factor Rae1 (Figure 5). M protein also inhibits translation factor eIF4F to further prevent translation of host proteins and mediates overexpression of Bcl-2 as a mechanism to delay apoptosis until the virus has had sufficient time for replication. Deleting M51 allows host mRNA export and translation and makes the virus more cancer-targeting since VSV is very sensitive to IFN-I pathways, allowing healthy cells to clear viral infection while cancer cells with defects in IFN signaling fail to do so. In addition to improving tumor specificity, the ∆M51 mutation of VSV also enhances pathogenesis by inducing TNF-α. Another modification is VSV-GP, which has an edited glycoprotein to prevent neurotropism. 

As an additional example of how cancer genetics can influence viral infection, a recent study has shown that sensitivity to VSV∆M51 infection can be increased by the small GTPase Ran, which is overexpressed in HGSCs and associated with tumor progression. While a previous study had suggested that the M protein of VSV was able to inhibit Ran, it was found that Ran activation was not consistently modulated by VSV in EOCs. However, cell lines with higher Ran activity increased VSV replication, indicating that VSV could be a beneficial treatment for patients with higher Ran activity.

Our group has recently investigated how different mutations in OCs can affect the therapeutic efficacy of VSV∆51 viruses in preclinical models. After screening 10 different mouse EOC cell lines, in vitro and in vivo studies revealed that cells and syngeneic tumors with BRCA2 deletion or KrasG12V mutation are more responsive to VSV than other OC-related mutations, although modest improvements in survival were also achieved in mice with tumors carrying the Trp53R273H mutation. More research is needed to identify how specific cancer mutations increase the responsiveness to OV treatment.

Overall, given the limited efficacy of OVs in OC clinical trials, the field has shifted to testing combinational therapy approaches.

Combination therapies with OVs

Many different combinational approaches have aimed to improve clinical efficacy of OVs by testing different strains with chemotherapies, ICIs, or even other viruses. In this section, we explore some of the many combinational approaches tested for the treatment of OCs.

Virus with chemotherapy

One method of combining treatments includes pairing OVs with chemotherapeutic agents. A recent study used this approach to improve the efficacy of a Western Reserve vaccinia virus with deleted TK, F1 (a viral inhibitor of apoptosis) and vaccinia growth factor genes, replacing them with a Neon Green reporter and GM-CSF. By performing a drug screen using 9,000 compounds approved for clinical use by the FDA and/or the European Medicines Agency, they identified 20 compounds that could kill cancer cells without impairing viral replication or harming healthy cells. Since 7/20 compounds were tubulin polymerization inhibitors, they chose to test vinorelbine given that it had been previously shown to have some efficacy against OCs. The results from this preclinical study showed that mice with ID8-Trp-p53-/- ovarian tumors given the drug 24 hours before viral injections had prolonged survival (median survival 73.5 days) compared to mice given only drug (69 days) or virus alone (57 days).

CD44 is a marker often expressed on cancer-initiating cells and has been associated with chemoresistance in OCs. Preclinical studies have investigated the use of a vaccinia virus encoding a CD44 antagonist (CXCR4-A-Fc) to restore sensitivity to the chemotherapy doxorubicin (DOX). Using the paclitaxel and carboplatin-resistant cell lines, ID8-R and CAOV2-R, both cell lines were found to be more susceptible to the oncolytic effects of vaccinia virus but resistant to DOX. While cell lines treated with DOX 12 hours before viral treatment inhibited viral killing, DOX treatment 12 hours after viral treatment led to the highest cytopathic effects compared to monotherapies. In vivo experiments using the pegylated liposomal form of DOX (PLD) revealed that survival and tumor growth were improved the most when PLD was delivered 8 days after viral treatment. When a vaccinia encoding CXCR4-A was given prior to PLD, tumor-free survival reached 20% of the ID8-R tumor-bearing mice. The mechanism underlying these effects may be related viral activation of the cytoplasmic transcription factor CREB3L1 (cyclic AMP [cAMP] response element-binding protein 3-like 1), a transcription factor responsible for transcribing inhibitors of the cell cycle, such as p21.

The combination of p53MVA virus with gemcitabine chemotherapy has been tested in a phase I clinical trial. Gemcitabine was selected to help overcome immune suppression given that it is known to increase frequency of memory T cells, monocytes, and dendritic cells while decreasing frequency of regulatory T cells and MDSCs. Of the 11 OC patients given the combination therapy, three had stable disease, and one had a partial response with a decline in CA-125 levels over the course of 6 months. The median PFS was 0.95–9.2 months (3 months median). Additionally, 5/11 patients were found to have expansion of p53-reactive CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, 7/11 patients had decreased Tregs, and 6/11 had decreased MDSCs. The results for this study are promising in that they demonstrate how combining oncolytic vaccinia virus with chemotherapy can reduce the immunosuppressive nature of TMEs in OC and improve clinical outcomes.

Unfortunately, drugs that are standard of care for OC do not seem to improve VSV virotherapy. After identifying that 12/33 patient-derived cell lines were resistant to VSV infection, 6 resistant cell lines were treated with paclitaxel, carboplatin, doxorubicin, cyclophosphamide, gemcitabine, or olaparib to attempt to improve viral infection. Disappointingly, no improvements in VSV replication or cancer cell death were observed. However, antibodies against type I IFNs and their receptors successfully enhanced VSV replication in 8 OC cell lines, which varied in sensitivity to VSV. Therefore, FDA-approved drugs that inhibit IFN signaling via JAK inhibition were assessed in vitro. Ruxolitinib and baricitinib both improved viral infection and virus production while fedratinib only improved viral infection. Pre-treatment with ruxolitinib in ex vivo TOV3041G, OV3331, and OV3133 tumors enhanced VSV production. In in vivo experiments using OV866 xenograft models, mice given ruxolitinib 4 hours before intratumoral injections of VSV had better tumor control than mice given only VSV. Taken together, these findings indicate that for VSV viruses, combination with JAK inhibitors may be superior to combinations involving standard chemotherapies.

Combining viruses with other therapeutics

Our group has recently investigated the effects of combining an angiogenic drug with VSV in a preclinical OC study. Fc3TSR is an angiogenic fusion protein made of two peptides of the three type I repeat region (3TSR) of thrombospondin-1 combined with a non-immunostimulatory human IgG. By improving vascular normalization, Fc3TSR increases uptake of chemotherapy and its antitumoral effects are associated with reduced expression of immunosuppressive cytokines. To determine if this peptide could improve VSV∆M51 efficacy as well, mice injected with ID8 cells were treated with FcTSR prior to viral injection 50 days after tumor inoculation. Combining Fc3TSR with VSV∆M51 decreased tumor weights by 85% and increased the percentage of apoptotic cells compared to the use of either therapy alone, suggesting that this combination has considerable potential as an OC treatment.

There is growing interest in combining OVs with ICIs to increase therapeutic responses in OCs. In one study, Ad5/3-E2F-D24-hTNFα-IRES-hIL-2 (also called TILT-123) was used, which is an adenovirus encoding the cytokines TNFa and IL-2. Since TILT-123 enhanced survival in vivo in mice when combined with anti-PD-1 therapies, it was advanced to a phase 1a trial. Fifteen patients with EOC, fallopian tube or primary peritoneal carcinoma received an initial intravenous dose of virus on day 1, followed by a series of 5 i.p. or intratumoral doses in combination with pembrolizumab. The treatment regimen was found to be well-tolerated, with the majority of adverse events being grade 1 or 2, including fever, fatigue, and nausea. Only two grade ≥3 adverse events were reported, which were delirium and hemoperitoneum. Median survival was 190 days while PFS was 98 days. Overall disease control rate was found to be 64% (9/14). Given the tolerated safety profile, the treatment has advanced to a phase Ia/II trial and is now actively recruiting patients with OC (NCT05271318).

In preclinical models of colon and ovarian carcinomas, infection with vaccinia virus was found to lead to significantly higher expression of PD-L1 in tumor tissues compared to PBS-treated controls. Using the ID8 ovarian cancer model, mice given vaccinia virus or vaccinia with PD-L1 antibody had significantly smaller tumor burden compared to mice treated with PBS or antibody alone. While PD-L1 antibody and virus each resulted in similar reductions in tumor burden when used as monotherapies, the dual treatment led to a greater reduction in tumor burden and the best overall survival in both colon and OC models.

Another small molecule being investigated in combination with OVs is dimethyl fumarate (DMF), an FDA-approved drug for multiple sclerosis. Given its ability to inhibit IFN type I pathways by preventing the nuclear translocation of NF-κB, DMF was investigated for its ability to enhance the oncolytic potential of HSV-1 and VSV viruses in various cancer types, including OC tumor samples. Given toxicities associated with DMF, an analog called Tepilamide fumarate (TPF) was also investigated, which is thought to be safer and is currently in phase III trials for treatment of psoriasis. In OC patient explants, TPF was found to enhance VSV∆M51 replication by 10-fold, indicating a potential new avenue for combination treatment.

Combining VSV∆M51 or reovirus with natural killer T (NKT) cell-mediated immunotherapy was tested in ID8 i.p. and 4T1 breast cancer mouse models. While both combinations improved survival significantly compared to monotherapy controls, especially when virus was administered prior to NKT cell therapy, VSV∆M51 killed cells better than reovirus, especially in the 4T1 model. The authors hypothesized that this was due in part to reovirus’ susceptibility to RAS signaling, which is increased in 4T1 cells but not in ID8. VSV∆M51 infection also enhanced antigen presentation, leading to upregulation of MHC I, MHC II, CD1d, and Tap1 and Tap2 transcripts. VSV∆M51 combined with NKT therapy resulted in greater activation of both NK and T cells, which likely contributed to the success of the treatment.

Combining viruses with other viruses

One final approach to combination therapies is the use of multiple OVs. A well-known example is the integration of HSV-TK into an adenovirus for an approach called gene suicide therapy. In this approach, HSV delivers the TK gene to cancer cells where, once expressed, it converts the drug ganciclovir into its active form that kills virally-infected cells and surrounding neighboring cells via gap junctions (bystander effect). While retroviruses were originally used for delivery of HSV-TK to cancer cells, the approach was limited by transduction efficiency, its ability to target only dividing cells, and the risk of insertional mutagenesis. By integrating the HSV-TK into adenoviruses, high titers could be produced that could infect cells regardless of replication status and had high safety profiles. While pre-clinical studies showed promise for this therapy, clinical trials had disappointing results, with 14 OC patients in a phase I trial showing no toxicities or clinical benefits.

Retaining a focus on new ways to improve efficiency of HSV-TK delivery, others have modified the adenoviral fiber knob of type 5 adenovirus to generate an arginine-glycine-aspartate (RGD-4C) motif, a modification that allows the virus to enter cells via cell surface integrins instead of through CARs. A SSTR-expressing construct enabled imaging of viral infection. When used in a phase I clinical trial involving 12 patients with ovarian or endometrial cancers, no grade 3 or 4 adverse events were observed. A somewhat promising 5 patients had stable disease and 3 had decreased CA-125 levels, while the rest continued to experience disease progression. Since then, research groups have continued to make additional modifications to adenoviruses to improve the therapy’s effectiveness, while others have explored the use of other viral delivery methods, such as HPV pseudovirions in preclinical models.

A more recent and very promising approach of combining viral components for oncolytic therapy was performed by van den Pol et al. who combined components of the VSV and Lassa virus to engineer a chimeric virus. The goal of their study was to target OC and brain metastases – a rare but problematic complication in OCs due to the blood-brain barrier limiting therapeutic access. The hybrid was formed by swapping the glycoprotein in VSV with the glycoprotein encoded by Lassa, resulting in a virus that can reach the brain but does not contain any of the neurotropic or neurotoxic properties normally associated with VSV. This pseudotyped virus uses alpha-dystroglycan as its primary entry receptor, which is highly expressed on OC cells. When LASV-VSV was injected into immunocompromised nude mice bearing i.p. human mCherry-labeled OCSC1-F2 OC cells, there was no subsequent tumor growth and mice lived to 210 days with no detectable tumors. When the cancer cells were injected into the brains, LASV-VSV treated mice showed long-term survival with no mCherry ovarian tumors found in the brain postmortem, whereas controls treated with PBS or VSV-G died within 25 days. Further validation of this therapeutic approach was demonstrated in immune-competent mice, where mice that were repeatedly injected with OC cells infected with the virus before injection with uninfected cells failed to develop tumors, even when the injection of uninfected cells took place 3 months after priming with the infected cells. Needless-to-say, this is one of the most promising approaches using OVs for OC treatment.

Conclusion

To date, many OVs have been tested in OCs but they have had limited efficacy in clinical trials, in part due to the failure to take patient and viral genetics into account. As discussed, the genetics of both OC cells and OVs are closely interconnected, as the genetic makeup of tumor cells can influence viral replication, while the genetic design of the virus determines its selectivity, potency, and ability to evade host defenses. Several commonly found mutations in OCs make cells more susceptible to viral infection, including deletions in BRCA2, KrasG12V activating mutations, and increased Ran expression, indicating that patients with these mutations may be better candidates for receiving oncolytic therapies. Additionally, viral genetics must be considered to ensure that viruses are cancer-targeting while leaving healthy tissues unharmed. These mutations have included, but are not limited to, deletion of TK in vaccinia viruses, ∆M51 in VSV viruses, deletion of γ34.5 in HSV-1, and deletion of E1A and E1B in adenoviruses. While past studies focused on OVs as monotherapies have shown limited promising results, combinational therapies are the future for OCs. These strategies include many possibilities, such as combining viruses with chemotherapies, immunotherapies, or even together with other viruses. It is important to note, however, that each virus behaves differently. Additionally, more clinical trials are needed to investigate the effects of these approaches, especially with patients of various types of OC and at various stages of disease. Overall, OVs are an exciting new approach to OC treatment and hold promise for improving clinical outcomes for patients.

Article highlights

Adenoviruses, vaccinia viruses, herpes simplex 1, and vesicular stomatitis viruses are the most commonly used oncolytic viruses (OVs) for ovarian cancers.

Mutations in ovarian cancers make the cancer cells more susceptible to viral infection than healthy cells.

OVs are a promising therapeutic strategy for ovarian cancer, but clinical success has been limited primarily due to the use of monotherapies and the lack of genetic considerations.

Genetic mutations may influence patient response to OVs. Deletions in BRCA2, the KrasG12V mutation, and increased Ran expression all increase susceptibility to OVs, indicating patients with these mutations may be better candidates for OV therapies. Clinical trials should consider the role of cancer genetics in participant enrollment.

While viral monotherapies have been disappointing, combining OVs with other treatments can significantly enhance effects. Combinations include, but are not limited to, chemotherapies, immunotherapies, and other viruses.
