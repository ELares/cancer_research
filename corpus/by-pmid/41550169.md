# Recent progresses in combination cancer therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles.

## Abstract

Surgical resection, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy are traditional methods for cancer treatment. With the development of materials science, photodynamic, photothermal, and sonodynamic therapies have been established over the past few years. Despite these advances, the development of novel and efficient cancer treatment protocols remains highly desirable. Recently, combination therapy has emerged as a powerful tool for achieving this goal. In this context, cyanine-nanoparticles have attracted considerable interest. Cyanine dyes have high molar absorptivity, narrow absorption/emission bands, and also excellent biocompatibility. This has meant that they have been widely used in biomedical imaging and therapy. Cyanine nanoparticles assembled from cyanine dyes and amphiphilic polymers or liposomes are endowed with high biocompatibility and long-term circulation for cancer combination therapy. A plethora of cyanine-nanoparticle-based combination therapy systems have been reported, and research in this discipline continues to expand. In this review, we aim to summarize recent advances in the combination therapy of cancers using cyanine nanoparticles over the last five years (i.e., from 2018 to 2023), with an emphasis on the structures of cyanine dyes, design concepts, and combination strategies. Personal insights and challenges in this field are also discussed. We expect that this review will inspire creative progress in combination therapies based on cyanine nanoparticles and facilitate the investigation of future clinical applications.

Introduction

Cancer is a major public health problem and is the leading cause of death worldwide. The traditional cancer treatments include surgical resection, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy. However, these modalities are often associated with the risk of postoperative infection and complications. Photodynamic therapy (PDT) and photothermal therapy (PTT) have emerged as new cancer therapies with the advantages of reduced systemic toxicity, minimal invasiveness and reproducibility. PDT utilizes reactive oxygen species (ROS) to kill tumor cells. ROS are generated by the interaction of excited photosensitizers (PSs) with molecular oxygen or cell substrates (such as cell membranes or molecules). PTT uses photothermal reagents to convert absorbed light energy into heat, which increases the local temperature and results in tumor cell death. However, PDT suffers from tumor hypoxia and short excitation wavelengths. PTT also exhibits insufficient photothermal conversion efficiency (PCE) and high laser power density. These factors limit their applications in cancer treatment. Recently, combined therapies have been developed in order to improve the efficiencies of PDT and PTT. For instance, by combining PDT and PTT, the heat generated during PTT reshapes the tumor microenvironment, alleviates tumor hypoxia, and promotes ROS generation, which can significantly improve tumor treatment efficiency.

Cyanine dyes, linked by a π-conjugated polymethine chain, feature high molar absorptivity, narrow absorption/emission bands, good biocompatibility and tunable absorbance from UV–visible to near-infrared regions. In recent years, cyanine dyes have been widely used in biomedical imaging, PDT and PTT. However, cyanine dyes often suffer from solubility issues, and they tend to form aggregates under physiological conditions. Additionally, the short residence time at the tumor site and poor photostability of cyanine dyes largely limit their applications. Given the fact that nanoparticles (NPs) have a large surface area, good biocompatibility, and easy surface modification, they have been widely used in PDT and PTT to overcome the limitations of PS. As an important class of organic photosensitizes, a large number of cyanine dyes have self-assembled into nanoparticles to increase the efficiency of treatment and minimize the toxic side effects of PS on healthy tissues. Cyanine-NPs have broad applications in imaging (near-infrared fluorescent, photoacoustic, magnetic resonance imaging, etc.)-guided combination therapy, such as PDT/PTT, PDT/immunotherapy (IT), and PTT/chemodynamic therapy (CDT).

Recently, a plethora of combination therapy systems based on cyanine nanoparticles have been reported, and research in this discipline continues to expand in a productive fashion. Therefore, we have summarized recent advances in combination therapy for tumors based on cyanine nanoparticles (i.e., from 2018 to 2023), with an emphasis on the structures of cyanine dyes, design concepts, and combination strategies. A few personal insights and challenges in this field are discussed. We expect that this review will inspire creative progress in combination therapies based on cyanine nanoparticles and facilitate the investigation of future clinical applications.

Preparation of cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

In recent years, nanotechnology has been well developed, and nanomaterials have been widely used in catalysis, energy storage, chemical sensing, biological imaging, and cancer therapy. To improve biocompatibility and retention at tumor sites, the organic photosensitizer cyanine and its derivatives have been assembled with amphiphilic polymers (such as PEG and F127) or liposomes to form various nanoparticles for antitumor research. Typically, cyanine dyes contain two nitrogen-containing heterocycles linked by a π-conjugated polymethine chain. One of these heterocycles contains a positively charged nitrogen atom. These can be easily modified using other heterocycles, linker chains, and substituents on the nitrogen atom. The structures of these cyanines, preparation of the cyanine nanomaterials, and their biological applications appeared in this review are summarized in Table 1, Table 2 (see Appendix for details).

Cyanine nanoparticles self-assembled from polymers

Drug encapsulation efficiency has been a long-standing challenge in the use of polymeric nanoparticles in tumor therapy. Cyanine NPs self-assembled with polymers exhibit long circulation abilities in the body and accumulate near the tumor owing to the enhanced permeability and retention effect (EPR). Generally, two methods are used to prepare cyanine NPs from polymers: solvothermal methods and nanoprecipitation. Cyanine dyes can be encapsulated or covalently conjugated with polymers for combination therapy. As shown in Table 1, Rengan et al. prepared cyanine NPs by encapsulating the photosensitizer IR775 and polyphenols in the poly (lactic acid) polymeric nanosystem PpIR NPs. It was successfully introduced into the NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT treatment of a lung cancer cell line (A549). Dynamic light scattering experiments revealed that the average particle size of the PpIR NPs was 142.6 ± 2 nm. The temperature of the PpIR NPs steadily increased under 750 nm laser irradiation, indicating that the photothermal stability of the PpIR NPs was much higher than that of free IR775. Regarding in vitro experiments, PpIR NPs have been shown to not only kill A549 cells by the generated ROS and local high temperature, but they also induced cancer cell death by downregulating heat shock protein (HSP70) and poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) protein expression and upregulating γ-H2AX.

Cyanine nanoparticles self-assembled from liposomes

Liposomes are powerful nano-drug carriers in drug delivery, which feature high biocompatibility, low toxicity, facile functionalization and low immunogenicity. Liposome-containing NPs can accumulate near tumors through the EPR effect, and their body circulation time is prolonged. This can improve cancer treatment using PDT and PTT combination therapies. For instance, Ke et al. assembled Lipo-Cy nanoparticles by encapsulating cyanine dye cypate (Cy) in liposomes, which led to a red shift in the absorption wavelength of cypate and improved the quantum yield of 1O2 and the PCE of cypate. Both in vitro and in vivo experiments demonstrated the synergistic PDT/PTT effect of this nanodelivery system.

Cyanine nanoparticles self-assembled from proteins

Proteins exhibit good biocompatibility and high targetability and degradability, making them powerful and attractive tools for drug delivery. Indocyanine green (ICG), a cyanine dye with NIR absorption and a high PCE, has been used in PDT/PTT combined therapy using protein NPs. As shown in Table 1, Chen et al. in their study assembled ICG with gold nanocluster/pea protein isolates into AuNC/PPI-ICG nanoparticles for combined PDT/PTT therapy guided by NIRF and CT complementary imaging. Cao's group introduced I atoms into ICG to increase its interline-crossing ability and obtain CyI. Subsequently, the nanodelivery system HA-PEG-CYI (HPC) was obtained by covalently binding hyaluronic acid (HA) and PDT/PTT combined therapy guided by NIRF. PA imaging was used to activate the immune response for cancer therapy.

Cyanine nanoparticles self-assembled without carriers

In addition, cyanines can self-assemble into nanoparticles without carriers. For instance, Yang et al. started with classical IR780 as the main framework, substituted 4-chlorotetrahydropyridine as the cyclohexene ring to obtain aza-IR780, and used the NH site to prepare Cy-CO2Bz and Cy-CO2Et. The cationic nature of heptamethine cyanines allows them to form J-aggregates in the presence of NaCl through electrostatic interactions. The J-aggregates generated from Cy-CO2Bz exhibited a good fluorescence response to NaCl. NaCl can also promote the formation of J-aggregates of Cy-CO2Et, and the resulting J-aggregates can then be used as photothermal agents.

PDT/PTT combinational therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

Recently, combination therapy has gained a great amount of research interest for addressing the issues of single PDT or PTT. Several combination strategies have been developed, such as PDT/PTT, PDT/IT, and PTT/CDT. These combination therapies have been shown to improve the efficiency of cancer treatment, and remarkable synergistic effects have been observed. In this section, we discuss the recent progress in PDT/PTT combination therapy based on cyanine NPs.

NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT combinational therapy

Near-infrared fluorescence (NIRF) imaging has been widely used in the fields of bioimaging and cancer therapy owing to its deeper penetration depth, higher imaging resolution, and lower tissue damage. NIRF image-guided tumor treatment can locate tumors more precisely and improve treatment efficiency. The Zhang group developed a multistep consolidated phototherapy nanosystem (PpaCy7-PEG-biotin, PCB) by covalently linking the photosensitizer pyropheophorbide A (Ppa) and near-infrared (NIR) cyanine dyes to biotin. Upon 808 nm laser irradiation, cleavage of the photothermal agent Cy7 activated the photodynamic properties of Ppa and achieved NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT combination therapy for HepG2 mouse tumor models.

Guo, He, Liu, and co-workers prepared NIR nonlinear organic chromophore nanoparticles (NOC-NPs) using a julolidine moiety as the donor and a tricyanofuran moiety as the acceptor, and self-assembled with PEG114-b-PCL60 (Fig. 1A). NOC-NPs exhibit high singlet oxygen (1O2) yield (φΔ = 0.29) (Fig. 1B) and PCE (η = 36.2%) (Fig. 1C), which is attributed to the intrinsic intramolecular charge transfer (ICT) of NOC. NOC-NPs entered 4T1 cells through a clathrin-mediated endocytosis (Fig. 1D), preferentially localized to lysosomes (P = 85.6%), and effectively induced cell damage with low cytotoxicity and high cytophototoxicity (IC50 = 27.3 μg·mL−1) (Fig. 1E). After tail vein injection of NOC-NPs into 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, DHE staining and infrared thermal imaging showed that 1O2 was generated at the tumor site, and the local temperature increased after light irradiation (Fig. 1F). In the NOC-NP group, the tumors were completely suppressed within 30 d of treatment (Fig. 1G and H). This study demonstrated that nonlinear organic chromophore nanoparticles have excellent photophysical properties and great potential for NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT synergistic therapy for tumors.

Persistent light-emitting nanoparticles (PLNPs) are a new type of nanoparticle that can mitigate phototoxicity and thermal damage. They can store excitation energy and continue to emit light even after excitation is stopped. Yan et al. prepared monodisperse NIR PLNPs with an average particle size of 40 nm based on brominated asymmetric phalloidin and wrapped biotin (BT) - modified polyethylene glycol (PEGBT) around the PLNPs for NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT.

Wang et al. synthesized an asymmetrical heptamethine cyanine in which a sulfonic acid or carboxyl acid group was introduced into the indole ring or branch chain to obtain a series of new photosensitizers (Fig. 2A). Owing to the introduction of the acid group, the photodynamic performance of the photosensitizer BSS-ET was enhanced, and its photothermal characteristics were maintained. BSS-ET NPs were generated via self-assembly with DSPE-PEGm2000. Photophysical property detection showed that the BSS-ET NPs had strong ROS generation ability (49%) (Fig. 2B) and high PCE (37.6%) (Fig. 2D). Meanwhile, as shown in Fig. 2C, the calculation results of time-dependent density functional theory (TD-DFT) also showed that BSS-ET bears the largest intersystem crossover (ISC) rate, which indicates that the introduction of carboxyl group could reduce ΔEST and promote ROS production. BSS-ET NPs achieved NIR fluorescence image-guided PDT/PTT combination therapy by passively targeting 4T1 mouse tumors via the EPR effect (Fig. 2E–I).

Zhang et al. constructed a photoactivated nanosystem, UTL@IR780, by combining a thermally responsive porphyrin block copolymer (P(AAm-co-AN-co-TPP)-b-POEGMA) with the cyanine dye IR780. The fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) of the nanosystem quenched the photodynamic properties of tetraphenylporphyrin (TPP), and the copolymer chain upper critical solution temperature (UCST) increased locally to activate the photoactivity of TPP upon 808 nm laser irradiation. Therefore, thermally activated NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT can be actualized. This UCST-based nanoparticle system represents a new protocol for photodynamic therapy.

Other image-guided PDT/PTT combinational therapy

Indocyanine green (ICG) has been approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for clinical imaging. ICG-coated nanoparticles have been successfully used in fluorescence imaging-guided tumor therapy owing to their excellent PDT/PTT properties. In contrast, the Li group embedded chlorin e6 (Ce6) and IR780 in liposomes simultaneously and then chemically combined them with triphenylphosphine (TPP) and PEG chains in order to build the nano-delivery system TPP/IR780/Ce6TNS (TICT). The photodynamic properties of Ce6 burst in the absence of light due to FRET. Under light irradiation, the fluorescence and ROS production of Ce6 were significantly enhanced by the photobleaching of IR780. IR780 localizes to the tumor site, and cancer cells are destroyed by the PDT/PTT combination therapy through mitochondrial targeting.

PDT/PTT/CT combination therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

Currently, most patients with cancer cannot achieve the expected effect through surgical resection, and chemotherapy (CT) is usually used as a supplementary treatment. Nevertheless, CT indiscriminately damages normal and tumor cells, causing several adverse reactions and side effects. To solve these issues, the Wu group covalently coupled the cyanine dyes IR825-NH2 and cholesterol poly(ethylene glycol)2k-NH2 with HA to obtain HA-IR825-CHOl NPs with an average particle size of 43.5 nm. HA-IR825-Chol/HCPT NPs were produced by self-assembly with the chemotherapy drug 10-hydroxycamptothecin (HCPT), which can be used for combined PTT/CT treatment of 4T1 tumor mice. Later, Qian, Sun, Zhu and co-workers introduced naphthalimide into the cyanine skeleton to produce ML880, co-packaged with glutathione-S-transferase inhibitor NBDHEX in ROS-responsive mesoporous organosilica (RMON), yielding the nanodrug NBD&ML@RMON with an average particle size of 91 ± 5.6 nm (Fig. 3A and B). The introduction of naphthylimide has redshifted the absorption and emission wavelengths of ML880 to 880 and 912 nm, respectively (Fig. 3C). NBD&ML@RMON exhibited good photothermal stability and a PCE of 65.4% (Fig. 3D). The DCFH-DA experiment shows that it has excellent photodynamic characteristics (Fig. 3E). CCK8 assay showed that NBD&ML@RMON exhibited low dark cytotoxicity and high cytophototoxicity (Fig. 3F and G). In vivo experiments showed that the nanosystem was able to significantly inhibit HepG2 cells through PDT/PTT/CT combination therapy (Fig. 3H and I).

Chen et al. started with the cyanine dye IR780 as a photosensitizer, followed by the self-assembly of PEG-Cy-F18 with tamoxifen and 1,11-diperfluoro-tert-butoxyundecane (F-oil) to form SoFoTm/PEG-Cy-F18 (Fig. 4A). Compared with IR780, the absorption and emission wavelengths of PEG-Cy-F18 and PEG-Cy-F9 were blue-shifted, and the Stokes shifts reached 71 and 68 nm, respectively (Fig. 4B). The TEM images show that the materials are "fingerprint-like" aggregated (Fig. 4C), which suggest the material self-assembles in a "face-to-face" manner and undergoes H-aggregation. When S-oil, F-oil, and TAM self-assemble, H-aggregation is destroyed, and fluorescence and sensitive magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) can be performed. SoFoTm/PEG-Cy-F18 exhibited good photodynamic properties, a high PCE (19.4%), and high TAM release (>93%) (Fig. 4D-F). Xenotransplantation of MCF-7 mice showed favorable therapeutic effects through the combination of CT/PDT/PTT guided by NIR fluorescence and MRI dual-mode imaging (Fig. 4G–J).

Zhang, Liu, and co-workers assembled Gd3+ with carboxyl cyanine and the carboxyl ligand NH2-BDC to form a nano-coordinated polymer (Cy-NPC); then, the anticancer drug curcumin (CCM) was coated on the surface of the NPC. Subsequently, folic acid (FA) was coated on its surface in order to obtain CCM@Cy-NCP/FA nanoparticles, which achieved PTT/CT combined therapy with MRI, NIRF, and PA imaging in KB tumor mice, showing remarkable anti-tumor efficiency.

Supramolecular coordination complexes (SCCs) are widely used for molecular recognition and sensing. Because platinum is an excellent chemotherapeutic drug, platinum (II) metal complexes have provided new opportunities for the construction of antitumor nanomaterials. As shown in Fig. 5A, Zhang et al. used heptamethyl cyanine IR780 and platinum in order to obtain organoplatinum(II) metallacycle SCY, followed by self-assembly with DSPE-mPEG to generate P@SCY nanoparticles. 1H and 31P NMR spectra showed that Pt (II) in P@SCY was released in the presence of Cl– or Br– (Fig. 5B and C). P@SCY exhibited strong absorption at 780 nm (Fig. 5D). ROS can be effectively produced (Fig. 5E), and the temperature of the solution can be increased by irradiation with an 808 nm laser (Fig. 5F). CCK8 results showed that the material had lower dark toxicity and higher phototoxicity than P@CY (Fig. 5G), which was attributed to the synergistic effect of CT and mitochondrial-enhanced phototherapy. The in vivo antitumor experiments on 4T1 mice further illustrated the successful construction of the P@SCY nano-phototherapeutic platform for CT/PDT/PTT trimodal combination therapy (Fig. 5H and I).

PDT/PTT/IT combination therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

Cancer therapies, such as PDT and CT, usually induce apoptosis accompanied by immunogenic cell death (ICD), which promotes the release of tumor-associated antigens and increases the activation, proliferation, and infiltration of antigen-specific T cells. In recent years, numerous International Classification of Diseases (ICD)-based tumor combination therapies have been developed. Cao et al. encapsulated the cyanine dye CyI with hypoxia-activated tilapamide (TPZ) in liposomes using a thin-film hydration method to fabricate a new type of liposome LCT. Under 808 nm laser irradiation, the release rates of CyI and TPZ in the liposomal LCT were found to be 79.97% and 61.36%, respectively. The continuous consumption of O2 during PDT leads to hypoxia in tumor cells, activates TPZ for chemotherapy, and simultaneously triggers a series of immune responses. NIRF image-guided PDT/PTT/CT/IT combination therapy was performed in 4T1 tumor-bearing mice. Later, Wu et al. developed a nanodrug system using dc-IR825 as a photosensitizer, which had both good photodynamic and photothermal properties. In their system, a poly(amidoamine) (PAMAM) dendrimer was first used to encapsulate dc-IR825 through hydrophobic interactions, and then negatively charged HA was encapsulated by electrostatic interactions to form the dendrimer IR825@HA (DIH). DIH accumulates near the tumor through the EPR effect and is then targeted to CD44 receptor-overexpressing tumor cells by HA. Under 808 nm laser irradiation, the generated ROS and slight heat can kill cancer cells via mitochondrial stress, followed by immunotherapy to actualize PDT/PTT/IT combination therapy in 4T1 tumor mice.

Lu, Zhang, Huang, and co-workers synthesized NPs IR-780@MPDA for the combined PDT/PTT/IT treatment of 4T1 tumor mice. They used an NIR cyanine dye as the PDT/PTT photosensitizer and mesoporous polydopamine nanoparticles (MPDA) for PA imaging and PTT. In addition, Yu et al. synthesized heptamethyl cyanine photosensitizers C3–C11 with alkyl chains of different lengths and self-assembled them with albumin (Fig. 6A). According to TEM images, only C8 and albumin self-assembled into uniform nanoparticles with an average particle size of 51.9 nm (Fig. 6B). Under the excitation of near infrared light, the nanoparticles can be imaged in NIR light (Fig. 6C), and shows satisfying ROS generation ability (φ = 0.38) (Fig. 6D) and high PCE (52.8%) (Fig. 6E). In vitro experiments showed that C8 could perform PDT/PTT combined with phototherapy in 4T1 cells. C8 phototherapy triggered a strong immune response based on the detection of calreticulin (CRT) and high-mobilitygroupbox-1 (HMGB1) (Fig. 6F). In vivo experiments also indicated that C8 could bind to albumin and thus form nanoparticles in 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, enabling NIR image-guided PDT/PTT/IT combination therapy and the inhibition of primary, distant, and metastatic tumors (Fig. 6G).

PTT/CDT combination therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

Chemodynamic therapy (CDT) is an emerging cancer treatment method that converts the overexpressed H2O2 in tumors into •OH through Fenton or Fenton-like reactions. However, owing to the overexpression of reducing substances (GSH, Cys, etc.) in tumors, the therapeutic efficiency of CDT has been severely limited. To address this issue, several CDT-based tumor combination therapies have been developed in recent years. For instance, as shown in Fig. 7A, Fan et al. In their study selected heptamethyl cyanine dye as an organic ligand, which coordinates with Cu2+ to form nanoparticles. To target the tumor cells, they were wrapped with HA to form HA@Cy-Cu NPs. HA targets the CD44 receptor on the surface of tumor cells. The average particle size of the HA@Cy-Cu NPs was 80 nm (Fig. 7B), which can target tumors through the EPR effect. Under 808 nm laser irradiation, the PCE of the HA@Cy-Cu NPs was 42.3% (Fig. 7C). Cu2+ is reduced to Cu + by GSH, and Cu + reacts with the overexpressed H2O2 in the tumor to produce •OH (Fig. 7D), resulting in tumor cell death through CDT. The in vitro MTT experiments showed that the effect of combined PTT and CDT treatment on tumors was superior to that of a single treatment (Fig. 7F). HA@Cy-Cu NPs also achieved good tumor treatment efficiency in 4T1 tumor-bearing mice (Fig. 7G–I).

PDT/PTT/SDT combination therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is a new type of tumor therapy based on ultrasound (US). SDT has a greater depth of tissue penetration and good tumor targeting. SDT combined with PDT, PTT, and other tumor therapies can significantly improve the efficiency of tumor treatment. For example, Qian et al. selected the cyanine dye IR806 with near-infrared absorption as a photosensitizer to couple with chondroitin sulfate (CS) through disulfide bonds and obtained CSR NPs through self-assembly. The particle size of the CSR NPs could be controlled by adjusting the feed ratio of IR806 to CS. When the feed ratio was 0.39, the drug loading and encapsulation efficiencies of IR806 were calculated to be 17.6% and 39.9%, respectively (Fig. 8B). The CSR NPs had a high PCE (Fig. 8C), and the generation of •OH and 1O2 could be detected by CSR NPs under 808 nm laser irradiation (Fig. 8D and E). In vitro experiments showed that the therapeutic effect of combined US and laser irradiation was much greater than that seen in regard to single treatment (Fig. 8F). In vivo experiments showed that CSR NPs could achieve combined PDT/PTT/SDT therapy for prostate cancer (PC-3) (Fig. 8G–I).

Others

Cao et al. synthesized an amphiphilic block copolymer from the cyanine dye Cy7 and Poly (oligo (ethylene glycol) methyl ether methacrylate) (POEG-MA) and then self-assembled it with N-nitrosated naphthalimide (NORM) to obtain nanoparticles PCy7-NO. Among them, Cy7 not only acts as a photosensitizer to generate ROS (including O2•-) but also serves as a photothermophore that causes NORM and releases NO gas. O2•- reacts with NO to deliver ONOO-, achieving enhanced NIRF image-guided ONOO- combined with PDT/PTT for 4T1 tumor-bearing mouse treatment.

Zhang et al. synthesized IR780 liposomes by using a membrane hydration method for PA and NIR-II fluorescence image-guided surgical resection/PTT (Fig. 9A). The average particle size of IR780 liposome is about 107 ± 18 nm (Fig. 9B), with maximum absorbance at 800 nm and maximum emission peak at 822 nm (Fig. 9C). Under the excitation of the 808 nm laser, IR780 showed a broad emission peak (1000–1200 nm) in ethanol and liposomes (Fig. 9D), which reached the NIR-II region, and this meant that the photothermal stability of IR780 liposomes was excellent. In vitro CCK8 experiments showed that IR780 liposomes exhibited good cytotoxic effects on tumor cells (Fig. 9E). Additionally, the IR780 liposomes can be used for NIR-II fluorescence imaging of mouse blood vessels, guiding surgical procedures, and assisting in PTT in combination with the PA dual imaging mode (Fig. 9F–H).

Zhang et al. prepared drug carrier liposomes using the thin-film hydration method. Glucose oxidase (GOx) and NIR cyanine dye DiR were loaded into liposomes to synthesize GOx@Lipo and DiR@Lipo, respectively. These liposomes were used for combined PDT/PTT and as an adjunct treatment for starvation therapy.

Conclusions and perspectives

In light of their NIR absorption (650–900 nm), higher fluorescence quantum yield, and excellent photothermal conversion performance, cyanine dyes are widely used in biomedical imaging and therapy. To further extend their applications, scientists have fabricated nanoparticles by encapsulating cyanine dyes with amphiphilic polymers or liposomes, endowing them with high biocompatibility and long-term circulation for efficient combined cancer therapy. In this review, we have summarized the recent advances in tumor combination therapy using cyanine dye-based nanoparticles. Although many studies have shown that cyanine nanoparticles can be used in tumor combination therapy, there are still some challenges in this field. For instance, most cyanines are not very stable under photothermal conditions, which limit their application in photothermal treatments. The reasonable design of novel cyanines with excellent photothermal stabilities is a promising direction to address this issue. However, the penetration depth of the cyanine-based materials hinders their clinical application. The synthesis of cyanines with longer absorption wavelengths in the near infrared region is highly desirable. Additionally, most tumor models used in research are subcutaneous tumors, which are easy to operate, but cannot truly simulate the tumor microenvironment. To accurately reflect the real situation in human tumors, it is necessary to develop more orthotopic mouse and large animal tumor models.
