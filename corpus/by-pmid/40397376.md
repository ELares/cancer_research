# Embracing cancer immunotherapy with manganese particles.

## Abstract

A substance integral to the sustenance and functionality of virtually all forms of life is manganese (Mn), classified as an essential trace metal. Its significance lies in its pivotal role in facilitating metabolic processes crucial for survival. Additionally, Mn exerts influence over various biological functions including bone formation and maintenance, as well as regulation within systems governing immunity, nervous signaling, and digestion. Manganese nanoparticles (Mn-NP) stand out as a beacon of promise within the realm of immunotherapy, their focus honed on intricate mechanisms such as triggering immune pathways, igniting inflammasomes, inducing immunogenic cell death (ICD), and sculpting the nuances of the tumor microenvironment. These minuscule marvels have dazzled researchers with their potential in reshaping the landscape of cancer immunotherapy - serving as potent vaccine enhancers, efficient drug couriers, and formidable allies when paired with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) or cutting-edge photodynamic/photothermal therapies. Herein, we aim to provide a comprehensive review of recent advances in the application of Mn and Mn-NP in the immunotherapy of cancer. We hope that this review will display an insightful view of Mn-NPs and provide guidance for design and application of them in immune-based cancer therapies.

Introduction

Cancer is one of the leading causes of death worldwide. According to global cancer estimates, 19.3 million new cases and approximately 10.0 million cases of cancer death were estimated in 2020. Regardless of promising progress in the treatment of cancer, such as radiotherapy, chemotherapy, and surgical approaches, several challenges persist owing to serious side effects and limited efficacy. In recent years, immunotherapy of cancer has gained considerable attention as a therapeutic strategy in several types of cancers, especially solid tumors. In cancer adoptive cell therapy (ACT), immune cells are harnessed to be trained in vitro and specifically target malignant cells when returned to the patient’s body. Other aspects of cancer immunotherapy, including immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), oncolytic adenoviruses, monoclonal antibodies (mAbs), cytokine therapy, and nanoparticle-based cancer vaccine have been validated as an alternative treatment in tumors that tend to relapse or are resistant to standard therapeutic plans. However, the widespread application of immunotherapies is hampered due to the high toxicity profile when administrated systematically, and limited therapeutic responsiveness. For instance, following ICI therapy most patients experience immune-related adverse effects with only 20–30% of patients reaching clinical response. One impediment in achieving sufficient efficacy is related to immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). TME not only prevents pro-inflammatory responses and T cell proliferation but also causes drug resistance and accelerated tumor metastasis. Therefore, it is imperative to design alternative approaches to overcome TME and boost the loading of drugs and immune cells in the tumor site.

The convergence of nanotechnology and immunotherapy has made striking progress in cancer therapy. Using nanoparticles (NPs) enables us to deliver drugs to intended tissue while avoiding systematic toxic reactions. Besides, NPs using metal ions such as Fe, Cu, and Mn have been shown to elicit immunogenic cell death (ICD) in several tumor types, such as breast, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), and colorectal cancer. Manganese (Mn) is a nutritional trace element that plays a vital role in several physiological processes including development, neuronal activity, antioxidant systems, and endocrine function. Mn plays an important role in the activity of several enzymes such as arginase, superoxide dismutase, pyruvate carboxylase, and glutamine synthetase (GS). However, its role in immunity remains mostly unknown. Very recent studies have indicated that Mn can interact with the (cGAS)/stimulator of interferon genes (STING) pathway and enhance tumor clearance by promoting dendritic cell (DC) maturation and activation of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs). Additionally, it has been demonstrated that manganese dioxide nanoparticle (MnO2-NP) can alleviate TME immunosuppressive environment. Based on that, Mn-NPs have been utilized in combination with several cancer immunotherapies such as ICI, oncolytic viruses, and nano-vaccines. Herein, we aim to provide a comprehensive review of recent advances in the application of Mn and Mn-NP in the immunotherapy of cancer. Furthermore, we will discuss the challenges ahead and strategies to address them.

Biological characteristics of manganese

Mn is a nutritional trace element that is found in many dietary intakes. It is taken up by the small intestine by an active transport mechanism and, potentially, via diffusion under conditions of high intake. Following absorption, a portion of Mn stays unbound, while the majority becomes associated with plasma alpha-2-macroglobulin, albumin, and transferrin. Mn is absorbed by the liver and other tissues, although the specific method is unknown. The average concentration of Mn in mammalian cells is about 0.3–2.9 ppm, making it one of the essential elements found in the body. The vast majority of circulating Mn is absorbed by hepatocytes and stored in mitochondria. After being converted to bile, excess Mn is carried to the intestine and excreted in feces. Mn plays a crucial functional role in several biological enzymes such as superoxide dismutase, pyruvate carboxylase, and arginase. Through acting on these enzymes, Mn participates in the metabolism of glucose, amino acids, and cholesterol, as well as converting reactive oxygen species (ROS), formation of bones, reproduction function, and immunological responses.

The majority of intracellular Mn2+ is stored in the Golgi apparatus and mitochondria in the forms of Mn phosphate or Mn carbonate during the resting state. However, the exact subcellular distribution of Mn is still unknown. Most of the known transporters that are involved in transporting Mn2+ through cell membranes are not exclusive, meaning that they transport other metal ions simultaneously. To date, several transporters have been discovered to be involved in Mn cellular uptake, including divalent metal ion transporter-1 (DMT1), zinc transporter ZIP8/14, store-operated calcium channels, and several calcium channels including TRPM3/7. Meanwhile, zinc transporter ZnT10, ferroprotein iron transporter, and sodium-calcium exchanger are involved in the regulation of Mn cellular efflux.

Because of their diverse valence states (Mn2+, Mn3+, Mn4+, and Mn5+), transition metal Mn ions are gaining popularity in a variety of optoelectronic study domains. The distinct energy-level distributions of those Mn ions result in different transition behaviors, and the combination of those Mn ions would enrich the adoptable application modes and improve the relevant practical performance in the fields of lighting, thermochromism, pigments, and temperature sensing. Mn2+ typically exhibit high energy transfer efficiency from other activators in co-doping systems, indicating prospective uses in display and optical storage. Mn4+-based materials are extensively explored in the lighting area because to their significant red light emission, high quantum efficiencies (particularly for Mn4+-doped fluorides), and compatibility with commercial blue LED chips. Furthermore, the substantial temperature sensitivity of the Mn4+ photoluminescence (PL) makes high-performance optical thermometry possible.

In contrast, optical materials containing Mn3+ and Mn5+ are underutilized. Because of the Jahn-Teller effect and high electron-phonon coupling, octahedral coordinated Mn3+ typically emit throughout a wide range of wavelengths, from visible to near-infrared. Tetrahedrally coordinated Mn5+ often emit a narrow band in the near infrared range. In recent years, luminous thermometers based on Mn3+’s temperature-dependent PLchange have been presented, with sensing capabilities equivalent to those based on Mn4+.

Manganese oxide based nanoparticles

The landscape of cancer treatment has dramatically changed since the emergence of nanoparticles-based therapies. Several ion-based nanoparticles have been developed so far, which serve as biomedical carriers and smart composites to drive specific responses in tumor sites. New trends, focus on developing nanosized catalysts named nanozymes. Compared to the synthesis of traditional enzymes, nanozymes tend to be cost-effective and provide a better recovery process and purification. Among them, MnxOy-NPs particularly MnO2, have gained significant attraction in the treatment of cancer. There are several different oxidation states for Mn (ranging from Mn2+ to Mn7+) which provides a rich catalytic activity. As will be discussed later, this catalytic chemistry can promote the depletion of abundant tumor-resident species such as glutathione (GSH) and H2O2 in the typical pH range observed in TME (5.5–7.4). These reactions can end up in the generation of freestanding Mn2+ that can also mediate intracellular downstream pathways for immune-related responses.

Over the last two decades, MnO2-NPs have gained popularity for biomedical applications. He et al. created honeycomb MnO2-NPs for GSH-triggered drug release and photodynamic/gene-silencing treatment, respectively. MnOx nanospikes were also initially proposed as TME-responsive nanoadjuvants and cell death medicines for cancer nanovaccine immunotherapy using MR/PA (magnetic resonance, photoacoustic) imaging. The advantages of the produced nanovaccines based on MnOx nanosheets (NSs) are as follow: (a) biodegradable MnOx-NP vaccine exhibit TME-activated antigen delivery and immunosuppressive TME modulation; (b) MnOx NSs with large mesopore structures exhibit high antigen loading capacities; (c) MnOx-NSs display TME-responsive MR/PA dual-mode imaging capabilities. It was also initially demonstrated that MnOx immunoadjuvants may intrinsically trigger ICD by ferroptosis inductions and chemodynamic treatment (CDT), which observably improved therapeutic effects on primary and distal/metastatic malignancies. Due to their low cost, high and diverse catalytic activity, and extensive synthesis possibilities, MnxOy-NPs have become intriguing options for the therapy of cancer. Mn has a rich redox catalytic chemistry and a broad range of oxidation states (from Mn2+ to Mn7+), which can be particularly appropriate for tumor cell settings. For example, the interaction with GSH, which is excessively loaded in TMEs, is facilitated by the high oxidation potential of Mn4+. In order to optimize the effects of other antitumoral drugs and catalyze GSH depletion, Mn chemistry may be appropriate. MnO2-NSs have been used as starving-catalysts for cancer treatment because of their capacity to catalyze the oxidation of glucose into gluconic acid and H2O2. Multiple MnxOy-NPs ultimately convert into Mn²⁺ following their interaction with GSH.

Role of manganese in enhancing cancer immune responses

The application of Mn and Mn-NPs in regulating immune responses has long been investigated in in-vivo studies, although the exact underlying mechanism has not been well-defined. About 30 years ago, injection of MnCl2 in mice models enhanced natural killer (NK) cell activity which was proposed to be mediated through increased production of interferon alpha and beta. Besides, it is demonstrated that the Mn compounds can effectively reshape TME to favor macrophage polarization into inflammatory M1 phenotype. In this section, we discuss current evidence on the pathways in which Mn can affect immune responses against cancer.

cGAS/STING pathway

Recent studies have demonstrated that antitumor effects of various cancer treatments are mediated by activation of cyclic GMP-AMP synthase cGAS/STING pathway. In summary, chemotherapy agents or radiotherapy cause the release of damaged DNA in the cytosol. Thereafter, damaged cytosolic DNA is recognized by cGAS which activates the STING adaptor protein and results in type I interferon (INF) production. INF type-I promotes antigen presentation on tumor-infiltrating dendritic cells (DCs) and subsequently stimulates CD8+ cells for adaptive responses. Interestingly, it has been found that Mn plays a critical role in the activation of the cGAS/STING pathway through either enhancing the generation of cGAMP or augmenting the binding affinity of STING/cGAMP. This is further evidenced by the observation that mice with Mn insufficiency experienced exacerbated tumor growth and reduced CD8+ T cell profile in the tumor site. Subsequently, intranasal or intravenous injection of Mn in mice significantly increased the maturation of macrophages and DCs and promoted the activation of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells and NK cells. Moreover, Mn2+ directly increases NK cell cytotoxicity and encourages NK cell cytokine release, which activates CD8+ T cells and increases their antitumor activity. Additionally, Mn2+ can activate STING and NK-cell intrinsic cGAS at the same time, which will increase the expression of the ubiquitously transcribed tetratricopeptide repeat on chromosome X (UTX) and increase NK cell responsiveness.

These findings suggest that combinational therapy using interventions causing DNA damage such as chemotherapy agents or radiotherapy with Mn can potentially provoke robust anti-tumor responses in a synergetic manner. In a recent study, Hou et al. developed a nano-activator that increases the accumulation of Mn in the cytosol and simultaneously induces the presence of damaged DNA in the cytoplasm. In this regard, they loaded doxorubicin (DOX) and amorphous porous Mn phosphate (APMP) in a phospholipid (PL)-coated hybrid nanoparticle. After treatment with this newly formed nanoparticle, they demonstrated intensified production of pro-inflammatory cytokines and increased infiltration of NK cells and CTLs in the tumor site.

Moreover, recent works show that combinational therapy using Mn-NPs can boost the effectiveness of other immunotherapies. In this regard, Aikins et al. designed a lipid-coated nanoparticle platform composed of STING agonist c-di-AMP (CDA) and Mn. They demonstrated that cancer treatment combined therapy with this nano-activator carrying STING agonist and Mn2+ combined with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) against cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated Antigen 4 (anti-CTLA-4) initiated strong innate and adaptive immune response against acute malignant cells in a mouse model of acute myeloid leukemia (AML). Additionally, another study revealed the synergistic anti-tumor effect of combinational therapy of intratumor injection of Mn plus anti-TGF-β/PD-L1 bispecific antibody.

Mn-NPs have also been used in combination with cancer nano-vaccines. Nanoparticle-based cancer vaccine hold a unique potential in treatment plans regarding their antigen-specific immune responses. However, their efficacy is hampered due to inadequate antigen delivery to the tumor site. In this regard, Gao et al. designed a Mn-coordinated nano-vaccine to introduce antigen ovalbumin (OVA) to the mice bearing B16-OVA tumors. They demonstrated that this nano-vaccine not only facilitated antigen loading and its endosomal escape but simultaneously activated the interferon-related STING pathway. This approach greatly inhibited tumor growth and provided a prophylactic effect against cancer development. (Fig. 1)

In a study purified cGAS protein was used in an in vitro cGAS activity experiment to examine cGAS activation by Mn²+, and anion-exchange chromatography was used to measure the generation of cGAMP. As anticipated, 2′3′-cGAMP synthesis resulted when incubating human cGAS with ATP, GTP, Mg²+, and dsDNA. Interestingly, the strongest cGAS activator in the presence of DNA was Mn²+. Unexpectedly, Mn²+ by itself may also strongly increase the formation of cGAMP without DNA. Digitonin-permeabilized cGAS−/− HeLa cells were also used to further confirm Mn²+ -induced cGAMP production. Mn²+ treatment caused TBK1 and IRF3 phosphorylation in THP-1 cells, which in turn caused type I interferons and interferon-stimulated genes (ISGs) to be induced. Using thin-layer chromatography, a recombinant shrimp-alkaline-phosphatase digestion assay, and mass spectrometry, the specificity of Mn²+ -induced cGAMP was verified. Nuclease P1 enzymatic digestion confirmed that the generated product was 2′3′-cGAMP. All of these results show that Mn²+ directly activates human cGAS to promote the synthesis of 2′3′-cGAMP, both in vitro and in cellulo model. According to reports, cytosolic Mn²+ aids in cGAS’s dsDNA-sensing function and provides protection against DNA viruses. According to recent research, Mn²+ has the ability to directly activate cGAS without the use of dsDNA.

Mn²+ ions have been found to increase the sensitivity of cGAS. They also cause the synthesis of cGAMP, which in turn increases STING activity by enhancing the binding affinity between cGAMP and STING. Additionally, Mn²+ promote CD8+ T cell activity by stimulating IFN production in vivo, which indirectly inhibits tumor development. However, direct Mn²+ injection in vivo cannot ensure an effective concentration in the tumor microenvironment, and excessive amounts of Mn²+ can cause system toxicities such cardiovascular and permanent neurotoxicity. Because of the sheltering effect of ligands, Mn complexes are more stable and inert to biomolecules, which may reduce the toxicity of Mn²+. When Mn complexes damaged nuclear and/or mitochondrial DNA in tumor cells, γ-H2AX was up-regulated. At the same time, the complex blocked the activity of PARP1 and HDAC1/2, which reduced the ability to repair damaged DNA and exacerbated it. After being released from the nucleus and/or mitochondria into the cytoplasm, the accumulating DNA fragments were identified by cGAS, which triggered the STING. IFNs, IL-6, and TNF-α were produced as a result of the STING’s stimulation of TBK1 and IRF3 phosphorylation and translocation into the nucleus. These pro-inflammatory cytokines and IFNs were subsequently released from the nucleus into the cytosol and then into the tumor microenvironment, where they stimulated CTLs to kill cancer cells and enhanced the maturation and antigen presentation of DCs and macrophages.

Modulation of tumor microenvironment

A tumor microenvironment (TME) is often characterized by extreme hypoxia, high GSH concentrations, low pH levels, and excessive production of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2). These features not only render tumor cells with accelerated growth and metastasis but also result in drug resistance and ineffective treatment. It is demonstrated that TME can induce immunosuppression by reducing the function of CTLs and increasing the accumulation of immunosuppressive cells such as regulatory T cells (Treg), myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and M2 macrophages. Reportedly, MnO2-NPs can alleviate TME through the decomposition of H2O2, relief of oxygen, glucose consumption, and downregulating GSH.

Hypoxia can fail several cancer therapies, mainly due to the requirement of oxygen in the process of treatment. Studies have revealed that these MnO2-NPs possess the remarkable ability to break down hydrogen peroxide overexpressed by tumors into essential oxygen directly within the TME. This not only alleviates tumor hypoxia but also enhances visibility on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), all while being safely eliminated from the body by the kidneys, thus circumventing any potential long-term toxicity concerns. Reversing hypoxia can also repolarize tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) toward inflammatory M1 macrophages. Besides, in a study conducted by Liang et al., combinational therapy with core-shell gold nanocage@manganese dioxide (AuNC@MnO2) nanoparticles and photodynamic therapy (PDT) accelerated oxygen-mediated PDT and tumor cell killing. They demonstrated that the MnO2 shell degraded under acidic TME of metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (mTNBC), and generated massive amounts of oxygen to boost immunogenic PDT mediated by near-infrared (NIR)-triggered ROS generators. This approach not only killed primary tumor cells but also induced ICD by releasing damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). These cascades of events further increased DC maturation and infiltration of CTLs in tumor site. Moreover, in an animal model of HCC, using hydrazide-Mn-NPs loaded with DOX/ indocyanine green (ICG) alleviated TME hypoxia, increased M1 macrophages phenotype and boosted efficacy of photodynamic/ chemotherapy efficacy.

Moreover, other studies have demonstrated that Mn-NPs can not only degrade GSH, but also result in the release of free Mn2+ which can further mediate the initiation of the cGAS/STING pathway. Cao et al. developed a radiosensitizer nanoparticle containing Hafnium oxide (HfO2) and MnO2 in a core/shell design for radiotherapy (RT)-mediated immunomodulation. They demonstrated that with radiotherapy, HfO2 mediated ICD and released dsDNA in the cytosol, while MnO2 underwent a redox reaction with GSH to form glutathione disulfide (GSS) and subsequently released Mn2+ to further augment anti-tumor efficacy via activation of the cGAS/STRING pathway. On the other hand, Mn2+ can react with H2O2 through Fenton-like catalytic reaction and generate hydroxyl radical (·OH). Therefore, excessive production of ROS product such as OH and H2O2, in addition to decreased scavenging capacity of ROS products mediated through depletion of GSH, can further promote ROS production, resulting in ICD and release of DAMPs. Nevertheless, DAMP products can further activate DCs and macrophages to further drive cellular immune responses against cancer (Figs. 2 and 3).

Hyperglycemia, stimulation of the mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT), inhibition of the mitochondrial electron transport chain (ETC), and absorption of cations like Ca2+ and Mn2+ are reported variables that raise the generation of ROS in the mitochondria. It is indicated that, intramitochondrial Ca2+ activates a number of sites within the mitochondrion that increase the rate of ATP production by oxidative phosphorylation, including activation of three dehydrogenases in the TCA cycle that catalyze increased production of NADH. These dehydrogenases’ activation raises the metabolic rate and the NADH/NAD+ ratio, which should in turn cause a higher rate of ROS generation. Because Mn2+ binds to nearly all Ca2+ binding sites, it may simply act as a Ca2+ analog, increasing the formation of ROS. It also found that Mn2+ slows down the pace at which ATP is produced by blocking sites in the TCA cycle, the electron transport chain, and other locations. The known locations for O2−• synthesis in the mitochondria are those where there is a comparatively greater chance of electron capture by O2. ROS generation may increase as a result of any inhibition brought on by Mn2+ binding to intramitochondrial sites that results in an accumulation of occupancy in these sites of O2− • production.

Mn2+ is in conflict with iron, whether it is functioning as a non-proteinaceous Mn antioxidant or as a cofactor for Superoxide dismutase (SOD). Cellular iron usually exceeds cellular Mn on a mole basis, and the two can easily fight for binding to Mn-SOD enzymes. However, Mn insertion into Mn-SOD has been favored by evolutionary adaptations, such as subcellular compartmentalization of Mn in the case of secreted Mn-SODs or improvements in Mn ion bioavailability through chemical speciation of the metal. The sequestration of iron in the Fe-S pathway may also enhance Mn selectivity for Mn-SOD in eukaryotic mitochondria, where iron binding to SOD2 is essentially nonexistent. Mn can also function as an antioxidant in small non-proteinaceous complexes, and Mn-antioxidant activity is most effective in low-iron cellular environments. Mn-antioxidants can efficiently protect proteins from oxidative damage and radiation without having to counterbalance the pro-oxidant effects of iron. By replacing iron as a cofactor in some oxidatively vulnerable enzymes, Mn can also increase resistance to oxidative stress. Unlike Fe and Cu-oxides, which are most active at more acidic pH values, freestanding Mn2+ species can catalyze the oxidation of organic molecules assisted by H2O2 at pH levels that are closer to physiological. On the other hand, immunotherapy may benefit from freestanding Mn2+ species.

Modulation of regulated cell death

The field of nanomedicine is an evolving interdisciplinary branch of sciences that shows a great potential for the treatment of cancer. This growing strategy applied to personalized cancer therapy focuses mainly on progressive drug delivery systems like passive targeting, active targeting, and environmentally responsive systems. Nanomedicine also significantly enhances imaging diagnostics, such as in vivo imaging, tumor cell targeting, and tumor biomarker detection.

The various functions of MnO2-NPs, including Fenton-like activity, GSH oxidation, enzyme mimicking, and longitudinal (T1) relaxation, have been thoroughly investigated. MnO2-NPs have the potential to enhance the effectiveness of ICD inducers in three main ways. These include: (i) acting as traceable drug carriers to facilitate improved cellular uptake and controlled drug release; (ii) acting as an oxidant to deplete GSH and promote oxidative damage; and (iii) acting as catalase to break down H2O2 into molecular oxygen and iiii)increase photodynamic efficiency. About their inherent ability to directly induce antitumor immunity through immunomodulation, MnO2 nanoflowers (MnO2-NFs) produced using a one-pot self-assembly method can effectively cause ICD in cancer cells when they are nutrient-deficient but not nutrient-rich. It was also indicted that, the Mn3+ complex increases the activity of caspase 8 and caspase 9, increases the expression of the Bax/Bcl-2 ratio, and facilitates the binding of TNF-α to its receptor, suggesting that both intrinsic and extrinsic apoptotic pathways are activated simultaneously in cancer cells.

The role of Mn in resistance mechanisms

Drug resistance brought on by prolonged use of chemotherapy medications is still a pressing issue in the field of tumor treatment. The primary function of Mn-NPs and chemotherapy medications in combination is the delivery of chemotherapy medications via these NPs.The controlled release of chemotherapy drugs can also be achieved by certain pH-responsive, photo-responsive, electromagnetic-responsive, redox-responsive, and other responsive Mn-based nanoparticles, significantly lowering the toxicity and adverse effects of chemotherapy drugs.

An adjuvant that is often used alongside a tumoral process is radiative therapy(RT). Ionizing radiation is the most common method of killing cancer cells by damaging DNA. Tumor resistance (a significant challenge of RT) will have an important influence on the therapeutic effect of RT. RT mostly relies on the stimulation of tissues or cells through ionizing radiation for ROS production, yielding a cytotoxic effect on tumors, and this will be limited by the hypoxic nature of the tumor. By controlling the TME and reducing the oxygen state of tumors, Mn-NPs can enhance the ability of RT to kill tumors. Moreover, tumor-related immune responses are strengthened by RT. Moreover, RT has been shown to induce ICD, the immunogenic element found in all forms of cell death. Because of its effects on the TME, Mn-NPs can reduce the hypoxia brought on by RT, increasing its sensitivity. Mn’s immunomodulatory effects can enhance the efficacy of other therapies in eliminating tumor cells.

Photothermal therapy (PTT) and PDT are new therapies that can be used in conjunction with chemotherapy and radiation therapy because of their high tumor selectivity and minimal toxic side effects. In some cases, Mn-NPs can serve as photosensitizers and improve the heat conversion efficiency of nanomaterials under light. Mn in nanoparticles is able to regulate TME and generate ROS to enhance the antitumor effect. Moreover, functional nanoparticles are often used to enhance the targeting of photosensitizers. MnOx (e.g., MnO2 and Mn3O4) are among the most common of these and are widely used in Mn-NPs. GSH overexpression in tumor cells presents a significant obstacle to chemodynamic therapy (CDT). GSH significantly decreases the efficacy of CDT and increases tumor cells’ resistance to oxidative stress. The antitumor properties of nanoparticles can be improved by Mn’s ability to control the TME inside it and produce synergistic effects.

Manganese and cancer immunotherapy

Despite the advancements in medical treatments, cancer continues to stand as a formidable adversary claiming countless lives. In response to this ongoing threat, scientists are diligently working towards refining existing treatment methods and pioneering innovative approaches like photodynamic therapy, starvation therapy, chemodynamic therapy and immunotherapy that directly engage with the intricate tumor microenvironment. By synergizing these cutting-edge therapies with the GSH-depletion capabilities of MnO2-NPs, there is hopeful anticipation for a significant boost in treatment efficacy against this relentless disease.

Agents of immunotherapeutics like genes and antibodies, are vulnerable to enzymatic degradation within the body, hampering their efficacy. To address this issue, nanostructured biomaterials are commonly used as nanocarriers for drug delivery to protect these bioactive molecules. A specialized tumor microenvironment-responsive drug delivery system was devised by encasing MnO2 around a chitosan core loaded with metformin. Upon entering the acidic environment of the tumor, the drug promptly releases thereby inhibiting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway and heightening tumor immunotherapy effects.

It has been indicated that, the synergistic effects of the B16-TCL@MnP vaccination combined with anti-PD-1 therapy proved to be remarkably successful in suppressing residual tumor growth, underscoring the promising potential of this biomineralization strategy in the creation of potent autologous cancer vaccines aimed at preventing postoperative tumor relapse when utilized alongside ICB immunotherapy. Following numerous appraisals of Mn-based cancer immunotherapy, it is prudent to explore novel opportunities such as Mn and tumor-targeting mAbs, Mn coupled with ICIs, Mn in conjunction with ICD inducers, and lastly Mn integrated into oncolytic viruses for further advancement in cancer treatment protocols. Manganese and tumor-targeting monoclonal antibodies

The noble landscape of medicine has been graced by the emergence of immunotherapy, employing the illustrious mAbs as a beacon of hope for those patients who find solace in treatments that defy conventional knowledge. In this regard, where chemotherapy and radiation have fallen short, these meticulously crafted agents stand tall on the battleground against illness. Originating from identical progenies of specialized B cells, these mAbs are bestowed with a singular purpose - to seek out and vanquish specific antigens through their unique epitope-binding capabilities. Each clone represents a harmonious fusion of precision and potency, embodying a bespoke antibody isotype tailored to engage with designated antigenic territories. Through their remarkable design and unwavering dedication to target-specific combat, they illuminate new paths towards healing in the vast expanse of therapeutic possibilities.

The progression and dissemination of malignant growths are intrinsically connected to specific proto-oncogenic protein receptor tyrosine kinases such as the HER2/neu receptor and epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR). Even though mAbs targeting these receptors have been sanctioned for a range of illnesses, the diversified nature of tumors poses a formidable obstacle in focused cancer therapy. Investigations have revealed fluctuating levels of expression of HER2/neu and EGFR in instances of breast, colorectal, and prostate cancers. In a study, multifaceted nanoparticles (MnMEIO-CyTE777-(Bis)-mPEG NPs) were formulated comprising near infrared fluorescence pigment along with anti-HER2/neu and anti-EGFR bispecific antibodies tailored towards distinguishing tumors exhibiting these particular receptors. The nanoparticles displayed targeted bonding with HER2/neu- and EGFR-presenting tumor cells, hinting at their potential effectiveness as a contrasting agent for preliminary identification of cancers overexpressing HER2/neu or EGFR.

The mAbs, have brought about a significant transformation in the field of hemato-oncology by becoming the first targeted biologicals to receive approval for clinical application. The remarkable success achieved with anti-CD20 rituximab has spurred advancements in the creation of next-generation and glycoengineered MAbs. These specialized antibodies primarily function by stimulating the host’s immune response through processes such as complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC), antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC), and antibody-dependent cell-mediated phagocytosis (ADCP). Moreover, MAbs play a vital role in targeted drug delivery strategies like radioimmunotherapy (RIT) and antibody-drug conjugates (ADC), exemplifying their versatility as indispensable tools in cancer treatment. Despite advances in medical treatments, there remains a population of patients resistant to the widely-used drug rituximab. These individuals face the daunting prospect of relapse and limited options for further treatment. In an effort to combat this resistance, scientists have turned their attention to enhancing the efficacy of anti-CD20 antibodies through intricate antibody engineering techniques. One such method involves increasing afucosylation, a process that has been proven to heighten ADCC activity. Through careful manipulation of osmolality levels in cell cultures, researchers have successfully upregulated afucosylation levels, resulting in improved ADCC activity and enhanced binding affinity to FcγRIIIa receptors on effector cells. These groundbreaking findings suggest that by regulating galactosylation and afucosylation through strategic supplementation methods, the therapeutic potential of mAbs in cancer treatment can be significantly bolstered.

The depletion of some immune progenitors, referred to as TAMs stands as a potent strategy in the battle against tumor growth and drug resistance within breast cancer. Through the administration of chemotherapeutic agents such as DOX and docetaxel (DTX), TAMs can be effectively eradicated, thereby arresting further progression of malignancy. mAbs armed with immunotoxins have also demonstrated success in directly targeting TAM antigens. Challenges and mitigation strategies

The pharmacokinetics of nanoparticles, Mn-NPs in particular, rely heavily on their physical (e.g. particle type, size, zoning, coating, and protein barrier) and chemical specification (e.g. hour, dose, and species of administration). Free Mn²⁺ generally shows rapid blood clearance, but nanoparticles can be designed to circulate for longer periods meaning improved delivery to target tissues. For example, the half-life of Mn-NPs in healthy rats is approximately 13 ± 3.5 min or about 10-fold longer than free Mn²⁺. The individual prolonged time in circulation allows Mn-NPs better accumulation at target sites (i.e. tumors) while minimizing exposure to non-target tissues. Surface modifications of Mn-NPs (e.g. PEG) can also improve circulation time by preventing uptake by the reticuloendothelial system. Continued circulation time over extended periods will greatly benefit therapeutic clinical applications that require sustained delivery of Mn to target tissues. Further, they can be altered to respond to local environmental triggers (e.g. pH or, GSH) resulting in controlled release of Mn in targeted tissues. For instance, under acidic conditions that exist in tumor microenvironments, the rate of drug release from nanoparticles can increase by 2.5-fold and or than Mn²+.

Mn-NPs exhibit an excellent promise for use in drug delivery and immunotherapy; however, their toxicity and biosafety, both in vitro and in vivo models, limit their clinical translation. Liu et al. and Shi et al. showed that H-MnO2-NPs have acceptable levels of tissue toxicity and biocompatibility. The cytotoxicity of Mn may be a result of the accumulation of their degradation product, such as Mn2+, in organs (i.e. liver and kidneys), leading to potential neurotoxicity or manganism. Mn accumulation in the brain results in manganism, a disorder with clinical features that parallel those of Parkinson’s disease. Although the mechanism of Mn accumulation is not completely understood, the most prevalent route of entry is through inhalation with other possible routes including via skin exposure and the olfactory nerve pathways. In the brain, Mn has been documented to disrupt neurotransmitters, interfere with synaptic conducting properties, induce free radical-level damage, and promote apoptosis. Key mechanisms in Mn-induced neurotoxicity includes the development of oxidative stress, toxic protein aggregation, endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, dysregulated autophagy and apoptosis. Autophagy is essential in cellular regulatory mechanisms; an essential aspect of autophagy is that it serves dual roles under same stressors, which is hypothesized to be related to the mechanisms of neurodegeneration. The roles of autophagy in Mn neurotoxicity has come into focus in studies of environmental toxicology, including studies specifically related to oligomerization of alpha-synuclein, ER stress, and aberrant protein S-nitrosylation. Interestingly, in vivo studies further demonstrated that Mn2+ released from H-MnO2-NPs can be rapidly cleared from the kidneys. Overall, the studies demonstrated good biocompatibility and great biodegradability as well as quick rates of dissolution of these nanoparticles, presenting a safe pathway for biomedical applications and promising aspects for use in targeted drug delivery systems for the treatment of cancer.

Mn-NPs biodistribution is influenced by particle size, surface charge, surface modifications, surface chemistry, and interactions with biological systems; other properties of Mn-NPs that affect biodistribution include magnetic properties and the use of targeting molecules. Recent studies show that some Mn-NPs will accumulate in the kidneys, liver, and spleen, with the potential for a long-term retention. The long-term studies take even longer for the final removal of Mn-NP from the body, highlighting the significance of long-term biodistribution studies to inform future clinical opportunities.

The distribution of Mn-NPs within the body affects both the accuracy of targeting specific tissues or organs when delivering a drug, and the quality of the imaging results for MRI. Smaller particles are known to more efficiently accumulate in tumors because of the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect. However, relying on passive targeting for delivery of MnO₂-NPs through the EPR effect alone may not lead to sufficient delivery for every tumor type. Therefore, investigation of active targeting strategies to improve the specificity of MnO₂-NPs for cancer cells is underway. For example, when MnO₂-NPs are conjugated to tumor-specific ligands like folic acid or antibodies targeting a specific tumor-associated antigen, the accumulation of the materials at the tumor site is enhanced relative to other tissues, resulting in fewer off-target effects.

Regarding biosafety, larger MnxOy-NPs may be more prone to bioaccumulation in designated organs and may be eliminated incorrectly. The development of damage to the neurological system may also develop over time with long-term exposures. Most of the recent studies have all pointed to MnxOy theragnostic performance instead of outlining MnxOy cytotoxicity profiles, and although MnxOy-NPs display favorable biocompatibility and reduced toxicity, such profiles were established/specified at certain concentration. To date, studies of the safety and use of MnxOy-NPs have not transitioned to the clinical level. An additional concern is the ongoing need for comprehensive and systematic bio-safety evaluations and in vivo assessments to achieve the same clinical applicability as other inorganic nano-formulations, considering the issues previously mentioned.

Some research has suggested, wrapping MnO2-NPs with human serum albumin (HSA) may improve their biocompatibility and size, which will help to increase the permeability and retention effect of the nanoparticles. Furthermore, research has demonstrated that loading MnO2-NPs with GC or PEG can improve their colloid stability and biocompatibility. According to another research, polydopamine (PDA) is a “connector” of functional materials because of its high chemical reactivity and strong adhesion. MnO2-NPs can also be attached to PDA with good biocompatibility. MnO2-NPs may become more biocompatible for the treatment of cancer if they are encapsulated in a carrier made of HA.

Although attempts have been made to improve biocompatibility and decrease the toxicity of MnO2-NPs, more attention needs to be given to improve the clinical applications. Systemic metabolism, the degradation behavior, and the mechanisms of biodistribution are aspects that need to be fully evaluated. Biocompatibility is important, but a top priority is to reduce toxicity and improve the biodegradability of MnO2-NPs while retaining therapeutic efficacy in vivo. In addition to toxicity and safety concerns, there presently does not exist a “gold standard” MnO2-NPs preparation protocol for clinical use. While various existing methods are available, they face challenges such as complexity, cost, quality, safety, and environmental issues. Overall, developing a simple, low-cost, safe, and environmentally friendly synthesis of MnO2-NPs is still a major challenge.

Another critical barrier to clinical translation of Mn-NPs is immune-related adverse events (irAEs). Even though potential benefit in improving tumor-specific immune responses by combining Mn-NPs with ICIs or other immunotherapies, Mn-NPs and ICIs may cause irAEs associated with autoimmune responses and/or systemic inflammation. The best way to alleviate these adverse events is through the optimization of dosing schedules and also by limiting the exposure of immune cells to MnO₂-NPs using focused delivery methods.

Conclusion and future perspective

The swift evolution of nanotechnology has ushered in a new era of innovation, with functionalized nanomaterials emerging as potent adjuncts in the fight against solid tumors. Among these groundbreaking materials, MnO2-NPs stand out as particularly promising allies in the fight against cancer within the TME as discussed above. Of special note are distinct characteristics of Mn2+ and its nanoparticles which have displayed exceptional efficacy as a combinational therapy, as a potent adjuvant for cancer vaccines, and drug delivery systems. These structures offer unprecedented precision and control over the release of therapeutic agents, further bolstering their potential in cancer treatment strategies. It is clear that these innovative technologies hold great promise for revolutionizing current approaches to combatting solid tumors and improving patient outcomes. This groundbreaking approach introduces the concept of “Metal immunotherapy” and showcases the immense possibilities nanomedicine offers in combating cancer. With these groundbreaking advancements on display, one can envision a landscape where improved treatment strategies beckon for those embroiled in battle against malignancies. Promisingly poised at the precipice of medical advancement is this remarkable concoction’s capacity to revolutionize current paradigms within oncological spheres.
