Main text

What is the biggest challenge for implementation of microbiome analyses in the clinic?

Our lab explores how intestinal microbiota impact tumor immunotherapy, facing challenges in implementing microbiome analyses in cancer clinics. The clinical complexity for cancer patients, including treatment history, comorbidities, and lifestyle factors, significantly affects the microbiome’s composition and function. Additionally, dietary habits and environmental exposures complicate isolating specific microbiome effects on cancer progression or treatment response. Moreover, cancer treatment often involves diverse approaches, like chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy, making it difficult to define treatment response in microbiome analyses. Identifying whether responses are solely due to immunotherapy or influenced by other treatments as well as predicting response or resistance based on microbiome profiles requires careful consideration of treatment interactions and patient heterogeneity. Lastly, high-quality, longitudinally collected microbiome data, paired with clinical outcomes, are crucial for understanding the microbiome’s role in cancer progression. Yet, scarcity of such datasets with long-term clinical follow-up in cancer immunotherapy hampers progress. Improving dataset quality and availability, standardizing methodologies, and conducting large-scale longitudinal studies with comprehensive clinical annotations are vital for advancing microbiome-based approaches in oncology through interdisciplinary collaboration, enhancing cancer diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment outcomes.

What will the impact of the microbiome be for patients with cancer?

Learning from the taxonomic composition of intestinal microbiota in 3,414 cancer patients and 5,398 healthy individuals from European registries, the French and European consortia that I coordinated over the past decade understood that more than half of stage III/IV cancer patients harbor a gut dysbiosis versus 20% in “healthy” cancer-free controls. This deviation of the microbial repertoire lining onto 200m2 of intestinal surface influences systemic inflammation, immunity, metabolism, and neuroendocrine systems to such an extent that cancer therapeutics, specifically based on immunotherapy, will not confer full-fledged efficacy. Our experimental models showed that intestinal dysbiosis can be rescued by oral administration of complex and rich ecosystems or live biotherapeutics composed of one or several immunogenic commensals that will pave the way to the success of immune checkpoint inhibitors. The first three clinical trials pioneering the field of fecal microbial transplantation (FMT) in cancer patients suggested that this approach may circumvent primary resistance to PD-1 blockade in melanoma and increase response rates in naive metastatic melanoma patients. Therefore, I believe that the clinical management of cancer patients will change in the near future by including systematic diagnosis of “gut dysbiosis” to stratify patients and compensate them with live biotherapeutics and/or immunogenic metabolites. This will go beyond stage III/IV carcinogenesis, reaching early malignancies. Moreover, interceptive measures will aim at preventing the prophylactic use of antibiotics or proton pump inhibitors disruptive for gut homeostasis and counseling for lifestyle interventions, because “all disease begins in the gut,” as said by Hippocrates (460–370 BC).

What will the impact of the microbiome be for patients with gastric cancer?

Gastric cancer (GC) stands as one of the leading causes of cancer death globally, particularly prevalent in the Asian population, attributed to various factors such as genetic mutations, dietary habits, and lifestyle choices. The gut microbiome, encompassing millions of microbes, plays a crucial role in the development, progression, and treatment outcomes of GC.

The discovery of Helicobacter pylori’s carcinogenic role in the 2005 Nobel Prize in Physiology has significantly advanced our understanding of the relationship between microbes/microbiomes and GC. Patients with GC exhibit distinct microbiome composition compared to healthy individuals. Disruptions in this microbial balance impact treatment responses, including chemotherapy and immunotherapy outcomes, and further impact patient survival. In a study involving HER2-negative patients, we showed that those with elevated levels of Lactobacillus displayed greater microbiome diversity and notably improved responses to immunotherapy, suggesting specific microbial taxa as potential predictive prognostic markers.

Efforts to manipulate the microbiome have emerged as promising strategies to improve the treatment effectiveness for GC. Our preliminary clinical investigations on FMT have shown encouraging findings, indicating that certain FMT approaches could overcome resistance to anti-PD-1 inhibitors in GC patients. The exploration of other microbiome-based interventions, such as live biotherapeutic products and microbial metabolites, is yielding promising results when combined with conventional treatment regimes. However, it is essential to recognize that these interventions are still in their early stages, underscoring the necessity for further research to fully harness the vast potential of the microbiome and its application in GC patient care.

What will the impact of the microbiome be on clinical care in the future?

The microbiome has a strong influence on metabolic and immune functions in homeostasis as well as in pathological conditions. The importance of the gut microbiome in influencing tumorigenesis, tumor progression, metastasis, and therapy responses has been established, both in preclinical and in clinical studies. These pilot studies have given optimistic results, but more data is needed to conclude the ultimate value of these strategies.

Most studies reporting tumor microbial findings are based on genomic information, but multiple layers of evidence will be needed to make solid conclusions including microbial species detection through in situ hybridization as well as ex vivo culturomics to determine microbial viability. Few studies have also described functional role of microbes within tumors, but knowledge is gathering. A lot of work remains to be conducted to establish the value of the tumor microbiome in clinical settings. From a biomarker perspective, there is a lot of potential to use tumor microbial information to guide treatment choices and even to use microbes highly abundant on tumors for either drugs or other materials. Because high heterogeneity exists between microbes in different tumor types but also within individuals, it is likely microbial knowledge may need to be determined in a personalized manner. This knowledge will inform future interventional clinical studies aiming to specifically modulate tumor microbes independently of gut microbes.

The integration between tumor genomics, transcriptomics, metabolomics, and microbial information may eventually reveal biologically relevant biological circuits. Ultimately, artificial intelligence will be required to use this integrated data for individual optimal therapy choices.
