# Integrating gene demethylation and immune modulation: PD-1 nanovesicles as a dual-action therapy for NSCLC.

## Abstract

Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) remains a formidable challenge in oncology, underscoring the urgent need for innovative therapeutic strategies. This study explores the potential of PD-1-modified multifunctional nanovesicles (NVs) loaded with 5-azacytidine (5-Aza) for NSCLC treatment. By integrating bioinformatics analyses with in vitro and in vivo experiments, methylation-driven genes closely associated with NSCLC progression and prognosis-CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2-were identified. Functional assays revealed that 5-Aza effectively demethylates these genes, reducing NSCLC cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. PD-1-modified NVs demonstrated precise targeting of NSCLC cells via PD-L1 binding, while the combination of PD-1 NVs and 5-Aza synergistically enhanced peripheral blood mononuclear cell activation, induced apoptosis, and amplified anti-tumor immunity. In vivo, studies confirmed the tumor-targeting ability and significant therapeutic efficacy of PD-1 NVs. This synergistic strategy of epigenetic modulation and immune activation offers a promising avenue for NSCLC management. These findings contribute valuable insights into developing targeted nanotherapeutics for effective NSCLC treatment.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is one of the most prevalent types of lung cancer, accounting for 85 % of all cases. The incidence and mortality rates of NSCLC are remarkably high worldwide, posing significant health threats to patients. Despite significant progress in the treatment of NSCLC in recent decades, the current therapeutic methods remain limited, and the survival rate for advanced NSCLC patients remains low. Therefore, the search for new treatment strategies and the investigation of their mechanisms are crucial for improving the prognosis of NSCLC patients.

The expression of immune checkpoints is a crucial factor in tumor development and progression. PD-1 (programmed cell death protein-1) and its ligand PD-L1 (programmed death-ligand 1) are essential members of the immune checkpoint family, involved in regulating the interaction between immune cells and tumor cells. Within the tumor microenvironment, tumor cells inhibit the activation of T cells by overexpressing PD-L1 and binding to PD-1, thereby evading clearance by the immune system. This immune evasion mechanism allows tumor cells to proliferate, spread, and invade. Consequently, therapeutic strategies targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway have emerged as significant breakthrough immunotherapy for NSCLC.

In recent years, the application of nanotechnology in cancer treatment has garnered widespread attention. Nanovesicles (NVs), as a multifunctional drug delivery system, offer advantages such as small size, large surface area, and strong targetability, markedly enhancing the bioavailability and therapeutic effects of drugs. In particular, NVs can achieve precise targeting of tumor cells by modifying specific molecules, thereby enhancing treatment specificity and effectiveness. Researchers have developed PD-1-modified nanovesicles (PD-1 NVs), which specifically target tumor cells expressing PD-L1 and deliver anticancer drugs, thereby enhancing the efficacy of cancer treatment. Thus, combining PD-1 NVs with the DNA methylation inhibitor 5-azacytidine may offer a novel synergistic therapeutic strategy for NSCLC. This study aims to investigate the therapeutic potential and molecular mechanisms of PD-1&5-Aza NVs in NSCLC through in vitro and in vivo experiments, providing new theoretical insights for clinical treatment.

5-azacytidine is a DNA methylation inhibitor that has been extensively studied and clinically used in treating various malignancies, including NSCLC. By binding to DNA methyltransferases, 5-Aza blocks DNA methylation modifications, leading to alterations in the methylation status of genomic DNA in tumor cells. Studies indicate that by reducing DNA methylation levels, 5-Aza can inhibit tumor cell proliferation, migration, and invasion, and induce apoptosis. Thus, 5-Aza demonstrates potential therapeutic effects in NSCLC treatment and is widely considered a promising combination therapy agent.

The aim of this study is to investigate the molecular mechanisms of the synergistic treatment of NSCLC using PD-1-modified multifunctional NVs (PD-1 NVs) loaded with 5-Aza (PD-1 & 5-Aza NVs). We obtained methylation and gene expression data of NSCLC from the UCSC Xena database and Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) database and performed differential analysis and clinical correlation analysis. The biological effects of PD-1 & 5-Aza NVs on NSCLC cells were validated through in vitro and in vivo experiments, and relevant prognostic models were constructed. This study is expected to uncover the potential of PD-1 & 5-Aza NVs in synergistically treating NSCLC by blocking the methylation of tumor suppressor genes and promoting immune escape. The results of this study can provide important scientific evidence for innovative NSCLC treatment strategies and hold significant clinical implications for improving patients' survival prognosis and treatment outcomes.

Materials and methods

Data acquisition and processing

The DNA methylation data and RNA-Seq data for NSCLC in the cancer genome atlas (TCGA) were downloaded from the UCSC Xena database (https://xena.ucsc.edu/). Additionally, phenotypic data and clinical prognosis data for NSCLC tumor samples were obtained. The methylation data consisted of 471 cancer tissues and 32 adjacent normal tissues, while the RNA-Seq data included 526 cancer tissues and 59 adjacent normal tissues. Perl language was used to group the samples, and the ensemble ID of the samples was converted using the GENCODE Gene Set-09.2019 annotation file. Ensemble IDs for lncRNAs and mRNAs not included in the GENCODE database were excluded. Since the UCSC Xena database provides public data, ethical approval or informed consent was not required for this study.

Differential analysis and visualization

The "limma” package in R software was utilized for differential expression analysis of the TCGA dataset. The false discovery rate (FDR) method was employed to correct the p-values for differential analysis. A threshold of FDR <0.05 was set to identify significantly differentially methylated sites and genes, and volcano plots were generated using the "ggplot2” package in R software. All analyses were conducted in R version 4.2.1 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing).

Survival analysis

The integration of survival time, patient status, and gene expression data for NSCLC patients in the TCGA dataset enabled survival prognosis analysis based on Cox analysis and Kaplan-Meier analysis using the "survival” package in R software. A p-value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Identification of methylation-driven genes

To investigate the correlation between the methylation levels and transcriptome expression levels of genes, Spearman correlation analysis was conducted, and the results were visualized using the "ggplot2” package in R. A negative correlation coefficient indicates a negative correlation between the two variables. Using a screening threshold of p < 0.05, genes with a significant negative correlation between methylation levels and transcriptome expression levels were identified as methylation-driven genes.

Clinical correlation analysis

Integrating gene expression data and clinical features of NSCLC patients from the TCGA dataset, clinical correlation analysis was performed using the "ComplexHeatmap” package in R, and a heatmap was generated.

Construction and validation of prognostic risk model

The "survival” package was employed to calculate the risk values for each NSCLC patient based on multifactor Cox analysis and to construct the prognostic risk model. The GSE5843 dataset (including data from 48 NSCLC patients) was downloaded from the GEO database (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/) to test the accuracy of the prognostic risk model. The "survival” and "survminer” packages in R were used to draw survival curves for the prognostic risk model and calculate p-values. Patients were divided into high-risk and low-risk groups based on the median risk score, and Kaplan-Meier survival analysis was performed to compare the overall survival differences between the two groups, with a significance threshold of p < 0.05. Multifactor Cox analysis was employed to evaluate the independent prognostic ability of the risk model. Immune cell content data from tumor tissues in the TCGA dataset were downloaded from online websites to assess the correlation between the prognostic risk model and immune cell types.

Construction of lentiviral vectors

The lentiviral overexpression vectors, pCDH-CMV-MCS-EF1α-copGFP (oe-overexpression vector, catalog number: CD511B-1, System Biosciences, USA), carrying the genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, were purchased. These vectors were packaged into lentiviral particles in HEK-293T cells (catalog number: iCell-h237, Cyagen Biosciences, Shanghai, China) using the Lentivirus Packaging Kit (catalog number: A35684CN, Invitrogen, USA). The supernatant containing the lentiviruses was collected 48 h after transfection, resulting in a titer of 1 × 108 TU/ml.

Cell culture and grouping

Human normal lung alveolar epithelial cells (BEAS-2B), mouse normal lung alveolar epithelial cells (MLE-12), human NSCLC cells (HCC78), and mouse NSCLC cells (LLC, Lewis lung cancer) were obtained from Ningbo Mingzhou Biotechnology Co., Ltd. (catalog numbers: B164023, MZ-0782, MZ-292, MZ-3138). The cells were cultured with the following media: BEAS-2B and MLE-12 were maintained in LHC-9 medium (catalog number: 12680013, ThermoFisher, USA), HCC78 was cultured in RPMI-1640 medium (catalog number: 11875176, ThermoFisher, USA), and LLC was cultured in DMEM (Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium) (catalog number: 11320033, ThermoFisher, USA). Mouse 3T3-L1 cells (catalog number: iCell-m066, Cyagen Biosciences, Shanghai, China) were cultured using a specialized medium (catalog number: iCell-m066-001b).

Based on the research objectives, HCC78 and LLC cells were divided into several groups: oe-NC group, oe-CLEC3B group, oe-CYP27A1 group, oe-CYP4B1 group, oe-NR0B2 group, DMSO group, and 5-azacytidine group. The overexpression groups were treated by adding 1 mL of the corresponding lentiviral particles to the cells, and lentiviral infection efficiency was assessed 48 h later. The DMSO group and 5-azacytidine group were treated with 0.1 μM of the DNA methyltransferase inhibitor 5-azacytidine (catalog number: HY-10586, Master of Bioactive Molecules), with an equal volume of DMSO serving as a control, and subsequent experiments were conducted 48 h after treatment.

Reverse transcription quantitative polymerase Chain reaction (RT-qPCR)

Total RNA was extracted from cells using TRIzol reagent (Catalog No: 15596026, ThermoFisher, USA), and the concentration and purity of the extracted RNA were measured using a nanodrop2000 micro-UV spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher, USA). The mRNA was reverse transcribed into cDNA using the PrimeScript RT reagent Kit (Takara Code: RR047A, Takara, Japan) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Primers for each gene were synthesized by TaKaRa (Table S1). Real-time fluorescent quantitative PCR was performed using the 7500 Fast Real-Time PCR system (Catalog No: 4351106, ThermoFisher, USA), with the reaction conditions consisting of an initial denaturation at 95 °C for 10 min, followed by denaturation at 95 °C for 10 s, annealing at 60 °C for 20 s, and extension at 72 °C for 34 s for a total of 40 cycles. The relative transcript levels of the target genes were calculated using the internal control primer GAPDH and the relative quantification method (2-△△CT method). Each experiment was performed in triplicate.

Methylation-specific PCR (MSP)

The methylation status of the promoter regions of the CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 genes was determined using MS-PCR. Genomic DNA was extracted from cells using a genomic DNA extraction kit (Catalog No: DP304-03, Beijing TianGen Biotech Co., Ltd, Beijing, China) according to the manufacturer's instructions. The concentration and purity of the DNA were measured using UV spectrophotometry, and the DNA was stored at −80 °C for later use. Sodium bisulfite treatment of DNA was performed using the EZ DNA Methylation Kit (Catalog No: ZD5003, Shenzhen HaudhwaBio Co., Ltd, Shenzhen, China), followed by purification using reaction columns. The purified DNA was used for subsequent PCR reactions. Methylation and non-methylation primers for the CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 gene promoter Cytosine-phosphate-Guanine (CpG) islands were designed (Table S2). The PCR products were analyzed by agarose gel electrophoresis and visualized using an imaging and analysis system. Each experiment was repeated three times.

Cell counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay

The results of the cell proliferation experiment were statistically analyzed using the CCK-8 kit (catalog number: CA1210, Beijing Solarbio Science & Technology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China). Logarithmic phase cells were seeded at a density of 1 × 104 cells per well in a 96-well plate and pre-incubated for 24 h. The cells were then treated with 10 μL of CCK-8 reagent at 0 h, 24 h, 48 h, and 72 h, and incubated at 37 °C for 3 h. The absorbance values at a wavelength of 450 nm were measured using an ELISA reader. The absorbance values were directly proportional to the number of proliferating cells in the culture medium, and a cell growth curve was plotted.

Transwell assay

Matrigel gel (catalog number: 356234, Shanghai Haoyang Biotechnology Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China) stored at −80 °C was thawed overnight at 4 °C. A total of 200 μL of serum-free medium and 200 μL of Matrigel gel were combined at 4 °C to dilute the matrix gel. Subsequently, 50 μL of the mixture was added to the upper chamber of each Transwell plate, and the plate was incubated in a cell culture incubator for 2–3 h until the gel solidified. The cells were digested, counted, and resuspended in a serum-free medium. A total of 200 μL of cell suspension was added to each upper chamber, while 800 μL of medium containing 20 % FBS was added to the lower chamber. The plates were incubated in a 37 °C cell culture incubator for 24 h. After removing the Transwell plate, the upper surface of the membrane was rinsed with PBS twice and gently wiped with a cotton ball to remove cells. The cells were then fixed with formaldehyde for 10 min, followed by three washes with water. Staining was performed using 0.1 % crystal violet, and the plate was incubated at room temperature for 30 min. After rinsing twice with PBS, the cells were observed, photographed, and counted under an inverted microscope. For the migration assay, no matrix gel was added, and the incubation period was 24 h. Cell counting was performed in at least four randomly selected microscopic regions. The experiment was repeated three times.

Detection of cell apoptosis by flow cytometry

Cells were collected in flow tubes and centrifuged, and the supernatant was discarded. Cells were washed three times with cold PBS, centrifuged, and the supernatant was discarded. According to the instructions of the Annexin-V-FITC Apoptosis Detection Kit (C1065, Bioworld Technology, Shanghai, China), Annexin-V-FITC, PI, and HEPES buffer were prepared in a ratio of 1:2:50 to form the Annexin-V-FITC/PI staining solution. Each 100 μL staining solution was resuspended with 1 × 106 cells, mixed by gentle shaking, and incubated at room temperature for 15 min. Then, 1 mL HEPES buffer was added and mixed again by gentle shaking. Flow cytometry was used to detect FITC and PI fluorescence at excitation wavelengths of 488 nm, with 525 nm and 620 nm bandpass filters, to assess cell apoptosis. The upper right quadrant represented apoptotic cells. The experiment was independently repeated three times. The apoptosis index (AI) was calculated as AI = number of apoptotic cells/(number of apoptotic cells + number of normal cells).

Preparation of PD-1 NVs

The human PD-1 open reading frame (ORF) cDNA expression plasmid and mouse PD-1 ORF cDNA expression plasmid, both conjugated with green fluorescent protein, were provided by Sino Biological Inc (Sino Biological, Beijing, China). Stable overexpression of PD-1 was achieved by lentivirus infection in HEK293T/3T3L1 cell lines (iCell-h237, iCell-m066, Cyagen Biosciences Inc, Shanghai, China). The HEK293T/3T3L1 cells (3 × 107 cells in total) were collected, then homogenized using a grinder, and centrifuged at different speeds of 5000 rpm (4 °C, 10 min) and 13,500 rpm (4 °C, 10 min). Finally, the supernatant was filtered through 0.45 μm and 0.22 μm filters to obtain nano exosomes (NC NVs) and PD-1 NVs.

Characterization of PD-1 NVs

The HEK293T/3T3-L1 cell membrane was dissolved in 0.5 mL of PBS and filtered through a 0.1 μm filter. Cell staining was performed using uranyl acetate, and the shape and size of the NVs were observed under a transmission electron microscope (Tecnai G2 Spirit BioTWIN, FEI Company, USA). Fluid dynamic diameter and zeta potential were measured using dynamic light scattering on the Zetasizer Nano ZS particle analyzer (Malvern Instruments Limited).

Cell binding experiment with PD-1 NVs

NSCLC cells were co-incubated with GFP-PD-1 NVs (25 mg/mL) at 37 °C for 2 h, followed by staining with WGA Alexa594 for 10 min. After washing with phosphate-buffered saline, images were captured using a confocal microscope with emission from the corresponding 594 nm laser.

Western blot

Cellular total protein was extracted using RIPA lysis buffer (P0013C, Beyotime, Shanghai, China) containing PMSF. The lysate was incubated on ice for 30 min at 4 °C, followed by centrifugation at 8000g for 10 min to collect the supernatant. The concentration of total protein was measured using a BCA assay kit (cat. no. 23227, ThermoFisher, USA). 50 μg of protein was dissolved in 2 × SDS loading buffer and boiled for 5 min at 100 °C. Subsequently, SDS-PAGE gel electrophoresis was performed, and the proteins were transferred to a PVDF membrane. The membrane was then blocked with 5 % non-fat milk at room temperature for 1 h before incubating with primary antibodies overnight at 4 °C. The primary antibodies used were PD-1 (1:1500; NBP1-77276; Novus Biologicals), PD-L1 (1:1000; NBP1-76769; Novus Biologicals), Na/K ATPase (1:1000; #3010; Cell Signaling Technology), and Actin (1:1000; ab8227; Abcam). After three washes with TBST for 10 min each, the membrane was incubated with Horseradish Peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG H&L (HRP) secondary antibody (ab97051; 1:2000; Abcam) for 1 h at room temperature. Following TBST washes, the membrane was placed on a clean glass plate. An equal mixture of A and B solutions from the ECL fluorescence detection kit (abs920, Absin Bioscience Inc., Shanghai, China) was added to the membrane and mixed in the dark. The membrane was then imaged using the Bio-Rad imaging system (BIO-RAD, USA) and analyzed using Quantity One v4.6.2 software to determine the relative protein content represented by the grayscale value of the corresponding protein band normalized to the Actin protein band. The experiment was repeated three times, and the average values were calculated.

Co-immunoprecipitation (CO-IP) experiment

In order to detect the interaction between PD-1 NVs and PD-L1 on NSCLC cells, 1 mL (500 μg/mL) of PD-1 NVs were added and incubated with NSCLC cells for 20 h. The cells were then washed three times with PBS to remove unbound NVs. Next, the cells were lysed in 1 mL of RIPA lysis buffer containing a phosphatase inhibitor (catalog numbers: 78420, 89901, ThermoFisher, USA). The cell lysate was centrifuged at 15,000×g for 10 min at 4 °C, and the supernatant was pre-cleared with protein G agarose beads (catalog number: 78610, ThermoFisher) by gently rotating at 4 °C for 1 h. The cell lysate was then incubated with PD-1 primary antibody (1:200, #84651, Cell Signaling Technology, USA) overnight at 4 °C with agitation. The next day, the lysate was treated with 10 μL of protein G-agarose beads for 2 h at 4 °C. After washing three times with cold RIPA buffer, the bound proteins were separated using 10 % SDS-PAGE and analyzed by Western blot using PD-1 and PD-L1 antibodies.

Flow cytometry for cell binding analysis

NSCLC cells were incubated with PD-1 NVs for 2 h or pre-incubated with PD-L1 antibody (NBP1-76769, novusbio) for 4 h, followed by the addition of PD-1 NVs to the culture medium. Flow cytometry analysis was performed to assess the binding of PD-1 NVs to NSCLC cells. To determine the level of PD-L1 in NSCLC cells, cell samples were collected and analyzed by flow cytometry using a fluorescently labeled PD-L1 antibody (1:500, NBP1-43262AF488, novusbio) to evaluate the fluorescence intensity as a measure of PD-L1 expression level, with IgG used as a control (1:500, NBP1-97079, FAB110R-100UG, novusbio).

Preparation of PD-1&5-Aza_NVs

PD-1 NVs were gently mixed with 1 mg of 5-azacytidine in 1 mL of electroporation buffer, which consisted of 1.15 mM potassium phosphate (pH 7.2) and 25 mM potassium chloride. Electroporation was performed using a Bio-Rad electroporation instrument in a 0.4-cm cuvette, followed by placing the sample on ice for 30 min. The sample was then washed and centrifuged at 12,000 rpm to obtain PD-1 NVs carrying 5-azacytidine, which were named PD-1&5-Aza_NVs. A standard calibration curve for 5-azacytidine was obtained through experimentation. The content of 5-azacytidine was determined based on the fluorescence absorbance intensity measured at 595 nm, allowing for the determination of the encapsulation efficiency and in vitro release of 5-azacytidine. The NSCLC cells were treated with PD-1&5-Aza_NVs and flow cytometry was used to assess cell apoptosis.

Cell co-culture

Blood samples were collected from healthy blood donors (3 males and 3 females, aged 20–45 years) and normal C57BL/6 mice into vials containing EDTA, followed by GE Healthcare's Ficoll Paque Plus separation solution. The blood was centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 30 min to obtain Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs). The PBMCs were washed twice with RPMI 1640 and then subjected to red blood cell lysis on ice for 2 min using 1 mL of lysis buffer. Finally, RPMI 1640 was added to neutralize and suspend the PBMCs. The ThermoFisher CFSE fluorescent dye was diluted 1:1000, added to the PBMC suspension, and gently mixed. After co-culturing labeled CFSE PBMCs with NSCLC cells for 48 h, the co-culture system was divided into three groups: NC NVs group, PD-1 NVs group, and PD-1&5-Aza NVs group. Flow cytometry was used to measure the CFSE density of each group.

Establishment of a mouse model of lung adenocarcinoma

Thirty-five 7-week-old C57BL/6 mice were purchased from Beijing Weitonglihua Experimental Animal Technology Co., Ltd (Catalog number 213, Beijing, China). These mice were housed in an Specific Pathogen Free (SPF)-grade animal facility with a humidity of 60–65 % and a temperature range of 22–25 °C. After one week of adaptation, the experiment was started, and the mice were observed for their health status prior to the experiment. The experimental procedures and animal usage protocol were approved by our institution's Animal Ethics Committee.

LLC NSCLC cell line was prepared as a cell suspension with a concentration of 2 × 107/mL. A volume of 0.2 mL of the cell suspension was subcutaneously injected into the side of the mice using a 1 mL syringe. When the tumor reached a size of 80–90 mm3, the mice were subjected to subsequent research and grouped accordingly.

Previous studies have shown that after intravenous administration of tumor-targeted NVs in mice, the drug concentration in tumor tissues reaches its peak within 1 h and remains at a detectable level for up to 12 h. To investigate the in vivo targeting ability of PD-1 NVs, ten mice were randomly selected and injected with Cy5-labeled NV NVs and mPD-1 NVs (1 mg/kg) via the tail vein, with 5 mice in each group. In vivo, fluorescence imaging was conducted at 0, 1, 4, and 12 h after administration. Subsequently, the mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation, and heart, liver, spleen, and kidney tissues were collected for Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) staining to evaluate the toxicity of NV NVs and mPD-1 NVs on the tissues.

Determination of the in vivo anti-tumor efficacy of PD-1&5-Aza NVs

Studies have also demonstrated that engineered NVs designed for cancer treatment exhibit significant anti-tumor effects approximately 20 days post-administration in mouse xenograft tumor models. To evaluate the therapeutic effect of PD-1&5-Aza NVs in vivo, tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into five groups, with five mice in each group: PBS group, NC NVs group, PD-1 NVs group, 5-Aza group, and PD-1&5-Aza NVs group. Tail vein injections were performed every other day, with a dose of 1 mg/kg in each injection. The tumor volume and body weight of the mice were measured every three days after injection. After three weeks, the mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation, and the tumors were weighed and photographed. In addition, tumor tissues were collected and flow cytometry analysis was performed to determine the expression of Ki67 protein and the content of CD8+ T cells in the tumor tissue.

Immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis

At the end of the in vivo anti-tumor experiments, the collected tumor tissues were fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde for 24 h. The fixed samples were then dehydrated, embedded in paraffin, and sectioned. The following primary antibodies were used for immunohistochemical staining: Ki67 antibody (1:200, ab15581, Abcam), CLEC3B antibody (1:50, CL488-20311, Proteintech), CYP27A1 antibody (1:250, 67045-1-Ig, Proteintech), CYP4B1 antibody (1:20, 11771-1-AP, Proteintech), NR0B2 antibody (1:100, HY-P83732, MCE). The expression levels of Ki67, CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 were analyzed using immunohistochemistry, and ImageJ software was used to quantify the positive cell area for each marker.

Statistical analysis

Data analysis was performed using Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS) software (version 21.0, IBM, USA). Continuous data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Unpaired Student's t-test was used to compare normally distributed data between two groups, while one-way ANOVA followed by Tukey's post hoc test was used for comparisons among multiple groups.

Results

Methylation-driven gene is associated with tumor progression and prognosis in NSCLC

Gene methylation is a prevalent form of epigenetic modification that typically occurs on CpG islands. In cancer, abnormal methylation of specific genes, including gene silencing caused by promoter region methylation of tumor suppressor genes, has been shown to be closely associated with tumorigenesis, chemotherapy resistance, disease progression, and prognosis. To uncover the complex patterns of gene methylation and its impact on tumor progression in NSCLC, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of gene methylation data from NSCLC patients in the TCGA dataset, aiming to explore potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets.

Firstly, we compiled the methylation site data of NSCLC patients' lung tissues from the TCGA dataset. The results of differential analysis (Fig. 1A) revealed that in NSCLC tumor tissues, there was a significant increase in methylation levels at 4380 methylation sites and a significant decrease in methylation levels at 5942 methylation sites. Furthermore, based on Cox analysis, we identified 289 high methylation sites that were associated with adverse prognoses. Among these, Table S3 displays the 10 high methylation sites specifically related to adverse prognosis. Additionally, through annotation files, we obtained a total of 312 genes corresponding to the high methylation sites associated with adverse prognosis.

Next, we further analyzed the gene expression differences in NSCLC patients' lung tissues from the TCGA dataset. The results (Fig. 1B) showed that in NSCLC tumor tissues, there were 719 significantly upregulated genes and 1240 significantly downregulated genes. Methylation occurring in gene promoter regions can cause downregulation of gene expression. Therefore, we selected prognostic-related genes from the downregulated genes and obtained 382 low-risk genes. Taking the intersection of these 382 genes with the previously identified 312 genes related to high methylation sites, we obtained 18 intersecting genes (Fig. 1C).

Considering that combining Kaplan-Meier and Cox survival analysis can provide a more comprehensive and precise prognostic evaluation, we performed Kaplan-Meier analysis and identified 5 genes (Fig. 1D) among the 18 intersecting genes that were significantly associated with prognosis. The negative correlation between gene methylation levels and gene expression levels is an important characteristic of methylation-driven genes. The correlation analysis results revealed that among these 5 genes, except for CAT, which showed no significant correlation, the gene expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 were all negatively correlated with promoter methylation levels (Fig. 1E, Fig. S1A–B), meeting the criteria of methylation-driven genes. Moreover, existing literature has documented the tumor-suppressive roles of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 in cancer.

Overall, increased promoter methylation levels suppress CYP27A1 protein secretion, thereby promoting tumor development. In contrast, demethylation of the promoter region can induce conformational changes, enhance transcription factor binding, and activate CYP27A1 transcription and expression, ultimately exerting anti-tumor effects. Thus, we proceeded with further investigations on CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2.

Immune infiltration analysis (Fig. S2A) revealed a significant positive correlation between the methylation levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 and the infiltration of Th1 and Exhausted immune cells, both associated with immune evasion, in NSCLC tumor tissues. The results of clinical correlation analysis (Fig. 1F) indicated a significant association between low expression of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 and malignant pathological staging and tumor metastasis. Furthermore, in tumor samples with low expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, there was a remarkable enhancement in the activity of tumor-related biological pathways (Fig. S2B).

In conclusion, CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, as methylation-driven genes, are likely to play crucial roles in NSCLC, with their abnormal methylation closely associated with tumor progression and adverse prognosis.

NSCLC prognostic risk model constructed based on methylation-driven genes demonstrates significant predictive value

The construction of a tumor prognostic risk model is crucial for improving the effectiveness and efficiency of cancer treatment, and it represents a key component of precision medicine and cancer therapy research. In this study, we utilized multivariable Cox analysis to develop a prognostic risk model based on four methylation-driven genes: CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, all of which were identified as low-risk genes (Hazard Ratio <1) (Fig. 2A). By calculating the risk score for each sample using the prognostic risk model and using the median risk score as the threshold, all NSCLC patients were divided into high-risk (n = 256) and low-risk groups (n = 257). Notably, the number of deceased individuals was significantly higher in the high-risk group compared to the low-risk group among NSCLC patients (Fig. 2B and C). Kaplan-Meier survival analysis of NSCLC patients (Fig. 2D) revealed a significantly lower overall survival rate in the high-risk group compared to the low-risk group. Additionally, multivariable Cox analysis confirmed that the prognostic risk model could serve as an independent prognostic factor, regardless of other clinical characteristics (Fig. 2E). These findings collectively demonstrate the strong predictive value of the NSCLC prognostic risk model constructed based on the methylation-driven genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2.

To further validate the accuracy of the NSCLC prognostic risk model, we obtained NSCLC-associated GEO datasets from the GEO database. By applying the prognostic risk model, we classified the NSCLC patients into high-risk and low-risk groups, each consisting of 24 cases. Consistent with our previous observations, the high-risk group displayed a significantly higher number of deceased individuals compared to the low-risk group (Fig. 2F and G). Furthermore, Kaplan-Meier analysis revealed a significant difference between the two groups (Fig. 2H), indicating the accurate prognostic prediction capabilities of the NSCLC prognostic risk model based on the methylation-driven genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2.

Lastly, correlation analysis between the NSCLC prognostic risk model and clinical pathological features of NSCLC patients in the TCGA dataset yielded the following results (Fig. S3A): the high-risk group showed a significant association with malignant pathological staging and tumor metastasis. Immune-related analysis results (Fig. S3B) revealed a significant negative correlation between the high-risk group and CD4_T cells and B cells. These findings suggest that the methylation-driven genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, which were involved in constructing the NSCLC prognostic risk model, may play a crucial role in tumor progression and patient immune response.

5-Azacytidine inhibits NSCLC cell proliferation, migration, and invasion and promotes apoptosis through DNA methylation

Through bioinformatics analysis, we have identified that DNA methylation-driven genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 may play crucial roles in the occurrence and progression of NSCLC. Therefore, in order to investigate therapeutic strategies for NSCLC, our study focused on the methyltransferase inhibitor 5-azacytidine.

Firstly, we used the online tool MethPrimer (Fig. S4A–B) to predict that CpG islands within the promoter regions of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 in both humans and mice have the potential for methylation. Through RT-qPCR and MSP experiments to assess the expression and methylation levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 in human and mouse NSCLC cells, our results showed (Fig. 3A and B) compared to normal cells, the expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 were significantly reduced in HCC78 (human) and LLC (mouse) cells, accompanied by significantly increased methylation levels in the gene promoter regions. Subsequently, upon treatment with 5-azacytidine, NSCLC cells exhibited significantly upregulated expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, along with significantly decreased methylation levels in the gene promoter regions (Fig. 3C and D). This indicates that 5-azacytidine can inhibit DNA methylation of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 in NSCLC cells.

To confirm the impact of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 on the biological function of NSCLC cells, we overexpressed these genes in NSCLC cells. RT-qPCR results demonstrated (Fig. 3E, Fig. S5A) that the expression levels of these genes were significantly upregulated in HCC78 and LLC cells upon overexpression. CCK-8 and Transwell experiments showed (Fig. 3F–G, Fig. S5B–C) that overexpression of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 led to a significant reduction in the proliferation, migration, and invasion abilities of HCC78 and LLC cells. Flow cytometry results indicated (Fig. 3H, Fig. S5D) a significant increase in apoptosis capacity of HCC78 and LLC cells after overexpressing CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2. These findings suggest that overexpression of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 can inhibit the proliferation, migration, and invasion of NSCLC cells and promote cell apoptosis. Compared to the oe-NC group, overexpression of CLEC3B, CYP4B1, CYP27A1, and NR0B2 resulted in a significantly higher level of apoptosis (p < 0.05). Overexpression of CLEC3B and CYP4B1 induced a higher apoptosis rate in NSCLC cells than CYP27A1 and NR0B2. However, there was no significant difference in apoptosis levels between the CLEC3B and CYP27A1 overexpression groups in NSCLC cells. Compared to NR0B2 overexpression, CLEC3B overexpression induced significantly higher levels of apoptosis (p < 0.05). CYP4B1 overexpression showed significant differences in apoptosis levels compared to CYP27A1 or NR0B2 overexpression groups. We observed that CYP4B1, CYP27A1, and NR0B2 overexpression induced apoptosis at similar levels, while CLEC3B overexpression exhibited the strongest pro-apoptotic effect among all tested genes. These findings have been incorporated into the revised manuscript. However, due to abnormal DNA methylation, these genes are downregulated in tumor tissues, which hampers their ability to suppress cancer cells.

Finally, we further investigated the specific effects of 5-azacytidine on the biological functions of NSCLC cells. CCK-8 and Transwell experiments revealed (Fig. 3I–J, Fig. S5E–F) that after treatment with 5-azacytidine, the proliferation, migration, and invasion abilities of HCC78 and LLC cells were significantly reduced. Flow cytometry results showed (Fig. 3K, Fig. S5G) a significant increase in apoptosis capacity of HCC78 and LLC cells after 5-azacytidine treatment. This indicates that 5-azacytidine can inhibit the proliferation, migration, and invasion of NSCLC cells and promote cell apoptosis.

In conclusion, 5-azacytidine may attenuate the proliferation, migration, and invasion of NSCLC cells and promote apoptosis by reducing the DNA methylation levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 (Fig. 3L).

Construction and characterization of PD-1 protein-loaded NVs targeting tumor cells

Previous studies have shown that lipid NVs can efficiently deliver drugs to specific disease sites within the body, thereby enhancing the therapeutic effects of medications. For instance, the development of NVs (PD-1 NVs) with PD-1 receptors, in view of the specific and high-affinity interaction between PD-1 and PD-L1, represents an ideal nanoscale delivery system for targeting tumor cells.

Based on the aforementioned research, we discovered that 5-azacytidine exerts anticancer effects by reducing the methylation level of tumor suppressor genes and enhancing their expression. Based on the above findings, we discovered that 5-azacytidine exerts anti-cancer effects by reducing the methylation levels of tumor suppressor genes and upregulating their expression. The tumor suppressor genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 may play a role in NSCLC suppression through mechanisms such as inhibiting proliferation, promoting apoptosis, suppressing invasion and metastasis, modulating immune responses, and influencing signaling pathways. CLEC3B, a C-type lectin family member, may enhance anti-tumor immune responses by regulating immune cell function. CYP27A1 may affect immune regulation by modulating cholesterol metabolism and the tumor immune microenvironment, as well as regulating Vitamin D3 metabolism, which influences immune cell function and tumor-related signaling pathways. Recent studies suggest that CYP4B1 may indirectly influence tumor immunotherapy outcomes by regulating lipid metabolism, inflammatory responses, and the tumor microenvironment. NR0B2 affects tumor immunotherapy by suppressing tumor-associated metabolic reprogramming, reducing inflammatory responses in the tumor microenvironment, enhancing effector T cell activity, and inhibiting Treg function. In order to enhance the therapeutic effects of 5-azacytidine on NSCLC while minimizing damage to normal cells, we developed membrane-derived NVs carrying the PD-1 protein (PD-1 NVs) (Fig. 4A).

To obtain PD-1 NVs, we first established HEK293T cells that stably overexpress PD-1-GFP using a lentiviral packaging system. Confocal microscopy analysis (Fig. 4B) revealed that PD-1-GFP localized on the cell membrane, while NC-GFP (used as a control) mainly localized within the cytoplasm, confirming the successful localization of PD-1 protein on the cell membrane. Furthermore, Western blot analysis of GFP expression in different groups of cells (Fig. 4C) showed that PD-1-GFP fusion protein yielded a larger band in the PD-1-GFP group, while only a smaller GFP band was observed in the NC-GFP group, confirming successful expression of the PD-1-GFP fusion protein in HEK293T cells.

Subsequently, cell membranes were isolated from HEK293T cells and NVs were prepared using a polycarbonate membrane filter. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) analysis revealed that the NVs exhibited closed membrane-bound structures with a diameter of approximately 100 nm (Fig. 4D). DLS analysis demonstrated that the NVs had a diameter of around 110 nm and zeta potential of approximately −9 mv, indicating stable membrane structures with similar negative surface charges (Fig. 4E and F). Western blot analysis of PD-1 and Na/K ATPase was performed to confirm the expression of PD-1 in the NVs, which confirmed the successful construction of PD-1 NVs (Fig. 4G).

Furthermore, to meet the demand for subsequent treatment on mouse transplanted tumors without inter-species reactivity, we also established a mouse 3T3L1 cell line with high expression of mPD-1-GFP (mouse PD-1-GFP) and successfully prepared mPD-1 NVs (Fig. S6).

Validation of targeting ability of PD-1 NVs through binding with overexpressed PD-L1 in NSCLC cells

To evaluate the targeting ability of PD-1 NVs on NSCLC cells, we first examined the expression levels of PD-L1 in NSCLC cells using FACS and RT-qPCR. The results demonstrated significantly high expression of PD-L1 in HCC78 and LLC cells (Fig. 5A and B, Fig. S7A–B). Furthermore, GFP-labeled PD-1 NVs and mPD-1 NVs were separately co-incubated with HCC78 and LLC cells for 2 h, and confocal microscopy observations showed evident colocalization of PD-1 NVs and mPD-1 NVs with the cell membrane of HCC78 and LLC cells (Fig. 5C, Fig. S7C). This suggests that PD-1 NVs might competitively block the PD-L1/PD-1 immune inhibitory pathway in NSCLC cells, leading to the reactivation of T-cell immune killing function.

To validate the molecular binding between PD-1 on NVs and PD-L1 on NSCLC cells, we utilized PD-L1 antibody (aPD-L1) to block PD-L1 on NSCLC cells (Fig. 5D). Confocal images revealed a significant reduction in the binding of PD-1 NVs to the cell membrane of NSCLC cells when pre-incubated with aPD-L1 (Fig. 5E, Fig. S7D). In addition, flow cytometry data showed a significant decrease in the binding of PD-1 NVs to NSCLC cells after pre-incubation with aPD-L1 antibody (Fig. 5F, Fig. S7E). Finally, we co-incubated PD-1 NVs with NSCLC cells and performed CO-IP experiments to detect the molecular interaction between PD-1 and PD-L1. The results demonstrated the pulldown of both PD-L1 and PD-1 upon using PD-1 antibody for pulldown of PD-1 on the surface of NVs, indicating a physical interaction between PD-1 NVs and PD-L1 expressed on NSCLC cells (Fig. 5G, Fig. S7F).

In conclusion, these findings confirm the effective interaction between PD-1 NVs presenting PD-1 on their surface and NSCLC cells through binding with PD-L1.

PD-1&5-Aza_NVs promote PBMC activation and NSCLC cell apoptosis

The targeting properties of PD-1 NVs make it an attractive platform for the delivery of therapeutic drugs in NSCLC tumor treatment. Therefore, we evaluated the biocompatibility of PD-1 NVs in normal lung cells to assess its potential in biomedical applications. Experimental results revealed (Fig. 6A) that PD-1 NVs showed no significant toxicity to BEAS-2B and MLE-12 cells after 48 h of incubation, indicating its high biocompatibility.

Based on the targeting ability of PD-1 NVs to NSCLC cells and the anticancer effect of 5-azacytidine, we developed PD-1 NVs loaded with 5-azacytidine to achieve synergistic therapy for NSCLC cells, referred to as PD-1&5-Aza_NVs (Fig. 6B). Encapsulation and in vitro drug release experiments were conducted in PD-1 NVs, and the results demonstrated (Fig. 6C and D) that 5-azacytidine was encapsulated in PD-1&5-Aza NVs and mPD-1&5-Aza NVs, with encapsulation efficiencies of approximately 28 %. As the incubation time increased, 5-azacytidine was gradually released from the NVs, with a peak release time of 24 h. This indicates that PD-1&5-Aza_NVs, as a drug-carrying nanovesicle, possess suitable drug encapsulation capability and sustained release characteristics, laying the foundation for targeted therapy in NSCLC.

Next, we investigated the impact of PD-1&5-Aza_NVs on NSCLC cell apoptosis using flow cytometry, and the findings revealed (Fig. 6E) a significant time-dependent promotion of apoptosis in NSCLC cells by PD-1&5-Aza_NVs. To further study the mechanism of tumor growth inhibition by PD-1&5-Aza NVs and whether it affects the activity of immune cells, we extracted PBMCs from healthy individuals and normal C57BL/6 mice PBMCs and co-cultured them with NSCLC cells (Fig. 6F), simulating the tumor immune microenvironment. Flow cytometry was employed to assess the proliferation of PBMCs in the co-culture system, which showed (Fig. 6G) a significant increase in the proliferation capacity of PBMCs in the co-culture systems of the PD-1 NVs group and PD-1&5-Aza NVs group compared to the NC NVs group. Notably, the PD-1&5-Aza NVs group exhibited the most significant induction of PBMC proliferation, potentially attributed to the release of antigens activating PBMCs upon 5-azacytidine-induced tumor cell death. Moreover, apoptosis detection of NSCLC cells in the co-culture system showed (Fig. 6H) a significant enhancement in apoptosis capacity for the PD-1 NVs group and PD-1&5-Aza NVs group compared to the NC NVs group, with the PD-1&5-Aza NVs group displaying the most notable NSCLC cell apoptosis.

In conclusion, PD-1&5-Aza_NVs have the ability to promote PBMC activation and NSCLC cell apoptosis.

Strong targeting capability of mPD-1 nanoparticles in NSCLC tumors

Previous studies have demonstrated that tumor-targeting NVs, when administered intravenously via the mouse tail vein, achieve peak drug concentration in tumor tissues within 1 h and maintain a detectable concentration for up to 12 h. To further investigate the targeting ability of PD-1 nanoparticles in NSCLC tumors, we initially administered mPD-1 nanoparticles derived from mouse 3T3-L1 cells to a lung adenocarcinoma mouse model through intravenous injection. Tissue samples were collected 0–12 h post-administration to assess toxicity (Fig. 7A). Histological analysis using H&E staining revealed that compared to the control group, the administration of NC nanoparticles and mPD-1 nanoparticles did not induce any pathological changes in the heart, liver, spleen, or kidneys of the lung adenocarcinoma mice (Fig. 7B). This indicates that both NC nanoparticles and mPD-1 nanoparticles are non-toxic to tissues and can be safely utilized.

Subsequently, we labeled the NC nanoparticles and mPD-1 nanoparticles with the fluorescent dye Cy5 and observed the enrichment of fluorescence signals in the lung adenocarcinoma mouse tumors through intravenous injection. The results demonstrated (Fig. 7C) that, compared to the NC nanoparticle group, the mice in the mPD-1 nanoparticle group exhibited higher fluorescence intensity in their bloodstream, indicating prolonged retention of mPD-1 nanoparticles in mice. Additionally, fluorescently labeled mPD-1 nanoparticles were found to accumulate densely at the tumor site. Imaging at different time points revealed that mPD-1 nanoparticles exhibited noticeable fluorescence enrichment at the tumor site 1 h post-injection, with the fluorescence signal peaking at 4 h, whereas no significant accumulation of fluorescently labeled NC nanoparticles was observed at the tumor site (Fig. 7D and E).

These findings indicate that mPD-1 nanoparticles possess strong targeting capability toward NSCLC tumors.

Significant antitumor therapeutic effects of mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs

Studies have also shown that engineered NVs designed for cancer therapy exhibit significant anti-tumor effects approximately 20 days post-administration in mouse xenograft models. To investigate the in vivo antitumor effects of mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs, we performed group treatment on mice with lung adenocarcinoma and assessed cytotoxicity after three weeks of administration (Fig. 8A). The results revealed no significant change in the average weight of mice in each group, indicating the biocompatibility of mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs in vivo (Fig. 8B). Moreover, we studied the therapeutic effect in vivo by monitoring tumor volume and the final size and weight of excised tumors. Our findings indicate that mice treated with 5-azacytidine and PD-1_NVs exhibit slower tumor growth and a significant reduction in final tumor weight compared to the PBS or NC NVs group. Notably, mice in the mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs group demonstrated the slowest tumor growth rate and achieved the best treatment effect (Fig. 8C–E). Consistently, immunohistochemical staining of Ki67 in tumor tissue revealed that mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs exhibited a superior inhibitory effect on tumor cell proliferation compared to other groups (Fig. 8F). Flow cytometry results (Fig. 8G) showed a significant increase in the number of CD8+ T cells in tumor tissue after treatment with 5-azacytidine and PD-1_NVs compared to the PBS or NC NVs group. Furthermore, the mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs group exhibited the highest number of CD8+ T cells in tumor tissue, further supporting the conclusion that mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs can inhibit the growth of NSCLC tumors by increasing the infiltration of CD8+ T cells in tumor tissue. To further validate the anti-tumor effects of our designed PD-1&5-Aza_NVs, as well as their impact on tumor suppressor genes CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2, we conducted immunohistochemistry (IHC) analysis on lung adenocarcinoma mouse tumor tissues three weeks post-treatment to examine the protein expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 (Fig. 8H). Results showed that compared to the PBS group, the NC NVs and mPD-1 groups did not exhibit significant changes in CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2-positive cells. However, compared to the PBS group, both the 5-azacytidine group and the mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs group exhibited a notable increase in CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2-positive cells.

In conclusion, mPD-1&5-Aza_NVs demonstrate significant antitumor therapeutic effects in vivo.

Discussion

NSCLC is a prevalent and highly fatal tumor, representing the major type of lung cancer, accounting for approximately 85 % of all lung cancer cases. It includes adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. Adenocarcinoma typically arises in the lung periphery and is associated with EGFR and ALK mutations. Squamous cell carcinoma commonly occurs in central lung regions. Large cell carcinoma is highly invasive but relatively rare. The molecular mechanisms underlying lung cancer involve oncogenic driver mutations that promote cell proliferation, inhibit apoptosis, enhance angiogenesis, and facilitate metastasis. Lung cancer is currently the second most common cancer worldwide, with 2.2 million new cases reported in 2020. NSCLC accounts for the majority of cases, with a high incidence in North America, Europe, and East Asia. The disease is more prevalent in men than women, but the incidence rate in women is rising. Currently, NSCLC treatment faces several major challenges: Early diagnosis is difficult, and low-dose CT screening is not widely available in low-income countries. NSCLC is driven by multiple oncogenic mutations, with over ten known driver genes. Drug resistance remains a significant issue. Personalized treatment remains complex and challenging. The therapeutic potential of PD-1 and 5-azacytidine for NSCLC has been extensively studied. Previous research indicates that PD-1 antibodies enhance immune cell attack against tumors, while 5-azacytidine inhibits tumor suppressor gene methylation. However, the mechanism of PD-1-modified multifunctional NVs loaded with 5-azacytidine in NSCLC treatment remains insufficiently investigated. Therefore, this study aims to elucidate its molecular mechanism.

To identify treatment strategies and mechanisms with significant prognostic value for NSCLC patients, To obtain methylation and gene expression data of NSCLC patients, this study utilized the UCSC Xena database and the GEO database. Through data analysis, we successfully constructed a prognostic risk model based on methylation-driven genes in NSCLC. Additionally, NSCLC cells were subjected to RT-qPCR, MSP experiments.

Through bioinformatics analysis of genes in NSCLC patients, we found that abnormally methylated driver genes are closely associated with NSCLC tumor progression and prognosis. By constructing a prognostic risk model based on these methylation-driven genes, we were able to accurately predict the prognosis of NSCLC patients. Compared with previous studies, our analysis provides new insights and evidence in the selection of methylation-driven genes and the construction of prognostic risk models. Existing literature has confirmed that CYP4B1 is consistently downregulated in multiple types of tumors, which may contribute to tumor progression and chemoresistance. Liu et al. performed a secondary analysis using TCGA and GEO datasets and found that in lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) patients, hypermethylation at the cg23440155 and cg23414387 sites in the CYP4B1 promoter and copy number alterations (CNA) led to reduced CYP4B1 expression. This resulted in significantly decreased CYP4B1 protein levels, promoting LUAD progression. The orphan nuclear receptor gene NR0B2 is primarily expressed in liver tissues across major organs but is significantly downregulated in hepatocellular carcinoma. It has been identified as one of the most critical prognostic factors in liver cancer, regulated by multiple cellular signaling pathways. Kudryavtseva et al. confirmed through quantitative PCR experiments that NR0B2 mRNA was significantly suppressed in 80 % of clear cell renal cell carcinoma samples, and this downregulation was accompanied by high methylation levels in the NR0B2 promoter region. These findings indicate that hypermethylation of the NR0B2 promoter is a key factor leading to its downregulation, thereby promoting tumor development and progression. CYP27A1 has been reported to inhibit breast cancer progression by suppressing tumor-promoting factors. Multiple studies have shown that significant increases in promoter CpG methylation result in a marked downregulation of CYP27A1 protein expression.

Our results confirm that CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 exhibit tumor-suppressive functions in cancer. Kaplan-Meier analysis revealed that gene expression levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2 are inversely correlated with their promoter methylation levels. Thus, we conducted in vitro experiments to further validate that 5-azacytidine suppresses the proliferation, migration, and invasion of NSCLC cells by lowering the methylation levels of CLEC3B, CYP27A1, CYP4B1, and NR0B2. Additionally, we successfully prepared PD-1 NVs and confirmed their specific binding to PD-L1 on the surface of NSCLC cells. These results suggest that PD-1 NVs may promote immune attack against NSCLC cells by targeting immune evasion pathways.

To further validate the tumor-targeting capability and therapeutic effects of PD-1 NVs in NSCLC, we established a murine model of pulmonary adenocarcinoma. Our observations indicated that PD-1 NVs effectively targeted NSCLC tumors and exhibited significant anti-tumor therapeutic effects.

In recent years, nanotechnology has been increasingly applied in cancer therapy, particularly for NSCLC, including targeted drug delivery, solubility enhancement, combination therapy, gene therapy, immunotherapy, theranostics, overcoming resistance, and reducing side effects. Tian et al. developed albumin-coated Pt(IV) nano-drugs, which disrupt iron homeostasis to induce ferroptosis for NSCLC treatment. Their findings demonstrated that these Pt(IV) nano-drugs exhibit significant therapeutic potential against NSCLC by inducing ferroptosis. These NVs can concurrently target tumor cells and immune cells, achieving synergistic treatment of NSCLC by restoring the expression of tumor suppressor genes and enhancing immune cell activity. In addition, our research also provides a novel approach for screening methylation-driven genes and constructing prognostic risk models.

Based on the comprehensive studies mentioned above, the following conclusions can be tentatively drawn: PD-1&5-Aza NVs, as multifunctional NVs, can effectively target NSCLC tumors and deliver 5-azacytidine. This approach collaboratively treats NSCLC by inhibiting tumor suppressor gene methylation and immune evasion. Through an in-depth analysis of NSCLC methylation and gene expression data, this study reveals key genes associated with tumor progression and prognosis, presenting a novel strategy for treating NSCLC by delivering 5-azacytidine through PD-1-modified NVs. Furthermore, the multi-tiered experimental design offers a comprehensive perspective for understanding and validating the findings.

The scientific value of this study lies in uncovering the potential mechanisms of PD-1-modified multifunctional NVs (PD-1&5-Aza NVs) in the treatment of NSCLC. Through bioinformatics analysis, methylation-driven genes were found to be closely related to NSCLC tumor progression and poor prognosis. The constructed NSCLC prognostic risk model accurately predicts patient prognosis. In cellular experiments, it was discovered that 5-azacytidine can inhibit the proliferation, migration, and invasion of NSCLC cells by reducing methylation levels. Targeted therapy of NSCLC tumors was successfully achieved through the preparation of PD-1 NVs, and PD-1&5-Aza NVs can promote immune cell activation and tumor cell apoptosis. In vivo, animal experiments confirmed the effectiveness of PD-1 NVs and their significant anti-tumor therapeutic effects.

The clinical significance of this study lies in providing a new strategy for the treatment of NSCLC. By delivering 5-azacytidine to NSCLC tumor cells through PD-1&5-Aza NVs, the methylation modification of tumor suppressor genes and immune escape can be simultaneously inhibited, leading to improved treatment outcomes. This offers a new avenue for personalized treatment of NSCLC patients and improvement in survival rates.

However, this study has certain limitations. Firstly, it is based on cellular experiments and animal models and has not yet been clinically tested or undergone human trials to validate its therapeutic efficacy and safety. Moreover, although the study found that gene methylation drives the prognosis of NSCLC, further clinical sample verification is still necessary to confirm the reliability and generalizability of these findings. Additionally, while PD-1&5-Aza NVs demonstrated significant anti-tumor effects in vivo, the application of this technology still faces numerous challenges, such as vesicle stability, drug release, and targeting.

Looking ahead, clinical trials and human experiments can be further conducted to verify the safety and efficacy of PD-1&5-Aza NVs in treating NSCLC. Furthermore, the design of PD-1&5-Aza NVs can be further optimized, for example, by controlling the release rate and adjusting targeting to improve therapeutic effectiveness. In addition, further research can be done on the application of PD-1&5-Aza NVs in combination immunotherapy, exploring its potential to enhance immune treatment outcomes. In conclusion, PD-1&5-Aza NVs have potential clinical prospects, but further research is still needed to refine and validate their value and potential in NSCLC treatment.
