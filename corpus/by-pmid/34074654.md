# Targeting a Radiosensitizing Antibody-Drug Conjugate to a Radiation-Inducible Antigen.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: We recently discovered that anti-TIP1 antibody activates endocytosis in cancer cells, which facilitates retention of antibody and dissociation of a conjugated drug. To improve the pharmacokinetics and cancer specificity of radiosensitizing drugs, we utilized antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) that bind specifically to radiation-inducible antigen, TIP1, on non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). This approach exploits the long circulation time of antibodies to deliver a radiosensitizing drug to cancer each day during radiotherapy.

**EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN**: Antibodies to TIP1 were prioritized based on affinity, cancer-specific binding, and internalization. The lead antibody, 7H5, was conjugated with a cytotoxic drug MMAE because of its ability to radiosensitize cancer. Cytotoxicity, colony formation, and tumor growth studies were performed with 7H5-VcMMAE in combination with radiation.

**RESULTS**: 7H5 showed a high affinity to recombinant TIP1 protein and radiation-inducible TIP1 on the cancer cell surface. 7H5 undergoes endocytosis in NSCLC cells in vitro. We obtained an average drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) of 4.25 for 7H5-VcMMAE. A 70% reduction in viable cells was observed following 7H5-VcMMAE treatment compared with 7H5 alone in both A549 and H1299 cells. 7H5-VcMMAE sensitized NSCLC cells to radiation, thereby significantly decreasing the surviving fraction. The ADC combined with radiation showed a prolonged delay in tumor growth and improved survival in A549 and H1299 tumor models.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Targeting radiation-inducible TIP1 with a radiosensitizing ADC is a promising strategy to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of NSCLC. This novel approach of targeting with ADCs to radiation-inducible antigens will lead to clinical trials in lung cancer patients treated with radiotherapy.

Introduction

Non-small cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC) ranks among the most common type of malignancy and is the leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide. According to the 2020 cancer statistics, there will be an estimated 228,820 new lung cancer cases and 135,720 lung cancer deaths in the United States. Concomitant chemoradiotherapy improves local control of non-small cell lung cancer over sequential radiotherapy followed by chemotherapy. Chemotherapeutic drugs that achieve radiosensitization are emerging as promising anticancer agents. In the current study, radiation-sensitizing chemotherapy was delivered specifically to cancer using antibody-drug conjugates. ADCs have long circulation times, which improve the pharmacokinetics, thereby achieving multiple passages through cancer during radiotherapy. This approach, therefore, delivers radiosensitizing drugs specifically to cancer each day as compared to the current once-weekly administration of systemic chemotherapy for NSCLC.

ADCs utilize antibodies to cancer antigens that are found in a limited number of patients. For example, antibodies to her2/neu or EGF receptor are useful in a small subset of patients. Thus, ADCs are useful in a limited number of cancer patients. In contrast, antibodies to inducible antigens bind to most cancers and are cancer-specific. In contrast, currently used systemic radiosensitizing drugs are non-specific. This limits dosing to the once-weekly administration of lower doses of chemotherapeutic agents.

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have demonstrated unprecedented cancer-specific drug delivery. ADCs comprise three components: an antibody that recognizes a cancer-specific receptor, a cytotoxic drug, and a linker for conjugating the drug to the antibody. After binding to the cancer cells, ADCs are internalized by endocytosis, and finally, the drug is released due to the action of lysosomal proteases. Site-specific targeting using ADCs with chemically stable linkers theoretically increases the therapeutic efficacy, while side effect profiles are decreased compared to conventional systemic chemotherapy. Maytansinoids and auristatin are potent anti-tubulin drugs that have been conjugated to antibodies and approved for cancer therapy. Monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) is a maytansinoid that has shown enhanced cancer cytotoxicity in combination with radiotherapy.

We hypothesized that targeting a radiation-inducible antigen with an ADC containing a radiosensitizing drug would selectively enhance the efficacy of radiation therapy in NSCLC. We discovered Tax-interacting protein 1 (TIP1) as a radiation-inducible cancer antigen. TIP1 is a multifunctional protein involved in cellular motility, adhesion, migration, metastasis, and cancer cell viability. Increased expression of TIP-1 is associated with higher grade and poorer prognosis in patients with glioblastoma and NSCLC. TIP1 expression in cancer cells is enhanced as compared to healthy tissue. Our studies have shown that targeting TIP1 with a radioimmunoconjugate enhances the efficacy of radiation. The 7H5 antibody in this manuscript binds specifically to the TIP-1 functional PDZ domain, which activates the endocytosis of the antibody. The 7H5 antibody binding site differs from the previously published antibody studies that bind outside of the PDZ domain. Thus, the most relevant and novel drug carrier molecule that targets TIP1 is the antibody that binds to the PDZ domain, which is internalized and retained intracellularly. Endocytosis facilitates dissociation of the drug conjugates.

In this study, we developed an ADC that targets radiation-inducible TIP1 during radiotherapy for NSCLC. We coupled an anti-TIP1 monoclonal antibody to a potent radiosensitizer, MMAE, to develop a novel cytotoxic ADC. We found potent radiosensitization and cytotoxicity of NSCLC cells and tumor xenografts treated with anti-TIP1 ADC combined with radiation. Our studies, therefore, propose a novel and potent treatment strategy for NSCLC.

Materials and methods

Cells, Reagents, and Irradiator

A549, H1299, and HUVEC cell lines were obtained from ATCC (Manassas, Virginia). MRC-5 cell line was purchased from Sigma (St. Louis, MO). Human bronchial-tracheal epithelial cells (HBTEC) were obtained from LifeLine Cell Technology (Oceanside, California). All cells were grown in a 37 °C humidified incubator with 5% CO2. DMEM/F12 and RPMI media supplemented with 1% Penicillin and Streptomycin and 10% fetal bovine serum was used for A549 and H1299 cell lines, respectively. MRC-5 were grown in EMEM media supplemented with 1% Penicillin and Streptomycin, 10% fetal bovine serum, non-essential amino acids, and glutamax. EBM-2/EGM-2MV media kit (Lonza) and BronchiaLife Cell Culture Medium Complete Kit (Lifeline Cell Technology) was used for HUVEC and HBTEC, respectively. All the cell lines were tested for mycoplasma contamination using the MycoAlert™ PLUS Mycoplasma Detection Kit (Lonza) every three months and tested negative. All cell lines were disregarded after 15 passages from thawing. For in vivo injections, cells were used within 10 passages from thawing.

Mouse anti-TIP1 monoclonal antibody (7H5) was developed using hybridoma technology. VcMMAE was obtained from MedChemExpress (Monmouth Junction, New Jersey). Ionizing radiation was delivered using an x-ray irradiator (RS 2000, Rad Source, USA) with an operating voltage and current of 160 kV and 25 mA, respectively. A dose rate of 0.0682 Gy/s and 0.0167 Gy/s was used for in vitro and animal radiation experiments, respectively. Mice were anesthetized with 2% isoflurane and placed in the irradiator with shielding of the body using lead. The target tissues (hindlimb tumors) were exposed during radiation for animal experiments.

Preparation of TIP1 Antibody-Drug Conjugates

7H5 antibody was buffer exchanged into 50 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 1 mM DTPA (HEPES/DTPA) using a centrifugal concentrator (10 kDa cutoff). Partial reduction of the TIP-1 antibody in HEPES/DTPA buffer was achieved by adding TCEP (10 equivalents) at 37 °C for 24 hours. The reaction vessel containing reduced antibody was cooled to 0–4 °C, and cold acetonitrile containing VcMMAE (10 equivalents/mAb) was added to reach an organic phase of 20% (v/v) and the aqueous phase of 80% (v/v). The resulting reaction vessel containing reduced antibody and VcMMAE was then incubated at 4 °C for 18 hours. The reaction was quenched by adding N-acetylcysteine (8 equivalents per VcMMAE) and incubated for an additional 45 minutes at 4 °C. The TIP1-VcMMAE product was purified through a Zeba column (40 kDa cutoff) equilibrated with 50 mM HEPES pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and then sterile filtered through a 0.2-μm filter before storage at 4 °C.

Analysis of Anti-TIP-1 Antibody-Drug Conjugate

Hydrophobic interaction chromatography (HIC) HPLC was performed to determine the drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) as previously described. Briefly, a 4.6 mm by 35 mm with a 2.5 μm particle size butyl-NPR (TOSOH Bioscience, Tokyo, Japan) column connected to an Agilent 1260 HPLC (Santa Clara, CA, USA) was used for HPLC analysis. Buffer A consisted of phosphate-buffered ammonium sulfate (0.25 M sodium phosphate, 1.5 M ammonium sulfate, pH 7.0), and buffer B consisted of 80% (v/v) 0.25 M sodium phosphate, pH 7.0 with 20% (v/v) isopropanol. The gradient included 100% buffer A to 100% buffer B over 15 min at a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min, and resolved drug-loaded species were detected using absorbance at 280 nm and 248 nm. The percent peak area was used to calculate the DAR of 7H5-VcMMAE.

UV-vis analysis was performed for confirmation of drug-loading onto the 7H5 antibody, as previously described. Briefly, differential absorption measurements of 7H5, VcMMAE, and 7H5-VcMMAE were performed using a NanoDrop One (Thermo Scientific) spectrophotometer to measure absorbance at 280 and 248 (Supplementary Table 1).

Binding Affinity of Antibody and ADC to TIP1 using ELISA

The binding affinity of the 7H5 antibody and 7H5-VcMMAE ADC to recombinant TIP1 protein was measured using ELISA. Recombinant human TIP1 protein at 10 μg/mL in PBS (100 μL/well) was coated on ELISA plates and incubated overnight at 4 ºC. Plates were blocked with blocking reagent (3% milk in PBS-0.1% tween) for 2 hours. ADC and 7H5 anti-TIP1 antibody (5 μg/mL) were two-fold serially diluted and added to ELISA plates for 1 hour at room temperature. The plate was washed three times, with PBS-0.1% tween. Anti-mouse IgG–HRP conjugated secondary antibody (Sigma) diluted in 3% milk in PBS-0.1% tween at 100 μl/well was added for 1 hour at room temperature. The ELISA plate was washed three times with PBS-T, followed by three times wash with PBS. After the washes, 100 μl of TMB substrate (Thermo Scientific) was added to each well. The reaction was quenched by adding 100 μl/well sulfuric acid (2N). The absorbance was read at 450 nm using a spectrophotometer.

Surface Plasmon Resonance (SPR)

The affinity of anti-TIP1 antibodies to TIP1 protein was determined using the biosensor-based surface plasmon resonance (SPR) technique using an automatic apparatus BIAcore 2000 (GE Healthcare, Sweden). The recombinant eukaryotic TIP1 protein (Prospec, USA) was immobilized by amine coupling on the CM5 sensor surface (ligand), and anti-TIP1 antibodies were used as the analyte. Experiments were performed at 25°C in HBS-EP buffer (GE healthcare). TIP1 protein was immobilized using surface preparation wizard for amine coupling. After activation of the sensor surface with N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) and N-ethyl-N′−3 (diethylamino propyl) carbodiimide (EDC), TIP1 protein (50 μg/ml in 10 mM sodium acetate, pH 4.7) was injected. The residual NHS esters were inactivated with ethanolamine (50 μl) for 10 min. A blank reference surface was also prepared with the same procedure by activation with EDC/NHS and then inactivation with ethanolamine. The rate constant KD was obtained by fitting the sensogram data after reference subtraction (data from the blank channel) using BIA evaluation 3.0 software.

Flow cytometry

Monoclonal antibodies against TIP1 were analyzed for their cell surface saturable binding to calculate affinity. Anti-mouse Alexa fluor 488 secondary antibody was obtained from Invitrogen, USA. For analysis, cells were incubated with antibodies in FACS staining buffer (PBS containing 5% (vol/vol) FBS and 0.1% sodium azide) at the indicated concentrations. Flow cytometry was performed on a BD LSR II (BD Biosciences) flow cytometer. The expression level was presented as geometric mean fluorescence intensity minus the secondary antibody control. Saturation binding curves were generated using the one-site specific binding equation in the GraphPad prism software to calculate the affinity.

In-vivo Near-infrared Imaging

All animal studies were performed following the guidelines of the IACUC and with protocols approved by the Washington University Division of Comparative Medicine. The 7H5 antibody or isotype control antibody was labeled with IRDye 800CW as per manufacturer’s instructions (Li-cor). Tumors were induced by injecting A549 (2–3 × 106) cells in the bilateral hind limbs of nude mice (Charles River). When tumors reached around 1cm3, the right hind limb of all mice were irradiated with three doses of 3 Gy radiation over 24h. Lead was used to block the body and the left hind limb during radiation. The tumor-bearing mice were then injected with 0.5mg/ml (in 100μl PBS) of labeled antibodies via the tail vein. For near-infrared (NIR) imaging, the mice were anesthetized with 2% isoflurane and imaged using the Pearl Trilogy small animal imaging system (Li-cor). Organs were harvested on day 6, imaged with NIR imaging and weighed for further biodistribution analysis. Fluorescence was detected using an 800 nm channel with excitation of 785 nm and an emission of 820 nm. Images were analyzed using Image Studio software. Background subtracted signal intensity was plotted using Graph Pad Prism software.

Antibody Internalization

7H5 antibody was labeled with the pHRodo red dye (Thermo Fisher) as per the manufacturer’s instructions. To compare internalization of 7H5 in irradiated versus non-irradiated A549 cells, labeled 7H5 antibody was incubated with cells growing on coverslips for 48 hours after either 3 Gy in 3 fractions or sham radiation over 24 hours. These cells were then washed with PBS, and cell nuclei were stained using NucBlue (Invitrogen). Fluorescent images were captured using an inverted fluorescent microscope (Zeiss). Image J software was used to quantify intensity by calculating the integrated density of individual 40x images using only the red filter corresponding to the internalized 7H5 antibody. Data are presented as mean ± SD of 3 independent images per labeled antibody treatment group.

Intracellular Release of MMAE from ADC using MS Analysis

Detection of the intracellular release of free MMAE from the parent ADC was measured via MS analysis, as described previously. Briefly, A549 and H1299 cells were plated at 2.5 × 105 cells in respective media, followed by 48-hour treatment with 7H5-VcMMAE (75 μg). Cells were then washed in PBS, pelleted, and homogenized via cooling to −20 °C in 66% methanol in PBS. Cells were then precipitated by centrifugation at 10,000 rpm for 5 min. The supernatant containing MMAE was evaporated and resuspended in 33% acetonitrile in water, followed by loop-injection ESI MSn analysis using a Thermo LTQ Orbitrap Velos instrument, which was set up for high resolution (R=100,000 at m/z 400) multiple stage mass spectrometric analysis for structure identification as described previously.

Cell Viability Assay

NSCLC cells (A549 and H1299) and normal cell lines (MRC-5, HUVEC, and HBTEC) were plated in 96 well plates at a density of 2 × 103 cells/well and 5 × 103 cells/well, respectively. Cells were treated with various concentrations of 7H5 or 7H5-VcMMAE for 48 or 72 hours. MTS reagent (Promega) was added to the cells and incubated at 37 ºC for 0.5–4 hours. The plate was read using a plate reader at 490 nm. Cell viability was normalized to untreated cells and presented as SD from three treatments.

Cell Cycle Analysis

A549 and H1299 cells were plated in 6 well plates in duplicate wells. Cells were either untreated, treated with 7H5 (10 nM) or 7H5-VcMMAE (10 nM) for 24 or 48 hours. Cells were then harvested, washed with PBS, and fixed in ethanol. A549 and H1299 cells received RNAse treatment and were then stained with propidium iodide, followed by MACSQuant® flow cytometry. The analysis was performed using FlowJo software.

Colony Formation Assay

In vitro characterization of the radiation sensitizing and cytotoxic effects of ADC was performed using the colony formation assay. Cancer cells (A549 and H1299) were plated in individual wells using 6 well plates at 1.0 × 105 cells/well and treated for 48 hours with 7H5 antibody (10 nM) or 7H5-VcMMAE ADC (10 nM, 3.3 nM or 1.1 nM) in individual wells. Cells were harvested and counted for cell viability using a Vi-Cell (Beckman Coulter) automated cell counter, sub-cultured in six-well plates, followed by irradiation with 0, 3, 5, or 7 Gy. The plated cells were incubated for 7–10 days, and the colonies were stained with 0.5% crystal violet. Colonies comprising of 25 cells or more were counted under a StemiVD4 dissecting microscope (Zeiss). The survival fractions were calculated via normalizing to plating efficiency and presented as mean surviving fraction relative to control with SD from three treatments.

In vivo Tumor Xenograft Studies

All studies were performed under the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. Animal protocols were approved by the Washington University Division of Comparative Medicine. A549 and H1299 (2.0 × 106 cells) were implanted into the bilateral hind limbs of athymic nude mice. Tumors reached at least an average of 100 mm3 before treatment. Treatment groups included a vehicle (PBS) with or without radiation therapy (XRT), TIP1 antibody (100 μg per dose) with radiation, or ADC (100 μg per dose) without or with radiation. Mice were serpentine sorted with the intent that mean tumor volumes were similar at the start of the study. Tumors received 2 Gy per day for five consecutive days. The bilateral hind limbs of each mouse in the radiotherapy groups were exposed to radiation simultaneously, while the rest of the body was shielded with lead. Tail vein injections were delivered on day 1 and day 3 of radiation therapy. The tumor growth was measured using a digital caliper. The tumor size was calculated using the formula ½ x length x width2. Each treatment group contained five mice (10 tumors), except H1299 XRT alone had four mice (8 tumors). Kaplan-Meier survival curves were used to determine the efficacy of combining ADCs with radiotherapy. Humane endpoint was used as the criteria to euthanize each mouse when tumors reached an average of 2000 mm3. Additionally, mice were weighed twice weekly for toxicity assessment.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using the Student’s t-test and or one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). All analyses were performed using Prism 8 (GraphPad Software, La Jolla, CA, USA), and statistical significance is indicated in each graph where appropriate. The tumor growth data were analyzed using Tukey’s’ multiple comparisons. Statistical analysis for Kaplan-Meier survival curves was performed using the Log-rank (Mantel-Cox) test. Prism software (Graphpad) was used for statistical analysis of in vivo efficacy studies.

Results

Anti-TIP1 antibodies bind recombinant TIP1 with high affinity

We developed mouse monoclonal antibodies against TIP1. To prioritize the lead antibody for developing ADCs, we screened three anti-TIP1 antibodies for their affinity to the recombinant TIP1 protein using ELISA (Figure 1A) and Biacore (Figure 1B-D). In the ELISA assay, we coated TIP1 protein and screened the hybridoma supernatants from the three TIP1 hybridomas 7H5, 3D6, and 2F10. We observed the highest relative binding for the 7H5 supernatant. For further characterization, anti-TIP1 antibodies were purified from hybridoma supernatants using protein G affinity chromatography. Supplementary Figure 1A shows an SDS-PAGE gel picture for the purity of the antibody. We obtained approximately ninety percent pure antibody after affinity chromatography (Supplementary Figure 1A). Surface plasmon resonance was used to evaluate the affinity (dissociation constant, KD), on rate, and off rates of the purified anti-TIP1 antibodies. Figure 1B-D shows the overlay sensograms of the various concentrations of antibodies passed on the immobilized recombinant TIP1 protein. The affinity of 7H5 for TIP1 protein was 0.3 nM, and the on-rate and off-rates were 4.5 × 104 (1/Ms) and 1.4 X 10−5 (1/s), respectively (Figure 1B). Similarly, the affinity of 3D6 for TIP1 was 0.05 nM, and the on-rate and off-rates were 2 × 105 (1/Ms) and 1 X 10−5 (1/s), respectively (Figure 1C). For 2F10, the dissociation constant was calculated as 0.1 nM, and the rate constants Kon and Koff were 7.5 × 104 (1/Ms) and 1 X 10−5 (1/s), respectively (Figure 1D).

Anti-TIP1 antibody 7H5 binds specifically to cancer cells and activates endocytosis

Anti-PDZ (functional domain) antibody activates endocytosis, which facilitates retention and dissociation of ADC. We performed flow cytometry to evaluate cancer cell surface binding of anti-TIP1 antibodies (Figure 2A). A549 cells were incubated with increasing concentrations of the anti-TIP1 antibodies and acquired in a flow cytometer. We found saturable binding for all three anti-TIP1 antibodies on A549 cells (Figure 2A). The Kd values for the 7H5, 3D6, and 2F10 antibodies were 4.8, 5.95, and 32.4 nM, respectively (Figure 2A). The highest cell surface binding affinity was observed for 7H5 (Kd=4.8 nM) (Figure 2A). Based on these data, we selected 7H5 for further studies.

Endocytosis of the anti-TIP1 antibody is essential for the targeted delivery of a conjugated drug to the cancer cells expressing TIP-1 protein. We labeled 7H5 with pHRodo red dye that fluoresces at acidic pH found in the endocytic compartments of the cells. To evaluate the effect of radiation on antibody internalization, we treated irradiated A549 cells (three fractions of 3 Gy) with pHRodo-labeled 7H5 (Figure 2B). The signal intensity of pHRodo-labeled 7H5 antibody in irradiated A549 cells compared to un-irradiated cells is shown in Figure 2B. As compared to the non-irradiated A549 cells (1447 ± 188.2 IntDen), cells receiving radiation showed significantly (p=0.04) enhanced internalization of the 7H5 antibody (2092 ± 316.7 IntDen).

We evaluated the effect of radiation on the binding of 7H5 to tumors in vivo. Mice bearing bilateral hindlimb tumors underwent radiation (3 Gy x 3 fractions) to the right hind limb or received no radiation (sham) prior to treatment with 7H5 or Isotype control antibodies labeled with IRdye800. Mice were then imaged, and organs were later harvested for quantification of labeled antibody uptake for biodistribution. As seen in Figure 2C, 7H5 specifically targets the irradiated right hindlimb with a 1.4-fold increased intensity compared to the sham tumor. Non-specific tumor uptake of the isotype control antibody was observed. Furthermore, the biodistribution of the 7H5 and Isotype control antibody in Figures 2D and E shows significantly increased 7H5 (p=0.046) signal intensity/gram in the irradiated tumor compared to isotype control antibody. The mean signal intensity/gram of 7H5 in the irradiated tumor (129.5) is 1.6-fold greater than the sham irradiated tumor (82.1).

Synthesis and characterization of 7H5-VcMMAE

The synthesis of the ADC 7H5-VcMMAE involved a partial reduction of the parent 7H5 antibody, followed by conjugation via thiol-maleimide chemistry. UV-vis analysis was performed for confirmation of drug-loading onto the 7H5 antibody using NanoDrop (Supplementary Table 1), as previously described. We then characterized the 7H5-VcMMAE using HIC HPLC to calculate the drug-antibody-ratio (DAR). We synthesized 7H5-VcMMAE with an average DAR of 4.25 ± 0.76 obtained from 2 separate reactions (Supplementary Figure 1B), as assessed through HIC analysis. To characterize the 7H5-VcMMAE ADC, we performed a TIP1 protein ELISA assay to compare the affinity of the anti-TIP1 ADC to the parent 7H5 anti-TIP1 antibody. The 7H5-VcMMAE displayed decreased affinity that remained in the nanomolar range (kd= 3.19 × 10−9 M) to TIP1 protein when compared to the parent 7H5 antibody (kd= 0.34 × 10−9 M) as shown in Supplementary Figure 1C.

MMAE is released from the ADC following internalization

ADCs release the conjugated drug inside of the cells following internalization, and thereby achieve specific cytotoxicity. We used mass spectrometry to characterize the release of free MMAE from the 7H5-VcMMAE ADC. MMAE was extracted from A549 and H1299 NSCLC cells treated with ADC, followed by MS analysis. Figures 3A and B show the high resolution (HR) ESI-MS ion chromatogram of the [M+H]+ ion of MMAE at 718.51 from A549 and H1299, respectively. HR mass measurement yielded the [M+H]+ ions at m/z 718.5123 (Figure 3C), and [M+Na]+ ions at m/z 740.4942 from A549 (Figure 3D), corresponding to the elemental composition of C39H68N5O7 (calc: 718.5113) and C39H67N5O7Na (calc: 740.4933), respectively, consistent with the elemental composition of MMAE. Tandem mass spectra of the [M+H]+ ions at m/z 718.51 (Figure 3E) contained fragment ions similar to those arising from the synthetic MMAE standard (Figure 3E insets),and the MS2 spectrum of the [M+Na]+ ions at m/z 740.49 (Figure 3F) is nearly identical to that arising from the synthetic MMAE standard (Figure 3F, insets). To further confirm the structure, we conducted MS3 on the ion of m/z 506 (718 → 506) which gave rise to a MS3 (Figure 3G) identical to that arising from the standard (Supplementary Figure 1D). The fragment ions as observed in Figure 3E and 3G are consistent with the fragmentation processes proposed for the MMAE structure (Figure 3H), indicating that the isolated molecule from A549 is indeed MMAE.

7H5-VcMMAE induces cytotoxicity and enhances the efficacy of radiation in vitro

We performed flow cytometry analysis to evaluate the effect of 7H5-VcMMAE on the cell cycle (Supplementary Figure 2). A549 and H1299 cells were treated with antibody alone (10 nM) or ADC (10 nM) for 48 hours prior to cell cycle analysis. Treatment with the ADC resulted in an increase in the G2/M phase of the cell cycle in both A549 (Supplementary Figure 2A and B) and H1299 (Supplementary Figure 2C and D) cells compared to untreated and 7H5 antibody treatment. The percentage of cells in the G2/M phase for A549 was 12.7 ± 0.71 vs. 15.5 ± 0.14 vs. 24.4 ± 1.1 for untreated, 7H5, and 7H5-VcMMAE groups, respectively (Supplementary Figure 2B). For H1299 cells, we found 16.2 ± 1.1 vs. 17.5 ± 2.33 vs. 29.75 ± 1.6 percent cells in the G2/M phase for untreated, 7H5, and 7H5-VcMMAE groups, respectively (Supplementary Figure 2D).

Cytotoxicity of the ADC was assessed using the MTS and colony formation assays. A549 and H1299 cells were treated with 7H5-VcMMAE or 7H5-antibody at various doses for 72 h, as indicated in Figure 4A and 4B. The ADC 7H5-VcMMAE displayed dose-dependent cytotoxicity at 72 hours in A549 (IC50= 23 nM) and H2199 (IC50= 13 nM) NSCLC cells (Figure 4A and B). We also assessed the cytotoxicity of the free drug MMAE compared to the 7H5-VcMMAE ADC in normal cells. Conjugating MMAE to the 7H5 antibody eliminated the cytotoxicity of MMAE in MRC-5, HUVEC, and HBTEC normal cell lines (Supplementary Figure 3).

The radiation sensitizing potential of 7H5-VcMMAE in NSCLC cells H1299 and A549 was further characterized using the colony formation assay at various doses of radiation. H1299 and A549 cells were treated with 7H5 antibody alone (10 nM) and varying doses of 7H5-VcMMAE (1.11 – 10 nM) for 48 hours, followed by sub-plating and irradiation from 0–7 Gy. We chose a treatment time of 48 hours prior to sub-plating since cell cycle analysis showed a significant G2/M phase arrest in both A549 and H1299 cells when treated with 7H5-VcMMAE for 48 hours. Doses from 3.33 to 10 nM of ADC resulted in nearly complete inhibition of colony formation without radiation. Figure 4C and D show the cell survival curves for control, 7H5 (10 nM) and 7H5-VcMMAE (1.1 nM) treated A549 and H1299 cells. The A549 and H1299 clonogenic assay results strongly correlated with the linear-quadratic cell death model. Figure 4C and D show the fitted least-squares nonlinear regression analysis corresponding to the solid line in each cohort. The results show that H1299 is more radioresistant than A549 and that the addition of the ADC prior to radiation sensitizes A549 and H1299 cells to radiotherapy. The dose modifying factor (DMF) at 35% survival for A549 was 1.56 vs. 1.63 and for H1299 was 2.04 and 2.34 when comparing the 7H5-VcMMAE treatment to untreated or 7H5 treated cells, respectively.

7H5-VcMMAE delays tumor growth and improves survival in irradiated xenograft tumor models

The in vivo therapeutic efficacy of 7H5-VcMMAE was determined in nude mice bearing heterotopic hind limb human NSCLC tumors A549 and H1299 (Figure 5). Subtherapeutic doses of ADC and radiation were administered to determine whether interactive cytotoxicity is achieved. In both the tumor models, we compared the tumor growth of the following groups: radiation (XRT) alone (five fractions of 2Gy), 7H5 antibody (two doses of 100 μg antibody) with radiation (five fractions of 2Gy), and 7H5-VcMMAE (two doses of 100 μg antibody) with and without radiation therapy. The treatment efficacy was evaluated by comparing the tumor growth on day 36 for A549 tumors (Figure 5A and C). 7H5-VcMMAE in combination with XRT treatment (881 mm3) delayed tumor growth in A549 hind-limb tumors as compared to vehicle (P < 0.0001), 7H5-VcMMAE alone (P < 0.0001), XRT (P= 0.0015) and 7H5 in combination with XRT (P= 0.02) on day 36 (Figure 5C). 7H5 antibody with XRT (1502 mm3) showed no significant difference in A549 tumor growth as compared to radiation alone (1676 mm3) on day 36 (Figure 5A and C). The 7H5-VcMMAE treatment alone did not delay tumor growth as compared to vehicle-treated mice.

Likewise, mice bearing hindlimb human NSCLC H1299 tumors were treated with vehicle, XRT, 7H5 with radiation, 7H5-VcMMAE, or 7H5-VcMMAE with radiation (Figure 5B and D). A significant delay in tumor growth was observed in mice treated with 7H5-VcMMAE when combined with XRT (477 mm3), as compared to vehicle (P < 0.0001), 7H5-VcMMAE alone (P < 0.0001), XRT (P= 0.02) and 7H5 in combination with XRT (P= 0.04) (Figure 5D). We determined the survival of mice in each of the treatment groups. Figures 5E and F show the Kaplan-Meier survival curves for mice bearing A549 and H1299 tumors, respectively. 7H5-VcMMAE significantly increases the survival of tumor-bearing mice when administered during radiotherapy as compared to 7H5-VcMMAE (P< 0.001). Furthermore, mice in all groups had weight gain during the study (Supplementary Figure 4) and did not show physical or physiological features suggesting toxicity from 7H5 or 7H5-VcMMAE.

Discussion

Radiation inducible antigens increase the number of molecular targets that can be exploited for the development of therapeutic antibodies to treat cancer. Radiation-inducible antigens have been discovered through the use of differential expression using genomic and proteomic analyses. More importantly, bacteriophage-displayed recombinant peptide libraries that contain over 2 billion recombinant peptides were useful for the discovery of cancer cell surface proteins. This approach of using antibodies to inducible antigens addresses the challenge of the paucity of cancer antigens for antibody development.

Patients with NSCLC are treated with weekly systemic chemotherapy administered during radiotherapy. This limits the pharmacokinetic and cancer specificity of cytotoxic drugs that serve to enhance the efficacy of radiotherapy. ADCs are promising for targeting chemotherapeutic agents in the treatment of B cell lymphomas and HER2 expressing breast cancer. Only a few antibody-drug conjugates are currently in clinical trials for NSCLC. We describe a novel antibody that targets radiation-inducible TIP1 in NSCLC. We conjugated this antibody to the auristatin class of potent microtubule inhibitor MMAE since it enhances the efficacy of radiotherapy in preclinical models.

We screened monoclonal antibodies against TIP1 for cancer-specific binding and internalization. 7H5 was the lead anti-TIP1 antibody used for conjugating anticancer drugs because the antibody displays a high affinity to recombinant and cell surface TIP1 protein (Figures 1 and 2). 7H5 binds to radiation inducible TIP1 antigen, as seen in NIR imaging (Figure 2). We observed higher binding of 7H5 to irradiated tumors compared to un-irradiated tumors (Figure 2). The enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect is likely contributing to the signal intensity in the control isotype tumors receiving radiation. Although the increased signal is detected early after isotype control antibody administration, the antibody control washes out of the tumor and shows a lower signal at later time points. Moreover, the 7H5 anti-TIP1 antibody is internalized, thereby improving retention of the cytotoxic chemotherapy within cancer cells expressing the TIP1 antigen (Figure 2). We obtained an average drug to antibody ratio (DAR) of 4.25. Previous studies have shown that ideal pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic properties of ADCs are elicited with a DAR between 3–4, and clinically therapeutic ADCs have a similar DAR of 4. Upon internalization into the cancer cells, MMAE is released to elicit cytotoxicity. Using mass spectrometry, we confirmed the presence of free MMAE within A549 and H1299 cells after internalization of 7H5-VcMMAE (Figure 3). We performed cell cycle analysis in A549 and H1299 cells treated with 7H5-VcMMAE. We found cells arrested in the G2/M phase of the cell cycle, which is consistent with the mechanism of action of the microtubule destabilizing payload MMAE.

Previous studies have demonstrated that MMAE is a radiation sensitizing anticancer agent. Thus, we postulated that MMAE would further sensitize cancer cells to radiation therapy by targeted delivery to a radiation-inducible surface protein on cancer cells, TIP-1. In cytotoxicity assays, we observed a dose-dependent effect of 7H5-VcMMAE on A549 cells and H1299 cells. Clonogenic assays showed improved therapeutic efficacy when A549 and H1299 NSCLC cells were treated with 7H5-VcMMAE in combination with radiation therapy. The 7H5-VcMMAE radiosensitization of A549 and H1299 cancer cells is similar to published data showing decreased clonogenic survival in cancer cells targeted with an agent carrying the potent antimitotic MMAE payload. This is likely achieved through the cytotoxic interaction between radiation and MMAE, which is delivered by TIP1-specific antibody-mediated drug delivery. Our data is consistent with the literature that shows extreme radioresistance in H1299 as compared to A549.

We also investigated the normal tissue toxicity of 7H5-VcMMAE. 7H5 antibody NIR imaging shows cancer-specific binding without normal tissue binding in mice. MMAE cytotoxicity was eliminated by conjugating MMAE to the 7H5 antibody in normal human endothelial, lung fibroblast, and lung epithelial cell lines (Supplementary Figure 3). Also, administration of 7H5-VcMMAE in mice shows no toxicity (Supplementary Figure 4).

We exploited the radiation sensitizing properties of MMAE as an antibody-drug conjugate in mice bearing A549 and H1299 heterotopic tumors. Due to the increased expression of TIP1 on cancer cells post-irradiation, it can be postulated that 7H5-VcMMAE has improved uptake into cancer cells expressing TIP1 antigen resulting in an increased therapeutic ratio. Our in vivo mouse models demonstrated a significant improvement in tumor control and survival when mice-bearing A549 and H1299 tumors were treated with concurrent radiation plus 7H5-VcMMAE (Figure 5). We used subtherapeutic doses of antibody, ADC, and MMAE to evaluate the radiation sensitizing effect of our ADC. Thus, 7H5-VcMMAE alone does not show a survival benefit but does show improved efficacy with concurrent radiotherapy. Studies have shown that a single 10 Gy fraction may result in vascular damage and decreased tumor blood flow resulting in poor tumor uptake of anticancer agents. Thus, we chose a standard fractionated radiation regimen in our tumor growth delay study that is commonly used with concurrent chemotherapy in NSCLC. This study shows that tumor growth is delayed when radiation therapy is combined with 7H5-VcMMAE, as seen on day 36 and day 18 in mice with A549 and H1299 tumors, respectively. H1299 tumors grow at a faster rate compared to A549 tumors. Although H1299 shows a higher surviving fraction compared to A549 (Figure 4), the volume reduction at the initial portion of the in vivo studies (Figure 5A and B) shows that H1299 is a more radiosensitive cell line as compared to A549. The greater growth rate in the H1299 irradiated tumors may be attributed to its faster doubling time.

Combining ADCs with radiation has several advantages. ADCs have long circulation times, which improve the pharmacokinetics, thereby achieving multiple passages through cancer during radiotherapy. This approach, therefore, delivers radiosensitizing drugs specifically to cancer each day as compared to the current once-weekly administration of systemic chemotherapy for NSCLC. Combinations with radiosensitizing ADCs will allow increasing the therapeutic ratio of XRT. This approach holds the potential to reduce the toxic side effects of single agents. Anti-TIP1 antibody-drug conjugates represent a novel class of ADC that has the potential to translate to clinical trials for the treatment of NSCLC because of the guided delivery of the ADC to tumors treated with radiation therapy. Further studies evaluating the therapeutic efficacy of humanized anti-TIP1 antibodies and ADCs are needed to characterize the lead anti-TIP1-drug conjugate for clinical cancer therapy.
