# Microbial modulators of the epigenome: probiotic regulation of MiRNAs and LncRNAs in health and disease and preventive medicine.

## Abstract

Current improvements in microbiome research have illuminated the serious function of probiotics in modulating host gene expression through epigenetic mechanisms, particularly via the regulation of non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs) such as long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) and microRNAs (miRNAs). These regulatory RNAs are essential mediators of gene silencing, chromatin remodeling, and cellular signaling pathways implicated in immunity, inflammation, cancer, and neurodegenerative diseases. This review comprehensively examines current evidence on how specific probiotic strains influence miRNA and lncRNA expression, leading to beneficial outcomes in various pathological and physiological conditions. We explore the underlying molecular mechanisms by which probiotic-derived metabolites, like extracellular vesicles and short-chain fatty acids, interrelate with host transcriptional machinery and ncRNA biogenesis. Special emphasis is placed on disease models including inflammatory bowel disease, colorectal cancer, metabolic syndrome and Alzheimer's disease, highlighting the beneficial possible of targeting the gut microbiota-ncRNA axis. Moreover, we discuss the prospects for personalized microbiome-based interventions, challenges in clinical translation, and future directions for leveraging probiotic-ncRNA interactions in precision medicine. The integration of probiotics into epigenetic therapy represents a promising, non-invasive strategy for modulating gene expression and restoring homeostasis in complex diseases.

Introduction

Research has elucidated that a mere fraction, specifically below 1.5% of genes within the human genome are responsible for encoding proteins, with the majority being recorded into noncoding RNAs (ncRNAs). Long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) represent a subclass of ncRNAs characterized by a length exceeding 200 nucleotides. Historically, lncRNAs were regarded as mere “transcriptional noise” devoid of any functional significance. In recent years, substantial evidence has emerged demonstrating that lncRNAs are integral to a myriad of biological processes, including cellular senescence, apoptosis, cell cycle regulation, immune responses and differentiation, operating via mechanisms like transcriptional regulation, chromatin modification, and posttranscriptional regulation. lncRNAs are posited to serve as potential biomarkers for a spectrum of diseases. Concerning intestinal barrier functionality, lncRNA has the capacity to sustain intestinal homeostasis through diverse mechanisms, including the regulation of intestinal immunity and intestinal epithelial regeneration.

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) establish a category of diminutive non-coding RNAs, measuring 20–22 nucleotides in length, which are situated within intragenic and intergenic domains of the genome, thereby establishing a multifaceted and intricate controlling framework for the modulation of engagement and gene expression in numerous biological procedures. miRNAs exert a significant and fundamental influence on post-transcriptional gene regulation, thereby contributing to processes such as proliferation, cellular differentiation, and apoptosis, while also being implicated in the etiology of numerous gastrointestinal disorders.

The human microbiota serves a serious function in controlling host physiology, including metabolism, immune function, and neurobiology. Among its many interactions with the host, the impact of gut microbes on epigenetic instruction is gaining rising recognition. Epigenetic regulation occurs through several mechanisms, including chromatin remodeling, transcriptional control, and post-transcriptional modulation. In this context, long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) and microRNAs (miRNAs) act as key regulatory molecules that participate in these processes by influencing chromatin structure, guiding transcriptional complexes, and modulating mRNA stability and translation. They are also vital in monitoring gene expression without changing the underlying DNA sequence and are now understood to be key players in disease and health, including neurodegeneration, cancer, metabolic syndromes, and inflammatory disorders. Recent advances have revealed that probiotics, live microorganisms that confer health benefits when administered in adequate amounts, can modulate the expression of lncRNAs and miRNAs in host cells, influencing cellular pathways associated with apoptosis, inflammation, oxidative stress, and tissue repair.

Despite accumulating evidence linking probiotics to the modulation of non-coding RNAs, a comprehensive synthesis of how specific probiotic strains influence miRNA and lncRNA expression in various diseases is lacking. Understanding this relationship is crucial for the development of probiotic-based interventions that leverage epigenetic mechanisms for therapeutic purposes. The present review aims to complete this gap by critically evaluating the present knowledge on probiotic-mediated regulation of miRNAs and lncRNAs across different physiological systems and disease contexts. By uncovering these interactions, we aim to highlight new frontiers in microbiome-based epigenetic therapy and pave the way for personalized microbial strategies in precision medicine.

MiRNAs as regulators of the gut microbiota (GM)

miRNAs represent the most extensively investigated class of molecules, functioning to control gene expression via their contact with the 3′ untranslated areas of target mRNA. These miRNAs are classified as ncRNA entities, typically encompassing 20–22 nucleotides in length, and they are integral to the regulation of gene expression. miRNAs engage with complementary sequences within mRNAs, leading to either the deprivation of mRNAs or the suppression of translation, thereby influencing the protein synthesis that originates from specific genes. The post-transcriptional controlling mechanism is necessary for a myriad of biological processes, encompassing cell differentiation, developmental processes, apoptosis, cell cycle regulation, as well as responses to immune challenges and environmental stressors. Dysfunctions in the regulatory system are implicated in a variety of diseases, including neurological disorders, cardiovascular diseases (CVD), oncological conditions, and inflammatory diseases. Disruptions in the GM, predominantly influenced by gene expression designs, have been correlated with gastrointestinal diseases and circumstances like cancer, diabetes, CVD, obesity, and neurodevelopmental disorders. The interaction between GM and the immune cells of the intestine encompasses a plethora of molecular constituents, wherein specific categories of RNA serve as pivotal physiological regulators that are fundamental to both health and pathological conditions. Upon the stimulation of intestinal cells by probiotics, miRNAs are concurrently expressed, which fulfill crucial modulatory roles, particularly in mediating anti-inflammatory responses within host cells. Consequently, miRNAs are instrumental in the instruction of immune responses and the preservation of intestinal homeostasis.

Formation, Processing, and regulatory functions of LncRNAs

The majority of lncRNAs, characterized by the presence of 3′-end poly(A) tails and 5′-end m7G caps, are synthesized by RNA polymerase II, while a minor subset of lncRNAs is synthesized by other RNA polymerases. In contrast to messenger RNAs (mRNAs), lncRNAs exhibit limited transcriptional splicing, which culminates in the addition of a minor fraction of chromatin, followed by dilapidation via exosomes within the nucleus, ultimately resulting in the persistence of this fraction of lncRNAs in the nuclear compartment. These residual fractions of lncRNAs possess the ability to interact with chromatin, histones, and gene loci located within the nucleus, thus serving to modulate chromatin architecture and functionality, in addition to influencing the transcription of adjacent or remote genes. Furthermore, the majority of lncRNAs are translocated to the cytoplasm by nuclear RNA export factor 1 utilizing a mechanism that bears resemblance to the export process of mRNAs. LncRNAs possess the capacity to modulate the activities of enhancers, promoters, and transcription factors related to target genes, consequently facilitating either the upregulation or downregulation of the parental genes or other genes. Furthermore, lncRNAs serve an important role in the regulation occurring post-transcriptionally. LncRNA is instrumental in mediating the cleavage and consequent degradation of mRNAs via its interaction with specific proteins. Additionally, lncRNAs are capable of competitively binding to miRNAs, thus influencing the control of mRNAs through the act of miRNAs. lncRNA H19 has been proved to engage in numerous pathological procedures via diverse mechanisms. In the context of gut pathologies, H19 actions as a molecular sponge for miRNAs, including let-7 g, miR-34a, and miR-874, thereby influencing the functionality of the intestinal barrier through the modulation of genes related to epithelial regeneration.

An investigation examined the influence of Bifidobacterium longum (BL) (isolated from the breast milk of women) on tumor suppressor and oncogenic miRNAs, alongside miRNAs targeted towards IL-6 and IL-1β, within a murine model of CRC that was created in the context of inflammatory ulcerative colitis. BL intake resulted in a substantial reduction of the oncomiRs miR-155 and miR-21a, which are known to modulate IL-1β and IL-6 expression, in both healthy and CRC-afflicted murine subjects. Furthermore, BL improved the induction of the tumor suppressor miRNAs miR-15a and miR-145 across both experimental groups. In addition, BL was observed to diminish the expression of miR-146a, a regulator of IL-6 and IL-1β, in both the CRC and healthy mice. Within the CRC murine model, BL resulted in a notable decrease in the elevated NF-kB levels. While BL administration increased IL-6 and IL-1β concentrations in the healthy and CRC mice, it elevated IL-1β concentrations while concurrently decreasing IL-6 levels.

The investigation assessed the impact of Bifidobacterium bifidum and Lactobacillus acidophilus probiotics on the modulation of specific miRNAs (miR-135b, miR-26b, miR-155, and miR-18a) along with their corresponding target genes (APC, KRAS, PTEN, and PU.1) utilizing a murine model of colon carcinoma elicited by azoxymethane (AOM). A cohort of 38 male BALB/c mice was stratified into 4 distinct groups: AOM-treated, Lactobacillus acidophilus-treated, control, and Bifidobacterium bifidum-treated. With the exception of the control group, all remaining groups were administered AOM to facilitate the development of colon cancer, while the probiotics were provided over a duration of five months. The findings indicated that within the AOM group, there was an upregulation of miR-135b, miR-155, and KRAS expression, contrasted by a downregulation of miR-26b, PU.1, miR-18a, APC, and PTEN expression when compared with the control cohort. Probiotic intervention was related to a reduction in miR-135b, miR-155, and KRAS expression, whereas it was linked to an elevation in the expression of miR-26b, PU.1, miR-18a, APC, and PTEN, thereby implying a plausible protective function of probiotics intake in the context of colon cancer. The investigation assessed the effects of the gut anti-inflammatory impacts of Saccharomyces boulardii within a murine model of colitis precipitated by dextran sodium sulfate (DSS), with a particular emphasis on the modulation of miRNA expression and alterations in GM ingredient. Mice were administered Saccharomyces boulardii (5 × 10⁹ CFU) for a duration of 26 days, with DSS incorporated into the drinking water to induce colitis subsequent to a two-week period. Probiotic intake caused a substantial reduction of colonic damage, as evidenced by reduced Disease Activity Index (DAI) scores and elevated colonic weight-to-length ratios compared with the control group. Furthermore, the treatment instigated modifications in the expression profiles of inflammatory markers, proteins associated with epithelial integrity, and miRNAs. In addition, Saccharomyces boulardii was found to augment bacterial diversity and alleviate the dysbiosis engendered by DSS-induced colitis. These results suggested that Saccharomyces boulardii created gut anti-inflammatory impacts in the context of DSS-induced colitis, potentially via mechanisms of immune control and enhancements in both GM and miRNA expression, which may be beneficial in the management of the pathogenesis associated with IBD.

A preliminary investigation involving cART-treated individuals infected with HIV-1, who exhibited sustained viral suppression, explored the implications and safety of high-concentration probiotic intake (Vivomixx®/Visbiome®) on neurocognitive capabilities. Neuropsychological evaluations demonstrated marked enhancements in memory retention, visual-spatial processing, verbal fluency, emotional awareness, levels of anxiety, and perceptual timing throughout the intervention period. Nevertheless, concentrations of miRNA-29a-c within cerebrospinal fluid remained stable, albeit displaying considerable individual variability. These outcomes indicate prospective neurocognitive advantages associated with probiotic supplementation, while not influencing miRNA-29 levels within the central nervous system. In another investigation, the probiotic properties of leuconostoc mesenteroides (extracted from conventional dairy products) were evaluated. HT-29 cells were exposed to the conditioned medium of the specified bacteria, and the phenomenon of cellular apoptosis was analyzed at the cellular levels. According to our results, L. mesenteroides improved apoptosis in a colon cancer cell line via the upregulation of MAPK1, Bax, and caspase 3, while simultaneously downregulating the expressions of AKT, NF-κB, Bcl-XL, along with several pivotal oncomiRNAs such as miRNA-200b and miRNA-21 significantly. An investigation was conducted to determine the anti-inflammatory effects of Lactobacillus fermentum and Lactobacillus salivarius utilizing a murine model of colitis, with an emphasis on miRNA expression and the ingredients of GM. Both probiotic strains were found to mitigate colonic damage and enhance immune-related biomarkers, alongside elevating the expression levels of miR-223 and miR-155. Notably, L. fermentum uniquely reinstated of miR-143 and miR-150 expression, which are critically linked to the preservation of intestinal barrier integrity. Furthermore, it demonstrated a more pronounced enhancement of microbiota diversity (as indicated by Pielou evenness, Chao richness, and the Shannon index), augmented populations of SCFAs and lactic acid-producing bacteria, reinstated regulatory T cells, and the balance of Th1/Th2 cytokines. In summary, L. fermentum exhibited markedly superior anti-inflammatory and microbiota-modulating effects in comparison to L. salivarius.

The another work examined the influence of several Bifidobacterium strains on the expression of intestinal miRNA and the regulation of gene activity. The findings revealed that Bifidobacterium bifidum MIMBb75, in contrast to B. longum NCC2705 and NCC390, markedly enhanced miR-148a expression in both Caco-2 cell lines and the cecum of murine models. This upregulation was observed following a brief exposure period (1–4 h in vitro; 2 days in vivo) and was correlated with a decrease in the expression levels of its target gene, EPAS1. The silencing of miR-148a reinstated the expression of EPAS1, thereby substantiating the regulatory role of miR-148a. Also, B. bifidum MIMBb75 caused an effect on intestinal gene expression mediated through the action of miR-148a. Another investigation examined the immune and miRNA responses of human monocytic (THP-1) and epithelial (T84) cells to Escherichia coli Nissle 1917 (EcN) and the pathogenic E. coli EPEC strain E2348/69. Infections initiated from the basolateral aspect elicited more robust cytokine (IL-8, CXCL1) and miRNA responses compared with apical infections. Notably, EcN stimulated a significant pro-inflammatory response when administered basolaterally. miR-146a expression in THP-1 cells varied according to the bacterial strain, implying a selective modulation of TLR/NF-κB signaling pathways. EcN mutants deficient in flagellin demonstrated diminished cytokine induction, underscoring the importance of flagellin in the activation of immune responses. Furthermore, T3SS-deficient EPEC mutants elicited enhanced cytokine expression relative to EPEC, demonstrating that T3SS may serve a function in the attenuation of host immune signaling.

A comprehensive investigation was conducted to evaluate the impact of Lactobacillus acidophilus (La) on cellular apoptosis and miRNAs expression within human umbilical vein endothelial cells subjected to exposure from Escherichia coli lipopolysaccharide (LPS). A variety of La-derived formulations, including aqueous extracts, conditioned media, as well as both filtered and unfiltered supernatants, were systematically assessed. The La-conditioned media (LaCM) exhibited a statistically significant reduction in both apoptosis and necrosis, alongside a decrease in the pro-inflammatory miRNA-155, while concurrently enhancing the levels of the anti-apoptotic miRNA-21. Notably, inverse correlations were identified between necrosis and miR-92a, miR-21, and miR-155 concentrations. These results exhibited that Lactobacillus acidophilus may serve a regulatory function in maintaining endothelial cell viability and modulating atherosclerosis-associated miRNAs, thereby highlighting its prospective utility as a preventive intervention for cardiovascular pathologies. A research investigation examined the impact of Enterococcus faecium NCIMB 10,415 on miRNA and gene expression within the ileal and jejunal lymphatic tissues of piglets, employing next-generation sequencing methodologies. The probiotic was found to significantly enhance miR-423-5p expression, while concurrently downregulating its immune-related target genes, IGKC and IGLC. A temporal inverse correlation between miR-423-5p and IGLC expression was documented, and luciferase assays substantiated IGLC as a direct target of miR-423-5p. These findings suggested the immunomodulatory potential of E. faecium through the regulation of miRNA.

Another investigation examined the miRNA profile present in human breast milk and assessed the potential for miRNAs to mediate the protective effects of probiotics in relation to atopic dermatitis (AD). Through the utilization of small RNA sequencing conducted on samples from 54 participants in the ProPACT trial, a consistent array of prevalent miRNAs was discerned, including miR-148a-3p, miR-22-3p, and let-7b-5p. Although certain miRNAs demonstrated differential expression between the probiotic and placebo cohorts, as well as between AD and non-AD infants, none achieved statistical significance when corrections for false discovery rate were applied, thereby leaving their roles in the prevention of AD ambiguous. The investigation revealed that prolonged exposure to ethanol in the situation of alcoholic liver disease enhanced the levels of intestinal miR-122a, which subsequently led to a downregulation of occludin expression and compromises the integrity of the intestinal barrier. Administration of LGGs led to a reduction in miR-122a levels, a restoration of occludin expression, and a mitigation of ethanol-induced hepatic injury in murine models. In a similar vein, in vitro studies indicated that overexpression of miR-122a led to a reduction of occludin levels, while the inhibition of miR-122a reinstated occludin expression, thereby underscoring the significance of miR-122a in the regulation of gut permeability.

Another investigation assessed the influence of LGG and Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus (L.del) on TLR4 expression and associated signaling molecules in dendritic cells (DCs) derived from human monocytes. The findings indicated that even upon inactivation, these probiotic strains were capable of decreasing TLR4 expression, akin to the effects observed with lipopolysaccharides. Notably, LGG exhibited a significant down-regulation of p38 expression and modulated immune responses at the post-transcriptional levels via the alteration of miRNA expression. In particular, LGG diminished the levels of miR-146a (a known regulator of NF-κB) while enhancing miR-155, which is consistent with the seen down-regulation of p38 in DCs treated with LGG. A research examined the barrier-enhancing properties of the Escherichia coli Nissle 1917 (EcN) concerning transepithelial resistance and its influence on miRNA regulation. The miRNA profiling conducted on T84 monolayers, prior to and subsequent to co-incubation with EcN, exhibited the differential regulation of miR-483-3p, miR-203, and miR-595. These specific miRNAs serve a serious function in controlling the expression of both structural and regulatory constituents of tight junction complexes, thereby enhancing barrier functionality. The application of targeted miRNA inhibitors effectively mitigated the disturbance of tight junctions instigated by enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC). The findings of investigation assert that these miRNAs significantly affect the regulatory and structural proteins associated with junction complexes, consequently bolstering the barrier function attributable to EcN.

Overall, probiotics represent a promising class of microbial modulators capable of influencing host gene expression through the regulation of microRNAs (Fig. 1). By modulating key miRNA pathways, probiotics can affect numerous pathological and physiological processes, including metabolism, inflammation, immunity, and cancer development. Understanding these mechanisms not only deepens our insight into host–microbe interactions but also opens new avenues for innovative therapeutic strategies based on miRNA-targeted modulation.

The beneficial effects of probiotics on LncRNAs modulation

lncRNAs have emerged as crucial epigenetic regulators involved in diverse biological processes, including gene expression, chromatin remodeling, immune responses, and disease progression (Table 2). Recent research has observed that the GM—and especially probiotics—can influence host lncRNA expression, thereby modulating cellular signaling pathways and physiological outcomes. This regulatory capacity of probiotics suggests a novel and underexplored layer of microbiota-host interaction with significant implications for human health. Understanding how probiotics modulate lncRNAs provides critical insights into the mechanisms by which microbial signals help to disease pathogenesis and health maintenance, including cancer, metabolic disorders, neurodegeneration, and inflammatory diseases. This field offers promising potential for developing microbiome-based interventions that target specific lncRNAs to restore epigenetic balance.

Colon cancer represents a predominant contributor to mortality associated with cancer on a global scale and has been progressively correlated with the GM. Clostridium butyricum (CB) has exhibited significant possible in controlling the behavior of colon cancer cells, particularly through the regulation of mRNAs and lncRNAs. A work investigated the impact of CB on mRNAs and lncRNAs expression in SW480 colon cancer cell lines and their relationship with apoptosis. Co-culturing with CB led to notable changes in lncRNAs and mRNAs expression, with 50 lncRNAs being upregulated and 152 lncRNAs downregulated by higher than five-fold. In a similar vein, 738 mRNAs were found to be upregulated, whereas 1,088 mRNAs were downregulated. The analysis of apoptosis indicated that treatment with CB prompted apoptotic activity in SW480 cells, substantiated by the upregulation of pro-apoptotic genes like CASP1, TNF, and BNIP3L, coupled with the downregulation of anti-apoptotic members of the BCL family. Pathway analysis implied the involvement of the MAPK signaling pathway, interactions between cytokines and their receptors, along with other pathways implicated in tumor progression. NAFLD/NASH has appeared as a significant global health issue, currently lacking an FDA-sanctioned treatment, thereby necessitating the investigation of innovative therapeutic modalities for NASH. Probiotics are recognized as a crucial adjunctive therapy in the management of NASH. Zbiotics (ZB183) represents the inaugural commercially available genetically modified probiotic. A subsequent study was conducted to assess the potential therapeutic impacts of Zbiotics intake on the management of NASH through the modulation of the cGAS-STING-signaling pathway-related RNA network. In silico data analysis was executed, resulting in the identification of three differentially expressed genes along with their respective epigenetic regulators (lncRNA RABGAP1L-DT-206 and miR-6888-5p miRNA). The oral administration of Zbiotics over a four-week duration resulted in the downregulation of the cGAS-STING-related network expression in models of NASH. Additionally, Zbiotics demonstrated a capacity to ameliorate hepatic steatosis and inflammation, as evidenced by a significant enhancement in the NAFLD Activity Score (NAS) and a reduction in hepatic TNF-α levels. Moreover, Zbiotics displayed beneficial impacts on colonic health, characterized by an elevation in crypt length, a reduction in inflammatory cell infiltration, and a restoration of occludin expression in the colonic mucosa.

Another investigation delineated a genetic-epigenetic network associated with hepatic stellate cell (HSC) signaling as a modulator of NASH and NAFLD pathogenesis, subsequently evaluating the impacts of Mutaflor® on this network. Initially, in silico methodologies were employed to construct a genetic-epigenetic network related to HSC signaling. Subsequently, a study involving rat models, incorporating a high-fat high-sugar diet induced NASH and Mutaflor® treated subjects, was devised. The experimental protocols encompassed histopathological and biochemical assessments of rat blood and liver specimens. Administration of Mutaflor® significantly mitigated the biochemical and histopathological perturbations induced by NAFLD/NASH. Stimulation of HSCs, IL-6 and TGF-β expression were attenuated through the inhibition of the hedgehog and hippo signaling pathways. These pathways may have been suppressed via the upregulation of RPARP AS-1 lncRNA, which subsequently downregulated the expression of miR-650, FOXA2 mRNA, and TEAD2 mRNA, while simultaneously upregulating LATS2 mRNA expression. A research investigation was conducted to examine the lncRNA-miR-mRNA molecular network in response to the intake of L. acidophilus in subjects diagnosed with rectal cancer. The results demonstrated a set of 12 differentially expressed miRNAs, 11 lncRNAs, and 12 genes identified in rectal cancer subjects. An important upregulation of the selected tumor suppressor miRNAs, lncRNAs, and genes, along with a notable downregulation of the selected oncomiRs, oncogenes, and onco-lncRNAs was observed following probiotic intake in comparison to the placebo cohort. There existed a robust correlation among several components of the network, such as between the IGF1 gene and miR-133b, the MSH2 gene and miR-548ac, as well as miR-21 and the SMAD4 gene. In rectal cancer subjects, L. acidophilus intake was linked to enhanced expression within the lncRNA-miR-mRNA network, potentially offering innovative avenues for monitoring and therapeutic strategies.

Recent investigations underscore the significant role of microbiota in a variety of pathological conditions, including neoplastic diseases such as cancer. Researchers have developed a competing endogenous RNA (ceRNA) network utilizing data from the GEO and TCGA databases to investigate the mechanisms by which microbiota may influence the etiology of CRC. They discerned a total of 8 miRNAs, 75 lncRNAs, and 9 mRNAs within the probiotics-mediated ceRNA network, alongside 4 miRNAs, 49 lncRNAs, and 3 mRNAs in the pathobiont-mediated network, both of which may serve a function in the progression and initiation of cancer. Functional enrichment analyses indicated a significant involvement in metabolic processes, particularly highlighting enrichment within the p53 signaling pathway and microRNA-related oncogenic pathways. A four-gene signature, comprising FRMD6-AS2, DIRC3, MRPL23-AS1, and LIFR-AS1, was proposed as an independent prognostic biomarker, while an additional four lncRNAs— KCNQ1OT1, HOTAIR, LINC00355, and LINC00491—were associated with adverse survival outcomes. Furthermore, 3 candidate therapeutic agents with anticancer properties were identified, and another four-gene ensemble (FAM129A, PMAIP1, BCL2, RPS6) exhibited a significant correlation with levels of immune infiltration. A research investigation was done to analyze the expression profiles of lncRNAs in rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) subjected to functional dietary regimens that incorporated prebiotics and probiotics over a duration of 30 days. Employing Illumina-based whole transcriptome sequencing methodologies, the researchers scrutinized 880 previously uncharacterized lncRNAs alongside 9067 annotated lncRNAs and protein-coding genes. Notable alterations in gene expression were predominantly recorded within the probiotic cohort, which indicated a twofold downregulation of lncRNAs in comparison to alternative dietary groups. Furthermore, distinct dietary-specific variations were identified in the expression patterns of coding genes implicated in a multitude of metabolic pathways, whereas the genomic co-localization between lncRNAs and coding genes was consistently maintained across all dietary interventions.

A comprehensive investigation assessed the prophylactic impacts of GM-oriented interventions on NASH by specifically targeting the Hippo signaling cascade and its associated epigenetic modulators (lncRNA SRD5A3-AS1and miR-1205) in a rodent model subjected to a HSHF dietary regimen. The oral administration of a multistrain probiotic formulation, in conjunction with prebiotic inulin, whether utilized independently or in combination, resulted in a significant reduction of steatosis, inflammatory responses, and fibrotic development. These therapeutic approaches also facilitated a decrease in serum liver enzyme activities, enhancement of lipid metabolic profiles, downregulation of miR-1205 and YAP1, and upregulation of NF2, LATS1, and SRD5A3-AS1 expression levels. Furthermore, there was a notable decline in the levels of IL6 and TGFβ1, alongside a decrease in α-SMA and an increase in the protein levels of LATS1/2 within hepatic tissues. A comprehensive investigation examined the protective properties of LGG alongside its modulation of lncRNA SNHG17 in the context of radiation-induced pulmonary fibrosis (RIPF). LGG led to a significant reduction of SNHG17 expression in irradiated A549 lung cells, consequently mitigating the effects of radiation-induced epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT). The overexpression of SNHG17 was correlated with unfavorable survival outcomes in lung cancer patients and was observed to stabilize PTBP1, which subsequently activates Notch1 signaling, exacerbating EMT and fibrosis. In contrast, the silencing of SNHG17 led to the suppression of PTBP1 and Notch1, thereby reversing these detrimental effects. Additionally, LGG was found to enhance apoptosis and induce G2/M cell cycle arrest following radiation exposure. In an in vivo model, LGG was shown to decrease SNHG17 expression and ameliorate lung fibrosis in RIPF mice, underscoring the therapeutic implications of targeting the SNHG17/PTBP1/Notch1 signaling axis.

An investigation was conducted to ascertain whether probiotics (GSL#5) possess the capacity to ameliorate skeletal muscle dysfunction that is associated with hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) deficiency. Utilizing CBS+/− murine models, which simulate H₂S deficiency, the researchers documented heightened intestinal permeability, systemic inflammatory responses characterized by enhanced TNF-α and IL-17 concentrations, along with alterations in GM structure. The investigation revealed a notable upregulation of lncRNA-PVT1 within the soleus muscle (4.2-fold), a phenomenon instigated by H₂S deficiency through the augmented binding of C/EBP-α to the PVT1 promoter region. It was established that lncRNA-PVT1 interacts with TNIP1, thereby facilitating the stimulation of NF-κB and the consequential increase of muscle atrophy biomarkers atrogin-1 and MuRF-1. The overexpression of lncRNA-PVT1 was demonstrated to make muscle atrophy both in vivo and in vitro, whereas its genetic knockout significantly mitigated muscle wasting. Notably, probiotic administration was found to diminish these adverse effects, thereby indicating a potential protective role in the preservation of muscle health through the modulation of lncRNA-PVT1 and inflammatory signaling pathways. A research investigation examined the anticancer properties of green-synthesized Lactobacillus plantarum-loaded nickel oxide nanoparticles (LpNiONPs) in the context of CRC by specifically targeting the vitamin D receptor (VDR) and the oncogenic lncRNA EIF3J-DT. Mutations in VDR and its downregulation, frequently influenced by specific lncRNAs, have been implicated in the advancement of CRC. Characterization methodologies substantiated that LpNiONPs exhibited stability, a positive charge, and a square morphology. In vitro assays conducted on HT-29 CRC cells demonstrated that LpNiONPs displayed a dose-dependent cytotoxic effect, suppressed cell migration, and triggered apoptotic pathways. Mechanistically, LpNiONPs led to the downregulation of lncRNA CYP24A1 and EIF3J-DT as well as the anti-apoptotic protein BCL2, while concurrently upregulating VDR and pro-apoptotic markers such as caspase-9, caspase-3, and BAX. Furthermore, an active compound derived from L. plantarum (Pyrrolo(1,2-a)pyrazine-1,4-dione), was identified to exhibit a strong binding affinity for VDR, thereby suggesting its therapeutic potential.

Overall, probiotics are increasingly recognized as key modulators of host epigenetic regulation, including the expression and function of lncRNAs. By influencing lncRNA-mediated pathways, probiotics can affect inflammation, metabolism, immune responses, and disease progression. Unraveling these interactions offers novel insights into host–microbe communication and highlights the potential of probiotic-based strategies in precision medicine. Continued research in this field may lead to innovative approaches for disease prevention, diagnosis, and therapy through targeted epigenetic modulation (Fig. 2).

Future research directions and limitations

Despite the growing body of evidence linking probiotics to the regulation of host miRNAs and lncRNAs, several important limitations remain. Most existing studies rely on in vitro systems or animal models, which may not fully capture the complexity of human microbiome–epigenome interactions. Strain-specific effects are often inconsistently reported, and the exact microbial metabolites or extracellular vesicle components responsible for ncRNA modulation remain poorly defined. Additionally, many findings are based on predictive bioinformatics analyses without experimental validation of direct ncRNA–target interactions. Human studies are limited in number, sample size, and methodological consistency, making it difficult to generalize results across diverse populations, diets, and disease backgrounds. Furthermore, inter-individual variability in microbiome composition significantly affects reproducibility and translation to clinical settings. Addressing these gaps will require standardized methodologies, mechanistic validation, and well-controlled clinical trials to confirm the therapeutic relevance of probiotic-driven ncRNA modulation. Future studies should focus on advancing beyond associative findings to achieve rigorous experimental and mechanistic validation of probiotic–ncRNA interactions. Although numerous miRNAs and lncRNAs have been computationally predicted or statistically correlated with probiotic exposure, direct confirmation of their molecular targets, through approaches such as reporter assays, RNA immunoprecipitation, CRISPR-based perturbation, and metabolite–ncRNA interaction studies, remains essential for establishing causality. Strain-specific effects also warrant deeper investigation, as different probiotic strains, even within the same species, can produce distinct extracellular vesicles, metabolites, and gene-regulatory signals. Elucidating these strain-level differences will help identify the most therapeutically relevant microorganisms and clarify the mechanisms driving ncRNA modulation. Furthermore, translating these insights into clinical practice requires well-designed human trials that incorporate multi-omics profiling, standardized dosing strategies, and long-term follow-up to determine safety, efficacy, and inter-individual variability. Integrating ncRNA biomarkers into clinical studies may also facilitate patient stratification and enable precision microbiome-based therapies. Ultimately, advancing the mechanistic understanding and clinical validation of probiotic-mediated ncRNA regulation will be critical for harnessing this emerging axis as a viable therapeutic strategy in inflammatory, metabolic, neurodegenerative, and neoplastic diseases.

Conclusions

Emerging evidence strongly supports the concept that probiotics exert profound effects on host health not only through traditional mechanisms like immune modulation and gut barrier reinforcement, but also by regulating the epigenetic landscape—particularly through modulation of lncRNAs and miRNAs. These ncRNAs are fundamental regulators of gene expression, inducing processes like cell proliferation, apoptosis, inflammation, and differentiation in various tissues. Probiotics have been indicated to effect the expression of disease-associated miRNAs and lncRNAs in models of cancer, neurological disorders, metabolic diseases, and gastrointestinal pathologies, suggesting a powerful axis of microbiome–epigenome interaction. Despite the promising findings, much residues to be uncovered about signaling pathways and the specific microbial metabolites that intercede these effects, as well as the consistency of these changes across different probiotic strains and disease contexts. The integration of probiotics into epigenetically guided therapeutic strategies holds significant promise for personalized medicine, especially when combined with omics technologies and biomarker-based diagnostics. Overall, probiotics represent a novel class of epigenetic modulators capable of fine-tuning host gene expression via non-coding RNA regulation. Advancing this field could open up transformative avenues for prevention and treatment across a broad range of chronic diseases, reinforcing the concept of the microbiota as a dynamic and accessible target for precision health interventions.
