# Novel NRF2-activated cancer treatments utilizing synthetic lethality.

## Abstract

The KEAP1-NRF2 pathway regulates the main inducible cellular response to oxidative and electrophilic stresses. Activating mutations in the KEAP1-NRF2 pathway occur commonly in human cancer, where they contribute to the formation of aggressive tumours that are associated with a poor prognosis for patients. An important clinical feature of these tumours is their defiance to all current anti-cancer treatment regimens, highlighting the need for the development of new therapeutic strategies to target NRF2-activated cancers. In this review, we discuss the mechanisms through which acquired NRF2 hyperactivation can result in resistance of tumours to immune checkpoint inhibitor therapies in addition to classical chemotherapeutics, and propose with examples that using a synthetic lethal strategy mediated by NRF2-target gene-dependent bioactivation of prodrugs represents a promising strategy to specifically enhance toxicity to heretofore untreatable NRF2-hyperactivated human tumours.

INTRODUCTION

The KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway plays a central role in prosurvival cellular responses to oxidative and electrophilic stresses. Under basal, non‐stressed conditions, the transcription factor NRF2 is maintained at low levels due to its negative regulation by the E3 ubiquitin ligase adapter KEAP1, which targets NRF2 for ubiquitination and proteasome‐dependent degradation (Figure 1). The KEAP1 protein contains multiple cysteine‐based stress sensors, prominent examples of which include Cys151, Cys273, and Cys288, which can mediate the modulation of KEAP1 function in response to changes in the cellular redox state. Interaction of oxidative and electrophilic stressors with these sensors leads to the inactivation of the ubiquitin ligase activity of KEAP1, resulting in the stabilization and nuclear accumulation of NRF2. The cytoprotective output of NRF2 accumulation is mediated through antioxidant response element‐dependent gene expression (ARE), which is also named the CNC sMaf binding element (CsMBE) because the same regulatory element is shared across the CNC family of transcription factors. The protein products of the ARE/CsMBE regulated genes function in concert to repair cellular damage and mitigate the effects of subsequent exposures to stressors. 

A wide variety of murine genetic models have revealed that, through the regulation of this stress response pathway, Nrf2 activation plays a central role in a range of critical physiological processes, prototypic examples of which include protection against carcinogenesis, inflammation and oxidative stress responses. Through its' actions, NRF2 contributes to maintenance of health in the settings of a plethora of human diseases, including inter alia, Alzheimer's disease, diabetes mellitus, and atherosclerosis. Thus, the development of compounds which can enhance NRF2 signalling are of great clinical interest.

Successful examples of compounds targeting the NRF2 pathway with clinical applications include dimethyl fumarate (tecfidera), which is an approved treatment for multiple sclerosis, and CDDO methyl ester (CDDO‐Me, or bardoxolone), which is currently undergoing multiple clinical trials for the treatment of chronic kidney disease. Of note, both dimethyl fumarate and the triterpenoid family of compounds from which CDDO‐Me is derived, were not originally conceived as NRF2 modulators. Specifically, dimethyl fumarate was originally used as a treatment for psoriasis, while bardoxolone was designed on a natural product scaffold to inhibit the induction of iNOS gene expression. These specific examples of clinically used NRF2 modulators suggest that, due to the diverse range of compounds that can activate NRF2, it may be a good candidate to benefit more broadly from drug repositioning strategies. In this review, we will discuss the myriad of reasons why NRF2 activated tumours are resistant to current anti‐cancer therapies, and will explain why using prodrugs that are bioactivated by NRF2 target genes may provide an effective strategy to specifically exploit aberrant NRF2 activation in cancer therapy.

THE KEAP1‐NRF2 PATHWAY IN CANCER

The human disease in which the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway is most commonly associated is cancer. Due to the cytoprotective functions of NRF2, it was initially predicted that NRF2's function in cancer would be primarily that of a tumour suppressor. In keeping with this idea, early analysis of Nrf2 KO mice revealed that the loss of Nrf2 results in an increase in DNA damage, which is a common prerequisite for tumourigenesis. Furthermore, Nrf2 knockout mice are also susceptible to tumour formation across a range of tissues, including the stomach, bladder, skin, and colon, which together support the idea that basal—and especially induced—NRF2 activity is protective against cancer initiation by many chemical carcinogens. 

Consistent with the idea that the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway plays an important role in carcinogenesis, mutations in the genes encoding both proteins have been identified in human tumours. However, in stark contrast to the a priori expectations based on data from Nrf2 knockout mice, in human cancer, mutations which activate NRF2 signalling are positively selected for during tumorigenesis, which implies that activation of NRF2 provides a significant survival advantage for cancer cells. In this regard, a multitude of studies have revealed that the cytoprotective, metabolic, and proliferative properties which aberrant NRF2 activation can confer upon cells together function in a pro‐oncogenic and tumour‐promoting capacity. Thus, pan‐cancer genomic studies have revealed that genetic activation of NRF2 is a common event in human tumours, with the highest rates of NRF2 activation being observed in tumours of the lung and oesophagus (Table 1). Genetic activation of NRF2 frequently occurs through mutations which inhibit the binding of NRF2 to KEAP1, or through mutations which directly inactivate KEAP1 or CUL3, which both function as negative regulators of NRF2. However, due to the multitude of mechanisms through which NRF2 can be activated (Table 2), genetic analyses alone likely underestimate the true contributions of NRF2 activity to tumour formation. For example, while activating mutations in the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway are observed in 22% of lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) patients, overexpression of the NRF2 protein is observed in 33% of LUAD tumours. Similarly, overexpression of NRF2 is observed in 56% of gastric tumours, despite the fact that mutations are only observed in 11% of patients. These data highlight the fact that aberrant activation of NRF2 signalling, through a multitude of mechanisms, contribute to tumour formation across a range of tissue types.

ACTIVATION OF NRF2 IN NON‐SMALL CELL LUNG CARCINOMA IS ASSOCIATED WITH A POOR PROGNOSIS FOR PATIENTS

Genetic activation of NRF2 is most commonly observed in non‐small cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC), a subtype which accounts for 85% of all lung tumours, where it is associated with the generation of aggressive tumours that are recalcitrant to most therapeutic interventions. Optimal treatment strategies for NSCLC patients depend on the stage of the tumour, with early stage I/II tumours most commonly being treated by surgery or radiation therapy. For locally advanced, lymph node positive tumours (stage IIB‐IIIC), the primary treatment option is concurrent radiation and chemotherapy, followed by 1 year of anti‐PD‐1 therapy. Unfortunately, at the time of diagnosis, the majority of patients present with advanced, metastatic disease. Due to advancements in cancer genomics, approximately 25% of these patients can benefit from targeted therapies, exemplified by inhibitors of EGFR, ALK, BRAF, and MET. However, in this regard, such Umbrella trials, which seek to match genetic vulnerabilities in tumours with molecularly targeted therapies, can only target pathways for which therapeutic options exist, and thus, despite the fact that KEAP1 is the third most commonly mutated gene in LUAD, the lack of NRF2 targeting therapies means that KEAP1‐NRF2 mutant tumours are not included in these trials. For patients with advanced stage tumours, but without mutations in targetable driver genes, anti‐PD‐1/PD‐L1 antibody‐based immunotherapies have become the first‐line therapeutic option, replacing older platinum‐doublet based regimens which consisted of combinations of cisplatin or carboplatin with either paclitaxel, gemcitabine or docetaxel. 

During tumourigenesis, as the cytoprotective functions of NRF2 are hijacked by the cancer cells, this protects them not only from the stressful conditions within the tumour microenvironment, but also from the cytotoxic effects of almost all anti‐cancer drugs (Figure 2, Tables 3 and 4). In a clinical context, this subversion of NRF2 function produces aggressive tumours that have a very poor prognosis for patients, and that are associated with significant resistance to all therapeutic strategies currently used to treat NSCLC, including chemotherapeutics, radiotherapy, and immunotherapies (Table 5a,b). In light of these facts, there is an urgent clinical need to identify compounds that can selectively target aberrant NRF2 activation in cancer. As the mechanism whereby NRF2 protects against immunotherapies is both surprising and distinct from that of classical chemotherapeutics, it will be explored in more detail below.

IMMUNE‐CHECKPOINT INHIBITOR THERAPIES HAVE REDUCED EFFICACY IN NRF2‐ACTIVATED TUMOURS

The immune system constitutes a major obstacle for tumour formation, as oncogenic cells can be actively removed from tissues through the immune surveillance pathway, primarily mediated by the actions of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. However, in order to maintain tissue homeostasis, T cell activity can be negatively regulated by a number of checkpoint pathways, which function to limit the deleterious effects of immune responses towards self‐antigens. These immune checkpoints include the PD‐1 and CTLA4 pathways, which are frequently activated aberrantly in cancers in order to suppress the immune system mediated anti‐cancer response. Thus, the administration of immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapies allows for the re‐activation of the immune response towards cancer cells, and has revolutionized anti‐cancer treatments towards a range of tumour types, including melanoma and lung cancer. 

The receptor PD‐1 is primarily expressed on helper and cytotoxic T cells, while its ligand PD‐L1 is expressed by a variety of cells within the tumour microenvironment, including directly by the malignant cells themselves. Activation of the PD‐1 immune checkpoint functions as a brake on T cell activity, leading to a reduction in cytotoxicity, cytokine secretion, and proliferation. Together, these critical modifications of the T cell phenotype function in concert to suppress the endogenous anti‐cancer response, which ultimately leads to immune tolerance towards the tumour cells. Therapeutic inhibition of PD‐1, through use of monoclonal antibodies targeting either the receptor or the ligand, can re‐activate the adaptive immune response towards the tumour, and has demonstrated significant clinical efficacy in NSCLC patients. 

Since the approval of the first anti‐PD‐1 antibody for use in NSCLC patients by the FDA in 2015, additional clinical trials have been carried out with the aim of further enhancing the efficacy of this ICI approach. These strategies include the simultaneous targeting of both the PD‐1 and CTLA4 immune checkpoint pathways, the use of anti‐PD‐1 therapies in combination with classical chemotherapeutics, and combination antibody therapies targeting the PD‐1 and VEGF pathways. In total, there are currently eight FDA approved anti‐PD‐1/anti‐PD‐L1 therapeutic regimens available for patients with NSCLC, all of which were approved based on their superiority over platinum‐doublet chemotherapy. In this context, the 5‐year overall survival rates from the KEYNOTE‐024 trial were 32% for patients receiving the anti‐PD‐1 therapy, compared to 16% for those receiving chemotherapy alone, which clearly supports the use of ICIs as the first‐line therapy for advanced NSCLC. 

Despite these impressive results, patients do not benefit uniformly from anti‐PD‐1/anti‐PD‐L1 therapies, with the mutation profile of the tumour contributing significantly to the efficacy of this treatment approach. For example, in the phase III IMpower150 trial, the median survival of patients was increased by 5 months by the co‐treatment of chemotherapy plus anti‐PD‐L1 and anti‐VEGF antibodies (ABCP treatment group), when compared to the chemotherapy plus anti‐VEGF control group (BCP) (19.0 vs. 14.1 months). In patients with mutations in the KRAS oncogene, the efficacy of the ABCP regimen was significantly enhanced, as the median survival was increased from 9.9 months in the control group, to 19.8 months by the co‐treatment with the anti‐PD‐L1 therapy atezolizumab. These data highlight the fact that patients with mutations in KRAS are particularly responsive to anti‐PD‐1/anti‐PD‐L1 therapies.

Interestingly, a similar analysis of KEAP1 mutations within the same patient population revealed that co‐treatment with the anti‐PD‐L1 antibody had no effect on patient survival (11.4 vs. 11.7 months for the BCP control). A similar result was also observed in response to anti‐PD‐1 therapies, where the co‐mutation of KEAP1 or NFE2L2 in the context of mutant KRAS significantly reduced patient survival relative to a KRAS mutation alone. Consistent with these results, the high expression of an NRF2‐dependent gene signature was also shown to be associated with decreased survival in NSCLC patients treated with the anti‐PD‐L1 therapy atezolizumab. Of note, while high tumour PD‐L1 expression is the best biomarker for the efficacy of ICI therapy, KEAP1 mutant tumours are associated with reduced PD‐L1 expression, which is consistent with the poor prognosis for this patient population in response to anti‐PD‐1/anti‐PD‐L1 therapies. Therefore, taken together, these clinical data strongly suggest that, similarly to classical chemotherapeutics, KEAP1 mutant, and thus NRF2‐activated, tumours may be recalcitrant to anti‐PD‐1/anti‐PD‐L1 therapies.

 NRF2‐DEPENDENT REMODELING OF THE TUMOUR IMMUNOLOGICAL PROFILE GENERATES RESISTANCE TO ICIs 

When considering the transcriptional program regulated by NRF2, which was first characterized as being composed of xenobiotic metabolizing enzymes and anti‐oxidative stress proteins, it is not surprising that aberrant NRF2 activation results in resistance to classical chemotherapeutic drugs (Figure 2). However, as antibody‐based ICI therapies function through a completely distinct pathway, it is not clear a priori why the chemoprotective function of NRF2 would result in resistance to these drugs.

The first insight into the mechanism responsible for this NRF2‐dependent resistance to ICI therapies was provided by transcriptome analysis of a cohort of KRAS mutant LUADs. This analysis revealed that KRAS mutant tumours could be divided into three sub‐clusters, one of which was enriched with mutations in KEAP1. Further analysis revealed that this KEAP1 mutant cluster was associated with reduced immune system engagement, exemplified by low expression of PD‐L1, in addition to other immune cell markers such as CD28, CD80, and CD86, all of which are all required for T cell activation. This finding was supported by a second transcriptome‐based analysis of TCGA data which focused on the expression of a T cell‐inflamed gene expression profile (GEP) metric. As the name suggests, high GEP expression is positively associated with T cell infiltration into the tumour, and therefore correlates with a favourable response to ICI therapies. Cristescu et al. found that KEAP1 mutant NSCLC (both LUAD and LUSC) showed a highly significant negative correlation with the GEP metric, which suggests that T cell engagement is particularly poor in the case of KEAP1 mutant tumours. Together, these studies suggest that mutations in KEAP1 (i.e., NRF2 activation) significantly impact the tumour microenvironment, and specifically immune cell infiltration and composition within the tumour, and that this phenotype may contribute to the reduced efficacy of ICI therapies in this patient population.

Direct experimental support for this idea was provided by a set of CRISPR screens in subcutaneous allografts, which were carried out in parallel using immunocompetent and immune compromised mice. By comparing the enrichment of sgRNAs across the experiments, the authors were able to determine which genes are required for tumour growth in the presence of a fully functioning adaptive immune system. This analysis revealed that sgRNAs targeting Keap1 and Cul3, both of which are negative regulators of Nrf2, were specifically enriched in the tumours from the immunocompetent mice. These experiments provide important support for the idea that Nrf2 activation results in resistance to immune system‐mediated removal of cancer cells.

The immunological profile of the majority of solid tumours can be divided into three groups, the names of which are autological descriptions of the tumour microenvironments: immune inflamed, immune excluded, and immune desert. From a clinical perspective, the immune inflamed group is characterized by the infiltration of T cells, and is therefore associated with the best response to immunotherapies. The genetic analysis of KEAP1 mutant tumours found that they are enriched in the immune desert phenotype, which is characterized by poor T cell invasion and has a worse response to immunotherapies. Additional support for this idea was provided by a recent scRNA‐Seq analysis of human LUAD, in which KEAP1 mutant tumours were found to be enriched in an immune signature consisting of proinflammatory monocyte‐derived macrophages and exhausted CD8+ T cells. In concordance with previous studies, this KEAP1‐associated immune signature was associated with a worse prognosis for patients, which supports the idea that the KEAP1‐NRF2 mediated interaction between tumour cells and the immune system is a clinically important phenotype.

Taken together, the available data paint an unexpected picture of the role of the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway in the clinical response to ICI therapies. It is now clear that the aberrant activation of NRF2 within cancer cells has a profound impact not only on the tumour cells themselves, but also on the tumour microenvironment, by generating a T cell‐depleted immune desert phenotype. Presumably, due to the absence of T cells, anti‐PD‐L1/anti‐PD‐1 therapies have reduced efficacy in tumours with KEAP1 mutations, resulting in a poor prognosis for patients in response to these drugs. This means that clinically, despite the recent advances in therapeutic treatments for advanced NSCLC patients, there are still no appropriate therapies which can be used to treat patients with mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2.

 NRF2‐TARGETING ANTI‐CANCER COMPOUNDS

In light of the abundant clinical data, it is clear that KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway‐specific therapeutic strategies will be required to treat tumours with aberrant NRF2 signalling. Initial attempts to target NRF2 activation in tumours focused on the development of direct NRF2 inhibitors. Thus, while brusatol and halofuginone reduce intracellular NRF2 activity, these compounds function as general translation inhibitors, and are therefore not specific to the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway. Although a number of compounds have been shown to exhibit NRF2‐dependent toxicity (Table 6), as a transcription factor, the lack of deep, druggable binding pockets makes NRF2 particularly recalcitrant to direct inhibition by small molecules. 

While the development of direct NRF2 inhibitors is challenging, this problem can be circumvented by a complementary drug development strategy that targets the NRF2 transcriptome, and not the transcription factor itself. In this context, as the NRF2‐dependent antioxidant response includes a multitude of xenobiotic and drug‐metabolizing enzymes, this cytoprotective function can be turned into a targetable vulnerability by harnessing these enzymes for the bioactivation of prodrug compounds (Box 1, Figure 3). As the enhanced expression of drug‐metabolizing enzymes is an integral property of NRF2 activation, it represents an inherent Achilles heel which can be exploited therapeutically through the identification of synthetic lethal interactions between small molecule prodrugs and NRF2 (Figure 4). Furthermore, as bioactivation of such prodrugs by NRF2 target genes will only occur in cells with high levels of NRF2 activity, this will significantly reduce toxicity in normal cells, and thus create a large therapeutic window which should produce favourable pharmacodynamics in patients.

Prodrugs are compounds with minimal pharmacological activity in their native state, but which contain a structural feature which facilitates their bioactivation under specific physiological conditions, or upon metabolism by a specific enzyme. This bioconversion can be achieved through multiple mechanisms, including removal of a masking promoiety, cleavage from a drug carrier scaffold, or structural modification to produce the active metabolite. Examples of enzymes that function in such bioactivation reactions include the cytochrome P450 enzyme CYP3A4, glutathione‐S‐transferases, NADPH‐cytochrome P450 reductase, and NO01, the later three of which are all NRF2 target genesis. Examples of prodrugs currently being optimized for use as cancer treatments include aldoxorubicin, a compound in which doxorubicin is released in the acidic tumour microenvironment, and DON, a glutamine mimetic which contains promoieties that are cleaved by tumour‐enriched to release the active connpound. The medical importance of this drug design approach is highlighted by fact that prodrugs represent approximately 10% of all drugs marketed worldwide. 

Of note, prior to the discovery of NRF2, it was found that glutathione‐S‐transferases could catalyze the β‐elimination of glutathione from prodrug compounds, resulting in the release of cytotoxic molecules specifically in cells with high levels of GST activity. Although these results had not been interpreted in the context of NRF2‐activated tumours, as GSTs are well characterized NRF2 target genes, these data provided preliminary support to suggest that a prodrug bioactivation approach may be effective for the treatment of NRF2‐activated cancers.

In this context, we developed a synthetic lethal screening system based on a differentially fluorescently labelled isogenic pair of WT and Keap1 knockout cells (Figure 5). Using this assay system, we identified the DNA damaging agent mitomycin C (MMC), and the geldanamycin‐derived family of HSP90 inhibitors to be synthetic lethal with NRF2. Consistent with the prodrug bioactivation paradigm, mechanistically, we found that the synthetic lethal interaction between NRF2 and all of these compounds was mediated by the activity of NRF2 target genes.

MMC FOR THE TREATMENT OF NRF2‐ACTIVATED CANCERS

MMC was originally isolated from the bacterium Streptomyces caspitosus, where it was shown to function as a naturally occurring antibiotic. MMC was first used as a clinical compound to treat cancer in 1956, where it functions as a cytotoxic DNA damaging agent. Specifically, MMC is a bifunctional DNA alkylating agent which can form G‐A or G‐G interstrand DNA crosslinks, in addition to 7‐alkylG and O6alkylG monoadducts. The covalent linkages that generate the interstrand crosslinks are usually irreversible, and are thus extremely toxic to cells as they are not only mutagenic, but also interfere with cellular processes that require strand separation, including transcription and DNA replication. 

While trying to determine the mechanism of MMC‐mediated cytotoxicity, it was found that MMC was unexpectedly stable when incubated with liver homogenates under aerobic, but not anaerobic, conditions. This finding, coupled with structural considerations of the MMC molecule, inspired Schwartz et al. to propose that MMC is bioactivated within cells specifically under anaerobic, reductive conditions, and that this metabolism is required to produce the potent DNA‐damaging agent. In support of this idea, subsequent work revealed that the bioactivation of MMC was enzyme mediated, with NAD(P) H quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1), cytochrome P450 reductase (CYPOR), xanthine oxidase, and NADH: cytochrome b 5 reductase enzymes all capable of generating the activated form of MMC, while NADPH was also shown to be required as a source of the reducing equivalents. As NQO1, cytochrome P450 reductase and NADH: cytochrome b 5 reductase are expressed in tumour cells, these enzymes are the most likely candidates which are responsible for the bioactivation of MMC in cancer patients. 

Cytochrome P450 reductase and NADH: cytochrome b 5 reductase both promote the one electron reduction of MMC, producing a semiquinone anion radical which readily reacts with oxygen within the cell to regenerate the prodrug form of MMC. However, under hypoxic conditions, which are commonly encountered in the tumour microenvironment, the half‐life of the semiquinone anion radical is extended, allowing it to function as a direct DNA damaging agent, or alternatively, it can be further reduced to form the hydroquinone MMCH2. In contrast, NQO1 catalyzes the two‐electron reduction of MMC to directly produce the hydroquinone MMCH2, utilizing NADPH as the source of the electrons. The formation of the MMC hydroquinone induces a series of structural changes within the MMC molecule, resulting in the production of the final cytotoxic agent with dual reactive sites at the C1 and C10 positions, which together allow MMC to generate interstand DNA crosslinks (Figure 6). Of note, the NQO1‐dependent bioactivation of MMC is increased in low pH conditions, such as those encountered in the hypoxic and acidic tumour microenvironment, a property which will contribute to the enhanced toxicity of MMC towards tumour cells. 

As NRF2 directly regulates the expression of NQO1, cytochrome P450 reductase, and all of the enzymes in the pentose phosphate pathway responsible for NADPH generation, NRF2 directly impacts every stage of MMC bioactivation, which makes NRF2 activated cells particularly sensitive to MMC toxicity. Furthermore, unlike paclitaxel and docetaxel, which are commonly used in platinum‐doublet therapies to treat advanced NSCLC patients, MMC is a poor substrate for efflux by the NRF2 target gene MDR1. This property of MMC will further enhance its toxicity towards NRF2 activated cells relative to other chemotherapeutic treatment options (Figure 2). Interestingly, in KEAP1 mutant H460 cells, MMC enhances the toxicity of PD‐L1 antibody treatment, suggesting that it may function synergistically with ICI therapies in NRF2‐activated lung tumours. 

To highlight the importance of NRF2 status for the sensitivity to MMC, we utilized the Genomics of Drug Sensitivity in Cancer (GDSC) database to analyze the toxicity of MMC in a panel of 30 human NSCLC‐derived cell lines, 15 of which have genetic activation of NRF2, and 15 of which have low NRF2 activity as determined by NRF2‐dependent gene expression (Figure 7). Analysis of these data revealed that the cell lines with high levels of NRF2 activity have an average MMC IC50 of 59.97 nM, while those with low levels of NRF2 are significantly less sensitive (833.44 nM, 13.9‐fold higher than NSCLC cell lines with genetic activation of NRF2). This clearly demonstrates the importance of NRF2‐dependent bioactivation of MMC for the toxicity of this chemotherapeutic drug in human NSCLC cell lines.

To explore whether this effect is limited to NSCLC, we selected the top 1% of cell lines with the highest sensitivity to MMC from the GDSC database, regardless of tissue of origin, and determined their NRF2 status based on the gene expression of a small panel of well characterized NRF2 target genes (Nqo1, Gclc, Txnrd1, Slc7a11, Akr1b10, G6pd), utilizing the RNA‐Seq data from the Cell Model Passport (Figure 8). This analysis revealed that 7/9 of these cell lines expressed high levels of at least three of the NRF2 target genes, which strongly suggests that, independent of tissue of origin, NRF2 activity is a major determinant of MMC toxicity. In the two cell lines without high expression of NRF2, their sensitivity to MMC could be dependent on an NRF2‐independent pathway, for example, through a deficiency in DNA damage repair, and so forth.

CLINICAL USES OF MMC

In NSCLC patients, when administered as a first‐line single‐agent therapy, MMC has an overall response rate of 25%. In addition to its use as a monotherapy, MMC has also been included in a variety of combination therapies, of which the MVP combination (MMC + vinca alkaloid + platinum therapy) has been the most commonly utilized protocol. Across 18 clinical trials in advanced NSCLC patients, MVP treatment resulted in an overall response rate of 39%. Furthermore, in the broader context of treatment options for advanced NSCLC patients, MMC‐based combination therapies are equally efficacious when compared to third‐generation platinum doublet treatment regimens, with the third‐generation therapies being favoured clinically due to favourable toxicity profiles. However, as NRF2 activity results in resistance to all current first‐line NSCLC treatment strategies, including platinum doublet therapies and immune‐checkpoint inhibitors (Table 5b), the NRF2‐dependent toxicity of MMC, which has extensive mechanistic support from the reductive bioactivation literature, argues that MMC‐based therapeutic strategies may be particularly efficacious in this patient population.

In addition to its use as a treatment for NCSLC, MMC has also shown efficacy in the treatment of other tumour types in which NRF2 activity is associated with poor prognosis (Table 5a). For example, in bladder cancer, when administered as a single agent, the response rate to MMC is 33%, while in gastric cancer it is 30%. Of note, in the case of bladder cancer, MMC has also been shown to have a sensitizing interaction with mutations in TP53. As approximately half of all bladder cancers have a mutation in TP53, the parallel MMC sensitivity conferred by NRF2‐activation and p53‐inactivation may make this patient population particularly responsive to treatment with MMC. In this regard, it is important to note that MMC is currently used to treat bladder cancer patients, with chemohyperthermia protocols demonstrating improved efficacy compared to Bacillus Calmette‐Guerin therapy for the treatment of non‐muscle invasive bladder cancer. Therefore, due to the synthetic lethal interaction between MMC and NRF2, using this approach to specifically target NRF2‐dependent tumours may be an effective clinical strategy to treat bladder cancer patients with mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2.

Despite the fact that MMC has been used as a chemotherapeutic since 1956, it is still subject to extensive research for novel applications in anti‐cancer therapies. For example, in order to provide a sustained release of the drug, MMC has been incorporated into a gel, named UGN‐102, which demonstrated a significant treatment response in a recent phase 2b clinical trial. Similarly, MMC has also been incorporated into a liposome‐based prodrug, named Promitil, which displays increased dose tolerability in patients due to the lipid‐prodrug encapsulation administration protocol. In metastatic lung cancer, a recent phase 1 clinical trial utilized MMC in an oil emulsion formulation as part of a chemoembolization protocol, while in patients with inactivating mutations in the Fanconi Anemia homologous recombination DNA repair pathway, MMC has been used in a combination therapy with the PARP inhibitor Veliparib. Together, these studies show that, due to its proven cytotoxic effects, a broad variety of novel therapeutic applications of MMC are being actively pursued. In addition to these examples, we would advocate that using MMC to target recalcitrant NRF2‐activated tumours is another approach which should be given clinical consideration.

GELDANAMYCIN‐DERIVED HSP90 INHIBITORS ARE ALSO SYNTHETIC LETHAL WITH NRF2 ACTIVATION

In addition to identifying a synthetic lethal interaction between NRF2 and MMC, the same isogenic fluorescent screening assay identified the geldanamycin‐derived family of HSP90 inhibitors to be synthetic lethal with NRF2. Of note, as this family of compounds have been used in multiple clinical trials targeting a range of tumour types, their drug repositioning towards the currently untreatable NRF2‐activated cancers is particularly intriguing.

By utilizing a library of small molecule cell stress modulators, we first identified the HSP90 inhibitor 17‐AAG to display enhanced toxicity in Keap1 KO cells. As this phenotype was completely rescued by the simultaneous loss of Nrf2, it strongly suggested that 17‐AAG may be synthetic lethal with Nrf2. By examining a structurally diverse collection of HSP90 inhibitors, we found that Keap1 KO cells were not sensitive to HSP90 inhibition per se, but only to those inhibitors which contain a quinone group within their chemical structure. Thus, in addition to 17‐AAG, we found that the compounds 17‐DMAG and IPI‐504 (Figure 9a) were also synthetic lethal with Nrf2, with all three compounds being structurally related as they are derived from the geldanamycin chemical scaffold. As it has previously been shown that 17‐AAG is metabolized into the more potent HSP90 inhibitor 17‐AAGH2 by the enzyme NQO1, which is a well‐characterized NRF2 target gene, we hypothesized that this bioactivation pathway may be responsible for the synthetic lethality with NRF2. Consistent with this idea, in NRF2 activated Huh‐1 cells, the enhanced toxicity of 17‐AAG was completely rescued by knocking out NQO1, which provided important mechanistic support for the bioactivation hypothesis (Figure 9b). Thus, through a mechanism reminiscent to that of MMC, the reductive metabolism of 17‐AAG by an NRF2 target gene, in this case specifically NQO1, effectively turns the administered compound into a prodrug which becomes a potent HSP90 inhibitor only in cells with high levels of NRF2‐NQO1 activity. 

CLINICAL REPOSITIONING OF HSP90 INHIBITORS TO TARGET NRF2 ACTIVATION IN CANCER

Drug‐mediated inhibition of HSP90 activity, such as with 17‐AAG, is an attractive anti‐cancer strategy. HSP90 functions as a molecular chaperone which helps proteins fold into their correct conformation, and thus mitigates the toxic effects of protein misfolding and aggregation. This functionality is particularly pertinent in the tumour microenvironment, where hypoxic and nutrient‐deprived conditions have a negative impact on cellular proteostasis, and therefore, upregulation of HSP90 gene expression commonly occurs in tumours. Furthermore, as HSP90 client proteins include the oncogenes EGFR, ALK, KRAS, AKT, and MEK, inhibition of HSP90 function should be particularly toxic to tumour cells. For these reasons, a wide variety of clinical trials have been carried out using a molecularly diverse group of HSP90 inhibitors. 

In the context of drug‐repositioning of HSP90 inhibitors to target NRF2‐dependent tumours, three compounds are of particular interest: tanespimycin (17‐AAG), alvespimycin (17‐DMAG), and retaspimycin (IPI‐504). All three of these compounds have been used in clinical trials, up to phase III in the case of tanespimycin and retaspimycin, but as yet, they have not been used in a molecularly targeted fashion to treat tumours with mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2. Tanespimycin showed promising results in combination therapies with bortezomib, to treat multiple myeloma, and trastuzumab, to treat metastatic breast cancer, but has not yet been used to treat lung cancer patients. In order to improve upon the physical–chemical properties of tanespimycin, which may limit its effectiveness in the clinic, alvespimycin and retaspimycin were both developed as water‐soluble analogues, with alvespimycin having been used as a monotherapy in a clinical trial in patients with acute myeloid leukaemia, as well as in a combination therapy formulation with trastuzumab. Of the three structurally related compounds, retaspimycin is the most interesting in the context of NRF2‐activated tumours, as it has already been used in a clinical trial in NSCLC patients. Of note, this trial used a molecularly targeted patient population; however, patients with mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2 were not included in this trial, presumably because the role of the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway in NSCLC was poorly described when the patients were enrolled in 2007. Thus, although mechanistically the rationale for using retaspimycin to target aberrant NRF2 activation is cancer is clear, this synthetic lethal interaction is yet to be explored in patients. The final clinical trial using retaspimycin was terminated in 2011 due to unacceptable toxicity, with the authors concluding that “if a cancer patient population were identified in which clinical benefit could be achieved, the observed profile might be acceptable with appropriate monitoring for, and management of, known toxicities.” We would postulate that the aggressive tumours generated by mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2 represent one such appropriate patient population, due to their recalcitrance to all currently used therapeutic strategies.

IMPROVEMENTS TO THE HSP90 INHIBITOR STRUCTURES AND TREATMENT REGIMENS IMPROVE THEIR TOXICITY PROFILES

Despite the fact that clinical use of tanespimycin, alvespimycin and retaspimycin to treat cancer patients is no longer being pursued, this family of compounds is still subject to ongoing research. While these compounds displayed limited efficacy in their clinical trials, their use was also associated with hepatic toxicity that is thought to be dependent on reactions with cellular nucleophiles, such as glutathione, at the unsubstituted 19‐position of the quinone ring. In order to reduce hepatotoxicity, novel 19‐substituted versions of these compounds have been generated, which prevent nucleophilic attack, and are significantly less toxic (Figure 9a). Importantly, these 19‐substituted compounds are still subject to bioactivation by NQO1, and therefore should maintain a synthetic lethal interaction with NRF2. A complementary strategy that has been used to increase the efficacy of 17‐AAG involved its incorporation into therapeutic nanoparticles. In pre‐clinical mouse studies, this formulation increased the tumour‐specific targeting of 17‐AAG, while simultaneously decreasing its hepatoxicity, a combination that represents an important improvement over the standard administration of 17‐AAG alone. 

Taken together, these newer 17‐AAG derivatives, which reduce its negative effects (hepatoxicity) while maintaining its NRF2‐NQO1 specificity (the quinone ring), represent a significant improvement over the formulations used in clinical trials over 10 years ago. If these compounds were used in a molecularly selected patient population that included KEAP1/NRF2 mutant tumours, the mechanistic support for the NRF2‐NQO1‐bioactivation pathway strongly suggests that they would be efficacious in tumours with aberrant NRF2 activation. Interestingly, data from the Cancer Genome Atlas Research Network showed that HSP90 functions as a critical signalling hub in urothelial bladder carcinoma. As NRF2 is activated in 12% of bladder tumours (Table 1), these data suggest that, in addition to targeting NRF2 activation in NSCLC, 17‐AAG derivatives may also be particularly efficacious in NRF2‐dependent bladder cancer.

TARGETING NRF2 ACTIVATION IN CANCER USING CONCURRENT SYNTHETIC LETHALITY

As MMC and the geldanamycin‐derived family of HSP90 inhibitors both induce cellular death through distinct pathways, DNA damage in the case of MMC, and proteostasis breakdown in the case of the 17‐AAG family, we hypothesized that the co‐administration of these synthetic lethal compounds would lead to the further enhancement of NRF2‐specific cell death. Consistent with our expectations, the co‐treatment of cells with MMC and 17‐AAG resulted in a synergistic NRF2‐specific toxicity phenotype that was greater than the additive effects of each compound alone.

To highlight the utility of this approach in NSCLC, we analyzed the IC50s for 17‐AAG (tanespimycin) using the same set of human cancer cell lines which were used in Figure 8 to analyze MMC sensitivity (Figure 10a). Importantly, of the 15 cell lines that we have shown to be sensitive to MMC, 10 are also extremely sensitive to 17‐AAG, which highlights the generality of the NRF2‐specific sensitivity towards both compounds, and strongly suggests that the co‐treatment of these compounds would be an effective strategy to treat NRF2‐dependent NSCLC tumours (Figure 10a). As, to the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that synthetic lethal interactions targeting two different cell death pathways have been combined to produce enhanced toxicity, we named this approach “concurrent synthetic lethality,” due to the parallel nature of the toxicity mechanisms (Figure 10b). Of note, clinical trials using tanespimycin in combination therapies with either bortezomib or trastuzumab have shown good efficacy, and therefore there is good precedent for using tanespimycin in combination therapy approaches. Furthermore, as retaspimycin has also been used in combination therapies with docetaxel, the available patient data suggests that this family of HSP90 inhibitors is well suited for use in combination therapies. Taken together, we believe that this efficacy would be further enhanced by using a molecularly targeted patient population consisting of patients with activating mutations in the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway. The clinical potential of this approach is not restricted to NSCLC patients, because, as noted above, TCGA analysis revealed HSP90 to be an important hub in bladder cancer patients, a tumour type that is particularly responsive to MMC‐based therapies. Therefore, the co‐administration of MMC with a geldanamycin‐derived HSP90 inhibitor in bladder cancer patient with activating mutations in NRF2 may also be a particularly efficacious treatment strategy.

CONCLUSION

Aberrant activation of NRF2 in human cancer produces aggressive tumours that are resistant to all current anti‐cancer therapies. The urgency of this problem is reinforced by recent studies using ICI therapies which revealed that, unlike tumours with activating mutations in KRAS, anti‐PD‐1/PD‐L1 therapies showed no beneficial effects in NSCLC patients with activating mutations in KEAP1/NRF2. These findings highlight the fact that, despite recent advances in anti‐cancer drug development, there are currently no suitable therapeutic options for this patient population. Furthermore, the recent KEAPSAKE clinical trial, which aimed to specifically target KEAP1 mutant lung tumours using the glutaminase inhibitor telaglenastat, was halted due to a lack of clinical efficacy, which confirms the difficulty of targeting the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway in cancer.

Within this clinical landscape, recent work has suggested that KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway anti‐cancer specificity may be achieved by targeting the NRF2‐dependent transcriptome, instead of the transcription factor itself. As NRF2 regulates the expression of a broad range of xenobiotic metabolizing enzymes, their metabolic activities can be utilized for the bioactivation of prodrug compounds. This strategy will minimize toxicity in cells with basal NRF2 activity, and will thus extend the therapeutic window for these compounds in patients. Prominent examples of this strategy include the bioactivation of the DNA damaging agent MMC, or the geldanamycin‐derived family of HSP90 inhibitors, by the well characterized NRF2 target gene NQO1. However, this NRF2‐dependent bioactivation strategy is not restricted to NQO1, as other NRF2 target genes, such as GSTs and AKR1C3, can also function in a similar way. Taken together, we propose that targeting NRF2 activation in cancer using a prodrug bioactivation strategy has the potential to generate pathway specificity for these currently untreatable tumours.

Of the compounds identified to date, MMC represents the best candidate for drug repositioning to treat NRF2‐activated tumours, due to its clinically proven cytotoxic activity, coupled with the fact that it is already approved for use in patients. For example, in NSCLC patients in which immune‐checkpoint inhibitor plus chemotherapy is the first line treatment option, patients with mutations in KEAP1 or NRF2 may specifically benefit from the use of MMC as the chemotherapeutic component of this regimen, due to its enhanced NRF2‐specific toxicity. Furthermore, MMC has also been shown to enhance the toxicity of anti‐PD‐L1 therapies in pre‐clinical models, which supports the idea that this combination therapy may be particularly efficacious in this patient population. Similarly, in NSCLC patients in which the currently recommended treatment is platinum doublet therapy, patients with aberrant NRF2 activation may respond better to MMC‐based combination therapies, which are equally efficacious to platinum doublet therapies across non‐genetically selected patients, but will display enhanced NRF2‐specific toxicity due to increased MMC bioactivation, without mitigation by NRF2‐dependent resistance to the platinum doublet components.

Thus, although there are currently no effective treatment strategies to target NRF2 activation in cancer, we believe that the deepening mechanistic knowledge of the KEAP1‐NRF2 pathway can be leveraged to improve upon the existing treatment regimens through the inclusion of a prodrug bioactivation component, of which MMC currently represents the best candidate compound.
