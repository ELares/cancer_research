# The circRNA cEMSY Induces Immunogenic Cell Death and Boosts Immunotherapy Efficacy in Lung Adenocarcinoma.

## Abstract

Immunogenic cell death (ICD) induces an active immune response. Activating ICD represents a potential approach to boost the antitumor activity of immunotherapy, highlighting the need to identify effective and safe ICD inducers. In this study, we identified a conserved, ICD-related circular RNA cEMSY by systematically screening ICD models induced by multiple cell stressors in lung adenocarcinoma. cEMSY triggered ICD in lung adenocarcinoma cells both in vitro and in vivo, leading to the release of damage-associated molecular patterns and promoting T-cell cross-priming by dendritic cells. Notably, the intratumoral delivery of lipid nanoparticle-encapsulated cEMSY induced a potent antitumor immune response in an immunosuppressed tumor model, which synergized with PD-1 blockade to facilitate long-term antitumor immunity with no apparent toxicities. Mechanistically, cEMSY mediated mitochondrial aggregation of the RNA-binding protein TDP-43 that enabled leakage of mitochondrial DNA to stimulate the cGAS-STING pathway, activating the antiviral immune response. Clinically, elevated expression of cEMSY correlated with enhanced infiltration of dendritic cells and CD8+ T cells and favorable immunotherapy response in lung adenocarcinoma. Together, these findings support the dual potential of cEMSY as a target and biomarker for improving immune checkpoint inhibitor responses in lung adenocarcinoma. Significance: cEMSY is a safe and effective immunogenic cell death inducer that synergizes with PD-1 blockade in lung adenocarcinoma, providing a potential strategy to enhance the efficacy of tumor immunotherapy.

Introduction

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA4 pathways have revolutionized the treatment landscape, yielding significant successes in lung adenocarcinoma. Nonetheless, the response rate to ICIs remains suboptimal; only a small proportion of patients with cancer benefit from ICIs; and most responding patients eventually developed acquired resistance to this treatment modality. Such a low response to ICIs could be ascribed to “cold” tumors in the majority of patients. Typically, highly immunogenic “hot” tumors show a signature of antitumor CD8+ T-cell responses, which corresponds to better clinical responses to ICIs. However, in immunosuppressive “cold” tumors, cancer cells often display low immunogenicity, lack the expression of cancer-specific or cancer-associated antigens, or have impaired antigen presentation machinery, which hinders adequate tumor T-cell infiltration. Therefore, considerable research efforts are directed toward identifying innovative therapeutic strategies or combination treatments that can convert “cold” tumors into “hot” ones. Within this framework, immunogenic cell death (ICD) has garnered increasing interest as a promising approach, which makes tumors visible to the immune system and amplifies tumor responses to ICIs.

ICD is a modulated form of regulated cell death, characterized by the release of danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMP), including the externalization of calreticulin, production of ATP, and release of high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), as well as the emission of type I IFNs and chemokines. These serve as “eat me” and “find me” signals to facilitate the maturation and cross-presentation of dendritic cells (DC), ultimately leading to tumor-specific T-cell–mediated antitumor immune responses. Clinically, drug-induced ICD has been reported to correlate positively with therapeutic responses and enhanced antitumor CD8+ T-cell immunity. To date, only a few anticancer drugs are considered bona fide ICD inducers. Moreover, most standard chemotherapeutic regimens, including cisplatin (CDDP) and gemcitabine, are deemed non-ICD inducers, necessitating adjuvant therapies to induce the release of ICD-associated DAMPs. Therefore, both the mechanisms by which ICD provides protective antitumor immunity and the ways to reliably induce ICD are yet unknown.

Circular RNAs (circRNA) have emerged as crucial regulators in antitumor immunity and immunotherapy. For instance, targeting circATXN7 in adoptively transferred tumor-reactive cytotoxic T lymphocytes improves their antitumor activities in mutant KRAS tumors. Moreover, targeting circMGA/HNRNPL complex in prostate cancer promotes the infiltration of CD8+ T lymphocytes and improves anti–PD-1 efficacy by reducing CCL5 secretion. Recently, the significant antitumor potential of circRNAs has extended its applications to the realm of RNA therapeutics, in which they offer advantages over traditional mRNA therapies, including superior stability and prolonged antigen presentation, and are therefore considered promising candidates for next-generation RNA-based therapies. It has been reported that a circRNA vaccine targeting SARS-CoV-2 elicited stable neutralizing antibody and cellular immune responses with minimal toxicity. It has been more recently shown that immunization with transporter-encapsulated circRNA encoding the antigens as therapeutic cancer vaccines successfully cleared B16 melanoma. However, no circRNAs have been reported as immunogenic enhancers to augment cancer immunotherapy.

In this study, we performed a systematic screening and identified a conserved circRNA cEMSY as a novel ICD-related circRNA in lung adenocarcinoma. cEMSY was dysregulated in lung adenocarcinoma, exhibiting lower expression levels in tumors than in normal tissues. Moreover, cEMSY played a distinct role in lung adenocarcinoma, whereas its expression did not affect cell viability in normal cells. Within the context of lung adenocarcinoma, cEMSY could induce ICD both in vitro and in vivo. Furthermore, the intratumoral administration of lipid nanoparticle (LNP)–encapsulated in vitro–transcribed cEMSY (cEMSY-LNP) sensitized lung adenocarcinoma cells to anti–PD-1 therapy. Mechanistically, cEMSY interacted with the RNA-binding protein (RBP) TAR DNA-binding protein 43 (TDP-43) via its RRM1 domain, inducing its aggregation in the mitochondria. This resulted in mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) leakage, activation of the cytosolic DNA–sensing cGAS–STING pathway, and a subsequent antiviral immune response. Clinically, we observed that patients with lung adenocarcinoma having higher cEMSY expression exhibited increased penetration of DCs and CD8+ T cells, as well as improved progression-free survival and response to ICI treatment. Collectively, our study revealed that the circRNA cEMSY was a potential ICD-inducing agent, highlighting the promise of circRNA-based strategies to augment cancer immunotherapy.

Materials and Methods

Tissue samples

The tissue samples from lung adenocarcinoma and matching normal tissues were obtained at Nanjing Medical University Affiliated Cancer Hospital (Jiangsu Cancer Hospital, Jiangsu Institute of Cancer Research, Nanjing, China) between 2013 and 2020. The diagnosis of lung adenocarcinoma was confirmed through histopathologic analysis by experienced pulmonary pathologists. RNA was extracted for qPCR using 40 lung adenocarcinoma samples and adjacent healthy tissues. Additionally, 65 lung adenocarcinoma tissues were used to develop tissue microarrays, followed by FISH and immunofluorescence (IF) analysis. Each sample was received from the Jiangsu Cancer Hospital’s biobank, and informed written consent was obtained from each participant or guardian. The detailed clinicopathologic data are shown in Supplementary Table S1.

Mice

Wild-type (WT) C57BL/6J mice (number N000013) and Rag1−/− mice (number T004753 C57BL/6-Rag1em1Cd3259/Gpt; ages 6 to 8 weeks) were acquired from GemPharmatech. WT T739 mice (ILAS-00) were purchased from the Cancer Institute Animal Center of the Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences and Beijing Union Medical College and maintained by following the guidelines authorized by the Nanjing Medical University Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee.

In the prophylactic cancer vaccination experiments, WT LLC1 cells were treated using the following regimens: pemetrexed (2.5 μmol/L) and CDDP (2.5 μmol/L); (R)-crizotinib (Criz; 10 μmol/L) and CDDP (150 μmol/L); dactinomycin (DACT; 1 μmol/L); oxaliplatin (500 μmol/L); bortezomib (100 nmol/L); or doxorubicin (DXR; 4 mmol/L) for 48 hours in 10-mm dishes. For LNP-based vaccination models, both WT LLC1 cells and LA795 cells were treated with cEMSY-LNP (20 μg) or DXR-LNP (4 mmol/L) for 48 hours in 10-mm dishes. After treatment, the cells were centrifuged, and the resulting pellet was washed with ice-cold PBS and then resuspended to achieve a density of 1 × 107 cells per milliliter. For vaccination, 100 μL of cell suspension was injected subcutaneously into the left flank of immunocompetent 8-week-old female C57BL/6 mice, delivering 1 × 106 cells per mouse. PBS and PBS-LNP served as negative controls. After 1 week, all mice were verified as tumor free at the injection site. Subsequently, the same kinds of live cancer cells (1 × 105 cells) were injected subcutaneously into the right flanks of immunized mice. The growth of the tumors was observed over the next several weeks, and the lack of tumors was interpreted as a sign of effective anticancer immunization.

For footpad vaccination assay, LLC1 cells were transfected with circTrim24(2,3,4S), circSlc33a(2,3,4,5,S6), circEmsy(10,11,12,13,14), or the control plasmid for 48 hours. For vaccination, 15 μL of the transfected cellular suspension was administered into the footpad of immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice at a density of 5 × 105 cells per mouse. The mice were euthanized 7 days after vaccination, and the vaccine-draining lymph nodes, specifically the popliteal lymph nodes, were harvested for analysis via flow cytometry.

The growth of transplanted tumors was initiated by subcutaneously injecting 1 × 106 LLC1 or LA795 cells, either overexpressing cEMSY or empty vector (EV), into the left flank of Rag1−/− or WT C57BL/6 mice (for LLC1) or T739 mice (for LA795). Tumor growth was consistently monitored in the subsequent weeks. For mice treated with T-cell depletion and CD11c cell neutralization antibodies, 100 μg of anti-CD4, anti-CD8, anti-CD11c, or a matched isotype control antibody (400 mg/dose) was injected intraperitoneally into the mice every 5 days starting from the day of tumor implantation, continuing for 2 weeks. Tumor development was observed over the ensuing weeks. A list of the antibodies used in this study is provided in Supplementary Table S2.

To verify the STING-dependent functionality of cEMSY-LNP, WT C57BL/6 mice were implanted subcutaneously with 1 × 106 LLC1 cells in the left flank. When the tumor volume reached 100 mm³, LLC1 tumor–bearing mice were intratumorally treated with cEMSY-LNP (10 μg/dose, every 2 days for three times) and/or H-151 (20 μg/dose, every 2 days for 2 weeks). LLC1 cells, either expressing truncated forms of TDP-43 or with co-knockout of TDP-43, were injected subcutaneously into the left flank to evaluate the impact of TDP-43 on cEMSY-LNP efficacy. Treatment with cEMSY-LNP (10 μg/dose, every 2 days for three doses) commenced when tumors reached a volume of 100 mm³. The survival analysis was conducted, in which mice were considered to have reached the endpoint when tumor volumes approached 3,000 mm³.

Furthermore, 1 × 106 WT LLC1 cells were injected subcutaneously into the left flank of C57BL/6 mice on day −7 to explore the potential synergistic effects of cEMSY-LNP with PD-1 therapy on immunosuppressive LLC1 tumors. Subsequently, 1 × 105 LLC1 cells were injected into the right flank on day 0. The mice were then divided into four groups (n = 8). The mice were injected intratumorally with 10 μg cEMSY-LNP on days 1, 3, and 5 on the left flank. After this, the mice were given 200 μg i.p. doses of anti–PD-1 + LNP as a combination treatment or PBS + LNP as a control treatment on days 5, 7, and 9. The growth of the tumors on the right flank was continuously monitored. The study’s endpoint was defined as the timepoint when the tumor on the left flank reached a volume of 2,000 mm³. Upon reaching this endpoint, the tumors on the right flank were removed for IF analysis to assess treatment efficacy, whereas the heart, liver, and kidneys were collected and subjected to hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining to evaluate potential systemic toxicity. For cytokine analysis, the plasma was separated on days 0, 3, and 10 after treatment with cEMSY-LNP. The following proteins in the plasma samples were detected using the corresponding ELISA kits following the manufacturer’s protocols: IL6, monocyte chemoattractant protein 1, TNFα, IL1β, and IFNβ. Supplementary Table S2 provides a list of ELISA kits used in this study.

For the immune memory model with lung metastasis, the left flank of C57BL/6 mice was injected subcutaneously with 1 × 106 WT LLC1-luc cells on day 0. The mice were then divided into four groups (n = 8). On days 6, 8, and 10, the mice received individual intratumoral injections of 10 μg cEMSY-LNP. From days 10 to 14, they received individual intraperitoneal injections of anti–PD-1 + LNP (200 μg) as a combination treatment or PBS + LNP as a control treatment. On day 20, the left-sided tumor was completely excised. On day 24, the mice were intravenously reinoculated via the tail vein with 1 × 105 WT LLC1-luc cells. Subsequent monitoring of pulmonary metastasis was facilitated using an IVIS spectrum imaging system (PerkinElmer) following intraperitoneal injection of D-luciferin (15 mg/mL, 200 μL) on day 36. In cases involving tumor rechallenge models, the mice exhibiting complete regression (the absence of regrowth after 1 month) of LLC1-luc tumors were reinjected with either LLC1 or MC38 cells into the left flank.

Cell culture and transfection

Human bronchial epithelial (HBE) cells and human lung adenocarcinoma cell lines (H358, A549, H23, H1975, PC9, A427, and H373), as well as murine lung adenocarcinoma cell lines (LLC1 and LA795), were obtained from the China Center for Type Culture Collection. The H358, PC9, A549, H23, H1975, A427, and H373 cell lines, along with all primary immune cells, were placed in RPMI1640 medium, which was enhanced with 1% penicillin–streptomycin and 10% FBS. Meanwhile, the HBE, LLC1, and LA795 cells were cultured in DMEM containing 10% FBS and 1% penicillin–streptomycin. The cells were carefully checked for Mycoplasma contamination, interspecies cross-infection, and authenticity before being used in experiments to ensure experimental integrity. Additionally, the cell lines used in the study were restricted to 20 passages to preserve dependability and consistency.

Single-guide RNAs were created for CRISPR/Cas9–mediated gene knockdown using the web-based CRISPR design tool (https://design.synthego.com/) and transformed into a vector lenti-CRISPR version 2 (Plasmid 49535, Addgene). The Supplementary Table S4 contains a list of the single-guide RNA–targeting sequences.

For lentivirus overexpressing mmu_circ_0013688 and hsa_circ_0008189, the target circRNA–encoding sequence and its partial intron sequence were inserted into the lentivirus circRNA expression vector pLV-circ-Lucipuro (Corues) through the enzyme digestion ligand. Then, the circRNA expression vector was packaged for lentivirus production. The transfected cells were screened for stable cell lines with puromycin. Plasmids overexpressing mmu_circ_0010753, mmu_circ_0013688, mmu_circ_0013341, FLAG-Tdp-43, and FLAG-truncated-Tdp-43 were created by the chemical synthesis of genes (Corues) using pcDNA3.1(+) or pCDH-CMVMCS-EF1copGFP-T2A-Puro as the supporting structure. The plasmids were transfected using Lipofectamine 3000 following the manufacturer’s protocols.

circRNA design and purification

The in vitro transcription for circRNA synthesis was performed as described in a previous study. Briefly, circRNA precursors were produced via runoff in vitro transcription using a T7 High Yield RNA Synthesis Kit (New England Biolabs) and a linearized plasmid DNA template. Subsequently, the synthesized circRNA was purified via lithium chloride precipitation and digested with RNase R to eliminate linear RNA contaminants. For high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), the circRNA was passed through an Agilent 2100 Series HPLC column using RNase-free Tris-EDTA (TE) buffer at a flow rate of 6 mL/minute. The circRNA was detected using UV absorbance at 260 nm, with circRNA fractions collected without UV detection. The resulting circRNA fractions were precipitated with 5 M ammonium acetate, resuspended in water, and subjected to additional treatment with RNase R as described previously. Finally, the cyclization integrity of the collected circRNA was validated through Sanger sequencing within the specified cyclization region.

Preparation and characterization of circRNA-loaded LNPs

The cEMSY-loaded LNPs were prepared by using a microfluidic method. Briefly, cEMSY was dissolved in 50 mmol/L pH 4.0 RNase-free citrate buffer at a concentration of 0.3 mg/mL. The lipids were dissolved in anhydrous ethanol. The total lipid concentration was 36.4 mmol/L. The lipid solution was then mixed with cEMSY solution in a 1:3 ratio using a microfluidic device and further diluted with Tris buffer. The diluted solution was transferred into ultrafiltration tubes (molecular weight cutoff, 100 kDa). The final product was obtained after diafiltration with 10 mmol/L Tris buffer (containing 10% sucrose) and sterile filtration and stored at −20°C until further use.

The average diameter and polydispersity index (PDI) of the cEMSY-loaded LNPs were measured by dynamic light scattering (Malvern Zetasizer Ultra). The circRNA content and encapsulation efficiency were determined using the Quant-iT RiboGreen Assay Kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. The circRNA integrity was measured by capillary electrophoresis using an Agilent 5200 fragment analyzer. The results showed that the LNPs had a mean size of 106.3 ± 0.61 nm, with a PDI of 0.114 ± 0.014. The encapsulation efficiency was 94.2%, and the circRNA integrity in LNPs was 75.2%.

DC development

Bone marrow–derived dendritic cells (BMDC) were produced in C57BL/6 mice. The bone marrow was flushed from femurs or tibias and sequentially filtered through 70-μm meshes (Biosharp) to remove aggregates. Then the cells were grown for 48 hours on 10-cm bacterial Petri plates in RPMI media supplemented with 20 ng/mL murine GM-CSF and 10 ng/mL murine IL4. The medium was replenished with fresh cytokines on day 5. BMDCs were obtained on day 7. BMDCs were cocultured with untreated, EV, or cEMSY-overexpressing cancer cells in a 1:1 ratio for 24 hours to evaluate DC activation. Subsequently, the markers of DC activation and maturation were analyzed by flow cytometry. Supplementary Tables S2 and S3 provide a list of the antibodies and cytokines used in this study, respectively.

Spleen CD8+ T-cell extraction

Splenocytes from C57BL/6 mice under different treatments (PBS + LNP, anti–PD-1 + LNP, cEMSY-LNP, and anti–PD-1 + cEMSY-LNP groups) or OT-I mice were gathered 7 to 10 days after the final treatment. Then red blood cell lysis buffer was used to lyse red blood cells, and the spleens were obtained. CD8+ T cells were then purified using a CD8+ T-cell isolation kit and used as the effector population.

Control, EV, and cEMSY-overexpressing cancer cells expressing ovalbumin (OVA) were cocultured with BMDCs in a 1:1 ratio for 24 hours to assess OT-I CD8+ T-cell stimulation by DCs. The BMDCs were then collected based on CD45 and CD11c and cocultured with OT-I CD8+ T cells tagged with carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester in a 1:10 ratio for 72 hours. The activation of OT-I CD8+ T cells was analyzed by flow cytometry to determine the CD69+ population within CD3+CD8+ cells. WT LLC1 cells were cocultured with CD8+ T cells in a 1:4 (LLC1:CD8+ T cell) ratio for 48 hours to evaluate the immune memory of splenic CD8+ T cells. The supernatants were collected, and TNFα and IFNγ levels were measured using the corresponding ELISA kits following the manufacturer’s protocol. After fixing, the adherent LLC1 tumor cells were stained with crystal violet.

Nucleocytoplasmic fractionation

Nucleocytoplasmic fractionation was performed using a commercial kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. The extracted nuclear and cytoplasmic RNAs were subjected to qPCR analysis to quantify the expression levels of target genes. GAPDH and U6 were used as cytoplasmic and nuclear reference genes, respectively, to determine the ratio of the nucleus to the cytoplasm and normalize the target gene expression levels. The primers used in this study are listed in Supplementary Table S4.

mtDNA release assay

The cytosolic and mitochondrial fractions were obtained from equal numbers of EV and cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 or LA795 cells using a mitochondrial isolation kit following the manufacturer’s protocol. mtDNA was extracted from the mitochondrial and cytosolic portions using the DNeasy Blood & Tissue Kit and quantified by qPCR; the primers used are listed in Supplementary Table S4. The mtDNA levels were normalized to the nuclear 18s gene sequence. For cGAS immunoprecipitation, anti-FLAG M2 affinity gel was incubated with FLAG-tagged mouse cGAS (pMIH-FLAG-mmcGAS) at 4°C for 2 hours with rotation. After extensive washing, DNA was extracted from the immunoprecipitates and purified. The relative DNA enrichment was analyzed using RT-PCR and normalized to GAPDH levels in total cell lysates.

For mtDNA depletion, EV and cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 or LA795 cells were cultured for 4 days, either with or without 150 ng/mL ethidium bromide. On day 4, the cells were gathered for in vitro suppression experiments as previously described.

Detection of ICD markers

The expression levels of ICD markers (DDIT3, CALR, ATF4, and B2M) were quantified using qPCR. The ELISA kit was used to measure the levels of HMGB1, IFNα, IFNβ, and CXCL10 in culture supernatants from cell lines or from resected tumors cultured ex vivo for 24 hours following the manufacturer’s protocols. ATP concentrations in supernatants were detected by luminescence using the CellTiter-Glo assay following the manufacturer’s protocols. The surface expression of HLA-I and MHC-I on cell lines was assessed by flow cytometry using fluorophore-conjugated antibodies specific to these molecules.

Flow cytometry

Enzymatic trypsinization was used to separate cells into individual units for analysis. Pipetting was used to extract nonadherent primary immune cells suspended in culture. Mouse-specific BD Fc Block was applied to mouse cells at 4°C for 15 minutes to reduce nonspecific combination. After a rinse, the cells were incubated with designated antibodies for 40 minutes at the same temperature. The procedure for isolating immune cells from tumor environments involved mincing the tumor tissues, followed by enzymatic breakdown using type-IV collagenase, hyaluronidase, and DNase at 37°C for 1 hour. The resulting mixtures were then passed through 70-μm nylon strainers (Biosharp). The surface antigens were detected by tagging the cells with suitable antibodies with BD Fc Block. The cells that were previously tagged on the surface were fixed using fixation and permeabilization solution, followed by three washes and staining with intracellular antibodies to detect intracellular markers. These cells were once again cleaned with Perm/Wash buffer. Flow cytometric analysis was conducted using a BD FACSAria Fusion system (BD Biosciences). The data were analyzed using FlowJo v10. Details on the antibodies conjugated for use in flow cytometry are provided in Supplementary Table S2.

Detection of mitochondrial status

Mitochondrial reactive oxygen species production was assessed by treating both EV and cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 or LA795 cells. This involved a 10-minute incubation at 37°C in Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution medium supplemented with either 5 mmol/L MitoSOX red dye or 5 mmol/L tetramethylrhodamine methyl ester, perchlorate (TMRM) dye. Subsequent to incubation, the cells were washed three times with Hank’s Balanced Salt Solution before being subjected to flow cytometric analysis. The resulting data on staining intensity were quantified by measuring the median fluorescence intensity using FlowJo v10. The chemicals used in the study are listed in Supplementary Table S3.

IF and FISH assay

For colocalization studies, EV or cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 or LA795 cells were plated at a density of 5,000 cells/well in confocal dishes (Corning) equipped with coverslips. After 24 hours, the cells were rinsed three times with ice-cold PBS and blocked at room temperature for 1 hour using 1× PBS solution containing 5% BSA. This was followed by overnight incubation, during which, the cells were exposed to various primary antibodies in the blocking solution. The dishes were then rinsed three times for 5 minutes each with 1× PBS at room temperature. Secondary antibodies were applied for 1 hour at room temperature, including anti-mouse Alexa Fluor 488 and anti-rabbit Alexa Fluor 568. After another set of three washes with 1× PBS at room temperature, the dishes were mounted with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole. The images were captured using an Axio Imager M2 (Carl Zeiss Ltd.) controlled by ZEN software (Carl Zeiss Ltd.). The image analysis was conducted using ImageJ software, in which the images were binary-converted, segmented using the watershed technique, and analyzed using the “Analyze Particles” tool. Mander overlap coefficients were computed using the JaCoP plugin. A list of antibodies used in the study is provided in Supplementary Table S2.

The RNA FISH kit was used for FISH tests. Initially, the cells were permeabilized and fixed with 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS. This was followed by hybridization overnight at 37°C in the dark. Cy3 and 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole channels were used to detect signals. Details on the FISH probes used in the study are provided in Supplementary Table S4. Tumor tissues were stained for IF using the TSA fluorescence double staining kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. The images were captured using an Axio Imager M2 (Carl Zeiss Ltd.), controlled via ZEN software (Carl Zeiss Ltd.), and analyzed using ImageJ. The analysis involved binary conversion of images and separation of dots using the watershed technique, followed by the application of the “Analyze Particles” tool.

Histology and IHC

The tissue samples were fixed in paraffin and deparaffinized by immersion in xylene baths, followed by rehydration through a graded ethanol series (100%, 95%, 85%, and 75%) for 5 minutes each. H&E staining was performed using Harris H&E Y alcoholic mixtures. After drying, the stained portions were mounted using organic mounting media. For IHC analysis, antigen retrieval was achieved by boiling the sections in 20 mmol/L sodium citrate buffer for 15 minutes, followed by ice bath incubation for 20 minutes and three PBS rinses. Endogenous peroxidase activity was quenched with 1% H2O2 for 15 minutes, and 5% goat serum was applied for 30 minutes to prevent nonspecific binding sites. The blocked sections were then incubated overnight using the designated primary antibody. The sections were then incubated with a horseradish peroxidase–conjugated secondary antibody for 45 minutes at room temperature the next day, after being washed three times with PBS for 5 minutes each. Subsequent to three 5-minute washes with distilled water, the tissue slices were prepared using diaminobenzidine tetrahydrochloride. Finally, the sections were dehydrated, sealed with neutral gum, and image acquisition and analysis performed using ImageJ software. The antibodies used in the study are listed in Supplementary Table S2.

Cell viability assay

The cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a density of 3.5 × 10³ cells per well for a 4-day viability assay. The cell viability was measured after 0, 24, 48, 72, and 96 hours using the CellTiter-Glo luminescent cell viability assay kit following the manufacturer’s protocol. The optical density at 450 nm was measured using an automatic microplate reader (BioTek).

Double-stranded RNA dot blot

The double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) dot blot analysis was carried out as reported in a previous study. Briefly, the cellular RNAs were extracted using TRIzol reagent. The extracted RNA was dotted on the Immobilon-Ny+ membrane (Merck) and subjected to incubation overnight with a dsRNA-specific J2 antibody. Subsequently, the membrane was incubated with a secondary antibody for 1 hour at room temperature. For visualization, enhanced chemiluminescence detection was used on the membrane, and the images were captured using the ChemiDoc Touch Imaging System (Bio-Rad).

circRNA pull-down assay

RNA pull-down studies were conducted using the Pierce Magnetic RNA-Protein Pull-Down Kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. In short, streptavidin magnetic beads were treated with 200 pmol of biotin-labeled RNA probes at 4°C for 1 hour. The biotinylated cEMSY or cEMSY probe sequence is provided in Supplementary Table S4, whereas the antisense sequences of each were used as the negative control probes. Immunoblotting and mass spectrometry (MS) were used to investigate the separated proteins. The proteins detected using MS are listed in Supplementary Table S5.

RNA immunoprecipitation

The RNA immunoprecipitation (RIP) test was carried out using the Magna RIP RNA-Binding Protein Immunoprecipitation Kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. In brief, magnetic beads were conjugated with 5 μg of either control IgG or anti–TDP-43 antibody and incubated with cell lysates overnight at 4°C. Subsequently, proteinase K was used to digest the proteins. The RNA that was immunoprecipitated was then isolated using TRIzol reagent and subjected to qPCR analysis. Supplementary Table S2 contains a list of the antibodies that were used.

Determination of mRNA half-life

After the stated treatment periods with 5 μmol/L actinomycin D, the cells were subjected to RNA extraction and cDNA synthesis. The remaining RNA levels were normalized to the starting level (0 hour) at the given timepoints. The kinetics of mRNA decay were evaluated using a one-phase exponential decay curve analysis (GraphPad Prism 8).

qPCR

TRIzol was used to extract total RNA from bulk tumor samples or single-cell specimens. The NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used to measure the RNA’s content and quality. The extracted RNA was reverse-transcribed using the RNA-to-cDNA Kit. The qPCR assays were conducted using SYBR Green Master Mix on either a QuantStudio 6 Flex system (Thermo Fisher Scientific) or a ViiA 7 Dx system (ABI), with specific primers listed in Supplementary Table S4.

Immunoblotting

The cells were first centrifuged at 1,000 rpm for 5 minutes and then washed three times with ice-cold PBS. The collected cells were subsequently lysed in RIPA buffer containing phosphatase with protease inhibitors at 4°C for 30 minutes. The supernatant was then collected after the lysate was cleared by centrifugation at 15,000g for 15 minutes at 4°C. The protein concentrations were measured using a bicinchoninic acid (BCA) protein assay. After denaturation, the lysates were placed onto polyvinylidene fluoride membranes (Merck) and electrophoresed on 4% to 20% SDS-PAGE gels. These membranes were blocked with 5% nonfat dry milk for 1 hour before overnight immunoblotting with specified primary antibodies. After primary antibody application, the membranes were exposed to the appropriate horseradish peroxidase–conjugated secondary antibodies to test for immunoreactivity, and the images were captured using the ChemiDoc Touch Imaging System (Bio-Rad). Supplementary Table S2 provides a list of the antibodies used in the study.

Whole-transcriptome sequencing

RNA extraction and quality control were conducted following the previously described protocol. Subsequently, sequencing libraries were created using the Illumina TruSeq Stranded Total RNA with Ribo-Zero Gold Kit following the manufacturer’s protocols. After pooling the libraries, sequencing was conducted on the Illumina HiSeq 2000 sequencing platform. CircRNA identification was performed using the circRNA identification toolkit.

Examination of immune checkpoint blockade and The Cancer Genome Atlas cohorts

Spearman correlations between our cEMSY signature and the abundance of DCs and CD8+ T cells in The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) dataset were computed using xCell, TIMER, CIBERSORT, and CIBERSORT-ABS. Human immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) datasets (GSE135222, GSE136961, and GSE78220) were acquired from openly accessible databases. The cEMSY signature levels of responders and nonresponders were compared after the samples were categorized based on the responders’ response status.

Statistical analysis

The statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 8. One- or two-way ANOVA tests and the unpaired Student t test were used as specified (ns, not significant; *, P < 0.05; **, P < 0.01; ***, P < 0.001). The Kaplan–Meier approach was used to estimate survival curves, and the log-rank test was used to compare them (univariate analysis). Additional information is provided in Supplementary Materials.

Data availability

Results

Identification of cEMSY as a potential ICD inducer

We used a spectrum of cellular stressors previously documented to elicit ICD, including anthracyclines (DXR), DNA-damaging agents (oxaliplatin), tyrosine kinase inhibitors [(R)-Criz], and platinum-based doublet chemotherapy (pemetrexed and CDDP chemotherapy), along with proteasomal inhibitor (bortezomib) and DNA intercalator (DACT; ref.), to treat LLC1 tumor cells for 48 hours (Fig. 1A) and observed a significant upregulation of the expression of surrogate ICD markers (Supplementary Fig. S1A–S1D). The successful induction of ICD was also confirmed using the prophylactic cancer vaccination models, which indicated that the mice injected subcutaneously with stressor-treated LLC1 cells could respond against the rechallenge by the same kind of live tumor cells injected into the opposite flank compared with the control group (Fig. 1A and B). Whole-transcriptome sequencing of stressor-treated LLC1 cells was performed to identify the potential ICD inducer derived from circRNAs, which revealed a significant upsurge in ICD-related mRNA expression (Supplementary Fig. S1E). A total of 467 upregulated circRNAs were identified, and 56 consistently upregulated across at least 3 ICD models were selected for in-depth analysis (Fig. 1C and D).

We constructed an ICD signature based on nine related biomarkers to explore the association between the circRNAs and the ICD signature using gene set variation analysis and identified seven circRNAs closely linked to ICD (Fig. 1E). Subsequently, we found three circRNAs highly homologous to those in human (Supplementary Fig. S1F). PCR and Sanger sequencing also confirmed the circRNA characteristics of these three candidates (Supplementary Fig. S1G–S1I). Furthermore, we used an in vivo footpad vaccination assay to identify potent ICD inducers, in which LLC1 cells overexpressing circRNAs were injected subcutaneously into the footpads of C57BL/6 mice. The cells overexpressing circEmsy (10, 11, 12, 13, 14) exhibited enhanced immunogenicity, as evidenced by the most prominent expression of DC maturation markers CD80 and CD86 in draining lymph nodes (Fig. 1F; Supplementary Fig. S1J). Furthermore, we confirmed that cEMSY was also upregulated by cellular stressors in the human H358 cell line (Supplementary Fig. S1K), consistent with previous findings. Overall, these findings underscored the significant association of circEmsy (10, 11, 12, 13, 14) with ICD and its role in augmenting cell immunogenicity, which was selected for further investigation.

 circEmsy (10, 11, 12, 13, 14) shared significant homology (93.14%) with its human counterpart circEMSY (10, 11, 12, 13, 14), both of which were conserved circRNAs produced by the backsplicing of exons 10 to 14 of the parental transcript of the Emsy/EMSY gene with a length of 831 nucleotides (thus termed cEMSY). DNA sequencing also confirmed the conserved backsplicing sites (Fig. 1G and H; Supplementary Fig. S2A). Moreover, cEMSY was resistant to RNase R digestion and had an extended half-life unlike its linear mRNA counterpart, validating its circular structure (Supplementary Fig. S2B and S2C). The analysis of 40 paired clinical lung adenocarcinoma samples and lung adenocarcinoma cell lines revealed a significant reduction in cEMSY levels in tumor tissues and cells (Supplementary Fig. S2D and S2E). Furthermore, IF and cell fractionation qPCR analysis revealed that the majority of cEMSY was located in the cytoplasm (Fig. 1I; Supplementary Fig. S2F). Additionally, to investigate the role of cEMSY under normal physiologic conditions, we performed overexpression and knockout (KO) experiments using HBE cells. We observed that neither overexpressing nor knocking out cEMSY affected the cell viability of HBE cells (Supplementary Fig. S2G and S2H). Finally, we stably overexpressed cEMSY in both murine LLC1 and human H358 cells, respectively (Supplementary Fig. S2I), and observed that both of them induced significant expression of ICD markers (6 of 10 in LLC1 and 5 of 10 in H358; Fig. 1J; Supplementary Fig. S3A–S3H), implying cEMSY as a potential immunogenic inducer.

Next, we generated cEMSY KO in the H358 and LLC1 cell lines (Supplementary Fig. S4A–S4F) to determine whether the overexpression of cEMSY was critical for the induction of ICD in the context of lung adenocarcinoma. The cellular stressors failed to induce cEMSY expression in the cEMSY-KO cell lines (Supplementary Fig. S4G and S4H). Subsequently, we investigated whether cellular stressors could still induce ICD in the context of cEMSY-KO cell lines. We compared the expression of ICD markers after treatment with cytotoxic drugs in KO cells and found that simply knocking out cEMSY did not significantly alter the expression of ICD markers, which was likely due to the low endogenous levels of cEMSY expression in tumor cells (Supplementary Fig. S4I–S4X). cEMSY KO reversed the upregulation of the expression of ICD markers induced by (R)-Criz + CDDP and DACT compared with that in WT cells, whereas this effect was not observed with DXR (Supplementary Fig. S4I–S4X). This was consistent with our previous findings, wherein (R)-Criz + CDDP and DACT significantly induced cEMSY expression but DXR did not (Supplementary Figs. S1H, S1K, and S4I–S4X). This indicated that the upregulation of cEMSY was crucial for the induction of ICD because neither (R)-Criz + CDDP nor DACT could fully induce ICD in the cEMSY-KO cell lines.

cEMSY elicits ICD-driven antitumor surveillance

Next, we explored the intracorporal role of cEMSY in immunocompetent mice. Murine lung adenocarcinoma LLC1 and LA795 cell lines stably overexpressing cEMSY were subcutaneously inoculated into the flanks of mice, which resulted in significantly reduced tumor growth compared with the control (Fig. 2A and B), as well as a prolonged survival (Supplementary Fig. S5A and S5B). Moreover, cEMSY overexpression induced a notable elevation in EIF2a phosphorylation (Supplementary Fig. S5C), which is a hallmark of ICD, along with an increase in the production of DAMPs such as extracellular ATP, HMGB1, IFNβ, and CXCL10 (Supplementary Fig. S5D–S5G). Although the adjuvanticity of ICD depends on the capability of cancer cells to elicit DAMPs, its immunogenicity depends on the priming of the host microenvironment for adaptive immunity. The antitumor effect observed in immunocompetent mice diminished in immunodeficient Rag1−/− mice, with cEMSY-overexpressing tumors growing at a rate comparable with controls in Rag1−/− mice (Fig. 2C). This suggested that adaptive immunity pressure was vital for the protective antitumor effect of cEMSY. Meanwhile, the flow cytometry analysis of tumor tissues revealed that cEMSY significantly enhanced the accumulation of CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, activated CD69+CD44+CD8+ T cells, and polyfunctional IFNγ+TNFα+CD8+ T cells (Fig. 2D–F; Supplementary Fig. S5H). DCs are professional antigen-presenting cells that facilitate the connection between innate and adaptive immunity responses by presenting antigens to T lymphocytes with antitumor activities. We found an increased proportion of CD11c+MHC-II+ DCs in cEMSY tumors, but not in other antigen-presenting cells such as macrophages, B cells, or NK cells (Fig. 2G; Supplementary Fig. S5I). Meanwhile, we further delineated the contributions of various immune populations to the suppression of tumor growth using lineage-specific antibodies to deplete each population and found that the antitumor impact of cEMSY in mice was considerably attenuated only by depleted CD8+ T cells or DCs, whereas CD4+ T-cell depletion had a moderate effect (Fig. 2H).

In the context of ICD, the release of DAMPs by tumor cells acts as adjuvant stimuli, facilitating DC activation and maturation. To evaluate this in vitro, we coincubated BMDCs with cEMSY-overexpressing cancer cells, accompanied by significantly enhanced expression of DC activation and maturation markers CD86 and CD80 (Fig. 2I; Supplementary Fig. S5J). Next, we investigated whether cEMSY overexpression led to more effective acquisition and presentation of tumor antigens by DCs. In light of this, we first cocultured BMDCs with either control or cEMSY-overexpressing cancer cells pulsed with OVA (LLC1-OVA/LA795-OVA). Subsequently, OT-I CD8+ T cells, which were able to recognize the OVA-derived SIINFEKL peptide specifically, were cocultured with these BMDCs. Our findings revealed that BMDCs preexposed to OVA-expressing cEMSY cancer cells more effectively induced activation (via CD69 expression) and proliferation (via carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester) of OT-I CD8+ T cells when compared with those preexposed to control cells (Fig. 2J–L). Meanwhile, the direct co-culture of OVA-expressing cEMSY cancer cells with OT-I CD8+ T cells did not activate the T cells (Supplementary Fig. S5K), underscoring the indispensable function of DCs in cEMSY-mediated CD8+ T-cell activation. Collectively, these observations affirmed the potent immunogenicity of cEMSY-expressing cancer cells, which significantly enhanced immune recruitment, DC activation, and effective cytotoxic T lymphocyte priming both in vivo and in vitro.

The tumor-suppressive capabilities of cEMSY inspired our investigation into its use as a therapeutic target in lung adenocarcinoma. Recent advancements in the synthesis of circRNAs have paved the way for a new generation of RNA-based therapeutics. Hence, we synthesized circRNA cEMSY using a permuted intron–exon splicing technique and used HPLC for its efficient purification (Supplementary Fig. S6A). RNase R digestion of the precursor RNA Emsy yielded three distinct bands, as compared with the single band observed for cEMSY, which was indicative of its circularity (Supplementary Fig. S6B). Additionally, further validation by Sanger sequencing confirmed the validity of the specific cyclization area (Supplementary Fig. S6C). Considering the clinical application challenges associated with naked mRNA, including its vulnerability to exonuclease degradation and the impeded cellular uptake due to the negative charge of mRNA and substantial molecular weight, LNPs are a viable delivery system, offering protection against nuclease degradation and facilitating intracellular delivery. Hence, we first assessed the kinetics of LNP uptake and translation. Luciferase mRNA was rapidly detectable in transfected LLC1 cells, and the transfection efficiency in the group of LNPs was higher than that of Lipofectin (Supplementary Fig. S6D). In vivo studies corroborated these findings, with intratumoral injection of luciferase mRNA-LNP reaching peak luciferase expression at 12 hours after injection, sustaining the expression for at least 48 hours (Supplementary Fig. S6E).

Next, we tested the capacity of circularized cEMSY to induce ICD in vivo using a prophylactic cancer vaccination model. The findings revealed that mice injected with in vitro cEMSY-LNP–treated cancer cells exhibited protection against subsequent challenges with live tumor cells in the contralateral flank (Fig. 2M). Therefore, in vitro–transcribed circRNA cEMSY effectively triggered ICD in tumor cells in immunocompetent mice.

 In vitro–transcribed cEMSY synergizes with ICB

Given the low immunogenicity of LLC1 cells and resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, we explored the potential of intratumoral injection of cEMSY-LNP vaccination to prime ICD and efficiency of immunotherapy in LLC1 models. Mice bearing primary WT LLC1 tumors for 7 days received a contralateral secondary WT LLC1 tumor injection. The secondary tumor growth monitoring indicated that combined treatment with cEMSY-LNP and anti–PD-1 markedly enhanced anticancer protection (represented by a prolonged latency period and a decreased tumor development rate) and DAMP release, outperforming the effects observed with either cEMSY-LNP or anti–PD-1 (similar to that in the control group; all of the mice developed tumors) monotherapy (Fig. 3A; Supplementary Fig. S7A–S7D). The multiplex IF analysis of the secondary tumor sites demonstrated that the coadministration of cEMSY-LNP and anti–PD-1 facilitated the localization of CD8+ T cells around DC niches, which were in close contact (within a distance of 20 μm; Fig. 3B). Additionally, we evaluated toxicity after cEMSY-LNP treatment. Histologic examination using H&E staining revealed no significant morphologic changes in major organs after intratumoral administration of cEMSY-LNP (Supplementary Fig. S7E). Furthermore, we measured the levels of inflammatory factors in the serum at different timepoints. Although cEMSY-LNP significantly increased the levels of IFNβ and monocyte chemoattractant protein 1, which may be partially attributed to the induction of ICD and the mobilization of immune cells, the levels of TNF, IL1β, and IL6 remained largely unchanged (Supplementary Fig. S7F–S7J), indicating that cEMSY-LNP has no significant toxicity. These findings suggested that cEMSY-LNP administration could initiate effective CD8+ T-cell activation, underscoring the synergistic effect of combination with anti–PD-1 therapy in overcoming LLC1 resistance to ICB.

We hypothesized that the induction of ICD in tumors by cEMSY-LNP could promote memory responses and, in principle, also impede the progression of nontreated distal tumors and metastases. After therapeutic interventions of cEMSY-LNP and/or anti–PD-1 in mice bearing LLC1, primary tumors were surgically removed, and the mice were subsequently challenged with an intravenous injection of WT LLC1-luc cells 4 days after surgery. Bioluminescence imaging revealed that mice with significant lung metastases were distributed in the group treated with the control or anti–PD-1. Nevertheless, cEMSY-LNP treatment significantly decelerated the growth of lung metastases, and the addition of anti–PD-1 yielded an even more pronounced reduction in metastatic burden (Fig. 3C). The survival analyses demonstrated that 37.25% of mice who received cEMSY-LNP + anti–PD-1 combination treatment survived beyond 50 days (Fig. 3D). Furthermore, the isolation of activated CD8+ T cells from the spleens of mice treated with different interventions and their coculture with WT LLC1 cells revealed that treatment with cEMSY-LNP + anti–PD-1 significantly augmented the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T cells, accompanied by the increased production of TNFα and IFNγ (Fig. 3E–G), indicating that cEMSY-LNP treatment induced an immune memory effect. Moreover, mice previously cleared of tumors through cEMSY-LNP treatment still exhibited complete resistance to rechallenge with WT LLC1 cells 30 days later, in contrast to tumor-naïve mice. However, this protective effect did not extend to unrelated murine colon adenocarcinoma cells (MC38; Fig. 3H), implying that the induction of prolonged T-cell immune responses to tumors was tumor specific. In summary, these findings indicated that cEMSY-LNP could induce ICD in poorly immunogenic tumors, enhance the efficacy of anti–PD-1 therapy, and establish durable antigen-specific immunologic memory.

cEMSY provokes antiviral innate immune responses

We conducted a comprehensive transcriptome analysis on EV and circRNA-overexpressed murine and human lung adenocarcinoma cell lines to investigate the mechanisms by which cEMSY augmented ICB therapeutic efficacy and triggered ICD. The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes and Gene Ontology biological processes pathway enrichment analyses revealed that cEMSY activation stimulated pathways related to immune defense and innate antiviral responses in LLC1 cells (Fig. 4A). Meanwhile, gene set enrichment analysis of differentially expressed genes identified co-enrichment of gene set characteristics of IFNα and IFNγ responses in cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 cells (Fig. 4B). Similarly, the predominant biological processes affected were associated with antiviral defense and the innate immune response in H358 cells overexpressing cEMSY (Supplementary Fig. S8A and S8B). During a viral infection of host cells, viral nucleic acid molecules trigger host innate immune pathways via the activation of transcription factors NF-κB and IRF3 in host cells, which, in turn, promotes IFN expression and stimulates the secretion of proinflammatory cytokines (Fig. 4C; ref.), a pathway also implicated in ICD induction. In line with these mechanisms, cEMSY led to an upregulation of proinflammatory cytokines (Supplementary Fig. S8C). Western blot analysis also confirmed enhanced activation of pivotal antiviral signaling molecules, including phospho-IRF3, phospho–NF-κB p65, and phospho-STAT1, in both murine and human cancer cells (Fig. 4D; Supplementary Fig. S8D). These findings suggested that cEMSY bolstered an innate antiviral immune response in lung adenocarcinoma cells, potentially underpinning the observed enhancement of ICB therapy and induction of ICD.

The initiation of antiviral immune response is typically predicated on the detection of anomalous cytoplasmic dsRNA or dsDNA by respective cellular sensors. However, cEMSY significantly elevated the protein expression of both cytosolic dsRNA sensors (MDA5, RIG1, and PKR) and dsDNA sensors (cGAS; Fig. 4E). Importantly, these sensors were part of the downstream of the antiviral pathway, forming a positive feedback loop that amplified the antiviral signaling. We therefore disrupted cytoplasmic RNA- and DNA-sensing pathways by, respectively, deleting the signaling adapters (MAVS and STING) and the sensor (PKR) in cEMSY-overexpressing cancer cells (Supplementary Fig. S8E). Only STING deletion abrogated the surge in proinflammatory cytokines, whereas the absence of MAVS did not (Fig. 4F). Additionally, cEMSY overexpression led to detectable increases in the levels of cGAMP, a secondary messenger synthesized by cGAS activation (Fig. 4G; ref.). Consistently, the cGAS–STING–TBK1 cascade activation was observed after cEMSY overexpression in A549 and H358 cells (Supplementary Fig. S8F). These results implicated that cEMSY activated the cGAS–STING signaling axis, contributing to the antiviral immune response in lung adenocarcinoma.

Furthermore, cGAS has been documented to sense dsDNA originating from either mitochondrial or nuclear compartments. We immunoprecipitated FLAG-tagged cGAS in cEMSY-overexpressing LLC1 and LA795 cells to elucidate the specific DNA source engaging cGAS in cEMSY overexpression, revealing that not the nuclear DNA-like LINE1 elements or the rDNA RNA 18s but mtDNA was enriched in cGAS with cEMSY overexpression (Fig. 4H). Cytosolic fractionation and Western blot analyses also confirmed an enrichment of transcription factor A (one of the core components of the mtDNA nucleoid) in the cytosolic fractions of cells overexpressing cEMSY (Fig. 4I; Supplementary Fig. S8G). Conversely, we observed that cEMSY triggered the colocalization of cGAS and transcription factor A (Fig. 4J), without altering total mtDNA levels (Supplementary Fig. S8H), demonstrating that cEMSY induced mtDNA leakage to the cytosol (Supplementary Fig. S8I). Although previous research associated ERV transcription and circRNAs with dsRNA formation, our qPCR and dot blot results indicated no dsRNA accumulation after cEMSY overexpression in two cell lines (Supplementary Fig. S8J and S8K). We generated mtDNA-depleted cell lines (known as r0) by culturing them with ethidium bromide at a low dosage to further confirm the role of mtDNA in cEMSY-induced cGAS–STING antiviral signaling (Supplementary Fig. S8L; ref.). The activation of cGAS–STING and antiviral immunity downstream upon cEMSY overexpression were attenuated in r0 cells (Fig. 4K). Extending these findings in vivo, we subcutaneously implanted r0 LLC1 and LA795 cells into immunocompetent mice. Consistent with in vitro results, the KO of mtDNA significantly reversed the cEMSY-mediated antitumor effects (Supplementary Fig. S9A and S9B) and activation of ICD (Supplementary Fig. S9C–S9O), indicating that mtDNA plays a crucial role in mediating the antiviral pathways activated by cEMSY, both in vivo and in vitro. Meanwhile, the treatment with the STING inhibitor H-151, largely abolished the therapeutic effect of cEMSY-LNP (Fig. 4L; Supplementary Fig. S9P). Overall, we identified that cEMSY triggered the cGAS–STING antiviral signaling activation by provoking the mitochondrion-derived mtDNA leakage into the cytosol.

cEMSY modulates immune responses via promoting the mitochondrial entry of TDP-43

circRNAs can perform biological functions by modulating the transcription of their parental genes via distinct RNA–RNA interactions, which was not evident for the cEMSY in our analysis of lung adenocarcinoma samples (Supplementary Fig. S10A). Recognizing that circRNAs typically exert their functions in concert with protein-binding partners, we performed an RNA pull-down assay using a biotin-labeled probe against the junction site, which was confirmed as an enrichment of cEMSY (Supplementary Fig. S10B). Subsequent analyses via SDS-PAGE, silver staining, and LC/MS identified 12 candidate proteins specifically binding to the cEMSY probe in LLC1 and LA795 cells (Fig. 5A and B; Supplementary Table S5). Western blot analysis identified TDP-43 as the exclusive RBP of cEMSY among the top five RBPs (Fig. 5C; Supplementary Fig. S10C). Moreover, RIP assay confirmed significant enrichment of cEMSY bound to TDP-43 in LLC1 and LA795 cells (Fig. 5D). We also validated the interaction of cEMSY with TDP-43 in human cell lines, which was first analyzed using the circAtlas 3.0 (https://ngdc.cncb.ac.cn/circatlas/) dataset, and revealed that TDP-43 was the candidate RBP of cEMSY (ranked eighth; Supplementary Fig. S10D). Validation with a biotin-labeled cEMSY probe in H358 cells confirmed this interaction (Supplementary Fig. S10E), underscoring a conserved and non–cell type–specific binding between cEMSY and TDP-43.

Next, we evaluated whether the cGAS–STING antiviral pathway induced by cEMSY depended on TDP-43. TDP-43 can shuttle between the nucleus and cytoplasm. Also, its aggregation within the mitochondria has been associated with acute organ injuries and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) through mitochondrial malfunction and mitochondrion-dependent cell death. Using confocal IF analyses, we depicted the colocalization of mitochondria (MitoTracker, red) and TDP-43 (green) in the presence of cEMSY overexpression, suggesting that cEMSY might promote TDP-43 mitochondrial aggregation (Fig. 5E). Subsequent cytosol–mitochondria fractionation also verified mitochondrial accumulation of TDP-43 in both cEMSY-/LNP-treated murine and cEMSY-overexpressing human lung adenocarcinoma cells (Fig. 5F; Supplementary Fig. S10F), without altering total TDP-43 protein levels (Supplementary Fig. S10G). Mitochondrial TDP-43 aggregation led to mitochondrial instability and mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) opening, facilitating mtDNA egress to the cytosol. Consistent with this cascade, cEMSY overexpression elicited increased reactive oxygen species production and membrane potential loss (mΔφ) in LLC1 and LA795 cells (Fig. 5G; Supplementary Fig. S10H). Moreover, the mPTP inhibitor cyclosporine A blocked the increase in mtDNA leakage and decreased it to baseline within 60 minutes (Fig. 5H). Thus, cEMSY attracted TDP-43 and caused its accumulation in the mitochondria, causing mtDNA leakage into the cytosol via mPTP.

TDP-43, an evolutionarily conserved RBP across eukaryotes, contains two RNA-binding domains, RRM1 and RRM2, which are pivotal for its interaction with RNA molecules. Full-length (FL) and individual truncated forms of TDP-43 were overexpressed in both LLC1 and H358 cells to depict the domain required for interacting with cEMSY. Subsequent RNA pull-down assays and subsequent Western blot analysis identified the RRM1 domain as crucial for this interaction (Fig. 5I; Supplementary Fig. S10I). We conducted rescue experiments in TDP-43-KO LLC1 cells to investigate the functional dependency of cEMSY on TDP-43 (Supplementary Fig. S10J). The reintroduction of FL TDP-43, but not its RRM1-deleted variant (Δ1), restored the cEMSY-induced enhancement of mtDNA cytosolic leakage (Fig. 5J; Supplementary Fig. S10K). Similarly, this phenomenon was also reflected in downstream signaling molecules [cGAS, STING, phosphorylated TANK-binding kinase 1 (pTBK1)], phospho-IRF3, phospho–NF-κB, and phospho-STAT1), underscoring that the cEMSY-interacting domain RRM1 was required for the observed cellular phenotypes (Fig. 5K). We subcutaneously inoculated TDP-43-FL–overexpressed or TDP-43-Δ1–overexpressed LLC1 cells with TDP-43-KO into the flanks of immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice to consolidate our observation in vivo. TDP-43-Δ1 overexpression markedly reduced antitumor activities of cEMSY-LNP and impeded the accumulation of intratumoral immune cells (Fig. 5L and M; Supplementary Fig. S10L). Taken together, we concluded that the roles of cEMSY in ICD depended on its interaction with TDP-43, particularly via its RRM1 domain.

cEMSY expression correlated with a favorable immunotherapy response in patients with lung adenocarcinoma

We systematically analyzed the role of cEMSY in T-cell–mediated antitumor immunity and therapeutic response in lung adenocarcinoma and other solid tumors. First, we used a tissue microarray containing 65 human lung adenocarcinoma tumors and performed multicolor IF staining to simultaneously detect cEMSY, CD11c, and CD8. It revealed that the elevated cEMSY expression level in lung adenocarcinoma cells was related to a high density of DCs and CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6A and B). Moreover, patients exhibiting increased cEMSY expression had prolonged disease-free survival (Fig. 6C). Additionally, we developed a cEMSY signature based on the top 31 transcripts enriched after cEMSY overexpression in H358 cells. We observed that the cEMSY signature was positively correlated with the infiltration of DCs and CD8+ T cells, as inferred by xCell, TIMER, CIBERSORT, and CIBERSORT-ABS in the TCGA cohorts (Fig. 6D).

Next, we examined two different cohorts of patients with lung adenocarcinoma treated with anti–PD-1 antibodies (GSE135222 and GSE136961) to assess how cEMSY affected the result of ICB therapy. A higher expression of the cEMSY signature was linked to enhanced ICB responsiveness and superior progression-free survival rates in these cohorts (Fig. 6E and F). Similar results were obtained among a group of patients with melanoma given anti–PD-1 (GSE78220; Supplementary Fig. S11A and S11B). Additionally, we observed that the cEMSY signature positively correlated with improved overall survival in diverse cancer types, including lung adenocarcinoma, uterine carcinosarcoma, sarcoma, and skin cutaneous melanoma (Supplementary Fig. S11C–S11F). Collectively, our data underscored a consistent correlation between cEMSY expression and favorable clinical outcomes and responses to ICB therapy across various cancers.

Discussion

Our study unveiled a novel and conserved circRNA cEMSY that could trigger ICD in lung adenocarcinoma cells and induce the hallmarks of ICD both in vitro and in vivo. For a translational application, the intratumoral administration of cEMSY-LNP boosted anti–PD-1 immunotherapy in the immunosuppressive tumor model, retarded tumor growth, alleviated lung metastasis, and prolonged the survival period with no apparent toxicities. Mechanistically speaking, cEMSY interacted with the RBP TDP-43 via its RRM1 domain, which promoted the aggregation of TDP-43 in the mitochondria, resulting in the leakage of mtDNA into the cytosol and the initiation of the cGAS–STING antiviral immune response. Finally, the analysis of patient samples revealed that cEMSY expression positively correlated with the infiltration of DCs and CD8+ T cells, as well as an improved response to ICB therapy.

Only a minority of patients with advanced NSCLC displayed a response to ICIs, which could be ascribed to the “cold” tumor environment in most patients, which lacked tumor-infiltrating T cells. Inducing ICD represents a promising strategy to sensitize tumors to ICI therapy by increasing tumor immunogenicity and promoting antitumor immunity. However, most standard chemotherapeutic regimens, including CDDP and gemcitabine, are deemed non–ICD-inducing monotherapies. Meanwhile, most ICD inducers, such as (R)-Criz and radiation, are administered at supraphysiologic doses to induce ICD, leading to severe toxicities and adverse effects. Thus, identifying novel low-toxicity ICD inducers is imperative for improving cancer immunotherapy. The potential of circRNAs as an RNA-based therapy has gained significant interest with the rapid development and FDA approval of the mRNA vaccine against SARS-CoV-2. A small amount of circRNAs can produce a sustained response because of their exceptional stability and endurance, offering superior safety compared with other RNA therapies. Previous studies reported that the in vivo delivery of circRNAs encoding antigens could elicit a durable antitumor T-cell response. Our study pioneered the recognition of circRNA as a potential ICD inducer in lung adenocarcinoma. The intratumoral injection of cEMSY-LNP significantly enhanced the antitumor immunity elicited by anti–PD-1 antibodies. No significant toxic side effects were observed in major organs after treatment. Furthermore, cEMSY-LNP treatment elicited durable and tumor-specific immunologic memory, conferring long-term protection against tumor rechallenge, suggesting that circRNAs as ICD inducers might offer a promising and potentially safer alternative with better tolerability.

STING regulates both immune-dependent and immune-independent processes as a multifunctional adapter. However, the role of cGAS–STING signal is not consistent even within the same biological setting. For instance, activating STING signaling in dormant tumor cells in metastasis can inhibit their transition from an indolent state to aggressive metastasis. Conversely, another study demonstrated that continuous chromosomal segregation errors promoted cell invasion and metastasis in a cGAS–STING–dependent manner. Similar observations have been noted in the mtDNA-induced cGAS–STING pathway activation. On the one hand, the mtDNA-activated cGAS–STING pathway exerts an antitumor immune effect by activating NF-κB and type I IFN signals. On the other hand, minimal mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization–induced cytosolic leakage of mtDNA activates the cGAS–STING pathway, driving the senescence-associated secretory phenotype and promoting immune evasion in senescent cells. The dichotomous role of the cGAS–STING pathway seems to be dictated by the quantity and intensity of its upstream substrates: Abundant and acute substrates promote immune activation, inducing immune cells to eliminate tumor cells, whereas the presence of slight, persistent substrates triggers chronic immune responses, leading to an immunosuppressive phenotype. The present study revealed that cEMSY-induced TDP-43 mitochondrial aggregation led to mitochondrial dysfunction, resulting in substantial mtDNA release from mPTP into the cytosol. This activated the mtDNA-sensing cGAS–STING pathway and downstream antiviral signaling cascades, ultimately inducing ICD and promoting immune activation. Moreover, using the STING inhibitor H-151 and truncated forms of TDP-43, we confirmed that the antitumor immune response of cEMSY-LNP in vivo also depended on TDP-43 binding and the STING pathway. Previous studies have shown that the delivery of unmodified circRNAs can nonspecifically activate the innate immune system. Indeed, we found that cEMSY-LNP treatment showed a trend toward reduced tumor growth compared with PBS-LNP treatment in the H-151- or TDP-43-KO groups, albeit without statistical significance. This further suggested that the binding to TDP-43 and subsequent cGAS–STING pathway activation were the key mechanisms for the therapeutic effect of cEMSY on tumor cells, shedding light on potential mechanisms by which circRNAs regulated cellular processes.

Although our findings are promising, several aspects deserve further investigation. The exact mechanism by which TDP-43 aggregation causes mtDNA leakage remains to be fully elucidated. Testing other tumor models, such as melanoma, can help generalize the therapeutic potential of this approach. Moreover, optimizing the administration methods and the pharmacokinetics/pharmacodynamics of the cEMSY construct can further improve the therapeutic efficacy. Finally, exploring combinations with other immunotherapies, such as chimeric antigen receptor T cells, warrants examination.

In conclusion, we identified cEMSY as a novel ICD inducer in lung adenocarcinoma cells, bolstering antitumor immunity and augmenting the impact of ICIs. Elucidating the cEMSY–TDP-43–mtDNA–cGAS–STING axis in ICD induction can provide mechanistic insights into the immunomodulatory functions of circRNAs. Our findings pave the way for developing circRNA-based immunotherapeutic strategies and emphasize the potential of cEMSY as a viable option for cancer immunotherapy, either alone or in combination with other immunotherapies.
