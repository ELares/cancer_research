# Oral Lichenoid lesions induced by programmed cell death protein 1 and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 bispecific antibody: a case report.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Cadonilimab is the first approved dual immune checkpoint inhibitor targeting programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4), currently utilized for the treatment of various solid tumors. Oral mucosal adverse reactions, such as oral lichenoid lesions, represent one of the most prevalent immune-related adverse events associated with immune checkpoint antibodies. However, reports detailing oral side effects specifically linked to Cadonilimab are lacking. Documenting these side effects is essential to alert oncologists and stomatologists, facilitating timely intervention for affected patients.

**CASE PRESENTATION**: We present a case involving a 35-year-old male patient diagnosed with hepatocellular carcinoma who received Cadonilimab following hepatectomy and subsequently developed extensive oral lichenoid lesions along with mucosal erosion at 13-14 weeks post-treatment initiation. A biopsy was conducted revealing immunohistochemical findings of CD3+, CD4+, CD8+, CD20 + lymphocytes, CD68 + macrophages, and α-SMA + myofibroblasts infiltrating the tissue of the oral lichenoid lesions. The patient's oral lesions improved after administration of systemic and local glucocorticoid therapy alongside cessation of Cadonilimab treatment.

**CONCLUSION**: This report marks the first documented instance of an oral adverse effect associated with Cadonilimab use. It underscores that administration of this agent may lead to significant lichenoid lesions and erosions within the oral cavity-an issue warranting increased vigilance from both oncologists and stomatologists.

Background

Immune checkpoint inhibitors have emerged as a standard treatment modality for a diverse array of tumor types. Adverse reactions affecting the skin (including vitiligo, exacerbation of psoriasis, lichenoid dermatitis, dermatomyositis, and lupus erythematosus) and oral mucosa (such as oral lichenoid lesions) are among the most prevalent immune-related adverse events associated with immune checkpoint antibodies. Current regulatory approvals have primarily concentrated on two inhibitory pathways: programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 (CTLA-4). Several anti-PD-1 antibodies have received global approval for the management of various malignancies. Nevertheless, certain studies indicate that the efficacy of anti-PD-1 monotherapy may be inadequate. The combination therapy utilizing both PD-1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors has demonstrated significant improvements in overall survival across multiple cancer types. The development of bispecific antibodies targeting PD-1/CTLA-4 represents a promising clinical strategy for oncological treatment. Cadonilimab (AK104) is recognized as the first PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific antibody drug independently developed in China and was also the first approved dual-target immune antibody drug globally in 2022. Currently, it is being utilized to treat various solid tumors including cervical cancer, lung cancer, gastric/gastroesophageal junction carcinoma, esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, liver cancer, and nasopharyngeal carcinoma. However, reports regarding adverse effects associated with Cadonilimab remain scarce. In this report, we present a case involving a patient with hepatocellular carcinoma who underwent treatment with Cadonilimab following hepatectomy and subsequently developed extensive oral lichenoid lesions along with mucosal erosions approximately 13 to 14 weeks post-treatment. This represents the first documented instance of oral adverse effects associated with Cadonilimab. Additionally, this paper provides a concise discussion on the clinical and histological characteristics as well as therapeutic recommendations for managing oral lesions induced by immune checkpoint inhibitors, aiming to furnish valuable insights and references for future clinical strategies in preventing and treating oral adverse reactions subsequent to Cadonilimab therapy.

Case presentation

On October 18, 2023, a 35-year-old male patient presented to our department with the primary complaint of white striae, mucosal roughness, and painful lesions persisting for over a month on his oral mucosa. A review of his medical history revealed that on May 11, 2023, he was diagnosed with primary liver cancer in the right lobe (BCLC-A stage, CNLC-1b stage, Edmondson grade I-III), alongside a long-standing history of chronic viral hepatitis B and cirrhosis. The patient has a smoking history exceeding 20 years and engages in dietary habits that include consuming raw fish and meat. He underwent resection for hepatocellular carcinoma on May 16, 2023, followed by treatment with Cadonilimab (AK104) post-surgery. Cadonilimab (AK104) was administered via injection at a dosage of 10 mg/kg every three weeks on July 7, July 28, August 17, September 7, and September 27 of the same year. Approximately one to two weeks after receiving Cadonilimab treatment on September 7, the patient noted the emergence of white patches on the left buccal mucosa accompanied by roughness. Following the fifth administration of Cadonilimab on September 27, 2023, there was a progressive increase in the extent of white patches. Subsequently, lesions characterized by white patches and stripes appeared on the right buccal and lip mucosa, followed by erosions on the buccal mucosa and exudative crusts on the lower lip mucosa. After the fifth infusion of Cadonilimab, the patient was diagnosed with a recurrence of liver cancer. Cadonilimab therapy was discontinued, and the second surgery was performed on October 23, 2023. In addition to Cadonilimab, he was administered Entecavir for the treatment of hepatitis B, a medication that has been utilized for over a decade without any occurrence of oral white lesions or erosions during the treatment period. The remaining medications were unremarkable.

The extra-oral examination revealed that the skin in the webbed space between the ring and middle fingers of the left hand exhibited white mossy lesions measuring 0.5 cm by 0.4 cm. Oral examination indicated crusting, bleeding, and short white stripes on the lower labial red mucosa, which was tender and painful. A significant presence of rough white stripes and plaques was observed in the buccal vestibular sulcus adjacent to tooth 47 and on the buccal mucosa. Additionally, scattered erosions were noted in both mid and posterior regions of the buccal mucosa, with maximum dimensions of 0.6 cm by 0.4 cm and minimum dimensions of 0.3 cm by 0.3 cm (Fig. 1A-D). The patient underwent a biopsy of the right buccal mucosa; pathological examination revealed a slightly disordered arrangement of epithelial cells, hyperkeratosis, epithelial spikes, subepithelial blisters, increased capillary density within lamina propria, as well as mild zonal infiltration by lymphocytes and plasma cells in the biopsy specimen from the oral buccal mucosa. Direct immunofluorescence analysis demonstrated an absence of IgG deposition at the epithelial basement membrane. Immunohistochemical staining showed infiltration by CD3+, CD4+, CD8+, CD20 + lymphocytes; CD68 + macrophages; and α-SMA + myofibroblasts within the biopsy specimen from the oral buccal mucosa (Fig. 2). Considering his medical history alongside clinical evaluations and pathological findings, a diagnosis of drug-induced oral lichenoid lesions (OLL) attributed to Cadonilimab was established for this patient. Treatment involved systemic and topical glucocorticoids including orally administered prednisolone acetate (15 mg once daily), a solution containing dexamethasone sodium phosphate at a concentration of 0.08 mg/ml, along with a chlorhexidine gluconate compound gargle solution at a concentration of 0.12% (to be rinsed for 5 minutes three times daily). For managing lip lesions specifically, we instructed local wet packing using gauze soaked in chlorhexidine gluconate compound gargle solution prior to gently removing softened crusts followed by application of prednisolone acetate suspension (125 mg:5 ml three times daily).

After this week-long treatment course, the patient did not return to our clinic as scheduled but informed us via phone in July that his pain and discomfort had resolved completely. During his most recent visit on September 19, 2024, it was noted that his oral mucosal lichenoid lesions along with other symptoms had fully healed without any signs of recurrence.

Discussion and conclusion

In this case, it is essential to diagnostically differentiate from traumatic inflammation, discoid lupus erythematosus (DLE), bullous diseases, and oral lichen planus (OLP). The stimulation of trauma and long-term chronic inflammation may also cause lichenoid reactions. However, in our patient’s situation, there were no foreign objects such as prosthetics, stump roots, sharp teeth and other factors that could easily cause oral trauma; additionally, the patient denied any history of bite injuries. Histopathological examination revealed no significant liquefactive degeneration of epithelial basal cells, thereby excluding DLE. Although subepithelial blister formation was noted, direct immunofluorescence staining did not demonstrate IgG deposition in the epithelial basement membrane, ruling out bullous disease. Furthermore, a dense infiltration of lymphocytes was observed in the lamina propria of the oral mucosa; however, no typical lymphocytic infiltration zones had formed. This indicates that diagnosing OLP requires careful consideration of the patient’s medical history. Based on the patient’s medical history and disease progression (Fig. 3), we found that the occurrence and development of oral mucosal lichenoid lesions were closely associated with Cadonilimab use; notably, these oral lesions improved gradually after discontinuation of Cadonilimab treatment. Therefore, we conclude that the diagnosis for this patient is Cadonilimab-related Oral Lichenoid Lesions.

Cadonilimab represents the world’s first PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific inhibitor, and this report details the inaugural oral adverse event associated with Cadonilimab in a patient primarily presenting with oral lichenoid lesions. To our knowledge, oral lichenoid lesions (OLL) constitute a category of diseases that exhibit clinical manifestations and histopathological characteristics akin to those of oral lichen planus (OLP), which is classified as an oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD). PD-1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors inhibit tumors by modulating host’s T cell activation and immune surveillance. Adverse cutaneous reactions associated with the combination of anti-CTLA-4 and anti-PD-1 therapies (such as ipilimumab and nivolumab) were observed to be more frequent, more severe, longer-lasting, and to develop earlier compared to those resulting from PD-1 or CTLA-4 inhibitors administered as monotherapy. A recent study indicated that 55.34% of patients receiving the PD-1 inhibitor pembrolizumab experienced cutaneous adverse reactions, while 11.2% developed oral mucosal lesions and 9.3% reported xerostomia. Cutaneous and oral adverse reactions manifested approximately 11 weeks post-treatment. The concurrent administration of PD-1 inhibitors and CTLA-4 inhibitors elevates the risk of cutaneous adverse events, whereas combination chemotherapy heightens the likelihood of oral adverse reactions. In the context of the oral cavity, patients treated with PD-1/Programmed cell death protein ligands-1 (PD-L1) primarily exhibited oral symptoms such as xerostomia, oral lichenoid lesions, and to a lesser extent, speech disturbances; conversely, anti-CTLA-4 treatment was significantly less associated with oral manifestations. Previous studies indicated that patients experienced both extraoral and intraoral adverse effects after a median of two infusions of PD-1 inhibitors (ranging from 1 to 16), compared to a median of four infusions (ranging from 1 to 16) for those who developed solely oral adverse effects. The median onset time for these oral adverse effects following the initiation of PD-1 inhibitor therapy was reported as 95 days (with a range of 7 to 284 days). In our case report, the patient presented with severe mucosal adverse reactions in the oral cavity while exhibiting only minor cutaneous lesions. Oral lesions manifested approximately twelve weeks post-treatment initiation (after the fourth infusion), with symptoms progressively worsening over time. The characteristics of oral adverse reactions associated with PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific inhibitors were found to be analogous to those previously documented for PD-1 inhibitors.

The histopathological features of OLL are regarded as analogous to those of OLP. In our case, we observed epithelial hyperkeratosis, subepithelial blister formation, lymphocytic infiltration in the lamina propria, and scattered plasma cell infiltration within the oral mucosa. Similar to OLP, there was a predominance of CD4 + and CD8 + T cell infiltration, with a notably higher density of CD4 + T cells. Additionally, scattered infiltrates of CD20 + B lymphocytes and CD68 + macrophages were noted. Notably, α-SMA-positive myoblasts were dispersed throughout the basal layer, lamina propria, and submucosa. Several studies have highlighted distinctions between OLL and OLP; for instance, Małgorzata et al. reported that P53 expression in OLL is marginally elevated compared to that in OLP; however, this difference did not reach statistical significance. Tulio et al. demonstrated that the density of CD68 + and CD163 + cells in OLP was significantly higher than that observed in OLL. Lodolo et al. reported an increase in eosinophilic and plasma cell infiltration in OLL compared to OLP, indicating that the mixed inflammatory infiltrate of both types can serve as a reliable histological marker for diagnosing OLLs. While these studies have highlighted differences between OLL and OLP, there remains a notable lack of comprehensive summaries and further validation through large-scale experiments.

In individuals with potential drug-induced OLL, it is advisable to discontinue the associated medication whenever feasible. This can often be challenging, as the medication in question is typically crucial for the patient’s health, and effective alternative treatments may not be readily available. The primary objective of any OLL therapy is symptomatic management. Topical agents are generally considered first-line treatment due to their minimal adverse effects; however, systemic therapies may become necessary if lesions are extensive and affect the skin or other mucosal surfaces, or in cases of refractory disease. A study indicated that patients receiving prednisone at a dosage of ≥ 10 mg equivalent at the initiation of PD-1 inhibitor therapy experienced significant reductions in response rates, progression-free survival, and disease-free intervals. Nonetheless, these findings require further validation. Additionally, the use of local treatment or drugs with fewer side effects to treat OLL is worth advocating and further researching, such as local steroids and vitamin A therapy.

In conclusion, oral adverse reactions can manifest in patients undergoing treatment with PD-1/CTLA-4 inhibitors and other immune checkpoint blockers, which may often go unnoticed. Therefore, it’s essential to conduct thorough oral examinations before, during, and after treatment. Both stomatologists and oncologists should remain vigilant regarding this issue and foster close collaboration to alleviate patients’ discomfort while enhancing their quality of life.

Histopathological examination revealed no significant liquefactive degeneration of epithelial basal cells, thereby excluding DLE. Although subepithelial blister formation was noted, direct immunofluorescence staining did not demonstrate IgG deposition in the epithelial basement membrane, ruling out bullous disease. Furthermore, a dense infiltration of lymphocytes was observed in the lamina propria of the oral mucosa; however, no typical lymphocytic infiltration zones had formed. This indicates that diagnosing OLP requires careful consideration of the patient’s medical history. Based on the patient’s medical history and disease progression (Fig. 3), we found that the occurrence and development of oral mucosal lichenoid lesions were closely associated with Cadonilimab use; notably, these oral lesions improved gradually after discontinuation of Cadonilimab treatment. Therefore, we conclude that the diagnosis for this patient is Cadonilimab-related Oral Lichenoid Lesions.

Cadonilimab represents the world’s first PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific inhibitor, and this report details the inaugural oral adverse event associated with Cadonilimab in a patient primarily presenting with oral lichenoid lesions. To our knowledge, oral lichenoid lesions (OLL) constitute a category of diseases that exhibit clinical manifestations and histopathological characteristics akin to those of oral lichen planus (OLP), which is classified as an oral potentially malignant disorder (OPMD). PD-1 and CTLA-4 inhibitors inhibit tumors by modulating host’s T cell activation and immune surveillance. Adverse cutaneous reactions associated with the combination of anti-CTLA-4 and anti-PD-1 therapies (such as ipilimumab and nivolumab) were observed to be more frequent, more severe, longer-lasting, and to develop earlier compared to those resulting from PD-1 or CTLA-4 inhibitors administered as monotherapy. A recent study indicated that 55.34% of patients receiving the PD-1 inhibitor pembrolizumab experienced cutaneous adverse reactions, while 11.2% developed oral mucosal lesions and 9.3% reported xerostomia. Cutaneous and oral adverse reactions manifested approximately 11 weeks post-treatment. The concurrent administration of PD-1 inhibitors and CTLA-4 inhibitors elevates the risk of cutaneous adverse events, whereas combination chemotherapy heightens the likelihood of oral adverse reactions. In the context of the oral cavity, patients treated with PD-1/Programmed cell death protein ligands-1 (PD-L1) primarily exhibited oral symptoms such as xerostomia, oral lichenoid lesions, and to a lesser extent, speech disturbances; conversely, anti-CTLA-4 treatment was significantly less associated with oral manifestations. Previous studies indicated that patients experienced both extraoral and intraoral adverse effects after a median of two infusions of PD-1 inhibitors (ranging from 1 to 16), compared to a median of four infusions (ranging from 1 to 16) for those who developed solely oral adverse effects. The median onset time for these oral adverse effects following the initiation of PD-1 inhibitor therapy was reported as 95 days (with a range of 7 to 284 days). In our case report, the patient presented with severe mucosal adverse reactions in the oral cavity while exhibiting only minor cutaneous lesions. Oral lesions manifested approximately twelve weeks post-treatment initiation (after the fourth infusion), with symptoms progressively worsening over time. The characteristics of oral adverse reactions associated with PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific inhibitors were found to be analogous to those previously documented for PD-1 inhibitors.

The histopathological features of OLL are regarded as analogous to those of OLP. In our case, we observed epithelial hyperkeratosis, subepithelial blister formation, lymphocytic infiltration in the lamina propria, and scattered plasma cell infiltration within the oral mucosa. Similar to OLP, there was a predominance of CD4 + and CD8 + T cell infiltration, with a notably higher density of CD4 + T cells. Additionally, scattered infiltrates of CD20 + B lymphocytes and CD68 + macrophages were noted. Notably, α-SMA-positive myoblasts were dispersed throughout the basal layer, lamina propria, and submucosa. Several studies have highlighted distinctions between OLL and OLP; for instance, Małgorzata et al. reported that P53 expression in OLL is marginally elevated compared to that in OLP; however, this difference did not reach statistical significance. Tulio et al. demonstrated that the density of CD68 + and CD163 + cells in OLP was significantly higher than that observed in OLL. Lodolo et al. reported an increase in eosinophilic and plasma cell infiltration in OLL compared to OLP, indicating that the mixed inflammatory infiltrate of both types can serve as a reliable histological marker for diagnosing OLLs. While these studies have highlighted differences between OLL and OLP, there remains a notable lack of comprehensive summaries and further validation through large-scale experiments.

In individuals with potential drug-induced OLL, it is advisable to discontinue the associated medication whenever feasible. This can often be challenging, as the medication in question is typically crucial for the patient’s health, and effective alternative treatments may not be readily available. The primary objective of any OLL therapy is symptomatic management. Topical agents are generally considered first-line treatment due to their minimal adverse effects; however, systemic therapies may become necessary if lesions are extensive and affect the skin or other mucosal surfaces, or in cases of refractory disease. A study indicated that patients receiving prednisone at a dosage of ≥ 10 mg equivalent at the initiation of PD-1 inhibitor therapy experienced significant reductions in response rates, progression-free survival, and disease-free intervals. Nonetheless, these findings require further validation. Additionally, the use of local treatment or drugs with fewer side effects to treat OLL is worth advocating and further researching, such as local steroids and vitamin A therapy.

In conclusion, oral adverse reactions can manifest in patients undergoing treatment with PD-1/CTLA-4 inhibitors and other immune checkpoint blockers, which may often go unnoticed. Therefore, it’s essential to conduct thorough oral examinations before, during, and after treatment. Both stomatologists and oncologists should remain vigilant regarding this issue and foster close collaboration to alleviate patients’ discomfort while enhancing their quality of life.
