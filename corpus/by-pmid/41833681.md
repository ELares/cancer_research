# A multimodal neoadjuvant strategy incorporating PDT, RFA, and ICIs results in a notable pathological response and preservation of the sphincter in microsatellite stable low rectal cancer: A highly distinctive case report and literature review.

## Abstract

Microsatellite stable (MSS) rectal cancer generally shows limited response to immunotherapy because of its low tumor mutational burden and immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Meanwhile, locally advanced low rectal cancer presents a major clinical challenge, as radical surgery often compromises anal sphincter function and postoperative quality of life. Emerging evidence suggests that photodynamic therapy (PDT) and radiofrequency ablation (RFA) can induce immunogenic cell death, promoting the release of tumor-associated antigens and damage-associated molecular patterns, thereby enhancing antitumor immune responses. Here, we report a case of locally advanced MSS low rectal cancer treated with a multimodal neoadjuvant regimen integrating sequential PDT and RFA with systemic therapy including immune checkpoint inhibitors, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy. This strategy achieved a near-complete pathological response (Tumor Regression Grade 1) and enabled successful sphincter-preserving radical surgery despite the tumor being located <5 cm from the anal verge. The treatment was well tolerated, with no severe treatment-related adverse events observed. This case suggests that combining local tumor ablation with systemic immunomodulatory therapy may represent a promising strategy to enhance immunotherapy responsiveness and facilitate organ-preserving treatment in selected patients with MSS rectal cancer.

1. Introduction
Colorectal cancer (CRC) poses a major global public health chal­
lenge, ranking as the third most common malignancy in terms of inci­
dence and the second leading cause of cancer-related mortalities [1]. 
According to 2020 cancer statistics in China, CRC was the second most 
frequently diagnosed cancer and the fourth leading cause of cancer 
deaths, with approximately 555,000 new cases reported annually [2]. 
Despite significant diagnostic and therapeutic advances, the global 
burden of CRC continues to rise, underscoring the urgent need for more 
effective treatment strategies [3]. Rectal cancer constitutes a major 
subtype of CRC. Carcinomas located within 5 cm of the anal verge are 
generally classified as low rectal cancer. Due to the complex anatomical 
relationships and limited surgical field in this region, resection of low 
rectal tumors is technically challenging. Moreover, the proximity of 
these tumors to the anal sphincter often makes it difficult to preserve 
anal function postoperatively, significantly impacting patients' quality 
of life [4].
The selection of neoadjuvant therapeutic strategies is particularly 
critical in the management of low rectal cancer. Currently, neoadjuvant 
chemoradiotherapy (CRT) remains the standard treatment for locally 
advanced rectal cancer; however, its efficacy is notably limited in mi­
crosatellite stable (MSS) tumors [5]. Although immune checkpoint in­
hibitors (ICIs) have yielded impressive results in neoadjuvant settings, 
their efficacy is heavily dependent on microsatellite status. Substantial 
evidence confirms that the immune response rate to ICIs in MSS-type 
colorectal cancer is significantly lower than that observed in microsat­
ellite instability-high (MSI-H) tumors [6–8]. In the neoadjuvant

underscore the substantial clinical challenges confronting MSS CRC

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) utilizes a photosensitizer and light of a 
specific wavelength to initiate a photochemical reaction, generating 
reactive oxygen species that kill tumor cells. Additionally, PDT can 
induce immunogenic cell death (ICD), leading to the release of tumor- 
associated antigens and damage-associated molecular patterns 
(DAMPs). This process promotes the maturation of dendritic cells and 
the infiltration of cytotoxic T cells, thereby activating anti-tumor im­
munity [11–13]. Owing to these immune-stimulatory mechanisms, PDT 
has also been clinically demonstrated to alleviate obstructive symptoms 
in colorectal cancer, with one study reporting an objective response rate 
of 44.4 % [14]. Radiofrequency ablation (RFA), on the other hand, 
employs thermal energy to induce coagulative necrosis of tumor cells, 
which also results in the release of tumor antigens and enhances 
T-cell-mediated immune responses [15–18]. Currently, RFA is primarily 
used for treating colorectal liver metastases and, in a limited number of 
cases, for unresectable local recurrences [19–20]. However, evidence 
regarding the application of RFA as a curative or primary treatment 
modality for primary rectal tumors remains scarce, and it has not been 
established as a standard therapeutic option. In summary, PDT and RFA 
theoretically possess the potential to synergistically activate anti-tumor 
immunity, particularly for tumors with low immunogenicity. Never­
theless, clinical translational studies investigating the combination of 
both modalities for rectal cancer, especially for primary lesions, are still 
severely lacking, representing an important direction for future 
exploration.
This study presents a rare case of locally advanced, MSS-type low 
rectal cancer with the distal tumor margin located <5 cm from the anal 
verge. Through an innovative combined regimen of PDT, RFA, and 
immunotherapy, a Tumor Regression Grade (TRG) 1 was achieved, 
along with successful preservation of the anal sphincter function. This 
multimodal therapeutic strategy concurrently addresses the dual chal­
lenges of local tumor control and systemic immune activation, offering a 
transformative new paradigm for the treatment of MSS-type colorectal 
cancer.
2. Case report
2.1. Case description
A 64-year-old female patient with a documented history of diabetes 
mellitus was admitted to the hospital following a one-month history of 
hematochezia. Colonoscopy revealed an annular protruding mass <5 cm 
from the anal verge, with a congested and bleeding surface. Addition­
ally, multiple polypoid nodular lesions were observed at 30–50 cm and 
70–90 cm from the anal verge (Fig. 1(a)). Histopathological examination 
of the biopsy specimens confirmed a diagnosis of moderately differen­
tiated adenocarcinoma (Fig. 2(a)). Immunohistochemical staining 
confirmed results: CK8/18+, Syn-, Her-2(0), P53(+), Ki67 (80 %+); 
MSH-2+, MSH-6+, MLH1+, PMS-2+, confirming the tumor as MSS 
(Fig. 2). Pelvic magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) revealed irregular 
thickening of the rectal wall up to 42 mm, with tumor invasion 
extending to the muscularis propria (cT3a). The proximal margin of the 
lesion was located 48 mm from the anal verge; mesorectal fascia was 
intact (MRF-), and no extramural vascular invasion (EMVI-) was iden­
tified. Several rounded mesorectal lymph nodes were observed, the 
largest with a short-axis diameter of 5.8 mm (cN1) (Fig. 3(a)). Computed 
tomography (CT) of the chest and abdomen showed no evidence of 
distant metastasis (cM0). According to the AJCC 8th edition staging 
system, the final clinical stage was cT3aN1M0. This patient's perfor­
mance status was assessed using the Eastern Cooperative Oncology 
Group (ECOG) score and the Karnofsky Performance Status (KPS) scale, 
with scores of 1 and >80, respectively.

2.2.1. PDT
Prior to PDT, the patient first underwent a skin test with the 
photosensitizer hematoporphyrin injection (Hiporfin; Milone Biophar­
maceutical Co., Ltd, Chongqing, China), with all procedures performed 
under light-restricted conditions. The test solution was prepared by 
diluting 0.1 mL of stock solution in 50 mL of normal saline (0.01 mg/ 
mL), stored at room temperature protected from light. An intradermal 
injection of 0.1 mL of this test solution was administered, and the re­
action was observed after 20 min. Results revealed no erythema or 
wheal at the injection site, and she reported no systemic discomfort, 
confirming a negative skin test result. Subsequently, the patient received 
an intravenous infusion of hematoporphyrin at a dose of 3 mg/kg while 
receiving continuous oxygen and being monitored for heart rate and 
oxygen saturation. The patient was closely monitored during the infu­
sion for potential adverse reactions, including palpitations, dyspnea, 
chest tightness, or pruritus. Following the infusion, strict photo­
protection was maintained for one month, including avoidance of direct 
sunlight, use of full-body protective clothing during outdoor activities, 
and avoidance of potential photosensitizing foods. Thirty-six hours after 
photosensitizer administration, a cylindrical optical diffuser fiber 
(emitting length 5 cm, diameter 0.15 cm) was inserted into the tumor 
lesion under colonoscopic guidance. PDT was then performed using a 
630-nm semiconductor laser system (PDT 630-II; Guilin Xingda Medical 
Instrument Co., Ltd., Guilin, China). The effective irradiation area was 
approximately 2.36 cm².

The patient received the first PDT session on November 25, 2024. 
Under endoscopic guidance, the optical fiber was positioned at the rectal 
lesion. A segmented irradiation strategy was adopted to ensure homo­
geneous light distribution across irregular tumor surfaces while pre­
venting excessive local energy deposition. During Session 1, the laser 
was delivered at a power density of 339.5 mW/cm² for 10 min, 
achieving an energy density of 203.7 J/cm² (total energy 480 J). In 
Session 2, the power density was increased to 424.4 mW/cm² for 15 min, 
resulting in an energy density of 382.0 J/cm² (900 J). Session 3 
employed a segmented approach at 424.4 mW/cm², delivering 178.3 J/ 
cm² to the left lesion (7 min) and 203.7 J/cm² to the right (8 min). 
Session 4 utilized a three-segment protocol at 424.4 mW/cm²: left side 
(178.3 J/cm², 7 min), right side (203.7 J/cm², 8 min), and interstitial 
irradiation (76.4 J/cm², 3 min) via direct fiber insertion. Following the 
four PDT sessions, endoscopic examination revealed characteristic 
mucosal changes, including congestion, edema, and purple discoloration 
(Fig. 1(b-e)). The patient demonstrated excellent tolerance, and strict 
photoprotective measures were maintained for four weeks.
2.2.2. RFA
On the fourth day following the final PDT session, the patient un­
derwent RFA to achieve further tumor reduction and enhance surgical 
resectability. The procedure was performed with the patient lying in the 
left lateral position under continuous oxygen supplementation and 
monitoring of vital signs. Colonoscopy confirmed a protruding lesion at 
5 cm from the anal verge (Fig. 1(f)). Under endoscopic guidance, a bi­
polar Habib™ EndoHPB catheter (Boston Scientific) was deployed, 
delivering 10 W for 90 s. Post-ablation examination revealed coagu­
lative necrosis on the tumor surface, without evidence of immediate 
complications such as deep-seated thermal injury or perforation, after 
which the endoscope was uneventfully withdrawn (Fig. 1(g)).
2.2.3. Systemic therapy
Upon completion of RFA, the patient initiated a systemic therapy 
regimen consisting of the CAPOX protocol, which includes capecitabine 
at a dosage of 1000 mg/m² administered on days 1–14 and oxaliplatin at 
130 mg/m² on day 1, with the cycle repeated every 21 days. This 
regimen was combined with tislelizumab at 200 mg every 21 days and 
bevacizumab at 7.5 mg/kg every 21 days. The systemic treatment 
commenced immediately after the RFA. The patient successfully 
completed four cycles of this regimen, each spanning 21 days, and 
subsequently underwent sphincter-preserving surgery one month after 
the conclusion of systemic therapy. One month postoperatively, an 
additional four cycles of the same regimen were administered as main­
tenance therapy. During follow-up, the patient maintained good clinical 
condition, with preserved mental status and appetite, and did not 
experience any adverse effects or reactions, such as nausea or vomiting.
2.2.4. Prophylactic stent placement following robot-assisted radical 
resection
Following completion of PDT, RFA, and four cycles of systemic 
therapy, preoperative investigations were repeated. The post- 
neoadjuvant clinical stage was assessed as ycT2N1M0 with MRF (-) 
and EMVI (-). Colonoscopy demonstrated a patent lumen without 
obstruction and showed post-treatment changes with scar formation at 
the rectal lesion site, located >5 cm from the anal verge. After excluding 
surgical contraindications, the patient underwent robot-assisted low 
anterior resection with sphincter preservation on March 30, 2025. 
Intraoperative blood loss was 50 mL, and postoperative drain output was 
200 mL on the first day. Follow-up imaging revealed a small amount of 
pelvic fluid collection. No other complications and abnormalities were 
detected. On the second postoperative day, considering the patient's 
history of diabetes and to proactively mitigate risks of anastomotic 
leakage and delayed recovery, a covered colorectal stent was placed 
endoscopically across the rectal anastomosis (Fig. 1(h)). The patient 
concurrently received supportive care including nutritional support, 
antibiotic prophylaxis, and fasting blood sugar glycemic control.
2.3. Treatment response evaluation and follow-up
Treatment response was evaluated using a multimodal assessment 
approach including routine laboratory assessments, pelvic MRI, colo­
noscopy and final pathological examination of the surgical specimen. To 
minimize the risk of rectal fistula formation, repeat biopsy was inten­
tionally avoided during the neoadjuvant treatment period.
Following four cycles of PDT, analysis of lymphocyte subsets indi­
cated mild enhancement of immune activity (Fig. 4). The final preop­
erative pelvic MRI demonstrated a significant reduction in tumor 
volume compared with baseline imaging, with rectal wall thickness 
decreasing from 11.14 mm to 2.11 mm (Fig. 3). Pathological examina­
tion of the resected specimen after the complete multimodal neo­
adjuvant therapy (PDT, RFA, and 4 cycles of systemic therapy) revealed: 
i) Microscopic findings of ulcerated, moderately differentiated rectal 
adenocarcinoma with negative proximal, distal, and circumferential 
resection margins. Metastatic carcinoma was present in 3 of 10 mesen­
teric lymph nodes. Tumor regression graded as TRG 1 (near-complete 
response, with only single cells or small cell clusters remaining) (Fig. 2
(b)), yielding a final pathological stage of ypT1N1Mx. ii) Immunohis­
tochemical staining results: CK8/18+, Syn-, Her-2 (0), P53(+), Ki-67 
(60 %+); MSH2+, MSH6+, MLH1+, PMS2+. Compared to the pre- 
treatment biopsy, the Ki-67 proliferation index was markedly 
decreased, indicating substantial suppression of tumor proliferative 
activity.
The combined treatment regimen was well-tolerated. No treatment- 
related adverse events of Grade ≥2 (CTCAE v5.0) were observed, and no 
specific complications related to PDT or RFA occurred. The patient 
experienced only mild transient discomfort at the treatment site after 
PDT and RFA, which resolved spontaneously without the need for 
analgesic medication. No treatment-related fever, bleeding, perforation, 
or fistula formation was observed. Laboratory monitoring demonstrated 
stable hematological parameters and hepatic/renal function throughout 
the treatment course (Fig. 4). A follow-up colonoscopy on postoperative 
day 9 showed a well-healed anastomosis without stricture or leakage. 
The previously placed stent was successfully removed endoscopically 
without complications such as bleeding or perforation (Fig. 1(i)). A 
subsequent colonoscopy two months postoperatively confirmed a 
satisfactory postoperative status with a patent and well-healed anasto­
mosis (Fig. 1(j)). At the most recent follow-up, approximately 12 months 
after surgery, the patient remains free of disease recurrence. The patient 
reported significant improvement in quality of life, with normal bowel 
function and no discomfort, indicating satisfactory functional recovery 
after sphincter-preserving treatment. The overall treatment timeline is

3. Discussion
In the context of limited immunotherapy efficacy for MSS-type rectal 
cancer, achieving both effective tumor control and sphincter preserva­
tion represents the central therapeutic challenge in low rectal cancer 
management. Currently, neoadjuvant CRT or total neoadjuvant therapy 
represents the standard treatment strategy; however, their therapeutic 
benefit in MSS tumors remains suboptimal, particularly with regard to 
enhancing responsiveness to ICIs [21,22]. These conventional ap­
proaches primarily rely on cytotoxic tumor reduction and passive tumor 
shrinkage, with limited ability to actively remodel tumor immunoge­
nicity [23]. In contrast, the multimodal strategy described in this case 
integrates PDT, RFA, and systemic immunotherapy as a coordinated 
neoadjuvant approach. By combining localized tumor ablation with 
systemic immune modulation, this strategy achieved substantial tumor 
regression and enabled successful sphincter-preserving surgery in a pa­
tient who might otherwise have required more radical resection. These 
findings suggest that multimodal immunomodulatory strategies may

An optimal cancer therapy should not only directly eliminate tumor 
cells but also activate the host immune system to induce a durable anti- 
tumor response. A central mechanism in this process is ICD. Its "anti­
genicity" enables antigens released from dying cells to be captured by 
antigen-presenting cells (APCs), leading to T-cell activation. Concur­
rently, its "adjuvanticity," mediated by damage-associated molecular 
patterns such as ATP, calreticulin, and HMGB1, promotes the recogni­
tion and phagocytosis of dead cells by APCs, thereby initiating adaptive 
immunity [24]. Both PDT and RFA can induce ICD, yet they exhibit 
distinct and complementary mechanisms of action and clinical appli­
cations (Table 1) [12,25]. Therefore, combining these two ICD-inducing 
modalities may provide both enhanced immune activation and 
improved local tumor control in low rectal cancer.
In the present case, the rationale for combining PDT and RFA was 
based on both complementary therapeutic mechanisms and safety con­
siderations. The integration of PDT and RFA presented three principal 
advantages: 1) Tumor Control: PDT facilitated the precise ablation of 
superficial tumor layers under the guidance of a photosensitizer, effec­
tively diminishing the local tumor burden and establishing optimal 
conditions for subsequent RFA. Utilizing its capability for deep-tissue 
penetration, RFA subsequently achieved comprehensive eradication of 
deeper tumor regions that were inaccessible to PDT alone. This 
sequential "surface-to-core" strategy resulted in thorough tumor clear­
ance, thereby laying a robust foundation for reducing the extent of 
surgical intervention and achieving radical preservation of the 
sphincter. 2) Tissue Preservation: The precise cytotoxic action of PDT 
maximized the preservation of critical functional structures, particularly 
the anal sphincter, while simultaneously reducing tumor volume and 
delineating lesion boundaries. This spatial optimization facilitated more 
targeted RFA energy delivery, thereby minimizing the risks associated 
with thermal diffusion and perforation [26]. 3) Immunological Synergy: 
PDT rapidly initiated an immune response through the release of 
damage-associated molecular patterns, including calreticulin, HMGB1, 
and ATP. RFA subsequently amplified this immune activation through 
upregulation of HSP70/HSP90 and substantial tumor antigen release.

overcoming immunotherapy resistance in MSS tumors.
Despite the potential benefits of combining PDT and RFA, treatment- 
related morbidity remains a concern. PDT for colorectal tumors may 
cause complications such as fistula formation and bowel perforation, 
while gastrointestinal perforation has also been reported after RFA due 
to thermal injury to adjacent organs [3,30]. The thinness of the rectal 
wall and its anatomical proximity to adjacent organs such as the vagina, 
prostate, and bladder increase the risk of transmural injury during local 
ablation procedures. To minimize these risks, a multi-stage safety 
strategy was adopted. During the neoadjuvant phase, a sequential 
PDT-first approach was utilized to achieve controlled superficial 
destruction, followed by endoscopic RFA with adequate intervals to 
allow mucosal recovery. This averted excessive thermal load on the

with diabetes. These measures effectively minimized the risk of rectal 
fistula and anastomotic leakage. Importantly, no fistula, perforation, or 
severe local complications occurred during the treatment or follow-up 
period, supporting the safety and feasibility of this integrated approach.
Another distinguishing aspect of this case is the systematic integra­
tion of multimodal therapy. Conventional chemotherapeutic agents, 
including oxaliplatin and cisplatin, exert direct antitumor effects by 
damaging DNA or disrupting mitosis, while concurrently inducing ICD 
to activate the immune system [31,32]. Molecular targeted drugs such 
as bevacizumab inhibit vascular endothelial proliferation, promote 
vascular normalization, reduce tumor perfusion, and enhance immune 
cell infiltration [33]. In regard to this patient, we innovatively combined 
local physical ablation, ICIs, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy into a 
coordinated neoadjuvant regimen. This approach was not a mere com­
bination of single treatments, but rather a strategically designed 
sequence based on synergistic mechanisms: PDT and RFA served as the 
"vanguard" to induce robust ICD, releasing tumor antigens and priming 
local immunity; ICIs then acted as the "main force," exploiting the 
activated immune milieu to reverse T-cell suppression and mount a 
systemic antitumor response; simultaneously, chemotherapy and bev­
acizumab not only directly killed tumor cells and suppressed aberrant 
angiogenesis, but also remodeled the immune microenvironment by 
depleting immunosuppressive cells (Fig. 6). This carefully orchestrated 
strategy successfully addressed immunotherapeutic resistance while 
establishing optimal conditions for extreme sphincter preservation.
In summary, the multimodal neoadjuvant strategy integrating PDT 
with RFA and immunotherapy, as implemented in this study, was 
designed to simultaneously address the dual challenges of tumor erad­
ication and functional sphincter preservation. This regimen successfully 
achieved a TRG 1 in a patient with MSS-type cancer, which typically 
demonstrates resistance to immunotherapy. This breakthrough not only 
secured an opportunity for radical sphincter-preserving surgery in this 
specific case but also provides clinical validation for the theoretical 
premise that sensitizing MSS tumors to immunotherapy through ICD 
inducers is feasible. Furthermore, advancements in robot-assisted sur­
gical systems offered critical technical support for sphincter preserva­
tion in this low rectal cancer case. The enhanced maneuverability and 
superior visualization facilitate more precise intersphincteric dissection 
and nerve preservation, thereby improving both the success rate of 
sphincter preservation and the quality of functional outcomes while 
maintaining oncological radicality [34]. Looking forward, the integra­
tion of such novel local immunomodulatory strategies with minimally 
invasive surgical techniques holds promise for delivering more person­
alized treatment options that equally prioritize curative outcomes and 
functional preservation for patients with MSS-type low rectal cancer.
4. Conclusion
This case illustrates the clinical potential of translating fundamental 
research—specifically, the concept that ICD inducers like PDT or RFA 
may modulate the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment—into 
a personalized therapeutic strategy. In this instance, a patient with MSS 
colorectal cancer, a condition typically unresponsive to immunotherapy, 
exhibited a favorable response to this multimodal approach. While these 
findings suggest a possible direction for developing novel immune- 
combination therapies for MSS-type colorectal cancer, particularly in 
refractory low rectal cancer, they should be interpreted with caution. As 
a single-case report, the broader applicability of this strategy remains to 
be established and necessitates validation in larger, well-designed clin­
ical cohorts. Furthermore, the specific contribution of each therapeutic 
modality, as well as the optimal treatment sequence and duration, 
warrant further investigation through prospective studies.
