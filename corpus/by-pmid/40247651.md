Curative or non-curative: immunotherapy for advanced melanoma

Abstract

Advanced melanoma was historically considered incurable; however, a 52% 10-year melanoma-specific survival rate from seminal immunotherapy trials challenges that conclusion.1 There is no literature exploring clinicians’ discussion of treatment intent with patients, or whether this represents cure. We performed a multicenter retrospective cohort analysis to examine treatment intent, using electronic medical records to identify 278 patients with unresectable or stage IV melanoma consented for immunotherapy from 2019 to 2023. Patients consented for CIT received significantly higher rates of combination immunotherapy than patients consented for non-curative-intent treatment (NCIT), 76% (16/21) vs 47% (116/246), P = .022. The definition of cure within the setting of advanced malignancy is not widely agreed upon and can mean different things to patients, physicians, politicians, and epidemiologists. The Australian Prime Minister himself, Anthony Albanese, stated that “advanced melanoma is now a curable disease” in congratulating the 2024 Australians of the Year for their contribution to melanoma research. The Siracusa Charter defined cure in advanced malignancy as “complete clinical remission of cancer…such that the patient’s risk of death does not, because of cancer, exceed that of a sex- and age-matched general population.” By contrast, the term psychological cure speaks more to the patient perspective, encompassing elements including quality of life, residual toxicities, anxiety regarding recurrence, and self-identification as a cancer patient.

In the absence of consensus, oncologists are reluctant to use the term cure, both in the published literature and the clinical setting. Despite this reluctance, use of the term curative-intent is common where long-term survival is possible in advanced, unresectable malignancy. Although it is challenging to determine the precise moment at which a patient is “cured” in real time, per the Siracusa Charter definition, in retrospect we can recognize this is achieved for a portion of the population. Curative-intent treatment (CIT) is therefore simpler to define than cure itself. Examples of unresectable malignancies routinely treated with curative intent include the use of chemoradiation for limited stage small cell lung cancer (LS-SCLC), stage III non-small cell lung cancer, or unresectable oesophageal cancer, all of which have significantly worse 5-year survival outcomes than that described above for advanced melanoma.

Overall quality of life among cancer survivors has been shown to be influenced predominantly by the domains of control, uncertainty, and the future. A recent systematic review also identified fear of cancer recurrence as the most significant unmet need of cancer survivors. Consenting patients for CIT may somewhat address this unmet need, provide hope, and have meaningful effects on patients’ quality of life.

The arrival of immunotherapy in the treatment of advanced melanoma has been revolutionary. Previously considered an incurable condition, the recently updated 10-year data from the seminal trial of ipilimumab and nivolumab combination immunotherapy in advanced melanoma demonstrates a 52% melanoma-specific survival, with only 4 of 314 patients in this group experiencing a relapse beyond 5 years. Results are similar in real-world settings, with 50% overall survival after 4 years in a population with similar disease characteristics. To consider this a curable condition would represent a paradigm shift, and a first in the post-immunotherapy era. Notably, the ESMO Magnitude of Clinical Benefit Scale version 1.1 updated the classification of immunotherapy for advanced melanoma as having the “potential for cure” defined by “a sustained plateau in the long-term survival data.”

This timepoint and disease context therefore provide a unique opportunity to examine oncologists’ interpretations of the term cure in this context. To capture clinician’s interpretation of immunotherapy treatment-intent, as well as their communication of treatment intent to patients, we sought to analyze written treatment consent forms that explicitly record treatment intent, curative vs non-curative.

All patients with unresectable or stage IV melanoma who were consented for treatment with immunotherapy between October 2019 and December 2023 were identified from three hospitals: The Alfred Hospital in Victoria and Royal Adelaide Hospital and The Queen Elizabeth Hospital (TQEH) in South Australia (SA). These sites were selected for their use of written consent processes that explicitly record the treatment intent, curative vs non-curative. Diagnostic codes on the electronic medical record (EMR) were aligned with the pharmacy dispensary for relevant medications (pembrolizumab, ipilimumab, and nivolumab) to identify patients. The electronic medical record was then used to derive patient characteristics, disease characteristics, and review consent documentation. Patients were excluded if they had early-stage resectable melanoma, if the consent form was absent or incomplete, or if treatment was never given after consent. For purposes of comparison, patients at the Alfred Hospital with LS-SCLC treated with chemoradiation were similarly identified across the same time period, and their consent documentation examined. Clinicopathological differences between patient groups were compared using t tests for continuous variables and χ2 analyses for categorical variables.

In total, 545 patients were identified, with 267 patients excluded from study. Most common reasons for exclusion included early-stage disease with adjuvant treatment (136 patients) or incomplete consent information (125 patients), which itself was mostly secondary to failure to specify treatment intent on the form 28% (n = 35) or enrollment in a clinical trial, which required alternative consent documentation 26% (n = 33).

A total of 278 patients were included for analysis. Patients were typically male (68%), were a median age of 68 years, had unresectable stage IV melanoma (88%), and were treated in the first-line setting (Table 1). A minority had a BRAF V600 mutation (35%) or CNS metastases (23%) at time of consent. In total, 32 (12%) were consented for CIT. All 11 patients with resected stage IV disease were consented for CIT, making up 34% of the total number consented for CIT. Of the 246 patients consented for non-curative intent treatment (NCIT), 4 included specific annotations on the consent form alluding to uncertainty, including “radical,” “unclear,” “?,” and “durable response.” When examining the whole cohort, rates of CIT were steady over time (Figure 1); however, in Victoria, the proportion consented for CIT rose from 8% in 2019 to 30% in 2023 (Figure 2).

Of the whole cohort, 86% of patients were treated in the first-line setting, 12% in the second-line setting, and 2% in the third-line setting. Only 1 patient was consented for CIT beyond the first-line setting.

A total of 67 doctors (17 consultants, 50 trainees) were responsible for the consent. There was no correlation seen between the consent for CIT and doctors’ frequency of overall immunotherapy prescribing, nor their clinical experience measured in years. All doctors responsible for consenting patients for CIT also consented patients for NCIT on at least 1 occasion.

Comparing the CIT and NCIT cohorts, there were no significant differences found when comparing patient characteristics, including age (median = 64 years vs 69 years) and gender (28% female vs 33% female). Similarly, there were no significant differences in disease characteristics between the CIT and NCIT cohorts, including rates of BRAF V600 mutation (44% vs 34%) and CNS metastases at time of consent (22% vs 24%).

Examining the cohort of 267 patients with unresectable disease only, the CIT cohort was treated with significantly higher rates of combination immunotherapy compared with the NCIT group, 76% (16/21) vs 47% (116/246), P = .022. Notably, there was no correlation between an individual doctor’s likelihood to prescribe combination immunotherapy and their likelihood to consent for CIT.

There were significant differences in practice between states. Among patients with unresectable disease only, rates of consent for CIT differed significantly between Victoria and SA: 14% (20/142) vs 0.8% (1/125), P < .001. Rates of resectable stage IV disease differed significantly, 10 patients in SA vs 1 patient in Victoria. Although all South Australian patients in this setting received single-agent immunotherapy, Victoria’s only patient with resected stage IV disease was managed with combination immunotherapy.

Across the same study period, 19 patients were identified at the Alfred and TQEH with LS-SCLC treated with chemoradiation. In total, 14 (74%) were consented for CIT.

This study highlights the uncertain interpretation of “curative intent” in the modern treatment of advanced cancer and suggests that institutional culture may influence clinician interpretation of treatment intent and subsequent consent with patients. Victoria’s trend toward consent for CIT was not reflected in SA. The Alfred Hospital’s specific focus on melanoma research, compared with SA sites, is of unknown impact in this regard. Treatment modality appeared to influence clinician’s interpretation of CIT, comparing immunotherapy with chemoradiation. That no doctor universally consented patients for CIT suggests that either (1) CIT is defined by a likelihood threshold of prolonged response or (2) that all doctors consenting for CIT changed their interpretation of CIT throughout the study period, secondary to updated evidence and/or clinical experience.

These three hospitals were the only ones found from a search of tertiary public hospitals in Victoria, New South Wales, and SA that document treatment intent as part of their written consent process. This places them in a clear minority and highlights a broader deficit in our communication of treatment intent to our patients. Our sample was less representative of the broader population as a result, which was considered a key limitation. Our second limitation, among the incomplete consent forms that failed to document treatment intent, it is unknown whether uncertainty about definitions of cure on behalf of clinicians contributed to this. Third, it is unknown whether the exclusion of patients treated on clinical trials affected patient characteristics or rates of consent for CIT. Last, clinicians’ interpretations of cure were unable to be qualitatively determined.

A minority of patients with advanced melanoma are consented for CIT. There is significant variability of individual and institutional practices in this regard. Further research is required to better understand the affects of consent for CIT on patient comprehension and quality of life.
