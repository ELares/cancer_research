# An oxygen-generating nanoplatform remodels the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment via synergistic lactate depletion and sonodynamic therapy.

## Abstract

Lactate is an immunosuppressive molecule that plays an important role in tumor progression. Regulating lactate metabolism to remodel the immunosuppressive microenvironment represents a promising strategy for cancer therapy. However, owing to the hypoxic nature of the tumor microenvironment and single intervention strategies, the effect of lactate oxidase for cancer therapy is not as expected. Therefore, we engineered a self-oxygen-generating nanoplatform by encapsulating lactate oxidase and manganese porphyrin within nanoliposomes (ML@Lip). Lactate depletion via lactate oxidase promoted the polarization of tumor-associated macrophages toward the M1 phenotype in the tumor microenvironment and modulated innate antitumor immunity. Manganese porphyrin-mediated sonodynamic therapy induced not only tumor cell apoptosis but also immunogenic cell death. The release of damage-associated molecular patterns promoted dendritic cell maturation and T-cell activation, leading to immune system activation and the initiation of adaptive immunity. Additionally, manganese catalyzed the decomposition of hydrogen peroxide (derived from lactate breakdown) to generate substantial oxygen. This process established a positive feedback loop via lactate depletion while amplifying sonodynamic therapeutic effects through enhanced oxygen production. Therefore, the strategy of combining lactate depletion-induced starvation therapy, sonodynamic therapy and self-circulating oxygen generation effectively remodeled the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment and inhibited tumor growth, thereby providing novel insights into targeted lactate metabolism therapy.

Background

Lactate has long been considered a metabolic waste of glycolysis. In-depth research on lactate has revealed that it influences tumor progression, angiogenesis, metastasis, and therapeutic resistance, while also playing a critical role in immunosuppression. For instance, lactate in the TME inhibits the differentiation of dendritic cells (DCs). Further research indicates that lactate produced by tumor cells activates GPR81 located on dendritic cells in a paracrine manner, preventing DCs from presenting tumor antigens to other immune cells. Studies demonstrate that lactate derived from tumor cells polarizes tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) into the M2 phenotype in a HIF-1α-dependent manner, thereby promoting tumor growth and metastasis. Tumor-derived lactate reduces the CD8+CD4+ T-cell population and inhibits CD8+CD4+ T-cell cytotoxicity by decreasing production of cytokines, perforin, and granzyme. Therefore, modulation of lactate in the TME has become a promising target for remodeling the immunosuppressive microenvironment in cancer therapy.

In fact, a variety of strategies for the modulation of lactate have been developed in cancer therapy, such as the inhibition of lactate dehydrogenase A (LDHA) and lactate transporters in cancer cells. LDHA is a key enzyme in lactate synthesis. Zhang et al. used cationic lipid-assisted nanoparticles for siRNA delivery, which efficiently downregulated LDHA expression and decreased lactate production. Moreover, this strategy increased the infiltration of CD8+ T and NK cells, decreased the number of immunosuppressive T cells, and thus significantly inhibited tumor growth. In the tumor microenvironment, monocarboxylate transporter 4 (MCT4) has been shown to mediate lactate export from hypoxic tumor cells. Li et al. constructed glutathione-responsive biodegradable hollow mesoporous silicon nanoparticles (HMONs) loaded with hydroxycamptothecin (HCPT) and siRNA to silence MCT4 to inhibit lactate efflux and effectively reduce the lactate concentration in the TME, thereby improving immunosuppression and achieving synergistic antitumor effects. However, the expression of glycolytic enzymes and MCTs in organisms is nonspecific. Although nanosystems can selectively increase the accumulation of therapeutics in tumors via passive and/or active targeting, the targeting efficiency is very limited. Moreover, the suppression of MCTs results in the unintended consequence of reduced lymphocyte proliferation. Therefore, direct depletion of lactate in the TME by lactate oxidase (LOX) is considered a promising strategy in cancer therapy.

Unfortunately, the therapeutic efficacy of LOX alone is often unsatisfactory. Because the process by which lactate oxidase catalyzes the conversion of lactate to pyruvate requires oxygen, the hypoxic TME restricts LOX activity. Furthermore, while LOX-mediated lactate depletion partially reverses the immunosuppressive microenvironment, it is insufficient to transform an immunologically"cold"tumor phenotype into an immunologically"hot"tumor phenotype. Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) utilizes ultrasound waves to activate sonosensitizers, thus generating electrons that react with surrounding oxygen to produce cytotoxic singlet oxygen, leading to apoptosis in cancer cells. Furthermore, there is ample evidence that the reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated by SDT can elicit immunogenic cell death (ICD), which triggers the exposure of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) to facilitate a robust adaptive immune response.

On the basis of these findings, we present a novel strategy combining lactate depletion and SDT with in situ oxygen generation for cancer therapy. Scheme 1 is a graphical abstract as well as Scheme 1, we synthesized ML@Lip, a biocompatible nanoliposome loaded with LOX and manganese porphyrin. After injection via the tail vein, ML@Lip accumulated in the tumor tissue and released LOX and manganese porphyrin. LOX decomposed lactate in tumors to produce hydrogen peroxide, which was catalyzed by manganese ions in manganese porphyrin to generate a substantial amount of oxygen. The oxygen was further utilized by LOX, forming a positive feedback loop of lactate depletion. Simultaneously, sonodynamic therapy was amplified by the produced oxygen. Additionally, lactate depletion reduced lactate efflux into the tumor microenvironment, which transformed TAMs from the M2 phenotype to the M1 phenotype in the TME, thereby ameliorating the immunosuppressive microenvironment. Moreover, the SDT of manganese porphyrin induced not only the apoptosis of tumor cells but also immunogenic cell death, thus facilitating the maturation of DCs to present antigens to T cells. T-cell activation led to more CD8+ cytotoxic T cells and CD4+ T cells infiltrating the TME and killing the tumor. The ML@Lip nanoplatform synergistically remodeled the immunosuppressive microenvironment through lactate depletion and SDT to inhibit tumor growth, thus revealing a promising tumor treatment strategy.

Results and Discussion

Synthesis and characterization of ML@Lip

ML@Lip was synthesized by combining thin-film hydration and ultrasonic emulsification methods. Dipalmitoyl phosphatidylcholine, distearoyl phosphatidylethanolamine-ethylene glycol, cholesterol, and manganese porphyrin were mixed and dissolved in chloroform. The mixture was then subjected to rotary evaporation in a flask to prepare a lipid film containing manganese porphyrin. The LOX solution was mixed with the lipid film, and under ultrasonic treatment, ML@Lip was obtained. According to transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images, ML@Lip exhibited a quasispherical morphology (Fig. 1a). The mean particle size of ML@Lip was determined to be 87.6 ± 14.8 nm via ImageJ software to analyze multiple TEM images (Fig. 1b), and the zeta potential was −19.9 mv (Fig. 1c). These physicochemical characteristics were conducive to the enrichment of ML@Lip at the tumor site via the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect. As shown in Additional file 1: Fig. S1, after the manganese porphyrin was encapsulated, the solution changed from white to dark green. The ultraviolet‒visible (UV‒Vis) spectrum revealed that LOX had a characteristic absorption peak at 380 nm, and manganese porphyrin had a characteristic absorption peak at 465 nm. ML@Lip showed two peaks at the corresponding positions (Fig. 1d), and the intensity of the peaks was concentration dependent (Fig. 1e), which proved that both LOX and manganese porphyrin were successfully encapsulated in the liposomes. The slight red shift observed in the characteristic peak of manganese porphyrin in ML@Lip was attributed to the increased solubility and reduced aggregation state conferred by the liposomal encapsulation, as reported for hydrophobic drugs in lipid-based carriers. Furthermore, on the basis of the standard curve of manganese porphyrin in the UV‒Vis spectrum (Additional file 1: Fig. S2), the encapsulation efficiency of manganese porphyrin was calculated to be 77.05%. By using a lactate kit to establish a standard curve (Additional file 1: Fig. S3), the encapsulation efficiency of LOX was calculated to be 18.46%. The stability of ML@Lip was evaluated by monitoring the change in particle size. As shown in Fig. 1f, ML@Lip was dissolved in phosphate-buffered saline, and the particle size was continuously detected for 7 days. There was no significant change in the particle size, demonstrating that ML@Lip had good stability. Thus, nanoliposomes encapsulating LOX and manganese porphyrin were successfully prepared.

Lactate depletion, oxygen generation and sonodynamic effects

Lactate oxidase catalyzes the decomposition of lactate into pyruvate and hydrogen peroxide. To evaluate the catalytic efficacy of ML@Lip, we cocultured ML@Lip with a 10-mM lactate solution and detected the lactate concentration at different times via a lactate assay kit. As shown in Fig. 1g, the lactate concentration decreased in a time-dependent manner, indicating that ML@Lip catalyzes the decomposition of lactate. Figure 1h demonstrates that manganese ions dissociated from M@Lip catalyze hydrogen peroxide decomposition into oxygen due to their intrinsic catalase-like activity. Notably, ML@Lip utilizes the hydrogen peroxide to produce a large amount of O₂ in a short time, creating an oxygen storm. The abundant oxygen not only provides a substrate for LOX to form a positive feedback loop of lactate depletion but also increases SDT. As a sonosensitizer, manganese porphyrin undergoes a photochemical reaction under the action of ultrasonic waves, converting the surrounding oxygen into singlet oxygen and leading to the formation of cytotoxic ROS. To investigate the SDT of ML@Lip, we detected singlet oxygen using singlet oxygen sensor green (SOSG). As shown in Fig. 1i, under LIFU irradiation, singlet oxygen was generated in the M@Lip group, the ML@Lip group, and the ML@Lip combined with lactate group. Moreover, the fluorescence intensity increased with time, suggesting that ML@Lip has a sonodynamic effect. Notably, the fluorescence intensity of singlet oxygen generation in the ML@Lip combined with lactate group was significantly greater than that in the ML@Lip group, indicating that ML@Lip utilized lactate to generate a substantial amount of oxygen and to increase singlet oxygen production. Therefore, the ML@Lip nanoplatform achieves multiple functions, such as oxygen generation, lactate depletion, and SDT.

Therapeutic performance and SDT effects in vitro

ML@Lip exhibited excellent lactate-consuming ability and SDT effects. It is necessary to evaluate the cytotoxicity of ML@Lip against tumor cells. First, ML@Lip was labeled with DiI to assess the internalization capacity of 4T1 cells. As the incubation time increased, the red fluorescence corresponding to DiI in the cytoplasm became stronger (Fig. 2a), indicating that ML@Lip was effectively internalized through endocytosis. Flow cytometry showed consistent results. As time progressed, the fluorescence intensity inside the cells gradually increased, and the uptake rate reached 72.5% at 12 h (Fig. 2b). Bio-TEM analysis revealed that ML@ Lip entered cells and subsequently fused with lysosomes, leading to the formation of endolysosomes (Additional file 1: Fig. S4). To evaluate whether ML@Lip released LOX and manganese porphyrin via lysosomal escape, we tracked intracellular dynamics using the green fluorescence of manganese porphyrin and Lyso-Tracker Red for lysosomal labeling. Confocal microscopy revealed colocalization of green (manganese porphyrin) and red (lysosome) fluorescence at 12 h, indicating lysosomal retention. By 24 h, partial separation of green and red fluorescence signals was observed, suggesting the escape of manganese porphyrin from lysosomes into the cytoplasm (Additional file 1: Fig. S5).

To evaluate the cytotoxicity of SDT and lactate depletion in tumor cells comprehensively, the standard Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay was subsequently used to assess the viability of 4T1 cells in different conditions. As shown in Fig. 2c, the PBS, US and M@Lip groups exhibited negligible cell damage. Compared with that in the M@Lip group, the cell viability in the M@Lip + US group decreased, indicating a good SDT effect. As expected, the cell viability in the ML@Lip + US group was only 23.75%, suggesting that the ML@Lip + US group had the strongest cytotoxicity against tumor cells. Apoptosis is a common way for tumor cells to die. We used flow cytometry to observe the degree of apoptosis in 4T1 cells (Fig. 2e). Compared with those in the PBS group, the 4T1 cells in each treatment group exhibited different degrees of apoptosis, and 57.53% of the tumor cells in the ML@Lip + US group underwent apoptosis (Fig. 2d), indicating that treatment with ML@Lip combined with LIFU led to extensive apoptosis of tumor cells.

Numerous studies have shown that SDT generates a large amount of ROS, induces mitochondrial damage, and causes tumor cell apoptosis. We detected the ROS level in 4T1 cells via DCFH-DA. As shown in Fig. 2f, after treatment with LIFU, both the ML@Lip + US group and the M@Lip + US group produced a large quantity of ROS, which was confirmed by the intense green fluorescence in the cells. Moreover, the ROS fluorescence intensity in the ML@Lip + US group was significantly greater than that in the M@Lip + US group, suggesting that the ML@Lip + US group had a better SDT effect. The results are consistent with the aforementioned solution experiments, as detected via SOSG. Flow cytometry analysis (Fig. 2g) also quantitatively measured ROS production in the cells, further indicating that the level of ROS production in the ML@Lip + US group was significantly greater than that in all the other groups. Therefore, under LIFU irradiation, ML@Lip underwent SDT, generated a large amount of ROS, and induced tumor cell apoptosis.

Amelioration of the hypoxic tumor microenvironment

On the basis of the excellent oxygen-generating ability of ML@Lip demonstrated in the solution experiments, we investigated the oxygen production of ML@Lip at the tumor sites in vivo. To elucidate the mechanism of oxygen production efficiency, photoacoustic (PA) imaging was utilized to explore blood oxygen saturation in 4T1 tumors after intravenous injection of ML@Lip. As shown in Fig. 3a, b, 24 h after tail-vein injection, the tumor sites in the ML@Lip group presented a much stronger blood oxygen signal, approximately five times greater than that of the control group. These results indicate that a large amount of oxygen was generated by the ML@Lip in the TME, thus increasing the blood oxygen saturation at the tumor sites. Twenty-four hours later, the mice were sacrificed, and the tumor tissues were dissected to detect the expression of hypoxia inducible factor 1α (HIF-1α). Immunofluorescence (Fig. 3c, d) revealed that the red fluorescence representing HIF-1α almost completely disappeared in the ML@Lip group, suggesting that ML@Lip significantly improved the hypoxic microenvironment of the tumor tissues in vivo.

Photoacoustic imaging and fluorescence tracking

To establish the optimal therapeutic window for LIFU-mediated SDT, photoacoustic (PA) imaging and fluorescence tracking were systematically employed. Thanks to the PA imaging characteristics of manganese porphyrin, ML@Lip also possesses photoacoustic imaging functionality. The intensity of the photoacoustic signal exhibited a positive correlation with the concentration of ML@Lip (Fig. 4a). As shown in Fig. 4b, c, after tail—vein injection of ML@Lip, the photoacoustic signal in the 4T1 tumors increased over time and reached a peak at 24 h. To evaluate the biodistribution profiles of nanoliposomes, DiR-labeled L@Lip, M@Lip, and ML@Lip were systemically administered via tail vein injection (n = 3 per group). Longitudinal in vivo fluorescence imaging revealed comparable tumor-targeting kinetics across all formulations, with peak tumor-specific fluorescence signals observed at 24 h post-injection (Fig. 4d). Quantitative analysis demonstrated no statistically significant differences in mean tumor accumulation between groups at this timepoint (p > 0.05, Fig. 4e). Ex vivo fluorescence imaging of major organs at 48 h post-injection (Additional file 1: Fig. S6) further demonstrated predominant nanoliposome accumulation in the liver, spleen, and tumor tissues across all groups, with quantitative analysis showing no significant intergroup differences in organ distribution profiles (p > 0.05, Fig. 4f). We simultaneously detected the Mn content in the tumor tissues at different time points using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP—MS). Consistent with the PA and fluorescence imaging results, the Mn content in the 4T1 tumor tissues was the highest at 24 h (Additional file 1: Fig. S7). These findings collectively highlight tumor-targeted retention as the critical driver of SDT optimization. The temporal concordance of PA signals, in vivo/ex vivo fluorescence tracking, and elemental analysis conclusively identified 24 h post-injection as the optimal LIFU irradiation timepoint, ensuring maximal therapeutic efficacy through synchronized tumor accumulation and SDT activation.

In vivo antitumor effect

Inspired by the remarkable cytotoxicity of ML@Lip against tumor cells in vitro, we further evaluated the antitumor efficacy of ML@Lip in vivo. To explore the antitumor efficacy of ML@Lip in vivo, we employed a 4T1 tumor xenograft BALB/c mouse model. The experimental protocol is shown in Fig. 5a. When the tumor volume reached 100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into 5 groups: the PBS group, the US group, the L@Lip group, the M@Lip + US group, and the ML@Lip + US group. The treatment effects of each group were subsequently evaluated by recording the tumor volume. As shown in Fig. 5b, compared with rapid tumor growth in the PBS group and the US group, tumor growth in the L@Lip group and the M@Lip + US group was effectively inhibited. This inhibition can be attributed to the lactate depletion and SDT effects. Some studies have designed a nanoplatform with hollow Fe₃O₄ loaded with LOX and syrosingopine, and they reported that tumor growth in the LOX-loaded group was inhibited to a certain extent. Other studies have designed a multifunctional nano-sonosensitizer system (FA-MnPs) by encapsulating manganese-protoporphyrin (MnP) into folic acid liposomes. Under ultrasonic vibration, FA-MnPs significantly inhibited tumor growth. Both previous studies and our work confirmed that lactate consumption and SDT are effective strategies for inhibiting tumor growth. Surprisingly, in the ML@Lip + US group, the tumor volume decreased compared with that before treatment, indicating an excellent antitumor effect. This effect was due to the synergistic amplification mechanism in which in situ generation of oxygen by ML@Lip not only creates a positive feedback loop for lactate consumption but also increases the effectiveness of SDT. During the treatment process, there was no significant difference in the body weights of the mice in each group (Fig. 5c). To observe the necrosis and apoptosis of tumors, H&E staining and terminal-deoxynucleotidyl transferase-mediated nick end labeling (TUNEL) immunofluorescence staining were performed. As shown in Fig. 5d, g, the ML@Lip + US group had the most severe degree of necrosis and apoptosis. Compared with that in the M@Lip + US group, there was a greater green fluorescence signal in the ML@Lip + US group, suggesting that more tumor cell apoptosis occurred in the ML@Lip + US group due to the large amount of oxygen produced by ML@Lip increasing SDT and inducing more tumor cell apoptosis. Moreover, we detected proliferating cell nuclear antigen 9 (PCNA) via immunohistochemistry to evaluate the proliferation ability of tumors. As shown in Fig. 5d, h, the ML@Lip + US group had the lowest positive rate, indicating that the proliferation of tumor cells was effectively inhibited. In conclusion, ML@Lip, through lactate consumption and SDT under LIFU, induced tumor necrosis and apoptosis and inhibited tumor cell proliferation. It showed a good in vivo antitumor effect, which provides a promising strategy for solid tumor treatment.

Remodeling the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment

A large amount of ROS generated by SDT triggers endoplasmic reticulum stress. Through the release of many DAMP signals, ICD in tumor cells is induced. For example, the release of high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), adenosine triphosphate (ATP), calreticulin (CRT), and other related substances sends out"find me"and"eat me"signals. This promotes the maturation of DCs, activates T cells, initiates an adaptive immune response, and exerts a killing effect on tumor cells. To explore the ICD of tumor cells by SDT, we employed immunofluorescence staining to evaluate the exposure of CRT. As shown in Fig. 5e, i, a large amount of CRT exposure (red fluorescence) was observed in the M@Lip + US group and the ML@Lip + US group, suggesting that a substantial amount of ICD occurred in the tumors of these two groups. This can be explained by the fact that SDT triggers ICD, as the manganese porphyrin in these two groups induced SDT under the action of LIFU. These results are consistent with those of previous studies. Chunmei Zhang et al. also reported that manganese porphyrin triggers ICD under the action of ultrasonic waves, thus promoting the exposure of CRT and the release of HMGB1 in tumors. We further detected the proportion of mature DCs (CD11c+CD80+CD86+) in tumors via flow cytometry (Fig. 6a, e). The SDT in the ML@Lip + US group induced a high level of DC maturation (35.27%), which was much greater than that in the M@Lip + US group (23.90%). Compared with that in the PBS group, the proportion of mature DCs in the L@Lip group also increased. Some studies have shown that lactate can promote antigen degradation, leading to the failure of MHC class I antigen presentation and hindering the maturation of DCs. Mature DCs are crucial for activating T lymphocytes to stimulate an effective adaptive immune response. We analyzed the activation of T cells by measuring CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) and CD4+ T cells (Fig. 6b). ML@Lip + US treatment significantly elevated the proportion of CD8+ CTLs in the TME. The CD8+ CTL ratios in this group were 3.52-fold, 3.39-fold, 1.89-fold, and 1.54-fold greater than those in the control, US, L@Lip, and M@Lip + US groups, respectively (Fig. 6f). The increase in the proportion of CD4+ T cells (23%) was also most obvious in the ML@Lip + US group (Fig. 6g). The above results suggest that the ML@Lip + US group induced significant CRT exposure through ICD, thereby promoting the maturation of dendritic cells. This activation led to the differentiation of a substantial number of CTLs and CD4 + T cells in the sentinel lymph nodes, which immunologically kill tumor cells, thereby synergistically inhibiting tumor growth via SDT.

Research indicates that the lactate-rich environment formed by cancer cells consistently directs recruited macrophages toward M2 polarization and forms tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), which are crucial factors in establishing the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Yuwei Li et al. reported that the synergistic effect of lactate depletion and SIRPα signal blockade can enhance the phagocytic ability of TAMs and promote their repolarization toward the antitumor M1 phenotype. Therefore, we used flow cytometry to detect M1-type and M2-type TAMs in the TME. Compared with that in the PBS group (7.24%), the proportion of M1 macrophages in the ML@Lip + US group (21.90%) and the L@Lip group (14.93%) was significantly greater (Fig. 6c, h). Compared with that in the PBS group (23.37%), the proportion of M2 macrophages in the ML@Lip + US group (14.70%) and the L@Lip group (17.60%) was significantly lower (Fig. 6d, i). These findings suggest that in the tumor microenvironment, the TAMs in the ML@Lip + US and L@Lip groups transformed from the M2 phenotype to the M1 phenotype, thereby ameliorating the immunosuppressive microenvironment.

Biosafety

Liposomes, as carriers, have been approved by the FDA and possess excellent biosafety. After intravenous administration of L@Lip, M@Lip, and ML@Lip, hematological analysis revealed that the WBC, RBC, HGB, and PLT levels in the three groups remained within normal physiological ranges. No significant fluctuations were observed in the proportions of neutrophils, lymphocytes, or monocytes, indicating no detectable inflammatory responses, hemorrhage, or hemolysis induced by the lipsomes. Biochemical assays demonstrated that key hepatic and renal function markers, including serum albumin (ALB), alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), blood urea nitrogen (UREA), and creatinine (CR), were maintained at normal levels in the three groups (Additional file 1: Fig. S8), confirming the absence of notable hepatotoxicity or nephrotoxicity. Pathological evaluation further supported these findings, with H&E-stained sections of major organs showing no significant histopathological abnormalities across the groups (Additional file 1: Figs. S9–S11). These results indicate that L@Lip, M@Lip and ML@Lip exhibited excellent biocompatibility and biosafety at therapeutic doses, with no observable acute or chronic toxic effects.

Conclusions

In summary, an oxygen-generating nanoplatform, namely, ML@Lip, was constructed to inhibit tumor growth by remodeling the immunosuppressive microenvironment through lactate metabolic therapy and SDT. LOX and manganese porphyrin were encapsulated in the nanoliposomes, thus enabling ML@Lip to achieve passive tumor targeting through the EPR effect. ML@Lip was observed to aggregate in tumors by PA. ML@Lip ingeniously exploited the decomposition products of lactate to generate substantial amounts of oxygen, thereby ameliorating hypoxia in the TME. The cascade reaction with self-sustained oxygen generation not only established a positive feedback loop for lactate depletion but also enhanced the efficacy of SDT. Under ultrasonic irradiation, ML@Lip triggered the generation of substantial amounts of ROS, inducing apoptosis in tumor cells both in vitro and in vivo. Additionally, ROS induced tumor ICD, which promoted the maturation of dendritic cells and the activation of T cells, leading to significant infiltration of CD8+ CTL and CD4+ T cells in the TME. Moreover, lactate depletion therapy induced phenotypic reprogramming of TAMs from the M2 phenotype to the M1 phenotype. Therefore, the immunosuppressive TME was reversed to an immunologically"hot"state through the activation of adaptive immunity triggered by ICD and the modulation of innate immunity associated with TAMs. The results demonstrated significant tumor growth inhibition and antiproliferative effects in vivo. This work offers novel insights into the development of nanomedicines that synergize lactate metabolism therapy with immunotherapy.

Materials and methods

Preparation of ML@Lip

ML@Lip was prepared by combining thin-film hydration and ultrasonic emulsification methods. First, 10 U of lactate oxidase (Sigma, Germany) was added to 5 ml of phosphate-buffered solution and dissolved thoroughly for later use. Then, 12 mg of dipalmitoyl phosphatidylcholine (Sigma, Germany), 4 mg of distearoyl phosphatidylethanolamine-ethylene glycol (Sigma, Germany), 4 mg of cholesterol (Sigma, Germany), and 2 mg of manganese porphyrin (Alladin, China) were added to chloroform and dissolved completely. The organic solvent was removed by rotary evaporation under vacuum to form a liposome film. Next, the lactate oxidase solution was added to the flask and rotated for 1 h to obtain an emulsified film. Under ice-bath conditions, the mixture was further emulsified into nanoliposome spheres via a sonicator with a pressure of 50 W/cm2, a duty cycle of 50%, and a treatment time of 5 min. Finally, the product was dialyzed for 8 h to remove free drugs. M@Lip was obtained by following the above procedure without the addition of lactate oxidase solution. L@Lip was prepared by omitting the manganese porphyrin.

The drug loading concentration and encapsulation efficiency of ML@Lip were quantified via their characteristic UV‒Vis absorption (465 nm) in a DMSO solution. The absorbance measurements were performed using a UV-2100 ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) spectrophotometer (RIGOL Technologies Co., Ltd., Beijing, China).

A Lactate Assay Kit (Beyotime, China) was also used to quantify the drug loading concentration and encapsulation efficiency of ML@Lip. These nanolipsomes were characterized via transmission electron microscopy (S-4800, Hitachi High-Technologies Corporation, Tokyo, Japan) and dynamic light scattering (ZEN3600, Malvern Instruments Ltd., Malvern, UK) analysis.

Lactate depletion

A 10-mM lactate solution was placed in centrifuge tubes. Tube A served as the blank control group. In tube B, 150 μg/ml of ML@Lip was added, and in tube C, 300 μg/ml of ML@Lip was added. The tubes were placed in a shaker at 37 °C. The lactate content in the solutions was detected at 1 min, 5 min, and 10 min via a Lactate Assay Kit.

Oxygen generation

A 10-ml solution of 50 mM H₂O₂ was placed in each centrifuge tube. Tube A served as the blank control group. M@Lip was added to tube B, and ML@Lip along with 10 mM lactate solution was added to tube C. The liquid surface was immediately sealed with liquid paraffin. A JPBJ-608 dissolved oxygen meter (INESA Scientific Instrument Co., Ltd., Shanghai, China) was inserted below the liquid surface to detect the oxygen content in the solution every minute.

Singlet oxygen generation

The generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS) was determined via a singlet oxygen fluorescence probe (Invitrogen, USA). Singlet oxygen sensor green (SOSG) was dissolved in methanol to a final concentration of 5 μM. The mixed solutions in each group were subjected to ultrasonic treatment for different durations with US (2 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle). Finally, the fluorescence intensity at 525 nm was detected via a fluorescence spectrophotometer (Agilent Technologies, China).

Cell culture and animals

4T1 cells (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA) were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, USA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (Servicebio, China) and 1% penicillin‒streptomycin (Gibco, USA). Monthly screening for mycoplasma contamination is conducted utilizing the Myco-Lumi™ Luminescent Mycoplasma Detection Kit (Beyotime, China). BALB/c mice (female, 6-week-old) were provided by the Experimental Animal Center of Chongqing Medical University for this experiment. The experiment was approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Chongqing Medical University, with the approval number IACUC—CQMU—2024—0016.

A 4T1 tumor-bearing mouse model was established by subcutaneously injecting 1 × 10⁶ 4T1 cells into the right hind leg of each mouse. The formula for calculating the tumor volume was as follows: tumor volume (mm3) = 1/2 × length × width2.

Cell internalization

To observe the intracellular uptake of ML@Lip, 4T1 cells were seeded in confocal dishes overnight and incubated with DiI-labeled ML@Lip for different time periods (3, 6, and 12 h). After being stained with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole (DAPI), the cells were examined via confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM, Nikon A1R, Tokyo, Japan). Additionally, flow cytometry (CytoFlex, Beckman Coulter, USA) was used to quantify the fluorescence intensity of DiI in 4T1 cells.

Lysosomal escape

To observe the lysosomal escape of ML@Lip, 4T1 cells were seeded in confocal dishes overnight and incubated with for different time periods (0, 12, and 24 h). After being stained with Lyso-Tracker (Beyotime, China), the cells were examined via CLSM.

Cell cytotoxicity

A total of 1 × 104 4T1 cells were seeded in each well of a 96-well plate and divided into 6 groups: the PBS group, the US group, the M@Lip group, the L@Lip group, the M@Lip + US group, and the ML@Lip + US group, n = 6. After 24 h, different treatments were applied according to the groups. The parameters of US were as follows: 1 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle, and 3 min. Twenty-four hours later, the cell viability was determined via a CCK8 assay kit (Beyotime, China).

ROS generation

4T1 cells (5 × 104 cells per dish) were seeded in CLSM dishes and divided into 5 groups: the PBS group, the US group, the M@Lip group, the M@Lip + US group, and the ML@Lip + US group. There were 3 dishes in each group. After 24 h, liposomes were added, and 12 h later, ultrasonic treatment was performed. The US parameters were 1 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle, and 3 min. Subsequently, the cells were stained with 10 μM 2′,7′ dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (DCFH—DA, an intracellular ROS detection reagent) for 30 min and then observed via CLSM (λex/λem = 488/525 nm).

PA imaging

A 4T1 tumor-bearing mouse model was established. When the tumor volume reached 100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into two groups (n = 3). The ML@Lip group was injected with 200 μl of ML@Lip solution (3 mg/ml) via the tail vein, and the control group was injected with 200 μl of PBS solution. Before injection and 24 h after injection, Oxy-Hemo mode imaging was performed on the tumor sites of the mice via the Vevo LAZA photoacoustic imaging system (VISUALSONICS, Canada) to evaluate the oxyhemoglobin content. For another 3 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, 200 μl of ML@Lip solution (3 mg/ml) was injected via the tail vein. Photoacoustic (PA) mode imaging was conducted on the tumor sites before injection and at 6 h, 12 h, 24 h, and 48 h after injection.

Fluorescence tracking

A 4T1 tumor-bearing mouse model was established. When the tumor volume reached 100 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into three groups (n = 3). Following intravenous injection via the tail vein of DiR-labeled L@Lip, M@Lip, and ML@Lip, in vivo imaging of the animals was performed at 3, 6, 12, 24, and 48 h via a small animal in vivo imaging system (IVIS Spectrum 2, Revvity, MA, USA)). Ex: 745 nm/Em: 780 nm. After 48 h, the mice were euthanized, and ex vivo fluorescence imaging of the major organs, including the heart, liver, spleen, lungs, kidneys and tumors was conducted. Quantitative analysis of the fluorescence signal was performed using Living Image software.

In vivo therapy

To evaluate the therapeutic effect of ML@Lip in vivo, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into 5 groups: the PBS group, the US group, the M@Lip group, the M@Lip + US group, and the ML@Lip + US group, with n = 5. Liposomes were injected via the tail vein on days 1, 3, and 5, and low-intensity focused ultrasound treatment was performed on days 2, 4, and 6. The US parameters were 2 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle, and 5 min. The tumor volume was measured every other day. The mice were sacrificed on the 7th day after the treatment ended. One mouse was randomly selected from each group, and the tumor tissues were collected, fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde, and subjected to hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase-mediated dUTP nick-end labeling (TUNEL) staining, and proliferating cell nuclear antigen (PCNA) staining.

In vivo immune microenvironment assessment

To evaluate the in vivo therapeutic effect of ML@Lip, 4T1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into 5 groups: the PBS group, the US group, the M@Lip group, the M@Lip + US group, and the ML@Lip + US group, with n = 3. The treatment protocol was the same as before. One day after the treatment ended, the mice were sacrificed, and the tumor tissues were collected and prepared as single-cell suspensions. To analyze M1- and M2-phenotype TAMs, the cells were stained with different fluorescent antibodies (anti-CD11b FITC, anti-F4/80 PE, anti-CD86 APC, and anti-CD206 APC). To analyze the maturation of DCs, the cells were stained with different fluorescent antibodies (anti-CD11c FITC, anti-CD80 PE, and anti-CD86 APC). To analyze T-cell activation, the cells were stained with different fluorescent antibodies (anti-CD3 FITC, anti-CD8a PE, and anti-CD4 APC). All fluorescent antibodies were purchased from BioLegend (USA). The fluorescence intensity was detected via a flow cytometer (CytoFlex, Beckman Coulter, USA).

Biosafety

BALB/c mice (female, 6-week-old) were injected with 200 μl of L@Lip, M@Lip, and ML@Lip solution (3 mg/ml) via the tail vein. The mice were sacrificed on days 1, 3, 7, 14 and 28 after injection. Blood samples were collected for routine blood and liver and kidney function tests. Additionally, the heart, liver, spleen, lungs, and kidneys were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for H&E staining.
