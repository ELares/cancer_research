# Synthetic lethality between RB-loss and E2F3 inhibition in small cell cancers targeted by pyrimidine synthesis blockade.

## Abstract

Small cell carcinoma is a highly lethal cancer variant often found with neuroendocrine (NE) features, as exemplified by small cell lung cancer and small cell NE prostate cancer (SCPC). A genome-wide CRISPR dependency screen using SCPC models generated through human prostate cell transformation identifies a requirement for the transcription factor E2F3. E2F3 dependency is linked to RB inactivation, a near universal occurrence across small cell cancers. The requirement for E2F3 is shared by RB-deficient cells originating from the prostate, lung, and adnexa. In RB-deficient cancer cells, E2F3 inhibition restrains cell cycle progression, proliferation, and tumor growth in vivo. Inhibition of de novo pyrimidine synthesis limits E2F3 expression and suppresses small cell carcinoma proliferation in culture. Directly or indirectly targeting E2F3 to leverage a pan-cancer synthetic lethality resulting from RB inactivation represents a potential treatment strategy.

Synthetic lethality between RB-loss and E2F3 inhibition in small cell cancers targeted by pyrimidine synthesis blockade

Significance

The synthetic lethality of E2F3 inactivation in RB-deficient small cell cancer cells is established. E2F3 dependency is shared across RB-deficient cells originating from multiple tissues. The requirement for E2F3 in RB-deficient small cell cancer offers a promising avenue for developing targeted therapeutics. Inhibition of de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis emerged as a rapidly translatable approach to restrain E2F3 expression for small cell cancer treatment.

Small cell cancers are aggressive malignancies frequently characterized by neuroendocrine (NE) features that arise in multiple tissues such as the lung, prostate, bladder, adnexa, and colon. Small cell cancer develops either de novo or via trans-differentiation from adenocarcinoma as a mechanism of resistance to targeted therapies. The incidence of treatment-driven small cell cancer transformation is increasing and is a mechanism of escape from androgen-deprivation therapy in prostate cancer and EGFR inhibition in lung cancer. The chemotherapy regimens that are transiently effective for the treatment of small cell lung cancer (SCLC) are also administered for nonpulmonary small cell cancers due to their similar histology and molecular characteristics, but they are generally less effective in these settings.

The dismal prognosis of patients with small cell cancers is in part due to a complete lack of treatment options that leverage knowledge of the genetic alterations that drive the disease. Small cell cancers maintain tissue-specific genetic features but converge at histological, transcriptional, genetic, and epigenetic levels. Inactivation of RB and TP53 occurs nearly universally across small cell cancers and loss of these tumor suppressors is a key event in the small cell NE transdifferentiation of prostate, lung, and bladder cells.The ubiquitous genetic alterations that are shared by small cell cancers may give rise to common vulnerabilities amenable to therapeutic targeting.

The Cancer Dependency Map (DepMap) project defined the landscape of genetic dependencies across cancer cell lines using CRISPR knockout (KO) and shRNA screens. While such data are available for over 25 SCLC cell lines, gene dependency data for nonpulmonary small cell cancer is scarce due to a limited number of representative models. NCI-H660 is the only small cell prostate cancer cell line profiled in DepMap, and small cell cancers derived from other tissue types, such as the bladder, have no DepMap-indexed cell lines.

To address this gap, we established a series of cell lines that recapitulate the transcriptional and phenotypic characteristics of human small cell NE prostate cancer (SCPC; 10, 16). To develop these models, human prostate basal cells from multiple donors were independently: i) transformed by introducing five alterations concurrently [dominant-negative TP53, myristoylated-AKT1, RB1 shRNA, cMYC, and BCL2 (termed PARCB)]; ii) cultured as organoids; and iii) propagated as tumors in immunodeficient mice. The tumors resulting from PARCB-transformed human prostate basal cells recapitulate the histological and transcriptional features of clinical SCPC. Cell lines derived from PARCB tumors grow as suspension aggregates in culture, a characteristic shared with SCLC cell lines, and are suitable for use in phenotypic screens to define SCPC-specific as well as pan-small cell cancer genetic dependencies.

Genome-wide, proliferation-based CRISPR KO screens in three PARCB SCPC cell lines identified E2F3 as a targetable vulnerability in this cancer subtype. Subsequent validation experiments revealed a synthetic lethal interaction between inhibition of the transcription factor E2F3 and RB-loss. The de novo pyrimidine synthesis pathway enables E2F3 expression, and inhibition of a key enzyme in this pathway, dihydroorotate dehydrogenase (DHODH), emerged as a rapidly translatable treatment approach to target small cell cancers originating from different tissue types.

Results

A CRISPR Dependency Screen Reveals SCPC Essential Genes.

To define the landscape of genetic vulnerabilities in SCPC, an in vitro pooled CRISPR gene dependency screen was performed using three SCPC cell lines derived via PARCB trans-differentiation. Each of the cell lines were derived from PARCB tumors originating from unique donor tissue and identified as PARCB1, PARCB3, or PARCB9 (Fig. 1A). After sgRNA library transduction, selection, 14 d of culture, and next-generation sequencing, sgRNA abundance was evaluated using the MAGeCK computational pipeline. Genes were classified as essential if sgRNAs dropped out, or suppressors if targeting sgRNAs were enriched over the culture period. In an integrated analysis of the results from independent screens across the three prostate PARCB cell lines, 1,398 essential genes were identified (Fig. 1B).

The results were cross-referenced with the DepMap gene dependency database to categorize genes emerging in the PARCB screen as i) common-essential (i.e. generally required for the in vitro proliferation of cancer cell line models); or ii) preferentially essential in SCPC. In our dataset an FDR < 0.05 filter retained ~4% (68/1,398) of established common essential genes, indicating excessive false negatives. By contrast, a nominal P value filtering recovered a substantial fraction of genes (423/1,398; 30%) contained in the DepMap common-essential gene set, and 69 of the top 100-ranking essential genes identified by the prostate PARCB cell screen are categorized as common-essential across DepMap models (Fig. 1B). The remaining 975 of the total 1,398 SCPC essential genes are necessary only in a subset of DepMap-indexed cancer cell line models and hereafter defined as “PARCB-essential.” The complete analysis of the PARCB CRISPR dependency screen is provided in Dataset S1.

The screen identified previously described vulnerabilities of small cell cancers, including CHEK1 and PLK1, but also unexpected mediators. E2F3 emerged as the only E2F transcription factor family member identified as a PARCB-essential gene, ranking in the top 5% (61st highest scoring) of all dependencies (Fig. 1C). The E2F family is a group of transcription factors well-studied for their role in regulating cell cycle progression and cancer development. Canonical activating members of the E2F family (E2F1, E2F2, and E2F3A) drive the expression of genes required for proliferation and may be sequestered and inactivated by RB via specific binding motifs.

The DepMap dataset was interrogated to determine if prostate PARCB-essential genes are also required by small cell cancer cells derived from other tissue types. An overlap in gene dependencies was observed across SCLC and prostate PARCB, with E2F3 emerging as a core, pan-small cell cancer essential gene (Fig. 1D). The findings from the prostate PARCB screen suggest that: i) small cell cancers, regardless of their tissue of origin, share common genetic dependencies that may broadly represent targetable vulnerabilities; ii) E2F3 is a pan-small cell cancer essential gene; and iii) E2F3 is the only member E2F family necessary for continued PARCB proliferation in vitro.

SCLC and SCPC Cells Require E2F3 for Continued Proliferation In Culture.

In the DepMap dataset, SCLC cell lines are preferentially dependent on E2F3 (Fig. 2A) and enhanced E2F3 expression is associated with increased E2F3 gene dependency (SI Appendix, Fig. S1A). To validate the effects of E2F3 blockade on SCPC cell proliferation, two of the prostate PARCB cell line models used in the CRISPR screen (PARCB3 and PARCB9) were transduced with lentivirus encoding an E2F3-targeting shRNA or a nontargeting control shRNA, with a blue fluorescent protein (BFP) selectable marker. The tested E2F3-targeting shRNA is specific for the 3’UTR of E2F3 shared by two widely expressed E2F3 isoforms, E2F3A and E2F3B, that differ only in their N-terminal sequences. E2F3 knockdown in PARCB9 cells was confirmed by immunoblot analysis 2 d after sorting for BFP-positive cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S1B).

The proliferation of sorted shControl and shE2F3 PARCB cells was tracked using Cell Titer Glo. In both PARCB3 and PARCB9 models, shControl cells proliferated rapidly, whereas proliferation was completely blocked in cells where E2F3 was inhibited (Fig. 2B). Impaired proliferation and inhibition of cell aggregate formation in PARCB9 cells was confirmed by BFP microscopy (Fig. 2C). In a separate experiment, inhibition of proliferation following E2F3 knockdown was confirmed using a doxycycline-regulated shRNA system (SI Appendix, Fig. S1 C and D).

SCLC encompasses transcriptional subtypes delineated by the expression of key transcriptional drivers: ASCL1 (A), POU2F3 (P), NEUROD (N), or YAP1 (Y), while recent studies have revealed a fifth subtype driven by HNF4A. To confirm E2F3 dependency across SCLC transcriptional subtypes, a panel of SCLC cell lines representing the P and N subtypes were transduced with shControl or shE2F3-encoding lentivirus. Consistent with the results in SCPC cell models, E2F3 knockdown blocked the proliferation of all tested SCLC lines (Fig. 2D). To determine if E2F3 knockdown triggers a cytotoxic or cytostatic response in SCLC cells, propidium iodide (PI) staining and flow cytometry cell cycle analysis was performed in SCLC-N subtype NCI-H82 cells. Immunoblot analysis 2 d after sorting for BFP-expression confirmed E2F3 knockdown (Fig. 2E). E2F3 knockdown increased the population of cells with a Sub-G1 DNA content while limiting the number of proliferating cells (Fig. 2F), indicating lethality of E2F3 inhibition in SCLC. We determined the effect of E2F3 KO on the proliferation of SCLC cell lines representing the A, P, N, and Y subtypes across the DepMap dataset (SI Appendix, Fig. S1E). A pattern of E2F3 dependency spanning cell lines representing A, N, and P subtypes, but excluding cell lines representing the YAP1 subtype, was observed. Importantly, in contrast to Subtypes A, P, and N which are characterized by near-ubiquitous RB/TP53 comutation, subtype Y includes a subset of RB-proficient SCLC with retained RB functionality.

Synthetic Lethal Interaction between E2F3 Inhibition and RB-Loss.

The unique genetic dependencies of small cell cancer cells across different tissues of origin may be secondary to alterations of RB, TP53, or the PTEN/AKT pathway, events that are near-universally observed across small cell cancers. To trace pathways linked to E2F3 dependency, the DepMap dataset was applied to test the association between the antiproliferative effect of E2F3 KO and the enrichment for transcriptional molecular pathway ontologies (Fig. 3A and Dataset S2). Notably, the strongest predictor for E2F3 dependency (across over 30,000 tests) was enrichment for a previously defined transcriptional signature for RB inactivation consisting of 68 genes highly expressed in RB-deficient models (Chen68; gene set in Dataset S3). Across DepMap-indexed models, RB loss of function mutations and deletions are associated with E2F3 dependency in lung cancer cell lines (Fig. 3B), and cell lines representing skin, brain, and bone cancers (SI Appendix, Fig. S2A). Alterations of other PARCB transforming factors (cMYC amplification, TP53 loss of function, BCL2 amplification, or AKT amplification) did not significantly associate with E2F3 dependency across DepMap cell lines (SI Appendix, Fig. S2B).

Across cancer cell lines in the DepMap dataset, E2F3 dependency is associated with: i) RB mutation (SI Appendix, Fig. S2C); ii) enrichment for a transcriptional signature of RB inactivation (Fig. 3A); and iii) phenotypic markers of RB inactivation (CDK4 dependency; SI Appendix, Fig. S2D).

The association between Chen68 RB-loss signature enrichment and gene dependency was evaluated for all genes evaluated in DepMap CRISPR KO screens across all cancer cell lines. E2F3 dependency ranked within the top 0.1% of genes with the highest scoring dependency correlation with Chen68 RB-loss signature enrichment in the CRISPR-based gene dependency dataset (Fig. 3C) and the top 0.4% highest scoring correlations in the shRNA-based gene dependency dataset (SI Appendix, Fig. S2E). A previously reported NE gene expression signature did not identify E2F3 dependency alone, and many low-grade NE tumors and pancreatic NE tumors do not commonly possess RB alterations (SI Appendix, Fig. S2F). In summary, cell lines exhibiting Chen68 RB-loss transcriptional signature enrichment tend to be susceptible to E2F3 inhibition.

Enrichment for the Chen68 RB-loss signature was tested in samples from prostate and lung PARCB tumor analysis published by our group. Enrichment for Chen68 RB-loss signature was observed only in models where RB was suppressed by shRNA and not in models lacking this PARCB component (Fig. 3D). Enrichment for this transcriptional signature of RB suppression tracked with down-regulation of RB expression, and elevated E2F3 expression associated with RB suppression across end stage tumors derived from various combinations of PARCB genetic alterations (Fig. 3E).

The Chen68 RB-loss signature is markedly enriched across SCLC cell lines relative to other DepMap-annotated cancer cell lines, and a range of score enrichment was observed across cancer types, with ovarian cancer cell lines exhibiting a particularly wide range of RB-loss score enrichment (Fig. 3F). RB-loss is a recurrent event in advanced ovarian cancer, and this alteration has a key role in enabling disease progression following platinum-based chemotherapy. The wide range in RB-loss transcriptional signature enrichment across ovarian cancer cell line models enables the testing of E2F3 dependency in RB-proficient and RB-deficient models from a single cancer type. To investigate whether ovarian cancer cell lines with RB defects are vulnerable to E2F3 knockdown, OVCAR8 and OVCAR5 cells, exhibiting the highest and lowest RB-loss score enrichment across ovarian cancer cell lines, respectively, were transduced with lentivirus to deliver control or E2F3 targeting shRNA (Fig. 3G). RB deficiency in these models was confirmed by immunoblot analysis (SI Appendix, Fig. S2G). Consistent with a selective requirement for E2F3 in RB-deficient cancer cells, E2F3 knockdown selectively impaired the proliferation of OVCAR8 cells while proliferation was maintained in RB-proficient OVCAR5 cells (Fig. 3H).

To confirm that RB inactivation is linked to E2F3 dependency, a stable RB-deficient cell line was generated using CRISPR/Cas9 on the background of RBwt TP53mut PC3 prostate cancer cells. E2F3 was knocked down using shRNA in the PC3 parental and PC3 RB KO isogenic cells (Fig. 4A). While E2F3 knockdown in parental PC3 cells did not affect proliferation, it potently suppressed the proliferation of PC3 RB KO cells (Fig. 4 B and C). Proliferation inhibition in PC3 RB KO cells with E2F3 knockdown was associated with a depletion of cells in the S and G2/M cell cycle phases and an increase in cells with a Sub-G1 DNA content (SI Appendix, Fig. S3A). In an EdU pulse and flow cytometry cell cycle analysis using the PC3 WT and RB KO models, E2F3 knockdown decreased the percentage of actively replicating, EdU-positive cells only when RB was absent (SI Appendix, Fig. S3B).

RB-proficient and -deficient isogenic PC3 cells were analyzed by RNAseq to test the enrichment of five previously defined transcriptional signatures for RB-loss (SI Appendix, Fig. S3C). Among these transcriptional signatures, only the Chen68 gene RB-loss signature distinguishes the isogenic RB-proficient and -deficient PC3 cells, the same signature that identifies E2F3-dependent DepMap models (Fig. 3C). This transcriptional signature is also enriched across SCLC cell lines and PARCB tumors.

To evaluate whether E2F3 is required for tumor growth in vivo, male NSG mice were implanted subcutaneously with PC3 parental cells or isogenic RB KO cells transduced with either control or E2F3-targeting shRNAs. Across two independent cohorts, using unique sets of transduced PC3 cells and murine hosts, E2F3 knockdown produced a modest reduction in the growth of tumors with intact RB (Fig. 4D and SI Appendix, Fig. S3D). In contrast, depletion of E2F3 had a pronounced effect on RB-deficient tumors, markedly suppressing their growth relative to controls. Tumor growth inhibition was confirmed by endpoint analyses (Fig. 4 E and F).

To validate the role of RB in E2F3 knockdown-driven proliferation inhibition, RB expression was restored in PC3 RB KO cells by lentiviral transduction (Fig. 4G). RB KO and restoration was functionally validated by monitoring cell cycle progression following CDK4/6 inhibitor treatment (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A). Restoration of RB expression in PC3 RB KO cells restored CDK4/6i sensitivity and provided resistance to E2F3 knockdown (Fig. 4H).

By analyzing the abundance of RNAseq reads associating uniquely with either E2F3A or E2F3B, we found that the E2F3A isoform was up-regulated across SCLC clinical specimens and cell lines (Fig. 4I). The previously tested E2F3 shRNA blocks both E2F3A and E2F3B expression and targets the 3’UTR shared by the E2F3A and E2F3B isoforms. To specifically elucidate the role of E2F3A inhibition in the proliferation inhibition mediated by E2F3A/B knockdown, PC3 RB KO cells were engineered to express shRNA-resistant E2F3A from a codon-optimized template (Fig. 4J). The RB KO cells engineered to express the shRNA-resistant E2F3A gene were completely resistant to the proliferation inhibition mediated by the E2F3A/B-targeting shRNA (Fig. 4K). Two unique shRNA specific for E2F3A confirmed that E2F3A knockdown limited the proliferation of RB KO PC3 cells (SI Appendix, Fig. S4 B and C). In summary, the E2F3 isoform E2F3A is required for the continued proliferation of RB-deficient cancer cells.

Inhibition of De Novo Nucleotide Synthesis Limits E2F3 Expression and Restrains Small Cell Cancer Proliferation.

While the synthetic lethality of RB inactivation and E2F3 inhibition positions E2F3 as a high-priority target for small cell cancer treatment, no selective E2F3 inhibitors are currently available. We reasoned that inhibitors of the signaling or metabolic networks regulating E2F3 expression could represent pharmacological interventions that can be rapidly integrated into treatment regimens. We focused on evaluating the role of the pyrimidine de novo synthesis pathway, in particular the rate-limiting enzyme DHODH, as a potential regulator of E2F3 expression based on the following: i) Inhibition of DHODH has been reported to have potent lethal effects in SCLC models, RB-deficient retinoblastoma cell lines, and cancers exhibiting transcriptional or phenotypic overlap with small cell cancers, such as neuroblastoma; ii) DHODH drives the transcriptional elongation of cancer-related genes (such as cMYC) in melanoma and the neural crest; and iii) DHODH inhibitors (teriflunomide and leflunomide) are currently FDA-approved for autoimmune conditions and may be immediately translated. Furthermore, several mediators of pyrimidine nucleotide synthesis were identified in the SCPC genome-wide dependency screen, including CAD (rank 188) and DTYMK (rank 25; Dataset S1).

We determined the effect of DHODH inhibition on E2F3A protein levels alongside other activating members of the E2F family (E2F1) in SCLC cells (Fig. 5 A and B). Loss of E2F3A protein levels is observed as early as 24 h after DHODH inhibitor treatment, while no effect on E2F1 levels was observed (Fig. 5A). Strikingly, this does not reflect cell cycle arrest in G1 but instead coincides with an accumulation of cells in S-phase, the cell cycle phase where E2F3A expression is highest (Fig. 5B). Pyrimidine nucleotides may be generated either by the de novo pathway, which requires DHODH activity, or via the salvage or preformed nucleosides such as uridine from the environment. The effect of DHODH inhibition on E2F3 protein levels, as well as markers of apoptosis (cleaved caspase 3), was prevented by supplementation with the pyrimidine nucleoside uridine in a dose-dependent manner, with complete rescue achieved by a physiological uridine concentration (10 µM; Fig. 5C). DHODH inhibitor-mediated down regulation of E2F3 protein levels was also observed in two PARCB SCPC models (Fig. 5D). Phenotypic responses to DHODH inhibitor-driven pyrimidine starvation were monitored in a panel of SCLC cell lines, the SCPC cell line H660, and cell lines derived from prostate PARCB tumors. DHODH inhibition potently prevented the growth of all small cell cancer models tested (Fig. 5E). A more pronounced disruption of cell cycle progression was observed in PC3 RB KO cells treated with a DHODH inhibitor compared to parental counterparts when cultured in media with serum containing nucleosides such as uridine (SI Appendix, Fig. S4D). This effect is further amplified when cells are cotreated with an inhibitor of the pyrimidine nucleoside transporter SLC29A1, which enables the salvage of extracellular pyrimidine nucleosides.

To determine the selective lethality of pyrimidine synthesis inhibition across cancer types, we evaluated DHODH inhibitor response across a library of cancer cell lines representing acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), peripheral T cell lymphoma (PCTL), SCLC, prostate adenocarcinoma (PCa), hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), and pancreatic cancer (PDAC), expanding on our previous analysis. A proliferation rate normalized growth inhibition metric was applied to account for heterogeneity in cell line doubling times and to isolate cytostatic or cytotoxic effects. SCLC cells were enriched among those exhibiting a cytotoxic response following suppression of DHODH activity, which contrasts with most cancer cell line models which exhibited partial or cytostatic responses (Fig. 5F). The results suggest that DHODH sustains the generation of pyrimidine nucleotides to enable E2F3 expression in small cell cancer (Fig. 5G). Limiting pyrimidine synthesis restricts the expression of E2F3 and suppresses the proliferation of small cell cancer cells derived from multiple tissue types.

Discussion

We demonstrate the genetic dependencies of SCPC and identify a pan-small cell cancer vulnerability to E2F3 inhibition. This systematic evaluation of SCPC genetic dependencies builds on previous computational analyses highlighting pan-small cell cancer pharmacological vulnerabilities.

Synthetic lethality driven by tumor suppressor loss may enable the selective targeting of tumor cells while sparing normal tissues. This principle has been exploited in current drug/gene combinations, such as the use of PARP inhibitors in BRCA-mutant cancers, ATR kinase inhibitors for ATM-mutant cancers, and PRMT5 inhibitors for MTAP-deleted cancers. We found that E2F3 inhibition exhibits synthetic lethality with inactivation of RB, a tumor suppressor gene that is frequently mutated, deleted, or otherwise inactivated across small cell cancers. This observation is supported by studies showing an increased reliance on E2F3 in RB-deficient cancer cells using the Colt2/Marcotte shRNA screens, as well as in DepMap CRISPR/Cas9 screens and subsequent validation experiments using siRNA in isogenic RB1-proficient and deficient MCF10A cells. E2F3 also emerged in a CRISPR transcription factor dependency screen in SCLC and ranks 316th (out of 11,346 genes) as an RNAi-based selective vulnerability of SCLC compared to the adenocarcinoma NSCLC subtype. Studies in mouse models also indicate a requirement for E2F3 in the development of the pulmonary NE hyperplasia observed in Rb−/− chimeric mice. Identifying shared genetic requirements across small cell cancer provides a conceptual foundation for applying treatments agnostic to tissue of origin.

Repurposing Pyrimidine Synthesis Inhibitors to Target Small Cell Cancer with RB-Loss.

DHODH is demonstrated to control E2F3 expression. Notably, DHODH inhibitors, such as teriflunomide and leflunomide, are FDA-approved for autoimmune conditions and are an immediately translatable approach for small cell cancer treatment due to their established safety profiles. Our findings support further investigation into the therapeutic window and potential synergistic effect of drug combinations targeting both pyrimidine synthesis and salvage pathways in SCPC and SCLC. The nucleoside salvage pathway provides an alternative biochemical route for pyrimidine nucleotide synthesis that bypasses DHODH and inhibitors of pyrimidine nucleoside salvage, such as nucleoside transport inhibitors, are FDA approved. Additionally, nucleoside kinase inhibitors are under clinical investigation for the treatment of solid cancers (NCT05055609).

Function of E2F3 Necessary for Continued RB-Deficient Cell Proliferation.

RB exerts cell cycle control through direct interaction with transcriptional regulators of the G1 to S cell cycle phase transition, such as the E2F family members. RB silencing remodels the transcriptional control of cell cycle progression, alters the activity of downstream transcription factors, and may create unique requirements for small cell cancer proliferation.

The synthetic lethality observed between E2F3 inhibition and RB-loss highlights the distinct functional role of E2F3 in small cell cancers. While E2F family members can compensate for one another in certain cellular contexts, our findings suggest that E2F3 is uniquely associated with the small cell NE cancer state. E2F3 exists as multiple isoforms, including E2F3A and E2F3B, which differ in their regulatory mechanisms and expression patterns. E2F3A transcription is controlled in a cell cycle-dependent manner, being induced upon S-phase entry and is positively regulated by MYC. In contrast, E2F3B is constitutively expressed across cell cycle phases. Whether MYC regulates E2F3 expression or dependency in SCPC cells remains to be determined but given the prevalence of elevated MYC activity in small cell cancers, this represents a key area for further investigation.

A challenge in leveraging RB inactivation as a therapeutic vulnerability is the accurate identification of patients with functionally inactivated RB. While genomic profiling can detect RB mutations or deletions, these alterations alone do not reliably predict RB functionality that may be suppressed by posttranscriptional and posttranslational mechanisms. Elevated expression of Cyclin D1, Cyclin E, CDK4, or CDK6 can drive unchecked phosphorylation of RB, effectively disabling its tumor-suppressive function. In our studies, an RB-loss transcriptional signature emerged as a potential biomarker for E2F3 dependency across cancer models.

Directly Targeting E2F3 for Cancer Treatment.

SCLC and SCPC rank among the most aggressive and lethal malignancies. Most patients with small cell cancer that receive standard-of-care chemotherapy and radiation treatments are dead within 24 mo, and less than 10% are alive at 5 y. The bleak outcomes result from a complete lack of treatments that leverage knowledge of the genetic alterations driving the disease. FDA-approval of the bispecific T cell engager targeting DLL3, Immdelltra, has provided a new immune-based avenue for SCLC treatment, but therapies that leverage intrinsic vulnerabilities are urgently needed.

Recent work by Singh et al. demonstrates that loss of RB and G1/S checkpoint integrity in SCLC leads to altered E2F regulation, which, while beneficial for sustained proliferation, also creates a vulnerability that can be therapeutically exploited. New macrocyclic peptides blocking RxL-mediated cyclin–substrate interactions hyperactivate E2F and induce mitotic cell death, showing potent efficacy against RB-deficient and E2F-driven cancers. Together, our work and Singh et al. support a model in which E2F activity must fall within a narrow range to confer proliferative advantage.

Our data positions E2F3 inhibition as a promising therapeutic strategy RB-deficient small cell cancers. Supporting the potential tolerability of therapeutic E2F3 inhibition are observations from murine studies evaluating its function in development. Mice with inactivation of either E2F3A or E2F3B are each fully viable and have no detectable defects. While KO of E2F3A and E2F3B together is embryonic lethal, adult mice with conditional E2F3 deletion have no apparent phenotype and in these mice the tissues characterized by a high proliferation rate are unaffected. Reinforcing its importance as a therapeutic target, E2F3 has a central role in bladder cancer development via copy number gains that drive tumorigenesis.

E2F3 has proven challenging to block directly, with no reported inhibitors to date. This limitation necessitates the exploration of alternative therapeutic strategies, such as targeted protein degraders (including PROTACs), for modulating E2F3 activity. Selectively blocking E2F3 activity is a promising avenue for future therapy development.

Limitations of the Study.

The in vivo studies were designed to establish proof of concept that modulation of E2F3 activity can influence tumor growth within an intact tumor microenvironment, rather than to provide a comprehensive preclinical evaluation across multiple models. While only a limited number of tumor models were examined, the observed effects are consistent with phenotypes seen across multiple cell lines in vitro. Extension of these findings to additional in vivo models will be pursued in future investigations focused on therapeutic development and optimization. Future studies will also explore alternative approaches to inhibit E2F3 as the only approach employed by the current study is shRNA delivered by lentiviral transduction.

The conclusions regarding DHODH inhibition are informed by prior work demonstrating antitumor activity of DHODH inhibitors in SCLC and further supports targeting DHODH in this cancer. DHODH inhibition is effective against patient-derived xenograft models and in autochthonous genetically engineered mouse models of lung cancer initiated by combined loss of Trp53, RB, and p130. DHODH inhibitors have pleiotropic effects on tumor cell fitness, including depletion of pyrimidine nucleotide pools, induction of replication stress, and widespread transcriptional alterations. Although E2F3 protein downregulation emerged as a reproducible consequence of DHODH inhibition in our study, we do not propose that E2F3 suppression is the sole determinant of cytotoxicity. Rather, reduced E2F3 activity likely contributes to impaired cell fitness alongside additional metabolic and cell cycle stresses induced by DHODH blockade. Furthermore, the precise mechanisms linking pyrimidine metabolism to E2F transcription factor regulation remain incompletely understood and may involve altered transcriptional output, posttranslational modifications, or changes in protein stability. Notably, pyrimidine starvation has been linked to reduced MYC stability through impaired O-GlcNAcylation, and analogous metabolic control mechanisms may influence E2F family members. A link between DHODH inhibition and the modulation of transcriptional elongation of cancer-driving genes, as observed in melanoma, provides a potential mechanism for E2F3 control. Elucidating these regulatory pathways will be a focus of future studies.

Materials and Methods

Catalog information for all reagents is available in the SI Appendix accompanying this article.

Cell Culture.

Cell cultures were maintained between passages 3 and 20 at 37 °C in 5% CO2. All PARCB and SCLC cells were maintained in stem cell culture media (SCM) consisting of Advanced DMEM/F12, 1x Glutamax, 1× Pen/Strep, 1× B27 supplement, 10 ng/mL human EGF, and 10 ng/mL human FGF-basic. SCLC cells, PC3 cells, and ovarian cancer cell lines were maintained in RPMI-1640 with 10% FBS and 1× Glutamax.

The development of prostate PARCB SCPC cells has been previously described. The SCPC cell line NCI-H660 (CRL-5813) was purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC). SCLC cell lines, OVCAR5 ovarian cancer cells, and PC3 (CRL-1435) cells were acquired from a commercial vendor (ATCC, Millipore Sigma) or from collaborators. The OVCAR8 ovarian cancer cell line was kindly provided by the Division of Cancer Treatment and Diagnosis (DCTD) Tumor Repository at the NIH (NIH). The identity of cell lines obtained from collaborators was independently authenticated by STR analysis (Laragen, Culver City, CA). Cell cultures were routinely monitored for Mycoplasma contamination using a luminescence-based detection kit.

Genome-Wide CRISPR KO Screening.

Library preparation.

The GECKO gRNA libraries were obtained in a plasmid format (Addgene #1000000048) and were expanded following a published protocol. After maxi-prep purification, the two sublibraries were combined into a complete library. The final library was packaged into a lentiviral vector using Calcium-phosphate-based transfection in 293 T cells. The collected virus titer was determined based on puromycin resistance in PARCB lines.

Lentiviral infection and cell collection.

PARCB cell lines were cultured in SCM in an anchorage independent manner. For each cell line, 3 million cells were seeded per well of 12-well plate and in total 96 million cells (32 wells) were used per replicate. The cells were then infected and centrifuged at 1,000×g for 1 h with lentivirus containing the GECKO library at a MOI of 0.25. This targets an estimated overrepresentation of 100 cells/sgRNA. After infection, the cells were cultured overnight. The next day, all cells were resuspended in SCM with 1 µg/ml puromycin and cultured for 48 h. The cells were then collected, and live cells were isolated using Ficoll. 12 million live cells were collected initially. The remaining cells were cultured for another 14 d in SCM. After culturing, the cells were collected and the live cells were isolated using Ficoll. 12 million live cells were collected.

DNA preparation and library construction.

Genomic DNA from the initial and endpoint cells was isolated using the QIAamp DNA Blood Midi Kit (Qiagen, 51183). sgRNA libraries were constructed following a published protocol. In addition, the complete library plasmid was also used as a template to build a sequencing library to estimate the sgRNA distribution in the lentiviral vectors. The samples were sequenced using Hiseq 3000 or Novaseq.

Data processing and analysis.

To quantify sgRNA abundance from FASTQ files, we used the previously published count_spacers.py script developed by Dr. Feng Zhang’s group. All sgRNA count files were then merged by sgRNA sequence into a single data frame. The merged file was filtered to retain only protein-coding genes, annotated using Ensembl via the biomaRt package (v3.21). Low-abundance guides with fewer than one read per million in any starting material (ST) sample were removed. The filtered counts were subsequently analyzed using the MAGeCK (Model-based Analysis of Genome-wide CRISPR/Cas9 KO) pipeline (v0.5.9.5). Specifically, the mageck test command was run with the --paired parameter to compare sgRNA abundances between treatment (e.g., postselection) and control (e.g., initial timepoint) groups to compute gene-level enrichment or depletion scores. Triplicates from all three cell lines were included in this analysis. The resulting negative P-value and log2 fold change were used as readouts to determine whether a gene was essential in PARCB lines. Hits were defined using a nominal MAGeCK P-value < 0.05. We chose a P value rather than FDR-based cutoff because applying FDR < 0.05 substantially reduced recovery of established common essential genes in this dataset, suggesting an inflated false-negative rate. Full gene rankings including P values and FDR are provided for transparency and reuse.

DepMap Data Processing.

The CRISPR genetic dependency data of cancer lines and the list of common essential genes were downloaded from the Depmap database (Version: DepMap Public 25Q3 Primary Files, CRISPRGeneEffect.csv, Achilles_common_essentials.csv, Model.csv). Cell lines identified as SCLC by their OncotreeCode ID were extracted. An average gene effect per KO was calculated across all SCLC lines. A rank–rank plot was to compare trends SCLC DepMap data and the PARCB CRISPR screen. Genes identified as PARCB-essential were ranked by average gene effect in SCLC and -log10(P value) in the PARCB screen. Ggplot2’s stat_density_2d (V3.5.1; RRID:SCR_014601) was used to highlight regions of high and low overlap in the rank–rank plot.

Cell Proliferation Analysis.

Cells were seeded at 1 × 103 cells per well in 100 µL of media in opaque 96-well plates. At indicated timepoints, 100 µL of 3D Cell Titer Glo reagent [diluted 1:5 in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS)] was added to each well, plates were incubated at room temperature for 5 min, and luminescence was measured using a microplate luminescence reader. For experiments testing shRNA effects, cells were plated immediately after flow cytometry sorting for expression of linked selectable markers. Lentivirus Generation and Transduction.

Lentiviral particles were produced by transfecting T-antigen-positive Human Embryonic Kidney 293 cells (HEK293T) with transfer and packaging plasmids using a standard calcium phosphate DNA transfection method. 6 h following transfection, cells were replenished with fresh culture medium and incubated for 48 h at 37 °C. Lentivirus-containing supernatant was then collected, filtered, and ultracentrifuged to concentrate viral particles. Concentrated virus was stored at −80 °C. Viral titers were determined by HEK239T cell transduction and flow cytometry for fluorescence selectable marker expression. All target cell lines were transduced with lentivirus at an MOI of 4. For transductions, target cells were centrifuged with lentivirus at 1,000×g for 90 min at 25 °C.

Tumor Model.

PC3 parental and PC3 RB1 KO cells were transduced with lentivirus for the stable expression of E2F3A/B shRNA or control shRNA. For E2F3A/B knockdown cells were transduced with a combination of lentiviruses encoding shE2F3A/B and shE2F3A#1. 3 d after transduction, FACS was performed to isolate selectable marker positive cells. 3 d after sorting, cells were collected and resuspended in a 1:1 mixture of PBS: Matrigel at a concentration of 5 × 107 cells/mL. 100 µL of cell suspension (corresponding to 5 × 106 cells) was inoculated into the flanks of 6 to 12-wk-old male NSG (NOD-scid IL2Rgammanull) mice. A single inoculation was performed for each mouse. Tumor growth was monitored by digital caliper measurement of animals anesthetized by isoflurane. Tumor volumes were calculated using the formula: volume = (length × (width)2)/2. Two independent experiments were performed using independently transduced and sorted cells.

Flow Cytometry.

All flow cytometry data were acquired on a BD FACS Canto and analyzed using FlowJo software. Cell sorting was performed using a BD FACS Aria.

PI cell cycle analysis.

Cells were collected, centrifuged at 500×g for 4 min at 4 °C, washed twice with PBS and suspended in 500 µL of cell cycle staining solution (100 mg/mL PI, 20 mg/mL ribonuclease A, 1 mg/mL sodium citrate, 0.3% Triton-X 100 in dH2O).

EdU pulse.

Cells were pulsed with 10 µM EdU for 4 h before collection. Isolated cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and permeabilized using saponin perm/wash reagent. Cells were then stained with azide-Alexa Fluor 488 by Click reaction according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Cells were subsequently washed twice with PBS and stained with 0.5 mL of SyTOX 7-AAD and Ribonuclease A in saponin perm/wash reagent before analysis.

Immunoblot Analysis.

Immunoblot analysis was performed as previously described. PBS-washed cell pellets were resuspended in cold RIPA buffer supplemented with a protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail. Sample protein content was determined using the BCA assay. Protein lysates were normalized by RIPA and Laemmli loading dye dilution, resolved on 4% to 12% Bis-Tris gels and electrotransferred to nitrocellulose membranes. After blocking with 5% nonfat milk in tris-buffered saline (TBS) + 0.1% tween-20 (TBS-T) for 30 min at room temperature, membranes were incubated overnight in primary antibodies diluted 1:1,000 in 5% BSA in TBS-T at 4 °C. Membranes were washed with TBS-T and incubated with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-linked secondary antibodies prepared at a 1:2500 dilution in 5% nonfat dry milk/TBS-T for 1 h at room temperature. HRP was activated by incubating membranes with a mixture of enhanced chemiluminescence reagents. Immunoblot images were acquired using the iBright system (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

For the experiments involving ovarian cancer models (Fig. 3G and SI Appendix, Fig. S2G) a modified immunoblot protocol was used with the following specifications: PBS + 0.1% tween-20 (PBS-T) was used for wash steps and reagent dilutions, blocking was performed for 1 h, immunoblots were probed with GAPDH antibody from Cell Signaling Technology, secondary antibodies were diluted at 1:10,000, HRP signals were developed using the Supersignal West Alto Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and immunoblots were imaged using the ChemiDoc Imaging System (BioRad). Antibody clone and catalog information is available in the SI Appendix.

RNAseq.

RNA from logarithmic growth phase parental and RB KO PC3 cells was collected using the Qiagen miRNeasy Mini Kit. Quality check was performed on the Agilent 4200 Tapestation. Sequencing was performed by the UCLA Technology Center for Genomics and Bioinformatics (TCGB) using the Novaseq X Plus 10B (1,250 M/lane) platform.

Processing of Short-Read RNA-seq Data from SCLC Cell Lines and Patients for Isoform Analysis.

We obtained short-read RNA-seq data for 10 SCLC cell lines (DMS79, NCI-H1105, NCI-H2081, NCI-H209, NCI-H2227, NCI-H2171, NCI-H446, NCI-H524, NCI-H82, and NCI-H211) from the CCLE (study accession: PRJNA523380), and short-read RNA-seq data from six SCLC patients from Liu et al. (PMID: 34489925; accession: GSE87410). Reads were aligned to the GRCh38 human genome using STAR (v2.7.10b) with the parameter “--twopassMode Basic.” Transcripts were quantified with Kallisto (v0.50.1) with default parameters, and splicing events were quantified with rMATS-turbo (v4.2.0) with default parameters. Additionally, normalized junction expression matrices for NE prostate cancer (NEPC), castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC), related tumor types in TCGA, and GTEx normal tissues were obtained from the IRIS_data.v2.0.0 resource. When calculating the percentage of reads mapped to the E2F3A-specific junction, we excluded samples in which the combined read count for the target junction (E2F3A-specific) and the reference junction (E2F3B-specific) was fewer than 10, to ensure the reliability and robustness of the results. The plotted GTEx tissue types contained at least 10 samples meeting the threshold of combined splice junction count of ≥10.

Quantification and Statistical Analysis.

Data are presented as mean ± SD with number of biological replicates indicated in figure legends. Comparisons of two groups were evaluated using the unpaired t-test and P values less than 0.05 were considered significant. Comparisons of more than two groups were evaluated using one-way ANOVA followed by Bonferroni’s multiple comparison test and P values less than 0.05/m, where m is the total number of possible comparisons, were considered significant.
