# Phase I/II clinical trial of a melanoma vaccine targeting shared non-mutated antigens and a shared mutated BRAF neoantigen with an agonistic CD40 antibody (CDX-1140) plus TLR3 agonist (poly-ICLC).

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: For patients with high-risk melanoma who are unresponsive or intolerant to immune checkpoint inhibitors, cancer vaccines may provide benefit with a favorable toxicity profile. CD4+ T cells provide essential help to dendritic cells (DCs) for optimal CD8+ T cell priming in the antitumor response, and induction of tumor-cognate CD4+ T cell responses may enhance vaccine efficacy. We report on a first-in-human approach to treat patients with high-risk melanoma using a vaccine composed of six non-mutated melanoma-specific helper peptides (6MHP) and a shared mutated BRAF-V600E neoantigen helper peptide (mBRAF) co-administered locally with a TLR3 agonist (poly-ICLC) and agonistic CD40 antibody (CDX-1140).

**METHODS**: Adults with high-risk melanoma arising from cutaneous, mucosal, or ocular primary sites who were rendered clinically free of disease after definitive treatment were enrolled to this nonrandomized phase I/II trial (NCT04364230) designed to assess safety and immunogenicity. Participants received vaccine (6MHP+mBRAF+poly-ICLC) with a dose-escalation allocation of CDX-1140 into the vaccine mixture at one of four dose levels (50, 200, 800, 3000 μg). Vaccines were administered at 3-week intervals for four doses. Primary endpoints were safety and peripheral CD4+ T cell response. Exploratory analysis to characterize the vaccine site microenvironment was performed.

**RESULTS**: Of 22 eligible participants, 11 (50%) had ocular melanoma. Sixteen (73%) received the maximum CDX-1140 dose. Toxicities were limited to grade 1 or 2 treatment-related adverse events, with no dose-limiting toxicities reported. Peripheral CD4+ T cell responses to 6MHP were found ex vivo in six (27%, 95% CI 11% to 50%), including four with the maximum CDX-1140 dose. One had a durable and persistent T cell response to week 25. T cell responses to mBRAF did not meet criteria for positivity ex vivo, but one participant had a durable response after in vitro stimulation, with expansion of multifunctional Th1-polarized CD4+ T cells. Favorable immune-related changes were observed at the vaccine site, including CDX-1140-mediated increases in mature (DC-LAMP+) DCs.

**CONCLUSIONS**: The vaccine was safe, well-tolerated, and immunogenic. Optimization of vaccines that include agonistic CD40 antibody is needed to enhance immunogenicity. Induction of a multifunctional CD4+ T cell response to mBRAF supports targeting shared mutated neoantigens with melanoma vaccines.

**TRIAL REGISTRATION NUMBER**: NCT04364230.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

There is an unmet need to extend the immunotherapeutic options available to patients with high-risk melanoma who are unresponsive or intolerant to immune checkpoint inhibitors. Cancer vaccines have been shown to induce tumor-specific T cell responses with associated clinical benefit in some patients, and further optimization holds promise for providing a low toxicity immunotherapeutic option to patients, particularly those with earlier stage high-risk melanoma.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This trial tested the safety and immunogenicity of a peptide vaccine composed of six melanoma-associated antigens and a shared mutated BRAFV600E neoantigen designed to stimulate CD4+ T cells, co-administered with a local adjuvant combination of TLR3 agonist and agonistic CD40 antibody, representing a first-in-humans approach to treating patients with high-risk melanoma, including ocular disease. The vaccine was safe and immunogenic, with evidence of a Th1-polarized CD4+ T cell response to the shared mutated BRAFV600E neoantigen.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

This trial builds on prior work to improve the efficacy of vaccines through identification of relevant antigen targets and adjuvants to enhance immunogenicity. This study highlights the need for further optimization of the use of agonistic CD40 antibody as a local vaccine adjuvant and supports further investigation into targeting shared mutated neoantigens with melanoma vaccines.

Introduction

 Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) can provide durable clinical benefit to patients with high-risk and advanced melanoma; however, some are unresponsive or intolerant to ICIs. For patients with earlier stage (IIB-IIC) cutaneous melanoma, the only approved systemic adjuvant treatment is anti-PD-1 therapy. Yet, some patients do not pursue ICI therapy due to toxicity risks. ICIs also have limited efficacy in treating ocular melanoma, and for these patients, there are no approved adjuvant systemic therapies. Cancer vaccines have a favorable safety profile and offer promise to address the unmet need of extending immunotherapeutic approaches to treating melanoma.

We previously found that a vaccine composed of six tumor-associated class II MHC-restricted antigens (6 melanoma helper peptides; 6MHP) induced CD4+ T cell responses with evidence of clinical activity in patients with advanced melanoma, including when combined with a vaccine targeting CD8+ T cells or anti-PD1 therapy. In patients with high-risk melanoma treated in the adjuvant setting, the combination of 6MHP with a vaccine targeting CD8+ T cells was associated with durable long-term survival compared with those vaccinated without 6MHP. Prior work with vaccines targeting driver mutations in solid tumors has demonstrated immunogenicity of shared neoantigens arising from point mutations, with evidence of durable antitumor immunity. BRAF mutations are common in cutaneous melanoma and represent an appealing target for melanoma vaccines. A peptide spanning the V600E mutation has been reported to be an epitope for CD4+ T cells, restricted by several class II MHC molecules; however, to our knowledge, the immunogenicity of this mutant BRAF peptide has not been tested in humans.

In addition to identifying relevant antigen targets, a major focus of cancer vaccine development is optimizing adjuvants to enhance T cell priming. Common vaccine adjuvants include incomplete Freund’s adjuvant (IFA), which provides a locally inflamed antigen depot at the injection site, and Toll-like receptor (TLR) agonists, which induce an innate immune response at the vaccine site. Targeting co-stimulatory molecules expressed on antigen-presenting cells (APCs), such as CD40, to activate T cells is an attractive approach to enhancing vaccine immunogenicity. Preclinical models have demonstrated superior immune response rates to cancer vaccines with the combination of a TLR agonist and agonistic CD40 Ab. Several agonistic CD40 Abs with variable activation and binding characteristics have been tested systemically in early clinical trials with favorable results in some patients treated in combination with immune checkpoint blockade for melanoma or chemotherapy for pancreatic adenocarcinoma. Agonistic CD40 Ab as a local vaccine adjuvant has not been previously tested in humans. CDX-1140 (Celldex Therapeutics) is a fully human IgG2 monoclonal Ab targeting CD40, which does not require Fc crosslinking for agonist activity and does not block the CD40L binding site. Enhancing APC activation at the vaccine site through potential synergistic effects of local CDX-1140 with CD40L-expressing helper T cells may improve vaccine immunogenicity.

We report the results of a nonrandomized, phase I/II trial (NCT04364230) that represented a first-in-human approach to treating patients with high-risk melanoma in the adjuvant setting using a vaccine composed of 6MHP and a class II MHC-restricted shared mutated BRAF-V600E neoantigen peptide (mBRAF), co-administered locally with a TLR3 agonist (poly-ICLC) and agonistic CD40 Ab (CDX-1140). Primary endpoints were vaccine safety and immunogenicity, including dedicated assessment of the mBRAF peptide separate from the 6MHP pool. We hypothesized that the vaccine would be safe and would induce durable CD4+ T cell responses.

Methods

Participants

Patients at least 18 years of age were eligible for enrollment at two academic centers if they had newly diagnosed or recurrent high-risk melanoma and were rendered clinically free of disease within 6 months prior to enrollment. High-risk melanoma was defined as American Joint Committee on Cancer eighth edition stage IB ulcerated cutaneous melanoma or stage II-IV melanoma arising from cutaneous, mucosal, ocular, or unknown primary sites. A BRAFV600E mutation was not required for eligibility. Exclusion criteria included ICI therapy within 12 weeks of registration or chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, or immunomodulatory medications within 4 weeks of registration. Complete eligibility criteria are provided in the protocol (online supplemental file 1).

Study design

The first participant was enrolled in April 2021 and treated with six vaccines on the original protocol (online supplemental figure S1). Due to slow enrollment, the protocol was revised on November 12, 2021 to reduce the extent of interventions, after which the remainder of participants was enrolled through September 2023 to meet the revised enrollment target (figure 1A). These participants received four vaccines administered at the same skin site every 3 weeks and underwent vaccine site punch biopsies at day 50. Blood was collected for immune analyses prior to each vaccine and during follow-up through day 176.

The vaccine was composed of seven synthetic intermediate-length peptides for epitopes recognized by CD4+ T cells (online supplemental table S1). Peptides in 6MHP are derived from melanoma-associated antigens. The mBRAF peptide is BRAF-V600E585-614, modified slightly from the previously reported MHC II-restricted epitope. Vaccines were prepared as an aqueous mixture of 200 μg of each peptide in 6MHP, 300 μg of mBRAF, and 0.9 mg of poly-ICLC (Hiltonol, Oncovir) with a dose escalation allocation of CDX-1140 at one of four dose levels to permit safety assessment of local administration. Dose escalation for CDX-1140 started at 50 μg, a dose shown to be safe and effective in murine studies, and increased to a maximum dose of 3000 μg, a dose ≤4% of the maximum tolerated systemic dose of 1.5 mg/kg based on minimum weight for inclusion in this study. All vaccines were injected half-subcutaneously and half-intradermally. Details on vaccine preparation and CDX-1140 dose allocation are in the protocol (online supplemental file 1 and online supplemental methods).

Objectives

Primary endpoints were vaccine safety and immunogenicity defined as peripheral CD4+ T cell responses to vaccine peptides. Secondary endpoints were to determine whether the vaccine induced Th1-polarized CD4+ T cell responses and to evaluate for changes in regulatory T cells (Tregs) in circulation and in the vaccine site microenvironment (VSME). Exploratory endpoints included evaluation of IgG Ab responses, immune-related changes at the vaccine site, and clinical outcomes.

Safety assessment

Adverse events (AEs) were evaluated by the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events V.5.0. AEs considered definitely, probably, or possibly related to treatment were considered treatment-related AEs (TRAEs); those that were defined as dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs) are listed in online supplemental methods and associated stopping rules are described in the protocol (online supplemental file 1). Acceptable toxicity was a DLT rate ≤30%.

Assessment of T cell response

Peripheral T cell responses were measured by direct and in vitro stimulated (IVS) IFNγ ELISpot assays, as described. To estimate the frequency of CD4+ T cell responses ex vivo, data were normalized to the proportion of circulating CD3+CD4+ cells in each sample by flow cytometry, as previously reported. A response ex vivo required a minimum of 20 spots/100 000 CD4+ T cells over the maximum negative control without overlapping SD and at least a twofold increase over the maximum negative control and any prevaccine reactivity. A higher threshold of 100 spots/100 000 PBMC was required for a response on the IVS assay. ELISpot data from IVS assays were not normalized by the fraction of live CD3+CD4+ cells in the stimulated PBMC by flow cytometry due to limited sample available for these assays. T cell responses to the mBRAF peptide were evaluated separately from the 6MHP pool. Persistent and durable responses were also assessed. Details are provided in online supplemental methods.

Assessment of mBRAF-reactive T cell phenotype

To determine whether the vaccine induced Th1-polarized responses to mBRAF, peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were evaluated by flow cytometry after surface and intracellular cytokine staining. The proportions of CD4+ T cell subsets were reported as the mean of two technical replicates. Details are provided in online supplemental methods.

Assessment of changes in circulating Tregs

To measure changes in circulating Tregs over time, PBMCs were evaluated by flow cytometry to determine the proportion of Tregs among CD4+ T cells in each sample. Tregs were defined as CD3+CD4+CD25hiCD127lo/-FoxP3+ (online supplemental figure S2). Details are provided in online supplemental methods.

Assessment of Ab response

Ab responses to the vaccine peptides were evaluated by ELISA, as described. Results were reported as the mean IgG titer of two assays. A response required a titer ≥100, as previously defined, and at least fourfold higher than the titers for the negative control peptide and baseline reactivity on both assays. Details are provided in online supplemental methods.

Assessment of the VSME

To investigate immune-related changes in the VSME, vaccine site biopsies were evaluated by Nanostring targeted gene expression profiling and multiplex immunofluorescence histology (mIFH). Skin controls were used from banked normal skin samples obtained from participants with melanoma enrolled on prior clinical trials. Details are provided in online supplemental methods.

Evaluation of clinical outcomes

Participants were followed for clinical outcomes, with the frequency of clinical follow-up and surveillance imaging determined by the treating oncologist according to standard of care. Overall survival (OS) was defined as the time from enrollment to date of death from any cause, with living participants censored at last date known alive. Disease-free survival (DFS) was defined as the time from enrollment to first disease recurrence, including new primaries, or death from any cause. Living participants without disease recurrence were censored at the date of last known disease status.

Statistical analysis

Immune response rates were reported as point estimates with 95% CI. Changes in circulating Tregs over time were evaluated by repeated measures modeling in SAS (V.9.4) using a heterogeneous autoregressive one covariance matrix on log transformed data and F-tests based on contrasts to assess specified comparisons, with p<0.05 considered statistically significant. Differential gene expression analysis of vaccine site biopsies was conducted within the ROSALIND platform (ROSALIND, Inc, San Diego, CA), with differentially expressed genes (DEGs) defined as |fold-change|≥2 and adjusted-p<0.05 by the Benjamini-Hochberg method. Data were exported for generating visualizations in R (V.4.5.0). Details of bioinformatics analyses are described in the online supplemental methods. Comparisons of immune cell subsets quantified by mIFH were performed using two-sided Mann-Whitney tests employing the exact null distribution for obtaining p values using the coin R package (V.1.4-3). The Benjamini-Hochberg method was used to adjust p values for multiple comparisons, with an adjusted p<0.05 considered statistically significant. OS and DFS were estimated for the intention-to-treat population by the Kaplan-Meier method. Survival analysis and generation of Kaplan-Meier curves were performed using the survival (V.3.8-3) and ggsurvfit (V.1.1.0) R packages.

Results

22 participants were enrolled and treated (figure 1B). The participant who enrolled on the original protocol (01-001) was treated at the lowest CDX-1140 dose allocation (D1). All participants received one or more vaccinations, and thus were considered evaluable for the primary endpoints of safety and immunogenicity. Most (18/22, 82%) completed all treatment. Reasons for early discontinuation of study treatment included disease relapse (2/22, 9%), participant refusal due to nausea (1/22, 5%), and physician decision due to participant inability to adhere to study visit schedule (1/22, 5%).

Baseline characteristics by CDX-1140 dose allocation are shown in table 1. Median (range) age was 64 (36-80) years and 13 (59%) were male. 11 (50%) had ocular melanoma. Two (9%) received prior systemic therapy: one with ocular melanoma treated with a shared antigen vaccine targeting CD8+ T cells and bevacizumab, and one with BRAF-mutated cutaneous melanoma treated with ipilimumab/nivolumab and dabrafenib/trametinib. Of the participants with primary cutaneous melanoma, BRAF mutation status was known for eight (80%), with BRAFV600E mutations identified in three participants.

Safety

All participants were evaluated for safety while on active treatment and at all study follow-up visits, even if treatment was discontinued early. No DLTs were observed. All TRAEs were either grade 1 or 2 (online supplemental table S2). The most common TRAEs were injection site reactions (100%), fatigue (73%), fever (55%), chills (50%), and myalgias (36%). No participants experienced ulceration at the injection site. Among the other most common TRAEs, incidence was not limited to the highest CDX-1140 dose and was generally transient. No deaths occurred during active treatment. All AEs are summarized in online supplemental table S3.

T cell response

All participants were evaluated for peripheral CD4+ T cell response by direct (ex vivo) IFNγ ELISpot. Half were also evaluated by IVS IFNγ ELISpot. One participant (01-008) was missing adequate sample for baseline flow cytometry, so the proportion of CD4+ T cells in the week 3 sample was used for baseline normalization. Another participant (02-004) was missing a baseline sample for both ELISpot and flow cytometry, so the week 3 sample was assigned as baseline. All participants, including those who discontinued treatment early, had at least one evaluable post-vaccination PBMC sample.

Six participants (6/22, 27%, 95% CI 11% to 50%) had a CD4+ T cell response to the 6MHP pool ex vivo, with five (5/6, 83%, 95% CI 36% to 100%) identified at week 7 (figure 2A). Of the participants who received the maximum CDX-1140 dose, four (4/16, 25%, 95% CI 7% to 52%) had a CD4+ T cell response to 6MHP. Overall, one participant (1/22, 5%, 95% CI 0 to 23%) had a durable response over two consecutive time points and persistent response to day 176. Representative ELISpot results are shown in online supplemental figure S3. CD4+:CD4neg ratios are shown in online supplemental figure S4.

None had a CD4+ T cell response to mBRAF that met positivity criteria ex vivo. 11 participants with a suggestion of reactivity to mBRAF over the negative controls ex vivo were evaluated by IVS ELIspot. One (1/11, 9%, 95% CI 0% to 41%) had a durable response to mBRAF (figure 2B). Five (5/11, 45%, 95% CI 17% to 77%) had a response to 6MHP, including the participant with the stimulated response to mBRAF who also did not have a response to 6MHP ex vivo (online supplemental figure S5).

Ab response

We previously found that IgG Ab responses to 6MHP were associated with both T cell response and improved clinical outcome in patients with advanced melanoma vaccinated with an IFA-based formulation. In the present study, all participants were evaluable for Ab responses to the vaccine peptides, with at least one postvaccination serum sample. For two participants (02-003; 02-004) missing a baseline serum sample, the earliest time point evaluable was assigned as baseline.

Five participants (5/22, 23%, 95% CI 8% to 45%) had an Ab response to the 6MHP pool, with the same number having an Ab response to mBRAF, including four with Ab responses to both (figure 2C,D). Among the participants with Ab responses to 6MHP, four (4/5, 80%) had responses at multiple time points, including three with a maximum titer at the last evaluable time point (figure 2C). Ab responses to mBRAF were transient. Among the participants with Ab responses to mBRAF, one had a response at two time points (figure 2D). Results for participants without Ab responses to the vaccine peptides are shown in online supplemental figure S6.

Ab responses did not correlate with T cell responses. Among the participants with an Ab response to 6MHP, two (2/5, 40%) had a T cell response to 6MHP ex vivo. The participants with Ab responses to mBRAF did not include the one participant with a stimulated T cell response to mBRAF. T cell response rates were balanced between the lower and maximum CDX-1140 dose levels, but a greater percentage of participants vaccinated with the lower CDX-1140 doses had Ab responses. Of those with Ab responses to 6MHP and mBRAF, four (4/5, 80%) and three (3/5, 60%), respectively, were vaccinated with the lower CDX-1140 doses.

Changes in circulating Tregs

To assess for the possibility that helper peptide vaccination may induce Treg expansion, PBMCs were evaluated by flow cytometry from baseline through week 25 for changes in circulating Tregs. Low viability samples (<70%) were excluded from the analysis. Participants without a baseline sample, due to either being missing or low viability, were excluded entirely from the analysis (n=6), including two participants who discontinued treatment early. Of the 16 participants included in the analysis, 13 had evaluable samples at all time points; one participant was missing the final week 25 sample, and two who discontinued treatment early only had samples through weeks 6 and 9. No significant difference was observed in circulating Tregs as a percentage of total CD4+ T cells over time by repeated measures modeling (online supplemental figure S7).

Characterization of the T cell response to mBRAF

We previously found that vaccination with 6MHP in IFA induces CD4+ T cell responses with Th1 polarization. To our knowledge, the shared mutated neoantigen, mBRAF, has not been previously tested in a vaccine in humans. We characterized the function of mBRAF-reactive T cells by flow cytometry using PBMC expanded through IVS with mBRAF from the participant (01-011) with a T cell response to mBRAF on IVS ELISpot.

First, we assessed whether this stimulated T cell response was primarily mediated by CD4+ T cells. After gating on live CD3+ cells, CD8+ and CD4+ T cells were identified (online supplemental figure S8). Consistent with the original report of the BRAFV600E epitope being recognized by CD4+ T cells cultured from melanoma tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes, CD4+ T cells were the dominant reactive T cell population expanding on stimulation with the 30-mer mBRAF585-614 peptide (online supplemental figure S8). We then evaluated for antigen-induced function of these CD4+ T cells by flow cytometry by staining for intracellular IFNγ, TNFα, IL-2, granzyme B, and Ki67 and surface CD69. Granzyme B, Ki67, and CD69 were identified in a high percentage of CD4+ T cells among the IVS control CD4+ T cells, without evident increases in the mBRAF-stimulated CD4+ T cells (online supplemental table S4 and figure S9). However, specific staining for Th1 cytokines (IFNγ, TNFα, and IL-2) was observed in the mBRAF-stimulated CD4+ T cells (figure 3; online supplemental table S4). The mean proportions of CD4+ T cells producing IFNγ, TNFα, and IL-2 after mBRAF re-stimulation were 24.6%, 37.2%, and 22.2%, respectively. Low-level cross-reactivity with wtBRAF peptide was observed. The mean proportions of mBRAF-reactive CD4+ T cells producing both IFNγ and TNFα, IL-2 and TNFα, and IL-2 and IFNγ were 22.6%, 22.8%, and 18.9%, respectively. The mean proportion of mBRAF-reactive CD4+T cells producing three Th1 cytokines was 15.0%. This CD4+ T cell population producing multiple Th1 cytokines is depicted by the UMAP generated from the live CD3+ cell population from both replicates (online supplemental figure S10). These results suggest that vaccination induced a Th1-polarized CD4+ T cell response to mBRAF, where most of the responding cells produced 2–3 Th1 cytokines.

Vaccine site microenvironment

To investigate immune-related changes in the VSME, vaccine site biopsies were evaluated by Nanostring targeted gene expression profiling and mIFH. Two participants (01-002; 01-016) did not undergo vaccine site biopsies due to early discontinuation of treatment prior to day 50. The sample from another participant (01-009) was evaluated by mIFH only. For participant 01-011 enrolled on the original protocol, the week 3 biopsy was used.

19 vaccine site biopsies and three normal skin controls met data quality control metrics for differential gene expression analysis. Of the 730 immune-related genes included in the Nanostring PanCancer Immune panel, 368 DEGs were identified in the VSME compared with normal skin, including 305 upregulated and 63 downregulated genes (figure 4A). Significant changes in gene expression suggested an overall proinflammatory environment with increased T cell recruitment and infiltration, activation, and cytotoxicity in the VSME compared with normal skin (figure 4A). These changes were reflected on pathway analysis with significant enrichment of the MSigDB Hallmark inflammatory response and allograft rejection pathways (online supplemental table S5). We found significant changes in the gene expression levels of T cell transcription factors in the VSME suggestive of a Type 1 (Tc1/Th1) T cell response (figure 4B). There were no significant differences in FOXP3 expression in the VSME compared with normal skin. There was significant upregulation in expression of genes associated with increased APC infiltration and activation in the VSME, including the dendritic cell (DC) maturation marker LAMP3 (DC-LAMP) (figure 4C).

To investigate local effects of CDX-1140, we performed a post-hoc comparison of the VSME from participants who received the maximum CDX-1140 dose compared with those who received the lower doses (figure 4D,E). We found significant upregulation of DEGs suggestive of CD40-mediated APC activation after vaccination with the maximum CDX-1140 dose compared with lower dose levels. No significant difference in CD70 expression was found by CDX-1140 dose, which may reflect decreased expression on CD40-activated DCs on engagement with CD27 on T cells. The VSME from participants who received the maximum CDX-1140 dose also had significantly increased IL12B expression, consistent with CD40 stimulation to support a Th1 CD4+ T cell response. These changes may have been primarily driven by CD40-mediated activation of DCs and B cells at the vaccine site, given the significantly increased expression of LAMP3 and MS4A1 (CD20) at the maximum CDX-1140 dose. While CD40 is also expressed by macrophages, no significant difference in CD14 expression was found by CDX-1140 dose. Overall, these differences in gene expression were reflected on pathway analysis with significant enrichment of TNF signaling pathways, supporting CDX-1140-mediated effects at the vaccine site (online supplemental table S6).

Vaccine site biopsies were also evaluated by mIFH to quantify protein expression of lymphocyte and DC markers (online supplemental table S7). Twenty vaccine site biopsies and 13 normal skin controls met staining and imaging quality control metrics. Results were consistent with the gene expression profiling. We found increased lymphocyte infiltrates in the VSME compared with normal skin (online supplemental table S8), including significantly increased B cell densities and proportions of proliferating CD8+ and CD4+ T cells (figure 5A,B). We also found significantly increased CD208+ area staining in the VSME compared with normal skin, suggestive of increased numbers of mature DCs at the vaccine site (figure 5C,D). No significant difference in the proportion of CD70+ mature DCs was observed in the VSME compared with normal skin (online supplemental table S8). However, we observed diffuse CD70+ staining in the epidermis of skin controls, suggesting non-specific binding of the Ab that limited interpretation of this data (online supplemental figure S11A). Evaluation of mature conventional DC (cDC) subsets was performed by assessing colocalization of CD208+ area with CD141+ and CD1c+ for cDC1 and cDC2 subsets, respectively. Compared with normal skin, the VSME had a significant decrease in the proportion of CD208+ area represented by the cDC2 subset, with no difference in the proportion of CD208+ area represented by the cDC1 subset (figure 5C). We observed a trend to an increased proportion of CCR7+ mature DCs in the VSME, suggesting possible enhanced migratory capacity of mature DCs induced by vaccination (figure 5C).

Given the CDX-1140 dose-related changes in LAMP3 expression, we also performed a post hoc comparison of mature DCs at the vaccine site by CDX-1140 dose, measured by CD208. We found a trend to increased CD208+ area in the VSME of participants who received the maximum CDX-1140 dose compared with the lower dose levels, suggesting that CDX-1140 increases the numbers of mature DCs at the vaccine site (figure 5E). CCR7 and MS4A1 gene expression also increased with CD40 Ab dose, but without significant differences in protein expression by mIFH (online supplemental table S9).

We evaluated for tertiary lymphoid structures (TLS), which are thought to promote lymphocyte recruitment, proliferation, and activation that support local priming of antigen-specific T cell and B cell responses. We have previously identified TLS in the VSME after IFA-based vaccination with 6MHP. In the present study, we identified well-organized B cell and T cell clusters with mature DCs suggestive of TLS formation in the VSME of four participants; however, minimal peripheral node addressin (PNAd+) area was observed in each (figure 5F and online supplemental figure S11C). Of these participants with possible TLS, three had immune responses (01-005; 01-008; 01-018).

Clinical outcomes

Median follow-up for clinical outcomes was 28 months as of May 2025. The 2-year and 3-year OS rates were 91% (95% CI 80% to 100%) and 73% (95% CI 46% to 100%), respectively (figure 6A). The 2-year and 3-year DFS rates were 59% (95% CI 41% to 84%) and 53% (95% CI 36% to 80%), respectively figure 6B. Outcomes for each participant are summarized in figure 6C. All participants had been rendered clinically free of disease prior to enrollment. 10 participants (10/22, 45%) had disease recurrence after enrollment, and three (3/22, 14%) died in follow-up, all of which were disease-related. Of the participants who died in follow-up, all had recurrent disease prior to enrollment, and two had either ocular or mucosal melanoma with failure of multiple therapies after on-study recurrence. Of participants who had recurrence, six (6/10, 60%) had stage III/IV disease prior to enrollment and six (6/10, 60%) had either ocular or mucosal melanoma. The study was not powered to make meaningful associations between immune response and clinical outcome, but of the 10 participants with T cell or Ab responses, eight remain alive at a median follow-up of 30.2 months, including three participants with ocular melanoma who had disease recurrence at a median of 5.9 months. Two participants with immune responses died of advanced melanoma, including the participant with a durable CD4+ T cell response (recurrence at 18.6 months). Of the 12 without T cell or Ab responses, five recurred at a median of 3.0 months, and one died of advanced melanoma at 14.0 months. Of the three participants with BRAFV600E-mutated cutaneous melanoma, two had Ab responses, but not T cell responses, to mBRAF and remain alive without evidence of disease.

Discussion

We report on a first-in-human approach to treating patients with high-risk melanoma in the adjuvant setting using a helper peptide vaccine coadministered locally with poly-ICLC and CDX-1140. To our knowledge, this is the first to report on the use of local agonistic CD40 Ab as a vaccine adjuvant. The vaccine was safe and well-tolerated. The vaccine induced T cell and Ab responses in a subset of participants. A novel component of this trial was the inclusion of a peptide (mBRAF) representing the driver mutation in cutaneous melanoma, BRAFV600E, and the data supported the induction of a multifunctional Th1 CD4+ T cell response to mBRAF. Favorable immune-related changes were observed at the vaccine site, including evidence of Tc1/Th1 polarization and CDX-1140 dose-mediated effects on the accumulation of mature DCs.

Our prior work with 6MHP vaccines emulsified in IFA, with or without poly-ICLC, has demonstrated safety overall, but some patients develop severe injection site reactions and DLTs. While all participants in this study developed injection site reactions, none were grade 3 or dose-limiting. Other common TRAEs were also observed across all CDX-1140 dose levels and were consistent with those reported in our prior trials. Overall, CDX-1140 as a local vaccine adjuvant has a favorable safety profile without evidence of synergistic toxicity with other vaccine components, including poly-ICLC.

The vaccine induced peripheral T cell responses in a subset of participants. The ex vivo T cell response rate to 6MHP in this trial (27%) was greater than the T cell response rates to cancer antigens in prior multipeptide vaccine trials using poly-ICLC or other TLR agonists alone (0%–18%). Thus, adding agonistic CD40 Ab to poly-ICLC shows promise compared with a TLR agonist alone. However, the immunogenicity of 6MHP with this adjuvant combination was lower than observed in our prior trials with IFA-based vaccination, with or without poly-ICLC (40%–60% T cell response rates ex vivo). In the present study, CD4+ T cell response rates to 6MHP were also transient, with only one durable and persistent response observed. CD4+ T cell responses to the mBRAF peptide were not detected ex vivo, and only one response was detected among a subset evaluated after IVS. These findings may reflect a less effective vaccine adjuvant combination compared with IFA-based preparations, or may guide future considerations to maximize the potency of local agonistic CD40 Ab. Specifically, the timing of CD40 activation of APCs relative to their exposure to peptides may require optimization, as mature DCs after CD40 stimulation may have limited uptake of new antigens. Vaccination before local CD40 Ab administration may improve T cell response rates. Also, preclinical models show that CD40 activation of DCs promotes effective CD8+ T cell activation independent of CD4+ T cells; thus, the impact of agonistic CD40 Ab as a local adjuvant may be greater for vaccines that include minimal epitopes for CD8+ T cells.

CDX-1140 does not block the CD40L binding site, allowing for CD40-CD40L interactions between APCs and CD4+ T cells to occur, particularly at low concentrations delivered systemically. It is possible that high local concentrations could result in partial blocking of the CD40L site. If true, such interaction may have limited the induction of CD4+ T cell responses. The local concentrations of CDX-1140 over time are not known, and more study is needed to understand effects of high local concentrations. We expect that it diffuses quickly; if so, the adjuvant effect may be enhanced by administering it in a depot for slow release or in a construct that anchors it to local tissue.

We detected, in one participant, a T cell response to mBRAF, which was predominantly mediated by CD4+ T cells, and those mBRAF-reactive T cells produced multiple Th1 cytokines. This finding is consistent with the Th1-polarized CD4+ T cell response observed after 6MHP vaccination. These data support immunogenicity of the mBRAF585-614 peptide for induction of a CD4+ T cell response, and in the one participant, a concurrent CD8+ T cell response was not observed. A peptide contained within this sequence has been reported as an epitope for CD8+ T cells, restricted by HLA-A2. We do not have HLA typing on the responding participant, but it is possible that vaccinating against mBRAF could induce CD8+ T cell responses in some patients. In prior work, we found that vaccinating with a 30-mer peptide encompassing a known minimal epitope for CD8+ T cells was less effective at inducing CD8+ T cell responses than the minimal epitope peptide itself; so, there may be an advantage of adding minimal epitopes for CD8+ T cells. Enhancing the vaccine adjuvant further may increase immunogenicity of mBRAF. We also detected IgG Ab responses to mBRAF in five participants, supporting immunogenicity of the peptide; however, the role of B cell activation and the humoral immune response in shaping antitumor immunity after therapeutic cancer vaccination has not been well characterized and warrants further study.

The changes in immune-related gene and protein expression detected in the VSME support enhanced APC activation after vaccination and suggest that CDX-1140 dose-mediated effects were achieved locally. Our results compare favorably to prior work investigating changes in markers associated with APC activation measured in circulation after systemic or intratumoral injection of agonistic CD40 Ab. Our data support a CDX-1140 dose-mediated increase in mature DCs in the VSME. This may reflect enhanced maturation of DCs on CD40 activation, though other possibilities are that CD40 agonism enhances recruitment or retention of mature DCs or promotes proliferation of existing mature DCs. We found no significant difference in mature cDC1s and a significant decrease in mature cDC2s in the VSME. This result may reflect a relative increase in maturation of other DC subsets not evaluated in the panel; however, it may reflect changes after helper peptide vaccination in the functional state of the cDC2 subset, which classically present antigen to CD4+ T cells. However, additional single-cell analyses are needed to better characterize these DCs. Additionally, we observed a trend to increased expression of CCR7 among mature DCs on mIFH, so it is possible that our results reflect changes in the DC compartment within the VSME due to migration of DCs to the vaccine-draining lymph nodes.

We observed lymphoid aggregates with mature DCs in the VSME that had features of TLS; however, minimal PNAd staining did not provide convincing evidence of high endothelial venules. Thus, these aggregates may represent early or immature TLS. Future studies may benefit from staining for markers of germinal center formation, such as CD21, CD23, and BCL6. On gene expression analysis, BCL6 was significantly downregulated, and the Ab responses detected in this trial were weak and transient compared with those detected after IFA-based vaccination. These findings may reflect the absence of B cell phenotypes in the VSME that enhance cell-mediated and humoral immune responses. CD40 is highly expressed on B cells, and thus our results support further investigation into the effects of local agonistic CD40 Ab on B cell activation.

Our study has several limitations. This trial was designed to evaluate safety and immunogenicity of this vaccine regimen. While the vaccine induced T cell and Ab responses to mBRAF, additional work is needed to further characterize its immunogenicity with other vaccine adjuvants. Furthermore, we had previously found evidence of epitope spreading, with induction of CD8+ T cell responses after 6MHP vaccination; however, we did not evaluate for epitope spreading in this trial. To our knowledge, epitopes for CD8+ T cells are not embedded within the peptides of 6MHP, thus we reported T cell responses after normalization for the CD4+ T cell fraction of PBMC; however, it is possible that a CD8+ T cell response could occur in some patients. Since this trial was not designed to evaluate for clinical outcomes, associations of immunologic endpoints with clinical outcomes were not assessed. Additionally, participant heterogeneity in primary disease site and stage limited interpretation of clinical outcomes. Finally, the favorable immune-related changes in the VSME were observed relative to normal skin; however, comparison of the VSME in this trial to that of other trials with different adjuvant combinations is needed. This study was not designed to test for differences by CDX-1140 dose, so the dose-related findings in the VSME require further investigation to confirm these results. Also, the evaluations of the VSME in this study were designed as exploratory endpoints based on small punch biopsies that did not allow for assessment of whether the observed CD4+ T cell infiltrates were reactive to antigens in the vaccine, as we have found for CD8+ T cells in a prior clinical trial.

In summary, this study provides support for the safety of agonistic CD40 Ab as a local vaccine adjuvant, though the results of our immunologic analyses highlight future considerations to optimize its use to enhance vaccine immunogenicity. The induction of a durable, multifunctional CD4+ T cell response to mBRAF supports further consideration of targeting shared mutated neoantigens with melanoma vaccines.
