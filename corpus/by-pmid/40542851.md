# Immunological synapse formation as a key mechanism in T cell-dependent bispecific antibody-mediated immune activation and cytotoxicity.

## Abstract

T cell-dependent bispecific antibodies (TDBs) are next-generation antibody therapies that link cancer cells and T cells to achieve potent antitumor effects. Despite the successful development of TDBs for hematological malignancies, their efficacy against solid tumors remains limited. Overcoming this challenge requires a deeper understanding of their mechanisms of action. While the basic process of immunological synapse (IS) formation and T cell activation by TDB is known, the detailed effects of IS on the bystander effect and T cell migration, both crucial for therapeutic efficacy, remain unclear. This study investigated these mechanisms using an EGFR/CD3 TDB (hEx3) and EGFR knockout cancer cells (KO). The results revealed that IS formation by TDB induced a bystander effect, leading to damage in surrounding KO, with the extent depending on the proportion of EGFR-positive wild-type cancer cells (WT) and the duration of co-culture. Furthermore, IS formation significantly enhanced T cell cytokine and chemokine secretion, promoting T cell migration. These findings provide critical insights into TDB efficacy mechanisms and highlight the importance of evaluating IS formation in developing new antibody drugs. Establishing a reliable system for assessing IS formation will be essential for advancing TDBs and other antibody-based therapies, particularly against solid tumors.

Introduction

Bispecific antibodies (BsAbs) are engineered antibodies capable of binding to two distinct targets, many of which have recently received FDA approval. Among them, T cell-dependent bispecific antibodies (TDBs) represent one of the most advanced approaches in cancer immunotherapy, as they are designed to enhance T cell-mediated cytotoxicity against tumor cells. TDBs typically target tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) in cancer cells while engaging CD3 receptors on T cells, effectively bridging the two cell types. Notably, TDBs can activate T cells independently of MHC–peptide complexes, thereby circumventing resistance mechanisms associated with losing MHC molecules in tumor cells. This unique feature positions TDBs as a promising therapeutic option for refractory and relapsed cancers resistant to conventional therapies.

A critical factor in the potent antitumor effects of TDBs is the formation of immunological synapse (IS), a specialized structure formed when T cells bind to cancer cells. IS formation involves the reorganization of the actin cytoskeleton and the clustering of receptors and signaling molecules, resulting in a supramolecular activation cluster (SMAC) with a distinct concentric three-layered structure. This structure facilitates strong cell–cell adhesion and triggers an intracellular tyrosine phosphorylation cascade, ultimately leading to robust T cell activation via transcription factors such as NF-κB. Beyond T cell activation, IS formation also enables the targeted delivery of cytolytic molecules, including perforins and granzymes, to tumor cells. While the basic mechanisms by which IS formation enhances TDB-mediated cytotoxicity and cytokine release, such as IFN-γ and TNF-α, are understood, several aspects remain incompletely characterized. Notably, some studies use TAA-low expressing cells as negative controls rather than completely antigen-negative cells, raising the possibility of minimal IS formation even in “control” settings. Antigen knockout cells have been employed in some studies to address this limitation; however, some studies often fail to comprehensively evaluate key factors critical for TDB efficacy, such as bystander effects and T cell migration.

To address these gaps, we investigated the impact of IS formation on T cell responses, bystander effects, and T cell migration using hEx3, a TDB targeting the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) on cancer cells and CD3 on T cells. By employing KO as an ideal negative control, we analyzed changes in cancer cell injury, cytokine release, bystander effects, and T cell migration in the presence and absence of IS formation. These findings aim to provide new insights into the mechanisms underlying TDB efficacy and inform the development of next-generation antibody-based therapies.

Material and methods

TDB

As TDB, we used hEx3-scDb-Fc(H237Y)-HL, a human IgG1 bispecific antibody that binds specifically to the EGFR of human cancer cells and CD3 of human T cells. This format is a stabilized mutant of the human Fc-fused hEx3 dual-specificity diabody.

Cell culture

AsPC-1 (pancreatic cancer cell line) was obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC, Manassas, VA, USA). Cancer cells were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute medium-1640 (RPMI-1640, FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemicals, Osaka, Japan) with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) and 1% penicillin, streptomycin, and amphotericin B (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemicals) at 37 °C under 5% CO2 conditions. Human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs, Cellular Technology Limited, Cleveland, OH) were cultured in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin–streptomycin–amphoterin B under conditions of 100 IU/mL human interleukin 2 (IL-2, Shionogi, Osaka, Japan) and 10 µg/mL anti-CD3 antibody OKT3 (Tonbo Biosciences, San Diego, CA) to expand human T cells.

Establishment of KO by CRISPR-Cas9

AsPC-1, EGFR-positive wild-type cancer cells (WT) (5.0 × 106 cells/500 µL/well) were cultured overnight in 24-well plates (Corning, New York, NY). After washing with PBS, 500 μL of culture medium containing 8 µg/mL Polybrene solution (Nacalai Tesque, Kyoto, Japan) was added to each well. Cells were then infected with 20 μL of lentivirus particles (EGFR sgRNA CRISPR All-in-One Lentivirus set, Applied Biological Materials Inc., Richmond, Canada) at 37 ℃ and incubated for 16 h. The cells were then replaced with 1 mL of culture medium, selected with 1–10 µg/mL of puromycin (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemicals), and subjected to limiting dilutions using 96-well plates to obtain clones of interest. KO clones were confirmed by genomic DNA sequencing, flow cytometry, and Western blotting.

Flow cytometry

To analyze the cell-binding activities of TDB, flow cytometry was employed. Each set of target cells (WT, KO, or T cells) was collected at 2.0 × 105 cells/200 µL and incubated with TDB at a concentration of 1 µg/mL in DPBS (Thermo Fisher Scientific) containing 0.1% BSA and 2 mM EDTA (B.E. PBS) for 30 min. Following incubation, cells were washed with B.E. PBS, and an Alexa Fluor 488-conjugated anti-human IgG antibody (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #A11013) in B.E. PBS was introduced as a secondary antibody. After another round of washing with B.E. PBS, 1 µg/mL propidium iodide (Thermo Fisher Scientific) in B.E. PBS was added to stain dead cells. Stained cells were analyzed using a Guava EasyCyte (Millipore, Burlington, MA) with the FlowJo program (Tree Star, Ashland, OR).

SDS-PAGE and Western blotting

WT and KO (1.0 × 106 cells) were collected and protein-extracted using RIPA buffer (Cell Signaling Tech., Danvers, MA). Proteins were separated by SDS-PAGE and transferred to a PVDF membrane (BIO-RAD, Hercules, CA). The membrane was blocked with PBS containing 5% skim milk. Beta Actin mouse mAb (Cell Signaling Tech., #8H10D10), EGF Receptor XP Rabbit mAb (Cell Signaling Tech., #D38B1), and anti-mouse IgG HRP-Linked Antibody (DAKO, #P0260, Glostrup, Denmark) or anti-rabbit IgG HRP-Linked Antibody (DAKO, #7074P2) were used for detection. After the membrane was reacted with Amersham ECL Prime Western Blotting Detection Reagent (Cytiva, Marlborough, MA), chemiluminescence imaging was taken by using ChemiDoc XRS + with Image Lab Software (BIO-RAD).

Immunofluorescence staining

1.5 × 104 cells/200 µL/well cancer cells were seeded onto Falcon 8-well culture slides (Corning) and cultured overnight at 37 ℃ under 5% CO2 conditions. Subsequently, T cells were added (E:T = 10:1, 1.5 × 105 cells/50 µL/well), and TDB was supplemented to a final concentration of 1 ng/mL in 100 µL of medium. Co-culture was carried out for 2.5 h. Following the co-culture, 4% paraformaldehyde was added and reacted at room temperature for 15 min. The samples were then blocked for 1 h using a blocking buffer (3% BSA, 0.05% Triton-X). Subsequently, the samples were stained with rabbit anti-EGFR mAb (Abcam, #ab32077, Cambridge, UK), rat anti-CD3 mAb (Abcam, #ab11089), and Acti-stain™ 555 phalloidin (Cytoskeleton, #PHDH1-A, Denver, CO) diluted in PBS. Goat anti-rabbit polyclonal antibody (pAb) AlexaFluor 488 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #A11008) and goat anti-rat pAb AlexaFluor 647 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #A21247) were used as secondary antibodies. DAPI (Thermo Fisher Scientific), diluted in PBS, was utilized for nuclear staining. Images were captured using a BZ-X710 microscope (KEYENCE, Osaka, Japan).

Cell killing assay

Cultured cancer cells (1.0 × 105 cells/100 µL/well) were seeded onto 96-well plates and incubated overnight. Subsequently, T cells (E:T = 5:1, 1.0 × 106 cells/50 µL/well) were introduced to cancer cells. TDB was prepared and added to the culture in 100 µL of the medium, followed by a 24–72 h incubation period. After incubation, cell viability was assessed using Cell Counting Kit-8 (Dojindo Laboratories, Kumamoto, Japan) in all experimental assays.

ELISA

Cancer cells (1.0 × 104 cells/100 µL/well) were seeded onto 96-well plates and cultured overnight at 37 ℃ under 5% CO2. T cells (E:T = 5:1, 1.0 × 106 cells/50 µL/well) were added and co-cultured. hEx3 was added at a final concentration of 0.1–100 ng/mL in 100 µL of medium and cultured for 24 h. The culture supernatant was then collected and assayed using human IFN-gamma ELISA Kit, human TNF-alpha ELISA Kit, human CXCL10 ELISA Kit (Proteintech, Rosemont, IL), and human HMGB1 ELISA Kit (Chondrex, Woodinville, WA) according to the protocols provided. Briefly, calibration curves were prepared using the standard proteins supplied with each ELISA kit, and then, diluted co-culture supernatants were used in a sandwich ELISA assay.

Bystander effect assay

To verify whether the cytokine release of TDB induces a bystander effect on KO, KO were previously stained with XenoLight DiR (PerkinElmer, Waltham, MA) stain. The T cells were then mixed with WT in several ratios, seeded in 96-well plates at a total concentration of 1.0 × 104 cells/100 µL/well, and incubated in light-shielded overnight. T cells were then co-cultured with TDB. T cells and TDB were then co-cultured and allowed to react for 24 h. After washing well, KO adhering to 96-well plates were measured by measuring adherent cells under a fluorescence microscope. The cell death rate was calculated by assuming that the adherent KO were the number of viable cells.

T cell migration assay

After seeding and adhering cancer cells (1.0 × 105 cells/500 µL/well) in 24-wells, T cells (E:T = 5:1, 1.0 × 106 cells/250 µL/well) and TDB were co-cultured in 500 µL of medium. Cytokine standards diluted in PBS were added as needed. After 24 h of co-culture, all the culture medium was removed and centrifuged (2000 rpm, 5 min, 4 °C), and only the supernatant was separated and placed in 24-well plates. 5.0 µm trans-wells (Corning) were placed on the plates, and T cells previously stained with DiR were added to the trans-wells. T cells (1.0 × 106 cells/200 µL) were inserted into 24-wells containing supernatant. The trans-wells were placed in an incubator, and the trans-wells were removed after 2 h. The 24-wells were then well suspended and transferred to 96-wells, and the sample was flowed for 30 s by flow cytometry to determine the number of cells.

Statistical analysis

All data are presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). One-way ANOVA with Tukey or one-way ANOVA with Dunnet was used for statistical treatment of three or more groups. Statistical processing was performed using R software (version 4.3.2).

Results

Establishment of KO

We established KO using CRISPR-Cas9 to evaluate T cell responses without TDB cross-linking and IS formation. The TDB used in this study was EGFR/CD3 TDB (hEx3). The knockout was performed by CRISPR-Cas9 using gRNA targeting exon 4 of EGFR (Fig. 1c). Sequencing revealed a 4 bp deletion and 1 bp insertion in exon 4 of EGFR (Fig. 1c), and protein expression was measured by Western blotting (Fig. 1d). The binding activity of TDB was measured by flow cytometry. T cells and WT showed binding activity to TDB, but KO lost binding activity to TDB (Fig. 1e). Therefore, we succeeded in establishing KO for use in this study. T cells used in this study were expanded from PBMCs, and surviving cells were confirmed to be T cells by flow cytometry (Supplementary Fig. 1).

Immunological synapse formation

Since IS formation is transient, this experiment was performed with an E:T ratio of 10:1 to enhance the likelihood of observing IS formation. As a result, fluorescence immunostaining confirmed that the IS formed by the action of TDB was formed at low concentrations of TDB. Furthermore, when a high concentration of TDB was applied, the IS between WT and T cells was more firmly formed (Fig. 2a–c). The amount of IS formation was also evaluated by taking extensive photographs at lower magnification. The common area of F-Actin and CD3 in the entire image was calculated and divided by the DAPI area to obtain the amount of IS formation per unit cell. The results showed that the amount of IS formation in WT increased significantly in a TDB concentration-dependent manner. On the other hand, there was no significant difference in the amount of IS formation in KO even when the TDB concentration was increased (Fig. 2d, supplementary Fig. 2).

Cell killing assay

We evaluated the cytotoxic activity in co-cultures of WT or KO with T cells and TDB. PBMCs used in the assay were expanded for more than 1 week. After 24 h of co-culture, the percentage of cancer cells adhering to the 96-wells was calculated using the WST-8 reagent. The results showed that T cells induced cytotoxicity against WT according to the concentration of TDB. On the other hand, T cells did not induce cytotoxicity against KO, showing a significant difference in the region of high TDB concentration (Fig. 3a). When the co-culture time was increased to 48 and 72 h, the efficacy of WT at lower concentrations was observed. However, KO showed almost no cytotoxicity even at high TDB concentrations (Fig. 3b, c).

Cytotoxic cytokine release

The mechanism of action of TDB, i.e., T cell-independent indirect action, was investigated. The supernatants were collected after co-culture, and ELISA quantified the released cytokines (IFN-γ, TNF-α). As the co-culture time was increased to 48 and 72 h, the release of IFN-γ increased in WT, while it was hardly observed in KO and negative control (Fig. 3d, e). In addition, regarding the amount of TNF-α released, KO showed no significant difference from the negative control at all TDB concentrations and IFN-γ at 24 h of co-culture. Still, WT showed a substantial increase at high TDB concentrations (Fig. 3g). WT decreased gradually as the co-culture time increased to 48 and 72 h, and at 72 h, WT decreased to the point where there was no significant difference between KO and negative control (Fig. 3h). When the cytokine release standards released at each co-culture time of WT were added to WT and KO, cytotoxicity was observed following the cytokine levels (Fig. 3f, i).

Bystander effect on KO

We tested whether cytotoxic activity also occurs in KO due to the high release of cytokines by the formation of IS between WT and T cells (Fig. 4a). Briefly, KO were stained with DiR and mixed with WT at an arbitrary ratio to assess KO cytotoxicity using fluorescence microscopy (Supplementary Fig. 3). In addition, experiments were conducted at two different E:T ratios, 5:1 and 2:1, to investigate the effect of the E:T ratio on the bystander effect. As a prerequisite, the viability of the remaining KO in the 96-well plates was confirmed by flow cytometry (Supplementary Fig. 4). After 24 h of co-culture, the injury rate of KO increased as the proportion of WT increased. Furthermore, more KO were injured because the co-culture time was extended to 48 and 72 h (Fig. 4b–d). In the case of E:T = 2:1, almost no cell injury was observed at 24 h of co-culture, but at 48 and 72 h of co-culture, the rate of cell injury to KO increased (Fig. 4e–g). Improving the E:T ratio also improved the efficacy against KO.

T cell migration assay and chemokine ELISA

To determine whether the supernatant of each culture condition contained chemokines, factors that cause T cell migration, we constructed an assay system to measure T cell migration (Fig. 5a). Prior to the assay, it was confirmed that the migrated cells were T cells (Supplementary Fig. 5). When T cell migration was measured using this assay system, the number of migrated T cells significantly increased when the TDB concentration was increased from 0.1 to 10 ng/mL in WT. On the other hand, in KO and negative control, the number of migrated T cells tended to increase with increasing TDB concentration. Still, the difference was insignificant (Fig. 5b). Furthermore, the release of CXCL10, an IFN-γ-inducible chemokine, was confirmed by ELISA (Fig. 5c). The results showed that the supernatant of KO was not significantly different from the negative control at all TDB concentrations, while WT showed a significant increase in IFN-γ release at high TDB concentrations; CXCL10, IFN-γ, and TNF-α standards were added to the supernatant of KO, alone or in combination. As a result, CXCL10, IFN-γ, and TNF-α standards alone did not significantly enhance T cell migration, whereas the combination of IFN-γ and TNF-α significantly increased the number of migrated T cells (Fig. 5d–g). Next, to examine the effect of DAMPs on T cell migration, representative HMGB1 release was measured by ELISA (Fig. 5h). On the other hand, the supernatant of KO was not significantly different from the negative control. Furthermore, when HMGB1 alone was examined for its possible involvement in T cell migration, it did not significantly enhance T cell migration (Fig. 5i). We then examined the combination of HMGB1 and each cytokine. The results showed that T cell migration was significantly enhanced under the conditions of HMGB1 and IFN-γ or the combination of HMGB1, IFN-γ, and TNF-α (Fig. 5j–l).

Discussion

Tumor heterogeneity and the complexity of the tumor microenvironment are significant challenges in cancer therapy, limiting the efficacy of targeted treatments. Tumor heterogeneity, including uneven distribution of cell types and antigens, can hinder the uniform effectiveness of targeted therapies. For instance, EGFR expression within tumors is often heterogeneous. To address this, evaluating the efficacy of T cell-dependent bispecific antibodies (TDBs) under heterogeneous conditions is crucial. TDBs hold the potential to mediate indirect antitumor effects, such as inducing bystander cytotoxicity or enhancing T cell migration to convert cold tumors into hot tumors. Additionally, immunogenic cell death mediated by releasing damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) may further contribute to TDB efficacy.

In this study, we visualized IS formation induced by TDB. Our findings demonstrated a concentration-dependent increase in IS formation in WT, with no significant effect in KO. This may be due to two factors: first, higher TDB concentrations likely increase the probability of bridging cancer cells and T cells; second, elevated TDB levels may lead to cross-linking at multiple points, strengthening cell–cell adhesion. However, previous studies suggest that excessively high TDB concentrations can negatively impact IS formation, emphasizing the importance of determining an optimal dosing strategy to maximize efficacy while minimizing side effects.

ELISA analysis revealed that the amount of IFN-γ and TNF-α release induced by IS formation and their kinetics were significantly different. The main functions of IFN-γ are stimulation of antigen-presenting cells and enhancement of tumor cell-killing ability. In contrast, excessive release of TNF-α can induce a robust inflammatory response and cause serious side effects such as cytokine release syndrome (CRS). For example, a clinical trial using blinatumomab, a BiTE targeting CD3 and CD19, reported that most CRS occurred after the first dose. Different release patterns of IFN-γ and TNF-α by IS formation have important therapeutic efficacy and safety. In particular, IS formation-enhanced release of TNF-α was observed only in the relatively early phase, which supports the results of the blinatumomab clinical trial. To summarize, while sustained release of IFN-γ contributes to T cell activation and tumor cell suppression, early suppression of excessive TNF-α release by anti-TNF-α antibodies or other means may improve treatment safety.

We developed an in vitro system using KO to investigate the bystander effect. Our data showed that the proportion of WT, T cells, and co-culture duration influenced cellular damage. These findings suggest that TDB efficacy may be enhanced in tumors with a higher percentage of antigen-expressing cells. However, this should only be the case when T cells are well infiltrated, and T cell recruitment by TDB is not compromised. Importantly, complete antigen positivity in all tumor cells may not be necessary, making this approach promising for heterogeneous tumors. This method provides a robust model for mimicking tumor heterogeneity and evaluating TDB efficacy, potentially streamlining drug discovery processes.

Moreover, IS formation was found to enhance T cell migration significantly. A combination of IFN-γ and HMGB1 synergistically promoted migration, consistent with prior studies showing that IFN-γ-inducible chemokines interact with HMGB1 to strengthen T cell recruitment. However, the migration levels observed in WT were not replicated in KO cultures supplemented with individual recombinant proteins. This suggests that complex and unidentified interactions among cytokines and chemokines play critical roles. Therefore, comprehensive analyses are needed to identify the key mediators influencing T cell migration.

Recent studies have identified N-acetyl aspartate (NAA) released from tumor cells as an inhibitor of IS formation, while exhausted T cells may show reduced IS-forming capacity. Therefore, combining TDBs with neutralizing antibodies against NAA or immune checkpoint inhibitors could mitigate the immunosuppressive tumor environment and enhance therapeutic efficacy. These strategies hold promise for overcoming the challenges posed by the tumor microenvironment.

In summary, our findings indicate that indirect cellular damage mediated by IS formation involves not only inflammatory cytokines but also complex interactions among cytokines, chemokines, and DAMPs (Fig. 6). These results provide essential groundwork for the clinical application of TDBs. However, a limitation of this study lies in its reliance on in vitro systems; in vivo validation is necessary to better understand the therapeutic impact of TDBs and to optimize their clinical application.
