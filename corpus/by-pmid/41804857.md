# Overcoming claudin family homology: discovery of ARC101, a highly potent CLDN6-specific T-cell engager with a novel CD3 binder for ovarian adenocarcinoma.

## Abstract

Claudin-6 (CLDN6) is an oncofetal tight junction protein with minimal to no expression in healthy adult tissues but aberrant upregulation in ovarian malignancies, making it an attractive tumor-selective antigen for T cell-based immunotherapy. The development of CLDN6-targeting antibodies, however, has been challenged by its high homology to CLDN9, which is expressed in normal tissues and differs by only three amino acids within the extracellular domains. Here, we describe the discovery and preclinical development of ARC101, a bispecific CLDN6×CD3 antibody featuring a naturally derived, highly potent CLDN6 binder with no cross-reactivity to CLDN9 or other human membrane proteins. The stringent specificity of ARC101 eliminates off-target binding and distinguishes it from other CLDN6-targeting antibodies in development. The effector arm of ARC101 incorporates a novel conformational CD3 binder, enabling potent T cell-mediated cytotoxicity against CLDN6-expressing tumor cells in vitro and in vivo. ARC101 also demonstrated a favorable pharmacokinetic profile in cynomolgus monkeys, low immunostimulatory responses in ex vivo human donor assays, and robust biophysical properties compatible with standard antibody manufacturing. Collectively, these findings support the clinical advancement of ARC101 as a differentiated, CLDN6-specific bispecific immunotherapy with exceptional tumor selectivity and optimized T cell activity for the treatment of solid tumors.

Introduction

Ovarian cancer is the most lethal gynecologic malignancy worldwide, with approximately 300,000 new cases and 200,000 deaths annually. In the United States alone, an estimated 20,890 women were diagnosed with ovarian cancer in 2025, and 12,730 are expected to die from the disease, making it the fifth leading cause of cancer-related mortality among women. Despite therapeutic advances, the 5-year survival rate for invasive epithelial ovarian cancer remains approximately 50%, and recurrence is common, with 70–90% of patients with advanced-stage disease relapsing within 5 years of initial therapy. Alarmingly, the global incidence of ovarian cancer is projected to approach 500,000 new cases by 2050, with associated deaths expected to rise to 350,000. These trends underscore the urgent need for novel, targeted therapeutic strategies to improve patient outcomes.

Claudin-6 (CLDN6) is an oncofetal tight junction protein expressed during early embryonic and fetal development, with minimal-to-undetectable levels in normal adult tissues. In contrast, it is aberrantly upregulated in multiple malignancies, displaying a highly tumor-specific expression profile. Notably, CLDN6 is abundantly expressed in a substantial proportion of ovarian cancers, as demonstrated by transcriptomic and proteomic datasets and validated at the protein level by independent studies. This tumor-selective expression, combined with minimal expression in healthy tissues, makes CLDN6 an attractive target for cytotoxic therapeutic modalities, including antibody drug conjugates, chimeric antigen receptor T cells, and bispecific T cell engagers (TCEs). However, successful translation of these approaches critically depends on achieving exceptional target specificity, given the potent cytotoxic mechanisms employed by these therapeutic modalities.

The development of CLDN6-specific monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) is inherently challenging due to the high sequence homology of CLDN6 within the claudin family, particularly with CLDN9, CLDN4, and CLDN3, which are abundantly expressed in normal tissues. The greatest risk stems from the similarity between CLDN6 and CLDN9, which differ by only three amino acids in their extracellular domains (ECDs). CLDN9 is expressed in vital tissues, such as the pituitary gland and cerebellum, where CLDN6 is absent. Thus, CLDN6-targeting mAbs must specifically distinguish CLDN6 from CLDN9 and other closely related claudins to be clinically viable. However, most CLDN6 mAbs currently in clinical development exhibit cross-reactivity with CLDN9, raising concerns regarding potential off-target toxicities. Moreover, the structural complexity of CLDN6 further complicates antibody discovery. As a four-pass transmembrane protein, it contains only two small extracellular loops comprising 54 and 23 amino acids. These conformationally constrained regions limit epitope accessibility, making it difficult to generate high-affinity mAbs that recognize the native CLDN6 conformation while maintaining specificity over other claudins.

To address these challenges and enable the development of a highly selective CLDN6-targeting therapeutic, we describe here the discovery and preclinical characterization of ARC101, a CLDN6xCD3 T cell-engaging bispecific antibody (bsAb) comprising a highly specific anti-CLDN6 mAb paired with an optimized anti-CD3 mAb, each recognizing conformationally distinct epitopes on their respective targets. We demonstrate the potential of ARC101 as a differentiated CLDN6-targeting bsAb by establishing its superior specificity relative to benchmark CLDN6 antibodies either in active clinical development or discontinued, identifying residues critical for CLDN6 binding and specificity, confirming the absence of cross-reactivity with CLDN9 and other human membrane proteins, and establishing its mechanism of action through potent T cell-mediated cytotoxicity against CLDN6-expressing cell lines in vitro and complete tumor regression in vivo. Furthermore, ARC101 exhibited key developability attributes, including extended half-life in nonhuman primates, low immunostimulatory responses in ex vivo human donor assays, and robust biophysical properties. Collectively, the findings of this study support the clinical evaluation of ARC101 in a Phase 1 study in patients with advanced solid tumors (NCT06672185).

Results

CLDN6 is overexpressed in ovarian cancers

To evaluate the relative expression and prevalence of CLDN6 in ovarian cancer, mass spectrometry (MS)-based surface proteomics and immunohistochemistry (IHC) were performed on patient samples spanning various tumor grades and histological subtypes, and the results were compared with normal ovarian tissues. MS analysis, using target-specific signature peptides for protein identification, revealed significant CLDN6 expression in tumors, with levels ranking in the upper-moderate range of the human surface proteome (Figure 1A). For IHC evaluation, samples were scored on a scale of 0 (negative) to 12 (strongly positive), integrating both staining intensity and the proportion of CLDN6-positive tumor cells. Using this scoring system, CLDN6 positivity was observed in 91%, 91%, 73%, and 0% of formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) samples from serous, papillary serous, metastatic, and mucinous tumor subtypes, respectively, with scores ranging from 0 to 9 (Table 1, Figure 1B). Tumor samples exhibited a multifocal to diffuse membranous staining pattern, highlighting the distinct spatial distribution of CLDN6 in malignant versus normal tissues (Figure 1D). Analysis of an ovarian tissue microarray (TMA) revealed an overall CLDN6 positivity rate of 43%, with the highest frequency in high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma (53%), followed by low-grade serous (33%), mucinous (20%), and mucinous papillary (18%) subtypes, with scores ranging from 0 to 11 (Table 1, Figure 1C). In contrast, CLDN6 expression was not detected via IHC in any normal ovarian tissue samples. Taken together, these complementary methodologies confirm CLDN6 expression in a clinically relevant subset of ovarian cancers, supporting its potential as a therapeutic target for disease intervention. 

Generation of highly specific CLDN6 antibodies with conformational epitopes

CLDN6 is a four-pass transmembrane protein that shares the highest homology with CLDN9 among the claudin family members, differing by only one amino acid in the first ECD (ECD1) and two amino acids in the second ECD (ECD2) (Supplementary Figures S1 and S2). To generate highly specific anti-CLDN6 mAbs without cross-reactivity to CLDN9 or other claudins, eight SJL and eight BALB/c mice were immunized with a DNA vector encoding the native human CLDN6 sequence. Immunizations were performed using either gene gun or tattoo-based delivery methods to maximize epitope diversity and antibody repertoire. B cells from mice with confirmed serum reactivity to CLDN6 were screened for binding to CHO-CLDN6 cells (Figure 2A), and positive clones were counter-screened against CHO-CLDN9 and CHO-CLDN4 cells to assess specificity. These screens identified dozens of potent clones that bound CLDN6 with minimal or no binding to CLDN9 (Figure 2B) or CLDN4 (data not shown). Nineteen clones were then selected for expression as chimeric IgGs and reevaluated for binding to CHO-CLDN6 and CHO-CLDN9 cells. Among these, binder 51H102/51K301 exhibited the strongest binding to CLDN6 with no detectable binding to CLDN9 (Figure 2C). Phylogenetic analysis of both heavy- and light-chain variable regions revealed that clones exhibiting CLDN9 cross-reactivity cluster together, whereas the CLDN6-specific clone 51H102/51K301 occupied a distinct branch in both heavy- and light-chain phylogenetic trees and was more divergent in sequence from the cross-reactive cluster (Supplementary Figure S3). 

To define the molecular basis of 51H102 binding specificity, site-directed mutagenesis was performed at the three differing amino acid positions in the ECDs between CLDN6 and CLDN9, enabling epitope mapping at the residue level (Supplementary Figure S4). Six CLDN6/CLDN9 hybrid constructs were generated by substituting CLDN6 residues with their CLDN9 counterparts, including three single-point mutants (M29L, R145Q, and Q156L) and three double-point mutants (M29L+R145Q, M29L+Q156L, and R145Q+Q156L), along with wild-type CLDN6 and CLDN9 controls. Constructs were transiently expressed in SKOV3 cells, which lack endogenous CLDN6 and CLDN9 expression, and antibody binding was assessed via flow cytometry. In addition to 51H102, a similarly potent CLDN6 binder 112H105, which exhibits 50% cross-reactivity with CLDN9, was included as a comparator. The results showed that both M29L and Q156L mutations impaired 51H102 binding to CLDN6 (Figure 2D). The Q156L substitution completely abolished binding, while M29L reduced binding by approximately 50%. R145Q alone had no effect; however, when combined with Q156L, CLDN6 binding was entirely lost. Similarly, the M29L+Q156L mutant abolished binding, mirroring the single Q156L effect. In contrast, the M29L+ R145Q mutant reduced binding by approximately 50%, comparable to the single M29L mutant. These findings indicate that Q156 in ECD2 is the primary contact residue for 51H102, with M29 in ECD1 also contributing to CLDN6 recognition, consistent with binding to a conformational epitope spanning both ECD domains. Data from the CLDN9-cross-reactive binder 112H105 showed a similar trend, with both M29 and Q156 contributing to CLDN6 binding, but with differential effects on CLDN9. The Q156L substitution, as well as its double mutants M29L+Q156L and R145Q+Q156L, significantly impaired binding to CLDN6 (Figure 2D). Unlike 51H102, for which Q156L completely abolished CLDN6 binding, this mutation reduced 112H105 binding to CLDN6 by 70–80% while also decreasing binding to CLDN9 by approximately 50%. In contrast, the M29L single and its M29L+ R145Q mutant had no negative impact on CLDN9, but reduced CLDN6 binding by approximately 30%. Collectively, these results indicate that Q156 is a critical determinant for interactions with both CLDN6 and CLDN9, while 51H102 achieves exquisite specificity by recognizing Q156 together with M29 in a CLDN6-specific conformational context.

ARC101: a bsAb with exceptional CLDN6 specificity and a novel CD3 arm

The bsAb ARC101 was constructed using a humanized 51H102 sequence combined with a CD3 effector arm to redirect CD3-positive T cells for the targeted killing of CLDN6-positive tumor cells. ARC101 contains L234A/L235A mutations in the Fc region to silence Fc-mediated effector functions, and “knobs-into-holes” mutations in the CH3 domain to promote heterodimerization between the “knob” chain (T366W) and the “hole” chain (T366S/L368A/Y407V) (Figure 3A). Following expression in CHO cells and purification by standard chromatography, ARC101 was obtained with >99% heterodimeric purity. The bsAb was subsequently subjected to stringent characterization (described below) to ensure the specificity of both target arms. 

The binding of ARC101 to claudin family proteins was compared with two publicly disclosed anti-CLDN6 antibodies: Ref CLDN6 Ab1 (US20220289839) (assignee: Xencor) and Ref CLDN6 Ab2 (WO2022096700A1) (assignee: Amgen), both of which are CLDN6xCD3 bsAbs developed as treatments for solid tumors. To evaluate their target specificity for CLDN6, CLDN9, and CLDN4, each protein was overexpressed in SKOV3 cells, and surface antibody binding was assessed using flow cytometry (Figure 3B). All three anti-CLDN6 antibodies showed comparable binding to CLDN6, with EC50 values of 10.2 nM (ARC101), 10.4 nM (Ref CLDN6 Ab1), and 16.4 nM (Ref CLDN6 Ab2). However, ARC101 uniquely exhibited no detectable cross-reactivity with either CLDN9 or CLDN4. By contrast, Ref CLDN6 Ab1 and Ref CLDN6 Ab2 bound to CLDN9, with EC50 values of 49.4 nM and 237 nM, respectively, and both showed weaker but detectable binding to CLDN4. In addition to these two benchmarks, we also evaluated the CLDN6 binding arm derived from a trispecific CLDN6×CD3×CD137 antibody in a separate study using the publicly disclosed CLDN6 variable region sequence. In this binding assessment using CHO overexpression cell lines, Ref CLDN6 Ab3 (WO2021200939A1) (assignee: Chugai) showed binding to CLDN6 comparable to ARC101 and minimal binding to CLDN9, with detectable signal observed only at antibody concentrations above 100 nM (Supplementary Figures S5). Notably, substantial binding was also observed on CLDN6-knockout OVCAR3 cells, whereas ARC101 showed no detectable binding.

The CD3-binding domain of ARC101 was generated by immunizing transgenic mice with a human CD3εδ heterodimer, followed by screening on Jurkat and human pan T cells. The anti-CD3 sequence was formatted as a single-chain variable fragment (scFv) paired with the CLDN6 antigen-binding fragment (Fab) in a bispecific construct. Binding of ARC101 to human pan T cells was evaluated across five donors (Figure 3C), and affinity was assessed against the recombinant CD3εδ protein (Figure 4A). ARC101 demonstrated double-digit nanomolar potency on cells (EC50 = 33 ± 3.6 nM), comparable to the affinity measured against the recombinant protein (KD = 12 nM). Kinetic analysis revealed a fast on-rate and fast off-rate, indicating efficient but transient engagement of CD3. The binding competition assay showed SP34 antibody signal in the presence of the ARC101-CD3εδ complex, indicating that ARC101 recognizes a distinct epitope from SP34 (Figure 4B). 

Epitope mapping of ARC101 binding to CD3 was subsequently performed using hydrogen-deuterium exchange mass spectrometry (HDX-MS), which confirmed interactions with both CD3ε and CD3δ subunits (Figure 4C). Comparative deuterium uptake between the ARC101– CD3εδ complex and CD3εδ alone identified the primary binding regions on CD3ε [Uniprot ID: P07766] at residues 54–58 (GSEIL) and 98–106 (CYPRGSKPE), both showing marked reductions in deuterium uptake. Additional residues, including 48–54 (TCPQYP), 92–97 (QSGYYV), and 107–116 (DANFYLYLRA), showed moderate reductions and were considered peripheral interaction sites. On CD3δ [Uniprot ID: P04234], residues 57–71 (RLDLGKRILDPRGIY) displayed substantial reductions in deuterium uptake upon ARC101 binding, indicating primary binding regions.

The specificity of ARC101 for both CLDN6 and CD3 was further confirmed by Retrogenix screening against approximately 6500 human proteins, including plasma membrane proteins, secreted proteins, and heterodimeric complexes, collectively covering > 90% of the known cell surface and secreted proteome. After background binding assessment using untransfected HEK293 cells and control target controls, ARC101 was screened on HEK293 cells overexpressing individual proteins via fluorescence detection. The results showed that ARC101 specifically bound to CLDN6 on both fixed and live cell arrays, and to CD3ε when co-expressed as a heterodimer with CD3δ or CD3γ on fixed arrays (Supplementary Figures S6A and S6B). No binding was detected to other proteins, including claudin paralogs CLDN1–CLDN25 and CLDN34 (data not shown). These findings were corroborated by a follow-up flow cytometry study, in which ARC101 showed strong fluorescence intensity for CLDN6 (Supplementary Figure S6C). Together, these results confirm that both the anti-CLDN6 and anti-CD3 domains of ARC101 exhibit high target specificity, with no detectable off-target interactions across the human surface proteome.

ARC101 elicits potent and specific T cell-mediated cytotoxicity against CLDN6-expressing cells

The ability of ARC101 to redirect T cells toward CLDN6-expressing cells and induce T cell-mediated cytotoxicity was evaluated using both engineered and endogenous CLDN6-positive cell lines. On-target functional activity was first assessed in SKOV3 cells, which lack endogenous CLDN6 expression and were engineered to overexpress individual claudin proteins, as well as in OVCAR3 cells, which naturally express CLDN6 but were genetically modified via CRISPR to knock out CLDN6 expression. Consistent with the binding data, ARC101 did not induce cytotoxicity in SKOV3-CLDN9, SKOV3-CLDN4, SKOV3 wild-type, or OVCAR3 CLDN6-knockout cells, while demonstrating dose-dependent cytotoxicity against CLDN6-expressing SKOV3 and OVCAR3 cells (Figure 5A). These results confirm the CLDN6-specific T cell redirection and cytotoxic function of ARC101. 

Next, several cancer cell lines (OVCAR3, PA1, OV90, and HepG2) that endogenously express CLDN6 RNA, based on RNA-seq data from the Human Protein Atlas (202.4, 832.4, 245.2, and 34.9 transcripts per million [TPM], respectively), were analyzed for surface CLDN6 expression by flow cytometry. ARC101 bound to all four cell lines, with PA1 showing the most homogeneous and intense staining (105.5–106.5), followed by OVCAR3 (104–106), OV90 (103–106, biphasic pattern), and HepG2 (103–105) (Supplementary Figure S7). ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity was then evaluated across a range of effector-to-target (E:T) ratios using pan T cells from five healthy donors. ARC101 induced dose- and E:T ratio-dependent cytotoxicity in all four cell lines, reaching maximal lysis of 97% (OVCAR3), 98% (PA1), 87% (OV90), and 53% (HepG2) at a 3:1 ratio (Figure 5B). Potency varied by cell lines, with EC50 values of 17–526 pM (OVCAR3), 33–490 pM (PA1), 450–1621 pM (OV90), and 28 pM–104 nM (HepG2) across 3:1, 1:1, and 1:3 E:T ratios. Collectively, these results indicate differences in both the magnitude and potency of ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity, with a rank order of OVCAR3 > PA1 >OV90 >HepG2.

In parallel, the mechanism and downstream consequences of ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity were examined by assessing T cell activation and cytokine production. At a 1:1 E:T ratio, ARC101 induced a dose-dependent activation of CD8+ T cells, as measured by CD25 expression. The EC50 values for activation were 1.2- to 3.9-fold lower than those for cytotoxicity at the 72-h timepoint (Figure 5C, Supplementary Table S1). ARC101 also elicited a concentration-dependent secretion of IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-2, with IFN-γ being the most abundant (Figure 5D). These cytokine responses showed notable donor-to-donor variability, consistent with interindividual immunological differences. Notably, cytokine dose-response curves were shifted to the right relative to activation curves, indicating a higher threshold for cytokine release. This shift was most pronounced in PA1 cells, followed by OVCAR3 and OV90, and least evident in HepG2 cells (Figure 5D, Supplementary Table S1).

ARC101 achieves complete tumor regression in the ovarian cancer xenograft model in vivo

To evaluate the in vivo efficacy of ARC101, a dose-ranging study was conducted in a humanized xenograft model using OVCAR3 ovarian cancer cells. Briefly, tumors were established in immunocompromised NSG (non-obese diabetic [NOD] severe immunodeficiency [SCID] gamma chain deficient (il2rg null)) mice engrafted with CD3+ pan T cells isolated from a single human donor. Once tumors reached a volume of 100–150 mm3, the mice were randomized and treated with ARC101 at dose levels of 0.01, 0.03, or 0.1 mg/kg, administered once weekly for 4 consecutive weeks (Figure 6A). ARC101 treatment resulted in a significant, dose-dependent reduction in tumor volume compared to the vehicle control (Figure 6B). By day 12, ARC101-treated groups exhibited a 35–39% reduction in tumor volume relative to the control group, indicating early-onset therapeutic activity. Continued treatment further enhanced the observed anti-tumor effects, with the most notable responses observed by day 22, when the highest dose group (0.1 mg/kg) achieved 95% tumor growth inhibition (TGI), while the 0.03 and 0.01 mg/kg groups reached 80% and 59% TGI, respectively. At the study endpoint on day 28, mice in the 0.1 mg/kg group maintained near-complete tumor clearance, corresponding to 97% TGI. The 0.03 mg/kg and 0.01 mg/kg groups showed no further reduction beyond day 22, plateauing at 85% and 62% TGI, respectively. These findings suggest a dose threshold for maximal efficacy, with the highest tested dose of ARC101 inducing durable tumor regression, while lower doses approached a saturation point in anti-tumor activity. No significant body weight loss or treatment-related toxicity was observed throughout the study (Supplementary Figure S8). 

To further characterize the in vivo behavior of ARC101 and support the observed anti-tumor activity, a dedicated pharmacokinetic (PK) study was conducted in a separate cohort of OVCAR3 tumor-bearing mice. Briefly, animals received single intraperitoneal doses of 0.1, 0.03, and 0.01 mg/kg ARC101, matching efficacy dose levels, along with an additional higher dose of 3 mg/kg to assess dose linearity across an expanded range. Serum samples were collected at multiple time points post-administration and analyzed for ARC101 concentration. ARC101 displayed a linear, dose-dependent PK profile across all tested groups (Figure 6C). Peak serum concentrations were reached approximately 6 h after dosing, indicating rapid systemic absorption following intraperitoneal injection. After reaching maximum levels, ARC101 concentrations gradually declined, with measurable exposure maintained throughout the sampling period. The calculated half-life (T1/2) ranged from 7.4 to 14.3 days across doses, consistent with the PK behavior of IgG-class antibodies in mice. Non-compartmental analysis of the concentration-time curves confirmed dose-proportional increases in both the peak serum concentration (Cmax) and area under the curve (AUC), demonstrating linear PK over the tested range (Supplementary Table S2). Moreover, measurable ARC101 concentrations were detected even at the lowest 0.01 mg/kg dose, confirming adequate systemic exposure to support the dose-dependent anti-tumor effects observed in the OVCAR3 xenograft model.

ARC101 exhibits favorable pharmacokinetics, low immunogenicity, and robust biophysical properties supporting clinical development

The PK profile of ARC101 was assessed in cynomolgus monkeys following a single intravenous bolus administration at doses of 1 and 10 mg/kg. Serum samples were collected up to 21 days post-dose and analyzed for ARC101 concentrations. ARC101 exhibited dose-proportional systemic exposure, with sustained serum levels and no evidence of target-mediated drug disposition (Figure 7). The Cmax was observed at the earliest sampling timepoint (1 h post-dose), reaching 35 µg/mL for the 1 mg/kg group and 390 µg/mL for the 10 mg/kg group, consistent with an approximate 10-fold difference in AUC between dose levels (Table 2). Mean clearance (CL) was dose-independent and aligned with reported linear clearance values for IgG-class antibodies in cynomolgus monkeys (0.27 mL/h/kg). The terminal T1/2, calculated across individuals based on the range of values from both groups, was 7.9–13.5 days. All six animals exhibited biphasic elimination, characterized by a rapid distribution phase through day 7, followed by a slower elimination phase to study completion. 

The immunogenic potential of ARC101 was evaluated ex vivo using a dendritic cell (DC) and CD4+ T cell restimulation assay, benchmarked against trastuzumab and bevacizumab, two clinically approved mAbs with low incidence of anti-drug antibodies (ADAs). Keyhole limpet hemocyanin (KLH) and CEFTA, a class II memory peptide pool, were included as positive controls to confirm assay sensitivity. Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from 30 healthy donors, representing 12 HLA-DRB1 alleles covering > 95% of the global population, were stimulated with test antibodies and controls. To enhance antigen presentation, a monocyte restimulation step (42 h incubation) was performed after 6 days of co-culture with DCs. T cell activation and intracellular cytokine production were assessed by flow cytometry using CD134 expression and IFN-γ staining on CD4+ T cells. These readouts were selected as representative markers to provide an initial gauge of CD4+ T cell activation and inflammatory cytokine induction during immunogenicity risk screening. In this assay, ARC101 elicited a significantly lower immunostimulatory response than KLH and CEFTA (p < 0.0001) and showed responses comparable to trastuzumab and bevacizumab, with no statistically significant differences in Stimulation Index (SI) or responder frequency (Figure 8). Donors with relatively higher responses were consistent across all three test antibodies, suggesting intrinsic donor-specific variability. Based on CD134+ CD4+ T cell activation, 13–17% of donors responded to the test antibodies, whereas IFN-γ producing CD4+ T cells were observed in only 1–2%. In contrast, KLH and CEFTA elicited T cell activation responses in 70–77% of donors and IFN-γ secretion in 73% of donors. 

To assess developability for manufacturing and clinical applications, ARC101 was subjected to a series of biophysical assays (Table 3, Supplementary Figure S9). ARC101 demonstrated robust expression and purification characteristics, achieving 84% heterodimer content following Protein A chromatography, a standard first-step purification method for antibody-based biologics, and 99% heterodimer content after second-step purification. Using the two-step purified materials, thermal stability analysis revealed unfolding transitions typical of mAbs, with the anti-CD3 scFv arm unfolding at 64.2°C and the anti-CLDN6 Fab arm at 80°C. Stability under conditions simulating key product lifecycle stages, including manufacturing, storage, and handling, was further evaluated. ARC101 maintained structural integrity with minimal aggregation during accelerated thermal stress, repeated freeze-thaw cycles, prolonged incubation in process-relevant buffers at elevated temperatures, and conditions promoting post-translational modifications. To examine colloidal stability and nonspecific interaction potential, ARC101 was evaluated in a charge-based surface binding assay and serum stability test. ARC101 exhibited no measurable binding to either positively charged lysozyme or negatively charged trypsin inhibitor by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) at physiological pH, indicating the absence of charge-mediated nonspecific interactions. These findings were corroborated by a binding stability test in 90% human serum, where ARC101 retained its CD3-binding kinetics with no change in association rates (ka) as measured by Octet, confirming specificity under physiologically relevant conditions.

Discussion

The development of therapeutics that selectively and potently eliminate tumor cells while sparing normal tissue has long been the central goal in targeted oncology drug development, with the aim of maximizing efficacy while minimizing toxicity and improving patient outcomes. TCEs have recently emerged as a promising modality for treating advanced solid tumors. These agents function by simultaneously binding tumor-associated antigens and CD3 on T cells, thereby redirecting T cell-mediated cytotoxic activity toward malignant cells and avoiding the systemic toxicity often associated with chemotherapy. Despite recent clinical progress, on-target/off-tumor toxicity remains a major challenge, driven largely by the scarcity of truly tumor-selective antigens and the insufficient specificity of targeting antibodies. While tumor selectivity reflects the inherent differential expression of antigens between normal and malignant tissues, antibody specificity is a tunable property that can be refined through rigorous discovery and optimization strategies.

CLDN6 overexpression has been well documented across multiple solid tumors through in silico analyses of large-scale cancer and normal tissue datasets, and further substantiated by IHC studies showing minimal to undetectable expression in normal adult tissues. Our findings corroborate these observations: both MS-based surface proteomics and IHC analyses revealed frequent and robust CLDN6 overexpression across diverse ovarian tumor subtypes, with no detectable expression in normal ovarian tissues. These complementary data reinforce CLDN6 as a tumor-restricted antigen and highlight its potential as a selective target for CLDN6-directed TCEs.

The development of CLDN6-targeted therapeutics has been hindered by the challenge of generating antibodies with exceptional specificity, owing to the complex multi-pass membrane structure of CLDN6 and its high sequence homology with CLDN9. To date, most CLDN6-targeted biotherapeutics in clinical development exhibit varying degrees of cross-reactivity with CLDN9, CLDN4, or CLDN3. Cross-reactivity with these claudins carries a substantial risk of off-target toxicity in normal tissues, as demonstrated by comparative RNA expression analyses showing that CLDN3, CLDN4, and CLDN9 are broadly expressed across multiple normal tissues types, in contrast to the highly restricted expression pattern of CLDN6 (Supplementary Figure S10). Although some candidates have been described as CLDN6-specific or selective based on their differential cytotoxicity against CLDN6 versus CLDN9/4/3-expressing cells, detectable activity at high antibody concentrations suggests incomplete specificity. Notably, one CLDN6-directed bispecific TCE (AMG 794) entered early clinical development, but was subsequently terminated during Phase 1 evaluation. Using publicly available sequence information, we generated this antibody and evaluated it side by side with ARC101, observing cross-reactive binding to both CLDN4 and CLDN9. While it remains unknown whether the clinical discontinuation of this program was driven by its cross-reactivity profile, such off-target binding could plausibly contribute to safety liabilities, given the well-recognized risk of off-target toxicity associated with potent T cell-engaging modalities. These observations motivated us to develop an exclusively CLDN6-specific antibody that demonstrates no measurable binding to CLDN9, other claudin isoforms, or unrelated membrane proteins, thereby enabling precise tumor targeting while minimizing the risk of off-target toxicity in normal tissues.

To address this challenge, we implemented a rigorous antibody discovery, screening, and characterization strategy. Because of the difficulty of expressing and purifying CLDN6, DNA immunization with plasmids encoding the native CLDN6 sequence was used, a well-established approach for eliciting antibodies against structurally challenging membrane proteins. This campaign yielded hundreds of CLDN6-binding clones with varying potency and specificity. To ensure strict CLDN6 specificity, CHO cells overexpressing CLDN6, CLDN9, or CLDN4 were generated for counter-screening, eliminating cross-reactive clones. Consistent with the high sequence homology between CLDN6 and CLDN9, the majority of identified CLDN6 binders exhibited cross-reactivity with CLDN9. CLDN6 and CLDN9 differ by only three amino acids across their ECDs, creating a very narrow specificity window for antibody discovery and strongly biasing immune responses toward recognition of conserved structural features shared between the two proteins. Across multiple independent antibody generation campaigns, we observed a similarly high frequency of CLDN6/CLDN9 cross-reactive binders, highlighting the intrinsic difficulty of obtaining antibodies that can discriminate between these closely related targets. Through iterative triaging and counter-selection, clone 51H102/51K301 emerged as the lead candidate, exhibiting potent binding to CLDN6 with no detectable binding to CLDN9. Sequence and phylogenetic analyses further demonstrated that CLDN9-cross-reactive clones clustered closely together, indicating strong sequence convergence toward a shared binding mode. In contrast, the CLDN6-specific 51H102/51K301 clone occupied a distinct branch in both heavy- and light-chain phylogenetic trees and was markedly more divergent in sequence from the cross-reactive cluster, consistent with its exceptional CLDN6 specificity and lack of detectable CLDN9 binding.

To further distinguish 51H102/51K301 from previously reported CLDN6 antibodies, we performed comparative studies with three benchmark CLDN6 antibodies, including one in active clinical development (XmAb541) and two discontinued during Phase 1 evaluation (AMG 794, SAIL66). These head-to-head analyses demonstrated the superior specificity of 51H102/51K301. Unlike the benchmark Ref CLDN6 Ab1 and Ref CLDN6 Ab2 antibodies, which retained residual binding to CLDN9 at higher concentrations, 51H102/51K301 showed no binding even at concentrations up to 1 µM. Functional validation supported these findings, as no cytotoxicity was observed in CLDN6-negative or other claudin-expressing cell lines. While Ref CLDN6 Ab3 showed a minimal CLDN9 binding profile, substantial nonspecific binding was observed on CLDN6-knockout cells, whereas 51H102/51K301 showed no detectable binding, indicating 51H102/51K301’s superior specificity beyond CLDN6 homologs and the absence of nonspecific interactions with irrelevant targets. Mutagenesis studies focusing on the three amino acid differences between CLDN6 and CLDN9 revealed that Q156 in ECD2 and M29 in ECD1 were essential for binding. While Q156 has been previously implicated in CLDN6-specific recognition, to the best of our knowledge, the contribution of M29 to CLDN6 binding was demonstrated here for the first time. The spatial separation of these two residues suggests that 51H102/51K301 recognizes a conformational epitope dependent on the native three-dimensional structure of CLDN6. Recognition of a conformational epitope was further substantiated using a chemical denaturation approach in cell-based assays, in which a significant reduction in CLDN6 binding (77–92%) was observed across antibody concentrations compared with native conditions (Supplementary Figure S11), consistent with disruption of a conformational-dependent epitope. Together, these denaturation data and site-redirected mutagenesis results establish 51H102/51K301 as uniquely specific among CLDN6 antibody candidates and provide a mechanistic explanation for its ability to overcome the stringent specificity barrier imposed by CLDN6/CLDN9 homology through conformational epitope recognition, thereby enabling selective targeting of CLDN6 while distinguishing it from other CLDN6 antibody candidates.

The identification of a truly specific CLDN6-binding antibody enabled the development of ARC101, a bsAb pairing the CLDN6-targeting arm with a novel anti-CD3 arm. We compared the CD3 binding of ARC101 with a well-characterized anti-human CD3 mAb derived from the murine SP34 clone, which has been humanized and adapted by multiple pharmaceutical groups for use in T cell-redirecting therapeutics that have advanced into clinical development. Unlike TCEs derived from the SP34 antibody, which binds CD3ε alone, the CD3 arm of ARC101 recognizes a non-continuous epitope spanning both CD3ε and CD3δ subunits, as revealed by HDX-MS epitope mapping studies. Retrogenix microarray screening further corroborated this finding, as ARC101 bound CD3ε only when co-expressed with CD3δ or CD3γ, indicating a requirement for heterodimer formation consistent with conformational epitope recognition. The conformational nature of CD3 binding was further evaluated using a chemical denaturation approach in human pan T cells, which resulted in substantial reduction in ARC101 binding (47–72%) compared with native conditions (Supplementary Figure S11). Together, these denaturation data, along with HDX-MS epitope mapping and heterodimer versus individual subunit binding results, support the conclusion that the CD3 binding arm of ARC101 recognizes a conformational epitope distinct from that targeted by SP34. Recognition of a conformational epitope across the CD3 heterodimer may offer potential advantages over linear CD3ε-only binding, including more physiologic engagement of the native CD3 complex, broader coverage of CD3 conformations on T cells, and potentially improved immune synapse formation.

In addition to recognizing a conformational epitope that supports optimized T cell engagement, the CD3-binding affinity of ARC101 was deliberately tuned to the double-digit nanomolar range to balance effective T cell activation with reduced cytokine release, consistent with affinity-tuning strategies to mitigate cytokine release syndrome in TCEs. ARC101 is formatted using a clinically validated knobs-into-holes Fab × scFv heterodimeric design, enabling efficient and scalable expression via a three-chain transfection system. The CLDN6 arm is presented as a monovalent Fab, given its tumor-restricted expression and high specificity, obviating the need for bivalency to achieve tumor selectivity. The CD3 arm was optimized for stability and formatted as a monovalent scFv to minimize aberrant T cell activation while maintaining functional potency through its intrinsic affinity (data not shown). The Fc domain was engineered to eliminate Fc-mediated effector functions while retaining an IgG1 backbone to support standard production and purification workflows in manufacturing.

To validate this design principle, ARC101 was evaluated through a series of in vitro and in vivo studies. Tumor antigen heterogeneity, both between patients and within tumors, is a well-recognized challenge for targeted immunotherapies, as also reflected in our IHC data showing variable CLDN6 expression across ovarian cancer subtypes. To further examine the relationship between antigen density and ARC101 activity, we evaluated ARC101 across multiple ovarian cancer cell lines with differing levels of CLDN6 expression and under a range of E:T ratios (3:1, 1:1, 1:3) to mimic variable immune microenvironments due to limited T cell infiltration in solid tumors. CLDN6 surface density on these cell lines was quantified using the CLDN6-binding arm of ARC101. PA1 cells exhibited the highest CLDN6 expression (~260,000 receptors per cell), followed by OVCAR3 and HepG2 cells (~25,000 receptors per cell), and OV90 cells (~9,000 receptors per cell) (data not shown). Although ARC101 mediated T cell – dependent cytotoxicity across all four cell lines, both potency and maximal killing varied, with the rank order OVCAR3 > PA1 >OV90 >HepG2. This ordering did not directly correlate with CLDN6 surface density, indicating that receptor number alone is not the dominant determinant of ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity. This observation is consistent with prior reports for TCEs and other cytotoxic antibody-based modalities, in which antigen density does not necessarily predict potency or extent of killing once a minimal threshold for productive target engagement is exceeded. Instead, functional activity is influenced by additional parameters, including epitope accessibility, membrane topology, antigen conformation, immune synapse geometry, and effector cell availability. These considerations are particularly relevant for multi-pass transmembrane proteins such as claudins, where surface density measurements may underestimate functional heterogeneity, as epitope presentation depends on native protein conformation, membrane organization, and tight junction architecture rather than absolute expression level alone. Importantly, ARC101 binds a conformational epitope on CLDN6, as supported by chemical denaturation sensitivity and loop-spanning mutagenesis studies. This conformational dependence introduces additional variability in target engagement across different cell lines, which may present CLDN6 in distinct native conformations, thereby contributing to the observed differences in cytotoxic potency and maximal response.

While robust tumor cell lysis is a primary objective, cytokine release is a critical consideration due to its association with systemic toxicity. Measurements of pro-inflammatory cytokines, including IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-2, at dose levels aligned with those used in cytotoxicity assays, showed that the cytokine release occurred only at concentrations significantly higher (ten- to several hundred-fold) than those required for cytotoxicity. This separation between effective tumor cell killing and cytokine induction indicates a favorable therapeutic window and supports the potential of ARC101 to achieve a clinically meaningful balance of efficacy and safety. These findings were substantiated in a humanized OVCAR3 xenograft model, where ARC101 induced robust tumor regression and complete clearance at a low dose of 0.1 mg/kg without evidence of systemic toxicity. Together, these results establish ARC101 as a potent and selective TCE with a potentially broad therapeutic window in CLDN6-positive tumors.

Beyond efficacy, the intrinsic developability of ARC101, which is crucial for successful manufacturing and clinical advancement, was comprehensively evaluated. These assessments included PK profiling, immunogenicity risk analysis, and biophysical characterization. PK understanding is essential not only for determining optimal dosing regimens to achieve therapeutic efficacy, but also for verifying molecular features that contribute to safety, stability, and in vivo durability. Studies have shown that the biophysical characteristics of antibodies, such as charge distribution, hydrophobicity, and thermal stability, can significantly influence PK and overall in vivo behavior. Accordingly, we used a suite of assays to evaluate these physiochemical properties in parallel with a preclinical PK study in cynomolgus monkeys, thereby providing a comprehensive view of ARC101’s intrinsic molecular integrity. Our data demonstrated that ARC101 exhibited a long half-life in cynomolgus monkeys, consistent with an anticipated 3–4 week half-life in humans, along with high thermal stability, minimal aggregation propensity, and no evidence of charge-mediated nonspecific interactions or serum interference, confirming its strong molecular integrity and favorable developability profile. Furthermore, Retrogenix screening revealed no off-target binding across more than 6500 human proteins, corroborating the specificity observed in vitro. In addition, ex vivo PBMC-based immunogenicity assessment using CD134 expression and IFN- γ production suggested low immunostimulatory potential, with responses comparable to clinically approved antibodies with a low incidence of ADAs, providing an initial indication of a favorable immunogenicity risk profile. Broader cytokine profiling and further translational and clinical immunogenicity studies will be conducted at later stages of development to more rigorously evaluate the safety and ADA liability of ARC101. Finally, ARC101 maintained high purity and stability under stress conditions, supporting its suitability for long-term storage and compatibility with standard manufacturing processes. Collectively, these data support ARC101 as a developable CLDN6-targeting TCE with favorable preclinical properties for continued clinical evaluation.

In summary, ARC101 demonstrates strong potential as a differentiated CLDN6-specific targeting TCE, supported by comprehensive preclinical evidence. Its exquisite target specificity, potent anti-tumor activity in vitro and in vivo, and favorable biophysical properties provide a compelling rationale for its advancement into the ongoing first-in-human study (NCT06672185) in patients with advanced solid tumors, including ovarian cancer.

Materials and methods

CLDN6 immunohistochemistry of ovarian tumor tissues and tissue microarray analysis

FFPE blocks of ovarian tumor and healthy ovary tissues were obtained from Rajiv Gandhi Cancer Institute and Research Centre (Delhi, India), Amsbio, or OriGene. Tumor blocks were sectioned using an automated rotary microtome (Epredia HM 355S) at a thickness of 4–5 µm, placed in a warm water bath (40–50 °C) for 5 s to facilitate spreading and flattening, and then transferred onto poly-L-lysine-coated glass slides for air drying. An ovarian cancer TMA slide was purchased from Tissue Array/US Biomax, Inc. (Cat# OV2001b).

All FFPE and TMA slides were deparaffinized prior to antigen retrieval and staining. Antigen retrieval was performed with Antigen Unmasking Solution (Tris-based, pH 9.0; Vector Laboratories, Cat# H-3301–250) at 115°C for 15 min. Endogenous peroxidase activity was blocked with BLOXALL® Solution (Vector Laboratories, Cat# SP-6000) for 10 minutes, followed by blocking with 2.5% normal goat serum (Vector Laboratories, Cat# S-1012–50) for 20 min at room temperature. Sections were then incubated with the primary antibody against Claudin-6 (E7U2O) XP® Rabbit mAb (Cell Signaling Technology, Cat# 18932) for 60 min at room temperature, followed by secondary antibody incubation with goat anti-rabbit IgG (Vector Laboratories, Cat# MP-7451–50) for 30 min. Signal was developed with diaminobenzidine (DAB) substrate (Vector Laboratories, Cat# SK-4105) for 1–2 min, and sections were counterstained with hematoxylin (Vector Laboratories, Cat# H-3401–500). DAB deposition in tumor cell membranes was interpreted as positive immunostaining. Representative images were captured at 20× magnification.

Evaluation of CLDN6 positivity, defined as membranous staining of tumor cells, was performed by a pathologist using conventional light microscopy. Staining intensity was scored as 0 (none), 1+ (weak), 2+ (moderate), or 3+ (strong). The proportion of tumor cells with positive staining was recorded as: 0 (≤5%), 1+ (6–25%), 2+ (26–50%), 3+ (51–75%), and 4+ (>75%). A final score (range 0–12) was calculated by summing the products of staining intensity and distribution scores according to the following formula: total score = (0 × % of cells) + (1 × % of cells) + (2 × % of cells) + (3 × % of cells).

Proteomic analysis via mass spectrometry

Deep proteomic profiling of tumor and normal tissues was performed using established MS workflows. Briefly, tissues were lysed in buffer, heated to 95°C, and homogenized using a bead beater. Lysates were centrifuged at 20,000 × g for 10 min at 4°C, and the resulting supernatants were collected. Total protein concentrations were determined using the bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay, and samples were normalized to obtain equal protein concentrations for downstream analysis.

Proteins were reduced and alkylated with dithiothreitol, iodoacetamide, and tris(2-carboxyethyl)phosphine (TCEP), followed by trypsin digestion. The resulting tryptic peptides were loaded onto Evosep Evotips and desalted on-tip prior to analysis. Liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) was performed on an Evosep One nano-LC system coupled to a Bruker timsTOF HT mass spectrometer operated in data-independent acquisition (DIA)-PASEF mode. Peptide identification and quantification were conducted using Spectronaut software (version 19). A spectral library was generated from pooled digest samples analyzed in technical replicates, and peptide sequences were matched against the UniProt human proteome database. Protein group quantification was conducted using the iBAQ method as implemented in Spectronaut.

CLDN6-endogenous, -overexpressing, and -knockout cell lines

Human cancer cell lines (OVCAR3, PA1, OV90, HepG2, and SKOV3) and CHO cell lines were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC). Parental cell lines were transduced with Incucyte® NucLight Green or NucLight Red lentiviral particles to express a nuclear-restricted green or red fluorescent protein and maintained under antibiotic selection. CHO and SKOV3 cell lines stably overexpressing CLDN4, CLDN6, or CLDN9 were generated by transducing parental cells with lentiviral particles encoding the respective genes of interest. CLDN6 was knocked out in OVCAR3 cells using CRISPR technology, with successful knockout confirmed through Inference of CRISPR Edits analysis. All cell lines were maintained in a log-phase growth in complete culture medium at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO2 incubator and were confirmed to be free of mycoplasma contamination.

Anti-CLDN6 antibody discovery

SJL and BALB/c mice were immunized with a pVAX CLDN6 vector encoding the human CLDN6 ECD domain (UniProt ID: P56747). Antibody titers were assessed throughout the immunization course via flow cytometry using serial dilutions of immune serum on CHO-CLDN6-overexpressing cells and parental control cells. Animals with the highest test bleed titers were selected for B cell isolation and culture, followed by primary screening via flow cytometry on CHO-CLDN6 cells. Top binders were advanced to secondary screening via flow cytometry to verify CLDN6 specificity and evaluate cross-reactivity on CHO-CLDN9-overexpressing cells. Variable region sequences were recovered by cloning and sequencing. Heavy and light chain variable regions were then cloned into chimeric IgGs for mammalian expression, and culture supernatants were analyzed via flow cytometry to confirm recombinant antibody binding to CHO-CLDN6 and CHO-CLDN9 cells.

Anti-CD3 antibody discovery

Human IgG kappa-lambda transgenic mice (Ablexis®; AlivaMab, LLC.) were immunized with a human CD3εδ protein (AlivaMab) and primary human T cells (StemCell Technologies, Cat# 70024). Antibody titers were assessed throughout the immunization course via flow cytometry using serial dilutions of immune serum on parental Jurkat and Jurkat CD3-KO cells (ATCC). Animals with the highest test bleed titers were selected for hybridoma generation. Lymph nodes and spleens were pooled and processed for B cell enrichment, followed by electrofusion-based hybridoma generation. Resulting hybridomas were plated and screened via flow cytometry on parental Jurkat and Jurkat CD3-KO cells during the primary screen. Selected hybridomas were advanced to confirmatory binding screening using human pan T cells. Hybridoma subcloning and sequencing were then performed to obtain heavy and light variable region sequences, which were subsequently reformatted into scFvs in the VL-linker-VH orientation for bsAb construction with the anti-CLDN6 binder.

Affinity measurement and competition assay

Binding affinity to human CD3 was measured using a Biacore™ 8K SPR system (Cytiva). An anti-human Fc antibody was immobilized onto a Series S CM5 chip (Cytiva, Cat# BR100530) using standard amine coupling (Cytiva, Cat# BR100839). ARC101 was captured onto the sensor surface at a level of 200 RU, followed by injection of recombinant human CD3εδ (ACROBiosystems, Cat# CDD-H82W6) at concentrations ranging from 100 nM to 0.4 nM in 3-fold dilutions. Binding interactions were monitored with a 2-min association phase and a 2-min dissociation phase at a flow rate of 50 µL/min. Data were analyzed using Biacore™ Insight software (Cytiva, version 4.0.1) by fitting blank- and reference-subtracted sensorgrams to a 1:1 Langmuir model to determine the association rate constant (ka), dissociation rate constant (kd), and equilibrium dissociation constant (KD).

A binding competition assay was performed using the Biotin CAPture kit Series S (Cytiva, Cat# 28920234) via streptavidin capturing. Biotinylated human CD3 was injected for 30 s at a flow rate of 10 µL/min onto two individual flow cells. Subsequently, either 100 nM TAB-CD3 antibody or buffer was injected at 30 µL/min for 2 min over the CD3-coated surfaces. Immediately afterward, either 100 nM TAB-CD3 antibody or 100 nM SP34-CD3 antibody was injected for 2 min at 30 µL/min over the antibody-CD3-bound surface.

HDX-MS epitope mapping

A mixture of ARC101 and human CD3εδ (ACROBiosystems, Cat# CDD-H52W1) was prepared at a 1:1.3 molar ratio, with final concentrations of 10.5 µM and 8.1 µM. The deuterium exchange reaction was initiated by mixing 10 µL of the mixture or 10 µL of CD3 alone into 90 µL of a deuterium oxide labeling buffer (50 mM sodium phosphate, 100 mM sodium chloride, pH 7.0). Reactions were incubated for 0, 15, 60, 600, and 3600 s at 20°C and quenched by adding 100 µL of 4 M guanidine HCl in 0.85 M TCEP buffer. Quenched samples were subjected to on-column digestion with pepsin and prolyl endopeptidase, followed by LC-MS analysis using a Waters Acquity UPLC coupled to an Orbitrap Exploris™ 240 mass spectrometer (Thermo Scientific). Peptide identification was performed by searching MS/MS spectra against the human CD3 sequence database using Protein Metrics, with mass tolerance set to 10 ppm for precursor and 0.02 Da for product ions. HDX WorkBench software was used to calculate deuterium uptake levels by determining the mass differences between deuterated and non-deuterated peptides. Peptides showing a significantly reduced average deuterium uptake in the presence of ARC101 were identified as part of the epitope.

Mutagenesis epitope mapping

A panel of six CLDN6 mutants containing CLDN9-derived residue substitutions was generated for transfection into SKOV3 cells. Three mutants contained single substitutions corresponding to the amino acid differences between CLDN6 and CLDN9 (M29L, R145Q, Q156L), while the other three contained double substitutions (M29L/R145Q, M29L/Q156L, R145Q/R156L). Wild-type CLDN6 and wild-type CLDN9 were included as controls. All constructs were cloned into pcDNA3.1, sequence verified, and transiently transfected into SKOV3 cells using Lipofectamine 3000 (Invitrogen, Cat# L3000015). Anti-CLDN6 antibody clones 51H102 and 112H105 (100 nM) were incubated with each transfected cell line for 1 h at 4°C. After washing with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), bound antibodies were detected with PE-conjugated polyclonal goat anti-human IgG (Abcam, Cat# ab98596) for 1 h at 4°C. Samples were acquired on a Novocyte Flow Cytometer (Agilent Technologies) using the PE channel, and data were analyzed with NovoExpress software (Agilent Technologies, version 1.2.5). Antibody binding to mutants relative to wild-type CLDN6 was calculated by subtracting background binding from untransfected cells and normalizing to wild-type CLDN6 binding.

Flow cytometry analysis of CLDN6-expressing cell lines

OVCAR3, PA1, OV90, and HepG2 cells were grown in T175 or T75 tissue culture flasks to reach 70–80% confluence, dissociated with Accutase solution (Himedia #TCL075), and collected in culture medium. Cells were centrifuged, resuspended in cell staining buffer (BioLegend, Cat# 420201), and plated at 2.0 × 105 cells per well in 96-well plates with a prepared Live/Dead (L/D) staining solution (Live/Dead Dye – Zombie Yellow™ Fixable Viability Dye, 1:1000 dilution in 1× Dulbecco’s phosphate-buffered saline (DPBS), BioLegend, Cat# 77168). After incubation for 15–20 min at room temperature in the dark, cells were washed and resuspended in 100 µL/well cell staining buffer containing 300 nM phycoerythrin (PE)-labeled ARC101. Following incubation for 1 h at 4°C in the dark, cells were washed and resuspended in a cell staining buffer. Samples were acquired in the PE channel on a Novocyte Flow Cytometer (Agilent Technologies), and data were analyzed using NovoExpress software (Agilent Technologies, version 1.2.5). Gating was performed first on forward scatter versus side scatter (FSC vs. SSC), followed by exclusion of Zombie Yellow-positive population to identify live cells. Histograms were then plotted in the PE channel.

Binding to human pan T cells

PBMCs from five donors were thawed and centrifuged at 1200 rpm for 5 min at room temperature. Pan T cells were isolated using the EasySep Human T Cell Isolation Kit (StemCell Technologies, Cat #17951) and resuspended in L/D staining solution (Violet Fluorescent Reactive Dye in 1× DPBS; 1 µL/million/mL) for 20 min at 37°C. The reaction was stopped by adding twice the volume of fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer (2% fetal bovine serum (FBS, Gibco, Cat# 10091141) in PBS), followed by centrifugation at 1200 rpm for 5 min. Cells were resuspended in FACS buffer to 2 × 106 cells/mL and seeded at 1.0 × 105 cells per well in 96-well plates. ARC101 was added in a 10-point, 3-fold serial dilution ranging from 2000 nM to 0.102 nM. Plates were incubated at 37°C for 1 h, washed three times with FACS buffer, and incubated with Alexa Fluor 647-labeled AffiniPure F(ab′)2 Fragment goat anti-human IgG (Jackson ImmunoResearch, Cat# 109–606-098) for 30 min at 4°C. After washing three times with FACS buffer, cells were resuspended in 60 µL buffer per well and acquired on a Novocyte Flow Cytometer. Data were analyzed using NovoExpress software (version 1.2.5). Cells were gated on FSC vs. SSC, followed by exclusion of violet-positive cells to identify live cells. Median fluorescence intensity (MFI) values were extracted, background-subtracted using secondary antibody control wells, and plotted in Prism (GraphPad, version 10.4) using a four-parameter nonlinear regression.

Binding to CLDN4-, CLDN6-, or CLDN9-overexpressing cells

CHO or SKOV3 cells overexpressing CLDN4, CLDN6, or CLDN9 were dissociated with Accutase, collected in culture medium, resuspended in L/D staining solution (Violet Fluorescent Reactive Dye; Invitrogen, Cat# L34955A), and incubated for 15–20 min at 37°C. The reaction was stopped by adding twice the volume of FACS buffer, followed by centrifugation at 1200 rpm for 5 min at room temperature. Cells were resuspended in FACS buffer and seeded at 1.0 × 105 cells per well in 96-well plates. ARC101 was added in a 10-point, 3-fold serial dilution ranging from 666 nM to 0.034 nM. Plates were incubated for 1 h at 37°C, washed three times with FACS buffer, and incubated with PE-labeled goat anti-human IgG Fc (Abcam, Cat# ab9859) for 30 min at 4°C. After three washes, cells were resuspended in 60 µL of FACS buffer per well and acquired on a Novocyte Flow Cytometer. Data were analyzed in NovoExpress (version 1.2.5). Cells were gated on FSC vs. SSC, followed by exclusion of violet-positive cells to identify live cells. MFI values were extracted, background-subtracted using secondary antibody control wells, and plotted in Prism (GraphPad, version 10.4) using a four-parameter nonlinear regression.

Off-target binding analysis

Off-target binding interactions of ARC101 with human membrane and secreted proteins were assessed using Retrogenix® Cell Microarray Technology (Charles River Laboratories). A pre-screen was first performed to determine background binding of ARC101 to fixed untransfected HEK293 cells and HEK293 cells transduced with vectors encoding ZsGreen1 and either CD3 complexes (CD3ε, CD3ε+CD3δ, CD3ε+CD3γ) or CLDN6. Following background establishment, ARC101 and Ab946 (a mAb containing only the anti-CD3 binding arm of ARC101) were tested at 2 µg/mL against 6105 expression vectors encoding ZsGreen1 and full-length human plasma membrane, secreted, or tethered proteins, plus 400 human heterodimers, spotted in duplicate across cell microarray slides. Interactions identified in the primary screen were re-expressed and re-probed with ARC101 and Ab946 in a confirmation screen using both fixed and live cells. Binding was detected with an Alexa Fluor 647–labeled anti-human IgG Fc antibody (Jackson ImmunoResearch). Fluorescence images from Alexa Fluor 647 and ZsGreen1 were analyzed and quantified using ImageQuant software (GE Healthcare, version 8.2). Flow cytometry – based validation was subsequently performed on live cells using the same detection antibody. A 7AAD viability dye was used to exclude dead cells, and ZsGreen-positive (transfected) cells were gated for further analysis.

Cytotoxicity assay with SKOV3-CLDN4-, SKOV3-CLDN6-, SKOV3-CLDN9-, and OVCAR3-CLDN6-knockout cells

The CellTiter-Glo® Luminescent Cell Viability Assay was performed to evaluate ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity in SKOV3-CLDN4-, SKOV3-CLDN6-, SKOV3-CLDN9-, and OVCAR3-CLDN6-knockout, as well as parental SKOV3 and OVCAR3 cells. Cells were dissociated from flasks with Accutase, washed with 1× DPBS, resuspended in assay medium, and seeded at 3000 cells/well in 100 µL of assay medium (McCoy’s 5A supplemented with 10% FBS and 1× penicillin – streptomycin) in 96-well plates the day before assay initiation. On the following day, human pan T cells were added at 5000 cells/well in 50 µL of medium, followed by ARC101 in a 9-point, 4-fold serial dilution, with final concentrations ranging from 25 nM to 0.0004 nM for SKOV3 wells and from 100 nM to 0.0015 nM for OVCAR3 wells. Plates were incubated at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO2 incubator for 48 or 72 h. At the end of incubation, the medium was aspirated to remove T cells and dead target cells. To ensure complete removal, 100 µL of 1× DPBS was gently added, swirled, and aspirated. Next, 100 µL/well of CTG reagent was added, and plates were incubated in the dark at room temperature for 15 min on an orbital shaker (150 rpm) to induce cell lysis and stabilize the luminescent signal. Following incubation, 80 µL/well was transferred to opaque white plates, and luminescence was measured using a Tecan Spark multimode microplate reader. Cytotoxicity was expressed as percent lysis (% Lysis), calculated as: % Lysis = [(E+T RLU – Sample RLU)/(E+T RLU)] × 100, where E+T = effector plus target. Dose – response curves were generated in Prism (GraphPad, version 10.4) using a four-parameter nonlinear regression model.

Cytotoxicity assay with endogenous CLDN6-expressing cells

The IncuCyte® Cytotoxicity Assay was used to assess ARC101-mediated cytotoxicity in OVCAR3, PA1, OV90, and HepG2 cells. NucLight Green – transduced cells were seeded at 7500, 5000, or 3000 cells/well in 100 µL of medium in 96-well plates and incubated overnight at 37°C in a 5% CO2 incubator. Human pan T cells were resuspended in RPMI medium (Gibco, Cat# 11875-093) supplemented with 10% FBS (Gibco, Cat# 10099-141) and added at 50 µL/well at E:T ratios of 3:1, 1:1, and 1:3. ARC101 was tested in 9-point, 4-fold serial dilutions ranging from 100 nM to 0.0015 nM for OVCAR3, PA1, and OV90, or in 10-point, 4-fold dilutions ranging from 30 nM to 0.001 nM for HepG2. Plates were placed in an IncuCyte chamber (Sartorius) at 37°C, 5% CO2, and scanned every 6 h up to 24 h, then every 12 h until 72 h. Time-lapse phase-contrast and fluorescence images were analyzed with IncuCyte software. Fluorescence intensity was measured as integrated intensity per well (GCU × µm2/well) and used to calculate % cytotoxicity as: % Cytotoxicity = 100 – [(Sample/Average Control) × 100], where control = E:T-only wells without antibody. The percentage of cells killed in antibody-treated samples was determined relative to the corresponding control. Data were exported to Prism (GraphPad, version 10.4) for plotting and curve fitting.

T cell activation and cytokine release measurements

For T cell activation and cytokine release assays, plates were prepared at a 1:1 E:T ratio as described above. ARC101 was tested in 10-point, 4-fold serial dilutions ranging from 30 nM to 0.0001 nM. Supernatants collected at 72 h were assayed for IFN-γ, TNF-α, and IL-2 using Luminex kits (R&D Systems, Cat# LXSAHM-05) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Samples were acquired on a Bio-Plex 200 system (Bio-Rad) and analyzed using Bio-Plex software. Remaining T cells were harvested, stained with Fixable Near-IR Dead Cell Stain (1 µL/mL in HBSS) and Fc block (5 µL per 100 µL), and incubated for 15 min at 4°C. After washing with FACS buffer, cells were resuspended in 100 µL of FACS buffer containing 2 µL each of PE – anti-human CD4 (BioLegend, Cat# 300539), AF647–anti-human CD8 (BioLegend, Cat# 344726), and BV785–anti-human CD25 (BioLegend, Cat# 302638). Cells were incubated for 30 min at 4°C, washed twice, and resuspended in 60 µL/well of FACS buffer. Samples were acquired on a Novocyte flow cytometer and analyzed with NovoExpress software. Cells were gated on FSC vs. SSC, followed by exclusion of APC-Cy7–positive cells to identify live cells. The % CD25+ values for CD4+ and CD8+ T cells were extracted and plotted in Prism (GraphPad, version 10.4) using a four-parameter nonlinear regression model.

In vivo xenograft study in mice

Female NSG (non-obese diabetic [NOD] severe immunodeficiency [SCID] gamma chain deficient (il2rg null)) mice (Jackson Laboratories, USA) aged 6–8 weeks and weighing 18–23 g were used. OVCAR3 cells were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 20% FBS (v/v), 0.01 mg/mL insulin, and 1% antibiotic – antimycotic, maintaining > 90% viability. On the day of implantation, cells at 70–80% confluency were detached with 0.25% trypsin – EDTA for 5 min at 37°C, washed with RPMI, and centrifuged at 900 rpm. Cell pellets were resuspended in serum-free RPMI-1640, counted using an automated cell counter, and mixed 1:1 with Matrigel to yield a final concentration of 5 × 106 cells/100 µL. Each mouse was injected subcutaneously in the dorsal right flank under mild isoflurane anesthesia (2.5% v/v). Tumor growth was monitored until volumes reached 100–150 mm3 before initiating treatment. To humanize the immune system, human pan T cells were expanded ex vivo using the T cell Activation/Expansion Kit (Miltenyi Biotech, Cat# 130–091-441). After 3 days of culture, cells were maintained in RPMI supplemented with 10% FBS and 20 IU/mL recombinant human IL-2, replenished every 2 days. On the day of engraftment, CD3 beads were removed using magnets (MACSiMAG, Miltenyi, Cat# 130–092-168), and T cells were resuspended in PBS at 1.0 × 106 cells/100 µL for intraperitoneal injection. Mice bearing approximate 100 mm3 OVCAR3 tumors were randomized into four groups (n = 8–10/group): vehicle control (PBS, 10 mL/kg) or ARC101 treatment groups (0.01, 0.03, or 0.1 mg/kg). Doses were administered intraperitoneally 30 min after T cell implantation and weekly for 4 weeks. Tumor growth inhibition (%TGI) was calculated relative to the vehicle group. Body weight and clinical signs were monitored throughout the study.

Pharmacokinetic study in tumor-bearing mice

The PK profile and dose – response of ARC101 were evaluated in OVCAR3 tumor-bearing mice. Animals bearing approximately 100 mm3 tumors were randomized into four groups (n = 3/group) and administered a single intraperitoneal dose of ARC101 at 3, 0.1, 0.03, or 0.01 mg/kg. Blood samples were collected at 0 (pre-dose), 1, 6, 24, 72, and 144 h post-dose. Body weight and tumor volume were recorded throughout. ARC101 serum concentrations were measured by ELISA. Plates were coated with goat F(ab′)2 anti-human kappa antibody (Southern Biotech, Cat# 2063–01) for capture and detected with horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated goat anti-human Fcγ IgG (Southern Biotech, Cat# 2014–05). A standard curve (0.05–50 ng/mL) was used to quantify serum concentrations. Data were acquired on a SpectraMax Plus 384 spectrophotometer (Molecular Devices) using SoftMax Pro 7 GxP software (v7.1.1). PK parameters were calculated by non-compartmental analysis (NCA) with Phoenix WinNonlin (v8.2).

Pharmacokinetic study in cynomolgus monkeys

Male cynomolgus monkeys (n = 3/dose group; 4.47–6.22 kg) naïve to prior biologics were administered a single intravenous dose of ARC101 at 1 or 10 mg/kg. Blood was collected at 0, 1, 24, 48, 96, 168, 240, 336, and 504 h post-dose. Samples were allowed to clot for 30 min at room temperature, centrifuged at 1500 × g for 10 min, followed by serum storage at −80°C. Body weight and clinical observations were recorded on study days 8, 15, and 22. ARC101 serum concentrations were measured using a commercial ELISA kit (Life Diagnostics, Cat# IGG-20) following the manufacturer’s instructions, with solid-phase immobilization by affinity-purified goat anti-human IgG and detection by HRP-conjugated anti-human IgG. A standard curve (0.781–50 ng/mL in 10% monkey serum) was generated. Study samples were diluted 10- to 1000-fold in 10% monkey serum to fall within the assay’s quantitative range. Data were acquired on a SpectraMax i3X spectrophotometer (Molecular Devices) using SoftMax Pro 7 GxP software (v7.2). PK parameters were determined using NCA with Phoenix WinNonlin (v8.2).

Immunogenicity assessment

A DC and CD4+ T cell restimulation assay was established to evaluate ARC101-specific T cell responses using PBMCs from 30 human donors of diverse ethnic backgrounds (African American, Caucasian, Asian, Hispanic, and Pacific Islander; Hemacare). DC generation: CD14+ monocytes were isolated from the CD3- depleted PBMC fraction using the EasySep Human Monocyte Isolation Kit (StemCell Technologies, Cat# 19,359). Monocytes were differentiated into immature DCs in serum-free medium (StemXVivo® DC Base Medium, R&D Systems, Cat# CCM003) supplemented with 1000 IU/mL GM-CSF (Miltenyi Biotec, Cat# 130–093-866), 1000 IU/mL IL-4 (Miltenyi Biotec, Cat# 130–093-922), and 50 µg/mL gentamicin (Thermo-Fisher Scientific, Cat# 15710064). Cells were cultured in 24-well plates for 5 days at 37°C and 5% CO2, with a medium change on day 3. Immature DCs were harvested by adding cold DPBS and incubating on ice for 15–30 min. Antigen stimulation and maturation: DCs were seeded at 2.0–3.0 × 106 cells/500 µL per well in 48-well plates and incubated for 4 h at 37°C with 0.6 µM ARC101, bevacizumab (MedChem, Cat# HY-P9906), trastuzumab (MedChem, Cat# HY-P9907), 240 µg/mL keyhole limpet hemocyanin (KLH), or 2 µg/mL CEFTA (Mabtech, Cat# 3617–1). DC maturation was then induced using a cocktail containing TNF-α (Miltenyi Biotec, Cat# 130–094-024) and IL-1β (Miltenyi Biotec, Cat# 130–093-898), followed by a 40–42 h incubation. Co-culture with CD4+ T cells: in parallel, CD4+ T cells were isolated from the same donor PBMCs using the Human CD4+ T Cell Isolation Kit (StemCell, Cat# 17952) and co-cultured with mature DCs at a 1:10 ratio (DC:T cell) for 6 days at 37°C. Co-cultures were restimulated with a second round of antigen-pulsed monocytes for 40–42 h. Flow cytometry: triplicate wells were analyzed for CD4+ T cell activation and intracellular IFN-γ expression using VioBlue anti-CD4 (Miltenyi Biotec, Cat# 130–114-534), AF647 anti-CD134 (BioLegend, Cat# 350018), and BV785 anti-IFN-γ (BioLegend, Cat# 502542) antibodies. Data analysis: the stimulation index (SI) was calculated as the percentage of positive cells in treated wells divided by the mean percentage in untreated control wells. Statistical analysis was performed using Prism (GraphPad, v10.4). SI and responder frequency p-values were determined by the Wilcoxon matched-pairs signed rank test and Fisher’s exact test, respectively. A 90%/95% tolerance interval was applied to the low-immunogenicity control bevacizumab, establishing SI thresholds of 1.84 for CD4+ T cell activation and 1.78 for IFN-γ to define positive responders.

Biophysical characterizations

Expression and purification

ARC101 was expressed in ExpiCHO-S™ cells (Thermo Fisher Scientific) by co-transfection of three plasmid DNAs at a ratio of 1×Knob, 2×Hole, and 3×LC, following the ExpiCHO Max Titer Transfection Protocol and the manufacturer’s instructions. Cells were maintained at 0.25–6.0 × 106 cells/mL with ≥95% viability and incubated at 37°C with 8% CO2 and shaking at 100 rpm. Cultures were harvested on day 12 by centrifugation, and supernatants were clarified through a 0.22-µm membrane filter. The filtrate was purified by Protein A affinity capture (MabSelectSure XK 16/20 column), followed by size exclusion chromatography (HiLoad 26/600 Superdex200). Protein concentration was determined using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. Purity was assessed by SDS-PAGE and SEC-HPLC, identity was confirmed by LC-MS, and endotoxin levels were determined with an Endosafe® Kinetic Chromogenic Cartridge. The final product exhibited > 99% monomer content, sequence verification, and endotoxin levels < 1 EU/mg.

Thermostability assessment by NanoDSF

Thermostability was assessed by NanoDSF using a Prometheus NT.48 instrument (NanoTemper). ARC101 was diluted to 0.25 mg/mL in PBS (pH 7.2), loaded in duplicate into standard-grade capillaries (NanoTemper PR-C002), and heated with a linear temperature ramp (1 °C/min, 25–87 °C). Fluorescence from intrinsic tryptophan residues (excitation 280 nm; emission 330/350 nm) was recorded. Unfolding onset (Ton) and melting temperature (Tm) values were calculated using PR.ThermControl software.

Nonspecific binding by SPR

Nonspecific binding was evaluated on a Biacore™ T200 instrument (Cytiva). Lysozyme (Roche, Cat# 10837059001) and soybean trypsin inhibitor (Roche, Cat# 10109886001) were immobilized on individual flow cells of a Series S CM5 chip using standard amine coupling. ARC101 (1000 nM in HBS-EP+ buffer) was injected at 20 µL/min for 2 min of association. Anti-lysozyme (Rockland, Cat# 200–401-072) and anti-soybean trypsin inhibitor (Abcam, Cat# ab34549) antibodies served as positive controls. Surfaces were regenerated with two injections of 0.85% phosphoric acid for 60 s at 20 µL/min between binding cycles.

Serum interference by BLI

Binding of ARC101 to human CD3εδ (ACROBiosystems, Cat# CDD-H82W6) in the presence of serum was measured on an Octet RED384 system (Sartorius). Biotinylated CD3 was immobilized on streptavidin biosensors (Sartorius, Cat# 18–5019) for 600 s at 1000 rpm. Sensors were dipped into ARC101 dilutions (500–0.7 nM, 3-fold series) prepared in PBS-T (PBS +0.1% BSA +0.05% Tween) or 90% human serum (Sigma, Cat# H4522). Association and dissociation were monitored for 600 s at 1000 rpm. Data were reference- and blank-subtracted and fitted to a 1:1 Langmuir model.

Accelerated stability

Stability was assessed under thermal stress (37 °C) versus real-time conditions (25 °C) over 2 weeks. ARC101 was concentrated to 50 mg/mL in 50 mM phosphate buffer (pH 7.2) and aliquoted. Samples were incubated and analyzed on days 7 and 14 for total protein content (UV absorbance) and aggregation (SEC).

Forced degradation

ARC101 (10 mg/mL) was subjected to stress conditions: five freeze – thaw cycles, 6 h at room temperature in 10 mM acetate buffer (pH 3.5), and 1 week at 40°C in 10 mM phosphate buffer (pH 8.5). Purity, monomer, and aggregate content were analyzed by SEC. A sample incubated at pH 8.5 was additionally analyzed by LC-MS peptide mapping to assess deamidation.
