# Identification of 68 HLA-A24 and -A2-restricted cytotoxic T lymphocyte-inducing peptides derived from 10 common cancer-specific antigens frequently expressed in various solid cancers.

## Abstract

Targeting cancer antigens expressed in cancer cells is necessary to develop cancer-specific immunotherapy. We have performed immunohistochemical analysis of various solid cancer specimens, adding ROBO1, AFP, TGFBI, EphB4, CLDN1, and LAT1 to the previously studied glypican-3 (GPC3), HSP105α, FOXM1, and SPARC, and found that these 10 common cancer antigens are sufficient to cover most solid cancers. These antigens were frequently expressed in various solid cancers but shown to be rarely ex-pressed, with some exceptions, in non-cancerous normal organs adjacent to the cancer. In this study, we predicted 72 and 73 peptides that bind to HLA-A24 and -A2 in silico from the full-length amino acid sequences of these 10 common cancer antigens and immunized each HLA transgenic mouse with a cocktail of synthesized peptides together with the poly I:CLC three times weekly to analyze the antigen-specific immune response. As a result, 68 peptide sequences (30 and 38, respectively) were identified that had higher cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) induction ability than GPC3 298-306 and GPC3 144-152 used in the clinical trials. Furthermore, experiments with cocktail peptide vaccines using mouse models expressing subcutaneous tumors of each antigen showed promising results in terms of safety and efficacy. These peptides identified in this study, derived from 10 common cancer antigens covering all solid cancers, are expected to be clinically applicable as cocktail peptide vaccines.

Introduction

Clinical trials using glypican-3 (GPC3)-derived peptides have confirmed the safety and immunological efficacy of the vaccine, mainly in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), hepatoblastoma, etc. In clinical trials, peptide-specific CTL reactions have been demonstrated in the peripheral blood of 80 % of patients treated and at the cancer site in some patients. Although the frequency was low even in advanced cancer, we have certainly experienced several successful cases, and have demonstrated the possibility that it can suppress recurrence after surgery for HCC that expresses GPC3 and contribute to long-term survival. On the other hand, we have experienced several patients whose HCC did not express GPC3 recurred despite the detection of many peptide-specific CTLs in the blood after HCC surgery. Thus, although GPC3 is highly expressed in many HCCs, the expression of the antigen in cancer cells is not uniform, making it difficult to prevent HCC recurrence with a single GPC3 peptide vaccine. It is also clear that a single peptide vaccine for GPC3 is insufficient to prevent the recurrence of HCC. As a notable result, seven children with refractory childhood cancer, including five with refractory hepatoblastoma who had repeated relapses and remissions, all survived more than 10 years without relapse using only the GPC3 peptide vaccine. Thus, although the GPC3 peptide vaccine has not been approved as a drug, it shows that if a vaccine targeting a cancer antigen that is properly expressed in cancer cells is developed, it can be expected to be effective. Many clinical trials of cancer vaccines have failed but were the cancer antigens targeted in those trials properly expressed by cancer cells? GPC3 is expressed in some childhood cancers such as HCC, hepatoblastoma, nephroblastoma, and germ cell tumors, as well as ovarian clear cell carcinoma, lung squamous cell carcinoma, and some esophageal cancers, but rarely expressed in other cancers. We have collected 10 types of common cancer antigens, such as GPC3, which are highly cancer-specific and frequently expressed in various cancer types. In this study, we synthesized 145 peptides predicted to bind to HLA-A*02:01 or -A*24:02 derived from these 10 common cancer antigens. We tested whether these peptide vaccines could induce peptide-specific CTLs in human HLA-expressing mice. As a result, 68 novel CTL epitope peptides were successfully identified. This indicates that these 10 common cancer antigens are highly immunogenic and can be expected to be developed as peptide and mRNA vaccines. Cancer vaccines using multispecies antigens highly expressed in a wide range of cancer types could apply to many cancer patients and overcome antigen heterogeneity in cancer.

Materials and methods

Antigen peptides

Using an algorithm for predicting HLA class I-restricted epitope peptides, peptides that bind to HLA-A24 or HLA-A2 were predicted from peptides with sequences common to the human and mouse homolog sequences of the full-length amino acid sequences of 10 common cancer antigens. For HLA class I restricted peptides (short peptides), 10-20 types of 9-10 mer amino acid peptides were selected for each antigen based on the highest binding score from the algorithm results. A 20-30 mer amino acid peptide (long peptide) containing a short peptide sequence capable of inducing CTLs was designed by adding 1-6 mer sequences to both ends of the short peptide. Biologica performed peptide synthesis. Synthetic peptides were dissolved in Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO; Wako) at 10 mg/ml and used for experiments. The 145 peptides used are shown in Supplements Tables 3 and 4. Details of the peptides among them that induced peptide-specific CTLs are shown in Supplements Tables 1 and 2.

Mice

HLA-A2 transgenic mice (HLA-A2 Tgm) are transgenic mice with HLA-A2HHD (a chimeric molecule consisting of human β2m, HLA-A2(α1+α2) and H2-Db (from α3 to cytoplasmic region)) in which H2-Db and mouse β2-microglobulin (β2m) are knocked out in C57BL/6 background, and HLA-A24 transgenic mice (HLA-A24 Tgm) are transgenic mice with HLA-A24, human β2m and human CD8 genes in which H2-Db and H2-Kb are knocked out in C57BL/6 background. These mice were created by Dr. Lemmonier and his colleagues at the Institut Pasteur and were kindly provided to us under the MTA. These mice were bred under contract in Sankyo Labo Service Corporation, Inc. The required number of animals was delivered to the SPF area in the Experimental Animal Facility at the National Cancer Center for Advanced Medical Science for use in experiments. Mice aged 6-25 weeks, both male and female, were used for the experiments. The Ethics Committee approved this animal experiment for Animal Experiments of the National Cancer Center for Advanced Medical Care. It was conducted following the guidelines for animal experiments at the National Cancer Center.

Peptide vaccine and collection of mouse spleen cells

During screening evaluation, peptides to be immunized were divided into groups of 5-6 peptides, each with close predictive binding scores. Polylysine and carboxymethylcellulose (Poly I:CLC, Hiltonol), a TLR3 agonist produced by Oncovir, USA, was used as an immunostimulant (adjuvant) for the peptide vaccines, introduced under MTA. Each peptide 50 μg and 16 μg Poly I:CLC were mixed immediately before administration, adjusted with 5 % glucose injection solution (Otsuka Pharmaceutical), and applied to both sides of the HLA-A2 Tgm or HLA-A24 Tgm ridge using a 27 G needle/1 ml syringe (Terumo). Vaccines were administered intradermally at a volume of 100 µL every week for three doses, and spleens were removed one week after the last vaccine. The excised spleen was frosted to separate splenocytes, hemolyzed, and filtered with a 70 μm filter. Then it was placed in the final medium (10 % FBS/1 % L-glutamine/streptomycin-penicillin/NEAA/10 mM HEPES/Sodium RPMI 1640 with pyruvate/2-ME) and used for experiments.

IFN-γ ELISPOT assay

According to the manufacturer's protocol, IFN-γ produced by antigen-specific CTLs was detected using the IFN-γ ELISPOT kit (BD Biosciences). In brief, 2 × 106 mouse splenocytes were seeded in each well containing 1 μg/mL of each peptide and the suspensions were incubated for 20 h. The plates were washed and stained with biotinylated secondary antibody and HRP‐conjugated streptavidin. IFN‐γ was detected as spots using a chromogenic substrate. The number of spots was measured using an ELIPHOTO counter (Minerva Tech). The protocols used were reported previously.

Intracellular staining assay

IFN-γ intracellular staining (ICS) followed standard methods. Briefly, 2 × 106 splenocytes were cultured for 12 h in the presence of 1 μg/mL of each peptide, and then 2 μM monensin (Sigma-Aldrich) was added for another 8 h. Dead cells were blocked and stained, and cell surface markers were stained with monoclonal antibodies against CD3 (145-2C11), CD8α (53-6.7), and CD4 (RM4-5) (BioLegend). Stained cells were fixed in PBS containing 4 % PFA and incubated in permeabilization buffer (containing 50 mM NaCl, 5 mM EDTA, 0.02 % NaN3, and 0.5 % Triton X (adjusted to pH 7.5)) at 4 °C, for 10 min. After incubation in PBS containing 3 % BSA for blocking, the cells were stained with anti-IFN-γ mAb (XMG1.2) and evaluated. Flow cytometry data were acquired using a FACSCanto II system (BD Biosciences) and analyzed using Flow-Jo software (Tree Star, Ashland, Oregon, USA).

Tumor cell lines for ELISPOT assays

MCA205 (mouse fibrosarcoma cell line of C57BL background) and MC38 (mouse colorectal carcinoma cell line of C57BL background) were used to test the recognition by T cells in ELISPOT assays. Cells were maintained in RPMI 1640 with 10 % demobilized (56°C, 30 min) FBS in a humidified incubator at 37°C, 5 % CO2. MCA205 and MC38 were stably transfected with HLA-A02HHD or HLA-A24HHD by lentiviruses using vectors CSII-CDF-MCS-PRE containing neoR component. Human common cancer antigens (human GPC3 var2 (RefSeq: NM_004484), human EphB4 (RefSeq: NM_004444), human CLDN1 var1 (RefSeq: NM_020384), human AFP var1 (Genbank: BC027881), human HSPH1 var1 (RefSeq: NM_006644), human FOXM1 (Genbank: BC012863), human SPARC (RefSeq: NM_003118), human TGFBI (Genbank: M77349), human ROBO1 varX6 (RefSeq: XM_006713277), human LAT1 (RefSeq: NM_003486) and mock control were further transfected stably by lentiviruses using the same vector containing puroR component and used in the experiment.

Tumor cell lines for in vivo transplantation

H2-D1 and H2-K1 were knocked out from MCA205 cell line by introducing 3xSTOP codons (TGATGATGA) after the codons for Thr20 of H2-D1 and Thr17 of H2-K1 using CRSPR-Cas9 system. MCA205 clones, which lost both H2-D1 and H2-K1, were stably transfected with HLA-A2402 and human B2M by lentiviruses using vectors CSII-CDF-MCS-PRE containing neoR and hygroR components, respectively. Human EphB4 and human CLDN1 var1 and mock control were further transfected stably by lentiviruses using the same vector containing puroR component.

Expression analysis of cancer antigens

mRNA expression of cancer antigens was analyzed by real-time qPCR using TaqMan probes and 7500 Fast (Thermo Fisher). Cancer antigens expressed on the cell surface were analyzed by flow cytometry using the FACSCanto II system (BD Biosciences). Antibodies used were anti-hGPC3-PE (Novus Bio., NBP2-47760PE), anti-hEphB4-PE (R&D, FAB3038P) and anti-hClaudin-1-AF647 (R&D, FAB4618R). Cancer antigens expressed in the cells were analyzed by western blotting. Cells were lysed in RIPA buffer (20 mM Tris (pH7.4), 150 mM NaCl, 1 % NP40, 3 mM EDTA, 0.1 % SDS, 0.1 % sodium deoxycholate) and separated by SDS-PAGE. Antibodies used in IHC and anti-GAPDH (Santa cruz, sc-32233) were used for detection and visualized by chemiluminescence using Clarity western ECL substrate and ChemiDoc (BIO-RAD).

Mouse tumor model

MCA205 cells (5 × 105), whose MHC was modified to compatible with HLA-Tg mice and transduced with EphB4 and CLDN1, were suspended in 100μL saline and injected subcutaneously into HLA-Tg mice. Mice were sacrificed 14 days after tumor cell administration, and spleens were used for ELISPOT assay, and other normal organs and transplanted tumors were removed for safety evaluation by IHC.

IHC analysis of mouse subcutaneous tumors and various normal organs

For the excised subcutaneous tumors and various normal organs, 4 μm paraffin sections were prepared from formalin-fixed paraffin blocks, and IHC was performed. Normal organs were stained with CD8, and tumors were stained with CD8, and EphB4, CLDN1 for tumors expressing each antigen. The specimens were made into virtual slides using a NanoZoomer.

Clinical samples

The subjects of this study were Stage 2–4 head and neck cancer, breast cancer, esophageal cancer, lung cancer, gastric cancer, liver cancer, biliary tract cancer, pancreatic cancer, colorectal cancer, renal cell carcinoma, ovarian cancer, uterine cancer, various pediatric cancers (neuroblastoma, germ cell tumor, nephroblastoma: Wilms tumor, hepatoblastoma), and melanoma cases surgically resected at the National Cancer Center Hospital East, Kyoto Prefectural University of Medicine Hospital, Chiba University Hospital, and Kumamoto University Hospital from January 1, 2000 to August 31, 2024, as well as Stage 1 gastric cancer cases endoscopically resected at the National Cancer Center Hospital East. Among these, cases in which thin-section specimens could be prepared from formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded blocks were the subjects of this study. The study was conducted with the approval of the research ethics committees of each institution (approval number 2020-352 from the National Cancer Center Research Ethics Committee).

To facilitate comparison and analysis, we used specimens that contained cancerous and non-cancerous areas in the same block. Written informed consent was obtained from the patients, and the Declaration of Helsinki was used to conduct the study.

Immunohistochemical analysis of expression of 10 kinds of common cancer antigens and HLA class I

Immunohistochemical analysis was performed on 4 μm paraffin sections prepared from formalin-fixed paraffin blocks of human cancer tissues. Commercially available microarray slides of normal tissues were used (BioChain Institute.). Deparaffinization was performed using xylene and ethanol, and endogenous peroxidase was inactivated using 0.3 % H2O2/methanol. Antigen retrieval was performed using Target Retrieval Solution, pH 9 (Agilent Technologies) or heat treatment in a pressure cooker or microwave in AR6 (Akoya Biosciences) inactivation buffer. After blocking with porcine serum, primary antibodies against each antigen were reacted. Glypican-3 (BIOMOSICS®GPC3, 300-fold dilution), α-fetoprotein (Agilent Technologies®AFP, Ready to Use), roundabout guidance receptor 1 (Proteintech® ROBO1, 300-fold dilution), Ephrin type-B receptor 4 (Cell Signaling Technology® EphB4, 300-fold dilution), Claudin 1 (Cell Signaling Technology® CLDN1, 300-fold dilution), Forkhead Box M1 (abcam® FOXM1, 150-fold dilution), heat shock protein 105α (abcam or NovusBio® HSP105α, 200-fold dilution), Secreted Protein Acidic and Rich in Cysteine (Santa Cruz Biotechnology® SPARC, 250-fold dilution), Transforming growth factor beta induced (abcam® TGFBI, 100-fold dilution), HLA class I (Hokudo®HLA-AB, 200-fold dilution), L-type amino acid transporter 1 (abcam® LAT1, 200-fold dilution). The secondary antibody was used as polymer reagent (EnVision+ System- HRP Labelled Polymer Anti-Rabbit or EnVision+ System- HRP Labelled Polymer Anti-Mouse). Liquid DAB+ Substrate Chromogen System was used for color development. After counterstaining with hematoxylin, the specimens were dehydrated and cleared using ethanol and xylene, and the specimens were made into virtual slides using a NanoZoomer (Hamamatsu Photonics).

The intracellular localization of tumor tissue was also evaluated by immunohistochemical analysis. The presence and localization (whether cell membrane or not) of staining were evaluated in tumor cells for each case. Three researchers analyzed immunohistochemical staining with these methods.

Results

Identification and confirmation of expression of common cancer antigens by IHC staining

We discovered the cancer-specific antigen glypican-3 (GPC3) and identified peptides that can induce cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) in an HLA-A24 or -A2-restricted manner and conducted clinical trials of peptide vaccines for hepatocellular carcinoma, ovarian clear cell carcinoma, and pediatric cancer. However, cancer has heterogeneity, and it is not possible to eradicate cancer by targeting only one antigen. In fact, in clinical trials of GPC3 peptide vaccines, the vaccine was administered to prevent recurrence after hepatocellular carcinoma resection, but we experienced several cases of GPC3-negative cancer recurrence. Therefore, in this study, we challenged ourselves to collect some common cancer antigens, including GPC3, expressed in a wide range of solid cancers but only in limited amounts in normal tissues.

In this study, we performed immunohistochemical analysis of various solid cancer specimens using antibodies against ROBO1, TGFBI, AFP, EphB4, CLDN1, and LAT1 in addition to GPC3, HSP105α, FOXM1, and SPARC, which we have previously studied. Immunostaining was performed using formalin-fixed paraffin sections, including cancerous and non-cancerous areas, from a total of 385 cases of advanced cancer that had been surgically resected. The breakdown of the 385 cases is as follows: 57 cases of head and neck cancer (21 oral cancer, 36 pharyngeal cancer), 36 breast cancer, 16 esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, 72 lung cancer (45 adenocarcinoma, 27 squamous cell carcinoma), 29 gastric cancer (including 19 endoscopically resected early stage 1 gastric cancer), 20 hepatocellular carcinoma, 30 biliary tract cancer (10 intrahepatic bile duct cancer, 10 extrahepatic bile duct cancer, 10 gallbladder cancer), 13 pancreatic cancer, 15 colorectal cancer, 19 renal cell carcinoma, 18 ovarian cancer (17 serous adenocarcinoma, 1 clear cell carcinoma), 7 uterine cancer, 20 melanoma (7 ALM, 5 mucosal mucous, 5 SSM, 3 LMM), 33 childhood cancer (9 hepatoblastoma, 8 neuroblastoma: Wilms tumor, 10 nephroblastoma, 6 germ cell tumor). Representative examples of the expression patterns of 10 common cancer-specific antigens are shown in Fig. 1.These antigens were frequently expressed in various solid cancers but shown to be rarely expressed, with some exceptions, in non-cancerous normal organs adjacent to the cancer (Fig. 2). And, for CTLs to be effective, HLA class I must be expressed on the surface of cancer cells. It was revealed that most solid cancers, except for lung adenocarcinoma and pediatric cancer, frequently express HLA class I on the cell membrane (Fig. 3).

Immunostaining was performed on formalin-fixed paraffin sections, including cancerous and non-cancerous areas, from a total of 385 cases of surgically resected advanced cancer. Representative examples of the expression patterns of the 10 common cancer-specific antigens in each cancer type are shown in Fig. 1, and the number of cases analyzed and the positive rate are shown in Table 1.

It was revealed that ROBO1 is mainly expressed in cytoplasm, and when cases with cell membrane expression patterns were also included, it was surprisingly frequently expressed in all cancers. HSP105α is mainly expressed in the cytoplasm, and when the pattern of expression in the cell nucleus is also taken into account, the positive rate was high at 80–100 %, except for renal cell carcinoma (63 %) and breast cancer (69 %), and the positive rate was 90 % overall. FOXM1 is a nuclear protein that was not expressed at all in hepatocellular carcinoma, was also expressed in 33 % of hepatoblastoma, 35 % of all lung cancers, and had particularly low expression rates of 18 % in lung adenocarcinoma, 53 % in breast cancer, and 58 % in renal cell carcinoma. However, in other cancers, the positive rate was high at 75-100 %, and the overall rate was 69 %. SPARC was mainly expressed in the cytoplasm of CAFs. Still, the positive rates were somewhat lower in melanoma (60 %), hepatocellular carcinoma (72 %), hepatoblastoma (67 %), and esophageal cancer (75 %), with the remaining positive rates ranging from 83 % to 100 % and 89 % overall. TGFBI stains interstitial collagen fibers in both cancer and normal tissues, giving the impression that it has a ubiquitous expression pattern at first glance. Still, it is hardly expressed in normal tissue cells, whereas in the cytoplasm of cancer cells, although not as frequently as other antigens. AFP is a well-known antigen as a blood tumor marker for hepatocellular carcinoma, pediatric hepatoblastoma, and germ cell tumors. It is also a secreted protein that is expressed in the cytoplasm. It was expressed in 30 % of hepatocellular carcinoma, 57 % of hepatoblastoma, and 50 % of germ cell tumor, and was positive in one case of gallbladder cancer, but was not expressed in other cancers, and the positive rate was only 9 % across all cancer types examined. GPC3 is expressed in 60 % of hepatocellular carcinoma, 100 % of hepatoblastoma, 90 % % of nephroblastoma: Wilms tumor, 50 % of germ cell tumor, 37 % of lung squamous cell carcinoma, and ovarian clear cell carcinoma. It was also positive in 25 % of pharyngeal cancer, 13 % of esophageal cancer, 11 % of early gastric cancer, 30 % of gallbladder cancer, 24 % of ovarian serous adenocarcinoma, and one case each of oral cancer, lung adenocarcinoma, colon cancer, uterine cancer, neuroblastoma, and melanoma. The overall positive rate was only 19 %. CLDN1 was that the positive rate of 32 % for early gastric cancer was low compared to 80 % for advanced gastric cancer. On the other hand, the positive rate was 25 % for melanoma, 16 % for renal cell carcinoma, only 2 % for breast cancer, and 0 % for neuroblastoma, resulting in an overall positive rate of 64 %. Next, EphB4 showed high positive rates in 100 % of oral cancer, 92 % of pharyngeal cancer, 83 % of breast cancer, 94 % of esophageal cancer, 79 % of lung cancer, 100 % of gastric cancer (including 89 % of early gastric cancer), 80 % of biliary tract cancer, 75 % of pancreatic cancer, 70 % of hepatocellular carcinoma, 93 % of colorectal cancer, 83 % of ovarian cancer, 86 % of uterine cancer, 89 % of hepatoblastoma, and 65 % of melanoma. Finally, LAT1 positivity rate was 21 % for early stage gastric cancer compared to 80 % for advanced gastric cancer. The other positive rates were 22 % for hepatoblastoma, 16 % for renal cell carcinoma, and 0 % for hepatocellular carcinoma, resulting in an overall positive rate of 58 %.

CTLs induced by cancer vaccines and T cells genetically engineered with TCR recognize cancer antigen-derived peptides presented by HLA class I on the surface of cancer cells and damage the cancer cells. Therefore, cancer vaccines and TCR-T are ineffective against cancer cells that do not express HLA class I as a membrane protein on their surface. On the other hand, if the cancer antigen is also expressed on normal cells, and those normal cells express HLA class I on their surface, CTLs and TCR-T will also damage the normal cells, resulting in adverse events. Next, we investigated the expression patterns of HLA class I in the cancerous areas and adjacent normal organs of a total of 364 cases of surgically resected advanced cancer, head and neck cancer, breast cancer, esophageal cancer, lung cancer, gastric cancer, hepatocellular carcinoma, biliary tract cancer, pancreatic cancer, colorectal cancer, renal cancer, ovarian cancer, uterine cancer, melanoma, and pediatric cancer (Fig. 3 and Supplementary Table 5). The left side of Fig. 3 shows representative staining images of HLA class I for each of the three expression patterns (membrane, cytoplasm, no expression) of each cancer type, and the frequency of the three expression patterns of HLA class I for each cancer type is shown in Supplementary Table 5. The right side of Supplementary Fig. 5 shows stained images of HLA class I in cancerous areas as representative examples of cell membrane expression in each cancer type and adjacent non-cancerous normal organs. As can be seen, the expression pattern of HLA class I in normal organs is mostly cytoplasmic or has no expression, probably to avoid a CTL attack. Hepatic cells in which granular expression of HSP105α was observed and pancreatic acinar cells in which CLDN1 expression was observed do not express HLA class I or the expression is much weaker than that in cancer cells, so it is thought that there is a low risk of damage by CTL or TCR-T induced by cancer vaccines. On the other hand, stained images of HLA class I on the cell membrane were observed in the basal layer cells of the squamous epithelium of the pharynx and esophagus, mucosal epithelial cells of the digestive tract, and stratified squamous epithelium of the skin, so caution is required when administering vaccines or TCR-T with antigens that are expressed in these cells.

Prediction of epitope peptides of common cancer antigens and screening with vaccines and identification of peptides that induce antigen-specific immune responses

Epitope peptides were predicted in silico for the 10 common cancer antigens identified in Fig. 1 using an algorithm for predicting neoantigen epitopes (Fig. 4A). As the short peptides derived from common cancer antigens predicted to bind to HLA were HLA-A24 and HLA-A02, 145 kinds were derived (Supplementary Tables 3, 4), and these were synthesized. Next, we screened whether these antigenic peptides could induce specific CTLs using transgenic mice transfected with the HLA gene. As a method, five to six epitope peptides were selected from peptides predicted to bind to HLA-A*24:02 or HLA-A*02:01, pooled, and vaccinated in HLA-Tg mice (Fig. 4B). Pooled peptides were combined in the order of their binding score values to ensure that there was no competition between peptides when mixed and to prevent binding inhibition due to immunodominance.

IFN-γ production of lymphocytes adjusted from spleens removed one week after the final vaccine was evaluated by ELISPOT assay. Representative results obtained after vaccination are shown in Fig. 4C; GPC3 545-553, EphB4 47-55, and EphB4 581-589 were spot confirmed, indicating antigen-specific induction of CTLs. On the other hand, FOXM1 377-385 showed almost no spots, indicating that this peptide did not elicit an immune response. A similar screen was performed on all 145 peptides predicted to bind to HLA and synthesized to evaluate whether they could induce antigen-specific CTLs. Of these 145 peptides, 38 out of 73 HLA-A02-binding peptides and 30 out of 72 HLA-A24-binding peptides, in total 68 peptides were identified as short peptides with CTL-inducing ability (Supplementary Tables 1, 2). The results of the ELISPOT assay using these CTL-inducing peptides are shown in Fig. 4D: HLA-A02, Fig. 4E: HLA-A24.

The number of CTLs activated by antigen-specific IFN-γ production was quantified using an ELIPHOTO counter (Supplementary Fig. 1 A: HLA-A02, B: HLA-A24). The number of peptides with higher scores than the peptides used in the clinical trials (HLA-A02-restricted GPC3 144-152, HLA-A24-restricted GPC3 298-306) was 38 for HLA-A02 restriction (Fig. 4D) and 30 for HLA-A24 (Fig. 4E). By using these peptides in vaccines, higher immune responses and antitumor effects can be expected. Among the peptides predicted to bind to HLA in silico, the peptides that induced CTLs were approximately 42 % for HLA-A24 restriction and approximately 52 % for HLA-A02 restriction. This positive rate was almost the same as when predicting neoantigen epitope peptides.

Verification of immunogenic peptides that endogenously present antigens by long peptide vaccines

In vaccine therapy, it is important from the standpoint of immunogenicity that the peptides are taken up by antigen-presenting cells and undergo intracellular processing for antigen presentation. Therefore, we examined whether short peptides could be processed during the presentation to HLA class I using long peptides (Supplementary Fig. 2A). CTLs activated by antigen presentation and immune responses were evaluated using the IFN-γ ELISPOT assay (Supplementary Fig. 2B). Screening results identified peptides that showed reactivity to the vaccinated long peptide and short peptides with epitope sequences contained in the sequence: the peptide-specific CTL induction was confirmed in 12 out of 17 HLA-A02-restricted long peptide and 14 of 22 HLA-A24-restricted long peptides (Supplementary Fig. 2C, D). Since long peptides may induce helper T cells and CTLs after vaccination, flow cytometry was used to analyze the percentage of activated CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells in splenocytes of mice vaccinated with long peptides (Supplementary Fig. 2E). For some long peptides, we confirmed that the peptides induced antigen peptide-specific activated CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells, as shown in Supplementary Fig. 2. In addition, many of the long peptides that showed activity were predominantly CD8+ T cell immune responses, indicating that they are likely to be epitope peptides suitable for induction of CTLs, i.e., peptides processed into short peptides. The epitope peptides predicted from the amino acid sequences of the common cancer antigens in this study were shown to contain many CTL epitope peptides recognized by CD8+ T cells, suggesting that vaccination with these peptides is likely to be a powerful inducer of specific CTLs. The results of this study suggest that these peptides are likely to be a powerful inducer of specific CTLs.

Evaluation of the safety and efficacy of a cocktail vaccine with a mixture of multiple peptides using a mouse model of antigen-expressing subcutaneous tumors

As the next step, we tested a cocktail vaccine mixing peptides derived from 10 common cancer antigens to confirm whether these peptide-specific CTLs recognize peptides presented on HLA class I on the surface of cancer cells and show antitumor effects. Human HLA-A24-expressing mice are on a C57BL/6 background, but they have complex MHC genetic modifications, and transplantation of C57BL/6-derived cancer cell lines is rejected. We have successfully generated a cell line that can be engrafted into human HLA-A24-expressing mice by genetically modifying the C57BL/6-derived cancer cell line, MCA205, with the same MHC as in HLA-A24-expressing mice. The cell lines were transfected with each human common cancer antigen, and mock cell lines were generated and used in the experiment as negative control cells. Among the peptides that were confirmed to induce CTLs by screening, peptides with higher immunoreactivity and anti-tumor effect than the clinical trial peptides were selected, and a cocktail vaccine combining these peptides was made. Fig. 5 shows representative results of the cocktail vaccine. To determine whether the EphB4/CLDN1 cocktail vaccine elicited cancer-specific immune responses, the IFN- γ ELISPOT assay (Fig. 5B). The results showed that the cocktail vaccine of three EphB4-derived peptides could induce each peptide-specific CTL, confirming an immune response against each peptide. Discussion

We first selected promising common cancer antigens. The criteria for selection were that the antigens must be expressed in multiple solid tumors with little or very limited expression in normal tissues. As a result, 10 common cancer antigens were determined as antigens for vaccines and other applications. As membrane proteins, glypican 3 (GPC3) is highly expressed in HCC, ovarian clear cell carcinoma, lung squamous cell carcinoma, esophageal carcinoma, melanoma, hepatoblastoma, nephroblastoma, and yolk sac tumor; roundabout guidance receptor 1 (ROBO1): highly expressed in breast cancer, pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, HCC, etc. Eph receptor B4 (EphB4): highly expressed in prostate cancer, breast cancer, head and neck cancer, colon cancer, esophagus cancer, skin cancer, pancreatic cancer, lung cancer, etc. claudin-1 (CLDN1): highly expressed in ovarian cancer, lung cancer, liver cancer, colorectal cancer, oral squamous cell carcinoma, melanoma, etc. LAT1 (L-type amino acid transporter 1): highly expressed in melanoma, lung cancer, and colon cancer, were candidates. In addition, as intracellular proteins, alpha-fetoprotein (AFP): highly expressed in testicular cancer and HCC, forkhead box M1 (FOXM1): highly expressed in breast cancer and HCC, secreted protein acidic and rich in cysteine (SPARC): highly expressed in breast cancer, glioblastoma, melanoma, etc., transforming growth factor beta induced (TGFBI): highly expressed in oral squamous cell carcinoma, cholangiocarcinoma, nephroblastoma, ovarian cancer, rectum cancer, cervix cancer, bladder cancer, etc. heat shock protein 105 (HSP105): highly expressed in colon cancer, pancreatic cancer, esophageal cancer, etc. were candidates.

Common cancer antigens are said to be less immunogenic than neoantigens, and antigen expression is localized and heterogeneous. On the other hand, the fact that they do not need to be studied in each patient as neoantigens do reduces time and financial costs. Thus, identifying epitope peptides targeting common cancer antigens that exhibit strong CTL induction potential is a major advantage in vaccine therapy. Furthermore, re-stimulation of mouse splenocytes vaccinated with long peptides containing the identified peptide sequences (Supplementaly Fig. 2A) with long and short peptides showed strong IFN-γ production in CD8 positive cells (Supplementary Fig. 2C–E). Thus, some of the long peptides underwent a series of processing after uptake by antigen-presenting cells. They were presented as antigens by HLA class I, suggesting that they are highly immunogenic. It was also interesting that several peptides were identified more potent in inducing CTLs than the epitope peptides we used in our GPC3-targeted clinical trials. Since multiple peptides with potent CTL induction ability against each common cancer antigen were obtained, these peptides are expected to be applied to cocktail vaccines. This is because peptide vaccines targeting only one type of cancer antigen, as described above, are difficult and insufficient to fight cancer. On the other hand, cocktail vaccines incorporating multiple common cancer antigen epitope peptides can be expected to overcome antigen heterogeneity of cancer and provide more potent anti-tumor immune responses, including prophylactic and postoperative adjuvant effects. They can apply to many cancer types.

Cocktail vaccine experiments using a mouse model of antigen-expressing subcutaneous tumors resulted in results suggesting the efficacy and safety of cocktail vaccines. Initially, there was concern that mixing multiple peptides into a vaccine would cause competition between peptides due to their HLA class I binding affinity. Peptides with higher affinity could interfere with the binding of peptides with lower affinity, reducing the level of peptide presentation required for CTL recognition. As a result, the ELISPOT assay confirmed an immune response to each peptide in the cocktail. The combination of peptides in the cocktail suggests that peptides can induce CTLs without competitive inhibition. We also examined antigen-expressing tumors implanted in mice and confirmed that antigen-specific CD8-positive cells infiltrated the tumors. The cocktail vaccine was shown to activate multiple antigen-derived peptide-specific CTLs, which accumulated within tumors that highly expressed the antigen. Furthermore, the safety of the cocktail vaccine was confirmed by staining normal organs of the vaccinated mice with CD8. Compared to non-vaccinated mice, no significant CD8 accumulation was observed, indicating a high level of safety.

We are currently working on generating HLA-knock-in mouse cancer cell lines by endogenously expressing 10 common cancer antigens through genetic recombination. We will conduct cocktail vaccine experiments in therapeutic and prophylactic models using HLA-Tg mice carrying these cancer cells to develop safe and effective vaccines. To prevent competition between peptides and immunogenicity inhibition, we must pay attention to the combination of peptides to be mixed in the cocktail. Based on the binding affinity of the peptide HLA, the peptides to be combined must be selected to determine the optimal combination of peptides derived from each cancer antigen that will elicit an immune response. In addition, since the expression pattern and frequency of antigens differ depending on the cancer type, it is possible to design antigen peptides to be used in cocktails according to the cancer type, thereby providing patient-specific cocktail vaccines.

Proper selection of immunostimulatory adjuvants is important to enhance the immune response and for safe vaccine development. The purpose of adjuvants is to elicit and activate immune responses. At the same time, however, side effects and cytotoxicity must also be considered, and from this perspective, non-inflammatory adjuvants are considered desirable. Poly I:CLC, used in this study, functions as a TLR3 agonist that can improve immune responses to vaccines by stimulating dendritic cells and improving antigen presentation. Intradermal administration of vaccines has also been reported to be useful. Intradermal administration is expected to improve immunogenicity and is safe, potent, and specific. Intradermal administration has been demonstrated to improve immunogenicity, be safe, and induce a strong and specific immune response. In the future, we will continue to optimize the combination of peptides used in cocktail vaccines, the selection of adjuvants, and the method of administration to develop effective vaccines against various solid tumors.

Although mRNA vaccines were originally developed as cancer vaccines, the success of the COVID-19 mRNA vaccine has made them a promising platform for cancer immunotherapy, with advantages such as high immunogenicity, safety of administration, and rapid development. This study indicates that these 10 common cancer antigens are highly immunogenic and can be expected to be developed as peptide vaccines and mRNA vaccines. Developing an mRNA vaccine that encodes many epitope sequences using the epitope peptides derived from the common cancer antigens identified in this study is possible. The advantage of an mRNA vaccine is that it can encode the full-length cancer antigen so that antigen-presenting cells can simultaneously present or cross-present many epitopes in both HLA class I and class II of all human beings and can induce various types of CTL and helper T cells. In other words, there is no restriction based on human HLA type, and it can stimulate a broader range of T cell responses. Furthermore, in our mRNA vaccine of membrane protein common cancer antigen, the induced antibodies and neutralizing antibodies may also contribute to the antitumor effect. We have produced mRNA encoding the full-length proteins of 10 common cancer antigens and are conducting experiments and validating the effectiveness of the mRNA vaccine using HLA-Tg mice.
