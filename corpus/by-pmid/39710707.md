# Nanomedicines harnessing cGAS-STING pathway: sparking immune revitalization to transform 'cold' tumors into 'hot' tumors.

## Abstract

cGAS-STING pathway stands at the forefront of innate immunity and plays a critical role in regulating adaptive immune responses, making it as a key orchestrator of anti-tumor immunity. Despite the great potential, clinical outcomes with cGAS-STING activators have been disappointing due to their unfavorable in vivo fate, signaling an urgent need for innovative solutions to bridge the gap in clinical translation. Recent advancements in nanotechnology have propelled cGAS-STING-targeting nanomedicines to the cutting-edge of cancer therapy, leveraging precise drug delivery systems and multifunctional platforms to achieve remarkable region-specific biodistribution and potent therapeutic efficacy. In this review, we provide an in-depth exploration of the molecular mechanisms that govern cGAS-STING signaling and its potential to dynamically modulate the anti-tumor immune cycle. We subsequently introduced several investigational cGAS-STING-dependent anti-tumor agents and summarized their clinical trial progress. Additionally, we provided a comprehensive review of the unique advantages of cGAS-STING-targeted nanomedicines, highlighting the transformative potential of nanotechnology in this field. Furthermore, we comprehensively reviewed and comparatively analyzed the latest breakthroughs cGAS-STING-targeting nanomedicine, focusing on strategies that induce cytosolic DNA generation via exogenous DNA delivery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, or dynamic therapies, as well as the nanodelivery of STING agonists. Lastly, we discuss the future prospects and challenges in cGAS-STING-targeting nanomedicine development, offering new insights to bridge the gap between mechanistic research and drug development, thereby opening new pathways in cancer treatment.

Introduction

The immune system, as the body’s most formidable shield against invading pathogens, conducts vigilant and comprehensive surveillance through the innate and adaptive immunity. By distinguishing “self” from “non-self”, it eradicates pathogenic microorganisms, senescent cells, apoptotic cells, and other foreign substances, safeguarding the body from external and internal threats. Beyond infection defense, the immune system plays a critical role in tumor prevention, progression, and metastasis. It not only regulates the effects of traditional cancer treatments but also serves as a cornerstone for emerging anti-tumor immunotherapies. In recent years, immunotherapy has rapidly emerged as a major therapeutic modality in oncology. Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) and chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR-T) cells have achieved unprecedented success in treating both solid and hematological cancers by manipulating adaptive immunity. Nevertheless, adaptive immunity-based cancer therapies do not function independently but must initially rely on the innate immunity to detect, regulate, and eliminate tumor cells to fully activate the immune system. Among the various pathways activating innate immunity, the cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) synthase (cGAS)-stimulator of interferon genes (STING) pathway stands out as the critical mechanism responsible for detecting cytoplasmic DNA (cytoDNA) and triggering immune responses. DNA, the genetic code for all organisms—from viruses to humans—serves as a crucial target for the immune system identify invading pathogens. In healthy eukaryotic cells, DNA is strictly confined to the nucleus and mitochondria. However, when foreign pathogens strike, viral DNA can invade host cells. Under certain clinical conditions, even the organism’s own DNA may leak into the cytoplasm, where it is detected by cGAS, inducing autoimmune responses to eliminate potential threats. This signaling cascade, sparked by DNA and guided by the innate immune response, culminates in the full activation of adaptive immunity, becoming a central force in maintaining the body’s defense network. Since its breakthrough discovery in 2013, the cGAS-STING pathway has unfolded a compelling story, revealing its growing importance in host defense, autoimmune disorders, and cancer biology, with ongoing research continuing to reveal its broader significance. 

Nowadays, tapping into the cancer-fighting potential of the cGAS-STING pathway to break through the barriers of the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) has emerged as a transformative strategy in cancer immunotherapy. A feasible approach is to deliver exogenous DNA (exDNA) directly or stimulate the production of endogenous cytoDNA. It is well-established that chemotherapy, radiation therapy (RT), and dynamic therapies can cause DNA damage and cytoplasmic leakage in tumor cells, showing great potential to activate the cGAS-STING pathway and induce a robust immune response. Another promising strategy is the direct activation of STING, with numerous STING agonists currently under developed for cancer immunotherapy. Regrettably, both strategies encounter inevitable obstacles in practical application, which significantly limit their clinical efficacy. First, their in vivo journey has faced hurdles such as instability, rapid clearance, and poor cellular uptake, ultimately preventing their effective accumulation at the target site. Moreover, exDNA, chemotherapy, RT and STING agonists all carry risk, including unexpected autoimmune disorders or toxicity to healthy tissues resulting from off-target effects. The convergence of nanotechnology and cancer medicine has sparked a wave of innovation, introducing novel materials and ideas that are transforming the field. On one hand, nanotechnology, with its strong capabilities in targeted delivery, offers an intelligent solution for applying both approaches in cancer treatment: enhancing the in vivo pharmacokinetic properties of drugs, improving their targeting precision towards tumor tissues and even tumor cells, which not only boosts efficacy but also reduces toxicity. On the other hand, multifunctional nanomaterials not only extend cGAS-STING activation through sustained and controlled release but also serve as platforms to deliver DNA repair inhibitors and immune-stimulating metal ions into tumor cells, enhancing cGAS-STING activation from multiple dimensions. With these innovations, nanomedicine is poised to become a pivotal force in reshaping cancer immunotherapy.

In recent years, nanotechnology has emerged as a promising tool for activating the underactive cGAS-STING pathway in the TME, driving notable advancements in cancer immunotherapy. However, despite these rapid developments, a comprehensive review is notably absent to consolidate these developments, which underscores the urgent need for a thorough analysis to synthesize current progress and offer clear guidance for future studies. To make the rapidly evolving field of cancer immunotherapy more accessible, this review unravels the molecular mechanisms of the cGAS-STING pathway and its powerful anti-cancer effects. We highlighted promising cGAS-STING-targeted therapeutics and reviewed their current clinical progress. Additionally, we systematically summarized the unique advantages of nanomedicine in this field, emphasizing how advanced nanotechnologies enhance their therapeutic potential. Next, we delved into cGAS-STING activation via nanomedicines, highlighting two critical perspectives: how nanomaterials promote the accumulation of cytoDNA and how nanocarriers improve the delivery of STING agonists. We explored the different mechanisms behind cytoplasmic dsDNA production, organizing the research into categories such as exogenous dsDNA delivery, chemotherapy, RT, and nanodynamic therapies, along with other methods to induce endogenous dsDNA. Additionally, we examined nanomedicines that precisely target STING depending on the type of agonist, including nucleoside and non-nucleoside STING agonists, immunostimulatory metal ions, STING-activating heterocycles, epigenetic drugs, and STING agonist-based cancer nanovaccines with particular emphasis (Scheme 1). After thoroughly reviewing these strategies, we conducted a secondary analysis based on STING activation methods, cancer-immunity cycle (CIC) phases, indications, and their advantages and limitations to further clarify their clinical translation potential. In closing, we summarized the potential and challenges that nanomaterials present as a novel strategy for cancer immunotherapy via cGAS-STING pathway activation, aiming at offering valuable insights into the future of this evolving field.

The cGAS-STING pathway

Molecular mechanism of the cGAS-STING pathway

DNA, the primary repository of biological information, is confined to specialized compartments of human cells, segregated from other cellular constituents. These compartments, found as the cell nucleus or endosymbiotic organelles (i.e., mitochondria), can specialize and compartmentalize the control of DNA functions and cellular processes, thereby regulating biological activities like gene expression, genome replication, and the repair of damaged DNA. Over time, cellular responses have evolved to sense exDNA signals in compartments where normally do not contain DNA and to activate downstream immune responses. This ability has become a key player in the innate defense, allowing for rapid reactions to invaders like viruses and bacteria. Interestingly, mislocalized endogenous cytoDNA can also activate these same pathways, driving cell-autonomous responses even in the absence of exogenous infection. 

Endogenous cytoDNA primarily consists of four types: micronuclei (MN), cytoplasmic chromatin fragments (CCF), mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA), and retrotransposon. Due to mitotic errors or flawed nuclear DNA (nDNA) repair, missegregated chromosomes can recruit their own nuclear envelope to form MN. Initially, they are encased in a nuclear membrane, keeping them isolated from the cytoplasm. However, defects in nuclear lamina assembly can trigger catastrophic nuclear envelope collapse, exposing the DNA to the cytoplasmic environment. CCF emerges when chromatin escapes through gaps in a weakened nuclear membrane, escaping into the cytoplasm—a telltale sign of a cell entering its twilight, as aging slowly erodes its structural integrity. MtDNA typically resides safely within the mitochondrial matrix. Upon mitochondrial stress, pro-apoptotic proteins BAX and BAK are activated and form macropores in the outer mitochondrial membrane, resulting in mitochondrial outer-membrane permeabilization (MOMP) and subsequent mtDNA leakage. Meanwhile, reduced TFAM expression, which disrupts mtDNA packaging, organization, and distribution, also promotes mtDNA release. Additionally, the opening of the mitochondrial permeability transition pore (mPTP) and changes in mitochondrial network dynamics contribute to mtDNA release. Retrotransposons are the majority class of transposable elements (TEs) present in the human genome. The long-interspersed nuclear element (LINE) 1 family, as one specific type, includes the only endogenous autonomous retroelements that retain the capacity to autonomously retrotranspose, which can generate cytoplasmic L1 cDNA through two possible mechanisms. In one scenario, the accumulation of large amounts of L1 mRNA and L1 proteins in the cytoplasm may enable reverse transcription to occur without a conventional DNA template, thereby leading to an increase in cytoplasmic cDNA elements. Alternatively, during target-primed reverse transcription (TPRT) in nucleus, the generated cDNA may fail to reintegrate into the genome and instead exit the nucleus into the cytosol through an unknown mechanism. 

All forms of cytoDNA converge on similar signaling pathways, the most significant being the cGAS-STING pathway, with cGAS acting as the vigilant DNA sensor (Fig. 1). Human cGAS, a nucleotidyl transferase, consists of an unstructured, non-conserved N-terminus (130–150 residues), and a C-terminus containing the Mab21 domain with a highly conserved zinc-ribbon. The N-terminus of cGAS enables sequence-independent DNA binding activity, allowing it to recognize nearly all dsDNA by detecting the phosphate-ribose backbone structure. The C-terminal catalytic domain contains three DNA binding sites, referred to as “site A”, “site B”, and “site C”. Site A plays a major role in inducing conformational changes in cGAS upon interaction with dsDNA, while sites B and C assist in dsDNA binding and cGAS dimerization. The zinc-ribbon at the C-terminus binds Zn2+ to maintain the advanced structure and physiological function of cGAS, thereby promoting dsDNA-cGAS interactions and stabilizing the cGAS-dsDNA complex. In its resting state, cGAS is anchored to the cell membrane through its N-terminal domain binding to plasma membrane lipids, preventing unwanted activation by trace amounts of cytoDNA. Upon detecting abnormal accumulation of cytoDNA, cGAS translocates from the membrane to the cytosol. Through extensive electrostatic interactions and hydrogen bonds, dsDNA binds to cGAS, inducing conformational changes and forming a 2:2 dimer or a higher-order complex, thereby activating cGAS and rearranging its catalytic pocket. Once activated, cGAS catalyzes the formation of a phosphodiester bond between adenosine 5’-triphosphate (ATP) and guanosine 5’-triphosphate (GTP) to produce cGAMP, a second messenger that binds to STING in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). Additionally, cGAS functions independently of the STING signaling axis, serving as a negative regulator of homologous recombination-mediated DNA repair within the nucleus, accelerating the formation of micronuclei and cell death under genomic stress. 

Human STING is a transmembrane protein consisting of the transmembrane domain (TMD), the cytoplasmic ligand-binding domain (LBD), and the C-terminal tail (CTT). Physiologically, STING exists as a dimer, with its stability maintained through intramolecular and intermolecular interactions between its domains. Both the C-terminal part of STING that contains the LBD and the CTT face the cytosol, creating an “open” V-shaped binding pocket. The LBD can bind to the two phosphate groups of cGAMP, resulting in a conformational change in the C-terminal. Subsequently, the V-shaped dimer adopts a tighter “closed” conformation and forms a “lid” covering the cGAMP binding site. Such conformational change initiates the translocation of STING from the ER–Golgi intermediate compartment (ERGIC) to the Golgi. After reaching the Golgi, STING undergoes palmitoylation at two cysteine residues (Cys88 and Cys91) on its N-terminus, which promotes its interaction with and activation of TANK-binding kinase 1 (TBK1). 

TBK1 is a central kinase that directly phosphorylates interferon regulatory factor 3 (IRF3), thereby upregulating the expression of IFN-I. The eight amino acid residues (369–377) in the CTT of STING serve as the TBK1 binding group, which is critical to promoting the binding of STING to TBK1. Higher-order oligomerization of the STING-TBK1 complex further enhances the cis-autophosphorylation of TBK1 and the phosphorylation of nearby STING. The phosphorylated LXIS motif (363–366) in STING forms an IRF3-binding site that recruits IRF3 and enables TBK1 to phosphorylate IRF3 as well. Upon phosphorylation, IRF3 forms a tight dimer structure. The dimerized IRF3 then translocates into the nucleus, where its N-terminal DNA -binding domain attaches to DNA, initiating the transcription of downstream genes and driving the expression and secretion of IFN-I. Concurrently, TBK1 activates NF-κB, a transcription factor with multiple regulatory functions. Once translocates into the nucleus, NF-κB collaborates with IRF3 to stimulate robust gene expression of various cytokines, including IFN-I, interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α). 

Through the meticulously orchestrated signaling cascade, cytoDNA triggers a robust immune response via the cGAS-STING pathway, which is primarily designed to defend against internal and external threats and maintain homeostasis. However, when the innate immune system goes unchecked, it can backfire, leading to autoimmune disorders. To prevent this, the body has devised intricate safeguards—mechanisms that either intercept self-DNA before it triggers a response or shut down signaling pathways afterward. These built-in safeguards, covering the key nodes of the entire pathway, ensure that the immune system remains a protector, not a threat. Firstly, it starts with upstream defenses—dsDNA, a potential trigger, is swiftly cleared away by enzymes like cytosolic 3’ repair exonuclease 1 (TREX1), lysosomal DNase II, and adenosine deaminase 2 (ADA2), preventing cGAS from initiating an immune response. Meanwhile, mtDNA is cleverly kept out of sight, hidden inside the mitochondria, far away from cGAS. Secondly, cGAS is carefully controlled by being tethered to specific locations. On the plasma membrane, it’s anchored to PtdIns(4,5)P2, limiting its access to its own DNA, while in the nucleus, it’s bound to nucleosomes and chromatin, further reducing the risk of misactivation. Thirdly, even when cGAS is activated, the second messenger it produces, cGAMP, is swiftly exported by proteins like ABCC1 and degraded by ectonucleotide pyrophosphatase/phosphodiesterase 1 (ENPP1), a membrane-bound protein with a catalytically active domain projecting into extracellular space, to prevent excessive immune responses. As the signal moves downstream, more control layers come into play. Activated STING, the key component in this pathway, is quickly transferred from the Golgi apparatus to the lysosome, where the signal is effectively shut down. This precise choreography ensures that the cGAS-STING pathway only responds when necessary, preventing runaway immune reactions and keeping autoinflammatory diseases at bay.

The cGAS-STING pathway in cancer immunotherapy

The cGAS-STING pathway exerts diverse effects on tumor immunogenicity, affecting tumor cell-autonomous responses and interactions among tumor cells, immune cells, and other tissue cells. This dynamic interaction significantly influences tumor initiation and progression and plays a crucial role in promoting both natural and therapy-induced anti-tumor immunity (Fig. 2).

The cGAS-STING pathway conducts immune surveillance on abnormal cells with malignant potential (precancerous cells). Precancerous cells with chromosomal abnormalities or genomic DNA damage often form micronuclei or cytosolic chromatin fragments, which serve as initiators of the cGAS-STING pathway cascade. IFN-I, the end product of the cGAS-STING pathway, is a component of the senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) that effectively activates the p53-p21 axis and increases the level of p16INK4a, further promoting and sustaining the secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines, chemokines, and growth factors by precancerous cells. SASP promotes cell senescence by activating the p53 or p16-retinoblastoma protein pathway, imposes permanent cell cycle arrest, and prevents the proliferation of precancerous cells. Besides, SASP factors released by precancerous cells help recruit immune cells, stimulating a local immune response, that promotes the clearance of precancerous cells. In malignant tumor cells, the cGAS-STING pathway demonstrates the ability to induce apoptosis through key apoptosis-regulating factors like Noxa and Puma. Activation of the cGAS-STING pathway induces transcriptional activation of IRF3 and p53, which in turn upregulate Noxa and Puma expression, promoting tumor cell apoptosis. 

Beyond its direct impact on tumor cells, the cGAS-STING pathway casts a wide net, deeply shaping the behavior of diverse immune cells. In the tumor immune microenvironment, immune cells sense signals from tumor cells, activating their own cGAS-STING pathway which sparks a response that either enhances their own costimulatory signals to maintain an activated immune state or releases cytokines and chemokines to recruit nearby immune cells, shaping the strength and focus of the immune response. Tumor-derived dsDNA can enter adjacent dendritic cells (DCs) through membrane fusion or be released into the extracellular space following tumor cell apoptosis. Once bound to the LL37 protein, the dsDNA forms a dense complex that enters antigen-presenting cells (APCs) like DCs through a non-receptor-dependent endocytosis pathway. Inside APCs, the dsDNA escapes the endosome and activates the cGAS-STING pathway, signaling DCs to mature and initiate an immune response. Meanwhile, cGAMP from tumor cells can be transferred to neighboring DCs via gap junctions or a special transporter (SLC19A1), acting as a messenger to further stimulate STING. The cGAS-STING activation drives DCs maturation, increasing the expression of molecules like MHC class II and CD80/CD86, which enable DCs to present tumor antigens to specific T cells. Simultaneously, IFN-I produced by cGAS-STING in both tumor cells and DCs triggers the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-12, TNF-α, IFN-γ) and chemokines (CXCL9, CXCL10) that attract cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) to the tumor site, transforming immunosuppressive “cold” tumors into active, T-cell-rich “hot” tumors. Additionally, the cGAS-STING pathway enhances the cytotoxicity of natural killer (NK) cells. On one hand, IFN-I induces the upregulation of NKG2D, an NK cell receptor ligand on the surface of tumor cells, prompting the recruitment of NKG2D+ NK cells; on the other hand, NF-κB activation promotes NK cells to secrete tumor-killing effector molecules such as perforin and granzyme, strengthening their ability to eliminate tumor cells. 

Notably, the anti-tumor efficacy of cGAS-STING extends beyond its direct impact on tumor cells and immune cells, playing a pivotal role in regulating tumor angiogenesis and promoting vascular normalization in the TME. Activation of cGAS-STING in tumor vascular endothelial cells leads to an increase in the expression of IFN I/II genes, vascular stabilization genes (including Angpt1, Pdgfrb, and Col4a), and adhesion molecule genes (including Icam, Vcam, and Sell), all of which contribute to the normalization of tumor vasculature. The restoration of vascular structure facilitates immune cells crossing the endothelial barrier, promoting T cell migration and tumor-specific CTL infiltration. Furthermore, normalized tumor vasculature improves oxygen supply, alleviating tumor hypoxia, which helps to reverse the immunosuppressive microenvironment and further strengthens the immune clearance of tumor.

In summary, the cGAS-STING pathway has been identified as a critical innate immune pathway, which described as the master regulator of the cancer immunity cycle. Upon activated by DNA, cGAS-STING triggers extensive and profound immune responses that either propel the tumor immunity cycle forward or remove barriers to its proper function, ultimately impacting nearly all aspects of antitumor immunity—from suppressing malignant cell transformation to promoting immune cell proliferation and infiltration. Consequently, the cGAS-STING pathway has emerged as one of the most exciting and promising targets in immuno-oncology.

The cGAS-STING-dependent anti-tumor drugs

With deeper exploration of cGAS-STING, its significant potential in activating cancer immune responses and generating anti-tumor effects has become more evident. Motivated by the clear and growing demand for new immunotherapies centered around cGAS-STING, numerous researchers are developing cGAS-STING agonists and racing to translate them into clinical (Table 1). The cGAS-STING pathway presents two pivotal druggable targets: cGAS and STING. Additionally, inhibiting ENPP1 to prevent cGAMP from degradation offers promising complementary as well. This section will explore existing cGAS-STING agonists from these three perspectives, showcasing their characteristics and analyzing the challenges in their clinical applications. Finally, the discussion highlights how nanotechnology can leverage its advantages to help these agonists overcome barriers and advance toward clinical application. 

 cGAS agonist 

Despite cGAS activation more closely mirrors the endogenous STING pathway, it has been largely underexplored, with the development of cGAS agonists lagging far behind the rapid advancements seen with STING agonists. This disparity may be rooted in the inherent challenges of nucleic acid delivery and the intricate complexity of cGAS activation. Although some free dsDNA molecules designed for cGAS activation have been developed, they are often rapidly cleared and degraded when administered in vivo resulting in poor cellular uptake. Therefore, dsDNA-based cGAS agonists require precise engineering to achieve their intended effect. In contrast, small molecule cGAS agonists can bypass the degradation challenges faced by dsDNA, yet the intricate complexity of cGAS activation remains a significant hurdle. Therefore, the design of small molecule agonists for cGAS activation imposes stringent requirements: on one hand, the agonists must exhibit high pathway specificity to avoid off-target effects that could interfere other cellular processes; on the other hand, they must possess the ability to induce multivalent interactions and self-multimerization, promoting DNA-bridging-like phase separation required for cGAS oligomerization and efficient cGAMP production. These complex demands present formidable challenges in the development of small molecule cGAS agonists, and to date, no such agonists have been successfully reported. Nonetheless, some existing clinical cancer treatments may unlock new possibilities for developing cGAS agonists, with deeper exploration of these strategies poised to significantly sensitize cGAS.

Compared to the challenges of delivering exDNA, damaging endogenous DNA offers a more practical and efficient means of pharmacologically activating cGAS. In fact, numerous studies have investigated the activation of the cGAS-STING pathway by inducing DNA damage through clinical existing cancer treatments such as chemotherapy and RT. Topoisomerase inhibitors such as camptothecin, doxorubicin, and mitoxantrone can induce DNA replication errors, triggering the activation of cGAS and its downstream NF-κB and IFN I pathways, which in turn promotes the infiltration of DCs and CD8+ T lymphocytes, targeting and inhibiting tumor growth. The microtubule-targeting agent paclitaxel interferes with chromosome segregation and induces IFN-I and TNF-α, thereby promoting the generation of pro-apoptotic vesicles in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) via the cGAS-STING pathway. Similarly, RT can activate cGAS through mechanisms such as micronucleus formation due to DNA damage, cytoplasmic chromatin fragments from senescence, and mtDNA release triggered by mitochondrial stress, all of which drive potent anti-tumor responses. 

STING agonist

Manganese ion. Mn2+ is especially vital for defending the host against DNA viruses by orchestrating the entire cGAS-STING pathway, becoming a focal point in discussions as a potential STING agonist. By enhancing the sensitivity of cGAS and its downstream adaptor protein STING, it contributes substantially to innate immunity. Intracellular Mn2+ is primarily confined to membrane-bound compartments such as the Golgi apparatus and mitochondria, only released into the cytosol during viral infection. Once in the cytosol, Mn2+ binds to cGAS, inducing a conformational change similar to that triggered by dsDNA binding. The structural change then promotes the formation of a η1 helix, expanding the catalytic pocket of cGAS and facilitating the synthesis of cGAMP. Consequently, the responsiveness of cGAS to dsDNA and its ability to perform enzymatic functions are greatly enhanced. At the same time, Mn2+-cGAMP complexation significantly improve the STING binding affinity of cGAMP, amplifying STING activation, by two orders of magnitude compared to original cGAMP. Additionally, Mn2+ can even directly activate cGAS independently of DNA, and Mn2+-activated cGAS exhibits higher enzymatic efficiency, thereby inducing the production of IFN-I and cytokines in the absence of infection. The combination of Mn2+ with immune checkpoint PD-1 antibodies, known as “manganese-based immunotherapy,” has been shown to significantly enhance the therapeutic efficacy of tumor clearance. Phase I clinical trials have preliminarily confirmed the clinical safety of this therapy in patients with relapsed, refractory, and advanced-stage tumors. Although Mn2+ can regulate cGAS activity and promote the binding of cGAMP to the STING protein, the high required concentrations required for such effects carries significant adverse effects on the human body and even cause poisoning, making them less viable for therapeutic use. As a result, there are no reported of using free Mn2+ alone as cGAS-STING agonists to date.

CDNs. Natural CDNs including STING’s native ligand 2’, 3’-cGAMP, as well as c-di-GMP, c-di-AMP and 3’, 3’-cGAMP found in prokaryotic cells, have been shown to be effective inducers of host IFN-I responses in bone marrow macrophages through the cGAS-STING pathway. Among them, 2’,3’-cGAMP has demonstrated excellent efficacy in inducing apoptosis and delaying tumor growth in mouse models of breast cancer and melanoma. Similarly, c-di-GMP has been effective in overcoming immune suppression and improving vaccination against metastatic breast cancer. Natural CDNs hold promising potential in triggering STING activation, disappointingly, they are highly susceptible to enzymatic degradation by phosphodiesterases like ENPP1, which hydrolyze them and block STING activation. To overcome this limitation, a series of synthetic CDNs has been developed based on the structure of 2’,3’-cGAMP. For example, Li et al. synthesized a 2’,3’-cGAMP analogue, 2’,3’-cGSASMP, which possesses similar STING affinity to the natural ligand but exhibits 40 times greater resistance to ENPP1 hydrolysis. Gajewski et al. synthesized compounds including ML RR-S2 CDG, ML RR-S2 cGAMP, and ML RR-S2 CDA (ADU-S100), which also have a high binding affinity for STING and are resistant to phosphodiesterase degradation. These CDNs derivative improved stability, increased lipophilicity, and significantly enhanced STING activation, with strong anti-cancer effects validated in mouse models of B16 melanoma, CT26 colon cancer, and 4T1 breast cancer. Among them, ADU-S100 was the first STING agonist to enter human clinical trials for cancer immunotherapy. However, ADU-S100 showed a short half-life and limited clinical efficacy, with only one patient achieving complete tumor remission. Subsequently, Eisai Inc. developed a new class of macrocyclic-bridged STING agonists (MBSAs). Due to the conformational rigidity of the macrocyclic bridge, MBSAs demonstrate high chemical and metabolic stability. E7766, a MBSA derivative of ADU-S100, has shown sustained antitumor activity and was selected for Phase Ia/Ib clinical trials (NCT04144140). However, natural CDNs face challenges as STING agonists in cancer immunotherapy, including their poor stability, low bioavailability, and a narrow therapeutic window.

Non-nucleotide STING agonists. Non-nucleotide STING agonists hold significant promise over anionic and highly water-soluble CDNs due to their potential for enhanced drug-like properties, improved membrane permeability, and better suitability for oral administration. Consequently, research has increasingly focused on developing small-molecule STING agonists, with several non-nucleotide candidates now in development. The most typical example is DMXAA, which initially exhibited promising tumor-suppressing activity in preclinical mouse models. Unfortunately, due to the polymorphism of STING, DMXAA was unable to bind to human STING and failed in a Phase III clinical trial for non-small cell lung cancer. Nonetheless, DMXAA provided critical proof-of-concept for the existence of non-nucleotide small molecule STING agonists as alternatives to CDNs in immunotherapy One of the most promising non-nucleotide STING agonists currently is diamidobenzimidazole (diABZI), reported by GlaxoSmithKline. They identified that the ABZI STING agonist could bind to one subunit of hSTING the homodimer with significant but relatively low potency. To improve potency, researchers further dimerized ABZI to form diABZI, which can target the homodimeric STING and improve its competitive binding by 1,000-fold. DiABZI has shown substantial tumor shrinkage and improved survival in BALB/c mice with colorectal tumor. More recently, extensive screening efforts have led to the discovery of additional non-nucleotide STING agonists, such as MSA-2 and SR-717, identified through their ability to induce IFN-β secretion and activate the cGAS-STING pathway. Both of these small molecules have shown potent tumor inhibition and survival benefits in animal models, making them promising candidates for future cancer immunotherapy development.

STING agonists are regarded as the most direct and efficient method of activating the cGAS-STING pathway, given that STING operates more downstream and with greater specificity than cGAS, thereby representing the leading focus of cGAS-STING-mediated cancer immunotherapy. Recently, the substantial pre-clinical anti-tumor activity of STING agonists has driven the development of multiple pharmacologic classes of agents at various stages of being translated into the clinic, including immunostimulatory metal ions, cyclic dinucleotides (CDNs) and non-nucleotide STING agonists.

ENPP1 inhibitor

As mentioned before, ENPP1, a key phosphodiesterase, catalyzes the hydrolysis of ATP or GTP into AMP or GMP, thereby influencing STING activity by degrading cGAMP. Since its role in regulating the cGAS-STING pathway was identified, the therapeutic potential of ENPP1 inhibitors has gained significant attention. Unlike direct STING agonists, which indiscriminately activate STING in both cancer and host cells, ENPP1 inhibitors shift the balance of cGAS-STING pathway activation toward host cells, thereby driving immune responses specifically against tumors. The most extensively studied ENPP1 inhibitors are nucleotide-based compounds (such as α,β-metADP/ATP, 2-mesADP/ATP, and bzATP), which are structurally similar to the natural substrates of ENPP1. These inhibitors competitively bind to ENPP1, replacing ATP or GTP, thereby promoting the activation of the cGAS-STING pathway. Despite their therapeutic potential, the low oral bioavailability—caused by the high acidity of the phosphate backbone, low chemical stability, and possible off-target effects—has hindered further development as viable drugs. To improve potency, selectivity, and oral bioavailability, a series of small-molecule non-nucleotide inhibitors have been developed and are in preclinical studies or early clinical. For example, in cancer spheroid models, SR-8541 A induced a strong immune response by promoting the migration and infiltration of peripheral blood myeloid cells into the tumor. The oral small-molecule ENPP1 inhibitor RBS2418 has entered Phase I clinical trials for the treatment of advanced unresectable, recurrent or metastatic tumors in combination with monoclonal antibody treatment or as a monotherapy. 

cGAS-STING agonists entering clinical trials

The pursuit of cGAS-STING agonists has emerged as a cutting-edge frontier in cancer immunotherapy, with a growing number of drug candidates advancing into clinical trials. To date, nearly 20 trials—spanning Phase I and II—have been launched to explore the therapeutic potential of these agonists (Table 2). In these studies, STING agonists have notably taken center stage, leading the charge in cGAS-STING-driven cancer treatment. ADU-S100 was the first STING agonist to enter human clinical trials as an investigational cancer immunotherapy, although its initial clinical efficacy was limited. In subsequent studies, it has often been combined with other immunotherapies, yielding promising results. For example, the combination of ADU-S100 and pembrolizumab demonstrated favorable tolerability in patients, with clinical benefit observed in 25% of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) cases. Another trial involving ADU-S100 in solid tumors and lymphoma patients showed partial responses when used in conjunction with the PD-1 inhibitor PDR001. Similarly, the CDN-based STING agonist E7766 has been approved for Phase I clinical trials to evaluate its safety, tolerability, and anti-tumor activity in patients with advanced solid tumors, lymphoma, and bladder cancer (NCT04144140, NCT04109092). In addition, several non-CDN small molecule STING agonists are gradually entering clinical trials. For example, GSK3745417, either alone or in combination with dostarlimab, is being tested in various cancer patient cohorts to assess its safety, tolerability, and preliminary anti-tumor efficacy (NCT03843359). Beyond STING agonists, ENPP1 inhibitors are steadily advancing into clinical trials. RBS2418 is currently being tested in combination with pembrolizumab to assess its efficacy in treating advanced metastatic solid tumors (NCT05270213). Meanwhile, SR-8541 A is being explored as an oral monotherapy to evaluate its safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics in patients with advanced or metastatic solid tumors (NCT06063681). A steady stream of candidate drugs targeting this pathway is being developed, refined, and advanced into clinical trials, unveiling exciting therapeutic potential. However, emerging clinical data highlight pressing challenges, primarily linked to the inherent shortcomings of these small-molecule agonists, including suboptimal pharmacokinetics, nonspecific biodistribution, and the risk of toxicity.

Advantages of nanomedicines targeting cGAS-STING activation

Stability challenges: The physicochemical and biological properties of these drugs limit the in vivo stability. For example, the hydrophilic nature of the STING agonist ADU-S100, combined with interactions with systemic proteins, accelerates its metabolism and degradation. When administered intratumorally, its terminal half-life ranges from only 10 to 23 min, contributing to suboptimal clinical outcomes. Furthermore, cGAS-STING agonists are susceptible to enzymatic degradation, such as by phosphodiesterases, which significantly diminishes their therapeutic efficacy.

Tumor targeting limitations: Challenges in tumor-specific delivery greatly reduce the efficacy of cGAS-STING agonists. These small-molecule agonists lack sufficient tumor specificity and are prone to systemic distribution. Their negative charge and high hydrophilicity hinder interactions with cell membranes, lowering transmembrane internalization efficiency. While intratumoral injection improves targeting to some extent, the therapeutic range remains limited, failing to induce sustained immune responses, especially against distant metastases.

Systemic toxicity: Systemic toxicity further constrains the clinical utility. As STING proteins are widely expressed in normal tissues, high-dose local or systemic administration of STING agonists can trigger nonspecific innate immune activation, potentially causing cytokine storms and severe inflammatory responses, leading to fatal damage.

Despite their significant potential in cancer immunotherapy, cGAS-STING-based anti-tumor drugs face substantial clinical translation challenges, such as poor stability, limited targeting efficiency, and systemic toxicity—issues that cannot be fully addressed by optimizing the drug molecules alone (Fig. 3a).

Improving drug stability and prolonging activity: Encapsulation of cGAS-STING agonists within nanocarriers effectively enhances their solubility, bioavailability, and resistance to degradation. Incorporating stabilizing polymers, such as polyethylene glycol (PEG) or polysaccharides, creates “stealth” nanoparticles, reducing nonspecific protein binding. Additionally, biomimetic nanoparticles coated with membranes from red blood cells, platelets, immune cells, or tumor cells can evade immune detection, extending circulation time. Gel-based nanomedicines further employ controlled release mechanisms to maintain high drug concentrations locally, ensuring prolonged therapeutic effects.

Enhancing tumor targeting while minimizing side effects: By optimizing physicochemical properties (e.g., size and surface charge) and functional modifications, nanomedicines achieve superior tumor-targeting efficiency and accumulation. In one hand, nanoparticles smaller than 500 nm employ the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect to preferentially accumulate in tumor tissues. In another hand, functionalizing the surfaces of nanomedicines with targeting ligands or antibodies improves tumor cell recognition while reducing off-target effects. Moreover, TME-responsive designs (e.g., pH, reactive oxygen species (ROS), or enzyme-sensitive linkers) enable on-demand drug release, minimizing exposure to non-target tissues. Some customized nanomedicines can even target different cell populations in TME, thereby improving the overall therapeutic efficacy of heterogeneous tumors. Notably, these delivery advantages not only significantly improve the tumor specificity, but also significantly reduces the non-specific distribution, avoiding widespread inflammatory responses, and thus alleviating the immune adverse events associated with cGAS-STING agonists.

Facilitating intracellular delivery: Nanomedicines significantly enhance the intracellular delivery of cGAS-STING agonists, which act on intracellular targets. Cationic polymer nanoparticles or liposomes can interact with cell membranes via electrostatic attraction, promoting internalization. Their cationic properties also facilitate lysosomal escape through the “proton sponge effect,” ensuring precise delivery to target cellular compartments.

Enabling combination therapies: Given the complexity of cancer, combination strategies are often more effective than monotherapies. Nanoplatforms allow cGAS-STING agonists to synergize with treatments such as chemotherapy, RT, phototherapy, and dynamic therapies, not only overcoming the inherent limitations of these therapies but also effectively eradicating tumor masses and inducing lasting anti-tumor immune memory.

Fortunately, nanomedicine, as a rapidly evolving discipline, holds great promise in bridging the gap between theoretical potential and practical challenges of cGAS-STING agonists. By leveraging nanoscale materials and systems, nanomedicine enhances the stability, targeting, and efficacy of therapeutic agents, addressing several key limitations of conventional cGAS-STING agonists (Fig. 3b).

In conclusion, nanomedicine optimizes the therapeutic efficacy and safety of cGAS-STING agonists through precise spatiotemporal control of drug release. By targeting specific organs and cells, it enhances therapeutic accuracy and minimizes off-target effects. Integrating diverse therapeutic strategies reprograms the TME continuously, driving more effective cancer treatments. Accordingly, nanomedicine-based approaches integrate and optimize immunotherapies centered on the cGAS-STING pathway, offering a transformative upgrade to cancer treatment. The following sections highlight recent advances in the application of nanotechnology in cGAS-STING agonists. Based on the distinct mechanisms of these nanotherapeutic platforms, they are categorized into six groups: nanomedicines that facilitate exDNA introduction, therapies such as chemotherapy, RT, dynamic therapies, and other treatments that induce endogenous DNA generation, and nanomedicines based on STING agonists, including STING agonist-based cancer nanovaccines.

 Nanomedicines facilitate exDNA introduction 

As cytoDNA is the critical trigger for cGAS-STING pathway activation, delivering exDNA to target upstream cGAS and enhance its signaling represents the most direct activation strategy. However, attaining efficient intracellular accumulation of exDNA to trigger substantial cGAS-STING activation poses several hurdles: (a) DNA is continuously degraded by nucleases in vivo; (b) non-specific cellular uptake often leads to reduced efficacy and increased side effects; (c) endosomes add another obstacle to the cytoplasmic delivery; (d) the unstable cGAS-DNA complex struggles to sustain a prolonged activation signal. Fortunately, emerging nanomedicines have been developed to overcome these obstacles, paving the way for cGAS-STING activation via exDNA (Table S1). On one hand, functionalized nanoplatforms not only elevate the accessibility of exDNA to the TME but also cultivate the selective uptake of exDNA by specific cells, and even solve the problem of endosomal escape. On the other hand, the addition of auxiliary ions (Mn2+) can greatly boost cGAS activity; the structurally optimized DNA (long and dense) provides strong assistance for the formation of stable cGAS-DNA complexes, achieving powerful cGAS-STING activation.

 Targeting delivery 

The degradation by DNases stands as the frontline defense against the entry of exDNA. The subsequent TME and endosomal barriers intercept and prevent the cytoplasmic enrichment of exDNA. In consequence, various strategies to extend the circulation of exDNA and aid its escape from the endosomal barrier are continually being explored. To illustrate, the CaCO3-based delivery system was employed to bolster the in vivo stability of exDNA and facilitate its endosomal escape, thereby fostering its cytosolic accumulation. However, stable in vivo DNA delivery introduces another dilemma —the low-level cGAS-STING activation in cancer cells and nonspecific STING activation in normal tissues due to non-selective uptake. To overcome this predicament, Chen et al. devised a modularized DNA nanodevice agonist (DNDA) that enables selective macrophage uptake and programmable cGAS-STING activation. The DNDA comprised four functional modules: (1) a macrophage-selective uptake modules (Ya, Yb); (2) an endosomal escape module (Yl-p); (3) an ATP-responsive module; (4) a STING activation module (G3) (Fig. 4A). Following intratumoral injection, DNDA selectively targeted macrophages through optimized size (~ 40 nm) and ligand specificity, escaped endosomes, and released G3 in response to cytoplasmic ATP, achieving potent STING activation. In a B16 melanoma mouse model, DNDA significantly suppressed tumor growth, achieving a suppression rate of 79.71% (Fig. 4B).

Nanotechnology also provide a customized platform for pairing exDNA with other drugs to elicit more powerful immune response. Thoughtfully crafted drug combinations can target multiple therapeutic sites, not only overcoming drug resistance but also amplifying synergistic effects against tumors. Lin et al. devised an αPD-L1/mannose-modified MMP-2-sensitive nanocomplex (ISD@PPM/MP) for hierarchical targeting of tumors and local APCs, which concurrently blocked the programmed death ligand 1 (PD-L1) and delivered a STING agonist into tumor-infiltrating APCs (Fig. 4C). The primary ligand, αPD-L1, mediated active targeting of tumor cells and tumor-infiltrating APCs. Subsequently, tumor-enriched MMP-2 cleaved the PD-L1-binding peptide, then the exposed secondary ligand, mannose, facilitated the targeted delivery of ISD via mannose receptors on APC membranes, specifically activating the STING pathway and initiating an anti-tumor immune response. Harnessing the hierarchical targeting strategy that precisely delivers αPD-L1 and ISD to tumor cells and APCs, ISD@PPM/MP demonstrates superior immunostimulatory activity and significantly enhanced tumor inhibition, outperforming ISD@PPM combined with free αPD-L1 (90.31% vs. 74.30%) (Fig. 4D-H).

 Optimizing DNA structure 

The length and concentration of dsDNA are closely linked to its ability to activate cGAS. Short dsDNA (< 45 bp) weakly induces cGAS oligomerization, while longer dsDNA, especially with a compact structure, significantly stimulates cGAS activation by forming stable, ladder-like, or even networked cGAS-DNA complexes. Moreover, lengthy, high-concentration dsDNA promotes liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) of the cGAS-DNA complex, which hinders the entry and diffusion of 3’ repair exonuclease I (TREX1), confining DNA degradation to the outer shell of the droplets. Inspired by this, Xu et al. proposed a TME-responsive virus-like DNF@LIPO nanoparticle, which is densely stacked with long-chain DNA (Fig. 4I). Each DNF@LIPO template contained 5 × 106 repeating units, equating to 3.75 × 108 bases, with a nucleic acid content far exceeding that of DNA viruses and conventional short-chain ISDs. Upon injection into tumors, DNF@LIPO released its DNA payload in response to phospholipase overexpressed in the TME. Compared to ISD, its long, U-shaped, and compact structure markedly enhanced the liquid-phase condensation of cGAS. Owing to its finely-tuned DNA structure and advanced nanodelivery system, DNF@LIPO proficiently galvanized the cGAS-STING pathway and counteracted the immunosuppressive TME, resulting in a marked suppression of tumor expansion (202 mm3, 0.25 g vs. PBS group 567 mm3, 0.48 g).

Beyond optimizing DNA strands in two dimensions, assembling DNA into more stable three-dimensional structures also emerges as a potent strategy to prolong the in vivo half-life of exDNA. Specifically, Liang et al. investigated the novel immuno-stimulatory effects of tetrahedral DNA nanostructures (TDNs) in cancer therapy. Structured TDNs demonstrated superior stability in serum compared to dsDNA (Fig. 4J). Notably, the STING activation triggered by TDNs showed a pronounced size dependency, with T17 (a TDN with a 17 bp edge length) exhibiting excellent prowess in augmenting cGAS-STING pathway activation and eliciting IFN-β synthesis. Given Mn2+ is pivotal in the cGAS-STING pathway, Liang et al. adsorbed T17 onto MnO2 nanosheets to form T17-MnO2, which mitigated the potential cytotoxicity of Mn2+ and gradually released it in response to intracellular GSH. Capitalizing on the sensitizing effect of Mn2+ on cGAS, intratumoral injection of T17-MnO2 yielded markedly superior antitumor efficacy compared to T17, significantly shrinking tumor volume (Fig. 4K).

In summary, nanomedicines have revolutionized cGAS-STING activation by addressing key barriers in DNA delivery through innovative designs such as functionalized platforms, enhanced endosomal escape strategies, and optimized DNA structures. However, their clinical translation remains limited by several challenges. For instance, nanomaterials carrying exDNA face immune rejection and off-target effects, leading to rapid clearance and reduced therapeutic efficacy. Additionally, the diverse DNA frameworks often complicate manufacturing and escalate costs. To achieve more cost-effective and direct cGAS-STING activation, emerging strategies increasingly focus on inducing in situ DNA damage via chemotherapy, RT, or dynamic therapy (Sects. 5, 6, 7, 8, 9), enabling multifaceted tumor eradication.

 Nanochemotherapy drugs induce endogenous dsDNA production 

Chemotherapy remains a cornerstone of cancer treatment. According to the target, chemotherapy drugs can be divided into two categories: DNA-targeted and protein-targeted. DNA-targeted chemotherapy drugs induce DNA damage through various mechanisms. Alkylating agents like cyclophosphamide add alkyl groups to DNA bases, causing cross-linking and strand breaks. Topoisomerase inhibitors, such as camptothecin, prevent DNA unwinding by inhibiting topoisomerases, resulting in strand breaks during replication. Antimetabolites like 5-fluorouracil incorporate into DNA, disrupting replication and repair. These DNA damages ultimately lead to cell death and promote tumor regression. Nonetheless, the efficacy of chemotherapy is limited by tumor heterogeneity, off-target effects, drug resistance, and minimal residual disease. With the revelation of the complex and dynamic relationship between chemotherapy and immunotherapy, a promising new approach—chemo-immunotherapy—has emerged, which revitalizes traditional chemotherapy. Specifically, when damaged DNA is released extracellularly and recognized by immune cells, it cserves as initiate signal to activate the cGAS-STING pathway, triggering an innate immune response, enhancing anti-tumor activity, and making it a key mechanism in chemo-immunotherapy strategies. However, several challenges impede the efficacy of chemo-immunotherapy: (a) the complex TME; (b) the difficulty of traditional chemotherapy agents in producing sufficient concentrations of dsDNA; (c) short-term anti-tumor effects; (d) intrinsic tumor resistance mechanisms, etc. Nanomedicines offer various optimized strategies to surmount these struggles (Table S2). First, through targeted modifications, and carrier selection, nanomedicines profoundly augment the accumulation and recognition of dsDNA. Second, local administration and controlled-release strategies mitigate systemic toxicity while inducing sustained DNA damage, ensuring long-term antitumor effects. Finally, nanomedicines act on multiple stages of cancer cell immune evasion and provide precise strikes against tumor resistance pathways.

 Enhancing dsDNA accumulation and recognition 

Primarily, effective accumulation of chemotherapy drugs at the tumor site is a prerequisite for inducing DNA damage. Yet, the dense extracellular matrix and elevated interstitial fluid pressure in solid tumors drastically limit drugs diffusion into the stroma, diminishing their potential chemotherapeutic and immunotherapeutic. In osteosarcoma (OS), the highly mineralized and rigid tissue surrounding the tumor greatly impedes the effective drug delivery. Moreover, even if the drug successfully accumulates at the tumor site, its internalization in cancer cells is still restricted due to the dense stromal tissue of OS. To address this challenge, Shen et al. designed an alendronate (Ale)-based cationic platinum prodrug nanoparticle (Ale NP) tailored to the complex physiological characteristics of OS. First, the negatively charged Ale NP reduced rapid clearance by the reticuloendothelial system, extending circulation time. By virtue of Ale’s robust affinity for hydroxyapatite (HA), Ale NPs achieved remarkable enrichment at the tumor site. Then in the acidic TME, Ale chelated with the elevated Ca2+ in OS, resulting in the shedding of Ale and the subsequent exposure of the positively charged core NP to enhanced the tumor cell uptake of NPs (Fig. 5A-C). Once internalized, the elevated GSH levels in tumor cells prompted the NPs to dissociate, unleashing phenanthroline (Pt (II)) for precise chemotherapy delivery. Therefore, the Ale NP group induced more DNA damage than the cisplatin group, stimulating more efficient STING activation, thereby triggering a strong anti-tumor chemoimmune response, manifested as the highest tumor inhibition rate.

Furthermore, the activation of the cGAS-STING signaling pathway and the subsequent immune response hinges on effective dsDNA sensing, which requires tumor-derived dsDNA to reach the minimum concentration threshold. However, in clinical settings, chemotherapy doses are frequently too modest to fully activate this pathway. Moreover, following multiple treatment cycles, cancer cells can adapt and evade drug toxicity through mechanisms like drug efflux, metabolism, and cell cycle regulation. In this regard, Zhang et al. formulated an innovative nanoparticle (PhenPt NP). The encapsulated PhenPt is a cationic monofunctional platinum (II) drug with a sterically hindered phenanthridine ligand. This advanced ligand powerfully blocked the interference from GSH and metallothioneins, dramatically diminishing the detoxification of platinum drugs by cancer cells. Furthermore, it boosted the stability of drug-DNA adducts, leading to a markedly higher DNA toxicity compared to traditional platinum-based therapies (Fig. 5D). Additionally, employing specialized carriers to simultaneously induce endogenous dsDNA production while introducing exogenous DNA as a dual-action cGAS activation strategy holds promise as a potent chemo-immunotherapy approach. For instance, Zhang et al. developed a distinct TDN structure (84 bp-TDNISD/56MESS) that carried a chemotherapeutic platinum (II) complex (56MESS) As anticipated, the potent DNA-damaging agent 56MESS synergized with 84 bp-TDNISD to efficiently initiate cGAS-STING signaling, leading to enhanced maturation of DCs and infiltration of cytotoxic T lymphocytes in both tumors and tumor-draining lymph nodes, thereby demonstrating superior tumor suppression compared to single-treatment groups in the 4T1 tumor-bearing mouse model (Fig. 5E). Remarkably, in addition to elevating dsDNA levels, sensitizing cGAS stands as an effective strategy to intensify downstream immune responses. As previously mentioned, Mn2+ and Zn2+ can reduce the threshold for host cell detection of damaged dsDNA by several orders of magnitude. Building on this, Wang et al. crafted a Mn2+-based nanoadjuvant (BSA-Man@Mn2+-Ft@Lap). In contrast to the BSA-Man-Ft group, the synergy of chemotherapy agents and Mn2+ elevated cGAMP levels by thirty-fourfold, markedly intensifying the cGAS-STING signaling cascade in DCs. Therefore, the BSA-Man@Mn2+-Ft@Lap group exhibited the most pronounced antitumor effect, shrinking the average tumor volume from 1 cm3 to 0.27 cm3 after 21 days of treatment.

 Eliciting persistent DNA damage 

Due to the rapid metabolism and excretion of chemotherapy agents, coupled with the asynchronous cell cycles of tumor cells (where G0 phase cells are resistant to cell cycle-specific drugs), a single systemic administration fails to induce a sustained chemotherapeutic immune response, often leading to tumor recurrence and metastasis, as well as potential bone marrow suppression. Thus, attaining localized and regulated delivery of chemotherapy agents emerges as a promising strategy to optimize oncocyte eradication and restrain tumor proliferation. Wang et al. developed a GSH-responsive supramolecular hydrogel system (CDA-NT), which formed a localized gel depot upon injection (Fig. 5F). Over a span of 45 days, the in situ-formed CDA-NT gradually biodegraded, releasing STING agonist CDA and the chemotherapeutic camptothecin (CPT) in a sustained manner, thereby inducing prolonged DNA damage and STING activation. CDA-NT demonstrated a more pronounced cGAS-STING activation compared to the free CPT + CDA, resulting in a significantly enhanced antitumor response that achieved near-total tumor regression. Remarkably, the localized administration of CDA-NT induced T-cell memory and established enduring immunity against tumor relapse: by day 130, CDA-NT-treated mice exhibited the highest proportions of CD8+ T cells.

Frequent low-dose chemotherapy, as an administered metronomic chemotherapy regimen, may offer a novel method for eliciting sustained antitumor chemotherapeutic immune responses. Unlike single high-dose chemotherapy that leads to rapid dsDNA accumulation and excessive activation of the TREX1, rhythmic chemotherapy consistently induces prolonged DNA damage, which is crucial for stable and efficient IFN-I production. For instance, Park et al. developed an oral administered metronomic chemotherapy regimen, termed oral CAPOX, which involves daily coadministration of the nano-micelle encapsulated oxaliplatin complex OPt/LDC-NM and capecitabine for 21 days. The oral CAPOX + αPD-1 group, which combined chemotherapeutic and immunotherapeutic, enhanced anticancer efficacy, achieving a 91% complete remission rate in mice with CT26 colon cancer. Crucially, tumor antigen-specific memory effects were observed in the spleen cells of mice receiving the oral CAPOX + αPD-1 group, indicating that this regimen bolstered the antitumor efficacy of immune cells and generated a sustained, robust antitumor immune response.

 Blocking immune evasion 

Under the stress of chemotherapeutic drugs, cancer cells exhibit remarkable resilience by activating resistance mechanisms, particularly DNA damage repair (DDR), which preserves genomic stability. Targeting and inhibiting DDR components could dismantle this defense and enhance cGAS-STING-mediated chemoimmunotherapy. Wang et al. ingeniously crafted a GSH-responsive nanoparticle (NP2) that encapsulated a cisplatin prodrug (Pt (IV)) and a WEE1 inhibitor (MK1775). After tumor cell uptake, NP2 released Pt (IV) and MK1775 in response to GSH. Pt (IV) was converted into CisPt, triggering DNA damage and stimulating the cGAS-STING pathway. Concurrently, MK1775, a WEE1 inhibitor, countered the S and G2 phase arrest caused by the key DDR kinase WEE1, pushing cells with unrepaired DNA damage into mitosis, which synergized with CisPt’s antitumor effects and further amplified the cGAS-STING pathway. In contrast to NP1, which lacked MK1775, NP2 generated a higher quantity of Pt-DNA adducts, inducing more extensive DNA damage and better tumor suppression (Fig. 5G).

In addition to active mechanisms such as DDR repair, passive compensatory immunosuppression induced by chemotherapy represents another critical facet of immune evasion. This process includes the upregulation of immunosuppressive cytokines and chemokines (e.g., CCL2/7) in the TME, which facilitates the substantial infiltration of myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs). Therefore, the crux of successful combined chemotherapeutic and immunotherapy lies in deftly balancing this double-edged sword: mitigating immune suppression while effectively enhancing T cell activation. Wan et al. adeptly devised a nanoparticle-based “two-in-one” strategy for tackling pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), which was designed to unleash the STING-driven immune response and dismantle the immune resistance. They first engineered gemcitabine (Gem) into ultrasmall prodrug polymer carriers (PGEM) to enhance drug penetration into tumors. PGEM effectively induced dsDNA damage, amplified cGAS binding to micronuclei, and activated the cGAS-STING pathway. Regrettably, the PGEM also led to elevated CCL2/7 expression and a sharp increase in MDSC and TAM (Fig. 5H-I). To address this, Wan et al. loaded the CCR2 antagonist PF-6309 (PF) into PGEM (PGEM/PF). The addition of PF significantly reversed the MDSC and TAM-mediated immune suppression induced by PGEM, while preserving NK and CD8+ T cell tumor infiltration. Ultimately, PF substantially enhanced the therapeutic efficacy of PGEM, resulting in the lowest tumor weight and most severe tissue damage in the PGEM/PF group (Fig. 5J-K).

In summay, the advent of cancer immunotherapy based on the cGAS-STING pathway has revolutionized traditional chemotherapy, giving rise to various innovative nanoparticle strategies, which are designed to construct more effective combination therapies. Central to these approaches are the “first punch” of amplifying chemotherapy-induced DNA damage; Complementing this, the “second punch” which further amplifies the efficacy of chemo-immunotherapy, involves a range of strategies: sensitizing cGAS (co-delivery of metal ions and chemotherapeutic drugs), inhibiting the DDR (disturbing homologous recombination repair (HRR) and non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), or downregulating DNA repair enzymes like poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP)), and enhancing T cell activity (inhibiting indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO)). Collectively, these multi-faceted and intricate strategies carve out groundbreaking paths in cancer therapy. Chemoresistance remains a troublesome issue. While the strategies mentioned in this article, such as enhancing targeted delivery and inhibiting DDR, have shown good efficacy in corresponding preclinical studies, these resistance mechanisms are rarely seen in isolation in clinical practice. Instead, they often act synergistically as multiple mechanisms, leading to complex treatment resistance and tumor recurrence. Therefore, current research has not yet comprehensively or multi-dimensionally addressed the tumor resistance mechanisms to chemotherapy. Additionally, the primary resistance mechanisms differ among various chemotherapeutic drugs, and researchers should conduct targeted drug design to strengthen the effectiveness of chemotherapy-immunotherapy combined therapies.

 Nanoradiosensitizers induce endogenous dsDNA production 

RT is a mainstream cancer treatment that primarily targets DNA. On one hand, radiation deposits energy into biomolecules, causing damage to DNA structure and function. On the other hand, it ionizes H2O2, generating primary radiolysis products (·OH and electrons), which further interact with H2O2 or O2 to produce highly toxic ROS. Given the high nucleophilicity of DNA, it becomes a primary target for ROS attacks. ROS induce DNA damage through oxidative modification of bases, and causing single-strand or double-strand breaks. Damaged DNA is released into the tumor cytoplasm or taken up by immune cells along with apoptotic tumor cells. Such lethal DNA damage activates the cGAS-STING pathway, triggering anti-tumor immunity. This immune response in turn enhances the overall efficacy of RT, making it a potent strategy for cancer treatment. However, the systemic toxicity caused by high-dose radiation, along with the radioresistant TME resulting from factors such as hypoxia and antioxidants, constitute an insurmountable obstacle to effective radio-immunotherapy. In order to tackle these difficulties, substantial advancements have been achieved in the study of nanoparticles as radiosensitizers (Table S3). On the one hand, metal nanoparticles with high atomic number (Z) elements are expected to efficiently deposit and release energy under low-dose X-ray irradiation, thereby effectively inducing DNA damage. On the other hand, the radioresistant TME can be reversed by increasing oxygen supply to the tumor site and consuming highly expressed GSH, thus improving the efficiency of radio-immunotherapy.

 High Z metal-based nanoradiosensitizers 

How to balance the anti-tumor efficacy of RT with the side effects of high-dose ionizing radiation on normal tissues has been a persistent problem. The emergence of high-Z metal-based nanoradiosensitizers presents a novel strategy for handling this problem. Through precise targeting mechanisms, nanoradiosensitizers can accumulate at tumor sites, which enables localized treatment. More importantly, high-Z elements, with their excellent photoelectric effect and Compton scattering properties, can efficiently attenuate and absorb the energy of high-energy radiation, and transfer it directly to tumor cells or promote water radiolysis to produce ROS. Owing to these properties, they can significantly improve tumor radiosensitivity, allowing for a reduction in radiation dose without compromising therapeutic effectiveness, while sparing healthy tissues from excessive radiation exposure. For instance, Yan et al. constructed a novel lanthanide-doped radiosensitizer-based metal-phenolic network DSPM, which achieved potent radiosensitization and optimized cancer RT through a cGAS-STING pathway-mediated immune stimulation strategy (Fig. 6A). Leveraging the radiosensitizing property of lanthanides and the activation of cGAS by Mn2+, DSPM induced significant STING activation and improved the immunosuppressive TME. Under low-dose radiation (6 Gy × 3 times), DSPM revealed strong anti-tumor effects, caused extensive tumor necrosis and substantially delayed tumor growth.

Inspired by this, a series of high Z metal-based nanoradiosensitizers have been developed and refined through various innovations to achieve more optimal anti-tumor effects. As an illustration, a GSH-responsive in situ aggregation strategy overcomed the drawback of small nanoparticles that easily returning to the bloodstream and quickly diffusing into surrounding tissues, and prolonged the retention time of radiosensitizers in tumors. Due to the 3D arrays and highly ordered structures, nanoscale metal-organic frameworks (nMOFs) excel in scattering secondary photons and electrons, have a high mass attenuation coefficient and strong dose enhancement effect, and present heightened radiosensitization effects. Besides, the intervention of strategies to inhibit protective DDR further amplifies the DNA damage caused by high-Z metal-based nanoradiosensitizers. For example, Liu et al. combined the nanoradiosensitizer hafnium oxide (HfO2) nanoparticles with the ataxia telangiectasia mutation and RAD3-related (ATR) kinase inhibitor (ATRi) VE-822 to obtain T-HONs@VE-822, which not only enhanced the direct effect of RT, but also provided a strong boost for STING activation, thereby driving the comprehensive upgrade of radio-immunotherapy (Fig. 6B-E). Whatsmore, image-guided therapy is a key component in improving the precision and efficacy of RT. Duo et al. employed the Salmonella VNP20009 as an active bacterial carrier to deliver a novel Aggregation-Induced Emission luminogen (AIEgen) (TBTP-Au, VNP@TBTP-Au), which combined bacterial-targeted delivery with image-guided RT. Building on the enhanced radiosensitivity of tumors through high-Z elements Au, AIEgen mediated image-guiding ensured accurate radiation targeting by enabling precise localization and real-time monitoring of the lesion (Fig. 6F). Ultimately, VNP@TBTP-Au effectively inhibited tumor growth, with the tumor volume dropped from 160 mm3 to 77 mm3, after 16 days of treatment.

 Reversing the radioresistant TME 

In addition to externally enhancing the deposition of radiation energy at the tumor site, strategies aimed at internally reversing the radioresistant TME are also widely explored to improve the efficacy of radio-immunotherapy. One classic approach is increasing oxygen supply to the tumor, which stabilizes DNA breaks and prevents self-repair. As an example, Liu et al. mixed the high-Z element Hf with 2-nitroimidazole (2-nIm), an X-ray responsive donor of NO, to fabricate Hf-nIm@PEG (HNP). Because of the X-ray responsiveness of 2-nIm and the excellent energy deposition ability of Hf, HNP precisely controlled NO release under X-ray stimulation (Fig. 6G). The released NO enhances RT sensitivity through multiple mechanisms, including stabilizing DNA free radicals to prevent self-repair, inducing mitochondrial damage, alleviating hypoxic TME (by dilating blood vessels and promoting angiogenesis), and producing reactive nitrogen species (RNS). This dual strategy—combining an effective external attack with a comprehensive internal attack—led to the highest level of DNA damage in the HNP + RT group, compared to the single-treatment groups. The damaged DNA activated the cGAS-STING pathway and promoted intense activation of the immune system (Fig. 6H). Finally, the HNP + RT group exhibited the most potent anti-tumor effect.

The anti-oxidative TME severs as another solid defense for tumors, which makes them more stubborn and difficult to conquer. It reinforces tumor defense mechanisms by weakening the oxygen sensitization effect, neutralizing radiation-induced free radicals, enhancing DDR, and supporting immunosuppression. Hence, reprogramming the antioxidant TME and converting it into a “battlefield” that intensifies DNA damage have emerged as a vital approach to conquer tumor radioresistance. He et al. designed PVCL-MnO2-CpG nanogel to treat glioblastoma via magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)-guided immune-activated RT. Intravenously administered PVCL-MnO2-CpG crossed the disrupted blood-brain barrier to reach the tumor site, where it dissociated in response to high GSH, releasing Mn2+ and CpG ODNs (Toll-like receptor 9 agonist). MnO2 induced C6 cells to produce more ROS, which was beneficial for attacking the covalent bonds of DNA, causing more severe DNA damage, thereby effectively decreasing radioresistance during RT (Fig. 6I). Attributable to the synergistic effects of GSH consumption, ROS-induced radioresistance alleviation, cGAS-STING-enhanced activation, and CpG-ODN-triggered immune stimulation, PVCL-MnO2-CpG successfully recruited DCs and promoted cytotoxic T cells infiltration. Consequently, the RT + PVCL-MnO2-CpG group achieved satisfactorily tumor suppression, with a tumor growth inhibition rate far exceeding that of the RT + PVCL-CpG group.

Limited by systemic toxicity and tumor radioresistance, conventional RT is becoming less prominent in cancer treatment. However, cGAS-STING-based immunotherapy has opened up new prospects for it. By utilizing high-Z radiosensitizers and optimizing the radioresistant TME, emerging functional nanomedicines can severely damage cancer cells at low radiation doses and effectively activate the cGAS-STING axis, achieving exponential anti-tumor efficacy. Other than that, the integration of radio-immunotherapy with additional treatments such as chemotherapy, starvation therapy, and ferroptosis is also under investigation. This multimodal combination therapy strategy expands the scope and depth of cancer treatment, offersing more possibilities for future combination medicines. Despite these advancements, challenges remain. High-Z metal nanoparticles may pose long-term toxicity risks and complex interactions with metabolism, necessitating comprehensive in vivo studies. Moreover, while nanoparticle design effectively improves local TME, their universal applicability across diverse tumor types warrants further exploration. Another critical issue is RT-induced immune cell damage, which can undermine antitumor immune responses but has been underexplored in the above-mentioned studies. These challenges again highlight the importance of cell-specific, nanotechnology-mediated targeting strategies.

 Nanodynamic therapies induce endogenous dsDNA production 

Similar to RT, ROS-based dynamic therapies is emerging as a key therapeutic approach in cancer treatment. These methods can be categorized into photodynamic therapy (PDT), sonodynamic therapy (SDT), and chemodynamic therapy (CDT), according to the different exogenous stimuli and reaction mediators. These methods induce ROS bursts, extensive DNA damage, and cGAS-STING-mediated immune cascades in a safer and more controlled manner. Thereupon, dynamic therapies that generate ROS in situ have emerged as an imptant synergistic groundbreaking approach for cGAS-STING mediated immune therapy. The application of these therapies, especially in deep-seated tumors, may be hindered by the inherent limitations of ROS, such as the short lifespan, limited diffusion distance, high reactivity, and uncontrollable in vivo processes, as well as insufficient stimulation and immunosuppressive conditions within tumor tissues. The introduction of nano-strategies ignites a new beacon of hope (Table S4). By accurately regulating drug delivery and overcoming the adverse conditions of TME (such as hypoxia, weak acidity, and endogenous antioxidant mechanisms), nanotechnology dramatically increases the ability of ROS to damage DNA and activate cGAS-STING.

 Photodynamic therapy 

PDT involves three core elements: photosensitizer (PS), light, and tissue oxygen. Once activation by light at a specific wavelength, the PS transfers energy to surrounding oxygen, generating abundant ROS that damaging DNA and trigger the cGAS-STING pathway to stimulate immune responses. Despite PDT drawing considerable attention for its potential to activate the cGAS-STING pathway and promising outlook in clinical applications, its development continues to grapple with several daunting challenges.

First, the specific accumulation of PS in cancer cells, particularly in mitochondria, is crucial for ensuring the subsequent PDT effect. However, this process is frequently hindered by factors such as nonspecific adsorption, severe systemic toxicity, and biotransformation. Thus, exploring and employing diverse strategies to fine-tune and optimize the in vivo behavior of PS is essential not only for enhancing the precision and efficacy of PDT but also for minimizing unnecessary side effects. For instance, Zhao et al. engineered nanoparticles (ALA&Dz@ZIF-PEG) loaded with a PS precursor and a metabolic-regulating DNAzyme for mitochondria-targeted PDT. Once inside tumor cells, ALA&Dz@ZIF-PEG escaped from the lysosomal lumen driven by the protonation of the imidazole ring. In response to the acidic TME, it then released Zn2+, ALA, and DNAzyme. The released Zn2+ significantly inhibited the activity of mitochondrial complex IV, effectively blocking mitochondrial aerobic respiration and alleviating tumor hypoxia, which drove the conversion of ALA to PpIX, acting as an “opensource strategy” for PpIX mitochondrial production (Fig. 7A). Simultaneously, the released DNAzyme, empowered by Zn2+ cofactors, silenced FECH and suppressed the conversion of PpIX into photoinactive heme, termed as “reduced expenditure” in PpIX inactivation (Fig. 7B). This “open source and reduced expenditure” strategy vastly boosted intracellular PpIX accumulation and inflicted severe mitochondrial damage. In 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, ALA&Dz@ZIF-PEG activated the cGAS-STING signaling axis and induced robust immune activation under light irradiation.

Second, the inherent limitations of ROS restrict the advancement of PDT. While ROS can inflict severe damage on mitochondria, targeting mtDNA alone is insufficient to activate the cGAS-STING pathway fully due to its small amount compared to nDNA. Additionally, although singlet oxygen, the principal byproduct of PDT, possesses strong reactivity, its extremely short half-life (10–320 ns) and limited diffusion range (0.01–0.02 μm), combined with the high density of chromatin in nDNA, further constrain PDT’s effectiveness. To address this, Zhao et al. engineered an intracellular self-assembly driven nucleustargeted photo-immune stimulator (PIS) with chromatin decompaction function to co-activate innate and adaptive antitumor immunity. The PIS consisted of vorinostat (SAHA)-loaded manganese-porphyrin metal-organic framework (Mn (III)-TCPP MOF) with further modification of AS1411 aptamer, collectively known as SMTA. Once targeting tumor cells, the high GSH level triggered the unlocking of TCPP, initiated PDT, released SAHA, TCPP, AS1411, and Mn2+. The released TCPP and AS1411 underwent in situ self-assembly to drive nucleustargeted delivery of TCPP via the ucleolin receptor mediation (Fig. 7C). Meanwhile, SAHA induced chromatin decompaction through histone acetylation, thereby increasing the accessibility of the AS1411/TCPP components to nDNA. This design, which simultaneously breached the nuclear/chromatin barriers, enabled SMTA to achieve the highest extent of nDNA fragmentation and its subsequent release into the cytoplasm to activate cGAS-STING. Moreover, the incorporation of nuclear-targeted chimeric peptides or nuclear-targeted chemotherapeutic agents, coupled with targeted DDR inhibition strategies, offered additional avenues for enhancing the potency of PDT in inflicting damage nDNA. 

 Sonodynamic therapy 

SDT harnesses ultrasound to activate sonosensitizers, creating localized heating and cavitation effects that ignite ROS production directly in cancer cells. Compared to light, the superior tissue penetration of ultrasound enables SDT to bypass depth constraints, unlocking new potential for targeting deep-seated tumors. Despite its excellent performance in inducing immune cell death (ICD), SDT’s focus on tumor cell destruction leaves it with limited capacity to modulate the immune system, which can diminish its effectiveness when targeting multiple tumor sites. Moreover, immune evasion driven by intrinsic tumor immune editing further undermines the success of sonodynamic immunotherapy. These obstacles have spurred the creation of innovative nanotechnological approaches to elevate sonodynamic immunotherapy: optimizing SDT to amplify its immune-enhancing capabilities; crafting strategies targeting multiple non-redundant immune pathways to fully endow cancer cells with immunogenicity and enhance sensitivity to endogenous tumor immunity.

Initially, the refinement of SDT focused on enhancing the sonosensitizers to boost their sonocatalytic efficacy and immunostimulatory effects. Lei et al. ingeniously combined mitochondrial-targeting ligand TPP with sonodynamic cobalt-based metal-organic framework nanosheets, creating an ultrasound-responsive cGAS-STING amplifying nano-stimulator (TPP@CoTCPP). The coordination of TPP reduced the bandgap, improved O2 adsorption, and intensified electron transfer, thereby enhancing the sonodynamic effects of CoTCPP (Fig. 7D-E). Following intratumoral injection, TPP@CoTCPP localized to the mitochondria of tumor cells. Subsequently, ultrasound-induced ROS disrupted mitochondrial homeostasis, causing cytoplasmic escape of mtDNA (Fig. 7F). Meanwhile, Co enhanced STING activity in a cGAMP-dependent manner, which avoided unnecessary inflammatory responses. TPP@CoTCPP + US activated the cGAS-STING pathway in bone metastases, eliciting the highest levels of immune responses.

Although the optimized SDT can markedly enhance T-cell activation in tumors, numerous tumors exhibit substantive resistance to T cells due to the inherent process of cancer immunoediting, ultimately posing substantial barriers to tumoricidal effect. Tumor cells may be more recognizable and susceptible to immune attack under certain death modalities, and exploiting these vulnerabilities could present unique opportunities to bolster T-cell-mediated antitumor immunity. Zhou et al. introduced a cancer immunoengineering strategy leveraging ultrasound (US)-assisted immune modulation, termed NUITT, which induced tumor highly immunogenic PANoptosis, enabled ultrasound-triggered immune activation, iteratively started up the energization of cancer innate immunity cycle, and boosted the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy. The nanovesicle, PFH@Lipo-PpIX@EVs (PLPE), contained highly expressed PD-L1 on the surface, PpIX as well as internally loaded GSDMD protein and phase-changing perfluorohexane (PFH). Employing GSDMD, PpIX, and PFH, the PLPE thus possessed the immunogenic PANoptosis (pyroptosis, apoptosis, and necroptosis) through triple catalysis, namely biological catalysis (membrane pore formation), chemical catalysis (ROS generation), and physical catalysis (PFH phase transition and cavitation effect), maximally destroying the selfprotection barrier mechanism of tumor cells and repeatedly liberating DMAPs to generate a potent and enduring tumor-specific immune response (Fig. 7G). Through the PANoptosis, PLPE triggered STING-mediated T-cell activation, initiating a robust cancer immune cycle, which fostered durable protective memory against tumors, with PLPE-treated mice retaining the ability to resist new tumor cell challenges 30 days post-treatment. Overall, this innovative approach targeting the PANoptosis offered a novel perspective for overcoming persistent cancer immune resistance, holding promise not only for curbing tumor immune evasion but also for enhancing immune activation through a positive feedback mechanism.

 Chemodynamic therapy 

Compared to the exogenous triggers of PDT or SDT, CDT chooses the unique biochemical characteristics of TME as endogenous triggers, initiating in situ chemical reactions in the tumor to generate ROS. With deeper insights into cancer cell responses, the focus of researchers has shifted from optimizing Fenton reagents to enhancing downstream effects of CDT. Efforts now emphasize promoting phagocytosis of dead tumor cells and mitigating protective autophagy to amplify the cGAS-STING activation triggered by CDT.

Although CDT prompts dsDNA release from dying tumor cells via ICD, the swift elimination of dsDNA by TAMs and the tumor itself frequently fosters immune tolerance and evasion, markedly undermining the STING pathway activation that CDT aims to achieve. To tackle this, Chen et al. engineered an innovative drug delivery system, CHCSPA (CCM@HMON@CS-Pt(IV)@ANX5), which amplified cGAS-STING signaling by obstructing endocytosis. The CHCSPA comprised a cancer cell membrane (CCM), hollow mesoporous silica nanoparticles (HMONs), Cu2-XSe (CS), cisplatin prodrug (Pt (IV)), and annexin A5 (ANX5). In CHCSPA, CS NPs inflicted oxidative damage to mitochondria through CDT, leading to the release of mtDNA. Meanwhile, Pt (IV) was reduced to the cytotoxic Cisplatin (Pt (II)) under GSH, which not only activated nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) oxidase (NOX) to generate H2O2 but also interacted with DNA to disrupt nDNA. ANX5 then bound to the phosphatidylserine (PtdSer) on apoptotic cell surfaces, blocking its recognition by TAMs via the Mertk receptor tyrosine kinase (Fig. 7H). This blockade disrupted TAM-mediated phagocytosis and degradation of apoptotic tumor cells, which led to secondary necrosis and the release of damaged mtDNA and nDNA. When tumor-derived dsDNA was ingested by DCs, the STING pathway within these cells was further activated, resulting in the increased expression of IFN-β, IL6, and CXCL1, and a strengthened immune cycle.

Additionally, the tumor’s protective autophagy, activated in response to damage, negatively regulated the STING pathway, acting as a “brake” on STING signaling. Consequently, inhibiting autophagy can amplify STING pathway activation and enhance CDT-mediated tumor immunotherapy. For instance, Wang et al. used a hydrogel system to deliver Mn2+, glucose oxidase (GOx), and the autophagy inhibitor hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) to postoperative tumor sites, preventing TNBC recurrence and suppressing metastatic tumor growth. GOx and Mn2+ jointly drove CDT effects, triggering ICD and DNA damage. Remarkably, HCQ disrupted the tumor cells’ protective autophagy, removing the autophagic “brake” and enhancing immunotherapy effectiveness, with significantly reduced tumor volume (Fig. 7I).

In conclusion, substantial progressed have been made in ROS-based cancer dynamic therapy. Various nanomaterials with unique regulatory capabilities over ROS and immune responses have been developed to orchestrate the spatiotemporal dynamics of ROS within tumor and amplify immune activation. Similarly, strategies involving gas therapies (such as NO, CO, and H2S) or converting high H2O2 concentrations to O2 have been integrated into dynamic therapies. These approaches not only heighten cancer cells’ sensitivity to ROS but also alleviate the harsh hypoxic TME, setting the stage for more effective cancer immunotherapy. However, for dynamic therapies like PDT and SDT that require precise coordination, determining the optimal therapeutic window remains challenging. The above work showed insufficient research on optimizing illumination or ultrasound timing, duration, and frequency, which deserves greater attention, as treatment timing is critical for maximizing efficacy. Comparatively, CDT is more convenient but still requires addressing TME heterogeneity, which determines the degree of drug response and efficacy.

 Other nanoparticle therapies induce endogenous dsDNA production 

In the preceding sections, we delved into various nanoparticles commonly used to induce endogenous dsDNA production and activate the cGAS-STING pathway. Each nanoparticle possesses unique characteristics, and the exploration of the cGAS-STING pathway delves far beyond this. Researchers are relentlessly pushing the boundaries, crafting diverse nanomedicines that propel cancer immunotherapy forward via strategies like PTT, mitochondrial stress, and ER stress (Table S5). These groundbreaking approaches not only enrich the arsenal of cancer immunotherapy but also provide valuable insights for future research in the field.

 Photothermal therapy 

PTT has emerged as a prominent cancer treatment due to its precise spatiotemporal targeting, minimal systemic toxicity, and low therapeutic resistance. The therapy utilizes photothermal agents (PTAs) to heat localized tumor, disrupting cellular structures and DNA integrity. Damaged DNA is then released as cells rupture, which triggers the cGAS-STING pathway to activate innate immune responses. The combination of PTT and cGAS-STING-based immunotherapy not only accelerates tumor clearance but also strengthens the immune response, resulting in more sustained therapeutic effects and potential for long-term tumor control. In this intricate process, the thermal conductivity and photothermal conversion efficiency (PTCE) of PTAs are crucial. Thus, optimizing PTAs has become the primary focus for enhancing the efficacy of PTT. Recently, Song et al. pioneered the use of a Schottky heterojunction—a physical model—in cancer immunotherapy to co-amplify the photothermal and adjuvant effects. They anchored ultra-small γ-MnO2 nanodots onto BSA-modified Ti3C2(OH)2, creating TC-MnO2@BSA and forming the Schottky heterojunction. This heterojunction markedly augmented the PTCE and the ROS generation by enhancing exciton dissociation/transfer, curbing carrier recombination, and catalyzing redox activity (Fig. 8A-C). These improved physical properties allowed TC-MnO2@BSA to elicit the most significant HMGB1 translocation and p-STING expression under near-infrared irradiation, which indicatd the robust activation of both tumor ICD and the cGAS-STING signaling pathway in the TME.

Triggering tumor cell apoptosis via PTT is just the initial spark in igniting the immune response. In the following phase, apoptotic cells must be engulfed by APCs, triggering the release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), which in turn initiates a cascade of immune responses. However, tumor tissues often deploy complex mechanisms to inhibit the full activation of the immune response. For instance, TAMs may engulf apoptotic tumor cells via Rubicon-mediated LC3-associated phagocytosis (LAP) in an “immune-silent” manner, leading to inefficient tumor antigen presentation and an immunosuppressive TME. In this context, targeted inhibition of Rubicon within TAMs becomes essential. As an illustration, Shang et al. leveraged the tropism of Rhizopus oryzae towards TAMs to develop TAM-targeted nanopore carriers (PC-CW) loaded with shRNA-expressing plasmids that specifically targeted Rubicon, aiming to selectively inhibit LAP in TAMs (Fig. 8D). When paired with DTIG—a nanocomplex with chemo-PTT capabilities—PC-CW effectively halted LAP in TAMs. In the B16F10 melanoma model, DTIG + PC-CW significantly enhanced STING activation and reshaped the tumor immune microenvironment through functional M1 polarization, CTL infiltration, and Treg reduction. The DTIG + PC-CW delivered the best antitumor efficacy, with an inhibition rate of 84.1%, compared to 29.7% with DTIG monotherapy (Fig. 8E).

 Mitochondrial stress 

In contrast to nDNA, mtDNA lacks the protective mechanisms provided by histones, making it highly vulnerable to damage. Its bacterial-like features, such as its circular structure and CpG-rich sequences, render it more recognizable by innate immune receptors. Additionally, mtDNA’s high replication rate and prolonged single-strand exposure further increase damage susceptibility. As a result, mtDNA is more prone to damage and owns higher immunogenicity than nDNA, leading to more extensive and lasting consequences. Mitochondria, as primary sites of cellular respiration, generate substantial amounts of ROS, which act as potent agents of mitochondrial stress. Mitochondrial stress can disrupt membrane integrity, triggering mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP) and releasing mtDNA into the cytoplasm. This released mtDNA, acting as a DAMP, strongly activates the cGAS-STING pathway. Given these factors, researchers have increasingly turned their attention to inducing mitochondrial stress to release mtDNA and the subsequent activation of STING downstream. For example, Zhao et al. engineered a biomimetic nanoparticle (Vir-ZM@TD) to induce mitochondrial stress for cancer immunotherapy. The core of Vir-ZM@TD consisted of Mn-doped ZIF-90 nanoparticles (Mn-ZIF-90) loaded with DNAzymes, forming ZM@TD. This was then sequentially modified with a red blood cell membrane (RBCM), RGD peptides, and HA2 peptides, ultimately creating Vir-ZM@TD. After intravenous injection, Vir-ZM@TD specifically bound to αVβ3 integrin receptors on tumor cells via RGD peptides. Then, the HA2 peptide facilitated the fusion of RBCM with the endosomal membrane through conformational changes, enabling endosomal escape of ZM@TD. Once inside the cell, ZM@TD disintegrated in the presence of ATP, releasing Zn2+, Mn2+, and DNAzymes. The DNAzymes, catalyzed by the cofactor Zn2+, inhibited 47% of mtDNA-binding protein (TFAM) mRNA, reducing TFAM protein levels by 53%. In 4T1 cells, the absence of TFAM triggered mitochondrial elongation and caused mtDNA to escape into the cytoplasm (Fig. 8F). Thanks to the presence of mtDNA and Mn2+ in the cytoplasm, the cGAS-STING pathway was efficiently activated, resulting in a 1.9-fold increase in cGAMP levels and a significant elevation of IFN-β. In 4T1 tumor-bearing mice, Vir-ZM@TD potently depleted TFAM, significantly boosting cGAS-STING signaling (with IFN-β levels 2.5 times higher than the saline group) and eliciting a robust and sustained immune response. Furthermore, methods that disrupt mitochondrial respiratory chain homeostasis (such as Zn2+, Ca2+, chitosan (CS), etc.) or alter mitochondrial inner membrane permeability (such as antimicrobial peptides) have also been widely used to induce mtDNA release into the cytoplasm and activate cGAS-STING. 

Naturally, tumor cells have developed a range of countermeasures in response to such assaults. Mitochondrial stress initiates a cascade of restorative processes, such as mitochondrial fission/fusion and mitophagy, which impair the cGAS-STING pathway-dependent innate immune response. Therefore, simultaneously damaging mitochondria while circumventing mitophagy to ensure sustained mtDNA release may offer a novel breakthrough in overcoming immune suppression in malignant tumors. Very recently, Zhang et al. reported a Zn2+-doped, weakly alkaline layered double hydroxide (Zn-LDH) as an immunomodulatory adjuvant, engineered to simultaneously supplement Zn2+ and disrupt autophagy. Once internalized by tumor cells, alkaline Zn-LDH efficiently disrupted autophagic flux by blocking the acidification of autolysosomes, resulting in significant mitochondrial damage (Fig. 8G). At the same time, the released Zn2+ triggered ROS production and accelerated mitochondrial damage, leading to the release of mtDNA. Therefore, the cytoplasmic mtDNA, in tandem with Zn2+, activated the cGAS-STING pathway, thereby boosting tumor-specific immunity.

 Endoplasmic reticulum stress 

Recent findings suggest that ER stress can be a potential pathway for cancer immunotherapy through cGAS-STING activation. On one hand, due to the ER-mitochondria interface, the tight connections and functional feedback between the two may supply cGAS with its missing ligand—cytoplasmic DNA—in the form of released mtDNA. On the other hand, the static STING, which typically resides in the ER, may be triggered by ER stress through mechanisms that bypass mitochondrial involvement, such as stabilizing STING oligomerization or altering its translocation. Zhang et al. developed an ER-targeted, CaO2-based site-specific delivery platform (PEG/CaO2@p-780) to amplify ER stress and effectively activate cGAS-STING for cancer immunotherapy. PEG/CaO2@p-780 was composed of the ER-targeting p-methylbenzene sulfonamide-tailored IR780 (p-780), DSPE-PEG-biotin, and CaO2 nanoparticles. Utilizing sulfonylurea receptors (sur) on the ER, PEG/CaO2@p-780 achieved high co-localization with the endoplasmic reticulum. Then, in response to the acidic environment of cancer cells, PEG/CaO2@p-780 disintegrated, releasing Ca2+, H2O2, and p-780. The introduced Ca2+ disrupted ER calcium homeostasis, inducing ER stress, which was further amplified by the ER-targeted photosensitizer p-780 via PDT and PTT (Fig. 8H-J). Therefore, a large number of unfolded protein responses were observed in breast cancer cells after PEG/CaO2@p780 NP treatment, and the structural characteristics of the endoplasmic reticulum changed significantly: the size enlarged and ruptured. This excessive ER stress in turn activated the cGAS-STING pathway, thereby enhancing the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy.

In summary, these diverse therapeutic strategies targeting the activation of cGAS by promoting the production of endogenous dsDNA have emerged as a research focus. Leveraging the versatile modifiability of nanomaterials, these combinatorial nanotherapies strategically target critical stages—from drug delivery and dsDNA production to STING activation and the initiation of both innate and adaptive immunity, which not only enhances the existing anticancer effects but also boosts cGAS-STING activation, thus amplifying their overall therapeutic impact. However, the nonspecific thermal effects of PTT pose notable safety concerns, with limited research addressing damage to peritumoral tissues, which warrants deeper investigation. Furthermore, organelle stress induction represents a novel perspective for cGAS-STING activation; for instance, therapies targeting mitochondrial stress have demonstrated remarkable antitumor efficacy. Conversely, studies on endoplasmic reticulum stress remain scarce, and therapeutic strategies targeting Golgi stress are virtually unexplored. Such approaches could be promising, as the Golgi apparatus is a critical site for STING activation and downstream signaling.

 Nanomedicines based on STING agonists 

Compared to cytoDNA, STING agonists offer greater specificity and precision by avoiding the interference caused by other nonspecific cellular processes triggered by DNA. Therefore, the specificity has driven the rapid development of STING agonists, with several related drugs quickly advancing to clinical trials. However, the poor cytosolic delivery of STING agonists, stemming from physicochemical properties such as high electronegativity, hydrophilicity, rapid clearance, and short half-life, has significantly hindered their clinical translation and application. Nanodelivery systems with optimized size, charge, and surface modifications hold great promise for overcoming these challenges. This section will focus on the application of nanodelivery systems in various STING agonists, including nucleoside and non-nucleoside STING agonists, immunostimulatory metal ions, STING-activating heterocyclic compounds, and epigenetic drugs (Table S6).

 Nucleoside and non-nucleoside STING agonists 

In fact, various nanocarriers, including liposomes, polymeric nanoplatforms, inorganic nanoparticles, micelles, exosomes, and nanoprodrugs, have been extensively developed for the delivery of nucleoside and non-nucleoside STING agonists. By meticulously designing the morphology (small size and high aspect ratio lipid nanodiscs) and composition of these nanocarriers (using endogenous materials or biomembranes with low cholesterol content as carriers), STING agonists are significantly protected from enzymatic degradation and other harsh physiological conditions. Simultaneously, these optimized designs improve tumor penetration and accumulation of STING agonists, enhancing cellular uptake and endosomal escape, thereby substantially boosting their bioactivity. For example, Shae et al. designed STING-activating nanoparticles (STING-NPs) to enhance cytosolic delivery of cGAMP via endosome-disrupting polymer vesicles. The appropriately sized (~ 80 nm) STING-NPs preferentially accumulated in tumors and sentinel lymph nodes, and were efficiently internalized by various cells. Upon internalization, the STING-NPs responded to the acidic environment of endolysosomes by releasing membrane-disrupting polymer fragments that interact with the endosomal membrane through protonation and hydrophobic groups, compromising its integrity and facilitating the intracellular release of cGAMP. By efficiently encapsulating cGAMP and cross-linking vesicle membranes to enhance endosomal disruption, STING-NPs enhanced cGAMP potency by 240–610 times (Fig. 9A). When injected into freshly excised human metastatic melanoma tissue samples, STING-NPs exhibited remarkable immunostimulatory activity, underscoring the clinical translational potential of STING-NPs. However, most of the studies focus on designing acid-sensitive nanodelivery systems to promote endosomal escape and deliver cGAMP into the cytosol, which doesn’t entirely prevent drug degradation by endolysosomes. Recently, Li et al. developed a microbubble-assisted, ultrasound-guided immunotherapy of cancer (MUSIC) strategy that using nanocomplex-conjugated microbubbles (ncMBs) to circumvent endolysosomal degradation by directly delivering cGAMP to the cytosol. Upon binding of ncMBs to APCs and under US exposure, cGAMP was delivered directly into the cytosol of the APCs by sonoporation (Fig. 9B). Similarly, Chen et al. utilized thiol-exchange-mediated internalization to bypass lysosomal degradation and enhance the cytosolic transport of cGAMP, thereby strengthening STING pathway activation and boosting the antitumor immune response. 

Additionally, nanoplatforms that integrate various functions and enable slow and sustained release of STING agonists in the TME present unique advantages. For example, Ma et al. proposed a multifunctional photoimmunothernostic nanomedicine SAPTN combined with mild PTT, which could enhance the sustained release of STING agonist diABZI for triggering robust systemic anticancer immunity. After intravenous injection, mild PTT recruited activated neutrophils, which secrete MPO in the TME. The bis-5-HT modified SAPTN then reacted with MPO, self-aggregated, and anchored onto ECM laminin, achieving high enrichment and prolonged retention within the tumor. Benefiting from the above advantages, SAPTN slowly and steadily released diABZIs at the tumor site, triggering robust and long-lasting antitumor immune responses, leading to tumor regression and long-term, tumor-free survival of at least 2 months in about 100% of the mice. Furthermore, with the elucidation of the relationship between hypoxia and the STING axis, incorporating hypoxia-relief strategies into STING agonist nanodelivery platforms offers a new approach to reprogramming the TME and sensitizing STING-based immunotherapy. Zhang et al. constructed a magnetic biohybrid system based on Spirulina (SP) as an oxygen factory, loading the STING agonist ADU-S100 (ADU@Fe-SP) for tumor immunotherapy. The magnetically guided biohybrid SP actively targeted tumor tissues and generated oxygen in situ through photosynthesis, thereby reversing the hypoxic TME and promoting immune cell function (Fig. 9C). Additionally, the targeted delivery of ADU-S100 activated the STING/TBK1/IRF3 signaling pathway and promoted cytokine production in both tumor and innate immune cells. In colorectal cancer, the ADU@Fe-SP group achieved the highest tumor inhibition rate of 90.8%.

 Immunostimulatory metal ions 

To date, numerous metal ions have been shown to participate in the immune activation of the cGAS-STING pathway. Among them, Mn2+ is the most extensively studied metal ion in cGAS-STING activation. However, a significant challenge in utilizing Mn2+ for tumor immunotherapy lies in targeting delivery to the tumor site. Zheng et al. proposed a Mn2+-based immunostimulatory MOF (ISAMn-MOF) for cancer immunotherapy. ISAMn-MOF entered DCs via clathrin-coated pit-mediated endocytosis and fast endophilin-mediated endocytosis. Once internalized, ISAMn-MOF rapidly released Mn2+ upon stimulation by the acidic endosomes, generating high local concentrations of Mn2+ in cytoplasm, thus exhibiting greater potency in activating the cGAS-STING pathway compared to free MnCl2. Additionally, Mn2+ holds multiple roles in cancer therapy, and harnessing its multifunctional properties can further advance cancer treatment. Recently, Sun et al. developed biodegradable MnO-based nanoparticles (MnO@mSiO2-iRGD NP) for MRI-guided cancer immuno-chemodynamic combination therapy. MnO@mSiO2-iRGD NP decomposed in the acidic TME, releasing Mn2+ with three primary effects: First, Mn2+ mediated a Fenton-like reaction for CDT. Second, Mn2+ served as a cGAS-STING agonist, promoting IFN secretion and subsequent tumor immunotherapy through effector T cell activation. Third, Mn2+ enabled tumor T1-weighted MRI through its high longitudinal relaxivity (Fig. 9D).

Growing evidence suggests that STING activation is often insufficient due to downstream negative regulatory mechanisms. Therefore, it is crucial to explore effective combinations that enhance STING activation to amplify the antitumor immune response. Liu et al. developed TME-responsive nanoparticles (PMM NPs) co-encapsulating the STING agonist Mn3O4 and the TLR4 agonist MPLA to achieve spatiotemporal coordination of STING activation (Fig. 9E). PMM NPs remained stable at physiological pH but rapidly dissociated into monomers in the acidic TME, releasing both agonists promptly. The released Mn2+ activated the STING pathway in BMDCs. More importantly, MPLA promoted NF-κB translocation to the nucleus, thereby preventing microtubule-mediated STING lysosomal degradation and enhancing STING signaling (PMM NPs increased total STING protein levels). Therefore, the inclusion of MPLA significantly amplified Mn2+-mediated STING pathway activation, boosting IFN-β secretion by 4.0 times compared to the PMO NP group. Compared to single agonist treatments, PMM NPs demonstrated superior antitumor efficacy, significantly inhibiting tumor growth and prolonging the survival of tumor-bearing mice. The similar design principles have been applied in various studies; for instance, Xu et al. and Li et al. amplified Mn2+-mediated STING activation by inhibiting HER2 and mutant p53 (mutp53), respectively. 

 STING-activating heterocycles 

Beyond conventional STING agonists and immune-stimulating metal ions, some studies have unexpectedly discovered that ionizable polymers with cyclic or linear tertiary amines in their side chains can induce STING activation. Specifically, Gao et al. reported an ultra-pH-sensitive polymer (PC7A) that its cyclic seven-membered tertiary amine ring effectively condenses multivalent proteins to activate STING and trigger an IFN-I response. Similarly, Miao et al. demonstrated that piperidine-based six-membered ring groups, resembling the structure of PC7A, could induce APC maturation via the intracellular STING pathway. 

As research interest in PC7A and its structural analogs continues to grow, Gao et al. delved deeper into the characteristics of PC7A and made a surprising discovery: PC7A binds to a unique surface binding site on the STING protein, activating STING through a mechanism independent of cGAMP. Moreover, PC7A could induce slower but more sustained STING activation both in vitro and in vivo, possibly due to its endolysosomal pH buffering capacity, slower cytoplasmic translocation, and delayed formation of STING condensates, compared to cGAMP. The non-competitive binding site and differing spatiotemporal dynamics lay the foundation for the combined use of PC7A and cGAMP. Correspondingly, GAMP-PC7A NPs exhibited potent immune activation effects in both tumor-bearing mice and human tissues (Fig. 9F).

Epigenetic regulatory medicines

The efficacy of STING agonists is influenced by various factors, with much research focusing on overcoming intracellular delivery challenges. However, some studies have revealed that the expression levels of cGAS-STING genes in tumor cells can be regulated, and many cancer types, such as gastric cancer, colorectal cancer, and advanced-stage cancers, evade immune detection by downregulating STING expression. In such cases, simply delivering STING agonists is insufficient, necessitating the intervention of regulatory medicines. To address the functional deficiencies in STING signaling in certain tumors, Wang et al. designed a universal STING mimic, termed uniSTING, which could activate downstream STING signaling independently of endogenous STING. UniSTING could self-assemble into tetrameric STING, further nucleating into higher-order structures independent of the membrane system, which could lead to constitutive activation of downstream STING signaling (Fig. 9J-K). Moreover, uniSTING exhibited high selectivity in downstream activation, favoring pro-apoptotic, tumor-suppressing IRF3/IFN-I cascades over pro-inflammatory, tumor-promoting NF-κB signaling. Moreover, Wang et al. overcame the in vivo instability of mRNA by utilizing redox-responsive lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), which facilitated the effective delivery of uniSTING-mRNA. Therefore, LNP-uniSTING-mRNA effectively inhibited the growth of 4T1-Luc2 breast tumors and extended survival compared to free cGAMP. Due to the negative feedback inhibition of STING by Wnt2b signaling, dual therapy combining LNP-uniSTING-mRNA with α-Wnt2b showed potential for further tumor growth suppression. Additionally, mRNAs encoding constitutively active STING mutants, such as STINGV155M and STINGR284S, have been explored for cancer immunotherapy in STING-silent tumors as well. 

The strategy of using STING simultaneously as a biologic agent and a delivery vehicle offers an excellent paradigm for overcoming the epigenetic silencing of STING. Li et al. developed a strategy where the cytosolic C-terminal domain (CTD) of the STING protein (STINGΔTM, lacking the transmembrane components) was self-assembled with cGAMP to form a tetrameric complex. Remarkably, this STINGΔTM-cGAMP complex was able to induce STING signaling even in STING-deficient cell lines. When combined with tumor vaccines (SIINFEKL, derived from OVA amino acids 252–272), the cGAMP-SIINFEKL-STINGΔTM complex completely inhibited the growth of MC38 colon cancer cells expressing SIINFEKL, with all 10 mice in the treated group remaining tumor-free (Fig. 9L).

In Summary, nanomaterials have significantly advanced the development of STING agonists. Nanodelivery systems enable the targeted delivery of STING agonists to the correct tissues and specific cell populations, inducing the appropriate intracellular signaling intensity at the right time, which shows the potential to broaden the therapeutic window of STING agonists. Additionally, with the help of nanodelivery systems, STING agonists can be easily combined with other anti-tumor therapies, which not only reduces the required dosage of STING agonists but also synergistically enhances anti-tumor effects, overcoming resistance to cancer treatments. In the future, we need further mechanistic studies and advancements in delivery technologies in this field to enhance our understanding of the pharmacology of STING agonist immunotherapy and promote the design of effective delivery strategies.

STING agonist-based cancer nanovaccines

STING agonists play a pivotal role in activating and mobilizing endogenous anti-tumor T cell immunity. By triggering the IFN-I response, they enhance antigen presentation by DCs, thereby driving robust tumor-specific T cell activation. This mechanism overcomes the limitations of traditional cancer vaccines, such as poor immunogenicity and inefficient antigen cross-presentation. Unlike cGAS agonists, which rely on dsDNA delivery or production, STING agonists offer a more direct and efficient pathway, establishing them as a powerful adjuvant in cancer vaccines and paving the way for STING-based cancer nanovaccines.

The STING-based cancer nanovaccines use lipid nanoparticles, polymer systems, and inorganic nanostructures as carriers to co-deliver STING agonists and multiple antigens to enhance the immune response. For example, Zhou et al. designed a STING pathway-targeting nanovaccine (Man-PDPM@OVA) for personalized cancer immunotherapy. Leveraging the STING activation properties of DMXAA, the vaccine markedly enhances DC maturation, antigen presentation, and CTL activation, resulting in efficient tumor growth inhibition (Fig. 10A). However, exogenous antigens often suffer from inadequate immunogenic epitopes and tumor immune evasion. Consequently, tumor-associated antigens derived from autologous tumor cells have emerged as a crucial source for personalized cancer vaccines. Nevertheless, this approach struggles to preserve all potential tumor antigens during production, limiting the breadth and potency of the immune response. To address this, He et al. developed a whole-cell cancer vaccine (LMP vaccine). Specifically, LPS-modified manganese(II)-phenolic network (MnTA nanocloak) coated and inactivated tumor cells, successfully preventing the loss of potential tumor antigens (Fig. 10B). After internalization by DCs, Mn2+ served as a powerful immune adjuvant, activating the STING pathway to promote DC maturation and antigen presentation. This, consequently, enhances T-cell immune responses in synergy with tumor antigens, significantly boosting anti-tumor efficacy (Fig. 10C).

The complexity of co-delivery systems for antigens and adjuvants often complicates the design and manufacturing of nanovaccines. In this context, minimalist nanovaccines focusing on core components (specific antigens and adjuvants) are emerging as a new trend. Heterocyclic compounds that activate the STING pathway provide strong technical support for this approach. These heterocyclic polymers, when combined with antigens, form streamlined nanovaccines that enable precise targeting, potent immune activation, and reduced off-target toxicity. For instance, Zhao et al. leveraged the STING activation properties of PEI-M to serve as a minimalistic binary vaccine (BiVax) carrier and immunoadjuvant for antigen delivery. Due to the inherent immunostimulatory properties of PEI-M, BiVax effectively overcame intracellular coordination challenges related to APC activation and antigen cross-presentation to CD8+ CTLs (Fig. 10D-G). PEI-4BImi/OVA showed optimal efficacy in inhibiting B16-OVA tumor growth, with a tumor inhibition rate of 83.2% on day 22 (Fig. 10H-L). Inspired by these findings, similar structured heterocyclic lipid polymers have been extensively developed and utilized as carriers and adjuvants in nanovaccine platforms. 

In conclusion, tumor vaccines represent a critical component of immunotherapy strategies. In the above-mentioned cutting-edge research, the use of STING agonists as adjuvants comprehensively promotes DC maturation and antigen presentation, activating specific T cell responses, thus significantly enhancing the therapeutic efficacy and durability of tumor vaccines. However, the challenges of precisely regulating the timing of adjuvant and antigen release remain significant due to the complexities of antigen delivery and the adjuvant effect. Additionally, further research is needed to overcome tumor immune evasion mechanisms and address the heterogeneity of individual immune responses.

Discussion

Overall, cGAS-STING-based cancer therapies aim to enhance key immune events of CIC to strengthen anti-cancer immunity. CIC primarily encompasses four critical steps: (a) tumor antigen release and presentation; (b) T-cell initiation and activation; (c) T-cell trafficking and infiltration; (d) CTL recognition and tumor cell killing. Traditional cancer immunotherapy mechanisms typically targeting only a single phase of CIC. For example, cancer vaccines focus on tumor antigen presentation, cytokine therapies promote T-cell activation, immune checkpoint inhibitors enhance CTL recognition and killing of tumor cells, and CAR-T therapies directly target and eliminate cancer cells. However, such enhancements targeting specific phases of CIC are often constrained by insufficient immune regulation, resulting in inherent shortcomings. Cancer vaccines suffer from low immunogenicity and poor antigen presentation. Cytokine therapies are hindered by the inability of T cells to penetrate the complex tumor vasculature. Immune checkpoint inhibitors are effective only in “hot” tumors, with efficacy ranging from 10 to 30%, and are ineffective in most “cold” tumors. Similarly, struggle with poor infiltration into solid tumors, immune suppression in the TME, and T-cell exhaustion. In contrast, cGAS-STING-based therapies provide holistic modulation across the entire CIC, addressing the limitations of traditional approaches. At the antigen release and presentation phase, cGAS-STING pathway enhances DC maturation and antigen presentation, promoting DC migration to tumor-draining lymph nodes. During T-cell initiation and activation, moderate cGAS-STING pathway activation upregulates TA-MHC I complex expression on DCs, enabling TCR recognition and subsequent CTL activation. For T-cell trafficking and infiltration, cGAS-STING pathway drives the expression of chemokines and adhesion molecules, facilitates vascular normalization, and removes barriers to T-cell migration, aiding CTL penetration into tumor tissues. Finally, during CTL recognition and tumor cell killing, cGAS-STING pathway upregulates MHC-I expression on cancer cells to enhance CTL-mediated tumor destruction and recruits NK cells for additional tumor elimination. 

The intricate interaction between cancer and the immune system poses significant challenges for immunotherapy. Tumor progression involves prolonged immune selection and the establishment of an immune-tolerant microenvironment. As a result, activation of the cGAS-STING pathway alone may be inadequate to eliminate cancer cells in a shot time and sustain the normal and complete functionality of the CIC. Therefore, combining external agents to restart the immune cycle at one or more stages is a critical strategy to enhance cGAS-STING-based cancer immunotherapy. As introduced above, cGAS-STING activation is often combined with therapies such as chemotherapy, RT, and PDT with the support of nanotechnology to synergistically enhance the efficacy and overcome their respective limitations. Table 3 outlines the benefits and limitations of these anti-tumor strategies based on nanomedicines. Firstly, nanoplatforms delivering exDNA can specifically induce intense cGAS-STING pathway activation, though their complex delivery systems require optimization for in vivo stability. Nanochemotherapeutic agents directly induce DNA damage and promote tumor antigen release, thereby significantly activating the cGAS-STING pathway. However, their efficacy is hindered by persistent challenges, including drug resistance and adverse side effects. Similarly, nanoradiosensitizers significantly enhance RT efficacy and strenthen cGAS-STING mediated inmmune activation by amplifying DNA damage. But their application remains limited to solid malignancies, and the associated risk of radiation-induced toxicity poses challenges to broader clinical utility. Advanced nanodynamic systems generate highly toxic ROS through milder mechanisms, enabling substantial cellular destruction and DNA damage. Positioned at the forefront of minimally invasive treatments, dynamic therapies offer targeted and localized therapeutic potential. However, PDT and SDT face inherent limitations in tissue penetration depth, and hypoxia TME, while more stable and sustainable oxygen-providing nanomaterials remain further optimization. CDT depends heavily on endogenous activating factors, while insufficient endogenous H2O2 restricts its therapeutic efficiency. Additionally, ROS is only effective at the nanoscale, making cellular uptake efficiency a critical consideration. PTT induces tumor cell necrosis and DNA release through localized thermal effects, offering high specificity and non-invasiveness. However, its efficacy is diminished for deep tumors and hindered by hypoxia. Mitochondrial and ER stress can promote highly immunogenic mtDNA release to strongly trigger cGAS-STING mediated immune responses and potentially disrupt tumor cell metabolism and immune evasion mechanisms. These stratgies, however, require precise subcellular organelle targeting capability. Nanodelivery of STING agonists circumvents the DNA damage step, enabling direct and efficient STING activation with shorter response times, controllable immune effects, and minimal damage to normal cells. Nevertheless, STING agonists alone are often insufficient and require combination with other immunotherapies, which heightens the risk of immune-related adverse events. Finally, cancer nanovaccines integrating STING agonists and antigens address challenges like antigen degradation and insufficient presentation, but still face hurdles such as antigen-adjuvant interactions and designing tumor-specific antigens for optimal efficacy. 

Various nanotechnology-based cancer immunotherapies demonstrate distinct advantages and challenges in activating the cGAS-STING pathway. Integrating different nanostrategies to complement each treatment phase will be key to enhancing the clinical efficacy of cGAS-STING-based immunotherapies in the future.

 Summary and prospect 

Deepening and expanding cognition

Despite the current enthusiasm and rapid progress in drug development targeting the cGAS-STING signaling pathway, our understanding and regulatory control of this pathway remain at a nascent stage, with more questions and challenges than answers. (i) The cGAS-STING signaling pathway involves multiple promoting and inhibiting effects, and its activation mechanisms are not yet fully elucidated. It is crucial to determine whether and how this vast and complex regulatory network interacts, which would help explain cancer initiation and progression mechanisms. (ii) In cancer pathogenesis, cGAS-STING exhibits two opposing functions: tumor suppression and promotion. While cGAS-STING activation might be an effective therapeutic strategy in early and chromosomally stable tumors, its role in established malignancies is complex. However, if tumors have already co-opted the STING pathway to drive various oncogenic processes, excessive STING activation could inadvertently worsen clinical outcomes. For instance, chronic STING activation might induce chronic inflammation, upregulate non-classical NF-κB signaling, release IDO and pro-inflammatory cytokines, and promote tumor growth and metastasis. Additionally, STING may contribute to immune evasion and metastasis by regulating PD-L1 expression and inducing autophagy. Beyond its autonomous effects in cancer cells, tumors can transfer cGAMP to neighboring cells via gap junctions, facilitating direct communication with the external environment and accelerating tumor progression. Chen et al. demonstrated that cGAMP transfer between cancer cells and astrocytes mediates STING activation and inflammatory cytokine secretion in astrocytes, which promotes brain metastasis of cancer cells. Moreover, high doses of STING agonists have been found to inhibit T cell proliferation and induce T cell death, potentially diminishing the anti-tumor activity of tumor-infiltrating T cells. (iii) Persistent STING activation may trigger a cytokine storm, which could be severely toxic to healthy tissues or even fatal. However, little is known about the clinical likelihood of such events and how to properly shut down activated signals once cytokine storm triggered. (iv) Most research currently focuses on stimulating cGAS-STING activity in tumor cells or APCs to reverse immune suppression. However, it is also important to consider the critical role of cGAS-STING in vascular normalization and remodeling the vascular-immune interface. Wilson et al. found that STING activation in the TME could mediate tumor vascular remodeling by improving vascular integrity, reducing tumor hypoxia, and increasing the expression of T cell adhesion molecules on endothelial cells, which enhances T cell infiltration, proliferation, and function, thereby improving responses to various T cell-based immunotherapies. In summary, exploring the significant functions of the cGAS-STING signaling pathway in cancer will undoubtedly be a focus of long-term research. Continued in-depth studies will not only help reveal the underlying mechanisms of cGAS-STING signaling in cancer but also expand its application in anti-cancer therapies.

Focusing on STING-deficient tumor

In some tumors, STING expression is silenced by epigenetic modifications. Some tumors even suppress the STING pathway mediated immune response through various mechanisms, such as inhibiting cGAS and STING transcription, reducing cGAS-STING activators, promoting STING degradation, or blocking STING signalosome assembly. Current research primarily focuses on activating cGAS or STING to stimulate cancer immune responses, inadvertently neglecting the exploration of cGAS or STING-deficient tumors, leaving a significant research gap. Although we previously mentioned some reports on activating cGAS-STING in STING-silenced tumors, this is far from sufficient. We anticipate more studies will explore various mechanisms (such as inhibiting KDM5 to restore STING expression or inhibiting endolysosomes to prevent STING degradation) to reactivate cGAS-STING in these deficient tumors.

Overcoming technical challenges

The development of nanomedicines targeting the cGAS-STING pathway is still in its early stages, with evaluations limited to animal models, leaving their therapeutic efficacy in humans yet to be validated. Several technical barriers must be overcome before clinical translation. On one hand, it is essential to explore the properties of nanoparticles. Firstly, the reliability and safety of these nanomedicines need further clarification. The effectiveness of nanomedicines relying on the EPR effect remains uncertain, with potential off-target risks. Additionally, the complex interactions between nanomedicines and physiological tissues may cause adverse outcomes, such as lipid nanoparticles triggering inflammation or excessive PEGylation inducing neutralizing antibody formation. Secondly, the stability of nanomedicines is influenced by biomolecules and components in circulation. Upon contact with these blood components, nanomedicines may be recognized by the immune system, accelerating their clearance. While modifications can extend circulation time and improve delivery efficacy, a careful balance must be struck among stable loading, targeted delivery, and biological responses. As nanotechnology continues to evolve, we believe that nanomedicines targeting cGAS-STING will gradually transform into precise, intelligent systems with high bioavailability and low toxicity. On the other hand, we must carefully assess whether results obtained from animal models can be effectively applied to humans. Tumors established using tumor cell lines are often homogeneous, making it challenging to simulate the heterogeneous tumors found in clinical patients, let al.one more heterogeneous metastatic tumor. Therefore, cancer mouse models established using patient-derived tumor tissue may better replicate actual clinical tumors. Additionally, the most common metastasis model in the above-mentioned studies is lung metastasis. However, clinical metastases can occur in various organs, such as the liver, bones, brain, intestines, and lymph nodes. It is in urgent need to develop appropriate tumor animal models that fully replicate the histology and progression patterns of corresponding human cancers at different stages. In sum, continued attention is required to ensure the rational design and clinical translation of these anti-cancer nanomedicines.

Combining multiple immunotherapies

Through the co-delivering of cGAS-STING-based nanomedicines with other immunomodulators, an avenue for activating the whole cancer immunity cycle is emerging. Although the combined strategies have been highlighted in the research we discussed earlier, most combinations involving STING agonists have been limited to immune checkpoint inhibitors. However, given the involvement of different cell types in STING-dependent immune responses, exploring combinations of STING agonists with other immunomodulators is undeniably compelling. Co-delivering these cGAS-STING-activating nanomedicines with vaccines or other PRR-activating adjuvants may yield synergistic effects for enhanced antitumor efficacy. For instance, preliminary studies have shown that STING agonists can effectively boost the antitumor activity of CAR-T and CAR-NK cells both in vitro and in vivo. Additionally, researchers are also focusing on simultaneously stimulating cGAS-STING and other immune signaling pathways, such as retinoic acid-inducible gene I-like receptors and absent in melanoma 2, which may help overcome immune dysfunction.

The cGAS-STING pathway, functioning as a natural bridge between innate and adaptive immunity, holds an indisputable role in anticancer immunity, with associated strategies on the eve of clinical application. In this wave of the cancer immunotherapy revolution, nanodrugs targeting cGAS-STING have demonstrated tremendous potential in mitigating tumor burden. In this review, we first systematically elucidate the foundational architecture of the cGAS-STING pathway and its intricate anticancer mechanisms, followed by an overview about a range of cGAS-STING-dependent anticancer drugs currently under development, aiming to deepen the comprehension of this complex signaling network and establish a robust theoretical framework for subsequent innovative research. Subsequently, nanomedicines that specifically target the cGAS-STING pathway are introduced in detail to underscore their superiority over conventional therapies. These cutting-edge nanomedicines employ a variety of strategies—such as the introduction of exDNA, chemotherapy, RT, and ROS-based dynamic therapies—to induce the production of cytoplasmic dsDNA (the activating ligand for cGAS), alongside the precise activation of STING to initiate the cGAS-STING cascade. Our emphasis is on the distinctive advantages that nanotechnology presents in this domain, not only significantly enhancing drug delivery efficiency and tumor-targeting specificity but also integrating multifaceted functionalities to actively modulate the TME and counteract the negative feedback mechanisms inhibiting cGAS-STING, thereby amplifying the positive immune cycle of cGAS-STING. Nonetheless, despite its tremendous potential and prospects, the advancement of this field still comes with significant challenges and necessitate extensive multidisciplinary collaboration to achieve further breakthroughs and the eventual clinical application. We then explore and highlight these potential obstacles that may impede progress in this field, while offering our perspectives on the possible trajectories for its future evolution.

In conclusion, cGAS-STING-based nanomedicines have achieved significant milestones in cancer immunotherapy, showing remarkable tumor suppression and even tumor eradication capabilities, both as standalone treatments and in combination with other therapies. However, translating these promising scientific breakthroughs into clinical practice will demand further advancements across multiple fronts. Our shared objective is to propel the development of cGAS-STING-based nanotherapeutics, exploring new avenues and opportunities to initiate and sustain effective cancer immunotherapy.
