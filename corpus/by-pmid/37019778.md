# A tiny intrathymic bronchogenic cyst detected by continually boosting chest pain following COVID-19 mRNA vaccine administration.

## Full Text

A tiny intrathymic bronchogenic cyst detected by continually boosting chest pain felt after COVID-19 mRNA vaccine administration

To the editor,

Bronchogenic cysts are uncommon congenital cystic lesions, and anterior mediastinal and intrathymic bronchogenic cysts are extremely rare. We present a case of a patient who complained of chest pain induced by COVID-19 vaccinations and ended up incidentally discovering an intrathymic bronchogenic cyst on a CT scan during diagnosis. A 40-year-old male was referred to our outpatient department due to the incidental finding of a trivial thymic lesion during a survey regarding chest pain bouts after COVID-19 vaccine administration. The patient received a first dose of Pfizer–BioNTech mRNA COVID-19 vaccine in October 2021, where he complained of an onset of mild chest pain but soon subsided. A second dose of Pfizer–BioNTech mRNA COVID-19 vaccine was administered in December 2021, and the episodes of chest pain exacerbated, manifested as intermittent substernal tightness and compression, with each episode lasting approximately 3 min, on and off for 7 days. Severe chest pain was felt immediately after the third dose of an mRNA COVID-19 vaccination, Moderna, in April 2022. The intensity of intermittent chest pain obviously enhanced, lasting one month.

A chest radiograph showed mild widening of the mediastinum with increased pulmonary vascularity. Transthoracic echocardiogram reported a prolapse of the anterior mitral leaflet with mild mitral valve regurgitation, as well as a dilated pulmonary artery trunk (2.6 cm) with mild pulmonary valve regurgitation. A chest CT scan without contrast media revealed a 0.7 × 0.8 × 1.0 cm sized, well-defined cystic mass with homogeneous attenuation (Hounsfield units of 10) intheanterior mediastinum (Fig. 1 A). The patient subsequently underwent a video-assisted thoracoscopic thymectomy (Fig. 1B). Histological examination revealed a unilocular cyst surrounded by thymic tissue, filled with whitish mucoid fluid. It was lined with ciliated pseudostratified bronchial epithelium which supported the diagnosis of bronchogenic cysts (Fig. 1C–D).

Patients experiencing symptoms of chest pain, dyspnea, accompanied by any of the following findings: elevated troponin, abnormal ECG findings, decreased function on an echocardiogram, or cMRI findings have been validated as probable cases of acute myocarditis or pericarditis. The reported cases of bronchogenic cysts located in the anterior mediastinum previously were summarized by Lee and Chang; with only one case being symptomatic. Awaya et al report a case of vasospastic angina following COVID-19 mRNA vaccination with the clinical symptoms and examination results of their case quite similar to those of ours. One of the implicated mechanisms is Kounis syndrome, defined as an acute coronary syndrome, including coronary spasm, which are an aphylactoid reactions associated with mast cell activation. 

Aikawa et al reported a case involving non-infectious endocarditis and myocarditis after the male patient's 2nd dose of the COVID-19 mRNA vaccine (Moderna), presented with transient self-limited chest pain. The pathogenesis is hypothesizedas a maladaptive immune activation triggered by mRNA vaccination causing endothelial and myocardial damage. For patients with underlying valve pathology, the risk of non-infectious endocarditis should not be ignored. Cardiovascular adverse effects resulting from COVID-19 vaccinations always remain a concern. Therefore, further investigation regarding additional doses of COVID-19 vaccine should be demanded.
