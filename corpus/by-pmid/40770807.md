# Investigating the potential role of miR-451a as a tumor suppressor in immunogenic cell death induction and dendritic cell maturation in colorectal cancer cell lines.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains a leading cause of cancer-related mortality, as conventional therapies are frequently hampered by treatment resistance and the presence of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Immunotherapy, particularly strategies based on immunogenic cell death (ICD) induction can activate the TME by enhancing tumor immunogenicity and promoting T cell infiltration, potentially improving the efficacy of cancer immunotherapies in CRC. This study investigates the potential of miR-451a as a tumor suppressor in the release of ICD associated damage-associated molecular patterns in CRC cell lines and maturation of dendritic cells (DCs).

**METHODS**: Human CRC cell lines (SW48, SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2) were treated with oxaliplatin alone or transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, scrambled miRNA, or a combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin for 48 h. Cell viability was measured using MTT assays, and apoptosis was assessed through annexin V and PI staining. Flow cytometry was employed to evaluate calreticulin (CRT) levels on the cell surface and to analyze the percentages of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + mature DCs. Additionally, ATP levels were quantified using a luminescence assay, and HMGB1 levels were measured by ELISA.

**RESULTS**: Our results demonstrated that the overexpression of miR-451a significantly increased apoptosis and CRT surface exposure in all four CRC cell lines, like the effects observed with oxaliplatin, when compared to both the control and scrambled miRNA groups (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, P < 0.001, and P < 0.0001). However, the combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin did not yield synergistic effects across all cell lines. Additionally, the ability of miR-451a to increase extracellular ATP and HMGB1 levels, as well as its effect on DC maturation, varied among cell lines.

**CONCLUSIONS**: The findings of this study presents novel insights into the potential role of miR-451a as a tumor-suppressive agent in CRC. By examining its effect on ICD across four different CRC cell lines, we provide a comprehensive analysis of its impact on both tumor cells and the immune system. Our results suggest that miR-451a induce certain characteristics of the ICD response, which vary depending on the cellular context.

Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the world’s third most frequent malignancy that remains a leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide. Disease progression and resistance to conventional therapies, including chemotherapy or radiotherapy, remain as the significant challenges, particularly for metastatic CRC. This is while, targeted therapies have emerged as promising alternatives, demonstrating improved patient’s outcomes. Recent studies have highlighted the critical role of the tumor microenvironment (TME) in CRC progression and treatment resistance. The TME undergoes biological changes during cancer development, affecting cancer cell behavior and potentially limiting therapeutic efficacy. The TME encompasses several critical components, including the tumor immune microenvironment, inflammatory microenvironment, extracellular matrix, tumor vasculature, and CRC cancer stem cells. These components exhibit pro-tumor and anti-tumor functions, influencing tumor initiation, invasion, and metastasis. Tumor-infiltrating immune cells, as the central hub in TME, play a pivotal role in the initial elimination of tumors. Enhancing these immune responses by selectively targeting specific cell populations offers a promising therapeutic strategy for CRC treatment. While immunotherapy has shown promising results, its effectiveness is often limited by an immunosuppressive TME and a low immunogenicity in certain CRC subtypes. Recent research focuses on developing strategies to modify the TME, restoring anti-tumor immune cell function and improving therapeutic efficacy via approaches such as combination therapies aimed at reprogramming the tumor microenvironment to enhance immunogenicity and targeting specific immune checkpoints to stimulate immune responses.

Immunogenic cell death (ICD) is an emerging and promising approach in cancer immunotherapy, which activates the immune system against tumors. The activation of tumor cells to release tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) and tumor-specific antigens (TSAs) can serve as a foundation for immunotherapy particularly in tumors exhibiting low levels of immunogenicity, because it induces tumor-specific T lymphocytes. ICD is a type of controlled cell death that activates the host immune system in response to the release of TAAs and TSAs. This process is characterized by the release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), including calreticulin (CRT), adenosine triphosphate (ATP), and high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1). These molecules are released by dying cancer cells, serving as potent signals that alert the immune system to the presence of tumor cells. These DAMPs, acting as immune adjuvants, activate innate immune cells, including dendritic cells (DCs) and macrophages. This activation is crucial for initiating a robust immune response. DCs, in particular, play a pivotal role in capturing and presenting tumor antigens to T cells, thereby facilitating the development of a targeted anti-tumor response that leads to long-lasting immunity. So, ICD has garnered significant attention in cancer immunotherapy by combining direct tumor cytotoxicity with adjuvanticity and immunogenicity, disrupting the immunosuppressive TME, and activating innate and adaptive immune responses. This approach holds promise for preventing secondary metastases and enhancing response rates, particularly in ‘cold tumors’ with low immunogenicity. Therefore, a comprehensive understanding of the mechanisms underlying ICD and the identification of novel therapeutic factors that stimulate ICD are urgently needed to enhance the effectiveness of immunotherapy.

Endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress arises when the accumulation of unfolded or misfolded proteins surpasses the ER’s capacity, a condition triggered by factors such as hypoxia and nutrient deprivation. Targeting ER stress and unfolded protein response (UPR) components has emerged as a promising approach for the induction of ICD. While chronic persistence of mild ER stress may facilitate tumorigenesis, excessively severe ER stress can lead to cancer cell death through various mechanisms. Therefore, the severe induction of ER stress, accompanied by DAMP release and reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, can act as a specific and effective ICD inducer, offering a promising strategy in cancer treatment. A notable point in this regard is that, conventional treatments for CRC, such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy can indirectly induce robust ER stress and enhance ICD-associated immunotherapy by targeting the UPR components. Nevertheless, cancers can develop resistance to traditional therapies through various mechanisms such as drug inactivation and increased expression of different immunosuppressive genes, which can offset the impacts of ICD.

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are short non-coding RNAs that influence gene expression after transcription, playing important roles in cancer development and progression. They can also function as tumor suppressors or oncomiRs, making them interesting targets for cancer treatment. Furthermore, miRNA treatments have the benefit of simultaneously targeting many effectors of pathways important in cell differentiation, proliferation, and survival. Additionally, restoring miRNA function may re-sensitize resistant tumor cells to chemotherapeutic drugs. MiRNA-based therapeutic strategies such as the use of miRNA mimics and antisense miRNAs could have a broad and strong efficacy in cancer treatment as they have low immune-reactivity or limited half-life and diminish the concern of toxicity in vivo. So, combining miRNA therapeutics with other anticancer ways may provide synergistic effects and improve overall safety profiles in cancer patients. The interplay between ER stress and miRNAs can help to develop effective cancer treatments because certain miRNAs are essential in regulating ER stress and the UPR in cancer cells.

Interestingly, miRNAs associated with the UPR can potentially enhance ICD through the PERK/eIF2α/ATF4/CHOP signaling pathway. For instance, miR-27a, which is upregulated in CRC, has been demonstrated to inhibit drug-induced ICD by disrupting the kinetics of CRT surface exposure and impairing the secretion of ATP and HMGB1. Additionally, miRNA-489 has been shown to activate ICD in triple-negative breast cancer cells by inducing ER stress and the release of DAMPs, including CRT exposure on cellular surface and ATP release. These findings indicate the potential for miRNA-targeted therapies to improve ICD induction and cancer immunotherapy outcomes by modulating ER stress. In this regard, in our previous study, we initially systematically summarized and categorized miRNAs that play a role in various UPR pathways associated with ER stress-induced apoptosis. We then focused on miRNAs involved in the PERK/eIF2α/ATF4/CHOP axis, which is recognized as a crucial pathway for ICD. Subsequently, we conducted bioinformatics analyses to elucidate the relationships between the target proteins and signaling pathways associated with these miRNAs and ERS-induced ICD. Among the miRNAs identified, miR-451a emerged as a notable candidate that participates in multiple physiological and pathological processes, including hematopoietic system differentiation, embryonic development, epithelial cell polarity, and nervous system development. It is dysregulated in multiple cancers and participates in numerous cancer-related biological processes, including proliferation, apoptosis, angiogenesis, epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT), drug resistance, and metastasis. The acidic microenvironment decreased miR-451a expression, related to increase the proliferation, migration, invasion, and stem cell stemness of pancreatic cancer cells. Accumulating evidence highlights the pivotal role of miR-451a in CRC, where it functions as a tumor suppressor. Overexpression of miR-451a in CRC cell lines result in decreased cell growth, enhanced cell death, and changes in cell cycle advancement. MiR-451a impacts various pathways by targeting the PI3K/AKT pathway, as well as some of genes such as CAB39 and EMSY, and B cell receptor–associated protein 31 (BAP31). These findings suggest that miR-451a could be a potential therapeutic target for CRC treatment. Also, Xu and colleagues showed in their research that miR-451a could control the survival of CRC cells through the activation of ER stress. Increased levels of miR-451a raise the levels of ER stress-related proteins such as BIP and PERK/elF2α/ATF4/CHOP. Dual-luciferase reporter assays detected that BAP31 was a direct target of miR-451a. MiR-451a inhibits proliferation and increases apoptosis by suppressing BAP31 to induce ER stress in CRC. So, it seems that overexpression miR-451a in CRC cells can induce ICD via induction of ER stress and ROS generation. Based on these findings, the aim of this study was to investigate the potential of miR-451a as a tumor suppressor for the release of ICD related DAMPs in various CRC cell lines. To better estimate the scope of effects, oxaliplatin— a crucial chemotherapy drug in CRC treatment that can also induce ICD by increasing ER stress—was used as an ICD inducer, both alone and in conjunction with miR-451a. The study further investigated whether the overexpression of miR-451a, either independently or in combination with oxaliplatin, could promote the maturation of DCs.

Materials and methods

Bioinformatics analysis

For bioinformatics analysis and the identification of target mRNAs interacting with miR-451a, the multiMiR R package (version 2.3.0) was utilized in the R Studio software (version 1.2.5042). Three databases, including TargetScan, miRDB, and miRTarBase were investigated, and 20% of the highest reliable interactions from them were recognized by the multiMiR R package. Subsequently, interactions identified by strong validation methods and with context + + score < − 0.6 were selected from the miRTarBase and TargetScan results, respectively. Cytoscape software (version 3.8.1) was utilized to establish a miRNA-mRNA network. Enrichment analysis, consisting of Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathways and Gene Ontology (GO) terms were performed based on the mRNAs from the miRNA-mRNA network utilizing the Enrichr online database. The ggplot2 R package was used to generate a dot plot of the 30 most significant KEGG pathways. Furthermore, ggplot2 and ggpubr R packages were utilized for representing the 10 most significant GO terms in each GO categories (biological process (BP), cellular component (CC), and molecular function (MF)). A Protein-Protein Interaction (PPI) network was then constructed by STRING online database (version 11.5). For this aim, disconnected nodes were omitted, and the minimum required interaction score was set as the highest confidence.

Cell culture and transient transfection in vitro

Human colorectal adenocarcinoma cell lines including SW-48, SW480, SW-1116 and Caco-2 were purchased from the National Cell Bank of Iran (Pasteur Institute, Iran). The cells were cultured in high-glucose Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM; Bio Idea, Iran) with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS; Gibco, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Inc.), 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Bio Idea, Iran) and 2mM L-glutamine (Bio Idea, Iran). The cells were maintained in an incubator at 37 °C with 95% humidity and a 5% CO2 atmosphere. Logarithmically growing cells were used for all experiments.

For cell transfection, cells were seeded at a density of 70 × 103 cells/well in 24-well plates and incubated in 5% CO2 at 37 °C for 24 h to reach 50–60% confluence. Cells were then transfected with either the hsa-miR-451a mimic (AccuTarget human miRNA-451a mimic, Bioneer, Daejeon, Korea), scrambled miRNA (miRNA mimic negative control 1, Bioneer, Daejeon, Korea) or FITC-conjugated scrambled miRNA as a negative control, at a final concentration of 100 nM using Lipofectamine 2000 (Thermo Scientifc, USA) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Transfection efficiency was assessed 24 h post-transfection using florescent microscopy and flow cytometry. The relative expression of miR-451a was demonstrated by RT-qPCR. Following the confirmation of transfection efficiency and the successful overexpression of miR-451a, cells were harvested 48 h after transfection for subsequent experiments.

Real-time quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR)

Total RNA of the cells was extracted using the AnaCell Super RNA extraction kit (AnaCell; Iran) according to the manufacturer’s protocols. An equal amount of all isolated RNA was reverse-transcribed to specific cDNA using a stem-loop reverse transcription primer AnaCell kit (AnaCell; Iran). Subsequently, qPCR reactions were conducted using the Real Q Plus 2x Master Mix Green high Rox (Amplicon, Denmark) on the StepOnePlus real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, USA) and specific primer pair (AnaCell; Iran) using the following profile: 95 °C for 15 min; and 40 cycles at 95 °C for 25 s and 60 °C for 55 s. The miR-25-3p was chosen as a reference control based on research that identified the most suitable internal control gene for microRNAs in CRC and utilized to normalize miRNA expression levels. The specific sequence for the primers designed for hsa-miR-451a (Accession No. MIMAT0001631) and miR-25-3p (Accession No. MIMAT0000081) were obtained from miRBase (http://www.mirbase.org) and primers were designed by AnaCell company. Each sample was run in triplicate and the specificity of the primers was verified through a melting curve analysis. The fold changes in the relative expression of the targets were calculated using the 2−ΔΔCt method.

Cell viability analysis

To evaluate the cytotoxic effects of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and Lipofectamine 2000, an MTT (3- (4, 5‐dimethylthiazol‐2yl) ‐2, 5-diphenyltetrazolium bromide) assay was performed. Tumor cell lines were seeded at a density of 30 × 103 cells per well in 96-well plates and allowed to attach for 24 h. Next, the cells were transfected at 50–60% confluence with 100 nM hsa-miR-451a mimic and Lipofectamine 2000, and incubated for an additional 48 h. Subsequently, 100 µL of MTT solution (0.5 mg/mL, Sigma‐Aldrich, Germany) was added to each well after the removal of the culture medium and incubated at 37 °C for 4 h. Then, the MTT solution was discarded, and 100 µL of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was added to each well. The absorbance was then measured at 570 nm using an enzyme immunoassay analyzer (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). All experiments were performed in triplicate, and cell viability was normalized to that of the control group.

For the oxaliplatin treatment, cells were exposed to varying concentrations of oxaliplatin (0, 12.5, 25, 50, 75, 100, 200, and 400 µM) for 48 h. The half-maximal inhibitory concentration (IC50) of oxaliplatin (Alvoxal®, NanoAlvand, Tehran, Iran) was calculated based on the cell viability curve for each cell line obtained from the MTT assay. The concentration of the drug that resulted in IC50 growth inhibition was determined using Curve Expert Professional software (Version 1.4).

Quantitative apoptosis analysis

The quantitative assessment of cell apoptosis was conducted using an Annexin V-FITC/propidium iodide (PI) double-staining kit (BioLegend, San Diego, CA) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, cells were seeded at a density of 70 × 103 cells per well in 24-well plates and allowed to adhere overnight. Following this, the cells were treated with oxaliplatin for either 24–48 h, or they were transfected with either the hsa-miR-451a mimic, a scrambled miRNA, or a combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin for a duration of 48 h. In the group receiving the combined the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin, oxaliplatin was administered 24 h after transfection. Untreated cells served as the control group. Subsequently, a cell suspension was prepared, and 100 µL of 1× binding buffer was added to resuspend the cells. To this suspension, 5 µL of Annexin V-FITC and 10 µL of PI Staining solution were gently added, and the cells were incubated at room temperature (25 °C) for 15 min in the dark. Flow cytometry was performed using a BD FACSCalibur cytometer (Becton Dickinson, San Jose, CA, USA), and the data were analyzed using FlowJo software version 10. Cells that were Annexin V-positive and PI-negative were classified as early apoptotic, while those that were both Annexin V-positive and PI-positive were classified as late apoptotic. Together, these populations were considered total apoptotic cells.

Calreticulin expression analysis by flow cytometry

SW-48, SW-1116, SW480, and Caco-2 cells were plated at a density of 70,000 cells per well in 24-well microplates and incubated for 24 h. Subsequently, the cells were treated with oxaliplatin for either 24–48 h, or they were transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, scrambled miRNA, or a combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin for 48 h. In the group receiving the combined the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin, oxaliplatin was administered 24 h after transfection. Untreated cells served as the control group. To assess CRT expression on the cell surface, an anti-CRT antibody conjugated to phycoerythrin (PE) (Abcam, USA) was employed according to established protocols. After treatment, the cells were collected, washed twice with cold phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), and incubated at 4 °C for 40 min with the anti-CRT antibody, diluted 1:5000 in cold blocking buffer (2% fetal bovine serum in PBS). Following this incubation, the cells were washed again and treated with 7-AAD for 15 min. Subsequently, 400 µl of 1× binding buffer were added to the cells, and the percentage of live cells exhibiting both CRT-positive staining and 7-AAD-negative staining was quantified using a BD FACSCalibur cytometer (Becton Dickinson, San Jose, CA, USA).

Quantification of ATP and HMGB1

Tumor cell lines (70 × 103 cells/well) were seeded and adhered for 24 h in 24-well plates and then treated with hsa-miR-451a mimic, scrambled miRNA, oxaliplatin, or a combination of has-miR-451a and oxaliplatin for 48 h. Following treatment, supernatants were collected, and dying floating cells were removed by centrifugation before the supernatants were immediately frozen. ATP levels in the supernatants were quantified using a Luminescent ATP Detection Assay Kit (No. ab 113849, Abcam, USA). Briefly, the collected supernatants were transferred to the wells of a 96-well microplate, and 50 µl of detergent buffer (containing an ATPase inhibitor) from the kit was added. The plate was shaken for 5 min, after which 50 µl of substrate solution was added to each well. Following an additional 5 min of shaking, the plate was dark-adapted by covering it for 10 min, and luminescence was measured using a multifunctional plate reader. The amount of ATP in the supernatant was determined based on a standard curve generated from a series of known ATP concentrations. Quantification of released HMGB1 in the supernatants was assessed by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (Biorbyt, UK) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Absorbance was measured at 450 nm using an enzyme immunoassay analyzer (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA, USA). The concentration of HMGB1 in the samples was calculated by generating a standard curve with known amounts of recombinant human HMGB1 available in the kit.

In vitro generation of monocytic DCs

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from healthy donors were isolated by density gradient centrifugation using Ficoll (Lymphodex) (Inno-Train, Tehran, Iran) and were cultured in complete RPMI 1640 medium for 2 h at 37 °C. The non-adherent cells were removed and CD14 + monocytes were isolated by adherence to plastic culture flasks and cultured in DC medium consisting of RPMI 1640 supplemented with 10% FBS, 2 mM glutamine and 1% penicillin/streptomycin in the presence of 50 ng/ml (day 1) or 25 ng/ml (day 3, 5, and 7) recombinant human granulocyte/macrophage colony stimulating factor (GM-CSF; Biolegend) and 20 ng/ml (day 1) or 10 ng/ml (day 3, 5, 7) recombinant human IL-4 (Biolegend) for 7 days to obtain a population of immature DCs. Cytokines were replenished every other day along with 20% fresh medium. On day 7, non-adherent immature DCs were harvested, counted, and seeded into 96-well plates to co-culture with tumor cell lines.

Co-culture of immature DCs with tumor cell

Tumor cell lines were seeded in 24-well plates (70 × 103 cells/well) and transfected with has-miR-451a mimic, either alone or in combination with oxaliplatin, scrambled miRNA, and oxaliplatin alone. After 48 h treatment, cells were washed and co-cultured with immature DCs at a ratio of 1:1 in RPMI 1640 supplemented with GM-CSF (25 ng/mL), IL-4 (10 ng/mL) and 10% FBS for 24 h. Also, immature DCs that were co-cultured with untreated tumor cells or stimulated with 0.1 mg/mL lipopolysaccharide (LPS; Sigma Aldrich, L2630) for 24 h were used as control groups. The co-culture of immature DCs with tumor cells was carried out together in the same compartment 96-well culture plate. To determine the maturation of mo-DCs by flow cytometry, cells were washed twice with PBS and incubated with PBS containing 3% normal human serum for 30 min at 4 °C to reduce nonspecific binding to FcRs and subsequently stained with PE-conjugated anti-human CD11c, FITC-conjugated anti-human CD80, and APC-conjugated anti-human CD86 antibodies (Biolegend, USA). Mature DCs were identified by calculating the percentage of CD86 + and CD80 + cells within the CD11c + DC population. Data analysis was performed using Flowjo software 10.

Statistical analysis

All experiments were repeated at least three times, and statistical analysis were performed using GraphPad Prism 9.0 (GraphPad Software, San Diego, CA). One-way ANOVA with post hoc Tukey’s multiple comparisons tests was used to compare variables. The data were expressed as the mean ± standard deviation. The P-values of < 0.05 were considered to represent significant differences.

Results

miRNA-mRNA network, Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes, gene ontology, and protein-protein interaction network analysis

Following our pervious study, was performed a bioinformatics analysis aimed at better identifying target gene and proteins, important signaling pathways, biological processes, molecular functions, and cellular components related to the miR-451a. A miRNA-mRNA network including 38 mRNAs interacting with miR-451a was established (Fig. 1A). Among these mRNA can be pointed out mRNA related to MYC, AKT1, IL-6R, MIF, CAB39, and ATF2 genes that have key role in cellular processes and cancer development. The KEGG pathways were mainly related to “Hepatitis B”, “Human cytomegalovirus infection”, “PI3K-Akt signaling pathway”, “TNF signaling pathway”, “Pathways in cancer”, “mTOR signaling pathway”, and “MAPK signaling pathway” (Fig. 1B). Furthermore, significant GO terms consisting of BP, CC, and MF were recognized (Fig. 1C). Among the GO terms, negative regulation of apoptotic process (GO: 0043066), interleukin-6 receptor binding (GO: 0005138), and ficolin-1-rich granule lumen (GO: 1904813) were the most significant terms relating to the BP, MF, and CC categories, respectively. A PPI network including 15 nodes and 18 edges was constructed (Fig. 1D). Among these proteins can be pointed out MAPK1, AKT1, BCL2, ATF2, IL-6, IL-6R, and MMPs. Therefore, these results reflect the potential mechanism of miR-451a in cell proliferation, apoptosis, invasion, migration, EMT, drug resistance and angiogenesis by regulating different signaling pathways.

The miR-451a expression in CRC cell lines

Four types of colon cancer cells (SW48, SW480, SW1116 and Caco2) were selected based on consensus molecular subtypes (CMS) to detect the expression level of miR-451a in CRC cell lines. The qRT-PCR analysis revealed that the expression level of miR-451a was significantly lower in all four CRC cell lines when compared to human primary colonic fibroblasts isolated from normal colon tissue. The miR-451a levels were lower in SW1116 and Caco2 compared to SW48 and SW480. This finding suggests a potential downregulation of miR-451a in the context of CRC, highlighting its possible role as a tumor suppressor in cancerous cells (Fig. 2A). In order to further explore the effects of miR-451a in CRC, various cell lines were transfected with the mimic of has-miR-451a. First, the cells were transfected with FITC-labeled scrambled oligonucleotides using lipofectamine 2000 to assess the feasibility of introducing these oligonucleotides into the cell lines via this method. Fluorescence microscopy revealed the presence of green fluorescent cells and flow cytometry indicated that over 50% of the cells were successfully transfected in four different cell lines. These findings suggest that scrambled oligonucleotides were effective in entering the cells through this methodology. The SW1116 cell line demonstrated the highest transfection efficiency at 75%, which may significantly impact subsequent experiments designed to evaluate the functional roles of miR-451a (Fig. 2B). Subsequently, cell lines were transfected with either the hsa-miR-451a mimic or scrambled miRNA, and the expression of miR-451a was evaluated using qRT-PCR 24 h post-transfection. The levels of miR-451a were significantly elevated in the different cell lines transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic (P < 0.0001), while the scrambled miRNA exhibited no significant increase in miR-451a expression when compared to the control group. The relative fold change of miR-451a in SW48 and SW1116 was greater than that observed in SW480 and Caco2 following transfection, which was aligned with the transfection efficiency percentages (Fig. 2C). Overall, the significant elevation of miR-451a levels post-transfection further supports its potential as a therapeutic target, as restoring its expression may counteract the tumorigenic processes associated with its downregulation. Additionally, the expression level of miR-451a in cells treated with oxaliplatin was assessed, and the results indicated that this drug cannot induce miR-451a expression in different cell lines. This finding demonstrated that miR-451a may not play a direct role in the response to oxaliplatin and combining miR-451a mimic therapy with oxaliplatin may enhance treatment efficacy.

The effect of miR-451a on CRC cell growth and apoptosis

To investigate the effects of miR-451a on the cell growth and proliferation of CRC cell lines, cells were transfected with 100 nM of has-miR-451a mimic and 48 h after transfection MTT assay was conducted. The results indicated that overexpression of miR-451a in cell lines significantly reduced cell viability compared to the control group (P < 0.0001). Additionally, MTT assay revealed that Lipofectamine 2000 had minimal adverse effects on cell proliferation and viability (Fig. 3A). This finding validated the observed outcomes being attributed to the action of the miR-451a mimic rather than the transfection method itself. Furthermore, a MTT assay was performed to determine the oxaliplatin IC50 values for each cell line, which are essential for subsequent experiments. The IC50 values obtained were as follows: SW48 (134 µM), SW480 (39 µM), SW1116 (132 µM), and Caco-2 (100 µM). The varying IC50 values—ranging from 39 µM in SW480 to 134 µM in SW48—highlight the differential responses of CRC cell lines to oxaliplatin. The heightened sensitivity of SW480 cells to oxaliplatin suggests that these cells may possess unique molecular characteristics that amenable to therapeutic interventions involving oxaliplatin.

To assess the level of apoptosis induced by transfection with the hsa-miR-451a mimic compared to other experimental groups, an Annexin V-FITC/PI Kit was utilized. The results indicated that apoptosis was significantly elevated in four cell lines (SW48, SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2) transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, both alone and in conjunction with oxaliplatin, similar to oxaliplatin alone, compared to both the control and scrambled miRNA groups (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, P < 0.001 and P < 0.0001) (Fig. 3B, C and D, and 3E). The treatment of SW48 and Caco-2 cell lines with a combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin resulted in higher levels of apoptosis compared to hsa-miR-451a group and oxaliplatin alone after 48 h; however, this increase was not statistically significant (Fig. 3B and E). Conversely, in SW1116 and SW480 cell lines, treatment with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h led to a greater increase in apoptosis compared to the combination treatment of hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin, although this difference was not statistically significant (Fig. 3C and D). Treatment of SW1116 and SW480 cell lines with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h resulted in a significantly greater induction of apoptosis compared to the has-miR-451a group (Fig. 3C and D). Nevertheless, oxaliplatin did not induce a more apoptotic than hsa-miR-451a mimic in the SW48 and Caco-2 cell lines (Fig. 3B and E). Incubation of SW48, SW1116, and Caco-2 cell lines with the has-miR-451a mimic for 48 h, in combination with oxaliplatin for 24 h, induced more apoptosis than treatment with oxaliplatin alone for 24 h (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, and P < 0.0001) (Fig. 3B and C, and 3E); however, this increase in apoptosis was not statistically significant in SW480 cells (Fig. 3D). Overall, these findings indicate that although the hsa-miR-451a mimic enhances apoptosis in various cell lines, the effects of oxaliplatin may vary depending on the specific cellular context. This variability may limit the potential for synergistic effects between the two treatments.

The ability of miR-451a in release of ICD related DAMPS in CRC cell lines

To determine whether cell death induced by miR-451a is immunogenic, we examined the cell surface exposure of CRT and the release of ATP and HMGB1 from cells following the transfection of miR-451a into CRC cell lines. After transfecting the hsa-miR-451a mimic into CRC cell lines, either alone or in combination oxaliplatin, the levels of cell surface CRT was measured using flow cytometry in the viable cell population which was defined as 7-AAD negative and compared these results to those from groups treated with scrambled miRNA, oxaliplatin, and the control. The cell surface exposure of CRT in cell lines transfected with 100 nM of has-miR-451a, both alone and in combination with oxaliplatin, similar to oxaliplatin treatment alone, was significantly higher than that observed in the control and scrambled miRNA groups in the each of four CRC cell lines (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, P < 0.001 and P < 0.0001) (Fig. 4A, B and C, and 4D). The increase in plasma membrane CRT levels in SW48 and Caco-2 cell lines was significantly enhanced by the addition of oxaliplatin to cells transfected with hsa-miR-451a compared to treatment with the hsa-miR-451a mimic alone (P < 0.05 and P < 0.01). However, this increase was not statistically significant when compared to the effects of oxaliplatin treatment alone for 48 h (Fig. 4A and D). In SW1116 and SW480 cell lines, the increase in sCRT levels in the group treated with the hsa-miR-451a mimic combined with oxaliplatin was not statistically significant when compared to the group treated with the hsa-miR-451a mimic alone (Fig. 4B and C). Treatment of SW1116 and SW480 cell lines with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h induced a greater increase in CRT levels compared to the combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin. This effect was statistically significant in SW1116 cells (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 4B). Incubation of cell lines with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h led to a significant increase in surface CRT levels compared to the miR-451a group in the SW1116 cell lines (Fig. 4B); however, it increase did not reach statistical significance in the SW48, SW480, and Caco-2 cell lines (Fig. 4A and C, and 4D). After a 24 h treatment of SW48, SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2 cell lines with oxaliplatin alone, the levels of surface CRT were significantly lower than those observed in the group transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic for 48 h in combination with oxaliplatin for 24 h (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, and P < 0.0001) (Fig. 4A, B and C, and 4D). These findings suggest that miR-451a overexpression can enhance the exposure of CRT in CRC cell lines.

Quantification of extracellular ATP levels using the luminescent ATP detection assay revealed a trend of increased ATP in cell lines transfected with has-miR-451a mimic. This increase was statistically significant in SW48 and SW1116 cell lines when compared to both control and scrambled miRNA groups; however, it was not significant in SW480 and Caco-2 cell lines (Fig. 4E). However, the combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic with oxaliplatin, similar to oxaliplatin alone, significantly elevated ATP secretion in the four CRC cell lines compared to both control and scrambled miRNA groups. The addition of oxaliplatin to cells transfected with hsa-miR-451a significantly enhanced the secretion of extracellular ATP in SW48, SW1116, and Caco-2 cell lines compared to treatment with hsa-miR-451a alone (P < 0.01, P < 0.001, and P < 0.05, respectively) (Fig. 4E). Also, in SW1116 cells, extracellular ATP levels in the group transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic in combination with oxaliplatin were significantly higher than those observed in the oxaliplatin-only treatment group (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 4E). The incubation of SW48 and Caco-2 cells with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h resulted in an enhancement in the secretion of extracellular ATP compared to the hsa-miR-451a group and the combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic with oxaliplatin. This enhancement was statistically significant only when compared to the hsa-miR-451a group (Fig. 4E).

HMGB1 was detected in the supernatants of both unstimulated and stimulated cells after 48 h using a human HMGB1 ELISA kit. The results indicated that increase in HMGB1 levels in SW48 and Caco-2 cells transfected with has-miR-451a was not statistically significant when compared to the control and scrambled miRNA groups. However, HMGB1 levels were significantly elevated in both the oxaliplatin group and the group receiving a combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin in SW48 and Caco-2 cells, compared to the control and scrambled miRNA groups (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, and P < 0.001) (Fig. 4F). In the SW48 cell line, the increase in HMGB1 levels in the group treated with the hsa-miR-451a mimic in combination with oxaliplatin was statistically significant compared to the group treated with the hsa-miR-451a mimic alone (P < 0.05), but this increase was not significant when compared to the oxaliplatin-only treatment. In SW1116 cells, no significant increase in HMGB1 levels was observed among the groups. Also, in SW480 cells, only the oxaliplatin treatment resulted in a significant increase in HMGB1 compared to both the control and scrambled miRNA groups (P < 0.05) (Fig. 4F).

The role of miR-451a in the in vitro DC maturation

To further evaluate the immunogenicity of the dead CRC cell lines obtained through transfection with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, both alone and in combination with oxaliplatin, as well as oxaliplatin treatment alone, the capacity of these cell groups to induce DC maturation were assessed in comparison to scrambled miRNA and untreated cells groups. For this analysis monocyte-derived immature DCs, characterized as CD11c + with low levels of CD80 and CD86, were co-cultured with killed SW48, SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2 cell lines in various experimental groups, as detailed in the methods section, and the maturation of CD11c + DCs was evaluated by measuring CD80 and CD86 expression levels using flow cytometry (Fig. 5A). In the co-culture of immature DC with CRC cell lines transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, both alone and in combination with oxaliplatin, similar to oxaliplatin treatment alone, the percentage of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + DCs was significantly higher than scrambled miRNA and control groups in all four cell lines (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, P < 0.001 and P < 0.0001) (Fig. 5B). The findings were consistent with the response seen in LPS-stimulated immature DCs, used as a positive control. However, LPS stimulation resulted in an increase in the frequency of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + DCs, reaching 40%. This proportion was significantly higher than that observed in all other experimental groups (P < 0.001 and P < 0.0001). The addition of oxaliplatin to SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2 cell lines after transfection with miR-451a significantly increased the percentage of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + DCs in co-cultures compared to cells treated with miR-451a alone (P < 0.05, P < 0.01, and P < 0.001). However, this increase was statistically significant only in SW480 when compared to the oxaliplatin-only treatment (Fig. 5B). Incubation of SW1116 cells with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h led to a significant increase in the percentage of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + DCs in co-culture compared to the miR-451a group (P < 0.0001) (Fig. 5B).

Discussion

ICD is emerging as a promising therapeutic approach that can increase responsiveness to immunotherapy in various cancers such as CRC. The induction of ICD can activate the TME by enhancing tumor immunogenicity, promoting T cell infiltration, and modulating the immunosuppressive features of the TME, potentially improving the efficacy of cancer immunotherapies. Various anticancer therapies, including chemotherapy, can induce ICD and reshape the TME. However, cancers can develop resistance to traditional therapies through different mechanisms such as drug inactivation, target al.teration, efflux, and DNA repair. Therefore, the development of novel ICD-inducing therapies presents significant potential for improving the efficacy of immunotherapies. MiRNAs offer a unique advantage in cancer treatment due to simultaneously targeting multiple genes, thereby influencing various cancer-related processes. Approaches such as the use of synthetic miRNA mimics, anti-sense oligonucleotides, and natural agents targeting specific microRNAs, can effectively overcome drug resistance and enhance the efficacy of cancer therapies. In our previous study, we focused on miRNAs involved in the PERK/eIF2α/ATF4/CHOP axis, which is recognized as a crucial pathway for ICD and conducted bioinformatics analysis to elucidate the relationships between the target proteins and signaling pathways associated with these miRNAs and ERS-induced ICD. Among the identified microRNAs, miR-451a has emerged as a prominent candidate, with growing evidence highlighting its essential role as a tumor suppressor in CRC. The present study, aimed to evaluate the immunogenicity of dead CRC cell lines obtained through transfection with the hsa-miR-451a mimic, both alone and in combination with oxaliplatin, while comparing these effects to those induced by oxaliplatin alone, scrambled miRNA, and untreated control groups. The results of this study provide critical insights into the role of miR-451a in various CRC cell lines categorized by distinct CMS, emphasizing its potential significance as a tumor suppressor. To the best of our knowledge, this study is the first to demonstrate that miR-451a can affect release of ICD related DAMPs in CRC cell lines potentially through mechanisms involving ER stress and had effects on DC maturation.

Previous studies have shown that miR-451a levels are significantly lower in CRC tissues and cell lines compared to normal tissues and cells. This downregulation of miR-451a is associated with aggressive tumor characteristics, such as increased cell proliferation, colony formation, invasion, and drug resistance. Consistent these investigations, our results of four distinct CRC cell lines—SW48, SW1116, SW480 and Caco-2—revealed a downregulation of miR-451a compared to normal colonic fibroblasts. Furthermore, transfection of these cell lines with the hsa-miR-451a mimic resulted in a marked reduction in cell viability. This reduction confirms the potential tumor-suppressive effects of miR-451a, indicating that its overexpression may inhibit CRC cell proliferation and induce apoptosis. To further explore this, we transfected the hsa-miR-451a mimic into the cells and examined the effects of miR-451a overexpression on apoptosis, the release of ICD-related DAMPs, and the maturation of immature DCs in CRC cell lines in vitro. In our experiments, SW1116 and Caco-2 cells had a relatively lower expression of miR-451a compared to SW48 and SW480. Notably, transfection efficiency in SW48 and, particularly, SW1116 cell lines was higher than in SW480 and Caco-2. These differential characteristics may reflect the distinct molecular profiles inherent to each cell line. Overall, the selection of these specific cell lines was based on the diversity of CRC subtypes. SW48 was selected from CMS1 (14%), which exhibits microsatellite instability (MSI) along with significant anti-tumor immune activation. SW1116 selected from CMS2 (37%) that exhibits epithelial characteristics with marked WNT and MYC activated pathway signaling and low mutation rates. Both SW480 and Caco2 were selected from CMS4 (23%), which displays attributes of EMT accompanied by significant stromal invasion and angiogenesis via the activation of the TGF-β and VEGFR pathways. Consequently, in our study, the observed different responses appear to be due to the type of cell line selected according to CRC subtypes, because as mentioned above, these cells are placed in different subtypes, and their gene expression profiles, and functional properties can vary widely.

Our bioinformatics analysis suggests that miR-451a may play a pivotal role in regulating apoptosis and cell proliferation by targeting specific genes involved in key signaling pathways, including the PI3K-Akt, MAPK, mTOR, FOXO, and JAK-STAT pathways. In our study, apoptosis assessment using the Annexin V-FITC/PI assay revealed that transfection with the hsa-miR-451a mimic—either alone or in combination with oxaliplatin—significantly increased apoptotic rates across all tested CRC cell lines compared to both the control and scrambled miRNA groups. These effects were comparable to those observed with oxaliplatin treatment alone.

Our findings align with previous studies demonstrating that miR-451a overexpression suppresses tumor cell proliferation and promotes apoptosis through the downregulation of key oncogenic targets such as IL-6R, MYC, PSMB8, DCBLD2, BCL-2, MIF and AKT1 in various cancer types. Notably, our results indicate that miR-451a contributes to the induction of apoptosis. However, while its combination with oxaliplatin significantly enhanced the apoptotic responsein CRC cell lines compared of 24 h of oxaliplatin treatment alone, it did not significantly increase apoptosis beyond what is achieved with oxaliplatin alone after 48 h. Interestingly, SW480 cells exhibited greater susceptibility to oxaliplatin-induced apoptosis compared to other cell lines. In SW1116 and SW480 cells, treatment with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h led to a significantly higher induction of apoptosis than transfection with miR-451a. In contrast, in SW48 and Caco-2 cells, the hsa-miR-451a mimic induced levels of apoptosis comparable to or exceeding those achieved with oxaliplatin. These observations suggest that the molecular mechanisms underlying apoptosis differ among CRC cell lines. A deeper understanding of these context-dependent pathways is essential for optimizing therapeutic strategies that integrate miR-451a with oxaliplatin.

In a study, Xu et al. demonstrated that an acidic tumor microenvironment (TME) downregulates miR-451a expression in pancreatic cancer cells, which in turn promotes enhanced cell proliferation, migration, and invasion. Their findings revealed that miR-451a overexpression targets and suppresses the MEF2D gene, thereby counteracting the detrimental effects of acidic conditions. This leads to increased oxygen consumption, disruption of mitochondrial membrane potential, and elevated reactive oxygen species (ROS) production, ultimately promoting apoptosis. As previously mentioned, the release of DAMPs correlates with the induction of severe ER stress and increased ROS production. Therefore, it appears that miR-451a, in addition to inducing apoptosis, may facilitate the release of CRT, ATP, and HMGB1. In our study, transfection with the hsa-miR-451a mimic resulted in significantly elevated levels of cell surface exposure of CRT in CRC cell lines compared to control and scrambled miRNA groups. Previous studies have demonstrated that the cell surface exposure of CRT in response to ICD inducers involves several critical steps. These steps include the activation of the PERK pathway, the phosphorylation of eIF2α, the caspase-8-dependent cleavage of BAP31, and the subsequent activation of BCL2-associated X protein (Bax) and Bcl-2 homologous antagonist/killer (Bak). BAP31 plays a crucial role in impairing protein transport from the ER to the Golgi complex. The proteolytic activation of caspase-8 is essential for the cleavage of BAP31, which facilitates the translocation of CRT from the endoplasmic reticulum to the plasma membrane. Consequently, the knockdown of BAP31 or the mutation of BAP31 to render it uncleavable similarly disrupts CRT exposure. Xu et al. reported that BAP31 protein expression is significantly elevated in CRC cell lines and tissues compared to normal colonic epithelial cells and pericarcinous tissues. They found that the overexpression of miR-451a in CRC cell lines resulted in a marked downregulation of BAP31, which is a target gene of miR-451a. Their results indicated that both the overexpression of miR-451a and the silencing of BAP31 inhibited CRC cell proliferation and induced apoptosis by upregulating ER stress-associated proteins, including GRP78/BIP, BAX, and the PERK/eIF2α/ATF4/CHOP pathway. Our bioinformatics analysis demonstrated that the PI3K/Akt signaling pathway is a significant pathway identified in the KEGG analysis of miR-451a. This pathway promotes cell proliferation and prevents ROS-induced cell death, thereby sustaining cell growth under persistent ER stress conditions commonly found in tumors. Furthermore, it has been shown that the inhibition of the PI3K/Akt pathway increases the expression of C/EBP homologous protein (CHOP), which is generally regarded as a pro-apoptotic protein in the context of ER stress. Consequently, miR-451a has the potential to enhance the exposure of CRT on the cell surface, likely through mechanisms involving the induction of ER stress and the production of ROS. As earlier noted, the results of the current study demonstrated that the transfection of miR-451a enhances sCRT exposure in CRC cell lines. The increase in sCRT levels in miR-451a-transfected SW48 and Caco-2 cell lines was further amplified following the addition of oxaliplatin. While the combination of miR-451a and oxaliplatin significantly enhanced sCRT levels compared to 24 h of oxaliplatin treatment alone, this increase was not statistically significant when compared to the sCRT levels achieved with oxaliplatin alone after 48 h in these cell lines. Notably, in SW1116 and SW480 cell lines, treatment with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h resulted in higher sCRT levels compared to the combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin and other groups. However, the combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin still significantly enhanced sCRT levels in these cell lines compared to 24 h of oxalipatin treatment alone. Our results also indicated that oxaliplatin does not induce miR-451a in CRC cell lines, suggesting that miR-451a may not play a role in the mechanisms underlying the ICD induced by oxaliplatin.

The quantification of extracellular ATP levels using the luminescent ATP detection assay revealed a notable trend of increased ATP secretion in cell lines transfected with the hsa-miR-451a mimic. This increase was statistically significant in the SW48 and SW1116 cell lines when compared to both control and scrambled miRNA groups. However, it is important to note that this effect was not observed in the SW480 and Caco-2 cell lines, suggesting a differential response to miR-451a transfection that may be influenced by the unique molecular characteristics of each cell line. The addition of oxaliplatin to cells transfected with the miR-451a mimic further enhanced the secretion of extracellular ATP in SW48, SW1116, and Caco-2 cell lines, with statistically significant increases observed compared to miR-451a treatment alone. However, combination of the hsa-miR-451a mimic and oxaliplatin only in SW1116 cells resulted in extracellular ATP levels that were significantly higher than those observed with oxaliplatin treatment alone. I Martins et al. have reported that multiple mechanisms such as autophagy, lysosomal exocytosis, apoptosis, membrane blebbing, and plasma membrane permeabilization are involved in ATP secretion during ICD. Defects in any of these systems can prevent ATP release from cells exposed to ICD inducers. They also demonstrated that ICD inducers, such as mitoxantrone and oxaliplatin, activate several cytoprotective and cytotoxic signaling pathways, including the ER stress response, the caspase cascade, and autophagy, which the interaction among these pathways is essential for ATP release associated with ICD. The enhancements observed in ATP secretion in response to miR-451a and oxaliplatin may reflect the activation of these pathways, thereby contributing to the overall efficacy of ICD. However, this effect appears to vary among different cell lines, likely due to different targets of miR-451a and the intrinsic properties of the cells themselves.

HMGB1 is a typical DAMP that can be released during various types of cell death, including pyroptosis, necrosis, apoptosis, necroptosis, ferroptosis, and NETosis. Its release mechanisms are diverse and can be both passive and active, depending on the type of cell death and cellular conditions. In our study, transfection with has-miR-451a alone did not result in a statistically significant elevation of HMGB1 in CRC cell lines. Increasing evidence emphasizes the critical roles of ROS, tumor necrosis factor (TNF), mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK), signal transducer and activator of transcription (STAT), and inflammasomes in the active secretion and passive release of HMGB1 in both immune and non-immune cells. Disruption of the JAK-STAT and MAPK signaling pathways, as well as direct suppression of TNF, inhibits HMGB1 release in response to various exogenous and endogenous danger signals. Our bioinformatics analysis revealed that miR-451a targets genes such as IL-6, IL-6R, and MAPK, as well as pathways including the TNF signaling pathway and MAPK signaling pathway. Furthermore, previous studies have indicated that miR-451a suppresses inflammation by targeting specific molecules, including JAK-STAT, TNF-α and IL-6, in various cell types. Therefore, our experiments likely failed to demonstrate HMGB1 release due to the targeting of essential pathways involved in HMGB1 release by miR-451a. Previous studies have demonstrated that oxaliplatin promotes the expression of HMGB1 in CRC cells. In our study, both oxaliplatin alone and the combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin resulted in a significant elevation of HMGB1 levels in SW48 and Caco-2 cells compared to both control and scrambled miRNA groups. In SW480 cells, only the oxaliplatin treatment led to a significant elevation of HMGB1. Importantly, the combination of miR-451a with oxaliplatin seemed to suppress this elevation, indicating that miR-451a may counteract the HMGB1 increase induced by oxaliplatin in this specific cell line. The lack of significant HMGB1 elevation in SW1116 cells across all treatment groups raises questions about the cell line-specific responses to these treatments, indicating that genetic or epigenetic factors may influence HMGB1 expression.

In the co-culture of immature DC with killed SW48, SW1116, SW480, and Caco-2 cell lines in various experimental groups, a notable increase in the percentage of CD11c + CD86 + CD80 + mature DCs was seen in the miR-451a group compared to the scrambled miRNA and control groups. This suggests that miR-451a may play a crucial role in promoting the maturation of DCs, potentially by enhancing the immunogenicity of tumor cells. The role of miR-451a in the maturation and function of DCs within the tumor microenvironment has not yet been explored. However, existing literature suggests that miR-451a via suppressing the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6, TNF, CCL5/RANTES, and CCL3/MIP1α along with a reduction in endogenous 14-3-3ζ protein levels, can lead to decreased expression of type I interferons in macrophages and dendritic cells in responses to influenza whole-virus vaccine. Studies have shown that miR-451a inhibits CD4 + T cell activation and proliferation by targeting MYC. Therefore, it appears that the effects of miR-451a differ between immune cells and tumor cells, and further studies are necessary to explore the underlying mechanisms and potential therapeutic implications of miR-451a within the TME.

In conclusion, this study provides significant insights into the role of miR-451a as a potential tumor-suppressive microRNA and its influence on ICD across various CRC cell lines (Fig. 6). Our findings demonstrate that miR-451a overexpression enhances apoptosis, inhibits cell proliferation, and increases the surface exposure of CRT in CRC cell lines with distinct molecular profiles—effects comparable to those induced by oxaliplatin treatment. Furthermore, while miR-451a could promote extracellular ATP and HMGB1 release, as well as DC maturation, these effects were variable among cell lines and generally less pronounced compared to oxaliplatin. This variability may be attributed to the pleiotropic nature of miR-451a, which simultaneously targets multiple genes and thereby modulates diverse signaling pathways involved in ICD induction.

Overall, the combination of oxaliplatin and miR-451a frequently failed to produce synergistic anticancer effects in our experimental setting when compared to treatment with oxaliplatin alone for 48 h. This lack of synergy is likely attributable to multiple biological, pharmacological, and mechanistic factors. Chemotherapeutic agents such as oxaliplatin primarily exert their effects through the induction of DNA damage, whereas miRNAs function as post-transcriptional regulators of gene expression. When both a miRNA and a chemotherapeutic agent target overlapping pathways, the mechanisms of action may interfere with one another, potentially diminishing the overall therapeutic impact. In such cases, the effects may not be additive, as they converge on similar endpoints without amplifying the desired response.

Furthermore, miRNAs have the capacity to regulate hundreds of target genes simultaneously, which can lead to unintended off-target effects that may counteract or diminish the benefits conferred by chemotherapy. Additionally, the optimal timing for miRNA-mediated gene silencing may not coincide with the peak activity of the chemotherapeutic agent. Therefore, defining the appropriate dosing regimen and treatment schedule for combining miR-451a with oxaliplatin is crucial for maximizing therapeutic efficacy.

Importantly, the absence of synergistic effects across all tested cell lines highlights the role of tumor heterogeneity in shaping the response to combinatorial therapies. Tumors are composed of diverse cellular populations with distinct genetic and epigenetic landscapes; consequently, a miRNA that effectively sensitizes one subset of cancer cells may not exert the same effect on others. These findings underscore the importance of considering molecular subtype-specific responses when designing miRNA-based therapeutic strategies in combination with conventional chemotherapies.

Future studies should aim to further elucidate the therapeutic potential of miR-451a within the TME by expanding investigations to include a broader spectrum of CRC cell lines and patient-derived models. Employing advanced delivery systems, such as viral or non-viral vectors for stable miRNA expression, could provide more consistent and physiologically relevant outcomes. Additionally, in-depth exploration of the specific molecular interactions and signaling pathways modulated by miR-451a—including the PERK/eIF2α/ATF4/CHOP pathway, ROS production, BAP31 cleavage, and PI3K-Akt signaling axis —will enhance our understanding of its mechanistic role in immunogenic cell death and immune modulation.

Moreover, systematic evaluation of various dosing regimens, treatment schedules, and alternative combination strategies—particularly with immunotherapeutic agents—could optimize the efficacy of miR-451a-based therapies.

Such comprehensive approaches are essential for translating these findings into clinically relevant applications and for tailoring miR-451a interventions to the molecular heterogeneity of colorectal tumors.

Furthermore, future investigations should include direct assessment of phagocytic activity by antigen-presenting cells (e.g., macrophages and dendritic cells) toward miR-451a-treated tumor cells. Co-culture experiments using monocyte-derived macrophages or DCs would help validate the pro-phagocytic effects of CRT exposure and other ICD-related DAMPs, thereby strengthening the evidence for miR-451a’s role in promoting anti-tumor immunity.

Taken together, these directions provide a robust framework for advancing miR-451a-based therapeutic strategies and may ultimately contribute to the development of novel, mechanism-driven immunotherapies for CRC.
