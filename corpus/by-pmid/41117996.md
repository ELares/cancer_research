# NKG2D CAR-NK adoptive cellular immunotherapy combined with or without PD-1 blockade in the treatment of patients with metastatic colorectal cancer: an exploratory study.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Colorectal cancer (CRC) remains a leading cause of cancer-related death worldwide, with limited treatment options for patients with metastatic disease. Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-engineered natural killer (NK) cells represent a novel immunotherapeutic approach with potential advantages in safety and allogeneic applicability.

**METHODS**: This is a multi-arm phase I clinical trial (NCT05213195) evaluating the safety and feasibility of NKG2D-based CAR-NK cells expressing membrane-bound IL-15 (mbIL-15) in advanced CRC. Here, we report the results from the cohort expansion stage of this trial. Totally, six heavily pretreated patients were enrolled and received lymphodepletion chemotherapy followed by four infusions of CAR-NK cells in 2 weeks. Two cohorts were designed to assess extended infusion routes and combination with anti-PD-1 antibodies.

**RESULTS**: CAR-NK therapy was well tolerated, with no treatment-related death or serious non-hematologic toxicities. Common adverse events included reversible cytokine-release syndrome (CRS) and mild gastrointestinal symptoms. Notably, potential clinical benefits were observed in patients who received CAR-NK cells in combination with anti-PD-1 therapy, including one patient who achieved stable disease (SD) and two others who experienced prolonged overall survival exceeding 700 days, suggesting a potential synergistic effect. Meanwhile, CAR-NK cells were detectable in peripheral blood within two weeks after infusion. And the combination with PD-1 blockade was associated with substantially increased peak CAR transgene levels compared to monotherapy (median CAR copies, 45,593 versus 1001).

**CONCLUSION**: NKG2D CAR-NK cell therapy, alone or combined with PD-1 blockade, demonstrated a favorable safety profile and potential clinical benefit in advanced CRC, warranting further investigation in larger cohorts.

Introduction

Colorectal cancer (CRC) was one of the leading causes of cancer-related mortality worldwide. Patients with metastatic disease typically have a poor clinical prognosis, with a five-year survival rate of approximately 15%. Systemic therapies combining traditional chemotherapy and targeted agents remain the standard treatment for advanced CRC, although many patients develop resistance or achieve limited benefit. Only a small subset of CRC patients with mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) or microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) molecular subtypes may respond to emerging immune checkpoint inhibitor therapies. These limitations underscore the urgent need for more effective and broadly applicable treatment strategies.

Cell-based immunotherapies offer a novel approach worth further investigation. Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphoid cells capable of rapidly eliminating virus-infected and malignant cells without prior antigen exposure. Notably, their ability to target cells with downregulated MHC class I expression makes NK cells well-suited for combating immune-evasive tumors. Genetic engineering allows NK cells to express chimeric antigen receptors (CARs), enhancing their specificity, cytotoxicity, and persistence for adoptive immunotherapy. Although CAR-NK therapies have shown encouraging outcomes in hematologic malignancies, clinical studies in solid tumors remain scarce and largely preclinical.

NKG2D is a transmembrane glycoprotein activating receptor expressed on the surface of NK cells and recognizes a remarkably diverse array of ligands including MICA, MICB, and up to six different UL16-binding proteins. These ligands are typically absent or minimally expressed on the surface of healthy cells but can be upregulated in response to various cellular stress signals. Previous studies have demonstrated that NKG2D ligands are frequently expressed in primary colorectal tumors, suggesting that NKG2D may serve as a promising immunotherapeutic target for the treatment of advanced CRC. Membrane-bound IL-15 (mbIL-15) was also used to enhance NK cell survival, proliferation, and in vivo persistence.

In our previously published results from the lead-in stage (stage 1) of the ongoing phase I clinical trial (NCT05213195), intraperitoneal (IP) infusion of NKG2D CAR-NK cells in heavily pretreated CRC patients with peritoneal metastases demonstrated acceptable safety and preliminary antitumor activity. Locoregional infusion of NKG2D CAR-NK cells induced systemic immune activation, with expansion of an activated CD8⁺ T-cell subset that retained effector function while expressing PD-1. This immune transformation appeared to be closely associated with TCR pathway activation and cytokine secretion during CAR-NK-mediated tumor lysis, and created a potential therapeutic window for PD-1 blockade. Based on these findings, we defined the recommended dose for subsequent infusions and initiated stage 2 of this trial, which included an expanded infusion strategy and combination treatment with PD-1 blockade. In this study, we present updated data of this trial, aiming to further evaluate the safety and therapeutic potential of this optimized CAR-NK cell-based approach in advanced CRC.

Materials and methods

Study description

This study was conducted as part of a single-center, open-label, phase I clinical trial designed to evaluate the safety and preliminary antitumor activity of NKG2D CAR-NK cell therapy in advanced CRC with or without peritoneal metastases. The trial was registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05213195). Eligible patients were required to have an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0 to 1, along with adequate cardiac, hepatic, and renal function. All enrolled patients had at least one measurable lesion and had experienced disease progression following at least one line of prior systemic therapy. Detailed inclusion and exclusion criteria are provided in Table S1. As shown in Figure S1, the CAR construct used in this study consisted of the NKG2D ectodomain fused to a streptavidin tag, followed by the CD28 transmembrane domain, the 4-1BB costimulatory domain, and the CD3ζ signaling domain. It was co-expressed with the mbIL-15 gene.

In the first phase of the trial (stage 1), a classical “3 + 3” dose-escalation design was employed to determine the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and/or the recommended dose for expansion (RDE) of NKG2D CAR-NK cells in advanced CRC. In stage 1, patients with peritoneal metastases were treated via IP infusion. The current study represents the second phase (stage 2) of the clinical trial and serves as a cohort expansion study. Given the favorable safety profile observed in stage 1, we selected the highest tested dose from that stage for further evaluation. In addition, by referencing data from a phase II NK cell therapy trial, we finally determined 2 × 109 CAR-NK cells as the RDE.

As shown in Fig. 1, stage 2 comprises two cohorts: cohort 1, which evaluates extended infusion routes, and cohort 2, which follows the same infusion routes as cohort 1 while further assessing drug combination therapy. Considering that intravenous (IV) administration remains the primary route for systemic treatment of metastatic lesions across various organs, stage 2 (cohort 1 and cohort 2) was designed to investigate IV infusion of NKG2D CAR-NK cells with or without IP administration. And cohort 2 further explored the combination of NKG2D CAR-NK cells with anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibodies. For patients with peritoneal metastases, the choice to combine IV with IP infusion was based on lesion distribution and the potential risks of IP-related complications. Patients were sequentially enrolled into each cohort according to the predefined study design, and allocation was not based on investigator choice.

Preclinical studies have demonstrated that IP infusion of NK cells achieved levels of engraftment comparable to those obtained with IV delivery across multiple organs. On the basis of these findings, the IV infusion dose of CAR-NK product in stage 2 was also set at 2 × 109 cells per infusion. CAR-NK cells were delivered in a total volume of 100 mL of cell preservation solution, which consisted of normal saline supplemented with 5% human serum albumin (HSA). All patients in stage 2 received four infusions of CAR-NK cells within a two-week period. Specifically, patients in cohort 1 were treated on day 0, 4, 7, and 10. As for cohort 2, short-interval consecutive CAR-NK infusions (day 0, 1, 2, and 3) were administered to induce rapid tumor lysis, promptly activate endogenous T cells, and simultaneously block the PD-1/PD-L1 axis to maximize therapeutic benefit. Patients in cohort 2 additionally received IV administration of 3 mg/kg serplulimab every two weeks, beginning on day 0 in conjunction with the first CAR-NK cell infusion.

Prior to CAR-NK infusion, patients were evaluated to ensure suitability for lymphodepletion. Key requirements included absence of clinically significant infection or cardiac dysfunction, serum creatinine < 2 × upper limit of normal, and no acute neurotoxicity > grade 1 except peripheral sensory neuropathy. If any of these conditions were not met, or if fever ≥ 38.0 °C occurred within 48 h before treatment, the lymphodepletion was delayed. The lymphodepletion regimen consisted of fludarabine (20–25 mg/m2/day) and cyclophosphamide (500 mg/m2/day), both administered on two consecutive days (day-4 to day-3 prior to the first infusion). Subsequently, chidamide was also given orally at a dose of 10 mg on day-2 and 20 mg on day-1. An interval of up to three days between completion of lymphodepletion and CAR-NK infusion was permitted.

As this was an exploratory study, the protocol permitted investigators to adjust the number or dose of CAR-NK infusions according to cell preparation and the clinical status of individual patients, with the aim of maximizing potential benefit. Specifically, if the principal investigator considered that a patient might derive additional clinical benefit from further CAR-NK therapy, additional cycles were allowed.

Study endpoints

The primary endpoints of the current study were the safety and tolerability of NKG2D CAR-NK cell therapy. Adverse events were assessed according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) v5.0. Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and neurotoxicity were graded based on the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy (ASTCT) consensus criteria. Secondary endpoints included objective response rate (ORR), duration of response (DOR) and overall survival (OS). Tumor responses were evaluated by CT imaging in accordance with the RECIST v1.1 guidelines. And imaging assessments were conducted at baseline, at prespecified intervals up to 12 months (day 28 and month 2, 3, 6, 9, and 12), and subsequently every 3 months in patients without disease progression. The last follow-up was conducted on May 31, 2025.

qPCR analysis of CAR-NK cell expansion and persistence

Genomic DNA was isolated from peripheral blood samples using a commercial genomic DNA Extraction Kit (Tiangen, DP304-02) according to the manufacture’s instruction. All real-time fluorescent quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) were performed using an LightCycler 480 Real-Time PCR System instrument (Roche). The primers, probes, and standardized procedures for quantifying NKG2D-CAR vector copy number in peripheral blood were employed as previously described.

Statistical analyses

Descriptive statistics were presented as medians with ranges for continuous variables, and as counts with corresponding percentages for categorical variables. Progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) were estimated using the Kaplan–Meier method. All statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism v8.0.

Results

Trial design, study population and clinicopathologic features

This study reports clinical findings from stage 2, the cohort expansion phase of an ongoing phase I clinical trial (NCT05213195) evaluating allogeneic CAR-NK cell therapy in patients with advanced CRC, with or without peritoneal metastases. In stage 2, two cohorts were designed to assess modified infusion strategies and the addition of PD-1 blockade. As shown in Fig. 2, seven patients with metastatic CRC were screened between February and May 2023. Six of them met the predefined inclusion and exclusion criteria and were enrolled after providing written informed consent. All enrolled patients received lymphodepleting chemotherapy, followed by infusion of allogeneic CAR-NK cells.

The clinical characteristics of the six patients who received CAR-NK cell therapy in the current study are summarized in Table 1. Among the six participants, two were female and four were male. The median age was 68 years (range, 57–71). Four patients (66.7%) had liver metastases, five (83.3%) had lung metastases, two (33.3%) had peritoneal metastases, one (16.7%) had bone metastasis, and one (16.7%) had brain metastasis. Four patients (66.7%) harbored RAS/BRAF mutations, and one patient (16.7%) had MSI-H/dMMR disease. All six patients had previously received at least two lines of systemic therapy, which included fluorouracil or its analogs (100%), platinum-based chemotherapy (100%), anti-VEGF antibodies (100%), irinotecan (83.3%), and anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies (50%). Baseline characteristics were generally comparable between the two cohorts-those receiving CAR-NK monotherapy (cohort 1) and those receiving CAR-NK cells in combination with anti-PD-1 antibodies (cohort 2). 

Safety and toxic effects

Owing to the predominance of liver and lung metastases, all six patients were administered CAR-NK cells via the IV route alone, without IP infusion. In the current study, CAR-NK cell therapy, whether administered alone or in combination with PD-1 blockade, demonstrated a favorable safety profile. No patients discontinued the trial due to treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs), and no treatment-related deaths occurred during this phase of the study.

All patients experienced transient and reversible hematologic toxicities, most of which were grade 3 or 4 (Table 2), including leukopenia (100%), lymphopenia (100%), neutropenia (100%), and anemia (66.7%). These hematologic TRAEs were clearly attributable to the lymphodepleting chemotherapy and were considered manageable. As for non-hematologic grade 3 or 4 TRAEs, Patient 05 (dMMR) developed grade 3 elevations in alanine aminotransferase and aspartate aminotransferase. Patient 02 experienced grade 3 anorexia. All other non-hematologic AEs were grade 1 or 2 and primarily involved mild cytokine release syndrome (CRS) or gastrointestinal events including nausea and vomiting. In the patient with dMMR disease (Patient 05), aside from the previously mentioned TRAEs, no additional AEs were observed (Table S2). 

Efficacy

Efficacy assessments are summarized in Fig. 3. According to the RECIST v1.1 criteria, the best clinical response observed across both two cohorts was stable disease (SD). Specifically, one patient achieved SD, while the remaining five patients experienced progressive disease (PD). Notably, the patient who achieved SD was from the combination therapy cohort. In addition, one patient (Patient 05) with dMMR disease was enrolled in cohort 2. The addition of PD-1 blockade did not translate into enhanced clinical benefit, as the patient still experienced PD during CAR-NK therapy. Among the six treated patients, the median PFS was 28 days (range: 26–65 days), and the median OS was 292.5 days (Fig. 4). The 6-month, 1-year, and 2-year OS rates were 83.3%, 33.3%, and 33.3%, respectively. Two patients survived for more than two years after NKG2D CAR-NK therapy and remained alive at the time of last follow-up; both were from the combination cohort. All participants initiated subsequent treatments or received best supportive care after discontinuation from the trial.

In addition, higher CAR transgene copy numbers may correlate with better in vivo persistence of CAR-NK cells and potentially improved clinical outcomes. We performed qPCR analysis to monitor CAR transgene copy numbers in peripheral blood. In our study, CAR-NK cells were detectable within the first two weeks post-infusion, with comparable in vivo persistence between cohort 1 and cohort 2 (Fig. 5). In cohort 1, the median peak CAR transgene copy number was 1001 (range: 521–18,692), whereas in cohort 2, the median peak was significantly higher at 45,593 (range: 13,408–118,567). Of note, the patient who achieved SD (Patient 4) did not have either the highest peak copy number or the longest persistence among the six treated patients. Patient 2, who had a shorter OS, showed limited CAR-NK expansion in peripheral blood.

Tumor marker and cytokine detection

During CAR-NK cell infusion and subsequent follow-up, serial monitoring of tumor markers and cytokines was performed to assist in evaluating therapeutic response and potential CRS events (Fig. 6, Figure S2). A marked decline in plasma CA19-9 levels was observed in patient 01 following infusion. In contrast, patient 04 who achieved SD exhibited varying degrees of increase in plasma levels of CEA, CA19-9, and CA125 (Fig. 6). Patient 3 also showed elevated baseline levels of IL-6, which further increased after CAR-NK cell infusion (Figure S2). This trend may be partially attributed to the patient’s inherently high baseline secretion profile. Fluctuations in several cytokines, including IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, and IFN-γ, were noted during the infusion period across multiple patients. However, based on the available data, no clear correlation was observed between cytokine levels and the occurrence or severity of CRS-related events, and the results were comparable across different cohorts as well as between patients with different clinical outcomes.

Discussion

To date, clinical trial data on CAR-NK cell therapy for solid tumors remain limited. This phase I study reported the clinical application of NKG2D-based CAR-NK cell therapy in patients with advanced CRC. Both monotherapy and combination therapy with PD-1 blockade demonstrated acceptable safety profiles and modest clinical activity.

Previous early-phase trials of adoptive cell therapy for solid tumors have raised concerns about excessive toxicity, particularly serious adverse events (SAEs) caused by on-target, off-tumor (OTOT) effects and CRS. In our trial, the most common TRAEs were reversible hematologic toxicities associated with lymphodepleting chemotherapy. Other TRAEs including low-grade fever, transient liver dysfunction, diarrhea and gastrointestinal symptoms were generally mild. Notably, no patients experienced grade ≥ 3 CRS or discontinued the trial due to AEs, and all tolerated repeated CAR-NK infusions, supporting the feasibility of multi-dose regimens. Compared to monotherapy, the addition of PD-1 blockade did not increase toxicity. Inflammatory cytokines such as IL-6 and IFN-γ remained largely stable post-infusion, further suggesting a low risk of CRS.

In stage 2 of this trial, the route of CAR-NK cell infusion was determined based on individual tumor burden and lesion location. Although the protocol allowed for either IV or combined IV and IP infusion, all six enrolled patients received IV-only administration due to predominant liver and lung metastases. Among these patients, the best clinical response was SD in one case, yielding a DCR of 16.7%. Immune checkpoint inhibitors, such as PD-1 monoclonal antibodies, have shown durable responses in CRC patients with dMMR or MSI-H. NK cells possess inherent immunoregulatory functions which can promote antigen presentation by dendritic cells, secrete pro-inflammatory cytokines, and mediate tumor antigen release through direct cytotoxicity. Our previous data also revealed that despite a cytotoxic phenotype, endogenous CD8⁺ T cells upregulate exhaustion markers such as PD-1 after CAR-NK therapy, suggesting a potential therapeutic window for PD-1 blockade. A preclinical study further supported the synergistic anti-tumor effect of combining PD-1 inhibitors with iPSC-derived NK cells. In our study, all three patients who received CAR-NK plus PD-1 blockade demonstrated clinical benefits, including one case of SD and two long-term survivors with overall survival exceeding 700 days. However, limited sample size and absence of clear baseline differences make it difficult to determine whether survival reflects treatment benefit or natural disease course, underscoring the need for further investigation into CAR-NK combination strategies.

Peripheral persistence and expansion of CAR-engineered immune cells are widely recognized as key correlates of clinical efficacy. In this study, we used qPCR to assess peripheral CAR transgene copies and observed detectability only within two weeks post-infusion. This transient presence may partly explain the limited efficacy and suggests that in vivo persistence remains a critical hurdle for durable responses. We observed that peripheral CAR transgene copies appeared notably higher in patients receiving combination therapy with anti-PD-1 agents compared to CAR-NK monotherapy. However, this difference may be partially influenced by the distinct infusion schedules between these two cohorts. In our study, CAR-NK cells expressing NKG2D CAR with mbIL-15 were designed to enhance in vivo expansion and persistence. However, their actual persistence remained limited. Several factors, including short in vivo lifespan, host immune rejection, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, continue to restrict the therapeutic potential of CAR-NK cells. To address this issue, various strategies are being explored, including CISH knockout, co-expression of supportive cytokines, engineering CAR-NK cells to resist inhibitory signals, and incorporation of additional costimulatory domains.

This study has several limitations. First, as a phase I trial with a small sample size, all the findings should be interpreted with caution. Potential correlations between clinicopathological or molecular characteristics and clinical outcome cannot yet be determined. Second, the study was conducted as an open-label, non-randomized sequential cohort design, which may introduce selection bias. Third, given the possibility of pseudoprogression with immunotherapy, trial design should account for this phenomenon, with extended observation periods applied when necessary.

In summary, NKG2D CAR-NK cell therapy was safe and well tolerated in patients with advanced CRC. No severe AEs such as CRS or graft-versus-host disease were observed. However, the clinical efficacy of CAR-NK therapy in this context remains modest. Future research should focus on improving in vivo persistence, optimizing infusion timing, and exploring synergistic combination regimens to enhance therapeutic benefit. These will be key priorities for the next phase of preclinical and clinical studies.
