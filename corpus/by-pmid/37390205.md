# A localized hydrogel-mediated chemotherapy causes immunogenic cell death via activation of ceramide-mediated unfolded protein response.

## Abstract

Treatment of triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is challenging because of its "COLD" tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment (TIME). Here, we present a hydrogel-mediated localized delivery of a combination of docetaxel (DTX) and carboplatin (CPT) (called DTX-CPT-Gel therapy) that ensured enhanced anticancer effect and tumor regression on multiple murine syngeneic and xenograft tumor models. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy modulated the TIME by an increase of antitumorigenic M1 macrophages, attenuation of myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and increase of granzyme B+CD8+ T cells. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy elevated ceramide levels in tumor tissues that activated the protein kinase R (PKR)-like endoplasmic reticulum kinase (PERK)-mediated unfolded protein response (UPR). This UPR-mediated activation of apoptotic cell death led to release of damage-associated molecular patterns, thereby activating the immunogenic cell death that could even clear the metastatic tumors. This study provides a promising hydrogel-mediated platform for DTX-CPT therapy that induces tumor regression and effective immune modulation and, therefore, can be explored further for treatment of TNBC.

Hydrogel-mediated delivery of docetaxel and carboplatin causes tumor regression through immune cell stimulation.

INTRODUCTION

Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) accounts for 15 to 20% of total patients with breast cancer and is characterized by negative expression of estrogen, progesterone, and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) receptors (ER−PR−HER2−). Chemotherapeutic drugs such as taxanes, anthracyclines, and platinates are commonly used for treatment as TNBC tissues lack therapeutic targets that are usually present in luminal (ER+ and PR+) and HER2-enriched tumors. However, combination of taxanes and anthracyclines is associated with high toxicity and increased relapse rates, causing poor overall survival in patients with TNBC over other subtypes. In addition, TNBC tumor tissues develop a characteristic “COLD” tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment (TIME) enriched in protumorigenic M2 macrophages, myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and T regulatory (Treg) cells, and are devoid of antitumorigenic M1 macrophages and cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. Therefore, there is a need for alternative strategies that can deliver chemotherapeutic drugs at the tumor site in a sustained manner and reprogram the “COLD” TIME to “HOT” beds enriched in M1 macrophages and cytotoxic CD8+ T cells.

Tumors acquire high mutational burden and can provide neoantigens for activation of antitumor immune response. However, cancer cells usually escape from the immune response as they do not allow the exposure of neoepitopes to antigen-presenting cells (APCs), especially the dendritic cells. Simultaneously, cancer cells also secrete cytokines and chemokines that cause polarization of infiltrated immune cells into immunosuppressive subtypes. Therefore, two major strategies for immune-induced antitumor response include involvement of antibodies, natural killer (NK) cells, and T cells as a part of adaptive immunity or dendritic cell–mediated production of proinflammatory cytokines and activation of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. Active immunity in tumor tissues is dependent on combination of antigenicity provided by neoepitopes and adjuvanticity of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Calreticulin (CRT); high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), a non-histone chromatin protein; and adenosine triphosphate (ATP) are well-known DAMPs secreted by apoptotic cancer cells that, along with released neoantigens, can activate immune cells to cause immunogenic cell death (ICD). Therefore, cancer cell death can allow immune cells to recognize released neoantigens and DAMPs, and can lead to activation of active immunity.

Chemotherapeutic drugs cause apoptotic cell death by targeting DNA/protein/RNA depending on the context and signaling pathways that are activated. More recent studies showed that, apart from their focused effect on apoptosis, chemotherapeutic drugs also cause collateral damage by inducing CD4+ and CD8+ T cell–mediated ICD. Chemotherapeutic drugs can activate unfolded protein response (UPR) that can either induce C/EBP homologous protein (CHOP)–mediated apoptotic cell death and/or help in release of DAMPs triggering ICD. In addition, chemotherapeutic drugs can also induce alterations in sphingolipid metabolism, and increase in ceramide levels can cause apoptotic cell death and activation of UPR. However, chemotherapeutic treatment for patients with TNBC is challenged by high toxicity of drugs because of their accumulation in undesired organs, lack of ideal therapeutic combinations, and inability of the delivery vehicles to release chemotherapeutic drugs at the tumor site in a desired manner. In addition, the role of UPR and sphingolipid metabolism in ICD is an area that has not been explored enough.

Current chemotherapeutic regimens of anthracyclines and taxanes for patients with TNBC are challenged by irreversible and long-term adverse effects of these drugs, as well as relapse of the tumors in 30 to 40% of patients with TNBC. Recent studies showed that addition of platinate drugs such as carboplatin (CPT) enhanced the clinical response in patients with TNBC. However, favorable overall clinical response is not attainable to date because of high toxicity and decrease in tolerability to these chemotherapeutic drugs. Therefore, we hypothesize that hydrogel-mediated sustained delivery of a combination of docetaxel (DTX) and CPT (DTX-CPT-Gel therapy) can attenuate the tumor progression effectively with minimal toxic effects. In this study, we investigated the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on ICD in tumor tissues. As chemotherapy is known to cause alterations in sphingolipid metabolism and activate UPR, we elucidated the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on sphingolipid-mediated activation of UPR and its effect on ICD (Fig. 1A).

RESULTS

TNBC tumors are immunosuppressive in nature

Tumor tissues are enriched with a heterogeneous population of tumor-promoting MDSCs and Treg cells, and there is a lack of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. To validate the TIME in breast tumor tissues, we quantified and compared different tumor-promoting and tumor-inhibiting immune cells in the normal mammary fat pad and the TNBC tumor tissues developed from murine TNBC breast cancer (4T1) cells in BALB/c mice by flow cytometry. We observed more than fourfold increase in the leukocytes (CD45+) in tumor tissues compared to the normal mammary fat pad (Fig. 1B). MDSCs are known to inhibit the polarization of M2 macrophages to M1 macrophages and also inhibit the activation of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. We found a >20-fold increase in the MDSCs (CD11b+Gr1+) in tumor tissues as compared to the normal tissues, thereby confirming the TIME in TNBC tissues (Fig. 1C). Dendritic cells through antigen presentation can activate the CD8 T cells, and these activated CD8 T cells can inhibit the tumor progression, whereas Treg cells can cause TIME by inhibiting the function of CD8+ T cells. Flow cytometry analysis showed an increase in recruitment of CD8+ T cells and Treg cells in tumor tissues (Fig. 1, D and E). As the ratio of CD8+/Treg cells determines the fate of tumor tissues, we observed a >25-fold decrease in the CD8+/Treg ratio in the 4T1 tumors as compared to normal tissues (Fig. 1F). Therefore, TNBC tumor tissues are highly immunosuppressive in nature because of the presence of MDSCs and a reduced CD8+/Treg cell ratio.

DTX-CPT therapy causes synergistic cell death

As a combination of taxanes and platinate drugs such as CPT showed a good clinical response in patients with TNBC, we first determined the median inhibitory concentration (IC50) of DTX and CPT against TNBC, representing murine breast (4T1) cancer cells representing TNBC. DTX showed an IC50 of 90.3 ± 17.4 nM, and CPT showed an IC50 of 0.97 ± 0.3 μM against 4T1 cells (fig. S1, A and B). We then investigated the combination of DTX and CPT at different ratios of 1:1, 1:2, and 2:1 by varying the concentrations of DTX and CPT. We hypothesized that combination of DTX and CPT at a lower concentration will confer a better therapeutic effect as compared to the individual drugs, as both drugs target a different but specific therapeutic target. To find the synergistic, additive, or antagonistic effect, we used the Chou-Talalay method to calculate the combination index (CI) that can achieve cell death and emulate IC50 of drugs when used alone. Among the different 1:1 ratios of DTX and CPT tested, combination of DTX and CPT (60, 70, 80, or 90 nM each) showed synergism with a CI of <0.8 (fig. S1, C and D). In contrast, all combinations of DTX and CPT at 1:2 ratio showed synergism (fig. S1, C and E). The ratio of 2:1 was found to be the most effective as all concentration ratios showed the highest synergism with combination indices of <0.2 (fig. S1, C and F).

We then tested the effect of the combination of DTX and CPT on cell death, where cancer cells were treated with a combination of 60 nM DTX and 30 nM CPT (2:1), and the percentage of cell death was quantified by the annexin V–fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC)–propidium iodide assay. Combination of DTX and CPT caused enhanced apoptotic cell death (~30%) in 4T1 cells as compared to only ~12 and ~14% cell death observed with DTX or CPT treatment alone, respectively (fig. S1G). Cell cycle assay upon treatment of cancer cells showed a >1.2-fold increase in the G2-M phase arrest on using the combination of DTX and CPT as compared to only DTX or CPT treatment alone (fig. S1H). Reactive oxygen species (ROS) quantification further confirmed that the combination of DTX and CPT results in ~70% ROS-positive 4T1 cells as compared to only ~28 and ~10% ROS-positive cells with DTX or CPT treatment alone, respectively (fig. S1I). Therefore, these results suggest that the DTX-CPT therapy causes synergistic cell death in breast cancer cells.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy mitigates tumor progression

Low–molecular weight hydrogels (LMWHs) have emerged as alternative biomaterials for drug delivery over systemic use of nanoparticles because of the ability of LMWHs to deliver chemotherapeutics locally at the diseased site in a sustained manner. In our earlier study, we engineered a nonimmunogenic and biodegradable lithocholic acid–derived LMWH (A13 hydrogel) that can maintain sustained release of combinations of chemotherapeutic drugs at the injected site (Fig. 2A). As hydrophobic drugs can get precipitated during entrapment and lead to the loss of gel strength, we first tested the ability of A13 hydrogel to entrap hydrophobic DTX and found that the A13 hydrogel can entrap a maximum of 10 mg of DTX in 70 mg (1 ml) of the gelator (~14% by weight) (Fig. 2A). Rheological parameters for DTX-Gel (5 mg in 1 ml of gel) reveal enhanced elasticity with a crossover point at ~61% as compared to the A13 hydrogel (~5%) because of hydrophobic interactions between DTX and the lithocholic acid backbone (fig. S2, A and B). Field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) showed a fibrous morphology of DTX-Gel with no change in the nanoarchitecture of the gel by addition of drugs (fig. S2C). Drug release kinetic studies showed that a small burst (3%) of DTX is released following a zero-order release (fig. S2D). We then compared the effect of DTX-Gel on tumor growth with intravenous (DTX-IV) and tumor site (DTX-TS) (without hydrogel) delivery of DTX in a murine breast cancer (4T1) model representing TNBC subtype. DTX-Gel did not show any advantage in tumor regression over other treatments (fig. S2E), and there was no advantage in terms of mice survival as well (fig. S2F). Insignificant advantage of DTX-Gel over other treatments might be due to the very slow release of DTX from DTX-Gel in tumor tissues, making it less effective.

A13 can also entrap a maximum 8 mg of CPT in 70 mg (1 ml) of the gelator (~11% by weight) (Fig. 2A), and CPT-Gel (2.5 mg in 1 ml of gel) showed a crossover point at ~10%, suggesting that the gel can also entrap a hydrophilic drug without loss of elasticity and injectability (fig. S2, G and H). This was further confirmed by FESEM, showing that the addition of this concentration of CPT does not alter its nanoarchitecture (fig. S2I). Drug release kinetic profile showed a first-order release profile (fig. S2J). CPT-Gel is more effective in reducing the tumor volume as compared to intravenous (CPT-IV) and tumor site–mediated (without hydrogel) delivery of CPT (CPT-TS) (fig. S2K), but without any notable advantage in terms of mice survival (fig. S2L). First-order fast release of CPT from CPT-Gel might be responsible for the insignificant change in mice survival. As treatment of DTX-Gel or CPT-Gel alone did not produce much increase in tumor regression and mice survival, we hypothesized that the entrapment of a combination of DTX and CPT in hydrogel (DTX-CPT-Gel) will provide sequential and sustained release of CPT and DTX, and DTX-CPT-Gel therapy may enhance the anticancer effect and mice survival.

Screening for different combinations of DTX and CPT in A13 hydrogel revealed that the A13 gel can entrap a maximum of 12 mg of CPT and 10 mg of DTX in a combination in 70 mg of gel (1 ml) (Fig. 2A). Rheology of DTX-CPT-Gel having 5 mg of DTX and 2.5 mg of CPT in 1 ml of gel showed no significant change in storage (G′) and loss modulus (G″), supporting the mechanical and injectable properties of DTX-CPT-Gel (Fig. 2B and fig. S3, A and B). We observed more than threefold (from ~5 to ~18%) increase in the percentage of strain on entrapment of a combination of DTX and CPT in A13 hydrogel, implying its ability to entrap hydrophobic and hydrophilic drugs efficiently without compromising injectability and elasticity (Fig. 2B and fig. S3, A and B). FESEM further confirmed that both the drugs are homogenously distributed in the hydrogel matrix (fig. S3C). Drug release kinetic profiles confirmed that CPT can maintain first-order release profiles, and DTX show zero-order release profiles even when entrapped in a combination (Fig. 2C). These studies suggest that A13 hydrogel can allow simultaneous encapsulation of multiple drugs with distinct hydrophobicity and is able to maintain its differential release kinetics.

We compared the efficacy of DTX-CPT-Gel with tumor site (without hydrogel) and intravenous delivery of a combination of DTX and CPT in the murine breast (4T1) cancer model. We randomized 4T1 tumor-bearing BALB/c mice into four different groups, where no treatment was given to the group 1 mice (fig S3D). We administrated 10 doses of DTX (5 mg/kg) and CPT (2.5 mg/kg) in polysorbate 80 and 12% ethanol solution (further suspended in saline) via tail vein on alternate days (DTX-CPT-IV) in the group 2 mice (fig. S3D). We used a combination of DTX (50 mg/kg) and CPT (25 mg/kg) dissolved in polysorbate 80 and 12% ethanol solution and injected it near the tumor site (DTX-CPT-TS) in the group 3 mice and injected a single dose of DTX (50 mg/kg) and CPT (25 mg/kg) entrapped in A13 hydrogel near the tumor site (DTX-CPT-Gel) in the group 4 mice (fig. S3D). DTX-CPT-Gel induced a significant decrease in tumor growth kinetics over the DTX-CPT-TS– and DTX-CPT-IV–treated tumors (fig. S3E). We observed an approximately threefold decrease in tumor volume on day 18 of DTX-CPT-Gel treatment as compared to the untreated tumors and an approximately twofold decrease as compared to DTX-CPT-IV– and DTX-CPT-TS–treated tumors (fig. S3E). DTX-CPT-IV did not provide any advantage in terms of mice survival over the untreated mice, whereas we observed a significant increase in median survival on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment (fig. S3F). There was a >20% decrease in mice body weight on DTX-CPT-IV treatment, which might be because of the distribution of drugs to all body organs. In contrast, DTX-CPT-Gel did not induce any significant decrease in mice body weight because of the localized and sustained release of chemotherapeutic drugs (fig. S3G). Similarly, anticancer activity against the Lewis lung carcinoma (LLC1) model in C57BL/6 mice showed that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy is more effective than other treatments in tumor growth inhibition (fig. S3H), with enhanced survival (fig. S3I) while maintaining the mice body weight (fig. S3J).

Next, we compared the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy with DTX-Gel and CPT-Gel on tumor regression in murine 4T1 and LLC1 tumor models. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy caused a significant decrease in tumor growth kinetics of 4T1 tumors as compared to other treatments (Fig. 2D). There was more than twofold decrease in tumor volume on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment as compared to DTX-Gel and CPT-Gel therapies (Fig. 2D) without any significant alteration in mice body weight (Fig. 2E). We observed ~10-day increase in median survival on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment as compared to untreated mice (Fig. 2F). Immunohistochemical staining of treated 4T1 tumor tissues validated significant decrease in proliferation using the Ki67 proliferation marker (Fig. 2G), and TUNEL (terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase–mediated deoxyuridine triphosphate nick end labeling) assay confirmed an increase in apoptosis (Fig. 2H). Similarly, anticancer studies in LLC1 tumor model showed that the DTX-CPT-Gel induced a >3.5-fold decrease in tumor volume over untreated mice, and ~2-fold decrease over DTX-Gel– and CPT-Gel–treated mice (fig. S4A), with no significant change in body weight (fig. S4B). We also observed a ~13-day increase in median survival on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (fig. S4C).

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy is effective against murine xenograft models

To probe the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on xenograft models using human cancer cell lines, we compared the efficacy of DTX-CPT-Gel with DTX-Gel and CPT-Gel treatments in three breast cancer models, representing TNBC (MDA-MB-231 cells), luminal A (MCF-7), and luminal B (BT-474) subtypes in the nonobese diabetic–severe combined immunodeficient (NOD-SCID) mice. Tumor growth kinetic studies demonstrated the advantage of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy in inhibiting the tumor progression in MDA-MB-231 tumors (Fig. 2I). We observed no significant decrease in mice body weight (Fig. 2J). Untreated MDA-MB-231 tumor-bearing mice showed a median survival of ~50 days. Mice on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment survived >85 days, with >35 days of increase in median survival (Fig. 2K). Similarly, anticancer studies in MCF-7 (fig. S4, D to F) and BT-474 (fig. S4, G to I) tumor models confirmed the advantage of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy over DTX-Gel and CPT-Gel treatments. Therefore, these results validated that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy is highly effective against TNBC and luminal xenograft models, and TNBC tumors showed better response.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy is not toxic and does not cause a cytokine storm

Chemotherapeutic drugs can cause systemic toxicity because of accumulation in undesired organs and can also induce nonspecific inflammatory responses, leading to release of cytokines in systemic circulation. Therefore, we quantified the changes in different hematological, serological parameters and levels of different cytokines in serum samples after 20 days of implantation of DTX-CPT-Gel in BALB/c mice and compared it with healthy mice. We did not find any significant alterations in the levels of hematological parameters (platelets, lymphocytes, neutrophils, monocytes, and eosinophils) and serological parameters [aspartate aminotransferase (AST), alanine transaminase (ALT), alkaline phosphatase (ALP)], albumin, creatinine, blood urea nitrogen, and urea) on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (fig. S5A). DTX-CPT-Gel therapy did not promote activation of any of the proinflammatory and anti-inflammatory cytokines as observed in the case of lipopolysaccharide (used as positive control) (fig. S5, B and C). We also assessed the toxicity of DTX-CPT-Gel on vital organs including liver, lung, heart, kidney, and spleen by hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining and compared them with healthy mice. We did not observe any significant morphological differences in these organs of DTX-CPT-Gel–treated mice in comparison to healthy mice (fig. S5D). Therefore, these results clearly validated that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy does not cause any significant toxic effects.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy deregulates immune and inflammatory responsive genes

We performed paired-end RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) followed by pairwise comparative analysis of untreated, DTX-Gel–, CPT-Gel–, and DTX-CPT-Gel–treated 4T1 tumor tissues to find the alterations in gene expression pattern. In contrast to untreated tumors, DTX-Gel–treated tumors showed 2110 differentially expressed genes, with 1599 up-regulated and 511 down-regulated genes (≥1.5 with P ≤ 0.05) (Fig. 3A). CPT-Gel treatment deregulated expression of 5051 genes, with 2946 up-regulated and 2105 down-regulated genes (≥1.5 with P ≤ 0.05) (Fig. 3B). We observed a total of 2999 differentially expressed genes upon DTX-CPT-Gel treatment, with 2297 up-regulated and 702 down-regulated genes (≥1.5 with P ≤ 0.05) (Fig. 3C). We identified biological processes targeted by different therapy groups using the Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery–Gene Ontology (DAVID-GO) tool. Different treatment groups showed enrichment of several diverse and common biological processes, where cell adhesion (Cdh6, Col19a1, and Lamc2) processes were enriched among all the groups for significantly up- and down-regulated genes (Fig. 3D and fig. S6, A and B). DTX-Gel and DTX-CPT-Gel showed enrichment for genes involved in inflammatory response (Il15, Cxcl9, Tlr5, Ccl5, and Il6), immune response (Il7, Mcpt4, Tnfsf10, and Tnfsf11), and negative regulation of inflammatory response (Il10, Mefv, and Mir147) (Fig. 3D and fig. S6A), and CPT-Gel deregulated cell differentiation (Notch2, Foxo1, and Sox9) and mitogen-activated protein kinase cascade (Cdk10, Ctnnb1, and Cdon) genes (fig. S6B). Normalized fragments per kilo base per million mapped read (FPKM) values of genes involved in inflammatory and immune response were plotted for heatmap that showed a unique gene expression signature on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment compared to untreated and other treatment groups. Pairwise comparison analysis showed 773 genes exclusively deregulated in DTX-CPT-Gel–treated tumors in contrast to other treatment groups, and 953 genes were deregulated in all the groups (Fig. 3F). We validated several differentially expressed genes enriched in immune response pathways using quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) including Mcpt4 (mast cell protease 4), Cma1 (chymase 1), Il5 (interleukin-5), Il6, Il12a, Il12b, Il23, and TNF-α (tumor necrosis factor–α) that show significant deregulation upon DTX-CPT-Gel treatment (Fig. 3G). Although we did not observe any significant change in expression of granzyme B (Gzmb) in RNA-seq data, qRT-PCR results confirmed a >3.5-fold increase in expression on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment (Fig. 3G).

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy modulates TIME

As we observed differential regulation of inflammatory and immune response–related genes (Fig. 3E), we quantified the changes in the population of MDSCs, macrophages, and T cells in 4T1 tumors on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy using flow cytometry (fig. S7). We observed more than twofold decrease in the total MDSCs (CD45+CD11b+Gr1+) on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment as compared to untreated tissues (fig. S8A). MDSCs can be monocytic in nature with Ly6ChiLy6Glo expression or can be granulocytic with Ly6CloLy6Ghi expression, with the granulocytic MDSCs being more immunosuppressive in nature. We observed a ~1.5-fold decrease in the monocytic MDSCs (Ly6ChiLy6Glo) (fig. S8B) and a ~3-fold decrease in granulocytic MDSCs (Ly6CloLy6Ghi) on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment as compared to untreated tumors (fig. S8C). Tumor-associated macrophages (M2 macrophages) produce immunosuppressive cytokines, activate Treg cells, and suppress functions of CD8+ cytotoxic T cells. To decipher the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on macrophage subsets, we quantified the changes in the population of CD80+ (M1-like) and CD206+ (M2-like) macrophages (fig. S7B). DTX-CPT-Gel therapy caused a >2.5-fold increase in CD80+ (M1-like) macrophages (Fig. 4A). In contrast, we did not observe any change in the percentage of CD206+ (M2-like) macrophages (Fig. 4B). Ratio of M1/M2 macrophages confirmed a ~2.3-fold enhancement of M1 macrophages on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment, thereby alleviating the TIME (Fig. 4C).

Among the T cells, CD4+CD183+ [T helper (TH1)] cells are antitumorigenic and help in activation of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells. In contrast, CD4+CD194+ (TH2) and CD4+CD25+ (Treg) cells inhibit the cytotoxic function of CD8+ T cells (fig. S7C). DTX-CPT-Gel therapy induced a >2.2-fold increase in the number of CD4+CD183+ (TH1) cells (Fig. 4D) and a >8-fold reduction in CD4+CD194+ (TH2) cells (Fig. 4E) as compared to untreated control. Adaptive immune response by T cells depends on the ratio of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells to Treg cells, as Treg cells are immunosuppressive in nature and inhibit the activity of CD8+ T cells. We observed a >1.5-fold reduction in Treg cells (Fig. 4F) and a >2-fold increase in CD8+ T–to–Treg cell ratio on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 4G), thereby activating the CD8 T cell–mediated ICD. Therefore, DTX-CPT-Gel therapy modulated TIME by reducing granulocytic and monocytic MDSCs, by increasing M1-to-M2 macrophage ratio, and augmented ICD by increasing TH1 cells and CD8+ T–to–Treg cell ratio.

To validate the contribution of T cell–mediated ICD on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, we investigated the effect of depletion of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells on tumor growth kinetics in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy. Tumor (4T1)–bearing mice were randomized into five groups, where no treatment was given to group 1 mice, and groups 2 to 5 mice were treated with DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (fig. S8D). We gave intraperitoneal injections of anti-CD4 and anti-CD8 antibodies to mice of groups 3 and 4 to deplete CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, respectively, and a combination of anti-CD4 and anti-CD8 antibodies to group 5 mice to deplete both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. We used a nontargeting control immunoglobulin G (IgG) antibody for group 2 mice (fig. S8D). Depletion of CD4+ or CD8+ T cells by anti-CD4 or anti-CD8 antibodies, respectively, caused more than fivefold increase in the percentage of growth kinetics of tumors as compared to the control IgG-treated tumors (Fig. 4H). As CD4+ T cells are known to assist the cytotoxic function of CD8+ T cells, use of a combination of anti-CD4 and anti-CD8 antibodies did not cause any additive effect on increased tumor progression (Fig. 4H). We validated the depletion of CD4+and CD8+ T cells in blood by flow cytometry following different treatments (Fig. 4, I and J, and fig. S9A). Therefore, apart from classical, the apoptotic cell death, chemotherapy-mediated activation of immune cells induces ICD, and CD4+ and CD8+ T cells contribute toward tumor regression.

To decipher whether other immune cells are also involved in tumor regression in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, we quantified the effect of depletion of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells on M1 and M2 macrophages in tumor tissues (fig. S9B). We observed a >2.5-fold increase in CD11b+F4/80+iNOS+ M1 macrophages in DTX-CPT-Gel–treated tumor tissues even on the depletion of CD4 and CD8 cells (Fig. 4K) and >1.4-fold decrease in CD11b+F4/80+Arg1+ M2 macrophages (Fig. 4L) compared to untreated controls. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy caused an approximately threefold increase in the M1/M2 macrophage ratio, and depletion of CD4 and CD8 did not alter this ratio (Fig. 4M), Therefore, apart from the T cell–mediated ICD, increase in M1 macrophages is also responsible for tumor regression observed on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy.

Combination of DTX and CPT causes release of DAMPs and activates dendritic cells

To validate CD4/CD8-mediated cytotoxic response on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, we quantified the effect of the combination of DTX and CPT on the release of CRT and HMGB1 by 4T1 cells. CRT gets exposed on the cell surface of dying cells and helps in the uptake of apoptotic debris by dendritic cells. Therefore, we stained chemotherapy-treated 4T1 cells with anti-CRT antibody, followed by immunofluorescence studies that confirmed the release of CRT (on the cell surface) on DTX-CPT treatment (fig. S10A). Flow cytometry studies further confirmed a ~2-fold increase in the percentage of CRT-positive 4T1 cells (fig. S10B) and a >2.2-fold increase in the median fluorescence intensity (MFI) (fig. S10C). Quantification of HMGB1 release by immunoblotting in the supernatant of DTX-CPT–treated 4T1 cells revealed ~2.3-fold increase as compared to untreated cells (fig. S10, D and E). Next, we treated mouse dendritic (JAWSII) cells with cell debris from DTX-CPT–treated 4T1 cells and quantified the activation of dendritic cells by measuring the expression of CD86. There was a ~17% increase in the number of CD86+ dendritic cells, with ~1.5-fold increase in MFI of CD86 expression on DTX-CPT treatment (fig. S10, F and G). To further validate the effect of DTX-CPT therapy on generating immune response, we injected the cell debris of DTX-CPT–treated 4T1 cancer cells (called vaccination) subcutaneously followed by a booster dose after 10 days. We then rechallenged the mice with 4T1 cells and measured the tumor growth kinetics. Injection of debris from DTX-CPT–treated 4T1 cells inhibited the tumor growth (fig. S10H). These results demonstrate that, apart from its apoptotic function, DTX-CPT treatment stimulates the release of DAMPs from dying cancer cells that can activate the dendritic cells, contributing to ICD.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy can regress the distant tumors

To validate T cell–mediated ICD by DTX-CPT-Gel therapy in murine tumor model, we implanted the 4T1 cells on the left and right flanks of BALB/c mice and treated the left (primary) tumors with DTX-CPT-Gel (Fig. 5A). Tumor growth kinetics showed that the DTX-CPT-Gel therapy regressed the primary and the secondary tumors (Fig. 5, B and C). We observed a >4-fold decrease in the final day tumor volume of the primary tumors (Fig. 5B) and there was a ~1.8-fold decrease in the volume of secondary tumors (Fig. 5C). To confirm that ICD caused by DTX-CPT-Gel therapy was tumor antigen specific, we induced the 4T1 tumors on the left flank of mice and CT26 tumors on the right flank of mice and treated the 4T1 tumors only with DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 5D). There was no tumor regression of secondary CT26 tumors, suggesting that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy neither caused any apoptotic cell death in distant tumors through release of chemotherapeutic drugs in the systemic circulation nor generated any cytotoxic CD8+ T cell response against the CT26 cells (Fig. 5, E and F). To validate that tumor regression in secondary 4T1 tumors is due to cytotoxic CD8+ T cell response, we evaluated CD8+ T cell responses in primary and secondary tumors using flow cytometry (fig. S11). There was a ~1.2-fold increase in CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5G) in primary 4T1 tumors, with a ~2-fold decrease in PD1+CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5H) and ~2-fold increase in granzyme B+CD8+ T cells on DTX-CPT Gel therapy (Fig. 5I). We observed similar trends in the secondary 4T1 tumors, showing an increase in CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5J), decrease in PD1+CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5K), and increase in granzyme B+CD8+ T cells (Fig. 5L). These results prove that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, apart from stimulating the apoptotic response, also causes a specific immunogenic response that can regress the distant tumor via cytotoxic CD8 T cell–mediated cell death.

To decipher the impact of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on tumor recurrence after tumor resection, we induced 4T1 tumors in BALB/c mice. Tumors were resected as they reached ~100 mm3, and DTX-CPT-Gel was then implanted at the surgical site. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy did not allow the recurrence of tumor growth as compared to untreated resected tumors (Fig. 5M). To evaluate the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on metastasis, we implanted 4T1-12B luciferase-expressing cells in the mammary fat pad and treated the tumors with DTX-CPT-Gel. Bioluminescence imaging showed significant regression in metastasis in the thoracic region as compared to the untreated tumors (Fig. 5N and fig. S12). Therefore, these results confirmed the ability of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy in effectively mitigating the distant and metastatic tumors.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy induces the UPR

Apart from the focused effect on apoptosis, chemotherapeutic drugs also cause collateral damage by inducing UPR that can further induce apoptotic cell death and/or ICD. PERK, inositol-requiring transmembrane kinase/endoribonuclease 1α (IRE1α), and activating transcription factor 6 (ATF6) are three mediators of UPR that are maintained in an inactive state by the glucose-regulated protein, GRP-78 (BIP), and control survival and cell death pathways (Fig. 6A). We probed the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on UPR activation through PERK, IRE1α, and ATF6 pathways in 4T1 tumors. Immunoblot analysis showed more than sevenfold increase in phosphorylated PERK (p-PERK) on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 6, B and C). As activated p-PERK causes phosphorylation of eukaryotic translation initiation factor (p-EIF2α), we observed more than twofold increase in the p-EIF2α expression, confirming the activation of UPR (Fig. 6, B and C). Increase in p-EIF2α expression leads to global down-regulation in protein synthesis to attain cellular homeostasis; however, certain selected mRNAs including Atf4 are augmented, which is an important transcription factor for stress adaptation and CHOP expression, responsible for apoptosis. We observed more than twofold increase in the expression of CHOP, thereby suggesting the activation of apoptosis (Fig. 6, B and C). Although UPR-mediated activation of BAX and BAK activates mitochondria-mediated apoptosis, up-regulation of BAX and BAK also helps in release of molecular chaperons such as CRT to the cell surface of preapoptotic cells for activation of ICD. Immunoblot studies revealed enhanced expression of BAX and BAK without any significant change in BCL-2 (Fig. 6, B and C), thereby suggesting the role of apoptosis and ICD in DTX-CPT-Gel–mediated tumor regression. Increase in expression of caspase 3 as shown by immunoblotting (Fig. 6, B and C) and immunohistochemistry (Fig. 6D), as well as transcriptional activation of BIP, ATF4, CHOP, and GADD34 (Fig. 6E), further validated the activation of the PERK-EIF2α pathway.

IRE1α works as an adaptive response during early stages of UPR by boosting cell survival through splicing of Xbp1 (X-box binding protein 1) (Fig. 6A). XBP1s (spliced isoform of Xbp1) translocate into nucleus, binds to promoters of its target genes, and allows escape from UPR-mediated cell death. The immunoblot analysis of the IRE1α-XBP1s axis revealed ~1.6-fold decrease in levels of p-IRE1α upon DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 6, F and G). Furthermore, there was a ninefold decrease in the protein expression of XBP1s (Fig. 6, F and G), and a twofold decrease in Xbp1s transcript levels upon DTX-CPT-Gel therapy in tumor tissue (Fig. 6H). ATF6 transcription factor is a crucial cell survival and cell death mediator, and site-1 and site-2 proteases cleave ATF6 in Golgi in response to UPR. Cleaved cytosolic ATF6 translocates into the nucleus and activates gene expression for cell survival and homeostasis (Fig. 6A). Immunoblotting revealed an increase in ATF6 cleavage upon DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, suggesting ATF6 activation (Fig. 6, F and G). Therefore, these results confirm the activation of the PERK- and ATF6-mediated pathway and inhibition of the IRE1α-mediated pathway on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy.

To validate the effect of UPR activation on tumor regression by DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, we treated the 4T1 tumor-bearing mice with DTX-CPT-Gel or DTX-CPT-TUDCA-Gel, where TUDCA (tauroursodeoxycholic acid) inhibits UPR. Treatment of tumors with DTX-CPT-TUDCA-Gel inhibited tumor regression caused by DTX-CPT-Gel (fig. S13A), and there was a >1.5-fold increase in tumor volume in DTX-CPT-TUDCA-Gel–treated tumors as compared to DTX-CPT-Gel–treated tumors (fig. S13B). To validate the role of p-PERK in DTX-CPT-Gel–mediated tumor regression and ICD, we treated 4T1 tumor-bearing mice with DTX-CPT-Gel or DTX-CPT-iPERK-Gel, where iPERK is GSK2606414, a known p-PERK inhibitor. Treatment of tumors with DTX-CPT-iPERK-Gel induced more than twofold increase in tumor growth as compared to DTX-CPT-Gel–treated tumors (Fig. 6I). Flow cytometry analysis revealed that DTX-CPT-iPERK-Gel therapy resulted in a decrease in total CD8+ T cells and granzyme B–secreting CD8+ T cells (of total cells) as compared to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 6, J and K). Therefore, these results confirmed that activation of PERK pathway along with inhibition of the IRE1α-XBP1s axis in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy results in UPR-mediated apoptotic cell death and ICD.

DTX-CPT-Gel therapy causes ceramide-mediated UPR activation

Sphingolipids are important structural and signaling molecules that play a critical role in inducing UPR, and increase in ceramides is known to activate UPR. As chemotherapeutic drugs are known to dysregulate the sphingolipid-metabolic pathway, we investigated the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on the levels of different sphingolipid species (Fig. 7A). We quantified the levels of sphingolipid species in untreated and DTX-CPT-Gel–treated 4T1 tumors by liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry in the multiple reaction monitoring mode. Among the different ceramide species, C18, C20, C22, C24, and C24:1 ceramide species showed 1.2- to 2-fold increase after DTX-CPT-Gel therapy as compared to the untreated tumor tissues (Fig. 7B). DTX-CPT-Gel treatment also caused a significant decrease in the C16, C24, and C24:1 sphingomyelin species (fig. S14A). We observed a significant increase in ceramide/sphingomyelin ratio for C16, C20, C22, C24, and C24:1 species (fig. S14B). Among glucosylceramides, DTX-CPT-Gel therapy caused a significant decrease in C16 glucosylceramides (fig. S14C) and an increase in ceramide-to-glucosylceramide ratio for all the species (fig. S14D). Therefore, DTX-CPT-Gel therapy contributes to an increase in levels of ceramides by shifting ceramide-sphingomyelin and ceramide-glucosylceramide rheostats of sphingolipid metabolism toward higher ceramide levels.

To explain the alterations in levels of different sphingolipids in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy, we performed qRT-PCR to quantify the changes in gene expression for sphingolipid-metabolizing enzymes (Fig. 7C). Among all ceramide synthases, Cers1, Cers2, and Cers6 showed a ~1.5-fold increase in the expression on DTX-CPT-Gel treatment (Fig. 7C). We observed a ~1.5-fold decrease in the expression of uridine diphosphate–glucose ceramide glucosyltransferase (Ugcg) that synthesizes glucosylceramides, but there was no significant change in the expression of β-glucocerebrosidase (Gba1) that hydrolyses glucosylceramides (Fig. 7C). In the ceramide-sphingomyelin pathway, sphingomyelin phosphodiesterase 1, Smpd1, showed ~1.5-fold increase in expression on DTX-CPT-Gel therapy (Fig. 7C). Therefore, DTX-CPT-Gel therapy induced an increase in ceramides via up-regulating the expression of ceramide synthases and Smpd1 and via down-regulating the expression of Ugcg at the transcriptional level.

We quantified and validated the alterations in expression of sphingolipid-metabolizing enzymes at translation level by immunoblotting. CERS1 showed a ~6-fold increase as compared to untreated tumors, and there was a 1.2- to 3.0-fold increase in the expression of other ceramide synthases such as CERS2, CERS4, CERS5, and CERS6 (Fig. 7, D and E). These results suggest a significant activation of de novo pathway of ceramide synthesis in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy. We observed a 3- to 4-fold increase in expression of SMPD2, SMPD3, and SMPD4 that is responsible for the degradation of sphingomyelins to ceramides, as well as a >1.5-fold decrease in expression of sphingomyelin synthases (SMS1 and SMS2) (Fig. 7, D and E). Therefore, degradation of sphingomyelins to ceramides and attenuation of sphingomyelin synthesis also contribute to an increase in levels of ceramides. As interconversion of ceramides and glucosylceramides is catalyzed by UGCG and GBA1, we observed a >1.5-fold decrease in the expression of UGCG and a >3-fold increase in the expression of GBA1 (Fig. 7, D and E). Therefore, DTX-CPT-Gel therapy led to an increase in de novo synthesis of ceramides along with shifting of ceramide-sphingomyelin and ceramide-glucosylceramide rheostats toward ceramides, thereby causing an overall increase in ceramides.

We probed whether this increase in ceramides in response to DTX-CPT-Gel therapy is responsible for tumor regression and activation of UPR. For this, we inhibited serine palmitoyl transferase (SPT1/2), a key enzyme of de novo ceramide synthesis pathway, by myriocin (Fig. 7A) and studied its effect on tumor regression and UPR activation. We randomized 4T1 tumor-bearing mice into three groups, where no treatment was given to group 1 mice. We treated group 2 mice with DTX-CPT-Gel and group 3 mice with DTX-CPT-Myriocin-Gel. There was >1.8-fold increase in tumor growth kinetics in mice treated with DTX-CPT-Myriocin-Gel as compared to mice treated with DTX-CPT-Gel, thereby validating that increase in ceramides as one of the factors responsible for tumor regression (Fig. 7F). Immunoblotting using DTX-CPT-Myriocin-Gel–treated tumor tissues for p-PERK and p-EIF2α showed inactivation of UPR in the presence of myriocin (Fig. 7, G and H). Therefore, above results confirmed that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy causes an increase in ceramides through activation of de novo pathway, and this increase in ceramides further activates UPR responsible for apoptosis and ICD.

DISCUSSION

Systemic delivery of chemotherapeutic drugs causes severe toxicity because of nonspecific targeting and undesired accumulation of cytotoxic drugs in various organs. Therefore, a delivery vehicle that produces localized apoptotic cell death and induces systemic T cell–based cytotoxic response against cancer cells is desirable. Such delivery strategies can bypass toxicity associated with systemic circulation of drugs and clear distant and metastatic tumors through cytotoxic T cell–mediated cell death. Our results showed that lithocholic acid–derived hydrogel can entrap and maintain a localized sustained release of a combination of DTX and CPT as hydrophobic interactions of lithocholic acid with DTX, and electrostatic interactions of peptide scaffold with CPT strengthen the gel elasticity. This kind of gel behavior (retaining gel integrity at higher strain) is highly advantageous for cancer therapy applications as compared to self-healing hydrogels that disintegrate under strain during injection and induce uncontrolled burst release of encapsulated drugs. The benefits of such hydrogel-based systems for delivery of chemotherapeutic drugs for cancer, anti-inflammatory bowel disease, arthritis, and infectious diseases have been advocated in many recent studies.

Taxanes including DTX are used for treatment of ovarian, breast, and non–small cell lung cancer. DTX in combination with ceramide agents augment intracellular ceramide levels and synergistically increase cell apoptosis in murine melanoma cells as well as human breast and prostate cancer cells. Antitumor efficacy of ceramide-based nanosuspensions delivering DTX at the tumor site in a murine melanoma model got attention because of their synergistic effect on destruction of the cytoskeleton followed by apoptosis. DTX also down-regulates sphingosine kinase 1 expression in breast and prostate cancer cells and increases apoptosis and chemosensitivity of cancer cells. The combination therapy of platin drugs such as CPT with taxane or tamoxifen is effective in ovarian cancers by targeting the ceramide/S1P rheostat. Therefore, it was worthwhile to unravel the mechanism of ICD by which the DTX-CPT-Gel caused tumor regression in a murine TNBC model.

ICD occurs through three critical processes where release of CRT as a complex with ERp57 on cell surface of early apoptotic cells provides an “eat me” signal to phagocytic cells in the first phase. In the second phase, release of ATP from apoptotic/dying cells helps in infiltration of dendritic cells and macrophages at the tumor site by acting as the “find me” signal. During the third phase, dying cells secrete DAMPs such as HMGB1 that can bind with TLR4 (Toll-like receptor 4) on dendritic cells and activate them to engulf tumor-associated antigens. The nature of chemotherapeutic drugs influences the release of DAMPs that subsequently activate dendritic cells and leads to polarization of naïve CD8+ T cell to cytotoxic granzyme B+CD8+ T cells. Our results showed that localized DTX-CPT-Gel therapy can activate the release of CRT and HMGB1, eventually generating a systemic cytotoxic T cell response. We further showed that these cytotoxic T cells can abrogate distant and metastatic tumors. DTX-CPT-Gel therapy also inhibits MDSCs that are known inhibitors of cytotoxic T cells and also causes repolarization of M2 macrophages to M1 macrophages. As treatment of 4T1 tumors using DTX-CPT-Gel therapy did not extend any apoptotic effect on distant CT26 tumors, the localized DTX-CPT-Gel therapy assured no systemic toxicity and cell abnormality in the mice that was further validated by the cytokine release assay and histochemical staining of tissues from different organs.

Release of DAMPs via chemotherapy-mediated apoptotic cell death is the key requirement for activation of dendritic cells that prime the systemic T cell response. Chemotherapeutic drugs are known to cause apoptotic cell death via disrupting the mitochondrial membrane potential and/or by UPR activation. Therefore, UPR can act as a double-edged sword where stress sensors (PERK) activate CHOP-mediated apoptosis, and activation of Ire1α induces survival signals. Our results showed that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy directs the activity of these sensor kinases toward apoptosis via activation of PERK and by deactivation of IRE1α. Earlier studies have shown that activation of PERK-mediated UPR releases endoplasmic reticulum chaperons such as CRT via the activation of BAX/BAK and soluble N-ethylmaleimide-sensitive factor-attachment protein receptor (SNARE)–mediated exocytosis. As release of DAMPs such as CRT is contingent on activation of UPR, we demonstrated that UPR (PERK) inhibition accelerated tumor progression and deactivates the chemotherapy-mediated polarization of cytotoxic T cells (Fig. 8).

Apart from increased translation and accumulation of misfolded proteins, changes in the metabolism of sphingolipids such as ceramides, sphingosine-1-phosphate, and glycosphingolipids also activate UPR. Ceramides activate UPR through different pathways similar to cathepsin activation where ceramide generated by the inhibition of ceramidases or activation of acid sphingomyelinase in lysosomes binds to cathepsin D, thereby leading to the cleavage of active cathepsin D. Ceramides generated by de novo synthesis pathway can also activate CD95-PERK signaling by facilitating the receptor trimerization. Our results showed that DTX-CPT-Gel therapy induces the expression of sphingolipid metabolic genes that increase ceramide levels specifically via de novo and salvage pathways, and this up-regulation in ceramides lead to UPR activation (Fig. 8). We finally showed that inhibition of de novo sphingolipid pathway by myriocin inhibits UPR activation and cell death. Therefore, chemotherapy-mediated alterations in ceramides cause UPR activation that subsequently releases DAMPs to activate cytotoxic T cell response.

In summary, the proposed hydrogel-mediated delivery of DTX and CPT can overcome systemic toxicity by causing a localized apoptotic effect and, at the same time, can generate systemic T cell response for clearing distant and metastatic tumors. These results reinforce the paradigm that chemotherapy-mediated alterations in sphingolipid metabolic and UPR pathways can be exploited as potential therapeutic targets for future cancer therapeutic strategies.

Methods

 Cell culture 

Murine breast cancer 4T1 (#CRL-2539) and LLC1 (#CRL-1642), colon cancer (CT26) (#CRL-2638), and human MCF-7 (#HTB-22), BT-474 (#HTB-20), and MDA-MB-231 (#HTB-26) breast cancer cells were purchased from American Type Culture Collection, USA. 4T1-12B cells were a gift from G. Sahagian, Tufts University, USA. 4T1 and 4T12B cells were cultured in RPMI-1640, and LLC1, MCF-7, MDA-MB-231, and BT-474 cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1× penicillin-streptomycin solution at 37°C and 5% CO2. Cells were grown to 70 to 80% confluency and harvested using 1× trypsin.

 Cell viability assay 

We determined the IC50 of DTX and CPT using a previously published protocol. To determine the synergistic effect of combination of DTX and CPT, 4T1 cells (30,000 cells per well) were seeded in 24-well plates. Cells in each plate were treated with DTX and CPT in combination ratios of 2:1, 1:2 and 1:1, with concentration of DTX being fixed (40, 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90 nM) and concentration of CPT being varied. After 24 hours of treatment, cells were harvested, and cell viability was assessed. Cell viability was then plotted as a function of fraction affected (fraction of cell death) per concentration, and synergy was determined by calculating the CI using Calcu-Syn software. The CI was calculated using the formulawhere D1 and D2 represent the concentration of drugs D1 (DTX) and D2 (CPT) at which they alone cause 50% cell death, and d1 and d2 represent concentration of DTX and CPT in combination.

 Apoptotic assay 

Cancer cells (4T1) (1 × 105 cells per well) were plated on a 12-well plate. After 24 hours, cells were treated with DTX (60 nM), CPT (30 nM), or combination of DTX (60 nM) and CPT (30 nM) and incubated for 24 hours. Supernatant (for dead cells) and adherent cells were collected by adding 1× trypsin for 5 min and were centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 5 min. Collected cells were washed twice with 1× phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and resuspended in 100 μl of 1× PBS. Cell were stained with annexin V–FITC (2 μl) for 20 min in the dark at 4°C. Propidium iodide solution (1 μl, 200 μg/ml) was added in each vial and incubated for 15 min. Cells were immediately analyzed using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

 Cell cycle assay 

Cancer cells (4T1) (1 × 105 cells per well) were plated on a 12-well plate. After 24 hours, the medium was removed, and incomplete medium (without FBS) was added for synchronization of cells. After 8 hours, the incomplete medium was removed, and cells were treated with DTX (60 nM), CPT (30 nM), or combination of DTX (60 nM) and CPT (30 nM) for 24 hours. Cells were harvested using 1× trypsin and washed with cold 1× PBS followed by fixation in 70% ethanol at 4°C for 2 hours. Cells were again washed with PBS twice and treated with staining buffer including ribonuclease (1 μl, 10 mg/ml) and propidium iodide (5 μl, 1 mg/ml) in 1× PBS overnight. Cells were acquired using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

 ROS assay 

Cancer cells (4T1) (1 × 105 cells per well) were plated on a 12-well plate. After 24 hours of incubation, cells were washed with 1× PBS, followed by incubation with 25 μM DCHF-DA (2,7-dichlorodihydrofluorescin diacetate) for 30 min in 0.2% FBS containing a phenol red–free medium. After incubation, cells were washed with 1× PBS to remove the excess dye solution. Cells were further treated DTX (60 nM), CPT (30 nM), or combination of DTX (60 nM) and CPT (30 nM) and incubated for another 3 hours. Cells were harvested using 1× trypsin and were immediately analyzed using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

 Dendritic cell activation by cancer cell debris 

Cancer cells (4T1) (1 × 105 cells per well) were plated on a 12-well plate in RPMI-1640 complete medium. Cells were then treated with combination of DTX and CPT (250:125 ng/ml) for 24 hours. Cells debris was harvested and centrifuged to remove any traces of drug that were not associated with cell debris. Cell debris was washed thrice in 1× PBS and resuspended in minimum essential medium α (MEM α) complete medium. Cell debris were added to mouse dendritic cells (JAWSII) (1 × 105 cells per well on a 12-well plate) in MEM α media with 20% FBS and granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (5 ng/ml). After 48 hours of stimulation by cell debris, JAWSII cells were harvested and stained with FITC-conjugated anti-mouse CD86 antibody at 4°C for 15 min. Cells were washed twice, were resuspended in fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) buffer, and were acquired using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

 Cell surface expression of CRT and HMGB1 secretion by cancer cells 

Cancer cells (4T1) (1 × 105 cells per well) were plated on a 12-well plate in RPMI-1640 complete medium. Cells were then treated with combination of DTX and CPT (250:125 ng/ml) for 24 hours. Dead cells in the supernatant were collected, and other cells were harvested using trypsin. Cells were washed with 1× PBS and stained with CRT-FITC antibody (1:50) at 4°C for 15 min. Cells were washed twice and resuspended in FACS buffer. Samples were acquired using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA).

For HMGB1 quantification, protein was quantified in a supernatant using bicinchoninic acid protein estimation kit. Protein samples (~15 μg) were loaded in 10% SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis for separation and transferred to a nitrocellulose membrane. Immunostaining was done using HMGB1 antibody (1:1000). After washing, blots were incubated with goat anti-rabbit IgG secondary antibody (1:10,000) for 1 hour and were developed using Immobilon Western Chemiluminescent HRP.

 Gelation studies 

Gelation behavior in the presence of drugs was tested by vial inverting assay, where no flow in the presence of gravitation implies formation of gel. For DTX, the required amount of gelator and DTX (from ethanol stock) was taken in a vial. The solvent was removed under nitrogen and dried under vacuum. The mixture was dissolved in water with heating and sonication and allowed to cool for gelation. CPT entrapment was studied simply by mixing the water-soluble CPT formulation with hydrogelator in water. Drug concentration below which A13 gelator retains its ability to form gel is considered as the maximum drug loading efficiency. Hydrogel loaded with combination of DTX and CPT was prepared by mixing DTX with A13 gelator followed by addition of aqueous solution of CPT. Resultant suspension was heated until the solution turned clear and was allowed to cool at room temperature. The drug-loaded heated solution was taken in a syringe, allowed to cool to form the gel, and was used for animal models using 22- to 26-gauze needle. Gels were characterized for rheology using a Rheoplus MCR102 (Anton-Paar, Graz, Austria) rheometer and for morphology using an ultra plus scanning electron microscope (Carl Zeiss, Germany) as per the published protocols.

 Drug release studies 

Gels (300 μl) loaded with individual or combination of drugs were placed in 5-ml vials covered with 2 ml of release medium [1× PBS (pH 7.4) with 0.05% Tween 80] and incubated at 37°C. At predetermined time intervals, 1 ml of the released medium was collected periodically and replenished with an equal volume of fresh medium. CPT content was analyzed by atomic absorption spectroscopy using platinum standard. Collected samples were reduced at speed vacuum, extracted with ethanol, and quantified using high-performance liquid chromatography for DTX (ultraviolet detector set at 230 nm). Elution was carried out with isocratic solvent system [50% acetonitrile and 50% solvent mixture of water (37.5):methanol (60):tetrahydrofuran (2.5) with 0.2% aq. NH3] using a TOSOH TSK-Gel ODS-100V 5-μm column (4.6 inner diameter × 250 mm) (Waters Ltd., USA).

 Animal experiments 

All animal experiments were conducted using a syngeneic 4T1 murine breast cancer model in female BALB/c female mice (6 to 8 weeks; ~20 g), syngeneic LLC1 model in male C57BL/6 mice (6 to 8 weeks; ~20 g), or xenograft tumor models using MCF-7, BT-474, and MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cell lines in NOD-SCID mice (6 to 8 weeks; ~20 g). Mice were shaved at the flank region to remove hair before the start of the experiment. On day 0, murine 4T1/LLC1 cells (1.5 × 106) suspended in FBS:Matrigel (1:1; 200 μl per mouse) were implanted subcutaneously in the left flank regions of mice. Similarly, for xenograft tumor models, MDA-MB-231 (3 × 106), BT-474 (3 × 106), and MCF-7 (1.5 × 106) cells suspended in FBS:Matrigel (1:1; 200 μl per mouse) were implanted subcutaneously. Mice were randomized in various groups after they develop palpable tumor of 30 to 50 mm3 and were subjected to different treatment regimens.

For testing the efficacy of DTX delivery, mice were divided into four groups such as group 1: untreated control; group 2: DTX-TS [a single dose (200 μl) of DTX (50 mg/kg) was injected near the tumor site]; group 3: DTX-IV [10 doses of intravenous injection of DTX (5 mg/kg) suspended in 2.5% polysorbate 80 (Tween 80) and 1.25% dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)];and group 4: DTX-Gel [a single injection (200 μl) of DTX-loaded A13 hydrogel with DTX (50 mg/kg) near the tumor site]. For testing the efficacy of CPT delivery, mice were divided into four groups such as group 1: untreated control; group 2: CPT-TS [a single dose (200 μl) of CPT (25 mg/kg) was injected near the tumor site]; group 3: CPT-IV [10 doses of intravenous injection of CPT (2.5 mg/kg) in the form of CPT solution on alternate days]; and group 4: CPT-Gel [a single injection (200 μl) of CPT-loaded A13 hydrogel with CPT (25 mg/kg)]. To test the efficacy of combination of DTX and CPT, tumor-bearing mice were randomized into four groups such as group 1: untreated control; group 2: DTX-CPT-TS, where a combination of DTX (50 mg/kg) and CPT (25 mg/kg) was injected as a single dose (200 μl) near the tumor site; group 3: DTX-CPT-IV, where 10 doses of intravenous injection of DTX (5 mg/kg) suspended in 2.5% polysorbate 80 (Tween 80) and 1.25% DMSO and CPT (2.5 mg/kg) in the form of CPT solution on alternate days were injected; and group 4: DTX-CPT-Gel, where a single injection (200 μl) of DTX and CPT-loaded A13 hydrogel with DTX (50 mg/kg) and CPT (25 mg/kg) was injected near the tumor site.

For comparing the effect of DTX-Gel, CPT-Gel, and DTX-CPT-Gel, tumor-bearing mice were randomized into different groups, such as group 1 mice were left untreated. Mice in groups 2 to 4 were treated with a single injection of 200 μl of DTX-Gel (50 mg/kg of DTX), CPT-Gel (25 mg/kg of CPT), or DTX-CPT-Gel (50 mg/kg of DTX and 25 mg/kg of CPT) near the tumor site.

Tumor volume (length × breadth2/2) and body weight were measured on alternate days, and survivability of mice was also monitored. Mice were considered as dead when they fail to eat, drink, or show labored respiration and affected locomotion due to tumor size.

 Distant tumor model 

To evaluate the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on distant tumors, we injected 4T1 cells (1.5 × 106) and 4T1/CT26 cells (0.5 × 106) subcutaneously in the left and right flanks of shaved BALB/c female mice, respectively. After tumor volume reached 50 mm3, 4T1 tumors on the left flank (primary tumor) were either left untreated (group 1) or treated with DTX-CPT-Gel (group 2), and tumors on the right flank (4T1 or CT26) (secondary tumor) were left untreated in both the groups. Tumor volumes were measured every alternate day, and on day 18, mice were euthanized, and tumor tissues were excised for flow cytometry analysis.

 Tumor recurrence model 

To evaluate the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on tumor recurrence, we injected 4T1 cells (1.5 × 106) subcutaneously in shaved BALB/c female mice. After 6 to 7 days, when tumor volume reached ~100 mm3, 4T1 tumors were surgically removed, and the skin at the site of the surgery was sealed using tissue adhesive. After 1 day, mice were randomized in two groups, where group 1 mice were left untreated and DTX-CPT-Gel was implanted in group 2 mice at the surgical site to see the effect on tumor recurrence. Mice were observed every alternate day to monitor the growth of tumors in both groups after tumor resection.

 Metastasis model 

To decipher the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel therapy on metastasis, we injected 4T1-12B (luciferase expressing 4T1 cells) (1.5 × 106) subcutaneously in female BALB/c mice. After 3 to 4 days, tumors were visualized for bioluminescence under IVIS (In Vivo Imaging System) after injecting 100 μl of d-luciferin (10 mg/ml). Mice were randomized in two groups, where group 1 mice were left untreated and DTX-CPT-Gel was implanted in group 2 mice to see the effect on metastasis. Bioluminescence imaging was observed after every 2 days to monitor the effect of DTX-CPT-Gel on tumor growth and metastasis in the upper thoracic cavity using an imaging system (IVIS Spectrum, PerkinElmer, Waltham, USA).

 Toxicity studies 

Female BALB/c mice were randomized into two groups, where group 1 mice were left untreated and DTX-CPT-Gel was implanted subcutaneously in group 2 mice. At day 20, serum and different vital organs including liver, lung, kidney, spleen, and heart were collected; fixed in 10% formalin solution; and processed for H&E staining. From serum samples, blood counts, liver function test, and kidney function, tests along with cytokine analysis were performed.

 H&E staining 

Formalin-fixed tissues were embedded in paraffin blocks by dehydration and then soaking wax. Tissue sections (5 μm) were sliced from paraffin blocks and mounted onto glass slides. Dewaxing was performed by placing slides in xylene solution for 5 to 10 min. Slides were further rehydrated by placing them in 100, 90, 85, and 70% ethanol solution followed by water rinse for 15 min. Slides were then stained with hematoxylin for 30 s and 1% hydrochloride acid water for another 30 s and rinsed with water until sections turn blue. Eosin staining was done for 2 min followed by rinsing in alcohol solution. Slides were further dehydrated by placing it in 95 and 100% ethanol solution followed by xylene solution for 5 min each. Slides were allowed to dry, and later, mounting was done using dibutylphthalate polystyrene xylene (DPX) and coverslip was placed. Sections were observed under a microscope (TiE, Nikon, Minato, Japan) with 40× immersion oil lens, and images were acquired.

 Cytokine quantification by enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay 

Serum samples were processed for cytokine quantification using a LEGENDplex mouse inflammation panel (13-plex) with filter plate, were processed using the manufacturer’s protocol, and were analyzed using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA). Data analysis was done using LEGENDplex Data Analysis Software Suite (BioLegend, San Diego, CA, USA).

 T cell depletion studies 

Tumor (4T1)–bearing mice were divided into five groups as untreated control, DTX-CPT-Gel with isotype IgG control antibody, DTX-CPT-Gel with anti-CD4 antibody, DTX-CPT-Gel with anti-CD8 antibody, and DTX-CPT-Gel with combination of anti-CD4 and anti-CD8 antibodies. Depleting antibodies anti-CD4, anti-CD8, and isotype control were administered intraperitoneally at dose 250 μg per mouse after every 3 days starting from day −1 with a total of four doses. At day 0, mice were treated with subcutaneous injection of DTX-CPT-Gel near the tumor site. Tumor volume was measured every alternate day. Blood samples and excised tumor tissues were collected on last day for flow cytometry analysis.

 Inhibitor studies 

Tumor (4T1)–bearing mice were divided into three groups such as untreated control, DTX-CPT-Gel and DTX-CPT-Gel with TUDCA or DTX-CPT-Gel with PERK inhibitor (GSK2606414, Sigma-Aldrich), or DTX-CPT-Gel with myriocin. At day 0, mice in group 2 were treated with subcutaneous injection of DTX-CPT-Gel, and mice in group 3 were treated with DTX-CPT-TUDCA-Gel (having 25 mg/kg of TUDCA), DTX-CPT-iPERK-Gel (having 10 mg/kg of PERK inhibitor), or DTX-CPT-Myriocin-Gel (having 4 mg/kg of myriocin) near the tumor site. Tumor volume and body weight were measured every alternate day, and tumor tissues were excised on last day for further analysis.

 Vaccination studies 

4T1 cells (2 × 106) were treated with combination of DTX and CPT at a concentration of 20 μM each for 24 hours to induce complete cell death. Cellular debris was rinsed thoroughly with 1× PBS twice to remove traces of drugs. Cellular debris was then subcutaneously injected in female BALB/c mice. Cell debris was again injected after 10 days. Cancer (4T1) cells (0.2 × 106) were then injected in these vaccinated mice and unvaccinated healthy mice (as control), and tumor growth kinetics was measured.

 Flow cytometry studies 

Freshly isolated tumors were first washed with cold 1× PBS (pH 7.4). Samples were chopped, homogenized, subjected to enzymatic digestion using collagenase IV (0.75 mg/ml) and DNase I (50 U/ml) in RPMI-1640 supplemented with 10% FBS (3 to 5 ml per sample), and incubated at 37°C with constant shaking for 45 min. Furthermore, cells were strained using a 70-μm filter and centrifuged at 400g for 5 min at 4°C. For peripheral blood, samples were incubated with 1× red blood cell lysis buffer for 10 min and centrifuged at 400g for 5 min. This step was repeated twice to completely remove erythrocytes. Cells (~5 × 106) were blocked using FACS buffer (1× PBS with 2 mM EDTA with 4% goat serum) at room temperature for 5 to 10 min before staining with cocktail of antibodies for 15 to 10 min in the dark at 4°C. Antibodies used for MDSCs includes anti–Ly6C-FITC, anti–Gr1-PE, anti–Ly6G-PerCP-Cy5.5, anti–CD11b-APC, and anti–CD45-BV510. Combination of anti–IA/IE-PE, anti–Ly6G-PerCP-Cy5.5, anti–CD206-PE-Cy7, anti–CD45-APC, anti–CD11b-APC-Cy7, and anti–CD80-BV510 was used for macrophage staining, and combination of anti–CD8a-FITC, anti–CD196-PE, anti–CD194-PerCP-Cy5.5, anti–CD25-PE-Cy7, anti–CD183-APC, anti–CD4-APC-Cy7, and anti–CD3-BV510 was used for T cells. In peripheral blood, cells were stained with combination of anti–CD3-BV510, anti–CD8-FITC, and anti–CD4-PE-Cy7. Cells were then washed with 1× PBS twice, resuspended in FACS buffer, and stained with 4′,6-diamidino-2-phenylindole for live/dead discrimination. The stained cells were acquired using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA) and were analyzed using Flowjo v10 software.

To determine granzyme B+CD8+ and PD1+CD8+ T cells or inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and arginase 1–expressing macrophages, single cells were first stained with Zombie Aqua fixable viability dye (1:100) in 1× PBS for live/dead staining for 15 min. After washing with 1× PBS, cells were resuspended in FACS buffer. Cells were then stained with combination of anti–CD45-PerCP-Cy5.5, anti–CD3-BV421, anti–CD8-FITC, anti–CD4-PE-Cy7, and anti–PD1-APC-Cy7 for T cells or with combination of anti–CD45-BV421, anti–Ly6G-PerCP-Cy5.5, anti–CD11b-APC-Cy7, and anti–F4/80-APC for macrophages and incubated for 15 min at 4°C in the dark. Cells were then washed with 500 μl of FACS buffer, resuspended in 150 μl of Cyto-Fast Fix/Perm buffer, and incubated for 20 min in the dark. Cells were washed using 1 ml of 1X Cyto-Fast Perm wash solution and resuspended in 100 μl of 1× Cyto-Fast Fix/Perm wash solution. Cells were then stained with anti–granzyme B-PE for T cells or anti–iNOS-PE and anti–arginase 1–PE–Cy7 for macrophages and incubated for 20 min in the dark. Cells were then washed with 500 μl of FACS buffer and resuspended in FACS buffer. Data were then acquired using a flow cytometer (FACSVerse, BD Biosciences, NJ, USA) and analyzed using Flowjo v10 software.

 Immunofluorescence and immunohistochemistry 

4T1 cells (5 × 104 cells per well) were seeded on a coverslip on a 24-well plate in complete RPMI-1640. After 24 hours, cells were treated with combination of DTX and CPT (250:125 ng/ml) for 24 hours. Medium was discarded from the wells followed by Dulbecco’s PBS washing for two times. Cells on a coverslip were fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde solution for 15 min and stained with primary CRT antibody (1:500) followed by incubation with anti-rabbit IgG conjugated with Alexa Flour 594 (1:750). For immunofluorescence staining in tumor tissues, tumor sections were prepared (5 μm) and stained using a published protocol.

For immunohistochemistry, slides containing 5-μm tissue sections were fixed with 10% formalin for 15 to 20 min followed by washing with 1× PBS twice. Sections were then incubated in 0.1% Triton X-100 for 5 to 10 min to permeabilize the cell membrane. Heat-induced epitope retrieval was done using tris-EDTA buffer (pH 9.0) in a pressure cooker for 20 min followed by washing with 1× PBS twice. Slides were further incubated with 3% H2O2 solution for 10 min followed by washing with 1× PBS twice. Sections were then incubated in blocking buffer (10% goat serum in 1× PBS) for 1 hour. Staining was done with caspase 3 (1:1000) overnight at 4°C in a humidified chamber. Sections were then washed with 1× PBST (1× PBS with 0.1% Tween 20) followed by incubation with anti-rabbit IgG conjugated with HRP for 2 hours in the dark. Slides were then incubated with DAB substrate until the brown color appears. It was further stained with hematoxylin for 30 s to stain nuclei and was washed to remove excess stain. Slides were then dipped in 70, 80, and 90% ethanol followed by xylene to dehydrate sections. Mounting was done using DPX, and coverslip was placed. Sections were observed under a microscope with 40× immersion oil lens, and images were acquired. For assessing apoptosis, TUNEL assay was performed as per the published protocol.

 Sphingolipid quantification 

Lipids were isolated from tumor tissues and were quantified as per the previously published protocol.

 RNA isolation and qRT-PCR 

RNA isolation, cDNA synthesis, and RT-PCR were done using methods described previously. All primer sequences used for RT-PCR are listed in table S1.

 RNA-seq and analysis 

RNA isolation, preparation of cDNA libraries, and the sequencing were carried out using a previously mentioned protocol. The reads were aligned with the TopHat2 software against the mouse reference genome (Genome Reference Consortium Mouse Build 38.93). The read summarization and differential expression analysis were carried out using Finding Alternative Splicing Events (FASE) pipeline. Genes with a fold change of ±1.5 and a P value ≤0.05 (in the FASE ranking) were filtered as differentially expressed. GO term enrichment analysis was conducted using the DAVID, with all default parameters and a cut-off set at a P value ≤0.05 (Fisher’s exact test) and/or a false discovery rate ≤ 25. The functional association was established using Biological Processes (GO_BP_Direct) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes pathways.

 Immunoblotting 

Protein samples were prepared and run using the previously published protocol. Immunostaining was done using CERS1 (1:5000), CERS2 (1:5000), CERS4 (1:5000), CERS5 (1:5000), CERS6 (1:5000), SMPD1 (1:2500), SMPD2 (1:2500), SMPD3 (1:5000), SMPD4 (1:1000), SMS1 (1:5000), SMS2 (1:5000), UGCG (1:5000), GBA1 (1:5000), BIP (1:5000), PERK (1:5000), p-PERK (1:5000), EIF2α (1:5000), p-EIF2α (1:5000), CHOP (1:5000), ATF4 (1:5000), BAX (1:5000), BAK (1:5000), BCL-2 (1:5000), caspase 3 (1:5000), IRE1α (1:5000, CST), p-IRE1α (1:5000), XBP1s (1:5000), ATF6 (1:5000), and actin (1:5000) by overnight incubation at 4°C in 5% BSA in Tris-buffered saline with 0.1% Tween 20 detergent (TBST) solution. After washing, the blots were incubated with goat anti-mouse IgG (L + H) secondary antibody and goat anti-rabbit IgG secondary antibody (1:10,000) for 1 hour. Exposed x-ray sheets were developed using the Immobilon Western Chemiluminescent HRP. β-Actin presented in the figures is the representative from three replicates.

 Statistical analysis 

We performed statistical analyses using GraphPad Prism 7 (GraphPad Software). Data are represented as means ± SD or means ± SEM. We performed pairwise comparisons using unpaired Student’s t test, multiple comparisons among groups using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA), growth kinetic analysis using two-way ANOVA, and survival analysis using log-rank Mantel-Cox test. Differences between groups were considered significant at P values below 0.05 (*P < 0.05, **P < 0.005, ***P < 0.0005, and ****P < 0.0001).
