# Functional genomics for improving adoptive T-cell transfer therapies.

## Abstract

Adoptive cell therapy (ACT) has shown remarkable success in the treatment of some malignancies, particularly leukemia. However, there are multiple factors that limit the durability of ACT in solid tumors, including dose-limiting toxicities, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, and T-cell exhaustion. As the manufacture and preparation of adoptive T-cell therapies allows time and adequate conditions for ex vivo T-cell engineering, forward genetic screens can identify novel genetic targets that could improve their effectiveness. CRISPR is a commonly used functional genomics tool that has been successfully used to both enhance our understanding of mechanisms of resistance and to discover potential genetic edits to improve ACT. A complementary approach, Sleeping Beauty transposon mutagenesis provides additional opportunities to identify novel genetic edits without being constrained by the annotated human genome. Here, we summarize forward genetic screens and their tools to uncover strategies to enhance ACT. Complementary approaches can be combined and improved on to identify translatable genetic editing strategies through studies that accurately recapitulate disease-specific challenges.

Abstract

Improving adoptive T-cell transfer therapy for cancer

 Adoptive cell transfer (ACT) has proven an effective strategy for treating cancer, producing durable therapeutic responses in patients with B-cell malignancies such as acute lymphoblastic leukemia, non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma, and multiple myeloma. Autologous T cells are isolated and expanded ex vivo before re-infusion, providing opportunity to genetically engineer the cells to increase their antitumor capabilities. T-cell engineering is an attractive strategy because of the design flexibility that exemplifies precision therapy. Natural or synthetic receptors can be used to redirect T-cell specificity to specific targets. Inclusion of different intracellular signaling domains in these receptors enables fine-tuning of T-cell effector functions, and co-delivery of additional coding sequences like cytokine receptors further customizes the therapy to an individual patient’s needs. The popularity of cellular therapy is evident in the large number of clinical trials: as of this writing, ClinicalTrials.gov lists 1915 interventional trials employing T-cell ACT (online supplemental table S1), and the US Food and Drug Administration has approved nine ACT therapies for cancer treatment.

The increasing adoption of ACT has led to the identification of several significant barriers to therapeutic success (figure 1). Biological challenges include identification of suitable targets, increasing and maintaining effector function, appropriate trafficking and persistence, reduction of immune-related toxicities, and prevention of immune escape. Manufacturing challenges include reducing the cost and time required to produce the product, and creation of off-the-shelf approaches that optimize precision with individualization. Attempts to overcome these challenges often leverage known biologic mechanisms. For example, cytokines interleukin (IL)-2, IL-15, and IL-7 have been known for decades to support T-cell survival and homeostasis, and so these have been employed to enhance the persistence of adoptively transferred cells. While rational, testing known pathways requires a priori knowledge of biological targets and limits state-of-the-art discovery.

Forward genetic screens, a type of functional genomics, are efficient and powerful tools to discover novel processes underlying common ACT barriers. Unlike targeted approaches, screens can identify the essential genes for a phenotype of interest in a rapid and high-throughput manner. Further, screens can discover unexpected pathways and yield novel functional insights. Finally, some screens can be conducted in vivo under physiologically relevant conditions mimicking the complex tumor microenvironment (TME), which may improve the translational success rate. This review discusses functional genomic technologies that have been employed to improve ACT for cancer.

CRISPR technologies enable targeted and genome-wide discovery

The Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR)-CRISPR-associated proteins (Cas) or CRISPR-Cas system is a powerful tool for mutagenesis screens identifying genes that could overcome key barriers to ACT (figure 2A). Cas endonuclease engineering has produced CRISPR knockout (CRISPR-KO), CRISPR activation (CRISPRa), CRISPR inhibition (CRISPRi), and base-editing capabilities. All CRISPR-based screens require a single guide RNA (sgRNA) of complementary sequence to a target DNA strand to direct Cas binding to this site and subsequent genetic perturbation (figure 2B). Thus, sgRNA design plays a critical role in determining the action and efficiency of CRISPR editing.

CRISPR has proven successful as a screening tool and is the most widely employed technology for this purpose. Both in vitro and in vivo screens are feasible so long as the Cas enzyme and a library of sgRNAs can be stably delivered to the cells of interest. Delivery is often viral and is one of the first points where variance is introduced. To account for variance in delivery, a pre-selection sample must be taken that can represent the control starting pool. Once selection pressure has been applied (eg, treatment conditions under which only cells carrying mutations in GeneX survive), mutated cells are recovered and sgRNAs from both pre-selection and selected pools are sequenced. Thus, gene candidates in CRISPR screens are indirectly inferred by observing proxy sgRNA sequences and mapping these to the genes they target (figure 2C).

Awareness of system attributes and technical limitations is critical for screen design and interpretation. CRISPR screens can be targeted by limiting inclusion of sgRNAs to a set list of genes or genome-wide by including sgRNAs to all genes in the annotated genome. Gene coverage varies widely between studies and the majority of screens use a small, targeted pool of sgRNAs to reduce complexity and cost. Importantly, all sgRNAs are not equally functional. Guides vary in both off-target effects and targeting efficiency for genes. To reduce the impact this variability has on screen interpretation, CRISPR screens include multiple sgRNA sequences for every gene targeted in the screen. Typically, it is recommended that a genome-wide library is used when mechanisms are unknown and cell numbers are ample, and a custom/targeted (or tiling) set when higher sensitivity or more replicates are needed, or one has a limited number of cells available. One should maintain 300–500 cells per sgRNA at every step, although some reports suggest 1,000 cells per sgRNA. It is also recommended that one include at least three biological replicates and run for roughly 10–14 doublings for selection to occur.

Other artifacts can result from copy number alterations on a gene-by-gene level. CRISPR can induce telomeric truncations and genomic rearrangements that may cause functional consequences that are not detectable by gRNA sequencing. Copy number bias, when a region targeted by the sgRNA is present in multiple or variable copies in the genome, may artificially increase or decrease apparent mutation rates, leading to spurious candidate identification. These biases can be mitigated with downstream analysis tools.

MAGeCK is a popular computational tool to analyze CRISPR screens. Two measures are used to calculate the selection of sgRNAs: robust rank aggregation (RRA) and maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). RRA is used to compare sgRNA counts in two conditions and MLE is used to compare three or more conditions. MAGeCK and other screen analysis tools (reviewed and benchmarked in) are capable of mitigating some of the biases discussed above, including read count normalization of sgRNAs targeting non-essential genes to correct for variance in sgRNA delivery, and optional copy number correction requiring input of known copy number alterations.

Importantly, not all tools can accurately handle unknown copy number variations. This is especially problematic in CRISPR-KO screens where sgRNA-induced breaks may introduce large-scale deletion and chromosome arm loss. Lazar, et al have shown that approximately 4–25% of CRISPR-targeted KO genes can result in the loss of an entire chromosome arm, leading to unexpected phenotypic similarities with genes located on the lost chromosome arm. This phenomenon, known as “proximity bias”, tends to enrich clones with the loss of genes involved in cell cycle regulation and apoptosis, such as TP53 and CDKN2A, which are not directly related to the sgRNA targets. Such undesired genomic rearrangements can compromise the accuracy of CRISPR-KO screening results by generating false-positive hits if CRISPR-induced copy number variation is not corrected. Unsupervised correction cannot be performed using MAGeCK; however, other tools were better designed for proximity bias mitigation, including Crispy and AC-Chronos (reviewed in). There is no consensus on which tool is best for all screen designs, and CRISPR analysis methods continue to be developed (table 1).

A variety of tools have been engineered to analyze results of insertional mutation screens, including Sleeping Beauty (SB) screens. Unlike CRISPR tools, there is not an established best practice for sequencing methods and subsequent computational analyses.

SB technologies are especially well-suited to genome-wide discovery

SB mutagenesis is a complementary technology to CRISPR and was designed for functional genomics. In contrast to CRISPR, SB mutagenesis uses a cut-and-paste transposon sequence that inserts randomly at TA dinucleotide sites in the genome rather than a library of sgRNAs targeting specific genes, negating all reliance on genome annotation and variance in sgRNA specificity and efficiency (figure 2). SB transposons, such as T2/Onc and T2/Onc2, are engineered to create both gain-of-function and loss-of-function alleles in native genes, just as with CRISPR. Most often used SB transposon elements carry a murine stem cell virus (MSCV) long terminal repeat (LTR) promoter, or another promoter, with a splice-donor (SD) that can drive transcription from the transposon into nearby exons; when the SD splices into downstream endogenous splice-acceptors, chimeric messenger RNAs are produced that often encode full-length or N-terminally truncated (activated) proteins, that is, gain of function. The choice of the promoter in the SB transposon is thus important, since it should be highly active in the target cell type to be useful for creating gain of function genetic alterations. The MSCV LTR is very active in T lymphocytes and is thus suitable for screens in this cell type. The transposon cassettes also contain bidirectional splice-acceptors and polyadenylation signals (polyA) that trap and prematurely terminate host transcripts when the element inserts within a gene, yielding C-terminally truncated or otherwise truncated products and effectively creating loss-of-function alleles. These features allow SB vectors to generate activating, inactivating, and fusion events across the genome in an insertion-site-dependent manner, making them broadly useful for unbiased genetic screens in vitro and in vivo (figure 3).

As with CRISPR, both in vitro and in vivo screens can be performed using SB. In vitro screens require transient expression of transposase and delivery of donor transposon template. Transposases have been engineered for varying levels of efficiency and there is experimental control over the number of transposons introduced per cell. In vivo screens were made feasible on engineering of transgenic mice with a concatemer of transposons and conditional transposase expression. Mutagenesis thus produces dozens to hundreds of genome-wide perturbations at the single-cell level. Combined, these attributes make the practice of performing an SB screen much easier than performing a CRISPR screen.

Unlike CRISPR, SB mutagenesis does not produce off-target mutations, nor does it exhibit proximity bias. Instead, SB transposition is impacted by epigenetic accessibility of the insertion sites and location of the donor transposon site within the genome. Transposition tends to favor regions near the donor site, a phenomenon known as “local hopping”. This bias can result in 30–50% of total insertions occurring within 10 Mb of the transgenic concatemer donor sites. Localized transposition may restrict the diversity of mutagenesis and could impact the identification of distant genetic targets. This can be circumvented by using two different construct systems on different chromosomes and removing the insertions on each respective chromosome. Together, these limitations increase the complexity of SB screens and their subsequent analysis.

Unlike CRISPR, SB mutations are directly observed via sequencing the transposon insertion site itself using either gDNA or RNA as template material. Different library preparation methods introduce distinct technical artifacts. For instance, linker-mediated polymerase chain reaction (LM-PCR) methods for gDNA amplification introduce PCR bias due to uneven amplification during library preparation, and the interpretation of the insertion data is tied to the sequencing library preparation method. As such, many tools have been created to analyze SB screens that mitigate known system biases and perform statistical analyses using different methodologies. The tools used for SB analysis are described for comparison in table 1. It is recommended to consider analysis in the early stages of experimental design. As with CRISPR analysis tools, methods are expected to continually improve as usage increases.

T-cell screens

ACT uses cells from a patient with cancer’s own blood (T cell receptor, TCR-T and chimeric antigen receptor, CAR-T) or tumor (tumor infiltrating lymphocytes, TIL). Taking the cells out of the patient’s body allows for an ex vivo respite from hostile conditions during which cells are expanded under optimal conditions for activation and proliferation. If cells are genetically modified, it takes place during this period prior to infusion back into the patient. Thus, identifying and modulating T cell-intrinsic targets that can overcome the critical barriers in figure 1 is an attractive prospect.

CRISPR has been widely employed to discover T cell-intrinsic genes mechanistically involved in cytotoxicity, exhaustion, and persistence (online supplemental table S2). An in vitro CRISPRa screen including over 20,000 gene targets was performed in primary murine CD8+ T cells to identify which genes positively regulate T-cell cytotoxicity. Authors discovered that enforcing proline dehydrogenase 2 expression in CAR-T cells increased proline metabolism that supported cytotoxic function. To identify regulators of CAR-T cell exhaustion, a genome-wide CRISPR-KO screen was performed using healthy human donor CAR-T cells. These CAR-Ts underwent mutagenesis and genetic selection using an in vitro model for exhaustion. This study identified a role for the IL-4 pathway in CAR-T cell dysfunction and validation studies showed the improvement of CAR-T cell activity and a reduction in exhaustion when the IL-4 pathway was interrupted. Another study evaluated regulators of CAR-T cell persistence by performing an in vitro genome-wide CRISPR screen by sorting CAR-T cells into programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1)+ and PD-1− groups after exposure to an in vitro tumor rechallenge model. This study showed that targeted knockout of the transcription co-repressor TLE family member 4 and HELIOS family zinc finger 2 can improve CAR-T cell activity and reduce the incidence of exhaustion.

While in vitro genetic screens can be successful in identifying genes impacting T-cell functions, the complexities of the TME are difficult to replicate with in vitro two-dimensional or even three-dimensional killing assays. Specifically, in vivo methods better model cell–cell interactions, bioactive molecule distribution, spatially appropriate interactions with the extracellular matrix (ECM), and nutrient availability. This is particularly relevant in solid tumors like pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma, where the majority of the tumor mass is not tumor cells, but a complex milieu of immune cells, fibroblasts, endothelial cells and extracellular matrix.

In vivo screens present unique technical challenges, as in situ mutagenesis is difficult to achieve in T cells. As such, many of these screens perform mutagenesis ex vivo and transfer cells back into a tumor-bearing recipient for genetic selection. One notable exception to this is an in vivo SB screen, where endogenous T cells are mutagenized prior to tumor introduction. This screen successfully identified hundreds of T-cell genes that improve intratumoral accumulation, and adapter-associated kinase 1, small proline-rich protein 1b, and enoyl-coenzyme A hydratase were validated experimentally in subsequent in vivo adoptive transfer models, improving the number of T cells making it to the solid TME.

In vivo screen designs are more costly than in vitro screens, and one way to reduce cost is to reduce the genome coverage. Reducing the mathematical space of the screen effectively limits the number of observations required to achieve statistical significance. Many in vivo screens using CRISPR or shRNA technologies have taken this approach, as both CRISPR and shRNA require a library of sgRNAs or shRNAs to perturb target gene expression. Indeed, ~45% of T-cell screens we identified for ACT interrogated 255 or fewer genes (online supplemental table S2). One in vivo screen chose to target potentially druggable kinases and phosphatases, reasoning that these targets may already have small molecule pharmacologic agents available to facilitate translation to the clinic. Limiting screens to this extent reduces power for discovery and introduces bias in target selection.

Tumor cell screens

Cancer cells have evolved numerous mechanisms to evade endogenous immune recognition, and these have been recently reviewed. Many evasive mechanisms, such as reduced tumor antigen expression or presentation, immune exclusion, and production of immunosuppressive molecules are directly involved in therapeutic failure of ACT. While direct genetic manipulation of tumor intrinsic mediators of ACT resistance is not as facile as in the therapeutic T cells, it is still important to identify these regulators to rationally design combinatorial therapies that would support ACT success. Identification of tumor-intrinsic mechanisms of resistance to T-cell mediated killing is the goal of many of the screens identified in online supplemental table S2.

In vivo screen design most commonly employs an “indirect” or “transplantation” approach where CRISPR components are delivered to cell lines in vitro, followed by implantation in vivo. This approach has commonly been used to identify immunotherapy targets. Such indirect methods allow for use of large sgRNA libraries to broadly interrogate the cancer genome. Additionally, indirect methods allow for use of well-characterized tumor cell lines that recapitulate human cancer and can be further genetically manipulated to answer specific questions. However, large sgRNA libraries require implantation of considerable numbers of cells. Further, many studies have used subcutaneously implanted tumors that fail to model tissue-specific phenomena and, in worst-case scenarios, could identify irrelevant targets.

Importantly, in vitro and in vivo screens can produce distinct and sometimes conflicting results, as was the case in two CRISPR screens interrogating the interferon-γ pathway in CAR-T resistance. In the context of an immunocompetent mouse model of leukemia, loss of interferon gamma (IFNγR/JAK/STAT) signaling sensitized tumors to CAR-T cell therapy; however, in the in vitro screen, loss of IFNγR signaling protected tumor cells from CAR-T cell killing. These conflicting findings highlight the need to validate screening results both in vitro and in vivo in the context of immunocompetent models. The optimization of single-cell CRISPR screening approaches in the context of a competent immune system may also be a promising future direction.

One notable limitation of the CRISPR/Cas9 machinery for immune-related screens is that it can incite adaptive and humoral immune responses in both mice and humans. Notably, while this limitation can be partially overcome through use of alternative viral delivery systems, such as adeno-associated virus, non-viral cloning and other genetic engineering components can still generate immunity. Attempts to completely mitigate immunogenicity of screening tools involve removable systems, which can be used to excise all engineering components following transduction using Cre-mediated recombination. However, excision techniques also typically excise sgRNAs, which typically serve as barcodes at experimental endpoints. In response, integrase-deficient lentivirus has been used to deliver Cre, allowing for removal of CRISPR/Cas9 and viral elements from cells without disturbing sgRNA.

A key driver of cancer immune evasion is antigenic heterogeneity, and tumor cell lines do not adequately model this feature. Indeed, this is part of the original rationale for designing SB as a mutagenic tool to identify tumor driver genes and accurately recapitulate heterogeneous and spontaneous tumor development. An SB screen aimed at identifying tumor driver genes that regulate immune escape has been performed. This screen initiated SB mutagenesis in all tissues in two selection contexts: normal immune selective pressure and immunodeficient (Rag2-deleted) selective pressure. By comparing tumor driver gene selection in both cohorts, differential gene selection was observed and ubiquitin-like modifier activating enzyme 1 (Uba1) was identified as an immunogenic factor that prevents immune escape when expressed. These results indicate that tumors expressing Uba1 might be more successfully treated with ACT and other immunotherapies.

CRISPR and SB mutagenesis: two complementary technologies

CRISPR and SB are distinct technologies to induce mutations for functional genomics. The strength of CRISPR lies in the ability to perform smaller, targeted screens where mutagenesis occurs in vitro. Because detection of sgRNA serves as mutation readout, it is also feasible to perform “drop out” screens (eg, identify sgRNAs that are selected against and disappear from the unselected pool on applying selective pressure). However, scaling up to whole genome coverage and inducing mutation in vivo is extremely costly and laborious. In these situations, it is preferable to use SB technology, which is by nature a whole genome mutagenic system. Whole genome SB screens are cheaper to perform than CRISPR and can be as easily applied to in vitro and in vivo screen designs.

Nuanced differences in the nature of genomic perturbation capabilities exist between technologies (figure 4). Loss-of-function mutations include gene deletion, gene expression downregulation, and point mutation. These are not mechanistically equivalent. For example, deletion of essential genes results in lethality, such that the role of essential genes in a different phenotype of interest cannot be evaluated. However, downregulation or hypomorphic mutation of the same gene can enable evaluation of phenotypic consequence. Similarly, gain-of-function mutations are pleiotropic and include gene expression upregulation, copy number amplification, point mutation, and truncation. There are many examples where overexpression of a full-length gene product differs from overexpression of a truncated mutant.

Thus, it is scientifically, if not financially, advantageous to employ multiple screen technologies when implementing a functional genomics screen, as distinct technologies have the power to produce distinct molecular insights. Rank aggregation methods may be used to combine independent screen results at the gene level regardless of their genome coverage or screen tool choice. Rank aggregation takes two or more ranked lists and combines them into a single ranked list by optimizing the preservation of relative positions between the items. This method is especially useful if there are multiple screens focused on the same biological process; however, ranking focuses on strength of selection for each gene and does not account for differential impact that point mutation may have versus deletion. In the future, it may be desirable to produce aggregate rankings based on both selection strength and their predicted pathogenic effect on protein function.

Cutting-edge innovations

Most of the screens discussed in this review analyzed pools of cells, such that genetic interactions within single cells (intracellular genetic interaction) and between cells (intercellular genetic interaction) may only be inferred with difficulty. As single-cell and spatial sequencing technologies increase throughput and come down in price, it will be highly informative to adopt and, if necessary, innovate methods and tools that allow direct observation of genetic interactions with single-cell and spatial resolution. In this last section, we describe the current progress in extension of CRISPR and SB screen tools to single cell and spatially resolved screen designs.

Integration of single-cell methods into CRISPR/Cas9 screening approaches has begun with methods like Perturb-seq, though most approaches remain feasible only with in vitro assays. Such techniques (reviewed elsewhere) have powerful advantages over bulk RNA sequencing but have not been fully integrated with in vivo immune cell screens. This is, in large part, due to threshold limitations in cell numbers required for modern single-cell technologies. To date, single cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has been combined with in vivo screening only once and with a small (180 genes) sgRNA library. Single-cell analysis allowed for dissection of how two gene candidates influenced distinct T-cell differentiation states. Similar advances have been made using SB technology, whereby significant cooperativity was discovered in tumor cells between NEDD4L and BRAF mutations to confer resistance to drug-mediated killing. Interpretation of these two types of intracellular genetic interactions would not have been possible without single-cell resolution.

A limitation of both bulk and single-cell methods is that tissues are dissociated prior to mutation identification, obscuring intercellular genetic interactions. Such intercellular interactions are important in the TME. For example, T-cell distribution within the tumor is heterogenous, and specific T-cell intrinsic mutations that lead to increased overall infiltration do not necessarily increase direct engagement with tumor cells. To measure local effects, a CRISPR approach has been devised called Perturb-map. Authors of this approach implemented a targeted (35 genes) screen in murine lung cancer cells and identified phenotypically distinct regions harboring distinct CRISPR mutations. A spatially resolved SB equivalent technology has not yet been described but is a highly desirable companion technology to spatial CRISPR approaches.

Conclusions and future directions

Forward genetic screens are powerful tools to discover key drivers of ACT success or failure, and distinct but complementary mutagenesis tools are more powerful when combined. As the research community amasses more data, there will be movement toward leveraging these data to build sophisticated computational models describing the spatiotemporal complexity of molecular circuits in whole organisms. This will be achieved through functional genomics performed using many different genetic perturbation approaches under many different selection conditions. While many T cell and tumor cell targets have been identified that may improve ACT, there remains a knowledge gap in understanding how to prioritize targets, validate safety and efficacy, and use faithful animal models of human malignancy to predict clinical benefit.
