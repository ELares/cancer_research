# IL-10-expressing CAR T cells resist dysfunction and mediate durable clearance of solid tumors and metastases.

## Abstract

The success of chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cell therapy in treating several hematopoietic malignancies has been difficult to replicate in solid tumors, in part because of T cell exhaustion and eventually dysfunction. To counter T cell dysfunction in the tumor microenvironment, we metabolically armored CAR T cells by engineering them to secrete interleukin-10 (IL-10). We show that IL-10 CAR T cells preserve intact mitochondrial structure and function in the tumor microenvironment and increase oxidative phosphorylation in a mitochondrial pyruvate carrier-dependent manner. IL-10 secretion promoted proliferation and effector function of CAR T cells, leading to complete regression of established solid tumors and metastatic cancers across several cancer types in syngeneic and xenograft mouse models, including colon cancer, breast cancer, melanoma and pancreatic cancer. IL-10 CAR T cells also induced stem cell-like memory responses in lymphoid organs that imparted durable protection against tumor rechallenge. Our results establish a generalizable approach to counter CAR T cell dysfunction through metabolic armoring, leading to solid tumor eradication and long-lasting immune protection.

16

Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy has achieved a remarkable clinical 
1 
success in treating B cell malignancies. However, CAR-T cells have so far shown 
2 
limited efficacy against solid tumors, and long-term disease control remains rare. One 
3 
of the major factors that hinder the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapy is T cell 
4 
dysfunction in tumors due to T cell exhaustion1. Exhausted T cells are characterized 
5 
as a distinct population with complete or partial loss of proliferative capacity and 
6 
effector function, upregulation of multiple immune inhibitory receptors, and 
7 
transcriptional and epigenetic alterations2–5. Metabolic fitness is essential to sustain T 
8 
cell’s survival and function. Emerging evidence suggests that metabolic alteration and 
9 
deficiency of intratumoral T cells drive T cell exhaustion6–8. Mitochondrial 
10 
dysfunction imposed by mitochondrial depolarization and oxidative stress was shown 
11 
to reinforce phenotypic and epigenetic exhaustion programs in tumor-infiltrating T 
12 
cells7,8. In addition, impairing mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) 
13 
suppresses T cell proliferation and promotes exhaustion9. Strategies sustaining 
14 
mitochondrial fitness and respiration, such as antioxidant treatment, could restore the 
15 
proliferation and effector function of exhausted T cells7,8. CAR-T cells cultured in 
16 
media supplemented with cytokines, such as engineered interleukin (IL)-2 or IL-15, 
17 
exhibited improved metabolic fitness and enhanced anti-tumor immunity in vivo 
18 
when transferred to treat tumor bearing mice10,11. However, it remains challenging to 
19 
modulate T cell metabolism in vivo and rejuvenate exhausted T cells in the tumor 
20 
microenvironment (TME).   
21 
 
22 
We recently reported that in vivo administration of an IL-10–Fc fusion protein 
23 
reprograms intratumoral T cell metabolism towards OXPHOS and increases the 
24 
mitochondrial respiratory capacity, leading to potently enhanced expansion and 
25

effector function of terminally exhausted CD8+ tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes 
1 
(TILs)12. The metabolic reprogramming strategy could therefore be exploited to 
2 
engineer metabolically armored CAR-T cell therapy to better treat solid tumors. Here, 
3 
we show that CAR-T cells engineered to express IL-10 prevented the functional 
4 
impairment associated with T cell exhaustion in the TME, leading to enhanced 
5 
proliferative capacity and effector function. Further analyses revealed that IL-10 
6 
expression improved the mitochondrial fitness and increased OXPHOS of CAR-T 
7 
cells in a mitochondrial pyruvate carrier (MPC)-dependent manner. Infusion of mouse 
8 
and human IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells eradicated established solid tumors in 
9 
multiple syngeneic and xenograft mouse models, respectively, and induced stem cell-
10 
like memory (Tscm) responses to control tumor recurrence. The metabolically 
11 
armored CAR-T cell is a promising therapeutic strategy to counter T cell exhaustion-
12 
associated dysfunction and induce potent and long-term antitumor immunity.

Results 
15 
IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells counter dysfunction in tumors 
16 
Adoptive transfer of CAR-T cells against human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 
17 
(HER2) failed to control established solid tumors partially due to functional 
18 
impairment of tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells, which exhibited reduced cytotoxicity 
19 
and higher expression levels of inhibitory receptors as compared to the transferred 
20 
CAR-T cells found in spleen (Extended Data Fig. 1a-e). To counter the T cell 
21 
dysfunction in the TME, we produced a mouse IL-10-expressing HER2 CAR-T cell 
22 
(IL-10 HER2 CAR-T) using a tandem construct encoding the second-generation anti-
23 
HER2 CAR and mouse IL-10 linked with a cleavable 2A peptide sequence (Fig. 1a). 
24 
The IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells expressed approximately the same level of CAR as the 
25

HER2 CAR-T cells (Extended Data Fig. 1f) but secreted a high level of IL-10 in the 
1 
culture (Extended Data Fig. 1g). The secreted IL-10 showed negligible impact on the 
2 
phenotypes and viability of CAR-T cells during the ex vivo preparation phase prior to 
3 
infusion (Extended Data Fig. 1h-q). 
4 
 
5 
We next intravenously (i.v.) transferred IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells (3 × 106) or HER2 
6 
CAR-T cells (3 × 106) in the presence or absence of i.v. administered free mouse IL-
7 
10 to treat established subcutaneous (s.c.) HER2-expressing MC38 (MC38-HER2) 
8 
murine colon adenocarcinoma tumors in a mouse model (Fig. 1b). In comparison to 
9 
the conventional CAR-T cells, IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells exhibited ~5.3-fold increase 
10 
of cell counts, augmented Ki67 expression, and increased expansion of the population 
11 
that secrets IL-2 in the tumor, suggesting IL-10 expression markedly enhanced CAR-
12 
T cell proliferation (Fig. 1c-e). Consistent with previous reports12, the PD-1+TIM-3+ 
13 
terminally exhausted subset was notably expanded whereas the counts of progenitor 
14 
exhausted T cells (TCF-1+PD-1+TIM-3−) remained unchanged (Fig. 1f; Extended 
15 
Data Fig. 2a). IL-10 secretion enhanced cytotoxicity and polyfunction of HER2 CAR-
16 
T cells including both terminally and progenitor exhausted subsets (Fig. 1g, h; 
17 
Extended Data Fig. 2b, c). IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells also exhibited reduced 
18 
expression level of PD-1 suggesting alleviated exhaustion (Fig. 1i). On contrast, 
19 
secreted IL-10 showed negligible effects on other immune cells or tumor-infiltrating 
20 
endogenous T cells including regulatory T cells (Tregs) (Extended Data Fig. 2d-g). 
21 
HER2 CAR-T cells with the highest CAR expression density showed decreased 
22 
cytokine production and increased PD-1 and TIM-3 expression (Fig. 1j-l; Extended 
23 
Data Fig. 2h, i), suggesting strong antigen stimulation through CAR receptor may 
24 
drive the exhaustion of intratumoral CAR-T cells13,14. The extent of functional 
25

restoration of IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells by IL-10 secretion seemed dependent on the 
1 
CAR signaling; the reinvigoration of exhausted CAR-T cells was in general more 
2 
prominent when there was a higher CAR density. Of note, we found delivery of IL-10 
3 
through CAR-T cell secretion was superior to i.v. administration in reinvigorating 
4 
exhausted CAR-T cells (Fig. 1c-l) likely because the secreted IL-10 was enriched in 
5 
tumors and predominantly acted on tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells.

7 
To extend this strategy to human CAR-T (hCAR-T) cells, we prepared IL-10-
8 
expressing CD19-targeted T cell receptor alpha constant (TRAC)-deficient hCAR-T 
9 
cells (CD19 hCAR) (Extended Data Fig. 3a, b). IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T cells secreted 
10 
human IL-10 in culture and showed enhanced tumor-lytic potential against CD19-
11 
expressing PANC1 human pancreatic tumor cells (PANC1-CD19) and Raji 
12 
lymphoma cells in vitro (Extended Data Fig. 3c-e). Upon multiple rounds of CAR 
13 
stimulation by CD19 expressing 3T3 or PANC1 cells in long-term culture, IL-10 
14 
CD19 hCAR-T cells exhibited increased expansion as compared to conventional 
15 
CD19 hCAR-T cells (Extended Data Fig. 3f, g). Similar to the mouse CAR-T cells, 
16 
IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T cells that were transferred (i.v.) to immune-deficient NSG mice 
17 
bearing PANC1-CD19 tumors showed enhanced production of granzyme B and IFNγ 
18 
as compared to conventional CD19 hCAR-T cells (Fig. 1m-o). Altogether, 
19 
incorporation of IL-10 secretion countered CAR-T cell dysfunction in tumor by 
20 
promoting their proliferation and effector function.

22 
IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells sustain mitochondrial fitness 
23 
Impaired mitochondrial fitness has been shown to reinforce T cell exhaustion8,15. We 
24 
next examined the mitochondrial mass and membrane potential of CAR-T cells by 
25

staining them with MitoTracker Green (MG) and MitoTracker Deep Red (MDR), 
1 
respectively. Consistent with prior reports of TILs8, we found that CAR-T cells with 
2 
dysfunctional mitochondria (defined as low ratio of MDR to MG, MDR/MGlo) were 
3 
notably enriched in tumor compared to that in spleen (Fig. 2a, b). The MDR/MGlo 
4 
subpopulation was substantially less active in degranulation and expressed much 
5 
higher levels of PD-1 and TIM-3 as compared to the MDR/MGhi population, showing 
6 
an exhaustion phenotype (Extended Data Fig. 4a). Further, analysis of mitochondrial 
7 
ultrastructure by electron microscopy (EM) indicated that CAR-T cells in tumor 
8 
possessed mitochondria with enlarged balloon-like shape and loosely structured 
9 
cristae, whereas splenic CAR-T cells exhibited tubular mitochondria with tight cristae 
10 
(Fig. 2c). In addition, mitochondria in tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells showed reduced 
11 
crista numbers as well as decreased length of cristae per mitochondrion as compared 
12 
to that in the spleen (Fig. 2d, e). These data reveal the accumulation of dysfunctional 
13 
mitochondria in exhausted CAR-T cells in tumor.  
14 
 
15 
 IL-10 expression sustained the mitochondria fitness in tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells 
16 
with substantially reduced frequency of dysfunctional mitochondria in IL-10 HER2 
17 
CAR-T cells (5.5%) as compared to HER2 CAR-T cells alone (22.9%) or HER2 
18 
CAR-T cells combined with exogenous IL-10 (23.5%) (Fig. 2f, g). IL-10 expression 
19 
also increased the ratio of MDR to MG in IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 2h). EM 
20 
imaging analysis of mitochondrial ultrastructure provided additional evidence of 
21 
enriched mitochondria with tubular shape, well-structured cristae, increased cristae 
22 
numbers, and enlarged length of cristae per mitochondrion in tumor-infiltrating IL-10 
23 
HER2 CAR-T cells as compared to conventional HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 2i-l; 
24 
Extended Data Fig. 4b). HER2 CAR-T cells with increased CAR expression density 
25

exhibited higher frequency of dysfunctional mitochondria, which was considerably 
1 
reduced in IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 2m; Extended Data Fig. 4c). Similarly, IL-
2 
10 CD19 hCAR-T exhibited decreased frequency of cells with dysfunctional 
3 
mitochondria and increased ratio of MDR to MG in tumor as compared to CD19 
4 
hCAR-T cells (Fig. 2n, o). These results together suggest that antigen stimulation may 
5 
drive mitochondrial dysfunction in CAR-T cells while IL-10 expression could sustain 
6 
mitochondrial fitness of CAR-T cells, especially those experiencing strong antigen 
7 
stimulation.

9 
IL-10 expression promotes OXPHOS of CAR-T cells 
10 
We next assessed whether IL-10 expression could reprogram CAR-T cell metabolism. 
11 
To mimic the persistent tumor-antigen stimulation in the TME, we cocultured MC38-
12 
HER2 cells and HER2 CAR-T cells with or without IL-10 expression. Upon antigen 
13 
stimulation, IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cell exhibited markedly elevated basal and maximal 
14 
oxygen consumption rates (OCR) as compared to HER2 CAR-T cell alone (Fig. 3a, 
15 
b), whereas extracellular acidification rate (ECAR) remained almost unchanged 
16 
(Extended Data Fig. 4d, e). Therefore, IL-10 expression increased the ratios of OCR 
17 
to ECAR and reprogramed CAR-T cell metabolism (Fig. 3c). Addition of exogenous 
18 
IL-10 also increased the basal OCR of HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 3b). Notably, in the 
19 
absence of antigen stimulation, neither exogenous nor secreted IL-10 had any effects 
20 
on the OCR of CAR-T cells (Extended Data Fig. 4f). In addition, metabolomic 
21 
analysis confirmed that intratumoral IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells exhibited a distinct 
22 
metabolomic profile as compared to the conventional HER2 CAR-T cells with 
23 
markedly increased the levels of pyruvate production intermediate, 
24 
phosphoenolpyruvate, and tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle intermediate, succinate 
25

(Extended Data Fig. 4g-j; Supplementary Table 1). These results indicate that IL-10 
1 
expression reprograms CAR-T cell metabolism toward OXPHOS in an antigen-
2 
dependent manner.  
3 
 
4 
We recently showed that IL-10–Fc promotes OXPHOS in terminally exhausted CD8+ 
5 
TILs through MPC12. Consistently, by blocking pyruvate transportation using a MPC 
6 
inhibitor UK5099 but not by inhibiting fatty acid oxidation (FAO) with etomoxir 
7 
(ETO), we could completely abrogate the increase of OXPHOS of IL-10 HER2 CAR-
8 
T cells (Fig. 3d). In the coculture with MC38-HER2 cells, IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells 
9 
exhibited greatly enhanced proliferation, polyfunctionality, and killing efficiency of 
10 
target cells as compared to HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 3e-g), which were consistent 
11 
with the in vivo results as shown above. Inhibition of MPC-dependent OXPHOS 
12 
substantially diminished the enhancement of proliferation and polyfunctionality of IL-
13 
10 HER2 CAR-T cells (Extended Data Fig. 4k, l), suggesting the elevated 
14 
mitochondrial OXPHOS was necessary for reinvigoration of exhausted CAR-T cells. 
15 
Furthermore, we used MPC1-deficient T cells (MPC1-KO) to generate IL-10 HER2 
16 
CAR-T cells, which exhibited increased frequency of accumulated dysfunctional 
17 
mitochondria and reduced ratio of OCR to ECAR as compared to IL-10 HER2 CAR-
18 
T cells generated from wild-type (WT) T cells (Fig. 3h-j). Moreover, MPC1-KO IL-
19 
10 HER2 CAR-T cells failed to induce improved proliferation, polyfunctionality, or 
20 
tumor cell-killing capacity as compared to WT IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cell (Fig. 3k-m). 
21 
Collectively, we show that IL-10 expression promoted OXPHOS metabolism of 
22 
CAR-T cells in a MPC dependent manner, which was essential for rejuvenating 
23 
exhausted CAR-T cells with enhanced proliferative capacity and effector function.

IL-10 expression alters transcription of CAR-T cells 
1 
To investigate the impact of IL-10 expression on CAR-T cell fate in the TME at the 
2 
transcriptional level, we performed a single-cell RNA-sequencing (scRNA-seq) 
3 
analysis of freshly sorted HER2 CAR-T or IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells from MC38-
4 
HER2 tumors (Extended Data Fig. 5a). Upon unsupervised clustering, four distinct 
5 
clusters were identified based on their gene expression profiles (Fig. 4a; Extended 
6 
Data Fig. 5b). Notably, cluster 1 was dominantly enriched in IL-10 HER2 CAR-T 
7 
cells, whereas HER2 CAR-T cells were mostly found in cluster 0 (Fig. 4b, c). 
8 
Projection of these scRNA-seq data into a reference atlas of TILs using ProjecTILs16 
9 
revealed that the majority of CAR-T cells were mapped to the CD8+ terminally-
10 
exhausted T cell subtype (Extended Data Fig. 5c). However, compared to cluster 0, 
11 
cluster 1 displayed higher expression of cytotoxic and effector molecules including 
12 
Gzmb, Gzmc, Prf1, Gzmf, and Ifng, as well as transcription factors Jun, Junb and Fos 
13 
(Fig. 4d, e; Extended Data Fig. 5d). Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) between 
14 
these two clusters indicated that cluster 1 was strongly associated with mitochondrial 
15 
OXPHOS (Fig. 4f; Extended Data Fig. 5d). Consistently with our previous 
16 
observation that IL-10–Fc enhanced mitochondrial respiration via pyruvate, we found 
17 
that cluster 1 showed an enriched pyruvate metabolism pathway as compared to 
18 
cluster 0 (Fig. 4f). In addition, Cluster 2, a cluster associated with high expression 
19 
levels of cell proliferation and cycling genes (Fig. 4d; Extended Data Fig. 5d), was 
20 
also enriched in IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells (Fig. 4a-c), suggesting higher proliferative 
21 
capacity of IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells. These results align with our previous 
22 
observations and suggest that IL-10 expression rejuvenated exhausted CAR-T cells 
23 
sustaining a cell state with enhanced cytotoxicity, effector function, and proliferative 
24 
capacity, which was engaged with metabolic reprogramming towards mitochondrial 
25

OXPHOS. 
1 
 
2 
IL-10 CAR-T cells eradicate established solid tumors 
3 
The discovery that IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells resisted dysfunction motivated us to 
4 
assess their efficacy against solid tumors. In a therapeutic setting of pre-established 
5 
MC38-HER2 tumors in mice with lymphodepletion preconditioning, adoptive transfer 
6 
of IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells (3 × 106) induced complete tumor regression and durable 
7 
cures in 90% of treated mice (Fig. 5a-c; Extended Data Fig. 6a). By contrast, adoptive 
8 
transfer of HER2 CAR-T cells alone or combined with single or multiple i.v. 
9 
administration of native IL-10 transiently controlled tumor growth but failed to induce 
10 
any durable tumor regression. In fact, treatment with IL-10 HER2 CAR-T was 
11 
equivalent to the combination of HER2 CAR-T cells and IL-10−Fc (peritumoral), and 
12 
outperformed the combination of HER2 CAR-T cells and IL-10−Fc (i.v.) in 
13 
therapeutic efficacy (Extended Data Fig. 6b-e). No body weight loss or overt 
14 
elevation of serum cytokines were observed upon IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cell treatment, 
15 
suggesting good safety profile of the therapy (Extended Data Fig. 6f, g). We next 
16 
extended this strategy to the CAR specific to melanoma-associated antigen tyrosinase-
17 
related protein-1 (TRP-1) and prepared IL-10 TRP-1 CAR-T cells (Extended Data 
18 
Fig. 7a-c). IL-10 TRP-1 CAR-T cells exhibited enhanced killing efficiency of TRP-1-
19 
expressing B16F10 melanoma cells in vitro compared to conventional TRP-1 CAR-T 
20 
cells (Extended Data Fig. 7d). To treat the poorly immunogenic and highly aggressive 
21 
mouse B16F10 melanoma tumor in mice, we transferred IL-10 TRP-1 CAR-T cells 
22 
(3 × 106) through i.v. administration, which led to 60% of tumor clearance and 
23 
curative responses in the subcutaneous tumor model without body weight loss (Fig. 
24 
5a, d, e; Extended Data Fig. 7e, f). Furthermore, when applied to treat an orthotopic 
25

B16F10 melanoma model, the IL-10 TRP-1 CAR-T cells induced complete responses 
1 
in about 40% of treated mice (Extended Data Fig. 7g-j). 
2 
 
3 
We next evaluated whether IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells could control metastatic 
4 
cancer. We prepared epidermal growth factor receptor variant III (EGFRvIII)-
5 
targeting CAR-T cells with IL-10 expression (Extended Data Fig. 8a-c). Consistent 
6 
with the finding in HER2 CAR-T cells with a 4-1BB endodomain, IL-10 expression 
7 
also enhanced basal OCR of CD28-incorporated IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR T-cells when 
8 
cocultured with target cells (Extended Data Fig. 8d). IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells 
9 
exhibited enhanced in vitro killing efficiency of 4T1 tumor cells that were transduced 
10 
to express EGFRvIII and luciferase (4T1-EGFRvIII-Luc) as compared to EGFRvIII 
11 
CAR-T cells (Extended Data Fig. 8e). To establish a metastasis-like tumor model, 
12 
BALB/c mice received i.v. injection of 4T1-EGFRvIII-Luc cells. The mice that 
13 
developed metastatic nodules in the lungs were sublethally lymphodepleted and then 
14 
received the adoptive transfer of EGFRvIII CAR-T cells alone or supported by IL-10 
15 
infusion or IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells (Fig. 5a). The tumor growth was monitored 
16 
by bioluminescence imaging (Fig. 5f). Notably, IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells 
17 
exhibited superior anti-metastases activity and led to durable cures in 100% of treated 
18 
mice with no body weight loss, whereas EGFRvIII CAR-T cells with or without 
19 
exogenous IL-10 infusion showed only modest tumor burden control (Fig. 5f-h; 
20 
Extended Data Fig. 8f). The long-lasting suppression of metastases was likely due to 
21 
the persistence of IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells in the blood (Extended Data Fig. 8g). 
22 
 
23 
We further extended this therapeutic strategy to hCAR-T cells. In immune-deficient 
24 
mice bearing established subcutaneous Raji or PANC1-CD19 tumors, transfer of 
25

conventional CD19 hCAR-T cells (1 × 106) resulted in minimum or transient tumor 
1 
growth inhibition, respectively, but ultimately failed to control tumor progression 
2 
(Fig. 5i-l; Extended Data Fig. 9a, b). By contrast, all mice treated with IL-10-
3 
expressing CD19 hCAR-T cells exhibited complete tumor regression in both models 
4 
with no recurrence (Fig. 5i-l; Extended Data Fig. 9a, b) suggesting that IL-10-
5 
expressing human CAR-T cells had superior anti-tumor capacity against solid tumors 
6 
in xenograft models. In addition, we established an orthotopic human tumor model of 
7 
pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) with PANC1-CD19-Luc tumor cells 
8 
inoculated in the pancreas of mice (Extended Data Fig. 9c-e). IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T 
9 
cells (1 × 105) eliminated the orthotopic PDAC tumors much more effectively than the 
10 
conventional CD19 hCAR-T cells (1 × 105), leading to complete responses in 100% of 
11 
treated mice (Extended Data Fig. 9c-e). Notably, IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T cells 
12 
demonstrated markedly higher expansion post hCAR-T cell infusion until at least day 
13 
50 (Extended Data Fig. 9f, g). Collectively, these results suggest that IL-10-
14 
expressing CAR-T cells represent a potent immunotherapy against multiple solid 
15 
tumors.

IL-10 expression induces stem cell-like memory  
18 
To investigate whether IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells developed anti-tumor immune 
19 
memory, we rechallenged the surviving mice 3 months post adoptive CAR-T cell 
20 
transfer (Extended Data Fig. 10a). 100 % of long-term survivors treated with IL-10 
21 
HER2 CAR-T, IL-10 TRP-1 CAR-T, or IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells rejected the 
22 
second challenge of the original tumor cells (Fig. 6a-c). Similarly, in the human 
23 
PDAC tumor model, upon orthotopic tumor cell rechallenge, all the survivors from 
24 
the treatment group of IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T cells rapidly rejected the second 
25

challenge (Extended Data Fig. 9f, h, and i). The robust immune memory response 
1 
motivated us to examine the memory phenotypes of IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells in 
2 
the lymphoid tissues and the circulation (Fig. 6d).  Flow cytometry analysis of CAR-T 
3 
cells (initially transferred CAR-T cells were all CD44hi) 12 days post treatment 
4 
showed that IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells enriched a population with Tscm phenotype 
5 
(defined as CD62LhiCD44lo and stem cell antigen-1 (Sca-1)+CD122+)17–20 in spleen 
6 
and peripheral blood (Fig. 6e, f; Extended Data Fig. 10b, c). IL-10 HER2 CAR-T 
7 
cells in the spleen showed ~3.2-fold higher frequency of CD62LhiCD44lo T cells 
8 
compared to HER2 CAR-T cells alone, among which the majority (~71.2%) were 
9 
Sca-1+CD122+ Tscm (Fig. 6e, f). In addition, IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells exhibited 
10 
substantially increased expression level of Sca-1 compared to HER2 CAR-T cells 
11 
alone or HER2 CAR-T cells plus exogenous IL-10 among all CD62LhiCD44lo CAR-T 
12 
cells (Fig. 6g). Similarly, IL-10 EGFRvIII CAR-T cells enriched a higher frequency 
13 
of Tscm cell in spleen and bone marrow compared to EGFRvIII CAR-T cells 
14 
(Extended Data Fig. 10e-i). This finding was further confirmed by the observation 
15 
that IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells were composed of  ~3.7- and 2.6-fold higher 
16 
proportion of IL-7Rα+KLRG1− long-lived memory precursor T cells21 than HER2 
17 
CAR-T cells in spleen and blood, respectively (Fig. 6h; Extended Data Fig. 10d). 
18 
Gene set enrichment analysis confirmed that splenic IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells 
19 
exhibited a clear transcriptomic state of “Stemness/Memory” (identified by murine 
20 
Sell, Tcf7, Lef1, Il7r, Ccr7) and highly enriched Tscm-associated genes including Id2, 
21 
Ly6a, Cxcr3, Eomes and Bcl2 (Fig. 6i, j; Extended Data Fig. 10j)22. Additionally, we 
22 
observed that compared to CD19 hCAR-T cells, treatment of IL-10 CD19 hCAR-T 
23 
cells appeared enriched with Tscm, inducing 22.4- and 31.9-fold higher counts of 
24 
CD8+ CAR-T cells with Tscm phenotype (defined by 
25

CD45RA+CD27+CD62L+CD95+CCR7+) in spleen and bone marrow, respectively 
1 
(Extended Data Fig.10k-m). Furthermore, scRNA-seq analysis confirmed that IL10 
2 
CD19 hCAR-T in spleen had a markedly higher fraction of cells with the gene 
3 
signature of “Stemness/Memory” (identified by human TCF7, CCR7, LEF1, SELL) 
4 
and expressed higher levels of Tscm associated genes including IL7R and CXCR3, 
5 
compared to the conventional CD19 hCAR-T cells (Fig. 6k-n; Extended Data Fig. 
6 
10n). Altogether, these results give clear evidence that IL-10 signaling may induce the 
7 
formation of mouse and human Tscm CAR-T cells contributing to long-term 
8 
antitumor immunity.

Discussion 
11 
Metabolic modulation is a promising strategy to counter T cell dysfunction for 
12 
improved CAR-T immunotherapy. Among different strategies, increasing 
13 
mitochondrial fitness and OXPHOS seems an effective intervention to improve 
14 
persistence and function of CAR-T cells and other adoptive T cell therapies. In the 
15 
design of second generation CAR-T, 4-1BB co-stimulation has been reported to 
16 
enhance mitochondrial biogenesis, OXPHOS, and ameliorate T cell exhaustion, and 
17 
therefore induces better therapeutic outcomes as compared to the CD28 co-
18 
stimulatory domain23,24. Moreover, preconditioning T cells with IL-1511 or co-
19 
expression of IL-1525, or Regnase-1 deletion26 could improve antitumor immunity of 
20 
T cells through enhanced mitochondrial fitness and OXPHOS.  Here we developed 
21 
metabolically armored IL-10-secreting CAR-T cells, which exhibited enhanced 
22 
proliferation and effector function by sustaining mitochondrial fitness and promoting 
23 
OXPHOS, leading to complete remission in multiple syngeneic and xenograft solid 
24 
tumor models. Moreover, IL-10 secretion in CAR-T cells induced Tscm responses in 
25

the peripheral blood and spleen, which bestowed durable protection in treated mice 
1 
against tumor rechallenge.  
2 
 
3 
Although enhancement of pyruvate-dependent OXPHOS was critical for countering 
4 
CAR-T cell dysfunction, OXPHOS activities that rely on other substrates, in 
5 
particular, fatty acids, can also preserve proliferation and function of tumor-
6 
infiltrating T cells27,28. Therefore, how specific metabolic pathways determine T cell 
7 
differentiation remains unclear. Recently, it has been reported that CAR-T cells 
8 
engineered to express supporting cytokines29–31, checkpoint blockade antibody32, or c-
9 
Jun33, or targeting a CAR to the TRAC locus34 resist exhaustion or dysfunction. Yet, 
10 
whether and how these strategies alter T cell metabolism remains to be determined. 
11 
Comparing to most existing armored CAR-T cells, IL-10 expression in CAR-T cells 
12 
enhanced proliferation and function of terminally exhausted CD8+ T cells (PD-
13 
1+TIM-3+), a subset of exhausted T cells that lack responses to other stimulatory 
14 
cytokines8 or checkpoint blockades35 but are direct killers of cancer cells owing to its 
15 
toxicity12,35. Therefore, the IL-10 expression complements existing strategies for 
16 
developing dysfunction-resistant CAR-T cell therapy. 
17 
 
18 
We previously show that multiple local administration of the half-life extended IL-10–
19 
Fc fusion protein is highly effective in reinvigorating intratumoral exhausted T cells 
20 
and greatly enhances therapeutic efficacy in combination with ACT therapies 
21 
including CAR-T cell therapy. Efficient delivery and accumulation of the cytokine in 
22 
the TME seems crucial because half-life extension through the fusion protein design, 
23 
local injection, and multiple dosages are all required. In this report, we achieved 
24 
highly efficient intratumoral delivery of IL-10 by engineering CAR-T cells with IL-10 
25

expression. CAR-T cells secreting a high level of IL-10 likely had the capacity to 
1 
maintain an effective local concentration of IL-10 in the TME for invigoration of 
2 
tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells. In contrast, i.v. administered exogenous recombinant 
3 
IL-10 (without Fc fusion) of an equivalent dose showed minimal effects on 
4 
intratumoral T cells in vivo likely due to its short half-life and low level of tumor 
5 
accumulation. Consequently, a single dose of IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells through 
6 
systemic administration led to equivalent therapeutic efficacy to the combination 
7 
therapy of CAR-T cells in tandem with multiple intratumoral injections of IL-10–Fc12. 
8 
Therefore, IL-10-expressing CAR-T cells as a monotherapy is promising for future 
9 
clinical application, particularly, when intratumoral injection is not feasible.  
10 
 
11 
We found that the heterogeneity of CAR expression density leads to sharp differences 
12 
in T cell phenotypes, function, and extent of responses to the secreted IL-10. In 
13 
general, CAR-T cells with higher CAR density expressed higher levels of inhibitory 
14 
receptors (PD-1, TIM-3), displayed reduced effector function, and responded more 
15 
prominently to IL-10. The results suggest that strong antigen stimulation signal likely 
16 
drives T cell exhaustion3,14; the CAR density may therefore be an important parameter 
17 
to control in the development of CAR-T cells with well-balanced tumoricidal activity 
18 
and resistance toward antigen stimulation-induced exhaustion and dysfunction13. IL-
19 
10 was most effective toward the highly exhausted subset with the highest CAR 
20 
density for metabolic reprogramming and reinvigoration, but showed less effects in 
21 
the scenario with low strength of antigen stimulation. These results were in line with 
22 
in vitro observation that exogenous or secreted IL-10 failed to enhance OXPHOS in 
23 
CAR-T cells without antigen stimulation. It has been recently reported that IL-10 
24 
receptor (IL-10R)-STAT3 signaling controls the balance between PD-1hi exhausted 
25

CD8+ T cells and functional PD-1intTCF-1+CD8+ T cells by limiting excessive 
1 
activation of CD8+ T cells36. In a microenvironment of low tumor antigen burden, 
2 
such as peripheral blood or healthy tissues, secreted IL-10 likely has less effects in 
3 
expanding or activating CAR-T cells, thus inducing less risks of systemic toxicities. 
4 
This intrinsic safeguard mechanism could be an additional advantage for the IL-10-
5 
expressing CAR-T cells for potential clinical application.  
6 
 
7 
IL-10R-STAT3 pathway has been reported to be important for T cell memory 
8 
formation. IL-10 deficiency leads to a significantly reduced frequency of KLRG1loIL-
9 
7Rhi memory precursor CD8+ T cells in acute infection37. Furthermore, IL-10 
10 
produced from either Tregs or CD11c+ dendritic cells during the resolution of 
11 
infection promotes the development of KLRG1loCD127hi and CD62LhiKLRG1lo 
12 
central memory CD8+ T cells38. Here, we observed the constitutive expression of IL-
13 
10 by CAR-T cells induced loss of CD44 expression and majority of IL-10-secreting 
14 
CAR-T cells acquired a stem cell-like memory phenotype (CD44loCD62LhiSca-
15 
1+CD122+) during tumor clearance. Stem cell-like memory T cells possess superior 
16 
survival capacity, robust proliferative potential post antigen re-exposure, and the 
17 
ability to give rise to all memory and effector T cell subsets18–20. Promoting stemness 
18 
is essential for durable protection against relapse with CAR-T cell therapy. 
19 
Additionally, this finding suggests that controlling the strength and persistence of IL-
20 
10 signaling may fine tune the CAR-T cell differentiation states. Metabolically, 
21 
memory T cells typically have higher mitochondrial mass and respiration spare 
22 
capacity39,40, which is in accordance with our observation that IL-10-expressing CAR-
23 
T cells have increased mitochondrial activity as compared to the conventional CAR-T 
24 
cells.

files were generated and demultiplexed by cellranger mkfastq from 10x Genomics 
2 
(version 3.0.2) and primary data analysis was performed with Cell Ranger (version 
3 
2.2.0) using a custom reference package based on reference genome (mm10 or 
4 
GRCh38) and GENCODE gene models. For IL-10 HER2 CAR-T cells from spleen, 
5

6

Cells were lysis and mRNA capturing beads were retrieved. cDNAs obtained via

reverse transcription, amplified, and purification. For 5′Gene Expression library this 
12

13

14

15 
generated and demultiplexed by CeleScope rna from Singleron (version 3.0.1) and 
16

17 
reference package based on reference genome (Mus_musculus_ensembl_92).

In order to demultiplex samples of HER2 CAR-T cells from tumors based on LMO 
20

21 
default settings. Quality control was applied to remove outlier cells with less than 500 
22 
or over 50,000 UMI counts, less than 500 or over 6000 genes, as well as abnormal 
23 
content of ribosomal or mitochondrial genes (over 40% and 7.5% respectively). To 
24 
remove potential contaminants or cell doublets, we applied the scGate package 
25

v1.0.046 using a default T cell filter. Dimensionality reduction was performed by 
1 
calculating 10 principal components (PC) from 500 variable genes, further reducing 
2 
dimensionality by Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) for 2D 
3 
visualization. Unsupervised clustering was performed on the PC reduction using the 
4

5

6

7

9

10

11 
signature scores for these gene sets were calculated using the UCell package22. Gene 
12 
set enrichment analysis between clusters was performed on average gene expression 
13

14
