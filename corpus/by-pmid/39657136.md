# Lisocabtagene maraleucel for relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma: a cell therapy consortium real-world analysis.

## Abstract

Lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel) is an autologous CD19-directed chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy approved for the treatment of relapsed/refractory large B-cell lymphoma. We present a multicenter retrospective study evaluating safety, efficacy, and resource use of liso-cel in the standard-of-care setting. Patients received commercial liso-cel at 7 US medical centers, and patient selection, toxicity management, and disease assessment followed institutional practices. Among 101 patients who received infusion, the median age was 71 years (35% aged ≥75 years), 68% had a Charlson comorbidity index score of ≥3, and 10% had secondary central nervous system involvement. Median number of prior therapies was 3; and because of comorbidities, 33% would have been ineligible for the TRANSCEND study. Bridging therapy was used in 60% (43% received polatuzumab-based treatment). Any-grade cytokine-release syndrome occurred in 49% (3% grade ≥3) with any-grade immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome occurring in 26% (10% grade ≥3). The overall response rate (ORR) to bridging therapy was 45%, with 18% achieving a complete response (CR). Following liso-cel infusion, the day 90 ORR was 66% (60% CR); and with a median follow-up of 15.5 months, 12-month progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) were 55% and 68%, respectively. A normal lactate dehydrogenase level before lymphodepletion was associated with improved PFS and OS. These analyses confirm similar efficacy and safety of commercial liso-cel compared with pivotal trial results. Notably, these outcomes were achieved in patients predominantly of advanced age and with significant comorbidities. Results also likely reflect advancements in patient selection, toxicity management, and the use of novel bridging strategies.

Introduction

Large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL) represents a heterogenous group of aggressive B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphomas for which standard anthracycline- and rituximab-based induction therapy yields long-term remissions in 50% to 70% of patients. Despite these encouraging results, patients suffering from relapsed or refractory (R/R) LBCL demonstrate poor outcomes. Anti-CD19 chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy has revolutionized treatment of R/R LBCL, offering the promise of long-term disease-free survival and likely cure in patients who have either exhausted, or are not candidates for, conventional approaches.

Lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel) is an autologous CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapy approved for the treatment of R/R LBCL based on the TRANSCEND, TRANSFORM, and PILOT studies. Although these prospective trials demonstrated impressive efficacy and safety, real-world evidence is essential to confirm that such results reflect outcomes in patients treated in the standard-of-care setting. Real-world experience has confirmed the safety and efficacy of other commercially available CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapies including axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel) and tisagenlecleucel (tisa-cel), but data are limited in relation to commercial liso-cel.

To better understand the application of commercial liso-cel therapy for R/R LBCL, we conducted a retrospective analysis using the Cell Therapy Consortium (CTC) registry to delineate the safety and efficacy of liso-cel, including an evaluation of resource use metrics, and characteristics associated with response and toxicity.

Methods

Study design and patients

This retrospective, multicenter study included data from the CTC registry, a working group of 8 US academic centers established to pool data and resources for conducting observational research studies. In this analysis, 7 CTC centers (supplemental Table 1) contributed data on adults aged ≥18 years with R/R LBCL who underwent commercial liso-cel therapy between 5 February 2021 (date of US Food and Drug Administration approval) and 30 June 2023. The date of data cutoff was 4 March 2024. All participating centers were site-approved to administer all 3 US Food and Drug Administration–approved CAR T-cell products for R/R LBCL. Clinical characteristics, laboratory data, pathology, CAR T-cell treatment details, toxicities, and responses from each institution were recorded in a centralized research electronic data capture database. The study was approved by the individual institutional review boards.

Patient selection, product choice, bridging therapy, toxicity management, response assessment, and site of administration (inpatient or outpatient) followed institutional practices. Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and immune effector cell–associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) were graded per the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy consensus criteria. Bridging therapy was defined as antilymphoma therapy provided after apheresis and before the initiation of lymphodepleting chemotherapy. Autologous or allogeneic stem cell transplant (SCT) and the associated conditioning regimen was not categorized as a line of therapy. Patients receiving a nonconforming liso-cel product, which did not meet commercial release specifications, were treated in the context of an expanded access study (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT04400591). After liso-cel infusion, response assessment was performed per Lugano criteria by the treating clinician.

Statistical analyses

Descriptive statistics and frequency tables were used to summarize baseline patient and treatment characteristics and variables regarding toxicity, efficacy, and resources use. Efficacy outcomes were reported among response-evaluable patients; the entire population was analyzed for toxicity and resource use. Kaplan-Meier methods and log-rank tests were used to estimate progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS) for all patients, and duration of response (DOR) among those who achieved a complete response (CR) or partial response (PR) was calculated in relation to specified disease evaluation time points (ie, 30 and 90 days). PFS was defined as the time from liso-cel infusion to the earliest disease progression, death, or initiation of subsequent antilymphoma therapy. OS was defined as time from liso-cel infusion to death from any cause. Data were censored at date of last follow-up or 4 March 2024, whichever came first. Median follow-up was calculated using the reverse Kaplan-Meier method from the date of infusion to last follow-up; deaths were censored. The cumulative incidence function and the Gray tests were used to estimate nonrelapse mortality (NRM) defined as the time from liso-cel infusion to death from any cause without evidence of lymphoma relapse or progression; relapse/progression and death due to lymphoma were considered competing events.

Univariate and multivariable logistic regression modelling was performed for the outcome of response (ie, PR/CR) at the 90-day evaluation, and Cox regressions were performed for PFS and OS to identify factors associated with these outcomes. Variables considered in the univariable analysis and multivariable analysis (MVA) are shown in supplemental Figure 1. No significant violation of the proportional hazards assumption for the Cox models was identified for OS and PFS based on the smoothing plots for Schoenfeld residuals. MVA was conducted to determine independent predictors of the outcome variables using a parsimonious model selection based on Akaike Information Criterion. All statistical analyses were performed using R version 4.2.0, and a P < .05 was considered significant.

Results

Patient and treatment characteristics

The study included 101 patients who underwent liso-cel infusion. Baseline patient characteristics are listed in Table 1. Median age at apheresis was 71 years (range, 30-85) with 35 (35%) recipients aged ≥75 years, 61 (60%) were male, 14 (16%) had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status score of ≥2 at apheresis, and 14 (14%) were minority populations. At infusion, 10 (10%) patients had active secondary central nervous system (CNS) involvement. By histology, 82 (81%) patients had diffuse LBCL, 10 (10%) transformed indolent lymphoma, 7 (7%) high-grade BCL, and 2 (2%) primary mediastinal BCL. Baseline comorbidities included diabetes in 18 (18%) patients, stage IV chronic kidney disease in 5 (5%), cerebrovascular disease in 5 (5%), impaired cardiac ejection fraction in 2 (2%), severe pulmonary dysfunction in 2 (2%), impaired hepatic function in 2 (2%), and 2 (2%) patients had active infection at the time of infusion. A high comorbidity burden (score of ≥3), per the Charlson comorbidity index, was seen in 68 (68%) recipients. Because of comorbidities, 30 (33%) recipients would have been ineligible for the TRANSCEND clinical trial. The median number of prior therapies was 3 (range, 1-8) and 16 (16%) had a prior autologous SCT. Among 15 (15%) patients receiving second-line liso-cel, 10 experienced primary refractory or early relapsing (≤12 months) disease, whereas 5 experienced late relapse (>12 months) yet were deemed ineligible for SCT. Seven (7%) patients received liso-cel on an expanded access trial because of nonconforming product.

Bridging therapy was used in 61 (60%) patients including 26 (43%) who received polatuzumab-based treatment, 13 (21%) other chemoimmunotherapy, and 12 (20%) radiation therapy. Supplemental Table 2 lists bridging therapies used in this cohort. Among 93 (92%) patients with available response data before liso-cel, 16 (17%) were infused in a CR, whereas 77 (83%) had active disease. An elevated lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) level at the time of lymphodepletion was seen in 41 (44%) recipients. The median time from apheresis to liso-cel infusion was 39 days (interquartile range [IQR], 34-43 days). For lymphodepletion, 75 (74%) received fludarabine/cyclophosphamide per the product label, whereas 26 (26%) received bendamustine (90 mg/m2 daily for 2 days).

Efficacy

Response assessment was performed in all 101 patients at day 30 and/or day 90 according to treating center practice, or in those determined to have clinical progression (supplemental Table 3). The best ORR among 101 liso-cel recipients was 81% (CR, 66%). At 90 days after infusion, 91 of 101 patients (90%) were evaluable for response and the 90-day ORR was 66% (CR, 60%). Ten patients were not evaluable at day 90 because of missed disease assessment. On MVA, a prelymphodepletion LDH equal to, or less than, the institutional upper limit of normal (ULN) was associated with higher odds of 90-day response (ie, PR/CR; odds ratio, 5.26; 95% confidence interval [CI], 2.02-14.7; P < .001) compared with LDH of >ULN (Table 2). Among all 101 patients responding at the 30-day or 90-day evaluation, the median DOR was not reached (NR) (95% CI, 17.6 to NR), with a 12-month DOR of 61% (95% CI, 50-74; Figure 1A). Among patients responding at the 90-day evaluation, the median DOR was NR (95% CI, NR to NR), with a 12-month DOR of 73% (95% CI, 61-87).

Among 101 liso-cel recipients, the median follow-up was 15.5 months (95% CI, 12.3-18.1), and the median PFS was 18.5 months (95% CI, 6.5 to NR), with a 12-month PFS of 55% (95% CI, 46-66; Figure 1B). On MVA, a prelymphodepletion LDH of ≤ULN was associated with improved PFS (hazard ratio [HR], 0.25; 95% CI, 0.12-0.55; P < .001) compared with LDH of >ULN, whereas ineligibility for the TRANSCEND clinical trial was associated with worsened PFS (HR, 2.64; 95% CI, 1.29-5.40; P = .008; Table 2).

Median OS was NR (95% CI, NR to NR), with a 12-month OS of 68% (95% CI, 59-78; Figure 1C). On MVA, experiencing stable disease or progressive disease to bridging therapy compared with no bridging therapy (HR, 4.32; 95% CI, 1.81-10.30; P < .001), and receiving fludarabine/cyclophosphamide compared with bendamustine lymphodepletion (HR, 3.76; 95% CI, 1.11-12.70; P = .033) were associated with an increased risk of mortality. Conversely, a prelymphodepletion LDH of ≤ULN was associated with improved OS (HR, 0.30; 95% CI, 0.13-0.68; P = .004) compared with LDH of >ULN (Table 2).

Among the 61 liso-cel recipients who received bridging therapy after apheresis, 51 (84%) were evaluable for response. Bridging therapy was associated with an ORR of 45% (CR, 18%). By bridging therapy regimen, polatuzumab-based bridging was associated with the highest response rates (ORR, 52%; CR, 35%), compared with chemotherapy-based (ORR, 30%; CR, 0%) or radiation therapy–based (ORR, 50%; CR, 0%) treatments.

When examining the association of response (ie, PR/CR) to bridging therapy on PFS in liso-cel recipients who received bridging therapy we noted a median PFS of 12.7 months (95% CI, 10.5 to NR) compared with 3.7 months for those with stable disease or progressive disease (95% CI, 2.3 to NR; P = .066; supplemental Figure 2B). Similar PFS outcomes were seen in patients who received bridging therapy compared with those that did not (supplemental Figure 2A).

We evaluated the association of both the lymphodepleting chemotherapy regimen (bendamustine vs fludarabine/cyclophosphamide) and age (≥75 vs <75 years) on efficacy outcomes. The use of bendamustine lymphodepletion was associated with similar 90-day ORRs (67% vs 66%; P > .99), 90-day CR rates (58% vs 61%; P = .81), median 90-day DOR (NR vs NR; P = .475), median PFS (NR vs 12.4 months; P = .305), and median OS (NR vs NR; P = .189) when compared with fludarabine/cyclophosphamide. By age (≥75 vs <75 years), we noted 90-day ORRs of 75% vs 61% (P = .25), 90-day CR rates of 66% vs 58% (P = .51), median 90-day DOR NR vs NR (P = .775), median PFS NR vs 13.4 months (P = .492), and median OS NR vs NR (P = .468) in recipients aged ≥75 years compared with those aged <75 years.

Safety

The incidence of any-grade CRS was 49%, with grade ≥3 CRS occurring in 3 (3%) liso-cel recipients (Table 3). One death within 30 days of infusion was attributed to grade 5 CRS with concurrent grade 5 neurotoxicity. The median onset of CRS was 4 days (IQR, 3-5) after infusion. Among patients experiencing CRS, median CRS duration was 4 days (IQR, 2-5).

The incidence of any-grade ICANS was 26%, with grade ≥3 ICANS in 10% of liso-cel recipients. Median onset of ICANS was 6 days (IQR, 3-9) after liso-cel infusion. Two (2%) ICANS-related deaths occurred, both within 30 days of infusion, including the aforementioned case with concurrent grade 5 CRS. The median duration of ICANS was 4 days (IQR, 1-13).

We noted a significantly lower incidence of both any-grade CRS (31% vs 55%; P = .042) and any-grade ICANS (1% vs 33%; P = .003) with bendamustine than with fludarabine/cyclophosphamide lymphodepleting chemotherapy. The incidence of grade ≥3 CRS was 0% vs 4% (P = .570) and grade ≥3 ICANS was 0% vs 13% (P = .060) between these cohorts. Additionally, age (≥75 vs <75 years) was not a significant factor associated with safety outcomes, wherein we noted a similar incidence of any-grade CRS (51% vs 47%; P = .68), grade ≥3 CRS (6% vs 2%; P = .27), any-grade ICANS (29% vs 24%; P = .64), and grade ≥3 ICANS (11% vs 9%; P = .73) in recipients aged ≥75 years compared with those aged <75 years.

Within the first 30 days after liso-cel infusion, infectious complications of any grade were common, as seen in 20% of the population. Supplemental Table 4 delineates the nature of early infectious complications in our study.

Toxicity management

Tocilizumab was administered in 31 (31%) liso-cel recipients, with a median of 1 dose (IQR, 1-2). Corticosteroids were used for toxicity management in 31 (31%) patients; 24 (77%) patients received standard-dose corticosteroids (eg, dexamethasone 10 mg, 1-4 times per day), whereas 7 (23%) received high-dose corticosteroids (eg, 1000 mg methylprednisolone per day or equivalent). Two patients received siltuximab and 9 received anakinra for toxicity management. Among 9 anakinra recipients, 7 experienced grade ≥3 ICANS.

Resource use

Liso-cel was infused as an outpatient in 15 (15%) recipients (Table 4). Among outpatient recipients, 5 patients required hospitalization within 30 days after infusion, and the median length of stay (LOS) was 5 days (range, 3-11). Reasons for hospitalization included CRS management in 4 (80%) and management of concurrent CRS and ICANS in 1 (20%). Among recipients of an inpatient liso-cel infusion, the median LOS after infusion was 12 days (IQR, 8-16). Intensive care unit (ICU) transfer occurred in 10 (10%) liso-cel recipients and the median ICU LOS was 5 days (IQR, 4-21). Common reasons for ICU transfer included neurotoxicity (6 patients), hypoxia (2 patients), and hypotension (1 patient). Four patients required mechanical ventilation or noninvasive positive pressure ventilation.

NRM and causes of death

The primary cause of death was progressive lymphoma in 22 (22%) recipients. After excluding 3 cases with an unknown cause of death, the cumulative incidence of NRM at 1 year after liso-cel infusion was 7% (95% CI, 3-13; Figure 1D). Seven (7%) deaths were unrelated to lymphoma progression, including 3 (3%) within 30 days of infusion related to infection, CRS, or neurotoxicity. Other causes of non-lymphoma–related death included second malignancies and unknown causes (supplemental Table 5). No cases of secondary T-cell lymphoma were observed.

Outcomes in secondary CNS lymphoma

Ten patients underwent liso-cel infusion with secondary CNS lymphoma. Among this subset, 7 patients had parenchymal brain involvement, 1 had leptomeningeal involvement, and 2 had both. Eight (80%) patients received bridging therapy. Supplemental Table 6 delineates bridging therapies used in this subset. Among 8 evaluable patients, 7 entered CAR T-cell therapy with active disease, including 2 patients in a PR; 1 patient received liso-cel in a CR. Liso-cel treatment was overall well tolerated with any-grade CRS occurring in 7 (70%) patients, with no grade ≥3 events. Similarly, any-grade ICANS was noted in 4 (40%) patients, with grade ≥3 ICANS in 3 (30%). In patients with secondary CNS involvement, response assessment was performed at day 30 and/or day 90 after infusion. Liso-cel was associated with a best overall response of 90% and a best CR rate of 70% (supplemental Figure 3). The median PFS for this cohort was 21.3 months (95% CI, 5.3 to NR), with a 12-month PFS of 60% (95% CI, 36-100). In this cohort, 3 patients died because of lymphoma progression; I patient died from unknown causes.

Discussion

In this retrospective multicenter analysis, we report favorable efficacy, toxicity, and resource use metrics for 101 patients who underwent standard-of-care liso-cel therapy between February 2021 and June 2023 at 7 US academic medical centers. Our postapproval experience is relevant to the broader community given that approximately a third of recipients would have been ineligible for the TRANSCEND clinical trial because of comorbidities such as renal insufficiency, cerebrovascular disease, impaired cardiac ejection fraction, severe pulmonary dysfunction, or impaired performance status. Liso-cel recipients were heavily pretreated (median of 3 prior lines of therapy) and harbored high-risk features including advanced age, comorbidities, and CNS involvement. Despite these high-risk features, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status was preserved (ie, 0-1) in the majority (84%) of evaluable recipients, which may partly account for our favorable survival outcomes, as demonstrated in prior reports.

Numerically, the median vein-to-vein time for liso-cel was slightly longer than noted in the pivotal trials. We speculate that this may partly reflect liso-cel manufacturing challenges that existed during the early postmarketing period, a factor that may have influenced patient selection for this product over other commercially available CD19-directed CAR T-cell therapies.

In line with the TRANSCEND clinical trial, we saw a broad use of bridging therapy, possibly reflective of advances in practice patterns with a move toward more aggressive disease debulking before CAR T-cell infusion. Furthermore, bridging strategies in this analysis reveal a shift toward increased use of polatuzumab- and radiotherapy-based approaches, strategies either not permitted or infrequently used in the pivotal clinical trials. These results bolster prior reports demonstrating encouraging efficacy and safety with polatuzumab- and radiotherapy-based bridging strategies.

We note that LDH, a biomarker often reflective of tumor burden and proliferative activity, was associated with efficacy outcomes on MVA. Collectively, these data lend to the growing body of evidence suggesting that controlling the underlying lymphoma and entering CAR T-cell therapy with a lower tumor bulk may be a key determinant of efficacy outcomes. In our analysis, 18% of liso-cel recipients were in a CR at the time of liso-cel infusion, and prior data have demonstrated that entering CAR T-cell therapy with no evidence of disease may be associated with favorable outcomes. However, patients receiving liso-cel in a CR still harbor a substantial risk of disease progression, underscoring the need to further explore and refine the optimal level of disease control before CAR T-cell therapy.

Rates of grade ≥3 CRS and ICANS per the American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy consensus grading system were comparable with those demonstrated in the pivotal trials, which is notable given the advanced age of this population and the fact that approximately a third of recipients would have been ineligible for TRANSCEND. In comparison with the liso-cel pivotal trials, there was a higher use of both tocilizumab and corticosteroids, which may be related to the fact that the study was partly conducted during the COVID-19 pandemic, which may have influenced toxicity management practices. Furthermore, we speculate that these use trends may reflect advances in our understanding of the optimal management of CAR T-cell–related toxicities, especially in patients harboring disease risk features or significant comorbidities. The increased use of anticytokine therapies in the commercial setting did not appear to translate into a more favorable safety profile although when compared with results seen in prospective clinical trials. We speculate that more aggressive toxicity management facilitated our ability to maintain liso-cel’s safety profile while treating patients of advanced age and with significant comorbidities. However, the occurrence of grade 5 events, although rare, suggests that further refinement in toxicity management is needed. In our analysis, immune effector cell–associated hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis-like syndrome was not noted; however, we acknowledge that this may have been underreported because of the fact that its definition was only more recently formalized. These data augment work supporting the use of cellular therapy treatment in a broad patient population, including those with advanced age and medical comorbidities.

With a median follow-up of 15.5-months, ORRs, PFS, and OS are comparable with results seen in the TRANSCEND trial. Response rates were assessed at 90 days after infusion, because data from pivotal CAR T-cell trials suggest that this time point is likely more informative of long-term efficacy. Compared with data seen in the pivotal liso-cel trials, we note a continued decreased in the DOR with longer follow-up, which may be partly explained by the patient population treated in this study. Importantly, ∼73% of responding patients at the 90-day assessment are alive and progression-free at 12-months after infusion.

Patients of advanced age are often not considered for CAR T-cell therapy on the basis of impaired performance status, comorbidities, and a perceived increased risk for treatment-related toxicities. However, CAR T-cell therapy has shown promise in elderly patients with R/R LBCL. A subset analysis of the ZUMA-1 trial evaluating outcomes of patients aged ≥65 years and <65 years demonstrated similar ORRs (92% vs 81%), and a higher proportion of patients aged ≥65 years achieved a best response of CR (75% vs 53%) and had a longer median DOR (12 months vs 8.1 months) than recipients aged <65 years. The authors demonstrated comparable rates of grade ≥3 CRS (12% vs 7%), although there were numerically higher rates of grade ≥3 neurotoxicity (44% vs 28%) in patients aged ≥65 years than those aged <65 years. Similarly, a large retrospective real-world analysis demonstrated no significant association between age and CRS, however increasing age was associated with both the incidence and grade of ICANS. In our analysis, we did not note any significant differences in the efficacy or safety profile of liso-cel in patients aged ≥75 years compared with those aged <75 years. Safety outcomes with standard-of-care liso-cel compare favorably with those seen with axi-cel in ZUMA-1, suggesting that liso-cel may be a preferred cellular therapy treatment in patients of advanced age with R/R LBCL. Our findings build on other real-world experiences with axi-cel and tisa-cel, collectively arguing that age should not serve as a barrier to CAR T-cell treatment.

In this analysis, 26% of liso-cel recipients received bendamustine lymphodepletion, which may reflect, in part, the worldwide fludarabine shortage, which affected both cellular therapy and allogeneic SCT conditioning regimens at CTC member institutions starting in December 2021 and extending into 2023. Prior analyses comparing fludarabine/cyclophosphamide with bendamustine lymphodepletion with tisa-cel and axi-cel have demonstrated similar efficacy with potentially a lower incidence of CRS and ICANS with bendamustine. Although small patient numbers, our data bolster other real-world liso-cel data sets suggesting that bendamustine lymphodepletion may be associated with similar efficacy and improved safety compared with fludarabine/cyclophosphamide.

In this study, the treatment setting (inpatient vs outpatient) was influenced by institutional practice patterns, center-specific infrastructure, and resources for outpatient treatment. Despite many centers moving toward providing CAR T-cell therapy as an outpatient, only 15% received an outpatient liso-cel infusion. Factors such as advanced patient age, medical comorbidities, socioeconomic considerations, and logistical challenges including the COVID-19 pandemic may have led to more inpatient use. However, ∼2/3 of outpatient liso-cel recipients did not require hospital admission within 30 days of infusion, suggesting the feasibility of outpatient treatment.

In contrast to other CD19-directed CAR T-cell trials, liso-cel pivotal trials permitted patients with active secondary CNS involvement based on encouraging preliminary activity, although this only accounted for 3% and 1% of enrollees on TRANSCEND and TRANSFORM, respectively. Among 10 patients with secondary CNS lymphoma, liso-cel was active demonstrating a best overall response of 90% (best CR rate, 70%) with a 12-month PFS of 60%, and the incidence and severity of CRS and ICANS were comparable with liso-cel in systemic LBCL.

These data are in-line with another retrospective liso-cel real-world analysis by Crombie et al, which demonstrated CR rates of 65% and a 6-month DOR of 73% in a broad population including those harboring high-risk features. As in our analysis, toxicity was largely similar to clinical trial experience. These data help to confirm the activity and safety of commercial liso-cel.

As with any retrospective analysis, there are several limitations, including patient-selection biases and the influence of center-specific practices patterns. CTC centers participating in this study had equal access to liso-cel, axi-cel, and tisa-cel treatment during the conduct of this study. Gathering companion data from patients treated with axi-cel and tisa-cel during the same period would help to better inform practice patterns and expand our understanding of factors influencing treatment selection. Our real-world experience reflects an early time period after liso-cel approval for R/R LBCL, and outcomes may be further optimized with future advancements in toxicity management, patient selection, and the use of liso-cel in earlier treatment lines. In addition, longer follow-up will be needed to fully understand the durability of liso-cel–induced remissions.

Despite these limitations, our data confirm that response and toxicity after standard-of-care liso-cel are similar to results seen in pivotal trials. Even with high-risk disease features, advanced age, and medical comorbidities, liso-cel treatment may be associated with sustained complete remissions in a subset of patients, emphasizing the impact of liso-cel in the treatment of R/R LBCL.
