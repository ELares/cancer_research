# Transgenerational inheritance of diminished ovarian reserve triggered by prenatal propylparaben exposure in mice.

## Abstract

Diminished ovarian reserve (DOR) is associated with heightened risk of infertility, premature menopause, and various long-term health issues. Our previous research demonstrated a correlation between prenatal propylparaben exposure and DOR in F1 mice. Here, we further reveal that the DOR phenotypes can be transgenerationally inherited in F1-F3 mice, manifested through increased follicular atresia and decreased anti-Müllerian hormone levels. Excessive apoptosis of granulosa cells is found to underlie these pathological processes. By combining diverse sequencing techniques, we identify persistent Rhobtb1 hypomethylation across multiple generations. Further exploration reveals that RhoBTB1 regulates FGF18 via ubiquitination, triggering MAPK pathway activation and subsequent granulosa cell apoptosis. Notably, similar Rhobtb1 hypomethylation patterns are observed in blood samples from DOR patients. Furthermore, intervention with a methyl-donor diet effectively ameliorates DOR phenotypes in F1-F3 offspring. These findings highlight the transgenerational effects of DOR, elucidate its underlying causes and pathogenic mechanisms, and propose potential epigenetic therapy strategies.

Introduction

Diminished ovarian reserve (DOR) is characterized by a reduced quantity or compromised quality of oocytes, leading to poor fertility outcomes. Roughly 26% of female patients receiving assisted reproductive technology exhibit DOR. The detrimental impacts of DOR on fertility are increasingly pronounced due to the persistent trend of delayed childbearing. The etiology of DOR is complex and multifaceted, encompassing both genetic and non-genetic factors. In recent years, global concern has arisen regarding the adverse impacts of environmental factors on the health of female reproduction and offspring. Notably, exposure to harmful substances during crucial stages of embryonic development can significantly heighten the vulnerability of offspring to diseases in later life. Nevertheless, the potential for such damage to persist across generations remains inadequately understood and warrants further investigation.

Transgenerational epigenetic inheritance (TEI) allows for the transmission of epigenetic information including altered epigenetic states and associated disease traits across generations via the germline, irrespective of their direct exposure to harmful environmental factors. This biological process emphasizes the profound and enduring impact that environmental stressors can exert on the epigenetic landscape of descendants, and reveals the potential origins of many diseases. Studies suggest that DNA methylation, histone modifications, and non-coding RNAs are involved in TEI. Preterm birth, prenatal PM2.5 exposure, and intrauterine alcohol exposure led to conditions such as hypertension, neurodevelopmental disturbance, and compromised immune function, which can be passed down through generations. Particularly, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) is a strongly heritable disease that can be transmitted across three generations through the female germline. Recent studies have indicated that exposure to di (2-ethylhexyl) phthalate (DEHP) and dexamethasone during gestational and lactational periods adversely affects ovarian reserve and diminishes fertility across multiple generations. This suggests the potential for transgenerational inheritance of DOR. However, the TEI of DOR and underlying mechanisms are not yet fully understood, necessitating further exploration.

Endocrine-disrupting chemicals (EDCs) are exogenous substances that disrupt hormonal function, posing a significant global environmental hazard and an urgent public health due to their association with numerous diseases such as cancer, reproductive disorders, cognitive impairments, and obesity. Parabens (PBs), a type of EDCs, have been widely used as preservatives in the cosmetic, food, and pharmaceutical industries for nearly a century. Various female biological samples, including urine, blood, adipose tissue, milk, and placenta, have been demonstrated to contain four forms of parabens: methylparaben (MeP), ethylparaben (EtP), propylparaben (PrP), and butylparaben (BuP). Exposure to parabens has been shown to compromise reproductive function in both rodents and humans. Our previous research demonstrated that prenatal exposure to PrP led to DOR in the F1 generation of mice; however, the impacts on the ovarian reserve across multiple generations remain unexplored.

In this study, we provide evidence for the transgenerational inheritance of DOR and elucidate the mechanism by which PrP exposure leads to a decline in ovarian reserve across successive generations. Additionally, we confirm distinct methylation patterns in the blood of women with DOR, which are consistent with those observed in murine models. These findings highlight the possibility that DOR in descendants may originate from ancestral PrP exposure, and suggest that epigenetic dietary interventions could potentially mitigate multigenerational ovarian impairment.

Results

Transgenerational inheritance of DOR phenotypes was observed after prenatal PrP exposure

To determine the long-term effects of PrP exposure on offspring reproductive systems, we intraperitoneally injected pregnant ICR mice with human-relevant dosages of PrP during the fetal sex determination period. The experimental procedure performed on animals was shown in Fig. 1a. Prenatal PrP exposure had no significant effect on the body weight of pregnant mice (Supplementary Fig. 1a), the number of birth litters per dam (Supplementary Fig. 1b–d), or the sex ratio of offspring mice (Supplementary Fig. 1e–g). A significant decrease in the male birth weight of F1–F3 offspring and the female birth weight of F3 offspring was found in the PrP-exposed groups (Supplementary Fig. 1h–m), which was consistent with previous findings in a cohort study. PrP exposure did not affect ovarian weight or index of F1–F3 female offspring at 12 weeks (Supplementary Table 1).

Ovarian reserves of offspring mice were assessed by Anti-Müllerian hormone (AMH) levels and follicular quantity. AMH levels in F1–F3 offspring of the PrP-exposed group decreased significantly compared to those of the control group (Fig. 1b). The morphological features of ovarian follicles at various developmental stages in mice are presented in Supplementary Fig. 2. In the three generations, the PrP-exposed group experienced a considerable rise in the number of atretic follicles (Fig. 1c, d). In parallel, the number of primordial follicles significantly decreased in the PrP-exposed group of F1–F3 offspring (Fig. 1d). Additionally, PrP exposure led to a higher proportion of irregular estrous cycles in the F1–F3 offspring and a significant difference in the F3 generation with protracted metestrus and diestrus periods (Fig. 1e, f). The 17β-estradiol (E2) levels in F1 and F3 offspring in the PrP-exposed group displayed a decreasing trend (Fig. 1g) and progesterone (P4) levels did not differ between the control and the exposed groups in all three generations (Fig. 1h). Collectively, these findings suggest that decreased ovarian reserve induced by prenatal PrP exposure can be inherited transgenerationally.

Apoptosis of granulosa cells and deteriorated oocyte quality were seen in multiple generations

The apoptosis of granulosa cells (GCs) is the leading cause of follicular atresia and poor ovarian reserve. Therefore, apoptotic GCs in F1–F3 offspring were detected by terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase-mediated nick-end labeling (TUNEL) staining. Across all F1–F3 generations, GC apoptosis exhibited a marked elevation in the PrP-exposed groups compared to controls (Fig. 2a, b). Electron microscopy was performed to observe the structure of GCs in F1–F3 offspring mice and revealed significantly increased mitochondria abnormalities (Fig. 2c), mainly manifesting as a swollen morphology and broken cristae (Fig. 2d). Interestingly, the synapses between GCs and oocytes were also blurred in the F3 PrP-exposed group (Fig. 2d).

Since deteriorated oocyte quality is an important hallmark of DOR and oocytes serve as the medium for TEI, we further assessed the oocyte quality of offspring by ovulation induction (Fig. 2e) and observed the stages of oocyte maturation and meiosis (Fig. 2f). The number of MII oocytes drastically decreased in PrP-exposed group compared to the control group in F1 and F3 generations (Fig. 2g). This observation was further corroborated by a marked decrease in the protein expression levels of bone morphogenetic protein 15 (BMP15), a known protective factor for oocyte quality, in the F1–F3 offspring from the PrP-exposed group (Fig. 2h, i). Collectively, these data indicate that GC apoptosis is a mechanistical contributor to the observed reduction in ovarian reserve and the suppression of AMH. Importantly, the compromised oocytes may harbor transgenerational epigenetic signatures, potentially mediating intergenerational transmission of reproductive dysfunction.

The hypomethylation of Rhobtb1 was observed in successive generations

DNA methylation is the dominant mechanism underlying TEI caused by ancestral exposure, with the transmission of epigenetic information carried by germ cells across generations being the most crucial component. To identify the DNA methylation alterations in oocytes at single-base resolution upon PrP exposure, we conducted single-cell whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (scWGBS) on 72 MII oocytes from F2 offspring after ovulation induction (Fig. 3a). Additionally, the whole-genome bisulfite sequencing (WGBS) was employed to determine the methylation levels in ovarian tissues from both F2 and F3 offspring mice (Fig. 3a).

Overall, cytosine-phosphate-guanine (CpG) sites methylation levels displayed a similar pattern in the ovarian tissues of F2 and F3 generations (Fig. 3b). However, an increase in hypomethylated CpG sites (~30%) and hypermethylated CpG sites (~70%) was observed in F2 MII oocytes upon PrP exposure (Fig. 3b). We identified 2114 and 2147 significantly hypermethylated and hypomethylated regions respectively in the ovarian tissues of PrP-exposed F2 offspring. These regions correlate to 1308 exclusively hypermethylated and 1251 hypomethylated genes. The oocytes of PrP-exposed F2 offspring exhibited 12,721 significantly hypermethylated regions and 16,631 hypomethylated regions, corresponding to 7253 genes that were exclusively hypermethylated and 10,117 hypomethylated genes. The ovaries of PrP-exposed F3 offspring had 1505 significantly hypermethylated regions and 1283 hypomethylated regions, corresponding to 963 exclusively hypermethylated genes and 766 hypomethylated genes, respectively (Fig. 3c). The percentage distribution of each functional annotation was shown in Fig. 3d. Notably, in contrast to the ovarian tissues, most hypomethylated regions in the oocytes were located within the promoter region (56.8%) (Fig. 3d).

To identify epigenetic molecules transmitted across generations, we performed an overlap analysis of methylation sequencing data from F2 ovaries, F3 ovaries, and F2 oocytes. A Sankey diagram was constructed to illustrates the concordant methylation changes in differentially methylated regions (DMRs) across these groups (Fig. 3e). Remarkably, DMRs associated with Rho-related BTB domain-containing protein 1 (Rhobtb1) and the gene 4930519F24Rik exhibited consistent hypomethylation patterns in F2 ovaries, F2 oocytes, and F3 ovaries (Fig. 3e). It is noteworthy that 4930519F24Rik has not been named or studied yet, and given the regulatory roles of Rhobtb1 in cell growth and apoptosis, we prioritized the Rhobtb1 gene for further investigation. Surprisingly, methylation levels in Rhobtb1 promoter region were significantly reduced in both F2 and F3 ovarian tissues, as well as in F2 oocytes of PrP-exposed group, compared to the control group (Fig. 3f, p < 0.05; F2 ovary diff.Methyl = 0.45, F2 oocyte diff.Methyl = 0.33, F3 ovary diff.Methyl = 0.34). This suggests that aberrant methylation of Rhobtb1 may serve as an epigenetic factor transmitted from F2 to F3 generation. The heatmap displayed the methylation patterns in the Rhobtb1 promoter region across ovarian tissues from F2 and F3 offspring, and oocytes from F2 offspring (Fig. 3g). Furthermore, RNA sequencing of the F3 offspring ovaries revealed an increased trend in Rhobtb1 expression levels in the exposed group compared to the control group (Fig. 3h, p = 0.198). Correspondingly, the protein levels of RhoBTB1 in F2 and F3 ovaries (Fig. 3i, j) and F2 oocytes increased significantly in the PrP-exposed group relative to the control group (Fig. 3k, l). These results suggest that the hypomethylation in the promoter region of Rhobtb1 mediate the TEI of DOR induced by prenatal PrP exposure.

Hypomethylated Rhobtb1 contributed to granulosa cell apoptosis and reduced AMH secretion

As established previously, prenatal PrP exposure induced excessive GC apoptosis in F1–F3 offspring ovaries, identifying GC apoptosis as a primary mechanism underlying transgenerational DOR pathogenesis. To further investigate the impact of PrP exposure on Rhobtb1 in GCs, we employed the human ovarian granulosa cell line (KGN) for in vitro exposure experiments. KGN cells were treated with 50 μM and 100 μM PrP for 48 h, based on the results of the IC50 (Supplementary Fig. 3). This treatment resulted in a significant reduction in methylation levels at the CpG site −1555 to the transcription start site (TSS) within the promoter region of Rhobtb1 in the PrP-exposed groups (Fig. 4a). Correspondingly, there was a marked increase in RhoBTB1 proteins levels after PrP exposure (Fig. 4b, c). The concentrations of AMH and E2 were significantly decreased post-exposure (Fig. 4d, e). Moreover, a dose-dependent increase in apoptosis was observed (Fig. 4f, g). Subsequently, western blotting analysis was used to determine the protein levels of key genes involved in the mitochondrial apoptotic pathway in GCs. As expected, a noticeable decrease in B-cell lymphoma 2 (BCL-2), and a significant increase in Bcl-2 associated X protein (BAX) and cleaved Caspase-3 (active-Caspase3) were observed (Fig. 4h, i).

To further clarify the effects of RhoBTB1 overexpression on GC apoptosis, we employed an adenoviral vector encoding Rhobtb1 to up-regulate RhoBTB1 in KGN cells in the absence of PrP exposure. The infection efficiency exceeded 90% (Fig. 4j), resulting in a significant increase of RhoBTB1 proteins expression (Fig. 4k, l). Upon the overexpression of RhoBTB1, there was a notable decrease in AMH and E2 levels (Fig. 4m, n), accompanied by an elevated number of apoptotic GCs (Fig. 4o, p). Similarly, the mitochondrial apoptosis pathway-related genes including BAX, BCL-2, and active-Caspase3 exhibited significant alterations (Fig. 4q, r). Collectively, these findings suggest that Rhobtb1 hypomethylation may contribute to the excessive apoptosis of GCs.

Potential targets for RhoBTB1 identified through ovarian transcriptomic profiles and DNA methylation patterns

The abnormal epigenetic molecules carried by germ cells lead to the TEI of diseases by regulating downstream target genes or pathways. To explore the mechanisms underlying the TEI of DOR induced by hypomethylated Rhobtb1, we conducted RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) and WGBS on the ovarian tissues from the F3 generation (Fig. 5a). We discovered 544 differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in the ovaries of F3 progeny exposed to PrP, in comparison to control ovaries, with 288 down-regulated and 256 genes being up-regulated (Fig. 5b; Supplementary Fig. 4a). The Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis revealed significant up-regulation of the p53 signaling pathway and oxidative phosphorylation (Fig. 5c), both of which are implicated in apoptosis in GCs. Conversely, genes involved in steroid biosynthesis, which are integral to ovarian endocrine function, were significantly down-regulated (Fig. 5d). Subsequently, the RNA levels of genes involved in these three pathways were validated by quantitative real-time polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) (Fig. 5e–j). Protein levels of Catalase (CAT), superoxide dismutase 2 (SOD2), and hydroxysteroid 17-beta dehydrogenase 7 (HSD17B7) protein levels in the ovaries of the PrP-exposed F3 offspring were significantly lower than those of the control group, while BAX exhibited the opposite trend (Supplementary Fig. 4b–i). These results suggest that oxidative stress, increased apoptosis, and the disturbance of sex hormone synthesis are the main biological processes involved in the DOR-like phenotypes of F3 offspring.

Epigenetic changes in the ovaries of F3 PrP-exposed mice were uniformly distributed across all the chromosomes (Fig. 5k). The comparison of the ovaries of PrP-exposed F3 offspring to controls identified 1505 significantly hypermethylated and 1283 significantly hypomethylated regions, corresponding to 963 exclusively hypermethylated and 766 exclusively hypomethylated genes (Fig. 5l). To gain more insight into the functional implications of these epigenetic modifications after PrP exposure, we assessed the enrichment of DMRs within annotated regions relative to their background regions (Fig. 5m). KEGG analysis revealed the pathways that hypermethylated and hypomethylated genes enriched respectively (Supplementary Fig. 4j, k).

We searched for overlap between differentially methylated genes and DEGs in F3 offspring to determine whether variations in gene expression were related to changes in DNA methylation (Fig. 5n). 38 genes were identified through this cross-omics integration (Fig. 5o). Chymotrypsin-like elastase family, member 3B (Cela3b), claudin 18 (Cldn18), cathepsin K (Ctsk), and gap junction protein, gamma 3 (Gjc3) displayed methylation changes in the promoter region (Fig. 5p). Cela3b might serve as a novel potential prognostic biomarker for ovarian serous cystadenocarcinoma. In ovarian tumors, Cldn18 has frequent ectopic activation. Ctsk is regarded as a regulator of oocyte quality. However, to date, no studies have explicitly reported the expression profile or functional regulatory roles of Gjc3 in ovarian tissues. Among the 38 common genes, fibroblast growth factor 18 (Fgf18) is closely related to GC apoptosis and atretic follicles. However, the altered methylation region of Fgf18 was located in the distal intergenic region (Fig. 5p). As the hypothalamus is the primary regulator of reproductive function, we performed next-generation sequencing-based bisulfite sequencing PCR (BSP) in the hypothalamus of control and PrP-exposed mice of F3 generation to determine whether alterations in DNA methylation in the ovaries of PrP-exposed mice could occur in other tissues. Similar to the results observed for ovaries, the methylation levels of Cldn18, Ctsk, and Gjc3, in the promotor regions increased significantly in the PrP-exposed group compared to the control group (Supplementary Fig. 4l). RNA expression of the genes was validated in the hypothalamus of control and PrP-exposed mice by qRT-PCR (Supplementary Fig. 4m). Similar to the results observed for ovaries, the RNA expression levels of Cldn18 and Ctsk decreased significantly in the PrP-exposed group compared to the control group. These results suggest that the five genes mentioned above are potential targets for DOR.

RhoBTB1 activated the MAPK pathway and induced GC apoptosis by upregulating FGF18 via a ubiquitination-dependent pathway

RhoBTB1, an atypical member of the Rho-GTPase family, exhibits a unique structural architecture composed of an N-terminal GTPase domain, a proline-rich hinge region, tandem BTB domains (BTB1 and BTB2), and a conserved C-terminal region. Notably, the BTB domains facilitate protein complex assembly through homomeric or heteromeric oligomerization. Classical mechanistic studies demonstrate that the BTB1 domain specifically recruits the Cullin 3-based E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, while substrate recognition is cooperatively mediated by the BTB2 domain, GTPase domain, and C-terminal region. This coordinated interaction enables substrate ubiquitination and subsequent degradation via the ubiquitin-proteasome system, thereby regulating protein homeostasis.

To explore the interaction and potential regulatory patterns between Rhobtb1 and the five aforementioned genes, we utilized the STRING database. Our analysis revealed indirect interactions between RhoBTB1 and FGF18, as well as between RhoBTB1and CTSK (Fig. 6a; Supplementary Fig. 5a). Notably, CTSK protein levels exhibited an increased trend following GCs PrP exposure (Supplementary Fig. 5b), a finding that contrasts with the results of ovarian RNA-seq assay from the unexposed F3 generation (Fig. 5p). The observed reduction in CTSK expression in F3 ovaries implies that decreased CTSK expression may function as a protective factor, mitigating the detrimental effects of PrP exposure. It is noteworthy that the methylation level of Fgf18 in the F3 PrP-exposed group showed no significant difference in the promoter region, indicating that Fgf18 is not an epigenetic messenger molecule, but is likely a target regulated by abnormal epigenetic molecules, which inherited across generations. Additionally, as shown in Fig. 6a, mitogen-activated protein kinase 3 (MAPK3) had an interaction with FGF18. Further, an increase in FGF18 proteins and an activation of MAPK signaling pathway were observed after PrP exposure in KGN cells (Fig. 6b, c). This phenomenon was also observed in Rhobtb1 over-expression models (Fig. 6d, e), which was in accordance with previous studies that the MAPK signaling pathway can be activated by fibroblast growth factors (FGFs). Upon silencing Fgf18 using siRNA-F02 in GCs (Supplementary Fig. 5c), identified as the most effective siRNA, following PrP exposure, there was a significant reduction in the activation of the MAPK signaling pathway, whereas the expression of RhoBTB1 remained unchanged (Fig. 6f, g).

To reveal the exact regulatory relationship between Rhobtb1 and Fgf18, we further knock down Rhobtb1 in GCs (Supplementary Fig. 5d). Silencing Rhobtb1 with siRNA-R03 post-PrP exposure resulted in a significant decrease in both FGF18 expression and MAPK pathway activation (Fig. 6h, i). These results indicate that RhoBTB1 may serve as an upstream regulator of FGF18. To determine whether RhoBTB1 regulated FGF18 through the canonical ubiquitination pathway, we treated cells with MLN4924 (a neddylation inhibitor that blocks Cullin3 complex activation; Fig. 6j–m) and MG132 (a proteasome inhibitor; Fig. 6n–q) under conditions of PrP exposure or RhoBTB1 overexpression. Notably, both treatments result in a significant reduction in FGF18 expression levels. These results imply that RhoBTB1 enhances FGF18 expression through the ubiquitination pathway, not by directly regulating FGF18, but rather by a “de-repression” mechanism involving the selective elimination of FGF18 inhibitors.

Therefore, our study demonstrates that prenatal PrP exposure induced Rhobtb1 promoter hypomethylation. This abnormal epigenetic imprint escapes DNA methylation reprogramming and is passed down through oocytes to the F3 generation, resulting in the accumulation of RhoBTB1 protein in the ovaries of the descendants. Elevated RhoBTB1 levels leads to FGF18 overexpression via the ubiquitin-proteasome system, which subsequently activated the MAPK signaling pathway, triggering mitochondrial-dependent granulosa cells apoptosis and ultimately predisposing multiple generations to DOR.

Hypomethylated Rhobtb1 in blood samples may be a potential marker for women with DOR

DOR and premature ovarian insufficiency (POI) are two concepts related to premature ovarian aging. Compared with POI, DOR focuses on diminished ovarian reserve without age restriction. To determine whether the findings in mice could be applied to human patients with DOR or POI, we examined blood samples from women with DOR (n = 63), POI (n = 60) and healthy women (n = 60) to identify shared epigenetic signatures by using BSP (Fig. 7a). The methylation levels of Rhobtb1 in the blood samples from these three groups of women were assessed. Significant hypomethylation of Rhobtb1 was observed at CpG sites 2632, 2621, 2593, 2577, 1555, 1396, 1391, 1349, 1311 bps upstream (Fig. 7b, c) to the TSS in women with DOR compared to controls. In women with POI, hypomethylation was additionally detected at the site 1259 alongside the aforementioned sites. Conversely, the CpG sites 800 bps upstream (Fig. 7c) were significantly hypermethylated in women with POI compared to controls. In DOR patients, significant hypermethylation was also observed at two additional sites (809 and 805 bps). Finally, the methylation levels at the remaining 3 CpG sites (−1523, −1345, and −1247 pos) showed identical between patients and controls (Fig. 7c).

Additionally, we also found that the methylation levels of CpG sites −1776, −1146, −1141, and −1128 (Supplementary Fig. 6a, b) to the TSS of Ctsk decreased significantly in women with DOR and POI compared to the controls. Furthermore, women with POI exhibited significantly elevated methylation levels at CpG sites −223 and −173 relative to the TSS of Gjc3 (Supplementary Fig. 6c, d), consistent with the results from F3 ovaries; methylation levels at the remaining CpG sites −218, −195 and −153 were comparable across the three groups (Supplementary Fig. 6d). In contrast to the results from F3 ovaries, the methylation levels of CpG sites −744 relative to the TSS of Cela3b increased significantly in women with POI (Supplementary Fig. 6e, f), while CpG sites −734 and −728 showed no significant difference among the groups (Supplementary Fig. 6e, f). Finally, the methylation levels of CpG sites −78, −60, and −51 of Cldn18 exhibited no difference between patients and controls (Supplementary Fig. 6g, h). All observed changes were located in the promoter region. Collectively, these results suggest that shared epigenetic signatures in blood samples may serve as potential biomarkers for women with DOR.

Methyl-donor diet treatment of F0 mice ameliorated PrP-induced transgenerational inheritance of DOR

A diet enriched with methyl-donor (MD), comprising dietary constituents such as folic acid, methionine, choline, betaine, vitamin B12, and vitamin B6, has been reported to alleviate the ovarian metabolic phenotypes in mice with PCOS by correcting the hypomethylation. Considering that hypomethylated Rhobtb1 presented in ovarian tissues of PrP-exposed offspring, we examined the therapeutic potential of MD in an epigenetic preclinical investigation (Fig. 8a). Administration of the MD diet to F0 mice during pregnancy and lactation resulted in a reduction in the proportion of irregular estrous cycles among the PrP-exposed F1–F3 offspring (Fig. 8b). Moreover, MD treatment significantly improved AMH levels in the PrP-exposed F1–F3 offspring (Fig. 8c). Compared with the PrP-exposed group, there was an increase in the number of primordial follicles across three generations, with statistical significance observed in the F1 and F2 offspring (Fig. 8d–f). Notably, the number of atretic follicles decreased significantly in PrP+MD groups of F1 and F3 offspring (Fig. 8d–f). Consistently, the apoptosis of GCs decreased significantly after MD treatment compared to the exposure group (Fig. 8g, h). Additionally, the expression levels of RhoBTB1 in the ovaries were rescued in the F1–F3 offspring of the PrP+MD group (Fig. 8i, j). Therefore, MD could serve as an epigenetic-based therapy to ameliorate the transgenerational inheritance of DOR induced by prenatal PrP exposure.

Discussion

The incidence of DOR has gradually increased, posing a significant public health threat to women’s health across their entire lifespan. However, the etiology of DOR remains largely elusive. Our previous research suggested a potential embryonic origin of DOR, instigated by intrauterine exposure to PrP. This study demonstrated that DOR, triggered by prenatal PrP exposure, could be inherited across generations. To dissect the cascade of molecular events, we conducted multi-omics sequencing, including RNA-seq, WGBS, and scWGBS, and identified hypomethylated Rhobtb1 as a contributor to the TEI of DOR. Furthermore, the presence of hypomethylated Rhobtb1 was validated in blood samples of women with DOR and POI. Finally, we demonstrated that MD treatment ameliorated the transgenerational effects of prenatal PrP exposure.

We observed DOR-like phenotypes characterized by reduced serum AMH levels, increased follicular atresia, decreased primordial follicle number and oocyte quality, persisting across three generations. GC apoptosis in the PrP-exposed group, key driver of these DOR phenotypes, was observed to continue through successive generations. The heightened follicular atresia may potentially expedite the activation of primordial follicles, thereby contributing to a diminished ovarian reserve; however, this remains speculative and warrants further investigation. Consistently, existing literature indicates that ancestral exposure to various environmental compounds and relevant mixtures significantly reduced the number of primordial follicles in F1–F3 offspring mice. No significant differences in E2 and P4 levels were found between exposed and control groups, though RNA-seq results revealed many down-regulated genes related to steroid biogenesis, such as 7-dehydrocholesterol reductase (Dhcr7), 17β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase (Hsd17b7), luteinizing hormone/choriogonadotropin receptor (Lhcgr), and 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (Hsd11b1). Manikkam et al. showed that 200 mg/(kg·d) methoxide exposure during embryonic gonadal differentiation increased ovarian disease incidence without affecting estrogen levels in F3 female mice, which is consistent with the results of our study. These findings demonstrate that PrP mainly affects ovarian reserve, without significant impacts on E2 and P4.

Accumulating evidence indicates that environmental stressors influence a wide range of traits in offspring via germline-mediated epigenetic inheritance. Recent studies have demonstrated that DNA methylation of promoter-related CpG islands in mouse sperm and oocytes can evade reprogramming, thereby facilitating transgenerational inheritance. While the predominant focus on the transmission of epigenetic information via sperm, research on oocytes-mediated epigenetic inheritance remains in its infancy. This disparity may be attributed to technical challenges in obtaining adequate oocytes for sequencing and molecular biological analyses, due to their relative scarcity and the complexity of their isolation process. The single-cell transcriptome sequencing (scRNA-seq) and scWGBS have illuminated the intricate epigenetic codes of oocytes. By integrating scWGBS of F2 MII oocytes with WGBS of F2 and F3 ovarian tissues, we demonstrated that hypomethylated Rhobtb1 was transgenerationally inherited. Notably, the F3 offspring mice, representing the first generation not directly exposed to the initial stimulus, exhibited DOR-like phenotypes, indicating the functional significance of the inherited Rhobtb1 hypomethylation in DOR pathogenesis. RhoBTB1, known for its unique dual BTB domains, primarily acts as a substrate adaptor for the Cullin 3-based E3 ubiquitin ligase complex, facilitating protein degradation and influencing various diseases, including hypertension, atherosclerosis, and tumors. However, its role in ovarian function is not well-documented. This study shows that RhoBTB1 regulates FGF18 via the Cullin3 ubiquitination pathway through “de-repression”, supporting CM McKinnon’s initial hypothesis of RhoBTB1 modulating C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 14 (CXCL14) activity by removing suppressors. Additionally, RhoBTB1 expression is significantly reduced in head and neck tumors and breast cancer, identifying as a novel tumor suppressor gene. Freeman et al. demonstrated that RhoBTB1 recombinant protein inhibited tumor cell proliferation and promotes apoptosis via a mitochondria-dependent pathway, consistent with our findings that RhoBTB1 induces apoptosis in GCs.

The function of GCs is regulated by multiple mechanisms involving DNA damage, epigenetic modifications, oxidative stress, and mitochondrial dysfunction. Here, we observed enhanced oxidative phosphorylation and increased abnormal mitochondria, which may drive GC apoptosis. As a member of the fibroblast growth factor family, FGF18 orchestrates cellular processes including proliferation, differentiation, migration, and survival. Although FGF18 has been demonstrated to promote bovine GC apoptosis and follicular atresia, its underlying mechanisms require further elucidation. Our RNA-seq analysis revealed a significant enrichment of p53 signaling pathway genes, a key regulator of cell cycle and apoptosis. It has been reported that the MAPK pathway can interact with p53 protein and concurrent implicated in aging process. Through in vitro validation, we confirmed that FGF18 induced GC apoptosis via MAPK signaling activation, aligning with tumor biology observations. Notably, we have effectively validated a robust correlation between RhoBTB1 and FGF18, providing strong evidence that RhoBTB1 may contribute to GC dysfunction through FGF18 upregulation and subsequent MAPK pathway hyperactivation.

Epigenetic changes like DNA methylation, which are more stable than gene expression alterations, can serve as diagnostic markers for diseases. Several DEGs in oocytes from PCOS mice of F1–F3 offspring also showed changes in blood samples from women with PCOS, providing potential candidate genes for predicting PCOS in future generations. Our study confirms that methylation levels at CpG sites in key gene promoters, identified in mouse ovarian tissues and oocytes, could serve as diagnostic markers for ovarian aging in women with DOR and POI. Several disorders involve both hypermethylation and hypomethylation. While the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has licensed several DNA methylation inhibitors to treat various diseases, no pharmaceutical approaches targeting hypomethylation have yet been authorized. A methyl-donor diet supports one-carbon metabolism balance and has been shown to alleviate PCOS symptoms, metabolic disorders and pathological vascular changes in rodents. These beneficial effects are further supported by population study linking folic acid intake to improved embryo quality and reduced risk of infertility. One-carbon metabolism diets reportedly increase AMH levels and natural conception rates, offering new treatment strategies for combating infertility. Our study showed that MD treatment can improve DOR-like phenotypes in F1–F3 generations, suggesting methylating agents as potential epigenetic therapies for women with DOR. However, its clinical translation requires further research to address individualized needs, long-term health risks, efficacy evaluation and the development of guiding principles.

Despite the strict regulation on PBs in children’s products, their widespread application persists across various domains. Given the well-documented associations between PBs exposure and adverse health issues, including reproductive dysfunction, breast cancer, obesity, thyroid disorders, and allergic dermatitis, it is crucial to implement stricter controls on PBs exposure among pregnant women to safeguard the intergenerational health. Specific measures should prioritize raising awareness among pregnant women about PBs risks, promoting proactive avoidance of PBs exposure, and encouraging the use of organic-certified and paraben-free skincare products and food. Furthermore, considering the extensive use and complex exposure pathways of parabens, comprehensive epidemiological studies are required to better understand population-level exposure and to establish evidence-based safety standards. From a policy perspective, it is imperative to restrict the use of certain EDCs and to modernize preservative systems to mitigate daily exposure risks. Such measures are critical for preventing ovarian aging and other health conditions linked to paraben exposure.

Collectively, our findings indicate that DOR can be inherited across generations following ancestral exposure to detrimental factors. This provides novel insights into the etiology of female fertility decline and highlights potential epigenetic-based therapeutic interventions for DOR. Future research should encompass a broader spectrum, including histone modifications and non-coding RNAs, to achieve a more comprehensive understanding of this transgenerational phenomenon. Additionally, intraperitoneal injection allows precise dosing with minimal stress in mice but differs from human paraben exposure routes like dermal absorption, ingestion, or inhalation, thereby limiting direct application to humans. Future studies employing realistic exposure routes and extending this research to primate models, which closely mimic human reproductive biology, will facilitate the translation of these findings into clinical applications. While our study identified a significant reduction in the birth weight of male mice in PrP-exposed groups across the F1–F3 generations, further investigation is needed to fully understand the long-term health implications for male mice. Furthermore, we specifically targeted the embryonic gonadal differentiation period for exposure; however, the potential effects of preconception PrP exposure on offspring ovarian function require further exploration through multiple exposure time points. Lastly, organisms are concurrently exposed to multiple EDCs and lifestyle factors in real-world scenarios, future research should therefore focus on deciphering the integrated epigenetic effects and interaction networks of these combined environmental stressors.

Methods

Ethical approval for the research

The Animal Ethics Council of Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, China, approved all animal experiments (TJH-201903002). Women were screened for POI (n = 60), DOR (n = 63) and Con (n = 60) at the Department of Obstetrics and Gynecology, Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, China. The Medicine Ethics Committee of Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, China, approved all the sample collection (TJ-IRB20211016), and all participants provided written informed consent.

Chemicals

PrP (CAS 94-13-3; P5835; Purity ≥ 99%) and a mixture of methyl donor diets (including betaine, folic acid, vitamin B12, and choline chloride) were purchased from Sigma. Corn oil was purchased from Promotor Biotechnology, Ltd. (P6023). Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kits for E2 (CEA461Ge, detection range: 12.35-1000 pg/ml) and P4 (CEA459Ge, detection range: 1.23–100 ng/ml) were purchased from Uscn Life Science. ELISA kits of AMH were purchased from CUSABIO (CSB-E12756h, sensitivity: 15.6 pg/ml, detection range: 62.5 pg–4000 pg/ml). The FITC-Annexin V Apoptosis Detection Kit I was purchased from Boster (MK1018). TRIzol was purchased from Invitrogen (15596-026). HiScript II Q RT SuperMix for qPCR was obtained from Vazyme (R223-01). Inhibitors of the ubiquitin-proteasome system, MLN4924 (HY-70062) and MG132 (HY-13259), were purchased from MedChemExpress. The Antibodies were purchased from different companies: Cell Signaling Technology for ERK (Extracellular Signal-Regulated Kinase; 4695T), p-ERK (Phosphorylated ERK; 4370T), p38 (p38 Mitogen-Activated Protein Kinase; 8690T), p-p38 (Phosphorylated p38; 4511T), JNK (c-Jun N-terminal Kinase; 9252T), and p-JNK (Phosphorylated JNK; 4668T); Abclonal for β-ACTIN (AC038), active-Caspase3 (A19654), BCL-2 (A19693), BAX (A12009), SOD2 (A19576), BMP15 (A7321), CAT (A11777), HSD17B7 (A17157), and CTSK (A1782); Proteintech for FGF18 (60341-1-lg); Immunoway for RhoBTB1 (YT4084); Servicebio for HRP conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (GB23303), and Alexa Fluor® 488-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (GB25303).

Animals

All animal care procedures and experiments were performed in accordance with the ARRIVE guidelines. ICR mice were selected as the experimental subjects for studies on transgenerational epigenetic inheritance studies due to their homogeneous genetic background, robust reproductive capability, and stable physiological traits. The Beijing HFK Bio-Technology Co., Ltd. supplied 8-week-old ICR mice, which were fed ad libitum for two weeks to allow them to acclimatize to the surroundings. Four mice were housed in each cage, and the animals were kept under a controlled 12 h light/12 h dark cycle with regulated temperature (20–25 °C) and relative humidity (40–60%). Throughout the investigation, these mice were housed in tidy polyphenylene sulfone resin cages and given unlimited access to purified water and estrogen-free mouse food (Laboratory Rodent Diet, LabDiet, 5001).

PrP exposure and MD treatment protocol

Female mice aged 10 weeks were allowed to mate with male mice of the same age at a 2:1 ratio. This ratio was chosen to maximize the efficient use of males and enhance reproductive success. The morning after, female mice were checked; the day with a sperm plug or sperm-positive vaginal smear was designated as E0. On E0, we randomly allocated 24 pregnant mice into the control and the exposure groups, with each group containing 12 mice. Pregnant mice received daily intraperitoneal injections of PrP dissolved in corn oil from E7.5 to E13.5 at doses of 0 mg/(kg·d) and 7.5 mg/(kg·d). Intraperitoneal injection is convenient, causes minimal stress to animals, and allows precise control of dosage and timing to achieve a target exposure of 7.5 mg/(kg·d). The selection of the E7.5–E13.5 developmental window is justified by its dual biological significance: this phase is characterized by both genome-wide epigenetic reprogramming, especially global genome DNA demethylation, and the initiation of gonadal sexual differentiation. The temporal concurrence of these two critical processes provides an optimal experimental paradigm for investigating how PrP exposure affects ovarian development, as it enables simultaneous analysis of epigenetic modifications and structural differentiation in the developing reproductive system. The medication dose conversion coefficient for mature mice and humans is 9.01 (The Chinese Animal Experiment Information website). The conversion coefficient was used to translate the PrP global exposure estimate for mice: 0.83 mg/(kg·d) ×9.01 = 7.5 mg/(kg·d). Throughout the dosing period, body weight changes were considered for determining the exposure doses. The F1 mice were fed regularly from birth until weaning, after which they were given estrogen-free mouse food and distilled water as needed until they were 12 weeks of age (12 w). The F2 and F3 generations were created by mating F1 and F2 female mice with wild-type male mice at 10 w, respectively. Following 2-week vaginal smears, the offspring mice were euthanized at diestrus using a combination of carbon dioxide anesthesia and cervical dislocation. We strictly adhere to the principles of humane euthanasia, ensuring that the procedure minimizes pain and anxiety for the animals involved. The ovaries were dissected and weighed, and the serum was isolated and kept at −80 °C. The left and right ovaries were kept at −80 °C and 4% formalin, respectively.

A specific experimental diet was formulated by adding components related to a methyl-donor diet (MD), including 4.3 mg of folic acid/kg diet, 0.5 mg of vitamin B12/kg diet, 5 g of betaine/kg diet, and 7.97 g of choline chloride/kg diet, to a standard diet. Dosages of MD were those previously used to study the effects of BPA on the offspring epigenome. The MD supplement was administered to F0 female mice during pregnancy and lactation, and the treatment protocol was performed as described above, with the number of pregnant mice in each intervention group being 10. It is worth emphasizing that we strictly adhere to the principle of randomly selecting mice from different litters in each experiment.

Monitoring the estrous cycle

Using cells from vaginal fluid collected every day at 10:00 am for 14 days, we assessed the estrous cyclicity of female offspring from different exposure groups. In brief, the liquid was poured onto a glass slide and let to dry naturally. Hematoxylin-eosin (HE) staining was performed on the slides (i.e., stain with hematoxylin for 5 min, and then stain with eosin for 2 min). Subsequently, microscopic analysis was used to determine the estrous phases. Based on cytology, the estrous cycle is divided into four stages: proestrus, estrus, metestrus, and diestrus. The collected information was utilized to determine the estrous cycle’s regularity.

Ovarian follicle counting

The ovaries were serially sectioned at 5-μm thickness. Five successive sections were mounted on a glass slide and stained with HE. Then, the stained slides were imaged using a microscope (Olympus, BX51) for morphological observation. Follicles were counted in sections at least 25 μm apart (each fifth section) spanning the entire ovary. Only the follicles with clear oocyte nuclei were counted. Two individuals, blinded to the origin of the sections inspected the samples. The exact numbers of follicles were determined by multiplying the raw numbers by a correction factor, which was 5 in this study. Primordial follicles (PMF), primary follicles (PF), secondary follicles (SF), antral follicles (ANF), and atretic follicles (ATF) were identified as previously reported. PMF were identified as follicles surrounded by a partial or complete layer of squamous granulosa cells. PF were identified as follicles with a single layer of cuboidal granulosa cells. SF were those that possessed more than one layer of granulosa cells without an antrum. ANF were those that had one or two antra or a large antrum. ATF were identified as follicles that had lost the normal follicular morphology and showed nuclear pyknosis, nuclear lysis, and nuclear fragmentation in the granulosa cells.

Serum concentrations of E2, P4, and AMH

E2, P4, and AMH concentrations were measured using ELISA on the separated serum samples following the manufacturer’s instructions.

Apoptosis analysis in mouse GCs

An apoptosis detection kit was used to identify apoptotic cells in mouse ovaries. For each group, five mice were selected, and five sections from the whole ovarian tissue of each mouse were used for TUNEL detection and subsequent data analysis. Every slide was subjected to a TUNEL reaction mixture for 1 h at 37 °C, per manufacturer’s instructions. We used a microscope to observe apoptotic cells that were fluorescein-labeled green on a blue background (Olympus, BX51). The number of TUNEL-positive cells was counted using the Image-Pro Plus software.

Ovulation induction and evaluation of oocyte quality

At 12 w, offspring mice in each group were randomly selected for ovulation induction, and 10 U of pregnant mare serum gonadotropin was intraperitoneally administered into each mouse, followed 48 h later by 10 U of human chorionic gonadotropin. Oocyte cumulus complexes (OCCs) were removed under a microscope from the ampulla of the fallopian tubes after 16–18 h. OCCs were treated with 80 IU/ml hyaluronidase for 1–3 min, and cumulus cells were gently blown off the sample. The oocytes were then transferred into phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), their morphology was observed, and the types of oocytes were determined and counted. After ovulation induction, oocytes were mainly divided into four types: germinal vesicle (GV) stage, germinal vesicle breakdown (GVBD) stage, second meiosis period (Meiosis II; MII), and degeneration (D). The quality of the oocytes was determined by the number of oocytes that reached the MII stage.

Observation of ovarian ultrastructure by transmission electron microscope

Immediately after dissection, three ovaries per group were fixed for 60 min in electron microscopy fixative (2.5% glutaraldehyde and 2% paraformaldehyde in 0.1 M Na-cacodylate buffer, PH 7.3) and prepared for transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The ovaries were cut into ~1 mm3 pieces using a scalpel. After dehydration in an ascending ethanol series, the ovarian tissues were fixed in osmium tetroxide and embedded in epoxy resin. The sections were examined after Reynold’s lead citrate staining. Follicles at the same developmental stage with visible oocyte nuclei were randomly selected, and images were obtained using a transmission electron microscope (CM 10 Philips, Eindhoven, The Netherlands) at 80 kV.

RNA extraction and quantitative reverse transcription-PCR

Total RNA was isolated from the ovaries using TRIzol. The RNA concentration was measured with a NanoDrop spectrophotometer (λ = 260/280 nm; ND 1000; NanoDrop Technologies, Wilmington, DE, USA). Equal amount of RNA (2 μg) was used for quantitative reverse transcription-PCR, which was conducted using an AB StepOne Plus PCR machine (Applied Biosystems, Foster City, CA). The PCR reaction parameters were set as follows. Initial denaturation: 95 °C for 3 min; Cycling (35 cycles) including Denaturation: 95 °C for 15 s, Annealing: 60 °C for 20 s, Extension: 72 °C for 30 s; Final extension: 72 °C for 5 min; Hold: 4 °C. Gapdh was utilized to compute the relative transcript levels of the target genes. Supplementary Table 2 includes a list of primer sequences, and primer pairs were validated for efficiency (95–105%) using a 5-point standard curve of cDNA dilution series (R² >0.99).

Western blotting

Cells were lysed in RIPA buffer (RadiolmmunoPrecipitation Assay Buffer; containing protease/phosphatase inhibitors) on ice for 30 min, followed by centrifugation (16,000 × g, 25 min, 4 °C) to collect supernatants. Protein concentration was determined via Coomassie Brilliant Blue. Samples were diluted with Loading Buffer, denatured at 95 °C for 8 min, and 30 μg per well was loaded onto SDS-PAGE gels. After electrophoresis, proteins were transferred to PVDF membranes, blocked with 5% bovine serum albumin (BSA), and incubated with primary antibodies (overnight, 4 °C) and HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies (1 h, RT). The primary antibodies used are as follows: RhoBTB1 (1:500 dilution), FGF18 (1:500 dilution), JNK (1:1000 dilution), p-JNK (1:1000 dilution), ERK (1:1000 dilution), p-ERK (1:1000 dilution), p38 (1:1000 dilution), p-p38 (1:1000 dilution), BAX (1:500 dilution), BCL-2 (1:500 dilution), active Caspase-3 (1:500 dilution), CTSK (1:500 dilution). The secondary antibody used included HRP-conjugated goat anti-rabbit IgG (1:2000 dilution). Signals were visualized using enhanced chemiluminescence (ECL) substrate and captured via a ChemiDoc imaging system (Bio-Rad), followed by densitometric analysis using Image Lab software. β-ACTIN was used as the internal control. Source data are provided as a Source Data file.

Immunohistochemical staining

For each group, five mice were selected, and five sections from the whole ovarian tissue of each mouse were used for immunohistochemical (IHC) staining and subsequent data analysis. Ovarian sections were prepared for IHC staining using a routine IHC procedure. The primary antibodies against BMP15 (1:200 dilution), CAT (1:200 dilution), SOD2 (1:200 dilution), BAX (1:200 dilution), HSD17B7 (1:200 dilution), and RhoBTB1 (1:200 dilution) were used on the sections. The following morning, the ovary sections were incubated with goat anti-rabbit antibody (1:2000 dilution) for 1 h at 37 °C. Ovarian visibility under a microscope was accomplished using diaminobenzidine. The sections were then sealed with a neutral glue after being counterstained with hematoxylin for 3 min. A microscope was used to capture the images (Olympus, BX51). IHC scores based on integrated optical density (IOD)/Area were computed and used to assess the relative expression of associated proteins using Image-Pro Plus.

RNA sequencing

Ovary tissues from F3 generation (n = 4 for each group) were included in the RNA sequencing. Total RNA was isolated from ovaries utilizing a previously mentioned procedure. The A260/A280 ratio was measured to assess RNA purity. A 1.5% agarose gel electrophoresis was employed to confirm RNA integrity. Qubit 3.0 and the QubitTM RNA Broad Range Assay Kit from Life Technologies were used to measure approved RNAs.

Following the manufacturer’s instructions, total RNA (2 μg) was utilized to prepare the stranded RNA sequencing library using the KCTM Stranded mRNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina® (DR08402). Using the Novaseq 6000 sequencer (Illumina), PCR products measuring 200–500 bps were isolated, quantified, and sequenced.

Raw RNA-seq reads were cleaned and assessed for quality. The remaining reads were mapped to the reference mouse genome (mm10) using STAR (v2.7.4) with default parameters, and the expression levels of all genes were determined. The function “ComBat-seq” in the sva package (v3.38.0) was applied to the raw read counts to correct for the batch effect of biological experiments and sequencing. Genes differentially expressed between groups were identified using the DESeq2 package (v1.30.1) with a cutoff of p-value < 0.05, and |log2(Fold Change)| >1. KEGG analysis for differentially expressed genes was performed using the clusterProfiler package (v3.18.1) with a p-value cutoff of 0.05.

Whole genome bisulfite sequencing of ovarian tissues

Ovary tissues from F2 generation (n = 3 for each group) and F3 generation (Con n = 3, PrP n = 4) were included in the whole genome bisulfite sequencing. Total DNA was extracted from samples using the Hi-DNAsecure Plant Kit (Tiangen, DP350). Quality was verified by 0.8% agarose gel electrophoresis. 2–3 μg DNA was sonicated to 200–500 bp fragments. The conversion of unmethylated cytosines to thymidines of 1 μg fragmented DNA was carried out using the EZ DNA Methylation-Gold™ Kit (Zymo, D5005). Sequencing libraries were prepared with the Accel-NGS® Methyl-Seq DNA Library Kit (Swift, 30024). PCR products corresponding to 300–500 bp were enriched, quantified and finally sequenced on NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina).

Single-cell whole genome bisulfite sequencing of oocytes

Oocytes from F2 generation (n = 3 samples for each group, 12 oocytes per sample) were included in the single-cell whole genome bisulfite sequencing. Oocyte samples was resuspended in 1× M-Digestion Buffer from EZ DNA Methylation-DirectTM kit (Zymo, D5021) and digested with proteinase K for 20 min at 50 °C. Digested oocyte samples were directly subjected to bisulfite treatment followed the rest step of EZ DNA Methylation-DirectTM kit. Sequencing libraries were prepared using the Accel-NGS® Methyl-Seq DNA Library kit (Swift, 30024). Library corresponding to 300–500 bp were enriched, quantified and sequenced on NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina).

Data analysis of whole genome bisulfite sequencing

Trim Galore (v0.6.6) was used first to process the raw data to eliminate any remaining adaptor sequences and reads with poor quality ratings. Duplicate reads were then eliminated using Bismark (v0.22.3), and clean reads were mapped to the reference genome (mm10). The CpG methylation level along the whole genome at base resolution was calculated using Bismark. Bedtools (v2.29.2) was used to convert the bam files to the bigwig format for UCSC genome browser viewing. The DMRs between different groups were detected using the DSS package (v2.38.0) with a significant 10% methylation difference and p ≤ 0.05. DMRs were mapped to genes using the ChIPseeker package (v1.26.2) with promoter regions of −3 kb to 3 kb around the TSS. Circos diagrams were drawn using the circlize package (v0.4.13) to show the distribution of DMRs across chromosomes. Alluvial diagrams of methylation variation between the generations were plotted using the ggalluvial package (v0.12.3) in ggplot2 (v3.3.5).

Exposure of KGN cells to PrP

The KGN cell line was purchased from the Hycyte (TCH-C230), which derived from the granulosa cell tumor of a 69-year-old woman. These cells were authenticated by the supplier with STR profiling, and cultured in DMEM/F12 medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. For cryopreservation, the cells were stored in liquid nitrogen in a solution containing 70% base medium, 20% FBS, and 10% DMSO. KGN cells were incubated for 24 h and exposed to PrP at 0 μM, 50 μM, and 100 μM. After 48 h treatment, the cells were harvested for flow cytometric analysis to measure apoptosis and for western blotting of proteins. We collected the cell supernatants for ELISA detection.

In vitro analysis of apoptosis in GCs

The FITC-Annexin V Apoptosis Detection Kit I was used to measure apoptosis by fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) per manufacturer’s instructions. PrP was added to KGN cells in varying concentrations (0 μM, 50 μM, and 100 μM). After 48 h treatment, the cells were collected with trypsin, washed in PBS, and incubated with Annexin V-propidium iodide (PI) for 15 min. The percentage of Annexin V-positive cells was measured using a flow cytometer (BD LSRFortessaTM X-20). The percentage of apoptotic cells was calculated in the upper and lower right quadrants by FlowJo (v10.8.1).

Immunofluorescence staining of oocytes

Oocytes were fixed in paraformaldehyde for 30 min. The oocytes were first penetrated with 0.5% Triton X-100 for 20 min, blocked with 1% bovine serum albumin (BSA) for 1 h, and incubated with the primary RhoBTB1 antibody overnight at 4 °C (1:200 dilution). The following morning, the oocytes were treated with Alexa Fluor® 488-conjugated Goat Anti-Rabbit IgG (1:500 dilution) for 1 h at 37 °C. DAPI (4,6-diamino-2-phenyl indole) was used as a counterstain on the oocytes for 15 min at 37 °C. A microscope was used to observe the expression of RhoBTB1, which was greenly fluorescein-labeled against a blue background (Olympus, BX51).

Adenovirus infection and siRNA transduction

KGN cells were infected with adenovirus-encoding Rhobtb1 (DesignGene Biotechnology, Shanghai, China) at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 25 for 12 h, and adenovirus-puromycin was used as a control. RhoBTB1 was detected by western blotting. SiRNA oligos (RiboBio) were transfected into cells using Lipofectamine 3000 Transfection Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific L3000015) following the manufacturer’s recommendations. Total protein was extracted after 48 h to determine the most suitable sequence. The Rhobtb1 silencing targeted sequence was CCTGGAAGCTGTTAATCGA. The Fgf18 silencing targeted sequence was TCGAGAAGGTTCTGGAGAA.

Ubiquitination assays

KGN cells were treated with PrP at concentrations of 0 μM and 100 μM for 48 h. To assess ubiquitination, cells were pre-treated with either MLN4924 (1 μM for 24 h) or MG132 (10 μM for 4 h) prior to harvesting for western blotting analysis. The same experimental protocol was applied to the KGN cells overexpression RhoBTB1.

Next-generation sequencing-based bisulfite sequencing PCR

According to a previously described technique, gene-specific DNA methylation was evaluated by BSP. The online MethPrimer software was used to create the BSP primers for the target genes (Supplementary Table 3). All samples’ barcoded libraries were sequenced on a platform of Illumina. At a p-value of 0.05, differences in the levels of methylation (>0.05) between the various groups were deemed significant.

Human case-control study population

Women were screened for Control (n = 60), DOR (n = 63), and POI (n = 60) diagnosis at the Department of Obstetrics and Gynecology, Tongji Hospital, Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology, Wuhan, China. Women diagnosed with DOR met the criteria of having AMH below 1.1 ng/ml, and/or the antral follicle count (AFC) fewer than 6 and/or basal follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) levels exceeding 10 U/L. The classification of women with POI was based on the Consensus of clinical diagnosis and treatment of premature ovarian insufficiency (2023). This classification includes women under 40 years of age who experience amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea for a duration exceeding 4 months, and exhibit FSH levels greater than 25 U/L on two separate occasions with a 4-week interval, with or without accompanying symptoms of low estrogen. The inclusion criteria for the control group were as follows: age <40 years, regular menstruation, normal sex hormone levels, and no history of ovarian surgery. The exclusion criteria were as follows: currently pregnant or lactating, use of exogenous sex hormones in the past three months, history of serious organic diseases requiring long-term medication, and current participation in other clinical trials. Peripheral blood of the participating women was collected, and DNA was extracted for BSP. The participants’ demographic information is provided in Supplementary Table 4. Witten informed consent was obtained from all donors.

Statistical analyses

Data in the bar plots are shown as the mean ± SEM. The continuous variables were analyzed using unpaired two-tailed t-test (GraphPad Prism v9.0) or one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) to compare differences between groups (SPSS Statistics v24.0). For categorical variables, Fisher’s exact test or Chi-squared test was utilized (GraphPad Prism v9.0). P-value < 0.05 were considered to be statistically significant.
