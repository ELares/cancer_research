# Overcoming resistance to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy: mechanisms, combination strategies, and future directions.

## Abstract

Cancer cells express high levels of programmed cell death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) to evade immune surveillance. PD-L1 interacts with PD-1 on T cells to make them non-functional. Thus, PD-L1 and PD-1 are pivotal targets in cancer immunotherapy. While anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies have offered renewed hope for many patients, their modest efficacy remains a critical concern. This underscores the urgent need to unravel the intricate mechanisms that govern both therapeutic responses as well as resistance to immunotherapy. This review explores the multifaceted nature of PD-L1, including factors that regulate its expression, tumor-immune interactions, and the resistance mechanisms associated with anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Several promising strategies have been explored to overcome these challenges, such as combination therapies, modulation of the tumor microenvironment, neoantigen targeting, and dynamic biomarker monitoring. Outcomes of these approaches, integrating advanced technologies like high-resolution imaging, machine learning, multi-omics profiling, and liquid biopsy for soluble PD-L1 detection emerge as a powerful means to refine patient stratification. Together, these innovations pave the way toward more precise and personalized immunotherapy, maximizing clinical benefits for cancer patients. Additionally, the evolving landscape of clinical trials involving anti-PD-1/PD-L1 monoclonal antibodies has been explored, emphasizing the integration of immune checkpoint therapies with chemotherapy, radiotherapy, targeted therapy, CAR-T, and metabolic immunotherapy to overcome resistance in refractory cancers. By embarking on these challenges and leveraging novel therapeutic strategies, this review intends to advance the understanding of more effective, personalized cancer immunotherapies, ultimately improving outcomes for a broader range of patients.

Introduction

Cancer is characterized by uncontrolled cell proliferation resulting from dynamic alterations in the genome and post-genomic events, leading to the formation of tumor tissues. Cancer cells from the tumor can invade neighboring tissues, spread to distant organs, and form secondary tumors, a process known as metastasis. Cancer is the second leading cause of death worldwide. Approximately 100 different types of cancer are detected in humans, with breast cancer having the highest incidence, while lung cancer is the deadliest cancer among all human malignancies. Several therapeutic strategies have been adopted to treat cancer; however, therapeutic responses to various cancers vary significantly due to intra- and inter-tumor heterogeneity and numerous other factors. Traditional therapeutic strategies include surgery, hormone therapy, chemotherapy, and targeted therapy aimed at impairing the function of oncogenic proteins within cancer cells. Despite advancements in these therapies, frequent disease recurrence and severe side effects remain significant challenges, underscoring the need for personalized therapeutic regimens. 

One significant barrier to effective cancer treatment is immune evasion, a critical hallmark of cancer cells. The immune system’s involvement in cancer development is a multifaceted and dynamic process that includes a range of protective and adaptive mechanisms. This interaction is best described by the “three Es” concept (elimination, equilibrium, and escape), which collectively form the “Cancer Immunity Cycle”. In the elimination phase, immune cells destroy malignant cells. If some tumor cells survive this initial response, they enter the equilibrium phase, during which the immune system controls tumor growth without completely eradicating it. Over time, however, cancer cells may acquire mechanisms to resist immune attacks, leading to the escape phase, where they evade immune surveillance and continue to grow unchecked. To further refine the understanding of immune evasion, researchers have more recently introduced the “three Cs” concept. The “three Cs” concept—camouflage, coercion, and cytoprotection—explains how tumors achieve immune evasion. Camouflage conceals cancer from immune detection, coercion suppresses immune responses, and cytoprotection protects tumors from immune-mediated destruction, making these mechanisms critical targets for cancer immunotherapy.

A breakthrough in cancer treatment emerged with the advent of immunotherapy, which seeks to restore the immune system’s ability to combat cancer. Immunotherapy enhances the cytotoxic activity of CD8+ T cells to eliminate cancer cells, offering a promising direction for improving therapeutic outcomes. The significance of this therapy was recognized by the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2018, awarded to James P. Allison and Tasuku Honjo for their groundbreaking contributions to immune checkpoint inhibition. Their discoveries laid the foundation for therapies targeting CTLA-4 (cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4) and PD-1 (Programmed cell death protein 1)/PD-L1 (programmed cell death-ligand 1), revolutionizing cancer treatment.

Immune evasion in cancer – contribution of CTLA-4

The primary function of CTLA-4 is to prevent excessive immunological responses and maintain immune system equilibrium by controlling T-cell activation. It has been proposed that CTLA-4 accomplishes this by reducing T-cell activation and restricting their ability to attack healthy cells. Mechanistically, CTLA-4 is highly expressed in Treg cells, and its binding to CD80/86 induces dendritic cells (DCs) to produce the enzyme indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), which breaks down tryptophan and thus prevents T-cell activation. In addition, due to its high affinity for CD80/CD86, CTLA-4 inhibits CD28-mediated co-stimulation of T cells by obstructing the interaction between T cells and antigen-presenting cells. CTLA-4 is minimally expressed intracellularly but dramatically increases on the cell surface upon activation. However, it is constitutively expressed in activated Treg cells, preventing T-cell activation in the cancer microenvironment and facilitating immune evasion.

In 2011, a breakthrough was achieved in cancer immunotherapy. For the first time, the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved the use of ipilimumab (Yervoy™, Bristol-Meyers Squibb, New York, NY), a monoclonal antibody that blocks the CTLA-4 protein, to treat advanced or metastatic melanoma. It is the first immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) used for cancer therapy. This approval accelerated clinical progress for ICIs and other immuno-oncology (IO) therapies.

Immune evasion in cancer—contribution of PD-L1

Under normal homeostatic conditions, cells express PD-L1 primarily during excessive immune reactions. PD-L1 subsequently interacts with the PD-1 receptor present on activated T cells. This interaction inhibits T-cell proliferation, cytokine production, and effector functions. PD-L1 is an inhibitory immune checkpoint molecule that promotes peripheral immune tolerance. Therefore, PD-L1 acts as a brake in the immune system (Fig. 1).

In contrast, cancer cells highly express PD-L1 on their surface through several mechanisms to evade immune attack. Highly activated PD-L1 binds to the PD-1 receptor on activated T cells, enabling immune response evasion in the tumor microenvironment (TME). To date, based on the success of clinical trials, the FDA has approved several ICIs for the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway: pembrolizumab (Keytruda), nivolumab (Opdivo), cemiplimab (Libtayo), and toripalimab (approved in China, not in the United States) for PD-1 and atezolizumab (Tecentriq), durvalumab (Imfinzi), and avelumab (Bavencio) for PD-L1 (Table 1). 

In this review, we have discussed the challenges of using PD-L1 as a biomarker for immunotherapy, the influence of sex bias on immunotherapy responses, the reasons behind poor responses in many patients, combination strategies to enhance immunotherapy responses, and the exploration of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and multi-omics approaches in predicting immunotherapy responses, along with future perspectives for improving anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy. Finally, we highlight key open questions in the field, emphasizing that a deeper understanding of immune evasion mechanisms is essential for the development of effective cancer immunotherapies.

Biomarker dilemma: PD-L1 expression levels

In clinical settings, the primary factor considered for using anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy treatment in cancer depends on the expression level of PD-L1 present in the patient's tumor. The use of PD-L1 as a biomarker has played a central role in the field of cancer immunotherapy. Its expression has been used as a predictive biomarker to select patients who are likely to respond to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Higher PD-L1 expression often correlates with better responses to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapies. However, it is not a perfect predictor, as some patients with low or no PD-L1 expression also respond to treatment, and vice versa. The Society for Immunotherapy of Cancer (SITC) highlights the key biomarkers needed in immunotherapy trials to better match patients with the right treatments and improve outcomes. These essential biomarkers—like the neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR), serum lactate dehydrogenase (LDH), PD-L1 expression, microsatellite stability, and tumor mutational burden (TMB)—were chosen because they are widely relevant and practical to measure. SITC also emphasizes the need to standardize biomarker testing across trials and plans to update these guidelines regularly to keep pace with advances in immunotherapy (Fig. 2).

Despite promising applications of anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy, one of the major challenges lies in the heterogeneity of its expression within tumors. PD-L1 expression levels vary in different regions of the same tumor as well as other metastatic sites in the same patient. A large cohort of lung cancer patient samples revealed that PD-L1 expression is much higher in metastatic sites (lymph nodes, pleural fluid, soft tissue and the adrenal gland) than in primary tumor sites. In addition, the authors reported that PD-L1 expression varies with histological subtype, sex, and sampling site, indicating that careful assessment of PD-L1 expression is crucial for determining the accuracy of immunotherapy.

The FDA has approved several companion diagnostic assays for the evaluation of PD-L1 expression via immunohistochemical staining of tumor tissue sections. Several scoring systems have also been devised to evaluate PD-L1 expression; however, the tumor proportion score (TPS) and the combined positive score (CPS) are widely used. The TPS assesses the proportion of viable tumor cells displaying PD-L1 staining compared with the total number of viable tumor cells. In contrast, the CPS assesses the proportion of both PD-L1-stained tumor and immune cells compared with the total number of viable tumor cells.

The TPS versus the CPS varies between different cancer types and immunotherapies, adding to the complexity of using PD-L1 as a biomarker (Table 2). Additionally, the detection of PD-L1 expression varies with different detection antibodies against PD-L1 (e.g., 22C3, 28–8, SP263, and SP142). Variations in how tissue is processed and methods for scoring PD-L1 expression make the test less consistent and reliable. In response to these challenges, efforts are underway to standardize PD-L1 testing, including harmonization initiatives and consensus guidelines from organizations such as the European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO) and the College of American Pathologists (CAP) to reduce the inter-assay variability and improve clinical decision-making. ESMO emphasizes the need for standardized protocols and training to reduce variability in PD-L1 testing. They advocate for the adoption of validated assays and adherence to established guidelines to ensure accurate and reliable PD-L1 assessment, which is crucial for selecting appropriate patients for immunotherapy. Further, the Blueprint study found that the 22C3, 28–8, and SP263 assays had similar tumor cell staining patterns, whereas SP142 showed lower staining intensity. Thus, prescribing anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy is a very challenging decision. Nonetheless, numerous clinical trials have been conducted to harness the full potential of this groundbreaking treatment for various cancers. Notably, the approval of many monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) for clinical use underscores the promise of immunotherapy. However, the response rates for anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies may not be as high as initially anticipated. This realization has prompted a shift in focus toward a more targeted and personalized approach. Given the significant cost of these treatments, identifying the patients who stand to benefit the most from immunotherapy has become increasingly crucial. 

Therefore, clinicians and researchers need to consider multiple factors, including tumor type, patient history, and the specific immunotherapy to be used. In addition, researchers are also actively exploring the utilization of additional biomarkers, such as high TMB, microsatellite instability (MSI), and mismatch repair deficiency (dMMR) to determine whether anti-PD-1/PD-L1 treatment can improve patients’ outcomes. Tailoring immunotherapy to the right individuals would offer a more efficient and effective way to leverage the potential of these therapies, ultimately leading to improved outcomes and a more cost-effective healthcare system.

A crucial step toward this personalized approach is to understand the factors that influence a patient's likelihood of responding to immunotherapy. One of the most critical factors is tumor immunogenicity. Tumor immunogenicity refers to the ability of a tumor to provoke an immune response, primarily by presenting recognizable antigens that alert and activate immune cells. Tumor immunogenicity and the capacity of T cells to target cancer cells are positively correlated, a relationship that underpins the effectiveness of immunotherapy. TMB has emerged as a key determinant among the factors that positively influence tumor immunogenicity. Tumors with elevated TMB are postulated to generate more neoantigens, resulting in increased tumor-specific T cell infiltration. Several studies suggest a positive correlation between TMB and improved responses to T-cell-directed immunotherapies. It is reported that colorectal cancer with dMMR (deficient mismatch repair), which usually has a higher TMB, shows a greater response to anti-PD-1 therapy. Conversely, patients with a low TMB may experience diminished efficacy in response to ICIs. Recently, Cai et al. developed a comprehensive pan-cancer immunopeptidome atlas, identifying both canonical and noncanonical HLA-presented peptides from 531 tumor and normal tissue samples. They introduced a machine learning pipeline, MaNeo, to prioritize immunogenic neoantigens for personalized cancer immunotherapy. Experimental validation showed that selected neo-peptides effectively activated CD8+ T cells and killed tumor cells without affecting healthy cells. Therefore, the identification of these neoantigens or how they are involved in the cancer immunity cycle may help to identify novel predictive additional biomarkers. Therefore, prospective randomized trials are needed to determine the roles of predictive biomarkers in specific clinical settings and enhance their effectiveness in guiding personalized treatment strategies.

Building on the importance of identifying robust biomarkers such as neoantigens and TMB, recent efforts have also compared a broader range of predictive tools to refine patient selection for immunotherapy. A systematic review and meta-analysis by Lu et al. compared four biomarkers—PD-L1 IHC, TMB, gene expression profiling (GEP), and multiplex immunohistochemistry/immunofluorescence (mIHC/IF)—for predicting response to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibitors. Multiplex IHC/IF demonstrated the highest predictive accuracy due to its ability to assess multiple immune markers with spatial context. The study suggests that mIHC/IF may outperform commonly used biomarkers like PD-L1 IHC in guiding immunotherapy decisions. Meanwhile, the SITC has published best practice guidelines for implementing and validating mIHC/IF, aiming to ensure reliable, reproducible results across research and clinical settings.

Though anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy potently restores the immune responses in the TME, the use of mAbs to inhibit PD-L1 can potentially disrupt immune tolerance, leading to the manifestation of autoimmune responses, termed immune-related adverse events (irAEs). irAEs can affect any organ, including the skin, gastrointestinal tract, endocrine system, liver, and lungs (Fig. 3A). While irAEs are generally manageable, they can be severe or even life-threatening in some cases. Therefore, achieving a delicate balance between therapeutic efficacy and minimizing irAEs is a critical concern for clinicians. A crucial step in achieving a balance between the efficacy and toxicity of immunotherapeutic drugs is selecting an appropriate anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapeutic drug regimen. In addition, patients’ physiological conditions, such as baseline immune status, comorbidities, and tumor type, should be also considered when selecting patients who are most likely to benefit from treatment while minimizing the risk of severe irAEs. Thus, tailoring treatment plans for the patient’s unique profile is crucial. The plans may involve dose adjustments, treatment pauses, or the use of corticosteroids or other immunosuppressive agents to manage irAEs while maintaining therapeutic efficacy (Fig. 3B). In addition to these irAEs, the clinical success of anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies is Limited to only 15–25% of cancer patients; the majority of cancer patients either do not respond to treatment or develop resistance to treatment. Thus, our knowledge of immunotherapy success and failure is limited. Therefore, there is a pressing need to understand the detailed mechanism of PD-L1 regulation at the epigenetic, transcriptional, and posttranslational levels so that it can be targeted effectively in a precise manner to improve clinical efficacy.

Sex biases in anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy response

Studies show that males have a higher incidence of most malignancies and nearly twice the mortality risk compared to females in non-sex-specific cancers. This disparity is especially pronounced in lung, esophageal, and bladder cancers, where biological differences such as hormonal influences and immune system variations impact disease progression and treatment outcomes. Notably, sex also plays a crucial role in cancer incidence, mortality, and response to ICIs, particularly PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors, emphasizing the need for sex-specific considerations in immunotherapy.

Meta-analyses and clinical studies reveal that males and females respond differently to these treatments, likely due to variations in immune activation, tumor microenvironment composition, and genetic factors. Females with stronger T-cell-mediated immunity may experience better responses to ICIs but also face a higher risk of irAEs, whereas males having more immunosuppressive tumor environments may exhibit lower response rates. Additionally, estrogen has been shown to enhance PD-L1 expression and suppress the immune functions of T-cells, further shaping immunotherapy outcomes in females. Building on this, a recent study investigated the role of the NOTCH1 signaling pathway in shaping gender-specific immune responses in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). The researchers found that NOTCH1 activation creates distinct immune environments in male and female HCC patients, contributing to differences in tumor progression and response to immunotherapies. Another study shows that loss of the Y chromosome (LOY) in both cancer cells and T cells is linked to worse outcomes in male cancer patients. LOY is associated with immune suppression, particularly in T cells, which show transcriptomic signs of exhaustion and dysfunction, while LOY in tumor cells promotes aggressiveness and immune evasion.

An interesting recent study by Zhu et al. reveals that microbiota-induced neutrophil senescence contributes to sexual disparity in bladder cancer. The authors identified a subpopulation of senescence-like neutrophils that preferentially accumulate in the male tumor microenvironment, suppressing antitumor T-cell activity and promoting tumor progression. Further, authors found that gut bacterium Alistipes shahii is more abundant in females that produce the metabolite lurasidone. This metabolite targets the iron-sequestering protein lipocalin-2 (LCN2) in these neutrophils to eliminate them through ferroptosis and thereby enhances antitumor immunity. These findings highlight a microbiota–metabolite–immune axis with potential therapeutic implications for male cancer patients.

Given these findings, understanding sex-based differences in PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy response across different cancer is essential for developing more effective and personalized treatment strategies. Future research should prioritize incorporating sex as a biological variable in both preclinical and clinical studies to uncover the mechanisms driving differential responses and resistance. Large-scale, sex-stratified clinical trials will be crucial in determining whether tailored dosing, combination therapies, or alternative treatment approaches can optimize outcomes for both male and female patients.

Resistance mechanisms associated with anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy

Anti-PD-L1 immunotherapies activate T-cells and restore the immune system by blocking the interaction between PD-1 on T cells and PD-L1 on tumor cells. However, like other therapies, cancer cells also encounter resistance or relapse to PD-L1 immunotherapy, and several mechanisms are associated with this phenomenon. One of the primary mechanisms of resistance to PD-L1 immunotherapies is the underexpression of PD-L1 by tumor cells within the TME. Therefore, cancers are classified into four types based on the expression of PD-L1 and the presence of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) in TMEs: (I) PD-L1+/TIL+ (type I); (II) PD-L1−/TIL− (type II); (III) PD-L1+/TIL− (type III); and (IV) PD-L1−/TIL + (type IV). Further, according to clinical outcomes, resistance to anti-PD-L1 therapy can be classified as primary and acquired (or secondary).

In a clinical scenario, when patients fail to respond to therapy at the onset of treatment, it is considered primary resistance. Primary resistance to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy remains an active area of research, and new findings have emerged since then. Acquired resistance to anti-PD-L1 therapy refers to the development of resistance after certain periods of ongoing treatment wherein patients respond to the initial phase of treatment. Thus, gaining more insight into the mechanisms associated with cancer immunotherapy resistance is important to broaden the clinical implications of PD-L1 ICI in cancer immunotherapy. In addition, understanding the mechanisms underlying primary as well as acquired resistance is crucial for developing strategies to overcome this hurdle. In the following sections, the factors contributing to the development of primary and secondary resistances during PD-L1 blockade therapy are discussed:

Anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy resistance due to a lack of or low PD-L1 expression

It is observed that patients with high levels of PD-L1-expressing tumor cells, along with immune cell infiltration (type I), exhibit a better overall response rate (ORR) and progression-free survival (PFS). Thus, PD-L1 levels and TILs are essential for the success of anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Although patients with high levels of PD-L1 respond very well to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy but a considerable number of patients do not respond to this treatment, indicating that PD-L1 expression may not be the sole determinant for anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy response. In contrast to high-level, low-level or undetectable PD-L1-expressing tumors (Type II) are resistant to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Shin and colleagues further support this link between low PD-L1 levels and a poorer response to PD-1 blockade. They reported that JAK1/2 mutations led to a loss of PD-L1 expression in melanoma, and patients failed to respond to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy, indicating that PD-L1 is essential for effective response to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Similarly, it is reported that epithelial ovarian cancer patients with low PD-L1 expression are associated with unfavorable outcomes from anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy (Fig. 4A). Therefore, assessing PD-L1 levels is essential for optimizing patient selection and improving the success rate of anti-PD-L1 therapies.

Absence of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs)

The presence or absence of TILs within the tumor microenvironment can be an important indicator of the immune system response as well as the effectiveness of immune therapies. TILs in the tumor microenvironment are crucial for recognizing and attacking cancer cells. A lack of TILs may indicate an “immunologically cold” tumor with low immune activity and correlated with poorer prognosis cancers such as melanoma, breast cancer, and colorectal cancer. Therefore, enhancing T-cell infiltration into tumors to improve the effectiveness of anti-PD-L1 therapies remains a major challenge. Several studies have identified multiple mechanisms associated with blocking cytotoxic T-cell infiltration in tumors.

A hostile TME could be less conducive to lymphocyte survival and function. The hostile TME is characterized by low oxygen levels (hypoxia), acidic pH, and the presence of immunosuppressive cells such as regulatory T cells (Tregs) or myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) (Fig. 4B). Clinically, the absence of TILs is often associated with a poorer prognosis. Thus, tumors with low TIL levels might be more aggressive and capable of effectively evading the immune response.

Therefore, understanding the mechanisms associated with the absence of TILs in the TME would help to increase the permissiveness of immune cell infiltration and develop targeted therapies. Such strategies include using drugs to alter the immunosuppressive conditions or directly enhancing TIL migration to and activity within the tumor site. Tailoring immunotherapies on the basis of the presence or absence of TILs and modifying the tumor environment to support immune cell activity are active areas of immunooncology research that hold promise for improving cancer treatment outcomes. However, blocking these mechanisms in human cancer patients has yet to be thoroughly tested across different cancers.

Inhibition of antigen presentation

Antigen presentation is a critical process for an effective immune response against cancer. Major histocompatibility complex (MHC) I molecules present the antigens from cancer cells, thereby activating T cells to eliminate cancer cells. The disruption of this process is linked to immunotherapy resistance. The mechanisms associated with antigen presentation impairment in cancer are discussed in the following section:

Loss or downregulation of MHC-I molecules

MHC-I is responsible for antigen presentation to cytotoxic T cells. Therefore, deregulation of the components of MHC-I impairs their ability to present neoantigens to TILs (Fig. 4C.a). Tumor cells deregulate MHC-I to hide themselves from the immune system attack in several ways. Previous studies have reported that the beta-2-microglobulin (B2M) component of MHC-I is inactivated in melanoma due to mutation, deletion or loss of heterozygosity (LOH), and the frequency of B2M inactivation is significantly higher in non-responder groups of immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapies (Fig. 4C.b). LOH in the B2M gene (B2M LOH) was three times more common in ICB therapy non-responders (~ 30%) than in responders (~ 10%). This genetic alteration was also linked to worse overall survival. Similarly, lung cancer patients with LOH or downregulation of B2M are resistant to immune checkpoint PD-1/PD-L1 blockade therapy. The importance of B2M was further validated via CRISPR technology in a mouse model of lung cancer, and loss of B2M led to resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 therapy. These observations underscore the importance of B2M deregulation in immune checkpoint blockade therapy resistance and highlight the potential implications for patient stratification.

A recent study identified macbecin II [Inhibits Heat shock protein 90 (Hsp90)] as a promising agent that counteracts this immune evasion by upregulating MHC-I expression in solid tumors, including breast cancer and melanoma. It was observed that macbecin II restores antigen presentation and enhances T cell-mediated tumor killing by blocking lysosomal degradation of MHC-I. This pharmacologic restoration of MHC-I significantly potentiates the effects of both active immunotherapies (e.g., dendritic cell-derived vesicle vaccines) and anti-PD-1 therapies, highlighting its potential to overcome immunotherapy resistance linked to MHC-I and B2M downregulation. Further studies are needed to identify strategies that can similarly overcome immune evasion in cases of B2M LOH. Additionally, similar regulatory mechanisms have been observed in prostate cancer, where androgen receptor (AR) inhibition was shown to relieve repression of MHC-I expression, further underscoring the therapeutic value of targeting MHC-I modulators in immune-resistant cancers.

Notably, studies across various cancer patient cohorts have shown that defects in MHC-I antigen presentation contribute to resistance against ICB therapy. However, the extent and prevalence of this resistance mechanism remain to be fully understood.

Alteration in antigen processing machinery

MHC-I molecules are inherently unstable without binding with antigens. Thus, tumor cells deregulate the antigen presentation process to evade the immune response by deregulating the components of the antigen processing machinery. It is evident that several components (TAP, Tapasin, and ERAP1) of the antigen presentation Machinery are deregulated in cancer. A previous study reported that the transporter associated with antigen processing 1 (TAP1) downregulation is closely associated with immune escape and poor prognosis in several cancers, including colorectal cancer. Similarly, cancer cells escape the immune system due to underexpression of Tapasin, an essential component of the peptide-loading complex of MHC-I. Defects in the antigen presentation process are linked to the loss of TAP, which further impacts T-cell priming (Fig. 4C.c).

In addition to these well-characterized components of the antigen presentation machinery, recent attention has turned toward the role of protein degradation pathways in regulating immune recognition. In particular, the ubiquitin–proteasome system (UPS), particularly E3 ligases, plays a crucial regulatory role in substrate specificity within the UPS, controlling the degradation of key immune-related proteins, including those involved in antigen processing. Aberrant activity of specific E3 ligases can lead to modifications of immunopeptidome (the complete set of peptides presented by major histocompatibility complexes), further weakening immune surveillance. This disruption suggests that a malfunctioning proteasomal pathway, which hinders effective antigen presentation, is a key factor in the resistance observed in immunotherapy treatments. Therefore, future studies must explore how proteasomal dysfunction and dysregulated E3 ligase activity contribute to immune evasion and therapy resistance. In addition, future studies also need to identify modulators or develop specific inhibitors of deregulated E3 ligases that could restore proper antigen presentation, enhance tumor immunogenicity, and improve current immunotherapies. Therefore, continued research in understanding the mechanisms of antigen presentation and their role in immunotherapy resistance would be important to develop more effective cancer treatments.

Immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment

The TME is composed of cancer cells, immune cells and stromal cells and acts as a crucial contributor to promote the outgrowth of cancer. The interplay between these cells and various other immunosuppressive factors creates an immunologically cold TME to favor cancer progression. Therefore, understanding the TME in detail and its ability to rewire the immune system to make “cold tumors” into “hot tumors” will have a substantial impact on influencing the response to immunotherapy. In addition, it is crucial to identify other essential regulators associated with diverse immune evasion tactics employed by cancer cells within the TME and target them to restore anti-tumor immune response. Therefore, intense studies are going on in this field.

The effectiveness of PD-L1 blockade, as well as other ICIs, is influenced by various factors within TME. It was observed that the presence of suppressive immune cells, cytokine profiles, and TME heterogeneity can influence the efficiency of PD-L1 blockade therapy. The immune response against tumors is impeded by the existence of immunosuppressive cells, including cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), MDSCs, Tregs and M2 macrophages in the TME. Tregs suppress effector T cells, whereas MDSCs inhibit the activation of immune cells. Lamorte et al. reveals that medullary sinus macrophages in lymph nodes produce IL-33 in response to apoptotic tumor cells, that promote immune tolerance and resistance to cancer therapy. Mechanistically, it was observed that IL-33 activates Tregs, suppressing anti-tumor immunity, and thereby weakening the effectiveness of treatments like chemotherapy and targeted therapy. Therefore, blocking IL-33 enhances immune responses, suggesting that it could be a potential target to improve cancer therapy outcomes.

Even if PD-L1 on tumor cells is blocked, the overall suppressive environment may still impede the anti-tumor immune activity. In addition, these immune suppressive cells weaken the function of cytotoxic T cells and other anti-tumor immune cells by releasing inhibitory cytokines such as IL-10 and TGF-β.

An inflammatory cytokine profile may enhance the effectiveness of PD-L1 blockade by promoting T-cell activation. Conversely, an immunosuppressive cytokine environment creates limited T-cell priming as well as their infiltration to make cold tumor TMEs, and therefore dampening the response to PD-L1 blockade. Thus, the cytokine milieu of the TME plays a vital role in determining the tumor progression. Some cytokines, such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF), TGF-β and interleukins (e.g., IL-10), can have pro-inflammatory or immunosuppressive effects in a context-dependent manner (Fig. 4D). TGF-β overexpression in the tumor microenvironment evades the immune system in colorectal cancer through T-cell exclusion and is resistant to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Similarly, TGF-β evades the immune response by upregulating IDO and CCL22 in breast cancer. Another study reported that anti-PD-L1 ICI’s poor response in metastatic urothelial cancer is associated with activation of TGFβ signaling. Further, it is reported that TGFβ and PD-L1 positively regulate each other to promote immune evasion in several cancers. Therefore, simultaneous inactivation of TGFβ and PD-L1 could evade immune suppressive TME.

Rodriguez-Baena and colleagues found that microglial reprogramming can enhance antitumor immunity and improve immunotherapy responses in melanoma brain metastases (MBM). Mechanistically, it was observed that activation of the Rela/NF-κB pathway in microglia promotes MBM progression. Therefore, inactivation of Rela/NF-κB reprograms microglia toward a proinflammatory state that enhances antitumor immune responses, reduces metastatic burden, and improves the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors.

Tumors often exhibit heterogeneity in terms of cell types, genetic mutations, and the composition of the TME. Therefore, different tumor regions may have distinct immune characteristics and pose challenges to the PD-L1 blockade therapy to work uniformly across the whole tumor. Therefore, the presence of immune-privileged niches or immune-excluded areas can limit the overall success of immunotherapy treatment.

The presence and functionality of TILs, particularly effector T cells, are crucial for an effective antitumor immune response. A TME rich in activated and functional TILs is associated with a better response to PD-L1 blockade. Tumors with a greater density of infiltrating effector T cells are more likely to respond positively. In addition, TILs may express multiple immune checkpoint inhibitory receptors, such as CTLA-4, TIM-3, or LAG-3 (Fig. 4E). These receptors can act synergistically or redundantly with the PD-1/PD-L1 axis to regulate immune responses. Thus, understanding the complex interplay of these factors is essential for developing strategies to improve the effectiveness of the PD-L1 blockade therapy. Thus, the blockade of one receptor/ligand by immunotherapy may contribute to resistance to other immunotherapy treatments. For example, in HNSCC, PD-1 blockade increases Tim-3 expression among TILs, dampening T-cell activation and facilitating tumor evasion from PD-1 blockade through the PI3K/Akt pathway. In this scenario, combination therapies, such as dual checkpoint inhibition or combination immunotherapy with other treatment modalities, are being investigated to address the multifaceted challenges posed by the TME. Further, current studies are looking for biomarkers that can predict PD-L1 blockade responses and individualized treatment plans. Therefore, gaining a deeper understanding of shifting the TME from being protective of the tumor to activating the immune system (turning “cold tumor” to “hot tumor”) would offer a promising path to improve treatment outcomes for patients.

Epigenetic changes and resistance to immunotherapy

Epigenetic reprogramming plays a crucial role in cancer initiation, progression, and immune evasion. Unlike genetic mutations, epigenetic alterations—such as DNA methylation, histone modifications, and chromatin remodeling—are reversible and dynamically regulated, making them attractive therapeutic targets. Importantly, epigenetic dysregulation can impair antigen presentation, promote T cell exhaustion, and create immunologically cold tumors (characterized by a lack of PD-L1 expression, infiltrating lymphocytes, and the presence of immunosuppressive cell populations including TAMs and MDSCs), all of which contribute to resistance against immune checkpoint blockade. Recent findings demonstrate that the loss of SMARCA4, a component of SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex, induces profound enhancer reprogramming in tumor cells, resulting in downregulation of several innate immune genes such as STING1 and IL1β. This leads to significant blockade of dendritic cells and CD4+ T cells infiltration in TME and impaired response to anti-PD-1 immunotherapy.

It is reported that epigenetic reprogramming in tumor cells and immune cells within immune cold tumors is beneficial towards immunotherapy. Epigenetic reprogramming by different epigenetic modifiers including histone deacetylases, histone methyltransferases, and DNA methyltransferases in tumor cells and other cells in TME plays a role in overall immune response (Fig. 4F). Epigenetic reprogramming is closely associated with all stages of the immune cell differentiation process including effector cells. Therefore, the deregulation of the epigenetic process plays a critical role in immunosuppressive TME. Hence, several epigenetic drugs (epidrugs) are developed to restore immune-sensitive TME by decreasing the abundance of MDSCs, TAMs and increasing the population CD8+ effector T cells, memory T cells, and tumor antigens by restoration of the MHC class I antigen processing and presentation machinery. In addition to DNA methyl transferase inhibitor and HDAC inhibitor, it is reported that inhibition of Enhancer of Zeste Homolog 2 (EZH2), a part of the PRC2 complex can enhance the reversion of immune evasion in TME. By influencing various key aspects of the tumor microenvironment, EZH2 contributes to ICB resistance. Therefore, targeting EZH2 and similar epigenetic factors represents a promising strategy to overcome ICB resistance in cancer immunotherapy. The study by Porazzi et al. shows that inhibiting EZH2 and EZH1 (a functional homolog of EZH2) epigenetic regulators using tazemetostat (EZH2 inhibitor) or valemetostat (EZH1/EZH2 dual inhibitor) enhances the effectiveness of adoptive T cell therapies (CAR-T and TCR-T) across multiple cancer models. By reprogramming tumor cells to become more immunogenic—boosting antigen presentation, T cell activation, and infiltration—these inhibitors significantly improve T cell-mediated tumor killing. Dual EZH1/2 inhibition proved especially potent, offering a promising strategy to overcome resistance in both liquid and solid tumors.

Further, it is reported that promoter hypermethylation of genes harboring neoantigens was identified as an epigenetic mechanism of immunoediting in NSCLC. Thus, further studies are needed to investigate the synergistic effects of combining epigenetic inhibitors with existing immunotherapies, such as checkpoint inhibitors, to increase antitumor efficacy and overcome resistance.

Dysregulation of interferon signaling

Interferon signaling is crucial for the activation of antitumor immune responses. Interferons are expressed by activated T cells in response to their interaction with tumor antigens through the MHC class I-TCR complex. Type I Interferons signaling drives cancer cells and other cells in the TME to express PD-L1, which in turn suppresses the antitumor immune response. Tumors have evolved mechanisms that either reduce the production or impair the signaling of interferons within the TME through several ways including the downregulation of IFN receptors to prevent T cell activation by dendritic cells and/or increase the Treg cell infiltration. IFN receptor inactivation, in turn, results in alterations in downstream signaling pathways such as the JAK-STAT pathway. Reduced interferon signaling can lead to decreased activation of immune cells, lowered antigen presentation, and an overall reduction in immune surveillance and response. Tumors that initially respond to immunotherapy may adapt to immune suppression by developing mechanisms that inhibit interferon signaling. In addition, it was reported that interferon signaling is abrogated through JAK1/2 mutations, which led to acquired resistance (Fig. 4G). Altogether, this suggests that resistance occurs through dysregulation of interferon signaling pathways. This adaptive resistance can lead to a relapse where the tumor becomes less sensitive to the effects of immunotherapy.

Metabolic adaptations in immunotherapy resistance

Altered cancer cell metabolism and immune suppression

Metabolic adaptation of cancer cells has emerged as one of the crucial mechanisms to evade immune surveillance. Metabolic changes occur as cancer progresses from premalignant to malignant metastatic tumors, and these changes help cancer cells evade immune surveillance. Several studies have demonstrated that cancer cells adopt metabolic alterations upon nutrient availability to impair the effectiveness of immunological responses.

Notably, metabolic alterations such as increased glycolysis and decreased oxidative phosphorylation, can influence immune cell function and promote immune evasion. Furthermore, several studies showed that cancer cells produce metabolites such as lactate, kynurenine, adenosine, and acetate and these metabolites are associated with impairment of the function of cytotoxic T cells, natural killer cells, and antigen-presenting cells, thereby dampening antitumor immune responses (Fig. 5A).

Additionally, metabolic resource competition within the TME also plays a pivotal role in immunotherapy resistance. Both tumor cells and T cells compete for resources like oxygen and glucose within the TME, and this metabolic competition has the potential to accelerate cancer growth and limit the MHC class I antigen presentation. Further studies found that ICB antibodies against CTLA-4, PD-1, and PD-L1 restore glucose in TME, allowing for T-cell glycolysis and IFN-γ production. These findings underscore the central role of metabolic constraints in modulating T-cell activity within tumors. Moreover, metabolic checkpoints such as HIFs and mTOR regulate the expression of immune checkpoint molecules, further modulating immune cell activity within the TME.

Itaconate (ITA) has recently attracted considerable attention for its broad immunoregulatory properties. It is synthesized from cis-aconitate, an intermediate of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, via the catalytic activity of the enzyme aconitate decarboxylase (Acod1). A recent study by Schofield et al. reported that cancer cells resistant to anti-PD-1 therapy exhibit elevated itaconate production, which is attributed to increased expression of Acod1. Mechanistically, it was observed that upregulation of Acod1 plays a pivotal role in mediating resistance to anti-PD-1 treatment. Therefore, reducing Acod1 expression in these resistant cancer cells restores their sensitivity to anti-PD-1 therapy, highlighting Acod1 as a potential therapeutic target for overcoming immunotherapy resistance. In a related study, Fan et al. showed that uptake of ITA by Tumor cells via solute carrier family 13 member 3 (SLC13A3) stabilizes PD-L1, contributing to immune evasion and therapy resistance in NSCLC, highlighting SLC13A3 as a novel target for therapy intervention.

To further support the link between metabolism and immune suppression, a clinical study revealed that in patients with different malignancies, a low expression pattern of PD-L1 and a high level of NAMPT, an enzyme involved in NAD + metabolism, predict poor prognosis. Chang et al. expanded on this concept by demonstrating that L-asparaginase, traditionally used for leukemias, depletes asparagine, creating an unfavorable tumor microenvironment. When combined with pembrolizumab in a compassionate-use trial for metastatic nasopharyngeal carcinoma, this combination reduced tumor burden and extended progression-free survival.

Collectively, these studies emphasize the intricate interplay between tumor and immune cell metabolism as a crucial driver of immune evasion and resistance to immunotherapy. Further, the presence of other cells in the TME, such as CAFs, also contributes to this metabolic rewiring, influencing immunotherapy outcomes.

Mitochondrial dysfunction in T cells drives immune evasion

Effective cancer immunotherapy depends on the robust function of cytotoxic T cells. However, many tumors develop therapy resistance due to T cell exhaustion, a dysfunctional state marked by reduced proliferation, cytokine secretion, and cytotoxicity. Mitochondria are central to T cell metabolism and function, and therefore play vital roles across the distinct phases of immune responses. Recent evidences indicate that mitochondrial dysfunction within T cells significantly undermines the antitumor immunity and contributes to immune evasion mechanisms in the TME. Building on this, recent reviews have highlighted mitochondria-targeted strategies as promising approaches to prevent T cell exhaustion and enhance the efficacy of cancer immunotherapy. Farsani et al. shows that activation of pyruvate kinase M2 (PKM2) reprograms mitochondrial metabolism in CD8+ T cells, thereby enhances their anti-tumor activity. PKM2 activation enhances mitochondrial biogenesis and structural organization (more cristae, membrane potential, and ATP). PKM2 activation also synergizes with anti-PD-1 therapy, offering a strategy to overcome immune resistance in tumors.

In the TME, chronic antigen stimulation, hypoxia, and nutrient deprivation lead to progressive mitochondrial damage. This includes impaired oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), diminished mitochondrial biogenesis and excessive accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS). Such stress-induced mitochondrial defects drive cytotoxic T cells into an exhausted phenotype, impeding their antitumor efficacy. Notably, the loss of mitochondrial complex I function has been shown to accelerate both T cell exhaustion and tumor progression.

Previous study revealed that TILs often exhibit reduced mitochondrial mass and dysfunction. This decline in mitochondrial mass is closely associated with Suppression of PPAR-gamma coactivator 1α (PGC-1α), a master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. Mechanistically, it was shown that chronic activation of the AKT signaling pathway promotes PGC-1α downregulation to prevent mitochondria biogenesis in tumor-specific T cells, thereby weakening immune responses to cancer cells.

In clinical contexts, T cells from patients with chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) also exhibit mitochondrial dysfunction, characterized by reduced membrane potential and impaired OXPHOS. This metabolic impairment correlates with poor outcomes in CAR T-cell therapies. However, using a CLL mouse model, researchers demonstrated that PI3Kδ inhibition during CAR T-cell manufacturing restored mitochondrial health, promoted a memory-like phenotype, and significantly enhanced antitumor efficacy.

In contrast to AKT signaling, Chamoto et al. demonstrated that ROS-mediated activation of AMPK-mTOR signaling promotes mitochondria biogenesis in CD8+ T cells through activation of PGC-1α. They observed that ROS synergizes with anti-PD-1 blockade therapy to promote tumor regression.Pharmacologic activation of these pathways improved the efficacy of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, suggesting a promising combinatorial strategy for overcoming resistance.

Another study shows that activation of 4-1BB through costimulation boosts mitochondrial biogenesis and function in CD8+ T cells through activation of the p38-MAPK pathway, thereby improving their metabolic fitness. Therefore, it was observed that 4-1BB costimulation significantly strengthens antitumor immune responses of PD-1 blockade therapy. Importantly, a short course of 4-1BB agonism treatment is sufficient to metabolically prime T cells, suggesting a strategy that could maximize the efficacy of immunotherapy while minimizing toxicity.

Recently, Monson et al. reported that inherited mitochondrial DNA, particularly haplogroup T (HG-T), predicts poor response to ICIs like nivolumab in melanoma patients. HG-T is linked to a distinct CD8+ T cell profile with low effector differentiation and high ROS detoxification, leading to ICI resistance. This suggests mitochondrial genetics can serve as a host-derived biomarker for immunotherapy outcomes.

Yu et al. provided further insight by demonstrating that CD8+ TILs accumulate dysfunctional, depolarized mitochondria due to impaired mitophagy—the cellular process of mitochondrial quality control. This leads to elevated mitochondrial ROS (mtROS), disrupted metabolic signaling, and altered chromatin architecture, locking CD8+ T cells into a terminally exhausted and epigenetically fixed state. Treatment with nicotinamide ribosidesignificantly improved T cell function and response to PD-1 blockade through restoring mitophagy and reducing mtROS.

In addition to metabolites and signaling pathways, tumor cells can actively undermine the T cell metabolism through the direct transfer of T cell mitochondria to themselves. Mechanistically, it was shown that cancer cells hijack mitochondria from T cells via nanotubular structures and thereby deplete their energy supply and promote exhaustion. Alternatively, cancer cells transfer their mitochondria with mutated mitochondrial DNA to T cells, along with mitophagy inhibitors like USP30. Then, these foreign mitochondria in T cells are unable to undergo mitophagy, leading to impair metabolism. Therefore, the ability of T cells to respond to immune checkpoint therapy is markedly reduced in cancers such as melanoma and NSCLC (Fig. 5B).

On a more hopeful note, mitochondrial transfer is not always harmful. A recent study by Baldwin et al. showed that bone marrow stromal cells can transfer healthy mitochondria to CD8+ T cells via intercellular nanotubes. This transfer, dependent on the protein Talin 2, enhances the metabolic fitness, proliferation, and tumor-infiltration capacity of T cells, ultimately rejuvenating the exhausted cells and boosting their antitumor responses.

Brestoff et al. present a Consensus Statement establishing standardized terminology for intercellular mitochondria transfer and transplantation. They define key concepts, different mechanisms of transfer (contact-dependent and -independent), and outline emerging therapeutic applications such as mitochondria transplantation and cell engineering. The aim is to unify the field with consistent nomenclature as it advances in both research and clinical settings.

Together, these findings underscore the critical role of mitochondria health in determining the success of cancer immunotherapy. Strategies that restore or enhance mitochondria function through metabolic reprogramming, mitophagy activation, or intercellular mitochondrial transfer (“organelle medicine”) offer promising avenues to overcome resistance and improve therapeutic outcomes.

Senescence-associated immune evasion and resistance

Cellular senescence represents another major contributor to immune evasion and immunotherapy resistance. It is reported that senescent tumor cells foster an immunosuppressive microenvironment by upregulating immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1. For instance, PD-L1 in tumor-derived extracellular vesicles (EVs) can induce T-cell senescence via upregulation of fatty acid oxidation enzymes.

Further, therapy-induced senescent cancer cells escape immune surveillance by enhancing PD-L1 glycosylation through ribophorin 1 (RPN1), a component of the oligosaccharyltransferase (OST) complex that directs N-linked glycosylation. Therefore, RPN1 depletion has been found to restore T-cell activity and reduce tumor recurrence, providing a promising strategy to overcome resistance to immunotherapy. A recent study showed that immune checkpoint blockade therapy eliminates PD-L1+ senescent immune cells. Overall, these insights highlight the promise of senescence-directed combinatorial approaches to overcome immunotherapy resistance and reduce tumor recurrence, warranting further investigation in both preclinical and clinical settings.

Spatial metabolic heterogeneity in T Cells

Beyond tumor cell metabolism, spatial metabolic heterogeneity within immune cells also plays a crucial role in shaping antitumor immunity. Single-cell metabolic regulome profiling (scMEP) has identified tissue-specific, metabolically repressed cytotoxic T cells in human colorectal cancer, which differ metabolically from excluded T cells at the tumor-immune boundary. This spatial metabolic heterogeneity underlines the complexity of T-cell dysfunction in cancer. Further, integrative multi-omics analysis of The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) databases revealed that patients with a high arginine biosynthesis score have reduced proportion of T and B cells in an immune desert TME and were insensitive to immunotherapy, suggesting that arginine biosynthesis genes are associated with immune evasion in several cancers. Supporting this finding, a recent study demonstrated that cancer cell-derived arginine drives polyamine biosynthesis in TAMs, promoting their pro-tumor polarization and suppressing CD8⁺ T cell activity. Mechanistically, it was shown that activation of the p53–TDG (thymine DNA glycosylase)–PPARG (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma) axis in TAMs promotes their pro-tumor polarization in TME, highlighting a key metabolic-epigenetic mechanism of immune evasion. Thus, targeting the arginine–polyamine pathway offers a promising strategy to modulate TME and improve immunotherapy outcomes.

Therapeutic implications: towards metabolic immunotherapy

In light of the above findings, there is growing interest in engineering immune cells to overcome metabolic constraints within TME. Previous studies demonstrated that introducing the fructose-specific transporter GLUT5 into immune cells such as CD8+ T cells, macrophages, and CAR T cells that enables them to utilize fructose in glucose-depleted TME. These modified immune cells displayed enhanced effector functions and antitumor activity in both in vitro and in mouse models, suggesting a novel approach in which fructose metabolism could be leveraged to enhance cancer immunotherapy.

The growing body of evidence highlights the discovery of new targets involved in cancer cell metabolism, and developing drugs that can block these targets has shown real promise in fighting cancer. This approach involves finding specific metabolic enzymes associated with cancer cell progression and developing small-molecule inhibitors to prevent them from functioning. This approach is similar to finding weak spots in cancer cells and then using targeted treatments to overcome those weaknesses, ultimately offering new hope in the battle against cancer through the use of metabolic therapy in combination with immunotherapy, called “metabolic immunotherapy”. As an emerging strategy, metabolic immunotherapy warrants further investigation to highlight its innovative potential and growing relevance in clinical applications.

Importantly, resistance mechanisms to cancer immunotherapy vary in frequency and severity across cancer types—for example, antigen presentation loss is frequent and severe in melanoma and NSCLC, while metabolic suppression is dominant in pancreatic and brain cancers (Table 3). A key question moving forward is how the interplay of metabolic, immune, and oncogenic factors within distinct tumor microenvironments co-evolves to drive resistance, and how these networks can be effectively targeted. 

Role of extracellular PD-L1 in immunotherapy resistance

Several factors are associated with immune checkpoint inhibitor resistance. Cancer cells evade the immune system either by upregulating immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1, PD-L2, LAG-3 (lymphocyte-activation gene 3) and mucin-domain containing-3 (TIM-3) or by downregulating the expression of tumor antigens like MHC class I. Since its discovery, most studies have focused on the role of membrane-localized PD-L1 in immune evasion. However, recent studies have demonstrated that the extracellular form of PD-L1 also contributes to poor survival. Extracellular PD-L1 (known as soluble PD-L1) is a variant of PD-L1 that lacks the transmembrane and intracellular domains. Its presence has increased immunotherapy resistance through several mechanisms that impact the immune response. A recent study suggested that soluble PD-L1 inhibits T-cell function by directly binding to the PD-1 receptor. Furthermore, the secretory spliced form of PD-L1 (secPD-L1) competes with membrane-bound PD-L1 on tumor cells for binding to the PD-1 receptor. Gong et al. reported two secreted forms (PD-L1v242 and PD-L1v229) of PD-L1 from NSCLC patients who relapsed from anti-PD-L1 treatment, and these forms were associated with the worst prognosis in these patients. This competition can lead to a reduced interaction between membrane-bound PD-L1 on tumor cells and PD-1 on T cells, potentially lowering the efficacy of PD-L1 immunotherapy. Soluble sPD-L1 can circulate in the bloodstream and lymphatic system, affecting immune responses at distant sites in the body. This systemic inhibition of immune function can contribute to immunotherapy resistance.

Increasing evidence suggests that cancer cells may secrete sPD-L1 as a strategy for immune escape. This can create an immunosuppressive microenvironment, making it difficult for the immune system to mount an effective antitumor response. Elevated levels of secreted sPD-L1 in patient serum have been associated with poorer outcomes in some cancer types. It can serve as a prognostic biomarker for identifying patients at increased risk of immunotherapy resistance (Fig. 5C). Elevated levels of sPD-L1 have been observed in relatively advanced tumors and are associated with aggressive biological activity. It has been reported that sPD-L1 Levels are associated with reduced 5-year overall survival (OS, p = 0.003) and progression-free survival (PFS, p = 0.019) in epithelial ovarian cancer patients. However, the mechanisms controlling the secretion of sPD-L1 are still poorly understood. Factors such as inflammation, cytokines, and cellular stress may influence the release of soluble PD-L1.

Understanding the role of secretory sPD-L1 in immunotherapy resistance is an evolving area of research. sPD-L1-mediated resistance adds another layer of complexity to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy. Combining therapies that target both secretory and membrane-bound PD-L1 to neutralize/block the inhibitory effects of sPD-L1 are among the strategies being explored to increase the effectiveness of PD-L1 immunotherapy and overcome resistance in cancer treatment in the future.

Researchers continue to investigate these mechanisms to develop strategies to overcome acquired resistance to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy. Improved knowledge of ICI resistance mechanisms will help to identify prognostic and predictive biomarkers and help to determine the most successful combinations with the optimal order of administering these treatment regimens.

Role of the microbiota in immunotherapy resistance

The microbiota plays a crucial role in modulating the immune system. Beyond microbial composition, specific microbial metabolites play pivotal roles in shaping the immune landscape. For instance, short-chain fatty acids such as butyrate have been shown to promote the differentiation of Tregs while suppressing pro-inflammatory Th17 responses in the large intestine. This dual role contributes to the modulation of immune tolerance and the regulation of inflammation. Butyrate plays a key role in Treg development through inhibition of histone deacetylases (HDACs). This inhibition increases histone acetylation at Foxp3 gene locus, thereby enhancing Foxp3 expression and supporting Treg differentiation. In addition, the differentiation of antigen-stimulated T cells into either Th17 or Tregs is influenced by the abundance of TGF-β. At low levels, TGF-β in combination with IL-6 and IL-21 induces the expression of the IL-23 receptor (IL-23R), thereby favoring Th17 cell differentiation. In contrast, high levels of TGF-β suppress IL-23R expression and promote the development of Foxp3+ Tregs, which in turn inhibit RORγt, the key transcription factor for driving Th17 differentiation. However, cytokines such as IL-21 and IL-23 can override Foxp3-mediated suppression of RORγt, supporting Th17 lineage commitment.

Similarly, the vitamin A metabolite retinoic acid (RA) contributes to immune homeostasis in the intestine. RA promotes Treg cell development in the presence of TGF-β and suppresses Th17 differentiation. Mechanistically, RA enhances histone acetylation at the Foxp3 promoter, reinforcing the identity and function of Treg cells.

Further, tryptophan-derived metabolites, such as indole-3-aldehyde (IAld) and kynurenine, influence immune cell fate through activation of the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) pathway. Engagement of AhR modulates dendritic cell function and promotes the differentiation of T cells into Tregs (Fig. 5D). Additionally, AhR activation drives IL-22 production by innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) that stimulates the secretion of antimicrobial peptides, including lipocalin-2, S100A8, and S100A9, the key components of host defense against Candida albicans infection. It is reported that IAld, produced by Lactobacillus reuteri, enhances CD8+ T cell activity and boosts anti-PD-L1 therapy by activating the AhR pathway, whereas Lactobacillus gallinarum-derived indole-3-carboxaldehyde enhances anti-PD-1 therapy in colorectal cancer by inhibiting Treg cell differentiation and promoting CD8+ T cell function via the IDO/kynurenine/AhR pathway.

Recently, Jia et al. reported that Lactobacillus johnsonii, a gut bacterium, enhances the efficacy of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy by cooperating with Clostridium sporogenes to produce indole-3-propionic acid. This microbial metabolite supports the maintenance of a specialized subset of CD8+ T cells for improving immune responses to treatment across multiple cancer types. These findings underscore the capacity of microbiota-derived metabolites to fine-tune immune responses, highlighting the gut microbiome as a modifiable factor with significant therapeutic potential in overcoming resistance to cancer immunotherapy.

Several studies have also reported the role of the microbiome in influencing the response to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. Likewise, immunotherapy also changes the microbiota in patients. For example, treatment with anti-PD-1 therapy has led to changes in the gut microbiome. Patients can be stratified into responders and non-responders to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy based on their intestinal microbiome. Clinical studies have revealed correlations between gut microbiota composition and patient response to immunotherapy. For example, patients with metastatic colorectal cancer (mCRC) who are unresponsive to anti-PD-1 immunotherapy have increased levels of Fusobacterium nucleatum and succinic acid, which suppresses the cGAS-interferon-β pathway and reduces CD8+ T cell trafficking to tumors. Fecal microbiota transfer from responders or treatment with the antibiotic metronidazole can reduce F. nucleatum and succinic acid levels, resensitizing tumors to immunotherapy. In support of these findings, a multicenter phase I trial investigated the combination of fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) from healthy donors with the PD-1 inhibitors Nivolumab or pembrolizumab in 20 untreated advanced melanoma patients. The primary endpoint was safety, with no grade 3 adverse events from FMT alone, although 25% of the patients experienced grade 3 irAEs from the combination therapy. The objective response rate was 65%, including 20% complete responses. The responders presented increased similarity to the donor microbiome and favorable microbiome changes, indicating that the composition of the intestinal microbiome is a significant factor in the response to anti-PD therapy (Fig. 5E). Mouse models further supported the efficacy of FMT. These results suggest that FMT from healthy donors is safe and potentially beneficial, warranting further investigation (NCT03772899). Ongoing clinical trials are actively manipulating the gut microbiota in cancer patients to better understand its impact on immune responses to cancer. These trials aim to elucidate how alterations in the microbiota composition can influence the efficacy of immune-based cancer therapies such as immunotherapy. By directly intervening in and modulating the gut microbiota through fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), researchers hope to uncover novel strategies to improve treatment outcomes and overcome resistance to therapy. Therefore, there is a promising avenue for clarifying the intricate relationship between the microbiota and immune responses in cancer, potentially paving the way for personalized therapeutic interventions tailored to individual patients.

Crosstalk between metabolic and other resistance mechanisms in immunotherapy resistance

Tumor cells employ various resistance strategies to evade immune surveillance, among which metabolic adaptation plays a central role. Metabolic rewiring supports tumor growth under stress and shapes the immune microenvironment by depleting key nutrients, producing immunosuppressive metabolites, and altering signaling pathways. Importantly, these metabolic changes often interact with other resistance mechanisms, such as the upregulation of immune checkpoints, recruitment of regulatory immune cells, and modulation of antigen presentation, to facilitate a coordinated immune evasion program. Therefore, dissecting how metabolic pathways intersect with immune regulatory networks is critical, potentially uncovering synergistic targets for combination therapies that can more effectively overcome immune resistance (Fig. 6).

Role of cancer stem cells in immunotherapy resistance

Cancer stem cells (CSCs) constitute a unique subpopulation within tumors and are distinguished by their remarkable ability to self-renew, differentiate, and resist conventional therapies. These cells pose a formidable challenge in cancer treatment because of their capacity to evade immune surveillance and drive resistance to immunotherapy. CSCs are highly plastic and adept at adapting to stress. CSCs employ various mechanisms to escape immune responses, positioning themselves as a critical focus in cancer biology.

One key strategy that CSCs use to evade immune detection is downregulating MHC class I molecules, which impairs recognition by cytotoxic T cells. TMEs enriched with CSCs are often associated with diminished CD8+ T cell infiltration. Furthermore, CSCs express high levels of immune checkpoint molecules such as PD-L1, CTLA-4, and TIM-3, effectively suppressing T-cell activity. These cells also recruit immunosuppressive cell types, including Tregs, MDSCs, and M2 macrophages to further dampen the immune response. For instance, it is reported that M2 macrophages enhance CSC invasion and drug resistance through secreting TGF-β.

Despite the eradication of bulk tumor cells by conventional therapies, CSCs often persist and contribute to therapy-resistant. This limitation has driven the exploration of combination strategies that integrate ICIs with agents that target CSCs. For example, BMI1, a key component of the polycomb repressive complex 1 (PRC1), plays a vital role in coordinating immune evasion and maintaining CSC stemness. Authors found that inhibition of BMI1 along with PD-1 blockade has been shown to eliminate residual CSCs, increase CD8+ T cell infiltration, and prevent metastasis and relapse in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC).

CSCs exploit several signaling pathways to reinforce their immune evasion and resistance to treatment. TGF-β plays a pivotal role in preserving the CSC phenotype by inducing the dedifferentiation of non-CSCs and supporting CSC survival in metastatic niches. This pathway contributes to immunotherapy resistance by upregulating PD-L1 expression. Further, it was observed that TGF-β/SMAD axis diminishes the efficacy of therapies such as anti-PD-1, indicating that a bifunctional inhibitor could be appropriate to inactivate CSCc-mediated immune evasion. Indeed, emerging bifunctional agents, such as bintrafusp alfa (BA), which targets both TGF-β and PD-L1, have demonstrated promise in restoring antitumor immunity.

Constitutive activation of the STAT3 pathway is another hallmark of CSC biology. STAT3 promotes CSC expansion, suppresses T-cell activity, and induces the expression of PD-L1 and PD-1, further reinforcing immune suppression. Selective STAT3 inhibitors, such as ruxolitinib, have shown potential in improving T-cell infiltration and synergizing with anti-PD-1 therapies. Similarly, hyperactive WNT signaling sustains CSC stemness and facilitates immune evasion by increasing PD-L1 expression. Thus, inhibiting the WNT pathway sensitizes CSCs to immunotherapy, leading to significant tumor regression when combined with anti-PD-1 therapy. Similarly, the AKT pathway also bolsters CSC survival and PD-L1 expression, making PI3K inhibitors a potential tool for reducing immunotherapy resistance, albeit with the need for precision to avoid unintended effects on T cells (Fig. 7).

The inherent heterogeneity of CSC populations complicates therapeutic targeting, emphasizing the need for reliable CSC-specific biomarkers to enable precision medicine. Therefore, bridging the gap between preclinical discoveries and clinical applications demands robust trials and multidisciplinary efforts. CSCs stand at the intersection of immune evasion, metabolic reprogramming, and TME modulation, shaping their pivotal role in immunotherapy resistance. Advances in understanding these mechanisms will pave the way for innovative strategies to target CSCs and enhance the efficacy of cancer treatments. A comprehensive approach that integrates insights from CSC biology, immunology, and therapeutic development holds promise for overcoming resistance and achieving durable cancer remission.

Anti-PD-L1 ICI resistance in hematological malignancies Vs solid tumors

Several reports suggest that the mechanisms underlying resistance to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy differ greatly in solid tumors compared to hematological malignancies. The differences in the anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy resistance between hematological malignancies and solid tumors primarily stem from variations in the TME and immune cell composition. For example, hematological malignancies are immunologically active, often enriched with immune cells and therefore respond better to anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy. On the other hand, solid tumors are generally considered immunosuppressive having stromal barriers and hypoxia in the TME as contributors of immunotherapy resistance. Given that several resistance mechanisms are associated with anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint blockade, strategies such as combination therapies are being explored with increasing frequency to address these challenges and enhance patient outcomes. 

Beyond PD-L1: unlocking the power of combination therapies to overcome immunotherapy resistance

While therapies targeting PD-1/PD-L1 have transformed the Landscape of cancer treatment, resistance remains a significant hurdle, underscoring the need for new targets for immunotherapy. The Landscape of 6758 anti-PD-1/PD-L1 monoclonal antibody-based clinical trials, with 3919 ongoing trials, represents an impressive 278% growth over the last decade. Pembrolizumab has Led the way with 1,481 trials since 2014, followed by Nivolumab, Durvalumab, atezolizumab, avelumab, cemiplimab, and dostarlimab. In addition, 1,631 trials explored other therapies targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway. However, recent trends have shown that the number of clinical trials has declined annually since 2018. Therefore, combination approaches with anti-PD-L1 therapy aim to address tumor heterogeneity, resistance mechanisms, and immune escape pathways to achieve more profound and durable responses. Current research trends underscore a significant shift toward PD-1/PD-L1 combination regimens, particularly ICIs and targeted therapies (Fig. 8).

Anti-PD-L1 ICIs in combination with other immunotherapies

Dual immune checkpoint blockade therapy

The lower response rate suggests that blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis alone is insufficient to activate an effective antitumor immune response. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop a strategy to restore the anticancer immune response in the TME. One such strategy involves the combination of nivolumab (anti-PD-1) and ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) therapy, which significantly increases the long-term survival of advanced melanoma patients. A phase 2 clinical trial (NCT03799445) showed that in HPV-positive oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma, treatment with ipilimumab and nivolumab led to tumor shrinkage that was linked to a drop in effector regulatory T cells (eTregs). Responders had more tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells and a significant drop in eTregs, likely driven by FCGR3A + NK cells via antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity. These results highlight eTreg depletion as a key mechanism to boost immunotherapy efficacy.

Similarly, combination therapy with atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1) and bevacizumab (anti-VEGF) has resulted in improved overall and progression-free survival in patients with unresectable hepatocellular carcinoma. In cholangiocarcinoma, VEGF blockade has been shown to enhance ICB therapy by inducing a B cell activating factor (BAFF) and IL-12-driven immune response. This immune modulation transforms regulatory T cells into a Th1-like fragile state, thereby promoting antitumor immunity. Preclinical and early clinical evidence support the notion that targeting VEGF, in conjunction with CTLA-4 and PD-L1 blockade, can rewire the immune microenvironment, revealing VEGFR2’s pivotal role in Treg cell programming.

Researchers are actively exploring alternative immunotherapeutic targets beyond PD-L1, including CTLA-4, LAG-3 (lymphocyte-activating gene 3), TIM-3 (T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain-3), TIGIT (T-cell immunoglobulin and ITIM domain), IDO (indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase), and bispecific antibodies. A recent Phase 1/2 study evaluated the IDO1 inhibitor linrodostat mesylate combined with nivolumab ± ipilimumab in patients with advanced solid tumors or hematologic malignancies. The combination was well tolerated and showed effective IDO1 pathway inhibition, though it did not significantly improve objective response rates beyond checkpoint inhibitors alone. A high IFN-γ gene signature and low tryptophan 2,3-dioxygenase (TDO2) expression were associated with better responses, suggesting potential biomarkers for future patient selection.

Dual blockade of LAG-3 and PD-1 has synergistic effects, potentially due to their nonredundant pathways in regulating T-cell function. Clinical trials involving relatlimab, an anti-LAG-3 antibody, have demonstrated improved survival rates when combined with nivolumab (anti-PD-1) (NCT03470922), raising the following question: what are the biomarkers that might predict responsiveness to such combinations? Furthermore, the NICHE-3 trial (NCT03026140) reported a remarkable 97% pathologic response rate—comprising 92% Major and 68% complete responses—following neoadjuvant therapy with Nivolumab and relatlimab in patients with locally advanced dMMR colon cancer. Only one recurrence was observed at a median follow-up of eight months, and the treatment was well tolerated, with only 10% of patients experiencing grade 3–4 immune-related adverse events, supporting this regimen as a potentially practice-changing approach.

TIM-3 is expressed in T cells and is involved in T-cell exhaustion. Inhibitors targeting TIM-3 are being investigated for their potential to restore T-cell effector functions and are considered especially promising for use in combination therapies to target multiple pathways of immune escape. The RELATIVITY-020 trial (NCT01968109) revealed that patients who had previously received ICIs exhibited higher baseline levels of LAG-3, PD-L1, and CD8 expression compared to those treated with non-ICI therapies. Interestingly, combination therapy with nivolumab and relatlimab increased LAG-3 expression in both ICI-naïve and ICI-refractory patients, while PD-L1 and CD8 expression rose significantly in ICI-naïve patients. These findings suggest that nivolumab and relatlimab can modulate the tumor microenvironment despite prior ICI resistance, highlighting the need for further research to optimize patient selection and understand resistance mechanisms.

Recent studies in HNSCC, such as the trial led by Li et al., have further demonstrated the value of combination immunotherapy (NCT04080804). Both nivolumab plus ipilimumab and nivolumab plus relatlimab outperformed monotherapy in tumor reduction. Mechanistically, the nivolumab-ipilimumab combination expanded pre-existing effector memory CD8+ T cells, whereas nivolumab-relatlimab reprogrammed exhausted CD8+ T cells into more functional states, increasing T cell diversity and infiltration. These findings highlight distinct mechanisms of action and support a personalized approach to immunotherapy (Table 5). TIGIT competes with the costimulatory receptor CD226 to bind to the same ligands, thereby exerting inhibitory effects on T cells. Blocking TIGIT has been shown to enhance T-cell responses and NK cell antitumor activity, providing a compelling rationale for its inclusion in combination treatment regimens. 

Triplet immune checkpoint blockade therapy

Long et al. reported the first case of using a combination of three checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapies (anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA4, and anti-LAG3) as a neoadjuvant treatment before surgery in a glioblastoma patient. In line with this approach, the RELATIVITY-048 trial (NCT03459222) evaluated a triplet immunotherapy regimen combining nivolumab (anti-PD-1), relatlimab (anti-LAG-3), and ipilimumab (anti-CTLA-4) in advanced melanoma. The study demonstrated a promising objective response rate (ORR) of 58.7% and a 48-month OS rate of 69.1% (Table 5). These findings reinforce the rationale for “triplet checkpoint blockade” approaches in solid tumors. By enhancing immune response and improving long-term outcomes, triplet checkpoint blockade approaches pave the way for novel combination strategies in cancer immunotherapy.

Anti-PD-L1 ICIs in combination with chemotherapy

In addition to the combination of immunotherapies, the combination of immunotherapy and chemotherapy markedly increases patient survival. Furthermore, atezolizumab plus bevacizumab plus chemotherapy (carboplatin plus paclitaxel) significantly improved the progression-free survival and overall survival of metastatic nonsquamous NSCLC patients (NCT02366143). Similarly, anti-PD-1/PD-L1 combination regimens are being tested in 4,062 out of 4,897 ongoing trials in conjunction with various immune-oncology (IO) treatments, chemotherapies, targeted therapies, and radiotherapies. Using single-cell RNA sequencing, the researchers discovered that combining nab-paclitaxel (Nab-PTX) with the anti-PD-L1 antibody (atezolizumab) reshapes immune responses in TNBC. This combination expands stem-like effector memory CD8+ T cells and follicular helper CD4 + T cells while also increasing mast cells, facilitating lymphocyte recruitment. Furthermore, mast cell activation enhanced the efficacy of PD-L1 blockade, suggesting that targeting mast cells could improve immunotherapy outcomes in TNBC.

It has been reported that multitargeted kinase inhibitors stop cancer growth by targeting multiple signaling pathways, bispecific antibodies, and fusion proteins that enhance tumor cell killing by engaging immune cells or blocking multiple signaling pathways simultaneously, and immunoconjugates/antibody‒drug conjugates (ADCs), consisting of a humanized mAb, linker and cytotoxic payload, deliver cytotoxic drugs directly to cancer cells while sparing healthy tissues. These innovative approaches may improve outcomes in cancer treatment by addressing tumor heterogeneity and resistance mechanisms. Combinations of tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) and immunotherapy have a well-established role in treating a variety of solid tumors, such as endometrial, hepatocellular, and renal cell carcinomas. Combination of Lenvatinib (a multikinase inhibitor) and pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) led to higher response rates and improved progression-free survival compared to pembrolizumab monotherapy (NCT04199104). Another study compared pembrolizumab alone to pembrolizumab plus chemotherapy in metastatic lung adenocarcinoma patients with PD-L1 ≥ 50%. Combination therapy resulted in a median OS of 22.6 months versus 14.2 months for monotherapy and significantly lowered early death risk, particularly in women, stage IVB patients, and those with PD-L1 ≥ 75%. These findings challenge existing PD-L1 treatment guidelines and indicate a need for personalized strategies. Similarly, Zhu et al. reported that the combination of oxaliplatin (a platinum-based chemotherapeutic agent) and an immune checkpoint inhibitor increased the degree of immunologic cell death in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells, further supporting the potential value of combining chemotherapy with immunotherapy to boost antitumor immune responses.

Compared with chemotherapy alone, the combination of a PD-1 inhibitor and chemotherapy appears to be a more favorable option for initial or subsequent treatment in patients with gastric cancer. In a phase III randomized study (TOPAZ-1 NCT03875235), durvalumab, a PD-L1 inhibitor, plus gemcitabine and cisplatin was associated with significant improvements in overall survival compared with placebo, gemcitabine, and cisplatin in people with advanced biliary tract cancer (Table 5). Although numerous combination therapy regimens are discussed, it is imperative to acknowledge potential risks such as increased toxicity and adverse reactions, particularly associated with combination chemotherapy, in order to present a more clinically balanced perspective.

Anti-PD-L1 ICIs in combination with radiotherapy

Beyond chemotherapy, combining ICB with radiotherapy (RT) has shown promise in overcoming resistance by inducing immunogenic cell death, enhancing tumor antigen presentation, and promoting T-cell activation. Expanding on this, Wu et al. demonstrated that RT synergizes with YM101 (a bispecific anti-TGF-β/PD-L1 antibody), boosts antitumor immunity by enhancing dendritic cell activation, increasing T-cell infiltration, and mitigating radiation-induced fibrosis, leading to improved tumor control and prolonged survival (Table 5). Moore et al. identified a subset of immune-poor (cold) tumors that are paradoxically responsive to ICB, despite lacking lymphocyte infiltration. These tumors, characterized by a high βAlt score (low TGF-β signaling and high error-prone DNA repair), can be converted into immune-responsive tumors using a combination of TGF-β inhibition, radiation, and ICB therapy, which activates NK cells to recruit lymphocytes. The findings suggest that the βAlt score could serve as a predictive biomarker for identifying patients whose cold tumors could be sensitized to immunotherapy. Building on the role of radiation in reshaping the tumor microenvironment, FLASH radiotherapy is an emerging cancer treatment that delivers ultra-high dose rate radiation (> 40 Gy/s) in a very short time, effectively killing tumor cells while sparing healthy tissues. A recent study by Ni et al. showed that FLASH radiation reduces tumor immunosuppression by shifting macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory state and downregulating PPARγ and arginase 1, both linked to anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive functions. It also enhances infiltration of T cells and CAR-T cells into medulloblastomas, a type of brain tumor. They found that combination of GD2-targeting CAR-T therapy along with FLASH significantly improved treatment outcomes in mouse models.

Similarly, Ran et al. demonstrated that combining PARP inhibitors with RT enhances anti-PD-L1 immunotherapy in small cell lung cancer (SCLC) by stabilizing chemokine mRNA (CCL5 and CXCL10) through a cGAS-STING pathway-dependent mechanism, thereby boosting the immune response and improving treatment efficacy. The EPOC1802 phase 2 trial assessed atezolizumab after definitive chemoradiotherapy (dCRT) in 40 patients with unresectable locally advanced esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC). The complete response rate was 42.1%, with a median overall Survival of 31.0 months and a 12-month progression-free Survival rate of 29.6%. The study suggests that atezolizumab shows promise in improving outcomes, though long-term data is needed. A recent study by Chen and colleagues investigated the effects of low-dose gut irradiation on the efficacy of PD-L1 blockade therapy in metastatic cancer patients. The researchers found that this combined treatment approach led to improved therapeutic outcomes. The study suggests that low-dose irradiation may modulate the gut microbiome, enhancing the immune system's response to PD-L1 inhibitors. These findings provide a potential new strategy to boost the effectiveness of immunotherapy in cancer treatment. ​ A recent study investigated the optimal combination and timing of ICIs with radiotherapy for cancer treatment using mathematical modeling and transcriptomic analysis. By examining lymphocyte interactions in esophageal cancer patients, the researchers found that the most effective approach may involve administering anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy concurrently with RT or using anti-CTLA-4/TIGIT therapy either concurrently or as an adjuvant treatment. These findings provide valuable insights for future clinical trials, potentially refining treatment strategies and improving patient outcomes.

Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 ICIs in combination with ADC/bispecific antibodies

The lists of clinical trials examining the effectiveness of chemotherapy/radiotherapy/angiogenesis inhibitor/bispecific antibodies plus anti-PD-L1 ICI therapy have been thoroughly reviewed elsewhere. Building on this, the HARMONi-2 phase 3 trial found that ivonescimab (a bispecific antibody against PD-L1 and VEGF) significantly improved progression-free survival (11.14 vs. 5.82 months) compared to pembrolizumab in PD-L1-positive advanced NSCLC, reducing the risk of progression or death by 49%. The safety profile was comparable between both treatments, with ivonescimab showing a potential benefit in delaying health deterioration, suggesting it as a promising first-line therapy option for this patient group (NCT05499390) (Table 5). Petosemtamab, a bispecific antibody, targets both EGFR and LGR5 (transmembrane receptors expressed on cancer stem cells) and has demonstrated promising efficacy with manageable safety in pretreated HNSCC patients. Bivatuzumab mertansine (BIWI 1), an ADC, is a highly potent antimicrotubule agent coupled with a monoclonal antibody against CD44v6, a tumor-associated antigen highly expressed in HNSCC and normal squamous epithelium. Additionally, DB-1419, a novel bispecific-ADC targeting B7-H3 and PD-L1, is designed to deliver a topoisomerase I inhibitor (P1003) selectively to tumors. It demonstrated potent antitumor activity in preclinical models through direct cytotoxicity, immune activation, and bystander effects. DB-1419 showed favorable pharmacokinetics and safety profiles, supporting its ongoing clinical evaluation (NCT06554795).

Recent studies have highlighted the pivotal role of TGF-β in mediating resistance to PD-1 blockade. Elevated TGF-β signaling within the tumor microenvironment contributes to immunosuppression by excluding T cell infiltration and promoting the development of regulatory T cells and myofibroblasts. To overcome this barrier, bispecific antibodies targeting both TGF-β and immune checkpoints have emerged as a promising strategy. Recent study reported that a bispecific antibody directed against the PD-1 and TGF-β receptor II showed a promising antitumor activity by blocking the TGF-β signaling (NCT05836324).

Further, Niu et al. demonstrated that a bispecific antibody targeting TGF-β and VEGF, when combined with anti-PD-1 therapy, significantly enhanced antitumor efficacy by reprogramming the tumor stroma and increasing CD8+ T cell infiltration in preclinical models. Another recent clinical trial study demonstrated that PD-1 and VEGF bispecific antibody, along with chemotherapy, efficiently suppresses advanced NSCLC progression in those were failed to respond to systemic therapy (NCT04736823). Similarly, Yi and colleagues reported that a bispecific antibody targeting TGF-β and PD-L1 robustly promoted T cell infiltration and elicited superior antitumor activity in triple-negative breast cancer models resistant to immune checkpoint blockade. These findings support the notion that dual inhibition of TGF-β and anti-PD-1/PD-L1 pathways can overcome primary and acquired resistance to PD-1 monotherapy. Integrating such bispecific approaches may represent a viable strategy to enhance the efficacy of immunotherapy in resistant tumors.

Therefore, these innovative strategies aim to address limitations and increase response rates, with the ultimate goal of overcoming resistance and broadening the spectrum of patients who may benefit from immunotherapy.

Combination therapy: CAR-T and ICIs

Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy has revolutionized blood cancer treatment by engineering T cells to target tumor-specific antigens. However, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment significantly hinders its efficacy in solid tumors, characterized by tumor heterogeneity and T-cell exclusion. A promising strategy to overcome these challenges is the combination of CAR-T therapy with ICIs, which can enhance T cell persistence, reinvigorate antitumor responses, and improve both safety and therapeutic success.

CAR-T cells engineered to target immune checkpoint molecules offer a potential solution by directly counteracting tumor-induced immunosuppression. Qin et al. compared two anti-PD-1 CAR designs, one incorporating PD-1 domains and another featuring a high-affinity anti-PD-L1 scFv, both with identical intracellular signaling components. Their findings demonstrated that anti-PD-L1 CAR-T cells exhibited superior efficacy in targeting PD-L1 + tumors, supporting the potential of PD-L1-directed CAR-T therapy as an effective approach for solid tumors. Similarly, Luo et al. further validated that PD-L1-directed CAR-T cells not only target tumor cells but also disrupt the immunosuppressive microenvironment, reinforcing the rationale for integrating CAR-T therapy with ICIs for enhanced efficacy.

Preclinical studies have shown promising results with genetically engineered T cell infusion combined with ICIs, and several clinical trials (NCT04639245, NCT03747484, NCT02706405, NCT02926833, NCT04995003) are currently underway to assess the therapeutic potential of this combinatorial approach. As evidence continues to grow, the synergy between CAR-T therapy and ICIs represents a compelling avenue for overcoming the barriers to effective solid tumor treatment.

Machine learning and AI tools in immunotherapy response prediction

Machine learning (ML), a powerful branch of artificial intelligence (AI), can analyze vast amounts of data to identify complex patterns, making it a valuable tool in cancer research for improving diagnosis and treatment. On the other hand, precision is lacking in current clinical techniques that rely on oversimplified threshold-based methods that lack precision and fail to capture the underlying biological complexity. ML technologies, therefore, offer a substantial improvement, especially in patient stratification and predicting responses to immunotherapy.

For example, a study developed a radiomics nomogram based on MRI and clinical data to preoperatively predict microvascular invasion in hepatocellular carcinoma, aiding in surgical decision-making and prognosis evaluation. Similarly, AI-driven methodologies, encompassing both machine learning and deep learning algorithms, have demonstrated significant potential in enhancing survival prediction accuracy by efficiently analyzing complex multi-omics data and integrating diverse clinical variables in lung cancer prognosis (Fig. 9).

Expanding on these advanced analytical approaches, Zheng et al. utilized highly multiplexed imaging and deep visual proteomics to decode tumor-immune interactions at the single-cell level. By integrating Imaging Mass Cytometry (IMC) with proteomic analysis, their study reveals immune and tumor cells'spatial organization and functional states, providing valuable insights for developing targeted immunotherapies and personalized treatments. In another pivotal study, Fomin and colleagues leveraged Machine Learning to analyze a large clinical-genomic dataset, identifying 36 genetic signatures that predict response to checkpoint inhibitor therapy in NSCLC. The model uncovered key genes (BRAF, BRIP1, FGF10, FLT1) and revealed that different mutations within the same gene could lead to contrasting therapy outcomes. Furthermore, pathway analysis pinpointed MAPK, PDGF, IL-6, and EGFR signaling as mechanisms of resistance, with IL-6 emerging as a notable biomarker associated with poor response. Another study by Liu et al. conducted single-cell RNA and TCR sequencing to analyze Tumors from 234 NSCLC patients treated with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, revealing five distinct tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) subtypes. Higher response rates were associated with FGFBP2 + NK/NK-like T cells, memory B cells, and precursor exhausted T cells (Texp), while poor responses were linked to CCR8 + regulatory T cells. Importantly, the proportion of Texp cells was a strong predictor of recurrence-free survival, offering a potential immune-based biomarker for patient stratification. These findings underscore the need for immune-based stratification strategies to refine treatment and follow-up plans in NSCLC.

Recently, scientists developed an AI tool called LORIS (logistic regression-based immunotherapy-response score) to predict how cancer patients respond to immunotherapy. Using a six-feature logistic regression model, this tool was trained on a dataset of 2881 ICB-treated and 841 non-ICB-treated patients across 18 solid tumor types. Likewise, Yoo et al. introduced SCORPIO, a Machine-learning model designed to predict ICI efficacy using routine blood tests and clinical data, thereby eliminating the need for complex genomic or immunologic assays. Developed using data from 9,745 patients across 21 cancer types, it was validated in real-world and clinical trial cohorts. It outperformed traditional biomarkers like TMB and PD-L1 expression, demonstrating strong predictive performance for overall survival and clinical benefit, and highlighting its potential for personalized immunotherapy decisions in diverse healthcare settings​.

Complementing these predictive approaches, the Immune-related CRISPR screen analyzer of functional targets (ICRAFT) platform integrates immune-related CRISPR screens, single-cell RNA sequencing, and clinical trial data to identify therapeutic targets that enhance cancer cell susceptibility to immune attack and promote T-cell activation. Through ICRAFT, the authors identified crucial immuno-oncology targets such as TNFAIP3, PTPN2, and SOCS1, and demonstrated that the deletion of TNFAIP3 activates the TNF-NF-κB pathway, thereby facilitating immune-mediated tumor cell destruction. This platform stands as a comprehensive resource for propelling the development of immuno-oncology drugs.

Moreover, combining machine learning with multi-omics has enabled novel strategies for predicting immunotherapy response and patient stratification. A key example is the Immunotherapy Resistance Cell–Cell Interaction Scanner (IRIS), a model that leverages transcriptomic data to identify Resistance Downregulated Interactions (RDIs)—ligand-receptor pairs suppressed in patients who do not respond to ICB therapy. By calculating a Resistance Downregulated Score (RDS), IRIS successfully stratified melanoma patients across multiple cohorts, outperforming traditional models like TIDE and IMPRES. Notably, the study uncovered downregulation of the CXCL9–CXCR3 axis, essential for CD8⁺ T cell infiltration, in ICB-resistant tumors.

In parallel, the ISAC study implemented systems-level immunomonitoring in a real-world cohort of 191 children with diverse solid tumors. By combining mass cytometry, plasma proteomics, and RNA sequencing, researchers uncovered that tumor type and age significantly shape systemic immune profiles. A random forest classifier identified distinct immune signatures associated with extracranial and intracranial tumors, with IL-6 and ANGPT2 standing out as strong predictive markers. In addition, TMB was correlated with infiltration of CD8⁺ T cells in neuroblastoma and Wilms tumor, reinforcing its role as a metric for immunogenicity. A longitudinal case study monitored a child with high-risk neuroblastoma and revealed post-treatment clonal expansion of CD8⁺ T cells that expressed cytotoxic markers, such as granzyme B (GZMB) and CX3CR1.

Together, these studies highlight the transformative potential of integrating machine learning and multi-omics into clinical research to uncover predictive biomarkers, guide treatment strategies, and monitor immune responses, ultimately paving the way for more precise and effective immunotherapy in both adult and pediatric oncology. AI-derived biomarkers from standard clinical data represent a scalable and promising approach to inform treatment decisions. While challenges remain, ongoing improvements in machine learning and enhanced data exchange and integration are expected to result in more precise, personalized cancer therapies, ultimately improving patient outcomes. Even if combination therapy proves successful, there is still room to optimize the number of ICIs by determining the best dosages, sequences, and timings for combination therapy to modify the tumor microenvironment without inducing acquired resistance. This is where multi-omics approaches come into play.

Multi-omics approach to understand cancer immunotherapy response

The multi-omics approach has proven invaluable in revealing the complex nature of cancer and its response to immunotherapy. By integrating data from various sources, such as genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics, researchers can gain a holistic view of the molecular landscape of tumors. This comprehensive perspective allows for the identification of new biomarkers, pathways, and targets, paving the way for personalized cancer immunotherapies. Moreover, multi-omics strategies not only advance our understanding of tumor-immune system interactions but also hold immense promise for improving patient outcomes (Fig. 9).

Beyond optimizing therapeutic efficacy, multi-omics approaches are also increasingly critical for predicting and managing treatment-related toxicities. For instance, irAEs caused by anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies can lead to fulminant and even fatal consequences, necessitating early detection and aggressive management. In this context, a study by Jing et al. combined pharmacovigilance and multi-omics data to identify predictive biomarkers for irAEs, developing a bivariate model based on LCP1 and ADPGK, genes linked to T-cell activation. This model showed high predictive accuracy and was validated in an independent cohort, offering a promising strategy for pre-therapy risk stratification and improving the safety of immune checkpoint blockade treatments.

Spatial transcriptomics for decoding the tumor microenvironment

A deeper understanding of the TME is crucial for advancing cancer immunotherapy. In this regard, spatial transcriptomics has emerged as a powerful tool for decoding the complex cellular and molecular architecture of the TME, offering insights into spatial gene expression patterns that were previously unattainable with traditional transcriptomic methods. Complementing this, AI has become increasingly important in analyzing the large-scale, multidimensional datasets generated by multi-omics approaches. AI-driven methods enable the extraction of meaningful biological insights from high-dimensional data, significantly enhancing our comprehension of tumor biology and the dynamic interactions within the TME.

Building on the necessity for deeper TME analysis in immunotherapy, Zeng et al. introduced IOBR 2.0, an advanced computational toolkit for multidimensional decoding of the TME using multi-omics data. This upgraded version integrates six analysis modules, enabling comprehensive TME profiling, genome-TME interaction analysis, and biomarker identification to enhance immuno-oncology research and improve insights into immunotherapy response mechanisms. Demonstrating the utility of such advanced technologies, Cui et al. employed high-resolution single-cell and spatial transcriptomic analyses in NSCLC patients, uncovering dynamic TME remodeling following neoadjuvant chemoimmunotherapy. Their findings revealed the accumulation of SELENOP-expressing macrophages and antigen-presenting CAFs at tumor boundaries, which served as indicators of a favorable therapeutic response. Similarly, further highlighting the potential of integrative multi-omics approaches, Chen et al. used CODEX imaging and multi-omics profiling to map the spatial organization of SCLC Tumors in 165 patients. This research identified distinct tumor neighborhoods and immune microenvironments and with the help of ColonyMap algorithm, detected an MT2 immune niche enriched in M1-like macrophages, CD8+ T cells, and NK T cells. This niche was associated with improved survival and better response to immunotherapy, offering new avenues for patient stratification and personalized treatment strategies. Further expanding the landscape of immunotherapy research, Li and colleagues employed single-cell multi-omics to identify four key T-cell subsets that influence ICB therapy response across cancers. Their study revealed that CXCL13 + T cells are associated with treatment responsiveness, while TGF-β1 + and Temra (terminally differentiated effector memory) T cells are linked to resistance. These findings contribute to the identification of predictive biomarkers and support the development of personalized immunotherapy approaches. Furthermore, the study by Shiao et al. identifies three distinct response types to pembrolizumab and radiation combination therapy in TNBC: early responders with pre-existing immune activity, late responders who develop immune responses after combination therapy, and non-responders lacking immune infiltration. Using scRNA-seq and spatial proteomics (CODEX), the researchers Mapped 12 TME “districts” that correlate with treatment outcomes. Effector T cell expansion was linked to antigen-presenting macrophages and specific spatial immune niches, particularly in late responders. These findings suggest that spatial and cellular profiling can better predict and guide immunotherapy responses in TNBC.

Recently, SpaLinker, an integrative computational framework, was developed to integrate spatial transcriptomics with bulk RNA-seq data for the de novo identification of phenotype-associated TME features. It detects spatial structures like tertiary lymphoid structures and tumor-normal interface, and links them to clinical outcomes. Applied across cancers, SpaLinker reveals spatial heterogeneity and prognostic markers relevant to survival and therapy response.

Expanding the spatial transcriptomics landscape, the ImmGenMaps project has provided an open-source, high-resolution atlas of immune and non-immune cells across mouse organs. This resource enables comprehensive insights into gene expression within native tissue contexts, significantly enriching our understanding of cellular interactions and immune dynamics in both healthy and diseased states.

In parallel with technological advances in spatial profiling, researchers have also developed new computational methods to enhance our understanding of immune responses. One such innovation is TRADE (Transcriptome-wide Analysis of Differential Expression), a statistical model designed to detect the global effects of genetic perturbations in single-cell CRISPR screens such as Perturb-seq. TRADE enhances the detection of subtle and distributed expression changes that traditional methods often miss, offering a systems-level view of how genetic perturbations reshape the immune transcriptome. This model has the potential to unravel the complex transcriptional rewiring that underlies immune evasion and resistance, particularly in heterogeneous and dose-sensitive settings, thereby identifying novel targets for overcoming immunotherapy resistance.

Beyond immune profiling, multi-omics analyses of the gut microbiome have also revealed critical modulators of immunotherapy efficacy. Researchers have identified five gut microbiome-based enterotypes, each characterized by distinct microbial and metabolic profiles that influence treatment outcomes. Of particular note, the microbial metabolite phenylacetylglutamine (PAGln) was found to impair the efficacy of anti-PD-1 therapy, highlighting the gut microbiome’s profound impact on immunotherapy resistance and suggesting PAGln as a potential target to enhance therapeutic responses.

In summary, integrating multi-omics data, spanning genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics, and spatial analyses, combined with AI and advanced computational tools, is reshaping the landscape of cancer immunotherapy. These cutting-edge approaches provide unprecedented insights into tumor heterogeneity, immune dynamics, and therapeutic resistance mechanisms. Consequently, they are driving the development of more effective, personalized treatment strategies and fostering a new era of precision oncology.

Conclusion and future perspectives

Anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies have transformed cancer immunotherapy, offering hope to many patients. However, suboptimal response rates highlight the need for a deeper understanding and innovative strategies. Addressing challenges such as PD-L1 expression variability, the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, resistance mechanisms, and patient selection is essential to realize the full potential of these therapies. The future of PD-L1 immunotherapy lies in personalization and data-driven approaches. Machine learning (ML) and artificial intelligence (AI) tools can revolutionize the analysis of PD-L1 expression and related biomarkers, enabling the integration of multi-biomarker data and more tailored treatment strategies. Dynamic monitoring of PD-L1 expression and immune biomarkers during therapy may also offer real-time clinical insights, allowing clinicians to adapt treatments for improved outcomes. Exploring neoantigens and tumor mutational burden (TMB) is another promising direction, with the potential to enhance the prediction of the immunotherapy response. Additionally, advancements in diagnostic tools, such as liquid biopsies and advanced imaging, could complement traditional tissue biopsies, providing a more comprehensive understanding of tumor dynamics.

Standardizing PD-L1 scoring systems, such as the tumor proportion score (TPS) and combined positive score (CPS), across cancer types will address variability in evaluating PD-L1 status. Epigenetic regulation of immune responses also warrants investigation; targeting reversible modifications that influence antigen presentation and immune checkpoint expression could unlock new therapeutic pathways. Resistance to PD-L1 therapies through alternative checkpoints such as TIM-3, LAG-3, and CTLA-4 further underscores the importance of understanding compensatory mechanisms for designing effective combination therapies. The metabolic profile of the tumor microenvironment (TME) plays a significant role in immune evasion. Tailoring immunotherapies to address differences in immune-excluded, immune-desert, and immune-inflamed tumors could optimize responses. Furthermore, the tumor-associated microbiome and its role in influencing metabolism and immunity offer novel therapeutic opportunities.

Combining PD-L1-targeted therapies with TME-modulating agents or other metabolic interventions (called “metabolic immunotherapy”) holds great promise. However, the optimal combinations and sequences of these therapies require further study. The integration of multi-omics data—including genomics, proteomics, and metabolomics—can support real-time clinical decisions, enhance patient outcomes, and reduce trial-and-error approaches. Personalized cancer immunotherapy represents one of the most exciting future directions. ML models that integrate genomic, transcriptomic, and single-cell data could uncover hidden patterns in treatment responsiveness and guide clinical decisions. Additionally, addressing resistance in cancers such as pancreatic and prostate cancer through combination therapies targeting multiple immune checkpoints and metabolic pathways may improve patient outcomes and response durability.

As the field of cancer immunotherapy progresses, it is imperative to critically assess both the potential and challenges of these novel targets. What will their impact be on the standard of care, and how can they be seamlessly integrated into current treatment protocols to enhance outcomes for a broader patient demographic? Exploring these targets holds great promise; however, it requires thoughtful scientific inquiry to unravel the complexities of their functions in cancer therapy.

In summary, the future of PD-L1 immunotherapy is promising. By addressing key challenges, leveraging advanced technologies, and focusing on personalization, we stand at the threshold of a transformative era in cancer treatment. The landscape of cancer immunotherapy is shifting toward precision strategies, where next-generation RNA-based vaccines, engineered cytokines, and combination therapies with checkpoint inhibitors and cell therapies work in harmony. Cancer vaccines will serve as key components of multi-modal regimens, aiming for durable remissions, broader solid tumor control, and, ultimately, potential cures (Fig. 10). These advances promise more effective, tailored therapies with fewer side effects, ultimately improving patients' lives worldwide.

Why do some patients with elevated soluble PD-L1 levels respond to therapy, while others develop resistance?

Recent evidence suggests that PD-L1 also undergoes nuclear localization, which regulates cancer progression; however, its role in immune evasion and immunotherapy response remains controversial. Does nuclear PD-L1 improve or worsen the response to ICIs? Can we use combination therapy to regulate the nuclear localization of PD-L1?

What are the optimal combinations of immune checkpoint inhibitors with other therapies (e.g., chemotherapy, targeted therapies, radiation, or engineered oncolytic viruses)?

How can the timing and sequencing of combination therapies be optimized to maximize efficacy while minimizing toxicity?

How can multi-omics data (such as genomics, proteomics, and metabolomics, etc.) be integrated into real-time clinical workflows to predict therapy outcomes?

Can aligning checkpoint inhibitor therapy with patients’ circadian immune cycles (“chrono-immunotherapy”) improve response rates and overcome resistance? A randomized trial should test whether administering immunotherapy at optimal immune-active times (e.g., morning vs. evening) enhances outcomes, particularly in patients with resistance-linked genetic signatures.

Recent studies suggest that cellular senescence contributes to immunotherapy resistance. Can senolytics enhance the efficacy of anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint inhibitors by eliminating senescent immune and stromal cells that foster an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment?

Recent research highlights that tumor-derived exosomes (TDEs), particularly those carrying PD-L1, play a significant role in mediating resistance to immunotherapy by suppressing immune responses both locally within the TME and systemically. An important question arises: how can targeting tumor-derived exosomal PD-L1 enhance the clinical efficacy of anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapies across various cancer types?

How can PROTAC-mediated protein degradation be leveraged to enhance anti-PD-1/PD-L1 immunotherapy responses, and what are the current challenges and future directions for PROTAC delivery in tumor immunotherapy?

Can we develop advanced predictive preclinical models, including organoid co-cultures (tumor + autologous immune cells) and humanized mouse models that faithfully recapitulate resistance mechanisms and accelerate translational advances?

How can multifunctional nanoparticles be designed to dynamically respond to the evolving tumor microenvironment in real-time to synergistically modulate antigen presentation, T cell activation, and immune checkpoint inhibition to overcome acquired and intrinsic cancer immunotherapy resistance?

We strongly believe that addressing these important questions will help to advance effective cancer immunotherapy treatments.
