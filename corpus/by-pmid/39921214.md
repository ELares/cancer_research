GD2 is a Crucial Ganglioside in the Signal Modulation and Application as a Target of Cancer Therapeutics

ABSTRACT

While various glycosphingolipids were identified as cancer‐associated carbohydrate antigens to be used as tumor markers, disialylated gangliosides such as GD3 and GD2 have particularly attracted attention from many researchers as promising cancer‐associated antigens. Simultaneously, their functions in cancer and normal tissues have also been reported. Although GD3 is expressed at the early neural developmental stage and in various cancers, it is also found in the activated status of some normal cells such as astrocytes and lymphocytes. On the other hand, GD2 is expressed in more restricted cells than GD3, enabling anti‐GD2 immune therapy to be more applicable for immunotherapy. Recently, the expression of GD2 has been reported in various epithelial cancers and neuroectoderm‐derived tumors. The involvement of GD2 in cancer stem cell propertiesand the roles of GD2 in the signal modulation to bring about cancer stemness are now some of the most fascinating research topics. Cancer immunotherapy targeting GD2 by anti‐GD2 antibody or anti‐GD2 CAR‐T is now widely being challenged with various modifications such as combination with cytokines, chemotherapy, or immune checkpoint blocking.

Among cancer‐associated antigens, ganglioside GD2 is the most crucial and essential carbohydrate antigen as a modulator of malignant signals and as a target of immunotherapy for refractory cancers. In particular, GD2 is now believed to be a marker of cancer stem cells and to be essentially involved in the features of cancer stemness.

Introduction

Gangliosides are widely expressed in a number of tissues and cells in the bodies of vertebrates. Although the majority of cells constituting our bodies express some kinds of gangliosides, nervous systems generally express high levels of gangliosides compared with other tissues, and this fact is a common and unique finding throughout all vertebrate species.

As cDNAs of glycosyltransferases responsible for the synthesis of gangliosides were isolated, genetic modification of gangliosides in cultured cells and in experimental animals became possible, and many studies have been performed to clearly elucidate ganglioside functions. In particular, gene targeting of glycosyltransferase genes enabled us to directly access the roles of gangliosides in our bodies. The synthetic pathway of main glycosphingolipids (GSLs) is shown in Figure 1.

Not only nervous tissues but some cancer cells also express high levels of particular gangliosides. During many efforts to search for cancer‐specific antigens, various carbohydrate antigens were defined. Monoclonal techniques developed at the end of the 1970s brought about the identification of various cancer‐associated antigens. Gangliosides were representative cancer‐associated antigens defined in these studies and turned out to be “class II” tumor antigens, that is, expressed mainly in some cancer cells but not in normal cells. Some of them can be found in some normal tissues and cells at some particular developmental stages, indicating they are a sort of “differentiation” antigens. Nevertheless, these tumor‐associated gangliosides have been used as tumor markers and as therapeutic targets. Watanabe et al. found anti‐GD2 antibody in a melanoma patient “AH” in the “autologous typing” project. They showed that the “AH” patient's immune system recognized GD2 on his/her own melanoma, suggesting that even differentiation antigens can be autoantigens to induce anti‐cancer immune response.

In this review, the present status of analyses on the expression and functions of disialyl gangliosides is summarized. Not only as cancer‐specific antigens, GD2/GD3 are being recognized as regulatory molecules of biological features of cancer cells and our bodies. Increasing expectations and present features for disialyl gangliosides as targets of immunotherapy are also summarized.

As for the nomenclature of GSLs, see Jürgen Kopitz's review.

Anti‐GD2 Antibody: Specificity and Determinant Structures

In the development of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) reactive with cancer cells, some simple gangliosides were defined as cancer‐associated antigens, that is, GD3, GM2, and GD2 were representative gangliosides expressed in the neuroectoderm‐derived cancers. In addition to mouse mAbs reactive with GD3 in melanomas and GD2 [ ] in neuroblastomas, human mAbs reactive with GD3, GM3, GM2, or GM2/GD2 [ ] were also established. Some of them were injected into patients' tumors with significant anti‐tumor effects.

 GD3 and GD2 are very similar gangliosides (Figure 2) and are sometimes expressed together in cancers and activated immune cells. Addition of GalNAc to galactose on GD3 with β1,4‐linkage results in the formation of distinct structure, function, and antigenicity. Generally, GD3 is expressed in the majority of melanoma cases, while GD2 is found in the advanced stage of the disease, the metastatic stage. GD3 is frequently expressed on activated T lymphocytes. Furthermore, GD3 is also expressed on stimulated melanocytes by proinflammatory cytokines. These expression modes of GD3 in the activated status of cells suggest that it might be a protective response of normal cells to extrinsic stresses.

Interestingly, GD3 and GD2 are found in the early stage of neural development. These expression profiles of GD3/GD2 in the normal tissue development are reflected in their expression in tumor cells derived from progenitor cells of the individual normal tissues.

Expression and Function of Ganglioside GD2 in Cancer Cells

Among glycoconjugate antigens defined as cancer‐associated antigens, gangliosides have been expected to be promising markers/targets of cancer therapeutics. Furthermore, regulation of malignant phenotypes and intracellular signaling by gangliosides has been reported in various cancers.

Gangliosides in malignant melanomas may be one of the representative examples of cancer‐associated glycolipids. To elucidate the roles of various gangliosides in melanoma cells, we established stable transfectant cell lines in which each ganglioside is dominantly expressed using a human melanoma cell line N1. N1 is a mutant subline of SK‐MEL‐28 lacking GD3. By introducing cDNAs of various ganglioside synthase genes, several lines expressing mainly GM3, GD3, GM2, GD2, or GM1 were established, as shown in Figure 1. Rigorous comparison of phenotypes among these transfectant cells revealed the following results, that is, cell growth increased in the following order: GD2+, GD3+ > GM1+, GM2+, GM3+ cells. Cell invasion activity increased as GD3+ ≧ GM2+ > GM1+, GM3+, GD2+ cells. The intensity of cell adhesion to collagen I (CL‐I) and spreading increased as GD2+ > > GD3+, GM1+ > GM2+, GM3+ cells (Table S1). These results suggested that GD3, “a melanoma‐specific ganglioside,” increases cell growth and invasion as a general basis for malignant tumors. On the other hand, GD2 characteristically increases cell adhesion to extracellular matrices and subsequent cell migration, corresponding with its expression at a metastatic stage (Figure 3). These results also correspond with our findings by enzyme‐mediated activation of radical sources/mass spectrometry (EMARS/MS), that is, GD2 cooperates with integrins by forming a cluster with each other, leading to enhanced malignant phenotypes.

As for ganglioside expression patterns in human cancers, neuroblastomas showed simple GD2 expression, which is ubiquitous and abundant on neuroblastoma cells, enabling us to focus on the common therapeutic target.

In human gliomas, several papers reported expression of GD3/GD2. GD3/GD2 expression in human glioma cells resulted in the enhancement of malignant properties . Ohkawa et al. demonstrated that mouse astrocytes transfected with human PDGFΒ cDNA expressed GD2 in vitro, and also in vivo using the RCAS Tgv‐a system.

In human leukemia cells, GD3 expression in T‐cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T‐ALL) was reported. Many T‐ALL cells express GD3, but ALL cells do not. Instead, the expression of O‐acetylated sialic acid‐containing gangliosides in ALL was reported. Interestingly, human T‐lymphotropic virus type 1 (HTLV‐1)‐positive T‐cell leukemia (ATL) cells expressed GD2. p40tax of the HTLV‐1 virus turned out to be a causal factor for this GD2 expression.

Expression of DISIALYL Gangliosides in Non‐NEUROECTODERM‐Derived Cancers

In human lung cancers, adenocarcinoma is a major group, expressing GM2. Although small cell lung cancer (SCLC) is a minor group among human lung cancers, it characteristically expresses GD2. Intriguingly, the addition of anti‐GD2 mAb resulted in the apoptosis of cultured SCLC cells, suggesting the possibility for therapeutic application of anti‐GD2 mAbs. Actually, anti‐GD2 mAbs induced dephosphorylation of focal adhesion kinase (FAK) and subsequent activation of MAPK p38, causing anoikis. The collaboration of GD2 with the integrins‐FAK axis in melanomas was also described later.

In human osteosarcomas, GD2 and GD3 are frequently expressed. Interestingly, in contrast to melanomas, GD2/GD3 expression in osteosarcoma cells strongly suppressed cell adhesion to ECM. These contrastive effects of GD3/GD2 expression between osteosarcomas and melanomas seemed to be due to differences in the associating molecules in the cytoplasm with gangliosides.

Recently, it was reported that not only neuroectoderm‐derived tumors or leukemias, but other epithelial cancers also express GD2 and can be tumor markers and/or therapeutic targets. In human breast cancers, GD2 has been expected to be a tumor marker and a target of the treatment. Intriguingly, GD2 was reported to be a breast cancer stem cell marker and to promote tumor progression. In breast cancer patient samples, many of them showed around 5% GD2+, but metastatic cases exhibited around 35.8%, indicating GD2+ cancer stem‐like cells are mixed as a minor population (Figure 4). They showed that NFkB signaling and FAK‐AKT–mTOR signaling pathways might be responsible for triple‐negative breast cancers (TNBCs) via GD2. The importance of GD2 with O‐acetylation was also reported.

In prostate cancers, it was reported that GD2 is expressed in some populations with a higher proportion of metastatic tumors. They also showed a prostate cancer population with higher tumorigenic potential in vitro and in vivo. In bladder cancers, they reported that high‐grade bladder cancer tissues and cell lines have higher GD2 expression than low‐grade ones. They also reported that the GD2+ population of cell lines showed the cancer stem cell properties (CD44highCD24lo ). These results suggested that even non‐neuroectoderm‐derived cancers may express GD2, particularly in the cancer stem‐like population.

As for regulatory mechanisms for GD2 expression in the cancer stem cells, it should be one of the most important issues to be clarified. Generally, ST8SIA1 (GD3 synthase) expression has been reported to be critical for the expression of GD2 and GD3 in some cancers. However, the expression of B4GALNT1 (GM2/GD2 synthase) is also essential because the combination of these two enzymes should be a basis for GD2 expression (Figure 1). Therefore, detailed regulatory mechanisms for simultaneous expression of these two genes are now under investigation, although they seem to be so complicated.

New Trials for Analysis of Ganglioside Signaling

As GSLs are expressed on the outer layer of a two‐lipid layer membrane, it is hard to directly transduce stimulations from outside into cells without cooperation with some membrane molecules present in the vicinity of them. In order to identify glycolipid‐associating molecules on the cell membrane, we have performed the EMARS/MS approach developed by Honke and Kotani. In human melanoma cells, neogenin‐1 was identified as a GD3‐associating molecule. Neogenin‐1 seemed to exert an enhancement of the malignant properties of melanoma cells under GD3 expression. We also tried to identify GD2‐associating molecules in melanomas, resulting in the detection of integrin β1 as a GD2‐associating molecule. Although it was reported that GD2 expression induces increased malignant properties in melanomas, it was newly demonstrated that many of these effects could be brought about by the collaboration with integrins. We also defined PFGFRα as an associating molecule with GD3 on the transformed mouse astrocytes using EMARS/MS. As for SCLC cells, GD2 turned out to associate with ASCT2, a glutamine transporter at lipid rafts of SCLC cells. Thus, EMARS/MS has been utilized to define ganglioside‐collaborating molecules on the membrane. The roles of gangliosides in the signal regulation at GEM/rafts are shown in Figure 5.

As Simons et al. reported membrane microdomains as a platform of signal modulation in a variety of cells. Hakomori named them glycolipid‐enriched microdomains (GEM)/rafts. GEM/rafts are now being prepared by sucrose density‐gradient ultracentrifugation of detergent‐resistant cell lysates. However, the border between raft and non‐raft is not necessarily definite. Therefore, the EMARS/MS should be suitable to define molecular clusters formed around GSLs on the membrane based on physical association.

Implication of GD2 Expression as A Regulator of Cell Signals in Normal Cells

As described above, ganglioside GD3 can be found not only in various cancers but also in mitogen‐activated T lymphocytes, activated astrocytes, and UV‐irradiated melanocytes. On the other hand, GD2 can be scarcely found in normal cells. Previously, we found that cytotoxic T‐cell clones of mice expressed GD2. T cell receptor (TCR)‐stimulated T cells also showed GD2 expression. These results suggest that ganglioside GD3 is induced under some kinds of activation/stimulation of cells by intrinsic and extrinsic factors, but GD2 can be induced by more restricted situations. On the other hand, in neural cell development, profiles of gangliosides vary along neural differentiation. Satoh reported that GM3, GM2, GD3, GD2, and GM4 are expressed in a small but distinctive population of glial fibrillary acidic protein‐positive (GFAP +) fetal human astrocytes in culture (isolated from 12 to 18 weeks' gestation). On the other hand, they found that large numbers of neuron‐specific enolase‐positive neurons expressed GD2 (72%), but not GD3. In GFAP + astrocytes, GD3 was expressed in a small population (3%) with a high proliferative capacity, while GD2 was found in 20% of them. Generally, GD3/GD2 is reported to be differentially expressed during the early stage of neurogenesis in contrast with the adult brain (Figure 6).

In human embryonic stem cells (hESC) and during the differentiation of hESC to neural progenitor cells, globo‐ and lacto‐series GSLs were identified, and core structure switching of these GSLs to gangliosides (GD3) was observed, while high expression of Gb4 in endodermal was found (Figure 6). These glycan structures identified in hESCs and their differentiated counterparts should play functional roles in related cancer or cancer stem cells.

Recently, there are reports indicating that ganglioside GD3 and GD3 synthase are markers of neuro‐progenitor cells (neural stem cells), and also key drivers for glioblastoma stem cells and tumorigenicity. Thus, the implication of GD3/GD2 in cell stemness, regardless of the malignancy or normal, seems to be universal and based on the common properties that GD2 expression confer.

Cancer Therapy Targeting GD2

Expression of GD2 is very confined in normal cells, and extremely high expression is found in some cancers. In particular, the fact that GD2 is highly concentrated in cancer stem cells attracts big attention from many researchers. The fact that GD2 expression is found not only in neuroectoderm‐derived tumors but also in other solid tumors enhances our expectation that anti‐GD2 therapy may be expanded to a wide range of cancers (Figure 4).

Anti‐GD2 antibody therapy has been widely challenged. For more than three decades, various styles of anti‐GD2 mAbs have been tried for mainly melanomas and neuroblastomas with fairly efficient effects. To treat these tumors, various types and modifications of antibody structures have been developed. First of all, humanization of the Fc portion was performed, and bispecific antibodies have been also tried. Fusion of effector domains of other molecules was tried. In addition to GD2 itself as a target, various similar or related targets have been tried, that is, O‐acetyl GD2, anti‐GD2 Ab‐idiotype Ab, and combination with cytokines and/or chemotherapy have brought about better effects on the enhancement of anti‐cancer activity.

In addition to the exponential progress in treating advanced cancers with immune checkpoint blockade, cellular immunotherapy like chimeric antigen receptor T (CAR‐T) cells has been challenged to improve suboptimal survival outcomes. Namely, CAR‐T therapy has proven to be a powerful treatment for hematological malignancies, and also for carbohydrate‐type antigens including GD2. 

Despite various challenges to develop novel immunotherapy targeting cancer‐associated GSLs, sufficient results to eradicate malignant tumors have not been achieved, and some difficult issues remain to be solved. Then, the anti‐GD2 CAR‐T strategy has now been rigorously challenged with various minor issues to be solved. Particularly in glioma and neuroblastoma, preceding trials of the application have been reported. Overcoming the limited ability of CAR‐T cells to traffic outside of the blood/lymphoid vessels, CAR‐T therapy is now expected to be the final choice, obtaining achievement of more than 60% response in advanced neuroblastomas refractory to standard therapy.

On the other hand, GD2‐expressing solid tumors such as breast cancers (esp. TNBC), prostate cancers, bladder cancers, and colon cancers in which a minor portion of tumor cells expresses GD2, hold the potential to survive through current therapies and to cause recurrence and metastasis (Figure 4). Consequently, the following issues are now being addressed: 1. Should CAR‐T be reserved until current therapies become resistant? 2. Which combination is the best as a partner for anti‐GD2 CAR‐T therapy? These issues will be solved in the near future.
