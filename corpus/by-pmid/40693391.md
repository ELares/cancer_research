# Stereotactic body radiotherapy plus cadonilimab (PD-1/CTLA-4 bispecific antibody) as third-line or beyond therapy for refractory solid tumors: A phase 1b study.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Cadonilimab is a humanized immunoglobulin G1 bispecific antibody targeting programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4). This study aimed to evaluate the safety and efficacy of stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) combined with cadonilimab in patients with advanced recurrent or refractory solid tumors.

**METHODS**: Patients with advanced solid tumors who progressed after at least two lines of systemic treatment, including immunotherapy, and were eligible for SBRT were enrolled. SBRT was administered to patients with high-burden/symptomatic lesions in combination with intravenous cadonilimab (6 mg/kg, once every 2 weeks). The primary endpoint was safety.

**RESULTS**: Sixty-three patients were enrolled from August 28, 2022, to September 14, 2023 (median follow-up: 9.1 months). The median prior treatment line was 3.0 (range 2.0-4.0). Approximately 46.0% (29/63) of patients had received prior PD-1/PD-L1 therapy, 36.5% (23/63) and 12.7% (8/63) of patients had non-small cell lung cancer and soft tissue sarcoma. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) occurred in 38.1% (24/63) of patients, with grade 3 TRAEs reported in 3.2% (2/63). The most common TRAEs included pain (12.7%), elevated transaminases (12.7%), pneumonia (6.4%), fatigue (6.4%), nausea (4.8%), and fever (4.8%). The objective response rate (ORR) was 23.8% (95% confidence interval [CI], 14.0%-36.2%). The median progression-free survival (PFS) was 7.2 months (95% CI, 6.3-8.2 months), and the median overall survival (OS) was 10.0 months (95% CI, 7.7-12.4 months). The 6-month and 12-month local control rates were 98.4% and 93.0%, respectively. In a subgroup analysis of 23 non-small cell lung cancer patients, the ORR was 17.4% (95% CI, 5.0%-38.8%), the median PFS was 6.9 months (95% CI, 4.7-9.1 months), and the median OS was 9.1 months (95% CI, 7.3-10.9 months). Multivariate analysis indicated that receiving ≥6 cycles of cadonilimab and having an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status score of 0-1 were significantly associated with improved PFS and OS (P < 0.05).

**CONCLUSIONS**: SBRT in combination with cadonilimab demonstrated manageable toxicity and promising efficacy in heavily pretreated patients with refractory solid tumors.

**TRIAL REGISTRATION**: This study was retrospectively registered at ClinicalTrials.gov (NCT05915481) on August 20, 2022.

BACKGROUND

Recurrent or refractory malignant solid tumors progressing after second‐line or subsequent systemic therapies remain a significant challenge in oncology, characterized by limited retreatment options, poor therapeutic outcomes, and unfavorable survival prognoses. The advent of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) has revolutionized treatment paradigms for advanced solid malignancies. ICIs targeting the programmed death‐ligand 1 (PD‐L1)/programmed cell death protein 1 (PD‐1) axis or cytotoxic T‐lymphocyte‐associated antigen 4 (CTLA‐4) pathway have demonstrated durable antitumor responses across various cancers, including lung cancer, melanoma, hepatocellular carcinoma, and others.

Cadonilimab (also known as AK104) is a bispecific IgG‐Single‐chain Fragment variable (ScFv) antibody targeting PD‐1 and CTLA‐4, and has shown promising clinical potential. Early‐phase trials (COMPASSION‐01 and COMPASSION‐03) established favorable safety profiles and antitumor activity in heavily pretreated advanced solid tumor patients. Notably, the phase 3 COMPASSION‐15 trial revealed cadonilimab plus chemotherapy as a potential first‐line standard for advanced gastric/gastroesophageal junction (G/GEJ) adenocarcinoma, particularly in PD‐L1‐low populations. Emerging evidence further supports its efficacy and safety in hepatocellular carcinoma, non‐small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), and metastatic nasopharyngeal carcinoma when used in combination therapies, positioning it as a promising candidate for third‐line or later treatments.

Stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT), delivering precise high‐dose radiation in 3‐5 fractions, offers distinct advantages over conventional radiotherapy through improved target conformity, dose escalation capabilities, and enhanced normal tissue sparing. Preclinical and clinical investigations of SBRT‐ICI combinations have demonstrated synergistic effects. The COSINR trial, presented at the 2023 World Congress on Lung Cancer (WCLC), reported a remarkable median overall survival (34 months) in metastatic NSCLC patients receiving first‐line SBRT with ipilimumab/nivolumab, even in PD‐L1‐negative subgroups. Given cadonilimab's superior safety profile compared to dual ICI regimens, its combination with SBRT may theoretically optimize therapeutic indices through enhanced efficacy and reduced toxicity.

Despite these advances, the safety and efficacy profile of the SBRT‐cadonilimab combination remains underexplored. This study, therefore, aims to evaluate the safety and preliminary efficacy of stereotactic radiotherapy combined with cadonilimab as third‐ or later‐line therapy for recurrent/refractory solid tumors.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study design and patients

This phase 1b, single‐arm, open‐label, clinical trial (NCT05915481) was conducted at Peking University Third Hospital. Key inclusion criteria for participant included: 1) age between 18 and 75 years; 2) histologically confirmed advanced refractory solid tumors that progressed after standard therapies (including immunotherapy, chemotherapy, targeted agents, or local interventions); 3) an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) performance status of 0‐2; and 4) controlled central nervous system metastases(defined as asymptomatic or radiologically stable ≥2 weeks post‐treatment).

Radiological eligibility criteria required ≥1 measurable lesion per Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) v1.1. Previously irradiated lesions were acceptable as target lesions if they showed documented progression. Patients could have concomitant non‐irradiated measurable lesions outside SBRT fields. Exclusion criteria encompassed recent palliative radiotherapy (<7 days pre‐treatment initiation).

The study protocol received ethical approval from Peking University Third Hospital Institutional Review Board (IRB No. S2023017), with written informed consent obtained from all participants prior to enrollment.

Procedures

Patients underwent a combined therapeutic regimen of SBRT and cadonilimab immunotherapy. Cadonilimab was administered intravenously at 6 mg/kg once every two weeks until disease progression, intolerable toxicity, death, investigator decision, or withdrawal of consent—whichever occurred first. Treatment with cadonilimab was planned to last no longer than 24 months. Dose reductions of cadonilimab were not permitted during the study. Cadonilimab initiation was synchronized with SBRT commencement, requiring administration within a ±3‐day window relative to the first radiation fraction.

The SBRT protocol permitted irradiation of multiple lesions without predefined numerical constraints. We implemented individualized treatment regimens based on comprehensive clinical evaluations, prioritizing lesions demonstrating substantial tumor burden or symptomatic presentation for CyberKnife‐based SBRT delivery. Gross tumor volume (GTV) was defined through multimodal imaging fusion, with subsequent planning target volume (PTV) generation incorporating anatomically appropriate margin expansions (3‐5 mm based on lesion mobility and adjacent critical structures).

Outcomes

The primary endpoint was safety. For the safety evaluation, the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities (MedDRA) version 23.0 was used to code and classify all adverse events. The severity of adverse events was graded according to the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (NCI‐CTCAE) version 5.0. The secondary endpoints were objective response rate (ORR), disease control rate (DCR), median progression‐free survival (PFS), median overall survival (OS), 6‐month and 12‐month local control (LC) rates. ORR was defined as the proportion of patients with complete or partial response according to RECIST v1.1. DCR was defined as the proportion of patients with complete response, partial response, or stable disease. PFS was defined as the time from the date of first treatment to disease progression per RECIST v1.1 or death from any cause—whichever occurred first. OS was defined as the time from the date of first treatment to the date of death from any cause. LC was defined as the absence of local failure, which included any of the following: local tumor enlargement (assessed per RECIST v1.1) confirmed by Positron Emission Tomography (PET) or biopsy, marginal failure, or involved lobe failure. The 6‐month and 12‐month LC rates were defined as the proportion of patients without local failure at the sixth and twelfth months, respectively, from the date of first treatment.

Adverse events (AEs), coded using the MedDRA version 23.0, were graded according to the NCI‐CTCAE Version 5.0. Safety monitoring was conducted up to 30 days after the final cycle of cadonilimab maintenance therapy. Tumor responses were evaluated according to RECIST version 1.1. Imaging assessment was performed every 2‐3 months during the first year and every 3‐6 months thereafter. Survival follow‐up was performed every 90 days after discontinuing the study treatment. For patients who discontinued the study due to toxicity or confirmed disease progression, follow‐up continued until death, withdrawal of consent, loss of follow‐up, or study termination, whichever occurred first.

Statistical analysis

For safety evaluation, all patients who received at least one dose of cadonilimab and fully finished the SBRT scheme were included in the safety set. Efficacy analysis was conducted based on the full analysis set (FAS) that included patients who received at least one dose of cadonilimab, a fully finished SBRT scheme, and at least one assessment of anti‐tumor efficacy after the first treatment. Statistical analyses were performed with SPSS 26.0. Safety data were summarized descriptively in the safety set. Frequencies and percentages of patients in terms of ORR and DCR were summarized, and the 95% confidence intervals (CI) were estimated using the Clopper and Pearson method. The best percentage change of the sum of target lesion diameters from baseline was demonstrated by the waterfall plot. The median survival time and its 95% CI for PFS and OS, and the 6‐month and 12‐month LC rates were estimated using the Kaplan‐Meier method. Survival curves of PFS, OS, and LC rates were plotted. Cox regression was used to analyze the prognostic factors related to the efficacy of the analysis of PFS and OS.

RESULTS

Patient baseline characteristics

From August 28, 2022, to September 14, 2023, 71 patients were screened, and 63 were ultimately enrolled in this study (Figure 1). The median age was 58 years (range 18‐75). The cancer types that patients suffered from included NSCLC (36.5%), soft tissue sarcoma (12.7%), renal cancer (9.5%), colorectal cancer (6.3%), pancreatic cancer (6.3%), liver cancer (4.8%), hilar cholangiocarcinoma (4.8%), osteosarcoma (4.8%), breast cancer (3.2%), gastric cancer (3.2%), glioma (1.6%), parotid adenoid cystic carcinoma (1.6%), endometrial cancer (1.6%), uterine sarcoma (1.6%) and malignant meningioma (1.6%). Patients had received a median of 3 prior treatment lines (range 2‐4), with 46.0% having previously received PD‐1/PD‐L1 therapy. Treatment parameters included a median of 9 cycles of cadonilimab (range 4‐24), and SBRT delivery to a median of 1 lesion (range 1‐7) at a median dose of 24 Gy (range 14‐50; Table 1).

Safety

At the follow‐up cut‐off of December 1, 2023, 38.1% (24/63) experienced at least 1 treatment‐related adverse events (TRAEs), including grade 3 TRAEs in 3.2% (2/63). No grade ≥4 TRAE, treatment‐related serious adverse events (SAEs), or treatment‐related deaths were observed. Individual patients experienced multiple types of TRAEs and were thus counted more than once. The safety profiles encompassed pain (8/63, 12.7%), alanine aminotransferase (ALT)/aspartate aminotransferase (AST) increased (8/63, 12.7%), pneumonia (4/63, 6.4%), fatigue (4/63, 6.4%), nausea (3/63, 4.8%), fever (3/63, 4.8%), vomit (2/63, 3.2%), rash (2/63, 3.2%), cough (1/63, 1.6%) and esophagitis (1/63, 1.6%; Table 2), the majority of these events were low‐grade (grade 1‐2). Notable observations included: (1) All pneumonia cases were at grade 1‐2 and occurred in previously irradiated patients; (2) Single instances of grade 3 pain and esophagitis; (3) Complete resolution of all TRAEs with supportive care.

Efficacy

With a median follow‐up of 9.1 months (range 2.6‐14.7), 41.3% (26/63) of patients had died. The overall ORR was 23.8% (95% CI, 14.0%‐36.2%), and DCR was 73.0% (95% CI, 60.3%‐83.4%; Table 3). Among the 63 enrolled patients, all target lesions of 4 patients received irradiation, while the remaining 59 patients still had target lesions that had not received irradiation. Analysis of these non‐irradiated target lesions included 10 patients with partial response (PR), 28 patients with stable disease (SD), and 21 patients with progressive disease (PD), yielding an ORR of 16.9% (95% CI, 7.1%‐26.8%) and DCR of 64.4% (95% CI, 51.8%‐77.0%). Waterfall plot depicts the best percentage change in target lesion size relative to baseline in all enrolled patients (Figure 2A) and those with non‐irradiated target lesions (n = 59; Figure 2B). Survival outcomes demonstrated 6‐month and 12‐month LC rates of 98.4% and 93.0%, respectively (Table 3 and Figure 3A). The median PFS was 7.2 months (95% CI, 6.3‐8.2 months), and the median OS was 10.0 months (95% CI, 7.7‐12.4 months; Figures 3B, C).

NSCLC subgroup analysis (n = 23) revealed comparable efficacy, with an ORR of 17.4% (95% CI, 5.0%‐38.8%), a DCR of 73.9% (95% CI, 51.6%‐89.8%), a median PFS of 6.9 months (95% CI, 4.7‐9.1 months), and a median OS of 9.1 months (95% CI, 7.3‐10.9 months; Table 4). This group comprised thirteen NSCLC patients with prior PD‐1/PD‐L1 inhibitor treatment, all of whom had achieved a best response of PR to previous therapy. Enrolled after a six‐week washout period following their last immunotherapy session, this subgroup exhibited no objective responses (CR/PR). However, the disease control rate reached 53.8% (95% CI, 22.5%‐85.2%).

Multivariate analysis for PFS and OS

In this section, we present the multivariate analyses conducted for PFS and OS. Six clinical variables were initially considered: gender, age, the number of radiation lesions, the purpose of local treatment, the number of cadonilimab treatment cycles, and the ECOG performance status score.

Univariate log‐rank tests were carried out first. These tests identified that both the number of cadonilimab treatment cycles (hazard ratio [HR] = 0.088; 95% CI, 0.030‐0.253; P < 0.001) and the ECOG performance status score (HR = 0.442; 95% CI: 0.233‐0.838; P = 0.012) were the significant predictors for PFS. Moreover, both factors were also identified as significant predictors for OS: the number of cadonilimab treatment cycles (HR = 0.172; 95% CI, 0.063‐0.473; P = 0.001), and the ECOG performance status score (HR = 0.302; 95% CI, 0.136‐0.672; P = 0.003).

A multivariate Cox regression analysis was then performed. The results of this analysis indicated that, regarding PFS, receiving ≥6 cycles of cadonilimab (HR = 0.09; 95% CI, 0.027‐0.304; P < 0.001) and having an ECOG performance status score of 0‐1 (HR = 0.463; 95% CI, 0.233‐0.919; P = 0.028) were independently associated with PFS (Table 5).

Similarly, for OS, ≥6 cycles of cadonilimab treatment (HR = 0.2; 95% CI, 0.056‐0.709; P = 0.013) and an ECOG performance status score of 0‐1 (HR = 0.382; 95% CI, 0.153‐0.953; P = 0.039) emerged as significant predictors of better OS (Table 6).

DISCUSSION

This phase 1b study represents the clinical evaluation of SBRT combined with cadonilimab in patients with recurrent/refractory advanced solid tumors, demonstrating an acceptable safety profile and promising clinical efficacy.

The observed safety profiles appear favorable compared to historical data from cadonilimab monotherapy and other SBRT‐immunotherapy combinations. In our study, the incidences of any‐grade TRAEs and grade ≥3 TRAEs were 38.1% (24/63) and 3.2% (2/63), respectively. This contrasts with higher grade ≥3 TRAE incidences reported in relevant studies: 13.4% (16/119) with cadonilimab monotherapy in advanced solid tumors (COMPASSION‐01), 17% (6/35) in NSCLC patients receiving SBRT followed by pembrolizumab (PEMBRO‐RT), and 10.5% (2/19) with concurrent pembrolizumab plus SBRT in advanced NSCLC (Phase 2 trial). The safety profile of SBRT plus cadonilimab was aligned with previous studies of SBRT combined with pembrolizumab, and with no unanticipated adverse events observed.

Notably, pneumonitis incidence was 6.4% (grades 1‐2) in our study, lower than comparative cohorts: 26% all‐grade/11% grade ≥ 3 with pembrolizumab + SBRT in PEMBRO‐RT study for advanced NSCLC; 21.1%/5.3% with pembrolizumab + SBRT for advanced NSCLC in a phase 2 trial, and 1.8%/0.9% with ipilimumab + radiotherapy for solid tumors in a phase 1/2 trial; 5.3% grade 3 pneumonitis with SBRT + nivolumab + ipilimumab for advanced NSCLC in the COSINR study.

Regarding other severe toxicities, no treatment‐related SAEs or deaths occurred in our study. Only one patient experienced a grade 3 immune‐related adverse event potentially related to cadonilimab, a rate lower than that reported for ipilimumab plus radiation for advanced solid tumors in a phase 1/2 study. Collectively, these findings support an acceptable safety profile for SBRT‐cadonilimab in the studied population.

In our study, the enrolled patients presented with unfavorable baseline characteristics, with a median of 3 prior treatment lines, and 81% had received ≥3 previous systemic treatments. Despite this heavily pretreated population, the combination therapy achieved an ORR of 23.8%, a median PFS of 7.2 months, and a median OS of 10.0 months. These outcomes compare favorably with historical trials: A phase 1 trial (n = 68) evaluating the safety and efficacy of SBRT‐pembrolizumab combination reported a median OS of 9.1 months and PFS of 3.1 months in immunotherapy‐naive patients. The COMPASSION‐01 trial of cadonilimab monotherapy showed an ORR (13.4%) in similarly heavily pretreated patients. Another phase 1 study investigating SBRT‐nivolumab‐urelumab/cabiralizumab combinations demonstrated shorter median PFS (3.0 months) in advanced solid tumors.

Multivariate analysis in our study revealed no significant association between the number of SBRT‐treated lesions and either PFS or OS. Not all metastatic lesions received radiation therapy in our study. This observation suggests that the extent of local radiotherapy demonstrated a limited impact on survival outcomes. Notably, the integration of cadonilimab with SBRT demonstrated favorable OS outcomes, suggesting the potential of this strategy as a promising therapeutic option for treatment‐refractory tumors.

The majority of enrolled patients in this study were diagnosed with NSCLC (23/63, 36.5%). The median PFS and median OS for NSCLC patients who were multi‐refractory/resistant to ICIs were 6.9 months (95% CI, 4.7‐9.1 months) and 9.1 months (95% CI, 7.3‐10.9 months), respectively. Comparative data from a phase 2 trial (AK104‐202) of cadonilimab monotherapy for NSCLC revealed distinct outcomes: cohort B (primary immunotherapy resistance, n = 7) showed a median OS of 4.93 months, while cohort C (acquired immunotherapy resistance, n = 16) demonstrated a median OS of 13.16 months, with no objective responses observed in either cohort. For patients with NSCLC who progress following first‐line chemoimmunotherapy, second‐line treatment options were historically limited to docetaxel monotherapy—a regimen that typically yields a median progression‐free survival (mPFS) of 4‐5 months and a median overall survival (mOS) of approximately 10 months., our combination therapy demonstrated superior survival outcomes. Compared to cadonilimab monotherapy or chemotherapy alone, the combination of SBRT and cadonilimab achieved favorable survival benefits as a third‐line or later treatment for refractory tumors.

In conclusion, the combination of SBRT and cadonilimab demonstrated manageable safety and promising antitumor efficacy in advanced refractory malignancies, highlighting its potential as a novel therapeutic approach for this challenging population. While this phase 1b study provides proof‐of‐concept evidence, inherent limitations, including its exploratory single‐arm design, heterogeneous tumor types, and limited sample size, necessitate validation through large‐scale, controlled phase 2/3 trials. Future investigations will focus on tumor‐specific efficacy and safety profiles, with parallel mechanistic investigations into immune activation pathways and optimal treatment sequencing strategies for refractory solid tumors.

CONCLUSIONS

In conclusion, our study provides preliminary clinical evidence that posterior‐line SBRT combined with cadonilimab therapy exhibits promising antitumor efficacy, durable OS benefits, and a manageable toxicity profile in patients with recurrent/intractable malignant solid tumors. These findings warrant further validation in tumor‐specific, large‐scale phase 2/3 trials. Future investigations should prioritize biomarker‐driven patient selection and mechanistic exploration of radiation‐enhanced systemic immune activation.
