# Systemic immunomodulation by irreversible electroporation versus stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy in locally advanced pancreatic cancer: the CROSSFIRE trial.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Irreversible electroporation (IRE) and stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy (SABR) are cytoreductive therapies for locally advanced pancreatic cancer (LAPC). Both may signify immunogenic cell death. We aimed to compare systemic immune responses between the treatments.

**METHODS**: As part of the randomized phase II CROSSFIRE trial (NCT02791503), comparing the oncological efficacy of IRE to SABR in patients with LAPC, pre- and post-treatment (2 weeks and 3 months) peripheral blood samples were collected. Frequency and activation status of lymphocytic and myeloid subsets were determined using flow cytometry. T cell responses to pancreatic cancer associated with Wilms tumor-1 (WT-1) and survivin tumor antigens were determined by interferon-γ enzyme-linked immunospot assay.

**RESULTS**: In total, 20 IRE and 20 SABR-treated participants were analyzed (20 men; median age 65 (IQR 55-70)). IRE induced immediate decreases in systemic regulatory T cell (Treg) and conventional type-1 dendritic cell rates, coinciding with CD4+/CD8+ T cell activation by upregulation of PD-1, which was associated with improved overall survival (OS). SABR similarly induced immediate CD4+/CD8+ T cell activation by upregulation of Ki67 and CD25 but resulted in asynchronously delayed Treg downregulation. SABR also induced a durable increase in CD4+ EM T cells, associated with improved OS. Ablation-induced WT-1 or survivin-specific T cell responses were observed in 9/16 (56%) immune competent participants (IRE n=5, SABR n=4) and were associated with longer OS.

**CONCLUSION**: Distinct immune stimulatory responses associated with improved OS, suggest that SABR might benefit from combined Treg depletion strategies while IRE could benefit from PD-1 checkpoint inhibition.

**TRIAL REGISTRATION NUMBER**: The trial was registered on clinical trials.gov (NCT02791503).

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Irreversible Electroporation and Stereotactic Ablative Body Radiotherapy are promising local therapies for patients with locally advanced pancreatic adenocarcinoma and have potential to induce systemic immune responses.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Circulating lymphocytic and myeloid cell subsets following Irreversible Electroporation and Stereotactic Ablative Body Radiotherapy were mapped and compared, showing distinctive tumor antigen specific immune kinetics associated with improved survival for both ablative techniques.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Irreversible Electroporation and Stereotactic Ablative Body Radiotherapy show potential for synergy with immunotherapeutic drugs in locally advanced pancreatic cancer. Mapping of their systemic immune fingerprints in terms of potency, timing, and durability can help to guide future endeavors in this direction.

Introduction

 Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is a lethal malignancy with an overall 5-year survival rate of less than 10%. About 30% of patients present with (inoperable) locally advanced pancreatic cancer (LAPC) and have a median prognosis of less than a year. Palliative chemo(radio)therapy is the standard of care in this situation but despite the introduction of novel improved regimens like folinic acid, 5-fluorouracil, irinotecan and oxaliplatin (FOLFIRINOX), clinical responses remain limited.

PDAC’s notorious chemoresistance is largely attributable to the fibrotic and hypoxic tumor microenvironment (TME) that consists of tumor cells, pro-tumorigenic stromal cells, extracellular matrix and immunosuppressive immune cells. A continuous process of tumor necrosis causes a desmoplastic reaction mediated by cancer-associated fibroblasts that remodel the TME into a dense fibrotic barrier that shields the cancer cells from toxic agents. New immunotherapeutic developments like administration of immune checkpoint inhibiting monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) and adoptive T cell therapies are similarly prevented from reaching their potential as the TME effectively excludes cancer cells from infiltration by immune effector cells.

Limited efficacy of surgical and systemic therapies for LAPC have led to the development of local ablation techniques aiming for minimally invasive cytoreduction to prolong survival without compromising quality of life. Among these are irreversible electroporation (IRE), a predominantly non-thermal needle-guided ablation technique that utilizes high-voltage electrical pulses to enforce cell death, and stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy (SABR), a form of external beam radiotherapy that accurately delivers high radiation doses through multiple precisely aimed beams. The randomized CROSSFIRE trial demonstrated no survival difference between both techniques after neoadjuvant FOLFIRINOX chemotherapy, with moderate toxicity. These results are separately published.

The hypothesis that ablation-induced systemic immune modulation may play a role in treatment efficacy has recently been supported by (pre-)clinical findings. Local ablation can result in antigen release, provoke inflammation, and reduce tumor-induced immune suppression, while simultaneously breaking down tumor integrity. Evidence for the induction of a systemic immune response exists for SABR, where it is referred to as the “abscopal effect” when clinically observable, and for IRE where it is termed “anenestic effect”. In a previous phase-II trial (PANFIRE-II, NCT01939665), we reported transient decreases in regulatory T cells (Tregs), and increases in antitumor effector T cells, signifying the induction of a window of increased antitumor immunity. Ablation may thus sensitize the tumor for potentiation with local or systemic immune enhancing drugs which is currently under investigation for both techniques (NCT04612530, NCT03563248). However, the composition, onset, longevity, and kinetics of IRE- and SABR-induced systemic immune responses remain relatively unexplored and have never been directly compared in a clinical setting. This immune monitoring study aimed to provide a comparative overview of systemic immune response characteristics and kinetics induced by IRE and SABR in patients with LAPC, as well as study their association with survival.

Results

Between May 2016 and March 2022, 68 participants were enrolled in the CROSSFIRE trial; 34 were allocated to SABR and 34 to IRE of which 40 were included in this immune monitoring study equally divided over treatment groups. See figure 1 for study flow diagram. A total of 6 IRE and 2 SABR treated participants had no or insufficient peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) collected for quantitative analysis at the 3 month time point. Among immune monitored participants (20 men/20 women; median age 65 [IQR 55–70]) there were no significant differences in key demographics, clinical characteristics, overall survival (OS) (table 1) and baseline measurement of immune cell subsets (online supplemental table 1). Only distant tumor-free survival was significantly prolonged in the IRE-treated cohort (HR 0.38; 95% CI 0.17 to 0.85; p=0.01) (see table 2 for procedural characteristics).

T cell frequencies and activation state

Both IRE and SABR induced transiently increased expression of Ki67 on CD8+ (p=0.04, and p=0.002, respectively) and CD4+ T cells (p=0.032, and p=0.018, respectively) 2 weeks post-treatment, suggesting activation of effector T cells (figure 2A, B, D and E); increases were significantly more prominent in the SABR-treated participants (p=0.001) (online supplemental table 1)

At the same time point, additional signs of SABR-induced effector T cell activation were observed by upregulation of CD25 (p=0.003, figure 2F) and Human Leucocyte Antigen - DR (p=0.073, online supplemental table 1) on CD4+ T cells, but not for IRE (figure 2C). IRE-treated participants showed a significant increase in expression of PD-1 on both CD4+ (p=0.005) and CD8+ (p=0.035) T cells 2 weeks after treatment (figure 2G,H), other checkpoint receptors (CTLA-4, LAG-3, TIM-3) remained unchanged (online supplemental table 1). In contrast, SABR-treated participants did not show increased PD-1 expression (figure 2I,J). Analysis of the distribution of naïve, central memory (CM), effector memory (EM), and terminally differentiated effector memory T cells revealed a significant shift from CD4+ naïve T cells to CD4+ EM T cells 2 weeks after IRE (p=0.035) (figure 2K,L) and also after SABR (p=0.0002) (figure 2M,N). This naïve to EM T cell shift was maintained up to 3 months (p=0.02) in SABR-treated participants only. No changes in T cell differentiation state were observed in the CD8 subsets (online supplemental table 1).

Regulatory T cell frequencies

A significant decrease in total Treg (p=0.002) and activated Treg (aTreg) (p=0.001) but not in resting Treg (rTreg) frequencies (p=0.211) was observed 2 weeks post-IRE (figure 3A–C). We applied gating according to Miyara et al based on CD3, CD4, CD45RA and FOxP3; importantly, a similar significant observation of transient decreases of (a)Tregs was made when pregating on CD127 and CD25 was applied (online supplemental figures 1A–C and 4B). SABR only induced a minor but significant decrease of aTregs (p=0.039) after 3 months (figure 3E–G), showing a delayed and less pronounced reduction in systemic immune suppression compared with IRE (p=0.033) (online supplemental table 1). Changes in aTreg rates from baseline on a per-patient basis are shown in online supplemental figure 1D.

Natural killer (NK) cell frequencies

IRE-treated participants showed a durable decrease in regulatory and increase in effector natural killer (NK) cells (p=0.044 and p=0.043, respectively) (figure 4A,B). Interestingly, on SABR treatment, regulatory NK remained stable (p=0.689), but effector NK frequencies (p<0.0001) were transiently reduced at the 2-week time point (figure 4C,D). There was accompanying increased expression of HLA-DR on both subsets (regulatory, p=0.034/effector, p=0.008), which has recently been associated with both cytolytic activity and dendritic cell (DC)-like antigen presenting capacity (figure 4E,F).

Myeloid subset frequencies and activation state

In general, myeloid subset frequencies decreased on IRE and increased on SABR, showing differential effects on mobilization of precursors from bone marrow and recruitment to tissues (figure 5A). Systemic conventional dendritic cell type-1 (cDC1) levels were reduced at the 2-week time point in the IRE-treated participants (p=0.015) and remained low up to 3 months (p=0.03) without any significant changes in cDC2 frequencies (figure 5B,C). In contrast, SABR-treated participants showed increased cDC2 levels after 2 weeks (p=0.017) that were maintained up to 3 months although non-significantly so (figure 5D,E). In SABR-treated participants, both classical (p=0.011) and non-classical monocyte (p=0.007) frequencies were transiently increased 2 weeks after treatment (figure 5F,G), whereas no significant changes were observed after IRE (online supplemental table 1). No changes were observed in intermediate monocytes after both treatments. No increased expression of activation markers was observed on any of the myeloid subsets (online supplemental table 1). Marked reduction of circulating early myeloid derived suppressor cells (eMDSC) was observed 3 months after IRE (p=0.0008) in 11/14 participants (figure 5H). No changes in MDSC were observed after SABR (online supplemental table 1).

Tumor antigen–specific T cell responses and relation to overall survival

A CEFT-specific T cell response, considered a measure of general immune competence, was found in all but two IRE- and two SABR-treated participants. Out of 16 immune competent participants, nine (56%) showed a positive tumor antigen-specific T cell response to either Wilms tumor-1 (WT-1) or survivin antigens (as defined in the methods section). Six participants harbored pre-existent tumor antigen–specific T cell responses (IRE n=4, SABR n=2), in which three of the these elevated responses were observed post-treatment (IRE n=3), while the remaining three showed maintained responses (IRE n=1, SABR n=2). In addition, three participants exhibited de novo induced tumor antigen responses (IRE n=1, SABR n=2). These nine participants were designated “responders,” generally showing roughly equal or elevated WT-1/survivin-specific reactivity rates post-treatment (figure 6A and online supplemental table 2).

Generally, survivin/WT-1 responses were observed in patients with longer OS, regardless of treatment modality (figure 6B). Indeed, survivin/WT-1-specific T cell response was associated with longer OS (p=0.0078) (figure 6C), and this was also observed when the patients were split in IRE-treated (p=0.0367) (figure 6D), or SABR-treated cohorts, although non-significant for the latter (p=0.1044) (figure 6E). IRE-treated survivin/WT-1 responders had a more maintained CD8+PD-1 expression at 3 months post-treatment when compared with non-responders (p=0.0262) (figure 6F). Furthermore, PD-1 upregulation on CD8+ and CD4+ T cells 2 weeks post-IRE was significantly associated with improved OS (p=0.046 and 0.024, respectively) (figure 6G–H). Similar correlations were observed after 3 months, but just failed to reach significance (PD-1+CD8+ T cells (p=0.252) and PD-1+CD4+ T cells (p=0.088)) (online supplemental figure 2A,B). SABR-treated survivin/WT-1 responders showed significantly increased CD4+ EM T cell frequencies up to 3 months after treatment (p=0.023) (figure 6I). Maintained upregulated CD8+ and CD4+ EM T cell frequencies at 3 months were also associated with improved OS (p=0.026 and 0.036, respectively) (figure 6J,K).

Discussion

The advent of cancer immunotherapy has focused renewed attention on local tumor ablation as a means of in vivo vaccination through the induction of so-called immunogenic cell death. There is an imperative to assess the immunomodulatory effects of the various ablation methods, including their kinetics, to facilitate the design of optimized combination therapies that will leverage their immune stimulatory effects in the fight on cancer. Here, we compared systemic immunomodulation, and the kinetics thereof, by IRE and SABR in patients with LAPC. Our results confirm previous findings of transient IRE-induced Treg downregulation within 2 weeks of the procedure in this study enforced by significant reduction in eMDSCs, allowing for the induction of simultaneous CD4+ and CD8+ T cell proliferation and selective upregulation of PD-1 on effector T cells, which was associated with improved OS. Our findings also reveal early (ie, within 2 weeks) SABR-induced adaptive immunity. Although no selective PD-1 upregulation was observed, SABR did provoke transient effector T cell proliferation, and delayed, but potentially long-lasting, reduction of aTreg rates, accompanied by an ongoing (ie, at 3 months post-treatment) shift in memory T cell distribution, which was associated with improved OS, but was only temporarily observed after IRE.

To ascertain that the improved immune status resulted in the induction of pancreatic cancer–specific reactive T cells, the frequency of interferon-γ (IFNγ)-secreting T cells in response to WT-1 and survivin-derived peptides was assessed. Although four patients had to be excluded from further analysis, due to an apparent immune compromised state based on CEFT recall response, we found pretreatment T cell reactivity in 6/16 immune competent participants, which is considered high for a weakly immunogenic tumor-type-like pancreatic cancer, but is actually in line with our own previous findings in a pilot study of the immune effects of IRE in LAPC. In three of these patients, IRE was able to further increase WT-1/survivin-specific T cell frequencies, and SABR did so in one other patient, demonstrating that local ablation can boost natural immunity and enhance systemic antitumor efficacy. Even more promising was the apparent de novo induction of WT-1/survivin-specific T cell responses in one IRE- and two SABR-treated patients, as this implied that IRE and SABR enabled presentation of antigens derived from the shielded pancreatic tumor to naïve T cells (most likely in tumor-draining lymph nodes) and confirmed our previous findings for IRE. Although caution is warranted when drawing conclusions from this relatively small cohort, it is encouraging that our previous observation of a possible relationship between IRE induced WT-1/survivin response and longer OS could also be confirmed by the current study. This trend was present in the SABR-treated participants as well.

Selective expression of PD-1 on effector T cells (ie, without simultaneous expression of additional immune checkpoints) has been linked to tumor antigen specificity. Although increased PD-1 expression on CD4+ and CD8+ T cells 2 weeks after IRE was significantly associated with improved OS, we did not find WT-1/survivin immune responders to upregulate PD-1 more extensively than non- responders at this time point, likely due to the smaller patient sample as well as the likely recognition of additional, untested tumor antigens, including possible neoantigens. However, IRE-treated WT-1/survivin responders did significantly maintain upregulated PD-1 expression on CD8+ T cells over time (ie, up to 3 months post-treatment), in line with the improved OS outcomes in these patients. Furthermore, SABR-treated participants with a positive WT-1/survivin response were more likely to maintain an ongoing effector-memory T cell response 3 months post-treatment, which was also significantly associated with improved OS.

These findings suggest that both SABR and IRE can induce or boost anti-tumor-specific T-cell responses that likely contribute to the improved clinical outcomes for ablative local treatment of LAPC in addition to chemotherapy. Nevertheless, our finding that IRE treatment in this trial was associated with significantly improved distant recurrence-free survival over that observed among SABR-treated patients suggests that IRE may induce a more effective systemic anti-tumor immune response than SABR. This difference may call for additional therapeutic immune modulation, the choice of which depends on the induced effects of the ablation moiety in question.

The underlying mechanism of ablation-induced systemic immune responses is likely determined by: (1) cellular release of danger-associated molecular patterns (DAMPS) to alert antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and (2) antigen release to facilitate adaptive effector T cell cross-priming in the lymph nodes. When these components occur within a microenvironment relieved from its immunosuppressive components, there is potential for a clinical response. DCs are the most potent APCs, which link innate and adaptive immunity. The cDC1 subset is known for its superior ability to ingest dying cells and cross-present antigens to CD8+ T cells but can also drive CD4+ T cells towards Th1 responses and activate NK cells through IL-12 release. The cDC2 subset is more proficient in activating CD4+ T cells; therefore, crosstalk between the two subsets is suggested to play a crucial role in tumor immunity. In line with our hypothesis of ablation-induced antigen release and subsequent effector T cell cross-priming, we found circulating cDC1 reduced from 2 weeks up to 3 months post-IRE, possibly as a result from their recruitment to the ablation site and its draining lymph nodes. Indeed, we found a similar decrease in cDC1 rates on local injection of a TLR9 ligand in participants with early-stage melanoma, which was related to increased rates of activated cDC1 in the sentinel lymph node and injection site and coincided with the induction of a type-1 IFN response. It is conceivable that IRE may have similarly affected a type-1 IFN response through the release of DAMPs. This is in keeping with the following observations: (1) the synchronicity of PD-1 upregulation on CD8+ T cells, (2) the observed ongoing increase in effector NK cell frequencies, and (3) the synchronous decrease of classical monocytes with the ability to migrate to sites of inflammation, including the tumor, to also function as APCs.

Our findings point to PD-1 checkpoint inhibition as the preferred therapeutic combination with IRE since effective PD-1 inhibition hinges on cDC1 activity. Interestingly, 60% (12/20) of SABR-treated participants showed an opposite response with transient increases in systemic cDC2, classical, and non-classical monocyte frequencies at 2 weeks after treatment. This suggests their increased mobilization and apparent tissue inflammation. Monocytes have the ability to migrate into tissue and replenish resident macrophages and DC, thus contributing to T cell activation and survival in the TME. Especially since their increase coincided with marked upregulation of CD4+ and CD8+ T cell proliferation markers after SABR.

Each ablation technique generates a different desmoplastic reaction and therefore creates a unique microenvironmental footprint. Differences in immune kinetics between IRE and SABR could be explained by their distinctive induction of cell death. For IRE, apoptosis is the most commonly described pathway of cell death and implies an immunologically silent way of demise with minimal DAMP and antigen release and phagocytosis of the cell remnants by T cell non-stimulatory macrophages (M2-like macrophages). However, multiple additional IRE-induced types of cell death have recently been revealed. While apoptosis occurs near the boarders of the ablation field, extensive necrotic (unregulated) and necroptotic (regulated) pathways of cell death take place near the electrodes and release large amounts of DAMPs, cytokines, and antigens. Production of these danger signals activates the innate immune system as evidenced by our observation of a significant shift from regulatory to effector NK cells. Simultaneously, massive apoptosis may saturate the phagocytic capacity of M2-like macrophages and lead to uptake by DCs, their activation, migration to the lymph nodes, and subsequent T cell (cross-)priming, in accordance with our observations. In addition, sparing of vasculature may facilitate cDC1 and effector T cell infiltration, while rapid cytoreduction (absolute tissue non-viability occurs within 24 hours) may cause synchronous reduction of immunosuppressive Tregs and eMDSC. An immune permissive window is thus established that may result in response to PD-1 inhibition as confirmed by recent (pre-)clinical studies.

SABR-induced immunogenic cell death involves apoptosis, autophagy, necrosis, necroptosis, and mitotic catastrophe secondary to DNA damage, and generation of reactive oxygen species that may occur at various time points after treatment. Additionally, indirect cell death is caused by vascular damage induced hypoxia. This is in line with our observations of SABR-induced immediate effector T cell proliferation and cDC2 and monocyte mobilization, indicating significant DAMP and antigen release. However, several cell divisions need to occur before sufficient unrepaired DNA damage has accumulated to decrease the tumor load and any associated immune suppression, which is likely the cause for the observed delayed decrease in circulating Treg rates. The growing number of studies combining SABR with immunotherapy is suggestive for the clinical potential of radiation-induced immunogenic cell death. However, the desired impact is not always reproducible with mixed systemic responses varying per tissue type, radiation fractionation, and choice/timing of the immune modulator. Our observations of asynchronous effector T cell activation and delayed reduced systemic immune suppression provide new insights in the optimal timing for combined immunotherapeutic treatment. Clinical efficacy may be enhanced by early combined administration with CTLA4 blockade to synchronously bring down Treg rates during effector T cell activation.

Some evidence has been reported for ablation to induce counteractive local and systemic inflammation, causing pro-oncogenic growth factors to promote aggressive tumor growth. It is thought that immature DCs can trigger immunosuppressive signals that lead to T cell inactivation resulting in immune tolerance; clinical evidence for these pro-oncogenic effects exists for hepatocellular carcinoma and colorectal liver metastases. Fortunately, our results do not support these findings for IRE or SABR in patients with LAPC.

Limitations of this study include the lack of tumor sampling to assess immune cell infiltration in the TME. Pancreatic core biopsies are high-risk procedures and had no therapeutic benefit for the participants. Moreover, researchers were not blinded for treatment while processing samples and analyzing the data.

The synchronicity of reduced Treg rates, cDC1 recruitment, and effector T cell activation by selective upregulation of PD-1, associated with longer OS after IRE, confirms a window of opportunity for PD-1 checkpoint inhibition following IRE. An avenue that is now being pursued clinically (NCT04612530). SABR induced asynchronous T-cell activation and Treg downregulation, with the latter being delayed, suggesting SABR might benefit from combined Treg depletion/inactivation strategies, for example, by (local) administration of CTLA4-blocking antibodies. Our data thus provide further direction to optimize the combination of ablation with immunotherapy in the fight against PDAC.

Methods

Study design

The CROSSFIRE trial was a single-center, open-label, randomized phase II superiority study conducted at the Amsterdam UMC (location VUmc) comparing systemic chemotherapy plus percutaneous IRE (experimental arm) to systemic chemotherapy plus SABR (control arm). The primary outcome measure was OS from the date of randomization following the intention to treat principle. Patients were randomly assigned (1:1) to SABR or IRE treatment without subgroup stratification using a computer-generated variable block randomization model (block sizes 4, 6, and 8). Randomisation was performed by one of the trained researchers of the CROSSFIRE trial group. This was an open-label trial, and hence participants, investigators, clinicians, lab technicians, and trial staff were not masked to group allocation. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants, human study IRB approval was obtained (NL2015.417), and the trial was registered on clinical trials.gov (NCT02791503). Efficacy and safety data are published separately. See online supplemental file 2 for the study protocol. The presented prospective immune monitoring side study was undertaken in accordance with the declaration of Helsinki and adhering to the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology statement for observational studies. The side study involved 40 participants (IRE n=20, SABR n=20) included in the trial. Results were not substantiated by repetition of the experiments due to limited sample yield.

Participants

Participants with histologically proven unresectable stage III LAPC—as defined by the American Joint Committee on Cancer staging system for pancreatic cancer (seventh edition) and National Comprehensive Cancer Network criteria—were pretreated with 3–8 cycles of FOLFIRINOX (5-fluorouracil, leucovorin, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin) or gemcitabine+/-nab-paclitaxel chemotherapy. After radiological (contrast-enhanced CT of chest and abdomen +/- 18F-FDG-PET) reaffirmation/confirmation of LAPC with a maximum tumor diameter of ≤5 cm. Study treatment commenced after a postchemotherapy interval period of minimally 4 weeks. Participant characteristics are shown in table 1.

Irreversible electroporation (IRE) and stereotactic ablative body radiotherapy (SABR) treatment

IRE procedures (NanoKnife, AngioDynamics, Queensbury, NY) were performed under general anesthesia. All participants received intravenously administered prophylactic antibiotics. Needle electrodes for IRE were placed percutaneously in and around the tumor with 1.5-cm probe exposure, under CT-fluoroscopy guidance. The number and spacing (range 1–2 cm) of the electrodes was determined by the size and shape of the tumor. Procedural details were previously described. SABR procedures were performed under MRI guidance (MRIdian). Based on inspiration planning, CT and MRI scans participants were treated with breath-hold gating. Radiation was delivered to the tumor only (not to the lymph nodes) in a hypofractionated scheme of five fractions of 8 Gray (Gy). The MRI-guided daily adaptive workflow, MRIdian real-time anatomic motion monitoring, and adaptive reoptimization of the fractions have been described previously. Total SABR treatment generally did not exceed 14 days. See table 2 for (pre-)treatment specifics.

Peripheral blood collection and processing

Heparinized peripheral blood samples (50 mL) were collected at baseline (T=0), at 2 weeks (T=2 wk) and at 3 months (T=3 mo) post-treatment. The 2-week timepoint was selected to detect changes in adaptive immunity and based on the optimal priming and boosting kinetics of tumor-specific effector T cells found in earlier studies. The 3-month time point allowed for assessment of long-term T cell memory. Within 24 hours after sampling, PBMC were isolated by Ficoll gradient centrifugation (Nycomed AS, Oslo, Norway) and cryopreserved for later analysis, as previously described.

Flow cytometry

Fluorescence activated cell sorting (FACS) was performed on PBMC to compare the frequencies and activation status of lymphocytic and myeloid subsets between the time points as previously described. FACS analysis was run on a BD LSRFortessa cell analyzer (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, New Jersey) and included effector T cells, B cells, DCs, NK cells, MDSCs, and Tregs, all with appropriate fluorescence minus one controls. Additionally, cell subsets were examined for expression of activation markers (HLA-DR, CD25, Ki67, CD40, CD80, CD86) and immune checkpoint markers (PD-1, PD-L1, PD-L2, CTLA-4, TIM-3, LAG-3). Details on the flowcytometry procedure, monoclonal antibodies/fluorochromes, and specific marker panels are provided in the online supplemental text. Details on the gating procedures are provided in the online supplemental files 3–9.

In vitro T cell stimulation and interferon-γ (IFNγ) enzyme-linked immunosorbent spot (ELISpot) assay

Tumor antigen–specific T cell responses were determined in 20 randomly selected participants following a previously described in vitro (re)-stimulation and expansion protocol. PBMCs were combined with one of the following peptide pools (JPT Peptide Technologies) at 1 µg/mL per peptide: (1) a pool of 110 15-mer overlapping peptides of Wilms tumor-1 (WT-1) protein; (2) a pool of 110 fifteen-mer overlapping peptides covering survivin protein; and (3) CEFT-positive control pool of 27 peptides selected from defined HLA Classes I and II-restricted epitopes from Cytomegalo virus, Epstein-Barr virus, influenza, and tetanus toxoid to ascertain recall antigen reactivity as a measure of immune competence. Survivin and WT-1 were selected as tumor antigens of choice based on their selective overexpression on PDAC cells observed in 80% and 75% of PDAC patients, respectively, and not at all in healthy pancreatic tissue. We aimed to identify if the observed T cell responses were armed against pancreatic cancer cells in general. Responses to either one tumor antigen were therefore combined in the analysis. PBMC were then cultured for 10 days in the presence of IL-2 (10 IU/mL; Novartis) and IL-15 (10 ng/mL; eBioscience). Cells were then harvested and seeded in a multiscreen 96-well plate (Millipore) coated with an IFNγ catching antibody (Mabtech). Cells were either rechallenged with the peptides overnight or cultured with a dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) vehicle control, and plates were developed following manufacturer’s instructions (Mabtech) the next day. An automated Elispot reader system (AID) was used to count the spots. Tumor antigen–specific T cell frequencies were calculated by subtracting the mean number of spots in the DMSO wells from the mean number of spots in the corresponding experimental wells (survivin, WT-1, CEFT). Tumor-antigen–specific T cell frequencies were considered a positive response when (I) the absolute mean spot count in the experimental wells exceeded those in the DMSO control wells by at least 10, and (IIa) the mean spot counts in the experimental wells exceeded those in the DMSO control well s≥1.5 fold or (IIb) the mean spot counts in the experimental wells was significantly higher than the mean spot counts in the DMSO control wells as determined by an unpaired T test. A CEFT-specific T cell response was considered a measure of immune competence. Participants with absolute post-treatment CEFT-specific mean spot counts <200 per 100 000 T cells, unless exceeding the DMSO control wells >3-fold, were considered generally immune compromised and were excluded for further correlation with survival and flowcytometry data.

Statistics

Characteristics between the two study arms were compared using the χ2 (categorical variables) or unpaired T-test (continuous variables). Immune subset changes over time compared with baseline (T=0) were analyzed within each treatment arm with one-way repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) and a post hoc Dunnett’s multiple comparisons test. In addition, differences between treatment cohorts were analyzed using an ordinary one-way ANOVA with Bonferroni multiple comparisons test as post hoc analysis. In case of missing values, a mixed effect analysis was used. No imputed data values were used. The two-sided unpaired Student’s T-test was used to test differences in survival and WT-1-specific T cell frequencies between subgroups. Differences were considered statistically significant when p≤0.05, as indicated with asterisks in the figures (*p≤0.05, **p<0.01, ***p<0.001, and ****p<0.0001). Statistical analyses were performed with the R software (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria), Prism GraphPad V.9.0.0 (Dotmatics, Boston, Massachusetts, USA), and SPSS statistics V.27.0 (IBM, Armonk, New York, USA).
