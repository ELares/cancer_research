# ROS-generating alginate-coated gold nanorods as biocompatible nanosonosensitisers for effective sonodynamic therapy of cancer.

## Abstract

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) emerges as a promising non-invasive alternative for eradicating malignant tumours. However, its therapeutic efficacy remains limited due to the lack of sonosensitisers with high potency and biosafety. Previously, gold nanorods (AuNRs) have been extensively studied for their applications in photodynamic or photothermal cancer therapy, but their sonosensitising properties are largely unexplored. Here, we reported the applicability of alginate-coated AuNRs (AuNRsALG) with improved biocompatibility profiles as promising nanosonosensitisers for SDT for the first time. AuNRsALG were found stable under ultrasound irradiation (1.0 W/cm2, 5 min) and maintained structural integrity for 3 cycles of irradiation. The exposure of the AuNRsALG to ultrasound irradiation (1.0 W/cm2, 5 min) was shown to enhance the cavitation effect significantly and generate a 3 to 8-fold higher amount of singlet oxygen (1O2) than other reported commercial titanium dioxide nanosonosensitisers. AuNRsALG exerted dose-dependent sonotoxicity on human MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells in vitro, with ∼ 81% cancer cell killing efficacy at a sub-nanomolar level (IC50 was 0.68 nM) predominantly through apoptosis. The protein expression analysis showed significant DNA damage and downregulation of anti-apoptotic Bcl-2, suggesting AuNRsALG induced cell death through the mitochondrial pathway. The addition of mannitol, a reactive oxygen species (ROS) scavenger, inhibited cancer-killing effect of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT, further verifying that the sonotoxicity of AuNRsALG is driven by the production of ROS. Overall, these results highlight the potential application of AuNRsALG as an effective nanosonosensitising agent in clinical settings.

Introduction

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT), a therapeutic modality that uses ultrasound (frequencies ≥ 20 kHz) to activate sonosensitisers to generate cell-damaging reactive oxygen species (ROS) is a promising approach to eradicate solid tumours in a non-invasive and site-directed manner. In comparison with photodynamic therapy (PDT) or photothermal therapy (PTT), SDT demonstrates greater potential in the treatment of deep-seated solid tumours owing to the low tissue attenuation coefficient and non-radiative nature of ultrasound waves, which enable deeper tissue penetration (up to 11 cm). However, the efficacy of SDT is critically dependent on the biological potency of sonosensitisers. Organic sonosensitisers such as derivatives of porphyrin, 5-aminolevulinic acid, phthalocyanine and xanthene have poor solubility as well as limited efficiencies in tumour targeting and accumulation. These limitations undermine the overall efficacy of SDT. Furthermore, many organic sonosensitisers are typically derived from photosensitisers and pose a risk of undesired phototoxicity to the skin, limiting their medical applications.

Recently, inorganic nanosonosensitisers have been shown promising for SDT applications due to their remarkable chemical stability and low phototoxicity. Among various types of inorganic nanosonosensitisers, titanium dioxide (TiO2) has been brought to the forefront of sonodynamic oncotherapy due to its chemical inertness with biological tissues and relatively cost-effective fabrication. However, the application of bare TiO2 nanospheres in SDT is impeded by its instability in the physiological environment and poor quantum yield of ROS, which requires further doping with noble metals, such as gold (Au) or platinum (Pt), to improve ROS generation. Moreover, surface modification with polymeric materials (PEG and carboxymethyl dextran) is often necessary to enhance the biosafety profile of TiO2 . In parallel, sonosensitising properties have also been reported in other metal-based nanomaterials such as manganese nanospheres and vanadium nanosheets, but these nanomaterials have rather laborious preparation procedures that require high energy input, which may complicate future upscaling production. Thus, the development of nanosonosensitisers with good biocompatibility, efficient ROS production and simple preparation is in need to promote the bench-to-bedside translation of SDT.

The tunable optical and thermal characteristics, low toxicity, good cell uptake and anti-angiogenic property of Au-based nanostructures have rendered them an attractive candidate for SDT. Previously, Au nanoparticles (AuNPs) were shown to facilitate ROS production during SDT when conjugated to the commercial sonosensitisers protoporphyrin IX, by acting as cavitation sites that promote gas bubble formation and collapse. When combined with TiO2, AuNPs act as electron traps that suppress electron-hole recombination, giving rise to an increased ROS yield and augmented sonotoxicity. On the other hand, studies have also revealed the potential of AuNPs as intrinsic sonosensitisers without doping to other inorganic sonosensitisers, showing strong growth inhibition of colon, skin, breast, and cervical cancers upon ultrasound irradiation. While Au nanoplates, nanocrystals and nanospheres have been explored for their sonosensitising properties, the potential therapeutic effect of Au nanorods (AuNRs) in SDT is yet to be investigated. In relation to Au nanospheres, AuNRs have advantages including longer systemic circulation and tumour retention time, higher tumour targeting and light-to-thermal conversion efficacy as well as more controllable surface modifications. Since AuNRs have garnered a broad research interest in numerous biomedical applications, such as biomedical imaging, PDT and PTT, we are interested to investigate its potential as a nanosonosensitiser for SDT. Herein, we synthesised and characterised the physicochemical properties of biocompatible alginate-coated AuNRs (AuNRsALG) and assessed their ROS generating capacity and sonodynamic performance on human breast cancer cells. We also compared the killing efficiency of SDT to PTT of AuNRsALG to evidence the potential of this new therapeutic treatment for in vivo and clinical applications.

Materials and methods

Materials

Sodium alginate (300 – 400 mPa⋅s) was purchased from Chemiz (Malaysia). Gold (III) chloride trihydrate (HAuCl4·3H2O), 3,3′-anthracene-9,10-diyldipropanoic acid (ADPA), 1,3-diphenylisobenzofuran (DPBF), triton X-100 and mannitol were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Germany). Sodium borohydride (NaBH4), dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and horseradish peroxidase (HRP) substrate were supplied by Merck (Germany). M-PERTM mammalian protein extraction reagent was purchased from Thermo Fischer Scientific (USA). Thiazolyl blue tetrazolium bromide (MTT) and ascorbic acid were obtained from BioBasic (Canada). Hexadecyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) was purchased from Sangon Biotech (China). Silver nitrate (AgNO3) was acquired from Bendosen (Malaysia). Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium (DMEM) was purchased from Nacalai Tesque (Japan). Fetal bovine serum (FBS) was purchased from GIBCO™ (USA). Apoptosis detection kit was supplied by Elabscience (USA). Unless stated otherwise, all reagents were prepared using Milli-Q ultrapure water.

Synthesis of alginate-coated Au nanorods (AuNRsALG)

AuNRs were synthesised by seed-mediated growth method with slight modifications. Briefly, the seed solution was prepared by mixing 5 ml of CTAB (200 mM) and 5 ml of HAuCl4·3H2O (0.5 mM) under continuous stirring (700 rpm). Then, 600 μl of ice-cold NaBH4 (10 mM) was added all at once, and the mixture was stirred for 2 min before leaving undisturbed for 2 h in the dark. The growth solution was prepared by mixing 10 ml of CTAB (200 mM) and 10 ml of HAuCl4·3H2O (1 mM) under continuous stirring. Then, 100 μl of AgNO3 (10 mM) and 170 μl of ascorbic acid (78.8 mM) were added rapidly in succession, resulting in a colourless solution. The formation of AuNRs was initiated by the addition of 24 μl of the seed solution. The final mixture was stirred for 10 s and left undisturbed for 4 h in the dark. The resulting CTAB-capped AuNRs (AuNRsCTAB) were centrifuged at 4,700 × g for 20 min and washed twice with ultrapure water to remove excess reagents. For alginate coating, 0.1 wt% sodium alginate was added to the as-synthesised dispersion of AuNRsCTAB at 1:1 v/v and mixed at 750 rpm for 4 h using Thermo-Shaker (Allsheng Instruments, China). Alginate-coated AuNRs (AuNRsALG) were centrifuged at 4,700 × g for 25 min and washed with ultrapure water to remove excess sodium alginate and stored at 4 °C until further use.

Physicochemical characterisation

The optical absorption spectra were measured using NanoDrop Spectrophotometer 2000c (Thermofisher, UK) from 200 to 800 nm. The mean hydrodynamic size and zeta potential of the as-synthesised AuNRsALG were measured at 25 °C using the dynamic light scattering method (NanoZS, Malvern Instruments, UK). The particle size and morphology of AuNRsALG were observed under a transmission electron microscope (TEM, JOEL-2100 PLUS, Japan) equipped with an Oxford Instruments X-Max EDX system and operated at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. The elemental analysis was performed using energy dispersive X-ray (EDX) spectroscopy. The presence of alginate coating on AuNRsALG was confirmed using FTIR-Spectrum 400 (Perkin Elmer, USA).

The stability of AuNRsALG was examined using a therapeutic ultrasound applicator (BTL-4710, BTL Industries, UK) equipped with a 5 cm2 probe head (1.0 MHz). The acoustic intensity and the beam pattern were previously verified using a needle hydrophone (Fig. S1-2). The as-synthesised dispersion of AuNRsALG was subjected to ultrasound irradiation (1.0 W/cm2; 50% duty cycle) for 10 min, and the optical absorption spectrum of the solution was measured every minute using the NanoDrop Spectrophotometer 2000c. In a separate setting, 100 µg/ml AuNRsALG was irradiated with ultrasound (1.0 W/cm2; 50% duty cycle) for 5 min. Then, the ultrasound was turned off for 5 min to allow the solution to cool down. The ultrasound irradiation and cooling processes were repeated for 3 cycles, and the solution temperature was recorded every 30 s using a thermocouple (HH127, OMEGA Engineering, USA).

The haemolytic activity of AuNRsALG was assessed by measuring the haemoglobin release from murine red blood cells (RBC) after incubating with AuNRsALG. One hundred microlitres of AuNRsALG at concentrations ranging from 0 to 300 µg/ml was added to the 2% RBC suspension in triplicates. The negative and positive controls were prepared by mixing 2% RBC suspension with PBS and 1% Triton X-100, respectively. Cell viability

The in vitro cytotoxicity of AuNRsALG was assessed using triple-negative breast cancer cells, MDA-MB-231 and murine fibroblasts, L929. The MDA-MB-231 and L929 were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin and maintained at 37 °C in a humidified 5% CO2 incubator. The cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5000 cells/well and grown overnight at 37 °C. The cells were incubated with AuNRsALG at various concentrations, ranging from 0 to 300 µg/ml, for 24 h and 48 h. Cell viability was assessed via MTT assay. The absorbance value of each well was measured at 570 nm using the microplate reader (Plate CHAMELEON V, Finland).

Assessment of ultrasound cavitation

Two millilitres of AuNRsALG were dispersed in degassed deionised water and subjected to the ultrasound (20 kHz; 1.0 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle; VCX750, Sonics and Materials, USA). The cavitation effect was imaged for 60 s using an ultrasonic diagnostic instrument (GE Logiq e; GE Healthcare, USA) and conventional B‑mode sonograms (mechanical index = 0.9). The centre frequency of the transducers was 16.0 MHz. The acoustic intensity and the beam pattern were previously verified using a needle hydrophone (Fig. S3).

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation assay

The methylene blue (MB) spectrophotometric method was used to detect the ultrasound-triggered production of hydroxyl radicals (•OH) by AuNRsALG . Briefly, 2 ml of mixing solution containing 100 µg/ml AuNRsALG and 4 µg/ml MB was prepared in a 24-well plate. The mixture was then irradiated with the ultrasound (1.0 MHz, 1.0 W/cm2, 50% duty cycle; BTL-4710, BTL Industries, UK) for 5 min. Singlet oxygen (1O2)-specific probe, ADPA, and non-specific probe, DPBF, were used to detect the generation of 1O2 and overall ROS. Two millilitres of mixing solution containing 100 µg/ml AuNRsALG and 50 µM ADPA or 50 µM DPBF were prepared and irradiated with the ultrasound for 5 min in the dark. Fenton-like catalytic activity of AuNRsALG was also investigated using MB method according to Wolski et al. , with slight modifications. Briefly, 1 ml of mixture dispersion containing 100 µg/ml AuNRsALG and 4 µg/ml MB was prepared and adjusted to pH 9.0 using 0.1 M sodium hydroxide. In vitro cancer cell killing efficiency

The cancer cell killing efficiency of AuNRsALG induced by ultrasound was investigated on MDA-MB-231 cells. The cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5000 cells/well and grown overnight. For sonodynamic therapy (SDT), the therapeutic ultrasound applicator (BTL-4710, BTL Industries, UK) was used. The probe with a surface area of 5 cm2 was positioned beneath the plate containing cells at a fixed distance of 2 mm to standardise the intensity, and a thin layer of gel was applied between the probe and the cell plate to ensure a proper conductivity of ultrasonic waves. At such configuration, the ultrasound pressure loss due to the presence of culture plate wall was approximately 10%. The MDA-MB-231 were co-incubated with AuNRsALG (0 – 300 µg/ml; in DMEM) and irradiated with ultrasound (1.0 MHz, 50% duty cycle) at 0 – 1.5 W/cm2 for 3 min (one well at a time). The cell viability was evaluated via MTT assay. To establish the cancer cell killing efficiency of AuNRsALG as sonodynamic agents, the cells were also subjected separately to a typical photothermal therapy (PTT) using a 638 nm diode laser at 1.0 W/cm2 for 3 min (HL63193, Oclaro, USA). The laser was positioned at a fixed distance of 30 cm from the cells to standardise the laser intensity.

Flow cytometry

Cellular death pattern analysis was performed using flow cytometry based on Annexin V-FITC (fluorescein isothiocyanate) and propidium iodide (PI) staining. MDA-MB-231 cells were seeded in 96-well plates at 5000 cells/well for 24 h. The cells were treated with 200 µg/ml AuNRsALG and subjected to ultrasound or laser irradiation following the aforementioned procedure. The cells were collected at 6 h post-irradiation and transferred into small microcentrifuge tubes, followed by centrifugation at 500 × g for 5 min. Supernatants were discarded, and the cells were washed twice with PBS. At the final washing,100 µl of Annexin V Binding buffer containing 2.5 µl of Annexin V-FITC and 2.5 µl of PI was added to each tube. The tubes were incubated in the dark at 25 °C for 20 min. Then, 150 µl of Annexin V binding buffer was added into each tube and mixed gently before analysis. The samples were analysed using a flow cytometer (BD FACSCanto II, BD Biosciences, USA) and FlowJo software (v8.1, TreeStar Inc., USA). The apoptosis rate was evaluated based on the fluorescence of 5000 events.

Western blot analysis

Western blot analysis was performed to analyse the expression of apoptosis-associated proteins in MDA-MB-231 cells after AuNRsALG-mediated SDT. The cells (1 × 106) were seeded in 10 cm culture dishes and grown overnight. The cells were treated with 200 µg/ml AuNRsALG and subjected to ultrasound following the aforementioned procedure. After 24 h, lysis buffer (M−PERTM mammalian protein extraction reagent) with protease/phosphatase inhibitor was added to the cells for 30 min on ice. The cells were then collected and centrifuged at 20,000 × g for 20 min at 4 °C to remove the precipitates. Equal amounts of the protein lysate (20 – 50 μg total protein/lane) were added to each well of a 10% sodium dodecyl sulphate–polyacrylamide gel and were separated through electrophoresis. The proteins were transferred to a 0.45 μm polyvinylidene difluoride membrane and immunoblotted using primary antibodies (γH2AX, Bcl-2) after the membrane was blocked using 5% skim milk. The membranes were then washed using Tris-buffered saline supplemented with 0.1% Tween-20 and then incubated again with HRP-conjugated secondary antibodies. The corresponding bands were detected using a chemiluminescent HRP substrate and captured using an imaging system (UVP BioSpectrum; Analytik Jena US LLC, USA). The images were analysed using ImageJ software (NIH, Bethesda, USA) for protein expression normalisation and quantification.

Oxidative radical scavenging assay

The effect of oxidative radical scavenging on cell survival after ultrasound irradiation was investigated by using mannitol as a ROS scavenger. The cells were incubated in the medium containing 200 µg/ml of AuNRsALG and 25 mM of mannitol for 1 h prior to irradiation with ultrasound for 3 min. The treated cells were incubated for 24 h at 37 °C. The cell viability was assessed via MTT assay. In addition, the cells were double-stained with Annexin-FITC/PI after 6 h of irradiation and the cell survivability was assessed using flow cytometry.

Statistical analysis

The data are represented as the mean ± standard deviation (SD) unless stated otherwise. All experiments were conducted in triplicates. One-way and two-way ANOVA (*p < 0.01, **p < 0.001) using Dunnett’s or Tukey’s post hoc analysis were employed to assess the significance between tested groups.

Results

Physicochemical characterisation of Au nanorods (AuNRs)

AuNRs were synthesised through a seed-mediated growth approach using CTAB as a surfactant (AuNRsCTAB). It is well documented that the presence of CTAB on the surface of AuNRs results in high cytotoxicity that limits their potential biomedical applications. Thus, the anionic alginate polymer was used to coat AuNRsCTAB (AuNRsALG) to improve the biocompatibility of the nanorods. The optical absorption spectrum of AuNRsCTAB (Fig. 1A) showed two characteristic local surface plasmon resonance (LSPR) peaks at 519 and 652 nm, attributed to electron oscillations along the transversal and longitudinal axis of the nanorods, respectively. Coating with sodium alginate shifted longitudinal LSPR from 652 to 642 nm.

The hydrodynamic size distribution of AuNRs was assessed using dynamic light scattering. Both AuNRsCTAB and AuNRsALG showed a double-peak size distribution due to the rotational diffusion of the anisotropic AuNRs (Fig. S4). The average particle lengths of AuNRsCTAB and AuNRsALG were 50 ± 1 and 69 ± 4 nm, respectively, while the zeta potential was 36 ± 1 mV for AuNRsCTAB and –24 ± 2 mV for AuNRsALG (Fig. 1B). The increment in hydrodynamic size and the reversal of surface charge indicated deposition of alginate layer on AuNRs via charge-to-charge interaction. Fourier transform infrared analysis further confirmed the successful alginate coating with appearances of the characteristic stretching vibrations peaks for O–H, asymmetric and symmetric COO–, and C–O at 3270, 1597, 1408 and 1026 cm−1, respectively (Fig. 1C). The AuNRsALG were shown to be stable in water and PBS (with 10% FBS) for up to six months at 4 °C (Fig. S5).

From TEM micrographs, the water-dispersed AuNRsALG had a uniform shape with an average longitudinal length of 51 ± 5 nm and transversal diameter of 23 ± 3 nm (Fig. 1D-E). The average aspect ratio was calculated to be 2.3. Elemental mapping analysis of AuNRsALG confirmed an even distribution of elemental gold Lα1 (Fig. 1F). The energy dispersive X-ray spectrum of AuNRsALG showed an intense optical absorption band peak at 2.1 keV and 9.7 keV, which are characteristic absorptions of elemental gold Mα1 and Lα1, respectively (Fig. 1G). In addition, the presence of bands for carbon and oxygen demonstrated the presence of alginate coating.

Previous studies have shown that the stability of AuNRs is essential to achieve maximal treatment efficacy. Hence, we assessed the stability of AuNRsALG upon exposure to ultrasound. AuNRsALG displayed strong transversal and longitudinal absorption peaks consistently over 10 min of ultrasound irradiation, indicating a preservation of rod structure and aspect ratio (Fig. 1H). On the contrary, AuNRsCTAB showed an increasing broadening of both transversal and longitudinal absorption peaks under ultrasound irradiation. The disappearance of these peaks from the sixth minute onwards indicated a complete loss of structural integrity (Fig. 1I-J). The temperature profile of AuNRsALG under ultrasound irradiation was maintained throughout three consecutive heating and cooling cycles. In contrast, AuNRsCTAB showed diminishing performance after each cycle (Fig. 1K), attaining a profile similar to the blank sample (Fig. S6). Furthermore, no significant change in the zeta potential of AuNRsALG was observed between multiple rounds of ultrasound (Fig. S7), suggesting minimal detachment of the alginate coating. These results uncovered the remarkable property of alginate coating in protecting AuNRs against degradation under ultrasound, which helps to retain their morphology and efficacy during SDT.

Biocompatibility of AuNRsALG

For the haemocompatibility assessment, a haemolysis test was performed on murine red blood cells (RBCs) using AuNRsCTAB or AuNRsALG of various concentrations, ranging from 0 to 300 µg/ml. According to the ASTM E2524-08 standard, haemolytic levels higher than 5% indicate damage to RBCs. AuNRsALG at concentrations lower than 200 µg/ml induced a negligible level of haemolytic damage, resulting in<4.5% RBC lysis (Fig. 2A). Haemolysis above 5% was observed only at the highest concentration of AuNRsALG tested (300 µg/ml), exceeding the criterion by a minuscule degree (5.8 ± 0.3%). In contrast, AuNRsCTAB showed significant RBC lysis starting from 37.5 µg/ml, causing up to 60.4% haemolysis at 300 µg/ml (Fig. S8A), which is consistent with other studies on CTAB-stabilised AuNRs. The results evidenced that alginate-coated AuNRs have better haemocompatibility than AuNRsCTAB, deeming them suitable for subsequent in vitro applications.

The cytocompatibility of AuNRsCTAB and AuNRsALG was evaluated using human MDA-MB-231 breast cancer cells. The cells were exposed to media containing different concentrations (0 – 300 µg/ml) of AuNRsCTAB or AuNRsALG for 24 or 48 h. At 24 h, AuNRsALG were found to be non-toxic (∼6% cell death) to MDA-MB-231 cells even at the highest concentration tested (300 µg/ml) (Fig. 2B). At 48 h, the cell viability remained>80% for all concentrations except for 300 µg/ml, where significant cytotoxicity (p < 0.001) was observed. In contrast, AuNRsCTAB caused significant cytotoxicity (p < 0.001) at concentrations starting from 150 µg/ml after 24 h (Fig. S8B). At 48 h, AuNRsCTAB caused significant toxicity (p < 0.01) at 75 µg/ml and higher, causing up to 96% cell death at the highest concentration tested. The results showed that alginate coating contributes to the improved cytocompatibility of AuNRs.

In addition, the cytocompatibility of AuNRsALG was further assessed using the non-cancerous murine L929 fibroblast cells. AuNRsALG were not toxic (>80% cell viability) up to 300 µg/ml after 24 h (Fig. 2C). At 48 h, AuNRsALG elicited significant cytotoxicity (p < 0.01) only at the highest concentration. The results suggested good biocompatibility of AuNRsALG for in vitro cancer cell treatment, consistent with existing studies on alginate-coated Au nanospheres and AuNRs.

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) generation

The cavitation effect of AuNRsALG was detected by an ultrasonic diagnostic instrument (GE Logiq e; GE Healthcare, USA). As observed from the ultrasound images, the cavitation rate was enhanced in the presence of AuNRsALG (Fig. 3A). It is known that inertial cavitation causes the formation and collapse of gas bubbles that raise the medium temperature and transiently create high pressures in surrounding liquids during shockwave propagation. Therefore, the changes in temperature during ultrasound irradiation in the presence or absence of AuNRsALG were monitored to further examine the effect of AuNRsALG on cavitation. AuNRsALG caused a significant increase in temperature, reaching 54.1 °C after 5 min, compared to blank samples (43.1 °C) (Fig. S6). This suggested that AuNRsALG may act as cavitation bubble nuclei and enhance the rate of inertial cavitation in biological systems.

The degree of cavitation was determined by measuring hydroxyl radicals (•OH) production using the methylene blue (MB) method. In the presence of •OH, the N–S heterocycle chromophore group in the MB structure is first attacked, initiating a series of reactions that leads to the degradation of MB into short-chain acids that are subsequently degraded to water and carbon dioxide. Hence, the decolouration of MB is indicative of •OH production. A gradual decrease in the optical intensity of MB under ultrasound irradiation in the presence of AuNRsALG confirmed the generation of •OH (Fig. 3B). The rate constant was 1.32 × 10-1 min−1 (Fig. 3C).

Enhanced cavitation produced by AuNRsALG subsequently creates extreme thermal conditions that cause the emission of a short-pulsed light, a phenomenon termed sonoluminescence. AuNRsALG may harness this light energy to convert molecular oxygen into highly toxic singlet oxygen (1O2). To evaluate this effect, a highly 1O2-specific ADPA probe, which forms non-fluorescent endoperoxide ADPAO2 when oxidised by 1O2, was used. Ultrasound alone showed minimal production of 1O2 as no obvious reduction in the characteristic absorbance peak of ADPA at 400 nm was observed (Fig. S9). In contrast, AuNRsALG caused a significant decrease in ADPA concentration with increasing duration of ultrasound (Fig. 3D). The absorbance of ADPA decreased by 2.6-fold after 5 min of ultrasound irradiation in the presence of AuNRsALG, and the calculated first-order rate constant for 1O2 production was 2.12 × 10-1 min−1 (Fig. 3E).

The ability of AuNRsALG to serve as Fenton-like catalysts and convert hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) to •OH radicals was assessed using the MB method. A gradual reduction in MB absorbance was observed in a sample containing AuNRsALG, indicating the generation of •OH and confirming that AuNRsALG are capable of carrying out Fenton-like reactions (Fig. 3F). This observation is consistent with previous studies on Fenton-like catalytic activity of Au-based nanoparticles.

The overall ultrasound-triggered ROS production by AuNRsALG was measured using a non-specific DPBF probe, which forms non-fluorescent endoperoxide when reacting with various ROS, such as •OH, 1O2, hydrogen peroxide and superoxide anion. AuNRsALG caused a sharp reduction in the optical intensity of DPBF, resulting in a nearly 2.7-fold decrease in absorption peak at 420 nm after 5 min of ultrasound irradiation (Fig. 3G). The combined treatment of AuNRsALG and ultrasound caused over 63% decrease in DPBF concentration at the end of the treatment (Fig. 3H). The first-order rate constant calculated for ROS generation by AuNRsALG was 1.96 × 10-1 min−1 (Fig. 3I). As anticipated, these findings demonstrated the AuNRsALG could serve as potent nanosonosensitisers and Fenton-like agents for enhanced SDT by producing high amount of ROS under ultrasound irradiation. The schematic summary of findings is shown in Fig. 3J.

In vitro sonodynamic therapy (SDT) of AuNRsALG

To investigate the sonotherapeutic effect of AuNRsALG upon ultrasound irradiation, the viability of the cancer cells at various treatment conditions was evaluated. First, the sonotoxicity of AuNRsALG to MDA-MB-231 at different power intensities of ultrasound (0 – 1.5 W/cm2) was assessed via MTT assay. At the intensity of 1.5 W/cm2, cytotoxicity was observed in both untreated and AuNRsALG groups (Fig. 4A). Three minutes of ultrasound irradiation at 1.5 W/cm2 reduced the cell viability respectively by ∼92% (to 7.7 ± 0.3%) in untreated MDA-MB-231 and ∼95.1% (to 4.9 ± 2.1%) in MDA-MB-231 treated with 200 µg/ml AuNRsALG, suggesting that the cytotoxicity observed was primarily contributed by the extreme heat generated during the ultrasound irradiation. On the other hand, the ultrasound at 0.5 W/cm2 and 1.0 W/cm2 did not cause sonotoxicity (p > 0.05) in cells that were not treated with AuNRsALG, but reduced the cell viability respectively by ∼15% (to 85.4 ± 6.8%) and ∼69% (to 31.2 ± 10.8%) in MDA-MB-231 treated with 200 µg/ml AuNRsALG. Based on these findings, the ultrasound intensity of 1.0 W/cm2 that caused minimal damage to cells from inertial cavitation alone, while eliciting significant sonotoxicity when combined with AuNRsALG, was selected for the subsequent cell studies.

The in vitro dose-dependent sonotoxicity effect of AuNRsALG (0 – 300 µg/ml) at 1.0 W/cm2 was further evaluated on MDA-MB-231 via MTT assay (Fig. 4B). A significant sonotoxicity (p < 0.01) was observed at 150 µg/ml of AuNRsALG (approx. 0.75 nM of nanoparticles) while maximum cell death of 81.3 ± 9.6% was achieved at 300 µg/ml or ∼1.5 nM of nanoparticles equivalent, showing therapeutic potency at a nanomolar range of AuNRsALG when activated by ultrasound. The IC50 value of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT was 136 µg/ml or ∼0.68 nM. We also compared the in vitro cytotoxicity profiles of AuNRsALG activated via ultrasound (3 min of ultrasound irradiation at 1.0 W/cm2) to that activated by visible light (3 min of 638 nm laser irradiation at 1.0 W/cm2), considering the established role of AuNRs as photothermal transducers. The in vitro cytotoxicity profiles of AuNRsALG + ultrasound treatment in MDA-MB-231 cells resembled that of AuNRsALG + laser treatment, with insignificant cytotoxicity at AuNRsALG concentrations below 100 µg/ml (Fig. 4C). Although the laser treatment may cause a seemingly higher percentage of cell death (∼8%, ∼19% and ∼11% higher) when compared to that of the ultrasound group at 150, 200 and 300 µg/ml of AuNRsALG, these differences were statistically insignificant (p > 0.05). Such comparable in vitro cytotoxicity profiles have suggested the potential of using ultrasound, that has better tissue penetration than light, as the energy source for external activation of AuNRsALG to treat cancer.

Flow cytometry analysis was then performed to study the cell death pattern induced by the SDT of AuNRsALG. MDA-MB-231 were double-stained with Annexin V-FITC and propidium iodide (PI) fluorescent staining 6 h after treatment to evaluate the percentage of apoptotic and necrotic cells. The numbers of early apoptotic and late apoptotic cells increased obviously after AuNRsALG-mediated SDT treatment. The viability of MDA-MB-231 in AuNRsALG and ultrasound only groups were 91.5 ± 2.8% and 96.9 ± 1.5%, respectively, similar to the untreated group (Fig. 4D). In contrast, the proportion of viable cells treated with AuNRsALG + ultrasound decreased to 16.0 ± 2.6%, showing a nearly 6-fold reduction in cell survivability compared to the untreated group. Furthermore, the proportions of early and late apoptotic cells increased to 53.4 ± 6.6% and 30.0 ± 3.9%, respectively, while only a negligible population of necrotic cells (<1%) was detected (Fig. 4E). These results suggested that AuNRsALG-mediated SDT induces cancer cell death mainly via apoptosis rather than necrotic pathway, making it a suitable candidate for anti-cancer applications as necrosis often elicits an undesirable immune response in vivo .

To gain a systematic understanding of the mechanism underlying AuNRsALG-induced apoptosis, Western blot analysis was performed on cells after AuNRsALG + ultrasound treatment to evaluate the expression levels of apoptosis-associated proteins (Fig. 4F). In comparison to the untreated cells, the cells treated with AuNRsALG + ultrasound showed a significant increase (p < 0.001) in the expression of phosphorylated H2A histone (γH2AX), a marker for double-strand breaks in DNA. The γH2AX expression level in the combined treatment group was 15-fold and 3-fold higher compared to that induced by AuNRsALG or ultrasound alone, respectively (Fig. 4G). In addition, the combined treatment resulted in a significant downregulation (p < 0.001) of an anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2, which regulates the mitochondria-dependent pathway of cell death. The Bcl-2 expression level in the combined treatment was also 3-fold and 2-fold lower than AuNRsALG or ultrasound alone, respectively. These data confirmed that AuNRsALG-mediated SDT could cause DNA damage in cancer cells and likely induce apoptosis through the mitochondrial pathway.

Oxidative radical scavenging

The role of free oxidative radicals in the oncotherapeutic action of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT was investigated using the ROS scavenging method. Mannitol has been previously reported for its capability of scavenging free oxidative radicals. We showed that mannitol decreased the rate of ultrasound-triggered ROS production by AuNRsALG in vitro (Fig. 5A). MDA-MB-231, treated with 200 µg/ml of AuNRsALG and 25 mM mannitol, were irradiated with the ultrasound and assessed in terms of cell viability via MTT assay. The in vitro cytotoxicity of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT was found to decrease by 4-fold (from 68.8 ± 10.8% to 17.5 ± 4.4% cell death) with the addition of mannitol (p < 0.001, Fig. 5B). Further, the cell death pattern showed that the percentage of apoptotic cells at 6 h post-ultrasound irradiation decreased significantly (p < 0.001) to 9.9 ± 2.9% when mannitol was added, which was similar to the cell pattern of untreated MDA-MB-231 (Fig. 5C-D). These results further supported the theory that the sonotoxicity of AuNRsALG is ROS-dependent. Overall, the current findings evidenced that AuNRsALG have nanomolar scale sonosensitising properties and generate a high amount of oxidative radicals under ultrasound irradiation, killing cancer cells through ROS-mediated apoptosis.

Discussion

The advancements in sonodynamic therapy (SDT) have prompted investigations on more nanomaterials with sonosensitising properties to overcome the limitations of commercial sonosensitisers. In the past, gold nanorods (AuNRs) have been widely employed as photodynamic and photothermal agents, owing to their excellent biocompatibility, tunable optical properties and flexible surface functionalisation. However, no attempts have been made to investigate their potential as nanosonosensitising agents for SDT. Thus, in this study, we reported the feasibility of alginate-coated AuNRs (AuNRsALG) to serve as nanosonosensitisers under ultrasound irradiation and kill breast cancer cells.

AuNRsALG have demonstrated an excellent ability to generate a high amount of free oxidative radicals and induce sub-nanomolar therapeutic potency (IC50 at 0.68 nM) in triple-negative breast cancer cells. The anti-cancer effectivity of AuNRsALG can be explained by their remarkable ability to utilise ultrasound energy to catalyse a series of sonochemical reactions, producing diverse reactive oxygen species (ROS). The proposed mechanism is as follows:

Firstly, AuNRsALG were found to enhance the rate of inertial cavitation during ultrasound irradiation. Under continuous ultrasound, the cavitation bubbles oscillate, grow to their critical size and collapse, releasing the energy to the surrounding medium and leading to a significant rise in the local temperature (>5000 K) and pressure (>800 atm). Such extreme conditions promote the pyrolysis of water and the generation of toxic •OH radicals (Equation 3), contributing to the sonodynamic eradication of cancers. In the presence of AuNRsALG, we observed an enhanced cavitation bubble formation (Fig. 3A), along with a significant rise in temperature (Fig. S6) and considerable production of •OH under ultrasound irradiation (Fig. 3C). Our results suggested that AuNRsALG can act as a source of bubble nucleation upon ultrasound irradiation, reducing the cavitation threshold and increasing the formation rate of cavitation bubbles, which is consistent with previous studies on Au nanospheres. The ability of AuNRsALG to promote cavitation and production of •OH indicated their role in mediating sonochemical reactions to cause cancer cell death.

Secondly, AuNRsALG were also activable by the ultrasound to stimulate singlet oxygen (1O2) generation (Fig. 3E). Extreme thermal conditions following the bubble collapse create sub-nanosecond pulses of light in the wavelength range of 200 – 800 nm, a phenomenon termed sonoluminescence. Nanosonosensitisers can harness energy from this emitted light to convert molecular oxygen from the triplet ground state 3O2 to the first excited singlet state 1O2 (Equation 4). AuNRs have a strong local surface plasmon resonance effect, characterised by the collective oscillation of electrons in the conduction band upon excitation by light of a specific wavelength. These unique optical properties of AuNRsALG enable them to harness light energy from the sonoluminescence and generate 1O2 under ultrasound irradiation, and may have contributed to their sonotoxicity on the tested breast cancer cells.

Thirdly, AuNRsALG can act as Fenton-like catalysts and decompose hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) into •OH radicals. H2O2 is highly abundant in the tumour microenvironment and contributes to therapy resistance in cancers. Thus, targeted degradation of H2O2 in the tumour microenvironment can further increase the concentration of free oxidative radicals and maximise the efficacy of ROS-based therapies. In our study, it is proposed that AuNRsALG increased the concentration of •OH radicals through catalysing Fenton-like process, wherein H2O2 is reduced by Au0 in AuNRsALG to •OH, while Au0 is oxidised into Au3+ (Equation 5).

In addition, AuNRs have recently been reported to have a dipole-like potential gradient on their surface, attributed to the uneven distribution of surface coating moieties. The resulting electrostatic asymmetry along the long axes of nanorods makes them behave like non-centrosymmetric particles and creates unequal polarisation on the two ends. This, in principle, can allow AuNRsALG to be polarised under applied mechanical force, such as ultrasound irradiation, and carry out piezocatalytic redox reactions in different poles of the nanorods. The negatively charged region of AuNRsALG can interact with dissolved oxygen to produce superoxide anions (Equation 6), while the positively charged region of AuNRsALG can react with water molecules to form •OH and H+ (Equation 7). Previously, a similar piezocatalytic effect has been reported in other nanomaterials that show a lack of central symmetry for sonodynamic applications. This piezocatalytic effect could have contributed to the high production rate of ROS of AuNRsALG under ultrasound irradiation and further highlighted their potential to be used as nanosonosensitisers for SDT by inducing a synergistic sono-piezocatalytic effect to kill breast cancer cells in this study. The proposed sonocatalytic mechanism of AuNRsALG is illustrated in Fig. 6.

It is noteworthy that the overall ROS production by AuNRsALG was better than that of existing nanosonosensitisers. Currently, titanium dioxide (TiO2) nanospheres are deemed one of the most extensively studied inorganic nanosonosensitisers, with its mechanism of SDT being relatively well-defined compared to other nanomaterials. The sonodynamic performance of bare TiO2 nanospheres, however, is limited by the low quantum yield of ROS generation due to fast electron-hole recombination. Therefore, attempts have been made to integrate TiO2 nanospheres with noble metals (e.g., Au and platinum) or other doping metals (e.g., copper, iron and tungsten) in efforts to enhance ROS generation, resulting in the overall rise in system complexity. Our results showed that AuNRsALG induced ROS generation more efficiently than commercial and Au-containing TiO2 sonosensitisers. The rate constant for the ROS production by AuNRsALG was calculated to be 1.96 × 10-1 min−1, which is 3 to 8-fold higher than the reported values of TiO2 nanospheres, Au-TiO2 nanosheets or Au-TiO2 nanocomposites previously studied for SDT. In addition, compared with Ti-coated Au nanoplates that achieved about 30% DPBF depletion after 5 min of ultrasound irradiation (1.5 W/cm2, 3.0 MHz), AuNRsALG achieved 63.4% DPBF depletion in the same duration at a lower ultrasound intensity and frequency (1.0 W/cm2, 1.0 MHz). The results, hence, demonstrate the potential of AuNRsALG as efficient nanosonosensitisers on their own without doping to other inorganic sonosensitisers, which substantially reduces the complexity of the overall nanosystem. Such property underscores the practicality of AuNRsALG as nanosonosensitisers due to their time and cost-effective preparation.

Moreover, a sonotoxicity study showed that the high ROS generation efficacy of AuNRsALG corresponded to effective cancer cell killing in vitro. When coupled with ultrasound irradiation, AuNRsALG elicited significant sonotoxicity on breast cancer cells at a concentration as low as 150 µg/ml, in a dose-dependent and power-dependent manner. AuNRsALG irradiated by ultrasound at lower power intensity (1.0 W/cm2) and shorter duration (3 min) was also found to produce higher in vitro efficacy (10 – 45% higher sonotoxicity) than the conventional unmodified Ti-based nanospheres and nanorods irradiated by ultrasound at a higher intensity (1.5 – 3.0 W/cm2) and longer duration (5 min). Apart from this, AuNRsALG-mediated SDT also displayed comparable cancer cell killing efficacy to that of other TiO2-based nanosonosensitisers modified with Au (1.5 W/cm2; 5 min), copper (2.0 W/cm2; 5 min), tungsten (2.0 W/cm2; 5 min), or vanadium (3.0 W/cm2; 5 min). The efficient sonotoxicity exhibited by stand-alone AuNRsALG when irradiated with ultrasound at moderate power intensity suggests their potential as clinically useful sonosensitisers.

Previous experimental results have shown that metal-based nanosonosensitisers inhibit cancer cell proliferation by acting through apoptosis and/or necrosis pathway. Thus, we performed Annexin V-FITC/PI staining followed by flow cytometry analysis to elucidate the mechanistic basis of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT. Treatment of cancer cells with AuNRsALG resulted in an approximately 6-fold reduction in cell viability, primarily through apoptosis (∼83%). The ability of AuNRsALG to trigger an apoptotic response in cancer cells is better than that of TiO2 nanorods, nanosheets, and nanocomposites, which reported 22 – 52% tumour cell apoptosis under ultrasound treatment of similar parameters. Among sonosensitisers of noble metals, the apoptosis rate of AuNRsALG is also more prominent than that of the Au nanospheres (30 – 69% apoptosis) and Pt nanohybrids (15 – 38% apoptosis). Additionally, our finding showed that AuNRsALG-mediated SDT caused minimal necrosis, which is in concord with other studies of Ti or porphyrin-based sonosensitisers. Notably, the ability of AuNRsALG to induce predominantly apoptosis instead of necrosis is beneficial because the latter is often associated with undesirable immune responses in vivo .

Metal nanosonosensitisers have been shown to elicit apoptosis through oxidative stress-induced DNA damage. Phosphorylated H2AX histone (γH2AX) is a crucial marker for DNA double-strand breaks. In this study, SDT of AuNRsALG was shown to significantly increase the expression level of γH2AX, indicating severe DNA fragmentation in cancer cells. A large-scale DNA damage leads to the activation of DNA damage response pathway and expression of p53 genes. p53 is a transcriptional factor that governs cancer cell apoptosis by upregulating the expression of pro-apoptotic protein Bax and downregulating the expression of anti-apoptotic protein Bcl-2, followed by cell death through the mitochondrial pathway. A prominent decrease in Bcl-2 expression after treatment was observed in this study. Together with the high expression of DNA damage marker, these results indicate that AuNRsALG can cause serious DNA damage and induce mitochondrial membrane permeabilisation in cancer cells.

To validate that the anti-cancer effect of AuNRsALG-mediated SDT is driven by ROS production, we performed a ROS scavenging assay. The addition of mannitol, a ROS scavenger, resulted in a significant increase (∼51%) in cell viability and a decrease (∼73%) in the apoptotic cell population to a level similar to that of untreated cells. This confirmed that the sonotoxicity induced by AuNRsALG-mediated SDT is ROS-dependent. The ultrasound-triggered ROS can promote apoptotic cell death by activating a cascade of physiological events including DNA fragmentation, cytoskeletal shrinkage, and chromatin condensation, and may be connected with mitochondrial dysfunction. Therefore, AuNRsALG are posed as promising nanosonosensitisers for SDT.

Conclusion

This study exhibited the first evidence on the ultrasound-triggered reactive oxygen species (ROS) production by AuNRs for effective sonodynamic therapy (SDT). In the presence of AuNRsALG, the therapeutic effects of ultrasound irradiation were strongly enhanced, achieving high cancer cell killing efficacy in a dose-dependent manner at a nanomolar level. Furthermore, investigations showed that the killing mechanism by SDT of AuNRsALG was ROS-dependent, eliciting cancer cell death through DNA damage-induced apoptosis. Our discovery unfolds a new function of AuNRs in addition to their well-established roles as photodynamic and photothermal agents, further expanding their range of biomedical applications. Although further studies of the purported mechanisms are needed, AuNRsALG-mediated SDT poses as a promising treatment approach owing to the biocompatibility of the AuNRsALG as well as the non-invasiveness and deep tissue penetration of the ultrasound.
