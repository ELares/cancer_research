# Redox-responsive metal-organic framework nanocapsules enhance tumor chemo-immunotherapy by modulating tumor metabolic reprogramming.

## Abstract

Immunotherapy, particularly immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), has opened the era of modern oncology, offering significant promise for modern oncology. However, the efficacy of immunotherapy is frequently curtailed by the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (ITM), a milieu shaped by tumor metabolic reprogramming. Herein, a novel tumor microenvironment-responsive nanocapsules (DNMCs) were developed that simultaneously modulate tumor metabolism and the ITM to enhance the effectiveness of chemo-immunotherapy. DNMCs consist of an acidic and redox-sensitive metal-organic framework (MOF) encapsulating Doxorubicin (DOX) and the indoleamine-2,3-dioxygenase1 (IDO1) inhibitor NLG919. In the tumor microenvironment, DNMCs degrade, rapidly releasing DOX and NLG919. DOX induces immunogenic cell death (ICD), while NLG919 regulates amino acid metabolism by modulating IDO1 activity, thereby reversing the immunosuppressive of ITM. Consequently, DNMCs elicit effective anti-tumor immune responses, characterized by an increased density of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ cytotoxic T cells as well as depletion of immunosuppressive regulatory T cells (Tregs), thus effectively suppressing pancreatic cancer growth in KPC mice through combined chemo-immunotherapy. Overall, DNMCs exhibit significant tumor growth inhibition in pancreatic cancer patient-derived organoids (PDOs) and mouse models. This study presents a promising approach to enhancing chemo-immunotherapy by targeting tumor metabolic reprogramming and augmenting immune response against malignant tumors.

Introduction

Tumors often develop stealthily and progress rapidly, presenting significant challenges for clinical management. Recently, checkpoint blockade immunotherapy (CBI) has shown promise in treating pancreatic cancer. However, its clinical effectiveness is hindered by a low response rate, largely due to the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (ITM). Emerging evidence suggests that metabolic reprogramming plays a critical role in shaping the ITM. Specifically, tumors often overexpress indoleamine-2,3-dioxygenase1 (IDO1), which accelerates the degradation of L-tryptophan (Trp) into kynurenine (Kyn). Trp depletion suppresses the activity of CD8+ T lymphocytes, while the accumulation of Kyn further impairs anti-tumor immunity by promoting regulatory T cells (Tregs) and inhibiting cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs). This metabolic alteration contributes to the ITM, enhancing tumor growth and metastasis. Inhibiting IDO1 activity and modulating tumor metabolism are therefore critical strategies to reverse the ITM and improve the efficacy of CBI. However, the clinical use of IDO1 inhibitors as a monotherapy has limited success in controlling tumor growth. On the other hand, chemotherapy drugs like Doxorubicin (DOX) can induce immunogenic cell death (ICD), releasing damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) that promote dendritic cells (DCs) maturation, CTL infiltration and initiate anti-tumor immunity. Despite this, the immune response triggered by ICD is often dampened by the tumor-specific ITM, which significantly reduces its effectiveness. To overcome IDO1-mediated immunosuppression and enhance the immune response, combining chemotherapy with IDO1 inhibitors represents a promising strategy. This approach may help to not only boost anti-tumor immunity but also improve the overall efficacy of cancer therapies.

Nano-drug delivery systems (Nano-DDS) offer an efficient means of co-delivering anti-cancer drugs and IDO1 inhibitors, enabling the activation of ICD to stimulate anti-tumor immune responses while counteracting the immunosuppressive effects of IDO1. For example, Luo's group developed a phenylboronic acid (PBA)-shieldable dendritic nanomedicine that combines an ICD-inducing agent (epirubicin) with an IDO1 inhibitor (NLG919) for enhanced tumor chemo-immunotherapy. Similarly, Zhao's group created hierarchical hydrogel microparticles (MDDP) that deliver oxaliplatin and NLG919 as nano-prodrugs, improving therapeutic efficacy while minimizing the systemic toxicity of chemotherapeutic agents. Despite promising results, self-assembled co-delivery strategies involving chemotherapeutic drugs and IDO1 inhibitors still face challenges related to low drug loading capacities and inefficient targeting or release within the tumor microenvironment. To address these limitations, there is a critical need for Nano-DDS that can efficiently load and precisely release drugs. Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are a promising solution due to their highly porous structure, which allows for the accommodation of a large number of drug molecules, improving loading capacity and delivery efficiency. However, MOF nanomedicines typically respond only to weak acids in the tumor microenvironment, resulting in suboptimal drug release rates. To enhance the effectiveness of chemo-immunotherapy, it is essential to design redox-responsive MOF-based nanomedicines that not only exhibit high drug loading but also trigger rapid release in response to both weak acidic conditions and elevated levels of glutathione (GSH) commonly found in the tumor microenvironment. By combining redox-sensitive MOFs with chemotherapy and immunotherapy, this approach could provide a highly effective strategy for the treatment of pancreatic cancer.

In this study, a novel tumor microenvironment-responsive binary synergistic nanocapsules were developed for immunochemotherapy, designed by encapsulating DOX and NLG919 within a redox-responsive MOF nanorod, referred to as DNMCs. These DNMCs offer high drug loading efficiency and a redox-responsive release profile, achieved through the incorporation of redox-sensitive small molecule ligands. The tumor microenvironment triggers DNMCs degradation and releases DOX and NLG919. DOX induces ICD in dying tumor cells, stimulating adaptive anti-tumor immune response, while NLG919 inhibits IDO1 enzyme activity, counteracting the ITM. In a pancreatic cancer xenograft mouse model, DNMCs significantly outperformed free DOX or the combination of free DOX and NLG919 in inhibiting tumor growth. Thus, DNMCs offer a promising alternative to checkpoint blockade therapy by reversing the ITM and enhancing the anti-tumor immune response.

Materials and methods

Preparation of MOF-SS nanorod

The MOF-SS was synthesized using a simple solvothermal method. Typically, 4.0 mmol of FeCl3·6H2O and 5.0 mmol of 2,2′-dithiodibenzoic acid (2,2′-DTBA) were dissolved in 54 mL of DMF, along with 6 mL of 0.4 M NaOH. The resulting solution was transferred to a 100 mL Teflon-lined stainless-steel autoclave, sealed, and heated at 100 °C for 24 h. After cooling, the mixture was centrifuged and washed three times with DMF and ethanol. The final product, MOF-SS, was obtained as a brown powder.

Preparation and characterization of the DNMCs nanocapsules

50 mg of MOF-SS were dispersed in 10 mL of methanol, and then 5 mL of methanol containing DOX (15 mg), NLG919 (15 mg), and Et3N (15 μL) was added. The mixture was stirred at 25 °C for 24 h. After evaporating the methanol, the resulting mixture was dispersed in 10 mL of deionized water containing 50 mg of F127. After stirring overnight, the drug-loaded DNMCs (DOX+NLG919@MOF-SS nanocapsules) were obtained by centrifugation, washed with deionized water, and dried under vacuum.

The drug loading content (DLC) of DOX and NLG919 in DNMCs was determined as follows: 5 mg of DNMCs was dispersed in an acetic acid buffer solution (pH 5.0) containing 10 mM GSH. The mixture was sonicated for 30 min, then centrifuged. The resulting precipitate was washed with the buffer solution until it became colorless, and the supernatant was collected. The drug content of DOX was quantified by measuring the absorbance at 485 nm using a UV–vis spectrophotometer. For NLG919, the insoluble substance was dissolved in methanol, and the concentration was determined by High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC).

Cell viability

The cell viability was analyzed through a standard CCK-8 method using PANC-1 cells. PANC-1 cells was cultured in DMEM (containing 10 % fetal bovine serum) at 5 × 103 cells/well, transferred into 96-well plates, and incubated for 24 h. Then, the culture medium was replaced by fresh DMEM containing free NLG919, free DOX, and DNMCs. After incubation for 48 h, 10 μL of CCK-8 solution (5 mg/mL) was added. The absorbance at 450 nm was then measured on a microplate reader. The biocompatibility of MOF-SS and DNMCs to HPNE cells was tested by the same method mentioned above.

Construction and proliferation of pancreatic patient-derived organoids (PDOs)

PDOs were generated using fresh tumor tissues from patients with pancreatic cancer who underwent surgery at Lanzhou University Second Hospital. The tumor tissues were collected in accordance with the approval of the human research ethics committee of Lanzhou university second hospital and with the informed consent of all study participants (Approval No: 2024A-143). The construction method of pancreatic cancer PDOs is referred to our previous work.

For viability detection of PC PDOs after treatment of free DOX and DNMCs, approximately 100 spheroids per well were suspended in BME diluted with medium and seeded in 96-well plates. Free DOX and DNMCs (the equivalent concentration of DOX was 0, 0.0032, 0.016, 0.08, 0.4, 2, 10, 50 μg/mL) was added, cultured for 48 h, CellTiter-Glo® 3D cell viability assay kit was used to detect the viability of organoids.

In order to observe the effect of DNMCs on organoid growth more directly, free DOX and DNMCs (the equivalent concentration of DOX was 0.15 μg/mL) was added when organoids were growing well. Bright field photographs of organoids were taken by fluorescence microscope at set intervals, the growth rate of organoids was evaluated by measuring the area and morphology of organoid throughout the intervention period for 6 days, respectively.

In vivo antitumor effect

Six-week-old female C57BL/6JGpt mice were obtained from the Gempharmatech Co., Ltd (Chengdu, China). All animal experiments were performed according to the protocols approved by the Laboratory Animal Ethics Committee of Lanzhou University Second Hospital (Approval No: D2024-162). The construction method of genetically engineered mice (LSL-KrasG12D/+, LSL-Trp53R172H/+, Pdx1-Cre (KPC)) used in this experiment was described in our previous work. Pancreatic tumor-bearing mice were established by implanting the tumor from KPC mice into the right axilla of C57BL/6JGpt mice. When the volume of tumors reached ∼150 mm3, the mice were randomly divided into six groups (saline, MOF-SS, free DOX, free NLG919, DOX+NLG919 and DNMCs, 6 mice/group). The injection was performed every 3 days, equivalent doses of DOX and NLG919 for related groups were 5.0 and 2.8 mg kg−1, respectively. The body weight and tumor volume of mice were measured every other day. Tumor volume (Vt) = a × b2/2, where a represents the length of tumor and b represents the width of the tumor. The relative tumor volume = Vt/V0, where V0 represents initial volume of tumor in every group before treatments. When the tumor volume reaches 1500 mm3, all mice were sacrificed, main organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney) and the tumors tissues were resected and fixed with 4 % paraformaldehyde for H&E staining, immunochemical staining for Ki67, CD8 and FOXP3 and immunofluorescent staining for CRT.

Statistical analysis

All experimental data were represented as the mean ± SD through at least three experiments. One-way analysis of variance statistical method was used to analyze the experimental results through GraphPad Prism 8.4.3 software (San Diego, CA). A P value of 0.05 was selected as a significance level, and the data were indicated with (∗) for P < 0.05, (∗∗) for P < 0.01, (∗∗∗) for P < 0.001 and ns represent no significance, one-way ANOVA.

Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization of redox-responsive DNMCs

The synthesis of the redox-responsive nanocapsules (DNMCs) is illustrated in Fig. 1. The redox-responsive metal-organic framework (MOF-SS) nanorods were synthesized via a simple solvothermal reaction using Fe3+ ions and 2,2′-DTBA. As shown in Fig. 2a and b, the morphology of the MOF-SS was characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), revealing a regular rod-like structure with a width of approximately 28 nm. Its hydrodynamic diameter is about 220 nm (Fig. S2a). Additionally, energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) analysis confirmed a uniform distribution of Fe, S, C, and O elements within the MOF-SS (Fig. 2c).

The redox-responsive MOF-SS was used as a template to encapsulate NLG919 and DOX, followed by the coating of F127 on the surface to stabilize the drug carrier, resulting in the successful formation of dark red powder DNMCs (Fig. S1). As shown in Fig. 2d and e, the DNMCs retain a uniform nanorod structure and its mean hydrodynamic diameter is 248 nm (Fig. S2b). The redox-responsive behavior of DNMCs was further evaluated by TEM imaging after incubation in a GSH solution for various time periods (Fig. 2f–h). Initially, the DNMCs maintained their nanorod structure after 1 h of incubation in a pH 5.0 and 10 mM GSH solution. After 4 h, the rod-like structure began to degrade, with the formation of smaller particles. After 12 h, substantial degradation occurred, with only a minimal amount of nanorod structure remaining. The degradation characteristics of DNMCs in pH 5.0 ABS containing 10.0 mM GSH were investigated using dynamic light scattering (DLS). The results indicated that over time, DNMCs gradually degraded, leading to agglomeration and an increase in hydrodynamic particle size. Meanwhile, the presence of smaller particle sizes was attributed to the dispersion of some degradation products in the solution (Fig. S2c). These observations confirm that DNMCs exhibit clear redox-responsive behavior, as the GSH concentration in the tumor microenvironment is significantly higher than in normal physiological conditions, making DNMCs suitable for targeted drug delivery in cancer therapy. The X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis reveals distinct diffraction peaks for MOF-SS (Fig. 2i), indicating the formation of a crystalline structure. After loading with DOX and NLG919, DNMCs maintain similar diffraction peaks, suggesting the preservation of the crystalline structure. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to analyze the chemical composition of MOF-SS, revealing the presence of Fe, S, C, and O elements. DNMCs exhibit a similar composition, confirming the successful encapsulation of the drugs (Fig. 2j). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed to assess the thermal stability and organic content of MOF-SS. The results show that MOF-SS nanorod begin to degrade at 200 °C, with complete decomposition at 500 °C and approximately 50 wt% organic ligand content (Fig. S3). Fourier-transform infrared (FT-IR) spectroscopy further confirmed the structure of the precursor compound and DNMCs (Fig. S4a). UV–vis absorbance spectra showed that DOX had a characteristic peak at 484 nm, while NLG919 had a peak at 280 nm, which overlaps with other components, complicating content determination (Fig. S4b). Consequently, UV–vis spectrum was used to measure DOX content, and HPLC was employed to quantify NLG919. The drug content of DOX in DNMCs was found to be approximately 21.02 wt%, while NLG919 content was 11.72 wt%. To investigate the redox-sensitive drug release properties of DNMCs, the drug release profile was examined in different buffer solutions over time. As shown in Fig. 2k, DOX release from DNMCs was significantly higher in a pH 5.0 buffer with 10 mM GSH (simulating the tumor microenvironment), with a cumulative release of 87.4 % over 86 h. This enhanced release is attributed to the combined effects of high glutathione concentration, which induces cleavage of disulfide bonds in the organic ligands, protonation of the organic ligands in the acidic environment that disrupts coordination bonds between iron and 2,2′-DTBA, and the increased solubility of doxorubicin under acidic conditions. In contrast, only 22 % of DOX was released in a pH 7.4 PBS solution with 10 μM GSH (representing the physiological environment), highlighting the strong redox responsiveness of DNMCs to the tumor microenvironment.

Anti-proliferation effect in vitro

The endocytosis of DNMCs in PANC-1 cells was assessed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM). The cell nuclei were stained with DAPI, and cells were incubated with either free DOX or DNMCs. The distribution of the drug within the cells was observed by comparing the fluorescence signals of DOX and DAPI. As shown in Fig. 3a, after 6 h of incubation, DNMCs exhibited stronger red fluorescence in both the cytoplasm and nuclei of the cancer cells compared to free DOX. This suggests that DNMCs are efficiently internalized and subsequently deliver DOX into the nucleus, where it exerts its cytotoxic effects.

The in vitro cytotoxicity of MOF-SS and DNMCs was evaluated using HPNE and PANC-1 cells separately. After 48 h of incubation with either MOF-SS or DNMCs, cell viability was assessed using a cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8) assay. The results showed that MOF-SS exhibited no significant cytotoxicity on HPNE cells, even at a concentration of 160 μg/mL (Fig. S5a). For DNMCs, the viability of HPNE cells remained above 60 % when DOX equivalent concentration was as high as 10 μg/mL, indicating that DNMCs had little biological toxicity to normal cells, indicating its good stability and toxic side effects in normal physiological environment (Fig. S5b). In contrast, the cytotoxicity of DNMCs to PANC-1 increased in proportion to their concentration (Figs. S5 and 3b). DNMCs demonstrated an IC50 of 0.56 μg/mL (based on DOX content), which was approximately 1.48-fold lower than that of free DOX (Fig. 3b). In addition, the IC50 of NLG919 on PANC-1 cells was determined to be 9.53 μg/mL (Fig. S5c), which was 17-fold higher than that of DMNCs, indicating that NLG919 alone possesses no apparent cytotoxicity against PANC-1 cells at the IC50 dose of DMNCs.

The anti-proliferative effects of the different components were assessed using Ki67 immunofluorescence staining. As shown in Fig. 3c and d, the confocal images and Ki67 fluorescence signals indicated that MOF-SS and NLG919 had no significant impact on cell proliferation. In contrast, formulations containing DOX (free DOX, DOX+NLG919, and DNMCs) exhibited marked anti-proliferative activity. Notably, DNMCs demonstrated the strongest inhibition of cell proliferation, likely due to the higher concentration of DOX delivered into the cells and nuclei, which induces DNA damage and suppresses cell growth.

ICD effect, DCs maturation and IDO1 inhibition induced by DNMCs in vitro

Previous studies have shown that DOX can induce ICD in various cancer cells. During ICD induction, dying tumor cells release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), such as calreticulin (CRT), a pro-apoptotic protein that migrates from the endoplasmic reticulum to the cell surface, triggering anti-tumor immune responses. Additionally, tumor cell-derived ATP and high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) promote macrophage secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines and stimulate DCs maturation. To assess ICD induction in PANC-1 cells, the release of DAMPs following different treatments were evaluated. The cell surface expression of CRT and extracellular release of HMGB1 were analyzed using immunofluorescence. As shown in Fig. 4a and c, DNMCs induced higher CRT expression compared to the DOX and DOX+NLG919 groups. Additionally, free DOX, DOX+NLG919, and DNMCs, significantly promoted the extracellular release of HMGB1 (Fig. 4b and d). The release of ATP, an additional DAMP, was quantified using a commercial ATP assay kit (Fig. 4e). DNMCs elicited a notably higher ATP release from tumor cells, confirming their high efficacy in inducing ICD. These findings highlight the potent ability of DNMCs to trigger ICD in cancer cells.

DCs play a crucial role in bridging innate and adaptive immunity. Mature DCs are highly effective at processing and presenting antigens by binding antigen fragments to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules and presenting them to T cells to initiate an immune response. Therefore, the level of DCs maturation is an important indicator of the immune system's capability. Cytokines released during tumor cell death can stimulate DCs maturation, enhancing their ability to recognize and present tumor antigens. To assess the effect of ICD-induced DCs maturation, mouse bone marrow-derived immature dendritic cells (JAWSII) were incubated with MOF-SS, DOX, NLG919, DOX+NLG919, and DNMCs. DCs maturation was evaluated by flow cytometry based on the expression of CD11c, CD80, and CD86 (Fig. 5a and b). The results showed that DOX and DOX+NLG919 treatments induced modest DCs maturation (5.58 % and 11.3 %) after 48 h. In contrast, DNMCs induced a significant increase in DC maturation (17.5 %). These findings suggest that DNMCs effectively enhance the immune response against tumor cells through ICD-induced DCs maturation, positioning DNMCs as a promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy.

The IDO1 enzyme is highly expressed in tumors, where it serves as a negative regulator of immune responses. It catalyzes the degradation of Trp into Kyn, which contributes to the ITM by promoting the proliferation and differentiation of regulatory T cells (Tregs). Therefore, inhibiting IDO1 activity to regulate Trp/Kyn metabolism in tumor cells is a promising strategy to reverse the ITM. To evaluate the ability of NLG919 and DNMCs to inhibit IDO1 enzyme activity, high IDO1 expression in PANC-1 cells was induced using exogenous interferon-γ (IFN-γ), followed by measurement of Kyn levels in the cells after treatment with free NLG919 or DNMCs (Fig. 5c).The results showed that at the equivalent dose of NLG919, free NLG919 and DNMCs significantly reduced Kyn levels compared to the control group. DNMCs exhibited IDO1 inhibitory activity comparable to that of free NLG919. Next, we assessed the impact of chemotherapy combined with immunotherapy using DNMCs on apoptosis rates via flow cytometry. The results revealed that MOF-SS and NLG919 alone had no significant effect on apoptosis. Treatment with DOX alone and the DOX+NLG919 combination induced moderate apoptosis, while DNMCs with an equivalent DOX concentration resulted in the highest level of apoptosis (Fig. 5d and e). This can be primarily attributed to the superior drug delivery efficiency and greater intracellular drug accumulation of DNMCs compared to DOX and DOX+NLG919, thereby enhancing the drug's biological activity.

Effect of DNMCs on PDOs viability of PDAC

To further evaluate the therapeutic efficacy of DNMCs against pancreatic cancer in vitro, we established a PDOs model of pancreatic cancer to simulate the inhibitory effects of DNMCs on tumor progression more accurately. Initially, the IC50 values of free DOX and DNMCs for the pancreatic cancer PDOs were determined. The results showed that the IC50 of DNMCs (0.29 μg/mL) was approximately 8 times lower than that of free DOX (Fig. S6a). Next, the growth of pancreatic cancer PDOs treated with DOX and DNMCs was observed under a microscope. The control group (Ctrl) displayed a clear increase in both area and well-defined outline, indicating active growth. In contrast, both the DOX and DNMCs groups effectively inhibited the growth of the organoids (Figs. S6b–c). After extended observation, the organoids treated with DNMCs began to disintegrate, suggesting that DNMCs were more effective in suppressing the growth of human pancreatic cancer organoids and exhibited superior ability to inhibit pancreatic cancer progression.

In vivo therapeutic study in KPC model

The antitumor efficacy of DNMCs, along with its control groups, was evaluated in a KPC pancreatic cancer mouse model. As shown in Fig. 6a, KPC mice were randomly divided into six groups (n = 6) when the tumor volume reached 150 mm³. The groups received intravenous injections of different treatments: Ctrl (saline), MOF-SS, DOX, NLG919, DOX+NLG919, and DNMCs. The doses of DOX and NLG919 were 5.0 mg/kg and 2.8 mg/kg, respectively. Tumor growth was monitored until it reached the ethical endpoint of 1500 mm³. As shown in Fig. 6b, the MOF-SS and free NLG919 groups did not exhibit significant tumor regression. DOX alone showed moderate tumor growth inhibition. The combination of DOX and NLG919 enhanced antitumor activity compared to DOX monotherapy. Notably, DNMCs demonstrated the most significant antitumor effect, completely inhibiting pancreatic cancer tumor growth without causing substantial weight loss (Fig. 6c–e). In contrast, the free NLG919 and DOX+NLG919 groups showed significant weight loss, indicating stronger toxic side effects associated with the free drugs. To further assess the safety of DNMCs, histological analysis of major organs was performed using hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining, along with a hemolysis test. As shown in Fig. 6f and Fig. S7, the heart tissues from mice treated with DOX and DOX+NLG919 displayed severe histological damage, whereas heart sections from the DNMCs-treated mice showed minimal damage and no noticeable hemolysis. These results suggest that DNMCs offer excellent biocompatibility and a favorable safety profile compared to other treatment options.

In vivo ICD effect, IDO1 inhibition and immune microenvironment regulation

To investigate the superior antitumor mechanism of DNMCs, tumor tissues from different treatment groups were paraffin-embedded and stained for the ICD marker calreticulin (CRT) to assess the synergistic effects of DNMCs-induced ICD. CLSM images and corresponding quantification of tumor sections revealed that DNMCs significantly enhanced CRT expression in tumor cells (Fig. S8). The CRT-positive area in the DNMCs group was 1.3- and 1.7-fold higher compared to the DOX+NLG919 and DOX groups, respectively, indicating that DNMCs strongly induce ICD in vivo. Next, whether ICD induction by DNMCs accelerates DCs maturation, a key component of the systemic immune response was evaluated. Flow cytometry analysis of DCs maturation (CD86+CD80+CD11c+ cells) in tumor tissues revealed that DNMCs treatment resulted in a 1.88-fold and 1.25-fold higher maturation rate compared to free DOX (11.5 %) and DOX+NLG919 (17.0 %) groups, respectively (Fig. 7a and b). To further assess the systemic immune response, the concentration of IFN-γ, a cytokine released by activated DCs was measured, using an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). The intratumoral IFN-γ levels in the DNMCs group were 1.44-fold higher than those in the DOX+NLG919 group (Fig. 7c). These results confirm that DNMCs effectively promote DCs maturation and elicit antitumor immunity by inducing ICD in both tumor cells and the systemic immune system.

The enzymatic activity of IDO1 was assessed by measuring the Kyn/Trp ratio in tumor tissues. Notably, the highest Kyn/Trp ratio was observed in the Ctrl and MOF-SS groups, indicating the presence of a strong tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment. However, treatment with DOX+NLG919 or DNMCs significantly reduced the Kyn/Trp ratio, which was approximately 1.8- and 2.5-fold lower, respectively, compared to the NLG919 group. This reduction reflects the IDO1 inhibition by NLG919 and suggests effective reprogramming of tumor amino acid metabolism (Fig. 7d). To further investigate the impact of treatments on tumor growth, immunohistochemical staining was performed to evaluate cell proliferation and CD8+ T cell infiltration in tumor tissues. Mice treated with DOX+NLG919 or DNMCs showed significantly reduced tumor proliferation and enhanced CD8+ T cell infiltration, while the other treatment groups exhibited minimal immune cell infiltration (Fig. 7e and f). Since Kyn levels influence the activity of regulatory T cells (Tregs), which are associated with an immunosuppressive microenvironment, the expression of Tregs in KPC tumor tissues were examined. The Ctrl group showed 3.25 % positive staining for Treg infiltration, confirming an immunosuppressive environment. However, DNMCs treatment drastically reduced Treg infiltration to 0.26 % (Fig. 7e and g), accompanied by a significant decrease in tumor cell proliferation (Fig. 7e and h). These results indicate that DNMCs effectively activate antitumor immunity by both chemotherapy and metabolic reprogramming, leading to a sustained immune response and notable tumor regression.

Conclusions

In summary, a novel tumor microenvironment-responsive nanomedicine (DNMCs) was constructed that enhances the efficacy of chemotherapy combined with immunotherapy by reversing the ITM. DNMCs consist of organic ligands with disulfide bonds and Fe3+ ions, which simultaneously load the chemotherapeutic drug DOX and the immunomodulator NLG919. This formulation effectively delivers the drugs to the tumor site, where they are released in response to the high GSH levels and acidic conditions typical of tumor tissues. DOX triggers ICD in tumor cells, enhancing their immunogenicity, while NLG919 inhibits the immunosuppressive effects of Tregs by blocking the IDO1 enzyme, which prevents the conversion of Trp into Kyn. The ICD effect induced by DNMCs promotes the maturation of DCs, while the inhibition of IDO1 reduces Treg infiltration and suppresses their activity. This combined effect boosts the infiltration of CD8+ T cells into the tumor, enhancing the overall anti-tumor immune response. Due to its high drug payload, tumor-targeting capability, and responsive drug release, DNMCs holds great promise for improving the outcomes of chemo-immunotherapy strategies.
