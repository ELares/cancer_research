# NI-3201 Is a Bispecific Antibody Mediating PD-L1-Dependent CD28 Co-stimulation on T Cells for Enhanced Tumor Control.

## Abstract

Despite advances in cancer immunotherapy, such as targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis, a substantial number of patients harbor tumors that are resistant or relapse. Selective engagement of T-cell co-stimulatory molecules with bispecific antibodies may offer novel therapeutic options by enhancing signal 1-driven activation occurring via T-cell receptor engagement. In this study, we report the development and preclinical characterization of NI-3201, a PD-L1×CD28 bispecific antibody generated on the κλ-body platform that was designed to promote T-cell activity and antitumor function through a dual mechanism of action. We confirmed that NI-3201 blocks the PD-L1/PD-1 immune checkpoint pathway and conditionally provides T-cell co-stimulation via CD28 (signal 2) when engaging PD-L1+ tumors or immune cells. In systems with signal 1-primed T cells, NI-3201 enhanced potent effector functionality: in vitro through antigen-specific recall assays with cytomegalovirus-specific T cells and in vivo by inducing tumor regression and immunologic memory in tumor-associated antigen-expressing MC38 syngeneic mouse models. When T-cell engagers were used to provide synthetic signal 1, the combination with NI-3201 resulted in synergistic T cell-dependent cytotoxicity and potent antitumor activity in two humanized mouse tumor models. Nonhuman primate safety assessments showed favorable tolerability and pharmacokinetics at pharmacologically active doses. Quantitative systems pharmacology modeling predicted that NI-3201 exposure results in antitumor activity in patients, but this remains to be investigated. Overall, this study suggests that by combining PD-L1 blockade with safe and effective CD28 co-stimulation, NI-3201 has the potential to improve cancer immunotherapy outcomes, and the clinical development of NI-3201 for PD-L1+ solid tumors is planned.

Introduction

The advent of immunotherapy has led to a remarkable revolution in cancer treatment over the past decade. Immunotherapy leverages the power of the immune system to eradicate cancer cells. One of the most notable breakthroughs was the targeting of the PD-L1/PD-1 immune checkpoint (IC) axis. PD-L1 expressed on tumor cells binds to PD-1 on T cells, leading to the inhibition of immune responses via dephosphorylation of T-cell receptor (TCR)/CD28 proximal signaling molecules. mAbs that block ICs, termed IC inhibitors (ICI), unleash antitumor T-cell responses, leading to tumor regression and long-term survival in a subset of patients across a variety of cancer types.

Despite the undeniable success of ICIs, a substantial proportion of patients do not experience clinical benefit. The reasons for this variable response are multifaceted and are not fully understood. Tumor heterogeneity, an immunosuppressive microenvironment, and intrinsic resistance mechanisms within cancer cells can contribute to treatment failure. Neutralizing antidrug antibodies (ADA) may also negatively affect treatment. This highlights the need for novel therapeutic strategies that expand the responder population and address the limitations of current immunotherapies.

Agonist antibodies designed to target co-stimulatory pathways in T cells have emerged as promising candidates for immunotherapy. Such antibodies mimic the natural activation signals received by T cells through co-stimulatory molecules, thereby enhancing their effector function and proliferation. CD28 is one of the primary co-stimulatory receptors on T cells, and upon engagement with its ligands CD80/CD86 on antigen-presenting cells, CD28 delivers a co-stimulatory signal that synergizes with the TCR signal to promote T-cell cytokine production, survival, differentiation, and expansion.

Several CD28-specific mAbs have been proposed for therapeutic targeting of CD28. A fraction, termed superagonist (SA) antibodies, induces full activation of primary resting T cells even in the absence of TCR ligation (signal 1). The first-in-human study of the high-affinity (1.88 nmol/L) SA anti-CD28 TGN1412 resulted in catastrophic outcomes with cytokine release syndrome and multiorgan failure in all administered healthy volunteers.

Although the outcomes associated with TGN1412 cast doubt about the use of CD28 agonists, the receptor itself remained a potentially promising target for immunotherapy. Emerging strategies have focused on developing safer and more targeted approaches to exploit CD28 in cancer treatment. One promising strategy involves the use of bispecific antibodies (bsAb) that simultaneously engage CD28 and a tumor-associated antigen (TAA), directing T-cell activation specifically toward cancer cells while minimizing systemic toxicity. Preclinical studies have shown the benefits of combining co-stimulatory TAA×CD28 bsAbs with bispecific T-cell engagers (TCE; ref.) or PD-1 ICIs. Early clinical data indicate manageable safety profiles and tangible antitumor activity in patients with advanced cancer.

The elegant synergy of combining TAA×CD28 bsAbs and PD-1 blockade to control tumor growth was recently demonstrated. Blockade of the PD-1 pathway is also achieved by targeting its ligand, PD-L1, which could serve as an anchoring molecule for CD28 bsAbs. We hypothesized that merging a blocking anti–PD-L1 arm with an agonist anti-CD28 arm could consolidate two distinct yet complementary mechanisms of action (MoA) into a single molecule, potentially yielding synergistic effects. Here, we present NI-3201, a PD-L1×CD28 bsAb based on a fully human IgG scaffold with potential single-agent activity for the treatment of PD-L1–expressing solid tumors.

Materials and Methods

Monovalent antibodies, bivalent antibodies, and bsAbs

In-house generated antibodies were derived from single-chain variable fragment (scFv) phage-display libraries that share the same variable heavy (VH) domain (based on the IGHV3–23 germline) and that are solely diversified in the variable light (VL) domain. These libraries were generated, selected, and screened as previously described. Notably, to identify suitable anti-CD28 arms, different selection strategies were performed using soluble recombinant human CD28 (ACROBiosystems, Cat. #CD8-H82E5) or cells naturally expressing human CD28 (Jurkat cells, described in the "Cell lines and primary cells" section below), as well as combinations of both. Selection outputs were screened as scFv periplasm extracts for specific binding to the target by using either ELISA or flow cytometry and by adapting the protocol described by Fischer and colleagues to the use of recombinant CD28 or CD28-expressing cells, respectively. Briefly, MaxiSorp plates (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #456537) were coated with NeutrAvidin (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #31000) at 5 μg/mL and blocked with 1% casein/1× PBS. Biotinylated recombinant CD28 (ACROBiosystems, Cat. #CD8-H82E5) was coated at 5 nmol/L in 0.1% casein/1× PBS. scFv periplasmic extracts were diluted 1:5 in 0.1% casein/1× PBS and applied to the coated plates. scFvs were then detected using mouse anti–c-myc (Roche, Cat. #11667203001, RRID: AB_390911, 1:1,000 diluted) and a horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated donkey anti–mouse IgG (Jackson ImmunoResearch, Cat. #715-035-150, RRID: AB_2340770, 1:5,000 diluted). 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine (TMB) development (Sigma-Aldrich, Cat. #T0565-100ML) was stopped with H2SO4 before reading the plates on a BioTek ELx808 Microplate Reader.

Binding to CD28+ (Jurkat E6-1) or CD28– (J.RT3-T3.5) cells was assessed by flow cytometry. A 1:5 dilution of periplasmic extracts was preincubated with mouse anti–c-myc (Roche, Cat. #11667203001, RRID: AB_390911, 1:5,000 diluted) and applied to 2 × 105 cells. An allophycocyanin (APC)-conjugated goat anti–mouse IgG (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #A-865, RRID: AB_2536211, 1:500 diluted) was used for detection. Analysis was performed on an iQue3 screener equipment (Sartorius). Positive clones were reformatted from scFvs into human IgG1 using standard molecular biology methods. Briefly, scFv variable light chain sequences were amplified by PCR to introduce the BsrDI enzyme restriction site at both 5′ and 3′ ends. PCR products were then purified using the QIAquick PCR Purification Kit (QIAGEN, Cat. #28106) following the manufacturer's instruction, digested with BsrDI enzyme (New England Biolabs, Cat. #R0574), and loaded on an E-Gel 1% agarose (Invitrogen, Cat. #A42100) for insert purification using the MinElute Gel Extraction Kit (QIAGEN, Cat. #28606). The variable light chain sequences were then cloned into the pNovi bicistronic vector (Light Chain Bioscience proprietary vector) containing the light chain constant part and human IgG1 heavy chain (HC) constant and variable domains. Briefly, the pNovi vector was digested using BsrDI restriction enzyme, dephosphorylated using alkaline phosphatase (Roche, Cat. #11097075001), and purified on E-Gel as described above. Ligation was performed using the Rapid DNA Ligation Kit (Roche, Cat. #11635379001). Transformed human embryo kidney monolayer epithelial cells (PEAK cells; ATCC) were transiently transfected using a mix containing 30 μg of DNA and 42 μL of Lipofectamine 2000 Transfection Reagent (Invitrogen, Cat. #11668027) in 1.4 mL of DMEM (Gibco, Cat. #11965084). After 7 days of culture at 37°C in 5% CO2 in a humidified incubator, the supernatants were clarified using diatomaceous earth and a 0.22-μm filtration unit, and IgGs were purified by affinity chromatography using the CaptureSelect FcXP resin (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #194371201). Antibodies were tested by flow cytometry for binding to a CD28-positive cell line. AI3 was derived from naïve κλ body–compatible libraries without the need for affinity maturation.

The sequences coding for the previously described anti–PD-L1 S79 arm and the newly identified anti-CD28 AI3 arm were cloned into a single tricistronic pNovi vector containing three expression cassettes under transcriptional control of the human cytomegalovirus (CMV) promoter. The first, second, and third promoters drive the expression of the κ light chain (AI3), common HC, and λ light chain (S79).

Expi293 cells (RRID: CVCL_D615) were transiently transfected using polyethylenimine (Polysciences, Cat. #23966) at a final concentration of 5 μg/mL and a DNA concentration of 1.25 μg/mL. Six to seven days after transfection, the supernatants were harvested and purified applying a three-step purification process, as described previously. Sample purity and aggregate levels were assessed by electrophoresis on a 2100 Bioanalyzer system (Agilent Technologies, Cat. #5067-1515) and size-exclusion chromatography (SEC) and ultra-performance liquid chromatography under isocratic conditions. Briefly, samples were diluted to 1 mg/mL in a mobile phase composed of 0.2 mol/L sodium phosphate, pH 6.8 and 10% acetonitrile. A measure of 4 μL was injected into the ACQUITY UPLC BEH SEC column (Waters, Cat. #186005225) at a 0.4 mL/minute flow rate in mobile phase. Sample elution was detected using UV at 280 nm, and the chromatogram peaks were analyzed using Empower software (Waters).

Bicistronic pNovi variants carrying one single light-chain gene (i.e., AI3 light chain or S29 light chain) were used to generate bivalent (monoclonal) controls, whereas the expression of monovalent controls was driven by tricistronic pNovi variants carrying, in addition to the light chain of interest (i.e., AI3 light chain or S29 light chain), a compatible but irrelevant light-chain gene that, as IgG, was proved not to bind to any protein. Expression and purification were performed as detailed above.

An analogue of the TGN1412 mAb was generated in-house according to WO2006050949; functional grade OKT3 was purchased from Thermo Fischer Scientific (Cat. #16-0037-85, RRID: AB_468855); and the carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA)×CD3 used herein has been described elsewhere.

Atezolizumab (Tecentriq), pembrolizumab (Keytruda), and ipilimumab (Yervoy) were purchased from FarmaMondo (Cat. #100021576, 100017341, and 100005531, respectively).

Cell lines and primary cells

Details about the different cell lines used are provided in Supplementary Table S1. The number of CD28 and PD-L1 antigens/cells was determined using QIFIKIT (Agilent Dako) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Mycoplasma contaminations were excluded during cell bank generation and after each thawing by using a MycoAlert Mycoplasma Detection Kit (RUWAG Life Science, Cat. #LT07-418).

Transformed human embryonic kidney monolayer epithelial cells (PEAK, ATCC) were transiently transfected to express either human CD28 or human CTLA-4; the full-length DNA sequence coding for these proteins (CD28: NM_006139.3; CTLA-4: NM_005214.5) was cloned into the pEAK8 vector (Edge Biosystems) and transfected into the cells using Lipofectamine 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #11668-019) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Transiently expressing cells were used 48 hours after transfection upon transgene expression validation by flow cytometry using anti-CD28 (BD Biosciences, Cat. #556622, RRID: AB_396494) or anti–CTLA-4 (BD Biosciences, Cat. #555853, RRID: AB_396176). Nontransfected cells served as a negative control.

huCEA-MC38 corresponds to an engineered MC38 clonal cell line expressing the CEA protein at its surface. It was generated by cloning the human CEA full-length gene (accession number NM_004363.2) into the pAAVS1-Puro-DNR vector (OriGene, Cat. #GE100024) allowing for the targeted transgene insertion into the human AAVS1 locus. This vector carries the puromycin resistance marker that allowed for the generation of stable clones. MC38 cells were transfected using Lipofectamine 2000 Transfection Reagent (Invitrogen, Cat. #11668027), and clones stably and homogeneously expressing huCEA at the cell surface were isolated by applying puromycin selection and cell sorting.

Human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were isolated from buffy coats (purchased from the Geneva University Hospitals) in SepMate-50 PBMC isolation tubes (STEMCELL Technologies, Cat. #85460).

Flow cytometric analysis

Antibody binding to cells was determined by flow cytometry according to the standard operating methods. Briefly, 2.5 × 105 cells/well were centrifuged in a 96-well V-bottom plate and incubated for 20 minutes at 4°C with 100 μL of antibody serially diluted in FACS buffer [PBS containing 2% (w/v) BSA]. Cells were washed twice with FACS buffer, and 100 μL of secondary antibody (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #A-21445, RRID: AB_2535862; 200× diluted in FACS buffer) was added and incubated for 20 minutes. After washing, the cells were resuspended in 150 μL of FACS buffer containing SYTOX Blue (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #S34857; 5,000× diluted) and analyzed using a CytoFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter). Raw data were extracted using FlowJo version 10 (RRID: SCR_008520) and plotted using GraphPad Prism version 10 (RRID: SCR_002798). The gating strategy and a representative histogram for a “binding experiment” are shown in Supplementary Fig. S1A.

T-cell proliferation assay with wet- and dry-plate coating

The ability of antibodies to induce proliferation of primary resting T cells was assessed using a protocol adapted from Stebbings and colleagues. Briefly, antibodies were set to 10 μg/mL using PBS and absorbed on 96-well round-bottom sterile polypropylene plates (Corning, Cat. #3359) either by wet coating (100 μL/well, with sealed plates stored overnight at 4°C) or dry coating (50 μL/well, uncovered and subjected to the laminar flow of a cell culture hood). The following day, both wet- and dry-coated plates were washed twice with PBS (200 μL/well) before the addition of 100,000 cells/well (in 200 μL) of PBMCs stained with 5 µmol/L CellTrace Violet dye (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #C34557). The plates were incubated for 96 hours at 37°C. They were stained with APC anti-huCD4 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #17-0049-42, RRID: AB_1272048) and PerCP/Cyanine5.5 anti-human CD8 (BioLegend, Cat. #301032, RRID: AB_893422). The proliferation of CD4+ or CD8+ T cells was quantified by dye dilution using a CytoFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter). Raw data were extracted using FlowJo (RRID: SCR_008520) and plotted using GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798). The gating strategy and representative dot plots for a “T-cell proliferation assay” are shown in Supplementary Fig. S1B.

Cytokine release assay with high-density preculture of PBMCs

To increase the sensitivity of T-cell responses, PBMCs were precultured at high density (HD, corresponding to 10 × 106 cells/mL) for 2 days in 24-well plates according to Römer and colleagues. HD PBMCs were then dispersed in 96-well flat-bottom cell culture plates at a density of 0.1 × 106 cells/mL. A dose range of antibodies was added to both fresh and HD precultured PBMCs, and the plates were incubated for 24 hours at 37°C. The supernatants were then harvested, and IL2 and IFNγ were quantified using a multiplex kit [Meso Scale Discovery (MSD) U-PLEX platform, Cat. #K15067L-4 with Calibrator Cat. #C0060-2, lot A00U0291] according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

AI3 fragment-antigen binding generation and crystallography studies

For crystallography studies, the AI3 fragment-antigen binding (Fab) was used instead of the full-length IgG. For that, a vector containing AI3 light chain and the variable (VH) and constant 1 (CH1) domains of the HC was generated by cloning both chains in the proprietary bicistronic pNovi vector. Expi293 cells were transiently transfected with this vector as described above. After 7 days of culture, the supernatant was harvested, clarified, and purified by affinity using the CaptureSelect CH1-XL Affinity Matrix (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #194346201).

CD28-Fc protein was produced in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells and purified by affinity chromatography (MabSelect PrismA, Cytiva, Cat. #17549803). The CD28-Fc protein was deglycosylated and the Fc-tag cleaved by mixing the CD28-Fc protein with Endo Hf at a 10:1 protein:Endo Hf mass ratio and thrombin (Cytiva, Cat. #27-0846-01) at 1 U/100 µg protein. The deglycosylated and cleaved CD28 sample was purified by protein A affinity chromatography, SEC, and amylose resin purification.

The complex Fab:CD28 was formed by mixing Fab AI3 and CD28 at a 1:1.2 molar ratio. The mixture was incubated on ice overnight and then loaded onto a HiLoad 16/600 Superdex 200 pg column (Sigma-Aldrich, Cat. #GE28-9893-35) preequilibrated with 1× PBS pH 7.4 buffer (10 mmol/L NaH2PO4, 1.8 mmol/L KH2PO4, 137 mmol/L NaCl, and 2.7 mmol/L KCl). The elution was collected by fractionation, and the fractions were analyzed by SDS-PAGE.

Crystallization trials were set up using the CD28:AI3 Fab complex at 15.8 mg/mL in PBS, pH 7.4. Drops were set up using 100 nL protein and 100 nL reservoir solution containing 200 mmol/L zinc acetate, 0.1 mol/L imidazole, pH 8.0, 20% w/v polyethylene glycol 3000 (PEG 3000). The crystal was flash-frozen by first transferring the crystal to 200 mmol/L zinc acetate, 0.1 mol/L imidazole, pH 8.0, 20% w/v polyethylene glycol 3000, and 25% v/v glycerol and then plunged into liquid N2. Data were collected at 100 K and wavelength 0.9999 to 3.05 Å resolution at the BioMAX station at the MAX IV synchrotron in Lund, Sweden. The beamline was equipped with an EIGER 16M Hybrid-pixel detector. Data were processed using the autoPROC pipeline (RRID: SCR_015748; ref.). The CD28:AI3 structure was determined using molecular replacement and Phaser software (RRID: SCR_014219; ref.). The search models were the CD28 coordinates from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) ID 6O8D that were determined to 3.55 Å resolution but later replaced by those of PDB ID 1YJD (determined to 2.7 Å resolution), and the Fab model, with its complementarity-determining region loops removed, was derived from the structure of a Fab complex with Zika envelope domain III (PDB ID 6UTA). The structure was refined using Refmac5 software (RRID: SCR_014225; ref.), and model building was carried out in Coot (RRID: SCR_014222; ref.). Refinement in BUSTER using the target option in combination with noncrystallographic symmetry restraints (RRID: SCR_015653; ref.) was also used during this study. The Fab coordinates were later replaced by an AlphaFold2-generated Fab model. The target structure used for CD28 chain C was derived from the PDB ID 1YJD, determined to 2.7 Å resolution, and that for the AI3 was derived from the AlphaFold2-generated Fab model. The final rounds of refinement were again carried out in Refmac5 to R and R-free of 19.8% and 25.7%, respectively. The average areas excluded in pairwise interactions between CD28 and the AI3 Fab molecule were calculated in AREAIMOL. Epitope and paratope residues were identified based on distance calculations using PyMOL (RRID: SCR_000305). The final refined crystal structure, encompassing two crystallographically independent CD28:AI3 Fab complexes, was deposited in the Research Collaboratory for Structural Bioinformatics Protein Data Bank (RRID: SCR_012820) with the PDB ID 8S6Z (DOI: 10.2210/pdb8S6Z/pdb). AI3 Fab molecules were numbered according to Kabat, whereas CD28 numbering reflected the final mature protein as presented in UniProt (RRID: SCR_002380, UniProt ID P10747). Additional data and refinement statistics are summarized in Supplementary Table S2. The residues involved in the epitope/paratope interaction in the AI3:CD28 complex are listed in Supplementary Table S3.

PD-1/PD-L1 blockade assay

The PD-1/PD-L1 blockade reporter bioluminescent cell-based assay (Promega, Cat. #J1250) was used according to the manufacturer’s instructions to measure the potency of the mentioned antibodies in blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction. Briefly, a vial of PD-L1+ artificial antigen-presenting cells (aAPC/CHO-K1 cells, that is CHO-K1 cells engineered to express both PD-L1 and a cell surface protein designed to activate cognate TCRs in an antigen-independent manner) was thawed, resuspended in 14.5 mL of cell recovery medium (90% Ham’s F-12/10% FBS), and dispensed in white 96-well flat-bottom plates (100 μL/well). The cells were incubated at 37°C for 16 to 20 hours before performing the assay. On the day of the assay, the cell supernatant was removed from the assay plates, and 40 μL of twofold concentrated antibody dilution was added. A vial of PD-1 effector T cells [Jurkat T cells stably expressing human PD-1 and nuclear factor of activated T cells (NFAT)-induced luciferase] was thawed, resuspended in 5.9 mL assay buffer (99% RPMI1640/1% FBS), and 40 μL dispensed to the assay plates (80 μL/well total volume). The assay plates were incubated for 6 hours at 37°C, followed by the addition of 80 μL of freshly reconstituted Bio-Glo reagent. The plates were incubated at room temperature in the dark for 10 minutes, and luminescence [relative luminescence units (RLU)] was measured using a SpectraMax i3x Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices). Raw data were analyzed as fold increase (FI) when compared with baseline luminescence in the absence of the anti–PD-L1: FI = RLU sample/RLU baseline. The FI results were reported in GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798) to establish potency curves and determine the EC50 value of the antibody response using appropriate curve fitting.

CD28 blockade assay

A CD28 blockade reporter bioluminescent cell-based assay (Promega, Cat. #JA6101) was used according to the manufacturer’s instructions to measure the potency of antibodies that bind and block CD28.

Briefly, a vial of aAPC/Raji target cells (Raji cells expressing an engineered cell surface protein designed to activate TCR/CD3 in an antigen-independent manner and endogenously expressing the B7 ligands) and a vial of CD28 Jurkat effector T cells (Jurkat T cells expressing endogenous TCR/CD3 and CD28 and a luciferase reporter driven by TCR/CD3 and CD28 pathway–dependent response elements) were thawed and resuspended in 3 mL assay buffer (90% RPMI1640/10% FBS) each.

The CD28 Jurkat effector cell suspension was dispensed into white 96-well flat-bottom plates (25 μL/well). Next, 25 μL/well of antibody (threefold concentrated, prepared in assay buffer) was added, followed by a 5-minute incubation at room temperature. Finally, 25 μL/well of aAPC/Raji target cell suspension was added, and the assay plates were incubated for 5 hours at 37°C. At the end of the incubation period, 75 μL of freshly reconstituted Bio-Glo reagent was added to each well. The plates were incubated at room temperature in the dark for 10 minutes, and luminescence (RLU) was measured using a luminometer. Raw data were analyzed as the percentage of CD28 activation, as compared with the maximal luminescence obtained in the absence of anti-CD28: % CD28 activation = (RLU sample/RLU Max) × 100. Data were reported in GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798) to establish potency curves and determine the IC50 value of antibody response using appropriate curve fitting.

B7-H2 blockade assay

The ability of the antibody to block the interaction between human CD28 and B7-H2 was assessed by flow cytometry. Briefly, 2 × 105 PEAK cells transiently expressing CD28 were incubated for 20 minutes at 4°C with 50 μL of antibody diluted in FACS buffer (defined in the "Flow cytometric analysis" section above) at 50 μg/mL in a 96-well V-bottom plate. Recombinant human B7-H2 Fc Chimera Alexa Fluor 647 Protein (R&D Systems, Cat. #AFR165-020) was then added directly to the wells to 5 μg/mL final concentration, and the cells were incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature. After two washing steps with FACS buffer, a viability marker (SYTOX Green Dead Cell Stain, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #S34860) was added (diluted 1:5,000 in FACS buffer), and the cells were analyzed using a CytoFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter). Raw data were extracted using FlowJo version 10 (RRID: SCR_008520) and plotted using GraphPad Prism version 10 (RRID: SCR_002798).

Affinity measurements by biolayer interferometry

Affinity of the S79 arm to PD-L1 was previously reported, whereas the affinity of the newly identified anti-CD28 arm AI3 was determined here by biolayer interferometry on an Octet RED96 instrument (Sartorius). All experiments were performed at 30°C. HIS1K biosensors (Sartorius) were loaded with CD28 (ACROBiosystems, Cat. #CD8-H52H3) at a concentration of 2 μg/mL for 300 seconds, and a loading signal of 0.5 nm was obtained for all experiments. A 2× serial dilution of NI-3201 was prepared in kinetics buffer (Sartorius), starting at 500 nmol/L, on seven different concentrations. The association and dissociation steps were monitored for 300 and 600 seconds, respectively. Data were processed and analyzed using Data Analysis Software (ForteBio). A double reference subtraction was applied, and both association and dissociation steps were fitted using a global 1.1 fitting model. For CD28 experiments, only the first 50 seconds of the dissociation phase were analyzed to improve fitting accuracy.

Determination of CTLA-4 cross-reactivity by ELISA

T cell–dependent cellular cytotoxicity assay

A fixed number of IFNγ-primed pancreatic adenocarcinoma cell line (HPAC) cells (target cells, 10,000 cells/well) were seeded in 96-well flat-bottom plates and cultured for 24 hours at 37°C. On the day of the assay, PBMCs were added to the target cells at the effector-to-target (E:T) ratios of 10:1, 3:1, 1:1, and 1:3. To analyze T-cell proliferation, dedicated plates were established in which PBMCs were stained with 5 µmol/L CellTrace Violet (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #C34557) before being added to the target cells. Target and effector cells were cultured in the presence of a dose range of CEA×CD3 TCEs (started at 3 nmol/L and followed by 1:3 dilutions) supplemented or not with a fixed dose of NI-3201 (2.5 μg/mL) for 6 days at 37°C. NI-3201 alone was used as a control. Wells containing only effector and target cells served as controls to calculate specific killing.

On the day of the readout, the plates were shaken on a rocking platform to resuspend PBMCs. The plates were subsequently washed twice with PBS to remove all PBMCs from the wells. PBMCs collected from the same duplicate were pooled and stored at 4°C for T-cell activation analysis.

The specific lysis results were analyzed using GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798).

From the collected PBMCs, T-cell activation was analyzed by assessing the expression of CD25 (late activation marker) on both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. After centrifugation, the pelleted cells were resuspended in 30 μL Fc block (BD Biosciences, Cat. #564220, 1:20 diluted) and incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes. For staining the T-cell surface markers, the following antibodies were used: PerCP/Cyanine5.5 anti-human CD8 (BioLegend, Cat. #301032, RRID: AB_893422), APC anti-human CD4 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #17-0049-42, RRID: AB_1272048), and PE anti-human CD25 (BioLegend, Cat. #356104, RRID: AB_2561861). The plates were incubated for 20 minutes at 4°C.

After washing, the cells were resuspended in 100 μL cold PBS buffer containing a viability marker (eFluor 780, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. #65-0865-14, 1:1,000 diluted) and incubated at 4°C for 30 minutes. Cells were washed twice, resuspended in 150 μL of FACS buffer, and analyzed using a CytoFLEX flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter). Data were extracted and analyzed using FlowJo (RRID: SCR_008520) and plotted using GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798).

Quantitative systems pharmacology modeling

A quantitative systems pharmacology (QSP) model was developed based on known mechanisms of antibody first-order plasma elimination, tissue and tumor biodistribution, receptor binding, and receptor and drug-bound receptor endocytosis (see Supplementary Methods File for further information on how the QSP model was developed). All models were implemented using MATLAB version 9.14 (R2023a) and SimBiology version 6.4.1 (R2023a) software (MathWorks, Inc.). The model was developed as a system of ordinary differential equations, as previously described, and built as a three-compartment (central, peripheral, and tumor) semimechanistic pharmacokinetic/pharmacodynamic (PK/PD) human model that includes binding between NI-3201 and its target receptors, CD28 and PD-L1. Steady-state receptor levels were maintained by setting equal endocytosis and protein synthesis rates and set to be proportional to their respective cell levels. Cells were simulated to be produced and eliminated to establish a steady-state level of target-expressing cells. To account for potential target-mediated drug disposition, the model includes PD-L1 target burden (PD-L1+ cells) in the central and peripheral compartments. The PD-L1 target burden and turnover half-time were estimated by co-optimizing a separate two-compartment (central and peripheral) PD-L1 mAb human model (Supplementary Fig. S2; Supplementary Table S5) to the available atezolizumab and durvalumab clinical PK data with equal cell burden and turnover half-time in each compartment (Supplementary Fig. S3). Supplementary Table S5 contains information retrieved from the literature. Transcellular avidity for NI-3201 binding to its receptors, PD-L1 and CD28, was included in the model as previously described by calibrating the model to the single-agent TDCC activity data. To estimate the first-order elimination rate in humans, a two-compartment nonhuman primate (NHP) model (Supplementary Fig. S4; Supplementary Table S6) was calibrated to NHP PK data following the administration of NI-3201 (Supplementary Fig. S5), and the first-order rate constants in humans were calculated based on allometry (Supplementary Table S7) as previously described. Supplementary Table S7 contains information retrieved from the literature.

Results

The anti-CD28 arm AI3 is not a superagonist

The κλ bodies are fully human bsAbs assembled by co-expression of one HC and two different LCs: one κ and one λ. Clone AI3 is a CD28-binding κ-arm identified based on its capacity to bind to CD28 that is natively expressed on Jurkat cells with moderate binding affinity, with no detectable binding on CD28– J.RT3-T3.5 cells (Fig. 1A). Compared with a TGN1412 analogue, used as a positive control and reference antibody, the bivalent AI3 mAb is a relatively weaker CD28 binder. Compared with the bivalent AI3 mAb, the monovalent binding capacity of AI3 was further weakened, as demonstrated using a bsAb composed of the AI3 arm coupled to an irrelevant second arm (Fig. 1A). The monovalent binding affinity of AI3 to huCD28 was quantified to be 54 ± 3 nmol/L using Octet Biolayer Interferometry.

To rule out SA activity, the bivalent AI3 mAb was tested for its capacity to induce T-cell proliferation without signal 1 using modified dry/wet plate assays. TGN1412 induced strong T-cell proliferation, here exemplified in the CD4+ population (Fig. 1B), confirming its SA properties. The bivalent AI3 mAb showed T-cell proliferation similar to PBS and the isotype control in both CD4+ and CD8+ subsets, ruling out unwanted SA activity.

AI3 binds to a distal epitope on CD28 and does not cross-react with CTLA-4

To determine the CD28-binding epitope of AI3, the X-ray crystallographic structure of the AI3 Fab in complex with human CD28 was determined (PDB ID 8S6Z). An average antibody–antigen interface of 915.65 Å2 was calculated between CD28 and the AI3 Fab. The CD28 and AI3 residues involved in this interaction are listed in Supplementary Table S3 as epitope and paratope, respectively. The epitope recognized by AI3 on CD28 encompasses the MYPPPY motif, which corresponds to the B7 ligand–binding region of CD28 (Fig. 1C). The light chain (LC) complementarity-determining region 1 was found to strongly interact with CD28, possibly stabilizing the interaction between CD28 and AI3 (Fig. 1D).

AI3’s interaction with the MYPPPY motif prompted an investigation of its ability to block the CD28/B7 interaction using a CD28 blockade reporter bioassay. Maximum luminescence was observed with basal CD3 and CD28 stimulation (Fig. 1E). A concentration-dependent decrease in luminescence was detected with increasing concentrations of control blocking anti-CD28. Similar CD28 blockade was induced by the bivalent AI3 mAb (IC50 = 0.3 nmol/L); in the context of a bsAb, a much higher concentration was required to achieve a similar blockade because of the monovalent engagement of CD28 (IC50 = 14.3 and 14.2 nmol/L, for AI3 monovalent #1 and #2, respectively). Not surprisingly, as inducible costimulator-ligand (ICOSL) also interacts with the MYPPPY motif on CD28, AI3 also blocked binding of recombinant ICOSL to CD28 in a competition assay (Supplementary Fig. S6).

As the MYPPPY motif is fully conserved between CD28 and CTLA-4 (Supplementary Fig. S7A), the specificity of AI3 in binding to CD28 was further explored. Contrary to clone AI5, which was found to bind to CTLA-4 exogenously expressed on PEAK cells (Supplementary Fig. S7B) and to recombinant CTLA-4 (Supplementary Fig. S7C), AI3 failed to bind to CTLA-4, confirming its specificity for CD28.

NI-3201: A PD-L1×CD28 bsAb that co-stimulates T cells via PD-L1 blockade

The anti-CD28 κ-arm AI3 was paired with the high-affinity (KD = 0.5 ± 0.3 nmol/L) anti–human PD-L1 neutralizing λ-arm S79 (Fig. 2A; ref.). The κλ-body bsAb was generated on a human IgG1 backbone carrying mutations (LALAPA, corresponding to Leu234Ala, Leu235Ala, and Pro329Ala) intended to abrogate binding to FcγRs and C1q and, consequently, IgG-mediated effector functions. The resulting bsAb AI3S79/N (NI-3201) was designed to reverse PD-1 inhibition of TCR/CD28 signaling through its anti–PD-L1 arm, allowing the anti-CD28 arm to co-stimulate T cells by agonizing the CD28 receptor (Fig. 2B). As PD-L1 expression is not limited to tumor cells, NI-3201 can provide co-stimulation in the presence of other PD-L1+ cells outside the TME. A key example of beneficial on-target off-tumor activity is the cross-priming of tumor-specific T cells in dLNs by PD-L1+ antigen-presenting cells, such as dendritic cells (Fig. 2C).

NI-3201 was confirmed to bind in a dose-dependent manner to PD-L1+ HPAC cells (Fig. 2D) and CD28+ Jurkat cells [Supplementary Fig. S8A (left)], with no detectable binding on PD-L1−CD28− double-negative J.RT3-T3.5 cells [Supplementary Fig. S8A (right)]. Contrary to TGN1412, but in agreement with the data generated during characterization of the AI3 mAb (Fig. 1B), NI-3201 had no SA in the Stebbings assay, independent of the way the antibody was coated onto plates (Supplementary Fig. S8B). The lack of SA activity of NI-3201 was confirmed using the “RESTORE” protocol. Under the applied conditions, TGN1412 induced dose-dependent secretion of IL2 and IFNγ in all tested donors, with the heterogeneous response reflecting donor variability (Fig. 2E; Supplementary Fig. S8C). Inversely, NI-3201 did not induce IL2 or IFNγ secretion.

The capacity of NI-3201 to block the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction was assessed using a biologically relevant reporter gene assay. The S79 arm was confirmed to block PD-1/PD-L1 as determined by the induction of the reporter gene expression mediated by the S79 monovalent control (Fig. 2F). Because reporter T cells also express CD28 at the cell surface, NI-3201–triggered reporter gene expression was further enhanced via CD28 co-stimulation, resulting in stronger activation, as compared with sole PD-L1 blockade mediated by atezolizumab or the bivalent S79 control, illustrating the added value granted by the CD28 co-stimulation (Fig. 2F). As expected, neither AI3 monovalent nor AI3 bivalent controls induced NFAT signaling, with no increase in luminescence as compared with the background. Accordingly, the combination of the AI3 monovalent and S79 monovalent resulted in a signal similar to that of the S79 monovalent alone. This reporter assay showed that in the presence of T-cell signal 1 (provided by the artificial APCs) and PD-L1, NI-3201 enhanced the T-cell response by inhibiting the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction while further delivering co-stimulatory signal 2 to the reporter T cells. Even when the PD-L1/PD-1 axis was fully blocked by pembrolizumab, NI-3201 enhanced reporter gene expression through CD28 co-stimulation, which S79 monovalent or AI3 monovalent could not achieve (Supplementary Fig. S9).

Collectively, these results show that NI-3201 is not an SA of CD28 and blocks the PD-1/PD-L1 interaction while triggering CD28 signaling in a PD-L1–dependent manner.

In the presence of T-cell signal 1, NI-3201 displays potent single-agent activity

NI-3201 was designed to require T-cell activation signal 1 to exert its full pharmacologic effect. To this end, a mixed lymphocyte reaction assay was developed in which CD4+ T cells (responders) and MoDCs (stimulators) were co-cultured in the presence of the Staphylococcal aureus T-cell superantigen staphylococcal enterotoxin A. By blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis, the anti–PD-L1 atezolizumab and S79 enhanced the mixed lymphocyte reaction, as quantified by increased IL2 secretion over the isotype control (Fig. 3A). Given the similar IL2 levels obtained with both atezolizumab and the S79 mAb, an equivalent blockade capacity in the context of bivalent engagement of the target is expected. In contrast, a higher concentration of S79 monovalent was required to achieve a similar blockade of the PD-L1/PD-1 axis because of the lack of avidity of this molecule. By blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis and providing CD28 co-stimulation to CD4+ T cells, NI-3201 induced higher IL2 levels (Fig. 3A). However, the sole engagement of CD28 by AI3 monovalent did not induce IL2 secretion above the isotype control levels. In contrast, reduced IL2 secretion was observed at concentrations >10 nmol/L of AI3 monovalent. Of note, pembrolizumab resulted in higher IL-2 secretion than PD-L1–blocking mAbs, possibly owing to the blockade of both PD-L1 and PD-L2. When the highest tested dose of pembrolizumab was added to a dose–response of NI-3201, AI3 monovalent, or S79 monovalent, only NI-3201 could increase IL2 secretion: sole PD-L1 blockade by S79 monovalent was redundant with the blockade of PD-1 by pembrolizumab, whereas the engagement of CD28 by AI3 monovalent could not co-stimulate T cells. Alongside IL2, IFNγ was also quantified, and similar results were obtained for the various antibodies and antibody combinations (Supplementary Fig. S10).

In another assay, PD-L1+ MDA-MB-231 tumor cells were ectopically loaded with a pool of CMV-derived peptides, including the NLV-peptide. These tumor cells were then co-cultured with HLA-compatible PBMC donors containing CMV-reactive T cells (Fig. 3B). For both donors, NI-3201 dose-dependent expansion of NLV-specific T cells was observed (Fig. 3B). When target cells were not loaded with CMV peptides and were thus unable to provide T-cell signal 1, NI-3201 induced no T-cell proliferation (non–CMV-loaded controls).

Overall, the data suggest NI-3201 can be a potent single agent if T cells receive sufficient signal 1.

NI-3201 has potent single-agent activity in immunocompetent mouse models

To assess single-agent potential in vivo, NI-3201 was tested in immunocompetent mouse models, beginning with a syngeneic tumor model in which huPD-L1-MC38 cells were engrafted subcutaneously into huCD28-Tg mice. The mice received three weekly injections of the test articles, starting when the average tumor volume was close to 100 mm3 (Fig. 4A). Atezolizumab treatment led to a heterogeneous antitumor response, with a minority of the mice responding to treatment, resulting in tumor growth delay compared with the vehicle group (Fig. 4B; Supplementary Fig. S11A). In contrast to atezolizumab, treatment with NI-3201 led to robust tumor regression in all treated mice, with 6 of 10 mice considered tumor free by study termination on day 28 (Fig. 4B; Supplementary Fig. S11A), resulting in a significant survival advantage over vehicle or atezolizumab treatment (Fig. 4C). Quantification of circulating cytokines 6 hours after the first injection showed mild, nonstatistically significant levels induced by NI-3201, with IL2, IL6, and TNFα values similar to those in atezolizumab-treated mice (Fig. 4D). Only IFNγ secretion was significantly higher in the NI-3201 treatment group than that in the vehicle group. No clinical signs suggestive of toxicity were observed in the NI-3201 treatment group, and all mice gained weight normally throughout the study (Supplementary Fig. S11B).

At 59 days after the primary tumor challenge, tumor-free mice were rechallenged with WT-MC38 cells on the opposite flank. Contrary to naïve control mice, all of which developed tumors when engrafted with WT-MC38, none of the mice ultimately rejecting huPD-L1-MC38 developed a tumor when rechallenged with WT-MC38 (Fig. 4E), suggesting the induction of immunological memory against MC38 tumor antigens.

NI-3201 was next tested in huCD28-Tg mice engrafted with MC38 cells engineered to express either the human CEA antigen (huCEA-MC38) or ovalbumin (OVA-MC38). Contrary to the previous experiment, in which PD-L1 was overexpressed (as huPD-L1), both the huCEA-MC38 and the OVA-MC38 cells expressed physiologic levels of PD-L1 (as endogenous mouse PD-L1), which can be recognized and blocked by NI-3201 because of its mouse cross-reactive anti–PD-L1 arm.

Given MoA of NI-3201, we first aimed to evaluate how the timing of treatment initiation might influence the outcome. Treatment of huCEA-MC38–engrafted mice started either the day after tumor implantation (early treatment) or when the tumor volume was more than 50 mm3 (late treatment; Fig. 4F). In the early and late NI-3201 treatment groups, 30.8% (4/13) and 61.5% (8/13) of the mice, respectively, achieved complete rejection of the primary tumor (Fig. 4F; Supplementary Fig. S11C), which resulted in a significant survival advantage for mice treated with NI-3201 over control mice (Fig. 4G). Tumor-free mice were rechallenged with WT-MC38 cells on the opposite flank, and again, none developed a tumor, reinforcing the fact that NI-3201 induced immunological memory against the parental cancer cell line (Fig. 4H).

To gain insights into the MoAs of NI-3201 in vivo, mice bearing OVA-MC38 tumors were sacrificed 14 days after engraftment following two injections of NI-3201 (Fig. 4I), when the average tumor volume in the treated group started to be significantly lower than that in the vehicle-treated group (Fig. 4J). Immunophenotyping was performed on blood, tumors, and dLNs. NI-3201 did not alter the number and subset of T cells in circulation. In contrast, the TME analysis revealed an increase in the number of CD3+ T cells, in particular CD8+ T cells [Fig. 4K (top row)]. Of note, no changes were observed in the frequency of the central memory T cells or effector T cells [Fig. 4K (bottom row)]. No significant changes were detected in the dLNs, except for a small but significant increase in CD4+ T effector memory frequency (Supplementary Fig. S11D).

In a similar experiment, mice were administered FTY720 to prevent T-cell infiltration into the TME (Fig. 4L). The antitumor response mediated by NI-3201 was largely impaired in mice receiving continuous FTY720 administration (Supplementary Fig. S11E) and resulted in lowered survival probability (Fig. 4M).

Last, NI-3201 was administered to huCD28-Tg mice engrafted with WT-MC38 cells. Despite being less efficient than in models expressing neoantigens such as huCEA or ovalbumin, treatment with NI-3201 resulted in statistically significant antitumor activity (Supplementary Fig. S12). Although the survival benefit conferred by NI-3201 did not reach statistical significance when compared with atezolizumab, NI-3201 demonstrated a stronger statistical significance than atezolizumab relative to the vehicle control (*** vs. *).

NI-3201 demonstrated strong in vivo activity in immunocompetent mouse tumor models with varying PD-L1 expression. It enhanced T-cell responses and immunologic memory when a neoantigen was present, such as human PD-L1 or CEA. Without an anti-neoantigen response, NI-3201 had reduced single-agent activity but still showed significant antitumor effects. Its mechanism involves CD8+ T cells, likely recruited to the TME after priming in the dLNs.

NI-3201 synergizes with T-cell signal 1 provided by a TCE

TCEs are a class of bsAbs that redirect T cells to tumor cells independently of TCR specificity. By simultaneously binding to a TAA on a target cell and to CD3 on T cells, TCEs provide synthetic T-cell signal 1, mediating cytotoxic activity against the targeted cancer cells. Consequently, by providing signal 2, NI-3201 is expected to synergize with TCEs to kill tumor cells and enhance T-cell activation/proliferation.

To assess the synergy between NI-3201 and a CEA×CD3 TCE, the killing of the PD-L1+CEA+ HPAC cell line by T cells was monitored at different E:T ratios (Fig. 5A). In these TDCC assays, the TCE alone induced strong dose-dependent killing at an E:T ratio of 10:1, with up to 85.1% of the target cells killed at the end of the co-culture. However, the TCE activity progressively decreased with lower E:T ratios, with no killing of the target cells mediated by the TCE alone at an E:T ratio of 1:3. Synergy between the TCE and NI-3201 could be observed across the entire E:T ratio range, with the combination treatment resulting in significantly higher specific killing of the target cells across all E:T ratios. In the absence of T-cell signal 1, NI-3201 was unable to induce the killing of the target cells, confirming the requirement for primary T-cell stimulation via the TCR complex.

Similarly, the combination of NI-3201 with a TCE resulted in enhanced T-cell activation, as shown by the increased expression of CD25 at the T-cell surface (Supplementary Fig. S13A). Both CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell subpopulations reacted to the co-stimulatory signal provided by NI-3201, but CD4+ T-cell activation was deeper and observable across all E:T ratios, whereas for CD8+ T cells, the effect was tangible mainly at E:T ratios of 10:1 and 3:1. T cells were also found to proliferate significantly better in the presence of T-cell co-stimulation provided by the addition of NI-3201, with the percentage of proliferating T cells mirroring T-cell activation data (Supplementary Fig. S13B).

The in vivo antitumor activity of the combination of NI-3201 and TCE was investigated in a pancreatic adenocarcinoma model resistant to CEA×CD3 TCE monotherapy where BRGSF-HIS mice were engrafted subcutaneously with HPAF-II cells. Treatment was started on day 8 after engraftment, when the average tumor volume reached 65 mm3, with two additional administrations on days 11 and 14. The TCE alone displayed no antitumor activity (Fig. 5B). However, the combination of the TCE with NI-3201 initially resulted in a small tumor volume regression, followed by delayed tumor growth, resulting in significant tumor inhibition on day 23, when the first control mouse reached the tumor volume endpoint (Fig. 5B). Analysis of tumor growth in individual mice suggests a rather homogeneous tumor control in the combination group beyond day 23 and until day 31, when the experiment was stopped (Fig. 5C).

The effect of NI-3201 on the activity of the TCE was confirmed in a second mouse model using subcutaneously engrafted HPAC as the target and human PBMCs as the source of effector cells (Supplementary Fig. S14A). Treatment started on day 15, when the average tumor volume reached 300 mm3. Although tumors in the vehicle and single-treatment groups grew steadily until day 31, that is, when the onset of GVHD led to experimental termination, mice receiving the combination treatment experienced significant tumor regression starting on day 22 (Supplementary Fig. S14B). The heterogeneous antitumor response is probably explained by the fact that two PBMC donors were used to humanize the mice (Supplementary Fig. S14C).

Single- and repeat-dose PK and tolerability in NHPs

The PK and tolerability of NI-3201 were evaluated in male and female cynomolgus monkeys after a single intravenous injection at 0.57 or 10 mg/kg (groups 1 and 2) or two repeated intravenous injections at 10 mg/kg (group 3). The NI-3201 concentration as a function of time is shown in Fig. 6A for the three study groups. NI-3201 PK was close to dose proportionality, with the mean Cmax, AUCinf, and AUC0–168 hours values being 13 to 23 times higher after administration of 10 mg/kg than of 0.57 mg/kg. In several profiles, a sharp decline in concentration was observed after 336 hours, which hampered long-term PK investigation. This decline was shown to be associated with ADA development, which is not uncommon for human antibodies, particularly for cyno-cross-reactive antibodies targeting ICs. Nonetheless, all NHPs treated with a single injection of NI-3201 had quantifiable drug levels until at least 336 hours postdose, and the PK profiles of the animals that received two repeated injections (group 3) did not suggest ADA development in this group. The accumulation of NI-3201 was observed after repeated injections of 10 mg/kg.

No mortality or abnormalities in the clinical signs were observed throughout the study. No significant changes were observed in body weight or food intake. No treatment-related changes were noted in the hematologic or clinical chemistry parameters at any of the two doses tested. Single intravenous treatment with NI-3201 did not alter the number of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6B) or the relative levels of naïve, central memory (CM), effector memory (EM), and terminally differentiated effector memory (TEMRA) cell subsets in whole blood (Supplementary Fig. S15). Changes were observed in their proliferation and activation state as measured by the increased expression of Ki67, CD25, and inducible costimulator (ICOS) observed from day 6 to day 15, with subsequent recovery in almost all animals in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 6C–E). After single and/or repeated intravenous administration of NI-3201, there was no evidence of marked cytokine release in the sera at any of the tested doses (Supplementary Fig. S16). The minimal increase in IL6 might explain the weak, poorly dose-related CRP release observed across animals (Supplementary Fig. S17). At postmortem examinations, no treatment-related macroscopic or microscopic changes were noted in the examined organs of animals sacrificed after repeated intravenous administration of NI-3201 at 10 mg/kg.

QSP predicts favorable PK and pharmacologic activity in patients

Mechanistic modeling approaches are powerful integrative tools to understand the complexities and aid in clinical translation, trial design, and prediction of regimens and strategies to reduce the dose-limiting toxicities of bsAbs.

In this study, a three-compartment human semimechanistic PK/PD model was built and parameterized to inform on NI-3201 PK and pharmacology in patients (Fig. 7A). Data from published and internal experiments were used to parameterize the model, and in certain cases, the parameters were fitted using separate models. In this model, a trimer corresponds to a trimolecular complex that is composed of an NI-3201 molecule bridging a CD28+ T cell and a PD-L1+ target cell. Trimer formation is assumed to be a surrogate for T-cell co-stimulation, and a bell-shaped concentration–response relationship is expected for co-stimulatory bsAbs: beyond an optimal concentration for trimer formation, the excess of antibody favors monovalent binding to form pharmacologically inactive dimers. Alongside trimer formation, PD-L1 receptor occupancy (RO) was set as a surrogate output of PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint inhibition. Once built and parameterized, the human three-compartment NI-3201 model was used to predict drug exposure across the three compartments and trimer formation and PD-L1 RO in the tumor compartment.

Because the nominal partitioning coefficient for the tumor compartment was set to a value lower than that for the peripheral compartment, the model-projected NI-3201 PK showed a higher concentration in the peripheral compartment than in the tumor compartment (Fig. 7B). Drug concentrations with minimal local target-mediated drug disposition within the tumor compartment were predicted for NI-3201 doses of 0.3 mg/kg or higher across the modeled time window (Fig. 7B). At both PD-L1 densities modeled (10,000 and 30,000 receptors/cell) and with increasing NI-3201 dose levels, the model predicted an increase in PD-L1 RO, with complete PD-L1 blockade expected for the highest dose modeled (Fig. 7C), whereas trimer formation improved up to 1 mg/kg but then decreased in accordance with the bell-shaped concentration–response relationship expected from antibodies mediating transcellular interactions (Fig. 7D).

Discussion

Despite the unfavorable outcome of the SA anti-CD28 TGN1412 administration in humans, CD28 remains a promising target for cancer immunotherapy. The potential to modulate T-cell co-stimulatory molecules in the context of cancer is undeniable. However, safety concerns had prevented the development of additional CD28 agonists for over a decade. Recently, TAA×CD28 bsAbs have shown the ability to activate CD28 on TAA+ tumor cells, offering a targeted and safer T-cell activation modality. Nonetheless, the CD28 agonist arm of such bsAbs must be carefully selected to ensure T-cell activation only in the presence of TCR signaling (signal 1) and upon co-engagement of the anti-TAA arm on the target cells.

The anti-CD28 AI3 arm described herein demonstrated no evidence of CD28 SA activity. As revealed by crystallography studies, AI3 binds to a distinct epitope on CD28 compared with TGN1412, which binds basolaterally. By targeting the apical side of CD28 dimers, AI3 has the potential to hinder CD80/CD86/ICOSL binding. However, due to the relatively low affinity of the AI3 arm and the monovalent engagement of CD28 in a bsAb format, the potential antagonistic activity is not expected to impair endogenous signaling in patients. Despite binding to a region that is partially conserved between CD28 and CTLA-4, AI3 is CD28 specific. This reduces the risk of unwanted pharmacologic effects associated with co-blocking PD-(L)1 and CTLA-4, as dual PD-1/CTLA-4 inhibition has been linked to the development of fulminant myocarditis in both preclinical models and patients.

AI3 has the same HC as several other antibody arms identified using the κλ-body platform. Unlike natural human antibodies, which primarily rely on the HC to establish contact with their cognate antigen, target binding and specificity of κλ bodies are driven predominantly by the LC. Indeed, additional structural analyses have demonstrated that the LC plays a dominant role in epitope binding of κλ-body arms generated to CD47 and PD-L1. Interestingly, the distribution of AI3 LC and HC contact points identified for CD28 suggests a more balanced contribution of both chains for the AI3 antibody arm.

NI-3201 is a PD-L1×CD28 bsAb generated by pairing the AI3 arm with a blocking anti–PD-L1 arm, resulting in a molecule designed to provide conditional T-cell co-stimulation upon engagement of PD-L1 on tumor or immune cells. Contrary to TAA×CD28 bsAbs, which have to be combined with either TCEs or PD-1 blockade to potentiate T-cell activity, NI-3201 may afford single-agent activity in patients owing to its dual MoA.

Nevertheless, for NI-3201 to unleash its full potential as a stand-alone agent, tumor-specific T cells must be present. Notably, weaker responses were observed when WT-MC38 cells were engrafted in huCD28-Tg mice, likely because of insufficient antigenicity of the tumor, leading to an suboptimal endogenous T-cell response. The timing of NI-3201 administration proved crucial: delayed treatment (initiated with established tumors) demonstrated greater effectiveness than early administration (starting the day after tumor engraftment). We hypothesize that the variance in response to NI-3201 stems from two simultaneous factors. First, PD-L1 expression on tumor cells is regulated by IFNγ. Second, the release of IFNγ within the TME is anticipated to increase PD-L1 expression on MC38 cells, potentially enhancing trimer formation without encountering a possible hook effect. Alternatively, delayed administration of NI-3201 may coincide with peak activation of the adaptive immune response, which typically occurs after a delay following tumor cell engraftment, rather than immediately. Mechanistically, TME and dLN analyses demonstrated that NI-3201 was primarily active within the TME and its efficacy was at least partly dependent on lymphocyte egress from lymphoid tissues, as evidenced by the reduced antitumor effect in the presence of FTY720.

In patients with poorly antigenic tumors, combining NI-3201 with TCEs or other immunotherapeutic agents may offer synergistic combinations. Indeed, NI-3201 was shown to synergize with a TCE; in TDCC assays, the combination was active at an E:T ratio at which the TCE alone had no effect, whereas in mouse tumor models resistant to TCE single treatment, the combination led to tumor regression or tumor-growth stasis/delay, depending on models.

Single and repeated intravenous administration of NI-3201 in NHP at doses up to 10 mg/kg was well tolerated and did not cause any in-life findings or test item–related changes in clinical pathology and histopathology endpoints. A transient T-lymphocyte activation was observed upon treatment, as well as a minimal, poorly dose-related CRP release, stimulated by a slight increase in IL6. Despite documented limitations in using cynomolgus monkeys to assess the risks of CD28-driven cytokine release syndrome, recent NHP studies have shown a significant induction of IL6 (and a consequent increase in CRP levels) in animals that were administered a TGN1412 analogue at 10 mg/kg. In comparison, the amplitude of IL6 and CRP increases in animals treated with NI-3201 seems to be significantly lower, transient in nature, and may reflect the MoA of NI-3201. The repeated 10 mg/kg dose was selected to provide some indications on the safety and tolerability of NI-3201 when administered more than once. It is well documented that T cell–activating agents typically yield the highest incidence of immune-related adverse events after the initial injection, with subsequent administrations being better tolerated. Accordingly, the low-grade inflammatory response observed in NI-3201–treated animals was lower after the second injection than after the first, suggesting that repeated dosing with NI-3201 is not associated with an increased risk of cytokine release.

QSP modeling integrating in vitro and in vivo data informed on NI-3201 potential exposure across different compartments in humans. Both PD-L1 RO and trimer formation were modeled as surrogate values for PD-1/PD-L1 blockade and CD28 co-stimulation, respectively. To account for PD-L1 expression variability among patients and to increase the predictability of the model, two distinct PD-L1 densities were parameterized, namely, 10,000 and 30,000 receptors/cell. The model predicted that PD-L1 density has a direct impact on both trimer formation and PD-L1 RO, as the NI-3201 concentration required to saturate all PD-L1 receptors within the tumor or to lead to optimal trimer formation varies with PD-L1 expression. Across the different PD-L1 densities simulated, the model predicts that selected doses of NI-3201 will have favorable exposure and biological activity in patients.

NI-3201 exhibits potent antitumor activity both in vitro and in vivo, either alone or in combination with TCEs. Coupled with its favorable safety profile, these findings underpin its planned clinical development, slated to commence in H1 2025, for patients with PD-L1+ solid tumors.
