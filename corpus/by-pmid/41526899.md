# Impact of Helicobacter pylori infection on gut and intratumoral microbiome and its association with immunotherapy response in gastrointestinal cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) infection is associated with enhanced efficacy of immunotherapy in gastric cancer (GC). However, the mechanisms underlying this enhancement are not fully understood.

**METHODS**: We recruited 218 GC patients, 134 esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) patients, and 86 dMMR/MSI-H colorectal cancer (CC) patients and collected their stool and tumor samples to analyze the gut and intratumoral microbiome. We assessed microbial diversity and composition and correlated these findings with clinical outcomes to evaluate the relationship between H. pylori status, microbiome alterations, and immunotherapy efficacy.

**RESULTS**: H. pylori-positive patients showed higher alpha diversity and unique microbial signatures, which were associated with increased immune-related progression-free survival (irPFS) and overall survival (irOS). In addition, we found that the abundance of 45 gut microbiome species was significantly different between the two groups. The gut microbiome of the H. pylori-positive GC group was enriched with species such as Clostridium leptum, Oscillibacter sp. ER4, and Ruminococcus bromii, which were associated with improved treatment response. However, they predicted poor prognosis in patients with esophageal squamous cell carcinoma and colorectal cancer patients with dMMR/MSI-H. Microbial co-occurrence network revealed significantly distinct interaction patterns among the groups. In addition, we found enhanced L-arginine biosynthesis in the gut microbiome of H. pylori-positive GC. In terms of intratumoral bacteria, we identified two genera, Streptococcus and Granulicatella, that were mutually exclusive with H. pylori infection in GC. Enhanced L-lysine fermentation to acetate and butanoate was observed among intratumoral bacteria, suggesting potential metabolic shifts in the tumor microenvironment. Incorporating H. pylori infection status into the microbiome-based prediction model further improved the accuracy of predicting immunotherapy outcomes in GC.

**CONCLUSION**: These findings suggest that H. pylori had significant effects on the structure and functional activity of gut and intratumoral microbiome, some of which may affect the efficacy of immunotherapy. The clinical value of H. pylori infection status should be considered when establishing a prediction model for immunotherapy efficacy based on gut microbiome.

Background

Gastric cancer (GC) ranks fifth in terms of cancer incidence and is the fourth leading cause of cancer-related deaths. Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) has been categorized by the World Health Organization as a Group I carcinogen of GC. It is a bacterium that can persist in the gastric mucosa for an extended period and infects approximately 40% of the global population. Immunotherapies targeting immune checkpoints, such as programmed cell death protein 1(PD-1)/programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), opened up fresh avenues of hope for GC patients. While the carcinogenesis of H. pylori has been well-established 30 years ago, it was only in the recent large cohort study that we identified H. pylori infection as a favorable factor for immunotherapy in GC. Based on our anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy cohort, we found that the median immune-related progression-free survival (irPFS) in H. pylori-positive GC was significantly longer than that in H. pylori-negative GC (6.97 months vs. 5.03 months, p < 0.001). However, how H. pylori infection promotes the efficacy of immunotherapy is still unclear.

The microbiome plays a pivotal role in human metabolism, physiology, and immune regulation, with its constituents intricately linked to an individual's health status and medical history. In non-cancer population, H. pylori infection significantly influenced the composition and diversity of the gut microbiome, driven by alterations in the host immune response, the production of cytotoxins and enzymes, direct interactions, and changes in gastric acidity, while its impact on the profile of gut microbiome in GC patients is unclear. Recent studies suggested that gut microbiome can serve as an immunotherapy sensitizer, highlighting its role in immune regulation and therapeutic response. Therefore, it is urgent to clarify how H. pylori infection remodels the profile of gut microbiome in GC patients, and their comprehensive influence on immunotherapy. Additionally, intratumoral microbiome has become a new component of the tumor microenvironment (TME) that cannot be ignored. It is reported that intratumoral microbiome was associated with metastasis, prognosis and microsatellite-instability-high (MSI-H) status of GC. However, the impact of H. pylori on intratumoral microbiome is largely unknown either, making it difficult to assess whether intratumoral microbiome are a pathway through which H. pylori affects prognosis and therapeutic response.

This study evaluates the role of H. pylori-driven microbiome changes in gastrointestinal cancer patients and its impact on immunotherapy. Stool and tissue samples were collected from GC, esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC), and MSI-H colorectal cancer (CC) patients before immunotherapy. The gut and intratumoral microbiomes were analyzed for diversity and abundance, comparing H. pylori-positive and negative groups. The relationship between the microbiome and immunotherapy response (irPFS) was examined, along with functional enrichment analysis to explore the mechanisms underlying these microbial changes. The study highlights how H. pylori-positive GC may be more sensitive to immunotherapy.

Methods

Cohort recruitment and sample collection

Patients diagnosed with gastrointestinal cancers, including GC, ESCC, and CC, were enrolled in this study. A total of 218 GC patients, 134 ESCC patients, and 86 CC patients were identified from those who received immunotherapy treatments at Beijing Cancer Hospital from 2018 to 2024. Due to the limited availability of clinical samples, we included as many eligible cases as possible to ensure adequate statistical power for the planned analyses. This study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Peking University Cancer Hospital (approval number: 2024KT154). Informed consent for sample collection and subsequent analysis was obtained from participants. All tests and procedures were performed in accordance with the principles outlined in the Helsinki Declaration of 1964. The efficacy of treatments was assessed every 6 weeks through periodic clinical evaluations and imaging studies to accurately measure tumor response and progression. This structured approach ensured a thorough analysis of treatment impact, with confirmation checks conducted no less than 4 weeks post-initial recording to confirm treatment outcomes. In accordance with guidelines of Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors version 1.1 (RECIST 1.1), participants in this study were categorized as either responders or non-responders. Responders included two groups: those with acquired resistance (AR), characterized by an initial response of stable disease (SD), partial response (PR), or complete response (CR), followed by the development of progressive disease (PD) after 6 months; and long responders (LR), defined by the persistence of SD or PR for more than 1 year without any evidence of disease progression at the time of recruitment. Conversely, patients were classified as non-responders if their best response was PD or if they exhibited SD/PR/CR followed by acquired PD in 6 months. The definition of AR in ICI treatment is still evolving, and our approach was based on the current expert consensus from Society for Immunotherapy of Cancer (SITC), which provide a framework for defining and studying resistance to ICI therapy. To mitigate potential confounding variables that could impact study results, rigorous inclusion and exclusion criteria have been established. The inclusion criteria included (1) patients aged 18 years or older diagnosed with GC; (2) patients who are anticipated to undergo immunotherapy; and (3) patients who provided written informed consent for trial participation. The exclusion criteria encompassed as follows: (1) patients with a recent history of fever or serious infection within 1 month prior to initiation of immunotherapy; (2) patients who have undergone gastrointestinal resection or diversion surgery within the previous 6 months; (3) patients who have received broad-spectrum antibiotics orally or intravenously within one month before or during immunotherapy; and (4) patients with active autoimmune diseases.

H. pylori status assessment

Before starting first-line treatment or at the patient’s first clinical visit, a 13C-urea breath test (UBT) was used to assess for Helicobacter pylori infection. To minimize the possibility of false-positive results, patients who had received standard anti-H. pylori therapy (one proton pump inhibitor, one bismuth agent, and two antibiotics) were excluded from the test. After fasting for at least 4 h, each participant exhaled into a first collection bag through a face mask to obtain a baseline breath sample. The patient then swallowed a capsule containing 75 mg of 13C-urea (HEADWAY, Guangdong, China). Thirty minutes later, a post-intake breath sample was collected into a second collection bag. Trained research nurses used an HCBT-01 analyzer (HEADWAY, Guangdong, China) to identify the 13CO2 and 12CO2 absorption peaks in the samples. The change in the 13CO2/12CO2 ratio before and after administration was quantified using infrared spectroscopy. A change from baseline (DOB) greater than 4.0 indicated Helicobacter pylori infection. A potential source of bias is that a small number of patients with esophageal cancer were unable to swallow the capsule and therefore did not undergo urea breath testing.

Fecal samples collection, metagenomics sequencing, and data processing

Stool samples were collected from patients undergoing immunotherapy using a standardized collection protocol. Briefly, baseline fecal samples were obtained after patient admission and prior to the initiation of the first treatment. We assessed treatment efficacy at each bi-weekly patient visit. The collected fecal samples were stored using the Wehealthgene Fecal Microlution TM Collection kit (catalog No. ML-001A, Wehealthgene) to ensure the preservation of microbial DNA integrity. Then the samples were transported to the laboratory within 3 days to maintain sample quality. DNA was extracted using QIAamp PowerFecal DNA Kit (catalog No. 12830–50, QIAGEN), followed by library construction and sequencing on an Illumina NovoSeq 6000 platform (Novo Gene).

The raw sequencing reads were quality controlled via KneadData (version 0.6.1), which integrated several quality control (QC) tools [e.g., FastQC. After trimming low-quality bases from reads and omitting reads less than 100 bp in length, the filtered reads were mapped to the human genome (hg 19) using bowtie2 (https://bowtie-bio.sourceforge.net/bowtie2/index.shtml), and host contaminants were removed. Taxonomic profiling was accomplished using MetaPhlAn4 with default parameters, while functional analyses were performed using HUMAnN3 with the Uniref90 database (https://www.uniprot.org/help/uniref).

Tumor samples collection and tumor microbiome profiling using third-generation sequencing technology

Tumor tissues were harvested during surgical resection, ensuring the exclusion of adjacent non-malignant areas. The collection is performed under the most sterile conditions possible to minimize the risk of contamination. The collected sample was snap-frozen in liquid nitrogen and stored at − 80 °C to preserve biomolecule integrity. Samples are anonymized and logged with detailed clinical annotations. Employing hexadecyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB) or sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) methods, genomic DNA is extracted from samples. Purity and concentration are assessed using agarose gel electrophoresis. Suitable volumes of samples are diluted to a concentration of 1 ng/μl with sterile water. Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification is conducted using diluted genomic DNA as a template. Specific primers with Barcodes, Phusion® High-Fidelity PCR Master Mix with GC Buffer from New England Biolabs, and high-fidelity enzymes ensure efficient and accurate amplification. PCR products are evaluated using a 2% agarose gel electrophoresis; equal amounts are pooled and further assessed with a 2% agarose gel. Target bands are purified using the QIAGEN gel recovery kit. DNA ligase is used to attach sequencing adapters to the amplified DNA fragments. AMPure PB magnetic beads are employed for size selection and purification of the DNA fragments. SMRT Bell libraries are constructed, and after resuspension in buffer, BluePipin is used to sort specific fragment sizes, followed by further purification with AMPure PB magnetic beads. The prepared libraries are quantified using Qubit, and insert fragment sizes are confirmed with Agilent 2100. Subsequently, sequencing is performed on the PacBio platform.

Data processing and taxonomic annotation of full-length 16S microbiome profiles

Raw PacBio sequencing data is initially exported as BAM format files. The lima software is then employed to distinguish data from individual samples based on barcode sequences, with all sequences saved in BAM format. Subsequently, the circular consensus sequencing (CCS) within SMRT Link v7.0 is utilized to correct the sequences with the following parameters: CCS score set to 3 and a minimum accuracy rate of 0.99. Sequences that are shorter than 1340 bases or longer than 1740 bases are eliminated and saved in both fastq and fasta formats. Subsequently, SSR (Short Sequence Repeat) filtering is conducted, and primers are removed using cutadapt, discarding sequences with more than 8 consecutive identical nucleotides. The reads obtained after these processes represent the final valid data, termed Clean Reads.

The Uparse software (Uparse v7.0.1001) is employed to cluster all Clean Reads from samples with a default consensus of 97% identity, forming operational taxonomic units (OTUs). Representative sequences for each OTU are selected based on the frequency of occurrence within the OTU, with the most abundant sequence designated as the representative. Taxonomic annotation of OTU representative sequences is performed using the Mothur method against the SILVA SSUrRNA database (http://www.arb-silva.de/) with a threshold setting of 0.8 to 1, yielding taxonomic information. Community composition at various taxonomic levels (kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genera, and species) is statistically analyzed for each sample. MUSCLE software (Version 3.8.31) is utilized for rapid multiple sequence alignment, establishing the phylogenetic relationships among all OTU representative sequences. Finally, data normalization is conducted for each sample, with the sample containing the largest amount of data serving as the standard for normalization. Subsequent alpha and beta diversity analyses are based on the normalized data.

Analysis of microbiome diversity and the identification of differential microbes from gut and tumor microbiome

After generating feature tables for taxa and pathways, we calculated alpha diversity with the VEGAN R package, which enabled us to determine the Shannon index, richness, and evenness of each sample’s microbial community. For beta diversity, we relied on both Bray–Curtis and Jaccard distances, and we used principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) to visualize the differences between groups. To address the complexity of clinical data, we examined the effects of various clinical variables, such as age, sex, tumor stage, medication history, BMI, and MSI status, on the microbiome. We began by conducting a permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) to assess the impact of these factors on microbial community composition. This analysis allowed us to differentiate between biologically significant differences and those caused by confounding variables by comparing PERMANOVA p values between groups and beta dispersion p values within groups.

We then applied MaAsLin2 (https://github.com/biobakery/Maaslin2) to account for confounding factors and to identify the gut microbiome most associated with treatment response. This method, termed “covariate adjustment,” removes the influence of confounding variables from the analysis, ensuring a more accurate assessment of the relationship between microbial taxa and clinical outcomes. By including confounders as covariates in the regression model, we were able to isolate the true association between the microbiome and treatment response. Additionally, we employed LEfSe (LDA Effect Size, https://huttenhower.sph.harvard.edu/lefse/) and ANCOMBC to perform the differential analysis. LEfSe was used to identify and interpret biomarkers in the context of microbial communities, capitalizing on linear discriminant analysis (LDA) to estimate the effect size of each feature, with a significance threshold set at LDA score > 2 and p < 0.05. ANCOMBC, a non-parametric method, was also applied to detect significant differences in microbial abundances between groups, offering a robust statistical framework to control for multiple comparisons. These complementary methods provided a comprehensive statistical evaluation, enhancing the reliability of our findings and solidifying the link between microbial composition and clinical outcomes. Statistical significance across methods (MaAsLin2 and ANCOMBC) was evaluated using false discovery rate (FDR) correction, with associations considered significant at q < 0.3.

Machine learning model construction and validation

To assess the impact of Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) status on model performance, we incorporated the presence or absence of H. pylori as a feature in our LightGBM classifier and compared this model’s predictive accuracy with a version of the model that lacked this feature. This binary feature, indicating H. pylori positivity or negativity, was added to capture potential variations in microbial communities associated with H. pylori infection, which could influence treatment response.

The dataset was split into training and testing sets, with 20% reserved for evaluation, and the models were evaluated using tenfold cross-validation. BorutaPy was used for feature selection, a method that leverages a randomized algorithm to assess the importance of each feature by comparing them to shadow features, resulting in a significance ranking. Models were subjected to hyperparameter tuning using Hyperopt to optimize their predictive capabilities. The performance of the models was then compared based on their accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC-ROC). The “SHAP” package was utilized to interpret the impact of the H. pylori feature on the model’s predictions, providing insights into its contribution to the predictive performance. This comparative analysis helped us to assess the significance of H. pylori status as a predictive biomarker within our models.

Univariate meta-analysis for the identification of H. pylori-associated intratumoral microbiome

This approach is primarily based on the reported methods. Specifically, we have used the refined logarithmic fold change metric, universally applied to read abundance datasets, to enhance resolution in sparse microbiome profiles where zero values obscure the informativeness of median-based estimates, particularly for taxa with a prevalence below 0.5. The refined fold change is determined by the mean difference across predefined quantiles, utilizing quantiles from 0.1 to 0.9 with 0.1 increments. In the evaluation of the study’s precision and recall for identifying microbial taxa associations derived from a meta-analysis, the reference set was delineated by taxa correlating at a specified false discovery rate (FDR) within the meta-analysis framework.

Statistics analysis

To evaluate the correlation between clinical features and patient outcomes, we utilized Fisher’s exact test to assess associations between various clinical characteristics and treatment responses. Additionally, Wilcoxon tests were employed to compare the relative abundance of microbial taxa and metabolic pathways across distinct patient groups. For progression-free survival (PFS) analysis, we performed both univariate and multivariate Cox proportional hazards regression to identify factors associated with survival outcomes. Survival curves were compared using the Kaplan–Meier method and log-rank tests. Patients lost to follow-up were censored at the date of last contact in accordance with standard survival analysis procedures. All analyses and survival curve generation were conducted using R Studio (R version 3.6.3), ensuring a robust and comprehensive statistical approach.

Results

Clinical and pathological characteristics of the gut microbiome cohort

From 2018 to 2024, this study collected and analyzed gut microbiome samples of baseline from 218 GC patients, including 175 GC patients receiving anti-PD-1/PD-L1-based immunotherapy (Fig. 1A) and 43 GC patients treated with chemotherapy or target therapy. The median follow-up time for GC patients was 24.6 months. The clinicopathological characteristics of 218 GC patients are summarized in Table 1. The cohort included 98 H. pylori-negative and 120 H. pylori-positive patients. There were no significant differences in sex, age, body mass index (BMI), stage at diagnosis, tumor differentiation, Lauren classification, tumor location, or expression levels of EGFR and HER2 between the two groups. Additionally, microsatellite and Epstein–Barr virus (EBV) statuses were similar across the groups. Most patients were diagnosed at stage IV (72.9%).

In terms of treatment, 80.3% of GC patients received PD-1/PD-L1-based immunotherapy, which allowed us to analyze the complex relationship between H. pylori infection, intestinal microbial profile, and immunotherapy efficacy (Additional file 1: Table 1). It is worth noting that there was no significant difference in the regimen and number of lines of immunotherapy between the H. pylori-positive and -negative groups, which reduced the potential bias and made the results reliable. As shown in Fig. 1 B and C, we observed that the median irPFS and immune-related overall survival (irOS) were significantly prolonged in the H. pylori-positive group compared with those in the H. pylori-negative group (7.4 months vs. 4.8 months, p = 0.048; 21.4 months vs. 12.0 months, p = 0.028, respectively). The COX regression models also showed a consistent trend (Additional file 1: Table 2). We investigated the optimal efficacy of immunotherapy and found that more patients achieved CR or PR in the H. pylori-positive group, indicating that H. pylori-positive GC is more sensitive to immunotherapy (Fig. 1D), which is consistent with our previous findings.

This study also included immunotherapy cohorts of 88 CC with deficient mismatch repair (dMMR)/MSI-H and 134 ESCC patients (Additional file 1: Fig. S1). The median follow-up time for CC patients and EC patients was 30.8 months and 20.5 months, respectively. No significant differences were observed between the H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative groups in terms of age at diagnosis, gender, stage at diagnosis, microsatellite status, type of therapy (Additional file 1: Table 3). For the ESCC immunotherapy cohort, the only significant difference between the two groups was the line of immunotherapy, with a higher number of patients in the H. pylori-positive group receiving first-line treatment (Additional file 1: Table 4). Even after adjusting for therapy lines, hazard ratios for H. pylori-positive ESCC patients remained > 1, consistent with a trend toward increased hazard (poorer irPFS) in the H. pylori-positive subgroup, although the findings did not reach statistical significance due to limited sample size (Additional file 1: Table 5).

H. pylori-driven changes in gut microbiome shaped a unique microbial environment

Since EBV-positive GC and dMMR/MSI-H GC are known immunotherapy-sensitive GC subtypes with unique molecular features, immune profiles, and microbiome, we first separated them from the whole population and performed exploratory analyses. In EBV-negative MSS GC, we found that the gut microbiome of H. pylori-positive patients had higher alpha diversity. In addition, the beta diversity of the gut microbiome from the same patient sub-group also showed significant differences (Fig. 2A). Similar trends were found in EBV-positive GC and dMMR/MSI-H GC patients (Fig. 2B, C). Subsequently, we compared the species with different abundances between H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative groups in EBV-negative MSS GC (Fig. 2D). We first found that 45 species were different between the two groups, and then we performed multivariate correction and survival analysis (Fig. 2E, Additional file 1: Fig. S2A).

To understand how gut microbiome may mediate the effect of H. pylori infection on immunotherapy efficacy, we further explored the association between gut microbiome abundance, H. pylori infection, and irPFS. Clostridium leptum, Oscillibacter sp. ER4, and Ruminococcus bromii were significantly enriched in the H. pylori-positive group and were associated with longer irPFS. Conversely, Megasphaera micronuciformis was more abundant in the H. pylori-negative group and predicted shorter irPFS (Figs. 2F and 3A). In CC and ESCC patients, we did not find any abundance differences of these four microbes between H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative patients (Additional file 1: Fig. S3A and Additional file 1: Fig. S3C). Interestingly, we noted that three of them, including Clostridium leptum, Oscillibacter sp. ER4, and Ruminococcus bromii, have negative impacts on the immunotherapy for CC and ESCC (Additional file 1: Fig. S3B and Additional file 1: Fig. S3D). We observed a significant difference in the number of treatment lines between the H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative groups in the ESCC cohort. Therefore, we performed a multivariate Cox regression with the number of treatment lines as a covariate (Additional file 1: Table 6). We noted a trend of hazard ratio (HR) greater than 1, indicating that the association between the microbiota and irPFS was not reversed after adjusting for the number of treatment lines.

To investigate the overall characteristics of the 45 differential species, we constructed microbial co-occurrence networks for H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative GC patients respectively (Fig. 3B) and quantified them with metrics, including betweenness centrality (Fig. 3C), degree, and closeness centrality (Additional file 1: Fig. S2B). These quantitative measurements confirmed that H. pylori infection induces significant shifts in the interaction patterns of the gut microbiome. For example, the betweenness centrality value of Eubacterium ramulus differed markedly between the H. pylori-positive and H. pylori-negative groups (150 vs. 32), suggesting its pivotal “bridge” role in the gut microbiome of H. pylori-positive GC patients, a role not observed in H. pylori-negative individuals.

We also observed alterations in several microbiome-related metabolic activities (Fig. 3D), which could be classified into four categories: amino acid metabolism, lipid metabolism, energy metabolism, and carbohydrate and sugar metabolism. Notably, l-arginine biosynthesis was significantly enhanced, while l-arginine degradation was markedly reduced in H. pylori-positive GC. In two independent clinical trials (NCT02534649, initiated in December 2015; and NCT03000257, initiated on December 14, 2016), high levels of l-arginine were identified as a predictor for longer irPFS on immunotherapy. Additionally, other microbiome-related metabolic changes favoring immunotherapy were identified in H. pylori-positive GC, such as decreased glycolysis activity.

The profile and metabolic characteristics of intratumoral bacteria imply the possible mechanism of H. pylori-positive GC response to immunotherapy

We performed 16S rRNA full-length sequencing to profile intratumoral microbiome in 32 FFPE GC samples. Following the recommendations of the Communicable Disease Center in the United States, we removed common environmental contaminants typically found in hospital settings. Subsequently, we conducted a meta-analysis by including another public study (PRJNA239281) to identify consistently robust features (see detailed in Methods). After rarefaction, a total of 40 samples with confirmed H. pylori status were included in the final analysis (Additional file 1: Table 7). Consistent with the results of the 13C urea breath test, we initially observed a higher abundance of Helicobacter in the H. pylori-positive group. Jakob Wirbel et al. developed a metagenomic meta-analysis approach for cross-cohort integration. By applying this method to our study, we again detected an enrichment of Helicobacter in H. pylori-positive GC tissues, underscoring the high quality of our sequencing data. Additionally, we identified two bacterial genera enriched in H. pylori-negative GC samples, namely Streptococcus and Granulicatella (Fig. 4A). Based on the microbial taxonomy data, we inferred potential metabolic pathway alterations using MetaCyc (Fig. 4B). We found that l-lysine fermentation to acetate and butanoate (P163_PWY) and mandelate degradation to acetyl-CoA pathways (PWY_6957) were significantly enhanced in the H. pylori-positive group. Among them, butanoate is believed to promote cell metabolism and enhance the memory potential of activated CD8+ T cells, which is beneficial for immunotherapy.

Incorporating H. pylori can improve the performance of microbiome-based prognosis and response prediction for GC immunotherapy

Our previous studies have demonstrated that fecal microbiome-based prediction models are effective in identifying patients who are more likely to benefit from immunotherapy. Given the findings that H. pylori infection affects the efficacy of immunotherapy, we investigated whether incorporating H. pylori infection status into the fecal microbiome-based model would enhance the prediction of immunotherapy outcomes in GC. As shown in Fig. 5A, we divided GC patients into four groups, revealing significant differences in median survival across these groups. Notably, H. pylori-positive patients with high abundance of Clostridium leptum exhibited significantly longer irPFS compared to H. pylori-negative patients with low abundance of Clostridium leptum (13.3 months vs. 4.4 months, p = 0.018). Furthermore, we observed enhanced stratification in immunotherapy-related overall survival (irOS), with H. pylori-positive patients with high abundance of Oscillibacter sp. ER4 demonstrating prolonged median irOS compared to H. pylori-negative patients with low abundance of Oscillibacter sp. ER4 (25.6 months vs. 8.0 months, p = 0.008). In our predictive model for treatment response, the model incorporating gut microbiome and H. pylori infection yielded a higher area under the curve (AUC) compared to the gut microbiome-only model in both the training and testing cohorts (Fig. 5B). For the integrated model (gut microbiome + H. pylori), the training set yielded a mean AUC of 0.811 (95% CI: 0.742–0.870), while the test set mean AUC was 0.733 (95% CI: 0.576–0.868). For the gut microbiome-only model, the training and test AUCs were 0.747 (95% CI: 0.675–0.816) and 0.709 (95% CI: 0.548–0.851), respectively. The DCA analysis and calibration curves of the predictive model are shown in Supplementary Additional file 1: Fig. S4.

Discussion

This study investigates the impact of H. pylori infection on the gut microbiome and its role in the efficacy of immunotherapy in GC patients. The cohort included 218 GC patients, with 80.3% receiving PD-1/PD-L1-based immunotherapy. H. pylori-positive patients showed significantly longer irPFS and irOS compared to H. pylori-negative patients. The gut microbiome of H. pylori-positive GC patients exhibited higher alpha and beta diversity, with specific microbial species such as Clostridium leptum and Oscillibacter sp. ER4 linked to improved immunotherapy outcomes. Metabolic pathway analysis revealed alterations in amino acid and lipid metabolism, including enhanced l-arginine biosynthesis, which has been associated with better immunotherapy response. Intratumoral microbiome analysis revealed the enrichment of Streptococcus and Granulicatella in H. pylori-negative tissues. Notably, microbial pathways related to l-lysine fermentation and butanoate production were significantly enhanced in H. pylori-positive tumors. Furthermore, incorporating H. pylori infection status into microbiome-based prediction models improved the accuracy of immunotherapy outcome predictions, highlighting the potential of microbiome profiling in personalized treatment strategies for GC.

H. pylori is one of the most prevalent bacteria worldwide, and an increasing number of studies have begun to explore its association with immunotherapy outcomes in gastric cancer. A recent meta-analysis by Moraes et al. systematically examined this relationship. However, the overall consensus is still limited, partly due to heterogeneity among studies in defining infection activity, prior eradication history, tumor subtype, and host immune background, all of which may influence clinical outcomes. Our previous work suggested that H. pylori-positive GC may exhibit higher PD-L1 expression and distinct transcriptomic features, potentially contributing to enhanced immunotherapy sensitivity. Moreover, a study from the Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center categorized patients into H. pylori-negative, prior infection, and active infection groups, showing that both irPFS and overall survival (OS) were longer in the active infection group compared to the prior infection group, implying that H. pylori eradication might be associated with shortened irPFS and OS in previously infected patients. Although statistical tests were not provided, this trend supports the possibility that H. pylori infection could modulate immunotherapy outcomes. Together, these findings provide converging evidence that H. pylori-positive GC may be more responsive to immune checkpoint blockade, while our present study extends these observations by revealing the underlying microbial and metabolic mechanisms.

Building upon these findings, previous research has also shown that H. pylori infection is not only a major risk factor for gastric carcinogenesis in healthy individuals but also influences the composition of the gut microbiota. Nevertheless, the impact of H. pylori infection on the gut flora of GC patients remains largely unknown. Two of our studies have confirmed that specific alterations in gut microbiota composition are associated with sensitivity to immunotherapy. Therefore, the next question is how H. pylori infection shapes the gut and tumor microbiome in GC, and whether this may partly explain why H. pylori-positive tumors are more responsive to immunotherapy.

The exploration of gut microbiome diversity revealed that H. pylori-positive GC patients exhibited higher alpha diversity and distinct beta diversity profiles than their H. pylori-negative GC counterparts, particularly within the EBV-negative MSS GC subgroup. The higher alpha diversity of gut flora was the characteristic of immunotherapy responders in several studies. This disparity underscores the role of H. pylori in shaping a unique microbial environment for GC that may favor therapeutic responses. The identification of specific microbial species, such as Clostridium leptum and Ruminococcus bromii, enriched in the H. pylori-positive GC group, correlates with improved irPFS. Notably, both Clostridium leptum and Ruminococcus bromii were able to produce butyrate, which has been demonstrated to improve the efficacy of immunotherapy by moduling cytotoxic CD8+ T cells in previous studies. However, we cannot oversimplify our understanding of the complex roles of these bacteria. In this study, we found that both Clostridium leptum and Ruminococcus bromii were negative for immunotherapy in CC and ESCC cohort. In fact, gut microbiome’s contradictory prognostic value of immunotherapy was also reported in past studies. For instance, Ruminococcus bromii was found to be enriched in the responders of melanoma and non-small cell lung cancer immunotherapy patients. However, in PURE-01 study that is a prospective clinical trial to evaluate if perioperative pembrolizumab can lead to sustained remission post-cystectomy, researchers found that Ruminococcus bromii was enriched in non-responders and was related to shorter event-free survival, which has been demonstrated in mice model. In addition, Jiang et al. believed that Fusobacterium nucleatum induces tumor resistance to immunotherapy in colorectal cancer through succinic acid. At the same time, Wang et al. reported that Fusobacterium nucleatum can promote immunotherapy in colorectal cancer through butyric acid. These results suggest that this contradictory effect may depend on the dominant metabolite. We also found that Megasphaera micronuciformis was enriched in the H. pylori-negative GC group. Although the mechanism by which it exhibits mutual exclusivity with H. pylori is unclear, we found that it can abrogate the benefit of immunotherapy. These findings suggest that H. pylori-driven gut microbiome signatures are associated with immune responses and treatment outcomes, and their mechanisms of action warrant further investigation.

We observed that the microbiome of H. pylori-positive gastric cancer patients exhibited enhanced l-arginine biosynthesis and reduced l-arginine degradation. Clinically, this metabolic shift was associated with improved immune-related progression-free survival (irPFS) during immunotherapy, consistent with prior observations that higher baseline plasma l-arginine levels correlate with better responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors, including higher clinical benefit rates and longer progression-free and overall survival. While these findings are correlative, preclinical studies provide mechanistic insights suggesting a potential causal link. For example, Roger Geiger et al. demonstrated that elevated l-arginine levels in activated T cells induce a metabolic shift from glycolysis to oxidative phosphorylation and promote the generation of central memory-like T cells with higher survival capacity, enhancing antitumor activity and responses to immune checkpoint blockade. In addition, we observed reduced glycolysis and alterations in amino acid metabolism in the tumor microenvironment, which may further support immune activation. Taken together, these results highlight a potential bridge role of the microbiome between H. pylori infection and immune modulation.

Recent studies suggest that the tumoral microbiome, particularly intratumoral bacteria, not only plays a role in tumorigenesis but also modulates the tumor microenvironment and immune cell function. Certain bacteria can produce metabolites, such as short-chain fatty acids, which enhance antitumor immune responses by promoting T cell activation and proliferation. Additionally, the diversity and composition of the intratumoral microbiome may influence the effectiveness of immune checkpoint inhibitors, leading to varying clinical responses among patients undergoing immunotherapy. Therefore, studying the types of intratumoral bacteria and their metabolite profiles will help innovate strategies for personalized immunotherapy. Our study identifies two genera of intratumoral bacteria that were significantly diminished in H. pylori-positive GC, suggesting a competitive exclusion relationship with H. pylori. Streptococcus is a diverse generas that includes both pathogenic and commensal species. In the context of tumors, certain Streptococcus species have been associated with inflammation and immune modulation, potentially influencing tumor progression or response to therapies. Granulicatella was often found in the oral cavity, but some studies shown it was related to the efficacy of immunotherapy for hepatocellular carcinoma. Interestingly, our data showed that the fermentation of l-lysine to acetate and butyrate was enhanced in H. pylori-positive gastric cancer patients. Short-chain fatty acids such as butyrate have been reported to modulate cytotoxic CD8+ T cell activity and potentially enhance the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade through mechanisms including TCR signaling modulation, induction of granzyme B, IFN-γ, and TNF-α expression, and NF-κB activation via TLR5 binding. Although we did not directly measure metabolite flux, overlapping functional pathways between gut and tumor microbiota (e.g., l-lysine biosynthesis in gut and l-lysine fermentation to acetate/butyrate in tumors; Figs. 3D and 4B). These observations in our cohort are correlative and provide complementary evidence to prior mechanistic studies, suggesting that microbiome-mediated l-lysine degradation may be associated with a more favorable tumor microenvironment for immune activation. Therefore, whether H. pylori infection can enhance immunotherapy responses through butyrate-related metabolic pathways, potentially driven by microbiome-mediated l-lysine degradation, warrants further investigation.

The role of the gut microbiome in predicting the efficacy of immunotherapy has garnered increasing attention in recent years. Emerging evidence suggests that the composition and diversity of gut bacteria can significantly influence immune responses, thereby impacting therapeutic outcomes. For instance, specific microbial profiles have been associated with enhanced anti-tumor immunity, suggesting that a diverse and resilient microbiome may facilitate improved responses to treatments such as immune checkpoint inhibitors. In this context, our study underscores the importance of integrating H. pylori infection status into microbiome-based predictive models for immunotherapy efficacy. By incorporating H. pylori as a variable, we observed a marked improvement in the model’s predictive performance. This enhancement may be attributed to H. pylori’s established role in modulating the gut microbiome and influencing local immune responses. The findings highlight the complexity of interactions between microbial communities and tumor immunology, suggesting that a more nuanced understanding of these relationships could lead to more accurate predictions of treatment outcomes.

Despite these observations, the carcinogenic potential of H. pylori remains unequivocal. While H. pylori-positive gastric cancers may exhibit improved responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors, this does not imply that H. pylori infection enhances immunotherapy efficacy. It is plausible that the long-term evolution of H. pylori-associated carcinogenesis, which typically develops over years or decades, endows tumors with unique molecular and immune characteristics that render them more immunologically active and thus more responsive to checkpoint blockade. Moreover, H. pylori-positive gastric cancers may represent a heterogeneous group. Tumors arising as a consequence of chronic H. pylori infection could differ biologically from those secondarily colonized after tumor onset, despite both being classified as H. pylori-positive. Understanding these distinctions is critical, as careful stratification of patient populations will help clarify the multifaceted role of H. pylori in tumor initiation, progression, and therapeutic response.

This study’s limitation lies in the fact that all tumor samples were sourced from a single medicine center. Nonetheless, as a tier-one cancer hospital in China, we draw patients from diverse regions across the country, enhancing the likelihood that our sample reflects the broader patient population. In addition, 218 patients were included in this study, which enabled us to observe some rare GC subtypes, including MSI-H GC and EBV-positive GC. Since MSI-H GC and EBV-positive GC are known subtypes that are sensitive to immunotherapy, this study focused on EBV-negative MSS GC, which is also the bottleneck of GC immunotherapy. We had no validation cohort to support our prediction model of GC immunotherapy prognosis and response. Additionally, tumor samples and fecal samples were not collected from the same patient population, preventing direct correlation analyses between gut and intratumoral microbiota. Future studies will aim to systematically evaluate the microbiota–metabolite dynamics through paired sampling and longitudinal tracking, thereby enabling a more comprehensive understanding of how H. pylori–induced gut dysbiosis may influence intratumoral microbial community and metabolic activity. Patients who had received standard anti-H. pylori therapy within the past month were excluded from the study. However, due to clinical feasibility constraints, information regarding patients’ more remote prior H. pylori eradication history was unavailable. As this study mainly focused on infection status at treatment baseline in relation to microbiome features and therapeutic response, the absence of more remote eradication history is unlikely to have a substantial impact on the principal conclusions.

Conclusions

H. pylori infection significantly affects the composition and metabolic activities of intratumoral and intestinal microbiome, which may sensitize gastric cancer to immunotherapy. Incorporating H. pylori infection into the microbiome-based prediction model can improve model performance.
