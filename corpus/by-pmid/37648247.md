# Adjuvant Systemic Therapies for Resected Stages III and IV Melanoma: A Multi-Center Retrospective Clinical Study.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Adjuvant therapies have been approved for resected melanoma based on improved recurrence-free survival. We present early findings from a real-world study on adjuvant treatments for melanoma.

**METHODS**: A comprehensive chart review was conducted for patients receiving adjuvant systemic therapy for resected high-risk stages III and IV melanoma. Statistical analysis was performed to assess recurrence-free survival and subgroup differences.

**RESULTS**: A total of 149 patients (median age = 58.0 years, 61.1% men, 49.7% with BRAF V600E/K genotypes) were included, with 94.6% having resected stage III melanoma. Anti-PD-1 immunotherapy was received by 86.5% of patients, while 13.4% received BRAF-targeted therapy. At a median follow-up of 22.4 months, the recurrence rate was 31.5%, with 1-year and 2-year recurrence-free survival rates of 79% and 62%, respectively. Similar recurrence rates were observed between anti-PD-1 immunotherapy and BRAF-targeted therapy. Long-term toxicity affected 27.4% of patients, with endocrinopathies and late-emergent immune-related adverse events being common.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Real-world adjuvant systemic therapy aligns with clinical trial practice. Recurrence rates remain high despite treatment, and long-term toxicities, including endocrinopathies and chronic inflammatory conditions, are not uncommon.

Implications for Practice

This study provides significant insights into the adjuvant treatment landscape for high-risk melanoma. The findings emphasize the importance of considering not only treatment efficacy but also long-term toxicity profiles and potential chronic immune-related adverse events. These insights will assist healthcare professionals in making informed treatment decisions and optimizing patient care for individuals with high-risk melanoma.

Introduction

Two therapeutic strategies have significantly improved melanoma outcomes over the past decade: molecularly targeted therapy for BRAF-mutant melanoma, also known as target therapy\ and immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) that are not mutation specific. In metastatic melanoma, BRAF/MEK-targeted therapy, such as dabrafenib plus trametinib, can induce rapid tumor response in BRAF-mutant melanoma and improve survival. Additionally, ICIs targeting a cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA-4), such as ipilimumab and programmed death-1 (PD-1), nivolumab or pembrolizumab, can produce sustained tumor regression with the possibility of prolonged survival and long-term disease control.

Between 2017 and 2018, 3 adjuvant systemic therapies gained rapid regulatory approval for high-risk resected stage III or IV melanoma based on a significant improvement in recurrence-free survival (RFS). Randomized phase III clinical trials have consistently demonstrated the clinical benefit of adjuvant therapy. Notably, in CHECKMATE-238, the hazard ratio for disease recurrence or death for nivolumab was 0.65 (97.56% CI, 0.51-0.83 at 1 year, P < .001 vs. ipilimumab) in American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) 7 stage IIIB-C or IV melanoma, including mucosal melanoma. In KEYNOTE-045, pembrolizumab showed a hazard ratio of 0.57 (98.4% CI, 0.43-0.74 at 1 year, P < .001 vs. placebo) in AJCC-7 stage IIIA-C melanoma without in-transit disease. Additionally, COMBI-AD demonstrated a hazard ratio of 0.57 (95% CI, 0.42-0.79, P = .0006 at 3 years vs. placebo) for dabrafenib plus trametinib in AJCC-7 stage IIIA-C melanoma with BRAF V600E or V600K mutations.

Stage III melanoma has a poor outcome after locoregional resection, with a 5-year survival rate of only 30%-60%.Consequently, consideration of adjuvant systemic therapies as the standard of care was almost immediate in clinical practice. However, several key clinical questions remain. For instance, it is yet to be determined if the recurrence-free survival benefit of BRAF-targeted and anti-PD-1 adjuvant therapies will translate into an overall survival (OS) benefit, given the significant efficacy of systemic therapies for advanced melanoma. Notably, first-line ipilimumab plus nivolumab has demonstrated a 5-year survival rate of 52%, and a median overall survival of 72.1 months. Additionally, adjuvant anti-PD-1 immunotherapy can lead to irreversible long-term toxicity, particularly endocrinopathies. In the adjuvant setting, accurately predicting an individual’s likelihood of disease relapse remains a challenge. Therefore, assessing the risk-to-benefit ratio is of paramount importance. Clinical trials, like the EORTC 18071 phase III trial, have demonstrated the efficacy of adjuvant therapies such as ipilimumab in improving overall survival compared to placebo. Ipilimumab’s high discontinuation rate of 52% due to adverse events and a treatment-related death rate of 1.1% prevented its establishment as a standard of care. These findings highlight the importance of considering treatment-related toxicities when making treatment decisions. Recent evidence from a multicenter cohort further emphasizes the need to evaluate the risks of chronic immune-related adverse events in treatment decision-making to optimize patient outcomes.

Appropriate patient selection has also been complicated by the migration from AJCC 7th to 8th edition and may result in lower-risk patients being now offered adjuvant systemic therapy. Additionally, elective regional lymph node dissection (RLND) in stage III melanoma with microscopic nodal disease was mandatory with stringent quality control in all 3 pivotal trials. However, completion of RLND is no longer commonly practiced and not considered mandatory based on findings from the Multicenter Selective Lymphadenectomy Trial (MSLT-II) and the Dermatologic Cooperative Oncology Group (DeCOG) trials. The impact of these changes on clinical practice compared to clinical trials is unknown and treating providers must be aware of this nuisance.

This study aims to highlight the systemic treatment choice, pattern of locoregional care, treatment delivery, locoregional and distant recurrence, subsequent treatments, survival, and long-term toxicities in the context of adjuvant therapy for melanoma.

Materials and Methods

Patients

Using a local pharmacy database, we retrospectively identified all patients who had received adjuvant systemic therapies for resected melanoma in the Province of Alberta: 2 tertiary cancer centers (Tom Baker Cancer Centre, Cross Cancer Institute) and 4 regional cancer centers (Central Alberta Cancer Center, Grande Prairie Cancer Center, Jack Ady Cancer Center, Margery E. Yuill Cancer Center), Canada, between September 2017 and March 2021. All the patients had histologically confirmed melanoma and received at least 1 cycle of adjuvant systemic therapy. We excluded patients treated on clinical trials to best reflect the real-world clinical practice. The Health Research Ethics Board of Alberta Cancer Committee approved this study, and patient consent was waived due to the retrospective and anonymous nature of the work. Lastly, this study followed the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology (STROBE) checklist to construct an adequately presented manuscript.

Treatment and Assessment

Sentinel lymph node biopsy was performed in patients with a cutaneous primary melanoma greater than 1.0 mm in thickness with any characteristic or greater than 0.8 mm with ulceration. Patients with positive sentinel lymph node biopsy (SLNB) were considered for elective RLND or regional lymph basin surveillance based on their risk of non-sentinel node involvement and of operative morbidity. Adjuvant radiation was considered in patients with an increased risk of local-regional recurrence. Patients with clinical presentation of regional lymph node metastasis received therapeutic RLND. Patients with stage IV melanoma had complete surgical resection. All patients received baseline staging with body computed tomography (CT) or positron emission tomography-CT as a standard of care prior to initiating adjuvant systemic therapy. BRAF mutation analysis was uniformly performed using PCR amplification and a single base extension technique. Beyond this, brain imaging and other imaging modalities, such as magnetic resonance imaging and bone scans, were performed based on clinical indication at the discretion of the treating physician.

Provincial approval for indications of adjuvant therapies (nivolumab, pembrolizumab, or dabrafenib/trametinib) was centralized, despite variations in the enrollment populations with partially overlapping stages. These included completely resected melanoma stage IIIA to IIIC or IV according to the AJCC-7th edition. After migration to AJCC-8th edition in 2018, the indication for IIIA melanoma was restricted to lymph node micrometastasis measuring more than or equal to 1 mm at the greatest diameter. Nivolumab was approved in 2017 and was dosed at 3 mg/kg intravenously every 2 weeks, for a total of 24 doses for approximately 1 year, or until or unless unacceptable toxicity developed, or disease recurred. After March 2020, the dosing schedule for nivolumab was transitioned to 6 mg/kg intravenously every 4 weeks. In 2017, the combination of adjuvant dabrafenib (150 mg twice daily) plus trametinib (2 mg once daily) every 28 days for 1 year was approved for the treatment of patients with BRAF-mutant melanoma, specifically those with BRAF V600 mutations. Lastly, adjuvant pembrolizumab (2 mg/kg intravenously every 3 weeks, for a total of 18 doses) was approved for the standard-of-care use in 2018.

Disease recurrence was determined using serial physical examinations and radiographic assessments, typically every 6 months. The regional lymph node basin was assessed with ultrasonography every 4 months in the absence of prior lymph node dissection. RFS was defined as the time from the initiation of adjuvant therapy to the appearance of one or more new melanoma lesions or death by any cause. Locoregional recurrences included local recurrences at the periphery of the previous surgical bed, regional in-transit metastases, and regional nodal failure. Distant metastases are defined as any recurrences outside the locoregional area. Toxicity was assessed using the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE) Version 5.0. Adverse events (AEs) and immune-related adverse advents (irAE) of clinical significance were defined as any immune-breakthrough adverse event that was grade 3 or higher or any event that caused a treatment delay greater than 4 weeks, caused discontinuation, or resulted in permanent organ dysfunction. Late-emergent adverse events were defined as adverse events with onsets greater than 100 days after the last dose of treatment, as in the CheckMate-238 study. Finally, long-term toxicities were defined as persistent functional impairments after the completion or discontinuation of systemic therapies and were unresolved at the last follow-up assessment.

Statistical Analyses

Patient demographics and clinical characteristics were summarized using descriptive statistics for the overall cohort and patient subgroups, with categorical variables presented as frequencies (percentages) and continuous variables expressed as median (range). Pathological stage was uniformly converted to the AJCC-8th edition for this analysis. Next, a subgroup analysis was conducted to characterize safety and disease outcomes by treatment type and clinical attributes by recurrence pattern. The safety profile of each treatment type was assessed by the frequency of treatment delays and early discontinuation due to adverse events. The Kaplan-Meier method was used to estimate the RFS for the overall cohort and to elucidate RFS differences between patient subgroups, stratifying by BRAF status and treatment type. Statistical comparisons of survival were completed using the log-rank test. Lastly, a statistical analysis was conducted using R, the significance level was defined a priori as < .05, and all tests were 2-sided.

Results

Patient Characteristics

Of the 149 patients who received adjuvant nivolumab, pembrolizumab, or dabrafenib plus trametinib from September 2017 to March 2021, 91 (61.1%) were male, and the median age at treatment initiation was 58.0 years (range, 15.0-88.0 years). Baseline patient and disease characteristics are summarized in Table 1. The primary melanoma site was cutaneous in 136 patients (91.3%), unknown primary in 10 (6.7%), and mucosal in three (2.0%) patients, respectively. Furthermore, 141 patients (94.6%) had stage III melanoma, 7 (4.7%) had resected stage IV melanoma, and 1 (0.7%) had stage II desmoplastic melanoma. In the latter case, wide local excision was not possible due to anatomical location. Among stage III melanoma patients, the most common stage grouping was IIIC at 49.0%, followed by IIIB at 29.5%, IIIA at 11.4%, and IIID at 2.0%. Additionally, BRAF mutation analyses were available in 143 patients, with BRAF V600E or V600K genotypes detected in 74 (49.7%) patients. Among the patients with primary cutaneous melanoma, 46 (33.8%) had high-chronic sun-damaged melanoma, which refers to melanoma originating from the head and neck or distal extremities. Additionally, 81 (59.6%) had low-chronic sun-damaged melanoma, indicating melanoma arising from the truncal or proximal extremities. Furthermore, among the 99 patients with available body mass indices, 63 patients (35.4%) were overweight, and 36 (36.4%) were obese. In addition, 103 patients (69.1%) received adjuvant systemic treatment following their initial diagnosis with melanoma, whereas 46 (30.9%) received adjuvant systemic treatment following complete resection of recurrent disease.

Adjuvant Treatment Patterns

Locoregional Therapies

In the 107 patients with stage III melanoma diagnosed after positive SLNB, only 24 (22.4%) received elective RLND, whereas most opted for regional nodal surveillance. Of the 7 patients with extra nodal extension observed on sentinel lymph node biopsy, 5 underwent elective RLND, and 1 received adjuvant radiation. Among the 32 patients with macroscopic stage III melanoma, all completed therapeutic RLND. Additionally, 5 patients received adjuvant radiation, 4 to the lymph node basin and 1 to the primary site for positive margin.

Systemic Therapies

Of the 149 patients, 99 (66.4%) received nivolumab, 30 (20.1%) received pembrolizumab and among the 74 patients with BRAF V600E or V600K mutations, 20 (13.4%) received dabrafenib with trametinib. At the time of this analysis, 90 patients (60.4%) had completed a full course of adjuvant systemic therapy, and 2 patients (1.3%) were still on active treatment (Table 2). The median treatment duration was 11.0 months (range, 0.9-16.8), with a duration longer than 12 months due to treatment delays. Beyond this, 57 patients (38.3%) discontinued treatment. Among the 129 patients treated with immunotherapy, (19.4%) discontinued treatment due to intolerance, 19 (14.7%) due to recurrence during adjuvant therapy, and 4 (3.1%) due to other reasons (patient preference, competing comorbidities). Among the 20 patients treated with dabrafenib with trametinib, 4 (20.0%) discontinued treatment due to intolerance, including 1 patient who was subsequently treated with adjuvant nivolumab. Dose reductions were not routinely prescribed.

Recurrence-Free Survival

Overall, the recurrence-free survival (RFS) rates for the entire group were 79% at 1 year and 62% at 2 years, as shown by the Kaplan-Meier curve (Fig. 1A). The median RFS was 38.4 months in the BRAF-mutant group and not reached in the BRAF-wild type group. There was no statistically significant difference between these 2 groups (Fig. 1B).

The recurrence rates were similar between patients treated with anti-PD-1 immuno-therapy and BRAF-targeted therapy (30.0% vs. 31.6%, respectively, Fig. 1C). However, in this retrospective study design, the significant difference in patient numbers between adjuvant ICI and targeted therapy limited direct comparison between these groups for recurrences. Finally, immunotherapy seemed equally effective in improving recurrence-free survival (RFS), regardless of the BRAF status, as shown in Fig. 1D.

Patterns of Disease Recurrence

With a median follow-up of 22.4 months (range: 0.0-43.5), 102 (68.5%) patients were alive and recurrence-free. However, 47 patients (31.5%) experienced disease recurrence. Among them, 22 (14.8%) had locoregional recurrence, 16 (10.7%) had distant recurrence, and 9 (6.0%) had both locoregional and distant recurrence (Table 3; Supplementary Table S1). Nineteen patients (12.8%) experienced recurrence during adjuvant treatment, and 28 patients (18.8%) experienced recurrence after completing treatment.

The sites of first recurrence involvement were as follows: cutaneous or subcutaneous in 19 patients (40.4%), distant lymph nodes in 18 (38.3%), lungs in 13 (27.7%), brain in 4 (8.5%), liver in 3 (6.4%), adrenal glands in 1 (2.1%), and other locations in 6 (12.8%). Among the 3 patients with mucosal melanoma, 2 experienced recurrences, with both cases showing distant metastases.

Patterns of Recurrence According to Initial Versus Recurrent Melanoma

Among the 103 patients who received adjuvant systemic therapy at the initial melanoma diagnosis, 28 out of 103 (27.1%) experienced recurrence. Among these 28 patients, 13 (12.6%) had locoregional recurrence, 10 (9.7%) had distant recurrence, and 5 (4.9%) had both locoregional and distant recurrence.

In contrast, among the 46 patients who received adjuvant systemic therapy after resection of relapsed disease, 19 out of 46 (41.3%) experienced recurrence. Among these 19 patients, 9 (19.6%) had locoregional recurrence, 6 (13.0%) had distant recurrence, and 4 (8.7%) had both locoregional and distant recurrence.

Patterns of Recurrence According to BRAF Mutation Status

Out of the 74 patients with BRAF V600E or V600K mutation, 20 received adjuvant dabrafenib plus trametinib, while 54 received anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. There were no significant differences observed in the patterns of recurrence between these 2 treatment groups.

Among the 20 patients treated with dabrafenib plus trametinib, 6 (30.0%) experienced recurrence, with 2 (10.0%) having locoregional recurrence, 3 (15.0%) having distant recurrence, and 1 (5.0%) having both locoregional and distant recurrence.

Among the 54 patients with BRAF V600E or V600K mutation treated with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, 18 (33.3%) experienced recurrence, with 9 (16.7%) having locoregional recurrence, 5 (9.3%) having distant recurrence, and 4 (7.4%) having both locoregional and distant recurrence.

Interestingly, the timing of recurrences appeared to be different between the 2 treatment groups. In patients who received BRAF-targeted therapy, relapse was observed only after treatment completion or discontinuation. In contrast, in patients who received anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, recurrences were observed both during and after treatment completion (Table 3).

Grade 3 or Higher Adverse Events and Long-Term Toxicity

At the time of this analysis, a total of 71 (47.6%) patients had experienced adverse events of clinical significance. Among these patients, 15 (10.3%) experienced events that caused treatment delays, and 30 (20.5%) experienced events that led to treatment discontinuation. Of note, there was 1 death potentially related to adrenal failure secondary to immunotherapy.

Among the 20 patients treated with dabrafenib plus trametinib, 6 (31.6%) developed pyrexia (fever) and pyrexia-related symptoms that were clinically significant. As a result, 4 of these patients (25%) had to discontinue their treatments.

Of the 129 patients treated with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, 62 (48.0%) experienced some immune-related adverse events of clinical significance. Out of these 62 patients, 26 (20.5%) had to discontinue their treatments. The causes for treatment discontinuation included colitis in 4 patients, pneumonitis in 3, hepatitis in 2 patients, hemolytic anemia in 2 patients, pancreatitis in 1 patient, and thrombocytopenia in 1 patient, among other reasons (refer to Table 4 for more details). Note that the information provided here is based on the available data at the time of the analysis.

Among patients treated with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, 48 (37.2%) experienced unresolved immune-related adverse events. The most common side effect was thyroid dysfunction, which occurred in 17 (13.2%) patients. This was followed by inflammatory arthritis in 7 (5.4%) patients, hypophysitis or adrenal insufficiency in 6 (4.7%) patients, and insulin-dependent diabetes in 2 (1.6%) patients. It is worth noting that a significant number of patients developed more than 1 irAE, and all endocrine toxicities were reported as unresolved at the time of analysis.

Additionally, 6 (4.7%) patients experienced late emergent irAE, which refers to immune-related adverse events that occur more than 100 days after the last dose of immunotherapy. Among 5 patients who had completed their course of treatments, 1 (0.8%) developed hepatitis at 7 months, 2 (1.6%) developed colitis at 6 and 7 months, respectively, and 2 (1.6%) developed inflammatory arthritis at 7 months after their last treatment. Another patient received only 8 doses of nivolumab due to worsening kidney injury and developed hypophysitis 7 months later. It is important to note that all of these individuals were alive at the time of analysis, and only 1 patient experienced a recurrence of the disease.

Of the 84 patients who were alive and without disease recurrence, 22 (26.1%) patients reported unresolved irAE. The most common irAE in this group were thyroid dysfunction in 12 (14.3%) patients, chronic hypopituitarism in 3 (7.1%) patients, inflammatory arthritis in 3 (3.6%) patients, and insulin-dependent diabetes in 1 (1.2%) patient. It is noteworthy that 1 of the patients with arthritis developed concurrent immune-related polyneuropathy.

First Line Therapy After Melanoma Recurrence

After melanoma recurrence, various therapies were administered to the patients, including surgery, radiotherapy, and systemic therapies. Among the 47 patients who experienced recurrence, systemic therapy with ipilimumab plus nivolumab was administered to 21 (44.7%) patients, followed by nivolumab in 18 (38.3%) patients, and ipilimumab in 2 (4.3%) patients. Additionally, 18 (38.3%) patients with localized recurrences underwent surgery as the first treatment option, and 13 (27.7%) patients received radiotherapy.

Melanoma With BRAF V600E or V600K Mutations

In the case of melanoma with BRAF V600E or V600K mutations, out of the 47 patients who experienced recurrence, 24 (51.0%) had these specific mutations. Among the 6 patients who relapsed after adjuvant dabrafenib plus trametinib, 3 received immunotherapy with ipilimumab plus nivolumab. Two of these patients developed irAE (colitis and hepatitis) after the first cycle of ipilimumab plus nivolumab. Unfortunately, 1 patient experienced disease progression and passed away shortly after switching to palliative dabrafenib with trametinib. At the time of analysis, the remaining 5 patients were alive.

Among the 18 patients with BRAF V600E or V600K mutant melanoma who relapsed during or after adjuvant therapy with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, 6 received ipilimumab plus nivolumab, and 3 of them were alive. Six patients received BRAF-targeted therapy, including 1 patient treated with vemurafenib and cobimetinib, and 4 were alive. Additionally, 3 patients underwent surgical treatments and were alive, while 1 patient received radiotherapy only and passed away. Overall, at the time of this analysis, 7 patients had died after disease relapse, 10 remained alive, and 1 patient lost follow-up.

Melanoma Without BRAF Mutation

Among the 23 melanoma patients without BRAF mutation who experienced relapse, only one-third of them had completed the full courses of adjuvant anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. Subsequently, in the first-line setting for relapsed melanoma,12 patients received ipilimumab plus nivolumab, 2 received ipilimumab alone, and another 2 nivolumab as a single agent. Unfortunately, 5 patients had died, with 4 deaths attributed to disease recurrence and 1 due to treatment intolerance. Notably, the overall response rates (ORR) were remarkably high, reaching 43.7% for these patients (Fig. 2).

Overall Survival

In the present analysis, there were 129 patients who were still alive. Among them, 101 patients had no evidence of disease recurrence, while 28 patients were living with recurrent or active melanoma. Unfortunately, 17 patients (36%) had passed away.

Among the deceased patients, 4 individuals had symptomatic brain metastasis as the first site of relapse, and additional 4 of them subsequently developed brain metastasis, which became the main cause of death. One patient’s death was unrelated to their treatment or cancer diagnosis and was attributed to comorbidity.

Discussion

Our study presents our early clinical experience with adjuvant systemic therapies for resected stages III and IV melanoma. We aimed to report on the outcomes and key findings observed in a real-world setting. The results shed light on several important aspects of adjuvant treatment in melanoma.

Treatment delivery was generally adequate, with a median treatment duration of 11 months. However, the rate of treatment discontinuation in our cohort was higher compared to what has been reported in clinical trials. This higher discontinuation rate was particularly notable for targeted therapy with dabrafenib plus trametinib, which is known to have higher rates of treatment-related adverse events (Table 5).

Interestingly, we observed a significant preference for immunotherapy over targeted therapy in our cohort. The majority of patients received anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, likely influenced by provider choices based on their experience with immunotherapy in the advanced setting. However, further investigation is needed to fully understand the reasons behind this preference and explore any other factors that may have influenced treatment decisions.

The efficacy of adjuvant therapies in our cohort aligns with the reported data from pivotal trials, although direct comparisons are challenging due to differences in patient selection and BRAF mutation status. The RFS rates we observed were comparable to those reported in clinical trials for adjuvant nivolumab and pembrolizumab.

We also observed long-term toxicity and immune-related adverse events in living, recurrence-free patients. Chronic inflammatory arthritis emerged as a significant condition causing debilitating symptoms in patients after adjuvant immunotherapy, highlighting the need for further research on the long-term toxicity of immunotherapy and its impact on patients’ quality of life.

Regarding the management of recurrence, our study found high rates of early relapse in the first several months of adjuvant treatment initiation, particularly among patients treated with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI). There are fundamental differences in the mechanisms of action between ICI and BRAF/ MEK inhibitors that may contribute to these findings. Still, most importantly, this emphasizes the importance of implementing effective surveillance strategies during this critical period. Additionally, the detection and management of brain metastases, including asymptomatic ones, require further investigation.

We acknowledge several limitations of our study, including its retrospective nature, lack of standardized treatment protocols, incomplete data on previous treatment for a subgroup of patients, and limited sample size. These limitations should be considered when interpreting the findings, and larger studies are needed for further validation.

Recent advances in therapeutic strategies have significantly improved melanoma outcomes and led to the approval of new adjuvant systemic therapies. Our real-world data reflects the results from the pivotal clinical trials, demonstrating the efficacy of adjuvant therapies in resected stages III and IV melanoma.

It is worth noting the impact of recent neoadjuvant therapy data in this field. Neoadjuvant therapies have shown promising results in terms of pathologic response rates and event-free survival. These findings suggest that neoadjuvant treatment may play a role in shaping adjuvant treatment decisions in the future. Integrating neoadjuvant therapy data into our understanding of adjuvant treatment strategies is an important area for further research, as it can help optimize treatment approaches and improve patient outcomes.

Moreover, our study highlights the need to better understand and manage chronic immuno-related conditions that may arise as a result of adjuvant immunotherapy. These conditions can have a significant impact on patients’ quality of life, emphasizing the importance of long-term follow-up and supportive care.

Conclusion

The results of this study contribute to the growing body of evidence supporting the use of adjuvant systemic therapies in melanoma. The results underscore the need for ongoing research to optimize treatment strategies, minimize long-term toxicities, and improve patient outcomes. By better understanding the implications of adjuvant therapy and addressing the challenges associated with chronic immuno-related conditions, we can continue to enhance the quality of care for patients with melanoma.
