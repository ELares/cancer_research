# Cadonilimab combined with taxane and cisplatin as the first-line treatment of advanced esophageal squamous cell carcinoma: an open-label, multicenter phase II trial.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Cadonilimab, a bispecific antibody simultaneously targeting programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4, may further boost antitumor activity compared with PD-1 or programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) inhibitors. Here, we evaluated the safety and efficacy of cadonilimab combined with chemotherapy as the first-line treatment in advanced esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC).

**METHODS**: Treatment-naïve patients with unresectable locally advanced or metastatic ESCC were eligible. Cadonilimab combined with paclitaxel or nab-paclitaxel and cisplatin was administrated for up to six cycles, then cadonilimab monotherapy continued as maintenance until progressive disease or unacceptable toxicity, with a maximum of 24 months. The primary endpoint was objective response rate (ORR). Secondary endpoints were progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), disease control rate (DCR) and safety. Plasma cell-free DNA samples were collected before and after treatment and prepared for DNA methylation level sequencing.

**RESULTS**: As of September 27, 2024, 43 patients were enrolled with a median age of 61 years (range 44-75), 39.5% had PD-L1 combined positive score ≥10 and 95.3% had distant metastases. The ORR was 81.4% (95% CI 66.6% to 91.6%) and DCR was 97.7% (95% CI 86.2% to 99.9%). The median PFS was 7.10 months (95% CI 5.68 to 8.48) while OS remained immature. The mean pretreatment cytosine-phosphate-guanine (CpG) site methylation levels of APBA2, EPAS1, TRIM58, ITPKA and LINC00554 were significantly higher in responders than those in non-responders. Grade 3-4 treatment-related adverse events were reported in 53.5% (23/43) patients.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Cadonilimab combined with taxane and cisplatin as first-line treatment revealed encouraging antitumor activity and manageable safety in patients with advanced ESCC. DNA methylation level might be a potential biomarker for guiding patient outcomes.

**TRIAL REGISTRATION NUMBER**: NCT05522894.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) or PD-ligand 1 inhibitor plus chemotherapy has already become the standard first-line treatment for patients with advanced esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC). Durable responses were observed only in a portion of patients, while treatment outcomes remain to be improved.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

In the present phase II study, cadonilimab, a bispecific antibody binding PD-1 and cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 simultaneously, plus taxane and cisplatin as first-line treatment revealed encouraging antitumor activity and manageable safety in patients with advanced ESCC. Our findings suggested a greater antitumor immune regulatory effect with the novel combination.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Cadonilimab combined with chemotherapy might become a novel first-line treatment for patients with ESCC.

Background

 Esophageal cancer is among the most common cancers globally. It ranks as the 11th most prevalent neoplasm worldwide, and represents the 6th most prevalent malignancy and the 4th leading cause of cancer-related deaths in China. As the predominant histologic subtype in Asia, esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (ESCC) constitutes 85% of the cases and is characterized by its aggressive progression and dismal prognosis, with 5-year survival rates stagnating at 15–25% despite therapeutic advances. Due to advancements in immunotherapy in recent years, programmed cell death 1 (PD-1)/programmed cell death ligand 1 (PD-L1) inhibitor in combination with chemotherapy has emerged as the standard first-line treatment for advanced ESCC, yielding objective response rates (ORRs) of approximately 40–60% and median overall survival durations of 12.6–15.3 months. However, a certain portion of patients with ESCC could not benefit from the synergistic effect of PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor and chemotherapy, urging the need for the development of novel antitumor strategies.

Cadonilimab, a first-in-class bispecific antibody, is characterized with crystallizable fragment (Fc)-null design targeting both PD-1 and cytotoxic T lymphocyte antigen 4 (CTLA-4). Among published studies of cadonilimab, only the phase Ib/II COMPASSION-03 study enrolled patients with esophageal cancer, in which 9% (22/240) of enrollment was attributed to ESCC. In those patients, cadonilimab monotherapy demonstrated an encouraging efficacy with an ORR of 18.2% (95% CI 5.2% to 40.3%). Due to its dual regulation of immune checkpoints with PD-1 and CTLA-4, cadonilimab may perform better than PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor in the combination treatment of patients with ESCC.

The therapeutic effect of cadonilimab plus chemotherapy in treatment-naïve patients with advanced ESCC had not been explored. Therefore, we conducted a multicenter, phase II clinical trial to evaluate the feasibility, safety, and efficacy of cadonilimab coupled with taxane and cisplatin as the first-line treatment for advanced ESCC, in the hope of expanding the treatment landscape in these patients.

Methods

Participants and procedures

This phase II study was conducted in three hospitals in China. Patients who had unresectable locally advanced or metastatic ESCC and were aged 18–75; had an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status (ECOG PS) of 0 or 1; had an expected life expectancy of more than 3 months; with no previous systemic treatment (patients who had progressed >6 months after adjuvant or neoadjuvant chemotherapy or definitive chemoradiotherapy were eligible) and had at least one measurable lesion as per the Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) V.1.1 were enrolled. Patients with ESCC who had previously received immunotherapy; had interstitial lung disease or other pre-existing active autoimmune diseases; and had been treated with corticosteroids (>10 mg/day prednisone or equivalent) or other immunosuppressants within 2 weeks before the first drug administration were excluded. The study was conducted in accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and the Good Clinical Practice guidelines. The registration number on ClinicalTrials.gov was NCT05522894.

Cadonilimab (10 mg/kg intravenous, day 1) was administrated every 21 days until disease progression, intolerable adverse events, investigator decision, or withdrawal of consent, with a maximum of 24 months. While paclitaxel or nanoparticle albumin-bound (nab)-paclitaxel (175 mg/m2 intravenous, day 1) and cisplatin (65–75 mg/m2 intravenous, day 1) were administrated every 21 days for up to six cycles. Dose reductions of cadonilimab were not permitted during the study while dose reductions of chemotherapy were allowed and conducted according to local standard practice.

The PD-L1 expression in tumor samples was examined using the Dako 22C3 immunohistochemistry kit (Agilent Technologies, California, USA) and reported using the combined positive score (CPS). Plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) samples were collected and prepared for sequencing at baseline and after treatment. An ESCC customized next-generation sequencing-based methylation panel was used to measure DNA methylation level. More than 30,000 cytosine-phosphate-guanine (CpG) sites across 3,000 genomic regions with average fragment length of 100 base pairs, located in more than 700 genes, were included in the targeted methyl panel, based on the data mining results from public database of methylation marks associated with cancer risk. An Illumina NovaSeq 6000 sequencer was used to perform 150 base pair paired-end sequencing.

Endpoint

The primary endpoint of the study was the ORR assessed by the investigators, with secondary endpoints consisting of safety, disease control rate (DCR), progression-free survival (PFS) and overall survival (OS). Exploratory endpoints included the correlation between PD-L1 expression and antitumor response. Tumor responses were evaluated by the investigators with computed tomography or magnetic resonance imaging scans according to RECIST V.1.1. PFS was defined as the time from the initiation of treatment to disease progression or death from any cause, whichever occurred first. OS was defined as the time from the initiation of treatment to death from any cause. Safety analysis was performed in all patients who received at least one cycle of the study drug medication. Adverse events were graded according to the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events V.5.0.

Statistical analysis

With the ORR as the primary endpoint, the sample size was calculated based on the assumed ORR of 65% with cadonilimab and chemotherapy in this study. The history control value was 45% from the previous reports of the efficacy of chemotherapy in the first-line setting for advanced ESCC. The significance level was set at one-sided 0.05, with a power of 80%. The calculated sample size was 38. Considering a dropout rate of 10%, the final sample size was 43.

ORR and DCR analyses were performed based on the full analysis set, with 95% accurate two-sided confidence intervals (CI) estimated using the Clopper Pearson method. The Kaplan-Meier method was used to estimate OS and PFS. Log-rank tests were used for the comparison. Statistical analyses were performed using the software SAS V.9.4 and GraphPad Prism V.8.0.2. All significance tests were two-tailed, and a p value<0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Results

A total of 43 patients with ESCC with a median age of 61 years (44–75 years) were enrolled in this phase II study from February 2023 to April 2024 (figure 1). 35 (81.4%) of patients were male and 51.2% had PD-L1 CPS<10. Distant metastasis was confirmed in 41 patients (95.4%) and the specific clinicopathological characteristics of patients are shown in table 1. As of data cut-off (September 27, 2024), the median follow-up period was 12.88 months (95% CI 10.02 to 13.60) and six patients (14.0%) were still receiving treatment. All patients received at least two cycles of treatment and nab-paclitaxel was applied in 39 (90.7%) patients. The median cycles of taxanes and cisplatin administration were both 6, while the median cycles for cadonilimab administration were 7. The primary reason for treatment discontinuation was disease progression (58.1%).

All patients received at least one tumor response assessment. The ORR was 81.4% (95% CI 66.6% to 91.6%) and the DCR was 97.7% (95% CI 86.2% to 99.9%). The median PFS (mPFS) was 7.10 months (95% CI 5.68 to 8.48) while the median OS was immature (figure 2). In patients with PD-L1 CPS≥10, the ORR was 100.0% (95% CI 80.5% to 100.0%) and mPFS was 7.10 months (95% CI 4.44 to not estimable). In patients with the PD-L1 CPS<10 group, the ORR was 77.3% (95% CI 54.6% to 92.2%) and mPFS was 7.26 months (95% CI 5.29 to 8.71). In addition, the ORR was 87.5% (95% CI 47.4% to 99.7%) in the eight patients with PD-L1 CPS<1, and mPFS was 8.41 months (95% CI 4.90 to not estimable) (figure 3). In the four patients receiving paclitaxel treatment, the ORR was 100% (95% CI 39.6% to 100%).

Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) of any grades occurred in all patients (table 2). 23 (53.5%) patients experienced grade 3–4 TRAEs, which mainly included neutropenia (25.6%), leukopenia (9.3%) and hyponatremia (7.0%). Immune-related AEs (irAEs) were observed in 19 patients (44.2%), and among them, 5 (11.6%) patients experienced grade 3–4 irAEs. The median time for the onset of irAEs was 2.33 months (range: 0.17–10.00) for patients with grade 3–4. All five patients with grade 3–4 irAEs received moderate to high doses of corticosteroids and three of them terminated cadonilimab treatment after the irAEs developed. Five patients (11.6%) suffered from serious AEs, and no grade 5 TRAE (treatment-related death) was observed. TRAEs led to discontinuation of cadonilimab in 7 (16.3%) of enrolled patients. In the four patients receiving paclitaxel treatment, a 75.0% incidence of grade 3–4 TRAEs was observed.

Pretreatment and post-treatment plasma were available in 36 and 30 patients respectively for the analysis of DNA methylation. A 5-CpG signature (APBA2, EPAS1, TRIM58, ITPKA, LINC00554) was identified from the methylation profiling, and the baseline hypermethylation status of the 5-CpG panel robustly predicts responders (complete/partial response) from non-responders (stable/progressive disease) (p<0.001) (figure 4). A threshold of 35% methylation was set to stratify therapeutic efficacy. All 19 patients with baseline methylation levels <35% achieved PR, whereas only 35.3% (6/17) of those above this threshold responded.

Discussion

To our knowledge, this is the first trial to evaluate treatment outcomes of cadonilimab and chemotherapy in patients with advanced ESCC, and the trial had met its primary endpoint. The addition of the bi-specific antibody targeting PD-1 and CTLA-4 to chemotherapy resulted in an encouraging ORR of 81.4%, higher than the preset efficacy margin of 65%. The efficacy of PD-1 inhibitors plus chemotherapy as the first-line treatment for advanced ESCC had been reported in several phase III studies. PD-1 antibody in combination with paclitaxel and cisplatin was found to improve PFS and OS in advanced ESCC with an ORR of 69.3–72.1% based on ESCORT-1st and JUPITER 06. Our trial showed a numerically superior ORR of 81.4%, and the improved efficacy may be attributed to the advantages of dual blockade immunotherapy. The blockade of both PD-1 and CTLA-4 strengthens the tumor immune response and achieves an effect more than merely additive. As with mismatch-repair-deficient (dMMR)/microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) metastatic colorectal cancer, it has been shown that dual checkpoint inhibition with nivolumab and ipilimumab significantly improved patient survival compared with nivolumab alone. Similar results might be anticipated in patients with ESCC. However, better response observed in our trial might also be attributed to the enrolled patients with better ECOG score and lower tumor burden compared with other phase III studies, as the proportion of patients with ECOG 0 in our trial was approximately 10% higher than those in ESCORT-1st and JUPITER 06 trials. Therefore, cross-trial comparisons should be interpreted with caution.

Except for the bispecific antibody, the choice of chemotherapy regimen may also affect treatment outcomes. The standard first-line chemotherapy to accompany PD-1 inhibitors for ESCC consists of cisplatin with either 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) or paclitaxel. Nab-paclitaxel was proved to be safe and effective as both neoadjuvant and subsequent-line treatment in patients with ESCC. Due to the immunosuppressive effect of corticosteroids, simultaneous administration of steroids with immunotherapy might have a negative impact on treatment efficacy. Premedication with steroids is mandatory for solvent-based paclitaxel but is not required for nab-paclitaxel; therefore, nab-paclitaxel could be a better companion for immunotherapy. Thus, both of these two different taxane formulations were allowed based on regional preferences or the investigators’ discretion. Indeed, in the recent ESCORT-NEO study, patients with locally advanced ESCC achieved a pathological complete response rate of 28.0% when treated with camrelizumab and nab-paclitaxel, compared with 15.4% with camrelizumab and solvent-based paclitaxel. These findings, and the fact in our present study that most patients received nab-paclitaxel supported its use in the first-line setting for patients with ESCC.

Our investigation found cadonilimab plus chemotherapy to be well tolerated, and no unexpected safety signal was observed. The combination of cadonilimab and chemotherapy had a 53.5% incidence of grade 3 or 4 TRAEs, which is comparable to other anti-PD-1/L1 antibody and chemotherapy combinations in the first-line setting for ESCC, with incidences between 32% and 72%. Likewise, the phase Ib/II COMPASSION-04 and phase III COMPASSION-15 studies found 69.1% and 65.9% grade 3 or higher TRAEs for cadonilimab plus chemotherapy in gastric cancer, demonstrating a safety profile comparable to other anti-PD-1/L1 combo treatments. Patients in our trial had similar rates of bone marrow suppression and infusion-related complications as in earlier studies with chemotherapy and cadonilimab. Moreover, 11.6% patients experienced grade 3 or 4 irAEs, which is also numerically close to the reported data of 7–9.4% in those previous phase III studies in patients with ESCC receiving anti-PD-1/L1 antibody and chemotherapy.

PD-L1 expression had emerged as a useful biomarker to aid patient selection for immunotherapy in the treatment of advanced ESCC. Recently, the US Food and Drug Administration’s Oncologic Drugs Advisory Committee voted against the use of PD-1 inhibitor in patients with ESCC with PD-L1 CPS<1, based on exploratory analyses of individual phase III trials. In our present study, the combination therapy showed promising ORR and PFS for patients with CPS<1 and CPS<10, with similar outcomes to those with CPS≥10. Cadonilimab plus chemotherapy could be a new treatment option in the first-line treatment for advanced ESCC, especially in patients with low-PD-L1 expression. While given the limited number of patients and subsequent wide CIs, further investigation in randomized trials is warranted for robust conclusions.

DNA methylation is a key epigenetic modulator of immunotherapy response. Results from our trial also demonstrated the potential predictive value of DNA methylation in esophageal cancer, notably in identifying interpatient heterogeneity. Genome-wide methylation patterns applied in our study to find dynamic epigenetic cues could predict the efficacy of the dual blockage of PD-1 and CTLA-4 combined with chemotherapy. A unique five-gene methylation panel (APBA2, EPAS1, TRIM58, ITPKA, LINC00554) proved to stratify patients into discrete response groups based on hypermethylation/hypomethylation status. Hypermethylation of this 5-CpG panel may correlate to intrinsic treatment resistance against chemoimmunotherapy. As the hypermethylation of EPAS1 was demonstrated to drive immunosuppressive microenvironments via vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)-mediated T-cell exclusion, TRIM58 silencing led by hypermethylation could impair antigen presentation. Intriguingly, post-treatment cfDNA methylation yielded divergent dynamics among patients with various treatment response. Global hypomethylation was seen in non-responders, while responders showed stabilized methylation status. These findings position the methylation of ctDNA not merely as a static biomarker but as a dynamic orchestrator of tumor-immune interactions. With further experimental validation, the overall methylation level of ctDNA may be implicated for real-time monitoring. Further investigations are imperative, bridging epigenetics with actionable therapeutic strategies in ESCC.

Limitations of the present study included the small sample size, a relatively short follow-up period, and the single-arm design. The OS was not yet mature, and whether the encouraging ORR result could turn into survival benefits was still unclear. Besides, the two different taxane types adopted in the trial (nab-paclitaxel and solvent-based paclitaxel) may alter the tumor microenvironment differently, thereby creating extra bias. Meanwhile, most of the patients enrolled in this study had good ECOG PS with relatively low visceral involvements. A large-scale randomized trial is needed to further assert the efficacy of cadonilmab plus chemotherapy compared with the current standard of care as first-line treatment for advanced ESCC.

In conclusion, cadonilimab and chemotherapy showed encouraging antitumor activity and manageable safety in patients with advanced ESCC regardless of PD-L1 expression. DNA methylation level might be a potential biomarker for the tumor response.
