# GD2 in Breast Cancer: A Potential Biomarker and Therapeutic Target.

## Abstract

Expression of disialoganglioside GD2 in normal tissues is primarily limited to the central nervous system, peripheral sensory nerve fibers, dermal melanocytes, lymphocytes, and mesenchymal stem cells. Its widespread overexpression in various cancer types allows it to be classified as a tumor-associated antigen with potential diagnostic and therapeutic implications. This article reviews the synthesis pathways of GD2 and its role in cancer cell adhesion, proliferation, and metastasis with a focus on breast cancer. GD2 appears to be overexpressed on the outer membrane of most breast cancer cells and breast cancer stem cells (BCSCs) and is closely linked to epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT). GD3 synthase (GD3S) is considered to be the rate-determining step in GD2 synthesis. Clinical studies indicate that GD2 expression is increased in 35-70% of breast cancer samples, with higher levels in triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC). This overexpression correlates with more aggressive tumor features and worse prognosis. Therapeutic targeting of GD2 with monoclonal antibodies (moABs) like dinutuximab and naxitamab has demonstrated anti-cancer activity in preclinical cancer models and human clinical trials against high-risk neuroblastoma reducing tumor growth and enhancing survival. GD2-specific chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy and GD3S inhibition present other promising therapeutic strategies to improve clinical outcomes. Furthermore, GD2-targeted vaccines are currently being investigated in cancer therapy. This narrative review article underscores the critical role of GD2 in breast cancer pathogenesis and highlights the promising therapeutic opportunities it offers. It advocates for the initiation of clinical trials to further explore the potential of GD2-targeted treatment in combination with standard breast cancer therapies.

GD2 in Breast Cancer: A Potential Biomarker and Therapeutic Target

Disialogangliosides (GDs) are b-series gangliosides containing two sialic acid residues per molecule. Predominantly found in the central nervous system (CNS), peripheral nerve tissue, and lymphocytes, GD3 and GD2 are considered tumor-associated antigens, with elevated expressions in cancers of neuroectodermal origin. On the cell surface, GD2 is involved in cell adhesion and signal transduction, playing key roles in embryogenesis and carcinogenesis, including proliferation, invasion, neoangiogenesis, and immune evasion. Furthermore, GD2 has been demonstrated to be a more specific marker of breast cancer stem cells (BCSCs) than CD44(high)/CD24(low) markers that have widespread expression in normal tissues rendering them unsuitable targets for therapeutic interventions. However, in breast cancer models, GD2 expression was found to correlate with CD44 expression and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) state suggesting an important role in BCSCs.

Figure 1 demonstrates the multi-step process of GD2 synthesis. The B4GalT5 gene encodes an enzyme that catalyzes the synthesis of lactosylceramide (LacCer) by transferring galactose from UDP-galactose to glucosylceramide (GlcCer). LacCer is the starting point in the biosynthesis of all gangliosides. B4GALNT1 encodes the key enzyme that generates gangliosides GM2/GD2.

However, the rate-limiting enzyme in GD2 biosynthesis is GD3 synthase, encoded by the ST8SIA1 gene. Therefore, these three genes (B4GalT5, B4GALNT1 and ST8SIA1) contribute significantly to the biosynthesis of GD2 ganglioside with ST8SIA1 being the rate limiting determinant. GD3 synthase plays a critical role in EMT and cancer stemness and was reported to be associated with more aggressive cancer phenotypes and poor patient outcomes. GD2’s role in promoting mammary carcinogenesis appears to involve the c-Met and mTOR signaling pathways.

This narrative review explores the critical role of the disialoganglioside GD2 in breast cancer pathogenesis and stem cells and its promising role as a valid biomarker and potential therapeutic target.

GD2 Expression in Breast Cancer

Several studies have investigated the GD2 enzyme expression in breast cancer cell lines and human breast cancer using immunohistochemistry (IHC) and immunofluorescence (IF) on tissue microarrays.

Battula and colleagues reported that most basal breast cancer cell lines displayed a higher percentage of GD2+ cells compared to luminal-derived breast cancer cell lines. Furthermore, GD2+ cells appeared mesenchymal, and GD3S expression was induced during EMT, suggesting that the GD2/GD3S pathway could be a potential biomarker for BCSCs and EMT state, as well as a potential therapeutic target.

Erber et al. investigated GD2 expression in a cohort of breast cancer patients using IHC and IF on tissue microarrays and assessed its impact on survival. The authors analyzed GD2 expression in 568 samples by IHC and 503 samples by IF, correlating the results with cancer subtypes and patient outcomes. They found that 50.2% of the IHC-assessed samples and 69.8% of the IF-assessed samples were GD2-positive. Contrary to other studies, the authors found the highest proportion of GD2-positive tumors in luminal subtypes. Additionally, they reported that all adjacent noncancerous tissue samples (n=130) were either negative (77%) or only focally positive (23%) suggesting that GD2 is a specific biomarker of malignancy. However, the authors found no significant associations between GD2 expression and clinical outcome in terms of disease-free survival (DFS) and overall survival (OS).

Orsi and colleagues used semi-quantitative IHC to analyze GD2 expression in 63 breast cancer samples and reported GD2 positivity in 59% of patients. The univariate regression logistic analysis revealed a significant association between GD2 expression and triple-receptor negative phenotype and older age (>78) at diagnosis.

In another study focused on TNBC (n=76), Higashi et al. reported increased GD2 expression in 45% of TNBC patients. The authors observed no significant association between GD2 expression and clinicopathological parameters. However, the 5-year recurrence free survival (RFS) rate among patients with GD2-positive TNBC was significantly worse than that among patients with GD2-negative TNBC samples (75.4% and 94.9%; HR=4.931; 95%CI=1.024-23.752; p=0.027). The OS in patients with GD2-positive disease tended to be inferior to that of patients with GD2-negative tumors (HR=5.357; 95%CI=0.599-47.939; p=0.092). Interestingly, in patients with GD2-positive TNBC, a higher degree of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) was associated with a superior OS (TILs-high vs. TILs-low; p=0.04).

In 386 breast carcinomas, GD2 expression was assessed using anti-GD2 3F8 moAB IHC and correlated with clinicopathologic features and survival outcomes. GD2 was positive in 134 (35%) cases, and the staining was granular and mainly cytoplasmic or perinuclear, confined to neoplastic tissue. GD2 positivity was significantly associated with tumor histologic type, low grade, estrogen receptor positivity, low stage, and multifocality. RFS and OS were not significantly different between GD2-positive and GD2-negative tumors. The findings of this study are similar to those reported by Erber et al. in terms of association with the luminal phenotype and lack of association with clinical outcome.

Ly et al. used IHC to study GD2 expression in 89 frozen samples of TNBC. The authors reported that GD2 was expressed in approximately 60% of primary TNBC tumors at varying levels and was associated with worse OS (p=0.002). GD2 expression was observed in both tumors and stroma, while adjacent normal ducts and lobules exhibited low or no GD2 staining, providing further evidence that GD2 is a potential novel biomarker for the tumor and its microenvironment. Treatment with dinutuximab, a moAB targeting GD2 and approved for the treatment of high-risk neuroblastoma (NB), significantly reduced adhesion and migration of MDA-MB-231 and SUM159 TNBC cells and inhibited mTOR signaling, which is regulated by GD2 in BCSCs. Additionally, dinutuximab decreased tumor growth in nude mice bearing TNBC cell-line xenografts. In combination with activated natural killer (NK) cells, dinutuximab inhibited tumor growth in a TNBC PDX model and improved OS in tumor-bearing mice.

In another IHC study of 168 primary breast carcinomas, Mansoori et al. reported that higher GD2 expression was associated with advanced histological grade (p=0.02), lymph node invasion (p=0.04), larger tumor size (p=0.04), and older age (p=0.04).

In summary, with a total of 1,350 human breast cancer samples, the six studies (Table I) described above demonstrated that GD2 was present in most cases of breast cancer, with rare expression in histologically normal breast tissues supporting the notion that GD2 is a tumor associated antigen. Furthermore, most studies revealed a positive association between GD2 expression and TNBC molecular subtype which is recognized to contain a relatively high proportion of BCSCs. Nevertheless, the studies reported conflicting results regarding the association between GD2 and prognostic parameters and clinical outcomes.

Targeting the GD2 System

There is a growing body of evidence that GD2 plays a significant role in mammary carcinogenesis and its progression. Furthermore, it appears to be a specific marker for BCSCs and EMT which are known to mediate distant metastases, treatment resistance and relapse. Therefore, targeting the GD2 system presents a promising novel therapeutic strategy. When combined with other drugs, this approach could enhance breast cancer treatment, overcome drug resistance, and reduce the risk of relapse in the future. Figure 2 illustrates the potential therapeutic strategies targeting GD2.

Monoclonal antibodies against GD2 ganglioside. Dinutuximab (Unituxin; ch14.18/SP2.0) is a chimeric moAB targeting GD2 which is overexpressed on the outer membrane of neuroblastoma tumor cells. It is an IgG1 human/mouse chimeric variant of the murine moAB 14G2a that can bind to GD2 and induce antibody-dependent and complement-dependent cytotoxicity. Dinutuximab beta refers to the chimeric variant ch14.18/CHO.

Dinutuximab is manufactured using mouse cells, while dinutuximab beta is manufactured using hamster cells. The United States Food and Drug Administration (US FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) approved dinutuximab combination therapy for treating high-risk NB in pediatric patients. The drug has been shown to improved survival of patients with high-risk NB in a phase 3 randomized clinical trial. Immunotherapy with dinutuximab beta is commonly associated with manageable adverse events, such as neuropathic pain, fever, infections, hypersensitivity reactions, and capillary leak syndrome. Other adverse effects include transaminitis [elevated alanine transaminase (ALT) 23%; elevated aspartate transaminase (AST); 10%], gastrointestinal side effects (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea; 22%), hypotension (18%), hypoxia (13%), and urticaria (13%). Despite the widespread expression of GD2 in the central and peripheral nervous systems, no significant incidence of grade 3 or 4 neurological toxicity has been reported.

In the context of breast cancer, dinutuximab was investigated using cell lines and animal models where it significantly decreased adhesion and migration of MDA-MB-231 and SUM159 TNBC cells, inhibited mTOR signaling regulated by GD2 in BCSCs, and reduced tumor growth in nude mice bearing TNBC xenografts. Additionally, combining dinutuximab with activated NK cells inhibited tumor growth in a TNBC PDX model and improved OS of tumor-bearing mice.

More recently, the FDA approved naxitamab, a humanized moAB targeting GD-2 (Danyelza®, Y-mAbs Therapeutics, Inc.) for the treatment of high-risk refractory or relapsed NB in bone marrow and/or bone in combination with granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF). Dinutuximab and dinutuximab beta are administered for ≥8 hours (and up to 10 days for dinutuximab beta), whereas naxitamab intravenous infusion is administered over 30-60 minutes in the outpatient setting. The higher affinity and shorter infusion time of naxitamab make it more attractive than dinutuximab for clinical trial investigations. The addition of GM-CSF has been shown to improve the anti-GD2 moAB (i.e., dinutuximab, dinutuximab beta or naxitamab) response in high-risk NB by enhancing antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity.

Ongoing research is exploring the potential of antigen-specific targeting with anti-GD2 mAbs to deliver toxic agents, radioactive compounds and drugs directly into the tumor microenvironment (TME), potentially improving the treatment of solid tumors. Furthermore, the therapeutic potential of bispecific antibodies (e.g., GD2/B7-H3, GD2/Her2, GD2/PD-1) is the focus of ongoing research. Moreover, O-acetylated gangliosides (OACs) which are exclusively expressed in cancer tissues are emerging as promising therapeutic targets, offering an alternative strategy to non-O-acetylated gangliosides with a greater efficacy. It should be noted however that there have been no clinical trials investigating anti-GD2 moABs in patients with breast cancer.

GD2 chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T-cell therapy. Chimeric antigen receptors (CARs) are engineered receptors that provide immune effector cells, primarily T cells, with specific targeting capabilities. Consequently, CAR-T cells can effectively eliminate tumor cells with a designated target. This therapeutic strategy has demonstrated improvement in clinical outcomes in patients with B cell hematological malignancies.

Bocca and colleagues investigated the anti-NB activity of GD2-CAR T cells combined with the angiogenesis inhibitor bevacizumab (BEV) in an orthotopic xenograft model of human NB. Two weeks after tumor implantation, mice received BEV, GD2-CAR T cells, or both via single intravenous administration. GD2-CAR T cells showed significant anti-NB activity only when combined with BEV, even at the lowest concentration tested, which alone did not inhibit tumor growth. In combination with BEV, GD2-CAR T cells infiltrated the tumor mass leading to the production of interferon-γ (IFN-γ), which induced CXCL10 expression by NB cells. IFN-γ and possibly other cytokines upregulated PD-L1 expression in NB cells, while GD2-CAR T cells expressed PD-1, suggesting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis can limit the efficacy of the GD2-CAR T cell/BEV combination.

Phase 1/2 human clinical trials have demonstrated promising results for anti-GD2 CAR-T cell therapy in 19 patients with high-risk NB, showing successful in vivo expansion of selected autologous T cells and complete and durable responses in some patients with brain tumors. Louis et al. reported that GD2-CAR T cells could induce complete tumor responses in patients with active NB, and persistence of these cells was associated with longer survival. In another phase 1/2 clinical trial, 27 heavily pretreated patients with NB were treated with anti-GD2 CART01cell therapy. The use of GD2-CART01 was feasible and safe, with treatment-related toxic effects controlled by the activation of a suicide gene with evidence of a sustained antitumor effect. More recently, Lin et al. reported that intravenous GD2-CARTs, both with and without C7R, were well tolerated and patients receiving C7R-GD2 CARTs experienced temporary improvements in neurologic deficits and elevated levels of circulating cytokines and chemokines. This clinical trial involved 11 patients with diffuse midline glioma or recurrent GD2-expressing brain tumors.

Challenges to effective CAR-T cell therapy include severe life-threatening toxicities, modest anti-tumor activity, antigen escape, restricted trafficking, and limited tumor infiltration. In addition, the host and TME interactions with CAR-T cells critically alter CAR-T cell function. Future optimization of CAR design and cell modification protocols should focus on enhancing safety and preventing early cellular depletion. Potential improvements include using humanized single-chain variable fragment (scFv), reducing CAR receptor tonic signaling, employing NK, NKT cells, or macrophages to reduce toxic side effects, and optimizing production protocols for scalable, ready-to-use cells without functional depletion.

Targeting GD3 Synthase

Aligned with GD3S being the rate limiting step of GD2 biosynthesis, the expressions GD3S and GD2 are significantly increased following the induction of EMT in transformed human mammary epithelial cells. GD3S expression has been shown to increase and correlate with poor prognosis in patients with triple-negative breast tumors, which are known to be enriched in BCSCs and mesenchymal cells. In MDA-MB-231 cells, GD3S expression causes GD3 and GD2 to accumulate on the cell surface and promotes a proliferative phenotype under serum-free conditions. Furthermore, GD3S appears to enhance malignancy by activating the c-Met receptor via GD2, triggering the Erk/MAPK and PI3K/Akt signaling pathways. Moreover, increased GD3S, ST8SIA1, and MET expression was observed in the basal-like subtype of breast tumors and osteolytic MDA-MET metastatic breast cancer cells. Using microarray data of 1,269 human breast cancers, GD3S expression was found to be higher in ER-negative tumors and was associated with a worse clinical outcome among patients with this molecular subtype. Therefore, these findings suggest that targeting GD3S could be an effective therapeutic strategy.

NF-ĸB inhibitors, such as triptolide and BMS-345541, have been shown to reduce GD3S expression and inhibit cancer progression. Triptolide, derived from a Chinese medicinal herb, was reported to decrease GD3S mRNA in melanoma cells and induce epithelial-like morphology in breast cancer cells by targeting NF-ĸB binding sites in the GD3S promoter. The small molecule BMS-345541 was demonstrated to decrease GD3S expression, reduce GD2+ cells by >90%, impair BCSC function, and inhibit tumor growth and metastasis suggesting that GD2 expression/function and NFĸB signaling are related. Additionally, combining GD3S knockdown with other treatments, such as gefitinib achieved further suppression of tumor growth.

Recent studies have provided further evidence supporting the role of GD3S in EMT and tumor progression, revealing novel regulatory mechanisms involving long non-coding RNA (lncRNA). For instance, lncRNA MIR4435-2HG, which binds to the ST8SIA1 gene, was observed to activate cancer pathways and its inhibition reduced malignancy. In vivo analyses revealed that GD3S-KO mice had decreased expression of genes linked to cell cycle and growth factors. Additionally, increased GD3S levels in chemo-resistant TNBC patients suggest that targeting GD3S with siRNA/lncRNA could overcome drug resistance.

Moreover, Wan et al. found that chemo-resistant TNBC cells were more sensitive to ST8SIA1 inhibition than chemo-sensitive cells, resulting in decreased growth and viability. Furthermore, ST8SIA1 inhibition enhanced the efficacy of chemotherapy in TNBC cells. Mechanistic studies showed that ST8SIA1 inhibition suppressed the FAK/Akt/mTOR and Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathways.

Vaccines

Bivalent vaccines that include the neuroblastoma-associated antigens GD2 and GD3, combined with the immunologic adjuvant OPT-821, have elicited anti-GD2 and anti-GD3 IgG antibody responses in NB patients and are currently undergoing phase II trials.

Future Directions

The evidence presented above provides compelling support for targeting GD2 as part of breast cancer treatment. There is a need to initiate clinical trials to assess the efficacy of naxitamab in combination with standard care in the neoadjuvant setting of TNBC, as well as its addition to standard adjuvant treatment for ER-positive breast cancer and residual disease after neoadjuvant therapy for TNBC and HER2-positive disease.

A signature combining the mRNA or protein levels of the 3 genes (B4GalT5, B4GALNT1 and ST8SIA1) involved in GD2 and GD3 biosynthesis could prove a powerful prognostic indicator and should be investigated in future studies.

Targeting the GD2 system could potentially eradicate the dormant breast cancer cells in the peripheral circulation (circulating tumor cells) or distant organs (disseminated tumor cells) thus increasing the likelihood of permanent breast cancer cure.

Conclusion

GD2 is a tumor associated antigen that is upregulated in breast cancer cells, BCSCs and closely associated with EMT, contributing to mammary carcinogenesis and its progression. GD3S is the rate-determining enzyme in GD2 biosynthesis.

Clinical studies have demonstrated that GD2 is upregulated in 35-70% of breast cancer samples, with higher levels in TNBC and possible associations with pathological features and clinical outcomes. Therapeutic targeting of GD2 glycolipid with moABs like naxitamab, GD2-specific chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy, GD3S inhibition and GD2-targeted vaccines present promising therapeutic strategies which require further refinement.

The evidence presented in this review is sufficient to advocate for the initiation of clinical trials investigating the potential therapeutic benefits of anti-GD2 moABs such as naxitamab in the treatment of breast cancer.
