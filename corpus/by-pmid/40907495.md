# Epigenetic modulation of BARD1 to enhance anti-VEGF therapy.

## Abstract

Despite the clinical use of anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) antibodies (AVAs) in cancer therapy, resistance frequently develops, leading to disease progression. To address this, we identify a previously unknown role for breast cancer type 1 susceptibility protein (BRCA1)-associated RING domain 1 (BARD1) in modulating AVA sensitivity. Epigenetic modulation-via global and targeted DNA methylation-reveals BARD1 as a key regulator of angiogenesis. Sequential treatment with azacytidine overcomes AVA resistance in vivo. To enable precise epigenetic reactivation, we develop a liposomal CRISPR-deactivated Cas9 (dCas9)-TET1 system guided by BARD1-targeting single-guide RNAs (sgRNAs). This platform achieves CpG-specific demethylation of the BARD1 promoter, restores expression, and enhances AVA response. Additionally, BARD1 restoration, through either dCas9-TET1 or small interfering RNA (siRNA), significantly reduces tumor growth in combination with AVA in ovarian cancer models. These findings uncover a previously unrecognized function of BARD1 in tumor angiogenesis and demonstrate the potential of gene-specific epigenetic targeting to overcome AVA resistance.

Introduction

Anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) antibody (AVA) therapy has demonstrated clinical benefit across various cancers, including ovarian cancer (OC). However, despite initial responses, resistance to both chemotherapy and anti-angiogenesis therapies frequently develops, limiting long-term treatment efficacy. Resistance to AVA therapy—whether innate or acquired—remains a major clinical challenge. Emerging evidence suggests that tumors can evade anti-angiogenic pressure through alternative mechanisms of vascularization such as vessel co-option, whereby tumor cells hijack existing blood vessels instead of promoting new vessel formation. Nonetheless, secondary angiogenesis may still occur in these settings, highlighting the continued importance of understanding and targeting angiogenic pathways.

Bevacizumab is a humanized monoclonal antibody that targets VEGF and is approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA), the European Medicines Agency, and other regulatory bodies worldwide for use in patients with ovarian and several other cancers. Despite its broad activity in both preclinical and clinical settings, a substantial proportion of patients eventually develop resistance. This resistance may arise through diverse mechanisms, including upregulation of pro-angiogenic factors in the tumor microenvironment or the acquisition of mutations and epigenetic alterations that drive progression and invasiveness.

Epigenetic changes (e.g., methylation of gene promoter or gene body) are known to play pivotal roles in tumor development, growth, and resistance to chemotherapy. DNA methylation is regulated by a family of enzymes known as DNA methyltransferases, including DNMT1, DNMT3A, and DNMT3B. While DNMT1 maintains established methylation patterns, DNMT3A and DNMT3B are primarily responsible for de novo methylation. DNMT2, in contrast, functions as an RNA methyltransferase. Clinical studies have the potential of hypomethylating agents in reversing platinum resistance in ovarian, colon, and other cancers. DNA methylation inhibitors such as 5-azacytidine (Aza) and 5-aza-2′-deoxycytidine have been extensively used in hematologic malignancies and other neoplasms since their FDA approval over a decade ago. However, global DNA demethylation often leads to intolerable toxicities, likely due to the broad genome-wide effects. Therefore, gene-specific epigenetic modulation—such as targeted demethylation—may represent a more precise and effective approach in cancer therapy. Integrating site-specific methylation/demethylation with AVA therapy may help overcome resistance and improve therapeutic outcomes. Although epigenetic changes are known to influence many aspects of tumor biology, their role in modulating response to tumor microenvironment-targeted therapies such as AVA remains poorly understood. This prompted us to explore whether epigenetic changes are induced in response to the dynamic alterations in the AVA-treated tumor microenvironment.

Here, we identify a specific molecular mechanism of AVA resistance driven by methylation of BARD1 (breast cancer type 1 susceptibility protein [BRCA1]-associated RING domain 1). We further develop and apply a targeted demethylation strategy for BARD1 to enhance AVA efficacy. Specifically, we engineered a deactivated Cas9 (dCas9)-CRISPR-based system to induce CpG-specific demethylation of BARD1 (derived by fusing the dCas9 with the TET1 catalytic domain) delivered in vivo using a nanoliposomal carrier. We demonstrate that this gene-specific approach robustly enhances antitumor efficacy when combined with AVA therapy in multiple cancer models. Collectively, our findings provide a CRISPR-based, BARD1-targeted demethylation strategy—delivered via a liposomal system—as a promising therapeutic avenue to overcome AVA resistance.

Results

Aza restores sensitivity to AVA therapy

Given the lack of data on the role of demethylating drugs such as Aza on AVA therapy sensitivity, we first conducted an in vivo study using the orthotopic SKOV3ip1-luc OC mouse model. Tumor-bearing mice were treated with the anti-VEGF antibody B20 (targets both human and mouse VEGF) as monotherapy until resistance developed. Tumors collected at various time points displayed either sensitivity or resistance to AVA. We then continued treating AVA-resistant mice with either B20 alone or combination of B20 and Aza (administered twice weekly) (Figure 1A). To assess genome-wide methylation changes in AVA-resistant tumors, we performed methylation profiling using the Illumina Infinium Human Methylation 450k BeadChip array, which allows for single-CpG-site resolution. We found that resistance to AVA was associated with widespread methylation changes, many of which were reversed by Aza treatment, both alone and in combination with B20 (Figure 1B). To evaluate the impact of combination therapy on angiogenesis, we performed CD31 immunostaining in SKOV3ip1 tumors. Tumor from the combination group showed a significant reduction in microvessel density (Figure S1A, related to Figure 1).

To further evaluate therapeutic efficacy, we tested Aza in combination with AVA in several preclinical models. First, we tested the HT1080 fibrosarcoma model, which is well known to be highly angiogenic. As shown in Figures 1C and 1D, combination treatment significantly reduced tumor weight and volume relative to control and monotherapy groups, with no observable changes in mouse body weight (Figure S1B, related to Figure 1). In a platinum-resistant patient-derived xenograft model, peritoneally implanted in NOD/SCID mice, combination therapy significantly reduced tumor weight (Figure 1E) and nodule count (Figure 1F). Mouse body weight was lower in the treatment groups (Figure S1C, related to Figure 1), consistent with a decrease in ascites volume (Figure S1D, related to Figure 1). Similarly, in the orthotopic SKOV3ip1-luc model, the Aza and B20 combination significantly reduced tumor burden and nodule number compared to controls or monotherapy groups, again with no change in body weight (Figures 1G and 1H; Figure S1E, related to Figure 1).

Next, we evaluated the in vivo toxicity profile of the combination regimen by performing complete blood counts and serum biochemical analyses to assess potential systemic effects. C57BL/6 mice were treated with B20 and Aza at a dosage of 5 mg/kg over a 2-week period, with Aza administered three times and B20 twice per week. Hematologic parameters —including white blood cell count, hemoglobin concentration, platelet count, and eosinophil and monocyte levels—were measured alongside serum markers of liver and tissue damage (lactate dehydrogenase, alanine aminotransferase, and aspartate aminotransferase). No significant differences were observed between treatment and control groups, indicating that the combination is well tolerated (Figure S2, related to Figure 1).

High-throughput analysis reveals significant genomic changes after Aza therapy

To better understand the mechanisms underlying Aza efficacy in AVA-resistant tumors, we performed integrated epigenomic and transcriptomic profiling in the SKOV3ip1-luc model (described earlier; mRNA array; Figure 2A) combined with a methylation array (HumanMethylation450 platform; Figure 1B). Methylation array analysis revealed 43,830 tumor-specific methylated loci (β value > 0.6; 9% of 485,513 total probes), which were compared between B20 and combination groups and overlaid with mRNA expression data from a gene array. Genes with >1.5-fold change in expression were considered part of a candidate gene signature of AVA resistance. To identify genes with reduced methylation levels alongside elevated expression in the combination treatment group relative to the B20 monotherapy group, we integrated the methylation array dataset with the gene expression data. A visual representation highlighting the top genes based on their fold change is shown in Figure 2B. Among these, BARD1 mRNA (Figure 2C) and protein (Figure 2D) expression were significantly increased by Aza in adaptive AVA-resistant tumors, particularly in the combination group. Pathway analysis implicated BARD1 in DNA repair, homology-directed repair, and cell cycle regulation (Figure 2E). Although other genes, including charged multivesicular body protein 4A (CHMP4A) and cysteine-rich intestinal protein 1 (CRIP1), showed similar patterns of expression and promoter methylation, BARD1 was prioritized for further investigation due to its marked upregulation in adaptive AVA-resistant tumors following Aza treatment (Figures 2C and 2D; Figure S3A, related to Figure 2). Given BARD1’s established roles in DNA repair and tumor suppression, the combination of its differential methylation and significant transcriptional activation made it a more compelling candidate. In contrast, CHMP4A showed minimal changes in expression, and CRIP1’s role in resistance pathways remains unclear. Quantitative analysis of CHMP4A and CRIP1 mRNA levels in Aza-treated adaptive AVA-resistant tumors confirmed no significant changes compared to control or monotherapy groups (Figures S3B and S3C, related to Figure 2). These findings underscore the relevance of BARD1 in mediating Aza-induced effects in the context of AVA resistance and highlight its distinction from other candidate genes.

Before performing additional in vitro experiments to assess the biological effects of BARD1 in OC, we determined its expression level in normal fallopian tube epithelium, non-transformed ovarian surface epithelial cells (HIO180), and a panel of OC cell lines (SKOV3ip1, SKOV3-TR, A2780ip1, HeyA8, OVCAR3, OVCAR5, and OVCAR8ip1). Under normoxic conditions (20% O2 at 37°C), BARD1 mRNA (Figure 2F) and protein (Figure 2G) expression levels were highest in the SKOV3ip1 cells and lowest in OVCAR8ip1 cells; therefore, we chose the SKOV3ip1 cell line for knockdown experiments and OVCAR8ip1 for overexpression. Treatment of SKOV3ip1 cells with small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting BARD1 resulted in >90% knockdown of BARD1 mRNA (48 h) and protein (72 h) levels compared with a nontargeting (control) siRNA (Figures S3D and S3E, related to Figure 2). We also expressed BARD1 in OVCAR8ip1 cells using pReceiver-Lv122 plasmid, which resulted in >7.5-fold and 5-fold increases in mRNA and protein levels, respectively (Figures S3F and S3G, related to Figure 2). We selected BARD1-2 siRNA and a stable OVCAR8-BARD1-Clone-2 for further experiments.

BARD1 affects tumor angiogenesis indirectly via cancer cells

To determine if BARD1 impacts angiogenesis directly (i.e., endothelial cells) or indirectly (mediated via cancer cells), we silenced BARD1 in RF24 endothelial cells using siRNA (Figure S4A, related to Figure 3) but found no significant change in tube formation (Figure 3A). However, conditioned media (CM) from BARD1-silenced SKOV3ip1 cells significantly increased tube formation in RF24 cells (Figure 3B), indicating an indirect tumor cell-mediated effect on angiogenesis.

To understand the effect of BARD1 silencing on tumor angiogenesis pathways, we performed RNA sequencing of BARD1-silenced SKOV3ip1 cells. Results showed that 952 genes were differentially expressed: 268 were downregulated and 684 were upregulated (DESeq2 padj ≤ 0.05 |log2FoldChange| ≥ 1.0) in the BARD1 siRNA-treated group compared with the control siRNA group. Among these, several pro-angiogenic genes, including fibronectin 1 (FN1), endothelial cell-specific molecule 1 (ESM1), C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 8 (CXCL8), ETS proto-oncogene 1 (ETS1), pentraxin 3 (PTX3), C-C motif chemokine ligand 2 (CCL2), and others, were upregulated (Table S1, related to Figure 3). Moreover, enrichment analysis with Gene Ontology showed an increase in the angiogenesis pathway after BARD1 knockdown (Figure 3C).

To further validate the role of BARD1 in tumor growth, we collected CM after BARD1 knockdown in SKOV3ip1 cells for an in vivo Matrigel plug assay. Silencing BARD1 resulted in significantly increased angiogenesis, as reflected by the highest hemoglobin level compared with other groups (Figure 3D). To further confirm the effect of BARD1 on angiogenesis, we tested CM from BARD1-silenced SKOV3ip1 cells with an array for angiogenesis-related proteins. BARD1 silencing led to increased levels of several pro-angiogenic proteins, including interleukin (IL)-8, plasminogen activator, urokinase (uPA), PTX3, and thrombospondin-1, compared to controls (Figure 3E; Figure S4B, related to Figure 3). To determine whether CHMP4A or CRIP1 might exert similar effects on angiogenesis, we performed siRNA-mediated knockdown of these genes in SKOV3ip1 cells and analyzed the CM using an angiogenesis array. Unlike BARD1, silencing of CHMP4A or CRIP1 did not result in any significant changes in angiogenic factor levels compared to control siRNA-treated cells (Figures S4C–S4E, related to Figure 3).

Next, we ectopically increased BARD1 expression in OVCAR8ip1 cells and then subjected them to RNA sequencing. Compared to BARD1-silenced cells, which showed upregulation of several pro-angiogenic genes, BARD1-expressing cells exhibited a reduction in the expression of several pro-angiogenic genes (Figures S4B and S4F; Table S1, related to Figure 3). To further confirm the role of BARD1 in tumor growth, we collected CM from OVCAR8ip1 cells expressing BARD1 for an in vivo Matrigel plug assay. The BARD1-expressing group showed significantly decreased angiogenesis, indicated by the lowest hemoglobin levels compared to the other groups (Figure S4G, related to Figure 3). Moreover, RF24 cells treated with CM from BARD1-expressing OVCAR8ip1 cells showed significantly reduced tube formation compared to cells treated with control siRNA (Figure S4H, related to Figure 3). To assess the effect of BARD1 expression on tumor growth, we injected OVCAR8ip1-BARD1-expressing cells into mice. Tumors derived from BARD1-expressing cells showed reduced tumor burden compared to controls (p value = 0.0103) (Figure S4I, related to Figure 3) with no significant change in mouse weight (p value = 0.7834) (Figure S4J, related to Figure 3).

To determine whether BARD1 silencing affects tumor growth and progression, we developed a TetOn system for the SKOV3ip1 model. Treatment of SKOV3ip1-shBARD1-TetOn cells with doxycycline for 24 h resulted in >80% reduction in BARD1 expression in vitro (Figure S5A, related to Figure 3). We used the same TetOn model in vivo and found that BARD1 knockdown (animals received dox-containing food) led to increased tumor burden in the control group (BARD1−) compared with the BARD1+ group. The same trend was observed for group 2 (BARD1− compared with BARD1+ group treated with bevacizumab [AVA] alone) and group 3 (BARD1− compared with BARD1+ group treated with bevacizumab and Aza; Figures 3F and 3G). Tumor weight and BARD1 expression were inversely correlated (Figure 3H), and no significant change was observed in mouse body weight (Figure S5B, related to Figure 3). Immunohistochemical staining showed that there was a significant increase in the number of blood vessels in the BARD1 knockdown group compared to the untreated group (Figure S5C, related to Figure 3).

BARD1 is an obligatory partner of BRCA1, and there is evidence that BARD1 is also a tumor suppressor. To test our hypothesis that BARD1 has an independent role in angiogenesis, we compared BARD1 and BRCA1 knockdown groups in vitro and found that the expression of BARD1 and BRCA1 was positively correlated (Figures S5D and S5E, related to Figure 3). BARD1 knockdown resulted in significant changes in mRNA expression levels of pro-angiogenic genes ESM1 and FN1 whereas no significant differences were observed after BRCA1 knockdown (Figures S5F and S5G, related to Figure 3). We compared the mRNA expression of BARD1 and ESM1 in high-grade serous ovarian cancer (HGSC) samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database and found that they are positively correlated with each other (R = 0.36, p value = 2.5e−14) (Figure S5H, related to Figure 3).

Next, we silenced BARD1 and BRCA1 in SKOV3ip1 cells using siRNA for 48 h and collected the CM. RF24 cells were cultured in the conditioned medium for 8 h followed by tube formation assay. As shown in Figure S5I, CM from BARD1-silenced cells, but not BRCA1-silenced cells, increased tube formation in RF24 cells compared to controls. Similarly, CM from BARD1-silenced COV362ip1 cells (BRCA1-mutant OC with a frameshift mutation [BRCA1 c.2288delT, p.Val763fs]) promoted tube formation in RF24 cells compared to untreated cells or those treated with control siRNA (Figure S5J, related to Figure 3).

To evaluate the impact of BARD1 modulation on tumor growth and therapeutic response in a BRCA1-independent context, we performed an in vivo study. Mice were intraperitoneally injected with COV362ip1 cells, and treatment began on day 4 post injection, with Aza and B20 administered at 5 mg/kg (Aza three times per week, B20 twice per week). A significant reduction in tumor weight was observed in the combination group compared to controls (p < 0.0001; Figure S5K, related to Figure 3). Although a reduction in mouse weight was also noted; this was largely attributed to decreased ascites (Figures S5L and S5M, related to Figure 3). We also evaluated BARD1 mRNA expression level and found a significant increase in the combination group compared to the control group (p value < 0.001) (Figure S5N, related to Figure 3). Together, these results demonstrate that BARD1 has a BRCA1-independent role on angiogenesis.

BARD1 epigenetic modulation under hypoxic conditions

Since AVA therapy is known to induce hypoxia in the tumor, we further investigated the mechanisms by which DNMT3A and BARD1 expression could be altered in hypoxic conditions. We first checked BARD1 mRNA and protein expression levels in SKOV3ip1 cells under hypoxic conditions (1% O2) and found that BARD1 expression levels were significantly decreased compared to normoxic conditions (Figures 4A–4D). Extending this finding further, we incubated SKOV3ip1 cells with a hypoxia inducible factor 1 subunit alpha (HIF1α)-stabilizing compound (CoCl2 for 24 h under normoxic conditions) and found that BARD1 mRNA expression levels were significantly lower after 24 h of CoCl2 incubation (Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3, Figure 4, Figure 5S6A and S6B, related to Figure 4).

To determine whether HIF1α blockade would abrogate the effects of hypoxia on BARD1 expression, we silenced HIF1α using siRNA in SKOV3ip1 cells under hypoxic conditions (1% O2). There was a significant increase in BARD1 mRNA expression compared with untreated and control siRNA-treated cells (Figures 4E and S6C, related to Figure 4). Furthermore, incubation of SKOV3ip1 cells under hypoxic conditions (1% O2) resulted in significantly increased DNMT3A mRNA expression levels compared to normoxic conditions (Figure 4F). HIF1α siRNA-treated cells had decreased DNMT3A mRNA expression under hypoxic conditions (1% O2) (Figure 4G). Correlative data from publicly available transcriptomic data showed that DNMT3A levels are positively correlated with HIF1α expression in human OC samples (Figure S6D, TCGA, related to Figure 4). Next, we performed in silico promoter analysis and chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) followed by qPCR to determine HIF1α enrichment at the DNMT3A promoter. We first checked for potential binding sites for HIF1α within a 2-kb region spanning the DNMT3A promoter using TFSEARCH and chose acute myeloid leukemia 1 protein (AML1) as a positive control for binding and DNMT1a as a negative control. Upon promoter analysis, HIF1α was predicted to bind to the DNMT3A promoter region. ChIP assays revealed that the binding of HIF1α to the promoter region was significantly higher under hypoxic compared to normoxic conditions, suggesting that the increase in DNMT3A under hypoxic conditions is HIF1α dependent (Figure 4H).

To investigate whether hypoxic conditions affect BARD1 expression, we exposed SKOV3ip1 cells to hypoxia (1% O2 or treatment with CoCl2) for 48 h. To understand whether HIF1α regulates BARD1 by direct binding to the promoter, we exposed SKOV3ip1 cells to hypoxia for 48 h and performed a ChIP assay and found that HIF1α binding to the promoter region of BARD1 at sites 1 and 4 was significantly enriched under hypoxic conditions compared with normoxic conditions (Figure 4I; Figure S6E, related to Figure 4). We also checked whether DNA demethylases are altered by hypoxia and found that ten-eleven translocation methylcytosine dioxygenase 1 (TET1) mRNA and protein expression levels were reduced under low-oxygen conditions (Figure 4J; Figure S6F, related to Figure 4). Moreover, HIF1α siRNA-treated cells had increased TET1 mRNA expression (Figure S6G, related to Figure 4).

Liposomal dCas9-CRISPR system demethylates BARD1

We identified several CpG island sites in the BARD1 gene (cg26049092, cg08563601, cg24607575, and cg24715680) from methylation array analysis of adaptive AVA-resistant tumors and confirmed that methylation was associated with reduced BARD1 expression. Although the molecular mechanism underlying this methylation remains unclear, we sought to evaluate the effects of directly inducing demethylation. To achieve targeted demethylation of BARD1, we developed a nanoengineered system using a liposomal dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1 construct incorporated into a single-expression plasmid. Four pairs of single-guide RNAs (sgRNAs) targeting the BARD1 promoter region were designed and cloned into individual dCas9-TET1 vectors driven by the cytomegalovirus (CMV) promoter. BLAST-like alignment tool analysis confirmed precise genomic localization of the sgRNAs and verified that they overlapped with the intended CpG sites in the BARD1 promoter. Off-target effects were evaluated using CasOFFinder (allowing up to two base mismatches), and although several potential off-target sites were predicted, all Chr2-related hits overlapped the intended target region and were deemed irrelevant. Ultimately, seven potential off-target sites remained across all four sgRNAs. Specifically, sgRNA-3 showed four off-target sites located in intergenic regions (Chr6 and Chr3) and intronic regions of R3HCC1L (Chr10) and LSM14A (Chr19) (Table S2, related to Figure 4). As these locations are non-coding or intronic, their functional impact is likely negligible. These results support the high specificity of the sgRNAs for BARD1, with minimal risk of off-target demethylation. We next tested these sgRNAs in vitro by transfecting SKOV3ip1 cells and selected sgRNA-1 and -3 for further studies (Figure S6H, related to Figure 4). The dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1 plasmids were scaled up for in vivo experiments and incorporated into the neutral lipid 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DOPC) liposome formulation.

To determine whether BARD1 expression is regulated by DNA demethylation, bisulfite sequencing was performed in both in vitro and in vivo models. In vitro, SKOV3ip1 cells were transfected with three different sgRNAs targeting BARD1 using lipofectamine, and DNA was collected 48 h post transfection. No significant changes in methylation levels were observed between conditions (Figure S6I), consistent with limited re-expression of BARD1 in vitro. In contrast, in vivo treatment of mice bearing SKOV3ip1 tumors with DOPC-based TET1-sgControl or TET1-sgBARD1-3 for 6 weeks revealed a significant reduction in BARD1 promoter methylation in the sgBARD1-treated tumors (Figure S6J, related to Figure 4). This result underscores the context-dependent role of DNA demethylation regulating BARD1, with a more pronounced effect observed in vivo (Figure S6J, related to Figure 4).

To determine whether targeted demethylation of BARD1 would have antitumor effects in vivo, we used an orthotopic SKOV3ip1-luc mouse model. Mice were treated with DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgControl (control) or DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1-3 (sgBARD1-3) administered intravenously twice weekly for 6 weeks. Tumor weight was significantly lower in the sgBARD1-3 group compared with the control group (Figure 4K), with no significant change in mouse body weight (Figure S7A, related to Figure 4). BARD1 expression was also significantly increased in sgBARD1-3-treated tumors compared to controls (Figure 4L).

We next examined whether BARD1 demethylation would combine well with AVA therapy in the SKOV3ip1 model. Combination treatment of sgBARD1-3 and bevacizumab significantly reduced tumor burden compared to either sgControl or bevacizumab monotherapy groups (Figures 4M and 4N). No significant change was observed in mouse body weight in any group (Figure S7B, related to Figure 4). BARD1 expression was significantly increased in tumors treated with sgBARD1-3 alone or in combination with bevacizumab (Figure S7C). To confirm these findings, we tested a second sgRNA (sgBARD1-1 construct) in vivo. SKOV3ip1 tumor-bearing mice were treated with DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgControl (control) or DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1-1 (sgBARD1-1) for 5 weeks. Tumor burden in the sgBARD1-1 group was significantly reduced compared with the control group (Figure 4O). No significant changes in mouse body weight were noted in any group (Figure S7D, related to Figure 4).

Finally, we validated the antitumor effects of sgBARD1-3 in an independent orthotopic OC model using the HGSC cell line OVCAR5. Tumor-bearing mice (n = 10 per group) treated with either DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgControl or DOPC-dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1-3 showed significantly reduced tumor burden in the sgBARD1-3 group (p < 0.0001), with no changes in mouse body weight (Figures S7E and S7F, related to Figure 4).

Discussion

Here, we identify epigenetic modulation as a fundamental mechanism of adaptive resistance to AVA. Specifically, we demonstrate that BARD1 silencing promotes the upregulation of alternative pro-angiogenic pathways (Figure 5). We developed and tested a highly specific liposomal dCas9-TET1-sgBARD1 approach for targeted BARD1 CpG demethylation. This resulted in BARD1-specific demethylation in vivo, which had robust antitumor effects in combination with AVA. This combined therapy warrants further exploration given the potential to expand the utility and efficacy of bevacizumab in treating OC, particularly in AVA-resistant disease.

We prioritized BARD1 due to its distinct and compelling role in adaptive AVA resistance. While CHMP4A and CRIP1 also exhibited altered methylation and expression patterns in resistant tumors—characterized by hypomethylation and increased expression—BARD1 stood out for several reasons. Notably, BARD1 expression increased significantly following Aza treatment in adaptive AVA-resistant tumors, a pattern not mirrored by CHMP4A or CRIP1. Moreover, BARD1’s established roles in DNA repair and tumor suppression provide a mechanistic link between its epigenetic silencing and resistance development. In contrast, CHMP4A, a component of the endosomal sorting complex required for transport III, is involved in membrane remodeling and extracellular vesicle formation but showed only modest expression changes in our analysis. CRIP1, a zinc-binding LIM domain protein, plays a role in cellular homeostasis and immune function but lacks a clear connection to AVA resistance mechanisms. Furthermore, silencing CHMP4A or CRIP1 did not affect angiogenesis, whereas BARD1 knockdown significantly impacted this process, emphasizing its broader relevance in resistance pathways. While CHMP4A and CRIP1 may still contribute to cancer biology, BARD1’s functional importance and pronounced molecular changes made it the most compelling candidate for further study in AVA resistance.

Anti-VEGF therapies are approved for multiple cancer types, and although they improve objective response and progression-free survival, their effect on overall survival remains modest due to acquired resistance. The mechanisms underlying this adaptive resistance are multifaceted, with tumor microenvironment playing a central role. Prior studies, including our own, have highlighted the contribution of stromal components—such as endothelial cells, platelets, pericytes, macrophages, and cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs)—to the adaptive response to AVA. While bevacizumab is often administered in combination with chemotherapy, its potential as monotherapy is supported by clinical evidence. The gynecologic oncology group (GOG) 170D trial reported a 21% clinical response rate with bevacizumab monotherapy. A phase 3 clinical trial showed that the greatest benefit was seen in patients receiving bevacizumab maintenance therapy. These data suggest that bevacizumab’s therapeutic effect is not solely dependent on its combination with chemotherapy but may also be attributed to its ability to modulate the tumor microenvironment and angiogenic signaling independently. Nonetheless, in relapsed OC, combination therapy remains standard, as shown in the avastin use in platinum-resistant epithelial ovarian cancer (AURELIA) trial, where adding bevacizumab to chemotherapy improved both response rate and progression-free survival.

The dosing regimen of Aza plays an important role in its epigenetic modulation and therapeutic efficacy. In this study, we used a specific dosing regimen optimized for our experimental models, but different dosing schedules could influence the clinical relevance of Aza in combination with AVA therapy. Extended dosing schedules, such as 14 or 21 days of oral Aza (CC-486) at 300 mg daily, have demonstrated improved methylation reversal and sustained DNA hypomethylation compared to shorter regimens. These extended schedules allow for more extensive exposure, enhancing the drug’s effect on diseased cells by targeting active cell cycling phases and reducing global DNA methylation. Prolonged low-dose administration may enable more sustained and selective epigenetic modulation, potentially reducing off-target effects and global hypomethylation. This strategy could result in a more refined restoration of BARD1 expression, particularly in tumor-specific CpG demethylation, while minimizing toxicity and adverse effects in normal tissues. Continuous low-dose regimens align more closely with the pharmacokinetics seen in clinical practice, where such schedules are used to balance efficacy and patient tolerability. Conversely, higher doses of Aza administered intermittently may induce broader demethylation effects, potentially disrupting other genes and pathways unrelated to AVA resistance. While high-dose, short-term schedules may prioritize cytotoxic synergy over epigenetic modulation, they are less likely to yield sustained effects and carry a higher risk of systemic toxicity. Future studies should compare dosing schedules to optimize BARD1 demethylation and maximize therapeutic synergy with AVA while minimizing adverse effects.

Our findings indicate that alterations in DNA methylation patterns contribute to the development of AVA resistance. While Aza offers therapeutic promise, its global hypomethylation effects can produce undesirable toxicities, including hematologic complications, gastrointestinal side effects, and secondary malignancies. Aza-induced transcriptional “noise” may lead to aberrant gene activation but also improved immunogenicity. To circumvent global off-target effects, we developed a liposomal dCas9-TET1-sgRNA delivery platform for precise, gene-specific demethylation. This system reduced BARD1 promoter methylation in vivo, supporting its specificity and therapeutic relevance. Clinically, the related agent decitabine (5-Aza-2′-deoxycytidine) has demonstrated global methylation reduction (∼14%) in leukemia, illustrating the translational relevance of DNA methylation targeting. Site-specific demethylation of BARD1 represents a next-generation strategy to overcome AVA resistance while minimizing toxicity.

Our data also reveal a BRCA1-independent role for BARD1 in regulating angiogenesis. We observed that BARD1 may regulate pro-angiogenic genes through interactions with downstream targets such as ESM1 and FN1. ESM1 enhances endothelial cell proliferation and vascular permeability, while FN1 mediates extracellular matrix remodeling and endothelial cell adhesion—both essential for angiogenesis. These findings suggest that BARD1 contributes to a compensatory angiogenic network, highlighting its previously unrecognized role in adaptive resistance to anti-angiogenic therapy. Further studies are needed to dissect these mechanisms, using gene editing and transcriptomic profiling to validate BARD1’s regulatory impact on ESM1, FN1, and other pro-angiogenic effectors.

Given the increasing use of bevacizumab in clinical settings, developing strategies to overcome adaptive resistance is a critical challenge. While preclinical and limited clinical studies suggest that the upregulation of alternative pro-angiogenic growth factors (e.g., bFGF, IL-6, IL-8) contributes to resistance, therapeutic targeting of these pathways has yielded limited clinical benefit. Nevertheless, rational combinations may hold promise. A recent phase 1b clinical trial of a VEGF/DLL4 bispecific antibody plus paclitaxel demonstrated striking clinical activity, earning fast-track designation by the FDA. These findings support a broader approach to co-targeting anti-angiogenic pathways and further investigating epigenetic drivers of resistance.

In conclusion, we demonstrate that targeted demethylation of BARD1 using a liposomal CRISPR-dCas9-TET1 system effectively reverses AVA resistance in OC models. This strategy restores BARD1 expression and enhances therapeutic response, offering a highly specific and translationally relevant epigenetic therapy. Our findings establish a foundation for gene-specific demethylation as a viable strategy to overcome drug resistance, with high translational potential to improve outcomes of patients with cancer.

Limitations of the study

One limitation of this study is the absence of patient-derived samples, clinical specimens, or clinical trial data to validate the findings. Additionally, the specific molecular mechanisms by which BARD1 regulates alternative pro-angiogenic pathways remain incompletely defined. While preliminary data suggest the involvement of ESM1 and FN1, further investigation is needed to clarify the downstream targets and their roles in resistance mechanisms. Moreover, the study focuses exclusively on OC models, and it remains unclear whether these findings are applicable to other tumor types that develop resistance to AVA. Although this work underscores the potential of targeting BARD1 to overcome AVA resistance, the broader clinical relevance remains unclear, given the modest overall benefits of anti-VEGF therapies and the influence of alternative vascularization mechanisms such as vessel co-option. Future research should incorporate a wider range of cancer models and include clinical validation to enhance the translational impact of this therapeutic strategy.

Experimental model and subject details

Cell maintenance

Human ovarian cancer cells, SKOV3ip1-luc, SKOV3-TR, OVCAR4, A2780ip2, HeyA8, OVCAR3 and OVCAR8ip1, were cultured in RPMI-1640. OVCAR5, COV362ip1 and MDA-OVCA-1 (PDX from chemotherapy-resistant patient tumor) cells were cultured in Dulbecco modified Eagle medium (DMEM). Both media were supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 0.1% gentamicin sulfate (Gemini Bioproducts, Calabasas, CA). Human fibrosarcoma cells, HT1080, were cultured in Eagle minimum essential medium with 5% FBS and 0.1% gentamicin sulfate. Human immortalized umbilical vein endothelial cells (RF24) were maintained in MEM supplemented with nonessential amino acids, sodium pyruvate, MEM vitamins, and glutamine (Life Technologies, Grand Island, NY). Cell culture was performed at 37°C in a 5% CO2 incubator with 95% humidity. All cell lines were routinely tested for mycoplasma contamination using the Universal Mycoplasma Detection Kit (ATCC) and were authenticated by short tandem repeat (STR) profiling through the Cytogenetics & Cell Authentication Core at The University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. Cells were used within ∼20 passages after thawing for in vitro experiments and ∼10 passages after thawing for in vivo experiments.

In vivo studies

All mouse studies were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at MD Anderson. All animal experiments were performed with 4- to 6-week-old female athymic nude (NCr-nude) and NOD/SCID mice obtained from Taconic Biosciences (Rensselaer, NY). Five mice per cage were housed under pathogen-free conditions at a constant temperature and humidity. All mice were fed a regular diet (unless otherwise specified) and water ad libitum according to American Association for Laboratory Animal Science guidelines and the US Public Health Service Policy on Human Care and Use of Laboratory Animals. Mice were euthanized via cervical dislocation at the end of the treatment period or if found moribund by the investigators, who have approval from the IACUC committee. Female athymic nude mice were intraperitoneally injected with 1 × 106 SKOV3ip1-luc and SKOV3-TetOn-shBARD1. HT1080 cells were subcutaneously injected. MDA-OVCA-1 (PDX, IRB no: PA15-0441) chunks were implanted into the peritoneal cavity. Mice were randomly assigned to experimental groups after cell injection. For the SKOV3ip1 model, adaptive resistance to AVA was established by injecting luciferase-labeled cells, as described previously. After 3 weeks, each mouse received initial treatments with a control IgG antibody or B20, a murine monoclonal VEGF-A antibody (5 mg/kg, injected intraperitoneally twice weekly), and tumor growth was monitored by IVIS imaging (Xenogen, Alameda, CA). After 3 weeks, the mice were divided into B20-sensitive and B20-resistant groups according to their responses to the drug.

To analyze the effect of knockdown of BARD1 in an in vivo model, we injected 1 × 106 SKOV3ip1-shBARD1-TetON cells intraperitoneally in 0.2 mL of Hank’s balanced saline solution. Mice were divided into 6 groups: standard diet groups are group 1 (UT), group 3 (shBARD1+Bev), group 5 (shBARD1 + Bev + Aza); doxycycline-diet (200 mg/kg) groups are group 2 (UT), and group 4 (shBARD1+Bev), group 6 (shBARD1 + Bev + Aza).

Tumor growth was monitored, and mice were euthanized 5–10 weeks after inoculation. Samples were processed and stored for histopathologic analyses. Once mice in any group became moribund, all mice were euthanized by cervical dislocation. Individuals who performed the necropsies were blinded to the treatment group assignments. Tumor weights, nodule numbers, and the presence of ascites were recorded at the time of gross necropsy. All tumor tissues were dissected. Samples were snap-frozen for later DNA, RNA, or protein analyses, fixed in formalin for paraffin embedding, or snap-frozen in optimal cutting temperature compound (Miles, Inc, Elkhart, IN), for frozen slide preparation.

Bevacizumab (Avastin) was obtained from MD Anderson Cancer Center pharmacy. The AVA B20 (Genentech) is a cross-species reactive, function-blocking monoclonal antibody targeting both human and murine VEGF-A. Azacytidine (Vidaza) was obtained from MD Anderson Cancer Center pharmacy. Bevacizumab, B20 (5 mg/kg, twice per week), and Aza were administered intraperitoneally (5 mg/kg, three times per week).

Toxicity assessment of azacytidine and B20 combination therapy in mice

Wild-type C57BL/6 mice were treated with either vehicle control or a combination of azacytidine and B20 (AVA) at a dose of 5 mg/kg over a two-week period. Azacytidine was administered three times per week, and B20 was administered twice per week. At the end of the treatment period, blood was collected for complete blood counts (CBC), including white blood cell (WBC) count, hemoglobin concentration, platelet count, eosinophils, and monocytes. In addition, serum biochemical analyses were performed to assess liver and tissue toxicity, including measurements of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) levels.

Method details

Protein extraction and western blot analysis

Total protein lysates were prepared using ice-cold RIPA lysis buffer (25 mM Tris⋅HCl pH 7.6, 150 mM NaCl, 1% NP-40, 1% sodium deoxycholate, 0.1% SDS) containing a protease and phosphatase inhibitor cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Pittsburgh, PA). Protein was quantified using the BCA Protein Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific). A total of 30 μg of protein was used for Western blot analysis using 4–20% and 7.5% sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis separation gel. The membranes were then blocked and incubated in primary antibody diluted in 5% milk in Tris-buffered saline-Tween-20 overnight at 4°C. Membranes were probed with a horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibody depending on their primary antibodies and developed with ECL substrate (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Quantification of band intensity was done using ImageJ software version 1.54p.

RNA isolation, cDNA synthesis, and real-time PCR

Tumor tissues or cells of different types/conditions were harvested in Trizol (Life Technologies). Total RNA was extracted using the Direct-zol RNA miniprep kit (Zymo Research) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. A total of 1 μg of RNA was used for preparing cDNA with random hexamer oligonucleotide primers using a Verso cDNA synthesis kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the supplier’s recommended protocol. Gene-specific primers were used for real-time PCR in a 7500 Real-Time PCR System (Applied BioSystems, Carlsbad, CA) using 2× SYBR green master mix (Applied BioSystems; Table S3). The expression of 18S rRNA in each sample was used to normalize the expression of the gene(s) of interest. Fold change in expression was calculated using the comparative 2−ΔΔCT method.

Gene silencing by siRNA

SiRNA targeted to BARD1, BRCA1, and HIF1α were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich (Woodlands, TX). In vitro, transient transfection was performed as described previously. Briefly, siRNA (2 μg) was incubated with 6 μL of Lipofectamine 2000 transfection reagent (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) for 20 min at room temperature and then added to cells cultured in 6-well plates at 60% confluence. siRNA with a nonspecific function that shared no sequence homology with any known mRNA in a BLAST search was used to control the target siRNA (Mission siRNA Universal Negative Control#1, Sigma). At selected time intervals, cells were harvested to measure mRNA levels of BARD1, BRCA1, ESM1, and FN1 using quantitative reverse-transcriptase PCR.

BARD1 overexpression

A BARD1-expressing OVCAR8ip1 cell line was generated by transduction with a BARD1 plasmid (EX-Q0613-Lv122, GeneCopoeia, Inc). Lentivirus particles containing BARD1 open reading frames were produced using the Lenti-Pac HIV Expression Packaging Kit protocol (HPK-LvTR-20; GeneCopoeia) and transduced into OVCAR8ip1 cells (1 × 106) that were previously plated in a complete RPMI-1640 medium in a 10-cm culture dish and incubated overnight at 37°C with 5% CO2. Virus particles and polybrene were diluted in Opti-MEM and added to the cells the following day, and the cells were incubated overnight at 37°C with 5% CO2. The next day, the media was replaced with complete media, and after 48 h, the infected cells were analyzed for transient expression of GFP-BARD1 using a fluorescent microscope. Antibiotic (Puromycin) selection was started for 6 days, and GFP+ cells were then sorted by fluorescence-activated cell sorting and used for further experiments.

DNA methylation arrays

Genomic DNA was extracted from formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) samples and was hybridized to the Illumina Infinium 450K Human DNA Methylation Beadchip array to obtain DNA methylation profiles according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Microarray analysis

We extracted total RNA from tumor samples of control IgG, Aza, B20, and combination groups using RNeasy mini kit (Qiagen). A NanoDrop spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was used for the assessment of both RNA quality and RNA quantity. Total RNA (700 ng) was labeled and hybridized to Bead Chips according to the manufacturer’s protocols (Illumina, San Diego, CA). We scanned Bead Chips with an Illumina BeadArray Reader, and all array normalization, filtering, and statistical analysis were performed using BRB-ArrayTools (National Cancer Institute).

RNA sequencing

Total RNA was extracted using Direct-zol RNA Miniprep Plus kit (ZYMO Research, Irvine, CA, USA) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. RNA quality was determined by RNA integrity number using a Bioanalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA, USA), and the samples were shipped to Novogene (Sacramento, CA, USA) for RNA-Seq analysis on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform. Gene expression level was calculated using the number of mapped reads. Fragments per kilobase of transcript sequence per millions of base pairs sequenced (FPKM) was used to estimate gene expression levels; this method takes into consideration the effects of both sequencing depth and gene length on counting of fragments. Genes with adjusted p-value <0.05 and |log2(FoldChange)| > 0 were considered as differentially expressed. Downstream analysis was performed using a combination of programs, including hisat2, DEseq2, and ClusterProfiler software.

Promoter analysis and ChIP assay

ChIP assays were performed at the MD Anderson Cancer Center Epigenomics Profiling Core. SKOV3ip1-luc cells were cultured in hypoxic (1% oxygen) and normoxic (20% oxygen) conditions for 16 h. Next, the cells were crosslinked with 1% formaldehyde for 10 min followed by quenching with glycine. Chromatin lysate from these cells was subjected to ChIP with antibodies specific to HIF1α, DNMT3A, AML-1, DNMT1A or normal IgG as control. ChIP assays were performed as described previously with modifications. DNMT1A was used as negative, and AML-1 was used as positive control. The DNA region of interest was detected by SYBR real-time quantitative PCR, and enrichment relative to input was calculated. Gene-specific primers used for qPCR assay were included in (Table S3, Related to Figure 2).

Proteome profiler human angiogenesis array

Supernatants from cells were collected and applied to the array membranes following the manufacturer’s instructions (R&D Systems, MN, USA). The measurement of the signal was performed according to the instructions of the manufacturer and the pixel density of each array spot was analyzed using ImageJ software version 1.54p. (National Institutes of Health).

Hypoxia induction

Hypoxia was induced using two complementary approaches: 1% O2 incubation and CoCl2 treatment. For physiological hypoxia, cells were cultured in a hypoxia chamber maintained at 1% O2, 5% CO2, and 94% N2 for the indicated time points. Alternatively, chemical hypoxia was induced by treating cells with 100–200 μM CoCl2 for 16–24 h to stabilize HIF-1α. Both methods were used to ensure consistency in hypoxic responses across experiments.

Bisulfite conversion and pyrosequencing for DNA methylation analysis

Genomic DNA (1 μg) was subjected to sodium bisulfite conversion using the EZ DNA Methylation-Gold Kit (Zymo Research, Irvine, CA), following the manufacturer’s instructions. Converted DNA was eluted in 40 μL of M-Elution Buffer, and 2 μL of this was used per PCR reaction. All bisulfite treatment and subsequent pyrosequencing were performed by the DNA Methylation Analysis Core Facility at The University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. PCR primers for pyrosequencing-based methylation analysis of BARD1 were designed using PyroMark Assay Design Software 2.0 (Qiagen, Germany). Briefly, a sequencing primer was selected within 1–5 base pairs of the targeted CpG sites, with an optimal annealing temperature of 40 ± 5°C. Forward and reverse primers were subsequently positioned upstream and downstream of the sequencing primer, respectively. All primer sequences and targeted regions are listed in Table S3. Gradient PCR was used to determine the ideal annealing temperature for each primer set. Each reaction included controls representing high methylation (SssI-treated DNA), low methylation (whole genome amplified DNA), and no-template controls. PCR amplifications were carried out in 20 μL reactions, and the full volume was used for pyrosequencing, as previously described. PCR products were purified using streptavidin-coated Sepharose high-performance beads (GE Healthcare Life Sciences, Piscataway, NJ), and sequencing primers (3.6 pmol per reaction) were annealed to the biotinylated PCR products per the PSQ96 sample preparation protocol. Sequencing was performed on the PyroMark Q96 ID system using PyroMark Gold Q96 reagents (Qiagen, Germany), according to the manufacturer’s protocol. Methylation levels at individual CpG sites were quantified using PyroMark Q96 software, and average methylation across all CpG sites and technical replicates was reported for each sample.

Plasmid design and construction of CRISPR/dCas9-based epigenetic editing system

The CRISPR/dCas9-based epigenetic editing system was constructed using the Fuw-dCas9-Tet1CD plasmid (Addgene plasmid #84475), which encodes a catalytically inactive Cas9 fused to the catalytic domain of Tet1, as previously described. This plasmid was obtained from Addgene (http://www.addgene.org/). Single guide RNA (sgRNA) expression vectors were generated by annealing BARD1 target-specific oligonucleotides and inserting them into the BsmBI restriction site of a modified gRNA backbone (Addgene plasmid #52963). The resulting gRNA scaffold was first cloned into the lentiGuide-Puro vector and subsequently subcloned into the Fuw-dCas9-Tet1CD plasmid to enable co-expression of the gRNA and the dCas9-Tet1CD fusion protein in a single vector. All constructs were verified by Sanger sequencing prior to transfection. Primer sequences and additional details for gRNA design and cloning are provided in Table S3.

Targeted demethylation liposomal delivery system

DOPC and dCas9-TET1-sgRNA-BARD1 were mixed in the presence of excess tertiary butanol at a ratio of 1:10 (w/w) DNA/DOPC. Tween 20 was added to the mixture in a ratio of 1:19 Tween 20:DNA/DOPC. The mixture was vortexed, frozen in an acetone/dry ice bath, and lyophilized. Before in vivo administration, this preparation was hydrated with phosphate-buffered saline without Ca+ and Mg2+ at a concentration of 50 or 25 μg/mL to achieve the desired dose of 100 μL per injection.

Quantification and statistical analyses

All in vitro experiments were done at least in technical triplicates with differences by group assessed by the Student’s t test or analysis of variance (for comparison of all groups). For RNA sequencing, first, the raw read count was normalized (mainly to correct the sequencing depth); next, the statistical model was used to calculate the hypothesis test’s probability (p value). Finally, multiple hypothesis test corrections were used to obtain false discovery rate values. For in vivo experiments, tumor weight was analyzed by one-way analysis of variance, and groups were compared by analysis of variance while tumor volume across the four experimental conditions was evaluated using two-way ANOVA. GraphPad Prism software (version 8.0.0) was used for all analyses. All data are presented as mean ± SD or mean ± SEM and two-sided unless otherwise indicated. The p values were two-tailed, and values less than 0.05 were considered significant. All statistical details including the tests used, exact number (n) values (e.g., n of animals or replicates), definitions of center, and measures of variation can be found in the corresponding figure legends.
