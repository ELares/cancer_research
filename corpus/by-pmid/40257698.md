# Mapping the landscape of metabolic reprogramming research in lung cancer: a bibliometric and visualized analysis.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Lung cancer is the leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide, with a poor prognosis despite advancements in diagnosis and treatment. Metabolic reprogramming, a key feature of cancer, allows tumor cells to survive and grow under harsh conditions, making it a crucial area of study. This study provides a comprehensive analysis of global trends, influential studies, and key developments in this field.

**METHODS**: We conducted a bibliometric analysis using the Web of Science Core Collection (WoSCC) database from 2004 to 2024. Publications related to metabolic reprogramming and lung cancer were retrieved and analyzed using VOSviewer and CiteSpace to examine publication trends, research collaborations, keyword co-occurrence, and citation networks.

**RESULTS**: A total of 1078 publications were analyzed, with research output increasing significantly after 2015. China and the United States were the leading contributors, engaging in extensive international collaborations. Pioneering studies by researchers such as Ralph J. DeBerardinis and Otto Warburg underscored the importance of altered metabolism in lung cancer. Key emerging topics included the role of cancer stem cells, changes in tumor metabolism, and new treatment approaches targeting metabolic pathways. The integration of laboratory research with clinical applications, including novel drugs and immunotherapies, demonstrated promising directions for future treatments.

**CONCLUSIONS**: This bibliometric analysis maps the research landscape of metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer, identifying influential contributors and emerging research themes. Future studies should explore advanced technologies like single-cell analysis and investigate how metabolic changes are regulated at the molecular level. A deeper understanding of these processes could lead to innovative treatment strategies and better patient outcomes.

Introduction

Lung cancer remains the leading cause of cancer-related mortality worldwide, contributing significantly to the global health and economic burden. Despite advancements in diagnostic methods, targeted therapies, and immunotherapies, the prognosis for lung cancer patients continues to be poor, with a 5-year survival rate of less than 20%. The aggressive nature of lung cancer, coupled with its late-stage diagnosis and resistance to conventional treatments, underscores the urgent need to identify novel molecular mechanisms and therapeutic strategies.

Metabolic reprogramming has emerged as a pivotal hallmark of cancer, including lung cancer. Unlike normal cells, which rely primarily on oxidative phosphorylation for energy production, cancer cells rewire their metabolism to meet their elevated demands for energy, biosynthetic precursors, and redox balance. This metabolic adaptability allows cancer cells to thrive under challenging conditions, such as hypoxia and nutrient deprivation, and contributes to tumor initiation, progression, and metastasis. A well-known example is the Warburg effect, wherein cancer cells preferentially utilize aerobic glycolysis, even in the presence of oxygen. Beyond glycolysis, metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer involves alterations in lipid synthesis and uptake, amino acid metabolism, and mitochondrial dynamics. These metabolic pathways not only support the rapid growth and proliferation of cancer cells but also enhance their ability to evade immune responses and resist therapy.

The study of these metabolic pathways has revealed intricate regulatory mechanisms involving genetic and environmental factors. Oncogenes such as MYC and KRAS, tumor suppressors like p53, and microenvironmental conditions such as hypoxia drive metabolic alterations in lung cancer cells. Moreover, specific metabolites, including lactate and succinate, have been identified as signaling molecules that promote tumor progression and modulate the immune landscape. These findings highlight the critical role of metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer biology and its potential as a therapeutic target. Several metabolic inhibitors, including glycolysis blockers and fatty acid synthase inhibitors, have been investigated in preclinical and clinical studies, offering hope for more effective treatment options.

Given the growing body of research on metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer, it was essential to systematically assess the current state of this field. Bibliometric analysis, a method of quantitatively evaluating scientific publications, provided valuable insights into research trends, influential studies, and emerging hotspots. By mapping the knowledge landscape, bibliometric studies helped identify gaps in the literature and guided future research directions.

This study aimed to explore the global research trends in metabolic reprogramming and lung cancer through a bibliometric and visualized analysis of publications over the past two decades. Using data from the Web of Science Core Collection (WoSCC) database, the analysis examined publication outputs, keyword co-occurrences, and citation networks to identify key contributors, influential studies, and research hotspots. By synthesizing these findings, the study provided a comprehensive overview of the field and offered insights into potential directions for advancing both basic and translational research in metabolic reprogramming and lung cancer.

Materials and methods

Data source and search strategy

This bibliometric analysis utilized data from the Web of Science Core Collection, a comprehensive database widely used for high-quality bibliometric studies. The search strategy was designed to identify publications related to metabolic reprogramming and lung cancer.

The following search query was used: (TS = (metabolic reprogramming) OR TS = (metabolic remodelling) OR TS = (metabolic rewriting) OR TS = (reprogramming of metabolism)) AND (TS = (lung cancer) OR TS = (lung neoplasm) OR TS = (pulmonary neoplasm) OR TS = (lung tumor) OR TS = (cancer of lung) OR TS = (lung adenocarcinoma) OR TS = (lung squamous cell carcinoma) OR TS = (adenocarcinoma of the lung) OR TS = (bronchial neoplasm) OR TS = (pancoast syndrome) OR TS = (non-small cell lung cancer) OR TS = (NSCLC) OR TS = (non-small cell lung carcinoma) OR TS = (non-small lung cancer) OR TS = (none small cell lung cancer) OR TS = (large cell lung cancer) OR TS = (SCLC) OR TS = (small-cell lung cancer) OR TS = (small-cell lung carcinoma)). (NSCLC: Non-small cell lung carcinoma; SCLC: small-cell lung cancer).

The search was limited to publications classified as articles or reviews, written in English, and published between January 1, 2004, and September 20, 2024. Duplicate records, conference proceedings, editorials, and non-English publications were excluded.

Data processing and analysis

Bibliographic data, including titles, authors, journals, publication years, countries, institutions, citations, and keywords, were extracted from the WoSCC and exported in plain text format. Duplicate and irrelevant records were removed, and inconsistencies in author names or institutional affiliations were standardized.

Bibliometric analyses were performed using VOSviewer (version 1.6.19) and CiteSpace (version 6.1.6). The analysis focused on publication trends, geographic and institutional contributions, co-authorship networks, keyword co-occurrence, and citation patterns to identify research hotspots and influential contributors. Network maps and cluster analyses were generated to visualize relationships between authors, keywords, and cited references. Emerging trends and research themes were identified through keyword burst analysis in CiteSpace.

Discussion

This bibliometric analysis provided a comprehensive overview of the research landscape on metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer over the past two decades. By analyzing publication trends, collaboration networks, and thematic clusters, the study elucidated the rapid expansion and diversification of this interdisciplinary field. The findings revealed a shift from foundational studies on cancer metabolism to an emphasis on molecular mechanisms, therapeutic interventions, and translational research. This evolution reflects the growing recognition of metabolic reprogramming as a critical driver of tumor progression and a promising target for lung cancer therapy.

Summary of key findings

Metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer is driven by a dynamic interplay of genetic, epigenetic, and environmental factors. Early studies focused on the"Warburg Effect"laid the groundwork for understanding altered glucose metabolism in cancer. More recently, research has expanded to include glutamine metabolism, lipid biosynthesis, and mitochondrial reprogramming. These metabolic adaptations enable lung cancer cells to sustain their aggressive growth, evade immune responses, and resist therapies.

The bibliometric analysis highlighted several influential contributors and collaborations that have advanced the field. Leading authors, such as Ralph J. DeBerardinis and Matthew G. Vander Heiden, have played pivotal roles in elucidating the metabolic underpinnings of lung cancer. Journals like Cancer Research, Nature, and Science have served as key platforms for disseminating high-impact studies. Emerging research themes, including cancer stem cells, metabolic plasticity, and immune-metabolic crosstalk, were identified as critical areas of ongoing exploration.

The translational potential of metabolic reprogramming is increasingly evident. Novel therapeutic strategies targeting glycolysis, glutaminolysis, and mitochondrial function are currently being evaluated in preclinical and clinical settings. Furthermore, integrating metabolic inhibitors with immune checkpoint blockade represents a promising avenue for overcoming immune suppression and enhancing therapeutic efficacy.

Metabolic reprogramming and cancer biology

Metabolic reprogramming represents a hallmark of cancer, enabling tumor cells to adapt to their microenvironment and meet the demands of rapid proliferation. The"Warburg Effect,"one of the earliest discoveries in this field, remains foundational in understanding cancer metabolism. Beyond glycolysis, recent studies have uncovered a broader spectrum of metabolic alterations in lung cancer, including dysregulated glutamine metabolism, aberrant fatty acid synthesis, and shifts in mitochondrial dynamics.

For example, glutamine metabolism has been shown to play an indispensable role in supplying carbon and nitrogen for biosynthesis and maintaining redox homeostasis in lung cancer cells. Targeting glutaminase (GLS), a key enzyme in glutamine catabolism, has emerged as a promising therapeutic strategy. Additionally, the interplay between altered lipid metabolism and cancer progression has gained traction, with studies highlighting the role of fatty acid synthase (FASN) in promoting metastasis and therapy resistance. The integration of lipidomics into cancer research has further unraveled how lipid droplet accumulation facilitates energy storage and protects cancer cells from oxidative damage.

Molecular mechanisms driving metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer

The co-citation analysis and clustering results highlighted the involvement of oncogenes such as KRAS and MYC, as well as tumor suppressors like p53, in driving metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer. KRAS-mutated lung adenocarcinomas, for instance, exhibit enhanced dependency on glucose and glutamine pathways, making them susceptible to metabolic inhibitors targeting these pathways. Similarly, MYC amplification orchestrates a broad spectrum of metabolic adaptations, including increased glycolysis and nucleotide biosynthesis.

Moreover, the tumor microenvironment, particularly hypoxia, has emerged as a critical driver of metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer. Under hypoxic conditions, hypoxia-inducible factor 1-alpha (HIF-1α) promotes the upregulation of glycolytic enzymes and alters mitochondrial function. Recent research has also explored how lactate, once considered a metabolic waste product, acts as a signaling molecule, shaping the immune microenvironment and facilitating immune evasion.

Therapeutic potential of targeting metabolic pathways

The translational implications of metabolic reprogramming are profound, with several metabolic inhibitors progressing through preclinical and clinical evaluation. Glycolytic inhibitors, such as 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG), and fatty acid synthase inhibitors, like TVB-2640, have shown promise in disrupting the metabolic flexibility of lung cancer cells. Combining these metabolic therapies with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) represents an emerging frontier. By modulating the metabolic landscape, researchers aim to enhance immune cell infiltration and activation, overcoming immune suppression within the tumor microenvironment.

Additionally, studies have highlighted the potential of mitochondrial-targeted therapies in disrupting cancer cell metabolism. Agents that target mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), such as IACS-010759, have shown efficacy in lung cancer subtypes that rely on mitochondrial energy production. These strategies align with the observed metabolic heterogeneity in lung cancer, where distinct subsets of tumors display dependencies on glycolysis versus mitochondrial respiration. Recent advancements in nanotechnology have demonstrated the potential of self-nanoemulsifying hydrogel systems (SNEDDS), zein nanoparticles, and quantum dots (QDs) in enhancing drug delivery and cancer treatment. These approaches improve bioavailability, enable targeted drug release, and facilitate theranostic applications, offering promising strategies to enhance therapeutic efficacy and reduce toxicity.

Emerging frontiers in research

The bibliometric analysis revealed a growing focus on cancer stem cells (CSCs) in the context of metabolic reprogramming. Lung cancer stem cells are characterized by metabolic plasticity, enabling them to transition between glycolysis and OXPHOS to sustain tumor growth and resist therapy. Recent studies have identified metabolic vulnerabilities in CSCs, such as dependencies on specific amino acid pathways (e.g., serine and glycine), presenting opportunities for therapeutic intervention.

Furthermore, the integration of single-cell omics and spatial transcriptomics into metabolic research has provided unprecedented insights into the heterogeneity of metabolic reprogramming within tumors. These technologies allow researchers to map metabolic states at single-cell resolution, identifying subpopulations of cancer cells with distinct metabolic dependencies.

Limitations of the study

Despite the valuable insights provided, this bibliometric analysis has several limitations. First, the analysis was confined to publications indexed in the WoSCC, which may have excluded relevant studies from other databases, such as PubMed or Scopus. This limitation could lead to a potential bias in capturing the full breadth of research activities in this field. Second, while bibliometric tools such as CiteSpace and VOSviewer offer robust visualization of research trends, their reliance on co-citation and co-occurrence metrics may overlook nuanced qualitative aspects of individual studies. For instance, the significance of groundbreaking discoveries or the clinical impact of specific therapies may not be fully represented in network analyses. Third, this study focused exclusively on English-language publications, which may have excluded contributions from non-English-speaking researchers, particularly from regions with rapidly growing research output, such as China and South Korea. This linguistic bias may underrepresent global contributions to the field. Finally, the dynamic and evolving nature of research on metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer poses challenges in capturing the latest advancements. Studies published after the cutoff date of this analysis (September 2024) and ongoing clinical trials may yield new insights that could further refine our understanding of this complex field.

Future directions

Despite significant progress, the role of epigenetic regulation in shaping the metabolic phenotype of lung cancer cells warrants further investigation, as does the contribution of metabolic crosstalk between cancer cells and the tumor microenvironment. Understanding the systemic effects of metabolic reprogramming, such as its impact on cachexia and systemic inflammation, could also inform the development of holistic therapeutic strategies. Another emerging area is the role of non-coding RNAs, such as microRNAs and long non-coding RNAs, in regulating metabolic pathways. These molecules have been implicated in modulating enzymes and transporters involved in glycolysis, glutaminolysis, and fatty acid metabolism. Their dual roles as diagnostic biomarkers and therapeutic targets hold promise for personalized medicine in lung cancer.

As research on metabolic reprogramming continues to evolve, leveraging emerging technologies such as single-cell omics and spatial transcriptomics will be crucial for uncovering the heterogeneity of metabolic states within tumors. These insights could pave the way for the development of precision medicine approaches that target specific metabolic vulnerabilities in lung cancer. Collaborative efforts across disciplines and international borders will be essential for advancing this promising field and translating discoveries into tangible clinical benefits for patients.

Conclusions

Metabolic reprogramming in lung cancer represents a rapidly evolving field with profound implications for understanding tumor biology and developing innovative therapies. By leveraging advanced technologies and integrating insights from diverse disciplines, future research has the potential to uncover novel mechanisms and therapeutic targets. This bibliometric analysis provides a foundation for guiding these efforts, highlighting key areas of focus and emerging trends that will shape the future of metabolic research in lung cancer.
