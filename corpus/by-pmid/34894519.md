# Therapeutic potential of CAR T cell in malignancies: A scoping review.

## Abstract

Although tremendous advancements in cancer therapy over the last several years, cancer still is a complex illness to cure. Traditional cancer treatments, including chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and surgery, have a poor therapeutic effect, emphasizing the significance of employing innovative treatments like activated cell therapy. Chimeric antigen receptor T cell is one of the most prevalent types of activated cell therapy have been developed to direct T lymphocytes toward cancers (CAR-T cells). CAR-T cells therapy has illustrated poor impact versus solid tumors despite the remarkable success in patients suffering from hematological malignancies. CAR-T cells must overcome various hurdles to obtain full responses to solid tumors, including growth, stability, trafficking, and destiny inside tumors. As a result, novel treatment methods will entail overcoming the challenges that CAR-T cells face in solid tumors. The use of CAR-T cells in combination with other therapeutic approaches such as chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immuno-checkpoint inhibitors, and oncolytic viruses can promote the effectiveness of CAR-T cell therapy for the treatment of solid tumors. However, more research is needed to determine the safety and effectiveness of these therapies. CAR-T cell treatment success rates vary by type of disease, but are predicted to reach up to 90% in patients with leukemia. However, since this kind of immunotherapy is still in its infancy, there is much to learn about its efficacy. This review provided an in-depth examination of CAR-T cell therapy and its success and failure as a cancer treatment approach. We also discuss combination therapies with CAR-T Cell.

1. Introduction 
Malignancy is one of the world’s main causes of death [1], and 
despite significant advancements in cancer treatment, cancer remains a 
challenging disease [2]. 
Cancer immunoediting illustrated the interaction between the im­
mune system and cancer cells [3,4]. This process is known as adaptive 
immunity, especially T cells, engaged in the exclusion of cancer cells in 
physiological situations and is consists of three phases: elimination, 
equilibrium, and escape, respectively [4,5]. Inconsistent with the im­
mune surveillance theory, elimination stage refers to destroy developing 
tumor cells by the mechanisms of both natural and adaptive immune 
response [1,6]. As the tumor grows, it induces angiogenic mechanisms 
along with inflammation, leading to recruits of immune cells including 
dendritic cells, macrophages, γδ T-cells, NKT, and NK, and the produc­
tion of cytokines such as IFN-γ, which eventually leads to cancer cell 
death [1,7,8]. A limited number of tumor cells keep on the elimination 
stage, creating a dynamic equilibrium phase associated with immune 
response and tumor growth. The selective pressure exerted by the im­
mune system on the tumor cells is not sufficient to completely extinguish 
them [1,4]. Therefore, due to the loss of appropriate DNA damage 
checkpoints, their rapid proliferation and practical strategies to escape 
the immune system, malignant cells are developed [1,4]. Equilibrium 
may persist until the clinical manifestation of the tumor [1]. As a result 
of the epigenetic and genetic alterations in the cancer cell, the tumors 
develop resistance to immune detection, allowing them to become 
clinically recognizable [1,4]. 
Further inhibition of the immune surveillance will also occur by 
forming a tumor microenvironment, inhibiting the immune system’s 
ability to perform immune editing [1]. Comprehension all the activities 
that guide to immunologic tumor rejection is of key importance in

with other therapeutic approaches. This review aims to overview CAR T 
cell therapy and address its limitations in advancing cancer therapy. We 
also point to the combination therapies with CAR-T cell therapy. 
2. CAR-T cell structure 
CAR comprises three major components: an extracellular domain, a 
transmembrane domain, and an intracellular domain [34,35]. The 
extracellular domain consists of signal peptide, a tumor-associated an­
tigen (TAA) recognition region, and spacer. One characteristic feature of 
the extracellular domain is the single-chain variable fragment (scFv) 
region, which is similar to the variable part of heavy (VH) and light (VL) 
chains of the region of an antibody fused through a flexible linker [19, 
36,37]. The spacer is necessary to connect the antigen recognition re­
gion to the transmembrane portion, which is an alpha helix within the 
cell membrane that links the extracellular antigen-binding domain to 
the intracellular cytoplasmic domain [38]. 
The intracellular domain of the CAR is its functional terminus, which 
generally consists of activation and co-stimulatory domains. ITAMs 
(immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs) are the most abun­
dant intracellular domain component found in the cytoplasmic domain 
of CD3 [38]. In particular, when an antigen recognition domain interacts 
with an antigen, an activation signal is sent to the T cell. Additionally, 
effective T cell activity requires co-stimulatory signaling [39]. 
CAR T cells have been categorized into fifth distinct generations 
depending on the shape of the intracellular domain (Fig. 1). Commonly, 
the majority of effort in CAR engineering has been directed on under­
standing the effects of CAR co-stimulation in order to optimize the 
intracellular domain of CAR structures [40]. 
2.1. First-generation 
The initial version of CARs was designed with just the CD3 chain 
from the CD3 TCR or Fc receptor -chain (FcR), linked to an external scFv 
to give a signal for T cells to activate. Furthermore, simply communi­
cating with these areas is insufficient to produce efficient T cell re­
sponses or persistent cytokine production [41,42]. As a result, this 
generation of CARs was phased out owing to a lack of sufficient signaling 
capacity, durability, and antitumor effectiveness in vivo [38].

2.2. Second-generation 
In the early 2000 s, with the understanding of the significance of co- 
stimulation for lasting CAR-T cell therapy, second-generation CARs with 
one co-stimulatory domain such as CD28 or 4–1BB (CD137) in series 
with the CD3ζ intracellular domain were generated [43]. The presence 
of these intracellular signaling domains improves T-cell persistence, 
cytokine secretion, anti-tumor efficacy in pre-clinical models [44], and 
have been confirmed to mediate strong anti-tumor efficacy in patients 
with B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia and Non-Hodgkin lymphoma 
in clinical trials [21,45,46]. 
2.3. Third-generation 
The third-generation CARs were generated by combining multiple 
co-stimulatory domains, such as CD28-41BB or CD28-OX40, to enhance 
CAR-T cells potency with more substantial cytokine production, anti- 
tumor ability, and increase T-cell proliferation [47]. Moreover, some 
preclinical studies of the third-generation CARs have been reported to 
enhance efficacy, proliferation, and cytokine production in the clinic as 
compared to second-generation CARs. However, there is contradictory 
evidence on whether second- or third-generation CAR T-cells generate 
stronger responses in patients [48]. 
2.4. Fourth-generation 
A fourth-generation (also known as TRUCKs), a kind of T cell-based 
immunotherapy, was created by using newer, more transgenic, genetic 
modifications comprising transgenes for cytokine release and other co- 
stimulatory ligands [49]. As a result, immunostimulatory cytokines 
such as IL-2 enhance CAR-T cell resistance to the immunosuppressive 
tumor microenvironment, as well as their function and growth. Addi­
tionally, these cytokines activate and recruit the innate immune system 
to the tumor location [50]. 
2.5. Fifth-generation 
The fifth generation CAR-T cells contain an extra intracellular 
domain than their predecessors. The CARs comprise truncated intra­
cellular domains of cytokine receptors (e.g., IL-2R chain fragment) with 
a motif for binding transcription factors such as STAT-3/5. The secreted 
signal thus not only drives CAR-T cells to remain active and generate 
memory T cells, but it also reactivates and stimulates the immune system 
[51]. 
3. CAR T cell therapy 
This treatment involves genetically modifying the autologous T-cells 
to express reconstruction of the CAR receptor for tumor-specific antigens 
[52]. CARs are fusion proteins comprising the antigen-recognition 
component of a monoclonal antibody with the intracellular signaling 
domains of one or more T-cell receptors [16,41,53,54]. Generally, 
CAR-T cell therapy can take several weeks. Leukapheresis is used to 
separate autologous T-cells from the blood [55], which are then genet­
ically manipulated ex vivo through viral [56] and non-viral transfection 
methods (Fig. 2). CAR-T cells are multiplied in the laboratory after being 
generated to produce a large number of CAR-T cells for this treatment. 
Patient gets a short course of lymphodepleting chemotherapy, which 
creates a favorable immunological milieu for transplanted CAR-T cells. 
Finally, CAR T cells are infused back into the patient’s circulation [1]. 
They can aid to capture a particular antigen on tumor cells and destroy 
them once they start binding with cancer cells. These cells kill target 
cells via cytotoxicity, widespread cell proliferation, and enhanced pro­
duction of cytokines, interleukins, and growth factors [57].

4.1. Antigen escape 
Antigen escape and downregulation have emerged as critical con­
cerns for the long-term viability of CAR T-cell therapy [60]. Although 
CAR-T cells, which target a single antigen, can provide substantial 
response rates at first, cancer cells in a considerable number of patients 
who underwent CAR-T therapy showed partial or full loss of the 
expression of the target antigen [22,23,61]. When sufficient 
tumor-specific antigen is presented on cancer cells, CAR-T cells become 
activated and destroy the cancer cells. Tumor cells can evade killing by 
producing antigens that lack the extracellular epitopes recognized by 
CAR-T cells [60]. Patients with multiple myeloma (MM) who received 
CAR-T treatment that targeted B cell maturation antigen (BCMA) had 
less or no B cell maturation antigen (BCM) [62]. 
Further, solid tumors show similar patterns of antigen escape resis­
tance [63]. Targeting multiple antigens is becoming a popular technique 
for decreasing the recurrence rate in CAR-T cell-treated cancer patients. 
Dual CAR constructs or tandem CARs are used, and single CAR con­
structs containing two scFvs are used to target multiple tumor antigens 
simultaneously [64]. 
4.2. CAR T cell toxicities 
Despite the tremendous achievements of CAR T cell therapy, several 
limitations must be highlighted, including the occurrence of both 
expected and unexpected toxicities, for example immune effector cell- 
associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS), which is a neuropsychi­
atric syndrome and cytokine release syndrome (CRS) [65]. 
CRS is an inflammatory disease that results in increasing pro- 
inflammatory cytokines like soluble interleukin-2 receptor alpha (IL- 
2Ra), IL-10, IL-6, and interferon-gamma (IFN-γ) [66]. Clinical and test 
findings vary from mild cold-like sickness to severe CRS multiorgan 
failure that may be fatal. CRS often manifests up during the first several 
weeks after CAR T cells injection, at the peak of T cell growth [67]. CRS 
is the most often seen severe adverse event in individuals treated with 
CAR T cells. In general, CRS is linked to the expansion and maturation of 
adoptively transplanted T cells, as well as their increased immunological 
response. As a result, it seems as if there is a correlation between CRS 
and CAR T cell effectiveness in patients receiving inadequate therapy. 
Although CRS is the most common form of toxicity associated with CAR 
treatment, its prevalence varies by illness type. CRS is more prevalent in 
ALL patients (77–93%) than in lymphoma patients (37–93%) [67,68]. 
ICANS is another frequent toxicity in patients treated with CAR-T cells, 
and its features includes confusion, delirium, aphasia, myoclonus, and 
seizure [69]. The incidence of ICANS differs between diseases for 
example, ICANS rates estimated 40–62% for patients with ALL [67,68] 
and 23–67% for patients with lymphoma [20,70,71]. The emergence of 
neurologic toxicities in a significant number of individuals treated with 
CAR-T cell therapy directed against CD19. Even though there is no ev­
idence of CD19 expression in the afflicted brain regions, there is evi­
dence of CAR T cell infiltration into the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) [72, 
73]. As a result, elucidating the mechanism behind these neurologic side 
effects will be critical for developing a low-risk and regulated CAR T-cell 
therapy [73]. In recent years, substantial progress has been achieved in 
the treatment of CRS and ICANS caused by CAR T cell therapy [74]. 
After CAR T cell infusion, IL-6 induces severe CRS, and the FDA has 
authorized tocilizumab, an IL-6 receptor blocker, to cure CRS [75].

development of CRS and ICANS. While an IL-1 receptor antagonist (also

4.3. Antigen heterogeneity 
The first step for successful cell therapy is to select an appropriate 
target antigen, but because tumor cells have high antigenic heteroge­
neity, the recognition of the target cell by T cells is impaired. Moreover, 
the expression of TAAs is reduced by tumor cells after the CAR T cell 
therapy [77,78]. Production of CAR T cells that express two or more 
specific CARs on their surface that can recognize more than one TAA, or 
using CAR T cells targeting cancer stem cells, can be proper methods to 
overcome this challenge [22,79–81]. 
4.4. Trafficking of the CAR T cells into solid tumors 
Even if antigen heterogeneity challenges are resolved in solid tu­
mors, infiltration of these cells into the tumor site is necessary to elicit an 
effective response. Unfortunately, in solid tumors, due to insufficient 
expression of adhesion molecules in both endothelial cells and CAR T 
cells, mismatch of chemokines expression at the tumor site and che­
mokine receptors on the surface of CAR T cells, and the presence of 
dense tumor-associated fibroblasts, the infiltration of the T cells is 
difficult, and this challenge reduces the chance of successful CAR T cell 
therapy [82,83]. This challenge can be overcome by producing CAR T 
cells expressing matched chemokine receptors, intratumoral injection of 
CAR T cells, and producing T cells that produce extracellular matrix 
degrading enzymes [84–86]. 
4.5. Immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) 
If the challenges associated with CAR T cell infiltration into the 
tumor microenvironment are resolved, this cell will be exposed to an 
immunosuppressive microenvironment after entering the tumor site. 
There are several factors involved in creating this immunosuppressive 
environment, which fall into four groups: (1) Immunosuppressive sol­
uble factors such as prostaglandin E2, adenosine, and inhibitory cyto­
kines such as TGF-β, IL10, and IL-4, (2) Immunosuppressive cells such as 
Tregs, M2 macrophages, myeloid-derived suppressor cells, and N2 
neutrophils, (3) Expression of inhibitory molecules on the surface of 
CAR T cells, such as PD-1, CTLA-4, LAG3 and TIM3 and (4) immuno­
suppressive metabolic profiles due to hypoxia, nutrient starvation and 
the presence of indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase [87–89]. 
5. Combination therapy of CAR-T cells 
CAR-T cells must overcome many hurdles in order to produce 
maximum and sustained responses in patients with solid malignancies 
[90]. Consequently, except the adjustments of CAR T cells, many studies 
have paid attention to the combination therapy in order to increase the 
usefulness of CAR T cells in treating solid tumors [91]. 
5.1. Combination with oncolytic viruses 
Oncolytic viruses (OVs) are anti-tumor agents that are lytic and 
immunogenic, selectively infecting and killing tumor cells [92,93]. 
Oncolytic virotherapy has received increased interest in cancer treat­
ment after the FDA approval of Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC) [94]. 
T-VEC is the only FDA-approved oncolytic virus produced from herpes 
simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) for unrespectable cutaneous, subcutane­
ous, and nodal metastases in patients with melanoma [95]. By and large, 
OVs exert their antitumor impact through two distinct mechanisms: 
direct lysis of infected cancer cells (oncolysis) and development of 
antitumor adaptive immunity. Additionally, OV may be genetically 
engineered to express numerous transgenes in the tumor microenvi­
ronment, enhancing tumor-specific T cells’ effector activities. As a 
result, OV has the potential to synergize with a variety of combinatorial 
immunotherapy methods [96]. OVs have an exceptional ability to syn­
ergize with CAR-T cells, assisting them in overcoming solid malig­
nancies. To begin, viruses emit danger signals that may activate 
anti-tumor immunity and reverse immunosuppression, promoting the 
trafficking of CAR-T cells and tumor invasion. Second, the oncolytic 
process produces TAAs, which may trigger an adaptive immune 
response, thus reducing tumor immune escape through antigen loss. 
Third, combining OVs and CAR-T cells may enhance T cells’ effector 
activities for tumor suppression and prolongation of life [97,98]. 
Additionally, the engineering flexibility of OVs and CAR T cells enables 
the most suitable combinations of numerous transgenes and 
co-stimulation of CAR T cells to be tailored to the particular features of 
the targeted tumor and tumor microenvironment [97]. Numerous pre­
clinical experiments have been conducted to determine the efficacy of 
different transgene-armed OV in conjunction with CAR-T cells [99]. The 
findings of these studies provide light on the anti-cancer properties of 
CAR-T cells when combined with OV transgene delivery. Despite 
encouraging preclinical findings, only one authorized clinical study 
(NCT03740256) examined the combination of CAR T and OV treatment 
for HER2-positive malignancies [100]. Translation of preclinical 
experimental results to clinical trials is often difficult due to using 
immunodeficient NOD scid gamma (NSG) mice as preclinical models 
[101]. The results of this experimental system cannot illustrate the 
actual interactions between OV and CAR T cells in the human immune 
system. In addition, the prediction of the safety of combining two potent 
proinflammatory immunotherapies is difficult. Although the general 
value of TVEC is widely accepted, armed OVs designed to improve T cell 
function could increase the severity of CAR-T cell side effects [97]. 
Therefore, it is critical to establish safe doses of these therapeutic agents 
in combination with each other. 
5.2. Combination with chemotherapy and radiotherapy 
It has been shown that chemotherapeutic agents such as cyclophos­
phamide, doxorubicin, fluorouracil, and etc., can improve the effec­
tiveness of CAR T cell therapy by various mechanisms [91,102]. One of 
these mechanisms is to increase the infiltration of these cells into solid 
tumors, possibly by increasing the production of chemokines at the 
tumor site that match with chemokine receptors on CAR T cells [103]. 
The other way is to sensitize the tumor cells to granzyme B, i.e., the 
granzyme B freed by the CAR T cells can more easily enter the layer of 
tumor cells. The deliverance of antigens is increased by chemotherapy. It 
also makes it easier for CAR T cells to identify them [104]. 
Many researches have paid attention to the synergistic effects of 
radiotherapy with CAR T cell therapy. Because radiotherapy can in­
crease the release of antigens or even increase the existence of tumor 
antigens by MHC I molecules which are expressed on the surface of 
tumor cells after radiation [39]. Radiotherapy can also increase the 
release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) at the tumor 
site, which in turn can enhance the expression of chemokines and 
facilitate the infiltration of CAR T cells. On the other hand, following the 
release of DAMPs, innate immune cells are activated and can play a role 
in activation of CAR T cells [105,106].

etc. On the one hand, the use of monoclonal antibodies against these 
molecules (immune checkpoint blockade) as monoimmunotherapy has 
shown strong anti-tumor effects and has been approved by the FDA 
[107]. On the other hand, recent reports have shown that combining 
these ICBs with CAR T cells can dramatically increase the effectiveness 
of CAR T cell therapy. These antibodies can prevent exhaustion in CAR T

6. CAR-T cell clinical trials 
6.1. Hematological malignancies 
Clinical research of CAR treatments for cancer has been expanding at 
a breakneck pace [109]. Over 450 clinical studies using CAR-T cells are 
now underway (clinicaltrials.gov). The bulk of these clinical evaluations 
are for hematological cancers. 165 of these clinical trials were phase II 
investigations, while three trials progressed to phase III. The following 
table summarizes the details of phase III clinical studies. Across all 
clinical trials, CD19 antigen, a glycoprotein expressed on the surface of B 
lymphocytes as well as B cell leukemias and lymphomas, has garnered 
the most attention as a potential target for CAR-T cell therapy in the 
treatment of B-cell cancers such as acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) 
and diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) [110–112]. CAR-T clinical 
studies have shown up to 90% survival rates in end-stage patients with 
ALL [111]. (Table 1). 
These studies ultimately resulted in the FDA approval of the first 
CAR-T treatment for ALL, tisagenlecleucel-T (Kymriah, Novartis) [60, 
61]. Furthermore, axicabtagene ciloleucel (YesCAR Ta, Kite Phama) is 
the second CAR-T cell therapy to be authorized by the FDA for the 
treatment of aggressive B-cell non-lymphoma Hodgkin’s [113,114]. 
Because these FDA-approved CAR-T cell treatments used retroviral 
vector-mediated gene transfer, there are concerns regarding delayed 
adverse effects associated with insertional mutagenesis. Because these 
treatments carry the potential of serious side effects like as neurotoxicity 
and cytokine release syndrome, the FDA requires post-marketing sur­
veillance to assess the long-term risk and safety of second malignancies 
[109]. Recently, various research have focused on the survivability of 
additional antigens, including CD20. CD20 and CD19 have a same 
expression profile, with the exception that CD20 is not expressed in 
pro-B cells. CD20 was being identified as a useful target for B-cell NHL 
treatment due to its presence in about 90% of B-cell lymphomas [112]. 
Additionally, there are significant current efforts to develop CARs tar­
geting other hematological malignancy antigens such as, CD123, CD33, 
and FLT3 in acute myeloid leukemia (AML), CD30 in refractory Hodg­
kin’s lymphoma, and BCMA in MM [109,115,116]. CAR-T cell treatment 
logistics are complex, since cell products must be transported among 
hospitals and companies in a timely and long-distance manner. As a 
result, using commercial CAR-T cells in the therapy of human cancer 
necessitates careful planning.

7. Conclusions 
CAR-T cells are self-replicating cells that have made significant 
strides in the treatment of malignancies. The advancement of CAR T cell 
treatment is an example of translating fundamental research into clinical 
practice. In recent years, new generations of CAR-T cells have generated 
a great lot of excitement in the field of cancer treatment. While CAR T- 
cell treatment seems to have the potential to eventually replace the 
majority of transplants, it is now only approved to treat patients who do 
not react to transplants or who have post-transplant recurrence. 
Generally, the usefulness of CAR-T cells for cancer therapy is dependent 
on the cell’s capacity to be manufactured effectively and administered 
safely. Despite recent success in many clinical studies, CAR-T cell 
treatment has resulted in substantial morbidity and death from related 
toxicities. The cytokine release syndrome and neurotoxicity are the 
primary reasons for the clinic’s limited usage of CAR-T cell treatment. 
With a greater knowledge of the mechanism of toxicity, substantial 
advances in CAR-T cell therapy toxicity control may be made. While 
CAR treatment has shown significant effectiveness in hematological 
malignancies, its main goal is to investigate its potential in non- 
hematological tumors. It seems as if novel immunotherapy methods 
will be required to overcome the many obstacles that CAR-T cell treat­
ment faces in solid tumors. Chemotherapy, radiotherapy, immune 
checkpoint inhibitors, and oncolytic viruses are anti-tumor medicines 
that have the potential to work in conjunction with CAR-T cell therapy 
in the treatment of solid tumors. Nonetheless, with such a variety of 
oncolytic viruses available, it does not seem to be simple to determine 
which ones are appropriate for use in conjunction with CAR treatment. 
Generally, success rates of CARs vary depending on the type of cancer. 
However, this type of immunotherapy is still very new, so there is still 
much to learn about how well it works.
