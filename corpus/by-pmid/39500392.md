# Mini and enhanced CRISPR activators for cancer therapies.

## Abstract

**INTRODUCTION**: The RNA-guided nuclease Cas9 can be used as a programmable transcription activator, but there is still room for improvement in its effectiveness in eukaryotes, and its potential in cancer genetic therapy has been poorly investigated.

**OBJECTIVES**: We aim to construct optimized CRISPRa tools and detect their potential role in cancer therapy by screening 9aa-TAD.

**METHODS**: We selected a range of transcriptional coactivators for programmable activation and analyzed their effects on the expression of multiple endogenous genes using Flow cytometry and qRT-PCR. In order to improve the activation capacity of the CRISPRa tool, we fused the coactivators with the efficient dCas9-VPR system to construct a new activation system. Utilize RNA-seq to assess the activation specificity of genome-wide. To evaluate the value of the newly constructed activation system in cancer gene therapy, we activated the expression of the tumor suppressor genes PER2 and ZNF382, and performed changes in cancer cell proliferation qRT-PCR and clonal formation analysis.

**RESULTS**: In this study, we screened the NHR module from C. elegans, which demonstrated a high transcription activation capacity with a compact size compared to VP64. We successfully demonstrated its efficiency in activating endogenous genes in mammalian cells. Furthermore, we developed an enhanced fused variant called NHR-VP64-p65-Rta (NVPR), which showed even higher efficiency compared to the previously established VPR module, making it an effective CRISPRa tool. The dCas9-NVPR complex also exhibited high specificity on a genome-wide scale. Finally, we utilized the dCas9-NVPR tool to restore the expression of tumor suppressor genes PER2 and ZNF382, effectively inhibiting the malignant phenotype of cancer cells.

**CONCLUSION**: We have successfully developed and demonstrated a breakthrough CRISPRa tool with promising implications for cancer genetic therapy. This innovation expands the range of available gene editing tools and further validates the immense potential of CRISPR-based approaches in precision medicine.

Introduction

The Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeat (CRISPR) system offers a convenient method for reprogramming and targeting a diverse array of DNA or RNA sequences through base pairing with a guide RNA molecule. The CRISPR system has emerged as a groundbreaking genome editing technology, surpassing the earlier Zinc-finger nucleases (ZFN) and Transcription activator-like effector nucleases (TALEN) technologies, and has opened up new avenues for precise genetic modifications. The continued emergence of Cas9 mutants continues to broaden its scope of applications. For example, dead Cas9 (dCas9), an enzymatically inactive variant of Cas9, has proven to be a powerful tool for genetic regulation in mammalian systems. It enables the development of CRISPR activation/interference (CRISPRa/i) tools, offering precise control over gene expression.

The CRISPRa tool primarily consists of three components: dCas9, gRNA, and activation modules. By fusing activating effectors to the dCas9/gRNA system, specifically targeting gene promoters, the transcriptional activation can be significantly enhanced. This is achieved by consecutively recruiting multiple functional molecules, amplifying the activation effect. Kemaladewi et al. upregulated the expression of the Lama1 gene in mice and prevented muscle fibrosis through AAV carrying SadCa9-2xVP64. Zhang et al. demonstrated that BCL-2 and B3GNT2 play crucial roles in conferring cancer cell resistance to cytotoxic T cells through CRISPRa screening. Despite this significant finding, there is still a need for further exploration into the optimization and utilization of CRISPRa in various applications.

The first generation of dCas9 activation system was fused with one type of transcriptional activation molecule, such as VP64. After that, researchers exploited recruiting multiple activation molecules, for example the dCas9-VP64-p65-Rta (VPR) system including VP64, p65 and Rta. In comparison to dCas9-VP64, dCas9-VPR demonstrates significantly higher activation levels on endogenous genes. Moreover, even more potent synergistic activation can be achieved through the implementation of the synergistic activation mediator (SAM) or SunTag system. These systems enable the concurrent recruitment of multiple transactivation molecules, resulting in extensive activation effects across various cell types and species. Recently, Liu et al. constructed a new CRISPRa tool- dCas9-VPR-FUS IDR (dCas9-VPRF) via fusing FUS intrinsically disordered region (IDR) molecule to dCas9-VPR, which overcome the target strand bias and broadened gRNA windows through the phase separation mechanism. Nevertheless, there is still room for improvement in the capacity of the CRISPRa tool. Additionally, its molecular size should be further optimized, taking into account the cellular-delivery capacity, such as AAV-mediated transfection.

Previously, Taubert lab identified a series of Nine amino acid Trans-activation domains (9aa-TADs) with universal transcription activation throughout the evolution of eukaryotes, which are recognized by multiple mediators of transcription including MED15 and p300. Intriguingly, it was also demonstrated that these 9aa-TADs from canonical transcription factors such as p53 and SOX proteins could achieve high activation as small peptides. Considering the limitations of current CRISPRa tools in certain therapeutic applications, such as AAV-mediated gene therapy, there is a need to further compact their compact size. To address this, we propose an upgrade to the CRISPRa module by incorporating the 9aa-TADs. This enhancement aims to achieve a balance between a compact size and potent activation capacity, thus facilitating more efficient and versatile therapeutic applications.

In this study, we aimed to optimize the efficiency and AAV-mediated employment of the CRISPRa system. To achieve this, we conducted a comprehensive screening of potential candidates from the 9aa-TADs. The newly reconstructed dCas9-NVPR demonstrated remarkable efficiency and specificity in activating endogenous genes. Furthermore, it showed promising therapeutic potential in cancer genetic therapy.

Materials and Methods

Cell lines and Cell transfection

Cell lines used in this research were obtained from the ATCC. HEK293T, HeLa and HepG2 cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10 % FBS in the humidified incubator equilibrated with 5 % CO2 at 37 ℃. For transfected experiments, cells were seeded in 24-well plates (Corning, USA). Approximately 24 h after seeding, cells were transfected with polyethyienimine (Polysciences, USA) based on the manufacturer’s recommended protocol. 600  ng of Cas9 plasmid and 200  ng of sgRNA-expressing plasmid were together transfected with 50  μL of DMEM containing 2.4  μL of polyethyienimine for 24  h. And cells were cultured with fresh medium with 5  μg/ml puromycin for another 48 h, cells were then harvested.

Plasmid construction

NHR was synthesized in AZENTA. All SP-dCas9 plasmids were based on Cas9m4-VP64 (Addgene #47319). All the DNA templates were PCR amplified with PrimeSTAR HS DNA polymerase (Takara, Japan). PCR products were gel purified, digested with DpnI restriction enzyme (NEB, USA), and assembled via Gibson assembly method based on the manufacturer’s instructions. In addition，all gRNA expression plasmids were assembled using the Golden Gate method with the N20 sequence embedded in the primers, and RNF2 sgRNA expression plasmids were used as the template. The most of gRNAs used in this study are three gRNAs to ensure robust transcriptional activation, of which NEUROD1, ASCL1, and ACTC1 used a single gRNA to achieve high activation. The main primers are listed in Sup. Table S1.

Fluorescence Reporter Assay

Fluorescence reporter assays were performed by targeting all dCas9-ADs with a mixed sgRNA to a mini-CMV promoter, driving expression of a fluorescent reporter. The fluorescence was evaluated via Flow Cytometry. Briefly, the HeLa cells were seeded into 96-well plates and co-transfected with 150 ng of dCas9-ADs plasmid, 50 ng of sgRNA plasmid and 33.3 ng miniCMV-GFP plasmid with 100 μL of DMEM containing 0.7 μL of polyethyienimine. Then after 72 h of transfection, fluorescence was detected. All fluorescence was performed by BD Fortessa X-20 analysis system. All the experiments were performed in triplicates.

Analysis of quantitative reverse transcription PCR

Total cellular RNA was extracted using the Simplyp Total DNA Extraction Kit (Biospin, China) and used for the first strand cDNA synthesis with the cDNA Reverse Transcription kit (TAKARA, Japan). Approximately 500 ng of total RNA was used for each reverse transcription reaction. Quantitation of all gene transcripts was done by qPCR using BioEasy Master Mix (SYBR Green, No ROX) and a Roche Roche LC96 sequence detection system with the expression of GAPDH as an internal reference. All primer pairs used were listed within the Sup. Table S2.

Western blotting

The western blotting assay was performed as previously reported. Briefly, cellular extracts from HEK293T cells were prepared with lysis buffer (50  mM Tris-HCl, pH8.0, 150  mM NaCl, 0.5 % NP-40) for 30  min at 4 ℃ and then denatured for 10  min at 95 ℃. The cell lysates were resolved using 10 % SDS-PAGE gels and transferred onto acetate cellulose membranes. For incubation, membranes were incubated with NEUROD1 (ABclonal, dilution: 1:500, Cat: A1147, China), OCT4 (ABclonal, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: A7920, China), PER2 (Santa Cruz Biotechnology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: sc-377290,USA), GILZ (Proteintech, dilution: 1:500, Cat: 12352–1-AP, China), CRY1 (Proteintech, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 13474–1-AP, China), HBG1 (Proteintech, dilution: 1:2000, Cat: 25728–1-AP, China), HBG2 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: PA5-97822, USA), SNAIL (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 3879, USA), SLUG (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 9585, USA), ZNF382 (Proteintech, dilution: 1:2000, Cat: 26560–1-AP, China), CDK6 (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 13331, USA), MYC (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 13987, USA), N-cad (Proteintech, dilution: 1:4000, Cat: 22018–1-AP, China), β-Actin (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 4970, USA), Vinculin (Cell Signaling Technology, dilution: 1:1000, Cat: 13901, USA) or GAPDH (ABclonal, dilution: 1:10000, Cat: AC001, China) antibodies at 4 ℃ overnight followed by incubation with a secondary antibody HRP-conjugated Affinipure Goat Anti-Mouse IgG(H + L) (Proteintech, dilution: 1:10000, Cat: SA00001-1, China) or HRP-conjugated Affinipure Goat Anti--Rabbit IgG(H + L) (Proteintech, dilution: 1:10000, Cat: SA00001-2, China). Immunoreactive bands were visualized using western blotting luminal reagent (Millipore, USA) according to the manufacturer’s recommendation.

RNA-seq analysis for evaluating the specificity of dCas9-NVPR

The RNA-seq was performed according to the standard Illumina protocols by AZENTA company. For library preparation, 1 µg of total RNA per sample was used as input. Sequencing libraries were generated using the Next Ultra RNA Library Prep Kit for Illumina following the manufacturer’s protocol. Each sample was PCR amplified using P5 and P7 primers. The libraries were analyzed on the Illumina Novaseq instrument. To ensure data quality, Fastp (version 0.20.0) was used to filter out low-quality reads, trim adapters, and assess the quality of raw FASTQ files to obtain clean reads. Subsequently, clean reads were used to quantify expression levels using Htseq (version 0.6.1), with gene expression calculated in Fragments Per Kilo bases per Million reads (FPKM). A > 2 folds change and a < 0.01 adjust-P value was selected as the significance cutoffs. All the experiments were performed in twice.

Colony formation assay

The corresponding activating plasmid was transfected into HeLa and HepG2 cells, which were then plated in a 6-well plate at 1 × 103 cells per well and cultured for 14–20 days, with the medium being refreshed every 2–3 days. Surviving colonies were fixed in 4 % paraformaldehyde for 30 min, and counted after staining with Crystal Violet (Solarbio, China). The experiments were performed in triplicate wells three times.

Statistical analysis

Unless otherwise noted, all data are presented as Mean ± SD and statistical analyses were performed on at least two independent experiments. The significance of the difference between the control and experiment group was calculated via student’s t test using GraphPad Prism 8. P < 0.05 was considered to be statistically significant.

Result

Analysis of the candidate transcriptional coactivators for programmable activation

In our research, we aimed to further enhance the CRISPRa tools by selecting a range of transcriptional coactivators and analyzing their function in gene activation. We fused several 9aa-TADs, including activation domains from NHR, NF-κB, Sp1, p53, SOX2, MYC, as well as known coactivators with verified transcription activation capacity such as VP64, p65, and Rta, with dCas9 to create multiple CRISPRa variants (Figure 1A). These variants, along with an indicated gRNA targeting the miniCMV promoter for enhanced green fluorescent protein (EGFP) activation, were transfected into HeLa cells to evaluate their potency (Figure 1A-B). Our results demonstrated that the majority of CRISPRa variants effectively enhanced EGFP expression compared to the control group, with VP64, 2NHR, and Rta exhibiting the highest levels of EGFP expression (Figure 1C; Supplementary Figure S1A). Particularly intriguing was the substantial transcriptional activity displayed by the NHR from C. elegans, which had a remarkably compact protein size of only 16 amino acids (Figure 1C). Notably, the fusion of NHR with dCas9 had not been previously reported. Overall, our findings highlight the promising potential of the 9aa-TAD, NHR, which exhibited potent transcriptional activity in a compact protein size.

NHR module combined with dCas9 function as an effective activator in mammalian cells

In the subsequent phase of our study, we conducted a thorough characterization of the gene activation potential of the dCas9-NHR and dCas9-2NHR systems by targeting various endogenous loci in HEK293T cells. Our observations revealed that both dCas9-NHR and dCas9-2NHR successfully induced endogenous gene activation compared to the control group (Figure 1D). Notably, we observed a remarkable over six-fold increase in RNA expression for HBG1 and a four-fold increase for NEUROD1, compared to the control. Furthermore, we found that dCas9-NHR and dCas9-2NHR exhibited similar or even higher efficiency compared to dCas9-VP64 when targeting the majority of tested genes (Figure 1D). To further validate the potential of the NHR module in endogenous gene activation, we transfected dCas9-NHR and dCas9-2NHR along with gRNA vectors into HeLa cells. Our data demonstrated a similar gene activation trend as observed in HEK293T cells (Figure 1E). Particularly noteworthy was the significant activation of IL1B by the NHR module, resulting in a 20-fold increase compared to the control. Taken together, our findings provide strong evidence that the NHR module, in conjunction with dCas9, can be effectively employed as a programmable activator in mammalian cells.

Improved activation of endogenous genes by fusion NHR to dCas9-VPR

In our quest to enhance the capacity of CRISPRa tools, we explored the fusion of the NHR module with the highly efficient dCas9-VPR system. Considering that dCas9-NHR exhibited slightly higher efficiency and a more compact protein size compared to dCas9-2NHR, we selected NHR for the protein fusion construction. VP64, VPR, and Rta were individually replaced with the NHR module to create the dCas9-NPR, dCas9-VNR, and dCas9-VPN variants, respectively. These variants were co-transfected with a vector containing the miniCMV promoter for EGFP and sgRNA in HeLa cells to evaluate their performance. Our data demonstrated that the replacement of dCas9-VPR with the NHR module had a moderate effect on EGFP and endogenous gene activation compared to dCas9-VPR (Figure 2A-C; Supplementary figure S1B). Notably, dCas9-NPR exhibited the highest activation among the three variants, with GILZ being activated approximately 7-fold. Hela cells showed a similar trend, with dCas9-NPR leading to more potent activation compared to the other variants (Figure 2D). Furthermore, we also fused the NHR module to dCas9-VPR, generating the dCas9-NVPR, dCas9-VNPR, dCas9-VPNR, and dCas9-VPRN variants. Significantly, we discovered that dCas9-NVPR exhibited the highest EGFP activation among these variants, with approximately a 2-fold increase in fluorescence expression compared to VPR (Figure 3A-B; Supplementary figure S1C). Further, qRT-PCR analysis indicated that dCas9-NVPR significantly enhanced endogenous gene activation compared to dCas9-VPR in both HEK293T (Figure 3C) and HeLa cells (Figure 3D).In HEK293T cells, the dCas9-NVPR system significantly enhanced the activation of the NEUROD1 and ASCL1 genes compared to the tripartite dCas9-VPR variant, showing increases of 3.32-fold and 3.25-fold, respectively. Additionally, protein levels of the targeted genes were markedly elevated with the improved CRISPRa system in both HEK293T and HeLa cells, corroborating our qRT-PCR findings (Supplementary figure 2A-B). Collectively, these results clearly demonstrate that the fusion of the NHR module with dCas9-VPR further enhances the activating capacity of the CRISPRa system.

The activating effect of dCas9-NVPR was specific

To address concerns regarding potential off-target effects and widespread epigenetic changes caused by effector domains, we conducted an assessment of the targeting specificity of dCas9-NVPR. Significantly, to evaluate the specificity of dCas9-NVPR on a genome-wide scale, we performed whole-transcriptome RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) after targeting both HBG1 and HBG2 (Figure 4A). The protein and mRNA levels of HBG1 and HBG2 was firstly consolidated by qRT-PCR and western blot (Figure 4B). Then we compared the results with cells transfected with control sgRNA and observed that dCas9-NVPR exhibited the strongest activation for HBG1 and HBG2 (Figure 4C) with RNA-seq. The degree of activation and statistical significance for other potentially activated genes were not substantial compared to HBG1 and HBG2 at the transcriptional level (Figure 4C). Taken together, our findings suggest that dCas9-NVPR can specifically activate gene expression while minimizing widespread effects on the transcriptome.

Programmable activation of PER2 with dCas9-NVPR inhibits HeLa cell proliferation and metastasis

Given the robust gene activation capabilities of dCas9-NVPR in mammalian cells and the limited research on CRISPRa tools in cancer genetic therapy, we embarked on a journey to delve deeper into the therapeutic potential of the dCas9-NVPR tool for cancer treatment. The circadian clock gene PER2 was reported to serve as a tumor suppressor to regulate epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) genes in a cell-specific manner to restrict cancer development. Loss of PER2 enhances invasion and tumor cell proliferation and activates expression of EMT genes. In this study, we employed the dCas9-NVPR system to enhance the expression of PER2 in cancer cells with the aim of reducing their invasive potential. Subsequently, we conducted qRT-PCR analysis to examine the expression levels of key EMT regulators, SNAIL and SLUG. Our findings revealed that the dCas9-NVPR system effectively activated PER2 expression in cancer cells (Figure 5A), leading to corresponding changes in the expression of EMT genes (Figure 5B). The relevant protein levels were also verified using western blot (Figure 5C). To assess the impact on cancer cell proliferation, we performed colony formation analysis, which demonstrated a significant inhibition of cancer cell proliferation (Figure 5D). These results strongly support the notion that PER2 serves as a crucial tumor suppressor in tumorigenesis. The dCas9-NVPR system presents a promising therapeutic strategy by activating PER2 expression in cancer cells.

Programmable activation of ZNF382 with dCas9-NVPR suppresses hepatocellular carcinoma progression

The zinc finger protein ZNF382 has been identified as being silenced in various malignant tumors due to promoter methylation. Its silencing has been implicated in acting as a tumor suppressor in hepatocellular carcinoma by inhibiting EMT in cancer cells or promoting cell apoptosis. Hence, it could be a promising strategy to restore the ZNF382 expression via dCas9-NVPR combined with CRISPRon system in the cancer cells. Subsequently, we performed transfections of dCas9-NVPR and TETv4, along with sgRNA targeting ZNF382, into HepG2 cells (Figure 6A). The results demonstrated that the combined treatment of dCas9-NVPR and TETv4 effectively restored the expression of ZNF382 (Figure 6B), with further enhancement observed in the combined strategy. Consequently, the restoration of ZNF382 led to a notable reduction in the expression of the mesenchymal marker N-cadherin, as well as key EMT genes such as TWIST1, SLUG, and SNAIL (Figure 6C). Additionally, given that ZNF382 functions as a tumor suppressor by inducing apoptosis and inhibiting the proliferation of tumor cells. Furthermore, we conducted additional analyses to assess the impact of dCas9-NVPR on the expression of genes involved in cell cycle regulation, apoptosis, and neoplastic transformation, such as MYC, STAT3, MITF, and CDK6. Our findings revealed a downregulation of these genes (Figure 6D). Partial protein levels were also confirmed by western blot analysis (Figure 6E). Additionally, colony formation analysis demonstrated a significant inhibition of cancer cell proliferation (Figure 6F). Collectively, these results provide compelling evidence for the potential of dCas9-NVPR in cancer genetic therapy.

Discussion

CRISPR-directed gene activation has been extensively studied for functional screening and genetic therapy. However, the transactivation capacity of existing CRISPRa tools varies significantly depending on the genotype or cell lines used. Further, the employment of CRISPRa in cancer genetic therapy has not been thoroughly investigated. In this study, we identified a highly active transactivator from C. elegans that was also compatible with the current dCas9-VPR system.

The NHR transactivation module was identified from a pool of 9aa-TADs that are widely expressed across different species. Significantly, the fusion of dCas9-NHR displayed superior efficiency compared to all other 9aa variants. At the cellular level, dCas9-NHR exhibited significant transcriptional activation, albeit with variations in effectiveness across targeted genes. Notably, the NHR module demonstrated the ability to recruit multiple transcriptional cofactors, including P300 and the mediator complex, which likely contributed to its transactivation capabilities. Intriguingly, a distinct disparity in efficiency was observed between dCas9-NHR and dCas9-VP64, with the NHR module displaying higher efficacy in activating such NEUROD1, PER2 and IL1B compared to VP64. This outcome further emphasizes the functional diversity of transcriptional factors and underscores the superiority of the NHR module. Additionally, the compact size of the NHR protein may enhance its delivery efficiency for AAV-related genetic therapy, which is typically constrained by limited packaging capacity.

In our quest to enhance the CRISPRa tool, we endeavored to fuse the NHR module with the highly efficient dCas9-VPR system. Initially, we explored the possibility of omitting one component of VPR to reduce the overall size while maintaining optimal activity. However, all tripartite variants exhibited a moderate decrease in efficiency. Undeterred, we proceeded to construct a quadruple variant by incorporating the NHR module. Interestingly, we observed an overall increase in efficiency across all four variants, with dCas9-NVPR exhibiting the highest transactivation capacity among the dCas9-VPR derivatives. This finding suggests that the NHR module may engage distinct coactivators compared to VP64, p65, and Rta, thereby further enhancing the activity of CRISPRa-VPR. The observed differences among the four variants could potentially be attributed to the diverse arrangements of each module, as steric hindrance between neighboring activators may limit recruitment flexibility for the transcriptional machinery.

An important concern in genetic therapy is the safety of gene editing tools. Therefore, to further interrogate the newly constructed CRISPRa tool, transcriptome sequencing was performed to assess the specificity of dCas9-NVPR. The data confirmed a significant upregulation of HBG1/2 expression and a high correlation in gene expression between dCas9-NVPR and the control sample. This result further highlighted the potential of dCas9-NVPR for in vivo genetic therapy. The CRISPRa technology has already been employed in several hereditary diseases and has shown immense potential. We subsequently expanded the application of dCas9-NVPR to the realm of cancer genetic therapy, an area that has received limited investigation thus far. In this context, we focused on reactivating several critical tumor suppressor genes, including PER2 and ZNF382, which are frequently silenced in various cancers. Employing dCas9-NVPR, we successfully achieved upregulation of both PER2 and ZNF382 in cancer cell lines such as HeLa and HepG2. The dysregulation of circadian rhythms has been identified as a potential risk factor contributing to tumorigenesis, yet the underlying mechanisms remain largely unknown. Our findings present a compelling therapeutic strategy, highlighting the role of PER2 in regulating EMT genes to impede cancer development. This mechanism serves as a crucial initial step in preventing the progression of early-stage tumors into invasive malignancies. Furthermore, the utilization of TETv4 for demethylation, in conjunction with NVPR, greatly augments the activation of ZNF382. Consequently, the expression of EMT-related genes, which are known to be regulated by ZNF382, exhibits significant alterations. Additionally, the RNA levels of multiple oncogenes involved in the apoptotic cell cycle and tumor cell proliferation are effectively suppressed through the expression of ZNF382 . In line with these mechanistic insights, the expression of ZNF382 led to a notable decrease in the colony formation efficiency of HepG2 cells. These findings provide additional evidence for the potential of dCas9-NVPR in cancer genetic therapy. However, it is important to note that the in vivo application of dCas9-NVPR, utilizing AAV or other delivery tools, requires further investigation in future studies.

In summary, we have successfully developed and showcased a groundbreaking CRISPRa tool with promising implications for cancer genetic therapy. This innovation expands the range of available genetic editing tools and further validates the immense potential of CRISPR-based approaches in precision medicine.
