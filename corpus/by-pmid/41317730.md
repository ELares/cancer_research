# Molecular subtypes of hepatocellular carcinoma linked to liver cell lineages and clinical outcomes of combination immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Understanding the biology and clinical relevance of disease heterogeneity in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is important for guiding therapeutic strategies. Through multi-omics and in situ analyses in three independent cohorts of patients with advanced HCC including GO30140 phase 1b and IMbrave150 phase 3 trials, we identified three robust molecular subtypes of HCC, i.e., cholangiocyte-like, progenitor-like, and hepatocyte-like, based on their association with different liver epithelial cell lineages. These subtypes showed distinct tumor cell-intrinsic and extrinsic features, including different immune contextures, and importantly an association with clinical response to atezolizumab plus bevacizumab combination immunotherapy. In a humanized HCC xenograft mouse model recapitulating the GPC3-high progenitor-like subtype, a GPC3/CD3 bispecific antibody elicited strong antitumor activity mediated by intratumoral recruitment and activation of T cells. Our study provides biological insights into HCC heterogeneity and potential strategies for targeting subtype-specific vulnerabilities.

Introduction

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the sixth most common malignancy worldwide and the fourth most lethal. Combination immunotherapy with atezolizumab and bevacizumab has become a new standard of care as the first-line therapy for unresectable HCC by demonstrating strong antitumor activity in an initial phase 1b study (GO30140) and subsequently superior overall survival (OS), progression-free survival (PFS), and response rate compared to sorafenib in a phase 3 randomized study (IMbrave150). However, nearly a third of patients still had the best response of progressive disease, highlighting the need to understand HCC tumor heterogeneity and mechanisms of response and/or resistance to guide treatment strategies.

HCC is clinically and biologically heterogeneous disease with complex etiological factors as well as diverse molecular and cellular properties. In addition, liver-specific function and biology may further contribute to heterogeneity in the pathological development of HCC, including cell plasticity for injury-induced liver regeneration as well as metabolic liver zonations revealed by single-cell and spatial studies. Several molecular and immune classifications of HCC have been described previously. Molecular classes of HCC have been defined based on gene expression signatures, genetic/epigenetic landscape, metabolic networks, or the immune status of the tumors. Potential associations of these subtypes with morphology and cell differentiation stages have also been observed. Nevertheless, the clinical relevance of these HCC subtypes, especially in the context of immunotherapy, is lacking.

Here, we report a molecular classification of HCC based on an unsupervised machine learning approach. Defined by distinct tumor-intrinsic and tumor microenvironment (TME) features, these HCC molecular subtypes also showed strong linkages to different liver epithelial cell lineages. More importantly, via multi-omics analyses of 268 tumor tissues from patients enrolled in GO30140 phase 1b and IMbrave150 phase 3 clinical trials, we identified that these HCC molecular subtypes were associated with differential clinical outcomes to atezolizumab-bevacizumab treatment. Using a GPC3-high HCC mouse model that recapitulates the progenitor-like subtype, we showed a proof-of-principle of antitumor activity of a GPC3/CD3 bispecific antibody by converting a previously “cold” tumor into an “inflamed” one. The biological insights into HCC heterogeneity generated by our study may be used to guide the future development of more effective therapies targeting subtype-specific vulnerabilities.

Results

Identification and characterization of molecular subtypes of HCC

To identify and characterize biologically and clinically relevant molecular subtypes of HCC, we conducted multi-omic analyses using tumor samples from a cohort of patients with advanced HCC (n = 99, Table S1) as the initial discovery cohort and tumor samples collected from the GO30140 (n = 90, Table S2) and IMbrave150 (n = 178, Table S3) clinical trials as additional cohorts for further confirmation and exploration of their clinical relevance (Figure 1A). Using the RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) dataset from the discovery cohort, we first took an unsupervised machine learning approach by utilizing non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) to identify and refine gene-expression-based subgroups. NMF is a clustering algorithm that can maximize correlation values and iteratively select the most robust clustering pattern within a given dataset. Here, NMF identified three robust clusters with the highest cophenetic correlation value (0.98) based on the top 10% (∼3,000) most variably expressed genes in the discovery dataset (Figures 1B and S1A). We also compared the identified three NMF clusters with two previously described classifications, based on transcriptome analysis (S1–S3) or metabolic features (iHCC1–iHCC3). We observed a high concordance between the NMF1–NMF3 clusters with the S1–S3 subtypes, respectively, confirming the robustness of these subtypes. We also found that the iHCC3 subtype was enriched in both the NMF1 and NMF2 clusters, and the iHCC1 and iHCC2 subtypes were enriched in the NMF3 cluster (Figure 1B).

Because histologic heterogeneity has been previously observed in HCC, we first investigated whether the identified NMF subtypes were associated with different liver cell lineages. Two main types of epithelial cells constitute the liver tissue: hepatocytes, which account for ∼80% of liver cells and are responsible for most of the liver biosynthesis and metabolic functions, and cholangiocytes, also called biliary epithelial cells, that line the bile ducts and are important for bile secretion. In addition, a third type of epithelial cells, hepatoblasts, have been described as bi-potent progenitor cells possibly residing in the canal of herring, which can give rise to either cholangiocytes lining the bile duct near portal vein or mature hepatocytes by central vein, guided by molecular cues such as Notch and Wnt signaling (Figure 1C). Using published signatures of the three major liver cell types (hepatocyte and cholangiocytes and bi-potent progenitor,; Table S4), we observed a unique association of each of the three liver cell types with a specific NMF cluster (Figures 1D and S1B). This finding was further supported by the dichromic expression of known lineage markers for cholangiocyte (KRT19 [cytokeratin 19 mRNA] and SOX9) and hepatocyte (CPS1 [Hep Par 1 mRNA] and HNF4A) between the NMF1 and NMF3 clusters, while the NMF2 cluster showed a gradient expression pattern of these markers (Figures S1C and S1D). We, therefore, named these NMF clusters cholangiocyte-like, progenitor-like, and hepatocyte-like subtypes, respectively.

To identify the key biological features driving these HCC subtypes, we performed Hallmark gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) (Figure 1E) and cell type enrichment analysis by xCell (http://xCell.ucsf.edu/) (Figure S2A). The progenitor-like subtype was enriched with proliferation-related pathways (i.e., G2M, E2F, and MYC targets and mitotic spindle signature) accompanied by a significantly under-represented interferon-alpha and -gamma immune responses (Figure 1E) and fewer conventional dendritic cells (aDCs) and stromal cells (Figure S2A), indicating a highly proliferative but immune-desert phenotype. The cholangiocyte-like subtype was characterized by high epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition, high inflammatory responses (tumor necrosis factor alpha signaling, interferon-gamma [IFNγ] response), and proliferative features (mitotic spindle signature) (Figure 1E). xCell deconvolution analysis further confirmed that the cholangiocyte-like subtype enriched for both innate and adaptive immune cell types and high ImmuneScore and cholangiocyte features (Figure S2A). Lastly, the hepatocyte-like subtype featured enrichment of pathways pertaining to hepatocyte functions including bile acid and xenobiotic metabolism (Figure 1E) and cell type enrichment for hepatocytes (Figure S2A). Proliferation-related pathways were significantly under-represented in this subtype.

To complement the global GSEA analysis, we further dissected the tumor-intrinsic and TME features characterizing these subtypes by using previously established gene signatures representing key pathways associated with HCC pathogenesis, prognosis, liver metabolism, and response to cancer immunotherapies (Table S4). Consistent with the GSEA analysis, the cholangiocyte-like subtype signified an inflammatory immune microenvironment with high expression of antigen presentation, T effector, natural killer cell signatures (Figure 1F), and CD274 (PD-L1 mRNA, Figure S2B). The cholangiocyte-like subtype was also associated with high expression of the fibroblast transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) response (F-TBRS). We also found that the cholangiocyte-like subtype was associated with high Hippo pathway activity with high expression of yes associated protein(YAP)/tafazzin (TAZ) activation signatures, where Hippo pathway has been associated with carcinogenesis and prognosis of HCC (Figure 1F). The progenitor-like subtype has many stem-like features, including re-expression of oncofetal genes such as GPC3 and AFP and many imprinted genes (i.e., DLK1, IGF2, and ZIM2) (Table S4) as well as high expression of MYCN, an oncogenic driver highly expressed in liver cancer stem cells (Figure 1F). The progenitor-like subtype was also associated with a unique metabolic profile including mevalonate metabolism and fatty acid biosynthesis. Lastly, the hepatocyte-like subtype was characterized by pathways representing key functions of well-differentiated hepatocytes, such as high expression of cytochrome 450s genes (CYPs) that are involved in the detoxification of xenobiotics. This subtype also had the lowest expression of cell cycle-related genes compared to the other two subtypes, consistent with a less proliferative profile (Figure 1F). Metabolically, the hepatocyte-like subtype was associated with the highest activity of fatty acid oxidation and lowest activity of glycolysis among the three subtypes. It is also worth noting that both the progenitor-like and hepatocyte-like subtypes have shown lower expression of immune-related genes (i.e., antigen presentation and T effector signatures) compared to the cholangiocyte-like subtype (Figure 1F).

Genetically, the mutation landscape derived from the discovery dataset confirmed frequent disease-associated somatic mutations in HCC, including TP53 (48%), TERT promoter (39%), and CTNNB1 mutations (17%) (Figure 1G). Importantly, we identified subtype-specific genetic profiles underscoring additional tumor cell-intrinsic molecular drivers of these HCC subtypes. For example, CTNNB1 mutations were mostly restricted to the hepatocyte-like subtype (15/17, 88%, p value = 0.0009), whereas TP53 mutations showed no significant differences among different subtypes (Figure 1G). Interestingly, we found that CTNNB1 mutation status within the hepatocyte-like subtype was also highly associated with liver zonation markers, indicating additional genetic and spatially defined heterogeneity within this subtype (Figure 1H). While zonation markers were highly expressed in the hepatocyte-like subtype compared to the other two subtypes (Figure S3A), CTNNB1 mutants showed significantly higher expression of the peri-central landmark gene (CYP2E1) and signature (Table S4), and CTNNB1 wild types showed significantly higher expression of the peri-portal gene (ARG1) and signature (Table S4) (Figure 1H). No significant difference was observed among subtypes in terms of tumor mutation burden (Figure S3B).

Clinical and in situ validation of molecular subtypes of HCC

To validate the identified molecular subtypes of HCC in independent cohorts, we developed a random forest classifier from the discovery dataset (STAR Methods) and applied it to the RNA-seq datasets from the GO30140 phase 1b group A (n = 90) and the IMbrave150 phase 3 HCC clinical trial (n = 178) to classify their molecular subtypes. All three subtypes were recapitulated in the two additional clinical trial cohorts, and the prevalence of the subtypes was consistent with that in the discovery cohort (Figures 2A and S4), confirming the robustness of these subtypes. Consistent with findings from the discovery cohort, enrichment of immune presence, i.e., high PD-L1 expression, was observed in the cholangiocyte-like subtype compared to the other two subtypes in both clinical trial cohorts, where no particular etiology was found to be associated with a specific subtype (Figure S4). Similar enrichment of CTNNB1 mutations in the hepatocyte-like subtype was also observed (Figure S4).

To further distinguish tumor cell-intrinsic vs. microenvironment features shaping the HCC subtypes, we carried out several in situ analyses. We confirmed that there was a significantly higher nuclear YAP expression in tumor cells by IHC in the cholangiocyte-like and progenitor-like tumors than those of hepatocyte-like subtype (cholangiocyte-like vs. hepatocyte-like: p = 0.025; progenitor-like vs. hepatocyte-like: p = 0.010) (Figure 2B), validating YAP/TAZ pathway activation in these subtypes. Notably, YAP/TAZ activation signature expression is higher in cholangiocyte-like than progenitor-like or hepatocyte-like subtypes (Figure 1F) whereas IHC scores for nuclear YAP staining in tumor cells were similar between cholangiocyte-like and progenitor-like (Figure 2B). This discrepancy might be due to YAP activation in cell types other than tumor cells (i.e., stromal cells), especially in the cholangiocyte-like tumors. We also confirmed the enrichment of CYP expression in hepatocyte-like subtype with an IHC assay detecting CYP2C8, CYP2C9, CYP2C18, and CYP2C19. Consistent with the RNA-seq analysis, CYPs expression in the hepatocyte-like subtype was significantly higher than those of other subtypes (p < 0.0001 for both cholangiocyte-like vs. hepatocyte-like and progenitor-like vs. hepatocyte-like) (Figure 2B).

We have previously reported the development and analyses of two panels of five-plex IHC assays with accompanying whole-slide digital pathology algorithms on 61 baseline tumor samples from the GO30140 Group A cohort. While both panels used HepPar 1/Arginase 1 pooled antibodies for staining of hepatic tumor cells, panel 1 (HepPar 1/Arginase 1-FAP-CD31-CD8-major histocompatibility complex [MHC] class I) was designed to assess the tumor-immune-stroma contexture in the TME while panel 2 (HepPar 1/Arginase 1-CD3-PD-1-PD-L1-Granzyme B (GZMB)) was aimed at characterizing the functional states of T cell. Consistent with the cell lineage association revealed by the gene expression analysis (Figures 1D and S1B), the density of HepPar1/Arg1+ tumor cells was significantly higher in the hepatocyte-like than in the cholangiocyte-like subtype (Figure 2C).

In addition to the tumor-intrinsic features, multiplex IHC analysis also confirmed several distinct TME features defining the molecular subtypes. The cholangiocyte-like subtype has been associated with enriched immune presence and TGF-β activities in fibroblasts by gene expression analysis (Figure 1F). Supporting these findings, multiplex IHC analysis showed that the cholangiocyte-like subtype also had a significantly higher density of CD8+ T cells, CD3+GZMB+-activated T cells in the tumor area, and a higher percentage of FAP+ area in the tumor stroma (Figure 2C).

HCC subtypes are associated with different clinical outcomes from anti-PD-L1 and anti-VEGF combination immunotherapy

Atezolizumab-bevacizumab combination immunotherapy has become a widely established first-line standard of care for unresectable HCC. However, not all patients derive clinical benefit from this treatment. In this study, we aimed to further dissect the molecular basis of response and resistance to combination immunotherapy in the context of HCC molecular subtypes and underlying disease biology.

From the GO30140 group A cohort, a single-arm study evaluating atezolizumab plus bevacizumab combination, a distinct association with different HCC subtypes was observed. Compared to the 36% confirmed objective response rate (ORR) in the intent-to-treat (ITT) population of group A patients, the cholangiocyte-like subtype showed the highest ORR (49%, 18/37), while the progenitor-like subtype showed the lowest ORR (17%, 2/12) (Figure 3A). Consistent with the response rate, patients from the cholangiocyte-like also showed significantly longer PFS compared to those from the progenitor-like subtype (median PFS: 8.9 months vs. 1.9 months, hazadous ratio (HR) = 0.42, 95% confidence interval [CI] = 0.20–0.87, p value = 0.018) (Figure 3B). The hepatocyte-like subtype patients had an intermediate ORR of 29% (12/41) and median PFS of 7.4 months and showed significantly longer PFS than the progenitor-like subtype (HR = 0.44, 95% CI = 0.22–0.89, p = 0.022).

The association of HCC subtypes with clinical outcomes from combination immunotherapy was further evaluated in the IMbrave150 phase 3 study. Compared to outcomes in the ITT population treated with atezolizumab-bevacizumab vs. sorafenib (OS: HR = 0.58, 95% CI = 0.42–0.79, p < 0.001; PFS: HR = 0.59, 95% CI = 0.47–0.76, p < 0.001), OS benefit appeared to be enhanced in both the cholangiocyte-like (HR = 0.38, 95% CI = 0.15–0.93; p = 0.035) and the hepatocyte-like subtypes (HR = 0.42, 95% CI = 0.19–0.94; p = 0.034) (Figure 3C). In contrast, patients with the progenitor-like subtype appeared to have the least OS benefit (HR = 0.46, 95% CI = 0.11–2; p = 0.3) from the atezolizumab-bevacizumab combination (Figure 3C). A similar trend was also observed between PFS and HCC subtypes although these analyses did not reach statistical significance (Figure 3C). It is worth mentioning that, in the sorafenib control arm of IMbrave150 study, the progenitor subtype showed a trend of poor prognosis with shorter PFS and OS compared to the other subtypes (Figure S5). In addition, we classified molecular subtypes for a core of HCC The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) dataset of 193 tumors for which RNA-seq and genomic integration data were available. Interestingly, we found no significant association between the HCC subtypes and OS in this dataset (Figure S6).

Taken together, these findings indicated that the HCC molecular subtypes identified in this study were not only biologically relevant but also may have clinical significance. While the cholangiocyte-like and hepatocyte-like subtypes benefit from atezolizumab-bevacizumab combination, the progenitor-like subtype continues to remain a potential unmet need among HCC patients. Identifying and targeting progenitor-like subtype-specific vulnerabilities may overcome resistance to treatment and further improve the clinical outcomes of these patients.

GPC3-CD3 bispecific antibody (ERY974) shows antitumor activity and increased T cell infiltration and activation in progenitor-like xenograft models

We have identified several key molecular features characterizing the progenitor-like subtype, including overexpression of the oncofetal gene GPC3 and an overall immune-desert phenotype (Figures 1E and 1F). The association of GPC3 with progenitor-like subtype was further validated in two additional datasets, including TCGA HCC dataset (n = 193) and a recently published HCC RNA-seq dataset (n = 171) (Figure S7). We therefore hypothesized that ERY974, a humanized immunoglobulin G4-bispecific T cell-redirecting antibody that recognizes GPC3 and CD3, might show antitumor activity in the progenitor-like subtype by recruiting T cells to GPC3 expressing tumors. To test this hypothesis, we first established two humanized xenograft mouse models recapitulating the human progenitor-like subtype. Consensus clustering of transcriptome profiles of 16 HCC cell lines recapitulated two main subtypes (cholangiocyte-like and progenitor-like) that have been identified in human HCC patients (Tables S5 and S6; Figure S8). Among them, the huH-1 and huH-7 cell lines were selected for representing the progenitor-like subtype based on their high gene expression of GPC3 (Figure 4A; Table S7). SK-HEP-1, a previously identified GPC3-negative HCC cell line (Table S7), was used as a control (Figure 4C). Fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) analysis further confirmed that the huH-1 and huH-7 cell lines have high protein expression of GPC3 at the cell surface, while the SK-HEP-1 cell line showed no detectable GPC3 cell surface expression (Figure 4B). Furthermore, the huH-1 and huH-7 cell lines showed significantly higher antibody binding capacity of ERY974 than SK-HEP-1 (Figure 4C). An in vitro T cell-dependent cytotoxicity (TDCC) assay further confirmed that the binding of ERY974 results in strong T cell-mediated cell killing in huh-1 and huh-7 cells but not in SK-HEP-1 cells (Figure 4D).

Next, we examined the in vivo antitumor activity of ERY974 in humanized huh-1 and huh-7 xenograft models. As the anti-human CD3 arm of ERY974 does not cross-react with murine CD3, to supply human T cells to huh-1 huNOG models, human CD34+ stem cells were injected intravenously. For the huh-7 model, T cells from healthy donors were ex vivo expanded and activated before their administration to circulation. As shown in Figure 4E, ERY974 showed a strong antitumor activity in both progenitor-like huh-1 and huh-7 huNOG models. The tumor growth inhibition index for huh-1 models is 109% and is 105% for huh-7 models. On the other hand, ERY974 showed no antitumor activity in the GPC3-negative SK-HEP-1 model in our previous study. Mechanistically, FACS analysis on tumors collected at vehicle vs. ERY974 treatment (3 days) showed drastically increased CD3 including both CD4 and CD8 T cell infiltration into the tumor (Figures 4F, 4G, and S9A). Furthermore, several markers of T cell activation, including GZMB, PD-1, and IFNγ, were also dramatically increased in these T cell compartments (Figure 4G). Histopathological

evaluation of intratumoral CD3 and CD8 T cells by IHC further validated increased T cell infiltration upon ERY974 treatment (Figure 4H). Gene expression analysis further confirmed that ERY974 treatment appeared to transform a previous immune-desert tumor into an inflamed one with elevated expression of signature genes representing T cells in different functional states including activated/exhausted T cells (Figure 4I). Lastly, we also analyzed the regulatory T cell (Treg) (CD4+/CD25+/Foxp3+)/T effector (Teff) (Gzmb+CD8) ratio in the vehicle and ERY974 treatment groups on day 3 after administering the antibody. Interestingly, Treg/Teff ratio was drastically reduced in the ERY974 treatment group as compared to the vehicle control group (Figure S9B).

Collectively, our study provides preclinical proof of principle for antitumor activity of ERY974 in progenitor-like HCC tumors. By induction of T cell infiltration via GPC3, a highly expressed target specific to the progenitor-like subtype, ERY974, may represent a potential therapeutic strategy for overcoming resistance and improving the clinical outcomes of progenitor-like HCC patients.

Discussion

Through integrated multi-omics and in situ analyses of 367 tumor tissues from three independent cohorts of patients with advanced HCC including GO30140 phase 1b and IMbrave150 phase 3 clinical trials, we have identified and validated three molecular subtypes of HCC that are both biologically relevant and clinically significant.

Based on an approach of unsupervised machine learning (NMF), the three molecular subtypes of HCC identified in this study were characterized with distinct tumor cell-intrinsic, TME features, as well as a different immune landscape. The large overlap of our NMF-based classification with the previously identified S1–S3 subtypes, further re-enforces the robustness of these subtypes and their distinct underlying biology. One of the key findings of our study is the identification of a strong link between these HCC molecular subtypes and distinct liver epithelial cell lineages. HCC has been proposed to be derived from mature hepatocytes, progenitor hepatocytes, or precursor hepatocytes, but not from cholangiocytes. Although the cholangiocyte-like subtype identified in this study may share some cholangiocyte traits, we do not suggest that cholangiocytes may be the cell of origin of this type of HCC. Since liver damage from hepatitis B or C virus infections, excessive alcohol consumption, or steatosis almost invariably precedes oncogenic transformation in the liver, a potential mechanism underlying cell lineage-associated subtypes could be dysregulation and disruption of hepatocyte differentiation, dedifferentiation, or transdifferentiation programs during liver damage repair. Supporting this hypothesis, YAP activation, a molecular feature of cholangiocyte-like HCC, has been shown to be able to subvert hepatocyte differentiation and gear cells toward the biliary cell lineage. Further, we also identified additional heterogeneity within the hepatocyte-like subtype that may be genetically defined by CTNNB1 mutation status and spatially defined by liver metabolic zonations. The important links among HCC heterogeneity, liver cell lineages and plasticity, and spatiotemporally organized liver metabolic zonations provide insights and a deeper understanding of HCC heterogeneity and underlying liver and disease biology.

Another important finding of this study is that the cell lineage-linked HCC subtypes are also associated with differential clinical outcomes to anti-PD-L1 and anti-vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) combination immunotherapy. We have previously identified key molecular correlates of clinical response and resistance to such combination immunotherapy. Specifically, pre-existing immunity (high expression of CD274, T effector signature, and intratumoral CD8+ T cell density) was associated with better clinical outcomes with the combination, while reduced clinical benefit was associated with high Treg-to-Teff ratio and expression of oncofetal genes (GPC3 and AFP). Consistent with these findings, the cholangiocyte-like subtypes featured a more inflamed tumor immune microenvironment with higher expression of PD-L1, T-effector signature, and intratumoral CD8+ T cells. On the other hand, the progenitor-like subtype is signified by high expression of oncofetal genes such as GPC3 (Figures 1F, S7, and S10A) and a high Treg/Teff ratio (Figures S11A and S11B) and mostly exhibits an immune-desert phenotype (Figure 1E). Notably, these subtype-specific molecular correlates of response and resistance to atezolizumab + bevacizumab appeared to be independent and did not have significant association with each other (Figures S10B–S10F). Since non-viral etiology, especially nonalcoholic steatohepatitis-driven HCC, may respond poorly to immunotherapy (Pfister, D. et al.,2021), it is also worth mentioning that we observed no significant association between HCC molecular subtypes and a particular etiology. Taken together, the in-depth characterization of the HCC subtype disease biology in this study has provided further biological context and insights into our previously identified molecular correlates of response and resistance to combination immunotherapy in HCC. We also recognize that the sample size for the three subtypes in our retrospective clinical trial analyses is relatively small, especially for the less prevalent progenitor-like subtype. The clinical relevance of these HCC subtypes with atezolizumab-bevacizumab treatment and other therapies requires further confirmation and investigation in future studies.

Finally, the comprehensive molecular features characterizing HCC molecular subtypes cataloged by our study may offer an important framework for identifying new therapeutic targets and designing new combination therapies for advanced HCC patients. Using a humanized HCC xenograft mouse model recapitulating the GPC3-high progenitor-like subtype, we provided in vivo proof of principle for antitumor activity of a GPC3/CD3 bispecific antibody by converting a previously “cold” tumor into an “inflamed” one. Informed by these promising findings, ERY974 is now being evaluated in combination with atezolizumab-bevacizumab in advanced HCC patients in a phase 1 study (NCT05022927). In addition to GPC3, many other potential subtype-specific targets/pathways identified in our study, i.e., TGF-β, Hippo/YAP/TAZ (cholangiocyte-like), mevalonate/fatty acid biosynthesis (progenitor-like), and fatty acid oxidation and glycolysis (hepatocyte-like), may be explored to further deepen or expand clinical benefits of combination immunotherapy in HCC patients.

Limitations of the study

A more direct comparison of the GPC3/CD3 bispecific antibody with the anti-PD-L1 + anti-VEGF combination is needed to further support the value of the bispecific approach, even though we have demonstrated its antitumor activity in GPC3-high progenitor-like mouse models. However, the humanized mouse model we currently use limits our ability to properly assess the antitumor efficacy of anti-PD-L1. This is because clinically relevant immune checkpoint inhibitors such as pembrolizumab, nivolumab, and atezolizumab cannot be effectively evaluated due to MHC class I mismatch between the tumor model and the administered human T cells. Future studies will also include a direct comparison between the bispecific antibody and anti-GPC3 to better assess the efficacy of these two strategies for treating GPC3-high HCC.

To better understand the correlation between GPC3 expression levels and efficacy, future studies can incorporate more GPC3-low and GPC3-high controls. We have identified SNU449 (ATCC) and JHH6 (Janpanese Collection of Research Bioresources), additional cholangiocyte-like HCC cell lines confirmed to be GPC3-negative, which we will test in TDCC or in vivo efficacy studies. We also intend to create GPC3 knockout cell lines in HuH-1 and HuH-7 and generate GPC3-overexpressing GPC3-negative cell lines for future research.

Finally, while we have thoroughly characterized the tumor-intrinsic and tumor immune microenvironment features of HCC molecular subtypes and shown their clinical relevance in combination immunotherapy, a more profound mechanistic understanding of the interplay between tumor-intrinsic features and their specific tumor immune microenvironment is necessary for future investigation. For instance, the reason behind the immune-desert phenotype in GPC3-high progenitor cells requires further exploration.

Experimental model and study participant details

HCC clinical tissue samples

Resected tumor and matched adjacent cirrhotic formalin fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tissue samples from 99 Stage IV HCC patients were obtained from Folio Biosciences (Powell, OH) with local Institutional Review Board (IRB) approval. 90 and 178 pretreatment FFPE samples from Ph1b GO30140 Group A (NCT02715531) and phase 3 IMbrave150 (NCT03434379) clinical trials, respectively, were collected from either resected HCC tumor tissues at ≤ 6 months or fresh core needle biopsies taken ≥7 days prior to cycle 1 day 1 (C1D1). Collection and submission of FFPE samples from HCC trials to the Roche Clinical Repository (RCR) is contingent upon the review and approval of the exploratory research and the RCR portion of the Informed Consent Form by each site’s Institutional Review Board or Ethics Committee (IRB/EC) and, if applicable, an appropriate regulatory body. All tissue sections have been stained with hematoxylin and eosin and are subject to pathologist review for tumor content.

Preclinical mouse models

All animal studies were performed following the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) of Chugai Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd. The NOD/Shi-scid, IL-2Rγnull (NOG) mice purchased from In-Vivo Science Inc. (Kanagawa, Japan) were used to establish the huNOG mice.

HCC cell lines

All the HCC cell lines were obtained directly from the Genentech Cell Line Bank and authenticated by short tandem repeat profiling prior to banking and SNP fingerprinting after expansion. Cells were cultured in RPMI-1640 medium with 10% fetal calf serum and 2mmol/L of glutamine. The HCC cell lines huh-1 and huh-7 were purchased from Japanese Cancer Research Resources Bank (JCRB; Ibaraki, Japan). Consensus clustering of molecular subtype classifier genes (288-gene panel associated with HCC disease biology, Table S4) was used to classify cell lines.

Method details

RNAseq and DNAseq

Macrodissection was performed on formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor tissues to enrich tumor content to >50%. The FFPE tissues are from 99 stage IVA HCC tumors and matched adjacent cirrhosis tissues procured from Folio Biosciences (Powell, OH), 90 pre-treatment biopsy or archival tissues from GO30140 and 178 pre-treatment archival tissues from IMbrave 150. Total RNA was isolated using High Pure FFPE RNA Micro Kit (Roche Diagnostics). RNA sequencing was performed using TruSeq RNA Access technology (Illumina). RNA-seq reads were first aligned to ribosomal RNA sequences to remove ribosomal reads. The remaining reads were aligned to the human reference genome (NCBI Build 38) using GSNAP, version 2013-11-01. To quantify gene expression levels, the numbers of reads mapped to the exons of each RefSeq gene were calculated using the functionality provided by the R/Bioconductor package GenomicAlignments. For each cohort separately, raw counts were filtered for lowly expressed genes, whereby low expression was defined as counts per million (CPM) < 0.25 in at least 10% of samples, and CPM was calculated with the cpm function in the edgeR package. Raw counts for the expressed genes were then normalized based on size factors as calculated with CalcNormFactors in the edgeR package. The TMM normalized counts were subsequently voom transformed with the voom function in the limma package, resulting in normalized log2 CPM data. Gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) and xCell was performed using the fgsea package and the Hallmark gene set from the molecular signatures database collection using the msigdbr package. In detail, the differentially expressed gene list was ranked according to the combined log fold change and adjusted p-value and used as an input for the gene set enrichment analysis.

Genomic DNA from 76 patients from GO30140 Group A was isolated from archival formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor tissue with the Cobas DNA Sample Preparation Kit. DNA from patient matched peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) samples was isolated using the QIAamp DNA Blood Midi Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Isolated genomic DNA (150 ng) was used for whole exome sequencing (WES) library construction with Agilent SureSelectXT Human All Exon v5 (Agilent) bait sets. Exome libraries were then sequenced with paired-end 2 × 100bp reads on the Illumina HiSeq2000 or HiSeq2500 platform (Illumina) to a mean depth of 100× for tumor samples and 50× for PBMC samples. Sequence data was aligned to the publicly available human reference genome version (NCBI Build 38) using GSNAP version 2013-11-01. Tumor-specific somatic mutations were detected for each tumor and PBMC data pair using Strelka 1.0.4 and Variant Effect Predictor. Tumor mutational burden is calculated as the number of somatic base substitutions and short insertions and deletions identified from coding regions covered by WES bait sets.

For 130 baseline FFPE tissues from IMbrave 150, we performed FoundationOne (F1CDx) sequencing. Genomic DNA was extracted by Omega Bio-Tek FFPE DNA Extraction Kit (Customized FMI) on the Kingfisher instrument. F1CDx method uses targeted high throughput hybridization-based capture technology for the detection of substitutions, insertion and deletion alterations (indels), and copy number alterations (CNAs) in 324 genes and select gene rearrangements, as well as genomic signatures including microsatellite instability (MSI) and tumor mutational burden (TMB) using DNA isolated from formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) tumor tissue specimens. Tumor mutational burden is calculated as the number of somatic base substitutions and short insertions and deletions identified from coding regions within FoundationOne panel (filtering 0).

NMF clustering and random forest classifier for HCC subtypes

An unsupervised machine learning approach based on consensus non-negative matrix factorization (cNMF) was applied to normalized RNAseq data to identify transcriptomic bases subtypes. This type of clustering is based on the dimensional reduction methodology of NMF which reduces the expression data from thousands of genes to a few metagenes (CRAN. R package version 0.22.0) combined with the consensus clustering to test stability of iterative NMF runs. This method computes multiple k-factor factorization decompositions of the expression matrix and evaluates the stability of the solutions using a cophenetic coefficient. Using Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) analysis we first selected the top 10% (n = 3072) genes with the highest variability across 99 tumors. Then, we applied consensus NMF clustering testing k = 2 to k = 8 and identified k = 3 (Figure S1A) as the most robust number of subtypes using the cophenetic correlation. In order to verify the robustness of using the top 10% most variable genes, we performed NMF clustering using 2 additional MAD cutoffs −20% (n = 6,144 genes), and 30% (n = 9,216). When plotting genes by descending MAD scores, we observed a noticeable plateau in values, with only modest differences between the 10% (MAD = 1.21) and 30% (MAD = 0.67) thresholds, suggesting limited gain in information beyond the top 10% (Figure S12A). Importantly, the optimal number of clusters remained consistent at three across all cutoffs (Figure S12B). Patient assignments to clusters were also highly concordant, with chi-squared p-values <2.2e−16 for all pairwise comparisons (Figure S12C). Furthermore, a Sankey plot illustrates that differences in patient clustering between MAD cutoffs are minimal (Figure S12D) and likely reflect background noise. These findings support the robustness of using the top 10% of genes.

A machine learning based classifier using the random forest machine learning algorithm (R package randomForest) was trained on the NMF discovery cohort to derive a robust gene expression based classifier to predict the NMF clusters in two independent datasets (GO30140 phase 1b group A (n = 90) and IMbrave150 phase3 HCC clinical trial (n = 178). A random forest classifier involves learning a large number of binary decision trees from random subsets of a training set. These trees in the classifier can then be used in a prediction algorithm to identify the similarity of a given sample to a given class in the training set. Before learning the random forest classifier, we preprocessed the data. To ensure accurate prediction of all three NMF classes, we down-sampled by randomly removing observations from the majority classes to prevent its signal from dominating the learning algorithm. We also normalized (Z score transformed) the gene expression values.

Immunohistochemistry (IHC) and multiplex immunofluorescence panels

IHC of YAP nuclear immunolabeling was performed at Seventh Wave Laboratories (Inotiv, Maryland Heights, MO) on approximately 5 μm, formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded tissue sections. Heat-induced antigen retrieval was performed in a decloaker (Biocare Medical, Pacheco, CA) with pH = 6 citrate buffer. 0.11 μg/mL recombinant rabbit monoclonal anti-YAP antibody (ab52771, EP1674Y, Abcam, Cambridge, MA) and SignalStain Boost IHC Detection Reagent (HRP, Rabbit) (Catalog #8114, Cell Signaling Technologies, Danvers, MA) were used to detect nuclear signals of YAP protein. Slides were counterstained with Mayer’s hematoxylin. Tumor xenografts with and without YAP knockdown were used as positive and negative controls. Nuclear YAP staining of tumor cells was scored using the H-score method (H-score = (%weak positive x 1) + (%moderate positive x 2) + (%strong positive x 3)).

CYP2C9 IHC was performed on 2.5 μm sections using mouse monoclonal antibody clone OTI1D7 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, US). Of note, Western Blot analysis showed slight cross-reactivity of this antibody with CYP2C8, CYP2C18 and CYP2C19. The staining was performed on an automated slide stainer Discovery ULTRA IHC/ISH research platform (Roche Tissue Diagnostics) at a contract research organization (HistoGenex N.V., Belgium). After deparaffinization and antigen recovery (32 min CC1 at 96°C) the CYP2C9 antibody was applied at 36°C for 32 min with a dispenser concentration of 5 μg/mL, diluted in Dako REAL diluent followed by the DISCOVERY ChromoMAP DAB Kit, Hematoxylin II and Bluing reagent (as counterstain) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The stained slides were then scanned, uploaded in an image viewer and evaluated by a pathologist. An H-score was applied to evaluate the expression of CYP2C9 on tumor cells. CYP cytoplasmic staining of normal liver tissues serves as a positive control. The H-score takes into account the percentage of only stained tumor cells at a given staining intensity. Percentages of unstained tumor cells as well as of tumor cells stained at low, moderate and high intensity were estimated per case. The H-score was calculated according to the formula: H-score = percentage of tumor cells stained at low intensity + (2x the percentage of tumor cells at moderate intensity) + (3x the percentage of tumor cells at high intensity).

Two panels of 5-plex immunofluorescence (IF) assays, i.e., tumor–stroma–immune context panel (Panel 1, P1) containing antibodies for HepPar 1/Arginase 1, Fibroblast activation protein (FAP), CD31, and MHC class I and T cell functionality panel (Panel 2, P2) containing antibodies HepPar 1/Arginase 1, CD3, PD-L1, PD-1, and Granzyme B (GZMB), were conducted on 64 pre-treatment tumor tissues from GO30140 Group A study The fluorescent image acquisition, analysis and digital pathology algorithm used to identify phenotypes, the density of objects and their spatial interrelationships in automatically computed ROIs were described in detail previously.

T cell dependent cytotoxicity (TDCC) assay of ERY974 in vitro

The TDCC of ERY974 was examined using the LDH Cytotoxicity detection kit (TAKARA, Shiga Japan). The huh-1 and huh-7 (1 × 104/well) as target cells were incubated with peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) as effector cells at an effector-to-target (E: T) ratio of 20:1 in 5% CO2 incubator at 37°C for 24h. Then LDH level in culture medium was measured according to the manufacturer’s instructions with optical density (OD) at 490–600 nm wavelength. Cytotoxicity was calculated by the following formula, TDCC (%) was calculated as follows: TDCC (%) = (A – B– C) × 100/(D– C), where A, B, C, D is the value of OD 490–600 of ERY974 treated wells, only effector cells, only target cells, and only target cells treated with Triton X-100, respectively. Data was represented with the mean ± SD (n = 3).

Efficacy and pharmacodynamic evaluation of ERY974 in vivo

The hepatocellular carcinoma cell lines huh-1 and huh-7 were purchased from Japanese Cancer Research Resources Bank (JCRB; Ibaraki, Japan). These cell lines were cultured in DMEM with 10%FBS according to manufacturers’ instructions. The expression of GPC3 in HCC cell lines was confirmed by flow cytometry using QIFIKIT (DAKO, Glostrup, Denmark) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. ERY974 was prepared by Chugai Pharmaceutical., Co. Ltd (Tokyo, Japan) as previously described.

All animal studies were performed following the guidelines of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) of Chugai Pharmaceutical Co., Ltd. The NOD/Shi-scid, IL-2Rγnull (NOG) mice purchased from In-Vivo Science Inc. (Kanagawa, Japan) were used to establish the huNOG mice. The NOG mice were irradiated (2.5 Gy; MBR-1520R-3; Hitachi Power Solutions Co. Ltd., Ibaraki, Japan), and then 1 × 105 cells/mouse of human CD34+ cells (AllCells, Alameda, CA) were intravenously injected, and huNOG mice was established after around 3 months. The huh-1 cells (1 × 107/mouse) were subcutaneously transplanted in the right flank of huNOG mice. When the tumor was established, 5 mg/kg ERY974 was intravenously administered once. For the T cell injected model, the NOD/ShiJic-scidJcl (NOD scid) mice (CLEA Japan, Inc., Tokyo, Japan) were subcutaneously inoculated with huh-7 cells (1 × 107/mouse) in the right flank of mice. Human T cells were purified from human PBMC (Cellular Technology Ltd., Shaker Heights, OH) using the Dynabeads Untouched Human T cells kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA), and then expanded together with Dynabeads Human T-Activator CD3/CD28 (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Expanded human T cells (3 × 107 cells/mouse) were administered intraperitoneally, and then 1 mg/kg ERY974 was administered intravenously once.

Tumor length and width was measured with the Antitumor evaluation system (ANTES) in Chugai Pharm. Tumor volume (TV) was calculated using the formula: TV (mm3) = ab2/2, where a is the length of the tumor and b is the width of the tumor. Tumor growth inhibition (TGI) was calculated using the formula: TGI (%) = [1 − (T − T0)/(C − C0)] × 100, where T is the tumor volume of ERY974 group on a specific day and T0 is the tumor volume of ERY974 group on the initial day, and C is the tumor volumes of the vehicle group on a specific day and C0 is the tumor volumes of the vehicle group on the initial day. Statistical analysis was performed using JMP software (SAS Institute, Cary, NC) and JMP preclinical package 11. Data is presented as the mean ± SD (n = 5). p values <0.05 were considered statistically significant.

The huh-1 tumors xenografted in huNOG mice was collected as described above, and was minced with scissors, and then dissociated using the human Tumor Dissociation Kit (Miltenyi Biotech, Bergisch Gladbach, Germany) and gentleMACS Octo Dissociator (Miltenyi Biotech). Single tumor cells were incubated with ACK lysis buffer (Invitrogen, Carlsbad, CA) for 10 min at room temperature, and then incubated with PE-CF594 conjugated anti-CD3 antibody (clone UCHT1, BioLegend, San Diego CA), BUV737-conjugated anti-CD4 antibody (clone SK3, BD), BV786-conjugated anti-CD8 antibody (clone RPA-T8, BioLegend), BUV395-conjugated anti-CD45 antibody (clone HI30, BD), Alexa 488-conjugated anti-PD-1 antibody (clone EH12.2H7, BioLegend) for 30 min at 4°C. Then, cells were fixed and permeabilized with FoxP3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set (Thermo Fischer Scientific, Waltham, MA) for 20 min with room temperature and incubated with PE-conjugated anti-GzmB antibody (clone GB11, BD), BV605-conjugated anti-IFNγ antibody (clone B27, BioLegend) for 30 min at 4°C. The samples were resuspended in autoMACS Rinsing Solution with MACS BSA Stock Solution, followed by analysis with Fortessa-X20 (BD Biosciences) and data analysis using FlowJo v10.2 (Tree Star Inc., Ashland, OR).

For the histopathological analysis, isolated tumors were fixed in 10% neutral-buffered formalin, followed by embedding in ordinary paraffin through a routine method. Paraffin-embedded specimens were sectioned and then stained with HE. Immunohistochemical staining for human CD3 and CD8 was also performed; samples were incubated with the primary antibodies mouse anti-human CD3 (clone EP449E; Abcam) and anti-human CD8 (clone D8A8Y; Cell Signal technology in Target Retrieval Solution (DAKO) after heating the samples in a microwave. A labeled polymer reagent (EnVision+ Single Reagents, HRP. Rabbit; K4003; Agilent, Santa Clara, CA) was applied as the secondary antibody and the reaction was visualized using 3, 3ʹ-diaminobenzidine solution (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemical Corporation). Finally, the slides were counterstained with hematoxylin and imaged under a light microscope.

For PD analysis, 5 mg/kg ERY974 was administered into huNOG inoculated with huh-1 tumor. Tumor was collected 3 days after ERY974 administration. Tumor was divided into three pieces and used for RNA, TIL and histopathological analysis. Tumor piece for transcriptome analysis was submerged in RNAlator (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA) overnight at 4°C and then stored at −80°C. Total RNA was extracted using the miRNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, Hilden, Germany), and then applied to transcriptome analysis using nCounter PanCancer Immune Profiling Panel (human; NanoString Technologies, Seattle, WA). The raw counts were normalized with geometric mean counts of housekeeping genes and log2 transformed. T cell signature genes used in the heatmap were quoted from a previous study.

Quantification and statistical analysis

For paired-wise comparison of gene and signature expression and IHC scores between molecular subtypes, a two-tailed unpaired Student’s t test was used. For evaluating mutual exclusivity of two alterations, two-tailed Fisher Exact test was used. For survival analysis, the prognostic value of molecular subtypes was assessed according to PFS and OS for each treatment arm. The predictive value was investigated by comparing OS and PFS across arms within each molecular subtype using univariate analysis. For the antitumor efficacy study, statistical analysis was conducted using JMP preclinical package 11 and JMP software by Wilcoxon rank-sum test. For TIL data analysis, statistical analysis was conducted using JMP preclinical package 11 and JMP software by two-tailed unpaired t test.
