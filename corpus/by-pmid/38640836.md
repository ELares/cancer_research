# PARP inhibitors suppress tumours via centrosome error-induced senescence independent of DNA damage response.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors have emerged as promising chemotherapeutic drugs primarily against BRCA1/2-associated tumours, known as synthetic lethality. However, recent clinical trials reported patients' survival benefits from PARP inhibitor treatments, irrelevant to homologous recombination deficiency. Therefore, revealing the therapeutic mechanism of PARP inhibitors beyond DNA damage repair is urgently needed, which can facilitate precision medicine.

**METHODS**: A CRISPR-based knock-in technology was used to establish stable BRCA1 mutant cancer cells. The effects of PARP inhibitors on BRCA1 mutant cancer cells were evaluated by biochemical and cell biological experiments. Finally, we validated its in vivo effects in xenograft and patient-derived xenograft (PDX) tumour mice.

**FINDINGS**: In this study, we uncovered that the majority of clinical BRCA1 mutations in breast cancers were in and near the middle of the gene, rather than in essential regions for DNA damage repair. Representative mutations such as R1085I and E1222Q caused transient extra spindle poles during mitosis in cancer cells. PAR, which is synthesized by PARP2 but not PARP1 at mitotic centrosomes, clustered these transient extra poles, independent of DNA damage response. Common PARP inhibitors could effectively suppress PARP2-synthesized PAR and induce cell senescence by abrogating the correction of mitotic extra-pole error.

**INTERPRETATION**: Our findings uncover an alternative mechanism by which PARP inhibitors efficiently suppress tumours, thereby pointing to a potential new therapeutic strategy for centrosome error-related tumours.

Research in context

Evidence before this study

The discovery of the synthetic lethality effect between PARP inhibitors and the deficiency of homologous recombination (HR) genes BRCA1/2 led to the use of PARP inhibitors in chemotherapy for BRCA1-associated breast and ovarian cancers. However, recent clinical trials have shown apparent benefits for patients even in the absence of HR gene mutations. Meanwhile, many patients with other HR gene mutations have not benefited from PARP inhibitors in clinical trials as expected. These observations indicate that the therapeutic action of PARP inhibitors remains complex and not fully understood.

Added value of this study

We found that a considerable portion of clinical mutations of BRCA1 resides in and near the middle region of the protein, which are far away from the core DNA repair domains of RING and BRCTs, and are not essential for DNA repair. Representative mutations such as R1085I and E1222Q lead to transient extra spindle poles during mitosis in cancer cells. PAR, which is synthesized by PARP2 but not PARP1 at mitotic centrosomes, clusters the transient extra poles independent of DNA damage response, and thus avoids mitotic error. Common PARP inhibitors such as niraparib effectively inhibit PAR synthesis at centrosomes by PARP2 and result in senescence of the cancer cells by abrogating the correction of mitotic extra-pole error.

Implications of all the available evidence

Given the new finding of PAR function on clustering extra spindle poles, and senescence-based anti-tumour effect of PARP inhibitors, this study represents a significant conceptual advance and may reshape clinical treatment strategies of PARP inhibitors.

Introduction

As an essential homologous recombination (HR) factor, BRCA1 functions in the maintenance of genome stability. Mutations of BRCA1 are known to confer a high lifetime risk of breast and ovarian cancers in women. The advent of poly (ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors represented a therapeutic paradigm of synthetic lethality for cancer treatment, in which tumour cells bearing BRCA mutations are killed by exploiting tumour-specific defects to create a vulnerability that causes tumour cell death. With the administration of PARP inhibitors, better survival outcomes of patients have been reported in cancers associated with BRCA1 deficiency including breast, ovary, prostate, and pancreatic cancers. However, recent clinical trials showed that patients without HR gene mutation also benefited from PARP inhibitors. In the randomized, double-blind, phase III trial of niraparib in newly diagnosed advanced ovarian cancer, median durations of progression-free survival (PFS) for patients with HR-deficient (21.9 months with niraparib vs. 10.4 months with placebo) or HR-proficient (8.1 months with niraparib vs. 5.4 months with placebo) were substantially extended by PARP inhibitors. Also, a phase II trial of metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer treatment showed that median radiographic PFS of patients in abiraterone plus olaparib and abiraterone plus placebo groups were 17.8 vs. 6.5 months for HR-deficient and 15.0 vs. 9.7 months for HR-proficient, respectively. These results indicate apparent benefits for patients regardless of HR gene mutations. Meanwhile, these clinical trials suggest the existence of unknown therapeutic mechanisms of PARP inhibitors, other than DNA damage repair.

Based on the earlier hypothesis that BRCA-deficient tumour cells cannot repair DNA double-stranded breaks (DSBs) derived from DNA single-stranded breaks (SSBs) caused by PARP inhibitors, most cancer-associated BRCA1 mutations should be localized in either the N-terminal RING domain or the C-terminal BRCTs domain of BRCA1 because both of these domains contribute to core functions in DNA damage response (DDR) via ubiquitin E3 ligase activity and phosphor binding-mediated protein interaction, respectively. However, a large number of BRCA1 mutations in real-world screens of patients with cancer are located in or near the middle region of the protein rather than in its RING and BRCTs domains. In addition, based on the HR deficiency-mediated anti-tumour effect of PARP inhibitors, patients with cancer harbouring non-BRCA HR gene mutations should receive a similar benefit as those harbouring BRCA mutations. Counterintuitively, among dozens of HR factors that have been identified over the past decades, mutations in very few non-BRCA HR genes (e.g., PALB2) in tumours have conferred sensitivity to PARP inhibitors in clinical trials. For certain core HR factors such as ataxia telangiectasia mutated (ATM), recent evidence demonstrates that the gene loss confers significant sensitivity to ataxia telangiectasia and Rad3-related (ATR) inhibition but not PARP inhibition. All these clues inspired us to explore the unknown role of PARP inhibitors in tumour chemotherapy.

In this study, we analysed all BRCA1 missense mutations identified in patients with breast cancer in the COSMIC and cBioPortal databases that integrate genomic data and clinical profiles from both public databases including TCGA and published reports, and found that most of them reside in or near the middle region of the BRCA1, which is not essential for DNA damage repair. Representative mutations, R1085I and E1222Q, lead to an error of transient extra spindles during mitosis. Interestingly, the transient extra poles could be clustered by centrosomal PAR that is mainly synthesized by PARP2 but not PARP1, independently of DNA damage response. Mechanistically, highly branched and negatively charged PAR attracts positively charged centrosomal proteins such as SFI1 and ZNF721 and restricts the escape of extra centrosomes out of the spindle pole during mitosis. PARP inhibitors effectively inhibit PAR synthesis at centrosomes and cause senescence of tumour cells by abrogating the correction of mitotic extra-pole error. These findings uncover a different mechanism by which PARP inhibitors suppress tumours and may inspire a new therapeutic strategy for centrosome error-related tumours.

Mapping BRCA1 mutations from patients with breast cancer in COSMIC and cBioPortal databases

A total of 297 BRCA1 gene mutations from 277 patient samples were available for analysis. Cell culture, PARP inhibitor treatment, live cell imaging, and IR treatment

HEK-293T (RRID: CVCL_0063), A2780 (RRID: CVCL_0134), C4-2 (RRID: CVCL_4782), and MCF7 (RRID: CVCL_0031) cells were grown in DMEM (Gibco, 11965118) with 10% foetal bovine serum (FBS, VivaCell, C04001). Cell synchronization

To synchronize MCF7 cells to the early S phase, double thymidine block procedure was employed. Initially, when cells reached around 40% confluence, they were exposed to thymidine (2 mM, 15 h) (Selleck, Cat# S4803). Subsequently, they were released for 9 h and subjected to a second block with thymidine (2 mM, 15 h). After PBS washing extensively, the cells were exposed to nocodazole (0.1 μg/mL, 10 h) (Sigma, Cat# M1404). The arrested cells were washed with warm medium containing biotin-phenol (Adipogen, Cat# CDX-B0270) 3 times and released for 45 min to progress into the mitotic phase, after which they were utilized for the APEX labelling reaction.

Immunofluorescence and 3D reconstruction

Cells were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde for 30 min and then permeabilized with 0.5% Triton-X-100 (Sigma, Cat# X100) solution for 30 min. For centrosomal PAR staining, the cells were fixed and permeabilized simultaneously with pre-cooled methanol for 5 min at −20 °C. The permeabilized cells were blocked with 1% BSA solution for 1 h, and subsequently exposed to specific primary antibody dilutions (1:200–1:500) at 4 °C for 8 h. After washing with washing solution (0.1% Tween-20 and 0.01% Triton-X 100 in PBS), cells were incubated with a suitable fluorescent secondary antibody for 1–2 h. Ultimately, samples were mounted on glass slides after cell nuclei staining with Hoechst 33342 (10 μg/mL) (Sigma, Cat# 14533). Image capture was performed employing a Carl Zeiss 880 confocal laser scanning microscope. Z-stack scanning (0.33-μm intervals) was performed to acquire images of the whole centrosome for 3D reconstruction. After analysing fluorescence signals of chromosomes, BRCA1, PCNT, and PAR using the surface function within the Imaris software (Bitplane, Belfast, United Kingdom), 3D reconstruction and volume-specific values of centrosome components in mitotic cells were obtained.

DR-GFP HR reporter assay

The DR-GFP reporter plasmids (pDRGFP, Addgene, Cat# 26475) were transfected into MCF7 cells expressing either BRCA1WT or various BRCA1 variants using Lipofectamine 3000 (Thermo, Cat# L3000015). Subsequently, the transfected cells were cultured for an additional 24 h. Following this period, the I-SceI plasmids (Addgene, Cat# 26477) were electroporated into the indicated MCF7 cells to induce a double-strand break. After 48 h of culture, the cells were rinsed with PBS, detached using trypsin, and centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 3 min. The cells were then washed again with PBS and resuspended in PBS. Subsequently, 10,000 cells from each sample was subjected to analysis using a BD FACScan flow cytometry system (Becton Dickinson, Franklin, NJ, USA). The proportion of GFP-positive cells was determined using FlowJo software.

APEX2-mediated biotinylation

APEX2-mediated biotinylation was conducted as previously described with slight modifications. In brief, the substrate (500 μM biotin-phenol) was added to the medium of PCNT-APEX2-EGFP stable-expressing MCF7 cells (∼3 × 107), and incubated for 45 min. The biotinylation reaction was initiated by adding 1 mM H2O2 for 1 min at RT, and completed by washing the cells 3 times with “quencher solution”. Next, the cells were collected and lysed, followed by gentle sonication on ice and centrifugation at 12,000 rpm at 4 °C. After adding 10 volumes of pre-cooled methanol to the supernatants, the samples were placed in a −80 °C refrigerator for 6 h to precipitate proteins, and then centrifugated at 4000 rpm at 4 °C for 30 min to collect proteins. The precipitated proteins were dissolved in 1% SDS RIPA lysis buffer and subjected to concentration detection. Biotinylated proteins were separated by incubating 4 mg proteins with 150 μL streptavidin-coated beads (Thermo, Cat# 88817) in 0.2% SDS RIPA buffer on a rotator at RT for 2 h. The beads were washed sequentially with RIPA lysis buffer (1 mL, twice), 1 M KCl (1 mL, once) (Sigma, Cat# P9541), 0.1 M Na2CO3 (1 mL, once) (Sigma, Cat# 451614), 2 M urea (Sigma, Cat# U5378) in 10 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.0, 1 mL, once) (Thermo, Cat# 15568025), and RIPA lysis buffer (1 mL, twice). Subsequently, the beads were boiled in protein loading buffer containing biotin (2 mM) (Sigma, Cat# B4501) and DTT (20 mM) (Sigma, Cat# 10708984001) to dissociate biotinylated proteins.

Western blot and immunoprecipitation

For western blot, pre-cooled RIPA buffer was used to lyse cells and to obtain total proteins, which were separated by SDS-PAGE, and then electrically transferred onto PVDF membranes (Millipore, Cat# IPVH00010). After blocking with 5% skimmed milk, the membranes were exposed to specific primary antibodies for 8 h at 4 °C. Subsequently, the membranes were washed with TBST and exposed to the corresponding HRP-conjugated secondary antibody for 2 h at RT. Additionally, streptavidin-HRP conjugate was employed to visualize biotin-labelled proteins in the APEX reaction. Protein bands were visualized using a western blotting substrate (Thermo, 32106). For immunoprecipitation, approximately 3 × 106 cells were collected and lysed in 500 μL pre-cooled RIPA lysis buffer. After centrifugation, the supernatant was taken out and incubated with 2 μg of the indicated primary antibody and 40 μL of pre-cleared protein A/G beads (Cytiva, Cat# 17127901, 17061801) for 6 h at 4 °C with gentle rotation. The beads were subsequently washed extensively to remove non-specific binding. Finally, the immunoprecipitated proteins were dissociated through boiling the beads and detected by western blotting.

Senescent cell staining

The senescent cell Staining reagents (Cell Signaling, Cat# 9860) were used for the assay. Cells were washed with PBS twice after fixation in 1 × fixative solution, and exposed to staining solution at 37 °C overnight. For tumour tissues, frozen sections (5 μm thick) were prepared after embedding in OCT compound (Yuabio, Cat# 4583) and freezing in liquid nitrogen. The sections were washed with PBS after fixation with 1% formalin, and exposed to staining solution at 37 °C overnight. The BX51 microscope (Olympus, Japan), incorporating a DP73 CCD photographic system, was utilized for capturing the images of senescent cells.

RT-qPCR

Approximately 3 × 106 cells were used for total RNA extraction using TRIzol (Gibco, Cat# 15596026). The RNA samples were quantified using NanoDrop (Thermo, USA), and reverse transcribed into first-strand cDNA using a reverse transcription reagent (Thermo, Cat# K1691). The synthesized cDNA was utilized as templates for qPCR reactions, which were completed using SYBR Green (Thermo, Cat# A25742) on the StepOnePlus instrument (Thermo, USA). The threshold cycle (Ct) values were determined, and the relative expression levels of target genes were calculated using the comparative Ct method (2−ΔΔCt), with normalization to reference gene GAPDH. The primer sequences are listed in Table S1.

Calculation of protein isoelectric point and prediction of charged residues on the protein surface

The amino acid sequences of 530 centrosome proteins were downloaded from UniProt (https://www.uniprot.org/), and then submitted to Compute pI/Mw tool of ExPASy server (http://www.expasy.org) for the calculation of their isoelectric points (pI). Using SPIDER3-Single, a neural network-based solvent accessibility prediction tool, we estimated each residue's absolute solvent accessible surface area (SASA) of specific protein. Predicted results were normalized by the maximum SASA of each residue to get the so-called relative solvent accessibility. Then the ‘exposed’ residues, defined by relative solvent accessibility >40%, were obtained and considered as surface residues in this work. With the predicted ‘exposed’ residues, we counted the number of positively and negatively charged residues on the protein surface. Subsequently, we computed ratios of positively charged residues to negatively charged residues. Due to lack of the experimental structures for the candidate proteins, we performed protein structure predictions using AlphaFold2, a cutting-edge tool for accurate prediction. Using the predicted structures, we calculated the electrostatic potential of each candidate protein with the built-in electrostatic package of PyMol (https://pymol.org/) and coloured the protein surface according to the electrostatic potential. All of the parameters were set to default in the above calculations.

Microscale thermophoresis

MST was conducted by our previous procedure. In brief, RED-NHS kit (NanoTemper, Cat# MO-L001) was used to label proteins. Next, equal labelled protein (10–50 nM) was mixed with twofold serially diluted ADP-ribose in an optimal solution thoroughly, and then aspirated into glass capillaries (NanoTemper, Cat# MO-K2022). After a 15-min incubation at room temperature, the glass capillaries were placed into the Monolith NT.115 instrument (NanoTemper, Germany). The timeline for measurement was fluorescence before 5 s, MST for half a minute, fluorescence after 5 s, delay for 25 s. For all the measurements, 200–1000 counts were obtained for the fluorescence intensity. The power was set to 20% and 40% during the measurements. The normalized fluorescence (Fnorm) was determined as the ratio of fluorescence following thermodiffusion (F1) to initial fluorescence or fluorescence after T-jump (F0). Kd values were calculated by the software from NanoTemper.

Centrosome isolation, centrosome incubation, and centrosome capture assay

Centrosome isolation was conducted following a previously established procedure. In brief, microtubules and actin filaments of mitotic cells or interphase cells were depolymerized by 1 μM nocodazole (Sigma, Cat# M1404) and 10 mM Cytochalasin D (Thermo, Cat# PHZ1063). After washing, cells were suspended with 8% sucrose in 0.1 × PBS and then lysed in specific buffer (1 × protease inhibitor cocktail [Roche, Cat# 04693116001], 0.5 mM MgCl2 [Sigma, Cat# M4880], 0.5% NP-40 [Thermo, Cat# 85124], 1 mM HEPES [Sigma, Cat# H3375] at pH 7.2, 0.1% β-mercaptoethanol [Sigma, Cat# M3148]). The supernatant was taken out after centrifugation and filtered using a nylon mesh. The filtered supernatant was treated with DNAse1 (Roche, Cat# 10104159001) to remove the remaining DNA followed by concentration on a 60% sucrose cushion at 7500 rpm for 30 min. Lastly, the centrosomes were further enriched by sucrose gradient centrifugation at 25,000 rpm. The procedures above were conducted on ice or at 4 °C.

For the incubation of mitotic centrosomes with proteins, the purified protein was labelled with TRITC fluorescent dye using a protein labelling kit (Invitrogen, Cat# A30007). Then isolated centrosomes were incubated with TRITC fluorescent dye-labelled protein (5 μM) in PB buffer (80 mM PIPES (Sigma, Cat# P6757) supplemented with 1 mM each of EGTA (Sigma, Cat# E3889) and MgC12 (Sigma, Cat# M4880), adjusted the pH to 6.8 using KOH), with or without 3 nM PARG, for 10 min at 37 °C. Once the incubated mixture underwent centrifugation at 10,000 g to allow sedimentation onto a glass coverslip, the sedimented centrosomes were rinsed twice with PB buffer. After fixation with methanol at −20 °C for 5 min, the fixed centrosomes were used for the immunofluorescence experiments.

For centrosome capture assay, isolated interphase centrosomes lacking PAR were incubated with GST protein or PAR-coated Protein G Sepharose beads (Cytiva, Cat# 17061801) in PB buffer, in the absence or presence of 3 nM PARG, under room temperature for 10 min. The bead-based capture of centrosomes was visualized using a Carl Zeiss 880 confocal microscope.

Real-time cell analyzer (RTCA) for monitoring cell proliferation

The RTCA system from xCELLigence was used for continuous, non-invasive monitoring of cell proliferation. Briefly, an E-16-well plate (Roche) was seeded with 5000–10,000 cells per well. Impedance changes exhibited a correlation with the quantity of adherent cells, as detected using microelectrodes on the plate's bottom surface. With cells incubated at 37 °C under 5% CO2 for a period of 70 h, each well's impedance value was automatically recorded and then transformed into a cell proliferation index, which represents cell proliferation status.

Xenograft and patient-derived xenograft tumour mouse model

All mice were raised in the Animal Centre of Peking University Health Science Centre in an SPF environment. For the tumour growth curve of MCF7, A2780, or C4-2 cells in xenograft experiments, 100 μL solution (50 μL Matrigel and 50 μL PBS) containing 2 × 106 MCF7, A2780, or C4-2 cells was implanted into flank regions of BALB/c nude female mice (RRID: IMSR_CRL:194) aged five to six weeks. Tumour volumes were weekly measured with callipers. Once reaching a tumour size of 50 mm3, mice were randomized and intraperitoneally injected daily with either vehicle or niraparib (25 mg/kg). Each group included six mice. Tumour growth was monitored until it reached approximately 1500 mm3 in the control group, which occurred about 40 days after implantation. Tumour volume = longest diameter × (shortest diameter)2 × 1/2.

The PDX model was constructed by following our previous procedure. Tumour tissues were subcutaneously introduced into the flanks of 5- to 6-week-old NOD-SCID female mice (RRID: IMSR_CRL:394) to initiate the first round of tumour growth. Subsequently, fresh tumour fragments were removed and re-implanted into flank regions of BALB/C nude female mice aged five to six weeks to establish the second round of tumour growth. Tumour volumes were weekly measured with callipers. Once reaching a tumour size of 200 mm3, mice were randomized and intraperitoneally injected daily with either vehicle or niraparib (25 mg/kg). Each group included six mice. Tumour growth was monitored daily until it reached approximately 1000 mm3 in the control groups. Tumour volume = longest diameter × (shortest diameter)2 × 1/2.

Comet assay

A neutral comet assay for DSB detection was performed by our previous procedure. Cells were subjected to trypsinization, followed by double rinsing with pre-cooled PBS. The cell concentration was adjusted to 2 × 104/mL, and subsequently cell suspension (0.4 mL) was mixed with low-melting-temperature agarose (1%, 1.2 mL; Sigma, Cat# A9414) at 40 °C. After mixing, the gel was immediately pipetted onto slides. Once the gel had solidified, the neutral lysis buffer was used to cover the slides for 8 h in darkness at 37 °C for cellular lysis. Subsequently, double rinsing was performed with washing buffer for half an hour. After electrophoresis (20 V, 25 min) and propidium iodide staining (2.5 μg/mL, 20 min) (Sigma, Cat# 537059), slides were observed with an Olympus IX73 microscope. The images were analysed using Comet Assay IV software. Tail moment = tail DNA % (tail DNA content as a percentage of comet DNA content) × tail length (distance along the x-axis of the comet between the intensity centroids of the head and the tail).

Statistical analyses

Unless otherwise stated, each experiment was carried out in triplicate. The figures presented means ± standard deviations (SDs) or means ± standard errors (SEMs). Statistical analyses were conducted using Student's t-test with GraphPad Prism software, considering p < 0.05 as the threshold for significance. Detailed statistical information can be found in the figure legends.

Results

BRCA1 mutations cause transient extra spindle poles that can be clustered

To determine the full-scale distribution of cancer-associated mutations in BRCA1 from clinical patients with breast cancer, we analysed all known BRCA1 mutations in COSMIC and cBioPortal databases. Among 297 reported BRCA1 mutations, most of them (56.9%) are missense substitutions, while 15.2% are frameshift deletions, 11.5% are nonsense substitutions, 8.1% are frameshift insertions, etc (Fig. 1a, Table S2). For all missense mutations, we noticed that they are evenly distributed along the full sequence of the BRCA1 protein (Fig. 1b). Using CScape, the cancer variant predictor that predicts the oncogenic status of somatic variations, we assessed the potential risk of 67 missense mutations located within the middle region of BRCA1 (500–1400 amino acids). Fifteen out of 67 variations were predicted as deleterious mutations with pathogenic scores higher than 0.5 (Fig. 1c). To identify the potential effects of these mutations, we selected four candidates that typically reside in or near the middle region of BRCA1, i.e., R613K, R1085I, E1222Q, and T1249P and used CRISPR-mediated knock-in to insert each of them into the genome of MCF7 cells, a well-characterized breast cancer cell line with wild-type (WT) BRCA1. CRISPR-mediated knock-in was used to avoid variations in the expression levels of these genes, which may be caused by traditional transfection methods, such as transient transfection or lentivirus-based transfection (Fig. S1a). We first examined protein levels of endogenous BRCA1 in MCF7 cells bearing different BRCA1 mutants and found that these mutations did not affect the global level of BRCA1 protein in whole cells (Fig. S1b). Next, we explored whether these mutations affected DDR of BRCA1. R1699Q, a mutation that is known to affect DDR, was used as a positive control (Fig. S1a). WT MCF7 cells or cells bearing different BRCA1 mutations were treated with the DNA damage agent of ionizing radiation (IR). Compared with BRCA1WT, BRCA1R1699Q formed much fewer foci after IR treatment (4 h post IR) marked by γH2AX. However, BRCA1R613K, BRCA1R1085I, BRCA1E1222Q, and BRCA1T1249P formed obvious foci at DNA damage sites as BRCA1WT (Fig. S1c and d). Meanwhile, we detected the foci formation of RAD51, a core recombinase for HR repair, in these IR-treated cells. No difference in RAD51 foci formation was observed between the mutant and WT cells, except for the BRCA1R1699Q cells (Fig. S1e and f). After 16 h of recovery from IR, BRCA1R1699Q cells still harboured massive DNA damage as visualized in the comet assay. In contrast, cells expressing BRCA1WT, BRCA1R613K, BRCA1R1085I, BRCA1E1222Q, or BRCA1T1249P had repaired most of damage (Fig. S1g and h). In addition, we further investigated whether these BRCA1 variants were associated with functional changes in HR using the HR reporter assay, which is based on the expression of functional GFP as a result of HR in response to a double-strand break induced by the I-SceI endonuclease. HR activity was found to be substantially reduced following I-SceI expression in BRCA1R1699Q cells compared with BRCA1WT cells. However, BRCA1R613K, BRCA1R1085I, BRCA1E1222Q, and BRCA1T1249P cells exhibited normal HR activity as BRCA1WT (Fig. S1i). These results indicate that mutations of R613K, R1085I, E1222Q, and T1249P in BRCA1 do not affect DDR.

Given that BRCA1 is localized at mitotic centrosomes and serves as a negative regulator of centrosome amplification, we were curious whether these BRCA1 mutants would affect the dynamics of centrosomes and mitosis. Thus, we labelled microtubules and nuclei in MCF7 cells bearing different BRCA1 mutants and monitored the mitotic behaviour of these cells by live-cell imaging. Like control cells with BRCA1WT, cells bearing BRCA1R613K and BRCA1T1249P formed normal spindles and completed mitosis smoothly (Fig. 1d and e, Video S1–S5). However, cells bearing BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q formed transient extra spindle poles during a prolonged mitosis stage, with extra-pole rates (the proportion of mitotic cells with additional spindle poles) of 37.6% and 43.2%, respectively. Intriguingly, most (78.2%–85.5%) of the cells with transient extra poles clustered the extra foci into two poles like those observed in normal cells, completed mitosis, and survived (Fig. 1d and e, Video S1–S5). To confirm the transient extra pole phenotype, we stably knocked down endogenous BRCA1 in WT MCF7 cells using shRNA (Fig. S1j) and monitored mitosis. Again, we still observed transient extra spindle poles in 40.4% of mitotic cells, and nearly 80% of these cells could cluster the extra poles and complete mitosis (Fig. 1f and g, Video S6 and S7). These findings echo the proven function of BRCA1 in controlling centrosome amplification and provide a different perspective for understanding BRCA1's function.

To determine how these BRCA1 variants led to transient extra spindle poles, we examined BRCA1 localization on centrosomes during mitosis in MCF7 cells. As shown in Fig. 1h and i, BRCA1WT, BRCA1R613K, and BRCA1T1249P were clearly retained at centrosomes labelled by centrosome marker pericentrin (PCNT), whereas signals of centrosomal BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q were significantly weaker. To further validate the reduction of BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q proteins at centrosomes, we took advantage of the APEX2 proximity labelling technique and established a centrosomal protein capture system in MCF7 BRCA1WT and BRCA1mutant cells by stably expressing a centrosome targeting APEX2 (PCNT-APEX2-EGFP) that can label centrosomal proteome with biotin in live cells (Fig. 1j, Video S8). In mitotic synchronized cells (Fig. 1k), PCNT-APEX2-EGFP successfully captured centrosome proteins (Fig. 1l and m). We found that BRCA1R613K and BRCA1T1249P were present in centrosomes at levels similar to BRCA1WT, while the levels of BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q were significantly lower (Fig. 1n). Given that BRCA1 suppresses centrosome amplification, we proposed that the R1085I and E1222Q mutations weaken the ability of BRCA1 to localize at centrosomes, leading to the occurrence of transient extra spindle poles during mitosis.

PAR is enriched at centrosomes and synthesized by PARP2 and TNKS

Earlier studies demonstrated that PAR is present on the spindle and spindle poles in Xenopus egg extracts and HeLa cells. We were curious whether PAR and BRCA1 are closely associated at centrosomes, and thus co-stained BRCA1 and PAR in diverse types of cells including 293T, MCF10A, MCF7, SKOV3 cells, as well as primary breast and ovarian cancer cells from patients. Under normal conditions without DNA damage agents, BRCA1 was mainly expressed in the nucleus, while little PAR was synthesized, in all types of interphase cells (Fig. S2a). Of note, both BRCA1 and PAR were obviously present at centrosomes in all of these mitotic cells (Fig. 2a and b). To validate the existence of PAR at centrosomes, we transfected CEP192-PARG-EGFP that locates PAR glycohydrolase (PARG) at centrosomes, into MCF7 cells. The fusion protein CEP192-PARG localized at centrosomes specifically degrades centrosomal PAR (Fig. S2b and c). With 3D modelling analysis of the relationship between BRCA1 and PAR at the centrosome, we found that BRCA1 was localized in the inner part of the centrosome, while PAR occupied a large area of the centrosome that was even bigger than pericentriolar material marker PCNT (Fig. 2c and d). To identify the polymerase(s) responsible for PAR synthesis at centrosomes, we examined the subcellular localization of all PARPs that can catalyse the production of PAR rather than mono (ADP-ribose), namely PARP1, PARP2, PARP3, PARP4, and PARP5A/5B (TNKS1/2). Unexpectedly, in addition to TNKS1/2, which were previously recognized to be localized at centrosomes, PARP2 also showed signals at centrosomes (Fig. 2e and f). Using our centrosomal protein capture system, MCF7 cells stably expressing PCNT-APEX2-EGFP, we acquired centrosomal proteins that were biotinylated and confirmed the presence of PARP2 at the mitotic centrosome (Fig. 2g). Also, we confirmed the existence of PAR in the APEX2-captured extracts by dot blot (Fig. 2h). Then, to study the contribution of TNKS1/2 and PARP2 to PAR synthesis at centrosomes, we used the TNKS1/2 inhibitor G007-LK, the PARP1 specific inhibitor AZD5305, and three other PARP inhibitors (niraparib, olaparib, and rucaparib) targeting both PARP1 and PARP2. Notably, PARP1 inhibition resulted in nearly no decrease, and TNKS1/2 inhibition caused a mild decrease in PAR at centrosomes, while the remaining three PARP inhibitors significantly suppressed PAR synthesis at centrosomes (Fig. 2i and j). Consistently, knockdown of TNKS1/2 by shRNA (Fig. S2d) lessened centrosomal PAR moderately, whereas depletion of PARP2 but not PARP1 (Fig. S2e and f) significantly suppressed centrosomal PAR (Fig. 2k and l), confirming that PARP2 contributed most to PAR synthesis at centrosomes, which could be significantly inhibited by common PARP inhibitors. These results strongly inspired us the speculation that PARP inhibitors may affect the status of centrosomes.

PARP inhibitors prevent the clustering of extra spindle poles in BRCA1 mutant cells and cause cell senescence

Considering the polymeric structure and large-scale molecule interactions of PAR, we hypothesized that centrosomal PAR may medicate the clustering of extra spindle poles resulting from the mutated BRCA1 (BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q). Thus, MCF7-BRCA1WT and MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells were treated with PARP inhibitor, niraparib. Live-cell imaging was performed subsequently. Intriguingly, PARP inhibition resulted in failures of extra pole clustering and cell division in both BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q MCF7 cells but not in BRCA1WT MCF7 cells (Fig. 3a and b, Video S9–S11). When we harvested mitotic cells using the mitotic shake-off method and continued culturing them with niraparib, we found that a considerable portion of the BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells contained multiple nuclei, a giant nucleus, or micronuclei, and were arrested at the next G1 stage (Fig. 3c). The phenotype resembles the typical process of ‘mitotic slippage’, a phenomenon in which cells exit from mitosis without undergoing cytokinesis to generate tetraploid or polyploid cells. Mitotic slippage frequently results in cell death or senescence through the activation of p53. Therefore, we further investigated whether niraparib-induced mitotic slippage causes cellular senescence in MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells. We performed SA-β-Gal staining on niraparib-treated MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q mitotic cells and found the occurrence of cellular senescence (Fig. 3d and e). Meanwhile, we examined a panel of senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) genes, namely MMP12, CXCL1, IL1α, and GM-CSF, and found higher expression levels of these genes in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells, validating the occurrence of senescence (Fig. 3f). Consistent with these results, the p53 expression in these cells was also elevated (Fig. 3g). When WT-BRCA1 was re-transfected into the BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q MCF7 cells (Fig. S3a), we observed that the extra-pole rates in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells decreased to 10.6% and 11.7% (Fig. S3b and c, Video S12–S14), respectively, significantly lower than those in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells (Fig. 1d and e, Video S1–S5). The results indicate that WT-BRCA1 could rescue the transient extra spindle poles during mitosis in MCF7 cells bearing BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q. Accordingly, when we harvested the WT-BRCA1 re-transfected mitotic cells using the mitotic shake-off method and continued culturing them with niraparib, we found that senescence of MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells was significantly rescued (Fig. S3d and e), confirming BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q variant is the substantial cause of niraparib-induced senescence phenotype in cancer cells. Based on the results in Fig. 2, we confirmed that PARP2 contributes the most to PAR synthesis at centrosomes. Subsequently, we knocked down PARP2 by shRNA in MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells (Fig. S3f) and observed that the depletion of PARP2 promoted cellular senescence (Fig. S3g and h), resembling the effect of niraparib. In contrast, depletion of PARP1 did not generate similar effects on cells. Moreover, when we treated MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells with the PARP1-specific inhibitor AZD5305, it did not induce cellular senescence in cancer cells as niraparib did (Fig. S3i and j), indicating that the inhibition of PARP2-synthesized PAR at centrosomes caused senescence in BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells. Additionally, to confirm that PARP inhibitor-induced senescence was caused by extra spindle poles but not defective DDR, we established cells prone to developing extra spindle poles by inducibly overexpressing PLK4, a multiple-centriole inducer, in MCF7 cells (referred to as MCF7-PLK4 cells, Fig. S3k). Inducible upregulation of PLK4 resulted in a significantly higher rate of transient extra pole formation followed by clustering in MCF7-PLK4 cells compared with WT MCF7 cells. The phenotype of MCF7-PLK4 cells resembled that of BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells. In contrast, in the presence of niraparib, MCF7-PLK4 cells bearing extra poles were incapable of clustering the poles and failed to divide (Fig. 3h and i, Video S15 and S16). Similarly, when these cells were collected through mitotic shake-off and continued to be cultured with niraparib, they underwent senescence that was validated with SA-β-Gal staining, p53 expression, and SASP gene expression (Fig. 3j–m). These data indicate that the PARP inhibitor prevents the clustering of extra spindle poles in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells and causes cell senescence.

Negatively charged PAR attracts and traps centrosomes

Next, we explored the mechanism by which centrosomal PAR mediates the clustering of extra spindle poles. The PAR polymer is abundant in a significant negative charge due to the presence of two negatively charged phosphate groups in each ADP-ribose of PAR. We were thus curious whether this huge negatively charged matrix could attract centrosomal proteins and achieve centrosome clustering. Toward this end, we extracted the information of all known centrosomal proteins (530 proteins) from the Human Protein Atlas (www.proteinatlas.org), the most comprehensive subcellular map of human proteome, and analysed the charge property of each protein using the ExPASy server for computation of theoretical isoelectric point (pI) of proteins. Among all the 530 centrosome proteins, we filtered out the top 100 proteins with the highest pI values, ranging 8.67–11.58, which may be significantly positively charged (Fig. 4a, Table S3). For these 100 high-pI proteins, we predicted the likely surface residues from protein sequences using the tool SPIDER3-Single. Then we counted the number of positively and negatively charged residues among the surface residues (Fig. S4a, Table S4), and calculated the ratio of positively charged to negatively charged residues (Fig. 4b, Fig. S4b, Table S4). All the 100 proteins were ranked by this ratio, and the top 20 proteins with the highest ratios were selected for subsequent experimental verification, as these proteins may have a surface of positive electrostatic potential and therefore exhibit high affinities with negatively charged PAR. Also, the 3D structures of the top 20 candidate proteins were predicted and the electrostatic potentials on their surfaces were represented for reference (Fig. S4c).

To validate the abilities of the 20 candidates to bind to PAR, we purified or purchased these proteins (Fig. 4c, Fig. S5a) and examined their affinity with ADP-ribose (the structural and functional unit of PAR), by microscale thermophoresis (MST), a sensitive analysis of binding affinities of biomolecules. Among the 20 candidates, SFI1 and ZNF721 showed the highest affinity for PAR, with dissociation constants (Kd) of 1.91 μM and 4.07 μM, respectively (Fig. 4d, Fig. S5b). Recently, a study revealed that SFI1 serves as a molecular component of the centriole, co-localizing with a specific pool of CETN2/3 at the distal tip of human centrioles. SFI1/CETN complexes play a pivotal role, being recruited during the early stages of procentriole assembly. Depleting SFI1 leads to the elimination of the CETN pool at the distal tip of centrioles and defective centriole architecture, but with no effect on centriole duplication. Due to the positive charge carried by SFI1, negatively charged PAR attracts the SFI1 protein within the extra spindle poles, thereby achieving centrosome clustering in MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells. To validate the interaction between SFI1 and PAR in cellular context, mitotic MCF7 cells were collected, lysed, and then subjected to co-immunoprecipitation. As shown in Fig. 4e and f, reciprocal immunoprecipitation by SFI1 or PAR antibody indicated the association between PAR and SFI1. In parallel, we also confirmed the interaction between ZNF721 and PAR in cellular context (Fig. S5c and d). Next, we investigated the binding affinities of SFI1 and ZNF721 with the intact centrosome enriched with PAR. MCF7 cells expressing PCNT-EGFP were synchronized to mitosis and fresh centrosomes were isolated by density gradient fractionation. We confirmed the existence of PAR on isolated centrosomes by immunofluorescent staining against PAR and the centriole protein CETN3 (Fig. 4g). The isolated centrosomes were incubated with purified SFI1 and ZNF721, which were prelabelled with TRITC fluorescent dye, and then sedimented onto a glass coverslip. Two centrosome proteins (CEP68 and UBE2Q1) that were not predicted to have a positive charge were used as negative controls. As seen in Fig. 4h, both purified proteins of SFI1 and ZNF721 interacted with PAR-enriched centrosomes. In contrast, the signal of CEP68 nad UBE2Q1 were almost undetectable on these centrosomes. When we added purified protein of PAR glycohydrolase (PARG) that specifically degrades PAR, into the solution containing SFI1 and centrosomes, nearly no signal of SFI1 was observed on the centrosomes, further confirming the interaction between SFI1 and PAR. In addition, we pre-coated beads with purified GST protein or PAR and incubated the beads with S-staged centrosomes, which are enriched with SFI1 but lack PAR. Consistently, the isolated centrosomes could be reciprocally attracted by the PAR-coated beads but not GST-coated beads. Similarly, this binding could be abolished with PARG (Fig. 4i). To validate the cellular function of SFI1 and ZNF721, we knocked down SFI1 or ZNF721 in MCF7-BRCA1WT and MCF7-BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q cells (Fig. S5e and f). As expected, SFI1 or ZNF721 knockdown resulted in a higher rate of cells bearing mitotic errors in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells than that in BRCA1WT cells (Fig. 4j and k, Fig. S5g and h). Accordingly, obvious senescence signals were observed in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells but not in BRCA1WT cells upon SFI1 or ZNF721 knockdown (Fig. 4l and m, Fig. S5i and j). These results indicate that PAR matrix can attract and trap centrosomes by binding centrosome proteins such as SFI1 and ZNF721.

BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells are sensitive to PARP inhibitor in the absence of DNA damage agents

Previously, it was commonly believed that the antitumor effect of PARP inhibitors primarily depended on the existence of DNA damage, particularly DSBs, which require BRCA1 for repair. We thus examined intrinsic DNA damage (SSBs and DSBs) in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q knock-in MCF7 cells without DNA damage agents. Positive controls for SSBs and DSBs were established using BRCA1WT MCF7 cells treated with H2O2 or IR, respectively. Notably, without an artificial DNA damage agent, all the BRCA1WT and BRCA1mutant cells contained low level of SSBs (detected with the marker XRCC1) and few numbers of DSBs (detected with the marker γH2AX), which were significantly different from their positive controls treated with H2O2 and IR (Fig. 5a–d). Upon treatment with PARP inhibitor niraparib without DNA damage agents, the proliferation of cells bearing R1085I or E1222Q mutation was significantly suppressed, while WT cells and cells bearing R1699Q, a BRCA1-BRCT mutation that causes DSB repair deficiency, showed lower sensitivity to the drug (Fig. 5e). To directly compare PARP inhibitor sensitivity of BRCA1E1222Q cells and BRCA1R1699Q cells, we distinguished these cells by stably expressing GFP and RFP, respectively. An equal number of BRCA1E1222Q and BRCA1R1699Q cells were mixed and cultured in the presence of niraparib without DNA damage agents. After 70 h of culture, we observed that 73.5% of the surviving cells were BRCA1R1699Q cells (GFP), and only 26.5% cells were BRCA1E1222Q cells (RFP) (Fig. 5f and g), confirming that cells expressing BRCA1E1222Q are more sensitive to niraparib. These data indicate that BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells, but not BRCA1R1699Q cells, are more sensitive to the PARP inhibitor without DNA damage agents.

PARP inhibitor niraparib suppresses tumour growth in vivo

To validate the antitumor effect of the PARP inhibitor niraparib on tumours bearing mutations in the middle region of BRCA1, we implanted different types of cancer cells, namely MCF7-BRCA1WT, BRCA1R1085I, MCF7-BRCA1E1222Q, and MCF7-BRCA1R1699Q cells, into BALB/c nude mice. All cancer cells developed into tumour masses in mice (Fig. 6a). Once reaching a tumour size of 50 mm3, the mice were intraperitoneally injected daily with either vehicle or niraparib (25 mg/kg). We found that the drug had different effects on these tumours. Specifically, the mean reductions in volumes of MCF7-BRCA1WT, MCF7-BRCA1R1085I, MCF7-BRCA1E1222Q, and MCF7-BRCA1R1699Q tumours were 19.3%, 81.6%, 78.8%, and 56.8%, respectively (Fig. 6a), suggesting that niraparib had a stronger effect on BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours. When harvesting tumour masses and detecting mitotic status by immunofluorescent staining, we found a significantly higher rate of cells with mitotic errors in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours than in BRCA1WT and BRCA1R1699Q tumours (Fig. 6b and c). Accordingly, obvious senescence signals were observed in both BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours but not in BRCA1WT and BRCA1R1699Q tumours (Fig. 6d and e). In addition, DSB accumulation was detected in BRCA1R1699Q tumours but not in other types of tumours (Fig. 6f and g). The occurrence of senescence in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours and the existence of DSBs in BRCA1R1699Q tumours were further confirmed by the examination of SASP gene expression (MMP12, CXCL1, IL1α, and GM-CSF) and by neutral comet assay (Fig. 6h–j).

In parallel, we looked up clinical information and selected primary tumour masses from three patients with breast cancer carrying different mutations, i.e., #1. p.S955del (amino acid S955 deleted, representing a BRCA1middle mutation tumour), #2. p.I1824Dfs∗3 (a frameshift resulting in a mutation from I1824 to D, and termination at amino acid 1826, representing a BRCA1BRCT mutation tumour), and #3. p.Q1785X (terminated at amino acid Q1785, also representing a BRCA1BRCT mutation tumour) (Table S5), for a patient-derived xenograft (PDX) tumour mouse model study. Primary cancer cells expressing BRCA1S955del, BRCA1I1824Dfs∗3, and BRCA1Q1785X developed into tumour masses in mice (Fig. S6a). Once reaching a tumour size of 200 mm3, those mice were intraperitoneally injected daily with either vehicle or niraparib (25 mg/kg) for 10 days. The mean reductions in volumes of BRCA1S955del, BRCA1I1824Dfs∗3, and BRCA1Q1785X tumours were 67.6%, 59.2%, and 64.9%, respectively (Fig. S6a), reflecting the efficacy of niraparib on these BRCA mutate tumours. Then, the tumour masses were harvested and subjected to paraffin sectioning. Using a Zeiss Axioscan.Z1 microscope, which can capture whole-section images, we recorded all mitotic cells in the sections by co-staining of α-tubulin and the centrosome marker PCNT (Fig. 6k, Fig. S6b). We found much fewer mitotic cells (9 and 13) in whole sections of BRCA1I1824Dfs∗3 and BRCA1Q1785X tumours. In contrast, 42 mitotic cells were found in the whole section of BRCA1S955del tumour, and 69.0% of these mitotic cells displayed extra spindle poles (Fig. 6l). The whole section data of PDX tumours derived from patients with breast cancer suggest that after PARP inhibitor treatment, BRCA1middle mutation tumour cells had a prolonged mitotic stage and were prone to mitotic errors. In addition, obvious senescence signals were observed in BRCA1S955del tumours but not in BRCA1I1824Dfs∗3 and BRCA1Q1785X tumours (Fig. S6c and d). The occurrence of senescence in BRCA1S955del tumours was further confirmed by the examination of SASP gene expression (MMP12, CXCL1, IL1α, and GM-CSF) (Fig. S6e). Together, these results indicate that the PARP inhibitor niraparib can suppress the growth of tumour cells bearing BRCA1 centrosome-related mutations in vivo.

Given the validated functional defects of BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q in mitotic centrosomes in breast cancer, we wondered about the proportion of BRCA1 mutation affecting centrosome dynamics and mitosis in clinical. To investigate this, we constructed plasmids for all 15 BRCA1 mutations predicted to be deleterious by CScape (Fig. 1c) and transfected MCF7 cells with these plasmids (Fig. S7a). Each type of MCF7 cells was subjected to infection with a 3′-UTR-specific BRCA1-shRNA lentivirus to knock down endogenous BRCA1 (Fig. S1j). Upon treatment with the PARP inhibitor niraparib without DNA damage agents, 7 (S868L, R1074T, R1085I, D1220N, E1222Q, Q1395E, and S578F) out of 15 (46.7%) types of MCF7 cells were responded, whose proliferation was suppressed at different levels (Fig. S7b). Meanwhile, immunofluorescent staining of CETN-3 in those 15 cell types showed that 6 BRCA1 mutations (S868L, R1074T, R1085I, D1220N, E1222Q, and Q1395E) caused functional deficiencies, leading to centrosome amplification in MCF7 cells (Fig. S7c and d). Hence, the response of BRCA1S578F cells to PARP inhibitor may be due to other functional defects. We also found that combining the prediction results of three tools (CScape, FATHMM, and PolyPhen-2; Table S6), 5 out of the 15 mutations were predicted to be deleterious by three tools, and 4 of them (80%) caused cancer cells to be sensitive to niraparib. Therefore, integrating different prediction tools can improve the predictive accuracy of mutation pathogenic status.

PARP inhibitor niraparib suppresses ovarian and prostate tumours bearing BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q

We further explored whether ovarian or prostate tumour cells bearing the BRCA1middle mutation are also prone to mitotic errors that can be targeted by PARP inhibitors. Therefore, we conducted CRISPR-mediated knock-in to introduce the BRCA1 middle mutations (R1085I and E1222Q) into A2780 ovarian cancer cells and C4-2 prostate cancer cells, both of which have wild-type BRCA1(Fig. S8a and b). Subsequently, the mitotic status of BRCA1WT and BRCA1R1085I or E1222Q A2780 and C4-2 cells was monitored upon the treatment of PARP inhibitor niraparib. Intriguingly, a higher rate of cells bearing mitotic errors was observed in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells than in BRCA1WT cells for both cancer types (Fig. 7a–d). Accordingly, obvious senescence signals were observed in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q cells, but not in BRCA1WT cells, upon niraparib treatment (Fig. 7e–h). Meanwhile, we observed higher levels of SASP gene expression in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q A2780 or C4-2 cells, validating the occurrence of senescence (Fig. S8c and d).

To validate the antitumor effect of niraparib on tumours bearing BRCA1middle mutation in ovarian and prostate cancers, we implanted A2780 or C4-2 cells with BRCA1WT, BRCA1R1085I, and BRCA1E1222Q into BALB/c nude mice. All cancer cells developed into tumour masses in mice (Fig. 7i and j). Once reaching a tumour size of 50 mm3, the mice were intraperitoneally injected daily with either vehicle or niraparib (25 mg/kg). Specifically, the mean reductions in volumes of BRCA1WT, BRCA1R1085I, and BRCA1E1222Q tumours were 32.5%, 73.4%, and 78.1% in ovarian cancer and 13.5%, 69.7%, and 65.5% in prostate cancer, respectively (Fig. 7i and j), indicating that niraparib had a more pronounced effect on BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours. Consistently, obvious senescence signals were observed in both BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours but not in BRCA1WT tumours (Fig. 7k–n). The occurrence of senescence in BRCA1R1085I and BRCA1E1222Q tumours was further confirmed by the examination of SASP gene expression (MMP12, CXCL1, IL1α, and GM-CSF) (Fig. S8e and f). Collectively, these results indicate that the PARP inhibitor niraparib can effectively suppress the growth of ovarian and prostate cancer cells bearing BRCA1 centrosome-related mutations in vivo.

Discussion

Over the past decade, PARP inhibitors have been shown to have impressive antitumor effects through the therapeutic mechanism of synthetic lethality. Previous studies proved that RING and BRCT domains contribute to the core functions of DDR. However, in real-world patients with cancer, a larger proportion of mutations present in the middle (or near the middle) region of BRCA1. Interestingly, several recent phase III trials showed that diverse types of PARP inhibitors could significantly improve the survival of patients with ovarian cancer regardless of the presence of HR gene variations. This phenomenon cannot be well explained by currently existing theories or models. In this study, we found that missense mutations in the middle region of BRCA1 caused an error of transient extra spindle poles that could be restored by centrosomal PAR during cancer cell mitosis. The majority of centrosomal PAR was synthesized by PARP2, and the synthesis was effectively inhibited by PARP inhibitors including niraparib, olaparib, and rucaparib. This finding suggests a new synthetic lethality of BRCA1 deficiency and PARP inhibitors, which we called ‘alternative synthetic lethality’; that is, certain mutations in the middle region of BRCA1 impair the localization of BRCA1 to the centrosomes and tend to cause an extra pole-induced mitotic error in cancer cells, whereas centrosomal PAR could correct this error by clustering extra poles. The inhibition of PAR synthesis thus abrogates the correction of the extra pole-induced mitotic error and pushes cancer cells toward senescence.

It is well known that massive amounts of PAR are synthesized under stresses particularly under DNA damage. Of the 17 members in the human PARP family, PARP1 has been most intensively studied because its activity increases sharply and accounts for a vast majority (∼85%) synthesis of PAR upon DNA damage stress. Because PAR is rarely synthesized under non-stress conditions, very few studies have focused on PAR functions under the physiological status of cells (without artificial stress treatments). In addition, very few studies have explained the existence of multiple members of PARPs within a cell, especially the seemingly redundant PARP2. Inspired by early studies showing that PAR was present on the spindle and spindle poles in Xenopus egg extracts and HeLa cells, we investigated the cellular localization of PAR and found that this polymer was specifically present in mitotic centrosomes in all types of examined cells in the absence of treatment of artificial DNA damage agents. Unexpectedly, we discovered that PARP2 but not PARP1 was retained at the centrosome and contributed to a major synthesis of centrosomal PAR. As most current PARP inhibitors target both PARP1 and PARP2, these findings establish a direct link between PARP inhibitors and centrosomal PAR during cell mitosis. The earlier chemotherapeutic principle of the use of PARP inhibitors relies on the presence of SSBs or DSBs in cancer cells. However, it is hard to assess the actual level of these DNA lesions in cancer cells from patients in situ. In contrast, all cancer cells undergo mitosis and risk the formation of extra spindle poles. In these regards, our ‘alternative synthetic lethality’ model may provide a universal and reasonable explanation for the currently observed clinical effects of PARP inhibitors, and also emphasizes the potential application of targeting PARP2 for tumour suppression.

In our study, we revealed that mutations R1085I and E1222Q of BRCA1 impair centrosome localization of the protein, which causes transient extra spindle poles during mitosis. Most of the transient extra-pole cells can be corrected by centrosome PAR, while for the minor cells that cannot be restored and still have extra poles, they would flow into senescence. Thus, a BRCA1 mutant cell has two chances to avoid tumorigenic transformation. However, if senescence is not successfully activated in certain (or sporadic) cells bearing extra poles, these cells will divide into aneuploid daughter cells with high genome instability, which could potentially cause tumours.

To date, patients with cancer bearing mutations from only a few HR genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, and PALB2) have been proven to benefit from PARP inhibitors in clinical trials. Although our ‘alternative synthetic lethality’ was based on the findings of BRCA1 mutation, it could also be a universal mechanism for other HR factors located on centrosomes such as BRCA2 and PALB2, which have also been found present at centrosomes. A previous study showed that disrupting centrosomal localization of BRCA2 led to centrosome abnormalities and multinucleation. Recently, a study reported that cells lacking BRCA2 exhibited pronounced mitotic abnormalities, including cytokinesis failure, polyploidy, aberrant multipolar spindles, and centrosome amplification. These effects were independent of DNA damage response but attributed to the inhibition of Rho kinase, a centrosome-localized protein essential for centrosome movement and positioning. Given the BRCA2 mutant cells were also sensitive to PARP inhibitors, the involvement of the ‘centrosome-related mutation’ of BRCA2 is worthy to be explored. Thus, mapping the regions of BRCA1/2 and PALB2 that are required for their centrosome localization and then matching patient mutations to these regions will facilitate individual treatment of PARP inhibitors and precision medicine. More importantly, because cancer cells inducibly overexpressing PLK4 mimicked the transient extra pole formation induced by BRCA1 mutations and PLK4-overexpressing cells were sensitive to PARP inhibitors, the ‘alternative synthetic lethality’ model might also apply to tumours bearing mutations of centrosome-related genes, such as centriole duplication genes, centrosome clustering genes, or spindle assembly genes. High throughput screening of cancer cells bearing clinically relevant mutations in these genes established by knock-in library in the presence of PARP inhibitors will surely discover many new types of cancers that are sensitive to PARP inhibitors in the future. Collectively, our results not only reveal a different chemotherapy mechanism of PARP inhibitors, but also extend the potential use of PARP inhibitor treatment for patients with cancer.
