# Tumor-colonizing Lachnoclostridium-mediated chemokine expression enhances the immune infiltration of bladder urothelial carcinoma.

## Abstract

Limited research into the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) for bladder urothelial carcinoma (BUC), particularly the neglect of the intratumoral microbiota, has hindered the development of immunotherapies targeting BUC. Here, we collect 401 patients with BUC with host transcriptome samples and matched tumor microbiome samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas database. Besides, two independent BUC cohorts receiving immunotherapy were obtained. First, we find that the TIME profile is closely related to the prognosis of patients with BUC. Additionally, the genus Lachnoclostridium in tumors could regulate the accumulation of chemokines to recruit immune cell populations into bladder tumors. Among them, chemokines include CCL3, CCL4, CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL11, and immune cells mainly involve macrophages and CD8+ T cells. Analyses based on two independent immunotherapy cohorts suggest that these immune-related chemokines strongly influence the immunotherapeutic efficacy of BUC. Furthermore, drug predictive analyses show that immune-related chemokines impact patients' sensitivity to diverse drugs. These results suggest a dual role of immune-related chemokines in combination therapy against BUC. Collectively, our study provides new insights into the regulation of TIME by intratumoral microbiota and provides guidance for improving immunotherapy against BUC.

Introduction

Bladder urothelial carcinoma (BUC) is a malignant tumor originating from the urothelium and is one of the most common malignant tumors in the urogenital system. In general, the prognosis of early BUC is relatively good through combined therapy such as surgery and chemotherapy. However, for late or metastatic BUC, the prognosis is usually poor. With the development of tumor immunotherapy in recent years, some novel immunotherapeutic agents, such as PD-L1 inhibitors, have been approved for the treatment of locally advanced or metastatic urothelial carcinoma. These new drugs offer new therapeutic options for patients and are expected to improve outcomes. However, the lack of response to immunotherapy in a significant proportion of patients prompts the need to investigate the heterogeneity of patients with BUC and develop targeted immunotherapy regimens.

In the tumor microenvironment (TME), malignant cells are embedded in a complex ecosystem, surrounded by a network of non-malignant cells, including stromal cells, fibroblasts, and infiltrating immune cells. In addition to these cellular components, there is evidence that microbial communities are also integral to the TME. For instance, in pancreatic tumors, elevated numbers of CD3+ and CD8+ T cells were positively correlated with the enrichment of Saccharopolyspora, Pseudoxanthomonas, and Streptomyces. Sun et al. reported that CD68+ macrophages tend to be distributed in areas where the intratumoral microbiome is more enriched, suggesting that the intratumoral microbiome may affect macrophage infiltration in hepatocellular carcinoma. Additionally, Ghaddar et al. identified bacteria that primarily host tumor cells and found that their presence was associated with cell-type-specific gene expression and pathway activity. In bladder tumors, preliminary studies have shown that bacterial diversity in cancer tissues is significantly reduced, but specific bacteria such as Cupriavidus, Acinetobacter, and Anoxybacillus are more abundant, which may be used as a biomarker of bladder cancer. However, the regulatory role of the broad spectrum of bacteria in BUC tumors on the TME remains to be investigated, and the association of specific cellular components in the TME, particularly immune cells, with intratumoral bacteria remains unknown.

To address this, we enrolled 401 BUC patients with paired host transcriptome and tumor microbiome samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) and obtained their complete clinical follow-up information. We aim to elucidate the regulatory mechanism of intratumoral microbiota on immune infiltration of bladder tumors and its impact on patient prognosis and response to therapy.

Results

The OS of patients with BUC is related to tumor immune infiltration

Paired host transcriptome samples and tumor microbiome samples from 401 patients with BUC in TCGA were collected. Univariate and multivariate Cox regression analysis based on BUC-related immune cell marker genes significantly stratified the OS of patients (Supplementary Figure S1B and Supplementary Table 1; log-rank test, p = 0.00012), suggesting that the prognosis of patients with BUC is strongly associated with tumor immunity. Additionally, patients in the high-survival-risk group had a higher degree of intratumoral immune infiltration than those in the low-survival-risk group (Fig. 1), illustrating that the high- and low-survival-risk groups presented “Hot” and “Cold” tumors, respectively. Additionally, there were differences in multiple clinical indicators between the two groups. For example, in terms of T stage and N stage, a higher proportion of patients in the high-survival-risk group had advanced stage. Consequently, our results suggest that the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) strongly influences the aggressiveness of BUC.

Chemokines impact the landscape of immune infiltration within bladder tumors

Next, we sought to explore the underlying factors that contributed to the differentiation of the immune landscape between the two groups. Multiple studies have reported that chemokines are important factors that promote the entry of immune cells into tumors. Therefore, we collected 45 common chemokines from published studies and comprehensively correlated them with the intratumoral immune components (Fig. 2). First, we found that chemokine expression was significantly elevated in the high-survival-risk group (Fig. 2B; Wilcoxon test, p < 0.05). Furthermore, chemokine expression was significantly positively correlated with the abundance of most immune cell populations (Spearman correlation, p < 0.001), particularly between chemokines including CCL3, CCL4, CCL5, CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL13 and immune cell populations involving natural killer cells, CD8+ T cells, macrophages, and myeloid dendritic cells (Fig. 2A).

Additionally, we found a general positive correlation between chemokines, indicating that they have a positive overall role in attraction and co-regulation (Fig. 3C). We then performed functional enrichment analyses of differentially expressed genes (DEGs) between the two groups. The biological process analysis showed that the DEGs were enriched in cytokine–cytokine receptor interaction, humoral immune response, and cell chemotaxis (Supplementary Figure S2). Moreover, GSEA was performed to further explore the most significantly enriched functional terms between the two groups. We found that the cell chemotaxis, myeloid leukocyte migration, and humoral immune response were all enriched in the high-survival-risk group (Fig. 2D), which was consistent with elevated chemokine expression in the high-survival-risk group. Consequently, these results suggest that chemokines are related to enhanced immune infiltration in bladder tumors.

The Lachnoclostridium genus enhances the immune infiltration by regulating chemokine accumulation

Then, we attempted to explore the regulatory effect of intratumoral microbes on the TIME in BUC. At the genus level, Pseudomonas (13.20%), Staphylococcus (6.52%), and Mycobacterium (6.04%) constituted the main components of the intratumoral microbiota (Supplementary Figure S3). Next, there were no significant differences in alpha-diversity between the two BUC groups (Supplementary Figure S4). However, principal co-ordinates analysis (PCoA) and non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity in genus-level microbial composition showed significant difference between the two groups (Fig. 3A and B; PERMANOVA, p < 0.05). We detected that three genera were enriched in the low-survival-risk group, including Lactobacillus, Mycobacterium, and Bacteroides (Fig. 3C). Four genera, including Lachnoclostridium, Terrabacter, Acinetobacter, and Pseudomonas, were overabundant in the high-survival-risk group.

Next, we correlated the seven microbial biomarkers with immune cells populations (Fig. 3D). Intriguingly, the relative abundance of the four genera enriched in the high-survival-risk group was significantly positively correlated with the abundance of immune cells, while the three genera enriched in the low-survival-risk group were opposite. Lachnoclostridium, a genus significantly enriched in the high-survival-risk group, was strongly positively associated with the vast majority of immune cells. Additionally, the microbe–chemokine association was consistent with the microbe-immune cell association pattern (Fig. 3E). Similarly, Lachnoclostridium was strongly positively correlated with the most of chemokines. Prominently, the expression of CCL3, CCL4, CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL11 was significantly positively correlated with the abundance of Lachnoclostridium.

Using the median relative abundance of Lachnoclostridium as a threshold, we divided all BUC patients into two groups: Lachnoclostridium-enriched and Lachnoclostridium-depleted, and then we performed a functional enrichment analysis for the DEGs in these two groups (Supplementary Figure S5). The biological process analysis showed that the DEGs were enriched in cytokine–cytokine receptor interaction, chemokine signaling pathway, and humoral immune response. Consequently, in combination with chemokine–immune associations and intratumoral microbe–chemokine associations, we summarized the following biological pathways: High relative abundance of Lachnoclostridium is related to accumulation of immune-related chemokines such as CCL3, CCL4, CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL11 to enhance immune infiltration in bladder tumors, such as macrophages, CD8+ T cells, and natural killer cell (Fig. 3F).

Mediation analysis underscores immune-related chemokines as intermediate variables in the regulation of immune infiltration by Lachnoclostridium

Next, we carried out a mediation analysis across all samples to investigate the links among the genus Lachnoclostridium, immune-related chemokines, and immune cells. For the genus Lachnoclostridium that were associated with both immune-related chemokines and immune cells, we applied a mediation analysis to evaluate whether the effect of the genus Lachnoclostridium on immune cells is mediated via chemokines. We observed that the abundance of four types of immune cells, including activated natural killer cells, M1 macrophages, CD8+ T cells, and activated CD4+ memory T cell, was largely indirectly regulated by the genus Lachnoclostridium via chemokines (Fig. 4A). For example, the effect of Lachnoclostridium on activated CD4+ memory T cell was mediated via CXCL11. We also observed that the effect of Lachnoclostridium on CD8+ T cells was mediated via CCL4. The effect of Lachnoclostridium on M1 macrophages was mediated via CXCL10, and the effect of Lachnoclostridium on activated natural killer cells was mediated via CCL3 (Fig. 4B–E). Overall, these results demonstrate that immune-related chemokines may act as mediating variables of genus Lachnoclostridium in regulating immune infiltration of bladder tumors.

Immune-related chemokines modulate the response of patients with BUC to immunotherapy

Next, the IMvigor210 cohort was used to explore the effect of immune-related chemokines on BUC-targeted immunotherapy. The IMvigor210 cohort comprises a total of 298 bladder cancer patients who were treated with anti-PD-L1, providing expression data, overall survival data, and immunotherapy response data. We applied the five immune-related chemokines-based Cox model to the IMvigor210 cohort (Supplementary Table 2). The results showed that patient survival was significantly stratified (Fig. 5A; p < 0.0001). The expression of these chemokines was significantly higher in the low-survival-risk group compared to the high-survival-risk group (Fig. 5B). Moreover, patients in the high-survival-risk group had a higher risk score than those in the low-survival-risk group (Fig. 5C). Intriguingly, the low-survival-risk group displayed a significantly higher response rate compared to the high-survival-risk group (Fig. 5D, Fisher’s exact test, p = 0.004), and the patients who responded well to the immunotherapy demonstrated a longer survival time (Fig. 5E). Similarly, the consistent results were also verified in another cohort (GSE176307) (Supplementary Table 3), although not statistically significant due to the small sample size (Supplementary Figure S6).

Additionally, because immunotherapy outcome information was unavailable for patients in TCGA, we used Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE) scores to assess the potential clinical efficacy of immunotherapy in these patients. The results showed that the TIDE scores of the high-survival-risk group were significantly higher than those of the low-survival-risk group (Supplementary Figure S7A; p = 5.5e-07), suggesting that immunotherapy efficacy may be worse in the high-survival-risk group. Moreover, the TIDE scores were significantly positively correlated with the expression of immune-related chemokines and the abundance of genus Lachnoclostridium (Supplementary Figure S7B and C). Consequently, these results suggest that the immune-related chemokines strongly influence the response of BUC patients to immunotherapy.

Immune-related chemokines shape patient's sensitivity to potential drug candidates

However, traditional therapy or the combination of traditional therapy and immunotherapy is still the main theme of BUC therapy. Therefore, we sought to explore the effect of immune-related chemokines on the sensitivity of patients to potential drugs. In TCGA-BUC, NMDS and PCoA based on scores for 198 drug candidates showed significant differences in drug sensitivity between the high-survival-risk and low-survival-risk group (Fig. 6A and B; PERMANOVA, p = 0.001). Of the 198 drug candidates, 118 exhibited substantial differences in drug scores between the two groups (Fig. 6C; Wilcoxon test, p < 0.05). Furthermore, we performed Spearman correlation for the five chemokines (CCL3, CCL4, CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL11) and the 30 drugs with the most significant differences in scores between the two groups (Fig. 6D). Results showed that the five chemokines were closely related to the scores of these drugs. Moreover, drugs positively associated with chemokines were more sensitive in the low-survival-risk group, whereas patients in the high-survival-risk group were more sensitive to drugs negatively associated with chemokines. Specifically, patients in the high-survival-risk group exhibited greater sensitivity to Staurosporine and Luminespib, whereas those in the low-survival-risk group displayed heightened responsiveness to Daporinad. Then, PubChem website (https://pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) was used to visualize the 3D conformations of these three small molecules (Fig. 6E–G). Consequently, our results suggest that these chemokines can also mediate the response of patients with BUC to antitumor inhibitors.

Discussion

Our study comprehensively characterizes the regulatory role of microbial communities in bladder tumors on immune infiltration. A complete biological pathway is proposed for the first time: Lachnoclostridium enhances tumor immune infiltration in BUC patients by controlling chemokine expression. In particular, chemokines in this pathway strongly influence patient response to immunotherapy and diverse inhibitors.

The relationship between the TIME and the prognosis of a variety of cancers has been extensively studied. Wang et al. report that overexpression of Nei endonuclease VIII-like protein 3 can enhance the level of immune infiltration in hepatocellular carcinoma and then induce unfavorable prognosis. However, a lung cancer study that analyzes gene enrichment for 19 immune cell subsets in high- and low-risk groups find that the less immune cell infiltration, the worse the prognosis. Notably, we find that in BUC, a high degree of intratumoral immune infiltration is associated with adverse patient survival. These contradictory results reflect cancer-type heterogeneity at the level of the immune infiltration–prognosis association. Future studies that focus on the association between the TIME and prognosis deserve rigorous consideration of different subtypes of a tumor, including molecular and pathological types.

The genus Lachnoclostridium is the core bacterium in the gut that affects many human diseases, including cancer. Ma et al. reported that gut microbiota-mediated bile acid metabolism regulates liver cancer via natural killer T cells, with Lachnoclostridium playing a dominant role. In recent years, there have been numerous studies on the relationship between the gut-bladder axis and diseases, but all of them point to the influence of gut microbiota on distal organs and tissues, while ignoring the translocation and colonization of gut microbiota to connected organs, and then play at a particular position. Thus, the next focus will be on uncovering how Lachnoclostridium translocates from the gut into bladder tumor tissue, and the biological mechanisms by which Lachnoclostridium in the tumor regulates immune infiltration. Additionally, future research needs to be based on deeper second-generation sequencing techniques, such as metagenomes, to identify species-level bacteria that regulate immune infiltration of bladder tumors to design novel cancer therapies that target the intratumoral microbiome.

Our study shows that targeting chemokines can convert “Cold” tumors into “Hot” tumors, which in turn makes patients with BUC more likely to benefit from immunotherapy, as has been reported in other studies. Furthermore, our study also reveals that immune-related chemokines strongly influence the sensitivity of BUC patients to diverse drugs, which greatly improves the clinical significance of chemokine-targeted therapy development. Utilizing the regulation of chemokine expression by intratumoral microbiota, future research should focus on exploring the underlying biological mechanisms to facilitate the development of novel therapies targeting BUC.

Several limitations exist in our study. First, our microbiome data had a limited taxonomic resolution, and the application of metagenomic sequencing technology in bladder tumors will refine our results. Second, our findings were mainly based on observed correlations and in vivo and in vitro experiments are needed to reveal the potential mechanism. Last, potential confounders were not assessed due to the nature of retrospective study. Well-designed prospective cohorts are needed to adjust data for all potential confounders.

Conclusion

In conclusion, our study suggests that intratumoral Lachnoclostridium influences chemokine expression and is related to enhanced immune infiltration in bladder tumors, providing new insights into the development of BUC-targeted immunotherapies. Furthermore, these immune-associated chemokines exhibit a dual effect of modulating patient response to immunotherapy and antitumor inhibitors.

Materials and methods

Data acquisition

The RNA-seq, mutation, and follow-up data of patients with BUC were downloaded from the Genomic Data Commons Data Portal (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov/). Intratumoral microbiome abundance data were downloaded at https://github.com/knightlab-analyses/mycobiome. Additionally, to independently validate the effect of chemokines on immunotherapy, two independent bladder cancer cohorts receiving immunotherapy were collected, the IMvigor210 and GSE176307 cohorts. The gene expression data and detailed clinical information were downloaded from http://research-pub.gene.com/IMvigor210CoreBiologies/ and the GEO database (access no. GSE176307).

Acquisition of BUC-associated immune cell marker genes

The immune cell marker genes of bladder tissue were downloaded from the CellMarker 2.0 database and labeled G1 (http://bio-bigdata.hrbmu.edu.cn/CellMarker). The BUC-associated genes were obtained from the DisGeNET database and labeled G2 (https://www.disgenet.org/). Then G1 and G2 were intersected to obtain BUC-related immune cell marker genes (Supplementary Figure S1A).

Construction of the prognostic risk model

Univariate Cox regression analyses based on the expression profiles of BUC-associated immune cell marker genes were performed. Genes that were significantly associated with patient survival were then used to perform multivariate Cox regression analyses. The risk score was obtained by multiplying the expression of each gene by the corresponding coefficient and adding them together. Patients were then divided into high- and low-survival-risk groups based on the median risk score.

Immune infiltration analysis

Based on deconvolution and marker gene algorithms, six tools were used to calculate the abundance of immune cell populations from gene expression matrix, including the CIBERSORT, EPIC, MCP-counter, quanTIseq, TIMER, and xCell. Among them, xCell and MCP-counter are based on marker gene expression levels and the rest are based on deconvolution analysis.

Diversity analysis of microbial communities

The alpha-diversity and beta-diversity analyses were conducted using the R package “vegan.” Linear discriminant analysis Effect Size (LEfSe) analysis (http://huttenhower.sph.harvard.edu/lefse/) was conducted to identify microbial biomarkers between the high- and low-survival-risk groups. The Bray–Curtis distance of the microbial data was subjected to principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) utilizing the R package “vegan.” Permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PERMANOVA) was carried out to perform the statistical test in the difference of microbial community between the two groups.

Drug sensitivity prediction

The R package “oncoPredict” was used to predict the response of BUC patient to drug candidates. “oncoPredict” is an R package that predicts drug sensitivity based on gene expression. It can calculate the IC50 value of each drug based on the gene expression in a sample. The lower the IC50, the more useful the drug. The IC50 value of 198 drug candidates was calculated and compared between the high- and low-survival-risk groups.

Evaluation of clinical efficacy of immunotherapy

Tumor Immune Dysfunction and Exclusion (TIDE) score was calculated online (http://tide.dfci.harvard.edu/). Wilcoxon test was used to perform the statistical test, and a P value of < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

Causal mediation analysis

In our study, we hypothesized that the genus Lachnoclostridium regulated immune infiltration by regulating chemokine expression. Therefore, we assumed that chemokines were mediating variables, and the genus Lachnoclostridium and immune cell abundance were independent and dependent variables, respectively. Then, mediation analysis was performed with R package “mediation.” We calculated the influence proportion and significance of independent variables on dependent variables through mediating variables.

Statistical analysis

The log-rank test was used to perform the statistical test on the overall survival (OS) between groups. The Wilcoxon rank-sum test was used to perform statistical tests for comparison of differences between groups. The Spearman method was used to generate the correlation coefficient and P value in the correlation analysis of two variables. All statistical analyses were conducted using R version 4.3.1, with a P value threshold of 0.05 for significance.
