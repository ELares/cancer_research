# Redox-manipulating nanocarriers for anticancer drug delivery: a systematic review.

## Abstract

Spatiotemporally controlled cargo release is a key advantage of nanocarriers in anti-tumor therapy. Various external or internal stimuli-responsive nanomedicines have been reported for their ability to increase drug levels at the diseased site and enhance therapeutic efficacy through a triggered release mechanism. Redox-manipulating nanocarriers, by exploiting the redox imbalances in tumor tissues, can achieve precise drug release, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing damage to healthy cells. As a typical redox-sensitive bond, the disulfide bond is considered a promising tool for designing tumor-specific, stimulus-responsive drug delivery systems (DDS). The intracellular redox imbalance caused by tumor microenvironment (TME) regulation has emerged as an appealing therapeutic target for cancer treatment. Sustained glutathione (GSH) depletion in the TME by redox-manipulating nanocarriers can exacerbate oxidative stress through the exchange of disulfide-thiol bonds, thereby enhancing the efficacy of ROS-based cancer therapy. Intriguingly, GSH depletion is simultaneously associated with glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) inhibition and dihydrolipoamide S-acetyltransferase (DLAT) oligomerization, triggering mechanisms such as ferroptosis and cuproptosis, which increase the sensitivity of tumor cells. Hence, in this review, we present a comprehensive summary of the advances in disulfide based redox-manipulating nanocarriers for anticancer drug delivery and provide an overview of some representative achievements for combinational therapy and theragnostic. The high concentration of GSH in the TME enables the engineering of redox-responsive nanocarriers for GSH-triggered on-demand drug delivery, which relies on the thiol-disulfide exchange reaction between GSH and disulfide-containing vehicles. Conversely, redox-manipulating nanocarriers can deplete GSH, thereby enhancing the efficacy of ROS-based treatment nanoplatforms. In brief, we summarize the up-to-date developments of the redox-manipulating nanocarriers for cancer therapy based on DDS and provide viewpoints for the establishment of more stringent anti-tumor nanoplatform.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

Targeted and controlled delivery of anti-tumor agents is crucial for enhancing the effectiveness and reducing the toxicity of cancer therapies. Various external or internal stimuli-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines have been reported for controlled cargo release (Fig. 1). Among them, redox-responsive nanocarriers are extensively investigated. The redox-sensitive vehicles can respond to the high intracellular level of glutathione (GSH) and release the payload in a triggered manner. As a result, the intracellular drug concentration could be increased post the treatment by redox-sensitive nanocarriers, which could improve the therapeutic efficiency and reduce side effects of the parent drugs, simultaneously, the lack of cell selectivity is still something we need to consider. Redox-responsive chemical bonds originally used in nanotechnology include diselenide bonds (Se-Se), succinimide-sulfide bonds, nitroimidazoles, ferrocene, azo-group, and the most widely used disulfide bonds (S-S). As a well-studied dynamic covalent bond, disulfide bonds are suitable for a diverse range of materials with different functional groups. Meanwhile, the tumor microenvironment (TME)-associated redox system take shape an elaborate antioxidant defense system to adjust redox homeostasis, which acts a pivotal role in cell growth, proliferation and apoptosis in the reported articles.

Redox homeostasis refers to the process whereby the intracellular microenvironment remains balanced and stable regarding the oxidant and antioxidant. High levels of antioxidant GSH in tumor cells play an irreplacable role in scavenging ROS and maintaining redox homeostasis, which can also cause tumorigenic cells to be more resistant to the ROS-based therapies. Therefore, the design of the GSH-sensitive nanoplatform was significant for the cargo release, and further cause the GSH depletion in the tumor cells. GSH depletion by nanocarriers can improve the efficacy of ROS-based cancer therapy. Intriguingly, GSH depletion was simultaneously associated with GPX4 inhibition and ferroptosis induction. Ferroptosis is a form of regulated cell death due to the iron-dependent lipid peroxides accumulation. Excessive lipid peroxidation leads to death by ferroptosis and the phenotype can be manipulated by GSH depletion and iron enrichment. GPX4 normally removes the toxic lipid peroxides, protecting cell membranes from ferroptotic cell damage. The role of GPX4 in the detoxification of lipid peroxides into lipid alcohols requires the auxiliary effect of GSH. Therefore, GSH depletion indirectly leads the loss of GPX4 activity, leading to ferroptotic cell death. In cuproptosis, the depletion of GSH can enhance dihydrolipoamide S-acetyltransferase (DLAT) oligomerization and increase the sensitivity of tumor cells to this process.

The construction of various redox-sensitive nanocarriers relayed on the significant difference of redox gradient between the intracellular GSH in cancer and normal cells. The concentration of GSH in the cytosol and subcellular compartments lysosomes or endosomes is approximately 2–10 mM, which cursory three orders of magnitude higher than the extracellular one. Such diversity made the design and fabrication of redox-rely on-demand drug release platform ideal. Among them, disulfide bond is usually employed to engineer redox-sensitive vehicles which can make the cargoes be physically encapsulated or chemically conjugated to the vehicles. Further, the self-immolative linkers (SILs) make it possible to conjugate any cargo to the vehicles via a disulfide bond, and the SILs were suitable for a diverse range of materials with different functional groups (e.g. -OH, COOH, and amine). Upon the thiol-disulfide exchange reaction between GSH and disulfide-containing nanocarrier, the drug is released post particle collapse. The thiol-disulfide exchange reaction is thermodynamically favored with a negative Gibbs free energy (∆G < 0), but it is kinetically slow in contrast to the thiol-diselenide exchange reaction. Hence, tailored regulation of thiol-disulfide reaction kinetics could enhance redox-responsive drug delivery and therapeutic outcome of cancer therapy.

Instead of traditional discussions on redox-responsive nanoplatforms, this review provides a comprehensive overview of recent advancements in redox-manipulating disulfide-based SILs and GSH-responsive nanoplatforms for diagnosis and treatment. Meanwhile, the strategies by synergistically boosting GSH depletion and ROS-based anti-tumor therapies for amplifying oxidative stress are highlighted. The mechanism of ferroptotic cancer therapy and cuprotosis are also mentioned in this review, which depended on the GSH depletion. Above all, it was significative to design redox-manipulation nanoplatforms for drug delivery, triggering and enhancing ROS-based therapy, and inducing cell death. Otherwise, to further strengthen the theranostic platform, the consumption of GSH and the production of ROS can effectively achieve the imbalance of redox homeostasis and the amplification of oxidative stress, therefore leading to tumor cells apoptosis, cuproptosis or ferroptosis (Fig. 2).

Nanocarriers for anti-tumor drug delivery

Benefits of nanomedicines

Nanomedicines are promising in drug delivery in several aspects. Nanoparticles can enhance the solubility of hydrophobic drugs without the use of organic solvent e.g. albumin-bound paclitaxel (PTX). The chemical stability of unstable drugs can be improved by nano-encapsulation. Nanocarriers can modulate the pharmacokinetics and biodistribution of drugs upon delivery. One important application field of nanomedicine is cancer treatment. Nanotechnology can realize passive drug targeting to diseased site, e.g. solid tumor, and enhance the drug internalization via surface modification and active targeting. The nanocarriers can also control drug release via multiple approaches (Fig. 3). Nanocarriers may also be able to address the efflux problem that often induces drug resistance and therapy failure.

Inorganic nanoparticles for cancer therapy

Based on the type of excipients for nanocarrier production, nanocarrier can be categorized into inorganic and organic subdivisions. Typical inorganic nanocarriers include iron oxide nanoparticles, quantum dots, gold nanoparticles, carbon material carriers, and mesoporous silicon nanocarriers. This type of nanocarriers is usually inert and show high chemical stability, which is beneficial for particle processing under harsh conditions. It is easy to modulate the size, porosity, and surface chemistry of inorganic particles. The biocompatibility and aqueous stability of these particles can be modulated by surface coating via a polymer or lipid shell. Moreover, many inorganic particles exhibit unique imaging properties, which can be used for engineering theragnostic nanoplatform.

Organic nanocarriers for cancer therapy

In contrast to inorganic nanoparticles, the building blocks of organic nanocarriers are organic compounds. Depending the molecular weight and source, they may be small molecules or macromolecules, natural or synthetic. In biomedical and pharmaceutical applications, organic materials offer excellent biocompatibility and safety. They are also widely available and easy to modify and functionalize. Therefore, organic nanocarriers have been widely employed in delivering therapeutic agents for cancer therapy. Typical examples are liposomes, lipid nanocarriers, micelles, polymer-drug conjugate, polymersomes, and dendrimers.

Metal organic frameworks (MOFs) for cancer therapy

MOFs are three-dimensional structures consisting of metal ions and the corresponding coordination organic ligands (Fig. 4). Nanoscale MOFs are porous nanomaterials. Compared with traditional porous materials, nanoscale MOFs exhibit tunable pore size and physicochemical features, and are ideal vehicles for drug delivery and cancer therapy. MOFs show adjustable size, high surface area, high drug loading capacity and good biocompatibility, which makes them a promising drug delivery vehicle. In contrast to polymeric vehicles, the major benefit of MOFs is the avoidance of the tedious synthesis and purification of polymers. Because of the high cargo loading in MOFs, various therapeutic molecules such as camptothecin (CPT), doxorubicin (DOX), β-estradiol, Chlorin e6 (Ce6), as well as various therapeutic biomacromolecules (polypeptide, protein, DNA and RNA). There are two approaches of drug loading in MOFs, i.e. direct encapsulation and post-synthetic loading.

In contrast to the traditional porous nanocarriers, the porous structure of MOFs can be adjusted by changing the type of organic ligands and metal ions. The production of nanoscale MOFs mainly involves conventional hydro/solvothermal synthesis, solvent evaporation synthesis, microwave synthesis, electrochemical synthesis and microemulsion synthesis approaches. Surface engineering can attach functional molecules beyond the MOFs core. This approach can improve the in vivo stability of MOFs, extend the circulation time, and enable site-specific cargo release. A myriad of molecules (e.g. PEG, nucleic acid, protein, and peptide) could be absorbed to the nanoscale MOFs by either covalent or non-covalent means, which could enhance the delivery efficacy of MOFs in terms of cancer therapy.

The recent developments of MOFs in the field of cancer therapy, including chemotherapy, photodynamic therapy (PDT), and combinational treatment have been extensively reviewed. Due to their well-defined structure, high surface area and high drug loading capacity, MOFs can deliver different therapeutic agents for cancer treatment. Horcajada et al. confirmed that MOFs have great potential in drug delivery because of their efficient drug loading and controlled drug release. Different from traditional chemotherapy, phototherapies need local treatment of tissue by laser irradiation. MOFs can efficiently load photosensitizers (PSs) into porous frameworks, and avoid the energy quenching of PSs. Lu et al. reported a Hf-porphyrin nanoscale MOF (DBP-UiO), as the nano-PSs for photodynamic treatment of resistant head and neck cancer. The isolation of porphyrin-based linkers of DBP-UiO could significantly reduce the fluorescence quenching and improve the anti-tumor efficacy of PDT. Moreover, the surface of MOFs can be facilely engineered with different coatings materials to modulate aqueous stability and enable cell targeting. However, one potential issue of MOF in terms systemic drug delivery is the potential toxicity of metals and organic ligands.

The potential issue and challenge

In systemic drug delivery, inorganic nanoparticles, organic nanocarriers, and metal-organic frameworks each present unique advantages and challenges in clinical applicability. Inorganic nanoparticles, such as gold nanoparticles and quantum dots, offer high stability and easy surface modification for targeted delivery. However, they often face significant toxicity issues due to heavy metal content and can induce oxidative stress, leading to cell damage. Moreover, their non-biodegradable nature can result in long-term accumulation in organs, potentially causing chronic toxicity. Rapid clearance by the reticuloendothelial system (RES) further complicates their use, reducing their efficacy. Currently, some inorganic nanoparticles have been applied in clinical settings, but many are still in the clinical trial phase, predominantly for in vivo imaging purposes, with fewer being used for drug delivery. The clinical translation of inorganic nanomaterials as drug delivery systems remains a key issue that needs to be addressed in the clinical development.

Organic nanocarriers, including liposomes, micelles, and dendrimers, are generally more biocompatible and biodegradable compared to inorganic nanoparticles. They offer versatility in design, enabling tailored drug release profiles and reduced toxicity. However, they can suffer from stability issues in biological environments, leading to unpredictable drug release. Additionally, their drug loading capacity is often lower than that of inorganic and MOF-based carriers, which can limit their therapeutic potential.

MOFs are a promising class of nanocarriers due to their high surface area and porosity, allowing for high drug loading capacities and tunable release profiles. They can be engineered to release drugs in response to specific stimuli, such as pH, redox or temperature changes. Despite these advantages, MOFs face challenges related to their stability in physiological conditions and potential toxicity from the release of metal ions or organic linkers. Furthermore, scalability and reproducibility in their synthesis can be challenging in clinical applicability, impacting their practical application in systemic drug delivery.

In nanomedicine, the delivery mechanisms of nanoparticles also impact their clinical applications in the treatment and diagnosis of cancer. The enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect has long been considered the primary mechanism for the tumor delivery of nanoparticles (passive targeting), which suggests that nanoparticles smaller than the endothelial gaps enter tumors and cannot leave. However, pharmacokinetic studies indicate that the number of nanoparticles in tumors decreases over time and is influenced by tumor heterogeneity. Sindhwani et al. suggested that although the gaps between endothelial cells are considered a dominant factor in the EPR effect, studies have observed that these gaps are not frequent. The number of endothelial gaps is 60 times less than the number of nanoparticles accumulated, as measured in their recent experiments. While this provides some evidence for the EPR effect, it still cannot fully explain the accumulation of nanoparticles at tumor sites. To date, only ten nanomedicines have received FDA approval, and only 14% of these drugs have demonstrated clinical efficacy. Research indicates that after systemic administration, only 0.7% of nanomedicines reach solid tumors. Undoubtedly, nanomedicines are beneficial in cancer treatment. As carriers for chemotherapeutic drugs, they significantly enhance patient tolerance by limiting systemic toxicity. However, the high attrition rate has led to some doubts about the effectiveness of nanomedicine. Consequently, despite being a research hotspot in the biomedical field in recent years, nanomedicines still face several challenges and issues in clinical translation and application.

Stimuli-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines

Stimuli-responsive nanocarriers are promising platform in drug delivery in several aspects. One major advantage of triggered nanomedicine is the avoidance of premature cargo release, namely, the drug is secured in the nanocarrier during the systemic circulation prior to reaching the target. Upon the application of either internal or external stimuli, the cargos can be liberated in a triggered manner to increase the drug level in the diseased site and enhance therapeutic efficacy. Moreover, there is a rich collection of stimuli that can be selected for triggering drug release depending on the type, location and pathological pattern of cancers. In certain cases, a singlet nanoplatform can respond to multiple stimuli to produce a multifunctional anticancer nanomedicine.

External stimuli for controlled nanoscale drug delivery usually refer to ultrasounds, temperature, light and magnetic field. These stimuli generally do not rely on the tumor pathology and microenvironment. Internal stimuli, also known as endogenous or biological stimuli, primarily effect on the impaired tissues, which are used for increasing the specificity of biomedicine. Internal stimuli for controlled drug delivery mainly contain specific pH, high concentration of GSH due to the oxidative stress in tumor and other specific tissues, and over-expressed enzymes (i.e. protease, glycosidase and phospholipase). These stimuli generally rely on the tumor pathology and microenvironment. Therefore, optimal stimuli-responsive vehicles should be designed to respond to particular internal triggers, then disintegrate gradually and release the enclosed cargos.

The tumor microenvironment is characterized by distinct redox imbalances, including elevated levels of ROS and an overexpression of antioxidant molecules like GSH. This disrupted redox homeostasis plays a critical role in tumor progression, survival, and resistance to therapy. Cancer cells exploit the redox imbalance to support their rapid growth and adaptation, creating a unique environment that can be targeted for therapeutic interventions. By manipulating redox homeostasis within the TME, it is possible to selectively trigger oxidative stress in cancer cells, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of treatments and overcoming resistance mechanisms. In cancer cells, redox homeostasis can be disrupted by redox-manipulating nanocarriers, leading to elevated ROS levels while simultaneously depleting antioxidant system defenses, further intensifying ROS-induced damage.

Redox-responsive drug delivery nanosystems are efficient for cancer treatment (Table 1). The cytosol is a reducing environment due to the high level of GSH, whereas the extracellular space is basically oxidative. It has been reported that the concentration of GSH inside tumor cells was slightly higher than that in normal cells. The intracellular content of GSH in tumor cells is approximately 2–10 mM (Fig. 5), while in blood and normal tissues are nearly 2–20 µM. In tumor cells, the redox status was determined by a number of molecules including GSH, cysteine, and thioredoxin-1/thioredoxin-2. Among them, GSH is the major intracellular antioxidant and plays a critical role in fighting against the oxidative stress, and additionally, GSH has been considered as a pregnant signal for distinguishing between normal and cancer tissue. This means that it helps fight free radicals. These molecules will destroy your body’s cells and play a role in many chemical reactions in your body. Meanwhile, the endosome also has higher redox condition compared to other organelles because of the reducing enzyme (i.e. gamma-interferon inducible thiol reductase) which could generate some reducing agents (i.e. cysteine).

The design and fabrication of nanoparticles responsive to GSH can be a promising approach for on-demand drug delivery. GSH-responsive vehicles are usually used to increase the concentration of anti-cancer agents in the cytosol, and improve the therapeutic efficacy. GSH-responsive drug delivery systems have been widely researched for intracellular cancer therapy, which can quickly release cargos under intracellular reductive microenvironments. Redox-responsive nanocarriers often bear disulfide or diselenide bonds. Among them, disulfide bond is usually employed to engineer redox-sensitive vehicles because the selenium is a tricky element that shows toxicity beyond a certain level. Disulfide bond is often imbeded in a linker molecule that covalently connects therapeutic agents and the building block materials of nanocarriers. Upon the thiol-disulfide exchange reaction between GSH and disulfide-containing nanocarrier, the drug is released post particle collapse (Fig. 5). The thiol-disulfide exchange reaction is thermodynamically favored with a negative Gibbs free energy (∆G < 0), but it is kinetically slow in contrast to the thiol-diselenide exchange reaction. Hence, tailored regulation of thiol-disulfide reaction kinetics could enhance redox-responsive drug delivery and therapeutic outcome of cancer therapy. In addition, redox-responsive vehicles could not discriminate cancer cell and normal cells because of the reducing cytosol and millimolar GSH therein for both. However, redox-sensitive nanocarriers can be surface-engineered with specific ligands to enhance the targeting to tumor cells.

Although progress has been made in the design and evaluation of stimuli-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines, the large-scale manufacture and clinical translation remain a big issue. There is already a couple of nanomedicine products in clinical trials, but only a few among them have been approved by the regulatory body. There are two main reasons to hinder the translation of stimuli-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines. First, the in vivo anti-tumor efficacy is not significantly better than the current formulations. Second, the design of the nanomedicine is too complicated and thus the quality control is very difficult. Therefore, in order to achieve the desired therapeutic effect, the stimuli-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines need to be further optimized and simplified.

Redox-responsive anti-tumor nanomedicines

Glutathione

Reduced glutathione (L-γ-glutamyl-L-cysteinyl-glycine, GSH) is a prevalent low-molecular-weight thiol in mammalian cells. GSH is a tripeptide made of glutamic acid, cysteine, and glycine. There are two different forms of glutathione. One is the reduced form GSH that exists at millimolar concentration in the intracellular cytosolic compartment; the other is the oxidized form (GSSG) that accounts for only a small percentage of total glutathione inside cells. Thiol-disulfide (GSH/GSSG) ratio in the cells is usually used as an index of the intracellular redox balance, together with the nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP/NADPH) and thioredoxin antioxicant systems. As one of the major antioxidants in cells, the biosynthesis and metabolism of GSH affect numerous intracellular processes, i.e. enzyme catalysis, drug detoxification and cell signal transduction. In contrast to normal cells, the intracellular level of GSH in cancer cells is elevated to counteract with the high concentration of ROS and to maintain redox homeostasis. GSH also acts as the coenzyme of glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4) that is the key guardian of ferroptotic cell death. Hence, targeted GSH depletion has been a promising approach for ferroptotic cancer therapy.

GSH can regulate cancer progression via different ways. Oxidative stress has commonly been implicated in cancer progression due to the high metabolic rate and activation of reactive oxygen-coupled signal pathway. Based on this, GSH as well as other intracellular antioxidants protect cells via counteracting the oxidative stress (Fig. 6). The impairment of GSH antioxidant function can result in redox imbalance and cell death. Therefore, numerous anticancer strategies have been developed for depleting intracellular GSH, including inhibition of GSH synthesis, and promotion of GSH efflux. Meanwhile, the high concentration of GSH in tumor cells enables the engineering of redox-responsive nanocarriers for intracellularly GSH-triggered on-demand drug delivery. The intracellular thiol redox state, controlled by GSH, is crucial for regulating the mitochondrial permeability transition pore complex. Therefore, thiol oxidation may contribute to mitochondrial mechanisms that lead to tissue damage. Among them, disulfide is usually incorporated within the GSH-responsive vehicles. The triggered cargo release depends on the thiol-disulfide exchange reaction between GSH and disulfide-containing vehicle. In the next section, we will introduce the foundamentals of thiol-disulfide exchange reaction, and their potential influence on redox-responsive anticancer drug delivery.

Disulfide bond

In response to the overexpressed GSH in tumor cells, the disulfide bond has been widely used as the “golden standard” to design redox-responsive DDS. Disulfide bonds are widely present in human proteins and are usually formed between two cysteine residues. The process of forming disulfide bonds is an oxidation reaction, that is, the sulfur atoms of two cysteines lose two hydrogen atoms and form disulfide bonds through oxidation reaction. As a very typical reduction-sensitive bond, disulfide bond has high redox sensitivity and is widely used in the design of tumor-specific stimuli-responsive DDS. In the DDS, disulfide bonds can be used as an adjustable release mechanism, and disulfide bond-driven redox-sensitive nanoparticles can be broken in a specific reduction environment such as TME, thereby releasing the encapsulated drug. The disulfide bond can be reduced to the hydrophilic thiol group by GSH, facilitating the release of parent drugs. Disulfide bonds are usually used as hydrophilic and hydrophobic polymers, the connection between polymers and drugs, or as cross-linking agents in polymer drug carriers. In recent years, new progress has been made in the study of intracellular drug release induced by disulfide bonds.

The design and synthesis strategies of disulfide driven redox-manipulating NCs

The redox-sensitive nanocarrier driven by disulfide bonds is a nanomaterial that can respond and disintegrate in a specific reducing tumor microenvironment. These nanocarriers are usually based on disulfide bonds as their main redox-responsive structures. Disulfide bond functional groups are widely present in various chemical and biological reagents. The design of disulfide bonds plays a vital role in drug delivery systems and is widely used in the design of tumor-specific stimuli-responsive DDS. Next, we will introduce in detail the design and synthesis strategies of disulfide-driven redox-sensitive nanocarriers. There are two main methods to induce the introduction of disulfide bonds in nanocarriers, including direct introduction and indirect introduction.

The direct introduction method is to insert the disulfide-containing fragment directly into the nanocarrier. Based on the structural design of polymer micelles, disulfide-containing macromolecules can be roughly classified on the main chain or side chain of the nanoparticles according to the position of the disulfide bond. The possible structures containing disulfide bonds in the main chain of polymer micelles are polyethylene glycol-disulfide bond-polyphosphate (PEG-SS-PEEP), polyethylene glycol-disulfide bond-polylysine (PEG-SS-PzLL), polyethylene glycol-disulfide bond-polycaprolactone (PEG-SS-PCL), etc. The disulfide bonds directly exist in the backbone of the polymer micelles. Chen et al. ’s research synthesized a self-assembled reduction-sensitive polymer micelle (mPEG-SS-PzLL/TPGS/DOX), which has a biodegradable skeleton. The polymer micelle enters the tumor cells, and the disulfide bond can be broken by the high level of GSH, resulting in the destruction of the mPEG-SS-PzLL/TPGS/DOX structure.

The advantage of disulfide bonds in the main chain is that they impart biodegradability and bio-reducibility to the nanocarriers. Compared with ordinary nanoplatform, the disulfide group can be reduced and broken in the presence of high levels of GSH, thereby triggering the degradation of the chain. However, disulfide bonds in the main chain of polymer micelles pose certain limitations for the subsequent functionalization of polymers. The insertion of disulfide bonds in the polymer side chain provides the possibility of convenient chemical modification through thiol-disulfide exchange reaction.

The redox reaction transfer system with disulfide bonds on the side chain is easier to chemically modify and offers more functionality than systems with disulfide bonds on the main chain. Side-chain disulfide bonds can securely attach more drugs to nanoparticles, thereby increasing drug loading. Additionally, these side chains are often used as linkers to design and synthesize polymer micelles and other macromolecule with controlled release characteristics via self-immolative linkers (SILs). Active drugs are typically connected to the proximal end of SILs, with the distal end protected, SILs decomposition and drug release can be triggered by removing the protective variants. To this end, Deng et al. reported disulfide-crosslinked micellar nanoparticles that exhibited a drug release rate dependent on the linker type, highlighting the importance of linker design in developing redox-responsive drug delivery nanocarriers.

The indirect introduction method involves incorporating a thiol group into the nanocarrier, which then forms a disulfide bond through an oxidation reaction or a thiol-disulfide exchange reaction. In a study by Cai et al., a reduction-responsive all-PEG micelle loaded with PTX was prepared by self-assembling PEG200 and PEG10k with mercaptosuccinic acid. In the presence of H2O2, the sulfhydryl groups in the micelle core were oxidized to disulfide bonds, creating a core cross-linked nanodrug. This core cross-linked structure not only imparts redox sensitivity to the nanomedicine, but also enhances its stability. When these nanodrugs target tumor cells, the disulfide bonds are reduced by the high levels of GSH, causing the core cross-linked structure to disintegrate and thereby releasing PTX. This discovery opens up the possibility for applying the indirect introduction design strategy in disulfide bond-driven nanocarriers.

The preparation methods of disulfide driven redox-manipulating NCs

Therapeutic agents can be loaded into disulfide bond-driven redox-responsive nanocarriers through either non-covalent (physical) or covalent (chemical) approaches. In the former approach, the drug does not need to be chemically linked to the carrier, making it suitable for a wider range of active agents. However, a significant drawback of physical drug encapsulation is the typically low drug loading, often below 5%. Triggered cargo release can occur through the action of GSH, which induces a thiol-disulfide exchange reaction, leading to the collapse or swelling of the nanocarrier. In the following section, we will briefly introduce the preparation methods for disulfide bond-driven nanocarriers using physical drug encapsulation or chemical drug loading.

Lin et al. reported a chitosan derivative-modified MSN for redox-sensitive drug and gene co-delivery. They introduced disulfide bond as the moiety of MSN framework to co-load the anticancer agent DOX and p53 gene for controlled drug release and enhanced gene transfection. Similar to MSN, MOF is also a porous nanostructure that is usually generated by the coordination between organic ligands and metal ions. Basically, the design principle of redox-sensitive MOF is to incorporate the disulfide in the organic ligand. For example, He et al. developed a novel redox-responsive MOF for curcumin delivery, 4,4-dithiobenzoic acid (4,4-DTBA) was the ligand and multiple ions (iron, aluminum, or zirconium) can act as the coordination metal nodes.

Polymeric micelles are robust vehicles for triggered drug delivery due to the abudance of polymer materials and the ease of Mult functionalization of micelles. The micelles can be crosslinked to improve the vehicle stability and avoid premature drug release. Redox-sensitive polymer micelles can be produced via employing the disulfide-bearing molecules as the crosslinker. Samarajeewa et al. designed PTX-loaded shell cross-linked polymeric nanoparticles; the crosslinking was achieved by the amidation reaction between the carboxy group in the polymer backbone and the amine group in the linker molecule (Fig. 7A). Meng et al. highlighted the role of linker length on the therapeutic efficacy of redox-responsive polymer-curcumin conjugate micelles. This was because the different linker length could generate different side products. These side products exhibited different ring strain and could kinetically affect the rate of thiol-disulfide reaction (Fig. 7B). Compared to traditional nanoparticles by physical encapsulation, covalent cross-linking vehicles with disulfide bonds can minimize the off-target drug release.

In contrast to the physical drug encapsulation approach that shows the problem of poor drug loading and potential drug leaking during systemic circulation, chemical drug loading can circumvent these issues to a certain extent. Conjugating the active drug to the nanocarrier via disulfide-bearing linkers generate redox-responsive drug delivery systems. The multivalency effect can enable a high drug loading via the covalent mode. A large collection of anticancer therapeutic agents has been loaded nanocarriers via the chemical approach, including doxorubicin, paclitaxel, idarubicin, and camptothecin. To enable these nanocarriers redox-sensitive, various disulfide-bearing self-immolative linkers have been employed for drug conjugation. In many cases, the bulk material used for drug conjugation is multivalent polymer that generates polymer-drug conjugates upon chemical drug attachment. However, a key problem of chemical drug conjugation is the necessity of the presence of functional groups or moieties, which is not possible for certain drugs.

The ultimate aim of redox-responsive nanocarriers is to rapidly release the parent drug without modification upon the triggering of GSH. However, the majority of drugs do not contain the thiol group, which can be well addressed by the empolyment of SILs. SILs are functional spacers that can freely breadown via end-to-end decomposition or cyclization mechanisms. The active drug is often connected to the proximal end of the SIL, whereas the distal end is protected and can intiate the SIL disintegration and drug release upon the removal of protection property. The SIL concept can be integrated with disulfide to enable the redox-responsive release of various types of therapeutic agents, including alcohols, amines, amides, and acids (Fig. 7C and D). Despite this, the SIL architecture showed a dramatic influence on the drug release kinetics. For example, Deng et al. reported the disulfide crosslinked micellar nanoparticles which showed a linker type-dependent drug release rate (Fig. 7E). This highlights the importance of linker design in engineering redox-responsive drug delivery nanocarriers.

Thiol-disulfide exchange reaction

The thiol-disulfide exchange has pivotal roles in biomedicine and pharmaceutical science since the first report by Fava et al. in 1956. Such reaction is reversible chemical reaction where the -S- atom from the thiol attacks the disulfide, resulting in the replacement of the existing disulfide (RSSR) by a fresh one (R′SSR), and a release of a new thiolate (RSH). The equilibrium constant of the thiol/disulfide exchange reaction is determined by the stability difference between the two disulfides and thiols. Upon the thiol-disulfide exchange reaction between GSH and disulfide-containing nanocarrier, the drug is released post particle collapse. The thiol-disulfide exchange reaction is thermodynamically favored with a negative Gibbs free energy (∆G < 0), but the reaction kinetics is relatively slow. This point is often neglected in the design of redox-responsive drug delivery nanocarriers.

Application and prospect

Disulfide based redox-manipulating nanocarriers have been widely utilized for targeted drug release in response to the tumor microenvironment. When these nanocarriers, loaded with anti-tumor drugs, reach the tumor site, the unique conditions of the tumor microenvironment allow for precise drug release. This targeted approach reduces the impact on normal tissues, thereby enhancing treatment specificity. By controlling the timing and location of drug release in redox-sensitive nanocarriers, the therapeutic effects can be improved, addressing issues such as uneven drug distribution and drug resistance in vivo. These nanocarriers remain stable in normal tissues, but the elevated GSH levels in tumor tissues trigger the cleavage of disulfide bonds, leading to nanocarrier disintegration and rapid drug release. This mechanism enables controlled drug release and enhances the efficacy of the treatment.

Sun et al. designed three novel PTX-citronellol conjugates linked by carbon chains of varying lengths to disulfide bonds (Fig. 8A). These prodrugs can self-assemble into nanoparticles with uniform size and high drug loading capacity. The disulfide-bridged precursor drug nanoparticles exhibit a dual redox-responsive drug release mechanism. This study provides new insights into the stimuli-responsiveness of disulfide bonds and lays a strong foundation for developing innovative redox-sensitive nanocarriers for cancer treatment via DDS. Zhang et al. created RHPNs (CSDD-RHPNs) using PTX as a model drug and incorporating a carbon-spaced disulfide bridge (CSDD) (Fig. 8B). In this system, two disulfide-bridged homodimeric prodrugs (SD-HP) maintain consistent chemical bond lengths, enhancing both assembly stability and drug release rate. The CSDD chemical ligation offers a new direction for RHPN design, potentially overcoming limitations related to drug release rate and assembly stability in direct design approaches. Cui et al. prepared PBA-COS-ss-DOX/γ-PGA polymeric nano-prodrugs that are responsive to GSH (Fig. 8D). The disulfide bond in the DOX-ss prodrug reacts with the high GSH levels in tumor cells, leading to bond cleavage and targeted release of DOX at specific sites. The redox-sensitive covalent organic framework material (F68@SS-COFs) was prepared by Liu and his colleague (Fig. 8E). The F68@SS-COFs specifically respond to the tumor microenvironment, reacting with elevated GSH levels to disintegrate and release doxorubicin rapidly. This design aims to enhance doxorubicin delivery efficiency to tumors by achieving rapid and efficient drug release at targeted sites.

Trisulfide bonds offer intriguing potential compared to traditional disulfide bonds in the design of redox-responsive nanocarriers. The key distinction lies in the additional sulfur atom in trisulfide bonds, which endows them with a lower bond dissociation energy. This makes trisulfide bonds more susceptible to reduction, particularly in environments rich in reducing agents like glutathione, which is typically elevated in cancer cells. As a result, trisulfide bonds could facilitate a more rapid and targeted drug release in tumor tissues, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing the adverse effects associated with premature drug release in non-target tissues.

To investigate the impact of trisulfide bonds on homodimeric prodrug nano assemblies, Yang et al. designed three homodimeric DOX prodrugs (DOX-S-DOX, DOX-SS-DOX, and DOX-SSS-DOX). The trisulfide bonds demonstrated greater GSH sensitivity compared to traditional disulfide bonds, which enhanced tumor-specific drug release. Additionally, the trisulfide-bridged prodrug nano assemblies showed higher selectivity in cytotoxicity toward tumor cells, significantly reducing the systemic toxicity of doxorubicin (Fig. 8H). The team further highlighted the potential of trisulfide bonds in developing redox dual-responsive nanomedicines. They were the first to discover that trisulfide bonds can function as redox dual-responsive linkers and conducted a detailed study of the underlying mechanism. Li and colleagues developed redox-responsive NCs (NP(3 S)s) containing trisulfide bonds for targeted delivery of a platinum (IV) prodrug, aiming to enhance efficacy and reduce toxicity. The trisulfide bonds in the polymer can be cleaved to produce H2S, which boosts the anticancer effects of cisplatin while protecting against cisplatin-induced nephrotoxicity. This discovery confirms the anticancer potential of platinum prodrug nanocomplexes with trisulfide bonds and systematically examines their mechanism of action, providing important evidence to clarify drug mechanisms and accelerate clinical application (Fig. 8G).

Thus, among the various stimuli-sensitive anti-tumor drug nanocarriers, redox-sensitive nanocarriers have gained increasing attention for their ability to control drug delivery and release in specific environments. Encapsulating drugs in these redox-sensitive carriers improves targeted delivery efficiency, enhances selectivity for tumor cells, reduces toxic side effects on normal cells, and allows the drug to reach the tumor site more effectively, thereby lowering systemic toxicity. Compared to other DDS, drug carriers with reduction-sensitive disulfide bonds offer unique advantages. They enhance carrier structure integrity during circulation, reduce the likelihood of drug resistance, and improve the effectiveness of cancer treatment. These benefits make disulfide bonds to be one of the most promising linkages in DDS, with broad clinical application potential. Additionally, the previously mentioned trisulfide bonds offer the potential for fine-tuning the degradation kinetics of nanocarriers, providing a pathway for constructing more sophisticated and controllable nanoplatforms. This makes it possible to develop nanocarriers with customizable release profiles. The development of disulfide bonds and similar sulfur-based structures enriches redox-manipulating nanocarrier systems, opening new avenues for therapeutic innovation and prompting a re-evaluation of traditional nanomedicine design approaches.

Redox homeostasis manipulation for anti-tumor therapy

GSH depletion

To control the overproduction of ROS and prevent its harmful effects to cells, cells have evolved a complex array of defensive systems. These systems include scavenging enzymes and some endogenous or dietary-assumed antioxidant agents that can limit ROS accumulation. Cancer cells grow rapidly and often face metabolic stress that can induce apoptotic cell death. However, cancer cells can adapt to it by changing metabolic pathways. The exposure of cells to free radicals is protected by a series of enzyme and non-enzyme defense mechanisms. The enzyme-based antioxidant defense system includes catalase (CAT), glutathione peroxidase (GPX), and superoxide dismutase (SOD). Typical non-enzyme antioxidants include flavonoids, tocopherols (vitamin E, VE), ascorbic acid (vitamin C, VC), GSH, and carotenoids. Under normal circumstances, there is a balance between antioxidants and intracellular ROS, which is essential for the survival of the organisms. In tumor cells, inducing redox imbalance can cause cell death for therapeutic purpose.

Typical non-enzymatic antioxidants include GSH, vitamin C, vitamin E, and carotenoids, among which GSH is central to the detoxification of ROS. The main protective roles of GSH against oxidative stress are as follows: (i) GSH is a cofactor of several detoxifying enzymes against oxidative stress, i.e. GPX and glutathione transferase; (ii) GSH is involved with the membrane transport of amino acids; (iii) GSH can not only scavenge hydroxyl radical and singlet oxygen directly, but also detoxify hydrogen peroxide and lipid peroxides together with GPX; (iv) GSH can regenerate vitamin C and vitamin E. A high concentration of GSSG may damage many enzymes. Under the conditions of enhanced oxidative stress, the content of GSSG increases at the cost of GSH, which in turn increases the content of mixed disulfide proteins. Therefore, the ratio of GSH/GSSG provides a simple and convenient method for indicating cell redox state. Reducing the intracellular level of GSH in contrast to GSSG can increase the degree of oxidative stress in tumor cells, which provides a new approach for cancer therapy.

Excess ROS will make oxidative damage to DNA, proteins or lipids and lead to redox imbalance. The frequently-used ROS-based cancer therapy methods, i.e. chemical dynamic therapy (CDT), sonodynamic therapy (SDT) as well as PDT can induce oxidative stress and cell death. However, the GSH-related cellular antioxidant defense system limits the efficacy of ROS-therapy. Although the intracellular GSH concentration is high, its depletion can be observed when sufficient ROS is produced. In 1995, Cain et al. investigated into the effects of SDT or PDT on intracellular thiol levels. After ultrasound or irradiation treatment, intracellular thiol pools were significantly reduced, leading to GSH depletion. This depletion by nanocarriers can enhance the efficacy of ROS-based cancer therapy. Fan et al. reported a MnO2-based nanomedicine for improving PDT efficiency. The photosensitizer Ce6 was adsorbed by the MnO2 nanosheets. After endocytosis, the nanosystem is disintegrated to simultaneously release Ce6 and decrease the level of GSH, resulting in highly efficient PDT. Lin et al. reported a self-reinforced MnO2-based CDT nanomedicine. When MnO2-coated MSNs (MS@MnO2 NPs) were internalized, the MnO2 shell underwent a redox reaction with GSH, causing GSH depletion and CDT enhancement.

Intriguingly, GSH depletion is associated with GPX4 inhibition and ferroptosis induction. The action of GPX4 in detoxifying lipid peroxides to lipid alcohols necessitates the assistance of GSH as a cofactor. Meng et al. reported the tailored nanocarrier is activatable on GSH triggering, and the degree of iron apoptosis can be adjusted by adjusting the dose of the carrier and the photosensitizer and the light irradiation conditions. Because the nanocarrier itself can induce ferroptosis, it is a “all-active” delivery system, while the traditional PDT nanodrug carrier is inert and has no therapeutic effect (Fig. 9A). Gan et al. proposed theragnostic ferroptosis inducer (IR780-SPhF), which can simultaneously realize the imaging and treatment of triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) by targeting mitochondria. The entry of IR780-SPhF into cancer cells and mitochondria is mediated by the overexpressed OATP transporter and its lipophilic cation on the surface of cancer cells. IR780-SPhF induces apoptosis-mediated cell death through GSH depletion, GPX4 inactivation and mitochondrial lipid peroxide accumulation (Fig. 9B). He et al. designed tumor microenvironment-responsive Mn-based nanozymes (Mn-based NEs) to amplify immune-driven iron apoptosis. After intracellular delivery, GSH is consumed by disulfide-thiol exchange and Mn3+/Mn2+ redox (Fig. 9C). Fang et al. prepared a single component organic photosensitizer PPR-2CN with near infrared-I (NIR-I). The specific glutamate (Glu) and GSH consumption capacity of PPR-2CN inhibited the intracellular biosynthesis of GSH, leading to redox imbalance and GSH depletion, eventually leading to ferroptosis (Fig. 9D). When the concentration of PPR-2CN NPs increased gradually, the relative total GSH level decreased (Fig. 9E), and Fig. 9F showed a diagram of iron sagging caused by GSH depletion. Therefore, GSH depletion indirectly causes the loss of GPX4 activity, resulting in ferroptotic cell death.

Redox-manipulating NCs enhance ROS-based anti-tumor efficacy

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) mainly include singlet oxygen (1O2), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), hydroxyl radical (•OH) and superoxide (•O2-), which are continuously produced during cell metabolism in response to signal stimulation in the environment. In normal cells, ROS is considered to be a host defense molecule released by neutrophils; it is used to kill exogenous pathogens, i.e. bacteria. ROS plays a key role in intracellular signal transduction that involves a series of cellular processes. In highly proliferative cells, the ROS production also increases due to the active metabolism. This phenomenon is often observed in cancer cells where the maintenance of intracellular redox homeostasis is essential for cell survival. Generally, cells can be damaged by excess ROS. These free radicals can indiscriminately react with biological macromolecules in the cells, causing oxidative damage to DNA, proteins or lipids. In order to reduce the damage of oxidative stress and regulate redox homeostasis, complicated antioxidant systems have been developed, such as the antioxidant thiols and enzymes mentioned above. The induction of redox imbalance has been a means of cancer therapy.

Antioxidants play a vital role in protection cells against ROS-induced cell damage. Therefore, inhibition of antioxidant systems (i.e. antioxidant thiols) is considered to induce oxidative stress. GSH as the representative of non-enzymatic antioxidants systems is central to the detoxification of ROS. GSH can directly neutralize ROS, thereby reducing the efficiency of ROS-based anti-tumor therapies. Recent studies suggest that depleting GSH through a disulfide bond-driven thiol-disulfide exchange mechanism may be a viable approach to enhance the therapeutic performance and selectivity of many cancer drugs. This method could also help prevent the acquisition and development of drug resistance, indicating its potential as a key strategy in supporting cancer treatment. Wan et al. designed a disulfide bond-driven nanodrug delivery system (CM-HSADSP@[PS-Sor]) to disrupt redox balance and enhance AIE-gens’ PDT treatment. The system depletes excess GSH by utilizing abundant disulfide bonds, accelerating GSH depletion and increasing ROS levels in cancer cells, thereby amplifying PDT efficacy and achieving effective breast cancer therapy results in vivo. This study offers a new strategy for ROS-based anti-tumor therapy through disulfide bond-driven GSH depletion. Non-invasive SDT holds great potential for treating squamous cell carcinoma, but the ROS generated by ultrasound sensitizers heavily depends on the high levels of GSH within tumor cells. To address this, Fan and colleagues designed a nanodrug composed of GSH-sensitive polyphosphate (SS-PPE) and ROS-sensitive polyphosphate (S-PPE). The disulfide bonds in SS-PPE effectively deplete GSH, preventing ROS consumption and enabling a novel synergistic chemo-SDT strategy for squamous cell carcinoma.

Ferroptosis

Definition of ferroptosis

Ferroptosis is a form of regulated cell death that is dependent on ferrous iron; it has attracted widespread attention in the fields of oncology, cell biology, and pharmaceutical science (Fig. 10). Ferroptosis is featured with increased mitochondrial membrane density. Since the first discovery in 2012, numerous strategies have been proposed to induce ferroptosis of cancer cells, including the use of nanomaterials, small molecular agents, and biomacromolecules. Differing from traditional apoptosis and necrosis, ferroptosis can induce cell death due to iron-dependent lipid peroxide accumulation. The three key elements of ferroptosis are redox-active iron (Fe2+), the loss of lipid peroxide repair ability caused by the inhibition of GPX4, and the accumulation of tailored lipid peroxides. GSH is essential for cellular antioxidant defense systems, including glutathione-dependent glutathione transferases (GSTs) and GPX4. Additionally, in some tumor cells, the biosynthesis of GSH relies on the continuous introduction of cystine/glutamate antiporter xCT. Furthermore, as the major detoxification enzyme of ferroptotic cell death, GPX4 can convert lipid hydrogen peroxide (L-OOH) into non-toxic lipid alcohol (L-OH). Ferroptosis may be employed for suppressing the growth of cancer cells. It has been discovered that some of the most drug-resistant and hard-to-kill cancer cells are highly sensitive to GPX4 inhibition. These results suggest that ferroptosis-inducing agents might be developed as a new type of cancer therapy.

Molecular and cellular mechanisms of ferroptosis

Iron metabolism abnormality (Fig. 11A). As an important trace element in organisms, iron is essential for many important biological functions. Iron metabolism plays a vital regulatory role in ferroptosis. Iron is an essential element for lipid peroxidation accumulation and ferroptosis. It mainly produces a large number of lipid peroxides through Fenton reaction and activation of iron-containing enzymes, while providing energy for cells. Excessive iron converts hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) into more toxic •OH through the Fenton reaction, thereby promoting ferroptosis. Non-enzymatic iron-dependent Fenton chain reaction may be crucial for ferroptosis. When GPX4 is inhibited, phospholipid hydroperoxides (PLOOHs, a lipid-based ROS form) can last longer and initiate Fenton reaction to rapidly amplify PLOOHs, which is an important marker of ferroptosis. PLOOHs can react with ferrous ions and iron ions to generate free radicals PLO• and PLOO•, respectively, driving destructive peroxide chain reactions. Meanwhile, some lipoxygenases (LOXs) and cytochrome P450 oxidoreductases (POR) require iron as a catalyst. Iron is also essential for many redox-based metabolic processes involved in cellular ROS production.

Abnormal lipid peroxidation metabolism (Fig. 11B). Lipid production is the basis of survival, and unlimited lipid peroxidation is another sign of ferroptosis. Lipid peroxidation caused by ROS is considered to be a biochemical indicator of ferroptosis. Due to the high content of polyunsaturated fatty acids, cell membranes and organelle membranes are particularly susceptible to lipid peroxidation. Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) play an important role in death. Long-chain fatty acid-coenzyme a ligase 4 (ACSL4) and lysophosphatidyl transferase 5 promote the binding of PUFAs to phospholipids to form PUFA-containing phospholipids, which are easily oxidized by arachidonate lipoxygenase (ALOX) and various ROS to form lipid peroxides, resulting in the destruction of lipid bilayers and affecting membrane function, eventually leading to ferroptosis.

Amino acid metabolism (Fig. 11C). Ferroptosis is closely related to amino acid metabolism. GSH biosynthesis and GPX4 activity depend on amino acid metabolism. GSH is a key substance in amino acid metabolism during ferroptosis, which is mainly synthesized by glycine, glutamic acid and cysteine. Cysteine inside and outside the cell is an essential substance for GSH biosynthesis. Under physiological conditions, the cystine/glutamate anti-transport system (System Xc−) on the cell membrane takes extracellular cystine, transports it into the cell, and releases glutamate into the cytoplasm. At the same time, cystine is reduced to cysteine and used to synthesize GSH. High concentrations of glutamate can produce toxic effects, affecting the function of system Xc− and ultimately leading to ferroptosis. Therefore, abnormal amino acid metabolism is another important mechanism of ferroptosis.

At the cellular level, ferroptosis includes membrane lipid peroxidation, abnormal movement of ions on the plasma membrane, cell swelling and plasma membrane rupture. Plasma membrane rupture is a key event of ferroptosis. The increase of lipid peroxidation will increase the membrane tension, change the ion flux of the plasma membrane, increase the Ca2+ uptake mediated by Piezo, increase the Ca2+ and Na+ uptake mediated by transient receptor potential (TRP), and decrease the Na+ excretion and K+ uptake mediated by Na+/K+ ATPase, which may be due to the change of the interaction between the protein and the lipid peroxide membrane phospholipid. In general, these changes lead to loss of ion homeostasis and swelling of permeable cells, eventually leading to rupture of the plasma membrane and eventually ferroptosis. Therefore, ferroptosis is distinguished from other death mechanisms at the molecular and cellular levels by characteristic changes in iron ions, ROS, GSH and lipid peroxidation levels, and mitochondrial morphology (Fig. 11E).

Ferroptotic anti-tumor therapy

A large number of studies have outlined the blueprint of ferroptosis as a new choice for cancer treatment. As a new type of cell death caused by iron-dependent lipid peroxide accumulation, ferroptosis has attracted vast attention. Ferroptosis can be triggered by inhibition of GPX4 or GSH depletion that is often achieved by disulfide bond driving. In addition, the accumulation of oxidizable polyunsaturated phospholipids (PE-AA/PE-AdA) or manipulation of iron homeostasis can also induce ferroptosis. Notably, nanomedicine provides new options in inducing ferroptosis for anti-tumor therapy. Nanocarriers can address the following issues of ferroptotic inducers, e.g. poor water solubility, low stability, and low targeting efficiency. Additionally, it can integrate ferroptosis with other tumor treatment modalities, such as photodynamic and photothermal therapies. In the following sections, we will focus on ferroptotic anti-tumor therapies based on GSH depletion, GPX4 inhibition, immune activation, and overcoming tumor drug resistance.

xCT is essential to transport cystine and synthesis GSH. Consequently, the survival and growth of cancer cells are strongly dependent on the activity of system xCT, making it a potential target for cancer therapy. System xCT is a membrane Na+-dependent cysteine-glutamate exchange antiporter that is composed of solute carrier family 3 membrane 2 (SLC3A2) and solute carrier family 7 membrane 11 (SLC7A11). xCT typically mediates the exchange of extracellular L-cystine and intracellular L-glutamate across the cellular plasma membrane. The metabolic consequence of system xCT inhibition is dramatic depletion of intracellular cysteine and GSH. GSH is central to the detoxification of ROS because it acts as the coenzyme of GPX4. Hence, the depletion of system xCT can induce the depletion of GSH and then cause the iron-dependent accumulation of lipid peroxides which eventually causes ferroptosis. Erastin can suppress system xCT and activate mitochondrial voltage-dependent anion channels; it is the first identified chemical compound that is able to cause ferroptotic cell death. In Sato et al.’s report, the ferroptosis inducer erastin can irreversibly inhibit system xCT and synergize with cisplatin to increase its cytotoxicity towards cancer cells. In this context, the small molecule erastin can target and inhibit system xCT, leading to GSH depletion and consequently ferroptotic cancer cell death.

As an essential regulator in ferroptosis, GPX4 plays an important role in preventing ferroptotic cancer cell death. As mentioned above, GPX4 is a kind of antioxidant defense enzyme which can catalyze the reduction of cell membrane-bound phospholipid hydroperoxides, protect against lipid peroxidation, and eventually prevent cell death induced by oxidative damage. GSH is utilized as the coenzyme of GPX4; ferroptosis can be induced by GPX4 inactivation caused by GSH depletion. It has been demonstrated that GSH depletion can inactivate GPX4 and induce ferroptosis. Wang et al. discovered arginine-rich manganese silicate nanobubbles (AMSNs) to induce ferroptosis by GSH depletion and GPX4 inhibition (Fig. 12). Subsequently, ferroptosis induced by GSH depletion could achieve significant tumor suppressive effects. Meanwhile, (1 S, 3R)-RSL3 is another molecule for ferroptosis initiation. RSL3 targets enzymes with a nucleophilic site, and inactivates GPX4 directly via alkylation of the selenocysteine. An et al. designed a calcium phosphate (CaP) core-lipid shell hybrid nanocarrier which loaded hydrophobic GPX4 inhibitor (RSL3) and ferric ammonium citrate (FAC) in the vector shell and core, respectively. FAC in pharmacological concentrations can increase hydrogen peroxide in the tumor cells, which would favor the Fenton reaction and ferroptotic cell death.

Ferroptosis of tumor cells can promote cancer immunotherapy. Immunotherapy can stimulate CD8+ T cells to release interferon-γ (IFNγ). IFNγ reduced the expression of two subunits SLC3A2 and SLC7A11 of the System Xc−, damaged the uptake of cystine by tumor cells, thereby consuming GSH in tumor cells, and ultimately caused lipid peroxidation-dependent ferroptosis in tumor cells. Additionally, IFNγ stimulates ACSL4 expression in the presence of arachidonic acid and other fatty acids, thereby increasing the binding of arachidonic acid to phospholipids containing C16 and C18 acyl chains, and ultimately causing ferroptosis in tumor cells. Therefore, tumor-infiltrating T cells can induce ferroptosis of tumor cells through a variety of mechanisms. Liao et al. found that IFNγ released by CD8+ T cells combined with PUFAs as a natural ferroptosis inducer produces tumor ferroptosis and promotes anti-tumor immunity in an ACSL4-dependent manner (Fig. 13A). In the paper, an erastin-resistant ID8 cell was designed, and the mice were injected with this cell and then treated with programmed death ligand 1 (PD-L1) antibody. It was found that the effect of immunotherapy almost disappeared. This strongly suggests that ferroptosis of tumor cells is related to the role of immunotherapy. Fang et al. synthesized nanomaterial Fe/Allayered double hydroxide (Fe/Al-LDH), which can induce ferroptosis of tumor cells and M1 polarization of macrophages, thereby enhancing anti-tumor immunotherapy (Fig. 13B). LDHP was obtained by PEG modification on the surface of Fe/Al-LDH. In tumor cells, Fe2+ released by LDHP can catalyze PUFAs to produce a large amount of ROS, induce lipid peroxidation, and finally cause ferroptosis of tumor cells, which in turn triggers T cell activation and tumor infiltration. At the same time, the free Fe2+ ions released by LDHP endocytosed by macrophages will catalyze H2O2 to generate •OH through Fenton reaction, thus reversing TAMs from tumor-promoting phenotype (M2) to tumor-inhibiting phenotype (M1), and synergistically enhancing anti-tumor immunity with cytotoxic T cells. It will be a promising strategy to use the synergistic effect between ferroptosis of tumor cells and M1 polarization of macrophages for tumor immunotherapy.

The emergence of multidrug resistance (MDR) is one of the main reasons for the failure of cancer chemotherapy. Ferroptosis is associated with tumor chemotherapy resistance. Induction of ferroptosis restores the sensitivity of certain cancer types with intrinsic or acquired resistance to chemotherapy, which can reverse cancer chemotherapy resistance. Persister cancer cells (PCCs) play an important role in the development of MDR. This is due to the close relationship between the formation of PCCs and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT). PCCs are rare immortal cells in the tumor microenvironment, which can produce a variety of cell types to form tumor stroma by division. PCCs are mainly in a non-dividing cell cycle state, so they can escape conventional chemotherapy. Hence, PCCs are considered to be one of the intrinsic drug resistance causes of tumor recurrence. Gao et al.’s study found that iron apoptosis eliminates EMT-state cells by manipulating intracellular redox homeostasis, and triggers polymer micelles to load typical iron-inducing inducers (RSL3) to achieve efficient PCCs reduction and MDR reversal (Fig. 13D and E). Zheng et al. established an organoid biobank derived from colorectal cancer and induced acquired resistance by repeating low-level exposure to chemotherapy drugs. They also designed a monoclonal antibody (LGR4-mAb) combined with chemotherapy drugs, and found that ferroptosis was involved in cell death after LGR4-mAb combined with chemotherapy, especially in drug-resistant organoid groups (DR PDOs). Immunohistochemical staining showed that the level of 4-hydroxynonenal (4HNE) in DR PDOs was higher after combined treatment, and the membrane was combined with transferrin receptor, which was the characteristic of ferroptosis. In addition, the expression of molecular markers of ferroptosis was increased by more than five times under the combined treatment of LGR4-mAb and chemotherapeutic drugs. These results suggest that LGR4-mAb selectively sensitizes the organoids of colorectal cancer (CRC PDOs) to ferroptosis to overcome acquired resistance. Ferroptosis of tumor cells can promote cancer immunotherapy, and can improve drug-induced ferroptosis to reverse tumor drug resistance. In short, ferroptosis has great development prospects in immunotherapy and anti-tumor drug resistance.

Cuproptosis

Cuproptosis is a newly identified form of programmed cell death caused by excessive accumulation of intracellular copper. Copper-induced cell death is regulated by mitochondrial ferredoxin 1-mediated protein lipoylation. Copper-dependent death occurs via direct binding of copper to lipoylated components of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle. Enriched Cu2+ binds to and oligomerizes dihydrolipoamide S-acetyltransferase (DLAT) within mitochondria, triggering disruption of the TCA and activating the cellular cuproptosis pathway. This leads to the aggregation of lipoylated proteins and the subsequent loss of iron-sulfur (Fe-S) cluster proteins, causing proteotoxic stress and ultimately resulting in cell death. Guo et al. designed a ROS responsive NCs (PHPM) with elesclomol and copper combined with αPD-L1 (NP@ESCu) by inducing cuproptosis for enhanced tumor immunotherapy. The research presents the initial report on the combination of nanomedicine capable of inducing cuproptosis with αPD-L1 to improve cancer treatment, offering a new approach for future cancer therapies. Ding et al. incorporated Cu2+ onto a carbon photocatalyst (Cu@CDCN), facilitating the sustained production of H2 and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) in tumors through a two-electron visible light water splitting mechanism. This innovative approach merges hydrogen therapy with cuproptosis by utilizing metal-enhanced visible solar water splitting within the realm of nanomedicine, presenting a promising and safe method for cancer treatment. Despite its significant potential in cancer treatment, the clinical application of cuproptosis still encounters obstacles.

The tumor microenvironment and redox balance are crucial for maintaining normal physiological functions. To counteract oxidative stress, the key antioxidant GSH is upregulated, which impedes the effectiveness of both cuproptosis and ferroptosis. In cuproptosis, the depletion of GSH can enhance DLAT oligomerization and increase the sensitivity of tumor cells to this process. In ferroptosis, GSH not only directly neutralizes ROS but also serves as a crucial co-substrate for GPX4, reducing toxic LPO to non-toxic lipid hydroxyl derivatives (LOH), thereby preventing membrane damage and inducing ferroptosis. Consequently, GSH depletion can simultaneously enhance the efficacy of both cuproptosis and ferroptosis, leading to the concurrent occurrence of these two cell death mechanisms in various cancer therapies (Fig. 14). Huang et al. reported nanoparticles (HMCIS) that create a self-accelerating cycle of ferroptosis and cuproptosis for effective anti-tumor therapy. Wang et al. found that the ferroptosis inducers sorafenib and erastin intensified copper ionophore-induced cuproptosis in liver cancer by upregulating protein lipoylation and suppressing intracellular GSH synthesis. Based on these findings, they proposed an anti-liver cancer strategy that combines ferroptosis inducers with copper ionophores. Therefore, the therapeutic potential of cuproptosis suggests that copper-based drugs could be promising for cancer treatment. Combining these therapies with other anti-tumor strategies may offer more targeted, personalized, and effective treatment options, potentially improving cancer outcomes.

Perspective

Drug delivery system via redox-manipulating nanocarriers is an important strategy to increase anti-tumor efficacy and reduce adverse effects. This review highlights the impacts of the biological property of redox-manipulating nanoparticles on tumor drug delivery through adjusting their biodistribution, tumor retention, cell permeability and controlled release of tumor microenvironment. The redox-manipulating nanocarriers show oppositely high drug loading without premature drug release. However, the disulfide-driven redox-sensitive bond which mediated thiol-disulfide exchange reaction is kinetically slow, which presents additional barrier for drug release and hence limits the therapeutic outcome to a certain extent. Replace disulfide with diseliunium in the future work may remove such barrier because the thiol-diselinium exchange reaction is kinetically much faster compared to the thiol-disulfide counterpart. Trisulfide bonds offer intriguing potential compared to traditional disulfide bonds in the design of redox-responsive nanocarriers due to their lower bond dissociation energy and faster reduction capabilities. As a result, trisulfide bonds could enable more rapid and targeted drug release in tumor tissues, enhancing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing the adverse effects associated with premature drug release in non-target tissues.

Reductive sensitive disulfide bonds are usually used as hydrophilic and hydrophobic polymers, the connection between polymers and drugs, or as cross-linking agents in polymer drug carriers. Compared with other DDS, the polymer drug carrier with disulfide bond has unique advantages. It can enhance the integrity of the carrier structure in the circulation, avoid drug leakage before reaching the tumor tissue, improve the effect of anti-cancer treatment, and reduce the probability of drug resistance. These advantages make the disulfide bond one of the most striking coupling bonds in DDS and have broad clinical application prospects.

Considering the increasing demands of redox-manipulating NPs for nanomedicine, more endeavors should be given to systematic and in-depth investigate the mechanisms for drug release, drug effect and degradation mechanism. Compared to exogenous stimuli, redox as endogenous stimuli are more practical and flexible in the design of drug delivery system, but their release mechanism is more complex. Simultaneously, the issue regarding redox-manipulating nanocarrier is that both cancer cells and normal cells contain high levels of GSH; the cell-selectivity is poor in terms of on-demand drug delivery. In addition, clinical data of these materials except for a small amount of liposomes and micelles are still limited, preventing immediate use of most nanomedicines. It is very crucial to evaluate resulting toxicity in the future. Assembling nanostructure as the individual structures after the disassembly by the redox reactions passably increase the safe usage of redox-responsive NPs in clinical settings, smaller particle sizes may also allow inorganic NPs to reduce clinical toxicity through renal metabolism.

Ferroptosis is an iron-dependent non-apoptotic cell death that can elicited by pharmacological inhibiting the cystine/glutamate antiporter, system Xc− or directly binding and loss of activity of GPX4 in cancer cells. GPX4 reported to be a regulator of ferroptosis by direct binding to GPX4 and indirectly loss of GSH respectively. Redox-sensitive NPs can disintegrate in the presence of high concentrations of GSH in the tumor microenvironment. At the same time, NPs also consume GSH, which can indirectly lead to ferroptosis. The apoptosis-induced cell death could induce an immune response for eliminating tumor cells. Meanwhile, the interaction between ferroptosis and the immune systems is still an enigma, which merits further investigation. Although the exact mechanism remains to be established, more and more evidence suggests that ferroptosis may lead to aseptic inflammation through lipid peroxidation-mediated plasma membrane rupture. Hence, the sensitization of tumor cells to ferroptosis may provide an original measure for anti-cancer immunity and may offer the optimization of established immunotherapies.

Conclusion

In summary, targeted and controlled delivery of anti-tumor agents is an important way to increase efficiency and reduce toxicity of cancer therapies. Based on the reducing microenvironment inside the tumor cells, redox-manipulating nanocarriers can achieve on-demand cargo release. It is predictable that more redox-sensitive nanomedicine will be developed in the future. As of today, a broad variety of drug delivery systems is engineered and described, releasing incorporating drug(s), and/or acquiring the ability to specifically interact/bind cancer cells and enhancing drug therapeutic action. Despite multiple publications in this exciting field, its clinical transformation is still in its infancy, and only a few systems have entered clinical application. Considering that these systems have obvious therapeutic anticancer potential and the ability to be fine-tuned, it is certain that in the near future, we will witness a sharp increase in their transformation, and this transformation will positively change the overall pattern of cancer treatment and personalized medicine.
