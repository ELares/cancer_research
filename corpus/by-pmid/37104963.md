# Development and characterization of lung surfactant-coated polymer nanoparticles for pulmonary drug delivery.

## Abstract

Lung cancer is often diagnosed at an advanced stage where tumors are usually inoperable and first-line therapies are inefficient and have off-targeted adverse effects, resulting in poor patient survival. Here, we report the development of an inhalable poly lactic-co-glycolic acid polymer-based nanoparticle (PLGA-NP) formulation with a biomimetic Infasurf® lung surfactant (LS) coating, for localized and sustained lung cancer drug delivery. The nanoparticles (188 ± 7 nm) were stable in phosphate buffered saline, serum and Gamble's solution (simulated lung fluid), and demonstrated cytocompatibility up to 1000 μg/mL concentration and dose-dependent uptake by lung cancer cells. The LS coating significantly decreased nanoparticle (NP) uptake by NR8383 alveolar macrophages in vitro compared to uncoated NPs. The coating, however, did not impair NP uptake by A549 lung adenocarcinoma cells. The anti-cancer drug gemcitabine hydrochloride encapsulated in the PLGA core was released in a sustained manner while the paclitaxel loaded in the LS shell demonstrated a rapid or burst release profile over 21 days. The drug-loaded NPs significantly decreased cancer cell survival and colony formation in vitro compared to free drugs and single drug-loaded NPs. In vivo studies confirmed greater retention of LS-coated NPs in the lungs of C57BL/6 WT mice compared to uncoated NPs, at 24 h and 72 h following intranasal administration. The overall results confirm that LS coating is a unique strategy for cloaking polymeric NPs to potentially prevent their rapid lung clearance and facilitate prolonged pulmonary drug delivery.

Introduction

Lung cancer, the leading cause of cancer-related deaths worldwide, accounted for approximately 25% of the cancer mortality globally in 2022. It is estimated that by 2035, world lung cancer deaths will increase by 86%, leading to approximately 3 million deaths worldwide. Lung cancer is usually asymptomatic and is hence diagnosed at a late stage, necessitating rigorous treatment upon diagnosis. Chemotherapy, radiotherapy, surgical resection or a combination of these are the primary treatment methods available depending on the severity and the stage of the cancer at the time of diagnosis. Surgery is often recommended only for early-stage cancer, and is not suitable for advanced or metastatic disease. Radiotherapy in the thoracic region on the other hand, is associated with severe adverse effects such as lung damage due to radiosensitivity of the healthy lung tissue and ineffectiveness against metastatic cancer. Systemic chemotherapy has been the standard first-line treatment for lung cancer, however it has only modest effects on overall patient survival due to poor response rate, rapid relapse, limited therapeutic efficacy and severe off-target toxic effects leading to patient discomfort and poor long-term survival. Therefore, the lung cancer mortality rate continues to be high.

Drug delivery by inhalation offers several advantages over conventional drug administration methods, including localized drug deposition for treatment of respiratory diseases, high bioavailability due to avoidance of first-pass metabolism, and rapid onset of action. Pulmonary delivery of chemotherapeutics is useful for concentrating chemotherapeutic drugs at the tumor site; however frequent administration may be troublesome to lung cancer patients whose breathing is already weak compared to healthy individuals. A platform for long- term sustained drug release following single administration is therefore advantageous.

Drug-containing polymeric nanoparticles (NPs) are being increasingly investigated for inhalational drug delivery to treat lung cancer and other chronic lung ailments. Following inhalation, NPs will localize within the deep lung tissue and provide sustained release of the encapsulated therapeutic agent over times for site-specific and effective treatment. Natural polymers such as albumin, chitosan, sodium alginate and gelatin, and synthetic polymers like poly lactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA), poly lactic acid, poloxamer and poly(ethylene glycol) have been investigated by us and others previously to formulate NPs as they are known to be safe, biodegradable and biocompatible, thus minimizing toxicological problems usually associated with the polymer degradation by-products. PLGA, a biodegradable and biocompatible polymer that is FDA-approved for many biomedical applications, is known to be effectively taken up by cells through specific and non-specific endocytosis and release the encapsulated drug slowly, thus maintaining a sustained drug effect. We have previously shown that PLGA nanoparticles (PLGA-NPs) can be delivered to the deep lung tissue by nebulization, and that the fluorescence from these NPs persisted in the lungs for up to 10 days although a decrease in fluorescence was observed over time as the particles were cleared.

The lung has a dynamic environment which is actively involved in eliminating or clearing objects entering the respiratory system. Alveolar macrophages present at the interface between air and lung tissue are a major barrier to effective pulmonary drug delivery as they phagocytose foreign particles that enter the lower respiratory tract. Foreign particles, NPs or pathogens are engulfed by the macrophages either by phagocytosis or pinocytosis depending on the particle size. The muco-ciliary escalator operating in the airways is also actively involved in eliminating deposited drugs and particles, thus resulting in poor accumulation at the desired target sites. Previous research by Saduskas et al. showed that intratracheally administered gold NPs ranging 40 – 100 nm in diameter was phagocytosed by alveolar macrophages within 1 hour of administration. Gelatin NPs targeting epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR)-overexpressing lung cancer cells were found to accumulate not just in the lungs but also in the kidneys, heart, liver, spleen and brain 24 hours following nebulization. We hypothesized that coating the NPs with a biomimetic material intrinsic to the lungs will potentially prevent their recognition as foreign material by alveolar macrophages, thereby ensuring longer lung retention of these NPs for sustained therapy. Previously, Poelma et al. demonstrated that alveolar macrophages internalized significantly lower amounts of dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC) - the main phospholipid in naturally produced lung surfactant, compared to other surfactant lipids. Others have also demonstrated that coating microspheres with DPPC lipids decreases uptake by about 30–40%.

Lung surfactant (LS) is a complex mixture of phospholipids, the most prominent being DPPC (80–90%), and surfactant proteins. It is native to the lungs as it is secreted by the Type II alveolar epithelial cells. In this work, we coated PLGA-NPs with Infasurf® (also known as Calfactant), which is a commercially available calf LS extract developed by ONY Biotech. Infasurf is currently being used in the treatment of infant respiratory distress syndrome as it lowers the surface tension in the lungs and also plays an important role in forestalling alveolar collapse at low lung volume. Of the several drugs and drug combinations used for lung cancer treatment, gemcitabine hydrochloride (GEM) and paclitaxel (PTX) have demonstrated promising results in the treatment of advanced stages of lung cancer. Recent studies have also shown that this combination could be a second-line treatment regimen after platinum-based therapy. Similar to other chemo regimens, this drug combination also displays side effects like neuropathy and neutropenia in some patients which can be potentially overcome by encapsulating the free drugs within an NP formulation for targeted, localized and sustained release over time. Therefore, in this study, GEM was encapsulated within the PLGA core whereas PTX was chosen for encapsulation within the LS shell of the NP formulation. The formulation thus developed was characterized in vitro and an in vivo study was done to visualize NP deposition and retention in the lungs.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Materials

Resomer RG 503 H, Poly(D,L-lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) (copolymer ratio 50:50), poly(vinyl alcohol), cholesterol, ethyl acetate and phosphate buffered saline (PBS) (0.01 M phosphate buffer, 0.0027 M potassium chloride and 0.137 M sodium chloride, pH 7.4) were purchased from Sigma–Aldrich (St. Louis, MO, USA). Infasurf (Calfactant) was gifted to us by Dr. Edmund Egan at ONY, Inc. (Amherst, NY, USA). GEM and chloroform were purchased from Acros Organics (Morris, NJ, USA). PTX was purchased from Alfa Alesar (Ward Hill, MA, USA). All other chemicals and solvents used were of analytical reagent grade.

A549 and NR8383 cells were purchased from ATCC (Manassas, VA, USA). Dulbecco’s minimum essential media (DMEM) was purchased from Sigma Life Science (MO, USA). F12-K culture medium, 0.25% trypsin, fetal bovine serum (FBS) and penicillin-streptomycin (pen-strep, antibiotic) were all purchased from Gibco (NY, USA). The bicinchoninic acid (BCA)® Protein Assay Kit was purchased from Pierce, Rockford, IL and FITC Annexin V/ Dead Cell Apoptosis Kit was purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific (MA, USA).

Preparation of the GEM-loaded PLGA-NPs

The PLGA-NPs containing GEM were prepared by a double-emulsion solvent evaporation method. Briefly, 5 mg GEM was dissolved in 500 μL of 1% poly(vinyl alcohol) and emulsified with 25 mg of PLGA dissolved in 2.50 ml of ethyl acetate using a probe sonicator (Fisher Scientific CL-18, NJ, USA) for 30 s. This primary (w/o) emulsion was then added dropwise under constant stirring to 5% PVA and probe-sonicated for 1 min. The resultant emulsion was allowed to stir overnight to ensure complete evaporation of organic solvent. The NPs were then collected and washed with deionized water by ultracentrifugation (Optima L-100 XP, Beckman Coulter, USA) at 25,000 g for 30 min at 10 °C, lyophilized and stored at −20 °C.

Preparation of LS coated PLGA-NPs (LS-PLGA NPs) encapsulating GEM and PTX

The GEM-loaded PLGANPs were coated with LS containing PTX (GEM+PTX NPs) using a thin-film hydration technique. 5 mg LS, cholesterol (lipid: cholesterol molar ratio = 15:1) and 0.2 mg PTX were dissolved in 2 mL chloroform and dried to a thin film using a rotary evaporator (R-100, Buchi, USA) under reduced pressure with water bath temperature maintained at 50 °C. After ensuring complete removal of chloroform, the lipid film formed was hydrated for 1 h at 50 °C with 4 ml of 0.5 mg/ml GEM-loaded PLGA-NP aqueous dispersion. The mixture was then probe sonicated for 6 min at an amplitude of 50%. The prepared GEM+PTX NPs were then purified by dialysis wherein the NP suspension was transferred into a preequilibrated dialysis bag (12–14 kDa molecular weight cutoff) and dialyzed against 1 L of Milli-Q water for 24 h, replaced with fresh Milli-Q water every 3–4 h. The purified NPs were then lyophilized and stored at −20 °C until future use.

For preparing GEM NPs, GEM-loaded PLGA-NPs prepared as described in 2.2 was coated with LS without the PTX using the thin-film hydration technique. To develop the PTX NPs, blank PLGA-NPs were coated with LS containing PTX using the thin-film hydration technique.

Characterization of size and morphology of the NPs

Size distribution, polydispersity index (PDI) and zeta potential (ZP) of the NPs were determined by dynamic light scattering (DLS) (NanoZS, Malvern, USA). The shape and morphology of the NPs were analyzed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Briefly, a drop of the NP suspension was deposited onto the carbon-coated copper grid. Excess liquid was drawn off using a filter paper and the grids were air dried before adding a negative stain (UranyLess 22409). The grids were air dried once more before visualization under TEM. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT-IR) spectra of the NPs were generated to detect and analyze any possible chemical interactions between the drugs, polymer and lipids (Jasco FT-IR Spectrophotometer, MD, USA). The spectra were recorded from 400 to 4000 cm−1 and the interpretation of the different vibrational and functional peaks were performed with the help of Ominc™ FT-IR Software.

Confirmation and quantification of the LS coating

The LS coating of the PLGA-NPs was visualized using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM 710, Carl Zeiss Meditec AG, NY, USA). Since nanoparticles cannot be distinctly visualized using confocal microscopy, we synthesized larger LS-coated PLGA microparticles encapsulated with coumarin-6 in the same manner as described in 2.2 but with a slight modification. Briefly, 1 mg of coumarin-6 and 25 mg of PLGA were dissolved in 2.5 mL of ethyl acetate and added dropwise into 0.5% PVA solution under constant stirring. The resultant suspension was then emulsified by probe sonication for 15 secs and then allowed to stir overnight, purified and then lyophilized in the same manner as described previously. The coumarin-6 loaded microparticles were then coated with the LS containing Nile red dye using the thin film hydration as described above in 2.3. In brief, 5 mg LS, cholesterol (lipid: cholesterol molar ratio = 15:1) and 1 mg Nile red were dissolved in 2 mL chloroform and a thin film was formed which was then hydrated using coumarin-6-loaded PLGA microparticles. A small drop of the resultant suspension was then mounted onto a glass bottom dish and visualized.

Additionally, the LS coating over the PLGA-NPs were quantified using Stewart’s phospholipid colorimetric assay that forms a complex between the phospholipid and ammonium ferrothiocyanate. Briefly, lyophilized LS-coated PLGA-NPs (hereafter referred to as LS-PLGA NPs) were dissolved in 2 mL of chloroform and then 2 mL of freshly prepared ammonium ferrothiocyanate was added. The resultant mixture was vortexed vigorously for 1 min and then allowed to stand for at least 15–20 min to facilitate phase separation. The resultant lower chloroform layer containing the extracted phospholipid was then measured at 470 nm using Genesys™ 50 UV-Vis spectrophotometer (ThermoFisher Scientific, MA, USA) and quantified from the standard curve prepared using LS dissolved in chloroform.

NP stability study

To evaluate the in vitro stability of NPs, both PLGA and LS-PLGA NPs were suspended separately in 100% of PBS pH 7.4, FBS and simulated lung fluid (Gamble’s solution) prepared as described by Marques et al. The samples were incubated at 37 °C for 7 days and the size, PDI and ZP were measured every 24 h.

Quantification of GEM and PTX encapsulation in the NPs

The drug content in NPs was determined by high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) (Schimadzu Corporation, MD, USA). The mobile phase was a mixture of acetonitrile and water (50:50) set at a flow rate of 1.0 mL/min. Separation was carried out using an EC 125/4.6 Nucleosil 100–5 C 18 column maintained at a temperature of 30 °C. PTX and GEM was simultaneously detected at a wavelength of 227 nm. The injection volume was set at 20 μL. For encapsulation efficiency, weighed amount of NPs was dispersed in acetonitrile and vigorously vortexed ensuring complete rupture of the NPs followed by addition of equal amount of PBS. The resultant was then filtered and injected into the HPLC for analysis.

In vitro drug release studies

Briefly, 1 mg of GEM+PTX NPs (containing approx. 15.4 μg GEM and 17 μg PTX) were dispersed in 1 mL of PBS and transferred to a dialysis bag (molecular weight cut-off (MWCO) of 12–14 kDa), immersed in 5 mL of PBS in screw capped tubes and placed on an orbital shaker which was set at 60 rpm and maintained at 37 ± 0.5 °C for 21 days. At predetermined time intervals, 0.5 mL of dialysate was collected and replaced with same volume of pre-warmed PBS to maintain sink conditions. PTX and GEM released from the NPs were then analyzed using HPLC as previously described.

Cell culture

A549 lung adenocarcinoma cells were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% v/v FBS and 1% v/v pen-strep. NR8383 alveolar macrophages cells were cultured in Ham’s F-12 medium supplemented with 15% v/v FBS and 1% v/v pen-strep. The cells were cultured at 37 °C in a humidified incubator supplied with 5% CO2.

In vitro cytocompatibility studies

NP cytocompatibility against A549 and NR8383 cells were evaluated using colorimetric MTT assays (Vybrant MTT Cell Proliferation Assay Kit, ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at 10 × 103 cells/well and incubated for 24 h to facilitate cell attachment. The cells were then treated with varying concentrations (0, 250, 500 and 1000 μg/mL) of PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs suspended in media and incubated for 24 h at 37 °C. Cells not treated with NPs served as a control. After 24 h, the cells were washed gently with PBS, following which MTT assays were carried out per manufacturer’s instructions. Absorbance readings were read at 540 nm using Synergy H1 microplate reader (BioTek, Winooski, VT, USA) and cell viability was calculated as percent survival of treated cells relative to the untreated cells (control group).

In vitro cell uptake studies

Coumarin-6 loaded NPs were used to analyze cellular uptake of the NPs. The NPs were prepared in the same manner as described previously in 2.2 and 2.3 with the exception of replacing GEM with 1 mg of coumarin-6 in the PLGA core. A549 and NR8383 cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at 10 × 103 cells/well. After 24 h incubation, these cells were starved for 2 h followed by treatment with varying concentrations of NP formulations (0, 250, 500 and 1000 μg/mL). Cells that were not treated with NPs were used as a control. After 2 h the cells were lysed with Triton X-100 and the uptake of NPs was quantified by measuring fluorescence intensity and normalizing the particle concentration with the total cell protein content in each well, determined by Pierce BCA protein assay. Uptake was also visualized using an EVOS® FL Auto Imaging System (ThermoFisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). For EVOS imaging, the cells were seeded in a 48-well plate at 20 × 103 cells/well and incubated for 24 h. The cells were starved and treated in the same manner as described above. After 2 h of treatment, the cells were washed with PBS and then fixed using 4% formaldehyde and the nuclei stained with DAPI prior to imaging.

In vitro therapeutic efficacy studies

Anti-proliferative activity of the drug loaded NPs were evaluated using MTT assays. Briefly, A549 cells were seeded in a 96-well plate at 10 × 103 cells/well. After 24 h incubation, the cells were treated with varying NP concentrations (0, 125, 250, 500 μg/mL) of GEM and PTX containing LS-PLGA NPs (GEM+PTX NPs), GEM-containing LS-PLGA NPs (GEM NPs), PTX-containing LS-PLGA NPs (PTX NPs), free GEM, free PTX and free GEM and PTX combination (GEM+PTX) for 2 h while the control group consisted of cells in media with no treatment. After 2 h, the treated media was discarded and the cells were washed with PBS, followed by incubation for 24 h in drug-free media. GEM concentrations of 1.9, 3.9 and 7.7 μg/mL and PTX concentrations of 2.1, 4.3 and 8.5 μg/mL were used for the study, which corresponded to the approximate amounts of drugs released by the NPs within 24 hours following uptake. After the incubation period, cell viability was determined by MTT assays as described previously.

Clonogenic survival assays

Briefly, A549 cells were seeded in a 6-well plate at a seeding density of 100 cells/well. After 24 h incubation, the cells were treated with the same treatment groups as the in vitro therapeutic efficacy studies described above at a NP concentration of 500 μg/mL for 2 h. Then the treated media was discarded, and the cells were washed twice gently with PBS and replaced with fresh media containing 20% FBS. The cells were then incubated at 37 °C for 10 days to allow colony formation. The plates were then washed with PBS, fixed and stained with 0.1% crystal violet for 30 min, gently rinsed with water to remove excess stain and then air dried. Cell cycle analysis

The NPs were also evaluated for their ability to induce apoptosis in A549 cells using the FITC Annexin V/ Dead Cell Apoptosis Kit. Briefly, A549 cells (1 x 106 cells/mL) were treated with 500 μg/mL GEM+PTX NPs having drug concentrations of GEM (approx. 7.7 μg/mL) and PTX (approx. 8.5 μg/mL) respectively and blank LS-PLGA NPs for 2 h, after which the treated media was discarded. The cells were washed gently with PBS then replaced with fresh media and allowed to incubate for 24, 48 and 72 h. After the specific time intervals, the cells were harvested and incubated at room temperature in 1X annexin-binding buffer containing 5 μL of FITC annexin V and 1 μL of Propidium iodide (PI) working solution (100 μg/mL) for 15 min. After 15 min incubation, 400 μL of 1X Annexin binding buffer was added and the stained cells were then analyzed using a BD FACSVerse™ flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, NJ, USA).

In vivo studies

The in vivo studies were carried out following approval from The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Brown University in accordance with federal guidelines. 8–12 weeks old WT C57BL/6 mice (Jackson Laboratories) were intranasally administered a single dose of either LS-PLGA or uncoated PLGA-NPs containing Nile red dye (80 μL of 3 mg/mL NP suspension in PBS). The control group received PBS by intranasal instillation. At 24 and 72 h post NP instillation, the mice were anesthetized by urethane (40–60 mg/kg) by single dose of intraperitoneal injection, and the lungs were inflated and harvested. The fixed and paraffin-embedded tissue sections were examined by confocal microscopy at excitation/ emission wavelengths of 552/ 636 nm for Nile red. The fluorescence intensity in the images obtained was quantified using ImageJ.

Statistical Analysis

All experiments were performed independently in quadruplicates and the results were expressed as the mean ± deviation. Statistical analysis was performed with the help of GraphPad Prism software. Statistical significance was indicated as *p<0.05, **p < 0.01, ***p < 0.001, ****p < 0.0001.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Preparation and characterization of NPs

The PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs were successfully synthesized and characterized for their size and morphology. LS-PLGA NPs were formed by self-assembly in which the lipid bilayer arranged itself on the surface of the polymeric NPs by a thin film hydration – solvent evaporation method. DLS measurements of blank PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs exhibited average diameters of 185.5 ± 5 nm and 188.0 ± 7 nm respectively in terms of size intensity (Figure 1A). The size of the particles contributes significantly to the distribution and deposition in the lungs. Levofloxacin-loaded PLGA-NPs (146 nm) delivered by dry powder insufflation in mice were found to be deposited deep in the alveolar region with a significantly higher area under the curve/minimum inhibitory concentration (375) as compared to free levofloxacin (60.4) showing an enhanced antibacterial activity in the treatment of tuberculosis. Our previous studies also demonstrated that nebulized PLGA-NPs encapsulated with erythropoietin receptor cDNA tagged with green fluorescence protein (196 nm) were distributed within the rat lobes with the fluorescence remaining over baseline for over 21 days. The PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs exhibited narrow particle size distributions with PDI values of 0.14 ± 0.05 and 0.18 ± 0.08 respectively, and negative ZP values of −35 ± 0.64 and −32.3 ± 0.97 respectively (Figure 1B, C). The negative ZP value of PLGA-NPs is due to the terminal carboxylic acid groups present in the PLGA polymer. No significant variations in NP diameters, PDI and ZP was observed pre- and post- lyophilization (Table S1). DPPC is a zwitterionic lipid, which may have accounted for the minor difference in ZP values between both NPs. The morphology of all NP formulations was visualized by TEM. As shown in Figure 1D and E, the NPs were spherical in morphology and relatively uniformly dispersed. A faint core-shell structure was observed in the TEM images of the LS-PLGA NPs. The comparatively smaller diameters of particles in the TEM images compared to the DLS images can be attributed to the fact that DLS measures hydrodynamic diameters of NPs suspended in a solution while TEM images are of air-dried NPs.

Confirmation and quantification of the LS layer on the surface of PLGA-NPs

To further confirm that our techniques led to the formation of an LS layer and to visualize the core-shell structure following LS coating, fluorescent microparticles were prepared wherein the LS shell was loaded with Nile red dye and the PLGA core was loaded with coumarin-6 dye. We synthesized microparticles instead of NPs in this study for easy visualization of the core-shell structure without the use of electron microscopes. Figure 1F shows the confocal laser scanning fluorescence image of the microparticles. A distinct core-shell structure involving a purple LS shell (Nile red), and a green PLGA core (coumarin-6) was observed. This confirms the successful coating of LS on the PLGA core. To further analyze the coated NPs, we performed FT-IR of LS-PLGA NPs, LS and PLGA polymer. FT-IR spectra of lyophilized LS-PLGA NPs showed the presence of characteristics peaks of free PLGA (1760 cm−1) due to the stretching of the carbonyl groups present in lactide and glycolide while the peaks between 1273 and 1132 cm−1 are due to the asymmetric and symmetric stretching of C-C(=O)-O. LS-PLGA NPs also displayed the characteristic peaks of lyophilized LS (3400 cm−1 −OH, 2919 cm−1 −CH2 antisymmetric stretching, 2850 cm−1 −CH2 symmetric stretching, 1737 cm−1 −C=O stretching and 1223 cm−1 −PO2 antisymmetric stretching double bonds) (Figure 1G). The chemical composition of Infasurf® is similar to native LS (~84% phospholipids) with the exception of surfactant protein-A (SP-A). Therefore, a Stewart’s assay was performed to quantify the amount of phospholipids from the LS that is coated on the PLGA-NPs. About 0.665 mg of lipids/mg of LS-PLGA NPs were present and the coating efficiency was determined to be 92.3 ± 0.05%.

NP drug content and in vitro release kinetics

The drug encapsulation efficiency and release kinetics from the LS-coated NPs was determined next. The drug content was determined using a modified HPLC method (Figure S1) to simultaneously detect both GEM and PTX. The encapsulation efficiency of GEM and PTX in the NP system were found to be 39 ± 1.15% and 68 ± 3.01% respectively. The release kinetics of GEM and PTX from the NP system was evaluated for 21 days in PBS at 37 °C. A bi-phasic release of GEM was observed wherein an initial burst release of 38 ± 7.11% of the encapsulated GEM from the NPs was observed at 37°C within the first 3h followed by a sustained release of about 70 ± 1.61% over a period of 21 days (Figure 2A). Similar bi-phasic release profile of GEM from PLGA-NPs were reported by Rammohan et al wherein 10% and 29% of GEM was released after 4 h and 24 h respectively followed by a sustained release over time with a cumulative release of 67% after 7 days. PTX displayed a rapid release profile with a cumulative release of approximately 63 ± 0.98% within 24 h. After 72 h, a decline in the release was observed, which persisted over 21 days. Our findings seem to indicate base-catalyzed hydrolysis and epimerization of PTX in aqueous media, as observed by Mohammad Sadegh et al. when they carried out a stability study of PTX in PBS for 29 days. Hydrolysis products (10%) and 7-epi-taxol (epimerization product of taxol) (12–15%) were identified within initial hours of the study and by the end of the study the concentration of hydrolysis products increased to ~ 90%. Therefore, to determine the release profile of encapsulated drugs from the LS layer, rhodamine-B dye was incorporated as a model drug in the LS shell. The rhodamine-B loaded NPs were prepared in the same manner as described in 2.3 except PTX was replaced with rhodamine-B dye (1 mg). The EE% of rhodamine-B in the lipid layer were determined to be 96.67 ± 1.36%. The initial release pattern of rhodamine-B (Figure 2B) was similar to PTX. A cumulative release of approximately 63% of the encapsulated dye occurred within 24 h. But, unlike PTX, rhodamine-B showed a cumulative release of 98% within 7 days from the LS shell.

In vitro stability study

The stability of NPs was investigated by taking DLS measurements of NPs dispersed in PBS, FBS and Gamble’s solution at 37 °C, every 24 h for 7 days. The NPs remained fairly stable in all the test media for over 7 days, which is consistent with the previous findings of our group and others with no significant aggregation. The diameters of all NP samples at all timepoints demonstrated a variation of less than 10–15% of their initial diameters, indicating that they were stable in all tested solutions. The PDI value of the NPs remained fairly constant in both the PBS and Gamble’s solution throughout the study interval. The average PDI of PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs in PBS were found to be 0.10 ± 0.03 and 0.27 ± 0.02 respectively while in Gamble’s solution they were found to be 0.32 ± 0.03 and 0.24 ± 0.03 respectively. Although higher PDI values were observed in the NPs dispersed in FBS due to background readings from albumin and small proteins in the latter, these PDI values remained consistent throughout the study (0.70 ± 0.02 and 0.70 ± 0.13 for PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs respectively) (Figure 2C–E). The zeta potential values of both PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs also showed minimal variations over 7 days in all three solutions (Figure 2F, G). Thus, the NPs were relatively stable in vitro which agrees with previous studies where siRNA-encapsulated PLGA-NPs (~200 nm) were found to be stable for up to one week at 37 °C in PBS and in 10% serum in PBS. Our results also concur with those observed by Wang and Yu, which showed fairly good stability of antigen loaded lipid-PLGA hybrid NPs (~200 nm) in PBS for 8 days at room temperature.

In vitro cell studies

A549 lung adenocarcinoma and NR8383 alveolar macrophage cell viability was investigated as a function of NP concentration (100–1000 μg/mL) to determine NP cytocompatibility using MTT colorimetric assays. Cells treated with different concentrations of blank PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs displayed close to 100% cell viability, even at the highest concentration (1000 μg/mL) tested (Figure 3A). The results from this study are consistent with our previously reported results using PLGA and PLGA-based NPs, which also demonstrated excellent cytocompatibility at high concentrations.

We next studied the in vitro cellular uptake of the NPs by A549 and NR8383 cell lines following 2 h treatment. The incubation time of 2 h was chosen based on previous studies by our group and others where 2 h was found to be optimal for observing dose-dependent uptake kinetics of PLGA- and lipid-based nanoparticles on lung and other cell types. A 24 h uptake study on NR8383 cells was also carried out by us, which led to completely saturation of NP uptake at almost all concentrations, making it difficult to determine the dose-dependent uptake trend at that timepoint (Figure S2). Uptake of LS-PLGA NPs by NR8383 cells at the 24 h timepoint was observed to be significantly lower than uptake of PLGA-NPs by these cells. DPPC, a major component of LS have been shown to significantly decrease phagocytosis of inhaled particles, as described above. NR8383 cells incubated with DPPC-coated PLGA microspheres or media containing DPPC have previously demonstrated reduced phagocytic activity. Dose-dependent uptake of both PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs was observed by both A549 and NR8383 cells during the cellular uptake studies. However as shown in Figure 3B, NR8383 macrophage uptake of LS-PLGA NPs was significantly less compared to uptake of PLGA-NPs at all concentrations (p-value = 0.015). The LS coating did not lead to a similar drastic decrease in uptake by A549 cells when compared to uncoated PLGA-NPs (Figure 3C). Comparison of uptake of LS-PLGA NPs by both cell lines showed significantly less uptake by NR8383 cells compared to the A549 cells at all concentrations (p-value = 0.010) (Figure 3D). These findings were also confirmed by EVOS imaging of cellular uptake (Figures 3E, F). The fluorescence from the coumarin-6 dye-loaded PLGA-NPs were observed in both A549 and NR8383 cells. However, upon treatment with coumarin-6 dye-loaded LS-PLGA NPs, minimal fluorescence was observed in the NR8383 cells while significantly higher fluorescence was seen in the A549 cells. Previous studies on DPPC-coated microspheres have also similarly demonstrated a decrease in phagocytosis by NR8383 cells compared to uncoated microspheres.

In vitro anti-cancer evaluation of the drug loaded NP system

The anti-cancer effects of drug-loaded NPs consisting of GEM in the PLGA core and PTX in the lipid layer was evaluated next. A549 cells were treated with free GEM, free PTX, GEM+PTX, GEM NPs, PTX NPs and GEM+PTX NPs for 2 h after which they were washed and incubated in fresh media for 24 h to determine the effects of NP-mediated drug release following uptake, on cell viability. The NP concentrations used for all the in vitro studies were determined according to the IC-50 values of the drugs for A549 cell lines, cell uptake studies and amount of drug released within the first 24 h. The IC50 for GEM and PTX against A549 cells for 24 h were determined by us to be 3.34 ± 3.98 μg/mL and 6.35 ± 1.41 μg/mL respectively. Free PTX was found to cause more cancer cell kill than GEM upon 2 h treatment (Figure 4A), which is consistent with previous reports. During the initial low concentrations (125 and 250 μg/ml) for the free GEM+PTX group, there appears to be minimal contribution from the GEM towards cancer cell kill. This can be attributed to the limited therapeutic efficacy of free GEM as observed by us at the low concentrations. There are also reports of antagonistic or less than additive effects of GEM and PTX in leukemia, A549 cells, MCF-7 breast cancer cells and pancreas adenocarcinoma P-SW cells, which could have also contributed to less cell kill by free GEM+PTX. In the future, we will encapsulate and study other anti-cancer drug combinations within the LS-PLGA NPs. GEM NPs demonstrated marginally greater effects than GEM alone, which may be due to the sustained release profile of GEM from the NPs and differences in the uptake routes and fate for both treatments within the cells. GEM is a hydrophilic drug and dependent on human equilibrative nucleoside transporters to uptake while GEM NPs are primarily trafficked into the cells by endocytosis. Cells treated with free PTX and PTX NPs displayed inhibitory effects similar to other findings although no significant difference was observed between them. This may be due to the fast or “burst” release of PTX from the LS coating as observed in the drug release study. There was a dose-dependent increase in cell death following treatment with GEM+PTX NPs. The A549 cell viability was 52 ± 4.6% when treated with 125 μg/mL of GEM+PTX NPs, which decreased further to 32 ± 12.6% when treated with 500 μg/mL of GEM+PTX NPs. When encapsulated within LS-PLGA NPs, PTX is released first from the LS shell followed by sustained release of GEM from the PLGA core. According to previous reports, scheduling plays an important role in the therapeutic effects of GEM and PTX. When PTX administration preceded GEM both in clinical studies and in vitro, which is similar to their release profiles observed from our NPs, synergistic effects were observed. The sequential release from the NPs therefore may have attributed to the significant anti-cancer effects observed by us for the GEM+PTX NPs.

A colony-forming assay was carried out next, wherein A549 cells were treated with the same treatment groups as above to evaluate their ability to inhibit colony formation by cancer cells. As seen in Figure 4B, C, the cells treated with GEM+PTX NPs formed the least number of colonies, and these colonies had the smallest diameters compared to cells treated with the other treatment groups. The plating efficiency was estimated to be 57 ± 0.04%. A NP dose-dependent effect was seen on the SF; cells treated with GEM+PTX NPs demonstrated the lowest SF of 0.31 ± 0.11 and 0.23 ± 0.01 when treated with the NP concentrations of 125 μg/mL and 500 μg/mL respectively. Cells treated with drug-loaded NPs in general demonstrated lower SF compared to those treated with free drugs. Upon treatment with GEM NPs, a SF of 0.57 ± 0.15 was observed while free GEM gave a SF = 0.74 ± 0.05. Cells treated with PTX NPs exhibited a SF of 0.45 ± 0.07. This confirms that the drug-loaded NPs exhibited better chemotherapeutic effects compared to free drugs due to the internalization and subsequent release of the drugs from the NPs within the cells.

Cell cycle analysis

The effects of GEM+PTX NPs on the cell cycle of A549 cells was also investigated using Annexin V/ PI assay via flow cytometry. In this study, the main objective was to evaluate the effectiveness of GEM+PTX NPs to induce apoptosis/ necrosis as compared to free GEM and PTX combination. Figure 5A shows the percentages of live (LL), early apoptosis (LR), late apoptosis (UR) and necrotic cells (UL) for 24, 48 and 72 h. GEM, a deoxycytidine analogue, binds to DNA and blocks replication while PTX induces tubulin polymerization and inhibits cell division by binding to the microtubules located at the alpha beta-tubulin dimer. Previous studies have reported that GEM and PTX displays cytotoxic effects when given as a combination.

As seen in (Figure 5A–D), both GEM+PTX NPs and free GEM+PTX combination exhibited apoptosis (LR and UR) and necrosis (UL) of A549. The apoptosis of free GEM+PTX combination was 9.54%, 15.17% and 7.04% for 24, 48 and 72 h respectively, while that of GEM+PTX NPs was 8.29%, 17.03% and 10.61% respectively. However, in comparison to free GEM+PTX combination, GEM+PTX NPs demonstrated a significant enhancement in the necrosis of cells from 1.77% (24 h) to 14.45% (72 h). The increased necrosis (7.16% increase) displayed by GEM+PTX NPs may be attributed to the effects of sustained drug release over time following internalization of the NPs by A549 cells.

In vivo studies of NPs

After evaluating the developed NPs for its ability to evade macrophages and therapeutic efficacy in vitro, an in vivo study to assess the deep lung deposition and retention of the NPs was done. In this study, the retention of coated (LS-PLGA NPs) and uncoated (PLGA-NPs) NPs following intranasal instillation was determined by fluorescence imaging of the mouse lung sections at 24 h and 72 h post treatment. As seen in Figure 6A, widespread fluorescence was observed in the mouse lung following a single dose of PLGA-NPs and LS-PLGA NPs instillation, 24 h following administration. This concurs with our previous research where fluorescence was observed in the lung tissue following administration of PLGA-NPs containing rhodamine-tagged erythropoietin; this fluorescence began to decrease over time as the PLGA-NPs were cleared from the lungs. Similarly in this research, we noted a decrease in fluorescence in the lung slices of mice given uncoated PLGA-NPs, at the 72 h timepoint. Comparatively greater fluorescence was observed in the lungs of mice given LS-PLGA NPs. This was confirmed with normalized fluorescence intensity measurements using ImageJ, where the lung sections of mice given LS-PLGA NPs had significantly greater fluorescence intensities than the lung sections from mice given uncoated PLGA-NPs at both the 24 h and 72 h timepoints, indicating greater retention. Similar findings were reported by Zerong Li et al, where in DPPC-coated lipid NP containing resveratrol were able to evade macrophage phagocytosis and the released drug was detected even after 12 h of intratracheal administration. Our future studies will focus on characterizing the physicochemical properties of the LS-PLGA NPs following nebulization. We will also investigate the therapeutic effects, cell specificity as well as safety and toxicity profiles of the dual-drug containing LS-PLGA NPs in vivo.

CONCLUSIONS

We report the successful development and characterization of LS-PLGA NPs (188 ± 7 nm) for pulmonary drug delivery. Confocal microscopy and FT-IR confirmed the successful coating of the polymeric NPs by the FDA approved product Infasurf®. The drug combination GEM and PTX were successfully loaded within the system in two separate layers, GEM within the polymeric core while PTX within the LS coating. The in vitro drug release displayed varying drug release profiles for the drugs loaded in the shell and the core. Significantly lower macrophage uptake of LS-PLGA NPs than uncoated PLGA-NPs was observed in vitro indicating that the NP formulation can potentially evade macrophage phagocytosis and be retained in the deep lung tissue for treatment for a longer period of time. In vitro studies also exhibited higher cytoxicity and anti-proliferative effects of the GEM and PTX loaded NPs against tumor cells compared to single drug loaded NPs and free drugs. Additionally, the in vivo evaluation showed longer pulmonary retention of LS-PLGA NPs as compared to the PLGA-NPs.
