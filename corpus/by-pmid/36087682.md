# DNMT and EZH2 inhibitors synergize to activate therapeutic targets in hepatocellular carcinoma.

## Abstract

The development of more effective targeted therapies for hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) patients due to its aggressiveness is urgently needed. DNA methyltransferase inhibitors (DNMTis) represented the first clinical breakthrough to target aberrant cancer epigenomes. However, their clinical efficacies are still limited, in part due to an "epigenetic switch" in which a large group of genes that are demethylated by DNMTi treatment remain silenced by polycomb repressive complex 2 (PRC2) occupancy. EZH2 is the member of PRC2 that catalyzes the placement of H3K27me3 marks. EZH2 overexpression is correlated with poor HCC patient survival. We tested the combination of a DNMTi (5-aza-2'-deoxycytidine, DAC) and the EZH2 inhibitor (EZH2i) GSK126 in human HCC cell lines on drug sensitivity, DNA methylation, nucleosome accessibility, and gene expression profiles. Compared with single agent treatments, all HCC cell lines studied showed increased sensitivity after receiving both drugs concomitant with prolonged anti-proliferative changes and sustained reactivation of nascently-silenced genes. The increased number of up-regulated genes after combination treatment correlated with prolonged anti-proliferation effects and increased nucleosome accessibility. Combination treatments also activate demethylated promoters that are repressed by PRC2 occupancy. Furthermore, 13-31% of genes down-regulated by DNA methylation in primary HCC tumors were reactivated through this combination treatment scheme in vitro. Finally, the combination treatment also exacerbates anti-tumor immune responses, while most of these genes were downregulated in over 50% of primary HCC tumors. We have linked the anti-tumor effects of DAC and GSK126 combination treatments to detailed epigenetic alterations in HCC cells, identified potential therapeutic targets and provided a rationale for treatment efficacy for HCC patients.

Introduction

Although most other forms of cancer are on the decline, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) incidence rates continue to increase dramatically. As a result, HCC is one of most common cancer-related deaths worldwide. HCC is an aggressive cancer with poor survival statistics and a growing incidence rate related to hepatitis virus infections and other liver diseases in the population. Patients with advanced HCC have very limited options for treatment. Advanced HCC patients only receive a marginal 2–3 month survival benefit when given approved tyrosine kinase inhibitor therapies such as Sorafenib, or regorafenib as a second line regimen. In addition, nivolumab, an anti-PD-L1 antibody, is now approved for HCC patients who have previously received sorafenib. The median overall survival (OS) of HCC patients is still limited to 10 months, hence, there is an urgent need for developing new and more effective therapies for these patients.

DNA methyltransferase inhibitors (DNMTis) are the most used epigenetic therapies to target cancer-associated DNA hypermethylation to reactivate genes and ultimately reverse malignant phenotypes. We and others have recently demonstrated that the clinical efficacy of treating multiple types of cancer with DNMTis stems from their abilities to downregulate oncogene expression due to gene body DNA demethylation and activate transposable elements (TEs) including endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) and LINE-1 elements to stimulate an immune response via viral mimicry mechanisms. Indeed, epigenetic alterations, most notably DNA methylation changes, are hallmarks of HCC and are integral to disease initiation and progression. Since epigenetic alterations vary greatly between cancer types, the anti-tumor effects of epigenetic therapies are most likely specific to individual tissue and/or cancer types. The FDA-approved first-generation DNMTis including azacytidine (5-Aza-CR, AZA), decitabine (5-Aza-CdR, DAC), the second generation DNMTi Guadecitabine (SGI-110), and the most recent selective DNMT1 inhibitor, GSK3685032, represented breakthroughs in the development of epigenetic therapy. DNMTis have become part of the standard treatment for myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), chronic myelomonocytic leukemia (CMML) and acute myeloid leukemia (AML) patients. Furthermore, DNMTis have been introduced in clinical trials for patients with solid tumors including HCC, not only as sole drugs, but also in combinatorial approaches.

Although DNMTis induce significant DNA demethylation in the genome, restoration of gene expression is still limited and may explain their limited anti-tumor effects in clinical trials of cancer patients. We recently demonstrated that most functional genomic regions, such as gene promoters, do not acquire accessible (open) chromatin after DNA demethylation upon DNMTi treatment, suggesting that most DNA demethylation is passive and does not correlate with gene expression changes. This is because a large portion of demethylated promoters undergo an epigenetic switch to a bivalent status marked by both repressive H3K27me3 and active H3K4me3 histone modifications after DNMTi treatment. Furthermore, a report from Jadhav and colleagues demonstrated that PRC2 specifically represses genes with bivalent H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 occupancy but not genes marked with H3K27me3 alone. As a result, the PRC2 complex may be an attractive drug target to combine with DNMTi treatment for HCC patients, compounded by the discovery that the core PRC2 complex members EZH2, EED and SUZ12 are overexpressed in primary HCC tumors based on gene expression data from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) data set. EZH2 is a key component of the PRC2 complex that catalyzes the placement of the repressive H3K27me3 histone modification, therefore, the combination of an EZH2 inhibitor (EZH2i) with a DNMTi may result in synergistic anti-tumor effects by reactivating a larger number of therapeutic genes. Indeed, EZH2 inhibitors such as Tazemetostat (EPZ6438) and GSK126 are currently under evaluation in clinical trials.

Synergetic anti-tumor effects based on the combination treatment of DNA methylation and EZH2 inhibition have been reported including upregulation of immune response genes, however, the mechanism of action is not yet fully understood. In this study, we tested our hypothesis that the combination of DNA methylation and EZH2 inhibition exacerbates gene expression and anti-tumor modulation in HCC, most notably activating demethylated genes that remained silenced by PRC2 complex after treatment with DNMTi alone. We performed an integrative analysis of genome-wide DNA methylation, nucleosome accessibility, and gene expression in human HCC cell lines to identify the potential mechanism for gene reactivation and to identify therapeutic targets that contribute to drug efficacy of using DNMTi (DAC) and EZH2i (GSK126) in combination. Our findings provide a mechanistic rationale for the synergistic anti-tumor effects by not only reactivating genes with demethylated gene promoters after inhibiting DNA methylation alone, but also upregulating tumor suppressor genes, immune response genes, and viral mimicry activity. This combinatorial drug treatment approach has potential clinical efficacy for HCC patients.

Methods

Cell culture and drug treatments

SNU398, HepG2 and SNU475 cells were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC; www.atcc.org). Each cell line was treated with daily doses of 100 nM DAC (Sigma-Aldrich) and/or 500 nM GSK-126 (Active Biochem, LTD, Hong Kong) for a total of three days. Treated cells continued to grow after the drug(s) were removed. SNU398 and HepG2 cells were harvested on day 14 after treatment, while SNU475 cells were harvested on day 24. Cell survival rates were assessed on specific days using a Coulter counter analyzer (Beckman Coulter, Inc.). Results are normalized to control group (cells treated with phosphate buffered saline (DPBS)). The data are presented as means of triplicate measurements; error bars represent standard errors of the mean (SEMs). The cell doubling times were calculated as described below: Doubling time = A*log(2)/(log(B) − log(C)), in which A is the number of days from cell seeded to harvested, B is the total number of harvested cells, and C is the number of seeded cells.

Colony formation assay

Each cell line was seeded at 2,000 cells per well in 6-well plates and treated with drug(s) as described above. The cell culture media was replaced regularly after each drug treatment. At day 10, cells were stained and fixed with 0.5% crystal violet in 40% methanol and then counted. The colony formation results are scanned images of six-well plates with the indicated treatment scheme. We used ImageJ (https://imagej.nih.gov/ij/) to measure mean colony intensities.

Western blot analysis

HepG2, SNU398 and SNU475 cells (3 × 105 were untreated or treated with 100 nM DAC, and or 500 nM GSK126 for 72 h. Parallel cultures of untreated cells in which DPBS is added served as controls. Cells were then trypsinized, washed with PBS and resuspend in M-PER buffer (Thermo Scientific) with 4% SDS and protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche). The cells were then sonicated on ice and cellular debris was removed by centrifugation. Protein (30 μg) was mixed with SDS/β-mercaptoethanol loading buffer and resolved on a 4–15% gradient SDS/PAGE gel (BioRad). Antibodies against the H3K27me3 (#9733, Cell Signaling Technology) and H3 (SKU: 13–0001 EpiCypher) were used. Proteins were visualized using the ECL detection system (Thermo Scientific) and ChemiDoc™ system (BioRad).

RNA sequencing

SNU398 and HepG2 cells were harvested at day 14 and SNU475 sells were harvested at day 24 after GSK126, DAC, DAC + GSK126 treatments and DPBS as the control. Total RNA was extracted with Trizol reagent (Invitrogen), followed by clean-up withTurbo DNase I (Invitrogen) treatment and Zymo Direct-Zol RNA mini prep kit (Zymo Research) according to the manufacturer’s protocol. RNA quality was assessed using Agilent 2100 bioanalyzer with RNA Nano chips (Agilent Technologies, Inc.). For directional RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) with ribosomal RNA (rRNA) reduction, libraries were prepared using KAPA Stranded RNA-Seq Kit with RiboErase (HMR) (KapaBiosystems) and sequenced as single-end (SE) 75 base pair reads on an Illumina NextSeq 500 instrument at the Van Andel Research Institute Genomics Core (Grand Rapids, MI). Reads were mapped to the human transcriptome using TopHat version 2.1.0 with NCBI RefSeq as the reference annotation of transcripts. Transcripts were assembled and quantified using Cufflinks version 2.2.1.

AcceSssIble assay

Nuclei preparation and M.SssI methyltransferase (New England BioLabs) treatments were performed as previously described. Briefly, 5 × 105 cells were trypsinized, washed with cold PBS, and lysed in 1 ml of Lysis Buffer (10 mM Tris pH 7.4, 10 mM NaCl, 3 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM EDTA, 0.5% NP-40). Nuclei were collected and washed in wash buffer containing (10 mM Tris pH 7.4, 10 mM NaCl, 3 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mM EDTA), then separated into two microcentrifuge tubes (no enzyme control and M.SssI reaction, respectively). Nuclei were then resuspended in 150 μl reaction mix containing 76 μl ddH2O, 15 μl 10X NEBuffer 2, 45 μl 1 M sucrose, 1.5 μl 32 mM S-adenosylmethionine (SAM), and 12.5 μl 4 U/μl M.SssI (or ddH2O for no enzyme control). The reactions were incubated at 37 °C for 15 min. To stop the reactions, pre-warmed (37 °C) 300 μl Stop Solution (10 mM Tris-HCl pH 7.9, 600 mM NaCl, 1% SDS, 0.1 mM EDTA) and 6 μl Proteinase K (Roche) were added to each tube, and each reaction mixtures were incubated at 55 °C overnight. DNAs were then purified by phenol/chloroform extraction and ethanol precipitation and re-dissolved in 50 μl 1X TE pH 8.0 for subsequent analyses.

Illumina infinium human MethylationEPIC (EPIC) BeadChip

Infinium DNA methylationEPIC (EPIC) BeadArray data production was performed at the University of Southern California Molecular Genomics Core Facility. Briefly, DNA samples were bisulfite converted using the Zymo EZ DNA methylation kit as specified by the manufacturer. Bisulfite-converted DNA samples were evaluated for conversion completeness and the amount of converted DNA using TaqMan-based real-time PCR assays as described by Campan et al.. All samples passed the QC tests and then entered the EPIC array data production pipeline. After the chemistry steps, EPIC arrays were scanned, and the raw signal intensities were extracted from the *.IDAT files using the R package minfi. The intensities were corrected for background fluorescence and red-green dye-bias using the ‘noob’ functionality in minfi. Beta (β) values were calculated as (M/(M + U)), in which M and U refer to the (pre-processed) mean methylated and unmethylated probe signal intensities, respectively. The EPIC probes, interrogating 863,159 CpG sites, were filtered to remove probes with fluorescent intensity statistically insignificant above background signal (detection p-value >0.05), located within 15 base pairs of a single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP), mapped to multiple locations or are located on the sex chromosomes.

AcceSssIble assay data analysis

We have previously described the AcceSssIble assay data analysis scheme. Corrected β-values (range 0–1) of no enzyme control (NOE, endogenous DNA methylation values for each CpG locus) were subtracted from corrected β-values of M.SssI-treated samples to obtain DNA accessibility values (Acc) for each EPIC DNA methylation probe. β- and Acc-values for each sample after treatments were then subtracted from the respective values in the vehicle control samples, resulting in delta-methylation (ΔMeth) and delta-accessibility (ΔAcc) beta values. ΔMeth and ΔAcc values were used in subsequent analyses as values for changes in DNA methylation and accessibility, respectively, at each locus using a cutoff of Δβ-value±0.2 for changes in DNA methylation and ΔAcc-value±0.3 for changes in accessibility. Four groups with unique DNA methylation and chromatin accessibility changes were identified as Groups a, b, c and d (Figs. 4 and 5).

Differentially expressed gene (DEG) analysis

The R limma package was used to identify DEGs. Volcano plots were generated using the ggplot2 package in R computing language (http://ggplot2.org) to assess overall gene differential expression. The empirical Bayes moderated t-test method was used to calculate the p-values of each gene. The Benjamini and Hochberg method (14) was used to calculate the adjusted p-values [the false discovery rate (FDR)]. Genes with |log2fold change (FC)|>2 and FDR<0.05 were identified as DEGs. Upregulated and downregulated DEGs were compared using Venn diagrams.

Gene ontology (GO) pathway enrichment analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) pathway enrichment analysis was performed using the GSEA tools (java GSEA Desktop Application version 3.0; http://software.broadinstitute.org/gsea/downloads.jsp) in which the DAC, GSK and combination treatment data were compared to the untreated (DPBS) data. The GO pathways with Normalized Enrichment Score (NES) > 0.5 and FDR<0.05 were displayed as a bubble plot using R software tools.

Tumor suppressor and stemness analyses

DGEs were searched against the Tumor Suppressor Gene database (TSGene, https://bioinfo.uth.edu/TSGene/) and the StemChecker database (http://193.136.227.155/stemchecker/stemsethelp.jsf). The number of overlapped genes is represented by bar graphs.

Quantitative real-time RT-PCR

The expression of tumor suppressor and stem genes after treatment of DAC and/or GSK-126 was measured by real-time quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (qRT-PCR) using KAPA SYBR FAST universal 2 × qPCR master mix (KAPA) with primers listed in the Supplementary Table 1. TATA-Box Binding Protein (TBP) was used as reference genes.

TCGA data analysis

The DNA methylation and gene expression data from TCGA were downloaded from the TCGA dataportal. The percentage of leukocyte infiltration information was previously published.

Statistical analysis: Statistical tests were analyzed by using R software (R version 3.1.2, R Development Core Team, https://cran.r-project.org/) or Prism software version 8 as indicated in the figure legend.

Accession codes: All data have been compacted with accession code GSE202561 at the website of Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) (http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/) with a reviewer’s link: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/geo/query/acc.cgi?acc=GSE202561 and token ojgvuemazhyddij.

Results

Combination treatment of 5-Aza-CdR (DAC) and GSK126 shows prolonged effects on the inhibition of cell growth and colony formation in human liver cancer cell lines.

Aberrant DNA methylation and chromatin modifications are hallmarks of every cancer type and are among the most abundant epigenetic therapeutic targets. In addition to promoter DNA hypermethylation, the polycomb repressive complex 2 (PRC2) functions by placing histone H3 lysine 27 trimethylation (H3K27me3) marks for maintaining transcriptional repression. The key components of the PRC2 complex, EZH2, EED and SUZ12, are overexpressed in various cancers. We previously demonstrated that DNA methylation alterations and PRC2 component overexpression are efficacious therapeutic targets in HCC.

Using data from the TCGA HCC dataset (LIHC), high expression levels of EZH2, EDD and SUZ12 are significantly correlated with poor overall survival (OS) in HCC patients (Fig. 1A, B, and C). In contrast, expression of EZH1, a non-canonical PRC2 complex member, was not significantly correlated with HCC patient OS (Fig. 1D). Thus, we hypothesized that the combination treatment of DNMTi and EZH2i exacerbates gene expression activation by demethylating gene promoters and inhibiting H3K27me3 occupancy. The consequence of the combination treatment also could result in synergetic anti-tumor effect in HCC patients.

We used 5-Aza-CdR (DAC) as the DNMTi and GSK126 as an EZH2i on SNU398, HepG2 and SNU475 liver cancer cell lines that display unique DNA methylation profiles and represent the three major DNA methylation profiles in primary HCC tumors. The demethylation activity of DAC depends on cell doubling while GSK126 directly blocks EZH2 enzyme activity. Each cell line was treated with three consecutive 24-h doses (72 h total) of 100 nM DAC, or 500 nM GSK126, or 100 nM DAC + 500 nM GSK126 in combination such that the drug was active for at least one cell doubling cycle before drug removal. Treated cells were subsequently cultured until the population doubling times were no longer significantly different from untreated cells, usually 20–25 days after drug treatment (Fig. 2A and B). Depletion of DNMT1, DNMT3A and DNMT3B proteins at day 3 after DAC treatment was confirmed as previously described (data not shown), while depletion of H3K27me3 after GSK-126 treatment were also confirmed by Western blot analysis (Supplemental Figure S1). DAC treatment resulted in prolonged increases in cell inhibition and population doubling times for each cell line that were exacerbated after DAC + GSK126 combination treatment. Interestingly, GSK126 treatments alone only showed limited anti-tumor effects (Fig. 2A and B). SNU475 cells displayed a longer duration of cell inhibition than SNU398 and HepG2 cells(Fig. 2A and B), the growth inhibition after DAC treatment of SNU475 cells is longer in duration due to slower cell doubling to incorporate DAC into genomic DNA and subsequent remethylation to restore cell growth. Cell growth inhibition due to DAC, GSK126 or DAC + GSK126 treatments was confirmed by colony formation assays, with synergistic effects shown after DAC + GSK126 treatments in all three cell lines (Fig. 2C and D).

DAC + GSK126 combination treatments strongly up-regulate tumor suppressor and immune response genes while concurrently down-regulating cancer stem-like genes.

To understand whether epigenetic therapies result in gene expression changes in human HCC cell lines, we performed RNA-sequencing (RNA-seq) for control, DAC, GSK126, and DAC + GSK126 treatments in HepG2, SNU398, and SNU475 cells. Using a >2-fold expression change and FDR <0.05 thresholds, we discovered that DAC + GSK126 treatments up-regulated the greatest number of genes (894 in HepG2, 444 in SNU398, and 251 in SNU475) (Fig. 3A), while GSK126 treatment alone resulted in mostly down-regulated genes (413 in HepG2, 163 in SNU398 and 163 in SNU475) (Fig. 3B). The number of activated and repressed genes after DAC treatment alone was intermediate to GSK126 treatment alone and the DAC + GSK126 combination scheme. Interestingly, the genes upregulated by DAC + GSK126 treatments highly overlapped with those resulting from DAC treatment alone but showed limited correlation with the set of genes activated by GSK126 treatment alone (Fig. 3A). These data suggest that the DAC + GSK126 combination treatment alters the expression of genes that could not be achieved by either drug alone.

We next performed gene ontology (GO) analysis for all differentially expressed genes (DEGs) after GSK126, DAC, or DAC + GSK126 treatments in all three HCC cell lines using Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA). The genes up-regulated by DAC + GSK126 treatments in all three HCC cell lines are involved in multiple pathways including in cell apoptotic processes and G protein coupled receptor signaling pathways, however, the majority of activated pathways are involved in immune response processes (Fig. 3C). Most notably, each activated pathway has a direct link to the synergetic anti-tumor effects of the DAC + GSK126 treatment approach (Fig. 2).

GSK126 treatment alone resulted in the highest number of down-regulated genes, most of which are involved in biosynthesis and metabolic pathways (Fig. 3C). Therefore, GSK126 treatment alone results in limited anti-tumor effects (Fig. 2), and the genes down-regulated by GSK126 may not be directly involved in anti-tumor processes. Querying Differentially Expressed Genes (DEGs) in the Tumor Suppressor Gene (TSG) database (https://bioinfo.uth.edu/TSGene/) revealed that tumor suppressor genes were up-regulated by epigenetic therapies, particularly for the DAC + GSK126 combination treatments, in all three HCC cell lines evaluated (Fig. 3D). Expression changes of a panel of eight genes were validated by quantitative RT-PCR (Supplemental Figure S2). In addition, by searching against the StemChecker database (http://stemchecker.sysbiolab.eu), we found that expression of stemness pathway genes, such as those regulated by E2F4, SOX2, NANOG, and OCT4, were generally downregulated after DAC or DAC + GSK126 treatments (Fig. 3E). The expression changes of eight selected stemness genes were also validated by quantitative RT-PCR (Supplemental Figure S3). Taken together, these findings suggest that DAC + GSK126 treatments result in strong anti-tumor effects by up-regulating immune response and tumor suppressor pathways while concurrently down-regulating cancer stem-like genes and pathways.

Combination treatment of DAC + GSK126 increases chromatin accessibility and gene expression alterations as a mechanism of action

We previously demonstrated that most DNA demethylation events after DAC treatment are passenger effects and do not result in gene activation due to retained inaccessible chromatin and PRC2 repression at demethylated gene promoters. We hypothesized that these genes with retained inaccessible chromatin status and PRC2 repression after DAC treatment can be activated by the addition of an EZH2 inhibitor, thereby accounting for cellular phenotype changes upon treatment. Since nucleosome positioning also plays an important role in regulating gene expression, we performed AcceSssIble analysis to simultaneously analyze DNA methylation and chromatin accessibility in HCC cells lines after GSK126, DAC, or DAC + GSK126 treatments. In this assay, chromatin accessibility levels at individual CpG loci were obtained for each probe on the Illumina EPIC DNA methylation platform by subtracting the endogenous DNA methylation β-value from the β-value after M.SssI treatment.

We first measured the DNA methylation and chromatin accessibility levels before and after treatments for all three HCC cell lines. Next, we plotted DNA methylation changes (≥0.2 Δβ-value) against the levels of chromatin accessibility changes (≥0.3 ΔAcc-value) between untreated and treated cells in 2-D Kernel density scatter plots to examine the epigenetic changes caused by GSK126, DAC, or DAC + GSK126 treatments (Fig. 4A). These epigenetic changes could be theoretically illustrated by four potential models (Groups a, b, c, and d) (Fig. 4B). Groups a and b represent DNA demethylation without (Group a) or with (Group b) chromatin accessibility increases after drug treatment (Fig. 4A and B). Furthermore, Group b probes show strong inverse correlation between DNA hypomethylation and gain of chromatin accessibility (r among −0.35 to −0.66, p < 2.2 × 10−16). Groups c and d represent chromatin accessibility alterations without DNA methylation involvement. However, because DAC or DAC + GSK126 treatments mainly induce DNA demethylation, most changes were in Groups a and b (Fig. 4A and B).

Treatment with DAC and DAC + GSK126, but not GSK126 alone, resulted in most DNA demethylation events that were not associated with corresponding gains in chromatin accessibility (Fig. 4). Approximately 19–42% of all probes showed DNA demethylation alone after DAC treatment (Group a), however, this varied between the HCC cell lines, with SNU398 cells showing the most widespread DNA demethylation in 42% of all probes. However, <0.1% of all probes gained chromatin accessibility together with DNA demethylation (Group b) (Fig. 4B). As expected, the DAC + GSK126 treatment further increased the percentage of DNA demethylated probes (29–55%) and dramatically increased the percentage of probes (0.06–2.6%, or 2–28 fold increase) that gained chromatin accessibility (Group b) in each HCC cell line (Fig. 4B). Importantly, these findings strongly support our hypothesis that most DAC-induced gene promoter DNA demethylation is passive and gene activation is blocked by PRC2-mediated chromatin inaccessibility that is subsequently alleviated after EZH2 inhibition. Interestingly, a small number of probes lost (Group c, <2.2%) or gained (Group d, <0.2%) chromatin accessibility without DNA methylation involvement (Fig. 4A and B). Although increased chromatin accessibility without DNA methylation modulation (Group d) can be explained by EZH2 inhibition or indirectly by DNA methylation inhibition, decreased chromatin accessibility induced by DAC + GSK126 combination treatments appear to be indirect effects.

Using AcceSssIble assay data, we discriminated functional DNA demethylation events, namely those events that are accompanied by chromatin accessibility changes (Group b), from potential passenger effects of DNA demethylation with continued inaccessible chromatin (Group a). The DAC + GSK126 combination treatment scheme demonstrated potential clinical efficacy in shifting targeted genes from Group a into Group b, thereby widening the breadth of therapeutic targets and anti-tumor effects.

Commonly altered genes in primary HCC tumors are therapeutic targets of DAC + GSK combination treatment

Genomic regions that undergo DNA demethylation (Group a) or DNA demethylation together with increased chromatin accessibility (Group b) after DAC + GSK126 therapy are in gene promoters, transcribed exonic regions, enhancers and others. Linking enhancer DNA methylation to gene expression without approaches such as chromosome conformation capture are challenging. Since DNA methylation in promoter regions is well established, we focused our analysis on promoter-associated probes in Groups a and b (Fig. 4) to determine whether DNA demethylation and chromatin accessibility gains at gene promoters are therapeutic targets of DAC + GSK126 combination treatment. Specifically, we analyzed the Group a and b CpG loci to focus on demethylated probes in promoter regions that overlap with Illumina HumanMethylation450 (HM450) DNA methylation BeadArray data of primary liver hepatocellular carcinoma (LIHC) patient samples from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) Research Network (https://cancergenome.nih.gov/cancersselected/LiverHepatocellularCarcinoma) (Fig. 4B).

In this analysis, we focused on the probes that are unmethylated (β value < 0.3, 7,881 probes) in normal tissues to characterize aberrant promoter DNA hypermethylation events in primary HCC tumors and cell lines tumors in response to drug treatments (Fig. 5A). Among these, we found that >25% of the promoter probes (n = 2,169 probes, corresponding to 1,031 genes) were frequently methylated in TCGA tumors compared to adjacent-normal tissues. Leukocyte infiltration, a measure of tumor cell purity, did not correlate with tumor DNA methylation levels based on published data (Fig. 5B). Interestingly, we noticed that genes whose promoters were frequently methylated in the TCGA dataset (Fig. 5B) were also frequently down-regulated compared to their paired adjacent-normal tissues (Fig. 5C). Upon querying the set of n = 1, 031 genes against those targeted by DAC + GSK126 treatments in HepG2, SNU398, and SNU475 HCC cell lines, we identified 321, 239, and 140 genes that were upregulated in HepG2, SNU398 and SNU475 cells, respectively, after the combination drug treatment. (Fig. 5D).

As expected, DAC + GSK126 treatments activated a larger set of genes and to greater levels of gene expression compared to DAC or GSK126 alone in all three HCC cell lines (Fig. 5D and E). We also identified 288 up-regulated genes without DNA methylation involvement in the TCGA HCC database that are also down-regulated by DAC, GSK126 and DAC + GSK126 treatments in the panel of HCC cell lines (Supplemental Figure S4). The majority of these genes were down-regulated by GSK126 treatment alone, suggesting that PRC2 occupancy and nucleosome positioning are the mechanisms responsible for their silencing.

Overall, these findings suggest that a large portion of aberrantly down-regulated genes by DNA methylation in primary HCC can be reshaped by epigenetic therapies and represent therapeutic targets of DAC or DAC + GSK126 treatments. However, down-regulation of genes by GSK126 treatments in HCC cell lines may have limited correlation with anti-tumor effects as treatment with GSK126 alone does not result in sustained therapeutic efficacy (Fig. 2).

DAC and DAC + GSK126 treatments strongly up-regulate immune response and interferon-stimulated genes

DAC and other DNMTis induce viral mimicry responses by reactivating transposable elements (TEs, that include long terminal repeats (LTRs), short interspersed nuclear elements (SINEs), and long interspersed nuclear elements (LINEs), all of which stimulate immune response and convert immune cold tumor cells into immune hot tumor cells to ultimately improve immunotherapy sensitivity and effectiveness. EZH2 has been reported to be directly involved in repressing TE activation in cancer, and we recently reported that dual DNA methylation and EZH2 inhibition resulted in synergetic TE reactivation. Gene ontology (GO) analysis suggests strong immune pathway upregulation after DAC + GSK126 treatment (Fig. 3C).

We performed RNA-seq on the three HCC cell lines before and after DAC, GSK126 and DAC + GSK126 treatments in order to characterize the gene expression profiles of immune response pathways including leukocyte, T cell, lymphocyte mediated immunity and 27-curated viral defense genes. Immune-related genes and pathways were strongly up-regulated by DAC and DAC + GSK126 treatments, however, GSK126 treatments alone resulted in only limited immune pathway activation (Fig. 6A–C). In addition, viral defense gene upregulation (Fig. 6D) was highly correlated with TE upregulation by DAC or DAC + GSK126 treatments (Supplemental Figure S5), as we described previously.

Surveying the expression profiles of immune-related genes in the TCGA HCC RNA-seq dataset revealed that >50% of primary HCC primary tumors expressed immune related genes at low levels (Fig. 6E), suggesting that most HCC tumor cells are immune cold but have the potential to become immune hot when given epigenetic therapies. From a clinical perspective, DAC or DAC + GSK126 therapies have tremendous utility in activating immune response genes and priming HCC tumor cells for immune-based therapeutics.

Discussion

HCC is an aggressive form of human cancer with poor patient survival, therefore, there is an urgent need for developing more efficacious therapies. Most HCC therapies are focused on specific HCC pathways, but with limited clinical success. Epigenetic alterations are pharmacologically reversible and epigenetic-driver genes have been identified in HCCs and virtually every tumor type. We previously reported anti-tumor effects after treatment of HCC cells with the second generation DNMTi Guadecitabine (SGI-110), however, DNMTi-based anti-tumor efficacies can certainly be improved. DNA demethylation-based gene expression alterations are limited after DNMTi treatment; most DNA demethylated promoters remain silenced and inaccessible by an epigenetic switch that places H3K27me3 repressive and H3K4me3 active histone modifications at demethylated DNA promoters. The synergistic gene expression activation by combination treatments is also associated with increased chromatin accessibility of functional gene regions compared to DNMTi (DAC) treatment alone. Therefore, the additional inhibition of EZH2 to block H3K27me3 marks in bivalent regions overcomes the limitations of using DNMTi alone.

Inhibiting both DNA methylation (DAC) and EZH2 (GSK126) synergistically induces anti-tumor effects compared to either treatment alone. This synergistic effect is associated with tumor suppressor and immune response gene upregulation together with viral mimicry activation and downregulation of stem-like genes. The upregulated genes are involved in inhibiting cell growth, increasing immune response pathways and inhibiting cancer stem-like cells to reduce tumor aggressiveness. We previously demonstrated immune response (viral mimicry) upregulation after DNMTi treatment alone. However, the DAC + GSK126 combination treatment scheme further upregulates leukocyte, T cell and lymphocyte mediated immune responses that are above and beyond viral mimicry, thereby leading to a more pronounced immune response activation compared to DNMTi treatment alone. Unexpectedly, the majority of TCGA HCC primary tumors present as immune cold with downregulation of not only viral defense genes, but also leukocyte, T cell, and lymphocyte mediated immunity, suggesting that most primary HCC tumors have the potential to be converted to immune hot status by the in vitro DAC + GSK126 combination treatment scheme described here. DAC + GSK126 combination treatments also induced strong viral mimicry activity via TE reactivation, indicating that DNA methylation and PRC2 occupancy are directly involved in TE silencing as well as DAC-mediated TE re-silencing. Taken together, our findings suggest that combination treatments of DNMTi and EZH2i strongly activate tumor suppressor genes and prime the tumor cells for treatment with immunotherapies, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors,.

The existence of cancer stem cells (CSCs) has been demonstrated through the identification of stemness gene expression signatures in HCC. CSCs are closely related to the HCC tumorigenesis and promote HCC aggressiveness features including high invasion, drug resistance, recurrence, metastasis and poor patient survival. The oncogenic cancer stem and progenitor cells can be dysregulated by epigenetic mechanisms including aberrant DNA methylation and H3K27me3 (PRC2) occupancy, which can block cellular differentiation by suppressing gene expression through DNA methylation and/or H3K27me3 marks. Thus, the CSCs have been considered as therapeutical targets in human cancers. Our results demonstrate that DAC + GSK126 combination treatments target CSCs by strongly downregulating stemness genes, thereby decreasing HCC stem cell aggressiveness and inducing cellular differentiation. Although we do not have direct evidence to explain down-regulation of cancer stem related genes by the treatment, it may be a consequence of inducing cell differentiation and epigenetic reprogramming by treatment with epigenetic inhibitors (DAC + GSK126).

EZH2 is involved in the repression of TE-driven anti-tumor immune response and tumorigenesis. However, treatment with GSK126 alone showed limited antitumor effects and only induces viral mimicry when combined with a DNMTi (DAC), which supported by recent studies regarding epigenetic switch by loss DNA methylation and gain of PRC2. Gene expression profiles after GSK126 treatment were different than DAC alone or when combined with DAC. Chromatin accessibility changes by GSK126 alone mainly occurred independent of DNA methylation changes after the treatment but without significant cell inhibition in vitro. Therefore, these changes may not be directly involved in driving the cellular anti-tumor changes associated with treatment response.

Although our results are limited to three cell lines, they represent the three major DNA methylation profiles in primary HCC tumors. Our findings support the potential use of this combination drug treatment scheme in clinical trials. Indeed, both inhibitors have been independently evaluated in HCC clinical trials, however, in vivo combination treatments may be required before initiating clinical trials. We predict that this combination will be efficacious for cancer types that exhibit PRC2 overexpression and can be combined with immunotherapies for effective cancer treatment.
