# Next-generation dynamic and combinatorial nanotherapies for liver cancer: mechanisms, current advances and future perspectives.

## Abstract

Liver Cancer, one of the most lethal cancers in adults, is distinguished by its aggressive invasion, distinctive tumor microenvironment (TME) and resistance to standard treatments, posing challenges. The TME and fibrotic extracellular matrix (ECM) hampers effective drug distribution; hence, new developments in therapeutics have brought creative solutions to these problems. To temporarily breach these barriers and enable targeted treatment, various dynamic therapies using stimuli such as focused Ultrasound, light, chemical reactions, mechanical stress, microwave induction and magnetic fields have demonstrated great promise in inducing localized and spatiotemporal therapeutic effects. This comprehensive review highlights the therapeutic mechanisms, including both chemical and biological effects and elucidates the therapeutic promise of emerging nanomedicine across individual modalities such as sonodynamic therapy (SDT), photodynamic therapy (PDT) and chemodynamic therapy (CDT), supported by preclinical evidence. Thereafter, promising combinatorial dynamic strategies with superior therapeutic effects are outlined. Furthermore, emerging next-generation modalities, including piezodynamic therapy (PZDT), microwave dynamic therapy (MWDT) and magnetodynamic therapy (MDT), with their therapeutic perspectives are discussed in detail. Although these strategies employing emerging nanomedicines have shown remarkable therapeutic potential for clinical translation, controlling physical stimulation and ensuring nanoparticle biocompatibility remain challenging. Continued innovations in medicine and chemistry will be essential for transforming dynamic strategies into clinically viable strategies for liver oncology.

Introduction

Liver Cancer, primarily comprising Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC), cholangiocarcinoma, or mixed carcinoma, develops in the liver and intrahepatic bile duct. Liver Cancer ranks as the second foremost cause of cancer-related mortality globally, after lung cancer, exhibiting an exceptionally elevated fatality-to-incidence ratio exceeding 0.85, as per statistical data. In 2025, the estimated number of diagnosed cases of Liver Cancer is approximately 42,000, with 30,000 projected deaths. It typically arises in the setting of advanced chronic liver disease and is primarily linked to Hepatitis B/C virus (HBV/CV), diabetes mellitus, hemochromatosis, and alcohol misuse. The patients of Liver Cancer experience a poor metastatic trajectory, which firstly affects the lungs, then the bones and eventually the brain in advanced cases. Surgical resection is presently the most effective and primary treatment for Liver Cancer. Additional therapeutic methods, including radiofrequency ablation (RFA), trans-arterial chemoembolization (TACE), percutaneous ethanol injection (PEI), chemotherapy, radiation and systemic chemotherapy, have been implemented to treat Liver Cancer. Liver Cancer is unique because it develops in the chronically inflamed or cirrhotic liver. Furthermore, progressive arterialization in HCCs, heterogeneous TME, high perfusion, heat-sink effect, high metabolic rate, dysregulated redox mechanisms, and the predominance of Kupffer cells and sinusoidal endothelial cells distinguish HCCs from other cancers. Because of these distinctive features, current therapeutic modalities for Liver Cancer face significant limitations that reduce their overall effectiveness and applicability. For instance, RFA is effective against small tumors measuring 3–4 cm and ineffective against large, deep-seated tumors due to the heat-sink effect. Similarly, TACE is palliative rather than curative, providing only temporary control and requiring repeated cycles, which can impair liver function. PEI is also effective against small lesions but less effective against fibrotic and septated tumors. From studies, it has been found that the liver has low radiation tolerance, which limits its application in Liver Cancer treatment as well. Chemotherapy and systemic chemotherapy are somewhat better than the above modalities; still, they provide modest survival improvements but are often hindered by toxicity, resistance, and variable patient adverse responses. In these instances, the necessity to find new curative drugs, cancer diagnostic methods and treatment modalities is urgent.

Over the last few years, cancer treatment has shifted paradigm towards dynamic therapies. These promising modalities accurately and precisely rely on either external or internal stimuli and are also spatiotemporally governed. Such stimuli may be light, Ultrasound (US), magnetic fields, hypoxic state, or TME to produce therapeutic effects in tumor tissue. In preclinical trials, several modalities have been utilized, including PDT, CDT, SDT and emerging modalities in PZDT, MWDT and MDT. It selectively stimulates PSs or NPs in cancerous tissues in response to specific stimuli. It generates cytotoxic reactive oxygen species (ROS), which trigger controlled biological processes, including apoptosis, necrosis, or proptosis, leading to the death of cancer cells. Such strategies are significantly beneficial to treat deep-seated tumors, especially when Liver Cancer is concerned, where safe and efficient selective activation in the TME of the tumor is crucial.

It is a fact that recent cutting-edge reviews have highlighted the potential of dynamic treatment modalities for deadly cancers, such as Liver Cancer. Hu et al. provide a comprehensive overview of advanced methods in their research for membrane-targeted PDT. Some of the targeting approaches, including membrane-targeting PDT, therapeutic preclinical case studies and simplistic targeting mechanisms, were discussed. A state-of-the-art review paper on fluorescent glycoconjugate probes, published by Han et al., was dedicated to biosensing, targeted imaging and phototherapy and covered the developments since 2021. They explained their design principles and future development of theranostic agents that detect and image cell glycosidase activity using these probes. The article by Shi et al. discusses the modalities of the HCC. Their article stresses the need for improved early diagnosis through biomarkers and state-of-the-art equipment, including next-generation sequencing. It underscores the importance of understanding the molecular mechanisms at play, particularly the Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway, to develop more individualized and effective treatments. Collins et al. shed light on the function and effectiveness of various sonosensitizers, as well as the molecular and immunological processes underlying SDT-induced primary and metastatic cancer cell death. Notably, they observed abscopal effects, highlighting the need for further research into the “vaccine-effect” caused by SDT as a possible tactic to improve systemic anti-tumor immunity and fight metastatic disease. All these reviews are excellent, but recently, none have thoroughly highlighted the dynamic therapeutic effects, their mechanism, their combinatory effects, and emerging dynamic therapies in Liver Cancer treatment. Therefore, we highlighted the dynamic therapies utilized in Liver Cancer treatment and perspective therapies with their combinatorial potential in treating Liver Cancer.

This review comprehensively summarizes the dynamic therapeutic modalities for Liver Cancer, focusing on the emerging stimulus-responsive modalities and their synergistic potential. Firstly, it outlines the fundamental principles and mechanisms of the individual dynamic strategies, including SDT, PDT and CDT, emphasizing their underlying chemical as well as biological mechanisms, design of novel nanoplatforms and their preclinical trials, to demonstrate their potential application as promising therapies. Subsequently, this review delineates the combinatory dynamic modalities, such as SDT-chemotherapy, SDT-PTT, SDT-immunotherapy, SDT-CDT, CDT-PTT, PDT-PTT and CDT-Starvation therapy. These therapies demonstrate enhanced antitumor efficacy through synergistic mechanisms and improved tumor selectivity. In the final section, attention is given to next-generation dynamic therapeutic-based perspectives, including PZDT, MWDT and MDT, which represent precise, non-invasive emerging frontiers in Liver Cancer treatment (Scheme 1). The review concludes by outlining the current challenges and prospective solutions in clinical translation, providing insights into the future trajectory of dynamic therapies for Liver Cancer treatment.

SDT for liver cancer treatment

SDT mechanism

SDT involves several interrelated physicochemical mechanisms that collectively result in the selective ablation of tumors. The prevailing view that SDT initiates anticancer activity is comparable to PDT, in which a photoactive molecule, also known as a photosensitizer, absorbs light energy and participates in photodynamic processes. In SDT, sonosensitizers are organic or synthetic chemicals that are activated by the acoustic energy of the Ultrasound.

The activation route is complex, but sonoluminescence is the hypothesis most people accept. The collapse of an endogenous gas bubble or an exogenously supplied microbubble near cancer tissue leads to the emission of visible light, resulting in sonoluminescence. The thermal bremsstrahlung hypothesis is the most commonly accepted to explain the beginning of sonoluminescence. This happens when ions or positively charged atoms attract free electrons, releasing visible light over an extended period.

The operation mechanism of the sonosensitizer (SS) (sonosensitization) is nearly identical to that of a photosensitizer (PS). The energy absorption causes the SS to enter a momentary excited state, often called the singlet state or SS1. The SS can then undergo either direct decay or, via intersystem crossing (ISC), decay to an SS3. These SS states can trigger type I and type II reactions at different points of the deexcitation process and generate ROS. The type I reactions comprise the singlet and triplet excited states and involve electron transfer and H-abstraction. These results are transferred into the generation of radical anions (SS•−) upon acquiring an electron from a substrate. These radical anions then interact with oxygen, producing highly reactive, transient oxygen radicals. ROS are produced due to the interaction between these free radicals and molecular oxygen. These ROS activate cytotoxic processes, including lipid peroxidation of cell membranes, ultimately leading to cell death.

Only the triplet state of the sonosensitizer is involved in type II reactions and these processes interact directly with molecular oxygen (3O2), producing cytotoxic singlet oxygen, 1O2 (Fig. 1). This state triggers cell death by activating cytotoxic pathways.

The development of effective sonosensitizers is critical, especially for deep-seated tumors, such as Liver Cancers. Many sonosensitizers for various disease treatments are currently available, including both organic and inorganic SSs. Organic SSs such as BODIPY, porphyrin, 5-ALA, Phthalocyanine, and PPIX have attracted the attention of researchers due to their low toxicity, tunable structures, and biocompatibility, as well as their enhanced ROS production and aggregation-caused SDT performance. However, for clinical translation, many issues need to be resolved first, such as the hydrophobicity, lower ROS efficiency in hypoxic regions, and instability in psychological conditions. On the other hand, inorganic SSs such as carbon-based NPs, titanium hydrides and metal oxygen-based SSs have gained attention due to robust ROS production, even in the hypoxic region, which resolves the issue of the ROS production of organic SSs, but raises issues of biosafety, biocompatibility, and long-term metal toxicity, which limit their clinical translation. Organic SSs follow the mechanism of molecular excitation by Type I and II to produce ROS. They are less dependent on inertial cavitation, while inorganic SSs are strongly dependent on cavitation to produce ROS. By using the hybrid approach, the mechanisms of both SS types can yield excellent ROS-producing SSs, even under hypoxic conditions, with biocompatibility. They can be promising for the treatment of deep-seated hypoxic tumors (Table 1).

SDT in liver cancer therapy

SDT has garnered promising interest in liver tumor treatment, distinguished by its excellent deep tissue penetrating capability, precise spatial controllability and superior biosafety associated with US activation. Nonetheless, its therapeutic efficacy depends on the effectiveness of the sonosensitizers. The hypoxic microenvironment and aberrant stromal matrix limit the efficacy of sonosensitizers. SDT has clear advantages over frontline surgery, especially for cancers that are deeply situated, such as Liver Cancer. In liver tumor treatments, the main challenge is the environment, which is fibrotic and hypoxic with elevated antioxidant systems, which limit the ROS accumulation, but in SDT, US and cavitation-driven ROS generation occurs, which is less restricted by the hypoxic environment, so it is possibly effective for Liver Cancer. Without harming tissue surfaces or transmission pathways, focused Ultrasound (FUS) in SDT enables deep, accurate targeting at tens of cm, eliminating the need for invasive instruments such as endoscopes. SDT dramatically lowers the risk of problems close to the hepatic portal during the treatment of Liver Cancer, increasing patient survival rates. Additionally, the risks of recurrence and metastasis from needle tracts, surgical wounds and ablation are reduced when SDT is combined with US therapy. SDT is therefore regarded as a potentially secure and adaptable cancer treatment approach. However, many sonosensitizers exhibit low ROS production rates, especially under hypoxic conditions.

Recent advancements in SDT nanoplatform designs have shifted the paradigm for integrating vasodilation, targeted delivery, and metal redistribution to overcome the challenges of classical SDT. To address this issue, novel and efficient sonosensitizers for hypoxic environments have been developed. For example, Peng et al. introduced a novel nanoplatform that addresses these obstacles by integrating transient vasodilation of tumor microvessels with the clinically prevalent antianginal agent isosorbide mononitrate (ISN) and an acceptor-donor-acceptor-donor-acceptor type organic nano-sonosensitizer (BTz) designed for a narrow bandgap and improved Ultrasound responsiveness (Fig. 2a-b). This work demonstrated the sonodynamic efficacy of the nanosonosensitizer through comprehensive cellular validation on Hepa1-6 HCC cells. The nanosonosensitizer was highly compatible with cells, maintaining viability above 90%. However, upon Ultrasound irradiation, it demonstrated efficient conversion of US energy into ROS and other cytotoxic intermediates, indicating the substantial impact of dynamic Ultrasound (Fig. 2c). Isosorbide mononitrate enhances nano-sonosensitizer accumulation by approximately 1.8-fold. US irradiation of the nano-sonosensitizer resulted in significant ROS production, achieving 78% tumor growth inhibition in mouse HCC models, almost double that of SDT alone, without observable systemic damage (Fig. 2d). In this work, ISMN-modified BTz was used; ISMN is a clinically approved vasodilator and, given the leaky vasculature in the tumor region, induces vasodilation of tumor microvessels. This vasodilation increased the permeability of the vasculature, allowing the nanosensitizer BTz to accumulate in HCC by 1.8x and making this compound highly HCC-selective, along with being an excellent sonodynamic therapeutic agent. Notably, the use of the clinically approved vasodilation approach addresses the issue of the heterogeneous perfusion in liver tumors where sinusoidal collapse and irregular blood flow often limit NPs delivery.

Ouyang et al. used a nanobubble-based system that integrated protoporphyrin (PPIX) and elesclomol-Cu2+ (ES-Cu) to form PPIX@ES-Cu NBs (Fig. 2e). PPIX is an excellent sonosensitizer and produces ROS under the US cavitation, while ES-Cu undergoes the cuproptosis in mitochondria, leading to apoptosis and tumor cell death (Fig. 2f). In this study, they treated HCC using nanobubbles and ES-Cu with PPIX. PPIX has the potential to generate ROS, though its generation is restricted, whereas the use of nanobubbles and ES-Cu is oxygen-independent. Nanobubbles accumulate in the tumor vasculature, release payloads upon the US cavitation and modulate the TME. In comparison to the nano sensitizers systems, depending upon the systematic delivery and vascular modulation, embolization-induced SDT further deals with the chronic hypoxia and elevated antioxidant defense while emphasizing the spatial confinement and retention specifically within liver cancers.

Similarly, Xu et al. utilized the SDT-trans-arterial embolization method to address the issues of ROS production and biosafety. They used MnWOX encapsulated gelatin microspheres (Mn-GMSs) for Liver Cancer treatment (Fig. 2g). Here, Mn-GMS generated high ROS due to MnWOX nanodots and the high potential of cavitation of the porous microspheres. Here, TAE results in higher retention of the sensitizers, offsets hypoxia, and enhances the spatially localized delivery of sonosensitizers. This nanoplatform has shown significant therapeutic efficacy in treating HCC in mice (Fig. 2i). As this approach integrates SDT with TAE, exploiting an exclusive hepatic interventional route, it minimizes systemic exposure. It enables localized SS retention within hepatocytes, an advantage largely unavailable in extrahepatic tumors.

SDT is a potential non-invasive treatment method; nevertheless, its progress is hindered by issues in accurately co-localizing the tumor, sonosensitizers and focused Ultrasound (FUS). Beyond superior therapeutic efficacy, a critical challenge of the SDT in liver cancer is the precise colocalization of SSs, cavitation activity, and ROS generation within the perfused and fibrotic sinusoidal region. The lack of viable techniques for in vivo detection of ROS impedes continued study and progress in this domain. Wang et al. presented a novel sonosensitizer, a phthalocyanine-conjugated mesoporous silicate NP (PAMSN) infused with allylhydrazine. PAMSN not only enhances the fluorescence signal of phthalocyanine by 1.9-fold but also produces nitrogen gas bubbles through the interaction of allylhydrazine and ROS. This unique characteristic establishes PAMSN as a versatile multimodal contrast agent appropriate for in vivo tumor imaging and SDT applications (Fig. 3a). The fluorescence images, exhibiting the renal metabolism as it was diminished in the bladder, as shown in (Fig. 3b). Furthermore, they developed an FUS platform that combines fluorescence and ultrasonic imaging (USI) guidance, ensuring accurate Ultrasound delivery to the designated region (Fig. 3c). They also used passive cavitation imaging (CVI) to determine the FUS range and ROS generation from the SDT, which covered the tumor region. The group FUS with PAMSN exhibited stronger cavitation signals and it was restricted to the liver tumor area, validating the precision and safety of the designed SDT system (Fig. 3d). This imaging-guided SDT showed excellent therapeutic efficacy with renal clearance, as the bioluminescence images with PAMSN, exhibited a significant decrease, as shown in (Fig. 3e). Growth curves of the bioluminescence signals also showed considerable tumor suppression with PAMSN in the FUS group, as compared to other groups and no body weight alterations were observed (Fig. 3f-g). This indicated that this probe with SDT has minimal toxic effects, further validating its safety and efficacy. Thus, in collaboration with PAMSN, this approach can effectively treat orthotopic Liver Cancer in a mouse model while facilitating in vivo monitoring of ROS and detection of cavitation. In orthotopic Liver Cancers, leaky vasculature and poor lymphatic drainage result in the EPR effect, leading to PAMSN accumulation in tumor cells only. Also, the ROS triggered by allylhydrazine reacts only with the nitrogen bubbles generated in the liver tumor region. So, this SDT-based nanoplatform exhibits excellent tumor-targeting potential without affecting healthy liver tissue. Compared with delivery and embolization SDT strategies, PAMSN-mediated SDT using the multimodal imaging-guided SDT platform enables a precise, controllable procedure, particularly challenging in hepatic tumors because of their proximity to vital hepatic structures, offering a promising tool for safer, more effective SDT in clinical tumor therapy.

Overall, these multidimensional SDT approaches provide a robust framework for Liver Cancer treatment. ISMN-BTz nanoplatform develops a translatable, minimally invasive approach for targeted SDT of localized, inoperable, or metastatic HCC by repurposing a clinically approved safe vasodilator ISMN to generate high ROS yields and target hypoxic regions. PPIX@ES-Cu provides an excellent sensitizer, combining nanobubbles and ES-Cu, offering an outstanding platform for localized cavitation of the sensitizer and ROS generation in the low-oxygen zone. This study provides a tumor-selective, hypoxic-resistant approach that addresses SDT feasibility in HCC treatment. Mn-GMS integrated SDT with embolization, providing a superior platform for ROS production and overcoming the hypoxic environment induced by the TAE which is approved clinically for HCC interventions, thereby facilitating localized retention. Here, the use of TAE improved the localized delivery, thereby enabling targeted therapy. PAMSN, on the other hand, provided imaging-guided SDT with an EPR effect, leading to high ROS generation and selective liver tumor targeting via the bubbles. Together, these approaches revealed the applicability of the SDT in the deep-seated hypoxic tumors by combining the deep tissue penetration, high ROS yield, hypoxia resistance and targeted delivery. Although these have demonstrated strong biosafety, ROS production, and the ability to overcome hypoxia-related limitations, there remain pivotal challenges, such as long-term metal toxicity in these hybrid SSs, renal clearance, unpredictable cavitation, and a high-powered complex system in SDT, that must be addressed for their successful clinical translation.

PDT for liver cancer treatment

PDT mechanism

The PDT process can be categorized into two categories based on the mechanisms utilized. When exposed to appropriate light, PSs are initially stimulated to an unstable singlet state for a millisecond. Thereupon, they quickly shift between the ISC system and a more stable triplet state. In the Type I mechanism, these triplet PSs, undergo electron transfer with their immediate substrates to produce radical cations or anions. Radicals may also react with substances containing oxygen, such as water or oxygen gas, to form ROS, including the superoxide anion (O2−) and the hydroxyl radical (OH−). In type II mechanisms, direct energy transfer from the triplet PS to ground-state molecular oxygen produces highly reactive singlet oxygen (1O2). Cytotoxic ROS can directly oxidize and irreparably damage the biomolecules around the site, like lipids, protein amino acids, nucleobases and sugar linkers of nucleic acids. The ultimate result of the altered metabolism from normality is the amplification of cytokine and stress protein synthesis, which disrupts cellular physiology and induces cell death. The antioxidant capacity and high tolerance to oxidative stress of Liver Cancer cells are attributed to high glutathione (GSH) levels and catalase activity. Therefore, the photochemical reactions are of special interest. Thus, producing sufficient ROS to accumulate in the liver tumor cells is not easy. This requires PSs or oxygen self-supplying systems that are efficient in a low-oxygen TME. In addition, the thick ECM and disordered vasculature in the liver hinder the movement of oxygen and the penetration of light into the liver tumor zone. This shows that PSs with good red or near-infrared (NIR) absorption are required to increase the PDT efficacy by penetrating deeper into the tissue.

Nonetheless, it is essential to acknowledge that ROS have a limited lifespan and exhibit limited diffusion distances due to their exceedingly high reactivity. The half-life 1O2 is brief, lasting less than 40 ns and its effective radius is limited to under 20 nm. As a result, the damage inflicted by ROS is confined to regions with high photosystem concentration. Nonetheless, the inconsistent distribution of PSs within tumor tissues or cells presents a significant obstacle to modulating ROS activity, frequently resulting in suboptimal PDT effects.

These subcellular PDT approaches, which focus on mitochondria, the cytomembrane, the Golgi apparatus, lysosomes and the nucleus, may reduce the distance between ROS-generating sites and their targets, enabling direct, immediate interaction with the reactive sites (Fig. 4). This technique can improve bioavailability and maximize the anticancer efficacy of PDT while reducing the required PS concentration. Moreover, ligand-mediated PS delivery systems modified with galactose, glycyrrhetinic acid, or transferrin have exhibited increased selectivity for hepatic tumor tissues via receptor-mediated endocytosis, thereby reducing off-target effects on normal hepatocytes. This precise targeting is beneficial for treating HCC because if ROS were generated nonselectively, they could cause oxidative damage to healthy liver tissue.

Biological membranes are another potential therapeutic intervention against cancer, as they can trigger cellular processes such as autophagy, necrosis, and apoptosis in response to severe plasma membrane injury induced by ROS. In the treatment of Liver Cancer, membrane-targeted PDT offers distinct therapeutic benefits. Oxidative damage to these lipid components can cause cell death that appears to be ferroptosis. This works exceptionally well against HCC cells that don’t respond to apoptosis. Furthermore, membrane-specific ROS generation can undermine multidrug resistance (MDR) transporters, which are frequently overexpressed in Liver Cancers, thereby enhancing drug absorption and increasing cell sensitivity to combination therapies. Consequently, combining membrane-targeted PDT with additional therapeutic approaches, such as chemotherapy, PTT, or SDT, may offer a synergistic strategy to mitigate hypoxia-induced treatment resistance in hepatic malignancies.

PDT in liver cancer therapy

Primary liver tumors are significant to diagnose and treat, and optical imaging has been used extensively in clinical imaging-guided liver tumor surgery. PDT is exceptionally effective in treating exophytic tumors but faces limitations in treating the deep-seated tumors. The primary limitation is light penetration, as visible and NIR-I light used to activate PSs is strongly attenuated by scattering and absorption in biological tissues, limiting penetration to only a few millimeters. Higher power can resolve this issue, but it leads to the damaging of the healthy liver stem cells, so it is not a good option. Also, the hypoxic environment reduces the PDT efficacy in deep-seated tumors, as oxygen is required to generate cytotoxic singlet oxygen and ROS. Studies have shown that the liver environment is hypoxic, and antioxidants such as GSH, SOD, and CAT impair the effectiveness of PDT. Liver Cancer, mainly HCC, exhibits hypoxia, which hampers therapeutic outcomes, surges recurrence rates and promotes metastasis, especially during PDT in several preclinical and clinical practices. To deal with these issues, several new PDT-based NPs and strategies have been devised and utilized in the deep-seated tumors, including the liver tumors. NIR II window emissive photosensitizers have garnered a lot of interest due to their deeper penetration and higher-resolution bioimaging. Also, it can lead to the localized activation of the PSs in the deep-seated tumors, such as the liver, specifically without damaging the healthy liver stem cells. Similarly, two-photon excitation (TPE) or X-ray-based PDT is a good option. Here are some excellent examples of the above-described strategies, each explored in detail.

Li et al. engineered and synthesized gold nanostars with CD147 modifications on their surface, subsequently loading a photosensitive drug, IR820, to create a multifunctional nanoprobe. Inherent protective abilities of gold confer oxygen and heat energy generation capabilities, enabling efficient drug delivery into tumor cells (Fig. 5a). Furthermore, the nanoprobe demonstrates remarkable photothermal and photodynamic therapeutic efficacy. Observations through PAI confirmed the exceptional tumor-targeting attributes of our nanoprobe. Ultimately, in vivo treatment experiments revealed significant tumor-suppressive effects, eliminating the tumor growth to 90% following photothermal and PDT. In this, they utilized hyaluronic acid (HA), which is selective to the CD44 and ASPGR receptors, which are predominantly overexpressed in HCC, and elevated levels of GSH also contribute to its overexpression. In this way, they utilized PDT as an effective treatment against the liver tumor even in the hypoxic environment, which hinders the classical PDT. This study contributes to advancing novel combined tumor-therapy strategies using nanomaterials. The combined CD147 and HA results in specific HCC targeting due to the overexpression of ASPGR and CD44 in malignant hepatocytes, while minimally affecting healthy Kupffer cells, providing a liver cancer-selective PDT approach.

NIR-II emissive NPs have shown great promise as PSs in deep-seated tumors. On this basis, Zheng et al. developed BLICP@O2, which utilized thermosensitive liposomes and hybrid tumor cell membranes as oxygen carriers, including NIR-II dye IR1048, photosensitizer chlorin e6 (Ce6) and perfluorohexane. When exposed to radiation at 1064 and 690 nm in succession, BLICP@O2 shows notable phototherapeutic effects in the liver. Here, the Ce6 is a good NIR-II-responsive PS. At the same time, perfluorohexane is used as a modification, as it is selective for liver tumor tissues and binds specifically to Liver Cancer biomarkers. By releasing oxygen, photothermal heating amplifies the PDT effects of Ce6. Using a 1064 nm laser, this probe detected even minute details, exhibiting PCE of 51.1%, while eliminating virtually all tumors with an apoptosis rate of 60%. Moreover, multispectral PAI, which provides real-time monitoring, tracks changes in blood oxygen during PDT (Fig. 5b-e). This work offers valuable advances in PDT-based organ cancer therapy by introducing a cascaded treatment that combines imaging guidance with a dual-wavelength control strategy to detect and clear HCC. In contrast to the receptor-specific PDT approaches, this strategy prioritizes the self-sufficient oxygen delivery and optical penetration, overcoming the hypoxic and light penetration issues, particularly in the arterialized hepatic system.

Similarly, Wang et al. reported a novel multimodal NIR fluorescent probe (CEP1) designed for carboxylesterase imaging (CEI) and PDT of HCC, with successful in vitro and in vivo applications. The probe was created by adding a carbamate to S-substituted Nile Blue (ENBS) as an identification and fluorescence-quenching unit, employing a substituted chloride as an alternative trigger-responsive spacer. Upon activation by CE, the probe hydrolyses to release fluorescent ENBS, which generates (O2•) anions and restores fluorescence at 700 nm when exposed to NIR light. The probe effectively distinguishes cancer cells from healthy cells in live CE imaging. In vivo CE imaging-guided PDT decreased tumor growth by 91.3%. This work offers a viable theranostic platform for activatable NIR FI-guided PDT in the treatment of HCC (Fig. 5f). The combination of ENBS with a carbamate in the probe enables specific identification and fluorescence quenching, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of live-cell CE imaging. This innovative approach shows great potential to improve treatment outcomes for HCC through image-guided PDT. As compared to the receptor, this enzyme-responsive activation strategy is advantageous as CE is selective in liver cancer as compared to the other cancers and elevated in the cancer’s hepatocytes, enabling enzyme level discrimination and minimizing off-target effects.

In clinical practice, one of the most crucial challenges in real-time monitoring-based tumor treatment is distinguishing residual small tumors from healthy progenitor tissues. A single imaging modality has shown spatial visualization, penetration and contrast issues, which can be resolved by using dual or multimodal imaging with the treatment module. In some studies, the combination of NIR-II FI and PAI offers many benefits, including deep imaging penetration, high spatial resolution, and exceptional sensitivity, making it a perfect diagnostic technique for cancer treatment. Huang et al. used multimodal imaging-guided PDT-based HCC-selective carbonized ZIF loaded with a cytotoxin (CTX) and Ce6 PSs (RGD-PDA@C-ZIFA@CTX+Ce6). This approach provided excellent real-time monitoring therapy with the selective modified NPs. Here, RGD enhances the EPR effect, while C-ZIF integrates into the hypoxic TME and releases a CTX-guided PDT agent. These C-ZIF and Ce6 together enabled multimodal imaging (FI, PAI, and CT), providing excellent molecular monitoring with Ce6 tracking, deep tissue penetration, and high-resolution anatomical imaging. Due to this multimodal imaging-guided treatment, superior Ce6 accumulation and ROS generation at tumor sites, along with reduced off-target effects, were observed. This study provides insights into the effectiveness of the integrated imaging platform for the selective treatment of Liver Cancer. Compared with the single-modal imaging technique, this approach reduced off-target effects and helped distinguish residual tumor nodules from regenerating hepatic tissue, a great challenge.

TPE-based PDT has shown excellent promise for treating deep-seated tumors due to its deep light penetration, which activates PSs deep within tumors. TPE-PDT is a nonlinear process and uses high photon density at the focal point of the laser. This optical-sectioning potential enables precise activation of the PSs only in the tumor region, resulting in minimal damage to surrounding healthy cells. In 2025, Tan et al. used DBEP@αMV, a bioengineered, vesicle-encapsulated, self-assembled nanomedicine. DBEP includes indomethacin and a two-photon PS targeting mitochondria, whereas αMV includes a CD47 blocker, a MMP2-cleavable peptide, and a VSV-G fusion protein. CDT for liver cancer treatment

CDT, an intriguing substitute for conventional cancer treatments (such as surgery, radiation and chemotherapy), has been regarded as a tumor-specific treatment with few adverse effects in the field of emerging nano-catalytic medicine. From the studies, it has been found that the most prevalent type of CDT is the Fenton/Fenton-like reactions induced by TME. Breakdown of hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and generation of hazardous hydroxyl radicals, or •OH, are generally catalyzed by transition metal ions, including Fe, Co, Ni, Cu and Mn. The typical mechanism of reaction is Fe2+ + H2O2 → Fe3+ + •OH + OH−. The above leads to oxidative stress, eventually killing cancer cells by damaging their biomolecular components: proteins, lipids and nucleic acids. The advantages of CDT compared to conventional therapeutic procedures include decreased systemic toxicity, reduced side effects and greater tumor specificity and selectivity. The exceptional selectivity minimizes trauma to normal organs as it targets tumor cells, being selective for their physiological and metabolic peculiarities. This personalized therapy would transform the treatment of cancer since it reduces the side effects of the conventional cancer therapies. Also, CDT has shown promise in fighting against MDR by providing alternatives to deal with the persistent previous dilemma of resistance in cancer treatment. In addition, the possibility of CDT integrating with SDT, starvation therapy and PTT, among others, to enhance the effectiveness of cancer treatment and provide a complex, coordinated solution exists. It has been an exciting field of research over the last several years, driven by these advantages.

In cancer treatment, modern cancer research has given nanomedicine significant attention. Localized drug delivery within the TME begins by precisely controlling the pharmacokinetics of drug release from morphologically versatile structures. Targeting various forms and types of cancer based on their stage is now possible because of the adaptability of nanomedicine, which enables different chemodynamic agents to be loaded. In addition, nanomedicine enables maximum drug loading due to its large surface area-to-volume ratio, enabling the drug to carry a greater therapeutic payload to tumor sites. In addition, the excellent biocompatibility of nanomedicine ensures patient safety with minimal side effects. It prevents immune activation and prolongs circulation time, thereby enhancing tumor growth. The good biodistribution properties also protect normal tissues from undesired exposure and improve therapeutic effectiveness. Finally, optical nanomedicine imaging and diagnostic capabilities enable real-time therapy monitoring, offering precision medicine for specific patients. This diverse use of nanomedicine highlights its potential to develop new cancer therapy approaches.

CDT mechanism

Numerous preclinical and clinical studies have shown that ROS-based therapies can effectively promote tumor regression by disrupting the redox equilibrium of tumor cells. Various oncological treatment modalities, such as PDT, SDT and CDT, have recently emerged, exploiting the overproduction of ROS in tumor tissues. These modalities represent a new and promising approach to cancer treatment. CDT uses the chemical properties of medications to achieve therapeutic effects. In particular, CDT produces ROS via Fenton or Fenton-like reactions, exploiting the low pH and elevated H2O2 levels at the tumor site. In some circumstances, CDT’s independence from external energy sources makes it more adaptable and controllable especially for treating deep-seated tissues. Mainly, CDT-based treatment of Liver Cancers works by producing lethal amounts of •OH radicals through the degradation of H2O2, catalyzed by iron and other metal ions. In Fenton reactions, Fe2+ reacts with H2O2 to produce ROS, while in Fenton-like reactions, transition metals are used in place of Fe2+.

These short-lived radicals are irreversibly damaging to cells as their oxidation potential is 2.8 V, widely large (high) enough to oxidize biomacromolecules such as membrane lipids, nucleic acids and structural proteins, as shown in (Fig. 6). As reported by Zhao et al., recent advancements in CDT have shifted from traditional Fenton chemistry to catalytic nanoplatforms that integrate multiple functions to enhance catalytic turnover and TME adaptability. These encompass cascade nanozymes that can alter the local biochemical environment by producing additional H2O2 (through oxidase-like modules), replenishing low-valence metal ions, pH-responsive systems and antioxidant depletion techniques, especially GSH peroxidase inhibition.

Biologically, CDT initiates a series of oxidative events that disrupt cellular homeostasis via lipid peroxidation, mitochondrial dysfunction, DNA fragmentation and the activation of programmed cell death pathways, including apoptosis, necroptosis and ferroptosis. In HCC, the pathophysiological traits of the liver, such as increased oxidative metabolism, elevated basal H₂O₂ levels and persistent inflammation, make CDT particularly relevant. However, the liver’s robust antioxidant network, makes it very difficult to increase therapeutic ROS levels. To tackle this issue, CDT systems designed for Liver Cancer are progressively integrating microenvironment-modulating modules, such as H2O2-generating enzymes (e.g., glucose oxidase), pH-activated metal ion release and nanoarchitectures that promote GSH depletion or oxygen regulation. Moreover, integrating CDT with other dynamic treatment modalities, such as PDT, SDT, or PTT, can boost ROS production via synergistic pathways and spatially limited catalytic amplification. These multimodal platforms not only increase redox stress and tumor-specific cytotoxicity but also enable precision therapy guided by imaging. So, CDT and its hybrid variants represent a new approach to redox-regulated nanomedicine for treating Liver Cancer. They combine chemical catalysis with biological precision to eliminate tumors in deep tissue and at specific times and places (Table 2).

CDT in liver cancer therapy

CDT is a preferential therapeutic modality for Liver Cancer treatment because of the high abnormal levels of H2O2, iron, copper and other transition metals as a result of the dysregulated hepatic metabolism; these abnormally elevated levels of these metals and H2O2 make Liver Cancer susceptible to the CDT, which relies on Fenton-like reactions to generate hydroxyl radicals.

Xu et al. created the nanocomposites GD@GR-Cu/ZIF-8 for targeted therapy of Liver Cancer by integrating doxorubicin (DOX) and S-nitrosoglutathione (GSNO) into N-acetyl-galactosamine-rhodamine B (GalNAc-RhB)-modified zeolitic imidazolidine acid frameworks-8 with copper (Cu/ZIF-8) as shown in (Fig. 7a). GD@GR-Cu/ZIF-8 can generate (•OH) from H2O2, which enhances ROS levels in Huh-7 cells and results in the strongest fluorescence (Fig. 7b). ASPGR, which is the receptor, is overexpressed only in the HCC cells and not in the healthy liver tissues. They used GalNAc, a ligand that binds only to the ASPGR, making it selective for Liver Cancer cells. Also, in the HCC, there is TME and elevated GSH levels to maintain redox stability. According to the in vitro data, GD@GR-Cu/ZIF-8 has good biosafety and targets HCC cells by reducing glutathione (GSH) by 39.27% and increasing nitric oxide (NO) and hydroxyl radical (•OH) levels. This reduction in GSH weakens the defense system of tumor cells, making HCC susceptible to ROS and cuproptosis. By releasing Cu2+, GD@GR-Cu/ZIF-8 can also successfully cause HCC cells to undergo cuproptosis. Excellent biodistribution of GD@GR-Cu/ZIF-8 nanocomposites and its tumor inhibition rate of 89.9%, indicating the effectiveness of CDT (Fig. 7c-e). This study offers a novel approach to treating HCC using cuproptosis-induced CDT. Here, GaInAc is a selective ligand that binds only to the overexpressed receptors. The release of copper, which triggers cuproptosis, leads to mitochondrial metabolic death in Liver Cancer cells, which are uniquely susceptible due to an abnormal redox environment. Notably, this approach exploits liver-specific vulnerabilities simultaneously, such as ASPGR-mediated uptake, copper sensitivity and elevated intratumoral GSH, which makes this approach particularly effective in HCC tumors compared with non-hepatic tumors.

Here, Cheng et al. described the facile creation of a flexible CDT-based theranostic nanoplatform (D@LADFe) for integrative imaging-guided CDT and tumor-specific magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). DOX and coordinating iron (Fe3+) were encapsulated into chimeric peptide (LAD) NPs to create the nanoplatform D@LADFe as shown in (Fig. 8a). This work proposes a logical design approach for a unique self-assembling peptide-based nanoplatform for tumor-specific MR imaging and CDT therapeutic applications, owing to its advantageous biocompatibility and biosafety. This approach uses ferroptosis and undergoes Fenton reactions, while the above-discussed strategy involves cuproptosis and Fenton-like reactions; both are CDT-complementary pathways for HCC. As a result, such tailored CDT systems transcend proof-of-proof demonstrations and move towards liver-specific, clinically translatable systems that can directly disrupt the redox balance and metabolic resistance in liver cancers.

CDT, which is based on the Fenton or Fenton-like reaction in vivo to eliminate malignant cells, has emerged as a novel anticancer approach. SDT-chemotherapy for liver cancer treatment

SDT has become a potential therapeutic approach in clinics in recent years. It uses US to activate SDT sensitizers, which cause malignant cells to produce significant amounts of harmful ROS. Although SDT generates ROS under US, which can directly ablate tumors, these tumors sometimes cannot reach all regions of tumors, especially in deep-seated parts of liver tumors. Similarly, most liver tissues develop resistance to chemotherapeutic drugs, limiting their effectiveness. Combinatory strategies, such as the SDT-Chemotherapy, can be more effective in dealing with these issues. The combination of SDT and chemotherapy can be effective, as SDT can increase cell membrane permeability and induce ROS-mediated stress, making resistant cells more sensitive to chemotherapeutic drugs. Also, combining therapy results in lower doses of chemotherapeutic drugs, mitigating adverse effects.

To produce an anti-HCC effect, Li et al. used a sonodynamic-chemotherapy combination technique. Cepharanthine (Cep), a phytomedicine, is incorporated into the Tris(chlorisopropyl)Phosphate (TCPP) Metal–Organic Framework (MOF) (Fig. 9a). In vivo imaging confirmed the tumor-targeting capability of the Cep@TCPP-MOF (Fig. 9d-e). The produced TCPP-MOF functioned as a carrier for loading Cep and as a targeting mechanism for its distribution into HCC tissues. In vitro tests revealed that Huh-7 and HepG2 cells can bind Cep@TCPP-MOF and collapse upon exposure to SDT (Fig. 9f). While TCPP increases mitochondrial damage by generating ROS under SDT conditions, the released Cep can bind to an inactive form of the translocator protein (TSPO), a mitochondrial membrane transporter (Fig. 9g). In vivo experiments also validated the anti-HCC efficacy of the Cep@TCPP-MOF (US). Subsequent exploration revealed that the Cep@TCPP-MOF therapy causes marked pyroptosis, which inhibits HCC. This work presents a sonodynamic-chemotherapy nanoplatform that exhibits sonodynamic responsiveness, enabling the release of Cep and TCPP from Cep-loaded TCPP-MOF. Together, the ROS produced by TCPP-SDT and the Cep-driven mitochondrial damage driven by TSPO inhibition induce pyroptosis, effectively exerting anti-HCC effects.

SDT-CDT for liver cancer treatment

Till now, many ROS-generated therapeutic methods have been thoroughly investigated, including SDT, PDT and CDT. Among these, SDT provides clear benefits as a non-invasive therapy option for deep-seated malignancies like HCC because of US superior tissue penetration and advantageous biosafety profile. Low-intensity US activates sonosensitizers during SDT, causing them to interact with ambient oxygen to generate highly cytotoxic ROS and cause localized cytotoxicity. The use of SDT for tumor therapy has shown promise in numerous trials, but many obstacles remain before it can be used in practice. Traditional sonosensitizers have poor solubility, high clearance rates and potential toxicity, which limit their effectiveness in SDT and raise safety concerns. Second, the TME became hypoxic due to fast tumor growth-induced oxygen consumption and aberrant vasculature-induced insufficient oxygen supply. This significantly limits the effectiveness of oxygen-dependent SDT. Cancer cells are shielded from oxidative stress by reducing GSH in the TME, which hinders SDT-induced cell death. To achieve effective SDT, it is crucial to investigate new sonosensitizers that can control the TME, exhibit minimal toxicity and demonstrate high efficacy.

SDT monotherapy may also occasionally be insufficient to successfully and consistently battle HCC due to its high malignancy and complexity. Therefore, there is an urgent need to create a combination of SDT therapy techniques to improve therapeutic outcomes. In this regard, SDT-CDT has shown great promise in treating cancer and several preclinical studies have also confirmed its potential for Liver Cancer treatment. Meng et al. utilized SDT-CDT-based NPs, bimetallic FeMoO4, which was camouflaged with Sp94 peptide (Fig. 10a). Here, multivalent metallic elements, the nanoplatform not only decomposes H2O2 to create oxygen and alleviate tumor hypoxia but also depletes GSH to inactivate GSH peroxides 4, which increases SDT. Meanwhile, the NPs also trigger the Fenton reaction, generating hydroxyl radicals that contribute to CDT (Fig. 10b-c). In both subcutaneous and orthotopic HCC models, the nanoplatform exhibits vigorous anti-tumor activity under US irradiation with excellent biosafety, indicating a viable treatment approach for HCC (Fig. 10d-e). The results also provide fresh perspectives on how to modify the TME to enhance US-triggered multimodal cancer treatment.

Li et al. developed an innovative multimodal image-guided phthalocyanine-iron-based complex, FeS2@PcD, functioning as a theranostic nanoreactor as illustrated in (Fig. 10f), which is modulated by the dual effects of H+ and H2O2 within the TME. FeS2@PcD demonstrated exceptional proficiency in tumor FI, and reacted with H+ to liberate PcD and generate Fe2+, which H2O2 subsequently oxidized to yield Fe3+. Aside from the fluorescence signal, the MR signal and sonosensitizer activity of FeS2@PcD transitioned from the “OFF” state to the “ON” state under the programmable modulation of H+ and H2O2 (Fig. 10g-h). The DCFH-DA staining analysis revealed that HePG2 cells treated with the probe exhibited robust ROS generation. At the same time, with integration of the US, it showed a superior response, as depicted in (Fig. 10i). Furthermore, this probe demonstrated excellent imaging capabilities, as demonstrated by in vivo and ex vivo studies, with strong fluorescence. (Fig. 10j). The combination of the SDT with CDT significantly inhibited tumor growth by apoptosis and increased the survival rates of the mice suffering from Liver Cancer as depicted in (Fig. 10k). The program-regulated strategy utilizing FeS2@PcD enabled specific dual-modality fluorescence and MR imaging-guided SDT-CDT, demonstrating a viable multimodal approach to treat Liver Cancer.

Similarly, Feng et al. utilized heterojunction-based sensitizers which have Fe-doped CDs in treating liver cancer. Because of better electron-hole separation, Fe-CDs@Cu2O showed improved SDT effects. Furthermore, the addition of Fe ions to CDs enhances Fenton-like reactions with Cu ions in Cu2O, thereby synergistically improving the impacts of CDT. Additionally, Fe-CDs@Cu2O showed rapid GSH depletion, thereby reducing TME resistance. Tumors are successfully removed by single-drug injection, ultrasonic (US) irradiation, and substantial GSH depletion, along with high rates of 1O2 and radical •OH produced by Fe-CDs@Cu2O. This novel heterojunction sonosensitizer provides a promising avenue for clinical anti-tumor treatment.

Overall, the discussion of recent SDT-CDT-based nanoplatforms reveals a convergent therapeutic rationale, including simultaneous modulation of hypoxia in perfused hepatic tissues, redox buffering, and US-based amplification of ROS generation. Despite differences in nanomaterial composition, these systems exploit elevated levels of H2O2 in the intratumoral zone, GSH overexpression, and amplification of US-responsive ROS generation via Fenton reactions and sonodynamic effects. Although these studies share a unifying approach, they differ substantially in their mechanistic approaches and potential suitability for the heterogeneous TME of the liver. Bimetallic oxides such as FeMoO4 primarily depend on sustained oxygenation and alleviated hypoxia, which reinforce SDT efficacy in cirrhotic liver tumors. In contrast, FeS2@PcD employs pH- and H2O2-responsive Fe release, enabling coordination of sonodynamic activation with Fenton reactions and multimodal imaging for real-time monitoring and therapeutic synchronization in moderately perfused liver lesions. While heterojunction-based SSs of Fe-CDs@Cu2O is a distinctive study in which electron-hole separations amplify ROS generation, and Fe-Cu lead to synergistic Fenton-like reaction and rapid depletion of GSH, ultimately enhancing the CDT efficacy. This charge separation is advantageous in heterogeneous hypoxic regions and is relatively independent of oxygen supply. Nevertheless, all these materials utilize the metal-based sensitizers, which can lead to metal toxicity, a key issue in the rapid uptake and clearance of Kupffer cells in the liver. Also, all these rely on H2O2 availability, iron metabolism and US penetration in the perfused, unstable fibrotic HCC regions, which still need probable improvements for clinical translation.

These sonosensitizers can improve the precision of orthotopic liver tumor treatment by combining US and PAI for dual localization, offering a promising therapeutic option for deep tumors.

SDT-immunotherapy for liver cancer treatment

ROS-mediated SDT is a promising treatment option for deep-seated tumors due to its excellent tissue penetration. However, performing in situ SDT is challenging due to the unavoidable accumulation of sonosensitizers in normal tissues, which can also induce sonodynamic adverse effects. To address these issues, combining SDT with immunotherapy is a promising strategy for cancer theranostics. For instance, Sun et al. designed HMME-RGD@C3F8 NBs for combinatorial SDT-immunotherapy of liver HCC (LIHC). Here, Cu2+ was loaded onto these NPs, further enhancing SDT performance by elevating oxidative stress in the TME. RGD similarly improved the permeability of the leaky vasculature, thereby increasing the selectivity of the material in the HCC region. Excellent therapeutic results can be obtained when PTT is combined with the other phototheranostic techniques, especially PDT.

PTAs that preferentially accumulate in tumor tissues because of their distinct metabolic characteristics are administered using this method. The PTAs produce localized heat in the TME in response to light at a particular wavelength. Through processes like necrosis (traumatic cell death), apoptosis (programmed cell death) and disruption of the blood supply of tumor, this hyperthermia causes cancer cell death. PTT has drawbacks that affect its clinical application, despite its potential. One major drawback is the depth of light penetration; the typically employed near-infrared (NIR) light in PTT can only penetrate a few cm into tissue, which limits the usefulness of technique for deep-seated tumors. Furthermore, PTT’s effect is transient, as tumor cell death ceases once the laser is switched off. Compared to conventional medicines, PTT has a higher specificity, less invasiveness and less adverse effects. It is a flexible therapeutic approach that increases tumor perfusion, improves drug delivery, overcomes hypoxia-related resistance, increases cell membrane permeability and can be used with CDT or SDT for increased anticancer efficacy.

CDT-starvation therapy for liver cancer treatment

Tumor starvation treatment is a promising strategy because it blocks the transport of oxygen and nutrients through tumor blood vessels via embolization, ultimately inhibiting tumor growth. This is consistent with the Warburg effect, which states that tumor cells rely more on glucose than normal cells.

The glucose oxidase (GOD)-mediated glucose metabolism may convert glucose to gluconic acid and H2O2, thereby starving the tumor, as glucose is essential for tumor metabolism. The creation of low pH (gluconate) and the formation of H2O2 also favor Fenton/Fenton-like reactions, leading to the formation of the highly phototoxic •OH and increasing the efficiency of CDT. Therefore, designing a medical system and offering medicines that can be administered for starvation therapy and exert Fenton action is very ingenious. The metabolic pathways of tumor cells differ from those of most normal tissue cells. The resulting TME provides unique physicochemical conditions for targeted treatment of malignancies.

Analysis of clinical specimens and databases reveals that HCC, a kind of Liver Cancer, is susceptible to cuproptosis. On this basis, Han et al. used a synergistic strategy of CDT-starvation therapy in which a copper-based nanotube (NT) including copper ionophores is engineered to promote enhanced cuproptosis. When used alongside starvation therapy, the NT markedly suppressed energy metabolism in HCC cells (Fig. 16a). The CDT effect of the NT was assessed using the measurement of ROS production and glutathione depletion. The DCFH-DA probe revealed that Cu HAT-2DG-ES induced the most significant increase in ROS levels among the treatments, as evidenced by the highest fluorescence intensity (Fig. 16b). In contrast, Cu HAT-2DG and Cu HAT + Ele induce a substantial increase in ROS levels. Subsequently, FCM demonstrated a measurable difference in ROS levels across groups, corroborating the fluorescence microscopy findings. The Cell viability was increased substantially after adding GSH and NAC, especially with Cu-HAT-2G-ES (Fig. 16c). The results indicate that Cu2+−induced depletion of GSH exacerbated oxidative stress and cuproptosis, leading to ROS production and DLAT aggregation, thereby amplifying cuproptosis and the Fenton reaction. The therapeutic effects of the alone and synergistic therapies were also evaluated, with starvation therapy and CDT alone showing specific, albeit incomplete, tumor inhibition, while synergistic Cu HAT-2DG ES showed complete tumor inhibition, further validating their therapeutic effects (Fig. 16d). Also, tumor weight did not differ significantly across groups (Fig. 16e). H &E staining revealed that apoptosis was most pronounced in the superior region, leading to the death of superior tumor cells (Fig. 16f). This study provides an excellent platform for the controlled release of localized Cu2 + ions by leveraging the redox reactivity of copper, which undergoes cuproptosis. Here, this platform offers precise control over redox reactions through the coordination chemistry of copper (II), enabling gradual ion leaching in acidic TME. Furthermore, the integration of starvation therapy disrupts metabolism, amplifying ROS and providing excellent therapeutic outcomes. In conjunction with synergetic cuproptosis, the Fenton reaction of CDT and starvation therapy, the NT offers significant insights into the potential treatment of HCC.

Many of the dynamic therapies discussed in Chaps. 2–4 have shown significant progress, but still lag in translation from preclinical to clinical settings. There are many challenges, even at the preclinical stage, such as the complex, high-power setup for US cavitation; limited biocompatible sonosensitizers; an unclear mechanism; PDT; ROS production in a hypoxic environment; light penetration; and, for CDT, metal toxicity, which limits their applications. To address these issues, integrative techniques have been formulated and utilized at the preclinical stage, which have shown excellent promise. SDT-chemotherapy has now been considered for some tumor treatments and is under clinical development. Still, the issues of complex development, complex systems, exorbitant costs, and the diversity and selectivity of nanomaterials hinder their clinical translation.

Emerging dynamic therapies

In the above sections, dynamic and combinatorial therapies are discussed in detail, with case studies for Liver Cancer treatment. These therapies are quite promising, but their research is still at the validation level. These therapies all have limitations during preclinical implementation, including biocompatibility, biosafety, potential off-target effects of the material, and a complex, cost-burdened system. To deal with these issues, some emerging therapies have shown promise in the treatment of wounds and some diseases, which can be utilized in the future for deep-seated tumors. In this section, some emerging therapies with their case studies are briefly discussed Fig. 17.

Piezodynamic therapy (PZDT)

PZDT, a promising therapeutic avenue closely linked to piezocatalysis, offers possible solutions to the problems of conventional biomedical methods. The ambient piezocatalysis of water, oxygen, or other biologically relevant molecules could generate therapeutically beneficial species, such as ROS and oxidized metabolites. The combination of mechanical forces and electrical forces, along with reaction products, may lead to bacterial or cell apoptosis, necrosis and stimulation of cellular Ca2+ influx can boost regeneration by acting on cell components (such as proteins, lipids, or deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)). One of the properties of most cancers, especially Liver Cancer, is hypoxia; piezoelectric NPs (PENPs) can potentially improve the case by creating a small electrical field in treating tumors. In fact, mechanically stressed PENPs can decrease hypoxia because of the resulting ROS or at least a light electrical field. Amelioration of hypoxic conditions results in diminished levels of hypoxia-inducible factor α (HIFα), correlating with decreased tumor invasion and metastatic capability. This link is well-established, as reducing hypoxia generally decreases HIFα expression, thereby lowering the likelihood of invasion and metastasis. When a piezoelectric material is subjected to mechanical stress, it generates an electric field. This imbalance triggers two categories of redox reaction, producing supplementary ROS. The surface charge buildup on the material attracts electrolytes, initiating a secondary reaction. This interaction facilitates water splitting, leading to oxygen production.

Conversely, the promise of safe implantable bioelectronics is made possible by the special biodegradable qualities of organic piezoelectric biomaterials, which include protein-based polymers (like collagen and chitin), amino acids (like glycine and diphenylalanine) and polysaccharides (like cellulose and chitin), as well as synthetic polymers like piezoelectric polylactic acid (PLLA). Therefore, research on biodegradable PLLA for a range of applications, including self-stimulated tissue scaffolds, biodegradable force sensors and biodegradable ultrasonic devices, has recently been reported by Chorsi et al.. Despite its exceptional safety profile and biodegradability, the moderate piezoelectric constant of PLLA, compared with that of conventional piezoelectric ceramics like PZT, precludes its use for applications requiring high piezoelectric efficiency, such as powerful ultrasonic transducers or ultrasensitive sensors. This novel approach enabled Paclitaxel (PTX) delivery across the BBB, and the glycine-PCL device demonstrated superior GBM tumor suppression. Many clinical implants need to function for several months to years, even though the device’s biodegradability allows its active lifespan to be customized from a few days to almost a month. Therefore, identifying a safe, biodegradable encapsulation material that maintains the integrity and potency of the glycine-PCL nanofiber coating over extended periods is a vital next step for clinical translation.

PZDT, as a standalone therapy, has rarely been utilized in the treatment of tumors. It has predominantly been used in the biomedical arena, i.e., treating cancer with synergistic drugs. Zhan et al., who employed an electrotherapy mode in breast cancer with the PZDT, brought an excellent synergistic modality. Similarly, in another study that employed a chemotherapy mode in breast cancer, the PZDT brought about apoptotic cell death in cancerous cells. Tian et al. also used Mn-ZnO with PZDT to induce ferroptosis against cancer, demonstrating excellent ROS generation and tumor suppression. In a current study, Jin et al. utilized a synergistic strategy denoted as Sono-PZDT. In this process, TTFs have been utilized as injectable, biocompatible, dynamic piezocatalysts for PCT, along with the US piezoelectric energy harvester (U-PEH). The UPEH maximized the conversion of acoustic energy to electrical energy using piezoelectric composites of high-performance lead-free potassium sodium niobate ceramics. The convenience and comfort of modifying the U-PEH are ascribed to the presence of two sets of half-ring electrodes and to the design of a flexible printed circuit board that offers a wide range of alternating electric fields. Potassium sodium niobate injectable NPs also exhibit enhanced piezoelectric properties, serving as piezocatalysts that catalyze several redox reactions. Thus, the interaction between the US stimulation of the TTFs and the US stimulation causes the U-PEH to produce an alternating electric field, which determines the movement of the charge carriers and enhances ROS production by maintaining an electric field within the activated NPs. This directly interferes with critical bodily functions. The combination of retrospective physical therapy is a dual-fold synergistic approach to reduce systemic toxicity and maximize therapeutic efficacy. This was illustrated in vitro and in vivo, where the combination therapy effectively decreased tumor cell proliferation and tumor growth, induced tumor cell death and modulated the tumor immune microenvironment. It is a less invasive complementary therapy and there is hope that more patients will be adherent and that doctors will be mainstays Fig. 18.

PZDT has shown remarkable potential for various biomedical applications, as many Piezo materials have been used in wound healing. Recent preclinical studies have demonstrated that Piezo materials have also been used in oncology, especially for deep-seated tumors. PZDT, as it does not rely on oxygen and uses US or mechanical and electrical stimulation, overcomes the hypoxia-related and deep-penetration issues associated with the above-discussed dynamic therapies. Although it has shown promise in GBM treatment preclinically, it is emerging, and its research in the biomedical field is at a preliminary stage, so extensive validation is required for the method and piezo materials. Piezo-nanomaterials still have uncertain biosafety, especially inorganic materials, which can cause issues of degradation and renal clearability, leading to systemic toxicity. As Chorsi et al. discussed, PLLA has a low piezoelectricity, which somewhat limits its therapeutic efficacy. Furthermore, in vivo mechanisms are not fully understood, including ROS generation and ion reflux, and the dose is not optimized. Before implementing this approach, these challenges should be addressed.

Microwave dynamic therapy (MWDT)

MWDT has recently emerged as a viable, non-invasive, energy-driven therapeutic technique that integrates MW irradiation with catalytic or photochemical energy conversion to destroy tumor tissue selectively. MWDT primarily operates by harnessing ROS and microwave-induced heat and by leveraging microwave-induced catalytic activation of sensitive nanomaterials. Unlike the conventional process of ablating cancerous cells with heat, which is the only way to kill them, ROS enhances the cytotoxicity of MWDT. Microwave polarization and nanomaterial dipole-vibration catalysts catalyze ROS generation and localized electronic excitation in MWDT. The separation of electrons and holes, as well as rapid dielectric loss, has been observed in materials under microwave conditions, particularly in Fe3O4, TiO2, MnO2 and CuO. MWDT has become a promising clinical strategy for the treatment of deep-seated tumors, involving both dynamic treatment and hyperthermia. Compared to near-infrared light or Ultrasound, microwaves have greater penetration, allowing effective treatment of deep-seated tumors without being hampered by bone-air reflections. One significant benefit of microwaves in cancer treatment is their exceptional ability to penetrate biological tissues with minimal hindrance. In addition to their anticancer effects, microwaves have been shown to have osteogenic, anti-inflammatory, and anti-infective properties. In recent years, it has been utilized in several cancer treatments in preclinical studies and has shown excellent therapeutic efficiency.

For instance, Wang et al. presented the design of a high-efficiency microwave sensitizer based on TiMOF (TM) for MWDT. When subjected to microwave irradiation, TM can generate heat and harmful ROS, It was also observed that, incorporating Ni NPs imparts MRI capabilities to nanomaterials. Furthermore, this approach with these NPs exhibited excellent antitumor efficiency. This study introduces an MW sensitizer utilizing TM for the first time. It preliminarily investigates how COF coating enhances TM’s MW dynamic sensitization, offering a novel concept for advancing high-potential MW sensitizers. Similarly, Zhou et al. reported a MWDT-based approach using Cu-Cy NPs that may efficiently kill HCT15 colorectal cancer (CRC) cells, with an average IC-50 value of 20 µg/mL upon exposure to MW radiation. These NPs demonstrated that Cu-Cy NPs have a safe and promising therapeutic potential for MWDT in deep-seated tumors. They also successfully impair tumor cell growth by inducing ferroptosis, offering a viable approach to overcoming cancer resistance.

Although microwave dynamic therapy has shown excellent promise in eliminating cancer cells, some limitations remain to be addressed. Combinatorial therapies have demonstrated superior efficacy in resolving these limitations. For instance, in 2024, Shu et al. devised a new synergistic approach, MWDT-CDT, to treat cancer. MgFe2O4@ZOL, a microwave-responsive nanoplatform, was built. In the acidic TME, MgFe2O4@ZOL NPs liberate the Fe3+, Mg2+ and zoledronic acid (ZOL) cargos. CDT occurs from Fe3+ ability to deplete intracellular GSH and catalyze H2O2 to produce •OH. Furthermore, the microwave significantly increased ROS generation, enabling MWDT. Additionally, ZOL and Mg2+ encourage osteoblast differentiation. MgFe2O4@ZOL NPs may also be able to target and heat tumor tissue specifically, improving the results of microwave thermal treatment (MTT). Synergistic targeting, GSH-depletion-enhanced CDT, MWDT and selective MTT demonstrated notable anticancer activity and bone regeneration in both in vitro and in vivo studies. Patients with lung cancer who have bone metastases may benefit from this multimodal combo therapy. Similarly, MWDT with magnetic and thermodynamic therapies has also been used to treat cancer, but further research is required to see the possible anti-cancerous effects of these strategies.

MWDT demonstrates enhanced penetrative capabilities compared to conventional inducers, such as NIR light. They are distinguished by a lack of bone-air reflection, negligible interference and improved preclinical safety. Research has demonstrated that microwaves possess inherent characteristics that facilitate osteogenesis, exhibit anti-inflammatory and anti-infective properties and demonstrate anticancer efficacy, all while allowing repeated administration. Another benefit that MWDT provides is that, in addition to damaging mitochondria and other cellular components, these ROS trigger the cell death cascade by inducing oxidative stress. Additionally, mild hyperthermia induced by microwave absorption further enhances metabolic processes and membrane permeability, promoting ROS-mediated cytotoxicity. The oscillating electromagnetic induction further enhances interfacial polarization and charge redistribution, accelerating the reaction even in acidic conditions and at low TME. Although MWDT offers many advantages, its research in the biomedical field is still emerging and needs exploration. Secondly, until the MWDT agents have shown moderate ROS production. Thirdly, the clearance of MWDT-based nanomaterials from the body and their uneven distribution are causing uneven treatment. To address all these limitations, extensive research on that dynamic therapy is necessary. All these issues cannot be resolved in a single study or research project, so extensive research is needed to develop MWDT-based drugs that can be beneficial in the future as well. However, these studies have opened new avenues for the nontherapeutic and oncology researchers.

MDT

An external magnetic field activates specific nanomaterials to produce ROS, enabling precise tumor ablation. MDT is a new non-invasive dynamic treatment approach.

MDT mechanism

MDT emphasizes the non-thermal effects triggered by the magnetic field, rather than the typical M-TDT, which mainly depend on thermal effects. The production of ROS by nanomaterials for tumor treatment is enabled by the synergistic interactions among magnetic, mechanical, electrical and chemical properties. The magneto-piezocatalytic effect, which uses the complementary interaction between magnetostrictive and piezoelectric materials, has been the subject of contemporary research. This overcomes the drawbacks of conventional dynamic therapies in deep tissue applications by establishing a novel channel for converting magnetic, mechanical, electrical and chemical energy. The periodic mechanical deformation of magnetostrictive materials in the presence of an AMF is the fundamental process of magneto-piezocatalysis. Piezoelectric polarisation results from this deformation’s conversion of magnetic energy into mechanical energy, which is subsequently transmitted to the piezoelectric material via the interface. A local electric field generated by this polarization drives redox reactions, producing ROS. This technique offers a novel non-invasive therapeutic option for deep tumors by enabling MDT to efficiently trigger ROS generation without needing external light or Ultrasound sources.

MDT for cancer treatment

MDT alone has still not been employed in the treatment of cancers. However, some research using synergistic approaches, such as MDT-PZDT, MDT-TDT and MDT-MWDT, has shown significant therapeutic results. For instance, Ge and colleagues created CFO-BFO, an effective core-shell nanoplatform based on magneto-piezocatalysis. This nanoplatform’s outer shell is composed of bismuth ferrite (BiFeO3, BFO), a material with remarkable piezoelectric capabilities and its core is composed of cobalt ferrite (CoFe2O4, CFO), a magnetostrictive material with exceptional performance. A considerable piezoelectric potential is created at the interface and redox reactions are triggered when the CFO core experiences substantial mechanical deformation under the influence of an alternating magnetic field. This distortion is effectively transferred to the BFO shell. In particular, O2 molecules are reduced to form •O2− in the cathode region, while water molecules are oxidized to produce •OH in the anode region (Fig. 19a-b). As they accumulate rapidly in tumor tissue, these extremely potent radicals oxidize proteins, lipids and DNA, causing tumor cells to necrotize and undergo apoptosis. In animal tests, the CFO-BFO nanoplatform dramatically outperformed the untreated control group in destroying tumor tissue in tumor-bearing mice after 5–6 days of AMF treatment. The promise of MDT in treating cancer was further demonstrated by this method, which successfully increased the survival span of tumor-bearing mice from about 20 days in the control group to more than 40 days. Similarly, Gao et al. demonstrated the use of the MDT with combination of Chemo-PTT in the treatment of the 4T1 cancers in mice model. Kolovskaya et al. also used chemo-MDT to treat 4T1 tumors. Recently, Li et al. also used MDT-CDT (Fig. 19c) for tumor treatment, demonstrating promising therapeutic results. These studies of the MDT in oncotherapy have demonstrated its immense promise. Although only a few studies have shown significant results, we still need to go further to assess the outcomes of novel magnetic-field-active materials in other organs of the animal body Fig. 20.

The advent of MDT provides a new non-invasive method for treating deep tumors. MDT leverages the high tissue penetration of magnetic fields and their distinct energy conversion mechanisms, offering greater application prospects for the precision treatment of deep tumors than PDT and SDT, which are constrained by the penetration depth of light or Ultrasound. But even with all of the potential of MDT, the discipline is still in its infancy and several significant problems remain unresolved. First, to guarantee their controlled metabolism and degradability in vivo, biocompatibility and long-term stability of NPs need to be carefully assessed. Second, magnetic field characteristics (e.g., strength and frequency) and nanomaterial features (e.g., magnetic-mechanical-electrical conversion efficiency) must be optimally matched to maximize ROS formation and thereby boost therapeutic efficiency. To reduce potential toxicity risks associated with long-term accumulation, more research is required to examine the retention, degradation and excretion pathways of the nanomedicine across various tissue environments. Additionally, the in vivo metabolism and clearance mechanisms of nanomedicines remain unknown. To advance MDT towards clinical application, future research should focus on improving energy conversion efficiency, optimizing material design and systematically evaluating biocompatibility.

Challenges

Dynamic therapies, such as SDT, PDT, and CDT, have shown excellent promise in preclinical studies but still face many limitations.

SDT and PDT both share the common issue that their performance is suppressed in hypoxic environments, and in liver tumors, the environment is highly hypoxic, which limits their efficiency. In a hypoxic environment, the ROS production drops and cavitation collapses unpredictably. Although ROS production in SDT is due to FUS, cavitation, and sonolysis, which are independent of oxygen, the resulting nanomaterials exhibit phototoxicity, biosafety concerns, and other side effects. For SDT, a highly advanced US system is needed even for the preclinical mice therapy, which still limits its application. Other challenges include the obscure mechanism of sonosensitizers, the limited diversity of biocompatible sonosensitizers, renal clearance, and unpredictable cavitation.

Similarly, PDT PSs also don’t function effectively under hypoxic conditions. This issue has recently been addressed using nanomedicine that can deliver exogenous or endogenous oxygen, normalize tumor vasculature, or suppress the HIF-1 pathway. Another issue was limited light penetration, which was improved by using TPE and NIR responsive NPs. But the nanomaterials utilized are still at a conceptual stage and require further in-depth research, including biocompatibility, NPs leakage, and selective targeting.

Unlike SDT and PDT, CDT relies on intrinsic factors, so it is less affected by hypoxia; however, it still faces several challenges: suboptimal TME, where H2O2 levels remain imbalanced, hindering CDT efficiency; long-term metal-ion toxicity due to the use of the iron, copper, manganese or transitional metals for Fenton or Fenton like reactions, which causes the issue of renal clearance; off-target effects; biocompatibility and retention of NPs; off-target catalysis; and kinetic constraints.

Combinatorial therapies have partially resolved these challenges. Such as SDT-chemotherapy, SDT-PTT, SDT-immunotherapy resolved the issue of the hypoxia and tissue penetration, but on the other hand, the cumulative side effects of the nanocarriers, such as the long-term toxicity, tumor selective accumulation and controlled release of the NPs; complex system, complex development of drug; unclear integrative mechanism and exorbitant cost are the challenges at preclinical levels.

Emerging therapies such as PZDT, MWDT, and MDT have shown promise in resolving the above issues, but many challenges remain. Such as in PZDT, the low piezoelectric constraints, potential overheating and limited PZDT biocompatible agents, in MWDT, imbalanced ROS production and low in vivo research and in MDT, the uncontrolled mechanism, optimal matching of magnetic field with operation of NPs and limited NPs for in vivo research are the potential issues hindering preclinically.

Dynamic therapies depend on external or internal physical stimuli, such as Ultrasound, chemical reactions, light, microwaves, magnetic fields, or mechanical forces, to activate therapeutic agents. Accurate, homogeneous stimulation in deep tissues is challenging, potentially leading to variable therapy outcomes. So, further research is needed for the controllable stimuli. To prevent off-target effects, sensitizers or nanoparticles must selectively accumulate in tumors. Clinical effectiveness is limited by nonspecific dispersion, fast clearance, or ineffective targeting. Emerging NPs make up a large number of dynamic therapeutic agents. Their systemic toxicity, potential immunogenicity and long-term biocompatibility remain serious safety concerns. Differences in tumor oxygenation, pH, density, or vascularization can reduce ROS production or therapeutic activation, leading to erratic patient responses. It can be challenging to scale up from the lab to the clinic and manufacture these therapeutic medicines with consistent quality. Particle size, stability and therapeutic efficacy can all be impacted by variations in synthesis techniques. Additionally, most of the above-discussed modalities have shown promise but remain limited, hindering their translation to clinical trials. It should be noted that the efficient delivery of meds to the tumor site is a significant challenge for maximizing CDT efficacy. Similarly, in SDT, most SSs are hydrophobic and are not readily degraded or rapidly cleared. SDT SSs sometimes cannot generate substantial ROS in the liver TME and GSH environment, limiting their therapeutic effectiveness. Combinatorial therapies have shown strong promise and therapeutic efficacy in addressing these issues. However, we can encounter problems, such as combinatorial therapies, which may lead to the use of additional drugs that affect pharmacokinetics and side effects, and may also complicate translation. Clinical translation of some dynamic modalities, such as SDT and PDT, is progressing and, when combined with other therapies, many clinical trial phases are advancing. But after completing these trial phases, we may encounter adverse issues as well. Still, most of these strategies are emerging and further preclinical research is needed to develop these nanomedicines in accordance with foreseeable clinical standards.

Perspective and future directions

Even though scientists have worked hard to develop safe and efficient novel sensitizers and have produced many encouraging results in preclinical research, several pivotal problems and obstacles remain to be resolved before they can be successfully applied in clinical settings. These include the adverse side effects of sensitizing molecules, their effectiveness in hypoxic conditions, their mechanisms, their evaluation, and intricate preparation methods; deep tissue penetration of stimuli, off-target effects, and immune responsiveness. Thus, future studies ought to concentrate more on:

Effectiveness in hypoxia

Hypoxia is the main issue in treating liver tumors with dynamic therapies. To address this issue, synergistic approaches are the best option, such as SDT-chemotherapy, SDT-PTT, PDT-PTT, CDT-PTT, and CDT-starvation therapy. Future research should focus on developing drugs with synergistic potential for preclinical studies. Secondly, alleviating the hypoxia by enhancing oxygen content in the tumor. It can be done by in situ oxygen generation using H2O2, or splitting water with catalase nanozymes or by photosynthesising microorganisms such as cyanobacteria, microalgae or chlorella. In 2025, Zheng et al. utilized microalgae with a PDT agent in hypoxic tumors, thereby achieving simultaneous O2 generation. Thirdly, it can be achieved using oxygen-releasing nanomaterials in combination with sensitizers or by developing oxygen-releasing sensitizers, such as BLICP@O2, which can release oxygen and overcome hypoxia. Fourthly, using HIF-signalizing inhibitors with the sensitizer can help resist hypoxia. In the case of other integrative treatments as described above, such as TAE, these can increase NP retention and improve CDT efficacy but, in turn, exacerbate oxygen depletion, which can severely decrease PDT efficacy in the liver while promoting the HIF-mediated angiogenic rebound. So, future research should focus on embolization-induced ischemia and the integration of Vaso-modulation, which is specific to cirrhotic-derived liver cancers.

Tackle suboptimal TME

CDT relies on Fenton or Fenton-like reactions that require a sufficient amount of H2O2, which is naturally not enough for the complete eradication of liver tumors. CDT in liver cancer treatment is particularly challenged by the unique antioxidant buffering capacity of hepatocytes and elevated catalytic activity, along with high GSH levels. To address this issue, synergistic mechanisms, such as CDT-starvation therapy, can be an excellent option. So, future research should focus on the development of the CDT platforms that can incorporate liver-specific amplification approaches, such as GSH depleting components, Kupffer cells-mediated ROS generation, and metabolic rewiring that can exploit metal ion dysregulation. These approaches can address the resistance mechanisms in liver cancer treatment, which are far less pronounced in extrahepatic tumors. In 2024, Asantewwa et al. showed that GSH is the fulcrum in balancing redox-antioxidant buffering, and its deletion can overcome dysregulated antioxidant mechanisms, thereby sensitizing liver tumors to CDT. In 2024, Li et al. reported that metabolic rewiring in liver TME can help balance metal ions in accordance with the therapeutic module. So, future research should focus on the deployment of nanoplatforms that incorporate these features to improve treatment.

Deep-light penetration

Limited light penetration is a significant challenge, especially in PDT therapies and in combinatory PDT-PTT. This limitation can be addressed by using the TPE or NIR-II PSs, self-luminescent NPs, chemiluminescent NPs, Cerenkov radiation (CR) and synergistic approaches. Researchers have used the indirect chemiluminescence method to design CL-PDT devices. When CL is activated by oxidation, it can be covalently modified or non-covalently assembled with PS to produce ROS by stimulating the surrounding PS through CRET. This method does not require external light irradiation to produce the PDT effect. CR is the phenomenon in which charged particles move through the medium faster than light and polarize the molecules. When these molecules interact with PSs, CRET produces ROS. CR-PDT can be an excellent option for addressing this issue. In the future, PSs based on the CR-PDT can be effective and shouldn’t be attenuated by tissues. In liver cancer, light is more attenuated than in other cancers due to rib shielding, organ depth, and the proximity of large blood vessels. The above-described methods can offer partial solutions, but delivering them to liver cancer tissues remains a significant challenge. For an effective PDT, interventional alignments such as intratumoral direct light delivery via optical fibers by a catheter system or a wireless system. Thirdly, the use of wireless-PDT can be an excellent option for addressing tissue penetration. Liu et al. in 2024 used a wireless microLED-based device to deliver two-wavelength light into a deep HCC rat model, activating the PSs even at low doses and achieving effective PDT. Fourthly, the use of synergistic therapies such as the SDT-PDT can be a great choice where the sonosensitizers with photosensitizers can reduce the dependency on light, but sonosensitizers have the issue of lower ROS production, so future research should be based upon the development of higher ROS-producing sensitizers. The energy supply for these devices hampers their use. To address this issue, metronomic PDT devices can be a great option, as Itazaki utilized in 2025 in deep organ tumor treatment. So, future research should focus on developing implantable wireless PDT (or PZDT) devices, which could be helpful.

Biocompatibility

The biocompatibility and safety of the nanomaterials used in current, synergistic, and next-generation therapies are not sufficiently well established. In the liver, the challenge observed is the dynamic, therapeutically based SSs and catalyst interactions with CYP450 and β-glucuronidases, leading to drug inactivation in hepatocytes and the generation of hepatotoxic metabolites.

Furthermore, there is a lack of thorough validation of the delivery, uptake, pharmacokinetics, and distribution in biological systems. In particular, the use of inorganic sensitizers in cells and their effects on healthy cells require further validation.

Drug delivery and targeting

Selective delivery of the sensitizers to the liver tumor tissue is a significant challenge. Although some selective modulation, selective biomarker linking, and carrier systems have been developed and utilized, further research is required to validate them fully. For instance, Oxyberberine, a specific liver cancer targeting group, has shown great promise. Its translation and validation in the dynamic therapies for liver cancer should be the research focus. Despite the ability of nanocarriers to target specific receptors or accumulate in tumors via the EPR effect, their penetration into large, poorly perfused hepatic tumors remains inadequate. Additionally, uneven distribution within a dual blood supply with high arterialization of liver complicates the uniform delivery of ROS. Therefore, effective nanoplatforms that can accumulate and penetrate tumor tissues are essential for enhancing the efficacy of dynamic therapies. Morphology- and size-deformable nanoparticles with endosomal escape potential are particularly promising due to their ability to generate ROS.

NPs degradation and hepatic cells retention

Balanced degradation and retention of the nano-sensitizers is another challenge which needs to be tackled preclinically first, and then it will be thought for the clinical means. In liver cancers, validation does not only remain at the cellular level; it should be based on whole-organ function. For instance, in SDT-chemotherapy, Cep@TCPP-MOF was utilized in treating liver cancer in mice that contain both properties with synergistic retention and degradation, which is an excellent option. So, future research should focus on developing sensitizers that are therapeutically effective without prolonged hepatic accumulation. Also, the size of the sensor or NPs is an essential factor involved in the clearance and retention. Studies have shown that a size below 5 nm can lead to rapid clearance and reduced therapeutic efficacy, while 25–70 nm can be an excellent size for balanced retention and clearance. For instance, in 2025, Kong et al.. demonstrated that NPs with a 70 nm size used in liver cancer treatment showed excellent therapeutic effects and hepatic clearance. Many sensitizers with balanced retention times have been developed; still, further research is required. The specificity, effectiveness, and safety of tumor treatment can be significantly enhanced by rationally developing and fabricating TME-responsive nano-PTs.

Clarity of mechanism

Some treatments, such as SDT, PDT-SDT, SDT-CDT and emerging therapies, are ambiguous. So, to construct realistic dynamic therapies and their sensitizers, it is first necessary to thoroughly clarify the mechanism. In SDT, the dynamics of cavitation and the mechanisms of energy deposition in perfused hepatic tumor tissues remain poorly understood. Also, future research should focus on designing a liver-specific dosimeter framework and on real-time monitoring based on cavitation and multimodal imaging, which should be stable and compatible with hepatic motion and perfusion. In liver cancer treatment, the pathways of ROS generation and their death mechanism are still not fully defined preclinically, so mechanistic studies are still required.

Development of novel sensitizers

Sensitizers remain the most widely researched today, although many of these compounds can have detrimental side effects, including significant phototoxicity and off-target effects. The development of safe and effective sensitizers with high stability, high sensitivity, and good tumor selectivity is therefore imperative Fig. 21.

Conclusion

It is impossible to overlook the adverse consequences that conventional frontline modalities have on patients. As a result, numerous researchers have developed innovative therapeutic approaches using nanomedicines. In this pursuit, dynamic therapies such as SDT, PDT, CDT, PZDT, MWDT and MDT have emerged as a novel “green” treatment that exhibit selectivity and specificity. More significantly, these modalities kill the tumor in-situ by combining the TME with traditional Fenton and Fenton-like reactions, which may have therapeutic translation potential. Numerous studies documenting novel nanomaterials that respond to external stimuli and control the Fenton reaction in specific environments have aided the development of nanomaterials as agents in biomedicine. This review highlights the latest nanomedicines used in these dynamic therapies and their therapeutic effects in Liver Cancer treatment, supported by preclinical evidence. It covers the selection of individual and combinatorial dynamic strategies and their preclinical therapeutic effectiveness when applied in response to an external stimulus. At the end, perspective strategies and their prospective insights in Liver Cancer treatment are highlighted.
