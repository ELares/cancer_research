# Engineering CRISPR System-Based Bacterial Outer Membrane Vesicle Potentiates T Cell Immunity for Enhanced Cancer Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy has revolutionized cancer treatment but only benefits a subset of patients because of insufficient infiltration and inactivation of effector T cells. Bacterial outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) can activate immunity and deliver therapeutic agents for immunotherapy. However, efficiently targeting and packaging therapeutic molecules into OMVs remains challenging. Here, the engineered E. coli BL21-derived OMVs enable the packaging of multiple genes, resulting in a 7-fold increase in DNA enrichment efficiency and gene silencing in vitro. Moreover, the engineered OMVs carrying genes encoding CXCL9 and IL12 (OMV-C9I12) reprogram tumor cells to secrete these factors, significantly enhancing T-cell chemotaxis and activation. More importantly, this system markedly inhibits tumors, extends survival, and synergizes with anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy in murine MB49 and B16F10 tumor models. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) further reveals significant upregulation of T-cell chemotaxis and activation-related pathways following OMV-C9I12 treatment. Finally, OMV-C9I12 potentiates T cell-mediated immunotherapy and suppresses the growth of bladder and breast cancer tumors in humanized mouse models. These findings highlight the potential of this engineered OMV platform for cancer gene therapy and provide novel strategies to overcome resistance to immunotherapy.

Engineering CRISPR System‐Based Bacterial Outer Membrane Vesicle Potentiates T Cell Immunity for Enhanced Cancer Immunotherapy

Abstract

This study develops a multifunctional CRISPR‐dCas9‐based OMV platform termed OMV‐C9I12, which facilitates the coexpression of CXCL9 and IL‐12 within tumor cells. This platform enhances T cell recruitment and activation, synergizes with anti‐PD‐1/PD‐L1 immunotherapy, and amplifies antitumor T cell immunity. This demonstrates significant therapeutic efficacy in a broad range of tumors and offers a promising strategy to overcome immunotherapy resistance.  

Introduction

Cancer immunotherapy, which leverages the immune system to recognize and eliminate cancer cells precisely, has revolutionized cancer treatment.[  ] To date, the most effective immunotherapy is immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy. ICBs targeting programmed cell death‐1 (PD‐1)/PD‐1 ligand (PD‐L1) reinvigorate effector T cells and demonstrate tremendous clinical benefits in multiple advanced tumors.[  ] Nevertheless, the overall efficacy of ICB therapy remains limited, with response rates reaching 20%–30%,[  ] particularly in noninflamed solid tumors.[  ] Increasing evidence has demonstrated that insufficient infiltration and inactivation of effector T cells contribute to the failure of ICB therapy.[  ] To address this, new strategies that can effectively recruit and activate T cells in the tumor microenvironment (TME) are urgently needed.

Bacteria have been reported to be among the most effective stimuli for triggering the immune system.[  ] Previous studies have used live bacteria or engineered probiotics for cancer therapy.[  ] A successful example is the use of Bacillus Calmette‐Guérin (BCG) for the treatment of bladder cancer.[  ] However, the use of live bacteria as therapeutic agents in clinical treatments presents substantial challenges, mainly due to potential infection‐related toxicity and biosafety concerns,[  ] which arise from the uncontrollable proliferation of bacteria and difficulties in fully eliminating live bacteria from the body. Moreover, bacterial bioactive components, rather than complete live bacteria, have been widely researched and developed and retain some of the advantages of bacteria without the risk of bacterial infection.[  ] Bacterial outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) serve as promising representatives, which are formed by the budding of the outer membrane of Gram‐negative bacteria and consist of the cellular components of the bacterial periplasm and outer membrane,[  ] including lipopolysaccharides (LPS), lipids and lipoproteins, as well as some intracellular components, such as proteins and DNA.[  ] OMVs have been demonstrated to activate immune responses due to their abundance of pathogen‐associated molecular patterns (PAMPs).[  ] Furthermore, with the rapid development of synthetic biology, OMVs can be repurposed as vehicles for delivering therapeutic agents into tumors for cancer immunotherapy.[  ] Engineered OMVs carrying PD1 plasmids via electroporation enhanced the effector function of T cells.[  ] However, efficiently targeting and packaging therapeutic molecules into OMVs remains challenging. Despite their potential to suppress tumors and promote antitumor immunity, OMVs require further optimization for effective cancer treatment.

The clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)‐associated protein 9 (Cas9) system provides a powerful gene editing tool derived from the adaptive immune system for bacteria and archaea.[  ] Nuclease‐dead Cas9 (dCas9) is generated through mutations in the Cas9 protein, preserving its ability to target and bind to specific DNA sequences with no endonuclease activity. Additionally, the applications of the CRISPR/dCas9 system have expanded and diversified across different species.[  ] However, whether the CRISPR/dCas9 system can be expressed and delivered in OMVs while binding to target DNA has not yet been explored.

Here, we engineered a CRISPR/dCas9‐based OMV platform that delivers inflammatory CXC‐chemokine ligand 9 (CXCL9) and interleukin‐12 (IL‐12) to tumor cells to increase T cell recruitment and activation in the TME (Figure  1A). Using three preclinical cancer models, we demonstrated that OMV‐C9I12 effectively enhances the antitumor effects of OMVs and synergizes with anti‐PD1/PD‐L1 therapy. These findings highlight the great potential of this engineered OMV platform in cancer therapy and provide novel strategies to overcome resistance to immunotherapy.

Results

Characterization of Wild‐Type OMVs and Their Ability to Activate Antitumor T Cell Immunity

We first isolated OMVs from three different bacterial strains, namely, MG1655, Nissle 1917, and BL21, all of which are Escherichia coli species cultured under identical conditions. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and dynamic light scattering (DLS) confirmed that all the OMVs were nanosized lipid bilayer vesicles, with average diameters ranging from 150 to 200 nm (Figure 1B; Figure S1A, Supporting Information). To compare the tumor suppression efficacy of OMVs from different sources, mice bearing MB49 tumors were intratumorally injected with PBS or each type of OMV (Figure 1C). Compared with the PBS control, all the OMVs inhibited tumor growth and reduced tumor weight, and the OMVs derived from BL21 exhibited the most potent tumor suppression (Figure 1D,E; Figure S1B, Supporting Information). We conducted nanoparticle tracking analysis (NTA) to measure the number of OMVs extracted under identical conditions and found that, compared with the other two types, BL21‐derived OMVs contained fewer particles per µg of total protein (Figure S1C, Supporting Information), indicating that the protein content of BL21‐derived OMV nanoparticles was relatively greater.

To compare the immunostimulatory potency of different OMVs, we collected tumor tissues for flow cytometry analysis. Consistent with the tumor suppression effects, the highest proportion of CD8+ T cell infiltration within the tumor and the relatively highest expression of the activation marker IFNγ in CD8+ T cells were observed upon treatment with the three OMVs in the BL21‐derived group (Figure 1F,G; Figure S1D,E, Supporting Information). Additionally, all three types of OMVs decreased the proportion of Treg cells with BL21‐derived OMVs, resulting in the most pronounced downregulation and consequently the highest CD8/Treg ratio (Figure 1H,I; Figure S1F, Supporting Information). These findings suggested that, compared with the other two types of OMVs, BL21‐derived OMVs had relatively better T‐cell chemotaxis and activation effects.

To further investigate the potential mechanisms underlying tumor suppression induced by OMVs, we performed qPCR to analyze the differentially expressed genes within tumor tissues (Figure 1J). Compared with the other two OMV groups, the BL21‐derived OMV group presented significantly upregulated cytokine gene expression related to CD8+ T cell recruitment and activation. Moreover, genes associated with apoptosis, such as Nod1 and Tnfa, were also notably elevated in the BL21‐derived OMV group (Figure 1J). These findings suggested that OMVs derived from BL21 cells significantly upregulated the expression of immune activation‐related genes within the tumor, thereby more effectively promoting the chemotaxis and activation of CD8+ T cells. Therefore, we selected BL21‐derived OMVs as the vehicle for subsequent engineering modifications.

Construction of a CRISPR System‐Based OMV Packaging and Delivery Platform

To improve OMV‐based tumor therapies, many therapeutic genes have been introduced into OMVs through electroporation or coincubation methods,[  ] which face challenges such as low preparation efficiency and high cost. To address this, we applied the CRISPR‐dCas9 system to OMVs. The dCas9 protein was fused to the N‐terminus of the bacterial membrane protein ClyA (dCas9‐ClyA), and the sgRNA was placed on the same plasmid vector under the prokaryotic promoter J23119 (Figure  2A). We hypothesized that the dCas9‐ClyA protein specifically binds to the target gene of interest (GOI) plasmid via sgRNA and that this dCas9‐ClyA‐sgRNA‐GOI plasmid complex could be efficiently packaged into engineered OMVs through the localization function of ClyA.

We cotransformed two constructed plasmids (Table S1, Supporting Information) into competent E. coli BL21 cells and produced engineered OMVs through a series of steps (Figure 2A). The western blotting results confirmed the successful expression of the His‐tag dCas9‐ClyA fusion protein in the genetically modified E. coli BL21 cells, as well as in the extracted engineered OMVs (Figure 2B). ELISA was subsequently conducted and revealed that approximately two‐thirds of the dCas9‐ClyA protein was enveloped in OMVs (Figure 2C). These results demonstrated that the dCas9‐ClyA protein can be effectively packaged into engineered OMVs via ClyA localization. Therefore, we proceeded to design and screen sgRNA sequences.

We designed three sgRNAs targeting different binding sites of the GOI plasmid as well as a control sgRNA without target specificity. Additionally, the EGFP gene termed the EGFP plasmid, was inserted into the GOI plasmid for validation. For each sgRNA, we transformed BL21 cells with either the dCas9‐ClyA‐sgRNA plasmid, the EGFP plasmid, or both, with or without the addition of isopropyl β‐D‐thiogalactoside (IPTG), and subsequently extracted the corresponding engineered OMVs. We observed that neither the amount of plasmid transfected nor the presence of IPTG affected the total number of OMVs produced by the BL21 cells. To compare the enrichment efficiency of different sgRNAs for the EGFP plasmid, we extracted total DNA from the engineered OMVs and performed qPCR and PCR gel electrophoresis assays. When only the EGFP plasmid was transformed into BL21 cells, the relative content of the EGFP plasmid in the OMVs increased, indicating that the EGFP plasmid could randomly enter the OMVs. Compared with the other control groups, when both the dCas9‐ClyA‐sgRNA plasmid and the EGFP plasmid were cotransformed into BL21 cells with IPTG addition, the relative content of the EGFP plasmid in the OMVs reached its highest level. Specifically, the relative EGFP content in the sgRNA1, sgRNA2, and sgRNA3 groups increased by 7.1‐fold, 1.6‐fold, and 1.5‐fold, respectively, whereas that in the control group did not increase (Figure 2D,E; Figure S2A,B, Supporting Information). Similarly, PCR gel electrophoresis also revealed a consistent trend, with the final lane for the sgRNA1 group displaying the brightest band (Figure 2E). These results indicated that sgRNA1 possessed the strongest enrichment capacity for the EGFP plasmid. Therefore, we chose sgRNA1 for subsequent packaging and delivery of the EGFP plasmid. Additionally, TEM and DLS experiments revealed that the engineered OMVs from different groups displayed vesicular structures (Figure S2C, Supporting Information), with the average diameter slightly increasing to ≈200 nm (Figure S2D, Supporting Information).

Validation of the CRISPR System‐Based OMV Packaging and Delivery Platform

Next, to investigate the tumor‐targeting ability of the engineered OMVs and their potential as tumor delivery carriers, PKH67‐labeled engineered OMVs or Cy5.5‐labeled engineered OMVs were incubated with MB49 cells or six other tumor cell lines, respectively. Significant green or red fluorescence was observed in MB49 or other tumor cells, as visualized by fluorescence microscopy and flow cytometric histograms (Figure 2F,G; Figure S2E–G, Supporting Information). These findings confirmed that engineered OMVs could be effectively taken up by a variety of tumor cells, highlighting their potential as efficient tumor delivery vehicles. Furthermore, MB49 and B16‐F10 cells were cocultured with control or EGFP plasmid‐loaded OMVs for 48 h. The relative expression levels of EGFP mRNA and protein in the recipient cells were significantly greater than those in the control cells (Figure 2H; Figure S2H, Supporting Information). In addition, fluorescence microscopy revealed strong EGFP green fluorescence in MB49 and B16‐F10 cells (Figure S2I, Supporting Information), demonstrating that the EGFP gene carried by engineered OMVs could be effectively delivered to the nuclei of tumor cells and subsequently expressed by tumor cells. Additionally, the viability of MB49 and B16‐F10 cells was not affected when they were coincubated with OMV‐WT or OMV‐EGFP (Figure S3A,B, Supporting Information).

We further explored whether this strategy could be used to package and deliver larger plasmids. The lentiCRISPR‐v2 plasmid, which is 14,873 bp in length, was selected. LentiCRISPR‐v2 (termed the V2 plasmid) is a lentiviral vector used for CRISPR‐Cas9 gene editing, where its effective delivery and expression in target cells are crucial for achieving its gene‐editing function.[  ] We incubated B16‐F10 cells with either control or V2 plasmid‐packaged OMVs and found that the relative expression of Cas9 mRNA was significantly increased when the cells were transfected with sgRNA3‐packaged engineered OMVs (OMV‐sgRNA3/V2). Similarly, PCR gel electrophoresis also revealed a consistent trend, with the final lane showing the brightest band in the OMV‐sgRNA3/V2 group (Figure S4, Supporting Information). These results demonstrated that dCas9‐ClyA‐sgRNA‐engineered OMVs can effectively package any plasmid of interest, including very large plasmids, and deliver them to the nuclei of target cells for expression.

The CRISPR‐dCas9‐Krab Engineered OMV System Inhibits Both Exogenous and Endogenous Gene Expression In Vitro

To further develop a multifunctional engineered OMV delivery platform, we attempted to insert the Krab domain expression sequence into the dCas9‐ClyA‐sgRNA plasmid (termed the dCas9‐Krab‐ClyA‐sgRNA plasmid) (Figure S5A, Supporting Information). The western blotting results confirmed the expression of the fusion dCas9‐Krab‐ClyA protein in the transfected BL21 cells and the derived engineered OMVs (Figure S5B, Supporting Information). We performed a proof‐of‐concept experiment to evaluate the ability of these engineered OMVs to deliver gene interference functionality to target cells. We constructed an MB49 cell line stably expressing mCherry via lentivirus transfection. OMVs harboring dCas9‐Krab and sgRNA targeting three different sites of the mCherry gene, named OMV‐sgmCherry1, 2, and 3, and OMVs harboring dCas9‐Krab and control sgRNA, named OMV‐sgctrl, were added to the media of the aforementioned MB49 cells. The results from the qPCR, fluorescence microscopy and mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) analyses revealed that all three OMVs significantly inhibited mCherry expression in MB49 cells, whereas OMV‐sgmCherry2 had the highest inhibition rate compared with the other control groups (Figure S5C–E, Supporting Information). Additionally, we validated the gene interference functionality of this OMV system in HEK293T cells and found that both OMV‐sgmCherry1 and OMV‐sgmCherry2 significantly suppressed the expression of the exogenous mCherry gene (Figure S5F–I, Supporting Information). Furthermore, we constructed bladder cancer patient‐derived organoids expressing both mCherry and EGFP fluorescent proteins via an adeno‐associated virus (AAV) to examine whether engineered OMVs could function in a 3D human tumor model (Figure S6A, Supporting Information). The fluorescence intensity of mCherry was markedly lower than that of EGFP in both the OMV‐sgmCherry1 and OMV‐sgmCherry2 groups. However, the expression of mCherry and EGFP was comparable between the OMV‐sgctrl group and the no‐OMV blank group because the expression‐driving capabilities of the CAG and CMV promoters were similar (Figure S6B,C, Supporting Information). In addition, we further examined whether this OMV system could knock down endogenous PDL1 expression in various cell lines. The relative PD‐L1 mRNA expression level was decreased by less than half after OMV‐sgPD‐L1 treatment in B16‐F10, 4T1, and CT26 cells (Figure S6D, Supporting Information). On the basis of these results, CRISPR‐dCas9‐Krab‐based engineered OMVs effectively inhibited both exogenous and endogenous gene expression.

OMV Encoding CXCL9 and IL12 (OMV‐C9I12) Enhances T‐Cell Chemotaxis and Activation In Vitro

Next, we modified the delivered GOI plasmid to further enhance OMV‐based antitumor immunotherapy. CXC‐chemokine ligand 9 (CXCL9) is crucial for T‐cell chemotaxis and accumulation in solid tumors.[  ] Interleukin‐12 (IL‐12) is a powerful cytokine that can potently activate lymphocytes.[  ] We linked the CXCL9 and IL12 expression cassettes via a P2A cleavage peptide and constructed them into the GOI plasmid, denoted as pC9I12 (Figure S7A, Supporting Information). Then, pC9I12 was packaged into the engineered OMVs via sgRNA1 (OMV‐C9I12). We also separately constructed plasmids expressing only CXCL9 or IL12 and control plasmids packaged them into OMVs, and named them OMV‐CXCL9, OMV‐IL12, and OMV‐Ctrl, respectively. The expression of CXCL9 and IL12 was preferentially quantified via qPCR and ELISA after the incubation of OMV‐C9I12 cells with MB49 cells. CXCL9 mRNA expression in OMV‐CXCL9‐ or OMV‐C9I12‐transfected MB49 cells was 4.46‐fold or 3.7‐fold greater, respectively than that in the PBS group. IL12 mRNA expression was increased by 4.03‐fold or 4.19‐fold, respectively (Figure S7B, Supporting Information). Similarly, the concentration of the CXCL9 protein in the culture supernatants of MB49 cells reached 9.66‐fold and 9.74‐fold greater than that in the culture supernatants of control cells following transfection with OMV‐CXCL9 and OMV‐C9I12, respectively (Figure  3A). Additionally, the IL12 protein concentrations in the supernatants of OMV‐IL12‐ and OMV‐C9I12‐transfected MB49 cells were 18.3‐fold and 17.5‐fold greater, respectively, than those in the PBS group (Figure 3B).

We subsequently investigated the efficacy of OMV‐C9I12 in enhancing the recruitment, activation, and tumor‐killing ability of T cells. Compared with those in the PBS, OMV‐Ctrl, and OMV‐IL12 groups, the supernatants of MB49 cells transfected with OMV‐CXCL9 or OMV‐C9I12 recruited more CD8+ T cells. Interestingly, the chemotaxis index in the OMV‐C9I12 group was 5.85‐fold greater, accompanied by a greater number of CD8+ T cells, than the 4.4‐fold increase observed in the OMV‐CXCL9 group (Figure 3C; Figure S7C, Supporting Information), suggesting that IL12, present exclusively in the OMV‐C9I12 group, had a synergistic effect on the CXCL9‐mediated chemotaxis of CD8+ T cells.

Next, we evaluated the ability of OMV‐C9I12 to increase the activity of CD8+ T cells via Ki67. Flow cytometry revealed that the proportion of Ki67+ cells among CD8+ T cells incubated with supernatants from MB49 cells transfected with OMV‐IL12 or OMV‐C9I12 was significantly greater than that in the PBS or OMV‐Ctrl groups (Figure 3D). Notably, the proportion of CD8+Ki67+ T cells was significantly greater in the OMV‐C9I12 group (32.52%) than in the OMV‐IL12 group (21.35%), indicating that CXCL9 expression in the OMV‐C9I12 group enhances the IL12‐mediated activation of CD8+ T cells (Figure 3E).

We subsequently evaluated the combined effects of OMV‐C9I12 on tumor cell death through the recruitment and activation of T cells (Figure 3F). qPCR was performed to confirm the expression of CXCL9 and IL12 in B16‐F10‐OVA cells (Figure 3G). We found that in the OMV‐C9I12 group, B16‐F10‐OVA cells were efficiently killed, resulting in 41.8% PI‐positive B16‐F10‐OVA cells (Figure 3H,I). However, the OMV‐CXCL9 group presented a lower percentage of PI‐positive cells (26.1%) because of the lack of IL12. The OMV‐IL12 group also exhibited a 30.6% killing rate, possibly because the IL12‐mediated activation of CD8+ T cells promoted the production of IFNγ, which in turn killed the tumor cells. In contrast, the PBS and OMV‐Ctrl groups presented minimal tumor‐killing ability because CXCL9 and IL12 were not expressed (Figure 3H,I). Collectively, these results demonstrated that OMV‐C9I12 could drive the coexpression of CXCL9 and IL12 in tumor cells to enhance T‐cell chemotaxis and activation, thereby exerting synergistic tumor‐killing effects in vitro.

OMV‐C9I12 Synergizes with Anti‐PD‐1/PD‐L1 Inhibitors to Inhibit Tumors In Vivo Tumor Models

To evaluate the therapeutic potential of OMV‐C9I12 for in vivo tumor delivery, we initially established a subcutaneous MB49‐luc tumor model in mice. Following a single intratumoral injection of DiD‐labeled OMVs, in vivo imaging revealed significant fluorescence accumulation at the tumor site in both OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐Ctrl groups compared to the PBS control, colocalized with the intrinsic bioluminescence of tumor cells. The signals showed no apparent attenuation within 72 h (Figure S8A, Supporting Information). Ex vivo imaging at 72 h postinjection demonstrated effective retention of OMV‐C9I12 specifically within tumor tissue, with minimal detection in major organs (Figure S8B, Supporting Information). Subsequent flow cytometry analysis of tumor tissues showed that over 80% of DiD‐positive cells were tumor cells in both OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐Ctrl groups. Among the remaining populations, ≈10% were macrophages, with dendritic cells accounting for less than 10% (Figure S8C, Supporting Information). These results demonstrate that intratumorally administered OMV‐C9I12 is internalized predominantly by tumor cells, supporting its utility as a targeted delivery vehicle for immunotherapy.

Next, we investigated the ability of OMV‐C9I12 to boost immunotherapy in murine syngeneic MB49 and B16F10 tumor models (Figure  4A). In the MB49 bladder cancer model, compared with the PBS, αPD1 alone and OMV‐Ctrl moderately inhibited tumor growth, resulting in tumor growth inhibition (TGI) rates of 29.1% and 63.2%, respectively (Figure 4B; Figure S9A, Supporting Information). Notably, OMV‐C9I12 exhibited superior tumor suppression, with a TGI rate reaching 79.4%. Furthermore, the combination of OMV‐C9I12 and αPD1 achieved maximal tumor growth inhibition, with a TGI of 88.1% (Figure 4B; Figure S9A, Supporting Information). Additionally, the tumor weights measured at the treatment endpoint in the OMV‐C9I12 group were only 19% of those in the PBS group, demonstrating the greater therapeutic efficacy of OMV‐C9I12 than the αPD1 (65.9% of the PBS group) or OMV‐Ctrl (39.3% of the PBS group) groups (Figure 4C; Figure S9B, Supporting Information). Consistently, Ki67 expression and TUNEL staining of tumor sections also confirmed the in vivo therapeutic effects (Figure S9C–F, Supporting Information). OMV‐C9I12 significantly extended the median survival time to 55 days compared with 44 days in the OMV‐Ctrl group and extended beyond 60 days when OMV‐C9I12 was combined with αPD1, suggesting that OMV‐C9I12 can increase the therapeutic efficacy of OMVs and sensitize tumors to αPD1 treatment in vivo (Figure 4D). Moreover, liver and kidney function markers (Figure S10A–F, Supporting Information) and H&E staining of major organ sections (Figure S10G, Supporting Information) from MB49 tumor‐bearing mice treated with OMV‐Ctrl or OMV‐C9I12 did not significantly differ from those of the PBS control, indicating favorable safety and biocompatibility of OMV‐C9I12. Additionally, we observed similar trends in the immunologically cold B16F10 melanoma model, which is characterized by sparse T‐cell infiltration and a poor response to checkpoint blockade therapy (Figure 4E–G; Figure S11A–F, Supporting Information).

Then, the sequences of CXCL9 and IL12 were replaced with human expression sequences. The efficacy of OMV‐C9I12 in human cancers was further evaluated in a humanized breast cancer mouse model. NOD scid gamma (NSG) mice were inoculated with MDA‐MB‐231 breast cancer cells, intravenously injected with PBMCs to reconstitute a functional human immune system,[  ] and intratumorally administered different treatments. Tumor progression was monitored until day 33 (Figure 4H). Compared with PBS, αPD1 and OMV‐Ctrl were able to partially inhibit tumor growth. OMV‐C9I12, in comparison with αPD1 alone, further suppressed tumor growth (Figure 4I). Notably, the mice treated with OMV‐C9I12 or αPD1 presented the best tumor control, with average tumor volumes remaining under 100 mm3 throughout the study (Figure 4I; Figure S12A,B, Supporting Information). The weights of the tumors from the different treatment groups were also analyzed, revealing that the weights of the tumors treated with OMV‐C9I12 were only 23.9% greater than those of the tumors treated with PBS, which was significantly lower than those of the tumors treated with αPD1 (50.5% of PBS) or OMV‐Ctrl (32.9% of PBS), demonstrating the enhanced therapeutic efficacy of OMV‐C9I12 (Figure 4J). Furthermore, tumors treated with the OMV‐C9I12 and αPD1 combination had the lowest weight, at only 9.2% of that of the PBS group, and were significantly lower than those treated with αPD1 alone or the OMV‐C9I12 alone (Figure 4J). This clearly indicated that OMV‐C9I12 significantly enhanced the therapeutic effect of αPD1 on breast cancer and exhibited a synergistic effect with αPD1. Importantly, there were no significant differences in body weight among the different treatment groups, indicating the tolerability and safety of OMV‐C9I12 (Figure S12C, Supporting Information).

T Cell Population Profiling of OMV‐C9I12‐ and anti‐PD‐1/PD‐L1 Inhibitor‐Treated Cells

We subsequently characterized the inflammatory status and tumor‐infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) within solid tumors at the treatment endpoint. We analyzed the levels of CXCL9, IL12, and IFNγ within the MB49 tumors and found that all three factors were significantly increased after OMV‐C9I12 treatment compared with those in the PBS and αPD1 groups (Figure  5A–C). Furthermore, Pearson correlation analysis revealed a significant negative correlation between the concentrations of CXCL9, IL12, and IFNγ in tumor tissues and tumor volume (Figure S13A–C, Supporting Information), whereas the expression levels of CXCL9 and IL12 were significantly positively correlated with IFNγ (Figure S13D,E, Supporting Information). These data indicated that OMV‐C9I12 promoted T‐cell infiltration, proliferation, and activation, thereby increasing the secretion of the antitumor factor IFNγ.

Furthermore, we employed flow cytometry to characterize the number and phenotype of TILs in the tumor microenvironment (TME) comprehensively. In the MB49 bladder cancer model, compared with those in the PBS group, the percentages of CD45+ cells in the OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐C9I12/αPD1 groups increased from ≈15.37% to 35.4% and 37.02% (Figure 5D), respectively, and the percentages of CD3+ T cells increased from 13.94% to 24.28% and 41.42% (Figure 5E), respectively. Additionally, the proportions of CD8+ cells among CD45+ cells and CD3+ T cells were significantly greater in the OMV‐C9I12 group than in the PBS and OMV‐Ctrl groups and were further elevated under OMV‐C9I12/αPD1 treatment (Figure 5F; Figure S13F, Supporting Information). Further analysis revealed that OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐C9I12/αPD1 treatment also significantly increased the proportions of IFNγ+CD8+ T cells and Granzyme B+CD8+ T cells, respectively (Figure 5G,H; Figure S13G,H, Supporting Information). The immunohistochemistry (IHC) results of the tumor sections also corroborated the findings mentioned above (Figure S13I, Supporting Information). The increased T‐cell infiltration and activity likely accounted for the potentiating effect of OMV‐C9I12 on αPD‐1 therapy.

We further analyzed the T cell immune status in the B16F10 melanoma model and observed a nearly identical trend in which both the OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐C9I12/αPDL1 treatments significantly increased the populations of CD45+ cells, CD3+ T cells, and CD8+ T cells (Figure 5I,J; Figure S14A, Supporting Information), promoted IFNγ production in CD3+ and CD8+ T cells (Figure 5K; Figure S14B–D, Supporting Information), and reduced the proportion of CD25+Foxp3+ Treg cells (Figure 5L; Figure S14E, Supporting Information). Additionally, immunofluorescence analysis revealed that treatment with OMV‐C9I12 or OMV‐C9I12/αPDL1 markedly increased the number of infiltrating CD3+ T cells and increased granzyme B expression in CD3+ T cells (Figure 5M). These data suggested that OMV‐C9I12 could not only promote CTL infiltration and activation but also significantly alleviate immunosuppression in the TME, effectively converting immunologically cold into hot and sensitizing tumors to checkpoint blockade therapy.

Additionally, we assessed the proportion of tumor‐infiltrating immune cells, especially T cells, which constitute the major component of PBMCs, in the humanized breast cancer mouse model. Consistent with the aforementioned trend, noticeable increases in populations of CD45+ cells, CD3+ T cells, and CD8+ T cells were observed in the OMV‐C9I12 and OMV‐C9I12/αPDL1 groups. In contrast, OMV‐Ctrl did not increase these immune cell populations (Figure 5N,O; Figure S15, Supporting Information), suggesting that the immune factors carried by OMV‐C9I12 promoted T‐cell infiltration into solid tumors. The activation marker CD69 for T cells was also measured, which revealed that, compared with the other controls, OMV‐C9I12 effectively increased CD69 expression in CD3+ T cells and CD8+ T cells, which was further improved by αPD1 treatment (Figure 5P,Q).

ScRNA‐seq Reveals that OMV‐C9I12 Promotes T Cell‐Mediated Antitumor Immunity

To further characterize how OMV‐C9I12 promoted antitumor immunity, tumor‐infiltrating CD45‐positive cells in MB49 bladder cancer from the PBS and OMV‐C9I12 groups were sorted for single‐cell RNA sequencing (scRNA‐seq) analysis, which identified six types of immune cells, including T cells, monocytes/macrophages, dendritic cells, neutrophils, NK cells, and mast cells (Figure  6A,B). We further subdivided the T cell population into distinct subgroups (Figure S16A–C, Supporting Information). Gene Ontology (GO) analysis revealed that OMV‐C9I12 significantly upregulated genes associated with T cell activation and chemotaxis in T cells (Figure 6C), and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) analysis revealed that cytokine and chemokine signaling pathways were also activated in T cells (Figure 6D). Additionally, gene network analysis and gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) confirmed that OMV‐C9I12 treatment significantly upregulated genes associated with pathways that positively modulate T cell activation, chemotaxis, proliferation, and the immune response (Figure 6E–H; Figure S17A–O, Supporting Information). Notably, the JAK‐STAT signaling pathway, NF‐kappa B signaling pathway, TNF signaling pathway, and cytokine‒cytokine receptor interaction pathway, which is closely associated with immune activation, were upregulated. Further analysis revealed that OMV‐C9I12 also increased the expression of genes related to the cytokine response, such as Granzymes and Xcl1, as well as the immune response in dendritic cells and monocytes/macrophages (Figure 6I; Figure S18A,B, Supporting Information). This upregulation enhances immune cell activation and promotes effector T‐cell activation and cytotoxicity for tumor rejection. Additionally, we analyzed the expression of Cxcl9 receptors (Cxcr3) and Il12 receptors (Il12rb1/2) across different cell types and T‐cell subpopulations. We found that these three genes were stimulated primarily in T cells, especially in effector and cycling T cells (Figure 6J,K), indicating that the elevated expression of Cxcl9 and Il12 primarily mediated T cell chemotaxis and activation. These findings mechanistically demonstrate that OMV‐C9I12 effectively promotes T‐cell chemotaxis and activation within tumors, thereby enhancing immune responses.

Therapeutic Efficacy of OMV‐C9I12‐Based Cancer Immunotherapy in a Humanized Orthotopic Bladder Cancer Mouse Model

Finally, the therapeutic efficacy of OMV‐C9I12 was first examined in a humanized orthotopic bladder tumor mouse model through intravesical instillation (Figure  7A). We observed that αPD1 treatment slightly reduced tumor growth (Figure 7B–D), possibly due to the insufficient penetration of αPD1 into the solid tumor or the inability of αPD1 to recruit enough T cells to exert its effect. However, intravesical instillation of OMV‐C9I12 further suppressed the bioluminescence signal, decreasing the average signal intensity to 45.9% of that in the PBS group. When αPD1 was combined with OMV‐C9I12, bioluminescence was significantly suppressed to only 23% of that in the PBS group and 36.3% of that in the αPD1 group (Figure 7C,D; Figure S19A, Supporting Information). We further analyzed bladder tumor size on the basis of H&E‐stained whole‐bladder sections. In the OMV‐C9I12 group, we observed a significant reduction in tumor size, with the tumor size being only 42.8% of that in the PBS group. When αPD1 was combined with OMV‐C9I12, the tumor size was reduced to 26% of that in the αPD1 group (Figure 7E,F; Figure S19B, Supporting Information). Accordingly, tumors treated with OMV‐C9I12 presented a greater proportion of TUNEL‐positive cells, and this effect was further enhanced when OMV‐C9I12 was combined with αPD1 (Figure 7G,H). The H&E staining results of the bladder sections also revealed no significant inflammatory cell infiltration or mucosal necrosis in the bladder mucosa epithelium following OMV‐C9I12 instillation (Figure S19B, Supporting Information), demonstrating the safety and feasibility of OMV‐C9I12‐based intravesical instillation therapy. Thus, these results demonstrated that combining αPD‐1 therapy with OMV‐C9I12 exhibited excellent tumor suppression effects and safety in a humanized bladder cancer mouse model.

Discussion

ICB therapy, which uses checkpoint inhibitors to target and block regulatory signaling pathways in T cells, reverses effector T‐cell exhaustion and enhances antitumor immune responses. However, a subset of patients still experience unsatisfactory therapeutic benefits because of insufficient infiltration and inactivation of effector T cells in solid tumors.[  ] OMVs can activate immunity and be engineered as vehicles for delivering therapeutic agents. However, efficiently targeting and packaging therapeutic molecules into OMVs remains a challenge. Here, we demonstrated the use of E. coli BL21‐derived OMVs as immune activators and engineered them by fusing CRISPR/dCas9 with ClyA. These engineered OMVs are capable of enveloping multiple genes and achieving gene silencing in vitro. We further engineered OMVs carrying genes encoding CXCL9 and IL12 (OMV‐C9I12), which drive the coexpression of CXCL9 and IL12 in the TME, significantly enhancing effector T cell chemotaxis and activation. Notably, OMV‐C9I12 effectively improved the therapeutic efficacy of OMVs and sensitized tumors to ICB therapy in vivo. Overall, we engineered a potent OMV platform for cancer gene therapy and offered novel options to potentiate cancer immunotherapy.

OMVs are naturally derived nanoscale vesicles that are easy to produce and can be readily absorbed by tumor cells,[  ] with good biocompatibility.[  ] Currently, several studies have modified OMVs as delivery vehicles for cancer therapy. For example, PD1 plasmids[  ] or siRNAs[  ] have been introduced into OMVs via electroporation. However, the electroporation efficiency is considerably low, and inefficient electroporation consumes large amounts of expensive materials such as siRNAs.[  ] Additionally, the high cost of electroporation instruments, complex processes, and potential impact on OMV membrane stability severely limit large‐scale production and clinical translation. Other studies have engineered OMV membrane surfaces to bind various materials, such as mRNAs,[  ] proteins, or peptide antigens.[  ] However, these delivered molecules are located on the outer surface of the OMV membrane. Thus, these delivered substances may degrade before the OMVs reach the tumor microenvironment and are taken up due to the lack of membrane shielding, thus limiting delivery efficiency and causing unstable therapeutic effects. In this study, we first fused CRISPR/dCas9 with the bacterial outer membrane protein ClyA. On the basis of synthetic biology techniques, we designed a dual plasmid system that was cotransformed into E. coli BL21 and produced OMVs in large quantities through bacterial fermentation. By leveraging the high‐affinity binding of the dCas9‐sgRNA complex to target DNA, the CRISPR‐dCas9‐ClyA complexes mediate efficient self‐assembly of target genes into OMVs. Consequently, we obtained an OMV system capable of carrying any gene of interest in one step, significantly improving gene enrichment efficiency in OMVs while enhancing potential transformation efficiency. Meanwhile, the delivered target genes are amplified and synthesized by bacteria, eliminating the need for artificial preparation and substantially reducing production costs. Additionally, the DNA molecules were encapsulated within the OMV membrane, protected from degradation by the membrane's shielding effect.

As shown in this study, our design is modular, allowing for the replacement of the OMVs packaged and delivering output genes to express different therapeutic molecules, including immunomodulators, proapoptotic proteins, or even shRNAs, indicating the broad applicability of this OMV platform. Moreover, we can coexpress two or more genes in tandem via self‐cleaving peptides such as P2A or T2A, which results in synergistic effects. Importantly, this OMV platform can also encapsulate and deliver large plasmids, such as the LentiCRISPR‐v2 plasmid expressing CRISPR‐Cas9, to eukaryotic cells. This demonstrates the superiority of this OMV platform over naturally occurring exosomes, which cannot carry large DNA molecules.[  ] This advantage may be attributed to OMVs possessing a larger and more flexible membrane structure than exosomes, enabling higher cargo‐loading efficiency for therapeutic molecules. Furthermore, OMVs can intrinsically activate immune responses by enhancing antigen presentation and T‐cell priming, whereas natural exosomes lack such immunostimulatory properties. Additionally, OMVs can be rapidly produced with high yield and significantly lower preparation costs, making them superior to exosomes for delivering cancer immunotherapies. Their ability to simultaneously act as adjuvants and targeted carriers addresses key limitations of exosome‐based systems. Notably, when we inserted the Krab repression domain into the CRISPR‐dCas9 system, the CRISPR‐dCas9‐Krab‐based engineered OMVs effectively and specifically suppressed both exogenous and endogenous gene expression in in vitro models, achieving CRISPR interference functionality. Therefore, we constructed a multifunctional and modular OMV delivery platform.

In this study, immune checkpoint blockade therapy alone showed poor efficacy in an immunologically cold murine melanoma model. However, the dual expression of CXCL9 and IL12 via an engineered OMV‐C9I12 system potently inflamed the tumor immune microenvironment by enhancing the inflammatory immune status. Notably, the combination of CXCL9 and IL12 synergistically promoted the recruitment and activation of T cells, ultimately resulting in enhanced tumor‐killing effects in vitro. As shown, the OMV‐C9I12 system upregulated multiple signaling pathways associated with T‐cell chemotaxis, proliferation, and activation, which enabled effective combination immunotherapy with immune checkpoint blockade. We also verified the function of the OMV‐C9I12 system in both a murine syngeneic MB49 tumor model and a humanized breast cancer mouse model, which revealed good therapeutic outcomes and favorable safety. In addition, we anticipate that this OMV platform‐based strategy could be extended in combination with other immunotherapies to improve treatment response rates, such as CAR‐T‐cell therapy, which has shown poor performance in solid tumors due to insufficient infiltration.[  ] 

Additionally, we innovatively applied the OMV‐C9I12 system to treat bladder cancer via intravesical instillation in a humanized orthotopic bladder tumor mouse model. Owing to the natural communication between the bladder cavity and the outside through the urethra, intravesical instillation is a common administration route for bladder cancer treatment. Compared with systemic administration of tislelizumab alone, intravesical instillation of OMV‐C9I12 resulted in better tumor suppression, and tumor inhibition achieved the optimal effect when OMV‐C9I12 was combined with tislelizumab. These findings demonstrated the feasibility of using OMV‐C9I12 for intravesical instillation to enhance cancer immunotherapy, indicating the broad applicability of this OMV platform.

Our study also has limitations. We validated the efficacy of this engineered OMV platform through local administration, including intratumoral injection and intravesical instillation, which are routinely used for the treatment of bladder cancer, breast cancer, melanoma, and lymphoma.[  ] For future studies, we will further evaluate its efficacy through systemic injection, which requires improvements in the targeting ability and modifications to reduce the immunogenicity of engineered OMVs. Moreover, we did not comprehensively characterize other immune cell populations within the tumor immune microenvironment, such as dendritic cells and macrophages, beyond T cell populations, which may also contribute to enhanced tumor immunotherapy. Further characterization in this regard is worthwhile. Furthermore, the safety risks associated with the delivered plasmids, including immunogenicity and potential risks of gene insertion,[  ] need to be carefully evaluated before clinical application.

Conclusion

In conclusion, we initially developed a multifunctional OMV platform capable of carrying multiple genes, achieving high gene loading efficiency and low production costs. The OMV‐C9I12 system enhances T‐cell immune responses by effectively promoting effector T‐cell infiltration and activation, thereby enhancing antitumor therapeutic effects and improving sensitivity to ICB therapy in multiple solid tumor models. These results highlight the potential of this engineered and modular OMV platform in cancer gene therapy and provide novel strategies to overcome therapeutic resistance. More importantly, these preclinical findings lay a solid foundation for future clinical translation of the OMV‐C9I12 system in humans, which should be initiated in the near future.
