# TNFR2/CCR8 bispecific antibody enhances antitumor activity through depleting Ti-Tregs and boosting effector CD8+ T cell function.

## Abstract

Modulation or depletion of tumor-infiltrating Tregs (Ti-Tregs) is a promising strategy in the field of antitumor immunotherapy. However, this approach poses challenges due to the diversity within the Treg population and the lack of precision in targeting Ti-Tregs. To selectively and efficiently eliminate Ti-Tregs while sparing other immune cells, we developed a bispecific antibody, FT10-Fab, targeting TNFR2 and CCR8, which are highly expressed on Ti-Tregs. Our results showed that FT10-Fab outperformed the monotherapies in several tumor models by significantly reducing the proportion of Ti-Tregs while increasing the proportion of CD8+ T cells. FT10-Fab was able to target and eliminate Ti-Tregs expressing TNFR2 or CCR8 (TNFR2+or CCR8+ Tregs), particularly TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs, which are the most important proliferative and protumorigenic Tregs. In addition, FT10-Fab relies on CD8+ T cells for its antitumor function and induces robust immune memory. Furthermore, the combination of FT10-Fab with PD-1 blockade showed synergistic therapeutic efficacy against tumors by significantly suppressing Tregs and enhancing effector CD8+ T cell function. Taken together, our findings suggest that precision depletion of Ti-Tregs via the bispecific TNFR2/CCR8 antibody is a potential therapeutic for cancer immunotherapy, while combination with anti-PD1 amplifies the antitumor effect.

TNFR2/CCR8 bispecific antibody enhances antitumor activity through depleting Ti-Tregs and boosting effector CD8+ T cell function

ABSTRACT

Modulation or depletion of tumor-infiltrating Tregs (Ti-Tregs) is a promising strategy in the field of antitumor immunotherapy. However, this approach poses challenges due to the diversity within the Treg population and the lack of precision in targeting Ti-Tregs. Introduction

Tregs are formidable obstacles to initiating antitumour immune responses and immunotherapy. A large number of Tregs infiltrate tumor tissues to suppress antitumour immunity in patients. The depletion of Tregs in murine models has been shown to significantly inhibit tumor growth. Consequently, targeting Tregs to either inhibit their function or deplete them has become a key strategy in cancer immunotherapy. Cytotoxic T-Lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) is an inhibitory immune checkpoint receptor that plays a critical role in regulating T-cell responses. However, CTLA-4 antibodies deplete Tregs both in tumor microenvironments and in peripheral tissues, which can lead to immune-related adverse events in clinical settings. Therefore, the current focus of cancer immunotherapy targeting Tregs is to selectively deplete Ti-Tregs while minimizing the impact on peripheral Treg function.

Bispecific antibodies (BsAbs) hold significant promise in the field of immunotherapy due to their unique ability to simultaneously engage two distinct antigens or antigenic epitopes. An advanced strategy involves using BsAbs to target dual inhibitory checkpoints, thereby enhancing antitumour responses. This dual targeting approach enables more precise and efficient depletion of Ti-Tregs through the rational selection of specific targets. In this study, we consulted and analyzed mouse and human tumor-associated immunological bioinformatics datasets and selected C-C chemokine receptor type 8 (CCR8) and tumor necrosis factor receptor 2 (TNFR2) for selectively targeting Ti-Tregs.

CCR8 is more highly expressed in Ti-Tregs compared to other immune cells in the tumor or in peripheral healthy tissues. The coexpression of CCR8 with CTLA-4, FOXP3, KI67 and ICOS marks Tregs with strong inhibitory function and proliferative capacity in tumors. The efficacy of anti-CCR8 relies on antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) effects. The use of blocking anti-CCR8 reduces the accumulation of Ti-Tregs and impairs their immunosuppressive function in tumor microenvironment (TME). TNFR2, encoded by the gene TNFRSF1B, is specifically and highly expressed on Ti-Tregs. Elevated TNFR2 expression on Ti-Tregs is correlated with robust suppressive activity. Multiple monoclonal antibodies targeting mouse TNFR2 have been shown to deplete Ti-Tregs, increase the number of CD8+ T cells and improve the proportion of IFN-γ+ CD8 T cells. Overall, these studies suggest that targeting TNFR2 and CCR8 represents a promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy.

Tregs exhibit substantial heterogeneity, consisting of distinct subsets with varying origins, phenotypic characteristics, and functional properties. Due to the variability in surface marker expression on Tregs, selective targeting is required. However, targeting a single surface molecule is insufficient to effectively cover Ti-Tregs and lacks tumor specificity. To overcome this limitation, we designed a bispecific antibody, TNFR2/CCR8 (FT10-Fab), aimed at selectively targeting and depleting Ti-Tregs, thereby enhancing antitumour immunity.

Our data demonstrate that FT10-Fab exhibits comprehensive effectiveness against tumors with minimal adverse effects. Compared to monotherapies, this bispecific antibody significantly reduces Ti-Tregs and enhances the antitumor responses of effector T cells. Notably, FT10-Fab treatment establishes potent immune memory in tumor-bearing mice. Furthermore, the combination of FT10-Fab with PD-1 antibodies exhibits synergistic therapeutic effects. Our findings provide new insights and support the development of therapies targeting tumor-infiltrating regulatory T cells.

Methods

Mice

The wild-type mice used in this study were of C57BL/6 or BALB/c genetic backgrounds, obtained from Shanghai Ling Chang or Shanghai Silaike Experimental Animal Co. Ltd. (Shanghai, China). Rag1−/− mice were housed at the Chinese Academy of Sciences in Shanghai, China. All experimental mice, aged 6–8 weeks and female, were raised or bred in the SPF (Specific Pathogen-Free) animal facility at the Center for Excellence in Molecular and Cellular Sciences, Chinese Academy of Sciences. All experimental procedures involving mice were conducted in accordance with the animal experimentation guidelines set by the National Institutes of Health (NIH). The protocols for animal experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the Center for Excellence in Molecular and Cellular Sciences.

Cell lines

The mouse colon adenocarcinoma cell line MC38, CT26 and melanoma cell line B16F10 were gifts from Dr. Yong Cang (ShanghaiTech University, Shanghai, China). The KP13 cell line, a lung adenocarcinoma model derived from Kras/P53-mutated non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), was generously provided by Dr. Hongbin Ji at the CAS Center for Excellence in Molecular Cell Science. This cell line was developed from primary cells isolated from dissected Kras/P53-mutant lung adenocarcinoma tissue. 293T cells were purchased from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China) and were used for viruse packaging. CHOK1 cells, a subclone of the CHO cell line derived from adult Chinese hamster ovaries, were used to construct stably transfected strains with the target genes. All of the above cell lines are adherent. MC38, KP13, B16F10, CT26 and 293T cells were cultured in complete DMEM (BasalMedia, L110KJ) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (BasalMedia, S110JV). CHOK1 cells were cultured in complete DMEM/F-12 (1:1) (BasalMedia, L310KJ) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. All cells were cultured in an incubator at 37°C with appropriate humidity and 5% carbon dioxide. ExpiCHO-S cells (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Cat. No. A29133), a suspension cell line derived from Chinese hamster ovaries, were used for antibody production and cultured in ExpiCHOTM Expression Medium. The cells was cultured in an incubator at 37 °C with a constant humidity and 8% carbon dioxide.

Tumour models to evaluate antibody therapeutic efficacy in vivo

For subcutaneous tumor models, 5  ×  105 MC38 cells, 5  ×  105 CT26 cells, 1  ×  106 KP13 cells, and 2.5  ×  105 B16F10 cells were subcutaneously injected into the left flank of approximately 6- to 8-week-old C57BL/6 female mice. Tumor growth was monitored every other day starting 4 days after injection. Tumor volume was calculated using the formula: volume = length × width × width/2. Treatment was initiated when the tumor volume reached 50–100 mm3 and was administered every other day for a total of four doses via intraperitoneal injection, with an equimolar dose for each injection. The tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into five groups: Vehicle (IgG1), anti-TNFR2 (003F10), anti-CCR8 (TPP14099), Combination (anti-TNFR2 and anti-CCR8), and FT10-Fab. Mice were euthanized on the day following the fourth antibody administration. Tumors were carefully excised, photographed, and weighed. Additionally, liver, kidney, spleen, and draining lymph node were harvested for further analysis. In cases where euthanasia was not performed after the fourth dose, survival data were collected, with the endpoint defined as a tumor volume exceeding 2000 mm3. The time of death for each mouse was recorded, and survival curves were generated for all animals.

CD8+ T cell depletion in vivo

For the CD8+ T cell depletion experiments, 5  ×  105 MC38 cells were subcutaneously injected into the left flank of approximately 6- to 8-week-old wild-type female mice. Tumor-bearing mice were randomly assigned into three groups: Vehicle, FT10-Fab (10 mg/kg) and FT10-Fab (10 mg/kg) combined with anti-CD8a depletion antibody (5 mg/kg, Selleck#A2102). C57BL/6 mice were inoculated with 5  ×  105 MC38 tumor cells and treated with FT10-Fab on days 6, 9, 12, and 15. Anti-CD8a was administered one day before the start of treatment with FT10-Fab and then every other day for a total of four doses. All the antibodies were administered via intraperitoneal injection. Both Vehicle (IgG1) and FT10-Fab antibodies were given every two days for a total of four doses.

In vivo tumour rechallenge experiment

CT26 tumor-bearing mice were treated with 10 mg/kg FT10-Fab, and some of the mice achieved complete tumor remission (cured mice). These cured mice, along with naive BALB/c mice that had never been tumor-inoculated, were allowed a 50-day rest period. Following this period, both the cured mice and naive BALB/c mice were reinoculated with 5  ×  105 CT26 cells. Tumor growth was monitored every other day. When the tumor size reached approximately 1,500 mm3, tumor tissues were excised for photographic documentation and weighing. Additionally, peripheral blood and spleen tissues were collected for flow cytometry analysis.

Flow cytometry and immune analysis

Tumor tissues were digested with 25 µg/mL Liberase TL (Roche, Switzerland) and 10 U/mL DNase Ι (Roche). After digestion, the tissue suspension was mechanically dissociated through a 70-µm filter. Immune cells were then enriched using 40% and 80% Percoll gradient centrifugation. The enriched cells were incubated with an Fc receptor blocker for 10 min at room temperature and then stained with Fixable Viability Stain (BD Biosciences) at 4 °C for 15 min. For surface proteins staining, cells were incubated with antibodies against surface antigens at 4°C for 30 minutes. For intranuclear staining of transcription factors or nuclear protein cytokines, cells were initially stained with antibodies against surface antigens, followed by fixation and permeabilization according to the manufacturer’s instructions (Foxp3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set; eBioscience, 00-5223-56, 00-8333-56). For intracellular cytokine staining, enriched immune cells were activated by incubation with 50 ng/mL phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA; Merck Millipore 524,400), 1 µg/mL ionomycin (Merck Millipore 407,952), and 1 µg/mL brefeldin A (BFA; eBioscience, 00-4506-51) for 4 hours at 37 °C. Flow cytometry data were acquired using an LSR Fortessa (BD Pharmingen) or A3 (BD Symphony) flow cytometer. Data analysis was performed with FlowJo V10 software (FlowJo). Antibody details are provided in the supplementary materials (Table S1).

Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-luciferase reporter assay for assessing ADCC in vitro

The Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-luciferase reporter assay was utilized to evaluate antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) activity in vitro, specifically determining the EC50 value. This assay quantifies luciferase activity upon substrate addition, which correlates with the strength of the antibody’s ADCC response.

CHO-mTNFR2 and CHO-mCCR8 target cells were seeded at a density of 2  ×  104 cells per well in 96-well culture plates. These target cells were co-cultured with Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-luciferase cells at a 1:5 ratio in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10% FBS, with a final volume of 50 μL per well. The cells were then incubated with antibodies in 75 μL 96-well culture plates. The antibodies were subjected to a 4-fold serial dilution, ranging from 22.67 nM to 0.000341 nM, and incubated for 6 hours at 37°C in a humidified 5% CO2 incubator. Following incubation, 75 μL of luciferase substrate buffer (One-Lite Luciferase Assay System, DD1203, Vazyme) was added to each well. ADCC activity was measured by assessing the luciferase signal using a BioTek SynergyNEO plate reader.

ADCC detection assay of tumour-infiltrating Treg cells

Tumor-infiltrating Treg cells (Ti-Tregs) were mixed with Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-Luciferase cells at a ratio of 1:5, with 3  ×  104 Ti-Tregs per well. The mixture was incubated with 150 nM of FT10-Fab antibody for 6 hours. After the incubation, 75 µL of luciferase substrate buffer (One-Lite Luciferase Assay System, DD1203, Vazyme) was added to each well. ADCC activity and the viability of the target cells were then assessed using a Bio Tek SynergyNEO instrument.

Apoptosis assay of FT10-Fab using PBMCs

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) were thawed and incubated overnight. The following day, CHO-mTNFR2-mCCR8 cells were labeled with a CellTrace™ Violet Cell Proliferation Kit (C34557, Thermo Fisher). Then, the labeled cells at a density of 2  ×  104 cells per well were incubated with 150 nM FT10-Fab antibody for 1 hour. Afterward, the culture medium was removed from the CHO-mTNFR2-mCCR8 cells. The antibody-bound cells were then co-cultured with freshly prepared PBMCs at a ratio of 1:50 for 24 hours. Finally, the viability of the target cells was assessed and calculated using an Annexin V-FITC/PI Apoptosis Detection Kit (A211–01).

Bulk RNA-seq assay

Tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells were enriched from MC38 tumor models using the CellTrace™ Mouse CD8 Enrichment Kit (STEMCELL Technologies). The purified CD8+ T cells were then extracted using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen). RNA-seq libraries were sequenced on the Illumina HiSeq PE150 platform. Differential gene expression was defined as a twofold change with a p value < 0.05. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) was performed using GSEA software. The publicly available dataset (GEO: GSE146771) was utilized to investigate the expression of Treg-associated markers, such as TNFRSF1B and CCR8. Additionally, the expression of immunosuppressive-related genes was analyzed in Ti-Tregs subpopulations.

Statistical analysis

Prism v.8 or v.9 (GraphPad Software) were used for statistical analysis. Two-tailed unpaired Student’s t-tests were used for comparisons between two groups, while one-way ANOVA was used for comparisons among multiple groups. For temporal comparisons, two-way ANOVA was applied. Kaplan-Meier was used for survival analysis. Data (mean ± SEM) are representative at least two independent experiments. *p < .05**p < .01***p < .001****p < .0001.

Results

The expression and function of TNFR2/CCR8 in Ti-Tregs

Previous studies have documented the high and specific expression of TNFR2 or CCR8 in tumor-infiltrating Tregs (Ti-Tregs). Analysis of human colorectal cancer (public dataset GEO: GSE146771) revealed that Tregs exhibit greater expression of TNFR2 and CCR8 than other immune cells (Figure S1a). To further validate the accuracy of the RNA-seq database analysis, we established several subcutaneous tumor models (MC38, KP-13, B16F10 and CT26). TNFR2 and CCR8 were more highly and specifically expressed in Ti-Tregs (Figure 1a,b, Figure S1b-d). The flow cytometry gating strategy as shown in Figure S2. Ti-Tregs exhibit diversity in surface molecule expression and function. Based on this diversity, Ti-Tregs can be categorized into TNFR2+CCR8+ Ti-Treg, TNFR2+CCR8− Ti-Treg, TNFR2−CCR8+ Ti-Treg, and TNFR2−CCR8− Ti-Treg. Among these, the Treg populations co-expressing TNFR2 and CCR8, along with the TNFR2+ CCR8− subset, are the most abundant. In contrast, Treg cells in the spleen and CD8+ T cells within the tumor predominantly consist of TNFR2 and CCR8 double-negative cells (Figure 1c). 

To explore the characteristics of Ti-Tregs expressing either TNFR2 or CCR8, we further analyzed a CRC database (public dataset GEO: GSE146771). The results showed that genes associated with immunosuppressive functions were upregulated in Treg cells expressing TNFR2 or CCR8, particularly in double-positive Treg cells (Figure 1d). We validated the expression of Treg immunosuppressive function-related genes, including Tigit, Ki67, and CTLA4, at both the RNA and protein levels. (Figure 1e,f, Figure S1e,f). The results above indicate that Treg cells expressing both TNFR2 and CCR8 exhibit the main enhancement in proliferative activity and immunosuppressive function, compared to those expressing neither TNFR2 nor CCR8. In contrast, Treg cells expressing only TNFR2 or CCR8 display moderate immunosuppressive capabilities.

Development and characterization of the TNFR2/CCR8 bispecific antibody FT10-Fab

Based on the above observations, we designed and purified a scFv-Fab format bispecific antibody targeting TNFR2 and CCR8 (Figure S3a), with enhanced ADCC capability, and featuring an hIgG1 Fc region (Figure 2a). The amino acid sequences of the antibodies were from public patent. The molecular weight of the FT10-Fab antibody was 125 kDa, with the heavy and light chains 50 kDa and 25 kDa, respectively (Figure S3b). Then, we assessed its characterization. The results demonstrated that FT10-Fab exhibits a lower binding activity compared to anti-TNFR2 or anti-CCR8 monoclonal antibodies (Figure 2b). This difference was likely attributable to its bivalent asymmetric bispecific antibody structure, which possesses only one antigen-binding arm per target, in contrast to the two antigen-binding arms present in monoclonal antibodies that enhance binding through avidity. Nevertheless, the EC50 values of FT10-Fab remain within the nanomolar range, with 2.15 nM for CHO-mTNFR2 and 44.84 nM for CHO-mCCR8, respectively (Figure 2b). We investigate the binding preference of FT10-Fab for tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes. The results demonstrated that FT10-Fab exhibited greater binding activity to Tregs than to either CD8+ T cells or Tconv cells (Figure 2c). Furthermore, FT10-Fab demonstrated superior binding efficiency to Ti-Tregs compared to both anti-TNFR2 and anti-CCR8 antibodies (Figure 2d). Additionally, compared to TNFR2 or CCR8 single-positive Treg cells, FT10-Fab showed higher binding activity toward double-positive Treg cells (Figure 2e). 

FT10-Fab was designed to eliminate Ti-Treg cells, so its ADCC activity was assessed. The ADCC activities of FT10-Fab for CHO-mTNFR2 and CHO-mCCR8, detected using the Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-Luciferase cell line, were 0.005 nM and 0.0068 nM, respectively (Figure 2f). Subsequently, we utilized PBMC cells to assess the apoptotic effects of FT10-Fab on CHO-mTNFR2-mCCR8 target cells (Figure 2g). The data demonstrated that FT10-Fab effectively induced apoptosis in CHO-mTNFR2-mCCR8 cells compared to anti-TNFR2 and anti-CCR8 antibodies (Figure 2h). Furthermore, we assessed the ADCC activity of FT10-Fab against Ti-Tregs using the Jurkat-FcγRIIIa-Luciferase cell line. FT10-Fab showed ADCC activity against Ti-Tregs (Figure 2i). In conclusion, the results confirm that FT10-Fab can specifically bind to Ti-Tregs and exhibit excellent ADCC activity.

FT10-Fab exhibits a superior antitumor effect compared to monoclonal antibodies in vivo

We then sought to study whether the FT10-Fab has a beneficial outcome for tumor control. As shown in Figure 3a and Figure S4a, we established subcutaneous colorectal cancer tumor models in mice using the MC38 and CT26 cell lines. Founding that, although anti-TNFR2 and anti-CCR8 monoclonal antibodies demonstrated partially suppressed tumor growth and decreased tumor weight, the bispecific antibody FT10-Fab exhibited more pronounced anti-tumor activity (Figure 3b,c, Figure S4b,c). Besides, we evaluated the antitumour activity of FT10-Fab in the KP13 non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) tumor model (Figure 3d). We also observed that the antitumour efficacy of FT10-Fab was better than that of the other antibodies (Figure 3e,f). Moreover, the ratio of complete regression was higher in FT10-Fab (5 of 6) (Figure S4d). In the melanoma B16F10 model (Figure 3g), which exhibits a greater degree of malignancy and low immune response, FT10-Fab exhibited superior antitumor efficacy to inhibit tumor growth (Figure 3h) and reduce tumor weight (Figure 3i). Additionally, in multiple tumor models (MC38, KP13, B16F10), treatment with FT10-Fab significantly prolonged survival in mice compared to other treatment groups (Figure S4e). Collectively, these data indicate that in multiple tumor models, bispecific antibody FT10-Fab demonstrates superior anti-tumor efficacy compared to monoclonal antibodies targeting TNFR2 or CCR8. 

FT10-Fab potently suppresses Treg-mediated immunosuppression and augments effector T cell responses

We next investigated the impact of FT10-Fab treatment on individual immune subsets using the MC38 model by flow cytometry (Figure 4a–g). Compared with the vehicle and monotherapy groups, FT10-Fab treatment significantly reduced the proportion of Ti-Tregs (Figure 4a). TNFR2+ CCR8+ and CD73+ Treg cells, which are associated with immunosuppressive functions, were decreased with the treatment of FT10-Fab (Figure 4b). Conversely, the frequency of tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells and CD8/Treg ratio was notably greater in the FT10-Fab therapy group than in the other groups (Figure 4c,d). Moreover, we examined the impact of FT10-Fab on CD8+ T cell infiltration through IHC analysis. The results revealed a significant increase in the number of CD8+ T cells in the FT10-Fab therapy group (Figure 4e,f, Figure S4f). To evaluate the effect of FT10-Fab on CD8 T cell function, we measured the cytotoxic cytokines. The frequency of IL-2, TNF-α and IFN-γ CD8+ T cells were elevated after FT10-Fab treatment relative to the other treatment groups (Figure 4g). Our findings indicated that FT10-Fab modestly increases the absolute number of Tconv cells without altering their proportion within the CD45.2 population (Figure S4g). Additionally, FT10-Fab reduced the proportion of naïve Tconv cells while maintaining effector Tconv cell levels (Figure S4h). Safety assessments revealed that FT10-Fab does not deplete peripheral Treg cells in the blood or spleen (Figure S5a) and does not lead to significant elevations in serum AST or ALT levels, nor does it cause detectable liver or kidney tissue damage, indicating minimal systemic toxicity (Figure S5b-d). Furthermore, analysis of the immunological phenotypes in the KP13 and B16F10 tumor models showed that FT10-Fab treatment elicited immune responses similar to those observed with the MC38 model (Figure S6). Taken together, these findings clearly demonstrate that FT10-Fab effectively depletes Ti-Tregs and enhances effector T cell responses, thereby achieving anti-tumor efficacy. 

The antitumor effectiveness of FT10-Fab depends on CD8+ T cells and promotes robust T-cell memory

To further investigate whether the action of FT10-Fab depends on the immune system, we used Rag1−/− mice to establish an MC38 tumor model (Figure 5a). We found that the antitumor effect of FT10-Fab was abolished, indicating a requirement for adaptive immune responses (Figure 5b). As indicated by the above data, the proportion of CD8+ T cells was increased with the treatment of FT10-Fab. Thus, we used a CD8a depletion antibody to examine whether the antitumor efficacy of the FT10-Fab relies on CD8+ T cells (Figure 5c). Depleting assay confirmed that CD8+ T cells contributed to the therapeutic effect of FT10-Fab (Figure 5d). We also attempted to determine whether treatment with FT10-Fab could induce long-term memory. With the FT10-Fab – treated mice having eradicated CT26 tumors after tumor inoculation, the cured mice were rechallenged with the same tumor cells (Figure 5e). As data showed that all FT10-Fab-cured mice rejected rechallenge tumors (Figure 5f). The FT10-Fab-cured group exhibited significantly greater proportion and proliferation of CD8+ T cells in the spleen (Figure S7a). Moreover, there was a significant increase in the proportion of effector memory CD8+ T cells (CD44+ CD62L−, Tem CD8) in the spleen and blood (Figure S7b. 7c). These findings suggest that FT10-Fab exerts its antitumor effects relying on CD8+ T cells and can induce persistent anti-tumor immune responses. 

FT10-Fab induces changes in the expression pattern of genes related to CD8 functionality

To better understand how FT10-Fab achieves robust antitumour efficacy, we analyzed the changes in the gene expression profile of CD8+ T cells from MC38 model using RNA-seq. The data indicated that genes promoting T-cell activation, such as Prf1, Tnf-α, Il2rα and Il2rβ (Figure 6a,b), and immune memory, such as Cd44, Il7r and Id2 (Figure 6c,d), were upregulated after treatment with FT10-Fab, compared to the monoclonal antibodies. Correspondingly, the gene Sell, which is negatively associated with immune memory, was downregulated after FT10-Fab treatment (Figure 6c). GSEA analysis revealed that the NF-kappa-B signaling pathway was enriched following FT10-Fab treatment. Activation of this pathway can stimulate cell activation and enhance cellular functions (Figure 6e). GO analysis further confirmed the enhanced activation of CD8+ T cells (Figure 6f). Taken together, transcriptome data indicate that FT10-Fab enhances CD8+ T cell function and promotes the formation of immune memory. 

FT10-Fab combined PD-1 blockade induces a strong antitumor effect

The response rate for solid tumors to αPD-1/PD-L1 was only 10% to 30%, with many patients failing to achieve durable clinical benefits. To enhance the response rate, the MC38 tumor-bearing mice were treated with a combination of anti-PD1 and FT10-Fab. The results showed that the combination of FT10-Fab and anti-PD1 significantly outperformed the individual treatments by reducing tumor size and weight (Figure 7a,b). In addition, the combination of PD-1 blockades and FT10-Fab demonstrated a significant survival benefit in mice compared to the control groups (Figure 7c). Notably, FT10-Fab demonstrated significant therapeutic efficacy when combined with PD-1 antibodies in the highly aggressive B16F10 tumor model (Figure S8a. 8b). Compared to the vehicle and anti-PD1 groups, the combination group significantly reduced the proportion of Tregs and TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs (Figure 7d,e). CD8+ T cells and the ratio of CD8/Treg were obviously increased in the combination group than in the other groups (Figure 7f, Figure S8c). We further investigated the impact of combining PD-1 blockade with FT10-Fab on T cell function and found an increase in effector CD8+ T cells, while naive CD8 T cells decreased (Figure 7g). Concurrently, there was a significant increase in the production of cytotoxic cytokines TNF-α and IFN-γ by CD8+ T cells (Figure 7h). From a mechanistic perspective, PD-1 antibody directly enhances CD8+ T cell functionality by relieving exhaustion, while FT10-Fab depletes immunosuppressive Treg cells within the tumor microenvironment, indirectly amplifying CD8+ T cell responses. Therefore, these data indicate that combination therapy with anti-PD1 induced a strong and durable antitumor effect. 

Discussion

Tregs pose a substantial barrier to the initiation of antitumour immune responses and tumor immunotherapy. Hence, it is essential to selectively eliminate tumor-infiltrating Tregs while preserving other immune cells and peripheral Tregs. Importantly, targeting a single surface molecule may be insufficient to effectively encompass Ti-Tregs unless monoclonal antibodies are specifically engineered. For these reasons, we developed a bispecific antibody, FT10-Fab, which targets TNFR2 and CCR8 to specifically deplete Ti-Tregs more efficiently.

In our study, we confirmed the high expression specificity of TNFR2 and CCR8 on Ti-Tregs in various tumor models, and TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs show a greater

immunosuppressive capability, which is consistent with the findings of previous studies. TNFR2 and CCR8 antibodies eliminate tumor-infiltrating Tregs, increase the number of CD8+ T cells and increase the proportion of IFNγ+ CD8 cytotoxic T cells. Our findings show that anti-CCR8 exhibits superior antitumor efficacy compared to anti-TNFR2 in vivo, likely due to its enhanced specificity for Tregs, enabling more efficient depletion of Ti-Tregs. Consequently, bispecific antibodies such as FT10-Fab, which concurrently target both CCR8 and TNFR2, facilitate the selective depletion of distinct tumor-infiltrating Treg subsets, particularly TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs, thereby optimizing therapeutic outcomes. We observed that the antitumour effect of FT10-Fab was superior to that of single anti-TNFR2 or anti-CCR8 treatment in several murine tumor models, especially in the MC38 and KP13 tumor models. FT10-Fab has the ability to bind and eradicate Ti-Tregs expressing TNFR2 or CCR8, particularly the TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs. Treatment with FT10-Fab markedly enhances the frequency of CD8+ T cells within tumors and facilitates long-lasting immune memory. What’s more, an examination of potential side effects revealed that there were no significant adverse effects associated with FT10-Fab in mice.

Compared to targeting a single molecule, bispecific antibodies possess distinct advantages. Therapies that target a single molecule encounter challenge, notably, the increased risk of therapeutic resistance due to the downregulation of a specific molecular target. The FT10-Fab antibody has the potential to reduce the occurrence of antibody tolerance resulting from the downregulation of either TNFR2 or CCR8. However, more evidence is needed to confirm this hypothesis. What’s more, FT10-Fab shows a slight improvement in antitumor efficacy over combination therapy, likely due to its enhanced specificity in targeting tumor-infiltrating Tregs, particularly TNFR2+ CCR8+ Tregs. In contrast, the anti-TNFR2 and anti-CCR8 combination may lack this precision, potentially affecting peripheral immune cells.

Previous studies have shown that TNFR2 is expressed on various cancer cells. Knocking down TNFR2 in tumor cells can inhibit tumor growth and migration. However, in Rag1−/− KO mice implanted with the MC38 tumor, FT10-Fab antibody showed a lack of therapeutic effectiveness (Figure 5b). These data demonstrated that FT10-Fab exhibits limited direct cytotoxicity against tumor cells. We hypothesized that this could be attributed to the lower expression levels of TNFR2 on tumor cells. Other members of the TNFR family, 4-1BB and OX-40, are essential for effector cell activation but are associated with toxicities, including liver damage and anaphylaxis. In contrast, TNFR2-targeting antibodies effectively deplete Tregs without significant toxic side effects thus far. To enhance Treg depletion, the bispecific antibody FT10-Fab was developed to target both TNFR2 and CCR8, directing immune activation to the tumor microenvironment and minimizing off-target effects. This design provides a more selective and safer strategy for tumor-specific immune modulation.

Combining immune checkpoint inhibitors or other immunotherapy methods introduces new treatment strategies and advances in the field of tumor immunotherapy. Anti-PD1 therapy enhances the function of CD8+ T cells by blocking PD-1/PD-L1 signaling to inhibits tumor growth. However, the presence of a substantial number of Tregs within the tumor contributes to the ineffectiveness of PD-1 blockade. In our data, the proportion of Ti-Tregs was decreased following treatment with the FT10-Fab antibody. Therefore, the combination of FT10-Fab and anti-PD1 directly targets two functional immune cell types, promoting CD8+ T cell function while eliminating Ti-Tregs.

In summary, FT10-Fab exerts antitumor effects through dual mechanisms: reduction of Tregs and stimulation of effector CD8+ T cells. Moreover, its combination with PD-1 blockades markedly improved the therapeutic effects (Figure S9). Overall, FT10-Fab, which specifically targets TNFR2 and CCR8 of Ti-Tregs, represents a next-generation immune therapeutic approach.
