# Cancer Cell Permeability Induced by Tumor Treating Fields (TTFields) as a Physical Approach to Improve Chemotherapy Uptake and Overcome Multidrug Resistance.

## Abstract

Multidrug resistance (MDR) is a major challenge in cancer treatment. One predominant MDR mechanism involves the overexpression of ATP-binding cassette transporter proteins on the cell membrane, leading to increased chemotherapy efflux. Strategies to resolve MDR have not yet yielded substantial survival benefits. Tumor Treating Fields (TTField) represent an innovative therapeutic modality for cancer treatment and have been shown to enhance membrane permeability in glioblastoma cells. The current study aimed to characterize this phenomenon and evaluate its potential to increase chemotherapy accumulation, thus overcoming MDR. In vitro analyses using the exclusion dye 7-aminoactinomycin D demonstrated that TTFields-induced enhancement of membrane permeability is pan-cancer while specific to cancer cells, reversible, and requires cell-cycle progression through the G2-M phase. Furthermore, TTFields significantly increased intracellular accumulation of doxorubicin (DOX), mitoxantrone, and cisplatin in resistant cells, restoring uptake to levels observed in sensitive cells, without altering MDR transporter expression. Increased chemotherapy accumulation was confirmed in vivo as demonstrated by elevated DOX accumulation in breast tumors and increased paclitaxel accumulation in lung tumors. Importantly, TTFields sensitized both DOX-sensitive and DOX-resistant cells to DOX-induced cytotoxicity in vitro. In mouse models bearing breast tumors, co-administration of therapeutic or sub-therapeutic DOX doses together with TTFields significantly reduced tumor growth compared with either treatment alone. In conclusion, the findings suggest that adding TTFields to chemotherapy regimens may enhance drug delivery and efficacy in tumors exhibiting MDR. Further clinical studies evaluating TTFields concomitant with chemotherapy in patients with MDR cancer are warranted.

Introduction

Multidrug resistance (MDR) remains a major clinical obstacle in cancer treatment, affecting a wide range of malignancies. MDR mechanisms can be intrinsic or acquired following chemotherapy exposure. The MDR phenotype is multifaceted, involving suppression of apoptotic pathways, activation of survival signals (i.e., the PI3K/AKT pathway), changes in tumor–host interactions, enhanced metabolism of xenobiotics, increased DNA repair capacity, and altered expression of membrane transporters. Per the latter, a predominant mechanism involves increased drug efflux driven by the overexpression of ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters on cancer cell membranes, particularly P-glycoprotein (P-gp)/ABC subfamily B member 1, MDR protein 1 (MRP1)/ABC subfamily C member 1, and breast cancer resistance protein/ABC subfamily G member 2.

Various strategies have been explored to enhance local chemotherapy concentrations within tumors, thus reducing systemic toxicity and potentially overcoming transporter-mediated MDR. Despite significant efforts, the development of nontoxic ABC transporter inhibitors to sensitize cancer cells to chemotherapeutic agents has not substantially improved patient survival. Alternative approaches being actively investigated include nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems, gene silencing of MDR-related genes via siRNA, and electrochemotherapy (ECT) - a technique employing short-duration (nanoseconds to milliseconds) high-intensity electric pulses (250–3,000 V/cm) that transiently permeabilize cancer cell membranes, enhancing drug entry.

Tumor Treating Fields (TTField) represent an innovative, noninvasive therapeutic modality involving continuous, low-intensity (1–3 V/cm) alternating electric fields applied to tumors through arrays placed on the skin. TTFields therapy is currently approved for glioblastoma, pleural mesothelioma, and metastatic non–small cell lung cancer when administered alongside standard chemotherapies. Ongoing research is investigating TTFields’ efficacy across a broader spectrum of solid tumors. TTFields disrupt essential cellular processes, specifically within cancer cells, by interfering with polarized intracellular structures such as spindle microtubules, thereby selectively impairing cancer cell division and survival. Disruption of spindle assembly during mitosis results in chromosome missegregation and subsequent cancer cell death, effectively slowing tumor growth. In addition to their antimitotic properties, recent studies demonstrate that TTFields can enhance cancer cell membrane permeability, suggesting that they may potentially facilitate increased chemotherapeutic uptake, similarly to ECT. Unlike ECT, TTFields offer continuous, noninvasive treatment suitable for deep-seated solid tumors without harming surrounding normal tissues, making them uniquely fitted for co-application with repeated chemotherapy cycles.

The current study aimed to further characterize the potential of TTFields to increase cancer cell membrane permeability and assess whether this phenomenon may enhance chemotherapy uptake in order to overcome transporter-mediated MDR and sensitize cancer cells to chemotherapy.

Materials and Methods

Cell culture

The mammary carcinoma [4T1-Luc2 (4T1); RRID: CVCL_A4BM], glioblastoma (U-87 MG; RRID: CVCL_0022), Lewis lung carcinoma (LL/2; RRID: CVCL_A4CM), uterine sarcoma (MES-SA; RRID: CVCL_1404), human brain microvascular endothelial cells (HBMVEC), and lung fibroblast (MRC-5; RRID: CVCL_0440) cell lines were obtained from the ATCC. The ovarian adenocarcinoma (A2780; RRID: CVCL_0134) cell line was obtained from the European Collection of Cell Cultures. The resistant MES-SA/MX2 (RRID: CVCL_2599) and A2780cis (RRID: CVCL_1942) cells were obtained from ATCC and AddexBio, respectively. Resistant 4T1 cells (4T1-MDR) were developed from parental 4T1 cells through stepwise exposure to increasing concentrations of doxorubicin (DOX) up to 500 nmol/L.

Cells were cultured at 37°C in a humidified incubator containing 5% CO2 using the following media: RPMI for 4T1, 4T1-MDR (supplemented with 500 nmol/L DOX), A2780, and A2780cis [supplemented with 1 µmol/L cisplatin (CIS)]; DMEM for U-87 MG and LL/2; Eagle Minimum Essential Medium for MRC-5; McCoy 5A for MES-SA and MES-SA/MX2; and F-12K for HBMVEC. Media were supplemented with 10% (v/v) FBS, 2 mmol/L L-glutamine, and penicillin/streptomycin (50 µg/mL). All media and supplements were obtained from Biological Industries (Beit Haemek). Cell lines were purchased between 2016 and 2023. Cells were used for up to 15 passages after thawing. No authentication test was performed. All cell lines were tested for Mycoplasma contamination using MycoStrips (InvivoGen, cat. #rep-mys1). The standard laboratory protocol for Mycoplasma testing included screening prior to cell bank cryopreservation, followed by re-testing of all thawed cells on a monthly basis.

TTFields application in vitro

Cells were seeded onto 22-mm-diameter glass coverslips and allowed to adhere overnight. Coverslips were then transferred into inovitro dishes containing 2 mL of culture medium. TTFields (1.7V/cm RMS) were applied using the inovitro system as previously described at frequencies and durations described below.

TTFields frequency scans

4T1, U-87 MG, A2780, LL/2, MES-SA, MRC-5, and HBMVEC cells were exposed to TTFields at frequencies ranging from 100 to 400 kHz. 7-aminoactinomycin D (7-AAD; BioLegend, cat. #420404) at a final concentration of 1 µg/mL was added to the cells 15 minutes prior to the end of 24-hour TTFields treatment. Flow cytometry was conducted using a MACSQuant Analyzer 10 (Miltenyi Biotec), and the percentage of 7-AAD–positive cells and the fold change in 7-AAD median fluorescence intensity were calculated. Flow cytometry was also used to assess cell counts (expressed as percentage relative to control) of cells exposed to differnet TTFields frequencies for 72 hours.

7-AAD accumulation kinetics

4T1 and U-87 MG cells were treated with TTFields (300 kHz) for durations of up to 72 hours. 7-AAD (1 µg/mL) was added 15 minutes prior to treatment end and measured by flow cytometry as described above. To test reversibility (release), 7-AAD was added 30 minutes, 15 minutes, or immediately following cessation of 24-hour TTFields application.

Cell cycle analysis

4T1 cells were treated with TTFields (300 kHz) for 24, 48, or 72 hours with or without synchronization at the G1–S boundary using 2 mmol/L thymidine (Sigma, cat. #T9250) at the initiation of TTFields application. 7-AAD (1 µg/mL) was added to cells 15 minutes prior to the end of TTFields treatment. Upon treatment cessation, the cells were stained with Hoechst reagent (10 µg/mL; Thermo Fisher Scientific, cat. #33342) and Zombie NIR viability dye (BioLegend, cat. #423106, 1:100) and analyzed by flow cytometry.

DOX accumulation in vitro

4T1 cells were exposed to TTFields (300 kHz) for 24 hours. DOX (Sigma, cat. #D2975000) at a final concentration of 10 µmol/L was added 1 hour before or 30 minutes after (release) TTFields treatment completion, and confocal microscopy (LSM 700, Zeiss) was conducted following fixation in 4% paraformaldehyde for 10 minutes.

Chemotherapy accumulation in parental versus MDR cells

4T1 and 4T1-MDR, MES-SA and MES-SA/MX2, or A2780 and A2780cis cells were exposed to TTFields (300 kHz for 4T1 cells; 150 kHz for MES-SA and A2780 cells) for 24 hours. DOX at 10 µmol/L, mitoxantrone (MTX; Sigma, cat. #M2305000) at 1 µmol/L, or cy3-tagged CIS (MedChemExpress, cat. #HY-D2452) at 10 µmol/L were added 1 hour before treatment completion. Chemotherapy uptake was evaluated by flow cytometry and expressed as fold change relative to untreated parental cells.

qRT-PCR

RNA was isolated from the cells using the Total RNA Purification Plus Kit (Norgen Biotek, cat. #48300), and cDNA was synthesized using the qScript Synthesis Kit (Quantabio, cat. #95047) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. qRT-PCR was carried out with the qPCRBIO SyGreen Blue Mix (PCR Biosystems, cat. #PB20.15-05) on a QuantStudio 1 thermocycler (Applied Biosystems) utilizing the primers listed in Table 1. The housekeeping genes human UBC and mouse RPLP13a were used for normalization using the QuantStudio software.

Western blot analysis

Cells were treated with TTFields for 24 hours or left untreated. Cell lysates containing 30 µg of protein were analyzed by Western blot analysis as described previously. The membranes were probed with rabbit monoclonal anti–P-gp 1 antibody (Abcam, RRID: AB_2687930; 1:1,000), followed by incubation with goat anti-rabbit horseradish peroxidase–conjugated secondary antibody (Abcam, cat. #ab6721; 1:10,000). Protein signals were visualized using Immobilon Forte substrate (Millipore) and the GeneGnome XRQ imaging system (AlphaMetrix Biotech).

DOX efficacy in vitro

4T1 and 4T1-MDR cells were treated for 72 hours with TTFields (300 kHz), alone or concomitantly with 10 nmol/L DOX for 4T1 and 300 nmol/L DOX for 4T1-MDR cells. Cell counts were measured by flow cytometry at the end of the treatment period and expressed as percentage relative to control. Alternatively, the cells were stained with annexin V-FITC (BioLegend, cat. #640945) and Viobility dye (Miltenyi Biotec, cat. #130-110-205) according to the manufacturer’s instructions, and apoptosis was assessed by flow cytometry.

Animal care

Animal experiments were approved by the Novocure Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee and the Israeli National Committee Council for Experiments on Animal Subjects (approval numbers IL-19-10-422, NPC-No-IL-2212-355-4, and NPC-No-IL-2307-424-4). All procedures were conducted in accordance with applicable guidelines. Female BALB/c (RRID: MGI:2683685) and male C57BL/6 (RRID: IMSR_JAX:000664) mice (10–12 weeks old) were purchased from Harlan Laboratories. Housing and anesthesia protocols followed previously described methods.

DOX accumulation in vivo

Female BALB/c mice were orthotopically inoculated with 350,000 4T1 cells (in 50 µL PBS) into the third mammary fat pad. Seven days after inoculation, mice were randomized into sham (heat) or TTFields-treated groups (2.74 ± 0.27 V/cm RMS; 300 kHz; inovivo device; refs.). TTFields treatment was continuous for 3 days using arrays designed for subcutaneous tumors. DOX (5 mg/kg) was administered intraperitoneally 24 hours prior to treatment cessation. DOX fluorescence was quantified ex vivo for whole tumor using the In Vivo Imaging System (PerkinElmer) and by flow cytometry following tumor dissociation (Miltenyi Biotech, cat. #130-096-730).

Paclitaxel accumulation in vivo

Male C57BL/6 mice were orthotopically inoculated with 4,500 LL/2 cells in Matrigel (5 µL) into the left lung lobe. After 10 days, tumors were confirmed by MRI and mice randomized to sham (heat) or TTFields treatment (1.86 ± 0.67 V/cm RMS; 300 kHz; inovivo device; refs.). TTFields were applied continuously for 3 days using arrays designed for torso-residing tumors. Paclitaxel (PTX; 13.5 mg/kg) was administered intraperitoneally 1.5 hours before treatment end. PTX concentrations were determined ex vivo by LC/MS-MS analysis.

LC/MS-MS quantification of PTX

PTX concentrations were quantified by LC/MS-MS using exemestane as an internal standard. Tumor and plasma samples were prepared via methanol extraction. To 10 μL plasma samples, 450 μL of ice-cold methanol and 5 μL of exemestane (10 μg/mL; internal standard) methanolic solution were added and vortexed thoroughly. To tumor samples, 200 μL of ice-cold methanol and 5 μL of exemestane (10 μg/mL; internal standard) methanolic solution were added, followed by homogenization in a bead beater (22 Hz; 1 minute, 3 × 2.5-mm diameter beads; MM 400 mixer mill, Retsch) and addition of 300 μL ice-cold methanol. Plasma and tumor samples were then agitated on a shaker (10°C at 1,200 rpm for 40 minutes) and centrifuged (6°C at 21,000 g for 15 minutes), and 100 μL the supernatant was collected for LC/MS-MS analysis.

The LC/MS-MS instrument consisted of an Acquity UPLC I-Class System (Waters) and a Xevo TQ-S Triple Quadrupole Mass Spectrometer (Waters) equipped with an electrospray ion source and operated in the positive ion mode. MassLynx and TargetLynx software (v.4.2, Waters) were used for data acquisition and analysis. Chromatographic separation was performed on a 1.7-μm Acquity UPLC BEH C18 column (100 × 2.1 mm internal diameter; Waters), with mobile phases A (0.1% aqueous formic acid, v/v) and B (0.1% formic acid in acetonitrile, v/v) at a flow rate of 0.3 mL/minute. A gradient used was 0 to 6 minutes linear increase from 40% to 100% B, 6 to 6.5 minutes 100% B, 6.5 to 7 minutes back to 40% B, and 7 to 9 minutes 40% B. For mass spectrometry, argon was used as the collision gas at a flow of 0.10 mL/minute. Analytes were detected in positive mode using multiple reaction monitoring at 854.3 > 105.0, 854.3 > 286.1, and 854.3 > 569.3 m/z, with collision energies of 55, 14, and 14 eV, respectively, for PTX, and 297.1 > 121.1 and 297.1 > 149.1 m/z for exemestane. The PTX concentration was calculated using a standard curve.

DOX efficacy in vivo

Female BALB/c mice were inoculated orthotopically with 4T1 or 4T1-MDR cells as described above. After tumor establishment (7 or 10 days, respectively), mice were treated with TTFields (as described above) or sham alongside vehicle or DOX (1 mg/kg for 4T1; 5 mg/kg for 4T1-MDR). Tumor volumes were monitored using Vernier calipers, and the volume was calculated using the formula width2 × length × 0.5.

Statistical analysis

Data are presented as mean ± SEM for in vitro or mean ± SD for in vivo. Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 10 software (RRID: SCR_002798), as detailed in figure legends, with significance defined as *, P < 0.05; **, P < 0.01, and ***, P < 0.001.

Results

TTFields-induced cancer cell permeability is a pan-cancer phenomenon absent in noncancerous cells

The optimal frequency for the cytotoxic effects of TTFields varies according to cancer type, prompting evaluation of their effects on cellular permeability across multiple cancer cell lines over a relevant frequency range. Cellular permeability was assessed by measuring the uptake of the fluorescent exclusion dye 7-AAD, which cannot traverse intact plasma membranes of viable cells. Cells were exposed to TTFields for 24 hours, and 7-AAD was added 15 minutes prior to the end of treatment. Maximal membrane permeability occurred at 300 kHz in mammary carcinoma (4T1), glioblastoma (U-87 MG), and Lewis lung carcinoma (LL/2) cells, whereas uterine sarcoma (MES-SA) and ovarian carcinoma (A2780) cells exhibited peak permeability at 150 kHz (Fig. 1A–E; Supplementary S1A–S1D). These frequencies differed from those inducing maximal cytotoxicity [150 kHz for 4T1 (Supplementary Fig. S1E) and LL/2, 200 kHz for U-87 MG and A2780, and 100 kHz for MES-SA cells (Supplementary Fig. S1F)]. Notably, noncancerous cells (lung fibroblasts, MRC-5; brain endothelial cells, HBMVEC) showed no increased permeability following TTFields exposure (Fig. 1F and G).

TTFields-induced permeability is time-dependent, reversible, and cell cycle specific

Time course experiments using 4T1 cells demonstrated progressively increasing membrane permeability during TTFields application, with significant intracellular accumulation of 7-AAD detectable from 16 hours onward (Fig. 2A). Reversibility assays in 4T1 and U-87 MG cells indicated no detectable 7-AAD accumulation when the dye was introduced after termination of a 24-hour TTFields treatment (Fig. 2B; Supplementary Fig. S2A). Importantly, cell counts for cells exposed to TTFields for 24 hours revealed no reduction compared with untreated controls (Supplementary Fig. S2B). Together, these findings strongly suggest transient enhancement of membrane permeability rather than irreversible, cell death–associated membrane damage.

To further elucidate the mechanism, cell cycle distribution was analyzed in TTFields-treated cells differentiated by permeability status (7-AAD–positive versus 7-AAD–negative cells; gating strategy in Supplementary Fig. S2C). Although total cell populations showed no significant cell cycle alterations after 24, 48, or 72 hours of TTFields exposure compared with untreated controls (approximately 50% G1 phase and 25% G2–M phase; Supplementary Fig. S2D), permeable cells predominantly distributed to the G2–M phase after 24 hours of TTFields exposure (50%; with only 22% in the G1 phase; Fig. 2C). Extended treatment durations increased permeability among G1-phase cells (up to 42%), accompanied by a corresponding decrease in G2–M-phase cells. Collectively, these results suggest that the G2–M phase is critical for TTFields-induced permeability.

To confirm the necessity of G2–M transition for permeability induction, the cells were arrested at G1–S phase using thymidine treatment (Fig. 2D). G1–S arrest completely prevented TTFields-induced permeability (Fig. 2E), reinforcing the requirement for progression through G2–M to induce permeability. Permeability observed in the subsequent cell cycle phases thus likely results from previous G2–M transition under TTFields exposure.

TTFields enhance chemotherapy uptake and cytotoxicity in both sensitive and resistant cancer cell lines in vitro

The transient increase in membrane permeability following TTFields exposure prompted us to assess DOX uptake, exploiting its intrinsic fluorescence for quantitative analysis (analogous to 7-AAD assays). To avoid any confounding cytotoxic effects of DOX, cells were incubated with DOX for only 1 hour immediately before measurement. As shown in Fig. 3A, control cells exhibited baseline DOX accumulation, whereas cells treated with 24 hours of TTFields displayed a significant increase in intracellular DOX. In the “release” condition—in which DOX was added immediately after cessation of TTFields treatment—uptake returned to control levels. These findings confirm that enhanced DOX uptake is driven by the transient TTFields-induced permeability and not by nonspecific cell death.

To investigate the potential reversal of MDR by TTFields, resistant variants of 4T1 cells (4T1-MDR) and MES-SA cells (MES-SA/MX2), both of which overexpress P-gp compared with their parental counterparts (Supplementary Fig. S3A), were utilized. TTFields significantly enhanced DOX uptake in resistant 4T1-MDR and MES-SA/MX2 cells, bringing uptake levels close to those observed in their sensitive parental cell lines (Fig. 3B), without altering P-gp expression (Supplementary Fig. S3B). Similar enhancement was observed for MTX uptake in both resistant cell lines. TTFields also significantly increased CIS uptake in resistant A2780cis cells compared with untreated controls. Notably, A2780cis cells did not overexpress P-gp. Taken together, these results indicate that TTFields-mediated enhancement of chemotherapy uptake is neither specific to a particular chemotherapeutic agent nor to a particular cell type. Importantly, as transporter expression remained unchanged, the data suggest that TTFields enhance drug uptake primarily by increasing chemotherapy influx rather than by impairing drug efflux.

Next, we investigated the cytotoxic effect of DOX alone relative to DOX concomitant with TTFields. DOX cytotoxicity experiments utilized minimal effective concentrations (Supplementary Fig. S4A for DOX dose–response curves). TTFields treatment alone reduced the cell count to 60% and 84% of control in 4T1 and 4T1-MDR cells, respectively. Concomitant DOX and TTFields application exhibited synergistic cytotoxicity, reducing cell count to 40% and 48% of control for 4T1 and 4T1-MDR cells, respectively, and increasing apoptosis rates (Fig. 3C; Supplementary Fig. S4B).

TTFields enhance in vivo tumor uptake of DOX and PTX

To further investigate the influence of TTFields on cancer cell permeability, we used two syngeneic mouse models, with TTFields treatment initiated prior to chemotherapy administration. In the first model, 4T1 breast cancer cells were inoculated into the mammary fat pad, TTFields or sham heat were applied for 72 hours, and DOX was administered 24 hours prior to treatment cessation (Fig. 4A and B). Orthotopic 4T1 breast cancer–bearing mice exhibited elevated DOX accumulation after TTFields treatment compared with controls, both in whole tumors and tumor-derived single-cell suspensions (Fig. 4C). The single‐cell suspensions assays enabled exclution of infiltrating leukocytes and thereby ensured that measurements reflect uptake by tumor cells rather than the bulk tumor (Supplementary Fig. S5 for gating strategy). In the second model, LL/2 Lewis lung carcinoma cells were inoculated into the lung, TTFields or sham heat were applied for 72 hours, and PTX was administered 1.5 hours prior to treatment cessation (Fig. 4D and E). Orthotopic LL/2 lung carcinoma–bearing mice demonstrated significantly enhanced PTX accumulation following TTFields treatment compared with controls (Fig. 4F).

In the breast cancer model TTFields delivery was conducted using arrays designed for subcutaneous tumors, and was hence completely locoregional to the tumor. However, in the lung model, torso TTField arrays were used, exposing also the healthy lung (to which tumor cells were not inoculated) to TTFields. TTFields application did not elevate PTX uptake in noncancerous lung tissue, supporting the cancer cell specificity of TTFields-induced permeability (Fig. 4F).

TTFields enhance in vivo efficacy of DOX in DOX-sensitive and MDR tumors

Based on our observation that TTFields can resensitize cancer cells to DOX in vitro and improve DOX accumulation in vivo, we selected the 4T1 and 4T1-MDR cell lines to test the potential of using TTFields to improve the efficacy of DOX in vivo. The cells were inoculated into the third mammary fat pad, a model that is less aggressive than the classical fourth mammary fat pad model. Injection into this anatomic site was selected as it allows a good fit around the tumor of the subcutaneous TTFields and sham arrays. Tumor progression was tracked throughout the 8 days of TTFields or sham treatment, with two DOX or vehicle administrations given 2 and 6 days after treatment initiation.

In 4T1 tumors, DOX monotherapy at a dose five-fold lower than the customary dose used in this model did not cause a meaningful tumor volume reduction relative to the control group. Adding TTFields significantly enhanced the therapeutic response, reducing tumor volumes compared with control, DOX alone, or TTFields alone (Fig. 5A and B). Notably, there were no significant differences in tumor volumes between mice treated with TTFields alone and the control group; however, the TTFields frequency used in these experiments was tuned to the maximal effect on cellular permeability and not to the maximal cytotoxic effect. Importantly, minor body weight reduction was seen in the control group because of tumor progression and procedural stress. As a low dose of DOX was used in these experiments, the DOX treatment did not significantly reduce mouse body weight relative to control, either when applied alone or together with TTFields (Fig. 5C).

In 4T1-MDR tumors, TTFields combined with therapeutic DOX doses similarly demonstrated significant tumor growth inhibition relative to single-agent treatments (Fig. 5D and E). TTFields application did not exacerbate DOX-associated weight loss, indicating no additional systemic toxicity (Fig. 5F).

Discussion

TTFields constitute an established antimitotic treatment modality inducing mitotic catastrophe in actively dividing cancer cells. Chang and colleagues previously described a phenomenon in which TTFields enhance cancer cell membrane permeability. The current study aimed to further characterize the optimal physical and biological conditions underlying this permeability enhancement, evaluating its potential to improve chemotherapy delivery and therapeutic efficacy.

Our findings indicate that TTFields-induced membrane permeability is a universal phenomenon across various cancer cell types, including brain, lung, breast, ovarian, and uterine tumors, and across different chemotherapy agents, as demonstrated for DOX, MTX, CIS, and PTX, allowing for reversal of the cancer cells’ MDR phenotype. Interestingly, the frequencies that optimally enhanced membrane permeability differed from those previously associated with maximum cytotoxicity.

Noncancerous cell lines (lung fibroblasts and brain endothelial cells) did not exhibit increased permeability under similar conditions, supporting Chang and colleagues’ findings that this permeability enhancement is specific to cancer cells. Furthermore, orthotopic breast and lung cancer mouse models confirmed increased tumor accumulation of chemotherapeutic agents following TTFields exposure without affecting adjacent healthy tissues, reinforcing this selective permeability effect in vivo.

Our data also provide another layer of evidence to support the kinetic analyses by Chang and colleagues, demonstrating that membrane permeability enhancement by TTFields is dependent on time, becoming significantly evident after 16 hours. The reversibility observed immediately after TTFields cessation emphasizes that permeability enhancement is transient and distinct from irreversible cell membrane damage associated with cell death. Our data suggest a critical role for the G2–M phase of the cell cycle as permeability predominantly occurs in cells progressing through this phase. Cell cycle blockage experiments confirmed that entry into the G2–M phase is necessary for permeability induction, indicating that permeability observed in other phases results from cells having previously transitioned through G2–M under TTFields exposure. Additionally, cancer cells typically exhibit distinct, often more depolarized membrane potentials compared with normal cells. The dependence of TTFields-induced permeability on G2–M progression, coupled with inherent differences in membrane potential, likely explains the observed specificity of TTFields effects toward cancer cells and the absence of permeability enhancement in normal, nonproliferating cells.

Importantly, TTFields significantly sensitized both sensitive and MDR cells to DOX cytotoxicity in vitro and improved therapeutic outcomes in vivo. These observations indicate the potential clinical utility of TTFields in overcoming chemotherapy resistance. Additionally, the kinetics of TTFields-induced permeability suggest that their application prior to and during chemotherapy administration may permit dose reduction, potentially mitigating chemotherapy-related adverse effects.

Although the precise mechanism underlying TTFields-induced permeability remains unclear, we have identified several key features of this phenomenon, including reversibility, lack of effect on ABC transporter expression, specific susceptibility of cells in the G2–M phase, and cancer cell specificity. These characteristics suggest that future mechanistic investigations should focus on voltage-gated ion channels and differences in membrane potentials between cancerous and normal cells. Currently, precise electrophysiologic measurements are limited by technical constraints because of electrical noise inherent in the TTFields experimental system. Exposure to electric fields can induce transmembrane voltage alterations, potentially leading to transient electroporation at sufficient intensities. The effects of membrane electroporation only become detectable when the induced transmembrane voltage exceeds a certain “critical” value. This value can range from 250 mV to 1 V, depending on the duration of exposure and experimental conditions. The potential of sine waves to induce electro-permeabilization has been previously demonstrated; however, it is unclear whether the intensities used in this study (1.7 V/cm RMS) would suffice to exceed the “threshold” voltage for membrane electroporation, even though it is strongly dependent on the duration of the applied electric field. Although TTFields intensities used here are typically below electroporation thresholds, theoretical analyses by Aguilar and colleagues suggest localized signal amplification at membrane pores and cellular junctions, providing a plausible explanation for TTFields-induced membrane permeability effects. Similar models predicted TTFields-induced depolarization to be sufficient to open voltage-gated calcium channels or induce mechanical membrane stress. Although this mechanism of TTFields resembles that of ECT, it is important to point out that the noninvasiveness, lack of systemic toxicity, and continuous delivery of TTFields therapy provide major advantages to this modality for the treatment of solid tumors.

A critical unresolved question is whether the antimitotic (cytotoxic) and permeability-enhancing effects of TTFields represent two distinct mechanisms or different manifestations of a single underlying process. Several observations support the existence of distinct mechanisms: (i) different optimal frequencies for cytotoxic versus permeabilizing effects, (ii) rapid reversibility of permeability upon cessation of TTFields, and (iii) exclusion of cell death as a primary cause for membrane permeability. Nevertheless, the requirement for cells to progress through the G2–M phase in both processes complicates definitive mechanistic differentiation.

In conclusion, our findings highlight important implications for incorporating TTFields into clinical strategies aiming to enhance the effectiveness of standard chemotherapeutics, particularly in cancers exhibiting MDR phenotypes. Future studies could explore exploiting TTFields-induced cancer cell permeability for use with small-molecule targeted therapies, such as PARP inhibitors.
