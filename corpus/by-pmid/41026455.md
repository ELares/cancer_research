# Genetic and epigenetic contribution to tamoxifen resistance in breast cancer, concepts to drive therapeutic strategies.

## Abstract

Patients with estrogen receptor-positive (ER+) breast carcinoma often receive tamoxifen as an anti-estrogen therapy; however, a significant number still experience disease recurrence during treatment. The molecular mechanisms behind tamoxifen resistance are not yet fully understood. TAM mainly works by inhibiting estrogen receptor signaling pathways, inducing apoptosis, and causing growth arrest. Factors that influence TAM response include impaired bioactivation of tamoxifen and changes in the expression of tumor suppressor genes and proto-oncogenes, which are essential for regulating the cell cycle. Additionally, host factors, such as the processes involved in TAM bioactivation and drug transporters, play a crucial role in the therapeutic response. Understanding the interplay between tumor characteristics and host factors could provide valuable insights into TAM efficacy. Variations in gene expression associated with tamoxifen resistance may result from a combination of genetic mutations and epigenetic modifications. Clarifying the precise mechanisms of TAM response is crucial for improving treatment efficacy. Several therapeutic strategies have been explored to enhance TAM response, including drug replacement, epigenetic therapy, natural compounds, and innovative approaches like Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASOs). Combining these treatment modalities may improve patient survival more effectively than using tamoxifen alone.

Introduction

Breast cancer (BC) is the most common cancer and the second leading cause of cancer-related death among women worldwide. The primary therapeutic goals for estrogen receptor positive (ER+) patients are to reduce estrogen expression levels and block ER signaling pathways. Estrogen, mainly produced by the ovaries, is vital for the growth and development of mammary glands. Its production stops in postmenopausal women, leading to a reliance on adrenal androgens for estrogen synthesis. The ER is crucial for breast development and serves as a key target in breast cancer therapy, with specific domains regulating its transcriptional activity.

Tamoxifen (1-[4-(2-dimethylaminoethoxy)-phenyl]1,2-diphenylbut-1(Z)-ene) has been used as an anti-estrogen treatment for over four decades and was approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1977. Tamoxifen therapy is an essential part of BC treatment for premenopausal ER+ patients. It acts as an anti-estrogen by competing with estrogen for ER binding, thereby inhibiting ER signaling and reducing recurrence in breast tumors. Additionally, TAM was approved as a chemopreventive agent for women with a familial history of BC. Although this anti-estrogen has saved numerous lives, approximately half of patients experience de novo or acquired resistance during treatment, indicating that not all patients benefit uniformly. Like other malignancies, the molecular basis of breast cancers is heterogeneous and complex. Major areas of BC research focus on intertumor and intratumor heterogeneity related to disease recurrence. Different molecular subtypes of breast tumors exhibit various clinical behaviors and therapeutic outcomes. Significant individual variations in genetics, epigenetics, pharmacogenetics, and tolerance to anticancer drugs among BC patients lead to differing responses to chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and anti-hormone treatment. Furthermore, breast cancer is a dynamic disease, and discrepancies in immunohistochemical characteristics, including HER2 (human epidermal growth factor receptor 2) status and hormone receptor statuses (estrogen receptor and progesterone receptor), have been reported between primary tumors and their corresponding metastatic tissues. A clearer understanding of the underlying mechanisms of TAM resistance will provide better insights into the disease and lead to the development of more effective treatment strategies. This review provides a comprehensive overview of the genetic and epigenetic modifications associated with tamoxifen resistance in breast cancer. Initially, the ER was identified as the primary target of TAM in hormone-responsive tumors. The review explores the interplay between ER signaling and growth factor receptors, which are critical for the TAM response, and highlights how activated growth factor pathways and defects in apoptosis contribute to treatment failure. Additionally, it addresses the roles of tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes in regulating cellular growth and programmed cell death. Epigenetic modifications, such as DNA methylation and histone modifications, further influence chromatin dynamics and gene expression, leading to TAM resistance by silencing tumor suppressor genes or activating oncogenes. Considering the interplay between tumor and host characteristics can provide a clearer understanding of TAM treatment outcomes. Host characteristics primarily include cell-membrane transporters and drug metabolism pathways. The expression levels of transporters significantly affect the intracellular concentration of TAM in breast cancer cells. A reduction in intracellular TAM concentrations, due to decreased influx or increased efflux, can be described as pharmacological tolerance mechanisms. Tamoxifen metabolism pathways play crucial roles in converting tamoxifen to its active metabolites. Overall, the interactions among environmental factors, host characteristics, and the molecular traits of breast tumors can offer precise evidence to predict TAM response. This complex mechanism underscores the necessity of study about targeted epigenetic therapies and innovative treatment strategies to overcome disease recurrence in breast cancer patients treated with TAM, emphasizing potential natural and synthetic therapeutic approaches. Tamoxifen’s mechanism: disrupting ER signaling and promoting apoptosis

TAM plays a crucial role in inhibiting the ER signaling pathway, especially in the context of ER+ breast tumors. In premenopausal women, estrogen is primarily produced by the ovaries, while in postmenopausal women, it is generated through the peripheral conversion of androgens. Estrogen activates the ER, leading to the transcription of genes that promote breast tissue growth. As a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM), TAM acts as an anti-estrogen by competitively binding to the ligand-binding domain of the ER, particularly near Activation Function-2 (AF2). This binding inactivates ER signaling pathways, significantly reducing both local and distant recurrence of ER+ tumors. Additionally, TAM recruits co-repressors such as nuclear receptor co-repressor (N-CoR) and silencing mediator for retinoid and thyroid receptors (SMRT) to ER-regulated promoters, thereby repressing transcription and mitigating the adverse effects of estrogen on tumor growth.

TAM affects cell cycle regulation in breast cancer by decreasing the expression of key promoters such as c-Myc and cyclin D1, while increasing inhibitors like p21WAF1/CIP1. This shift can induce cell cycle arrest and apoptosis. The loss of p21 expression is associated with the progression of breast carcinoma and a poor prognosis in TAM-treated patients. Furthermore, the retinoblastoma (pRB) tumor suppressor protein is essential for controlling the G1 checkpoint; a deficiency in pRB allows tumor cells to bypass TAM-induced cell cycle arrest. Cyclin D1, an important regulator of the G1 phase, is frequently overexpressed in breast cancer and its transcription is dependent on the estrogen receptor, which TAM can reduce. Notably, cyclin D1 can also activate the estrogen receptor independently. Patients with moderate levels of cyclin D1 may benefit from TAM treatment, whereas those with low or high levels do not. In summary, the interaction between tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes is crucial for regulating cell growth and apoptosis in the context of TAM resistance.

Evasion of apoptosis is crucial in the development and recurrence of breast cancer. The extrinsic apoptosis pathway is activated when ligands bind to death receptors, forming the death-inducing signaling complex (DISC) and activating caspases that initiate cell death. This process also involves the mitochondrial pathway, where caspase-8 cleaves BID, leading to the release of cytochrome c and other factors that activate caspase-9, ultimately resulting in apoptosis through executioner caspases.

The intrinsic apoptosis pathway is regulated by a balance between pro-apoptotic and anti-apoptotic members of the BCL-2 family in response to various internal signals. While TAM is primarily recognized for its anti-estrogenic effects in estrogen receptor-positive patients, it also promotes apoptosis through mechanisms that do not involve estrogen receptors. This enhances its effectiveness in treating various malignancies, including breast cancer. Inducing successful apoptosis with TAM is essential for countering cancer progression, as it reduces BCL-2 expression at both transcriptional and protein levels in a dose- and time-dependent manner.

Estradiol enhances BCL-2 protein expression, counteracting the inhibitory effects of TAM on the BCL-2 pathway. As a result, the apoptosis-inducing effects of TAM may be reduced by estrogen. Furthermore, the cytostatic effects of TAM can be diminished by the expression of survivin, an anti-apoptotic protein predominantly expressed during the G2/M phase of the cell cycle. Survivin directly inhibits the activity of terminal effector proteases involved in cell death, such as caspase-3 and caspase-7. Protein phosphatase 2 A (PP2A), a family of proteins with serine/threonine phosphatase activity, acts as a pro-apoptotic agent by inhibiting cell cycle progression, growth, and survival. It negatively regulates cdc2 kinase, MAP kinase, and PK-B/Akt kinase. The cancerous inhibitor of protein phosphatase 2 A (CIP2A) interacts with PP2A, preventing the proteolytic degradation of C-myc by inhibiting its dephosphorylation at serine 62. In estrogen receptor-negative (ER−) breast cancer cells, low levels of phosphorylated AKT (p-AKT) and CIP2A are associated with characteristics of TAM-induced apoptosis. Increased expression of CIP2A or AKT reduces the apoptosis rate in TAM-treated cells. Moreover, CIP2A knockdown through short interfering RNA (siRNA) transfection can induce apoptosis and resensitize resistant cells to TAM. Thus, in ER− BC cells, TAM may promote apoptosis by downregulating CIP2A and p-AKT. Inhibiting AKT phosphorylation could offer effective treatment strategies to enhance sensitivity to TAM in ER− breast tumor cells. Additionally, several biological processes, including oxidative stress, mitochondrial permeability transition (MPT), ceramide generation, and changes in cell membrane fluidity, play crucial roles in TAM-mediated apoptosis induction. TAM therapy promotes mitochondrial-mediated apoptosis and decreases cell viability in breast cancer cells. The apoptosis-inducing properties of TAM make it a promising candidate for treatment protocols for patients with ER− breast tumors (Fig. 2).

Key molecular tumor characteristics: genes influencing Tamoxifen response

Tamoxifen is a widely used therapeutic agent for treating ER+ breast cancer; however, its effectiveness can be compromised by disease recurrence. The molecular mechanisms underlying this recurrence are often associated with the dynamics of ER and progesterone receptor (PR) signaling pathways. When estrogen binds to the ER, it activates the transcription of genes that promote cell proliferation and survival, contributing to tumor growth. However, mutations in the ER or alterations in its expression can lead to tamoxifen resistance. The PR also plays a critical role in modulating estrogen signaling; its presence can enhance the proliferation of ER+ cells, and dysregulation of PR signaling may further exacerbate resistance by promoting alternative growth pathways. Additionally, various mechanisms affecting tamoxifen response include modifications in ER expression and function, as well as interactions between ER signaling and growth factor receptors, which can significantly influence treatment outcomes. Defects in apoptosis pathways, increased expression of oncogenes and the downregulation of tumor suppressor genes, such as retinoblastoma (RB), phosphatase and tensin homolog (PTEN), and p53, have also been linked to disease recurrence in tamoxifen-treated patients.

Defects in tumor suppressor genes via apoptosis failure drive TAM resistance

Tumor suppressor genes (TSGs) play a crucial role in regulating cellular proliferation by modulating apoptosis-related signaling pathways and enforcing cell cycle checkpoints. These mechanisms are vital for maintaining cellular homeostasis and preventing tumor formation. Extensive research has focused on the involvement of TSGs in the development of resistance to TAM. When TSG expression or function is dysregulated, apoptotic signaling can be disrupted, diminishing TAM’s anti-proliferative effects. This disruption not only enhances cellular survival in the presence of TAM but also significantly contributes to disease recurrence, highlighting the critical role of TSGs in therapeutic resistance and cancer progression.

Dueling p53 states: apoptosis induction vs. ER degradation in TAM outcomes

Apoptosis induction in breast carcinoma relies on the presence of functional p53. When p53 levels are reduced, apoptosis may be hindered. Breast cancer cell lines with inactivated or mutated p53 display decreased sensitivity to the cytotoxic effects of endoxifen, the active metabolite of tamoxifen. In fact, endoxifen can promote cell proliferation in p53-deficient breast cancer cells.

Patients with ER− breast carcinoma and p53 mutations typically have poorer responses to TAM compared to ER+ patients with wild-type p53. In addition, Molecular analyses indicate that TAM can induce p53 expression in ER+ breast tumor cells, and higher levels of p53 expression are linked to improved patient survival. Furthermore, p53 mutations can inhibit estrogen receptor expression, resulting in less favorable outcomes for patients treated with TAM. There is also a noted interaction between p53 and ER, as both bind to estrogen response elements (EREs) within the promoter sequences of estrogen-responsive genes, suggesting that p53 may help suppress hormone-driven tumor growth. Elevated levels of mutant p53 could serve as a potential marker for treatment resistance and disease recurrence, while wild-type p53 may act as a favorable prognostic factor in ER+ breast carcinoma patients receiving TAM.

PTEN Inactivation-driven PI3K/AKT/mTOR hyperactivation mediates Tamoxifen resistance

Phosphatase and tensin homolog deleted on chromosome ten (PTEN) is a well-known tumor suppressor protein, also called mutated in multiple advanced cancers 1 (MMAC1) and TGFβ-regulated and epithelial-cell-enriched phosphatase (TEP1). As a lipid phosphatase, PTEN plays a crucial role by dephosphorylating phosphatidylinositol triphosphate (PtdInsP), which counteracts the signaling of the phosphatidylinositol-3 (PI-3) kinase pathway.

The PI3K/AKT/mTOR (PAM) signaling pathway is a highly conserved network that regulates cell survival, growth, and the cell cycle. It is essential for cellular responses to growth factors, hormonal stimulation, and nutrient availability, significantly influencing tumor cell growth and proliferation. PTEN’s tumor-suppressive effects primarily arise from its ability to negatively regulate the PAM signaling pathway, thereby inhibiting cell division.

Mutations in the PTEN gene are frequently linked to an increased risk of various malignancies, including brain, prostate, breast, melanoma, Cowden syndrome, and endometrial cancers. Notably, low PTEN expression levels have been associated with TAM resistance in ER+ breast cancer patients, primarily through the activation of the PAM pathway. The downregulation of PTEN enhances PI3K activity, leading to elevated levels of 3′-phosphorylated phosphoinositides that activate PK-B/AKT. Once activated, PK-B/AKT phosphorylates and modulates various transcription factors, promoting cell survival and contributing to TAM resistance. Thus, inhibiting PI-3 kinase may provide a promising strategy for improving disease management in breast carcinoma.

Retinoblastoma-deficient breast tumors indicate resistance to Tamoxifen

Retinoblastoma protein (pRB) is a critical tumor suppressor that plays an essential role in regulating the cell cycle, particularly at the G1 checkpoint, which acts as a significant restriction point. pRB functions by inhibiting E2Fs, a group of vital transcription factors necessary for the expression of S-phase proteins and cell proliferation, thereby blocking entry into the S-phase. The knockdown of Retinoblastoma has been linked to increased tumor proliferation. Furthermore, pRB is involved in modulating the cellular response to various therapeutic agents. For example, TAM induces a G0/G1-phase arrest; however, pRB-deficient cells can bypass this TAM-mediated cell cycle arrest, allowing RB-deficient breast tumor cells to continue progressing even in the presence of TAM.

Oncogenes promote Tamoxifen resistance

Certain eukaryotic genes may display dominant oncogenic traits as tumors progress. Oncogenes originate from mutations in normal genes called proto-oncogenes. These proto-oncogenes are evolutionarily conserved and are crucial for regulating normal cell division. The proteins produced by most proto-oncogenes are vital components of key signaling pathways related to cell growth, including protein kinases, G proteins, and transcription factors. They regulate essential processes such as metabolism, apoptosis, growth, development, and cell differentiation. These significant proto-oncogenes modulate cell growth in response to metabolic changes, developmental stages, or growth factors. However, mutations in proto-oncogenes can lead to structural changes in their encoded proteins, potentially resulting in uncontrolled cell division and cancer progression. Over-activation or overexpression of oncogenes has been observed in various tumors, including breast cancer. The conversion of proto-oncogenes to oncogenes can occur through point mutations, gene amplification, chromosomal translocations, and other genetic or epigenetic alterations. The crucial roles of key oncogenes in the development of tamoxifen resistance have been clearly demonstrated.

WT1 as a biomarker and therapeutic target in Tamoxifen resistance

The role of WT1 (Wilms’ Tumor 1) in the progression of tamoxifen resistance in breast cancer is increasingly recognized as a critical factor in understanding treatment challenges. WT1, originally identified as a tumor suppressor gene, has been found to exhibit oncogenic properties in various cancers, including breast cancer. Its expression is often elevated in tumor tissues compared to normal breast cells, suggesting a shift from a protective to a promoting role in malignancy. One of the key mechanisms by which WT1 contributes to tamoxifen resistance is through the downregulation of estrogen receptor. WT1 overexpression can inhibit the transcriptional activity of ER by directly binding to its promoter. This downregulation reduces the receptor’s ability to mediate the effects of tamoxifen, which relies on functional ER to exert its therapeutic effects. Additionally, WT1 enhances the expression of growth factor receptors, particularly the epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR). This activation leads to increased cell proliferation and survival signaling pathways, counteracting the apoptotic effects of tamoxifen.

A critical aspect of WT1’s role in breast cancer is its interaction with cyclin D1, a key regulator of the cell cycle. Moreover, interfering with WT1 expression resulted in decreased cyclin D1 levels, which is crucial for cell cycle progression. It has been also reported that silencing WT1 expression decreased the proportion of cells in the S phase of the cell cycle, leading to cell proliferation arrest, differentiation, and apoptosis, likely due to the reduced expression of cyclin D1.

Moreover, WT1 is involved in the regulation of anti-apoptotic genes. It can upregulate anti-apoptotic proteins such as BCL-2 while downregulating pro-apoptotic factors like BAX and caspases. This shift in the balance of apoptotic regulators promotes cell survival even in the presence of tamoxifen. Furthermore, WT1 has been implicated in promoting EMT, a process that enhances cellular invasiveness and resistance to therapies. By facilitating EMT, WT1 contributes to a more aggressive tumor phenotype that is less responsive to tamoxifen.

Overall, the interplay of these mechanisms, including downregulation of ER, activation of EGFR, regulation of anti-apoptotic genes and induction of EMT, highlights the challenges in treating breast cancer. Understanding these mechanisms not only clarifies the difficulties in overcoming resistance but also points to potential therapeutic strategies targeting WT1 pathways to improve patient outcomes.

MUC1-C drives Tamoxifen resistance via ER stability and PI3K signaling

The transmembrane mucin 1 (MUC1) gene encodes a single-stranded polypeptide that, after translation, undergoes auto-cleavage to produce two subunits: the N-terminal (MUC1-N) and the C-terminal (MUC1-C). These subunits form a stable, noncovalent heterodimeric complex. MUC1 expression can increase up to 100-fold in various malignancies. MUC1-C is composed of three domains: extracellular, transmembrane, and cytoplasmic. The extracellular domain contains an arginine-glycine-proline (NPG) motif, which includes an asparagine residue that is subject to N-glycosylation. Glycosylated NPG can bind to galectin-3, facilitating the formation of an extracellular bridge between MUC1-C and EGFRs. In the cytoplasmic domain of MUC1-C, there is a Tyr-His-Pro-Met (YHPM) motif that serves as a substrate for phosphorylation by EGFRs on the tyrosine residue. This motif acts as a binding site for phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K) src homology 2 (SH2) domains, activating the PI3K-AKT pathway.

Over-activation of this pathway can lead to resistance to TAM. MUC1-C stabilizes ER by binding to their DNA-binding domain, thereby preventing their ubiquitination and degradation. Notably, TAM does not inhibit the MUC1-C/ER complex. Additionally, MUC1-C enhances the recruitment of coactivators, promoting ER-mediated transcription pathways.

As a result, the inhibitory effects of TAM on ER-mediated gene transcription can be counteracted by MUC1-C. Moreover, silencing MUC1-C significantly reduces HER2 expression and enhances the inhibitory effects of TAM on cell growth.

BCL-2 family proteins: key regulators and therapeutic targets in HER2-positive breast cancer

Two subclasses of the BCL-2 family have been identified: anti-apoptotic and pro-apoptotic members. This protein family plays a crucial role in determining a cell’s commitment to apoptosis through various protein-protein interactions. A key member of the anti-apoptotic group is Myeloid Cell Leukemia-1 (MCL-1), which is involved in the intrinsic mitochondrial apoptotic pathway. Amplification of MCL-1 is associated with a poor prognosis in TAM therapy. Similar findings have emerged from other studies, indicating that MCL-1 knockdown can trigger apoptosis in HER2-positive breast carcinoma cells. The dysregulation of the BCL-2 family contributes to cancer progression and disease recurrence. In addition, HER2 overexpression leading to increased BCL-2 expression. This highlights BCL-2 as a potential therapeutic target for patients with HER2-positive breast carcinoma.

AIB-1 and PAX-2 as key regulators of ERBB2 expression and Tamoxifen response

The AIB-1 protein, also known as amplified in breast cancer 1, is encoded by the nuclear receptor coactivator 3 (NCOA3) gene and is part of the p160 nuclear receptor coactivator family. Elevated AIB-1 expression has been observed in various cancers, including ovarian, prostate, gastric, pancreatic, and colorectal cancers, leading to its classification as an oncogene. Importantly, AIB-1 has been linked to the development of resistance to tamoxifen in BC patients.

PAX-2, a member of the paired box gene family, consists of nine nuclear transcription factors that regulate developmental control genes. Typically, PAX gene expression is rare in adult tissues. During embryonic development, PAX-2 plays a crucial role in organogenesis, influencing the formation of the central nervous system, eyes, ears, kidneys, and urogenital tract. Normally, PAX-2 expression decreases following terminal differentiation in the fetus; however, persistent expression has been observed in the normal breast tissues of adults. Both PAX-2 and AIB-1 are essential for the response to TAM through the regulation of ERBB2. Increased HER2 expression promotes the proliferation of breast tumor cells. The ERBB2 promoter contains an estrogen receptor binding site that acts as a regulatory element. Typically, PAX-2 mediates the silencing of the ERBB2 gene by binding to its regulatory sequence. Silencing PAX-2 using siRNA leads to increased ERBB2 expression in TAM-treated breast cancer cells. Conversely, elevated AIB-1 expression and its recruitment to the ER binding site within the ERBB2 promoter mitigate the anti-proliferative effects of TAM in ER+ breast tumor cells, such as MCF-7. The regulation of ERBB2 expression depends on the competition between PAX-2 and AIB-1 for binding to the regulatory element in the ERBB2 promoter, significantly influencing ERBB2 expression and the cellular response to TAM. Methylation-mediated suppression of PAX-2 contributes to disease recurrence in patients undergoing TAM therapy. Additionally, PAX-2 acts as a negative transcriptional regulator of p53 and serves as an activator of WT1. Collectively, TAM-treated breast carcinoma patients with elevated PAX-2 expression tend to have better survival outcomes, while those with high AIB-1 expression exhibit poorer survival rates.

AGR-2 influence Tamoxifen response through EGFR signaling and p53 activity

AGR-2, or human anterior gradient 2, is a member of the protein disulfide isomerase gene family. This protein plays a role in the growth and differentiation of mammary gland epithelium and exhibits estrogen-dependent expression. Initially identified in ER+ breast cells, AGR-2 has since been found to be overexpressed in various cancers, including esophageal, lung, colorectal, pancreatic, ovarian, and prostate cancers. Elevated levels of AGR-2 are associated with disease recurrence and an increased incidence of metastasis. Notably, patients with high AGR-2 expression show reduced efficacy of TAM treatment. Interestingly, the oncogenic effects of AGR-2 can be countered by letrozole, indicating the potential for targeted therapeutic strategies in patients with elevated AGR-2 levels.

AGR-2 also serves as a regulatory element in the EGFR signaling pathway. It can induce the expression of amphiregulin (AREG), an EGFR ligand, through the activation of YAP1, a co-activator in the Hippo signaling pathway. This suggests that AGR-2 promotes cell growth by regulating EGFR and Hippo signaling cascades. Additionally, AGR-2 has been shown to inhibit p53 activity and facilitate cell migration and metastasis.

Cyclin D1: cell cycle regulator and its role in Tamoxifen response

Cyclin D1 is a key regulatory component of the cell cycle during the G1 phase. This protein interacts with cyclin-dependent kinases 4 (CDK4) and 6 (CDK6), facilitating the phosphorylation of retinoblastoma. Overexpression of cyclin D1 is observed in breast tumors. Its transcription is regulated by estrogen receptors, meaning that anti-estrogen drugs like TAM may reduce cyclin D1 levels. Additionally, cyclin D1 can directly bind to the estrogen receptor, activating it even in the absence of estrogen. Moreover, ERBB2 can enhance cyclin D1 expression through signaling pathways involving mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) and phosphoinositide 3-kinase (PI3K)/AKT. Cyclin D1 serves as a negative predictive marker in breast carcinoma; however, patients with moderate cyclin D1 expression tend to benefit from TAM treatment, while those with low or high expression do not. Notably, combining tamoxifen with a PI3K inhibitor effectively counters RBP2-mediated resistance, positioning RBP2 as a promising therapeutic target in ER-driven breast cancer.

EGFR mediates estrogen-independent Tamoxifen response

Epidermal growth factor receptors (EGFRs) are part of the tyrosine kinase receptor family and are classified as cytoplasmic-anchored membrane proteins. They share a common structural framework comprising an extracellular domain for ligand binding, a hydrophobic transmembrane domain, and an intracytoplasmic tyrosine kinase domain. EGFR activation can occur through various ligands, such as epidermal growth factors and transforming growth factor α (TGFα), as well as through ligand-independent mechanisms. Overactivation of EGFRs is crucial in the initiation and progression of several cancers, leading to significant research focused on developing targeted anticancer therapies aimed at specific components of the EGFR signaling pathways. High levels of EGFR expression have been linked to resistance to TAM therapy. In HER2-positive breast tumor cells undergoing TAM treatment, disease progression can continue due to the interplay between estrogen receptor and HER2 signaling pathways, characterized as a non-genomic ER signaling mechanism. This pathway activates the ER through estrogen-independent mechanisms.

The activation of the estrogen receptor and its coregulatory proteins can be mediated by phosphorylation through two key components of the MAPK signaling cascade: glucocorticoid receptor-interacting protein (GRIP-1) and cAMP response element-binding protein (CBP), even without the presence of estrogen. Moreover, the upregulation of growth factor receptors, including insulin-like growth factor I receptor (IGF-IR), EGFR, and ErbB2/HER2, has been observed in patients experiencing disease recurrence during TAM therapy. Similar patterns have been noted with the overactivation of small GTPases such as Ras, Raf, MEK, MAPK, PI3K/AKT, PKC, PKA, and c-src, which mediate growth factor signaling pathways. Additionally, increased expression or activation of nuclear transcription factors directed by growth factors, including ER, AP-1, ETS2, E2F, and their coactivators like AIB-1, have been implicated in TAM resistance.

Autophagy and autophagy-related genes in Tamoxifen response

Autophagy is a vital homeostatic and self-digestive process that degrades defective or excess intracellular proteins and organelles by delivering them to lysosomes. This mechanism recycles metabolic precursors, promoting cell survival. Autophagy acts as a protective pathway that reduces tumor necrosis and inflammation while also decreasing genomic instability in tumor cells under various metabolic stresses. However, it can also facilitate tumorigenesis by creating favorable conditions for cancer cell survival. In breast tumor cells, autophagy is associated with the development of multidrug resistance through the inhibition of apoptosis. Studies have demonstrated that preventing autophagy can restore chemosensitivity and increase the apoptotic rate in tumor cells. This blockade can be achieved by silencing autophagy-related genes such as Atg5, Beclin-1, and Atg7 using small interfering RNA (siRNA). Moreover, autophagy plays a crucial pro-survival role during TAM therapy, and TAM has been shown to induce autophagy in breast cancer cells. Therefore, inhibiting autophagy during TAM treatment could be a valuable strategy, potentially providing therapeutic benefits for patients with increased expression of autophagy-related genes.

Angiogenesis Inhibition enhances Tamoxifen response

Angiogenesis is the process of forming new blood vessels from existing ones. This complex, multi-step process is vital for several physiological functions, including embryonic development, wound healing, and bone formation. In tumors, angiogenesis promotes the creation of new blood vessels, enhancing tumor vascularization. This increased blood supply not only supports local tumor growth but also raises the risk of distant metastasis by supplying tumor cells with essential oxygen and nutrients, thereby fostering a favorable microenvironment for their survival.

Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) is crucial in promoting neoangiogenesis and facilitating tumor metastasis. Estrogen further impacts tumor angiogenesis by directly interacting with vascular endothelial cells or indirectly stimulating VEGF secretion from breast tumor cells. Notably, TAM exhibits anti-angiogenic properties by inhibiting VEGF secretion.

In contrast, TAM-resistant BC cells show increased angiogenic activity, mediated by the overexpression of VEGF, which is regulated by Pin1 (Peptidyl-prolyl cis/trans isomerase NIMA-interacting 1). Pin1, part of the parvulin family and the peptidyl-prolyl cis/trans isomerase (PPIase) protein group, is positively correlated with VEGF expression. Therefore, understanding the interplay between these factors is essential for developing targeted therapies in breast cancer management.

Elevated levels of embryonal stem cell transcription factors are associated with Tamoxifen unresponsiveness

Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) is a biological process in which tumor cells undergo molecular changes, leading to the loss of epithelial characteristics and the acquisition of mesenchymal traits. This transition involves the disruption of intracellular tight junctions and a decrease in cell–cell adhesion. Increasing evidence highlights the role of EMT in promoting tumor cell proliferation, invasion, and metastasis. Key stem cell factors, such as NANOG, OCT-4, and SOX-2, are transcription factors vital for maintaining self-renewal and pluripotency in embryonic stem cells (ESCs). These factors are also crucial in the formation of cancer stem cells (CSCs). The upregulation of NANOG, OCT-4, and SOX-2 has been linked to the progression of EMT. Specifically, NANOG and SOX-2 have been shown to enhance tumorigenesis and metastasis in BC tumors. Elevated levels of these stem cell factors are associated with disease recurrence in patients receiving TAM treatment. Host factors in Tamoxifen resistance: the role of drug metabolism and transport

Host characteristics, encompassing traits of individuals with malignancies, are crucial for drug metabolism and the conversion of tamoxifen into its active metabolite. Moreover, cell membrane transporters play a significant role in determining intracellular tamoxifen concentrations. A reduction in these levels due to decreased influx or increased efflux can lead to disease recurrence and contribute to pharmacological tolerance. Ultimately, the interplay between tumor and host characteristics may serve as a predictor for tamoxifen treatment outcomes, highlighting the need for a comprehensive understanding of these molecular interactions to develop strategies that mitigate recurrence and enhance therapeutic efficacy.

Cytochrome P450 enzymes and their roles in Tamoxifen metabolism and breast cancer recurrence

The study of drug metabolism originated in the 19th century and has progressively evolved. Significant attention has been given to cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzymes and their role in drug metabolism and disease recurrence, particularly in cancer research. Variations in polymorphic alleles and altered functions of CYP enzymes lead to considerable interindividual differences in the metabolism of therapeutic agents. These enzymes have been extensively characterized in various tissues, including the liver, adrenal glands, and gonads. In breast carcinoma, CYP450 enzymes are essential for converting tamoxifen into its active metabolites, 4-hydroxyTAM and endoxifen. Notably, endoxifen binds to estrogen receptors with approximately 100-fold greater affinity than tamoxifen. The bioactivation of tamoxifen primarily involves CYP2D6 and CYP3A4, with CYP2D6 playing a particularly critical role in its metabolism. The plasma levels of endoxifen are directly correlated with CYP2D6 enzymatic activity, positioning it as a potential biomarker for disease outcomes in patients treated with tamoxifen.

A strong positive correlation exists between the protein and mRNA expression levels of CYP2D6, suggesting that mRNA expression can reliably estimate protein levels and serve as a predictive factor for tamoxifen response. Additionally, polymorphisms in CYP450 enzymes, especially CYP2D6, are significant determinants of recurrence events. Patients can be categorized into four distinct phenotypes based on their CYP2D6 alleles: poor metabolizers (PMs), intermediate metabolizers, extensive metabolizers (EMs), and ultra-rapid metabolizers (UMs). PMs have two alleles encoding enzymes with reduced activity, while IMs possess one normal function allele and one non-functional allele. EMs demonstrate normal CYP2D6 function, whereas UMs have multiple copies of CYP2D6 alleles. Endoxifen, an orally administered form of tamoxifen, has been proposed as a suitable treatment option for patients classified as intermediate or poor metabolizers, as it circumvents the need for CYP2D6 in tamoxifen metabolism and may improve survival outcomes.

Role of sulfotransferases and UDP-Glucuronosyltransferases in Tamoxifen metabolism and response variability

Sulfotransferases 1A1 (SULT1A1) and various isoforms of UDP-glucuronosyltransferases (UGT), including UGT1A8, UGT1A10, UGT2B7, UGT2B15, and UGT2B17, are essential for the inactivation of TAM metabolites. SULT1A1 specifically mediates the sulfation of these metabolites, while UGT2B15 is responsible for their glucuronidation, both of which aid in the excretion of TAM from the body. Genetic variations in SULT1A1 may explain the considerable inter-individual variability observed in TAM response.

Drug efflux and ABC transporters associated with poor Tamoxifen response

Drug efflux is a key factor in the recurrence of breast carcinoma. This process entails the expulsion of chemotherapeutic agents from cells through molecular pumps, which act as essential transporters in the cell membrane. By lowering the intracellular concentration of these drugs, efflux mechanisms can significantly reduce the effectiveness of treatment. A major player in this process is the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter superfamily, which consists of a variety of cell membrane proteins categorized into seven subfamilies. Several members of this superfamily are known to influence treatment responses in various cancers. Noteworthy examples include P-glycoprotein (P-gp or MDR1), MDR-related protein 1 (MRP1), MDR-related protein 2 (MRP2), and breast cancer resistance protein (BCRP).

P-glycoproteins are among the most extensively researched transporters related to multidrug resistance. Many strategies aimed at overcoming MDR focus on P-gp due to its significant impact on the pharmacokinetics of endoxifen and 4-hydroxy tamoxifen (4-HOT). In breast tumor tissues, increased exposure to tamoxifen leads to the overexpression of P-gp. Additionally, higher levels of P-gp expression have been observed in locally advanced and metastatic breast tumors following treatment with various chemotherapeutic agents. This heightened expression of P-gp in tumor tissues is linked to poorer overall survival outcomes for breast cancer patients treated with TAM. Notably, 4-HOT has been shown to upregulate the expression of MDR1, the gene that encodes P-gp, in breast tumor cells by activating the nuclear receptor Pregnane X receptor (PXR). Moreover, PXR mRNA expression is found to be higher in breast tumors compared to normal breast tissue.

Genetic biomarkers of Tamoxifen resistance: implications for personalized treatment outcomes

Genetic alterations in the regulatory or coding regions of key genes associated with TAM resistance can serve as valuable biomarkers for predicting treatment outcomes. Mutations in the ER gene, which may lead to dysfunctional protein activity or loss of expression, are particularly important for evaluating TAM response. Notably, changes in the 3’ untranslated region (3’ UTR) of the ER gene are a critical mechanism for downregulating ER expression, thus affecting treatment efficacy.

The therapeutic effect of TAM primarily relies on its active metabolites, making effective metabolic conversion essential. CYP2D6, a key enzyme in the cytochrome P450 superfamily, plays a crucial role in converting TAM into its pharmacologically active forms. Extensive research highlights the significant influence of CYP2D6 genotype on TAM treatment outcomes. Due to its high polymorphism, different CYP2D6 alleles exhibit varying enzymatic activities, resulting in diverse metabolic phenotypes among breast cancer patients.

MDR1, also known as P-glycoprotein, acts as an ATP-dependent efflux transporter that significantly affects intracellular drug accumulation and distribution. Polymorphisms in the MDR1 gene have been linked to disease recurrence in patients receiving TAM therapy, indicating a potential connection between genetic variability and treatment resistance.

Additionally, the tumor suppressor gene p53 is critical for maintaining cellular homeostasis, regulating cell cycle progression and apoptosis. The polymorphism at codon 72 of the p53 gene has emerged as a significant genetic modification that may serve as a predictive biomarker for TAM response.

In summary, identifying various genetic modifications in TAM resistance-related genes offers promise for improving the predictive accuracy of TAM treatment outcomes. These insights are vital for advancing personalized medicine, allowing for tailored therapeutic strategies that reflect the unique genetic profiles of individual patients.

Key roles of epigenetic modifications in Tamoxifen response

Epigenetic alterations play a crucial role in establishing stable and heritable phenotypes without altering the DNA nucleotide sequence. Epigenetic changes are essential in regulating chromatin dynamics, affecting gene accessibility and playing a significant role in the development of tamoxifen resistance in breast cancer. Key epigenetic mechanisms include DNA methylation, histone modifications, and post-translational modifications, as well as changes in expression of non-coding RNAs, including, microRNAs and long non-coding RNAs (LncRNAs). These Key epigenetic processes modify the chromatin structure, which in turn influences transcriptional activity. Irregular patterns of DNA methylation can lead to the silencing of tumor suppressor genes or the activation of oncogenes, promoting cancer progression and resistance to treatment. Furthermore, histone modifications, such as methylation and acetylation, also affect chromatin configuration and gene expression. These modifications are vital for normal human growth and development, allowing for the precise regulation of gene expression in response to various environmental and climatic factors. However, the effects of epigenetic changes are not always beneficial. Dysregulation of epigenetic markers has been linked to the development of several cancers, including breast cancer. Recent studies have highlighted the significant role of epigenetic modifications in breast cancer etiology and their impact on treatment responses, especially to tamoxifen. The complex interaction between epigenetic changes and gene expression regulation illustrates the intricacies of cancer biology. For example, abnormal DNA methylation patterns and histone modifications can silence tumor suppressor genes or activate oncogenes, contributing to tumorigenesis and drug resistance. Additionally, emerging evidence indicates that specific epigenetic alterations may serve as biomarkers for predicting treatment outcomes and could represent new therapeutic targets in breast cancer management. In summary, the complex landscape of epigenetic modifications significantly influences both the occurrence of breast cancer and the effectiveness of tamoxifen treatment, highlighting the need for further research into their potential as targets for personalized therapies. Collectively, epigenetic changes contribute to tumor development and the efficacy of tamoxifen therapy, highlighting the importance of exploring targeted epigenetic treatments in managing breast cancer. DNA methylation modifications in progression of Tamoxifen resistance

Changes in DNA methylation status are a crucial focus in epigenetics research. Increasing evidence shows that abnormal DNA methylation patterns primarily disrupt normal gene expression, which contributes to oncogenesis, metastasis, and the development of drug resistance in various cancers. Consequently, DNA methylation biomarkers have emerged as promising candidates for diagnostic and prognostic applications in oncology. Notably, alterations in DNA methylation may indicate disease recurrence in patients receiving TAM therapy.

As shown in Table 2, the methylation of regulatory CpG islands within the promoter or first exon regions of key genes—such as ER, PR, and cytochrome P450 enzymes—has been linked to reduced responses to TAM treatment. DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs) are a family of highly conserved enzymes in mammals that add methyl groups to cytosine residues at the 5’ carbon position of CpG dinucleotides, thereby establishing genomic methylation patterns. Generally, hypermethylation of regulatory elements, including promoters, leads to gene silencing. The principal DNMTs—DNMT1, DNMT3A, and DNMT3B—each have distinct roles in methylation dynamics. DNMT1 primarily maintains methylation patterns on daughter DNA strands during replication, while DNMT3A and DNMT3B are crucial for de novo methylation processes, significantly contributing to embryonic development and genomic imprinting. The interaction among these three DNMTs is vital for establishing and maintaining the normal methylation landscape in the human genome. Altered DNA methylation patterns are often observed in tumor cells, especially during malignant transformation. Notably, the overexpression of DNMTs has been linked to tamoxifen resistance in breast cancer patients. Interestingly, the promoter regions of DNMTs contain CpG islands, making them subject to regulation by methylation. Breast cancer patients with decreased promoter methylation of DNMT3A may face a higher risk of disease recurrence during TAM treatment, highlighting the potential of DNMT3A as a biomarker for predicting therapeutic outcomes.

In summary, the complex relationship between DNA methylation alterations and gene expression regulation underscores the significance of these epigenetic modifications in cancer biology, particularly regarding tamoxifen treatment. Further investigation into the mechanisms underlying these modifications may lead to novel therapeutic strategies and enhanced patient management in breast cancer.

Histone modifications as regulators of transcription and therapeutic targets

Crucial post-translational modifications of histone tails, significantly influence gene expression and cancer progression. These modifications, including methylation, acetylation, phosphorylation, and ubiquitination, alter DNA structure, thereby affecting transcription factor accessibility to gene promoters. Histone methylation, regulated by histone methyltransferases (HMTs) and demethylases (HDMs), impacts gene transcription without changing histone charge. Methylation primarily occurs on lysine or arginine residues of histones H3 and H4, playing vital roles in heterochromatin formation, gene imprinting, X chromosome inactivation, and transcriptional regulation.

Methylation patterns can either activate or repress transcription, influencing critical cellular processes such as DNA repair and cancer progression. For instance, EZH2, an HMT, is linked to aggressive breast cancer phenotypes by repressing tumor suppressor genes, with inhibitors like DZNep showing promise in preclinical studies. Similarly, lysine-specific demethylase 1 (LSD1) correlates with poor cancer prognosis, and its inhibitors are under investigation for their potential to inhibit cancer cell growth. G9a, another methyltransferase, is associated with increased cancer aggressiveness, and its inhibitors have demonstrated efficacy in reducing cancer cell invasiveness. It has been indicated that methylation levels of histone H3 at lysine 4 (H3K4) and lysine 9 (H3K9) are elevated in MCF7 breast cancer cells that exhibit resistance to tamoxifen.

Histone acetylation, governed by histone acetyltransferases (HATs) and histone deacetylases (HDACs), is a dynamic process impacting chromatin structure. HATs add acetyl groups to lysine residues, promoting transcription, while HDACs remove them, leading to chromatin compaction and transcriptional repression. HATs are implicated in cancer development, with hyper-acetylation of oncogenes contributing to tumor progression. Notably, MYST3, a HAT, has been identified as significant in breast cancer, suggesting that targeting its activity may offer new treatment avenues. HDACs, which include zinc-dependent and NAD-dependent types, are critical in cancer biology, with elevated levels observed in various cancers. HDAC inhibitors like SAHA and romidepsin enhance protein acetylation and promote apoptosis in cancer cells, demonstrating efficacy in clinical trials. Overall, targeting histone-modifying enzymes presents a promising strategy for therapeutic intervention in malignancies, warranting further exploration of their mechanisms and clinical applications.

MicroRNAs as post-translational regulatory elements

MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are short noncoding RNA molecules that provide a post-transcriptional regulatory mechanism, by directly binding to the 3′-untranslated region (3′-UTR) of their target genes. miRNAs work as post-transcriptional regulators through targeting of mRNAs and translation inhibition. As shown in Table 3, miRNAs have the ability to regulate various cellular pathways. Alterations in miRNAs expression could provide an important post-translational regulatory factor for tamoxifen resistance-related genes which are included in ER signaling pathways, growth factor receptor pathways, apoptosis pathways, epigenetic pathways, etc.

Long noncoding RNAs as key players in Tamoxifen response

The emergence of tamoxifen resistance in estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer presents a complex biological challenge, with long noncoding RNAs (lncRNAs) playing crucial roles in this process. These regulatory molecules, which are over 200 nucleotides long, contribute to resistance through various mechanisms, including genomic regulation, modulation of the tumor microenvironment, and post-translational modifications. Similar to microRNAs, which also participate in the intricate regulatory networks affecting gene expression, lncRNAs can serve as key players in the development of resistance by interacting with and modulating the activity of microRNAs. This interplay highlights the multifaceted nature of regulatory RNAs in cancer biology.

Notably, SOX2OT is significant due to its co-expression with the stemness factor SOX2 in basal-like breast cancers, which is vital for maintaining tumor cell plasticity. SOX2OT influences not only tumor cells but also stromal components, potentially aiding in the evasion of estrogen-dependent growth control. Another important lncRNA, LINP1, inversely correlates with functional estrogen receptor signaling and promotes therapeutic resistance by activating the mTOR pathway and establishing a feedback loop that supports hypoxic adaptation in resistant cells.

DILA1 exemplifies another resistance mechanism by binding to Cyclin D1, preventing its degradation and allowing cells to bypass tamoxifen-induced cell cycle arrest, thus fostering uncontrolled proliferation. The oncogenic lncRNA ATXN8OS is linked to aggressive tumor behavior and poor prognosis, enhancing proliferative signaling while reducing apoptotic responses in tamoxifen-resistant cells. Other lncRNAs, such as HOTAIR and UCA1, further contribute to resistance by remodeling chromatin and hijacking mTOR signaling pathways.

Despite their potential as biomarkers and therapeutic targets, lncRNAs exhibit context-dependent functions, complicating their clinical application. For instance, SNHG6 may act as an oncogene in some breast cancer subtypes while functioning as a tumor suppressor in others. Future research should focus on characterizing lncRNA interaction networks across different breast cancer subtypes and developing targeted delivery systems for RNA-based therapies. As our understanding of these regulatory RNAs advances, they could significantly impact the management of tamoxifen resistance, leading to improved treatment outcomes for ER+ breast cancer patients.

Therapeutic strategies for overcoming resistance to improve patient outcomes

The biological and molecular heterogeneity of human malignancies, combined with individual variations in patients’ genomes and epigenomes, results in diverse responses to cancer treatments. To date, no universally effective cancer treatment protocol has been established. However, a deeper understanding of the underlying biological mechanisms and the identification of suitable target molecules could enhance our insights into breast cancer incidence and recurrence. In this evolving context, various novel therapeutic strategies may emerge, potentially improving disease management and outcomes. This article discusses several promising approaches aimed at addressing the complexities of breast cancer treatment.

Next-generation serms and their role in disrupting recurrence pathways of hormone-driven breast malignancies

Replacing TAM with alternative SERMs like idoxifene, ERA-923, EM-800, and lasofoxifene may provide effective treatment options for patients resistant to TAM. Idoxifene has a higher binding affinity for the estrogen receptor than TAM. ERA-923, a newly characterized orally active SERM, shows no estrogenic activity on the endometrium or mammary gland while effectively inhibiting estrogen binding to the ER. Lasofoxifene not only has a high binding affinity for the ER but also offers improved oral bioavailability compared to other SERMs, including raloxifene and TAM. Additionally, endoxifen, the active metabolite of TAM, may be prescribed for breast cancer patients with deficiencies in cytochrome P450 enzymes, presenting a potential therapeutic option for this group. Exploring these alternative SERMs may enhance treatment efficacy for patients resistant to TAM.

Antibody-facilitated HER2 signaling arrest and angiogenesis modulation to restore Tamoxifen effectiveness

Primary breast carcinoma is typically treated with a combination of chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and hormone therapy, often followed by targeted antibody treatments to prevent metastasis. Patients with high amplification or overexpression of HER2 tend to have more aggressive tumors and generally face a poorer prognosis than those with normal HER2 expression. Approximately 18–20% of breast tumors exhibit increased ERBB2/HER2 protein expression. The interaction between HER2 and ER signaling pathways indicates that HER2 overexpression may reduce the effectiveness of TAM in ER+ breast cancer. Given HER2’s significant role in modulating TAM response, targeted therapies against HER2 could potentially improve treatment outcomes. Trastuzumab (Herceptin), a recombinant humanized monoclonal antibody, has a high affinity and specificity for the extracellular domain of HER2, making it a promising option for reducing disease recurrence in patients with ER-positive, HER2-positive breast cancer who have received TAM. Additionally, angiogenesis is a factor that can diminish TAM efficacy. Bevacizumab (Avastin), a humanized anti-VEGF monoclonal antibody, has the potential to modulate angiogenesis and enhance survival outcomes. Furthermore, NVP-BEZ235, a dual inhibitor of PI3K and mTOR, has demonstrated the ability to induce apoptosis and inhibit cellular proliferation, suggesting it could improve treatment effectiveness in patients with over-activation of the PI3K signaling pathway. These therapeutic strategies underscore the importance of personalized approaches in breast cancer management.

Inhibition of ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters to improve Tamoxifen response

ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporters play important roles in reducing the intracellular accumulation of TAM to sub-therapeutic levels in breast tumor tissues. The heightened expression of ABC transporters is directly linked to multidrug resistance (MDR) in various cancers, including breast carcinoma. This resistance can potentially be reversed by the introduction of P-gp inhibitors, which enhance the effectiveness of anti-hormone therapy. Research has shown that P-gp inhibitors can significantly increase TAM concentrations within breast tumor cells.

Over the last two decades, extensive research has aimed at reversing MDR by reducing or inhibiting P-gp activity, leading to the discovery of numerous MDR modulators. P-gp inhibitors are classified into three main categories. First-generation P-gp inhibitors, such as verapamil and cyclosporin, act as substrates for P-gp and compete for efflux through the P-gp pump. However, these inhibitors require high doses to effectively suppress P-gp activity, which can result in adverse cardiovascular effects. Moreover, they may also bind to other transporters, leading to unpredictable pharmacokinetic toxicities.

To overcome these limitations, significant efforts have been made to develop second- and third-generation P-gp inhibitors. Second-generation inhibitors are more potent and less toxic than their predecessors, with examples including dexniguldipine, dexverapamil, biricodar, and valspodar. Numerous clinical trials have evaluated the effects of these second-generation P-gp inhibitors in cancer therapy, showing MDR reversal when administered alongside cytotoxic drugs. However, challenges persist in their clinical application. Membrane transporters protect normal cells from the cytotoxic effects of chemotherapy through efflux activity. Unfortunately, P-gp inhibitors can also inhibit drug transporters in normal cells, leading to harmful effects. Additionally, second-generation P-gp inhibitors can suppress the metabolism of chemotherapeutic agents, resulting in increased toxicity. Therefore, it is essential to reduce the dosage of chemotherapeutic drugs when used in combination with P-gp inhibitors to mitigate these toxic effects.

Lastly, third-generation P-gp inhibitors have been developed, offering highly specific action on P-gp without affecting the pharmacokinetics of cytotoxic drugs. Their impact on normal tissues is also significantly reduced compared to second-generation inhibitors. Notable examples of third-generation P-gp inhibitors include tariquidar, zosuquidar, and elacridar. In summary, the co-delivery of P-gp inhibitors and anti-hormonal treatments like TAM shows potential for overcoming P-gp-mediated TAM resistance. However, further studies and well-designed clinical trials are essential to validate this hypothesis in clinical settings.

Natural products as innovative strategies to address Tamoxifen resistance

Due to the drawbacks and side effects of chemical drugs, such as unwanted toxicity to healthy cells, scientists have shifted their focus to researching natural products as viable alternatives. Generally non-toxic and well tolerated by the human body. Numerous studies across different cancer types have highlighted the therapeutic benefits of these natural products, including the induction of apoptosis, inactivation of DNA repair mechanisms, inhibition of angiogenesis, suppression of DNMT1, and inhibitory effects on P-glycoprotein. The role of epigenetic therapy in mitigating Tamoxifen resistance

The reversal of genetic alterations poses a significant challenge in oncology. In contrast, epigenetic modifications are naturally reversible, making them a key focus for therapeutic exploration. The development of new treatment protocols based on epigenetic therapy shows promise for improving the management of malignancies influenced by these mechanisms. Such therapies can reactivate silenced genes and modulate gene expression, potentially reversing cancer phenotypes, including drug resistance. DNA methylation abnormalities, recognized as a major epigenetic disorder, are linked to the development of TAM resistance in breast cancer. Previous studies have shown that ER+ breast cancer cells with altered methylation patterns do not benefit from TAM treatment. Furthermore, overexpression of DNMTs has been observed in TAM-resistant breast tumor tissues.

Given their role in tumorigenesis and disease recurrence in breast cancer, DNMTs present a compelling therapeutic target for improving disease management. Epigenetic treatments can be implemented using both synthetic and naturally occurring compounds.

Synthetic DNA methylation inhibitors (DMIs) have been explored in clinical trials as anticancer agents for over forty years. These inhibitors can effectively reverse hypermethylation in the promoter regions of tumor suppressor genes, leading to the reprogramming of cancer cells, which induces growth arrest and apoptosis. The inhibitory effects on DNA methylation have been documented across various chemical compounds, including both nucleoside and non-nucleoside inhibitors. Initially, non-nucleoside analogues were discovered by chance, however subsequent molecular modeling studies have clarified the interactions between the crystallographic structures of DMIs and the catalytic domains of DNMTs, spurring efforts to develop potent and selective non-nucleoside DNMT inhibitors. Nucleoside inhibitors, such as 5-azacytidine and decitabine (5-aza-2ʹ-deoxycytidine), work by intercalating into DNA. The FDA has approved azacytidine and decitabine as first-generation epigenetic inhibitors for treating myeloid leukemia and other hematological malignancies.

However, further studies are needed to confirm the efficacy of epigenetic drugs in enhancing TAM response. Combining conventional therapies with epigenetic agents may lead to better treatment outcomes. Although the synthetic class of epigenetic drugs is well-established, these inhibitors often show cytotoxic effects on normal cells. Additional investigations are necessary to clarify the direct and critical roles of DNA methylation inhibitors in TAM treatment responses. Natural products are gaining attention as promising DNMT inhibitors or demethylating agents that can restore normal epigenetic patterns, largely due to their minimized or negligible adverse effects. As a result, these compounds may offer viable alternatives to synthetic agents for treating multidrug resistance in patients with epigenetic modifications. Natural products can be classified into various groups based on their mechanisms of action. Notable DNMT1 inhibitors include peyssonenynes A and B, which are isolated from the red alga Peyssonnelia caulifera, as well as curcumin and parthenolide, Nanaomycin A is recognized as a selective inhibitor of DNMT3B. Additionally, annurca apple and Mahanine, a plant-derived carbazole alkaloid, have shown inhibitory effects on both DNMT1 and DNMT3B. Other natural compounds that contribute to the reduction of DNA methylation levels include the polyphenol Epi-gallocatechin-3-gallate, found in green tea and the phenolic compound 5-caffeoyl quinic acid from green coffee, Furthermore, two isoflavones—Biochanin A and Daidzein, along with the active ingredient in soy called Genistein—have also been identified as reducing the DNA methylation state. Enhancing Tamoxifen efficacy through antisense technology

Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) are synthetic, short single-stranded DNA or RNA molecules that serve as powerful regulators of gene expression by inhibiting translation. Recent advancements in antisense technology have emerged as a promising therapeutic approach in oncology. ASOs exert their effects by hybridizing with complementary mRNA sequences, leading to RNase H-mediated degradation of the target transcript. Therefore, ideal candidates for antisense intervention are genes that are upregulated during tumor progression and recurrence. The overexpression of oncogenes is often associated with disease recurrence; thus, targeting oncogenes like ERBB2 and BCL-2 may improve disease management in breast cancer. The dysregulated expression of ERBB2 and BCL-2 in patients with poor responses to tamoxifen underscores the potential of targeted therapies against these oncogenes to enhance patient outcomes. For instance, the ASO BP1002, which specifically targets BCL-2, is currently in clinical trials for lymphoid malignancies. Another ASO, HA6722, has shown specificity for the ERBB2 receptor in both in vitro and in vivo models of breast cancer. Additionally, various DNMTs have been implicated in the mechanisms behind TAM resistance, suggesting they could be molecular targets for innovative therapies aimed at reducing disease recurrence in breast cancer. The antisense oligodeoxynucleotide MG98 has been demonstrated to inhibit DNA methyltransferase 1, effectively reactivating tumor suppressor genes with continuous dosing regimens.

Despite the promising benefits of antisense therapy, several challenges remain. These include optimizing tissue exposure, enhancing cellular uptake, and developing specific and efficient delivery systems to tumors, as well as ensuring uniform distribution within tumor masses. The primary barriers to the clinical application of ASOs involve their safety profiles and antitumor efficacy. Antisense therapy represents a significant frontier in developing treatments for resistant cancer cells and holds substantial potential in medical research, particularly in improving TAM responsiveness. It is essential to recognize that multiple genes contribute to tumor progression and recurrence; therefore, inhibiting a single target gene may be insufficient for effective disease management. A multimodal treatment strategy that integrates ASOs with other chemotherapeutic agents could produce synergistic antineoplastic effects across various tumor models.

Conclusions and future directions

The emergence of tamoxifen resistance presents a complex challenge involving various genes and regulatory elements. Key tumor-related factors affecting the response to tamoxifen include components of estrogen signaling pathways, oncogenes, stemness factors, tumor suppressor genes, cell cycle regulators, growth factors, apoptotic pathways, and autophagy mechanisms. Additionally, the interaction between specific signaling pathways significantly influences treatment efficacy. Evaluating HER2 and ER signaling pathways together, along with the essential roles of PAX2 and AIB1, may lead to better therapeutic outcomes than examining them separately.

Beyond the tumor’s molecular characteristics, host factors such as drug transporters and cytochrome P450 enzymes also play crucial roles in disease recurrence. The widespread presence of CpG islands in the promoter regions of many genes associated with tamoxifen response highlights the importance of promoter methylation in disease recurrence. Promoter hypermethylation, one of the most studied epigenetic modifications, is linked to reduced gene expression or complete gene silencing. Abnormal DNA methylation patterns are often observed in the early stages of cancer progression and are frequently associated with disease recurrence. These methylation changes can serve as valuable prognostic indicators, suggesting that epigenetic therapies may benefit patients with altered DNA methylation profiles.

Recent technological advancements, such as ASOs, show promise in reducing disease recurrence rates. The introduction of improved predictive and prognostic markers, along with more effective treatment protocols, could significantly enhance patient survival outcomes. Combination therapy using tamoxifen with other medications appears promising for improving breast cancer treatment and preventing drug resistance. While early studies suggest potential synergistic effects that could boost treatment efficacy, further research and rigorous clinical trials are necessary to validate these findings, ensure safety, and optimize treatment strategies. Understanding the benefits and risks of such combinations is essential for effectively integrating these innovative approaches into clinical practice, ultimately aiming to improve patient outcomes in breast cancer management.

The reversible nature of epigenetic modifications has generated significant interest in exploring new natural or synthetic therapeutic agents to enhance tamoxifen efficacy. Clarifying the mechanisms underlying tamoxifen resistance is vital for developing next-generation targeted therapies aimed at improving patient survival. Research strategies that focus on selectively inhibiting oncogenes and inducing tumor suppressor gene expression represent promising avenues for overcoming tamoxifen resistance. Additionally, replacing tamoxifen with alternative estrogen receptor inhibitors may provide a viable therapeutic option for patients at risk of developing resistance, particularly when guided by molecular screening assays. Ultimately, a thorough understanding of the interplay among genetic, epigenetic, and host factors is crucial for advancing therapeutic strategies and improving clinical outcomes for patients facing tamoxifen resistance.
