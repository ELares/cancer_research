Plain Language Summary

Platinum-based chemotherapy is used for first-line treatment of locally advanced or metastatic urothelial cancer (la/mUC), a cancer that starts in the lining of the urinary tract and spreads to nearby tissue, lymph nodes, or other parts of the body. Recently, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), which are made up of an antibody attached via a linker to an agent that can kill cells, have become treatment options in la/mUC. About 60% to 80% of people with la/mUC produce a type of protein known as HER2 on their tumor. Treatments that target HER2 May therefore be beneficial. Disitamab vedotin, a type of ADC, targets HER2 on the surface of cells. Disitamab vedotin can then enter the cells and deliver an anticancer agent called MMAE directly to them. Combining disitamab vedotin with immunotherapy (treatment that enables a person’s own immune system to find and kill cancer cells) may work better than either drug by itself. The SGNDV-001 trial is evaluating disitamab vedotin combined with a type of immunotherapy (called pembrolizumab) in patients with tumors that express HER2 and who have not been treated for la/mUC before. The goal of this trial is to see if this combination can help delay the progression of the cancer and allow patients to live longer compared with patients receiving chemotherapy. Researchers are also looking at how safe this combination is. If the trial is successful, disitamab vedotin plus pembrolizumab could become a treatment option for this patient population.

Introduction

Background and rationale

Locally advanced/metastatic urothelial carcinoma (la/mUC) is an aggressive disease with a poor prognosis. Until recently, platinum-based chemotherapy was the standard of care for first-line treatment of la/mUC, followed by avelumab maintenance immunotherapy in patients without disease progression on chemotherapy. However, the development of antibody-drug conjugates are reshaping the treatment landscape. Enfortumab vedotin, a Nectin-4–directed antibody with a monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) payload, first demonstrated efficacy as monotherapy in patients previously treated with chemotherapy and an immune checkpoint inhibitor (EV-201). In the EV-302 trial, the combination of enfortumab vedotin with pembrolizumab improved survival outcomes versus chemotherapy in the first-line setting. There have also been significant advances in the early detection and diagnosis of bladder cancer using modern technologies.

Despite these advances in the diagnosis and treatment of la/mUC, there are few biomarker-targeted therapies for treatment of bladder cancer. Biomarker driven approaches such as targeting fibroblast growth factor receptor are in development, but further progress is necessary. The human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) is a member of the human epidermal growth factor receptor family that is expressed across cancer types and is an established prognostic and predictive biomarker in breast and gastrointestinal cancers. In UC, approximately 60% to 80% of patients have tumors that demonstrate HER2 expression, with expression levels varying across tumors. There is currently no standardized methodology for defining HER2 expression in UC, and HER2 testing is not part of standard clinical practice. However, many studies categorize HER2 expression as HER2-positive (immunohistochemistry [IHC] 3+ or IHC 2+ and fluorescence in situ hybridization [FISH]/ISH-positive), HER2-low (IHC 2+ and FISH/ISH-negative or IHC 1+), or HER2-negative (IHC 0). HER2 is known to contribute to the mechanisms of oncogenesis. HER2 expression correlates with higher histological grade, higher tumor stage, and recurrence in UC. HER2 overexpression in UC has also generally been associated with poor outcomes. Given the high prevalence of HER2 expression in UC and the contribution of HER2 to tumor progression, HER2-directed vedotin-based antibody-drug conjugates may be effective for the treatment of patients with la/mUC with HER2-expressing tumors.

Disitamab vedotin (RC48-ADC) is an investigational antibody-drug conjugate comprising a humanized HER2-directed monoclonal antibody, disitamab, conjugated to the microtubule-disrupting agent MMAE via a protease cleavable mc-vc linker. This vedotin mc-vc-MMAE linker-payload system is also the foundation of other approved antibody-drug conjugates such as enfortumab vedotin. Disitamab binds with high molecular affinity to a distinct epitope of the HER2 receptor on the cell surface and, once internalized, preferentially releases cytotoxic MMAE intracellularly. Released MMAE binds to and disrupts the microtubule network, initiating cell cycle arrest and apoptosis in HER2-expressing cells. MMAE also diffuses to adjacent cells across cell membranes and leads to apoptosis of these cells, regardless of their HER2 levels. This mechanism, known as the bystander effect, provides a rationale for the investigation of disitamab vedotin in patients with HER2-low tumors. Additionally, MMAE mediates immunogenic cell death, which promotes activation and recruitment of immune cells to tumors to elicit more durable antitumor activity. Preclinical data have shown enhanced tumor suppression, antitumor immunity, and memory T-cell response upon exposure to MMAE, providing a rationale for combining disitamab vedotin with immune checkpoint inhibitors.

Disitamab vedotin monotherapy has demonstrated encouraging efficacy across HER2-expressing tumor types, including breast, gastric, and la/mUC, and is conditionally approved in China for HER2-positive (HER2 IHC 2+/3+) locally advanced or metastatic gastric or gastroesophageal junction cancer. The single-arm RC48-C005 and RC48-C009 trials of disitamab vedotin monotherapy in heavily pretreated patients with la/mUC demonstrated a combined median progression-free survival (PFS) of 5.9 months and combined overall survival (OS) of 14.2 months. Across the RC48-C005 and RC48–009 trials, confirmed overall response rate (ORR) with disitamab vedotin monotherapy was 62.2% for patients with HER2-positive (IHC 3+ or IHC 2+ and FISH-positive) tumors and 39.6% in patients with HER2-low (IHC 2+ and FISH-negative) tumors. In another single-arm phase 2 study (RC48-C011), patients with la/mUC with HER2 IHC 1+ tumors benefited from disitamab vedotin monotherapy, with an ORR of 38.5%. Based on these studies, disitamab vedotin received conditional approval in China for the treatment of patients with HER2‑positive (HER2 IHC 2+/3+) la/mUC who have received prior platinum-based chemotherapy, and the US FDA has granted Breakthrough Therapy designation for the treatment of patients with HER2-positive (HER2 IHC 2+/3+) la/mUC who have previously received platinum-containing chemotherapy treatment.

Given the known synergy of vedotin-based antibody-drug conjugates and programmed death 1 (PD-1) inhibitors, the combination of disitamab vedotin and PD-1 inhibitors has also been explored. The combination of disitamab vedotin with toripalimab, a PD-1 inhibitor, in the RC-C014 trial resulted in a confirmed ORR of 73.2% in patients with la/mUC at the primary analysis, with antitumor activity observed in the HER2-positive (IHC 3+; 80.0% or IHC 2+; 84.2%), HER2-low (IHC 1+; 64.3%), and HER2-negative (IHC 0; 33.3%) subgroups. After nearly 3 years of median follow-up, this combination continued to demonstrate promising efficacy, with a median PFS and OS of 9 and 33 months, respectively, and a manageable safety profile. Disitamab vedotin in combination with pembrolizumab (RC48G001 Cohort C) also showed encouraging preliminary efficacy and safety data in a phase 2 study in treatment-naive patients with HER2-expressing (IHC ≥1+) la/mUC. The confirmed ORR was 66.7% in patients with HER2-positive (IHC 3+ or IHC 2+ and ISH-positive) tumors and 78.6% in patients with HER2-low (IHC 2+ and ISH-negative or IHC 1+) tumors. These data support further evaluation of the combination of disitamab vedotin with pembrolizumab for the first-line treatment of patients with HER2-expressing la/mUC.

Objectives

SGNDV-001 (C5731001; NCT05911295) is a randomized, controlled, open-label, multicenter phase 3 trial investigating disitamab vedotin plus the PD-1 inhibitor pembrolizumab compared with chemotherapy in treatment-naive patients with HER2-expressing la/mUC.

Trial design

Approximately 400 treatment-naive patients with la/mUC and central laboratory HER2 status IHC ≥ 1+ will be enrolled. HER2-high is defined as IHC 3+ or 2+ and ISH positive, and HER2-low is defined as IHC 2+ and ISH negative or IHC 1 + . Patients will be randomized (1:1) to disitamab vedotin with pembrolizumab (Arm A) or platinum-containing chemotherapy (either cisplatin or carboplatin) plus gemcitabine (Arm B) (Figure 1). 

Methods

Methods: participants, interventions and outcomes

Participants

The trial is currently enrolling in Asia, Europe, Latin America, and North America, and enrollment has been completed in Australia.

Eligibility criteria

Eligible participants are treatment-naive adults with la/mUC who are eligible for platinum-containing chemotherapy and have tumors that express HER2, defined as being IHC 1+, 2+, or 3 +. HER2 expression will be centrally evaluated; the most recent tissue sample (archival or fresh tumor) from a muscle-invasive or metastatic lesion will be analyzed. Additional key inclusion and key exclusion criteria can be found in Table 1. The planned sample size is 200 patients per arm, to provide sufficient power in OS and PFS superiority tests between Arms A and B.

Interventions

Patients in arm A will receive disitamab vedotin every 2 weeks and pembrolizumab (400 mg) every 6 weeks (both intravenously), until disease progression, intolerable toxicity, or withdrawal from the trial. Pembrolizumab administration will be for a maximum of 18 six-weekly infusions (approximately 2 years). Patients in Arm B will be treated with either cisplatin (70 mg/m2) or carboplatin (area under the plasma concentration-time curve 4.5 or 5, per local guidelines) intravenously on Day 1 plus gemcitabine (1000 mg/m2) intravenously on Days 1 and 8 of every 3-week cycle. Treatment will continue until disease progression, intolerable toxicity, withdrawal from the trial, or a maximum of 6 cycles. Maintenance therapy with avelumab is permitted (if avelumab is locally approved and available), after 4 to 6 cycles of first-line platinum chemotherapy have been completed. Tumor assessments will continue for patients in the control arm who receive maintenance therapy.

Outcomes

The trial has dual primary endpoints: PFS (per blinded independent central review [BICR]) and OS. Secondary endpoints include the following: ORR, duration of response, disease control rate per Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) version 1.1 by BICR, ORR, duration of response, disease control rate, and PFS per RECIST 1.1 by investigator assessment. Additional secondary endpoints include safety and quality of life, assessed using the European Organisation for Research and Treatment of Cancer Core Quality of Life questionnaire (EORTC QLQ-C30). The full list of trial endpoints is included in Table 2.

Methods: assignment of interventions

Sequence generation

Randomization based on stratification factors will occur at Days −3 to 1. Stratification factors are cisplatin eligibility (yes or no), presence of liver metastases (yes or no), HER2 status (“high” defined as IHC 3+/IHC 2+ and ISH positive, and “low” defined as IHC 2+ and ISH negative/IHC 1+), and intent of avelumab maintenance use (yes or no).

Methods: data collection, management, and analysis

Data collection methods

Tumor assessments will occur every 8 weeks during treatment, irrespective of treatment delays or interim tumor assessments, until Week 72, after which tumor assessments will be undertaken every 12 weeks. Tumor assessments will continue until the first occurrence of BICR-confirmed disease progression, withdrawal of consent, death, pregnancy, or trial closure. Patient-reported outcomes will be collected until disease progression or last dose of study treatment, whichever is later. Safety will be assessed throughout the trial.

Statistical methods

For the dual primary endpoints of OS and PFS, a stratified log-rank test stratified by randomization stratification factors will be used for comparison of the experimental and control arms. A stratified Cox proportional hazards regression model will be used to estimate the hazard ratio and corresponding 95% confidence interval. The Kaplan-Meier method will be used to estimate the median survival time, which will be reported with the corresponding 95% confidence interval by treatment arm.

Methods: data monitoring

An external data monitoring committee will monitor the safety of patients participating in this trial and make recommendations to the sponsor.

Ethics and dissemination

This trial will be conducted in accordance with the protocol; with international guidelines, including the Declaration of Helsinki and Council for International Organizations of Medical Sciences (CIOMS) international ethical guidelines; with applicable International Council for Harmonisation (ICH) Good Clinical Practice guidelines; and with all other applicable laws and regulations. Amendments to the protocol will require Institutional Review Board/Independent Ethics Committee approval before any changes are made to the trial design, except for changes required to eliminate an immediate hazard to trial patients. The results of SGNDV-001 will be publicly disclosed in accordance with applicable laws and regulations; results may also be published in peer-reviewed journals or presented at scientific meetings.

Conclusion

Historically, platinum-based chemotherapy has been the standard of care for the first-line treatment of la/mUC. The landscape of previously untreated la/mUC has been transformed by vedotin-based antibody-drug conjugates, and the high prevalence of HER2 expression in UC makes HER2 an appealing therapeutic target for the delivery of antibody-drug conjugates that can preferentially release cytotoxic agents in HER2-expressing cells.

In the randomized phase 3 SGNDV-001 trial, the combination of disitamab vedotin, a novel antibody-drug conjugate directed to HER2, in combination with pembrolizumab is being evaluated versus platinum-based chemotherapy and gemcitabine in treatment-naive patients with HER2-expressing la/mUC. Platinum-based chemotherapy remains a first-line treatment option in most regions, especially in countries without access to vedotin-based antibody-drug conjugates. As the landscape continues to evolve with vedotin-based agents, future studies will need to address the differences between various vedotin-based ADCs in terms of benefit-risk profiles.

Disitamab vedotin has been shown to inhibit the growth and induce apoptosis of HER2-expressing and adjacent tumor cells. The combination of disitamab vedotin with PD-1 inhibitors has resulted in robust tumor responses in patients with la/mUC. In an ongoing phase 2 study (RC48G001 Cohort C), the combination of disitamab vedotin and pembrolizumab has resulted in a confirmed ORR of 75%. Tumor response was seen across HER2-expression subgroups, with a confirmed ORR of 78.6% in patients with tumors with HER2-low expression, providing rationale for inclusion of this subgroup in trials of disitamab vedotin plus pembrolizumab. A systematic literature review of 11 studies in la/mUC found the prevalence of patients with HER2-positive tumors, defined in the review as IHC 3+ or IHC 2+ and ISH/FISH positive, to range from 6.7% to 37.5%. Likewise, the prevalence of patients with HER2-low tumors, defined as IHC 2+ and ISH/FISH negative or IHC 1+, was estimated to range from 13.4% to 56.3%. In the SGNDV-001 study, the presence of tumor cell surface HER2 receptors is assessed by IHC and ISH, enabling the evaluation of disitamab vedotin with pembrolizumab in patients with tumors that are HER2-positive and HER2-low.

The potentially synergistic effect of disitamab vedotin when combined with a PD-1 inhibitor, together with a manageable safety profile, support further examination of disitamab vedotin plus pembrolizumab for first-line treatment of patients with HER2-expressing la/mUC.
