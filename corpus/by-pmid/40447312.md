# Effective CAR T-cell targeting of an MUC1 cleavage product.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: We developed Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells targeting mucin 1 (MUC1)* (muk * (muk 1 star)), which is the tumor-associated growth factor receptor form of MUC1. Our antibody, MNC2, uniquely binds to MUC1* on cancer cells but does not bind to full-length MUC1, which is expressed on all normal epithelial cells. We tested the ability of the Tyr to Phe mutations in CD3ζ, known as "1XX", to increase in vivo persistence and enable the recognition and killing of low antigen-expressing cancer cells.

**METHODS**: We performed in vivo experiments comparing CARs with either 4-1BB or CD28 co-stimulatory domains, with or without the "1XX" Tyr to Phe mutations in ITAMs 2 and 3 of the CD3ζ signaling domain. All CARs were targeted to the tumor using the same huMNC2-scFv. To explore the sensitivity of each CAR, tumors comprising varying percentages of high MUC1* expressing cancer cells were xenografted. Further, wild-type low MUC1* expressing cells were engineered to fluoresce red while the cells engineered to express more MUC1* were made to fluoresce green. This experimental design allowed us to compare the sensitivity limits of the CARs against low versus high antigen-expressing cancer cells.

**RESULTS**: At high dose, all the CAR T cells effectively killed high antigen-expressing tumors in the short term. However, only the CAR bearing the 1XX mutations inhibited tumor recurrence in long-term experiments. Interestingly, in animals treated with CARs bearing wild-type CD3ζ, tumor recurrence was driven by the low antigen-expressing cells. Only the CAR bearing 1XX mutations demonstrated the ability to kill low antigen-expressing tumors, even when administered at low dose. Post-sacrifice analysis showed that the CAR T cells with 1XX mutations persisted longer in vivo than either 4-1BB or CD28 CAR T cells with wild-type CD3ζ.

**CONCLUSIONS**: These results support that the combination of targeting MUC1*, the growth factor receptor form of MUC1, with a CAR T bearing the 1XX mutations in CD3ζ has therapeutic potential for the treatment of solid tumor cancers.

Abstract

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

A dearth of suitable cell surface targets hinders the development of successful CAR T-cell therapy for the treatment of solid tumor cancers.

Current CAR T cells may not persist long enough, be sensitive enough, or maintain enough effector function to successfully treat solid tumor cancers.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

We describe a new target, mucin 1 (MUC1)*, for the treatment of solid tumor cancers. We compared the efficacy, persistence and antigen sensitivity of three CARs, all directed to the tumor by the same antibody fragment, but bearing different co-stimulatory domains and one construct bearing the 1XX mutations in the CD3ζ cytoplasmic domain. Our study showed that in animal models, the CAR-1XX suppressed tumor recurrence, increased CAR T-cell persistence and enabled the killing of low antigen-expressing tumors.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

Although MUC1 has been known for 30 years to be aberrantly expressed in 75% of solid tumors, no therapeutic targeting MUC1 has ever been Food and Drug Administration approved. The development of an antibody that selectively recognizes the cancer-associated growth factor receptor form, as described here, is an important advance.

Introduction

 Mucin 1 (MUC1), a single pass type I transmembrane protein, has long been an attractive target for cancer therapeutics because it is aberrantly expressed on about 75% of solid tumor cancers. Originally, aberrant expression was defined as expressed over the entire cell surface as opposed to being restricted to the apical border of tissues, as it is on normal epithelium. Although MUC1 was cloned more than 30 years ago, elucidation of a mechanism of action, identification of a cognate ligand, significance of glycosylation patterns or even its expression in embryonic cells, healthy tissues, cancerous tissues or other disease conditions have remained elusive or controversial. Baruch et al, reported that MUC1 self-cleaves, whereupon the free portion comes down and binds to the remaining transmembrane portion, forming a tumor-promoting heterodimer, although no mechanism of action was ever demonstrated. Others have focused on variations in glycosylation patterns. More recently, researchers have targeted trapped glycans, which can only deposit onto sites for O-linked glycosylation, which are only in the tandem repeat domain and are shed after enzymatic cleavage.

Despite several attempts to therapeutically target MUC1, there are still no Food and Drug Administration approved MUC1-targeting drugs. Nearly all previous attempts targeted the tandem repeat domain, which on cancer cells is shed into the bloodstream after enzymatic cleavage. Unlike the previous work, our targeting antibodies bind to the novel target, MUC1* (muk 1 star), which is the portion that remains on cancer cells after cleavage and release of most of its extracellular domain. The anti-MUC1* antibody used here, MNC2, is unique in that it binds to a specific conformation that is created after MUC1 is cleaved by specific tumor-associated enzymes that are present in the tumor microenvironment. Importantly, MNC2 does not bind to full-length MUC1 which is expressed on normal epithelial cells. We previously reported that an MUC1* with an extracellular domain of 45 amino acids (MUC1*ecd45) functions as a Class I growth factor receptor that is activated by ligand-induced dimerization of its truncated extracellular domain, which in turn activates the MAP kinase growth pathway. Antibody MNC2 binds to the MUC1* form of MUC1 expressed by the majority of solid tumor cells, imparting greater tumor-selectivity to MNC2 directed CAR T cells.

CAR T-cell therapy has been very successful in the treatment of some blood cancers, achieving high complete response rates. However, comparable success has not yet been achieved in the treatment of solid tumor cancers, which make up the vast majority of cancers. Although early in the quest to develop CAR T cells that are effective in the treatment of solid tumor cancers, some of the hurdles that must be cleared include identifying targets that are sufficiently cancer-specific, delaying the onset of CAR T-cell exhaustion and enabling the specific killing of low antigen-expressing cancer cells.

Here we report the comparative analysis of huMNC2-scFv as the targeting head of three different CARs, including classic CD28 and 4-1BB co-stimulatory domains as well as the “1XX” design wherein the CD3ζ chain bears Tyr to Phe mutations in ITAMs 2 and 3, restricting signaling for T-cell activation through ITAM 1 alone.

We compared huMNC2-41BB-CD3ζ (CAR44), huMNC2-CD28-CD3ζ (CAR28) and huMNC2-CD28-CD3ζ−1XX (CAR22) for their ability to kill both high and low antigen-expressing cancer cells under stress conditions thought to induce CAR T-cell exhaustion. Endpoint analysis included the measure of CAR T-cell persistence in vitro and in vivo, changes in CAR T phenotype and the test of their ability to eliminate tumors comprising low antigen-expressing cells. To explore the sensitivity of each CAR, we differentially labeled low versus high MUC1* expressing cancer cells. T47D wild-type breast cancer cells, which express low amounts of MUC1* relative to patient breast tumor tissue, was engineered with mCherry to fluoresce red “T47D-wt”. The same breast cancer cells were also transduced to express more MUC1* as well as GFP to fluoresce green (“T47D-MUC1*). Xenografted tumors were heterogeneous, comprising defined mixtures of these two cell populations to more accurately mimic real patient tumors as well as providing a method of tracking cell killing as a function of antigen density.

Material and methods

Anti-MUC1* antibodies

Anti-MUC1* mouse monoclonal antibodies were obtained by immunizing with 45 amino acid extracellular domain, MUC1*ecd45, peptide conjugated at the C-terminus with KLH. Candidates were screened by flow cytometry, ELISAs, western blots and immunohistochemistry (IHC) for their ability to bind MUC1-positive cancer cells but not MUC1-negative cells, recognize cells transfected with an MUC1*ecd45, bind to some MUC1*-derived peptides but not to others, recognize a conformational epitope as indicated by failure to work in western blot, and have robust membrane staining of cancerous tissues and no or minimal binding to normal tissues. MNC2 was selected, sequenced and humanized by CDR grafting. Humanized MNC2scFv-Fc fusion protein (huMNC2-scFv-Fc) complementary DNA was cloned into the expression vector pSECTag2/A (Thermo Fisher Scientific, cat#V90020). Protein was produced using the ExpiCHO expression system (Thermo Fisher Scientific cat# A29133), then purified from the supernatant by protein A affinity chromatography (HiTrap MabSelect PrismA, Cytiva cat# 17549853) followed by size exclusion chromatography (Superdex 200 Increase 10/300 GL, Cytiva cat# 28990944).

CAR constructs

huMNC2-scFv is the antigen binding fragment of: (1) huMNC2-CAR44 (41BB-CD3ζ), (2) huMNC2-CAR28 (CD28-CD3ζ) and (3) huMNC2-CAR22 (CD28-CD3ζ−1XX). Lentiviral expression plasmid and lentiviral particle were produced by Lentigen (Miltenyi Biotech). Functional MOI (multiplicity of infection) is 0.92 for CAR44 and 1.06 for CAR22.

CAR T-cell production

CD3+T cells (AllCells) were stimulated with CD3/CD28 T cell TransAct (Miltenyi Biotech) for 48 hours after which TransAct was removed by centrifugation and T cells were transduced with huMNC2-CAR lentiviral vector particles (Lentigen), then expanded in TexMACS medium (Miltenyi Biotech) supplemented with 100 IU/mL recombinant human IL-2 (Miltenyi Biotech). Transduction efficiency was measured by flow cytometry.

Cell engineering and cell culture

T47D, HEK-293, and HCT-116 cancer cell lines were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC). T47D and HCT-116 cell lines were transduced with pIRESII-MUC1*-EGFP (Takara Bio) to overexpress MUC1* (T47D-MUC1*, HCT-MUC1*). Full-length MUC1 (HCT-MUC1-18) was generated from single-cell cloning and selected for slow growth. Flow cytometry and western blot using anti-tandem repeat antibody VU4H5 (Santa Cruz Biotechnologies) verified it was only full-length. To distinguish low MUC1* expressing cells from high MUC1* expressing cells, cells were transduced with firefly-luciferase (GeneCopoeia)-mCherry (low MUC1*) or GFP (high MUC1*).

Image analysis

Image processing, analysis of CAR T induced cell clustering (clusters defined as ≥980 pixels from a 1,024×1,024 pixel 8-bit image) and integrated pixel density analysis were performed using Image J Fiji.

Mouse tumor models

Female NSG mice (NOD.Cg-Prkdcscid Il2rgtm1Wjl/SzJ) 18–22 g, (Jackson Laboratories) were implanted with 90-day release estrogen pellets, then subcutaneously inoculated with 0.25E6 to 2.5E6 T47D breast cancer cells or 0.5E6 HCT-116 cell lines overexpressing MUC1* (HCT-MUC1*) in Matrigel (Corning). In some cases, heterogeneous tumors comprising defined ratios of low and high MUC1* expressing cells were implanted. CAR T cells were intravenously injected 5–7 days post-xenograft. Figures indicate the absolute number of CAR+T cells. Before CAR T treatment, mice were divided into groups of comparable bioluminescence (BLI) signal representing equivalent tumor burden.

All human cancer cells contained mCherry firefly luciferase (GeneCopoeia). Mice were intraperitoneally injected with 150 mg/kg D-Luciferin (Perkin Elmer) in phosphate-buffered saline to detect the BLI using the In Vivo Imaging System (IVIS, PerkinElmer/Revvity) and data were analyzed with Living Image software (Perkin/Elmer). To assess tumor MUC1* and CAR T cells, some mice were sacrificed at specific time points. Details provided in online supplemental table 1.

Animal health, body weight and tumor burden were assessed weekly. Mice were sacrificed at humane end points such as excessive tumor burden (≥1,000 mm3), ≥15% wt loss or poor condition. In vivo experiments were performed in accordance with the Boston University Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee and adhered to animal use guidelines and ethical regulations. We used the ARRIVE checklist when writing our reports.

Tissue collection and processing

Tumor, spleen and blood were harvested at the end of in vivo studies. Single-cell suspensions were processed for Fluorescence-Activated Cell Sorting (FACS) analysis of CD3+human T cells. Spleen suspensions were obtained by macerating tissues through a 40 µm nylon cell strainer in ACK buffer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Peripheral blood was collected in capillary tubes from retro-orbital bleeding and treated with ACK buffer. Tumor sections were minced and digested with 20× Collagenase II (Millipore Sigma) and 100× Dnase I (Millipore Sigma) before incubating overnight in culture medium at 37°C 5% CO2. After 24 hours, some tumor cultures were imaged for mCherry/GFP expression, then processed for flow cytometry to assess for the presence/absence of MUC1*-positive cancer cells.

Immunohistochemistry

Cancerous and normal human tissue arrays (US Biomax), mouse tissues, and xenografted tumors were fixed in 10% normal buffered formalin. Paraffin embedding and sectioning of mouse tissues was done by the Harvard University Histology Core. After deparaffinization, antigen retrieval (pH 6.0) was performed for 10 min. Sections were incubated in blocking buffer (10% normal goat serum and 5% Bovine Serum Albumin (BSA) in Tris Buffered Saline (TBS)) at room temperature for 1 hour before incubating in primary antibody in 1% BSA-TBS overnight at 4°C. Sections were washed with TBS-T, incubated with goat anti-mouse conjugated-HRP (Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) or streptavidin-conjugated-HRP (Jackson ImmunoResearch Laboratories) in TBS, developed using Betazoid DAB chromogen (Biocare Medical), counterstained with Modified Mayer’s Hematoxylin (Abcam) and NH4OH (Thermo Fisher Scientific), then dehydrated in ethanol (Fisher Scientific) and xylene (Millipore Sigma), cover-slipped and imaged using the Aperio ScanScope CS (Aperio Technologies, Inc/Leica Biosystems). Primary antibodies included huMNC2-scFV-Fc, sometimes with biotinylation at 50 µg/mL (Minerva), muMNC2-IgG at 15 µg/mL (Minerva), or 2 µg/mL VU4H5 (Santa Cruz). This protocol was CLIA-validated at two separate clinical sites.

H-score analysis

H-score is the product of the percentage of the tumor that is positive for the target antigen times the intensity of staining, where it is absent (0), weak (1+), moderate (2+), or strong (3+). H-scores range from 0 to 300 (100%*3+staining). The H-scores reported here were assessed and calculated by a board-certified pathologist.

CAR expression was measured with Goat F(ab’)2 Anti-Human IgG (Fab’)2 (biotin-conjugated) (Abcam) followed by Alexa-Fluor-647-Streptavidin (BioLegend). Data were collected using the Attune NxT cytometer with a 96-well plate loader, then analyzed with FlowJo Software (BD Biosciences).

Results

MNC2 binds to the cleavage product MUC1* but not to full-length MUC1

On normal epithelial cells, MUC1 is full-length with the largest domain comprising tandem repeats of a 20 amino acid motif. On cancer cells, MUC1 is cleaved, and the tandem repeat domain is shed. Cleavage and release of most of the extracellular domain expose a previously masked epitope to which activating ligands bind and dimerize the truncated extracellular domain of MUC1*, which activates the MAP kinase growth pathway (figure 1A). We developed an essentially cancer-specific monoclonal antibody, MNC2, that binds to this ectopic site but, importantly, does not bind to full-length MUC1. MNC2 binds to HCT-116 cells only when they are transduced with MUC1*, but not when transduced with full-length MUC1 (figure 1B). HCT-MUC1-18 is a single cell clone that is resistant to cleavage in the absence of an exogenously added cleavage enzyme. Similarly, CAR T cells directed to the cancer cells by huMNC2-scFv recognize and cluster the HCT-MUC1* cancer cells but do not recognize full-length MUC1 (figure 1C,D).

IHC studies were performed on a wide range of human breast cancerous tissue specimens using huMNC2-scFv or occasionally parent muMNC2. Anti-MUC1* antibody, MNC2, selectively recognizes human cancerous tissues (figure 1E) and shows little or no cross-reactivity to normal tissues (figure 1F). huMNC2-scFv reacted with 90% of breast cancers without bias toward subtype. In an extensive study of tissue specimens from over 650 patients with breast cancer, with various breast cancer subtypes, the vast majority of the MUC1* staining was on the cell membrane with staining intensity predominantly 2+ or 3+ (online supplemental table 2). MUC1* is also highly expressed in multiple cancer subtypes including greater than 80% ovarian cancers, 79% pancreatic cancers, 80% lung cancers, 65% gastro-intestinal cancers and 67% prostate cancers (online supplemental figure 1).

Most normal tissues did not react with MNC2. Of 35 types of normal tissues, MNC2 showed little to no binding, save sporadic, weak (+1) positivity restricted to the apical membrane in a subset of epithelial cells (online supplemental table 3). Stomach exhibited staining on the apical surface of chief cells and fundic type epithelial cells, areas that are typically inaccessible to T cells and where the high acidity would not be conducive to antibody binding. Taken together, the relative lack of strong MUC1* immunohistochemical staining in normal tissues versus the strong positivity in breast cancer supports a favorable risk-benefit ratio in patients with advanced breast cancer.

MUC1*-targeting CAR T bearing the 1XX mutations in CD3ζ

The cancer specificity of the MNC2 antibody enables a CAR T approach for the treatment of MUC1*-positive solid tumors. We generated three CAR constructs for comparison: huMNC2-41BB-CD3ζ (CAR44), huMNC2-CD28-CD3ζ (CAR28) and huMNC2-CD28-CD3ζ−1XX (CAR22), wherein “1XX” stands for the four Tyr to Phe substitutions in ITAMs 2 and 3 of CD3ζ that were designed to minimize CAR T-cell exhaustion and increase in vivo persistence. In vitro studies were first performed. Characterization by flow cytometry showed that all three CAR T cells were comparable in terms of per cent CAR expression, proportion of CD4 versus CD8 positive cells, and memory phenotype across several donors (online supplemental figure 2A–E). An in vitro real-time target cell killing assay was performed that showed that all three CARs effectively killed MUC1* positive cancer cells. However, at lower CAR T dose, against cancer cells expressing low levels of the target antigen, CAR22, with the 1XX mutations, outperformed the other CARs (online supplemental figure 2F–H).

For in vivo studies, we first compared the efficacy of CAR44 and CAR22. Both CARs are targeted to the tumor by huMNC2-scFv. Whereas huMNC2-CAR44 uses a 41BB co-stimulatory domain, huMNC2-CAR22 uses a CD28 co-stimulatory domain and also bears the 1XX Tyr to Phe mutations in the CD3ζ signaling domain (figure 2A). In vivo efficacy of CAR44 and CAR22 was initially evaluated in a model system of an MUC1* positive cancer. MUC1-negative colon cancer cells (HCT-116) were engineered to overexpress MUC1* (green fluorescent protein reporter) and mCherry/luciferase to enable visualization and quantification of tumor volume by bioluminescence. On day 0, 500,000 HCT-MUC1* cancer cells were implanted. Tumor-bearing mice were injected into the lateral tail vein with a single dose of approximately 10 million CAR44, CAR22, or untransduced T cells. At this high dose, both MUC1* CAR constructs eliminated tumors in most treated mice and significantly prolonged survival compared with control mice (figure 2B–D). CAR T cells recovered from spleen and tumor post-sacrifice at day 96 were quantified (figure 2E,F, online supplemental figure 3E). Analysis of the “recurrent” tumors in treated mice showed they had become MUC1* negative, by ejecting or silencing the MUC1* plasmid, as evidenced by becoming GFP negative (online supplemental figure 3A–D). However, analysis of CAR T cells recovered from spleen and tumor post-sacrifice at day 96 showed that huMNC2-CAR22 T cells persisted longer than huMNC2-CAR44 cells (figure 2E,F, online supplemental figure 3E).

Three CAR constructs are compared for efficacy, persistence and ability to kill low antigen-expressing cancer cells

We next sought to determine if the percentage of high MUC1* expressing cells in the tumor, differences in the co-stimulatory domain of the CAR, or the 1XX mutations in CD3ζ play a role in the killing of low versus high antigen-expressing cells or CAR T-cell persistence. Three CARs, huMNC2-CAR44 (41BB-CD3ζ), huMNC2-CAR28 (CD28-CD3ζ) or huMNC2-CAR22 (CD28-CD3ζ−1XX) all targeted to the tumor by the same antibody fragment, huMNC2-scFv, were generated (figure 3A). Pretreatment analysis showed that all three CARs had equivalent percentage CAR expression, and similar phenotype (online supplemental figure 4A,B). Heterogeneous breast tumors comprising different ratios of low versus high MUC1* expressing tumors were generated to more accurately mimic real patient tumors. T47D wild-type breast cancer cell line, “T47D-wt”, which expresses low amounts of MUC1* relative to patient breast tumor tissue, was engineered to express mCherry to fluoresce red. The same breast cancer cells were also transduced to express more MUC1* as well as GFP to fluoresce green, “T47D-MUC1*”. Xenografted tumors comprising different percentages of these two cell populations are referred to herein as “T47D-X%”, wherein X is the percentage of the cell population that expressed high levels of MUC1*.

Here, CAR44, CAR28 and CAR22 T cells were tested for their ability to kill heterogeneous breast tumors comprising 7.5%, 15% or 30% T47D cells that were engineered to express high levels of MUC1* and GFP. A total of 0.25E6 tumor cells were implanted into female NSG mice. A single dose of CAR44, CAR28 or CAR22 T cells was administered at either 2.5E6 or 0.25E6 CAR T cells for effector to target ratios of 10:1 or 1:1. Tumor size was tracked by bioluminescence (figure 3B). At high CAR T-cell dose, all three CAR T cells effectively inhibited the growth of high antigen-expressing cells until about day 40 when tumors in mice treated with both CARs bearing wild-type CD3ζ began to recur, while CAR22 bearing the 1XX mutations continued to suppress tumor growth. At the other extreme, animals bearing low MUC1* expressing tumors and treated with low dose CAR T dose could not be effectively treated by CAR44 or CAR28. Only CAR22, with the 1XX mutations, was able to continuously suppress tumor growth. After sacrifice, residual tumors were excised and examined to determine if tumor recurrence was mediated by the high MUC1* (GFP) expressing cells or the low MUC1* cells (mCherry). At high CAR T dose, tumor recurrence in animals treated with CAR44 or CAR28 was driven by the red, low MUC1* expressing cells. At low CAR T dose, CAR22 killed both high and low MUC1* expressing cells, while neither CAR44 nor CAR28 could, perhaps due to exhaustion (figure 3C).

Consistent with that idea, flow cytometry showed that residual tumors recovered from the low dose CAR44 or CAR28 treated mice essentially had no CAR T cells remaining, while tumors from CAR22 treated mice had more than a thousand. Tumors recovered from low dose CAR22 treated mice had significantly fewer MUC1* positive cells than tumors recovered from low dose CAR44 or CAR28 treated mice (online supplemental figure 4C–F).

Comparison of CAR22 and CAR44 in an in vitro exhaustion assay against heterogeneous tumors

Because expression of exhaustion markers may be imprecise, we developed an in vitro system that tests CAR T-cell exhaustion. In vitro, MUC1* CAR T cells (CAR44 or CAR22) were incubated with magnetic beads to which was chemically attached a synthetic MUC1* extracellular domain peptide. After 6 days, the antigen beads were magnetically removed and MUC1* positive T47D breast cancer cells were added. The breast cancer cell population consisted of 15% T47D cells that had been engineered to express more MUC1* and GFP and 85% T47D-wt cells that expressed relatively low levels of MUC1* and carried mCherry. The final CAR T to cancer cell ratio, effector to target ratio, was 1:1, with 25,000 cells each. The real-time CAR T-cell killing of high (green) and low (red) MUC1* expressing cells was recorded on an In Cell Analyzer 6000 (Cytiva) over a 72-hour period (online supplemental movie 1). Photographs of the low (red) versus high (green) MUC1* expressing cells were taken at specified time points during incubation with either CAR22 or huMNC2-CAR44. The change in integrated pixel density, fluorescence, as a function of time was quantified using image analysis software (figure 3D). Note that in some cases, the integrated pixel density, fluorescence, increases due to cancer cell clustering before it decreases as a result of cancer cell killing. As can be seen in the video, the CAR22 T cells appear to be more active as both high and low MUC1* expressing cells are rapidly killed. CAR44 T cells appear to kill the high MUC1* expressing cells at about the same rate as CAR22. However, CAR44 T cells cluster the low MUC1* expressing cells but are relatively slow to kill them. Statistical analysis involving an objective multiple changepoint regression approach, quantify these visual impressions. The action of CAR22 T cells against the low MUC1* expressing cells (red) results in an increase in fluorescence lasting 8.23 hours before the change in direction and corresponding decrease in fluorescence from target cell killing. In contrast, the action of CAR44 T cells against the low antigen-expressing cells shows increasing fluorescence for 31.33 hours before the breakpoint of decreasing fluorescence. At high antigen density, CAR22 and CAR44 behave similarly with the decreasing rate of change of fluorescence at −0.020 per hour and −0.030 per hour, respectively, although two breakpoints were detected for CAR44 where in the first 16.3 hours there is a slight increase in the rate of change of fluorescence (+0.0019) before the decrease in fluorescence, presumably due to target cell killing (online supplemental figure 5).

Ex vivo analysis of changes in phenotype as CAR T cells transition from active to exhausted

To further investigate causes or characteristics of CAR T-cell exhaustion, we next examined changes in CAR T-cell numbers and phenotype before and after tumor recurrence. Female NSG mice, xenografted with human breast cancer cells, were treated with either CAR22, CAR44, or CAR28 at an effector to target ratio of 1:1 or 10:1 CAR T cells (figure 4A,B, online supplemental table 1C). Animals were sacrificed either at day 20 or at day 53. The measure of tumor weights at day 20 demonstrates that at high CAR T-cell dose, all three CAR T cells were equivalent, but by day 53, only CAR22 continued to suppress tumor growth (figure 4C,D, online supplemental figure 6A,B). Compared with CAR44 and CAR28, CAR22 had higher numbers of CAR+ T cells recovered from the blood, spleen and tumor (figure 4E,F, online supplemental figure 6C,D). At low CAR T-cell dose, CAR22 T cells recovered at day 20 had significantly more effector state cells, and by day 53 had a higher naïve state population (figure 4G, online supplemental figure 6E). At high CAR T-cell dose, on both day 20 and 53, CAR T cells were recovered from the blood and spleen for all three CARs, except for CAR28, which could not be detected in the blood. In tumors, CAR44 and CAR22 T cells were recovered at day 20, but only CAR22 could be recovered at day 53 (figure 4H, online supplemental figure 6F and 7A,B). Taken together, the data are consistent with a conclusion that CAR22 T cells, with the 1XX mutations, persist longer and inhibit tumor recurrence better than CAR44 or CAR28 T cells. An overview of the phenotype shows that, compared with CAR44 and CAR28, CAR22 cells comprise a greater number in the effector/effector memory state and a smaller number in the naïve state.

Investigating the limits of CAR22 killing of very low antigen-expressing tumors

Having demonstrated that CAR22 has greater efficacy than CAR44 or CAR28 against low MUC1* expressing tumors, we next investigated low antigen threshold limits, while still triggering CAR22-mediated cancer cell killing. T47D-wt breast cancer cells express low levels of MUC1* (H-score: 10 out of 300). Figure 5A shows the bioluminescent photographs of T47D-wt tumor-bearing mice treated with a single injection of either 0.25E6 or 2.5E6 CAR22 T cells for an effector to target ratio of 1:1 or 10:1. There was a significant decrease in tumor volume in mice treated with either high or low CAR22 dose (figure 5B,C). However, the higher CAR22 dose more efficiently killed the tumor (figure 5D). Figure 5E,F shows that after nearly 100 days, mice treated once with low dose CAR22 cells persisted in high number and did not show significant co-expression of exhaustion markers. Tumors recovered post-sacrifice show some residual mCherry fluorescence (figure 5G), but the majority of MUC1*-positive cancer cells had been eliminated (figure 5H). In a parallel experiment, we investigated the effect of antigen density on the efficacy and memory phenotype of CAR22 T cells post-treatment in mice xenografted with breast cancer cells expressing varying MUC1* (online supplemental figure 8A–C). High numbers of CAR22 T cells were recovered from the blood, spleen, and tumors. Most of the recovered CAR22 T cells were in the effector and central memory state, where the population in the effector state increased with increasing antigen density. There was no significant expression of exhaustion markers T cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain-containing protein 3 (TIM3), Lymphocyte Activation Gene 3 (LAG3) or Programmed Cell Death Protein 1 (PD-1) (online supplemental figure 8D,E).

Model of potential size of the breast cancer population that could be effectively treated with either CAR44 or CAR22

Generation of heterogeneous tumors was to determine how different CAR T cells could impact tumors in real patients. IHC of excised heterogeneous tumors from untreated mice (figure 6A) covered the range of antigen densities found in a patient with the breast cancer population (figure 6B) as quantified by H-scores (range 0–300). Figure 6C shows a graph of H-scores of tumors from actual patients with breast cancer, overlayed with a graph of our animal experiments, wherein animals were treated with either CAR22 or CAR44. The dashed lines indicate the range of H-scores of tumors implanted into test mice that were cured by CAR22 or CAR44. Because of the greater ability of CAR22 to recognize and kill the low MUC1* expressing cells, this model predicts that CAR22 may be able to treat a very large percentage of patients with breast cancer.

Discussion

We were able to effectively target MUC1 positive tumors by targeting the transmembrane cleavage product, MUC1*, which is a growth factor receptor. For cancer therapeutics, we thought it was critical to develop an antibody that binds to MUC1* but not to full-length MUC1, which could introduce off-target toxicities as normal epithelia express full-length MUC1. MNC2 recognizes an epitope that is masked in full-length MUC1 and only revealed after cleavage and release of most of the MUC1 extracellular domain. MNC2 binds to a conformational epitope that is created after cleavage by tumor-associated enzymes. MNC2 reactive MUC1* is expressed in 85–95% of breast cancers of all subtypes, where positivity across essentially 100% of the tissue was seen in nearly 90% of the samples (online supplemental table 2; online supplemental figure 1B). In comparison, only about 15%–20% of breast cancers are HER2 positive. Only a few normal tissues express MNC2-reactive MUC1* and only a few showed membrane staining, which was consistently apical; the per cent of the tissue that was stained was relatively low, typically 1–5% (online supplemental table 3). Importantly, no basolateral reactivity was ever observed. It is the basolateral portion of the epithelial layers that is immediately adjacent to the lamina propria. Taken together, the relative lack of strong MUC1* staining in normal tissues versus the strong positivity in solid tumor cancers, and specifically breast cancers, supports a favorable risk-benefit ratio for patients.

The cancer selectivity of MNC2 makes it ideal for incorporation into CAR T or antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) format, where avoidance of normal tissues is critical. The high MUC1* expression on tumors, combined with low or no expression on normal tissues, provides for a high therapeutic index that allows dosing CAR T cells at high levels with little or no off-target toxicity.

We incorporated this new antibody, MNC2, into three different CARs wherein one construct bore the “1XX” mutations in CD3ζ that restrict signaling to only one of the three ITAMs: CAR44, huMNC2-41BB-CD3ζ; CAR28, huMNC2-CD28-CD3ζ; and CAR22, huMNC2- CD28-CD3ζ−1XX. Experiments compared their efficacy, persistence, and sensitivity to tumors comprising low antigen-expressing cells. The CAR bearing the 1XX mutations outperformed the other CARs. At high CAR T-cell dose, against tumors expressing moderately high amounts of MUC1*, all three CAR T cells effectively inhibited tumor growth, but only until about day 40, when tumors recurred in mice treated with either CAR44 or CAR28 that bear CD3ζ, but not in mice treated with CAR22 bearing CD3ζ−1XX. At lower CAR T doses, for treatment of tumors expressing low MUC1*, only CAR22 continuously suppressed tumor growth in long-term animal experiments. Post-sacrifice, CAR22 T cells were more abundant in blood, spleen and tumor than either CAR44 or CAR28 T cells.

One of the challenges of treating cancers is tumor recurrence, which can be caused by many factors, including the escape of low antigen-expressing cancer cells. To address this issue, we implanted heterogeneous tumors, where 15% of the tumor cells were engineered to express GFP and high MUC1*, where the wild-type population expressed mCherry and low amounts of MUC1*. Post-sacrifice analysis of recurrent tumors in CAR44 and CAR28 treated animals showed they were devoid of high antigen-expressing cells and comprised solely of low antigen-expressing cells. CAR22 suppressed tumor recurrence by killing both low and high antigen-expressing cells.

In these studies, CAR22 suppressed tumor growth for extended periods even at low doses, consistent with reduced exhaustion and increased persistence, corroborating and extending earlier findings in models of leukemia and ovarian cancer. A recent clinical trial in recurrent diffuse large B-cell lymphoma showed that a CD19-CAR bearing the 1XX mutations was effective at low CAR T-cell dose.

Conclusion

Taken together, our data support proceeding to a clinical trial of huMNC2-CAR22 for breast cancer and other cancers such as lung and pancreatic cancers, the majority of which express MNC2-reactive MUC1*.
