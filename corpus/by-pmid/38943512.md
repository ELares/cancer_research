# Multilayered proteomics reveals that JAM-A promotes breast cancer progression via regulation of amino acid transporter LAT1.

## Abstract

Recent studies have shown that transmembrane-type tight junction proteins are upregulated in various cancers compared with their levels in normal tissues and are involved in cancer progression, suggesting that they are potential therapeutic targets. Here, we demonstrated the expression profile and a novel role of junctional adhesion molecule-A (JAM-A) in breast cancer. Immunohistochemistry of surgical specimens showed that JAM-A was highly expressed from carcinoma in situ lesions, as in other adenocarcinomas, with higher expression in invasive carcinomas. High expression of JAM-A contributed to malignant aspects such as lymph node metastasis and lymphatic involvement positivity. In breast cancer cells, JAM-A expression status affects malignant potentials including proliferation and migration. Multilayered proteomics revealed that JAM-A interacts with the amino acid transporter LAT1 in breast cancer cells. JAM-A regulates the expression of LAT1 and interacts with it on the whole cell membrane, leading to enhanced amino acid uptake to promote tumor growth. Double high expression of JAM-A and LAT1 predicts poor prognosis in patients with breast cancer. Of note, an antibody against an extracellular domain of JAM-A suppressed the proliferation of breast cancer cells. Our findings indicate the possibility of JAM-A-targeted therapy ideally combined with LAT1-targeted therapy as a new therapeutic strategy against breast cancer.

INTRODUCTION

A TJ is a multiprotein junctional complex located in the apical‐most intercellular adhesion structures in epithelial and endothelial cellular sheets. TJ contributes to the maintenance of homeostasis of multicellular organisms under physiological conditions. Constituent proteins of TJ are divided into transmembrane‐type proteins and cytoplasmic proteins. Recently, we have reported that transmembrane‐type TJPs are upregulated in various cancers compared with their levels in normal tissues and are involved in cancer progression, suggesting that transmembrane‐type TJPs are potential therapeutic targets. 

Junctional adhesion molecules (JAMs) are transmembrane‐type TJPs and belong to the immunoglobulin superfamily. There are mainly three JAM family members: JAM‐A, JAM‐B, and JAM‐C12. In JAM family members, we have revealed that JAM‐A is upregulated in lung adenocarcinoma and uterine cervical adenocarcinoma and is involved in the progression of these cancers. In BC, the role of JAM‐A has been a controversial issue. It was initially noted that JAM‐A might play a key role in inhibiting BC cell invasion and metastasis. However, other reports have suggested that elevated expression of JAM‐A is involved in BC progression, invasion, metastasis, and poor prognosis. According to recent reports, JAM‐A regulates HER2 protein degradation and signaling, and the regulation is linked to HER2‐positive BC progression and resistance to HER2‐targeted therapies. However, JAM‐A is not yet fully understood as a mechanism of BC progression.

This study showed that aberrantly expressed JAM‐A is one of the potential therapeutic targets in BC. Through multilayered proteomic approaches, we revealed for the first time that JAM‐A regulates the expression of LAT1, leading to alterations in amino acid uptake. Our findings provide a new insight into a therapeutic strategy, JAM‐A‐targeted therapy ideally combined with LAT1‐targeted therapy, against BC.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Antibodies and siRNAs 

The antibodies and siRNAs used in this study are shown in Tables S1 and S2, respectively.

Patient samples

This study included 133 breast ductal carcinoma cases surgically resected at Sapporo Medical University Hospital between 2011 and 2014. Representative formalin‐fixed paraffin‐embedded blocks were selected from each archived material in our institution. The clinicopathological factors of selected cases are summarized in Table S3. Two experienced surgical pathologists independently evaluated the H&E‐stained slides and immunohistochemical results of JAM‐A and LAT1 expression. The immunohistochemistry of JAM‐A could be evaluated in 133 cases, but that of LAT1 could be evaluated in only 130 cases because ductal carcinoma lesions disappeared in immunohistochemical specimens of LAT1.

Immunohistochemistry

Immunohistochemistry from surgical specimens was conducted as we previously reported. Primary antibodies are shown in Table S1. To evaluate the immunohistochemical staining positivity of JAM‐A semi‐quantitatively, we assessed the IRS as in our previous studies. Immunohistochemistry of LAT1 was evaluated for membranous or cytoplasmic staining. Tumors with at least partial positivity of LAT1 were assigned to the LAT1‐positive group and tumors with no positivity of LAT1 were assigned to the LAT1‐negative group. When evaluating the slides, the observers (KM and AT) were blinded to the clinical data. When discordant cases appeared, each case was discussed and a consensus was reached.

Cell culture and transfection

We obtained BC cell lines including MCF7 (HTB‐22), T47D (HTB‐133), SKBR‐3 (HTB‐30), and MDA‐MB‐231 (HTB‐26) cell lines from the ATCC. MCF7 cells were maintained in Eagle's minimum essential medium (EMEM) (Wako). T47D, SKBR3, and MDA‐MB‐231 cells were maintained in DMEM (Nacalai Tesque). Each culture medium was supplemented with 10% FBS (Corning) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

Cells were transfected with gene‐specific or scrambled siRNAs using Lipofectamine™ RNAiMAX (Thermo Fisher Scientific) for knockdown of JAM‐A and LAT1 genes. Sequences of siRNA are shown in Table S2.

JAM‐A KO MCF7 cell line was constructed with CRISPR/Cas9 technology using a GeneArt CRISPR Nuclease Vector Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) according to the manufacturer's instructions as we previously reported. The sequence of the target site for the human JAM‐A gene is 5′‐GGACAAAGGCGCAAGTCGAG‐3′. The loss of JAM‐A protein expression was screened using western blotting.

Cell proliferation assay

WST‐8 assays and bromodeoxyuridine (BrdU) incorporation assays were conducted to assess cell proliferation as we previously reported. 

Transwell migration assay

We used Transwell chambers (#3422, Corning) to investigate cell migration ability in 24‐well plates. Cell suspensions (1 × 105 cells in EMEM with 1% FBS) were added to the upper chambers. The lower chambers were filled with EMEM with 10% FBS. At 24 h after adding each medium, transmigrating cells were fixed and visualized using 0.04% crystal violet and 2% ethanol for 10 min. The number of transmigrating cells was counted using a microscope (Olympus).

Immunocytochemistry of cell blocks

The preparation of cell blocks and evaluation of proliferation capacity from cultured cells were conducted as we had previously reported. 

Immunofluorescence microscopy

Immunofluorescence microscopy from cultured cells was conducted as we previously reported. Primary antibodies are shown in Table S1. Double‐stained samples of JAM‐A and LAT1 or 4F2hc were observed using a super‐resolution confocal laser microscope (ELYRAS.1LSM780, Zeiss), and other samples were observed using a fluorescence microscope (Olympus).

Amino acid uptake assay

We assessed amino acid uptake capacity in MCF7 cells with changes in JAM‐A expression using an Amino Acid Uptake Assay Kit (Dojindo Laboratories) according to the manufacturer's instructions. Briefly, amino acid transporter activity was evaluated by measuring the fluorescence intensity of the complex of BPA, amino acid analogs, with a BPA probe using a Varioskan LUX multimode microplate reader (Thermo Fisher Scientific).

Comprehensive proteomic analysis

Comprehensive proteomic analyses were conducted as we previously reported. For MS/MS data analysis, we used the SequestHT (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and Mascot version 2.5 (Matrix Science) algorithms embedded in the Proteome Discoverer 2.2 platform (Thermo Fisher Scientific), and the peak lists were searched against the UniProt human databases and N‐terminal 3 × FLAG‐tagged human JAM‐A sequence. The tolerance of precursor ions and that of fragment ions were set to 10 ppm and 0.02 Da, respectively. GO enrichment analysis and KEGG pathway analysis were performed on DAVID (https://david.ncifcrf.gov/). 

Immunoprecipitation and mass spectrometry

Cells were rinsed with ice‐cold PBS and scraped with ice‐cold lysis buffer (10 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0), 1% NP40, 150 mM NaCl, 1 mM EDTA and protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche Diagnostics 4,693,124,001)). The cell suspension was transferred into a microcentrifuge tube and kept on ice for 20 min. After centrifugation at 13,000 rpm for 10 min at 4°C, the supernatant was collected in a fresh tube. The supernatant was incubated with equilibrated anti‐FLAG M2 magnetic beads (M8823, Sigma) for 2 h at 4°C with gentle mixing. The magnetic beads were washed with lysis buffer and then with 20 mM Tris–HCl (pH 8.0). Immunoprecipitants were eluted with 100 ng/μL 3 × FLAG peptide elution buffer according to the manufacturer's instructions. Immunoprecipitants were alkylated, trypsinized, and desalted and then used for LC–MS/MS analysis. Obtained MS data were analyzed using Proteome Discoverer 2.2 as described above, and the PPI of the co‐immunoprecipitated proteins was scored using a quantitative scoring system, MiST (https://modbase.compbio.ucsf.edu/mist/). 

Statistical analysis

The measured values are represented as means or medians ± standard deviation. EZR software 1.61 was used to conduct statistical analysis. Data were analyzed and compared using the unpaired two‐tailed Student's t‐test, Fisher's exact test, one‐way analysis of variance, and Kruskal–Wallis test. Survival time analyses were conducted using the Kaplan–Meier method and log‐rank test.

Supporting information

Detailed information about methods of cell cycle analysis, RT‐qPCR analysis, and transcriptome analysis can be found in Doc S1.

RESULTS

 JAM‐A is significantly highly expressed in breast ductal carcinoma and associated with poor prognosis

Previous studies have shown that JAM‐A is highly expressed in various cancers. Therefore, we immunohistochemically evaluated JAM‐A expression and localization and their correlations with clinicopathological factors in surgical specimens. Details of the clinicopathological factors are shown in Table S3. In the DE, JAM‐A positivity was mainly observed in the most apical end of the adjacent cell membrane, equivalent to a TJ position. Conversely, in DCIS and IDC, JAM‐A positivity was ectopically observed in the whole cell membrane and cytoplasm (Figure 1A). IRSs of DCIS and IDC were significantly higher than those of DE (p < 0.001 vs. DE, respectively) (Figure 1B). Most cases in DE showed a score of 1+. In contrast, DCIS and IDC mainly showed scores of 2+ or 3+ (Figure 1C). JAM‐A was ectopically expressed in DCIS and IDC (p = 1.9E‐11 vs. DE in DCIS, p = 1.0E‐10 vs. DE in IDC, Figure 1D). In representative clinicopathological parameters, a high JAM‐A expression group (IRS of 28 or more) was significantly correlated with lymph node metastasis positivity (p = 0.02) and lymphatic involvement positivity (p = 0.016) (Table 1).

To investigate the association of JAM‐A expression with survival outcomes, we conducted Kaplan–Meier survival curve analyses. Using our immunohistochemical data for JAM‐A and clinical outcomes, the high JAM‐A expression group tended to have low overall and progression‐free survival rates, although the association between JAM‐A and survival outcomes was not statistically significant (N = 133; Figure S1). Using a public Kaplan–Meier plotter database (https://kmplot.com/analysis/), in which there was a much larger number of cases (N = 2976), a high level of JAM‐A mRNA expression was significantly associated with poor overall survival (N = 2976; Figure S2). These results suggest that a high level of JAM‐A expression plays some role in the malignancy of BCs.

 JAM‐A expression contributes to proliferation of breast cancer cells

We evaluated the alteration of proliferative capacity resulting from changes in JAM‐A expression in BC cells. The MCF7 cell line was selected for JAM‐A knockdown because western blot analysis showed that MCF7 cells expressed a high level of JAM‐A (Figure S3). MCF7 cells were transfected with siRNA targeting JAM‐A or scrambled siRNA (Figure 2A). JAM‐A knockdown significantly reduced proliferative capacity in the WST‐8 assay (Figure 2B). The percentage of Ki‐67‐positive cells, an indicator of proliferating cells, in cell blocks made from JAM‐A knockdown cells was significantly lower than that of control cells (Figure 2C). Also, in T47D, another BC cell line, JAM‐A knockdown by siRNA significantly reduced proliferative capacity in the WST‐8 assay (Figure S4A,B). Next, we established a JAM‐A KO clone from MCF7 cells using the CRISPR/Cas9 system (Figure 2D). Under a phase‐contrast microscope, the established JAM‐A KO MCF7 clone obscured the cobblestone‐like appearance of control MCF7 cells (Figure S5). The inhibitory effect on proliferative capacity was more obvious in the JAM‐A KO MCF7 clone (36% vs. control at 72 h; Figure 2E) than in siRNA‐based JAM‐A knockdown MCF7 cells (70% for siRNA‐A and 84% for siRNA‐B vs. control at 72 h; Figure 2B) in WST‐8 assays, as was found in our previous study for lung adenocarcinoma cells. The percentage of Ki‐67‐positive cells in cell blocks made from JAM‐A KO cells was also significantly lower than that of control cells (Figure 2F).

In contrast, overexpression of JAM‐A resulted in reciprocal changes: two MCF7 clones overexpressing N‐terminally 3× FLAG‐tagged JAM‐A (FLAG‐JAM‐A #1 and FLAG‐JAM‐A #2; Figure 3A) showed significantly increased proliferative capacity compared with that of control cells both in the WST‐8 assay (Figure 3B) and in immunocytochemistry of the proliferative marker Ki‐67 (Figure 3C). Of note, we found that JAM‐A overexpression significantly increased the number of transmigrating cells through the membrane of a Boyden chamber compared with that in MCF7 control cells, suggesting that JAM‐A overexpression also enhanced the migration capacity of BC cells (Figure 3D). These results indicated that JAM‐A expression status affects the malignant potentials of BC cells.

Comparative proteomic analysis revealed protein expression profile changes correlated with JAM‐A expression

To elucidate changes in protein expression profiles with changes in JAM‐A expression, we performed comparative proteomic analysis using mass spectrometry (Table S10). We focused on the proteins for which expression levels were correlated with the status of JAM‐A expression; therefore, we set two types of comparison, i.e., KO vs. control and JAM‐A‐restored KO vs. KO. We established two JAM‐A restoration clones from a JAM‐A KO clone by transfection of N‐terminally 3 × FLAG‐tagged JAM‐A (KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A #1 and KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A #2; Figure 3E). Before comparative proteomic analysis, we confirmed that restored JAM‐A was localized on the cell–cell border using immunofluorescence (Figure 3F) and that JAM‐A‐KO‐related morphological change (Figure S5) was rescued by restoration of JAM‐A expression. We also confirmed that JAM‐A‐KO‐related inhibition of cell proliferation (Figure 3G,H) and cell cycle arrest at G0/G1 (Figure S6) were rescued by restoration of JAM‐A expression.

To select the candidate JAM‐A‐correlated proteins from the MS‐based comparative proteomics, we searched proteins commonly identified in the group of ‘downregulated’ proteins, for which expression was decreased along with loss of JAM‐A expression (an abundance ratio of KO to control <0.67 and p < 0.05 in Figure 4A), and in the group of ‘recovered’ proteins, for which expression was increased with restoration of JAM‐A expression (an abundance ratio of KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A to KO >1.5 and p < 0.05 in Figure 4B,C). The number of downregulated proteins was 844 (Figure 4A). The numbers of recovered proteins were 809 in KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A #1 (Figure 4B) and 831 in KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A #2 (Figure 4C). Among the 844 downregulated proteins, 265 and 221 proteins belong to the recovered protein group for KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A #1 and #2, respectively (Figure 4D–F), and in total 316 proteins were finally identified as JAM‐A‐correlated proteins (Figure 4G). GO enrichment analysis and KEGG pathway analysis revealed that the JAM‐A‐correlated proteins were mainly enriched in GO terms involved in translation (Table S4). Additionally, resulting from RNA‐seq analysis (Doc S1), mRNAs correlated with JAM‐A expression were mainly enriched in the KEGG pathway term involving cell cycle, showing a tendency consistent with the phenotype of reversible proliferative capacity in MCF7 cells with altered JAM‐A expression (Tables S5 and S6).

Identification of JAM‐A‐interacting proteins by comparative proteomic and co‐immunoprecipitation mass spectrometry data

In addition to protein expression profile changes correlated with JAM‐A expression, we explored the proteins physically interacting with JAM‐A using the coIP‐MS assay (Table S10). Cell lysates were extracted from control and 3 × FLAG JAM‐A‐overexpressing MCF7 cells (FLAG‐JAM‐A #1 and FLAG‐JAM‐A #2), and IP was performed using an anti‐FLAG antibody. Co‐immunoprecipitants were analyzed using MS to comprehensively search for proteins physically interacting with JAM‐A. The numbers of proteins identified using coIP‐MS were 394 in MCF7 control, 343 in FLAG‐JAM‐A #1, and 426 in FLAG‐JAM‐A #2. To quantify PPIs, we calculated MiST scores from MS data as reported previously. The threshold of the MiST score was set at 0.75 following the developer's recommendation. The numbers of PPIs with a MiST score of 0.75 or more were 129 in JAM‐A‐overexpressing cells and 11 in control cells (Figure 5A). Proteins physically interacting with JAM‐A were mainly enriched in GO terms involved in a cellular metabolic or biosynthesis process in GO enrichment analysis and KEGG pathway analysis (Table S7). No PPI with a MiST score of 0.75 or more was commonly detected in MCF7 control cells and JAM‐A‐overexpressing MCF7 cells.

A Venn diagram was constructed using Venny 2.1 (https://bioinfogp.cnb.csic.es/tools/venny/index.html) to identify proteins that had altered expression correlated with JAM‐A expression and had high‐confidence PPI involved in JAM‐A (Figure 5B). Eleven proteins including JAM‐A fulfilled all of these criteria. All of these proteins except for JAM‐A are shown in Table 2. The abundance ratios and MiST scores of these ten proteins are shown in Figure 5C. Finally, we focused on RCN1, LAT1, and NPM3, for which changes in the expression level are strongly correlated with JAM‐A expression and for which there was a high‐confidence PPI with JAM‐A.

 JAM‐A altered the ability of amino acid uptake in breast cancer cells via changes in LAT1 expression

Next, expression levels of the three candidate proteins were confirmed using western blotting and immunofluorescence. Among the proteins, LAT1 expression was shown using western blotting to be clearly correlated with JAM‐A expression status, whereas neither RCN1 nor NPM3 expression seemed to be correlated with JAM‐A expression status (Figure 5D). As well as protein levels of LAT1, the mRNA level was also correlated with JAM‐A expression (Figure S7; Table S8). In IP‐western blotting, LAT1 was confirmed to be co‐immunoprecipitated with 3 × FLAG‐JAM‐A using an anti‐FLAG antibody (Figure 5E). In immunofluorescence, LAT1 was observed on the cell–cell border as was JAM‐A under a fluorescence microscope, whereas RCN1 and NPM3 were not colocalized with JAM‐A in MCF7 cells (Figure S8). The immunofluorescent signal of LAT1 was further examined using super‐resolution confocal laser microscopy, which showed that LAT1 was mainly colocalized with JAM‐A on a basolateral membrane including a TJ position in MCF7 cells (Figure 5F). These results indicate that LAT1 interacts with JAM‐A on the basolateral membrane in MCF7 cells.

LAT1, encoded by the SLC7A5 gene, is a transmembrane protein that usually requires 4F2hc to function as a transporter of neutral amino acids. We found that 4F2hc was also colocalized with JAM‐A on the plasma membrane in MCF7 cells (Figure S9) and co‐immunoprecipitated with 3xFLAG JAM‐A (Figure 5E). We next evaluated whether the amino acid uptake of MCF7 cells is affected by JAM‐A expression status because expression levels of LAT1 and 4F2hc were altered by JAM‐A expression status (Figure 5D). As expected, JAM‐A KO significantly reduced amino acid uptake, and restoration of JAM‐A expression significantly increased amino acid uptake (Figure 5G), indicating that amino acid uptake is affected by JAM‐A expression status. Of note, the knockdown of LAT1 by siRNA in MCF7 cells resulted in no obvious change in JAM‐A expression (Figure S10), suggesting that the upstream molecule was not LAT1 but JAM‐A.

 JAM‐A is a novel potential therapeutic target for breast cancer

Although LAT1 has been reported to be overexpressed in a variety of cancers, including BC, the correlation between LAT1 and JAM‐A expression has not been mentioned elsewhere. We conducted immunohistochemistry for LAT1 in surgical specimens and analyzed the correlation between LAT1 and JAM‐A. LAT1 expression was observed on the whole cell membrane in DCIS and IDC (Figure S11), whereas none of the DEs expressed LAT1. The JAM‐A high expression group in which IRS of JAM‐A is 28 or more had a significantly higher proportion of LAT1‐positive cases than that in the JAM‐A low expression group, indicating a positive correlation between JAM‐A expression and LAT1 expression in BC (p = 0.03; Figure 6A,B). Kaplan–Meier survival curve analysis showed that patients in the JAM‐A high‐expressing and LAT1‐positive group tended to have shorter recurrence‐free survival (Log‐rank p = 0.073) and significantly shorter overall survival (Log‐rank p = 0.031) than those in the other patient groups (Figure 6C). These results indicated that double high expression of JAM‐A and LAT1 was correlated with poor prognosis.

Both JAM‐A and LAT1 are attractive therapeutic targets of BC because extracellular domains of cell surface transmembrane proteins are easily accessible by antibodies, peptides, and small molecules. In fact, LAT1 inhibitors, such as nanvuranlat (JPH203), have been reported to have anti‐tumor effects in several types of cancer. Interestingly, we found that only exposure to an antibody against an extracellular domain of JAM‐A inhibited cultured cell growth of both MCF7 and T47D BC cells (Figure 6D,E).

DISCUSSION

In this study, detailed investigations using immunohistological and cell biological techniques revealed that JAM‐A expression in BC is involved in the malignant transformation of BC. Using a multi‐omics approach, we found for the first time that JAM‐A interacts with and regulates the expression of LAT1, which is known as an amino acid transporter predominantly expressed in proliferating cells such as cancer cells. In BC tissues and cells, both JAM‐A and LAT1 localize to the whole cell membrane, as transmembrane proteins with an extracellular domain, and both are suitable therapeutic targets as they are easily accessible by antibodies, peptides, and small molecules. 

Immunohistochemical studies using surgical specimens showed that JAM‐A was highly expressed from carcinoma in situ lesions, as in other adenocarcinomas, with higher expression in invasive carcinomas, and that high expression of JAM‐A contributed to malignant aspects such as lymph node metastasis and lymphatic involvement positivity (Table 1). Of note, we did not find significant differences in IRSs of JAM‐A among the five intrinsic subtypes, i.e., luminal A, luminal B, HER2‐enriched, basal‐like, and normal breast tissue‐like (data not shown), indicating that JAM‐A was highly expressed in neoplastic lesions in the presence or absence of HER2. In the HER2‐enriched subtype (a subtype found in ~10%–20% of BC), JAM‐A has been reported to regulate HER2 protein degradation and signaling, and the regulation is closely linked to HER2‐positive BC progression and resistance to HER2‐targeted therapies. However, our results suggested that JAM‐A plays some role other than HER2 regulation in BC progression, at least in HER2‐negative subtypes consisting of the majority of BC.

Using multi‐omics analysis of the luminal type BC cell line MCF7, we demonstrated a JAM‐A‐related PPI landscape for the first time and successfully identified LAT1 as a novel JAM‐A‐interacting protein that is regulated by and colocalizes with JAM‐A (Figure 5). Due to recent technical progress, we could obtain a large scale view of the PPI landscape from a single IP‐MS analysis. However, it was not easy to focus on reliable PPIs from the landscape. To overcome this issue, we utilized the affinity purification MS scoring system, MiST score, to identify reliable PPIs involving JAM‐A (Figure 5). The scoring system worked well in this study to identify JAM‐A itself in the list of reliable PPIs. A combination of several comparative proteomic approaches (control vs. JAM‐A‐KO and JAM‐A‐KO vs KO + FLAG‐JAM‐A) also helped us to narrow down the candidate molecules (Figure 5). This kind of multi‐proteomics approach might be a useful strategy for exploring therapeutic targets in a future study.

Cancer cells have an increased requirement for amino acids to maintain their vigorous proliferative capacity, and the inhibition of amino acid uptake by LAT1 inhibitors is a promising cancer therapy strategy. In this study, we found that there was a positive correlation between JAM‐A expression and LAT1 expression in surgical specimens of BC and that double high expression of JAM‐A and LAT1 was correlated with poor prognosis (Figure 6). Importantly, we found for the first time that JAM‐A is an upstream factor that colocalizes and regulates the expression of LAT1, which seems to form a heterodimer with 4F2hc (Figures 5D,E, S9, S10; Table S9), and that JAM‐A expression status affected amino acid uptake by BC cells (Figure 5). Notably, JAM‐A expression status had an impact on the expression of c‐Myc, which is known as a positive regulator of LAT1 (Figure S12). These results suggest that JAM‐A expression contributed to a feedforward activation loop consisting of c‐Myc and LAT1 to promote essential amino acid uptake as previously described. The mode of interaction between JAM‐A and LAT1 has not yet been fully elucidated. At least, PDZ‐mediated direct interaction between JAM‐A and LAT1/4F2hc can be excluded because neither LAT1 nor 4F2hc has a PDZ domain (https://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/). JAM‐A may interact with LAT1 via a scaffold protein such as LLGL2, which has been reported to stabilize LAT1 together with some TJPs, or may physically interact with LAT1 through its transmembrane domain and/or extracellular Ig‐like domain. 

Cancer progression caused by ectopic high expression of JAM‐A is a complex phenomenon resulting from interactions with various downstream factors. We think that JAM‐A contributed to malignant potentials, part of which is achieved by LAT1 function and the others being LAT1‐independent (Figure 6F). In fact, as we have reported for lung adenocarcinoma and uterine cervical adenocarcinoma, JAM‐A was involved in the progression of these cancers independent of LAT1, and an interaction of JAM‐A and LAT1 was not found. In the present study, however, JAM‐A expression status was simultaneously correlated with proliferative capacity (Figures 2B,C,E,F, 3G,H), LAT1 expression status (Figure 5D) and changes in LAT1‐associated amino acid uptake capacities (Figure 5G), and it is therefore difficult to precisely determine the respective contributions of JAM‐A and LAT1 to cell proliferation. Our preliminary experiments revealed that an inhibitory antibody against JAM‐A dimerization (clone J10.4) did not significantly affect the protein expression level of LAT1 or amino acid uptake ability (unpublished data), whereas it suppressed the proliferation of MCF7 cells. These results suggest that JAM‐A can contribute to cell proliferation partly independent of LAT1 function. Moreover, enhancement of β1‐integrin‐mediated cell migration may explain our results. McSherry et al. reported that overexpressed JAM‐A formed a downstream signaling complex with AF‐6 and PDZ‐GEF2 through the PDZ domains to activate Rap‐1 and upregulate β1‐integrin levels. At least, we confirmed by western blotting that β1‐integrin expression was increased in JAM‐A‐overexpressing cells compared with its expression in control cells (Figure S13). Taken together, these results suggested that aberrantly expressed JAM‐A contributed to malignant potentials of BC cells, partly due to upregulation of the LAT1‐axis.

In recent years, transmembrane‐type TJPs have emerged as promising therapeutic targets for various cancers. Their expression levels are increased and their localization is altered (from TJ‐restricted to whole cell membrane), making them suitable as therapeutic targets because their extracellular domains can be easily accessed by antibodies, affinity peptides, and small molecule inhibitors. Moreover, aberrantly expressed transmembrane‐type TJPs can be approached not only from the apical side but also from the extracellular matrix side, including blood vessels. Several clinical trials targeting transmembrane‐type TJPs as therapeutic targets have been progressing. In the present study, we demonstrated that JAM‐A is significantly overexpressed in BC cells (Figure 1) and that an antibody against an extracellular domain of JAM‐A inhibited the proliferation of BC cells (Figure 6). We think that JAM‐A‐targeted therapy might be effective in JAM‐A‐positive BCs as well as other transmembrane‐type TJPs.

In conclusion, JAM‐A is strongly expressed in BC and contributes to the malignant potentials of tumor cells. Comprehensive and multiple proteomic analyses revealed that JAM‐A regulated the expression of LAT1 and interacted with it, leading to enhanced amino acid uptake to promote tumor growth. Our findings provide a new insight into a therapeutic strategy, JAM‐A‐targeted therapy ideally combined with LAT1‐targeted therapy, against BC (Figure 6F).
