## Full Text

Photothermal therapy co-localized with CD137 agonism improves survival in an SM1 melanoma model without hepatotoxicity

Aim: We investigate combining Prussian Blue nanoparticles (PBNPs), as photothermal therapy (PTT) agents, with agonistic CD137 antibodies (αCD137) on a single nanoparticle platform to deliver non-toxic, anti-tumor efficacy in SM1 murine melanoma.

Methods: We electrostatically coated PBNPs with αCD137 (αCD137-PBNPs) and quantified their physicochemical characteristics, photothermal and co-stimulatory capabilities. Next, we tested the efficacy and hepatotoxicity of PTT using αCD137-PBNPs (αCD137-PBNP-PTT) in SM1 tumor-bearing mice.

Results: The αCD137-PBNPs retained both the photothermal and agonistic properties of the PBNPs and αCD137, respectively. In vivo, SM1 tumor-bearing mice treated with αCD137-PBNP-PTT exhibited a significantly higher survival rate (50%) without hepatotoxicity, compared with control treatments.

Conclusion: These data suggest the potential utility of co-localizing PBNP-PTT with αCD137-based agonism as a novel combination nanomedicine.

Plain Language Summary

Photothermal therapy is a strategy to kill cancer cells that uses nanoparticles and lasers to generate heat. Here, we combine photothermal therapy with an immunotherapy that activates the body's T cells, a type of white blood cell, on a single platform, to treat melanoma, a type of skin cancer in a mouse. We find that this novel nanoparticle-based platform significantly improves the survival of mice bearing melanoma, without increasing liver toxicity.

Article highlights

Agonistic antibodies targeting CD137 (αCD137) can be transiently coated onto Prussian blue nanoparticles (PBNPs; αCD137-PBNPs).

αCD137-PBNPs function as effective PTT agents and heat SM1 murine melanoma cells in a dose-dependent manner.

αCD137 retains agonistic function on αCD137-PBNPs as measured by sustained T cell activation in vitro.

Photothermal therapy using αCD137-PBNPs (αCD137-PBNP-PTT) initiates similar changes in immunogenic markers on SM1 cells as PBNP-PTT.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT effectively treats SM1 tumors in vivo by decreasing tumor growth and significantly improving mouse survival.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT does not increase hepatotoxicity in treated mice.

Introduction

Photothermal therapy (PTT) is a cancer treatment modality that utilizes near infrared (NIR) lasers to heat photothermal agents and ablate tumors. A beneficial effect of PTT is the rapid release of tumor-associated antigens and damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Our group uses Prussian blue nanoparticles (PBNPs) as photothermal agents in PBNP-based PTT (PBNP-PTT). We have established that PBNP-PTT is effective in improving survival in several murine models of solid tumors, including melanoma. In addition to inducing tumor cell cytotoxicity, we have observed that under certain conditions, PBNP-PTT also induces immunogenic cell death (ICD), a form of regulated cell death that can engage immune cells and generate immunological memory. We and others have illustrated the role of ICD in generating an abscopal effect in response to PTT, where a local administration of PTT can result in systemic anti-tumor immunity. While ICD and abscopal efficacy have been observed in response to PTT alone in some preclinical tumor models, effectively treating melanoma often requires a complementary therapeutic strategy, such as immunotherapy, to generate a robust response. In particular, immunotherapies and immune adjuvants such as immune checkpoint blockade, toll-like receptor agonists and the adoptive transfer of immune effector cells have been demonstrated as effective combination therapies with PBNP-PTT. However, it is unlikely that the aforementioned combination treatments using PBNP-PTT and immunotherapies could effectively treat the diverse patient population in need of therapy. This suggests a need for additional discovery and/or refining of combination treatment strategies using PBNP-PTT.

Motivated by this need and pertinent to melanoma, we have demonstrated that systemic administration of agonistic monoclonal antibodies against CD137 (αCD137), a co-stimulatory receptor on T cells, potentiates the abscopal efficacy of PBNP-PTT in a murine SM1 melanoma tumor model in vivo. Systemic (intraperitoneal; ip.) delivery of αCD137 combined with local (intratumoral; it.) PBNP-PTT (αCD137 + PBNP-PTT) generated a significant reduction of tumor burden and improved survival in single and metachronous SM1 tumor-bearing mice. Despite this promising efficacy, mice receiving αCD137 or αCD137 + PBNP-PTT both exhibited acute liver inflammation 24 h post-αCD137 administration. This hepatic inflammation in mice mirrors the clinical outcomes of dose-limiting hepatotoxicity observed in early clinical trials testing the use of αCD137, highlighting the need for a safe alternative formulation of αCD137 or a strategy to maintain efficacy while decreasing toxicity.

In this study, we investigate whether co-localizing the two therapeutic modalities on a single PBNP-based platform and delivered locally into the tumor microenvironment (TME) mitigates hepatotoxicity while maintaining anti-tumor efficacy of the combination. To test this, we electrostatically attached αCD137 onto PBNPs to form αCD137-conjugated PBNPs (αCD137-PBNPs). First, we assessed the physicochemical quality attributes of the αCD137-PBNPs as measured by size, charge, coating efficiency and stability. Next, we measured the photothermal and agonistic properties of αCD137-PBNPs and applied αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1 cells in vitro. Finally, we administered αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1-tumor bearing mice, in comparison to PBNP-PTT and αCD137 monotherapies and other controls and evaluated tumor growth, survival and hepatotoxicity. The overall study design and proposed mechanism of action is illustrated in Figure 1 . Overall, we aim to describe a novel and effective combined nanoimmunotherapy platform for the treatment of melanoma.

Materials & methods

Materials

Iron (III) chloride hexahydrate (MW 270.3; FeCl3·6H2O), potassium hexacyanoferrate (II) trihydrate (MW 422.39; K4[Fe(CN)6]·3H2O), acetone, sodium chloride and citric acid were purchased from MilliporeSigma (St. Louis, MO, USA). Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM), penicillin-streptomycin, hydroxyethylpiperazine-N-2-ethane sulfonic acid (HEPES), non-essential amino acids (NEAA) and β-mercaptoethanol (BME) were purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Roswell Park Memorial Institute media (RPMI) was purchased from Gibco (Carlsbad, CA, USA). Fetal bovine serum (FBS) was purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific and R&D Systems (Minneapolis, MN, USA). Live/dead Zombie Violet/Aqua dyes (catalog #423101), and fluorescent antibodies against PD-L1 (clone 10F.9G2; #124336), B7-H3 (clone MIH35; #135613), CD80 (clone 16-10A1; #104723), CD86 (clone GL-1; #105025), CD137-L (clone TKS-1; #104526), CD3 (clone 17A2; #100241), CD4 (clone GK1.5; #100443), CD8 (clone 53-6.7; #100734), CD25 (clone PC61; #102020) and CD69 (clone H1.2F3; #104526) were purchased from BioLegend (San Diego, CA, USA). Fluorescent antibodies against MHC-1 (clone IVA26; #MA5-44137) were purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific. Fluorescent antibodies against calreticulin (clone FMC75; #ab196159) were purchased from Abcam (Cambridge, UK). Agonist CD137 antibodies (αCD137; clone 3H3; #BE0239) were purchased from BioXCell (Lebanon, NH, USA).

Synthesis of PBNPs

PBNPs were synthesized using a scheme as described previously in our previous work. Briefly, 5 mM citric acid was added to individual aqueous solutions of FeCl3·6H2O (10 mM; 20 ml) and K4[Fe(CN)6]·3H2O (10 mM; 20 ml), stirring at 400–600 RPM at 60°C. Once dissolved, the K4[Fe(CN)6] solution was added to the FeCl3 solution under stirring at 400–600 RPM. After stirring for 10 min at 60°C, the solution was left to cool to room temperature (RT) with maintained stirring. The resulting PBNPs were isolated from intermediate agents by centrifugation in equal volumes of acetone and water (10,000 × g for 15 min at RT), whereupon the unreacted agents were discarded, and the PBNPs were then rinsed and resuspended by microtip sonication (40% amplitude for 30 s) in Milli-Q water using a Q500 sonicator (QSonica LLC, Newton, CT) with a standard microtip probe. The isolation and rinse steps were repeated three-times before the PBNPs were finally resuspended in Milli-Q water with sonication at the desired concentration. PBNPs were analyzed for concentration by oven-drying the particles at 80°C and measuring their weight. A standard curve was generated for PBNP concentration versus absorbance at 680 nm using SpectraMax microplate reader (Molecular Devices, San Jose, CA). The size distributions and surface charge of the PBNPs were characterized by dynamic light scattering on a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK).

Synthesis of αCD137-PBNPs

αCD137-PBNPs were synthesized by adding αCD137 to PBNPs at a ratio of 2 mg PBNPs to 1 mg αCD137 in water. This mass ratio of 2:1 PBNP: αCD137 was selected from a PBNP coating optimization study described in Supplementary Figure S1. The solution was contacted on ice for 30 min on a microplate shaker set to 400 RPM (VWR, PA, USA) and then isolated from intermediate agents by centrifugation (15,000 RPM for 30 min at RT), whereupon the unreacted agents were separated and saved for analysis of αCD137 concentration by BCA assay (ThermoFisher Scientific), and the αCD137-PBNPs were resuspended in Milli-Q water by microtip sonication (30% amplitude for 30 s). Once the 100% αCD137 retention was established for the 2:1 ratio (Supplementary Figure S1), the solution of αCD137 and PBNPs were not centrifuged after their incubation; rather, the αCD137-PBNPs were used immediately after contacting for 30 min on ice. We eliminated the washing step as we did not find unreacted intermediate agents in the supernatant. αCD137-PBNPs were evaluated for size distribution and surface charge by dynamic light scattering on a Zetasizer Nano ZS. The absorbance spectra of PBNPs, αCD137 and αCD137-PBNPs were measured by UV-Vis-NIR spectrophotometry on the Genesys 10S UV-Vis machine (ThermoFisher Scientific).

Cell culture

Murine SM1 melanoma cells (BRAFV600E mutation) were provided by Dr Alejandro Villagra, as described in our previous publication. SM1 cells were cultured in RPMI media containing 1% L-glutamine (pre-supplemented), 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (termed cRPMI). Murine T cells, isolated from the spleens of C57BL/6 mice (detailed in the Animals section below), were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, 1% HEPES, 1% NEAA and 0.1 M BME (termed cDMEM).

Photothermal property validation of αCD137-PBNPs

To test the photothermal properties of αCD137-PBNPs as compared with uncoated PBNPs, PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs were suspended in Milli-Q water at a concentration of 0.15 mg/ml PBNP for both samples. A near infrared (NIR) laser (808 nm; Laserglow Technologies, Toronto, Canada) was shone onto the nanoparticle suspensions at laser powers of 0.5 W, 1 W, 1.5 W, or 2 W. Laser output power was confirmed using a power meter (PM100D, Thorlabs, NJ, USA). The spot size of the beam was 5 mm by 8 mm (40 mm2). The maximum temperatures of the suspensions were measured and recorded every minute for 10 min using a thermal camera (FLIR E5-XT, Arlington, VA, USA). Vehicle (water; no laser administration) and Laser (2 W laser; no nanoparticles) samples were used as controls. The thermal doses administered were calculated and represented as the logarithm of the cumulative equivalent min at 43°C (log(CEM43)), as previously described. Briefly, the formula ∑CEM43°C = Δt × R(43-T) was used, where Δt is the time interval, T is the average temperature during that time interval, and R is a constant with a value of 0.25 for T <43°C and 0.5 for T >43°C. For cyclic heating, the suspensions were exposed to 2.0 W NIR laser power for 10 min, then allowed to cool with the laser turned off for 10 min. This process was repeated twice for a total of three cycles of heating. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Agonistic function validation of αCD137-PBNPs

To evaluate the retained function of αCD137 once conjugated onto PBNPs, T cells were isolated from spleens from 5-week-old female C57BL/6 mice using the EasySep Mouse T Cell Isolation Kit (StemCell Technologies, Vancouver, Canada). Spleens from six mice were used to generate biological replicates (n = 6) for this experiment. Splenic T cells were stained with CFSE (ThermoFisher Scientific) and plated at 1e6 cells per well in 48-well non-tissue culture treated plates pre-coated with soluble αCD3 (ThermoFisher Scientific). T cells were then treated with either cDMEM alone, or αCD137, PBNPs, αCD137-PBNPS, or αCD137-PBNPs post-PTT (1 W) in cDMEM. After 3 days, the T cells were collected from each well and stained for CD3, CD4, CD8, CD25 and CD69 for flow cytometric analysis. Flow cytometry was performed by acquiring 100,000 events per sample using the BD Biosciences Celesta Cell Analyzer (Franklin Lakes, NJ), and analyzed with FlowJo software (Ashland, OR).

PTT on SM1 melanoma cells in vitro & subsequent in vitro analyses

To administer PTT to SM1 cells in vitro, 5 million SM1 cells were resuspended in 500 μl cRPMI containing vehicle (water), 0.15 mg/ml PBNPs, or 0.15 mg/ml αCD137-PBNPs (based on the PBNP concentration). PTT was applied to the cell suspensions as described above at laser powers spanning 0.5–2 W. Treated cells were then plated in 6-well plates and incubated at 37°C for 24 h. Cells were then harvested and aliquoted for assessing intracellular ATP or flow cytometry. For flow cytometry, cells were stained with Zombie Aqua Fixable viability dye, and subsequently with fluorescent antibodies against calreticulin, PD-L1, B7-H3, CD80, MHC-1, CD86, or CD137-L. Flow cytometry was performed using the BD Biosciences Celesta Cell Analyzer and analyzed with FlowJo software. Intracellular ATP was measured using the CellTiter-Glo assay (Promega, WI, USA), according to the manufacturer's specifications. Luminescence was measured using a SpectraMax i3x microplate reader (Molecular Devices, CA, USA). These in vitro studies were repeated in five independent experiments; averages and standard deviations are presented in the data. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Animals

All animal studies were conducted in accordance with protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the George Washington University (protocol #A2021-001) and in accordance with the humane care of research animals. For tumor growth and survival studies, 5- to 6-week-old female C57BL/6 mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratory (ME, USA) and acclimated for a week (n = 62 total mice used in the studies). To establish tumors, 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS were subcutaneously (sc.) injected into the shaved backs of C57BL/6 mice. Tumors were treated once volumes reached a size of 5 mm (44 to 60 mm3), and monitored frequently for tumor measurements. For tumor rechallenge studies, age-matched C57BL/6 mice were acquired from Jackson Laboratory. All animal studies were performed in a non-blinded fashion, and mice were randomized into the treatment groups. Mice were humanely euthanized by CO2 inhalation and follow-up cervical dislocation when tumor diameter reached 15 mm, or if the tumor ulcerated or the mouse exhibited signs of pain or distress, in accordance with the IACUC-approved protocol.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT in vivo

To test the efficacy of the novel nanoparticle platform in vivo, C57BL/6 mice were inoculated sc. with 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS on the right flank. Mice were divided into 6 groups: untreated (receiving no treatment), αCD137-PBNP-PTT (receiving one intratumoral (it.) injection of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137-PBNPs (comprising 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs at the established 2:1 ratio of PBNP:αCD137) for PTT and booster doses of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137-PBNPs it. on days 3 and 6), PBNP-PTT (receiving one it. injection of 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs for PTT), αCD137-PBNP (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137-PBNPs injected it. on days 0, 3 and 6), αCD137 it. (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137 injected it. on days 0, 3 and 6) and αCD137 ip. (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137 injected interperitoneally (ip.) on days 0, 3 and 6). The dose of αCD137 was kept consistent in all treatments containing αCD137. Mice were anesthetized prior to and during treatment using 2–5% isoflurane. Mice treated with PTT received PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs it. and 10 min of laser exposure. Temperatures were measured using the FLIR camera at one-minute intervals with the recorded temperature being the hottest area of each tumor. Laser power was adjusted over the treatment period to maintain a surface temperature range of 80–90°C at the hottest area. After treatment, tumor size was measured every other day and the health of the mice was monitored. Three independent in vivo experiments were performed, and data was pooled together. When needed for the study, blood was collected for serum analysis using submandibular blood draw technique on days 1, 15 and at tumor volume end point. Surviving mice were rechallenged at Day 127 post-treatment with a sc. injection of 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS in the opposite (left) flank and monitored for tumor growth. Age-matched female C57BL/6 mice that had not previously been injected with SM1 cells served as “treatment naive” controls for the rechallenge studies.

Liver enzyme analysis

Blood was collected at days 1, 15 and end point (based on tumor volume) by submandibular blood drawing into EDTA-free Eppendorf tubes (VWR). Blood was allowed to clot at RT for 30 min. Sera from these samples were then collected by centrifugation at 1500 xg for 15 min at 4°C. The collected sera were stored at -80°C until analysis. Alanine aminotransferase (ALT) activity was evaluated using the colorimetric ALT activity assay kit (Abcam). The protocol from the manufacturer was followed using a 1:10 dilution of the sera. Absorbance at 570 nm was measured by SpectraMax i3X microplate reader. The aspartate aminotransferase (AST) activity was evaluated using the AST activity assay kit (MilliporeSigma), using the manufacturer's protocol with a 1:10 dilution of the sera. Absorbance at 450 nm was measured using the SpectraMax i3X microplate reader. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Statistics

Results obtained in this study are expressed as mean ± standard deviation. All experimental groups were tested for normal distribution using D'Agostino and Pearson test, Anderson-Darling test, Shapiro-Wilk test and Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. Statistical significance between peak intensity, peak volume, zeta potential and polydispersity index of uncoated and coated PBNPs was determined using the Wilcoxon Sign-Rank test and Friedman's Test with Dunn's post-hoc multiple comparisons test. Significance between cell surface marker expression by flow cytometry (percent and MFI fold change) was determined using Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn's multiple comparisons post-hoc test and Mann-Whitney test to account for the 20% chance of Type II and Type 1 error, respectively, for each marker. Serum liver enzyme activity was analyzed using Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn's multiple comparisons post-hoc test and the mixed effects model. Survival results were analyzed according to Kaplan-Meier curves and log-rank tests. Statistical significance throughout the study is indicated as n.s.: non-significant, */#p < 0.05; **/##p < 0.01; ***/###p < 0.001 and ****/####p < 0.0001. Statistical tests were performed using the Prism GraphPad10.0.1 software.

Results

αCD137 transiently coats PBNPs as measured by size, charge, & polydispersity index

In order to co-localize the dual effects of PBNP-PTT and αCD137 immunotherapy in the melanoma TME, we first sought to synthesize αCD137-coated PBNPs as a single nanoparticle platform. Using an electrostatic coating strategy, we contacted PBNPs and αCD137 at various mass ratios of PBNP:αCD137 (i.e. 0.5:1, 1:1, 2:1, 5:1, 10:1; n = 3 each). Because we employed an electrostatic binding strategy, we hypothesized that αCD137 would attach to PBNPs in a randomized orientation, consistent with similar approaches described in literature precedent. We monitored the properties of the resulting αCD137-PBNPs over a four-day period. We observed that regardless of the synthesis ratio, αCD137-PBNPs were susceptible to aggregation and sedimentation 3 days after synthesis (Supplementary Figure S2), and thus we did not store the nanoparticles for extended periods of time. We observed that αCD137-PBNPs synthesized at a ratio of 2:1 yielded the most favorable particle properties in terms of consistency of size distribution, polydispersity index (PDI), zeta potential, coating efficiency of αCD137 and retention of PBNPs after synthesis while maximizing the amount of αCD137 coated and retained on the PBNPs (Supplementary Figure S1). In particular, we found that synthesizing αCD137-PBNPs using a 2:1 mass ratio yielded a coating efficiency of 96.2% with negligible (0.5%) loss of PBNPs. Thus, we proceeded to use αCD137-PBNPs synthesized at a ratio of 2:1 for all subsequent studies. We then compared the size and electrochemical properties of uncoated PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs. The hydrodynamic diameter of αCD137-PBNPs appeared significantly larger than PBNPs (Figure 2A) with corresponding peak intensities of 162.7 and 86.7 nm, respectively (Figure 2B). There was an increase in PDI from 0.25 (PBNPs) to 0.41 (αCD137-PBNPs), which reflects the widening in the distribution of nanoparticle size caused by the attachment of αCD137 (Figure 2C). As another validation of αCD137 coating onto PBNPs, we measured the zeta potential of both nanoparticles, predicting that coating αCD137 on PBNPs would increase the zeta potential if it were electrostatically driven. Indeed, we found significantly different potentials of -30.91 mV for PBNPs and -23.54 mV for αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 2D), suggesting the attachment of αCD137 to the surface of PBNPs in the αCD137-PBNPs via electrostatic interactions. To measure stability of the αCD137-PBNPs, we assessed how αCD137-PBNPs size, zeta potential and PDI changed over 4 days post-synthesis (Figure 2E–G). The changes in the measured properties between days were not statistically significant. These studies illustrate that αCD137-PBNPs can be formulated to co-localize both αCD137 and PBNPs. While the αCD137-PBNPs are transiently stable, the facile coating scheme seeks to leverage the therapeutic benefits of both component agents.

αCD137-PBNPs function as effective PTT agents

To assess the effect of αCD137 on the photothermal heating properties of the resultant αCD137-PBNPs, we conducted photothermal heating studies in vitro as a function of laser power and cyclic heating. Since PBNPs function as PTT agents by absorbing NIR light, we first assessed the UV-Vis-NIR absorbance spectra up to 1000 nm, spanning the well-established extinction peak wavelengths of the constituent components of αCD137-PBNPs as compared with PBNPs. αCD137-PBNPs yielded identical absorbance curves as PBNPs indicating that αCD137 electrostatic coating has no effect on the light absorbance of PBNPs (Figure 3A). PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (n = 5) were then illuminated with NIR light for ten min at varied laser power (0 W–2.0 W) and temperatures were measured. Heating was laser power-dependent for both PBNPs (Figure 3B) and αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 3C), suggesting the sustained photothermal functionality of the αCD137-PBNPs. Likewise, thermal doses achieved with the varied heating schemes were calculated as a logarithmic function of the cumulative equivalent min at 43°C (CEM43). The thermal dose represents a parameter that converts temperature versus time heating curves into thermal dose based on seminal studies by Sapareto and Dewey, and extensively studied in the field of thermal medicine, including us. At each laser power tested, PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs achieved statistically equivalent thermal doses (7.0, 12.4–12.5, 14.1–14.4, 15.3 log (CEM43) for 0.5 W, 1.0 W, 1.5 W and 2.0 W, respectively) (Figure 3D), further suggesting that αCD137-PBNPs did not lose their capacity as agents of PTT compared with PBNPs. To assess the stability of αCD137-PBNPs as PTT agents, we conducted cyclic heating studies (n = 4) as described in the Methods. αCD137-PBNPs showed differential photothermal heating with PBNPs with each subsequent cycle of PTT (Figure 3E). Although PBNPs maintained equivalent temperature curves with each cycle of heating, αCD137-PBNPs appeared to lose heating capacity by cycle 3, when PBNP-PTT achieved a thermal dose of 16.0 log (CEM43) while αCD137-PBNP-PTT achieved 11.8 log (CEM43) (Figure 3F). However, since we propose to use αCD137-PBNP for a single administration of PTT, consistent with their widely documented use in the literature, the benefit of adding αCD137 on the particles, tested subsequently, is expected to outweigh the loss in PTT performance of the particles.

αCD137 retains agonistic function on αCD137-PBNPs in vitro

In addition to αCD137-PBNPs maintaining photothermal heating capability, we tested whether αCD137 attached on to PBNPs retained their co-stimulatory action on T cells equivalent to free αCD137. Therefore, we isolated murine T cells from mouse spleens using magnetic bead isolation, and cultured them for 72 h prior to treatment with αCD137, PBNPs, αCD137-PBNPs, or αCD137-PBNPs after administering PTT for ten min at 1.0 W. αCD137 dosing was kept consistent throughout the treatment groups. The T cells were also stimulated with soluble αCD3 for 72 h in vitro prior to addition of the listed treatments. To evaluate T cell activation, we measured the expression of CD69 and CD25 on splenic T cells. Although αCD137, αCD137-PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs post-PTT did not induce CD69 expression alone (Figure 4A), T cells receiving these treatments significantly increased the co-expression of CD69 and CD25 (34.9, 30.8 and 25.4%, respectively) (Figure 4B) and the expression of CD25 alone (62.0, 65.0, 77.0%, respectively) (Figure 4C), as compared with unstimulated T cells. The population of CD69-/CD25- T cells appeared negligible for the treatments containing αCD137 (Figure 4D). Importantly, αCD137 and αCD137-PBNPs (pre- or post-PTT) generated equivalent activation of T cells as measured by CD69 and CD25 expression (Supplementary Figure S3), suggesting the retention of the agonistic function of αCD137 after synthesis with PBNPs as well as after αCD137-PBNP-PTT had been administered. Importantly, subjecting αCD137-PBNP to PTT did not diminish the T cell agonistic function of the bound αCD137, a criterion for the potential success of the component therapeutic agents in their envisioned co-localized use. Because these assays were performed in the absence of SM1 melanoma cells, these data represent the agonist functionality of the αCD137-PBNPs and not necessarily the immunological effect of the treatment scheme.

In vitro αCD137-PBNP-PTT initiates similar changes in immunogenic markers on SM1 cells as PBNP-PTT

Having established that αCD137-PBNPs sustained the functional properties of both αCD137 and PBNPs, we next administered either PBNP-PTT or αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1 cells in vitro, and measured several phenotypic markers post-PTT. A representative flow cytometric gating strategy is illustrated in Supplementary Figure S4. For this analysis, we included both the Kruskal-Wallis and Mann-Whitney tests for statistical significance to account for the Type II and Type I errors, respectively. PTT using 1.0 W laser power generated a significant reduction in SM1 cell viability using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (16.4 ± 18.9% and 0.6 ± 0.5%, respectively) (Figure 5A). Intracellular ATP was then evaluated as an indication of released ATP post-PTT, an immunogenic marker and a marker associated with ICD. Due to the differences in luminescence values between independent replicates of this experiment, we used the untreated luminescence values as a normalization metric. We found a significant decrease of intracellular ATP in a dose-dependent manner for both PBNP- or αCD137-PBNP-based PTT (Figure 5B). Calreticulin, another marker of ICD, appeared significantly increased on the surface of SM1 cells after treatment with 1.0 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5C), suggesting the initiation of ICD by both treatments. We observed significant increases in the expression of PD-L1 on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-PTT at 1.0 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5D). B7-H3 significantly increased on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-αCD137-PBNP-PTT at 1.0 W and post-PBNP-PTT at both 0.5 and 1.0 W (Figure 5E). CD80 increased after treatment with PBNPs, or αCD137-PBNP-PTT at 1.0 W or PBNP-PTT at 0.5 W (Figure 5F). MHC-1 significantly increased on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-PTT at 0.5 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5G). CD86, however, only appeared significantly increased on the surface of SM1 cells post 1.0 W PBNP-PTT (Figure 5H). The ligand for CD137 (CD137-L) was unchanged on the surface of surviving SM1 cells after any treatment (Figure 5I). These observations suggest that PTT using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs elicits similar changes in several markers associated with immunogenicity on the surface of surviving SM1 cells, which may induce a change in immunogenicity in the TME post PTT.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT effectively treats SM1 tumors in vivo

To evaluate the efficacy of αCD137-PBNP-PTT on SM1 melanoma in vivo, we compared the effects of the following treatments on SM1 tumor-bearing mice (all mice were female): three ip. injections of αCD137 at 1.25 mg/kg (αCD137 ip.), three it. injections of αCD137 at 1.25 mg/kg (αCD137 it.), three it. injections of αCD137-PBNPs (comprising 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs; αCD137-PBNP it.), PBNP-PTT (2.5 mg/kg PBNPs) and αCD137-PBNP-PTT with two it. booster doses of αCD137-PBNPs comprising of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs (αCD137-PBNP-PTT) (Figure 6A). Mice treated with PTT received statistically equivalent thermal doses of 13.7 ± 0.52 (PBNP-PTT) or 14.2 ± 0.95 (αCD137-PBNP PTT) log(CEM43) (Supplementary Figure S5). We tracked tumor volume progression for each group (Figure 6B–F), and quantified survival based on tumor burden end point (any dimension measuring ≥15 mm). Mice receiving αCD137-PBNP-PTT survived significantly longer than all other groups by Day 50 (50% survival), compared with mice receiving no treatment (0%), PBNP-PTT (10%), αCD137 it. (20%), αCD137 ip. (20%) and αCD137-PBNP i.t (20%) (Figure 6G). To evaluate whether immunological memory had been established after treatment in the tumor-free mice, the long-term surviving mice were rechallenged 127 days after initial treatment. All rechallenged mice rejected the SM1 tumor (n = 1 from PBNP-PTT, n = 2 from αCD137 it., n = 1 from αCD137 ip., n = 1 from αCD137-PBNP it. and n = 5 from αCD137-PBNP-PTT), while all age-matched naive mice (n = 3) were euthanized due to tumor burden by Day 40 post-rechallenge, suggesting that immunological memory was generated in all previously treated groups (Figure 6H). These findings demonstrate the efficacy of the αCD137-PBNP-PTT platform in eliciting significantly higher complete tumor regression, long-term survival and protection against tumor rechallenge in the SM1 melanoma model compared with all other treatments.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT does not increase hepatotoxicity in treated mice

To investigate whether αCD137-PBNP-PTT generated hepatoxicity similar to that observed when using αCD137 alone, we collected sera from all mice on days 1 and 15, as well as at the timepoint when animals were euthanized. We measured the activity of the liver enzymes aspartate aminotransferase (AST) (Figure 7A) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) (Figure 7B) in the sera as measures for hepatotoxicity. Although we observed a significant increase in ALT activity in mice treated with αCD137-PBNP-PTT compared with naive (tumor-free) mice at their terminal end point, there was no increased AST or ALT activity in any of the treated mice compared with Untreated SM1 tumor-bearing mice. Thus, any increased AST or ALT activity measured in the mice over time was likely due to the presence or growth of the SM1 tumor (present in all groups at end point except naive) and not due to hepatotoxicity generated by αCD137 or αCD137-PBNPs. These data suggest that at the doses of αCD137 used for αCD137-PBNP-PTT, which is significantly lower than the doses of αCD137 that generated hepatotoxicity clinically, αCD137-PBNPs are potentially safe for use as agents of PTT for melanoma.

Discussion

The studies presented herein illustrate the efficacy of αCD137-PBNP-PTT, a combination of PBNP-based PTT with agonistic αCD137 antibodies as a novel single platform nanoimmunotherapy for treating melanoma. PBNP-PTT has been explored by us and others in the literature, and has demonstrated tumor cell cytotoxicity, immunogenicity and a mild abscopal effect. Although PBNP-PTT increases T cell infiltration into treated tumors, it often requires combination with other agents (e.g. immune checkpoint inhibitors, toll-like receptor agonists, chemotherapeutics) to exert maximum anti-tumor efficacy. Our previously published studies have demonstrated the combination of PBNP-PTT with systemic αCD137 monoclonal antibody administration for the treatment of SM1 melanoma. Despite the robust anti-tumor efficacy we observed in this prior study, we also observed acute hepatotoxicity following the treatment with either αCD137 antibodies or the combination of PBNP-PTT with αCD137, perhaps due to the relatively high concentration of antibody required for efficacy, and/or the systemic (ip.) route of administration. This hepatotoxicity reflects what is also observed in humans, where agonistic αCD137 antibodies investigated in Phases I and II clinical trials have generated hepatotoxicity in treated patients. Since the failure of the first generation of clinical αCD137 antibodies, many groups have developed alternative αCD137 agonists to address liver toxicity. Indeed, there are several ongoing early phase clinical trials testing these second generation CD137 agonists, many in combination with other drugs or in bi- or tri-specific formulations, although many have not yet been published. To our knowledge, we are the first to test the combination of PTT with αCD137 for treating melanoma. Here we sought to test whether synthesizing a novel nanoparticle platform comprising both PBNPs and αCD137 antibodies (αCD137-PBNPs) to be administered locally in the TME could effectively treat melanoma while mitigating hepatotoxicity.

First, we investigated whether combining the two therapies in a single nanoparticle would impact the functionality of either component. Our studies illustrated that αCD137-PBNPs could be formulated to transiently co-localize both αCD137 and PBNPs as measured by time-dependent dynamic light scattering and zetametry (Figure 2). Although prior studies, including our own have described electron microscopy-based morphological analyses of PBNP-based nanoformulations, a limitation of the present study is a lack of such EM-based morphological data on αCD137-PBNPs, which is the focus of ongoing work in our group. We found that αCD137-PBNPs maintained equivalent heating capacity as PBNPs, although their cyclic heating capability was diminished after three rounds of PTT (Figure 3). This is a finding relevant to the stability of the nanoparticles, but should not impact in vivo efficacy since only one round of PTT is used for treatment. Importantly, we also observed that αCD137-PBNPs activated T cells, as measured by CD69 and CD25 expression, in an equivalent manner to free αCD137 antibodies (Figure 4). These findings allowed us to use the αCD137-PBNPs to test whether the it. administration of the combined platform facilitated anti-tumor efficacy similarly to the dual administration of ip. αCD137 and it. PBNP-PTT without toxicity.

Indeed, αCD137-PBNP-PTT was effective in generating tumor regression and a significant improvement in survival of mice (Figure 6). These data suggest that combining the two therapeutic approaches into a single platform does not diminish the functionality of either component; instead, they work in a complementary manner. Importantly, mice receiving PTT using αCD137-PBNPs did not exhibit higher liver enzyme activity as compared with untreated mice, suggesting no acute of chronic hepatoxicity was initiated (Figure 7). These findings suggest the potential safety of the therapy, and the mitigation of toxicity that was observed when systemic administration of αCD137 was used. It is important to note that the effective dose administered in this study (1.25 mg/kg) was 12-fold lower than the dose used in our previous publication (15 mg/kg) that first illustrated the efficacy of PBNP-PTT combined with αCD137. We propose that immobilizing the αCD137 on PBNPs and administering it it. allows for a lower dose to effectively treat SM1 tumors, while lowering liver toxicity.

Overall, we propose the following mechanism of action of the novel αCD137-PBNP-PTT (Figure 1C). Locally administered αCD137-PBNP-PTT generates heating of melanoma tumor cells, tumor cell death and an immunogenic phenotype in the TME (Figure 5). Local antigen-presenting cells in the TME may engage with treated melanoma cells and take up and present tumor antigens. Because of the immunogenic impact of PTT, T cells may infiltrate the TME and activate in response to tumor antigens. Concurrently, αCD137 on the αCD137-PBNPs is transiently released into the TME and may co-stimulate nearby T cells to promote activation and expansion of these tumor-specific T cells. These effects work together to effectively diminish tumor burden and generate immunological memory (Figure 6).

Conclusion

The data presented in this investigation illustrate a novel nanoimmunotherapy comprising PBNP-based PTT with agonistic αCD137 antibodies on a single nanoparticle platform. This therapeutic strategy combines the complementary actions of the two components, generating tumor cell death and immunogenicity and subsequent activation of infiltrating T cells, resulting in a robust anti-tumor immune effect. Importantly, it appears that co-localizing the agonistic co-stimulatory function of αCD137 within the TME on the PBNP platform lowers the effective dose and mitigates the hepatotoxicity observed upon systemic administration of αCD137 in prior studies. Consequently, the results described in this paper may facilitate future studies investigating the effect of αCD137-PBNP-based PTT on abscopal efficacy in models of synchronous, metachronous and metastatic melanoma models in vivo, with the goal of translating the novel and safe therapeutic approach of αCD137-PBNP-PTT to patients with melanoma.

Photothermal therapy co-localized with CD137 agonism improves survival in an SM1 melanoma model without hepatotoxicity

Aim: We investigate combining Prussian Blue nanoparticles (PBNPs), as photothermal therapy (PTT) agents, with agonistic CD137 antibodies (αCD137) on a single nanoparticle platform to deliver non-toxic, anti-tumor efficacy in SM1 murine melanoma.

Methods: We electrostatically coated PBNPs with αCD137 (αCD137-PBNPs) and quantified their physicochemical characteristics, photothermal and co-stimulatory capabilities. Next, we tested the efficacy and hepatotoxicity of PTT using αCD137-PBNPs (αCD137-PBNP-PTT) in SM1 tumor-bearing mice.

Conclusion: These data suggest the potential utility of co-localizing PBNP-PTT with αCD137-based agonism as a novel combination nanomedicine.

Introduction

Photothermal therapy (PTT) is a cancer treatment modality that utilizes near infrared (NIR) lasers to heat photothermal agents and ablate tumors. A beneficial effect of PTT is the rapid release of tumor-associated antigens and damage associated molecular patterns (DAMPs). Our group uses Prussian blue nanoparticles (PBNPs) as photothermal agents in PBNP-based PTT (PBNP-PTT). We have established that PBNP-PTT is effective in improving survival in several murine models of solid tumors, including melanoma. In addition to inducing tumor cell cytotoxicity, we have observed that under certain conditions, PBNP-PTT also induces immunogenic cell death (ICD), a form of regulated cell death that can engage immune cells and generate immunological memory. We and others have illustrated the role of ICD in generating an abscopal effect in response to PTT, where a local administration of PTT can result in systemic anti-tumor immunity. While ICD and abscopal efficacy have been observed in response to PTT alone in some preclinical tumor models, effectively treating melanoma often requires a complementary therapeutic strategy, such as immunotherapy, to generate a robust response. In particular, immunotherapies and immune adjuvants such as immune checkpoint blockade, toll-like receptor agonists and the adoptive transfer of immune effector cells have been demonstrated as effective combination therapies with PBNP-PTT. However, it is unlikely that the aforementioned combination treatments using PBNP-PTT and immunotherapies could effectively treat the diverse patient population in need of therapy. This suggests a need for additional discovery and/or refining of combination treatment strategies using PBNP-PTT.

Motivated by this need and pertinent to melanoma, we have demonstrated that systemic administration of agonistic monoclonal antibodies against CD137 (αCD137), a co-stimulatory receptor on T cells, potentiates the abscopal efficacy of PBNP-PTT in a murine SM1 melanoma tumor model in vivo. Systemic (intraperitoneal; ip.) delivery of αCD137 combined with local (intratumoral; it.) PBNP-PTT (αCD137 + PBNP-PTT) generated a significant reduction of tumor burden and improved survival in single and metachronous SM1 tumor-bearing mice. Despite this promising efficacy, mice receiving αCD137 or αCD137 + PBNP-PTT both exhibited acute liver inflammation 24 h post-αCD137 administration. This hepatic inflammation in mice mirrors the clinical outcomes of dose-limiting hepatotoxicity observed in early clinical trials testing the use of αCD137, highlighting the need for a safe alternative formulation of αCD137 or a strategy to maintain efficacy while decreasing toxicity.

In this study, we investigate whether co-localizing the two therapeutic modalities on a single PBNP-based platform and delivered locally into the tumor microenvironment (TME) mitigates hepatotoxicity while maintaining anti-tumor efficacy of the combination. To test this, we electrostatically attached αCD137 onto PBNPs to form αCD137-conjugated PBNPs (αCD137-PBNPs). First, we assessed the physicochemical quality attributes of the αCD137-PBNPs as measured by size, charge, coating efficiency and stability. Next, we measured the photothermal and agonistic properties of αCD137-PBNPs and applied αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1 cells in vitro. Finally, we administered αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1-tumor bearing mice, in comparison to PBNP-PTT and αCD137 monotherapies and other controls and evaluated tumor growth, survival and hepatotoxicity. The overall study design and proposed mechanism of action is illustrated in Figure 1 . Overall, we aim to describe a novel and effective combined nanoimmunotherapy platform for the treatment of melanoma.

Materials & methods

Materials

Iron (III) chloride hexahydrate (MW 270.3; FeCl3·6H2O), potassium hexacyanoferrate (II) trihydrate (MW 422.39; K4[Fe(CN)6]·3H2O), acetone, sodium chloride and citric acid were purchased from MilliporeSigma (St. Louis, MO, USA). Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS), Dulbecco's Modified Eagle's Medium (DMEM), penicillin-streptomycin, hydroxyethylpiperazine-N-2-ethane sulfonic acid (HEPES), non-essential amino acids (NEAA) and β-mercaptoethanol (BME) were purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific (Waltham, MA, USA). Roswell Park Memorial Institute media (RPMI) was purchased from Gibco (Carlsbad, CA, USA). Fetal bovine serum (FBS) was purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific and R&D Systems (Minneapolis, MN, USA). Live/dead Zombie Violet/Aqua dyes (catalog #423101), and fluorescent antibodies against PD-L1 (clone 10F.9G2; #124336), B7-H3 (clone MIH35; #135613), CD80 (clone 16-10A1; #104723), CD86 (clone GL-1; #105025), CD137-L (clone TKS-1; #104526), CD3 (clone 17A2; #100241), CD4 (clone GK1.5; #100443), CD8 (clone 53-6.7; #100734), CD25 (clone PC61; #102020) and CD69 (clone H1.2F3; #104526) were purchased from BioLegend (San Diego, CA, USA). Fluorescent antibodies against MHC-1 (clone IVA26; #MA5-44137) were purchased from ThermoFisher Scientific. Fluorescent antibodies against calreticulin (clone FMC75; #ab196159) were purchased from Abcam (Cambridge, UK). Agonist CD137 antibodies (αCD137; clone 3H3; #BE0239) were purchased from BioXCell (Lebanon, NH, USA).

Synthesis of PBNPs

PBNPs were synthesized using a scheme as described previously in our previous work. Briefly, 5 mM citric acid was added to individual aqueous solutions of FeCl3·6H2O (10 mM; 20 ml) and K4[Fe(CN)6]·3H2O (10 mM; 20 ml), stirring at 400–600 RPM at 60°C. Once dissolved, the K4[Fe(CN)6] solution was added to the FeCl3 solution under stirring at 400–600 RPM. After stirring for 10 min at 60°C, the solution was left to cool to room temperature (RT) with maintained stirring. The resulting PBNPs were isolated from intermediate agents by centrifugation in equal volumes of acetone and water (10,000 × g for 15 min at RT), whereupon the unreacted agents were discarded, and the PBNPs were then rinsed and resuspended by microtip sonication (40% amplitude for 30 s) in Milli-Q water using a Q500 sonicator (QSonica LLC, Newton, CT) with a standard microtip probe. The isolation and rinse steps were repeated three-times before the PBNPs were finally resuspended in Milli-Q water with sonication at the desired concentration. PBNPs were analyzed for concentration by oven-drying the particles at 80°C and measuring their weight. A standard curve was generated for PBNP concentration versus absorbance at 680 nm using SpectraMax microplate reader (Molecular Devices, San Jose, CA). The size distributions and surface charge of the PBNPs were characterized by dynamic light scattering on a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK).

Synthesis of αCD137-PBNPs

αCD137-PBNPs were synthesized by adding αCD137 to PBNPs at a ratio of 2 mg PBNPs to 1 mg αCD137 in water. This mass ratio of 2:1 PBNP: αCD137 was selected from a PBNP coating optimization study described in Supplementary Figure S1. The solution was contacted on ice for 30 min on a microplate shaker set to 400 RPM (VWR, PA, USA) and then isolated from intermediate agents by centrifugation (15,000 RPM for 30 min at RT), whereupon the unreacted agents were separated and saved for analysis of αCD137 concentration by BCA assay (ThermoFisher Scientific), and the αCD137-PBNPs were resuspended in Milli-Q water by microtip sonication (30% amplitude for 30 s). Once the 100% αCD137 retention was established for the 2:1 ratio (Supplementary Figure S1), the solution of αCD137 and PBNPs were not centrifuged after their incubation; rather, the αCD137-PBNPs were used immediately after contacting for 30 min on ice. We eliminated the washing step as we did not find unreacted intermediate agents in the supernatant. αCD137-PBNPs were evaluated for size distribution and surface charge by dynamic light scattering on a Zetasizer Nano ZS. The absorbance spectra of PBNPs, αCD137 and αCD137-PBNPs were measured by UV-Vis-NIR spectrophotometry on the Genesys 10S UV-Vis machine (ThermoFisher Scientific).

Cell culture

Murine SM1 melanoma cells (BRAFV600E mutation) were provided by Dr Alejandro Villagra, as described in our previous publication. SM1 cells were cultured in RPMI media containing 1% L-glutamine (pre-supplemented), 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin (termed cRPMI). Murine T cells, isolated from the spleens of C57BL/6 mice (detailed in the Animals section below), were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS, 1% penicillin/streptomycin, 1% HEPES, 1% NEAA and 0.1 M BME (termed cDMEM).

Photothermal property validation of αCD137-PBNPs

To test the photothermal properties of αCD137-PBNPs as compared with uncoated PBNPs, PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs were suspended in Milli-Q water at a concentration of 0.15 mg/ml PBNP for both samples. A near infrared (NIR) laser (808 nm; Laserglow Technologies, Toronto, Canada) was shone onto the nanoparticle suspensions at laser powers of 0.5 W, 1 W, 1.5 W, or 2 W. Laser output power was confirmed using a power meter (PM100D, Thorlabs, NJ, USA). The spot size of the beam was 5 mm by 8 mm (40 mm2). The maximum temperatures of the suspensions were measured and recorded every minute for 10 min using a thermal camera (FLIR E5-XT, Arlington, VA, USA). Vehicle (water; no laser administration) and Laser (2 W laser; no nanoparticles) samples were used as controls. The thermal doses administered were calculated and represented as the logarithm of the cumulative equivalent min at 43°C (log(CEM43)), as previously described. Briefly, the formula ∑CEM43°C = Δt × R(43-T) was used, where Δt is the time interval, T is the average temperature during that time interval, and R is a constant with a value of 0.25 for T <43°C and 0.5 for T >43°C. For cyclic heating, the suspensions were exposed to 2.0 W NIR laser power for 10 min, then allowed to cool with the laser turned off for 10 min. This process was repeated twice for a total of three cycles of heating. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Agonistic function validation of αCD137-PBNPs

To evaluate the retained function of αCD137 once conjugated onto PBNPs, T cells were isolated from spleens from 5-week-old female C57BL/6 mice using the EasySep Mouse T Cell Isolation Kit (StemCell Technologies, Vancouver, Canada). Spleens from six mice were used to generate biological replicates (n = 6) for this experiment. Splenic T cells were stained with CFSE (ThermoFisher Scientific) and plated at 1e6 cells per well in 48-well non-tissue culture treated plates pre-coated with soluble αCD3 (ThermoFisher Scientific). T cells were then treated with either cDMEM alone, or αCD137, PBNPs, αCD137-PBNPS, or αCD137-PBNPs post-PTT (1 W) in cDMEM. After 3 days, the T cells were collected from each well and stained for CD3, CD4, CD8, CD25 and CD69 for flow cytometric analysis. Flow cytometry was performed by acquiring 100,000 events per sample using the BD Biosciences Celesta Cell Analyzer (Franklin Lakes, NJ), and analyzed with FlowJo software (Ashland, OR).

PTT on SM1 melanoma cells in vitro & subsequent in vitro analyses

To administer PTT to SM1 cells in vitro, 5 million SM1 cells were resuspended in 500 μl cRPMI containing vehicle (water), 0.15 mg/ml PBNPs, or 0.15 mg/ml αCD137-PBNPs (based on the PBNP concentration). PTT was applied to the cell suspensions as described above at laser powers spanning 0.5–2 W. Treated cells were then plated in 6-well plates and incubated at 37°C for 24 h. Cells were then harvested and aliquoted for assessing intracellular ATP or flow cytometry. For flow cytometry, cells were stained with Zombie Aqua Fixable viability dye, and subsequently with fluorescent antibodies against calreticulin, PD-L1, B7-H3, CD80, MHC-1, CD86, or CD137-L. Flow cytometry was performed using the BD Biosciences Celesta Cell Analyzer and analyzed with FlowJo software. Intracellular ATP was measured using the CellTiter-Glo assay (Promega, WI, USA), according to the manufacturer's specifications. Luminescence was measured using a SpectraMax i3x microplate reader (Molecular Devices, CA, USA). These in vitro studies were repeated in five independent experiments; averages and standard deviations are presented in the data. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Animals

All animal studies were conducted in accordance with protocols approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of the George Washington University (protocol #A2021-001) and in accordance with the humane care of research animals. For tumor growth and survival studies, 5- to 6-week-old female C57BL/6 mice were purchased from Jackson Laboratory (ME, USA) and acclimated for a week (n = 62 total mice used in the studies). To establish tumors, 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS were subcutaneously (sc.) injected into the shaved backs of C57BL/6 mice. Tumors were treated once volumes reached a size of 5 mm (44 to 60 mm3), and monitored frequently for tumor measurements. For tumor rechallenge studies, age-matched C57BL/6 mice were acquired from Jackson Laboratory. All animal studies were performed in a non-blinded fashion, and mice were randomized into the treatment groups. Mice were humanely euthanized by CO2 inhalation and follow-up cervical dislocation when tumor diameter reached 15 mm, or if the tumor ulcerated or the mouse exhibited signs of pain or distress, in accordance with the IACUC-approved protocol.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT in vivo

To test the efficacy of the novel nanoparticle platform in vivo, C57BL/6 mice were inoculated sc. with 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS on the right flank. Mice were divided into 6 groups: untreated (receiving no treatment), αCD137-PBNP-PTT (receiving one intratumoral (it.) injection of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137-PBNPs (comprising 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs at the established 2:1 ratio of PBNP:αCD137) for PTT and booster doses of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137-PBNPs it. on days 3 and 6), PBNP-PTT (receiving one it. injection of 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs for PTT), αCD137-PBNP (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137-PBNPs injected it. on days 0, 3 and 6), αCD137 it. (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137 injected it. on days 0, 3 and 6) and αCD137 ip. (no PTT; three 1.25 mg/kg doses of αCD137 injected interperitoneally (ip.) on days 0, 3 and 6). The dose of αCD137 was kept consistent in all treatments containing αCD137. Mice were anesthetized prior to and during treatment using 2–5% isoflurane. Mice treated with PTT received PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs it. and 10 min of laser exposure. Temperatures were measured using the FLIR camera at one-minute intervals with the recorded temperature being the hottest area of each tumor. Laser power was adjusted over the treatment period to maintain a surface temperature range of 80–90°C at the hottest area. After treatment, tumor size was measured every other day and the health of the mice was monitored. Three independent in vivo experiments were performed, and data was pooled together. When needed for the study, blood was collected for serum analysis using submandibular blood draw technique on days 1, 15 and at tumor volume end point. Surviving mice were rechallenged at Day 127 post-treatment with a sc. injection of 1 million SM1 cells in 100 μl PBS in the opposite (left) flank and monitored for tumor growth. Age-matched female C57BL/6 mice that had not previously been injected with SM1 cells served as “treatment naive” controls for the rechallenge studies.

Liver enzyme analysis

Blood was collected at days 1, 15 and end point (based on tumor volume) by submandibular blood drawing into EDTA-free Eppendorf tubes (VWR). Blood was allowed to clot at RT for 30 min. Sera from these samples were then collected by centrifugation at 1500 xg for 15 min at 4°C. The collected sera were stored at -80°C until analysis. Alanine aminotransferase (ALT) activity was evaluated using the colorimetric ALT activity assay kit (Abcam). The protocol from the manufacturer was followed using a 1:10 dilution of the sera. Absorbance at 570 nm was measured by SpectraMax i3X microplate reader. The aspartate aminotransferase (AST) activity was evaluated using the AST activity assay kit (MilliporeSigma), using the manufacturer's protocol with a 1:10 dilution of the sera. Absorbance at 450 nm was measured using the SpectraMax i3X microplate reader. These methods have been adapted from our previous work.

Statistics

Results obtained in this study are expressed as mean ± standard deviation. All experimental groups were tested for normal distribution using D'Agostino and Pearson test, Anderson-Darling test, Shapiro-Wilk test and Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. Statistical significance between peak intensity, peak volume, zeta potential and polydispersity index of uncoated and coated PBNPs was determined using the Wilcoxon Sign-Rank test and Friedman's Test with Dunn's post-hoc multiple comparisons test. Significance between cell surface marker expression by flow cytometry (percent and MFI fold change) was determined using Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn's multiple comparisons post-hoc test and Mann-Whitney test to account for the 20% chance of Type II and Type 1 error, respectively, for each marker. Serum liver enzyme activity was analyzed using Kruskal-Wallis test with Dunn's multiple comparisons post-hoc test and the mixed effects model. Survival results were analyzed according to Kaplan-Meier curves and log-rank tests. Results

αCD137 transiently coats PBNPs as measured by size, charge, & polydispersity index

In order to co-localize the dual effects of PBNP-PTT and αCD137 immunotherapy in the melanoma TME, we first sought to synthesize αCD137-coated PBNPs as a single nanoparticle platform. Using an electrostatic coating strategy, we contacted PBNPs and αCD137 at various mass ratios of PBNP:αCD137 (i.e. 0.5:1, 1:1, 2:1, 5:1, 10:1; n = 3 each). Because we employed an electrostatic binding strategy, we hypothesized that αCD137 would attach to PBNPs in a randomized orientation, consistent with similar approaches described in literature precedent. We monitored the properties of the resulting αCD137-PBNPs over a four-day period. We observed that regardless of the synthesis ratio, αCD137-PBNPs were susceptible to aggregation and sedimentation 3 days after synthesis (Supplementary Figure S2), and thus we did not store the nanoparticles for extended periods of time. We observed that αCD137-PBNPs synthesized at a ratio of 2:1 yielded the most favorable particle properties in terms of consistency of size distribution, polydispersity index (PDI), zeta potential, coating efficiency of αCD137 and retention of PBNPs after synthesis while maximizing the amount of αCD137 coated and retained on the PBNPs (Supplementary Figure S1). In particular, we found that synthesizing αCD137-PBNPs using a 2:1 mass ratio yielded a coating efficiency of 96.2% with negligible (0.5%) loss of PBNPs. Thus, we proceeded to use αCD137-PBNPs synthesized at a ratio of 2:1 for all subsequent studies. We then compared the size and electrochemical properties of uncoated PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs. The hydrodynamic diameter of αCD137-PBNPs appeared significantly larger than PBNPs (Figure 2A) with corresponding peak intensities of 162.7 and 86.7 nm, respectively (Figure 2B). There was an increase in PDI from 0.25 (PBNPs) to 0.41 (αCD137-PBNPs), which reflects the widening in the distribution of nanoparticle size caused by the attachment of αCD137 (Figure 2C). As another validation of αCD137 coating onto PBNPs, we measured the zeta potential of both nanoparticles, predicting that coating αCD137 on PBNPs would increase the zeta potential if it were electrostatically driven. Indeed, we found significantly different potentials of -30.91 mV for PBNPs and -23.54 mV for αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 2D), suggesting the attachment of αCD137 to the surface of PBNPs in the αCD137-PBNPs via electrostatic interactions. To measure stability of the αCD137-PBNPs, we assessed how αCD137-PBNPs size, zeta potential and PDI changed over 4 days post-synthesis (Figure 2E–G). The changes in the measured properties between days were not statistically significant. These studies illustrate that αCD137-PBNPs can be formulated to co-localize both αCD137 and PBNPs. While the αCD137-PBNPs are transiently stable, the facile coating scheme seeks to leverage the therapeutic benefits of both component agents.

αCD137-PBNPs function as effective PTT agents

To assess the effect of αCD137 on the photothermal heating properties of the resultant αCD137-PBNPs, we conducted photothermal heating studies in vitro as a function of laser power and cyclic heating. Since PBNPs function as PTT agents by absorbing NIR light, we first assessed the UV-Vis-NIR absorbance spectra up to 1000 nm, spanning the well-established extinction peak wavelengths of the constituent components of αCD137-PBNPs as compared with PBNPs. αCD137-PBNPs yielded identical absorbance curves as PBNPs indicating that αCD137 electrostatic coating has no effect on the light absorbance of PBNPs (Figure 3A). PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (n = 5) were then illuminated with NIR light for ten min at varied laser power (0 W–2.0 W) and temperatures were measured. Heating was laser power-dependent for both PBNPs (Figure 3B) and αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 3C), suggesting the sustained photothermal functionality of the αCD137-PBNPs. Likewise, thermal doses achieved with the varied heating schemes were calculated as a logarithmic function of the cumulative equivalent min at 43°C (CEM43). The thermal dose represents a parameter that converts temperature versus time heating curves into thermal dose based on seminal studies by Sapareto and Dewey, and extensively studied in the field of thermal medicine, including us. At each laser power tested, PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs achieved statistically equivalent thermal doses (7.0, 12.4–12.5, 14.1–14.4, 15.3 log (CEM43) for 0.5 W, 1.0 W, 1.5 W and 2.0 W, respectively) (Figure 3D), further suggesting that αCD137-PBNPs did not lose their capacity as agents of PTT compared with PBNPs. To assess the stability of αCD137-PBNPs as PTT agents, we conducted cyclic heating studies (n = 4) as described in the Methods. αCD137-PBNPs showed differential photothermal heating with PBNPs with each subsequent cycle of PTT (Figure 3E). Although PBNPs maintained equivalent temperature curves with each cycle of heating, αCD137-PBNPs appeared to lose heating capacity by cycle 3, when PBNP-PTT achieved a thermal dose of 16.0 log (CEM43) while αCD137-PBNP-PTT achieved 11.8 log (CEM43) (Figure 3F). However, since we propose to use αCD137-PBNP for a single administration of PTT, consistent with their widely documented use in the literature, the benefit of adding αCD137 on the particles, tested subsequently, is expected to outweigh the loss in PTT performance of the particles.

αCD137 retains agonistic function on αCD137-PBNPs in vitro

In addition to αCD137-PBNPs maintaining photothermal heating capability, we tested whether αCD137 attached on to PBNPs retained their co-stimulatory action on T cells equivalent to free αCD137. Therefore, we isolated murine T cells from mouse spleens using magnetic bead isolation, and cultured them for 72 h prior to treatment with αCD137, PBNPs, αCD137-PBNPs, or αCD137-PBNPs after administering PTT for ten min at 1.0 W. αCD137 dosing was kept consistent throughout the treatment groups. The T cells were also stimulated with soluble αCD3 for 72 h in vitro prior to addition of the listed treatments. To evaluate T cell activation, we measured the expression of CD69 and CD25 on splenic T cells. Although αCD137, αCD137-PBNPs and αCD137-PBNPs post-PTT did not induce CD69 expression alone (Figure 4A), T cells receiving these treatments significantly increased the co-expression of CD69 and CD25 (34.9, 30.8 and 25.4%, respectively) (Figure 4B) and the expression of CD25 alone (62.0, 65.0, 77.0%, respectively) (Figure 4C), as compared with unstimulated T cells. The population of CD69-/CD25- T cells appeared negligible for the treatments containing αCD137 (Figure 4D). Importantly, αCD137 and αCD137-PBNPs (pre- or post-PTT) generated equivalent activation of T cells as measured by CD69 and CD25 expression (Supplementary Figure S3), suggesting the retention of the agonistic function of αCD137 after synthesis with PBNPs as well as after αCD137-PBNP-PTT had been administered. Importantly, subjecting αCD137-PBNP to PTT did not diminish the T cell agonistic function of the bound αCD137, a criterion for the potential success of the component therapeutic agents in their envisioned co-localized use. Because these assays were performed in the absence of SM1 melanoma cells, these data represent the agonist functionality of the αCD137-PBNPs and not necessarily the immunological effect of the treatment scheme.

In vitro αCD137-PBNP-PTT initiates similar changes in immunogenic markers on SM1 cells as PBNP-PTT

Having established that αCD137-PBNPs sustained the functional properties of both αCD137 and PBNPs, we next administered either PBNP-PTT or αCD137-PBNP-PTT to SM1 cells in vitro, and measured several phenotypic markers post-PTT. A representative flow cytometric gating strategy is illustrated in Supplementary Figure S4. For this analysis, we included both the Kruskal-Wallis and Mann-Whitney tests for statistical significance to account for the Type II and Type I errors, respectively. PTT using 1.0 W laser power generated a significant reduction in SM1 cell viability using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (16.4 ± 18.9% and 0.6 ± 0.5%, respectively) (Figure 5A). Intracellular ATP was then evaluated as an indication of released ATP post-PTT, an immunogenic marker and a marker associated with ICD. Due to the differences in luminescence values between independent replicates of this experiment, we used the untreated luminescence values as a normalization metric. We found a significant decrease of intracellular ATP in a dose-dependent manner for both PBNP- or αCD137-PBNP-based PTT (Figure 5B). Calreticulin, another marker of ICD, appeared significantly increased on the surface of SM1 cells after treatment with 1.0 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5C), suggesting the initiation of ICD by both treatments. We observed significant increases in the expression of PD-L1 on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-PTT at 1.0 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5D). B7-H3 significantly increased on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-αCD137-PBNP-PTT at 1.0 W and post-PBNP-PTT at both 0.5 and 1.0 W (Figure 5E). CD80 increased after treatment with PBNPs, or αCD137-PBNP-PTT at 1.0 W or PBNP-PTT at 0.5 W (Figure 5F). MHC-1 significantly increased on the surface of surviving SM1 cells post-PTT at 0.5 W using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs (Figure 5G). CD86, however, only appeared significantly increased on the surface of SM1 cells post 1.0 W PBNP-PTT (Figure 5H). The ligand for CD137 (CD137-L) was unchanged on the surface of surviving SM1 cells after any treatment (Figure 5I). These observations suggest that PTT using either PBNPs or αCD137-PBNPs elicits similar changes in several markers associated with immunogenicity on the surface of surviving SM1 cells, which may induce a change in immunogenicity in the TME post PTT.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT effectively treats SM1 tumors in vivo

To evaluate the efficacy of αCD137-PBNP-PTT on SM1 melanoma in vivo, we compared the effects of the following treatments on SM1 tumor-bearing mice (all mice were female): three ip. injections of αCD137 at 1.25 mg/kg (αCD137 ip.), three it. injections of αCD137 at 1.25 mg/kg (αCD137 it.), three it. injections of αCD137-PBNPs (comprising 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs; αCD137-PBNP it.), PBNP-PTT (2.5 mg/kg PBNPs) and αCD137-PBNP-PTT with two it. booster doses of αCD137-PBNPs comprising of 1.25 mg/kg αCD137 and 2.5 mg/kg PBNPs (αCD137-PBNP-PTT) (Figure 6A). Mice treated with PTT received statistically equivalent thermal doses of 13.7 ± 0.52 (PBNP-PTT) or 14.2 ± 0.95 (αCD137-PBNP PTT) log(CEM43) (Supplementary Figure S5). We tracked tumor volume progression for each group (Figure 6B–F), and quantified survival based on tumor burden end point (any dimension measuring ≥15 mm). Mice receiving αCD137-PBNP-PTT survived significantly longer than all other groups by Day 50 (50% survival), compared with mice receiving no treatment (0%), PBNP-PTT (10%), αCD137 it. (20%), αCD137 ip. (20%) and αCD137-PBNP i.t (20%) (Figure 6G). To evaluate whether immunological memory had been established after treatment in the tumor-free mice, the long-term surviving mice were rechallenged 127 days after initial treatment. All rechallenged mice rejected the SM1 tumor (n = 1 from PBNP-PTT, n = 2 from αCD137 it., n = 1 from αCD137 ip., n = 1 from αCD137-PBNP it. and n = 5 from αCD137-PBNP-PTT), while all age-matched naive mice (n = 3) were euthanized due to tumor burden by Day 40 post-rechallenge, suggesting that immunological memory was generated in all previously treated groups (Figure 6H). These findings demonstrate the efficacy of the αCD137-PBNP-PTT platform in eliciting significantly higher complete tumor regression, long-term survival and protection against tumor rechallenge in the SM1 melanoma model compared with all other treatments.

αCD137-PBNP-PTT does not increase hepatotoxicity in treated mice

To investigate whether αCD137-PBNP-PTT generated hepatoxicity similar to that observed when using αCD137 alone, we collected sera from all mice on days 1 and 15, as well as at the timepoint when animals were euthanized. We measured the activity of the liver enzymes aspartate aminotransferase (AST) (Figure 7A) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT) (Figure 7B) in the sera as measures for hepatotoxicity. Although we observed a significant increase in ALT activity in mice treated with αCD137-PBNP-PTT compared with naive (tumor-free) mice at their terminal end point, there was no increased AST or ALT activity in any of the treated mice compared with Untreated SM1 tumor-bearing mice. Thus, any increased AST or ALT activity measured in the mice over time was likely due to the presence or growth of the SM1 tumor (present in all groups at end point except naive) and not due to hepatotoxicity generated by αCD137 or αCD137-PBNPs. These data suggest that at the doses of αCD137 used for αCD137-PBNP-PTT, which is significantly lower than the doses of αCD137 that generated hepatotoxicity clinically, αCD137-PBNPs are potentially safe for use as agents of PTT for melanoma.

Discussion

The studies presented herein illustrate the efficacy of αCD137-PBNP-PTT, a combination of PBNP-based PTT with agonistic αCD137 antibodies as a novel single platform nanoimmunotherapy for treating melanoma. PBNP-PTT has been explored by us and others in the literature, and has demonstrated tumor cell cytotoxicity, immunogenicity and a mild abscopal effect. Although PBNP-PTT increases T cell infiltration into treated tumors, it often requires combination with other agents (e.g. immune checkpoint inhibitors, toll-like receptor agonists, chemotherapeutics) to exert maximum anti-tumor efficacy. Our previously published studies have demonstrated the combination of PBNP-PTT with systemic αCD137 monoclonal antibody administration for the treatment of SM1 melanoma. Despite the robust anti-tumor efficacy we observed in this prior study, we also observed acute hepatotoxicity following the treatment with either αCD137 antibodies or the combination of PBNP-PTT with αCD137, perhaps due to the relatively high concentration of antibody required for efficacy, and/or the systemic (ip.) route of administration. This hepatotoxicity reflects what is also observed in humans, where agonistic αCD137 antibodies investigated in Phases I and II clinical trials have generated hepatotoxicity in treated patients. Since the failure of the first generation of clinical αCD137 antibodies, many groups have developed alternative αCD137 agonists to address liver toxicity. Indeed, there are several ongoing early phase clinical trials testing these second generation CD137 agonists, many in combination with other drugs or in bi- or tri-specific formulations, although many have not yet been published. To our knowledge, we are the first to test the combination of PTT with αCD137 for treating melanoma. Here we sought to test whether synthesizing a novel nanoparticle platform comprising both PBNPs and αCD137 antibodies (αCD137-PBNPs) to be administered locally in the TME could effectively treat melanoma while mitigating hepatotoxicity.

First, we investigated whether combining the two therapies in a single nanoparticle would impact the functionality of either component. Our studies illustrated that αCD137-PBNPs could be formulated to transiently co-localize both αCD137 and PBNPs as measured by time-dependent dynamic light scattering and zetametry (Figure 2). Although prior studies, including our own have described electron microscopy-based morphological analyses of PBNP-based nanoformulations, a limitation of the present study is a lack of such EM-based morphological data on αCD137-PBNPs, which is the focus of ongoing work in our group. We found that αCD137-PBNPs maintained equivalent heating capacity as PBNPs, although their cyclic heating capability was diminished after three rounds of PTT (Figure 3). This is a finding relevant to the stability of the nanoparticles, but should not impact in vivo efficacy since only one round of PTT is used for treatment. Importantly, we also observed that αCD137-PBNPs activated T cells, as measured by CD69 and CD25 expression, in an equivalent manner to free αCD137 antibodies (Figure 4). These findings allowed us to use the αCD137-PBNPs to test whether the it. administration of the combined platform facilitated anti-tumor efficacy similarly to the dual administration of ip. αCD137 and it. PBNP-PTT without toxicity.

Indeed, αCD137-PBNP-PTT was effective in generating tumor regression and a significant improvement in survival of mice (Figure 6). These data suggest that combining the two therapeutic approaches into a single platform does not diminish the functionality of either component; instead, they work in a complementary manner. Importantly, mice receiving PTT using αCD137-PBNPs did not exhibit higher liver enzyme activity as compared with untreated mice, suggesting no acute of chronic hepatoxicity was initiated (Figure 7). These findings suggest the potential safety of the therapy, and the mitigation of toxicity that was observed when systemic administration of αCD137 was used. It is important to note that the effective dose administered in this study (1.25 mg/kg) was 12-fold lower than the dose used in our previous publication (15 mg/kg) that first illustrated the efficacy of PBNP-PTT combined with αCD137. We propose that immobilizing the αCD137 on PBNPs and administering it it. allows for a lower dose to effectively treat SM1 tumors, while lowering liver toxicity.

Overall, we propose the following mechanism of action of the novel αCD137-PBNP-PTT (Figure 1C). Locally administered αCD137-PBNP-PTT generates heating of melanoma tumor cells, tumor cell death and an immunogenic phenotype in the TME (Figure 5). Local antigen-presenting cells in the TME may engage with treated melanoma cells and take up and present tumor antigens. Because of the immunogenic impact of PTT, T cells may infiltrate the TME and activate in response to tumor antigens. Concurrently, αCD137 on the αCD137-PBNPs is transiently released into the TME and may co-stimulate nearby T cells to promote activation and expansion of these tumor-specific T cells. These effects work together to effectively diminish tumor burden and generate immunological memory (Figure 6).

Conclusion

The data presented in this investigation illustrate a novel nanoimmunotherapy comprising PBNP-based PTT with agonistic αCD137 antibodies on a single nanoparticle platform. This therapeutic strategy combines the complementary actions of the two components, generating tumor cell death and immunogenicity and subsequent activation of infiltrating T cells, resulting in a robust anti-tumor immune effect. Importantly, it appears that co-localizing the agonistic co-stimulatory function of αCD137 within the TME on the PBNP platform lowers the effective dose and mitigates the hepatotoxicity observed upon systemic administration of αCD137 in prior studies. Consequently, the results described in this paper may facilitate future studies investigating the effect of αCD137-PBNP-based PTT on abscopal efficacy in models of synchronous, metachronous and metastatic melanoma models in vivo, with the goal of translating the novel and safe therapeutic approach of αCD137-PBNP-PTT to patients with melanoma.
