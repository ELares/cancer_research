# MGANSL: multi-network representation generating with generative adversarial network for synthetic lethality prediction.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Cancer is a complex disease that arises from the simultaneous mutations of multiple biological molecules. An effective therapeutic strategy is to exploit synthetic lethality (SL) by targeting the SL partner of cancer driver genes. Computational approaches have emerged as efficient complements to traditional methods. Although some methods integrate heterogeneous sources to learn multi-network representations, they often neglect consistent information shared across different networks and specific characteristic specific to individual network. Therefore, a comprehensive representation learning framework for capturing both multi-network consistency and network-specific information of gene pair is needed.

**RESULTS**: We proposed a novel approach capturing Multi-network consistent and specific representation with Generative Adversarial Network for Synthetic Lethality prediction (MGANSL). MGANSL employs network-aligned and network-specific encoding modules to cooperatively learn comprehensive multi-network representations of gene pair. In particular, network-aligned encoding module can capture cross-modal consistent information via cross-network adversarial generation, and network-specific encoding module can capture single network specific information via intra-network adversarial generation.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Comprehensive experiments conducted on two human synthetic lethality datasets demonstrate the superiority of proposed method in SL prediction. Moreover, the novel predicted SL associations could aid in designing anti-cancer drugs and providing potential drug targets.

Introduction

Cancer arises from the co-mutations of diverse biological molecules which lead to aberrant cell proliferation and eventual metastasis. Elucidating these complex molecular interactions is essential for understanding the mechanism behind cancer and developing innovative therapeutic strategies. Traditional targeted cancer therapies focus on gene products that are mutated or overexpressed to a particular cancer type. However, it is well known that targeting loss-of-function mutations and some instances of gene overexpression faces with huge challenges, which presents obstacles in drug development. Encouragingly, the concept of synthetic lethality (SL) offers a promising alternative for addressing these by indirectly targeting intractable genes. Specifically, synthetic lethality depicts a type of genetic relationship wherein the simultaneous inhibition of two genes results in lethality, but the inhibition of each gene individually being viable. It enables the targeting SL partner of cancer driver, which effectively mitigates the challenges posed by direct targeting of mutated or overexpressed genes. The clinical approval and successful application of the PARP inhibitor olaparib further highlight the therapeutic potential of SL-based strategies. Consequently, synthetic lethality has attracted much attention from the area of anti-cancer drug development, which prompt a series of works dedicated to the exploration and identification of SL gene pairs.

In recent decades, numerous wet lab-based and statistics-based works have been presented to infer novel SL interactions. For example, Jerby-Arnon et al. propose a data-driven approach (DAISY) to infer SL gene pairs by integrating cancer patients, somatic mutation, and shRNA data. However, these methods are costly, time-consuming and off-target effects. The rapid advancements of high-throughput technologies have produced abundant biological heterogeneous data, such as protein-protein interactions, gene functional annotations and so on. Therefore, computational models for SL prediction with these heterogeneous sources have been effectively complementary to traditional approaches.

Biological networks depict complex interaction between different biological molecules (gene, protein) and genetic interactions are affected by complex network regulation and other biological interaction. Mining the structural and functional properties embedded within these networks enables researchers to uncover potential mechanisms underlying synthetic lethality (SL) and identify candidate SL gene pairs. Early works in synthetic lethality (SL) prediction primarily utilized single network approaches, focusing on individual biological network such as protein-protein interaction (PPI) network, gene signaling network or SL interactive network. These methods typically analyze topological features or perturbation-induced network alterations. The representative approach is DDGCN that learn gene representation in SL interactive network with graph convolutional network to explore SL network topological structure information. However, single network-based methods cannot model complex interaction between genes and neglect other biological networks which provides more comprehensive insights and information to mechanism hidden in SL. As research progressed, the field has increasingly shifted toward multi-network integration to leverage diverse biological contexts, including PPI networks, Gene Ontology (GO) functional networks, and so on.

To address these challenges, recent efforts have focused on integrating multiple biological networks that encode different interaction patterns. For example, to integrate different gene feature network, Long et al. proposed a graph attention network method GCATSL to learn gene multi-network features with graph attention network in which two-level attention mechanism are utilized to aggregate different networks. Similarly, Hao et al. proposed a graph auto-encoder method SLMGAE to learn gene cross-view representation from different gene networks, in which attention mechanism are utilized to aggregate different network representation. Furthermore, Wang et al. proposed a method KG4SL to integrate different kinds of entity relationships for learning gene interpretable representation from different gene knowledge networks. However, current multi-network methods concentrate on learning consistent gene representations from various biological networks, they implicitly assume that different network properties have same distributions. Different networks depict different types of genetic interactions and thus gene characteristics in diverse networks have different distribution. Furthermore, each network usually contains private information which describes specific biological interactive pattern. Existing methods often overlook these network-specific features, resulting in incomplete gene-pair representations.

Recently, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have gained substantial traction in the deep learning community and have been successfully applied to tasks such as image synthesis, information retrieval, and graph representation learning. A GAN model typically model antagonistic game between generators and discriminators in which generators aim to produce a fake image or representation, and discriminators aim to distinguish the image or representation produced by generators. For example, Zhang et al. proposed a Self-Attention Generative Adversarial Network (SAGAN) for image generation tasks with attention-driven and long-range dependency. Furthermore, GAN have been extended in multi-modal data for learning cross-modal representation from different data sources. For example, Hu et al. proposed a multi-modal adversarial network to retrieve samples from different modalities by learning comprehensive representation, in which two encoder module are utilized to learn modal-common and modal-specific during adversarial game. Vukotic et al. verify the effectiveness of GANS to obtain multi-modal representation in retrieving video segments task. These successes highlight the strong potential of GANs for handling heterogeneous biological networks with diverse distributions and interaction patterns.

Inspired by these, we present a novel approach to learn multi-network representation of gene pair with Generative Adversarial Networks for SL prediction (MGANSL). MGANSL comprised of two encoding modules to cooperatively learn comprehensive multi-network representation from diverse network properties. In network-common encoding module, MGANSL utilize encoder based on generative adversarial network to learn cross-network consistent representation of gene pair by projecting different network features into a common subspace. In network-private encoding module, we adopt network-specific encoders to individually extract network-specific representation by projecting different network features into network -private subspaces. Inter-network consistent representation and intra-network specific representation are concatenated to learna multilayer perceptron (MLP) classifier trained in a supervised manner. Comprehensive experiments performed on benchmark SL dataset verify that our approach is superior to baseline approaches. Additionally, ablation study demonstrates that cross-network consistent representation and network-specific representation provide complementary view for SL prediction. Four kinds of case study further illustrate the potential in anti-cancer drug discovery of SL pairs predicted by our approach.

Method

Data process

We evaluated MGANSL on two human synthetic lethal datasets, including SynLethDB and SynLethDB2.0. SynLethDB contains 19667 SL associations between 6375 genes, while SynLethDB2.0 contains 35943 SL associations between 9856 genes respectively. For each dataset, an equal number of unlabeled gene pairs (i.e., gene pairs without known SL associations) were randomly selected as negative samples (19667 for SynLethDB and 35943 for SynLethDB 2.0). Therefore, we select 19667 non-SL samples in SynLethDB and 35943 non-SL samples in SynLethDB2.0. For each sample, we utilize three network representations: PPI structure network, GO functional network, and SL interactive network. For PPI structure network, we download human protein-protein interactions from HPRD database and calculated the gene topological similarity network in PPI network with FSweight. For GO functional network, we download the Gene Ontology annotation data from Gene Ontology dataset and calculate the gene functional similarity network following the method described in. For SL interactive network, we calculate the gaussian kernel similarity of gene with known SL associations. Finally, we adopt Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to extract more discriminative network representations from three gene similarity network and concatenate the two gene features as representation of gene pair.

Results

Experimental setups

Experimental setting and measuring metrics

To evaluate the performance of different methods in discovering novel SL pairs and identifying SL partners for genes lacking known SL associations, we implement two kinds of data partition strategies to conduct cross validation: (1)CV1: 5-fold cross validation based on SL pairs. (2)CV2: 5-fold cross validation based on genes. Given the extremely large number of unlabeled gene pairs, we randomly sample an equal number of unknown pairs as negative samples. For CV1, all positive and negative samples are randomly divided into five subsets, with four subsets used for training and one for testing. For CV2, all genes are randomly divided into five subsets, and samples involving four subsets are used as the training set, while those involving the remaining subset form the testing set. To ensure the robustness and mitigate potential sampling bias, this 5-fold cross validation was independently repeated 10 times, and the final performance metrics are the averaged values over these runs. For assessing the effectiveness of model, we employ AUC, AUPR, F1 and Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) as measurement indicators.

GCATSL fuses different feature graphs to construct gene features and predict SL associations with learned factor matrix.

SL2MF learns probabilistic latent representations of genes to infer SL matrix by incorporating GO and PPI information.

GRSMF learns gene representations and correlation matrix between genes from known SL matrix.

CMF fuses multiple biological sources to obtain gene latent representation with collective matrix factorization (CMF).

TARSL adopt a cross-network representation learning to learn gene representation for inferring novel SL associations.

In the predicted top 1000 SL pairs, 20 combinations of gene pair and drug show significantly high drug sensitivity. Conclusion

In this study, we proposed multi-network adversarial generating model for capturing intricate feature of gene pairs. Specifically, a novel computational model named MGANSL was proposed to predict novel SL associations. MGANSL integrates a network-common encoder and a network-specific encoder to jointly learn shared and private representations from heterogeneous biological networks. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate MGANSL can effectively predicting SL interactions by capturing comprehensive multi-network feature of gene pairs. However, there are some limitations that needed be addressed in the future works. First, incorporating additional biological data types (e.g., methylation and co-expression profiles) may further enrich the learned representations. Second, more advanced multi-network integration strategies could improve the robustness of feature fusion. Finally, experimental validation of predicted SL pairs will be essential for translating computational predictions into biological and clinical applications.
