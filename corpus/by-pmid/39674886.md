# Single-cell data-driven design of armed oncolytic virus to boost cooperative innate-adaptive immunity against cancer.

## Abstract

Oncolytic viruses have been considered promising cancer immunotherapies. However, oncovirotherapy agents impart durable responses in only a subset of cancer patients. Thus, exploring the cellular and molecular mechanisms underlying the heterogeneous responses in patients can provide guidance to develop more effective oncolytic virus therapies. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis of tumors responsive and non-responsive to oncovirotherapy revealed signatures of the tumor immune microenvironment associated with immune response. Thus, we designed and constructed an armed oncolytic virus, OV-5A, that expressed five genes with non-redundant functions. OV-5A treatment exhibits robust immune response against various tumors in multiple mouse models, peripheral blood mononuclear cell -patient-derived xenograft models, organoid-immune cell co-culture systems, and patient tissue sections by activating a cooperative innate-adaptive immune response against tumor cells. scRNA-seq analysis of complete responders and partial responders to OV-5A treatment guided the design of combination therapy of OV-5A. This data-driven approach paves an innovative way to rationalize the design of oncolytic virus and multi-agent combination therapies.

Graphical abstract

Zhang and colleagues proposed a data-driven approach to modifying oncolytic viruses based on the landscape of the tumor immune microenvironment associated with therapeutic responses. The modified oncolytic virus effectively eradicated tumors by activating cooperative innate-adaptive immunity. This work accelerates the development of more effective oncolytic virotherapy and combination regimens.

Introduction

Oncolytic viruses (OVs), a promising cancer immunotherapy, have multiple mechanisms of action. The major feature is to selectively replicate in and eventually kill the tumor cells. In addition, OVs can remodel the tumor microenvironment and turn cold tumors into hot tumors. Furthermore, OVs can be modified to express immunostimulatory cytokines. For example, Talimogene laherparepvec (T-VEC), a herpes simplex virus 1 (HSV-1)-derived OV, modified with the granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) gene, has been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration for malignant melanoma treatment. However, only a subset of patients with advanced melanoma showed durable responses to T-VEC. OVs modified with other transgenes also demonstrated robust immune responses against tumors.

Since the reasons for the lack of robust efficacy are multifactorial, a multiplexed arming strategy is required to develop more effective OVs. Understanding the tumor immune microenvironment (TIME) that underlies the heterogeneous response to OV treatment could guide the rational design of the more effective OV. Recently, DePeaux et al. reported resistance to oncolytic vaccinia virus associated with the elevation of transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) by comparing cytokines between OV-sensitive and OV-resistant tumors, resulting in engineering the OV with a TGFβRII inhibitor. The emerging single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) analysis provides a powerful tool to study the landscape of the entire tumor microenvironment with unprecedented resolution, which can provide important information to guide drug development and clinical practice. Groups have characterized the intratumoral immune heterogeneity of different mouse tumor models such as immune checkpoint inhibitor-insensitive B16 and -sensitive MC38, using multiparameter flow cytometry or scRNA-seq to investigate potential drug-resistance mechanisms. To our knowledge, no studies to date have examined the TIME landscape at the single-cell level in relation to differential outcomes of OV therapy.

In this study, the scRNA-seq data from the TIME of the responder and non-responder tumors revealed critical features of the TIME related to the responsiveness to OVs, leading to our design of OV-5A, which includes genes encoding C-C motif chemokine ligand 5 (CCL5), C-X-C motif chemokine ligand 16 (CXCL16), interferon-γ (IFN-γ), interleukin-12 (IL-12), and GM-CSF. The efficacy and mechanism of action of OV-5A have been investigated in several syngeneic mouse models, the peripheral blood mononuclear cell-patient-derived xenograft (PBMC-PDX) model, organoids-immune cell co-culture systems, and patient tissue sections. Based on the scRNA-seq data from TIME of complete responders (CRs) and partial responders (PRs) to OV-5A treatment, the efficacy of OV-5A could be further enhanced by combining with the programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) antagonist antibody, the CD40 agonist antibody, and OV-mFlt3L. Here, we provide an omics-guided approach and proof-of-concept study to develop more potent OV and its combination therapies for various cancer treatments.

Results

Gene expression signatures of tumor microenvironment associated with response to OV

HSV-1-derived OV was modified by deleting both the ICP34.5 and ICP47 coding regions, as previously reported. The pancreatic cancer as cold tumor model and colon cancer as immunogenic tumor model showed dichotomous outcomes in response to treatment with the HSV-1-derived OV.

To ascertain whether the complex TIME is associated with the heterogeneous response to OV and to exclude model-specific effects, we analyzed the tumor-infiltrating CD45+ immune cells of the responders and non-responders in both the pancreatic Pan02 model (Pan02 cells stably expressing human herpesvirus entry mediator [HVEM], referred to as Pan02 hereafter) and the MC38 colon cancer model using scRNA-seq analysis (Figure 1A). MC38 is a mouse colon adenocarcinoma cell line that resembles microsatellite-instability-high human colorectal cancers (CRCs), and several studies have shown that the cell line is immunogenic. Pan02 tumors exhibit the poor lymphoid cell infiltration into the tumors and abundance of immunosuppressive myeloid cell populations, which is characteristic of a non-immunogenic or “cold” tumor model, similar to that observed in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma patients. Both models have been used extensively for investigating anticancer immunity and immunotherapy. For both models, we used a 40% tumor inhibition rate (T/C%) 3 days after the last treatment as the criteria to distinguish responders and non-responders to OV. Specifically, the response rates to OV therapy were 53% (8/15) in the Pan02 model and 71% (10/14) in the MC38 model (Figure 1B). We selected three mice with the best response in the responder group and three mice with the worst response in the non-responder group for scRNA-seq analysis.

In total, 20,505 immune cells in the Pan02 model and 7,532 immune cells in the MC38 model passed quality control. Unbiased clustering of variable genes resulted in seven immune cell clusters. CD4+ and CD8+ T cells were defined with the expression of Cd4 and Cd8a, respectively. T regulatory (Treg) cells were identified by the expression of Tnfrsf18 and Foxp3, and tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) and monocytes were characterized using the genes Csf1r, Itgam (CD11b), and Adgre1 (F4/80). Dendritic cells (DCs) were defined by the expression of ITGAX (CD11c) and major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II. Natural killer (NK) cells expressed Ncr1 and Klrc1, and neutrophils expressed S100a9 (Figures 1C, 1D, and S1). Compared to the non-responder group, the responder group exhibited a significant increase in CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells and a decrease in Treg cells, TAMs, and monocytes in both cancer models (Figures 1E and S2). The pathways including activation of T cell receptor signal transduction, production of IFN-γ, and IL-2 were upregulated in both CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells of the responder group compared with the non-responder group (Figure 1F). Visualization of immunologically relevant genes in T cell clusters showed higher expression of effector molecules such as CCL5 and IFN-γ in both CD4+ T and CD8+ T clusters (Figures 1G and 1H). Flow cytometry analysis confirmed that more cytotoxic CD8+ T cells were activated in the responders compared with the non-responders (Figure 1N). Gene Ontology (GO) pathway analysis of macrophages revealed the cellular response to IFN-γ, and antigen processing and presentation were upregulated in the responder group (Figure 1F). Accordingly, the expression of inflammatory and antigen-presenting markers, including Nos2, MHC class II, and CD86, was more abundant in TAMs of the responders than the non-responders. In contrast, the immunosuppressive marker SPP1 was reduced in TAMs of the responders compared with the non-responders (Figure 1I).

We performed a longitudinal analysis of tumor-infiltrating immune cells of responders and non-responders on days 9, 12, and 15 post-OV treatment using flow cytometry (Figure 1J). The results showed that the proportion of CD45+ cells gradually increased in the responder group and became significantly higher than in the non-responder group on days 12 and 15 (Figures 1K–1Q). Tumor infiltration and activation of CD8+ T cells showed no significant difference between responders and non-responders on day 9, while responders showed increased and activated CD8+ T cells on days 12 and 15 (Figures 1M and1N). The macrophages in the responder group showed a greater tendency toward M1 polarization at the early phase but not at day 15, suggesting that macrophage M1 polarization might be used to predict response to OV therapy (Figures 1O–1Q).

Depletion of CD4+ T or CD8+ T cells prior to OV treatment abolished the antitumor response of OV, and depletion of NK cells modestly affected the efficacy of OV. Although OV treatment reprogrammed macrophages to M1-like macrophages, depletion of macrophages with anti-CSF1R antibody have no obvious effect on the efficacy of OV, potentially because a broad depletion strategy may affect both beneficial and immunosuppressive TAM subsets (Figure 1R).

The three most prominent differences in TIME between responders and non-responders include (1) immune cell infiltration into the tumor, (2) T cell activation, and (3) the inflammatory status of myeloid cells.

A publication describing a phase 1 clinical trial of HSV-1-derived OVs in patients with recurrent glioblastoma reported that post-treatment survival, particularly in individuals seropositive for HSV-1, was significantly associated with (1) an increased number of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in the tumor, (2) T cell activation evidenced by upregulated pathways of effector cell activity, T helper 1 responses and checkpoint inhibition, and (3) a highly inflammatory and immunologically activated tumor microenvironment characterized by M1 macrophage signatures and antigen presentation. These features are consistent with our findings in our syngeneic mouse models. Therefore, we preferentially target these three aspects to create a favorable TIME to enhance the efficacy of OV treatment.

Analysis of the difference in cell-cell interaction networks between R and NR to OV treatment

We further explored the cell-cell interactions associated with the response to OV using CellChat. Notably, the pathways enriched in the responder group included CCL (CCL4/CCR5, CCL5/CCR1, CCL5/CCR5), CXCL (CXCL9/CXCR3, CXCL10/CXCR3, CXCL16/CXCR6), IFN-II (IFN-γ/IFN-γR), IL-10 (IL-10/IL-10R), IL-1 (IL-18/IL-18R), and CSF (CSF1/CSF1R). Conversely, the pathways enriched in the non-responder group, including Spp1, Gas, and Pros, were predominantly observed in immunosuppressive TAMs (Figure 2A). The upregulation of pathways such as IFN-II, CCL, CXCL, and signal strength and downregulation of Spp1 were also observed in the responders and non-responders of the MC38 model (Figure 2B).

In the Pan02 and MC38 models, the different levels of cytokines and chemokines in both tumor homogenates and serum between the responder and non-responder groups were verified using the multiplex immunoassays. Cytokines such as IFN-γ, GM-CSF, IL-2, IL-12p70, and IL-1β, and chemokines such as CCL4, CCL5, CCL22, CXCL10, CXCL12, and CXCL16, were significantly higher in the responder group than in the non-responder group (Figures 2C, 2D, and S3–S7).

Based on the known functions of these cytokines and chemokines, the most differentially expressed genes between the responder and non-responder groups are divided into three categories. Category 1 includes genes involved in immune cell migration, such as the chemokines CCLs and CXCLs; category 2 consists of those that activate T cells, such as IL-2, IL-12, and IL-18; and category 3 consists of those that reprogram myeloid cells, such as IFN-γ and GM-CSF. The individual and collective effect of these genes could contribute to the more favorable responsiveness to OV therapy.

The cytokine, chemokine, or their combination showed synergistic activity with OVs in preclinical models

To enable high-throughput screening of the above-mentioned cytokines and chemokines for the enhancement of OV efficacy, cell lines expressing selected genes by the tetracycline-controlled Tet-On gene expression system were established. The inducible gene expression in tumors was confirmed with MC38-TetOne-luciferase cells (Figure S9A). After the formation of a palpable tumor, mice were given doxycycline (DOX) in their drinking water to induce cytokine expression in the tumor and subsequently treated with OVs (Figure S9B). The tumor growth and survival analysis results showed that the CCL4, CCL5, CXCL9, CXCL16, IL-2, IL-10, IL-12, IL-18, IFN-γ, or GM-CSF significantly augmented the efficacy of the unmodified OVs (Figures 2E–2H and S9C–S9F). Based on the effectiveness of the individual genes and the prior knowledge of their function, we selected IL-2 and/or IL-12 of category 1, CCL5 and/or CXCL16 of category 2, and IFN-γ and/or GM-CSF of category 3 for insertion in OVs. The combination is not intended to be exhaustive, and the iterative rounds of single-cell data-driven OV modification may be carried out to further optimize OV.

Subsequently, murine CCL5, CXCL16, IL-2, IL-12, IFN-γ, or GM-CSF was inserted into the ICP34.5 loci of the OV backbone to construct OV-mCCL5, OV-mCXCL16, OV-mIL-2, OV-mIL-12, OV-mIFN-γ and OV-mGM-CSF, respectively (Figure 2I). Modified OVs retained their capability to replicate in tumor cells and allowed the infected cells to express OV-encoded cytokines (Figures 2J and 2K). Treatment with individual-gene-modified OVs prolonged survival compared to the unmodified OVs in the Pan02 model, whereas no mice were cured by any of the single-gene-modified OVs (Figures S10A–S10G). The bulk RNA-seq analysis revealed that CCL5 and CXCL16 from category 1, IL-2 and IL-12 from category 2, and IFN-γ and GM-CSF from category 3 have some non-redundant roles (Figure S10H).

To evaluate whether more potent efficacy could be achieved by the combination of the six single-gene-modified OVs (OV-six), Pan02-bearing mice were treated with PBS (control), unmodified OVs, OV-six containing equal amounts of six single-gene-modified OVs or “OV-six minus one” containing five out of six single-gene-modified OVs. OV-six resulted in a complete responder (CR) in three out of six mice, whereas no mice were cured by the unmodified OV (Figure 2L). Removing IL-2 from the modification did not reduce the survival rates compared with the OV-six group (Figures 2L and S11A; Table S1). Further deletion of any other cytokine or chemokine from OV-five (mixture of equal amounts of OV-mCCL5, OV-mCXCL16, OV-mIL-12, OV-mIFN-γ, and OV-mGM-CSF) resulted in an impaired antitumor efficacy (Figures 2M and S11B; Table S2). These data supported the idea that OV could be modified with CCL5, CXCL16, IL-12, IFN-γ, and GM-CSF to enhance its antitumor efficacy.

The combination of CCL5, CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF augmented the efficacy of OV and elicited durable antitumor memory

Next, we engineered OV-5A by inserting expression cassettes of CCL5, CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF. To minimize the total size of the exogenous genes accommodated into the HSV-1 genome, we selected the smallest gene murine CCL5 to replace the two ICP34.5 loci. The CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF were linked with the T2A or P2A sequence and inserted into the UL26-UL27 intergenic regions (Figure 3A).

OV-5A exhibited antitumor efficacy as potent as the mixtures of five single-gene-modified OVs in the Pan02 mouse model (Figure 3B). The expression levels of CCL5, CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF in OV-5A-infected tumors persisted up to 120 h post-intratumoral (i.t.) injection of OV-5A (Figure 3C). We observed that there were differences in the levels of these cytokines expressed by the infected Pan02 cells and in the Pan02 tumor microenvironment after infection, suggesting that the cytokines in the tumor microenvironment can be attributed to either the OV-encoded cytokines or the cytokines endogenously produced by immune cells. No significant change in body weight loss and levels of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) were observed after OV-5A treatment (Figures 3D and 3E), indicating good tolerance of the therapy. The viral genome was detected exclusively in the tumors for up to 3 days, with no virus detected in the spleen, liver, kidney, or lung (Figure 3F). In addition, H&E staining showed that OV-5A did not lead to inflammation and injury in the spleen and liver (Figure 3G).

OV-5A-cured Pan02-bearing mice were rechallenged with Pan02 cells 2–3 months after the initial cure. These mice rejected the tumors, while all age-matched treatment-naive control mice developed tumors, indicating that OV-5A treatment elicited durable antitumor memory (Figure 3H). The OV-5A treatment inhibited tumor growth on both injected and non-injected sides in a bilateral model (Figure 3I), supporting that OV-5A elicited a systematic antitumor response. A similar inhibitory effect of OV-5A treatment on tumor growth was also observed in MC38, B16-F10, KPC, and GL261 tumor models (Figures 3J, 3K, and S12).

We used serial in vivo passaging of a tumor to generate paired isogenic tumor cell lines stably resistant to OV treatment. Tumor was resected from the nonresponsive mice, digested, and implanted into new mice. These tumors were treated with OV, and treatment-resistant tumors were again resected. This process was repeated three times until an MC38 derivative (called MC38OVR) stably resistant to OV therapy was generated as a good model to study the intrinsic tumor innate immunity (Figures S13A and S13B). Although OV-5A was not specifically designed to overcome this intrinsic tumor-resistance mechanism, treatment of MC38OVR tumors with OV-5A exhibited a significant inhibition of MC38OVR tumor growth (Figures S13C and S13D).

These data revealed that OV-5A treatment represented a promising therapeutic against various tumors by triggering a systemic and persistent immune response.

OV-5A promoted immune cell infiltration in tumor, activation of both CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells, and repolarization of TAMs toward the M1-like phenotype

The mechanism of action of OV-5A was explored using scRNA-seq, flow cytometry, and multiplex immunofluorescence (mIF). The scRNA-seq data showed that the T cell activation pathway in both conventional CD4+ T and CD8+ T cells, as well as antigen processing and presentation and inflammatory function in macrophages, were upregulated following the OV-5A treatment compared to OV treatment (Figure S14). The flow cytometry analysis showed that the treatment of OV-5A resulted in a significant increase in conventional CD4+ T cells and CD8+ T cells and a reduction in Treg cells and macrophages in tumors compared to the non-modified OV treatment group and the vehicle control group (Figures 4A–4F). OV-5A treatment led to an increased expression of Gzmb, Ifn-γ in both CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells, while reducing the expression of exhaustion markers of PD-1 and Havcr2 (Figures 4D and 4G–4I). In addition, OV-5A treatment increased pro-inflammatory signature markers such as inducible nitric oxide synthase (iNOS) and decreased immunosuppressive markers such as CD206 on macrophages, and supporting macrophages were reprogrammed to an M1-like phenotype (Figures 4J–4L). The mIF further validated the results of scRNA-seq and flow cytometry (Figures 4M and 4N).

Depletion of CD4+ T cells and CD8+ T cells significantly alleviated the tumor inhibition effect of either OV or OV-5A. Notably, the depletion of macrophages abolished the efficacy of OV-5A, suggesting the M1-polarized TAMs contributed to the antitumor efficacy of OV-5A (Figures 4O and 4P). Although the depletion of NK cells did not affect the efficacy at an early point (approximately day 18), the depletion of NK cells shortened the overall survival of the OV-5A treatment group, indicating that NK cells also contributed to the efficacy at a later stage. Therefore, the efficacy of OV-5A was dependent on CD8+ T cells, CD4+ T cells, and macrophages, and to a lesser extent, NK cells.

OV-5A facilitated T cell migration, repolarized macrophage cells to the M1 phenotype, and augmented the cytotoxicity of CD8+ T and macrophage cells in vitro

Next, we performed a panel of in vitro assays to investigate the effect of OV-5A on CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, and macrophages. A transwell assay was performed to investigate the regulation of OV-5A on T cell migration. Pan02 cells in the lower chamber were infected with different OVs, and then CD4+ T cells or CD8+ T cells were added into the upper chambers. The OV-mCCL5-infected tumor cells attracted both CD4+ T cells and CD8+ T cells. In contrast, OV-mCXCL16-infected tumor cells selectively attracted CD8+ T cells. The greatest increase in T cell migration was observed in the OV-5A group (Figures 5A and 5B).

The cytotoxic activity of CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, and macrophage cells was assessed by immune cell and tumor cell co-culture assays. Immune cells were harvested from Pan02 tumors 3 days after the last treatment and co-cultured with Pan02 cells (Figure 5C). CD8+ T cells and macrophage cells isolated from the OV-5A treatment group exhibited more potent direct tumor-killing capability than those cells isolated from the unmodified OV group, whereas CD4+ T cells from the unmodified OV or OV-5A group exhibited similar cytolytic activity against tumor cells (Figures 5D–5F). Furthermore, the tumoricidal activity of macrophages from the OV-5A group was dependent on peroxynitrite (ONOO−) generation by NOS (Figure 5F).

To explore the potential of OV in promoting macrophage differentiation toward a pro-inflammatory phenotype, we added supernatants from the infected tumor cells to the bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) (Figure 5G). Supernatants from OV-mIFN-γ, OV-mGM-CSF, or OV-5A-infected tumor cells appreciably upregulated the expression of iNOS in BMDM cells (Figure 5H). To further study whether the stimulated BMDMs could more effectively prime T cells, stimulated BMDMs were loaded with ovalbumin (OVA) and co-cultured with OT-I mouse derived-CD8+ T cells or OT-II mouse derived-CD4+ T cells, respectively. BMDMs treated with the supernatants of OV-mIFN-γ, OV-mGM-CSF, or OV-5A-infected Pan02 cells can activate both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Figures 5I and 5J) and CD8+ T cell secretion of IFN-γ (Figure 5K). Collectively, these in vitro assays recapitulate different aspects of the activity of OV-5A treatment.

OV-5A treatment promoted T cell activation and repolarization of macrophages in patient-derived tumor organoids, tumor tissue sections, and PDXs

To study whether the potential efficacy of OVs can be translated to human patients, OVs expressing human CXCL16, CCL5, IFN-γ, IL-12, or GM-CSF were constructed. To evaluate the effects of armed OVs on T cells and macrophages-mediated tumor killing, organoids derived from two colon cancer patients were infected with unmodified OV or a mixture of human-version single-gene modified OVs (OV-h5A) and co-cultured with the carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE)-stained autologous tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) or THP-1-derived macrophages. When the organoids were co-cultured with TILs alone, T cells were excluded from the organoids. In the presence of both OV-h5A and TIL, T cell infiltration in tumor organoids was significantly increased and tumor organoids were completely lysed (Figures 6A, 6B, S15A, and S15B). Similarly, macrophages plus OV-h5A treatment exhibited robust cytotoxicity against the organoids (Figures 6C, 6D, S15C, and S15D). The levels of human CCL5, CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF were much higher in the OV-h5A group than those in the PBS and unmodified OV group (Figures 6E and S15E).

To bridge this gap between the pre-clinical models and the heterogeneous architecture of the human tumors, 200-μm slices were generated from patient tumor tissues using vibratome and treated with OV or OV-h5A (Figure 6F). OV-h5A treatment induced more tumor cell apoptosis than the same titer of OV, suggesting that OV-h5A induces an antitumor immune response in addition to its oncolysis effect (Figures 6G and S15F). Flow cytometry analysis of the immune cells in the slices showed that treatment with OV-h5A upregulated CD69, CD25, and IFN-γ on CD8+ T cells and increased the ratio of CD80+ macrophages-to-CD163+ macrophages (Figures 6H, 6I, S15G–S15J). Despite the limited number of patient samples used here, the results suggested that OV-h5A represents a potential therapy in human patients.

To evaluate the efficacy of OV-h5A in a more clinically relevant mouse model, we established a PBMC-PDX model of oral cancer, as previously reported, and the mice were intravenously transplanted with human PBMCs for immune reconstitution (Figure 6J). Considering the occurrence of graft-versus-host disease in the PBMC model, we monitored tumor growth for 2 weeks. Treatment with OV-h5A inhibited tumor growth more effectively than OV treatment (Figure 6K), and body weight was not significantly changed with OV-h5A treatment (Figure 6L).

The scRNA-seq data-guided design of combination therapy to enhance the efficacy of OV-5A

Despite that OV-5A significantly delayed tumor growth in all treated mice, about half of the treated mice have not been cured. Therefore, we performed scRNA-seq analysis on tumor-infiltrating immune cells of the CR group and weak PR group after OV-5A treatment (Figure 7A).

The CRs of OV-5A treatment were characterized by significantly fewer Treg cells, TAMs, and DCs in tumors compared to the PRs (Figure 7B). Pathway analysis revealed that the M2-like macrophage-related pathways, such as oxidative phosphorylation and response to IL-4, were upregulated in macrophages of the PR group compared with the CR group (Figure 7C). Analysis of immune checkpoint expression showed that PD-1 was the most upregulated among the immune checkpoint receptors on CD8+ T cells of the PR group compared to the CR group (Figure 7D). Programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression was upregulated in tumor cells and myeloid cells upon OV treatment (Figure 7E), which can in turn engage PD-1 on T cells to induce exhaustion of T cells.

The scRNA-seq data revealed that the antigen presentation pathway was upregulated in the CR group compared to the PR group (Figure 7C), which was also upregulated in the responders to the unmodified OV treatment compared to the non-responders. These data highlight that DC maturation was a critical factor in the antitumor efficacy of OVs. It is reported that the combination of Flt3L and agonistic CD40 antibody led to enhanced DC maturation and reduced tumor burden. Therefore, CD40 agonist antibody (αCD40) and a murine Flt3L-armed OV (OV-mFlt3L) were combined to inhibit tumor growth (Figure 7F) by enhancing DC maturation in tumor DCs and tumor-draining lymph nodes (TdLNs) (Figures 7G and 7H).

The combination of OV-5A with PD-1 antagonist antibody (αPD-1), αCD40, and OV-mFlt3L, was evaluated in Pan02, MC38, and B16-F10 models. The combination of OV-5A, αPD-1, αCD40, and OV-mFlt3L resulted in the most remarkable effect on inhibiting tumor growth and achieved the highest cure rate in all three models (Figures 7I–7K). In conclusion, the combination therapy of OV-5A, αPD-1, αCD40, and OV-mFlt3L resulted in high CR rates in multiple tumor models.

Discussion

OVs hold great potential in cancer immunotherapy. An in-depth understanding of resistance mechanisms to OV can facilitate the development of rational combinations and the next generation of OV designs. The research originated from our curiosity as to why some model mice are non-responsive to OV treatment. With this question in mind, we analyzed the omics data and confirmed the distinguished expression of several factors. Thereafter, we further modified the OV as an omic-guided approach and proof-of-concept study to pursue higher efficacy of OV.

Although the modification of OVs with various genes such as CCL5, IL-12, and IFN-γ has been reported, it is a challenging task to identify the optimal combination of genes for OV modification from an increasing number of genes due to the “combination explosion” problem. Potential tactics were proposed to engineer OVs to target crucial cell-signaling pathways in cancer and immune cells for the development of productive antitumor T cell immunity. According to our knowledge, the OV ONCR-177, containing IL-12, Flt3L, CCL4, and antagonists to PD-1 and CTLA-4, represented the OV containing the largest number of transgenes. The 5 transgenes were selected by combination screen of 14 candidate genes for in vivo efficacy. As the number of candidate genes increases, the problem of finding the best combination of genes becomes intractable to the brute force solutions. With the emergence of scRNA-seq technologies, we can study the landscape of TIME (e.g., the TIME of different responsiveness). Moreover, the scRNA-seq data help us to identify innovative and critical pathways associated with the response to OV treatment. For example, the CXCL16-CXCR6 axis plays a critical role in generating antitumor immunity and is upregulated in the responders, leading to the modification of OV with CXCL16. As far as we know, this is the initial report of CXCL16-modified OV.

According to the cancer-immunity cycle theory, an orderly sequence of events generates anticancer immune responses, including presentation of cancer antigens by DCs, priming and activation of T cells, infiltration of T cells into tumors and stroma, recognition of tumor by T cells, and killing of cancer cells by T cells. The genes used to modify the OV in this study can enhance different steps of the cycle. For example, Flt3L and CD40 agonists enhance DC maturation, and CXCL16 and CCL5 enhance CD8+ and CD4+ T cell infiltration. Accordingly, the timing of each cytokine expression may influence the therapeutic outcome of OV. A drug-inducible system would allow the impact of the timing of cytokine expression on efficacy to be studied. This would be particularly useful when combined with tumor-specific expression of transgenes using a tumor-specific promoter or microRNA, allowing full spatiotemporal control.

We conducted an iterative analysis of scRNA-seq data from tumor-infiltrating immune cells in both CRs and weak PRs following treatment with OV-5A. The pronounced distinctions between the two groups have provided a clear roadmap for the development of a synergistic combination therapy to significantly enhance antitumor efficacy. Our analysis revealed a significant upregulation of antigen presentation capacity within DCs of the CR group compared to the PR group. It is reported that OVs engineered to carry trimerized CD40L or Flt3L demonstrated the potential to bolster the antigen presentation capabilities of dendritic cells to enhance the antitumor T cell response. These studies supported our findings, underscoring the pivotal role of antigen presentation in achieving favorable outcomes from OV treatment.

While a single OV discovery and development campaign cannot exhaustively target every aspect of tumor immunity, our research has demonstrated that OVs can be iteratively improved by evaluating current OVs and subsequently fine-tuning the OV in response to varying levels of response observed in mice.

Although our study represents an initial proof-of-concept within a preclinical model, it lays the groundwork for future patient-centric research of our therapeutic OV candidates and sparks the imagination for a more dynamic drug development process in patients. Recently, an increasing number of patient-centered research studies have been reported. First, the clinicians will generate multidimensional patient genomic data linked to clinical response variables, providing a rich source for analysis and hypothesis generation. Second, researchers will formulate testable hypotheses based on the differences in cell populations, gene expression, and pathway regulation between responding and non-responding patients and work backwards from clinical observations to uncover the underlying mechanisms that drive treatment response using appropriate mouse models. Finally, the insights gained from the basic research will guide the development of innovative immunotherapies or provide biomarkers for stratifying the patients most likely to respond to OV therapy.

There are some directions to explore in the future. Our study focused on the interactions between immune cells, while the interactions between the non-immune cells (e.g., stromal fibroblasts, tumor cells) and the immune cells should also be investigated. For example, we found that OX40L-armed OV infected cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) and stimulated CAFs to secrete IL-6, which inhibited anticancer immune responses. Flt3L and OX40L-modified oncolytic vaccinia virus-infected TAMs, resulting in the activation of the cyclic guanosine monophosphate-adenosine monophosphate synthase/stimulator of interferon genes pathway to activate CD8+ and CD4+ T cells. Besides studying the tumor microenvironment, an emerging appreciation for the potential implication of TdLNs in immunotherapy suggested that the status of TdLNs may be associated with heterogeneous patient outcomes, and understanding the role of TdLNs in OV treatment could provide additional guidance to design OV.

In summary, we identified the defining features of the TIME associated with response to OV, leading us to design more effective oncolytic virotherapy OV-5A and its combination regimens. This work laid a foundation for accelerating the development of cancer immunotherapies such as OV and their combination regimens in preclinical models and patients.

Materials and methods

Cell lines

The HEK293FT-single-guide sgRNA GFP cell line was established by the introduction of CRISPR-Cas9 and two GFP-targeting sgRNAs. The Pan02 and B16-F10 cell lines were Pan02- or B16-F10-derived cells that are susceptible to HSV-1 infection and created by the introduction of human HVEM or Nectin1 into the parental cells using lentivirus. From the published work using B78H1 murine melanoma cells expressing human HVEM or human Nectin1, the efficacy of HSV1716 treatment in both derived models exhibited equivalent outcomes in the aspect of immunology features. The susceptibility of each cell line to OV was confirmed by infecting the cells with GFP encoding OV.

The Tet-On 3G cell lines were constructed using Lenti-X Tet-On 3G Inducible Expression System (631847, Takara, Kyoto, Japan). The Tet-On 3G cell lines (e.g., MC38-Tet-On-luciferase cell line) contained a constitutively expressed Tet-On 3G transactivator protein and the genes of interest under the control of the tetracycline-inducible promoter. Expression of the gene of interest is activated by adding DOX (T1687L, TargetMol, Boston, MA) to the culture medium or giving DOX to the cell-bearing mice.

MC38 cells were cultivated with DMEM medium (6123166, Gibco, MA, USA) supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) (A5669701, Gibco, Waltham, MA) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin (P/S, PB180120, Procell, Shanghai, China). The Tet-On 3G cell lines were grown in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS (C2720, FBS-tetracycline, Vivacell, Shanghai, China) and 1% P/S. Pan02- and B16-F10-derived cells were grown in RPMI-1640 medium (90023, Solarbio, Beijing, China) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% P/S. HEK293FT, HEK293T, HEK293FT-sgGFP, and Vero cells were cultured with DMEM medium enriched with 10% FBS (164210, Procell, Shanghai, China) and 1% P/S.

Animals

The animal use for research was authorized by the Animals Ethical and Welfare Committee at Nankai University (2021-SYDWLL-000125). Female wild-type C57BL/6J mice, aged 6–8 weeks, were acquired from Vital River Laboratories (Beijing, China). OT-I, OT-II, and C-NKG mice were purchased from Cyagen Biosciences (Santa Clara, CA). hCD40 mice (catalog no. NM-HU-00076) were purchased from Shanghai Model Organisms Center.

OV modification

The basic OV-GFP was constructed based on the wild-type HSV-1, which was modified by replacing both copies of the ICP34.5-coding regions with the GFP gene and deleting the ICP47 gene by CRISPR-Cas9-mediated editing of the HSV-1 genome. For single-gene-modified OVs, the individual genes were inserted into ICP34.5 loci. For OV-5A, the murine IFN-γ, CXCL16, GM-CSF, and IL-12 genes were assembled with the T2A or P2A sequence and inserted between the UL26 and UL27 loci, and murine CCL5 was inserted in the ICP34.5 loci. The donor plasmid contained the gene of interest flanked by ICP34.5 or UL26- and UL27-derived homology recombination arms. Briefly, HEK293FT-sgGFP cells were transfected with the donor plasmids, followed by infection with OV-GFP at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.1 the next day, and SCR7 (HY-12742, MCE, NJ) was added to enhance the homology-directed repair. At 48 h post-infection (hpi), the recombinant virus containing supernatants was collected and diluted supernatants were added to Vero cells to form plaques. The plaques were picked and characterized by PCR and ELISA. The monoclonal recombinant viruses were obtained after five rounds of plaque purification.

OVs preparations

OVs were added to Vero cells of 70% confluency at an MOI of 0.1. Once a substantial level of cell cytopathic effect was detected around 48–72 hpi, the supernatant was collected, and cellular debris was eliminated via centrifugation at 4,000 rpm for 10 min. The collected supernatant was filtered with a 0.45-μm filter and further purified with column HiScreen Capto Core 700 (17-5481-15, GE Healthcare, Piscataway, NJ). The purified virus was concentrated in ultrafiltration tubes, and the aliquoted virus was stored at −80°C.

ELISA

Pan02 cells were infected with OV, and the supernatants were collected and centrifuged to remove cellular debris. To detect the production of cytokines in tumor sites following OV treatment, tumor tissues were dissected and homogenized in PBS containing protease inhibitor cocktail (K1007, ApexBio, Houston, TX). The concentration of individual cytokine was measured with the ELISA kits (all from Elabscience, Wuhan, China): mCCL5 (E-EL-M0009), mCXCL16 (E-EL-M0270), mIFN-γ (E-EL-M0048), mIL-12 (E-EL-M3062), mGM-CSF (E-EL-M0032), hCCL5 (E-EL-H6179), hCXCL16 (E-EL-H6009), hIFN-γ (E-EL-H0108), hIL-12 (E-EL-H0150), and hGM-CSF (E-EL-H0081).

Production of cell lines using Tet-On system

The pLVX-TetOne-Puro Vector plasmid (631847) was purchased from Takara, in which the Puro gene was substituted with the GFP and the candidate gene of interest, including murine CCL4, CCL5, CCL22, CXCL9, CXCL10, CXCL16, IL-2, IL-10, IL-18, CSF-1, and IFN-γ to generate the pLVX-TetOne-GOI-GFP plasmids. Next, HEK293FT cells were transfected with each pLVX-TetOne-GOI-GFP plasmid together with the packaging plasmids psPAX2 and pMD2.G. Following a 48-h co-transfection, a supernatant containing the lentivirus was collected and subsequently concentrated. To establish the cell lines inducibly expressing the desired genes, the lentivirus was used to transduce the Pan02 or MC38 cells, and GFP+ cells were sorted by flow cytometry using the BD FACSAria Fusion instrument at 24 h post-transduction. To validate the system, the MC38-TetOne-luciferase cell line was generated, and C57BL/6J mice were inoculated with 1 × 106 MC38-TetOne-luciferase cells. When tumors reached a range of 80–100 mm3, mice were given drinking water containing 2 mg/mL DOX. After 12 h, bioluminance was monitored using IVIS Spectrum. The bioluminance was also monitored 24 h after discontinuing DOX.

Mouse experiments

For the syngeneic tumor models, 5 × 105 Pan02 cells, 1 × 106 MC38 tumor cells, or 5 × 105 B16-F10 cells were subcutaneously inoculated into the flanks of C57BL/6J mice. When tumors reached approximately 50 mm3, mice were i.t. injected with three doses of 5 × 106 plaque-forming units (pfu) OV or 5 × 106 pfu OV-5A every 3 days. Three days after the last treatment, tumors were harvested for scRNA-seq and flow cytometry analysis of tumor-infiltrating immune cells, measuring the level of intratumoral cytokines using Luminex multiple cytokine immunoassays and bulk RNA-seq of the whole tumors.

In the immune cells depletion study, we administered CD4 antibody (300 μg/dose, BE0003-1, Bio X Cell, Lebanon, NH), CD8a antibody (300 μg/dose, BE0004-1, Bio X Cell), NK1.1 antibody (200 μg/dose, BE0036, Bio X Cell), or CSF1R antibody (500 μg/dose, BE0213, Bio X Cell) via intraperitoneal (i.p.) injection three times 24 h prior to OV or OV-5A treatment.

To screen the genes that can augment the efficacy of OV, 5 × 105 Pan02-TetOne-cytokine cells were subcutaneously injected into the left flanks of C57BL/6J mice (n = 6/group). Once the tumor reached approximately 50 mm3, mice were given DOX containing water and i.t. injected with three doses of 5 × 106 pfu OV or PBS every 3 days. Tumor size was measured every 2 or 3 days.

To evaluate the antitumoral efficacy of OV-six, OV-five, or OV-5A, 5 × 105 Pan02 cells, 1 × 106 MC38 cells, or 5 × 105 B16-F10 cells were injected into the flanks of C57BL/6J mice (N = 6–10/group), respectively. When tumors reached approximately 50 mm3, mice were i.t. injected with PBS, 5 × 106 pfu OV, 5 × 106 pfu OV-six (an equal amount mixture of OV-mCCL5, OV-mCXCL16, OV-mIFN-γ, OV-mIL-2, OV-mIL-12, and OV-mGM-CSF), OV-six minus one (which contained all minus one single-gene modified OV), 5 × 106 pfu OV-five (an equal amount mixture of OV-mCCL5, OV-mCXCL16, OV-mIFN-γ, OV-mIL-12, and OV-mGM-CSF), or OV-five minus one (which contained all minus one single-gene modified Ovs) every 3 days for a total of three treatments. Tumor size was measured every 2 or 3 days throughout the experiment.

In the toxicity detection study, serum samples were collected from Pan02 tumor-bearing mice treated with PBS, OV, or OV-5A at 24, 48, and 72 h following the final treatment, and the concentrations of ALT (JL12668, Jianglai, Shanghai, China) and AST (JL13992, Jianglai) were detected.

In bilateral tumor models, 5 × 105 Pan02 cells were implanted on both sides of mice. When tumors reached approximately 50 mm3, the left flank was injected with either PBS, 5 × 106 pfu OV or 5 × 106 pfu OV-5A every 3 days for a total of three treatments. The growth of the bilateral tumors was monitored throughout the experiment.

In the rechallenged study, a cohort of mice (n = 7–10) that had previously been cured by OV-5A treatment was rechallenged with 5 × 105 Pan02 cells on the 80th day following the initial tumor cell inoculation.

To evaluate the antitumor effects of individual-gene-modified OVs, including OV-mCCL5, OV-mCXCL16, OV-mIFN-γ, OV-mIL-2, OV-mIL-12, and OV-mGM-CSF, Pan02 cells were subcutaneously inoculated into C57BL/6J mice. When tumors reached approximately 50–100 mm3, tumor-bearing mice received i.t. injections of either PBS or 5 × 106 pfu OVs every 3 days three times. The tumor volume was measured every 3 days.

To evaluate the antitumor effects of combining OV-5A with anti-PD-1 antibody (BE0146, Bio X Cell) and/or OV-mFlt3L/αCD40, Pan02, MC38, and B16-F10 cells were subcutaneously inoculated into CD40-humanized mice. When tumors reached approximately 100–150 mm3, large-sized tumor-bearing mice received i.t. injections of either PBS, 6 × 106 pfu OV-5A, or 5 × 106 pfu OV-5A plus 1 × 106 pfu OV-mFlt3L every 3 days three times. Additionally, mice were treated with i.p. injections of 100 μg αPD-1 or 150 μg αCD40 called NK003 per mouse every 3 days for three doses. The tumor volume was measured and calculated with the formula length × width2/2.

Intratumoral and serum cytokine profile

Tumor-bearing mice were treated with PBS or 5 × 106 pfu OVs, and tumor tissues and blood were collected from both the responders and non-responders at 3 days post-treatment. Radioimmunoprecipitation assay lysate (equivalent to 10 mg or 100–200 μL lysate) was added to the tumor tissues, followed by grinding using a tissue homogenizer. The resulting lysate was centrifuged at 3,000 × g for 10 min, and the protein concentration was quantified using the bicinchoninic acid assay. Two multiple-cytokine immunoassays of LBX-MultiDTM-31 (64412017, Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) and LX-MultiDTM-23 (LXSAMSM-23, Bio-Rad) were used to measure levels of cytokines and chemokines in tumor homogenates and serum. This experiment was performed by the LabEx Multifactor Group of Shanghai Univision Biotechnology.

Tumor-infiltrating CD45+ immune cells preparation, library preparation, and construction for scRNA-seq

The NovaSeq 6000 (Illumina, San Diego, CA) was employed for sequencing of the constructed library.

Flow cytometry

The stained cells were analyzed using the BD LSR Fortessa X-20 flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, San Diego, CA), and the data analysis was performed using FlowJo version10 software.

Distribution of viruses in tissues

The tumor, liver, spleen, kidney, and lung tissues were collected and then homogenized. The quantity of HSV glycoprotein D gene copies in the extracted DNA was measured by qPCR using AceQ qPCR SYBR Green Master Mix GoScript (Q111-02, Vazyme, Nanjing, China).

Identification of cell types and cluster marker genes in Seurat

Seurat (version 4.0.5) was used to input gene barcode matrices for quality checking and subsequent analysis. Cells of low quality, defined as having fewer than 100 genes, more than 6,000 genes, more than 25,000 unique molecular identifiers, or more than 10% mitochondria per cell, were eliminated from the analysis. The data were subsequently standardized, and genes with significant variability were identified using the FindVariableFeatures tool. Principal-component analysis was used to decrease the number of dimensions in the scRNA-seq dataset. The downstream analysis was conducted with the top 58 main components, which accounted for 90% of the variation in the dataset. The batch correction was conducted with the Harmony package (version 1.0). The FindClusters function was employed to identify cell clusters with a resolution of 0.2. The data were subsequently represented using uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) plots. Every group of cells was classified into a recognized biological cell type through manual categorization. The FindMarkers function was run to identify cluster-specific marker genes or differentially expressed genes (DEGs) within a specific cluster. The average expression of the genes within each cluster was calculated, and heatmaps illustrating the mean gene expression were generated using the R package ComplexHeatmap (version 2.8.0). For samples labeled with hashtag antibodies and mixed for an scRNA-seq experiment, the data for each sample can be assigned by the demultiplexing tool, and statistical analysis is performed.

Pathway enrichment analysis

To elucidate the biological implications of the DEGs and marker genes, GO analysis was performed. Fisher’s exact test was applied to identify the significant GO categories, and a false discovery rate (FDR) was used to correct the p values.

WebGestalt was employed for conducting gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA), aiming to detect enriched pathways as annotated by the GO, Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes, and Reactome databases. Genes were ranked based on the log2(fold change) calculated by running FindMarkers in Seurat, and the size of each pathway was limited to 500. The FDR for the GSEA analysis was assessed by performing 1,000 permutations.

Cell communication network construction

R package CellChat (version 1.1.3) was utilized for forecasting the potential cellular interactions among the identified cell clusters derived from Seurat analysis. Two distinct CellChat objects were created for the non-responders and responders to OV treatment by executing the createCellChat function with the corresponding Seurat objects. The construction of cell communication networks involved the use of computeCommunProb, computeCommunProbPathway, and aggregate net functions. Comparative analysis of interactions was carried out using the compareInteractions, and the RankNet function was utilized to compare the information flow for each signaling pathway. This flow is determined by the sum of communication probabilities among all pairs of cell groups within the inferred network. The netVisual_aggregate functions were used to circle plots depicting the inferred ligand-receptor interactions or signaling network of signaling pathways between different cell clusters in each treatment group.

Statistical analysis

The statistical analysis in this study was performed using GraphPad Prism version 8 software. The data are presented as the mean (±SEM). Statistical significance was determined by unpaired Student’s t test, Wilcoxon test, or one-way ANOVA test. The data on mice survival were presented using Kaplan-Meier survival curves, and the log rank test was used to assess the statistical significance among groups. A p value below 0.05 was deemed to be statistically significant and determined as ns, not significant; ∗p < 0.05; ∗∗p < 0.01; ∗∗∗p < 0.001; ∗∗∗∗p < 0.0001. The T/C% represents the relative rate of tumor proliferation. A T/C% value below 40% is generally regarded as a sign of an effective therapeutic response. The T/C% was calculated as follows: T/C% = (TRTV/CRTV) × 100%, where Vt is the tumor volume after treatment, V0 is the tumor volume before treatment, RTV is the relative tumor volume (RTV = Vt/V0), TRTV is the RTV in the treatment group, and CRTV is the RTV in the control group.
