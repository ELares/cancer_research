# Do tunneling nanotubes drive chemoresistance in solid tumors and other malignancies?

## Abstract

Intercellular communication within the tumor microenvironment (TME) is essential for establishing, mediating, and synchronizing cancer cell invasion and metastasis. Cancer cells, individually and collectively, react at the cellular and molecular levels to insults from standard-of-care treatments used to treat patients with cancer. One form of cell communication that serves as a prime example of cellular phenotypic stress response is a type of cellular protrusion called tunneling nanotubes (TNTs). TNTs are ultrafine, actin-enriched contact-dependent forms of membrane protrusions that facilitate long distance cell communication through transfer of various cargo, including genetic materials, mitochondria, proteins, ions, and various other molecules. In the past 5-10 years, there has been a growing body of evidence that implicates TNTs as a novel mechanism of cell-cell communication in cancer that facilitates and propagates factors that drive or enhance chemotherapeutic resistance in a variety of cancer cell types. Notably, recent literature has highlighted the potential of TNTs to serve as cellular conduits and mediators of drug and nanoparticle delivery. Given that TNTs have also been shown to form in vivo in a variety of tumor types, disrupting TNT communication within the TME provides a novel strategy for enhancing the cytotoxic effect of existing chemotherapies while suppressing this form of cellular stress response. In this review, we examine current understanding of interplay between cancer cells occurring via TNTs, and even further, the implications of TNT-mediated tumor-stromal cross-talk and the potential to enhance chemoresistance. We then examine tumor microtubes, an analogous cell protrusion heavily implicated in mediating treatment resistance in glioblastoma multiforme, and end with a brief discussion of the effects of radiation and other emerging treatment modalities on TNT formation.

Do tunneling nanotubes drive chemoresistance in solid tumors and other malignancies?

Across the spectrum of physiology, intercellular networking between cells is a vital and inherent aspect of biology. Multiple forms of such cell communication exist, and are amplified or dysregulated in pathophysiologic conditions, and quite prominently in cancer. Intercellular communication between cancer cells plays a critical role in tumor progression and resistance to standard and novel therapeutic strategies alike. In the dynamic and constantly changing tumor microenvironment (TME), long and short-range communication allows for exchange of genetic material, growth and signaling factors, organelles, and other molecules that ultimately amplify signals that induce downstream effects leading to tumor growth and metastasis. Soluble factors such as hormonal signals and cytokines have been well identified and studied extensively for decades. Forms of communication dependent on membranous contact include connexin channel-lined gap junctions and extracellular vesicles (EVs) have also been well established as primary players in intercellular communication. In the past two decades, the role of membranous cell extensions has become more clearly identified as a complementary, if not equally important, mode of cell-cell communication in cancer. Tunneling nanotubes (TNTs) are a prototype for this newly emerging class of cancer cell communication mediators; this form of long-distance communication was first reported in 2004 by Rustom et al.. TNTs are membranous protrusions primarily composed of filamentous actin (F-actin) and are differentiated from other actin-based protrusions in that they are suspended above the substratum when cultured in vitro, and operate more as channels of transfer rather than mediators of cell motility. The primary role of TNTs is to act as membranous conduits physically connecting two or more cells (as long as 500 µm in length, or roughly 10-fold the diameter of many cancer cells) and are capable of transporting a variety of cytoplasmic or membrane-derived cargo, including proteins, genetic material, cell surface receptors, and organelles such as mitochondria (Figure 1). While TNTs are a natural means of cell-cell communication in the context of normal physiology, their potential roles in context of pathophysiology have attracted increased interest among researchers focused on disease states, including cancer. Generally, they are found in a variety of cell types, including neurons and fibroblasts, but what makes them prominent in cancer is the fact that they are highly up-regulated in cancer cells.

Our group and many others have contributed to a growing body of evidence that while TNTs do form under normal physiologic conditions, they are especially prominent in number and function after forming during physiological stress response; for example, they are quantitatively increased in number under conditions of metabolic stress, including hypoxia, and also conditions of the microenvironment characteristic of cancer, including altered pH or glucose. A variety of proteins, including M-sec (TNFaip2, or tumor necrosis factor α-inducing protein 2), Connexin-43 (Cx43), and others are implicated in maintaining the delicate structure of TNTs, allowing them to transport various cargo. However, just as TME as a whole is highly dynamic over tumor space and time, TNTs are likewise finite structures, often forming, performing transfer of cargo, and disengaging within a span of minutes to hours. Thus, structural integrity of TNTs can be both flexible and labile, and provide one among many reasons that they can be difficult to identify and follow in vivo. Knowledge to date has provided a landscape of TNTs as being a component of communication involved in propagating chemoresistance in vitro and in vivo. Here, we discuss mechanisms of chemoresistance in multiple cancer types through the lens of TNT biology, and how the intersection of these two factors has important implications for potential future therapeutic strategies in solid tumor malignancies.

There are multiple mechanisms for chemoresistance in TNTs, with primary questions to be answered: do TNTs directly mediate chemotherapeutic drug resistance through unique TNT-specific mechanisms, or do they simply provide a conduit for propagating existing cancer cell drug resistance mechanisms via direct cell-cell communication? Our group was initially able to discern an explanation for the latter, by initially observing an up-regulation of TNTs following exposure to chemotherapeutic doxorubicin, a sentinel observation also confirmed by other groups, that collectively paved the way for investigation of TNT formation and function as a form of cancer cell stress response. We investigated the possibility that TNTs could not only act as conduits for intercellular transfer of growth signals, but also as a conduit for efflux and thus redistribution of chemotherapeutic drugs. Using pancreatic adenocarcinoma cell lines (Figure 2), we discovered that TNT formation increased proportionally to increased doxorubicin exposure; in turn, these TNTs were capable of redistributing this drug from drug-resistant cells to drug-sensitive cancer cells. Doxorubicin is relatively unique among drugs in that it is autofluorescent and can be tracked using fluorescence microscopy; taking advantage of this property, we visualized bidirectional transfer of doxorubicin via time-lapse microscopy. This finding was confirmed in an ovarian cancer cell model, in which we observed transfer of drug from drug-resistant to drug-sensitive ovarian cancer cells. Interestingly, in one such example captured on time-lapse, over the course of several hours multiple TNTs formed, and transport of drug from a resistant to sensitive cell was followed by involution and apparent apoptosis of the recipient sensitive cell. This observation provided support for the notion that invasive, treatment-resistant cells might form and then leverage TNTs in order to induce a novel form of drug efflux following therapeutic insult — again, as a cellular reaction and stress response — in the process decreasing the surrounding population of drug-sensitive, and potentially less invasive, cells, and enrich its own population of drug-resistant counterparts. On the other hand, it should also be noted that TNTs facilitated the distribution of chemotherapy to a greater number of sensitive cancer cells, which would theoretically carry important implications stemming from reducing this subpopulation in tumors in a clinical context. Overall, the above line of investigation opened avenues and a new key question regarding the role of TNTs in chemoresistance: Do drug-resistant cells initiate TNT formation (donor cell potential) and mainly detect and connect to drug-sensitive (recipient) cells? In sum, these findings provide opportunity for further development of TNTs as a novel form of drug efflux that could lead directly to a more chemoresistant TME. In context of our previous report that homotypic interactions via TNTs tend to be more common among ovarian cancers (resistant-to-resistant cell TNTs, sensitive-to sensitive), then it appears that resistant-to-sensitive cell connections may be especially potent given that they occur less frequently.

The potential role of TNTs in chemoresistance is by no means limited to solid tumors. One can imagine that the paradigm can readily apply to biology of hematologic malignancies as well. Omsland et al. found that treatment of acute myeloid leukemia cells with cytarabine, a pyrimidine analog used in chemotherapy, down-regulated rather than increased the formation of TNTs, in contrast with our group's findings in pancreatic cancer cells. These investigators found that cytarabine in particular is well known to down-regulate the NF-kB pathway compared with other chemotherapeutic modalities and that the NF-kB pathway may be important in TNT formation. TNTs can have tumor-stromal effects within the bone marrow microenvironment, as observed in studies demonstrating downstream TNT-induced cytokine-induced cascades in acute lymphoblastic leukemic cells. Polak et al. showed that one effect included resistance to prednisolone, adding additional evidence to the repertoire implicating TNTs in cancer treatment resistance. Given the sum of results from these and other studies, one may postulate that although all chemotherapeutic drugs induce a cellular stress response, some may have stronger secondary effects depending on their individual mechanisms in relation to TNT formation.

TNTs have also been implicated in propagating drug resistance in chronic myeloid leukemia (CML), through both stromal and leukemic cell involvement. Previously, CML cells had been shown to be protected from imatinib-induced apoptosis when co-cultured with stromal cells, primarily through Wnt and N-cadherin signaling. To determine the potential role of TNT-mediated intercellular communication in this process, Kolba et al. tested the ability of TNTs to increase resistance to imatinib separately. They detected direct transfer of proteins implicated in cancer cell survival through CLEM tomography by confirming TNT formation between K562 (a CML cell line) and HS-5 (a bone marrow stromal cell line), then quantifying vesicular transfer, finding that transfer of vesicular cargo from HS-5 to K562 via TNTs was associated with decreased apoptosis. Transfer of over 351 proteins to K562 cells was quantified; many of these proteins were implicated in negative regulation of apoptotic signaling and drug efflux. Overall, the authors’ findings imply that TNTs may serve as conduits for propagating existing drug-resistance mechanisms. The exact mechanism by which this occurs remains unclear, though it is possible that it may involve enhancement of a resistant cell population through transport of cytotoxic drugs to sensitive cells. These findings open new avenues, and along with other studies in the field also opens a central question in the field of TNT biology overall: what filters exist, if any, for factors that are transferred via TNTs? Is there active regulation of entry and passage through TNTs, or is the process indiscriminate to the point where it may not be possible to ascertain cause-and-effect of observable changes following intercellular transfer of any given single or mix of molecular cargo? In the case of CML, there appears to be somewhat of a dependence on the surrounding stroma and transfer of proteins, suggesting the importance of the TME and tumor-stromal cross-talk in propagating tumor growth. This process may carry broader implications for clinical relevance of chemoresistance pending additional studies.

While transfer of specific proteins or drug redistribution via TNTs has been shown to propagate drug resistance, one of the most prominently and most frequently studied TNT-transferred cargo is mitochondria. This specific organelle has inherent properties that are of keen interest due to generation of cellular energy across physiology, in many pathophysiologic contexts most especially in cancer; and the fact that that they serve as reservoirs for genetic codes including microRNAs, and genetic material, makes the possibility of their cell-to-cell transfer even more intriguing. In the setting of cancer, and more specifically for implications on chemoresistance, this angle has been approached through study of multiple cancer types. Pasquier et al. provided one such early example of this process, through assessment of transfer of mitochondria between endothelial cells and ovarian cancer cell lines. Overall, the authors established that when ovarian cancer cells were co-cultured with endothelial stromal cells in the presence of doxorubicin, cells that received higher amounts of mitochondrial transfer compared with control groups were associated with higher cell viability, thus implying a possible mechanism for chemoresistance via mitochondrial transfer. However, this early study had not established a molecular explanation as to how mitochondrial transfer could enhance chemoresistance/cell survival. Other recent studies, however, such as the one described in Wang et al. suggest that mitochondrial transfer via TNTs in acute T-cell leukemia is primarily done to reduce reactive oxygen species (ROS) caused by chemotherapeutic drug exposure. In this study, the authors first established that Jurkat leukemic cells transfer mitochondria to mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs) via TNTs following exposure to chemotherapy. They subsequently discovered that inhibiting TNT formation, and thus mitochondria transfer, increased Jurkat cell apoptosis, suggesting that TNT communication is important for redistributing ROS. Burt et al. established a similar mechanism for the enhanced survival of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) cells in response to oxidative stress. As seen in other studies examining mitochondrial transfer and reduction of ROS, the authors found that cancer-associated fibroblasts were able to significantly reduce apoptosis in ALL cells by transferring mitochondria via TNTs and thus reducing ROS concentrations. Ultimately, this study was able to shed light on the ability of TNTs to redistribute ROS and decrease cytotoxic effect in agreement with other related findings involving mitochondrial ROS redistribution via TNTs.

Newer approaches, such as the one described by Nakhle et al., also establish the importance of mitochondria in mediating chemoresistance via TNTs, though the proposed mechanism seems to rely on directly altering metabolite production in cancer cells. The authors analyzed the relationship of mitochondrial transfer between glioblastoma stem cells (GSCs) and MSCs and the potentiation of resistance to temozolomide (TMZ), a standard-of-care chemotherapy in glioblastoma. The authors first established that transfer of MSC mitochondria to GSCs enhanced survival in response to TMZ treatment and that this transfer simultaneously enhances GSC energy and metabolism. Ultimately, they discovered that metabolic rewiring occurs following mitochondrial transfer through TNTs, isolating much of these metabolic effects to changes in levels of orotate, a precursor molecule to pyrimidines. When blocking orotate production following TMZ exposure, cell death significantly increased, establishing a metabolic mechanism for mediation of chemoresistance via mitochondrial transfer through TNTs. Overall, this study among others contributed to establishing the significance of mitochondria in mediating chemoresistance through multiple mechanisms. While TNTs are not the only source of mitochondrial transfer, they seem to facilitate cross-talk when gap-junction mediated communication is not possible. Admittedly, mitochondrial transfer through exosomal vesicles has also been recently established in mediating chemoresistance, and provides a potential confounding factor. What is needed in the field of TNT biology are methods that can, with high level of experimental integrity and specificity, separate effects of TNT-mediated transfer of cellular contents from downstream effects of transfer of the same or any other cargo occurring via EVs, or for that matter cell fusion and other modes of cell communication. More studies will be useful in addressing this important gap in knowledge in the field in order to move forward with understanding the extent and efficiency of TNT vs exosome communication in mediating chemoresistance.

In addition to their roles in directly propagating chemoresistance, recent publications have highlighted the possible paradoxical role of TNTs to serve as drug delivery channels. Can TNTs truly be exploited for this purpose? A comprehensive review by Ottonelli et al. illustrates the ability of TNTs to serve as drug delivery channels for nanomedicines (Nmeds), nanometric-sized delivery systems that can deliver most therapeutic agents at a greater efficacy than traditional pharmaceuticals. The authors further highlight that TNTs are able to transport inorganic, organic, and polymeric Nmeds. Interestingly, they also found that in some cases, transport of Nmeds between glial cells and neurons increased by as much as 25% when TNT formation was enhanced. These results are somewhat analogous to our lab's previous findings, in which we demonstrated an increase in TNT formation in response to chemotherapeutic drug exposure. It is likely that a two-way molecular relationship between TNT formation and amount of transported substrate exists, such that increases in TNT formation result in increased transportation of substrate and vice versa. In addition to these parameters, a study performed by Franco et al. demonstrates that TNTs that are thicker in diameter carry a higher nanoparticle transport rate, suggesting that perhaps thicker TNTs (defined as a diameter >700 nm) may have more stability at baseline and are preferentially used by cells for cargo transport. Optimization of nanoparticle delivery, especially as it relates to the dichotomy between ‘thick’ and ‘thin’ TNTs should be explored in further drug/nanoparticle delivery studies.

Having established the importance of TNTs in propagating drug resistance and facilitating drug delivery, it is important to recognize that various TNT subtypes and other membranous protrusions in a family of classifications exist, with similar functions. Tumor microtubes (TMs) are one such example of TNT-like structures. Like TNTs, TMs are primarily composed of F-actin, and have been shown to transfer various molecules, such as mitochondria and calcium. Notably, however, TMs are typically thicker than TNTs, averaging 1–2 µm in width, and tend to structurally last longer than TNTs on average. This structural disparity is not significantly different given the similar functions between TNTs and TMs, and could be explained by the fact that TMs are primarily found in astrocytomas, where there may be some structural homology to surrounding neurons. Indeed, recent studies have shown that certain subpopulations of glioblastoma cells form glutamate receptor-dependent synapses with neurons, and these synapses are often located on TMs. Additionally, TMs showed growth features that were morphologically analogous to axonal growth of neurons, further implicating a shared homology between TMs and the axons of neurons.

Regardless of structural differences between TNTs and TMs, TMs have also been heavily associated with cancer cell growth and invasion. Osswald et al. was able to demonstrate the significance of TMs in mediating a stress-induced survival response. Specifically, the authors found that when ablating glioma cells via laser irradiation, new TMs from surrounding viable glioma cells connected to ablated cells, and transfer of a new cell nucleus to the ablated cell was observed. Importantly, this repair mechanism was only rarely observed in non-TM connected cells. These findings suggested that, like TNTs, TMs formed in glioblastoma as a stress response to enhance cancer cell survival. In fact, TMs have also been implicated in enhancing cancer cell survival via resistance to chemotherapy and physical insults, such as surgical lesions. Weil et al. examined the relationship between TMs and treatment resistance in glioblastoma, where the standard chemotherapy treatment is TMZ, an alkylating agent. Resistance to TMZ is well-documented, with the primary driver of resistance known as the methylguanine-DNA methyltransferase protein. First, the authors established that, in response to surgical lesions, TMs contribute significantly to the glioma repair response, and this response is only temporarily inhibited by anti-inflammatory treatment. They then found that glioblastoma cells that were connected by a plethora of TMs had enhanced cell viability/resistance to TMZ compared with a control group with rare TM formation between cells. Future studies may elucidate the mechanism of resistance in TM-connected glioblastoma cells.

Resistance in chemotherapy via TNTs and TMs has been well documented. However, some studies have shown that TNTs and TMs may also be impacted by other non drug-based modalities of cancer-directed therapy, including application of Tumor-Treating Fields (TTFields) or ionizing radiation therapy. Our group studied the effects of TTFields — low-intensity alternating electric fields that are shown to disrupt cell division in cancer cells — on TNT formation in mesothelioma cells. Briefly, the primary proposed mechanism for TTFields is disruption of microtubule-based spindle formation, preventing cancer cell mitosis. Interestingly, our group found that application of a clinically relevant intensity of TTFields to mesothelioma cells significantly suppressed TNT formation in vitro. By applying spatial transcriptomic analysis to resected mesothelioma mice tumors treated with TTFields, we found that genes commonly associated with TNT formation were down-regulated, as was other genes associated with cancer cell survival/progression; in this study, confocal imaging also was used to demonstrate TNTs within regions of interest that are used for spatial transcriptomic assessment of regions of interest (ROI) within tumors, a new approach that we designated as ‘Spatial Profiling of Tunneling nanoTubes’. In combination with other studies highlighting the importance of TNTs in mediating cancer cell survival, these findings suggest that treatment using TTFields may serve as a novel form of ablating TNTs and thus reduce intercellular communication between cancer cells. In TMs, Osswald et al. discovered that up-regulation of TMs occurs in response to radiotherapy. Specifically, they found that glioblastoma cells were often able to escape the cytotoxic effects of radiotherapy by redistributing calcium to other glioma or stromal cells, reducing amounts of intracellular calcium within single cells, which would otherwise induce apoptosis. These findings corroborate multiple studies demonstrating the ability of TNTs to redistribute various molecules/chemotherapeutic drugs but also highlights the variability of the TNT/TM-radiation response.

In this brief review, we reviewed the prevalence and function of TNTs in a variety of settings, including in leukemia, mesothelioma, pancreatic cancer, and others. TNTs have been heavily implicated in mediating chemoresistance; the mechanism by which this occurs appears to be through facilitating existing resistance mechanisms or reducing cellular stress. Disruption of these intercellular networks or exploiting these cell-to-cell connections may serve as a novel form of enhancing current standard-of-care cancer therapies.

Perspectives

Intercellular communication is a crucial and inherently basic property of cells acting collectively in the ever-evolving dynamic TME. Identification and understanding of the function of TNTs in the microenvironmental niche has emerged as a new factor in this process that can explain how long-range cell-to-cell communication occurs between malignant cells, and also at the tumor-stromal interface.

TNTs are long membranous protrusions that can serve as direct contact-dependent connections between cancer cells in short- and long-range. Biologists are investigating the effects of TNT formation and function on enhancing cellular invasion and metastatic potential, with special focus on their role in chemoresistance and implications for the larger TME as a whole. This includes heterotypic (cancer-stromal) interactions as well as homotypic (cancer-cancer) connections and transfer of molecular or organelle transfer occurring via TNTs.

The field is moving rapidly in the direction of unraveling mechanisms of TNT formation, maintenance, and function, and determining whether and how TNTs may differ or have basic properties in common between differing cancer types, and also between cancer and other non-cancer pathophysiologic and also regular physiologic states.
