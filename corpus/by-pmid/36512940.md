# A hybrid nanoassembly for ultrasound-inducible cytosolic siRNA delivery and cancer sono-gene therapy.

## Abstract

Cancer gene therapy by small-interfering RNAs (siRNAs) holds great promise but is impeded by a low cytoplasmic delivery efficiency. The past two decades have witnessed many efforts that are dedicated to discover biomaterials in order to increase cellular uptake efficiency of siRNAs. However, less attention has been paid to the lysosomal trapping dilemma that greatly restricts gene silencing outcomes. Herein, to address this challenge, we developed a sono-controllable strategy for ultrasound-promoted cytosolic siRNA delivery. A hybrid nanoassembly (HNA) was prepared via electrostatic self-assembly of a siRNA and a nona-arginine modified with protoporphyrin IX that is a sonosensitizer. After cellular uptake and exposure to sono-irradiation, HNA generated singlet oxygen to facilitate the lysosomal escape of siRNA to knock down anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 in the cytoplasm. We showed that the colocalization ratios between siRNA and the lysosome decreased from 91 % to 33 % post sono-irradiation; meanwhile, the gene silencing efficacy increased from 46 % to 68 % at 300 nM of HNA. Furthermore, sonodynamic therapy was achieved by the sonosensitizer under ultrasound irradiation, which combined gene therapy to eradicate cancer cells, resulting in a cell death rate of 82 %. This study thus presents a novel ultrasonic approach for effective cytoplasmic delivery of siRNAs and combinational sono-gene therapy of cancer.

Introduction

Small-interfering RNAs (siRNAs) are promising anti-cancer agents, given their power to suppress the expression of undruggable oncogenes. However, siRNA-mediated cancer treatment suffers from an unsatisfactory potency. This arises mainly from poor efficacies of both cellular entry and lysosomal escape during the journey of siRNAs to reach cytoplasmic targets in cancer cells. The cellular impermeability of siRNAs results from the large size and polyanionic nature. To address this issue, various carriers, such as antibodies, peptides, lipids, polymers, and inorganic nanoparticles, have been developed. Despite cellular uptake has been greatly improved using these carriers, lysosomal trapping of the internalized siRNAs still remains a formidable challenge.

In order to facilitate the lysosomal escape of siRNAs following cellular entry, current methods mainly rely on the use of cationic polymers or peptides. These carriers can undergo protonation in the lysosome, which further induce osmotic rupture of the lysosomal membrane to facilitate a leakage of siRNAs trapped inside. Nevertheless, the use of a large amount of cationic carriers is usually associated with safety risks, and the lysosomal escape efficiency mediated by them is also insufficient. Therefore, to advance cancer gene therapy (GT), a facile and efficient strategy to augment cytosolic siRNA delivery is highly desirable.

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) has been validated to facilitate a lysosomal escape during drug delivery. When accumulated inside, ROS can efficiently rupture the lysosome membrane structure to elicit a leakage of trapped drugs. Since ROS can be generated by photosensitizers under light irradiation, we and others have previously demonstrated the use of photo-triggered ROS to accelerate the lysosomal escape of entrapped siRNAs. Nevertheless, issues regarding the phototoxicity and limited penetration depth of light still exist. Owing to its high non-invasiveness and deep penetration depth, ultrasound (US) is superior to light as an external stimulus, not to mention the spatiotemporal controllability. We thus envisioned that US in combination with sonosensitizers that can produce ROS under sono-irradiation might effectively promote cytosolic siRNA delivery to enhance GT. However, this possibility has not been explored.

We herein report a novel ultrasonic strategy to enhance cytosolic siRNA delivery (Scheme 1). We accomplish this by developing a sono-controllable hybrid nanoassembly (HNA). HNA self-assembles from a Bcl-2-targeting siRNA (siBcl-2) and a nona-arginine grafted with protoporphyrin IX (9R-PpIX) via electrostatic interaction. Bcl-2 is chosen as the target because of its overexpression in cancer cells and well-demonstrated anti-apoptosis function. 9R is a cationic cell-penetrating peptide that has a wide-ranging chemical flexibility and can condense and transport small RNAs. PpIX is an organic sonosensitizer possessing high ROS production and biosafety for sonodynamic therapy (SDT) study.

The working mechanism of HNA is proposed as follows. The nanoformulated HNA enters cancer cells via endocytosis. Under sono-irradiation, PpIX generates singlet oxygen (1O2) to rupture the lysosome to promote cytosolic delivery of siBcl-2, which subsequently cleaves Bcl-2 mRNA to exert GT. Meanwhile, following the liberation of PpIX from an aggregate status in HNA, its fluorescence emission and full potential of 1O2-generation are recovered. Thus, a second sono-irradiation is further applied to trigger SDT. As a result, HNA integrates GT with SDT to favor cancer cell apoptosis.

Materials and methods

General

We purchased the chemicals and solvents from Sigma-Aldrich or J&K Chemicals (Shanghai, China). The resins and amino acids were bought from GL Biochem (Shanghai, China). The DMEM (High-glucose Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium) and FBS (fetal bovine serum) were bought from ThermoFisher (Shanghai, China). We purchased the antibodies and protease inhibitor cocktail from Abcam and Cell Signaling (Shanghai, China), respectively. 5*SDS loading buffer and RIPA lysis buffer were bought from Solarbio (Beijing, China). We bought the annexin V-FITC/PI staining kit from BD Biosciences (Shanghai, China).

MALDI-TOF-MS was performed on ABSCIEX MALDI-TOF-TOF 4800 PLUS (AB Sciex, USA). The LC-ESI-MS was done on Agilent 6550 iFunnel Q-TOF LC/MS (Agilent, USA). We carried out HPLC on Agilent 1200 LC, and the eluent was CH3CN/H2O (1 ‰ CF3COOH). We measured the fluorescence spectra on a fluorescent spectrometer (RF-5301PC, Shimadzu, Japan). We measured the sizes and zetal potential of nanocomplexes on Brookhaven 90Plus Analyzer (Brookhaven, USA) and Malvern Nano-Z Analyzer (Malvern, UK), respectively. We performed transmission electron microscopy (TEM) imaging on transmission electron microscope (JEM-1011, JEOL, Ltd., Japan). Confocal fluorescence imaging was performed on a laser scanning focusing microscope (Olympus, Japan). Flow cytometry assay was done on FACSCalibur (BD, USA). Portable ultrasound instrument was purchased from Chattanooga (model No. CHA2776, USA, Fig. S1).

Synthesis and characterization

9R and 9R-PpIX

9R was synthesized according to a standard solid phase peptide synthesis (SPPS) protocol using 2-chorotrityl chloride resin and N-Fmoc-protected arginine. The resins were swelled in dry N, N-dimethylformamide (DMF) for 15 min. After that, Fmoc-Arg(Pbf)–OH (1.5 equiv.) was coupled to the free chlorine group on the resin. To remove Fmoc, the resins were treated with 20 % piperidine for 15 min. After wash by DMF for 3–5 times, Fmoc-Arg(Pbf)–OH (1.5 equiv.) was further coupled to the free amino group using HBTU as the coupling reagent. These two steps were repeated to elongate the peptide chain until 9R was formed. To obtain 9R-PpIX, protoporphyrin activated esters (3 equiv.) was loaded at the N-terminal of 9R using DIPEA. Then, the resins were cleaved with 95 % TFA (2.5 % ddH2O and 2.5 % triisopropylsilane) to get crude 9R or 9R-PpIX, which were further purified by reverse phase HPLC and lyophilized. MALDI-TOF-MS: 9R, calculated: 1424.72 [M + H]+; found: 1423.81; 9R-PpIX, calculated: 1969.37 [M + H]+, found 1969.91.

siBcl-2 and siNC

We synthesized siBcl-2, siNC, and Cy3-labeled siBcl-2 using a K&A H-8 DNA/RNA synthesizer. The sequences of siRNA are shown in Fig. S1. The solid support was CPG. We directly used Cy3 CE phosphonamidite as a monomer to synthesize 5′-Cy3-labeled siBcl-2 passenger strand. Following a standard solid-phase synthesis process, we purified siRNAs using HPLC with CH3CN/H2O (0.1 M CH3COONH+) as eluents (2 % to 40 % CH3CN in 30 min). The oligonucleotides were then desalted and lyophilized. After dispersion in DEPC-treated H2O, the concentrations of synthesized oligonucleotides were determined by NanoDrop 2000 (Thermo Scientific). The oligonucleotides were further characterized by LC-ESI-MS. Passenger strand of siBcl-2: calculated 6670; found 6670. Guide strand of siBcl-2: calculated 6647; found 6647. Passenger strand of siNC: calculated 6584; found 6584. Guide strand of siNC: calculated 6732; found 6733. 5′-Cy3-labeled passenger strand of siBcl-2: calculated 7302; found 7300.

Agarose gel electrophoresis assay

9R-PpIX and siBcl-2 were mixed at molar ratios ranging from 0:1 to 50:1. After standing at room temperature for 20 min, the mixtures were analyzed by 2 % agarose gels stained with ethidium bromide.

Preparation of nanoassemblies

HNA was prepared by incubating siBcl-2 with 9R-PpIX at a molar ratio of 1:30 for 20 min at room temperature. Similarly, NNA or CNA was prepared by incubating siNC with 9R-PpIX or siBcl-2 with 9R at a molar ratio of 1:30 for 20 min at room temperature.

Stability test

To study the stability, HNA was kept in PBS (pH = 7.4) at 37 ℃ for 0–7 days or in PBS with 10 % FBS at 37 ℃ for 0–72 h. The sizes were measured by DLS at indicated time points.

Measurement of 1O2-generation in vitro

HNA (50 nM) was mixed with SOSG (10 µM) in PBS. The mixture was then exposure to ultrasound irradiation (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle) for 1, 2, 3, 4, or 5 min. Fluorescence emission of SOSG at 530 nm was acquired with excitation at 488 nm.

Cell culture

HeLa cells were cultured in the culture medium consisting of DMEM with 10 % FBS and 1 % PS. The cells were maintained at 37 °C in 5 % CO2.

Measurement of 1O2-generation in living cells

1 × 105 HeLa cells were cultured on glass-bottom culture dishes. After reaching 60 % confluency, these cells were treated with HNA (300 nM). Cells were then washed using fresh medium for 3 times and incubated with H2DCFDA (20 μM) for 20 min. After 2 h, cells were treated with ultrasound irradiation for 5 min (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle). Or, cells were further exposure to a second ultrasound irradiation at 4 h time point. Then, cells were washed using PBS for 3 times. The fluorescence signals from H2DCFDA were measured under an Olympus fluorescent microscope. ImageJ was used to quantify the fluorescence intensities.

Confocal fluorescence imaging

To estimate the cellular uptake and lysosomal escape of HNA, 1 × 105 HeLa cells were cultured on glass-bottom culture dishes. After reaching 60 % confluency, these cells were treated with HNA (300 nM). To measure the disassembly and lysosomal escape of HNA, after 2 h, these cells were exposure to ultrasound irradiation for 5 min (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle). At indicated incubation time, these cells were washed using PBS for 3 times, incubated with Hoechst and Lysotracker following the manufacturer’s instructions, and fixed using 4 % paraformaldehyde. Finally, these cells were washed using PBS for 3 times and immediately imaged by confocal microscopy. ImageJ was used to quantify the fluorescence intensities.

RT-qPCR analysis

HeLa cells were cultured on 12 well plates. When reaching 60 % confluency, these cells were incubated with HNA, NNA or CNA at indicated concentrations. At 2 h post treatment, cells were exposure to ultrasound irradiation for 5 min (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle). After 48 h, these cells were washed using PBS for 3 times. The total RNA was extracted using TRIZOL reagent following the manufacturer’s protocol. The isolated RNA samples were reversely transcripted and quantified by qPCR using the Applied Biosystem StepOnePlus real-time PCR system. The internal control was GAPDH. Primers for GAPDH: forward (5′ to 3′) CGAGCCACATCGCTCAGACA; reverse (5′ to 3′) GTGGTGAAGACGCCAGTGGA. Primers for Bcl-2: forward (5′ to 3′) CCCTGTGGATGACTGAGTACCTG; reverse (5′ to 3′) GTGATGCAAGCTCCCACCAG.

Western blot analysis

HeLa cells were cultured on 6 well plates. After reaching 60 % confluency, these cells were incubated with HNA, NNA or CNA at 300 nM. At 2 h post treatment, cells were exposure to ultrasound irradiation for 5 min (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle). After 48 h, these cells were washed using PBS for 3 times. RIPA lysis buffer (10 mM Tris, 150 mM NaCl, 1 % NP40, pH 7.5, 0.1 % SDS, 1 % deoxycholate, protease inhibitor cocktail) was used to extract cellular lysates. BCA protein assay kit was used to determine protein concentration. 20 μg of protein sample was denatured in 1 × SDS loading buffer. The protein sample was further resolved by 12 % SDS-PAGE and transferred to a PVDF membrane. After blocking with 5 % nonfat milk, the PVDF membrane was incubated with Bcl-2 or vinculin (internal control) primary antibodies. After wash, the appropriate secondary antibody was incubated with the membrane, which was finally visualized with ECL reagents. Image J was used to quantify the band intensities.

CCK-8 assay

HeLa cells were seeded on 96-well plates with a density of 5,000 cells per well. After reaching 60 % confluency, cells were treated with HNA, NNA or CNA at 300 nM. At 2 h and 4 h post treatment, cells were exposure to ultrasound irradiation (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle) for 5 min. CCK-8 assay was carried out after a total culture time of 48 h. For each well, 10 μL CCK-8 was added. After 1 h incubation, absorbance at 450 nm was measured to indicate cell viability.

Tumor spheroid preparation and treatment

To prepare the multicellular tumor spheroids made of HeLa cells, 60 µL of sterile 1.5 % agarose (w/v) solution was dispensed into 96 well plates and cooled at 4 ℃ for solidification. The suspension of HeLa cells was seeded on the well and incubated at 37 ℃ for about 5 d until spheroids were formed. For penetration study, the medium was replaced by a fresh medium supplemented with 300 nM Cy3-HNA. At the 2 h time point, the spheroids were exposure to ultrasound irradiation (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle) for 5 min. At 4 h time point, the spheroids were transferred to a glass-bottom tissue culture dish, and immediately imaged by laser confocal microscopy.

Apoptosis assay

For the apoptosis assay, the medium of tumor spheroids was replaced by the fresh medium supplemented with 300 nM HNA. At 2 h and 4 h time point, HeLa cells were exposure to ultrasound irradiation (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle) for 5 min. After a total culture time of 48 h, tumor spheroids were washed with PBS for three times. The apoptotic cells in tumor spheroids were stained using an Annexin V-FITC/PI staining kit according to manufacturer’s protocol, followed by flow cytometry detection.

Statistical analysis

Statistical comparisons between groups were evaluated by Student’s t test. Data are shown as mean ± SEM. *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, and ***P < 0.001 were considered to be statistically significant.

Results and discussion

We first synthesized siBcl-2 and 9R using standard solid-phase protocols for oligonucleotides and peptides, respectively. PpIX was directly coupled to the N-terminal of 9R during the solid-phase synthesis. After purification with high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), siBcl-2 and 9R-PpIX were obtained and confirmed by mass spectrometry (Figs. S2 and S3). HNA was then prepared via a self-assembly between siBcl-2 and 9R-PpIX. Agarose gel electrophoresis showed that siBcl-2 was gradually compressed by 9R-PpIX upon increasing their molar ratios (Fig. S4). At a 30-equivalent excess of 9R-PpIX, siBcl-2 was completely assembled into HNA. Additionally, HNA featured good stability as evidenced by ignorable size change during long-term storage in phosphate buffered saline (PBS) and 10 % fetal bovine serum (Fig. S5).

We then studied the physical and optical properties of HNA. Dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis showed that HNA had an average hydrodynamic size of 129 nm with a polymer dispersity index (PDI) of 0.275 (Fig. 1a). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) imaging revealed a spherical morphology (Fig. 1a, inset). The zeta potential of HNA was + 8.3 mV (Fig. S6), suggesting that it may readily cross negatively charged cell membrane. The UV/Vis absorption spectra of HNA exhibited two characteristic peaks at 260 and 400 nm (Fig. 1b), which could be assigned to siBcl-2 and PpIX, respectively. However, the fluorescence emission of HNA at 630 nm relative to that of PpIX decreased by 94 % (Fig. 1c), indicating an aggregation-caused quenching (ACQ) effect that is commonly found during self-assembly.

The sonodynamic 1O2-generation activity of HNA was further measured using singlet oxygen sensor green (SOSG) as a fluorescent reporter. SOSG is non-fluorescent until 1O2-mediated oxidation to recover its green fluorescence emission. Upon 5 min sono-irradiation (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle), HNA increased SOSG fluorescence by 2.7-fold (Fig. 1d), verifying the US-initiated production of 1O2. The weaker sonodynamic property of HNA compared to that of PpIX could be attributed to the ACQ effect. These results demonstrate that HNA can produce 1O2 under ultrasound irradiation.

We next investigated the cellular uptake of HNA. To this end, Cy3-labeled siBcl-2 (Fig. S2) was used to prepare HNA for fluorescent tracking. After treating human cervical cancer HeLa cells with Cy3-HNA, we observed a time-dependent increase of Cy3 signals inside the cells, which reached a plateau at 2 h (Fig. 2a and Fig. S7). These data demonstrate a rapid and efficient cellular internalization of siBcl-2 mediated by HNA. However, the internalized siBcl-2 was largely trapped in the lysosome, as indicated by an 91 % overlap between Cy3 and Lysotracker signals (Fig. 2b). This result is consistent with previous evidences that siRNAs delivered by polyarginine are majorly transported into the lysosome.

Given the 1O2-generation ability of HNA and the lysosome-destructive role of 1O2 , we asked whether US could facilitate a cytosolic delivery of the trapped HNA. The US-triggered intracellular 1O2-production by HNA was confirmed using 2′,7′-dichlorodihydrofluorescein diacetate (H2DCFDA) as the fluorescent indicator (Fig. S8). Cy3-HNA-treated HeLa cells were then exposure to 5 min sono-irradiation after 2 h incubation. Afterwards, we found a gradual separation of Cy3 from Lysotracker signals, which uniformly distributed in the cytoplasm at 2 h post sono-irradiation (Fig. 2a and Fig. S9). Meanwhile, their colocalization ratio decreased to 33 % (Fig. 2b), proving a successful lysosomal escape of siBcl-2. Moreover, PpIX exhibited a similar distribution pattern, whereas its fluorescence remained quenched until US treatment and restored at 2 h after sono-irradiation (Fig. 2c). Collectively, these results validate a US-promoted cytosolic delivery of siBcl-2 and PpIX, warranting further functional studies.

The gene silencing activity of HNA in HeLa cells was assessed next. Real-time quantitative polymer chain reaction (RT-qPCR) assay showed that Bcl-2 mRNA levels were lowered by HNA in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. S10), reaching a maximal decrease of 46 % at 300 nM of siBcl-2 (Fig. 3a). To verify the role of PpIX and siBcl-2 in HNA, two control nanoassemblies, termed CNA and NNA (Fig. S11), were prepared using 9R and 9R-PpIX to package siBcl-2 and negative control siRNA (siNC), respectively. Compared to HNA, NNA barely modulated Bcl-2 expression due to the absence of siBcl-2, and CNA reduced Bcl-2 mRNA by 27 % (Fig. 3a). Notably, when 5 min sono-irradiation was combined, HNA elicited an obviously augmented knockdown (68 %) of Bcl-2 mRNA (Fig. 3a). The insensitivity of CNA- and NNA-regulated Bcl-2 levels towards US suggest that both PpIX and siBcl-2 in HNA are essential for its action. In addition, US alone would not alter Bcl-2 expression. Consistent with the above data, western blot analysis revealed that HNA with US induced a most significant downregulation (76 %) of Bcl-2 protein (Fig. 3b), compared to other groups. Overall, these data jointly demonstrate that HNA combined with US affords a greatly enhanced gene silencing, which results from the improved cytoplasmic siRNA delivery.

We further tested the anti-cancer activity of HNA, which was designed to exert both GT and SDT. While one-time US was enough to enhance GT by HNA, it was insufficient to elicit SDT, as NNA showed negligible cytotoxicity even after US treatment (Fig. S12). We therefore conducted a second 5 min sono-irradiation on HeLa cells at 2 h post the first US treatment to liberate PpIX, according to the cellular imaging results (Fig. 2c). A remarkable increment of intracellular 1O2-generation after twice sono-irradiation was confirmed using H2DCFDA (Fig. S8). As expected, cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8) assay showed that NNA reduced cell viability by 30 % after twice US treatment, corresponding to the SDT activity of PpIX (Fig. 4a). CNA was not responsive to US, and it decreased cell viability by 36 %, which was in line with the GT activity of siBcl-2. In comparison, HNA led to an 82 % reduction of cell viability upon twice sono-irradiation, proving a coaction of PpIX and siBcl-2.

Finally, we estimated the potential of HNA using three-dimensional tumor spheroids (3DTSs). The penetration of siBcl-2 into HeLa 3DTSs was first evaluated. After incubation with Cy3-HNA, the 3DTSs presented a clear Cy3 fluorescence signal, whereas it mainly located on the outside with limited infiltration (Fig. 4b). In contrast, when sono-irradiation was implemented, the 3DTSs treated with Cy3-HNA had an intense fluorescence signal from the outside in, suggesting a deep penetration of siBcl-2. This phenomenon might be ascribed to ultrasound-enhanced permeabilization. The pro-apoptosis capability of HNA toward HeLa 3DTSs was further quantified via flow cytometry with annexin V-FITC/(propidium iodide) PI double staining (Fig. 4c). Although 36 % of cellular apoptosis was caused by HNA alone, the apoptotic rate was raised up to 65 % by HNA under twice sono-irradiation (Fig. 4d). Taken together, these results validate a US-inducible synergistic GT and SDT by HNA.

Conclusion

In summary, we have developed a novel sono-controllable nucleic acid assembly, HNA, that allows ultrasound-promoted cytoplasmic siRNA delivery. HNA was prepared via the electrostatic self-assembly between siBcl-2 and 9R-PpIX. We demonstrated that HNA could be efficiently taken up by cancer cells, whereas it was largely trapped in the lysosome. Under sono-irradiation, 1O2 was generated by HNA to facilitate the lysosomal escape of siBcl-2, leading to enhanced suppression of anti-apoptotic Bcl-2. Moreover, following the liberation of PpIX, SDT was activated by a second sono-irradiation, which combined GT to potently induce cancer cell death. We also verified the potential of HNA in a multicellular tumor spheroid model. In addition to the non-invasiveness and deep tissue-penetrating ability of ultrasound, due to its biocompatibility and simplicity, we believe that HNA will serve as a readily accessible nanoplatform for siRNA delivery and anti-cancer applications. Further chemical modifications on polyarginine to increase the stability and cellular uptake efficiency of this nanoassembly as well as its potential in deep-seated tumors in mouse model deserve future exploration. Moreover, whether this nanoassembly can be used to deliver other genetic drugs such as CRISPR/Cas9 warrants further investigations. It will also be interesting to explore the combination of sonosensitizers with alternative biomaterials for ultrasound-enhanced drug delivery.
