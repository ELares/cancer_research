# Metabolic regulation of T cells in the tumor microenvironment by nutrient availability and diet.

## Abstract

Recent advances in immunotherapies such as immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) and chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T) for the treatment of cancer have generated excitement over their ability to yield durable, and potentially curative, responses in a multitude of cancers. These findings have established that the immune system is capable of eliminating tumors and led us to a better, albeit still incomplete, understanding of the mechanisms by which tumors interact with and evade destruction by the immune system. Given the central role of T cells in immunotherapy, elucidating the cell intrinsic and extrinsic factors that govern T cell function in tumors will facilitate the development of immunotherapies that establish durable responses in a greater number of patients. One such factor is metabolism, a set of fundamental cellular processes that not only sustains cell survival and proliferation, but also serves as a means for cells to interpret their local environment. Nutrient sensing is critical for T cells that must infiltrate into a metabolically challenging tumor microenvironment and expand under these harsh conditions to eliminate cancerous cells. Here we introduce T cell exhaustion with respect to cellular metabolism, followed by a discussion of nutrient availability at the tumor and organismal level in relation to T cell metabolism and function to provide rationale for the study and targeting of metabolism in anti-tumor immune responses.

Metabolic Regulation of T Cells in the Tumor Microenvironment by Nutrient Availability and Diet

Recent advances in immunotherapies such as immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) and chimeric antigen receptor T cells (CAR-T) for the treatment of cancer have generated excitement over their ability to yield durable, and potentially curative, responses in a multitude of cancers. These findings have established that the immune system is capable of eliminating tumors and led us to a better, albeit still incomplete, understanding of the mechanisms by which tumors interact with and evade destruction by the immune system. Given the central role of T cells in immunotherapy, elucidating the cell intrinsic and extrinsic factors that govern T cell function in tumors will facilitate the development of immunotherapies that establish durable responses in a greater number of patients. One such factor is metabolism, a set of fundamental cellular processes that not only sustains cell survival and proliferation, but also serves as a means for cells to interpret their local environment. Nutrient sensing is critical for T cells that must infiltrate into a metabolically challenging tumor microenvironment and expand under these harsh conditions to eliminate cancerous cells. Here we introduce T cell exhaustion with respect to cellular metabolism, followed by a discussion of nutrient availability at the tumor and organismal level in relation to T cell metabolism and function to provide rationale for the study and targeting of metabolism in anti-tumor immune responses.

INTRODUCTION

The success of immunotherapies for the treatment of cancer largely depends on the successful generation of cytotoxic effector CD8+ T cells. Naïve T cells are activated upon binding of the T cell receptor (TCR) to antigen presented by antigen-presenting cells (APC) and a co-stimulatory signal through CD80/86, thereafter gaining effector functions and expanding to eliminate infected or transformed cells. However, in the presence of persistent antigen and TCR signaling, such as in tumors or chronic infection, cytotoxic T cells acquire an exhausted phenotype characterized by increased expression of inhibitory receptors (e.g. PD-1, CTLA-4, TIM3, LAG3), as well as reduced effector cytokine production (e.g. TNF, IFNγ, IL-2) and proliferation (Figure 1). Notably, exhausted T cells are not completely dysfunctional, as they retain cytotoxic capabilities, modest IFNγ secretion, as well as the secretion of chemokines. Thus, T cell exhaustion is an intrinsic process by which CD8+ T cells are desensitized to chronic TCR stimulation to reduce activation-induced cell death and fine-tune effector functions to minimize CD8+ mediated immunopathology and tissue damage. From experimental models of cancer and chronic infection we now know that T cell exhaustion is a constellation of molecular subtypes characterized by distinct surface marker and transcriptional profiles (Figure 1). The HMG-box transcription factor (TF) TOX has been identified as a key TF inducing the differentiation of exhausted T cells (Figure 1). TOX, along with the TF T Cell Factor 1 (TCF1; Tcf7 gene), help to induce and maintain the pool of progenitor-exhausted CD8+ T cells (TCF1+PD1int) that display stem-like properties and are needed for long-term maintenance of the antigen-specific pool of CD8+ T cells. As these antigen-specific cells further differentiate into more terminally exhausted cells (TCF1-PD1high), TOX expression is sustained and cells upregulate additional co-inhibitory molecules (TIM3, LAG3, TIGIT, CD101) that further restrict proliferation and effector functions (Figure 1). Of importance for anti-tumor immunity, the progenitor or stem-like exhausted T cells are the subset that become reinvigorated by anti-PD-1 checkpoint blockade therapy and expand to eliminate cancer cells, whereas terminally exhausted T cells fail to respond as effectively. Thus, exhaustion represents an intrinsic barrier to T cell function and clearance of tumors and has previously been reviewed in further detail.

External variables also contribute to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). The TME is composed of fibroblasts, immune cells, extracellular matrix, and soluble cytokines and factors, acting as a barrier to all forms of cancer therapy, including immunotherapy. The suppression of anti-tumor immunity by the TME occurs at multiple levels, including physical exclusion of tumor-reactive immune cells from cancer cells by a dense stroma or chemokines that favor exclusion. Moreover, while not the focus of this review, other immune cells present in the TME such as regulatory CD4+ T cells (Treg) and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) suppress cytotoxic CD8+ T cell functions.

Another emerging factor in the suppression of anti-tumor immunity in the TME is nutrient availability. Driven by oncogenic growth signals, cancer cells rewire their metabolism to fuel a constant proliferative demand, depleting nutrients essential for growth from the environment. Thus, T cells must overcome intrinsic metabolic barriers established by exhaustion in addition to metabolic constraints imposed by an infertile nutrient milieu. Although significant progress has been made in recent years towards establishing how metabolism in the tumor microenvironment functions as a bottleneck for anti-tumor immunity, a deeper understanding of which nutrients are present in the tumor microenvironment, and how T cells integrate and interpret nutrient availability is needed to bring these findings from bench to bedside.

Mitochondrial Dysfunction and Reactive Oxygen Species in Exhaustion

The intricate link between cellular metabolism and T cell exhaustion is evidenced by observations that terminally exhausted T cells have lower mitochondrial membrane potential compared to progenitor exhausted T cells, indicative of mitochondrial damage and impaired bioenergetics. Mitochondria are dynamic organelles that integrate multiple nutrient inputs to regulate biosynthetic and bioenergetic pathways during the lifespan of T cells. Different CD8+ T cell subsets can be distinguished by their mitochondrial abundance and morphology. Naïve T cells are quiescent and have few mitochondria, but upon activation mitochondrial biogenesis and mass increases as T cells differentiate into effector cells. Memory T cells mitochondrial morphology is different from naïve and effector T cells, having fused mitochondrial networks and enhanced spare respiratory capacity (SRC). In contrast to memory T cells, exhausted CD8+ T cells undergo mitochondrial dysfunction, characterized by loss of mitochondrial mass, mitochondrial polarization and decreased SRC. Chronic TCR stimulus in CD8+ TIL represses PGC1α, a transcriptional coactivator of mitochondrial biogenesis, oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS), and ROS detoxicification. Nevertheless, it remains to be determined the exact mechanism by which dysfunctional mitochondria promote T cell exhaustion.

An emerging mechanism implicates elevated levels of mitochondrial reactive oxygen species (mROS), produced by dysfunctional mitochondria, as a contributor to T cell exhaustion. mROS are generated downstream of TCR ligation during OXPHOS by complexes I and III of the electron transport chain. In the context of acute TCR stimuli, mROS are critical for T cell activation, promoting NFAT nuclear translocation, IL-2 production and proliferation. However, T cells undergoing chronic TCR stimulus accumulate high levels of mROS. T cells that generate high mROS have dysfunctional mitochondria and reduced IFNγ production when compared to T cells producing low mROS. This is highlighted in exhausted CD8+ TIL, which produce increased levels of mROS. Inducing mROS in CD8+ T cells via chronic TCR stimulus or by treating with Antimycin A during activation increases the surface expression of co-inhibitory molecules and reduces polyfunctionality. Reducing levels of mROS with the antioxidant MitoTEMPO led to increased T cell activation of ex vivo stimulated CD8+ TIL, and decreases the formation of T cells with dysfunctional mitochondria after activation. Similarly, T cells activated with chronic TCR stimulus treated with the ROS scavenger N-acetylcysteine, displayed reduced mROS and co-inhibitory molecule expression, and had increased polyfunctionality. These studies highlight the deleterious role of mROS produced by dysfunctional mitochondria as an accelerator of T cell exhaustion. Exactly how mROS promotes exhaustion is still unknown, although the functions of ROS as potent inhibitors of protein tyrosine phosphatases may provide insight. Indeed, Scharping et al. showed that induction of mROS in cultured T cells using antimycin A increased phosphorylated tyrosine signaling and promoted sustained NFAT nuclear translocation in CD8+ T cells. Taken together, the data from these various studies suggest that mROS production by dysfunctional mitochondria could be sustaining or mimicking chronic TCR signaling, directly contributing to T cell exhaustion by promoting a chronic activation signal (Figure 1). Thus, mitochondrial quantity and quality closely follow the differentiation state of CD8+ T cells, indicative of an important role for nutrient assimilation and signaling in this process.

METABOLIC BARRIERS TO T CELL FUNCTION IN THE TUMOR MICROENVIRONMENT

Tumor interstitial fluid

In keeping with the fluctuations in mitochondrial mass and activity, T cells also dramatically change metabolic requirements during their lifetime, preferentially utilizing distinct metabolites at certain stages of differentiation. Thus, in addition to environmental cues such as cytokines and chemokines, T cells rely on an adequate nutrient supply to mount an effective response.

In the TME, metabolite abundances are in constant flux depending on the flow of nutrients into the tumor, the consumption of nutrients by cells, and the release of byproducts of cellular metabolism. These are all reflected in the interstitial fluid (IF) between cells and the capillary vasculature in tissues, which accepts blood-derived metabolites from circulation and byproducts of cellular metabolism. Healthy tissues are well-perfused by capillary vasculature, ensuring rapid metabolic exchange with plasma. Therefore, concentrations of metabolites in IF from healthy tissues are very similar to those found in circulation. In contrast, tumors are often poorly perfused by abnormal and leaky or collapsed vasculature. This leads to several key biochemical and biophysical differences between normal IF and TIF that impact nutrient availability to cells within the TME.

Variables such as the anatomical location of the tumor also factor into the nutrient composition of TIF. For example, the TIF composition of subcutaneously implanted tumors, a commonly used model for studying tumor immune responses, is significantly different than that of autochthonous tumors arising in the pancreas or lung. Thus, not surprisingly, the nutrient environment that T cells are exposed to within a tumor are shaped by both cancer intrinsic and extrinsic qualities. While our understanding of nutrient availability in tumors has rapidly grown in recent years, a comprehensive profiling of TIF from different primary and metastatic tumors, coupled with methods for spatially resolving metabolites in the TME, will greatly augment our understanding of how local immune functions correlate with heterogeneous nutrient availability. In this section, we highlight the abnormal nutrient milieu found in the TME, and how metabolic intermediates of these nutrients instruct the differentiation and function of distinct T cell lineages.

Glucose/Aerobic Glycolysis

T cells utilize glucose as a central source of carbon to generate ATP and to sustain several metabolic pathways (Figure 2). Whereas quiescent naïve T cells and memory T cells consume minimal glucose, upon TCR engagement, activated effector CD8+ T cells rapidly engage aerobic glycolysis converting glucose derived-pyruvate to lactate, while still utilizing glucose-derived carbons to fuel the TCA cycle and biosynthetic pathways. This early metabolic reprogramming is further imprinted by epigenetic modifications and changes to the transcriptional program. Co-stimulation via the CD28 receptor augments TCR signaling and metabolic rewiring by activating the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway to reinforce glucose uptake and glycolysis via increased expression of Glut1, hexokinase, GAPDH, LDH and other glycolytic enzymes.

Early studies on TIF samples using microcapillary devices identified that concentrations of glucose are significantly depleted in TIF compared to plasma, whereas lactate concentrations are nearly doubled, consistent with Warburg metabolism in cancer cells. More recent studies obtaining TIF by centrifugation of tumor tissue, paired with the sensitivity of modern mass spectrometers, has led to a more comprehensive definition of the nutrient milieu in murine pancreatic and lung adenocarcinomas. In agreement with earlier studies, the authors reported that glucose and lactate concentrations in murine pancreatic cancer TIF were lower and higher, respectively, than that of plasma. As previously described, glycolytic metabolism is crucial for T cell activation, and metabolic competition for glucose in the TME decreases CD8+ T cell infiltration and survival in tumors. TILs that infiltrate into the tumor space subsequently have impaired glycolytic capacity, mTOR activity, and effector functions. Mechanistically, limiting glucose downregulates Enolase 1 (ENO1) in CD8+ TILs, depleting glycolytic intermediates that are crucial for T cell functions, such as phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). This leads to decreased intracellular Ca2+ flux and subsequently impaired NFAT1 nuclear translocation and effector functions.

Regulation of glucose metabolism is also critical for proper T cell subset differentiation. PD-1 signaling represses glycolytic metabolism prior to the onset of many exhaustion phenotypes, indicating that metabolic rewiring is a critical component of CD8+ T cell exhaustion. However, the timing, duration, and amplitude of metabolic rewiring also play important roles in this process; enforcing glycolytic metabolism in activated CD8+ T cells impairs long-term survival and expansion upon antigen rechallenge, whereas activation of CD8+ T cells in the presence of the glycolysis inhibitor 2-deoxyglucose (2-DG) promotes differentiation into long-lived memory T cells. Similarly, CD4+ T cell lineages (Th1, Th2 and Th17) are highly glycolytic, and inhibition of glycolysis with 2-DG skews CD4+ T cell differentiation towards Treg cells. In addition to energetics, glucose supports the synthesis of metabolic intermediates necessary for histone modifications, such as cytosolic acetyl-CoA, which is required for histone acetylation of the IFNγ promoter and gene body in Th1 cells. On the other hand, pyruvate to lactate conversion and OXPHOS produce NAD+, the requisite cofactor for the sirtuin (SIRT) family of histone deacetylases (HDACs). SIRT1 inhibits Treg function by deacetylating the master regulator of Treg differentiation, Forkhead box protein P3 (FOXP3), leading to its degradation (Figure 2). Further investigation of mechanisms by which metabolic intermediates regulate the epigenome will be important to understand how abnormal nutrient availability drives T cell differentiation in the TME.

Lactate and Acidosis

In solid tumors, interstitial fluid pressure (IFP) is highest at the tumor core, forcing fluid towards the outer, less dense regions of the tumor, where IFP is similar to normal tissue. Further, the tumor core is less perfused by capillaries than the periphery, and cells near the core have limited access to extracellular nutrients and oxygen, are frequently hypoxic, and depend heavily on glycolysis. Activation of hypoxic programs upregulate the expression of MCT4, which preferentially effluxes lactate from cells. Lactate produced and secreted by these cells is transferred via outward fluid flow to more oxygenated cells at the tumor periphery that uptake and oxidize lactate as fuel. Recent reports have highlighted how lactate supports the generation of suppressive macrophages and regulatory T cells. Further, work from Watson et al. has revealed that regulatory T cells in the TME take up lactate via MCT1 and metabolize it to create higher order metabolic intermediates to fuel the TCA cycle and gluconeogenesis. Thus, lactate availability in the TME may benefit establishment of an immunosuppressive environment.

Moreover, lactate secretion is proton-coupled, leading to acidification of the TME. There is overwhelming evidence that extracellular acidosis suppresses effector T cell responses. T cells activated in vitro in low extracellular pH results in reduced cell cycle engagement, proliferation and secretion of IL-2 and IFNγ. Further, activating human and murine tumor specific cytotoxic lymphocytes (CTLs) in acidic conditions (pH 6–6.5) rendered T cells in an anergic state characterized by reduced cytotoxicity, secretion of inflammatory cytokines, and CD25 expression. Intriguingly, T cells were recently discovered to contribute to their own suppression by acidifying the lymph node, raising the possibility that the Warburg effect in cancer cells may be co-opting an intrinsic suppressive mechanism in addition to supporting proliferative demands. Indeed, studies have shown that blocking extracellular acidification either with monocarboxylate transporter (MCTs) inhibitors or bicarbonate monotherapies, leads to better efficacy of checkpoint immunotherapy. However, extracellular lactate could also be suppressing T cell anti-tumor immunity independently of pH, as CTLs cultured in lactic acid suppress expression of IFNγ and IL-2 as compared to CTLs cultured in HCl alone.

Hypoxia

As already alluded to, a common characteristic of solid tumors is the generation of regions with little to no oxygen, known as hypoxic regions, that are caused by increased oxygen consumption by tumor cells, aberrant microvascular networks, and continued tumor growth that eventually outstretches the diffusion limits of molecular oxygen from vasculature. Formation of hypoxic regions within tumors is a gradual and progressive process, allowing for cancer cells to acquire adaptive responses, such as stabilizing Hypoxia-Inducible Factors (HIF) to favor glycolysis and lactate production, while limiting oxidative metabolism. However, for immune cells that are experiencing this condition acutely upon infiltration, such adaptive mechanisms may not be properly mounted, leading to their absence from hypoxic regions. This adaptation is critical for anti-tumor immunity, as conditional knockout of Hypoxia-Inducible Factor 1α (HIF1α) in T cells reduces tumor infiltration and effector functions, promoting tumor growth. Conversely, enforcing HIF1α activation via deletion of its negative regulator Von Hippel-Lindau (VHL) in tumor-reactive CD8+ T cells establishes the generation of a resident-memory like phenotype with greater cytolytic capacity, cytokine synthesis, and increased accumulation in the TME.

Although HIF1α activity can promote TIL effector functions, hypoxia also induces T cell apoptosis via adenosine receptor signaling and subsequent downregulation of CCR7. Further, exposure to hypoxia of antigen experienced CD8+ T cell during reactivation decreases their proliferation and stimulates production of the suppressive cytokine, IL-10. Metabolically, activating T cells in hypoxic conditions suppresses PGC1α expression, leading to dysfunctional mitochondrial metabolism and exhaustion. This hypoxia-induced suppression is prominent in the TME, as highly oxidative murine melanoma cell lines contain a greater number of terminally exhausted CD8+ T cells than tumor cell lines that consume less oxygen. However, not all TIL experience hypoxia to the same extent, with PD1+Tim3+ terminally differentiated exhausted T cells displaying higher hypoxia signatures than other CD8+ TIL. Additionally, hypoxia triggers other maldaptive responses in the tumor that suppress TIL functions, including enhanced PD-L1 expression by tumor cells and myeloid cells. These data suggest that ameliorating hypoxia or conditioning T cells to hypoxic environments may enhance T cell anti-tumor immunity. Consistent with this, conditioning OT-I T cells in 1% O2 prior to adoptive transfer resulted in reduced tumor burden and longer survival in a B16 -OVA melanoma model. Furthermore, pharmacological targeting of tumor hypoxia via metformin or hypoxia pro-drugs improved efficacy of anti-PD1 therapy in multiple cancer models.

Lipids

In addition to rewiring glycolytic metabolism, hypoxia also affects nutrient utilization for lipid metabolism. Tumor cells rewire fatty acid metabolism by increasing uptake and de novo synthesis to meet proliferative demands for membrane synthesis during proliferation. Consequently, tumor cells also release greater amounts of fatty acids into their environment than normal tissue. Moreover, tumor cells recruit adipocytes to the TME, which undergo lipolysis and release fatty acids into the tumor. As a result of these mechanisms, fatty acids accumulate in the TME of multiple tumor types, and can be imported by T cells through plasma membrane lipid transporters such as CD36 (Figure 2). In CD8+ TILs, CD36 dampens effector functions by driving the uptake of oxidized lipids and lipid peroxidation which activate a p38-mediated suppression of cytokine production and anti-oxidant responses. Moreover, CD36 is upregulated and facilitates a metabolic adaptation in intratumoral Tregs to support Treg survival and function in the TME. Thus, CD36 has a multipronged role in linking altered metabolite availability and immunosuppression in the TME, and represents a promising therapeutic target.

De novo lipogenesis (DNL) and fatty acid oxidation (FAO) are also important for the development of T cells. Quiescent CD4+ and CD8+ T cells engage in FAO, breaking down lipids to acetyl-CoA in the mitochondria to fuel the TCA cycle and ATP production. Upon activation, both CD4+ and CD8+ T cells induce DNL. Further, CD28 signaling during early activation promotes a transient increase in CPT1a expression, endowing T cells with latent mitochondrial respiratory capacity and promoting memory T cell formation. Mammalian target of rapamycin (mTORc1) engages DNL through the rate limiting enzyme acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC1), which converts acetyl-CoA to malonyl-CoA in the cytoplasm. Malonyl-CoA in turn suppresses FAO by inhibiting CPT1a, thereby preventing futile cycling of fatty acids. Distinct T cell lineages preferentially engage DNL or FAO, and improper coordination of these pathways in CD4+ subsets leads to aberrant differentiation. ACC1 KO in CD4+ T cells results in elevated expression of Foxp3, even in Th17 skewing conditions. Intriguingly, regulation of effector functions by DNL is dependent on availability of other nutrients, as ACC1 inhibition increases IFNγ production by Tem cells in low, but not optimal, glucose conditions. CD8+ T cells differentially utilize lipid metabolism during effector and memory stages. Effector CD8+ T cells perform DNL during effector stages and switch to FAO during the transition to a memory T cell. Central memory T cells utilize intracellular lipids to sustain FAO, whereas effector CD8+ T cells had higher uptake of exogenous lipids than memory cells. However, recent studies using conditional knockouts of CPT1a in T cells revealed that while FAO is elevated in CD8+ and CD4+ memory T cells, CPT1a is not required for the differentiation, survival or recall responses of memory T cells. Thus, memory T cells compensate for impaired FAO, supporting its role as a dominant metabolic feature of resting memory.

In addition to supplying bioenergetics and biomaterial for membrane biogenesis, lipids function in cellular signaling as ligands for nuclear receptors (Figure 2). The peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARs) are a family of three distinct TFs (PPARα, PPARβ/δ, and PPARγ) that regulate the expression of lipid catabolism and storage genes in response to endogenous lipid ligands, as well as synthetic ligands that have been developed as therapeutics. PPAR activation is suppressed in intratumoral immune cells by tumor-derived metabolites, and in exhausted TILs, restoring expression of the PPAR coactivator, PGC1α, improves mitochondrial function and anti-tumor immunity. Activation of PPARα and PPARβ/δ signaling using synthetic agonists in combination with blockade of PD-1 receptor ligation enhanced expansion of functional CD8+ TILs over ICB alone, in part through the transcriptional activation of FAO that bolstered CD8+ T cell survival and effector functions. Treatment with the PPARγ agonist, rosiglitazone, also promoted TIL infiltration and tumor rejection of immunogenic tumors. Thus, despite the abundance of fatty acids in the tumor microenvironment, utilization of these fatty acids to support effector functions in TILs may be impaired by insufficient PPAR signaling and mitochondrial dysfunction. Alternatively, PPARβ/δ are activated by long chain fatty acid uptake in Tregs to promote their survival and further contributing to an immunosuppressive TME. However, why PPAR activation by endogenous fatty acid ligands in the tumor occurs more efficiently in Tregs than cytotoxic TILs remains unclear.

In addition to fatty acid metabolism, cancer cells also upregulate genes involved in cholesterol synthesis and uptake. On one hand, this increases abundance of cholesterol within tumors and promotes cholesterol accumulation within CD8+ T cell membranes, contributing to their exhaustion through activation of ER stress responses. On the other hand, there are data that indicate raising free cholesterol content by inhibiting cholesterol esterification in CD8+ T cells increases TCR clustering and signal transduction, and synergizes with PD-1 blockade to improve effector functions in TILs and reducing tumor growth.

Cholesterol and its metabolic products are also sensed intracellularly by the SREBP and the Liver X Receptor (LXR) TF families. SREBP-2 is a master regulator of cholesterol biosynthesis and uptake by controlling the expression of genes such as HMG-CoA Reductase (HMGCR) and the LDL receptor (LDLR). Upon activation, T cells activate SREBP-2 and its transcriptional program while simultaneously suppressing LXR-β-mediated transcription. SREBP-2 is required not only for the upregulation of cholesterol synthesis and uptake, but also for the induction of glycolytic and mitochondrial metabolism following activation. Genetic deletion of the SREBP activating chaperone, SCAP, abolished activation of SREBP-2 upon T cell activation and prevented expansion of antigen-specific CD8+ T cells. In addition to CD8+ T cells, the SCAP-SREBP pathway is also required for the suppressive function of intratumoral Tregs. Treg-specific deletion of SCAP disrupted Treg function and sensitized tumors to immunotherapy. This was in part by suppressing lipid metabolism, as well as a protein geranylgeranylation-dependent increase of PD-1 expression in Tregs, highlighting an important link between cellular metabolism and signaling. SCAP itself is a cholesterol sensor, and is inhibited from activating SREBPs when intracellular cholesterol is abundant. Thus, whether cholesterol accumulation within the TME and TILs prevents SCAP-mediated activation of SREBPs remains to be determined.

Whereas SREBP activity is inhibited by high sterol levels, LXR-α and LXR-β are nuclear receptors that regulate the expression of cholesterol transporters and other lipid metabolism genes in response to oxysterols, which are synthesized from cholesterol enzymatically or non-enzymatically through ROS-mediated reactions. LXR-β inhibits proliferation of activated CD8+ T cells by regulating expression of the cholesterol efflux transporter ABCG1, and is suppressed following TCR stimulation. Pharmacological inhibition of LXRs restores T cell functionality in the presence of tumor cell-derived LXR ligands in vitro, and promotes infiltration of CD8+ TILs into tumors in vivo. However, this may also be an indirect effect, as tumor-derived oxysterols inhibit dendritic cell migration to tumor draining lymph nodes by inhibiting expression of the chemokine receptor CCR7 in an LXR-α dependent manner, leading to impaired T cell priming and anti-tumor immune responses. A deeper understanding of the availability of lipids such as fatty acids and sterols in the TME, as well as the signaling programs tied to these metabolites, is needed to develop therapeutic strategies that specifically enhance cytotoxic TIL-mediated anti-tumor immunity.

Amino Acids

Glutamine is another key metabolic substrate for cellular proliferation, as well as T cell activation and differentiation. Like glucose, glutamine metabolism is relatively low in quiescent T cells, but upon activation, glutamine uptake and metabolism are rapidly upregulated to promote mTORC1 activation, c-Myc expression, proliferation, and cytokine production. Glutamine is metabolized to glutamate via glutaminase (GLS) and shuttled into the mitochondria where it is converted to α-ketoglutarate (α-KG) as a significant anapleurotic substrate to the TCA cycle. Glutamine-derived α-KG is also critical for the regulation of histone demethylases (HDMs) which utilize α-KG, oxygen and iron to catalyze histone and DNA demethylation. These reactions promote IL-2 dependent effector programs in CD4+ and CD8+ T cells. Although glutamine is a key anapleurotic metabolite in both cancer cells and T cells, glutamine antagonism synergizes with immune checkpoint therapies to promote tumor clearance by CD8+ T cells. Glutamine antagonism activates unique adaptive mechanisms in cancer cells and CD8+ TILs, leading to enhanced oxidative phosphorylation and proliferation specifically in the latter. This was accompanied by increased expression of pro-survival genes and memory signatures, potentially through epigenetic remodeling. In agreement with this, inhibition of α-KG-dependent demethylases by the oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate promotes a memory-like state with enhanced persistence and proliferative capacity in adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells.

In addition to glutamine, T cells require other essential amino acids to varying extents during different stages of differentiation. The essential amino acid leucine serves as an important regulator of mTORc1, and blocking the leucine transporter LAT1 in T cells reduces activation and proliferation by inhibiting NF-kB and AP-1 activity, as well as mTOR and c-Myc signaling. Methionine is another essential amino acid required to maintain protein and nucleotide synthesis. T cells upregulate methionine transporters and metabolism upon antigen exposure to meet proliferative demands, as well as to generate s-adenosylmethionine (SAM), which is utilized by DNA methyltransferases (DMNTs) or histone methyltransferases (HMTs) for methylation of cytosine and lysine residues on DNA and histones, respectively. In the TME, tumor cells outcompete CD8+ T cells for methionine by upregulating the amino acid transporter SLC43A2, creating an insufficiency of the methyl donor SAM in T cells. This leads to a decrease in H3K79me2 and upregulation of an apoptotic gene signature in CD8+ T cells. Supplementation of methionine alone or in combination with anti-PD-L1 in vivo restored H3K79me2 and increased T cell survival and function, leading to delayed tumor growth.

Other notable metabolic changes between the TIF and plasma include selective depletion of the amino acids arginine, cysteine, and tryptophan, but not amino acids produced by tumor cells, such as glutamate and glycine. Activated T cells rapidly upregulate arginine metabolism to support protein synthesis and polyamine biosynthesis, which is required for IL-2 dependent proliferation in cytotoxic lymphocytes. Moreover, CD8+ T cells cultured with arginine showed improved survival and tumor control following adoptive transfer. T cells are dependent on exogenous cysteine uptake for maintaining glutathione synthesis and redox homeostasis to support proliferation and effector functions. Tryptophan is essential for T cells to proliferate, and its depletion by cells expressing Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) causes cell cycle arrest. Moreover, IDO-expressing cells metabolize tryptophan to produce kynurenine, which possesses immunosuppressive functions and is currently the target of drug development. In addition to tumor cells, other stromal cells such as myeloid cells and fibroblasts can contribute to depletion of amino acids such as tryptophan, arginine, and cysteine/cystine (Figure 3). Thus, metabolic competition exists between multiple cell types within the TME, acting as a barrier to proper tumor immune responses.

SYSTEMIC METABOLISM, DIET, AND IMMUNITY

In addition to differences in TIF at the tumor and tissue levels, Sullivan et al. demonstrated that substituting defined amino acids into murine chow feed in lieu of natural protein sources significantly altered the nutrient composition in both plasma and TIF, indicating that dietary nutrients are passed from circulation to the TME (Figure 3). However, the extent to which dietary nutrients can alter the nutrient milieu of the tumor is still largely unknown. Additionally, how immune responses in the tumor are influenced by deranged systemic metabolism is not clear despite evidence that metabolic diseases such as obesity and T2D are bona fide risk factors for multiple cancers. Obesity is accompanied by a chronic low-grade inflammatory response, which has drawn comparisons to the inflammatory nature of tumors. In light of observations that immune function is altered by systemic metabolism, responses to immunotherapy are likely governed by a combination of the metabolic state of both the TME and host (Figure 3). In this section, we highlight what is known about the role of host metabolic state and diets in regulating T cell functions in tumors as a framework for considering how diet can synergize with immunotherapy. This coincides with renewed interest in understanding how specific diets can synergize with existing chemotherapies in the treatment of cancer.

Overnutrition and Dietary Fat

Due to shifts in diet and lifestyle, obesity has become pervasive world-wide, with nearly 20% of the global adult population now considered obese. Obesity accelerates thymic involution and decreases the TCR repertoire, potentially limiting T cell clones that are reactive against tumor-specific antigens and increasing the likelihood of immune evasion by tumors. Consumption of a high-fat diet is a widely used model of diet-induced obesity (DIO) in research animals, and accelerates tumorigenesis in numerous models of cancer. In murine models of mammary tumors and melanoma, feeding mice HFD increased tumor progression and CD8+ T cell exhaustion. Subsequent studies identified that leptin, a chronically elevated adipokine in obese mice and humans, triggers a STAT3 signaling mechanism that promotes FAO and suppresses effector functions in CD8+ T cells, consistent with features of PD-1+ exhausted T cells. However, leptin is also produced in response to inflammatory stimuli and augments T cell activation. This discrepancy may be attributed to the development of leptin resistance in obesity. For example, leptin is transiently increased upon secondary viral infection in lean mice, whereas DIO animals display persistently elevated leptin levels and impaired CD8+ memory responses. Likewise in tumors, enforcing leptin production in the TME metabolically reprograms CD8+ TILs to enable tumor clearance in lean, but not obese, mice. Furthermore, neutralizing leptin in DIO mice improves responses to immunotherapy, suggesting that the effects of leptin may depend on the metabolic state of the host. In the same study, obese mice genetically deficient for leptin (ob/ob) retained responses to immunotherapy, indicating an important role for leptin in altering the responses to immunotherapy in DIO animals and underscoring the importance of using models that reflect human obesity.

Another consequence of DIO is the development of insulin resistance, in part through the downregulation of insulin receptor in tissues such as adipose and liver. Of note, both myeloid immune cells and lymphocytes express the insulin receptor and similarly lose its expression in the obese state. In T cells, insulin signaling has been shown to drive the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway to promote expansion and inflammatory cytokine production. Consequently, insulin receptor-deficient T cells have impaired effector responses and fail to control influenza infection. These observations suggest that insulin resistance may contribute to immune evasion of tumors by impairing cytotoxic T cell functions but require further investigation.

While high fat DIO clearly impacts T cell-dependent immunity against tumors, how high fat diets change the nutrient milieu in the TME remained largely unknown until a recent study addressed this using DIO and a syngeneic colon cancer MC38. The authors identified, paradoxically, that TIF from HFD fed mice actually contained lower levels of fatty acids than animals fed control diet despite higher circulating FFA. Mechanistically, cancer cells and TILs adopted divergent metabolic programs in response to DIO; cancer cells increased fatty acid uptake and oxidation, whereas CD44+ CD8+ TILs reduced FA uptake compared to TILs from control diet mice. Blocking fatty acid metabolism in MC38 cancer cells restored CD8+ TIL-dependent control of tumor growth. Thus, optimal strategies to target tumor metabolism and immunometabolism may vary depending on the host metabolic state and diet.

An important consideration is that high fat chow diets used for rodent DIO models typically consist of 40–60 %kcal from fat, often lard or other saturated fatty acid sources (i.e. animal fats, vegetable shortening). Yet the human diet is a menagerie of various dietary lipids. Moreover, it is clear that lipids comprise a vast array of biochemical properties that translate to unique cellular and molecular outcomes in lymphocytes. Consumption of specific poly-unsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) can directly remodel the distribution of membrane lipids, and supply the metabolic precursors of eicosanoids, a family of secreted bioactive lipids that modulate immune responses. The essential dietary ω−6 PUFA, arachidonic acid, is converted by the cyclooxygenases COX-1 and COX-2 for the synthesis of various prostaglandins. Notably, COX-2 is upregulated in multiple tumor types and promotes tumorigenesis via synthesis of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2). In CTLs from chronic viral infection and tumors, binding of PGE2 to its receptors EP2 and EP4, which are highly co-expressed with PD-1, dampens TCR-induced proliferation and IL-2 production, and potentiates T cell exhaustion. Inhibition of PGE2 production using Celecoxib, a COX-2 specific inhibitor, synergizes with PD-1/PD-L1 blockade. Intriguingly, the production of prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) is dependent on dietary ω−6 fatty acids, and is reduced when dietary ω−6:ω−3 fatty acid ratio decreases. Consumption of fats high in ω−3 fatty acids reduces arachidonic acid content in cellular membranes and PGE2 production, and has been reported to suppress tumor growth, consistent with the immunosuppressive role of PGE2. Thus, the composition of dietary fat is rapidly reflected in cellular lipid content in vivo, and may influence immune evasion by tumors. To the best of our knowledge, a comprehensive study distinguishing the effects of total dietary fat content compared to type of dietary fat in nutrient-dependent regulation of immunity in cancer has not been done.

Dietary restriction as an intervention:

Caloric Restriction:

Contrary to overnutrition, dietary caloric restriction and calorie restriction mimetics (CRM) in various animal models have been shown to increase life span and reduce pathologies, including cancer. In subcutaneous murine fibrosarcoma tumors, caloric restriction through fasting or CRMs, such as the ATP citrate lyase inhibitor hydroxycitrate, enhanced chemotherapy efficacy in a CD8+ T cell mediated manner. Consistent with the role for acetyl-CoA in suppressing autophagy, this effect was through induction of autophagy in the cancer cells leading to a depletion of intratumoral Tregs, although the mechanistic link between these observations is not fully resolved. Interestingly, whereas induction of autophagy enhanced immune-mediated tumor clearance in this context, other studies have identified autophagy as a mechanism for immune evasion by other tumors. Moreover, caloric restriction induces autophagy in host tissues, which has been reported to promote immune tolerance by augmenting Treg function in tissues such as the liver. Thus, whether autophagy is a pro- or anti-immunogenic process is cancer type-specific or dependent on the nutritional context of the host remains an open question.

Using a fasting mimicking diet (FMD) low in calories and protein, Di Biase et al. reported that in combination with Doxorubicin, FMD promoted the accumulation of CD8+ TILs into tumors. FMD also increased the number of circulating CD8+ T cells, as well as common lymphoid progenitors in the bone marrow, forming an interesting parallel to another study demonstrating that caloric restriction promoted memory T cell trafficking to the bone marrow and more efficient responses to secondary challenge with tumors. Thus, metabolic adaptation to caloric restriction in tissues such as the bone marrow may be an important component of caloric restriction and anti-tumor immune responses.

Protein Restriction:

Limiting dietary amino acids has been the subject of investigation for nutrition and cancer therapy going back several decades. Using a murine model of lymphoma and two syngeneic tumors, Rubio-Patiño et al. demonstrated that tumor growth in mice fed a low protein diet was significantly decreased compared to that of mice fed an isocaloric low carbohydrate diet, and appeared dependent on immune-mediated clearance of the tumor. T cells isolated from tumor-bearing mice fed a low-protein diet were better at eliminating cancer cells in vitro. Moreover, cytotoxicity was tumor-antigen dependent, as CD8+ T cells failed to kill a different cancer cell line in vitro, and depletion of APCs abolished the effects in vivo. This complements observations that dietary protein restriction reprograms tumor-associated macrophages, and increases MHC-II+ macrophage infiltration to promote tumor clearance.

In addition to global protein restriction, recent studies exploring the relationship of restricting specific amino acids with cancer and immune function. Methionine is an essential amino acid that plays important roles in protein synthesis, nucleotide synthesis, and one-carbon metabolism. Circulating and intratumoral methionine levels are acutely sensitive to dietary methionine levels in mice and humans. Restriction of dietary methionine synergizes with chemotherapies targeting nucleotide synthesis, and reprograms TAMs to a less immunosuppressive phenotype, leading to increased GZMB+ CD8+ T cell recruitment and tumor clearance. As previously described, tumor cells and T cells compete for methionine and leads to aberrant epigenetic remodeling. Thus, it will be critical to understand how diet alters the generation of metabolic intermediates that regulate chromatin accessibility in TILs in the context of immunotherapy.

Serine is an important non-essential amino acid that fuels the serine-glycine one-carbon (SGOC) metabolic cycles. Many tumor types depend on increased uptake of exogenous serine or de novo serine synthesis to support nucleotide synthesis, NADPH production, and protein synthesis. Likewise, SGOC metabolism is required for nucleotide synthesis to support effector T cell proliferation upon activation. T cells cultured without serine in vitro retained activation kinetics, bioenergetics, and cytokine production, but failed to expand upon TCR engagement. This carried over in vivo, as restriction of dietary serine impaired proliferation of antigen-specific CD8+ T cells following challenge, indicating that although serine is non-essential at the organismal level, its availability directly impacts T cell responses. In tumors dependent on exogenous serine, restriction of dietary serine delays tumor growth, despite its requirement for proper T cell responses. How TILs to adapt to dietary serine restriction in tumors has yet to be elucidated.

Ketogenic Diet:

In contrast to protein restriction, ketogenic diets consist of high ratios of fat and protein over carbohydrates to promote production of ketone bodies as a fuel in lieu of glucose, and have been investigated as a potential therapeutic adjuvant in cancer treatment. An important breakthrough in the clinical application of ketogenic diet followed observations that PI3K inhibitors induced hyperglycemia and led to rebounds in PI3K signaling and tumor growth in an insulin-dependent manner following drug clearance. Using ketogenic diets to suppress circulating glucose in patients ameliorated this rebound signaling following treatment with PI3K inhibitors, and enforced the tumor-suppressive actions of the drug. The use of ketogenic diet in combination with immunotherapy, however, has not yet been extensively studied. In murine models of glioma, ketogenic diet prolonged survival in a CD8+ T cell-dependent manner. This was accompanied by decreased expression of the inhibitory receptors PD-1 and CTLA-4, as well as increases in production of IFNγ, TNFα, and IL-2. Interestingly, ketogenic diet had minimal effect on CD8+ T cell responses to acute or chronic LCMV infection, suggesting that the anti-tumor responses following ketogenic diet were potentially more specific to the tumor metabolic environment. Considering that CD8+ T cells compete with cancer cells and other suppressive cells in the TME for nutrients such as glucose, whether CD8+ TILs can metabolize ketones to support expansion and effector functions remains an important unanswered question.

CONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVE

The past decade has yielded major advances in our understanding of the metabolic pathways engaged by distinct T cell lineages, and the immunoregulatory role these pathways play in driving their function. Of particular interest have been exhausted CD8+ T cells infiltrating solid tumors, which must adapt to being outcompeted for metabolites by tumor cells and the generation of a hostile microenvironment with immunosuppressive factors such as acidosis and hypoxia. With the understanding that the TME is composed of a constellation of various immune and non-immune cell types, it will be critical to ask how the metabolic landscape of the TME affects their function, questions that will be borne out through deeper understanding of those cells’ individual metabolic requirements. To this end, it will be important to understand how tumor cells and T cells differentially adapt to metabolic stress, and how patient-specific variables may alter acquisition of these adaptations. For example, the identification of divergent metabolic adaptations to glutamine antagonism and high-fat feeding by TILs in mice raise important questions for the field. Are tumor cells more hardwired to their metabolic programs having undergone malignant transformation, and are T cells more adaptable to the available nutrients in different tissues? What are other differential metabolic adaptations between T cells and cancer cells, and what stresses are these induced by? The answers to these and other questions arising from the findings detailed in this review will potentially lead to novel strategies which can be exploited to improve efficacy of ICB therapies.

Additionally, the contribution of diet and systemic metabolism to nutrient availability in the TME remains an important frontier for understanding tumor metabolism and immunometabolism. Obesity due to poor diet and physical inactivity has become the second leading cause of preventable death in the United States behind smoking, and increases susceptibility to developing multiple types of cancer. Complicating the link between these syndromes and tumor immune responses, however, are observations that obesity correlates with improved responses to immunotherapies. A deeper understanding of how dietary nutrients reshape the metabolic landscape in tumors and delineating changes in tumor immune infiltrates between different host metabolic states will shed light on mechanisms behind the enhanced response to ICB in obese patients, and potentially heterogeneity in response to immunotherapy at large. Further, diet and systemic metabolism are actionable: diet modification and drugs that alter systemic metabolism may be attractive means to support more robust immune responses in the TME after immunotherapy.

Tangential to diet, increased gut microbial diversity is associated with improved responses to immunotherapy. The gut microbiota produces a variety of metabolites, most notably short-chain fatty acids (SCFA) from dietary fiber. Clinical studies have identified a positive association between fiber consumption and response to immune checkpoint inhibitors. Whether other microbial metabolites contribute to immune responses in tumors remains to be fully elucidated. For instance, microbiota-derived inosine augments anti-CTLA-4 therapy in a T cell-dependent manner. Intriguingly, inosine was also recently identified as an alternative carbon source for T cells in glucose-limiting conditions, such as the TME. Significantly more investigation is needed to unravel the intricacies of microbiota-derived metabolites and immunity.

Lastly, increased physical activity also induces changes to nutrient utilization and metabolism at the systemic and TME levels, and has been correlated with improved cancer outcomes, albeit frequently attributed to improved recovery and reduced treatment complications. Recent studies in mice demonstrate changes to the immune infiltrate in tumors in response to exercise, including increased CD8+ TILs engaging mitochondrial metabolism. However, the metabolic underpinnings behind exercise and anti-tumor immunity are still minimally understood.

Collectively, the works reviewed herein highlight the incredible capacity of T cells to adapt to local nutrient environments. However, these adaptations may be tolerogenic and therefore undesirable in the context of tumors and immunotherapy, underscoring the need to study immunometabolism in cancer using physiologically relevant models that recapitulate cellular and metabolic heterogeneity, as well as the contributions of systemic metabolism. Working towards a comprehensive understanding of nutrient availability in tumors and divergent metabolic requirements of T cells and tumor cells will provide a basis for the development of effective metabolic therapies in combination with the growing arsenal of cancer immunotherapies.
