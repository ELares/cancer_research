# Diagnostic and therapeutic advances for HER2-expressing or amplified gynecologic cancers.

## Abstract

Despite varied methodologies for HER2 testing, evidence supports that a substantial proportion of endometrial, ovarian, cervical, and vulvar cancers overexpress HER2. ## Full Text

Diagnostic and therapeutic advances for HER2-expressing or amplified gynecologic cancers

HER2-targeting therapies are well-described in breast, gastric, and lung cancers, however accumulating data supports a role for HER2-targeted therapies in gynecologic cancers. Despite varied methodologies for HER2 testing, evidence supports that a substantial proportion of endometrial, ovarian, cervical, and vulvar cancers overexpress HER2. This underscores the rationale for HER2-targeted therapies in these malignancies, including the use of HER2-directed tyrosine kinase inhibitors, antibody-drug conjugates, and immune-stimulating antibody conjugates. Understanding mechanisms of resistance to HER2-targeted therapies will inform possible combinatorial strategies.

Introduction

HER2, encoded by ERBB2, is a transmembrane receptor tyrosine kinase belonging to the HER family of receptors, which includes EGFR/HER1, HER3, and HER4. Homodimerization of HER2, or heterodimerization with other HER family receptors, initiates the transphosphorylation of these receptors' intracellular domains and subsequent activation of downstream signaling pathways, including the PI3K/AKT, MAPK, and JAK-STAT pathways. A growing body of evidence supports a role for HER2 and HER2-targeting therapies within gynecologic cancers. In this review, we discuss advances in our understanding of HER2 diagnostics, emerging therapeutic strategies, and mechanisms of resistance to HER2-directed therapies.

Diagnostic testing for HER2 in gynecologic cancers

Disease-specific HER2 grading criteria have been developed for both breast and gastric cancer by the American Society of Clinical Oncology and the College of American Pathologists (ASCO/CAP). However, there is currently no single widely accepted gynecologic-specific criteria, and several different scoring systems have been used to assess HER2 expression in gynecologic malignancies, including the modified 2007 ASCO/CAP breast critera, 2013 ASCO/CAP breast criteria, 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric criteria, and 2018 ASCO/CAP breast criteria, summarized in Table 1. The main differences between the scoring systems are: 1) the percentage of cells showing strong staining to count as positive (10 % vs 30 %), 2) if circumferential membranous staining is required or if basolateral staining suffices, 3) the criteria for amplification positivity by in situ hybridization (ISH) and 4) the order of testing (IHC first with ISH reflex versus allowing primary ISH testing). Although specific cut-offs vary, most institutions implement the systems by using HER2 immunohistochemistry (IHC) as a first line test scored as 0, 1+, 2+, or 3+, with reflex ISH for cases with equivocal (2+) protein expression. Discordances between ISH and IHC have been seen in up to 36 % of cases in some studies in endometrial and ovarian cancers, while others have shown excellent concordance between ISH and IHC. It is unknown if overexpression by IHC or amplification by ISH is more effective at predicting response to anti-HER2 monoclonal antibody therapy in gynecologic cancer. However, given the mechanism of action of HER2-targeting ADCs, which involves binding of the ADC to the cell surface HER2 receptor, it is logical that HER2 protein expression via IHC is imperative, regardless of ERBB2 amplification levels within the nucleus. As the 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric scoring criteria requires primary HER2 IHC testing, this criteria was utilized in the DESTINY Pantumor02 trial of trastuzumab deruxtecan, a HER2-targeting ADC.

Unlike breast carcinomas which typically show circumferential HER2 staining, cervical, endometrial, and ovarian carcinomas often show basolateral positivity with apical sparing, similar to gastric adenocarcinomas (Fig. 1). As a result, most gynecologic studies use a modification of the ASCO/CAP breast criteria which allows for basolateral staining. Some authors have suggested that gastric criteria should be used for mucinous ovarian and endocervical adenocarcinomas given their phenotypic similarity to gastrointestinal tumors. Two systems have predominantly been used in clinical trials to date: the modified 2007 ASCO/CAP breast criteria was used in a phase II trial of trastuzumab in uterine serous carcinoma (USC), while the 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric criteria was used in the phase II STATICE trial of trastuzumab deruxtecan in uterine carcinosarcoma (UCS) and the phase II DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial of trastuzumab deruxtecan, which included cervical, endometrial, and ovarian tumors. Of note, FISH was not used as inclusion criteria in either the STATICE or DESTINY-PanTumor02 trials and treatment was based solely on IHC expression.

Comparisons between different HER2 scoring systems in gynecologic malignancies have generally found good intersystem agreement, suggesting that the specific system may not change the HER2 status for the majority of patients. The 2013 and 2018 ASCO/CAP breast guidelines have a lower threshold for positivity compared to the 2007 guidelines (10 % vs 30 % of cells), and therefore the 2013/2018 criteria have a positivity rate 3–4 % higher than the 2007 guidelines. There has not yet been a study which directly addresses which system best predicts response to anti-HER2 therapy.

A multi-institutional interobserver variability study of the modified 2007 breast criteria used in the phase II trial of trastuzumab in USC by Fader et al. found good interobserver agreement (kappa 0.65) when considering three categories of staining (0/1+,2+,3+). However, when including 4 categories (i.e., differentiating 0 and 1+), kappa was 0.60 in USC, similar to the poor interobserver variability in assessing 0 staining in breast carcinomas. The imprecision of differentiating 0 and 1+ staining is of clinical relevance when selecting patients for HER2-low expressing therapies.

ERBB2 amplification can also be detected from sequencing data and has been validated in targeted panels for breast, gastroesophageal, and endometrial serous carcinomas. Although these studies demonstrated excellent concordance with IHC/FISH, sequencing is not routinely used as a first line test for ERBB2 amplification. It may have lower sensitivity, potentially secondary to tumor heterogeneity or low purity. HER2 expression and amplification has been examined in endometrial, ovarian, cervical, and vulvar malignancies (Table 2), although the rates of positivity vary widely between studies. Explanations for the wide ranges include differences in scoring criteria, anti-HER2 antibody clones, and patient populations. Several different monoclonal antibodies have been used in gynecologic HER2 studies, including 4B5, EP3, SP3, and CB1132clones. A comparison of different antibody clones in Mullerian clear cell carcinomas identified poor agreement between clones.

Although the precise definition of heterogeneity (localized clonal ERBB2 amplification) varies between studies, it is found in 10–55 % of USC, 6–39 % of carcinosarcomas, 17 % of cervical adenocarcinomas, 80 % of cervical gastric type adenocarcinomas, and in up to 26 % of mucinous ovarian carcinomas. Heterogeneous amplification has two implications. First, it is associated with a worse response to anti-HER2 therapies in endometrial carcinoma. Second, the block selected for HER2 testing may not be representative of the entire tumor. For example, Rottman et al. found a concordance rate of 84 % between USC endometrial biopsy and hysterectomy specimens. Halle et al., studying all histotypes of endometrial cancer, identified a discordance rate of 23 % between the primary tumor and metastatic sites. Furthermore, metastatic sites within the same patient showed different HER2 expression profiles in 16 % of cases. A lower discordance rate (4 %) was found between primary and metastatic sites of ovarian tumors. Overall, these findings support testing more than one specimen for HER2 status and suggest there may be value in re-testing recurrent disease.

There is considerable debate currently regarding which scoring criteria is best for patients with endometrial cancer. Until clinical trial data reports on treatment outcomes that differ based on scoring system, we cannot claim that one method is better than another. For example, ancillary results from the TOGA trial, which tested trastuzumab in addition to chemotherapy for patients with advanced GEJ cancer, showed that trastuzumab improved outcomes for patients with HER2 positive disease based on IHC testing, but not for those patients who were HER2 positive based on ISH only. This then informed the 2016 ASCO/ CAP gastric HER2 testing guidelines.

Given that no such clinical trial data has been reported in gynecologic malignancies, there is a lack of high quality evidence that one scoring system is better than others. However, in practice, there is an emerging consensus that all advanced stage and recurrent USC should have HER2 IHC performed and reported using the modified 2007 ASCO/CAP breast criteria from the Fader et al. clinical trial, with reflex FISH for cases with 2+ (equivocal) expression. Although this criterion has been endorsed by the College of American Pathologists for USC, many labs continue to use the 2018 ASCO/CAP breast criteria. For non-USC gynecologic cancers, HER2 testing requests are derived from results of the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial; therefore, in those cases, pathologists are advised to perform testing using HER2 IHC scored using the 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric criteria, without reflex FISH testing. HER2 IHC of all recurrent gynecologic cancers is now widely accepted as standard of care, given the accelerated FDA approval of T-DXd for recurrent HER2 IHC 3+ solid tumors. Communication between pathologists and medical oncologists is helpful to ensure HER2 is reported using a system which corresponds with the therapy under consideration. At this time, there is insufficient evidence to institute testing of all early stage gynecologic cancers, given the concomitant cost and workload requirements, and as standard first-line therapies do not currently incorporate HER2-directed agents. However, as ongoing and future clinical trials examine upfront treatment with HER2-directed therapies, HER2 testing of early stage gynecologic cancers may eventually become standard of care. At this time, it is recommended to obtain HER2 testing in early stage cancers if enrollment into a HER2-targeting clinical trial is under consideration (see Author Opinions on HER2 Testing).

Emerging HER2-directed therapies & combinatorial strategies

A rapidly growing array of HER2-targeting agents is under investigation in gynecologic cancers, spanning a wide range of therapeutic classes, from small molecule inhibitors to HER2-directed immunostimulatory compounds (Tables 3 & 4).

Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKI)

One therapeutic strategy targeting HER2 is to inhibit its tyrosine kinase function, mitigating the downstream pro-survival and cellular proliferation signaling that may contribute to cancer pathobiology. HER2-directed TKIs compete with ATP at the intracellular catalytic domain, thereby blocking phosphorylation and activation of downstream signaling.

Evidence suggests that the efficacy of HER2-directed TKIs in gynecologic cancers is influenced by the potency of the TKI, appropriate patient selection, and/or the use of combination strategies. In GOG-0170G, lapatinib, a reversible inhibitor of EGFR/HER1 and HER2, demonstrated limited clinical activity as monotherapy in recurrent epithelial ovarian carcinoma, with no responses observed. Similar results with lapatinib were seen in patients with endometrial cancer in the phase II GOG-0229D trial, in which 1 of 30 patients achieved a partial response (PR). Of note, HER2 expression was not required for enrollment to either GOG-0170G or GOG-0229D, and retrospective analyses of HER2 IHC demonstrated limited expression: HER2 expression was seen in only 15 % of ovarian cancer patients in GOG-1070G (HER2 IHC positivity defined as any staining of the tumor cell, intensity scored 1+ to 4+) and only 12 % of endometrial cancer patients in GOG-0229D (HER2 positivity by H-score, utilizing HER2 IHC scored 1+ to 3+).

Neratinib is an irreversible inhibitor that covalently binds the ATP-binding domain of EGFR/HER1, HER2, and HER4, augmenting endocytic degradation of HER2 and reducing levels of pRB and cyclin D1 to induce G1/S phase arrest. Anti-tumor activity of neratinib was demonstrated in trastuzumab-resistant breast cancer cell lines; these findings translated to clinical benefit with improved disease-free survival rates in women with HER2-positive breast cancer who had previously received trastuzumab. This highlights a potential role for neratinib in gynecologic cancers resistant to trastuzumab, with particular relevance for USC and UCS given the incorporation of trastuzumab into the treatment paradigm for primary advanced or recurrent disease. Similarly, afatinib, another irreversible inhibitor of EGFR/HER1, HER2, and HER4, is under evaluation in HER2 2+/3+ USC (NCT02491099).

In the SUMMIT basket trial of neratinib in HER2- and HER3-mutated solid tumors (NCT01953926), including cervical, endometrial, and ovarian cancer patients, RECIST responses were noted in patients with cervical cancer. An ORR of 25 % (3/12) was seen in cervical cancer patients, with a median PFS of 7.0 months, median OS of 16.8 months, and clinical benefit rate (PR + SD ≥ 16 weeks) of50 %. The expression of HER2 by IHC was not reported for these patients, however retrospective analysis concluded that ERBB2 amplification did not confer additional sensitivity to neratinib if an activating HER2 mutation was present.

Preclinical studies suggest a possible role for neratinib in ovarian cancer as well. Neratinib exhibited cytotoxic effects across ERBB2-amplified and HER2 IHC-positive ovarian cancer cell lines, including serous and clear cell histologic subtypes, however a more pronounced effect was seen when neratinib was combined with olaparib. This combination demonstrated greater anti-tumor effect compared to either agent alone in HER2-expressing ovarian cancer models. Inhibition of HER2, with downstream effects on the PI3K and MAPK pathways, may lead to altered genetic transcription and impaired capacity for homologous recombination repair, presenting an opportunity for synergy with PARP inhibitors. A similar combinatorial synergy was observed with the combination of neratinib and niraparib in additional preclinical ovarian cancer studies. The ongoing phase I/Ib iNNOVATE trial (NCT04502602) seeks to investigate the clinical activity of combined neratinib/niraparib in advanced solid tumors, with dose expansion in platinum resistant ovarian cancer. Combined HER2 and PARP inhibition was also effective in preclinical models of USC treated with neratinib and olaparib, a cancer type which shares several features associated with genomic instability with high grade serous ovarian carcinoma. Extrapolating from these data, neratinib as monotherapy or in combination with a PARP inhibitor may have a role in USC and UCS in a post-trastuzumab setting, given its efficacy in the setting of trastuzumab resistance and the use of trastuzumab with chemotherapy in HER2 2+/3+ USC and UCS.

Monospecific (monoparatopic) antibodies

Trastuzumab is a monospecific antibody which binds an epitope in the extracellular domain of HER2 to impair receptor dimerization, leading to HER2 endocytosis, antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), G1/S phase cell cycle arrest, and increased activity of PTEN with more pronounced inhibition of the PI3K pathway. Incorporation of trastuzumab into the treatment landscape for high grade endometrial cancers arose from a phase II clinical trial evaluating trastuzumab in primary advanced, metastatic, or recurrent USC. Patients with USC expressing HER2 IHC 2+ with a positive confirmatory FISH, or HER2 IHC 3+ (modified 2007 ASCO/CAP breast cancer scoring guidelines), were randomized to receive carboplatin and paclitaxel with or without trastuzumab, followed by trastuzumab maintenance therapy. The addition of trastuzumab to carboplatin/paclitaxel improved median progression free survival (PFS) by 4.9 months in all response-evaluable patients (12.9 months vs 8.0 months; HR 0.46, 95 % CI 0.28–0.76, p = 0.005). In subgroup analysis, patients with stage III or IV disease receiving primary treatment derived the greatest PFS benefit, with the addition of trastuzumab corresponding to an 8.4 month improvement in median PFS (17.7 months vs 9.3 months, HR 0.44, 90%CI 0.23–0.83, p = 0.015). Overall survival was also significantly higher in patients receiving trastuzumab, with prolongation of OS most notable in those with primary stage III or IV disease. Within this subgroup, median OS was not reached in the trastuzumab arm, compared to a median OS of 25.4 months in the control arm (HR = 0.49, 90 % CI 0.25–0.97, p = 0.041), underscoring the role of HER2 in USC. Notably, no significant OS benefit was observed in patients with recurrent USC receiving trastuzumab, demonstrating a continued unmet clinical need in this setting.

Combining HER2-directed monospecific antibodies may provide greater tumor-directed effect than the use of individual monospecific antibodies. Pertuzumab, a monospecific anti-HER2 antibody, binds within subdomain II of HER2, sterically blocking HER2 homo- and heterodimerization, distinct from trastuzumab binding of domain IV. Synergistic activity between trastuzumab and pertuzumab may be due to stronger inhibition of downstream HER2 signaling and augmented antibody- and complement-dependent cytotoxicity. In patients with metastatic HER2-positive breast cancer, the addition of trastuzumab/pertuzumab to chemotherapy significantly improved PFS and OS, compared to women receiving trastuzumab and chemotherapy. Extrapolating from these data in breast cancer, the ongoing randomized phase II/III NRG-GY026 trial in HER2-expressing endometrial cancer, using the 2018 ASCO/CAP breast criteria to define HER2 positivity, seeks to evaluate the combination of carboplatin/paclitaxel with trastuzumab with or without pertuzumab (NCT05256225).

The combination of an anti-HER2 monospecific antibody with a HER2-targeted TKI presents an avenue for further enhancing anticancer effects. Tucatinib, a highly selective and potent reversible HER2-targeting TKI with minimal inhibition of other HER family receptors, yielded enhanced and prolonged inhibition of downstream PI3K/ AKT and MAPK signaling when given in combination with trastuzumab in cancer xenograft models. Furthermore, the combination of trastuzumab/tucatinib caused significantly greater regression in patient-derived xenograft breast and gastric cancer models compared to either agent alone, highlighting the efficacy of dual mechanisms of HER2 blockade. These data underlie the rationale for the use of trastuzumab/tucatinib with capecitabine in HER2-positive breast cancer, a setting in which these agents received FDA approval based on results of the randomized phase III HER2CLIMB trial. This combination also received accelerated FDA approval based on a phase 2 trial in HER2-positive colorectal cancer, in which an objective response rate (ORR) of 38 % was demonstrated. Further evaluation of trastuzumab/tucatinib in other cancer types will be investigated in the SGNTUC-019 trial (NCT04579380), a phase II basket trial that will enroll cervical and uterine cancer patients in tumor-specific cohorts; a separate cohort will allow other ERBB2-amplified or HER2-overexpressed solid tumors.

Bispecific (biparatopic) antibodies

Efforts are underway to synthesize novel antibodies with improved anti-tumor activity, including the development of bispecific antibodies which bind to two epitopes. Zanidatamab (ZW25), a bispecific IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting two domains on HER2 (the same domains that are targeted by trastuzumab and pertuzumab) binds HER2 molecules in trans, inducing HER2 receptor clustering, receptor internalization and downregulation, ADCC, antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP), and complement-dependent cytotoxicity distinct from trastuzumab and pertuzumab. These effects contribute to a greater anti-tumor effect by zanidatamab in vivo compared to trastuzumab and pertuzumab. An ORR of36 % (13/36) and clinical benefit rate (CBR) of 53 % was observed in the dose expansion cohort of a phase I trial of zanidatamab, which included patients with endometrial, ovarian/fallopian tube, and vulvar cancers. Target lesion shrinkage ≥30 % was seen in 2 patients with ovarian cancer (both HER2 IHC 3+), 1 patient with vulvar cancer (HER2 IHC 2+), and 1 patient with endometrial cancer (HER2 IHC 3+). A phase 2 trial of zanidatamab (NCT04513665) seeks to determine the efficacy of zanidatamab in recurrent or persistent HER2-positive (IHC 3+, or 2+ with confirmatory FISH) or ERBB2-amplified endometrial cancers.

Antibody drug conjugates (ADCs)

ADCs are a rapidly evolving therapeutic class in gynecologic cancers. Typically comprised of an antibody engineered to recognize a tumor-specific antigen, conjugated to a cytotoxic payload via a chemical linker, ADCs exhibit substantial potential in cancer therapy, leveraging the targeted delivery of cytotoxic payloads to tumor cells while minimizing systemic toxicity.

Trastuzumab deruxtecan

Trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd, DS-8201a), utilizing a trastuzumab antibody backbone conjugated to an exatecan-derivative topoisomerase-1-inhibiting payload (DXd), has exhibited noteworthy clinical efficacy in gynecologic cancer cohorts in the phase II DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial (Table 5), leading to integration of T-DXd into the National Comprehensive Cancer Center Network (NCCN) guidelines for recurrent HER2 IHC 2+ and 3+ endometrial, cervical, and ovarian cancers and contributing to the accelerated FDA approval of T-DXd for HER2 IHC3+ solid tumors. Importantly, though the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial used the 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric scoring criteria, neither the NCCN guidelines nor the accelerated FDA approval specify a scoring system required for determination of HER2 positivity.

In patients with endometrial cancer, an ORR of 84.6 % (median PFS not reached, median OS 26.0 months) was seen in those with HER2 IHC 3+, and an ORR of 47.1 % (median PFS 8.5 months, median OS 16.4 months) was seen in patients with HER2 IHC 2+. T-DXd appears to be effective even in patients with low or undetectable HER2, though generalizability of efficacy in this subgroup is limited by the small sample size. On central HER2 testing, 10 % (4/40) of endometrial cancer patients were found to have IHC 1+ and 12.5 % (5/40) had IHC 0. Patients with IHC 1+ demonstrated an ORR of 25 %, a median PFS of 1.2 months, and median OS of 5.2 months; those with IHC 0 demonstrated an ORR of 60.0 %, median PFS of 9.1 months, and median OS was not reached. Additionally, T-DXd has demonstrated activity in UCS specifically, including in patients with HER2 IHC 1+. The phase II STATICE trial conducted in Japan revealed that among UCS patients with HER2 IHC ≥2+ (n = 22), an investigator-assessed ORR of 68.2 % (54.5 % by central review) was observed, along with a median DOR of 6.9 months, median PFS of 6.2 months, and median OS of 13.3 months [ ]. For the smaller subset of patients with HER2 IHC 1+ (n = 10), the investigator-assessed ORR was 60.0 %, median DOR was 8.1 months, median PFS was 6.7 months, and median OS was not reached. Acknowledging these encouraging data, it is unknown whether these results can be generalized outside of the STATICE trial’s ethnically homogeneous patient population.

In patients with cervical cancer in the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial, an ORR of 75 % was seen in those patients with IHC 3+, and the median PFS and median OS were not reached. An ORR of 40 % was seen in those with IHC 2+, with a median PFS of 4.8 months and median OS of 11.5 months. In patients with cervical cancer with HER2 IHC 1+ (20 %, 8/40), an ORR 50 % was seen, with a median PFS of 11.1 months and median OS was not reached. In those with IHC 0 (10 %, 4/40), an ORR 50 %, median PFS of 5.4 months, and median OS of 9.5 months was seen. These data continue to support the efficacy of T-DXd even in HER2-low settings and represents a promising therapeutic option in a cancer type with limited options for the treatment of recurrent disease.

Patients with ovarian cancer continued to demonstrate benefit with T-DXd in the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial. Patients with IHC 3+ had an ORR of 63.6 %, median PFS of 12.5 months and median OS of 20.0 months, recapitulating the trend of greater clinical activity with higher levels of HER2 expression. Ovarian cancer patients with HER2 IHC 2+ yielded an ORR of 36.8 %, median PFS of 4.1 months, and median OS of 13 months. Those with IHC 1+ (12.5 %, 5/40) demonstrated an ORR of 20 %, median PFS of 6.9 months, and median OS 7.7 months; and patients with IHC 0 (12.5 %, 5/40) demonstrated an ORR of 60 %, median PFS of 5.6 months, and median OS of 12.3 months.

Overall, the clinical activity observed in endometrial, cervical, and ovarian cancers, including cases with low HER2 expression (IHC 1+ and 0), establish the promising clinical activity of T-DXd in these settings. Acknowledging the overall small cohort sizes in DESTINY-PanTumor02, T-DXd should be considered for patients in the context of remaining standard of care therapies and available clinical trials. Enrollment of additional HER2 IHC 1+ and 2+ patients to the gynecologic cancer cohorts in DESTINY-Pantumor02 is ongoing, and results will further clarify the role of T-DXd in the treatment paradigms of these cancers. The responses seen across the range of HER2 expression, including levels considered negative by traditional scoring systems, underscore the diagnostic challenge of HER2 testing.

DB-1303

DB-1303, an alternative HER2-directed ADC employing a novel topoisomerase-1-inhibiting payload (P1003) has received FDA fast-track designation for the treatment of advanced or recurrent HER2-expressing malignant solid tumors. In preliminary results from a phase Ia/II trial (NCT05150691), evaluable endometrial cancer patients receiving DB-1303 achieved an ORR of 58.8 % (10/17). Particularly noteworthy was the observed efficacy in high-grade endometrial cancers, encompassing UCS with an ORR of 87.5 % (7/8) and USC with an ORR of 50 % (1/2). Enrollment of patients with HER2-expressing advanced solid tumors is ongoing.

Trastuzumab duocarmazine

Trastuzumab duocarmazine (SYD985), a HER2-targeting ADC conjugating a DNA-alkylating duocarmycin payload to trastuzumab, showed promise in a phase I trial involving 14 HER2-expressing endometrial cancer patients (HER2 IHC ≥1+ or ISH-positive). Among 13 evaluable endometrial cancer patients, an ORR of 39 % and a median PFS of 4.3 months were observed. Ocular toxicity emerged as a common adverse event. A phase 2 trial (NCT04205630) is ongoing in HER2 IHC ≥1+ endometrial cancers to further explore the clinical activity of trastuzumab duocarmazine.

Disitamab vedotin

HER2-targeting ADCs utilizing anti-mitotic payloads are also in development. Disitamab vedotin (RC48) utilizes a novel disitamab (also referred to as hertuzumab) antibody backbone conjugated to monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE). Preclinical studies suggest that internalization of the HER2-bound disitamab vedotin occurs to a greater degree than with the trastuzumab-based ADCs T-DM1 (ado-trastuzumab emtansine) and T-DXd. Disitamab vedotin is currently in development across solid tumors, including ovarian and endometrial cancers expressing HER2 IHC ≥1+ (NCT06003231).

ADC combinations: enhancing anti-cancer effects

One combinatorial strategy aims to enhance the efficacy of an ADC’s cytotoxic payload. For example, the addition of a PARP inhibitor with a topoisomerase-inhibiting payload prevents the resolution of topoisomerase I cleavage complexes and repair of subsequent single- and double-stranded DNA breaks, thereby potentiating DNA damage and genomic instability. This approach underlies the rationale for a phase I trial of T-DXd and olaparib in HER2-expressing solid tumors (IHC ≥1+) with dose expansion in USC (NCT04585958). A related strategy pairs a topoisomerase-inhibiting payload with an ATR inhibitor as an alternative means of promoting replication stress. Inhibiting ATR in the context of topoisomerase I inhibition disrupts proper replication fork initiation and elongation, resulting in replication fork collisions, accumulation of DNA damage (as evidenced by increased levels of phospho-RPA and yH2AX), and allows dysregulated progression through intra-S and G2/M-phase cell cycle checkpoints, ultimately leading to mitotic catastrophe. Preclinical studies of exatecan and ceralasertib (an ATR inhibitor) demonstrated increased cell death and tumor growth inhibition with the combination compared to each agent alone. Similar findings were observed in studies of ceralasertib and belotecan, a topoisomerase-inhibiting camptothecin analogue. Encouraging safety and initial efficacy results from a phase I trial combining sacitizumab govitecan, a Trop2-directed ADC containing the topoisomerase I inhibitor SN-38, with the ATR inhibitor berzosertib (VX-970), suggests the potential viability of this combinatorial strategy, potentially extending to HER2-directed ADCs. An ongoing phase I/Ib trial (DASH Trial, NCT04704661) is investigating the combination of T-DXd and ceralasertib in HER2-expressing (IHC ≥1+) or ERBB2-amplified solid tumors.

A second combinatorial strategy pairing HER2-directed TKIs with HER2-targeting ADCs combines dual mechanisms of HER2 receptor inhibition and TKI-induced augmentation of receptor endocytosis, thereby enhancing internalization of the HER2-bound ADC. This rationale underlies the combination of T-DXd and neratinib, an irreversible TKI shown to increase endocytosis and subsequent degradation of HER2. This combination is under investigation in a phase I trial (NCT05372614) enrolling solid tumors with HER2 overexpression (IHC 3+ or 2+ with confirmatory FISH), ERBB2 amplification, or activating ERBB2 mutations. Comparably, the planned DESTINY-Breast07 trial (NCT04538742) will evaluate the combination of T-DXd and tucatinib. The addition of tucatinib to disitamab vedotin also enhanced ADC internalization, with significant tumor growth inhibition of breast cancer xenograft models compared to disitamab vedotin alone. Notably, this anti-tumor effect was seen in HER2-low (IHC 0 and 1+) models and models resistant to T-DXd. A phase Ib/II trial of disitamab vedotin and tucatinib is planned to evaluate breast and gastric cancers (NCT06157892, SGNDV-004), though there is potential applicability of this combination in gynecologic cancers.

A third combinatorial strategy leverages the immunostimulatory effects of the payload and the antibody backbone by pairing an ADC with an immune checkpoint inhibitor. Anti-mitotic and topoisomerase-inhibiting chemotherapeutics induce immunogenic cell death, activating dendritic cells and T-cells, upregulating PD-L1 and MHC class I expression, and exhibiting synergy with immune checkpoint inhibitors in preclinical models. Furthermore, many ADCs utilize an IgG1 antibody, which harbors the ability to induce ADCC, ADCP, Fc receptor-based activation of immune cells within the local tumor microenvironment, and complement-dependent cytotoxicity. In preclinical studies, the combination of T-DXd with anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 antibodies demonstrated synergy in xenograft models, providing the rationale for ongoing phase I clinical trials of T-DXd combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors in breast, lung, and gastric cancers (NCT04538742, NCT04042701, NCT04379596). This strategy may be of particular interest in gynecologic cancers given the promising results from the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial of T-DXd and the emerging role of immune checkpoint inhibitors in the treatment of advanced or recurrent endometrial and cervical cancers. Similarly, preclinical studies of disitamab vedotin demonstrate increased intratumoral T-cell infiltration, with enhanced immune cell infiltration and synergistic anti-tumor activity when given in combination with anti-PD-1 or anti-PD-L1 agents. Clinical trials are exploring combinations of disitamab vedotin with anti-PD-1 immune checkpoint inhibitors tislelizumab (NCT05495724, NCT05493683), sintilimab (NCT05928897), toripalimab (NCT06155383, NCT06105008), and pembrolizumab (NCT04879329, NCT05911295) across gastrointestinal, lung, breast, and urothelial malignancies. A phase II trial of disitamab vedotin combined with zimberelimab (anti-PD-1) (NCT06155396) is planned for patients with recurrent or metastatic HER2-expressing cervical cancer.

Immune-stimulating antibody conjugates (ISACs)

ISACs are an emerging class of HER2-directed therapies, consisting of an anti-HER2 antibody to which an immune-stimulating payload, rather than a cytotoxic payload, is conjugated. BDC-1001, a first-in-class anti-HER2 ISAC, consists of a HER2-targeting trastuzumab biosimilar backbone with a TLR7/8 agonist payload, which induces FcR-dependent phagocytosis, toll-like receptor (TLR) stimulation, and priming and expansion of tumor-specific T-cells for a durable adaptive immune response. In HER2-expressing xenograft models, BDC-1001 demonstrated greater anti-tumor effect compared to trastuzumab and trastuzumab/pertuzumab and allowed mice to resist tumor re-challenge, indicating the formation of immunologic memory. Preliminary evidence of clinical activity was demonstrated in a phase I/II trial (NCT04278144) enrolling HER2 IHC 2+/3+ and ERBB2-amplified solid tumors to receive BDC-1001 monotherapy or the combination of BDC-1001 with nivolumab. Six patients achieved partial responses, including 1 patient with ovarian cancer treated with BDC-1001 + nivolumab, who had received previous HER2-directed therapy. A reduction in target lesions was seen in additional ovarian, endometrial, and cervical cancer patients. Twelve patients had prolonged stable disease ≥24 weeks, including 1 patient with endometrial cancer (BDC-1001 monotherapy, with 36 weeks of disease control), 1 patient with ovarian cancer (BDC-1001 + nivolumab, with 36 weeks of disease control), and 2 patients with cervical cancer (both receiving BDC-1001 monotherapy, with 24 weeks and 60 weeks of disease control respectively). This trial will enroll patients with HER2 IHC 3+ or HER2-amplified endometrial cancer in a phase II dose expansion cohort (NCT04278144).

Mechanisms of resistance to HER2-targeting therapies

Understanding the mechanisms governing resistance to HER2-directed therapies is critical to optimizing therapeutic efficacy, developing novel combinations, and defining the patient populations likely to derive clinical benefit from HER2-targeted therapies. Though multiple resistance mechanisms have been described (Fig. 2), including transcriptional downregulation of HER2, recycling of HER2-containing endosomes, upregulation of drug efflux pumps, and upregulation of anti-apoptotic proteins, the remainder of this discussion will focus on HER2 isoforms and downstream signaling.

HER2 isoforms and HER2 heterogeneity

Alterations occurring within the HER2 receptor can confer resistance to HER2-targeting agents. Modifications to the extracellular domain (ECD) of HER2 may lead to loss of the epitopes crucial for binding by HER2-directed antibodies and ADCs. Among several alterations is the emergence of p95HER2, a truncated form of HER2 lacking the ECD, including the epitope targeted by trastuzumab. Proteolytic cleavage of full-length HER2 by ADAM matrix metalloproteinases generates a 90–95 kDa protein which lacks the ECD but retains the intracellular domain (ICD) and kinase activity. This truncated isoform can remain membrane-anchored and continue to dimerize with full-length HER2 with resultant downstream signaling activation. A different HER2 isoform results from translation of the HER2 transcript beginning at an alternative intragenic initiation codon, resulting in a p95HER2 fragment of slightly larger size (100–115 kDa). This isoform, also referred to as the 611-carboxy-terminal-fragment (611-CTF), retains a few residues of the ECD which, importantly, allows the formation of disulfide bonds and thus can generate covalently bonded, constitutively active 611-CTF p95HER2 homodimers. A high ratio of 611-CTF p95HER2 to full-length HER2 is associated with poorer outcomes in women with breast cancer treated with trastuzumab-based therapies, indicating that this HER2 isoform may be clinically significant. In a single-institution study of women with high grade endometrial cancer, 53 % of cases were found to have high levels of 611-CTF p95HER2, which was associated with elevated levels of total HER2 protein expression. Levels of 611-CTF p95HER2 were significantly higher in endometrial cancer specimens compared to breast cancer specimens, highlighting potential differences in the biology of HER2 between these disease types.

In a separate study, levels of HER2 were assessed using antibodies targeted to the ICD or to the ECD: 8 % of USC specimens had high ICD and low ECD HER2 levels, compared to 42 % of high grade serous ovarian specimens, suggesting a mechanism by which ECD-binding HER2-targeting therapies, such as trastuzumab or T-DXd, may be less effective in ovarian cancer. These findings also demonstrate the limitation of HER2 IHC diagnostic antibodies, some of which bind the ICD and thus may inaccurately identify patients harboring truncated HER2 isoforms as candidates for ECD-binding HER2-directed therapies.

The d16HER2 splice variant (also denoted Δ16HER2), results from skipping of exon 16, thereby generating a truncated ECD. Crucially, a small, retained portion of the ECD exposes cysteine residues which facilitates the formation of covalently-bound, hyperactive d16HER2 homodimers. Emerging evidence points to a role for d16HER2 in driving epithelial-mesenchymal transition and metastasis, though evidence for its role in resistance or response to HER2 antibody-based therapeutics is conflicting. To our knowledge, the expression of d16HER2 in gynecologic malignancies has not yet been reported.

The expression of multiple HER2 isoforms may explain, in part, the heterogeneity of response to HER2-directed therapies, as different isoforms encode distinct tumor growth trajectories, and compounds the well-described intra- and inter-tumoral heterogeneity seen with total HER2 expression. Intra-tumoral heterogeneity in high-grade endometrial cancers is associated with resistance to trastuzumab and T-DM1, and discordance in HER2 expression between primary tumors and metastatic lesions in UCS is a poor prognostic factor. To address constitutively activated HER2 isoforms, strategies include targeting the intracellular kinase domain, inhibiting downstream pathways, or using ADCs with potent bystander effect to circumvent HER2 heterogeneity. Assessing HER2 isoforms in patients with intrinsic or acquired resistance to therapies targeting the extracellular domain will be crucial to understanding biomarkers of response or resistance to HER2-directed therapies.

Activation of downstream signaling pathways

Activating mutations within intracellular signaling pathways downstream of HER2 kinase activity effectively uncouple these pathways from therapies targeting HER2 and represent another mechanism of resistance to HER2-targeted agents.

Mutations in the PI3K/AKT pathway are prevalent in gynecologic cancers and are well-described mechanisms of resistance to HER2 therapies. Loss of PTEN, a key tumor suppressor that normally functions to inhibit the PI3K pathway, or activating mutations in PIK3CA, can lead to constitutive activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway. These genetic alterations are recognized as established contributors to resistance against HER2-directed antibodies trastuzumab and pertuzumab in breast cancer. Additionally, preclinical investigations underscore the association between activating alterations in the PI3K pathway and resistance to afatinib, a HER2-targeting TKI, in models of high-grade serous ovarian carcinoma and USC. Combining a HER2-directed therapy with an inhibitor of the PI3K pathway represents a promising approach to counteract this resistance mechanism. Clinical evidence supporting this strategy has emerged from studies demonstrating clinical benefit in breast cancer patients harboring PI3K pathway alterations treated with everolimus (mTOR inhibitor) in conjunction with trastuzumab and chemotherapy. A phase I trial exploring the combination of neratinib and everolimus in HER2-amplified or ERBB2-mutated solid tumors (NCT03065387) aims to explore this combinatorial approach.

The use of ADCs may circumvent the activation of downstream signaling pathways, as a main mechanism of action is through payload-induced cytotoxicity, independent of the PI3K pathway, and pairing an anti-HER2 ADC with a PI3K pathway inhibitor could potentially enhance anti-cancer activity. Preclinical evidence suggests that the combination of T-DXd and the pan-AKT inhibitor capivasertib has greater anti-tumor efficacy compared to either agent alone, with greater durability of response. However, the safety and tolerability of such combinations will be imperative to determine if this strategy can be translated into clinical practice. The planned DESTINY-Breast08 trial arm evaluating the combination of T-DXd and capivasertib in HER2 IHC 1+/2+ breast cancer (NCT04556773) will provide valuable insights into this therapeutic approach.

The MAP kinase (MAPK) pathway, a key intracellular signaling pathway downstream of HER2, is also frequently altered in gynecologic cancers and represents another mechanism of resistance to HER2-targeted therapy. In breast cancers previously exposed to anti-HER2 therapies, MAPK-activating mutations were enriched and associated with subsequent resistance to tucatinib and neratinib but sensitivity to MEK/ERK inhibition. Increased MAPK signaling was also observed in trastuzumab-resistant gastric cancer cells. Combining trametinib (MEK inhibitor) with trastuzumab augmented anti-tumor efficacy in trastuzumab-resistant gastric cancer xenografts, compared to the anti-tumor effect of either agent alone. However, the selection of HER2- and MAPK pathway-inhibiting agents is paramount to the clinical activity of the combination. A phase I trial investigating the combination of neratinib and trametinib in patients with ERBB2-amplified, ERBB2-mutant, or KRAS-mutant solid tumors, including patients with ovarian and cervical cancers, showed that this combination was intolerable. Pharmacokinetic analysis suggested that neratinib hindered trametinib clearance, resulting in elevated and prolonged exposure to trametinib, contributing to toxicity and possibly contributing to suboptimal dosing of both drugs. No RECIST-responses were seen and only 2 patients achieved SD ≥ 4 months. Consequently, while preclinical and translational studies support targeting the MAPK pathway as a rational approach to combat resistance to HER2-directed therapeutics, the judicious selection of agents remains pivotal for successful clinical outcomes.

Conclusions

HER2-directed therapies are a rapidly evolving area in the treatment of gynecologic cancers with significant potential to advance the standard of care. Gaining a deeper understanding of mechanisms of resistance to HER2-directed therapies is crucial to understanding the populations most likely to benefit, expanding our knowledge regarding the optimal sequencing of HER2-directed therapies, and advancing development of novel combinatorial strategies.

Author opinions on HER2 testing

Currently, there is insufficient evidence showing superiority of a specific ERBB2/HER2 testing modality (IHC, ISH, NGS), nor of a specific HER2 IHC scoring criteria, in predicting response to HER2-based therapies.

Testing HER2 in gynecologic cancers at initial diagnosis is recommended if participation in a clinical trial is under consideration.

Tumors from patients with recurrent gynecologic cancer should undergo HER2 testing, given the accelerated FDA approval of T-DXd for previously-treated, recurrent HER2 IHC 3+ solid tumors.

Emerging pathology consensus is that HER2 IHC be scored using the 2016 ASCO/CAP gastric criteria, based on the DESTINY-PanTumor02 trial informing on the accelerated FDA approval.

Tumors from patients with advanced stage and recurrent uterine serous carcinoma should be tested for HER2.

Pathology consensus is that HER2 IHC be scored using the modified 2007 ASCO/CAP breast criteria, based on the phase 2 trial of carboplatin/paclitaxel +/− trastuzumab in USC (Fader et al), leading to incorporation of this regimen within the NCCN guidelines.

It is reasonable to test more than one specimen, including at different time points, given the heterogeneity of HER2 IHC within tumors, across primary vs. metastatic sites, and as the evolution of HER2 IHC expression over time is not yet established.
