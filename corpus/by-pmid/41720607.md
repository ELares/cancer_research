# Therapeutic vaccination for active induction of T cell immunity against cancer, ready for a rich harvest after 40 years.

## Abstract

Therapeutic vaccines include DNA, mRNA, protein-loaded antigen-presenting cell, synthetic long peptide (SLP) and recombinant virus platforms. Only two therapeutic vaccines for neoplastic disease were approved by the Food and Drug Administration in the past 40 years: sipuleucel-T, approved in 2010 for hormone-resistant metastatic prostate cancer and targeting prostate acid phosphatase; and zopapogene imadenovec, approved in 2025 for recurrent respiratory papillomatosis, a rare, non-malignant but often invalidating disease caused by human papillomavirus (HPV) type 6 or type 11. In the last decade, DNA, mRNA and SLP vaccines directed against mutation-derived neoantigens have shown strong immunogenicity for T cells and clinical activity with or without additional immune checkpoint inhibition. Vaccine monotherapy is effective in premalignant disorders caused by high-risk HPV16 and virus-negative colonic polyps. Treatment of bulky or recurrent/metastatic (R/M) disease requires combination with surgery and/or chemotherapy or combination with an immune checkpoint inhibitor such as PD-1 blocker. In a recent study, adjuvant treatment with neoantigen-specific mRNA vaccine plus PD-1 blocker led to less melanoma recurrence than PD-1 blocker alone. In another randomized study, patients with R/M HPV16+ head and neck cancer only benefited from the combination of HPV16-specific SLP vaccine and a PD-1 blocker if they had high pretreatment PD-L1 biomarker expression in cancer tissue. Increasingly, biomarker-guided therapy is recommended.

Introduction

 Preventive vaccination against infectious diseases is a cornerstone of modern society and largely relies on the induction of virus-neutralizing or bactericidal antibodies aided by complement and innate immune cells. These usually operate in a virgin landscape not encumbered by undesirable immunoregulation. However, therapeutic vaccination against diseased cells and tissues relies on the induction of effector T cells with the capacity to eradicate the affected cells in a situation compromised by immunosuppressive mechanisms that abound in the tissue microenvironment of the afflicted tissues. Nowhere is this more visible than in the tumor microenvironment (TME) of established cancers, where a variety of immunosuppressive and immune-evasion mechanisms abound. Early studies showed the robust power of T cells in the eradication of murine tumors in combination with cyclophosphamide or interleukin-2. This soon led to successful therapeutic vaccination against defined tumor-associated T cell epitopes for established murine tumors. The importance of antigen-presenting dendritic cells (DC) for the induction of effective therapeutic T cells was shown early on. Therapeutic vaccines include DNA, mRNA, protein-loaded antigen-presenting cell, synthetic long peptide (SLP) and recombinant virus platforms. As a rule, the success of therapeutic cancer vaccination as monotherapy depends on striking early, such as in premalignant disease or in minimal residual disease following surgery. Success in patients with late-stage recurrent/metastatic (R/M) disease depends on the use of appropriate combinations of therapeutic vaccination with treatments that alleviate the strongest immunosuppressive influences in the TME and beyond.

Success of therapeutic vaccination as monotherapy in premalignant disease

High-risk human papillomavirus (HPV) such as HPV16 causes cancer only after a prolonged period of premalignant disease. This is well documented for premalignant disease of the uterine cervix and of the vulva. Therapeutic vaccination with a DNA vaccine against the two oncogenic proteins E6 and E7 of HPV16 has shown modest efficacy in eradicating cervical premalignant disease in a randomized trial. In two independent single-arm trials, a vaccine consisting of 25–35 amino acid long overlapping peptides (SLP) spanning the sequence of the E6 and E7 oncoproteins caused partial or complete regression of lesions in more than half of the patients. Complete histological regression was associated with viral clearance from the original lesion sites. Colonic polyps are a strong risk factor for the development of colorectal cancer (CRC). A peptide vaccine directed against the MUC-1 protein overexpressed on polyps may reduce the chances of later CRC. In many countries, an effective public health measure in the early detection of polyps and CRC is the demonstration of occult blood in stools. Arguably, vaccination against MUC-1 and other overexpressed antigens can help to further reduce the incidence of CRC in persons at risk. Preventive vaccines against the cancer viruses HPV and hepatitis B virus (HBV) are highly effective, although not yet universally applied in many parts of the world. Surprisingly, therapeutic vaccines designed to eradicate premalignant disease have not been further developed by the pharmaceutical industry, although the benefits would be obvious.

Food and Drug Administration approval of therapeutic vaccination against recurrent respiratory papillomatosis

Recurrent respiratory papillomatosis (RRP) is a rare disease caused by HPV6 or HPV11. Although these HPV types are not listed as high-risk types capable of causing cancer, RRP can be a devastating and mutilating disease, necessitating frequent surgical removal of papillomas obstructing the upper airways. In a landmark decision, the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approved in August 2025 the treatment of RRP with zopapogene imadenovec, a therapeutic vaccine consisting of a non-replicating gorilla adenovector harboring DNA, encoding large regions of the HPV6 and HPV11 E2, E4, E6, and E7 proteins. Many of the patients with RRP with severe disease, requiring multiple surgical procedures each year, experienced substantial relief following adjuvant vaccination after surgical debulking of papillomas.

The first FDA approval of a therapeutic cancer vaccine

The first therapeutic cancer vaccine to receive FDA approval for use in patients with cancer was sipuleucel-T, following the report of prolonged overall survival in metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer. While the survival advantage is limited, the treatment success of this vaccine may be promoted by the use of appropriate combination with treatments that alleviate immunosuppressive influences in the TME. Effective combinations in other cancers, but not necessarily prostate cancer, include some types of chemotherapy that eliminate immunosuppressive myeloid cells but not T cells or combination with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) such as CTLA-4 and PD-1 blockers.

Success of therapeutic vaccination combined with PD-1 blocker for minimal residual disease after cancer surgery

Malignant skin melanoma is one of the most immunogenic cancers, expressing many neoantigens caused by ultraviolet light-induced DNA mutations. Being foreign to the host, these human leukocyte antigen (HLA) class I and class II-presented neoantigens are highly immunogenic. Single-arm trials have shown induction of robust neoantigen-specific T cell responses by mRNA and SLP vaccines, associated with a favorable clinical course, with or without additional PD-1 blocker treatment. In a randomized study, patients with stage IIB–IV melanoma, whose tumors had been resected, either received adjuvant treatment with a combination of mRNA neoantigen-specific vaccine and pembrolizumab or pembrolizumab alone. Recurrence-free survival was longer with the combination versus monotherapy, and the incidence of recurrence or death was also lower in patients treated with the combination. One of the deadliest cancers is pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. By the time the patient experiences symptoms, it is usually too late for complete surgical removal. Even following ostensibly complete surgical removal, the cancer often recurs. Recently, adjuvant therapeutic neoantigen-directed mRNA vaccination plus PD-1 ligand (PD-L1) blocking immediately following surgical removal of pancreatic ductal adeno carcinoma (PDAC) and then followed up with chemotherapy led to induction of neoantigen-specific T cells in eight out of 16 patients. At 1.5 and 3.2 years’ follow-up, the eight patients with vaccine-induced immune responses had prolonged recurrence-free survival compared with the eight non-responders without demonstrable vaccine-induced T cell responses. These data call for a randomized confirmation study with larger numbers of patients testing immunogenicity and survival of the same treatment with a control arm without neoantigen-directed vaccination.

Therapeutic vaccination against shared non-viral oncogenic driver mutations

A frequent mutation in patients with breast cancer is Her-2/neu amplification. Characteristically, this receptor is truncated and overexpressed in the cancer cells in such patients. Because the receptor is expressed at the cell surface, it can be targeted by both antibodies and T cells. This has led to effective monoclonal antibody treatment with Her-2/neu-directed monoclonal antibodies such as trastuzumab and pertuzumab, but also to the development of antibody-inducing peptide vaccines against Her-2/neu directed against the same Her-2/neu B cell epitopes recognized by the anti-Her-2/neu-specific monoclonal antibodies. Clinical trials with such antibody-inducing vaccines are ongoing and show promising clinical activity. A T cell-inducing vaccine was developed against an HLA-A2 and HLA-A3-binding peptide representing amino acids 369–377 of the extracellular domain of Her-2/neu next to T cell vaccines directed against other HLA class I or class II-presented peptides. Numerous clinical trials were conducted with this peptide, also called E75 or nelipepimut-S, usually in combination with the adjuvant granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF). In a 50/50 randomized phase III trial, 758 patients with T1–T3, Her-2/neu low-expressing, lymph node-positive breast cancer were treated in the adjuvant setting with either 1000 μg nelipepimut-S vaccine formulated with 250 μg GM-CSF or placebo plus GM-CSF. On a prespecified interim analysis conducted after a median follow-up of 16.8 months, the trial was terminated because of futility associated with the finding that 74% of the recurrence events had occurred in the active vaccine arm. Although GM-CSF attracts myeloid cells including DCs, these DCs are not fully activated and may act as tolerogenic cells rather than optimal inducers of highly capable effector T cells. Montanide ISA-51 adjuvant and toll like receptor (TLR) ligands such as poly I:C or CpG have better track records as vaccine adjuvants for strong T cell response induction. In addition, vaccination with exact HLA-binding peptides is not optimal, because (1) it leads to exogenous loading of all nucleated cells that express major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I, most of which lack costimulatory molecules, and (2) it lacks proper CD4+ T cell help, crucial for robust CD8+ T cell induction and CD8 T cell memory.

Mutant K-ras molecules are attractive as targets for cancer vaccines, because of their frequent involvement in pancreatic, colorectal and other adenocarcinomas. In two early clinical studies, a total of 23 patients with pancreatic cancer were vaccinated intradermally after pancreatic cancer resection with a mixture of K-ras 17-mer peptides formulated in GM-CSF. The majority of patients showed positive T cell responses by either delayed hypersensitivity or in vitro T cell proliferation immediately following the vaccination, often associated with demonstrable memory years later. Strikingly, 10-year survival was 20% (4/20 evaluable patients) versus zero (0/87) in a cohort of non-vaccinated patients treated in the same period. In this study, the patients were not tested for the presence of individual K-ras mutations. Therefore, the clinical relevance of T cell responses in individual patients remains questionable. However, in a recent study, 20 patients with pancreatic cancer and five patients with CRC were treated for minimal residual disease following standard locoregional treatment with lymph node targeting mutant K-ras amphiphile 18-mer peptide antigens (containing the G12D and G12R mutations), formulated with CpG-7909 adjuvant. Patient tumors were verified to harbor at least one of the targeted mutations in their tumors. In 21/25 patients (84%), mutant K-ras-specific T cell responses, usually CD4+ and CD8+, were induced by the vaccine. At a median follow-up of 19.7 months, efficacy as measured by relapse-free survival and median overall survival correlated significantly with the size of the K-ras-specific vaccine-induced T cell response defined by a threshold value of 9.17-fold increase in interferon-gamma/granzyme B-positive T cells over baseline. This encouraging result calls for a randomized study with larger numbers of patients and a control arm with placebo vaccination.

Biomarkers for success of therapeutic vaccination in patients with R/M cancer

The study with pembrolizumab anti-PD-1 monotherapy that led to its approval for treatment of first-line R/M head and neck (H&N) cancer showed that this treatment worked best in patients with a high level of PD-L1 expression in the pretreatment cancer tissue. A commonly used approved laboratory assay for this is the combined positive score (CPS), reflecting PD-L1 expression on both the cancer cells and the infiltrating leukocytes. Patients with H&N cancer with a CPS ≥20 receive only pembrolizumab. Patients with CPS <1 receive in addition chemotherapy. Patients with CPS 1–20 optionally receive chemotherapy. In two recent studies, the importance of the CPS PD-L1 score for predicting the response to immunotherapy in patients with HPV16-positive oropharyngeal cancer was extended to combination treatment with a PD-1 blocker and therapeutic vaccination against HPV16 E6/E7. In both studies, only patients with a PD-L1 CPS ≥20 clearly benefited. In one of these studies, patients with R/M HPV16+ were randomized between treatment with the peltopepimut-S SLP vaccine plus anti-PD-1 combination versus placebo (vaccine adjuvant Montanide ISA-51 only) plus anti-PD-1. Patients with CPS ≥20 showed a much better overall response rate and improved median overall survival in the combination arm compared with the placebo arm.

Recently, mRNA vaccines against SARS-CoV-2, given within 100 days of subsequent lung cancer and melanoma immunotherapy with ICIs such as PD-1 inhibitors, were reported to improve the effect of the ICI treatment. Supposedly, even non-specific mRNA vaccination, by exerting systemic type I interferon-mediated effects, would install improved conditions for subsequent cancer immunotherapy. However, it now appears that this publication suffers from methodological limitations. In a reanalysis of the publicly available data presented in the original paper, no evidence was found that COVID-19 vaccination within 100 days prior to cancer immunotherapy improves survival.

Vaccination against immunosuppressive molecules in the TME

A recent innovative concept is that peptide vaccination against some of the most immunosuppressive molecules in the TME can confer clinical benefit in patients with melanoma in combination with PD-1 blocker, without added toxicity. The molecules first targeted in this way were indoleamine-pyrrole 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), an immunosuppressive enzyme, and PD-L1. In long-term follow-up of two cohorts treated in this manner, only PD-1 blocker-naïve patients benefited from the combination of anti-IDO/PD-L1 peptide vaccination and PD-1 blocker nivolumab, whereas patients progressing on prior anti-PD-1 treatment did not benefit from this combination. Other molecules that can be targeted in the TME include transforming growth factor-beta, arginase-1 and CCL22.

Conclusions

Therapeutic cancer vaccines are a safe, effective and non-toxic way to empower the patient’s immune system to actively fight the cancer cells. While mutation-derived neoantigens are great targets to direct T cell immunity against, the disadvantage is that personalized vaccines are required for each patient, driving up costs and causing a delay in availability. Exceptions are shared neoantigens encoded by driver mutations such as K-ras. Virus-encoded targets, overexpressed antigens such as Her-2/neu, selected cancer testis antigens and immunosuppressive molecules in the TME deserve equal attention. Therapeutic vaccination works best in a premalignant disease setting. In established cancer, adjuvant therapy following surgery in combination with PD-1 blockade is promising. Neoadjuvant vaccination in combination with immune checkpoint inhibition is also very attractive. In addition, high PD-L1 expression in the pretreatment cancer tissue is a promising biomarker to predict a clinical response to therapeutic vaccination in combination with PD-1 blockade in patients with R/M disease.
