# The ideal strategies of antibody‒drug conjugate sequential treatment in HER2-expressing metastatic breast cancer: A multi-center real-world study.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: A growing number of antibody‒drug conjugates (ADCs) have been approved for breast cancer treatment. However, the proper sequential strategies of ADCs remain uncertain. Our study aimed to explore the ideal ADC sequential treatment strategies in human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-expressing metastatic breast cancer (MBC).

**METHODS**: Our multi-centre retrospective study enrolled MBC patients who received at least 2 lines of different types of ADCs between Jan 1, 2018, and Jul 1, 2024. The efficacy of both ADC1 and ADC2 was evaluated.

**RESULTS**: A total of 111 patients (83 HER2-positive and 28 HER2-low) were included. In HER2-positive populations, Patients who received ADC2 with a different payload from ADC1 exhibited significantly longer progression-free survival 2 (PFS2) (6.8 vs. 2.7 months, p < 0.001) and overall PFS (progression-free Interval 1 (PFI1) + PFS2) (15.0 vs. 8.5 months, p = 0.043) compared to those treated with ADC2 containing a similar payload with ADC1. Patients received ADC2 immediately after ADC1 progression showed longer PFS2 ADC2 delayed sequential patients (median PFS2: 6.0 vs. 3.0 months, p = 0.004). In HER2-low patients, the efficacy of ADC2 tended to be lower than ADC1 (median PFI1 vs. PFS2: 3.1 vs. 2.4 months, p = 0.078). No significant differences of efficacy were observed, no matter what ADC sequential treatment strategy used.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Sequential treatment with ADCs showed clinical benefit especially for HER2-positive patients treated with ADC2 which have different types of payloads from ADC1. In HER2-low patients, the benefit of ADC sequential therapy seemed to be limited.

Introduction

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) are a rapidly growing class of targeted agents for cancer, particularly in human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2)-positive metastatic breast cancer (MBC). The diversification of antigenic targets and bioactive payloads is rapidly broadening the scope of tumor indications for ADCs.

For HER2-positive MBC, based on the EMILIA trial, trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1) had been recommended as a second-line treatment with progression-free survival (PFS) of 9.6 months. The therapeutic effect of trastuzumab–deruxtecan (T-DXd) was even more impressive in the DESTINY-Breast 01 trial, leading to a median PFS of 19.4 months in patients with a median 6 lines of previous treatment. T-DXd also presented superior efficacy and survival benefit than T-DM1 as a second-line treatment strategy as updated results reported in the DESTINY-Breast 03 trial (median PFS: 29.0 vs. 7.2 months, hazard ratio (HR) = 0.30, 95 % confidence interval (CI), 0.24–0.38; overall survival (OS): 52.6 vs. 42.7 months, HR = 0.73, 95 % CI, 0.56–0.94). As an innovative ADC composed of a fully humanized anti-HER2 antibody linked to the microtubule inhibitor monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) via a protease-cleavable vedotin linker, disitamab vedotin (RC48) also showed promising efficacy in HER2‐overexpression MBC with median PFS of 5.7 months (95 % CI: 5.3–8.4)6. Additionally, FS-1502 and BB-1701, were also well-tolerated with low toxicity and promising antitumor activity in HER2-positive MBC.

HER2-low status is defined as 1+ according to immunohistochemistry (IHC) or 2+ on IHC and negative results on fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH). Different ADCs showed different efficacy in this group of patients. For HER2-targeting ADCs, T-DM1 failed to present good treatment sensitivity in patients with low HER2 expression. However, T-DXd still presented well. In the DESTINY-Breast 04 trail, the median PFS was 9.9 months in the T-DXd group and 5.1 months in the physician's choice group (HR = 0.50, 95 % CI, 0.40–0.63), and OS was 23.4 months vs. 16.8 months, respectively (HR = 0.64, 95 % CI, 0.48–0.86). The DESTINY-Breast 06 trail further included patients with HER2-ultralow (IHC 0 with membrane staining), the median PFS was significantly longer than that of patients treated with chemotherapy in both the HER2-low (median PFS: 13.2 vs. 8.1 months, HR = 0.62, 95 % CI, 0.51–0.74) and the HER2-ultralow group (median PFS: 13.2 vs. 8.3 months, HR = 0.78, 95 % CI, 0.50–1.21). In addition, RC48 also showed promising antitumor activity in heavily treated patients with HER2-low disease. The median PFS was 5.1 months (95 % CI: 4.1–6.6 months) in HER2-low cohort in C003 CANCER. Trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP-2), which is known as an effective target in metastatic triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), is widely targeted for treatment. Sacituzumab govitecan (SG), an ADC that targets TROP-2 with an SN38 payload, presented a survival benefit over chemotherapy in both TNBC and endocrine-resistant hormone receptor-positive, HER2-negative (HR + HER2-) disease. In the subgroup analysis of the HER-evaluable population in the ASCENT study (N = 415, 78 % of the intention-to-treat (ITT) population), SG improved PFS and OS for both HER2 0 (N = 293) (median PFS: 4.3 vs. 1.6 months; median OS: 11.7 vs. 5.9 months) and HER-low patients (N = 122) (median PFS: 6.2 vs. 2.9 months; median OS: 13.4 vs. 8.7 months) over treatment of physician's choice. Thus, above studies suggest that both HER2-targeting ADCs and SG can provide therapeutic benefits for HER2-low patients.

With the continuous emergence of innovative ADCs, the sequential application of different types of ADCs is gradually conducted in clinical practice. Currently, several retrospective studies have focused on ADC sequential use. In A3 study, 68 patients who received two or more ADCs for metastatic TNBC or HR+/HER2- MBC were included. When the antibody target of the latter ADC was the same as the prior, cross-resistance was present in 11/14 cases (78.5 %) compared to 26/49 cases (53.1 %) when the later ADC targeted a different antigen. However, the percentage of cross-resistance was 60.0 % (6/10) in the group where ADC2 contained an identical payload to ADC1 and 52.4 % (22/42) in the group where ADC2 had a different payload. Indicating that mechanisms of ADCs resistance are likely heterogeneous given their complex structure. ADC-LOW is currently the largest sample size retrospective study evaluated the efficacy of subsequent use of SG and T-DXd in 179 patients with HR + HER2-and HER2-low MBC. The study reported that clinical benefit of sequentially administered SG and T-DXd is limited for most patients, which is generally consistent with the results reported in previous study. However, the real-world efficacy and the proper sequencing of cross-application of different types of ADCs in both HER2-positive and HER2-low disease remain uncertain. Accordingly, we conducted this retrospective real-world study on both HER2-positive and HER2-low MBC patients treated with at least 2 lines of different types of ADCs in order to explore the ideal ADC sequencing treatment strategy.

Material and methods

Study design and data collection

Our retrospective, multi-centre, real-world study included HER2-expressing patients with MBC who received at least 2 lines of different types of ADCs at the Cancer Hospital Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences, SanHuan Cancer Hospital and Beijing Chaoyang Huanxing Cancer Hospital between Jan 1, 2018, and Jul 1, 2024. All patients included should receive at least two lines of different types of ADCs treatment, regardless of other intervening treatments. The last follow-up date was Jul 1, 2024. The study protocol was approved by the ethical committee of the Cancer Hospital Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences (Approval No. 24/441–4721). The requirement for individual informed consent was waived by the committee because of the retrospective nature of the study.

Group characteristics

Sequential treatment strategy subgroup

According to the order of ADC application, the first ADC used was defined as ADC1, and the subsequent ADCs were labeled as ADC2 and ADC3. Sequential treatment strategies were categorized into 3 groups based on the types of antibodies and payloads of ADC1 and ADC2, as follows: 1) the AsPs group (Antibody similar, Payload similar) for patients treated with ADC2 which has a similar type of antibody (all ADCs targeting HER2 antigen) and a similar type of payload (those with the same cytotoxic mechanism, including antimitotic agents such as MMAE, monomethyl auristatin F (MMAF), the maytansine derivatives such as DM1 and eribulin and topoisomerase I inhibitors such as SN-38 and DXd) with ADC1; 2) the AsPd group (Antibody similar, Payload different) for patients treated with ADC2 which has a similar antibody (all ADCs targeting the HER2 antigen) but a different payload (payloads with different types of cytotoxic mechanisms) compared to ADC1 and 3) the AdPs group (Antibody different, Payload similar) for patients treated with ADC2 which has a different type of antibody (ADCs targeting either the HER2 or TROP-2 antigen) but shared a similar payload (as described above) with ADC1. No patients were treated with ADC2 targeting different antibody and with different payload from ADC1. Therefore, we did not designate such a group.

Treatment timing subgroup

Patients with ADC2 treatment directly after ADC1 progression were defined as instant sequential group. When other treatments were used between ADC1 and ADC2 treatment, patients would be divided into delayed sequential group. If a patient received treatment with three lines of ADCs, the group would be assessed based on the first two types of ADCs to avoid the impact of multiple ADC treatments on overall efficacy.

Pathology subgroup

HER2-positive was defined as IHC 3+ or IHC 2+ with FISH positive, and HER2-low was defined as IHC 1+ or IHC 2+ and FISH negative. HR-positive was defined as immunoreactivity for oestrogen or progesterone receptors in ≥1 % of tumor cell nuclei according to local testing. Patients were categorized into pathology groups based on their HER2 and HR status at the time of ADC1 treatment.

Assessment

Patient characteristics were extracted from the medical records. Patient information was collected, including patient demographics, pathological characteristics, previous treatment regimens, and imaging assessment. The treatment response was evaluated according to Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) version 1.1. Computed Tomography (CT) was performed routinely at baseline and every 6 weeks (2 cycles of routine treatment). The objective response rate (ORR) was calculated as the proportion of patients who achieved a complete response (CR) or partial response (PR). ORR1 and ORR2 represent the ORR of ADC1 and ADC2, respectively. The disease control rate (DCR) was calculated as the proportion of patients who achieved a CR, PR or stable disease (SD). DCR1 and DCR2 represent the ORR of ADC1 and ADC2, respectively. Progression-free interval after ADC1 (PFI1) was defined as the time from initiating ADC1 until documented PD. PFS2 was defined as the time from treatment initiation of the second ADC until documented disease progression or death. Overall PFS was defined as the sum of PFI1 and PFS2. Follow-up was censored on Jul 1, 2024.

Statistical analysis

Descriptive statistics were used to summarize patient characteristics, treatment administration, and antitumor activity. Hypothesis testing for categorical variables was performed via the chi-square test or Fisher's exact test. PFS was analysed via the Kaplan‒Meier method and Cox proportional hazard models. Two-sided P values < 0.05 were considered to indicate statistical significance. SPSS 26.0 (IBM, Armonk, NY, USA) and GraphPad Prism 9 (GraphPad Software Inc., San Diego, CA, USA) were used for statistical analysis.

Results

Population characteristics

A total of 111 MBC patients treated with at least 2 lines of different types of ADCs with complete profiles and at least one imaging efficacy evaluation were ultimately included. The grouping design of patients is shown in Fig. 1. The baseline characteristics and treatment information for all patients are summarized in Table 1. Patients ranged in age from 35 to 70 years, with 75 (67.6 %) patients aged ≤60 years. In total, 83 (74.8 %) patients had HER2-positive disease, and 28 (25.2 %) had HER2-low disease. Among for HER2-low patients, 71.4 % (20/28) were HR-positive and 28.6 % (8/28) were TNBC. Details are shown in Table 2. 104 patients received prior Taxol-based chemotherapy. A total of 27.9 % of the patients previously received cyclin-dependent kinase 4/6 (CDK4/6) inhibitor treatment (before ADC1 therapy starting). According to the different sequential treatment strategies of ADC1 and ADC2, 32, 69 and 10 patients were classified into the AsPs, AsPd and AdPs groups, respectively. The median number of prior treatment lines was 4 for ADC1 and 6 for ADC2. The details of the sequential treatment strategies for ADC1 and ADC2 in HER2-positive and HER2-low patients are shown in Table 3. 16 patients were sequentially treated with 3 types of ADCs. The characteristics of ADC mentioned in our study was summarized in Supplementary Table 1.

ADC sequential treatment in HER2-positive patients

Efficacy comparison between overall ADC1 and ADC2

To evaluate whether sequential treatment with an ADC can achieve sustained efficacy, we compared the overall efficacy of ADC1 and that of ADC2. The PFI1 and PFS2 were 4.1 and 4.8 months (p = 0.982, HR = 1.00, 95 % CI: 0.72–1.40) (Fig. 2A), the ORR1 and ORR2 were 28.9 % and 15.7 % (p = 0.040) as well as the DCR1 and DCR2 were 72.3 % and 71.1 % (p = 0.863) (Fig. 2B). In addition to the overall comparison of the treatment response of ADC1 and ADC2, we conducted a comparative analysis of the efficacy of each ADC drug treated as ADC1 or ADC2. Except for RC48, which had a higher DCR when it was treated as ADC1 than when it was used as ADC2 (87.0 % vs. 47.1 %, p = 0.013), no significant differences were found for other drugs. In 16 patients (14 were HER2-positive disease) who received 3 successive lines of different ADCs, there was still no statistically significant difference observed in the efficacy of 3 lines. In addition, among the 23 primary ADC resistance HER2-positive patients who were evaluated as PD during the first image evaluation after ADC1 treatment, most of them achieved disease control after ADC2 treatment, only 5 patients (21.7 %) were still resistant to ADC2. Overall, the percentage of patients whose best of response to ADC2 treatment was PD in the AsPs and AsPd groups was 33.3 % (3/9) and 14.3 % (2/14), respectively. Among them, 6 patients received secondary biopsy from progressed metastatic lesions after ADC1 treatment progression and 2 of them presented HER2 expression decrease (HER2 IHC 3+ changed to IHC 1+). Details are shown in Supplementary Table 4-6.

We further analysed the impact of the application order of ADC1 and ADC2 on overall PFS. Owing to sample size limitations, we mainly analysed the overall PFS of patients treated with RC48, T-DM1, and T-DXd drugs in different sequences. The results revealed that the sequential application order of two types of ADC as ADC1 or ADC2 had no significant effect on overall PFS in HER2-positive patients.

Ideal sequential treatment strategies for ADC1 followed by ADC2

We further explored the correlation between different sequential treatment strategies and the efficacy of ADC2 as well as the overall ADC therapy. Patients were categorized into instant sequential and delayed sequential groups based on whether ADC2 was administered immediately after ADC1 progression. The median number of prior treatment lines was 4 and 3 for ADC1 in instant sequential and delayed sequential group, as well as 5 and 6 for ADC2 respectively. Both of the instant sequential and delayed sequential group have 9 patients (23.7 % of instant sequential and 20.0 % of delayed sequential group) received ADC2 as last treatment for metastatic disease. Details of the characteristics of above two groups are shown in Supplementary Table 7. It is noteworthy that patients in instant sequential group showed significantly longer PFS2 than delayed sequential group in HER2-positive patients (median PFS2: 6.0 vs. 3.0 months, p = 0.004; HR 95 % CI = 0.48 (0.29–0.79)) (Fig. 2C), as well as the overall PFS (median overall PFS of 15.0 vs. 8.5 months, p = 0.004; HR 95 %CI = 0.47 (0.27–0.79)) (Fig. 2D). The DCR2 of ADC2 in the instant sequential group was also significantly higher than that in the delayed sequential group (84.2 % vs. 60.0 %, p = 0.015) (Fig. 2E), as well as a numerically trend in ORR2 (21.1 % vs. 11.1 %, p = 0.214) (Fig. 2F).

To explore the impact of sequential ADC types on the efficacy of sequential therapy, we divided the sequential treatment strategies into three groups (AsPs, AsPd and AdPs groups) on the basis of the antibodies and payloads of the two ADCs as defined in the methods section. Among the HER2-positive patients, 37.3 % (31/83) were in the AsPs group, whereas 62.7 % (52/83) were in the AsPd group. As for AsPs group, PFS2 showed numerically decrease trend compared to PFI1 (2.7 vs. 4.5 months, p = 0.114; HR 95 %CI = 1.56 (0.89–2.07)), while the trend is then the opposite in the AsPd group (6.8 vs. 4.0 months, p = 0.311; HR 95 %CI = 0.80 (0.53–1.23)). Compared with the AsPs group, the AsPd group presented a longer PFS2 (median PFS2: 6.8 vs. 2.7 months, p < 0.001; HR 95 %CI = 0.38 (0.22–0.66)), overall PFS (median PFS: 15.0 vs. 8.5 months, p = 0.043; HR 95 %CI = 0.60 (0.36–0.99)) and higher DCR2 (80.8 % vs. 54.8 %, p = 0.012) (Fig. 2G–I). Representatively, sequential treatment with T-DXd after T-DM1 progression had a numerically greater DCR2 (87.1 % vs. 58.3 %, p = 0.088) and longer PFS2 (median PFS2: 5.9 vs. 3.3 months, p = 0.071; HR 95 %CI = 0.49 (0.22–1.08)) than RC48. T-DXd sequential therapy after RC48 progression also significantly prolonged PFS2 compared to T-DM1 (median PFS2: 7.4 vs. 2.9 months, p = 0.018).

Changes in HER2 expression from the first ADC application

HER2 expression level is closely related to the efficacy of anti-HER2 ADC treatment. Thus, we further analysed the changes in HER2 expression levels after ADC1 treatment. The secondary biopsy tissues were all punctured after ADC1 treatment progression from the progressed metastatic lesions. A decrease in HER2 expression after ADC treatment was defined as a change from HER2-positive (HER2 IHC 3+/IHC 2+ FISH+) to HER2-low expression (HER2 IHC 2+ FISH-/IHC 1+) or loss in the HER2 IHC expression (IHC 0) in patients with low HER2 expression. A total of 27 patients had HER2 expression biopsy results after ADC1 treatment, 21 of whom were HER2-positive initially. Only 5 patients (33.8 %) showed a decrease in HER2 expression after ADC1 treatment. One patient received RC48 as ADC1 and T-DM1 as ADC2. 4 of them were divided into AsPd group received T-DXd (N = 3) and RC48 (N = 1) as ADC2, and achieved a ORR2 of 75 %. The details are shown in Supplementary Tables 5 and 6). The PFS2, overall PFS and DCR2 was not affected by HER2 changes.

ADC sequential treatment in HER2-low patients

Efficacy comparison between overall ADC1 and ADC2

A total of 28 patients with HER-low disease was included, with 20 HR-positive and 8 TNBC patients. Details are shown in Table 3 and Supplementary Table 9. The overall efficacy of ADC1 was higher than that of ADC2 (median PFI1 vs. PFS2: 3.1 vs. 2.4 months, p = 0.078, HR = 0.61, 95 % CI: 0.35–1.07; DCR1 vs. DCR2: 78.6 % vs. 53.6 %, p = 0.048). (Fig. 3A and B). The results were similar in HR-positive patients (median PFI1 vs. PFS2: 3.1 vs. 1.7 months, p = 0.053, HR = 0.52, 95 % CI: 0.27–1.00; DCR1 vs. DCR2: 80.0 % vs. 40.0 %, p = 0.010). However, no difference was observed in the TNBC subgroup with only 8 patients included (median PFI1 vs. PFS2: 2.6 vs. 2.7 months, p = 0.850, HR = 1.11, 95 % CI: 0.39–3.17; DCR1 vs. DCR2: 75.0 % vs. 87.5 %, p = 1.000) (Supplementary Table 2). In 6 patients showed primary resistance to ADC1 (4 HR-positive and 2 TNBC patients), the average ORR of ADC2 was 16.7 % (0.0 % and 50.0 % for AsPd and AdPs, respectively) and the average DCR of ADC2 was 66.7 % (75.0 % and 50 % for AsPd and AdPs, respectively). Details are shown in Supplementary Table 4.

Efficacy of different sequential treatment strategies for ADC1 followed by ADC2

Unlike HER2-positive patients, the sequential timing had no impact on the efficacy of ADC2 in HER2-low patients (Fig. 3C–F), regardless of HR status (Supplementary Tables 9 and 10). As for the sequential treatment strategy groups, 17 patients were in the AsPd group, and 10 patients were in the AdPs group. Only one patient was included in the AsPs group. Details of grouping of patients with HR-positive and TNBC are shown in Supplementary Table 10. Considering the small sample size of group AsPs, we analysed HER2-low patients as a whole and mainly compared the efficacy between AsPd and AdPs groups. No significant differences were detected in PFS2 (AsPd vs. AdPs: 2.4 vs. 1.6 months, p = 0.762; HR 95 %CI = 1.14 (0.49–2.65)), overall PFS (AsPs + AsPd vs. AdPs: 5.8 vs. 10.0 months, p = 0.183; HR 95 %CI = 1.76 (0.76–4.09)) and DCR2 (AsPd vs. AdPs: 52.9 % vs. 50.0 %, p = 1.000) (Fig. 3G–I). Although the sample size is limited, we further merged the three groups of patients based on antibody type (AsPs + AsPd vs. AdPs) or payload type (AsPs + AdPs vs. AsPd). Consistent results were found as above that no significant differences were detected regardless of whether the antibodies or payloads were different (Supplementary Table 11), as well as in HR-positive and TNBC subgroup shown in Supplementary Tables 9 and 10. Among 6 patients with secondary HER2 IHC results after ADC1 progression, none of them experienced HER2 expression loss.

Discussion

ADCs are gradually revolutionizing the clinical treatment of all subtypes of MBC. However, the clinical value of the sequential application of ADCs is not yet fully understood. Our study revealed that ADC2 treatment directly after ADC1 progression should better treatment benefits than delayed sequential treatment in HER2-positive patients. Sequential treatment with ADC2 with different payloads from ADC1 can lead to better PFS2, PFS and DCR2 than those with similar cytotoxicity payloads when their (ADC1 and ADC2) targeting antigens are same. For HER2-low patients, the benefit of sequential therapy seemed to be slightly limited. However, due to the small size and heavily-pretreated of included patients, this result still needs further validation. Our results further support the clinical application value of ADCs and provide supporting evidence for the feasibility of sequential  therapy.

As the pharmaceutical synthesis technology substantially advanced over the past decades, many more ADCs have been approved or are being studied in late-phase clinical trials. The differences exist in the pharmacological properties of different ADC drugs exist, which lays the foundation for the rationality and benefit of the sequential treatment of ADCs. Take T-DM1 and T-DXd which are representative ADCs composed of humanized monoclonal antibodies targeting HER2 for example. T-DM1 incorporates a microtubule-disrupting agent, which is tethered by a durable thioether bond, and the drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) is about 3.5. Whereas T-DXd employs a topoisomerase I inhibitor connected through a tetrapeptide-based cleavable linker, which enables greater specificity in targeting cancer cells, thereby diminishing off-target toxicity. Meanwhile, T-DXd has good cell membrane permeability as well as a high, homogeneous DAR of approximately 8, which makes it more effective and bystander killing effect. Actually, T-DXd truly achieve better therapeutic benefits than T-DM1 as reported in DESTINY-Breast 03 trial, and was proved to overcome resistance to T-DM1 in DESTINY-Breast 02 trial.

In recent years, ADC drugs have gradually gained an important position in breast cancer treatment due to their superior efficacy. Increasing attention and research have been focused on overcoming ADC resistance to maximize their therapeutic benefits. ADCs contain three major parts: a monoclonal antibody, a chemical linker and cytotoxic payloads. It is obviously that each of its parts is inextricably related to the drug resistance. In terms of antibodies, taking HER2 as an example. Decreased HER2 expression was reported in T-DM1-resistant cell lines, which indicated HER2 loss as a mechanism of T-DM1 resistance. In addition, impaired binding of HER2, HER2 heterogeneity as well as dysregulated HER2 downstream signalling pathways can also lead to HER2 targeting ADC drugs resistance. Tumor resistance to the payload can also lead to the development of ADCs resistance. A study focused on the resistance of T-DXd reported that T-DXd-resistant cells detected reduced sensitivity to the topoisomerase inhibitor topotecan, suggesting that acquired T-DXd resistance may be mediated by loss of sensitivity to the ADC payload. Thus, we suggest that the rearrangement combinations antibody and payload of different types of ADCs may produce the best pattern of ADC sequencing and has the potential to be generalized as the ADC's sequential model.

To date, no prospective studies have reported sequential treatment modalities for ADCs. As for HER2-positive MBC, Chen et al., conducted a small sample size retrospective study in 64 patients. This study mainly focused on the therapeutic sequencing of T-DXd, T-DM1 and RC48 and reported that the ORR for ADC2 with similar payload of ADC1 was lower than those switching to a different payload (5.3 % vs. 22.6 %). T-DXd showed superior PFS2 compared to RC48 after progression from T-DM1 (5.37 vs 3.30 months, HR = 0.40, 95 % CI 0.17–0.93, p = 0.034). However, T-DXd showed similar PFS2 compared with T-DM1 after progression from RC48. For patients who progressed after T-DXd, patients treated with RC48 showed longer PFS2 than T-DM1 (6.05 vs. 0.93 months, HR = 0.03, 95 % CI 0.002–0.353, p = 0.0093). The results above are partially consistent with us. With more types of ADCs included in our study, we likewise found that sequential therapy with ADCs does provide efficacy benefits, even in patients with primary resistance. In HER2-positive disease, the sequential approach of changing payloads presented better efficacy. As a representative drug sequencing approach, T-DXd sequential therapy after T-DM1 showed a trend of superiority over RC48, and the efficacy benefit is not affected by the order in which they are applied. Thus, sequential ADCs with different types of payloads can be the optimal treatment modality to improve the efficacy of sequential therapy in HER2-positive disease. However, it is important to consider that, in addition to the additional benefit of replacing the payload, the better pharmacological profile of T-DXd itself as well as its superior inner efficacy demonstrated in clinical studies compared to other anti-HER2 ADCs cannot be ignored. Besides, even if ADC2 and ADC1 are both HER2-targeting ADCs and have similar payloads, ADC2 treatment can still exhibit some degree of efficacy, although slightly inferior to payloads replacement. This may be related to the structural differences of each ADC in terms of their unique characteristics. In addition, RC48 or T-DM1 application after T-DXd is also feasible, the order between them does not affect the overall efficacy. In a slight departure from the findings of other studies, we found that the application of ADC2 immediately after ADC1 progression will achieve better efficacy than those use intermediary lines of chemotherapy. The exact mechanism of this is not yet clear, we speculate that it may be related to alterations in the tumor microenvironment brought by the previous antitumor therapy. However, the result was drawn from a small sample population, so we need to be cautious as this conclusion might be biased by the heavily pre-treated condition of patients in the delayed sequential group after several treatment. At the same time, we found that few patients showed HER2 expression decrease after HER2-targeting ADCs treatment, which also creates the opportunity for the sequential therapy with ADCs with the similar types of antibodies.

HER2-low tumors represent a diverse and heterogeneous group, exhibiting variations in prognosis and response to systemic treatments. The difference in ADC efficacy due to HER2 expression cannot be ignored, so the choice of ADC sequential therapy for breast cancer patients with low HER2 expression should not be confused with HER2-positive patients. T-DXd can also effectively target tumor cells that express low levels of HER2 and can deliver its potent cytotoxic payload through the bystander effect to neighbouring tumor cells heterogeneously expressing HER2. Trop-2, which is highly expressed in approximately 80 % of breast cancers regardless of subtype, is an important therapeutic target for patients with low HER2 expression. Given that ADCs with different targets are available for selection, sequential ADC therapy seems to have a good theoretical basis for patients with low HER2 expression. Poumeaud et al. reported their evaluation of the clinical efficacy of the sequential use of SG and T-DXd in 179 patients with low HER2 expression. mPFS2 was 3.2 months and 2.3 months respectively for patients with TNBC receiving SG as ADC1 and those with HR + disease receiving T-DXd first. Multivariate analysis of PFS2 after adjusted for HR status and other factors reflected that SG used as ADC1 associated with greater PFS2 than T-DXd as the first treatment (HR 0.59; p = 0.074). However, in general, the clinical benefit of sequentially administered SG and T-DXd is seemingly limited for most patients. In our study, the ADC2 benefit in patients with low HER2 expression seemed to be less significant than that in HER2-positive patients, and there also seems to be no significant difference in the selection of ADC2 types between antibodies or payloads change. Due to the limited sample size, we were unable to further analyse the potential differences in ADC sequential treatment outcomes for HER2-low patients based on HR and other factors. However, owing to the small sample size, our study cannot fully demonstrate the value of sequential ADC therapy in populations with low HER2 expression. Meanwhile, it must be noted that, with 7th of the median treatment line of ADC2, HER2-low patients in our study were heavily-pretreated, which inevitably associated with poor condition and serious tumor burden, making them more likely to develop drug resistance and thus leading to the quite worse efficacy of ADC2 treatment. Accordingly, the ADC sequential strategy still needs to be compared and validated through large sample size retrospective analysis or further prospective studies. Currently, ADC sequential therapy is receiving increasing clinical attention and several prospective clinical trials focus on ADC sequential treatment such as TRADE-DXd (NCT06533826), SERIES (NCT06263543) and ENCORE (NCT06774027) are undergoing. Stronger evidence on ADC sequential therapy is highly anticipated.

There are several limitations to our study. First, heterogeneity in patient baseline characteristics is inevitable in retrospective studies in the real world. Second, our study included a relatively small sample size of patients for analysis, especially in the population with low HER2 expression, which inevitably led to statistical bias. Thirdly, the included patients were heavily pretreated compared to those in clinical trials. As a result, the overall efficacy of ADC involved may be inferior to that reported in clinical research. The conclusions still require validation in large-scale cohorts. Moreover, we did not further analyse the adverse effects caused by sequential treatment, nor did we explore efficacy prediction biomarkers in this study. Finally, most patients are still under treatment, and the survival data are not yet mature.

Conclusion

ADC sequential therapy presents high clinical value in breast cancer, especially for HER2-positive patients. For HER2-targeting ADCs, the instant application of ADC2 after ADC1 progression with different types of payloads was acknowledged as the optimal sequential mode. Due to the small sample size of HER2-low patients, the results should be interpreted with caution. However, ADC sequential therapy in patients with low HER2 expression seemed to be relatively limited compared to HER2-positive patients, and should be further validated. More studies are needed to better understand the resistance mechanisms and optimal treatment sequence for ADCs.
