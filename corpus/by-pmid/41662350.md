# Multi-seed searching algorithm for integrated codon optimization of mRNA stability and translational efficiency in vaccine design.

## Abstract

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines have revolutionized vaccinology with their rapid development cycles and adaptability, yet their broad application is constrained by unresolved challenges in balancing mRNA structural stability and translational efficiency. Here, we introduce a groundbreaking multi-seed searching algorithm for mRNA codon optimization, an innovative framework that synergistically co-optimizes minimum free energy and codon adaptation index through adaptive integration of simulated annealing and genetic algorithms. This novel approach enhances global search capability to escape local optima, a critical limitation of existing tools. Evaluations across long therapeutic mRNA sequences and short peptides (neoantigens from bladder cancer and melanoma) reveal our algorithm outperforms state-of-the-art LinearDesign, delivering superior balanced improvements in both stability and translational efficiency validating its unique ability to navigate the inherent trade-offs between these two key metrics. Built on this algorithm, the Optiseed platform introduces transformative features including customizable scoring functions, flexible parameters for tailored optimization, and support for integrating untranslated regions (UTRs), poly(A) tails, and other elements to enable end-to-end vaccine construct design. This innovation addresses the rigidity of conventional tools, empowering precise, context-specific optimization. Optiseed represents a robust, scalable solution for mRNA vaccine codon optimization. Its superior performance across diverse sequences underscores its potential to accelerate mRNA-based therapeutic development, particularly in personalized cancer immunotherapy, while offering a framework adaptable for other applications such as infectious disease vaccine design.

Introduction

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines, a transformative technology, demonstrated exceptional speed and efficacy during COVID-19, marking a key advance in vaccinology. Developed for decades, they show promise in cancer immunotherapy and infectious disease prevention, using synthetic transcripts to direct host antigen production and trigger adaptive immunity, offering flexibility for rapid variant adaptation. mRNA vaccine success also stems from advances like optimized 5′ caps, modified nucleotides (reducing innate responses), and lipid nanoparticle delivery (protecting mRNA and enhancing uptake).

Unlike live/attenuated vaccines, mRNA eliminates infection risks even in immunocompromised individuals, with precise sequence design enabling tailored targeting and multi-antigen encoding for broad protection. However, inherent mRNA instability including nuclease sensitivity, temperature dependence, and rapid degradation, requires advanced delivery and cold-chain logistics, increasing production/distribution complexity and costs (especially in resource-limited settings) and limiting application/immunogenicity, highlighting urgent needs for sequence stabilization.

In personalized cancer immunotherapy, mRNA design must address tumor heterogeneity, requiring enhanced stability without compromising neoantigen antigenicity. Synonymous codon substitutions preserve amino acid sequences, making codon optimization a viable strategy to boost stability/translation efficiency while maintaining epitope integrity, critical for vaccines relying on precise patient-specific epitope presentation.

mRNA stability depends on interacting factors: GC content incompletely proxies stability, while RNA secondary structure minimum free energy (MFE) reflects thermodynamic stability (lower = slower degradation). The codon adaptation index (CAI) measures tRNA compatibility, influencing translation and indirect stability via ribosome protection. These metrics are interdependent but may trade off (high-stability structures hindering translation). Dual optimization of structure and codon usage is needed, yet traditional tools rely solely on CAI/GC, missing high-stability sequences. Recent algorithms like LinearDesign partially address this via coordinated optimization. However, existing platforms are overly complex, limiting refined codon optimization, critical for translation efficiency and efficacy. Current tools also lack balance between integrity, efficiency, and quality, demanding innovative solutions.

This study introduces the Multi-seed Searching Algorithm (MSSA), integrating MFE (structural stability) and CAI (translational efficiency) via adaptive simulated annealing/genetic algorithms to boost precision and global search. Benchmarked against LinearDesign, it achieves superior structure-translation balance across sequence lengths. Validation showed outperformance in 8/10 therapeutic proteins; for neoantigen peptides, it excelled in 49/258 bladder cancer and 77/437 melanoma sequences (versus 32 and 63 for LinearDesign), with most sequences showing similarity. Based on this, Optiseed, a modular, extensible platform supporting integrated design and visualization, enhances efficiency, and user experience, establishing a novel framework for intelligent mRNA vaccine optimization with significant theoretical and translational value.

Materials and methods

A novel approach for MSSA, is developed by combining mRNA stability (determining its intracellular lifespan and impacting immune response) and translation efficiency (vital for mRNA-to-protein conversion) as key factors in the evaluation framework. The simulated annealing and genetic algorithms are artfully and adaptively integrated, which strengthen the capacity to effectively circumvent local optimal solutions, thereby effecting a substantial augmentation of the optimization outcome. Operationally, MSSA methodically assigns scores and ranks an extensive array of mRNA codon sequences, steering the optimization model towards the attainment of more optimal and desirable outcomes within the context of mRNA codon sequence optimization, leveraging both biological insights and computational techniques.

Scoring functions

Sequence optimization is determined by evaluating a weighted linear combination of multiple metrics, each calculated through distinct scoring functions. The contribution of each scoring function is adjusted via an associated weight. In Multi-seed Searching, three scoring function options are provided for user selection.

Classical scoring function

The first scoring function is the classical scoring model, which considers two primary parameters—MFE and CAI—both of which strongly affect the in vivo stability of mRNA.

The secondary structure of RNA has been shown to be a key determinant of mRNA vaccine stability, influencing numerous biochemical and biological characteristics of mRNA. Results

Algorithmic design enabling co-optimization of mRNA stability and translation

To address the open reading frame (ORF) sequence optimization challenge for mRNA vaccines, we developed a Multi-seed Search Algorithm integrated with a modular heuristic workflow (Fig. 1a). This algorithm processes amino acid sequences and user-defined parameters to generate optimized RNA sequences through a systematic iterative refinement framework. Initially, the initialization module translates the amino acid sequence into k initial RNA seed sequences, which are then indexed and stored in a dynamic priority pool (Fig. 1b). These seeds are stochastically distributed across the search space to enable multi-directional exploration of the sequence landscape.

Subsequently, the sampling and mutation module (Fig. 1c) employs a statistical mechanics-derived Boltzmann distribution to identify suboptimal sequences within the pool, a strategy that may enhance mediated optimization efficiency. The mutation operator capitalizes on codon degeneracy to perform synonymous substitutions, with mutation frequency tunable via user-specified parameters and guided by Relative Synonymous Codon Usage to maximize CAI values. Unlike conventional genetic algorithms, this module restricts mutations to single sequences, reducing computational complexity during the evaluation phase.

Thereafter, mutated sequences undergo comprehensive evaluation via a composite scoring function integrating CAI, and MFE (Fig. 1d). This scoring matrix serves as the core metric for iterative optimization, with new sequences only entering the priority pool if they exceed the existing scores. Following predefined iterations, the annealing module (Fig. 1e) applies simulated annealing with a dynamically adjusted cooling schedule, enabling probabilistic jumps across the solution space to circumvent local optima. This mechanism, rooted in Metropolis principles, facilitates convergence to global optimality.

Upon meeting termination criteria, the algorithm discharges the top-ranked sequences from the priority pool, each annotated with structural stability metrics and translational efficiency indices. This approach demonstrates superior performance in balancing structural stability and translational efficiency across both long and short mRNA sequences, outperforming state-of-the-art tools like LinearDesign (as validated across a panoply of validation datasets including therapeutic gene transcripts and neoantigen-encoding sequences, strategically curated to represent clinical relevance, varied sequence lengths, and diverse amino acid compositions; see Results). The modular architecture ensures plug-and-play compatibility with diverse input parameters, enabling seamless application to both neoantigen-encoding short transcripts (9-mers) and long therapeutic mRNA sequences (full-length proteins).

The MSSA’s performance advantage arises from component interactions balancing search breadth, precision, and convergence. Multi-seed initialization enables broad exploration via distributed starting points across the search space. Boltzmann-guided sampling directs mutations toward favorable, underexplored configurations to boost improvement potential. A single-sequence mutation strategy focuses computation on top candidates, reducing recalculation costs and concentrating optimization pressure on high-potential sequences to accelerate convergence. A priority pool retains top-performing sequences across iterations to preserve progress, while simulated annealing allows occasional suboptimal moves to escape local optima. This shift from broad exploration to focused refinement lets the algorithm locate better solutions more efficiently than exhaustive or purely stochastic methods.

Validation of multi-seed algorithm on long therapeutic mRNA sequences

Initial validation confirmed that the Multi-seed Search Algorithm achieves high-fidelity mRNA sequence optimization by integrating critical determinants, including MFE for structural stability, CAI for translational efficiency, and additional biologically relevant parameters. Using the OX40L protein sequence (184 amino acids, corresponding to ~1.72 × 1090 candidate mRNA sequences) as a paradigmatic example (Fig. 2a), the algorithm efficiently navigates this combinatorially intractable codon space, identifying sequences with enhanced expression potential while minimizing computational overhead. By leveraging parallelized Boltzmann sampling and simulated annealing, the algorithm reduces computational complexity by >99.9% compared to exhaustive enumeration.

The optimized OX40L sequence generated by multi-seed search exhibits a MFE of −329.8 kcal/mol (indicating enhanced thermodynamic stability), a CAI of 0.769, and a GC ratio of 0.504 (within the optimal range for mRNA vaccines). These metrics contrast sharply with randomly generated controls, underscoring the algorithm’s efficacy in balancing structural integrity and translational efficiency. Structural predictions via ViennaRNA reveal a transition from abundant single-stranded loops in the native sequence to a predominantly double-stranded helical structure, with reduction in loop nucleotides and increase in base-paired regions, revealing that the resulting structure is more stable and therefore less prone to degradation in vivo.

To validate the algorithm’s efficacy across diverse data, the Multi-seed Search Algorithm was applied to optimize antigen sequences from mRNA vaccines and therapeutically relevant candidates sourced from public repositories (Fig. 2b). The algorithm’s optimization landscape was visualized by projecting the search space onto a 2D plane defined by MFE and CAI (Fig. 2c), the two primary objectives in our framework. Lower MFE values indicate enhanced structural stability, while higher CAI values reflect codon usage optimized for translational efficiency. These metrics exhibit a well-documented trade-off: improving one often degrades the other. Our algorithm addresses this challenge by introducing a tunable hyperparameter, λ, in the composite scoring function, which dynamically balances MFE and CAI contributions. This allows users to specify optimization preferences (e.g. prioritizing stability versus translation) via a continuous parameter space.

For this validation, we set λ = 1 to emphasize balanced optimization of both metrics. The Multi-seed algorithm systematically navigated the codon space, iteratively refining sequences to converge toward the Pareto frontier (Fig. 2c). To benchmark performance, we compared results against LinearDesign—a state-of-the-art algorithm—under identical scoring and parameter settings. Across 10 diverse antigen sequences, the Multi-seed approach demonstrated superiority in eight cases, achieving a median Score reduction of 12.3 kcal/mol (Fig. 2d, Supplementary Fig. S1). Specifically, three sequences exhibited concurrent enhancements in structural stability (median MFE reduction: −12.8 kcal/mol), translational efficiency (median CAI increase: 0.1%), while four sequences prioritized MFE reduction (−13.7 kcal/mol median) with CAI decreases (<5%), and one sequence achieved an MFE increase (+3.7 kcal/mol) alongside a CAI boost (2.4%), showcasing the algorithm’s adaptability to diverse optimization trajectories. These results underscore the algorithm’s capacity to navigate the MFE-CAI trade-off landscape, with 80% of sequences achieving Pareto-optimal solutions that outperformed LinearDesign in composite scoring.

Codon optimization of short neoantigen sequences: Insights from bladder cancer

While mRNA vaccines often use long sequences, short peptide-coding segments remain critical for computational and clinical evaluation. Assessing short peptides enables finer analysis of algorithmic behavior (notably synonymous codon variation) and facilitates high-throughput benchmarking/statistical validation, so our evaluation includes such short sequences with neoantigens serving as a key example. Neoantigen-mRNA vaccine integration is a promising frontier, with multiple neoantigen-based mRNA vaccines in clinical trials advancing personalized medicine. Unlike full-length proteins, neoantigens are short peptides (typically 8–11 amino acids), presenting unique mRNA sequence optimization challenges.

To validate the Multi-seed Search Algorithm across diverse tumor types, we first focused on bladder cancer neoantigens (peptide details in Fig. 3a), optimizing these sequences using both Multi-seed Search and LinearDesign under identical scoring functions with four λ values (0, 1, 2, 0.5). The scoring function, anchored in MFE, gradually shifted focus toward CAI as λ increased (when λ = 0, the score equates to MFE, with lower values denoting better optimization). As shown in Fig. 3b–d, Multi-seed-optimized sequences exhibited marginally lower MFE values than LinearDesign, with statistical significance observed only at λ = 0 (P < 0.001; λ = 1, P = 0.438; λ = 2, P = 0.215). Wilcoxon tests revealed significant score reductions for Multi-seed at λ = 0 and λ = 1 (Fig. 3e–g; λ = 0, P < 0.001; λ = 1, P = 0.043; λ = 2, P = 0.687), underscoring the algorithm’s efficacy in bladder cancer neoantigen optimization.

To characterize how algorithmic metrics respond to λ variations, we calculated average MFE, CAI, and scores for the neoantigen set across λ values, visualized in Fig. 3h–i. Both MFE and CAI trended upward with λ, while scores showed no strong linear correlation. Notably, CAI increased rapidly between λ = 0 and 0.5, with MFE rising more gradually, causing scores to surge. The inflection point at λ = 0.5—where the score curve’s slope approached zero—held mathematical significance, with Multi-seed optimization results remaining significantly superior to LinearDesign at this threshold (Fig. 3j). These findings highlight the algorithm’s adaptability to balance structural stability and translational efficiency across neoantigen landscapes.

Generalizability and robustness in melanoma neoantigen optimization

Next, we extended our analysis to a melanoma-derived dataset (Fig. 4a) to evaluate the Multi-seed Search Algorithm’s generalizability across tumor types. Sequences were optimized using both Multi-seed Search and LinearDesign under identical scoring functions with varying λ values (0, 1, 2, 0.5). Multi-seed-optimized sequences exhibited lower MFE values than LinearDesign across all λ settings (Fig. 4b–d), though statistical significance (P < 0.001) was confined to λ = 0—the configuration exclusively prioritizing MFE. This aligns with the algorithm’s design emphasis on structural stability optimization.

Notably, Multi-seed Search demonstrated superiority in composite scoring across all tested λ values (Fig. 4e–g, j). Wilcoxon tests revealed significant score reductions (λ = 0, P < 0.001; λ = 1, P = 0.008; λ = 2, P = 0.003; λ = 0.5, P < 0.001), underscoring the algorithm’s robustness in balancing MFE and CAI trade-offs. These results highlight Multi-seed Search’s competitive edge in navigating diverse optimization landscapes, outperforming LinearDesign even when translational efficiency (CAI) is prioritized.

Analysis of metric trends across λ values (Fig. 4h–j) revealed a critical inflection point at λ = 0.5, where the score curve plateaued (tangent slope → 0). Here, CAI increased by 12.3% (median) while MFE increased modestly (4.7%), indicating an optimal balance between translational efficiency and structural stability. This convergence suggests λ = 0.5 as a computationally derived sweet spot for dual-objective optimization, offering a data-driven default parameter for similar neoantigen datasets. Collectively, these findings validate Multi-seed Search’s efficacy across distinct tumor types, establishing it as a versatile tool for mRNA vaccine design.

Optiseed platform for integrated mRNA vaccine design

Building upon the Multi-seed Search Algorithm, we developed the Optiseed mRNA sequence optimization online platform—a user-friendly tool for converting amino acid sequences into optimized mRNA sequences. The platform employs a front-end-back-end separation architecture, ensuring development flexibility and modular scalability. In this framework, the front-end serves as a responsive user interface for result visualization, while the back-end algorithmic core communicates via HTTP requests mediated by the Django framework (Fig. 5a). The Multi-seed algorithm is encapsulated as a portable function, enabling seamless integration, and deployment across computing environments.

The platform's main interface (Fig. 5b) features three primary sections: Option 1, Option 2, and Results. In Option 1 (Fig. 5c), users input amino acid sequences in the left panel and adjust optimization parameters in the right panel—including scoring function selection, seed count, iteration steps, hyperparameter λ, and annealing parameters. Option 2 provides a library of mRNA vaccine components (5′ cap structures, UTRs, ORFs, poly(A) tails), enabling rapid assembly of complete mRNA constructs. After submitting inputs, users initiate optimization with real-time progress tracking; clicking “Structure” in the Results section redirects to the output interface (Fig. 5d), displaying ORF sequences, secondary structures, MFE, CAI, and composite scores. Within the current framework, these regulatory elements function as fixed templates concatenated to the optimized ORF to facilitate complete construct design, while the Multi-seed optimization process specifically targets the coding region.

For structural visualization, users can click “Add Molecule” to input optimized sequences, generating interactive secondary structure plots. The platform supports multi-sequence comparison, allowing simultaneous display of multiple structures for side-by-side analysis and editing. A built-in “Guide” function (Fig. 5e) provides step-by-step operational tutorials for novice users. This intuitive design balances advanced algorithmic capabilities with accessible functionality, positioning Optiseed mRNA as a versatile tool for mRNA vaccine design.

Discussion

In the rapidly evolving landscape of mRNA vaccine development, the challenge of efficiently optimizing mRNA sequences to balance structural stability and translational efficiency has emerged as a critical determinant for successful vaccine design. This study presents the Multi-seed Search Algorithm, a novel heuristic optimization strategy, and the Optiseed mRNA platform—a modular, visualization-driven tool developed to address this challenge. By integrating multi-directional seed initialization and simulated annealing, the algorithm demonstrates robust global search capabilities, enabling it to navigate the combinatorial complexity of codon space (e.g. up to 1.72 × 1090 possibilities for a 184-amino acid sequence) while optimizing both structural stability (MFE reduction) and translational efficiency (CAI enhancement). The Optiseed platform, built upon this algorithm, combines front-end-back-end separation architecture with flexible parameter tuning, allowing users to customize optimization trajectories (via hyperparameters like λ) and assemble complete mRNA constructs with components such as 5′ caps, UTRs, and poly(A) tails. This work not only introduces algorithmic innovation for overcoming local optima in sequence design but also delivers a practical, user-friendly solution that aligns with the urgent needs of modern mRNA vaccine development.

The Optiseed platform outperforms existing mRNA design tools (e.g. LinearDesign) by achieving statistically significant improvements in both translational efficiency and structural stability. Leveraging a multi-seed search strategy, it navigates large design spaces with 99.9% reduced computational complexity, effectively avoiding local optima and converging to Pareto-optimal solutions in 80% of tested sequences. Comparative benchmarks across diverse datasets (including bladder cancer and melanoma neoantigens) suggest that Optiseed achieves superior overall optimization performance across multiple λ settings, with statistically significant improvements observed at representative λ values, underscoring its robustness in balancing competing objectives.

Beyond algorithmic innovation, Optiseed offers practical utility through its modular architecture and user-centric design. The platform supports customizable scoring functions and hyperparameter tuning (e.g. seed count, annealing parameters), empowering users to tailor optimization trajectories to specific experimental goals. It is important to note that the optimal λ value is not universal but context-dependent. This flexibility is exemplified by the λ parameter, which dynamically balances MFE and CAI contributions, with empirical data identifying λ = 0.5 as an optimal threshold for dual-objective optimization. Such design freedom, combined with intuitive visualization tools for secondary structure analysis, positions Optiseed as a versatile solution for both academic researchers and industry professionals.

Notwithstanding the promising results, several limitations warrant acknowledgment. The Optiseed platform, while demonstrating robust optimization across diverse datasets, may encounter computational bottlenecks when handling large-scale mRNA designs, with memory consumption and runtime scaling non-linearly with sequence length. Although algorithmic refinements have reduced computational complexity, further optimizations—such as GPU parallelization or adaptive sampling—are needed to support high-throughput workflows.

Additionally, the Multi-seed Search Algorithm’s performance is constrained by inherent stochasticity. While random seed distribution enhances global search coverage, the evolutionary iteration process introduces inter-run variability. This variability, stemming from Boltzmann sampling and simulated annealing dynamics, may compromise result reproducibility in scenarios requiring strict consistency, such as clinical-grade vaccine development. Mitigation strategies—including ensemble optimization or deterministic seed initialization—are under investigation to balance exploration and reproducibility.

While the current study predominantly relies on in silico simulations, we recognize that the gold standard for optimization validation necessitates biological verification through in vitro transcription–translation assays and in vivo animal models. These experiments will assess key metrics such as protein expression levels and immunogenicity of optimized sequences. In subsequent work, we will synthesize platform-optimized mRNA sequences and conduct in vitro transcription–translation experiments to directly compare protein yields between optimized and unoptimized variants. Additionally, we will characterize mRNA stability and half-life in cell lines to confirm resistance to intracellular degradation pathways. Immunogenicity will be evaluated in murine models by quantifying antibody titers and T-cell responses, thereby establishing a comprehensive validation pipeline from computational design to biological functionality.

The Optiseed platform serves as both an efficient design tool for contemporary mRNA vaccine development and a pioneering framework for innovative applications like personalized cancer immunotherapy. By fostering wider adoption, we envision its potential to revolutionize mRNA vaccine development—particularly for rapid-response strategies against emerging pathogens and public health crises. Beyond mRNA vaccines, Optiseed acts as a technical cornerstone for diverse therapeutic mRNA modalities. Its integration with neoantigen vaccine design exemplifies this versatility, holding significant promise for advancing cancer immunotherapy and treating other refractory diseases.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the Multi-seed Search Algorithm and the Optiseed mRNA platform pioneered herein address long-standing bottlenecks in mRNA vaccine design and codon optimization. This framework not only represents a technological breakthrough but also delivers a pragmatic solution tailored to the evolving needs of mRNA vaccine R&D. By enabling efficient codon optimization and integrated construct design, Optiseed has the potential to expedite R&D workflows, reduce development costs, and enhance therapeutic efficacy of mRNA vaccines.

Through its capacity to optimize mRNA sequences for both structural stability and translational efficiency, this platform holds transformative potential for advancing mRNA-based therapeutics across biomedical fields. It paves the way for next-generation vaccines against non-infectious disorders like cancer and holds promise for broader applications in infectious diseases (e.g. influenza, COVID-19). As a pioneering tool at the intersection of computational biology and vaccine development, Optiseed exemplifies how algorithmic innovation can drive translational impact in precision medicine.

Key Points

Novel Multi-seed Searching Algorithm (MSSA) optimizes mRNA codon by synergistically co-adjusting minimum free energy (stability) and codon adaptation index (translational efficiency) via adaptive simulated annealing-genetic algorithm integration, with enhanced global search to avoid local optima.

MSSA has state-of-the-art performance in long mRNA/short neoantigen (bladder cancer/melanoma) tests, achieving superior balanced stability/translational efficiency and resolving their inherent trade-offs.

MSSA-based Optiseed platform enables customizable scoring, flexible parameters and UTR/poly(A) tail integration for end-to-end vaccine design, overcoming rigid conventional tools for precise context-specific optimization.
