# Identification of Immune Subtypes and Candidate mRNA Vaccine Antigens in Small Cell Lung Cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have demonstrated promising outcomes in small cell lung cancer (SCLC), but not all patients benefit from it. Thus, developing precise treatments for SCLC is a particularly urgent need. In our study, we constructed a novel phenotype for SCLC based on immune signatures.

**METHODS**: We clustered patients with SCLC hierarchically in 3 publicly available datasets according to the immune signatures. ESTIMATE and CIBERSORT algorithm were used to evaluate the components of the tumor microenvironment. Moreover, we identified potential mRNA vaccine antigens for patients with SCLC, and qRT-PCR were performed to detect the gene expression.

**RESULTS**: We identified 2 SCLC subtypes and named Immunity High (Immunity_H) and Immunity Low (Immunity_L). Meanwhile, we obtained generally consistent results by analyzing different datasets, suggesting that this classification was reliable. Immunity_H contained the higher number of immune cells and a better prognosis compared to Immunity_L. Gene-set enrichment analysis revealed that several immune-related pathways such as cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, programmed cell death-Ligand 1 expression and programmed cell death-1 checkpoint pathway in cancer were hyperactivated in the Immunity_H. However, most of the pathways enriched in the Immunity_L were not associated with immunity. Furthermore, we identified 5 potential mRNA vaccine antigens of SCLC (NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2), and they were expressed higher in Immunity_L, it indicated that Immunity_L maybe more suitable for tumor vaccine development.

**CONCLUSIONS**: SCLC can be divided into Immunity_H and Immunity_L subtypes. Immunity_H may be more suitable for treatment with ICIs. NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2 may be act as potential antigens for SCLC.

Background

Global Cancer Statistics 2020 showed that there were approximately 2.2 million new lung cancer cases and 1.8 million deaths annually, representing the second most common and deadliest malignancies globally. Small cell lung cancer (SCLC) is a highly aggressive neuroendocrine lung cancer, accounting for approximately 13%-15% of all lung cancer cases. It is characterized by high circulating tumor cell, rapid tumor growth, early and extensive metastasis, and the survival time is dismal. Although chemotherapy and radiotherapy have been the standard treatment for the past few decades, it is extremely vulnerable to drug resistance and recurrence, and no substantial progress has been made in systemic therapy.

Recently, precision oncology has been well developed, and molecular subtype hold a crucial role in the treatment of tumors. Substantial evidence suggests that SCLC is a molecular and clinical heterogeneous cancer. Gay et al. classified patients with SCLC into 4 subgroups according to the ASCL1, NEUROD1, and POU2F3 expression, and named these 4 subgroups as SCLC-A, SCLC-N, SCLC-P, and SCLC-I. The former 3 groups overexpressed ASCL1, NEUROD1, and POU2F3, respectively. SCLC-I was associated with lower expression of these 3 transcription factors but had higher expression of genes related to human leukocyte antigens (HLAs), interferon-γ activation, and several immune checkpoint molecules, including programmed cell death-Ligand 1 (PD-L1), programmed cell death-1 (PD-1), cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA4), and other targetable immune checkpoints such as CD38, IDO1, TIGIT, C10orf54, ICOS, and LAG3. SCLC-I subtype was accompanied by an inflamed gene signature, and the number of several immune cell populations were markedly increased in SCLC-I tumors, including cytotoxic T-cell, NK cells, and macrophages. These subgroups were shown to exhibit differential sensitivity to treatment, including a greater chance of benefit of ICIs in patients with SCLC-I tumors. Rudin et al proposed similar molecular subtypes as mentioned earlier and referred to these molecular subtypes as SCLC-A, SCLC-N, SCLC-P, and SCLC-Y. SCLC-Y was classified by YAP1 expression. However, most patients with SCLC were already at a late stage when diagnosed and few underwent surgery, so it was difficult to obtain enough tumor tissues to detect the expression of all these markers mentioned earlier in SCLC samples. Therefore, they posited that the characterization of SCLC subtypes remained an area of intensive exploration, and these designations may require modification or further definition in future. Baine et al detected ASCL1, NEUROD1, POU2F3, and YAP1 expression in 174 SCLC samples by immunohistochemistry. The results showed that the majority of SCLC were distinguished by a predominant expression of either ASCL1 and/or NEUROD1, whereas few SCLC lacked either of these regulators. Meanwhile, YAP1 was found to be significantly under expressed. The above studies revealed that SCLC was highly heterogeneous. Precisely identifying the specific subtypes of SCLC would greatly improve the treatment efficacy of SCLC and ultimately improve the prognosis of patients with SCLC. Although above studies probably established some foundations for SCLC treatment, it is still necessary to find new and more effective therapies to improve the survival of SCLC.

Recently, immunotherapy has become a rising star in cancer treatment. Notably, it has broken the awkward situation of no alternative treatment option for SCLC and demonstrated a promising treatment. Several recent clinical trials that combined immunotherapy targeting the PD-1, PD-L1, and CTLA4 antibodies with platinum-based regimens significantly improved survival time of patients with SCLC. IMpower133, a phase III clinical trial, reported that atezolizumab combined with the traditional etoposide-carboplatin regimen significantly improved median overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival in patients with SCLC compared to chemotherapy alone. Another clinical trial, CASPIAN, showed similar results to the IMpower133 study: the durvalumab combined with chemotherapy group had a significantly improved median OS and ORR compared to the group treated with chemotherapy alone. The results of these 2 trials show a more consistent effect on the difference in OS, suggesting that more patients with SCLC could benefit from chemotherapy combined with ICIs. However, ICIs only improved the survival time for patients with SCLC by 2-3 months. Moreover, IMpower133 and CASPIAN showed that only 12.6% and 18.0% of patients had remained progression-free at one year, respectively. The above results suggests that only a limited patients with SCLC benefit from ICIs, which could be mainly attributed to the “cold” tumor microenvironment (TME). Preclinical and clinical studies have suggested that the immunologically “cold” tumors were characterized by the low expression of PD-L1, non-T cell-inflamed, little antigen presentation cells, and an immunosuppressive TME. In contrast, the “hot” TME was characterized by a high density of tumor-infiltrating T cells, higher tumor mutation burden, as well as higher immune checkpoint expression. Accumulating studies have provided evidence that immune checkpoint blockade is effective in hot tumors while having minimal effects in cold tumors. In other words, immunotherapy for SCLC is currently limited to ICIs, and not all patients could response it. Thus, this work was conducted to further define the immune phenotype and identify candidate mRNA vaccine targets to expand the scope of immunotherapy for SCLC.

Materials and Methods

Data of SCLC

Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO) is an international public functional genomics data repository that archives and freely distributes microarray, next-generation sequencing, and other forms of high-throughput functional genomics data submitted by the research community. The following search terms were used: “SCLC” and “small cell lung cancer”. Moreover, the inclusion criteria of the characteristics: (1) the samples including SCLC and/or normal controls, (2) organism: homo sapiens, (3) sample source: tissue, (4) the gene expression could obtain, and (5) the sample size was greater than 20. Finally, GSE60052, GSE99316, and GSE30219 data were included, then sorted out the data for SCLC and control samples for further investigation.

Identification of the Immune Subtypes and Evaluation of the TME

As described in previous studies, the single-sample gene-set enrichment analysis (GSEA) score was used to quantify the enrichment of the 29 immune-related gene sets which represented diverse immune cell types, functions, and pathways for each SCLC sample, and based on it, we performed a hierarchical clustering of SCLC. Then, ESTIMATE and CIBERSORT algorithm were used to evaluate the TME.

The Differences in the Expression of Immunomodulators Between the SCLC Subtypes

The relationship between the immune subtypes and immune-related checkpoint molecules was assessed, such as HLA, chemokine-chemokine receptors. HLA includes HLA I, HLA II, and HLA III. HLA class I are primarily involved in the recognition and delivery of endogenous antigen, which could activate CD8+ T cells and transform activated CD8+ T cells into active cytotoxic T lymphocytes, thereby killing target cells. Its downregulation would lead to impaired recognition and resulting in immune escape. Moreover, HLA class II are mainly expressed by antigen-presenting cells and mainly involved in the delivery of exogenous antigen to CD4+ T cells and also initiating the body’s humoral immune response. Moreover, chemokines and chemokine receptors were also important for recruiting immune cells to the TME.

Survival Analysis Between SCLC Subtypes and GSEA

In the GSE30219, we obtained survival data and analyzed the relationship between 2 SCLC subtypes and survival status. The clinical characteristics of SCLC were supplemented in Supplementary Table S1. Moreover, GSEA analysis was performed and the upregulated or downregulated GO and KEGG pathways were identified in SCLC subtypes, respectively.

Identification of Potential Antigens of SCLC

Overexpressed genes between SCLC and normal samples in GSE60052 and GSE30219 were identified with criterions: log2|fold change (FC)| ≥ 3 and FDR < 0.05. The mutated genes about SCLC were downloaded from cBioPortal (https://www.cbioportal.org/study/summary?id=sclc_ucologne_2015). Then, the common overexpressed genes in GSE60052 and GSE30219 were intersected with the mutated genes to obtain the candidate mRNA vaccine antigens.

Cell Culture

SCLC cell lines NCI-H1688 and NCI-H446 and human normal lung epithelial cell lines BEAS-2B were purchased from American Type Culture Collection (ATCC). NCI-H1688 and NCI-H446 were cultured with RPMI 1640 medium, and BEAS-2B were cultured with DMEM medium.

Real-Time PCR Assay

RNAiso plus reagent (Takara, China) was applied to isolated Total RNA. HiScript II Reverse Transcriptase kit (Vazyme, China) was used for reverse transcription. The real-time PCR was performed by using 2× SYBR Green qPCR Master Mix (Bimake, USA). The primers were purchased from the Sangon Biotech (Shanghai) Co. Ltd, and information were as follows: GAPDH-F: GGAAGCTTGTCATCAATGGAAATC; GAPDH-R: TGATGACCCTTTTGGCTCCC; NEK2-F: AGAAGAGGGCGACAATTAGGAGA; NEK2-R: CTGCTCTAGCCAGTTTGTCCTCT; NOL4-F: AAACTGGGCTGGAGCAAGATGAA; NOL4-R: GTGCAGGAGACTTGCTTCCATTT; RALYL-F: GAGTGAGCGACATGCAAGAGC; RALYL-R: CATAACCGCCAACAGACAGGAA; SH3GL2-F: CATCTAAAGAAGTTGGAGGGTCG; SH3GL2-R: GGCTCACTTGTTCAATATCCATCTC; ZIC2-F: ATGGTACGTGTGACGGGTCG; ZIC2-R: GATCACAAGGTGCCCTCGCT; BEX1-F: TCCCTTTGGATGCTGGTGAAT, BEX1-R: TGACTGCCCGCAGACTATGAC; CDC20-F: GACGCCCACCAAGAAGGAAC; CDC20-R: CTGATAACCCTCTGGCGCATT; CDCA8-F: CTTGACTACTTCGCCCTTGGAG; CDCA8-R: AGTGTTAGCACGGCTTGACCTT; STIL-F: AACCCTTGCTTCATCAACCATC; STIL-R: GTAGACGGCTGCTCAGTTTCCTT. The 2−ΔΔCt method for relative quantification of the gene expression.

Results

Cluster SCLC Subtypes

We hierarchically clustered SCLC samples in 3 datasets (GSE60052, GSE99316, and GSE30219) according to the immune signatures (Additional file 1: Supplementary Table S2). Interestingly, all 3 datasets displayed 2 obvious clusters, and we named them Immunity High (Immunity_H) and Immunity Low (Immunity_L) (Fig. 1). Meanwhile, we observed that immune scores and stromal scores were exhibited noticeably elevated in Immunity_H compared to Immunity_L in all 3 datasets, (Fig. 2A, 2B) whereas the tumor purity was higher in the Immunity_L (Fig. 2C). Altogether, these results indicated that Immunity_H had the largest populations of immune cells and stromal cells, whereas Immunity_L contained more tumor cells.

The Differences in the Expression of Immunomodulators Between the SCLC Subtype

Our results indicated that most HLA genes were highly expressed in Immunity_H than in Immunity_L in 3 databases (Fig. 3A). Moreover, chemokines and chemokine receptors were also important for recruiting immune cells to the TME. We analyzed their expression in 3 databases in 2 SCLC subtypes, the result showed that they were expressed higher in the Immunity_H in GSE60052 (Fig. 3B, 3C). Among the GSE99316 and GSE30219 databases, we got the similar results, and the results were presented in Supplementary Fig. S1. Furthermore, we found that Immunity_H had the highest PD-L1, PD-1, and CTLA4 expression than Immunity_L (Fig. 4A). Moreover, other immune checkpoints were also analyzed in the 2 SCLC subtypes. We found that they were also highly expressed in Immunity_H groups than Immunity_L, including BTLA, C10orf54, CD200R1, CD38, CD80, CD86, ICOS, IDO1, LAIR1, LGALS3, and TIGIT. Surprisingly, the results were consistent in all 3 databases (Fig. 4B--4D). Survival analyses showed that Immunity_H likely had a better survival prognosis (Fig. 4E, 4F).

Estimation of the TME and Immune Subtypes

TME is a heterogeneous system consisting of many cells. In the GSE60052, we found that the expression of CD8 T cell subpopulation marker genes CCL5, CD8A, CD8B, CXCR3, GZMA, GZMB, PRF1, and TBX21 were expressed higher in Immunity_H than in Immunity_L (Fig. 5A), and it indicated that there was more CD8 T-cell infiltration in Immunity_H. The macrophages subpopulation marker genes C1QA, CLEC5A, CSF1R, CYBB, LILRA2, MARCO, MMP8, and MS4A6A were also overexpressed in the Immunity_H (Fig. 5B). The expression of C1QB, CRTAM, IL7R, NCR1, and PRR5L was significantly upregulated in Immunity_H group, suggesting greater NK cell infiltration in Immunity_H (Fig. 5C). Similarly, we also observed that Th1 cell and dendritic cell marker genes were also upregulated in the Immunity_H, and the results were represented in Fig. 5D and Fig. 5E respectively. CIBERSORT was used to fully evaluate the infiltration of immune cells. As expected, several immune cell populations were markedly increased in Immunity_H, including CD8 T cells, NK cells, and dendritic cell (Fig. 5F). To validate the reliability and accuracy of the results, we performed verification with another 2 datasets (GSE99316 and GSE30219). It was found that we have obtained almost similar results, and the results were presented in Supplementary Figs. S2 and S3, respectively.

GO and KEGG Pathway Analysis of SCLC Subtypes

To further investigate the differences between the 2 immune subtypes, GSEA analysis was performed. In the GSE30219 database, we found that many immune-related GO terms were enriched in the Immunity_H. Specifically, dendritic cell antigen processing and presentation and establishment of T cell polarity were enriched in biological process (BP) in Immunity_H (Fig. 6A). For cellular component (CC), MHC class II protein complex, immunoglobulin complex, T-cell receptor complex, and immunological synapse were the main enriched terms in Immunity_H group (Fig. 6B). In molecular function (MF), the main enriched categories were chemokine (C-C motif) receptor binding, toll-like receptor binding, MHC class I protein binding, chemokines and chemokine (C-C) receptors activity, and immune receptor activity (Fig. 6C). Furthermore, the enriched KEGG pathways were also analyzed. We found that immune-related pathways were activated in Immunity_H, such as cytokine-cytokine receptor interaction, toll-like receptor signaling pathway, PD-L1 expression and PD-1 checkpoint pathway in cancer and chemokine signaling pathway. However, the Immunity_L enriched pathways were negatively associated with immunity, including dopaminergic synapse, synaptic vesicle cycle, GABAergic synapse, insulin secretion, amphetamine addiction, circadian entrainment, cocaine addiction, taste transduction, maturity onset diabetes of the young, and nicotine addiction (Fig. 6D). In the GSE60052 database, we also found that the Immunity_H was mostly enriched in the immune-related signaling pathways, while the Immunity_L was mostly enriched in little immune-related pathways (Supplementary Fig. S4). In the GSE99316 database, we obtained the similar results, and the results were presented in Supplementary Fig. S5.

Identification of Potential mRNA Vaccine Antigens of SCLC

Encouraged by the above results, we found that the Immunity_H may be more suitable for ICIs. About Immunity_L group patients, we still need to find more effective methods. In recent years, tumor vaccines have become a promising treatment method and have great application prospects. However, the development of mRNA vaccines was based on the selection of tumor antigen. Usually, tumor-associated antigens were significantly overexpressed in cancer cells compared to normal cells and could trigger T-cell recognition, eliciting an anticancer response. To identify potential antigens for SCLC, we first screened the overexpressed genes in SCLC. According to the criterion of log2|fold change, (FC)|≥3 and FDR< 0.05. In the GSE60052, we obtained 104 overexpressed genes in SCLC compared to normal samples (Fig. 7A); In the GSE30219, we detected 123 overexpressed genes that might encode tumor-associated antigens (Fig. 7B). In addition, a total of 11 093 mutated genes were filtered, and the 10 most frequently mutated genes were ACVR2B, ALKBH3, ANTXR1, CENPB, DUSP8, GOLGA6A, GP1BB, HSD17B12, MARK2, and MBD5 (Fig. 7C, 7D). Overall, 9 overexpressed and frequently mutated genes (NEK2, RALYL, CDCA8, CDC20, STIL, NOL4, SH3GL2, ZIC2, BEX1) were identified (Fig. 7E). To verify the accuracy of the analysis, we applied PCR for validation. As shown in Fig. 8A, we found that NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2 were expressed higher in SCLC cell line NCI-H446 than BEAS-2B. But the expression level of BEX1, CDC20, CDCA8, and STIL4 were expressed lower in NCI-H446 compared to human normal lung epithelial cell lines BEAS-2B. The PCR results indicated that NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2 maybe the potential mRNA vaccine antigens of SCLC. Then, another SCLC cell line NCI-H1688, was used to verify the expression levels of NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2, the results similarly suggested that their expression levels were higher in NCI-H1688 than in BEAS-2B, and the results presented in Fig. 8B. Then, we further analyzed the potential mRNA vaccine antigens in 2 SCLC subtypes. The results showed that they were expressed higher in Immunity_L than in Immunity_H in GSE60052 and GSE99316 databases (Fig. 8C, 8D), and it indicated that the Immunity_L maybe more suitable for tumor vaccine development.

Discussion

In the present study, we divided patients with SCLC into Immunity_H and Immunity_L subtypes. We found that most immunomodulatory factors, such as HLA, PD-L1, PD-1, CTLA4, and chemokines and chemokine receptors, were elevated in the Immunity_H. Moreover, other immune checkpoints, such as BTLA, C10orf54, CD200R1, CD38, CD80, CD86, ICOS, IDO1, LAG3, LAIR1, LGALS3, and TIGIT, were also highly expressed in Immunity_H. Survival analyses showed that Immunity_H had a better prognosis. These results demonstrated that Immunity_H presented as “hot” tumor, while Immunity_L exhibited as “cold” tumor. The characteristics of Immunity_H were closely similar to the SCLC-I subtype which was previously studied by Gay et al. SCLC-I also had higher expression of genes related to HLAs, interferon-γ activation, and countless immune checkpoint molecules, including PD-L1, PD-1, CTLA4, and other targetable immune checkpoints. It indicated that SCLC-I also had the characteristics of “hot” tumor. Moreover, SCLC-I and Immunity_H subtype may respond better to ICIs. However, there were also some differences between SCLC-I and Immunity_H subtypes. SCLC-I subtype was defined based on the expression of ASCL1, NEUROD1, and POU2F3, while the Immunity_H subtype that we proposed was based on immune cell types, functions, and pathways. SCLC-I and Immunity_H subtype both had small sample sizes but could lay the foundation for subsequent investigations. In future, further rigorous and well-designed researches and multicenter clinical studies with sufficient sample sizes would be required to verify our results. In addition, our findings could be corroborated in some previous studies. Sabari et al indicated that SCLC was considered to be a particularly immune-cold cancer and only a limited proportion responded to immunotherapy. Chan et al. also proposed that patients with SCLC showed considerably clinical heterogeneity, and they pointed out that SCLC-N had an immune-cold phenotype with fewer CD8+ T cells compared to SCLC-A subtype by analyzing the TME. The clinical trials, IMpower133 and CASPIAN, showed that only a limited number of patients with SCLC could benefit from ICIs. As presented in our results, the Immunity_H might be more suitable for ICIs.

Encouraged by the above positive results, to further explore the possible mechanisms behind the Immunity_H subtype appropriateness for ICIs, we analyzed the cell subpopulations in the immune microenvironment and the possible pathways.It was found that the more immune cells infiltrated in TME, the superior its possible benefits for ICIs. However, there are both anti-tumor and pro-tumor immune cells in the TME. We further analyzed the content of immune cells in the 2 SCLC subtypes, and the results indicated that there were more CD8 T cells, NK cells, Th1 cells, and dendritic cells infiltration in Immunity_H. The majority of these immune cells were anti-tumor immune cells, and this finding was consistent with the expectation that Immunity_H was presented as “hot” tumor and maybe more suitable for ICIs. Moreover, KEGG pathway analysis showed that immune-related pathways were obvious activated in Immunity_H. These discoveries were reinforced by several recent studies showing that multiple immune characteristics positively correlated with natural killer cell-mediated cytotoxicity and chemokine signaling pathway in SCLC. In contrast, Immunity_L-enriched pathways was related negatively associated with SCLC immunity.

Immunity_H subtype may be more suitable for ICIs. However, Immunity_L subtype patients were characterized by the low expression of PD-L1, little T cell-inflamed, little antigen presentation cells, and an immunosuppressive TME. It exhibited as “cold” tumor and less likely to respond to ICIs. Currently, it is a great challenge to manage immune “cold” tumors. The mRNA vaccine, as a promising method of cancer immunotherapy, can be used to activate the host anti-tumor immunity by expressing tumor-specific or tumor-associated antigens and facilitating antigen-presenting cells (APCs) activation. Moreover, mRNA vaccine has more advantages of safe administration, rapid development potentials, minimal non-specific effects, high potency, and cost-effective manufacturing. Therefore, mRNA vaccine maybe an effective method to against Immunity_L SCLC. In our study, we identified NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2 as potential mRNA vaccine antigens for development mRNA vaccine in SCLC. These findings are supported by several recently observations. Motylewska et al examined the mRNA and protein levels of NEK2 in 22 cases of SCLC and found that elevated levels of NEK2 expression could be observed in patients with SCLC. Kim et al revealed that NOL4 was expressed highly in SCLC and weakly or undetectably in non-SCLC, suggesting that NOL4 could act as a potential tumor antigen in SCLC. Güre et al. detected the expression of ZIC2 mRNA in 11 SCLC cell lines and normal tissues with PCR, and the result showed that ZIC2 was detected in 9 out of 11 SCLC cell lines. Vural et al. investigated the expression levels of ZIC2 in 90 SCLC individuals, finding that anti-ZIC2 antibodies were observed in 28% of patients and patients with high-titer ZIC2 antibodies attained a tendency toward better survival. The above results were consistent with our results that NEK2, NOL4, RALYL, SH3GL2, and ZIC2 maybe the potential mRNA vaccine antigens of SCLC. Then, we further analyzed the potential mRNA vaccine antigens in 2 SCLC subtypes. The results showed that the 5 potential mRNA vaccine antigens were expressed higher in Immunity_L. The above clues suggested that the Immunity_L maybe more suitable for tumor vaccine development. To date, some preclinical experiments of mRNA vaccines have been heavily pursued to treat immunologically “cold” tumors, and promising outcomes have been observed in solid tumors, such as pancreatic cancer, kidney renal clear cell carcinoma (KIRC), prostate cancer,etc. For SCLC, similar interventions should be evaluated. In our study, we found that “cold” tumor subtypes may be more suitable for tumor vaccine development.

Our findings have laid the foundation for subsequent studies on the precision treatment of SCLC, but there are still some limitations. First, most patients with SCLC patients were already at a late stage when diagnosed and few underwent surgery, so it was difficult for us to obtain enough tumor tissues for intensive investigation as for other tumors. Further rigorous and well-designed researches and multicenter clinical studies with sufficient sample sizes would be required to verify our results. Second, there were few databases available on SCLC, although The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database contains most tumor types, it does not include SCLC data. The SCLC data obtained in our study had a small sample size, and subsequent expanded sample size was still necessary to validate our results.

Conclusions

SCLC can be divided into Immunity_H and Immunity_L subtypes. Immunity_H may be more suitable for treatment with ICIs, and Immunity_L possibly better suited for tumor vaccine development.
