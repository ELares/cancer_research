# Applications of CRISPR technology in cellular immunotherapy.

## Abstract

CRISPR technology has transformed multiple fields, including cancer and immunology. CRISPR-based gene editing and screening empowers direct genomic manipulation of immune cells, opening doors to unbiased functional genetic screens. These screens aid in the discovery of novel factors that regulate and reprogram immune responses, offering novel drug targets. The engineering of immune cells using CRISPR has sparked a transformation in the cellular immunotherapy field, resulting in a multitude of ongoing clinical trials. In this review, we discuss the development and applications of CRISPR and related gene editing technologies in immune cells, focusing on functional genomics screening, gene editing-based cell therapies, as well as future directions in this rapidly advancing field.

Application of CRISPR technology in cellular immunotherapy

Summary

CRISPR technology has transformed multiple fields including cancer and immunology. CRISPR based gene editing and screening empowers direct genomic manipulation of immune cells, opening doors to unbiased functional genetic screens. These screens aid in the discovery of novel factors that regulate and reprogram immune responses, offering novel drug targets. The engineering of immune cells using CRISPR has sparked a transformation in the cellular immunotherapy field, resulting in a multitude of ongoing clinical trials. In this review, we discuss the development and applications of CRISPR and related gene editing technologies in immune cells, focusing on functional genomics screening, gene editing based cell therapies, as well as future directions in this rapidly advancing field.

Introduction

Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) are part of an adaptive immune system present in most bacteria that enables them to defend against bacteriophages and other foreign genetic materials . Although many variations of this system exist, the most studied one is comprised of CRISPR that can be transcribed into CRISPR RNA (crRNA) and trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA), as well as CRISPR-associated nucleases (Cas) proteins . These Cas proteins can complex with crRNA and tracrRNA, and subsequently cut target foreign DNA that are complement to the crRNA sequences. Consequently, the resulting DNA fragments are integrated into the CRISPR array as new spacers, which will be used to guide the disruption of the same foreign DNA when the same pathogen invades again .

This intricate system has since been adapted to facilitate gene editing in many cell types and has revolutionized the field of genetic engineering. Extensive research has been conducted to enhance the effectiveness of gene editing within the CRISPR-Cas system. One notable advancement involves the utilization of single guide RNAs (sgRNA), which are derived from the fusion of crRNAs and tracrRNA sequences . These modified sgRNAs have proven to be highly efficient when implementing the CRISPR-Cas system in mammalian cells.

Furthermore, numerous Cas proteins with distinct characteristics have been discovered, with Cas9 and Cas12a being the most widely employed in both basic and translational research . In the gene editing process, Cas9 or Cas12a is guided to specific gene loci by sgRNAs to induce double-stranded DNA breaks (DSBs), which can be repaired through error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homology-directed repair (HDR). NHEJ is utilized to achieve loss-of-function (LOF) mutations by introducing random insertions or deletions at the cleavage sites, leading to the disruption of target genes. Conversely, HDR is commonly employed to achieve gain-of-function (GOF) mutations or transgene expression by providing a homologous repair template alongside the CRISPR-Cas system  (Figure 1).

Moreover, various engineering approaches involve the incorporation of catalytically inactive Cas or dead Cas (dCas) proteins paired with diverse trans-modifiers. For example, VP64 and its derivatives can be fused to a dCas protein for CRISPR activation (CRISPRa) , or a Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) repressor for CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) . Notably, novel gene editing tools, such as base editing and prime editing have been developed to facilitate precise modifications without introducing DSBs, thereby minimizing the risk of undesired genotoxicity. These innovative systems involve nickase Cas (nCas) proteins that have either one of the two catalytic sites inactivated, fused with different enzyme tools such as deaminase (base editing) or reverse transcriptase (prime editing). Specifically, base editing uses the nCas-deaminase fusion to directly convert one base pair to another . On the other hand, prime editing enables more precise modifications by combining nCas9 with a reverse transcriptase. It allows for the insertion, deletion, or substitution of DNA sequences at specific genomic locations by utilizing a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA)  (Figure 1). The cumulation of these strategies have expanded the versatility and adaptability of the CRISPR-Cas system, allowing it to be tailored to different gene editing purposes.

CRISPR technology has revolutionized cancer immunology research by enabling functional genetic screens to explore new facets of immune cell regulation and reprogramming. In addition, CRISPR-based engineering of cytotoxic immune cells has transformed the immunotherapeutic industry leading to several ongoing clinical trials. In this review, we discuss the applications of immune cell-based CRISPR technology with a focus on functional genomics screening, advancements in T cell-based therapies, as well as the future directions of CRISPR research in cancer immunology.

CRISPR screening in immune cells

The CRISPR/Cas system and its derivations are not just useful for single genetic perturbations but can be extended to high-throughput approaches, including pooled and arrayed screens. These screens have been extensively used in cancer cells to determine essential genes for survival, as well as novel gene combinations that contribute to proliferation and metastasis . Additional interests in CRISPR screening have been applied to T cells with a particular focus on improving immune function in the context of cancer. In this section, we present a thorough discussion of screening strategies, review current screens of T cell reprogramming, and describe directions for future studies.

CRISPR screen design

CRISPR screens can be powerful approaches to explore genetic factors that underlie important processes in immunity and cancer research; However, it is important to appreciate that pooled CRISPR screens involve several intermediate processes that can impart confounding effects on the results, such as guide efficiency, chromatin accessibility at the intended cut site, HDR/NHEJ rates, and PCR biases. All of these factors can create layers of experimental noise in the screen readout, which refers to the selection strategy that determines the cells to be sequenced for gRNA quantification . It is therefore critical to design a screen in a way that reduces additional sources of variation from the data.

Readout method

The readout of a screen is the comprehensive determination and quantification of the entire library of desire samples. Enrichment or depletion of specific constructs of the library can be then determined by statistical analysis of the library representation. Simple enrichment readouts can also be sorted by flow cytometry to include the top percentile of cells that express a marker of interest, such as LAMP-1, as an indicator of T cell degranulation , IL-2 and interferon-gamma . Enrichment-based screens work best when there is increased growth during or post selection, as this can help to create a log-fold increase in clones through the extra rounds of cellular division. A common alternative to enrichment is comparison between conditions, or before and after some selective pressure. Comparative screen analyses are very useful to distinguish the effects of a treatment or even the survival of unfavorable conditions, like the tumor microenvironment (TME). For example, recent studies have used this strategy to identify factors of cancer-mediated resistance in T cells, including genes of the interferon-γ receptor (IFNγR) signaling pathway (IFNGR1, JAK1 or JAK2) .

Selective pressure

Regardless of the readout method, strong selective pressure is critical to achieve a high signal-to-noise ratio, and thus reproducible results. In comparison-based enrichment, it is also important for the samples and/or controls to have good degree of similarity in most reads; Otherwise, the comparison will produce sporadic results because the samples are not comparable. It is also important to note that CRISPR screen analyses do not follow all of the assumptions of RNA-seq, particularly that transcriptional differences between different treatment samples is supposed to be a small proportion of the variation, whereas CRISPR screen samples can have drastic variability, due to the fact that the screen reads represent functional genetic changes and RNA-seq reads depict transcriptional quantification .

Library design and size

One often overlooked aspect of screening is the gRNA library, of which, there are an overwhelming number of genome-wide and gene set-specific designs, each with several iterations within the previous decade. The most important parameters are the (1) spacer design, (2) number of gRNA/gene, and (3) choice of gene targets. First, spacer design has evolved over the years to minimize off-targeting and maximize gene-disruption, yet the human Brunello and mouse Brie genome-wide knockout libraries that first incorporated these attributes , have remained the standard in the field. For CRISPR activation and inhibition, the respective Calabrese/Caprano (human/mouse) and Dolcetto/Dolomiti (human/mouse) libraries have become widely used , along with the synergistic activation modular (SAM) system library for activation , yet there are areas that could be optimized and/or customized for specific goals, given that this gRNA class targets genetic regulatory elements (RE), and the CRISPRi/a at these loci could have pleiotropic effects to other genes. Another important library parameter to consider is the number of gRNA/gene, which has drastically decreased over the years from as many of 10 guides/gene in the Sabatini/Lander library of 2014  to as few as 2 guides/gene in the Gattinara/Gouda (human/mouse) library , due to improved gRNA designs. This trend can also be attributed to a newfound appreciation for the idea that guides can have a broad variation in efficiency, and greater variation in the effect of same-gene gRNAs makes gene scoring more difficult to establish. While it should be noted that some analysis algorithms can incorporate predicted gRNA efficiencies in their analyses, such as MAGeCK, RIGER, and JACKS , it will still be preferred to lower gRNA variation with a smaller gRNA/gene ratio from the start of the screen, as there could be a discrepancy between true and predicted gRNA efficiency due to context specific experimental parameters. Another major consideration in choosing a gRNA library is its overall size, which is especially important with in vivo screens, given the limitations in the number of cells used in an adoptive cell transfer and that are reasonably localized to a tissue of interest, as described above. For activation screens, researchers could also opt to use a pooled library of barcoded gene cDNAs, which, compared to CRISPR-activation methods, should provide cleaner results, given that one vector per gene/transcript makes the library smaller, there is no need to worry about gRNA design or efficiency, and there is no need to induce expression of a Cas endonuclease .

In vivo CRISPR screen parameters and strategies

The in vivo tumor microenvironment can drastically complicate any experimental platform, given that the TME is a heterogeneous mixture of cell populations, often including divergent anti-tumor and pro-tumor functions, each that can exert considerable influence on each other through various direct and indirect communication pathways . Despite these complications, there are many critical questions that can only be appropriately addressed in vivo. It is therefore important to choose to perform a tumor-immunity screen in vivo or run an in vitro screen and anticipate that the top targets validate with an in vivo tumor model.

In vitro CRISPR screens in cytotoxic T cells

T cell-based in vitro CRISPR screens can be powerful tools to functionally explore complex intracellular processes and cell-to-cell communication signaling. One frequent focus in these screens has been the identification of factors that enhance activation and effector function. One study used in vitro genome-wide screens for regulators that enhanced the expression of the CD69 activation marker in Jurkat T cells and identified FAM49B as negative regulator of TCR signaling and downstream actin dynamics . In addition, an arrayed screen of 25 kinases involved in TCR signaling was used to identify p38 as a negative regulator of CD8 T cell proliferation, cytotoxic function, redox homeostasis, and memory . In a study by Ye et al., a CRISPR-activation screen in antigen-specific CD8 T cells identified PRODH2 as a metabolic regulatory gene that when overexpressed promotes greater effector function in primary mouse T cells and human CAR-T cells .

In vitro T cell screening platforms have also been combined with single-cell transcriptomics as a screen-readout method. The techniques for these multimodal analyses can be split into strategies that incorporate a barcoded sequence with a poly-A tail, such as Perturb-seq , CRISP-seq , and Mosaic-seq , as well as the non-barcoded CROP-seq strategy . One of the most methods is Perturb-seq, which was recently performed with a genome-wide CRISPRa screen that mapped regulatory factors to distinct cytokine transcriptional patterns, following T cell stimulation . An in vivo perturb-seq platform was also incorporated into the design of a study by Belk et al., in which INO80 and BAF genes were identified with in vitro CRISPR analyses of T cell persistence, and a small-scale in vivo perturb-seq analysis demonstrated that deficiency of Arid1a gene, of the cBAF complex, enhanced anti-tumor efficacy in vivo . The perturb-seq platform has also been combined with CITE-seq technology, that labels cell surface markers, to dissect the genetic factors that contribute resistance to immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) by patient-derived melanoma cancer cells. The study confirmed known ICB resistance from IFN-γ-JAK/STAT signaling and identified CD58 as a novel factor of immune evasion . In addition, a study led by Shifrut et al. investigated the factors of TCR signaling using in vitro CROP-seq screens of TCR-signaling factors that increased proliferation, based on CFSE staining. This screen confirmed the role of LCP2 as a positive regulator, as well as CBLB, CD5, and RASA2 as negative regulators to T cell activation .

More recently, complex readout strategies have been devised that incorporate generalized linear models, which are made possible by analysis tools such as MAGeCK-MLE  and JACKS . In particular, these methods have enabled robust analysis of time-course experiments, such the sequential stimulation of T cells in a study by Dai et al. that investigated factors that promote T cell persistence through the quantification of a pooled CRISPR screen over multiple time points . A GLM-based approach can therefore be a critical tool to address screen cofactors.

In vivo CRISPR application against immunosuppression

Due to the technical difficulties of in vivo CRISPR screens, the vast majority of in vivo screens have been performed in cancer cells. These screens have revealed new cancer drivers, such as Prkar1a in hepatocellular carcinoma  and novel combinations of driver mutations in glioblastoma , as well as KMT2D as an immune checkpoint regulator in multiple cancers . In addition, in vivo CRISPR screens of cancer cells have led to the development of immunotherapeutic strategies that directly target immune-tumor cell interactions. For example, Multiplexed Activation of Endogenous Genes as an Immunotherapy (MAEGI), is a pooled library of CRISPR-activation gRNAs that, when delivered to the tumor microenvironment by AAV vector, was shown to significantly enhance immune cell stimulation and tumor elimination . Multiplex Universal Combinatorial Immunotherapy via Gene-silencing (MUCIG), was developed that uses a series of gRNAs for immunosuppressive genes (Cd274, Lgals9, Lgals3, and Cd47), along with the mRNA-editing Cas13d endonuclease, to shift the intratumor immune population profile, decreasing myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and increasing cytotoxic CD8 T cell proportions .

T and NK cell in vivo screening

Tumor infiltration is a critical limitation of many immune cell types whereby the tumor can escape lysis by strategies that include decreased immune recognition, decreased antigen presentation, recruitment factors, dense matrix . While in vitro migration assays could be used to examine recruitment factors and other paracrine signaling events, only an in vivo tumor model can fully explore this multi-faceted process. In 2019, Dong et al. addressed the difficulties of in vivo tumor screening through convergent analyses of mouse OT-I;Cas-9 expressing CD8 T cells an in vivo tumor infiltration screen of antigen-specific E0771-OVA murine triple-negative breast cancer and an in vitro degranulation screen, identifying Dxh37 as a target whose knockout, modulates NF-kB signaling to improve T cell cytotoxicity and activation while decreasing exhaustion . Recently, a genome-wide in vivo screening in adoptively transferred mouse CD8+ T cells identified that deficiency of Pdia3, Mgat5, Emp1 or Lag3 genes enhances the survival of mice bearing GBM tumors, both in syngeneic and OT-I T-cell receptor transgenic models. In addition, Pdia3-KO improved effector function in mouse T cells, and PDIA3-KO improved anti-tumor efficacy of anti-EGFRvIII chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T cells . Another in vivo CRISPR screen of adoptively transferred CD8 T cells into tumor models identified that deficiency of ribonuclease REGNASE-1 improved both the persistence and effector functions of tumor-infiltrating T cells, which enhanced cancer elimination in models of murine melanoma and leukemia. The study also found that CRISPR targeting of PTPN2 and SOCS1 can have an additive effect to REGNASE-1-deficiency to improve CD8 T cell function .

Another major limitation of T cell-based immunotherapies is the T cell dysfunction and cell fate decisions that reduce cytotoxic functions. One study investigated the exhausted T cell fate with an in vivo screen to investigate transcription factors that promote effecter-vs-exhaustion polarization in CD8 T cells using a chronic infection model (LCMV Clone 13) to identify Fli1 as a novel negative regulator of effector function . Another study used in vivo screens to identify metabolic regulatory genes that polarized effector vs memory precursor cell fate decisions of CD8 T cells in an acute LCMV-arm infection model to identify Slc7a1 and Slc38a2 amino acid transporters decrease memory polarization by regulating mTORC1 signaling .

NK cells have received an increasing amount of attention due to their immunotherapeutic potential with CAR reprogramming with more than 50 ongoing CAR NK-based clinical trials, as of May 2023 . Similar to T cells that were the pioneering cell population for CAR therapy, NK cells have strong cytotoxic functions against cancer cells, including the secretion of granzymes, perforin, interferons, and tumor necrosis factor alpha . In addition to their direct role in tumor lysis, NK cells can also exert indirect cancer-control functions through their receptor-ligand interactions with dendritic cells , monocytes , T cells  and B cells . NK cells can be activated through innate immune receptors that detect environmental cues of cancer and infection, native NK receptors that can detect loss MHC class I receptors, common in many cancers, and Fc-receptors that participate in the humoral response through the antibody-dependent cell cytotoxicity . A recent preprint by Peng et al. addresses these critical NK limitations through the use a convergent analysis of single-cell transcriptomics and in vivo CRISPR screens that each explored NK cells in the tumor microenvironment and spleens across multiple in vivo samples . The combined analysis identified that CALHM2 gene deficiency in mouse and human NK cells improved tumor infiltration and function .

Challenges of in vivo tumor infiltration screening

While any in vivo CRISPR screen can be challenging, those performed in circulating immune cells, such as NK and T cells, present even more difficulties, as only a fraction of the injected cells will reach their target tissue, from which the clones they will be quantified. While this feature should increase selective pressure in tumor infiltration screens, it ironically creates several technical issues in the process, especially as a common genome-wide gRNA library can contain ~80,000 members, and the recommended 500-fold ratio of cells/gRNA ratio  would require 4e7 cells. A challenge will be to normalize samples as inconsistent missing information will make the samples hard to compare, especially given that gRNA distribution in each sample will not necessarily have a reliable background signature that is common amongst replicates, such as homeostatic transcriptional expression profiles found in RNA-seq analysis data .

Advancements of CRISPR technology in T cell-based therapy

In addition to promoting the discovery of new targets by enabling large-scale genetic screening, CRISPR technology has opened up new avenues for treating disease. One of the most promising areas of application for CRISPR is in the field of T cell-based therapy . T cell therapies have shown great promise in the treatment of various diseases, particularly cancer. There are currently 29 clinical trials registered utilizing CRISPR engineered T cells to treat cancer (Table 1). Despite these advances, creating engineered T cells that can effectively target disease-specific markers, and persist in vivo with minimal side effects has been a major challenge . CRISPR technology has revolutionized the field by enabling precise genome engineering of T cells, leading to significant advances in adaptive T cell therapy . In recent years, CRISPR has been used for many important aspects of T-cell-based therapy, including generating homogeneous T cell products through multifunctional gene delivery systems, improving T cell function through targeted gene editing, and producing universal off-the-shelf T-cell products (Figure 2).

Developing multifunctional gene editing systems

T cells with engineered T cell receptor (TCR)  or CAR  are emerging genetically engineered T-cell products in treating various diseases, especially cancer . To generate those cells, autologous or allogenic T cells are engineered with a TCR or CAR to recognize tumor specific antigen or tumor associated antigen, and to active T cells for targeted lysis . Traditionally, viral vectors such as lentivirus and retrovirus have been used to generate genetically engineered T-cell product . However, both lentivirus and retrovirus have limitations regarding their variable integration copy numbers, as well as the random genome sites at which they integrate. This can cause variability in transgene expression and cell phenotypes due to insertional mutagenesis . With the advent of CRISPR technology, it is now possible to produce homogeneous T-cell products with various platforms, offering a more effective and efficient way to translate these products for clinical practices  (Figure 3).

HDR templates delivered by viral vectors

Homology-directed repair (HDR) of double strand DNA (dsDNA) breaks introduced by CRISPR/Cas proteins has been utilized to introduce transgene knockout or knock-in at a defined genomic locus . Earlier approaches employ Cas9 RNP electroporation and AAV-HDR template introduction, to generate TCR-T cells with tumor antigen specific TCR that was stably integrated into TRAC locus . Adeno-associated virus (AAV) has been widely used as a vector to deliver donor DNA templates for HDR-mediated knock-in in T cells . By leveraging the multiplexed gene editing capability of Cas12a to process concatenated crRNA arrays, we achieved targeted gene knockout and knock-in simultaneously by electroporating Cas12a mRNA followed by AAV delivery of donor templates. This a system allows for efficient targeting of both HDR-mediated dual-CAR knock-in and immune-checkpoint gene knockout (KIKO) in primary human T cells . Our study demonstrated that CAR-T cells generated using the AAV-Cas12a platform, specifically the KIKO CAR-T cells, possess many advantageous features, including enhanced cancer cell killing ability, elevated effector function, and resistance to exhaustion. These findings represent an advancement in the creation of more complex genetically engineered T cells, as the AAV-Cas12a platform enables efficient generation of advanced CAR-T cells through modular processing.

We have built upon the KIKO system and developed a novel approach called CLASH that allows for high-throughput knock-in and knockout simultaneously. In this system, we electroporated human T cells with Cpf1 mRNA, followed by infection with pooled AAV library to mediate simultaneous gene knockout and CAR knock-in into TRAC locus using massive parallel HDR . Our research team utilized this technology to perform time-coursed CLASH experiments in blood cancer and solid tumor models using primary human T cells, specifically CD3, CD8, and CD4 T cells. This enabled the pooled generation and unbiased selection of favorable CAR-T variants, representing a significant advancement in the field of CAR-T cell therapy.

HDR templates delivered by non-viral vectors

Despite the high efficiency achieved by these systems with AAV, the potential to generate genome integration while combined with CRISPR engineering, the cargo size limit (~ 4.8kb) restrict their broad application towards clinical practices . To tackle this, many delivery systems, which involve either pseudoviral or nonviral components, have been developed recently. A recent paper applied an integrase-deficient lentivirus (pseudovirus) encoding a payload flanked by homology arms and ‘cut sites’ to insert payload upstream and in-frame of an endogenous essential gene, followed by the delivery of a CRISPR-associated ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complex via electroporation . With this system, large payloads such as CRISPR activation components could be stably expressed in primary human T cells.

The complicated and expensive manufacturing process limits the broad application of viral vectors to research and clinical usage. CRISPR technology brought the promise of stable genome editing with nonviral vectors. Currently, nonviral delivery systems using single strand DNA (ssDNA) , double strand DNA (dsDNA)  or plasmid DNA  have been actively studied in the field. As many cells have developed a defense mechanism to detect intracellular pathogens by binding to nuclear acids, genome editing with DNA in immune cells faces a significant obstacle due to its associated toxicity . This challenge is particularly relevant for dsDNA, which activates a wide range of innate immune signals and results in significant cell death . As single-stranded oligodeoxynucleotides (ssODNs), which do not activate immune responses when delivered intracellularly , HDR templates using ssODNs were reported to cause no notable T cell toxicity . However, this is approach typically is more suitable for small insertions due to the limitation of synthesizing long ssDNA.

Contrary to studies that showed that large linear dsDNA templates are toxic at high concentrations , several recent studies showed that co-electroporation of human primary T cells with Cas9 RNP complexes and long (>1 kb) linear dsDNA templates reduced the toxicity associated with the dsDNA template. Specifically, it was reported that higher viability of edited T cells could be achieved by electroporating with dsDNA carrying 800bp homology arms . Roth et. al applied this method in patient derived T cells to correct recessive loss-of-function IL2a mutations, which causes monogenic autoimmune disease. Moreover, they replaced the endogenous T cell receptor (TCR) locus with a new TCR that is reactive to a tumor specific antigen, NY-ESO-1. This approach was also used to perform a pooled knock-in screening of synthetic receptors to enhance fitness and therapeutic effect of TCR-T cells in solid tumor models . Despite the relative low throughput of the screen with only 36 synthetic candidates in the library, and the requirement of prior knowledge on the library design, this approach offers a promising path for gain-of-functional screening on synthetic receptors that do not naturally exist. As production and purification of linear dsDNA in large quantity is required for this approach using CRISPR mediated HDR knock-in, its versatility and scalability could be limited for cellular therapies.

To overcome this limitation, several studies have also demonstrated the feasibility of using plasmid DNA as HDR template while performing CRISPR mediated gene editing . Especially, Oh et al. developed a method using nano plasmid based donor templates and Cas9 RNP for efficient nonviral gene editing in human primary T cells. They showed HDR templates as large as 5.5kb delivered by nano plasmid achieved about 20% knock-in efficiency . This approach using plasmid DNA, which are amenable to large-scale and good manufacturing practices, provides an alternative way to manufacture genetic engineered cellular products for clinic usage.

Novel delivery approaches of CRISPR-Cas systems

In above mentioned approaches, CRISPR editing components were delivered either by viral transduction or electroporation. However, the use of viral transduction and electroporation for CRISPR-mediated gene editing can be problematic due to undesired mutagenesis and disturbances to the cell transcriptome and phenotype . To improve this, many groups are actively developing novel delivery methods to efficiently perform CRISPR mediated gene editing while avoiding genotoxicity.

Nanoparticles have been readily used as a vehicle to deliver a variety of cargos into cells, while they have been only recently adopted for gene editing in T cells . Compared with delivery approaches like viral transduction and electroporation, nanoparticles are less toxic and can be highly customizable by selective surface modification. This flexibility is critical for gene editing in vivo, where controlled cargo release or editing on targeted cell types is desired for efficient editing with minimal side effect. Previously, modified nanoparticles were shown to selectively release IL-15 super agonist complex in response to T cell activation, and demonstrated selectively anti-tumor T cell expansion in vivo . In an earlier study, Smith et. al demonstrated that nanoparticles could be modified to reprogram circulating T cells in vivo with CAR specific for cancer cells. Moreover, those CAR-T cells generated in vivo were as potent as those generated ex vivo . Similar delivery approach has been adopted to treat other disease. In another study, CD5 targeting nanoparticles carrying mRNA encoding CAR targeting cardiac fibroblasts efficiently reprogrammed circulating T cells against cardiac fibrosis . These studies show that generating CAR-T cells in vivo using nanoparticles represents a promising alternative to the expensive and complicated ex vivo manufacturing process currently used in clinics. By bypassing the need for ex vivo manipulation, this approach offers a more straightforward and cost-effective way to produce CAR-T cells. Additionally, it also has the potential to transform personalized products into readily available, off-the-shelf therapies, thus providing a new avenue for treating a wide range of diseases.

Nanoparticles have also been used to deliver CRISPR components to T cells ex vivo , and tumor microenvironment in vivo . Although nanoparticles offer many above-mentioned advantages over other methods, their high retention level in liver needs to be carefully evaluated when considered for in vivo applications . This challenge has motived the development of nanoparticles targeting specific organs. In a recent study , organ-tropism of lipid nanoparticles (LNP) could be precisely controlled by modifying the LNP formulations. Lung, spleen and liver specific LNPs carrying Cas9 mRNA and sgRNA or Cas9 RNP achieved gene editing in respective organs. Although relatively low editing efficiency were achieved in immune cells comparing with other cell types like hepatocytes and endothelial cells, this approach demonstrates great potential to treat many diseases in a highly targeted way in vivo.

Additional advances have been made in the methods of delivering CRISPR components into primary immune cells using cell penetrating peptides (CPPs) . In one study, Zhang et. al developed a system called PAGE, which used cell penetrating peptides (CPP) to generate Cas9-CPP or Cas12a-CPP fusion proteins. Efficient editing was achieved in human T cells using Cas9-CPP or Cas12a-CPP with an assistant CPP and guide RNAs delivered through a viral vector. However, efficient gene editing was only evident when guide RNA was delivered through Cas12a-CPP-RNP, but not Cas9-CPP-RNP, using nonviral RNP delivery . The requirement of Cas protein engineering and incompatibility with Cas9 RNP might limit the broad application of this study. Another study utilized commercially available Cas9 proteins along with a CCP chimera, which enabled RNP delivery for CRISPR engineering (PERC) . Using this system, they showed high efficiency in targeted CAR knock-in when delivering HDR template via AAV. Moreover, sequential editing with PERC achieved comparable multiplexed editing efficiency as electroporation, while induced remarkably lower level of unattended gene translation, which is typically associated with simultaneous multiplexed gene editing . However, due to the requirement of assistant peptides that only works in-trans with Cas9/Cas12a proteins, these two systems are currently only suitable for in vitro or ex-vivo genome editing, but not in vivo editing. Despite this, these independent studies demonstrated the feasibility to efficiently edit human primary T cells with the aid of CPPs, bypassing the need of viral transduction, electroporation, or nanoparticle formulation.

T cell editing with novel CRISPR editors

An ideal genome editing tool for T-cell-based immunotherapies should allow for editing at multiple genomic sites without introducing unintended mutations. Nevertheless, majority of methods mentioned above for generating therapeutic T cells introduce DNA double-stranded breaks (DSBs), leading to undesired mutagenesis such as complex genomic rearrangement and large gene fragment deletions . The frequency such mutagenesis notably higher if multiple DSBs are induced simultaneously , and this is observed in CRISPR engineered CAR-T cells after infusing into patients . Base editing  and prime editing  are emerging CRISPR genome editing tools that are capable of introducing point mutations, deletions and small insertions without generating DSBs. Thus, these newly developed tools offer a safer avenue to product engineered T cell product for clinical use.

Application of prime editing in human primary T cells seems to be challenging, partly because of the suboptimal design of prime editing gRNA (pegRNA) and subsequently low editing efficiency . To facilitate rapid design of pegRNAs, we have developed a user-friendly webtool named pegFinder (http://pegfinder.sidichenlab.org/), which provides a table of candidate pegRNAs by taking reference and edited DNA sequences as input . Nevertheless, for prime editing to be effectively utilized in therapeutic applications, future efforts are needed to develop prime editors with high efficiency but smaller size to facilitate efficient delivery into primary T cells.

Alternatively, several groups are currently making use of base editing for therapeutic purposes. Webber et al. first demonstrated the feasibility of adopting base editing for multiplexed gene knockout in human primary T cells, without detectable translation associated with DSBs . In a recent study, cytosine base editor (CBE) was adopted for multiplexed CAR-T engineering to treat T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL). To produce allogenic CAR-T cells, Diorio et al. edited human primary T cells using electroporation of mRNA encoding CBE and gRNA simultaneously targeting B2M, TRAC, PDCD1 and CD52, followed by lentiviral transduction to deliver anti-CD7 CAR transgene . Nonetheless, these studies rely on lentivirus to introduce the CAR transgene, which poses certain drawbacks, as mentioned above, including random insertional mutagenesis. To address this, Glaser et al. developed a system which combined the Cas12a for CAR knock-in and Cas9 base editor for multiplexed gene knockout. This system enabled generating allogenic CAR-T products using nonviral delivery of all components with minimal translocation . However, due to the relative low efficiency to introduce large insert such as CAR knock-in, base editing is more ideal to be used for correcting pathogenic point mutations in humans. As have shown by another group recently, human severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) in CD3D could be corrected efficiently using adenine base editing in human hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs) . This study demonstrates the great potential of base editing in HSPCs for the treatment of SCID diseases.

Generating T cells with enhanced functionality

While T cell-based adoptive cell transfer (ACT) of has achieved promising advances in treating certain cancers, its efficacy seen in solid tumor is unsatisfactory, due to challenges such as T cell exhaustion, poor persistence, and suppressive tumor microenvironment . To tackle these challenges, CRISPR editing has been broadly applied into ACT with the goal of generating therapeutic T cells with enhanced efficacy.

A number of studies have concentrated on utilizing gene editing to disrupt suppressive molecules that contribute to T cell exhaustion, poor proliferation, diminished effector function, or insufficient differentiation into central memory T cell (Tcm). Many groups showed that PD-1 / PDCD1 KO CAR-T demonstrated somewhat improved anti-tumor efficacy comparing with the unedited ones . We were among the first to explore this approach by developing a KIKO system that allows for simultaneous knock-in of CAR-T cells and knockout of PD-1 / PDCD1 . Recent studies identified a suppressive role of PRDM1 in CAR-T function . PRDM1 mutant CAR-T cells have increased proliferation, enhanced Tcm differentiation, resulting in remarkably improved efficacy in vivo across multiple cancer models . Similarly, CTLA-4 KO cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs)  and LAG-3 disrupted CAR-T cells  showed increased anti-tumor activity. Besides these, studies have used CRISPR to disrupt TGF-β receptor II (TGFBR2) to prevent exhaustion of CAR-T cells , target adenosine A2A receptor to overcome immunosuppression of adenosine in the tumor microenvironment , knockout Mediator kinase module (MED12)  or diacylglycerol kinase  to enhance effector function of CAR-T and TCR-T cells.

Generating universal therapeutic T cells

Currently, standard CAR-T therapies involves customized manufacturing using autologous T cells, which is time-consuming and expensive. Administration of allogeneic off-the-shelf product from healthy donor has the potential to overcome this critical hurdle. However, allogeneic T cell transplantation can induce graft-versus-host disease (GvHD) due to the activation of allogeneic T cells by highly polymorphic HLA-I molecules present in humans . To address this issue, various groups are currently adopting CRISPR technology to generate universal CAR-T cells using allogeneic T cells. Typically, to prevent graft-versus-host disease (GvHD) and host rejection of the graft, TRAC and/or TRBC, which encode the constant chain of TCRa or TCRb respectively, as well as B2M, which is essential for HLA-I expression, and class II major histocompatibility complex trans-activator (CIITA) for HLA-II expression are disrupted . It was also tested that deletion of CD52 on CAR-T cells may support anti-CD52 mediated depletion of autologous T cells in patients .

However, simply disrupting TCR and/or HLA-I on allogeneic CAR-T cells might not yield optimal outcomes. It has been shown that the number of allogenic CAR-T cells with TRAC and B2M deletion drops remarkably about two weeks after infusion into patients with relapsed/refractory lymphoma . This might be explained by another study showed that while CAR-T cells with TCR deletion showed reduced alloreactivity, they also had impaired persistence and efficacy in vivo . This study indicates that the endogenous TCR is essential for the persistence of anti-tumor response, but it comes with the cost of alloreactivity. Additionally, HLA-I deficient cells are susceptible to killing mediated by natural killer (NK) cell through the missing-self response . Allogenic CAR-T cells genetically edited with HLA-I deficiency can also be lysed by host NK cells after transplantation. This NK cell-mediated lysis can be inhibited through the interactions with minimally polymorphic HLA-E antigen . Moreover, CAR-T cells with triple knockout of TCR, HLA-I, and HLA-II have been demonstrated to be resistant to allogeneic NK cell mediated rejection when HLA-E is overexpressed . These studies highlight the potential of CRISPR technology to generate universal allogeneic CAR-T cells for cancer treatment, while emphasizing the importance of balancing immune tolerance with the persistence and efficacy of the CAR-T cells.

Translation into clinic

Many of above-mentioned engineering approaches have readily been translated into clinical practices (Table 1), especially novel approaches involving non-viral precise genome engineering . The feasibility of infusing nonviral CRISPR edited T cell into human was first shown by electroporating T cells with plasmids expressing Cas9 and PD-1 targeting sgRNAs . However, low response rate was observed in patient with refractory non-small-cell lung cancer due to the lack of antigen specificity of edited T cells. In another study, Zhang et. al demonstrated that by electroporating dsDNA template and Cas9 RNP, T cells with CAR integrated into PD-1 locus outperformed PD-1 knockout CAR-T cells generated by lentiviral transduction . More importantly, they have demonstrated that those non-viral, PD1-integrated CAR-T cells could efficiently eliminate tumor cells in patients with relapsed/refractory aggressive B cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma (r/r B-NHL), with 7 out of 8 patients showed complete remission without serious toxicity .

Moreover, autologous T cells were genetically engineered to express TCRs that are reactive to patient specific neoantigens using nonviral CRISPR genome editing . In one study, to capture neoantigen reactive T cells, Foy et. al began by generating personalized libraries of neoantigen peptides presented by HLA class I alleles of individual patients. They then sequenced TRAC and TRBC genes from those captured T cells and cloned them into vector plasmids. These HDR plasmids were then used together with Cas9 RNP to knockout the endogenous TCRβ chain and insert the transgenic TRAC and TRBC genes into the endogenous TRAC locus of CD8 and CD4 T cells. Among the enrolled 16 patients in this clinic trial, eleven patients had disease progression and five patients had stable disease . Although minimal clinic benefits were achieved, likely due to the low dose and poor in vivo expansion of infused T cells, this clinical trial demonstrated the ability to use CRISPR-based, non-viral knockout and knock-in editing to genetically modify T cells for ACT therapy in humans.

Conclusion and future direction

Representative existing challenges in CRISPR screening for future optimization

CRISPR screen approaches have demonstrated a great potential to identify genetic factors underlying critical biological processes; However, due to the molecular complexities of the CRISPR/Cas system and biological variability, there are several layers of variation that accumulate in a pooled CRISPR screen that can drastically impact the efficacy of this biotechnological breakthrough. Therefore, it is important to explore new methods and parameters to improve screen performance.

One of the greatest limitations to many studies lies in the screen analysis, which can be drastically hindered to the high variation that can be present in CRISPR screen data. While some statistical analysis issues can be addressed by increasing the selective pressure or increasing the number of cells and replicates, these solutions can be challenging or infeasible for in vivo screens. For this reason, it is very common to see high-profile studies opt to use a simple foldchange to determine hits rather than use any of the CRISPR analysis strategies available . Unfortunately, this simple method will fall short when analyzing any screen design more complicated than screen-vs-control. Another major bottleneck with screens in cancer-immunology is the high clone dropout of in vivo CRISPR screens. This problem can lead to substantial missing data in gRNA count-matrices, are difficult/impossible to filter when zeros are mixed between replicates within the guides of each gene, as it is unclear if each zero-count is due to true clonal depletion in the screen or a stochastic exclusion of the clone from the examined tissue. Furthermore, it is difficult to determine the best way to address the zero-counts when they become prevalent within the data.

Therefore, future directions in CRISPR screen research could aim to improve methods to analyze in vivo screen data and develop ways to address missing data. One potential strategy could be to fill in the missing data using non-missing values or a supplemental dataset via imputation. Another approach could be to use statistical models with additional controls to better investigate biological questions. For example, a screen for tumor infiltration by T cells could compare the gRNA distribution of tumor infiltrates from that of peripheral blood T cells. The combination of new screen methods and analysis tools could greatly improve the efficacy of CRISPR screening, especially in the in vivo settings, for which there are many cancer-immunology questions yet to explore.

New screening strategies to address old questions

The molecular mechanisms of the CRISPR/Cas system are well suited for modifications, such as the well-known use of activation, inhibition, and base-editing fusion domains to the Cas endonuclease. In addition, different Cas-species can have unique attributes that enable new research directions. For example, Cas12a/Cpf1 can effectively use multiple guide sequences in the same cell, allowing for combinatorial screens. These can be used to identify pairs of co-mutational drivers of metastasis  or immune escape . The use of a double-knockout screen also presents opportunities to explore epistatic interactions between genes, including synergistic and antagonistic interactions . Such studies can also be aided by the use of mice with conditional/constitutive Cpf1 expression . Furthermore, Cpf1 double-knockout strategies can be combined with floxed gRNAs and an inducible-CRE system to enable sequential genetic editing . Therefore, future screens with these technologies can help to unveil new knowledge of immune circuitry and functional relationships between signaling pathways, even across different timepoints. Another CRISPR platform that has great potential is prime-editing . The accessibility of this technology has been greatly enhanced by the development of new efficiency prediction strategies for prime-editing guides , and user-friendly guide design software . Although prime-editing could eventually be used for screen approaches, the very low efficiency of this method is a major obstacle to improve with future research.

Recently, several studies have combined CRISPR/Cas screening with other high-throughput modalities, especially single-cell approaches to be used as screen readouts. The most popular of these methods are the Perturb-seq  and CROP-seq  platforms that enable single-cell transcriptomic readout for a CRISPR screen and can potentially reveal contextual relevance to mutant clones . In addition, the transcriptomics-based readout can be multiplexed, in which several cellular parameters can be simultaneously assessed for gRNA enrichment. For example, a T cell screen could be performed that identifies genetic factors that contribute to proliferation, effector function, and exhaustion in parallel. Furthermore, the perturb-seq platform can be used as a pseudo-validation step for a genome-wide screen. In this context, the top hits of a primary screen could be used to create a sub-library of gRNAs for a secondary screen using Perturb-seq. Future studies could benefit by implementing a multi-modal strategy between CRISPR screens and other single-cell approaches, such as combining single-cell ATAC-seq with a CRISPR screen for causal regulatory networks in cells . Alternatively, CRISPR screening methods could be combined with image-based single-cell transcriptional readout methods, such as MERFISH , which uses a series of sequential oligo-probe images with immunofluorescence microscopy to decode the identity of different RNA species in a 2D array. Using MERFISH as a readout, a CRISPR screen could, in theory, be used to determine which genes influence different types of intercellular interactions in situ. Together, these multimodal methods expand CRISPR screen capabilities with high-throughput readout parameters that can address a broad new range of questions in cancer immunology.

Future directions in T cell engineering with CRISPR

CRISPR technology holds immense potential for transforming T-cell-based therapies, especially in the development of innovative gene editing systems. These advancements have made it possible to genetically engineer T cells for a range of therapeutic purposes, particularly in cancer immunotherapy. As a result, genetically edited T-cell products are being tested in numerous clinical trials. However, further efforts are necessary to develop T-cell products with enhanced efficacy and safety in a more cost-effective manner.

CRISPR technology has primarily been employed to introduce LOF mutations in TCR-T or CAR-T cells for therapeutic purposes. Although CRISPRa has been widely used for GOF mutations in preclinical studies , the requirement of long-term presence of CRISPR components raises safety concerns, making it less ideal for clinical practices. However, base editing  and prime editing  hold significant potential for introducing GOF mutations without causing DSBs, offering a safer approach to generating therapeutic T-cell products with enhanced functionality. To fully harness the potential of GOF mutations, an important direction of future work should aim at identifying targets that can significantly boost T-cell functionality. By gaining additional knowledge into GOF mutations, we can develop safer and more effective T-cell-based therapies with improved clinical outcomes.

One of the significant obstacles to achieving effective anti-tumor efficacy of genome-edited T cells is their poor persistence in vivo. Although several factors, such as exhaustion and hostile tumor microenvironment, could contribute to this issue, it is essential to consider the activation of endogenous immunity against CRISPR Cas peptides presented by the edited T cells, given that CRISPR-Cas systems are derived from pathogenic bacteria . It was reported that Cas9 is immunogenic, and that both cellular and humoral immunity pre-exist in majority of health human . This immune response can result in the clearance of the genome-edited T cells from the body, limiting their therapeutic effectiveness. Cas9 protein with the immunodominant epitopes eliminated but intact gene editing function should be considered for future genome editing therapies . Therefore, developing strategies to mitigate the immune response against CRISPR-Cas systems may improve the persistence and therapeutic efficacy of genome-edited T cells for cancer immunotherapy.

Currently, majority of studies on T-cell-based therapies rely on genetically editing T cells ex vivo and then transferring them to patients, which is a costly and time-consuming process. To address this, future research efforts could focus on developing novel delivery systems that enable the in vivo delivery of CRISPR components to specific T cell populations. Nanoparticles are emerging as a promising approach for in vivo gene delivery, as they can be engineered to accommodate a wide range of cargo and modified for organ-  or cell-specific targeting . When used to deliver CRISPR components for T-cell editing in vivo, nanoparticles offer the advantage of a relatively shorter expression period of active Cas proteins compared to AAVs, which are commonly used for gene delivery in humans. This feature enhances the safety of in vivo gene delivery, making it a more attractive option for developing gene therapies that require precise control of gene editing. Nevertheless, future efforts are necessary to overcome the technical challenge to effectively delivery nanoparticles into T cells, which are typically nonphagocytic . With continued advancements in delivery technologies, the potential of the customized “living drugs” such as off-the-shelf products, can be fully realized, thereby improving the accessibility and cost-effectiveness of T-cell-based therapies.
