# Mirvetuximab Soravtansine: Mechanism of Action, Clinical and Translational Science.

## Abstract

Mirvetuximab soravtansine (MIRV) is an antibody-drug conjugate (ADC) composed of the DM4 payload conjugated to a folate receptor α (FRα)-targeting antibody via the cleavable sulfo-SPDB linker. MIRV targets and binds to FRα with high affinity and specificity, releasing the DM4 payload intracellularly following MIRV-FRα complex internalization and degradation. DM4 and its metabolite S-methyl-DM4 suppress microtubule dynamic instability, which triggers cell cycle arrest and apoptosis. Selective FRα-overexpression in ≥ 90% of epithelial ovarian tumor cells and its ability to internalize large molecules make it a highly attractive ADC target for epithelial ovarian cancers (including primary peritoneal and fallopian tube cancers). Although up to 80% of patients initially respond to platinum-based therapies, the majority of tumors will recur and become platinum-resistant. Unfortunately, platinum-resistant ovarian cancer (PROC) carries a poor prognosis with an overall survival of 12-14 months from the time of platinum-resistance, and prior to MIRV approval in 2022, little had changed in treatment options for decades. The MIRV Phase 3 registrational trial (MIRASOL) showed superiority of MIRV vs. chemotherapy (paclitaxel, pegylated liposomal doxorubicin, or topotecan) in patients with high (≥ 75%) FRα-expression PROC, showing an objective response rate of 42% versus 16%, a median progression-free survival of 5.6 versus 4.0 months, and an overall survival of 16.5 versus 12.8 months. Here, we briefly review MIRV mechanism of action, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and key clinical efficacy and safety data.

Introduction

Epithelial ovarian cancer (including primary peritoneal and fallopian tube cancer) is among the deadliest gynecological malignancies. In 2021, an estimated 238,000 women were living with ovarian cancer in the US, but the 5‐year survival rate was only 50.9% between 2014 and 2020. Epithelial ovarian cancers can be divided into several histologic subtypes. High grade‐serous ovarian cancer (HGSOC) is most common, accounting for 70%–75% of all ovarian cancers. Other less common subtypes include low grade‐serous ovarian cancer, endometrioid carcinomas, clear‐cell carcinomas, and mucinous carcinomas.

Little has changed over the past decades in ovarian cancer treatment, with standard management including cytoreductive surgery and platinum‐based chemotherapy. Platinum agents cause DNA damage in tumors, the majority of which have impaired DNA repair mechanisms. As a result, accumulating DNA double‐strand breaks in tumor cells impair replication and lead to apoptosis. Although up to 80% of patients initially respond to platinum‐based therapies, the majority will develop recurrent disease. Unfortunately, most recurrent tumors are or become platinum‐resistant, currently defined as disease progression ≤ 6 months after platinum‐based therapy completion. Before 2022, platinum‐resistant ovarian cancer (PROC) treatment recommendations included nonplatinum chemotherapies (e.g., paclitaxel, topotecan, pegylated liposomal doxorubicin [PLD]) administered with or without the anti‐angiogenic agent bevacizumab. Unfortunately, PROC carries an extremely poor prognosis with an overall survival (OS) of 12–14 months from the time of disease platinum‐resistance.

FDA‐ and EMA‐approval of mirvetuximab soravtansine‐gynx (MIRV), a folate receptor α (FRα)‐targeted antibody and microtubule inhibitor conjugate, introduced a novel treatment option for some patients with PROC. Here we review the mechanism of action, pharmacokinetics (PK), pharmacodynamics (PD), and key clinical trial data of this antibody‐drug conjugate (ADC).

Drug Regulatory Approval

MIRV is an FRα‐targeted ADC approved in the US for the treatment of adults with FRα + PROC who have received 1–3 prior systemic treatment courses. Based on the initial registrational Phase 2 SORAYA trial, MIRV was approved for commercial use by the US Food and Drug Administration (US FDA) in November 2022 via the accelerated approval process. Following confirmatory results of the Phase 3 MIRASOL trial, the US FDA granted full approval in March 2024. MIRV has also received marketing authorization by the European Medicines Agency (EMA; November 2024) and Swiss Agency for Therapeutic Products (Swissmedic; March 2025) for the treatment of adults with FRα+, platinum‐resistant HGSOC who have received 1–3 prior systemic treatment regimens. Of note, FRα expression should be assessed via a companion diagnostic assay (CDx). MIRV approvals for commercial use in some Asian countries have been filed.

Mechanism of Action

Folate is necessary for DNA synthesis, cell proliferation, and cellular metabolism, with its uptake into cells mediated partially by FRα. Though a definitive role of FRα in tumor growth and/or survival has not yet been demonstrated, its selective overexpression on epithelial tumor cells and ability to internalize large molecules make it highly attractive as an ADC target. FRα was identified as a potential ADC target for ovarian cancer with expression in ≥ 90% of epithelial ovarian tumors and minimal expression on normal cells. Additionally, higher levels of FRα expression in ovarian tumor cells may be predictive of first‐line chemotherapy failure.

MIRV is an ADC consisting of an FRα‐targeted IgG1 monoclonal antibody M9346A (mirvetuximab) attached to the microtubule inhibitor, DM4 (N2′‐deacetyl‐N2′‐(4‐mercapto‐4‐methyl‐1‐oxopentyl)‐maytansine), via the cleavable Sulfo‐SPDB linker (N‐succinimidyl 4‐(2‐pyridyldithio)‐2‐sulfobutanoate linker; Figure 1). The mean drug‐to‐antibody ratio (DAR) is 3.4. The mirvetuximab antibody was chosen for MIRV because of its ability to optimally carry and deliver the maytansinoid DM4 payload to FRα‐expressing tumor cells. The antibody also has equivalent binding to human and cynomolgus monkey FRα, which enabled preclinical toxicokinetic and toxicology studies. The maytansinoid DM4 payload is a potent anti‐mitotic agent due to its anti‐tubulin activity.

Upon binding to FRα, MIRV undergoes receptor‐mediated internalization, and vesicular trafficking delivers it to lysosomes where degradation and release of thiol‐containing DM4 from the targeting antibody occur. Released DM4 can then undergo methylation to form S‐methyl‐DM4. Both DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 suppress microtubule dynamics, leading to cell cycle arrest followed by apoptosis (Figure 1). The high expression of FRα allows MIRV to effectively target tumor cells. As a result, MIRV monotherapy is indicated for the treatment of PROC with moderate‐to‐strong FRα staining in ≥ 75% of viable tumor cells. Of note, both DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 are hydrophobic, of low molecular weight, and can pass through the cell membrane. S‐methyl‐DM4 has a neutral charge, is stable, and its added methyl group enhances hydrophobicity, all of which increase cell membrane permeability. Therefore, DM4 payloads have a bystander effect where cytotoxic DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 diffuse across cell membranes, resulting in death of adjacent non‐targeted cells. The bystander effect has been shown to occur with MIRV exposure and to be beneficial for treating tumors with heterogeneous FRα expression in preclinical models. In a preclinical in vitro model, the unconjugated mirvetuximab antibody (M9346A) exhibited some antibody‐dependent cell‐mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) with no detectable complement‐dependent cytotoxicity or anti‐proliferative activity . The contribution of ADCC activity to MIRV clinical efficacy has not been specifically examined and remains unknown.

Pharmacokinetics/Pharmacodynamic Characteristics

MIRV pharmacokinetics have been characterized for doses between 0.16 and 8.7 mg/kg adjusted ideal body weight (AIBW), which encompasses the final recommended dose of 6 mg/kg AIBW administered once every 3 weeks (Q3W). Dosing by AIBW (equation provided in Table 1 footnote) was recommended over flat dosing or dosing by total body weight to reduce interpatient exposure variability associated with body composition differences and improve drug efficacy and tolerability, especially to avoid underdosing or overdosing patients who were overweight or obese (Figure 2A). PK parameters for patients receiving the approved monotherapy dose of 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W are shown in Table 1 and were similar to other ADCs. Consistent with the half‐life, no meaningful accumulation was observed following Q3W dosing.

The mirvetuximab antibody and DM4 payload undergo deconjugation prior to the free DM4 payload being metabolized. The unconjugated antibody is metabolized intracellularly via catabolic pathways into small peptides, and DM4 can undergo methylation in the cytoplasm to convert to S‐methyl‐DM4. Unconjugated DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 released into the circulation are metabolized hepatically via CYP3A4. The main circulating metabolites of MIRV in plasma were DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 (~0.4% and ~1.4% of MIRV AUC, respectively), with unconjugated DM4 and its metabolite S‐methyl‐DM4 nearly completely bound to plasma protein (> 99%).

Population PK modeling for MIRV revealed that AIBW was a significant covariate, with higher AIBW leading to faster clearance and larger distribution volumes. Plasma albumin concentration was also a significant covariate, with higher albumin concentrations leading to slower clearance. This correlation is consistent with other IgGs as both IgG and albumin are non‐competitively recycled by the neonatal receptor (FcRn). AIBW was also a covariate for DM4 clearance (higher AIBW led to faster clearance), and the liver enzyme aspartate aminotransferase (AST) was a minor covariate on DM4 metabolic rate (higher AST led to lower rate). Additionally, the transformation rate of DM4 to S‐methyl‐DM4 slightly decreased with increasing AST.

Population PK modeling was used to evaluate the influence of renal and hepatic impairment, age, and drug–drug interactions. MIRV CL was independent of renal or hepatic impairment, and MIRV, DM4, and S‐methyl‐DM4 exposures were similar in patients with and without mild‐to‐moderate renal impairment (creatinine clearance ≥ 30 and < 90 mL/min) and mild hepatic impairment (total bilirubin≤upper limit of normal [ULN] with AST>ULN or total bilirubin > 1–1.5 times ULN with any AST). MIRV, DM4, and S‐methyl‐DM4 exposures were also comparable between elderly (≥ 65 years) and younger patients (no recommended age‐based dose adjustment). In vitro evaluations indicated that unconjugated DM4 is a time‐dependent CYP3A4 inhibitor and that DM4 and S‐methyl‐DM4 are P‐glycoprotein (P‐gp) substrates. Based on population PK analyses, MIRV, DM4, and S‐methyl‐DM4 exposures were similar in patients with and without concomitant use of weak or moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors or P‐gp transport inhibitors. Therefore, dose adjustments were not recommended for patients with mild‐to‐moderate renal or mild hepatic impairment or for those also taking weak or moderate CYP3A4 inhibitors. However, concomitant use of MIRV with strong CYP3A4 inhibitors may increase unconjugated DM4 exposures, and patients should be closely monitored for adverse reactions when strong CYP3A inhibitors are co‐administered with MIRV.

Exposure‐Response Analyses

Exposure‐response analyses were performed utilizing pooled data from four clinical trials (first‐in‐human [FIH], FORWARD I, SORAYA, and MIRASOL; Table 2) and further supported the recommended 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W regimen. Significant relationships between efficacy endpoints and MIRV ADC exposure were demonstrated by exposure‐efficacy analyses for MIRASOL—higher ADC plasma trough concentration (C trough) and area under the concentration‐time curve over the 21‐day dosing interval (AUCtau) were associated with higher objective response rate (ORR; Figure 2B), longer progression‐free survival (PFS), and longer OS. Duration of response (DOR; time from first response to disease progression or death) did not show a statistically significant relationship with any examined MIRV exposure metric. However, patients within the highest quartile for MIRV maximum plasma concentration (C max) or C trough tended to experience the longest DOR (unpublished data). For ADC C trough relationships, covariates leading to lower efficacy included higher baseline tumor burden (PFS, OS; marginal difference), prior bevacizumab use (PFS, OS), higher Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group (ECOG) score (PFS), lower soluble FRα concentrations (PFS), no prior poly (ADP‐ribose) polymerase (PARP) therapy (OS), lower plasma albumin, and higher alkaline phosphatase concentrations (OS, marginal differences).

Exposure‐safety analysis of pooled data from the FIH, FORWARD I, SORAYA, and MIRASOL trials (Table 2) focused on ocular adverse events (AEs), peripheral neuropathy, pneumonitis, and serious AEs (SAEs; full safety profile discussed below). A strong relationship between MIRV ADC exposure (AUCtau) and the probability of experiencing a grade ≥ 2 ocular AE was demonstrated, with several covariates showing no significant clinical relevance (Figure 2B). Further, time to onset of grade ≥ 2 ocular AE decreased with increasing MIRV exposure. Higher MIRV ADC exposure (AUCtau) was associated with a higher probability of grade ≥ 2 peripheral neuropathy, with age as a covariate (Figure 2B). No relationship was identified between MIRV exposure and pneumonitis. S‐methyl‐DM4 exposure (particularly C max), but not MIRV ADC or DM4 exposure, was correlated with SAE occurrence (Figure 2B).

Dose Selection

The MIRV dose escalation phase in the FIH trial was completed in 2015, establishing 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W as both the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) and the recommended Phase 2 dose (RP2D). Dose expansion at 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W in the FIH trial supported the selection of this dose for the MIRV registrational trials, MIRASOL and SORAYA, initiated in 2019 and 2020, respectively (prior to Project Optimus launch in 2021). These registrational trials have demonstrated a significant clinical benefit of MIRV (see KEY CLINICAL TRIALS section). Exposure‐response analyses further support the selected dosing regimen, showing that higher exposures increase efficacy but also raise ocular toxicity. Conversely, lowering the dose while maintaining dosing frequency would mitigate toxicity but reduce efficacy. Therefore, 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W was chosen as the recommended monotherapy dose for MIRV based on the benefit–risk evaluation. A clinical study to further optimize dosing is ongoing as part of the FDA post‐marketing requirements (NCT06682988). This study compares the 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W regimen and a lower‐dose regimen given every 2 weeks (Q2W; similar average doses). Based on unpublished simulation results using the established PK model, the lower‐dose Q2W regimen would result in a lower C max and higher Ctrough compared with the 6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W regimen, potentially mitigating some AEs while maintaining efficacy.

Key Clinical Trials

Key MIRV monotherapy clinical trials are summarized in Table 2. A Phase 1 FIH dose‐escalation/expansion trial (NCT01609556) enrolled patients with advanced FRα + solid tumors. Based on safety, PK, and preliminary anti‐tumor activity data, 6.0 mg/kg AIBW Q3W was declared as both the MTD and the RP2D.

The Phase 3 FORWARD I randomized controlled trial (NCT02631876) compared MIRV versus investigator's choice chemotherapy (paclitaxel, topotecan, or PLD) in patients with advanced FRα + PROC. The FRα scoring method used in FORWARD I was later found to overestimate FRα expression, allowing for enrollment of patients with lower FRα‐expressing tumors and subsequently diluting MIRV treatment effects. As such, the trial failed to meet its primary endpoint of statistically longer PFS with MIRV versus chemotherapy in the intent‐to‐treat (ITT) population. However, it demonstrated an improved safety profile, numerically longer PFS (4.8 vs. 3.3 months) and OS (median: not‐ratable vs. 11.8 months), and numerically higher objective response rate (ORR; 24% vs. 10%) in patients with FRα‐high tumors (≥ 75% viable tumor cells with FRα staining) receiving MIRV. The safety profile was similar to that observed in the Phase 1 trial. Based on the FORWARD I findings and the high unmet need in PROC, further MIRV clinical trials focused on patients with FRα‐high PROC.

The registrational Phase 2 open‐label, single‐arm SORAYA study (NCT04296890) demonstrated MIRV monotherapy (6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W) antitumor activity and acceptable safety profiles in patients with FRα‐high PROC (≥ 75% viable tumor cells with ≥ 2+ FRα staining intensity; FDA‐approved scoring assay and method), who had received 1–3 prior lines of therapy including bevacizumab (N = 106 safety evaluable, 105 efficacy evaluable). The ORR was 32.4% (4.8% complete response, 27.6% partial response) with a median duration of response of 6.9 months. Median investigator‐assessed PFS was 4.3 months; median OS was 13.8 months. No new safety signals were identified in this population with mostly low‐grade, reversible gastrointestinal and ocular treatment‐related AEs reported. Findings from this trial were the basis for accelerated US FDA approval of this biomarker‐driven therapy.

The Phase 3 MIRASOL confirmatory open‐label, randomized controlled trial (NCT04209855) demonstrated superior efficacy and safety of MIRV monotherapy (6.0 mg/kg AIBW Q3W; N = 227) vs. chemotherapy (investigator's choice; paclitaxel, PLD, or topotecan; N = 226) in patients with FRα‐high PROC (≥ 75% viable tumor cells with ≥ 2+ FRα staining intensity). The study met its primary endpoint, showing significantly longer median PFS (5.62 vs. 3.98 months; p < 0.001) and OS (16.46 vs. 12.75 months; hazard ratio for death: 0.67, p = 0.005) in patients treated with MIRV vs. chemotherapy. Additionally, ORR was higher (42.3% vs. 15.9%; p < 0.001) and median OS was longer (16.46 vs. 12.75 months; hazard ratio for death: 0.67, p = 0.005) with MIRV. No new safety signals were identified, and MIRV was better tolerated in this study population than chemotherapy. Additionally, patient‐reported outcomes (European Organization for Research and Treatment of Cancer [EORTC] Quality of Life Questionnaire‐Ovarian Cancer [QLQ‐OV28] and ‐Core 30 [QLQ‐C30] Modules) showed maintenance of baseline health‐related quality of life in patients treated with MIRV, along with a lower symptom burden than chemotherapy. Following positive MIRASOL trial results, full FDA approval and EMA marketing authorizations were granted.

Lastly, the PICCOLO trial (NCT05041257) examined MIRV (6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W) safety and efficacy in patients with platinum‐sensitive ovarian cancer (PSOC) who had ≥ 2 prior lines of platinum‐based therapies (or documented platinum allergy). In this heavily pretreated population, MIRV had a high ORR (51.9%), a durable response (median DOR: 8.25 months), a median PFS of 6.93 months, and a median OS of 27.17 months. Consistent with studies in PROC, gastrointestinal, neurosensory, and resolvable ocular AEs were most common and overall manageable.

Summary of Clinical Efficacy and Safety

Mirvetuximab Soravtansine Efficacy

The ORR in patients with FRα‐high PROC receiving the clinically recommended MIRV dosing regimen (6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W) was 24.0%–42.3% across studies (RECIST 1.1 criteria; Phase 1–3 trials). Of note, ORR was higher in later trials (32.4%–42.3%; SORAYA, MIRASOL) which utilized the FDA‐approved FRα expression assay for tumor FRα scoring. Median PFS and OS ranged between 4.3–5.6 months and 13.8–16.5 months, respectively, in the later trials. The PICCOLO study specifically examined MIRV efficacy and safety in patients with recurrent FRα‐high PSOC who received MIRV as a third line or later therapy (or with platinum allergy). Efficacy was notable, with an ORR of 51.9%, a median PFS of 6.93 months, and a DOR of 8.25 months.

Mirvetuximab Soravtansine Safety

A pooled safety analysis across clinical trials in patients with PROC (FIH dose‐escalation/expansion, FORWARD I, SORAYA, and MIRASOL; N = 757) showed gastrointestinal (79% of patients), general/administration site (61%), ocular (59%), nervous system (55%), and metabolic/nutritional (42%) AEs were most common. More specific MedDRA classifications were reported in a later MIRV integrated safety summary, which reported blurred vision (all grade: 43%, grade ≥ 3: 5%), nausea (41%, 2%), diarrhea (39%, 3%), and fatigue (35%, 2%) as most common in patients with PROC (N = 682). Most treatment‐emergent AEs were grade 1–2 but grade ≥ 3 events occurred in 48% of patients, with keratopathy (5%), blurred vision (5%), cataract (4%), abdominal pain (4%), diarrhea (3%), and vomiting (3%) most common. Because grade ≥ 2 AEs led to MIRV dose delays, reductions, or discontinuations, US FDA labeling contains a boxed warning for ocular toxicity and warnings/precautions for pneumonitis and peripheral neuropathy. Of patients with FRα‐high PROC, 30% experienced ≥ 1 SAE and 11% experienced ≥ 1 treatment‐related SAE. The most common treatment‐related SAE was pneumonitis (4%; all others < 1%) and treatment was discontinued due to SAE in 7% of patients. Two deaths (< 1%) occurred across trials (respiratory failure [n = 1], neutropenic sepsis [n = 1]), both of which were deemed treatment‐related. 35 A similar safety profile was noted in patients with PSOC, with the most common treatment‐emergent AEs of blurred vision (63%), dry eye (37%), nausea (37%), keratopathy (33%), and diarrhea (30%).

Several AEs were more common in patients receiving MIRV than in those receiving chemotherapy, including blurred vision (all clinical trial patients: 40.8% vs. 2.4%), keratopathy (32.1% vs. 0%), peripheral neuropathy (21.6% vs. 14.5%), and pneumonitis (10% vs. < 1%). Despite participant prophylactic use of lubricating and corticosteroid eye drops, ≥ 1 ocular treatment‐emergent AE occurred in 59% of PROC patients (median onset: ~5–7 weeks), most commonly blurred vision (43%), keratopathy (29%), dry eye (27%), cataract (15%), photophobia (14%), decreased visual acuity (13%), and eye pain (10%). Most ocular AEs completely resolved and MIRV was very rarely discontinued due to ocular AE (1%). Corneal ulceration/perforation was not observed. Treatment‐emergent peripheral neuropathy of any grade was reported in 36% of PROC patients. Most cases had a maximum grade of 1 (59%) or 2 (34%), but only a minority of patients had AE resolution (partial: 12%, complete: 23%). Treatment‐emergent pneumonitis occurred in 10% of PROC patients, with most cases (87%) considered low grade. However, 26% of patients with a TEAE of pneumonitis discontinued treatment, including the 2 patients (< 1%) who experienced the grade 5 pneumonitis of respiratory failure. First treatment‐emergent pneumonitis onset occurred a median of ~18 weeks into treatment.

Other TEAEs of interest included infusion reaction, alopecia, and myelosuppression. Treatment‐emergent infusion reactions occurred in 9% of patients with PROC (Grade 1–2: > 99%), with 2 patients (< 1%) discontinuing treatment. Alopecia occurred in 2% of participants, with approximately half of cases deemed not related to MIRV treatment. Treatment‐related myelosuppression rates were relatively low, with anemia, thrombocytopenia, and neutropenia occurring in 9%, 9%, and 8% of patients, respectively.

Current and Future Clinical Developments in Ovarian Cancer Therapies

MIRV addresses a medical need for patients with PROC, providing a treatment option for those with high FRα expression. MIRV has also been and continues to be explored in combination with other anti‐tumor agents. The FORWARD II multi‐arm Phase 1b/2 study (NCT02606305) examined MIRV in combination with bevacizumab, carboplatin, PLD, pembrolizumab, or bevacizumab + carboplatin in adults with FRα‐positive (≥ 25%) advanced epithelial ovarian cancer. In the subset of women with high (≥ 75%) FRα‐expression PROC (N = 44), MIRV (6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W) + bevacizumab (15 mg/kg Q3W) had a manageable safety profile with an ORR of 48% and a median PFS of 9.7 months, both of which were greater than in patients who received MIRV monotherapy (MIRASOL; 42% and 5.6 months). Findings from FORWARD II also supported the National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) recommendation for the combined therapy in women with ≥ 25% FRα‐positive PROC. Preliminary studies with gemcitabine (800 mg/m2 [Days 1, 8] + MIRV 6 mg/kg AIBW [Day 1] Q3W) or rucaparib (400–600 mg BID + MIRV 4–6 mg/kg Q3W) showed promising efficacy in PROC (MIRV + gemcitabine: 5/6 [83%] with at least partial response, MIRV + rucaparib: ORR of 44% [N = 9]). MIRV + rucaparib also showed preliminary efficacy in endometrial cancer (ORR of 67% [N = 3]). Hematologic toxicities were frequent with MIRV + gemcitabine treatment.

Beyond PROC, the FORWARD II study demonstrated favorable tolerability and strong anti‐tumor activity of MIRV (6 mg/kg AIBW Q3W) + bevacizumab (15 mg/kg Q3W) in PSOC patients with FRα‐high tumors (ORR: 69%, median PFS: 13.3 months; N = 16). As a result, NCCN guidelines were updated to also recommend the combination therapy for PSOC. The ongoing Phase 3 GLORIOSA trial (NCT05445778) will provide further insight into this doublet as maintenance therapy for PSOC. In addition, in a small FORWARD II PSOC cohort (N = 17), MIRV (5–6 mg/kg AIBW) + carboplatin (AUC4‐AUC5) was well‐tolerated and highly active (ORR: 71%, median PFS: 15 months). MIRV (6 mg/kg AIBW) + carboplatin is currently being evaluated in two ongoing Phase 2 trials; one as a second‐line treatment in FRα‐positive PSOC (NCT05456685), one for newly diagnosed FRα‐high advanced‐stage ovarian cancer (NCT06890338). The triplet therapy, MIRV +‌ carboplatin +‌ bevacizumab, also showed promising results with an ORR of 83% and a median PFS of 13.5 months. As expected for carboplatin‐inclusive regimens, thrombocytopenia (71%) and neutropenia (51%) were common, with thrombocytopenia resulting in dose modifications/delays (66%) and discontinuation of carboplatin (32%), MIRV (15%), and/or bevacizumab (12%) in FORWARD II.

In addition to FRα‐positive ovarian cancer, MIRV has demonstrated clinical benefit in two patients (18.1%) with FRα‐positive endometrial cancer (N = 11; Phase 1 FIH study subpopulation [NCT01609556]). Safety data were not specifically reported for patients with endometrial cancer. MIRV in combination with gemcitabine and pembrolizumab is also being investigated, showing at least partial response in 2/4 patients (50%) with platinum‐resistant endometrial cancer (Phase 1 study) and 6/16 patients (38%) with poor prognosis endometrial cancer (preliminary Phase 2 result), respectively. Though further studies are needed, MIRV in combination with other therapies offers promise for patients with difficult‐to‐treat cancers including PROC, PSOC, and potentially other FRα‐expressing gynecological tumors.
