# Tomatine as a versatile adjuvant boosts mRNA vaccine responses.

## Abstract

The potent adaptive immune response of mRNA vaccines requires that sufficient antigens translated from mRNA cargos and highly efficient activation of innate immunity. However, it remains unclear if simple introduction of an adjuvant can help mRNA vaccines achieve these functions. In this study, a nature product, tomatine, was experimentally incorporated into the widely used nanocarriers, serving a multi-functional adjuvant to improve mRNA expression efficiency and activate innate immunity. Tomatine based liposome (T-LPX) exhibited elevated uptake in dendritic cells (DCs) via caveolin-mediated endocytosis and improved mRNA translation via the increased expression of DExH-box helicase 29 mediated by tomatine, leading to a marked increase in mRNA expression efficiency and ensuring highly efficient antigen presentation. Additionally, tomatine was shown to activate nucleotide-binding oligomerization domain (NOD)-like receptor pathway, thereby promoting the secretion of IFN-γ and upregulating the co-stimulatory molecules (CD80 and CD86) in DCs. Finally, T-LPX loaded with a tumor antigen-encoding mRNA was investigated and found to elicit potent immune responses against the E.G7-OVA murine tumor. Furthermore, such versatile adjuvant effect of tomatine was also demonstrated to equally apply to the other nanocarrier (lipid nanoparticle) and antigen. The tomatine-based LNP-mRNA vaccine against SARS-CoV-2 presented enduring humoral and cellular immune responses. These findings underscore tomatine's potential as a versatile adjuvant for mRNA nano-vaccines, with promising applications in both cancer therapy and infectious disease prevention.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccine employs nanocarriers, such as lipid nanoparticles (LNPs), to deliver antigen-encoding mRNA into host cells, facilitating antigen expression. This process stimulates both humoral and cellular immunity via cross-presentation, positioning mRNA vaccine as a promising approach for cancer immunotherapy and infectious disease prophylaxis. To date, only a few mRNA vaccines (e.g., BNT162b2) have been approved for emergency use during the COVID-19 pandemic. Accumulating evidence indicates that robust adaptive immune responses following mRNA vaccination depend on both effective innate immunity activation and efficient mRNA expression. However, current mRNA vaccines primarily rely on the inherent adjuvant properties of LNPs, which exhibit a limited capacity to stimulate innate immunity. Consequently, clinical mRNA vaccine strategies often incorporate combination therapies with other immunotherapeutic modalities, such as CAR-T cell therapy or immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs). The efficacy of mRNA vaccines along may be insufficient in providing protection against diseases. Continued research into innovative mRNA vaccines with enhanced immunogenicity, including the incorporation of adjuvants, remains essential. While numerous studies have investigated the co-delivery of exogenous adjuvants to activate innate immunity—including TLR agonists (e.g., MPLA, R848, CpG-ODN), STING agonists (e.g., MPLA, R848, CpG-ODN), STING agonists (e.g., Mn2+, cGAMP), and other immune modulatory factors (e.g., α-GalCer) —to enhance mRNA vaccine efficacy to varying degrees, the physicochemical properties of these adjuvants (e.g., MPLA’s poor solubility, Mn2+’s hydrophilicity) can impede their integration into LNP-mRNA formulations. Furthermore, these adjuvants often provide limited improvements in mRNA delivery efficiency and antigen expression. Therefore, an ideal mRNA vaccine adjuvant should readily integrate into LNPs and enhance vaccine efficacy by triggering a strong immune response while also improving mRNA expression efficiency.

Tomatine, a steroidal glycoalkaloid extracted from tomatoes and other solanaceous species, has demonstrated anticancer, antiviral, and immunomodulatory activities. However, the investigation of tomatine’s immunomodulatory effects remains limited, with few studies detailing its potential as an adjuvant for antigenic peptides or proteins. Tomatine has been shown to enhance both humoral and cellular immune responses in some subunit vaccines. The limited development of tomatine-based adjuvants likely stems from an incomplete understanding of the mechanisms underlying its immunomodulatory role. Notably, tomatine may possess unique advantages as an adjuvant for mRNA vaccines, stemming from its inherent amphiphilic molecular structure, which consists of a hydrophobic steroidal backbone and a hydrophilic tetrasaccharide side chain. From the perspective of “structural compatibility”, the structural homology between tomatine and cholesterol allows it to integrate into the lipid bilayer structure of nanoparticles, and thus perfectly compatible with the mainstream lipid delivery carriers for mRNA vaccines. Additionally, existing studies have shown that its amphiphilic properties can significantly facilitate the transport and delivery efficiency of drugs or oligonucleotides. Therefore, as a “structure-function integrated” adjuvant, tomatine can not only serve as a structural component embedded in the lipid bilayer of lipid nanocarriers, but also exert immunostimulatory effects, endowing it with incomparable advantages over traditional adjuvants in the application of mRNA vaccines.

In this study, we investigated the potential of tomatine as a novel adjuvant for mRNA vaccines and sought to elucidate its underlying mechanism of action. Tomatine and antigen-encoding mRNA were co-encapsulated into two distinct types of nanocarriers—liposomes and lipid nanoparticles—to generate mRNA nanovaccines (designated T-LPX and T-LNP, respectively). We found that T-LPX containing ovalbumin-encoding mRNA (OVA-mRNA) induced a robust cellular immune response and enhanced antitumor activity in a mouse tumor model. Similarly, T-LNP encoding the SARS-CoV-2 spike protein elicited strong and sustained immune responses against multiple SARS-CoV-2 variants, including Delta, Wild-Type, and Omicron (as illustrated in Scheme 1). Mechanistically, tomatine enhanced mRNA expression by promoting caveolin-mediated cellular uptake and reducing lysosomal-mediated mRNA degradation. Furthermore, tomatine improved mRNA translation efficiency by upregulating DExH-box helicase 29 (DHX29), a key regulator of mRNA translation initiation. Additionally, tomatine activated the NOD-like receptor pathway, resulting in increased IFN-γ secretion and the upregulation of co-stimulatory molecules (CD80 and CD86) on dendritic cells (DCs). These multifaceted effects significantly enhanced the immunogenicity of both T-LPX and T-LNP. These findings highlight tomatine as an innovative, broadly applicable adjuvant with versatile functions, warranting further investigation for the development of highly effective mRNA vaccines against cancer and infectious diseases.

Results

Co-delivery of tomatine simultaneously enhanced mRNA expression and DCs maturation

Initially, a liposomal formulation comprising (2,3-dioleoyloxy-propyl)-trimethylammonium-chloride (DOTAP) and cholesterol, capable of encapsulating hydrophobic tomatine (T-LP), was prepared using film dispersion methods. The resultant mRNA lipoplexes (T-LPX) were formed through the electrostatic incubation of T-LP and mRNA (Fig. 1a). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images showed that T-LPX had a typical structure of lipoplexes, which is a multi-layered vesicular structure with a hydrophilic internal cavity. (Fig. 1b). The average size of T-LPX was about 151.6 nm, the zeta potential was about +34.9 mV, and the mRNA encapsulation efficiency was about 94.6 % (Fig. 1c). And the incorporation of tomatine and the variation in the molar ratio of tomatine to DOTAP did not lead to any significantly alterations in the particle size or zeta potential of T-LPX, compare to LPX (Supplementary Fig. 1a and b). Gel retardation assays confirmed the successful encapsulation of mRNA within T-LP at a cationic lipid-to-mRNA (N/P) ratio of 3:1, regardless of the amount of tomatine present (Supplementary Fig. 1c). These results suggested that the addition of tomatine did not impact the liposomal characterization and the loading capacity of mRNA, and an N/P ratio of 3:1 was selected for subsequent experiments.

Subsequently, transfection experiments were conducted in a serum-containing environment to facilitate in vitro-to-in vivo translation. DC2.4 cells incubated with green fluorescent protein (GFP) mRNA lipoplexes exhibited GFP activity after 24 h. The highest transfection efficiency was achieved when the molar ratio of tomatine to DOTAP was 10:3 (Fig. 1d). To verify the necessity of tomatine co-loading in liposomes, a physical mixture of LPX and tomatine (LPX + T) were evaluated to further elucidate the mechanism by which tomatine enhances transfection. Lipo2K, a commercial transfection agent, served as a positive control. Although the transfection efficiency of the liposome formulation was lower than that of Lipo2K. The GFP expression in DC2.4 cells treated with T-LPX was significantly higher than that in cells treated with LPX (Supplementary Fig. 2). While LPX + T did not show enhanced mRNA expression, indicating that only the co-delivery of tomatine in liposomes could effectively augment mRNA expression in vitro. It is worth noting that all the formulations showed no significant cytotoxicity (Supplementary Fig. 3).

Next, we prepared mouse bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) for further study. First, the antigen presentation of BMDCs treated with OVA-mRNA loaded with LPX or T-LPX was detected, the antigen presentation was significantly improved by T-LPX (10:3), which was consistent with the strongest transfection of T-LPX (10:3) (Fig. 1e). The antigen-specific recognition signal is the first signal for T cells activation, while the co-stimulatory signal (such as CD80 and CD86), as the second signal, can amplify the antigen signal and promote the activation, proliferation and differentiation of T cells. Adjuvants usually enhance the production of co-stimulatory signals. Our results showed that the addition of tomatine significantly enhance the maturation activation of BMDCs, and the optimal molar ratio of tomatine to DOTAP is 10:3 was selected for the subsequent study (Fig. 1f). At this point, the mass ratio of tomatine to mRNA was approximately 3:1, and this ratio will be directly referenced when we prepare tomatine-based mRNA lipid nanoparticles (T-LNP).

Tomatine’s impact on uptake, release, and translation in augmenting mRNA expression of T-LPX in vitro

To explore the mechanism by which tomatine enhances mRNA expression, we examined the key cellular processes involved in T-LPX transfection, including mRNA uptake, release, and translation. Initially, we focused on the uptake behaviors of LPX and T-LPX loaded with Cy5-mRNA in DC2.4 cells. Flow cytometry analyses revealed that the cells rapidly internalize the lipoplexes, achieving a cell uptake efficiency of 100 %. Then, we compared the mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) of Cy5-positive cells. As illustrated in Fig. 2a, the MFI in the LPX group remained unchanged after 1 h while it showed a significantly increase over time in the T-LPX group. Representative flow cytometry histograms at 6 h mark revealed that the uptake of LPX + T was similar to LPX, whereas T-LPX uptake was notably enhanced (Fig. 2b).

To ascertain whether the incorporation of tomatine into lipoplexes altered uptake intensity by modifying the endocytosis pathway, we exposed DC2.4 cells to nano-preparations in the presence of various endocytosis inhibitors. These include: Cytochalasin D binds actin and blocks its polymerization, and is considered to be an inhibitor of endocytosis mediated by actin-dependent micropinocytosis. Chlorpromazine induces a loss of clathrin and adaptor protein complex 2 from the surface of the cells, and is considered a specific inhibitor of the clathrin-mediated endocytosis. Nystatin distorts the structure and functions of cholesterol-rich membrane domains, including caveolae, and is considered a selective inhibitor of the caveolae-mediated endocytosis. Fig. 2c showed that cytochalasin D, chlorpromazine, and nystatin substantially reduced the uptake of LPX, indicating that the uptake of LPX may involve multiple pathways. However, only chlorpromazine and nystatin reduced T-LPX uptake, with nystatin significantly decreasing T-LPX uptake by 22 %, suggesting that the uptake of T-LPX mainly involves endocytosis mediated by clathrin and caveolin, especially caveolin. Then, molecular docking was employed to further assess the binding affinity of tomatine with caveolin and clathrin (Supplementary Fig. 4a and b). The resulting docking scores for tomatine with clathrin and caveolin were −6.595 and −11.823, respectively (Supplementary Fig. 4c). To further refine the binding poses and estimate the binding free energy (ΔG), the Prime MM-GBSA module was employed. The calculated ΔG values were −18.76 kcal/mol for clathrin and −30.03 kcal/mol for caveolin, indicating a stronger binding interaction with caveolin. Therefore, we concluded that the addition of tomatine preferentially enhances or re-directs nanoparticle uptake through the caveolin pathway, which may attributable to the stronger binding affinity between tomatine and caveolin. It is worth noting that the inhibitory effect of cytochalasin D, chlorpromazine, and nystatin on the uptake of LPX + T is similar to that of LPX, indicating that the physical mixture of tomatine and LPX does not affect its endocytic pathway. This once again emphasizes the necessity of integrating tomatine into the LPX structure to optimize the delivery function.

The release of mRNA into the cytoplasm refers critical influence on mRNA expression. And the lysosomal escape capacity of nanocarriers plays a pivotal role in the effective release of mRNA into the cytoplasm. Nanocarriers utilizing the caveolae-mediated endocytosis pathway are typically delivered to caveosomes, thus circumventing the detrimental acidic and enzyme-rich environment of lysosomes. To elucidate the intracellular localization of T-LPX, we incubated DC2.4 cells with T-LPX loaded with Cy5-labeled mRNA and subsequently visualized the cells using confocal laser scanning microscopy. Prior to imaging, lysosomes were stained with LysoTracker Green. The imaging data showed that the colocalization of mRNA with lysosomes was similar in the LPX and LPX + T groups, but it was significantly diminished in the T-LPX group (Fig. 2d–f). This finding suggested that T-LPX was more adept at avoiding lysosome-related mRNA degradation, likely resulting in enhanced mRNA release into the cytoplasm for translation. These observations also affirmed the importance of co-loading tomatine in mRNA nano-preparations, as tomatine’s binding to caveolin facilitated the uptake and release of mRNA.

Next, we performed quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) analyses to assess the influence of tomatine on mRNA translation. The results revealed that DCs treated with T-LPX displayed increased expression of several genes associated with mRNA translation compared to those treated with LPX (Fig. 2g). Notably, the expression of DHX29 was elevated approximately sixfold in mRNA levels. Meanwhile, the protein level of DHX29 in the DCs treated with T-LPX was also significantly increased (Fig. 2h). DHX29 is known to function in remodeling the 40S/mRNA/eIFs complex, thereby enhancing mRNA translation (Fig. 2j). Considering that the half-life of delivered mRNA is relatively short (usually hours to tens of hours), we further detected the protein expression dynamics of DHX29 at 0, 2, 12, and 24 h after T-LPX treatment. The results show that the protein expression of DHX29 was initiated at 2 h after treatment, and the expression level had significantly increased at 12 h, and continued to increase with the extension of time (Fig. 2i). These data prove that tomatine can rapidly and efficiently upregulate the expression of DHX29, which promote the translation efficiency of delivered mRNA, and ultimately enhance the overall expression of mRNA. Collectively, these findings demonstrated that tomatine altered the cellular uptake pathway of mRNA lipoplexes, predominantly favoring a caveolin-mediated mechanism. This particular uptake pathway is advantageous for mRNA expression as it bypasses lysosomal degradation, allowing for efficient delivery into the cytoplasm for translation. Additionally, tomatine may augment mRNA translation in cells expressing DHX29. In summary, tomatine contributes to the augmented cellular protein expression by promoting the uptake, release, and translation of mRNA.

Tomatine boosted the immunogenicity of T-LPX via activation of the NOD-like receptor pathway

In order to explore the adjuvant activity of tomatine, we further investigated its ability to promote the secretion of cytokine signals from BMDCs. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) data indicated that BMDCs treated with T-LPX did not elicit severe cytokine storms, including type I interferon IFN-β (Fig. 3a), IL-12(Fig. 3b), and TNF-α (Fig. 3c), yet they secreted increased levels of type II interferon IFN-γ (Fig. 3d), compared with LPX. Overall, these results proved that the co-delivery of tomatine in mRNA nano-vaccines effectively stimulated DCs maturation and cytokines release, which are beneficial to mobilize the adaptive immune system.

Next, we explored the mechanism through which tomatine regulates the immune response. Kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes (KEGG) enrichment analysis suggested that T-LPX, in contrast to LPX, predominantly influenced the NOD-like signaling pathway (Fig. 3e), which is crucial for managing the host’s innate immune response. We measured the expression levels of genes associated with the NOD-like pathway using qPCR. The results showcased significantly higher expressions of interferon regulatory factors (IRFs, predominantly IRF3 and IRF7), NOD1, and TANK-binding kinase (TBK) in BMDCs treated with T-LPX than in those treated with LPX (Fig. 3f). The activation of the NOD-like pathway by T-LPX was further confirmed through western blot analysis of phosphorylated TBK1 (p-TBK1) and phosphorylated IRF7 (p-IRF7) levels (Fig. 3g). Interestingly, there was no significant difference in NOD1 protein levels between LPX and T-LPX treatments, leading to the hypothesis that tomatine might bind to NOD1 to activate downstream molecules. Then, molecular docking studies were conducted to predict the protein structure of NOD1 (Fig. 3j). As anticipated, molecular docking revealed that tomatine interacted with NOD1 at multiple sites via hydrogen bonds (Fig. 3h and Supplementary Fig. 5), with docking-2 exhibiting the best binding characterized by the lowest docking score (Fig. 3i). These findings represent the first demonstration that tomatine can activate the NOD-like pathway by binding with NOD1, thus initiating innate immunity.

Therapeutic efficacy of T-LPX/OVA on the E. G7 murine tumor model

Having demonstrated the effectiveness of tomatine in promoting mRNA expression and the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines in vitro, we explored the therapeutic potential of tomatine-doped mRNA tumor vaccines. First, the ability of tomatine to enhance the mRNA expression in vivo was confirmed by intravenous injection of T-LPX loaded with luciferase–encoding mRNA (Luc mRNA). As shown in Fig. 4a, due to the distribution characteristics of DOTAP cationic liposomes, LPX and T-LPX were mainly expressed in the lungs, and the luminescence intensity of T-LPX in the lungs is significantly higher than that of LPX. These findings motivated us to investigate the therapeutic efficacy of T-LPX loaded with ovalbumin-encoding mRNA (T-LPX/OVA) in an E. G7 murine lymphoma tumor model. For the in vivo study, mice were subcutaneously inoculated with OVA-expressing E. G7 lymphoma cells and vaccinated upon tumor palpability on day 7. Tumor sizes were monitored every three days. The control group received PBS, while the negative control group was treated with T-LPX/Luc, prepared with non-therapeutic Luc mRNA. In the treatment group, mice were administered either LPX/OVA or T-LPX/OVA. Both treatments resulted in a significant attenuation of tumor growth compared to the control and T-LPX/Luc groups (Fig. 4b). Dissection on day 25 revealed that the average tumor weight in the T-LPX/OVA group was markedly lower than that in the LPX/OVA group (Supplementary Fig. 6). Notably, a general trend of tumor regression was observed in T-LPX/OVA-treated mice, as evidenced by recording individual tumor growth curves (Supplementary Fig. 7).

Subsequently, we explored the role of the immune system in the antitumor effects of T-LPX/OVA by analyzing the splenic T cells isolated from immunized mice. The abundance of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells in the spleens of mice in the T-LPX/OVA group was significantly greater than that in the control group (Supplementary Fig. 8a and b). Notably, the ratio of CD8+ to CD4+ T cells was higher in the T-LPX/OVA group compared to other groups (Supplementary Fig. 8c). This increase in OVA-specific CD8+ T cells following T-LPX/OVA immunization (Fig. 4c) suggested that T-LPX/OVA induced a more robust cellular immune response and was more effective in reducing tumor growth than the mRNA nano-vaccine without tomatine.

We further delved into the immunotherapeutic mechanism of T-LPX/OVA in vivo. Our findings revealed that T-LPX augmented antigen signaling (Fig. 4e and Supplementary Fig. 9) by enhancing mRNA uptake in pulmonary dendritic cells (DCs) (Fig. 4d). Additionally, the co-delivery of tomatine significantly improved the activation of pulmonary DCs in vivo (Fig. 4f). Mature DCs then migrated to the immune organs, including the spleens and lymph nodes (Fig. 4g and h), thereby providing more antigen signals to activate T cells.

Finally, we conducted a comprehensive assessment of the in vivo safety of T-LPX/OVA. On the one hand, to assess the toxicity of T-LPX/OVA on tumor-bearing mice, we conducted serological analyses and hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining. H&E staining revealed no evidence of hyperemia, bleeding, edema, inflammation, or other pathological reactions in the hearts, livers, spleens, kidneys, or lungs across all groups (Fig. 4i). Biochemical indices in the T-LPX/OVA group displayed no significant deviations compared to those in the PBS-treated group and other lipoplex-treated groups (Fig. 4j). On the other hand, we investigated the acute toxicity of T-LPX/OVA. PBS (Control), LPX/OVA, and T-LPX/OVA were administered to mice according to the tumor treatment protocol. 24 h later, the bronchoalveolar lavage fluid, serum, and organs of the mice were collected for safety assessment. As shown in Supplementary Fig. 10a and b, H&E staining and biochemical indicators detection results indicated that there were no significant differences among the control group, LPX/OVA group and T-LPX/OVA group. Given the enhanced adjuvanticity of T-LPX/OVA, it is likely to induce increased inflammation in the lungs following intravenous vaccination. We further examined the inflammatory factors TNF-α and IL-1β in the bronchoalveolar lavage fluid of mice to more sensitively assess the acute inflammatory response in the lungs. All samples were uniformly processed and diluted to a total protein concentration of 10 μg/mL, and then the expression levels of TNF-α and IL-1β were detected using ELISA. The results showed that compared with the control group, the secretion levels of TNF-α and IL-1β in mice of the LPX/OVA group and T-LPX/OVA group did not show significant increases, indicating that the intravenous injection of T-LPX/OVA did not trigger acute inflammatory responses in the lungs (Supplementary Fig. 10c). In conclusion, the above results fully confirm the safety of intravenous administration of T-LPX/OVA.

Tomatine also exerted the adjuvant effect based on mRNA lipid nanoparticles

In order to evaluate the application potential of tomatine adjuvant, we investigated the universality of tomatine. Lipid nanoparticles (LNP) are another commonly used mRNA vaccine delivery vector. As illustrated in Fig. 5a, lipid nanoparticles co-loaded with mRNA and tomatine (T-LNP) were produced using a novel ionizable lipid (IC8) and microfluidic technology. The mass ratio of tomatine to mRNA was maintained at 3:1. The T-LNPs were observed by TEM as spherical or spheroidal structures, and most of the particles were smaller than 200 nm (Fig. 5b). Similar to lipoplexes, tomatine addition did not alter the average particle size (∼108 nm), zeta potential (∼0.4 mV) and mRNA encapsulation efficiency (∼96.5 %) of T-LNP compared to LNP (without tomatine) (Fig. 5c). Transfection efficiency for both LNP and T-LNP approached 100 %, on par with Lipo2K (Supplementary Fig. 11). Nevertheless, the MFI of T-LNP was greater than LNP (Fig. 5d). Additionally, LNP + T and LNP exhibited comparable transfection results, further confirming that tomatine’s effectiveness is contingent on co-loading with mRNA in delivery vectors. We then examined the uptake behaviors of LNP and T-LNP loaded with Cy5-mRNA in DC2.4 cells (Supplementary Fig. 12a). The uptake of T-LNP by DC2.4 cells was significantly higher than that of LNP, while LNP + T uptake was similar to LNP (Supplementary Fig. 12b). Supplementary Fig. 12c showed that cytochalasin D, chlorpromazine, and nystatin substantially reduced the uptake of LNP, indicating that the uptake of LNP may involve multiple endocytosis pathways. However, only nystatin reduced T-LNP uptake, suggesting that the uptake of T-LNP mainly involves endocytosis mediated by caveolin. Therefore, we concluded that the addition of tomatine also preferentially enhances T-LNP uptake through the caveolin-mediated endocytosis. Similarly, tomatine co-loaded T-LNP showed enhanced mRNA expression in vivo following intramuscular injection (Fig. 5e). Likewise, T-LNP treatment further enhanced BMDC maturation and IFN-γ secretion compared to the control and LNP groups (Fig. 5f and g). These results indicate that the versatile adjuvant effect of tomatine is also applicable to other nanocarriers.

T-LNP induced strong antibody and Th1-biased T-cell responses against SARS-CoV-2 delta, wild-type, and omicron variants

Following the demonstration of tomatine’s enhancement of the antitumor efficacy of an mRNA nano-vaccine, we proceeded to assess its impact on COVID-19 mRNA vaccines. To investigate immune responses, we utilized LNP and T-LNP containing mRNA encoding the SARS-CoV-2 delta variant spike protein. Groups of BALB/c mice (n = 6) received intramuscular inoculations at weeks 0 and 2 (Fig. 6a). Serum samples collected at weeks 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 underwent ELISA to measure RBD-specific IgG responses to SARS-CoV-2. IgG titers against SARS-CoV-2 and its variants (wild-type, delta and omicron) were significantly elevated in the T-LNP group compared to the LNP group (Fig. 6b), indicating tomatine’s capacity to enhance COVID-19 mRNA vaccination and elicit stronger protective antibody responses against SARS-CoV-2.

Cellular immune responses induced by LNP and T-LNP were assessed using flow cytometry and enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assays. Splenocytes and lymph node cells from vaccinated mice were analyzed for T cell responses. T-LNP induced a more substantial production of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6c). To evaluate IFN-γ secretion, splenocytes from LNP and T-LNP vaccinated mice were restimulated in vitro with S peptide pools of the delta variant of SARS-CoV-2. ELISpot results showed that T cells from T-LNP immunized mice secreted significantly higher levels of IFN-γ compared to those from LNP-immunized mice (Fig. 6d), underscoring tomatine’s role in eliciting strong humoral and Th1-biased T-cell responses.

Moreover, hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining revealed no notable histopathological differences in the main organs between the LNP and T-LNP treatment groups and the control group, suggesting the safety of T-LNP in vivo (Fig. 6e). In conclusion, tomatine has demonstrated its efficacy as a multifunctional universal adjuvant, showcasing exceptional adjuvant activity in the realm of infectious disease prevention for mRNA nano-vaccines.

Discussion

Adding exogenous adjuvants can significantly amplify the efficacy of mRNA vaccines. An ideal mRNA vaccine adjuvant should augment the vaccine efficacy by eliciting a strong immune response and addressing mRNA shortcomings, such as low expression efficiency. We previously applied a STING agonist (manganese, Mn) as a novel adjuvant for mRNA nano-vaccines against COVID-19, which exhibited promising efficacy against SARS-CoV-2 due to the additional upregulation of mRNA expression except the conventional adjuvant effects of Mn. This pioneering finding underscores that it’s necessary to develop groundbreaking adjuvants with multifunction to further advance mRNA vaccines as innovative powerful versatile strategies combating diseases. However, the water solubility of Mn2+ makes it unfavorable to integrate into LNP-mRNA system. Tomatine is a natural adjuvant with immune-enhancing effect, but it has only been reported as a subunit vaccine adjuvant, and the immune mechanism is unclear. Additionally, tomatine has been shown to improve drug transport and delivery by enhancing the permeability of cell membranes. Tomatine has been incorporated into cationic liposomes to boost transfection efficiency in plasmid DNA delivery. These suggest that tomatine may be an ideal adjuvant with the potential to enhance mRNA expression. Thus, in this study, we applied tomatine to mRNA nano-vaccines for probing its immune-enhancing effects as well as the underlying mechanism.

We firstly demonstrated that the incorporation of tomatine could significantly improve mRNA expression. The efficient expression of mRNA-encoded antigen is often a prerequisite for a successful mRNA vaccine. Endocytic uptake, mRNA release and translation are known to be the limiting steps for mRNA expression. Our research has revealed that tomatine can enhance the uptake of mRNA lipoplexes through the caveolin-mediated endocytic pathway. Previous study reported that tomatine regulates the endocytosis process and enhances the delivery of lipoplexes due to its unique membrane permeation properties. Our investigation further found that tomatine has better binding with caveolin, and thus the tomatine-coloaded lipoplexes enter the cells mainly through the caveolin-mediated pathway. Once endocytosed, mRNA must be able to escape the lysosomes to be translocated into the cytosol, for protein translation. Our result demonstrated that tomatine facilitated the transport of mRNA into the cytoplasm through the caveolin-mediated endocytic pathway. Because the caveolin-mediated endocytic pathway forms caveolae through caveolin proteins and lipid raft structures, directly delivering the ingested substances to non-lysosomal cellular substructures (such as the Golgi apparatus or endoplasmic reticulum), thereby avoiding lysosomal degradation. mRNA translation is the other critical step in the expression. Notably, tomatine was found to accelerate the translation initiation of mRNA via DHX29 upregulation, uniquely boosting mRNA expression, but the upstream signaling pathway that drives the upregulation of DHX29 expression by tomatine remains unclear.. Existing literature indicates that there is a certain correlation between DHX29 and immune-related responses. For example, DHX29 can interact with RIG-I and MAVS through its helicase 1 domain. So, whether the upregulation of DHX29 is related to the activation of NLR pathway by tomatine will be an interesting question.

Subsequently, the immune-enhancing effects of tomatine were also demonstrated. Although tomatine has previously been used as an adjuvant in subunit vaccines, the exact mechanism of its immune activation remains unclear. Our results of RNA-seq and WB all proved that, compared with LPX, T-LPX activated the NOD-like receptor pathway (NLR). Then, we used molecular docking to predict multiple binding sites of tomatine with NOD1. And we speculate that tomatine, by binding to NOD1, acts as an NLR agonist and thereby initiates the innate immune response. Of course, more experiments are needed, such as combining experiments or site-directed mutagenesis experiments, to verify this hypothesis. NLRs, cytosolic members of PRR family, function as cytoplasmic sensors. NLR-based adjuvants remain relatively underexplored, while another PRR family member, the toll-like receptor (TLR), has been widely studied in experimental mRNA vaccine researches. TLR agonists typically activate type I interferons (IFN-α/-β), potentially inhibiting protein translation and CD8+ T cell activation. In comparison, in our study, tomatine-mediated NLR activation did not significantly promote IFN-β production or elicit excessive inflammatory cytokine release, suggesting a favorable safety profile that may help sustain efficient antigen expression. This unique immunostimulatory signature “high IFN-γ, low Type I IFN” highlights the potential of tomatine as a well-balanced NLR agonist-based mRNA vaccine adjuvant, and may be the key mechanism for the “win-win” outcome of potent immune activation and high antigen expression.

More importantly, the versatility of tomatine lays the essential foundation for its broad application prospects. In the above experiments, the liposome (consisting of DOTAP and cholesterol) was adopted for the study of tomatine-coloaded mRNA nano-vaccines. As a lipid-soluble compound, tomatine has been reported to bind with sterols (e.g., cholesterol) to form complexes. Therefore, we speculated that tomatine may be encapsulated in the lipid layer formed by DOTAP and cholesterol. Given the fact that lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) composed of various lipids and cholesterol are currently the most advanced delivery vehicles in clinical studies of mRNA vaccines, tomatine doped LNP-mRNA (T-LNP) was successfully obtained by microfluidic technique. Our results revealed that tomatine in T-LNP also improved mRNA expression and vaccine immunogenicity. Importantly, T-LNP loaded with mRNA encoding the SARS-CoV-2 delta variant spike protein elicited robust and durable humoral immune responses and induced stronger Th1-biased immune responses compared to LNP. This aligns with previous findings that tomatine, acting as an adjuvant of subunit vaccines, stimulated effective antigen-specific humoral and cellular immune responses.

This study highlights the tomatine’s dual function of enhancing mRNA expression - synergistic regulation of immune response, providing a new strategy for the development of mRNA vaccine adjuvants. Interestingly, from a broader perspective of natural products, the advantages of tomatine may not be isolated cases. Various natural saponins (such as ginsenosides, astragalosides and notoginsenosides) have structural diversity (including aglycones and sugar chains) and good biocompatibility. Studies have reported that some saponins can exert adjuvant effects by promoting antigen delivery and regulating immune cell activation. We speculate that other structurally similar natural saponins may also have the potential to become “multifunctional vaccine adjuvants”.

Finally, although this study provides relatively sufficient experimental evidence for the application of tomatine in mRNA vaccines, there are still several limitations that need to be further explored in the future. On the one hand, although we have verified the adjuvant effect of tomatine in the EG.7 lymphoma model (using OVA as the antigen and liposomes as the carrier) and the infectious disease model (using the SARS-CoV-2 antigen and IC8 LNP as the carrier), indicating that tomatine has certain universality. However, whether it is applicable to more extensive antigen types (such as tumor neoantigens, polysaccharide antigens, etc.), LNP systems (such as the widely used ionizable lipids SM102, etc.) and disease backgrounds still requires further evaluation. On the other hand, this study is primarily conducted in mouse models, so the immune activation effect and safety of tomatine-based nanoparticles need to be verified in non-human primate models and other models closer to humans for the translation to clinical use.

Conclusions

In summary, we have innovatively applied tomatine as a universal and multifunctional adjuvant in mRNA nano-vaccines for both cancer therapy and infectious disease prophylaxis. Tomatine has been shown to enhance mRNA expression by promoting caveolin-mediated uptake, reducing lysosomal-related mRNA degradation, and improving DHX29-involved mRNA translation, thereby ensuring highly efficient antigen presentation. Additionally, tomatine was found to boost IFN-γ secretion and the expression of co-stimulatory factors (CD80 and CD86) in dendritic cells (DCs) by activating the NOD-like receptor pathway of innate immunity. These processes significantly promoted the immunogenicity of mRNA vaccines, hence achieving robust adaptive immune responses against both tumors and infectious diseases. Collectively, these findings provide a theoretical foundation and offer an advanced perspective for the development of next-generation adjuvants for advanced mRNA vaccines.

PE-conjugated H-2Kb/SIINFEKL tetramer was purchased Helixgen Guangzhou Co., Ltd. Total RNA Kit I (catalog no. R6834) was purchased from Omega. RevertAid Premix (catalog no. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) ELISA plates were purchased from Corning. HRP-conjugated anti-mouse IgG was purchased from Abcam. IFN-γ ELISpotPLUS kits were purchased from Mabtech.

mRNA synthesis

The mRNA was produced in vitro using T7 RNA polymerase-mediated transcription from a linearized DNA template with extra addition of pseudouridine. Brief, the vectors were linearized with Sal I (QuickCut™ Sal I, catlog no. #1636. TaKaRa), and used as templates for in vitro transcription (IVT) by T7 RNA polymerase (T7 High Yield RNA Transcription kit, catlog no. E131-01A, Novoprotein). UTP was replaced by N1-methyl pseudouridine triphosphate in the IVT reaction. Posttranscription capping was performed with vaccinia capping enzyme and 2′-O-methyltransferase (Cap 1 Capping System, catlog no. M082-01B, Novoprotein). The antigen sequence design of the SARS-CoV-2 delta variant spike protein was described in our previous work.

Animals and cells

C57BL/6 and BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks) were purchased from Beijing HFK Biotechnology. All animal experiments were approved by the “Animal Care and Use Committee” of Sichuan University. DC2.4 cells and E. G7-OVA cells were purchased from American Type Culture Collections (ATCC). DC2.4 cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 complete medium (containing 10 % FBS and 1 % P/S) in an incubator at 37 °C and 5 % CO2. E. G7-OVA cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 complete medium containing 0.4 mg/mL geneticin (G418) in an incubator at 37 °C and 5 % CO2.

Preparation and characterization of T-LPX

Cationic liposomes were prepared using the thin-film dispersion method. DOTAP and Chol (1:1 M ratio) were dissolved in ethanol and transferred into a round-bottom flask. For liposomes formulated with tomatine, tomatine at molar ratios of 10:1, 10:3, 10:5, 10:8, and 10:10 to DOTAP was added. Then, the ethanol was evaporated using a rotary evaporator, after which the lipid film was rehydrated in RNase-free water. The resulting lipid liquid was sonicated until the dispersion became clear to obtain tomatine-loaded liposomes (T-LP). Then, T-LP was mixed with mRNA at the corresponding N/P ratio to obtain mRNA liposomes (T-LPX). LPX was also obtained by the above steps without the addition of tomatine. The average particle size, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential of LPX and T-LPX were investigated by a Zetasizer Nano ZS90 (Malvern, United Kingdom). The mRNA encapsulation efficiency (%) of T-LPX can be calculated by measuring the total mRNA and free mRNA with the RiboGreen (Quant-iT RiboGreen RNA assay kit, Invitrogen).

Preparation and characterization of T-LNP

mRNA lipid nanoparticles (LNP) were prepared using a microfluidic device (INano L, Micro&Nano Biologics Co. Ltd., China) with the mass ratio of ionizable lipid (IC8) to mRNA set as 15:1. Specifically, IC8, DOPE, Chol and DMG-PEG2k were dissolved in ethanol at a molar ratio of 35:16:46.5:2.5 as the organic phase. Tomatine-doped mRNA lipid nanoparticles (T-LNP) were prepared by adding tomatine to the organic phase at a mass ratio of 3:1 to mRNA. In addition, mRNA was dissolved in citrate buffer (pH 6.0) as the aqueous phase. Afterwards, LNP and T-LNP were obtained by mixing the organic phase and aqueous phase (1:3 (v/v)) in the microfluidic device at a flow rate of 12 mL/min. Finally, LNP and T-LNP were ultrafiltrated with citrate buffer to remove ethanol. The average particle size, PDI, and zeta potential of LNP and T-LNP were investigated by a Zetasizer Nano ZS90. The mRNA encapsulation efficiency (%) of T-LNP can be calculated by measuring the total mRNA and free mRNA with the RiboGreen (Quant-iT RiboGreen RNA assay kit, Invitrogen).

Gel electrophoresis retardation assay

To evaluate the loading efficiency of mRNA by LP and T-LP, 0.5 μg free mRNA, LPX and T-LPX (containing 0.5 μg mRNA) at different N/P ratios (including 1, 2, 3, 4 and 5) were diluted with RNase-free water and mixed with NorthernMax® formaldehyde load dye containing ethidium bromide. The mixture samples were heated at 65 °C for 10 min, and then added into the gel hole of 1 % denaturing formaldehyde for electrophoresis. Retardation of mRNA was analyzed by a Bio-Rad ChemiDoc imaging system (Hercules, CA).

Cellular transfection

DC2.4 cells were collected and inoculated into 24-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well and cultured overnight. The next day, DC2.4 cells were treated with nanopreparations containing 0.5 μg green fluorescent protein (GFP) mRNA. Notably, the LPX + T group was added to the cells immediately after LPX was physically mixed with the tomatine solution. The commercial transfection reagent Lipo2K served as a positive control. 24 h after transfection, the expression of GFP was observed and photographed under an inverted fluorescence microscope (Nikon, Japan). Subsequently, the cells were collected and washed twice with phosphate buffered saline (PBS). The transfection efficiency and mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) of GFP-positive cells were detected by flow cytometry (Eisen Bioscience, USA).

Cellular uptake

DC2.4 cells were collected and inoculated into 24-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well and cultured overnight. The next day, DC2.4 cells were treated with nanopreparations containing 0.5 μg Cy5-mRNA. The LPX + T group was also added to the cells immediately after LPX was physically mixed with the tomatine solution. 1, 2 and 6 h later, the cells were collected and washed twice with PBS. The mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) of Cy5-positive cells was detected by flow cytometry.

Molecular docking

The three-dimensional structures of caveolin and clathrin were predicted using AlphaFold3. Molecular docking was executed using the Desmond simulation package (Desmond/Maestro non-commercial version 2023.4, D. E. Shaw Research, New York, NY). Protein structures were prepared, including filling in missing side chains, adding hydrogens and minimizing heavy atoms with default parameters using Protein Preparation Wizard. Then the structure file of the ligand, tomatine, was well pretreated in LigPrep, and docking was performed based on the Extra Precision (XP) mode of Glide with default settings.

Mechanisms of cellular internalization

To further study whether coloaded tomatine changed the cellular internalization pathway of T-LPX or T-LNP, an uptake inhibition experiment was carried out. DC2.4 cells were also inoculated into 24-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well and cultured overnight. Endocytosis inhibitors, including chloropromazine (10 μg/mL), cytochalasin D (2.5 μg/mL), and nystatin (10 μg/mL), were added to pretreat the cells for 30 min. Then, the cells were replaced with fresh RPMI 1640 complete medium and treated with nanopreparations containing 0.5 μg Cy5-mRNA for an additional 6 h. Lysosomal colocalization

To investigate the intracellular trafficking of mRNA, the colocalization of Cy5-labeled mRNA with lysosomes was imaged by confocal laser microscopy. DC2.4 cells were inoculated in confocal dishes (1.5 × 105 cells) and cultured overnight. The next day, LPX, LPX + T and T-LPX containing 0.5 μg Cy5-mRNA (red) were added to confocal dishes. After 4 h of incubation, LysoTracker DND-26 (green, 75 μM) was added to confocal dishes to mark the lysosomes of cells. At 6 h, the medium in the confocal dishes was discarded, and the cells were washed three times with precooled PBS to remove the redundant nanoparticles and lysosomal dyes. After that, 200 μL fixation buffer was used to fix the cells for 20 min. After washing the cells three times with precooled PBS, 1 × intracellular staining permeabilization wash buffer containing 1 % DAPI (blue) was applied to mark the nuclei of the cells. 10 min later, the cells were washed three times with precooled PBS again, and the samples were observed via confocal laser microscopy. In addition, the degree of colocalization between Cy5-mRNA (red) and lysosomes (green) was analyzed by ImageJ, and Pearson’s_Rr (PCC) was obtained.

Generation of bone marrow-derived DCs (BMDCs)

BMDCs were obtained as described previously with some modifications. In brief, the tibia and femur of the hind legs were dissected from a sacrificed C57BL/6 mouse (6–8 weeks). Then, bone marrow cells were isolated and treated with precooled erythrocyte lysis solution for 5 min to remove red blood cells. After centrifugation at 1200 rpm, the cells were collected and cultured in RPMI 1640 complete medium containing 20 ng/mL GM-CSF and 10 ng/mL IL-4 at 37 °C in a 5 % CO2 cell incubator, denoted day 0. On day 2, all the culture medium was gently removed and centrifuged, half of the supernatant was returned to the dish, and 5 mL of fresh medium was added. On day 4 and day 6, all the culture medium was gently removed and centrifuged, and half of the supernatant was poured. The remaining cells and culture medium were returned to the dish, and 5 mL of fresh medium was also added. On day 7, the BMDCs were collected for future experiments.

BMDC maturation

BMDCs were collected and inoculated into 5-well plates at 3 × 105 cells/well and cultured overnight. The next day, the cells were treated with nanopreparations containing 0.5 μg OVA-mRNA for 24 h. Then, the cells were collected for flow cytometry. Before testing, the cells were first blocked with anti-mouse CD16/CD32 (1:100 dilution) and then stained with PE anti-mouse CD11c (1:100 dilution), FITC anti-mouse CD80 (1:100 dilution) and APC anti-mouse CD86 (1:100 dilution) at 4 °C for 40 min to evaluate BMDC maturation. After washing twice with PBS, CD80+/CD86+ in CD11c+ cells were determined by flow cytometry to analyze the maturation of BMDCs.

qPCR analysis

Total RNA was extracted using the Total RNA Kit I, and cDNA was synthesized using RevertAid Premix. BMDCs were treated with LPX and T-LPX loaded with OVA-mRNA according to the procedure for the determination of BMDC maturation. The expression levels of genes associated with mRNA translation-associated or NOD-like pathways were detected using PowerUp™ SYBR™ Green Master Mix with the corresponding primers and normalized to GAPDH.

Western blotting (WB)

The proteins in BMDCs treated with LPX or T-LPX loaded with OVA-mRNA were extracted by lysing the cells with RIPA buffer containing protease inhibitor cocktail for 1 h on ice. Then, the proteins were separated by 10 % SDS polyacrylamide gels and transferred to polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membranes. After blocking with 5 % nonfat milk dissolved in TBS/0.05 % Tween-20 for 2 h, the PVDF membrane was incubated with primary antibodies, including anti-GAPDH (1:500 dilution), NOD1 (1:500 dilution), p-TBK1 (1:1000 dilution) and p-IRF7 (1:500 dilution), overnight at 4 °C. The PVDF membrane was then incubated with an HRP-conjugated secondary antibody (1:1000 dilution) for 2 h at room temperature. Immunoreactive proteins on the membrane were visualized with an ECL kit by a ChemiScope 6200 Touch system (CliNX, China).

In vivo bioimaging

C57BL/6 mice were intravenously administered LPX and T-LPX loaded with Luc-mRNA at a dose of 20 μg mRNA per mouse. After 6 h, the mice were intraperitoneally injected with 200 μL D-luciferin potassium solution (15 mg/mL). The bioluminescence intensity of the organs was measured by an IVIS spectrum system (Caliper Life Sciences).

BALB/c mice were intramuscularly administered LNP and T-LNP loaded with Luc-mRNA at a dose of 20 μg mRNA per mouse. After 6 h and 24 h, the mice were intraperitoneally injected with 200 μL D-luciferin potassium solution (15 mg/mL). The bioluminescence intensity of mice was measured by an IVIS spectrum system.

In vivo antitumor capability

C57BL/6 (8–10 weeks old) mice were injected subcutaneously with E. G7-OVA tumor cells (5 × 105 cells per mouse). After tumor sizes reached approximately 100 mm3 at day 7, mice were immunized with NaCl, T-LPX/Luc, LPX/OVA and T-LPX/OVA (30 μg mRNA per mouse, n = 5 for each group) by intravenous injection on days 7, 10, or 15. The tumor volumes of mice were measured every three days. All mice were euthanized on day 25. The tumors and spleens were collected for further studies.

CTL detection

On day 25 of immunotherapy in mice, the spleen was collected, and a splenic single-cell suspension was prepared. The cells were first incubated with anti-mouse CD16/32 antibody to block nonspecific FcR binding at 4 °C for 15 min. To stain OVA-specific T cells, the cells were then incubated with APC anti-mouse CD8a (1:100 dilution), FITC anti-mouse CD4 (1:100 dilution) and H-2Kb/SIINFEKL tetramer (OVA-tetramer) for 40 min at 4 °C.

In vivo cellular uptake

Due to the expression of T-LPX/Luc in the lung, the uptake of nanopreparations by pulmonary DCs was further investigated. Mice were intravenously administered LPX and T-LPX loaded with Cy5-mRNA (30 μg mRNA per mouse). After 24 h, the lung was collected, and single-cell suspensions were prepared. The cells were first incubated with anti-mouse CD16/32 (1:100 dilution) at 4 °C for 15 min and then stained with anti-mouse CD45 (1:100 dilution) and anti-mouse CD11c (1:100 dilution) for 40 min at 4 °C. Cy5+ cells (APC-positive) in CD45+CD11c+ cells were determined by flow cytometry to analyze the uptake of LPX/Cy5 and T-LPX/Cy5 in pulmonary DCs.

In vivo DC maturation and antigen presentation

To investigate the antitumor mechanism of T-LPX/OVA in vivo, the maturation and antigen presentation of DCs in vivo were examined. Mice were intravenously administered LPX and T-LPX loaded with OVA-mRNA (30 μg mRNA per mouse). After 24 h, the spleen, lung, and lymph node were collected, and single-cell suspensions were prepared. Then, according to the corresponding experimental requirements, the cells were incubated with different flow cytometry antibodies. For the maturation of DCs in the lung, the cells were first incubated with anti-mouse CD16/32 (1:100 dilution) at 4 °C for 15 min and then stained with anti-mouse CD11c (1:100 dilution), anti-mouse CD80 (1:100 dilution), and anti-mouse CD86 (1:100 dilution) for 40 min at 4 °C. CD80+/CD86+ in CD11c+ cells were determined by flow cytometry to analyze the maturation of DCs in vivo. The antigen presentation of DCs in the lung, spleen and lymph node: the cells were first incubated with anti-mouse CD16/32 (1:100 dilution) at 4 °C for 15 min and then stained with anti-mouse CD11c (1:100 dilution) and anti-mouse H-2Kb bound to SIINKEL (1:100 dilution). H-2Kb+ in CD11c+ cells was determined by flow cytometry to analyze the antigen presentation of DCs in vivo.

Antibody detection

Epitope-specific antibody titers were detected by a semiquantitative enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). ELISA plates were coated with SARS-CoV-2 delta RBD, wild-type RBD, and omicron RBD in coating buffer at a final concentration of 1 μg/mL. After incubation overnight at 4 °C, ELISA plates were washed four times with washing buffer and then blocked with 2 % bovine serum albumin (BSA) for 4 h at 25 °C. After washing twice with washing buffer, serial dilutions of the serum samples were added and incubated overnight at 4 °C. After washing four times with washing buffer, HRP-conjugated goat anti-mouse IgG (1:50,000 dilution) was added to the plates and incubated for 2 h at 25 °C. After another four washes with washing buffer, the trimethyl borane (TMB) solution was added to the plates, and the plates were incubated for 30 min. Finally, the reactions were stopped with 2 M sulfuric acid. The absorbance was measured at 450 nm by a microplate reader (PerkinElmer, USA).

Enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay

IFN-γ ELISpot assay of splenocytes was performed using the mouse IFN-γ ELISpotPLUS kit according to the manufacturer’s instructions. The 96-well plates precoated with IFN-γ antibodies were washed with PBS and incubated with RPMI 1640 medium coated with 10 % PBS for 30 min at room temperature. Then, splenocyte cells were stimulated with the peptide pools of the SARS-CoV-2 delta variant (2 μg/ml of individual peptide) and added to the plates (5 × 105 cells per well). The cells were cultured at 37 °C for 48 h. After that, the cells were removed, and the plates were washed five times with PBS. Subsequently, plates were incubated with biotinylated anti-IFN-γ antibody and streptavidin-ALP at room temperature for 2 h. Spots were exposed following the addition of substrate solution and rinsed in tap water. The plates were naturally dried in the shade and read by an ELISpot reader (AID).

Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism 8.01 software. One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) or two-tailed Student’s t tests were used to assess the significance of differences among groups.
