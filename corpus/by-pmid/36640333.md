# Cell-specific cargo delivery using synthetic bacterial spores.

## Abstract

Delivery of cancer therapeutics to non-specific sites decreases treatment efficacy while increasing toxicity. In ovarian cancer, overexpression of the cell surface marker HER2, which several therapeutics target, relates to poor prognosis. We recently reported the assembly of biocompatible bacterial spore-like particles, termed "SSHELs." Here, we modify SSHELs with an affibody directed against HER2 and load them with the chemotherapeutic agent doxorubicin. Drug-loaded SSHELs reduce tumor growth and increase survival with lower toxicity in a mouse tumor xenograft model compared with free drug and with liposomal doxorubicin by preferentially accumulating in the tumor mass. Target cells actively internalize and then traffic bound SSHELs to acidic compartments, whereupon the cargo is released to the cytosol in a pH-dependent manner. We propose that SSHELs represent a versatile strategy for targeted drug delivery, especially in cancer settings.

INTRODUCTION

The efficacy of cancer chemotherapeutics is abrogated by delivery to non-specific sites, which increases toxicity. To diminish this problem, many nanometer-scale vehicles have been devised to encase cargo. However, these strategies usually rely on passive particle accumulation near tumors via abnormal vasculature, which can be unreliable and result in overall toxicity, necessitating other strategies that actively deliver cargo to specific tissues.

We previously reported assembly of synthetic bacterial sporelike particles called “SSHELs” (synthetic spore husk-encased lipid bilayers). Spores of Bacillus subtilis are surrounded by a protein “coat,” built atop two structural proteins: a membrane anchor SpoVM and a structural ATPase SpoIVA that polymerizes. We reconstituted these proteins on membranes supported by silica beads and, by employing an engineered SpoIVA, covalently modified SSHELs to display molecules of interest.

In many epithelial cancers, receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are mutated or overexpressed. One RTK, human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), facilitates normal proliferation of ovaries, lung, breast, liver, kidney, and central nervous tissue. However, in ~25% of breast cancers it is overexpressed, leading to aberrant growth and cell signaling; in ovarian cancers, HER2 overexpression is related to poor prognosis. Monoclonal antibodies targeting HER2 effectively treat breast, but not ovarian, cancers where platinum-based therapy is preferred. To treat drug-resistant ovarian cancer, doxorubicin (dox), which promotes DNA doublestrand breaks, is used. However, dox causes side effects like hair loss, bone marrow suppression, and vomiting. Moreover, results of treatment with dox are often poor, likely due to reduced tissue penetrance.

To target ovarian cancers overexpressing HER2, we designed dox-loaded SSHELs built atop 1-μm mesoporous silica beads decorated with an affibody that binds HER2. These particles reduced tumor growth in a mouse tumor xenograft model of HER2+ ovarian cancer and improved survival compared with an equivalent dose of liposomal dox (the preferred dox delivery method in the clinic) with reduced toxicity. Mechanistically, SSHELs selectively bound to, and were internalized by, HER2+ cells and then trafficked to acidified compartments, whereupon reduced pH triggered cargo release and subsequent translocation of the membrane-permeable dox into the target cell cytosol. We propose that SSHELs are a versatile therapeutic vehicle displaying receptor-specific cargo release whose targeting specificity and cargo are easily modified.

RESULTS

Dox-encased SSHELs targeting HER2 inhibit tumor growth

To encase cargo, we soaked mesoporous silica beads in a solution of dox (Figure 1A) and applied a phosphatidylcholine bilayer, the major membrane-forming lipid in eukaryotes but largely absent in bacteria. Next, we applied synthesized SpoVM and added purified SpoIVA (harboring a single Cys to allow modification with trans-cyclooctene, TCO) in the presence of ATP to promote polymerization, resulting in stable SSHELs. The solid core was required because, in the absence of a support, polymerized SpoIVA deforms the underlying vesicle, similar to membranecoating proteins in other systems.

To target HER2, we covalently attached to the surface of TCO-modified SSHELs a tetrazine-conjugated affibody that binds to HER2 (Figure 1A). Fluorescence microscopy revealed that labeled affibody uniformly coated the SSHELs (Figure 1B). Quantitative flow cytometry indicated that each SSHEL displayed 94,000 ± 9,600 anti-HER2 affibodies (Figure 1C; n = 3 experiments; ~10,000 particles). Fluorescence microscopy of dox-loaded SSHELs (Dox-SSHEL) constructed using labeled SpoIVA indicated that dox was retained in the center of the particles after SpoIVA polymerized around the periphery (Figure 1D). Extraction of encased dox and measurement of its intrinsic fluorescence revealed that each SSHEL contained 1.5 × 108 ± 1.4 × 107 dox molecules.

We next injected female athymic nude mice subcutaneously in the flank with SKOV3 cells, a HER2+ ovarian cancer cell line. When tumors reached ~100 mm3, mice were treated intermittently with PBS, free dox (3 mg/kg), dox-encased SSHELs displaying the affibody (Dox-SSHELαHER2, using equivalent dox dose), or empty SSHELαHER2. We calculated mean tumor size for each group until the first mouse was sacrificed, according to endpoints set in the animal protocol (tumor size or ulceration, weight loss, or general distress). In PBS-treated mice, mean tumor size (n = 7) reached ~1,200 mm3 in 57 days, whereas treatment with dox or SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~800 mm3 (Figures 1E and S1C). However, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~300 mm3 (Trial 1, Figure 1E). At this dox concentration, all treatment groups showed no signs of toxicity or body weight loss (Figure S1A). We then repeated the experiment using 6 mg/kg of dox (Trial 2), where mean tumor size (n = 7) in PBS-treated mice reached ~1,000 mm3 in 40 days (Figure 1F), and 4/7 tumors displayed ulceration and 2/7 tumors were larger than 1,500 mm3 (Figure S1D). Treatment with dox or SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~600 mm3, but 4/7 tumors from dox-treated mice were ulcerated, and one mouse experienced >10% loss in body weight. Additionally, dox-treated mice displayed pale skin (5/7) and tissue damage at the injection site (3/7). In mice treated with SSHELαHER2, we observed ulcerated tumors (3/7), and one tumor that was larger than 1,500 mm3. In contrast, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~300 mm3 at day 40 (Figure 1F); none of the mice displayed weight loss (Figure S1B), and no tumors were ulcerated (Figure S1D). Administration of dox via SSHELαHER2 therefore enhanced therapeutic efficacy while reducing the toxicity of free dox.

Reduced toxicity from Dox-SSHELαHER2 compared with liposomal dox

We next compared the efficacy of targeted dox delivery with liposome-encased dox (commercially known as “Doxil”), a passive delivery system that is used in clinical settings. In Trials 3 and 4, we intermittently treated female athymic nude mice harboring subcutaneous SKOV3 tumors with PBS, Dox-SSHELαHER2, or liposomal dox (3 mg/kg of dox). Since administration of SSHELαHER2 modestly reduced tumor size (Figures 1E and 1F), we also tested co-administering SSHELαHER2 with external dox (SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox). In Trial 3, mean tumor size of PBS-treated mice reached ~2,200 mm3 in 58 days, whereas coadministration with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox reduced mean tumor size only to ~1,500 mm3 (Figure 2Ai). Similarly, Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced tumor size to ~1600 mm3, but treatment with liposomal dox reduced mean tumor size to ~800 mm3 after 58 days. However, mice treated with liposomal dox or Dox-SSHELαHER2 displayed different survival outcomes (Figure 2Bi), with 8/12 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 surviving after 135 days (one cannibalized by cage mates), and only 2/12 mice treated with liposomal dox surviving after 135 days. The endpoints for this experiment included tumor growth (>1,400 mm3), tumor ulceration, weight loss (>15%), and general discomfort. The major difference between the survival curves was due to toxicity that liposomal dox-treated mice experienced: at day 42, 8/12 mice displayed severe skin descaling that led to tumor ulceration, which required sacrificing these mice (Figure 2C). Two additional mice experienced tumor ulceration without skin descaling, and mice in this group experienced >15% weight loss (Figure S1E). In contrast, no mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 exhibited skin descaling or weight loss, and only 3/12 mice displayed ulcerated tumors (Figures 2C and S1E). We then repeated this experiment but eliminated tumor ulceration as an endpoint (“Trial 4”). Mice receiving liposomal dox were treated with an antibiotic-containing ointment to alleviate skin descaling. In Trial 4, mean tumor size of PBS-treated mice reached ~800 mm3 in 58 days; mice treated with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox similarly reached a mean tumor size of ~825 mm3 (Figure 2Aii). However, treatments with liposomal dox and Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced tumor size, with mean tumor sizes of ~600 mm3 and ~450 mm3, respectively. 5/10 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 survived after 86 days, compared with only 2/10 mice treated with liposomal dox, despite removing tumor ulceration as an endpoint (Figure 2Bii). Again, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 or liposomal dox displayed dissimilar toxicity. 12/12 mice treated with liposomal dox showed skin descaling, with ulcerated tumors in 10/12 mice (Figure 2C), but only 5/12 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 displayed skin descaling, with ulcerated tumors in 2/12 mice. In this trial, no mice experienced >15% weight loss, but by day 78, several mice treated with liposomal dox displayed decreasing weight (Figure S1F). We conclude that treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 increases survival probability with fewer toxicities, compared with liposomal dox treatment.

Next, we measured biodistribution of SSHELs and performed histopathology of internal organs. We injected three tumorbearing mice (independent from mice used in Trials 3–4) with SSHELαHER2 whose lipid was labeled with a fluorescent dye. 24 h post-injection, we harvested and disrupted select organs and examined fluorescence of the resulting cell suspensions by flow cytometry. Tumors isolated from all mice displayed increased fluorescence (Figure 2D), indicating enrichment of SSHELαHER2 at the HER2+ tumor. We observed reduced association of SSHELαHER2 in isolated kidney (5-fold less compared with tumor), liver (7-fold), heart (35-fold), spleen (81-fold), brain, and lungs (no signal detected; Figures 2D and 2E). Additionally, H&E-stained samples from skin, colon, spleen, kidneys, heart, lungs and brain, and lymph nodes and pancreas present within pulmonary or colon sections, respectively, failed to demonstrate any cumulative histo-morphologic abnormalities from mice in Trial 4, after 13 rounds of treatment. Sections of livers were unremarkable in 6/8 mice analyzed. Two mice demonstrated microgranuloma consistent with silica aggregates, which has been previously reported. One, treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2, demonstrated three microgranuloma on three liver cross-sections, and another, treated with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox, demonstrated a single microgranuloma on three cross-sections (Figures S2A–S2Dii). Finally, PBS-treated mice demonstrated prominent tumor cell mitosis and overall less inflammatory response, but tumors from Dox-SSHELαHER2-treated mice demonstrated fewer mitosis and increased neutrophils associated with the tumor necrosis (Figures S2E–S2H).

We previously reported that immunocompetent mice injected intramuscularly with SSHELs produced anti-SpoIVA antibodies but no memory T cell response and subsequently tolerated multiple injections with no obvious toxicity. However, SSHELs in that study did not contain dox. Since the xenograft model reported here required athymic mice, we tested the immunotoxicity of Dox-SSHELαHER2 ex vivo. Incubating purified mouse splenocytes with SSHELs αHER2 did not reduce splenocyte viability (Figure S3A). However, free dox displayed dose-dependent toxicity, which was partially alleviated when the cells were treated with an equivalent amount of Dox-SSHELs αHER2 (Figure S3A). Additionally, Dox-SSHELs αHER2 and SSHELsαHER2 did not appreciably stimulate production of TNFα by CD4+ cells, nor IFNγ or granzyme B by CD8+ cells isolated from mouse splenocytes or human PBMCs (Figures S3B–S3G).

We conclude that targeted dox delivery via SSHELs reduced toxicity and increased survival probability in a mouse tumor xenograft model compared with liposomal dox with reduced immunotoxicity. Although naked silica particles may be cytotoxic, our results with SSHELs are consistent with observations that surface modifications can mitigate toxicity.

Dox-SSHELαHER2 specifically binds to and kills HER2+ cells

Antibody treatment directs HER2 for internal degradation. To test the mechanism for SSHEL-delivered drugs, we measured binding of labeled SSHELαHER2 to two HER2+ cell lines: SKOV3 used above and SKBR3 (breast cancer) and compared it to two HER2− breast cancer cell lines (MDA-MB-231 and MCF7). SSHELs and cells were incubated at different multiplicities of incubation (MOI) and analyzed using flow cytometry. With increasing SSHELαHER2 MOI, SKBR3 and SKOV3 cells displayed increased fluorescence (Figures 3A and 3B), whereas MDA-MB-231 and MCF7 did not (Figures 3C and 3D), suggesting preferential binding. To test if binding was saturable, we first incubated the cells with a 1000-fold excess of unlabeled anti-HER2 affibody, which blocked the binding of SSHELαHER2 at MOI = 100 (Figures 3E and 3F), indicating that SSHEL binding was mediated by HER2. Specificity of HER2 binding was qualitatively confirmed using confocal microscopy (Figures 3G–3I).

We next examined if SSHELαHER2 could bind target cells in a complex tumor microenvironment by employing a zebrafish brain metastasis model that permits direct in vivo visualization of SSHELαHER2 and individual tumor cells using fluorescence microscopy. Zebrafish were injected in the mid-brain parenchyma with SKBR3 cells with labeled lysosomes (green, Figures 3M–M”). Labeled SSHELαHER2 (pink, Figures 3J–3J” and 3L–3L”) were then intraparenchymaly injected at a second site 4 h later at MOI = 100 and imaged by fluorescence microscopy after 30 min. At low magnification, injected cells colocalized with SSHELαHER2 in the hindbrain (Figures 3J–3M). At intermediate magnification, individual injected cells displayed SSHELαHER2 fluorescence around the periphery (Figures 3J–M’). At the highest magnification, we could discern an individual SKBR3 cell with multiple surface-bound SSHELαHER2 (Figures 3J”–3M”).

We next measured cytotoxic specificity of Dox-SSHELαHER2. Incubation of the two HER2+ cell lines with Dox-SSHELαHER2 resulted in dose-dependent cell death, whereas viability of the two HER2− cell lines was either unaffected (MCF7) or reduced to ~65% at MOI = 300 (MDA-MB-231; Figure 3N), corresponding to half maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) of 41 nM (SKBR3) and 86 nM (SKOV3), and comparable to the IC50 of free dox for both cell lines (56 nM for SKBR3; 73 nM for SKOV3; Figure 3O). Free dox reduced the viability of HER2− cells and displayed an IC50 of 96 nM for MDA-MB-231 and 135 nM MCF7 cells, whereas SSHELs or the affibody alone did not reduce viability of any cell line (Figure 3P and 3Q). Incubating cells with dox-loaded SSHELs displaying bovine serum albumin (Dox-SSHELBSA) showed reduced, but indiscriminate, cytotoxicity against all cell lines tested (Figure 3R), with an IC50 value ~3.3-fold (SKBR3) or ~2.3-fold (SKOV3) higher than that of Dox-SSHELαHER2. Lastly, incubation with SSHELαHER2 reduced viability of HER2+ cells only at the highest MOI (Figure 3S), and less effectively than Dox-SSHELαHER2, suggesting that combined action of encapsulated dox directed by the particles via the affibody was required to exert full anti-proliferative activity toward HER2+ cells.

Treatment with dox induces apoptosis, which we monitored by measuring caspase 3/7 activity of HER2+ cells. Treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 or equivalent dose of dox (120 nM), but not SSHELαHER2, induced caspase activity of SKBR3 cells over time (Figure 3T), albeit with differing kinetics, suggesting different uptake rates between free dox and SSHELαHER2. For SKOV3, 120 nM dox did not induce caspase activity (Figure 3U), but annexin V staining indicated that these cells were undergoing apoptosis (Figure S4). In contrast, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 induced both caspase activity (Figure 3U) and increased annexin V staining (Figure S4).

Internalization and trafficking to acidic organelles releases Dox-SSHELαHER2 cargo

To determine the fate of bound SSHELs, we incubated SKOV3 with labeled SSHELαHER2 and monitored colocalization with acidic organelles (visualized with LysoTracker red) using confocal microscopy. At 1 h, SSHELαHER2 were located at the cell periphery (Figure 4A). At 4 h, SSHELαHER2 began co-localizing with the dye, indicating particle internalization and trafficking to acidic compartments. By 48 h, the majority of cell-associated SSHELαHER2 colocalized with acidic compartments. Interestingly, we also observed colocalization of internalized SSHELαHER2 with lysosomes in zebrafish harboring SKBR3 in the hindbrain (Figure 3M”, arrowhead), suggesting that particle uptake by target cells occurs in vivo as well.

To determine the mechanism of particle uptake, we incubated labeled SSHELαHER2 with SKOV3 treated with latrunculin A (actin inhibitor) or MiTMAB (dynamin I and II inhibitor) and measured internalization using flow cytometry. MiTMAB reduced SSHELαHER2 uptake by ~50% (Figure 4B), and latrunculin A reduced SSHELαHER2 uptake by ~70%; cells treated with both displayed minimal particle uptake, indicating that SSHEL uptake requires an active internalization process, likely macropinocytosis.

We next monitored the destiny of dox after Dox-SSHELαHER2 uptake using fluorescence microscopy by taking advantage of the intrinsic fluorescence of dox. At 1 h, dox appeared as puncta on the cell periphery, consistent with the surface attachment of SSHEL-encased dox (Figure 4C). After 24 h, we observed fewer peripheral dox puncta, and increased cytosolic distribution of dox, suggesting that SSHEL-encased dox was released into the cytosol.

To test if exposure to reduced pH liberates SSHEL-encased cargo, we incubated Dox-SSHEL in either simulated serum (pH 7.4) or simulated endosomal conditions (pH 5.0) and monitored dox release over time. In acidic pH, encased dox was released with a half-life of 4 h (Figure 4D). In contrast, at pH 7.4, dox release was slower, with more than 50% of the drug remaining associated with SSHEL even after 92 h, suggesting that SSHELs can be destabilized in acidic conditions that simulate endosome trafficking.

DISCUSSION

The efficacy of targeting antibodies to HER2 occurs via three possible mechanisms: inhibition of HER2 extracellular domain shedding leading to internal degradation, reducing binding sites for signaling ligands, and stimulation of immune-regulated cellular cytotoxicity in breast cancer. Trastuzumab (Herceptin) functions via the first mechanism. However, SSHELs bound to surface HER2 were internalized via an active mechanism. Since HER2 is an endocytosis-resistant receptor, internalization likely occurs via a non-selective mechanism such as macropinocytosis. Internalized SSHELs were trafficked to acidic organelles, whereupon the membrane-permeable dox cargo was released and delivered to the cytosol. This mechanism is similar to that of the antibody-drug conjugate Trastuzumab emtansine. Our results suggest that we can merge the benefits of SSHELs with the efficacy of first-line therapies and raises the possible benefit of bystander killing where membrane-permeable drug is released from a killed cell. Moreover, a benefit we observed from targeted drug delivery was a reduction in overall toxicity, which could permit additional rounds of therapy or higher drug dosing.

Myriad strategies employing micro- and nanoparticles and even live bacteria as drug delivery vehicles have been proposed for the specific killing of cancerous cells. SSHELs provide several benefits and may be used as an additional strategy as they are easily modified to suit different needs. First, SSHELs are composed of defined and biologically compatible materials. Second, SSHELs are assembled in a straightforward manufacturing process. Third, low-pH-triggered cargo release upon trafficking to acidic organelles provides a cue for drug release at the correct place and time. Furthermore, the porous core can accept numerous molecule types to vary the delivered therapeutic agent. Since the size of SSHELs is determined by the silica bead, SSHEL size may be easily tuned without changing chemical characteristics. Fourth, SSHEL-encased dox reduced toxicity, with reduced skin descaling and weight loss. Fifth, active targeting of SSHELs to specific cell types instead of passive diffusion to a tumor may be advantageous, as de novo resistance to antibody-based treatment can arise via compensatory mechanisms that activate other RTKs. We therefore envision that the SSHELs represent a versatile and safe “microparticle” whose targeting specificity, size, and cargo may be modified to suit a specific application.

Limitations of the study

The use of a subcutaneous tumor model required defining additional endpoints for the survival curve in Trial 3 that required termination for ethical reasons (ulcerated tumors due to skin descaling) in addition to simply monitoring tumor burden. A future study will therefore address the efficacy of SSHEL-mediated doxorubicin delivery in an intraperitoneal or intrabursal murine model of ovarian cancer. In this study, the xenograft model required immunocompromised mice. Although we showed that SSHELs did not promote an inflammatory response or display toxicity toward immune cells, future studies will be performed in fully immunocompetent mice.

METHOD DETAILS

Protein purification and labeling

His6-SpoIVA harboring a single engineered N-terminal Cys was produced in E. coli “ClearColi” BL21(DE3) pJP120, which expresses a modified LPS that does not trigger endotoxic response, and purified using Ni+2 affinity chromatography and ion-exchange chromatography as described previously. For click chemistry conjugation, SpoIVA was labeled with 10-fold molar excess of trans-cyclooctene-PEG3-Maleimide (Click Chemistry Tools) overnight at 4°C, after which the excess reagent was removed by Zeba Spin Desalting Column (Thermo Scientific). Anti-HER2 affibody molecule (Ab31889) was purchased from Abcam and labeled with Methyltetrazine-PEG4-maleimide. For labeling Ab31889 with fluorophore Alexa 647, the affibody was incubated with 3-fold molar excess of Alexa 647 Succinimidyl Ester (Life Technologies) for 1 h at room temperature and desalted with Zeba Spin Desalting Column prior to labeling with Methyltetrazine.

SSLB preparation

Liposomes were produced by the liquid sonication method using 200 μL (25 mg/mL) of 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DOPC, Avanti), that were first evaporated under vacuum for 3 h at room temperature and hydrated in 1 mL 0.5X PBS as described previously. Briefly, resuspended lipids were subjected to five freeze-thaw cycles between ethanol-dry ice bath and 42°C water bath, followed by bath sonication until the suspension became transparent. Debris was removed by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 10 min, and the supernatant containing small unilamellar vesicles was retained. One micron-diameter mesoporous silica beads (pore size of 10 nm) (Glantreo) were prepared for coating by washing three times each in 1 mL ultrapure water, followed by methanol and 1M NaOH. Beads were then rinsed and resuspended in ultrapure water (12.5 mg/mL). SSLBs were constructed by mixing 200 μL beads with 200 μL prepared liposomes at room temperature for 90 min with occasional pipetting. SSLBs were collected by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 2 min, washed three times with 0.5X PBS and resuspended in 200 μL 0.5 X PBS.

SSHEL assembly

SSHELs were made largely as described previously. Briefly, synthesized SpoVM peptide (Biomatik Corp.) was incubated at 10 μM (final concentration) with 2.5 mg/mL SSLBs in 0.5X PBS, at room temperature for 3 h with occasional pipetting. SpoVM-coated SSLBs (1 mg) were collected by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 2 min and incubated with ~1.5 μM trans-cyclooctene-labeled SpoIVA in a final volume of 400 μL PBS containing 10 mM MgCl2 and 4 mM ATP, overnight at room temperature with gentle inversion in a darkened test tube. To make SSHELαHER2, SSHELs were collected by centrifugation, resuspended in 400 μL PBS containing 1.5 μM methyltetrazine-labeled Ab31889, and incubated overnight at room temperature with gentle inversion. SSHELαHER2 were collected by centrifugation, washed thrice with PBS, and stored in PBS at 4°C. Fluorescence microscopy images of SSHELs were obtained using Delta Vision Core microscope with a Photometrics CoolSnap HQ2 camera (Applied Precision) as described previously. For flow cytometry and confocal microscopy assays, 2 mg SSHELs were labeled with 10 μM of Alexa 488 Succinimidyl Ester (Life Technologies) for 1 h at room temperature prior to conjugation with affibodies. To estimate the copy number of Ab31889 conjugated onto SSHELs, the degree of labeling of Alexa 488 to Ab31889 was first estimated using absorbance measurements at 280 nm and 495 nm, and a correction factor of 0.11 for the Alexa Fluor 488 dye (Molecular Probes). The median fluorescence intensity (MFI) of SSHEL particles containing AlexaFluor488-labeled Ab31889 was measured using flow cytometry (FACSCanto II, BD Biosciences) and quantified using BD FACSDiva software. The MFIs were converted to molecules of equivalent soluble fluorochrome (MESF) of the SSHELs via a standard curve relating the fluorescence peaks of commercial Quantum Alexa Fluor 488 beads to their MESF values (Bangs Laboratories, Inc.); the estimated fluorophore to protein ratio (~0.25) was used for calculating the Ab31889 copy number. All analyses were performed using FlowJo software (version 10.8.1; Becton Dickinson & Company).

Drug loading

Mesoporous silica beads (25 mg) were soaked in 200 μL doxorubicin hydrochloride (5 mM in water, Sigma) for 1 h at room temperature. Excess drug was removed via centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 1 min and discarding the supernatant. After loading, targeted SSHELs were constructed as described above. To determine the capacity of SSHELs for doxorubicin, 1 × 108 doxorubicin-loaded SSHELαHER2 were incubated in 1% SDS (dissolved in 1 mL PBS) for 24 h with inverting at 37°C. After centrifugation (13,000 × g, 2 min), the fluorescence intensity of doxorubicin (excitation, 470 nm; emission, 585 nm) in the supernatant was measured using a fluorescence microplate reader (SPARK, Tecan) and the concentration of doxorubicin in the supernatant was calculated using a standard curve.

Cell binding assay

Cells grown in T-75 flasks (Corning) to 70–80% confluence were harvested with 10 mM EDTA in PBS for 15 min at 37°C. Cells were washed twice with Iscove’s Modified Dulbecco’s Medium (IMDM, Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS by centrifugation at 1,000 × g for 2 min and resuspended in IMDM/FBS. Increasing ratios (multiplicities of incubation ranging from 20 to 150) of SSHELαHER2 (labeled with Alexa Fluor 647) were incubated with 5 × 105 cells/mL in siliconized tubes (G-tubes, Bio Plas Inc.) for 1 h at 37°C with gentle inversion. Cells were then washed with PBS once, resuspended in 1 mL PBS, and examined by flow cytometry (FACSCanto II, BD Biosciences). All data analysis was performed using FlowJo software (Tree Star). For confocal live cell imaging, 2.5 × 105 cells were seeded in poly-d-lysine-coated 35 mm glass-bottom dishes (MatTek) in 2 mL complete growth medium. After 24 h cells were co-incubated with 5 × 107 SSHELs αHER2 labeled with Alexa Fluor 488 for 1 h at 37°C. Cells were then washed twice with PBS, once with OptiMEM (Gibco), and stained with Hoechst 33,342 (1 μg/mL in OptiMEM) for 30 min at 37°C. Samples were washed three times with PBS and visualized using confocal microscopy (Zeiss LSM780).

Confocal microscopy

SKOV3 cells were plated on 35 mm glass-bottom dishes (MatTek) at a density of 2 × 105 cells in 2 mL complete growth medium and incubated for 24 h. Cells were co-incubated with 5 × 107 Alexa 488-labeled SSHELs αHER2 for indicated time periods, washed twice with PBS, once with OptiMEM (Gibco), and stained with Hoechst 33,342 (1 μg/mL in OptiMEM) for 30 min at 37°C. After washing once with OptiMEM, the acidic organelles of cells were stained with 300 nM LysoTracker red DND-99 (Invitrogen) for 30 min at 37°C. Cells were washed three times with PBS and visualized under a confocal microscope (Zeiss LSM780).

In vitro drug release assay

To evaluate the doxorubicin release kinetics, 1 × 108 doxorubicin-loaded SSHELsαHER2 were resuspended in 1 mL of physiological buffer (PBS with 10% FBS, pH 7.4) or 0.1 M citrate buffer (pH 5.0) and incubated at 37°C with inverting. At defined time points, particles were removed via centrifugation (13,000 × g, 2 min) and fluorescence intensity of the supernatant was measured using a microplate reader. The concentration of released doxorubicin was converted into a percentage of the doxorubicin that was initially encapsulated within 108 particles.

Immunotoxicity and flow cytometry

Cell-specific cargo delivery using synthetic bacterial spores

SUMMARY

Delivery of cancer therapeutics to non-specific sites decreases treatment efficacy while increasing toxicity. In ovarian cancer, overexpression of the cell surface marker HER2, which several therapeutics target, relates to poor prognosis. We recently reported the assembly of biocompatible bacterial spore-like particles, termed “SSHELs.” Here, we modify SSHELs with an affibody directed against HER2 and load them with the chemotherapeutic agent doxorubicin. Drug-loaded SSHELs reduce tumor growth and increase survival with lower toxicity in a mouse tumor xenograft model compared with free drug and with liposomal doxorubicin by preferentially accumulating in the tumor mass. Target cells actively internalize and then traffic bound SSHELs to acidic compartments, whereupon the cargo is released to the cytosol in a pH-dependent manner. We propose that SSHELs represent a versatile strategy for targeted drug delivery, especially in cancer settings.

INTRODUCTION

The efficacy of cancer chemotherapeutics is abrogated by delivery to non-specific sites, which increases toxicity. To diminish this problem, many nanometer-scale vehicles have been devised to encase cargo. However, these strategies usually rely on passive particle accumulation near tumors via abnormal vasculature, which can be unreliable and result in overall toxicity, necessitating other strategies that actively deliver cargo to specific tissues.

We previously reported assembly of synthetic bacterial sporelike particles called “SSHELs” (synthetic spore husk-encased lipid bilayers). Spores of Bacillus subtilis are surrounded by a protein “coat,” built atop two structural proteins: a membrane anchor SpoVM and a structural ATPase SpoIVA that polymerizes. We reconstituted these proteins on membranes supported by silica beads and, by employing an engineered SpoIVA, covalently modified SSHELs to display molecules of interest.

In many epithelial cancers, receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs) are mutated or overexpressed. One RTK, human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), facilitates normal proliferation of ovaries, lung, breast, liver, kidney, and central nervous tissue. However, in ~25% of breast cancers it is overexpressed, leading to aberrant growth and cell signaling; in ovarian cancers, HER2 overexpression is related to poor prognosis. Monoclonal antibodies targeting HER2 effectively treat breast, but not ovarian, cancers where platinum-based therapy is preferred. To treat drug-resistant ovarian cancer, doxorubicin (dox), which promotes DNA doublestrand breaks, is used. However, dox causes side effects like hair loss, bone marrow suppression, and vomiting. Moreover, results of treatment with dox are often poor, likely due to reduced tissue penetrance.

To target ovarian cancers overexpressing HER2, we designed dox-loaded SSHELs built atop 1-μm mesoporous silica beads decorated with an affibody that binds HER2. These particles reduced tumor growth in a mouse tumor xenograft model of HER2+ ovarian cancer and improved survival compared with an equivalent dose of liposomal dox (the preferred dox delivery method in the clinic) with reduced toxicity. Mechanistically, SSHELs selectively bound to, and were internalized by, HER2+ cells and then trafficked to acidified compartments, whereupon reduced pH triggered cargo release and subsequent translocation of the membrane-permeable dox into the target cell cytosol. We propose that SSHELs are a versatile therapeutic vehicle displaying receptor-specific cargo release whose targeting specificity and cargo are easily modified.

RESULTS

Dox-encased SSHELs targeting HER2 inhibit tumor growth

To encase cargo, we soaked mesoporous silica beads in a solution of dox (Figure 1A) and applied a phosphatidylcholine bilayer, the major membrane-forming lipid in eukaryotes but largely absent in bacteria. Next, we applied synthesized SpoVM and added purified SpoIVA (harboring a single Cys to allow modification with trans-cyclooctene, TCO) in the presence of ATP to promote polymerization, resulting in stable SSHELs. The solid core was required because, in the absence of a support, polymerized SpoIVA deforms the underlying vesicle, similar to membranecoating proteins in other systems.

To target HER2, we covalently attached to the surface of TCO-modified SSHELs a tetrazine-conjugated affibody that binds to HER2 (Figure 1A). Fluorescence microscopy revealed that labeled affibody uniformly coated the SSHELs (Figure 1B). Quantitative flow cytometry indicated that each SSHEL displayed 94,000 ± 9,600 anti-HER2 affibodies (Figure 1C; n = 3 experiments; ~10,000 particles). Fluorescence microscopy of dox-loaded SSHELs (Dox-SSHEL) constructed using labeled SpoIVA indicated that dox was retained in the center of the particles after SpoIVA polymerized around the periphery (Figure 1D). Extraction of encased dox and measurement of its intrinsic fluorescence revealed that each SSHEL contained 1.5 × 108 ± 1.4 × 107 dox molecules.

We next injected female athymic nude mice subcutaneously in the flank with SKOV3 cells, a HER2+ ovarian cancer cell line. When tumors reached ~100 mm3, mice were treated intermittently with PBS, free dox (3 mg/kg), dox-encased SSHELs displaying the affibody (Dox-SSHELαHER2, using equivalent dox dose), or empty SSHELαHER2. We calculated mean tumor size for each group until the first mouse was sacrificed, according to endpoints set in the animal protocol (tumor size or ulceration, weight loss, or general distress). In PBS-treated mice, mean tumor size (n = 7) reached ~1,200 mm3 in 57 days, whereas treatment with dox or SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~800 mm3 (Figures 1E and S1C). However, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~300 mm3 (Trial 1, Figure 1E). At this dox concentration, all treatment groups showed no signs of toxicity or body weight loss (Figure S1A). We then repeated the experiment using 6 mg/kg of dox (Trial 2), where mean tumor size (n = 7) in PBS-treated mice reached ~1,000 mm3 in 40 days (Figure 1F), and 4/7 tumors displayed ulceration and 2/7 tumors were larger than 1,500 mm3 (Figure S1D). Treatment with dox or SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~600 mm3, but 4/7 tumors from dox-treated mice were ulcerated, and one mouse experienced >10% loss in body weight. Additionally, dox-treated mice displayed pale skin (5/7) and tissue damage at the injection site (3/7). In mice treated with SSHELαHER2, we observed ulcerated tumors (3/7), and one tumor that was larger than 1,500 mm3. In contrast, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced mean tumor size to ~300 mm3 at day 40 (Figure 1F); none of the mice displayed weight loss (Figure S1B), and no tumors were ulcerated (Figure S1D). Administration of dox via SSHELαHER2 therefore enhanced therapeutic efficacy while reducing the toxicity of free dox.

Reduced toxicity from Dox-SSHELαHER2 compared with liposomal dox

We next compared the efficacy of targeted dox delivery with liposome-encased dox (commercially known as “Doxil”), a passive delivery system that is used in clinical settings. In Trials 3 and 4, we intermittently treated female athymic nude mice harboring subcutaneous SKOV3 tumors with PBS, Dox-SSHELαHER2, or liposomal dox (3 mg/kg of dox). Since administration of SSHELαHER2 modestly reduced tumor size (Figures 1E and 1F), we also tested co-administering SSHELαHER2 with external dox (SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox). In Trial 3, mean tumor size of PBS-treated mice reached ~2,200 mm3 in 58 days, whereas coadministration with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox reduced mean tumor size only to ~1,500 mm3 (Figure 2Ai). Similarly, Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced tumor size to ~1600 mm3, but treatment with liposomal dox reduced mean tumor size to ~800 mm3 after 58 days. However, mice treated with liposomal dox or Dox-SSHELαHER2 displayed different survival outcomes (Figure 2Bi), with 8/12 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 surviving after 135 days (one cannibalized by cage mates), and only 2/12 mice treated with liposomal dox surviving after 135 days. The endpoints for this experiment included tumor growth (>1,400 mm3), tumor ulceration, weight loss (>15%), and general discomfort. The major difference between the survival curves was due to toxicity that liposomal dox-treated mice experienced: at day 42, 8/12 mice displayed severe skin descaling that led to tumor ulceration, which required sacrificing these mice (Figure 2C). Two additional mice experienced tumor ulceration without skin descaling, and mice in this group experienced >15% weight loss (Figure S1E). In contrast, no mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 exhibited skin descaling or weight loss, and only 3/12 mice displayed ulcerated tumors (Figures 2C and S1E). We then repeated this experiment but eliminated tumor ulceration as an endpoint (“Trial 4”). Mice receiving liposomal dox were treated with an antibiotic-containing ointment to alleviate skin descaling. In Trial 4, mean tumor size of PBS-treated mice reached ~800 mm3 in 58 days; mice treated with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox similarly reached a mean tumor size of ~825 mm3 (Figure 2Aii). However, treatments with liposomal dox and Dox-SSHELαHER2 reduced tumor size, with mean tumor sizes of ~600 mm3 and ~450 mm3, respectively. 5/10 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 survived after 86 days, compared with only 2/10 mice treated with liposomal dox, despite removing tumor ulceration as an endpoint (Figure 2Bii). Again, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 or liposomal dox displayed dissimilar toxicity. 12/12 mice treated with liposomal dox showed skin descaling, with ulcerated tumors in 10/12 mice (Figure 2C), but only 5/12 mice treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2 displayed skin descaling, with ulcerated tumors in 2/12 mice. In this trial, no mice experienced >15% weight loss, but by day 78, several mice treated with liposomal dox displayed decreasing weight (Figure S1F). We conclude that treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 increases survival probability with fewer toxicities, compared with liposomal dox treatment.

Next, we measured biodistribution of SSHELs and performed histopathology of internal organs. We injected three tumorbearing mice (independent from mice used in Trials 3–4) with SSHELαHER2 whose lipid was labeled with a fluorescent dye. 24 h post-injection, we harvested and disrupted select organs and examined fluorescence of the resulting cell suspensions by flow cytometry. Tumors isolated from all mice displayed increased fluorescence (Figure 2D), indicating enrichment of SSHELαHER2 at the HER2+ tumor. We observed reduced association of SSHELαHER2 in isolated kidney (5-fold less compared with tumor), liver (7-fold), heart (35-fold), spleen (81-fold), brain, and lungs (no signal detected; Figures 2D and 2E). Additionally, H&E-stained samples from skin, colon, spleen, kidneys, heart, lungs and brain, and lymph nodes and pancreas present within pulmonary or colon sections, respectively, failed to demonstrate any cumulative histo-morphologic abnormalities from mice in Trial 4, after 13 rounds of treatment. Sections of livers were unremarkable in 6/8 mice analyzed. Two mice demonstrated microgranuloma consistent with silica aggregates, which has been previously reported. One, treated with Dox-SSHELαHER2, demonstrated three microgranuloma on three liver cross-sections, and another, treated with SSHELαHER2 + ext. Dox, demonstrated a single microgranuloma on three cross-sections (Figures S2A–S2Dii). Finally, PBS-treated mice demonstrated prominent tumor cell mitosis and overall less inflammatory response, but tumors from Dox-SSHELαHER2-treated mice demonstrated fewer mitosis and increased neutrophils associated with the tumor necrosis (Figures S2E–S2H).

We previously reported that immunocompetent mice injected intramuscularly with SSHELs produced anti-SpoIVA antibodies but no memory T cell response and subsequently tolerated multiple injections with no obvious toxicity. However, SSHELs in that study did not contain dox. Since the xenograft model reported here required athymic mice, we tested the immunotoxicity of Dox-SSHELαHER2 ex vivo. Incubating purified mouse splenocytes with SSHELs αHER2 did not reduce splenocyte viability (Figure S3A). However, free dox displayed dose-dependent toxicity, which was partially alleviated when the cells were treated with an equivalent amount of Dox-SSHELs αHER2 (Figure S3A). Additionally, Dox-SSHELs αHER2 and SSHELsαHER2 did not appreciably stimulate production of TNFα by CD4+ cells, nor IFNγ or granzyme B by CD8+ cells isolated from mouse splenocytes or human PBMCs (Figures S3B–S3G).

We conclude that targeted dox delivery via SSHELs reduced toxicity and increased survival probability in a mouse tumor xenograft model compared with liposomal dox with reduced immunotoxicity. Although naked silica particles may be cytotoxic, our results with SSHELs are consistent with observations that surface modifications can mitigate toxicity.

Dox-SSHELαHER2 specifically binds to and kills HER2+ cells

Antibody treatment directs HER2 for internal degradation. To test the mechanism for SSHEL-delivered drugs, we measured binding of labeled SSHELαHER2 to two HER2+ cell lines: SKOV3 used above and SKBR3 (breast cancer) and compared it to two HER2− breast cancer cell lines (MDA-MB-231 and MCF7). SSHELs and cells were incubated at different multiplicities of incubation (MOI) and analyzed using flow cytometry. With increasing SSHELαHER2 MOI, SKBR3 and SKOV3 cells displayed increased fluorescence (Figures 3A and 3B), whereas MDA-MB-231 and MCF7 did not (Figures 3C and 3D), suggesting preferential binding. To test if binding was saturable, we first incubated the cells with a 1000-fold excess of unlabeled anti-HER2 affibody, which blocked the binding of SSHELαHER2 at MOI = 100 (Figures 3E and 3F), indicating that SSHEL binding was mediated by HER2. Specificity of HER2 binding was qualitatively confirmed using confocal microscopy (Figures 3G–3I).

We next examined if SSHELαHER2 could bind target cells in a complex tumor microenvironment by employing a zebrafish brain metastasis model that permits direct in vivo visualization of SSHELαHER2 and individual tumor cells using fluorescence microscopy. Zebrafish were injected in the mid-brain parenchyma with SKBR3 cells with labeled lysosomes (green, Figures 3M–M”). Labeled SSHELαHER2 (pink, Figures 3J–3J” and 3L–3L”) were then intraparenchymaly injected at a second site 4 h later at MOI = 100 and imaged by fluorescence microscopy after 30 min. At low magnification, injected cells colocalized with SSHELαHER2 in the hindbrain (Figures 3J–3M). At intermediate magnification, individual injected cells displayed SSHELαHER2 fluorescence around the periphery (Figures 3J–M’). At the highest magnification, we could discern an individual SKBR3 cell with multiple surface-bound SSHELαHER2 (Figures 3J”–3M”).

We next measured cytotoxic specificity of Dox-SSHELαHER2. Incubation of the two HER2+ cell lines with Dox-SSHELαHER2 resulted in dose-dependent cell death, whereas viability of the two HER2− cell lines was either unaffected (MCF7) or reduced to ~65% at MOI = 300 (MDA-MB-231; Figure 3N), corresponding to half maximal inhibitory concentrations (IC50) of 41 nM (SKBR3) and 86 nM (SKOV3), and comparable to the IC50 of free dox for both cell lines (56 nM for SKBR3; 73 nM for SKOV3; Figure 3O). Free dox reduced the viability of HER2− cells and displayed an IC50 of 96 nM for MDA-MB-231 and 135 nM MCF7 cells, whereas SSHELs or the affibody alone did not reduce viability of any cell line (Figure 3P and 3Q). Incubating cells with dox-loaded SSHELs displaying bovine serum albumin (Dox-SSHELBSA) showed reduced, but indiscriminate, cytotoxicity against all cell lines tested (Figure 3R), with an IC50 value ~3.3-fold (SKBR3) or ~2.3-fold (SKOV3) higher than that of Dox-SSHELαHER2. Lastly, incubation with SSHELαHER2 reduced viability of HER2+ cells only at the highest MOI (Figure 3S), and less effectively than Dox-SSHELαHER2, suggesting that combined action of encapsulated dox directed by the particles via the affibody was required to exert full anti-proliferative activity toward HER2+ cells.

Treatment with dox induces apoptosis, which we monitored by measuring caspase 3/7 activity of HER2+ cells. Treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 or equivalent dose of dox (120 nM), but not SSHELαHER2, induced caspase activity of SKBR3 cells over time (Figure 3T), albeit with differing kinetics, suggesting different uptake rates between free dox and SSHELαHER2. For SKOV3, 120 nM dox did not induce caspase activity (Figure 3U), but annexin V staining indicated that these cells were undergoing apoptosis (Figure S4). In contrast, treatment with Dox-SSHELαHER2 induced both caspase activity (Figure 3U) and increased annexin V staining (Figure S4).

Internalization and trafficking to acidic organelles releases Dox-SSHELαHER2 cargo

To determine the fate of bound SSHELs, we incubated SKOV3 with labeled SSHELαHER2 and monitored colocalization with acidic organelles (visualized with LysoTracker red) using confocal microscopy. At 1 h, SSHELαHER2 were located at the cell periphery (Figure 4A). At 4 h, SSHELαHER2 began co-localizing with the dye, indicating particle internalization and trafficking to acidic compartments. By 48 h, the majority of cell-associated SSHELαHER2 colocalized with acidic compartments. Interestingly, we also observed colocalization of internalized SSHELαHER2 with lysosomes in zebrafish harboring SKBR3 in the hindbrain (Figure 3M”, arrowhead), suggesting that particle uptake by target cells occurs in vivo as well.

To determine the mechanism of particle uptake, we incubated labeled SSHELαHER2 with SKOV3 treated with latrunculin A (actin inhibitor) or MiTMAB (dynamin I and II inhibitor) and measured internalization using flow cytometry. MiTMAB reduced SSHELαHER2 uptake by ~50% (Figure 4B), and latrunculin A reduced SSHELαHER2 uptake by ~70%; cells treated with both displayed minimal particle uptake, indicating that SSHEL uptake requires an active internalization process, likely macropinocytosis.

We next monitored the destiny of dox after Dox-SSHELαHER2 uptake using fluorescence microscopy by taking advantage of the intrinsic fluorescence of dox. At 1 h, dox appeared as puncta on the cell periphery, consistent with the surface attachment of SSHEL-encased dox (Figure 4C). After 24 h, we observed fewer peripheral dox puncta, and increased cytosolic distribution of dox, suggesting that SSHEL-encased dox was released into the cytosol.

To test if exposure to reduced pH liberates SSHEL-encased cargo, we incubated Dox-SSHEL in either simulated serum (pH 7.4) or simulated endosomal conditions (pH 5.0) and monitored dox release over time. In acidic pH, encased dox was released with a half-life of 4 h (Figure 4D). In contrast, at pH 7.4, dox release was slower, with more than 50% of the drug remaining associated with SSHEL even after 92 h, suggesting that SSHELs can be destabilized in acidic conditions that simulate endosome trafficking.

DISCUSSION

The efficacy of targeting antibodies to HER2 occurs via three possible mechanisms: inhibition of HER2 extracellular domain shedding leading to internal degradation, reducing binding sites for signaling ligands, and stimulation of immune-regulated cellular cytotoxicity in breast cancer. Trastuzumab (Herceptin) functions via the first mechanism. However, SSHELs bound to surface HER2 were internalized via an active mechanism. Since HER2 is an endocytosis-resistant receptor, internalization likely occurs via a non-selective mechanism such as macropinocytosis. Internalized SSHELs were trafficked to acidic organelles, whereupon the membrane-permeable dox cargo was released and delivered to the cytosol. This mechanism is similar to that of the antibody-drug conjugate Trastuzumab emtansine. Our results suggest that we can merge the benefits of SSHELs with the efficacy of first-line therapies and raises the possible benefit of bystander killing where membrane-permeable drug is released from a killed cell. Moreover, a benefit we observed from targeted drug delivery was a reduction in overall toxicity, which could permit additional rounds of therapy or higher drug dosing.

Myriad strategies employing micro- and nanoparticles and even live bacteria as drug delivery vehicles have been proposed for the specific killing of cancerous cells. SSHELs provide several benefits and may be used as an additional strategy as they are easily modified to suit different needs. First, SSHELs are composed of defined and biologically compatible materials. Second, SSHELs are assembled in a straightforward manufacturing process. Third, low-pH-triggered cargo release upon trafficking to acidic organelles provides a cue for drug release at the correct place and time. Furthermore, the porous core can accept numerous molecule types to vary the delivered therapeutic agent. Since the size of SSHELs is determined by the silica bead, SSHEL size may be easily tuned without changing chemical characteristics. Fourth, SSHEL-encased dox reduced toxicity, with reduced skin descaling and weight loss. Fifth, active targeting of SSHELs to specific cell types instead of passive diffusion to a tumor may be advantageous, as de novo resistance to antibody-based treatment can arise via compensatory mechanisms that activate other RTKs. We therefore envision that the SSHELs represent a versatile and safe “microparticle” whose targeting specificity, size, and cargo may be modified to suit a specific application.

Limitations of the study

The use of a subcutaneous tumor model required defining additional endpoints for the survival curve in Trial 3 that required termination for ethical reasons (ulcerated tumors due to skin descaling) in addition to simply monitoring tumor burden. A future study will therefore address the efficacy of SSHEL-mediated doxorubicin delivery in an intraperitoneal or intrabursal murine model of ovarian cancer. In this study, the xenograft model required immunocompromised mice. Although we showed that SSHELs did not promote an inflammatory response or display toxicity toward immune cells, future studies will be performed in fully immunocompetent mice.

STAR★METHODS

METHOD DETAILS

Protein purification and labeling

His6-SpoIVA harboring a single engineered N-terminal Cys was produced in E. coli “ClearColi” BL21(DE3) pJP120, which expresses a modified LPS that does not trigger endotoxic response, and purified using Ni+2 affinity chromatography and ion-exchange chromatography as described previously. For click chemistry conjugation, SpoIVA was labeled with 10-fold molar excess of trans-cyclooctene-PEG3-Maleimide (Click Chemistry Tools) overnight at 4°C, after which the excess reagent was removed by Zeba Spin Desalting Column (Thermo Scientific). Anti-HER2 affibody molecule (Ab31889) was purchased from Abcam and labeled with Methyltetrazine-PEG4-maleimide. For labeling Ab31889 with fluorophore Alexa 647, the affibody was incubated with 3-fold molar excess of Alexa 647 Succinimidyl Ester (Life Technologies) for 1 h at room temperature and desalted with Zeba Spin Desalting Column prior to labeling with Methyltetrazine.

SSLB preparation

Liposomes were produced by the liquid sonication method using 200 μL (25 mg/mL) of 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DOPC, Avanti), that were first evaporated under vacuum for 3 h at room temperature and hydrated in 1 mL 0.5X PBS as described previously. Briefly, resuspended lipids were subjected to five freeze-thaw cycles between ethanol-dry ice bath and 42°C water bath, followed by bath sonication until the suspension became transparent. Debris was removed by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 10 min, and the supernatant containing small unilamellar vesicles was retained. One micron-diameter mesoporous silica beads (pore size of 10 nm) (Glantreo) were prepared for coating by washing three times each in 1 mL ultrapure water, followed by methanol and 1M NaOH. Beads were then rinsed and resuspended in ultrapure water (12.5 mg/mL). SSLBs were constructed by mixing 200 μL beads with 200 μL prepared liposomes at room temperature for 90 min with occasional pipetting. SSLBs were collected by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 2 min, washed three times with 0.5X PBS and resuspended in 200 μL 0.5 X PBS.

SSHEL assembly

SSHELs were made largely as described previously. Briefly, synthesized SpoVM peptide (Biomatik Corp.) was incubated at 10 μM (final concentration) with 2.5 mg/mL SSLBs in 0.5X PBS, at room temperature for 3 h with occasional pipetting. SpoVM-coated SSLBs (1 mg) were collected by centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 2 min and incubated with ~1.5 μM trans-cyclooctene-labeled SpoIVA in a final volume of 400 μL PBS containing 10 mM MgCl2 and 4 mM ATP, overnight at room temperature with gentle inversion in a darkened test tube. To make SSHELαHER2, SSHELs were collected by centrifugation, resuspended in 400 μL PBS containing 1.5 μM methyltetrazine-labeled Ab31889, and incubated overnight at room temperature with gentle inversion. SSHELαHER2 were collected by centrifugation, washed thrice with PBS, and stored in PBS at 4°C. Fluorescence microscopy images of SSHELs were obtained using Delta Vision Core microscope with a Photometrics CoolSnap HQ2 camera (Applied Precision) as described previously. For flow cytometry and confocal microscopy assays, 2 mg SSHELs were labeled with 10 μM of Alexa 488 Succinimidyl Ester (Life Technologies) for 1 h at room temperature prior to conjugation with affibodies. To estimate the copy number of Ab31889 conjugated onto SSHELs, the degree of labeling of Alexa 488 to Ab31889 was first estimated using absorbance measurements at 280 nm and 495 nm, and a correction factor of 0.11 for the Alexa Fluor 488 dye (Molecular Probes). The median fluorescence intensity (MFI) of SSHEL particles containing AlexaFluor488-labeled Ab31889 was measured using flow cytometry (FACSCanto II, BD Biosciences) and quantified using BD FACSDiva software. The MFIs were converted to molecules of equivalent soluble fluorochrome (MESF) of the SSHELs via a standard curve relating the fluorescence peaks of commercial Quantum Alexa Fluor 488 beads to their MESF values (Bangs Laboratories, Inc.); the estimated fluorophore to protein ratio (~0.25) was used for calculating the Ab31889 copy number. All analyses were performed using FlowJo software (version 10.8.1; Becton Dickinson & Company).

Drug loading

Mesoporous silica beads (25 mg) were soaked in 200 μL doxorubicin hydrochloride (5 mM in water, Sigma) for 1 h at room temperature. Excess drug was removed via centrifugation at 13,000 × g for 1 min and discarding the supernatant. After loading, targeted SSHELs were constructed as described above. To determine the capacity of SSHELs for doxorubicin, 1 × 108 doxorubicin-loaded SSHELαHER2 were incubated in 1% SDS (dissolved in 1 mL PBS) for 24 h with inverting at 37°C. After centrifugation (13,000 × g, 2 min), the fluorescence intensity of doxorubicin (excitation, 470 nm; emission, 585 nm) in the supernatant was measured using a fluorescence microplate reader (SPARK, Tecan) and the concentration of doxorubicin in the supernatant was calculated using a standard curve.

Cell binding assay

Cells grown in T-75 flasks (Corning) to 70–80% confluence were harvested with 10 mM EDTA in PBS for 15 min at 37°C. Cells were washed twice with Iscove’s Modified Dulbecco’s Medium (IMDM, Gibco) supplemented with 10% FBS by centrifugation at 1,000 × g for 2 min and resuspended in IMDM/FBS. Increasing ratios (multiplicities of incubation ranging from 20 to 150) of SSHELαHER2 (labeled with Alexa Fluor 647) were incubated with 5 × 105 cells/mL in siliconized tubes (G-tubes, Bio Plas Inc.) for 1 h at 37°C with gentle inversion. Cells were then washed with PBS once, resuspended in 1 mL PBS, and examined by flow cytometry (FACSCanto II, BD Biosciences). All data analysis was performed using FlowJo software (Tree Star). For confocal live cell imaging, 2.5 × 105 cells were seeded in poly-d-lysine-coated 35 mm glass-bottom dishes (MatTek) in 2 mL complete growth medium. After 24 h cells were co-incubated with 5 × 107 SSHELs αHER2 labeled with Alexa Fluor 488 for 1 h at 37°C. Cells were then washed twice with PBS, once with OptiMEM (Gibco), and stained with Hoechst 33,342 (1 μg/mL in OptiMEM) for 30 min at 37°C. Samples were washed three times with PBS and visualized using confocal microscopy (Zeiss LSM780).

Confocal microscopy

SKOV3 cells were plated on 35 mm glass-bottom dishes (MatTek) at a density of 2 × 105 cells in 2 mL complete growth medium and incubated for 24 h. Cells were co-incubated with 5 × 107 Alexa 488-labeled SSHELs αHER2 for indicated time periods, washed twice with PBS, once with OptiMEM (Gibco), and stained with Hoechst 33,342 (1 μg/mL in OptiMEM) for 30 min at 37°C. After washing once with OptiMEM, the acidic organelles of cells were stained with 300 nM LysoTracker red DND-99 (Invitrogen) for 30 min at 37°C. Cells were washed three times with PBS and visualized under a confocal microscope (Zeiss LSM780).

In vitro drug release assay

To evaluate the doxorubicin release kinetics, 1 × 108 doxorubicin-loaded SSHELsαHER2 were resuspended in 1 mL of physiological buffer (PBS with 10% FBS, pH 7.4) or 0.1 M citrate buffer (pH 5.0) and incubated at 37°C with inverting. At defined time points, particles were removed via centrifugation (13,000 × g, 2 min) and fluorescence intensity of the supernatant was measured using a microplate reader. The concentration of released doxorubicin was converted into a percentage of the doxorubicin that was initially encapsulated within 108 particles.

Immunotoxicity and flow cytometry

Spleens were removed from naive BALB/c mice and were homogenized into a single-cell suspension using Dulbecco’s phosphate buffered saline (Gibco, Grand Island, NY). Red blood cells were lysed by resuspending spleen cells in ACK (Ammonium-Chloride-Potassium) lysing buffer and incubating at 4°C for 10 min. Splenocytes were washed and resuspended in complete medium (RPMI supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated FBS and Glutamax), at a concentration of 2 × 106 cells/mL. Human PBMC were purified using a Ficoll gradient from healthy donor buffy coats (obtained from the NIH Blood Bank). The cells were plated on a 24-well format for a total of 3 × 106 (splenocytes) or 2 × 106 (hPBMC) cells/well. Each well was treated with SSHELαHER2 or DOX-SSHELαHER2 (150 MOI, 5.4 × 108 particles per well), Doxorubicin (equivalent dose). PMA (25 ng/mL)/Ionomycin (1 mg/mL) were used as a positive control. Moreover, each well was treated with Brefeldin A (10 mg/mL), a Golgi trafficking inhibitor used to retain the cytokines inside the cells, that was later detected by flow cytometry. Cells were incubated overnight and then cytokine expression was evaluated with 0.5 × 106 PBMC stained with PE/Cynine7 anti-mouse CD45 (clone 30-F11), Brilliant violet 510™ anti-mouse CD8a (Biolegend), BUV395 Hamster anti-mouse CD3e (clone 145-2C11) and FITC Rat CD4 (clone RM4-5, BD Biosciences). Cytokine expression was evaluated using APC conjugated antibodies: anti-mouse IFN-g (BD Biosciences), Granzyme B (clone QA16A02) and TNF-a (clone MP6-XT22, Biolegend). Viability was assessed using eBioscience™ Fixable viability dye eFluor 780. The data were acquired on FACSymphony-BD Biosciences (SN: R66096400078) and analyzed on FlowJo 10.8.1 (Becton Dickinson & Company). The experiment was performed in three biological replicates.

Cellular viability

Splenocyte viability was determined using a CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay kit (Promega, Madison WI), which quantifies the amount of ATP present, and therefore indicates the presence of metabolically active cells. Briefly, splenocytes were plated in 96-well format at 10,000 cells/well. SSHELαHER2 or DOX-SSHELαHER2 were added at different concentrations (12.5–150 MOI). Cells were treated with an equivalent dose of Doxorubicin. Data are presented as a percentage of control (untreated) cells. Data are three independent replicates in duplicate.

QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

All quantitative data are reported as mean ± SD. For in vitro studies, at least three independent replicates were used. Two tailed unpaired t-test by GraphPad Prism 9.3 was used to compare the differences between groups. A p value of <0.05 was regarded as statistically significant. For in vivo studies, in Trial 1 and Trial 2 a sample value of n = 7 (number of mice) was used. For Trial 3 and Trial 4 a sample value of n = 12 (number of mice) was used. After average tumor size reached 100 mm3 the mice were randomly divided in 4 groups before starting the treatment. Nonparametric Kruskal-Wallis test by GraphPad Prism 9.3 was used to compare the differences between groups. A p value, p < 0.05, was considered as statistically significant.
