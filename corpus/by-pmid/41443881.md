# Chimeric antigen receptor T-cell and bispecific antibody therapies for follicular lymphoma: Recent advances and future prospects.

## Abstract

Therefore, development of treatments for relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma is an urgent issue. The recent introduction of cellular immunotherapy has significantly changed treatment of B-cell malignancies. Clinical development of anti-CD19 chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy and CD20×CD3 bispecific antibody therapy for follicular lymphoma is underway. Multiple studies have also reported the effectiveness of CAR-T cell and bispecific antibody therapies for relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma, and some cellular immunotherapies have already been approved in Japan. Bispecific antibodies, in contrast, have relatively mild toxicities and can be widely used in patients with poor general conditions, including elderly patients, but have problems such as the burden of long-term administration. Optimal patient selection, management of unique toxicities, and high costs remain issues to be resolved for cellular immunotherapy. This review summarizes the recent clinical data on cellular immunotherapy for follicular lymphoma.

INTRODUCTION

Follicular lymphoma (FL) is the second most common lymphoma, comprising about 20% of non-Hodgkin lymphomas, and being one of the most representative indolent B-cell lymphomas. Use of monoclonal anti-CD20 antibody has markedly improved treatment outcomes of patients with FL, but the disease is still considered to be incurable. FL is a slowly progressing disease that can be managed for a relatively long period; however, recurrence is common. Furthermore, patients with early relapses (e.g. progression of disease within 24 months of initial therapy, POD24) have particularly poor outcomes with conventional chemotherapies. Treatment of these high-risk cases of multiple relapsed or refractory (R/R) FL is an area of high unmet need for which newer therapies with stronger efficacy or novel mechanisms of action are required.

Immunotherapy has emerged as a revolutionary strategy in R/R FL. FL cells uniformly express B-cell antigens such as CD20 and CD19, making them ideal targets for immune-directed therapies. Two major immunotherapies, chimeric antigen receptor T cell (CAR-T) therapy and bispecific antibodies (BsAbs), have attracted recent attention. CAR-T therapy uses autologous T lymphocytes that have been genetically engineered to express a CAR that targets a B cell antigen (primarily CD19), and the engineered T cells work by directly recognizing and killing lymphoma cells. In contrast, BsAbs are antibody drugs that simultaneously bind to CD3 on T cells and CD20 on B cells, bringing the T cells and lymphoma cells closer together, thereby producing an immunological antitumor effect. Both approaches can produce deep remission, even in heavily pretreated FL, and have now entered clinical practice. In late-line FL, CAR-T cell therapy and CD20×CD3 BsAbs both give high overall and complete response rates that far exceed those of prior standard options, leading to multiple recent regulatory approvals. This review will discuss the development of CAR-T and BsAbs therapies in FL, including pivotal clinical trial results, toxicity profiles, current approval status, and how these therapies may shape future treatment paradigms in FL.

CAR-T THERAPIES IN FOLLICULAR LYMPHOMA

Three anti-CD19 CAR-T therapies, axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel), tisagenlecleucel (tisa-cel), and lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel), have been investigated in R/R FL. All of these CAR-T cells target CD19, but with different costimulatory domains. Axi-cel uses a CD28 costimulatory domain, whereas tisa-cel and liso-cel incorporate 4-1BB costimulation. These differences are thought to affect the proliferation rates and toxicity profiles of CAR-T therapies.

Axicabtagene ciloleucel (axi-cel)

Axi-cel was the first CAR-T therapy approved for indolent non-Hodgkin lymphoma by the United States (US) Food and Drug Administration (FDA); however, it is only approved for large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL) in Japan. In the phase 2 ZUMA-5 trial, 124 patients with grade 1-3A FL after ≥2 prior regimens (including rituximab and an alkylator) received axi-cel. The median age was 60 years (range: 34–79). Of these patients, 68 (55%) were POD24 patients, and 84 (68%) were refractory to their most recent prior therapy. In the primary analysis, the overall response rate (ORR) was 94% among the 86 evaluable FL cases, and the complete response rate (CRR) was 79%. With extended follow-up of 41.7 months, the ORR remained at 94% and median progression-free survival (PFS) reached 40.2 months. All 124 patients were evaluated for safety: 97 (77%) developed cytokine release syndrome (CRS), of which 8 (6%) had severe CRS of grade ≥3; and 70 (56%) had neurological events, of which 19 (15%) were grade ≥3. Cytopenia and infection were the most common other grade ≥3 adverse events. No new safety concerns emerged with longer follow-up. There are no randomized controlled trials (RCTs) comparing axi-cel with standard therapy for R/R FL, but patients treated in the ZUMA-5 trial had significantly better outcomes than those in a cohort of R/R FL patients receiving conventional chemoimmunotherapy in the SCHOLAR-5 trial.

Tisagenlecleucel (tisa-cel)

The ELARA trial was conducted as an international phase II clinical trial to evaluate the efficacy of tisa-cel in R/R FL. Patients with R/R FL who had received two or more prior regimens or undergone autologous peripheral blood stem cell transplantation were enrolled. Of 97 patients (median age 57 years) who were transfused, 66 (68%) were refractory to rituximab and alkylators (so-called double refractory), and 61 (63%) were POD24 patients. In the primary analysis with median follow-up of 16.59 months, ORR was 86.2% and the CR rate was 69.1% in 94 patients in whom efficacy was evaluable. This met the primary endpoint of the trial, confirming the robust activity of tisa-cel in multiply relapsed FL. An updated 4-year follow-up presented in 2024 had median PFS of 53.3 months in all patients; and a 48-month PFS of 50.2% in all patients and 66.1% in patients with a best overall response of CR, indicating sustained remission in most patients achieving CR. These outcomes are remarkable given that many of the ELARA patients were high-risk. In addition, biomarker analysis of the phenotype of T-cells infiltrating the tumor microenvironment suggested that patients with infiltrating LAG3+ exhausted T cells that were <3% of all CD3+ T cells had longer PFS. In 97 patients in whom safety could be evaluated within 8 weeks of infusion, the rates were CRS 48.5% (grade ≥3, 0%), neurological events 37.1% (grade ≥3, 3%), and immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS) 4.1% (grade ≥3, 1%). There were no treatment-related deaths. The lower incidence and severity of CRS reflect 4-1BB costimulation in tisa-cel, which yields slower T-cell expansion and cytokine release relative to CD28-based constructs. The US FDA approved tisa-cel for R/R FL in May 2022 based on these results. Efficacy was also reported in a Japanese subgroup, and approval was obtained in Japan in August 2022 for R/R FL that had received two or more prior regimens.

Lisocabtagene maraleucel (liso-cel)

Liso-cel is a defined-composition CAR-T product with a 4-1BB costimulatory domain, with a target 1:1 ratio of CD4+ and CD8+ cell components. The phase 2 TRANSCEND-FL study evaluated liso-cel in patients with R/R FL, including a pivotal cohort with ≥2 prior lines (3L+ cohort) and a separate cohort of high-risk second-line patients, i.e., those with POD24 from diagnosis and/or who met modified Groupe d’Etude des Lymphomes Folliculaires criteria. A total of 114 patients with FL who had received at least two prior therapies were enrolled, of whom 107 received liso-cel. The median age was 62 years (range: 23–80), and 23% had FL grade 3A, 43% were POD24 patients, and 64% were double refractory. In 101 patients in which efficacy was evaluable, the ORR was 97% and the CRR was 94%. With a median follow-up period of 17.6 months, the 1-year PFS and OS rates were 81% and 92%, respectively. Notably, even in the high-risk second-line group, the ORR was 96% and all patients achieved CR. In an analysis with a median follow-up period of 17.6 months, the 1-year PFS rate was 91%. Regarding safety, CRS occurred in 58% of patients (grade ≥3, 1%) and there were neurological events in 15% (grade ≥3, 2%). The most common adverse effects were cytopenia and infection. Overall, the toxicity profile of liso-cel appears to be the most benign of the three products. Based on these results, the US FDA approved liso-cel for R/R FL with at least two prior lines of therapy in May 2024. In August 2024, liso-cel was also approved in Japan for R/R FL cases with at least two prior lines of therapy and for POD24 patients or those with a high tumor burden with at least one prior line of therapy.

BISPECIFIC ANTIBODY THERAPIES IN FOLLICULAR LYMPHOMA

Bispecific T-cell engagers are a novel off-the-shelf immunotherapy approach that has rapidly advanced in treatment of FL. These antibodies link endogenous T cells to malignant B cells by dual specificity (typically anti-CD3 and anti-CD20) antibodies, inducing T-cell activation and cytotoxic synapse formation at the tumor. Currently, four CD20×CD3 BsAbs, mosunetuzumab, epcoritamab, odronextamab, and glofitamab, are in clinical development and are expected to be effective for FL. Here, we provide an overview of these bispecific antibodies.

Mosunetuzumab

Mosunetuzumab is a fully humanized IgG bispecific antibody targeting CD20 on B cells and CD3 on T cells. It has a 1:1 single-chain Fab structure and is given intravenously. A pivotal phase I/II trial (GO29781) was performed in 90 patients with R/R FL after two or more prior treatments. The patients had a median age of 60 years, and 79% were refractory to CD20 antibody therapy, 53% were double refractory, and 52% were POD24 patients. Mosunetuzumab monotherapy achieved an ORR of 80% with a 60% CR rate. In a median follow-up period of 18.3 months, the median PFS was 17.9 months and the 18-month PFS rate was 47%. Particularly durable efficacy of mosunetuzumab was found in patients who achieved CR. At updated 4-year follow-up, 64% of patients remained in CR at 42 months and median PFS was 24 months. Notably, some patients who relapsed after a fixed 8-cycle course were successfully retreated with mosunetuzumab, achieving repeat responses. In this trial, CRS was observed in 44% of patients, but only 2% developed grade ≥3 CRS. The most common time for CRS to occur was after first full-dose administration on day 15 of cycle 1. Neurological events related to mosunetuzumab and consistent with ICANS were a confusional state (3%; grade 1–2), disturbance in attention (1%; grade 1), and cognitive disorder (1%; grade 1). All these events resolved and there was no aphasia, seizures, encephalopathy, or cerebral edema. Based on these results, mosunetuzumab was approved in Europe in June 2022 for R/R FL with two or more prior therapies, making it the first BsAb worldwide to receive regulatory approval for treatment of malignant lymphoma. It was then approved in Japan in December 2024.

Epcoritamab

Epcoritamab is a bispecific antibody with a fully human IgG1 structure that targets CD20 and CD3. A distinguishing feature is its subcutaneous route of administration, which provides slow absorption and may reduce peak cytokine levels, potentially ameliorating CRS severity. The pivotal phase I/II EPCORE NHL-1 study included 128 patients with R/R FL after ≥2 lines, with a median age of 65 years. In the primary analysis of this cohort, with median follow-up of 17.4 months, epcoritamab monotherapy achieved an ORR of 82.0% and a CR rate of 62.5%. Three-year follow-up of epcoritamab showed sustained efficacy. Importantly, although epcoritamab in this study was administered until progression, in principle, CRs were maintained in patients who discontinued epcoritamab for reasons other than progression or death, showing response durability after treatment cessation. In EPCORE NHL-1, neutropenia was the most common grade 3–4 treatment-emergent adverse event. CRS occurred in 65% of FL patients during cycle 1, with almost all cases of grade 1–2; only 2% had grade 3 CRS and none had grade 4. Importantly, a protocol optimization adding a third step-up dose (days 1, 8, and 15 of cycle 1) further reduced the CRS rate to 49%, with no grade ≥3 CRS. In addition, no ICANS occurred in this study. Based on these results, epcoritamab received approval from the US FDA in June 2024. In Japan, its indication was expanded to include FL in February 2025. It should be noted that, unlike for LBCL, epcoritamab is approved as a three-step-up dose.

Odronextamab

Odronextamab is another CD20×CD3 IgG4-based bispecific antibody that is administered intravenously. Odronextamab had some early development setbacks, with an initial trial paused to refine CRS mitigation, but has shown high efficacy after mitigation measures. The phase 2 ELM-2 trial evaluated odronextamab in R/R FL after two or more lines, using a step-up schedule and prophylactic steroids in cycle 1 to reduce CRS. Among the 128 patients evaluated (median age 61 years, range: 22–84), 74% were CD20 antibody resistant, 41% were double refractory, and 49% were POD24 patients. This trial had an ORR of 80% and a CRR of 73%. In a median follow-up period of 20.1 months, median PFS and OS were 20.7 months and not reached, respectively. Furthermore, in a prespecified analysis of patient-reported outcomes of the ELM-2 trial, patients reported relatively good overall health-related quality of life, functioning, and low symptom burden at baseline, which were either maintained or improved through up to 50 weeks. The common treatment-emergent adverse events were CRS (56%), neutropenia (39%), and pyrexia (38%). Notably, almost all patients with CRS had grade 1–2, and no grade 3 or higher CRS was seen after protocol modification of introducing the intermediate step-up dose and steroid prophylaxis. In addition, there were no cases of ICANS after dosing optimization. Based on these results, R/R FL was approved in Europe in August 2024, but has not yet been approved by the US FDA or in Japan. Odronextamab is currently being evaluated as monotherapy against rituximab plus standard-of-care chemotherapies in a phase 3 confirmatory trial (OLYMPIA-1), and in combination with chemotherapy against rituximab plus standard-of-care chemotherapies in a separate phase 3 trial (OLYMPIA-2).

Glofitamab

Glofitamab is a distinct CD20×CD3 bispecific antibody that possesses two CD20-binding sites and has been shown to have higher potency than other T-cell redirecting BsAbs in preclinical studies. Glofitamab is administered intravenously with step-up dosing in cycle 1, then at a fixed full dose every 3 weeks for a maximum of 12 cycles. In an early phase I trial in an FL cohort (44 patients), ORR was 70.5% (CR, 47.7%) in the whole group and 61.9% (CR, 52.4%) in 21 patients treated at the recommended phase 2 dose. CRS and neurotoxicity were manageable and comparable to other BsAbs. Glofitamab is approved in the US and Europe for LCBL, but not yet for FL. Ongoing trials are exploring glofitamab in earlier lines and in combination for FL.

COMPARISON OF CAR-T AND BISPECIFIC ANTIBODIES IN FOLLICULAR LYMPHOMA

CAR-T and BsAbs are both expected to be highly effective, even in high-risk FL patients, such as POD24 and double refractory patients. The characteristics of representative CAR-T and BsAb therapies are shown in Table 1. CAR-T therapy tends to offer a more durable response, with some patients remaining in CR for years, but is subject to complex manufacturing requirements, limited accessibility, significant costs, and the risk of severe toxicities, including second primary malignancies. It is important to note that CAR-T therapy can cause prolonged cytopenia and infections, which is the leading cause of non-relapse mortality, limiting its use in especially elderly patients. Furthermore, it is unclear whether CAR-T therapy for FL can be curative, and this requires clarification further long-term observation. If further follow-up reveals that PFS curve plateaus with CAR-T therapy, its position as a treatment for FL may become more important, even if it is highly toxic. In contrast, BsAbs offer a more accessible and manageable approach, with fewer manufacturing hurdles and a potentially better safety profile but may require more frequent administration for sustained effects. Although BsAbs have been reported to have a high incidence of infectious adverse events in earlier cycles, cytopenias are generally less severe than with CAR-T therapy. However, severe and prolonged cytopenias are occasionally experienced, so careful management is essential.

Among the several BsAbs, efficacy is similar for all agents, with high ORR and CRR, and a similar response duration with early follow-up. Toxicities are also relatively comparable. Selection of BsAb therapy depends on the differences among the BsAbs, including treatment duration (until progression or fixed duration), route of administration (intravenous or subcutaneous), and protocol for CRS mitigation (dosing schedule and steroid prophylaxis).

There are currently no RCTs comparing the benefits of CAR-T therapy and BsAb therapy, or the efficacy of BsAbs, and there is a need to generate evidence for selection of these immunotherapies in clinical practice.

FUTURE DIRECTIONS

Future prospects for cellular immunotherapy in FL lie in overcoming the current limitations of CAR-T and BsAbs. Regarding CAR-T, strategies to improve T cell persistence and function are being developed, such as modifying CAR design using different costimulatory domains or utilizing pre-made allogeneic CAR-T cells. Regarding BsAbs, the potential for enhancing efficacy is being explored through optimization of target antigens and combination therapy with other immunotherapies. Cellular immunotherapy is also being developed for earlier lines of FL treatment. In particular, BsAb combination therapies are currently undergoing phase 3 trials for FL as first-line treatment, but no phase 3 trials of CAR-T for previously untreated FL are currently being conducted. Establishment of personalized treatment strategies using biomarkers to predict treatment response and toxicity profiles is desirable. Further advances in development of CAR-T and BsAbs may provide complementary treatment options and improve patient outcomes and quality of life.

CONCLUSIONS

CAR-T cell therapy and BsAbs are major advances in treatment of FL, offering the potential for durable remission in patients with R/R FL. Challenges such as toxicity, resistance, and accessibility remain, but ongoing clinical trials and research should address these issues.
