# Specific immune response to mRNA vaccines against COVID-19 in patients receiving allogeneic stem cell transplantation for myeloid malignancy was altered by immunosuppressive therapy.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant (allo-HSCT) recipients are at high risk of complications associated with COVID-19 infection due to dysfunction of their immune system. Vaccination can protect from the adverse consequences of COVID-19. However, studies on the efficacy of COVID-19 vaccines in HSCT recipients with insufficient post-HSCT immune reconstitution are still scarce. In our study, we determined how immunosuppressive medication and the reconstitution of the cellular immune system influenced T cell responses specific for the surface glycoprotein of SARS-CoV-2 virus (S antigen) after two doses of mRNA vaccine against COVID-19 in patients with myeloid malignancies treated with HSCT.

**METHODS**: Vaccination outcomes were followed in 18 (allo-HSCT) recipients and 8 healthy volunteers. The IgG antibodies against SARS-CoV-2 spike (S) and nucleocapsid (NCP) protein were determined in ELISA and S-specific T cells were detected using a sensitive ELISPOT-IFNγ based on in vitro expansion and restimulation of T cells in pre- and post-vaccination blood samples. Multiparametric flow cytometry analysis of peripheral blood leukocyte differentiation markers was employed for determination of reconstitution of the main subpopulations of T cells and NK cells at month 6 after HSCT.

**RESULTS**: S- specific IgG antibody response detected in 72% of the patients was lower than in healthy vaccinees (100%). Vaccine-induced T-cell responses to S1 or S2 antigen were significantly reduced in HSCT recipients, which were treated with corticosteroids in dose 5 mg of prednisone- equivalents or higher during the vaccination period or in preceeding 100 days in comparison with recipients un-affected with corticosteroids. A significant positive correlation was found between the level of anti-SARS-Cov-2 spike protein IgG antibodies and the number of functional S antigen-specific T cells. Further analysis also showed that the specific response to vaccination was significantly influenced by the interval between administration of vaccine and transplantation. Vaccination outcomes were not related to age, sex, type of mRNA vaccine used, basic diagnosis, HLA match between HSC donor and recipient, and blood counts of lymphocytes, neutrophils, and monocytes at the time of vaccination. Multiparametric flow cytometry analysis of peripheral blood leukocyte differentiation markers showed that good humoral and cellular S-specific immune responses induced by vaccination were associated with well-reconstituted CD4+ T cells, mainly CD4+ effector memory subpopulation at six months after HSCT.

**CONCLUSIONS**: The results showed that both humoral and cellular adaptive immune responses of HSCT recipients to the SARS-CoV-2 vaccine were significantly suppressed by corticosteroid therapy. Specific response to the vaccine was significantly affected by the length of the interval between HSCT and vaccination. Vaccination as early as 5 months after HSCT can lead to a good response. Immune response to the vaccine is not related to age, gender, HLA match between HSC donor and recipient, or type of myeloid malignancy. Vaccine efficacy was dependent on well-reconstituted CD4+ T cells, at six months after HSCT.

Introduction

Patients who have undergone allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (allo-HSCT) are at high risk of viral diseases. During COVID-19 pandemic, it has been shown that the probability of severe course of COVID-19 in allo-HSCT recipients is significantly elevated compared with normal population, and the mortality in HSCT recipients with COVID-19 has been reported to be 22–30% in four published studies. Most allo-HSCT recipients are vaccinated in the post-transplant period against COVID-19. There are numerous studies of vaccine against SARS-CoV-2 efficacy in these patients based on the detection of humoral immune responses (for a recent review, see Chaekal, 2022). Their specific T cell responses to the vaccine have been analysed in only six studies so far and deficient response to SARS-CoV-2 antigens was found in all of them. Two doses of the vaccine elicited positive T cell responses against the spike protein in 19–83% of HSCT recipients. The aim of our study was to determine how the reconstitution of the cellular immune system, immunosuppressive medications, and other demographic and clinical covariates influence T cell responses specific for the surface glycoprotein of SARS-CoV-2 virus (S antigen) after two doses of an mRNA vaccine against COVID-19 in patients with myeloid malignancies treated with allo-HSCT. To increase the sensitivity of ELISPOT-IFNγ assay a protocol employing in vitro expansion and restimulation of SARS-CoV-2- specific T cells was used.

Material and methods

Patients and samples

A response to COVID-19 vaccine was followed in 18 allo-HSCT recipients with myeloid malignancy who had been treated in the Institute of Haematology and Blood Transfusion (IHBT), Prague between April 2020 and June 2021. Clinical and demographic characteristics of the patients are summarized in Table 1. To follow reconstitution of their immune system, the patients were sampled in months 1,2,3,4,6,7,9,11, and 12 after HSCT. The samples taken at the nearest time before the vaccination and after the administration of the second dose of the vaccine were taken to evaluate the vaccine response. To set-up the assays, 8 healthy volunteers (6 female, 2 male, mean age 58 years, range 30–72) were sampled 30–60 days after the second dose of COVID-19 vaccine. Frozen peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) isolated from blood donors between years 2011–2018 were used as SARS-CoV-2 negative controls. All participants signed an informed consent form. The study was approved by the institutional ethical board. PBMC from the patients and healthy volunteers were collected on Ficoll-Paque Plus (GE Healthcare) using SepMate™ separation tubes (Stemcell Technologies, Vancouver, Canada). Fresh PBMC were used for SARS-CoV2-specific T-cell response testing. Aliquots of the patient´s PBMC were frozen in liquid nitrogen to measure markers of immune system reconstitution by flow cytometry. Plasma samples were aliquoted and frozen for SARS-CoV2-specific antibody examination.

Vaccination against COVID-19

The patients were indicated to vaccination according to guidelines for COVID-19 vaccination of The European Society for Blood and Marrow Transplantation (EBMT) (version 2.0) The vaccine was offered to the recipients who were at least three months after HSCT and did not suffer from serious health complications, (GVHD grade II or higher, hematological relapse of basal disease or the graft failure). The patients were intramuscularly injected with two doses of the mRNA based BNT162b2 (Pfizer Comirnaty, n = 13) or mRNA-1273 (Moderna Spikevax, n = 5). All healthy volunteers obtained BNT162b2 mRNA COVID-19 vaccine.

Measurement of SARS-CoV-2-specific antibodies

The presence of antibodies to SARS-CoV-2 was determined in plasma samples by semi-quantitative enzyme-linked immunosorbent assays (ELISA, EUROIMMUN Medizinische Labordiagnostika AG, Lübeck, Germany). Anti-SARS-CoV2 ELISA (IgG) test system was used for the detection of IgG antibodies against SARS-CoV-2 S1 domain of the spike protein including the immunologically relevant receptor binding domain (RBD) and Anti-SARS-CoV-2 NCP ELISA (IgG) test kit was used for the detection of IgG against SARS-CoV-2 immunodominant nucleocapsid protein (NCP). The tests were performed according to the provider´s instructions. The absorbance measurements were carried out using an automated microplate reader and the Magellan™ data analysis software (Tecan, Männedorf, Switzerland). The results were semi-quantitatively evaluated by calculating antibody index (AI) (the ratio of the sample absorbance to the cut-off values). The AI values lower than 0.8 were considered negative, borderline ratios were set at 0.8–1.1 and AIs higher than 1.1 were interpreted as positive.

Detection of a SARS-CoV2-specific T cell response

T-cell response against S and NCP SARS-CoV2 antigens was measured using pools of 15 amino acid long overlapping peptides (PepMix™). Pools of peptides derived from a peptide scan (15mers with 11aa overlap) through the entire Spike glycoprotein (Swiss-Prot ID: P59594) of SARS CoV-2 (PepMix™ SARS-CoV Spike Glycoprotein), divided into two parts (S1 and S2) and pool of peptides derived from a peptide scan through SARS CoV-2 Nucleoprotein (Swiss-Prot ID: P0DTC9) (PepMix™ SARS-CoV-2 NCAP) were purchased from JPT Peptide Technologies GmbH (Berlin, Germany). PBMC were pre-stimulated with the mixture of the antigens and subsequently cultivated in vitro in cell culture medium CTL (50% RPMI1640 with HEPES and glutamine, 45% Click's medium and 5% human AB-serum) supplemented with IL4 and IL7 (R&D Systems) for 11 days as described recently. The culture was harvested on the 12th day, and the frequency of SARS CoV-2-specific T cells among expanded T cells was examined by ELISPOT-IFNγ.

ELISPOT-IFNγ

T-cells were resuspended in the culture medium supplemented with 0.5 µg/mL of costimulatory molecules (purified Mouse anti-Human CD28 and purified Mouse anti-Human CD49d, BD Biosciences, San Diego, CA, USA) and seeded in triplicates on a MultiScreen®-IP Filter Plate (Merck Millipore), at concentrations of 1.25 × 105 and 2.5 × 105 per well, respectively. The wells of the MultiScreen®-IP Filter Plates were precoated with 0.75 µg/well of anti-interferon-γ purified capture antibody (anti-human IFN-γ mAb, clone 1-D1K, MabTech). Restimulation was performed with individual PepMixes™ (1 µg/mL) at 37 °C in 5%CO2 atmosphere for 20 h. Stimulation with 0.2 µg/mL of purified anti-human CD3 mAb CD3–2 (MabTech) was used as a positive control. Only samples with positive response after anti-CD3 stimulation were evaluated. The cells incubated without stimulants served as negative controls. IFN-γ spots were developed with 1 µg/mL of biotinylated anti-human IFN-γ detection antibody (mAb7-B6–1, MabTech) followed by incubation with Streptavidin-horseradish peroxidase (HRP, MabTech) diluted 1:100 and AEC substrate (BD™ ELISPOT AEC Substrate Set, BD Biosciences, San Diego, CA, USA). The mean SFC numbers were normalized for 2.5 × 105 cells. The SFC values of negative controls were subtracted from those determined in stimulated cultures. Frozen PBMC taken from blood donors in the years 2011–2018 were used for setting the cut-off for positive cellular response. The cut-off values for S1, S2, and NCP PepMixes were 270, 300, and 330 SFC/2.5 × 105 PBMC, respectively as shown in Fig. S1.

Intracellular Cytokine Detection by Flow Cytometry (ICS)

Lymphocytes 5 × 106 in 1 mL culture medium were stimulated with 1 µg/mL PepMix a for 2 h and then for an additional 12 h in the presence of 1:1000 diluted Golgi plug (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, NJ, USA). After incubation, cells were harvested, washed with PBS and stained with the LIVE/DEAD Blue Dead Cell Staining Kit (Thermo Fisher) and with antibodies to CD56-BV510 (Biolegend, San Diego, CA, USA), CD8-AlexaFluor700 (Exbio, Prague, Czech Republic). CD45RO-BV786, and CCR7-BV605 (BD Horizon, BD Biosciences). The cells were then washed with PBS, fixed using IC Fixation and Permeabilization Buffer (eBioscience, San Diego, CA, USA) for 20 min and stained intracellularly with antibodies against IFN-gamma-PE, CD3-APC-Cy7 (Biolegend, San Diego, CA, USA), and CD4-PacificBlue (Exbio) in a the permeabilization buffer. The cells were washed and resuspended in a FACS buffer (FB-PBS containing 0.09% sodium azide, 1% BSA) and measured using the BD LSR Fortessa 5 L flow cytometer (BD Biosciences). The obtained data were analysed by the FlowJo 10.5 software (TreeStar, Ashland, OR, USA). The gating strategy is shown in suplementary Fig. S 2.

Flow cytometry evaluation of the immune system reconstitution after HSCT

The recently published 29-colour panel of reagents OMIP-080 was employed for the quantitative and qualitative analysis of the major immune cell populations in HSCT recipients at month 6 after HSCT. Flow cytometry measurement was performed using the BD Symphony A5 instrument equipped with five lasers. Specification of antibodies, fluorochromes and instrument configuration was described previously and staining of cells was performed according to published protocol. We evaluated frequencies of αβT cells, γδT cells, NK cells and NKT cells. Cell subpopulations were identified by manual gating using the FlowJo 10.5 software (TreeStar, Ashland, OR, USA). The gating strategy is depicted in suplementary Fig. S3.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analyses were performed using Prism 9.1.0. (GraphPad Software, USA). Continuous variables are given as mean with min.-max. range. Paired comparisons between two groups were done using the paired t test. Unpaired comparisons were performed using t test with Welch correction or non-parametric Mann-Whitney test. Univariable and multivariable regression models were used to determine factors associated with S2-specific T cell response. The S2-specific T cell response was used as the reference category because it was the most frequently found positive immune response in the cohort under study. Categorical responses were tested using simple logistic regression model.

Results

The study enrolled 18 allo-HSCT recipients who received two doses of mRNA vaccine at median time 235 days after HSCT (range 108–358 days). Pre-vaccination and post-vaccination samples were obtained at average interval 58 days before the vaccination (range 30–72), and 83 days (range 22–162 days) after administration of the 2nd vaccine dose, respectively. Individual schedules of post-transplantation course, vaccination time and the vaccine response examination are illustrated in Fig. 1. Two patients (PS 4 and 7) showed anti-S IgG and/or anti-NCP IgG ( Fig. 2 A) before vaccination and three (PS 1, 7 and 16) showed NCP-specific T cell reactivity after vaccination (Fig. 2 B), suggesting probable infection of a HSCT recipient with SARS-CoV-2 or a related coronavirus before or during the vaccination period or transmission of the immunity from SARS-CoV2- immune donor of HSC. Thus, 14 SARS-CoV2- naive patients with complete vaccination were included in the final analysis (Fig. 2 CD).

Antibody response to COVID-19 vaccination in HSCT recipients

IgG antibody response to SARS-CoV-2 anti-S and anti-NCP antigens were tested simultaneously with T cell response. An increase of anti-S IgG levels in response to vaccination in patients in comparison with baseline was apparent (mean of differences 4.616 was statistically significant (p < 0.0001; 95% CI = 3.199–6.963 as determined using the paired t test). While all the healthy volunteers developed anti-S IgG in response to COVID-19 vaccination, five (28%) of allo-HSCT recipients remained seronegative (Fig. 2 A). Mean anti-S IgG levels in the healthy volunteers (mean AI= 8.02) were statistically different from those in allo-HSCT recipients (mean AI= 5.469) (difference between means 2.557 ± 0.9877; p = 0.002; 95% CI = 0.4927–4.620; as determined using the t test with Welch correction).

IFNγ T-cell responses to SARS-CoV-2 antigens S1, S2, and NCP

Post-vaccination responses of T lymphocytes to SARS-CoV-2 antigens S1, S2, and NCP were quantified after antigenic restimulation of in vitro expanded T lymphocytes. Responses to the S1 and S2 peptide pools were measured separately. The difference between means of anti-S1 and anti-S2 T cell response in allo-HSCT recipients was not statistically significant. Positive NCP-specific T cell response was accounted marker of corona virus infection.

The magnitude of the S-specific T cell response to vaccination of allo-HSC recipients (mean S1 =387; S2 =670 SFU/2.5 ×105 PBMC,) and of healthy volunteers (HV, mean S1 =426, S2 =413 SFU/2.5 ×105 PBMC) were not statistically significantly different (S1-p = 0.7032; 95% CI=−175.5 to 255.8; S2-p = 0.2872; 95% CI= −456.5 to 141.4). Interestingly, values of T cell response in allo-HSCT recipients were significantly more variable than those in healthy subjects, as determined by the F test (S1 F=5.480; p = 0.0444; S2 F=7.556; p = 0.0113). The T cells positive for S1 PepMix were detected in (50%) allo-HSC recipients and in 60% healthy volunteers. Positive response against S2 PepMix was detected in (61%) allo-HSC recipients and in 88% healthy subjects, respectively (Fig. 2 B).

If a sufficient number of cells for FACS analysis was available after in vitro culture, the abundance of CD4 and CD8 T lymphocyte subpopulations and presence of differentiation markers CD45 RO and CCR7 on S-specific IFNγ- producing T cells was measured by flow cytometry (Fig. 2 E, F). Flow cytometry was performed for six patients (PS 0, 1, 5, 7, 8 and 9) and seven healthy volunteers (HD 02, 03, 04, 06, 57, 59, 60). Response of CD8 and CD4 T cells to any S antigen were found in six patients and in six volunteers (Fig. 1 E). Cells responsive to stimulation with PepMix were predominantly CD4+ T cells with an efector memory T cell phenotype (CD45RO+, CCR7-). CD8 + T cells predominantly displayed markers of effector T cells or effector memory T cells (Fig. 1 F).

Association of clinical and demographic characteristics with immune response to vaccination

We examined the impact of demographic and clinical characteristics in allo-HSCT recipients on COVID-19 vaccination-induced T cell response to the more immunogenic S2 protein ( Table 2). When using univariate regression model in all 18 vaccinees, an age, gender, vaccine type, basic diagnosis, HLA match between HSC donor and recipient, blood count of lymphocytes, monocytes, and neutrophils at the time of the 1st and 2nd vaccine dose, were not significantly associated with S2-specific T cell response. As expected, the magnitude of S2-specific T cell response significantly positively correlated with the anti-S IgG levels (p = 0.0012) and with S1-specific T cell response (p = 0.0004) induced by vaccination. On the other hand, corticosteroid therapy with a prednisone-equivalent dose 5 mg /day up to 100 days before or during the vaccination and acute or chronic GVHD were significantly associated with a reduction in S2-specific T cell response in the univariate model (p = 0.0055 and p = 0.0156, respectively). In the multivariate model, the S2-specific T cell response was significantly associated only with the amount of S1-specific T cells (p = 0.0004). In a cohort of patients not treated with corticosteroids (PS 1, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 15, 16, 19, 20; n = 11) we analysed the role of the length of the interval between HSCT and administration of the first and second dose of vaccination in the vaccine immune response ( Table 3). The analysis showed a strong association between the time of 2nd dose administration as well as the time of immune response examination and the amount of S2-specific T cells.

Effect of corticosteroid therapy on immune response elicited by vaccination in allo-HSC recipients

Magnitude of humoral and cellular immune response to the COVID-19 vaccine in the corticosteroid–affected and un-affected patients is compared in the Fig. 2 C, D: The group of corticosteroid-affected patients comprised of those who received the therapy of prednisone-equivalent in the dose of 5 mg/day or higher within 100 days before or during the time of vaccination (PS 10, 6, 14, 12, 13, 3, and 2). Rest of the patients were included in the corticosteroid-un-affected group (PS 1, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9, 11, 15, 16, 19, and 20). In order to limit high variability in T cell responses, the patients with experience of SARS-CoV-2 infection were excluded from this analysis. Both the groups compared did not significantly differ in the age, gender, HSCT characteristics followed, the vaccine type or lymphocyte, monocyte, or neutrophil blood counts at the time of vaccination. On the other hand, the vaccine- specific T-cell response (Fig. 2 D), as well as anti-S IgG levels (Fig. 2 C) were significantly reduced in the corticoid-affected group. Mean anti-S IgG AI in corticosteroid- affected vs. un-affected groups were 1.008 and 7.910, respectively (p = 0.0003; difference between means= −6.903 ± 1.160; 95% IC= −9.563 to −4.242). Both the T cell response against S1 and against S2 were different (S1 mean 45.72 with corticosteroid- affected vs. 562.3 SFU/2.5 ×105 in un-affected; p = 0.0089; difference between means= −516.6 ± 142.7; 95% IC = −855.8 to −177.3) (S2 mean 137.0 vs. 983 SFU/2.5 ×105; p = 0.039; difference between means= −846.3 ± 199.4; 95% IC = −1319 to −373.8).

Impact of T cell reconstitution after HSCT on COVID-19 vaccination efficacy

Differences in the frequency of lymphocyte subpopulations were determined in the group of non-responders and poor responders (anti-S2-specific SFC < 300/2.5 ×105 lymphocytes) vs. vaccine responders ( Fig. 3) at month 6 after HSCT using multiparametric flow cytometry. The analysis was performed only in patients who received the first vaccine dose not earlier than 1 month before analysis of T cell subpopulations at month 6 after HSCT. We observed that the responders vs. non-responders had significantly increased frequency of CD4+ T cells (p = 0.0240; difference of medians 8.63; 96.4% CI = 0.76–18.96), specifically in the effector memory CD4+ T cell subpopulation (p = 0.0040; difference between medians 1.575; 96.4% CI = 0.650–3.090). A similar trend, though not significant, was found in the central memory CD4+ T cell subset (p = 0.14). Differences in the frequencies of CD8+ T cell subsets and other lymphocyte populations between responders and non-responders were not significant. The concordance of anti-S IgG and S1-specific T cells with CD4+ effector memory T cell levels (not shown) indicated that reconstituted CD4+ T cells are required for the induction of both humoral and cellular branches of the adaptive immune response.

Discussion

In a pilot prospective study, we followed the SARS-CoV2- specific immune response to mRNA vaccine against COVID-19 in 18 allo- HSCT recipients, 14 of them were shown to be SARS-CoV-2 infection naïve.

The six studies that have analysed the SARS-CoV-2 vaccination-induced T-cell response in HSCT recipients so far [RW.ERROR - Unable to find reference:254] have all unequivocally demonstrated reduced immunogenicity of vaccination with two doses of the vaccine compared to healthy controls. In accordance with these studies, we showed that both humoral and T cell responses to the vaccine are altered in allo-HSCT recipients in comparison with healthy vaccinees. While the anti-S IgG antibody levels in the patients were significantly lower when compared with the healthy volunteers, the differences in S antigen- specific T cell responses were not statistically significant. This was a consequence of the low numbers of individuals in both groups studied and of high T-cell response variability observed in the patients´ group, which resulted from the differences in their post-transplantation course, immunosuppressive therapy and the time of vaccination.

In previous studies, adaptive cellular responses have been analysed using various detection assays. Our approach was based on our experience with detection of T cell responses in HSCT recipients receiving corticosteroids. It is known that the reconstitution of their T cell population, can be significantly delayed. Determination of the T cell response directly from fresh blood would often show a negative result because the assay is not sensitive enough. The determination of a very low frequency of specific T cells requires an assay with an increased sensitivity than those assays used in the general population. The frequency of SARS-CoV2 specific IFNγ-producing T cells in the peripheral blood of some of our patients could only be detected after their pre- stimulation with virus-derived peptide pools followed by ex vivo expansion and use a highly sensitive ELISPOT-IFNγ. Antigenic stimulation of T cells in vitro in the presence of IL4 and IL7 results in expansion of both CD4+ and CD8+ responder subpopulations. Using an ELISPOT-IFNγ quantification of in vitro expanded T cells, our detection rate of S1- and S2- specific T cells (50% and 61% of HSCT recipients and 60% and 100% of HC, respectively) was higher than in the study of Ram et al., (6) and of Lindemann et al. (7) which referred positive response in 18% and 27% of HSCT recipients, respectively. On the other hand, in other studies, the detection rates of specific T- cell response in patient groups were higher (70, 78, 82%). The highest detection rate was achieved using the S1-specific T-cell derived IFNγ-release assay in whole blood where positive T cell response was detected in 90.4% of HC and 72% of HSCT recipients. The differences in T-cell response rates observed may be associated not only with variance in the sensitivity of the detection assays used but also with different study designs and characteristics of the vaccinated groups followed. The 24-month interval from HSC infusion to vaccine administration and the interval between vaccination and immune response testing have been described as critical factors that influence the magnitude of the T cell response after vaccination. Studies in patients vaccinated at intervals greater than 24 months after transplantation have found this factor to be significant. The significance of the HSCT-vaccination interval was also observed in our study: The best immune response was observed in the corticoid-un-affected patients who were vaccinated 7 months after HSCT or later. We observed positive responses in patients given the first dose between month 5 and 6 after HSCT (PS6, PS19, PS10) suggesting that vaccination starting at 5 months after HSCT is feasible. On the other hand, the patients vaccinated as early as at month 4 (PS13,14) showed a poor immune response. However, it is not possible to conclude that such an early start of vaccination was the only reason for the poor response, as both patients were also treated with corticosteroids.

In some of the previous studies, the effects of gender and type of vaccine were observed in connection with antibody response but not with T cell responses. In concordance with their results, we did not find a significant association between the vaccine- specific T- cell response and age, gender, basal diagnosis or HSCT characteristics. On the other hand, in univariate analysis a strong association was found between the patients´ T-cell response and corticosteroid therapy with prednisone-equivalent dose 5 mg/day given up to 100 day before or at the time of vaccination. Presence of GVHD that had a significant impact on the vaccine response, too, should be considered as a covariate, as most of the patients with GVHD were also corticosteroid- treated. Two studies reported an association of prednisone treatment with a poor T cell response to COVID-19 vaccine, but the difference was not statistically significant due to low number of the patients treated.

Coincidentally, in our study there was a significant representation of patients on corticosteroids (7/14) and the association of immunosuppressive therapy with low vaccine immunogenicity was significant (Table 2). A flow cytometric study of T lymphocytes in peripheral blood at month 6 after HSCT showed that higher reconstitution of memory CD4+ subsets of T lymphocytes was associated with effective vaccination against SARS-CoV-2. CD4+ T cells play an important role during the induction of humoral and cellular responses by the vaccine. As CD4+ T cells are known to be essential for the induction of immune responses and their reconstitution in HSCT patients is delayed, it is not surprising that CD4+ T cells have been shown to be critical for eliciting anti-S IgG and S-specific T cells after vaccination.

We confirmed the predominance of S-specific CD4+ T cell responses in vaccinated HSCT recipients observed in previous studies. Ram et al. in their study, in contrast to the CD4+ T cell response, they detected no specific CD8+ T cell response at all. Harrington et al. detected a CD4+ T lymphocyte response to vaccination in three times more vaccinated recipients than a CD8+ T lymphocyte response. Clémenceau et al. showed in vaccinated HSCT recipients that the predominance of S-specific CD4+ cell responses over CD8+ T cell responses is specific to the SARS-CoV-2 vaccine, as EBV-specific T cells in the same patients were almost exclusively CD8+ .

The low number of participants and an absence of match between the patients and healthy vaccinated volunteers represent main limitation of this study. Moreover, the fixed sampling schedule of the patients, due to concurrent immune system reconstitution study did not allow us to examine the vaccine- associated T-cell response in unified intervals before and after the vaccination. Despite this, our results documented for the first time, that corticosteroid therapy can significantly suppress the effectivity of the COVID-19 vaccine response in allo-HSCT recipients. We found that the levels of anti-S antigen antibodies and the numbers of functional S antigen-specific T lymphocytes significantly correlated. Our data confirmed that the specific response to the vaccine was significantly affected by the length of the interval between HSCT and vaccination and suggested that starting vaccination as late as 5 months after HSCT can lead to good responses. Immune response to the vaccine is not related to age, gender, vaccine mRNA type, HLA match between HSC donor and recipient, or type of myeloid malignancy. Vaccine efficacy was not affected by total blood count of lymphocytes and neutrophils at the time of vaccination. Multiparametric phenotypic analysis of T cell and NK cell populations six months after HSCT revealed a significant role of CD4+ T cells in the vaccine response, which was consistent with the importance of quantity and quality of virus antigen-specific CD4+ T cells for high levels of virus-neutralizing antibodies against SARS-CoV-2.
