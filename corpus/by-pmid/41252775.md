# Metabolic nanoblockers for synergistic cancer treatment through glutaminase inhibition and sonodynamic therapy.

## Abstract

The immunotherapeutic potential of sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is hindered by tumor defense mechanisms driven by glutamine metabolism, including glutathione (GSH)-dependent redox homeostasis and an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). To address these challenges, we herein developed metabolic nanoblockers (CBE@AM NPs) by encapsulating a glutamine metabolism inhibitor CB839 and a sonosensitizer chlorin e6 (Ce6) into melanin-inspired nanoparticles. The resulting nanoblockers triggered robust reactive oxygen species (ROS) production upon ultrasound irradiation, thereby destroying tumor cells and inducing immunogenic cell death (ICD). Concurrently, they inhibited the glutamine metabolism in the tumor, disrupting redox homeostasis and remodeling the immunosuppressive TME, thereby amplifying both SDT-generated oxidative stress and ICD-induced antitumor immunity. CBE@AM NPs demonstrated a potent tumor-inhibitory effect in tumor-bearing mice, highlighting their potential for immunometabolic reprogramming to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of SDT.

Introduction

Sonodynamic therapy (SDT), as a non-invasive approach to treat cancer, has made dramatic progress in recent years. In this therapy, sonosensitizers were activated by low-intensity ultrasound (US) to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS), which induced oxidative damage that ultimately led to tumor cell death. Compared with conventional radiotherapy or photodynamic therapy, SDT exhibits superior tissue penetration, precise spatiotemporal control, and minimal off-target toxicity, thereby offering distinct advantages for treating deep-seated or refractory tumors. Besides directly killing tumors, SDT can trigger immunogenic cell death (ICD) in tumor cells, with concurrent release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) that ignite robust antitumor immune response. Thus, SDT not only eradicates tumor cells locally but also holds immense potential to augment systemic immune surveillance. Yet, the therapeutic efficacy of SDT against solid tumors remains restricted by the hostile tumor microenvironment (TME). For example, the high intratumoral glutathione (GSH) levels rapidly scavenge the SDT-induced ROS, reducing the ROS-mediated cytotoxicity. This challenge is further compounded by limited tumor accumulation and rapid clearance of sonosensitizers, along with the inherently immunosuppressive TME, which together constrain ROS generation and attenuate antitumor immune activation. These limitations underscore the urgent need for innovative strategies to overcome these barriers and fully harness the potential of SDT in cancer immunotherapy.

Aiming to address these challenges, combining SDT with metabolic reprogramming strategies has gained increasing attention. Among various metabolic routes, targeting glutamine metabolism has emerged as a powerful metabolic intervention to enhance ROS-inducing therapies. Glutamine is the richest circulating amino acid indispensable for sustaining anabolic biosynthesis and driving the TCA cycle, which are processes fueling the uncontrolled proliferation of tumor cells. Glutamine-derived glutamate functions as a key precursor in GSH synthesis, which is essential for maintaining intracellular redox homeostasis. Furthermore, this metabolic dependency, which is termed “glutamine addiction”, not only sustains tumor growth but also enforces nutrient competition that deprives immune cells of key substrates required for their proliferation and activation, ultimately dampening antitumor immunity and fostering immunosuppression. Consequently, blocking glutamine metabolism can effectively dismantle antioxidant defenses, thereby increasing their susceptibility to oxidative stress, while simultaneously restoring immune cell metabolic fitness to combat tumors. Recent reports have shown that glutamine restriction sensitizes cancer stem cells to ROS-mediated killing and acts synergistically with photodynamic therapies. Inhibiting the metabolism of glutamine can polarize M2-like tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) into M1-like TAMs, remodeling the immunosuppressive TME. Nevertheless, clinical translation of glutamine metabolism inhibitors such as CB839 or C968 is limited by challenges including poor bioavailability, inadequate tumor penetration, and adaptive metabolic plasticity. Given their inherent tumor-targeting properties, nanoparticle (NP)-based systems designed to co-deliver glutaminase (GLS) inhibitors and sonosensitizers can help overcome these challenges, enhance therapeutic efficacy, and synergistically potentiate antitumor immunity.

Herein, we developed multifunctional metabolic nanoblockers (CBE@AM NPs) for sono-immunometabolic therapy by synthesizing melanin-inspired nanoparticles (AM NPs) via oxidative polymerization of 1,8-dihydroxynaphthalene (1,8-DHN) and subsequently loading them with the sonosensitizer chlorin e6 (Ce6) and the GLS inhibitor CB839 (Scheme 1A). The resulting nanoblockers exhibited high structural stability and favorable biocompatibility, markedly enhancing the bioavailability of Ce6 and CB839 through pronounced tumor accumulation after systemic administration. Upon US irradiation, CBE@AM NPs triggered robust ROS production, thereby destroying tumor cells and inducing the ICD effect to activate immune responses. Concurrently, the released CB839 disrupted GSH-dependent redox homeostasis, sensitizing tumor cells to SDT-generated ROS. Furthermore, CB839 blocked the glutamine metabolism in the tumor, remodeling the immunosuppressive TME and synergizing with SDT-induced ICD to elicit potent antitumor immunity, ultimately suppressing tumor growth (Scheme 1B). Collectively, this synergistic therapeutic strategy establishes a positive feedback process: US-induced ROS production boosts tumor immunogenicity to recruit antitumor immune responses, whereas glutamine restriction amplifies SDT efficacy and reprograms the TME to favor immune activation.

Materials and Methods

Preparation of CBE@AM NPs

All stock solutions were prepared individually: CB839 (4 mg/mL) was dissolved in methanol, and Ce6 (2 mg/mL) was dissolved in dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO). Melanin-inspired AM NPs were synthesized as previously described. Subsequently, 5 mg of AM NPs was dispersed in ultrapure water (1 mL), followed by the addition of CB839 solution (0.5 mL) and Ce6 solution (0.5 mL). Following a 12-h stirring period, CBE@AM NPs were obtained via centrifugation and washed three times to remove unbound reagents.

Cytotoxicity, apoptosis, and live/dead assay

The cytotoxicity of CBE@AM NPs against Lewis lung carcinoma line 1 (LLC) cells was assessed using MTT assays. LLC cells were cultured and incubated for 4 h with various samples in fresh DMEM medium. Fresh DMEM was exchanged, cells were exposed to 1 W/cm2 US (50 % duty cycle, 1 min). After a 20-hour incubation, MTT was supplied, and crystals were quantified using a microplate reader.

LLC cells were cultured and incubated for 24 h with the indicated treatments. Subsequently, cells were stained with an Annexin-FITC/propidium iodide (PI) Kit and analyzed via flow cytometry (Agilent, NovoCyte 3009, USA). Additionally, live/dead cell distribution was assessed via dual staining with calcein-AM and PI, with fluorescence images captured using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM).

Reactive oxygen species (ROS) and mitochondrial membrane potential (MMP) assay

LLC cells were cultured and incubated with the indicated treatments for 8 h. After a 20-min incubation with 2′,7′-dichlorofluorescein diacetate (DCFH-DA), intracellular ROS were imaged and quantified by CLSM and flow cytometry. MMP was evaluated with JC-1 and visualized by CLSM.

Evaluation of GSH content and GLS inhibition

LLC cells were cultured with the indicated samples for 12 h. Subsequently, the supernatant was collected for GSH content determination using an enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) kit. Meanwhile, cells were fixed and subjected to immunofluorescence staining to assess GLS expression. In parallel, LLC cells were lysed, and protein extracts were harvested for Western blot analysis to quantify GLS protein levels.

Evaluation of immunogenic cell death in vitro

LLC cells were cultured and treated with the indicated samples for 20 h. Following treatment, cells were exposed to US for 1 min and reincubated for another 20 h. Subsequently, the supernatants were collected for quantification of ATP and HMGB1 levels using ELISA kits. In parallel, cells were fixed and incubated with antibodies to visualize CRT and HMGB1 expression by immunofluorescence staining.

Animal study

All animal experiments were approved by the Guidelines for the Animal Care and Use Committee of Shandong First Medical University & Shandong Academy of Medical Sciences and the institutional ethics guidelines. Female C57BL/6 mice (Vital River Laboratory Animal Technology Co., China) received a subcutaneous injection of LLC cells (1 × 10⁶) to establish the tumor model. Tumor volume (V) was calculated as V = length × width2/2.

Biodistribution of CBE@AM NPs

Upon reaching a tumor volume of approximately 200 mm3, LLC-bearing mice were intravenously injected with a PBS solution containing CBE@AM NPs or free Ce6 at equivalent Ce6 concentrations. In vivo fluorescence imaging was carried out with an IVIS Spectrum system (Tanon ABL X6, Shanghai, China, ex/em = 649/670 nm). Mice were sacrificed 24 h post-injection, and major tissues were harvested for ex vivo fluorescence imaging.

Antitumor effect and antitumor immunity assay

LLC tumor-bearing mice were randomized into six groups: Control, CB839, Ce6, CBE@AM, Ce6 + US, and CBE@AM + US. Each mouse received an intravenous injection of 2 mg/kg (Ce6-equivalent). Two hours later, tumors in the Ce6 and CBE@AM NPs groups were exposed to US (1 W/cm2, 5 min). The treatment regimen was repeated every second day for three consecutive cycles, with body weight and tumor volume assessed every two days.

On day 14 post-injection, mice were euthanized, and tumors, along with tumor-draining lymph nodes (LNs), were harvested for DC characterization. Splenic lymphocytes were also isolated, fluorescently labeled, and analyzed by flow cytometry. Tumors were resected and enzymatically digested into single-cell suspensions. Following antibody staining, effector T cells, and macrophages were quantified by flow cytometry.

Results

Synthesis and characterization of CBE@AM NPs

Melanin-inspired AM NPs were first synthesized via oxidative polymerization of 1,8-DHN. Subsequently, Ce6 and CB839 were co-loaded onto AM NPs to fabricate multifunctional CBE@AM NPs (Fig. 1A). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images showed that CBE@AM NPs displayed uniform spherical morphology with an average diameter of ∼ 190 nm and were slightly larger than free AM NPs (∼170 nm) (Fig. 1B,C). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images further demonstrated the monodisperse distribution and smooth surface of CBE@AM NPs, with particle sizes ranging from 180 to 200 nm (Fig. 1D). As illustrated in Fig. 1E, dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis demonstrated the average hydrodynamic diameter of CBE@AM NPs (∼200 nm) to be marginally larger than that of AM NPs (∼180 nm), suggesting successful cargo encapsulation. As shown in Fig. 1F, CBE@AM NPs displayed a measured zeta potential of about − 30 mV, which reduced the protein adsorption. The stability assays showed negligible changes in hydrodynamic size and polydispersity index (PDI) in 7 days, confirming the favorable colloidal stability of CBE@AM NPs in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (Fig. 1G).

Ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) absorption spectra of CBE@AM NPs displayed diagnostic peaks of Ce6 (400 and 660 nm) and CB839 (250 nm) (Fig. 1H), indicating their successful loading by CBE@AM NPs. Furthermore, the Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) spectrum of CBE@AM NPs exhibited characteristic peaks at 3060, 1710, and 1270 cm−1, which corresponded to the O–H stretching, C=O stretching, and C–O stretching vibrations of Ce6, respectively. In addition, the peaks observed at 1640 and 1530 cm−1 correspond to the amide group vibrations of CB839. These results further verified the successful loading of Ce6 and CB839 within the NPs (Fig. 1I). Elemental mapping analysis also verified the presence of F and S elements, which are derived from CB839, in the CBE@AM NPs (Fig. S1). Subsequently, CBE@AM NPs were degraded using DMSO to determine the drug loading content. As shown in Fig. S2, the drug content of Ce6 in CBE@AM NPs was around 20 %, as assessed by UV–Vis spectroscopy. HPLC (WAYEAL LC3200) analyses indicated that the content of CB839 in CBE@AM NPs was approximately 26 %. The release kinetics of Ce6 and CB839 from CBE@AM NPs were further assessed under various conditions using UV–Vis and HPLC, respectively. Only about 30 % of Ce6 was released in 24 h at neutral pH (7.4), (Fig. 1J). In contrast, a substantial release of 80 % of Ce6 was observed under acidic conditions (pH = 5.5) within 24 h. As depicted in Fig. S3, CB839 exhibited a similar pH-responsive release behavior, with a dramatic release of 53 % CB839 under acidic conditions within 24 h.

Cellular uptake and biodistribution

The limited cellular permeability and inadequate tumor accumulation of therapeutic agents often restrict their clinical use, leading to suboptimal efficacy and pronounced side effects. The intracellular uptake of CBE@AM NPs was first examined using CLSM and flow cytometry. CLSM analysis showed that LLC cells treated with CBE@AM NPs displayed stronger fluorescence signals compared with cells treated with free Ce6 (Fig. 2A). Moreover, flow cytometry analysis revealed that LLC cells internalized more CBE@AM NPs than free Ce6 (Fig. 2B). Also, LLC cells in CBE@AM NPs treatment group showed significantly higher fluorescence than those in free Ce6 treatment group (Fig. S4), suggesting enhanced cellular internalization. Additionally, the internalization kinetics of CBE@AM NPs across a 24-h timescale in LLC cells were performed using quantitative flow cytometry. The results indicated a time-dependent increase in fluorescence intensity in LLC cells after CBE@AM NPs treatment from 2 to 24 h (Fig. 2C and S5), indicating progressive cellular internalization. To clarify the endocytic mechanism of CBE@AM NPs, a panel of endocytosis inhibitors was utilized for investigation. The results suggested that CBE@AM NPs are mainly internalized via phagocytosis, micropinocytosis, clathrin-mediated, and caveolae‐mediated endocytosis pathways (Fig. S6).

Inspired by these in vitro findings, the in vivo biodistribution of CBE@AM NPs was investigated using an IVIS imaging system. As indicated in Fig. 2D, CBE@AM NPs generated markedly stronger and more persistent fluorescence signals in tumors compared with free Ce6, with the fluorescence intensity reaching its maximum at 2 h after injection and gradually declining thereafter (Fig. S7). Ex vivo analysis of tumor tissues and major organs collected 24 h after injection demonstrated a significant enhancement of the tumor-associated fluorescence intensity of CBE@AM NPs compared with free Ce6 (Fig. 2E and S8), indicating predominant tumor accumulation and prolonged retention of CBE@AM NPs. These results indicated that CBE@AM NPs possessed both excellent cellular uptake and tumor-targeting capability.

In vitro assessments of the therapeutic effects

The therapeutic effects of CBE@AM NPs against LLC cells were assessed using MTT assays. As illustrated in Fig. 2F, free drugs (CB839 and Ce6) and CBE@AM NPs, either with or without US irradiation, exhibited concentration-dependent cytotoxicity. CBE@AM NPs in combination with US irradiation demonstrated significantly enhanced cell-killing effects compared with other treatments at equivalent concentrations of free drugs. This could be attributed to the Ce6-mediated SDT and enhanced intracellular accumulation of Ce6 facilitated by the NPs. Additionally, empty AM NPs exhibited negligible toxicity toward LLC cells even upon US irradiation, suggesting their minimal tumor-killing ability. As illustrated in Fig. 2, Fig. 2, CBE@AM NPs combined with US irradiation induced the death of the highest proportion of LLC cells. Live/Dead cell staining using with calcein-AM (green, viable) and PI (red, dead) was also performed to assess the therapeutic effect of CBE@AM NPs by CLSM. As shown in Fig. 3A, CBE@AM NPs in combination with US irradiation exerted a higher cell-killing effect compared with other treatments.

In vitro evaluation of oxidative stress and glutamine metabolism inhibition

US-triggered SDT relies on ROS generation, which induces oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, and subsequent apoptotic cell death. The intracellular ROS levels in different groups were measured using DCFH-DA probe to assess the sonodynamic performance of CBE@AM NPs. As depicted in Fig. 3B, the robust green fluorescence signals of ROS were observed in LLC cells after Ce6 and CBE@AM NPs treatment upon US irradiation, confirming efficient ROS generation. Notably, CBE@AM NPs induced markedly higher ROS accumulation than free Ce6 upon US irradiation, highlighting their enhanced sonodynamic potency. This more significant increase in intracellular ROS levels was further confirmed by flow cytometry (Fig. 3C and S9). Excessive ROS can directly induce alteration of the MMP, resulting in the damage and dysfunction of mitochondrial membrane. The polarization in MMP was assessed with the JC-1 probe using CLSM. As depicted in Fig. 3D, the cells in all groups displayed strong red fluorescence in the absence of US, indicating intact MMP. Following US stimulation, both Ce6 and CBE@AM NPs experienced a shift from red to green fluorescence, reflecting significant MMP depolarization. The most severe impairment was observed in the CBE@AM NPs + US group (Fig. 3D). Consistently, pronounced cytoplasmic leakage of double-stranded DNA (dsDNA), which is a hallmark of mitochondrial damage, was observed in the CBE@AM NPs + US group (Fig. S10), further confirming severe mitochondrial dysfunctions.

GSH, a key molecule for maintaining intracellular redox homeostasis, was quantified in LLC cells after various treatments. A remarkable decrease in intracellular GSH levels was observed in both CB839- and CBE@AM NP-treated groups compared with other treatment groups (Fig. 3E). The CBE@AM NP + US group exhibited the most pronounced depletion of GSH. Previous studies indicated that GLS inhibition disturbed glutamine metabolism, thereby impairing GSH synthesis. Western blot analysis was performed on LLC cells after various formulations to evaluate GLS expression. The results demonstrated a substantial reduce in GLS protein levels in cells treated with CB839 and CBE@AM NPs, with the latter inducing a stronger GLS downregulation compared with CB839 alone (Fig. 3F and S11). Immunofluorescence imaging further corroborated these findings, revealing significant downregulation of GLS protein levels in LLC cells treated with either CB839 or CBE@AM NPs (Fig. 3G). Collectively, these results suggested that CBE@AM NPs amplified intracellular oxidative stress through dual mechanisms: enhanced ROS production via SDT and GLS-dependent GSH depletion, ultimately driving increased tumor cell death.

Meanwhile, glutamine metabolism in macrophages was further investigated via a transwell assay. As shown Fig. S12, macrophages co-cultured with CBE@AM NP-treated LLC cells displayed markedly elevated intracellular levels of glutamine, glutathione, and ATP, indicating enhanced glutamine metabolism. Moreover, oxygen consumption rate (OCR) and extracellular acidification rate (ECAR) analyses revealed that CBE@AM NP treatment induced a metabolic shift from oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) toward glycolysis (Figs. S13, S14). These results demonstrated that CBE@AM NPs reprogram macrophage metabolism by promoting glutamine utilization and inhibiting mitochondrial respiration, thereby facilitating macrophage repolarization.

In vitro evaluation of ICD induction and immune activation

Emerging studies have confirmed that US-triggered intracellular ROS elevation induce ICD of tumor cells and the consequent release of DAMP, thereby enhancing antitumor immunity. ICD-related hallmarks were evaluated to determine the effect of CBE@AM NPs. CLSM imaging revealed a significant translocation of calreticulin (CRT) in LLC cells after Ce6 and CBE@AM NPs treatment upon US irradiation (Fig. 4A), suggesting enhanced surface CRT presentation. Upon ultrasound activation, cells exposed to CBE@AM NPs displayed stronger CRT fluorescence compared with the Ce6 group. Flow cytometry also revealed that the fluorescence intensity of CRT in LLC cells after CBE@AM NPs treatment upon US irradiation was higher than that under other treatments (Fig. 4B and S15). In parallel, the CLSM images demonstrated that CBE@AM NP + US treatment exhibited the weakest green fluorescence of high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) (Fig. 4C), suggesting the extracellular release of HMGB1. Additionally, CBE@AM NP + US treatment induced significantly higher HMGB1 release (Fig. 4D) and ATP secretion (Fig. 4E) from LLC cells compared with all other treatments, contributing extra evidence for effective ICD.

The interaction between tumor cells and adjacent immune cells can be enhanced by ICD, in which the surface-exposed tumor antigens and released DAMPs trigger strong immune responses. Flow cytometry was used to evaluate the maturation of bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) and macrophage repolarization driven by CBE@AM NPs-mediated ICD. The LLC cells supernatant preincubated with different samples was further co-cultured with BMDCs, and the DC maturation was evaluated. As depicted in Fig. 4F and H, Ce6 upon US irradiation modestly elevated CD80 and CD86 expression. This could be attributed to SDT-induced ICD. CBE@AM NP + US treatment most significantly enhanced DC maturation, benefiting from enhanced ICD mediated by GSH depletion-amplified cellular ROS level. Subsequently, the effects of CBE@AM NPs on macrophage repolarization in vitro were investigated. M2 macrophages derived from RAW264.7 cells were co-cultured with LLC cells supernatant after different treatment. Parallel to the DC maturation findings, CBE@AM NPs under ultrasound elicited the highest level of macrophage repolarization (Fig. 4G and I). This was attributed to enhanced ICD mediated by GSH depletion-amplified cellular ROS level. In addition, ELISA kits were used to detect the production of TNF-α and IL-6 by BMDCs. The results showed that CBE@AM NP + US treatment significantly increased the secretion of TNF-α and IL-6 (Fig. 4J and K), providing additional evidence for the immune activation triggered through CBE@AM NPs. Collectively, these findings demonstrated that CBE@AM NPs initiate strong immune responses, particularly under US activation, by inhibiting glutamine metabolism and inducing ICD, thereby effectively remodeling the immunosuppressive TME.

Antitumor efficacy and biosafety of CBE@AM NPs

Inspired by their strong performance in vitro, the in vivo therapeutic efficacy of CBE@AM NPs was further analyzed using the LLC subcutaneous tumor–bearing C57BL/6 mouse model. As displayed in Fig. 5A, mice were randomly assigned into six groups: (G1) PBS, (G2) CB839, (G3) Ce6, (G4) CBE@AM NPs, (G5) Ce6 + US, and (G6) CBE@AM NP + US. Treatments were administered intravenously at an equivalent Ce6 dose of 2 mg/kg on days 0, 2, and 4, followed by US irradiation 2 h after injection. As shown in Fig. 5B, C, the tumor volumes significantly decreased in the Ce6 + US and CBE@AM NP + US groups, whereas the tumor growth rates continued to increase uncontrollably in the other four groups. CBE@AM NPs displayed the more pronounced suppression compared with free Ce6 upon US irradiation. This was due to improved tumor accumulation and stronger sonodynamic activity. Furthermore, the images and average tumor weights showed that CBE@AM NP + US effectively inhibited the growth of the tumor compared with other treatments (Fig. 5E,F). Subsequently, immunostaining assays were performed to further elucidate the antitumor effects and gain mechanistic insights into CBE@AM NPs. Terminal-deoxynucleotidyl transferase mediated nick end labeling (TUNEL) staining revealed that CBE@AM NP + US treatment triggered the highest levels of cellular apoptosis among all treatment groups (Fig. 5G). Additionally, the Ki67 staining images revealed that CBE@AM NP + US treatment significantly restrained the tumor proliferation, as indicated by a decrease in brown-stained cells (Fig. 5G), suggesting its superior tumor-inhibitory potential. These results confirmed that the combination of CBE@AM NPs with US irradiation synergistically induced robust apoptosis and blocked tumor cell proliferation, achieving potent in vivo tumor suppression.

The body weights of LLC-bearing mice were monitored during the entire therapy to assess the biosafety of CBE@AM NPs in vivo. As illustrated in Fig. 5D, body weights of mice treated with CBE@AM NPs showed no significant fluctuations during the treatment period. Moreover, major organs were isolated from LLC-bearing mice for H&E staining. Negligible morphological changes were observed in these organ slices, suggesting the low systemic toxicity of CBE@AM NPs (Fig. S16). Furthermore, the blood samples were collected to assess the effects of CBE@AM NPs on liver and kidney function. No remarkable variations in the levels of urea, creatinine, alanine transaminase, albumin, alkaline phosphatase, and uric acid were observed among all the treatment groups (Fig. S17). Also, hemolysis assays demonstrated negligible hemolysis (all rates < 0.5 %), further supporting the favorable biocompatibility of CBE@AM NPs (Fig. S18). These findings implied that CBE@AM NPs achieved robust in vivo antitumor efficacy with excellent biocompatibility, highlighting their potential for safe and effective sono-immunometabolic therapy.

CBE@AM NPs enhance antitumor immune activation in vivo

To investigate whether CBE@AM NPs could effectively stimulate antitumor immunity in vivo, immune cells from LNs, spleens, and tumors were detected using flow cytometry. The percentage of CD11c+CD80+ and CD11c+CD86+ markedly increased in the CBE@AM NP + US group compared with the other groups in tumor-draining LNs (Fig. 6, Fig. 6, and S19), indicating enhanced DC maturation activity. A similar trend was observed in the tumor tissues, where the level of DC maturation also substantially increased in the CBE@AM NP + US group (Fig. 6, Fig. 6, and S20, suggesting that CBE@AM NP + US treatment effectively enhanced the activation of DCs and antigen presentation within the TME. The adaptive immune response was also significantly reinforced. Flow cytometry results demonstrated that the proportion of CD3+CD4+ and CD3+CD8+ T cells in LNs treated with CBE@AM NP + US was greater than those in LNs under other treatments (Fig. 6C and S21). In addition, the CBE@AM NP + US treatment produced over a twofold rise in CD3+CD4+ T cells compared with PBS in the spleens (Fig. 6D, and S22).

As shown in Fig. 6E, and S23, PBS-treated tumors exhibited low effector immune cell infiltration, consistent with the immunologically “cold” phenotype of the LLC model. In contrast, the infiltration of effect T cell in the tumors significantly increased in the CBE@AM NP + US group, where the proportion of CD8+ T cells achieved nearly 3-fold higher levels than those in the PBS group. These results confirmed that CBE@AM NPs efficiently promoted systemic T-cell activation and their recruitment into tumor sites. Furthermore, the tumor immune microenvironment was remodeled through macrophage reprogramming. Treatment with CBE@AM NPs shifted macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype (CD11b+F4/80+CD86+), further confirming systemic immune activation (Fig. 6F, and S24). Collectively, these findings demonstrated that CBE@AM NPs synergistically enhance antitumor immunity upon US irradiation through improving DC maturation, expanding CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell populations, and reprogramming macrophages. This multifaceted mechanism shifted the TME from immunosuppressive to an immunostimulatory state, thereby inducing potent antitumor immune responses to suppress tumor growth.

Discussion

Lung cancer persists as the top cause of cancer-related mortality across the globe, with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) making up roughly 85 % of the overall lung cancer incidence. Characterized by sparse tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes and an immunosuppressive TME enriched in immune-suppressive cells and inhibitory cytokines, NSCLCs are often classified as immunologically “cold” tumors. In this study, PBS-treated LLC tumors exhibited CD3+CD4+ and CD3+CD8+ T cell proportions of 5 % and 12 %, respectively—consistent with the “cold tumor” phenotype of the LLC model (Fig. 6E). Relative to this baseline immune profile, CBE@AM + US treatment significantly increased the proportion of mature DCs (2.4-fold), T cell infiltration (nearly 3-fold), and the proportion of M1 macrophages (1.8-fold) in tumors (Fig. 6). These results demonstrate that CBE@AM + US can remodel the immune microenvironment, converting “cold tumors” to “hot tumors”.

The excellent antitumor efficacy is mainly attributed to the amplification of SDT-induced cytotoxicity and reprogramming of the immunosuppressive TME by CBE@AM NPs. First, CBE@AM NPs enhanced the bioavailability of CB839 and Ce6, thereby promoting tumor accumulation. As a carrier, melanin-inspired NPs (AM NPs) exhibited no significant effects on tumor cell inhibition, ROS production, mitochondrial damage, and GSH generation even under US irradiation (Fig. 2, Fig. 3). This observation thereby confirms that the therapeutic efficacy is entirely derived from the loaded Ce6 and CB839. Upon US irradiation, released Ce6 triggered robust ROS production, which destroyed tumor cells and induced the ICD effect to activate immune responses. Concurrently, released CB839 disrupted GSH-dependent redox homeostasis, sensitizing tumor cells to SDT-generated ROS. Furthermore, CB839 blocked tumor glutamine metabolism, remodeling the immunosuppressive TME and synergizing with SDT-induced ICD to elicit potent antitumor immunity, ultimately suppressing tumor growth.

Although the CBE@AM NPs exhibit potential for immunometabolic reprogramming to enhance the therapeutic efficacy of SDT, several limitations of this work warrant consideration as key directions for future research. First, the simplified co-culture system, wherein immune cells were exposed to supernatants from treated tumor cells, can not fully recapitulate the complexity of the in vivo TME and may overestimate the degree of immune activation. Thus, additional relevant models (e.g. transwell co-culture, 3D co-culture systems) or single-cell metabolomic analysis of tumor-infiltrating immune cells should be employed to further validate the effects of CBE@AM NPs. Second, it is important to note that the current study lacks an assessment of long-term outcomes, including overall survival benefits and the establishment of immunological memory capable of preventing tumor metastasis and recurrence. Third, the comprehensive safety evaluation of CBE@AM NPs remains insufficient. Although hemolysis assays and H&E staining of major organs demonstrated their favorable biocompatibility, the long-term and repeated-dose toxicity of CBE@AM NPs also require investigation in future study. These issues are critical for evaluating the translational potential of this strategy and represent key directions for our subsequent research.

Conclusions

Here, we successfully engineered a metabolic nanoblocker (CBE@AM NP) that synergized SDT with glutamine metabolism inhibition to establish a closed-loop strategy for potent sono-immunometabolic therapy. After systemic administration, CBE@AM NPs efficiently accumulated at tumor sites and were internalized by tumor cells. Upon US irradiation, the nanoblocker amplified oxidative stress and ICD via SDT-mediated intracellular ROS generation and glutamine restriction–induced depletion of GSH. This dual action not only amplified SDT-induced cytotoxicity but also reprogrammed the immunosuppressive TME by promoting DC maturation, repolarizing macrophages toward an M1 phenotype, and enhancing T-cell infiltration. The resultant robust systemic immune activation in vivo highlights the strong translational potential of this combined approach. Overall, this study provides a promising nanotherapeutic platform by integrating ROS amplification and immunometabolic reprogramming for achieving effective and durable cancer therapy.
