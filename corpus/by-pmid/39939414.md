# Advances in Vaccine-Based Therapies for Pancreatic Cancer.

## Abstract

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains one of the most lethal cancers, with a 5-year survival rate that has improved only marginally over the past 30 years, despite numerous clinical trials. PDAC poses several unique challenges, including early metastatic spread and a predilection for liver metastasis. It is also highly resistant to anti-tumor immunity and immunotherapy due to its dense and immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, low immunogenicity, and systemic immune suppression. PDAC has a low mutational burden, defective antigen presentation, and immune checkpoint molecule upregulation, which reduce immune recognition. Together, these factors leave PDAC as an "immune cold" tumor with minimal cytotoxic T-cell activity. Novel therapeutic approaches are urgently needed to reinvigorate anti-tumor immunity. Recent advances, such as adjuvant personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines and mutant-KRAS targeted vaccines, have demonstrated sustained vaccine-induced T cell responses that are associated with improved recurrence-free survival in surgically resected PDAC. Combining different vaccine approaches with optimal sequencing of chemotherapy, surgery, radiotherapy, and other immunotherapies may further enhance outcomes. PDAC vaccines represent a promising strategy for overcoming PDAC's resistance to conventional therapies, with ongoing trials exploring their potential to improve long-term survival.

Advances in Vaccine-Based Therapies for Pancreatic Cancer

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) remains one of the most lethal cancers, with a 5-year survival rate that has improved only marginally over the past 30 years, despite numerous clinical trials. PDAC poses several unique challenges, including early metastatic spread and a predilection for liver metastasis. Introduction

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is the tenth most common cancer in the USA and the third leading cause of cancer-related mortality in the USA. Despite > 2000 interventional clinical trials over the past 30 years—including nearly 250 phase III randomized trials—the 5-year relative survival has only increased from 5 to 13%. The majority of immunotherapy breakthroughs in oncology over the past decade have involved immune checkpoint blockade (ICB), which has improved survival outcomes for many immunogenic solid tumor histologies (e.g., melanoma, lung cancer, urothelial carcinoma), including subgroups of patients with immunologically “cold” solid tumor histologies who have high microsatellite instability (MSI-H), DNA mismatch-repair deficiency (dMMR), and/or high tumor mutational burden. However, this subgroup of patients accounts for < 2% of all PDAC patients, and the ICB (e.g., anti-programmed death-ligand 1 [PD-L1] ± anti-cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 [CTLA-4]) response rate in unselected PDAC patients is < 5%. The major challenges to improving survival in PDAC can be distilled down to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME) (Fig. 1), few tumor-reactive T cells, and early metastatic dissemination, especially to the liver, which is a tolerogenic organ.

The PDAC TME is profoundly immunosuppressive, harboring regulatory T cells, M2-polarized tumor-associated macrophages, tumor-associated neutrophils, and myeloid-derived suppressor cells. These cells release high levels of immunosuppressive cytokines such as TGF-β, IL-10, and VEGF thereby dampening immune responses. The TME also presents a fibrotic stroma composed of cancer-associated fibroblasts, extracellular matrix, and hyaluronic acid. This stroma may act as a physical barrier, impeding immune cell access to the tumor and releasing factors that impair the proliferation, function, and infiltration of cytotoxic anti-tumor immune cells. In addition to this locally immunosuppressive niche, 50% of PDAC patients present with metastatic disease at the time of diagnosis. Even in those without macrometastases, disseminated tumor cells are frequently detected in liver biopsies at the time of resection. Although tumor-specific CD8+ T cells can eradicate disseminated tumor cells, PDAC patients have fewer such cells than patients with any other common solid tumor histology. PDAC eradication is also hindered by its proclivity for liver metastasis. The liver is intrinsically tolerogenic, as it continuously processes large quantities of immunostimulatory antigens from the gut without inciting chronic inflammation. Liver metastases take advantage of the liver’s natural immune tolerance mechanisms and recruit immunosuppressive immune cells to promote local and systemic immunosuppression. Consequently, patients with liver metastases have fewer tumor-specific CD8+ T cells in circulation and within the liver, lower response rates to immunotherapy, and worse overall survival than patients with metastases confined to other organs. Therefore, novel therapeutic strategies are needed to reinvigorate systemic anti-tumor immunity and promote the eradication of disseminated PDAC.

Types of Vaccines Used to Treat PDAC

mRNA- and DNA-Based Vaccines

Although most PDACs have a low mutational burden and few neoantigen-specific T cells, the vast majority of PDACs contain neoantigens. A recent phase 1 study (NCT04161755) conducted at Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center demonstrated that in patients with resected PDAC, sequential administration of atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1), individualized mRNA vaccines targeting up to 20 per-patient neoantigens (autogene cevumeran), and modified FOLFIRINOX (mFFX) is safe, feasible in a clinically-relevant time frame, and can induce high-magnitude neoantigen-specific T cell responses in 50% of PDAC patients after upfront surgery. Immune responses elicited by adjuvant mRNA neoantigen vaccination correlated with delayed PDAC recurrence. Notably, in that study, 98% of vaccine-expanded CD8+ T cell clones were expanded de novo, meaning that they were not present in the blood, tumor, tumor-draining lymph nodes, or adjacent tissue prior to vaccination. This underscores the paucity of neoantigen-specific CD8+ T cells in PDAC prior to vaccination.

At 3-year follow-up, several important findings were reported. First, mRNA vaccine-expanded CD8+ T cell clones are longevous with > 80% of neoantigen-specific CD8+ T cell clones persisting. Furthermore, these vaccine-expanded CD8+ T cells are still responsive to target neoantigens in ex vivo rechallenge experiments for 6 of 8 vaccine responders. Third, vaccine response still correlates with recurrence-free survival with 3-year recurrence-free survival rates of 75% and 12.5% for responders and non-responders, respectively (HR 0.14, p = 0.007) (Fig. 2). The promising findings from this phase I trial led to an ongoing multicenter phase 2 randomized trial, IMCODE003 (NCT05968326), which will assess the efficacy and safety of autogene cevumeran plus atezolizumab and mFFX versus mFFX alone after pancreaticoduodenectomy. In addition to IMCODE003, there are two other ongoing trials of personalized mRNA vaccines for PDAC (Table 1).

Recent work has shown that adjuvant DNA-based neoantigen vaccines are safe and can induce neoantigen-specific immune responses in patients with surgically resected PDAC. DNA vaccines offer several benefits compared to mRNA vaccines, particularly in terms of storage and stability. They can be preserved for extended periods under standard freezing conditions and tend to degrade less readily (Table 2). However, DNA vaccines also present certain challenges. One concern is the possibility of integrating into the host genome. Furthermore, DNA vaccines are less immunogenic compared to mRNA vaccines and may require co-delivery with an immune adjuvant. 

Dendritic Cell-Based Vaccines

Dendritic cells play a major role in bridging innate and adaptive immunity by presenting processed antigens to T cells via major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules. Type 1 conventional dendritic cells (cDC1s) are a specialized subset of dendritic cells (DCs) that play a pivotal role in initiating and regulating immune responses, particularly in anti-viral and anti-tumor immunity. Given the immunosuppressive PDAC tumor microenvironment and the importance of conventional dendritic cells for anti-tumor immunity, it is not surprising that there are few tumor-infiltrating dendritic cells in PDAC, with one study demonstrating that only 4% (N = 2/47) of resected tumors contain significant numbers (> 3 per high-powered field) of dendritic cells. In fact, the critical type 1 conventional dendritic cells are particularly rare and 100-fold less abundant compared to tumor-associated macrophages or neutrophils in human PDAC. Conversely, human lung tumors are characterized by much greater numbers of tumor-infiltrating type 1 conventional dendritic cells, which may partially explain enhanced CD8+ T cell activity in lung tumors. Patients with PDAC tumors that are cDC1-high or BATF3-high are associated with better overall survival than cDC1-low or BATF3-low PDAC tumors. The importance of type I conventional dendritic cells for ICB response in PDAC was demonstrated in a study of PDAC associated with pancreatitis where these tumors have an increase in activated type 1 conventional dendritic cells, and ICB in these tumors leads to the activation of cytotoxic CD8+ T cells.

Preclinical PDAC models have shown that increasing the number of tumor-infiltrating conventional dendritic cells enhances T cell-related anti-tumor immunity and restoring conventional dendritic cell function improves the effectiveness of radiotherapy for advanced PDAC. A recent single-center, phase I/II study tested dendritic cell-based immunotherapy in patients with surgically resected PDAC who had completed standard-of-care systemic therapy without disease progression. At 2 years, the recurrence-free survival rate was 64% and activated CD4+ T cell responses were detected in the blood.

Advantages to dendritic cell vaccines include the ability to ex vivo load tumor-specific antigens that can be directly presented to T cells upon infusion. Conversely, mRNA vaccines require the in vivo uptake and translation of mRNA into protein, processes that could potentially be impacted by constitutive tumor-intrinsic interferon signaling. Chronic interferon signaling activates conserved anti-viral mechanisms (e.g., phosphorylation of eIF2α) to reduce the translation of foreign mRNA into protein to prevent viral replication; it can also lead to the activation of RNases (e.g., RNase L), which can degrade mRNA in the cell prior to their translation into a neoantigen. Prolonged in vivo exposure to chronic interferon signaling can also lead to dendritic cell exhaustion and dysfunction, limiting their ability to present translated tumor-specific neoantigens to T cells. Despite the advantages conferred by dendritic cell vaccines, their cost and prolonged manufacturing timelines may limit their scalability and widespread adoption.

Peptide-Based Vaccines

Peptide-based neoantigen vaccines have been extensively studied in PDAC given their economical manufacture and ability to induce strong neoantigen-specific CD4+ T cell responses. The most common peptide-based vaccine targets are tumor-associated antigens that are overexpressed by PDAC cells and minimally expressed (or absent) in normal tissue, such as KRAS, WT1 (i.e., Wilms’ Tumor 1), CEA (i.e., carcinoembryonic antigen), MUC1 (i.e., mucin 1), and hTERT. KRAS driver mutations are present in approximately 90% of PDAC tumors, and as a result, a universal vaccine targeting mutant KRAS is highly desirable for PDAC. The AMPLIFY-201 trial demonstrated that off-the-shelf peptide vaccines (e.g., ELI-002 2P) can induce high-magnitude G12D- and G12R-mKRAS-specific CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses. Furthermore, the vaccine was safe and well tolerated. Notably, the presence of high-magnitude vaccine-induced T cell responses was associated with improved recurrence-free survival. Expanding the amphiphile modification to 7 KRAS and NRAS peptides—G12D, G12R, G12V, G12A, G12C, G12S, G13D—together with a CpG oligonucleotide adjuvant, the ongoing AMPLIFY-7P trial will assess adjuvant ELI-002 7P vs observation in a phase 2 randomized trial (NCT05726864).

The favorable toxicity profile, off-the-shelf convenience, and scalability of non-personalized peptide-based vaccines make them an appealing area for development. However, they are less immunogenic compared to nucleic-acid-based vaccines, and they generate less robust CD8+ T cell responses. Consequently, it is important to develop treatment regimens where these vaccines are paired with immune adjuvants, conventional cytotoxic therapies, and/or ICB to increase their effectiveness. Personalized peptide-based vaccines targeting immunogenic neoantigens might lead to more robust systemic T cell responses, but this can be restricted by human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing, and custom peptide synthesis and purification for personalized peptide-based vaccines can delay vaccine delivery to the patient.

Whole-Tumor Cell-Based Vaccines

Whole-tumor cell-based vaccines use either autologous or allogeneic whole-tumor cells to prime the immune system to a variety of tumor-associated antigens. The whole tumor cells are irradiated to impair proliferative capacity, and then, they are often genetically modified to secrete granulocyte–macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), so they attract critical antigen-presenting cells, such as dendritic cells, to the site of the vaccine. The most commonly tested whole-tumor cell-based vaccine for PDAC is GVAX, which consists of irradiated tumor cells that have been modified to secrete GM-CSF. In advanced PDAC, GVAX in combination with other immunotherapies has been largely ineffective in the first-line maintenance setting and as a second-line therapy; however, in patients with resectable PDAC, adding neoadjuvant nivolumab (anti-PD-1) to GVAX significantly increases the density of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells in lymph node aggregates and increases the diversity of the intratumoral CD8 + T cell population. Moreover, adding the CD137 agonist, urelumab, to GVAX and nivolumab increases intratumoral activated CD8+ CD137+ cytotoxic T cells and leads to numerically improved disease-free survival.

Whole-tumor cell-based vaccines, such as GVAX, likely need to be paired with other immunotherapies and/or cytotoxic therapies to maximize their effectiveness, but the selection and sequencing of complementary therapies is critical. A randomized controlled trial of neoadjuvant GVAX with or without low-dose cyclophosphamide identified significantly worse disease-free survival with GVAX plus oral cyclophosphamide (100 mg once daily for 1 week on and 1 week off starting on the day of vaccination) versus GVAX alone. The worse disease-free survival was especially surprising given that low-dose cyclophosphamide did decrease intratumoral regulatory T cells. Despite more promising results for GVAX in the resectable setting, larger clinical trials are needed comparing the efficacy of GVAX-based immunotherapy approaches versus personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccine-based immunotherapy approaches and peptide vaccine approaches.

Combination Therapies

Other Immunotherapies

Personalized neoantigen vaccines can broaden and intensify tumor-specific T cell responses. Inhibiting the PD-L1/PD-1 axis with an immune checkpoint inhibitor could amplify the effects of personalized neoantigen vaccines by enhancing initial T cell priming or reactivation upon encountering antigens, and/or by boosting the function of dysfunctional T cells exposed to prolonged antigen presence within the tumor. Therefore, combining personalized neoantigen vaccines with ICB (e.g., anti-PD-[L]1) may produce stronger anti-tumor immune responses, resulting in better clinical outcomes. This hypothesis is supported by pre-clinical models where antigen-specific immunity, combined with PD-L1/PD-1 pathway blockade, demonstrated greater efficacy than using either pathway inhibitor alone, even in cases where the single-agent vaccine showed limited activity. However, many questions remain with regard to the combination of vaccines with ICB. The sequencing of therapeutics may be critical. Combinatorial strategies may need to allow adequate time for vaccine priming before inhibiting the PD-1/PD-L1 axis and avoid blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 axis in unprimed or partially primed CD8+ T cells which can lead to dysfunctional PD-1+CD38hi cells and resistance to ICB.

Chemotherapy

Cytotoxic chemotherapy is an integral part of the standard-of-care treatment regimens across all stages of PDAC. However, the optimal timing for the administration of vaccines relative to chemotherapy is not fully understood. Some chemotherapy agents, such as low-dose cyclophosphamide administered prior to vaccination, induce the depletion of immunosuppressive regulatory T cells and myeloid-derived suppressor cells and immunogenic cell death, significantly expand vaccine-specific CD8+ T cell clones, and numerically improve survival in advanced PDAC. Alternatively, administering neoadjuvant low-dose cyclophosphamide on the same day as vaccination was associated with worse disease-free survival compared to vaccination alone.

The effect of PDAC chemotherapy regimens, such as mFFX (i.e., the preferred chemotherapy regimen for all PDAC patients with adequate ECOG performance status), on expanding neoantigen-specific T cell populations is not fully characterized. Moreover, the premedications administered with chemotherapy regimens such as mFFX include potent immunosuppressive corticosteroids, such as dexamethasone; therefore, caution is warranted when planning the sequencing of cytotoxic chemotherapy and vaccines in PDAC. This is an area deserving of further study.

Radiotherapy

Given that locally aggressive tumors are responsible for ~ 30% of PDAC-related deaths, in unresectable locally advanced PDAC, consolidative ablative radiotherapy (e.g., 50 Gy in 5 fractions or 67.5 Gy in 15 fractions or 75 Gy in 25 fractions) with or without concurrent chemotherapy is often used to maximize locoregional disease control. Similar to chemotherapy, there are some theoretical advantages to various radiotherapy-vaccine sequencing approaches. When planning for radiotherapy before/during/after vaccination, it is critical to protect primary (e.g., bone marrow, thymus) and secondary (spleen, lymph nodes, etc.) lymphoid organs from unnecessary radiation. Lipoplex (LPX) mRNA neoantigen vaccines target antigen-presenting cells in lymph-node compartments, especially plasmacytoid dendritic cells in the spleen. In fact, 4 to 5 h after intravenous LPX mRNA neoantigen vaccination, PET/CT imaging shows a 43 to 112% increase in FDG uptake specifically in the spleen, and increased FDG uptake in lymphoid organs is associated with the activation of antigen-presenting cells. Of note, in the phase I trial of adjuvant mRNA vaccination following upfront resection in resectable PDAC, vaccine response rates were numerically lower in the patients who underwent splenectomy (28.6% vs 66.7%, p = 0.3), suggesting the importance of splenic immune cell populations following IV LPX mRNA vaccination.

Although tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes are moderately radioresistant due to TGF-beta-mediated reprogramming, lymphocytes in the peripheral blood and lymph nodes are considered highly sensitive to ionizing radiation. In humans, studies have shown that increasing splenic radiotherapy dose is associated with reduced peripheral lymphocyte counts, with every 1 Gy increase in mean splenic dose corresponding to a 1% decrease in absolute peripheral lymphocyte count at nadir. And in locally advanced PDAC patients receiving conventional chemoradiotherapy, increasing splenic dose was associated with increased risk for severe lymphopenia and severe lymphopenia was associated with significantly worse overall survival. Given the importance of immune cells in the spleen for the mechanism of action of IV mRNA vaccines, and the close proximity of the spleen to the pancreas, arranging radiotherapy treatment fields in a way that minimizes dose to the spleen may have implications for IV mRNA neoantigen vaccine responses.

Future Directions

Neoantigen Selection and Tumor Heterogeneity

One of the key challenges for developing effective PDAC vaccines is appropriate neoantigen selection, which is particularly challenging in the setting of intra- and inter-tumoral heterogeneity (Fig. 1). A common feature of many heterogeneous tumors is chromosomal instability (CIN), which is the process of chromosome missegregation over consecutive cell divisions. CIN drives tumor evolution through generating genomic copy number heterogeneity as substrate for natural selection. Consequently, tumor subclones with genomic and/or epigenomic features conferring enhanced fitness (e.g., clonal neoantigen loss, MHC class I downregulation, tumor suppressor gene deletion, chemoresistance) survive when exposed to selective pressures—such as immunosurveillance, cell cycle checkpoints, harsh microenvironments, and cytotoxic therapies—at local and/or distant sites. With many CIN-high tumors, this systematic evolutionary selection ultimately yields resilient subclones capable of colonizing distant organs. This likely explains the increased rates of metastasis and poor overall survival observed with CIN-high tumors, including PDAC.

CIN can appear early in the PDAC disease course with resultant whole-genome doubling (WGD) identified in approximately 20% of resectable PDAC primary tumors. And, in resectable PDAC, CIN, WGD, and increased copy number alterations are all associated with worse overall survival. Given that advanced PDAC is associated with CIN and most PDAC patients have advanced disease at the time of diagnosis, identifying “high-quality” neoantigen targets for vaccination that confer tumor fitness—and are thus conserved in advanced PDAC subclones—is a critical area of investigation.

Vaccine Manufacturability After Neoadjuvant Therapy Using the Resected PDAC Specimen

A large fraction of the 20% of PDAC patients with resectable disease in the USA undergo neoadjuvant chemotherapy prior to pancreatectomy. It is unknown how neoadjuvant chemotherapy impacts the quantity and quality of immunogenic PDAC neoantigens that would be ideal for vaccination. Moreover, induction mFFX is the standard of care for fit patients with borderline-resectable, locally advanced, and metastatic PDAC. Consequently, establishing the feasibility of manufacturing personalized vaccines after neoadjuvant mFFX will be critical for future clinical trials testing personalized neoantigen vaccines in the vast majority of fit PDAC patients who have either borderline-resectable, locally advanced, or metastatic disease.

Vaccine Manufacturability Using EUS-Guided Core Biopsy Specimens

The current production times for personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines are approximately 6 to 8 weeks. For quick vaccination following surgery, the ideal situation would involve manufacturing the vaccine concurrently with surgical treatment and postoperative recovery. This necessitates starting the vaccine production before the surgery, even in the absence of the resected tumor. While personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines have been developed from core biopsies for unresectable tumors of non-PDAC histologies, the viability of this method has not been proven for PDAC. Several challenges complicate the creation of a personalized neoantigen vaccine using a PDAC primary tumor biopsy, including (i) low tumor content, (ii) dense surrounding stroma, and (iii) intratumoral heterogeneity. Therefore, demonstrating the feasibility of producing personalized pancreatic cancer vaccines using a pre-treatment endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)-guided core biopsy of the primary pancreatic tumor, which contains minimal neoplastic material, is essential for manufacturing mRNA vaccines for patients with more advanced unresectable disease.

Enhancing Vaccine Efficacy in the Advanced Disease Setting

Approximately 80% of PDAC patients have unresectable locally advanced or metastatic disease at the time of diagnosis. Given that vaccines work best in the setting of minimal residual disease rather than gross macroscopic disease, only a minority of PDAC patients are currently able to receive mRNA neoantigen vaccines in the “optimal” adjuvant setting. The adjuvant setting offers several benefits for evaluating personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines like autogene cevumeran, especially when compared to the advanced disease stage. The period between post-surgery recovery and the clinically acceptable start of adjuvant therapy aligns well with the time needed to develop and produce autogene cevumeran. Additionally, the presence of minimal residual disease after surgery enhances the therapy’s mechanism of action, as the balance between autogene cevumeran-generated T cells (which take weeks to form) and cancer cells is more favorable. This environment also likely features reduced tumor clonal diversity and a less immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment. Moreover, disseminated PDAC is more likely to have total loss of HLA class I compared to PDAC at the primary site (43% of metastases vs 6% of primary tumors); therefore, eradication of all disseminated disease would likely be more difficult—even with vaccine-induced neoantigen-specific T cells—in more advanced disease.

Technological advancements enabling the safe delivery of highly conformal radiotherapy to the pancreas to ablative doses have made durable local control a possibility for a majority of patients with unresectable locally advanced disease. Efforts to quantify residual disease burden after ablative radiotherapy will be critical for identifying favorable locally advanced PDAC patients (i.e., those with minimal residual disease) who may benefit from mRNA neoantigen vaccination plus ICB after induction chemotherapy and ablative radiotherapy. In addition to locally advanced PDAC, there is likely a role for personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccines in patients with resectable synchronous “limited oligometastatic disease.”

Oligometastatic disease has been proposed as an intermediate state, wherein patients with a limited burden of metastatic disease (e.g., ≤ 5 metastases), favorable disease biology, and favorable performance status may potentially benefit from local therapy. Randomized trials of patients with oligometastatic disease have demonstrated improved progression-free survival and/or OS with local therapy for pancreatic, colorectal, gastric, non-small cell lung, and prostate cancer. In PDAC, the term “limited oligometastatic disease” has been used to denote patients with metastatic disease meeting the following criteria: single-organ metastasis (i.e., lung only or liver only), limited metastatic disease burden (i.e., ≤ 4 metastases), and stable disease or partial response to induction chemotherapy. PDAC patients with “limited oligometastatic disease” represent ~ 7% of all newly diagnosed PDAC, and these patients survive significantly longer than patients with other metastatic PDAC phenotypes, suggesting more favorable disease biology. In fact, local therapy for patients with oligometastatic PDAC and a good performance status is associated with long-term survival in up to 10% of patients. Given that patients with “limited oligometastatic PDAC” appear to have more indolent metastatic disease amenable to definitive local therapy, these patients might be the most ideal stage IV PDAC patients for adjuvant vaccine and ICB following cytoreductive local therapy. Post-local therapy biomarkers might identify subgroups of patients with MRD-low “limited oligometastatic PDAC” who may derive the greatest benefit from adjuvant vaccination.

Conclusions

Vaccine development for PDAC has made significant progress over the past decade with the demonstrated safety and feasibility of personalized mRNA neoantigen vaccine manufacture for resectable PDAC, as well as the combination of vaccines with other immunotherapies, such as ICB, to induce more robust and durable T cell responses. Although phase I/II studies have shown the most promise in resectable PDAC, this only represents ~ 20% of all newly diagnosed PDAC, so further work must be done to assess the feasibility and efficacy of PDAC vaccines for the majority of PDAC patients who have borderline resectable, locally advanced, or metastatic disease. Given that vaccines work best in the setting of lower tumor burden, effectively using them in combination with local therapy (i.e., surgery and/or ablative radiotherapy), cytotoxic systemic therapy, and other immunotherapies might make PDAC curable for many more patients.
