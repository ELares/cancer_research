# Research Progress of γδT Cells in Tumor Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Background: γδT cells are special innate lymphoid cells, which are not restricted by major histocompatibility complex (MHC). γδT cells mainly exist in human epidermis and mucosal epithelium. They can secrete a variety of cytokines and chemokines involved in immune regulation, and produce effective cytotoxic responses to cancer cells. Purpose:  To investigate the role of γδT cells in tumor immunotherapy, to understand its anti-tumor mechanism, and to explore the synergistic effect with other treatment modalities. This therapy is expected to become an important means of cancer treatment. Research Design: In this review presents a comprehensive analysis of the existing literature, focusing on the efficacy of γδT cells in a variety of tumor types. Results: The mechanism of γδT cells recognizing tumor antigens and killing tumor was clarified. The tumor immunotherapy based on γδT cells and its application in clinical practice were summarized. Conclusions: γδT cells have shown promising potential in tumor immunotherapy, but the therapeutic effect varies according to the type of tumor, and some patients have poor response. There are still some challenges in the treatment of this disease, such as non-standard expansion regimens and different responses of patients, indicating that the existing treatment methods are not complete. Future research should focus on perfecting γδT cell expansion protocols, gaining a deeper understanding of its anti-tumor mechanisms, and exploring synergies with other treatment modalities. This multifaceted study will promote the development of γδT cells in the field of cancer immunotherapy.

Plain language summary

γδT cells are innate lymphocytes that are not restricted by the major histocompatibility complex (MHC). This cell can secrete a number of substances, these substances can produce effective killing effect on cancer cells. γδT cells are one of the major components of human intraepithelial lymphocytes and mucosal intraepithelial lymphocytes (IEL). γδT cells, which are composed of γ and δ chains, play an important role in anti-infection. In recent years, a large number of studies have confirmed that γδT cells have shown good anti-tumor effects in tumors of the digestive system, urinary system, blood system, reproductive system, respiratory system and other systems. Therefore, γδT cell-based cellular immunotherapy is a powerful supplement to tumor immunotherapy. This review will focus on the recognition of tumor antigens by γδT cells, the mechanism of tumor killing, the tumor immunotherapy based on γδT cells and its application in clinical practice.

Introduction

The classification of human peripheral blood T cells is based on the T cell receptor, resulting in two distinct subsets: αβT cells and γδT cells. The predominant population of lymphocytes in human peripheral blood consists of αβT cells, while γδT cells make up just a small percentage, ranging from 1% to 5%. The distribution of γδT cells is mostly observed in specific mucosal and subcutaneous tissues, including the intestinal system, respiratory tract, and genitourinary tract.   In their seminal study published in 1986, Brenner et al   made the groundbreaking discovery that the γδT T cell receptor (TCR) is a heterodimeric receptor consisting of a γ chain and a δ chain. This finding was established through the utilization of antibodies generated from the peptide derived from the γ gene sequence of the T cell antigen receptor.   According to the difference of TCRδ chain, γδT cells can be divided into Vδ1-Vδ8 groups,   and the main distinction to be made with respect to the research focus is the division of the innate-like Vg9Vd2-positive gdT cells and the adaptive Vg9Vd2-negative γδT cells. Vδ1 has the ability to simultaneously express many Vγ chains, including Vγ2-Vγ10, resulting in the formation of diverse subsets of γδT cells.   Vδ2 + γδT cells comprise approximately 50% to 75% of the overall population of peripheral γδT cells. In the process of TCRγδ recombination, Vδ2 + γδT cells predominantly express Vγ9 chains, resulting in the formation of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells.   Vδ3 + γδT cells mostly reside in the hepatic and intestinal epithelium, and have been shown to induce the maturation of dendritic cells (DC) and B cells.   Based on the expression patterns of CD27 and CD45RA, γδT cells can be categorized into four distinct subsets: naive cells (CD27+CD45RA +), central memory cells (CD27+CD45RA−), effector memory cells (CD27−CD45RA−), and terminally differentiated cells (CD27−CD45RA+). Based on distinct functional characteristics, this classification encompasses subsets including γδT1, γδT17, regulatory γδT (γδTreg), follicular helper γδT (γδTfh), and memory γδT cells.  

Since the initial identification and subsequent discovery of γδT cells, these particular immune cells have been found to fulfill a multitude of physiological functions. γδT cells, in conjunction with neutrophils and macrophages, together serve as the initial line of immunological defense against exogenous infections. The secretion of diverse cytokines and facilitation of communication between innate and adaptive immunity are mechanisms by which it can enhance the activation of adaptive immune cells, including T and B cells. γδT cells are not only crucial in the defense against external infections, but they also hold significant importance in the context of tumor immunity. Previous studies have demonstrated that Vδ1T cells exhibit significant anti-tumor efficacy across various cancer types, including chronic lymphocytic leukemia, multiple myeloma, and breast cancer. Vγ9Vδ2 T lymphocytes, serving as the primary effector, exert a substantial inhibitory impact on tumor cell proliferation, facilitate tumor cell death, and significantly impede tumor initiation and progression.γδT cells possess distinct attributes in the context of tumor immunity, including the ability to recognize tumor antigens outside of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) restrictions and the capacity to recognize a diverse array of antigens present on different types of cancer cells. γδT cells possess the capacity to promptly react to specific targets and secrete effector cytokines, hence exerting their functional activities. Notably, γδT cells exhibit superior homing capabilities compared to alpha beta (αβ) T cells. It has the ability to selectively bind with other T cells and natural killer cells, resulting in a potent cytotoxic impact on tumor cells. γδT cells expressing CD137 ligand (CD137L, also known as 4-1BBL) can synergistically stimulate NK cells through CD137 (4-1BB) to enhance the direct killing and ADCC effect on tumor cells.   The distinctive benefits exhibited by γδT cells in their anti-tumor activity render tumor immunotherapy with these cells highly promising for tumor treatment.

Peptide Ligands

Self Ligands

Apart from non-peptide ligands, Vγ9Vδ2-T cells also have the ability to identify other compounds originating from cells. The binding of Vγ9Vδ2TCR to many proteins, including heat shock protein (HSP-60),   human MutS homolog 2 (hMSH2), U16 binding protein-4 (ULBP-4),   and F1-ATPase (F1-ATPase), has been confirmed. In their study, Chen et al    conducted the synthesis of CDR3δ peptide by utilizing the CDR3 region of the TCR Vδ2 chain. Subsequently, they employed an immuno-biochemical method based on CD3δ peptide to identify HSP-60 and hMSH2 as the ligands of γδT cells. ULBP is classified as a member of the ligand family of NKG2D-activated receptors found in natural killer (NK) cells. Based on the findings of Kong et al,   it has been observed that the presence of soluble ULBP-4 has the ability to stimulate the proliferation of Vδ2T cells that originate from human ovarian epithelial cancer or colon cancer when cultured in vitro. This suggests that ULBP-4 may serve as a ligand for γδTCR. Tumor cell identification is facilitated by stable cell surface components as well. A study has demonstrated the presence of surface structures associated to mitochondrial F1-ATPase on Vγ9Vδ2 susceptible cancers. These structures have the ability to bind to Vγ9Vδ2TCR and play a role in the detection of malignancies. The regulation of this mechanism is similarly governed by F1 ligands.   The expression of these proteins is increased in various types of tumor cells, leading to the facilitation of Vγ9Vδ2-T cell identification. This process is believed to be crucial in the immune monitoring of tumor progression and the elimination of viral infections.

Non-Self Ligands

Furthermore, it should be noted that Vγ9Vδ2-T cell responses can also be stimulated by non-self ligands, including tetanus toxoid, Igλ light chain, and certain viral proteins.

Similar to Vγ9Vδ2-T cells, Vδ1T cells possess the ability to detect tumor cells through their T cell receptor (TCR). However, the specific ligands that interact with the Vδ1TCR have been sparsely characterized thus far. According to reports, Vδ1T cells have the ability to detect MHC class I-related molecule A (MICA), MHC class I-related molecule B (MICB), and UL16 binding proteins, which are produced in cells undergoing stress and tumor cells.   The binding interaction between the CD1d lipid complex and the Vδ1 TCR, as shown in the crystal structure, highlights the notable involvement of the γδTCR’s δ1 chain in this process. The expression of the MHC-like CD1d lipid antigen has been observed in several types of malignancies. This finding implies that the recognition of tumors by Vδ1 T cells is facilitated through the binding interaction between Vδ1 T cells and CD1d molecules expressed on the surface of tumor cells.

Cell Receptor

γδT cells have the ability to elicit anti-tumor reactions by means of natural killer cell receptors (NKR), particularly by identifying the specific ligands of NKG2D that are present on tumor cells. The NKG2D receptor, classified as a C-type lectin receptor, exhibits a notable affinity for ligands that are prominently produced in tumor cells, whereas their expression in the majority of normal tissues remains limited. Presently, Tumor cells have been revealed to contain NKG2D ligands, namely MICA, MICB, and six ULBP binding proteins (ULBP1-6). The expression of NKG2D is observed in γδT cells both before to and subsequent to activation by PAg. The expression levels of MICA, MICB, and UL16 binding proteins on the surface of tumor cells have been found to be associated with the cytotoxicity of tumor cells mediated by the NKG2D pathway. In contrast, the function of NKG2D exhibits variability among several types of tumors.   The natural cytotoxicity receptors (NCRs) that are primarily expressed by Vδ1T cells include NKp44, NKp30, and NKp46. These NCR-positive Vδ1-positive γδT cells exhibit enhanced efficacy in recognizing and eliminating tumor cells, as well as producing interferon-gamma (IFN-γ).   Like natural killer (NK) cells, γδT cells have the ability to upregulate CD16 expression when exposed to phosphorylated antigens (such as zoledronic acid and IL-2 during in vitro activation). This CD16 can then bind to tumor cells expressing IgG, leading to the activation of γδT cells and subsequent antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity.

Mechanisms by Which γδT Cells Kill Tumors

1γδT Cells Can Recognize Malignant Cells Directly

There are typically four types of direct anti-tumor action exhibited by γδT cells: (1) Following the initiation of γδT cell activation, the release of granzyme and perforin takes place as a means to facilitate the process of apoptosis. The cytotoxicity mechanism exerted by γδT cells may potentially be associated with the cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) and natural killer (NK) cells, both of which employ the pore-forming protein perforin (PFN) to eradicate tumor cells. Cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLS) are responsible for the production of cytotoxic granules, whereas perforin (PFNS) mediates the release of a specific group of serine proteases known as granzymes. These granzymes play a crucial role in the induction of death in target cells. (2)Tumor cell apoptosis was initiated by the activation of the TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand pathway (TRAIL) and the apoptosis-inducing protein-ligand pathway (Fas-FasL). (3) γδT cells that have been stimulated can release various cytokines, including interferon-γ (IFN-γ) and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α). The cytotoxic chemicals and cytokines are responsible for inducing apoptosis in cancer cells. Additionally, they have the ability to enhance anti-tumor immunity and prevent tumor angiogenesis. (4) Additionally, γδT cells have the capacity to induce tumor cell death via antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC). Certain γδT cells were found to exhibit the expression of CD16, a cell surface receptor. CD16 has the capability to interact with the Fc region of immunoglobulin G (IgG). CD16 is known to augment antibody-dependent cytotoxicity, leading to the eradication of target cells when anti-tumor cell monoclonal antibodies are present.

γδT Cells Can Also Kill Tumor Cells Through Indirect Effects

γδT cells have the ability to engage in interactions with B cells, dendritic cells (DC), alpha-beta T cells, and natural killer (NK) cells, hence exerting an indirect anti-tumor effect. T cells expressing the T-cell receptor Vγ9 Vδ2, resembling T follicular helper cells, have been seen to secrete interleukin-4 (IL-4), interleukin-10 (IL-10), and CXCL13. These T cells have also been found to support B cells in vitro, aiding in the production of antibodies. In addition, they possess the ability to function as antigen-presenting cells (APCs) in order to stimulate the activation of αβT cells. In contrast to αβT cells, γδT cells possess the ability to function as antigen-presenting cells, hence facilitating the up-regulation of CD36 and actively engaging in the process of apoptotic absorption by immature dendritic cells (DCs) and macrophages. γδT cells have the capacity to stimulate the differentiation of dendritic cells (DCs), which subsequently leads to the activation and proliferation of gamma-delta T cells. This process also enhances the cytotoxicity and immunomodulatory capabilities of gamma-delta T cells. Gamma delta T cells have the ability to initiate natural killer (NK) cell-mediated cytotoxicity against tumors via interacting with CD137.  

γδT Cell-Based Tumor Immunotherapy

Tumor immunotherapy has the potential to reinstate the normal functioning of the immune system by either initiating or augmenting the anti-tumor immune response in individuals with malignancies, thereby exerting control over and eradicating the tumors. The population of γδT cells in the human body is typically limited, necessitating their expansion and activation in order to enhance their anti-tumor efficacy. Currently, research pertaining to the anti-tumor properties of γδT cells predominantly centers around two subsets: Vγ9Vδ2 and Vδ1T cells. It is important to note that these subsets exhibit distinct expansion and activation patterns.

The utilization of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells in the context of tumor immunotherapy primarily involves their growth and activation both in vivo and in vitro. (1) In vivo expansion of Vγ9Vδ2T cells is achieved by using small molecular phosphate drugs such as pamidoate (PAM) and zoledrate (ZOL) in combination with IL-2 to achieve the expansion and activation of Vγ9Vδ2T cells for tumor treatment.   In a phase I clinical trial examining the in vivo expansion and activation of γδT cells for the purpose of immunotherapy in patients with hormone-refractory prostate cancer, it was observed that the numbers of γδT cells grew, and the levels of serum TRAIL were sustained in individuals who received treatment with ZOL + IL-2. On the other hand, it was observed that the majority of patients who received treatment with ZOL alone exhibited an inability to sustain adequate levels of γδT cells or TRAIL serum levels, which subsequently led to a gradual decline in their clinical condition,   by the activation of the TNF-related apoptosis-inducing ligand pathway (TRAIL) and the apoptosis-inducing protein-ligand pathway (Fas-FasL). Several previous studies have demonstrated that the administration of N-BPs in conjunction with IL-2 can augment the population of γδT cells in the peripheral blood of individuals with tumors. Additionally, this treatment regimen has been shown to enhance the susceptibility of γδT cells to the cytotoxic effects exerted by tumor cells. The proliferation and stimulation of Vδ1T cells is not contingent upon the presence of diverse bisphosphonates. While the quantity of Vδ1T cells in the peripheral circulation is lower compared to Vγ9Vδ2-T cells, Vδ1T cells exhibit the ability to circulate in vivo and persist for extended periods. Additionally, they possess a strong affinity for tumor tissue, suggesting their potential for sustaining long-term therapeutic efficacy.   In vitro expansion of Vδ1T cells yields a substantial increase in cell counts, while the activation of T cell receptor (TCR) agonists, interleukin-4 (IL-4), and interleukin-5 (IL-5) stimulation leads to the generation of Vδ1T cells exhibiting heightened cytotoxicity. During the initial stage of expansion, the cells were subjected to stimulation with IL-4 and TCR agonists for a duration of two weeks. This was subsequently followed by the differentiation phase. The γδT cells were subsequently exposed to IL-5 for a duration of one week, resulting in their differentiation into Vδ1 cells, which accounted for over 60% of the total population. These Vδ1 cells exhibited a high level of cytotoxicity and demonstrated their ability to suppress tumor growth.  

Clinical Application of γδT Cell Anti-Tumor Classical Immunotherapy

Numerous clinical investigations have been conducted to explore the potential of γδT cells for tumor therapy, owing to their distinctive advantages and the practicality of achieving quantitative multiplication. Multiple clinical investigations have substantiated the safety and effectiveness of γδT cells in the field of tumor immunotherapy. The classical approach to γδT cell-based immunotherapy for treating tumors involves two main strategies. The first strategy is autologous γδT cell anti-tumor immunotherapy, which entails using the patient’s own γδT cells. These cells can be expanded in vivo or through in vitro methods, and then reintroduced into the patient’s body to achieve the desired therapeutic outcome. The second strategy is allogeneic γδT cell anti-tumor immunotherapy, which involves expanding allogeneic γδT cells obtained from healthy individuals through specific ex vivo techniques. These expanded cells are then transferred to patients for the purpose of anti-tumor therapy (as shown in Figure 1). Currently, several clinical studies have demonstrated varying levels of effectiveness in the treatment of tumors in the digestive system, reproductive system, urinary system, blood system, respiratory system, and other tumors using classical anti-tumor immunotherapy that relies on γδT cells (refer to Table 1).

Digestive System

The resistance of malignant ascites, which is produced by the peritoneal spread of stomach cancer, to chemotherapy is well-documented in academic literature. Moreover, the prognosis for patients with this condition is generally considered to be unfavorable. Wada   expanded Vδ2γδT cells in vitro and administered intraperitoneal injections four times a week in seven patients. The results showed that the number of tumor cells present in ascites significantly decreased after cell injection in 2 patients, and no significant treatment-related adverse reactions occurred. Furthermore, the introduction of Vγ9Vδ2T cells that were precisely cultured in vitro into individuals with colon cancer led to the development of stable disease, with no occurrence of significant adverse effects.   In a study conducted by Alnagger et al,    Vγ9Vδ2T cells were grown and expanded in vitro from healthy volunteers. Subsequently, these cells were transfused into a patient diagnosed with stage IV cholangiocarcinoma on eight occasions. The results of this intervention demonstrated a significant reduction in both tumor volume and lymph node size. In addition, the implementation of γδT cell treatment has the potential to significantly enhance immune responses through the modulation of αβT cells and NK cells. Following the administration of medication, there is an observed augmentation in the ratio of functional CD4+ and CD8 + T cells, accompanied by a reduction in the proportion of exhausted old CD4+ and CD8 + T cells. Professor Zhi-nan Yin’s research team in China has conducted clinical trials on the use of allogeneic γδT cell infusion for tumor cell treatment, capitalizing on the MHC-independent antigen recognition property of γδT cells. Eight patients with liver cancer got ≥5 infusions of Vγ9Vδ2T cells. The results of regular follow-up revealed that a total of seven patients shown a survival duration of at least 10 months, while three patients exhibited a survival duration ranging between 30 and 35 months. Respiratory System

Clinical investigations utilizing γδT cell immunotherapy for respiratory tumors predominantly concentrate on lung cancer. Kakimi et al conducted a two-phase clinical trial involving non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) patients. In the initial phase, 12 patients underwent biweekly administrations of in vitro expanded γδT cells. Following the completion of six injections, six patients exhibited stable disease. In the subsequent phase II study, 25 patients treated with γδT cell injections displayed a median progression-free survival (PFS) of 95 days, featuring one partial response, 16 instances of stability, and eight cases of progression. Despite the well-tolerated nature of the autologous γδT cell therapy mentioned above, the anticipated 4-month PFS was not realized.

Conversely, Xu et al   undertook a clinical trial involving allogeneic γδT cell infusion for tumor cell therapy. In this trial, ten lung cancer patients received ≥5 infusions of Vγ9Vδ2T cells and underwent regular follow-ups. The survival period of eight patients extended beyond ≥10 months, while two lung cancer patients demonstrated a survival time between 30 and 35 months. These results underscore the therapy’s commendable safety profile and efficacy.

Reproductive System

Meraviglia et al   executed a phase I clinical trial investigating in vivo activation amplification therapy in ten individuals afflicted with advanced metastatic breast cancer. The administered treatment demonstrated favorable tolerability, with discernible reductions in CA15-3 levels observed in three patients. Notably, one participant exhibited a partial response, while two others displayed sustained stability of their disease. Additionally, a separate clinical trial focusing on γδT cells revealed that expanded γδT cells exhibited the potential to augment the effectiveness of the targeted drug trastuzumab in patients with HER-2-positive breast cancer. This augmentation was further substantiated by a significant reduction in tumor volume.  

The occurrence and development of tumors are often caused by cancer cells or dysfunctional cells escaping immune surveillance due to the long-term suppression of the body’s immune system. The above-mentioned clinical studies have shown that the anti-tumor therapy with autologous γδT cells has a certain effect. However, due to the systemic dysfunction of immune cells in cancer patients, the efficacy of classical immunotherapy based on autologous γδT cells in cancer treatment is relatively limited compared with γδT cells from healthy people, that is, allogeneic γδT cells therapy. Since the recognition of tumor antigens by γδT cells is not restricted by MHC, its application in tumor immunotherapy may be able to avoid immune escape and immune rejection caused by the down-regulation of MHC class I molecules. This advantage of γδT cells is especially suitable for the adoptive transfer strategy of allogeneic.

Optimization of Anti-Tumor Immunotherapy Based on γδT Cells

CAR-γδT Cell

In recent years, CAR-T cell therapy has been extensively studied in preclinical and clinical settings, with a primary focus on conventional αβT cells. Despite achieving positive therapeutic effects in hematological tumors, CAR-T therapy still faces significant challenges including serious toxicities such as off-target effects, cytokine storm, and neurotoxicity. Additionally, it shows limited efficacy against solid tumors and high rates of recurrence and metastasis.

The distinctive capability of γδT cells to recognize tumors, coupled with the use of allogeneic γδT cells sourced from healthy donors, presents several advantages. These cells exhibit robust in vivo and in vitro expansion without concurrent expansion of Treg, lack cytotoxic effects on normal cells within the human body, and boast high lethality without necessitating genetic modification. Through the synergistic integration of γδT cells with CAR-T therapy, a simultaneous generation of a substantial quantity of γδT cells is achievable. This approach not only minimizes by-products during amplification but also reduces the overall treatment costs, such as cytokine release syndrome (CRS), immune effector cell-associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS).   Consequently, there is a growing consideration regarding the potential of CAR-γδT cells as an effective candidate for adoptive cellular immunotherapy in the realm of tumors. This exploration aims to enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells while mitigating their associated side effects.   In the pioneering work of Rischer and colleagues,   CAR-γδT cells were initially characterized. These cells were isolated with the Vγ9Vδ2T cell phenotype following expansion and subjected to genetic modification to create GD2-targeting CAR-γδT cells for tumor treatment. GD2, a surface marker prevalent on neuroblastoma and various other tumor cells, exhibits low expression on normal cell surfaces. To minimize off-target effects and associated adverse reactions, Liu and Zhang also devised CAR-γδT cells specifically targeting GD2.   Notably, in contrast to conventional CD19 CAR-T cells, CD19-targeting CAR-γδT cells demonstrated reactivity and augmentation against both CD19-positive and CD19-negative tumor cells in vitro and in vivo, particularly with the addition of ZOL.   This observation suggests that the engineered cells retain the capacity to target leukemia cells even in scenarios of antigen loss. Furthermore, maintain pAg specificity through its TCR.

In addition to exhibiting significant therapeutic potential in hematological tumors, Makkouk et al   have recently undertaken the expansion and genetic engineering of allogeneic Vδ1T cells. These cells were modified to secrete interleukin-15 (IL-15) and express CAR-Vδ1T, targeting glypican-3 (GPC3) in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). In a HepG2 mouse model, GPC3-CAR-Vδ1T cells demonstrated accumulation and proliferation within the tumor, and a singular administration of these cells effectively controlled tumor growth without inducing graft-versus-host disease (GvHD). The CAR-Vδ1T cells exhibited an augmented anti-tumor activity, presenting an appealing and secure approach for treating solid tumors. Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) represents a subtype characterized by the most unfavorable prognosis due to the absence of specific targets. Ye and collaborators   addressed this challenge by developing 7 × 19 CAR-γδT cells expressing a chimeric antigen receptor specific to folate receptor alpha (FRa CAR), along with the secretion of interleukin-7 (IL-7) and chemokine C-C motif ligand 19 (CCL19). Their anti-tumor activity was meticulously assessed in both in vitro and in vivo settings. The findings underscored the substantial anti-TNBC tumor efficacy of 7 × 19 CAR-γδT cells. These cells not only exhibited the ability to lyse target tumor cells but also demonstrated the capability to penetrate the tumor microenvironment (TME) and lyse non-target tumor cells by stimulating the body’s immune response. Despite advancements in androgen receptor signaling inhibitors and targeted/chemotherapeutic interventions, the management of advanced prostate cancer remains suboptimal, particularly with bone metastases observed in 90% of patients succumbing to the disease. In a preclinical investigation focused on bone-metastatic prostate cancer, researchers from the Moffitt Cancer Center engineered CAR-γδT cells targeting prostate stem cell antigen (PSCA). Treatment with zoledronate resulted in substantial tumor regression, prolonged survival, and diminished cancer-associated bone disease.   This study highlights the potential of CAR-γδT cell immunotherapy as an effective strategy for refractory tumors.

Despite the proven efficacy of γδT cell immunotherapy, CAR-γδT cell immunotherapy is currently underrepresented in clinical trials compared to its classical counterpart.

Antibody-Based Strategy

An alternative strategy for harnessing γδT cells in potent anti-tumor therapy involves an antibody-based approach. While the T-cell receptor (TCR) of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells is inherently capable of recognizing phosphoantigens (PAgs), the activation of Vγ9Vδ2 T cells to induce tumor cell death generally necessitates cell-cell contact. This implies the existence of “PAg presenting molecules: in target cells.   Butyrophilin-3A (BTN3A) has emerged as a pivotal mediator of PAg signaling, crucial for activating Vγ9Vδ2T cells. PAg-mediated signaling is believed to involve binding to the B30.2 domain of BTN3A1, leading to a conformational change in the transmembrane protein. This change enables interaction with BTN2A1, which, in turn, directly binds to the T-cell receptor (TCR) Vγ9 chain on Vγ9Vδ2T cells, thereby activating them. In pursuit of this mechanism, Imcheck developed ICT01, a humanized, FC-free monoclonal antibody against BTN3A. Designed to lack antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) or complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) activity, ICT01 selectively binds to all three isoforms of BTN3A (BTN3A1, BTN3A2, BTN3A3) in a B30.2 domain-independent manner. This unique binding property serves to activate Vγ9Vδ2T cells, mitigating the PAg-mediated restriction associated with BTN3A1-only dependence for Vγ9Vδ2T cell activation.   This reduces the PAg-mediated restriction of BTN3A1-only dependence to activate Vγ9Vδ2T cells. The company conducted experiments utilizing human tumor cell xenografts and NGS mice subjected to adoptive infusion of human Vγ9Vδ2T cells to assess the in vivo induced anti-tumor activity of ICT01 in both blood and solid tumors. The outcomes revealed that mice treated with ICT01 exhibited prolonged median survival (42 days vs 29 days) and hindered the growth of the AML cell line MOLM14 compared to mice receiving Vγ9Vδ2T cell infusion alone. Similar promising results were observed in a solid tumor model, where the growth of SKOV-3 cells was delayed, leading to enhanced mouse survival.

Currently, the first-in-human phase I/IIa clinical trial of ICT01 is underway (NCT04243499), focusing on patients with advanced solid or hematologic malignancies. This trial aims to assess the safety and tolerability of ICT01 both as a monotherapy and in combination with a checkpoint inhibitor (pembrolizumab) for patients with advanced solid or hematologic malignancies. The study also explores the impact of sex and activity on treatment outcomes.   In addition to antibodies targeting BTN, LAVA Therapeutics has devised a bispecific γδT cell marker platform. This platform is engineered to target and activate Vγ9Vδ2T cells, inducing direct killing of tumor cells.   Notably, this approach enhances specific recognition of tumor antigens while preserving the capacity to recognize stress signals.

Combination of γδT Cells and Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors

Since the inception of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) in clinical interventions, they have emerged as pioneering agents in tumor immunotherapy, particularly anti-CTLA-4 and anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies. These inhibitors have demonstrated promising clinical efficacy across a spectrum of tumors. Investigations reveal that γδT cells infiltrating tumor tissues often exist in a depleted or anergic state, accompanied by the up-regulation of numerous immune checkpoints (ICs) on their surface. This observation suggests that γδT cells exhibit responses to ICIs similar to traditional effector T cells. Elevated expression of immune checkpoint ligands (ICLs) on tumor cells can instigate aberrant signaling pathways, swiftly leading to the inefficacy or exhaustion of γδT cells. ICs such as PD-1, CTLA-4, BTLA, and TIM-3 exert inhibitory effects by blocking the T-cell receptor (TCR) signal, disrupting costimulatory signals, and ultimately impeding γδT cell activation, resulting in γδT cell dysfunction.  

The proliferation and activation of γδT cells can be restored through ICI application. Notably, Vγ9Vδ2T cell proliferation, STAT phosphorylation levels, and IL-21 induction significantly increased following TIM-3 blockade.   Targeting B7-H3 using ICIs improved the proliferation and activation of dysregulated γδT cells. In colorectal cancer, blocking or knocking down B7-H3 inhibited Vδ2T cell apoptosis, promoted their proliferation, and induced the expression of activation markers.   ICIs enhance the cytotoxicity of compromised γδT cells, elevating the secretion of anti-tumor cytokines, perforin, and granzyme, thereby restoring their anti-tumor activity. Studies indicate that blocking PD-1 and TIM-3 can restore the anti-tumor activity of Vγ9Vδ2T cells. PD-1 blockade also promotes antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) mediated by γδT cells in follicular lymphoma,   and enhances the cytotoxicity of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) tumor cell lines. Targeting ICs holds the potential to reverse γδT cell dysfunction in the tumor microenvironment (TME), playing an anti-tumor role by improving γδT cell proliferation and activation, and augmenting cytotoxicity. The combined adoptive infusion of γδT cells with checkpoint inhibitors represents a novel strategy for γδT cell tumor immunotherapy. In a study of phase 1 b (NCT02298959), 10 cases with advanced pd - 1 resistant melanoma patients accepted once every 2 weeks of ziv-aflibercept (2 ∼ 4 mg/kg) + palmer bead single isle (2 mg/kg) combination therapy. Two patients (20%) achieved partial response and 2 patients (20%) achieved stable disease (SD). Researchers to explore the plasma analytes (e.g., platelet-derived growth factor and PD - L1), study shows that potential changes have taken place in myeloid cell function, no response in patients with impaired γδT cells.   In addition, Wang et al constructed novel armored γδT cells capable of secreting humanized anti-PD-1 antibodies, termed Lv-PD1-γδT cells. Lv-PD1-γδ T cells show stronger proliferation ability and killing effect on tumor cells in tumor-bearing mice, thereby enhancing the therapeutic effect and survival benefit. Moreover, Lv-PD1-γδ T cells showed good tolerance and safety in humanized NOD/SCID/γ deletion mice.   A study showed that tumor-reactive γδ T cells responded to PD-1 blockade and that patients had complete responses to therapy in patients with Meckel cell carcinoma (MCC). The Vγ2Vδ1 clonotype recognizes Merkel cancer cells in a TCR-dependent manner, and clonotypic γδ T cells are present in the blood and tumors after treatment with pembrolizumab. The results suggest that native-like T cells also contribute to the antitumor response after PD-1 blockade.  

Conclusions

In summary, owing to their distinctive properties and inherent advantages, γδT cells have emerged as a prominent force in cancer immune cell therapy. Extensive preclinical and clinical investigations have consistently underscored the efficacy and safety of γδT cells in the realm of tumor immunotherapy, thereby establishing γδT cell-based immunotherapy as a transformative frontier in cancer treatment. Within this domain, particular emphasis has been placed on two pivotal subsets of γδT cells—Vγ9Vδ2T cells and Vδ1T cells. These subsets have assumed a central role in the anti-tumor efficacy of γδT cells, marking a significant stride forward in the pursuit of effective anti-cancer immunotherapies.

However, notwithstanding the potent anti-tumor capabilities of γδT cells, research findings have highlighted a paradoxical aspect. Tumor-infiltrating γδT cells, through the secretion of interleukin-17 (IL-17), can induce angiogenesis and suppress the anti-tumor functions of other immune cells, thereby fostering tumor initiation and progression. IL-17, in turn, stimulates tumor cell proliferation by activating the IL-6/signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) and eukaryotic cell nuclear transcription factor B (NF-κB) pathways. Furthermore, it facilitates tumor metastasis by promoting the secretion of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and matrix metalloproteinase (MMP). Elevated levels of IL-17 are frequently observed in patients with advanced or metastatic tumors. Additionally, tumor-infiltrating γδT cells exhibit the capacity to impede the maturation of CD4+, CD8+, and dendritic cells (DCs), concurrently up-regulating the expression of programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), thereby hindering their anti-tumor effects.

Hence, γδT cells exhibit a dual role in cancer immunotherapy, demonstrating both anti-tumor and pro-tumor effects. Consequently, the challenge lies in ensuring that the application of γδT cells in tumor immunotherapy predominantly exerts an anti-tumor influence rather than a pro-tumor one. Notably, tumor-infiltrating Vδ1T cells are identified as the primary subset associated with a pro-tumor role.   Therefore, directing focus toward Vγ9Vδ2T cells in immunotherapy research could potentially address this concern. However, it is essential to acknowledge that some scholars contend that even Vγ9Vδ2T cells can be induced into different functional subsets, displaying tumor-promoting activity contingent on the cytokine milieu within the tumor microenvironment.   Consequently, the tumor microenvironment emerges as a pivotal factor influencing γδT cell function. Addressing how to modulate the tumor microenvironment, mitigating or even reversing the tumor-promoting effects of γδT cells, and optimizing their anti-tumor activity represent crucial considerations for future research.

Moreover, the lack of standardized and consistently effective expansion regimens for γδT cells in treatment poses a challenge. The therapeutic efficacy varies across different tumor types, with some patients exhibiting minimal or no response. Future research efforts should focus on refining the expansion protocols for γδT cells, elucidating the unique mechanisms of γδT cells in diverse tumors, and exploring the synergistic potential of adoptive γδT cell infusion with other treatment modalities. This multifaceted approach will advance the field of tumor immunotherapy with γδT cells. Looking ahead, γδT cell-based immunotherapy holds the promise of becoming a valuable tool in cancer treatment.
