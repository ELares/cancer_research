# Targeting acute myeloid leukemia through antibody engineering: innovations in immunotherapy and combination regimens.

## Abstract

Acute myeloid leukemia (AML), a heterogeneous and aggressive hematologic malignancy, remains challenging to treat due to high relapse rates, chemotherapy resistance, and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). While traditional therapies like chemotherapy and hematopoietic stem cell transplantation have improved outcomes, their efficacy is often limited by toxicity and disease recurrence. Recent advancements in antibody engineering have revolutionized AML immunotherapy, offering precision-targeted strategies to overcome these barriers. This narrative review explores the transformative role of monoclonal antibodies (mAbs), antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs), and bispecific antibodies (bsAbs) in redirecting immune effector cells, blocking immune checkpoints, and eradicating leukemic stem cells (LSCs). Key innovations include CD33-targeted gemtuzumab ozogamicin, CD123-directed bispecific engagers, and anti-CD47 agents that disrupt "don't eat me" signals. We highlight breakthroughs in antibody design-such as Fc optimization, trispecific constructs, and conditionally active biologics-that enhance specificity while minimizing on-target off-tumor toxicity. Clinical trials demonstrate promising results, including improved remission rates and survival in refractory/relapsed AML when combining antibodies with hypomethylating agents, venetoclax, or checkpoint inhibitors. However, challenges persist, including AML's genetic heterogeneity, adaptive immune evasion, and cytokine release syndrome (CRS) risks. Emerging strategies such as biomarker-driven personalization, TME modulation, and engineered NK-cell engagers are poised to address these limitations. By integrating preclinical insights with clinical data, this review underscores the potential of antibody-based combinatorial regimens to redefine AML therapy, offering durable responses and bridging the gap to curative approaches.

Introduction

Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) represents a heterogeneous form of hematologic malignancy distinguished by the aggressive clonal expansion of myeloid blasts within the bone marrow and peripheral blood. The etiology of the majority of AML cases may be attributed to a complex interplay of genetic, epigenetic, and environmental factors. Factors such as older age at onset, obesity, prior autoimmune diseases, and antecedent MDS or myeloproliferative neoplasms (MPNs) are risk factors for the development of AML. Progress in the genomic and epigenomic analysis of AML has facilitated a deeper comprehension of leukemogenesis, thereby enabling the formulation and subsequent approval of various innovative targeted therapies. Nevertheless, chemotherapy remains the cornerstone of treatment and has undergone minimal modifications over the previous four decades. Despite rigorous therapeutic interventions, a significant proportion of patients experience relapse, and the median overall survival rate for individuals diagnosed with AML remains suboptimal. Recent updates to the European LeukemiaNet (ELN) 2017, 2022, and 2024 guidelines emphasize molecular profiling for risk stratification, integrating mutations in genes such as FLT3, NPM1, and TP53 to refine prognostic categories, and incorporating the novel molecular Prognostic Risk Score (mPRS) for more precise outcome prediction. Despite these refinements, fewer than one-third of adult patients with AML achieve sustained remission, underscoring the ongoing need for alternative therapeutic strategies. Furthermore, recent epidemiological data indicate that the global incidence of AML has risen to approximately 5.2 cases per 100,000 persons per year, with a median age at diagnosis of 68 years, reflecting both demographic shifts and improved disease recognition.

AML relapse is still possible even though HSCT is still the most effective treatment option. Additionally, most AML patients are elderly, making them unsuitable candidates for this treatment strategy. The monitoring of leukemic stem cells (LSCs), which are resistant to both chemotherapy and radiation, is currently insufficient. It is thought that LSCs contribute to the presence of minimal residual disease (MRD), which is predictive of relapse, even though they may not preclude HSCT. Alternative approaches that produce long-term remission with less toxicity are therefore desperately needed, especially for patients who cannot receive current treatment modalities. Immunotherapy presents a promising avenue for the eradication of chemotherapy-resistant clones and the provision of prolonged disease control. The effectiveness of immunotherapies in the treatment of cancer has increasingly become evident. It is necessary to incorporate a broad range of immunotherapeutic interventions—including cytokines, monoclonal antibodies, immunomodulatory agents, and bispecific antibodies—into standard clinical practice. In recent years, several treatment regimens based on immune-mediated therapeutic mechanisms have been developed to target AML. The most significant antibody-based immunotherapeutic treatments for AML are reviewed in this review.

Mechanisms of immune evasion in AML

Tumor cells do not exist in isolation but within a complex TME composed of stromal, endothelial, immune, and soluble factors. This milieu undergoes extensive remodeling by LSCs, which sculpt an immunosuppressive niche favoring disease persistence and relapse. Recent single-cell multiomics at ASH 2024 revealed that TP53-mutant AML clones acquire monocytic features and upregulate immune-checkpoint genes, further blunting T cell responses and driving resistance to venetoclax therapies. During malignant transformation, LSCs employ immunoediting to evade elimination: they downregulate antigen‐presenting machinery (HLA loss or mutation), secrete immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10), and upregulate inhibitory ligands such as PD-L1, TIM-3, VISTA, and Gal-9, leading to T cell exhaustion and impaired cytotoxicity. An ASCO 2023 abstract demonstrated that blockade of VISTA on AML blasts restored CD8⁺ T cell proliferation in vitro, although in vivo data remain mixed. Moreover, extracellular vesicles released by AML cells carry TGF-β and miR-155, reprogramming bone marrow stromal cells toward an immunosuppressive phenotype and further inhibiting T cell activation. AML blasts also disrupt immune synapse formation via altered lipid metabolism and by shedding NKG2D ligands, preventing NK-cell recognition and cytotoxicity. Natural killer (NK) cells are key first responders in AML surveillance, yet multiple post-2020 studies have elucidated a two-layered NK-evasion mechanism. AML-derived prostaglandin E₂ (PGE₂) and soluble TGF-β act on NK cells to suppress NKG2D and NKp30 activation receptors, while simultaneously, myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) infiltrate the marrow and express IDO1, depleting tryptophan, while LSC-derived microvesicles further dampen NK-cell function via TGF-β cargo. An ASH 2023 abstract reported that simultaneous FcγR engagement and PGE₂ inhibition reactivates NK killing of AML in xenografts, highlighting a promising combinatorial approach. Regulatory T cells (Tregs) also accumulate within the AML TME. A 2022 Clin Cancer Res study showed that AML blasts secrete IFN-γ, driving expansion of CD4⁺CD25⁺FoxP3⁺ Tregs via upregulation of CCL17 and CCL22 chemokines; blocking these axes in mouse models delayed disease progression. Moreover, single-cell profiling presented at EHA 2024 found that high proportions of PD-1⁺TIM-3⁺ exhausted T cells correlate with inferior responses to induction therapy, suggesting immune-checkpoint blockade may need pairing with microenvironmental modulators.

Altogether, AML blasts co-opt multiple immunosuppressive networks—antigen loss, inhibitory ligand overexpression, soluble mediator secretion, extracellular vesicles, and stromal reprogramming—to protect LSCs and facilitate minimal residual disease, underscoring the imperative for therapies that not only target leukemic cells but also rewire the TME (Fig. 1).

Current antibody-based immunotherapy techniques for AML

Antibody-based therapies

At the beginning of the twentieth century, a German physician named “Paul Ehrlich” proposed the concept of antibodies as a "magic bullet" for the selective targeting of malignant cells, and it took years to realize the full potential of antibody-based therapies. The antibody facilitates cancer cell elimination by inhibiting proliferation signaling, attracting immune cells, or transporting attached therapeutic agents to cancer cells. Lineage-specific antigens (LSAs) refer to antigens that are expressed at different stages of hematopoietic differentiation. These LSAs offer a wide range of potential targets for therapeutic antibodies in blood cancers, particularly in the treatment of T cell, B cell, and myeloid lineage-related malignancies. However, both AML cells and healthy myeloid precursors may harbor the majority of potential antigens. This can easily result in an off-tumor target effect in the treatment of AML, which can lead to prolonged thrombocytopenia. Therefore, identifying appropriate antigens for targeted AML immunotherapy is crucial. Recently, antibody-based therapies for AML have focused on identifying and exploring new antigen targets as well as developing novel formats of unconjugated or conjugated monoclonal antibodies.

Monoclonal antibodies

In the field of AML therapeutics, monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) specifically target surface antigens (CD33, CD123, CD25, CD44, CD47, and CLL-1) that are expressed on leukemic blasts, allowing access to malignant cells in the blood and bone marrow while sparing normal hematopoietic stem cells. mAbs suppress cancerous cells by specifically targeting distinct or overexpressed antigens through various mechanisms. In fact, mAbs inhibit growth signaling and interfere with cancer cell proliferation and survival signals by binding to specific growth factor receptors, altering their activity, or blocking ligand binding via direct-action mechanisms. Indirect mechanisms of action of mAbs involve components of the host’s innate and acquired immune systems. These mechanisms include antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP), antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (CDC), and antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC). The variable fragment (Fv) and constant fragment (Fc) contribute to the biological characterization of IgG antibodies, which are the most commonly used compounds in cancer immunotherapy. Fv interacts with Ag, but Fc has a broader range of responsibilities spanning from the antibody half-life to the CDC, ADCP, or ADCC. Fc binds to immune cells or molecules, facilitating the coordination of cells carrying the antigen (or the antigen itself) with components of innate or acquired immunity. Lintuzumab, an unconjugated mAb developed by combining a humanized IgG1 with a murine CDR, has shown limited activity in clinical trials, resulting in unsatisfactory results with no significant improvement in the response rate or survival in AML patients. Hence, it is necessary to modify these antibodies to carry and deliver antitumor agents to AML cells with a comparatively low level of toxicity. Anti-CD33 antibodies

Research has indicated that the expression of CD33 is considerably lower in normal hematopoietic stem cells and mature granulocytes than in AML malignant cells. As a result, CD33 has emerged as the optimal target for AML therapy. As previously mentioned, GO, also referred to as Mylotarg®, was the first humanized anti-CD33 agent to be granted FDA approval. Although initially employed as an unconjugated form with only 15% remission, the conjugated form with calicheamycin showed highly encouraging results. GO withdrew from the market during a phase III study because of the inability to enhance clinical outcomes and the reported rise in hematologic and hepatic toxicity. A further investigation using conventional induction chemotherapy, with or without a reduced dosage of GO, revealed particularly advantageous outcomes in favorable-risk AML patients, with significant improvements in relapse-free survival, 2-year event-free survival, and overall survival. Ultimately, the US FDA approved GO on the basis of favorable outcomes from European trials. This approach was approved for the use of GO combined with daunorubicin and cytarabine in patients with de novo CD33 + AML. Clinical trials are currently underway in various settings, exploring the use of GO in combination with cytarabine, decitabine, azacitidine, and various induction regimens. In the ALFA-0701 phase III trial, GO combined with standard chemotherapy achieved a 3-year event-free survival (EFS) of 40.8% vs. 17.1% with chemotherapy alone (HR 0.58; p = 0.0003) in de novo AML patients, with a CR rate of 81% vs. 75%. However, GO monotherapy in relapsed/refractory (R/R) AML showed only a 26% CR rate in the MyloFrance-1 trial, compared to 10–15% CR rates with salvage chemotherapy.

Anti-CD45 antibodies

A type-I transmembrane protein, CD45, is present with multiple isoforms on nearly all hematopoietic cells, excluding erythrocytes and platelets. An estimated 85–90% of ALL cells and the majority of AML cells express CD45, although it is not present in nonhematopoietic tissues. To date, the predominant approach to therapeutically target CD45 in AML has been the use of radiolabeled monoclonal antibodies. The findings from a phase I trial assessing the use of a yttrium-90-labeled anti-CD45 antibody (90Y-DOTA-BC8) in high-risk AML, ALL, or MDS patients revealed that combining 90Y-DOTA-BC8 with an allogeneic HCT regimen was both effective and well tolerated and enhanced outcomes following allogeneic HCT. The promising findings of this study supported further clinical trials incorporating radioimmunotherapy into the conditioning regimen for allo-HCT. The initial randomized phase III trial is currently investigating the role of a 131I-labeled CD45 antibody (omab-B, apamistamab) in conditioning prior to allogeneic HCT in adults over 55 years of age with R/R AML. This trial followed a phase 1 trial that was conducted more than 20 years ago.

Anti-CD47 antibodies

CD47, a “don’t eat me” signal producer, interacts with signal-regulating protein α (SIRPα) for phagocytosis and inhibits the phagocytosis of tumor cells that are overexpressed in different malignancies. In AML, CD47 is associated with poor overall survival and has increased expression on LSCs to prevent phagocytosis. In preclinical models, monoclonal antibodies targeting CD47 effectively induce phagocytosis of LSCs, preventing AML engraftment. The anti-CD47 CC-90002 antibody hinders the interaction between CD47 and SIRPα, facilitating the destruction of tumor cells in hematological cancer cell lines via a macrophage mechanism. In AML xenograft models, CC-90002 was highly effective in rapidly and significantly reducing the tumor burden. Unfortunately, the phase 1 clinical trial of CC-90002 monotherapy did not provide enough evidence of its clinical effectiveness in patients with R/R AML or high-risk MDS to support further clinical development of CC-90002 monotherapy. In a phase Ib trial, magrolimab + azacitidine achieved a CR/CRi rate of 64% in untreated AML patients, with a median OS of 18.9 months vs. 12.8 months for azacitidine alone in historical controls. However, the phase III ENHANCE-2 trial (NCT04778397) was terminated due to futility, showing no OS benefit (median OS 8.2 vs. 8.4 months) compared to venetoclax + azacitidine in TP53-mutant AML. Additionally, several clinical trials are evaluating the efficacy of another anti-CD47 antibody, magrolimab (Hu5F9-G4), alone or in combination with other antileukemic agents in AML, but the investigation has been stopped for unknown reasons. Ligufalimab (AK117) is a newly developed humanized anti-CD47 IgG4 that has no hemagglutination effect and exhibits distinctive safety characteristics. Blocking CD47 effectively causes macrophages to eat AML cells, and when combined with azacitidine, it makes the “eat me” signals stronger. Recently, two phase 1/2 studies have evaluated the safety and efficacy of AK117, the only promising anti-CD47 monoclonal antibody in the AML therapeutic field, in combination with azacitidine in adult AML patients.

Anti-CD70 antibodies

CD70, a member of the TNF family, has been recognized as a promising target for AML treatment because of its increased expression in leukemic blasts but absence in normal hematopoietic stem cells. CD70 is essentially a lymphoid lineage marker. However, it is also expressed on AML blasts and interacts with its ligand CD27 to stimulate cell division and trigger the activation of multiple signaling pathways. Cusatuzumab, also known as ARGX-110, is a monoclonal antibody with a modified Fc region that specifically targets CD70 and exerts its antitumor potency by inducing ADCC. Azacitidine stimulates CD70 expression on LSCs, hence promoting in vitro cytotoxicity when used in combination with cusatuzumab. In older patients with untreated AML, a phase I/II trial evaluated the efficacy of a single dose of cusatuzumab followed by azacitidine therapy. The analysis revealed that four subjects obtained negative MRD results by flow cytometry, and ten patients (83%) achieved CR/Cri. Additionally, previous preclinical investigations have shown that cusatuzumab has cytotoxic effects on LSCs and that its antileukemic properties increase with venetoclax coadministration. In fact, venetoclax increases CD70 levels on LSCs, which shows that cusatuzumab might be able to target CD70-positive cells. Clinical trials assessing the effectiveness of cusatuzumab therapy in combination with azacitidine and venetoclax, a common treatment for newly diagnosed AML and previously untreated de novo or secondary AML, are currently in progress.

Anti-CD123 antibodies

CD123-directed targeted therapies have the capacity to eradicate the earliest stages of malignancies, which results in longer-lasting therapeutic effects. Compared with healthy hematopoietic stem cells and progenitor cells, AML blasts have higher levels of CD123, which is the α-subunit of the IL-3Rα. Although the two phase I studies of the unconjugated anti-CD123 CSL360 did not demonstrate a substantial advantage in AML patients, ongoing attempts to investigate SGN-CD123A and IMG632 as antibody‒drug conjugates have shown promising effectiveness in clinical trials. The IgG1 monoclonal antibody talacotuzumab (JNJ-56022473) specifically targets CD123-expressing cells and leads to NK-mediated ADCC. However, owing to the lack of efficacy and high toxicity rates reported in a pivotal study involving talacotuzumab in combination with decitabine as first-line therapy in AML, the manufacturer decided to prematurely terminate phase 2 of the clinical trial.

Bispecific antibodies

Bispecific antibodies (bsAbs) represent a novel type of therapeutic antibody designed to target specific antigens unattainable with conventional IgG-based monoclonal antibodies. This antibody-based strategy has attracted significant attention in the last ten years, and 11 bsAbs have received approval for cancer therapy by the end of 2023. Functionally, bsAbs with dual specificity mediate the engagement of antigens, including one for tumor-associated antigens and another for a surface antigen on effector cells (T cells or NK cells). This interaction brings tumor cells into close contact with the effectors, and the cytotoxic effects of the effectors destroy the malignant cells if engagement with the second antigen is agonistic. As a major obstacle in harnessing the host's adaptive immunity is the suppression of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) and costimulatory molecules on tumor cells, bispecific antibodies provide an intense effector response against cancer cells without relying on MHC-1 or costimulatory signals. Currently, bsAbs are specifically classified into two categories on the basis of their structure: immunoglobulin G (IgG)-based antibodies and variable fragment (Fv)-based antibodies. In terms of IgG subclasses, IgG2 and IgG4 are appropriate options because IgG1-based antibodies can suppress the activity of activated T cells. The Fc domains of these IgG-based bsAbs can induce ADCC, ADCP, and CDC by recruiting NK cells and macrophages. Additionally, IgG subclasses maintain the physical characteristics associated with the Fc, such as blood stability and standardized purification techniques. However, the manufacturing of IgG-based antibodies demands more advanced methodologies, and their permeability to tumor tissues is more limited than that of Fv-based BsAbs because of their higher molecular weight. In contrast, Fv-based bsAbs are smaller in size, allowing for improved penetration into tissues and less nonspecific activation of innate immune cells due to the lack of Fc domains. However, their short half-life should be considered. Bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs) constitute the first class of bsAbs that specifically recruit cytotoxic T cells to leukemia cells by binding to CD3 and leukemic antigens. This binding leads to T cell activation and the destruction of malignant cells. BiTEs are also employed for exhausted T cell stimulation by prolonged exposure to tumor antigens without costimulatory signals such as CD28 and interleukin (IL)-2. In BiTE contracts, a polypeptide linker connects four variable domains of heavy and light chains, maintaining the construct's flexibility and antigen-binding kinetics. Upon understanding the mechanism by which bsAbs target leukemic antigens via the immune system to combat cancer, additional variant constructs have emerged to enhance targeted therapies. Dual affinity retargeting antibodies (DARTs) and bispecific killer engager antibodies (BiKEs and TriKEs) are variant constructs of the bispecific engager approach. BiKEs and TriKEs exploit the innate immune system by using NK cells through the activation of CD16. Following activation, NK cells generate cytokines, specifically IL2, and initiate a cytotoxic response against targeted tumor cells. BiKEs consist of two single-chain variable fragments (scFVs) that target CD16, engaging both NK cells and tumor cell antigens. In contrast, TriKEs incorporate an IL-15 crosslinker to enhance the NK response as a third component alongside the two scFVs. DARTs possess a fundamental structure with 2 scFVs for targeting CD3 T cells and tumor antigens and utilizing a diabody backbone that incorporates a disulfide linker for enhanced stability. Altogether, bsAbs encompass a diverse range of constructs that are characterized by their ability to simultaneously bind to a tumor antigen and CD3 on T cells, thereby eliciting an endogenous, MHC-independent immune response against leukemic cells. However, the concurrent expression of target antigens in malignant AML cells and hematopoietic stem cells raises concerns regarding on-target off-tumor toxicities, especially cytopenia and hematologic toxicity (Fig. 2). Currently, many bsAbs for AML immunotherapy are undergoing clinical trials (Table 2), and numerous leukemic antigens represent promising targets for this therapeutic approach.

CD33 bispecific antibodies

Clinical trials assessing the efficacy of CD3 × CD33 bispecific T cell engager antibodies, including AMG 673, AMG-330, and GEM333, have been discontinued despite encouraging preliminary outcomes; however, two CD3 × CD33 bispecific antibodies, JNJ-67561244 and AMV564, are currently in clinical development following the results of initial phase 1 studies. The first BiTE to target CD33, AMG 330, binds specifically to a linear epitope in the V-type domain of CD33. Initial findings from the phase I trial indicated that 19% of patients with R/R AML exhibited a therapeutic response. AMG330 significantly upregulates PD-L1 on AML cells by inducing robust inflammatory cytokine responses; thus, the combination of BiTEs with immune checkpoint inhibitors such as anti-PD-1 may offer a novel strategy to mitigate treatment resistance. JNJ-67571244 binds to the C2 domain of CD33, causing a T cell response, and after in vitro and in vivo evaluation, it has entered phase 1 clinical trials in R/R AML and high-risk MDS due to encouraging preclinical results. AMV564, like previous bsAbs, has demonstrated a robust antitumor effect with prolonged survival in AML murine models. As anticipated, AMV564 demonstrated considerable antileukemic efficacy through T cell activation in phase 1 clinical trials.

CD123 bispecific antibodies

Clinical trials are currently evaluating a number of engineered bispecific antibodies targeting CD123, which have demonstrated strong antileukemic activity. Initial findings from a phase 1 dose-escalation study of XmAb14045 (vibecotamab), a CD123 × CD3-targeting bispecific antibody, in 104 R/R AML patients indicated an overall response rate (ORR) of 14% at the high dose level, with two patients attaining a complete response (CR). This ORR of 14% contrasts with the 19–31% CR/CRi rates observed with salvage chemotherapy (e.g., FLAG-Ida) in R/R AML. Notably, responders to XmAb14045 had a median OS of 8.7 months vs. 4.2 months in non-responders, comparable to historical outcomes with gemtuzumab ozogamicin (median OS 4.9–6.2 months). XmAb14045 is currently being evaluated in a phase 2 trial. Preclinical studies have demonstrated that flotetuzumab (MGD006), a CD123 × CD3 DART, is effective against primary AML samples and xenografts derived from a leukemic cell line. The efforts to enhance bsAbs resulted in the engineering of flotetuzumab, which has a greater affinity for CD123 than for CD3, facilitating selective binding to leukemic cells. In this scenario, circulating CD123-positive blasts are eradicated even at minimal doses of flotetuzumab, while normal hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow are maintained during drug administration. However, owing to the small size of the molecule, continuous infusion over a seven-day period is needed. Clinical trials with flotetuzumab are evaluating a dose escalation regimen for R/R AML, and MDS has demonstrated antileukemic activity with a manageable toxicity profile in R/R AML patients. The results from the AML patient subgroup revealed a median overall survival of 10.2 months among responders and a safety profile similar to that of other bispecific antibodies. Notably, studies have demonstrated that flotetuzumab activation leads to the upregulation of PD-1/PD-L1 expression on T cells. Clinical trials are currently investigating combination therapy using MGA012, an anti-PD-1 antibody, for R/R AML, with the hypothesis that it will demonstrate superior T cell activation compared with flotetuzumab monotherapy.

CLL-1/CLEC12A bispecific antibodies

Preliminary studies indicated that targeting CLEC12A with a bispecific antibody resulted in a reduction in the CLEC12A-positive cell population and dose-dependent activation of T cells. Krawczyk E et al. engineered a CLEC12A-targeting DART molecule (CLEC12A-ENG) by incorporating IL7Ra with a CD123-targeting ectodomain, which enhanced dual target recognition in vitro and in murine models while improving T cell survival and activation. Additionally, researchers developed a CLEC12A-targeting TriKE to address relapse-phase AML, where > 70% of CD33-negative cells express CLEC12A. Preclinical studies showed this TriKE minimized off-target effects on PBMCs compared to CD33-directed approaches, activated NK cells effectively, and selectively destroyed AML cells while sparing normal hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) in rodent models.

Antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs) in AML

ADCs represent an innovative class of therapeutics that combine the specificity of monoclonal antibodies with the cytotoxic potency of chemotherapeutic agents, offering a targeted approach to eradicate leukemic blasts while minimizing damage to normal hematopoietic cells. These agents consist of a monoclonal antibody directed against an antigen expressed on AML cells, a cytotoxic payload, and a linker that ensures drug release within the target cell upon internalization. ADCs have gained traction in AML therapy due to the overexpression of antigens like CD33 and CD123 on leukemic cells, providing a rational design framework for selective targeting.

The first ADC approved for AML was gemtuzumab ozogamicin (GO), a humanized anti-CD33 monoclonal antibody linked to the DNA-damaging agent calicheamicin. While GO was initially withdrawn due to toxicity concerns, subsequent studies using fractionated dosing demonstrated a favorable risk–benefit profile, particularly in combination with chemotherapy. The ALFA-0701 trial showed that GO combined with standard induction chemotherapy significantly improved event-free survival (EFS) in newly diagnosed AML patients, leading to its reapproval by the FDA and EMA. Despite this, GO monotherapy in relapsed/refractory (R/R) AML yields modest response rates, highlighting the need for optimized patient selection and combination strategies. Building on GO’s success, second-generation CD33-targeting ADCs have been developed. Vadastuximab talirine (SGN-33A) is a CD33-directed ADC conjugated to pyrrolobenzodiazepine (PBD) dimers, which crosslink DNA and induce apoptosis. Although promising in early-phase trials, the CASCADE phase III trial was terminated due to increased myelosuppression and early mortality, underscoring the importance of balancing efficacy with hematologic safety in older or unfit patients. Similarly, IMGN779, another CD33-directed ADC, links an anti-CD33 antibody to DGN462, a DNA-alkylating payload. Preclinical models demonstrated that IMGN779 is particularly effective against FLT3-mutated AML and synergizes with cytarabine through upregulation of CD33 expression, suggesting its potential in biomarker-guided combination regimens.

Beyond CD33, CD123-targeting ADCs have emerged as promising candidates. IMGN632, a CD123-directed ADC, has demonstrated potent activity in both preclinical and early-phase clinical trials, with manageable toxicity profiles and encouraging response rates in R/R AML. The targeting of CD123 is of particular interest due to its high expression on LSCs, which are implicated in disease relapse and resistance to conventional therapies.

An alternative ADC strategy involves the use of radioimmunoconjugates, such as lintuzumab-Ac225, which combines a CD33-targeting antibody with the alpha-emitting isotope actinium-225. This agent delivers highly localized cytotoxic radiation to AML cells. Recent phase I trials have shown high response rates and MRD negativity in R/R AML when lintuzumab-Ac225 was combined with intensive chemotherapy (CLAG-M) or venetoclax-based regimens, with acceptable toxicity profiles and no early mortality reported.

The integration of ADCs into combinatorial regimens represents a strategic evolution in AML therapy. For example, the combination of GO with hypomethylating agents (azacitidine or decitabine) or venetoclax is under active investigation, with early-phase data indicating synergistic activity and improved response durability in older patients and those unfit for intensive chemotherapy. Moreover, ongoing efforts are focused on optimizing ADC linker stability, reducing off-target effects, and developing ADCs against novel AML-restricted antigens such as CLEC12A and CLL-1 to further enhance specificity and reduce hematologic toxicity.

In summary, ADCs embody a paradigm shift in AML treatment, allowing selective delivery of cytotoxic agents to leukemic cells while sparing normal tissues. Although challenges such as on-target off-tumor toxicity, limited antigen exclusivity, and resistance mechanisms remain, continued innovation in ADC design and patient stratification holds promise for improving outcomes in both newly diagnosed and relapsed/refractory AML populations (Table 3). 

Combinational therapy

The short remission period and early relapse after antiapoptotic protein inhibition, as well as the substantial toxicity linked to nonspecific standard chemotherapy (e.g., G. venetoclox) in AML patients who develop mutations in FMS-like tyrosine kinase 3 (FLT3) family proteins and other BCL2 family proteins emphasizes the significance of combination therapy approaches. Clinical and preclinical research on AML has focused on epigenetic drugs because changes in epigenetic regulators are pharmacologically reversible and play a role in the pathophysiology of AML. As a result of this development, the "one-fits-all" approach to treating AML gave way to precision therapy. In recent decades, hypomethylating agents (HMAs) like decitabine and azacitidine have emerged as the go-to low-intensity treatments for unsuitable patients with AML. Specifically, in patients with low cytogenetic risk, azacitidine has shown better results than intensive chemotherapy and less myelotoxicity than decitabine. Moreover, the combination of immunotherapy techniques with targeted therapeutic agents and the synergistic use of targeted therapies have recently demonstrated encouraging synergistic effects in the treatment of AML, which will be covered below.

Combining anti-CD33 monoclonal antibodies with targeted therapies

Numerous phase I and II investigations are presently examining the advantages of combining Gemtuzumab ozogamicin (GO) with various pharmacological agents, including azacitidine, venetoclax, and other standard medications, during the induction phase of therapy. A phase Ib trial demonstrated the safety of the combination of GO and venetoclax, and the efficacy of this regimen is currently being investigated. A retrospective analysis involving 17 patients with R/R AML indicated that the combination of GO and azacitidine achieved an overall response rate of 76.9%, suggesting its potential as a bridging therapy for transplantation. In the phase I MOSAIC trial, GO was combined with midostaurin and standard 3 + 7 chemotherapy to treat upfront AML, resulting in a complete clinical response (cCR) rate of 91%. Next-generation anti-CD33 agents, including vadastuximab talirine (SNG33A), a humanized murine anti-CD33 immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1) monoclonal antibody conjugated with two molecules of pyrrolobenzodiazepine (PBD), are currently in development. This agent demonstrated favorable tolerability in monotherapy and exhibited a promising CR rate (CR + CRi 73%) in a study involving 53 elderly patients with AML who were ineligible for intensive chemotherapy.

Combining anti-CD47 monoclonal antibodies with HMAs

CD47 is a transmembrane agent that delivers antiphagocytic signals, and its inhibition has demonstrated potential for eradicating leukemia stem cells in preclinical models. The IgG4 anti-CD47 magrolimab demonstrated an acceptable tolerability profile, exhibiting limited antileukemic activity as a monotherapy in patients with R/R AML during a phase I trial. Considering that azacitidine enhances the expression of antiphagocytic signals on leukemic cells, increasing the efficacy of magrolimab serves as the basis for investigating the combined use of these agents. In a recent phase Ib trial, combining magrolimab with azacitidine demonstrated a cCR rate of 64% in untreated AML patients and 91% in high-risk MDS patients who were not eligible for intensive chemotherapy. Notably, 74% of cCRs were observed within the subgroup of AMLs characterized by mutations in the TP53 gene. This CR rate of 64% compares favorably to the 28–33% CR rates with azacitidine monotherapy in similar cohorts. However, the phase III ENHANCE trial (NCT05079230) showed no OS benefit (HR 1.02, p = 0.89) for magrolimab + azacitidine vs. placebo + azacitidine, leading to trial termination. A phase III investigation assessing the efficacy of magrolimab in conjunction with azacitidine (NCT04778397) is currently in progress, alongside a phase I study examining the combination of magrolimab, azacitidine, and venetoclax (NCT04435691).

Challenges future direction

The development of immunotherapy for AML has made significant strides, yet several challenges impede its widespread clinical success. Addressing these obstacles requires innovative strategies and a deeper understanding of AML biology.

Current challenges

Tumor heterogeneity and immune evasion mechanisms

AML’s genetic and epigenetic diversity contributes to variable treatment responses and relapse. The TME further complicates therapy by fostering immune evasion through downregulation of HLA molecules, overexpression of inhibitory checkpoints (PD-L1, TIM-3), and secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10). These mechanisms shield LSCs and MRD, perpetuating resistance.

On-target off-tumor toxicity

Many antigens targeted by monoclonal antibodies (e.g., CD33, CD123) are expressed on healthy myeloid cells, leading to myelosuppression and prolonged cytopenias. For instance, anti-CD47 therapies risk anemia due to CD47 expression on erythrocytes, while anti-CD33 agents like GO cause hematologic toxicity.

Treatment resistance

Resistance emerges through upregulation of inhibitory receptors (e.g., PD-1/PD-L1) and compensatory survival pathways. BiTEs such as AMG 330 induce PD-L1 on AML blasts, dampening T cell responses. Similarly, FLT3-mutated AML cells develop resistance to tyrosine kinase inhibitors via secondary mutations.

Immune-related adverse events

Cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and neurotoxicity remain significant risks with T cell-engaging therapies. Trials of AMG 673 and AMV564 reported CRS in 50% of patients, necessitating dose limitations.

Future directions

Biomarker-driven personalized therapy

Integrating genomic and epigenomic profiling with immune biomarkers (e.g., CD25, CD123 expression) could stratify patients for targeted therapies. For example, CD70 expression predicts response to cusatuzumab, while TP53 mutations may guide anti-CD47 combinations.

Novel targets with restricted expression

Antigens like CLEC12A/CLL-1, expressed on LSCs but absent on HSCs, offer safer therapeutic windows. CLEC12A-targeting bispecific antibodies and TriKEs show preclinical promise with reduced off-tumor effects.

Combination strategies to overcome resistance

Pairing checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1) with BiTEs or ADCs may counteract immune exhaustion. Preclinical data suggest that PD-1 blockade restores AMG 330 efficacy. Similarly, hypomethylating agents (azacitidine) synergize with anti-CD47 antibodies by enhancing “eat me” signals on AML cells.

Engineering next-generation antibodies

Fc optimization, trispecific antibodies, and conditionally active biologics (e.g., pH-sensitive constructs) could improve specificity. For instance, FLYSYN, an Fc-engineered anti-FLT3 antibody, enhances antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) while sparing normal cells.

Modulating the TME

Targeting immunosuppressive cytokines (TGF-β, IL-10) or MDSCs may re-sensitize AML to immunotherapy. Preclinical studies demonstrate that TGF-β blockade restores NK cell function.

Advanced preclinical models and trial design

Patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models and organoids better recapitulate AML heterogeneity, accelerating drug screening. Adaptive clinical trials incorporating MRD monitoring and dynamic biomarkers (e.g., soluble NKG2D ligands) could refine response assessment.

Conclusion

The landscape of AML therapy has been profoundly reshaped by advancements in antibody engineering, offering unprecedented precision in targeting leukemic cells while sparing healthy tissues. Monoclonal antibodies, BiTEs, and ADCs have demonstrated remarkable potential in redirecting the immune system against AML-specific antigens, such as CD33, CD123, and CLEC12A, overcoming traditional limitations of chemotherapy and radiation. Innovations like trispecific antibodies and Fc-optimized constructs further amplify therapeutic efficacy by enhancing immune cell engagement and minimizing off-target effects. Combination regimens integrating these engineered antibodies with hypomethylating agents, venetoclax, or checkpoint inhibitors have shown synergistic activity, addressing resistance mechanisms and prolonging remission in high-risk and relapsed/refractory populations.

However, challenges persist, including tumor heterogeneity, immune evasion via the immunosuppressive TME, and on-target off-tumor toxicities. The dynamic interplay between AML blasts and stromal components underscores the need for therapies that concurrently disrupt leukemic niches and reinvigorate immune surveillance. Future progress hinges on the discovery of novel, leukemia-restricted targets, the development of conditionally active or logic-gated antibodies, and biomarker-driven strategies to personalize treatment. Collaborative efforts between academia and industry will be critical to translate preclinical breakthroughs into clinically viable therapies, particularly for elderly and frail patients who remain underserved by current modalities.

As antibody engineering continues to evolve, the integration of multi-omics profiling, advanced preclinical models, and adaptive clinical trial designs will accelerate the development of next-generation immunotherapies. By marrying scientific innovation with clinical pragmatism, these approaches hold the promise of transforming AML from a lethal malignancy into a chronically manageable disease, ultimately improving survival and quality of life for patients worldwide. The journey from bench to bedside is far from complete, but the path forward is illuminated by the remarkable strides already achieved in this revolutionary era of immuno-oncology.
