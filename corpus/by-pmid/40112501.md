# Mechanisms of radioresistance and radiosensitization strategies for Triple Negative Breast Cancer.

## Abstract

Breast cancer is one of the most common malignant tumors in women. Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is a molecular subtype of breast cancer that is characterized by a high risk of recurrence and poor prognosis. With the increasingly prominent role of radiotherapy in TNBC treatment, patient resistance to radiotherapy is an attractive area of clinical research. Gene expression changes induced by multiple mechanisms can affect the radiosensitivity of TNBC cells to radiotherapy through a variety of ways, and the enhancement of radioresistance is an important factor in the malignant progression of TNBC. The above pathways mainly include DNA damage repair, programmed cell death, cancer stem cells (CSC), antioxidant function, tumor microenvironment, and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) pathway. Tumor cells can reduce the damage of radiotherapy to themselves through the above ways, resulting in radioresistance. Therefore, in this review, we aim to summarize the strategies for immunotherapy combined with radiotherapy, targeted therapy combined with radiotherapy, and epigenetic therapy combined with radiotherapy to identify the best treatment for TNBC and improve the cure and survival rates of patients with TNBC. This review will provide important guidance and inspiration for the clinical practice of radiotherapy for TNBC, which will help deepen our understanding of this field and promote its development.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

Breast cancer is one of the most common malignant tumor in women. In 2020, there were more than 2.26 million new cases of adenocarcinoma in women worldwide. Triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) is a molecular subtype of breast cancer characterized by strong invasiveness, high metastasis, and poor prognosis, which cannot be treated with conventional endocrine and targeted therapies. Therefore, radiotherapy can significantly affect the survival and prognosis of patients with TNBC.

Radiotherapy is an important component of comprehensive breast cancer treatment that uses high-energy radiation (e.g., photon and particle radiation) to directly or indirectly damage DNA structure in tumor cells. The direct effect is reflected in the direct effect of radiation energy on DNA molecules, and the consequence is the substantial damage of DNA structure. Relatively speaking, the indirect effect involves the effect of radiation energy on the molecules around DNA, which activates the generation of free radicals. These highly reactive free radicals indirectly lead to DNA damage through complex biochemical pathways, which prevents cell growth and division, regulates the cell cycle, and ultimately leads to necrosis, apoptosis, senescence, and autophagy of tumor cells. Tumor radioresistance occurs during continuous radiotherapy, which refers to the resistance of tumor cells to radiation therapy during radiotherapy. Specifically, it refers to the phenomenon that during the course of radiotherapy, cells that were originally sensitive to radiation therapy, after a period of radiotherapy, the response to radiation therapy is reduced, or even no longer sensitive, resulting in a decrease in treatment effect or treatment failure. This phenomenon was described by Lassueur in 1904. It was later examined for such important features as the ploidy level of the tumor; the condition of its mitochondria and ribosomes; the quantity of sulfhydryl groups present; the tumor's ability to undergo anaerobic glycolysis; the level of catalase activity; and alterations in the immunological interactions between the tumor and the host organism. The above definition of "radioresistance" is based on the continuous radiation model, but clinical TNBC radiotherapy uses a fractionated dose strategy. Although the modelling conditions are different, many of the molecular mechanisms mentioned in the paper are common to both scenarios, and therefore this review may provide a theoretical reference for clinical radioresistance. Radiotherapy can be divided into different types, for example, 3D conformal radiotherapy, intensity modulated radiation therapy, image-guided radiotherapy, and stereotactic body radiotherapy. Although radiotherapy plays a crucial role in the treatment of patients with TNBC, there are considerable differences in patient response and resistance to this treatment. Therefore, herein, we summarize the mechanisms that may affect the radiosensitivity to radiotherapy, including the regulation of DNA damage repair, programmed cell death, cancer stem cells (CSC), antioxidant function, tumor microenvironment, and epithelial–mesenchymal transition (EMT) pathway, to identify new targets for TNBC treatment. Our review will help to understand radiotherapy-sensitized drugs and provide a foundation for combining radiotherapy with immunotherapy, targeted therapy, and epigenetic therapy to develop better treatment strategies for patients with TNBC.

Mechanisms affecting radioresistance

DNA repair pathway

The DNA damage response is a complex system. When tumor cells are irradiated by IR, the enormous energy causes extensive damage to their DNA, the most serious of which are double-strand breaks (DSBs). Damage repair in DSBs relies mainly on the homologous recombination (HR) and non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathways. Upon initiation of the NHEJ pathway, it is mainly Ku80 and Ku70 that first recognizes and binds to DSBs and serves as a scaffold for the recruitment of other components. The recruited DNA-PKcs phosphorylate themselves and in turn the activated Artemis nuclease. Artemis and the C-terminal binding protein interacting protein (CtIP)-dependent stimulation of the MRE11/Rad50/NBS1 (MRN) complex processed and trimmed DNA ends to bring their DNA double strands closer together. Finally, the DNA ligase complex formed by DNA ligase IV, X-ray repair cross-complementing protein 4 (XRCC4) and XRCC4-like factor (XLF) connects the ends of the double strand and completes the repair. HR repair restores the previous original sequence by means of precise repair of sister chromatids, but is limited to the S/G2 phase of the cell cycle. When initiating HR, γ-H2AX and RAD17 recruit the MRN complex to the break site. NBS1 in the MRN complex activates Ataxia-telangiectasia mutated (ATM), while activated ATM phosphorylates the MRN complex and modulates the DSB cascade response. Then MRE11 exonuclease and EXO1/BLM were bi-directionally excised towards and away from the DNA end. Replication Protein A (RPA) is encapsulated on ssDNA, Rad51 form the presynaptic filament on RPA-coated ssDNA, and by invading and pairing sister chromatid monomers, the triple DNA structure formed provides a template for DNA repair. Repair allows the damage caused by radiotherapy to be eliminated, which may eventually lead to resistance to radiotherapy. γH2AX foci form in its early phases, and their size suggests the extent of DNA damage. Below we summarize the mechanisms by which multiple molecular signals directly or indirectly affect key proteins of DNA repair and thus lead to IR resistance or sensitivity in TNBC.

ATM and RAD3-related (ATR) are members of the phosphatidylinositol 3-kinase (PI3K)-related protein kinase family that are required in the homologous recombination (HR) pathway. Checkpoint kinase 1 (CHK1) acts as a downstream target of ATR and participates in HR repair processes. dual inhibition of CHK1 and Aurora kinase A (AURKA) induces excessive DNA damage, increasing radiosensitivity. Meanwhile MK-8776 was developed as an inhibitor of CHK1, and has now been tested in clinical trials in the field of leukemia and solid tumors to evaluate the feasibility of chemotherapy, and has also achieved promising efficacy. On this basis, Zhou et al. divided the groups into four and compared them with the control group by administering MK-8776 at 15 mg/kg and 40 mg/kg with or without a radiation dose of 15 Gy in a TNBC mouse model. The results showed that MK-8776 enhanced IR-induced DNA damage in vivo and also revealed that suppression of CHK1 expression increased BAK protein production and downregulated the expression of Rad51. The favorable outcome obtained in this study demonstrates the importance of this gene for radiotherapy and may be further explored as a priority molecular target. Moreover, RNF126 acts as an E3 ubiquitin ligase, under non-radiation conditions, RNF126 physically binds to the MRN complex and ubiquitinates MRE11 at the K339 and K480 sites to increase its DNA exonuclease activity. Most importantly, the HER2-AKT-NF-κB signaling pathway leads to high RNF126 expression after IR, which enhances the above mechanisms and increases IR-induced ATR-CHK1 activation, leading to radioresistance. Dysregulated Hippo/YAP pathway is associated with tumor progression and radioresistance in cancer. CD146 was able to interact with integrin β1 to form a co-receptor, which in turn inhibited the phosphorylation expression of LATS1 and increased the nuclear translocation of YAP. IR induces the upregulation of CD146 expression and enhances the interaction with integrin β1 further promoting the activation of LATS1-YAP signaling leading to radioresistance, not only that, CD146 also activates CHK1/2 activity and rapidly abrogates γH2A-X. Thus, CD146 may play an essential role in DNA repair.

The NHEJ pathway plays a dominant role in DSB repair, with XRCC4 as a core member involved in the NHEJ pathway. Low XRCC4 expression delays repair of irradiation (IR)-induced DNA damage, and there is a growing body of evidence that shows that IR promotes epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) phosphorylation in a ligand-independent manner and its translocation into the nucleus, thereby increasing the repair of DNA damage. Silencing CAVEOLIN-1 directly reduces this nuclear translocation causing a decrease in the expression of both p-DNA-PKcs and KU70/80 compared to treatment with IR alone and the involvement of IR-induced DNA damage response via γH2AX. It has been found that The AT-rich interactive domain 1A (SWI-like) gene (ARID1A) promotes the accumulation of Ku70/Ku80 proteins into the DSBs and increases the initiation of NHEJ. HuR stabilizes the mRNA of ARID1A, resulting in an increase in ARID1A expression and a decrease in IR-induced DNA damage. Additionally, IR binding to downregulated HuR resulted in an increase in P27 expression and a significant decrease in KU80. All of the genes described above may be potential targets for radiosensitization in triple-negative breast cancer. However, Musashi RNA-binding proteins (MSIs) are tightly correlated with the Notch pathway, and the inhibition of MSI expression can directly downregulate DNA-PKcs and EGFR expression, leading to reduced radioresistance.

When IR is combined with a monopolar spindle 1 (Mps1) knockdown, HR repair efficiency was decreased and sensitized cells to radiotherapy. Moreover, when BMI1 silencing was combined with radiotherapy, it decreased the expression of ATM, DNA-PK, Ku70, and Ku80, which also sensitized cells to radiotherapy. we summarize the mechanism underlying the DNA repair pathway in Fig. 1. As we know, the Notch signaling pathway, TGF-β signaling pathway, JAK/Stat3 signaling pathway, MAPK signaling pathway, Hedgehog signaling pathway, Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway, PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway, and NF-κB signaling pathway are activated in TNBC to promote the progression of TNBC. The Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway, JAK/Stat3 signaling pathway, Notch signaling pathway, NF-κB signaling pathway, and PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway were also activated in TNBC after irradiation, suggesting us whether these five pathways provide favorable conditions for TNBC survival after irradiation. Other signaling pathways need to be further investigated.

Abnormal regulation of programmed cell death

Apoptosis, ferroptosis, pyroptosis, and autophagy are forms of programmed cell death. Programmed cell death is crucial for organism development because it eliminates damaged or senescent cells, controls cell number, and maintains intracellular environmental stability. Abnormal cell death occurs when homeostasis is disrupted and therefore, conscious induced programmed cell death in TNBC cells can increase cell mortality and enhance radiotherapy sensitivity.

Apoptosis can be achieved via death receptors and mitochondrial and endoplasmic reticulum stress, in which caspase and Bcl-2 families play important roles as potential regulators of these processes. In TNBC, the inhibition of TMPRSS4 or knockdown of the protein disulfide isomerase family member 4 (PDIA4) gene downregulates Bcl-2 expression and upregulates Bax expression, and silencing transcription factor 3 (ATF3) increases the activity of caspase3, increasing the percentage of radiation-induced apoptosis. The inhibition of CXCR4 expression can lead to the accumulation of Bax and caspase-3. AMD3100 as an SDF-1 receptor CXCR4 antagonist can be used as a radiosensitizer in TNBC models. The authors performed experiments with immunodeficient nude mice and found that the addition of the inhibitor AMD3100 and the combination of radiation therapy had a much greater inhibitory effect on TNBC tumors than that of the inhibitor or radiation therapy alone, and thus the gene may be a preferred target to be involved in the treatment of TNBC. Moreover, the downregulation of complement factor H (CFH) promotes the activation of p38, which regulates the Bax/Bcl-2/caspase-3, -7, and -9 cascade pathways. O6-methylguanine DNA-methyltransferase (MGMT) deficiency promotes radiation-induced caspase activation in TNBC, promoting apoptosis. Additionally, Cyclin D1 deletion enhanced proton relative biological effectiveness (RBE) resulting in increased apoptosis. Irradiation of MDA-MB-231 with 4 Gy x-rays or protons silenced Cyclin D1 significantly promoted Cyclin D1-silenced group compared to control group radiation-induced apoptosis. In addition, we know that metformin, the most widely prescribed glucose-lowering biguanide for the treatment of patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM) is also one of the well-known anticancer drugs, and that metformin downregulates Cyclin D1 and upregulates P53 through an AMPK-α-independent mechanism to promote apoptosis in primary breast cancer cells. Therefore, it was speculated that Cyclin D1 could be involved as a preferred target in the treatment of TNBC. In irradiated TNBC cells, silencing miR-185-5p attenuated cell proliferation inhibition and apoptosis promotion caused by PCAT6 downregulation. The authors demonstrated in TNBC cells by a dual luciferase reporter gene assay that TPD52 contains a binding site for miR-185-5p and that apoptosis was higher in combined 4Gy cells compared to downregulation of TPD52 alone. It is suggested that inhibition of PCAT6 promotes apoptosis through upregulation of miR-185-5p expression and downregulation of TPD52 expression thereby enhancing radiotherapy sensitivity. Inhibition of HK2 gene promotes apoptosis and thus improves radiosensitivity. According to the Warburg effect, TNBC and HER2-positive BC have higher levels of glycolytic activity compared to ER-positive BC cells. The authors hypothesized that if the initial step of glycolysis is inhibited in tumor cells, it will stop the tumor anabolic process. Therefore HK2, as the enzyme that catalyzes the first step of glycolysis, seems to be an ideal target for cancer therapy. To verify the effect of radiotherapy on TNBC, the authors performed IR treatment (daily fraction of 2 Gy for a total of 10 Gy) in TNBC xenograft animals with and without knockdown of HK2. The study showed that the growth rate of knocked down HK2 was significantly slower than the growth rate of the control group. After IR, tumors in knocked down HK2 grew slower than tumors in the control group (p = 0.0016) suggesting that knocked down HK2 improves the radiosensitivity of TNBC. Musashi-1 (MSI) deletion resulted in an elevation of the expression of a pathway inhibitor, m-numb, which downregulated the expression of TNBC, thereby down-regulating the Notch-1 signaling pathway and promoting apoptosis. The Notch pathway has been described to confer radiation resistance by enhancing cancer stem cell properties, and thus down-regulation of MSI increases radiosensitivity. And apoptosis experiments showed that the anti-proliferative effect also appears to have pro-apoptotic properties: knockdown cells were more likely to bind membrane-bound protein V, indicating apoptotic characteristics. Meanwhile, down-regulation of CPNE1 increased cleaved caspase3 levels and promoted apoptosis, and in vivo xenograft studies also showed that knockdown of CPNE1 inhibited tumor growth and promoted apoptosis. The authors determined CPNE1 expression in irradiation-sensitive and non-irradiation-sensitive tissues in TNBC patients and showed that CPNE1 expression was higher in drug-resistant TNBC patients than in radiosensitive TNBC patients. Therefore targeting CPNE1 expression may be a strategy to sensitize TNBC to radiation therapy.Together, these findings indicate that apoptosis plays an important role in promoting the radiosensitivity of breast cancer.

A literature review indicated that autophagy may reduce the cytotoxic effects of radiation in TNBC. After IR, the expression of LC3-II in cells increased and the level of p62 protein decreased, indicating that autophagy was activated. The microRNA MiR-129-5p can inhibit autophagy and enhance radiosensitivity by targeting high mobility group protein box 1 (HMGB1). Pyroptosis is a gasdermin-mediated inflammatory form of cell death. When MiR-1290 is overexpressed in radioresistant cells, it potentially targets NLRP3 and can partially reverse the effect of miR-1290 on pyroptosis and radioresistance.

Ferroptosis is a form of programmed cell death that is caused by lipid peroxidation. High levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS) in cells caused by radiotherapy can induce lipid peroxidation and directly promote ferroptosis. The mechanisms underlying programmed cell death are illustrated Fig. 2.

Reactive oxygen species pathway

ROS are derivatives of molecular oxygen formed by redox reactions or electronic excitation. When their levels are elevated, oxidative damage is triggered and can lead to apoptosis, autophagy, and iron-related cell death. Radiotherapy can directly or indirectly increase the levels of free radicals and electrons, considerably increasing the concentration of ROS. Notably, the transcription of a series of cytoprotective genes and reprogramming of cellular metabolism can elevate antioxidant levels in tumor cells.

NF-κB is an important mediator of inflammatory factor release from tumor cells, and ROS-activated NF-κB mediates the release of IL-6 in breast cancer, which regulates CSC renewal and cancer metastasis through the IL6R/gp130/STAT3 signaling pathway. Furthermore, protein kinase R-like endoplasmic reticulum kinase (PERK) is an important sensor of the integrated stress response, which is triggered by phosphorylating eIF2α that can promote the expression of genes such as ATF4 and GCLC to induce glutathione biosynthesis. This pathway can protect tumor cells against damage caused by ROS, increase cellular activity after radiotherapy, and induce radioresistance in TNBC cells.It is worth mentioning that increased intracellular ROS can directly activate the PI3K-AKT pathway to produce NADPH, which helps cells resist ROS damage and enhances radioresistance.The aforementioned genes possess the capability to augment the resistance of triple-negative breast cancer to therapeutic radiotherapeutic interventions.

Subsequently, we summarized the genes that can enhance the radiosensitivity of triple-negative breast cancer and their mechanisms. The overexpression of thioredoxin reductase (TrxR) plays an important role in redox homeostasis, with HuR functioning as an mRNA-binding protein. Silencing HuR can enhance ROS accumulation and inhibit the TrxR system, which mediates the radiosensitization of tumor cells. Nuclear factor erythroid-2-related factor 2 (Nrf2) is another important regulator of intracellular antioxidant responses and is activated by IR. Knockdown of Nrf2 may enhance the effects of IR by promoting ROS production and strengthening the killing of TNBC CSCs through oxidative stress-mediated cytotoxicity. And in the study, the author proved that Nrf2 can also participate in the repair of damaged DNA, so we can think that inhibition of Nrf2 can enhance the sensitivity of tumors to radiotherapy from different ways. Therefore, ML385 as an Nrf2 inhibitor can be used as a new direction for future research to explore its optimal concentration with radiotherapy to achieve the best therapeutic effect and improve the prognosis of patients. Mitochondria are well-known sources of ROS, and their dysfunction increases ROS levels. Silencing of protein disulfide isomerase family member 4 (PDIA4) leads to higher ROS levels, mitochondrial damage, and increased cell death, which collectively increase radioresistance in TNBC.

Given that ROS play important roles in apoptosis, autophagy, and ferroptosis in tumor cells, modulating the signaling pathways related to ROS synthesis and action in tumor cells to disrupt the redox balance and inhibit the production of antioxidants can serve as an important mechanism to slow down radioresistance. However, high ROS concentrations can affect normal tissue activity and impede cell function. Therefore, it is particularly important to effectively control ROS levels and distribution as part of cancer treatment programs. Melatonin can be used as an antioxidant to resist damage caused by ROS. When combined with radiotherapy, melatonin can reduce the toxic effects on non-tumor cells. Tumor microenvironment

Radiotherapy not only affects the tumor cells themselves but also alters the tumor microenvironment, and these effects are critical to the sensitivity of tumors to radiotherapy. Radiation-induced vascular injury exacerbates tumor hypoxia, and tumor cells activate the hypoxia-responsive transcription factor HIF-1α to adapt to hypoxic conditions. HIF-1α promotes the expression of angiogenic factors and the formation of an abnormal vascular system, but this does not resolve tumor hypoxia. However, DNA damage is reduced under hypoxic conditions, which improves radioresistance. Furthermore, ATM activity is upregulated in hypoxic environments, and when DNA strands are broken, ATM is activated and phosphorylates proteins such as BRCA1 and FANCD2 to promote the repair of damaged DNA.

Radiotherapy in TNBC also influences the immune system. For example, radiotherapy can increase the expression of PD-L1, which reduces the activation of T cells, causing them to become inactivated and die, and M2 tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), Myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), and Tregs increase after localized RT. M2 TAMs secrete proangiogenic factors. Treg cells accumulate in the tumor microenvironment and inhibit activation of effector T cells by secreting the cytokines TGFβ and IL-10. MDSCs promote tumor cell survival, stimulate angiogenesis, and enhance tumor cell invasiveness and metastasis, resulting in enhanced tumor resistance to RT. Meanwhile, inhibition of BRD4 significantly reduced the expression of HIF-1α and PD-L1 and increased the proportion of TAMs in the M1 type and decreased the proportion of TAMs in the M2 type in tumors. The application of BRD4 inhibitors expanded the number of CD4 and CD8 T cells in the tumor microenvironment. Radiotherapy allows endogenous SERPINE1 from tumor cells to be secreted into the tumor microenvironment, enhances SERPINE1-mediated signaling, translocates to the nucleus, and promotes DSB repair.

The oncolytic viruses (OVs) can be used as a preoperative adjuvant therapy to promote the infiltration of T cells into the tumor microenvironment and enhance the immune response to tumors. In a phase II clinical trial, 150 patients with resectable stage IIIB-IVM1a melanoma were randomly assigned to receive OVs treatment or surgery alone. The results showed that the 2-year recurrence-free survival of patients receiving the OVs treatment was significantly improved, reaching 29.5%, whereas that of patients receiving surgery alone was 16.5% (HR 0.75, 80% CI 0.58–0.96). Therefore, the OVs can be used as part of an effective strategy for the treatment of TNBC. We summarize the effect of the tumor microenvironment on radiosensitivity in Fig. 4.

Cancer stem cell and epithelial–mesenchymal transition

Irradiation promotes the metastasis and invasion of tumor cells by inducing EMT, which is closely related to the morbidity and mortality of TNBC. Moreover, IR can induce CSC characteristics, including dedifferentiation and self-renewal, which can affect TNBC cells by activating the EMT pathway. Therefore, we further explored the effects of CSC and EMT on IR resistance through the PI3K/Akt/mTOR, Notch, MAPK/ERK, ATM/Chk2, Hippo, and other pathways.

The PI3K/Akt/mTOR signaling pathway plays a crucial role in enhancing the survival of stem cells and impacts radiosensitivity.Verteporfin, a drug known to be used to treat a variety of ophthalmic diseases, is gradually being explored as a potential YAP1 inhibitor. Recent studies have shown that the combination of verteporfin and photodynamic therapy (PDT) has been proven to be feasible and safe in phase I / II clinical trials of locally advanced pancreatic cancer. On this basis, iR-induced YAP1 silencing reduces the expression of EMT markers and inhibits breast cancer cell migration and invasion, as well as the EGFR/PI3K/AKT signaling axis and accumulating DNA damage. Conversely, POU3F2 interacts with ARNT2 to promote cell proliferation, migration, and invasion by activating the AKT pathway, enhancing radioresistance in TNBC. Thus, the PI3K/Akt/mTOR pathway plays a crucial role in regulating TNBC response to radiation.

The Notch signaling pathway is a well-known CSC pathway in different types of human tumors, such as breast cancer, that regulates self-renewal and inhibits differentiation. Troschel et al. found that MSI is a small-molecule intracellular protein and that its knockout decreases the radioresistance of breast cancer cells owing to reduced stem cell characteristics, decreased Notch signaling, and downregulation of DNA-PKcs and epidermal growth factor receptors. Additionally, Lee et al. observed that TRIB3 promotes stem cell characteristics and radioresistance in TNBC cells by activating Notch1. These results suggest that inhibition of the Notch pathway could be developed as an adjunct therapy to improve current radiotherapy for TNBC.

The MAPK signaling pathway includes three branches: ERK, JNK, and p38. Its activation can increase the sensitivity of tumor cells to radiotherapy and affect the proliferation and differentiation potential of stem cells. For example, CFH downregulation promotes the expression of EPB41L3 through the MAPK signaling pathway, inhibits the expression of breast cancer stem cell-specific genes, and inhibits the activation of ERK, all of which contribute to the inhibition of stemness in breast cancer cells. GSK484, as a specific inhibitor of PAD4, has been shown to significantly increase the radiosensitivity of colorectal cancer (CRC) and effectively inhibit the formation of neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) in vitro and in vivo. In addition, GSK484 can also inhibit IR-induced autophagy via the JNK pathway, resulting in increased sensitivity to radiotherapy. Overall, these findings demonstrate that MAPK signal transduction plays a key role in the regulation of TNBC radioresistance.

The ATM/Chk2 signaling pathway also plays a role in enhancing radioresistance. IR can stabilize ZEB1 through ATM signaling to promote EMT in tumor cells. Therefore, radiation-induced changes in the ATM/Chk2 pathway may regulate EMT and CSC characteristics to mediate the TNBC response to radiotherapy.

The Hippo signaling pathway affects several cellular processes. The transmembrane receptor protein CD146 can interact with integrin β1 to regulate LATS1-YAP signaling and DNA damage repair downstream of the Hippo signaling pathway. Consequently, CD146 promotes EMT and tumor cell stemness; therefore, targeting the Hippo signaling pathway may offer a wide range of therapeutic applications for radioresistance.

The Nrf2 signaling pathway enhances the resistance of tumor cells to oxidative stress. Milković et al. demonstrated the association between NQO1 upregulation and Nrf2 expression. Additionally, the long non-coding RNA NEAT1 positively regulates the activity of cancer stem cells by controlling the protein expression of NQO1. These studies suggest that targeting the Nrf2 pathway can improve the effectiveness of radiotherapy.

The Wnt/β-catenin signaling pathway affects stem cells. IR can induce the nuclear translocation of PK-M2, which can maintain the stem cell phenotype through the Wnt/β-catenin pathway. In Supplementary Figure 1, we summarize the effects of EMT and CSC on radiosensitivity.

Understanding how to reduce radioresistance by mediating IR-regulated signaling pathways is an important topic for further study. In this context, YAP1, MSI, and CFH play important roles in the Hippo, Notch, and MAPK/ERK pathways, respectively, and balancing the relationships between these signaling pathways to achieve the best effects is a critical direction for future research.

In summary, considering the various factors affecting radiotherapy, a key strategy to achieve optimal effects is to use key proteins or genes of the signaling pathway as therapeutic targets to reduce radioresistance by regulating their activity. Common effects of thoracic radiotherapy include radiation-induced lung injury and heart disease. Lung injury includes acute inflammation and chronic fibrosis, potentially leading to dyspnea and chronic pulmonary fibrosis. Radiation-induced heart diseases can occur several years after the completion of radiotherapy, including valvular disease, pericardial disease, coronary artery disease, cardiomyopathy, and conduction abnormalities. Although radiotherapy is the most important risk factor, traditional cardiovascular risk factors such as diabetes, hypertension, obesity, and smoking also increase the risk of disease after radiotherapy.

Strategies for avoiding radioresistance

Immunotherapy combined with radiotherapy

Immunotherapy is an emerging therapeutic strategy that can be combined with radiotherapy to synergistically enhance treatment effectiveness. Additionally, immunotherapy can neutralize the immunosuppressive effects of the tumor microenvironment and activate CD8+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes to alleviate the immune limitations of radiotherapy. Table 1 summarizes clinical trials in which PD-1 inhibitors were combined with radiotherapy for TNBC in various states. Current research focuses on enhancing the immune response by designing antibodies against PD-1 and its ligands. In a phase II clinical trial conducted in the United States, 17 patients were enrolled to evaluate the efficacy of pembrolizumab and radiotherapy in metastatic TNBC. The treatment schedule included a total dose of 3 000 centigrays of radiotherapy administered five times per day and pembrolizumab given intravenously at a dose of 200 mg within three days of the first radiotherapy session, followed by administration every three weeks ± three days until disease progression. By measuring the overall response rate, 3 complete remissions, 1 stable disease, and 13 cases of disease progression were identified (3 of 17 patients; 95% CI 4.7%-44.2%). The positive side of this study is that it demonstrated that this combination therapy is safe, but it did not demonstrate encouraging efficacy.The promise of combination therapy was further explored in a preliminary clinical study in China, where 10 TNBC patients were treated with Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy (SBRT) in combination with adebrelimab and chemotherapy. The primary endpoint was pathological complete response (pCR) after 8 cycles of treatment and final surgical resection, which showed that 9 out of 10 patients achieved pCR in the breast and axillary lymph nodes (90%, 95% CI 55.5-99.8%). This study can be considered as an initial exploration of successful efficacy, but unfortunately the lack of sample size and non-comparative trials make it impossible to directly state that the efficacy obtained was due to the combination therapy, and therefore further refinement of the clinical trial protocol may be required to demonstrate efficacy. A preclinical study compared the efficacy of radiotherapy alone versus radiotherapy combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors and found that the combination enhanced distant effects, reduced tumor size, and decreased the number of MDSCs in a mouse model of 4T1 breast cancer. This finding further demonstrates that a combination of radiotherapy and immunotherapy is more effective than radiotherapy alone. Notably, D-mannose as a hexose has been reported to be capable of degrading PD-L1, while the combination of D-mannose with IR sensitized tumor cells to IR in a 4T1 breast cancer mouse model. Not only that, this study further showed that D-mannose has a coordinated effect in combination with anti-PD-1 antibody. This suggests that D-mannose could be beneficial in combination therapy. Additionally, some valproic acid analogs can induce the polarization of M1-type macrophages. These compounds also enhance antitumor effects by remodeling the tumor immune microenvironment and can sustainably activate the immune response when combined with radiotherapy in a rat model of primary breast cancer, indicating a promising therapeutic application.

Targeted therapy combined with radiotherapy

Targeting the EGFR pathway

EGFR is a promising therapeutic target for breast cancer.Lapatinib, which targets EGFR, primarily induces radiosensitization by inhibiting the MEK/ERK pathway and arresting the cell cycle Cetuximab (C225) is a monoclonal antibody against the extracellular domain of EGFR that significantly inhibits the activation of the EGFR-MAPK signaling pathway induced by radiation and reduces the phosphorylation of p38-MAPK molecules, increasing apoptosis. In the phase II clinical trial of TNBC, the response rate of cetuximab combined with carboplatin was 16%, the progression-free survival was 2.1 months (95% CI 1.8-5.5 months), and the overall survival was 10.4 months (95% CI 7.7-13.1 months). The results showed that cetuximab blocked the expression of EGFR pathway in some metastatic TNBC patients. This study provides a valuable reference for preliminary exploration, but further clinical trials are still needed to verify its efficacy and safety. The combination of radiotherapy and the mixed EGFR/DNA-targeting molecule ZRBA1 can downregulate the expression of EGFR in TNBC and weaken DNA damage, which can be used as a potential radiosensitizer.

Targeting the VEGF/VEGFR pathway

Inhibition of VEGF expression can reduce local hypoxia and increase radiosensitivity. Regorafenib is a multitarget inhibitor that affects VEGF production when combined with radiation. It inhibits growth factors and receptor tyrosine kinases, inhibits cell migration and angiogenesis, and sensitizes patients to radiotherapy.

Targeting the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway

Metformin is an activator of AMPK that upregulates the expression of thyrotoxin via the AMPK-FOXO 3 pathway to induce ROS production and enhance radiosensitivity. Deguelin enhances radiosensitivity. This mechanism may involve the inhibition of phosphorylated Akt expression and prolonged cell cycle arrest in the G 2-M phase.

PI-103 inhibition of the PI3K signaling pathway may lead to compensatory activation of the ERK pathway, which in turn affects the expression of BRCA1/2 and makes BRCA-deficient breast cancer cells more sensitive to poly(ADP-ribose) polymerase (PARP) inhibitors. Additionally,In addition, in the MDA-MB-435S cell xenograft model in nude mice, it was found that PI-103 combined with olaparib was more effective in preventing radiation-induced cell death than olaparib alone, indicating that targeting the PI3K signaling pathway combined with PARP inhibition can enhance radiation sensitivity.

The Gallo tannin extract MPSE, which is abundant in the seeds of Phragmites australis, can sensitize TNBC cells to radiotherapy by targeting the PI3K/AKT pathway and can weaken the IR-induced EMT process and the stem cell-like phenotype.

Targeting the PARP pathway

Based on the literature, it is found that targeted therapy for PARP pathway has significant potential and application prospects. When breast cancer cells are irradiated with ionizing radiation, olaparib can be a more effective combination treatment because of BRCA1 deficiency, in which PARP is highly activated. Additionally, olaparib can interfere with NHEJ repair and has a positive effect on the treatment of early high-risk TNBC. In a clinical trial of therapeutic interventions for the PARP pathway, Olaparib showed significant efficacy. The study has reached the early reported efficacy boundary in the mid-term analysis. The results showed that the 3-year disease-free survival rate of patients treated with Olaparib was 85.9%, while that of the placebo group was 77.1%, indicating that Olaparib significantly prolonged the disease-free survival of patients by targeting the PARP pathway (hazard ratio 0.58; 99.5% confidence interval 0.41 to 0.82; p < 0.001). The OlympiAIII trial showed that patients with early breast cancer carrying BRCA1 / 2 mutations were treated with Olaparib adjuvant therapy. Compared with placebo, the overall survival risk ratio was 0.68 (98.5% CI 0.47-0.97; P¼ 0.009)The 4-year overall survival rate was increased by 3.4%, and the disease-free survival rate and distant recurrence-free survival rate were significantly improved. This III clinical trial demonstrates the potential advantages of olaparib in the treatment of TNBC by targeting the PARP pathway.Table 2 summarizes the key factors, pathways, and major mechanisms of molecular-targeted therapies related to radiosensitivity.

Single radiotherapy treatment often fails to achieve a satisfactory therapeutic effect; however, combining it with targeted therapy drugs may improve the success rate of treatment. At present, the OlympiA III trial and the cetuximab combined with carboplatin treatment regimen in phase II clinical trials have shown significant advantages in the combination of radiotherapy and targeted therapeutic drugs. In addition, in vivo studies have found that the combination of PI-103 and olaparib also shows broad application prospects.For instance, lapatinib combined with capecitabine may cause diarrhea and rashes, cetuximab may cause skin reactions, and olaparib may cause adverse reactions, such as anemia and nausea.

Through protein–protein interaction analysis, it is possible to identify common proteins within each pathway to block the effects of multiple pathways by inhibiting key node proteins. This approach may offer new insights into the development of chemotherapeutic drugs; however, additional clinical trials are required to determine the optimal treatment plan.

Epigenetic therapy combined with radiotherapy

DNA methylation inhibitors

DNA methylation leads to condensation of heterochromatin conformations that prevent gene transcription. DNMT1 inhibitors such as 5-aza-2′-deoxycytidine (DAC) can induce demethylation and reactivation of silent genes in MDA-MB-231 and MDA-MB-435 cells, reversing the methylation status of genes related to radiosensitization. DAC is a common DNMT inhibitor that is also suitable for the treatment of myelodysplastic syndrome. It strongly induces abnormal mitosis in human myeloid tumors at clinical concentrations, which is crucial for controlling cell proliferation. Consequently, non-proliferative cells are not sensitive to DAC. Clinical trials have shown antitumor activity in patients with leukemia and patients with myelodysplastic syndromes, but its use in TNBC still needs more research to confirm its effectiveness and safety. Tien-Hsien liquid induces G2/M arrest and radiosensitivity in MCF-7 cells by downregulating DNMT1 and Rad51. Yao et al. demonstrated that Tien-Hsien liquid inhibits the growth of human MDA-MB-231 breast cancer xenografts. Thus, Tien-Hsien liquid may benefit patients with TNBC. The use of DNMT inhibitors is an effective strategy for enhancing the sensitivity of tumor cells to radiotherapy; however, individual differences and tumor heterogeneity may affect therapeutic efficacy, and therefore, it is necessary to develop more precise individualized treatment strategies.

Histone modification inhibitors

Acetylation helps form a more open chromatin structure that promotes gene transcription and expression. Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitors may lead to chromosomal instability, cell cycle arrest, and the inhibition of DNA damage repair, which increase cell sensitivity to radiotherapy. Suberoylanilide hydroxamic acid (SAHA) is a common HDAC inhibitor that can inhibit DNA repair proteins, such as Rad51 and DNA-PK, when combined with IR. In addition, DNA damage induces endoplasmic reticulum stress and autophagy. Compared to a single treatment, IR combined with SAHA showed higher therapeutic effects in MCF-7, MDA-MB-231, and 4T1 cells. Tumors were induced by injecting 4T1-luc cells into the mammary fat pads of Balb/c mice, and SAHA was injected intraperitoneally into mice with successful tumor induction and combined with radiation therapy. The combination was found to increase LC3 and γH2AX in mouse tumors and significantly reduce in situ breast cancer tumor volume in mice compared to SAHA or IR treatment alone. SAHA is generally used as a supplementary treatment for TNBC; however, its use alone does not have a significant clinical effect on solid tumors. For example, de Cremoux et al. found that SAHA did not induce the re-expression of estrogen receptor 1 (ESR1) and estrogen receptor 2 (ESR2) genes in vitro despite its cytotoxicity. The cytotoxicity of TMU-35435 combined with radiotherapy in tumor cells was more significant than that of TMU-35435 or IR alone, which enhanced radiosensitivity by inducing misfolded protein aggregation and autophagy in TNBC. 4T1-Luc cells were stably transfected with luciferase and implanted into the mammary fat pad of Balb/c mice to establish a mouse in situ breast cancer model. The therapeutic effect of IR and TMU-35435 alone or in combination on the growth of 4T1 cells implanted in situ in mice was also evaluated. In vivo imaging system (IVIS) imaging was analyzed after weekly cell implantation. Bioluminescence imaging showed a gradual increase in tumor volume in the control group. Tumor volume was lower in the combination treatment group than in the control group (p < 0.01). Tumor weight was significantly lower in the combination treatment group compared to the control group (p < 0.01), and the authors found significantly higher expression of LC3, enhanced phosphorylation of eIF2α expression, and a significant reduction of the proliferation marker Ki-67 in the tumor tissues of the TMU-35435, IR, and combination groups, as well as a significant reduction of the proliferation marker Ki-67 in the tumor tissues of the combination group. These results in animal models suggest that the combination of TMU-35435 and IR inhibits tumor growth by inducing ER stress and autophagy in an in situ breast cancer mouse model. When combined with radiotherapy, the novel HDAC inhibitor YCW1 inhibits BNIP3 in TNBC, leading to a significant increase in autophagy and cytotoxicity. The authors injected 4T1-Luc cells into the mammary ducts of the 4th mammary fat pad of female Balb/c mice to induce tumors, and showed that tumor volume in the control group increased progressively in a time-dependent manner (p<0.05) and that tumor tissue from mice treated with YCW1 and IR showed lower expression of Bcl-2/adenovirus E1B 19-kDa protein interaction protein 3 (BNIP3) and higher LC3 expression and smaller tumor volume compared with the control group and treatment group alone. The combination of YCW1 and IR was shown to significantly inhibit BNIP3 and induce autophagic cell death in an in situ breast cancer model. Sodium butyrate (SB) can inhibit DSB repair alone and when combined with 5-aza-DC, SB inactivates DNA methylation and histone acetylation, producing a synergistic effect that enhances the radiosensitivity of breast cancer cells. Compared to a single treatment, IR combined with SB showed stronger efficacy, significantly enhancing cell death and growth arrest in the HCT116 human colorectal tumor cell line. Additionally, the HADC inhibitor valproic acid can induce or enhance IR-induced DNA DSBs to respond to DNA damage while inhibiting DNA repair ability at a safe dose, showing a radiosensitizing effect on breast cancer cells. Another study showed that after 48 h of treatment with valproic acid alone, MDA-MB-231 cells exhibited little or no change. In summary, HDAC inhibitors have the potential to enhance the therapeutic effect of radiotherapy and improve the sensitivity of tumor cells to radiotherapy by affecting chromatin structure and DNA repair. The development of novel HDAC inhibitors and adjuvants provides a new strategy for cancer treatment, they inhibit breast cancer in vivo and in vitro in combination with radiation therapy. However, their safety and efficacy in clinical applications still need to be further verified. Conclusions and projected outcomes

In conclusion, this review attempts to analyze the molecular mechanism of radioresistance of TNBC in some patients from different perspectives, and actively summarize the alternative strategies for the treatment of TNBC to improve the prognosis of patients.

Future developments in molecularly targeted therapies combined with radiotherapy may involve multi-target approaches such as inhibitors to address the limitations of traditional single-treatment methods. Additionally, in-depth studies of the specificity of signal transduction pathways and their links to specific cellular activities could lead to the design and synthesis of drugs with specific inhibitory activities. In this way, individualized treatment plans could be tailored to a patient's genomic, proteomic, and epigenetic information for more precise treatment. To explore the relationship between the genes mentioned in this review and ER, PR, and HER2 receptors, we performed PPI analysis. The results showed that most but not all the genes were related to these three receptors. This suggests that we should continue to explore the mechanisms of the pathway connections and interactions between them and identify new targets for TNBC treatment. For example, the important node protein AKT1, which is highly expressed in breast cancer, is related to the proliferation and migration of tumor cells and regulates the concentration of ROS, suggesting that inhibiting the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway may have a therapeutic effect against TNBC. ZEB1 is another important node protein identified in the analysis that is expressed at low levels in breast cancer and is associated with poor prognosis. Further exploration of its direct relationship with the three receptors and attempts to promote their expression by increasing the expression of ZEB1 are needed.

In-depth studies on the mechanism of radioresistance will provide new drugs and targets for future clinical treatments and reduce the negative impact of radioresistance on therapeutic effects. With progress in science, technology, and clinical research, we believe that more breakthroughs will be made in the field of combination therapy, bringing more hope and opportunities to patients with cancer.
