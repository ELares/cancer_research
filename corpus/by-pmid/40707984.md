# Hypoxia-augmented chemotherapy potentiates imaging-guided combinatorial radionuclide-sonodynamic therapy for pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

Radionuclide therapy and chemotherapy are effective for pancreatic cancer, yet their efficacy is often limited by tumor hypoxia. In this study, manganese porphyrin (MnTTP) and tirapazamine (TPZ) were encapsulated in polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) spheres, which were subsequently coated with polydopamine to label the radionuclide 131I, forming a theranostic nanoplatform. The nanoplatform demonstrated excellent biocompatibility, stable labeling efficiency, and dual-modal MRI/SPECT imaging capabilities. The nanoplatform generated reactive oxygen species (ROS) under ultrasound(US) activation, in combination with the β-rays emitted by 131I, synergistically eradicate tumor cells and exacerbate hypoxia in the tumor microenvironment. Furthermore, TPZ was activated to produce toxic free radicals under hypoxic conditions, enabling a synergistic therapeutic approach that combined radionuclide therapy and sonodynamic therapy. This approach effectively inhibited tumor stem cell formation and enhanced anti-tumor efficacy. Additionally, the nanoplatform's metabolism in vivo and the therapeutic effect were monitored in real-time under MRI/SPECT dual-modality imaging. This therapeutic strategy offers a promising solution for overcoming tumor hypoxia and achieving efficient combination therapy for tumors.

Pancreatic cancer is one of the most lethal solid tumors, with a five-year survival rate of only 11%. It is typically diagnosed at an advanced stage, making it challenging to achieve a cure. While radiotherapy and chemotherapy are the primary treatment options, their outcomes are often unsatisfactory. Pancreatic cancer exhibits resistance to most therapeutic approaches due to its dense stromal tissue and hypoxic microenvironment. Research indicates that hypoxia in pancreatic tumors promotes and sustains the cancer stem cell (CSC) phenotype, leading to chemotherapy resistance, tumor recurrence, metastasis, and poor treatment outcomes. Therefore, the development of therapeutic modalities capable of targeting tumor hypoxia and effectively removing tumor stem cells would be effective in improving the efficacy of anticancer treatments.

Radionuclide therapy, a form of internal radiation treatment, offers precise targeting and minimal damage to surrounding healthy tissues. This approach induces cancer cell apoptosis by emitting α or β rays and generating reactive oxygen species (ROS) that cleave single-strand DNA. Clinically, radionuclides are typically delivered minimally invasively to tumor sites, either via catheter infusion or intravenous injection with targeting molecules. However, clinical studies have shown that radionuclide therapy alone is insufficient to eradicate all the cancer cells, often necessitating combination with treatment modalities to achieve complementary benefits. Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is an emerging non-invasive cancer treatment that uses ultrasound to activate sonosensitizers, inducing ROS production to eradicate tumors. It is widely accepted that the main generation mechanisms of ROS in SDT are sonoluminescence or pyrolysis process. The sonosensitizers exposed to US experience periodic pressure oscillations, resulting in the formation of bubbles in the liquid. The implosion of cavitation bubbles generates photons with high energy, and the sonosensitizer molecules under sonoluminescence are activated by this energy from the ground state to the excited state. The second suggested mechanism is pyrolysis. In this case, the elevated temperature and pressure during the collapsing of bubbles could generate free radicals by either directly breaking apart the sonosensitizers or reacting sonosensitizers with H+ or OH− that would be produced by the thermolysis of water. SDT is advantageous due to its low invasiveness, deep penetration, and cost-effectiveness. However, its efficacy as a standalone therapy is limited by the relatively low energy efficiency of sound waves. Previous studies by our group have confirmed that combination of radionuclide therapy with SDT produces a significant synergistic effect, enhancing tumor treatment outcomes. 

Both radionuclide therapy and sonodynamic therapy rely on oxygen for their therapeutic efficacy. However, hypoxia, a hallmark of solid tumors resulting from irregular tumor cell proliferation and abnormal vasculature, significantly limits their effectiveness of these treatments. For instance, tumor hypoxia reduces the effectiveness of radiation therapy, often requiring frequent high-dose applications that can increase side effects. Additionally, both radionuclide and sonodynamic therapies can exacerbate tumor hypoxia, further diminishing treatment outcomes. Hypoxia-activated prodrugs (HAPs) offer a promising solution, as they are bioreductively activated under low-oxygen conditions, selectively converting into highly cytotoxic agents to target hypoxic cells in solid tumors. TPZ is a classic HAP with selective cytotoxicity towards hypoxic tumor cells while exhibiting lower toxicity to normal tissues. Under hypoxic conditions, TPZ undergoes intracellular metabolism to produce benzotriazine (BTZ) radicals and •OH, causing DNA strand breaks and cytotoxic effects. Thus, strategies that selectively increase tumor hypoxia levels can effectively activate bioreductive drugs, addressing the limitations of conventional cancer therapies and enhancing treatment outcomes.

In this study, polylactic-co-glycolic acid (PLGA) was employed as a nanocarrier to encapsulate the hypoxia-activated prodrug TPZ and the sonosensitizer manganese porphyrin (MnTTP). The outer layer was coated with polydopamine to provide a labelling site for 131I, resulting in a radionuclide-based diagnostic and therapeutic integrated nanoplatform (Scheme 1). PLGA, approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as a drug delivery, offers excellent biocompatibility and biodegradability, making it an ideal polymer for this application. In vitro and in vivo experiments demonstrated that combining sonodynamic therapy with radionuclide therapy intensifies tumor microenvironment hypoxia, effectively activating the bioreductive drug TPZ. This activation enhances tumor cell cytotoxicity and inhibits tumor stem cell formation. Furthermore, due to the characteristics of Mn2+ and 131I, the nanoplatform’s tumor accumulation and systemic metabolism were visualized through MR-T1 weighted imaging and SPECT at various time points. The diagnostic and therapeutic integrated nanoplatform developed in this study achieves a synergistic therapeutic approach by combining radionuclide therapy, sonodynamic therapy, and hypoxia-activated chemotherapy. This strategy provides a new therapeutic strategy for addressing hypoxic tumors, such as pancreatic cancer.

Experimental section

Materials

PLGA-PEG and mes-Tetratolylporphyrin-Mn(III) chloride manganese porphyrin (MnTTP) were purchased from Shaanxi Rui Xi Biological Co., Ltd.; Tirapazamine (TPZ), dopamine hydrochloride and polyvinyl alcohol (20000–40000) were purchased from Shanghai Aladdin Biochemical Technology Co., Ltd.; Dichloromethane was purchased from Shanghai Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd.; Na131I was provided by the Department of Nuclear Medicine, The First Affiliated Hospital of Shanxi Medical University. All the reagents used in the experiment were of analytical grade and used directly without treatment. BxPC-3 pancreatic cancer cells were purchased from Procell Life Science&Technology Co., Ltd.; 1640 medium, fetal bovine serum, penicillin-streptomycin solution and trypsin were purchased from Gibco Company; 4-weeks-old male Balb/C nude mice were purchased from SPF (Beijing) Biotechnology Co., Ltd.

Characterizations

The morphology of TMP@D was observed by HT7700 transmission electron microscope (Hitachi, Japan); Particle size and Zeta potential of TMP@D were measured by Zetasizer Labblue (Malvern, UK); The absorption spectrum was performed by UH 5700 UV-Vis Spectrophotometer (Hitachi, Japan); Sonodynamic therapy was performed by WED-100 full digital ultrasound therapy apparatus (Well.d MEDICAL Electronics, Shenzhen) as the ultrasound trigger source; Fluorescence spectrum changes were measured by an FS5 fluorescence spectrum analyzer (Edinburgh, UK).

Synthesis of TMP@D

MnTTP (0.5 mg), TPZ (1 mg), and PLGA-mPEG (20 mg) were co-dissolved in 1 mL of dichloromethane, sonicated for 5 min. This solution was then added drop-wise to 5 mL of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA, MW 20,000–40,000) under continuous stirring at room temperature and reacted for 12 h to obtain TMP solution. After 12 h, 5 mL of Tris base solution (10 mg·mL− 1) was added to adjust the reaction system to a mildly basic environment. Subsequently, 8 mg of dopamine was added, and the mixture was ultrasonicated for 2 min. The reaction system was then transferred to a magnetic stirrer and continuously stirred for an additional 12 h at room temperature. The resulting solution was then centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 10 min using a Millipore ultrafiltration tube (10 kDa), and centrifuged repeatedly until the supernatant became clear to obtain TMP@D solution. The final solution (TMP@D) was freeze-dried in a freeze-dryer for 48 h. The resulting product was stored at 4 ºC in a refrigerator.

Determination of encapsulation rate and drug loading rate

To evaluate the encapsulation efficiency and drug loading of TPZ, nanoplatforms were prepared using various amounts of TPZ (0.5, 1, 2, 3, and 4 mg) mixed with PLGA at different mass ratios (20:w). The supernatant was collected by ultrafiltration centrifugation, and the unencapsulated TPZ in the supernatant was determined by UV spectrophotometer at 474 nm according to the standard curve of TPZ. The encapsulation rate and drug loading rate were calculated according to the formula. Encapsulation rate = (input TPZ amount- supernatant TPZ amount)/input TPZ amount*100%; Drug loading rate = (input TPZ amount-supernatant TPZ amount)/(input TPZ + TMP@D amount)*100%.

The stability of TMP@D

To assess the stability of TMP@D, 1 mL of TMP@D solution at the same concentration was mixed with to 2 mL of pure water, PBS, DMEM, and DMEM + 10% FBS, respectively. The particle size and polydispersity index (PDI) of each mixture were measured using a particle size analyzer every two days over a 14-d period. Each condition was tested in triplicate to ensure accuracy and reproducibility.

In vitro drug release

TMP@D was incubated with PBS at pH 6.5 and pH 7.4. The release of TPZ under conditions with or without ultrasound irradiation was compared. At designated time points, samples were subjected to ultrafiltration centrifugation. The supernatant was collected and analyzed by UV-Vis spectrophotometry. The concentration of released TPZ was calculated based on its standard curve.

Protein corona detection

After incubating TMP@D with plasma at 37 °C for 2 h, the samples were washed with ultrapure water and centrifuged three times. Changes in hydrodynamic diameter and zeta potential were measured. Simultaneously, 10% SDS solution was added to lyse the adsorbed proteins, and was quantified using the BCA assay.

Ability of nanoplatform generate singlet oxygen in vitro

Detection of singlet oxygen (¹O₂) was performed using SOSG (Singlet Oxygen Sensor Green Fluorescent Probe), a fluorescent probe exhibiting high reactivity with this reactive oxygen species. A 10 µM SOSG working solution was prepared, and 20 µL of this solution was mixed with 1 mL of different concentrations of nanoplatforms (0, 50, 100, 200, and 400 µg·mL− 1). After thorough mixing, the samples were subjected to sonication at 1.5 W·cm− 2 power for 1 min, PBS was used as a control. The emission wavelength at 504 nm was recorded after sonication at different concentrations. Next, the emission wavelength of the 100 µg·mL− 1 nanoplatform was recorded after sonication for different sonication times (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 times).

MRI capabilities of TMP@D

TMP@D was prepared in different concentrations using purified water. T1-weighted and T1 Mapping images were acquired using a Bruker 7T MRI. The T1 relaxation times of the nanoplatform were calculated by detecting the mean value in the ROI of each group of Mapping images, and the relaxation rate was calculated by plotting a standard curve with the concentration on the horizontal axis and the r value (1/T1) on the vertical axis.

Synthesis and radiochemical purity of 131I-TMP@D

Na131I (2 mCi), TMP@D (2 mg·mL− 1), and chloramine T solution (10 mg·mL− 1, 400 µL) were added in a round-bottom flask. After stirring for 15 min at room temperature, the resulting solution was centrifuged in a Millipore ultrafiltration tube (10 kDa) at 5000 rpm for 10 min. The supernatant was repeatedly washed with pure water until the filtrate was inactive to obtain 131I-TMP@D. The resulting 131I-TMP@D was collected, and the radiopurification of 131I-TMP@D was assessed by silica gel thin-layer chromatography plate and FC-3600 Flow-Count system (USA, Bioscan), with acetone as developing agent.

Radiostability of 131I-TMP@D

1 mL of 131I-TMP@D were placed in 2 mL of PBS and 2 mL of 1640 medium containing 10% fetal bovine serum, respectively. Ultrafiltration centrifugation (5000 rmp, 10 min) was performed at different time points (0.5, 1, 2, 4, 6, 24, 48, 96, and 168 h). Pre-centrifugation nanoplatform activity was recorded as CPM0 and post-centrifugation cut-off precipitate activity was recorded as CPM1. The measurement was repeated three times. The radiostability was calculated using the following formula: radionuclide stabilization efficiency = CPM1/CPM0*100%.

In vitro cytotoxicity assay of TMP@D

The CCK-8 assay was able to detect tumor cell viability after nanoplatform incubation. BxPC-3 cells were cultured in 96-well plates at a density of 5 × 103 for 24 h. Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization

In this study, PLGA nanoparticles loaded with TPZ and MnTTP (TMP) were prepared using the emulsion-solvent evaporation method, followed by a polydopamine coating to produce non-radioactively labeled nanoplatforms (TMP@D). After encapsulation by polydopamine, the average particle size of TMP increased from 51.96 ± 5.52 nm to 101.6 ± 9.85 nm (Fig. 1A). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images revealed that compared with TMP, both TMP@D and ¹³¹I-TMP@D exhibited consistent morphologies (Fig. 1B, S1), featuring a more regular shape and well-dispersed. These observations were consistent with the dynamic light scattering (DLS) measurements. The UV-Vis absorption spectrum showed that TMP@D exhibited an absorption peak at 274 nm, which corresponds to the characteristic absorption of TPZ, confirming the successful loading of TPZ (Fig. 1C). We futher examined of the Raman spectra of both TPZ and TMP@D. The results revealed a high degree of consistency between the Raman spectra of these two compounds (Figure S2), suggesting that TPZ had been successfully loaded onto TMP@D while preserving the molecular structure of TPZ intact. As both MnTTP and TPZ share the same UV-visible characteristic absorption peak at 474 nm, the fluorescence emission spectroscopy was used to further confirm the loading of MnTTP. The emission spectra of TMP@D and MnTTP exhibited similar trends, with emission wavelengths ranging from 470 to 550 nm under excitation at 360 nm (Figure S3), confirming the successful loading of MnTTP. The Zeta potentials of PLGA nanoparticles loaded with TPZ (TP), MnTTP (MP) and both TPZ and MnTTP (TMP) were all positive. However, the surface potential of the TMP shifted from a positive potential of 28.85 ± 1.58 mV to a negative potential of -26.46 ± 1.93 mV after encapsulated with polydopamine (Fig. 1D). This is because when the pH of the solution is higher than the isoelectric point of 4, polydopamine acquires negative charges due to the deprotonation of its phenolic groups. The findings further indicated the successful encapsulation of TMP with the polydopamine layer. 

The standard curve for TPZ was generated using the UV-visible absorption spectra (Figure S4) by measuring the absorbance at 274 nm for various TPZ concentrations. The nanoplatforms were prepared by combining PLGA with TPZ at various mass ratios (20:0.5, 20:1, 20:2, 20:3 and 20:4). The encapsulation and drug loading ratios were then calculated. At a mass ratio of 20:2, the results showed an encapsulation rate of 42.27 ± 1.96% (Figure S5A) and a drug loading rate of 4.29 ± 0.11% (Figure S3B). As the mass of TPZ increased, the encapsulation rate slowly increased. To minimize drug wastage, a mass ratio of 20:2 was selected for subsequent experiments.

Colloidal stability was crucial for in vivo drug delivery using nanoplatforms. DLS results demonstrated that the particle size distribution of the nanoplatforms remained approximately consistent across different solutions (Figure S6A). After a 14-day observation period, TMP@D showed no aggregation in various solutions (Figure S6B). The polydispersity index (PDI) values revealed that, in the presence of 10% FBS, the PDI of the nanoparticles was slightly higher compared to the other three groups (water PDI = 0.32 ± 0.01, PBS PDI = 0.23 ± 0.01, DMEM PDI = 0.31 ± 0.004, DMEM + 10% FBS PDI = 0.47 ± 0.12), but the particles remained stably dispersed over the 14-day period (Figure S6C). We evaluated the protein corona formation of TMP@D after incubation in plasma. The formation of the protein corona was characterized using dynamic light scattering (DLS). The DLS results indicated an increase in the size and Zeta potential of TMP@D after 2 h incubation with plasma. This might be due to the fact that TMP@D had adsorbed certain positively charged proteins. This suggests that TMP@D could form a protein corona in plasma (Figure S7).

Ultrasound (US) activation of sonosensitizers produces singlet oxygen, a key cytotoxic agent responsible for the effects of sonodynamic therapy (SDT). To assess the ability of TMP@D to produce singlet oxygen upon US activation, we used a singlet oxygen sensor green fluorescent probe (SOSG) as a detection reagent. The results showed that, in the experimental group, under the same ultrasound power (1.5 W·cm− 2), the fluorescence signal significantly increased with higher concentrations of TMP@D (Fig. 1E). Additionally, the 100 µg·mL− 1 TMP@D solution, subjected to multiple rounds of ultrasound treatments (1.5 W·cm− 2), exhibited a gradual increase in fluorescence intensity with the number of ultrasound applications (Figs. 1F). These findings demonstrated that the amount of singlet oxygen generated by TMP@D under ultrasound stimulation was directly related to both TMP@D concentration and the number of ultrasound applications, indicating that TMP@D had a strong ability to generate singlet oxygen under ultrasound stimulation.

The Mn2+ ions in the manganese porphyrin ring had a shorter relaxation time and a higher rate of relaxation, significantly enhancing the quality of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). To evaluate the contrast-enhancing capability of TMP@D, a high magnetic field of 7.0 T was used. The longitudinal relaxation rate (r1) was calculated by fitting a straight line to the relationship between the inverse of relaxation time (1/T1) and the concentration of TMP@D on T1 mapping sequence images. The results showed that T1-weighted signal intensity increases with higher TMP@D concentrations, consistent with T1 mapping results. TMP@D showed an applicability for T1-MR imaging, with a relaxivity r1 of 6.53 mM− 1s− 1, more than that of commercially available Gd-DTPA (4.49 mM− 1s− 1) (Fig. 1G). These findings indicated that TMP@D exhibited strong magnetic resonance responsiveness, making it an effective MRI contrast agent.

The release kinetics of TPZ from TMP@D at different pH (7.4 and 6.5) and with US were also evaluated. It was observed that the cumulative release of TPZ at pH 6.5 exceeded that at pH 7.4 (Figure S8). This phenomenon was attributed to the gradual degradation of the polydopamine coating on the surface of TMP@D under acidic conditions, which accelerates the drug release process. These findings suggested that the acidic tumor microenvironment facilitates the release of TPZ. Furthermore, under identical pH conditions, ultrasonic stimulation enhances molecular mobility, thereby promoting an increased drug release rate from TMP@D.

In this study, the radionuclide 131I was used as an important part of the imaging-guided co-treatment, with radionuclide labeling performed by the chloramine T method. Thin layer chromatography (TLC) was employed to measure and compare the radiochemical purity of 131I and 131I-TMP@D. The results showed that, using acetone as a developing agent, the radiochemical purity of free 131I was 98.58% and 131I-TMP@D was 90.58% (Fig. 1H). The results indicated that 131I is efficiently labeled onto TMP@D nanoparticles via the chloramine-T method, with a labeling efficiency of 73.99 ± 1.51%. Then we assessed the labeling stability of 131I-TMP@D. The results showed that 131I-TMP@D maintained high radio-stability when stored in PBS and RPMI-1640 medium for 7 d, with radioactivity retention of 72 ± 2.02% and 66.53 ± 2.51%, respectively (Figure S9).

Haemolysis testing was a standard method for determining the haemocompatibility of biomaterials. When different concentrations of TMP@D solution were incubated with equal amounts of erythrocytes, the results of the haemolysis experiments showed that when the nanoplatform concentration was increased from 100 µg·mL− 1 to 1000 µg·mL− 1, the rate of haemolysis increased from 0.42 ± 0.24% to 3.57 ± 0.08% for 4 h of incubation. After 12 h, the hemolysis rate increased from 2.98 ± 0.05% to 4.06 ± 0.17% (Fig. 1I). The haemolysis rate of the nanoplatform remained below the 5% threshold set for nanomaterials, confirming the good biocompatibility of TMP@D.

Notably, the experimental results also demonstrated that in cells incubated with 131I-TMP@D, the overlap between the red fluorescence (Cy5) and the green fluorescence (Lyso-Tracker Green) increased over time, with the color tending towards orange-red. Meanwhile, the two fluorescent signals exhibited a high the Pearson’s correlation coefficient (Rr) (Fig. 2D). The internalization results were consistent across the three different cell types (Figure S10), indicating a uniform uptake pattern of the probe within the cells. Flow cytometry analysis corroborated these findings, showing a marked increase in fluorescence intensity after 3 and 6 h of co-incubation with the nanoplatform (Figure S11). Additionally, using a gamma counter, we evaluated the uptake of 131I-TMP@D in BxPC-3 cells. The results showed that the cellular uptake of ¹³¹I-TMP@D was consistently higher than that of free ¹³¹I. Moreover, there was no notable difference in the cellular uptake of ¹³¹I-TMP@D at 1, 3, and 6 h, and it remained elevated over an extended duration. (Figure S11B). This enhanced uptake and prolonged retention are attributed to the unique nanocarrier properties of TMP@D, which prevent the rapid excretion of the radionuclide by the cells, whereas small molecules of 131I were readily excreted by cells. To further investigate probe uptake, we used 3D tumor spheroids to mimic in vivo solid tumor tissue. In vitro combination therapy of 131I-TMP@D

The cytotoxicity of TMP@D was evaluated using the CCK-8 assay under different oxygen conditions. Under normoxic conditions, cell viability remained high at 85.35 ± 3.54% when exposed to a probe concentration of 1000 µg·mL− 1. In contrast, under hypoxic conditions, cell viability was reduced to 82.29 ± 2.85% at a concentration of 400 µg·mL− 1. However, when the probe concentration was increased to 1000 µg·mL− 1 under hypoxic conditions, cell viability significantly decreased to 56.44 ± 5.87%, indicating a notable cytotoxic effect (Figure S12). These results indicated that the TMP@D nanoplatform exhibits minimal cytotoxicity at lower concentrations, with substantial toxicity occurring only at higher concentrations under hypoxic conditions, thus demonstrating its potential advantage in targeting hypoxic tumors.

Subsequently, the synergistic therapeutic effect of 131I-TMP@D (200 µg·mL− 1) was examined on tumor cells using the CCK-8 assay (Fig. 3A). Results showed no significant statistical difference in cell viability between hypoxic and normoxic conditions for the free 131I and TMP@D groups, indicating that neither free 131I nor conventional hypoxia effectively activates TPZ against tumor cells. Nevertheless, when TMP@D was combined with ultrasound (US) stimulation, cell viability under hypoxia (42.63 ± 6.06%) was significantly reduced compared to normoxia conditions.

(56.94 ± 4.28%). This reduction was attributed to US-triggered consumption of environmental oxygen by the sonosensitizer, thereby activating TPZ and enhancing tumor cell cytotoxicity. The 131I-TMP@D + US group exhibited considerably lower cell viability in both normoxic and hypoxic conditions compared to other groups, with the lowest viability under hypoxia (22.36 ± 6.63%). This significant reduction was due to the combination of radionuclide therapy and sonodynamic therapy (SDT), which depleted environmental oxygen, generated more ROS, and improved the nanoplatform’s therapeutic efficacy against tumors. Simultaneously, TPZ was activated in this process, effectively addressing the hypoxic tumor environment and enhancing tumor cell elimination. Moreover, we employed Calcein-AM (green fluorescence) and propidium iodide (PI, red fluorescence) to differentiate live and dead cells respectively. Results showed minimal red fluorescence and predominant green fluorescence in the free 131I and TMP@D groups under both normoxic and hypoxic conditions, similar to the control group. This was attributed to the minimal retention of free 131I within cells and the low cytotoxicity of non-activated TPZ. In identical oxygen conditions, the TMP@D + US group exhibited significantly more red fluorescence than the 131I-TMP@D group, suggesting that US-triggered ROS production and activation of TPZ resulted in greater cytotoxicity compared to radionuclide therapy alone. The 131I-TMP@D + US group showed obviously higher red fluorescence under hypoxic conditions than normoxic conditions (Fig. 3B, S13), indicating that the combined effects of radionuclide therapy and sonodynamic therapy more effectively activated the bioreductive prodrug, enhancing cytotoxicity against tumor cells. This finding aligned with the CCK-8 assay findings.

Post-treatment apoptosis was further evaluated using flow cytometry with an Annexin V-FITC/PI kit (Fig. 3C). There were no significant differences in apoptosis levels between the free 131I group and the TMP@D group under both normoxic and hypoxic conditions, and these levels were lower than those in the 131I-TMP@D group. This is attributed to the fact that the small molecule free 131I is easily expelled from cells, resulting in relatively low cytotoxicity. Additionally, under normoxic conditions, TPZ exhibits low cytotoxicity, and conventional hypoxia has limited activation effects on TPZ. In contrast, in the 131I-TMP@D group, the prolonged retention of the probe within cells increased radiation-induced damage to the cells. Notably, the 131I-TMP@D + US group had the highest apoptosis under hypoxia. Mitochondrial membrane potential was further evaluated. Orange fluorescence indicates high mitochondrial membrane potential, where JC-1 aggregates to form polymers, suggesting healthy mitochondria without causing apoptosis. Green fluorescence indicates low mitochondrial membrane potential, where JC-1 exists as monomers, indicating mitochondrial depolarization. The results of the mitochondrial JC-1 staining experiment showed that the orange fluorescence was the weakest and the green fluorescence was the strongest in the 131I-TMP@D + US group, indicating a decrease in mitochondrial membrane potential (Figure S14). Moreover, the mitochondrial membrane potential was lower in the combined ultrasound group compared to the non-combined ultrasound group. This was consistent with the aforementioned cell experiment results, suggesting that mitochondrial depolarization and subsequent release of apoptotic factors induce cell apoptosis. Then, we assessed DNA damage in tumor cells using a γ-H2AX assay. The control group manifested no green fluorescence, and minimal fluorescence was observed in the 131I and TMP@D groups. The combination therapy groups, showed marked increases in green fluorescence, with the 131I-TMP@D + US group under hypoxia exhibiting the most intense and significant green fluorescence, indicating substantial DNA damage (Fig. 3D, E). Concurrently, BxPC-3 colony formation assays revealed fewer colonies under hypoxic conditions compared to normoxia, with the fewest colonies in the 131I-TMP@D + US group under hypoxia, in line with the apoptosis and DNA damage findings (Figure S15). These results collectively confirmed that the synergistic treatment involving radionuclide therapy, sonodynamic therapy, and bioreductive prodrugs was highly effective for treating hypoxic tumors.

In vivo safety evaluation

We conducted a biocompatibility assessment to evaluate the feasibility of using 131I-TMP@D in vivo. Mice were randomly divided into control and experimental groups (n = 3). The control group received tail vein injections of saline, while the experimental group received equivalent volumes of 131I-TMP@D (400 µCi, 20 mg·kg− 1). The experimental group was injected every other day for a total of three times. After 30 d, blood was collected from the mice for biochemical analysis, and organ indices were measured. Additionally, heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, small intestine, thyroid, and muscle tissues were collected for H&E staining and histological examination. Blood biochemical analysis showed no statistical differences in white blood cell count (WBC), red blood cell count (RBC), hemoglobin (HGB), and platelet count (PLT), confirming excellent hemocompatibility of 131I-TMP@D. Liver function, as assessed by the levels of aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine aminotransferase (ALT), showed no abnormality. Both AST and ALT levels remained within normal ranges and were comparable to the control group, with no statistical differences observed (Fig. 4A). Blood urea nitrogen (UREA) and creatinine (CREA) levels, which reflect glomerular filtration efficiency. Post-injection, both UREA and CREA levels remained normal and similar to the control group, with no statistical differences. This serum biochemical analysis confirmed no significant hepatorenal toxicity associated with 131I-TMP@D. For organ index assessment and H&E staining of vital organs showed no detectable abnormalities in the experimental group after 30 d (Fig. 4B, S16). The findings demonstrated that 131I-TMP@D exhibited excellent biocompatibility, supporting its potential for in vivo pharmacodynamic studies.

In vivo imaging and biodistribution

To evaluate the in vivo imaging capability of TMP@D, we injected the probe into mouse tumors, leveraging the significant enhancement of MRI signals by manganese porphyrin in TMP@D. MR T1-weight images displayed high signals at the tumor periphery after injection 1 h, with the core of the tumor showing uneven and weak enhancement. After 3 h post-injection, the enhanced regions within the tumor increased notably, suggesting the probe’s diffusion into the tumor. At 6 h, an enhancement of signal was observed in more heterogeneous regions within the tumor. After 12 h, both the area of enhanced tumor tissue and the signal intensity in the inhomogeneously enhanced regions continued to increase. By 24 h, the entire tumor tissue exhibited complete enhancement, appearing as bright high signals on T1-weight images (Fig. 5A). These MRI results confirmed that TMP@D remained in the tumor tissue for at least 24 h.

Given that 131I emits both β and γ rays, it also has SPECT imaging capabilities. We compared the vivo distribution of intratumorally injected free 131I and 131I-TMP@D using SPECT imaging. The results showed significant radionuclide accumulation in the tumor region following injection of free 131I. Nonetheless, 0.5 h later, notable accumulation was observed in the thyroid and liver, attributed to free 131I entering the bloodstream and being taken up by these organs. The radionuclide concentration in the tumor area significantly decreased after 1 h, with no accumulation observed after 3 h. Thyroid uptake continued to increase over time, with significant radionuclide accumulation still visible at 24 h. In contrast, with 131I-TMP@D, most radionuclides concentrated at the tumor site 0.5 h post-intratumoral injection, and minimal accumulation in the thyroid. Although a small amount appeared in the thyroid, the majority remained at the tumor site after 1 h. Over time, the radionuclide concentration at the tumor site gradually decreased, but was still evident at 24 h. After 3 h, nuclide accumulation in the thyroid gradually increased, likely due to the partial decomposition of 131I-TMP@D, releasing 131I into the bloodstream. Importantly, in the 131I-TMP@D group SPECT imaging, no obvious nuclide accumulation was observed outside the tumor and thyroid (Fig. 5B). This.

Gamma counters were employed to assess the circulation and distribution of 131I-TMP@D in the body. Major organs and tumors were collected from mice at various time points post tail vein injection, with radioactivity measured using gamma counters. Results indicated that tumor radioactivity peaked at 3 h, after which the signal at the tumor site gradually diminished, demonstrating effective enrichment of 131I-TMP@D at the tumor site. In vivo evaluation of combined therapy

Given that intravenous administration of radioactive isotopes may result in non-specific biodistribution within healthy organs, thereby inducing severe toxic effects on patients. To avoid this systemic toxicity, the direct local implantation of radioactive materials (such as 90Y, 125I or 131I-labeled microbeads) into the tumor for brachytherapy has become a common clinical treatment. The intratumoral injection can directly deliver radiopharmaceuticals to the tumor site, resulting in a high concentration accumulation within the local tumor area and significantly prolonged retention time, which can substantially reduce radiation exposure to normal tissues. Moreover, it avoids the dilution and metabolic loss of the drug during systemic circulation following intravenous administration. We evaluated the in vivo therapeutic effects of 131I-TMP@D after intratumoral injection in tumor-bearing mice. BxPC-3 tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into six groups and subjected to different treatments (n = 3): PBS, 131I, TMP@D, 131I-TMP@D, TMP@D + US, 131I-TMP@D + US (Fig. 6A). The changes in mouse weight and tumor volume were continuously observed during the 30-day experimental period. The results demonstrated that there was no significant difference in the trend of weight change between the groups (Figure S17), indicating that the nanoplatform treatment did not cause obvious adverse effects to mice. Tumor volume monitoring (Fig. 6B) and corresponding images (Fig. 6C) revealed that the tumor growth in the 131I group was slightly slower than in the PBS group, but growth remained relatively rapid. In the TMP@D group, tumor growth was significantly inhibited, likely due to the activation of TPZ in the hypoxic tumor microenvironment, enhancing its cytotoxic effects on tumor cells. The tumor growth of the 131I-TMP@D group and the TMP@D + US group was remarkably inhibited, with the TMP@D + US group showing significantly better results than 131I-TMP@D group. This result was likely due to the inherent radiation resistance of hypoxic tumors in the 131I-TMP@D group, while in the TMP@D + US group, the ultrasound-mediated sonodynamic activation of the sonosensitizer generated ROS, along with the ultrasound energy itself. These combined physical and chemical actions synergistically enhanced TPZ activation efficiency compared to isolated radiation exposure, thus improving the therapeutic outcome. In the 131I-TMP@D + US group, one tumor was completely eradicated, achieving a tumor growth inhibition rate of 97.2 ± 2.8% (Fig. 6D), a highly considerable difference compared to other groups. This was attributed to the combined effect of radionuclide therapy and sonodynamic therapy, which enhanced the activation of bioreductive prodrugs and increased tumor cytotoxicity. Given that cancer stem cells (CSCs) play a critical role in sustaining tumor growth through self-renewal, we assessed CSCs survival in tumor-bearing mice after different treatments. CD133 (prominin-1) is one of the most characteristic biomarkers for CSCs. The inhibition effect of solid tumor CSCs by the nanoplatforms was evaluated by measuring the CD133 protein levels in tumor cells. The results from the CD133 protein Western blot analysis of CD133 expression indicated that its levels were significantly lower in the 131I-TMP@D + US group compared to the control and other experimental groups (Fig. 6E, S18). This suggested that the synergistic treatment strategy with nanoplatforms effectively diminishes CSCs populations, thereby suppressing tumor growth, in alignment with the in vivo combination therapy findings. Consequently, the integration of radionuclide therapy, sonodynamic therapy, and bioreductive prodrugs effectively targets cancer stem cells and inhibits tumor progression.

TUNEL and H&E staining on tumor tissues after different treatments were performed to assess apoptosis and necrosis. Results showed few necrotic cells in the 131I and TMP@D groups (Fig. 6F). The 131I-TMP@D group displayed an increase in necrotic areas, though these were not extensive. The TMP@D + US group exhibited a significant increase in apoptotic cells forming large areas. In the 131I-TMP@D + US group, widespread apoptosis was observed, consistent with tumor volume reduction data post-treatment. These findings confirmed that the combination of 131I-TMP@D radionuclide therapy, sonodynamic therapy, and bioreductive prodrugs provides optimal therapeutic efficacy against tumors.

Conclusion

The study successfully developed a multimodal imaging-guided nanoplatform for synergistic tumor therapy. Using PLGA as the carrier, the nanoplatform encapsulated the sonosensitizer MnTTP and hypoxia-activated drug TPZ as the “core,” with an outer polydopamine layer for enhanced biocompatibility and provided 131I labeling sites. This facilitated MRI and SPECT-guided RNT-SDT-chemotherapy. In vitro and in vivo experiments demonstrated the nanoplatform’s excellent biosafety, blood compatibility, high radiochemical purity, and significant EPR effect, indicating promising application prospects. Imaging results confirmed its effectiveness in both MRI and SPECT modalities. In vivo studies on tumor-bearing mice validated the substantial tumor suppression achieved through the combination of radiotherapy, sonodynamic therapy, and hypoxia-activated chemotherapy. The combination therapy demonstrated a supra-additive therapeutic response, where the multimodal therapeutic strategy produced synergistic efficacy exceeding the arithmetic sum of individual monotherapy outcomes. 131I-TMP@D shows promising potential for both the diagnosis and treatment of tumors, offering a valuable approach for hypoxic solid tumors.
