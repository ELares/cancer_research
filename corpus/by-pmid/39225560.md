Mapping global research landscape and trend of nano-drug delivery system for urological cancers: a bibliometric analysis

Aim: We conducted a bibliometric analysis to quantitatively study the development pathway, research hotspots and evolutionary trends of nano-drug delivery systems (NDDS) in treating urological tumors.

Materials & methods: We used the Web of Science Core Collection to retrieve the literature related to NDDS in the urological tumors up to November 1, 2023. Bibliometric analysis and visualization were conducted using CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix. The major aspects of analysis included contributions from different countries/regions, authors' contributions, keywords identification, citation frequencies and overall research trends.

Results: We included 3,220 articles. The analysis of annual publication trends revealed significant growth in this field since 2010, which has continued to the present day. The United States and China have far exceeded other countries/regions in the publication volume of papers in this field. The progression of the shell structure of NDDS in the urinary system has gradually transitioned from non-biological materials to biocompatible materials and ultimately to completely biocompatible materials. Mucoadhesive NDDS for intravesical drug delivery is a hotspot and a potential research material for bladder cancer.

Conclusion: The field of NDDS in urological tumors has emerged as a research hotspot. Future research should focus on synergistic effects of NDDS with other treatment modalities.

Article highlights

NDDS studies for prostate, kidney and bladder cancers began to increase around 2010 and the number of studies has subsequently shown a steady increase.

The United States and China have made major contributions to NDDS research in urologic tumors, including prostate, kidney, bladder, testicular and penile cancers.

In the field of prostate cancer NDDS research, the largest research cluster was magnetic nanoparticles, while drug delivery represented an ongoing theme of research.

The progression of the shell structure of NDDS in the urinary system has gradually transitioned from non-biological materials to biocompatible materials and ultimately to completely biocompatible materials.

Early research in this field of renal cancer NDDS focused on cancer chemotherapy, cells, cytotoxicity, apoptosis and tissue distribution, whereas more recent studies have primarily concentrated on kidney microenvironment and survival benefits.

Bladder cancer-related NDDS studies have focused on their penetration of the urinary epithelium and the retention time of therapeutic agents in the bladder.

The evolution of NDDS is a significant hallmark of the continuous progress and optimization in the field of drug delivery, providing novel insights and directions for future drug development and therapy.

A combination of multimodal synergistic therapy of NDDS with dynamic therapies or with radiotherapy, chemotherapy, immunotherapy and other treatment modalities could bring breakthroughs to treating urological tumors.

Introduction

The most prevalent urological cancers are prostate cancer, bladder cancer, renal cancer, testicular cancer and penile cancers, in that order of incidence. These cancers present a significant global health challenge. Conventional treatments mainly include surgery, radiotherapy or chemotherapy, etc. However, the complication rate of treatment modalities such as surgery or radiotherapy is high, which affects the quality of life of patients. Current chemotherapy faces problems such as lack of specificity, cytotoxicity, induction of multi-drug resistance and stem-like cells growth, which impede the effective treatment of urologic cancers. Consequently, there exists a pressing need for more effective and less toxic therapy. Novel anti-tumor drug delivery systems, especially nano-drug delivery systems (NDDS), are small in size and flexible in structure, with good biocompatibility and low toxicity. NDDS can protect encapsulated drugs from degradation in the bloodstream, increase their solubility and reduce systemic toxicity while providing targeted drug delivery, thus improving pharmacokinetics and therapeutic efficacy. NDDS has opened up new avenues for the precise delivery of drugs and has immensely contributed to enhanced therapeutic effects and reduced systemic side effects, thereby continuously expanding the boundaries of tumor treatment. However, the field involves multiple interdisciplinary subjects such as molecular biology, chemistry and materials science because of which NDDS has not yet been widely developed, despite its promising prospects.

Bibliometrics is a discipline that employs mathematical and statistical methods to quantitatively analyze the scientific literature to uncover trends, impacts, collaborative relationships and other facets of research output. It is a crucial tool for scholars to identify recent advancements, anticipate research focal points and assess developmental trends within specific fields. Recently, bibliometrics has been instrumental in exploring research focal points within the realm of nanomaterials. The interdisciplinary nature of NDDS to urological tumors spans fields such as oncology, materials science, chemistry, pharmacology and others. It is essential to conduct bibliometric and visual analyses to systematically review perspectives and knowledge across these domains, which will be insightful for current practices and steering future research endeavors.

We used the Web of Science Core Collection (WOSCC) database and tools such as CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix to visually analyze the use of NDDS in urological tumors. The present study summarizes the relevant literature on different countries/regions, authors, keywords, citation frequencies and overall research trends to identify hotspots and predict future directions. The primary goal is to quantitatively showcase the development trajectory, hotspots and trends in NDDS for urological tumors and provide a comprehensive review through knowledge mapping.

Methods

Data sources & search strategies

Bibliometric analysis is crucial for understanding the landscape of academic fields. Its scientificity and effectiveness depend on appropriate data sources and comprehensive search strategies making it essential to adopt rigorous data collection and retrieval methods. We used the Science Citation Index-Expanded (SCIE) and Social Sciences Citation Index (SSCI) of the WOSCC as data sources. The WOSCC encompasses over 12,000 high-quality academic journals and boasts a relatively reliable database. Furthermore, it has been considered one of the best databases for bibliometric research in the past.

The literature search was conducted independently by two researchers (Yibo Shi & Xinyi Yu), with the search cutoff date set as November 1, 2023. The search strategy was as follows: TS = ((Nanoparticle* OR Nanocrystalline Material* OR Nanocrystal* OR Nano Particle* OR Nanocapsules* OR Nanodrugs*)) AND TS = ((Drug Delivery System* OR Drug Targeting* OR Drug Delivery*)) AND TS = ((Bladder* OR Urinary bladder* OR Prostatic* OR Prostate* OR Renal Cell* OR Nephroid* OR Renal* OR Kidney* OR Testicular* OR Testis* OR Testes* OR Penis* OR Penile*) AND (cancer* OR tumo$r* OR neoplasm* OR carcinoma* OR oncology* OR Adenocarcinoma*)). We used a wildcard character (*) that could replace any other character and allowed for variability at the end of keywords to include the maximum number of data sources. For example, using “cancer*” as a search term would retrieve results with terms “cancer” and “cancers”. The publication type was limited to original articles and reviews and the publication language was English. The retrieved papers were saved as “plain text” and exported as “full-text citation records”.

Bibliometric analysis

A total of 3,260 articles were retrieved, including 1,672 on prostate cancer, 1,241 on kidney cancer, 340 on bladder cancer, 6 on testicular cancer and 1 on penile cancer. The inclusion and exclusion criteria were applied and titles and abstracts were read, followed exclusion of 14 non-original articles, such as meeting abstracts, editorial materials and letters; 18 non-English language articles; and 8 duplicate publications. Ultimately, 3,220 documents were included (Figure 1).

VOSviewer is a software tool used for visualizing and constructing bibliometric network maps. CiteSpace software is a Java-based bibliometric tool designed to analyze the development dynamics and research clusters of specific topics. We used these to analyze the included literature, which involved conducting co-citation analysis by country/region, dual-map overlay of citations, keyword co-occurrence, timeline view, co-cited references analysis and identification of references with the strongest citation burst. SCImago Graphica was used to analyze the distribution of countries/regions and their collaborations. Microsoft Excel and PowerPoint were used to analyze the trends in the annual publication volume and create flowcharts.

Results

As of November 1, 2023, the WOSCC database comprised 3,220 publications related to NDDS and urological tumors. Among these, 1,653 were related to prostate cancer, 1,229 to renal cancer, 331 to bladder cancer, 6 to testicular cancer and 1 to penile cancer (Figure 1). There were fewer NDDS studies on testicular and penile cancers and therefore bibliometric analysis could not be performed.

Global trend in publication outputs

We observed an overall upward trend in the publication volume of NDDS-related literature in prostate cancer, renal cancer and bladder cancer (Figure 2). Figure 2A depicts that before 2010, the annual publication volume of NDDS research on prostate cancer was less than 30 articles. After 2010, a significant increase in the number of publications per year was noted, accounting for approximately 95% of all papers in this field (94.98%, 1,570 out of 1,653 articles). The global annual number of published articles increased from 40 in 2010 to 144 in 2016 and has since remained at around 140 articles annually. After 2021, the annual publication volume increased further, reaching a peak of 166 articles in 2022. As of November 01, 2023, 112 articles were published in 2023, with a steady increase in the number of publications.

The trend in the annual publication volume for NDDS research on renal cancer is similar to that observed for prostate cancer. Figure 2B displays that the annual publication volume of related literature started increasing in 2006. After 2012, rapid growth was noted in the annual number of publications, accounting for more than 90% of all papers in this field (91.62%; 1,126 out of 1,229 articles). The global annual publication volume of related literature increased from 35 articles in 2012 to 120 articles in 2018 and has since remained at around 120 articles annually. In 2021, although we observed a slight decrease in the number of publications, the annual publication volume reached a peak of 129 articles in 2022.

We found that among the three most common urological cancers, research on NDDS in bladder cancer was the least prevalent, the first reports on which appeared later. Figure 2C depicts that from 2003 to 2023, 331 articles on bladder cancer NDDS were published, with an uneven annual publication volume but an overall increasing trend. Before 2010, fewer than 12 articles were published annually. After 2010, the number of articles published accounted for more than 95% of all articles in this field (96.37%, 319 out of 331 articles). The global annual publication volume in this field gradually increased from 4 articles in 2010 to 43 articles in 2022. As of November 1, 2023, 46 articles were published in 2023, with a continuous growth trend in the number of publications.

Discussion

It is an increasingly challenging task to keep abreast with the latest research trends in today's age of exponential information growth. We conducted an in-depth analysis and visualized 3,260 research papers on NDDS for urological cancer published before November 1, 2023, using the following three bibliometric tools: CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix. We systematically organized the global research literature in the field of NDDS to provide a comprehensive review of the evolution of urological cancer NDDS and accurately identify the research hotspots within this domain. We thoroughly analyzed the existing research findings to construct a comprehensive framework regarding the development of NDDS for urological cancer, facilitating not only an understanding of past achievements but also offering insights for future research directions. Furthermore, we evaluated the contributions of different countries or regions to research on NDDS for genitourinary tumors, revealing the geographical distribution and collaboration patterns in global NDDS research. This is necessary for creating a detailed research landscape and offering essential reference materials for the advancement of this field.

Before the 21st century, research on NDDS for the three major urological cancers (prostate, renal and bladder cancer) was relatively limited. Although global research on NDDS for urological cancer began to emerge in the first decade of the 21st century, the growth in the number of publications has been relatively slow. Since 2010, a significant surge has been noticed in this field, demonstrating a continuous upward trend persisting to the present day. However, there are very few articles on NDDS for testicular and penile cancers and all of them have been done since 2010. At the national/regional level, the United States and China have far outpaced other countries/regions in the number of publications on urological cancer NDDS. Despite China's higher number of publications, the United States maintains a leading position in academic influence, possessing the highest H-index and occupying a central position in the global collaboration network. The United States exhibits a higher MCP ratio than China in research on the three major urological cancers, indicating widespread international influence and collaboration networks in this field. In summary, we described the rapid development of NDDS applications in research on urological cancer, suggesting that the coming years will witness critical growth. Simultaneously, the significant academic position of the United States in this field and the formation of global collaboration networks have laid a solid foundation for further advancements in NDDS research in urological cancers.

Evolution of NDDS shell structures

The shell structure of NDDS critically contributes to the efficient delivery of drugs. The continuous advancement of science and technology has placed the design and optimization of nanocarrier shell structures at a significant research focus in the field of NDDS. This evolutionary process not only involves the development of nanomaterial science but also closely correlates with a deeper understanding of disease treatment mechanisms. The development of nanocarrier shell structures has continuously progressed from the initial simple polymer coatings to the current multifunctional designs, thereby driving the advancement of drug delivery technologies and offering new possibilities for safer and more effective drug transport. Furthermore, the optimization of nanocarrier shell structures contributes to enhanced drug stability and targeting, thereby improving therapeutic efficacy and reducing side effects. In summary, the evolution of nanocarrier shell structures is a significant hallmark of the continuous progress and optimization in the field of drug delivery, providing novel insights and directions for future drug development and therapy.

The research on nanoparticle carriers in prostate cancer NDDS studies has progressed from early-stage ring-opening polymerization nanoparticles (ROPs), superparamagnetic nanoparticles (SPNPs), biodegradable nanoparticles, carbon nanotubes and mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) to lipid nanoparticles, further advancing to the current extracellular vesicles (Table 4). This evolutionary process reflects the continuous structural optimization of NDDS, transitioning from the initial non-biological materials to gradually incorporating biocompatible materials and ultimately developing into completely biocompatible materials. This process demonstrates the rapid advancement of NDDS and the pursuit of higher biocompatible materials.

ROP is a type of polymerization reaction, which involves opening one or more chemical bonds within a cyclic monomer to produce linear polymer products. For instance, Tong et al. and Liu et al. employed ROPs to stably incorporate hydrophobic drugs into polymeric nanoparticles, enabling fluorescence tracking and targeted therapy for prostate cancer. ROPs have controllable nanoparticle sizes and structures that increase the solubility of hydrophobic drugs, as well as sustained release, targeting and in vivo imaging capabilities. However, their synthesis process requires metal catalysts that are difficult to remove and potentially toxic for in vivo applications. Moreover, the types of polymers are limited and cannot be flexibly adapted to clinical needs.

SPNPs can be directed to accumulate in specific tissues to exhibit outstandingly enhanced magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), as well as photothermal and magnetic heating properties on exposure to an alternating magnetic field. In addition, these can be conjugated with drugs or targeting molecules for active targeted drug delivery. Wang et al. demonstrated that intravenous injection of SPNPs into mice tails and the application of an external magnetic field caused the accumulation of SPNPs at specific tumor sites. Magnetic hyperthermia therapy demonstrated significantly inhibited tumor growth. In separate work, Yu et al. synthesized thermally cross-linked superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (TCL-SPIONs) conjugated with a prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) aptamer. These nanoparticles could successfully detect prostate cancer in vivo via MRI and selectively deliver drugs to tumor tissues, effectively suppressing tumor growth. However, the preparation process of SPNPs is complicated and costly. Their structure contains a large number of metal inorganics, which may be immunogenic and the clearance mechanism and long-term toxicity in vivo are not clear.

Carbon nanotubes, initially described by the Japanese physicist Sumio Iijima in a letter to Nature in 1991, are carbon allotropes with high aspect ratios. Since their discovery, these have attracted widespread research attention, particularly in the fields of cancer cell imaging and drug delivery. In addition, carbon nanotubes serve as nanoscale drug delivery platforms, to thermally ablate cancer cells. Fisher et al. reported that the intracellular uptake of carbon nanotubes in PC-3 and RENCA cells increased with prolonged incubation. A study reported that a combination of laser irradiation and carbon nanotubes enhanced the therapeutic efficacy against cancer cells through improved and more controlled thermal deposition, increased tumor injury and reduced expression of heat shock proteins. Carbon nanotubes possess high photothermal conversion efficiency and can be used as heating materials, temperature sensors and drug carriers with multifunctional imaging and multimodal therapeutic capabilities. However, they are difficult and costly to synthesize and the mechanism of cellular uptake and in vivo distribution are unclear, as are the potential toxicity and long-term safety.

MSNs are inorganic nanocarriers with highly ordered mesoporous structures, characterized by a high specific surface area, large pore volume and tunable pore size. They possess excellent drug delivery and controlled release capabilities, enabling effective treatment outcomes while reducing the required in vivo concentrations, thus enhancing therapeutic efficiency. Mohanan et al. developed cabazitaxel-loaded core-shell MSNs, exhibiting faster drug release at acidic pH and effectively killing prostate cancer cells. Li et al. synthesized Fe3O4@mSiO2/PDDA/BSA-Gd2O3 nanocomposites, where BSA-Gd2O3 nanoparticles and Fe3O4 nanoparticles serve as T1 and T2 MRI contrast agents, respectively. A 20 nm mesoporous silica (mSiO2) nanoshell was introduced to reduce the interference between them, converting them into an efficient T1-T2 dual-mode contrast agent for targeted in vitro renal cancer cell imaging and enhanced MRI of mouse kidneys. Sweeney et al. enhanced the specificity of recognition and binding to bladder cancer cells by modifying MSNs with the bladder cancer-specific peptide Cyc6. Intravesical administration of the modified MSNs in tumor-bearing mice improved the MRI signal intensity in T1 and T2-weighted imaging, enhanced tumor boundary detection and provided a novel approach for the diagnosis and treatment of bladder cancer. In addition, MSNs have the advantages of tunable morphology and particle size, easy synthesis and surface modification. However, the in vivo biodegradability of this inorganic NDDS needs to be improved and its long-term toxicity and safety need to be further investigated.

Liposomal nanoparticles are small artificial, self-assembled vesicular nanostructures derived from phospholipids and cholesterol molecules. These have been employed as a drug delivery system in clinical therapy for several drugs, including anticancer agents, antifungal drugs and anesthetics and are considered the first nanomedicine delivery system to transition from the concept to clinical application. Currently, several liposomal nanomedicines, including PEGylated liposomal doxorubicin (Doxil®), PEGylated liposomal daunorubicin (DaunoXome®), Vincristine (Onco TCS®) and PEGylated liposomal cisplatin (SPI-77), are commercially available. Studies involving an LNCaP model of prostate cancer xenograft tumors demonstrated that liposomal gemcitabine exhibited over 40-times higher antitumor activity compared with free gemcitabine. Pal et al. synthesized a tumor-targeted liposomal nanomedicine using phospholipids, cholesterol, DSPE-(PEG)2000-OMe and a proprietary tumor-targeting peptide. These liposomal nanoparticles exhibited excellent renal cancer cell-specific uptake and significant growth inhibition. In addition, in vivo, dual-drug-loaded liposomal nanoparticles demonstrated significantly higher inhibition of renal cell carcinoma xenograft tumors and markedly reduced lung metastasis. Liposome nanoparticles use biocompatible materials with high biosafety. Their drug encapsulation efficiency is high and they can improve pharmacokinetics, enhance drug stability, control drug release and can encapsulate a variety of drugs to achieve multi-component therapy. However, its preparation process is complex, with stability problems and the consistency between batches is poor.

The key objectives of NDDS include protecting the cargo from the impact of the body's internal environment, releasing it at appropriate sites and avoiding triggering immunogenic reactions. PEGylated NDDS can overcome issues such as non-specific cytotoxicity, poor biocompatibility and low delivery efficacy. However, these could be quickly eliminated after the initial injection due to their immunogenicity. The use of nanocarriers derived from a patient's body has recently emerged as a research hotspot and primary choice in NDDS to avoid potential immune responses. Small vesicles released by cells, including cancer cells, can be cultured in vitro to generate extracellular vesicles (EVs), including exosomes, microvesicles (MVs) and apoptotic bodies. These EVs are subsequently used to load drugs and deliver them to the lesions of patients, serving as a personalized therapeutic approach based on the patient's cells, with a lower risk of eliciting immunogenic reactions. In addition, specific surface interactions between EVs and cells within the lesion tissue can enhance the targeting of treatment. Luo et al. used EVs in an immunocompetent mouse model of prostate cancer to significantly suppress tumor growth by activating CD8+ T cells and inducing macrophage polarization toward an M1-like phenotype. Pratiwi et al. used a biogenesis approach to encapsulate MSNs within EVs secreted by renal cancer cells, resulting in EV-MSN hybrid nanoparticles with natural membrane properties and improved stability and in vivo targeting of drugs. Duo et al. developed a bladder cancer patient-derived microvesicle/AIEgen hybrid system (AMVs) using electroporation, which exhibited superior tumor targeting capabilities and effective personalized sonodynamic therapy in a bladder cancer PDX model. As a fully biocompatible NDDS with no immunogenicity and high specificity, EVs can achieve personalized tumor targeting and effectively inhibit tumor growth. However, the high production cost and difficulty of purification prevent its large-scale application and EVs are more difficult to be stably preserved in vitro, the long-term safety is to be evaluated and the clinical application is still immature.

NDDS for bladder cancer

Unlike in prostate and renal cancers, bladder cancer NDDS includes a class of mucoadhesive nanocarriers designed for intravesical drug delivery. As standard treatment for bladder cancer, transurethral resection of bladder tumors could not completely eradicate or overlook small tumor lesions leading to recurrence. Intravesical chemotherapy and immunotherapy, which directly expose cancer lesions to therapeutic agents, are widely accepted adjunctive treatments for bladder cancer and prevent postoperative recurrence and disease progression. However, their application is limited by poor penetration of the urinary epithelium and the short retention time of therapeutic agents in the bladder. We demonstrated that NDDS for intravesical infusion include lipid nanoparticles, hydrogels, polymers, chitosan and mesoporous silica nanoparticles (Table 5).

During drug delivery, different types of lipid nanoparticles can be designed to enable “trigger release” based on changes in the local environment of the target tissue. Seth et al. encapsulated siRNA targeting bladder cancer biomarkers, namely, survivin and polo-like kinase-1 (PLK1) genes into lipid nanoparticles, to locally inhibit their expression in bladder cancer tissue through intravesical instillation, thereby suppressing tumor growth. Furthermore, functionalized liposomes can enhance drug permeation in the bladder and prolong drug retention time. For instance, Kaldybekov et al. used maleimide-functionalized PEGylated liposomes (PEG-Mal) as an adhesive carrier to deliver drugs to the bladder. PEG-Mal liposomes exhibited stronger adhesion to mucosal surfaces, deeper penetration depth and longer drug release duration. Liposomal nanoparticles for intravesical instillation have a good biosafety due to their localized delivery. Moreover, excellent mucosal adhesion and penetration prolong drug retention time in the bladder for controlled drug release. However, its preparation process is complex and long-term effects and safety are unknown.

Hydrogels are three-dimensional hydrophilic or amphiphilic polymer networks, synthesized by physical or chemical crosslinking of polymers. These materials exhibit exceptional abilities to retain water or biological fluids. Lin et al. developed a floating hydrogel for the delivery of adriamycin (ADR) possessing in situ gelling and floating potentials within the bladder, enabling controlled release of ADR. Hydrogels are more biocompatible and can reduce bladder irritation, achieve controlled and sustained release properties of the drug and reduce repeated drug administration. However, its stability is not good and the drug loading capacity is poor. Some hydrogels require acidification of urine for use.

Chitosan is a positively charged nanopolymer that is non-toxic, degradable and exhibits good biocompatibility, adhesion and permeability. Şenyiğit et al. polymerized thiolated chitosan (chitosan–thioglycolic acid conjugate) to synthesize bioadhesive nanoparticles and dispersed them in a 2% chitosan gel or in situ gelling poloxamer formulation to enhance the intravesical delivery effectiveness of gemcitabine hydrochloride. The NDDS composed of chitosan was poorly stabilized and had a low drug loading capacity.

Polymers are large molecules composed of repeating subunits, which are characterized by controllable drug release time, hydrophilic/hydrophobic release and good biocompatibility. Tang et al. used fluorinated polylysine micelles (PLLF) to self-assemble into nanoparticles and load with the CRISPR-Cas9 system targeting DAD1 (Cas9-sgDAD1). The PLLF/Cas9-sgDAD1 nanoparticles significantly inhibited the expression of DAD1 in bladder cancer cells, whereas their intravesical administration markedly suppressed orthotopic bladder tumors. However, polymeric nanomedicines are complicated to prepare, costly to develop and difficult to translate clinically.

MSNs can be applied to drug delivery for intravesical infusion. The release of drugs from their mesoporous structure increases the local drug concentrations, thereby reducing the necessary drug dosage and lowering drug toxicity. Functionalizing the surface of MSNs by targeting ligands and polymers can extend the retention time of the drug. Chen et al. synthesized externally thiolated hollow mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSN-SH(E)) to combine intravesical chemotherapy (mitomycin C, MMC) with immunotherapy (via macrophage reprogramming). MSN-SH (E) promotes the transformation of M2 macrophages into anti-cancer M1 macrophages. Animal experiments have demonstrated strong adhesion of this nanomaterial to bladder mucosa and membrane permeability. MMC@MSN-SH(E) reduces the expression of transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β), inhibits tumor cell proliferation and exerts an anticancer effect. However, MSNs for intravesical instillation cannot achieve controlled release of the drug and long-term toxicity is unknown, requiring further clinical studies.

Combination of NDDS with other dynamic therapies

The application of NDDS is limited by certain shortcomings, especially when solely depending on NDDS for tumor treatment. The heterogeneity of tumors and the complexity of the tumor microenvironment do not allow the NDDS to comprehensively meet the spatio-temporal control requirements for drug release, or its targeting could not be sufficient to achieve precise strikes against specific pathological tissues. Therefore, researchers have started exploring the combination of NDDS with different dynamic therapies (augmented nanodynamic therapy, NDT). The use of exogenous activation methods such as ultrasound, light, electricity and ionizing radiation, as well as magnetic fields, has enhanced the efficacy, selectivity and tissue penetration of nanomedicines. This novel therapeutic strategy offers high selectivity and specificity, effectively killing diseased cells while minimizing damage to normal tissues. Moreover, a combination of dynamic therapeutic modalities has been reported to synergistically enhance treatment outcomes, shorten treatment cycles and reduce side effects. Therefore, the integration of nanomedicines with different dynamic therapies is a current hotspot and key developmental direction in nanomedicine research.

We demonstrated that in renal cancer, NDDS clusters are associated with photothermal therapy (PTT) #0. NDDS-based PTT is a minimally invasive local treatment modality characterized by the loading of photothermal agents onto NDDS to convert near-infrared light into thermal energy. This not only directly kills the cancer cells but also triggers the release of drugs from the NDDS. Compared with the traditional treatment methods, NDDS-based PTT allows for remote control and entails fewer side effects, increasing its suitability for biomedical applications. For instance, Qiu et al. used PEG-IR780-C13 for systemic delivery, which accumulated in renal tumors. Under near-infrared light irradiation, the photothermal effect of PEG-IR780-C13 can effectively destroy RENCA cells in vitro and substantially inhibit the growth of in situ renal tumors in vivo.

NDDS clusters are associated with photodynamic therapy (PDT) #0 in the case of bladder cancer. PDT is a clinically approved minimally invasive treatment that uses light of specific wavelengths to irradiate photosensitizers and kill tumor cells, eventually leading to microvascular damage and local inflammatory responses. Zhu et al. designed bladder cancer-specific porphyrin-based PLZ4-nanoparticles (PNP) for PDT. PLZ4–PNP not only directly kills tumor cells through PDT but also enhances the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) by inducing immunogenic cell death, promoting dendritic cell maturation and enhancing immune cell infiltration. Experiments using mouse models have demonstrated that PDT with PLZ4-PNP combined with ICI therapy significantly prolonged the survival of bladder cancer mice and inhibited tumor growth and metastasis.

In addition to traditional PTT and PDT, different NDDS-based NDTs, including sonodynamic therapy, thermodynamic therapy, electrodynamic therapy, piezoelectric dynamic therapy, pyroelectric dynamic therapy and radiodynamic therapy are available. These therapies use different physical or chemical stimuli to activate nanosensitizers to selectively destroy diseased sites. The combination of NDDS with different NDTs has significantly expanded the application scope of nanomedicines. Altogether, this approach enhances therapeutic outcomes and holds promise for providing patients with more effective and safer treatment options through precise targeting and synergistic effects, potentially offering new breakthroughs in cancer therapy.

Conclusion

Over the past 15 years, the literature on NDDS for urological tumors has witnessed a steady and rapid increase, with the United States and China exerting significant influence in this field. Specifically, the tumor microenvironment is a research hotspot in NDDS for prostate cancer, whereas targeted delivery and its integration with other NDTs are focal points in NDDS for kidney and bladder cancers. Doxorubicin is frequently employed in NDDS for the three major urological tumors. Furthermore, continuous optimization of NDDS structures has evolved from non-biological materials to biocompatible materials and ultimately to completely biocompatible materials such as extracellular vesicles, marking a significant focus for future research. Similarly, mucoadhesive NDDS for intravesical drug delivery possesses immense potential and future direction for bladder cancer. A combination of multimodal synergistic therapy of NDDS with other dynamic therapies or with radiotherapy, chemotherapy, immunotherapy and other treatment modalities could bring breakthroughs to treating urological tumors. This could potentially offer patients more effective and safer treatment options. However, comprehensive studies are required are still required to understand the potential mechanisms, assess efficacy, analyze safety and facilitate the clinical translation of these combined therapies.

Mapping global research landscape and trend of nano-drug delivery system for urological cancers: a bibliometric analysis

Aim: We conducted a bibliometric analysis to quantitatively study the development pathway, research hotspots and evolutionary trends of nano-drug delivery systems (NDDS) in treating urological tumors.

Materials & methods: We used the Web of Science Core Collection to retrieve the literature related to NDDS in the urological tumors up to November 1, 2023. Bibliometric analysis and visualization were conducted using CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix. The major aspects of analysis included contributions from different countries/regions, authors' contributions, keywords identification, citation frequencies and overall research trends.

Results: We included 3,220 articles. The analysis of annual publication trends revealed significant growth in this field since 2010, which has continued to the present day. The United States and China have far exceeded other countries/regions in the publication volume of papers in this field. The progression of the shell structure of NDDS in the urinary system has gradually transitioned from non-biological materials to biocompatible materials and ultimately to completely biocompatible materials. Mucoadhesive NDDS for intravesical drug delivery is a hotspot and a potential research material for bladder cancer.

Conclusion: The field of NDDS in urological tumors has emerged as a research hotspot. Future research should focus on synergistic effects of NDDS with other treatment modalities.

Introduction

The most prevalent urological cancers are prostate cancer, bladder cancer, renal cancer, testicular cancer and penile cancers, in that order of incidence. These cancers present a significant global health challenge. Conventional treatments mainly include surgery, radiotherapy or chemotherapy, etc. However, the complication rate of treatment modalities such as surgery or radiotherapy is high, which affects the quality of life of patients. Current chemotherapy faces problems such as lack of specificity, cytotoxicity, induction of multi-drug resistance and stem-like cells growth, which impede the effective treatment of urologic cancers. Consequently, there exists a pressing need for more effective and less toxic therapy. Novel anti-tumor drug delivery systems, especially nano-drug delivery systems (NDDS), are small in size and flexible in structure, with good biocompatibility and low toxicity. NDDS can protect encapsulated drugs from degradation in the bloodstream, increase their solubility and reduce systemic toxicity while providing targeted drug delivery, thus improving pharmacokinetics and therapeutic efficacy. NDDS has opened up new avenues for the precise delivery of drugs and has immensely contributed to enhanced therapeutic effects and reduced systemic side effects, thereby continuously expanding the boundaries of tumor treatment. However, the field involves multiple interdisciplinary subjects such as molecular biology, chemistry and materials science because of which NDDS has not yet been widely developed, despite its promising prospects.

Bibliometrics is a discipline that employs mathematical and statistical methods to quantitatively analyze the scientific literature to uncover trends, impacts, collaborative relationships and other facets of research output. It is a crucial tool for scholars to identify recent advancements, anticipate research focal points and assess developmental trends within specific fields. Recently, bibliometrics has been instrumental in exploring research focal points within the realm of nanomaterials. The interdisciplinary nature of NDDS to urological tumors spans fields such as oncology, materials science, chemistry, pharmacology and others. It is essential to conduct bibliometric and visual analyses to systematically review perspectives and knowledge across these domains, which will be insightful for current practices and steering future research endeavors.

We used the Web of Science Core Collection (WOSCC) database and tools such as CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix to visually analyze the use of NDDS in urological tumors. The present study summarizes the relevant literature on different countries/regions, authors, keywords, citation frequencies and overall research trends to identify hotspots and predict future directions. The primary goal is to quantitatively showcase the development trajectory, hotspots and trends in NDDS for urological tumors and provide a comprehensive review through knowledge mapping.

Methods

Data sources & search strategies

Bibliometric analysis is crucial for understanding the landscape of academic fields. Its scientificity and effectiveness depend on appropriate data sources and comprehensive search strategies making it essential to adopt rigorous data collection and retrieval methods. We used the Science Citation Index-Expanded (SCIE) and Social Sciences Citation Index (SSCI) of the WOSCC as data sources. The WOSCC encompasses over 12,000 high-quality academic journals and boasts a relatively reliable database. Furthermore, it has been considered one of the best databases for bibliometric research in the past.

The literature search was conducted independently by two researchers (Yibo Shi & Xinyi Yu), with the search cutoff date set as November 1, 2023. The search strategy was as follows: TS = ((Nanoparticle* OR Nanocrystalline Material* OR Nanocrystal* OR Nano Particle* OR Nanocapsules* OR Nanodrugs*)) AND TS = ((Drug Delivery System* OR Drug Targeting* OR Drug Delivery*)) AND TS = ((Bladder* OR Urinary bladder* OR Prostatic* OR Prostate* OR Renal Cell* OR Nephroid* OR Renal* OR Kidney* OR Testicular* OR Testis* OR Testes* OR Penis* OR Penile*) AND (cancer* OR tumo$r* OR neoplasm* OR carcinoma* OR oncology* OR Adenocarcinoma*)). We used a wildcard character (*) that could replace any other character and allowed for variability at the end of keywords to include the maximum number of data sources. For example, using “cancer*” as a search term would retrieve results with terms “cancer” and “cancers”. The publication type was limited to original articles and reviews and the publication language was English. The retrieved papers were saved as “plain text” and exported as “full-text citation records”.

Bibliometric analysis

A total of 3,260 articles were retrieved, including 1,672 on prostate cancer, 1,241 on kidney cancer, 340 on bladder cancer, 6 on testicular cancer and 1 on penile cancer. The inclusion and exclusion criteria were applied and titles and abstracts were read, followed exclusion of 14 non-original articles, such as meeting abstracts, editorial materials and letters; 18 non-English language articles; and 8 duplicate publications. Ultimately, 3,220 documents were included (Figure 1).

VOSviewer is a software tool used for visualizing and constructing bibliometric network maps. CiteSpace software is a Java-based bibliometric tool designed to analyze the development dynamics and research clusters of specific topics. We used these to analyze the included literature, which involved conducting co-citation analysis by country/region, dual-map overlay of citations, keyword co-occurrence, timeline view, co-cited references analysis and identification of references with the strongest citation burst. SCImago Graphica was used to analyze the distribution of countries/regions and their collaborations. Microsoft Excel and PowerPoint were used to analyze the trends in the annual publication volume and create flowcharts.

Discussion

It is an increasingly challenging task to keep abreast with the latest research trends in today's age of exponential information growth. We conducted an in-depth analysis and visualized 3,260 research papers on NDDS for urological cancer published before November 1, 2023, using the following three bibliometric tools: CiteSpace, VOSviewer and R-Bibliometrix. We systematically organized the global research literature in the field of NDDS to provide a comprehensive review of the evolution of urological cancer NDDS and accurately identify the research hotspots within this domain. We thoroughly analyzed the existing research findings to construct a comprehensive framework regarding the development of NDDS for urological cancer, facilitating not only an understanding of past achievements but also offering insights for future research directions. Furthermore, we evaluated the contributions of different countries or regions to research on NDDS for genitourinary tumors, revealing the geographical distribution and collaboration patterns in global NDDS research. This is necessary for creating a detailed research landscape and offering essential reference materials for the advancement of this field.

Before the 21st century, research on NDDS for the three major urological cancers (prostate, renal and bladder cancer) was relatively limited. Although global research on NDDS for urological cancer began to emerge in the first decade of the 21st century, the growth in the number of publications has been relatively slow. Since 2010, a significant surge has been noticed in this field, demonstrating a continuous upward trend persisting to the present day. However, there are very few articles on NDDS for testicular and penile cancers and all of them have been done since 2010. At the national/regional level, the United States and China have far outpaced other countries/regions in the number of publications on urological cancer NDDS. Despite China's higher number of publications, the United States maintains a leading position in academic influence, possessing the highest H-index and occupying a central position in the global collaboration network. The United States exhibits a higher MCP ratio than China in research on the three major urological cancers, indicating widespread international influence and collaboration networks in this field. In summary, we described the rapid development of NDDS applications in research on urological cancer, suggesting that the coming years will witness critical growth. Simultaneously, the significant academic position of the United States in this field and the formation of global collaboration networks have laid a solid foundation for further advancements in NDDS research in urological cancers.

Evolution of NDDS shell structures

The shell structure of NDDS critically contributes to the efficient delivery of drugs. The continuous advancement of science and technology has placed the design and optimization of nanocarrier shell structures at a significant research focus in the field of NDDS. This evolutionary process not only involves the development of nanomaterial science but also closely correlates with a deeper understanding of disease treatment mechanisms. The development of nanocarrier shell structures has continuously progressed from the initial simple polymer coatings to the current multifunctional designs, thereby driving the advancement of drug delivery technologies and offering new possibilities for safer and more effective drug transport. Furthermore, the optimization of nanocarrier shell structures contributes to enhanced drug stability and targeting, thereby improving therapeutic efficacy and reducing side effects. In summary, the evolution of nanocarrier shell structures is a significant hallmark of the continuous progress and optimization in the field of drug delivery, providing novel insights and directions for future drug development and therapy.

The research on nanoparticle carriers in prostate cancer NDDS studies has progressed from early-stage ring-opening polymerization nanoparticles (ROPs), superparamagnetic nanoparticles (SPNPs), biodegradable nanoparticles, carbon nanotubes and mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSNs) to lipid nanoparticles, further advancing to the current extracellular vesicles (Table 4). This evolutionary process reflects the continuous structural optimization of NDDS, transitioning from the initial non-biological materials to gradually incorporating biocompatible materials and ultimately developing into completely biocompatible materials. This process demonstrates the rapid advancement of NDDS and the pursuit of higher biocompatible materials.

ROP is a type of polymerization reaction, which involves opening one or more chemical bonds within a cyclic monomer to produce linear polymer products. For instance, Tong et al. and Liu et al. employed ROPs to stably incorporate hydrophobic drugs into polymeric nanoparticles, enabling fluorescence tracking and targeted therapy for prostate cancer. ROPs have controllable nanoparticle sizes and structures that increase the solubility of hydrophobic drugs, as well as sustained release, targeting and in vivo imaging capabilities. However, their synthesis process requires metal catalysts that are difficult to remove and potentially toxic for in vivo applications. Moreover, the types of polymers are limited and cannot be flexibly adapted to clinical needs.

SPNPs can be directed to accumulate in specific tissues to exhibit outstandingly enhanced magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), as well as photothermal and magnetic heating properties on exposure to an alternating magnetic field. In addition, these can be conjugated with drugs or targeting molecules for active targeted drug delivery. Wang et al. demonstrated that intravenous injection of SPNPs into mice tails and the application of an external magnetic field caused the accumulation of SPNPs at specific tumor sites. Magnetic hyperthermia therapy demonstrated significantly inhibited tumor growth. In separate work, Yu et al. synthesized thermally cross-linked superparamagnetic iron oxide nanoparticles (TCL-SPIONs) conjugated with a prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) aptamer. These nanoparticles could successfully detect prostate cancer in vivo via MRI and selectively deliver drugs to tumor tissues, effectively suppressing tumor growth. However, the preparation process of SPNPs is complicated and costly. Their structure contains a large number of metal inorganics, which may be immunogenic and the clearance mechanism and long-term toxicity in vivo are not clear.

Carbon nanotubes, initially described by the Japanese physicist Sumio Iijima in a letter to Nature in 1991, are carbon allotropes with high aspect ratios. Since their discovery, these have attracted widespread research attention, particularly in the fields of cancer cell imaging and drug delivery. In addition, carbon nanotubes serve as nanoscale drug delivery platforms, to thermally ablate cancer cells. Fisher et al. reported that the intracellular uptake of carbon nanotubes in PC-3 and RENCA cells increased with prolonged incubation. A study reported that a combination of laser irradiation and carbon nanotubes enhanced the therapeutic efficacy against cancer cells through improved and more controlled thermal deposition, increased tumor injury and reduced expression of heat shock proteins. Carbon nanotubes possess high photothermal conversion efficiency and can be used as heating materials, temperature sensors and drug carriers with multifunctional imaging and multimodal therapeutic capabilities. However, they are difficult and costly to synthesize and the mechanism of cellular uptake and in vivo distribution are unclear, as are the potential toxicity and long-term safety.

MSNs are inorganic nanocarriers with highly ordered mesoporous structures, characterized by a high specific surface area, large pore volume and tunable pore size. They possess excellent drug delivery and controlled release capabilities, enabling effective treatment outcomes while reducing the required in vivo concentrations, thus enhancing therapeutic efficiency. Mohanan et al. developed cabazitaxel-loaded core-shell MSNs, exhibiting faster drug release at acidic pH and effectively killing prostate cancer cells. Li et al. synthesized Fe3O4@mSiO2/PDDA/BSA-Gd2O3 nanocomposites, where BSA-Gd2O3 nanoparticles and Fe3O4 nanoparticles serve as T1 and T2 MRI contrast agents, respectively. A 20 nm mesoporous silica (mSiO2) nanoshell was introduced to reduce the interference between them, converting them into an efficient T1-T2 dual-mode contrast agent for targeted in vitro renal cancer cell imaging and enhanced MRI of mouse kidneys. Sweeney et al. enhanced the specificity of recognition and binding to bladder cancer cells by modifying MSNs with the bladder cancer-specific peptide Cyc6. Intravesical administration of the modified MSNs in tumor-bearing mice improved the MRI signal intensity in T1 and T2-weighted imaging, enhanced tumor boundary detection and provided a novel approach for the diagnosis and treatment of bladder cancer. In addition, MSNs have the advantages of tunable morphology and particle size, easy synthesis and surface modification. However, the in vivo biodegradability of this inorganic NDDS needs to be improved and its long-term toxicity and safety need to be further investigated.

Liposomal nanoparticles are small artificial, self-assembled vesicular nanostructures derived from phospholipids and cholesterol molecules. These have been employed as a drug delivery system in clinical therapy for several drugs, including anticancer agents, antifungal drugs and anesthetics and are considered the first nanomedicine delivery system to transition from the concept to clinical application. Currently, several liposomal nanomedicines, including PEGylated liposomal doxorubicin (Doxil®), PEGylated liposomal daunorubicin (DaunoXome®), Vincristine (Onco TCS®) and PEGylated liposomal cisplatin (SPI-77), are commercially available. Studies involving an LNCaP model of prostate cancer xenograft tumors demonstrated that liposomal gemcitabine exhibited over 40-times higher antitumor activity compared with free gemcitabine. Pal et al. synthesized a tumor-targeted liposomal nanomedicine using phospholipids, cholesterol, DSPE-(PEG)2000-OMe and a proprietary tumor-targeting peptide. These liposomal nanoparticles exhibited excellent renal cancer cell-specific uptake and significant growth inhibition. In addition, in vivo, dual-drug-loaded liposomal nanoparticles demonstrated significantly higher inhibition of renal cell carcinoma xenograft tumors and markedly reduced lung metastasis. Liposome nanoparticles use biocompatible materials with high biosafety. Their drug encapsulation efficiency is high and they can improve pharmacokinetics, enhance drug stability, control drug release and can encapsulate a variety of drugs to achieve multi-component therapy. However, its preparation process is complex, with stability problems and the consistency between batches is poor.

The key objectives of NDDS include protecting the cargo from the impact of the body's internal environment, releasing it at appropriate sites and avoiding triggering immunogenic reactions. PEGylated NDDS can overcome issues such as non-specific cytotoxicity, poor biocompatibility and low delivery efficacy. However, these could be quickly eliminated after the initial injection due to their immunogenicity. The use of nanocarriers derived from a patient's body has recently emerged as a research hotspot and primary choice in NDDS to avoid potential immune responses. Small vesicles released by cells, including cancer cells, can be cultured in vitro to generate extracellular vesicles (EVs), including exosomes, microvesicles (MVs) and apoptotic bodies. These EVs are subsequently used to load drugs and deliver them to the lesions of patients, serving as a personalized therapeutic approach based on the patient's cells, with a lower risk of eliciting immunogenic reactions. In addition, specific surface interactions between EVs and cells within the lesion tissue can enhance the targeting of treatment. Luo et al. used EVs in an immunocompetent mouse model of prostate cancer to significantly suppress tumor growth by activating CD8+ T cells and inducing macrophage polarization toward an M1-like phenotype. Pratiwi et al. used a biogenesis approach to encapsulate MSNs within EVs secreted by renal cancer cells, resulting in EV-MSN hybrid nanoparticles with natural membrane properties and improved stability and in vivo targeting of drugs. Duo et al. developed a bladder cancer patient-derived microvesicle/AIEgen hybrid system (AMVs) using electroporation, which exhibited superior tumor targeting capabilities and effective personalized sonodynamic therapy in a bladder cancer PDX model. As a fully biocompatible NDDS with no immunogenicity and high specificity, EVs can achieve personalized tumor targeting and effectively inhibit tumor growth. However, the high production cost and difficulty of purification prevent its large-scale application and EVs are more difficult to be stably preserved in vitro, the long-term safety is to be evaluated and the clinical application is still immature.

NDDS for bladder cancer

Unlike in prostate and renal cancers, bladder cancer NDDS includes a class of mucoadhesive nanocarriers designed for intravesical drug delivery. As standard treatment for bladder cancer, transurethral resection of bladder tumors could not completely eradicate or overlook small tumor lesions leading to recurrence. Intravesical chemotherapy and immunotherapy, which directly expose cancer lesions to therapeutic agents, are widely accepted adjunctive treatments for bladder cancer and prevent postoperative recurrence and disease progression. However, their application is limited by poor penetration of the urinary epithelium and the short retention time of therapeutic agents in the bladder. We demonstrated that NDDS for intravesical infusion include lipid nanoparticles, hydrogels, polymers, chitosan and mesoporous silica nanoparticles (Table 5).

During drug delivery, different types of lipid nanoparticles can be designed to enable “trigger release” based on changes in the local environment of the target tissue. Seth et al. encapsulated siRNA targeting bladder cancer biomarkers, namely, survivin and polo-like kinase-1 (PLK1) genes into lipid nanoparticles, to locally inhibit their expression in bladder cancer tissue through intravesical instillation, thereby suppressing tumor growth. Furthermore, functionalized liposomes can enhance drug permeation in the bladder and prolong drug retention time. For instance, Kaldybekov et al. used maleimide-functionalized PEGylated liposomes (PEG-Mal) as an adhesive carrier to deliver drugs to the bladder. PEG-Mal liposomes exhibited stronger adhesion to mucosal surfaces, deeper penetration depth and longer drug release duration. Liposomal nanoparticles for intravesical instillation have a good biosafety due to their localized delivery. Moreover, excellent mucosal adhesion and penetration prolong drug retention time in the bladder for controlled drug release. However, its preparation process is complex and long-term effects and safety are unknown.

Hydrogels are three-dimensional hydrophilic or amphiphilic polymer networks, synthesized by physical or chemical crosslinking of polymers. These materials exhibit exceptional abilities to retain water or biological fluids. Lin et al. developed a floating hydrogel for the delivery of adriamycin (ADR) possessing in situ gelling and floating potentials within the bladder, enabling controlled release of ADR. Hydrogels are more biocompatible and can reduce bladder irritation, achieve controlled and sustained release properties of the drug and reduce repeated drug administration. However, its stability is not good and the drug loading capacity is poor. Some hydrogels require acidification of urine for use.

Chitosan is a positively charged nanopolymer that is non-toxic, degradable and exhibits good biocompatibility, adhesion and permeability. Şenyiğit et al. polymerized thiolated chitosan (chitosan–thioglycolic acid conjugate) to synthesize bioadhesive nanoparticles and dispersed them in a 2% chitosan gel or in situ gelling poloxamer formulation to enhance the intravesical delivery effectiveness of gemcitabine hydrochloride. The NDDS composed of chitosan was poorly stabilized and had a low drug loading capacity.

Polymers are large molecules composed of repeating subunits, which are characterized by controllable drug release time, hydrophilic/hydrophobic release and good biocompatibility. Tang et al. used fluorinated polylysine micelles (PLLF) to self-assemble into nanoparticles and load with the CRISPR-Cas9 system targeting DAD1 (Cas9-sgDAD1). The PLLF/Cas9-sgDAD1 nanoparticles significantly inhibited the expression of DAD1 in bladder cancer cells, whereas their intravesical administration markedly suppressed orthotopic bladder tumors. However, polymeric nanomedicines are complicated to prepare, costly to develop and difficult to translate clinically.

MSNs can be applied to drug delivery for intravesical infusion. The release of drugs from their mesoporous structure increases the local drug concentrations, thereby reducing the necessary drug dosage and lowering drug toxicity. Functionalizing the surface of MSNs by targeting ligands and polymers can extend the retention time of the drug. Chen et al. synthesized externally thiolated hollow mesoporous silica nanoparticles (MSN-SH(E)) to combine intravesical chemotherapy (mitomycin C, MMC) with immunotherapy (via macrophage reprogramming). MSN-SH (E) promotes the transformation of M2 macrophages into anti-cancer M1 macrophages. Animal experiments have demonstrated strong adhesion of this nanomaterial to bladder mucosa and membrane permeability. MMC@MSN-SH(E) reduces the expression of transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β), inhibits tumor cell proliferation and exerts an anticancer effect. However, MSNs for intravesical instillation cannot achieve controlled release of the drug and long-term toxicity is unknown, requiring further clinical studies.

Combination of NDDS with other dynamic therapies

The application of NDDS is limited by certain shortcomings, especially when solely depending on NDDS for tumor treatment. The heterogeneity of tumors and the complexity of the tumor microenvironment do not allow the NDDS to comprehensively meet the spatio-temporal control requirements for drug release, or its targeting could not be sufficient to achieve precise strikes against specific pathological tissues. Therefore, researchers have started exploring the combination of NDDS with different dynamic therapies (augmented nanodynamic therapy, NDT). The use of exogenous activation methods such as ultrasound, light, electricity and ionizing radiation, as well as magnetic fields, has enhanced the efficacy, selectivity and tissue penetration of nanomedicines. This novel therapeutic strategy offers high selectivity and specificity, effectively killing diseased cells while minimizing damage to normal tissues. Moreover, a combination of dynamic therapeutic modalities has been reported to synergistically enhance treatment outcomes, shorten treatment cycles and reduce side effects. Therefore, the integration of nanomedicines with different dynamic therapies is a current hotspot and key developmental direction in nanomedicine research.

We demonstrated that in renal cancer, NDDS clusters are associated with photothermal therapy (PTT) #0. NDDS-based PTT is a minimally invasive local treatment modality characterized by the loading of photothermal agents onto NDDS to convert near-infrared light into thermal energy. This not only directly kills the cancer cells but also triggers the release of drugs from the NDDS. Compared with the traditional treatment methods, NDDS-based PTT allows for remote control and entails fewer side effects, increasing its suitability for biomedical applications. For instance, Qiu et al. used PEG-IR780-C13 for systemic delivery, which accumulated in renal tumors. Under near-infrared light irradiation, the photothermal effect of PEG-IR780-C13 can effectively destroy RENCA cells in vitro and substantially inhibit the growth of in situ renal tumors in vivo.

NDDS clusters are associated with photodynamic therapy (PDT) #0 in the case of bladder cancer. PDT is a clinically approved minimally invasive treatment that uses light of specific wavelengths to irradiate photosensitizers and kill tumor cells, eventually leading to microvascular damage and local inflammatory responses. Zhu et al. designed bladder cancer-specific porphyrin-based PLZ4-nanoparticles (PNP) for PDT. PLZ4–PNP not only directly kills tumor cells through PDT but also enhances the efficacy of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) by inducing immunogenic cell death, promoting dendritic cell maturation and enhancing immune cell infiltration. Experiments using mouse models have demonstrated that PDT with PLZ4-PNP combined with ICI therapy significantly prolonged the survival of bladder cancer mice and inhibited tumor growth and metastasis.

In addition to traditional PTT and PDT, different NDDS-based NDTs, including sonodynamic therapy, thermodynamic therapy, electrodynamic therapy, piezoelectric dynamic therapy, pyroelectric dynamic therapy and radiodynamic therapy are available. These therapies use different physical or chemical stimuli to activate nanosensitizers to selectively destroy diseased sites. The combination of NDDS with different NDTs has significantly expanded the application scope of nanomedicines. Altogether, this approach enhances therapeutic outcomes and holds promise for providing patients with more effective and safer treatment options through precise targeting and synergistic effects, potentially offering new breakthroughs in cancer therapy.

Conclusion

Over the past 15 years, the literature on NDDS for urological tumors has witnessed a steady and rapid increase, with the United States and China exerting significant influence in this field. Specifically, the tumor microenvironment is a research hotspot in NDDS for prostate cancer, whereas targeted delivery and its integration with other NDTs are focal points in NDDS for kidney and bladder cancers. Doxorubicin is frequently employed in NDDS for the three major urological tumors. Furthermore, continuous optimization of NDDS structures has evolved from non-biological materials to biocompatible materials and ultimately to completely biocompatible materials such as extracellular vesicles, marking a significant focus for future research. Similarly, mucoadhesive NDDS for intravesical drug delivery possesses immense potential and future direction for bladder cancer. A combination of multimodal synergistic therapy of NDDS with other dynamic therapies or with radiotherapy, chemotherapy, immunotherapy and other treatment modalities could bring breakthroughs to treating urological tumors. This could potentially offer patients more effective and safer treatment options. However, comprehensive studies are required are still required to understand the potential mechanisms, assess efficacy, analyze safety and facilitate the clinical translation of these combined therapies.
