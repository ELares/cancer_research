# IDO1 inhibition enhances CLDN18.2-CAR-T cell therapy in gastrointestinal cancers by overcoming kynurenine-mediated metabolic suppression in the tumor microenvironment.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy has achieved remarkable success in hematologic malignancies but faces significant limitations in gastrointestinal tumors due to the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase 1 (IDO1), a key enzyme in the TME, suppresses T cell efficacy by catalyzing tryptophan degradation to kynurenine (Kyn), leading to T cell exhaustion and reduced cytotoxicity. This study investigates the role of IDO1 inhibition in overcoming metabolic suppression by kynurenine and enhancing Claudin18.2 (CLDN18.2) CAR-T cell therapy in gastric and pancreatic adenocarcinoma models.

**METHODS**: We evaluated the impact of genetic knockdown and pharmacological inhibition of IDO1 (using epacadostat) on CAR-T cell functionality, including cytokine production and exhaustion marker expression. The effects of fludarabine and cyclophosphamide preconditioning on IDO1 expression, CAR-T cell infiltration, and antitumor activity was also examined. In vivo tumor models of gastric and pancreatic adenocarcinomas were used to assess the efficacy of combining IDO1 inhibition with CLDN18.2-CAR-T therapy.

**RESULTS**: IDO1 inhibition significantly enhanced CAR-T cell function by increasing cytokine production, reducing exhaustion markers by decreasing TOX expression and improving tumor cell lysis. Preconditioning with fludarabine and cyclophosphamide further suppressed IDO1 expression in the TME, facilitating enhanced CAR-T cell infiltration. In vivo studies demonstrated that combining IDO1 inhibition with CAR-T therapy led to robust tumor growth suppression and prolonged survival in gastric and pancreatic tumor models.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Targeting IDO1 represents a promising strategy to overcome immunosuppressive barriers in gastrointestinal cancers, improving the efficacy of CLDN18.2-CAR-T therapy. These findings highlight the potential for integrating IDO1 inhibition into CAR-T treatment regimens to address resistance in treatment-refractory cancers.

Introduction

The advent of CAR-T cell therapy has rekindled the fervor in the field of cancer immunotherapy, particularly in light of the unprecedented success achieved with CD19-targeted CAR-T cells in the treatment of hematologic malignancies. Building on this success, Claudin18.2 (CLDN18.2), a tight junction protein, has emerged as a promising target for CAR-T cell therapy in gastrointestinal cancers. It is expressed on the surface of gastric and pancreatic adenocarcinomas, but not in healthy tissues, making it an ideal target for CAR-T cell-mediated immunotherapy. CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells have demonstrated strong safety profiles and show significant promise in preclinical and clinical studies. However, despite this potential, the TME presents a major challenge, as the immunosuppressive molecules within the TME inhibit CAR-T cell function. Therefore, improving the tumor microenvironment and overcoming its suppressive effects is critical for enhancing the efficacy of CLDN18.2 CAR-T therapy in gastrointestinal tumors.

In response to these hurdles, recent research endeavors have concentrated on the development of combination therapies designed to augment the potency of CAR-T cells against gastrointestinal tumors. Prominent among these is the enzyme IDO1, which facilitates the conversion of tryptophan into the immunosuppressive metabolite Kyn and has, as such, attracted considerable interest. This metabolic pathway creates an immunosuppressive environment in tumors and in tumor-draining lymph nodes. The consequent depletion of tryptophan and the accumulation of immunosuppressive tryptophan catabolites can induce T cell anergy and apoptosis, negating their tumoricidal capabilities. Notably, the IDO1 pathway operates as a negative feedback loop that modulates the infiltration of CD8 + T cells into tumors. While IDO1 is typically induced by T cell-derived interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), its excessive expression can reciprocally impair T cell function. The overexpression of IDO1 in various human cancers and its correlation with elevated serum Kyn levels are indicative of a poor patient prognosis. Consequently, the targeting of tryptophan catabolism has emerged as a compelling strategy to mitigate tumor progression and bolster antitumor immunity in cancer therapy.

Our study advances the discourse by examining the inhibitory effects of tumoral IDO activity on CLDN18.2-CAR-T cell therapy, elucidating how tryptophan metabolites mediate this suppression. Furthermore, we reveal that the preconditioning chemotherapy drugs fludarabine and cyclophosphamide, commonly administered prior to CAR-T cell infusion to augment therapeutic efficacy, significantly reduce IDO expression in gastrointestinal tumors. These findings suggest a potential strategy to enhance the impact of CLDN18.2-CAR-T cell therapy, offering hope for patients with gastrointestinal cancers that are typically unresponsive to current treatments. This approach may pave the way for a new paradigm in overcoming the immunosuppressive barriers posed by gastrointestinal cancers, thereby revitalizing the potential of CAR-T cell therapies in oncology.

Materials and methods

Bioinformatics analysis

For our analysis, we sourced RNA sequencing data from 33 distinct tumor types within the project STAR from the TCGA database, accessible through the Genomic Data Commons (GDC) portal (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov). Utilizing bioinformatics tools, we meticulously curated and processed the data to extract gene expression levels in TPM (Transcripts Per Million) format. To ascertain the correlation between IDO1 expression levels and overall survival rates in stomach adenocarcinoma (STAD), we employed the statistical R packages "survival" and "survminer." Patient cohorts were stratified into "high" and "low" IDO1-expressing groups based on a predetermined expression threshold. Furthermore, we accessed the TCGA IDO1 expression database (https://portal.gdc.cancer.gov) to obtain and organize RNAseq data in TPM format, alongside relevant clinical data for gastric adenocarcinoma cases. The abundance of immune cells within the samples was estimated employing the R packages "ssGSEA" (single sample gene set enrichment analysis) and "CIBERSORT," leveraging the provided cellular markers. Only samples with a statistically significant p-value of less than 0.05 were advanced to subsequent analyses. Lastly, we conducted a Spearman correlation analysis to evaluate the relationship between IDO1 expression levels and immune cell abundance, utilizing the R package "corrplot" for a comprehensive visualization of the correlation matrix.

Cell lines

A gastric epithelial cell line, GES-1, seven gastric cancer (GC) cell lines (AGS, SGC7901, Hs746T, MGC-803, HGC-27, NCI-N87, MKN45) and pancreatic tumor cells (Panc02, KPC) were procured from the Chinese Academy of Sciences Cell Repertoire, Shanghai, China. Prior to experimentation, all cell lines underwent rigorous screening to ensure they were devoid of mycoplasma contamination. The cell lines were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 1640 medium, enriched with 10% FBS, 100 U/ml penicillin–streptomycin (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and were maintained in a controlled environment of a humidified incubator at 37 °C with an atmospheric composition of 5% CO2. The tumor antigen CLDN18.2 lentivirus was utilized to transfect the tumor cell lines AGS and Hs746T, resulting in the construction of modified cell lines AGS18.2 and Hs746T18.2. The AGS18.2 and Panc0218.2 cell lines were selected for transduction with a retroviral vector harboring a small hairpin RNA (shRNA) construct targeting human or mouse IDO1, and these modified cell lines were designated as AGS18.2-shIDO1 and Panc0218.2-shIDO1, respectively. A control group was also established by transducing cells with a scramble shRNA vector, termed AGS18.2-shCtrl. All vectors included a puromycin-resistance gene to facilitate selection. Post-transduction, cells were subjected to single cell cloning via the limiting dilution method to isolate and expand monoclonal cell populations. The mouse pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma cells PANC02-OVA overexpressing OVA were purchased from Wuhan Warner Bio Co., Ltd. and cultured according to the instructions provided.

Human samples

Patient samples were collected following a tissue-collection protocol approved by the institutional review board of Renji Hospital, affiliated with Shanghai Jiao Tong University. All patients provided written informed consent to participate in the study.

T cell isolation and CAR-T cell preparation

Human CD3+ T cells were isolated from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) using an autoMACS cell separation system in conjunction with human CD3 MicroBeads, as per the manufacturer's protocol (Miltenyi Biotec). The isolated T cells were resuspended to a final concentration of 1 × 106 cells/ml in a complete RPMI-1640 medium. This medium was fortified with 10% FBS, 100 U/ml penicillin–streptomycin (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Activation of CD3+ T cells was achieved using pre-coated (5 μg/ml) anti-CD3 (Novoprotein, Cat# GMP-A018)/CD28 (Novoprotein, Cat# GMP-A063) antibodies. Post-activation, the cells were cultured in the presence of 100 IU/mL recombinant human interleukin-2 (IL-2), procured from Beijing SL Pharmaceutical, Beijing, China. The activated T cells were infected with lentivirus containing CAR construct at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 3, and transduction rates were measured by flow cytometry at 48-h post-activation. Murine T cells were initially isolated from mouse spleens utilizing a specialized T cell isolation kit provided by STEMCELL Technologies. Subsequently, these cells were propagated in a tailored T cell culture medium composed of RPMI, supplemented with 10% FBS, 1 × NEAA, 1 × Sodium pyruvate, 1 × Insulin-Transferrin-Selenium, 1 × 2-mercaptoethanol and 40 IU/ml of mIL-2. Prior to activating the T cells, a T-75 flask was prepared by coating it with 10 mL of PBS containing anti-mouse CD3 (5 μg/mL) (BioLegend) and anti-mouse CD28 antibodies (5 μg/ml) (BioLegend), which was incubated at 4 °C overnight. Following this preparatory step, the T cells were cultured undisturbed for 24 h at 37 °C to induce activation. Retrovirus generation began with the use of PlateE-ECO cells. A total of 18 μg of plasmid DNA, consisting of the CAR gene-containing plasmid and the ECO packaging plasmid in a 3:2 ratio, was transfected into PlateE-ECO cells. Forty-eight hours after transfection, the retrovirus-enriched supernatant was collected and filtered through a 0.45 μm filter to remove cell debris. The filtered supernatant was then centrifuged at room temperature for 2 h to concentrate the virus onto Novonectin pre-coated plates. Activated CD3+ T cells were subsequently added to the virus-coated plates for transfection, which proceeded for 24 h. CAR expression in the total T cell population was assessed 48 h post-transduction using flow cytometry.

Western blot analysis

Cell lysates were prepared by lysing the cells and subsequently clarifying the lysates through high-speed centrifugation to remove cellular debris. The protein content of the supernatant was then resolved using SDS–polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE), with a 10% polyacrylamide gel to ensure adequate separation of proteins based on their molecular weight. For the transfer of resolved proteins onto a membrane, polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) membranes (Bio-Rad, Hercules, CA) were utilized. After the transfer process, the membranes were blocked to minimize non-specific binding and then incubated with a primary antibody specific for IDO1 (CY-15493R) to detect the protein of interest. To ensure equal protein loading and account for any variability in sample preparation, a secondary incubation with an anti-β-actin antibody (Cell Signaling Technology) was performed, serving as an internal loading control.

mRNA preparation and quantitative real-time RT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from cells using RNAiso Plus (TaKaRa Biotechnology, Dalian, China) following the manufacturer’s instructions. RNA concentration and quality were measured using a NanoDrop 2000 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA). Next, 1 μg of total RNA was reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA) using the PrimeScript™ RT Reagent Kit (TaKaRa) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Quantitative RT-PCR was then performed using SYBR Green I Master (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland) on a LightCycler 480 instrument (Roche) following the manufacturer's guidelines. The relative expression levels of target genes were calculated using the 2−ΔΔCT method and normalized to β-actin in each sample.

Cytotoxicity assays in vitro

To evaluate the cytotoxic effects of genetically CAR-T cells on different target cells, we performed coculture experiments at varying effector to target (E:T) ratios, specifically at 10:1, 5:1, 3:1, 1:1, and 0.5:1. This range of ratios was selected to comprehensively assess the dose–response relationship of CAR-T cell mediated cytotoxicity. After 8 h of coculture, the levels of lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) released into the supernatant were quantified as a measure of cell membrane integrity and cytotoxicity. The CytoTox 96 nonradioactive cytotoxicity assay kit (Promega, Madison, WI) was employed, following the protocol provided by the manufacturer. This colorimetric assay detects LDH released by damaged cells, with the absorbance read at 490 nm using a microplate reader. To monitor the effect of Kyn on CAR-T cell cytotoxicity in vitro, varying concentrations of Kyn were added to the cytotoxicity assay system. After an 8-h incubation of CAR-T cells with tumor cells, LDH release was measured to determine the cytotoxicity ratio. The IDO1 inhibitor 1-MT or epacadostat was purchased from Topscience and dissolved in DMSO. AGS18.2 and Hs746T18.2 cells were separately seeded into 12 well plates a day in advance. On the following day, cell culture medium was aspirated, and PBS was used to wash off unadhered cells before fresh medium was replaced. IDO1 inhibitor at a concentration of 10 μM were added and incubated for 48 h. Subsequently, CAR-T cells were introduced to assess tumor cell lysis, and the cytotoxicity ratio was monitored 8 h after CAR-T cell addition. For the long-term serial killing assay, AGS18.2 cells were stained with CFSE and seeded in 24-well plates at 5 × 105 cells/well in X-vivo medium supplemented with 5% FBS, the day before T cell seeding. CAR-T cells, sorted for 95% purity, were suspended in X-vivo medium with 5% FBS at a concentration of 5 × 105 cells/ml and then mixed at a 1:1 ratio with target cells in round 1. After 3 days, the co-cultures were thoroughly suspended by frequent pipetting, and the cell suspension was used for T cell counting and flow cytometry staining with human CD3. The remaining cell suspension was spun down, resuspended in T cell culture media, and transferred to AGS18.2-coated plates (5 × 105 cells/well) for continuous co-culture. At the same time, the supernatant after co-incubation was collected to detect IFN-γ. This process was repeated for a total of 4 rounds. To ensure the reliability and reproducibility of our findings, each experimental condition was conducted in triplicate, and the mean values were calculated to represent the results. The supernatant was harvested to measure the concentration of tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α) and IFN-γ using the ELISA kit.

ELISA (enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay)

The concentrations of IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2, Kynurenine, and tryptophan (Trp) were determined in the supernatants, serum, or cell lysates. Each cytokine and analyte was assessed using a specific ELISA kit, all procured from CUSABIO, following the manufacturer's detailed instructions. The ELISA procedure involves the capture of target cytokines by specific antibodies coated on the wells of a microplate, followed by the addition of a detection antibody and a colorimetric substrate. The absorbance, which is proportional to the concentration of cytokines present, was measured at a wavelength of 450 nm using a Molecular Devices Multifilter F5 plate reader. This high-throughput instrument allows for the accurate and simultaneous quantification of multiple cytokines in the samples.

Isolation of tumor-infiltrating cells

Tumor tissues were first minced using sterile scissors and then subjected to enzymatic digestion to facilitate cell separation. Specifically, the minced samples were incubated with a solution containing collagenase IV (0.5 mg/ml, C5138-5G, Sigma) and DNase I (0.1 mg/ml, DN25-1G, Sigma) in RPMI medium at a temperature of 37 °C for 30 min. This step aids in the breakdown of the extracellular matrix and the release of tumor-infiltrating cells. Following enzymatic treatment, the samples were processed through repeated pipetting to create a homogeneous cell suspension. This suspension was then filtered using a 70-μm nylon mesh (Sangon) to obtain a single-cell suspension free from tissue fragments and cell clumps. Red blood cells were lysed using ACK lysis buffer, followed by two washes in PBS containing 1% FBS and 0.5 mM EDTA. The cell mixture was stained with Fixable Viability Dye eFluor 780 (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Invitrogen, cat# 65–086514) and fluorescence-conjugated antibodies. Prior to flow cytometry analysis, the cells were washed again to ensure removal of any residual lysis buffer and resuspended in MACS buffer (PBS supplemented with 2 mM EDTA and 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA)). This buffer is used to maintain cell viability and prevent non-specific antibody binding. The cells were then incubated with specific antibodies for flow cytometry analysis.

Flow cytometry and intracellular cytokine staining

For phenotypic and functional analysis, antibodies were sourced from BioLegend and used according to the manufacturer's recommendations. A total of 5 × 105 cells were collected by centrifugation, washed twice with PBS to remove any traces of serum or cellular debris, and then resuspended for staining. The cell suspension was incubated with a panel of fluorescence-conjugated antibodies for 20 min in the dark to ensure specific binding and minimize non-specific fluorescence. For the assessment of intracellular cytokines, CAR-T cells was stimulated with a combination of brefeldin (1 µg/mL; BioLegend), phorbol 12-myristate 13-acetate (PMA, 10 ng/mL; Sigma-Aldrich), and ionomycin (5 ng/mL; Sigma-Aldrich) for a period of 10 h. This cocktail of stimulants promotes the production of cytokines within the cells. Meanwhile, a cocktail including 1.5 μL BD GolgiStop and 1 μL BD Golgiplug were added into culture medium following standard cytokine staining protocols. Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) were directly harvested from mouse tumors and used for analysis. Tumor CAR-T cells were then surface-stained with antibodies against CD45, CD3 and CLDN18.2 CAR(VHH +) for 20 min on ice in the dark, followed by a fixative step involving the addition of 4% formalin to cross-link the cellular components. After washing, the cells were permeabilized using a permeabilization washing buffer, allowing for the intracellular staining with antibodies specific for IFN-γ, IL-2, and TNF-α, for an additional 20 min. The stained cells were then analyzed on a FACS Canto II flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, Franklin Lakes, NJ), which provides multiparameter data on the expression of cell surface and intracellular markers. To analyze cell viability, activated CAR-T cells treated with different drugs (CTX, FA, CTX + FA, Kyn) for 24 h. Cells were stained with Annexin-V and Propidium iodide (PI) and analyzed by flow cytometry. The percentage of live cells was determined by the population negative for both Annexin-V and PI.

Animals study in vivo

All animal procedures were conducted in strict compliance with the protocols and guidelines established by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) for Shanghai model organisms center. NCG mice were subcutaneously implanted with 5 × 106 AGS18.2/AGS18.2-shIDO1/3 × 106 Hs746T18.2 on the right flank. Upon reaching a tumor volume of 100 mm3, the mice were randomly assigned to different groups, each comprising five mice (n = 5). The subcutaneous tumor model was established by subcutaneously injection of 2.5 × 106 Panc02-OVA / Panc0218.2 / Panc0218.2-shIOD1 cells on the right flank of mice. The subcutaneous KPC tumor model was established by subcutaneously injection of 5 × 106 KPC tumor cells on the right flank of mice. IDO1 inhibitors or DMSO were administered at a dose of 50 mg/kg/day through subcutaneous injection every three days. CAR-T cells were injected at a dosage of 5 × 106 cells per mouse via the tail vein. Kyn was also administered intraperitoneally at a dosage of 50 ug per mouse, following the same intermittent schedule of once every three days. AGS18.2 and Hs746T18.2 were incubated for 24 h with Cyclophosphamide (2 ug/mL), Fludarabine (20 uM), or both. Cells were incubated with or without IFN-γ (50 U/mL) for 24 h before proteins were extracted. Throughout the study, we continuously monitored the weight changes, mental state, tumor size, and survival time of the mice. Additionally, blood was collected weekly to assess circulating CAR-T cell levels. At the experiment's endpoint, tumor tissues were harvested to prepare homogenates, and CAR-T cells were isolated and sorted for flow cytometric analysis of specific markers. The supernatant was retained for ELISA to measure levels of IFN-γ, TNF-α, IL-2, Kyn, and Trp. Tumor volumes were estimated using the formula V = (π/6) × length × width2, with length and width representing the tumor's longest and shortest dimensions, respectively. Mouse body weight and tumor volume were measured twice a week. The experimental endpoint was defined by a body weight loss exceeding 20% of the initial weight, loss of mobility, or the development of ulceration in the tumor for mice in the control groups, at which point the mice were humanely euthanized.

Statistical analysis

Experimental data are presented as the mean with standard deviation (SD). For in vitro experiments involving two-sample comparisons, statistical significance was determined using an unpaired two-tailed Student’s t-test. In contrast, data from in vivo animal studies were analyzed using either a one-way or two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with a Bonferroni post hoc test to adjust for multiple comparisons. All statistical computations were performed using SPSS Statistics version 17.0 and GraphPad Prism 5.0 software. The statistical tests were conducted in a two-sided manner, and a P value of less than 0.05 was set as the threshold for statistical significance.

Results

IDO1 accumulation in gastrointestinal tumor may restrict the efficiency of CAR-T cell therapy

In our comprehensive analysis, we investigated the expression patterns of IDO1 across a spectrum of gastrointestinal tumors, including breast, renal clear cell carcinoma, hepatic, and STAD (Fig. 1A). The consistently high IDO1 expression observed across these malignancies, especially in STAD, suggests a potentially significant role in tumor biology, which warrants a detailed investigation into its functional impact on STAD progression and therapy resistance. Notably, IDO1 expression was significantly higher at tumor sites than in adjacent normal tissues (Fig. 1B, C), suggesting a correlation between IDO1 overexpression and tumorigenesis. Utilizing data from the Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), we correlated IDO1 expression levels with the pathological stage and overall survival outcomes in STAD patients to elucidate its potential as a prognostic indicator. IDO1 expression levels were observed to be positively correlated with the stage of disease at diagnosis (Fig. 1D). It also revealed that patients exhibiting elevated IDO1 expression levels had a diminished average overall survival rate compared to those with reduced IDO1 expression (Fig. 1E). Elevated IDO1 expression was significantly correlated with adverse clinical outcomes in patients with STAD, indicating a potential role for IDO1 in tumor progression and patient prognosis. Analysis of TCGA data demonstrating a significant positive correlation between IDO1 and infiltration of Treg cells (Fig. 1F). This association implies that IDO1 pathways may function as negative feedback mechanisms within the tumor microenvironment.

The impact of IDO1 expression on CAR-T cell-induced cytotoxicity was subsequently evaluated. Initially, IDO1 expression was quantified in a gastric epithelial cell line (GES-1) and several stomach adenocarcinoma cell lines (AGS, Hs746T, SGC7901, MGC803, HGC27, NCI-N87, and MKN45) using Western blot and quantitative reverse transcription polymerase chain reaction (RT-PCR). IDO1 expression is nearly absent in non-neoplastic GES-1 cells. In contrast, nearly all gastric tumor cells expressed IDO1, with the highest levels observed in the AGS cell line (Fig. 2A and S1A). Thus, AGS was selected as the primary cellular model for this investigation. To evaluate the impact of IDO1 knockdown in tumors on the efficacy of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell therapy, AGS cells overexpressing CLDN18.2 (AGS18.2) were generated in vitro, and monoclonal cell lines were subsequently isolated (Figure S1B). Furthermore, treatment with IFN-γ effectively upregulated IDO1 in AGS18.2 cells, while GES-1 cells did not respond to IFN-γ stimulation (Fig. 2A and S1C). Treatment with small hairpin RNA (shRNA) and CRISPR-Cas9 effectively reduced IDO1 mRNA and protein levels, with the shIDO1-1 construct and CRISPR-Cas9 showing the highest knockout efficiency (Fig. 2A and Figure S1D). IDO1 knockdown or knockout in the AGS18.2 cell line did not affect cell growth (Figure S1E). In vitro cytotoxicity assays showed that CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells selectively lysed AGS18.2 cells, with minimal activity against the parental AGS cell line. In contrast, Untransduced T cells (UTD) exhibited no significant lytic activity against any of the target cells tested (Figure S1F). Notably, the cytotoxic capacity of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells was significantly enhanced in AGS18.2 cells with IDO1 knockdown (AGS18.2-shIDO1) compared to control cells (Fig. 2B). To assess the impact of tumoral IDO1 on the efficacy of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell therapy, NCG mice were inoculated with AGS18.2 cells, either transduced with control shRNA (AGS18.2-shCtrl) or shRNA targeting IDO1 (AGS18.2-shIDO1) (Fig. 2C). After confirming tumor establishment ten days post-inoculation, either UTD or CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells were administered intravenously. In AGS18.2 cells, CLDN18.2 CAR-T treatment alone was insufficient to inhibit tumor progression effectively. However, when IDO1 was knocked down in AGS18.2 cells, CAR-T therapy significantly suppressed tumor progression (Fig. 2D). The AGS18.2-shIDO1 group treated with CAR-T therapy exhibited a significant delay in disease progression compared to other groups (Fig. 2E). Peripheral blood from the AGS18.2-shIDO1 group treated with CAR-T contained a markedly higher frequency of CAR-T cells, corroborating our experimental findings (Fig. 2F). Additionally, CAR-T cells infiltrating AGS18.2-shIDO1 tumors displayed reduced expression of exhaustion markers (PD-1, TIM3, LAG3) compared to CAR-T cells infiltrating AGS18.2-shCtrl tumors (Fig. 2G). Conversely, activation markers (CD25, CD69), along with IFN-γ, TNF-α, and Ki67, were elevated in the AGS18.2-shIDO1 group compared to the AGS18.2-shCtrl group (Fig. 2H, I). In conclusion, IDO1 expression is associated with tumor progression and poor prognosis in gastrointestinal tumors, particularly stomach adenocarcinoma. Targeting IDO1 significantly enhances the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapy, indicating its potential as a therapeutic target to improve patient outcomes.

IDO1 inhibits CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell function via its metabolite Kyn

IDO1 has been extensively studied for its pivotal role in the metabolism of Trp to Kyn, a process known to inhibit T cell function. However, the effects of Kyn on CAR-T cells have not yet been established. The AGS18.2 cell line efficiently converted Trp to Kyn, reaching 16.46 ± 1.82 μM within 48 h, while the AGS18.2-shIDO1 line produced only 2.82 ± 0.69 μM. IDOi 1-MT reduced Kyn to 3.23 ± 0.85 μM, with Trp levels preserved at 18.14 ± 1.07 μM (Supplementary Fig. 2A). IDOi significantly and dose-dependently inhibited the production of Kyn in IFN-γ-stimulated AGS18.2 cells, with a calculated IC50 value of 5.85 nM for Kyn. Meanwhile, the viability of CAR-T cells decreased under Kyn stimulation, with an IC50 value of 15.16 μM (Supplementary Fig. 2B-2C).

CAR-T cells were subsequently incubated with Kyn, and even at low concentrations (5 μM), Kyn significantly suppressed the expression of key functional cytokines, including IFN-γ and IL-2, in CAR-T cells (Fig. 3A, B). Conversely, Kyn significantly upregulated the expression of exhaustion markers PD-1, Tim-3, and LAG-3 in CAR-T cells (Fig. 3C). Following exposure to Kyn, CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells were cocultured with AGS18.2 tumor cells for 8 h. Flow cytometric analysis demonstrated that Kyn significantly inhibited the cytotoxicity of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 3D). The secretion of IFN-γ decreased, correlating with the attenuated cytotoxic function (Fig. 3E). Kyn was also associated with increased CAR-T apoptosis, as assessed by Annexin-V and PI staining. Percent positive cells in 0, 2.5, 5, and 10 μM Kyn were 8.24 ± 2.51%, 18.5 ± 4.33%, 23.09 ± 1.58%, and 46.86 ± 6.27%, respectively (Fig. 3F).

Next, in vivo experiments demonstrated that mice receiving Kyn injections showed a marked reduction in the tumor-inhibitory effects of CAR-T cells (Fig. 3G–I). On day 25 post-treatment, analysis of tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells in the Kyn group revealed a significant reduction in IFN-γ and IL-2 expression, indicating a suppressed anti-tumor immune response within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 3J). Concurrently, CAR-T cell exhaustion markers, including PD-1, TIM-3, and LAG-3, were notably upregulated following Kyn treatment (Fig. 3K). The combination of CAR-T cells and Kyn significantly diminished the tumor-inhibitory efficacy of CAR-T therapy, attributed to the increased Kyn concentration within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. 3L). Our results indicate that kynurenine significantly reduces CAR-T cell cytotoxicity and increases apoptosis both in vitro and in vivo. These findings highlight the potential of targeting IDO1 and kynurenine metabolism to enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapies in cancer treatment.

IDO1 inhibitor mitigate the inhibitory effects of tryptophan metabolites

Unlike 1-MT, which broadly inhibits both IDO1 and IDO2, epacadostat (IDO1i) is a selective inhibitor specifically designed to bind to the active site of IDO1, forming a coordinate covalent bond with ferrous iron in the heme group. To assess the efficacy of this inhibition, Kyn production in cells cultured with and without epacadostat was quantified by ELISA. Notably, the epacadostat group exhibited the most pronounced suppression of Kyn production (Fig. 4A and Figure S3A). Proliferation of AGS18.2 cells was not significantly affected by IDO1i treatment (Fig. 4B). CAR-T cells exhibited enhanced cytotoxicity when cocultured with AGS18.2/HS746T18.2 tumor cells pretreated with IDO1i. Additionally, the enhancement of CAR-T efficacy by IDO1i is more effective in tumor-suppressive microenvironments (Fig. 4C, S3C). Furthermore, pre-treatment of tumor cells with IDO1i led to increased IFN-γ release from CAR-T cells (Fig. 4D). Kyn levels in the supernatant of the in vitro cytotoxicity assay revealed that Kyn concentrations were significantly lower in the IDO1i group compared to the control group (Figure S3D).

To evaluate the therapeutic efficacy of IDO1i in conjunction with CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell treatment, mice were subcutaneously injected with AGS18.2 cells and subsequently treated with different therapeutic regimens, including UTD, UTD combined with IDO1 inhibitor (UTD + IDO1i), CAR-T cells alone, and CAR-T cells combined with IDO1 inhibitor (CAR-T + IDO1i). IDO1i was injected intraperitoneally 24 h before CAR-T cells were injected, and injections were administered every three days during the observation period (Fig. 4E). In the CAR-T + IDO1i group, tumor growth was significantly suppressed, and the survival rate of the mice was enhanced (Fig. 4F, G). Notably, IDO1i did not significantly affect the cytotoxic efficacy of UTD, but it substantially improved the cytotoxic activity of CAR-T cells. After combination treatment with IDO1 inhibitors, tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells exhibited significantly increased expression of IFN-γ, TNF-α, and CD107a (Fig. 4H). Additionally, the proliferative ability of CAR-T cells was enhanced, as indicated by higher Ki67 levels (Fig. 4H). Meanwhile, IDO1 inhibitors decreased the expression of exhaustion markers such as PD-1, TIM-3, and LAG-3 on tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells, while the release of IFN-γ and TNF-α was increased (Fig. 4I and S3F). The analysis of Kyn and Trp concentrations in the tumor microenvironment revealed that IDO1i significantly reduced the Kyn/Trp ratio in the tumor (Figures S3E). Consequently, the concurrent application of IDO1i with CAR-T cell therapy has proven to be more efficacious than CAR-T cell therapy alone, significantly enhancing CAR-T cell functionality in the treatment of gastrointestinal tumors. The improvement in CAR-T cell function is primarily characterized by increased expression of functional molecules and reduced expression of inhibitory molecules. Similar results were observed in the HS746T18.2 tumor model (Fig. 4J–L and S3G).

To further test this concept, the impact of IDO1i on OT-I cell-mediated antitumor activities was evaluated. C57BL/6 mice were implanted with Panc02-OVA tumor cells (high IDO1 expression) and treated with different regimens over 25 days (Fig. 4M and S3H). The combination therapy of OT-1 cells and IDO1i significantly reduced tumor volume compared to the control group, while individual treatments were less effective (Fig. 4N). Combination therapy also enhanced OT-1 cell infiltration into the tumor and increased their frequency in peripheral blood (Fig. 4O). Furthermore, combination therapy resulted in higher levels of IFN-γ, TNF-α, and CD107a in tumor-infiltrating OT-1 cells, indicating improved cytotoxic and immunomodulatory functions (Fig. 4P, Q). These findings suggest a synergistic effect between OT-1 cell therapy and IDO1i in enhancing antitumor responses. In conclusion, our findings demonstrate that combining IDO1i with CAR-T cell therapy significantly enhances anti-tumor efficacy, characterized by increased cytotoxic cytokine production and reduced exhaustion markers in tumor-infiltrating CAR-T cells. Additionally, the synergistic effect observed with OT-1 cell therapy further underscores the potential of IDO1 inhibition to improve therapeutic outcomes in gastrointestinal tumors.

Enhanced antitumor efficacy of CAR-T with IDO1 inhibition demonstrated in pancreatic cancer mouse models

In continuation of our investigation into the efficacy of the combination therapy with CAR-T cells and IDO1 inhibitors in gastrointestinal tumors, we conducted further validation using pancreatic cancer cell lines that express IDO1(Figure S3E). Two models of pancreatic cancer were established: Panc02-CLDN18.2 (Panc0218.2), a Panc02 cell line engineered to express the tumor-associated antigen CLDN18.2, and KPC, a cell line inherently expressing CLDN18.2 (Figures S4A, S4B).

Upon reaching a tumor volume of approximately 100 mm3, Panc0218.2/Panc0218.2-shIDO1 tumor-bearing mice were randomly assigned to groups. Mice were then injected with IDO1i, CAR-T, or CAR-T + IDO1i (Fig. 5A). Panc0218.2 mice treated with CAR-T + IDO1i or Panc0218.2-shIDO1 tumor-bearing mice treated with CAR-T significantly inhibited tumor growth and prolonged survival, with the effect being most significant in the Panc0218.2-shIDO1 mice treated with CAR-T + IDO1i (Fig. 5B, C). Analysis of Trp and Kyn levels in mouse blood 20 days post-CAR-T injection revealed that IDO1i or knockdown of IDO1 expression in tumor cells slightly decreased plasma Kyn content while elevating Trp concentration (Fig. 5D). Moreover, IFN-γ secretion in the CAR-T combined with IDO1i group in Panc0218.2-shIDO1 tumor-bearing mice were notably higher than in other groups, consistent with observed in vivo efficacy (Fig. 5E). Examination of CD4+T and CD8+T cell infiltration within tumors indicated that CAR-T combined with IDO1i increased T cell infiltration, particularly in the Panc0218.2-shIDO1 group (Fig. 5F). Additionally, IDO1i or knockdown of IDO1 reduced Treg infiltration in tumors, with Treg infiltration in the CAR-T combined with IDO1i group in Panc0218.2-shIDO1 mice being even lower (Fig. 5G). Notably, the CD8/Treg cell ratio in the CAR-T combined with IDO1i group in the Panc0218.2-shIDO1 mice was significantly elevated compared to other groups, indicating a sustained, robust antitumor immunity induced by CAR-T + IDO1i in Panc0218.2-shIDO1 tumor-bearing mice (Fig. 5H).

Furthermore, to demonstrate the immune modulation effects and antitumor performance of IDO1i, a KPC tumor model was established in C57BL/6 mice (Fig. 5I). Remarkably, the combination of mCAR-T and IDO1i treatment significantly suppressed tumor growth and improved survival rates compared to IDO1i or mCAR-T treatment alone (Fig. 5J, K). Subsequently, antitumor immunity was evaluated at the late treatment stage (20 days post-treatment). In the mCAR-T + IDO1i group, tumor-infiltrating IFN-γ+CD8+T cells, GranzymeB+CD8+T cells, and TNF-α+CD8+T cells increased by 5.38-, 4.0-, and 4.29-fold, respectively, compared to their counterparts (Fig. 5L–N). To evaluate the potential effects of long-term IDO1 inhibitor use on CAR-T therapy, continuous killing experiments were conducted in vitro. After four rounds of CAR-T cell-mediated killing, the combination treatment group, consisting of CAR-T cells and IDO1 inhibitors, exhibited significantly enhanced tumor-killing efficacy and improved CAR-T cell expansion compared to the group treated with CAR-T cells alone (Figure S4C-S4F). In vivo experiments were conducted using Panc02-OVA tumor-bearing mice treated with daily injections of an IDO1 inhibitor combined with CD45.1⁺ OT-1 cells (Figure S4G). The results demonstrated that the combined treatment exhibited a significantly enhanced anti-tumor effect compared to treatment with OT-1 cells alone (Figure S4H). Additionally, analysis of CD45.2⁺ T cells in the mice revealed that the IDO1 inhibitor group showed a significant increase in endogenous CD45.2⁺ T cells within the tumor (Figure S4I). Furthermore, T cell activation levels were enhanced, while exhaustion-related signals were markedly reduced (Figure S4J-S4L). These findings suggest that IDO1 inhibitors not only improve the anti-tumor efficacy of OT-1 cells but also play a critical role in enhancing the activation and function of endogenous T cells. In conclusion, our results demonstrate that this combinatorial approach not only inhibits tumor growth and prolongs survival but also improves immune cell infiltration and activity within the tumor microenvironment. Enhanced levels of IFN-γ and increased CD8/Treg ratios further underscore the potential of IDO1 inhibition to potentiate CAR-T cell function, offering a promising strategy for improving outcomes in pancreatic cancer treatment.

Fludarabine and cyclophosphamide downregulate IDO expression

Fludarabine and cyclophosphamide (FluCy), commonly used clinically prior to CAR-T cell infusion, are hypothesized to enhance CAR-T cell efficacy. Given fludarabine's established ability to inhibit IDO1 expression in lymphoma cells, its impact on AGS18.2 cell lines following exposure to FluCy was investigated. Results indicated that fludarabine significantly reduced IDO1 expression in AGS18.2 cells, with a more pronounced effect observed when combined with cyclophosphamide, whereas mafosfamide alone had no significant effect (Fig. 6A). Notably, IFN-γ failed to upregulate IDO1 in AGS18.2 cells post-treatment with fludarabine or FluCy. A similar suppression of IFN-γ-induced IDO1 activity was observed in Hs746T18.2 cells (Fig. 6B). Importantly, FluCy treatment did not compromise AGS18.2 cell viability (Fig. 6C). Cyclophosphamide alone had little effect on tryptophan metabolism. However, fludarabine significantly reduced the conversion of tryptophan to kynurenine. The combination of cyclophosphamide and fludarabine exhibited a synergistic inhibitory effect on tryptophan metabolism (Fig. 6D).

Subsequently, the therapeutic benefits of FluCy administration prior to CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell infusion were assessed in an AGS18.2 tumor model (Fig. 6E). Our findings demonstrated that pretreatment with FluCy significantly boosted the antitumor activity of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells against AGS18.2 tumors (Fig. 6F). Meanwhile, the FluCy + CLDN18.2 CAR-T cell group showed a significant increase in CAR-T cells in blood circulation (Fig. 6G). Additionally, the FluCy + CAR-T group exhibited notably elevated secretion of IL-2, TNF-α, and IFN-γ compared to other groups (Fig. 6H). FluCy also markedly enhanced CAR-T cell infiltration within tumors (Fig. 6I). Moreover, the FluCy + CLDN18.2 CAR-T group notably increased the quality of CLDN18.2 CAR-T cells within tumors, with CD4+IFN-γ+ and CD8+IFN-γ+ significantly elevated compared to control groups (Fig. 6J, K). To further explore whether the enhancement of CAR-T cell efficacy by Flu/Cy is mediated through the inhibition of IDO1 activity, we conducted an experiment with exogenous KYN supplementation. The results showed that the tumor suppression effect of CAR-T cells combined with IDO1i was significantly reversed upon KYN supplementation. Furthermore, IDO1i proved to be substantially more effective than Flu/Cy in inhibiting IDO1 activity, resulting in a correspondingly stronger tumor inhibition effect (Figures S5A-S5E).

Our findings demonstrate that FluCy pretreatment significantly improves CAR-T cell-mediated antitumor activity, increases CAR-T cell circulation, and elevates the secretion of key cytokines. Additionally, this combination therapy promotes greater CAR-T cell infiltration and functional quality within tumors, highlighting its potential to enhance therapeutic outcomes in cancer treatment and serving as a guiding strategy for clinical CAR-T therapy.

IDO1/Kyn promoted CAR-T cell exhaustion by upregulating TOX expression

To investigate how Kyn upregulates PD-1 expression, Kyn was directly added to CAR-T cells. Notably, CAR-T cells exhibited a significant, dose-dependent upregulation of PD-1 expression (Fig. 7A). It was also found that the Kyn-induced increase in PD-1⁺ CAR-T cells was attributed to the conversion of PD-1⁻ to PD-1⁺ CAR-T cells, rather than the depletion of PD-1⁻ CAR-T cells or the expansion of PD-1⁺ CAR-T cells (Fig. 7B). Next, the mechanism by which Kyn upregulates PD-1 expression in T cells was explored. The aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), a cytoplasmic transcription factor, can be activated by endogenous metabolites such as Kyn and plays an essential role in regulating gene expression. we found that Kyn increased AhR expression in both resting and active T cells (Fig. 7C). Meanwhile, the expression of TOX, NR4A, and NFAT in CAR-T cells after KYN stimulation was detected. We found that TOX expression was predominantly upregulated following KYN stimulation (Fig. 7D). Conversely, inhibition of AhR activity using 3,4'-dimethoxyflavone (DMF) prevented the Kyn-dependent induction of PD-1 expression (Fig. 7E). Next, we investigated whether enhanced IDO1/Kyn activity promotes CAR-T-cell exhaustion by regulating the activity of TOX, a key inducer of the canonical features of CAR-T-cell exhaustion. IDO1 knockdown in AGS18.2 cells significantly reduced TOX mRNA levels in CAR-T cells isolated from AGS18.2-bearing mice (Fig. 7F). Consistently, TOX expression was significantly downregulated on the surface of CAR-T cells isolated from IDO1 shRNA-treated tumor tissues compared to those from negative control tissues (Fig. 7G). CAR-T cells were treated with varying concentrations of Kyn, and the TOX mRNA level was found to be significantly upregulated in a concentration-dependent manner (Fig. 7H). Kyn increased TOX expression in CAR-T cells in a concentration- and time-dependent manner (Fig. 7I). To further elucidate the relationship between Kyn and TOX in CAR-T cell exhaustion, TOX expression was knocked down using siRNA, and the knockdown efficiency was confirmed by flow cytometry (Fig. 7J). PD-1 expressions were upregulated in CAR-T cells treated with Kyn compared to those treated with PBS in vitro. However, this effects were reversed following the knockdown of TOX expression. These findings indicate that TOX serves as a critical regulator of Kyn-induced CAR-T cell exhaustion (Fig. 7K).

Discussion

The role of IDO1 in limiting the effectiveness of immunotherapy has been increasingly recognized. Rather than directly affecting cancer cells, IDO1 exerts its influence by significantly suppressing the immune system, particularly by inhibiting T cell activity. In this study, elevated IDO1 expression was found to be associated with immune infiltration and poor prognosis in patients with gastrointestinal cancers. This suggests that the IDO1 pathway may function as a critical negative feedback mechanism. Given its immunosuppressive effects, targeting IDO1 could offer therapeutic benefits for patients with a pre-existing T cell-inflamed tumor microenvironment. Recently, CAR-T cell therapy has shown great promise in treating hematologic cancers, inspiring efforts to extend its application to gastrointestinal tumors. CLDN18.2 is a stomach-specific isoform of claudin-18 and is considered an ideal therapeutic target for the treatment of advanced gastric cancer and pancreatic adenocarcinoma due to its widespread expression in both primary and metastatic tumors, while being minimally expressed in normal tissues. However, the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapy is still limited by the suppressive tumor microenvironment. Immunosuppressive factors within this environment, including IDO1, present significant challenges to the effectiveness of CAR-T therapies in these cancers. Therefore, blocking IDO1 could potentially enhance CAR-T cell-mediated cytotoxicity against gastrointestinal tumors.

The present study provides compelling evidence that IDO1 plays a pivotal role in modulating the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapy in gastrointestinal tumors, specifically stomach adenocarcinoma. Our findings highlight the negative regulatory function of IDO1 within the TME and underscore its potential as a therapeutic target to enhance CAR-T cell cytotoxicity.

Tumor cells escape immune surveillance through various mechanisms, such as the inhibition of T cell activation via checkpoint pathways and the upregulation of IDO1, which catalyzes the degradation of tryptophan in the kynurenine pathway. Analysis of IDO1 expression across various gastrointestinal tumors revealed a strong correlation with poor clinical outcomes, particularly in STAD. This aligns with previous studies that demonstrate IDO1 overexpression contributes to an immunosuppressive TME and resistance to immunotherapy. Our findings also suggest that the IDO1 inhibitor alone does not effectively inhibit the growth of gastrointestinal cancers. Existing studies have demonstrated that IDO1 inhibitors show greater potential when used in combination with other therapies, rather than as a monotherapy, in cancer treatment. Therefore, combining CAR-T therapy with IDO1 inhibitors is a promising and necessary approach.

In order to further explore the efficacy and underlying mechanisms of CAR-T combined with IDO1 inhibitors, we investigated the impact of kynurenine. We found that kynurenine, a metabolite produced by IDO1, significantly impairs CAR-T cell cytotoxicity and promotes apoptosis. This is consistent with the established role of kynurenine in inducing CAR-T cell anergy exhaustion, which ultimately suppresses effective antitumor responses. At the same time, we discovered that the activation of IDO1 through AHR led to the expression of TOX in CAR-T cells, resulting in CAR-T cell exhaustion and significantly diminishing the efficacy of CAR-T therapy.

Importantly, the use of the IDO1 inhibitor 1-methyltryptophan and epacadostat to suppress kynurenine production restored CAR-T cell therapeutic efficacy, demonstrating that IDO1 inhibition could overcome metabolic suppression in the TME. Additionally, our study underscores the synergistic effect of preconditioning chemotherapy drugs like fludarabine and cyclophosphamide, which are commonly administered prior to CAR-T therapy. The FluCy chemotherapy protocol effectively downregulated IDO1 expression in gastric and pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma cells, significantly enhancing the in vivo antitumor activity of CLDN18.2-CAR-T cells. This finding suggests that existing chemotherapeutic regimens could serve as a valuable strategy to pretreat tumors, potentially enhancing the efficacy of CAR-T therapies in gastrointestinal cancers and offering a promising approach to improve treatment outcomes.

These findings are particularly relevant given the challenges CAR-T cell therapy faces in gastrointestinal tumors, which are more resistant to treatment compared to hematological malignancies. The immunosuppressive TME in gastrointestinal tumors acts as a major barrier to effective CAR-T cell function. Our results suggest that targeting IDO1, a key mediator of immunosuppression, could be a promising strategy to enhance the clinical efficacy of CAR-T therapy in gastrointestinal tumors. However, there are limitations to this study. The optimal dosing and timing of IDO1i in combination with CAR-T therapy require further investigation. Additionally, patient variability in response to IDO1 inhibition must be considered, emphasizing the need for personalized approaches. Moreover, while the preclinical models used in this study are promising, translating these findings into clinical practice requires rigorous testing in human trials.

In conclusion, our study delineates the critical role of IDO1 in CAR-T cell therapy and demonstrates that combining IDO1 inhibitors significantly enhances the antitumor effects of CAR-T cells. This discovery opens new avenues for improving CAR-T therapy in gastrointestinal tumors and underscores the potential of combining IDO1i with CAR-T cells in clinical regimens. Future research should focus on translating these findings into clinical settings, with the ultimate goal of improving outcomes for patients with gastrointestinal tumors that are resistant to current therapies.
