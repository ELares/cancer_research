# Targeting cancer epigenetics with CRISPR-dCAS9: Principles and prospects.

## Abstract

Cancer therapeutics is an ever-evolving field due to incessant demands for effective and precise treatment options. Over the last few decades, cancer treatment strategies have shifted somewhat from surgery to targeted precision medicine. CRISPR-dCas9 is an emerging version of precision cancer therapy that has been adapted from the prokaryotic CRISPR-Cas system. Once ligated to epigenetic effectors (EE), CRISPR-dCas9 can function as an epigenetic editing tool and CRISPR-dCas9-EE complexes could be exploited to alter cancerous epigenetic features associated with different cancer hallmarks. In this article, we discuss the rationale of epigenetic editing as a therapeutic strategy against cancer. We also outline how sgRNA-dCas9 was derived from the CRISPR-Cas system. In addition, the current status of sgRNA-dCas9 use (in vivo and in vitro) in cancer is updated with a molecular illustration of CRISPR-dCas9-mediated epigenetic and transcriptional modulation. As sgRNA-dCas9 is still at the developmental phase, challenges are inherent to its use. We evaluate major challenges in targeting cancer with sgRNA-dCas9 such as off-target effects, lack of sgRNA designing rubrics, target site selection dilemmas and deficient sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems. Finally, we appraise the sgRNA-dCas9 as a prospective cancer therapeutic by summarizing ongoing improvements of sgRNA-dCas9 methodology.

Introduction

Cancer is one of the major causes of morbidity and mortality from non-communicable disease in the 21st century. According to global cancer statistics 2018, one in every five to six individuals has the chance of developing cancer during their lifetime. Following similar statistics to morbidity, cancer mortality leads to one in every 6 deaths globally. Despite this, however, cancer survivability improved gradually during the last few decades especially in developed countries. Interestingly, an apparent shift and improvement in available cancer treatment options were observed during the same tenure. Admittedly, growing cancer survivability could be attributed to advances in therapeutic options against cancer along with earlier diagnosis.

Tracking the history of cancer treatment suggests surgical therapy as the earliest treatment option which was followed by radiation therapy. Surgical and radiation therapy were mostly suitable against nonmetastatic localized cancers. The systemic treatments of cancer were first introduced around 1940 through hormonal therapy, antimetabolites and chemotherapeutics intended to treat both nonmetastatic and metastatic cancers. The advent of targeted therapeutics such as selective kinase inhibitors and monoclonal antibodies has revolutionized cancer treatment effectiveness and has improved cancer survival rates. Furthermore, sophisticated diagnostic technologies such as next generation sequencing have introduced the notion of personalized cancer therapy that has shaped the development of advanced cancer treatment options including gene therapy and CAR-T cell therapy. Of late, we have seen the emergence of CRISPR-Cas9, adopted from the prokaryotic CRISPR-CAS system, as one among the latest versions of cancer therapy that are showing huge promise toward precision cancer therapy.

CRISPR-Cas is a genome memory system that occurs naturally in diverse bacteria and archaea. When present in a natural host, CRISPR-Cas confers selective immune resistance against foreign genetic material of viral or plasmid origin. The CRISPR-Cas9 system is composed of a CRISPR guide and a Cas9 endonuclease. Once endonuclease activity of Cas9 proteins is ceased through loss-of-function mutagenesis (dCas9), the CRISPR-dCas9 system could serve as a customizable genome interacting device that can bind to DNA sites guided by CRISPRs. Recently, a variety of epigenetic modulators have been linked to dCas9 without interrupting their genome interacting ability. With suitable epigenetic effectors, dCas9-effector fusion proteins may exert a significant epigenetic impact on surrounding cis/trans regulator elements upon binding to a target locus.

Epigenetic dysregulation is a feature common to cancer besides a genetic anomaly. While cancer-associated genetic alterations are less readily tunable, epigenetic modifications can be reversed. Such reversibility of epigenetic alterations opens a new avenue to targeted cancer therapeutics often termed as epigenetic editing. In the case of epigenetic editing, dCas-effector fusion proteins could be utilized as a molecular machine to edit an abrogated epigenetic mark. Thus, the CRISPR-dCas system, mechanistically, offers a molecular tool for directed epigenomic editing that could be exploited as a promising therapeutic tool against cancer.

Cancer Epigenetics

Cancer is a somatic evolutionary process where a normal cell acquires neoplastic phenotypes through progressive reshuffling of its genetic and epigenetic characteristics. Tumorigenesis entails successive genetic mutation and selection events that favor unlimited cellular proliferation and growth inhibition resistance. Further acquisition of genetic defects fuels cancer progression through immune evasion and metastasis. Other epigenetic changes may occur in parallel with genetic mutations. Therefore, the Darwinian model of cancer-associated somatic evolution relies largely on genetic alterations while epigenetic changes assist in acquiring cancer hallmarks.

During carcinogenesis, cells undergoing neoplastic transformation often behave differently under similar selection constraints though they share an identical genetic background. The “genetic mutation/ selection” model for neoplastic transformation fails to explain such deviation of canonical evolutionary dynamics. Taking the epigenetic factors into consideration along with genetic mutations would rationalize the deflection of canonical evolutionary dynamics during tumorigenesis. In support of the above notion, a recent study showed that different chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) lineages respond to ibrutinib treatment differently though they bear similar genetic backgrounds. Importantly, CLL lineages were characterized by distinct DNA methylation and transcriptional profiles. Indeed, epigenetic reshuffling may alter the genomic neutrality of a cell undergoing neoplastic transformation. Together, epigenetic homeostasis alterations can drive oncogenic transformation in addition to genetic alterations 

Aberrant DNA methylation (i.e., cytosine methylation) and reversible histone modifications are the most pronounced epigenetic alterations implicated with cancer. During DNA methylation a methyl (-CH3) group from S-adenosyl methionine (SAM) is transferred to the fifth carbon of the cytosine ring in genomic CpG sites through the catalysis of DNA methyltransferases (DNMTs). DNMT1 methylates hemimethylated DNA sites of a nascent strand to restore parental DNA methylation patterns notably during DNA replication. DNMT3A and DNMT3B methylate genomic CpG sites de novo to generate novel DNA methylation patterns during developmental phases. Conversely, pre-existing methyl groups on CpG sites can be removed by the ten-eleven translocation (TET) family enzymes (i.e., TET1, TET2, and TET3). The transcriptional consequence of DNA methylation, grossly, follows the rules where promoter CpG methylation inactivates gene expression. Interestingly, tumorigenesis reshuffles ongoing DNA methylation and demethylation profiles distinctively. For example, a pan-cancer DNA methylation analysis reported 5450 hypermethylated and 4433 hypomethylated CpG sites analyzing ~5400 DNA methylation profiles of 15 cancer types. Indeed, a generic feature of cancer-associated DNA methylation entails global hypomethylation with region-specific hypermethylation. Alongside directed study with specific cancer types, several pan-cancer DNA methylation analyses revealed a more detailed molecular consequence of cancer-oriented DNA methylation. Recently, Saghafinia et al identified 581 epigenetically down-regulated and 85 epigenetically upregulated genes across 24 human cancer types. Functional enrichment analysis further suggested silenced genes were mostly involved in transcriptional regulation, apoptosis, oxidoreductase activity and cellular metabolism while enhanced genes were involved with cell proliferation, germ cell development and chromatin organization. Most importantly, only 25 out of 581 silenced genes and 6 out of 85 enhanced genes incurred genetic (i.e., mutation, deletion or amplification) anomalies.

Numerous empirical studies have reported multifarious involvement of altered covalent histone modifications in tumorigenesis, clonal divergence and metastasis. Histone acetylation/deacetylation and methylation/demethylation are frequently reported histone modifications involved in cancer besides atypical histone modifications such as citrullination, ubiquitination, ADP-ribosylation, deamination, N6-formylation, and O-GlcNAcylation. Histone acetylation/deacetylation dynamics is regulated by the functionality of histone acetyltransferases (HATs)/ histone deacetylases (HDACs) respectively. HATs catalyze the addition of an acetyl group from acetyl-CoA to lysine (K) residues on the histones while HDACs remove an acetyl group from acetylated K residues. Histone acetylation readout is typically interpreted as transcriptional upregulation while histone deacetylation usually leads to transcriptional silencing. Hyperacetylation may activate proto-oncogenes whereas hypoacetylation may silence tumor suppressor genes. The acetylation/deacetylation imbalance on H3K9, H3K18, H3K23, H3K27, H4K5, H4K8, K12, and H4K16 has been reported in different cancers such as carcinoma, sarcoma, leukemia and lymphoma. Additionally, pan-cancer analysis for genomic alterations of 33 cancer types suggested ubiquitous expression of histone acetylation-modulator proteins to yield cancer type-specific acetylation status.

Similar to histone acetylation/deacetylation, histone methylation/demethylation equilibrium is derailed during oncogenesis and metastasis. Histone methyltransferases (HMTs) and histone demethylases (HDMs) are two distinct classes of enzymes that work in opposite directions to alter the histone methylation/demethylation status in cancer. Lysine/arginine (K/R) residues on H3 and H4 are usual sites for histone methylation. HMTs catalyze methylation while HDMs erase a methyl group from methylated K/R residues. The readout of the histone methylation could be diverse depending on the histone type (i.e., H3/H4) and modified residue (i.e., K/R). For example, transcriptional activation is featured by H3K4me2, H3K4me3, H3R17me, H3K36me3 and H4R3me while H3K9me3, H3K27me3, H4K20me1 and H4K20me3 are associated with transcriptional silencing. In the context of cancer, an assorted pattern of histone methylation marks (e.g., H3K4me1, H3K4me2, H3K4me3, H3K9me2, H3K9me3, H3K27me1, H3K27me2, H3K27me3, H4R3me2, H4K20me1, H4K20me2, H4K20me3, etc.) have been identified in all major types of cancer. Recent pan-cancer analysis of over 15 cancer types demonstrated the reduction of H3K9me2, H3K9me3, H3K36me2 and H3K36me3 to be an oncogenic feature.

CRISPR-dCas9 structure: from CRISPR-Cas to sgRNA-dCas9

CRISPR-Cas is a multicomponent system. A high-level architecture of the CRISPR-Cas system involves a CRISPR guide and Cas effector module. The CRISPR guide module directs DNA recognition whereas Cas effector module digests target DNA. Recently, the CRISPR-Cas system has been classified into three major types (type I, type II and type III) according to synchronized phylogenetic analysis of Cas proteins and genomic comparison of CRISPR-Cas loci structure. The type I and type III systems have a multi-subunit effector system. In contrast, however, the type II CRISPR-Cas system has a single protein effector, Cas9. Due to simplicity of the effector, the type II CRISPR-Cas9 system has been adapted for genomic/epigenomic application.

Naturally, the guide module of the type II CRISPR-Cas system consists of mature CRISPR RNAs (crRNAs) and a trans-activating crRNA (tracrRNA). TracrRNAs are ~75 nucleotides long and possess 24 nucleotides complementary with crRNAs. This base complementarity allows the co-processing of precrRNAs:tracrRNAs duplex by endogenous RNase III to yield mature crRNAs and subsequent active Cas9-tracrRNA:crRNA formation. However, a physical separation of tracrRNA and crRNA was not mandatory for the formation of a successful Cas9-tracrRNA:crRNA riboneucleoprotein complex. In 2012, Jinek et al linked the 3′ end of crRNA to the 5′ end of tracrRNA through a GAAA tetraloop to construct a chimeric RNA without affecting native base-pairing interactions between tracrRNA and crRNA. Interestingly, this chimeric RNA efficiently targeted and generated site-specific DNA cleavage similar to the wild version of tracrRNA:crRNA duplex. The chimeric version of tracrRNA:crRNA complex is now known as single guide RNA (sgRNA) that has been applied to current genomic/ epigenomic applications of the CRISPR-Cas9 tool (Fig. 1).

CRISPR associated protein 9 (Cas9) is the Cas effector and belongs to the type II CRISPR-Cas system. Usually, Cas9 orthologs vary in size but share similar domain design. Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 (SpCas9) is a comprehensively studied and a widely used Cas9. The SpCas9 apo enzyme is a 158.4 kDa protein with 1,368 amino acids (Fig. 1A). Grossly, spCas9 has a bilobular structure: target recognition (REC) lobe and nuclease (NUC) lobe. The lobs are connected through two short (4–6 residues) disordered linkers. The REC lobe is continuous whereas the NUC lobe is dispersed. The NUC possesses two separate endonuclease domains (RuvC and HNH) and protospacer adjacent motif (PAM)-interacting (PI) domain. Though split in primary structure, the RuvC nuclease domain is assembled against HNH nuclease and PI domain in 3D coordination (Fig. 1C). SpCas9 has sgRNA penetrated in holoenzyme. A dramatic REC domain reshuffling enables apo-Cas9 to adapt a conformational change that leads to opening a cleft between the REC and NUC lobes to accommodate sgRNA. Once the target DNA binds to the sgRNA-Cas9 complex, the HNH nuclease domain cleaves the target DNA strand whereas the RuvC nuclease domain cleaves non-target DNA.

A number of mutations have been reported in the active site of nuclease domains that abolish the catalytic activity of the domain. Mutational inactivation of both nuclease domains in Cas9 generates a nuclease-deficient version of Cas9 known as catalytically death Cas9 (dCas9). For example, a version of dCas9 with D10A (Asp 10→ Ala) and H840A (His 840→ Ala) mutation lacks nuclease activity but works as a RNA-guided DNA recognizing protein. Structural examining of dCas9-sgRNA complex against wild-type Cas9-sgRNA complex suggested an identical configuration and conformation of sgRNA within the holoenzyme except for slight movements of the REC1 and REC3 domains. Thus, dCas9-sgRNA shares all the structural features of wild-type CRISPR-Cas9 except for the missense mutations in the active site of the RuvC and HNH nuclease domains.

Mechanism of sgRNA-dCAS9 function

The integration of sgRNA within apo-Cas9 occurs through extensive RNA-protein interactions. The guide region of sgRNA interacts with the REC 1 domain as well as the bridge and RuvC domains. The repeat:anti-repeat duplex and stem loop 1 of sgRNA primarily interacts with the REC 1 domain and the PI domain. All these interactions, indeed, facilitate seed sgRNA preordering and PI domain repositioning for subsequent target DNA interrogation. Additionally, the interaction of the NUC lobe (PI domain and RuvC domain) with stem loop 2 and stem loop 3 stabilize the Cas9-sgRNA complex further.

Once dCas9 is loaded with sgRNA, the binary complex is ready for target DNA interrogation. A successful target bears two different sequence features: a protospacer sequence that is complementary to the guide sequence and a short PAM sequence immediate downstream to the protospacer sequence. A target interrogation is fruitful only if the guide sequence bears Watson-Crick complementarity with a protospacer sequence adjacent to a PAM sequence. Canonically, PAMs are trinucleotide sequences though PAM size could vary between 2–6 nucleotides. 5′-NGG-3′ is an example of a canonical PAM for S. pyogenes where “N” denotes any nucleotide. PAM: PI interaction is concomitant with the local destabilization of PAM-adjacent DNA duplex and subsequent DNA melting and RNA strand invasion to form a DNA-RNA hybrid. Such DNA-RNA hybrid formation associates with displacement of the DNA strand (R-loop) from PAM-proximal to PAM-distal ends. Thermodynamically, free energy from spacer-protospacer base-pairing fuels supercoiled DNA unwinding, RNA strand invasion and R-loop formation. The formation of ternary complex triggers conversion of HNH from an inactive to active conformation which poses the HNH to a cleavage site on the target strand. This conformation change is simultaneous with REC2 disorder. Together HNH conformatial change and REC2 disorder allow REC3- distal PAM interaction and RuvC-distal DNA duplex interaction which ultimately results in cleavage of the nontarget strand. As mentioned earlier, dCas9 in unable to catalyze such endonuclease activity.

sgRNA-dCas9 in transcriptional and epigenetics modulation

Immediately after successful reconstruction in mammalian cells by the Church and Zhang group in 2012, the CRISPR-Cas9 system opened a new avenue to stem cell, biotechnology, regenerative medicine and basic research. This bacterial immune weapon drew vast attention of the scientific community and was exploited as a programmable, specific and efficient genome editing tool. The scope of the CRISPR-Cas9 system extended from the genome to the transcriptome level from the study of Gilbert et al. They constructed functional dCas9-fusion circuit ligating transcriptional repressor Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) domain (i.e., dCas9-KRAB) and transcriptional activation domain VP64 (i.e., dCas9-VP64) separately to the C-terminus of dCas9. Both of these constructs modulated expression of reporter gene (GFP) in yeast and HEK293 cells according to the effector domain attached to dCas9 when cotransfected with sgRNA construct targeting GFP. They also showed that transcriptional manipulation through synthetic peripheral circuits was reproducible by transfecting sgRNAs against transferrin receptor (CD71) and C-X-C chemokine receptor type 4 (CXCR4) in HeLa cells stably expressing dCas9-KRAB. The ultimate result was 60–80% repression of endogenous expression of CD71 and CXCR4 in HeLa cells. Indeed, the study by the Gilbert team, practically, proved the concept of ligating heterologous effector domains to dCas9 is feasible without disrupting dCas9 functionality to bring about reproducible transcriptional modulation. At the same time, a similar finding was reported by Maeder et al with a dCas9-VP64 construct. The authors reported expressional upregulation of human genes VEGFA and NTF3 in HEK293 cells at the transcriptional and translational levels upon co-transfection with VEGFA-targeted sgRNA and a NTF3-targeted sgRNA construct, respectively. The ingenuity of this experiment was the proposal of fusing other heterologous effector domains to dCas9.

The dCas9-fusion constructs capable of transcriptional tuning differ only in the use of transcriptional modular (TM). Some of the TMs are activatory whereas others are inhibitory in nature. Krüppel-associated box (KRAB) domain is most widely used for transcriptional repression whereas herpes simplex viral protein 16 (VP16) oligomers are commonly used for transcriptional activation. VP64 consists of 4 repeats of VP16 in tandem that promotes basal transcription machinery assembly at the target promoter. VP160 (10 VP16 repeats) and VP192 (12 VP16 repeats) are two other versions of VP16 oligomers used with dCas9 for transcription gene activation. The transcriptional induction of VP64 could be improved ~22 −320 fold with a trinary TM module VP64-p65-Rta (VPR). The transcriptional outcome of VP16 oligomers was improved further by introducing MS2 hairpin to gRNA tetraloop and stem loop 2 in a synergistic activation mediator (SAM) system. Such gRNA modification enables binding of both dCas9-VP64 to the target site and MS2-p65-HSF1 binding to modified gRNA which ultimately leads to highly effective transcriptional activation. SUperNova tagging system is another approach that extended potent transcriptional activation where dCas9 is fused to 10 repeats of GCN4 peptide and VP64 is fused with scFv. The gRNA direct GCN4 tagged dCas9 to its target loci where VP64 is recruited to that target site through GCN4-scFv interaction.

The earliest direct use of an epigenetic effector (EE) module with dCas9 was documented by the Gerald team. This group showed two-fold down-regulation of VEGF-A expression in the HEK-293T cell line by modularizing a CRISPR-dCas9-G9a construct targeting the VEGF-A promoter. Indeed, G9a is a histone methyltransferase that methylates H3K9 and promotes heterochromatin formation and eventual decreased gene expression. Starting from this point, a number of different epigenetic effectors have been employed in designing a synthetic sgRNA-dCas9 circuit. Most of the reported epigenetic effectors are either writer or eraser in nature (Table 1).

The epigenetic effectors, used for epigenetic editing, are broadly categorized as histone and DNA modifiers. Unlike the transcriptional modulator, epigenetic effectors are largely enzymatic in nature and either catalyze transcription activatory or inhibitory histone/DNA modification (Table 1). The total number of epigenetic effectors exploited in CRISPR science is nominal compared to the reported pools of epigenetic writers and eraser. However, two different strategies revealed from the reported dCas9-EE use have sweeping consequence. The first strategy is the use of only the catalytic domain for constructing the epigenetic effector circuit. This procedure literally enabled developing a new epigenetic effector circuit just by engrafting the catalytic domain in a pre-existing dCas9 backbone. This strategy is particularly artful and ensured avoidance of issues linked with the enzyme (covalent/allosteric) regulation. The second strategy was exploiting prokaryotic DNA methyltransferases (M. Sss1/MQ1) in a eukaryotic system. Such study, indeed, encourages exploring other prokaryotic DNA methyltransferases in eukaryotic systems to solve unmet DNA modification in pathological situations. For example, a recent study reported decrease of 6mA modification at the DNA level in human cancer cells and tissues. Prokaryotic adenine DNA methyltransferase could be utilized to study molecular mechanisms and consequences associated with DNA 6mA modifications. M.EcoGII from E. coli adenine DNA methyltransferase could be a prospective adenine DNA methyltransferase in such context.

sgRNA-dCAS9 and cancer

Though several reports exist about the exploitation of sgRNA-dCas9-EE in a cancer context, sgRNA-dCas9-TM circuits could indirectly deal with the epigenetic modification. VP16 oligomers interact and recruit basal transcription factors (e.g., TFIIA, TFIIB, TFIIF, TFIIH, TFIID subunits etc.), histone acetyltransferases (e.g., SAGA complex, p300 etc.) and SWI/SNF ATPase complex at the target site. Indeed, histone acetylation-assisted chromatin decondensation is an integral part of VP16 oligomers-triggered transcriptional upregulation. However, transcriptional depression by KRAB domain is executed through heterochromatinization of the target site due to the recruitment of heterochromatin protein 1 (HP1), HDACs, and Histone-lysine N-methyltransferase SETDB1.

Epigenetic modulation can instigate transcriptional tuning and vice versa. Though sgRNA-dCas9-TM influence transcriptional modulation is dissected literally from sgRNA-dCas9-EE induced epigenetic reshuffling, mechanistically they are interwoven and converge to the same final outcome. It is more methodically rational to appreciate the cross-talk between the transcriptional modulator and epigenetic effector rather than weighing them as different approaches (Fig. 2). Therefore, it is worthy to focus the use of sgRNA-dCas9-TM (e.g., VP64, VP160, VP192, KRAB etc.,) besides sgRNA-dCas9-EE pertinently in the context of cancer.

In vitro effects of sgRNA-dCas9

The use of sgRNA-dCas9 in cancer therapeutics has been a relatively new approach especially over the last 5 years. The pioneering studies of therapeutic sgRNA-dCas9 application began with cell lines in vitro. Early studies focused on the assessment of the specificity and efficiency of dCas9 cassettes rather than evaluating their therapeutic suitability. These studies often suffer from sound mechanistic experimental design for implementation in a cancerous scenario. Yet, different cancers have been targeted directly with sgRNA-dCas9-EE including breast cancer, lung cancer, liver cancer, colon cancer, cervical cancer and endometrial cancer (Table 2). The genes of interest (GOI) are broadly categorized into tumor suppressors (e.g., PTEN, BRCA1, CDKN2A, RASSF1, HIC1, etc.) or oncogenes (e.g., GRN, FHL2, CNKSR1, etc.). The majority of GOI were targeted with sgRNAs against promoter proximal regions. Use of sgRNA-dCas9-EE brought forth desirable epigenetic alteration as per epigenetic effectors of the dCas9-circuit.

The outcome of the sgRNA-dCas9-EE catalyzed epigenetic alterations in vitro was encouraging. During the early phase of cancer hallmark attainment, a normal cell undergoes neoplastic transformation to gain indefinite proliferation potential avoiding growth-suppressing signal and consequential apoptosis. On the other hand, targeting tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes with sgRNA-dCas9-EE led to a decrease in cell proliferation and growth besides increased cell viability (Table 2). As the cells advance toward malignant cancer hallmarks, neoplasticaly transformed cells invade the surrounding tissue and sustain tumorous growth through neo-angiogenesis. Upon metastatic transformation, cancerous cells move to a secondary anatomical location through the loss of cellular adhesiveness and immune evasion. Interestingly, targeting cancer cells with sgRNA-dCas9-EE could suppress advanced features of cancer hallmarks through reducing tumor sphere formation, invasion and migration (Table 2). Overall, sgRNA-dCas9-EE assisted epigenetic reshuffling could antagonize most of the features of cancer hallmarks.

Similar to direct epigenetic effector use, indirect modulation of epigenetic features by transcriptional modulators could change neoplastic features (Table 2). Transcriptional modulation of cancerous features with sgRNA-dCas9-TM follows strategies similar to the sgRNA-dCas9-EE use such as designing sgRNA against a proximal promoter, selecting GOI with tumor suppression or oncogenic effect, etc. The difference arises when epigenetic background of a cancerous cell is taken into consideration. A transcriptional modulator often works in a global pattern while epigenetic effectors are highly specific to particular epigenetic alterations that constitute a crucial feature of precision therapeutics. For example, the tumor suppressor REPRIMO promoter is highly methylated in gastric cancer cell line AGS compared to the breast cancer cell line SUM159. Targeting transcriptional upregulation of REPRIMO with a dCas9-fusion construct linked to VP64, p300 and VPR effector showed a universal upregulation of REPRIMO with dCas9-VPR in both of the cell lines. On the other hand, dCas9-p300 selectively increased the mRNA level of REPRIMO in the SUM159 cell line but not in the AGS cell line. Still, the employment of sgRNA-dCas9-TM contributed potentially to our current understanding of the features and flaws of harnessing a synthetic circuit therapeutically for epigenetic modification.

In vivo effects of sgRNA-dCas9

In vivo epigenetic remodeling of endogenous genes through sgRNA-dCas9-EE targeting, specifically under a cancerous scenario, is still at the initial phase. Early attempts of in vivo epigenetic editing with sgRNA-dCas9 were exhorted by several in vivo gene editing efforts in carcinogenesis. Only a few studies have been reported that use either a direct or indirect epigenetic modulatory circuit. Scrutiny of the delivery system suggests most of these studies are ex vivo in nature where dCas9-EE/TM cassette-transfected cancerous cell lines were transplanted into immune compromised mice.

Several studies reported the reactivation of tumor suppressor genes through dCas9-EE/TM. In a study, colon cancer cell line HCT116 and rectal carcinoma cell line RKO were stably transfected with dCas9TET1CD-sgRNA targeting BATF2 demethylation. Injection of stably transfected cell lines into BALB/c nude mice showed significantly reduced volume and weight of HCT116/ RKO tumors. Additionally, dCAS9-EE modulated selective demethylation of SARI-enhanced apoptosis and reduced angiogenesis in HCT116 tumor. In another study, expressional upregulation of tumor suppressor Kruppel like factor 4 (KLF4) was achieved through SAM system (dCas9-vp64/sgRNA-MS2-P65-HSF1) in human bladder cancer cell lines T24. SAM-modified T24 cells were then xenografted into BALB/c-nude mice. Ultimately, KLF4 upregulation inhibited both T24 tumor growth and T24 cell metastasis to the lung.

dCas9-EE/TM has also been used for investigating the carcinogenic role of oncogenes in vivo. Chen et al employed the SAM system (dCas9-vp64/sgRNA-MS2-P65-HSF1) to induce the overexpression of methyltransferase-like 3 (METTL3) in human hepatocellular carcinoma MHCC97L cells. Upon subcutaneous injection of METTL3-overexpressing MHCC97L cells into BABL/cAnN-nude mice, they observed a marked increase in tumor growth, accelerated anchorage-independent growth and enhanced cellular migration. Mechanistically, METTL3 upregulation was linked to knockdown of tumor suppressor SOCS2 that inhibits cellular proliferation, cellular migration and interference with cancer stemness. In another study, Myc expression was amplified with gRNA targeting Myc promoter in stably dCas9-VP160 expressing Trp53-null neurosphere cells. The cells were then transplanted orthotopically into the cerebral cortices of nude mice. Myc transactiveted cells developed tumors and eventually metastasized to the spinal cord.

Alongside the xenograft model, dCas9-EE/TM exploitation was also successful in a transgenic mouse model. Wangensteen et al developed a dCas9+ mouse strain electroporating a pAi9-dCas9 vector into mouse embryonic stem (ES) cells and injecting pAi9-dCas9-bearing ES cells into B6SJL/F1 mouse blastocysts. They used the dCas9+ mouse strain to target endogenous MYC expression by hydrodynamically injecting the MYC-targeting gRNAs into the tail vein that eventually led to ~ 23 fold upregulation of Myc transcription in liver. Consequentially, the mouse developed compact tumor-like masses in the liver. Histological feature analysis revealed hepatocyte carcinogenesis and highly proliferative tissue expressing hepatocellular carcinoma biomarkers such as alpha-fetoprotein (AFP), osteopontin (OPN), and yes-associate protein 1 (YAP1).

The dCas9-EE/TM use has been reported for the development of cancer progression and therapeutic resistance models. Braun et al prepared an in vivo model by injecting Trp53 knockdown B-cell lymphoma cell into immunocompetent C57BL/6J mice. The Trp53 knockdown was achieved through dCas9-VP64-mediated TSS occupancy and transcriptional elongation blockage. The authors showed that dCas9-mediated down-regulation of Trp53 promoted disease onset, reduced overall survival and rendered cancerous cells insensitive to cisplatin. Similarly, dCas9-VP64-mediated transcriptional activation of (O6-methylguanine–DNA methyltransferase (Mgmt) in B-cell lymphoma cells could lead to substantial resistance to Temozolomide treatment after xenografting into immunocompetent C57BL6/6J mice.

Another study by Liu et al extended the applicability of dCas9 beyond canonical cancer-associated gene regulation or cancer in vivo modeling. In this study, the authors targeted upregulation of miR-524 using a gRNA-dCas9-VP64 construct to enhance Pri-miR-524 transcription. A multistage delivery nanoparticle (MDNP) was used to carry the gRNA-dCas9-VP64 cargo to cancer cell lines (i.e., MDA-MB-231 and LN-229) and tumor-bearing mice. The miR-524 upregulation significantly decreased cell proliferation interfering with multiple cancer-associated signals such as TGF-β-, Notch-, and Hippo-signaling pathways. Systemic delivery of gRNA-dCas9-VP64 cargo loaded MDNP into MDA-MB-231 xenograft mice resulted in tumor growth inhibition with tumor exclusive upregulation of Pri-miR-524.

Challenges of targeting cancer with sgRNA-dCas9

sgRNA-dCas9 was devised from a prokaryotic immune defense tool CRISPR-Cas9 system that was exploited purposefully for eukaryotic transcriptional reshuffling. Admittedly, the genetic and transcriptional landscape of eukaryotes is quite different from that of prokaryotes. Moreover, cancer is featured by anomalous genetic and epigenetic regulation. Lacking endonuclease activity, dCas9 bypassed challenges particularly associated with the catalytic and post-catalytic operations of Cas9 such as off-target cutting, efficiency of endogenous repair mechanisms such as homology-directed repair (HDR) and homologous end joining (HNJ). Nonetheless, a major challenge of targeting cancer with sgRNA-dCas9 is the off-target effect. Additionally, sgRNA-dCas9 efforts often struggle from sgRNA designing rubrics, target site selection and sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems.

Off-target effect

Our early understanding about the off-target effect of dCas9 derived from off-target binding of dCas9. A study by Wu et al reported off-target binding of dCas9-sgRNA at ~20 to 5900 sites in mouse embryonic stem cells (ESCs) with four different sgRNA against the Nanog and Phc1 promoters. They traced most of the off-target sites within active genes. To check if the binding essentially leads to Cas9-catalized insertion-deletion (indel) mutation, the authors sequenced a panel of 295 off-target sites in ESCs cotransfected with Cas9 and identical sgRNA. Interestingly, they detected only one off-target indel above the background level. A similar study with genome-wide dCas9 binding reported about 10 to 1200 off-target sites in HEK293T cells for 12 different sgRNAs. The authors subjected 50% of off-target sites for Cas9-catalized indel crosschecking with significant indel mutation at 30 off-target sites. One of the practical limitations of the above studies was the use of an epigenetic effector-free dCas9 construct. A real scenario of off-target effect associated with dCas9-EE module was revealed by the Lin group. They performed genome-wide bisulfite sequencing to examine global methylation changes of dCas9-DNMT3A-expressing HEK293T cell transfected with sgRNAs against uPA or TGFBR3. They reported about 5500 hypermethylated regions with average length of 60–80 base pairs compared to methyltransferase-positive and methyltransferase-negative control cell lines. Stratifying hypermethylated regions on the genomic landscape showed predominant hypermethylations on promoters, 5-UTR, and CpG islands. The outcome of hypermethylations was reflected at the transcriptional level with differential expression of over 1000 genes in dCas9-methyltransferase and uPA sgRNAs-expressing HEK293T cells. Further, integrative analyses of differential gene expression and promoter methylation identified a few clusters of differentially expressed genes from dCas9-DNMT3A and uPA gRNAs.

Real time monitoring of Cas9 and DNA interaction suggests a weak initial Cas9-PAM interaction that enables Cas9 to interrogate neighboring sequences through facilitated diffusion. Precise and efficient targeting by dCas9-sgRNA involves a definite sequence feature for sgRNA as well as target site. An absolute target site requires a perfect (sgRNA) guide complementary sequence with a downstream PAM for unique dCas9 binding. In reality, PAM sequences have been reported to be versatile including both canonical (NGG) and non-canonical (i.e., NGA, NGA and NCG) PAMs. PAM screening using human cell lines by Hu et al added the possibility of tetranucleotide non-canonical PAM such as GCGG and CTGG to the list. On the other hand, neighboring sequences interrogation includes a relaxed interaction with guide sequence especially with the PAM-proximal sequence. Different studies suggested the length of a crucial PAM-proximal sequence can fluctuate between 5–12 nucleotides in length for efficient targeting whereas the guide sequence is typically 20 nucleotides long. The laxity of sequence requirement permits guide sequence promiscuity and often results in PAM-distal mismatch tolerance. For instance, experimental findings showed that a short (1–3 bp) internally mismatched up to eighteenth nucleotide position within PAM distal sites could produce cleavages experimentally parallel to on-target Cas9 cleavage. This PAM-distal mismatch tolerance has also been reported for as many as 5 nucleotides mismatch in human cells. Besides mismatch issue, one-nucleotide insertions (DNA bulge) and deletions (RNA bulge) throughout the protospacer sequence with respect to guide RNA sequence was well tolerated in human cell line (HEK293T) and resulted in Cas9 cleavage. Together PAM sequence flexibility and PAM-distal mismatch tolerance, together, expanded the prospective “target+PAM” hits from several to thousands.

Several protein-nucleic acid interactions can facilitate PAM-distal mismatch. For example, structural analysis of sgRNA:DNA:Cas9 ternary complex showed that sequence-independent interaction of a positively charged groove at the interface of the HNH, RuvC, and PI domains with nontarget DNA strand. Such interaction can sustain PAM-distal mismatch tolerance because this interaction was not governed by the guide RNA-target DNA base pairing. Neutralizing the positively charged groove, therefore, should reinforce committed interaction between guide-RNA and target DNA strand. However, normal cellular processes accompanied by DNA stretching prompts off-target Cas9 binding such as transcription, replication, DNA repair, DNA supercoiling etc. DNA stretching eases DNA melting barrier waiver and facilitates R-loop formation. DNA stretching-dependent binding could be directed by guide sequence with high mismatches tolerating even up to 10 mismatches. Therefore, normal cellular processes could increase off-target hits uncovering hidden off-target sites to Cas9.

sgRNA designing rubrics

dCAS9-sgRNA is directed to a specific sequence in the genome through the guide sequence of sgRNA. Aiming a genomic site precisely with sgRNA could be challenging due to erratic dCas9-sgRNA casting to unintended genomic locations. Previous studies suggest at least an ~98% chance of getting one off-target site for a selected guide RNA from a library of ~124,500 guide RNAs.Therefore, an issue of off-targeting appears to be a regular but potential obstacle to dCas9-sgRNA assisted epigenome editing. Careful and rational gRNA design can minimize sgRNA-associated off-target effects. With this solution in hand, there is a need for a comprehensive set of empirically proven rubrics for designing sgRNA.

Available computational tools for gRNAs design have not only eased CRISPR use but also broadened the application of CRISPR tools. Commendable effort has been devoted in developing optimized, efficient and powerful computational tools to fetch gRNA with maximum on-target but minimum off-target effects. The methods applied to develop existing computational tools for gRNA designing are largely categorized into three groups: (1) alignment-based methods; (2) hypothesis-based methods and (3) machine and deep learning (MDL)-based methods. Recent benchmarking study suggests performance variations, prediction heterogeneity and contextualized predictions among major gRNA design tools. Almost all available prediction models suffer from one or more data-associated issues such as data insufficiency, data heterogeneity and data imbalance. In addition, empirical rules applied by gRNA prediction tools are largely ambiguous. Crucially, in silico sgRNA prediction tools should accommodate various features associated with target sequences, sgRNA structure and chromatin-conformation in prediction logic 

Recent studies suggest that structural features of sgRNA beyond guide sequences are worthy of attention for efficient and versatile sgRNA designing. A study by Xu et al demonstrated a higher degree of editing efficiency in human cell lines from exchange of chimeric sgRNA with original full length crRNA:tracrRNA. Indeed, the deleted sequence of full length crRNA:tracrRNA may contribute to structural stability of guide RNA through extra loop formation. Another study suggested significant improvement of CRISPRCas9-based genome editing by four to six nucleotide extensions of repeat-antirepeat duplex and T→C/G mutation at position 4 of a spacer proximal thymines track.

Target site selection

Identifying a suitable genomic location as an optimal site for dCas-sgRNA function within a target gene of interest could be challenging due to diverse regulatory sequences, dynamic nucleosome occupancy and differential epigenetic marks (i.e., methylation, acetylation etc.). Strand-specificity and position with respect to transcription start site (TSS) vastly influence dCas9-sgRNA function. In prokaryotic systems, targeting non-template (NT) strand with an adjacent PAM has a better regulatory outcome which is also translatable in a eukaryotic system. Recently, a report of targeting HMS2 locus in Saccharomyces cerevisiae demonstrated that dCas9-sgRNA activity often may not be strand-specific. With such inconsistent findings in hand, target site selection requires further scrutiny.

Another determinant of efficient dCas9-sgRNA activity is the location of a target site. To rule out the effect of distance between the sgRNA site and TSS, Gilbert et al performed a comprehensive study with the sunCas9 system and sgRNA library targeting 49 genes expression in K562 cells. The group exhibited the highest efficiency for sgRNAs that targeted −400 to −50 nucleotides upstream from the TSS. Several other empirical findings showed that effective dCas9-sgRNA activity could be achieved by targeting not only a narrower TSS proximal region (−250 to −50 bp), but also an extended TSS distal region (−200 to −700 bp). Most importantly, dCas9-sgRNA-assisted alteration of epigenetic regulation could lead to deviation from conventional distance role of gene expression depending on the target gene and epigenomic context. Kwon et al targeted transcriptional silencing of the Mecp2, Smn1 and Isl1 genes with dCas9-HDAC3 and a set of gRNAs complementary to a site within +100 to −110bp, −400 to −600 bp, −30 to −530 bp of TSS respectively. The ultimate outcome was the expressional down-regulation of Mecp2 with gRNA against +100 to −110bp alone with Smn1 down-regulation for gRNA against −400 to −600 bp of TSS. No transcriptional inhibition for Isl1 genes was observed for any gRNAs applied. Interestingly, this observation could be better explained by the presence of histone acetylation marks (H3K27ac) around the target loci in addition to the distance of the target site from TSS.

Besides promoters, distal regulatory regions such as enhancers, locus control regions (LCRs) etc., are potential target sites for dCas9-EE/TM activity. The enhancers could be targeted for both transcriptional upregulation and down-regulation. Gao et al exploited a dCas9-VP64 construct to up-regulate Oct4 and Nanog expression co-transfecting gRNAs against distal enhancers about 2.4 kb and 5kb upstream of the TSS respectively. Conversely, dCas9-KRAB construct led to the down-regulation of Oct4 and Nanog mRNA level when the distal enhancers of respective genes were targeted with appropriate gRNAs. Interestingly, the gene regulatory output of dCas9-EE/TM may not be straightforward. This issue is particularly important when targeting enhancers since the cellular state has influence on enhancer activity. For example, proximal Oct4 enhancer targeting with dCas9-LSD1 had no effect on Oct4 expression though targeting distal Oct4 enhancer with dCas9-LSD1 result in Oct4 expressional reduction in mouse embryonic stem cells. This observation was better explained by the fact that a proximal Oct4 enhancer regulates the Oct4 expression in epiblast cells. However, current evidence also suggests successful targeting of LCRs with dCas9-EE/TM. For example, Hilton et al reported significant upregulation of hemoglobin epsilon 1, hemoglobin delta, hemoglobin gamma 1 and 2 transcription upon targeting mammalian β-globin LCR with dCas9-p300 core and gRNAs designed against DNase hypersensitive site 2 within the LCR region.

sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems

One of the feasible ways to avoid global off-target effects of dCas9-sgRNA, especially for in vivo use, could be precise delivery of dCas9-sgRNA components machinery to target a tissue of interest. The sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems are crucially important for restricting dCAS9-sgRNA-mediated epigenomic effect to the pathologically inflicted tissue. The Cas9-sgRNA cargo can be delivered in three different modes, all of which could be applied for dCas9-sgRNA components: (i) Single plasmid DNA construct encoding dCas9-EE and sgRNA, (ii) separate mRNA for dCas9-EE and sgRNA translation, (iii) sCas9-EE-sgRNA ribonucleoprotein complex. On the other hand, delivery systems are largely divided into physical process, viral delivery, and non-viral delivery. Each of these delivery methods has their own advantages and limitations. In an ideal situation, sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems should introduce dCas9-sgRNA components specifically to the tissue of interest without instigating any immunological reaction and other potential side effects such as mass tissue deposition and toxicity. In reality, experience from empirical studies deviates much from the expectation.

The physical methods of dCas9-sgRNA cargo delivery include microinjection, electroporation, hydrodynamic injection etc. The physical methods are usually well controlled, effective, reproducible and well suited for ex vivo manipulation. To be clinically competent, a delivery method should be capable of supplying dCas9-sgRNA cargo to a specific tissue of interest in a natural anatomical location. Physical measures, therefore, suffer from incompetency to be used under a clinical scenario. Adeno-associated viral vector, adenoviral vector and lentiviral vectors are the most common viral vectors of choice for dCas9-sgRNA cargo delivery. Despite ensuring a number for benefits toward dCas9-sgRNA cargo delivery such as high infection efficiency, broad cell tropism, stable gene expression etc., the viral delivery system is not the optimal delivery system of choice due to sporadic immunogenicity, packaging constraints, and transgene silencing. Non-viral delivery systems are often synthetic lipid, polymeric, and inorganic particles encapsulating either sCas9-EE-sgRNA ribonucleoprotein complex or dCas9-EE-sgRNA genetic material (plasmid DNA or mRNA). Immune protection, transient dCAS9 expression and easier preclinical to clinical scalability are several major advantages associated with non-viral delivery systems whereas disadvantages include endolysosomal degradation, phagocytic clearance, insufficient distribution at the target site, instability under physiological conditions, etc..

Several studies suggest the shifting of the dCas9 delivery strategy from non-targeted to targeted distribution. Theoretically, targeted dCas9 delivery could be designed to optimize any ligand-receptor interaction exclusive to normal anatomical site of interest. For example, Rouet et al synthesized Cas9 ribonucleoprotein complex fused to endosomolytic peptides that can be recognized by asialoglycoprotein receptors (ASGPr) ligand. Such a synthetic cargo promotes the selective uptake of Cas9 ribonucleoprotein into the cell with ASGPrL surface receptor through receptor-mediated endocytosis. As hepatocytes are ASGPr rich, the selective Cas9 cargo designed by Rouet et al preferential entered into hepatocytes. Practically, this study by Rouet group encourage optimizing other tissue specific ligand-receptor interactions for selective Cas9 cargo designing.

Another promising option of targeted dCas9 delivery relies on antigen-antibody interaction. Though appearing technically simple, practically utilizing antigen-antibody interaction for targeted dCas9 delivery could be tricky. In a recent attempt, the Brinkmann group was able to deliver CRISPR/Cas9 expression plasmids in MCF-7 cells utilizing antigen-antibody interaction. The group first reconstituted the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid DNA into nucleosomes which was followed by the assembly of antibody–chromatin complexes. The cargo antibody is designed in a way so that the variable regions face outwards whereas constant regions are associated with DNA backbone. As the variable regions bear paratopes against the cell surface antigen, antibody–chromatin scaffold can selectively interact with the target MCF-7 cells and lead to consequential internalization of plasmid DNA. The authors also demonstrated Cas9 expression in more than 60% of the target cells.

In addition to developing efficient sgRNA-dCas9 delivery systems, controlling dCas9 activity may be a novel strategy to offset undesirable effects associated with sgRNA-dCas9 systems. Several studies are scrutinizing the efficiency of controlling Cas9 spatiotemporally. In this regard, a chemical-inducible version of dCas9 may be promising. In an attempt to accomplish this, Balboa et al fused dihydrofolate reductase destabilization domain (DHFR DD) with dCas9-VP192. As a result, DHFR DD-dCas9-VP192 fusion protein showed Trimethoprim-dependent activation of dCas9. Similarly, the Meng group constructed a 4-hydroxytamoxifen inducible version of Cas9 fusing hormone-binding domain of the estrogen receptor on both of C- and N-terminus of Cas9 (ERT2–Cas9–ERT2).

Prospect of gRNA-dCas9 in cancer

sgRNA-dCas9 science is improving rapidly and constantly. As aforementioned, off-target effects represent a major drawback of sgRNA-dCas9 for its use in basic and translational research in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic systems. In this regard, recent advances with sgRNA-Cas9 specificity could be opted for precise sgRNA-dCas9 use.

Off-target effects ensued from conventional “NGG+seed” interrogation by SpCas9 could be overcome by enabling different “PAM+seed” interrogation. The notion of “PAM restriction” brings forth the use of Cas9 orthologs as a way for enhanced on-target effect. Across the human genome, SpCas9 has “NGG” PAM every 8 nucleotides whereas Neisseria meningitides Cas9 (NmCas9) has “N4GATT” PAM every 128 nucleotides. Interestingly, a comparison of SpCas9 and NmCas9 for genome-wide off-target effects showed superior on-target editing with NmCas9 even at the sites highly prone to SpyCas9 off-target editing. More than 15 different Cas9 orthologs have been identified with different PAM requirement and mutations required for nuclease deficiency. Besides Cas9 orthologs, directed evolution induction in vitro known as Sniper screen was performed in E. coli to isolate SpCas9 variants with high on-target specificity. Human genome-wide evaluation of off-target mutations with SpCas9 and Sniper-Cas9 suggested far fewer off-target sites for Sniper-Cas9 as compared to SpCas9. Similar to Sniper-Cas9, a phage-assisted evolution led to an xCas9 variant with broader PAM compatibility (i.e., NG, GAA and GAT) and better on-target activity. A recent structural analysis of xCas9 variety, xCas9 3.7, showed an unrestricted rotamerization of R1335 by E1219V point mutation allowing PAM recognition laxity.

Efforts were made to circumvent the off-target effects considering the physiochemical and structural properties of WT-Cas9 through rational engineering. As mentioned before, non-specific interactions of Cas9-RNA and Cas9-substrate complex may contribute to off-target activity. Hypothetically, rational engineering weakens those non-specific interactions to enhance on-target effect. One rationally engineered Cas9 is eSpCas9(1.0) which bears a K810A mutation in HNH and K1003A/R1060A mutations in RuvC. These mutations, indeed, neutralize positively charged amino acid residues within the nontarget strand groove that ultimately discourages nonspecific binding of nontarget strands while reinforcing target and guide interactions. Similarly, a structural feature of a Cas9/sgRNA/target DNACas9 ternary complexes were utilized to rationally design a quadruple (R245A/N413A/N419A/R654) mutant SaCas9-HF. In SaCas9-HF, R245A/N413A/N419A/R654 mutations altered polar contact between recognition lobe/RuvC-III and PAM-distal target strand to a SaCas9-HF to enhance genome-wide targeting accuracy.

To overcome the challenges with the sgRNA designing, deep learning techniques have been applied over the last few years. In deep learning techniques, sgRNAs prediction algorithms are first trained with a comprehensive dataset of sgRNA knockdown experiments carefully designed to influence the parameters that can affect sgRNA specificity and efficacy. Thus, a deep learning algorithm can identify features important for sgRNA specificity and efficacy. This training is followed by allowing the algorithm to predict and score sgRNAs. DeepCas9 is the first-reported deep learning-based sgRNA prediction tool that was trained with ten publicly available experimental gene knockout datasets in several cell types of five different species (e.g., human, C. elegans, mouse, zebrafish, etc.). Upon training, DeepCas9 could learn to capture key sequence features and nucleotide preferences for sgRNAs for on-target sgRNA prediction. Recently, DeepCRISPR tool has integrated epigenetic information, coding and non-coding discrimination features in learning algorithms for high performance sgRNA knockout efficacy.

The most promising feature of sgRNA-dCas9 to emerge as precision medicine against cancer is its versatility. Theoretically, sgRNA-dCas9 can target any gene associated with cancer while aiming a particular gene is much simpler with designing specific sgRNA. Versatility has made the sgRNA-dCas9 as a multipurpose tool to deal with any cancer hallmarks. In practice, versatility of sgRNA-dCas9 could ensure development of a comprehensive therapeutic strategy against cancer (Fig. 4). In other words, sgRNA-dCas9 can be modularized to influence multiple features of cancer.

The multiplexing capability of sgRNA-dCas9 is another attractive feature for treating an epigenetically complex disease like cancer. The cancer hallmarks are often achieved through epigenetic dysregulation of multiple genes. sgRNA-mediated target promiscuity of dCas9 could be optimized to interfere with epigenetic regulation of multiple genes. Existing reports suggest that target loci multiplexing are quite common in gene targeting. Usually, sgRNA is designed to target multiple loci while multiple genes targeting with a single sgRNA scaffold or multiple sgRNA pool has become a trend in sgRNA-dCas9 research. Even, sgRNA-dCas9 mediated multiplexing could be utilized to ensure distinct regulatory outcomes. For example, sgRNA-dCas9 mediated targeting of DR5 and cFLIP induce HeLa cells apoptosis synergistically upon tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) treatment. Indeed, sgRNA-dCas9 targeting upregulate DR5 expression while down-regulating cFLIP expression.

Finally, clinical trials with the Cas9 show hope for the clinical use of dCas9. Despite incumbent off-target effects of Cas9 in vivo, more than seven phase 1 clinical trials (NCT03545815, NCT03398967, NCT04035434, NCT03747965, NCT03164135, NCT03166878, NCT03044743) are ongoing with CRISPR/Cas9 against multiple cancers such as blood cancer, mesothelin positive multiple solid tumors, Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) associated malignancies in USA and China. Besides cancerous conditions, the FDA has approved phase 1 clinical trials for β-thalassemia (NCT03655678), severe sickle cell disease (NCT03745287) and leber congenital amaurosis type 10 (NCT03872479) over the last two years in the Unites States. These clinical trials not only denote the maturation of sgRNA-Cas9 as therapeutic tools but also encourage development of new sgRNA-Cas9-based toolsets such as sgRNA-dCas9-EM/TM for future use.

Concluding remarks

sgRNA-dCas9 science has advanced considerably over the past two decades. Despite the advances, we have uncertainty regarding the efficacy and specificity of sgRNA-dCas9-EE tools. Except for a few genome-wide screenings for sgRNA-dCas9-EE off-target sites, we lack a comprehensive genome-wide map of sgRNA-dCas9 off-target binding, knowledge about the factors governing off-target binding and the molecular/cellular environment that favors sgRNA-dCas9 off-target effects. Additionally, the biophysics of sgRNA-dCas9-EE and target loci interaction could provide important clues toward precise sgRNA-dCas9-EE exploitation in pathophysiological situations including cancer.

One of the crucial impediments for sgRNA-dCas9-EE that have emerged as prospective therapeutic tools against cancer is precise and optimized sgRNA designing. The sgRNA could be considered as a bullet for sgRNA-dCas9-EE rifle. Indeed, simplicity of sgRNA-dCas9-EE-aided epigenomic targeting greatly relies on efficient sgRNA designing. Unfortunately, we are still struggling with a detailed sgRNA designing rubric as well as efficient computation tools. We need more vigorous effort for designing precise and efficient sgRNA incorporating target sequence features, epigenetic status and chromatin context.

In vivo studies often provide important information about pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of therapeutics. The preclinical development of a therapeutic tool largely depends on in vivo studies. Though the findings from in vitro reports are really exhorting, the number of reported empirical studies in vivo on sgRNA-dCas9-EE are limited. This statement is exclusively true in the context of cancer. Therefore, more studies should focus on in vivo application of sgRNA-dCas9-EE especially in a cancer context. In this regard, attention should be diverted toward designing new and innovative empirical studies not only for targeting cancer hallmark-associated genes but also for delivery methods, dose, potency, tissue distribution and off-target effects.
