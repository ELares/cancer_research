# From Ablation to Neuromodulation Platform: The Evolving Role of Magnetic Resonance-Guided Focused Ultrasound in Functional Neurosurgery.

## Abstract

Magnetic resonance-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS) has rapidly evolved from an experimental concept into a versatile platform in functional neurosurgery. Initially pioneered as a noninvasive thermal ablation modality for essential tremor, MRgFUS has since gained regulatory approval and demonstrated durable long-term efficacy. Its clinical applications have expanded to include Parkinson's disease, chronic pain, psychiatric disorders, and investigational use in dystonia, epilepsy, and brain tumors. Beyond lesioning, low-intensity focused ultrasound enables reversible neuromodulation and transient blood-brain barrier opening, facilitating drug and gene delivery in conditions such as Alzheimer's disease and glioblastoma. Comparative analyses highlight MRgFUS as an incisionless alternative to traditional modalities like deep brain stimulation, radiofrequency ablation, and radiosurgery, offering unique advantages in precision, safety, and patient acceptability while retaining certain limitations, including irreversibility and eligibility constraints due to skull properties. Emerging innovations-such as dual-target strategies, staged bilateral procedures, adaptive focusing technologies, and integration with immuno- or gene therapies-are expanding its therapeutic potential. Collectively, these advances position MRgFUS as not only an ablative tool but also a transformative neuromodulation platform with broad implications for the treatment of movement disorders, neuropsychiatric disease, and neurodegeneration.

Graphical Abstract

INTRODUCTION

Magnetic resonance-guided focused ultrasound (MRgFUS) represents a paradigm-shifting, noninvasive neurosurgical modality that employs precisely concentrated ultrasonic energy to ablate targeted brain tissue without the need for incisions. Pioneering experiments in the mid-20th century (e.g., Lynn and Putnam, 1944) demonstrated that high-intensity focused ultrasound could create discrete brain lesions; however, widespread clinical use was impeded by acoustic interference from the skull. This fundamental obstacle was finally overcome in the late 1990s with the development of helmet-configured phased-array transducers and patient-specific phase correction algorithms, which enabled focused transmission through the intact cranium. By integrating these transducers with real-time MRI guidance and thermometry, modern transcranial MRgFUS systems surmounted the skull barrier and enabled controlled lesioning deep within the brain. A major clinical milestone occurred in 2016, when a pivotal randomized trial led to regulatory approval of MRgFUS thalamotomy for medication-refractory essential tremor. This provided immediate tremor relief without open surgery and established MRgFUS as a viable clinical tool. Since then, therapeutic applications have expanded rapidly. By 2018, investigational protocols were exploring its use in Parkinson’s disease (PD), chronic pain, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), and depression. Contemporary research on MRgFUS has extended to novel domains, including low-intensity neuromodulation for substance use disorders (SUDs) and transient blood–brain barrier (BBB) opening to enhance drug delivery in neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer’s disease (AD). In a few years, MRgFUS has evolved from an experimental concept into an established intervention for select neurological disorders. Long-term outcome studies now confirm the durability and safety of MRgFUS treatments, and ongoing innovations continue to broaden its indications and technical capabilities.

TECHNICAL PRINCIPLES

System configuration and targeting workflow

MRgFUS employs an array of ultrasound transducers to focus acoustic energy at predetermined intracranial targets, generating localized hyperthermia that induces thermocoagulative lesions while sparing intervening tissue. Contemporary systems, exemplified by the InSightec Exablate Neuro platform, use up to 1,024 transducer elements operating at approximately 650 kHz in a hemispheric helmet configuration. The patient’s head is rigidly immobilized in a frame and surrounded by a water bath, which cools the scalp and skull while providing acoustic coupling. Prior to sonication, high-resolution CT imaging of the skull is acquired for individualized phase corrections to compensate for skull thickness and density variations, ensuring precise focal convergence. Throughout treatment, real-time MR thermometry monitors temperature maps in the target and surrounding brain regions to verify that the focal zone reaches therapeutic temperatures (55℃–60℃) without overheating adjacent tissue. This integration of MRI guidance with closed-loop thermal feedback is critical for MRgFUS safety and precision, enabling real-time visualization of lesion formation.

Dose and bioeffects

The biological effects of focused ultrasound depend on acoustic intensity and pulse characteristics. High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), typically >1,000 W/cm2 in continuous-wave mode, causes rapid focal heating that results in protein denaturation, coagulative necrosis, and irreversible cell death within seconds. Animal studies have characterized cell-specific thermal sensitivity thresholds, with neurons showing the greatest vulnerability (irreversible damage at 42℃–43℃), followed by oligodendrocytes and myelin, whereas astrocytes and microglia are relatively more thermotolerant. Surrounding tissues receive much lower, unfocused intensities and remain thermally unaffected, preserving neurological function. In contrast, low-intensity focused ultrasound (LIFU), delivered in pulsed mode (time-averaged intensity <100 W/cm2), does not ablate tissue but can reversibly modulate neural activity or transiently disrupt the BBB. Pulsed LIFU exerts mechanical effects: direct deformation of neuronal membranes can activate mechanosensitive ion channels to induce neuromodulation, and pulsing in the presence of circulating microbubbles can cause stable cavitation that mechanically opens tight junctions in cerebral endothelium, transiently increasing BBB permeability. This facilitates targeted drug delivery to specific brain regions without permanent tissue damage. In summary, current MRgFUS systems can operate in high-intensity continuous mode for thermal ablation or in low-intensity pulsed mode for non-destructive applications (neuromodulation, BBB opening), greatly expanding the technology’s therapeutic versatility.

MRI guidance and real-time thermal monitoring

The revolutionary advancement that made focused ultrasound a practical neurosurgical tool was its seamless integration with MRI guidance, resulting in MRgFUS. This convergence allows unprecedented precision in target identification, treatment planning, and monitoring. High-resolution MRI provides superior soft-tissue contrast for accurate target localization and visualization of critical structures to avoid. Real-time imaging during sonication enables dynamic adjustment of acoustic parameters and immediate detection of off-target effects. Most critically, MR thermometry using the proton resonance frequency shift provides spatially resolved temperature maps with ±2℃ accuracy and second-by-second temporal resolution. This real-time thermal feedback allows precise control of the delivered dose, ensuring that the target reaches 55℃–60℃ for ablation while surrounding tissue remains below damage thresholds. Cumulative thermal dose can be calculated in real time (e.g., via the Arrhenius model), providing quantitative indication of lesion formation. This represents a paradigm shift from “blind” lesioning to controlled, image-guided thermal therapy with immediate verification of treatment efficacy.

HISTORICAL FOUNDATIONS

The foundations of MRgFUS trace back to pioneering investigations in the 1940s and 1950s. In 1944, Lynn and Putnam produced well-circumscribed cortical and subcortical lesions in 37 animals using HIFU, noting that lesions appeared almost instantaneously compared with the timing they appeared at with radiation-induced damage. In the early 1950s, William and Francis Fry at the University of Illinois conducted extensive feline experiments demonstrating that focused ultrasound could create precise thermal lesions without damaging intervening tissue. Their system employed multiple converging transducers to produce pinpoint lesions, and by 1957 they partnered with neurosurgeon Russell Meyers to treat human patients (e.g., PD) via craniotomy. However, broader clinical adoption stalled due to the need for skull trephination (to avoid acoustic distortion) and the lack of imaging guidance.

Subsequent preclinical studies throughout the 1960s–1990s provided the foundational biological understanding of focused ultrasound bioeffects. Systematic animal experiments defined temperature thresholds for reversible and irreversible tissue injury, establishing that neuronal necrosis occurs at sustained focal temperatures above approximately 55℃, while brief exposures at 42℃–45℃ induce only transient functional suppression without structural damage. These data delineated the therapeutic window that later guided modern MR-monitored thermal dosing.

It was not until the late 1990s that Hynynen and Jolesz achieved the critical breakthrough of transcranial FUS through an intact skull. In a landmark 1998 study, they demonstrated that multi-phase array transducers could deliver adequate power through human skull bone for therapeutic effects, overturning the long-standing belief that craniotomy was required. This established the basis for modern transcranial MRgFUS, combining head-mounted phased arrays with MRI guidance. Over subsequent years, continued engineering advances—such as larger element arrays, refined phase correction algorithms, and real-time MR thermometry—culminated in the first commercial MRgFUS systems in the 2000s. These developments set the stage for clinical translation in the 2010s, enabling focused ultrasound to re-emerge as a noninvasive surgical technology.

CLINICAL APPLICATIONS

Movement disorders

Essential tremor

Essential tremor (ET) was the first clinical indication for MRgFUS and remains the most established. In 2016, a multicenter sham-controlled trial of unilateral MRgFUS thalamotomy in medication-refractory ET demonstrated marked tremor reduction and improved function at 3 months. This led to FDA approval that year. Longer-term data have confirmed the durability of benefit: a 5-year follow-up of 40 patients from the pivotal trial showed sustained tremor improvement >70% from baseline with lasting quality-of-life gains and no progressive adverse effects. Early mild tremor recurrence within months is observed in a subset (10%–15%), but most patients maintain significant benefit at 3–5 years. Factors predicting long-term durability remain under study.

While standard MRgFUS treatment targets the ventral intermediate nucleus (VIM) of the thalamus (unilateral thalamotomy), alternative lesion targets have been explored. The posterior subthalamic area (PSA) has emerged as an alternative target to VIM in deep brain stimulation (DBS) for ET, with studies showing comparable or superior efficacy, prompting its investigation in MRgFUS applications. The cerebellothalamic tract in the PSA has shown efficacy when lesioned: Gallay et al. reported that PSA tractotomy via MRgFUS achieved tremor control comparable to VIM lesioning. Similarly, Schreglmann et al. demonstrated that lesioning the decussating cerebellar outflow tract in PSA yielded significant tremor improvement. These studies suggest targeting the cerebello-thalamic outflow tract can be as effective as thalamic nuclei, with possibly different side effect profiles. Combination approaches have also been tested. Jameel et al. similarly used dual lesions (VIM plus PSA) in ET and reported sustained benefits at 2 years.

While standard practice remains a single unilateral lesion, such multi-target strategies highlight the potential to optimize tremor control by disrupting both cortical outflow and feedback pathways. Historically, ET procedures have been unilateral due to risk of bilateral thalamotomy (speech, gait impairment), but recent studies support the safety of staged bilateral MRgFUS (Fig. 1). In 2024, an open-label trial of staged contralateral thalamotomy 9–12 months after initial treatment showed additional tremor reduction on the second side without serious permanent deficits. Some patients experienced transient ataxia or speech difficulty after the second lesion, but most effects resolved. These findings indicate that carefully staged bilateral MRgFUS is feasible for severe bilateral ET, offering an incisionless alternative to bilateral DBS in select cases.

Parkinson’s disease

Initial efforts in tremor-dominant PD used VIM thalamotomy (as in ET), which improved tremor and motor scores in patients not eligible for DBS. In 2018, the FDA approved MRgFUS for treating tremor-dominant PD.

More recently, MRgFUS applications in PD have expanded to other PD symptoms beyond treating parkinsonian tremor. The first successful case of MRgFUS pallidotomy in PD was reported in 2015, demonstrating significant improvements in both dyskinesia and motor symptoms in a 78-year-old woman with medication-refractory motor fluctuations, which paved the way for subsequent clinical trials. In 2021, the FDA approved MRgFUS pallidotomy (targeting the globus pallidus internus [GPi]) for medication-refractory PD motor symptoms (e.g., rigidity, dyskinesias) based on earlier safety and feasibility studies. Subsequently, a larger randomized controlled trial published in 2023 confirmed these benefits, showing that 69% of patients receiving unilateral MRgFUS pallidal lesions met the response criterion (improved motor function) versus 32% in the sham group. Patients had meaningful reductions in off-medication motor scores and levodopa-induced dyskinesias.

While traditional MRgFUS pallidotomy effectively addresses dyskinesia and some motor symptoms, it has technical challenges due to the lateral location of the GPi target, which requires larger acoustic beam angles and consequently reduces energy transmission efficiency. Additionally, bilateral pallidotomy carries significant risks of speech and cognitive complications, as demonstrated in historical radiofrequency ablation studies. Similarly, a recent staged bilateral MRgFUS subthalamotomy trial, in which 2 of 6 patients experienced persistent mild speech disturbances at 6 months. To overcome these limitations, Gallay et al. performed MRgFUS pallidothalamic tractotomy, creating smaller, more precise lesions in the Field H1 of Forel where pallidal efferent fibers converge, demonstrating significant improvements in tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia in 51 consecutive patients with chronic therapy-resistant PD. They reported promising results with reduction of 84% for tremor, 70% for rigidity, and 73% for distal hypobradykinesia in motor outcomes and 55% reduction in antiparkinsonian medication dosage, with speech disturbances in only 6 patients in 1-year follow-up.

To address the multifaceted nature of PD, emerging approaches combine multiple lesion targets in a single session. For example, clinicians have treated an individual patient with two different target lesions concurrently—one in the VIM (to alleviate tremor) and one in the palli–dothalamic tract (PTT, to reduce rigidity/bradykinesia) (Fig. 2). Building on this concept, dual-target MRgFUS approaches have emerged to address the multifaceted nature of PD symptoms. Chen et al. pioneered stepwise dual-target MRgFUS, targeting both VIM and PTT in the same session, showing significant improvements in both resting and postural tremor along with rigidity and bradykinesia in tremor-dominant PD patients. Their initial feasibility study of three patients demonstrated substantial tremor reduction without major adverse effects, with sustained benefits confirmed in their 1-year follow-up study of 10 patients. This dual-target MRgFUS approach yielded improvements in both tremor and akinesia without major complications, illustrating a personalized lesion strategy.

As with thalamotomy, pallidothalamic tractotomy protocols have traditionally been unilateral due to concerns about bilateral lesion effects (e.g., speech or cognitive impairment). However, recent studies have demonstrated the feasibility and efficacy of staged bilateral pallidothalamic tractotomy for advanced PD. The pioneering study by Gallay et al. reported outcomes of 10 patients who underwent bilateral MRgFUS PTT, with 8 receiving staged procedures and 2 undergoing simultaneous bilateral treatment. At 1-year follow-up after the second procedure, patients showed 52% improvement in total Movement Disorder Society-Unified Parkinson’s Disease Rating Scale motor scores, with significant reductions in tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia. However, speech disturbances occurred in most patients as the primary persistent complication.

Building on this foundation, a larger phase 3 multi-center trial (NCT04728295) evaluated staged bilateral PTT across 9 centers in the United States, Europe, and Asia. This prospective, open-label study included up to 50 patients with bilateral idiopathic PD, with procedures staged at least 6 months apart. The positive results from this trial provided the evidence base for the FDA’s historic approval in July 2025 of staged bilateral pallidothalamic tractotomy for advanced PD.

Psychiatric disorders

Obsessive-compulsive disorder

MRgFUS has revitalized lesioning in psychiatry by enabling precise, image-guided ablative psychosurgery. Work in Korea beginning in 2013 established bilateral anterior limb of the internal capsule (ALIC) lesions (capsulotomy) as an effective MRgFUS treatment for refractory OCD. In an initial proof-of-concept study, Jung et al. created lesions in the ALIC of four OCD patients; follow-ups demonstrated significant symptom reduction (mean Yale–Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale, Y-BOCS, improvement >50%) with minimal side effects. Subsequent trials have confirmed and extended these findings. A 2-year follow-up study from the Korean group reported a 52% mean Y-BOCS improvement in 11 patients, with 7 of 10 meeting full response (≥35% Y-BOCS reduction) and 2 achieving clinical remission. Notably, some cognitive measures (e.g., memory) improved after capsulotomy, and no patients experienced permanent neurological deficits. More recently, a 10-year follow-up of that cohort was published, demonstrating sustained OCD symptom relief a decade post-treatment (mean 52.3% Y-BOCS improvement) and continued absence of serious adverse events. This provides the first long-term evidence that MRgFUS capsulotomy yields durable benefit in OCD. In parallel, a phase I trial in Toronto evaluated MRgFUS ALIC capsulotomy in 15 patients with severe, refractory OCD. At 6 months post-procedure, mean Y-BOCS scores improved by approximately 23%, and by 12 months the mean reduction was approximately 35% (p<0.0001). A recent systematic review reported an overall full-response rate of approximately 67% across published MRgFUS OCD studies (using the ≥35% Y-BOCS reduction criterion). These outcomes approach those of traditional radiofrequency or Gamma Knife capsulotomies, but MRgFUS offers a much less invasive approach. Interestingly, neuroimaging correlates suggest lesion location may influence outcomes—for instance, more lateralized right-sided lesions were associated with greater clinical improvement in one trial, whereas lesions extending more ventrally were linked to poorer response. Importantly, MRgFUS capsulotomy’s safety profile has been excellent: detailed neuropsychological testing reveals no cognitive decline post-procedure, and in some cases improvements in executive function are noted. This noninvasive approach thus appears to achieve efficacy comparable to conventional psychosurgery without the risks of open neurosurgery.

Major depressive disorder

MRgFUS investigations in treatment-resistant depression have run in parallel to OCD work, often targeting the same ALIC region. Initial pilot studies (e.g., at Sunnybrook in Toronto) treated small numbers of patients with severe major depressive disorder (MDD) via bilateral capsulotomy. Clinical outcomes have been more modest than in OCD. In one trial of 12 MDD patients, Hamilton Depression Rating Scale scores showed a non-significant mean reduction of approximately 26% at 6 months and 25% at 12 months. A systematic review found an overall response rate of approximately 33% for MRgFUS in depression (using ≥50% reduction in depression scale as criterion)—substantially lower than the approximately 67% response in OCD. These differences align with the known lower success rates of lesional surgery in depression compared to those in OCD. Notably, imaging analyses hint that certain lesion placements might yield better antidepressant effects (e.g., lesions more lateral in the right capsule were linked to greater mood improvement in one study). As with OCD, the safety profile in depression trials has been favorable. No negative cognitive effects have been observed; on the contrary, some tests have shown improved executive performance post-capsulotomy. Psychiatric side effects like apathy or affective blunting—sometimes seen with conventional capsulotomies—have not been reported with MRgFUS. Overall, MRgFUS capsulotomy for depression appears feasible and safe, though efficacy is less robust than for OCD. Ongoing studies are exploring whether alternative targets (e.g., subcallosal cingulate) or adjunctive therapies might enhance outcomes for depression.

Chronic pain syndromes

Functional neurosurgical lesions for refractory pain (e.g., thalamotomy, cingulotomy) have long been performed with radiofrequency (RF) or radiosurgery. These approaches have now been adapted to MRgFUS. Jeanmonod and colleagues pioneered MRgFUS for neuropathic pain by targeting the central lateral thalamus (CLT), a known pain-modulatory node. In 2012, they published a study of 12 patients with chronic therapy-resistant neuropathic pain who underwent MRgFUS CLT lesioning. At 3 months post-treatment, mean pain relief was approximately 42%, and at 1 year it remained approximately 41% (on visual analog scales), paralleling results from prior RF lesion series. Lesions were created at peak focal temperatures of 51℃–64℃. A larger follow-up series from the same group reported on 63 MRgFUS CLT procedures in 55 patients, with mean follow-up approximately 55 months. Long-term outcomes showed sustained efficacy: mean pain relief was approximately 42% at last follow-up, and over half of treated patients reported ≥50% pain reduction lasting multiple years. The proportion with ≥30% pain relief was approximately 65% at 3 months and approximately 61% at last follow-up, indicating that although some had partial relief, a significant subset achieved very meaningful benefit. The safety profile was favorable—only one serious adverse event (a transient upper lip numbness) occurred in the 63 treatments, and no persistent neurological deficits were seen aside from one case of mild residual hemiparesis. These data suggest MRgFUS thalamotomy for neuropathic pain can be as effective as invasive techniques, with stable pain reduction over years in responders. Other pain targets are also under investigation; for example, MRgFUS anterior cingulotomy has been piloted for cancer pain, and MRgFUS pallidothalamic tractotomy has been considered for central pain syndromes. While clinical experience remains limited regarding movement disorders, these studies demonstrate that MRgFUS lesioning is a viable option for refractory pain patients, offering pain relief magnitudes similar to RF ablation but without open surgery. Larger controlled trials will help determine optimal candidates and refine targeting to maximize analgesia while minimizing side effects.

Emerging and investigational applications

Dystonia

DBS is the standard treatment for generalized dystonia, but MRgFUS lesioning is under investigation for focal dystonias and select cases. A prospective pilot study in Japan treated 10 patients with focal hand dystonia (e.g., writer’s cramp) via unilateral VO-thalamotomy (lesioning the ventro-oral nucleus of the thalamus) using MRgFUS. At 12 months, patients showed significant improvements in dystonia severity scores; for example, the Writer’s Cramp Rating Scale improved by approximately 75%, and functional disability scores likewise improved (from 59% to 82% performance on the Arm Dystonia Disability Scale). Most adverse effects (mild dysarthria, paresthesias) were transient, although one patient experienced prolonged dysarthria that eventually resolved. This pilot suggests MRgFUS thalamotomy can substantially improve focal dystonia symptoms. In extreme cases of generalized or segmental dystonia, MRgFUS pallidotomy may be life-saving. There are case reports of MRgFUS being used emergently to terminate status dystonicus (life-threatening dystonic storms) in patients unresponsive to medication. For example, Levi et al. reported performing bilateral MRgFUS pallidotomy under general anesthesia in two young patients with severe pantothenate kinase-associated neurodegeneration and GNAO1 encephalopathy who were in status dystonicus. In both cases, the relentless dystonic storm resolved within days of the MRgFUS lesions. Only mild, transient side effects—such as postoperative facial weakness that recovered—were noted. While these are only case reports, they demonstrate the potential of MRgFUS as a rapid, incisionless intervention in dire dystonic emergencies when DBS or open surgery is not feasible. Systematic evidence in dystonia remains limited, and focal hand dystonia is the furthest along in clinical testing. Nonetheless, these early results suggest MRgFUS could have a role in treating certain dystonias, particularly focal forms or as a rescue therapy in status dystonicus.

Epilepsy

Preliminary investigations are evaluating MRgFUS ablation for focal epilepsy, especially for deep targets that are difficult to access surgically. One strategy is lesioning the anterior nucleus of the thalamus (ANT) to disrupt seizure networks—analogous to the ANT DBS approach for epilepsy. While completed results of ANT MRgFUS ablation have not yet been widely published, feasibility studies are ongoing. A phase 1 open-label trial (NCT03417297) is evaluating focused ultrasound unilateral anterior thalamotomy for focal-onset epilepsy, with early results showing successful ANT lesion creation and significant seizure reduction in preliminary participants. A completed phase 1 open-label trial demonstrated successful ANT lesion creation and significant seizure reduction, with one participant remaining seizure-free for 12 months. Additional case reports have demonstrated the feasibility of MRgFUS for other epileptic targets, including hypothalamic hamartoma disconnection surgery and mesial temporal lobe epilepsy treatment. Theoretical modeling studies have further supported the feasibility of targeting mesial temporal epilepsy circuits with focused ultrasound. Preclinical work in rodent epilepsy models has shown that pulsed FUS can suppress epileptic electroencephalogram (EEG) activity and seizures without tissue damage. For example, FUS sonication applied after inducing status epilepticus in rats resulted in a significant reduction of abnormal EEG discharges and seizure behaviors in the treatment group, whereas no such effect was observed in the sham group, confirming a true neuromodulatory (rather than lesional) effect.

Building on this, a recent trial (Yang et al., 2021) in non-human primates demonstrated state-dependent, bidirectional modulation of cortical activity with FUS, raising optimism that appropriate ultrasound parameters could both inhibit and excite neural circuits on-demand. Overall, while MRgFUS for epilepsy is still experimental, these studies open the possibility of a completely noninvasive epilepsy therapy. Lesional MRgFUS might provide an incisionless alternative to RF thermocoagulation for focal epileptic foci or network nodes, and LIFU neuromodulation could eventually be used for functional seizure interruption akin to neuromodulation devices. Further research will determine its clinical utility in epilepsy management.

Brain tumor ablation

Another emerging application is the direct thermal ablation of brain tumors via MRgFUS. Because high-intensity FUS can destroy tissue, there has been interest in applying it to tumor therapy, particularly for deep or difficult-to-reach lesions. A key milestone was reported by Coluccia et al. in 2014, who performed the first noninvasive transcranial MRgFUS ablation of a brain tumor. Their case involved a 63-year-old patient with recurrent glioblastoma located deep in the brain. Using 25 high-power sonications guided by MRI, they thermally ablated a portion of the tumor. Post-procedure MRI and clinical examination indicated successful partial tumor destruction without new neurological deficits. This first-in-human result demonstrated the feasibility of using MRgFUS to ablate tumor tissue through the intact skull.

An earlier feasibility study by McDannold et al. had attempted transcranial MRgFUS in three glioblastoma patients. While they showed that the system could focus ultrasound and produce measurable heating at the target, temperatures sufficient for coagulative necrosis could not be achieved due to power limitations at that time. Since then, technological improvements (e.g., more powerful phased arrays) have increased attainable focal temperatures. Nonetheless, routine MRgFUS tumor ablation faces challenges: skull heating limits energy delivery, and tumors often require larger-volume ablation than a single focus can provide. Research is ongoing—small series have explored MRgFUS ablation for metastases and radiation necrosis, and one trial is assessing FUS ablation of benign tumors such as vestibular schwannomas. With further advances (e.g., lower-frequency transducers for deeper penetration or novel sonosensitizers to amplify ultrasound effects), MRgFUS could become a tool for minimally invasive cytoreduction of certain brain tumors. At present, however, its role in tumor treatment remains investigational and is largely supplanted by its role in opening the BBB for adjunct therapy (discussed below).

Blood-brain barrier opening and drug delivery

A transformative MRgFUS application is transient BBB disruption to enhance therapeutic delivery to the brain. LIFU sonication combined with systemically injected microbubbles can reversibly open the BBB in a targeted region without thermal damage. Numerous preclinical studies have shown that MRgFUS-mediated BBB opening markedly increases central nervous system (CNS) delivery of various agents.

The mechanism involves stable cavitation of microbubbles in capillaries, producing mechanical forces that separate endothelial tight junctions and enhance transcytosis (Fig. 3). At low acoustic pressures, stable cavitation of gas-filled microbubbles, microstreaming, and acoustic radiation forces exert shear stress on endothelial cells, activating mechanosensitive ion channels or deforming the vascular endothelium. This yields BBB opening while maintaining cell viability. The opening is highly localized (on the order of the ultrasound focus, a few millimeters) and reversible. In vivo studies show that BBB closure typically occurs within 3 days after disruption, depending on acoustic pressure and pulse length, while tight junctions are generally restored within 4–24 hours in most cases. In clinical settings, temporary MRI contrast enhancement and reversible T2 signal changes serve as indicators of adequate BBB opening rather than pathological microbleeding.

Preclinical evidence

Extensive animal work in mice and rats has established the safety and efficacy of FUS-induced BBB opening. Typical parameters are frequencies of 0.22–1.5 MHz, peak negative pressures of 0.3–0.6 MPa, delivered in 10–20 ms bursts at 1–2 Hz for 60–120 s, with microbubble doses of 10–40 L/kg. MRI with gadolinium demonstrates immediate contrast extravasation in sonicated regions, confirming increased BBB permeability, which then resolves within a few days. Histology from these studies shows no neuronal death, only transient changes such as reversible microglial activation that subsides within 2 weeks. Larger animal studies in pigs and non-human primates have been crucial for moving to the clinic. For example, non-human primate experiments have achieved BBB opening in 90%–100% of targeted locations (e.g., various cortical and subcortical areas) using lower frequencies (220–500 kHz) better suited to human skulls. These showed excellent safety—even with repeated BBB openings over many months, animals exhibited no lasting neurological deficits, only temporary microscopic changes (e.g., mild glial reaction that resolved). Long-term primate studies up to approximately 20 months with multiple BBB disruption sessions have found no evidence of cumulative harm. Mechanistic studies reveal that FUS-induced BBB opening involves a dual mechanism: paracellular loosening of tight junction proteins (like claudin-5, occludin, ZO-1) and transcellular upregulation of vesicular transport. The ultrasound/microbubble effect causes tight junction displacement and increases endothelial fenestrations, while also downregulating efflux pumps such as P-glycoprotein. This results in a window of enhanced permeability (typically a few hours) during which therapeutics can diffuse into the brain interstitium at higher concentrations than normal. Importantly, the opening can be modulated by acoustic parameters and microbubble dose to avoid significant vascular damage—aggressive sonication can cause inertial cavitation leading to micro-hemorrhages, but the use of calibrated pressures and real-time cavitation monitoring has largely mitigated this risk in research settings.

A broad range of therapeutic agents have been successfully delivered across the BBB in preclinical models using FUS. Small-molecule chemotherapies show especially dramatic enhancement: doxorubicin concentrations in the brain increase by 2–4 fold with FUS-BBB opening, enabling the drug to reach therapeutic levels against intracranial tumors in rats. Similarly, studies have delivered paclitaxel by packaging it in thermosensitive liposomes and using FUS to both release the drug and open the BBB, achieving 2–3 fold higher drug concentrations and effective tumor inhibition. Temozolomide, the standard chemo for gliomas, also shows higher tumor accumulation with FUS-BBB disruption, with studies reporting enhanced cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) penetration and improved therapeutic efficacy. Large molecules benefit even more proportionally—monoclonal antibodies (approximately 150 kDa) such as anti-amyloid beta antibodies or trastuzumab (Herceptin) have achieved 5–12 fold higher brain uptake in targeted regions with FUS than without. This is highly significant, as therapeutic antibodies normally penetrate the BBB very poorly. Enhanced delivery of immunotherapies (checkpoint inhibitors, etc.) and even cells (e.g., neural stem cells, CAR-T cells) has been reported, suggesting FUS can improve not just drugs but also biologic therapy access to the CNS. Nanoparticle delivery (50–200 nm liposomes, polymeric nanoparticles, gold nanoshells) is also markedly improved by BBB opening, with studies showing 3–10 fold enhancement, raising possibilities for advanced drug carriers or theranostic agents to be effectively deployed to brain targets.

Combination with immunotherapy and gene therapy

The ability to focally open the BBB has spurred creative combined-treatment approaches. For example, MRgFUS BBB opening is being explored to facilitate immunotherapy for brain tumors by enabling better immune cell infiltration or delivery of immune modulators. Some protocols use FUS to induce a local “immunogenic” tumor microenvironment by releasing tumor antigens and enhancing immune cell entry, potentially boosting checkpoint inhibitor efficacy. Likewise, FUS has been shown in animals to improve delivery of gene therapy vectors (like adeno-associated virus [AAV]) to the brain. Focused ultrasound can increase AAV transduction in target regions 5–20 fold, enabling therapeutic gene expression with much lower systemic vector doses. This raises hope for noninvasive gene therapy for neurodegenerative diseases by focally delivering neurotrophic factors, enzyme replacements, or anti-aggregant genes across the BBB. Early studies demonstrate that FUS-mediated BBB opening can markedly improve distribution and uptake of gene vectors and cell-based therapies in the brain.

Given these promising findings, several clinical trials have now employed MRgFUS BBB opening in human patients, particularly for neuro-oncology and AD.

Human brain tumor studies

The Sunnybrook/University of Toronto group, led by Mainprize, conducted the first-in-human trial of MRgFUS BBB opening in patients with brain tumors. In 2018, they treated five patients with recurrent glioblastoma one day prior to surgical resection. The goals were to open the BBB around the tumor and assess drug concentrations in resected tissue. They demonstrated 15%–50% increased MRI contrast enhancement in sonicated tumor regions and confirmed higher liposomal doxorubicin levels in sonicated tissue versus non-sonicated tissue. No adverse clinical or radiologic events occurred, and the BBB resealed within 24 hours. This landmark study proved the safety and feasibility of FUS BBB opening in human brain tumors and suggested it could enhance drug delivery. Meanwhile, in France, Carpentier et al. have pursued an implantable ultrasound device (SonoCloud) to repeatedly open the BBB in glioblastoma patients. In their Phase I trial, 15–20 patients with recurrent glioblastoma (GBM) had a 1 MHz ultrasound emitter implanted in a skull bone window; monthly sonications coupled with IV microbubbles were done before each carboplatin chemotherapy cycle. This achieved BBB opening (confirmed by MRI) in the majority of sessions. The approach was well-tolerated with no serious device-related events, and some patients showed tumor regressions on MRI suggestive of improved chemo efficacy. Several centers have completed or are conducting pilot studies using the Exablate system to open the BBB around tumor resection cavities in newly diagnosed GBM patients receiving temozolomide. These have demonstrated the scalability of FUS BBB opening across multiple hospitals and established protocols for sonicating larger brain volumes (by electronically “steering” the focus across a grid to cover the resection bed).

A particularly notable series comes from a team in Korea led by Chang. In 2020, they reported on six glioblastoma patients who underwent multiple BBB opening sessions during adjuvant temozolomide therapy. The one-year survival rate in this FUS-treated group was 100%. In a companion publication detailing the procedure, they documented 145 total BBB disruption sessions with a 92% technical success rate and no procedure-related complications—establishing safety and repeatability even with frequent treatments (up to six cycles with the temozolomide schedule). While patient numbers were small, the survival outcome was striking.

Human Alzheimer’s disease studies

Perhaps the most groundbreaking MRgFUS application is in AD. The prospect of opening the BBB to enhance delivery of anti-amyloid antibodies (and possibly mobilize amyloid clearance independently) has driven multiple AD trials. Lipsman et al. at Sunnybrook performed the first human AD trial in 2017–2018. They treated five patients with early-to-moderate AD, targeting the right frontal lobe in a repeated manner (each patient received two BBB opening sessions, one month apart). This study demonstrated that FUS could repeatedly open the BBB in AD patients safely, reversibly (closure in <24 h), and without cognitive decline or MRI abnormalities. Although not powered for efficacy, there were hints of possible memory benefit, and importantly, no worsening on detailed neuropsychological testing over 3 months.

Subsequently, Park et al., in Korea, expanded the approach by opening much larger brain volumes. In 2021, they published a proof-of-concept study treating bilateral frontal lobes in six AD patients. Each session sonicated volumes >20 cm3 (significantly larger than in prior studies) and each patient underwent two sessions a few months apart. Despite the extensive BBB opening, the procedure was well-tolerated and feasible. Intriguingly, amyloid positron emission tomography (PET) imaging showed a reduction in amyloid burden in the treated regions in some patients, and caregivers reported slight cognitive improvements on the Neuropsychiatric Inventory in one case. These findings suggest that more aggressive BBB opening, with appropriate safety measures, may be necessary for meaningful clinical or pathological effect in AD, challenging earlier cautious approaches that targeted very small brain areas.

Perhaps also the most comprehensive AD FUS program has been at West Virginia University by Rezai and colleagues. They have completed multiple trials and reported landmark findings. In 2020, they published a pilot in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences of the United States of America showing successful hippocampal BBB opening in 6 AD patients with 95% of planned targets achieved, again with no serious adverse events. In 2022, they reported long-term safety results from 10 AD patients who underwent up to three courses of BBB opening targeting the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex (30 total sessions). This represented the largest such cohort with the longest follow-up (6–12 months). Impressively, they found a 14% reduction in β-amyloid plaque burden (on PET Centiloid scale) in the sonicated brain regions compared to those at baseline. This provided the first human evidence that FUS BBB opening alone can reduce amyloid pathology in the brain. Cognitively, patients had no accelerated decline compared to AD controls, and some showed stabilization of symptoms over 1 year. Building on this, Rezai’s team initiated the first trial combining MRgFUS with an anti-amyloid antibody (aducanumab). In 2024, they published results in The New England Journal of Medicine demonstrating that monthly FUS BBB opening immediately before aducanumab infusions led to greater amyloid clearance on PET and CSF biomarkers than aducanumab alone. This is a groundbreaking proof-of-concept that focused ultrasound can enhance drug delivery and efficacy in a neurodegenerative disease.

Beyond simply enabling drug entry, FUS may have its own therapeutic mechanisms in AD. The act of repeatedly opening the BBB appears to stimulate beneficial processes: amyloid clearance is thought to occur partly via increased entry of endogenous anti-amyloid antibodies and activation of microglia to phagocytose plaques, as well as complement-mediated opsonization of amyloid deposits. FUS-induced BBB opening also provokes an immune response in the brain—transiently upregulating genes like TREM2 and APOE that are involved in microglial activation and lipid metabolism, recruiting peripheral macrophages and T-cells to amyloid-laden regions, and modulating neuroinflammation from a chronic harmful state to an acute clearing state. Enhanced glymphatic clearance has been observed as well: FUS can increase the exchange of interstitial and cerebrospinal fluid along perivascular channels, potentially accelerating the removal of toxic proteins like amyloid and tau from the brain parenchyma. In animal models, FUS has been shown to improve glymphatic flow and reduce amyloid levels even without any exogenous drug. Other effects reported include promotion of hippocampal neurogenesis, improvements in synaptic plasticity, increased regional cerebral blood flow, and restoration of neurovascular coupling in AD models. These pleiotropic effects suggest that FUS might confer some therapeutic benefit in AD beyond just helping drugs get in. Larger ongoing trials (e.g., a multi-center study combining FUS with lecanemab) will further elucidate these contributions.

Low-intensity neuromodulation

A rapidly growing area of MRgFUS research is its use for direct neuromodulation—i.e., transiently altering brain circuit activity using low-intensity ultrasound without causing lesions. Recent preclinical studies confirm that LIFU can directly excite or inhibit neuronal firing through non-thermal mechanical mechanisms. Mechanistic investigations have identified specific ion channels that mediate this effect. For example, a 2022 study by Yoo et al. showed that focused ultrasound primarily excites cortical neurons by activating calcium-permeable mechanosensitive channels (e.g., TRPC and ASIC) on the neuronal membrane. This activation leads to gradual intracellular Ca2+ accumulation, amplified by voltage-gated Ca2+ and Na+ channels, ultimately triggering bursts of action potentials. The study carefully ruled out confounding factors—neural excitation occurred without detectable cavitation, negligible temperature rise (<0.1℃), and without requiring synaptic transmission—indicating a direct mechanical activation of neurons. Pharmacological inhibition or genetic knockdown of these mechanosensitive channels significantly reduced neuronal responsiveness to FUS, whereas overexpression rendered neurons hyper-responsive, providing strong evidence for their causal role. These findings represent a significant advance in understanding ultrasound–neuron interactions at the cellular level and lay the groundwork for sonogenetics—the use of ultrasound-responsive mediators for noninvasive, selective control of neural activity.

Beyond cellular studies, in vivo experiments in animal brains have shown that ultrasound’s neuromodulatory effects can be state-dependent and bidirectional. In other words, the same ultrasound stimulus may increase firing in one brain state but decrease it in another. For instance, a 2021 study by Yang et al. in macaque monkeys applied 250 kHz ultrasound to the somatosensory cortex and found that, when the cortex was in a quiescent baseline state, FUS induced excitatory responses, but if the cortex was already activated (by tactile input), the same FUS could inhibit neural firing. This bidirectional modulation suggests that ultrasound might preferentially nudge circuits toward homeostasis—exciting underactive neurons and suppressing overactive ones—though this remains under investigation. The therapeutic implications are substantial: LIFU could potentially be tuned to normalize aberrant brain activity in conditions such as epilepsy, chronic pain, depression, or addiction without destroying tissue.

While sub-ablative MRgFUS neuromodulation studies in psychiatric disorders remain limited, neuronavigation-guided focused ultrasound and transcranial focused ultrasound (tFUS) studies have demonstrated preliminary modulation effects in depression and cognitive control networks, suggesting a translational continuum toward future MRgFUS-based protocols.

Substance use disorder

One especially promising neuromodulatory application is in SUD. The nucleus accumbens (NAc) in the mesolimbic reward pathway has been a target of interest for addiction (Fig. 4). Low-intensity FUS can reach the NAc noninvasively, unlike transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) or transcranial direct current stimulation which cannot effectively target such deep regions (Fig. 5). Early developments were led by a team at West Virginia University. In 2023, Mahoney et al. published the first-in-human trial of LIFU neuromodulation for SUD. They delivered focused ultrasound to the bilateral NAc of four patients with severe opioid and polysubstance use disorder, as a single 20-minute session (10 min sonication each side). The procedure was safe and well-tolerated, with no adverse events and no MRI-detected tissue changes. Remarkably, patients reported immediate reductions in drug craving during and after the sonication. Visual analog scale craving ratings for their primary substances dropped significantly (e.g., one patient’s opioid craving score decreased by approximately 50% acutely), whereas sham sonication produced no change. In two patients who received a higher ultrasound dose (90 W vs. 60 W), daily craving logs over the subsequent week showed substantial decreases compared to those observed in pre-treatment baseline. Through 3 months of follow-up, all four patients maintained reductions in cue-induced craving for multiple substances, and notably, most did not return to illicit drug use during that period.

Building on these encouraging results, the same investigators expanded the study to a larger cohort. In an open-label pilot involving eight patients with severe opioid use disorder, they again found that a single bilateral NAc LIFU session produced striking and durable effects. On average, participants experienced a 91% reduction in opioid craving scores that persisted throughout 90 days of follow-up. Objective outcomes were also promising—most participants provided drug-free urine screens and had vastly reduced self-reported substance use in the months after treatment. Functional MRI data indicated that FUS neuromodulation normalized the connectivity of reward circuitry: specifically, aberrant hyperconnectivity between the NAc and prefrontal cortex was reduced post-treatment, suggesting a restoration toward healthy network function. No serious adverse events occurred in the expanded cohort either. These preliminary findings, while uncontrolled, hint at the extraordinary potential of MRgFUS neuromodulation in addiction. They show that focused ultrasound can noninvasively reach deep addiction hubs and causatively modulate them to produce lasting behavioral changes (reduced craving and use). A sham-controlled trial in Korea (NCT06474026) for methamphetamine users is now underway to formally test efficacy. If validated, MRgFUS could emerge as a novel neuromodulation therapy for SUD—offering outpatient, incisionless “tuning” of reward circuits to suppress cravings, without the need for implanted stimulators or frequent TMS sessions.

COMPARISON WITH ALTERNATIVE MODALITIES

When considering MRgFUS, it is useful to compare it to other lesioning and neuromodulation techniques. A comparative overview of lesioning and neuromodulation modalities used in functional neurosurgery is summarized in Table 1. The table highlights the key differences among MRgFUS, DBS, RF lesioning, Gamma Knife radiosurgery, and MRI-guided laser interstitial thermal therapy (LITT) in terms of invasiveness, reversibility, targeting precision, and clinical applicability.

Deep brain stimulation vs. MRgFUS

DBS is an established surgical therapy for medication-refractory tremor and PD. Both DBS and MRgFUS can produce significant symptom improvement and quality-of-life gains, but they differ in invasiveness and side-effect profiles. Head-to-head comparisons indicate that the main determinant of tremor efficacy is whether the treatment is bilateral or unilateral, rather than the modality. For example, bilateral DBS is more effective than a single unilateral MRgFUS thalamotomy for ET, whereas unilateral DBS and unilateral MRgFUS provide similar tremor control. This suggests that MRgFUS could achieve parity with DBS if bilateral treatments are applied, which recent studies have shown is feasible. In terms of procedure, MRgFUS offers clear advantages: it is entirely incisionless, does not implant any hardware, and typically incurs lower overall cost (no device or battery replacements). The therapeutic effect of MRgFUS is immediate—patients often have tremor arrest during the sonication, as observed on real-time exam. By contrast, DBS requires a post-surgical programming period to optimize settings. However, DBS has its own advantages. It is adjustable and (in theory) reversible—stimulation parameters can be tuned to patient needs and turned off or leads repositioned if side effects occur. MRgFUS lesions, once made, cannot be altered; an imprecise lesion or unforeseen deficit is permanent. This lack of adaptability is especially relevant in progressive diseases like PD—DBS can be reprogrammed as symptoms evolve, whereas a lesion is static. Another consideration is bilateral treatment: DBS can be done bilaterally in one session for tremor or PD (with an acceptable risk profile in experienced centQolers), whereas MRgFUS historically was unilateral only, though staged bilateral thalamotomy and pallidotomy are now being explored. Safety profiles also differ. Meta-analyses suggest MRgFUS thalamotomy has higher rates of certain persistent neurologic side effects (like mild ataxia or paresthesias) than DBS, whereas DBS carries risks of surgical complications (hemorrhage, infection) and chronic hardware issues.

While MRgFUS and DBS are sometimes viewed as competing modalities, they may serve complementary roles depending on patient characteristics and symptom profiles. For instance, subthalamic nucleus (STN) DBS is often preferred for bradykinetic or rigidity-dominant PD due to its adjustable stimulation and bilateral capability, whereas MRgFUS has been more commonly utilized in tremor-dominant phenotypes or to manage levodopa-induced dyskinesia via unilateral pallidotomy or pallido-thalamic tractotomy. It emphasizes the potential for tailored application of each modality based on clinical phenotype and treatment goals.

Radiofrequency lesioning vs. MRgFUS

Prior to MRgFUS, RF thermocoagulation via stereotactic surgery was the conventional way to create lesions (e.g., thalamotomy, pallidotomy). RF lesioning is effective for tremor and other indications, but it requires a burr hole and probe insertion to the target—thus carrying similar surgical risks to DBS (bleeding, infection) and often similar long-term side effects as any lesion. The amergence of MRgFUS thalamotomy has revitalized the perceived utility of RF ablation thalamotomy in managing movement disorders. In terms of clinical efficacy, a properly placed RF lesion in VIM achieves tremor suppression comparable to MRgFUS or radiosurgical lesions. Systematic reviews have found that RF thalamotomy, DBS, Gamma Knife, and MRgFUS all significantly improve tremor, though onset and adverse profiles differ.

MRgFUS offers major procedural advantages over RF. It is incisionless and transcranial, eliminating the need for skull opening and electrode trajectory. Thus, MRgFUS avoids risks like intracerebral hemorrhage along the probe path, infection, and anesthesia complications. Another key advantage is real-time feedback: MRgFUS allows low-power test sonications to produce reversible effects or mild heating, confirming the target before making the permanent lesion. The surgeon can observe tremor improvement or MRI thermal spot location and adjust aim accordingly, then deliver a high-power sonication for the final lesion. RF lesioning has much more limited intraoperative feedback—typically only clinical test stimulation and patient reporting; lesion size and placement are only confirmed after the lesion has been created (often with postoperative imaging). The stepwise, feedback-guided nature of MRgFUS likely contributes to its favorable safety profile, as inadvertent off-target lesions can be avoided.

On the other hand, RF lesioning can reach virtually any target the surgeon can cannulate—there is no limitation except surgical access. MRgFUS targeting can be constrained by acoustic factors and current MRI visualization; extremely deep or inferior targets may be hard to sonicate due to skull absorption or inaccessible angles. In practice, some patients cannot receive MRgFUS because of unfavorable skull properties (see Procedural Considerations), whereas RF could still be done in those cases. RF equipment is also far less expensive and complex than MRgFUS, meaning it remains widely available and cost-effective globally. Indeed, despite newer techniques, RF thalamotomy continues to be used, given its long track record and low cost.

Creating a lesion (with any method) carries a certain fixed risk of irreversible deficit. MRgFUS appears to lower that risk slightly thanks to imaging and titration, but it is not zero. In summary, MRgFUS is essentially an advancement of the lesioning concept—it provides a less invasive and more controlled way to lesion, but the end result (a burn in the target) is akin to RF. Both will likely continue to have roles, with MRgFUS gradually replacing RF for many indications in centers where it’s available, while RF remains important in settings where resource constraints or patient factors favor a tried-and-true, simpler approach.

Stereotactic radiosurgery (Gamma Knife) vs. MRgFUS

Both MRgFUS thalamotomy and Gamma Knife thalamotomy are incisionless techniques for tremor. Each has proven efficacy in unilateral ET treatment. However, their mechanisms differ (thermal vs. radiation injury), and so do their time courses. The most striking difference is onset of therapeutic effect. MRgFUS lesioning stops tremor essentially immediately—patients often have near-complete tremor arrest by the end of the sonication session. In contrast, radiosurgical lesions (e.g., 130–150 Gy to VIM) cause delayed effects; patients may wait 1–3 months for maximal tremor relief as the radiation slowly induces tissue necrosis.

Another key difference is real-time control. MRgFUS provides MRI visualization and thermometry during the lesion creation, allowing verification of the lesion location and size in vivo. If the tremor arrest is insufficient or the lesion slightly mispositioned, additional sonications can be applied immediately to adjust. Gamma Knife offers no such feedback—targeting is based on preoperative images and tissue response is invisible at the time of treatment. The lesion can only be confirmed on follow-up MRI weeks later, and if tremor persists there is no straightforward way to retreat early other than repeating the radiosurgery months later.

In terms of safety, Gamma Knife involves delivering high-dose ionizing radiation to brain tissue. This carries a risk (albeit low) of radiation necrosis or delayed cognitive effects, and radiation exposure limits retreatment or additional dosing. MRgFUS uses no radiation, avoiding these concerns entirely. On the other hand, Gamma Knife is less limited by skull characteristics—dense skulls that might reflect or absorb ultrasound pose no problem for radiation. Patients disqualified from MRgFUS due to low skull density ratio (SDR) can still get Gamma Knife thalamotomy.

Side effect profiles also differ. Radiosurgical thalamotomy can cause edema and inflammation that peak weeks after treatment, which in up to 10% of cases leads to transient side effects (imbalance, sensory disturbances) during that period. These usually resolve as the radiation lesion matures. Because of the delayed lesioning, Gamma Knife has virtually no risk of acute neurological deficits right after the procedure (unlike MRgFUS or RF which, if off-target, cause immediate symptoms). In the long term, both techniques have comparable efficacy and complication rates, though MRgFUS is associated with a slightly higher incidence of acute side effects (e.g., immediate ataxia or paresthesia), while Gamma Knife has the aforementioned delayed risks.

Overall tremor relief magnitude is similar (often 50%–60% improvement in tremor severity scores, and significant gains in writing, drawing, and feeding tasks). With MRgFUS, there may be a slightly greater tremor reduction in some cases, but with a slightly higher risk of minor side effects (likely due to the immediacy of lesion). Gamma Knife provides equivalent long-term benefit with a different risk/time profile.

In practice, MRgFUS is often preferred now for ET if available, given the immediacy and avoidance of radiation. However, Gamma Knife remains an important option for patients who cannot undergo MRgFUS or who prefer a completely sensation-free outpatient procedure (some patients find MRgFUS headframe and sonications uncomfortable, whereas Gamma Knife is painless apart from headframe placement).

MR-guided laser interstitial thermal therapy

An emerging minimally invasive alternative is MRI-guided LITT, which ablates tissue via an optical fiber inserted through a small burr hole. LITT, like MRgFUS, creates thermal lesions under MRI thermometry control, but it is invasive. Recent pilot studies of LITT thalamotomy show promising results. In a 2024 single-center series, nine patients with ET underwent unilateral MRI-guided LITT ablation of VIM. At 12 months, tremor severity improved by 83.4% and quality-of-life scores by approximately 75%. This degree of tremor reduction is comparable with other modalities.

No significant cognitive changes were noted after LITT, and most side effects were temporary (transient dysarthria or balance disturbance in a few patients). One patient (11%) had a persistent mild hemiparesis at 1 year, highlighting that risk of hitting internal capsule is present with any lesional method.

MRgFUS has an advantage over LITT in being completely extracorporeal—no penetration of the skull or brain, and thus no bleeding risk along a trajectory. However, LITT can potentially treat larger or multiple targets in one session by repositioning the laser fiber, something not easily done with a fixed ultrasound focal point. Additionally, patients with unfavorable skulls (who fail MRgFUS screening) could still be candidates for LITT.

The adverse event profile of LITT In Initial studies appears acceptable and somewhat similar to other lesion approaches. More research is needed with larger cohorts and perhaps direct comparisons to MRgFUS. Currently, no trials have directly compared MRgFUS with LITT head-to-head, but both achieve tremor reduction by ablating the same targets. Choice may ultimately come down to availability and patient-specific considerations (e.g., LITT under local anesthesia might be an option for those who cannot tolerate MRgFUS acoustic coupling or prolonged supine MRI time).

Clinical decision framework

The expansion of incisionless surgical options raises questions about how to choose among MRgFUS, DBS, RF, Gamma Knife, or LITT for an individual patient. Key considerations include the patient’s overall health and preferences, symptom characteristics (unilateral vs. bilateral, severity, etiology), and the risk profile of each modality. All of these interventions require accurate targeting to be effective—a factor common to their success.

MRgFUS occupies a unique niche by offering incisionless treatment with efficacy approaching invasive surgery. For patients who are poor surgical candidates (e.g., due to age or comorbidities), have contraindications to implanted hardware (infection risk, no MRI follow-up if pacemaker, etc.), limited access to ongoing device programming, or simply a strong preference for non-invasive therapy, MRgFUS is extremely compelling. It can often be done in a single session3 with immediate results and no need for general anesthesia or hospitalization. Gamma Knife may be preferred if the skull is too thick or dense for MRgFUS, or if a patient cannot tolerate the MR environment but can undergo radiosurgery.

DBS remains favored for cases where adjustability is paramount (e.g., young Parkinson’s patients who will need changes over time). In some scenarios, combining modalities might yield optimal outcomes (for instance, a patient with tremor-dominant Parkinson’s disease might have MRgFUS on VIM and DBS on STN if bradykinetic symptoms progress). A patient with a partial response to MRgFUS may subsequently undergo DBS if needed. Conversely, MRgFUS can also be considered in selected cases where DBS fails or is not feasible.

As MRgFUS technology continues to advance—addressing current limitations in targeting and expanding treatable patient populations—it is likely to become a first-line option for an increasing range of functional neurosurgery indications. Already, for medication-refractory ET, many centers consider MRgFUS thalamotomy a treatment of choice for appropriately selected candidates. Ongoing studies in PD, psychiatric conditions, and other indications suggest that the role of MRgFUS will continue to grow, either replacing certain traditional surgeries or augmenting the neurosurgical toolkit as a complementary, less-invasive alternative.

PROCEDURAL CONSIDERATIONS

Lesion size and targeting constraints

Lesion size correlates with the accumulated thermal dose; however, MRgFUS produces relatively small lesion volumes, typically only a few cubic millimeters per sonication. Large or diffuse targets (e.g., extensive cortical areas, multi-centimeter tumors) cannot be fully ablated in a reasonable time without risking off-target heating. Current clinical use therefore focuses on small targets like specific nuclei or tracts. Anatomical accessibility is another constraint—midline and paramedian structures (thalamus, pallidum, etc.) near the brain’s center are ideal, whereas far lateral targets are harder to sonicate because the skull angle is more oblique (increasing reflection) and the distance is greater. Extremely inferior or skull-base–adjacent targets (e.g., deep brainstem or lower cerebellum) lie outside the MRgFUS transducer’s optimal geometric focus and are prone to excessive skull heating, limiting treatment primarily to mid-to-upper brain regions.

Another consequence of small lesion size is that achieving a therapeutic effect often depends on precise placement within an optimal target “sweet spot.” In ET, investigators have identified a specific subregion of the VIM that, when targeted, results in optimal tremor improvements while reducing side effects. Studies show that smaller lesions confined to the certain subregion of VIM correlate with better tremor reduction and fewer side effects than larger lesions extending beyond the optimal zone. One study found that achieving an ablation volume <0.06 cc within the ideal nucleus was associated with maximal tremor benefit and minimal ataxia. Some studies suggested that diffusion tractography can help identify specific white matter tracts of potential importance and serves as a complementary approach to existing targeting methods.

Skull properties and treatment eligibility

A fundamental technical challenge for transcranial FUS is the skull’s attenuation and distortion of ultrasound. The cranial bones absorb and reflect much of the ultrasound energy, and their irregular thickness and density cause aberration of the beams. In fact, only a minority of the emitted ultrasound power reaches the target in many cases. Patients with very thick or dense skulls may not receive sufficient focal energy for lesioning, as too much is lost as heat in the bone.

To quantify skull transmissivity, clinicians use the SDR—the ratio of cortical to trabecular bone density on CT—as a predictor of ultrasound penetration efficiency. Empirically, an SDR≥0.45 correlates with successful MRgFUS treatment; pivotal ET trials excluded patients below 0.40–0.45 SDR.

Patients with low SDR (<0.40) often require higher power and more sonications to reach target temperatures, and even then treatment may fail or be incomplete because the skull absorbs too much energy. That said, recent evidence suggests SDR thresholds need not be absolute. Successful thalamotomy has been reported in patients with SDR well below 0.45 by adjusting protocols (e.g., longer sonications). While low SDR patients tend to have less efficient treatments, many can still be treated safely with careful parameter adjustments.

Besides density, other skull features affect outcomes—for example, focal hyperostosis or abnormal contours can create “cold spots” or reflections that limit lesion formation. Variations in SDR across different skull regions (heterogeneity) may be more relevant than mean SDR. Studies have noted geographic and demographic disparities: an analysis of tremor patients in Korea suggests that the SDR in Asian populations is lower than in Western populations, reducing eligibility rates.

Technological advances to overcome skull limitations

Ongoing innovations aim to improve energy delivery in patients with suboptimal skull characteristics. One notable advancement is autofocusing echo-guidance. This technique uses microbubbles as acoustic reflectors to fine-tune the phase and amplitude of each transducer element in real time. In practice, microbubbles are injected, low-power sonications are delivered, and returning echoes from the microbubbles and skull are analyzed to detect phase distortions. The system then adjusts each element’s output to correct residual errors beyond the initial CT-based correction. Essentially, the skull is “pinged” and “listened to,” allowing adaptive refocusing—similar to how adaptive optics correct telescope images. In this study involving ET and PD patients with low SDR (mean 0.34–0.41), MRgFUS thalamotomy or pallidotomy performed using an autofocusing echo imaging technique achieved adequate lesion temperatures (55℃–56℃) without serious adverse events, suggesting that this method may safely expand MRgFUS eligibility to patients previously considered unsuitable due to low SDR. An adaptive aberration correction technique based on backscattered signals from intravenously injected microbubbles has been developed to overcome skull-induced phase distortion. While initially motivated by therapeutic applications such as MRgFUS, this approach has also been successfully applied to cerebrovascular imaging, further supporting its technical feasibility and robustness.

Other engineering improvements under exploration include using lower-frequency ultrasound (e.g., 220 kHz) to reduce skull absorption (lower frequencies penetrate bone better, albeit with larger focal spots), developing new transducer array geometries that conform more closely to the head (to improve energy transmission and coverage), and accelerating MR thermometry to allow faster feedback and tighter control. Collectively, these refinements are steadily enhancing the reliability and breadth of MRgFUS. As technology and techniques improve, it is expected that a greater proportion of patients—even those with challenging skull anatomy—will become eligible for safe and effective MRgFUS treatments.

MRgFUS limitations

Irreversible nature and lack of adjustability

Unlike neuromodulation technologies (DBS, transcranial stimulation) which can be adjusted over time, MRgFUS creates a permanent lesion. Once sonications are delivered and the target is lesioned, there is no turning back. This means optimal target selection is crucial—if lesioning the wrong location or if a patient’s symptoms later shift (as in progressive diseases), one cannot reprogram a lesion. For example, in PD, a lesion made in the current disease stage cannot adapt to future symptoms, whereas DBS can be reprogrammed or even relocated with a new surgery. The irreversibility also raises the stakes for side effects: any unintended damage (such as a lesion affecting internal capsule causing weakness) cannot be undone. MRgFUS is currently approved only for unilateral treatments largely for this reason—bilateral permanent lesions raise greater risk (e.g., for speech or cognitive adverse effects), whereas DBS can safely be done bilaterally because stimulation can be adjusted or turned off if issues arise. This limitation is gradually being challenged by new evidence (as bilateral FUS trials show safety), but caution is still warranted. Patients and clinicians must understand that MRgFUS trades adjustability for noninvasiveness—it is a one-time shot that must be placed perfectly.

Absence of tissue for pathology

Unlike an open surgical resection or biopsy, MRgFUS does not provide tissue samples. In functional cases like ET or PD this is not an issue (no tissue needed), but in applications like tumor ablation, lack of tissue confirmation can be a drawback. If MRgFUS is used to ablate a tumor, one forfeits the ability to get pathology from that tissue (unless a prior biopsy was done). In most functional indications this is inconsequential—e.g., for tremor, we do not require tissue diagnosis, and leaving the lesion in situ is fine. But it does limit MRgFUS use in scenarios where tissue characterization is important (such as initial treatment of a brain tumor or an undiagnosed lesion). At present, MRgFUS is not intended as a diagnostic intervention, and if pathology is needed, a biopsy or resection is still required.

Patient tolerance and anatomic factors

MRgFUS procedures typically last 2–4 hours and require the patient to lie still in an MRI scanner, often awake (for neurological monitoring). This can be challenging for some patients with claustrophobia, not tolerate the MRI environment. Additionally, the head is fixed in a frame which some find uncomfortable, and the sonications themselves (each lasting 10–40 seconds) can cause sensations of head heating, vertigo, or a pinging sound. While usually mild, these sensations can distress certain individuals. Patients with implanted devices contraindicated for MRI (pacemakers without MR-conditional status, older neurostimulators, cochlear implants, etc.) are generally excluded, as MRgFUS requires MRI throughout. This narrows eligibility compared to that of other modalities like RF or radiosurgery which do not need MRI during treatment. Finally, because MRgFUS causes an immediate lesion, patients must usually be kept awake for at least part of the procedure to perform neurological testing (e.g., checking for tremor improvement or side effects during test sonications). This awake requirement is similar to awake craniotomy or DBS and may not be suitable for all (though some centers have explored conscious sedation or even general anesthesia for MRgFUS in special cases). Overall, careful patient selection and preparation are required to ensure the patient can comfortably complete the procedure. Though burdensome, these requirements are offset by the fact that MRgFUS is typically a single session outpatient treatment—there is no prolonged recovery or multiple visits as with some other therapies.

FUTURE DIRECTION

Thermal ablation of HIFU remains the primary clinical application of MRgFUS, but emerging evidence indicates that LIFU can reversibly modulate deep brain circuits without tissue damage, heralding a new neuro-modulatory role in functional neurosurgery. LIFU enables incisionless targeting of structures implicated in neuropsychiatric disorders—offering a noninvasive approach to conditions like addiction, depression, and epilepsy. Recent human studies highlight this potential: for example, MRI-guided LIFU sonication of the NAc in patients with severe SUDs produced immediate and significant reductions in drug craving, with follow-up reports demonstrating dramatic (mean 91%) reductions in craving that persisted for at least three months after a single session.

For depression and epilepsy, most current studies have used tFUS without MRI guidance, demonstrating measurable antidepressant effects in early clinical trials and seizure suppression in preclinical models. These findings illustrate that LIFU can directly modulate dysfunctional brain networks while avoiding permanent tissue injury. As imaging and targeting techniques advance, these applications may transition into the MRgFUS indication, offering the precision and safety of MRI-guided delivery.

As such, low-intensity MRgFUS may evolve into a scalable neuromodulation platform—offering DBS-like benefits with fewer side effects and broader accessibility. In this way, MRgFUS is poised to transform from a purely ablative tool into a versatile, circuit-based neuromodulatory modality for neuropsychiatric disease.
