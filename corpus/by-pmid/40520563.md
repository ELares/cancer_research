# IL-15 functionalized biomimetic hybrid mRNA vaccine for enhanced NSCLC immunotherapy via synergistic activation of T cells and NK cells.

## Abstract

CD8+T cells-based mRNA vaccines represent a promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy. However, their development is significantly limited by the low-expression of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) I on tumor cells, which impairs effective antigen presentation and T cells recognition. Given that natural killer (NK) cells can kill tumors in an MHC-independent manner, dual activation of T cells and NK cells has the potential to enhance the efficacy of immunotherapy. Herein, we developed an IL-15 functionalized biomimetic hybrid mRNA vaccine (LMPR) that activates NK cells in collaboration with CD8+T cells, compensating the MHC-dependent limitation to boost antitumor therapy. Once vaccinated, LMPR is effectively taken up by dendritic cells (DCs) and successfully translated into antigenic proteins to initiate antigen-specific T-cell immune responses. Meanwhile, IL-15/IL-15 receptor α (IL-15Rα) induces oxidative stress tolerance of NK cells by regulating the balance of thioredoxin system, thereby promoting the proliferation of NK cells and synergistically exerting anti-tumor effect with CD8+T cells. Upon intranasal administration to lewis lung cancer (LLC) -bearing mice, LMPR signifcantly promotes infiltration of CD8+T cells and NK cells, accompanied by elevated level of interferon-γ (IFN-γ) and Granzyme B (GZMB), exhibiting superior cytotoxicity in suppressing tumor growth. Notably, LMPR potently facilitates the up-regulation of T-bet, thus enriching the differentiation of effector memory T cell (Tem) to inhibit tumor recurrence. Therefore, this innovative approach elicits dual activation by CD8+T cells and NK cells, holding great potential for potentiating therapeutic mRNA vaccination antitumor immunity.

Introduction

Cancer vaccines have attracted extensive attention as promising immunotherapy tool for the treatment of solid tumors. In cancer vaccines, messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines trigger an unprecedented wave of innovation because of their unique advantages. mRNA vaccines utilize in vitro transcribed mRNA (IVT mRNA) to stimulate immune system combating tumors effectively, hence any antigen protein of interest could theoretically be designed to treat cancer. To be specific, mRNA vaccines are internalized by antigen-presenting cells (APCs), primarily dendritic cells (DCs). mRNA encoding tumor antigen is translated into antigenic protein in cytosol, which subsequently is submitted on the cellular surface to CD8+T cells through MHC I. Upon activation, tumor-specific CD8+T cells bind tumour cell-derived antigens presented by MHC I on the tumour surface through T cell receptor (TCR), recognizing and killing tumor. From this, CD8+T lymphocytes play a pivotal role in mRNA vaccine-mediated tumor immunotherapy, demonstrating their ability to recognize and eliminate malignant cells. However, tumors are capable of evading presentation of tumor-associated antigens and subsequently T cell-mediated cytotoxicity by downregulating surface expression of MHC I. Several studies pinpoint MHC-I expression on tumour cells is correlated with the number of tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TIL), which substantiates the importance of MHC-I downregulation in tumor immune evasion. Accordingly, it is highly desirable to exploit new strategies that can relieve MHC molecule restrictions in the tumor immune microenvironment (TME), orchestrating the subsequent CD8+T cells antitumor response in mRNA vaccine anti-cancer therapy.

Unlike CD8+T cells, NK cells, as cytotoxic immune cells, can recognize tumor cells through multiple activating receptors without antigen presentation. Moreover, they can secrete abundant cytokines and bioactive protein such as tumor necrosis factor (TNF) α, perforin, granzyme, after activation, thereby enhancing overall cytotoxicity against cancer cells. Interestingly, tumor cells with low-expressed MHC I are more sensitive to NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity, suggesting that it may provide the rationale for compensating for MHC restrictions and achieving synergistic antitumor effect through both T cells and NK cells. Nonetheless, various mechanisms of TME could result in dysfunction of NK cells within tumors, evading NK cell-mediated cytotoxicity. In TME, oxidative stress can compromise NK cells, particularly through the generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), leading to DNA damage and apoptosis under normal physiological conditions of NK cells. Therefore, strategies aimed at restoring NK cells activation and enhancing cooperation with T cells are expected to improve the effectiveness and long-term efficacy of mRNA vaccines in tumor eradication.

Several cytokines such as IL-2, IL-12, IL-15, and IL-21 are being developed to promote NK cells activation and proliferation. Among them, Interleukin-15 (IL-15) plays a distinct role in facilitating the activation and prolonged survival of NK cells, as well as in promoting the development of memory CD8+T cells. IL-15, as a membrane-associated growth factor, binds to IL-15 receptor α (IL-15Rα) on the membrane of dendritic cells (DCs). This complex of IL-15/IL-15Rα (IL-15c) subsequently is presented to IL-15β/γc receptors expressed on neighboring NK cells and/or T cells to elicit biological responses. Meanwhile, compared to IL-2, IL-15 does not have the activation-induced cell death effect15. Preclinical studies have demonstrated that IL-15 and IL-15 agonists exhibit efficacy in tumor-bearing mice when administered as single agents. Although promising, the clinical translation of IL-15 therapies is limited due to suboptimal efficacy and systemic toxicity caused by nonspecific distribution.

In recent years, biomimetic nanotechnology has multifarious advantages, including excellent biocompatibility, minimal toxicity, and high targeting capability for clinical applications. Cell membrane, a promising natural carriers for drug delivery, exert specific biologic functions through membrane protein. Nevertheless, cell membrane and mRNA are negatively charged, which results in poor colloidal stability also hindering their clinical application as mRNA vaccine delivery systems. Cationic liposome as the most promising drug delivery systems, could protect mRNA from enzymatic degradation and be effective internalization. Liposome-fused cell membranes, in comparison to biomembranes, provide several advantages, including the ease of drug loading and surface modification, while still retaining membrane proteins with targeting functions. It makes them well-suited for fulfilling the aforementioned requirements.

Herein, we designed a IL-15-functionalized hybrid lipid complex mRNA vaccine (LMPR) to exert powerful immune effect for inhibiting tumor growth and recurrence (Scheme 1). Cytokeratin 19 fragment antigen (CK19), a highly abundant cytokeratin and a significant biomarker for the diagnosis of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), has been explored as a tumor-associated antigen for NSCLC immunotherapy. LMPR is composed of an activated bone marrow derived cells membrane (BMDCM) loaded with an IL-15-incorporated liposome and a CK19 mRNA/protamine compound, which reduced nonspecific distribution of IL-15 and lowered its side effects. After intranasal administration, LMPR effectively delivered mRNA into DCs, thereby enhancing their maturation and activating CD8+T cells. Additionally, LMPR targeted IL-15 to tumor-specific T cells, upregulating T-bet expression and maximize CD8+T activation, including their expression of IFN-γ-, CD107α- and CD44+CD62L− to drive tumor killing and immunological memory. In addition, LMPR induced oxidative stress tolerance, reversing the suppressive TME and subsequently increasing release of granzyme B and IFN-γ, which enhanced the NK cells cytotoxicity. The synergistic activation of NK cells in combination with CD8+T cells by LMPR compensated for MHC I limitation, which further activated cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) cells, eliminating existing tumors and establishing a lasting immune memory effect to prevent tumor recurrence. This synergistic strategy provides an encouraging possibility to potentiate the antitumor immune responses for mRNA vaccine immunotherapy.

Experimental section

Cell extraction and cell culture

Bone marrow-derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) were isolated from the C57BL/6J mice bone marrow as previously reported. In brief, C57BL/6J mice were sacrificed to obtain the femurs and tibias. The bone ends of the femurs and tibias obtained were cut open and RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco,11875093) was used to flush the cells from the bone marrow cavity and collected cells for further lysis of red blood cells. Cells were cultured in RPMI-1640 complete medium supplemented with 1 % penicillin-streptomycin (VivaCell, C3420), 10 % fetal bovine serum (FBS, Gibco, 10099141), 20 ng/mL mouse IL-4 (PeproTech, 214-14) and 20 ng/mL mouse GM-CSF (PeproTech, 315-03). The culture medium was completely replaced on the second day, half replaced on the fourth day and the cells were harvested on the sixth day.

Mouse dendritic cells (DC2.4) were cultured in RPMI-1640 complete medium supplemented with 1 % penicillin-streptomycin, 10 % FBS (VivaCell, C2910).

Mouse Lewis lung cancer cells (LLC) were cultured in Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM, VivaCell, C2910) supplemented with 1 % penicillin-streptomycin, 10 % FBS.

Human NK-92 cells were suspension cultured in Minimum Essential Medium-α (MEM-α, VivaCell, C3060) complete medium supplemented with 1 % penicillin-streptomycin, 12.5 % FBS, 12.5 % Horse Serum (VivaCell, C2510), 0.2 mM Inositol (Solarbio, I8050), 0.1 mM β-mercaptoethanol (Solarbio, M8210), 0.02 mM Folic Acid (yuanye BioTechnology, B83114) and 100–200 U/mL Human IL-2 (PeproTech, 200-02). All cells were cultured with complete medium and incubated 5 % CO2 at 37 °C.

Preparation of liposome

The cationic lipids 1,2-dioleoyl-3-trimethylammoniumpropane (DOTAP, Avanti Polar Lipids, 890890), cholesterol (Sigma, 57885), 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3- phosphoethanolamineN-[methoxy(polyethyleneglycol)-2000 (DSPE-PEG2000, Avanti Polar Lipids, 880120) and mannose-conjugated DSPE-PEG2000 (DSPE-PEG2000-mannose, benrochem) at 5 mg/mL mixed in a round-bottom flask at a molar ratio of 2:2:0.3:0.05. Lipid film obtained by rotary evaporating lipid mixture. And then, the dried lipid film was rehydrated in Tris buffer (pH 7.4) to prepare blank liposome (Lip), which were further homogenized using an extrusion (Avanti Polar Lipids, USA) containing a polycarbonate filter with 200 nm pores to get dispersed liposomes. The Lip were stored at 4 °C for further studies.

Preparation and characterization of IL-15 functionalized BMDCM

BMDCs were collected and inoculated into 24-well plates overnight, after that the BMDCs co-cultured with lipopolysaccharide (LPS, Sigma, L3129) for 36 h resulting in overexpression of the IL-15α receptor (IL-15Rα) on the cell surface. Afterwards, stained with anti-mouse CD11c (Biolegend, 117310), anti-mouse IL-15Rα (Thermo Fisher, 12-7149-82) antibodies for flow cytometry analysis.

The BMDCs with overexpression of IL-15Rα were collected and co-cultured with mouse IL-15 (PeproTech, 566302) at 4 °C for 30 min to obtained IL-15 functionalized BMDCs with expression of IL-15/IL-15Rα complex (IL-15c). Subsequently, the IL-15 functionalized BMDCs membrane (IL-15c BMDCM) were extracted by using Mem-PER™ Plus Membrane Protein Extraction Kit (Thermo Fisher, 89842). In detail, the collected BMDCs with IL-15c were washed, permeabilized and centrifuged to remove the supernatant containing cytoplasmic proteins. The solubilisation buffer was further added to the cell precipitate and resuspended. After constant mixing of the cell suspension at 4 °C, the supernatant containing soluble membrane proteins and membrane-associated proteins was obtained by centrifugation. The supernatant transferred to a new tube and protein concentration determined using BCA Protein Assay Kit (KeyGEN BioTECH, KGB2101). The IL-15c BMDCM were subpackaged and stored at −80 °C. The content of IL-15/IL-15Rα complex on IL-15c BMDCM was detected by Mouse IL-15/IL-15R Complex ELISA Kit (Multi Sciences, EK215R).

Preparation and characterization of LPR and LMPR

The prepared blank liposome (Lip) and IL-15c BMDCM were repeatedly extruded through Mini-Extruder (Avanti Polar Lipids) with 400 nm pore-sized membrane filter to acquire uniform biomimetic hybrid membrane (LM). Finally, CK19 mRNA (BiOligo Biotechnology) was mixed with protamine (sigma, 9012-00-4) solution and incubated 10min, and then LM was added and mixed to form the liposome-membrane-protamine-mRNA complex (LMPR) as IL-15 functionalized biomimetic hybrid mRNA vaccine delivery system. At the same time, liposome-protamine-mRNA complex (LPR) was prepared by static incubation of CK19 mRNA/protamine complex and lipsome as control group for subsequent experiments.

The ratio of LMPR encapsulated mRNA by gel retardation assay. In brief, 1 % (w/w) agarose gel was configured, after mixing with the loading buffer, the samples were added into the gel pores running at 100V for 30 min. The mobility of mRNA was observed by a Gel Doc XR+ (Bio-Rad). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was utilized to observe the morphology of different preparations. Particle size, zeta potential and PDI were determined using Malvern laser particle sizer (ZS90, England) and stability was recorded over 7 days at 4 °C. The retention of membrane proteins of different groups was examined by using coomassie brilliant blue staining and western blot (WB). In short, the protein molecules were separated using SDS-PAGE electrophoresis, the gels were respectively stained with coomassie brilliant blue staining solution (Solarbio, P1305) and de-stained with decolorizing solution. The change in protein content was observed through photography of the gel. In addition, the separated proteins were transported to PVDF membrane. Further, blocked membrane for 10 min with protein free rapid blocking buffer (Servicebio, G2052) and incubated with primary antibodies of anti-Na+/K+ ATPase (Bioss, bs-1152R, dilution:1:500), anti-IL15Rα (ThermoFisher, PA5-79467, dilution:0.5 μg/mL) at 4 °C respectively. After incubation with secondary antibody for 1 h at room temperature, signal development was performed using a chemiluminescence kit (Servicebio, G2014).

Cell viability study and hemocompatibility

The assessment of cytotoxicity was performed with DC2.4, BMDCs and NK-92 cells. Cells were inoculated in 96-well plates (104 cells/well) overnight. And then, the cells were treated with different formulations at 37 °C for 24 h. After incubation, the treated cells were added to 10 μL cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8, MeilunBio, MA0218) to each well. The absorbance at 450 nm was achieved using a microplate reader (Tecan infinite M200 Pro).

The mice blood was centrifuged (2500 rpm) for 5min to obtain erythrocytes. The obtained erythrocytes were washed three times with saline solution and diluted to a concentration of 2 % (v/v). 500 μL of samples was mixed with 500 μL of erythrocytes and incubated for 3 h at 37 °C, deionized water (H2O) was used as the positive control and PBS as the negative control. The solutions were then centrifuged at 2500 rpm for 5 min to get the supernatants. The collected supernatants was taken into a 96-well plate, and the absorbance at 540 nm was read using a microplate reader.

Cellular uptake

Cellular uptake experiments were conducted using DC2.4 and LLC cell. These cells were seeded in 6-well plates at a density of 1 × 105 cells per well with complete growth medium and incubated at 37 °C overnight. Subsequently, the cells were treated with FBS-free RPMI 1640 medium containing PBS, LPR and LMPR (EGFP mRNA, 5 μg) respectively for 24 h. The percentage of EGFP-positive cells were assessed by Flow CytoMetry (FCM) and confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM).

Lysosomal escape assay

DC2.4 cells at the density of 1 × 104/well were seeded in confocal dish. After 24 h incubation, the cells were treated with free mCherry mRNA, LPR (mCherry mRNA, 3 μg) and LMPR (mCherry mRNA, 3 μg) groups respectively for 2 h, 6 h, and 12 h. The cells were washed three times with PBS and treated with LysoTracker Green (Beyotime, C1047S) for 1 h. Then, the cells were washed three times with PBS and the nuclei were stained by Hoechst 33342 (Solarbio, C0031) for 10 min. Images were captured using a confocal laser scanning microscope (Zeiss LSM900).

Antigen protein translation

The translation of the CK19 protein in cytoplasm after treatment with LMPR was evaluated by WB. Briefly, DC2.4 cells and BMDCs were respectively inoculated into 6-well plates and LMPR was added for co-incubation for 24 h. PBS-treated DC2.4 cells were used as negative control and LLC cells were used as positive control. The total proteins were extracted from the different groups by total protein extraction kit (KeyGEN BioTECH, KGB5303). The protein molecules were separated using SDS-PAGE electrophoresis and transported to PVDF membrane. Next, blocked membrane incubated with primary antibodies of anti-cytokeratin 19 (Proteintech, 10712-1-AP, dilution:1:5000) and anti-GAPDH (Immunoway, YM3029, dilution:1:10000) at 4 °C respectively. Goat anti-mouse or goat anti-rabbit HRP-conjugated as secondary antibodies (Zenbio, 511103 and 511203, dilution:1:10000) were applied, and the membranes were detected and analyzed using a computerized chemiluminescent imaging system.

BMDCs maturation assay

BMDCs were seeded in 24-well plates at a density of 1 × 106 cells per well and incubated overnight. The cells were treated with various formulations or control groups, including PBS, LPR (mRNA 5 μg), LMPR (mRNA 5 μg), LPS (5 μg) and incubated for an additional 24 h. Following treatment, BMDCs were harvested and stained at 4 °C for 30 min with fluorophore-labeled antibodies against CD11c (Biolegend, 117310), CD86 (Biolegend, 159204), CD80 (Biolegend, 104707), MHC I (Thermo Fisher, 11-5958-82), followed by FCM.

Beyond that, the cytokines (IFN-γ, IL-12p70 and TNF-α) secretion were investigated through ELISA analysis. The supernatants of BMDCs treated with different formulations were collected and tested for IFN-γ (Multi Sciences, EK280), IL-12p70 (Multi Sciences, EK212) and TNF-α (Multi Sciences, EK282) with ELISA kit referring to the manufacturer's protocol.

In vitro antioxidant stress ability of NK-92 cells

The preparation of LMPR was as described previously, in which mouse IL-15 is replaced by human IL-15 (PeproTech, 570304). Detection of intracellular ROS was performed using the CellROX Deep Red kit (ThermoFisher, C104491). This reagent is usually non-fluorescent while in a reduced state, but exhibits strong fluorogenic signal upon oxidation, providing a reliable measure of ROS in live cells. The NK-92 cells were seeded in 6-well plates at a density of 5 × 104 cells/mL and cultured overnight. The cells were treated with tert-butyl hydroperoxide (TBHP, inducer of ROS production) as a positive control, TBHP combined with antioxidant N-acetylcysteine (NAC) as a negative group, PBS, TBHP combined with LPR and TBHP combined with LMPR for 24 h. After that, 1 μL of CellROX Deep Red Ragent was added to every 500 μL of cells treated with different formulations. The cells were incubated for 1 h in the cell culture incubator at 37 °C, protected from light. After staining, the nuclei were stained by hoechst 33342 (Solarbio, C0031) for 10 min at room temperature. The NK-92 cells directly observed by CLSM. In addition, in the last 15 min of CellROX Deep Red Ragent staining, 1 μL of SYTOX dead cell stain was added to every 1 mL of cells, and detected by FCM. Three independent biological replicates were performed.

NK-92 cells were inoculated at 5 × 104/mL in 6-well plates and cultured for 24 h. Total cytoplasmic proteins were obtained by lysis after treatment for 24 h with the different groups (positive group, negative group, PBS, TBHP combined with LPR and TBHP combined with LMPR) respectively, and the expression of Trx protein (abcam, rabbit monoclonal to Thioredoxin/TRX, ab133524) and TXNIP protein (abcam, rabbit monoclonal to TXNIP, ab188865) were detected. The results were visualized and quantitative analyzed using Image Lab software.

Examination of NK cells proliferation by CFSE method

CFSE-stained NK-92 cells were added to 12-well plates at 1 × 105 cells/well and cultured for 24 h. Following the addition of PBS, LPR and LMPR to induce NK-92 cells activation, the cultures were continued for an additional 24 h. The proliferation of NK-92 cells was determined by fluorescence microscopy (EVos auto2, Thermo Fisher) and FCM.

Therapeutic vaccination experiments

Female C57BL/6J mice aged 6–8 weeks were obtained from the Experimental Animal Center of Ningxia Medical University and were used in accordance with the protocol approved by Ethics Committee of Ningxia Medical University (IACUC-NYLAC-2023-088). These mice were maintained in specific pathogen-free and temperature-controlled facilities.

To establish tumor models, 100 μL 1 × 107 cells/mL LLC tumor cells (suspended in PBS) were inoculated subcutaneously into the axilla of female C57/BL6J mice. Approximately 7 days later, when the tumor size reached almost 100 mm3, the tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into 3 groups, with 10 mice in each group. Three groups were vaccinated separately with PBS, LPR and LMPR (containing 10 μg mRNA per mouse) by intranasal administration to evaluate the survival of mice. In therapeutic vaccination experiments, intranasal administration of different preparations was started on the 7th day. There were three groups, namely, the PBS, LPR and LMPR groups, with six mice in each group. The mice were inoculated by therapeutic vaccination every 5 days, 4 times in total. Tumor size and body weight were measured every three days after treatment. On day 27, the tumors of different groups were collected for H&E, TUNEL and immunofluorescence staining. On day 27, the following plasma samples were collected to assay the following biochemical indicators: alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), albumin (ALB), alkaline phosphatase (ALP), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine (CREA) and creatine kinase (CK). Hearts, livers, lungs and kidneys were excised for H&E staining. Spleens and tumors were also collected for flow cytometry on day 27.

The suppression of tumor recurrence

To investigate the capacity of LMPR in inhibiting postsurgical tumor regrowth, 100 μL 1 × 107/mL LLC tumor cells were inoculated subcutaneously into female C57/BL6J mice aged 6–8 weeks. On the 10th day after the tumour inoculation, the tumour was removed by surgery, leaving behind tumour tissue about 4 mm∼5 mm in length and width. The wound was then sutured. The post-operative mice were randomly divided into three groups with 6 mice and administered different preparations for immunisation to assess their inhibitory effects on tumour recurrence. On days 10, 15, 20, and 25, the PBS, LPR and LMPR (10 μg mRNA per mouse) were administered intranasally to the mice, respectively. The volume of recurrent tumors and weight of mice was measured using calipers every 3 days.

In vivo flow cytometry and cytokine profiling

On day 5 after the last immunization, tumors were isolated from mice of different groups. Spleen tissues were disrupted into small pieces, and subsequently homogenized into single-cell suspension. Tumors tissues were digested with collagenase type IV (Solarbio, 9001-12-1, 1 mg/mL) and DNase I (Solarbio, 9003-98-9, 1 mg/mL) in serumfree RPMI 1640 for 30 min at 37 °C with gentle shaking. The cell suspensions were filtered through a cell strainer (70 μm) and washed with PBS containing 2 % FBS. Then, the cell suspension was incubated with CD16/32 (BioLegend, 101301, 0.5 mg/mL) for 10 min to block nonspecific antibody binding. To detect the populations of CD11c+MHCI+DC cells in spleen and CD3+CD4+T cells, CD3+CD107α+CD8+T cells, CD3+IFN-γ+CD8+T cells, CD3+CD8+CD62LlowCD44hi Tem and CD3−granzyme B+NK1.1 in tumor, the cell suspension was incubated at 4 °C for 30 min with the following antibodies: APC anti-mouse CD11c antibody (BioLegend, 117310, 0.2 mg/mL), FITC anti-mouse MHC I antibody (Thermo Fisher, 11-5958-82, 0.5 mg/mL), PerCP/Cyanine5.5 anti-mouse CD3 antibody (BioLegend, 100218, 0.2 mg/mL), PE anti-mouse CD4 antibody (BioLegend, 100407, 0.2 mg/mL), APC anti-mouse CD8α antibody (BioLegend, 100712, 0.2 mg/mL), APC anti-mouse NK1.1 antibody (BioLegend, 108710, 0.2 mg/mL), PE anti-mouse CD107α antibody (BioLegend, 121611, 0.2 mg/mL), PE anti-mouse CD62L antibody (BioLegend, 104407, 0.2 mg/mL), FITC anti-mouse/human CD44 Antibody (BioLegend, 103005, 0.5 mg/mL), PE anti-mouse IFN-γ antibody (BioLegend, 163504, 0.2 mg/mL), Alexa Fluor® 700 anti-human/mouse Granzyme B (BioLegend, 372221, 5 μL). All cell samples were analyzed by BD FACSVerse™ flow cytometer (BD Biosciences, Bedford, USA) and analyzed with the FlowJo software.

The serum was extracted from orbital peripheral blood on day 5 after the last immunization. The levels of cytokines including IFN-γ, IL-12p70, TNF-α in the serum were determined by ELISA kit, which were the same as in vitro testing.

In vivo western blot and RT-qPCR analysis

On day 5 after the last immunization, tumor tissue harvested from tumor-bearing mice was homogenized in cold lysis buffer at 4 °C. The sample was centrifuged (12000 g) for 5 min, and the supernatant was gathered and tested total protein with BCA kit. WB assay was as mentioned before. Primary antibodies used in this experiment include JAK1 (Immunoway, YT2424, dilution:1:500), phospho-JAK1 (Tyr1022) (Zenbio, 340777, dilution:1:700), STAT3 (Immunoway, YT4443, dilution:1:1000), AKT (Immunoway, YT0185, dilution:1:1000), phospho-AKT (Thr308) (Zenbio, 341790, dilution:1:700).

On day 5 after the last immunization, total RNA from tumor were isolated by RNAsimple Total RNA kit (Tiangen, DP419) and the concentration of RNA was measured by NanoDrop micro-volume spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher). The reverse-transcribed into cDNAs by the PrimeScript RT reagent Kit (Takara, Japan). Subsequently, cDNAs were further amplified and quantified with gene-specific primers and the TB Green Premix EX TaqII (Takara, Japan) on a CFX96 Real-Time PCR Detection System (Bio-Rad, USA). The gene (IFN-γ and T-bet) expression was assessed using qRT-PCR. Primers are as follow GAPDH (For:5′-GCAAATTCAACGGCACAGTCAAG-3′,Rev:5′-TCGCTCCTGGAAGATGGTGATG-3′), IFN-γ (For:5′-CTGGAGGAAACTGGCAAAAGGATG-3′, Rev:5′-CAGGTGTGATTCAATGACGCTTATG-3′),T-bet(For:5′-ATGTACGCATCTGTTGATACGAGTG-3′, Rev:5′-TGGTTGGATAGAAGAGGTGAGAAGG-3′).

Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were conducted using GraphPad Prism 9.5 software and the results were presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). One-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Tukey post-hoc test and two-way ANOVA with Tukey's multiple comparison test were used to compare results across multiple groups. The significance levels denoted as ∗p < 0.05, ∗∗p < 0.01, ∗∗∗p < 0.001, ∗∗∗∗p < 0.0001, ns represents no significance.

Results and discussion

Preparation and characterization of LMPR

The preparation of IL-15 functionalized BMDCM are described in Fig. 1A. Bone marrow-derived monocytes can differentiate into dendritic cells (Mo-DCs) using the combination of GM-CSF/interleukin (IL)-4, which is used to study DC biology. CD11c is widely known as a dendritic cell surface marker. therefore the immature BMDCs (iBMDCs) were identified by anti-mouse CD11c using flow cytometry (FCM) and they accounted for 89.6 ± 1.3 % of the collected cells (Fig. 1B), it indicated that iBMDCs were successfully obtained. LPS induced the high expression of IL-15Rα on BMDCM in a time- and dose-dependent manner and was confirmed by FCM (Fig. 1C,D and Fig. S1). At the same time, BMDCs viability was less than 80 % when incubation time was 48 h (Fig. S2). In the follow-up experiment, the LPS concentration was set at 4 μg/mL, and the incubation time was 36 h. The content of IL-15c was measured by ELISA indicated excellent binding efficiency of IL-15 and IL-15Rα when the concentration of IL-15 was 60 ng/mL (Fig. 1E). To evaluate the retention of functional membrane proteins of BMDCM, coomassie brilliant blue assay and WB were performed. The findings showed that LM retained the mainly membrane protein including IL-15Rα and exhibited in a concentration-dependent manner (Fig. 1F and G). The above results indicated that we successfully prepared IL-15-functionalized BMDCM. The IL-15 stimulates proliferation, activation of immune effector cells, but clinical application is hindered by short half-life and severe toxicity on account of unspecific immune activation. Notably, the binding of IL-15 to IL-15Rα has been shown to increase the in vivo half-life of IL-15 and improve its binding to cells expressing IL-15Rβγ, including NK cells and CD8+T cells. The successful expression of IL-15-IL-15Rα on prepared hybrid membranes indicated that the LM is expected to interact with NK and CD8+T cells.

Liposomes are promising vehicles in the field of mRNA delivery, owning to their biocompatibility and the capacity to encapsulate and deliver negatively charged drugs. Subsequently, the liposome (Lip) was prepared by solvent evaporation method. Then, the preparation diagram of hybrid liposome-BMDCM loaded with mRNA/protamine compounds (LMPR) was shown in Fig. 1H. In Fig. 1I, the mean size of LM was 215.5 ± 0.3 nm. The inset image in Fig. 1I showed LM displayed representative a spherical vesicular construction. The gel retardation assay was used for evaluating the loading efficiency of mRNA. When the ratio of LM, protamine and mRNA was 6:0.5:1, mRNA was effectively encapsulated by LMPR (Fig. 1J). The subsequent experiment adopted this ratio to prepare LMPR and its particle size (Fig. 1K), encapsulation rate, and drug loading were 324.7 ± 3.1 nm, (70.7 ± 1.9)%, (6.1 ± 0.1)%, respectively. Furthermore, it is noteworthy that the charge was reversed after the Lip fused with BMDCM, which could be attributed to the insertion of multiple substances with different charges, such as BMDCM, protamine and mRNA (Figs. S3A,B,C). It could be beneficial for immobilization of anionic mRNA with the assistance of LM and protamine. LMPR could remain stable at 4 °C, and the diameter demonstrated negligible changes during 7 days storage (Fig. S3D), indicating it had good stability. The constructed LMPR is promising to deliver mRNA and IL-15/IL-15Rα to activate relevant immune cells achieving anti-tumour immune responses.

Biocompatibility evaluation in vitro

Biocompatibility of mRNA vaccine is a prerequisite for its clinical application. The cytocompatibility assay was performed using the Cell Counting Kit 8 kit (CCK8) with DC2.4, BMDCs and NK-92 cells as models. As outlined in Fig. S4, liposome did not cause any damages on cell survival as same as PBS group, exhibiting its excellent biocompatibility in vitro. However, the cell membrane have significant toxicity to DC cells, compared to the liposome group (p < 0.05), it may due to the detergent contained in permeabilization buffer. More importantly, after the fusion of liposome the cell viability has significantly increased compared with membrane group (p < 0.05). Moreover, after LMPR co-cultured with three different cell types for 24 h, the cell viability rate was above 80 %. In addition, the hemolysis rate (Fig. S5) have no significant change in LM and LMPR groups, further demonstrating the excellent biocompatibility of mRNA vaccine deliver system. Taking together, these results indicated that the LMPR exhibited exceptional cytocompatibility without causing adverse effects on cells, suggesting its potential for in vivo application.

The immune activation of DC cells treated with LMPR

Efficient mRNA delivery is a prerequisite for in mRNA-based cancer immunotherapy, where it can be translated into antigenic protein for processing and presentating. Therefore, we evaluated whether LMPR could serve as a delivery system for mRNA vaccines. As showed in Fig. 2A, we observed that the EGFP mRNA was rapidly internalized into the cytoplasm of DC2.4 compared to LLC. Furthermore, significant enhancement uptake of LMPR by DC2.4 was observed when BMDCM was incorporated, presumably as a result of homologous targeting of membrane. Given the importance of dendritic cells (DCs) in the regulation of the mRNA vaccine mediated-immune response, DC membrane-fused mRNA deliver system can be used to promote the uptake of mRNA in DC and to induce cellular immune responses. These results were further confirmed by FCM (p < 0.05) (Fig. 2B).

After cellular uptake, mRNA needs to overcome lysosomal degradation for antigen protein expression. We therefore evaluated the mRNA release from lysosome. As show in Fig. 2D, when free mCherry mRNA were incubated with DC2.4 cells for 2 h–12 h, the free mCherry mRNA rarely enters the lysosomes, it indicated that naked mRNA may be degraded during the uptake process. Both of LPR and LMPR loaded mCherry mRNA could be co-localized with lysosome. As the incubation time prolonged, the red fluorescence and green fluorescence no longer overlapped, indicating that most LMPR escaped from lysosomes. After 12 h of co-incubation, compared to the LPR group, LMPR was able to be released from the lysosomes more quickly. Many studies have shown that polycationic materials promote lysosomal escape through the proton sponge effect, the cationic lipids can interact with lysosomal membrane to destabilise the lysosome and promote the lysosomal escape of mRNA. Therefore, the lysosomal escape was not only attributed to the cationic lipid (DOTAP) in LPR and LMPR but also relied on the precise targeting of cell membrane nano-biomimetic drug delivery systems As shown in Fig. 2C, the result of WB proved that LMPR loaded with CK19 mRNA was taken up by DC2.4 and BMDCs, followed by translating into CK19 protein in the cytoplasm, which set the foundation for the subsequent antigenic effects. Taken together, these data suggested that LMPR have the potential for the mRNA delivery and cytosolic translation of mRNA.

Besides, the activated DC cells are vital for immune activation, which will exhibit high expression of co-stimulatory molecules and antigen-presenting molecules, futher activating T cells. Thus, we next investigated the immunological effects on BMDCs through LMPR. In Fig. 2E and F, the results showed that the expression of CD80/CD86 and MHC I on BMDC were significantly higher than that in the PBS group and LPR group (p < 0.01). These proteins play key roles in the antigen presenting process for T cells activation in antitumor immunotherapy. Subsequently, the secretion of cytokines by BMDCs was evaluated, LMPR promoted a robust secretion of TNF-α, IFN-γ and IL-12p70 from BMDCs (Fig. 2G). These elevated levels of cytokines are highly relevant to induce stimulate systemic immunity and tumor extinction. These results indicated that after the mRNA was successfully translated into antigen protein, it could activate immature BMDC and induce iBMDC maturation, and further inducing BMDC to product more cytokines. The activated dendritic cells have the potential to present antigen peptides to T cells and activate T cells to kill tumors.

In vitro antioxidant stress ability of NK-92 cells

Accumulation of ROS in the TME can lead to oxidative stress environment, resulting in decreased infiltration of NK cells and impaired tumor-killing function. The fluorescent ROS indicator, namely the CellROX Deep Red reagent, was used to represent the content of intracellular ROS. In cultured NK-92 cells (Fig. 3A and B), the cells treated with TBHP generated a large amount of red fluorescence and pre-treatment of NAC suppressed the increased CellROX signal. The LMPR could effectively decreased the fluorescence intensity of CellROX (p < 0.0001 vs TBHP + LPR) and achieve oxidative stress tolerance of NK cells, which indicated a connection with the IL-15 functionalized BMDCM incorporated in LMPR. Next, the mechanism of inducing oxidative stress tolerance was investigated by WB. As illustrated in Fig. 3C–G, compared with LPR, LMPR could significantly increase the expression of thioredoxin (Trx) with antioxidative function (p < 0.001) and down-regulate the expression of thioredoxin-interacting protein (TXNIP) (p < 0.05), which suggested it may be related to the thioredoxin reduction pathway. Among these treatments, LMPR induced the strongest oxidative stress tolerance in NK cells. This implied that LMPR could reduce TXNIP binding to Trx through IL-15. This result led to the inhibition of ROS production and accumulation, and thus eventually suppressing the apoptosis of NK cells.

Based on the results of the evaluation of oxidative stress tolerance, the proliferation of NK-92 cells was examined by carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester (CFSE) staining, followed by fluorescence imaging evaluation and FCM detection. The sequential decrease in fluorescent labeling in daughter cells. As shown in Fig. 3H and I, LMPR significantly increased the proliferation of NK cells compared to PBS group (p < 0.01). The CFSE fluorescence intensity of LMPR in Fig. 3J appears to be higher than that of the PBS and LPR groups, which may be due to the NK-92 is a suspension cell and the number of cells observed under the microscope during the instantaneous imaging was not consistent. These results indicated LMPR might induce oxidative stress tolerance and promote NK cells proliferation, providing a basis for synergistic mRNA vaccine-induced adaptive immune responses in T cells.

In vivo antitumor efficacy

We next investigated the antitumoral activity of LMPR in LLC tumor models. Tumor-bearing mice were treated with PBS, LPR and LMPR on days 7, 12, 17, and 22 (Fig. 4A). During the therapeutic period, the tumor volume was monitored every 3 days until day 24. As shown in Fig. 4B and. C and Figure.S8, compared with PBS group and LPR group, LMPR significantly inhibited tumour growth (p < 0.01) and prolonged survival. Furthermore, 5 days after the final immunisation, tumour tissue from each group of mice was extracted for histological examinations (H&E) staining and TUNEL staining. In Fig. 4E, the nuclei of the tumor cells could be stained blue by hematoxylin. The staining depth of the nucleus is related to the content of DNA in nucleus, it may be relevant to the abnormal proliferation of tumor cells. The LMPR group revealed reduction in the blue-purple nuclei of tumor cells, which indicated that LMPR can effectively inhibit the proliferation of the tumour cells. Additionally, the detection of apoptotic cells in tumor tissue through TUNEL staining. TUNEL results (Fig. 4F and Fig. S9) confirmed the significant increased green fluorescence in LMPR group (p < 0.0001 vs LPR group), suggesting effective induction of apoptosis in tumor cells. These results indicated that LMPR could effectively promote apoptotic of tumor cells to inhibit tumor proliferation in vivo.

As shown Fig. 4D, the body weight of the mice did not exhibit a significant decrease. Moreover, we performed the H&E staining of major organs of heart, liver, spleen, lung and kidney from mice of each group. Treatment of mice with LMPR did not result in significant acute organ damage or inflammatory lesions (Fig. S10). Biochemical analysis of serum was used for liver and renal function tests and myocardial damage test. All the indicators fluctuated within normal ranges among each group, confirming the IL-15 functionalized biomimetic hybrid mRNA vaccine displayed no overt toxicity under the administrable dosage (Fig. S11). In summary, the LMPR could target delivery of mRNA to DC and trans-present to NK cells or T cells using the high affinity binding of IL-15 and IL-15Rα, which achieving the effective tumor inhibition and showing favorable safety profile.

Synergistic immune responses

Encouraged by the extraordinary anti-tumor effects of LMPR, we further explored its underlying immune mechanisms. As shown in Fig. 5A and B, the percentage of DCs maturation significantly increased from approximately 3.45 % in the PBS group to 10.20 % in the LMPR group, indicating the LMPR could effective promote DC maturation in vivo and antigen presentation to stimulate antitumor immune responses. Subsequently, we assessed the differentiation of T cells in the tumors. The numbers of CD4+T cells were considerably higher in LMPR group (compared with PBS and LPR), which indicated that the LMPR effectively activated the immune response and promoted differentiation of T cells into more functional state. Moreover, cytotoxic T lymphocytes were differentiated, accompanied by the expression of IFN-γ and CD107α. CD107α, namely lysosomal associated membrane protein-1 (LAMP-1), is a sign of cell degranulation. Mice treated with LMPR had increased infiltration of CD107α+CD8+T cells in tumor, with 6.5 times more than that treated with PBS, which suggested the LMPR facilitated the transport of cytotoxic lytic particles out of the cell via CD107α on surface cell membrane to kill the tumor. At the same time, when CD8+T came into contact with tumour cells, CD107α is highly expressed on the surface of immune cells which was also able to protect effector immune cells from killing by degranulated of self-cells. Specifically, memory T cells play an important role in inhibiting tumor recurrence. Compared with other controls, the LMPR treatment further upregulated CD44hiCD62LlowCD8+T cells percentage. Moreover, the RT-qPCR results (Fig. 5C) exhibited the effective improvement of T-box expressed in T cell (T-bet) expression via LMPR, which plays essential roles in adjuvant-elicited T cell (Tvacs) immunity for the sustained memory T cell response to subunit vaccination. Simultaneously, the presence of T-bet in already committed T cells was required to maintain Th1 cells stability. Consequently, LMPR facilitated the differentiation of primary T cells into Tem, which would be conducive to inhibiting tumor recurrence. Consistently, immunofluorescence staining visualized the increased infiltration of CD8+T cells in tumor tissue after LMPR treatment (Fig. 5D). In addition to enhance T-cell immunity, LMPR also affected NK cells to compensate for MHC I restrictions. Fig. 5A displayed that the percentage of Granzyme B+NK cells was elevated by LMPR therapy, and Granzyme B released by cytotoxic lymphocytes penetrates tumor cells to promote NK cells killing ability. Western blot analysis (Fig. 5E) showed that LMPR induced protein expression of JAK1, STAT3 and AKT, which demonstrated that LMPR is capable of activating the JAK1-STAT3 signalling pathway through IL-15/IL-15Rα combined with IL-15β receptor on NK cells, further increasing the production of granzyme B via AKT signaling pathway and exhibiting superior anti-tumor effects. Cytoines are known to modulate innate and adaptive immunity, particularly stimulate T cell responses to kill tumor cells. The secretion of TNF-α and IL-12p70 increased after LMPR treatment (Fig. 5F). Additionally, the secretion of IFN-γ in the serum confirmed the activation antitumor immunity. The above evidence comprehensively demonstrated that the LMPR could validly drive tumor killing and immunological memory via synergistic CD8+T cells and NK cells.

The suppression of tumor recurrence

Considering that immune memory cells are critical for managing tumor rechallenge, we constructed the cancer resection model to evaluate the anti-recurrence effects of biomimetic mRNA vaccine (Fig. 6A). Tumors were resected with approximately 10 % left to simulate residual microtumors after surgery. As shown in Fig. 6B, the weight of the three groups of mice increased over time, on 10th day, the body weight of each group of mice decreased due to the removal of the tumors. LMPR delayed significantly tumor recurrence compared to LPR (p < 0.01) (Fig. 6C,D and Fig. S15). Notably, after administration of LMPR following surgery, 2 out of 3 mice showed smaller tumor recurrences at the primary inoculation site. These results suggested that LMPR effectively suppress post-surgical tumor recurrence, which may be attributed to the valid and specific antitumor immunity. Ki67 is a well-known proliferation marker for the evaluation of cell proliferation, as show in Fig. 6E, LMPR could effectively inhibit the proliferation of recurrent tumor cells. We next harvested the recurrent tumor following different treatments to analyze CD8+T cells and NK cells with flow cytometry. Compared with LPR, the LMPR treatment further upregulated the proportion of IFN-γ+NK cell and IFN-γ+CD8+T cell (Fig. 6G). In addition, we have also evaluated the differentiation of memory T cells and infiltration in tumors in vivo. The results showed that LMPR could significantly promote the differentiation of Tem cells. Consistently, immunofluorescence staining visualized the increased infiltration of Tem cells in recurrent tumor tissue after LMPR treatment (Fig. 6F). As a result, the LMPR treatment could evoke immune memory to retard tumor progression and effectively inhibit tumor recurrence. All these results preliminarily proved that the LMPR could improve the differentiation of effector memory T cells in the site of tumor tissue by through IL-15/IL-15Rα combined with IL-15β/γc receptor on T cells. Furthermore, cooperation of effector memory T cells and NK cells could inhibit the tumor cells growth and finally suppress tumor recurrence.

Conclusion

Lung cancer is the second most frequent cancer, with NSCLC representing 85 % of all lung cancers. The 5-year survival rate is about 60 % for resectable diseases, 33 % for unresectable regional diseases and 6.3 % for metastatic diseases. The use of mRNA vaccines in cancer management shows potent efficacy in preclinical trials, becoming one of the most promising treatments. However, there are still significant issues to be used in the clinical application of mRNA vaccines, such as instability and single function. In the study, we have developed a well-designed IL-15 functionalized biomimetic hybrid mRNA vaccine (LMPR) for cancer immunotherapy. The prepared LMPR not only had strong DC uptake capabilities and the ability of stimulation DC maturation, but also combined with IL-15 mediated the activation of NK cells. After nasal administration, LMPR markedly promoted the activation of DCs and T cell mediated immune response to suppressing tumor growth. In addition, immunization with LMPR could form immune memory against the challenge of LLC tumor. This study provides a new mRNA cancer vaccine delivery platform for cancer immunotherapy.
