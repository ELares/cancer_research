# Novel Strategy for Acquiring Metabolically-Tagged Nascent Extracellular Vesicles: Implications for Identifying Surface Protein Markers of Extracellular Vesicles From Neuroblastoma Cells Cultured With Native Serum.

## Abstract

Tumour-derived extracellular vesicles (tdEVs) have emerged as a promising representative of cancer manifestation that can be accessed non-invasively through liquid biopsy. Selective examination of tdEVs requires their isolation, which relies on tumour-specific surface markers. These markers are often identified using cancer cell lines cultured in EV-depleted serum or serum-free conditions to avoid interference by exogenous EVs in serum. However, these nutrient-deprived media can alter gene expression and the proteomic composition of EVs. This study aims to develop a method to identify potential EV surface markers for paediatric neuroblastoma from tumour cell lines grown in native serum. Our methodology enables distinguishing tumour-specific EVs from the exogenous serum EVs, without prior knowledge of any tumour-specific surface markers. By metabolically incorporating an azide-tagged sugar analogue into nascent glycoproteins, we differentially marked only tumour-derived EVs and captured them using copper-catalysed click chemistry-mediated biotinylation and affinity enrichment. Subsequent analysis through mass spectrometry and western blotting led to the identification of gap junction protein GJC1 (connexin 45) as a potential surface marker for neuroblastoma EVs. This methodology not only aids in EV surface profiling but also has significant implications for time-resolved and spatial EV studies in various biological contexts, including disease development, progression, therapy resistance, and cellular communication.

Introduction

Conventional tissue biopsies are routinely used for cancer diagnosis and classification (Elmalik and Davies; von Mehren et al.; Streicher et al.). However, these procedures involve highly invasive surgical resection or needle puncture, which is generally unable to capture the intratumoural heterogeneity within the limited local sampling area, labour‐intensive, and painful for the patient (Siravegna et al.). In contrast, a liquid biopsy drawn from biofluids, such as peripheral blood, enables systemic and thus more thorough sampling of cancer that is more cost‐effective and minimally invasive (Siravegna et al.). As their role in cancer development and progression is increasingly recognized, extracellular vesicles (EVs) have gained momentum for analysis by liquid biopsy (Xu et al.; Singhto et al.). These membrane‐enclosed nano‐sized (30–1000 nm) particles, shed by virtually all cell types, mediate intercellular communication as carriers of molecules such as lipids, nucleic acids, (membrane) proteins and metabolites (Berumen Sánchez et al.). The fact that they are continuously released by tumours and present the biomarkers of the phenotype of their parental cells makes tumour‐derived EVs (tdEVs) a valuable tool for early detection and diagnosis, as well as real‐time monitoring of tumour progression and treatment response (Singhto et al.).

Exploration studies for novel, EV‐based cancer biomarkers are often conducted on crude total EV fractions isolated from blood samples (Morini et al.; Hoshino et al.). However, this approach complicates the detection of low‐abundant tumour‐specific cargo, as tdEVs in blood circulation are accompanied by an abundance of EVs released from non‐malignant cells (Auber and Svenningsen). Moreover, compositional differences in total EV fractions between diseased subjects and healthy controls may not necessarily arise from the tumour, but rather from the surrounding tumour microenvironment or systemic effects of cancer on distant tissues and the immune system (Hoshino et al.). Specific evaluation of tumour characteristics requires enrichment steps based on tumour‐specific epitopes exposed on the EV surface (Pietrowska et al.; Zhao et al.). However, only a handful of EV surface markers are currently known for certain cancer types (Runz et al.; Melo et al., Sharma et al.), which may be dynamically down‐regulated along the disease course (Hyun et al.; Mabe et al.) or lost by ectodomain shedding (Rupp et al.).

EV surface markers are often inferred from studies using mono‐cultured cancer cell lines as the EV source (Melo et al.; Hüttmann et al.), allowing proteomic analyses in the absence of non‐tumour EVs. However, most cell lines require supplementation with serum to support viability and proliferation (Urzì et al.). Serum naturally contains an abundance of exogenous EVs, which are thus introduced into culture media and co‐isolated with those derived from cultured cells, owing to their identical physico‐chemical properties (Lehrich et al.). To eliminate this contamination, cells are often cultured in serum‐free media. Although this approach is effective (Böröczky et al.), such nutrient‐deprived media are known to impair cell viability (Rashid and Coombs) and change gene expression (Rashid and Coombs), intracellular signalling (Pirkmajer and Chibalin), EV secretion rates (Böröczky et al.; Li et al.; Sun et al.; Bost et al.), and the EV proteome composition (Böröczky et al.; Li et al.; Sun et al.). Alternatively, exogenous EVs can be depleted from serum before supplementation to cell cultures to minimize stress inflicted on cell physiology. Nonetheless, also these serum supplements have been reported to slow cellular proliferation and alter gene expression compared to native serum (Lehrich et al.), and may thus not be representative of EVs produced in vivo.

This study aims to develop a methodology that enables the identification of surface markers specific to paediatric cancer neuroblastoma from EVs produced by cells cultured in native serum, which provides a rich growth environment but contains exogenous EVs, with no prior knowledge of any biomarkers of tumour EVs. Neuroblastoma‐specific EVs are distinguished from exogenous serum EVs through unbiased metabolic labelling of glycoproteins with an azide‐tagged substrate analogue. This process endows nascent EVs with bioorthogonal reactive groups, allowing their selective capture through click chemistry‐mediated biotinylation, affinity enrichment and release from the affinity matrix for downstream analysis. In this study, captured EVs were used for surface profiling by mass spectrometry analysis and revealed a plethora of (membrane) proteins, which reflected characteristics of the parental cells, including the mesenchymal transcriptional program. In addition, we identified gap junction protein gamma‐1 (GJC1, connexin 45) as a potential EV surface marker in the studied MYCN‐amplified and non‐amplified cell lines, which may allow distinguishing circulating tdEVs in the blood of neuroblastoma patients. In addition to EV surface profiling, this methodology also has significant potential for time‐resolved and spatial EV studies in various biological contexts, including disease development, progression, therapy resistance and cellular communication.

Materials and Methods

Cell Culturing and Metabolic Labelling

Neuroblastoma cell lines SH‐EP2 and SH‐SY5Y were maintained in high‐glucose DMEM (Capricorn, catalogue no. DMEM‐HA) supplemented with 10% heat‐inactivated foetal bovine serum (FBS; Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. F7524), 2 mM L‐glutamine (Capricorn, catalogue no. GLN‐B), 1 mM sodium pyruvate (Gibco, catalogue no. 11360070), 1% penicillin/streptomycin (Capricorn, catalogue no. PS‐B), and 1% MEM non‐essential amino acids solution (Gibco, catalogue no. 11140050). Neuroblastoma cell line Kelly was maintained in RPMI 1640 (Capricorn, catalogue no. RPMI‐A) supplemented with 10% heat‐inactivated FBS, 2 mM L‐glutamine and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. Cells were cultured at 37°C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO2.

For metabolic incorporation of L‐azidohomoalanine (AHA; Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 900892), cells were washed with pre‐warmed HBSS and incubated with methionine‐free DMEM (Gibco, catalogue no. 21013024) with 1 mM sodium pyruvate, either serum‐free or supplemented with native or dialyzed FBS (Gibco, catalogue no. 15605639). For metabolic incorporation of tetraacetyl‐N‐azidoacetyl‐mannosamine (ManNAz; Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 900917), cells were cultured in high‐glucose DMEM with 1 mM sodium pyruvate, either serum‐free or supplemented with native or EV‐depleted FBS.

Fluorescence Microscopy of Fixed Cells

Cells were seeded (10,000 cells/cm2) onto glass coverslips in 12‐well plates (VWR, catalogue no. 10062–894) and cultured for 48 h. Cells were washed with HBSS and incubated with the respective substrate in serum‐free media. Following a wash with HBSS, cells were subjected to chemical fixation in 4% w/v paraformaldehyde (PFA) for 15 min at room temperature. Cells were washed twice with PBS (pH 7.4) and azide‐groups were stained by overnight incubation at room temperature with CuAAC reaction solution: 200 µM TBTA ligand (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 678937), 500 µM TCEP (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. C4706), 2 µM Fluor 488‐Alkyne (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 761621), and 50 µM CuSO4 (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 451657) in PBS. After two washes with PBS, nuclei were stained with 1 µg/mL DAPI (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. D9542) for 15 min at room temperature and washed three times with PBS. Slides were mounted using aqueous mounting medium (Dako, catalogue no. S3025) and imaged by widefield fluorescence microscopy.

ManNAz Visualization on the Cell Surface and in Fixed Cells

Cells were seeded (3250 cells/cm2) onto glass coverslips in 12‐well plates and cultured for 48 h. Cells were washed with HBSS and incubated with 100 µM ManNAz or 0.1% DMSO in 10% native serum‐supplemented media. For CuAAC‐staining of azide‐groups on live cell surfaces, cells were washed twice with ice‐cold HBSS and incubated with CuAAC reaction solution prepared in ice‐cold HBSS: 10 µM sulfo‐Cyanine3 alkyne (Lumiprobe, catalogue no. A13B0), 750 µM BTTAA ligand (Lumiprobe, catalogue no. 3253), 150 µM CuSO4 (added as a pre‐mixed solution with BTTAA), 750 µM aminoguanidine hydrochloride (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 368910250), and 750 µM sodium L‐ascorbate (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. A4034). After 40 min incubation at 4°C, cells were washed three times with ice‐cold HBSS. Nuclei were stained with 1 µg/mL Hoechst 33342 (Invitrogen, catalogue no. H3570) for 10 min at room temperature and then washed three times with HBSS. For visualization of ManNAz in fixed cells, cells were washed with HBSS and incubated in 4% w/v PFA for 15 min at room temperature. Cells were washed twice with PBS and incubated for 5 h with CuAAC reaction solution: 5 µM Fluor 488‐Alkyne, 750 µM BTTAA ligand, 150 µM CuSO4 (added as a pre‐mixed solution with BTTAA), 3.75 mM aminoguanidine hydrochloride, and 3.75 mM sodium L‐ascorbate. After two washes with PBS, nuclei were stained with 1 µg/mL DAPI for 15 min at room temperature and washed three times with PBS. All slides were mounted using aqueous mounting medium and imaged by widefield fluorescence microscopy.

Cell Viability Assay

Cell viability after treatment with the azide‐substrates was determined by the 3‐[4,5‐dimethylthiazol‐2‐yl]‐2,5‐diphenyltetrazolium bromide (MTT) assay. Cells were seeded (10,000 cells/cm2) in surface‐treated 24‐well plates (VWR, catalogue no. 10062–896) and cultured for 48 h. After washing with pre‐warmed HBSS, cells were incubated with the azide‐substrates at the selected concentrations in triplicates. Media were replaced by DMEM supplemented with 0.5 mg/mL MTT (Invitrogen, catalogue no. M6494) for 3 h incubation at 37°C. Formazan crystals were dissolved in DMSO and absorbance was recorded at 570 nm using a microplate reader.

Preparation of EV‐Depleted FBS

Heat‐inactivated FBS was diluted in high‐glucose DMEM with 1 mM sodium pyruvate to a concentration of 20% and subsequently ultracentrifuged at 110,000 × g for 18 h at 4°C (fixed angle MLA‐50 rotor, k‐factor 118, Optima MAX‐XP ultracentrifuge, Beckman Coulter) (Théry et al.; Shelke et al.). The supernatant was sterilized by passaging through a 0.2 µm PES bottle top filter (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 596–4520) and diluted with serum‐free medium supplemented with 1 mM sodium pyruvate to a final concentration of 10% FBS.

Proliferation Rate Assay

Cells were seeded (5000 cells/cm2) onto surface‐treated six‐well plates (VWR, catalogue no. 10062–982) and cultured for 48 h in complete media. Subsequently, fresh culture media with 10% of the respective serum supplementations were added to each well, followed by incubation for another 48 h. The initial (Ni) and final (Nf) cell numbers were determined in separate wells in quadruplicates at t = 48 h and t = 96 h, respectively. Cells were detached with 0.25% trypsin‐EDTA and cell suspensions were mixed with 0.4% trypan blue solution (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. T8154) to stain dead cells. Trypan blue‐negative cells were counted by a LUNA FL automated cell counter (Logos Biosystems) and proliferation rates were calculated using the equation: where ∆t is the time frame between the initial and final cell number (i.e., 2 days).

Preparation of Metabolically Labelled and Unlabelled Soluble Protein and EV Fractions

To obtain cell‐conditioned media, cells were seeded (5000 cells/cm2) in surface‐treated T182 flasks (VWR, catalogue no. 734–2315) and cultured for 3 days. Before incubation with EV‐depleted FBS or serum‐free media, cells were washed twice with pre‐warmed HBSS. Fresh media supplemented with native or EV‐depleted FBS (or serum‐free). For metabolic labelling, the respective azide‐substrate (e.g., AHA or ManNAz) were added and cells were incubated until harvest of conditioned media. After addition of complete EDTA‐free protease inhibitor cocktail (Roche, catalogue no. 11873580001), conditioned media were centrifuged at 300 × g for 10 min to discard detached cells and at 2000 × g for 20 min to discard cell debris and large apoptotic bodies. EVs were pelleted by ultracentrifugation at 110,000 × g for 2:15 h at 4°C (fixed angle MLA‐50 rotor, k‐factor 118, Optima MAX‐XP ultracentrifuge, Beckman Coulter). The supernatant was collected and referred to as the soluble protein fraction. For mass spectrometric analyses and validation experiments for GJC1, EVs were washed with PBS (pH 7.2) by another round of ultracentrifugation. EV pellets were resuspended in PBS (pH 7.2) and collected in protein LoBind tubes (Eppendorf).

Biotinylation of Azide‐Labelled EVs

PBS (7.2) was added to each sample for a total volume of 4 mL. Free thiols were blocked by incubation with 5 mM maleimide (Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. 129585) for 2 h at room temperature. Samples were centrifuged at 3000 × g for 10 min to pellet and discard any formed aggregates. Azide‐labelled EVs were biotinylated by addition of the CuAAC components in the following order: 25 µM PC biotin alkyne (Vector Laboratories, catalogue no. CCT‐1118), 750 µM BTTAA ligand, 150 µM CuSO4 (added as a pre‐mixed solution with BTTAA), 3.75 mM aminoguanidine hydrochloride, and 3.75 mM sodium L‐ascorbate. Following overnight incubation at room temperature in the dark while rotating end‐over‐end, samples were centrifuged at 3000 × g for 10 min to pellet and discard any formed aggregates. Samples were concentrated to 1 mL in Amicon Ultra‐4 centrifugal filter units (10 kDa MWCO, Millipore, catalogue no. UFC8010) and buffer‐exchanged to PBS (pH 7.2) by repeated dilution and concentration to 1 mL three times. In the final step, samples were concentrated to a volume of 500 µL.

Biotinylation of Azide‐Labelled Proteins

Fractions (4 mL) of the ultracentrifugation supernatant were concentrated to 2.5 mL in Amicon Ultra‐15 centrifugal filter units (10 kDa MWCO, Millipore, catalogue no. UFC9010) and buffer‐exchanged to PBS (pH 7.2) by repeated dilution and concentration to 2.5 mL three times. Concentrates were collected in protein LoBind tubes (Eppendorf) and an additional volume of 1.5 mL PBS (pH 7.2) was used to rinse the membrane for a total volume of 4 mL per sample. To block free thiols, samples were incubated with maleimide at a concentration of 100 mM (for serum‐supplemented media) or 5 mM (for serum‐free media) for 2 h at room temperature. To biotinylate azides by SPAAC, 25 µM PC DBCO biotin (Vector Laboratories, catalogue no. CCT‐1120) was added. For CuAAC‐mediated biotinylation of proteins in serum‐supplemented media, reaction components were added in the following order: 25 µM PC biotin alkyne, 10 mM BTTAA ligand, 2 mM CuSO4 (added as a pre‐mixed solution with BTTAA), 50 mM aminoguanidine hydrochloride and 50 mM sodium L‐ascorbate. For serum‐free media, the same CuAAC composition used for biotinylation of EVs was applied. SPAAC and CuAAC reaction mixtures were incubated overnight at room temperature in the dark while rotating end‐over‐end, followed by buffer‐exchange to PBS (pH 7.2) in Amicon Ultra‐15 centrifugal filter units (10 kDa MWCO) by repeated dilution and concentration to 2.5 mL three times.

Affinity Purification and UV‐Elution of Biotinylated Proteins and EVs

For each sample, that is, biotinylated proteins or EVs obtained in Sections 2.8 and 2.9, 150 µL (50% aqueous slurry) of Pierce high capacity NeutrAvidin agarose (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 29202) was pre‐washed with PBS (pH 7.2) three times and incubated with the biotinylated sample at 4°C overnight rotating end‐over‐end in a protein LoBind tube (Eppendorf). The agarose was pelleted by centrifugation at 300 × g for 2 min and the unbound fraction was collected. The agarose was then washed five times by the addition of PBS, inverting 10 times, and centrifugation. After the final wash, the agarose was resuspended in 250 µL PBS and transferred to a clear side macro cuvette (UV grade PMMA, Kartell, catalogue no. 10368834, 12 × 12 × 45 mm, 4.5 mL). Proteins or EVs were eluted from the agarose by irradiation with UV light (365 nm) for 2 h using a 6 W Spectroline Model ENF‐260C lamp at a distance of 4 cm while the agarose was continuously stirred by a magnetic micro stirring bar. Agarose was spun down and the supernatant (i.e., UV‐eluted fraction) was collected.

Plasma EV Isolation

Blood samples were collected from three healthy adult donors (age 29–35) at the TechMed Centre Donor Service of the University of Twente (Enschede, the Netherlands) in the morning (08.00 to 09.00). Informed consent was obtained from all donors, and the study protocol was approved by the Ethics Committee Natural Sciences and Engineering Sciences (University of Twente). See Table  S1  for donor characteristics. Whole blood was drawn using a 21 G Vacutainer Eclipse needle (BD, catalogue no. 368650) into VACUETTE 9 mL K2E K2EDTA collection tubes (Greiner Bio‐one, catalogue no. 455045). The first 3 mL were discarded. Blood was transferred to a standard 15 mL conical base tube and centrifuged at 2500 × g for 15 min at room temperature without brake (Coumans et al.). The platelet‐poor plasma was collected 1 cm above the buffy coat, transferred to a new 15 mL tube and centrifuged a second time at 2500 × g for 15 min at room temperature without brake. The resulting platelet‐free plasma was collected from the top, leaving 1 cm above the bottom of the tube. Fractions of 1 mL of platelet‐free plasma were diluted with 8 mL PBS (pH 7.4) and centrifuged at 3000 × g for 20 min at 4°C to discard any remaining debris. The supernatant was collected, and EVs were pelleted by ultracentrifugation at 110,000 × g for 2:15 h at 4°C (fixed angle MLA‐50 rotor, k‐factor 118, Optima MAX‐XP ultracentrifuge, Beckman Coulter) and resuspended in ice‐cold PBS. EV fractions from the same donor were pooled and washed in PBS by another ultracentrifugation step. EVs were resuspended in PBS, and their proteins were precipitated with methanol/chloroform.

SDS‐PAGE and Western Blot Analysis

For the preparation of cell lysates, confluent cell cultures were washed three times with PBS (pH 7.4) and ice‐cold RIPA buffer with complete EDTA‐free protease inhibitor cocktail was added. Lysates were sonicated on ice (three cycles 3 s on, 60 s off at 50% amplitude) and cleared by centrifugation at 14,000 × g for 15 min at 4°C. All protein concentrations were determined by Pierce BCA assay (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 23227) or micro BCA assay (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 23235) according to the manufacturer's protocol. Protein samples were either directly mixed with 4× sample loading buffer or precipitated with methanol/chloroform and reconstituted with 1× sample loading buffer. Samples were boiled at 95°C for 5 min and proteins were resolved by SDS‐PAGE on a 10% polyacrylamide gel at 60 V for 25 min followed by 120 V for 80 min. For total protein analysis, gels were stained with Coomassie blue solution (0.1% Coomassie brilliant blue R dye, 30% methanol, 60% water, 10% acetic acid) for 1 h and destained in deionized water. For western blot detection, proteins were transferred to a PVDF membrane (Roche, catalogue no. 03010040001) at 100 V for 2:15 h. Membranes were blocked with 5% w/v non‐fat dry milk (NFDM) in TBS (pH 7.6) for 1 h at room temperature, followed by overnight incubation at 4°C with the primary antibody diluted in 0.5% w/v NFDM in TBS: mouse monoclonal anti‐ALIX (1:800 dilution, Abcam, catalogue no. ab117600), rabbit monoclonal anti‐ALIX (1:3,000 dilution, Abcam, catalogue no. ab275377), mouse monoclonal anti‐CD63 (1:500 dilution, Invitrogen, catalogue no. 10628D), rabbit polyclonal anti‐bovine ApoB100 (1:1,000 dilution, antibodies‐online, catalogue no. ABIN6994867), rabbit polyclonal anti‐connexin 45/GJC1 (1:500 dilution, Invitrogen, catalogue no. PA5‐97784), rabbit monoclonal anti‐connexin 45/GJC1 (1:1,800 dilution, Abcam, catalogue no. ab316742), rabbit monoclonal anti‐CD9 (1:1,500 dilution, Cell Signalling Technology, catalogue no. 13174), rabbit polyclonal anti‐Hsc70/HSPA8 (1:3,500 dilution, Proteintech, catalogue no. 10654‐1‐AP), rabbit monoclonal anti‐syntenin‐1 (1:8,000 dilution, Invitrogen, catalogue no. MA5‐53245), rabbit monoclonal anti‐GM130 (1:1,200 dilution, Sigma–Aldrich, catalogue no. ZRB1513). Membranes were washed with TBST (0.1% Tween 20) and incubated for 1 h at room temperature with the corresponding horseradish peroxidase (HRP)‐conjugated secondary antibody in 0.5% NFDM in TBST: goat anti‐mouse HRP (1:2,500 dilution, Abcam, catalogue no. ab6789), goat anti‐rabbit HRP (1:2,500 dilution, Abcam, catalogue no. ab6721). For protein detection in plasma‐derived EVs, membranes were incubated with TidyBlot Western Blot Detection Reagent:HRP (1:200 dilution, Bio‐Rad, catalogue no. STAR209) in 0.5% NFDM in TBST to suppress interference from denatured IgG. After washing with TBST, membranes were developed using Pierce ECL Plus Western blotting substrate (Thermo Scientific, catalogue no. 32132) and the chemiluminescent signal was detected by a FluorChem M imaging system (ProteinSimple).

Cryogenic Transmission Electron Microscopy (Cryo‐TEM)

For cryo‐TEM analysis of isolated EVs, three sample aliquots of 3 µL were applied to a glow‐discharged (40 s, 10 mA) Quantifoil R 2/1 holey‐carbon 200 mesh gold TEM grid (Electron Microscopy Sciences) and blotted manually for 3 s. Excess liquid was drained with a filter paper each time. Another 3 µL aliquot was blotted on the grid and plunge‐frozen into liquid ethane with a Vitrobot Mark IV (room temperature, 100% humidity, force −10, blot time of 6 s; FEI). Vitrified samples were stored in liquid nitrogen until imaging in a TALOS F200C‐G2 transmission electron microscope (Thermo Fisher) at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. Images were recorded with 10 s exposure times by a Falcon 4i direct electron detector (Thermo Fisher) at a defocus range of 1–6 µm, with an electron dose of 0.65 e–/Å2/s or 4.5 e–/Å2/s at a nominal magnification of 36,000× (0.397 nm pixel size) or 73,000× (0.194 nm pixel size), respectively.

Mass Spectrometry Analysis

MS Sample Preparation—Protein Extraction

Proteins of EV samples were precipitated with methanol/chloroform and frozen at −80°C until further processing. A slightly modified protocol of the GeLC‐MS method described by Lawlor et al. was used for optimal performance with minimal starting material (Makridakis and Vlahou). In brief, the sample extracts were re‐suspended in 7 M urea, 2 M thiourea, 4% w/v CHAPS, 1% w/v DTE, 2% v/v IPG buffer (ampholytes), 3.6% v/v protease inhibitors and the total protein content was loaded into a polyacrylamide gel (5% stacking, 12% separating) and electrophoresis was performed at 40 V for 15 min followed by 120 V for 10 min at room temperature. Band excision and overnight in‐gel trypsin digestion were performed using 10 ng/µL trypsin stock solution in 0.01 M ammonium bicarbonate (pH 8.5). Afterward, the samples were lyophilized.

LC‐MS/MS

Lyophilized peptides from the soluble fractions were re‐dissolved in 100 µL of HPLC‐grade water. Subsequently, 5 µL of the peptide mixture was injected into a Dionex Ultimate 3000 RSLC nano flow system (Dionex, Camberley, United Kingdom) configured with a Dionex 0.1 × 20 mm 5 µm C18 nano trap column. Lyophilized peptides were re‐dissolved in 10 µL of HPLC‐grade water, while 5 µL of the peptide mixture was injected into a Dionex Ultimate 3000 RSLC nano flow system, respectively. Mobile phase was 2% ACN: 0.1% FA with a flow rate of 5 µL/min. The analytical column was an Acclaim PepMap C18 nano column 75 µm × 50 cm, 2 µm 100 Å at a flow rate of 300 nL/min. The trap and nano‐flow column were maintained at 35°C. The samples were eluted with a gradient starting at 1% B for 5 min rising to 5% B at 10 min then to 25% B at 150 min and 65% B at 240 min. Mobile phase A was 0.1% formic acid in water while mobile phase B was 0.1% formic acid in 80% acetonitrile. The column was washed and re‐equilibrated prior to each sample injection. The eluent was ionized using a Proxeon nano spray ESI source operating in positive ion mode. For mass spectrometry analysis, a Q Exactive Orbitrap (Thermo Finnigan, Bremen, Germany) was operated in MS/MS mode, scanning from 380 to 2000 m/z. Ionization voltage was 2.6 kV and the capillary temperature was 275°C. The resolution of ions in MS1 was 60,000 and 7500 for higher‐energy collisional dissociation (HCD) MS2. The top 20 multiply charged ions were selected from each scan for MS/MS analysis using HCD at 35% collision energy. Dynamic exclusion was enabled with a repeat count of 1, exclusion duration of 30 s.

Post‐Acquisition Proteomics Data Analysis

The RAW mass spectrometry data were processed and analysed as follows. Protein identification was performed with Proteome Discoverer 2.4 (Thermo Scientific) using the SEQUEST search engine and the SwissProt human and bovine protein databases. The human FASTA file was downloaded from Uniprot database (https://www.uniprot.org/) on 18 August 2022 and included 20,398 reviewed entries. The bovine FASTA file was downloaded from Uniprot database on 1 November 2022 and included 9913 reviewed entries. Only canonical sequences were considered. The following search parameters were applied: (i) precursor mass tolerance: 10 ppm and fragment mass tolerance: 0.05 Da; (ii) full tryptic digestion; (iii) maximum missed cleavage sites: 2; (iv) static modifications: carbamidomethylation of cysteine; (v) dynamic modifications: oxidation of methionine and proline. Subsequently, the datasets were exported at the peptide and protein level using the following filters: (i) peptide confidence: high and medium; (ii) peptide rank 1; and (iii) percolator software package for post‐processing proteomics data, (iv) 1% FDR for high peptide, (v) protein grouping enabled (based on strict parsimony principle). Quantification of proteomics data was based on the precursor ion peak area and all peptides were included. Protein abundance in each sample was calculated as the sum of all unique peptide peak areas for a given protein, followed by part per million (ppm) normalization.

FASTA files of canonical protein sequences were retrieved from reviewed entries of the human and bovine Uniprot databases to determine sequence homology to assign human or bovine species‐specificity to the detected peptides. Peptides with complete sequence homology between the two species, or those differing only by opposing leucine and isoleucine residues at the same position, were assigned species‐ambiguity. Peptides and proteins were considered detected when quantified in at least two of three biological replicates. Protein identifications based on species‐ambiguous sequences were discarded when the same protein was identified with both human and bovine species‐specificity within the same sample. For analyses of label‐free intensities, missing values were imputed at the peptide level with half of the minimum of the non‐missing elements.

Image Analysis

Fluorescence microscopy images were analysed in Python (v3.9.7, https://www.python.org/), utilizing Numpy (v1.20.0) (Harris et al.), Pandas (v1.3.4) (McKinney), skimage (v0.18.3) (van der Walt et al.) and SciPy (v1.7.1) (Virtanen et al.) packages. Cell numbers were determined by enumeration of cell nuclei in the DAPI channel images, which were segmented by the watershed algorithm using the OpenCV library (Bradski). To define the binary mask of pixel areas occupied by cells, Fluor‐488 channel images were smoothened with a size 3 median filter using the SciPy Ndimage submodule. Smoothened images were then thresholded to the mean value of the background, which was determined for each experimental replicate separately. After background subtraction, total fluorescence intensity was determined on the original image by summation of pixel values within the binary mask. Mean Fluor‐488 fluorescence was determined by normalization to the cell count of the corresponding DAPI channel image.

Overrepresentation Analysis

Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis for cellular component (CC) was performed by the Database for Annotation, Visualization and Integrated Discovery (DAVID) (Huang et al.; Sherman et al.) (v2023q4) web server (https://davidbioinformatics.nih.gov/) using default parameters and Benjamini–Hochberg corrected p values. Overrepresentation analyses of GO gene sets for Molecular Function (MF) and Biological Process (BP) were performed using the ‘enrichGO’ function from the Clusterprofiler package (Wu et al.) (v4.10.0) using default parameters. Redundant GO terms were removed using the ‘simplify’ function with a similarity cutoff set to 0.7. Enrichment of mesenchymal (MES), adrenergic (ADRN), and transitional (TRANS) gene signatures (van Groningen et al.; Yuan et al.) was assessed by two‐sided Fisher's exact test and Bonferroni multiple‐comparison correction using base R statistics (v4.3.1, https://www.r‐project.org/) with the reviewed Uniprot database of the human proteome (20,398 entries) as the background set. Fold enrichment was calculated as described previously (Wu et al.).

Gene Set Enrichment Analysis

Label‐free intensities were preranked according to the log2 fold‐differences (ManNAz/DMSO) and analysed by Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) to evaluate enrichment for GO MF, GO BP and Reactome pathway database gene sets using the Clusterprofiler ‘gseGO’ and ‘GSEA’ functions with default parameters. The Reactome gene set collection was obtained from the Molecular Signatures Database (MSigDB) using the msigdbr R package (v7.5.1) (Dolgalev).

STRING Network Analysis

Protein–protein interaction networks were predicted and plotted by the STRING tool (Szklarczyk et al.) (https://string‐db.org/) (v12.0) using the full STRING network with a medium‐to‐high confidence level (score 0.6). Clusters were assigned by k‐means clustering and disconnected proteins were excluded from the network.

Biomarker Prioritization and Databases

Protein identifications were searched against the reviewed human and bovine Uniprot databases to assign transmembrane localization. Primary neuroblastoma tumour gene expression datasets Kocak (GSE45547) (Kocak et al.), Versteeg (GSE16476) (Molenaar et al.) and NRC (GSE85047) (Rajbhandari et al.) were obtained and used for survival analysis using the optimal expression cutoff on the R2 Genomics Analysis and Visualization Platform (http://r2.amc.nl/). Gene dependencies enriched in neuroblastoma were searched in the Cancer Dependency Map (DepMap) portal (https://depmap.org/portal). Selected proteins were searched against the human Vesiclepedia database (Chitti et al.) for proteomic identification in EVs from any of the sources: platelets, endothelial cells, red blood cells, fibroblasts, keratinocytes, adipocytes, neutrophils, mast cells, monocytes, macrophage, dendritic cells, T cells, B cells, natural killer cells, lymphoblasts. Associations between the selected proteins and (1) initiation, (2) progression or (3) survival of neuroblastoma reported in the literature were searched in the PubMed database using the query format: ‘neuroblastoma AND (gene_name OR protein_name OR <all synonyms and alternative names listed on Uniprot>)’. Articles reporting MPP+‐stimulated neuroblastoma cells as a model for Parkinson's Disease were disregarded. Effects on neuroblastoma survival were only considered for stimulated conditions when normal cancer pathophysiology was modelled or standard‐of‐care neuroblastoma treatments were used. All databases were accessed on 11 November 2024.

Statistical Analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using base R statistics or the Stats module from the SciPy package in Python. Data were represented as mean ± standard deviation (SD) from three independent experiments. Means of label‐free intensities were compared by Student's t‐test and Benjamini–Hochberg correction for multiple testing. Paired data were analysed by paired sample Student's t‐test. For samples with non‐Gaussian distributions, non‐parametric Mann–Whitney U test was used. For multiple comparisons, repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Dunnett's post‐hoc test was used. Significant hazard ratios were determined by Cox regression analysis and Kaplan–Meier survival analysis was performed by log‐rank test using R2. Equal representation of proteins detected exclusively in EVd‐ManNAz and those shared with Nat‐ManNAz in the STRING network clusters was determined by Chi‐squared (χ 2) test after pooling the five smallest clusters. All tests were two‐tailed and differences were considered significant when p < 0.05.

Results

AHA Labels Nascent Proteins

Considering the presence of methionine in the majority of the human proteome (94%) (Dieterich et al.), we first investigated nascent protein labelling with the methionine analogue L‐azidohomoalanine (AHA) (Figure 1a), which is accepted by the mammalian translation machinery and inserted into the positions of its natural counterpart during protein synthesis (Dieterich et al.). We cultured the neuroblastoma cell line SH‐EP2 in complete media and then exposed the cells to AHA in serum‐ and methionine‐free conditions. After cell fixation, we visualized AHA by conjugation to alkyne‐Fluor 488 through Cu(I)‐catalysed azide–alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC). Fluorescence microscopy revealed that the incorporation of AHA occurred in a time‐ (Figure  S1a ) and concentration‐dependent manner (Figure 1b,c). In addition, treatment with the ribosomal translation inhibitor anisomycin impeded the incorporation of AHA, confirming metabolism‐dependence.

Effects of AHA Labelling on Cell Viability Limit Treatment Durations

Because introducing azide groups may interfere with protein structure and function, we assessed whether cell fitness was compromised upon incorporation of AHA. We treated cells with AHA‐pulses of increasing concentrations and durations in methionine‐free media and assessed the cellular viability by MTT assay relative to a control pulsed with natural methionine. Although no effects were observed upon a 6‐h AHA‐pulse at any of the tested concentrations (p = 0.097, repeated measures ANOVA) (Figure 1d), 24‐ and 48‐h pulses substantially reduced cellular viability already at a concentration of 1 µM (24 h: p = 0.021; 48 h: p < 0.0001, Dunnett's post‐hoc), which further decreased with increasing concentrations of AHA (Figure 1e,f).

Although it is unknown to what extent the observed viability effects are due to AHA incorporation or methionine deprivation, either may be mitigated by concurrent supplementation with natural methionine during AHA treatments (Bagert et al.), potentially permitting longer incubation times without loss of viability. To explore this possibility, we treated cells with AHA and added methionine in molar ratios increasing from 10:1 to 1:1 (AHA:methionine). Unfortunately, however, the extent of azide‐labelling from exclusive treatment with AHA (100 µM) was almost completely nullified by the lowest concentration of methionine (10 µM) (Figure  S1b ), which prompted us to restrict treatment durations as the only means to prevent cell viability effects.

Given that EV‐associated proteins are typically present in low abundance in conditioned culture media (Chhoy et al., Hisey et al.), cells are often cultured for 2–3 days to maximize EV yields (Sharma et al.; Colletti et al., Rontogianni et al.). Hence, we asked whether incorporated AHA residues may be depleted within these time frames. To test this, we subjected cells to a 3‐h pulse with AHA, followed by a methionine chase for 0, 21, 45, or 69 h. After fixation and fluorescent labelling, the retention of AHA was determined by quantifying the average total fluorescence intensity per cell (Figure 1g,h). As expected, the AHA levels peaked immediately after the 3‐h pulse (i.e., 100%), which was followed by a decay to 39% after 21 h, 26% after 45 h, and 17% after 69 h. These results indicate that EVs may still present azides upon secretion after any of the chase periods tested.

Secreted Proteins Cannot be Isolated After a 6‐h AHA Pulse

We then aimed to exploit the incorporated azide groups as a handle for selective capture of EVs secreted by the cultured cells. To this end, we developed a four‐step approach (Figure  2 ). After metabolic labelling of the cells with the azide‐substrate and enrichment of EVs from the conditioned culture media by ultracentrifugation, surface‐accessible thiol groups of cysteine residues in proteins are blocked by maleimide to prevent nonspecific thiol‐yne reactivity (van Geel et al.) (Figure  2  ‐i). Azide groups are then conjugated to a photocleavable (PC) PEG3‐biotin linker by azide–alkyne cycloaddition (Figure  2  ‐ii), followed by the removal of excessive linkers by ultrafiltration (not shown). Upon capture of biotinylated EVs on NeutrAvidin agarose (Figure  2  ‐iii), non‐biotinylated elements are removed by washes in PBS. Finally, irradiation with UV light (365 nm) cleaves the linker to facilitate selective and reagent‐free elution of bound EVs (Figure  2  ‐iv), thereby obviating the need for harsh washing steps and denaturing conditions that may impinge upon the structural integrity of EVs.

To assess the validity of our approach, we cultured cells with native serum and first sought to isolate secreted soluble proteins, given the typical scarcity of EVs produced in culture (Hisey et al.). We collected the conditioned culture media after a 6‐h AHA‐pulse and after the ensuing 42‐h methionine chase (Figure  S2a ). Considering the simplicity offered by copper‐free click chemistry, we initially explored biotinylation via strain‐promoted azide–alkyne cycloaddition (SPAAC) using the photocleavable (PC) DBCO‐PEG3‐biotin linker (Figure  S2b ). However, visualization of total isolated proteins by SDS‐PAGE revealed substantial capture of unlabelled proteins for both time points from the cells pulsed with methionine (Figure  S2c,d ). Instead, when proteins were biotinylated through CuAAC using PC alkyne‐PEG3‐biotin (Figure  2 ), barely any nonspecific isolation was observed (Figure  S2e,f ). When the thiol‐blocking step with maleimide was omitted, we found that unlabelled proteins were co‐isolated, demonstrating its necessity to minimize azide‐independent protein isolation. Unfortunately, however, the recovery of secreted proteins from AHA‐treated cells did not exceed the background of the methionine control, suggesting insufficient AHA‐incorporation into secreted proteins or limited solvent‐accessibility of azide‐groups for biotinylation.

Because serum supplements contain free amino acids, we asked whether methionine from serum supplementation could have reduced the incorporation of AHA, thus impairing the isolation of secreted proteins. When cells were cultured with 10% serum, we indeed observed a reduction in the presence of azides, which could not be rescued by the use of serum that was deprived of free amino acids by dialysis (Figure  S3 ). Nonetheless, a substantial amount of azide groups remained. This suggests that the serum supplementation may only partially explain why secreted proteins could not be isolated from the conditioned media. All in all, we conclude that AHA is not suitable for selective isolation of the secretome, at least within time constraints imposed by its adverse effects on cell viability and under non‐denaturing conditions.

ManNAz Labels Nascent Glycans

As the EV surface is substantially glycosylated (Lin et al.), we next explored the labelling of nascent glycans using the sugar analogue tetraacetyl‐N‐azidoacetyl‐mannosamine (ManNAz) in place of labelling nascent proteins with AHA (Figure  2 ). ManNAz passively diffuses across the plasma membrane, upon which its acetyl groups are cleaved by nonspecific cellular esterases (Saxon et al.). The sugar is then enzymatically converted to N‐azidoacetyl‐sialic acid (SiaNAz) and transferred to the terminal positions of nascent glycoconjugates.

When we treated cells with ManNAz, we observed robust azide incorporation compared to the unlabelled control (Figure 3a), showing a dose‐dependent increase within 48 h of labelling (Figure 3b). Detailed imaging revealed a concentrated and punctate staining pattern around the nuclei in fixed cells (Figure 3c), which is consistent with previous observations showing that ManNAz co‐localizes with the Golgi complex, where sialylation takes place (Scache et al.). Localization of ManNAz on the cell surface was visualized on live cells to avoid intracellular access of the fluorophore as a result of the increased permeability upon paraformaldehyde‐fixation. Here, we used the cell‐impermeable sulfo‐Cy3‐alkyne for conjugation, conducted at 4°C to prevent energy‐dependent internalization of the fluorophore (Hong et al.). We observed a notably different staining distribution (Figure 3c), with no detectable signal in unlabelled cells, showing that azide groups reached the cell surface and are accessible to CuAAC‐mediated conjugation. Importantly, we found that none of the tested concentrations of ManNAz substantially impaired cellular viability within 48 h (p = 0.375, repeated measures ANOVA) (Figure 3d).

When we employed ManNAz labelling (100 µM) for the selective isolation of proteins secreted in culture, proteins of various sizes were captured from serum‐free (Figure  S4 ) and native serum‐supplemented media (Figure 3e). By contrast, no proteins were isolated from unlabelled cells, treated with 0.1% DMSO (vehicle). In addition, protein elution was almost completely abrogated when the UV irradiation step was omitted, confirming that protein recovery depends on the cleavage of the linker.

ManNAz‐Labelled EVs are Selectively Isolated From Native Serum‐Supplemented Media

Encouraged by the successful isolation of soluble ManNAz‐labelled proteins, we employed metabolic glyco‐labelling to isolate nascent EVs secreted in culture media. To this end, we cultured cells in native serum‐supplemented media for 48 h with ManNAz (100 µM) or DMSO (0.1%). Subsequently, neuroblastoma‐derived EVs and exogenous serum EVs were collectively harvested from the media by ultracentrifugation and used in our isolation procedure (Figure  2 ).

The selective capture of ManNAz‐labelled EVs was demonstrated by western blotting for typical EV marker proteins, including transmembrane proteins (CD63, CD9) and proteins of the EV lumen (ALIX, syntenin‐1, HSPA8) (Figure 4a). Accordingly, unlabelled EVs did not adhere to the NeutrAvidin agarose and were exclusively detected in the unbound fraction. The absence of cellular organelle contamination in the EV fractions was assessed by analysis of the cis‐Golgi protein GM130, which was only detected in the cell lysate. To evaluate the capture of common non‐vesicular co‐isolates from serum, we studied the apolipoprotein B‐100 (ApoB100). We detected ApoB100 not only in the unbound fractions, but also in the UV‐eluted fraction of ManNAz‐labelled EVs, which would be consistent with several reported observations of lipoproteins adhering to EVs and participating in the formation of the EV ‘corona’ (Busatto et al.; Sódar et al.; Tóth et al.). Further characterization of eluted ManNAz‐labelled EVs by cryo‐TEM revealed spherical structures enclosed by a lipid bilayer (Figure 4b), with diameters corresponding to those reported for exosome (30−100 nm) and microvesicle (100−1000 nm) EV subtypes (Ståhl et al.).

Mass Spectrometry Analysis of Isolated EVs

Although complete omission of serum from culture media can impose cellular stresses and affect both the quantity and content of EVs released (Böröczky et al.; Li et al.), even the supplementation of EV‐depleted serum has been reported to slow cellular proliferation compared to its unprocessed counterpart (Eitan et al.), as we observed for the SH‐EP2 neuroblastoma cell line too (Figure  S6 ). Furthermore, ManNAz labelling may also affect protein expression. Therefore, we studied the proteome of EVs secreted in culture with native serum and mapped the differences with EVs from cells grown with EV‐depleted serum. For this, SH‐EP2 cells were cultured for 48 h with ManNAz (100 µM) or DMSO (0.1%) in the presence of 10% of either EV‐depleted serum (EVd‐DMSO, EVd‐ManNAz) or native serum (Nat‐ManNAz), as depicted (Figure 5a). Following the collection of conditioned media and enrichment of EVs by ultracentrifugation, only EVs from Nat‐ManNAz were used for selective isolation as in Figure  2 . EV proteomes were then trypsinized and subjected to label‐free LC‐MS/MS analysis (Table  S2 ). To avoid misinterpretation of bovine serum‐derived co‐isolates as protein identifications from the human neuroblastoma cell line, we conducted independent searches against the human and bovine Uniprot databases, using a 1% false discovery rate (FDR) identification threshold. Based on canonical sequence homology, peptides were assigned either (1) human‐specificity, (2) bovine‐specificity, or (3) species‐ambiguity, which were compiled to separate protein identifications and only counted when detected in at least two of three independent biological replicates.

Comparing EVd‐ManNAz to EVd‐DMSO revealed that ManNAz‐treatments did not alter the proteomic composition of secreted EVs. Of the 787 proteins identified, 741 (94.2%) were shared between the two conditions. The 134 proteins that were assigned human‐specificity showed a similar degree of overlap (96.3%) (Figure 5b), with strongly correlated label‐free intensities among the 129 intersecting proteins (Pearson r = 0.998, p = 4.12 × 10−158) (Figure 5c). GSEA was performed to pick up any biological changes that are too subtle to detect at the level of individual proteins. Here, we found no up‐ or down‐regulation for any of the GO BP, GO MF, or Reactome pathway database gene sets (FDR: 20%). Of note, among the 787 proteins identified in EVd‐ManNAz and EVd‐DMSO, 108 were assigned bovine specificity, including nine transmembrane proteins, underscoring the necessity to categorize peptide detections by species‐specificity, even when EV‐depleted serum is used (Pham et al.).

Proteomic analysis of EVs produced in native serum (Nat‐ManNAz) yielded a total of 510 protein detections, subdivided into 69 humans (Figure 5d), 339 ambiguous and 102 bovine identifications (Table  S2 ). Despite the possibility of non‐enzymatic conjugation of peracylated sugars to cysteine residues (Qin et al.), we found no bias toward the detection of proteins with high cysteine content in the total proteome (p = 0.678, Mann–Whitney U) (Figure  S7a ) nor in the extracellular domains of transmembrane proteins (p = 0.935, Mann–Whitney U) (Figure  S7b ). Among the proteins detected with human‐specificity, almost a third is associated with membranes according to Uniprot annotation, either by transmembrane domain(s) (n = 16), a GPI‐anchor (n = 1), or as a peripheral membrane protein (n = 5) (Figure 5d). A subset of 18 proteins overlapped with the Vesiclepedia (Chitti et al.) top‐100 most frequently identified proteins in EVs, including classical EV markers (CD63, CD81, CD9) and recently proposed universal exosomal markers (ITGB1, LGALS3BP, GAPDH) (Kugeratski et al.). In addition, GO analysis for CC yielded the strongest enrichment for the term ‘extracellular exosome’ (83%, p adj. = 1.97 × 10−40) (Figure 5e). Together, these findings confirm the capture of ManNAz‐labelled EVs and the amenability to mass spectrometric analysis of the (surface) proteome.

Although most proteins in Nat‐ManNAz (91.3%) were also identified in EVd‐ManNAz (Figure 5d), some proteins were identified only in EVd‐ManNAz. Thus, we questioned whether the proteins found exclusively in EVd‐ManNAz may be the result of altered cellular activities or EV cargo loading in culture with EV‐depleted serum (Lehrich et al.). To investigate this possibility, we studied the physical and functional interactions between the proteins identified exclusively in EVd‐ManNAz or Nat‐ManNAz and those found in both sets by network analysis using the STRING database (Szklarczyk et al.) (Figure 5f). Rather than clustering by the condition they were found in, proteins from the three sets were strongly interconnected. Sub‐networks were identified by k‐means clustering, with the three largest clusters showing functional enrichment for the terms ‘extracellular matrix organization’, ‘carbon/pyruvate metabolism’, and ‘protein folding’. Intersecting proteins and those found exclusively in EVd‐ManNAz were distributed equally among the clusters (p = 0.994, Chi‐squared test), concluding that the proteins identified in EVs produced in EV‐depleted and native serum are functionally equivalent and participate in the same protein interaction networks.

EV Proteins Reflect Attributes of the Parental Cells

To gain further insights into the functional profile of the proteins detected in Nat‐ManNAz, we performed overrepresentation analysis (ORA) for the MF and BP gene ontologies. The top enriched terms for GO MF mainly pertained to ligand‐receptor interactions with cells and the extracellular matrix (Figure 5g). Contributing to this result was the identification of several integrin subunits, which were reported to play a central role in directing organ‐specific localization of circulating tdEVs to promote the formation of pre‐metastatic niches (Hoshino et al.). Among the GO BP ontology, the top enriched terms revolve around wound healing and cell migration (Figure 5h), which are both cellular mechanisms underpinning cancer metastasis (Deyell et al.) and thus align with the metastatic origin of the SH‐EP2 neuroblastoma cell line (Colletti et al.).

We next asked whether EVs may represent the activity of gene expression programs that drive subtype‐specific malignant phenotypes, which have profound implications for the clinical management of cancer (Jiang et al.). Neuroblastoma phenotypes are epigenetically controlled by distinct core regulatory circuitries, conforming to either a committed adrenergic (ADRN) state or an undifferentiated mesenchymal/neural crest cell‐like (MES) state (van Groningen et al.; Boeva et al.). Accordingly, we compared identified proteins to gene signatures of the ADRN (n = 369 genes) and MES (n = 485 genes) types reported by van Groningen et al.. Consistent with the MES‐state of the SH‐EP2 cell line, this signature was strongly overrepresented in its EV proteome (19 of 69 proteins, Bonferroni p = 7.63 × 10−15) (Figure 5i), irrespective of the type of serum supplementation (Figure  S8 ). Conversely, none of the ADRN genes were detected. Similar results were obtained upon comparison to top‐150 gene expression sets defined by Yuan et al., which were based on single‐cell RNA‐seq analyses of surgically resected tumours and led to the discovery of a transitional (TRANS) state that intermediates between the ADRN and MES states. Again, the MES signature was strongly overrepresented (11 of 69 proteins, Bonferroni p = 1.48 × 10−11), with no identifications of the ADRN‐signature and only one of the TRANS‐signature.

Surface Marker Selection Procedure

From all identified proteins, we sought to identify those that could serve as specific epitopes for immunocapture of tdEVs in liquid biopsies obtained from neuroblastoma patients. Starting with a set of 724 unique proteins (Figure 6a ‐i), each detected in at least one of the three conditions (Figure 5a), we first subtracted those identified solely by bovine‐specific peptide sequences (Figure 6a ‐ii). We then searched the Uniprot database for annotations of transmembrane proteins to select those that are accessible on the EV surface, resulting in a set of 120 proteins (Figure 6a ‐iii). For specificity toward EVs of neuroblastoma origin, any potential marker should be undetectable in blood‐circulating EVs of healthy children. Thus, we compared the selected proteins to three mass spectrometric studies of EV fractions isolated from paediatric plasma or serum samples, considering only the included healthy control groups (Kobayashi et al.; Bai et al.; Maller et al.). Because the combined proteome depth was relatively limited, we also included a meta‐analysis of mass spectrometry datasets, encompassing 105 EV fractions from plasma of healthy individuals (including adults) from seven studies, amounting to 4611 unique protein identifications (Vallejo et al.). From these datasets, we eliminated all proteins that were encountered in any of the samples (Figure 6a ‐iv).

Circulating tdEVs are accompanied by an abundance of EVs secreted by non‐cancerous cell types, primarily derived from blood cells (99.8%) such as adaptive immune cells (e.g., T cells, B cells), innate immune cells (e.g., monocytes, neutrophils), and non‐immune blood cells (platelets and erythrocytes), while to a lesser extent from other tissue types such as endothelial cells, adipocytes, fibroblasts and keratinocytes (Auber and Svenningsen; Vallejo et al.; Li et al.). Because immunocapture techniques can purify EVs by targeting low‐abundant surface epitopes (Campos‐Silva et al.), even minimal exposure of a protein on EVs from any of these cell types may strongly diminish the specificity toward tdEVs. Mass spectrometry analyses by data‐dependent acquisition (DDA) are inherently biased toward detecting abundant proteins, resulting in the underrepresentation of lower abundant species (Hu et al.), especially in complex samples derived from plasma or serum. Hence, to discard potential low‐abundant EV surface proteins that may have been overlooked by mass spectrometry, we queried the Vesiclepedia database for proteomic studies of EVs obtained from mono‐cultures of the cell types most prominently contributing to the plasma EV pool (Chitti et al.), keeping only the 16 proteins that were not detected (Figure 6a ‐v, Table  S3 ).

Because neuroblastoma cell lines accumulate alterations under in vitro culture conditions, they may not fully recapitulate all original tumour characteristics and only partially reflect the heterogeneity of the disease (Krawczyk and Kitlińska). Hence, we prioritized the remaining candidates by disease associations (Figure 6a ‐vi), reasoning that proteins implicated in neuroblastoma development and progression are more likely to be expressed and secreted via EVs in vivo rather than being specific to the cell line or culture conditions. First, we looked for correlations between gene expression and overall survival (OS) using three publicly available transcriptomic datasets of primary neuroblastoma tumours (GSE45547 Kocak, et al., GSE16476 Molenaar, et al., GSE85047 Rajbhandari, et al.). Second, we searched the DepMap database (depmap.org/portal, 24Q2+ Chronos scores) for CRISPR knock‐out viability screens of >1100 different cancer cell lines to identify genes that are selectively essential for the growth and survival of neuroblastoma cell lines (n = 37). Third, we searched the PubMed database for known associations reported in the literature. From the 16 candidate markers, the one that fulfilled all criteria was the gap junction gamma‐1 protein (GJC1)—also called connexin 45 (Cx45)—which oligomerizes into hetero‐ (with other connexin isoforms) or homo‐hexamer hemichannels to allow the intercellular exchange of ions and small molecules by coupling onto to hemichannels of adjacent cells (Nielsen et al.). High expression of GJC1 correlated with poor overall survival (Figure 6b ‐d) and event‐free survival (EFS) (Figure  S9 ) probability in all three datasets and neuroblastoma cells are selectively dependent on GJC1 for survival and growth compared to cell lines of other cancer types (p = 1.62 × 10−14). In addition, expression of GJC1 was found to be highly upregulated during tumour formation in a TH‐MYCN mouse model of neuroblastoma (De Wyn et al.). Together, these findings suggest that GJC1 plays a substantial role in neuroblastoma initiation and progression.

Validation of GJC1 as a Potential Marker of Neuroblastoma EVs

Finally, we sought to validate the presence of GJC1 in neuroblastoma EVs and its tumour specificity in blood plasma. For this, we complemented the SH‐EP2 cell line (mesenchymal; MYCN‐non‐amplified) with the isogenic neuroblastoma cell line SH‐SY5Y (adrenergic; MYCN‐non‐amplified) and cell line Kelly (adrenergic; MYCN‐amplified) (Kirschner et al.). Cells were cultured for 48 h with EV‐depleted serum (without metabolic treatments) and EVs were isolated from the culture media by ultracentrifugation for western blot analysis (Figure 6e). Here, we detected GJC1 in EVs from all three cell lines, indicating that its expression and sorting into EVs is not exclusive to either epigenetic program (mesenchymal vs. adrenergic) or MYCN status. Accordingly, GJC1 was also detected in each cell lysate. As our mass spectrometric analyses identified GJC1 only in EVs from cells cultured with EV‐depleted serum, we questioned whether GJC1 expression and secretion via EVs may depend on this culture condition. However, this notion was refuted as GJC1 was readily detected in EVs isolated from SH‐EP2 cells cultured with native serum (Figure 6f). By contrast, GJC1 could not be detected in EVs isolated from the blood plasma of three healthy adult donors (age 29–35) (Figure 6e), underscoring the potential of this protein as a tumour‐specific epitope.

Discussion

Despite the increasingly recognized potential of EVs for cancer biomarker detection by liquid biopsy, there is a paucity of established surface markers that can be targeted to isolate tdEVs, which is essential to monitor tumour dynamics with sufficient sensitivity and specificity. To address the need for tdEV‐specific surface markers, we developed a strategy that involves unbiased metabolic labelling of (membrane) proteins and glycolipids with click chemistry‐reactive substrate analogues for selective isolation of EVs released by cultured cancer cells, allowing the identification of the EV (surface) proteome by mass spectrometry analysis, regardless of the presence of exogenous EVs in the culture media. In turn, selected surface proteins may be targeted to capture tdEVs from patient samples.

Our findings highlight the suitability of the sugar analogue ManNAz for metabolic labelling and capture of EVs. Initially, we explored the methionine surrogate azidohomoalanine (AHA), which is widely used in proteomics research and has the potential to label all methionine‐bearing proteins (Ma and Yates). However, treatment with this analogue in methionine‐free media substantially impacted the cell viability at all tested concentrations for any time frame exceeding 6 h. These findings agree well with those from a recent study, showing that AHA treatments were accompanied by a reduction in proliferation, induction of apoptosis, and profound alterations to gene expression (Kirschner et al.), which was attributed to cellular stress responses to protein unfolding and reduced translation. When we confined AHA labelling to a 6‐h pulse, however, we failed to capture soluble secreted proteins, possibly due to incomplete labelling or limited surface‐accessibility of methionines in proteins under non‐denaturing conditions (Rose et al.). As an alternative, we tested labelling with ManNAz, which is converted to sialic acid and used by sialyltransferases to cap glycans during post‐translational processing (Du et al.). Its distant positioning likely has a two‐fold advantage, that is, minimizing interference with the protein structure and providing steric accessibility to click chemistry‐mediated functionalization. Accordingly, ManNAz labelling enabled selective isolation of secreted soluble proteins and EVs, yet preserving cell viability and the fidelity of the proteomic composition of EVs. Nevertheless, optimal concentrations for ManNAz labelling may depend on the cell type or treatment duration and should therefore be determined empirically (Han et al.).

Although prior studies exploited ManNAz labelling for surface functionalization (Bhatta et al.; Song et al.; Wang et al.; Lu et al.) and capture of EVs (Bhatta et al.; Wu et al.), here we showcased the amenability of this approach to mass spectrometry‐based proteomics for EVs released into complex environments such as native serum. Unlike previous studies, however, our methodology involves blocking of undesired thiol‐yne reactivity (van Geel et al.), copper‐catalysed click chemistry, and selective elution by photocleavage, which resulted in an undetectable background signal for EV markers in the DMSO controls. Despite this, a degree of contamination from the serum persisted in the eluted EV fractions, which we observed for EVs from cultures with EV‐depleted serum, too. In light of the protein‐binding capacity of the EV proteome (Figure 5g), it is conceivable that serum‐derived (lipo)proteins were co‐isolated as the corona on the EV surface, which spontaneously forms upon contact with protein‐rich environments such as biofluids and serum‐supplemented media (Tóth et al.; Buzas). As physical separation of these proteins remains challenging, dual metabolic labelling approaches with both azide‐ and stable isotope‐tagged substrates could be employed to enrich and distinguish proteins originating from cultured cells, respectively (Eichelbaum et al.). Further, our methodology can be exploited as a framework for varying experimental needs. For instance, the photocleavable biotin‐linker may be exchanged for a DTT‐ or hydrazine‐cleavable alternative to study nucleic acids carried by EVs. In addition, cell‐selective analysis of EVs may be achieved in complex co‐culture or xenograft models by ectopic expression of bioorthogonal glycosylation pathways (Cioce et al.) or by exposure to caged metabolic precursors that are selectively released by enzymes overexpressed in cancer (Shim et al.; Li et al.; Wang et al.). Moreover, cellular incorporation of a labelled metabolic substrate introduces a ‘timestamp’ to nascent biomolecules, which can be used to study time‐resolved changes in EV contents in response to cellular stimulations (Shim et al.).

Although the vast majority of proteins identified in EVs from cells cultured with native serum (Nat‐ManNAz) overlapped with those cultured with EV‐depleted serum (EVd‐ManNAz), the protein coverage of the latter was higher. Despite the observed reduction in cellular proliferation in culture with EV‐depleted serum, STRING network analysis revealed that proteins identified exclusively in EVd‐ManNAz were part of the same protein networks as those shared with Nat‐ManNAz. This suggested that the exclusive protein identifications in EVd‐ManNAz may not be the result of changes to cellular activities. Rather, the greater coverage obtained for EVd‐ManNAz may be due to a higher protein mass isolated from the same volume of conditioned culture media. In turn, this could be the result of some inherent losses upon the additional sample handling steps during selective isolation of ManNAz‐labelled EVs in Nat‐ManNAz. Moreover, secretion of EVs is promoted by nutrient‐deprived media (Böröczky et al.; Li et al.; Sun et al.; Bost et al.), and may thus be increased in EV‐depleted serum compared to native serum.

In this study, we mapped the proteomic composition of EVs released by a cell line of the paediatric malignancy neuroblastoma, which exhibits remarkable heterogeneity regarding the disease progression and clinical outcome (Matthay et al.). Although low‐risk neuroblastoma tumours may undergo spontaneous regression, patients with high‐risk tumours receive intensive multimodal treatment, which is typically effective at first but often fails upon relapse due to developed therapy resistance mechanisms (Mañas et al.). Hence, detailed and systemic monitoring of tumour clonal evolution and transcriptional reprogramming is of tremendous value for patient stratification and effective treatment decision‐making. In this study, functional profiling of EV proteomes supported the widespread notion that EV contents can inform about the metastatic process (Colletti et al.; Hoshino et al.; Choi et al.), revealing enrichment for proteins involved in cell migration and wound healing (Deyell et al.), which are processes implicated in metastasis and thus congruent with the metastatic origin of the SH‐EP2 cell line. Furthermore, we found that the mesenchymal transcriptional program of the parental cells was strongly reflected in the EV proteome. Mesenchymal neuroblastoma cells are characterized by increased therapy resistance and were enriched in post‐treatment and recurrent tumours (van Groningen et al.; Mañas et al.). By contrast, predominance of the adrenergic phenotype after treatment has been associated with favourable outcomes (Mañas et al.). Thus, determining the abundance of either cellular identity from the proteome of tdEVs in circulation holds promise for complementary strategies for diagnosis, patient stratification, and monitoring of treatment effectiveness.

To date, the most well‐established surface marker of neuroblastoma is the disialoganglioside (GD2) antigen, which is targeted by immunotherapy (Voeller and Sondel), exploited for enrichment of single tumour cells (Carpenter et al.), and has been used to designate EVs of neuroblastoma origin (Morini et al.). However, because its expression can be down‐regulated upon transcriptional reprogramming (Mabe et al.) and exhibits strong variation among patients and individual tumour cells (Carpenter et al.; Terzic et al.; Schumacher‐Kuckelkorn et al.), certain cell populations may be overlooked. In this study, we sought to identify novel EV surface markers with tumour‐specificity in neuroblastoma. We prioritized candidate markers with disease associations, reasoning that these are more likely to be expressed and released in EVs in vivo, rather than being exclusive to the cell line and specific culture conditions. Thus, we proposed the gap junction protein GJC1 (connexin 45) as a potential biomarker, the high expression of which correlates with poor overall survival, exhibits selective dependence for growth and survival in neuroblastoma, and is overexpressed during early neuroblastoma development (De Wyn et al.). Moreover, the GJC1 gene is located on chromosome 17q21.31, which is gained in neuroblastoma with an incidence of 50% (Ho et al.).

Interestingly, both tumour‐promoting and ‐suppressive effects have been reported for connexins, depending on the connexin isoform, cancer type, and disease stage (Oliveira et al.). For example, overexpression of GJC1 reduced the metastatic potential of melanoma cells (Saito et al.). Conversely, increased GJC1 expression was recognized as an independent prognostic marker of poor prognosis in glioma (Ji et al.). Analyses of glioma expression datasets showed that genes most strongly positively correlated with GJC1 were involved in cell cycle regulation and DNA replication, suggesting that GJC1 promotes cell cycle progression in glioma cells. Accordingly, elevated expression of GJC1 promoted the proliferation of human induced pluripotent stem cells (hiPSCs) (Ke et al.) and transit‐amplifying precursor cells in the subventricular zone (SVZ) by promoting cell cycle re‐entry (Khodosevich et al.). However, the functional role of GJC1 in the context of neuroblastoma development and progression remains unexplored.

Although its expression is not exclusive to tumour cells (Lucaciu et al.), previous mass spectrometric studies and our validation experiment did not detect GJC1 in blood‐circulating EVs of healthy children (Kobayashi et al.; Bai et al., Maller et al.) or healthy adult donors, respectively. Conversely, GJC1 was readily detected in EVs from three neuroblastoma cell lines with different transcriptional programs (adrenergic vs. mesenchymal) and MYCN status. Our observations are supported by several other reported encounters of GJC1 in EV studies using neuroblastoma cell lines of varying origins and phenotypes (Colletti et al.; Marimpietri et al.; Fonseka et al.), suggesting that GJC1 can be a potential pan‐neuroblastoma EV marker.

Together, our findings highlight GJC1 as a potential surface marker for detecting or enriching tdEVs from liquid biopsies of neuroblastoma patients. We acknowledge that further validation of these findings in well‐powered clinical studies will be required to establish its utility as a biomarker. In the case of employing GJC1 for tdEV enrichment in liquid biopsy patient samples, it would require the ability to target an epitope of the extracellular regions of GJC1. However, the development of antibodies against one specific connexin isoform is notoriously challenging due to the small sizes of the two extracellular loops and the high sequence similarity across the 21 human connexin isoforms, while subfamily specificity may be more feasible (Buratto et al.). GJC1 closest relatives are the GJC2 (Cx46.6/Cx47) and GJC3 (Cx30.2/Cx31.3) family members (Bruzzone). GJC2 and GJC3 are expressed by only a small number of organs, with no reported evidence of expression by the vascular endothelium, immune cells, or platelets (Lucaciu et al.). Moreover, these isoforms were not detected in EVs from blood plasma or serum of healthy children or adults (Kobayashi et al.; Bai et al.; Maller et al.; Vallejo et al.). Affinity toward GJC2 or GJC3 by antibodies raised against GJC1 is therefore unlikely to increase the capture of non‐tumorous EVs substantially.

In summary, we report here a novel strategy to study the proteome of EVs secreted by cells cultured in a physiologically relevant in vitro environment in the presence of exogenous EVs. With the advent of novel surface markers, we believe that selective capture and analysis of tdEVs opens up new avenues for the discovery and clinical detection of EV‐based biomarkers, enabling more specific and sensitive methods for diagnosis, patient stratification, and real‐time monitoring of cancer. Moreover, this methodology has considerable implications for fundamental and applied research on time‐resolved and spatial EV studies.
