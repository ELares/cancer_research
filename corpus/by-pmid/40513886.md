# Aptamer-based applications in delivering cancer gene therapies and beyond: state of the art and the missing links to clinical translation.

## Abstract

The possibility of correcting genetic and epigenetic alterations through gene therapies has been considered a cornerstone in oncology. However, modest results have been achieved in clinics, mainly due to inefficient tumor targeting and side effects. Nucleic acid aptamers are three-dimensional folded single-stranded DNAs or RNAs that selectively bind receptors on cellular membranes, being subsequently internalized via receptor-mediated endocytosis. Thanks to this capability, internalizing aptamers have been investigated as targeting moieties to deliver gene therapies more efficiently and selectively in tumor cells. Promising preclinical results suggested that aptamers could represent the long-awaited step forward in cancer gene therapy. Nevertheless, no clinical trials of aptamer-based gene therapies have been carried out two decades after the first preclinical application, indicating the field could not be sufficiently mature for translatability. The review aims to update thestate of the art regarding aptamers' contribution to gene therapy delivery and to critically highlight the main shortcomings that could have hindered clinical evaluations. In addition, pioneering insights regarding the use of aptamers as co-factors in CRISPR/Cas9 technology or as direct epigenetic regulators are also summarized, revealing more extended applicability not limited to the delivery of cancer gene therapies.

Graphical Abstract

Introduction

Tumorigenesis and tumor progression are intricately linked to genetic mutations and epigenetic modifications, which cause loss-of-function of tumor suppressor genes and aberrant activation of oncogenes. Despite significant progress of precision medicine in targeting oncoproteins, available treatments typically become ineffective in most patients due to drug resistance, therefore supporting the urgency in developing novel effective therapies. In this scenario, gene therapy has changed the treatment paradigm by targeting tumor cells at the DNA or RNA level. Gene therapy is nowadays defined as a set of treatments working by inserting functional genes, correcting mutations through gene editing systems, such as CRISPR/Cas9, or regulating gene expression through small oligonucleotides, including small interfering RNAs (siRNAs), microRNAs (miRNAs or miRs), miR-sequestering oligonucleotides (anti-microRNAs or antimiRs), DNAzymes, and epigenetic modifiers. However, these nucleic acid-based therapeutics show higher molecular weight and polarity with respect to traditional small molecules, requiring a delivery system to overcome the barrier represented by cell membranes.

Viruses, naturally predisposed to transduce exogenous genomic material through cell infection, were the first investigated delivery systems for gene therapy and led to significant clinical results. The approval of Gendicine® in 2003 in China represents a historical milestone, being the first authorized cancer gene therapy worldwide. This viral-based gene therapy consists of a TP53-expressing recombinant human-type five adenovirus (rAd-p53) used to restore p53 function in head and neck squamous cell carcinoma. Nowadays, four additional viral-based therapies (Imlygic®, Rexin-G®, Oncorine®, and Adstiladring®) have been approved worldwide for cancer treatment. Nevertheless, viral vectors commonly show low transduction in tumor cells, thus limiting clinical efficacy. Moreover, severe immune responses to viral vectors have been observed, sadly in some cases associated with patients’ deaths, evoking safety concerns. Nanoparticles (NPs) can be a valid alternative to viruses, in particular after the successful use of lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) for the delivery of Spike mRNAs in COVID-19 vaccination. However, similarly to viral vectors, NPs are not intrinsically specific for tumor targeting and provide low gene therapy delivery in tumor cells. Hence, enhancing viral and NP-based delivery systems or developing innovative strategies are critical for achieving targeted and efficient gene therapies against cancer.

Integrating gene therapies with nucleic acid aptamers may address these unmet needs. Aptamers are small single-stranded DNAs or RNAs that fold into peculiar tertiary structures based on internal base pairing. Aptamers have been considered alternatives to antibodies since they can bind with high affinity and specificity to membrane oncoproteins, potentially acting as antagonists or agonists. Interestingly, aptamers can be internalized via receptor-mediated endocytosis following the tight binding to their specific target. Thanks to these capabilities, aptamers have been investigated as delivery agents in novel gene therapy systems, being considered particularly promising to overcome the current limitations of viruses and NPs. The first preclinical finding regarding the use of an aptamer to deliver a gene therapy dates back to 2006, when McNamara and colleagues exploited an anti-prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) aptamer, named A10, to deliver an anti-Polo-like kinase 1 (PLK1) siRNA into prostate cancer cells, paving the way for a new field of research, which has been developing for about two decades. However, despite encouraging findings discussed in previously published reviews, no mention of clinical trials testing aptamer-based gene therapies in cancer (ended, active, or ongoing) was found in clinical trial databases (The Journal of Gene Medicine Gene Therapy Clinical Trials Worldwide website – updated March 2023; clinicaltrials.gov; ICTRP Search Portal, European Union Clinical Trials Register), or in recent publications.

Herein, we present updates on the state of the art of aptamer-based applications in cancer gene therapies. First, we briefly introduce the historical milestones in aptamer research, starting from their first description and investigations as agonists or antagonists of membrane oncoproteins. We then extensively focus on their use as delivery agents in aptamer-based nanostructures, aptamer-functionalized NPs, and aptamer-functionalized viruses. In addition, we also report novel pioneering findings that have recently validated aptamers as direct epigenetic regulators and co-factors in CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing technology, thereby uncovering emerging applications beyond the delivery. Lastly, we identify key limits in the current state of the art, highlighting potential solutions that could promote the advancement of aptamer-based gene therapies in clinical trials.

Aptamers: SELEX methodology and first pharmacological applications

The discovery of aptamers dates to 1990, when C. Tuerk and L. Gold described the “Systematic Evolution of Ligands by EXponential Enrichment” (SELEX), an innovative in vitro methodology to identify high-affinity RNA ligands for ideally any target of interest. These RNA ligands, as well as those with a DNA backbone, were named “aptamers” later. Since the first description, numerous variants of the standard SELEX protocol have been developed. More details about this can be found in a recent article by Zhu C. and co-authors. In general, SELEX consists of these three main steps: 1) incubation of a high-complexity aptamer pool (approximately 1015 DNA or RNA aptamer sequences) with a target of interest, such as purified proteins, whole cells, or tissues; 2) partitioning of aptamers bound to the target from those unbound; 3) amplification of the collected bound aptamers through polymerase chain reaction (PCR) or reverse transcription (RT)-PCR, depending on their DNA or RNA-based backbone. SELEX can also include an additional step, called “Counter-Selection”, in which the aptamer pool, prior to the incubation with the specific target, is incubated with unrelated proteins/cells/tissues in order to exclude aptamers with non-specific binding. “Counter-selection” and “Selection” steps are reiterated for multiple rounds (typically 8–15), gradually increasing the stringency, modifying the experimental conditions, such as the ratio between the concentrations of the aptamers and the targets, the number of Counter-Selection steps, the time or the temperature of incubation. Therefore, at the end of SELEX, high-affinity aptamers are preferentially selected.

By such a protocol, aptamers against the extracellular domain of receptors and exerting an antagonistic action can be isolated. For example, Li and colleagues isolated the aptamer E07, which binds to both the human wild-type Epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) and the deleted mutant (EGFRvIII), competing with the endogenous ligand, blocking receptor autophosphorylation, and impeding the proliferation of EGFR-expressing cancer cells. Although fewer cases were reported, aptamers could also promote the activation of their target, providing an agonistic function. A dimeric RNA nanostructure constituted by two fused M12–23 aptamers belongs to this class. Thanks to the efficient binding of M12–23 to the murine tumor necrosis factor receptor superfamily member (4–1BB), a crucial costimulatory receptor for activated T cell survival and expansion, the aptamer-based nanostructure induced an anti-tumor immune response via CD8 + T cell activation, leading to tumor regression in mouse cancer models. The two aptamers described above are only two examples of antagonist or agonist aptamers since numerous others have been identified, preclinically characterized, and proposed as valuable alternatives to monoclonal antibodies (mAbs).

Although aptamers have been less investigated and developed than mAbs, they offer significant advantages for clinical applications, such as lack of immunogenicity, affordable chemical modifications, high stability to temperature and pH fluctuations, cost-effective production, and no batch-to-batch variations.

The first aptamer approved by the FDA for clinical application was Macugen (Pegaptanib) in 2004. It is a 2′-Fluoropyrimidine (2′F-Py) modified RNA that binds to the vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) and shows effectiveness for the treatment of neovascular age-related macular degeneration (AMD). Despite its efficacy, Macugen was discontinued for commercial reasons because of superior novel therapies. Nevertheless, this was only the first proof of aptamer clinical translatability. Recently, the FDA authorized a novel 2′F-Py modified RNA aptamer (Izervay) inhibiting the complement factor C5 for geographic atrophy (GA) or advanced dry AMD, and many others have been involved in clinical trials. In detail, Table 1 collects performed and ongoing clinical trials in which numerous therapeutic aptamers have been evaluated, including for antitumor indications, that could lead to the approval of novel aptamer-based therapeutics in the following years.

Aptamer-guided approaches for delivering cancer gene therapies

The boundary of potential applications was extended when aptamers’ internalization via receptor-mediated endocytosis was reported, suggesting their use as delivery agents. In this regard, an outstanding upgrade was provided by the design of “Cell-Internalizing SELEX”, a modified protocol to identify aptamers capable of selective internalization in cancer cells. Driven by these insights, numerous internalizing aptamers have been identified and used for the targeted delivery of oligonucleotide therapeutics (siRNAs, miRNAs, antimiRs, and DNAzymes), viral vectors, and NPs in pioneering cancer gene therapies (Fig. 1).

Aptamer-based nanostructures for siRNA delivery

SiRNAs are synthetic double-stranded RNAs (20–28 base pairs) that guide mRNA cleavage as part of the RNA interference (RNAi) machinery. Mechanistically, once in the cytoplasm, a siRNA is first assembled with numerous proteins to constitute a ribonucleoprotein complex, named RNA-induced silencing complex (RISC). Then, only one strand, defined as the guide strand, is selected because of higher thermodynamic stability and kept in the active RISC, where its nucleotide sequence is used to recognize a targeted mRNA based on base pairing, directing RISC components in mRNA cleavage. Hence, siRNAs can be theoretically designed to guide the silencing of any disease-related mRNA, showing great potential for therapeutic applications. Nevertheless, their high molecular weight and polarity do not allow passing cellular membranes and free access into the cytoplasm, strictly requiring their combination with cellular delivery systems. Nowadays, siRNA delivery has been successfully achieved in hepatocytes, leading to the approval of Patisiran®, Givosiran®, Inclisiran®, Lumasiran®, and Vutrisiran®, while translatability of siRNA-based therapies in cancer remains challenging due to low delivery efficiency. To overcome this issue, nucleic acid nanostructures constituted by siRNAs conjugated to internalizing aptamers (hereafter defined as aptamer/siRNA) were developed, an approach facilitated by the impressive progress made in nucleic acid manufacturing. Importantly, the pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of these aptamer-based nanostructures were easily adjusted by incorporating a plethora of modified nucleotides during chemical synthesis (e.g., 2′F-Py to enhance serum stability), enabling optimization for in vivo applications.

In this section, we review the main preclinical studies using aptamers for siRNA delivery in different cancer models, emphasizing the strategy to conjugate them in nucleic acid nanostructures and the experimental results (Table 2).

The first preclinical study reporting the suitability of aptamers for siRNA delivery dates back to 2006, when McNamara and colleagues used A10, an internalizing aptamer, for the selective delivery of an anti-PLK1 siRNA in PSMA-expressing prostate cancer cells. In detail, a 2′F-Py-modified RNA, including the sequence of A10 and the passenger strand of an anti-PLK1 siRNA (analogously modified with 2′F-Py), was annealed with the complementary anti-PLK1 siRNA guide strand (unmodified) (Fig. 2a). Using this approach, the authors developed a nucleic acid nanostructure constituted of two folded oligonucleotides (“two-blocks” strategy), which shaped both A10 and an anti-PLK1 siRNA (A10/anti-PLK1 siRNA). They reported that A10/anti-PLK1 siRNA was selectively internalized via receptor-mediated endocytosis in prostate cancer cells thanks to the aptamer moiety, which selectively recognized PSMA on the cell surface. Then, the anti-PLK1 siRNA moiety was processed by the RNAi machinery and recognized by RISC into the cytoplasm, guiding PLK1 mRNA degradation. In mouse models, the inhibition of the growth of PSMA-expressing xenograft tumors was observed, even if only when A10/anti-PLK1 siRNA was injected intra-tumorally, suggesting the necessity of optimizations to make this nucleic acid nanostructure suitable for clinical applications. In a subsequent publication, the same research group investigated different optimizations of A10/anti-PLK1 siRNA. First, the A10 aptamer, originally constituted of 71 nucleotides, was reduced to 39 nucleotides. This shorter aptamer, A10–3.2, preserved the binding to PSMA, allowing a cheaper and more affordable chemical synthesis. Modifications of the siRNA moiety were also pursued based on studies reporting that changes in the traditional siRNA configuration could promote more efficient recognition and processing by the RNAi machinery, increasing siRNA potency. Hence, different anti-PLK1 siRNAs were designed, conjugated to A10–3.2, and tested in vitro. In particular, the authors evaluated the benefits of using siRNA configurations containing a −U-U- overhang at the passenger 3’end (named OVH), a wobble base pair thanks to a mismatch mutation between the siRNA strands (wobble), an inversion between the passenger and guide strands (swap), or an engineering stem-loop structure in which siRNA stem was continuous to the aptamer loop (loop). Once the swap configuration was identified as one of the more active in vitro, Dassie et al. evaluated the in vivo efficacy of A10–3.2/anti-PLK1 siRNA (swap) further modified with a 20 kDa polyethylene glycol (PEG) tail at the 5’end of the passenger strand (pegylation). Pegylation was introduced to delay blood clearance, increasing the nanostructure’s half-life from less than 30 min to more than 30 h. The daily intraperitoneal injection of pegylated A10–3.2/anti-PLK1 siRNA (swap) improved the overall survival of PSMA-expressing tumor-bearing mice, demonstrating that a remarkable improvement in pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics could be achieved by optimizing the constituting moieties. A strategy successfully pursued a few years later by an independent group, which introduced OVH or swap siRNA configurations in anti-STAT-3 siRNAs, and delivered them as part of a “two-blocks” nanostructure in association with an anti-BAFF aptamer in B-cell malignancy cellular models.

PSMA and BAFF are specifically expressed in prostate and B-cell malignancies, respectively, limiting aptamer-mediated delivery of siRNAs to these cancers. The epithelial cell adhesion molecule (EpCAM) was investigated as an alternative target for siRNA delivery since it is upregulated in stem cells and different epithelial cancers, such as breast, colorectal, prostate, and pancreatic cancers, and it is internalized upon ligand binding. Wang et al. conjugated an anti-EpCAM aptamer to a siRNA able to repress the expression of survivin (EpCAM aptamer/anti-survivin siRNA), which was overexpressed and involved in doxorubicin resistance in a subline established from MCF-7 breast cancer (BC) cells after long-term drug exposure (MCF-7/Adr). Unlike progenitor MCF-7, this subline contained an enriched subpopulation of EpCAM-positive cells, which displayed relevant multi-potent differentiation and self-renewal capacities, properties typically associated with stem cells. EpCAM aptamer/anti-survivin siRNA (swap) was obtained with the “two-blocks” strategy by annealing the passenger strand with the guide (both modified with 2’F-Py), which was previously fused with the EpCAM aptamer sequence. Experiments in MCF-7/Adr cells demonstrated an efficient aptamer-mediated delivery of the anti-survivin siRNA, survivin repression, and restoration of doxorubicin sensitivity. However, the authors highlighted the absence of in vivo effects in xenograft models after intravenous injection. In agreement with the findings reported by Dassie and colleagues, a short tumor accumulation was observed, which lasted less than 4 h, suggesting the need to introduce pegylation to increase serum retention. Repeated intravenous administrations of the pegylated EpCAM aptamer/anti-survivin siRNA in combination with doxorubicin significantly improved the overall survival of MCF-7/Adr tumor-bearing mice, corroborating the efficient delivery of the anti-survivin siRNA mediated by the pegylated aptamer-based nanostructure. Interestingly, Gilboa-Geffen and colleagues, in the same year, reported successful siRNA delivery through the same anti-EpCAM aptamer in a “two-block” nanostructure without pegylation. Despite using the same anti-EpCAM aptamer, numerous differences were present between the two aptamer/siRNA nanostructures. Gilboa-Geffen et al. conjugated a different siRNA to the anti-EpCAM aptamer, which was anti-PLK1 instead of the anti-survivin. In addition, despite a swap configuration used by both authors, Gilboa-Geffen et al. also introduced dTdT overhangs at the 3’ ends of both siRNA strands to further ameliorate the in vivo stability. Moreover, they administered the non-pegylated aptamer-based nanostructure subcutaneously rather than intravenously and tested it in different BC models.

Although the “two-blocks” strategy was mainly pursued in the wake of initial promising findings, a further conjugation strategy, named “three-blocks”, was also developed and successfully applied in the context of siRNA delivery. As an example, the 2’F-Py-modified RNA sequence of an anti-platelet-derived growth factor receptor (PDGFR)β aptamer (named Gint4.T) and the passenger strand of an anti-STAT3 siRNA were extended with complementary and antiparallel sequences at 3’-ends (also called “sticky sequences”), used to anneal them by base pairing (Fig. 2b). The aptamer/siRNA conjugate (Gint4.T-STAT3) was completed with the anti-STAT3 siRNA guide strand (analogously modified with 2’F-Py), which was annealed to the passenger. Thus, a nucleic acid nanostructure formed by three oligonucleotides (“three-blocks”) was developed, shaping both Gint4.T and an anti-STAT3 siRNA. Once internalized thanks to the specific binding of Gint4.T to PDGFRβ, Gint4.T-STAT3 was successfully processed by the RNAi machinery, leading to specific and efficient repression of STAT3 expression in different PDGFRβ-expressing glioblastoma (GBM) cells. Notably, the impairment of the tumor growth in a GBM xenograft model upon intraperitoneal administration was also assessed, even without pegylation. Further, promising effects using the same aptamer-based nanostructure were also observed in patient-derived glioma-stem-like cells (GSCs), demonstrating the suppression of GSC phenotype and propagation in vitro. Moreover, the efficacy of Gint4.T-STAT3 has been recently reported in a different context besides GBM. It has been shown that Gint4.T-STAT3 effectively delivered STAT3 siRNA in PDGFRβ-expressing non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cells and cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), which exert a pivotal role in the NSCLC microenvironment for promoting tumor progression. In particular, Gint4.T-STAT3 altered the CAF phenotype and rendered epithelial cells irresponsive to CAF-pro-tumoral functions.

Even if aptamers show relevant advantages with respect to mAbs, these latter present two fragment-antigen-binding regions (Fab) per mAb, which allow the simultaneous recognition of two antigens and mAb avidity, properties not pursuable using the aptamer-based nanostructures described so far. To achieve mAb-like bivalence, Yan Liu et al. designed an innovative mAb mimicking aptamer/siRNA nanostructure (globally named PSEP) constituted by two A10–3.2 aptamers on either side of a survivin siRNA and an EGFR siRNA. The nanostructure was obtained through the annealing of three 2′F-Py modified RNA sequences, the first composed by the fused sequences of the A10–3.2 aptamer and the guide strand of the survivin siRNA; the second formed by the A10–3.2 and the passenger strands of both EGFR and survivin siRNAs in-series; the last with the guide strand of the EGFR siRNA. The aptamer and siRNA sequences were separated through an −A-A, while the two siRNAs were separated through a U-U-U-U bridge. The two spacers were introduced to distance the functional moieties and to confer flexibility to the structure. Notably, the authors demonstrated that the presence of two anti-PSMA aptamers per PSEP structure was more efficiently internalized and provided higher cytotoxicity in vitro compared to a control conjugate (PSEM), which contained only one A10–3.2 aptamer and a non-functional second aptamer. These in vitro findings suggest that bivalent conjugates could be more effective than monovalents. The efficacy of PSEP was also confirmed in prostate cancer xenograft models, where its daily intraperitoneal injections successfully delivered siRNA moieties and reduced the tumor burden. In line with these findings, further bivalent aptamer-based nanostructures for siRNA delivery in BC were reported, one based on two anti-HER2 aptamers (HEH) and another based on two different aptameric moieties, an anti-HER2 and anti-HER3 aptamer (H2EH3), confirming the relevance of this approach. The repeated intravenous injections of HEH or intraperitoneal injections of H2EH3 successfully delivered anti-EGFR siRNAs and significantly impaired tumor growth in vivo. Nevertheless, it is important to underline that no comparative studies between bivalent aptamer-based nanostructures and those with a single aptamer were performed in animal models, preventing any statement regarding their pharmacological superiority and supporting the necessity of additional in vivo studies.

Even if less investigated, likely due to the higher cost of chemical synthesis, aptamers were also validated for the targeted delivery of short hairpin RNAs (shRNAs), longer precursors of siRNA, demonstrating the variety of aptamer-integrated nanostructures that can be efficiently developed in the context of cancer gene therapies.

Aptamer-based nanostructures for miRNA and antimiR delivery

Different from siRNAs, miRNAs are endogenous RNAs that are naturally recruited within RISC. They originate from longer harpin-bearing RNAs as a result of processing catalyzed by nuclear and cytosolic RNases (Drosha and Dicer), which produce mature miRNA duplexes, each constituted by a guide and a passenger strand. As previously described, once in RISC, the passenger strands are removed, and the guide strands are used to recognize targeted mRNAs, leading to their translational inhibition or decay.

Downregulation of onco-suppressor miRNAs and upregulation of oncogenic miRNAs have been commonly observed in cancer, contributing to establishing canonical disease hallmarks. For this reason, developing treatments based on miRNA mimics or antimiRs has been considered promising in cancer therapy. However, as indicated above, they show analogous limitations in terms of delivery efficiency in tumor cells, supporting their integration in aptamer-based nanostructures.

This paragraph describes the key studies regarding aptamer-based nanostructures for delivering miRNA mimics and antimiRs in cancer models (Table 3).

Our group first explored the possibility of using aptamers to deliver miRNA mimics (miRNAs modified with 2′F-Py or 2′-fluoro-uridines). We used the GL21.T aptamer, specifically internalized into NSCLC cells once bound to its target, the tyrosine kinase receptor Axl. GL21.T was conjugated to a mimic of let-7 g miRNA, which was reported to impair NSCLC development in mouse models. By using an approach similar to that described by McNamara and colleagues, the sequences of GL21.T and the let-7 g passenger strand were fused in a single 2′F-Py modified RNA, which was then annealed with the let-7 g guide strand (analogously modified with 2′F-Py), generating a “two-blocks” nanostructure (GL21.T-let7g). A specific internalization of GL21.T-let7g in Axl-expressing NSCLC cells was first observed, corroborating the specific and efficient delivery promoted by the aptamer moiety. The cellular uptake of GL21.T-let7g was associated with the functional processing of the delivered let7g miRNA mimic, repressing its targeted mRNAs and promoting anti-tumor effects. Promising results were also observed in vivo since the non-pegylated GL21.T-let7g significantly reduced the tumor growth after repeated intravenous injections in xenograft mouse models. In accordance with these findings, Iaboni et al. developed an analogous GL21.T-based “two-blocks” nanostructure to restore the expression of a further miRNA, miR-212, implicated in the resistance to TNF–related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL)–mediated cell death. The authors demonstrated that GL21.T selectively delivered the miR-212 mimic in Axl-expressing NSCLC cells, inducing the re-sensitization to TRAIL treatment.

In addition to the “two-blocks” strategy, our group also pursued the “three-blocks” strategy (Fig. 2b) as a different conjugation method between GL21.T and let-7 g. The “three-blocks” nanostructure still recognized Axl and was efficient in the delivery of the let-7 g miRNA mimic in vitro, indicating that the sticky extension did not negatively affect the function of the aptamer and miRNA moieties. The main advantage of this strategy was the possibility of synthesizing a unique sticky-extended GL21.T aptamer, which could be easily conjugated to different anti-cancer miRNA mimics through the simple annealing of the two moieties. Preclinical data showed that this approach could be successfully applied for the delivery of numerous miRNA mimics, such as miR-16, miR-34c, and miR-137, in NSCLC cellular models. Despite a prevalent characterization in NSCLC, GL21.T was also successfully evaluated for miRNA delivery in other Axl-expressing cancers, such as BC and melanoma. The repeated intravenous injection of a “three-blocks” nanostructure consisting of GL21.T and a mimic of miR-148b reduced the formation of circulating tumor cells and, consequently, the number of metastases related to the growth of BC or melanoma in mice.

Besides GL21.T, other aptamers were conjugated to miRNA mimics through the described strategies and validated as suitable delivery agents in cancer models. Especially notable is the anti-KIT DNA aptamer, efficiently used to deliver a 2′-fluoro-uridines-modified mimic of miR-26a through a “two-blocks” nanostructure. In addition, TEG cholesterol was added at the 3’-end of the passenger strand since it was reported as a helpful modification to delay blood clearance and, consequently, improve the pharmacological activity. The authors first observed that the restoration of miR-26a in KIT-expressing BC tumors promoted a synergistic effect with fluorouracil (5-FU) or Carboplatin, increasing the suppression of tumor growth. Interestingly, the delivery of the miR-26a mimic was also observed in KIT-expressing hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs), even providing protection towards the toxicity commonly observed after administering 5-FU and Carboplatin. These findings are particularly significant since they support the idea that aptamer specificity and the multifaceted miRNA roles in different tissues could be simultaneously exploited to improve therapeutic outcomes. The anti-nucleolin AS1411 aptamer, previously validated as an internalizing 2’F-Py modified RNA aptamer, was similarly evaluated in the context of aptamer-based nanostructures for miRNA delivery in vitro. The primary relevance of these studies is that the authors developed a variant of the “two-blocks” nanostructure, performing a post-synthesis conjugation of the aptamer and the passenger moiety. In particular, they conjugated the AS1411 aptamer with a mimic of let-7d miRNA via a heterobifunctional crosslinker that contained NHS-ester and maleimide reactive groups at the opposite ends of a PEG spacer (SM(PEG)2) (Fig. 2c). This variant of the “two-blocks” strategy (post-synthesis “two-blocks”) could represent a valid alternative when the synthesis of long aptamer and passenger fused sequences cannot be pursued due to obvious problems related to yields and costs. Nevertheless, additional steps of purification are needed with this strategy. The authors also compared the nanostructure developed with the novel post-synthesis “two-blocks” strategy with respect to another obtained with the “three-block”. The nanostructure obtained with the SM(PEG)2 crosslinker showed higher serum stability, promoted increased intracellular let-7d levels, and a more potent antiproliferative effect in MKN-45 gastric cancer cells. Even if reported data are not sufficient to conclude the superiority of SM(PEG)2-based “two-blocks” nanostructures with respect to “three-blocks”, the study indicates that an optimization of the conjugation strategy, as well of both aptamer and miRNA chemistry, could remarkably contribute to their clinical translatability, and more efforts should be pursued to define the strategies to develop the best-in-class aptamer-based nanostructures.

AntimiRs, unlike miRNA mimics, require being delivered as single-stranded RNAs. Indeed, their mechanism of action provides for the annealing with miRNA guide strands, impeding these latter from recruiting targeted mRNAs in RISC complexes. Thus, verifying the absence of folding interference between the aptamer and antimiR sequences, even if partial, is crucial when aptamer-based nanostructures are designed for antimiR delivery. Following the “three-blocks” strategy, a nanostructure containing the GL21.T aptamer and a 2’F-Py modified antimiR-222 was developed (GL21.T/antimiR-222) and tested in GBM models. Preclinical data showed that GL21.T/antimiR-222 efficiently promoted miR-222 targeting in Axl-expressing U87MG cells and tumor-bearing mice, where the intravenous injection impaired tumor growth. Interestingly, since tumors commonly display simultaneous upregulation of different oncogenic miRNAs, GL21.T was similarly conjugated (“three-blocks” strategy) with two in-series 2’F-Py modified antimiRs, antimiR-222 and antimiR-10b, and the pharmacological activity was explored in vitro. Data confirmed that the two antimiRs, separated from each other by a 3-carbon chain linker (C3) spacer, internalized in Axl-expressing cells thanks to the endocytosis of GL21.T and successfully inhibited targeted oncogenic miRNAs. This insight supported the idea that a single aptamer could even deliver two antimiRs and allow the co-targeting of different oncogenic miRNAs. Despite the success, the above strategy could not always be pursued since folding interference between aptamers and antimiR sequences could be present, especially when two antimiRs are delivered. To overcome this limitation, a further study demonstrated the possibility of using two aptamer- based nanostructures in tandem to deliver different oligonucleotide therapeutics by targeting different membrane receptors. In particular, given the overexpression of both Axl and PDGFRβ in GSCs, GL21.T and Gint.4.T-based nanostructures were used to deliver a miR-137 mimic and an antimiR-10b (Gint4.T/miR-137 and GL21.T/antimiR-10b) simultaneously. The co-treatment with Gint4.T/miR-137 and GL21.T/antimiR-10b promoted specific miR-137 and antimiR-10b internalization and a synergistic effect on GSC expansion, suggesting that targeting two membrane receptors with two independent internalizing aptamers could be pursued in the context of personalized medicine.

The advancements in the nanotechnology field led to the development of more complex nanostructures that could be analogously efficient for gene therapy delivery, such as the three-way junction (3WJ) motif derived from the bacteriophage phi29. Shu et al. applied the 3-WJ motif as a scaffold to construct a self-assembling nanostructure containing multiple elements: an anti-EGFR aptamer as a delivery agent, an antimiR-21, and an Alexa-647 molecule. After repeated intravenous injections, this all-in-one platform promoted the delivery of the antimir-21 moiety and tumor regression in orthotopic triple-negative BC (TNBC) models. Although the development of this nanostructure is more complicated with respect to those previously described, and the higher molecular weight could reduce tumor accessibility, this latter could provide better tumor retention and potentially be used for multi-targeting and diagnostic purposes, paving the way for desired theranostic applications.

Aptamer-based nanostructures for DNAzyme delivery

DNAzymes are synthetic ssDNAs (with a length of 40–100 nt) equipped with a catalytic activity, typically selected from fully randomized libraries. They represent an evolution of naturally occurring ribozymes, ssRNAs that fold into tertiary structures and catalyze chemical reactions. One of the most studied ribozyme-mediated catalysis is the RNA cleavage, typical of the hammerhead and hairpin ribozymes, which were considered promising for potential therapeutic purposes. Different from ribonucleases, which display a non-specific mechanism of RNA cleavage, ribozymes only target specific RNAs. Indeed, unfolded anti-sense sequences, which commonly flank the central catalytic core of the ribozymes, only allow the recognition of complementary RNAs, positioning the cleavage site adjacent to the ribozyme catalytic core and promoting its cutting. Although efficiency in gene repression at the post-transcriptional level was observed in preclinical studies, no hammerhead or hairpin-like ribozymes were approved in clinics due to the failure of clinical endpoints. The reason could be found in their pharmacokinetics since ribozymes are more unstable to nucleases compared to siRNAs, which have been successfully translated to the clinics. Indeed, the siRNA backbone can be easily modified to improve serum stability, for example with 2′F-Py or 2′OMe-Pu modifications, without affecting their recognition in RNAi machinery. On the contrary, the introduction of modifications in ribozymes can severely inhibit their cleavage activity. DNAzymes have promised to overcome these ribozymes’ limitations since they comprise a more nuclease-resistant DNA backbone. Nevertheless, their specific and efficient delivery to cancer cells still represents a primary issue for clinical proposals. Considering this limitation, aptamers have also been recently explored as delivery agents of DNAzymes.

For example, the anti-HER2 DNA aptamer (42 nt) was fused to that of a DNAzyme (41 nt), able to repress the expression of GLUT-1. The obtained DNA sequence (83 nt) could be efficiently assembled in a metal-nucleic acid framework (MNF), named H-GDz/Ca, with an uniform morphology thanks to coordinated bonds promoted by bivalent calcium cations, a crucial functional element for the catalytic activity of the DNAzyme. The authors reported that more extended DNA sequences promoted the MNF assembly. The anti-HER2 aptamer in the MNF increased the uptake of H-GDz/Ca in HER2-expressing gastric cells, inducing the DNAzyme internalization and silencing of GLUT1. Noteworthy, results indicated that the aptamer-loaded MNF was also more internalized with respect to a traditional aptamer-DNAzyme nanostructure (H-GDz + Ca2+), which was not previously assembled in Ca2+-coordinated MNF. This is arguably consistent with the higher avidity expected for a MNF functionalized with numerous aptamer moieties for each nanostructure. However, the control MNF (MH-GDz/Ca), functionalized with a mutated version of the anti-HER2 aptamer, was highly internalized, even higher than those provided by H-GDz + Ca2+, suggesting relevant non-specific uptake of the MNF and indicating that more specificity could be achieved with traditional aptamer-based nanostructures. Another MRF, also named Metal-DNA nanocomplexes, was developed using the anti-nucleolin AS1411 aptamer, which was fused with a DNAzyme repressing the ATG5 mRNA, a crucial player of protective cellular autophagy. The complex was constituted in the presence of Mn2+, a cofactor of the anti ATG5 DNAzyme catalytic core and an inducer of hydroxyl radicals when internalized. This MRF is a promising example of multitargeting since the aptamer-mediated internalization in nucleolin-expressing cells led to intracellular Mn2+ accumulation, which led to cell damage prompted by hydroxyl radicals, an approach also named Chemo-dynamic therapy (CDT). At the same time, the DNAzyme repressed the expression of ATG5, preventing the activation of autophagy, which is used by tumor cells to repair damage. The authors showed that the AS1411-loaded MRFs (DACs-Mn) were selectively internalized in high nucleolin-expressing cells. Weaker in vitro internalization was instead observed in low-expressing nucleolin cells and when MRFs were assembled in the absence of AS1411 (DCs-Mn). The higher internalization of DACs-Mn promoted the rise of more late apoptotic cells with respect to DCs-Mn and ACs-Mn, which only contained AS1411 without the DNAzyme. In vitro data were then confirmed in vivo, where tumor growth was impaired by the intravenous injection of DACs-Mn compared to ACs-Mn. Nevertheless, a study limitation should be highlighted since no characterization of control MRFs, DCs-Mn, and ACs-Mn was reported. Based on findings from Yan et al. previously described, shorter DNA sequences could affect MRF assembly, suggesting that a deep characterization of control MRFs should be required.

Overall, reported data suggest that aptamer-loaded MRFs could be promising for delivering higher amounts of DNAzymes in targeted tumor cells and achieving multitargeting with respect to traditional aptamer-based nanostructures. However, being a more recent strategy, further preclinical studies are required to corroborate their feasibility and potential translatability.

Aptamer-coated viral vectors

As described in the introduction, viral vectors have been the primary strategy proposed for gene therapy. However, viral infection strictly depends on receptor-dependent tropism, a property that determines which cells can be successfully infected by viruses. This represents one of the most limiting factors of viral-based gene therapy in cancer, as tumor cells could not express sufficient receptors to promote efficient infections.

For example, the coxsackie and adenovirus receptor (CAR), crucial in the adenoviral tropism toward hepatocytes, cardiomyocytes, myoblasts, epithelial and endothelial cells, displays an oncosuppressor role in different cancers and can be downregulated in malignant cells, such as in glioma. This supports the necessity of engineering adenoviruses to achieve a CAR-independent infection and an improvement in gene therapy efficiency. Thanks to their capacity to bind membrane cancer biomarkers, aptamers have been investigated as an emerging approach to modify natural viral tropisms artificially. Chen H. and colleagues functionalized a genetically modified Ad5, the most used adenoviral-vector serotype in humans, with the anti-nucleolin AS1411 or the anti-tenascin-C GBI-10 aptamers. In detail, they introduced the biotin acceptor peptide (BAP) into the hexon, the main representative protein of the adenoviral capsid, which was consequently biotinylated during the assembly of viral particles in HEK293 host cells (Fig. 3a). Then, they coated the viral surface using streptavidin as a bridge, which allowed the binding between the biotinylated AS1411 or GBI-10 aptamers with the viral biotin-bearing surface. The transduction efficiency of the aptamer-coated viruses was then tested by monitoring the expression and function of a luciferase transgene, observing a 4–5 fold increase with respect to the control viral particles in a cell line naturally not infected by the Ad5 serotype.

Beyond Ad5, other commonly used types of viruses are the adeno-associated (AAV), a class of non-pathological viruses characterized by a broad tropism toward different cells thanks to their recognition of glycans, glycoconjugates, or sialic acid. However, despite the broad tropism in tissues such as the liver, retina, central nervous system, kidneys, pancreas, skeletal muscles, and lungs, no cancer uses of AAVs have been achieved so far due to low cell infection. To improve their infection capabilities, AAV2 viral vectors, naturally able to recognize heparin sulfate proteoglycan (HSPG), were first coated with DNA-based dendrimers, branched nanopolymers with a well-defined structure, which were used as an anchorage for aptamer coating (Fig. 3b). The dendrimers were covalently attached to the lysine residues of the viral capsid using a dithiobis (succinimidyl propionate) (DSP) cross-linker that allowed the formation of −S-S bonds. Then, the dendrimers were hybridized with anti-PTK7 DNA aptamers (sgc8) via a sticky-based approach since both aptamers and dendrimers were extended with complementary sequences. In this way, viral particles were functionalized with dendrimers that harbored four sgc8 aptamers each, generating multivalent aptamer-coated AAV2 vectors. The authors demonstrated that the sgc8 functionalized viruses efficiently infected HSPG-negative but PTK7-positive CEM cells, as shown by the exogenous expression of the green fluorescent protein (GFP) reporter transgene.

The two discussed in vitro findings demonstrated the possibility of extending the tropism of viruses towards cancer cells using aptamer-based tools. However, it’s crucial to note that the aptamer-functionalized Ad5 and AAV2 maintained their natural tropism, which would still allow the infection of such healthy tissues in vivo. This is a significant limit associated with safety concerns of viral-based therapeutics, a challenge that the field should address to ensure the safety and efficacy of these treatments. Puzzo and colleagues recently attempted a strategy to reduce the viral infection of healthy tissues and increase that of cancer cells. They developed an AAV strain deprived of the HSPG binding sites (AAV-DJR/A), partially impairing the natural tropism of the progenitor virus. Then, AAV-DJR/A viruses were functionalized with AS1411 aptamers using an innovative approach (Fig. 3c). In detail, further engineered AAV-DJR/A viruses (Nε-AAV) were produced using host cells capable of introducing unnatural amino acids, the azido-lysine amino acids, into the translated capsid proteins, which could be conjugated with dibenzocyclooctyne (DBCO) harboring AS1411 aptamers via a click chemistry reaction (AS1411-functionalized Nε-AAV). The infection capacity of AS1411 functionalized Nε-AAV was evaluated by detecting luciferase-based luminescence induced by viral infection. AS1411-functionalized Nε-AAV resulted in an enhanced luciferase signal in nucleolin-expressing cells (MCF-7, A549, and HELA) compared to Nε-AAV, indicating a higher infection capability promoted by AS1411 functionalization in vitro. Nevertheless, subcutaneous injection of AS1411-functionalized Nε-AAVs revealed unexpected in vivo results since lower bioluminescence in HELA subcutaneous tumors was observed with respect to Nε-AAV. Since a high luciferase signal was also observed in the liver of mice injected with Nε-AAV, which was instead undetectable in those that received AS1411-functionalized Nε-AAVs, the authors advanced the hypothesis that non-specific infection of Nε-AAV could still be relevant in the achieved results. More in-depth future studies regarding the origin of luciferase-related luminescence in Nε-AAV infected mice could confirm this hypothesis and corroborate the relevant contribution of the AS1411 aptamer in the specific infection of nucleolin-expressing tumor cells.

Overall, these findings indicate that viral vectors can be functionalized with aptamers through different approaches, but their evaluation is still preliminary since biological validations were only performed using reporter genes, such as luciferase or GFP, and not extensively investigated in vivo. Novel investigations are mandatory to unveil whether this new approach can deliver sufficient transgenes and therapeutic effects in cancer cells without infection in healthy tissues. Nevertheless, these promising preclinical studies support the concept that aptamer functionalization could help to achieve the goal of highly specific viruses for cancer gene therapy.

Aptamer-functionalized NPs

NPs are promising alternatives to viral vectors because of their better safety profiles, the more straightforward large-scale production, and the broad possibility of customization. In addition, NPs can be designed to allow controlled-release formulations and can be loaded with multiple therapeutics. However, these non-viral carriers, initially developed for the safe delivery of traditional chemotherapies, have shown low delivery efficiency in cancer, as demonstrated by the modest clinical results obtained in oncology. These results mainly depend on the lack of specific targeting, which is not intrinsically endowed in the standard nanomaterials used to assemble NPs. For this reason, preclinical studies have attempted the optimization of NPs through functionalization with targeting ligands, including aptamers, to promote cell-specific and efficient uptake. Since the literature on this topic is extensive and has grown remarkably in the recent decade, we are going to focus on the preclinical findings in which NPs, already approved in clinics or in advanced preclinical development stages, were functionalized with the aptamer that reached the most advanced stage in cancer clinical trials, the anti-nucleolin aptamer AS1411. Therefore, examples of AS1411-functionalized liposomes, metallic-based, and polyplexes are provided in the present paragraph.

Among clinically approved NPs in cancer therapy, about 60 % are lipid-based. It is not surprising that evaluations of potential benefits associated with aptamer-based functionalization were extensively made in this context. Yu et al. developed cationic liposomes using Dioleoylphosphatidylethanolamine (DOPE), Sphingomyelin (SM), didecyldimethylammonium bromide (DDAB), 1,2-distearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-[(polyethyleneglycol)-2000] DSPE-PEG2000, and DSPE-PEG2000 maleimide (DSPE-PEG2000-MAL). The maleimide group was introduced to allow the functionalization with AS1411. The authors loaded the hydrophobic layer of the liposomes with Paclitaxel (PTX) and promoted the absorption of anti-PLK1 siRNAs (siPLK1) onto the external liposomal surface (Fig. 4a). The AS1411 functionalized Lipo-PTX-siPLK1 (AS1411/Lipo-PTX-siPLK1) showed higher delivery of PTX and siPLK1 to MCF-7 BC cells with respect to unfunctionalized liposomes, providing a synergistic effect on cell proliferation. In vitro data were corroborated in mouse models where AS1411/Lipo-PTX-siPLK1 impaired tumor growth and extended the survival of tumor-bearing mice, demonstrating that aptamer-mediated delivery could significantly improve liposome performance. Promising results were also reported in the context of metallic-based nanoparticles, used in about 6 % of approved NP-based pharmaceuticals. An interesting example is the gold(Au)-based nanoparticles described by Deng et al., simultaneously functionalized through the constitution of −S-S bonds with AS1411 and antimir-221 (Fig. 4b). The authors also functionalized the Au surfaces with nuclear localization signal (NLS) peptides since they reported that most miR-221 and nucleolin resided and functioned in the nucleus. The functionalization with AS1411 significantly improved the effect of NPs, providing a synergistic inhibitory effect on the nucleolin/miR-221/NFκB/DNMT1 signaling both in vitro and in vivo. Besides liposomes and Au-based, although not similarly developed for clinical applications, polyplexes were also functionalized with aptamers. Aliabadi and colleagues have recently developed a copolymer-based polyplex constituted of Poly (lactic acid)121-b-Poly(N-3-aminopropyl)methacrylamide)103 (PLA121-b-PAPMA103) (Fig. 4c). Thanks to its polycation nature, this polyplex self-assembled in a spherical micelle that absorbed AS1411 aptamers and plasmids encoding anti-survivin shRNAs. The study demonstrated that AS1411-based functionalization improved the in vivo effect of the polyplexes, reducing tumor masses and increasing the survival rate in mouse models. This article is interesting not only for developing an innovative nanomaterial and its functionalization with AS1411 but also for attempting to deliver a plasmid, an option not reported for aptamer-based nanostructures.

In general, these exemplary studies underline the benefit of aptamer functionalization in improving the delivery performance of NPs in tumors independently from the different nanomaterials used to assemble them. These promising data are not limited to AS1411 since several other aptamers have been investigated for similar purposes, demonstrating the high flexibility of the system.

In addition to the above studies regarding aptamer functionalized NPs for the delivery of oligotherapeutics or plasmids, innovative findings should be mentioned regarding CRISPR/Cas9 delivery, the most powerful gene editing technique available nowadays. Although it seems promising for cancer applications, concerns about off-target genetic alterations in healthy tissues are holding its use in vivo, and the development of specific and customized delivery systems is considered crucial for clinical advancement. Delivery of CRISPR/Cas9 machinery is not easy due to the high molecular weight (> 160 KDa) and large size (hydrodynamic diameter of 7.5 nm) of its components. Indeed, to achieve the functionality of CRISPR/Cas9, it is necessary to deliver the macromolecular complex constituted by a single guide RNA (sgRNA) and the Cas9 nuclease or a DNA plasmid encoding for them. With this aim, a single adenoviral vector for the expression of the entire CRISPR/Cas9 machinery was developed, but the previously described limits associated with viral vectors require further improvements before clinical proposals. Aptamer coating of this viral vector could be pursued, but to our knowledge, no references are reported in the literature. Moreover, using aptamer-based nanostructures, as previously detailed, does not seem a pursuable direction due to the large size of the CRISPR/Cas9 complex. In this scenario, exploiting NPs and their aptamer-functionalized upgrade could represent an ideal tool. Liu and colleagues developed a protein-based NP system, representing 38 % of already approved NPs in clinics (Fig. 4d). In detail, they complexed a CDK11 silencing CRISPR/Cas9 expressing plasmid with protamine, a small polycationic natural peptide, constituting the core of the polymeric NP. Then, the central core was coated with AS1411-incorporated Carboxymethyl chitosan (CMC), which was absorbed thanks to its negative charges via electrostatic interactions. Lastly, the NP was completed with KALA, a positive-charged peptide. KALA and AS1411 were introduced to improve the cellular internalization of the gene delivery system. In line with this in silico design, KALA and AS1411 remarkably increased the internalization in MCF-7 BC cells. As expected by higher internalization of the plasmid, CDK11 expression was silenced due to CRISPR/Cas9 function. Although no in vivo validation was reported, this study highlighted preliminary information about the feasibility of aptamer-functionalized NPs for selective delivery of CRISPR/Cas9 in cancer cells. In vivo results were more recently reported with a different AS1411-functionalized NP. In this case, Khademi et al. incorporated a plasmid encoding the entire CRISPR/Cas9 machinery to impair the expression of FOXM1 into a chitosan-based core (CS) (Fig. 4e). The core was then coated with hyaluronic acid (HA) and AS1411 (Apt) for targeted delivery. Apt-HA-CS-CRISPR/Cas9 NPs successfully allowed the expression of CRISPR/Cas9 machinery into nucleolin-expressing cells (MCF-7, SK-MES-1, HeLa) but not into those that were negative (HEK293). In vivo, Apt-HA-CS-CRISPR/Cas9 promoted efficient tumor inhibition without observable distribution in other organs. In general, these studies’ collection, in addition to further preclinical findings not only limited to the AS1411 aptamer, suggest that aptamer functionalization of NPs could be decisive in achieving selective delivery of CRISPR/Cas9 technology.

Beyond delivery: Aptamers as CRISPR/Cas9 regulators and epigenetic modifiers

Although the most investigated application of aptamers in gene therapy is in the context of delivery, recent findings suggest that aptamers could have interesting roles in intracellular compartments, thus opening new avenues in the field. In the context of CRISPR/Cas9, aptamers have been recently used as allosteric sites to modulate the gene editing machinery. Kundert and colleagues demonstrated that the introduction of the anti-theophylline RNA aptamer in various positions of a sgRNA could induce relevant conformational changes because of theophylline binding, enabling the assembly or disassembly of CRISPR/Cas9 machinery on DNA sequences and, consequently, modulation of its function. These ligand-activated or ligand-deactivated sgRNAs were named ligRNAs, which permitted rapid and reversible CRISPR/Cas9 regulation depending on theophylline concentration in Escherichia coli. However, despite being an innovative approach, ligRNAs were not active in mammalian cells, underlining the necessity of optimizations. Yan Liu and colleagues exploited the crystal structure of the Cas9/sgRNA complex, identifying novel positions for the introduction of the anti-theophylline aptamer. They observed that the introduction of the aptamer as a switchable unit in the presence of theophylline could regulate the formation of the ternary complexes constituted of a sgRNA, which harbored the guiding RNA sequence for the ASCL1 gene, Cas9, and the targeted DNA in electrophoretic shift assays, supporting validation in living cells. A CRISPR-mediated gene activation (CRISPRa) assay was then performed in HEK293 cells, which stably expressed Cas9-VP64-p65-Rta (VPR). The authors observed that the activation of the ASCL1 gene could be positively regulated in HEK293 cells that were transfected with the plasmid encoding the aptamer-bearing sgRNA and treated with theophylline, demonstrating that the novel engineered aptamer-based system could modulate CRISPR/Cas9 machinery in a human cellular context. Noteworthy, since the introduction of the aptameric allosteric site was rationally included in positions unrelated to the guiding RNA sequence, at least theoretically, these sgRNAs could be modified to recognize other genes of interest besides ASCL1.

Besides their potential involvement as additional constituents and regulators of CRISPR/Cas9 machinery, aptamers can also play a role in epigenetics. Since epigenetic aberrations, including DNA methylation and histone acetylation, have been commonly found in cancer, developing molecules able to restore normal epigenetic profiles in tumor cells have attracted interest. Wang et al. first reported the selection of DNA aptamers able to target the DNA maintenance methyltransferase DNMT1. The isolated anti-DNMT1 aptamer (Apt. #9) used at the sub-micromolar scale could compete with the hemi-methylated DNA duplex, the original substrate of DNMT1, promoting an effective enzymatic inhibition. Apt. #9 displayed selectivity for DNMT1, since no binding activity toward DNMT3A and DNMT3B was observed. Intriguingly, the authors reported a significant internalization of Apt. #9 by simple incubation with DNMT1 high-expressing HeLa cells, also showing colocalization with DNMT1 inside the nuclei. This phenomenon also determined a more than 40 % reduction in DNA methylation when Apt. #9 was incubated with cells at 3 μM final concentration. However, the effect provided by Apt. #9 only caused a faint reduction of HeLa cell viability (about 20 %). Additional validations in different cancer cells could reveal if the modest result was related to the tumor model or the selected aptamer. Indeed, Apt. #9 showed fast degradation in the culture medium (11 h) that, in association with the affinity in the high nM range (770 nM), could suggest a temporary modification of the cellular epigenetic status and a faint biological effect. A further step in using aptamers as a modifier of the DNA methylation status was recently made. Starting from a class of RNAs with an inherent capability of inhibiting DNMT1 (DNMT1-interacting RNAs, also shortly named DiRs), our group applied a novel doped SELEX approach to produce 2′F-Py modified RNA aptamers that maintained the capacity of neutralizing DNMT1 but showed higher affinity and serum stability, both crucial for potential therapeutic applications. The procedure led to the identification of an aptameric DiRs (aptaDiRs), Ce-49 sh that showed nM affinity for DNMT1 (< 100 nM), no significant interaction with DNMT3A and DNMT3B, and longer stability in human serum (half-life longer than 48 h). Regarding functionality, Ce-49 sh exhibited efficient deregulation of DNA methylation in K562 leukemic cells and promising inhibition of tumor growth in vivo. Targeting epigenetics with aptamers is not limited to DNA methylation, since DNA aptamers were also used to neutralize Histone acetyltransferase 1 (HAT1). Indeed, a novel DNA aptamer (apHAT610) that efficiently targeted HAT1 in lung cancer cells has been recently reported by Klett-Mingo and colleagues. Therefore, although aptamer-mediated targeting of epigenetic modifications has been only evaluated preliminarily, further studies could reveal potentiality for cancer therapies.

The missing links to clinical translation

The state of the art reveals that different aptamer-based systems for gene therapy delivery were developed in preclinical studies. Among them, aptamer-based nanostructures and aptamer-functionalized NPs have been the most investigated and are in more advanced stages of development. In contrast, only a few preliminary studies have been reported for aptamer-functionalized viruses. This could be arguably linked to concerns associated with viral vectors. Indeed, despite modifications of viral tropism achieved with aptamer functionalization in preclinical studies, limits regarding the immune response to viral antigens remain a critical issue in terms of safety. Generally, numerous independent studies consistently state that aptamers represent valid delivery agents as part of nucleic acid nanostructures or functionalized NPs, promoting more efficient and selective delivery of gene therapies. However, despite promising insights, the absence of clinical trials indicates that the field is not sufficiently advanced for translatability. We critically highlighted relevant limitations in the current state of the art and provided possible solutions to boost the clinical evaluation of aptamer-based gene therapies (Table 4).

The first limit is associated with the cancer models typically used, since most preclinical studies have been only performed in cancer cell lines. These cell lines are preliminarily used as models characterized by the high expression of aptamer-recognized biomarkers. However, these cellular models could not accurately represent the high heterogeneity of tumors in patients. Therefore, as significant advances have been made in terms of preclinical models in the last years, especially in having access to patient cells to develop patient-derived xenografts (PDX), consistent efforts should be spent in assessing aptamer-based gene therapies in more advanced and reliable tumor models. This could strengthen the findings that have been reported so far. Nevertheless, as previously discussed, multiple aptamers have already been selected, and many others could be identifiable through SELEX, potentially providing an ideal aptamer-based delivery agent for treating each patient and meeting personalized medicine expectations.

Second, although it is widely recognized that aptamers are mainly internalized through receptor-mediated endocytosis, it is still unclear which mechanisms allow the partial release of aptamer-based gene therapies in the cytoplasm of targeted tumor cells. The step of releasing from endosomes is particularly relevant since findings suggest that only a low percentage (about 1 %) of endocytotic therapies could reach the cytoplasm, representing a significant issue for the efficiency of such an approach. While targeting highly expressed membrane oncoproteins may enhance the internalization of gene therapies, their efficiency is often limited by restricted cytoplasmic access from endosomes. Since aptamer-based gene therapies rely on this route of entry, further efforts are needed to deepen our understanding of endosomal escape mechanisms and to develop strategies that improve their efficiency.

The high structural heterogeneity of the developed aptamer-based gene therapies paradoxically contributes to the challenging scale-up toward clinical evaluations. Numerous research groups have developed and characterized functional aptamer-based nanostructures, which differ for the aptamer, the therapeutic oligonucleotide, the conjugation strategy, the spacing between the two moieties, and the chemical modifications. The authors rarely describe the process that supported the design of the presented nanostructure, and no negative results before the definition of the final nanostructure are commonly discussed. An analogous statement can be reported for aptamer-functionalized NPs, developed using several nanomaterials, loaded with different gene therapeutics, and functionalized with different aptamers applying various methods. Since no retrospective comparison between aptamer-based gene therapies in different publications can be consistently made, no rules for developing best-case aptamer-based delivery systems have been defined. Since their efficacy depends on multiple variables, including stability in biological fluids, biodistribution, elimination, aptamer binding, expression level of recognized membrane oncoproteins, rate of internalization, percentage of escape from the endosomes, and pharmacological effect once in the cytosol or nucleus, it seems evident that each modification could remarkably impact the indicated processes. For this reason, performing comparative studies is crucial. For example, a therapeutic siRNA could be delivered using the same aptamer in the context of an aptamer-based nanostructure or a functionalized NP. However, despite using the same carrier aptamer, the two delivery systems are expected to be remarkably different in pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics. The lack of comparisons between them challenges the role of pharmaceutical companies, which move in this highly heterogeneous context, hindering their investments in development stages and validation in more advanced preclinical models. More collaborations between academic groups should be pursued to facilitate this step forward, allowing the creation of multidisciplinary teams assembled in consortia to perform these crucial comparative experiments. Given the high costs associated with these studies in academic settings, the involvement of private stakeholders could be pivotal. Increased investments from pharmaceutical companies in the early development stages are essential. Additionally, promoting the dissemination of negative results could be equally valuable, as it would help prevent the repetition of ineffective strategies already explored by other research groups. In this context, we support the recent Editorial by Owen Sansom and colleagues, highlighting the importance of sharing negative results in the scientific literature.

Lastly, aptamers are not the only molecular targeting agents reported in the literature. Indeed, mAbs, nanobodies, nanofitins, and small molecules have similarly been or could be exploited in molecular targeting-guided gene therapies. However, no comparative studies have been conducted between aptamer-based delivery systems and the same systems using different molecular targeting agents. Hence, the potential advantages and disadvantages of using one over the others, in terms of development and production feasibility, as well as pharmacodynamics, pharmacokinetics, and eventual side effects, have not been elucidated so far. Therefore, these studies should be mandatory to identify delivery systems that would benefit most from integration with aptamers, thus promoting their successful scale-up in clinical validations.

Conclusion remarks and perspectives

Although gene therapy has emerged as an outstanding perspective direction in cancer therapy, its clinical application remains limited due to inefficient delivery in tumor cells and relevant concerns about side effects. The discovery that aptamers can internalize into targeted tumor cells via receptor-mediated endocytosis has paved the way for their use as delivery agents in novel aptamer-based gene therapies to improve their efficiency and selectivity. In general, aptamers exhibit numerous advantageous properties compared to other molecular targeting agents (mAbs, nanobodies, nanofitins, and small molecules), making them ideal for this purpose. In particular, the application of SELEX theoretically allows the identification of a high-affinity aptamer for each target of interest, an approach not pursuable with small molecules. Moreover, they offer a good compromise between a small size (commonly ranging from 5 to 20 kDa), which is significantly lower than that of a mAb (about 150 kDa), and an extensive surface to promote strong and cumulative binding to the target. In addition, with respect to similar-sized protein-based molecular targeting agents, such as nanobodies and nanofitins, they are produced through chemical synthesis, which is not subject to batch-to-batch variation, a significant advantage in terms of large-scale production. Aptamers can also be synthesized with chemically modified bases, allowing a more affordable conjugation to traditional gene therapy systems. Lastly, they are not immunogenic, a property unfortunately shared by all the mentioned protein-based molecular targeting agents. In this review, we have deeply summarized their use as part of gene therapy delivery systems, including aptamer-based nanostructures, aptamer-functionalized NPs, and aptamer-functionalized viruses, outlining the main achievements in preclinical studies and key limitations still hindering their progress in clinical evaluations. Regarding these latter, we have proposed a panel of potential study optimizations that could guide future studies and facilitate their advancement in clinics. Overall, further efforts are needed to complete the preclinical validation of aptamer-based gene therapies; however, current advancements strongly support continued research, potentially opening new avenues for more efficient and selective delivery of cancer gene therapies. Moreover, even if the primary application of aptamers in gene therapy currently lies in an optimized delivery, their roles as modulators of CRISPR/Cas9 gene-editing technologies and as epigenetic modifiers are also noteworthy. These emerging areas hold promise for aptamers to become innovative tools in the coming decade, offering potential benefits for CRISPR/Cas9 clinical applications and providing specificity and safety advantages over conventional epigenetic therapies.
