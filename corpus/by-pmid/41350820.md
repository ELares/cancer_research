# Functional analysis and verification of GYG2 in oncolytic virus-infected glioma.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Oncolytic virus has emerged as a promising candidate against tumors by infecting and killing cancer cells. In addition to poor prognosis of patients with glioma, the exact mechanisms driving the oncolytic virus therapy in glioma remain poorly understood.

**METHODS**: We employed Enterovirus A71 (EV-A71)-based dataset (GSE136330), vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV)-M51-based dataset (GSE166914) and TCGA-glioma cohort to identify potential candidate gene after oncolytic virus infection. Then we investigated the correlation between glycogenin-2 (GYG2) and crucial outcomes such as clinical features, overall survival, tumor microenvironment. GO enrichment and GSEA analysis were performed to identify GYG2-coexpressed genes and potential regulatory pathways in glioma. The expression level of GYG2 in glioma tissues and cells was evaluated by qRT-PCR. Loss- and gain-of-function and rescue studies were used to explore the functional effect of GYG2 on glioma cell growth and apoptosis in VSV-M51-infected cells.

**RESULTS**: Bioinformatics analysis show that GYG2 is downregulated after VSV-M51 or EV-A71 infection in glioma T98 cells. High expression of GYG2 in glioma is associated with high WHO grade and poor prognosis. In addition, GYG2 expression is linked with immune cell infiltration and immune microenvironment in glioma. GYG2 and its co-expressed protein are involved in regulating integrin 1 pathway and aurora B pathway in glioma. Downregulating GYG2 inhibits glioma cell growth and facilitates tumor cell apoptosis. Importantly, VSV-M51 infection promotes glioma cell apoptosis by downregulating GYG2.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Overall, this study advances our knowledge on the mechanisms of oncolytic virus infection and highlights a novel anti-apoptotic effect of GYG2 in glioma, further supporting it as an oncolytic virus target for therapy.

Introduction

Glioma represents a major global health challenge, significantly impacting patient survival. This is mainly due to tumor metastasis and the challenges posed by the blood-brain barrier, which hinder effective treatment and contribute to high mortality rates. While advancements in surgical resection and chemotherapy, including the use of carboplatin and cisplatin, have improved outcomes for glioma patients, the persistence of postoperative recurrence and drug resistance continues to negatively affect long-term survival. Therefore, identifying alternative therapeutic strategies that can target specific genes in glioma may offer valuable insights for advancing clinical treatment options.

Oncolytic virus therapy is an innovative anticancer approach that utilizes genetically engineered viruses to specifically target and eliminate cancer cells, minimizing harm to healthy tissues. The virus primarily destroys infected cells through the induction of apoptosis in tumor cells. Effective oncolytic virus therapy depends not only on the infectious ability of oncolytic virus but also on its ability to replicate. Oncorine (H101) is the first commercial oncolytic virus has received China approval for the treatment of advanced nasopharyngeal carcinoma in 2005. Enterovirus A71 (EV-A71), a member of the Enterovirus genus in the Picornaviridae family, preferentially infects and kills glioma cells. Researches have highlighted that intravenous administration by using oncolytic virus EV-A71 nanocapsules is an effective method for treating malignant glioma. In addition, vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) is a negate-stranded RNA virus, classified as the vesiculoviruses group of Rhabdoviridae family. Accumulating evidence suggests that VSV become a promising candidate drug towards lung cancer, liver cancer, breast cancer and glioma. Among various VSV variants, VSV-M51, which lacks residue 51 in its matrix protein, has demonstrated unique characteristics. VSV-M51 has reduced cytotoxicity against normal cells and the ability to shorten the time of tumor infection. Nonetheless, the complexity of the mechanisms through which EV-A71 and VSV-M51 induce glioma cell death remains unclear and requires further study.

Here, we explore the oncolytic viruses-related datasets by combining GEO datasets and TCGA-glioma cohort. We first leverage RNA-sequencing data to identify oncogene and oncolytic virus-targeting gene of glioma tumorigenesis. We aimed to evaluate the regulatory mechanism of oncolytic virus treatment in glioma. Additionally, we investigated the role of oncolytic virus-targeting oncogene glycogenin-2 (GYG2) in the progression of glioma. Taken together, we present evidence of GYG2’s implication in glioma biogenesis and its involvement in oncolytic virus treatment.

Materials and methods

Data acquisition and processing

The research involved RNA-seq analysis of EV-A71-infected glioma cells downloaded from the GEO database (GSE136330). Additionally, RNA-seq analysis of VSV-M51-infected glioma T98 cells, comprising 3 VSV-M51-infected group and 3 DMSO-treated group, was downloaded from the GEO database (GSE166914). The gene expression data for glioma from the TCGA database was downloaded from the UCSC Xena platform. We utilized the clusterProfiler package in R to conduct gene set enrichment analysis on KEGG pathways and GO gene sets.

Immune cell infiltration

The expression of the components in glioma immune microenvironment was scored through the ssGSEA and CIBERSORT algorithms. Gene expression data for GYG2 were input into the gene module within the TIMER database to evaluate the correlation between GYG2 expression, tumor purity levels, and immune cell infiltration proportions.

Clinical glioma tissues and glioma cell culture

Human glioma tissue (n = 20) and normal brain tissues (n = 30) were obtained from the Second Hospital of Shanxi Medical University in Taiyuan, Shanxi, between 2018 and 2022. The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of the Second Hospital of Shanxi Medical University, with all patients providing written informed consent. No patients had undergone chemotherapy or radiotherapy before surgery. The study utilized two glioma cell lines: U87MG and U251, both obtained from ATCC. Cell line authentication was performed for U87MG via STR analysis (Procell system), which confirmed a match with the corresponding ATCC STR profile. Mycoplasma contamination screening was conducted at regular intervals using a Real-time PCR detection kit (Applied Biosystems, USA). U87MG cells were maintained in MEM medium (Gibco, USA) containing 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (Gibco). U251 cells were cultured in DMEM medium (Gibco, USA) with 10% FBS added. All cell cultures were maintained without antibiotic supplementation at 37 °C in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2. For VSV-M51 infection experiments, cells were infected at a multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.5. We treat VSV-M51 with ultraviolet light to for 6 h to mimic non-oncolytic virus. The UV-treated non-oncolytic virus was used as control for cellular experimets.

Plasmids, ShRNA and transfection

The overexpression of GYG2 was accomplished through the use of the pCMV-GYG2 plasmid, which encodes the full-length GYG2 gene. The knockout of GYG2 was achieved by the synthesis of shRNA that targets GYG2. The sequence of shRNAs as follows: shGYG2#1: 5’-GGT GCT GTC CAA TGT CGA TGA-3’, shGYG2#2: 5’-GCC TTC TCT CCA CAC GCA TAA-3’. The plasmids coding GYG2, vectors, shRNAs or shNC were synthesize by Sangon Biotech (Shanghai, China). The plasmids or shRNA were transfected into glioma cells using Lipofectamine 3000 reagent (Thermo Fisher, USA) for 48 h followed by VSV-M51 infection or further experiments.

Quantitative real-time PCR

Total RNAs were extracted using Triol reagents (Thermo Fisher) and reversed transcript into cDNA using a PrimeScript™ RT reagent Kit (TaKara, Beijing, China). qRT-PCR was performed using TB Green® Premix (TaKara) on a quantitative Real-time PCR system (Applied Biosystems, USA). The relative gene expression was normalized to GAPDH and calculated using the 2-∆∆CT method. The sequences of qPCR primers are listed as follows: GYG2: forward 5’-GAA ACC ATC TTG CAG CCA GC-3’, reverse 5’-GCT TTT GCC GAC AGC TTC AA-3’, GAPDH: 5’-CCG CAT CTT CTT TTG CGT CG-3’, reverse 5’-GAG GGA GAG AAC AGT GAG CG-3’.

Cell counting kit-8 (CCK8) assay

The evaluation of cell growth employed the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK8) assay kit (Beyotime, Beijing, China), with measurements taken on a Microplate Reader (Thermo Fisher, USA). A total of 2,000 cells were plated on a 96-well plate and incubated for 12, 24, 48 h. At each time point, 15 µL of CCK-8 reagent was added to the culture medium and incubated with the cells for 4 h. The absorbance at 450 nm was measured to assess cell proliferation.

Flow cytometry

Using a Annexin V-FITC/PI Apoptosis Detection Kit (GloBio, USA), Annexin V-FITC/PI staining by flow cytometry illustrated the glioma cell apoptosis ratio after silencing GYG2 or VSV-M51 infection. Briefly, 5 × 10⁴ cells were seeded into each well of a six-well plate and incubated for 48 h. Subsequently, glioma cell lines were transfected with either shRNAs or ov-GYG2 plasmids and exposed to VSV-M51 infection. Following this, 200 µL of the binding buffer was combined with 5 µL of Annexin V-FITC and 10 µL of propidium iodide reagent. After a 15-minute incubation period in the dark at room temperature, the cell samples were processed using a MoFloAstrios EQ flow cytometer (Beckman, USA). Apoptotic data were further evaluated using FlowJo v10 software. Annexin V-FITC(-)/PI(-) (Q4) represent viable cells, Annexin V-FITC(-)/PI(+) (Q1) represent cell debris or mechanical damage, Annexin V-FITC(+)/PI(-) (Q3) represent early apoptosis cells, Annexin V-FITC(+)/PI(+) (Q2) represent late apoptosis cells.

Caspase-3 activity assay

Caspase-3 activity in glioma cells was assessed by using Caspase-3 Activity Assay Kit (#5723, Cell Signaling Technology). Firstly, glioma cells were plated in 96-well plate and incubate with plasmids or/and VSV-M51 for appropriate time. We spun the plate for 10 min and removed the medium. After rinsing with cold PBS, each well was lysed with 30 µl cell lysis buffer on ice for 5 min. Secondly, we mixed the 200 µl of substrate solution B (containing Ac-DEVD-AMC) and 25 µl lysate solution in a black plate. The plate was incubated at 37 °C in the dark for 1 h. The fluorescence unit (RFU) was measured with an excitation wavelength of 380 nm and emission wavelength at 460 nm.

Statistical analysis

The collection and analysis of data were conducted utilizing GraphPad Prism 8 software. All experiments were repeated in triplicate, and the data were presented as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Student’s t-test was used for comparing two groups. To determine statistical significance between multiple groups, a one-way ANOVA was conducted, followed by Tukey’s multiple comparison test. Statistical significance was defined as p < 0.05, indicated by asterisks or number signs in the figures.

Results

Identification of oncolytic virus-related genes in glioma

Considering the effective function of oncolytic virus against malignant brain tumors, we hypothesized that oncolytic virus infection inhibits oncogene expression in glioma. To explore this, we initially search on the GEO datasets and aim to find some expression profiling data related to oncolytic virus infection. We chose two datasets, GSE166914 and GSE136330, which respectively contain the sequencing data after glioma infection with VSV-M51 and EV-A71. Since we predict that oncolytic virus infection inhibits the oncogenes that are originally highly expressed in glioma tissues/cells, we firstly screened out the genes that are lowly expressed after oncolytic virus infection. As shown in Fig. 1A, the median-centered normalization of six GSM datasets demonstrates that the data are normalized, ensuring cross-comparability and suitability for differential expression analysis. Between the control cells and the VSV-M51-treated cells, we identified a substantial alteration in RNA expression. Figure 1B indicated that 1409 genes were downregulated in the VSV-M51-treated cells when compared to the control group, while 1755 genes showed upregulation (p < 0.05). Besides GSE166914 datasets, we also analyzed the GSE136330 dataset to screen out differential expression genes. As shown in previous research, we identified 441 significantly upregulated genes and 304 downregulated genes in the EV71 group compared to the control group. After overlapping the up-regulated genes from the TCGA-GBMLGG cohort and the down-regulated genes from oncolytic virus-related datasets, Fig. 1C show the three candidate genes: IDH1, ABCD3, and GYG2. Among these three candidates, the role of IDH1 in glioma has been extensively studied and documented in numerous studies. As for ABCD3, its function in glioma has been previously investigated and revealed by our research group. In contrast, the functional role of GYG2 in glioma remains largely unexplored. Therefore, we selected GYG2 as the primary focus for this study.

High expression of GYG2 in glioma is associated with poor prognosis

In TCGA-GBM samples (N = 163) and TCGA-LGG samples (N = 518), which are mostly glioma tissues, we found that GYG2 expression were 1.7 ~ fold and 1.5 ~ fold changes respectively, compared with TCGA normal and GTEx data (N = 207) (p < 0.05, Fig. 2A). To examine the relationship between GYG2 expression and various clinicopathologic factors, we analyzed expression levels in glioma patients with higher versus lower stages of glioma. Analysis of TCGA-glioma cohort revealed a significant correlation between GYG2 expression and advanced glioma, particularly in relation to higher WHO grade and histological type (p < 0.001, Fig. 2B and 2C). Glioma samples exhibited less GYG2 expression in mutant IDH status, which was indicative of a relatively good prognosis (p < 0.001, Fig. 2D). The evaluation of the relationship between GYG2 expression and various clinicopathological characteristics demonstrated significant associations between the GYG2high phenotype and WHO grade (p = 5.376E-05), IDH status (p = 7.6483E-18), and histological type (p = 0.0003197), as shown in Table 1. The Kaplan-Meier analysis demonstrated that elevated GYG2 expression led to a notably worse prognosis, including overall survival, disease-specific survival, and progression-free interval (p < 0.001, Fig. 2E-G). The ROC curve analysis indicated the AUC values of 0.660, 0.675, and 0.646 for the 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year periods, respectively (Fig. 2H). We conducted a univariate Cox proportional hazards analysis to evaluate the overall survival of glioma patients based on GYG2 expression in the TCGA-glioma cohort, which identified GYG2 as an independent prognostic factor in glioma patients (Fig. 2I).

GO enrichment and GSEA analysis of GYG2-coexpressed genes in glioma

To further understand the possible role of GYG2 in glioma, we conducted a screening analysis of GYG2 co-expression genes in TCGA-GBMLGG cohort. The main screening conditions were spearman’s correlation |R| >0.5 and p < 0.05. The results showed that a total of 51 genes were positively correlated with the expression of GYG2 in glioma. Additionally, the co-expression heat map was plotted to show the changing trends of the expression levels of top 25 genes along with GYG2 expression in glioma (Fig. 3A). Figure 3B-3G demonstrated the medium correlation of GYG2 expression to GFAP (R = 0.610), FOXJ1 (R = 0.579), ANKRD53 (R = 0.576), PLCD3 (R = 0.572), DLEC1 (R = 0.574) and PIRT (R = 572) (all p < 0.001). We utilized bioinformatics methods, specifically GO enrichment analysis and Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA), to analyze the GYG2-coexpressed genes in TCGA-glioma cohort for mechanistic insights. The results of the enrichment analysis revealed a significant difference in the differential expression of GYG2, impacting both metabolic pathway and Hippo pathway (Fig. 4A). We also observed over-expression of the Integrin 1 pathway (NES = 1.903, p = 0.015) and Aurora B pathway-associated genes (NES = 1.864, p = 0.034) in the glioma cohort (Fig. 4B and 4C).

GYG2 expression is linked with immune cell infiltration and immune microenvironment in glioma

We subsequently utilized the TIMER database to investigate the relationship between GYG2 expression and the infiltration levels of B cells, CD8 + T cells, CD4 + T cells, macrophages, neutrophils, and dendritic cells. As illustrated in Fig. 5A-B, elevated GYG2 expression demonstrated a strong positive correlation with increased infiltration of CD4 + T cells and neutrophils in glioblastoma multiforme (GBM), as well as CD4 + T cells, macrophages, neutrophils, and dendritic cells in low-grade gliomas (LGG), with statistical significance (P < 0.001). In addition, the tumor purity panel showed that GYG2 is highly expressed in the LGG microenvironment and is highly expressed in GBM cells. To better predict the immune infiltration, we also utilized ssGSEA algorithm to analyze TCGA-GBMLGG RNA-seq data. Using ssGSEA algorithm, GYG2 expression levels were correlated with eosinophils (R = 0.374, p < 0.001), macrophages (R = 0.328, p < 0.001), activated DCs (aDCs) (R = 0.273, p < 0.001) and neutrophils (R = 0.225, p < 0.001) (Fig. 6A). In order to further study the type proportions of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes in glioma, we utilized CIBERSORT algorithm for further analysis. The stacked column graph indicated that the level of M0 macrophages, M2 macrophages, dendritic cells and eosinophiles are all increasing in GYG2-high expressed glioma group (Fig. 6B). Figure 6C, 6D and 6E showed that GYG2 expression linked strongly with eosinophiles, macrophages and aDCs (p < 0.001).

Down-regulating GYG2 has antitumor effect in vitro

Using immunohistochemical (IHC) analysis of clinical glioma samples from the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) data, we observed that GYG2 expression was significantly higher in glioma tissues compared to normal cerebral cortex tissues (Fig. 7A). To investigate the relationship between GYG2 and glioma cellular function, we firstly collected 30 glioma clinical tissues and detected GYG2 mRNA expression levels. Figure 7B indicated that GYG2 expression was increased in glioma samples when comparing with normal brain tissues (p < 0.001). To explore the cellular function of GYG2 on glioma cells, we first assess the expression of GYG2 and conducted RT-qPCR analysis on U87MG and U251 cell lines, as well as normal human astrocytes (NHA). The findings revealed notably higher levels of GYG2 in both glioma cell lines, particularly in U87MG cells (Fig. 7C, p < 0.001). To further detect the functional role of GYG2 in glioma in vitro, we knocked down GYG2 in U87MG and U251 cell lines (Fig. 7D, p < 0.01, p < 0.001). In U87MG and U251 glioma cell lines, the suppression of GYG2 expression led to a significant decrease in cellular proliferation, with the inhibitory impact becoming more enhanced over time (Fig. 7E and 7F, p < 0.001). Additionally, flow cytometry confirmed that inhibiting GYG2 expression increased the apoptotic ratio of glioma cells, along with the increase of caspase-3 activity (Fig. 7G and 7H, p < 0.01, p < 0.001). Together, these findings suggest that downregulating GYG2 not only inhibiting glioma cell growth but also plays a crucial role in facilitates tumor cell apoptosis.

VSV-M51 infection promotes glioma cell apoptosis by downregulating GYG2

Considering the downregulation of VSV-M51 infection on GYG2 expression predicted by analysis of GSE166914 dataset, we hypothesized that VSV-M51 infection may also play a role in regulating GYG2 expression and glioma cell apoptosis process. Figure 8A-8B show that VSV-M51 infection significantly decreased the proliferation of U87MG and U251 cells (p < 0.001). Interestingly, the glioma cells transfected with GYG2-overexpressed plasmids and then treated with VSV-M51 had enhanced cell proliferative abilities, compared with VSV-M51-treated group (Fig. 8A and 8B, p < 0.05). As anticipated, VSV-M51 infection significantly enhanced glioma cell apoptosis and caspase-3 activity (Fig. 8C-8E, p < 0.001). Exogenous upregulation of GYG2 rescued the enhancement of cell apoptosis and caspase-3 activity in VSV-M51-infected glioma cells (Fig. 8C-8E, p < 0.01). These results suggested that VSV-M51 infection inhibited glioma cell growth by downregulating GYG2 expression.

Discussion

Our study demonstrates that GYG2 represents both a promising therapeutic target and a valuable prognostic biomarker for patients with glioma. Our findings not only demonstrated a correlation between GYG2 expression and oncolytic virus infection in glioma, but also highlighted its role in the immune status of the tumor microenvironment and tumor progression. Importantly, we assessed the potential role of targeting GYG2 in vitro, and our findings suggest that inhibiting GYG2 or VSV-M51 treatment can promote glioma cell apoptosis. This approach not only elucidates a underlying mechanism of VSV-M51 treatment in glioma but also offers a promising alternative strategy for oncolytic virotherapy.

GYG2 (also known as GN-2), a member of the glycogenin family, has some link with mitochondrial function, aging and glycogen biosynthesis. For instance, GYG2 has been shown to express in liver tissues and be crucial for the glycogen synthesis of skeletal muscle and myocardium. Nevertheless, emerging research indicates that GYG2 may function as an oncogene under certain contexts. In gastrointestinal cancer, Wu et al. employed a RiskScore model and a verification result from nomogram to demonstrate that GYG2 expression is an accurate prediction model of gastrointestinal cancer prognosis. In comparison to normal gastric epithelial cells, the expression levels of GYG2 mRNA were differential in the gastric cancer cells. Moreover, GYG2 was significantly enriched in tumor-related and immune-related pathways, suggesting that GYG2 may serve as a tumor or immune biomarker and potential therapeutic target. Wang et al. also reported that GYG2, as a glucose metabolism-related gene, can be used to predict the 3-year and 5-year survival rates of patients with clear cell renal cell carcinoma, with AUCs of 0.808 and 0.819, respectively. These results indicated that GYG2 may play a multifaceted role in the biology of cancer, highlighting the need for further investigation in glioma.

In recent years, oncolytic virus EV-A71 have gained considerable interest for its role in causing hand, foot and mouth disease; however, the tumor-suppressive role of EV-A71 in cancer treatment is not yet fully understood. Among the various unexpected effects of EV-A71 infection, one of the most notable is its capacity to induce pyroptosis in human neuroblastoma cells by activating caspase-1 and triggering pro-inflammatory secretion. An earlier study has shown that EV-A71 plays a central role in promoting the lysis of caspase-3 protein and leading to cell apoptosis, thereby exerting oncolytic properties in the treatment of colorectal cancer. Our recent studies have shown that oncolytic virus EV-A71-based biomarkers, KAT8 and ABCD3, participated in clinical prognosis and tumor immune infiltration of glioma. This study not only analyzed the EV-A71-related GSE166914 dataset, but also employed VSV-M51-realted GSE16630 dataset. We performed RNA-sequencing analysis including EV-A71-infected glioma cell samples, VSV-M51-infected glioma cell samples and uninfected samples. And then we take the intersection of the two datasets with the TCGA-glioma cohort in order to screen for potential oncolytic virus-related genes. Our results identified GYG2 as a target gene after oncolytic viruses’ infection. Here, we demonstrated that GYG2 was a biomarker for identifying glioma because it was expressed in high WHO grade glioma tissues, its detection was highly specific in glioblastoma. Moreover, GYG2 is a potential oncogene in glioma and its overexpression predicts an unfavorable prognosis in patients with glioma.

Alterations in the M protein, specifically mutations or deletions at the amino acid residue position 51, disrupt the VSV’s ability to suppress host cell gene expression while retaining functionality necessary for viral assembly. Compared with the wild type of VSV, VSV-M51 has stronger oncolytic activity against human glioblastoma cells. Felt et al. demonstrated that VSV-M51 has the potential to become an oncolytic agent and may be used as a sensitizer for chemotherapy in pancreatic cancer. Earlier studies utilizing in vitro and in vivo assays focused on tumor growth and cancer cell apoptosis, indicating that the infection of VSV-M51 resulted in cell apoptosis and effective tumor control with a defect of respond to type I IFN in endometrial cancer and melanomas. In an attempt to assess the function of VSV-M51 infection on multiple glioma cells, we found that VSV-M51 treatment had a significant ability to affect glioma cell proliferation and apoptosis in U87MG and U251 cell lines. Of note, evidences obtained from rescue experiments pointed towards VSV-M51 infection inhibited cell growth via downregulating GYG2 expression in glioma.

This study has some limitations. The potential functional role of GYG2 in glioma immune infiltration need to be explored in future studies. Additionally, while VSV-M51 infection showed efficacy in in vitro cell models, the tumor suppressive effect of VSV-M51 in glioma require further evaluation. These limitations, however, do not diminish the main findings but highlight directions for future research.

In summary, the present work is the first reporting the prognostic value of oncolytic virus-related gene GYG2 in patients with glioma. Moreover, this study is the first to demonstrate a functional role for the tumor inhibitory effect of GYG2 in glioma, proposing that GYG2 actively inhibits glioma cell proliferation and promotes cell apoptotic ability. Its potential as a therapeutic target for VSV-M51 infection offers exciting possibilities for advancing glioma treatment. These findings broadened the understanding of glioma cell apoptosis mechanisms and identifying potential targets for oncolytic virus therapy.
