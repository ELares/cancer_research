# Polymeric STING Pro-agonists for Tumor-Specific Sonodynamic Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

The efficacy of combination immunotherapy has been limited by tumor specificity and immune-related adverse events (irAEs). Herein, we report the development of polymeric STING pro-agonists (PSPA), whose sono-immunotherapeutic efficacy is activated by sono-irradiation and elevated glutathione (GSH) within the tumor microenvironment (TME). PSPA is composed of sonosensitizers (semiconducting polymer) and STING agonists (MSA-2) via the GSH-activatable linkers. Under sono-irradiation, PSPA serves as a sonosensitizer to generate 1 O2 and induce immunogenic cell death (ICD) of malignant tumor cells. Furthermore, MSA-2 is released specifically in tumor microenvironment with highly expressed GSH, minimizing off-target side effects. The activation of the STING pathway elevates the interferon-β level and synergizes with SDT to enhance the anti-tumor response. Therefore, this work proposes a universal approach for spatiotemporal regulation of cancer sono-immunotherapy.

Introduction 
Sonodynamic therapy (SDT) is a non-invasive therapeutic 
method that utilizes sonosensitizers to generate reactive oxygen 
species (ROS), cavitation, gas bubbles, and hyperthermia for 
cancer treatment.[1] Furthermore, SDT has made significant 
progress in overcoming limitations on the tissue penetration depth 
compared to photothermal therapy (PTT) and photodynamic 
therapy (PDT).[2] The most commonly used sonosensitizers are 
developed from traditional photosensitizers including organic 
sonosensitizers (e.g. porphyrin derivatives, methylene blue, 
indocyanine 
green, 
acridine 
orange), 
and 
inorganic 
sonosensitizers (e.g. titanium dioxide, black phosphorus).[3] The 
effectiveness of SDT is limited due to several factors such as the 
lower efficiency in generating ROS of current sonosensitizers, 
instability under sono-irradiation, and potential toxicity.[4] 
Therefore, there is a high demand to develop a variety of 
sonosensitizers that exhibit high stability and sufficient ROS 
production to achieve significant SDT therapeutic efficacy. 
Cancer immunotherapy harnesses natural immune system 
to trigger an antitumor response against malignancies.[5] Despite 
its potential benefits, it is crucial to address the significant 
challenge of insufficient immune infiltration in solid tumors.[6] 
Immunogenic cell death (ICD) and tumor cell fragments induced 
by SDT-mediated apoptosis of carcinoma cells act as ‘eat me’ 
signal to recruit various types of immune cells into the tumor 
microenvironment.[7] Activation of STING pathway by agonists 
stimulates the production of cytokines and chemokines, thus 
boosting immune infiltration and antitumor immune response after 
SDT.[8] STING agonists such as cyclic dimeric adenosine 
monophosphate (CDA), cyclic dinucleotide (CDN), or cyclic 
guanosine 
monophosphate–adenosine 
monophosphate 
(cGAMP), have been incorporated into nanoscale structures 
(such as PEG-lipid nanodiscs and extracellular vesicle) to 
improve their pharmacokinetics and biodistribution.[9] However, 
“always on” pharmacological activity of these agonists still 
activates STING pathway in normal tissues, resulting in immune-
related adverse events (irAEs). Thus, the development of 
activatable STING nanoagonists that are exclusively activated in 
tumor tissues for cancer sono-immunotherapy remains a 
challenge.[10] 
We herein report polymeric STING pro-agonists (PSPA) to 
specifically activate STING pathway via GSH-mediated release of 
MSA-2 in tumor microenvironment. Semiconducting polymers 
have been proven to generate ROS after sono-irradiation in our 
previous studies.[11] MSA-2 is a specific type of STING agonist 
that forms a dimer and binds noncovalently to STING in a “closed-
lid” conformation, resulting in the generation of interferon-β (IFN-
β) in both plasma and tumor.[12] In this work, we conjugated MSA-
2 with poly (ethylene glycol) (PEG) side chains on semiconducting 
polymer backbone via a GSH-activatable linker to afford PSPA. 
PSPA could passively accumulate in tumors via EPR effect. 
Under sono-irradiation, PSPA could generate 1O2 to induce the 
ICD of tumor cells. Moreover, MSA-2 was unleashed from PSPA 
in response to elevated GSH levels in tumor microenvironment, 
further amplifying tumor immunogenicity by activating STING

Results and Discussion 
Synthesis and characterization 
The amphiphilic semiconducting polymer was composed of 
a semiconducting polymeric backbone, a disulfide linker, and a 
STING agonist (MSA-2) (Figure1a). The synthesis routes of 
PSPA were displayed in Figure S1. Poly[2,7-(9,9-dioctylfluorene)-
alt-4,7-bis (thiophen-2-yl) benzo-2,1,3-thiadiazole] (PFODBT) 
was 
synthesized 
through 
stille 
polymerization. 
It 
was 
subsequently treated with sodium azide to produce PFODBT-N3 
(Figure S2). The azide moiety was then linked with alkyne on PEG 
using copper-catalyzed click chemistry to generate an amphiphilic 
polymer. Polymer was assembled into control semiconducting 
polymer nanoparticles (CSPN) finally. For MSA-2 modified 
semiconducting polymer nanoparticles (PSPA), MSA-2 was first 
conjugated with a disulfide linker (Figure S3, S4) and then reacted 
with PEG on the amphiphilic polymer (Figure S5) before assembly 
into nanoparticles in aqueous solution. 
The physical and optical characteristics of CSPN and PSPA 
were then examined and analyzed. Particle sizes were then 
measured using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images 
and dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis (Figure 2a). In TEM 
images, the average sizes of CSPN and PSPA were 
approximately 4.5 and 5.6 nm, respectively. In DLS, the average 
hydrodynamic sizes of CSPN and PSPA were approximately 21.0 
and 28.2 nm, respectively. Zeta potential value of CSPN and 
PSPA were approximately -12.4 and -7.24 mV, respectively 
(Figure 2b). The increased size and zeta potential were attributed 
to the modification of MSA-2 on PEG. Moreover, an analysis of 
the UV-visible absorption and fluorescence spectra of CSPN and 
PSPA was conducted, revealing similar spectra with distinctive 
absorption peaks at 550 nm and emission peaks at 691 nm 
(Figure 2c, 2d). The singlet oxygen (1O2) generation ability of 
CSPN and PSPA was also investigated using singlet oxygen 
sensor green (SOSG). Under sono-irradiation, the fluorescence 
intensity of SOSG exhibited a 4.2-fold increase for both CSPN 
and PSPA, which was higher than that for methylene blue (1.9-
fold) (Figure 2e). The grafting efficiency of MSA-2 was calculated 
to be 81.26%, and MSA-2 was released in a time-dependent and 
GSH-responsive manner (Figure 2f). GSH-mediated cleavage 
and subsequent hydrolysis by esterase lead to the activation of 
MSA-2, which was validated by HPLC (Figure 2g). 
Confocal fluorescence microscopy was used to assess the 
cellular endocytosis of 4T1 cells after incubation with CSPN and 
PSPA. Red fluorescence signals of CSPN and PSPA were 
detected, and the mean fluorescence intensities (MFIs) in both 
groups were similar (85.84 and 84.97, respectively) after 
incubation for 24 h (Figure 2h). Intracellular lysosome 
colocalization analysis further confirmed effective endocytosis (4 
h) and endosomal escape (24 h) of CSPN (Figure S6a).

In vitro immunological activation 
The sonodynamic activity and toxicity of CSPN and PSPA were 
studied in 4T1 cells. The production of 1O2 in 4T1 cells was 
measured 
using 
2',7'-dichlorodihydrofluorescein 
diacetate 
(H2DCFDA). H2DCFDA produced green fluorescence after 
treatment with CSPN or PSPA + US (Figure 2i, 2j and Figure S6d). 
The cytotoxicity of CSPN and PSPA was assessed using 5-(3-
carboxymethoxyphenyl)-2-(4,5-dimethylthiazolyl)-3-(4-
sulfophenyl)-tetrazolium (MTS) assay. CSPN and PSPA exhibited 
negligible cytotoxicity at even high concentrations before sono-
irradiation. Cell viability of CSPN- and PSPA-incubated 4T1 cells 
decreased to 16.9% at the [SP] concentration of 50 μg/mL after

To explore the mechanism underlying MSA-2-triggered STING 
pathway activation, the expression level of the key downstream 
signalling proteins p-TBK1 (TANK- binding kinase 1) and p-IRF3 
(interferon regulatory factor 3) in BMDCs were evaluated by 
western-blot assay. In PSPA + US group, these two signalling 
protein were significantly elevated compared to other groups, 
suggesting that STING pathway has been activated (Figure 3e). 
Additionally, there was a significant increase in IFN-β and 
interleukin-6 (IL-6) secretion in the PSPA + US group (Figure 3f, 
3g). The elevated expression levels of p-TBK1, p-IRF3, and IFN-
β in the PSPA and sono-irradiation group further confirmed the 
activation of STING pathway owing to the release of MSA-2. 
Further investigation was carried out on macrophage polarization 
by incubating BMDMs with 4T1 cell supernatants in different 
groups for 24 hours. In the group of CSPN with sono-irradiation, 
the proportion of M1 macrophages was significantly elevated to 
69.1% compared to the PBS group or 4T1 cell supernatant control 
incubation group, which showed only 26.1% or 28.6%, 
respectively (Figure 3h, 3i). Furthermore, in the group of PSPA 
with sono-irradiation, the proportion of M1 macrophages 
increased to 86.0%, indicating the activation function of MSA-2 on 
M1 macrophages. 
In vivo therapeutic efficacy 
To determine the optimal time for sono-irradiation, in vivo 
NIR fluorescence imaging was used to assess the accumulation 
and biodistribution of CSPN and PSPA in 4T1 tumors after 
intravenous injection. Following injection, the fluorescence 
intensity at the tumor sites exhibited a gradual increase, reaching 
its peak at 24 hours post-injection and sustaining a high level of 
fluorescence signal thereafter (Figure 4a, 4b). Ex vivo imaging 
data illustrated comparable tissue biodistribution patterns for 
CSPN and PSPA, with the highest accumulation observed in 
tumors, followed by livers (Figure S9). 
To confirm the generation of 1O2 in vivo, SOSG was injected 
intratumorally 30 minutes before sono-irradiation. The green 
fluorescence signal of SOSG in tumor tissues of CSPN and PSPA 
after sono-irradiation were 5.9-fold and 6.1-fold higher than 
unirradiated group, respectively (Figure 4c and Figure S10a). 
These findings indicated the successful generation of 1O2 in tumor 
tissues of CSPN and PSPA-injected and sono-irradiated mice.  
Furthermore, a bilateral tumor model was established to 
evaluate the therapeutic efficacy of CSPN or PSPA-mediated 
cancer sono-immunotherapy. (Figure 4d). Complete inhibition of 
primary and distant tumors was observed in PSPA-injected and 
sono-irradiated mice without recurrence (Figure 4e, 4f). Both 
CSPN and PSPA-mediated sono-immunotherapy induced tumor 
apoptosis, 
as 
evidenced 
by 
upregulated 
caspase-3 
in 
immunofluorescence and nuclear shrinkage, fragmentation, and 
absence in hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) staining analysis of 
tumors (Figure 4g, 4h and Figure S11). Mice in the group of PSPA 
with sono-irradiation exhibited prolonged survival compared to 
other groups (Figure S10b). Additionally, there were no significant 
changes observed in the body weights of the mice in these 
respective groups (Figure S10c). Furthermore, H&E staining 
images of major organs including the heart, liver, spleen, lung, 
and kidney, taken 22 days post-injection, did not reveal any 
significant damage or abnormality, indicating the high biosafety of 
PSPA-mediated cancer sono-immunotherapy (Figure S12). 
To assess the immunological memory effects of PSPA-
mediated sono-immunotherapy, a rechallenged tumor model was 
established (Figure 4i). The growth of tumors in the PSPA with 
sono-irradiation group was significantly inhibited compared to the 
group treated with PBS (Figure 4j). Additionally, obvious 
metastatic nodules were observed in the lungs of PBS treated 
mice compared with that of PSPA-injected and sono-irradiated 
mice, as confirmed by India ink staining and H&E staining images 
of lungs (Figure 4k, 4l). These findings confirmed that PSPA-
mediated sono-immunotherapy exhibited stronger anti-tumor and 
long-term anti-recurrence efficiency than the other groups. 
In vivo immunological activation  
To investigate the mechanisms underlying PSPA-mediated 
cancer sono-immunotherapy, the populations of various immune 
cells were studied. Mature DCs (CD11c+CD80+CD86+) in tumor-
draining lymph nodes (TDLNs) were examined by flow cytometry 
analysis. The proportion of matured DCs in the group of CSPN or 
PSPA with sono-irradiation increased by 2.88-fold and 3.53-fold, 
respectively, compared to the saline group (Figure 5a and Figure 
S13a). Furthermore, 49.73% and 95.02% of macrophages in 
tumors were polarized into the M1 type in the group of CSPN or 
PSPA with sono-irradiation, respectively, while in the saline 
treated group, M1 macrophages accounted for only 29.95% 
(Figure 5b and Figure S13b). These results indicated that PSPA-
mediated sono-immunotherapy increased the degree of DCs 
maturation and M1 macrophages polarization. 
The populations of cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) and 
regulatory T cells (Tregs) in tumors were also evaluated. There 
was a notable increase in the percentage of CD8+ T cells 
observed in the primary tumors (3.22-fold) and distant tumors 
(3.42-fold) in the PSPA + US group compared to the saline group, 
as shown in Figure 5c-e and Figure S14. Tregs (CD4+Foxp3+) 
were also studied to assess the immunosuppressive tumor 
microenvironment (TME). In comparison to the mice that were 
administered with saline, there was a significant reduction in the 
populations of Tregs in both primary and distant tumors of the 
PSPA + US group, with a decrease of 55.1% and 73.9% observed, 
respectively (Figure S15). The ratio of CD8+ T cells to Tregs was 
the highest in the PSPA + US group (Figure 5d). Additionally, 
treatment with CSPN or PSPA and sono-irradiation led to a 
significant increase in serum levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines, 
including IFN-γ, IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α). 
Furthermore, PSPA with sono irradiation treatment induced the 
elevation of IFN-β (4.5-fold compared to saline group) (Figure 5f).

Conclusion 
We synthesized GSH-activatable polymeric STING pro-
agonists (PSPA) for cancer sono-immunotherapy. Upon sono-
irradiation, PSPA produced nearly double amount of 1O2 as 
compared to conventional sonosensitizers (methylene blue). 
Additionally, PSPA combined tumor-specific activation of STING 
agonists with SDT, effectively inhibiting tumor growth on bilateral 
tumor model and further amplifying the immune response induced 
by SDT-mediated tumor ICD. Furthermore, activatable STING 
agonists has been shown to minimize irAEs that may arise from 
“always on” pharmacological activity of agonists, which would 
otherwise activate the STING pathway in normal tissues. The 
combined therapy effectively suppressed tumor growth, inhibited 
metastasis, and prevented tumor recurrence due to the activation 
of a durable systemic antitumor immune response. Our results
