# Hydrogel-Based intraperitoneal drug delivery platforms for peritoneal metastasis: strategies, advances, and prospects.

## Abstract

Peritoneal metastasis (PM), as a terminal stage of malignant tumors with extremely poor prognosis, remains a clinical challenge. Intraperitoneal (IP) administration enhances local drug concentrations, improving survival outcomes for PM patients. However, rapid drug clearance and uneven distribution limit its therapeutic potential. In recent years, hydrogel-based drug delivery systems have garnered attention due to their excellent biocompatibility, drug loading capacity, and controlled release properties. The use of hydrogel-loaded drugs via IP injection can significantly improve anti-cancer efficacy by increasing the local drug concentration, prolonging the retention time of the drug in the peritoneal cavity, and decreasing systemic toxicity. This review summarizes the pathogenesis and current treatment strategies of PM, emphasizing various drugs (including chemotherapy agents, immunotherapeutics, targeted drugs, radioactive isotopes, and herbal medicines) delivered via hydrogel-based IP administration. Furthermore, it highlights the potential of nanoparticles and microparticle-hydrogel composites to further improve drug delivery, offering new strategies for PM treatment and theoretical basis for clinical rational drug use.

Hydrogel-Based intraperitoneal drug delivery platforms for peritoneal metastasis: strategies, advances, and prospects

Abstract

Peritoneal metastasis (PM), as a terminal stage of malignant tumors with extremely poor prognosis, remains a clinical challenge. Intraperitoneal (IP) administration enhances local drug concentrations, improving survival outcomes for PM patients. However, rapid drug clearance and uneven distribution limit its therapeutic potential. In recent years, hydrogel-based drug delivery systems have garnered attention due to their excellent biocompatibility, drug loading capacity, and controlled release properties. The use of hydrogel-loaded drugs via IP injection can significantly improve anti-cancer efficacy by increasing the local drug concentration, prolonging the retention time of the drug in the peritoneal cavity, and decreasing systemic toxicity. This review summarizes the pathogenesis and current treatment strategies of PM, emphasizing various drugs (including chemotherapy agents, immunotherapeutics, targeted drugs, radioactive isotopes, and herbal medicines) delivered via hydrogel-based IP administration. Furthermore, it highlights the potential of nanoparticles and microparticle-hydrogel composites to further improve drug delivery, offering new strategies for PM treatment and theoretical basis for clinical rational drug use.

Introduction

Peritoneal metastasis (PM) commonly occurs in a range of gastrointestinal and gynecologic malignancies, such as colorectal, gastric, pancreatic, appendiceal, and ovarian cancers, and disseminates in the peritoneal cavity (Figure 1; Cortés-Guiral et al.; Ren et al.). PM can arise from various routes, typically involving the direct implantation of cancer cells into the peritoneum or dissemination via the lymphatic system. For example, primary tumors may fully invade the intestinal wall or damage blood and lymphatic vessels during surgery (Koppe et al.; Ceelen and Bracke; Ren et al.). Patients with untreated PM experience rapid onset of symptoms due to disease progression; the most common symptoms include small bowel obstruction, ascites, tumor-related pain, malnutrition, and so on. The inability to control or relieve patient's symptoms is a direct cause of death (Foster et al.). The occurrence of PM has been shown to significantly reduce the overall survival (OS) of patients, with a median survival that rarely exceeds six months. Therefore, it was often considered a terminal condition in the past (Sadeghi et al.).

Traditional treatments for PM include systemic chemotherapy, but the associated median survival remains suboptimal (Koppe et al.). Additionally, intravenous systemic chemotherapy has been linked to systemic adverse effects such as immunosuppression, neurotoxicity, and myelosuppression. These toxicities significantly restrict the intensity of chemotherapy and reduce the quality of life for patients (Pacheco et al.). A novel combined treatment strategy, integrating cytoreductive surgery (CRS) with hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy (HIPEC), has provided significant survival benefits for patients with PM (Wong et al.). The combination strategy is based on the surgical removal of the visible tumor, augmented by local intraperitoneal (IP) chemotherapy for the residual microscopic lesions (Roy et al.). Verwaal et al. conducted a randomized clinical trial showing that the median survival was significantly longer in the CRS + HIPEC group compared to receiving systemic chemotherapy (22.3 vs. 12.6 months), establishing this combination as the current standard of care for colonic PM (Verwaal et al.; Verwaal et al.). More recently, Van Driel et al. confirmed in a randomized phase III trial that CRS + HIPEC with cisplatin significantly improved recurrence-free and OS in stage III ovarian cancer, underscoring the broader applicability of this approach (van Driel et al.). The pharmacokinetic rationale for IP therapy lies in the peritoneal–plasma barrier, which slows systemic drug clearance and increases local drug exposure (Perelló-Trias et al.). However, its limitations—poor tissue penetration, uneven distribution, and partial systemic absorption—remain major obstacles (Jacquet and Sugarbaker; Ceelen et al.). Therefore, using preparations that can control drug release while minimizing absorption into the circulation is essential. Recent advancements in materials science have brought new hope for improving IP drug delivery.

Commonly used drug delivery systems (DDS) for PM include microspheres (Ms), micelles, nanoparticles (NPs), and hydrogels (Roy et al.). However, Ms may lead to the potential risk of peritoneal adhesions and localized inflammations (Kohane et al.), and while NPs have many advantages in targeted drug delivery, some of them are characterized by release bursts and poor bio-adhesion (Sepantafar et al.). In contrast, hydrogels, as an effective drug delivery strategy, have been extensively used in cancer treatment in recent years. The popularity of hydrogel can be attributed to its four characteristics: biocompatibility, biodegradability, drug loading capacity, and controlled release. Biocompatibility ensures that hydrogels do not result in severe cellular or systemic toxicity after implantation into the body. In addition, many of the natural and synthetic polymers used to prepare hydrogels for cancer therapy are biodegradable. Due to their porous nature, hydrogels are capable of absorbing large amounts of water or biofluids. Furthermore, the rate of release of the therapeutic agents can be controlled by modifying the structure of the hydrogel (Li and Mooney; Sun et al.). Hydrogels can be categorized into conventional hydrogels and responsive hydrogels. Conventional hydrogels are water-absorbing polymer networks that swell in aqueous media without any change in response to environmental stimuli. In contrast, responsive hydrogels undergo reversible volume phase transitions or sol-gel transitions in response to environmental stimuli, thereby delivering drugs at the appropriate time and place according to physiological needs (Darge et al.; Zhou et al.). Common stimulus-responsive hydrogels include thermo-sensitive, pH-sensitive, and enzyme-sensitive hydrogels (Tian and Liu). Compared to normal tissues, tumor tissues have hypoxia, acidic pH, and enhanced permeability and retention effects, which make hydrogels uniquely advantageous for designing drug delivery vehicles (Zhang et al.).

Hydrogels are capable of spatially and temporally controlling the release of a wide range of therapeutic agents, including small molecule drugs, large molecule drugs, and cells. Their tunable physical properties, controlled degradability and protection against unstable drugs make them an ideal platform for achieving controlled drug release (Li and Mooney). Hydrogel-based DDS has been proven to enhance therapeutic efficacy by prolonging drug half-life, facilitating controlled and modulated drug release, and reducing off-target exposure (Sepantafar et al.). These attributes highlight the great potential of hydrogels in cancer therapy; however, systematic reviews dedicated to their application in IP drug delivery for peritoneal metastases remain scarce, which further underscores the significance of this work.

This review aims to comprehensively summarize recent advances in hydrogel-based IP drug delivery for PM—including chemotherapeutics, immunotherapies, targeted agents, radioactive isotopes, and herbal medicines (Figure 2)—highlight synergistic designs combining hydrogels with NPs or Ms, and discuss translational challenges and future research directions to optimize their clinical application.

Hydrogel-based chemotherapy for PM

Chemotherapy remains the primary treatment for PM of gastrointestinal or gynecologic malignancies. Common IP chemotherapeutic agents, such as fluorouracil (Fu), cisplatin (CDDP), paclitaxel (PTX), oxaliplatin (OXA), doxorubicin (DOX), docetaxel (DTX), and mitomycin C, are depicted in Figure 1 for common PM tumors (Sugarbaker et al.; Lemoine et al.; Foster et al.). However, these drugs have limited penetration depth, and direct injection into the peritoneal cavity presents the following challenges: uneven drug distribution, rapid absorption leading to increased systemic toxicity, high local concentrations causing severe side effects, and short retention time. Recently, the use of hydrogel-based DDS has been shown to optimize IP chemotherapy delivery, prolong drug retention in the peritoneal cavity, and reduce systemic toxicity.

Thermo-responsive hydrogels

Thermo-responsive hydrogels, with their unique temperature-responsive ‘sol-gel’ transition properties, have great advantages for localized drug delivery and sustained release (Darge et al.; Shi et al.; Tian and Liu). At room temperature, they remain in solution form, enabling easy and non-destructive drug loading. Upon injection, the formulation undergoes gelation at physiological temperature, forming an in situ depot that provides controlled drug release. In addition, these injectable thermo-sensitive hydrogels have the advantage of easy administration and homogeneous distribution of the drug (Chen et al.). These properties make thermo-sensitive hydrogels a widely utilized sustained-release carrier for drugs in IP chemotherapy.

Single-drug delivery

A number of thermo-responsive hydrogels have been designed for single-agent chemotherapy in PM models, showing improved local retention, reduced systemic toxicity, and enhanced antitumor efficacy compared with free drugs. The performance of these hydrogels is strongly influenced by polymer properties (e.g. crosslinking density, hydrophilic–hydrophobic balance) and drug characteristics (e.g. molecular weight, solubility). For instance, small molecules such as DOX often display biphasic release-an initial burst followed by sustained release during gel degradation-while more hydrophobic or higher-molecular-weight drugs tend to be retained longer within the gel matrix. Stronger crosslinking slows drug diffusion but may limit tissue penetration, whereas weaker crosslinking accelerates release but reduces depot stability.

Representative examples are summarized in Table 1. In colorectal cancer (CRC) PM, chitosan–glycerophosphate thermosensitive hydrogels loaded with 5-FU demonstrated a rapid initial burst followed by sustained release over several days, which significantly increased local peritoneal drug exposure while lowering plasma levels, thereby reducing systemic toxicity and gastrointestinal side effects (Depani et al.). Similarly, a hyaluronic acid–chitosan–Pluronic F127 nanoparticle (HACPN)-based hydrogel incorporating DOX not only provided a biphasic release pattern but also acted as a physical anti-adhesion barrier after surgery, preventing post-operative adhesions while maintaining strong local cytotoxicity against tumor nodules, thus offering dual therapeutic functionality (Chen et al.). In our previous study, methylcellulose-based hydrogels carrying OXA were shown to prolong in vivo gel residence for about one week, ensuring continuous drug release within the peritoneal cavity and achieving marked tumor inhibition and survival benefit in CRC–PM mouse models (Yang et al.). These results underscore the ability of hydrogel systems to simultaneously enhance drug pharmacokinetics, provide mechanical support, and improve treatment safety in CRC–PM.

In the treatment of PM of gastric origin, thermo-sensitive hydrogels incorporating PTX or DTX demonstrated superior tumor growth suppression and significantly prolonged survival compared with free-drug formulations, largely due to improved drug retention within the peritoneum and reduced clearance through systemic circulation (Yu et al.; Han et al.). Notably, the DTX hydrogel allowed for less frequent dosing (weekly vs. daily injections), thereby improving treatment compliance and reducing cumulative toxicity. Furthermore, co-delivery systems such as hydrogels encapsulating both 5-FU and CDDP achieved spatially and temporally coordinated release, which not only prolonged local drug retention but also enhanced synergistic tumor inhibition through complementary mechanisms of DNA synthesis blockade and DNA crosslinking, while simultaneously minimizing nephrotoxicity and myelosuppression associated with systemic chemotherapy (Chen et al.). Together, these studies illustrated the versatility of hydrogel-based IP drug delivery platforms in gastric cancer PM, highlighting their potential to balance efficacy, safety, and patient convenience.

Micro-composite

Ms are small spherical entities formed by the dissolution or dispersion of a drug in a polymeric material, with particle sizes ranging from 1 to 250 μm (Wang et al.; Li and Zhu). Ms are widely utilized due to their ease of administration, stable drug adsorption within Ms, and low local irritation and adverse reactions (Shi et al.). Previous studies have compared the peritoneal targeting advantages and anti-tumor activity of NPs, micelles, and polymer Ms after IP injection. The results established the advantages of slower drug clearance from the peritoneal cavity, longer retention time, and higher peritoneal concentration of Ms (Tsai et al.).

Many researchers have dispersed Ms drug carriers in thermo-sensitive hydrogels to prepare dual-barrier, long-acting formulations, which exhibited sustained drug release rates (no bursts or platforms) and high biological activity (Duvvuri et al.; Wang et al.). For example, Wang et al. developed a bilayer PLGA Ms co-loaded with disulfiram and copper gluconate, embedded in a thermo-sensitive hydrogel for IP treatment of peritoneal carcinoma. This design exploited the biodegradability and biocompatibility of PLGA together with temperature-responsive gelation, achieving extended IP drug exposure and reduced systemic toxicity, while markedly suppressing tumor burden and ascites (Wang et al.). Similarly, to overcome hydrophobic drug aggregation during direct mixing with hydrogels, Liu et al. encapsulated camptothecin (CPT) into polymeric Ms before dispersing them within a thermo-sensitive hydrogel. This Ms-hydrogel composite significantly prolonged CPT release, enhanced apoptosis of CT26 tumor cells, and inhibited angiogenesis in vivo, thereby showing superior anti-tumor efficacy compared to free CPT (Liu et al.). In another study, Luo et al. reported a hyaluronic acid (HA)-based hydrogel system co-loaded with 5-FU, CDDP, and PTX Ms. IP administration of this composite DDS in colorectal PM models effectively suppressed tumor growth, prevented local infiltration, and reduced distant liver/lung metastases and ascites formation (Luo et al.). In summary, Ms-hydrogel composites provide a versatile platform for controlled and synergistic drug delivery in peritoneal metastases, enabling combination chemotherapy and enhanced therapeutic outcomes. However, several issues remain to be addressed. First, although the risk of peritoneal adhesions and local inflammation has been acknowledged, there is limited evidence directly evaluating whether embedding Ms in hydrogel matrices mitigates or exacerbates these adverse effects. Second, mechanical and rheological compatibility between Ms and hydrogel carriers-such as particle dispersion uniformity, potential aggregation, and long-term stability-remains insufficiently investigated. Optimizing the interfacial interactions between Ms and hydrogels will be critical for ensuring consistent drug distribution, reproducibility, and clinical translation.

Nano-composite

As mentioned previously, hydrogels have been extensively studied in IP therapy due to their smart response to environmental stimuli and their biocompatibility (Roy et al.). However, direct loading of drugs into hydrogels typically results in relatively rapid drug release due to the high-water content and large pore size of hydrogels, which may limit the amount and uniformity of hydrophobic drug loading (Qiu and Park; Hoare and Kohane). Advances in nanotechnology have enabled the development of nano-formulations such as NPs, nanocapsules, and micelles, which improve drug solubility, bioavailability, stability against degradation, and targeted delivery while prolonging therapeutic effects (Hamidi et al.). Embedding NPs within hydrogels has thus emerged as a promising hybrid strategy, combining the spatial retention and local depot effect of hydrogels with the tunable release and targeting capabilities of NPs (Table 2; Gao et al.).

Importantly, NP properties critically influence release kinetics once incorporated into hydrogel matrices. Particle size determines the diffusional mobility within the gel pores, with smaller NPs typically diffusing faster. Surface charge and hydrophilicity affect NP–hydrogel interactions: positively charged or hydrophobic NPs may strongly associate with negatively charged or hydrophilic gels, slowing drug release, whereas weakly interacting NPs may escape more readily. Furthermore, aggregation tendencies and NP stability influence both release uniformity and therapeutic reproducibility. Understanding and optimizing these parameters is essential for rational nano-hydrogel design.

Representative studies illustrate diverse strategies. Bae et al. developed a thermo-responsive Pluronic F-127/CLA hydrogel carrying 5-FU-loaded NPs (P-FU), which significantly suppressed tumor growth and peritoneal/liver metastases in colon cancer models (Bae et al.). The same group extended this approach to DTX, where CLA-conjugated Pluronic® micelles improved solubility and enabled in situ hydrogelation after IP injection, leading to prolonged survival and reduced peritoneal tumor burden in gastric cancer models (Bae et al.). Zhou et al. incorporated DTX and the LL37 peptide into polymeric NPs dispersed in a thermo-sensitive hydrogel, demonstrating potent anti-angiogenic effects and survival benefit in CRC–PM (Zhou et al.).

PTX analogs are considered ideal for IP chemotherapy, but due to the PTX's poor water solubility, its solubility in hydrogels is low (Mosca et al.). To address the low solubility of PTX, Wang et al. engineered amphiphilic triblock PECT NPs loaded with PTX and incorporated them into a thermo-sensitive hydrogel (PTX/PECT-gel). This design prevented PTX crystallization, achieved near-linear release over 42 days, and effectively inhibited tumor growth in both subcutaneous and peritoneal models (Wang et al.). Subsequently, the team used PTX/PECT-gel for IP treatment of colon cancer PM. After single IP injection, the drug concentration remained high for approximately seven days and significantly inhibited tumor growth (Xu et al.). Qian et al. further improved NP stability by cloaking PTX nanocrystals with red blood cell membranes (PRNPs) before embedding them in an injectable hydrogel (Figure 3). The PRNP-gel exhibited high loading efficiency, biocompatibility, and sustained local release with reduced systemic toxicity (Qian et al.). Similarly, Ren et al. formulated an oxaliplatin–tannic acid (OXA–TA) NP hydrogel composite, achieving enhanced anti-angiogenic and survival benefits in CRC-PM models (Liu et al.).

As mentioned above, combination chemotherapy can be synergistic, thus improving efficacy and reducing drug side effects (Liu et al.; Chen et al.). Combined IP chemotherapy requires the simultaneous delivery of drugs with varying solubilities. Among the most innovative developments are dual-drug micelle–hydrogel systems, which allow simultaneous delivery of hydrophilic and hydrophobic agents. Gong et al. reported a PCEC-based dual system encapsulating PTX in micelles and 5-FU in hydrogel, enabling synchronized delivery and markedly improving local drug retention and anti-metastatic activity in colorectal PM (Gong et al.). Yun et al. designed a chitosan-based hydrogel embedding 5-FU micelles together with cisplatin, which significantly prolonged survival and reduced distant organ metastases (Yun et al.). Xiao et al. further extended this strategy with norethindrone NPs plus oxaliplatin in PECE hydrogel, showing robust suppression of tumor proliferation, ascites, and angiogenesis without systemic toxicity (Xiao et al.).These dual-drug composites represent a significant step forward in overcoming the solubility mismatch challenge of combination IP chemotherapy.

Despite these promising results, several translational challenges must be considered. First, nanotoxicology remains insufficiently studied; prolonged NP accumulation may trigger immune responses or organ toxicity. Second, large-scale synthesis of uniform NPs and reproducible hydrogel–NP composites remains technically demanding, and dual-class regulatory hurdles (nanomedicine and hydrogel devices) may complicate clinical approval. Third, clinical formulation complexity – such as stability during storage, sterilization, and cost-effectiveness – will strongly impact feasibility. Addressing these barriers is essential for successful translation.

Enzyme-responsive hydrogels

Enzymes are catalytically active biomolecules essential to multiple physiological and pathological processes. Leveraging endogenous enzymatic activity for drug delivery has emerged as a rational strategy, as enzyme expression levels vary among tissues and disease states. Injectable enzyme-responsive hydrogels are typically designed to contain enzyme-cleavable motifs or substrates (e.g. glycosidic bonds for hyaluronidase, peptide linkers for MMPs), which undergo enzyme-specific reactions upon administration. These reactions can induce cleavage of the hydrogel backbone, trigger network disassembly, or modulate cross-link density, thereby controlling drug release kinetics in a reversible or irreversible manner (Sobczak). By tailoring the type and density of enzyme-responsive linkages, release profiles can be tuned to match the biological context, offering higher precision compared with non-specific stimuli-responsive systems (Liu et al.).

Single-drug delivery

Emoto et al. used an HA-based in situ cross-linked hydrogel as a carrier for CDDP to treat the peritoneal spread of gastric cancer. The HA enzyme physiologically present in the peritoneal cavity degraded the HA hydrogel, resulting in sustained release of CDDP. Compared to free drug administration, this system significantly enhanced anti-tumor efficacy while minimizing systemic exposure, highlighting enzyme-triggered release as a promising approach for peritoneal dissemination (Emoto et al.).

Micro-composite

Due to the favorable biocompatibility of the peritoneal cavity and the prevalence of HA enzymes, Bajaj et al. similarly utilized HA-based hydrogels as carriers for PTX micro-precipitates in ovarian cancer peritoneal metastases. PTX is hard to retain in the gel and the peritoneal cavity due to its small size, but PTX precipitates in the micrometer range can be retained. The HA-hydrogel is degraded by the HA enzyme physiologically present in the peritoneal cavity, allowing for the sustained release of PTX. The study results showed that particulate PTX encapsulated in the HA gel was best retained in the peritoneal cavity, but the solubility of PTX particles was limited and did not show enhanced anti-cancer effects (Bajaj et al.). This suggests that particle size and dissolution kinetics must be carefully matched with enzymatic degradation rates to optimize therapeutic benefit.

Gelatin is a protein with a randomly coiled structure consisting of 18 different types of linked amino acids. Cancer cells often secrete matrix metalloproteinases, such as collagenase and gelatinase, which can proteolytically degrade gelatin Ms, making it easier to release drugs near the cancer site. Due to this property, using gelatin as a drug carrier for cancer therapy is considered reasonable. Gunji et al. prepared biodegradable gelatin Ms doped with CDDP (GM-CDDP) for the treatment of peritoneal carcinomatosis. Enzymatic degradation of gelatin at tumor sites facilitated drug release, prolonging survival compared to free CDDP (74 vs. 40 days, p < 0.05) while significantly reducing nephrotoxicity and hematologic toxicity (Gunji et al.). However, the team observed an initial burst (30%) of CDDP, resulting in a rapid increase in IP CDDP concentrations followed by a sharp increase in blood concentrations. Such a phenomenon was caused by early drug release from non- or weakly bound drugs with macromolecular materials and was strongly associated with adverse effects. To address this, the group developed gelatin hydrogel particles (GHG-CDDP) with a rinsing step to remove loosely bound drug, thereby reducing burst release and systemic toxicity while maintaining therapeutic efficacy (Yamashita et al.).

Nano-composite

As previously mentioned, Bajaj et al. used HA-hydrogels loaded with PTX particles to maintain high local PTX concentrations in the peritoneal cavity for up to two weeks under the influence of HA enzymes. However, this did not translate into enhanced anti-cancer effects (Bajaj et al.). Possible explanations were that the dissolution of PTX particles was too slow to provide effective local drug concentrations and/or the duration of the experiments was too short to observe the benefit of prolonged IP retention of PTX. Nanocrystals have higher dissolution rates than large particles due to their small size and high surface area-to-volume ratio. To overcome the slow dissolution of PTX particles in enzyme-responsive matrices, Bajaj et al. later formulated paclitaxel nanocrystals (PNCs) within HA-based hydrogels. Compared with larger PTX particles, PNCs exhibited superior dissolution rates due to their increased surface area, ensuring faster drug availability upon HAase-mediated hydrogel degradation. In SKOV3 ovarian tumor xenograft models, PNC-loaded HA hydrogels demonstrated significantly stronger anti-tumor activity and extended survival compared with either free PTX or PTX-particle gels (Figure 4a; Sun et al.). This work highlights the importance of nanostructure–enzyme interactions, as nanocrystals enable better synchronization of drug release with enzymatic hydrogel degradation.

PH-responsive hydrogels

Changes in the pH environment can cause the hydrogel network to contract or expand, similar to temperature changes, thereby affecting the encapsulation and release of therapeutic drugs. These types of hydrogels are known as pH-responsive hydrogels. Generally, the phase change ability of pH-sensitive hydrogels depends on the weakly acidic or basic groups (e.g. amines, imines, carboxylic acids) within the hydrogel matrix. Upon protonation or deprotonation, these groups alter polymer solubility and volume, leading to network swelling, collapse, or dissociation (Liu et al.). In cancer, the high glycolytic rate of tumor cells results in extracellular acidification (pH 6.5–6.8), lower than that of normal tissues (Ding et al.; Zhou et al.). This acidic tumor microenvironment can therefore serve as a selective trigger: polymers with acidic groups contract under low pH, forming dense networks, while basic groups become protonated, leading to charge repulsion and network expansion. Such mechanisms make pH-responsive hydrogels attractive for IP chemotherapy, where they can release drugs preferentially at tumor sites, maximizing efficacy while limiting toxicity to normal tissues. Compared with thermo-responsive hydrogels, which rely on general physicochemical changes, and enzyme-responsive systems, which depend on enzyme overexpression, pH-sensitive hydrogels offer a uniform and predictable trigger because tumor acidity is a broadly conserved hallmark across malignancies. However, unlike enzyme systems that may achieve higher specificity (e.g. MMP-rich invasive fronts), pH-based responsiveness can be less precise, since peritoneal inflammation, infection, or ischemia may also alter pH, potentially affecting release profiles. Furthermore, inter-tumor and inter-patient variability in acidity may introduce unpredictability, highlighting the need for careful tuning of pH thresholds.

Single-drug delivery

Zahedi et al. developed a polymer-lipid hydrogel formulation (PoLigel) for the encapsulation of DTX. This system demonstrated significant anti-tumor effects in a murine xenograft model of human ovarian cancer. Following IP injection, PoLigel rapidly disintegrated under higher pH conditions (7.4), causing a rapid decrease in the solution pH and effective release of DTX from the hydrogel, thereby inhibiting tumor cell proliferation (Zahedi et al.; Zahedi et al.).

Nano-composite

As mentioned in Section 2.2.1, Emoto et al. successfully reduced the weight of peritoneal nodes by encapsulating CDDP in a HA-based hydrogel (Emoto et al.). However, the release of CDDP from this HA hydrogel was still not long enough, suggesting the need for further material improvements. Therefore, the team developed another material, HA-CDDP nanogel, in which CDDP was coordinated via a pH-sensitive chelating ligand attached to HA backbones. In murine gastric cancer PM models, this system preferentially accumulated in tumor nodules and achieved pH-dependent CDDP release lasting over a week, significantly reducing peritoneal nodules (Ohta et al.).

Research has shown that covalently cross-linked hydrogels are less susceptible to dilution because the degradation mechanism involves the breaking of covalent bonds and not just the weakening of physical interactions (Pereira and Bártolo,). Therefore, the use of chemically cross-linked hydrogels has the potential to further increase the retention of IP drugs and thus prolong the IP residence time of NPs. Surikutchi et al. developed a chemically cross-linked PEG-based hydrogel containing drug-carrying HA nanocapsules (NCs) in which DTX was loaded into the NCs, which were then incorporated into the PEG hydrogel for IP drug delivery. This system retained in the peritoneal cavity for 24 hours (Teja Surikutchi et al.). Zhou et al. further advanced this concept by designing a Schiff-based cross-linked collagen/PEG hydrogel containing DTX-loaded NPs. The hydrogel was asymmetrically coated with cysteine to resist nonspecific adhesion, enabling dual functionality: pH-responsive DTX release under acidic gradients (pH 6.5–5.5) and post-surgical anti-adhesion effects. This system synergistically reduced recurrence after abdominal tumor surgery (Zhou et al.).

For unresectable PM, the treatment can be performed using pressurized IP aerosolized chemotherapy (PIPAC), a locoregional drug delivery method. To further optimize the distribution uniformity and tumor permeability of the nebulized fluid, electrostatic precipitation PIPAC (ePIPAC) was developed. Combining hydrogels with high-pressure nebulization (ePIPAC) can improve the homogeneity of IP distribution and prolong drug release, thereby increasing efficacy. Braet et al. evaluated pH-sensitive supramolecular polymer hydrogels based on ureido-pyrimidinone-poly- (ethylene glycol) (UPy-PEG) as an aerosol-controlled-release DDS for improved PM therapy. Acidification of the UPy-PEG solution by CO2 in the peritoneal cavity stimulated in situ gelation, thus prolonging the retention time of the hydrogel. In vitro experiments demonstrated that paclitaxel nanocrystals were gradually released, resulting in sustained cytotoxicity for 10 days, successfully delaying tumor progression and significantly increasing OS time (Braet et al.).

Hydrogel-based immunotherapy for PM

Currently, the treatment of PM from malignant tumors relies heavily on chemotherapy. However, with a deeper understanding of T-cell immunity and antigen presentation mechanisms, immunotherapy can activate the systemic immune response, further inhibiting tumor recurrence and metastasis, offering new possibilities for treating refractory cancers. Primary immunotherapies include immunomodulatory drugs, cytokines, immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), adoptive cell therapy (ACT), and cancer vaccines (Shiravand et al.). Clinically, nivolumab and pembrolizumab have been used for the treatment of PM in CRC and gastric cancers, while catumaxomab has shown to be efficacious in malignant ascites from gastric, colorectal, and ovarian cancers (Thadi et al.; Shitara et al.; Ornella et al.). However, immunotherapy faces challenges such as low immune response rates, increased immunosuppression, and serious immune-related adverse events. These challenges are often attributed to multiple dosing, high-dose systemic administration, and suboptimal immune activation (Peng et al.). Furthermore, the release rate of immunizing agents is difficult to match the immune response process, and for immunotherapy, sustained, long-lasting stimulation is essential to activate the systemic immune response and establish immune memory effects (Si et al.). Thus, hydrogel-based DDS enables controlled drug release and specific biological functions, resulting in improved precision and therapeutic efficacy. Although hydrogels hold promise for drug delivery, challenges remain due to the immunosuppressive nature of the tumor microenvironment (TME) and immune evasion mechanisms (Bilotta et al.). To address this issue, bio-based hydrogels combining multiple immunomodulators are being explored to reverse the immunosuppressive TME and enhance immune activation. Common strategies include co-encapsulation with immunotherapeutic agents or agents that induce tumor-associated antigen (TAA) release (e.g. radiosensitizers, chemotherapeutics).

Meng et al. designed an injectable hydrogel DDS based on chitosan hydrochloride and oxidized dextran (CH-OD) for loading sulfasalazine (SSZ), a drug with ferroptosis-inducing capabilities that effectively kills tumors and activates anti-tumor immunity. SSZ-loaded CH-OD (CH-OD-SSZ) hydrogels exhibited greater cytotoxicity and induced higher levels of immunogenic ferroptosis compared to free SSZ. In an ascites model of hepatocellular carcinoma, IP injection of CH-OD-SSZ hydrogel significantly suppressed tumor progression and improved immune status. The combination of CH-OD-SSZ hydrogel and anti-programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) immunotherapy achieved over a 50% reduction in ascites and elicited long-term immune memory (Figure 4b) (Meng et al.). Although PD-1 inhibitors are typically administered intravenously in clinical settings, the study demonstrates that hydrogel-mediated local SSZ release enhanced the responsiveness to systemic PD1 therapy by priming the peritoneal TME, thereby justifying the combined approach.

Radiation can lead to plasma membrane disassembly by inducing DNA damage (Lin et al.), resulting in the leakage of cytoplasmic immunogenic material into the extracellular environment (Galluzzi et al.). Peritoneal plasma is filled with large numbers of macrophages that greatly control the malignant phenotype of peritoneal spread, leading to treatment resistance in ovarian cancer (Ghosn et al.). By converting peritoneal macrophages into tumor-killing effector cells, these macrophages can be effective targets for ovarian cancer therapy. Therefore, Li et al. developed a peritoneal-localized artificial exosome-based hydrogel to domesticate peritoneal macrophages as a therapeutic target for effective ovarian cancer treatment. The artificial exosomes were derived from genetically modified sialic acid-conjugated Ig-like lectin 10 (Siglec-10)-engineered M1-type macrophages and chemically engineered as a gelling agent (Figure 5). After triggering immunogenicity by X-ray irradiation, the hydrogel encapsulated with the apoptotic cell phagocytosis inhibitor MRX-2843 was able to achieve cascade modulation to orchestrate peritoneal macrophage polarization, apoptotic cell phagocytosis, and phagocytosis for potent phagocytosis and robust antigen presentation to tumor cells. This process effectively bridged the innate effector function of macrophages to their adaptive immune response, providing a potent treatment for ovarian cancer (Li et al.).

Oxaliplatin, a first-line drug for metastatic CRC, can induce immunogenic cell death (ICD) by releasing TAAs and danger signals (Fu et al.; Sprooten et al.; Lenz et al.). With advances in vaccine technology, immune adjuvants have become essential components of cancer vaccines (Cuzzubbo et al.). As the most potent antigen-presenting cells, the maturation of dendritic cells (DCs) is crucial for initiating anti-tumor immunity (Del Prete et al.). The immune adjuvant resiquimod (R848) can stimulate Toll-like receptor 7/8 (TLR7/8) and promote DC maturation, thus inducing cytotoxic T lymphocyte responses (Phuengkham et al.). Consequently, the combination of OXA and R848 exhibits a ‘tumor vaccine-like’ effect, triggering specific immunity and generating efficient and durable anti-tumor responses. Wang et al. synthesized a temperature-responsive PDLLA-PEG-PDLLA (PLEL) hydrogel and developed the OXA/R848@PLEL DDS through a simple physical mixing technique for IP injection. This system boasts excellent biocompatibility, injectability, and thermo-sensitivity, prolonging drug retention time in the abdominal cavity, increasing drug concentration and enhancing anti-cancer efficacy. In vivo experiments demonstrated that OXA/R848@PLEL significantly inhibited tumor growth and metastasis, completely suppressed ascites production, and improved the survival rate of mice to over 50% without notable toxicity. By inducing specific immunity and forming durable anti-cancer immune memory, this system effectively prevented tumor recurrence. Therefore, the OXA/R848@PLEL system demonstrated excellent safety and highly effective, long-lasting therapeutic effects in treating PM of CRC (Wang et al.). Unlike systemic PD-1 or adjuvant administration, local IP hydrogel delivery ensured high IP concentrations while minimizing systemic exposure.

PTX is a first-line agent for the treatment of ovarian cancer and has been reported to induce ICD and enhance PD-L1 expression in a variety of cancers. Combined strategies involving PTX and PD-1/PD-L1 blockade have demonstrated synergistic effects on ovarian cancer in preclinical models (Yang et al.). To achieve co-delivery of hydrophobic drug PTX and ICIs, recently, Liang et al. developed a nano-hydrogel based on bio- adhesive NPs (BNPs) and carboxymethyl chitosan (CMCS), a chemically modified polysaccharide rich in amino groups, to enhance the efficacy of the combination therapy for the treatment of PM in ovarian cancer. The hydrogel achieved sustained release for 7 days and adhered to peritoneal tissue via Schiff-base linkages, prolonging local drug exposure. In ovarian cancer PM models, this dual-delivery system increased CD8⁺ T-cell infiltration, reduced tumor burden more effectively than either monotherapy, and avoided systemic immune-related toxicity (Liang et al.). While PD-1 blockade is typically systemic in clinical protocols, the study highlights the potential of local co-delivery to enhance IP immune activation while lowering systemic side effects.

Hydrogel-based targeted therapy for PM

In recent years, targeted therapies that interfere with specific molecular targets have gained increasing recognition and have played a crucial role for cancer treatment. For instance, cetuximab and bevacizumab (Bev) have been approved for the treatment of advanced metastatic CRC (Ohishi et al.; Shin et al.), while trastuzumab, ramucirumab, and apatinib are recommended for advanced gastric cancer (Li et al.; Joshi and Badgwell,; Zeng and Jin,). Niraparib and Bev are used for the treatment of ovarian cancer (Mirza MR et al.). However, systemic administration is often associated with side effects, off-target accumulation, toxicity, and rapid clearance (Oliva et al.). Previous studies have shown that IP injection of targeted drugs exhibited significant anti-cancer effects in treating PM of ovarian (Hamilton et al.; Sjoquist et al.), colorectal (Jordan et al.), and breast cancers (Petrelli et al.), and effectively reduced the formation of ascites. Notably, vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) expressed by tumor cells in animal models was directly associated with malignant ascites. In addition, VEGF increased the permeability of small blood vessels, leading to a direct correlation between the amount of ascites and the level of VEGF in the effusion in animal models (Zhao et al.). Although IP injection of anti-VEGF antibodies (such as Bev) has been recommended for treating malignant ascites, free Bev is susceptible to inactivation in biological fluids. Systemic administration often fails to reach the target sites, resulting in poor pharmacokinetics, a short half-life, and the need for multiple injections (Darge et al.). To overcome these obstacles, high drug payloads through hydrogels represent a promising strategy for drug delivery.

Lee et al. developed an injectable hydrogel for sustained encapsulation and subcutaneous delivery of Bev for the treatment of metastatic CRC. When administered subcutaneously in mice, this hydrogel did not induce local or systemic inflammatory responses and completely degraded within six weeks post-injection. Compared to other groups, the hydrogel loaded with Bev significantly reduced tumor size, whereas the injection of Bev solution alone showed no therapeutic effect and resulted in extensive tumor metastasis. Furthermore, in mouse models with PM, a single subcutaneous injection of the Bev-loaded hydrogel led to a higher accumulation of Bev in tumor tissues and lower distribution in the liver, spleen, and kidneys compared to single intravenous and IP injections. This indicated a superior anti-cancer efficacy. Notably, the anti-metastatic activity generated by a single injection of the hydrogel was comparable to that of four weekly intravenous injections of Bev, significantly reducing the injection frequency (Lee et al.). Recently, Vakilian et al. investigated the effects of incorporating Bev into alginate (Alg) hydrogel for IP injection. The Alg served as a physical barrier, and the Bev acted as an anti-angiogenic agent, reducing the potential for peritoneal adhesions. The Bev-loaded Alg hydrogel was able to prevent intra-abdominal adhesion formation by reducing angiogenesis and simultaneously decreasing inflammation at the peritoneal sites (Vakilian et al.). The study results demonstrated that delivering targeted drugs via hydrogel could achieve effective release and drug accumulation within the peritoneal cavity. Eugster et al. further explored the synergistic effects of Alg hydrogel, electromagnetic droplet printing, and liposome-based nanocarriers in peritoneal drug delivery. They developed a 3D-printed composite platform for the sustained release of the tyrosine kinase inhibitor gefitinib. This platform effectively reduced the rapid clearance of gefitinib in the peritoneum, enhancing local drug concentration and efficacy (Eugster et al.).

Hydrogel-based radioactive isotopes for PM

In vivo radionuclide therapy is defined as the systemic or local delivery of radionuclides to target cancer cells, minimizing radiation exposure to non-targeted cells. Currently, common clinical examples include the use of iodine-131 (131I) for thyroid ablation, yttrium-90 (Wang et al.)Y) microparticles for treating hepatocellular carcinoma, radium-223 (223Ra) for bone metastases, and lutetium-177-DOTATATE (177Lu-DOTATATE) for neuroendocrine tumors (St. James et al.). These isotopes can remain in the body for extended period, maintaining efficacy during radioactive decay and effectively inhibiting tumor growth. Recent studies have investigated the safety of IP injection of Radspherin® (224 vanadium-labeled Ms). Preliminary results demonstrated homogeneous IP distribution across multiple dose levels, favorable safety, and no dose-limiting toxicities or severe adverse events (Larsen et al.). Nevertheless, radionuclide therapy faces intrinsic challenges, including the need to balance radiation dose with therapeutic benefit, careful selection of isotopes with suitable half-lives, and the design of carriers that can contain and confine radioactive agents to prevent systemic leakage. Hydrogels provide a promising solution by confining radionuclides within a cross-linked matrix, thereby reducing uncontrolled diffusion, extending local retention, and enabling sustained, low-dose release at the tumor site. This localized exposure is particularly advantageous for peritoneal metastases, as it maximizes tumor irradiation while minimizing off-target radiotoxicity to abdominal organs. Moreover, hydrogel matrices allow for fine-tuning of crosslinking density and degradation rates, which can be leveraged to control both release kinetics and radiation safety. For example, Huang et al. developed injectable macroscopic triblock copolymer micelles capable of locally delivering both chemotherapy drugs and radioactive isotopes, such as DOX and 131 I -labeled HA (131I-HA) (Figure 6). The study demonstrated strong anti-tumor effects of this hydrogel both in vitro and in vivo, with stable retention of 131I isotopes and DOX at tumor sites for approximately 4 weeks post-subcutaneous injection, without significant organ damage (Huang et al.).

In summary, hydrogel-mediated radionuclide delivery not only enhances therapeutic efficacy but also provides intrinsic safety advantages by confining radiation within the peritoneal cavity and allowing controlled release. These attributes position hydrogel-based systems as a promising platform for the integration of radionuclide therapy into multimodal treatment strategies for malignant PM.

Hydrogel-based herbal therapy for PM

Natural products derived from traditional Chinese medicine are gaining extensive research interest for disease prevention and treatment due to their inherent, such as excellent bioactivity, biocompatibility, and degradability (Hou et al.). Many supramolecular hydrogels loaded with natural herbal medicine have been successfully prepared for applications including inflammation alleviation (Selyutina and Polyakov,), wound repair (Deng et al.), bacterial resistance (Ouyang et al.), and tumor inhibition (Xu et al.; You et al.). Curcumin (Cur) is a natural hydrophobic polyphenolic compound and the active component of the perennial herb turmeric (Curcuma longa L). It offers a wide range of beneficial biological functions, including antioxidant and anti-inflammatory activities, as well as anti-cancer potential against various cancers (Weng and Goel,). Cur has been found to be pharmacologically safe even at very high doses in various animal models and many human clinical trials (Sharma et al.). However, low water solubility, poor bioavailability, and rapid metabolic breakdown hinder the clinical translation of the Cur's therapeutic effects (Omidi et al.). Hydrogels are ideal materials for effective delivering Cur by improving its solubility, stability, and bioavailability.

Aravind et al. pre-mixed Cur with fibrinogen or thrombin, which then coagulated to form a hydrogel. In vitro experiments demonstrated that active Cur achieved sustained release over seven days. After IP injection, the released Cur enhanced hemoglobin, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets in mice, indicating hematopoietic support and a non-toxic response. Additionally, Cur reduced tumor volume and the count of viable tumor cells, extending the survival period of the mice (Aravind et al.). Moreover, Zhang et al. developed an in situ gel-forming composite DDS for the treatment of colorectal peritoneal carcinomatosis. This composite DDS is based on polymeric micelles loaded with Cur (Cur-M) and a thermo-sensitive hydrogel, with drug loading and encapsulation efficiencies of 14.82% and 98.83%, respectively. After IP injection, the composite system significantly inhibited tumor growth and metastasis, extending the survival time of mice. Immunofluorescence staining results showed that the composite system exhibited anti-tumor cell proliferation and apoptosis-inducing effects (Zhang et al.). Additionally, honokiol (HK) is a natural product known for its potent anti-tumor activity, but the poor water solubility of HK has limited its application. To develop aqueous formulations of HK, Xie et al. synthesized biodegradable PECE thermos-sensitive hydrogels and investigated the anti-cancer effects of HK-NPs and HK-NPs-loaded thermos-sensitive hydrogels (HK-NPs/hydrogel) on ovarian cancer, both in vitro and in vivo. HK-NPs and HK-NPs/hydrogel significantly inhibited tumor growth and prolonged the survival in mice with ovarian peritoneal carcinoma, with the HK-NPs/hydrogel showing a more pronounced effect (p < 0.05) (Xie et al.).

Conclusions and future outlook

Patients with PM often face poor quality of life and survival prognosis. Due to the presence of the plasma-peritoneal barrier, IP drug concentrations are usually at lower level following systemic chemotherapy, resulting in limited therapeutic efficacy. IP-administration can increase local drug concentrations and has been proven to improve survival in PM patients. However, rapid clearance and uneven distribution of intraperitoneally injected drugs limit their therapeutic potential.

Hydrogel-based DDS have demonstrated significant advantages in the treatment of PM. Hydrogels possess excellent biocompatibility and degradability and can be engineered for sustained drug release by modifying their physicochemical properties. This review summarizes the application of hydrogel delivery systems loaded with chemotherapeutic drugs, immunotherapeutic agents, targeted drugs, radioactive isotopes, and herbal medicines for IP injection. These systems not only enhance local drug concentrations but also prolong the retention time of drugs in the peritoneal cavity, reduce treatment-related side effects, and significantly improve anti-cancer efficacy. Moreover, NPs and Ms also exhibit unique benefits for drug loading. NPs can improve drug targeting, control release, protect active agents from degradation, extend therapeutic effects, and reduce off-target effects. Microparticles, with their larger surface area and higher drug loading efficiency, have slower clearance rates and longer retention times. Combining NPs or Ms with hydrogels into hybrid delivery systems can achieve more effective drug delivery.

Combination therapy within the peritoneal cavity is an effective strategy to improve anti-cancer efficacy and minimize side effects. Common combination modalities include: 1) combining two or more chemotherapeutic agents with different therapeutic effects; 2) combining chemotherapeutic agents or radiosensitizers that induce TAA release with immunologic agents; and 3) combining chemotherapeutic agents with targeted agents. It is noteworthy that immunotherapy, as a revolutionary cancer treatment, has achieved remarkable clinical success. However, challenges such as low immune response rates and severe immune-related side effects persist. To achieve optimal results in cancer immunotherapy, it is essential to have a comprehensive understanding of the immune system and tumor immunology, including mechanisms of immune evasion, immune surveillance, and cancer immunotherapy. Hydrogel-based delivery systems allow for localized and controlled release of encapsulated drugs, achieving high therapeutic indexes while mitigating side effects. In the future, further research and optimization of these systems will contribute to the development of more effective and safer cancer therapies.

Despite the promise of these strategies, several barriers to clinical translation from the experimental stage remain. Firstly, while in vitro are relatively straightforward to demonstrate, in vivo release rates and metabolism remain unclear due to the difficulty in detecting therapeutic proteins and nucleic acids (Sun et al.). Secondly, the short-term biocompatibility of hydrogels can be evaluated using animal models, but their long-term biocompatibility, especially for newly synthesized materials, cannot be guaranteed. Additionally, the clinical application of nanomaterials encounters obstacles in precise targeting due to tumor diversity and challenges in nanotechnology scalability and consistency (Deng and Zhang). Finally, establishing stable foundational research animal models is challenging because preclinical studies relying primarily on animal models cannot fully replicate human physiological conditions. Hence, more clinical trials are required to validate the safety and efficacy of these delivery strategies.

We comprehensively searched the following clinical trial databases: ClinicalTrials.gov, EudraCT, and NIPH, and have not identified any human clinical trials evaluating hydrogel-based DDS for the treatment or prevention of PM. However, clinical studies on IP drug delivery in treating PM are actively underway. For example, an open-label, single-arm phase II trial (NCT06046963) is evaluating the efficacy and safety of IP injection of Sintilimab in combination with S-1/OXA and nab-PTX as first-line treatment for advanced gastric adenocarcinoma with malignant ascites. Another phase II study (NCT06003998) is investigating the impact of IP injection of irinotecan (75 mg) combined with FOLFOX4 and Bev on patient survival and pharmacokinetic profiles. As we previously described, PIPAC has shown great potential for treating patients with extensive peritoneal effusions by optimizing drug distribution and penetration. PIPAC combines laparoscopic surgery with IP delivery of chemotherapy via a nebulizer that generates an aerosol. In a single-center phase I trial (NCT05277766), the use of anti-cancer nanomedicines further enhanced the pharmacokinetic and clinical benefits of PIPAC. The study combined nanoliposomal irinotecan (Nal-IRI) with repeat PIPAC to determine its maximum tolerated dose. We eagerly await the results of these clinical trials to validate the safety and efficacy of IP drug therapy for malignant PM. Meanwhile, we believe hydrogel-based IP drug delivery holds promise for achieving superior therapeutic outcomes.

In summary, hydrogel-based IP drug delivery strategies present a promising approach for extending drug retention, reducing treatment-induced toxicity, and enhancing anti-cancer efficacy. Beyond the development of novel formulations, the repurposing of clinically approved hydrogels such as PEG-, HA-, or Pluronic F127-based systems may accelerate regulatory translation (Clegg et al.). Integration into clinical workflows is also critical: hydrogels could be applied intraoperatively after CRS, administered post-HIPEC to sustain drug levels, or injected postoperatively for minimally invasive delivery. Future clinical application should prioritize patient groups undergoing standardized CRS/HIPEC, such as colorectal, gastric, or ovarian cancer. Importantly, the path forward requires interdisciplinary collaboration among oncologists, surgeons, biomaterials scientists, and regulatory experts to ensure safe, feasible, and effective clinical adoption. With continued refinement in release kinetics, biocompatibility, and delivery strategies, hydrogels have the potential to become a transformative therapeutic platform for peritoneal metastases.
