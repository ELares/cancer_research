# TNG260 Is a Small-Molecule CoREST Inhibitor That Sensitizes STK11-Mutant Tumors to Anti-PD-1 Immunotherapy.

## Abstract

**UNLABELLED**: Patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) with loss of the tumor suppressor gene STK11 are resistant to immune checkpoint therapies like anti-PD-1. In this study, we conducted an in vivo CRISPR screen that identified histone deacetylase 1 as a target to reverse anti-PD-1 resistance driven by loss of STK11 and developed TNG260, a potent small-molecule inhibitor of the CoREST complex with selectivity exceeding previously generated inhibitors in this class in preclinical studies. Treatment with TNG260 led to increased expression of immunomodulatory genes in STK11-deficient cancer cells. When combined with anti-PD-1, TNG260 induced immune-mediated stasis and/or regression in STK11-deficient syngeneic tumor models and autochthonous NSCLC models. In the tumors of patients with STK11-deficient cancers in a clinical trial (NCT05887492), treatment with a combination of TNG260 and pembrolizumab increased intratumoral histone acetylation, PD-L1 tumor proportion scores, and T-cell infiltration into the tumor microenvironment. This study illustrates a promising treatment strategy for addressing immune evasion in patients with STK11-mutant NSCLC.

**SIGNIFICANCE**: Targeting CoREST with TNG260 sensitizes STK11-deficient non-small cell lung cancer to anti-PD-1 immunotherapy, offering a potential treatment for patients not served by existing therapies. See related commentary by Lin and Shen, p. 3821.

Introduction

 STK11 (or LKB1) loss-of-function mutations occur in 15% to 20% of non–small cell lung cancers (NSCLC), 10% of carcinoma of unknown primary, 5% of cervical/endocervical cancers, and lower frequencies in other cancer types. In NSCLC, patients whose tumors have loss of STK11 have lower response rates to standard of care, including immune checkpoint antibodies such as anti–PD-1. In a cohort of patients with KRAS-mutant lung adenocarcinoma treated with anti–PD-(L)1, those with STK11 mutations had an overall response rate (ORR) of 11.6% compared with 32.4% for patients with wild-type STK11. Overall survival in the same cohort for STK11-mutant patients was 6.2 months compared with 17.3 months in patients with wild-type STK11. Other studies have shown similar negative effects of STK11 mutations on progression-free and overall survival. Hence, there is an urgent medical need to identify novel therapeutic agents for this patient population.

 STK11 deficiency in human tumors and preclinical models contributes to immune evasion, leading to low response rates to immune checkpoint therapy. STK11-mutant tumors are characterized by lower T-cell infiltration, higher levels of immune-suppressive neutrophils, and low levels of antigen presentation compared with those wild-type for STK11, contributing to their immune evasive phenotype. Lung adenocarcinomas with STK11 loss have also been shown to have an altered epigenetic profile. For example, it has been observed that STING is silenced in STK11-deficient tumors, which contributes to their low immunogenicity.

Using the MC38 syngeneic mouse model, which is classically anti–PD-1 sensitive, we found that knockout of STK11 accelerated tumor growth in immunocompetent animals and drove resistance to anti–PD-1 treatment. We employed this model to perform an unbiased, in vivo CRISPR screen to discover novel targets for STK11-mutant cancer. We determined that knockout of histone deacetylase 1 (HDAC1) was sufficient to reverse anti–PD-1 resistance caused by loss of STK11.

HDACs are divided into four classes and are comprised of 11 zinc-dependent enzymes. Hydroxamic acids such as vorinostat or panobinostat inhibit most or all of these isoforms.

Benzamide inhibitors like entinostat are selective for class-I HDACs, which include HDAC1 to HDAC3 and HDAC8. Class-I HDAC enzymes participate in large, multiprotein corepressor complexes, including the HDAC1/2 complexes CoREST, NuRD, MiDAC, and Sin3, and the HDAC3-containing complex NCoR. In addition to selectivity for class-I HDACs, benzamides have been shown to have selectivity for particular corepressor complexes.

As HDAC1 knockout was sufficient to reverse resistance to anti–PD-1, we sought to develop a more selective inhibitor that could overcome the toxicities seen with drugs that target multiple HDAC isoforms. Here, we discovered and developed TNG260, a novel, selective, small-molecule inhibitor of CoREST deacetylase activity. TNG260 inhibits HDAC1 in the CoREST complex and spares other class-I HDAC complexes, including the HDAC3-containing complex NCoR. TNG260 is the first CoREST-selective inhibitor to be developed for the treatment of cancer.

We sought to characterize how TNG260 sensitized STK11-deficient cells to anti–PD-1 treatment. We found that CoREST inhibition by TNG260 directly stimulates immune-activating programs through promoter/enhancer spreading specifically in STK11-deficient tumors and in a cancer cell–intrinsic manner. In STK11-deficient syngeneic, allograft, and genetically engineered mouse models (GEMM), TNG260 treatment changed gene expression in tumor cells, yielding a more active immune microenvironment. These changes lead to complete or near-complete tumor regressions when combined with anti–PD-1. Paired biopsies collected from a phase I/II clinical trial (NCT05887492) that is evaluating TNG260 in combination with pembrolizumab in patients with STK11-mutated solid tumors demonstrated CoREST target engagement and a more active tumor microenvironment (TME). These findings identify a new potential treatment path for patients with cancers who do not typically respond to anti–PD-1–based standard-of-care therapies.

Materials and Methods

Cell culture

Annual short tandem repeat profiling and monthly Mycoplasma (MycoAlert Plus, Lonza, #LT07-701) testing were performed on cell lines used for this study. Cells were revived and minimally passaged prior to in vivo studies. A549 and MC38 cells were maintained in DMEM supplemented with 10% FBS (Sigma, #12306C). The KL and KP cell lines were isolated and passaged from tumor nodules from intranasally CRE-recombinase–induced KL (KRAS+/LSL-G12D; LKB1fl/fl) and KP (KRAS+/LSL-G12D; Trp53fl/fl) GEMMs. Both KL and KP cells were authenticated using PCR and Western blotting, as previously published, and were maintained in RPMI-1640 medium (Gibco, #11875) supplemented with 10% FBS (Sigma, #12306C) and 1% antibiotic–antimycotic (Gibco, #15240062). These adherent cells were detached with 0.25% trypsin/EDTA (Gibco, #25200056) for passaging. All cell lines were maintained in a cell culture incubator at 37°C, 95% humidified air, and 5% CO2 atmosphere.

DNA constructs and cell line engineering

Lentivirus was generated by transiently transfecting 293T cells with lentiviral packaging mix, Lipofectamine transfection reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific, L3000015), and plasmids diluted in Opti-MEM (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 31985062). Virus was collected from the cell supernatant after transfection and filtered with a 0.45-μm filter. Cells were infected with lentivirus and polybrene and selected with the appropriate antibiotic. BPCas9-expressing MC38 or CT26 cells were infected with lentivirus containing a nontargeting control single-guide RNA (sgRNA) or an STK11-targeting guide to generate MC38_sgStk11 or CT26_sgStk11 cell lines for in vivo pharmacology studies.

 In vivo CRISPR screen

The target identification screen was performed with two sequential lentiviral infections. The first was a viral sgRNA library targeting the druggable genome. MC38 cells were first infected with the sgRNA library. The sgRNA library–containing MC38 cells were then divided into two groups and infected with an all-in-one viral vector containing Cas9 and either a nontargeting control sgRNA or an STK11-targeting sgRNA. Infected cells were injected subcutaneously into C57BL/6 mice (N = 30), nude mice (N = 15), or maintained in culture. When tumors in C57BL/6 mice reached a volume of 50 mm3, mice were treated with either anti-IgG2a (Bio X Cell, #BE0089) or anti–PD-1 (Bio X Cell, #BE0146) at 5 mg/kg twice per week. Tumors were collected and processed for next-generation sequencing when the average tumor volume of the anti-IgG2α group reached 1,000 mm3.

Statistical analysis of CRISPR screen

FASTQ files were mapped to the reference file of guide sequences using custom Python scripts. After mismatches and low-quality reads were removed, only perfectly aligned reads were included in further analysis. The file of raw read count of sgRNAs for each sample and design matrix files were created as inputs for Marek MLE analysis. Beta scores and FDR-adjusted P values were estimated by 100 rounds of permutation of the dataset.

To derive the relative abundance of each guide in each sample, the raw counts were normalized by the ratio between the median of nontargeting control guides of the sample and the median of nontargeting control guide of plasmid sample. The normalized log2-fold change (Log2FC_plasmid) for each guide of each treatment group was computed by averaging the log2 ratio of relative abundance between each sample in the treatment group to their relative abundance in the plasmid sample. The normalization assumes that nontargeting control guides have no effect or association with the cancer cell survival in vivo or in vitro. The Log2FC_plasmid for guides in the gene of interest was plotted using the ggplot2 package in R.

Evaluation of HDAC potency by cellular NanoBRET assay

HDAC1, HDAC2, HDAC3, HDAC6, and HDAC10 were profiled by cellular NanoBRET Target Engagement Assay (Promega, N2170) in HEK293 cells according to the provided protocol. Test compounds and the supplied tracer molecule were incubated with cells expressing Nanoluc-tagged HDAC enzyme (Promega, N2170) for 3 hours before evaluating BRET signal. IC50s were determined by plotting the BRET ratio by test article concentration. Sourcing of test articles is listed in Supplementary Methods.

HDAC complex profiling

HDAC complex profiling was performed according to an established protocol from OmicScouts, GmbH. Sin3, NuRD, NCoR, and CoREST complexes were immunoprecipitated from A549 cells using antibodies targeting Sin3A, MTA3, HDAC3, and LSD1, respectively. Complexes were incubated for 3 hours at room temperature with a dose response of the indicated compound and evaluated for deacetylase activity using a fluorescent HDAC activity assay (Active Motif, 53200). Fluorescence values were plotted in GraphPad Prism to establish dose–response curves and to determine the IC50s of compounds with each complex.

RNA extraction followed by bulk RNA sequencing

KL and KP cells, cultured and grown in a monolayer to ∼70% confluency, were subjected to treatment with 1.0 μmol/L of TNG260 (Tango Therapeutics) or 1% DMSO for 7 days. Subsequently, cell monolayers were washed with ice-cold 1X PBS, scraped, and pelleted. Total genomic RNA was extracted from these pellets using the RNeasy Plus Mini Kit (Qiagen, #74136). Read qualities were evaluated using FASTQC (Babraham Institute), and reads were mapped to the GRCm38 (GENCODE M25) reference genome using STAR program with default parameters. Read counts, transcripts per million, and fragments per kilobase of transcript per million fragments mapped were calculated using RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) by the expectation-maximization program. DESeq2 in R/Bioconductor (R version 4.0.4) was used to identify differentially expressed genes. All plots were generated using customized R scripts. In addition, a customized gene set associated with indicated metabolic pathways was established and a heatmap was generated on the basis of their normalized expression values.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing and analysis

Approximately 50 to 100 mg of pulverized tumors, or 20 × 106 cells, was cross-linked with 1% formaldehyde in PBS for 10 to 12 minutes at room temperature, quenched with 5 mg/mL BSA in PBS, and then washed twice with cold PBS. Cell pellets were resuspended in 1 mL lysis buffer [50 mmol/L Tris-HCl, pH 8.1, 10 mmol/L EDTA, 1% SDS, and 1 × protease inhibitor cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific)] and sonicated with a Covaris M220 sonicator for 25 and 22 minutes for tumors and cell lines, respectively, to obtain chromatin fragment lengths of 100 to 1,000 bp. Fragments were evaluated using the Bioanalyzer DNA High Sensitivity kit (Agilent). Fragmented chromatin was diluted in immunoprecipitation buffer (20 mmol/L Tris-HCl pH 8.1, 150 mmol/L NaCl, 2 mmol/L EDTA, and 1% Triton X-100) and incubated overnight at 4°C with Protein G magnetic beads (Dynabeads: Life Technologies) that had been preincubated with anti-H3K9ac (Abcam, ab4441) antibody. Immunoprecipitants were washed six times with wash buffer (50 mmol/L HEPES pH 7.6, 0.5 mol/L LiCl, 1 mmol/L EDTA, 0.7% Na deoxycholate, and 1% NP-40) and twice with TE buffer. Immunoprecipitated and immunoprecipitation input was treated with RNase A and proteinase K on the beads, recovered in 1% SDS and 0.1 mol/L NaHCO3 over a period of 6 hours at 65°C, and purified with DNA clean and concentrator-25 (Zymo Research). Up to 10 ng of DNA was used for the library construction using the NEBNext Ultra II DNA Library Prep Kit (NEB, E7645). Sequencing was performed on NovaSeq (Illumina) for 150 nucleotides from paired ends according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP-seq) peaks were analyzed as follows. After sequenced reads for both ChIP and input samples were adapter trimmed with trimmomatic, aligned using bowtie2 (-k 1) to the mm10 reference genome, and filtered for ENCODE blacklist (ENCFF547MET) and repeat masker, histone K9 acetylated regions were called by MACS/2.1.0 (-f BAMPE). The resulting shifted ChIP signal bigwig files were visualized in Integrative Genomics Viewer browser. Variably lysine 9 residue on histone H3 (H3K9)–acetylated regions were detected by Haystack and variable regions were then k-means (k = 6) clustered and visualized using deepTools. Aggregate signal around transcription start sites (TSS) for differentially acetylated regions was plotted using deepTools. Differentially acetylated sites were further identified by the modified MAnorm algorithm and annotated using an R package ChIPseeker with TxDb.Mmusculus.UCSC.mm10.knownGene. Enriched ontology was identified and visualized using an R package ClusterProfiler.

Western blotting

Protein lysates were generated by lysing cells or frozen tumor tissue in RIPA buffer. Samples were normalized by protein concentration using the Rapid Gold BCA Protein Assay Kit (Pierce, A53225). SDS-PAGE was run using NuPAGE 4% to 12% Bis-Tris Midi Protein Gels (Invitrogen, WG1402BOX). Antibodies and their dilutions for Western blotting are provided in Supplementary Table S1.

Biochemical assay for deacetylase activity

Recombinant HDAC1 (#50051), HDAC2 (#50002), and HDAC3/NCor2 (#50003) enzymes were purchased from BPS Bioscience. Assay reagents for the detection of HDAC enzyme activity (Fluor de Lys; Green Substrate and Developer kits) were purchased from Enzo Life Science. Enzymes were preincubated with compound for 3 hours at room temperature. After 3 hours, the reaction was initiated using substrate from the Fluor de Lys kit. Plates were read for fluorescence on a SpectraMax plate reader (Molecular Devices).

 In vivo pharmacology

All pharmacology protocols were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of WuXi AppTec and Crown Bioscience following the guidance of the Association of Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care. After 7 days of acclimatization, 0.5 million MC38_sgNTC, MC38_sgStk11, or CT26_sgStk11 tumor cells in 0.1 mL of PBS were injected subcutaneously into the right flank of each animal. Once the tumors reached 50 mm3 in size, animals were randomized to N = 8 groups. TNG260 was administered by oral gavage once daily in a homogeneous solution of 5% DMA + 30% polyethylene glycol 400 + 65% of 30% HP-β-CD in water. Vorinostat was synthesized for in vivo studies and administered by oral gavage at 100 mg/kg every 2 days. Anti-IgG2a (Bio X Cell, clone: BE0089) and anti–PD-1 (Bio X Cell, clone: RMP1–14) were administered by intraperitoneal injection at 10 mg/kg, biweekly. Tumor volume was measured using a caliper and calculated as (length × width × width)/2.

Statistics

Tumor volume data were analyzed using SPSS 17.0. Comparisons with vehicle control were assessed via one-way ANOVA, followed by Games–Howell (equal variances not assumed) or Dunnett (equal variances assumed) tests. Survival data were analyzed using GraphPad Prism 6.0. Comparisons between groups were carried out with the log-rank test (*, P ≤ 0.05; **, P ≤ 0.01; ***, P ≤ 0.001 compared with isotype control; #, P ≤ 0.05; ##, P ≤ 0.01, and ###, P ≤ 0.001 compared with anti–PD-1). The Luminex data were analyzed by one-way ANOVA. Comparisons between groups were carried out with the Games–Howell test (equal variances not assumed) or Dunnett test (equal variances assumed). All groups were compared with the control group (*, P < 0.05; **, P < 0.01; ***, P < 0.001). Error bars represent SEM.

MRI imaging and quantification

Intranasal Cre-induced GEMMs were monitored for lung tumor nodule development via MRI of the lung fields. Briefly, the mice were anesthetized with isoflurane, and 16 consecutive thoracic sections were scanned using a 3T BioSpec USR70/30 horizontal bore system (Bruker). Images were acquired via Paravision software. MRI signal acquisition was adjusted with respiratory and cardiac cycles to reduce motion blurs. A 3D slicer software was used to measure and quantify the volume of the lung tumor nodules.

Animal studies

The Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at the New York University Grossman School of Medicine reviewed and approved all the animal studies conducted at New York University. Six- to seven-week-old female and male B6(Cg)-Tyrc−2J/J (B6-albino) mice (The Jackson Laboratory, strain #000058) were injected subcutaneously with 1 × 106 KL or KP cells, respectively, on their right flanks. A slide caliper was used to measure tumor length and width, which were used to calculate tumor volume as (length × width2)/2. Mice were randomized and divided into six treatment groups once the tumor volume reached approximately 100 mm3.

The genetically engineered LKB1/STK11 loss-of-function (Lkb1fl/fl) GEMMs were crossed with mice with mutated Kras (Kras+/LSL-G12D), as reported earlier. Intranasal induction with Cre recombinase was performed with 2 × 107 plaque-forming units. of Ad5mSPC-Cre (University of Iowa Viral Vector Core). MRI imaging was used to monitor these mice after induction for lung tumor nodule development. Once the lung tumor nodule reached approximately 50 mm3 in size, usually after 5 to 7 weeks, the mice were randomized and divided into four treatment groups. Mice were dosed daily with TNG260 (75 mg/kg of body weight) or the vehicle via oral gavage. They also received 200 mg anti–PD-1 (Bio X Cell, #BE0146) and isotype control anti-IgG2a (Bio X Cell, BE0089) antibodies three times a week via intraperitoneal injection. The maintenance, care, and treatment of all mice were performed in accordance with set standards of the Association for the Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care, International, the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, and all local and federal animal welfare laws.

Pharmacodynamic profiling of KL and KP subcutaneous tumors

The H3K9 acetylation mark from tumor tissues was determined by Western blotting. Briefly, proteins were extracted by homogenizing flash-frozen subcutaneous tumors in RIPA lysis buffer and were quantified by bicinchoninic acid assay. Therein, 25 μg of the protein lysate was loaded in each lane of an SDS-PAGE gel and acetylation of H3K9 was detected using acetyl-histone H3 (Lys9; C5B11) rabbit mAb, Cell Signaling Technology, 9649S). For NanoString assays, each flash-frozen tumor tissue was homogenized in RLT buffer (Qiagen, RNeasy kit) with antifoam reagent using the GentleMACS Octo Dissociator. The lysate was clarified by centrifugation, and the RNA was isolated using the Qiagen RNeasy kit per the manufacturer’s instructions, followed by quantification via NanoDrop. Then, 100 ng of each RNA sample was prepared for NanoString analysis using the murine NanoString IO 360 panel with the master kit.

Flow cytometry

MC38_sgStk11 tumors were treated for 7 days with either anti-IgG2α (10 mg/kg), TNG260 (10 mg/kg) + anti-IgG2α (10 mg/kg), anti–PD-1 (10 mg/kg), or TNG260 (10 mg/kg) + anti–PD-1 (10 mg/kg). Five mice were randomized across the four treatment groups. After tumor harvesting, cells were labeled with Zombie NIR for live/dead staining and with CD45 and CD3 to identify T cells. Subpopulations were labeled with CD4 or CD8 to identify CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell populations. CD25 and FoxP3 double-positive T cells were identified as T regulatory (“Treg”) cells. Myeloid cells were identified by negativity for CD3 and positivity for CD11b staining. Ly6G and Ly6C positivity were used to identify polymorphonuclear myeloid-derived suppressor cells (PMN-MDSC) and monocytic myeloid-derived suppressor cells (M-MDSC), respectively. Antibodies used are included in Supplementary Table S2.

Gene expression profiling

RNA from tissue culture cells was generated using the Qiagen RNeasy kit (#74004). RNA from tumor samples was generated using TRIzol extraction (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #15596026). For human cultured cell lines, gene expression profiling was performed using the NanoString IO 360 panel for human samples (#115000214). Mouse tumors were profiled using the NanoString IO 360 panel for mouse samples (#115000251). Data analysis was performed using NanoString nSolver software.

IFNγ secretion assay

IFNγ was detected in tissue culture media in tumor cell/peripheral blood mononuclear cell (PBMC) coculture as part of a BioMAP Combo ELECT study (Eurofins). TNG260 was tested alone and in combination with pembrolizumab against H1299 cells cultured with fibroblasts and PBMCs. Cells were stimulated with T-cell receptor ligands. IFNγ was detected in cell supernatants by ELISA. To analyze IFNγ levels in the in vivo samples, protein was extracted from the tumor samples, detected using the IFNγ Mouse ProcartaPlex Simplex Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, #EPX01A-20606-901), and read on the Bio-Plex 200 system (Bio-Rad, #171000201).

Hematopoietic colony formation assay

Colony formation unit assays were performed by culturing erythroid and myeloid progenitor cells in MethoCult media containing a dose response of TNG260 for 14 days. After this time, hematopoietic colonies were scored by visual inspection and an automated colony-forming unit assay reader (STEMCELL Technologies).

Patient samples and pharmacodynamic profiling

Patient tumor specimens were acquired from patients enrolled in the NCT05887492 study with approval by the Institutional Review Boards at individual institutions participating in the trial. All patients provided written informed consent, and studies were conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. Formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded (FFPE) slides were analyzed by hematoxylin and eosin to identify tumor regions on slides. Serial sections were used to evaluate histone acetylation, PD-L1 tumor proportion score (TPS; 22C3 and 535BPQ), and T-cell infiltration. Acetylated histone H4 lysine 5 (Lys5) and nonacetylated histone H4 Lys5 were evaluated by mass spectroscopy from FFPE slides. Acetyl H4K5 was divided by the sum of detected nonacetylated and acetylated histone H4K5 to determine a percentage of acetylation. Multiplex immunofluorescence was performed using the PhenoImager platform on a serial section of FFPE tissue for DAPI, cytokeratin (CK), CD3, CD4, and CD8. Intratumoral regions were defined by hematoxylin and eosin and verified by CK staining. Helper T cells were defined as CD3/CD4 positive, and cytotoxic T cells were identified as CD3/CD8 positive. Copositive cells were quantified within intratumoral regions and calculated by mm2 of tissue.

Results

STK11 loss causes immune evasion

Previously, we conducted an in vivo CRISPR screen in the MC38 mouse model to identify genes that, when knocked out, drove immune evasion and resistance to anti–PD-1 treatment. STK11 was one of several identified genes in the screen that caused resistance to immune checkpoint blockade. This finding is consistent with clinical data that identified STK11 loss-of-function alterations as a driver of immune evasion and resistance to immune checkpoint blockade in NSCLC tumors. To validate this finding, STK11 was knocked out by infecting the MC38 syngeneic mouse model with an sgRNA-targeting Stk11 (MC38_sgStk11), whereas a control cell line was infected with nontargeting control sgRNA (MC38_sgNTC). Appropriate knockout of STK11 was confirmed by Western blotting (Supplementary Fig. S1A). MC38_sgNTC tumors were sensitive to anti–PD-1 in C57BL/6 animals and exhibited tumor stasis on treatment (Fig. 1A). MC38_sgStk11 tumors grew more rapidly in immunocompetent mice compared with nude mice, consistent with its roles in tumor suppression and immune evasion (Fig. 1B). Although the MC38 model is colorectal cancer, in which STK11 mutations are infrequent, STK11 knockout in this model was sufficient to drive resistance to anti–PD-1 treatment compared with MC38_sgNTC (Fig. 1B), consistent with previously published data in other models. MC38_sgStk11 tumors had lower ratios of CD8+ T cells and CD4+ T cells to Treg cells than MC38_sgNTC tumors (Supplementary Fig. 1B), suggesting that STK11 knockout causes decreased T-cell activity in the TME. Additionally, STK11 knockout led to an increase in intratumoral MDSCs (Supplementary Fig. S1C), indicating that STK11 knockout was sufficient to induce an immune-suppressive environment in MC38 tumors in a manner consistent with STK11 loss-of-function in human tumors. An exemplar gating strategy for identification of these cell types is provided in Supplementary Fig. S1D. The MC38_sgStk11 system, in which immune evasion is driven by only STK11 loss, is useful for discovery of target genes specific to the STK11-mutant context.

HDAC1 knockout reverses STK11-mediated immune evasion

The isogenic MC38 tumor models were employed in an in vivo CRISPR screen to identify target genes that could resensitize STK11-deficient tumors to anti–PD-1 treatment. Cas9-expressing MC38 cells were infected with a library of 400 nontargeting sgRNAs and 3,176 sgRNAs targeting 794 genes. The library was designed to include 135 essential genes, such as PLK1, to evaluate the range of sgRNA dropout during the screen. Controls in the library also included 12 tumor suppressor genes and 13 genes with a known role in tumor-intrinsic immune responses. The remaining 634 genes were identified as having a high “druggability” score, which were selected to identify target enzymes that have the potential to be inhibited by a small-molecule approach. MC38 cells containing the sgRNA library were subsequently infected with either sgNTC or sgStk11, and appropriate knockout was confirmed by Western blotting (Supplementary Fig. S2A). MC38_sgNTC and MC38_sgStk11 were cultured in vitro or under different levels of immune pressure in vivo using either BALB/c nude or C57BL/6 mice. Tumor-bearing C57BL/6 mice were divided into two cohorts and treated with either anti-IgG2a or anti–PD-1 at 5 mg/kg twice weekly (Fig. 1C). Tumors were collected for gDNA extraction after control animals reached an average tumor volume of 1,000 mm3 and analyzed for sgRNA abundance.

After median normalization, nontargeting control sgRNAs in the library did not have significant depletion in any of the treatment groups (Supplementary Fig. S2B). Knockout of PLK1, an essential gene, resulted in significant depletion in all conditions (Supplementary Fig. S2C), indicating that adequate sgRNA depletion could be detected during the screening time period. As an additional control for anti–PD-1 treatment, knockout of CD274 was strongly depleted in C57BL/6 animals treated with anti-IgG but not with anti–PD-1 in MC38_sgNTC tumors. MC38_sgStk11 tumors showed modest depletion of cells containing CD274 knockout (Supplementary Fig. S2D), consistent with STK11’s role in anti–PD(L)-1 resistance.

First, we sought to identify target genes that, when knocked out, would resensitize STK11-null tumors to anti–PD-1 in immunocompetent mice versus athymic nude mice. This would represent potential drug targets that would require adaptive immunity to kill STK11-deficient tumors in combination with anti–PD-1, without causing tumor-intrinsic cell death (Fig. 1D; Supplementary Table S3). Next, the STK11 isogenic tumors were compared from C57BL/6 animals treated with anti–PD-1 to identify targets that would preferentially cause immune-mediated cell killing in STK11-deficient tumors (Fig. 1E; Supplementary Table S4). We sought target genes that were significantly depleted in both comparisons, as these would represent targets that, when inhibited, could reverse immune evasion driven by STK11 knockout and restore sensitivity to anti–PD-1. HDAC1 was significantly depleted in both screen comparisons (Fig. 1D and E). HDAC1 sgRNAs showed more significant depletion in the MC38_sgStk11 tumor model treated with anti–PD-1 than in the STK11 wild-type model or with control antibody treatment (Supplementary Fig. S2E). Thus, we identified HDAC1 as a potential inhibitor target to reverse anti–PD-1 resistance caused by loss of STK11.

TNG260 is a novel, potent, and selective inhibitor of HDAC1 deacetylase activity

Existing drugs with a benzamide scaffold inhibit class-I HDAC enzymes, such as entinostat, mocetinostat, and tucidinostat (Fig. 2A; ref.). These drugs are approximately equipotent for HDAC1, HDAC2, and HDAC3 in a biochemical deacetylation assay (Supplementary Fig. S3A). HDAC3 was found to be required for genome maintenance and DNA replication in hematopoietic cells; therefore, HDAC3 inhibition is suspected to be responsible for bone marrow toxicity seen with nonselective HDAC inhibitors. TNG260 (Fig. 2A) was developed to increase the selectivity of the benzamide scaffold toward HDAC1/2. We hypothesize that the selectivity of TNG260 toward HDAC1/2 is mediated by the fluorophenyl and sulfoximine groups, which, among others, distinguish TNG260 from known benzamide class-I HDAC inhibitors.

In the HDAC2 co-crystal structure of TNG260 (Fig. 2B), the compound chelates the active-site Zn2+ via the benzamide carbonyl group and arylamine nitrogen. The bound Zn2+ is essential for the catalytic deacetylation of lysine. All of the heteroatoms in the benzamide core hydrogen bond to residues in the binding site: the amide N-H to Gly150, the amide carbonyl to Tyr304, and the arylamine hydrogens to His141 and His142. Aryl hydrophobic interactions in the lysine tunnel and the so-called “foot pocket” also contribute to the tight binding of TNG260 (Fig. 2B). Because of 100% sequence identity between HDAC1 and HDAC2 in the binding site, it is expected that the interactions determined from the HDAC2 co-crystal structure would be consistent between these two protein–compound complexes. The fluorophenyl group of TNG260 provides further selectivity between HDAC1/2 and HDAC3 in terms of their access to the foot pocket. Although the residues in direct proximity of this group are the same in HDAC1 to HDAC3, the position and flexibility of a leucine in the pocket (Leu139:HDAC1, Leu140:HDAC2, and Leu133:HDAC3) is affected by a nonconserved change in an adjacent residue. As depicted in the overlay in Fig. 2C, Leu133 in a reported HDAC3 crystal structure (PDB 4A69; ref.) would clash with the fluorophenyl group in the TNG260 HDAC2 structure (PDB 9NTB). For HDAC3 to accommodate the fluorophenyl group, its Leu133 should have the same orientation as Leu140 in HDAC2 but that would result in a clash with the large adjacent residue, Tyr107.

TNG260 was profiled in a biochemical assay against HDAC1, HDAC2, and HDAC3. TNG260 was found to be 230 times more potent for HDAC1 and 37 times more potent for HDAC2 compared with HDAC3 (Fig. 2D; Supplementary Fig. S3A). This stands in contrast to a nonselective HDAC inhibitor such as vorinostat, which is equipotent for HDAC1 to HDAC3 (Supplementary Fig. S3A). TNG260 did not inhibit HDAC4 to HDAC9 or HDAC11 up to 50 µmol/L, whereas a nonselective control HDAC inhibitor could potently inhibit these enzymes (Supplementary Fig. S3B).

TNG260 is a selective CoREST complex inhibitor

TNG260 treatment leads to dose-dependent accumulations of acetylated histone H3 at Lys9 (acetyl-H3K9) in MC38 cells (Fig. 2E), demonstrating inhibition of cellular histone deacetylase activity. TNG260 was profiled using a cellular NanoBRET assay for HDAC1, HDAC2, HDAC3, HDAC6, and HDAC10. Competition of TNG260 with a nonselective HDAC tracer molecule showed that TNG260 is a potent binder of HDAC1 and HDAC2 in cells with an IC50 of 0.10 and 0.17 µmol/L, respectively (Fig. 2F). Consistent with the biochemical assay data, TNG260 was 10-fold more potent for HDAC1 than HDAC3, which had an IC50 of 1.07 µmol/L (Fig. 2F). The 10-fold selectivity against HDAC3 contrasts with other class I–selective benzamide HDAC inhibitors, which have equal potencies for HDAC1 and HDAC3 in this assay (Supplementary Fig. S3A).

In cells, HDAC1, HDAC2, and HDAC3 function in large multiprotein complexes to regulate gene expression. To determine whether TNG260’s selectivity against HDAC3 was maintained with intact complexes, CoREST, Sin3, NuRD, and NCoR were immunoprecipitated from A549 cells. The four complexes were incubated with a dose response of TNG260, tucidinostat, domatinostat, or vorinostat and assayed for deacetylase activity. The pan-HDAC inhibitor vorinostat inhibited all four complexes equally (Fig. 2G). The class I–selective inhibitors tucidinostat and domatinostat had selectivity against NuRD and Sin3 (Fig. 2G), as previously described for the benzamide class of HDAC inhibitors. Consistent with this class of inhibitor, an IC50 could not be detected for NuRD or Sin3 with TNG260 (Fig. 2G). TNG260 was selective against the HDAC3-containing complex NCoR, while having a potent IC50 of 0.17 µmol/L for CoREST, indicating that selectivity against HDAC3 is maintained with intact complexes (Fig. 2G).

TNG260 resensitizes STK11-deficient tumors to anti–PD-1

To evaluate the pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics of TNG260, MC38_sgStk11 tumors were established in C57BL/6 mice. Once daily oral administration of TNG260 at 30 mg/kg resulted in stable pharmacokinetics that sufficiently covered the IC50 of HDAC1, as determined by cellular NanoBRET (Fig. 3A). In MC38_sgStk11 tumor tissue, dose-dependent increases in acetyl-H3K9 were detectable by Western blotting and were stable for 24 hours after dosing (Fig. 3B). Increased levels of acetyl-H3K9 were also detected in tumor with TNG260 doses as low as 10 mg/kg after 7 days of treatment (Fig. 3C).

The MC38_sgStk11 model was used to evaluate the efficacy of TNG260 in combination with anti–PD-1. Of the eight mice in the combination arm, five had complete regressions (Fig. 3D), resulting in a 100% survival rate for the duration of this experiment (>35 days, P ≤ 0.001 vs. anti–PD-1 arm; Fig. 3E). Anti–PD-1 or TNG260, as single agents, caused modest improvements in survival, with median survival times of 23 and 17 days, respectively, compared with 13 days in the vehicle group (Fig. 3E). TNG260 or anti–PD-1 resulted in 52% and 48% tumor growth inhibition (TGI), respectively, compared with the vehicle control (P = 0.001 and 0.003) after 11 days of treatment. TNG260 at 30 mg/kg, in combination with biweekly dosing of anti–PD-1, resulted in strong antitumor efficacy of 83% TGI compared with vehicle control (P < 0.001) at the same time point. The ORR with TNG260 and anti–PD-1 was 75% compared with 0% for anti–PD-1 alone (Fig. 3D).

 STK11 knockout was also performed in the CT26 syngeneic model (CT26_sgStk11) with similar results. In the CT26 model, knockout of STK11 also led to increased resistance to anti–PD-1, as expected. Single-agent administration of either TNG260 at 75 mg/kg or anti–PD-1 resulted in TGI of 39% and 8% (P = not significant), respectively, after 13 days of treatment (Supplementary Fig. S4A). Consistent with the MC38 model, the combination of TNG260 at 75 mg/kg with anti–PD-1 resulted in an 88% ORR and 73% TGI (P < 0.001; Supplementary Fig. S4A). TNG260 with anti–PD-1 also improved survival to 87.5% versus 13 days median survival for the anti–PD-1 group (P < 0.001; Fig. 3F). Together, these data demonstrate that TNG260 can reverse immune evasion and resensitize STK11-deficient tumor models to immune checkpoint blockade.

Our in vivo pharmacology studies demonstrated that efficacy with the combination of TNG260 and anti–PD-1 correlated with the unbound Caverage of TNG260 that covered the cellular IC50 of HDAC1 (Fig. 3G). In contrast, impaired tolerability was driven by exposures of TNG260 that exceeded the cellular IC50 of HDAC3 (Fig. 3G). This resulted in a therapeutic window of ∼11 fold between doses that are expected to be efficacious and doses that were predicted to decrease tolerability driven by engagement of HDAC3. This 11-fold therapeutic window exceeds the therapeutic windows offered by less-selective HDAC inhibitors.

Antitumor efficacy of TNG260 with anti–PD-1 is mediated by the immune system

The five mice that had complete responses to the combination of TNG260 with anti–PD-1 (Fig. 3D) were observed off-treatment and evaluated for tumor regrowth. All five mice remained tumor free off-treatment for 21 days. At this time, the cured mice and a treatment-naïve cohort were reinjected with MC38_sgStk11 tumor cells. The previously cured animals rejected the tumor cells, whereas the naïve cohort grew tumors as expected (Fig. 3H). The rejection of tumor cells by the previously cured animals suggests that they retained an immune memory for the MC38_sgStk11 cell type.

To directly evaluate the role of the adaptive immune system in the efficacy of TNG260, we performed a head-to-head comparison of an immunocompetent C57BL/6 model with an athymic BALB/c nude mouse. Although the expected efficacy was seen in the immune-intact model (Fig. 3I), TNG260, alone or in combination with anti–PD-1, had no antitumor efficacy in the nude mouse (Fig. 3J). These data indicate that an intact T-cell compartment is required for the antitumor efficacy of TNG260 with anti–PD-1.

Immune profiling of MC38_sgStk11 tumors after 7 days of treatment did not show significant enrichment of T-cell populations with combination treatment (Supplementary Fig. S4B–S4D). However, depletion of Treg cells was observed with combination treatment that was not detected in either single-agent TNG260 or anti–PD-1–only arms (Supplementary Fig. S4E). Accordingly, the ratio of CD8+ effector T cells to Treg cells was significantly increased with combination treatment (Supplementary Fig. S4F). The decrease of Treg cells with the combination of TNG260 and anti–PD-1 corresponded with a decrease in the mRNA expression of Treg-recruiting chemokines CCL1 and CCL22 (Supplementary Fig. S4G). We measured CCL22 levels from cell media collected from a coculture system of PBMCs with a human NCI-H520 STK11 isogenic pair and found that STK11 knockout led to increased levels of CCL22 detected in coculture media, which were reduced with the treatment of TNG260, consistent with the CCL2 gene expression findings (Supplementary Fig. S4H).

The higher ratio of effector T cells to Treg cells suggested a more active immune microenvironment with combination treatment. An increase in IFNγ levels was observed in MC38_sgStk11 tumors treated with the TNG260 and anti–PD-1 combination, indicating that a more active immune microenvironment was established (Supplementary Fig. S4I).

These data suggest that a combination of TNG260 with anti–PD-1 enhanced IFNγ signaling and shifted the effector-to-Treg cell ratio to be more active. The lack of antitumor efficacy seen with TNG260 and anti–PD-1 in the nude mouse model, together with the demonstration of immune memory in the immunocompetent model, supports the hypothesis that tumor cell killing with combination treatment is mediated by T cells.

TNG260 has an improved therapeutic index over vorinostat

Vorinostat is an FDA-approved nonselective HDAC inhibitor. To compare TNG260 with the approved HDAC inhibitor vorinostat, we selected doses of TNG260 and vorinostat that provided equivalent increases in acetyl-H3K9 in A549 cells in vitro (Supplementary Fig. S5A). Profiling by the NanoString IO 360 panel of A549 cells in vitro showed that vorinostat significantly influenced the expression of 217 genes compared with a more selective set of 67 genes by TNG260 (Supplementary Fig. S5B). The top-ranked gene ontology (GO) categories for vorinostat included cell proliferation and DNA damage, which are consistent with its known mechanism as a cytotoxic compound. TNG260, however, regulated genes in the ontology categories of immune cell adhesion and migration, matrix remodeling and metastasis, and antigen presentation (Supplementary Fig. S5B), consistent with its discovery as an immune sensitizer.

We also performed a proteomics study to compare TNG260, vorinostat, and the class I–selective HDAC inhibitor entinostat. A549 cells were treated with doses of compound that led to similar increases in acetyl-H3K9 (Supplementary Fig. S5A). Acetylated peptides were enriched with a pan acetylation antibody and evaluated by mass spectrometry. Entinostat led to a significant change in acetylation of the 223 unique substrates, the highest of the three drugs evaluated, but also shared 201 substrates in common with TNG260 (Supplementary Fig. S5C; Supplementary Table S5). Of these shared substrates, the correlation in the change of acetylation of these substrates by entinostat and TNG260 was high (R = 0.69). Of 224 significantly changed substrates with vorinostat, only 62 were shared with TNG260, likely because of vorinostat affecting HDAC enzymes outside of the nucleus. The fold change seen in acetylation for shared substrates did not strongly correlate between vorinostat and TNG260 (R = 0.29; Supplementary Fig. S5D). TNG260 affected the acetylation of the fewest number of substrates, which largely overlapped with those influenced by a class I–selective inhibitor, entinostat, consistent with TNG260 being a more selective benzamide inhibitor.

To test whether a less selective HDAC inhibitor would provide equivalent efficacy in combination with anti–PD-1, a head-to-head comparison was performed between vorinostat and TNG260 in the MC38_sgStk11 model. We selected vorinostat for this study as FDA-approved human dosing and exposure information is available. The appropriate dosage of vorinostat was selected for the mouse study by body surface area conversion from the FDA-approved dosage of vorinostat of 400 mg daily. Based on mouse exposure and the potency of vorinostat against HDAC1, this dosage resulted in a 0.3-fold inhibition of the HDAC1 IC50 in mice (Supplementary Fig. S5E). Exceeding this dosage to improve coverage of HDAC1 would exceed the FDA-approved and tolerated exposures of vorinostat in humans. Comparatively, efficacious doses of TNG260 covered the HDAC1 IC50 by 3.5-fold (Supplementary Fig. S5E).

The combination of vorinostat with anti–PD-1 caused significant TGI in the MC38_sgStk11 model (Supplementary Fig. S5F); however, the ORR for vorinostat with anti–PD-1 was 12.5%. In contrast, the TNG260 combination provided strong antitumor efficacy at 30 mg/kg combined with anti–PD-1 and an ORR of 87.5% (Supplementary Fig. S5G). As the CRISPR screening results indicated that the HDAC enzyme primarily required for reversing immune evasion is HDAC1 (Fig. 1D and E), the limited efficacy seen with the vorinostat combination is consistent with the relatively low coverage of HDAC1 compared with TNG260.

Previously developed HDAC inhibitors, including vorinostat, have toxicity profiles that include bone marrow toxicities like anemia and neutropenia. We profiled TNG260 against hematopoietic progenitor cells to determine whether TNG260 was cytotoxic to these lineages. At doses relevant to efficacious exposures in vivo in combination with anti–PD-1, TNG260 was not directly cytotoxic to these cell types (Supplementary Fig. S6A). To directly compare with less-selective HDAC inhibitors, we normalized the IC50 of each compound against myeloid and erythroid cells against its potency with HDAC1 (Supplementary Fig. S6B). TNG260 had the highest erythroid and myeloid IC50-to-potency ratio compared with other HDAC inhibitors tested (Supplementary Fig. S6C).

TNG260 drives anti–PD-1–mediated antitumor efficacy in Stk11-deficient mouse models

We investigated the efficacy of TNG260 alone and in combination with anti–PD-1 in Stk11-deficient allograft and syngeneic models of lung adenocarcinoma. After 24 days of treatment in the KRAS+/LSL-G12D; STK11fl/fl (KL) allograft model, the combination of anti–PD-1 and 75 mg/kg TNG260 caused a significant arrest in subcutaneous tumor volume (by ∼5.5-fold; Fig. 4A). Combination treatment led to a 100% survival rate to the end of this experiment.

Western blotting with flash-frozen tumors revealed that the acetyl-H3K9 was significantly increased by ∼threefold in KL subcutaneous tumors from the mice receiving TNG260 monotherapy or anti–PD-1 combination treatment (Supplementary Fig. S7A), indicating engagement of the CoREST target. Conversely, in the KRAS+/LSL-G12D; TP53fl/fl (KP) allograft model, treatment with TNG260 in combination with anti–PD-1 did not affect the subcutaneous tumor growth (Fig. 4B), despite increased acetyl-H3K9 detected after 7 days of treatment (Supplementary Fig. S7B). As the majority of STK11 mutations in human tumors occur in lung adenocarcomina, we sought to evaluate the activity of TNG260 in an STK11-null lung cancer model growing within the lung environment. The KL GEMM utilizes inhaled adeno-cre recombinase to induce spontaneous lung tumor formation. Consistent with STK11-deficient human tumors, this lung tumor model is highly resistant to anti–PD-1 therapy. In the KL GEMM, 75 mg/kg of TNG260 with anti–PD-1 caused complete stasis of lung tumor burden compared with the single-agent treatment with either TNG260 or anti–PD-1 for the duration of the study (Fig. 4C). As the KL GEMM is highly resistant to anti–PD-1, these data show that CoREST inhibition by TNG260 sensitizes this STK11-null lung tumor model to anti–PD-1 therapy within the lung tumor environment.

Given the antitumor effects of TNG260 combined with anti–PD-1 in STK11-deficient models, and the known role of the CoREST complex, we examined the transcriptional changes to KL cells. We treated KL and KP lung adenocarcinoma cell lines with 1 µmol/L TNG260 for 7 days and performed RNA-seq. Transcriptomic analyses of TNG260-treated KL and KP lung adenocarcinoma cell lines revealed that the transcriptome of KL cells was significantly altered upon TNG260 treatment, whereas the transcriptome of KP cells did not significantly change (Fig. 4D; Supplementary Fig. S7C–S7E). Gene set enrichment analysis of the transcriptome of the TNG260-treated KL cells revealed increases in the IFNα and IFNγ response, the JAK–STAT3 pathway, and the inflammatory response (Supplementary Fig. S7F). To validate these transcriptomic findings, we evaluated a subset of genes in KL cells that were differentially expressed following treatment with 1 µmol/L TNG260 by qRT-PCR (Supplementary Fig. S7G). Consistent with RNA-seq data, we observed increased expression of the genes in TNG260-treated KL cells, including those associated with type I and II interferon pathways including IFNα and IFNγ: C1S1, LY6E, IFI27, IFIH1, B2M, IFITM1, IFITM3, PSMB8, NFKBIA, and NFKB1. Key components of the JAK/STAT3 signaling axis, including STAT3 and SOCS3, were upregulated upon TNG260 treatment, indicating engagement of cytokine-mediated transcriptional programs. Additionally, pro-inflammatory genes such as CXCL5, TNFRSF1B, and CD14 were elevated, supporting activation of inflammatory pathways, mediated by TNG260. This suggests that CoREST inhibition by TNG260 induces a cancer cell–intrinsic inflammatory response.

Previous work has shown that STK11-mutant tumors have impaired antigen presentation, characterized by reduced expression of genes related to neoantigen processing and presentation. MC38_sgStk11 cells treated with TNG260 in vitro were found to have transcriptional increases in genes for MHC complex members, the immunoproteasome, and peptide loading, suggesting that CoREST inhibition by TNG260 may reverse the antigen presentation defect of STK11-mutant cells (Supplementary Fig. S8A). As genes related to neoantigen processing and presentation were increased in response to TNG260 in the syngeneic models, we specifically interrogated whether TNG260 treatment altered the expression of antigen presentation genes in the KL model. Indeed, we observed that these genes were specifically increased in the TNG260-treated KL cell line and were not strongly induced in the KP model (Fig. 4D). Together, these data suggest an increase in immunomodulation upon TNG260 treatment in the STK11-mutant context.

To determine whether TNG260 affects antigen presentation, we utilized the MC38-OVA syngeneic model engineered with STK11 knockout (MC38-OVA_sgStk11). MC38-OVA_sgStk11 cells were treated with increasing doses of TNG260 in vitro and subsequently evaluated for the display of the SIINFEKL peptide bound to H-2kb MHC protein (Supplementary Fig. S8B). TNG260 caused dose-dependent increases in presentation of the SIINFEKL peptide by MHC class I in MC38-OVA cells above the background signal of MC38 parental cells that lack the OVA protein. These data indicate that TNG260 increases antigen presentation in STK11-deficient cells. To determine whether the increased antigen presentation was sufficient to alter immune response, we performed an in vitro T-cell kill assay. MC38-OVA cells were pretreated with TNG260 and cocultured with T effector cells isolated from OT-I mice. We found that pretreatment with TNG260 resulted in enhanced recognition and killing of tumor cells by T cells after 48 hours (Supplementary Fig. S8C and S8D).

The CoREST complex can silence target gene expression via deacetylation of the histone H3K9 residue. Therefore, to mechanistically dissect the impact of CoREST inhibition by TNG260, we performed ChIP-seq for acetyl-H3K9 in TNG260-treated KL and KP cells. First, we identified a set of genomic regions (cluster 7) with increased acetyl-H3K9 following TNG260 treatment in KL cells compared with control KL cells (Fig. 4E). By contrast, CoREST inhibition did not lead to accumulation of acetyl-H3K9 at the same genomic regions (cluster 7) in KP cells, suggesting that these effects are unique to KL cells. GO terms for regions in cluster 7 were enriched for leukocyte chemotaxis and differentiation, such as chemokine Cxcl3 (Fig. 4F; Supplementary Fig. S8E), consistent with gene expression data showing that TNG260 treatment increases the expression of immunomodulatory genes. Notably, we observed a unique pattern in the accumulation of acetyl-H3K9 at genomic regions extending beyond the TSS and into the gene body in TNG260-treated KL cells but not in KP cells (Fig. 4E and F). These findings suggest a KL-specific effect on gene regulation and are consistent with previous studies that HDAC is required for restricting acetylation of gene bodies. Next, we sought to investigate how the spread of acetyl-H3K9 altered the expression of downstream genes, so we performed integrative analysis of RNA-seq and ChIP-seq. First, we defined H3K9-acetylated regions that were unique to KL cells treated with TNG260 and found that these regions were enriched for genes that were upregulated in KL cells treated with TNG260 (Fig. 4G). This suggests that acetyl-H3K9 is retained at CoREST targets when inhibited with TNG260 treatment and allows for increased expression of those target genes. Second, we examined the acetyl-H3K9 signal at the top 1,000 upregulated genes in KL cells treated with TNG260 and classified these genomic regions into six clusters. We found that the acetyl-H3K9 profiles in clusters 1, 2, 5, and 6 remained largely unchanged after TNG260 treatment, whereas we observed an accumulation of acetyl-H3K9 from the TSS of the upregulated genes into the gene bodies in clusters 3 and 4 (Supplementary Fig. S8F). GO analysis of the genes associated with clusters 3 and 4 was enriched for migration of immune cells (Fig. 4H). Together, these analyses revealed an altered KL-specific chromatin landscape and immunomodulatory gene expression profile upon CoREST inhibition by TNG260.

Finally, to investigate whether accumulation of acetyl-H3K9 at gene bodies of genes associated with inflammation was consistent in vivo, we performed ChIP-seq for acetyl-H3K9 in KL and KP subcutaneous tumors. We found that upon TNG260 and anti–PD-1 combination treatment, KL tumors exhibited significantly increased acetyl-H3K9 in a larger number of genomic regions compared with tumors from vehicle-treated KL mice and combination-treated KP mice (Fig. 4I). Consistent with the in vitro data, we observed an accumulation of acetyl-H3K9 at regions extending the TSS and into the gene body at differentially accessible regions (Fig. 4I), and these regions were enriched for immune cell differentiation and activation (Supplementary Fig. S8G). Taken together, CoREST inhibition by TNG260 induced cell-intrinsic transcriptional reprogramming that renders STK11-mutant cancer cells susceptible to anti–PD-1 treatment.

TNG260 with pembrolizumab remodeled the immune microenvironment of STK11-mutant tumors

NCT05887492 is a phase I/II clinical trial evaluating the safety and tolerability, pharmacokinetics, and antineoplastic activity of TNG260 combined with pembrolizumab in patients with advanced STK11-mutated solid tumors. Ten biopsies were obtained from patients with STK11-mutant tumors after receiving two cycles of TNG260 in combination with pembrolizumab and were compared with biopsies collected prior to treatment (Supplementary Table S6). Doses of TNG260 at 80 and 120 mg led to increases in acetylation of histone H4 at lysine 5 in tumor tissue, whereas 40 mg of TNG260 did not induce the pharmacodynamic mark (Fig. 5A). Increased histone acetylation is consistent with TNG260 as an inhibitor of the CoREST complex.

At doses in which TNG260 exhibited CoREST target engagement (80 and 120 mg), the TPS of PD-L1 was increased in on-treatment biopsies compared with pretreatment, suggesting that a more active tumor immune microenvironment was present after combination therapy (Fig. 5B). Given that the biopsies treated with 40 mg of TNG260 did not increase PD-L1 TPS, we attribute the effects at 80 and 120 mg to TNG260. To characterize the immune microenvironment of these tumors, we performed multiplex immunofluorescence on FFPE tissue from the paired biopsies to identify active T-cell populations in proximity to tumor cells. We found that four of six patients had increased intratumoral helper T cells (CD3+/CD4+) at the 80 and 120 mg doses of TNG260 (Fig. 5C). The proportion of intratumoral Treg cells (CD3+/CD4+/FOXP3+) was not notably altered by TNG260 treatment (Supplementary Fig. S9). Intratumoral cytotoxic T cells (CD3+/CD8+) at the 80 and 120 mg doses of TNG260 were increased in six of six patients (Fig. 5D). An example of paired biopsy from a patient with lung cancer (patient 9, Supplementary Table S6) treated with 120 mg TNG260 shows infiltration of cells that are CD3+/CD4+ or CD3+/CD8+ in proximity to CK-positive tumor cells (Fig. 5E). Together, these data demonstrate that TNG260 targets the CoREST complex in human tumor tissue and leads to remodeling of the immune microenvironment of STK11-mutant tumors.

Discussion

Tumors with loss-of-function mutations in STK11 respond poorly to immune checkpoint blockade, leading to shorter progression-free survival and overall survival in these patients. In this report, we employed an in vivo CRISPR screen using a syngeneic mouse model in which resistance to anti–PD-1 treatment was driven by loss of STK11. This screening approach identified that knockout of HDAC1 reversed the anti–PD-1 resistance caused by STK11 loss. In both syngeneic and GEM models, we showed that TNG260, a highly selective compound for the HDAC1-containing CoREST complex, provides sustained inhibition of CoREST in vivo and causes immune-mediated tumor regressions when combined with anti–PD-1. In patients with STK11-deficient tumors, treatment with TNG260 and pembrolizumab led to increases in intratumoral histone acetylation, increases in PD-L1 TPS, and increases in T-cell infiltration of tumor tissue. These findings highlight the therapeutic potential of combining TNG260 with an anti–PD-1 antibody such as pembrolizumab for STK11-mutant solid tumors, a tumor type of high unmet medical need.

Our screening approach was tailored to identify genes that rescued the defective anti–PD-1 response in STK11-mutant tumor cells. Our screen comparisons focused on genes that had tumor cell–intrinsic roles in adaptive immunity. Accordingly, we did not identify targets related to pathways previously investigated for STK11-mutant cancer therapy, such as glutamine synthesis or mTOR signaling. It is notable that these targets have not yet had success in the clinic for the STK11-mutant population, emphasizing the critical unmet need of this group of patients.

Mechanistically, we showed that TNG260 can increase antigen presentation genes and lead to a shift to a more active TME. Several previous studies have demonstrated that inhibition of HDAC enzymes by less-selective compounds can lead to increases in antigen presentation, as well as changes to intratumoral T-cell populations. In models of triple-negative breast cancer, the pan-HDAC inhibitor vorinostat led to increases in PD-L1 and HLA expression in tumor cells and lower Treg cells in coculture systems and syngeneic mouse tumor models. When combined with immune checkpoint blockade, vorinostat led to improved responses in a syngeneic mouse model. As a result of these preclinical studies, several clinical trials have been performed combining HDAC inhibitors with immune checkpoint blockade.

A phase II clinical trial of vorinostat combined with pembrolizumab for first-line, metastatic NSCLC did not meet its endpoint for efficacy. During this study, half of patients required dose reductions or interruptions of vorinostat dosing because of toxicity such as grade 2 to 3 fatigue or nausea, likely contributing to impaired efficacy of the combination. Our study also found that the FDA-approved dose of vorinostat in humans did not provide adequate inhibition of HDAC1 to achieve responses in STK11-null syngeneic models when combined with anti–PD-1.

Class-I HDAC drugs such as entinostat and domatinostat were developed to be HDAC isotype selective to improve tolerability over nonselective HDAC inhibitors like vorinostat. Preclinically, these molecules have also demonstrated immune-mediated antitumor effects. Despite the improved selectivity of class-I HDAC inhibitors, they have not demonstrated efficacy when combined with immune checkpoint blockade. For example, a phase II trial evaluating the combination of entinostat and pembrolizumab in metastatic NSCLC did not meet its primary endpoint for efficacy. Previous studies that combined HDAC inhibitors with immune checkpoint blockade have not employed a genetic patient selection strategy. Our in vivo CRISPR screen identified that STK11-mutant tumors were particularly sensitive to the inhibition of HDAC1 with anti–PD-1 treatment. Indeed, studies have shown that STK11 loss-of-function mutations result in epigenetic changes that lead to silencing of immune genes. Patients with STK11-deficient tumors may represent a group of patients who can most benefit from the epigenetic reprogramming induced by HDAC1 inhibition from TNG260. Several clinical reports have emphasized the role of KRAS comutation on the impact of STK11 loss on immune evasion. In the MC38 syngeneic model, which is KRAS wild-type, loss of STK11 alone is sufficient to drive resistance to anti–PD-1. Notably, we found that TNG260 with anti–PD-1 is similarly efficacious in models with mutant or wild-type KRAS. Several studies have shown that loss of KEAP1 is also associated with immunotherapy resistance. The mouse models employed in our study are all KEAP1 wild-type and thus cannot evaluate the impact of KEAP1 comutation on TNG260 efficacy.

Class-I HDAC drugs target both CoREST and the HDAC3-containing complex NCoR. Our findings showed that (i) HDAC1 is the critical enzyme for reversing resistance to anti–PD-1 treatment and (ii) HDAC3, which functions in the NCoR complex, may be a critical source of toxicity by less-selective HDAC inhibitors. As TNG260 has selectivity against HDAC3 and its complex NCoR, it may avoid toxicities related to HDAC3 inhibition. In addition, TNG260 was found to have selectivity for the CoREST complex, sparing the Sin3 or NuRD complexes. This additional selectivity may reduce the toxicities that occur with previous HDAC inhibitors, allowing for more continuous inhibition of HDAC1 while supporting combinability with anti–PD-1.

In summary, we have demonstrated that CoREST inhibition can sensitize STK11-mutant solid tumors to anti–PD-1 treatment. We identified TNG260 as a selective inhibitor of HDAC1 in the CoREST complex that combines with anti–PD-1 in vivo and leads to tumor regressions in these resistant models. TNG260 combined with anti–PD-1 may provide a treatment strategy for a tumor type that does not respond well to standard of care and is of high unmet medical need. In a phase I/II study (NCT05887492) of TNG260 in combination with pembrolizumab, the treatment of patients with STK11-mutant tumors with the combination was shown to result in substantial upregulation of histone methylation and a resultant increase in the immunogenicity of the TME. The study in ongoing and has entered the expansion phase.

Limitations of the study

We performed several in vivo studies using syngeneic models with engineered loss of STK11. Although these models allow for a full complement of the immune system, these are tumor types that have evolved in the presence of intact STK11, so they may have differences from STK11-deficient human tumors. Also, although increases in antigen presentation and pro–T-cell cytokines are observed after treatment with TNG260, the molecular mechanism underlying the increase in T-cell activity with combination treatment is not yet well understood.
