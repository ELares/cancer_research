# Targeting myeloma metabolism: How abnormal metabolism contributes to multiple myeloma progression and resistance to proteasome inhibitors.

## Abstract

Like other types of cancers, myeloma cells have acquired functional capabilities which are referred to as "Hallmarks of Cancer", and one of their most important features is the metabolic disorders. Due to the high secretory load of the MM cells, the first-line medicine proteasome inhibitors have found their pronounced effects in MM cells for blocking the degradation of misfolded proteins, leading to their accumulation in the ER and overwhelming ER stress. Moreover, proteasome inhibitors have been reported to be effective in myeloma by targeting glucose, lipid, amino acid metabolism of MM cells. In this review, we have described the abnormal metabolism of the three major nutrients, such as glucose, lipid and amino acids, which participate in the cellular functions. We have described their roles in myeloma progression, how they could be exploited for therapeutic purposes, and current therapeutic strategies targeting these metabolites, hoping to uncover potential novel therapeutic targets and promote the development of future therapeutic approaches.

Targeting myeloma metabolism: How abnormal metabolism contributes to multiple myeloma progression and resistance to proteasome inhibitors

Introduction

Multiple myeloma (MM) accounts for approximately 10 % of hematological malignancies as well as 1.8 % of all neoplastic diseases. MM is known as the malignancy of the terminally differentiated effector cells that provides immunoglobins upon immune-response, which is also referred as the cancer of the plasma cells. The myeloma etiology is complicated in aspects such as changes in the tumor microenvironment, deregulated signaling pathways, genomic instabilities, etc.. Therapeutic approaches, such as immunomodulatory drugs, Chimeric Antigen Receptor-T cells Therapy (CAR-T therapy), autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplant (ASCT), antibody-drug conjugates, proteasome inhibitors (PIs), etc., have been developed over the past decades to target MM, yet it still remains incurable. Most patients relapse and become resistant to current therapies.

Several functional capabilities were referred to as the “Hallmarks of cancer”, meaning that with these capabilities, human cells are able to transform from normal to the neoplastic state, some of them even become malignancies when they acquire more specific capabilities. These hallmarks as they are still emerging, and include but are not limited to: evading growth suppressors, resisting cell death, enabling replicative immortality, senescent cells, deregulating cellular metabolism, etc.. Among these hallmarks, deregulation of cancer cell metabolism is an old concept as Dr. Otto Heinrich Warburg had first described the correlation of tumorigenesis and dysregulated glucose metabolism nearly a century ago. However, deregulation of cancer cell metabolism has now become a heated research area since various neoplastic diseases have shown disordered metabolic features. Metabolic changes geared transformed cell towards sustained aberrant accumulation and biomass generation, and also leads to nutrient depletion and accumulated metabolic wastes; moreover, metabolites can regulate gene and protein expression that will influence the behavior of non-transformed cells, neoplastic cells, and tumor microenvironments. These cells will in turn produce more metabolites or metabolic wastes for the regulation or activation of genes and proteins that affect disease progression.

MM cells evolve from the antibody-secreting B lymphocytes. These cells initiate clonal evolution when they encountered genetic alterations such as translocation and hyperdiploidy, followed by secondary genetic events, and eventually become a group of malignant cells that accumulate in the bone marrow (BM) and secrete high amounts of monoclonal immunoglobulin (Ig). Initially developed to explore the function of ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS), the proteasome inhibitors (PIs) have been found to have pronounced therapeutic effects in MM. Bortezomib is the first proteasome inhibitor to have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and the European Agency for the Evaluation of Medicinal Products for treating refractory/relapsed MM. Due to the high secretory load, the PIs have been found to have pronounced effect on MM cells for blocking the degradation of misfolded proteins, leading to their accumulation in the Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER) and overwhelming ER stress; moreover, MM cells depend highly on UPS to clear the misfolded proteins. PIs were also found to disrupting metabolic pathways in MM cells, such as decreasing the glucose uptake, downregulating the generation of mitochondrial energy, enhancing lipid catabolism, and reducing intracellular levels of amino acids such as glutamine. More evidence that shows PIs target MM metabolism are that they interact with mammalian target of rapamycin (mTOR), the master regulator of cell proliferation, survival, etc.. The PIs remain the most effective frontline medicine for MM, and it may affect myeloma metabolism one way or another, which enlightens us that targeting myeloma metabolism may be a promising therapeutic strategy for MM. In this review, we have summarized the role of three major nutrients that participate in the cellular function:glucose, lipid and amino acids in MM. We believe this will help us to better understand the MM pathology while inspiring more therapeutic options.

Targeting myeloma glucose metabolism

As one of the most important sources of nutrients, glucose is required for the cell viability in all living organisms. Glucose metabolism results in stored energy in the form of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), and the end product of glucose can either be carbon dioxide or lactate, depending on oxygen sufficiency (Fig. 1). Nearly a century ago, Dr. Otto Heinrich Warburg discovered that all cancer cells he investigated exhibited a reverse Pasteur effect, in which they metabolize glucose via glycolysis rather than mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation, regardless of the availability of oxygen. This phenomenon was later referred as “Warburg Effect”. The functions of Warburg effect include but are not limited to: rapid ATP synthesis for tumor cells to increase the access to a limited energy source with a selective advantage; promoting glucose fluxing into biosynthetic pathways to generate nucleotides, lipids, and proteins needed to support cell proliferation, and NAPDH in the TCA cycle; changing tumor microenvironments to enhance the disruption of tissue architecture and immune cell evasion, which is achieved by providing an advantageous multicellular environment for cancer cell growth via acidification of the microenvironment thus facilitating immunosuppressive M2 macrophage polarization, and limiting glucose availability to tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte; allowing chromatin modulation and/or signal transduction through Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) since Glycolysis and Oxidative phosphorylation (OXPHOS) pathways are linked through a core circuit containing HIF-1, AMPK, and ROS, as well as oncogenes such as RAS, MYC, and c-SRC. While no specific genomic instability or mutation is common to all cancers, as most mutations were found to have arisen sporadically, the Warburg Effect was found in almost all cancers regardless of solid or blood borne malignancies. The radiolabeled glucose analog, 2-Deoxy-2- [(7-Nitro-2,1,3-Benzoxadiazol-4-yl) amino]-D-Glucose (2-NBDG) have become an important diagnostic tool for detecting and managing cancer by using positron emission tomography (PET), since nearly all tumors depend heavily on glucose for survival. The International Myeloma Working Group (IMWG), European Myeloma Network and the European Society for Medical Oncology have recommended clinicians use the 18F-Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG)-PET scanning to quantify and localize MM load in patients, and for the assessment of Extramedullary Disease (EMD) as well as response to therapies.

The normal plasma cells utilize glucose for protein glycosylation, which influence how antibodies interact with Fc receptors, thereby conferring distinct effector functions; moreover, the uptake of extracellular glucose is important for the longevity of plasma cells, since the glucose concentration is related to spare respiratory capacity. MM cells retain many metabolic features of plasma cells while meeting exceptionally high demands for nutrients for their biomass production. The Warburg effect is required for neoplasia as well as drug resistance: for example, aerobic glycolysis with an increased oxidative stress response and enhanced survival and proliferation signaling contribute to Melphalan resistance in MM cells. The key factors that participate in aerobic glycolysis have been reported to play pivotal roles in myeloma WE, and they were considered as therapeutic targets. The human glucose transporters (GLUT), which contains 14 isoforms, play important roles in facilitating the transportation of hexose. In contrast to studies on GLUT1’s contributions to aerobic glycolysis in solid tumors, GLUT4 is constitutively activated with minimal expression of GLUT1 in MM cells. MM cells rely on the constitutive cell surface localized GLUT4 for basal glucose consumption and maintenance of Mcl-1 expression. The FDA approved HIV protease inhibitor ritonavir has shown to be effective in reducing the proliferation, viability and increasing chemosensitivity of MM cells by inhibiting GLUT4 dependent glucose consumption. This result has encouraged the development of novel biologics and compounds that can interfere with glucose transporters in an isoform-specific manner. A study was then carried out by the same group to determine MM cell with ritonavir with metformin, the mitochondrial complex I inhibitor. Ritonavir-treated MM cells not only exhibited increased reliance on glutamine metabolism but also sensitize MM cells to metformin, effectively eliciting cytotoxicity in both in vitro and in an in vivo MM xenograft models. This study warrants a promising therapeutic strategy for MM by targeting the metabolic plasticity to heterogeneity and prevent the reemergence of chemo-resistant aggressive MM;The hexokinases (HK) convert glucose to glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), and the elevated HK activity is plays an important role in driving tumor glycolysis. While normal tissues only express HK1, HK2 is identified in most cancers, and majority of cancers express both HK1 and HK2. MM is the cancer type with the highest HK1−HK2+ population. hHK2 Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) selectively inhibited the proliferation of human HK1−HK2+ MM cells while exerting no significant growth inhibition on human HK1+HK2+ MM cells. The combination of specific HK2 inhibition, partial OXPHOS inhibition, and partial FAO inhibition is a promising combination therapy human MM xenograft;Pyruvate Kinase is an important regulator of WE, and increased Pyruvate Kinase muscle PKM2/PKM1 ratio has been reported in various cancers. Never in mitosis (NIMA)-related kinase 2 (NEK2) is a serine/threonine kinase, Gu, et al. reported that NEK2 is transcriptionally regulated by c-MYC, and promotes WE in MM by regulating the alternative splicing of PKM pre-mRNA thereby increased PKM2/PKM1ratio;Pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase (PDK), a key regulator of glycolysis and oxidative phosphorylation, is activated by hypoxia-inducible factor-1α (HIF-1α) under hypoxic conditions. The activated PDK isoforms independently phosphorylates pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) to inhibit pyruvate's conversion to acetyl-CoA. Dichloroacetate (DCA) is a pyruvate mimetic that inhibits the phosphorylation of PDH by targeting PDK; DCA reactivates PDH, restores the supply of acetyl-CoA to the Krebs cycle and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) electron donation to the electron transport chain, leading to the generation of mitochondrial ROS and finally apoptotic cell death. The application of DCA has been studied in colorectal cancer cells, prostate cancer, etc.; in MM, DCA was reported to alter the metabolic phenotype by inhibiting WE and increasing sensitivity to bortezomib.

Several molecular targets have been reported to participate in MM aerobic glycolysis, and targeting WE has been carried out in several projects: Phosphatase of Regenerating Liver-3 (PRL-3), a phosphatase encoded by Recombinant Protein Tyrosine Phosphatase Type IV A3(PTP4A3), is usually expressed in metastatic cancers. PRL-3 is found highly expressed in MM, and participates in MM metabolism by conducting WE, oxidative phosphorylation, and ATP production independent of its phosphatase activity; it also upregulates protein involved in glycolysis and enzymes involved in the synthesize of serine/glycine; moreover, downregulation of glycine decarboxylase (GLDC) is able to counteract PRL-3 mediated WE; Forkhead Box Protein M1 (FOXM1) is an oncoprotein and a transcription factor from the forkhead box family of proteins that regulates genes related to cell cycle for DNA replication and mitosis. FOXM1 was also reported as a promising target in newly diagnosed (ND), high- risk myeloma (HRMM) and relapsed/refractory myeloma (RRMM), in that it positively regulates MM metabolism by promoting glycolysis and OXPHOS. Targeting FOXM1 with its specific inhibitor NB73 can enhance the proteasomal degradation of FOXM1, and therefore inhibits MM cell growth.

Targeting myeloma lipid metabolism and bone marrow adipocytes

Highly proliferative cancer cells have shown a high demand of exogenous lipids and lipoproteins. They activate their endogenous synthesis, such as lipogenesis and cholesterol synthesis, etc., to satisfy strong lipid and cholesterol avidity. Lipogenesis results from highly activated fatty acid synthase (FAS), which is observed in various neoplastic cells. De novo fatty acid synthesis, uptake and suppression of fatty acid oxidation have all been shown to contribute to lipid storage, which is a necessary tumor adaptation. Preventing FAS activity by inhibiting its formation has been considered a potential therapeutic strategy for various malignancies. Excessive lipids and cholesterol are stored in lipid droplets (LDs) in cancer cells, which are now considered hallmarks of cancer aggressiveness as well as a sign of chemoresistance.

The aberration of lipid metabolism was also observed in MM, which either directly promotes MM cell's survival or abnormal bone marrow adipocytes that affects MM's microenvironment (Fig. 2). Hyperlipidemia was reported in MM patients especially the immunoglobulin (Ig)-A subtype. MM cells need exogenous cholesterol for survival, and Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol was reported to be an antiapoptotic factor for MM cells. Statins, the 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase inhibitors, inhibits 3-Hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-CoA(HMG-CoA) reductase, the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol synthesis, making it the cornerstone and frontline medicine for lipid lowering treatment. The application of Statins was reported to reduce mortality in MM patients by inducing cell apoptosis and cell growth arrest. Statins inhibits the conversion of HMG-CoA into mevalonic acid, a cholesterol precursor, thereby preventing HMG-CoA reductase from getting its functional structure. This process has resulted in the reduction of intracellular cholesterol; moreover, it slices sterol regulatory element binding proteins (SREBPs) from ER. SREBPs translocated at the nucleus level and increases the gene expression for LDL receptor. The effect of statins on lowering cholesterol while increasing the LDL receptors leads to the reduction of circulating LDL. Moreover, the mevalonic acid metabolism generates isoprenoids that have certain functions in cell proliferation and differentiation, inhibiting this process leads to the death of cancer cells. Nitrogen-containing bisphosphonates, the downstream target of statins, participate in the mevalonate pathway by inhibiting farnesyl-diphosphate synthase. This nitrogen-containing class inhibition of the mevalonate pathway leads to a reduction in protein prenylation, which is a crucial step for MM cell growth and survival. This may explain the mechanism of statins in reducing MM patients’ mortality.

Lipids form lipid rafts, which is a part of the cell membrane components, enables them to facilitate the recruitment of signaling proteins, thus promoting signal transduction. Lipid rafts are now being considered as a therapeutic target for MM. The phospholipid ether edelfosine directly targets and accumulates in MM cells membrane raft, inducing the apoptosis of MM cells by co-clustering rafts with death receptor. Depletion of cholesterol is able to disrupt the killing effect of edelfosine on MM cells by inhibiting the drug uptake, and the reintroduction of cholesterol will restore the drug-induced apoptosis by edelfosine. Moreover, changes in the lipid component and abundance will insert influential compact on membrane fluidity and protein dynamics, thereby disturbing the signaling cascades that regulate cell growth, metastasis, etc., which will eventually lead to tumorigenesis. Lysophosphatidic Acid (LPA) and phosphatidic acid (PA) are phospholipids that play important roles in signaling transduction and lipogenesis. Lysophosphatidic Acid Acyltransferase (LPAAT) is a 1-acyl sn-glycerol-3-phosphate acyltransferase. It coverts LPA to PA, thereby mediating cytokine expression and inflammatory injuries after hemorrhage. Although MM cell adheres to bone marrow stromal cells (BMSC) to protect themselves from the apoptosis induced by dexamethasone, the isoform-specific functional inhibitors of LPAAT-β can induce cytotoxicity in MM cells even after they were adhered to BMSC. This apoptotic effect is acted via poly-ADP- ribose (PARP), caspase-8, caspase-3, and caspase-7.

The fatty acid elongase-elongation of very long chain fatty acids protein 6 (ELOVL6) is transcriptionally regulated by SREBP1. It catalyzes the first and rate-limiting reaction that constitute the long-chain fatty acids elongation cycle. SREBP1 undergoes proteolytic cleavage to generate trascriptionally active nuclear SREBP1 (nSREBP1) in response to endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress.The ER is a primary site for lipid biosynthesis, and certain lipids have been known to cause lipotoxic ER stress to activate UPR and cell death. In MM cells, the expression of SREBP1 is positively correlated with ELOVL6, and changes in the ELOVL6-depend lipidome correlates with the response to BTZ and drug resistance. The expression of ELOVL6 was lower in the cells of MM patients from BTZ-nonresponsive vs BTZ-responsive as well as MM cell lines. Depletion of ELOVL6 in parental MM cells suppressed BTZ-induced ER stress and cytotoxicity, the restoration of which re-sensitized the non-responsive MM cells to BTZ treatment by upregulating the ELOVL6-dependent ceramide species, indicating that ELOVL6 is clinically related to BTZ resistance in MM.

MM is highly dependent on tumor microenvironment (TME) for their survival progression and resistance to current therapies. Adipocytes occupy 70 % of the BM volume wherein MM originates and resides. The bone marrow adipose tissue (BMAT) expands with aging and obesity, two key risk factors in MM disease prevalence. The dysregulated bone marrow adipocyte and MM cells interact reciprocally and influence each other to promote growth, survival, chemoresistance of MM cells and Myeloma Bone Disease (MBD). They also affect the metabolic plasticity in both cells; adiponectin is an adipocyte-derived hormone, and has been reported to play pivotal roles in glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity. The adiponectin secreted by host bone marrow adipocytes plays a tumor suppressive role, the decreased expression of which promotes MM cells growth and osteolysis; MM cells can cause hypoadiponectinemia by downregulating adiponectin in the bone marrow adipocytes, thereby promoting MM progression; MM cells can induce lipolysis in BM adipocytes, then uptake the free fatty acids (FFA), leading to an increased proliferation of MM cells at lower concentration of FFA and lipotoxicity at a higher concentration. Moreover, MM cells can alter the gene expressions and cytokine secretory profile of bone marrow adipocytes by inducing a senescent-like phenotype; and the adipocytes in turn support MM cell's resistance to dexamethasone-induced cell-cycle arrest and apoptosis. By understanding these bidirectional interactions between MM cells and bone marrow adipocytes, we may develop new potentially therapeutic strategies for MM:For instance, the apolipoprotein peptide mimetic L-4F pharmacologically enhances adiponectin. L-4F reduced tumor burden, increased survival of myeloma-bearing mice, and prevented MBD.

Targeting amino acid metabolism

In addition to glucose and lipid, MM cells also rely on exogenous amino acid metabolism for their survival (Fig. 3). Amino acids, categorized into α-, β-, γ-, and δ-amino acids, are the building blocks of protein synthesis. Amino acids are comprised of amino and carboxyl groups, which are structural elements and energy sources to meet the demand for cellular functions. Abnormal amino acid metabolism has been reported to participate in various diseases, as well as cancer. The tumor cells rely on the external source of non-essential amino acids (NEAA) for their growth and development. Restriction of intaking these amino acids can inhibit tumor growth, thereby making targeting amino acids metabolism a promising therapeutic approach for cancers. Treating MM cells with proteasome inhibitors depletes intracellular amino acid, and this depletion of amino acids will induce compound mitochondria damage; additionally, the low level of amino acids will sometimes activate the eIF-2-alpha kinase-General Control Nonderepressible 2 (GCN2), thereby contributing to PI resistance by recovering MM cells from proteasome inhibition. Moreover, the activation of GCN2 in MM cells has been reported to be correlated with MYC signaling, and contributes to mTORC1 inhibition through leucine deprivation. By exploiting publicly available databases, higher expression of amino acid metabolism-related genes was found in MM patients compared to that of normal individuals, and aberrant asparagine synthetase (ASNS) hypermethylation was found in these cells. Consequently, the amino acid depletion and metabolic deregulation triggered by the clinically used drug Erwinia chrysanthemi–derived ASNase (Erw-ASNase) led to anti-MM activity; moreover, the combination therapy of Erw-ASNase and proteasome inhibitor carfilzomib made MM cells vulnerable to the latter by inducing mitochondria ROS-mediated cell death.

Glutamine (Gln), the most abundant amino acid in human, is a NEAA obtained from food. Cancer cells need glutamine metabolism to meet their urgent nutrient demands, and MM cells also show an addiction to glutamine, in which they showed a high glutaminase (GLS1) and low glutamine synthetase (GS) expression, making them highly dependent on extracellular glutamine. Glutamine serves both as the building blocks of proteins and the replenish component of the TCA cycle, thereby sustaining their metabolic status. Glutamine transports into the cells by ASCT2/SLC1A5 and converts to glutamate. The latter is catalyzed by Glutamate dehydrogenase (GDH), glutamate transaminase, or aspartate transaminase (TAs) to produce α- ketoglutarate (α-KG) for supplementing the TCA cycle, transaminase reaction, synthesize glutathione(GSH), or activation of other metabolic pathways. Therefore, targeting glutamine transport may be a promising option for MM. Inhibition on glutamine transporter ASCT2 has markedly decreased the glutamine uptake and downregulated MM cell growth. Moreover, ASCT2 inhibitors sensitize MM patients to PI treatment by acting synergistically with PIs. Together, they upregulate the intracellular ROS and oxidative stress marker levels to trigger the UPR while down regulating expression of glutamine metabolism regulators. Other than being obtained from food, the alteration of gut microbiome may be an alternate source of glutamine. The nitrogen-recycling bacteria was found to be enriched in MM patients, one of which Klebsiella pneumoniae was reported to promote MM progression and pneumonia via de novo synthesis of glutamine. Moreover, MM cells consume large amounts of glutamine, which left the bone marrow glutamine concentration lower-than-normal, further inducing glutamine synthetase in stromal cells while hindering the osteoblast differentiation of mesenchymal stromal cell (MSC). Osteoblastogenesis induces glutamine-dependent ASNS, which further generates asparagine. The latter rescues differentiation of glutamine-starved MSCs. Inhibiting glutamine metabolism in MM cells while supplying asparagine to stromal cells may be considered a novel treatment for the prevention MBD in MM patients. BH3 mimetic are potent small molecule mimetics that bind the BH3 binding groove of anti-apoptotic B cell lymphoma-2 (BCL-2) proteins by releasing BIM or other bound pro-apoptotics to induce apoptosis. Targeting glutamine metabolism in MM cells enhances the binding of BIM to BCL-2, and consequently lethally sensitizes MM cells to the BH3 mimetic Venetoclax. This process can be reversed through supplementation of α-KG. Moreover, venetoclax enhances cytotoxicity in MM elicited by GLUT4 inhibitor ritonavir. The oncometabolite 2-hydroxyglutarate (2-HG) is a derivative of glutamine, the production of which is associated with c-MYC overexpression. By performing 13C stable isotope resolved metabolomics (SIRM) for the utilization of both glucose and glutamine in human MM cell lines, glutamine was found to be preferentially utilized by the TCA cycle compared with glucose. The Smoldering Multiple Myeloma (SMM) patients with an elevated 2-HG with a higher c-MYC expression is associated with a shorter time to MM progression. Therefore, measuring the plasma or BM supernatant 2-HG level confers a promising prewarning for high risk in MM. More molecules and compounds are used to target glutamine metabolism in MM: for instance, L-asparaginases, a molecule that can degrade asparagine, hydrolyze glutamine, inhibit mTOR activity, and contribute to an intracellular depletion of amino acids, is able to toxify MM cells when acting in combination with bortezomib or carfilzomib.

Serine and glycine are NEAA that are required for proliferating cells by way of intracellular synthesis or in vitro uptake. Compared with the non-transformed cells, the transformed cells show a higher demand for serine and glycine in that they hyperactivate the anabolic serine and glycine synthesis side-branch of glycolysis for de novo production. Two steps are involved in the de novo serine and glycine synthesis:serine synthesized from glucose, and serine can be reversibly interconverted into glycine. Branched from glycolysis, the glycolytic intermediate 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PG) converts into serine via three enzymatic reactions: the first and rate-limiting enzyme in serine synthesis pathway (SSP)- the phosphoglycerate dehydrogenase (PHGDH) first oxidizes 3-PG to 3-phosphohydroxypyruvate (3-PHP); by utilizing glutamate as the nitrogen donor, phosphoserine aminotransferase 1 (PSAT1) transaminases 3-PHP, this process also generates α-KG; finally, phosphoserine phosphatase (PSPH) dephosphorylates phosphoserine into serine. The hyperactivated SSP is correlated with resistance to PIs, and the bortezomib-resistant MM cells lines have shown higher SSP than bortezomib-sensitive MM cells. PHGDH was found to be raised in patients with relapsed MM with poor survival. It promotes MM cells survival and contributes to bortezomib resistance by increasing glutathione synthesis while decreasing bortezomib-induced ROS. Thrombocytopenia is a major complication in some MM patients. Serine is identified as a key metabolic factor that suppresses megakaryopoiesis and thrombopoiesis, it mediates thrombocytopenia by suppressing megakaryocyte differentiation. Extrinsic serine transports into megakaryocyte and downregulate supervillin, thereby impair the megakaryopoiesis. Inhibition of serine utilization or treatment with Thrombopoietin (TPO) enhances megakaryopoiesis and thrombopoiesis and suppresses MM progression.

Moreover, MM cells secrete matrix metallopeptidase 13 (MMP13), which mediates the degradation of bone collagen, thereby leading to the upregulation of glycine concentration in the bone marrow and contributing to MM progression. Additionally, MM cells intake the extrinsic glycine via the channel protein solute carrier family 6 member 9 (SLC6A9) to synthesize glutathione (GSH) and purines. Knockdown of SLC6A9 or treating MM cells with a methyl-donor betaine (Trimethylglycine) can inhibit MM cell proliferation and enhance the effect of bortezomib.

Conclusion and perspective

In this review, we started with how PIs affect MM cells’ survival by disturbing their metabolic status. We then further summarized the abnormal glucose, lipid and amino acid metabolism that affect MM pathogenesis and drug resistance (Table 1). We described how they function irreplaceably to provide cells with energy sources to accomplish a normal cellular lifespan, and also act as an energy fuel to promote neoplasia and drug resistance. These nutrients can convert to one another under certain circumstances or stressed condition: For instance, when glutamine is converted to glutamate, the latter is catalyzed by GDH, glutamate transaminase, or TAs to produce α-KG for the supplementation of the TCA cycle. Cancer cells have a high demand for serine and glycine, and hyperactivate anabolic serine and glycine synthesis side-branch of glycolysis for de novo production. These studies indicate that when treating MM patients, targeting MM cells’ metabolism should be supplemented to traditional therapies in order to achieve the ideal outcome. Moreover, many drugs that were initially designed for treating metabolic disorders, such as Statins, have found profound effects in reducing MM mortality or acting in synergy with PIs to overcome its drug resistance. Therefore, by summarizing the abnormal metabolism in the three major nutrients that participate in the cellular function, namely glucose, lipid and amino acids in MM, we may be able to better understand the pathogenesis of MM, and develop novel therapeutic strategies or to act in concert with current treatment for a better overall survival rate.
