# The novel CD16A/anti-CD3 bifunctional protein, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, redirects T cell cytotoxicity toward antibody-bound target cells.

## Abstract

Monoclonal antibodies enhance innate immunity, while bispecific T cell engager antibodies redirect adaptive T cell immunity. To stimulate both innate and adaptive mechanisms, we created a bifunctional eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP adapter protein for combined use with clinically approved monoclonal IgG1 antibodies. The adaptor protein contains the extracellular domain of the human CD16A high-affinity variant, which binds the Fc domain of IgG1 antibodies, and an anti-human CD3 single-chain variable fragment that redirects T cell cytotoxicity. Functional characterization of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP was performed using in vitro, ex vivo, and in vivo animal assays. Combination treatments of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP with rituximab (anti-CD20), cetuximab (anti-EGFR), trastuzumab (anti-HER2) or anti-PD-L1 IgG1 led to specific killing of antigen-expressing tumor cells. In an ex vivo assay, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP combined with rituximab effectively eliminated B cells within peripheral blood samples from healthy donors and cancer patients; the effectiveness of this treatment was further enhanced by addition of either autologous or allogeneic γ9δ2 T cells. In mouse xenograft studies, the combination of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab led to rapid depletion of tumor cells. The presence of non-targeting competing serum immunoglobulins interfered with efficacy, but this interference could be overcome by eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP dose escalation or by using a Fc-glycoengineered version of rituximab. Moreover, combining eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP with anti-Epstein Barr virus (EBV) antibodies directed T cell cytotoxicity toward EBV-infected cells. These findings support further development of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP as a novel anti-cancer therapeutic to stimulate both innate and adaptive immunity.

Introduction

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) specifically target tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) and are extraordinarily successful cancer immunotherapies. The treatments trigger innate immune responses, which may involve antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC), antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis (ADCP), or direct apoptosis. In contrast, adaptive T cell-mediated cytotoxicity may be redirected toward tumors by chimeric antigen receptors or bispecific antibodies (bsAbs), which simultaneously target TAAs on tumor cells and CD3 on T cells. Approved bsAbs include blinatumomab, glofitamab, epcoritamab, mosunetuzumab, teclistamab, talquetamab, and elranatamab for hematological malignancies, and many more are under development.

Technical issues often impede efforts to develop novel bsAbs. For instance, random heavy and light chain variable domain assembly must be avoided. In addition, a novel bsAb cannot be constructed by simply replacing the TAA-binding domain on a functional bsAb scaffold. Even for a simple structure like blinatumomab, replacing one of the two single-chain variable fragments (scFvs) may affect the properties of the unchanged scFv via intra-molecular interactions. Moreover, converting a bsAb candidate into a drug is frequently hindered by developability issues including poor stability and solubility.

We hypothesized that a modular strategy might allow commercially available mAbs to indirectly trigger T cell cytotoxicity. To implement this idea, we developed eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, a novel bifunctional bridging protein composed of the extracellular domain of the human CD16A high-affinity variant (eCD16A) directly linked to an anti-human CD3 scFv (anti-CD3). We predicted that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP would form a bsAb-like complex with a cancer-targeting IgG1 mAb and promote targeted cell killing via adaptive T cell immunity. At the same time, the original mAb structure and associated innate immune response would be preserved. In this work, we performed a proof-of-concept study to demonstrate the function of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and clarify its clinical potential.

Patients and methods

Clinical samples and cell lines

Protocols involving human samples were approved by the IRB of National Taiwan University Hospital (201911036RINC, 202012179RIND). T cells were expanded with anti-CD3/CD28 beads, interleukin (IL)-7 and IL-15. γ9δ2 T cells were expanded with recombinant human IL-2 and zoledronate.

The listed cell lines were used in this work, including HEK-293, Daudi, Raji, Toledo, VAL and RS4, A431, MCF-7, BT474, T47D, Immortalized EBV-infected B cells, and Jurkat. All cell lines were grown in DMEM, EMEM, or RPMI-1640.

Flow cytometry analysis

Immunophenotyping procedures and analysis were performed according to our previously published protocols. Briefly, cells were washed and stained as general practice, and only propidium iodide (PI)-negative viable cells were analyzed.

Design of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

CD16A protein has two main allelic variants with F or V at position 158; the variant with higher affinity toward IgG (haCD16A) is CD16A-158 V. In addition, CD16A is down-regulated via shedding due to disintegrin and metalloprotease (ADAM)17 cleavage between residues 195 and 196 after lymphocyte activation. To retain high affinity for IgG and prevent proteolytic cleavage of the bifunctional protein, the extracellular domain of haCD16A before the ADAM17-cleavage site was included in eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP. The coding sequences and hexa-histidine tag (H-6) were fused and cloned into an adeno-associated virus (AAV) shuttle plasmid with a cytomegalovirus promoter.

Production of anti-LMP1 antibody

The sequence of anti-LMP1 antibody was from the literature, and the antibody was produced from transfected HEK-293 cells followed by purification.

In vitro binding assay for eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

To demonstrate the binding to IgG1 antibodies, Raji or A431 cells were incubated with rituximab or cetuximab, followed by anti-Fc antibody; Or cells were incubated with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, followed by anti-H-6 antibody. To evaluate binding to primary T cells, αβ T cells were incubated with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, followed by anti-CD16 antibody. The results were analyzed with flow cytometry.

In vitro activity assay for eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

The activity was analyzed in the presence of IgG1 antibodies and T cells, similar to Sheehy et al. PKH26 and CFSE labeled tumor cells were incubated with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, IgG1 antibody, and T cells (E:T = 10:1). After 6 h, tumor cell viability was determined by flow cytometry.

Ex vivo activity assay for eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

PBMCs from healthy donors were incubated with rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP for 144 h. The remaining CD19-positive B cells were quantified by flow cytometry to determine cytotoxicity.

In another experiment, PBMCs from chronic lymphocytic leukemia patients were stimulated with IL-2 and zoledronate to obtain γ9δ2T cells. On expansion day 10, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab were added to the cultures in serum-containing medium for three days, followed by flow cytometry analysis.

Mouse xenograft experiments

Animal experiments using ASID mice (NOD.Cg-PrkdcscidIl2rgtm1Wjl/Yck-Narl) were approved (109-NLAC-EA-018) and performed according to a published protocol with minor changes. The mice were implanted with luciferase- and GFP-expressing Raji and treated with γ9δ2 T, rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and monitored with bioluminescence imaging as indicated.

The details of the methodology section can be found in the supplementary.

Results

Structural characteristics and production of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

We designed and generated eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, a bifunctional fusion protein consisting of eCD16A (the extracellular domain of the human high-affinity CD16A-158 V variant before ADAM17 cleavage site) and anti-human CD3 (anti-CD3ε scFv) domains (Figure 1a). Its high purity was demonstrated by SDS-PAGE and anti-H-6 Western blot from three batches (Figure 1b). Expected and higher molecular weight forms were observed, probably reflecting glycosylation events. The two forms stimulated comparable T cell cytotoxicity in preliminary experiments (data not shown), so the mixture was used in experiments. 

Dual binding of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to fc domains of mAbs and CD3 on T cells

To determine whether eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP binds to Fc domains of human IgG1 antibodies, we used rituximab and cetuximab, which respectively recognize CD20 on Raji and EGFR on A431 cells. An anti-Fc antibody was used to confirm that each antibody bound to its target cell surface (Figure 2a). Then, the binding of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to mAb-bound target cells was analyzed (Figure 2b). eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP binding to mAb-coated cells was much higher than its binding to cells without mAb (Figure 2c). Next, we evaluated the binding of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to surface CD3 molecules on T cells (Figure 2d), finding that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP readily bound to T cell CD3 (Figure 2e). These results indicate that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP binds to the Fc domains of IgG1 antibodies on the surface of target cells and also to CD3 on T cells, in line with the intended mechanism of action.

Redirection of T cell cytotoxicity to cancer cells by combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and mAbs

To assess whether eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP mediates tumor cell killing (Figure 3a), we first tested if eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP could stimulate cytotoxicity in CD20-expressing lymphoma cells in the presence of rituximab and T cells. Figure 3b shows specific synergistic killing effects toward CD20-expressing Raji, VAL and Toledo cells, but not CD20-negative RS4 cells. Similar experiments were performed using cetuximab with EGFR-expressing A431 or EGFR-negative MCF-7 cells (Figure 3c) and using trastuzumab with HER2-positive BT474 or HER2-negative T47D cells. These experiments consistently showed that T cell-mediated cytotoxicity could be induced specifically in the antigen-positive solid tumor cell lines (Figure 3c,d). In addition to TAA-targeting mAbs, anti-PD-L1 could be combined with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to target PD-L1-overexpressing A431 but not PD-L1-negative MCF-7 cells (Figure 3e). Importantly, robust cytotoxicity was not seen when using either eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP or the antibody alone. Thus, we conclude that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP can effectively redirect T cell cytotoxicity to target cells when used with clinically approved mAbs. 

To confirm the mechanism of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP-mediated target cell cytotoxicity, we utilized γ9δ2 T cells from healthy donors. Donor-derived γ9δ2 T cells were heterogeneous for CD16, so we separated the cells according to CD16 expression status (Figure 3f). We then evaluated the killing effects of CD16-positive and CD16-negative γ9δ2 T cells toward lymphoma cells treated with rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP. With endogenous CD16 to mediate Fc recognition, the CD16-positive γ9δ2 T cells killed lymphoma cells in the presence of rituximab, regardless of the addition of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP (Figure 3g). In contrast, CD16-negative γ9δ2 T cells could only kill lymphoma cells with concurrent rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP treatment. This result confirmed that eCD16A is crucial for eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP-mediated redirection of T cells to tumor cells.

Activity of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP combined with mAbs ex vivo

Without co-stimulation, TCR signaling cannot efficiently activate naïve T cells, but it is adequate to enhance the activity of activated effector T cells. Hence, the level of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP activity should be correlated with the quantity of available effector T cells, similar to bsAbs. To test this prediction, we examined the ability of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to induce T cell-mediated cytotoxicity in PBMCs from healthy donors, who are presumed to have relatively low numbers of effector T cells as compared to cancer patients. In a representative experiment, PBMCs were derived from a healthy donor (~20% CD27− CD45RA− effector memory T cells plus CD27− CD45RA+ effector T cells; data not shown). The PBMCs were treated with combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab, and CD19-positive B cells were depleted over the course of 6 days (Figure 4a). Compared with the no treatment or rituximab alone group, CD19-expressing B cells were robustly depleted after combination treatment. Notably, the addition of activated autologous γ9δ2 T cells to the PBMC sample caused depletion of almost all CD19-positive B cells within 24 h, far more quickly than in the resting primary T cells from PBMCs (Figure 4b). Thus, we conclude that expanded γ9δ2 T cells can enhance the efficacy of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP. Using blood samples from two CLL patients, we next tested whether the combination of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab could promote primary cancer cell lysis ex vivo. Since T cells in CLL patients often exhibit exhaustion features, the PBMCs were stimulated to activate autologous effector γ9δ2 T cells. After a 10-day stimulation, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab were added to the cells. Only combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab caused CD19-positive B-CLL cell death (Figure 4c), while rituximab and stimulated T cells did not cause significant killing. Notably, replacing autologous γ9δ2T cells with allogeneic γ9δ2 T cells also led to substantial primary B-CLL tumor lysis with the combined treatment (Figure 4d). These ex vivo studies further supported the conclusion that combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and mAbs may effectively redirect T cell cytotoxicity toward primary cancer cells. 

Inhibition of lymphoma cell growth by eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP-redirected T cell cytotoxicity in ASID mice

We then tested the ability of combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and rituximab to clear lymphoma cells in ASID mice xenografted with luciferase/GFP-expressing Raji cells. γ9δ2 T cells, rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP were administered as indicated (Figure 5a). Compared to mice treated with rituximab and T cells, the addition of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP significantly enhanced Raji cell clearance when rituximab was treated twice (Figure 5b,c), suggesting that combined rituximab and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP can redirect T cell cytotoxicity toward cancer cells in vivo. 

Impact of endogenous immunoglobulins on the activity of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP

One concerning aspect of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP is its potential interaction with abundant endogenous polyclonal IgG1 in blood, which might compete with antigen-specific mAbs and compromise its activity. Human immunoglobulin (IVIG) was used to mimic an environment with abundant nonspecific IgG1 antibodies. Although IVIG suppressed eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP redirection of cytotoxicity toward Raji cells, escalated doses of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP could overcome this inhibition. Moreover, CHO-H01 (Fc-glycoengineered version of rituximab with enhanced affinity for CD16, was less susceptible to the inhibitory effect of IVIG (Figure 6). Therefore, dose escalation and mAb derivatives with high affinity for CD16 could be possible solutions to reduce the negative impacts of endogenous nonspecific immunoglobulins.  

eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP combined with serum containing anti-Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) polyclonal antibodies kills ebv-infected cells

We first synthesized an IgG1 antibody against LMP1 of EBV and established EBV-transformed B lymphoblastoid cell lines (LCLs, type III latency). Using the strategy in Figure 2b, the combination of anti-LMP1 IgG1 and eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP could be used to weakly stain Raji cells (Figure 7a). Nevertheless, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and different concentrations of anti-LMP1 IgG1 synergistically induced cytotoxicity toward Raji cells (Figure 7b). The combination of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and anti-LMP1 IgG1 also stimulated cytotoxicity toward LCLs but not EBV-negative Jurkat T cells (Figure 7c).

Finally, we explored the therapeutic potential of eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP with virus-specific polyclonal antibodies. EBV infects more than 90% of the general population in Taiwan, and our serum samples from healthy controls and CLL patients were positive for the anti-viral capsid antigen (VCA) antibody. Therefore, these samples were used as a source of anti-EBV polyclonal antibodies in subsequent experiments. Combined eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP and serum from either healthy donors or CLL patients in the presence of T cells could induce cytotoxicity toward LCLs (Figure 7d) but not toward EBV-negative HPAC or PANC-1 pancreatic cancer cell lines (Figure 7e). These results imply that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP may transform endogenous polyclonal IgG antibodies into functional bsAb-like complexes to treat EBV-related cancers, such as lymphomas and nasopharyngeal carcinoma.

Discussion

In this study, we demonstrated the feasibility of using clinically available mAbs to redirect adaptive T cell cytotoxicity with the bridging molecule, CD16A/anti-CD3-BFP. Our results suggested that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP may be able to transform various naked therapeutic mAbs into bsAb-like functional complexes that stimulate T cell-mediated killing of antigen-positive target cells.

A naked therapeutic mAb functions by inducing various innate immunity activities (e.g., apoptosis, CDC, ADCC, and ADCP), but it typically does not stimulate T cell activity. However, in combination with our bifunctional protein, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP, a functional complex similar to conventional bsAbs is created. This complex induces T cell-mediated killing (Figure 3b–e), providing proof-of-concept evidence that this modular approach is worthy of further development toward clinical application. Konishi et al. recently explored a similar strategy using a “bridging BiTE” fusion of two scFvs that respectively bind the Fc domain of human IgG and CD3ε. The bridging BiTE was covalently attached to therapeutic mAbs, such as rituximab and daratumumab, prior to treatment. Because this covalent linkage with the bridging BiTE may disrupt mAb-mediated induction of innate immunity, it was unclear whether the approach might work without covalent linkage. The ex vivo experiments (Figure 4a–d) and animal studies (Figure 5a–b) in our study confirm that co-treatment of our bifunctional molecule and rituximab is adequate to increase killing of malignant B cells. Thus, we speculate that our modular bridging strategy can redirect T cell immunity and retain the original innate immunity, thereby enhancing cancer cell-killing effects.

A major potential limitation of treating eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP in combination with mAbs is that non-targeting competing serum IgG antibodies may compromise the antitumor efficacy. Although endogenous polyclonal IgG antibodies may theoretically sequester the non-covalently linked bifunctional protein in vivo, our data suggest two feasible solutions to compensate for diminished effects (Figure 6). Either eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP dose escalation or mAb Fc-domain glycoengineering may improve CD16 binding affinity and overcome IVIG-mediated inhibition of cytotoxicity.

Most people, including those diagnosed with EBV-associated cancers, carry polyclonal antibodies against EBV-associated proteins. Unfortunately, these endogenous antibodies are not protective or functionally sufficient to kill EBV-infected cells. In our experiments, eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP equipped anti-viral antibodies with bsAb-like function to induce targeted killing of virus-infected cells or virus-associated cancers. Thus, augmenting the therapeutic potential of endogenous antibodies with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP could represent a new cancer therapeutic strategy with broad applicability.

Importantly, circulating T cells will likely infiltrate tumors and engage eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP. In one recent study, T cells in the tumor microenvironment reinforced mAb-mediated NK-cell ADCC. Similarly, the combination of eCD16A/anti- CD3-BFP with anti-PD-L1 might retain the original anti-PD-L1 action of mitigating T cell suppression while simultaneously stimulating T cell activation. In fact, a novel anti-PD-L1 and anti-4-1BB bispecific antibody was shown to enhance T cell infiltration, restore exhausted T cell function, and exhibit potent anti-cancer effects. Therefore, anti-PD-L1 antibodies with preserved Fc functions (e.g., avelumab) might act synergistically with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP to produce potent cancer-killing efficacy.

One key safety concern for eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP is that eCD16A may bind to autoantibodies, inducing unexpected autoimmune responses. Further experiments to determine eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP relative binding affinities to native IgGs in the serum and therapeutic mAbs may help to clarify whether there is potential risk for the bifunctional protein to stimulate autoimmune responses. Nevertheless, since eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP is a small protein with an expected short half-life, halting its infusion should quickly stop nonspecific reactivities. Another safety concern is that CD3 may bind to and revive senescent autoreactive T cells to trigger autoimmune responses. Therefore, patients with history of autoimmune disorders will require special attention throughout clinical treatment with eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP.

Conclusions

This study demonstrates that combining therapeutic mAbs or endogenous antibodies with the eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP adaptor molecule may be able to functionally mimic bsAbs, as these combinations redirect activated T cells to kill target cells. In future work, characterization of the adaptor molecule binding affinities for targets will be performed, and those affinities may be compared to existing bsAb products. Nevertheless, the results of this study suggest that eCD16A/anti-CD3-BFP may be a promising new immunotherapy tool for cancer treatment.
