# A TRAILR2/CDH3 bispecific antibody demonstrates selective apoptosis and tumor regression in CDH3-positive pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

Exploitation of extrinsic apoptosis signaling via TRAILR2 activation represents a promising therapeutic concept in cancer treatment. The limited clinical success of previous TRAILR2 agonistic agents, to date, has been ascribed to either poor efficacy or hepatotoxicity. TR2/CDH3 BAB is a human bispecific antibody that relies on binding both CDH3 and TRAILR2 on cell surfaces to achieve TRAILR2 hyperclustering and efficient apoptosis induction by TRAILR2 signaling selectively in CDH3-expressing tumor cells. We demonstrate target-dependent TR2/CDH3 BAB anti-tumor activity in CRISPR/Cas9-engineered TRAILR2 or CDH3 knock-out cells. By utilizing the cell line screening platform PRISM, we found selective TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy in various cancer types, such as pancreatic, gastric, colorectal, and triple negative breast cancer. The efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB correlated with caspase activation in cancer cell lines and in xenograft tumor tissues. In pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), where patient benefit from current cytotoxic therapy options is unsatisfactory, a close to uniform cell surface expression of CDH3 and TRAILR2 was observed, which will qualify the majority of PDAC patients for TR2/CDH3 BAB-based treatment. TR2/CDH3 BAB demonstrated anti-tumor activity in a panel of PDAC patient-derived xenograft models, including tumor regressions. By combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with chemotherapeutic agents, deeper and more sustained anti-tumor responses were observed when compared to monotherapy. Together with the potential to deliver a favorable safety profile, these data support clinical testing of TR2/CDH3 BAB in patients with PDAC.

Introduction

The exploitation of tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL) receptor agonists to selectively eradicate tumor cells by activation of extrinsic apoptotic signaling, while sparing normal tissues, represents an attractive cancer therapeutic concept. In recent years, this has driven the advancement of various TRAILR2-agonizing agents into clinical development (reviewed in). Elevated levels of TRAILR2, also known as death receptor 5 (DR5) and encoded by the TNFRSF10B gene, have been reported for late stages of various solid tumor types, including pancreatic and colorectal cancer (CRC). TRAIL, as a natural ligand of the death receptors TRAILR1 and TRAILR2, can elicit programmed cell death in a p53-independent manner. TRAIL itself induces apoptosis in preclinical models without eliciting toxicities in non-human primates. However, neither recombinant TRAIL itself nor bivalent monoclonal antibody (mAb)-based TRAILR2 agonists, which demonstrated excellent safety profiles with no signs of hepatotoxicity, met their expectations in the clinic (reviewed in). Clinical failure of these early compounds was attributed to an intrinsically weak pro-apoptotic activity. This originates mainly from their inability to organize TRAILR2 receptor-ligand trimers into hyperclusters with sufficient signaling potency.

Despite concerns of hepatotoxicity due to overactivation of TRAILR2 signaling in normal liver tissue, multivalent agents able to bind more than three TRAILR2 molecules simultaneously were engineered. Clinical safety and efficacy have been determined or are currently being evaluated for these compounds. Examples are the tetravalent Nanobody® TAS266, the Hexabody DR5/DR5 (Gen1029), the hexavalent Fc fusion protein eftozanermin alpha (formerly known as ABBV-621), the tetravalent mAb INBRX-109, and the immunoglobulin M (IgM)-based decavalent mAb IGM-8444 (aplitabart). Although TAS266 and eftozanermin had shown excellent efficacy in preclinical models, drug-induced hepatic adverse events led to termination of their clinical development. Similarly, hepatoxicity induced by Gen1029 led to the early termination of a Phase 1 clinical trial (NCT03576131). Nevertheless, the more recently developed multivalent TRAILR2 agonists INBRX-109 (Trial-ID: NCT04950075) and aplitabart (Trial-ID: NCT04553692) have shown promise in terms of safety and efficacy in Phase 1 clinical trials, although their clinical safety at confirmed therapeutically effective doses warrants further investigation. Learnings from previous TRAILR2 agonists, which have demonstrated distinct demands on drug selectivity and efficacy, should guide the design of next-generation TRAILR2 agonistic agents.

Besides covalent or secondary cross-linking of anti-TRAILR2 valences, higher-order receptor oligomerization can be achieved by ligand immobilization at the cell surface. Taking up this concept, we recently reported on the preclinical antitumor efficacy of BI 905711, a bispecific monoclonal antibody that uses CDH17 as a plasma membrane anchor to hypercluster and hence activate TRAILR2 selectively in CDH17-positive tumor cells. However, CDH17 expression is mostly restricted to gastrointestinal (GI) malignancies, such as CRC, which limits the expansion of BI 905711 development in other types of tumors with high medical need. By exploiting CDH3, also known as P-cadherin, as an anchor for activation of TRAILR2, we extend our therapeutic concept to additional cancer indications, such as pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC), non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), breast cancer, ovarian cancer, and head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC). Importantly, like CDH17, CDH3 was undetectable in normal liver tissues. Elevated expression levels of CDH3 in different solid cancer indications are associated with metastasis and poor prognosis. In PDAC, CDH3 is abundantly expressed and indicative of an invasive phenotype. Furthermore, plasma membrane expression of CDH3 increases during carcinogenesis from benign pancreatic intraepithelial neoplasia 1 (PanIN-1) to malignant PDAC. This suggests that CDH3 might represent an antibody anchor for potentiating TRAILR2-mediated apoptosis also in the invasive and potentially metastasis-forming cancer cell subpopulation in PDAC patients. We characterize TR2/CDH3 BAB, a human TRAILR2/CDH3 bispecific antibody (BAB). This study aims to establish the efficacy of our bispecific concept in clinically relevant models of pancreatic cancer as a single agent or in combination with indication-relevant cytotoxic drugs.

Materials and methods

Ethical considerations

The organs and tissues used for formalin fixed and paraffin embedded (FFPE) tissue sections and tissue microarrays (TMAs) meet all ethical standards and their use for oncological research was authorized by the donors via Informed Consent. The templates of the Informed Consent Forms were approved by the relevant Institutional Review Boards. All animal procedures were approved by Boehringer-Ingelheim internal ethics committee and the local governmental committee. The animals were handled according to the internal institutional, governmental (Austrian Animal Protection Laws), and European Union (ETS-123) guidelines. Studies on patient-derived xenograft models of PDAC were approved by The University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC), protocol number 00000884-RN03, and adhered to the National Institutes of Health (NIH) guidelines.

Cell lines and cell culture

Cells were maintained in tissue culture flasks until 70–80% confluence at 37°C and 5% CO2 in a humidified atmosphere using media recommended by the American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) and the German Collection of Microorganisms and Cell Culture (DSMZ). See Supplementary Methods for further details.

CRISPR/Cas9-mediated knockout of TNFRSF10B and CDH3 in GP2d CRC cells

CDH3-, TRAILR2-, and CASP8-deficient GP2d cells were generated using CRISPR/Cas9 gene editing technology. This was achieved by transient transfection of GP2d with a pSPCas9 (BB)-2A-GFP (gift from Feng Zhang (Addgene plasmid #48138, http://n2t.net/addgene:48138; RRID: Addgene_48138)) vector encoding the Cas9 nuclease and gene-specific guide RNA (gRNA). The CDH3-targeting gRNA sequence was 5′-AGATTTTCTACAGCATCACG-3′. The TRAILR2-targeting gRNA sequence was 5′-GACAACGAGCACAAGGGTCTT-3′. The CASP8-targeting gRNA sequence was 5′-TCCTTTGCGGAATGTAGTCCAGG-3′. In case of CDH3 targeting, cell clone isolation was conducted by single-cell seeding into 96-well plates. Candidate clones were expanded into 24-well plates for fluorescence-activated cell sorting analysis to identify CDH3 deficiency. To obtain the TRAILR2 knockout (KO) and CASP8 KO derivatives of GP2d, transiently transfected cells were exposed to selection pressure using 10 nM TR2/CDH3 BAB for 1 week, and the surviving GP2d-derivative pool was analyzed by flow cytometry to confirm TRAILR2 deficiency.

Test compounds and molecule sequences

Gemcitabine for in vitro experiments (98% purity, HPLC grade) was purchased from AK Scientific Inc. (Union City, CA, USA). The sequence source for TRAILR2-directed IgG antibody lexatumumab was the World Health Organization, as listed in the Recommended international nonproprietary names, list 57. For in vivo patient-derived xenograft (PDX) studies, gemcitabine was purchased from MedChemExpress (Cat. #HY-17026, CAS#95058-91-4). Nab-paclitaxel (Abraxane, 100 mg/vial, NDC 68818-134-650) was produced by Celgene Corporation.

Engineering, expression, and purification of TR2/CDH3 BAB

The TR2/CDH3 BAB is a two-chain homodimeric bispecific antibody, with Fab domains binding to the CDH3 and the TRAILR2-specific antibody formatted as a single-chain variable fragment (scFv) and appended to the C-terminus of the Fc domain. The human IgG1 Fc backbone was “silenced” with the L234A/L235A mutation, which ablates binding to FcγR and complement to avoid CDH3-independent crosslinking. The CDH3-building block of TR2/CDH3 BAB was derived from an immunization campaign using AlivaMab® transgenic mice, which generate human antibodies against a given target, precluding the need for any additional humanization. The CDH3-building block was then engineered to remove chemical liabilities and non-human residues. The scFv of TR2/CDH3 BAB was derived from a human TRAILR2-specific antibody identified using phage display technology. TR2/CDH3 BAB was produced in Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells and purified using standard processes as described previously. The optimized variable regions with drug-like properties (high percentage of human sequence, minimal chemical liabilities, and reduced immunogenicity potential) were formatted into bispecific expression construct using pTT5 expression vector (National Research Council, Canada) with common molecular biology techniques. TR2/CDH3 BAB was produced in CHO-E (National Research Council, Canada) cells by transient transfection with heavy chain to light chain plasmid mass ratio of 1:2, which enabled efficient purification of the bispecific moiety. After 10-day culturing, the supernatant was harvested and purified by Protein A affinity chromatography using MabSelect™ column (GE Healthcare). The bispecific antibody was further purified to homogeneity by cation exchange chromatography using a Poros 50 hS column (Applied Biosystems). The purified protein was concentrated and stored in 50 mm sodium acetate and 100 mm NaCl, pH 5.0 buffer. Analytical characterization by intact mass spectrometry, SDS-PAGE, and size-exclusion chromatography showed that the purified antibody was the expected heterodimeric mass and contained minimal high molecular weight aggregate (less than 1%) and no contaminating homodimeric species.

ATP content-based cell viability and apoptosis assessment

For cell viability assessment, cells were seeded at 2,000–5,000 cells in 100 µl medium per well in white opaque 96-well microtiter plates (#6005680, Perkin Elmer, Shelton, CT, USA) and incubated overnight at 37°C and 5% CO2. For single-agent treatment, serial dilutions of the corresponding compound were added using a HP300 dispenser (Hewlett-Packard Development Company) on the following day. In the chemotherapy combination treatment setting, serial dilutions of gemcitabine were added on the following day after plating of cells and 24 h prior to addition of TR2/CDH3 BAB serial dilutions. After the addition of compounds, plates were transferred back to the incubator and incubated for an additional 24 or 96 h as indicated. After this, CellTiter-Glo® reagent (#G924C, Promega) was added to cells, and plates were agitated on a plate shaker at 300 rpm for 2 min followed by further 10-min incubation at room temperature in the dark. Activation of caspase 8 as a surrogate for TR2/CDH3 BAB-mediated TRAILR2 pathway activation was determined using Caspase-Glo® 8 Glo reagent (#G8201, Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In brief, cells were seeded 24 h prior to treatment in white opaque 96-well microtiter plates (#6005680, Perkin Elmer) at a density of 20,000 cells/well. After treatment with 1 nM TR2/CDH3 BAB for 3 or 6 h, Caspase-Glo® 8 Glo reagent was added to the cells and the plates were agitated on a plate shaker at 300 rpm for 30 s followed by 1-h incubation at room temperature in the dark. Luminescence signal intensities were measured on EnSpire® Multimode Plate Reader (Perkin Elmer). Average background signal intensities were subtracted from each measurement.

PRISM cell line screen

Profiling Relative Inhibition Simultaneously in Mixtures (PRISM) is a high-throughput multiplexed cancer cell line viability screening platform developed by the Broad Institute of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and Harvard. Cell lines were maintained in Roswell Park Memorial Institute (RPMI) 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) without phenol red for adherent cell lines and RPMI 20% FBS without phenol red for suspension cell lines. Parental cell lines were stably equipped with a unique 24-nucleotide DNA barcode via lentiviral transduction and blasticidin selection. Next, the barcoded cell lines were expanded and subjected to quality control (QC) (mycoplasma contamination test, a single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) test to confirm cell line identity, and barcode ID confirmation). QCed cell lines were then pooled (20–25 cell lines per pool) based on doubling time for the compound screen. A detailed description of the protocol, including barcoding, culturing conditions, drug exposure, and assay controls is included in the PRISM publication.

Screening of cancer cell lines for TR2/CDH3 BAB sensitivity in the PRISM assay was performed using an eight-point dose (in threefold dilution) approach with 96-h treatment. A total of 863 cancer cell lines yielded interpretable results on drug sensitivity. Drug response vectors are provided as area under the curve (AUC) and “activity area” for each cell line. Since higher AUC values are indicative of drug resistance, drug sensitivity is expressed as 1-AUC which is equivalent to activity area. A high activity area or a low AUC are indicative of drug sensitivity.

Apoptosis detection via Incucyte® live cell imaging

Cancer cells were plated at 2000–5000 cells in 100 µl medium per well in clear-bottom 96-well microtiter plates (#3340, Corning). After overnight incubation at 37°C and 5% CO2, therapeutic compounds were added alone or, serially, in combination. Treatment of cells with 10 nM or 100 nM gemcitabine was performed 24 h prior to the addition of 1 nM TR2/CDH3 BAB. Directly after treatment, Incucyte® Annexin V (#4641, dilution 1:400) and Incucyte® Caspase 3/7 (#4400, dilution 1:3000) (both Sartorius) were added to the cells. Plates were then placed in the Incucyte® S3 2021C Live-Cell Analysis System (Sartorius) for multiplexed live cell measurement. Images in experimental wells were captured every 1–4 h in phase-contrast, green, and red fluorescent channels. The red and green fluorescent counts per image were measured to determine the extent of apoptosis in each well.

Efficacy and biomarker studies on cell line-derived mouse xenograft models

Seven- to 10-week-old female NMRI-Foxn1nu (BomTac:NMRI-Foxn1nu) mice were purchased from Taconic, Denmark, and acclimated for at least 5 days prior to the experiments. Mice were housed in Macrolon® type III cages in groups of six (biomarker study) or eight animals (efficacy study) under standardized conditions at 21.5 ± 1.5°C temperature and 55 ± 10% humidity (12-h light–dark cycles). Standardized diet (PROVIMI KLIBA) and autoclaved tap water were provided ad libitum. Subcutaneous microchips implanted under isoflurane anesthesia were used to identify each mouse. GP2d CRC cells (Ecacc, Cat# 95090714, lot# 11E013) were resuspended in phosphate-buffered saline + 5% FBS at 1 × 108 cells/ml. The cell suspension was mixed at a 1:1 ratio with Matrigel (Corning Cat. # 356231) to obtain a final concentration of 5 × 107 cells/ml and injected subcutaneously into the right flank of the mice (one site per mouse, 100 µl). TR2/CDH3 BAB working dilutions in 50 mm NaAc, 100 mm NaCl, pH 5.0 buffer were prepared freshly prior to injection.

Tumor diameters were measured three times a week with a caliper. The volume of each tumor [in mm3] was calculated according to the formula “tumor volume = length × diameter2 × π/6”. When tumors were well established and had reached volumes of 160.79 to 279.34 mm3 15 days after tumor cell injection, mice were randomly distributed by tumor size between the treatment and the vehicle control groups (8 animals/group for the efficacy study and 6 animals/group for the biomarker study, median tumor volume ~210 mm3 in each group) using the automated data storage system Sepia. Group sizes were determined by sample size calculations and mice from each treatment group were housed separately. A single dose or TR2/CDH3 BAB or the vehicle control was administered intravenously (i.v.) at a volume of 5 ml/kg body weight. The experimenters were not blinded to the treatment.

To monitor the side effects of treatment, mice were inspected daily for abnormalities, and body weight was determined daily. No animal had to be euthanized and excluded from the analysis due to body weight loss, health deterioration, or tumor necrosis. Animals were sacrificed on day 28 (efficacy study) or 24-h post-treatment (biomarker study), and tumors were collected for further analysis.

Mono- and combination therapy efficacy studies on PDAC PDX models

NSGTM female NOD.Cg-Prkdcscid Il2rgtm1Wjl/SzJ mice (Stock Number 005557; The Jackson Laboratory, Bar Harbor, ME) were obtained at 6 weeks of age and acclimated for 5 weeks before being used at 11 weeks old for experiments. Mice were housed in the state-of-the-art Association for the Assessment and Accreditation for Laboratory Animal Care (AAALAC)-accredited animal research facilities on the South Campus at the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center, 7777 Knight Rd, Houston, TX. Mice were housed in ventilated cage enclosures, with a maximum of five animals in an environment maintained at 50% humidity, 22°C, and 12-h light/dark cycles. One day prior to PDX implantation, mice were shaved in the surgical area, along the right flank. Mice were anesthetized with isoflurane before they were implanted subcutaneously on the right flank with one fresh fragment of tumor (4 × 4 × 4 mm) using aseptic surgical technique. Prior to study enrollment, tumors were measured at least twice and randomized into treatment groups with similar average tumor sizes (e.g., ~280 mm3 for the study shown in Figure 6c) and tumor size distribution was performed, ensuring average tumor sizes for each individual group are within ±3% of the average of all tumors. The experimenters were not blinded to the treatment. Prior to injection, stock solution of TR2/CDH3 BAB (30 mg/ml) was diluted with injection buffer (50 mm sodium acetate, pH 5.0, 100 mm sodium chloride) to a concentration of 0.2 mg/ml. Intraperitoneal (i.p.) injection at 5 ml/kg was performed to reach a final dose of 0.3 mg/kg or 1 mg/kg, and TR2/CDH3 BAB was dosed once every 2 weeks (Q14D). Body weights were measured twice weekly and used to adjust dosing volume and monitor animal health. Mice were monitored daily for any clinical signs until they were euthanized at the study endpoint. Individual determinations of tumor volumes were performed 1–2 times per week using the formula: Tumor volume (mm3) = 0.5 × (length × width2).

Evaluation of TRAILR2 and CDH3 normal tissue expression by immunohistochemistry

All immunohistochemistry (IHC) assays for the detection of CDH3 and TRAILR2 were performed on 2–4 µm sections of FFPE tissue blocks using the anti-CDH3 mouse monoclonal antibody clone #104805 (R&D Systems), and the anti-DR5 (TRAILR2) rabbit monoclonal antibody clone E9D7D (Cell Signaling Technologies). Normal tissue expression of CDH3 was assessed in a TMA block of normal human organs and tissues according to the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) panel (Provitro AG, Charité Campus-Mitte, Charité-Platz 1 in 10,117 Berlin (Cat.-No.: 401 1130, Lot: 385P150421.52-74_438P.01–23), comprising 132 spots plus 6 extra spots (separate row 16) with a spot diameter of 1.5 mm). The IHC assay was performed on the Leica Bond RX platform, using the following key specifications: Anti-CDH3 antibody was diluted to a working concentration of 2 μg/ml, and heat-induced epitope retrieval (HIER) ER1 (pH 6) was chosen for automated staining. After counterstaining with hematoxylin, the stained slide was subsequently scanned using Hamamatsu NanoZoomer S360 slide scanner, followed by pathological analysis of submitted e-slides.

IHC for detection of TRAILR2 and CDH3 protein expression across cancer indications

An automated IHC procedure for the detection of TRAILR2 and CDH3 was performed on FFPE tissue sections of 4 µm thickness originating from PDAC (n = 45), ovarian carcinoma (n = 46), AdNSCLC (n = 45), TNBC (n = 45), sqNSCLC (n = 44) as well as HNSCC (n = 43). Anti-CDH3 and anti-TRAILR2 antibodies were used at working concentrations of 6 μg/mL and 2 μg/mL, respectively. Staining was performed using the OptiView detection kit (Cat. #NC1689706) on the Benchmark ULTRA staining platform (both Roche Diagnostics). An immunoglobulin-matched monoclonal antibody was used as a negative control antibody to examine the presence of secondary antibody and detection chemistry reactivity.

Statistical analysis

GraphPad Prism software (v9.5.0) was used for statistical analyses. For in vitro experiments, calculation of significant differences between two groups of biological replicates was performed using a Student t test (unpaired, two-tailed, Holm–Šidák method, with α level = 0.05). For the comparison of three or more experimental groups, a multiple comparison one-way ANOVA test was applied. For unpaired data, one-way ANOVA in combination with a Tukey multiple comparisons test was performed. A Dunnett test was performed if more than one sample was compared with a nontreated control. In case of different unequal standard deviations between more than two groups, Brown-Forsythe and Welch ANOVA tests with Dunnett’s T3 multiple comparison were performed.

For the calculation of in vivo xenograft tumor volumes, absolute values were used for statistical analysis. Individual determinations of tumor volumes were performed 1–2 times per week using the formula: Tumor volume (mm3) = 0.5 × (length × width2). For an overview of possible treatment effects, the mean or median (as indicated) of the tumor volume of each treatment group T was referred to as the control group C as tumor growth inhibition (TGI) defined as: TGI = 100 × {1-[(treated final day – treated day1)/(control final day – control day1)]}. To compare the tumor volumes of different treatment groups of the GP2d CDX model (Supplementary Figure. S4A, B), one-sided non-parametric Mann–Whitney–Wilcoxon U-tests adjusted for multiple comparisons according to Bonferroni-Holm were performed to compare all treatment groups with the vehicle control. In the case of the PDAC PDX experiments (Figures 5b and 6d,f), the difference of mean tumor volumes at a given time point was tested for significance using the multiple t-tests function in GraphPad Prism 9.0. Parametric unpaired testing with a single pooled standard deviation was performed, followed by Holm–Šidák testing to correct for multiple comparisons. An (adjusted) p value of less than 0.05 was considered to show statistically significant differences between groups. Data are shown as dot plots with bar graphs or horizontal lines for mean ± SD or SEM, as indicated.

Data availability

Expression of TRAILR2 and CDH3 mRNA in neoplastic tissues was investigated in next-generation sequencing (RNAseq)-acquired datasets derived from The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), publicly available via the Genomic Data Commons (GDC) portal. TRAILR2 and CDH3 expression data from benign tissues were retrieved and internally processed from raw TCGA and GTEx V8 data. TCGA and GTEx expression data were processed as previously described.

Results

CDH3 represents a liver-sparing anchor molecule for TRAILR2 activation in different types of solid tumors

By exploiting next-generation sequencing (NGS) data publicly available from TCGA and GTEx databases, we analyzed 276 non-diseased liver tissue samples using mRNA expression as classifier. Here, CDH3 mRNA expression was defined as absent in normal liver tissues, as only one sample showed a value higher than 2 transcripts per million (TPM) and 254 samples (92.0%) displayed less than 1.1 TPM (Figure 1a). Expression of CDH3 and TRAILR2 mRNAs (signal cutoff: >10 TPM) in at least 90% of evaluated tumor samples was observed in PDAC, CRC, squamous NSCLC (sqNSCLC), and HNSCC (Figure 1a). Additional malignancies, such as triple negative breast carcinoma (TNBC), lung adenocarcinoma (AdNSCLC), and ovarian carcinoma also showed relevant TRAILR2/CDH3 double-positivity (89%, 85%, and 79%, respectively) (Figure 1a). 

Next, we determined the clinical prevalence of CDH3 and TRAILR2 protein expression in different indications using IHC staining on FFPE tissue sections. TRAILR2 and CDH3 positivity was observed in more than 90% of evaluable cases in PDAC, sqNSCLC, HNSCC, and ovarian cancer (Suplementary Table S1). More than 75% of evaluable cases from AdNSCLC and TNBC stained positive for either TRAILR2 or CDH3 (Supplementary Table S1). As expected for cell surface proteins, the staining pattern for both TRAILR2 and CDH3 was predominantly cyto-membranous in cancer tissues (as exemplary shown for PDAC in Figure 1b). Notably, a close to uniform TRAILR2 and CDH3 positivity across cancer cells was observed in PDAC specimens (Figure 1b).

In non-neoplastic tissues, moderate median expression of CDH3 mRNA, defined as ≥30 TPM, occurs, for example, in the oral cavity, larynx, tongue, and in ovarian tissues, with few cases showing up to 120 TPM (source: TCGA and GTEx database). All other non-neoplastic tissues display rather low (median <30 TPM) or no (median <10 TPM) expression of CDH3 mRNA (Supplementary Figure S1). To determine the levels of CDH3 protein in benign tissues, we performed IHC staining on a tissue microarray, according to the FDA panel. Here, squamous epithelia, such as esophageal and skin epidermis, showed expression of CDH3 (Figure 1c). In addition, a small percentage of ductal myoepithelial cells, which were reported to express the relatively highest levels of CDH3 mRNA in breast tissue, displayed detectable levels of CDH3 protein in one breast tissue core (Figure 1c). Ovarian tissue in the available sections, which lacked both granulosa cells and oocytes positive for CDH3 expression according to single-cell sequencing data, remained negative (data not shown). Importantly, expression of CDH3 protein in liver tissue was not detectable by IHC staining (Figure 1c, right panel).

A TRAILR2/CDH3-binding bispecific antibody TR2/CDH3 BAB elicits CDH3-dependent apoptosis in cancer cells in vitro

TR2/CDH3 BAB is a human bispecific antibody with bivalent binding to both CDH3 and TRAILR2 (Figure 2a). Binding affinity of TR2/CDH3 BAB to its targets was evaluated by surface plasmon resonance (SPR) (Supplementary Table S2-S4 and Figure S2). The dependence of TR2/CDH3 BAB epitope binding and activation of downstream signaling (as illustrated in Figure 2b) was experimentally addressed using CRISPR/Cas9-engineered knock-out (KO) derivatives of the CRC cell line GP2d (GP2d TRAILR2 KO and CDH3 KO), which lacked either CDH3 or TRAILR2 surface expression (Supplementary Figure S3A,B). Notably, TRAILR2 surface levels in GP2d CDH3 KO cells and CDH3 surface levels in GP2d TRAILR2 KO cells were comparable to GP2d parental cells (Supplementary Figure S3A,B). GP2d parental cells were sensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB at sub-nanomolar concentrations (IC50 = 0.05 nM), and titration of TR2/CDH3 BAB from 10 nM down to 1 pM yielded a typical s-shaped curve with a monotonic dose–response relationship (Figure 2c). The absence of TRAILR2 rendered tumor cells insensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB up to a dose of 100 nM, and binding of TR2/CDH3 BAB to CDH3 alone had no effect on GP2d cell viability (Figure 2c). More importantly, the sensitivity of GP2d CDH3 KO cells to TR2/CDH3 BAB was reduced by ~120-fold when compared to parental GP2d cell line (IC50 = 6 nM versus IC50 = 0.05 nM) (Figure 2c). 

In another experiment, TR2/CDH3 BAB displayed an almost two orders of magnitude higher efficacy in reducing GP2d cell viability when compared to an IgG-based mono-specific TRAILR2 agonist lexatumumab (IC50 = 0.01 nM versus IC50 = 0.73 nM) (Supplementary Figure S3C). However, the efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB was inferior when compared to lexatumumab in GP2d CDH3 KO cells (Supplementary Figure S3D). Exposure to parental anti-CDH3 IgG neither affected cell viability nor altered the response of tumor cells to lexatumumab treatment (Supplementary Figure S3C,D). Similar results were obtained with the TRAILR2/CDH3-positive lung cancer cell line NCI-H358 (Supplementary Figure S3E).

We next examined TRAILR2- and CDH3-dependent activation of extrinsic apoptotic signaling by TR2/CDH3 BAB. Caspase 8 activity was induced as early as 3 h (Figure 2d) and the maximal activation of intracellular caspase 3/7 (>30-fold) occurred at 8 h after treatment (Supplementary Figure S3F) in GP2d wild-type cells. In contrast, caspase activation at a dose of 1 nM TR2/CDH3 BAB was not detectable with the here applied methods in the TRAILR2- or CDH3-deficient cell line derivatives. Furthermore, CRISPR/Cas9-mediated ablation of caspase 8 rendered GP2d CASP8 KO cells insensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB-mediated cell growth inhibition and apoptosis, demonstrating that an intact extrinsic apoptotic machinery downstream of TRAILR2 is essential for anti-tumor activity of this CDH3-dependent TRAILR2 agonist (Supplementary Figure S3G,H). Taken together, these data show that TR2/CDH3 BAB-mediated cell growth inhibition and apoptosis depend on binding to both targets on cancer cell surfaces.

In vivo efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB in a CDH3/TRAILR2-positive CRC xenograft model

The GP2d CRC cell line-derived xenograft (CDX) model was used to demonstrate in vivo efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB. After single i.v. administration of different doses, tumor regression (TGI above 100%) was observed from 0.33 mg/kg to 6 mg/kg TR2/CDH3 BAB, while administration of lower drug doses achieved a moderate anti-tumor response (31% and 56% TGI, respectively) (Figure 2e and Supplementary Figure S4A,B). A significant modulation of pharmacodynamic (PD) biomarkers caspase 3/7 in tumor lysates was observed at 24 h after treatment with TR2/CDH3 BAB at doses ranging from 0.33 mg/kg to 6 mg/kg (Figure 2f). These data show that TR2/CDH3 BAB achieves a dose-dependent tumor regression by inducing apoptosis in a TRAILR2/CDH3-positive xenograft model of CRC.

TR2/CDH3 BAB in vitro efficacy across different cancer indications

To obtain unbiased data on TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy across various cancer indications, we took advantage of the multiplexed cancer cell line platform PRISM, which allowed us to screen 931 malignant cell lines representing more than 40 cancer types. We obtained interpretable TR2/CDH3 BAB response activity areas (AA, presented as 1 – area under the curve (AUC)) for 863 cancer cell lines (Supplementary Figure S5A). A k-Means algorithm was applied to stratify cell lines into clusters with high (AA: 0.54–0.96, n = 99), medium (AA: 0.20–0.53, n = 195), or low (AA: <0.20, n = 539) sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB (Figure 3a). In agreement with results published for anchor-independent TRAILR2 agonists, the mean expression of TRAILR2 was relatively higher in cell lines sensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB (Supplementary Figure S5B). In line with the TR2/CDH3 BAB mechanism of action, the mean expression of CDH3 mRNA was >10-times higher in TR2/CDH3 BAB high-sensitivity cell lines when compared to low-sensitivity cell lines (Figure 3b). Among the different solid cancer indications represented by the cell line panel, PDAC, CRC, and gastric cancer displayed the highest PRISM screen sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB, followed by ER−HER2− (enriched in TNBC) breast cancer and NSCLC (Figure 3c and Supplementary Table S5). An independent screening of TR2/CDH3 BAB across a smaller panel of 140 cell lines (2D OncoSignatureTM Cell Panel Screening, Horizon Discovery platform) yielded comparable results in terms of cell line responses (Supplementary Figure S5C). Focusing on the 297 cell lines positive for CDH3 expression (TPM ≥10) (Supplementary Figure S5D) we performed a Hallmark pathway enrichment analysis (Figure 3d). Here, gene sets indicative of apoptosis and p53 pathway activation were associated with high sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB. In line with this finding, tumor cells sensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB showed increased average expression of CASP8 and BID, genes playing a key role in core apoptosis pathways (Figure 2b and Supplementary Figure S5E,F). These data show that sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB depends on expression levels of CDH3, to a minor extent on TRAILR2, and on apoptosis marker gene expression. 

Efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB single-agent treatment in pancreatic cancer cells

Given the high prevalence of TRAILR2 and CDH3 expression in PDAC tumor tissues, the high PRISM screen sensitivity of cell lines representing this indication, and an increasing unmet need, we focused our attention on the activity of TR2/CDH3 BAB in PDAC. In individual cell viability assessments on PDAC cell lines, exposed to increasing doses of TR2/CDH3 BAB (0.5 pM to 10 nM), 10 of 19 cell lines were found to be sensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB (Figure 4a) and displayed a maximal cell growth inhibition (CGI(max)) of >50%. Six of these responders showed a CGI(max) >100% compared to treatment start, which is indicative of cell death. Nine PDAC cell lines were found insensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB (CGI(max) <50%), with five of these non-responders being negative (<10 TPM) for CDH3 expression (Figure 4a). It is likely that alterations within the apoptotic network downstream of TRAILR2 might render some target-positive PDAC cell lines less sensitive to TR2/CDH3 BAB. When comparing these results to the data obtained from the PRISM cell line screen, we found a solid overlap of either TR2/CDH3 BAB responder or non-responder PDAC cell lines, respectively, identified by the two approaches (Figure 4a). To exemplarily confirm the induction of apoptosis biomarkers in PDAC cells displaying either high or moderate sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB treatment, we analyzed induction of caspase 8 and caspase 3/7 in SU.86.86, PK59, and Capan-2 cell lines. As expected, treatment with 1 nM TR2/CDH3 BAB led to activation of caspases in all three cases, with SU.86.86 (high sensitivity, %CGI(max) = 187) displaying the fastest kinetics (Figure 4b,c). 

TR2/CDH3 BAB achieves tumor growth inhibition in PDAC PDX models

To address the dose-dependent efficacy of TR2/CDH3 BAB in a disease-relevant context, we treated a PDAC PDX model positively evaluated for bulk expression of TRAILR2 (34 TPM) and CDH3 (83 TPM) mRNA with different doses (0.3 and 1 mg/kg) of this compound. When compared to vehicle-treated animals, intraperitoneal administration of TR2/CDH3 BAB on a bi-weekly (Q14D) schedule led to a strong anti-tumor activity on day 29 (Figure 5a,b). Disease stabilization was achieved at 0.3 mg/kg, and tumor regression occurred at a dose of 1 mg/kg TR2/CDH3 BAB (Figure 5a). Elevated activity of caspase 3/7 (Caspase-Glo®) in tumor tissues and an increased percentage of tumor cells displaying cleaved caspase 3/8, as assessed by immunofluorescence staining, was observed 24 h after treatment of animals with 1 mg/kg and, albeit with a lower amplitude, 0.3 mg/kg TR2/CDH3 BAB (Figure 5c,d). 

To obtain a broader understanding of TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy in this cancer type, 12 TRAILR2/CDH3 mRNA-positive PDX models, each established from a different PDAC patient, were treated with 1 mg/kg TR2/CDH3 BAB. In this cohort, anti-tumor activity, defined as TGI above 50%, was observed in 8 cases, while 4 PDX models were insensitive to the treatment (Figure 5e). Three PDAC PDX models (PATX106, PATX141, and PATX153) displayed tumor regression, and one model (PATX107) achieved stabilization of disease (Figure 5f). To correlate the observed heterogeneous TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy with the percentage of TRAILR2/CDH3 mRNA double-positive cancer cells in PDAC PDX study tumors, we performed multiplexed nucleic acid in situ hybridization (ISH) by exploiting RNAscope™ ISH technology (exemplary images shown in Supplementary Fig S6A-C). Indeed, we found a positive trend (Pearson r = 0.54, P-value = 0.084) toward better TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy in tumors showing a relatively higher percentage of cells co-expressing both markers (Figure 5g). Taken together, these data demonstrate TGI upon TR2/CDH3 BAB treatment in PDAC in vivo, including tumor regressions in a subset of examined PDX models.

Combination of TR2/CDH3 BAB with gemcitabine shows augmented efficacy in PDAC cells in vitro

The DNA synthesis inhibitor gemcitabine, mostly administered in combination with albumin-bound paclitaxel (nab-paclitaxel), represents a commonly used agent for the treatment of PDAC. To address the potential of dual intrinsic plus extrinsic apoptosis pathway activation in PDAC in vitro, we examined the effect of gemcitabine in combination with TR2/CDH3 BAB on the viability of human PDAC cell lines PA-TU 8988S, PK-59, and Capan-2 (Figure 6a and Supplementary Figure S7A,B). Indeed, the addition of sub-nanomolar concentrations of TR2/CDH3 BAB to low, therapeutically ineffective (%CGI <70%) gemcitabine doses achieved cell growth inhibitions above 100%, which is indicative of cell death (Figure 6a and Supplementary Figure S7A,B). Furthermore, combined TR2/CDH3 BAB plus gemcitabine treatment achieved a stronger induction of annexin V and activated caspases 3/7 when compared to exposure to each single agent alone (Figure 6b and Supplementary Figure S7C,D). These data demonstrate beneficial effects on CGI and augmented apoptosis induction by combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with gemcitabine in PDAC cell lines. 

Combination of TR2/CDH3 BAB with chemotherapeutic agents achieves deeper and prolonged anti-tumor responses in PDAC PDX models

We next investigated the therapeutic potential of combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with clinically relevant chemotherapeutic agents in vivo. A twice-weekly intraperitoneal application of gemcitabine at 50 mg/kg and intraperitoneal administration of 1 mg/kg TR2/CDH3 BAB every other week allowed assessment of tumor growth up to 94 days after treatment start in a PDAC PDX model (PATX124). Here, we observed a substantial benefit of the drug combination when compared to each single- agent effect (Figure 6c). More importantly, combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with gemcitabine led to a deeper response and tumor regression, which lasted until day 78 (Figure 6c). The mean tumor volume on day 45 was significantly smaller when compared to each single-agent treatment group (Figure 6d). Combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with nab-paclitaxel (nab-P), dosed at 30 mg/kg and administered once weekly, achieved disease stabilization up to day 58 in the PDAC PDX model PATX107, while the single-agent treatments, albeit displaying tumor growth delay, suffered from early tumor progression under drug exposure (Figure 6e). The average tumor size in the TR2/CDH3 BAB plus nab-P combination treatment group on day 84 was smaller when compared to single-agent treatments, although the difference to nab-P alone did not reach significance (Figure 6f). Taken together, these data support TR2/CDH3 BAB development in combination with standard-of-care chemotherapeutic agents for the treatment of PDAC patients.

Discussion

In this study, we demonstrate selective anti-tumor activity of the TRAILR2 agonistic bispecific antibody TR2/CDH3 BAB. The efficacy of this compound was dependent on the cell surface protein CDH3, which serves as an anchor for TRAILR2 hyperclustering. We had reported previously on a TRAILR2-directed bispecific antibody BI 905,711, which uses CDH17 as an anchor molecule to support TRAILR2 activation in gastrointestinal malignancies, such as CRC. Expression of CDH3 protein occurs in a broader spectrum of solid tumors, such as PDAC, sqNSCLC, and AdNSCLC, TNBC, ovarian cancer, and HNSCC. This extends the scope of our anchor-driven TRAILR2 agonist concept to additional cancer indications with an unmet medical need. In PDAC, an indication where we observed a high prevalence and a close to uniform surface expression of TRAILR2 and CDH3, membranous expression of CDH3 has been reported to increase during carcinogenesis from benign PanIN-1 to PDAC. In another study, Dall’Ora et al. observed moderate to strong expression of TRAILR2 in 100% of analyzed PDAC specimen, which agrees with our data, and strong expression of TRAILR2 occurs in liver metastasis of PDAC. However, the relative level of CDH3 expression in distant metastasis compared to primary tumors in PDAC warrants further investigation.

Importantly, expression of CDH3 was absent in non-transformed liver tissues, which should minimize the risk of TRAILR2-activation in liver cells during TR2/CDH3 BAB-based cancer therapy. Clinical development of several previously designed multivalent TRAILR2 agonist, although highly efficient in preclinical models, has been stopped due to signs of liver damage, which provides a strong rationale for our liver-sparing TRAILR2 agonist design. Besides the strong potency of TAS266 and potentially elevated expression of TRAILR2 in hepatocytes of drug-exposed patients, the observed immunogenicity of this nanobody, which might have provoked anti-drug antibody-mediated TRAILR2 hyper-clustering and pro-apoptotic signaling beyond levels tolerated by liver cells, were considered as probable causes for the observed liver toxicity. In a first-in-human study designed to assess safety and tolerability of eftozanermin monotherapy in PDAC and CRC patients, a total of 21% of patients displayed grade ≥3 treatment-related adverse events, with the most common being hepatic or constitutional. Similarly, >50% of patients with Gen1029 in a Phase 1 study focusing on solid cancers showed serious adverse events, including signs of hepatotoxicity at higher drug doses (NCT03576131). Nevertheless, early clinical data from other recently developed multivalent TRAILR2 agonists have demonstrated acceptable safety profiles with a clinically non-problematic elevation of the AST/ALT ratio observed in a few cases. For example, INBRX109 monotherapy has delivered promising results in a Phase 1 trial on non-resectable/metastatic chondrosarcoma (NCT04950075). Ongoing Phase 1 studies addressing additional solid cancer indications with INBRX-109 either as a monotherapy or in combination with chemotherapy (NCT03715933) will provide further insight into the safety and efficacy profile of this compound. Furthermore, encouraging preclinical results as well as promising safety data from a Phase 1 trial in CRC have been reported from the multivalent IgM antibody aplitabart (NCT04553692). Notably, the objective response criterium (>30% ORR) for CRC was not met upon aplitabart single-agent treatment, suggesting that, even for multivalent TRAILR2 agonists, a combination with chemotherapeutic agents might be necessary for clinical success.

While absent in liver tissues, CDH3 expression in humans was not restricted to cancer tissues. CDH3-positive non-neoplastic tissues should be spared from potential on-target toxicity due to their lower sensitivity to TRAILR2 activation, which might contribute to a wider therapeutic window for TR2/CDH3 BAB. Our hypothesis of TR2/CDH3 BAB representing a liver and normal tissue sparing TRAILR2 agonist was further supported by the results of preclinical safety studies in cynomolgus monkeys, where administration of TR2/CDH3 BAB was well tolerated without any clinical signs of toxicity or target organ toxicity, as evidenced by the lack of histopathological findings (Supplementary Table S6). Although TR2/ CDH3 BAB can be considered a novel TRAILR2 agonist with potentially liver-sparing activity, the combination of TRAILR2 agonism with standard-of-care chemotherapy bears some risk of toxicity, including liver damage. The TRAILR2 agonist antibody lexatumumab in combination with different chemotherapies, including gemcitabine, showed manageable adverse events in patients with no signs of hepatotoxicity in Phase 1b clinical trials. We observed that the therapeutic effect of TR2/CDH3 BAB, indicative of its TRAILR2 agonist potency, was weaker in a CDH3-deficient GP2d background when compared to lexatumumab, which points to TR2/CDH3 BAB being relatively less potent in killing CDH3-deficient cells. Hence, we speculate that TR2/CDH3 BAB could deliver a similarly beneficial, liver-sparing safety profile in patients when used in combination with chemotherapy, while at the same time killing CDH3-positive cancer cells with a much higher efficacy.

According to our data obtained from the PRISM and Horizon cell line screening platforms, PDAC, CRC, gastric cancer, TNBC, and, to a lower extent, NSCLC showed sensitivity to TR2/CDH3 BAB treatment. Furthermore, CDH3 expression represented the strongest discriminator between TR2/CDH3 BAB-sensitive and -insensitive cases in these data sets. For the multivalent TRAILR2 agonists TAS266 and Aplitabart, TRAILR2 expression levels in cancer cell lines were shown to correlate with a sensitive cytotoxic response. Similarly, the PRISM screening data presented here showed significantly elevated mean levels of TRAILR2 mRNA in the TR2/CDH3 BAB-high sensitivity cluster when compared to TR2/CDH3 BAB-insensitive cell lines. In vivo, we observed a trend toward augmented TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy in PDAC PDX tumors characterized by a high percentage of TRAILR2 and CDH3 mRNA co-expressing tumor cells. This provides further evidence that TR2/CDH3 BAB therapeutic efficacy depends on combined and abundant TRAILR2/CDH3 expression. Patients displaying a heterogeneous expression pattern of CDH3 in tumors might exhibit differential sensitivity, presumably dependent on the fraction of CDH3-positive tumor cells. This also implies that the loss of either one of the TR2/CDH3 BAB binding targets during therapy in tumor cells represents a potential mechanism of acquired resistance to this bispecific antibody. Nevertheless, the variability of TR2/CDH3 BAB efficacy in these PDX models was not fully explained by TRAILR2/CDH3 double-positivity, suggesting that also other mechanisms can render tumor cells non-responsive to TR2/CDH3 BAB treatment.

In addition to the expression of TRAILR2 and CDH3, an intact downstream signaling cascade is required to effectively stimulate extrinsic apoptosis signaling by TR2/CDH3 BAB. In systematic CRISPR/Cas9 screens designed to reveal resistance mechanisms to a multivalent TRAILR2 agonist (MEDI3039), mainly the key players of extrinsic apoptosis signaling, including TRAILR2 itself, FADD, BID, and CASP8 were identified, suggesting that down-modulation or loss of either one of these factors might render tumor cells tolerant to TR2/CDH3 BAB. Accordingly, the mean expression of CASP8 and BID mRNA was enriched in CDH3-positive TR2/CDH3 BAB-responders in the PRISM screen data set, and the genetic ablation of CASP8 rendered tumor cells non-responsive to our CDH3 anchor-dependent TRAILR2 agonist. Furthermore, the PDAC cell lines DAN-G, AsPC-1, CFPAC-1, and SUIT-2 were previously reported to express elevated levels of the anti-apoptotic c-FLIP isoform c-FLIPs, which negatively regulates TRAIL-induced apoptosis in pancreatic cancer models. This might at least partially explain the here observed non-responsiveness of some PDAC cell lines to TR2/CDH3 BAB. A better understanding of acquired resistance mechanisms in the context of TR2/CDH3 BAB-mediated TRAILR2 activation will be key for designing strategies able to achieve longer-lasting success of our therapeutic concept in cancer patients.

Sensitization of tumor cells to TRAIL-mediated apoptosis has been previously demonstrated for several modalities of cytotoxic chemotherapy (reviewed in). In gastric cancer cell lines, the administration of paclitaxel augments TRAIL-induced apoptosis in vitro and in vivo, which is accompanied by mitochondrial apoptosis pathway activation, down-modulation of anti-apoptotic proteins, and upregulation of TRAIL receptors. In accordance with our data, a study by Elia et al. demonstrated enhanced cell growth inhibition and efficient induction of caspase-dependent apoptosis following gemcitabine and TRAIL combination treatment of PDAC cell lines. Notably, TR2/CDH3 BAB rendered PDAC cell lines responsive to gemcitabine doses that on their own had proven largely ineffective. Even more important, combining TR2/CDH3 BAB with different chemotherapeutics achieved durable tumor regression or disease stabilization in at least partially single agent-tolerant PDAC PDX models.

Despite these promising preclinical data, several challenges will need to be addressed in the clinic. Especially for higher molecular weight compounds, the poor bioavailability in PDAC originating from the mostly hypovascular nature of the desmoplastic stroma surrounding the tumor cells represents a hurdle that needs to be overcome (reviewed in). Notably, although drug tolerability studies on IgG-based antibodies in non-human primates play an important role in human risk assessment, these preclinical models might not always accurately predict the therapeutic safety profile in humans, e.g., with respect to drug immunogenicity. Besides this, due to their time-limited nature, they are non-informative with regard to late toxicities that might potentially occur with our bispecific approach. Furthermore, the combination of TR2/CDH3 BAB with chemotherapy could lead to unforeseen toxicities, an aspect which needs to be carefully addressed in the clinic. A planned Phase 1 clinical trial in PDAC patients, which includes a dose escalation study to identify the best and safest drug dose, will provide us with the opportunity to confirm the efficacy and safety of our liver-sparing concept.

In summary, we demonstrate potent and selective induction of extrinsic apoptosis signaling by the TRAILR2 agonist TR2/CDH3 BAB. A strong anti-tumor activity was observed in different PDAC models in vitro and vivo, especially when TR2/CDH3 BAB was combined with different types of chemotherapeutic agents used for treatment of PDAC in the clinic. Together with its anchor-dependent design, which supports a favorable safety profile, our data support TR2/CDH3 BAB as a promising therapeutic strategy for PDAC patients.
