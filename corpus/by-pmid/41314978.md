# International real-world study of combination immunotherapy sequences in metastatic melanoma.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have revolutionized melanoma treatment, with programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) inhibitors-alone or in combination with cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 or lymphocyte-activation gene 3 inhibitors-demonstrating significant efficacy. However, there is a critical lack of robust data to determine the optimal sequencing of these therapies for individual patients. In particular, the role of relatlimab+nivolumab (rela/nivo) within treatment sequences remains poorly defined. Choosing the right sequence is strategic, as an inappropriate order may compromise the effectiveness of subsequent treatments and limit long-term benefits.

**METHODS**: This multicenter retrospective and prospective study evaluated 190 patients across three treatment arms: rela/nivo followed by ipilimumab+nivolumab (ipi/nivo) (arm A, N=40), ipi/nivo followed by rela/nivo (arm B, N=71), and anti-PD-1 followed by rela/nivo (arm C, N=79). The study assessed the impact of treatment sequencing on outcomes including response rate, progression-free survival, and overall survival (OS).

**RESULTS**: The overall response rate to second treatment was highest in arm C (30.4%), followed by arm B (28.1%) and arm A (17.5%). Patients with secondary resistance to first-treatment ICIs had better responses to second-treatment ICIs than those with primary resistance, particularly in arm B (p=0026). Median OS from date of first ICI treatment was significantly longer in arms B (40.9 months) and C (42.5 months) compared with arm A (16.3 months).

**CONCLUSIONS**: Our findings indicate that rela/nivo may remain active following anti-PD-1 or ipi/nivo therapy. Additionally, our results suggest that sequencing ipi/nivo before rela/nivo may yield better outcomes than starting with rela/nivo. Patients who respond to the first combination regimen appear to derive greater benefit from the second. Further efforts are needed to optimize sequencing strategies in advanced melanoma, and future studies should consider the impact of prior treatment outcomes.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI), including anti-PD-1±ipilimumab or relatlimab, are the current standard of care in advanced melanoma. However, optimal sequencing of treatment in terms of efficacy and toxicity is not known.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

Patients with advanced melanoma can benefit from sequences including relatlimab/nivolumab as first or second line, with acceptable toxicity. Resistance to first treatment may guide second-line strategies.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

This real-world multicenter study provides a rationale for sequencing ICI including relatlimab/nivolumab in advanced melanoma, suggesting that patients may derive clinical benefit with good tolerance. These findings may help guide treatment decisions and shape future research in melanoma immunotherapy.

Background

 Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have revolutionized the treatment landscape of melanoma. Programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) monoclonal antibodies, whether as monotherapy or in combination with anti-cytotoxic T-lymphocyte–associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) or anti-lymphocyte-activation gene 3, have demonstrated efficacy in the first-line setting, with objective and often durable responses observed in 33–45%, 58%, and 44% of patients, respectively. These three ICI-based regimens are standards of care for first-line therapy in advanced melanoma. However, they are associated with varying rates of adverse events (online supplemental table S1).

The selection of the immunotherapy regimen usually relies on clinical and biological factors such as cancer stage, disease kinetics, lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) level, patient age, comorbidities, history of autoimmune/inflammatory conditions, melanoma subtype and, occasionally, programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression. Despite these considerations, robust predictive biomarkers for tumor response remain lacking. For patients with limited tumor burden and slow-evolving melanoma, anti-PD-1 with or without relatlimab is frequently preferred due to the lower toxicity of these regimens compared with the higher risk of grade >3 adverse events observed with the ipilimumab+nivolumab combination (16%, 21% and 59%, respectively). The decision-making process is further influenced by the availability of rescue therapy in cases of resistance to first-line treatment. Data suggest that ipilimumab+nivolumab can achieve a response rate of approximately 30% after progression on anti-PD-1 therapy. However, the place of the most recently approved immunotherapy regimen, relatlimab+nivolumab, in the treatment strategy remains unclear.

To better understand the positioning of the relatlimab+nivolumab combination in clinical practice, we conducted a multicenter, retrospective and prospective study. This study evaluates the outcomes of patients with advanced melanoma treated with relatlimab+nivolumab either after anti-PD-1 therapy±ipilimumab or before ipilimumab+nivolumab in a real-world setting. Additionally, we examined the potential impact of the type of resistance (primary or secondary) to first-line ICIs across these three patient groups.

Methods

This multicenter retrospective and prospective study recruited patients from 10 medical centers in Europe, USA and Australia. Eligible patients had unresectable or metastatic stage III/IV melanoma as per American Joint Committee on Cancer Tumor–Node–Metastasis eighth edition, and had progressed on ICI in the adjuvant or metastatic setting. The first treatment ICI could have been given in the context of a clinical trial.

Patients who had received relatlimab/nivolumab (rela/nivo) and, at progression, ipilimumab/nivolumab (ipi/nivo) were assigned to arm A. Patients who had progressed to ipi/nivo and were treated with rela/nivo were assigned to arm B. Patients who had received anti-PD-1 monotherapy either in the adjuvant or metastatic setting and were treated with rela/nivo at progression were assigned to arm C.

The primary endpoint was to evaluate the efficacy of sequencing strategies involving rela/nivo, defined as investigator-reported overall response rate (ORR) to the second therapy. Secondary objectives included progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), and tolerability of treatment regimens. Treatment response was evaluated with CT or positron emission tomography (PET) scans, according to RECIST (Response Criteria in Solid Tumors) V.1.1. and PRECIST (PET Response Criteria in Solid Tumors). ORR was defined as achieving complete response (CR) or partial response (PR) to treatment. Disease control rate (DCR) was defined as achieving CR, PR, or stable disease.

Investigator-reported treatment-related adverse events (TRAE) were graded according to the Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events V.5.0 guidelines, and only adverse events of at least grade 2 were included for analysis. Primary and secondary resistance to first-treatment ICIs was defined according to the Society for Immunotherapy of Cancer consensus, with a minimum drug exposure of 6 weeks or two full cycles and resistance cut-offs set at 6 months for disease control in the advanced setting and 12 weeks in the adjuvant setting.

Investigator-reported ORR and their 95% CIs were calculated with a binomial distribution. To estimate differences between response rates and treatment groups, χ2 test or Fisher’s exact test was performed, depending on sample size and expected cell frequencies.

OS was calculated from the start of the first ICI treatment to death from any cause or latest check-up. PFS1 and PFS2 represent the PFS after the first and the second treatments, respectively, and total PFS was calculated from the start of the first ICI treatment until disease progression to second treatment, death or latest check-up. It is important to highlight that the selection of patients included in this study consists of those who were able to reach a second treatment, excluding those who passed away after the first treatment. Survival curves were estimated with the Kaplan-Meier method. Univariable and multivariable analyses for OS and PFS2 were carried out with Cox-proportional hazards, with results presented as hazards ratio (HR) and their 95% confidence interval (CI). Variables included in the multivariable models were selected based on expert knowledge. Finally, standardization based on the multivariable models (ie, parametric g-formula that accounts only for baseline confounders which gives estimations similar to the ones of inverse of propensity score weighting) was used to estimate marginal survival rates (OS and PFS2) at 3, 6, 12 and 24 months in each treatment arm, as if the whole population would have received treatment as in arm A, B and C respectively. CIs (95% coverage) of marginal survival rates were estimated using bootstrapping (n=2,000 iterations, percentile method). A p value of <0.05 was considered statistically significant. Statistical analyses were performed using IBM-SPSS V.29.0.2.0 software and R studio V.4.3.2.

Results

Patients’ characteristics and treatment exposure

Between April 2021 and May 2024, 190 patients were included in the study. A total of 40 patients were included in arm A (rela/nivo and, at progression, ipi/nivo), 71 in arm B (ipi/nivo, and at progression, rela/nivo) and 79 in arm C (anti-PD-1 monotherapy and, at progression, rela/nivo) (figure 1).

Baseline characteristics before initiation of the second immunotherapy regimen are summarized in table 1. They were more favorable in arm C, with a lower prevalence of stage IV M1c-d, fewer patients with elevated LDH levels, and fewer patients with three or more metastatic sites compared with arms A and B. 19 patients (24.0%) had received the first ICI regimen as an adjuvant treatment in arm C, 9 of whom were primary resistant.

The rate of patients with primary resistance to the first treatment was higher in arms A (60%) and C (65.6%) compared with arm B (39.4%). Patients in arm B were more frequently pretreated before the first regimen (40.5%) versus 12.5% in A and 13% in C. They also had more patients with brain metastases (26.8% vs 15% in arm A and 5.1% in arm C) and with more than three metastatic sites (58.1% vs 40% in arm A and 15.2% in arm C).

The median first-treatment duration was 2.8 months (0.0–23.6) for rela/nivo (arm A), 2.5 months (0.0–29.6) for ipi/nivo (arm B), and 7.0 months (0.9–32.5) for anti-PD-1 (arm C). For the second treatment, the median duration was 1.4 months (0.0–8.8) for ipi/nivo (arm A) and 3.1 months (0.4–37.8) and 3.9 months (0.0–61.3) for rela/nivo (arms B and C, respectively) (table 1 and online supplemental figure S1). At data cut-off, after a median follow-up of 21.7 months for arm A, 28.6 months for arm B, and 67.2 months for arm C, 32.5% (arm A), 53.5% (arm B), and 38% (arm C) were alive, with 5, 15, and 12 patients still on treatment (online supplemental figure S2).

Response rates

The primary end-point—investigator-assessed ORR for the second treatment—was 17.5% (95% CI 7.7% to 34.3%) for ipi/nivo (arm A), 28.1% (95% CI 18.7% to 40.7%) for rela/nivo in arm B and 30.4% (95% CI 22.0% to 44.3%) for rela/nivo in arm C (table 2). Disease control lasting more than 6 months was observed in 28.9% (95% CI 15.4% to 45.9%) of patients in arm A, 39.1% (95% CI 27.6% to 51.6%) in arm B and 41.9% (95% CI 30.5% to 53.7%) in arm C. No statistically significant differences were seen in ORRs or DCRs between treatment arms.

A statistically significant increased ORR was observed in patients with secondary resistance to first-treatment ICIs (46.4%, 95% CI 29.9% to 70.1%) compared with those with primary resistance (14.3%, 95% CI 0.4% to 32.7%) in arm B (p=0.026). In arm A, a numerically higher ORR was also seen in patients with secondary resistance (40%) versus primary resistance (8.2%) (p=0.08). Excluding patients with uveal and mucosal melanoma did not yield statistically significant results either (online supplemental figures S3 and S4). Similar ORR to rela/nivo were observed for primary resistant (28.0%) and secondary resistant (35.2%) to anti-PD-1 monotherapy in arm C (figure 2 and online supplemental figure S5). For patients who received anti-PD-1 as an adjuvant treatment (25.3%), ORR to rela/nivo was 52.6% (95% CI 28.9% to 75.6%) versus 23.3% (95% CI 14.7% to 39.0%) for those who were treated at the metastatic stage, these differences reaching statistical significance (p=0.029) (online supplemental figures S6 and S7).

The proportion of patients who responded to both first and second treatments varied across treatment group: 5% (n=2) in arm A, 14.1% (n=10) in arm B, and 6.3% (n=5) in arm C. Among patients whose best response to first line was PD, 12.5% patients in arm A (3/24), 16.1% in arm B (5/31) and 21.6% (8/37) in arm C achieved an objective response to second treatment. Sankey plots illustrating treatment arms and corresponding responses are shown in figure 3.

Survival analysis

Figure 4 shows Kaplan-Meier survival curves. The median PFS to the second regimen (PFS2) was 2.6 months (95% CI 2.0 to 3.2) in arm A, 4.4 months (95% CI 2.4 to 6.4) in arm B, and 3.8 (95% CI 3.0 to 4.6) in arm C. The 2-year PFS2 rate was 6% for arm A, 25% for arm B and 22% for arm C. Median OS from the first treatment was 16.3 months (95% CI 10.2 to 22.5) in arm A, 40.9 months (95% CI 24.3 to 53.6) in arm B, and 42.5 months (95% CI 30.3 to 54.8) in arm C. The 2-year OS rate was 35% for arm A, 64% for arm B and 68% for arm C. Statistically significant differences in PFS2 and OS were observed when comparing arm A with arms B and C (figure 4).

PFS2 and OS differed significantly between adjuvant-treated and metastatic patients in arm C—with median PFS2 of 13.5 months versus 3.7 months, and median OS not reached versus 32.7 months, respectively. However, excluding adjuvant-treated patients from arm C did not impact the survival benefit (PFS and OS) observed of arms B and C compared with arm A (online supplemental figure S8). Excluding patients with uveal and mucosal melanoma did not alter the statistically significant worse survival observed in this cohort (online supplemental figures S9 and S10).

Univariable analysis found an association between primary resistance and PFS2 in arm A, with significantly poorer PFS2 in patients with primary resistance to rela/nivo (HR 2.13, 95% CI 1.02 to 4.44). For OS, primary resistance was statistically significant with reduced OS only in group C (HR for OS of 3.15; 95% CI 1.72 to 5.78, p<0001) (online supplemental figure S11).

Multivariable analysis for PFS2 suggested that primary resistance to first treatment was an independent factor that contributed to decreased PFS2, while high LDH and the presence of more than three metastatic sites were associated with decreased PFS2 and OS. Treatment with ipi/nivo followed by rela/nivo (arm B) appeared to benefit PFS2 and OS (figure 5). Excluding patients with uveal or mucosal melanoma showed increased OS for patients treated with anti-PD-1 followed by rela/nivo (arm C) (online supplemental figures S12 and S13).

When estimating marginal effects of treatment in arms A, B, and C in the whole population (instead of conditional effects as in the previous multivariable models) results were quite similar, with a trend in 12, 24 and 36 months differences in risks toward a better PFS2 and OS in arms B and C compared with arm A (online supplemental tables S2–S4).

Safety

Investigator-reported TRAEs were observed in 27.5% of patients with rela/nivo and 37.5% with ipi/nivo in arm A. In arm B, rates were 59.2% with ipi/nivo and 28.2% with rela/nivo, while in arm C, they were 31.6% with anti-PD-1 and 29.1% with rela/nivo (online supplemental table S5). Only a minority of patients experienced >grade 2 TRAEs across both treatments: 15% in arm A, 18.3% in arm B, and 15.1% in arm C. Most patients had different adverse events between treatments, though some had recurrent events including vitritis (arm A), hepatitis (arm B), and arthralgia (arm C).

The most frequent TRAE reported during the second treatment in arm A were colitis (15.0%) and hepatitis (7.5%). In arm B, the most reported events were arthralgia (5.6%), arthritis (4.2%) and fatigue (4.2%), whereas in arm C, they were represented by arthritis (5.1%) and fatigue (3.8%). Discontinuation due to toxicity occurred in 35% of arm A, 9.8% of arm B, and 8.9% of arm C (online supplemental figure S14). No treatment-related deaths were reported.

Discussion

Our study provides real-world data on outcomes and safety of patients receiving sequential immunotherapies involving rela/nivo. Our findings suggest that patients who received rela/nivo as a second treatment after progression on anti-PD-1±ipilimumab (arms B and C) experienced the highest numerical benefit in terms of response rates, with ORR of 28.1% and 30.4%, respectively, versus 17.5% in patients receiving ipi/nivo following rela/nivo. These results are further supported by statistically significant improvements in both PFS and OS in arms receiving rela/nivo as their last considered treatment (B and C) compared with those receiving ipi/nivo (arm A) as their last considered treatment, underscoring the potential efficacy of rela/nivo in the second or further-line setting. Notably, the ORR benefit in arm A was numerically higher and in arm B significantly higher in patients who had previously benefited from combination ICIs compared with those with primary resistance. In contrast, primary resistance to anti-PD-1 monotherapy did not impair the response to subsequent rela/nivo treatment in arm C compared with patients with secondary resistance. This distinction suggests potential differences in resistance mechanisms between progression on combination therapy versus monotherapy, as previously addressed in prior studies.

The 2023 American Society of Clinical Oncology guidelines recommend ipilimumab or ipilimumab-containing regimens following anti-PD-1 progression treatment. While no randomized trials have specifically evaluated these strategies, phase II trials assessing anti-PD-1 and ipilimumab combinations have reported ORRs ranging from 20% to 29%, with grade 3–4 TRAE in 27–50% of patients. Retrospective data report ORRs for ipi/nivo following anti-PD-1 ranging from 20% to 33%, with grade 3–4 TRAE 31–33%. Only one retrospective study including 36 patients treated with CTLA-4±PD-1 inhibition following rela/nivo has reported outcomes, showing a poor ORR of 11% and a median OS of only 9.6 months. However, only 17 patients in this study were treated with CTLA-4+PD-1 inhibition, and no efficacy within this specific group of patients was reported due to its small size. Our results characterize the efficacy of ipi/nivo following rela/nivo in the largest dataset available to our knowledge and suggest a less unfavorable outcome with an ORR of 17.5%, and a median OS of 16.3 months.

Two previous studies investigated the sequencing of rela/nivo after anti-PD-1 (arm C). The RELATIVITY-020 phase I/IIa trial reported a modest ORR of 9–12% for rela/nivo in patients progressing to anti-PD-1. Another retrospective study, which included 32 patients, showed a higher ORR of 33% for the combination rela/nivo. Our study is in line with this latter one with an ORR of 30,4%. This is also the regimen with the more favorable survival parameters in OS, with a median OS of 42.5 months, and a median PFS2 of nearly 4 months. This might also be explained by the presence of 25% of patients treated in the adjuvant setting, which showed statistically significant increased benefit as compared with metastatic patients in ORR (52.6% vs 23.3%), median PFS2 (13.5 months vs 3.7 months) and median OS (not reached vs 32.7 months).

Our study provides the largest cohort of patients receiving the sequence of rela/nivo following ipi/nivo (arm B). It demonstrates a surprisingly high response rate of 28.1%, especially in secondary resistant patients who experience an ORR of 46.4%, with median PFS2 of 4.5 months, and median OS of 41 months. Notably, in the pooled cohort, arm B emerged as an independent factor associated with improved PFS to second treatment ICI and OS in multivariable analysis, despite a higher proportion of heavily pretreated patients and a greater burden of metastatic disease, including brain metastases. A lower proportion of female patients was also observed in this arm, which—although not statistically significant—may reflect patient or physician-driven treatment decisions, possibly influenced by concerns about toxicity or by reports suggesting sex-related differences in immunotherapy outcomes. Furthermore, our results indicate that this sequencing strategy exhibits no additional safety concerns with grade 2 TRAE in 28.2% of patients. Although some patients who progress on rela/nivo—particularly those who initially derived benefit from this therapy—may still achieve a clinically meaningful response with subsequent ipi/nivo, this specific sequence of ICI regimen (arm A) is associated with poorer efficacy outcomes and the highest discontinuation rates due to toxicity compared with the other treatment arms. Nevertheless, the overall incidence of TRAE with ipi/nivo in the second-line setting appeared lower than expected from previous evidence, which may reflect a selection bias: patients eligible for second-line treatment with this strategy are typically more clinically fit and have survived first-line combination therapy, thus representing a subgroup with better tolerance and prognosis.

Although we cannot exclude the possibility that some benefit of the second treatment may be attributed to anti-PD-1 rechallenge rather than the different ICI regimen, this hypothesis is unlikely for most patients given the short median treatment-free interval between the two lines of therapy—1.2 months, 2.9 months and 1.5 months for arms A, B and C, respectively.

The main limitation of our study is the heterogeneity across baseline prognostic parameters of patients in the three groups that is inherent to the non-randomized and retrospective design of the study and introduced inevitable bias. The relatively short treatment durations observed, particularly with combination regimens, likely reflect early disease progression, limited treatment tolerance, or aggressive clinical behavior in this real-world cohort. Additionally, disparities in regulatory approvals and access to rela/nivo across participating countries represent a further limitation. In some settings, its use is restricted to PD-L1–negative melanoma or confined to the first-line setting, which may have influenced treatment sequencing decisions, the frequency of PD-L1 testing, and overall patient selection.

Conclusion

Despite the limitation of an observational, non-randomized study, our results based on real-world data from 10 teams worldwide indicate that patients with melanoma can benefit from sequential ICI treatments involving relatlimab and nivolumab as first or second treatment. It shows that patients who have benefited from an ICI combination are more likely to respond to a subsequent immunotherapy than those with primary resistance. Our study underscores the importance of taking into account the entire patient’s journey and suggests that prior treatment responses may serve as useful predictors of potential benefit from immunotherapies.
