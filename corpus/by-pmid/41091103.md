# Derivation of AZD5335, a Novel FRα-Targeted TOP1i-Loaded ADC, for the Treatment of FRα-Expressing Cancers.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Folate receptor α (FRα) is expressed in most ovarian cancers. However, only patients with high expression levels are eligible for Elahere, an FRα-targeted microtubule inhibitor antibody-drug conjugate (ADC). Efficacy limitations and safety concerns underscore the need to develop next-generation FRα-targeted ADC to treat tumors expressing variable levels of FRα and incorporate different payloads to reduce safety risks. Herein, we present the characterization of AZD5335, a novel FRα-targeted topoisomerase-1 inhibitor ADC.

**EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN**: The efficacy of AZD5335 was assessed and correlated with FRα expression using cell- and patient-derived models. Focusing on models with low FRα, AZD5335 was directly compared with an Elahere analogue. Additionally, AZD5335 was evaluated in a model of acquired Elahere resistance. Combined treatments, including AZD5335 plus either standard-of-care drugs or a PARP1 inhibitor, were also explored.

**RESULTS**: A single dose of AZD5335 (2.5 mg/kg) achieved an overall response rate of 82% in patient-derived ovarian cancer xenografts (n = 17). Antitumor responses were observed in models expressing both high and low levels of FRα. Specifically within FRα-low models, AZD5335 demonstrated superiority over an Elahere analogue. In the context of acquired Elahere resistance, AZD5335 treatments resulted in complete tumor regressions. Additionally, combining AZD5335 with standard-of-care drugs or a PARP1 inhibitor resulted in enhanced efficacy and sustained durability. Two clinical case studies that demonstrated significant AZD5335 responses in tumors exhibiting high and low FRα expression are also provided.

**CONCLUSIONS**: AZD5335 is a promising next-generation ADC capable of targeting ovarian cancers with both high and low FRα expression. AZD5335 demonstrates efficacy in overcoming Elahere resistance and supports combined treatment strategies.

Translational Relevance

AZD5335 is a novel FRα-targeted antibody–drug conjugate (ADC) incorporating a potent topoisomerase-1 inhibitor (TOP1i) payload. Here, we describe the preclinical development of AZD5335 for FRα-expressing ovarian cancers. Our studies confirm that the cytotoxic effects of AZD5335 are mediated via FRα and are associated with TOP1i DNA damage response mechanisms. A comprehensive assessment of AZD5335 monotherapy within a cohort of patient-derived ovarian cancer xenograft models demonstrates robust and durable antitumor responses. Notably, AZD5335 efficacy was observed in tumors with both high and low levels of FRα expression, marking a significant differentiation from Elahere (mirvetuximab soravtansine), a clinically approved anti-FRα ADC restricted to tumors with high FRα expression levels. Beyond monotherapy, we also demonstrated the efficacy of AZD5335 in combination with standard-of-care drugs for ovarian cancer (carboplatin and/or bevacizumab) and agents exploiting TOP1i synthetic lethality such as saruparib, a PARP1 inhibitor. In summary, these preclinical studies position AZD5335 as a promising next-generation FRα-targeted ADC, leading to clinical investigations in FRα-expressing tumor types (NCT No. 05797168).

Introduction

Folate receptor α (FRα), encoded by FOLR1, is a GPI-anchored cell surface receptor involved in folate transport. FRα exhibits limited expression within normal tissues; however, it is enriched within multiple tumor types. The elevated expression levels observed within ovarian cancers highlight FRα as a relevant target for therapeutic intervention. Although various FRα-targeted treatment strategies have been described, recent clinical advancements and the approval of Elahere (mirvetuximab soravtansine-gynx), an FRα-targeted microtubule inhibitor (MTi) antibody–drug conjugate (ADC; refs.), validate FRα as an ADC-relevant target.

Elahere confers significant progression-free and overall survival benefits for patients with platinum-resistant ovarian cancer; however, clinical usage is restricted to only a subset of cases defined by high levels of FRα expression (≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity; ref.). Additionally, Elahere and other FRα-targeted MTi ADC [i.e., MORAb-202 and STRO-002 ] in development are confounded by safety liabilities associated with their payloads. These efficacy and safety limitations highlight the importance of developing next-generation FRα-targeted ADC capable of targeting patients with variable FRα expression and carrying alternative payloads to minimize safety risks and maximize antitumor potency.

AZD5335 is a novel FRα-targeted ADC containing a potent topoisomerase-1 inhibitor (TOP1i) payload (AZ14170132). Topoisomerase-1 (TOP1) removes DNA supercoils and resolves the torsional stress that occurs during DNA transcription and replication. TOP1i specifically and reversibly trap TOP1–DNA cleavage complexes, which leads to replication- and transcription-mediated DNA damage, double-strand DNA breaks, and ultimately, cell death. DNA repair mechanisms are frequently impaired in tumors, and defects in these pathways have been shown to enhance TOP1i sensitivity. As a result, TOP1i is an attractive anticancer therapy. ADC with TOP1i payloads have achieved antitumor responses in heavily pretreated, treatment-refractory patients with cancer. Multiple TOP1i ADC are currently in clinical development, and three agents have been approved for clinical use across a broad range of tumor types, Trodelvy (sacituzumab govitecan-hziy; ref.), Enhertu (trastuzumab deruxtecan; ref.), and Datroway (datopotamab deruxtecan; ref.).

Here, we describe the preclinical characterization and efficacy of AZD5335 as either monotherapy or combination therapy in the context of differential FRα expression levels in ovarian cancer. Insights from these studies have led to FONTANA, a phase I/IIa clinical study of AZD5335 in patients with solid tumor types (NCT No. 05797168). Two clinical cases from the FONTANA trial offer additional insights into the effects of AZD5335 in patients exhibiting high and low expression levels of FRα.

Materials and Methods

Antibody discovery and screening

Antibodies targeting human FRα were generated by hybridoma technology using humanized transgenic Del-1 mice (engineered to express fully human antibody variable domains) that were immunized with recombinant human FRα extracellular domain (ECD) expressed in a suspension Chinese hamster ovary cell line. Antibody INT-019 was selected based on affinity, specificity, and cross-reactivity to human and cynomolgus monkey FRα and a high rate of internalization to KB cells. The heavy- and light-chain amino acid sequences for INT-019 are described previously in PCT Publication Number WO 2023/169896 A1. Detailed information about biochemical and surface plasmon resonance assays is provided in the Supplementary Methods.

Derivation and composition of AZD5335

AZD5335 was derived from the lead anti-FRα antibody AB1370049. AB1370049 was produced via hybridoma technology using humanized transgenic Del-1 mice (engineered to express fully human antibody variable domains) that were immunized with recombinant human FRα ECD with a repetitive immunization multiple-site strategy. Four days after the final boost, lymph nodes were aseptically harvested, and cells were isolated by mechanical disruption and then counted. These cells were mixed with SP2/0 myeloma cells (RRID: CVCL_2199) and fused using an electrofusion apparatus. Clonal colonies of hybridoma cells were grown out in semi-solid media. These colonies secrete IgG that is trapped in the vicinity of the colony with a FITC-conjugated anti-IgG present in the semi-solid media. The resultant immune complex formation is observed as a fluorescent “halo” when visualized by ClonePix FL colony, allowing colonies to be picked into 96-well microtiter plates. The supernatants of the picked colonies were harvested and screened for human FRα binding. AB1370049 was identified by binding to FRα using homogeneous time-resolved fluorescence (HTRF) binding assays. Hybridoma cell line supernatants were added to HTRF assays in which binding to either human, mouse, or cynomolgus FRα ECD that was biotinylated and bound to streptavidin cryptate was measured with an anti–mouse IgG antibody conjugated with AF647. IgG that were identified in the hybridoma supernatant screen as binding FRα were purified using Protein A Phynexus tips, and binding was confirmed in the FRα HTRF binding assays. cDNA was prepared from AB1370049 clones and sequenced for VH and VL. The VH cDNA was then cloned into a mammalian expression vector for human IgG1 heavy chain, and the VL cDNA was cloned into a mammalian expression vector for human IgG1κ light chain. Expression of these cloned constructs yielded an antibody intermediate designated INT-019. The drug–linker AZ14170133 containing the TOP1i drug AZ14170132 was covalently bound to native cysteines in the antibody through a thiosuccinimide linkage, with approximately eight drugs bound per antibody. This ADC is the candidate drug AZD5335, depicted in Fig. 1A.

Conjugation of antibody intermediate INT-019 with AZ14170133 (DAR8) to generate AZD5335

A 50-mmol/L solution of Tris (2-carboxyethyl)-phosphin-HCl (Pierce) in PBS pH 7.4 (Gibco) was added (50 M equivalent/antibody, 16.7 μmol/L, 333 μL) to a 20-mL solution of INT-019 antibody; 50 mg each, 333 nmol/L in reduction buffer containing PBS and 1-mmol/L EDTA (Molekula) to a final antibody concentration of 2.5 mg/mL. The reduction mixture was heated at +37°C for 2 hours [or until full reduction was observed by ultrahigh-performance liquid chromatography (UHPLC)] in an incubated orbital shaker with gentle (60 rpm) shaking. After cooling to room temperature, excess reducing agent was removed via spin filter centrifugation into PBS +1-mmol/L EDTA using a 15-mL Amicon Ultracell 50-kDa MWCO spin filter. AZ14170133 was added as a DMSO solution (12- to 24-mol/L equivalent/antibody, 4.8–9.0 μmol/L, in 2.0- to 2.3-mL DMSO) to 18 to 19 mL of this reduced antibody solution (37.0–46.9 mg, 247–313 nmol/L) at 2.0 to 2.5 mg/mL for a 10% (v/v) final DMSO concentration. The solution was mixed for 1 to 18 hours at room temperature, and then the conjugation was quenched by the addition of N-acetyl cysteine (22.2–45.2 μmol/L, 222–452 μL at 100 mmol/L) for >30 minutes at room temperature, then the solution was sterile filtered, then purified by spin filtration (PBS, followed by 20-mmol/L histidine +240-mmol/L sucrose pH 6.0), and concentrated using a 15-mL Amicon Ultracell 30-kDa MWCO spin filter, sterile-filtered, and analyzed.

Analytic characterization of AZD5335

Determination of drug-per-antibody ratio (DAR) by reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography: UHPLC analysis on a Shimadzu Prominence system using a Thermo Fisher Scientific MAbPac 50- × 2.1-mm column eluting with a gradient of water and acetonitrile on a reduced sample of ADC at 214 and 330 nm (AZ14170133 specific) shows a mixture of unconjugated light chains, light chains attached to a single molecule of AZ14170133, unconjugated heavy chains, and heavy chains attached to up to three molecules of AZ14170133. The DAR was calculated from the % areas of these peaks at 214 nm. Measurement of monomeric purity by size-exclusion high-performance liquid chromatography (SEC-HPLC): UHPLC analysis was performed on a Shimadzu Prominence system using a Tosoh Bioscience TSKgel SuperSW mAb HTP 4-µm 4.6- × 150-mm column (with a 4-µm 3.0- × 20-mm guard column) eluting with 0.3-mL/minute sterile-filtered SEC buffer containing 200-mmol/L potassium phosphate pH 6.95, 250-mmol/L potassium chloride, and 10% isopropanol (v/v) on a sample of AZD5335 at 280 nm. Hydrophobicity and DAR distribution by hydrophobic interaction chromatography high-performance liquid chromatography: UHPLC analysis on a Shimadzu Prominence system using a Sepax Butyl NP5 4.6- × 35-mm column eluting with a gradient of 80% 25-mmol/L NaPi (pH 7.40), 20% acetonitrile in 1.5 mol/L (NH4)2SO4, and 25-mmol/L NaPi (pH 7.40) on a diluted sample of AZD5335 mixed 1:1 with 1.5-mol/L (NH4)2SO4 and 25-mmol/L NaPi (pH 7.40) shows a mixture of species reflective of the DAR. The DAR was calculated from the % areas of these peaks at 214 nm. Determination of concentration by UV:UV-based analysis was performed in triplicate on an Unchained Labs Stunner instrument using the “ADC EC” program or on a Thermo Fisher Scientific NanoDrop 2000 instrument, using ADC extinction coefficient and Beer–Lambert law. Endotoxin content by LAL test: Endotoxin content was measured using a Charles River 0.5 to 0.005 EU/mL LAL test cartridge on a Charles River Endosafe PTS or Nexgen-PTS instrument.

Receptor quantification of gynecologic cancer cell lines

Receptor copy number was determined by quantitation of antibody binding sites using FACS staining in conjunction with a calibration curve prepared using Quantum Simply Cellular anti-Human IgG beads. Jeg-3 (RRID: CVCL_0363), KB (RRID: CVCL_0372), and IGROV-1 (RRID: CVCL_1304) cells were plated in a 96-well U-bottom plate and were incubated for 15 minutes with a pretitrated, saturating amount of Human TruStain FcXtrade (BioLegend), an Fc blocking solution, to prevent nonspecific antibody binding. Human FOLR1 antibody (INT-019) in a pre-titrated, saturating amount was added to the plated cells and to the Quantum Simply Cellular anti–Human IgG microspheres. The samples and beads were incubated for 30 minutes in the dark at 4°C. Alexa Fluor 647 Goat anti–Human IgG (H + L) Cross-Adsorbed Secondary Antibody (Molecular Probes, cat. No. A-21445, RRID: AB_2535862) was then added to the cell suspensions and Quantum Simply Cellular Beads and incubated for 30 minutes in the dark at 4°C. The samples and beads were analyzed by flow cytometry. A standard curve was raised using the GeoMean of each bead population’s fluorescence within the QuickCal software (Bangs Laboratories). With the derived regression coefficient and detection threshold established, the number of binding sites per cell was calculated using the beads as the standard.

 In vitro cytotoxicity assays

Jeg-3 (RRID: CVCL_0363), KB (RRID: CVCL_0372), and IGROV-1 (RRID: CVCL_1304) cells were each seeded at a density of 1,500 cells/well in 80 µL of complete growth media in white polystyrene tissue culture–treated 96-well plates, and plates were allowed to incubate overnight in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2 at 37°C. Treatments were prepared as 3× stocks in complete growth medium containing 10% heat-inactivated FBS, and 40 µL was spiked for a final volume of 120 µL per well. A titration curve of 666.66 to 0.001707 nmol/L was used for all three cell lines. Cells were then incubated in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2 at 37°C for 6 days. At the end of the experiment, 120 µL of CellTiter-Glo (CTG) 2.0 Reagent was added to each well. Plates were then incubated at room temperature on an orbital shaker for 2 minutes to induce cell lysis. Plates were then further incubated for 10 minutes at room temperature to further stabilize the luminescent signal. All incubations following the addition of CTG were conducted in dark conditions. Luminescence was measured using an EnVision 2105 Multilabel Reader. The test article potency was determined by generating IC50 values using a nonlinear regression model (log inhibitor vs. response) in GraphPad Prism, version 8.1, and presented as percent cell viability relative to mock-treated control cells (media alone) using the formula [(treated cells)/(mock-treated control cells)] × 100.

 In vitro combination cell viability assays

IGROV-1 (RRID: CVCL_1304), KB (RRID: CVCL_0372), and Jeg-3 (RRID: CVCL_0363) cells were seeded at a density of 350 cells/well in 60 µL of complete growth media in white 384-well polystyrene tissue culture–treated plates (Greiner, No. 781098). Two plates were seeded per cell line; one for the day 0 control and the other for the 6-day assay readout. After 24 hours, cells were dosed with AZD5335 either as a monotherapy or in combination with saruparib, bevacizumab (Genentech), or carboplatin (Ingenus Pharmaceuticals, LLC). Compounds were dosed using a five-point dose curve with concentrations ranging from 0.104 to 30 µg/mL for AZD5335, 0.042 to 3 µmol/L for saruparib, 2.125 to 300 µg/mL for bevacizumab, and 0.104 to 10 µmol/L for carboplatin. Automated dosing of these compounds was carried out using the Echo 655T robot (Beckman Coulter). Cells were then incubated in a humidified atmosphere with 5% CO2 at 37°C for 6 days. At the designated time point, day 0 or day 6, plates and CTG 2.0 Reagent (Promega) were equilibrated to room temperature for 30 minutes. Then, 30 µL of CTG 2.0 Reagent was added, and plates were shaken on an orbital shaker for 2 minutes to induce cell lysis. Plates were then incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes to ensure complete lysis. Luminescence was measured using an EnVision Nexus (PerkinElmer). Plates were normalized to their day 0 control plate. Combination activity was calculated and analyzed using the highest single agent (HSA) model of synergy via Genedata Screener software. HSA was reported as it reflects the clinical approach of assessing limited doses and datasets, as well as prioritizing combinations that outperform the most effective single agent.

FRα IHC

Anti–rabbit FRα monoclonal antibody [clone (EPR20277)] used for IHC was obtained from Abcam (Abcam, cat. No. ab221543, RRID: AB_2920895) and diluted in Antibody Diluent with background reducing agents (Agilent, cat. No. S3022). IHC was performed on a Ventana Discovery Ultra instrument (Roche Diagnostics). Sections of formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded tissue were cut at 4 µm, placed on StarFrost microscope slides, and loaded onto the auto-stainer. Slides were antigen-retrieved with Ultra Cell Conditioning 1 (Roche Diagnostic, cat. No. 05424569001) and blocked with Inhibitor CM (Roche Diagnostics, cat. No. 05266645001). Then, sections were incubated with primary antibody, horseradish peroxidase was blocked with DISCOVERY Inhibitor (Roche, cat. No. 07017944001), and the sample was incubated with anti–rabbit IgG horseradish peroxidase secondary antibody (Roche, cat. No. 5269717001, RRID: AB_3697448). Staining was visualized by brown 3,3′ diaminobenzidine (DAB) with the DISCOVERY ChromoMap DAB Kit (Roche Diagnostics, cat. No. 05266645001). Sections were counterstained with Hematoxylin II (Roche Diagnostics, cat. No. 05277965001) and bluing reagents (Roche Diagnostics, cat. No. 05266769001). Finally, sections were dehydrated in graded ethanol, cleared in xylene, and cover-slipped. Afterward, the glass slides were digitized using Aperio AT2 scanner and uploaded to our internal digital pathology platform for pathology review. The IHC images were then evaluated by a qualified pathologist who provided a numerical value to reflect FRα intensity and proportion of stained cells within tissue sample, ranging from 0 to 300. The semiquantitative method used is Histoscore, which is calculated by multiplying the percentage of invasive tumor cells in each staining intensity by the corresponding score and then summing the results. The pathologist visually assessed percentages of the invasive tumor cells into four staining intensity levels and the corresponding score were negative (0), weak (1), moderate (2), and strong (3). For clinical samples from FONTANA, FRα IHC using anti–mouse FRα monoclonal antibody [clone (FOLR1-2.1)] was performed by Roche Tissue Diagnostics, according to the methods described in the VENTANA FOLR1 (FOLR1-2.1) RxDx Assay package insert.

Image analysis quantification method for assessing FRα IHC expression

Whole-slide digital images were analyzed with the improved workflow as previously described. For region segmentation, particularly the detection of epithelial tumor regions, we used EfficientNetV2-S for fine-grained details and ResNet18 to provide global contextual information. Tumor regions were then segmented into cell instances by applying a second deep learning network for pixel classification into background, membrane, cytosol, cell centers, and nuclei: a DeepLabV3+ convolutional neural network architecture with an EfficientNetV2-S backbone was used. Both networks were trained in a supervised setting on a proprietary in-house dataset of expert-annotated IHC samples, spanning multiple tumor indications and IHC targets. Assessment of cellular segmentation was performed manually by a pathologist, confirming the proper identification of the tumor cells and their adequate segmentation into nuclear, cytoplasmic, and membranous components with at least 80% accuracy. Readouts were then generated using Python v3.10 and calculating the staining intensity or optical density (OD) in the different compartments, per cell, aggregating them at the slide level by using different quantiles and parameters. The main readouts investigated here are the membrane median OD (median OD) and the percentage of positive cells (based on membrane intensity). For each model, the target expression is represented as the mean of the readouts in the three tumors collected from independent, untreated study mice. The Wilcoxon rank-sum test was used to compare target expression in different groups; Spearman correlation coefficients were used to assess the correlation of the target with median response.

 In vivo AZD5335 pharmacology studies

All animal experiments were conducted in a facility accredited by the Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care under the guidelines of an approved AstraZeneca Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee. Animal studies conducted in the United Kingdom were performed in accordance with the United Kingdom Animal (Scientific Procedures) Act 1986/Directive 2010/63/EU. The cell-derived xenografts (CDX) of KB (RRID: CVCL_0372), IGROV-1 (RRID: CVCL_1304), SKOV-3 (RRID: CVCL_0532), OVCAR-3 (RRID: CVCL_0465), and OV90 (RRID: CVCL_3768) were established by subcutaneous right flank implantation of cells in 50% Matrigel (R&D Systems) into female CB-17 SCID (RRID: IMSR_CRL:236, Charles River Laboratories) or female NOD/SCID gamma (NSG; RRID: IMSR_JAX:005557, The Jackson Laboratory) mice. Although CB17-SCID were sufficient to establish robust KB and IGROV-1 xenografts, OVCAR-3 and SKOV-3 xenografts were variably and poorly tumorigenic. NSG provided an alternative approach to using OVCAR-3 and SKOV-3 in vivo. Tumors grew to a mean tumor volume of 150 to 250 mm3 before randomization according to tumor size. Patient-derived xenografts (PDX) were established by subcutaneous implantation into the flank of female NSG mice (The Jackson Laboratory). Several of the PDX models were tested at Champions Oncology. All mice were treated intravenously with AZD5335 or isotype-ADC diluted in 20-mmol/L histidine, pH 6, 240-mmol/L sucrose, and 0.02% PS-80. For saruparib studies, saruparib (AZD5305) was formulated in water/HCl pH 3.5 to 4 and administered by oral gavage once daily at 10-mL/kg final dose volume. Carboplatin was formulated in 0.9% physiologic saline prepared freshly on the day of dosing and administered intraperitoneally at a 5-mL/kg final dose volume. Bevacizumab was prepared using PBS and administered intraperitoneally at a 5-mL/kg final dose volume. Untreated refers to tumor-bearing animals that received vehicle(s). Tumor and body weights were measured once or twice weekly, and tumor volume was calculated using the equation, tumor volume (mm3) = [length (mm) × width (mm)2]/2. The median response was calculated based on the tumor volume at the analysis time point (ATV) compared with the initial tumor volume (ITV) from the start of dosing (ITV). The percent change was calculated as tumor growth (%) = [(ATV–ITV)/ITV] × 100. If the tumor volume increased between the ITV and the ATV, the tumor growth % will be a positive value. Models were considered responsive to the test agents if the percent tumor volume from baseline was −30% to −100%, inclusive. Reported and graphed tumor growth % represent the median tumor growth of the mice in the study at the time the ATV was recorded. The growth of tumors in each experimental group was expressed as the mean tumor volume (mm3) ± SEM of the number of animals used. Mice were removed from the study if the tumor volume was ≥ 2,500 mm3 and/or body weight loss was ≥20%.

Statistical analysis

For the mouse PDX/CDX tumor growth experiments conducted with various isotype controls and treatment conditions, statistical analysis was systematically employed to evaluate tumor shrinkage and treatment efficacy. The final observation day of the untreated group was designated as the time point for comparing different experimental conditions. For each group, the mean, SD, and SE of tumor volumes are presented along with tumor growth inhibition (TGI). Pairwise comparisons of mean tumor volume between treatment groups and control groups (e.g., untreated or isotype) were performed using independent sample t tests, implemented using R v 4.4.2. The results of hypothesis tests are reported for each comparison of mean tumor volume between the treatment groups and the controls. If the null hypothesis of no difference could not be rejected at the 0.05 significance level, then not significant (NS) is reported for that test. No adjustment for multiple comparisons was made. Although a large number of hypothesis tests were conducted, and within a particular model, there were up to 17 tests, a Bonferroni adjustment could have been made. If we were to make such an adjustment, the nominal significance level for a set of tests with 17 tests would be 0.05/17 two-sided. This translates to (0.05/17)/2 or 0.001471, one-sided. Several P values observed exceed this level of significance. Although our intent not to adjust for multiplicity is a limitation of the study, multiplicity may be overcome in a number of cases. The tumor volumes of each group were compared on the last day during which ≥66.7% of animals remained on study. TGI analysis was performed on the day indicated within the supplementary tables with statistical analysis for each xenograft model. TGI was calculated from the group means using the following formula: TGI (%) = (C–T)/C × 100, in which C is the mean tumor volume of the vehicle control group, and T is the mean tumor volume of the treated groups.

Clinical case studies

Participants enrolled into the FONTANA phase I/IIa first-in-human clinical study (NCT No. 05797168; ref.) were dosed with AZD5335 monotherapy as an intravenous infusion administered every 3 weeks. Tumor response was assessed by CT/MRI imaging performed every 6 weeks for the first 24 weeks and every 9 weeks thereafter until disease progression (or withdrawal of consent) and analyzed per the RECIST v1.1. Tumor FRα expression was determined by an analytically validated IHC assay conducted at a central laboratory, performed using archival or newly obtained formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded tissue samples.

Results

AZD5335 is a novel FRα-targeted TOP1i ADC

AZD5335 (Fig. 1A) consists of an anti-FRα human IgG1κ monoclonal antibody (INT-019) conjugated via a protease-cleavable maleimide-PEG8-valine-alanine linker to a TOP1i payload (AZ14170132). The entire linker–payload (containing conjugation handle, linker, and payload) is termed AZ14170133. AZ14170133 is conjugated to the native interchain cysteines within the INT-019 antibody through a covalent thiosuccinimide linkage with approximately eight drugs bound per antibody (Fig. 1A).

The starting point for the generation of AZD5335 was the discovery of an optimal antibody (INT-019). Following immunization of a human transgenic mouse, the resulting antibodies were screened for a series of properties desirable for an ADC: (1) specificity for human FRα over closely related paralogs FRβ and FRγ, (2) cross-reactivity with cynomolgus monkey FRα, (3) similar affinities for human and cynomolgus FRα, (4) physicochemical properties compatible with manufacturing, and (5) a high rate of internalization into a high FRα-expressing cell line. INT-019 was selected from the panel of molecules tested because it possesses superior attributes across this suite of requirements.

INT-019 specificity and cross-reactivity were assessed in an HTRF antigen binding assay (Supplementary Table S1). The antibody only demonstrated specific binding to human FRα; no binding to human FRβ or FRγ was detected. Although INT-019 bound both human and cynomolgus FRα, no binding to mouse FRα was detected. Species-specific INT-019 affinity was further determined in a surface plasmon resonance assay (Supplementary Table S2). INT-019 binds to both human (KD = 15.74 nmol/L) and cynomolgus monkey (KD = 36.86 nmol/L) FRα with similar affinities (within 2.5-fold). In contrast, the affinity of INT-019 for mouse FRα was ∼250-fold lower than for human FRα. No binding was detected for rat FRα. INT-019 and AZD5335 bind similarly to human FRα (IC50 = 7.4 and 10.6 nmol/L, respectively; Supplementary Fig. S1A), confirming that the binding properties of INT-019 are maintained after conjugation to AZ14170133.

The Retrogenix cellular microarray technology was used to further assess the specificity of INT-019. INT-019 binding was screened using 5,867 full-length human plasma membrane proteins and cell surface–tethered human secreted proteins plus an additional 397 human heterodimers. Using microarrays treated before and in the absence of fixation, INT-019 demonstrated specific interactions with tethered and membrane-bound FRα. A dose–response flow cytometry study determined that the EC50 of this interaction was 0.79 μg/mL. An additional target was identified, DLL1, but only observed in the absence of fixation. Further investigation in a dose–response flow cytometry study indicated that the EC50 of this interaction was >300 μg/mL.

Antibody internalization and lysosomal trafficking was confirmed via live cell imaging of INT-019 labeled with a pH-sensitive dye (Supplementary Fig. S1B). For FRα-expressing KB cells (Fig. 1B), INT-019 reached a maximum at approximately 8 hours, with a normalized total integrated intensity of 162,528 RCU × μm2 (1 μg/mL). In comparison, an isotype control antibody (R347) had a normalized total integrated intensity of 431 RCU × μm2 (1 μg/mL) at 8 hours. INT-019’s normalized total integrated intensity was more than 100× higher than R347’s across the entire time course at 1- and 2-μg/mL concentrations. Crucial for ADC processing, the substantial increase in the normalized total integrated intensity over time indicated that INT-019 was being internalized and trafficked to the lysosomal compartment.

Analytic characterization of AZD5335 was performed following bioconjugation, purification, and formulation. Reverse-phase high-performance liquid chromatography analysis at 214 and 330 nm (AZ14170133-specific) shows a mixture of unconjugated light chains, light chains attached to a single molecule of AZ14170133, unconjugated heavy chains, and heavy chains attached to up to three molecules of AZ14170133, consistent with a DAR of 7.82 to 8.00 molecules of AZ14170133 per antibody (Supplementary Fig. S2A). Size-Exclusion Chromatography – Ultra High Performance Liquid Chromatography (SEC-UHPLC) analysis at 280 nm showed a monomer purity of more than 98%. Hydrophobic interaction chromatography high-performance liquid chromatography analysis at 214 nm shows a majority of DAR8 species and no unconjugated mAb. UV-based analysis gave a concentration of AZD5335 at 0.92 to 2.10 mg/mL in 12.0 to 15.5 mL and obtained a mass of AZD5335 at 11.1 to 32.5 mg (22%–81% yield). The LAL test determined endotoxin levels of this batch of AZD5335 to be <0.048 to 0.149 EU/mg. Assessment of AZD5335 in vitro stability indicated that deconjugation was consistently low (<4%) across all time points and across all sera tested (Supplementary Fig. S2B).

AZD5335 exhibits in vitro cytotoxicity and in vivo efficacy in FRα-positive tumor models

The effect of AZD5335 treatment on cell viability was measured in FRα-expressing KB (Fig. 1B), Jeg-3 (Fig. 1C), and IGROV-1 (Fig. 1D) cancer cell lines. These cell-based models were previously characterized and successfully applied during the preclinical development of Elahere. Using a 6-day in vitro assay, AZD5335 cytotoxicity was observed within all three models with IC50 values of 0.5270 nmol/L (KB), 2.091 nmol/L (Jeg-3), and 5.284 nmol/L (IGROV-1). AZD5335 cytotoxicity was consistent with sensitivity to the TOP1i payload (AZ14170132). The isotype-ADC (NIP228-AZ14170133) was inactive in these cell lines. Quantification of FRα receptor numbers indicated that these models expressed variable but high levels, ranging from ∼470 K for KB, ∼430 K for Jeg-3, and ∼345 K for IGROV-1.

The effect of AZD5335 or the TOP1i payload (AZ14170132) on DNA damage response (DDR) signaling pathways was examined in KB (Supplementary Fig. S3). After treatment for 24 hours with either 66.7-nmol/L AZD5335 or 5-nmol/L AZ14170132, ATR signaling was activated, as evident by an increase in phosphorylation of ATR at threonine 1989 (Thr1989). Chk1, downstream of ATR, was also phosphorylated at 24 and 48 hours. Similarly, AZD5335 and AZ14170132 induced phosphorylation of ATM (Ser1981) and its downstream target, Chk2 (Thr68). An increase in other DNA damage markers, including phospho-KAP1 (pKAP1), phospho-RPA (pRPA), and phospho-γH2AX, was also observed after 24 hours of treatment and persisted through the 72-hour treatment period. Elevated levels of phospho-proteins were often coupled with increased levels of corresponding total proteins. PARP cleavage was also observed at 72 hours. Consistent with the mechanism of action of the TOP1i payload (AZ14170132), these studies confirmed that AZD5335 activates DDR.

The bystander potential of AZD5335 was evaluated using a Steady-Glo–based in vitro assay to assess the viability of luciferase-expressing FRα-knockout KB cells (lucKBFRα-KO) when cocultured with FRα-expressing KB cells. Both monoculture (KB or lucKBFRα-KO only) and coculture (20lucKBFRα-KO: 80KB) formats were treated with 0 to 300 nmol/L AZD5335 or an isotype-ADC over 6 days. CTG assays indicated that in monocultures, AZD5335 treatment induced significant target-mediated cytotoxicity in FRα-expressing KB cells, whereas lucKBFRα-KO cells exhibited no reduction in viability. The Steady-Glo readout demonstrated a reduction in viability of lucKBFRα-KO cells when cocultured with FRα-expressing KB cells but not in lucKBFRα-KO monocultures, suggesting the occurrence of bystander activity following AZD5335 treatment (Supplementary Fig. S4).

ADC pharmacokinetics (PK) associated with relevant mouse strains was also characterized (Supplementary Table S3). The plasma PK parameters of AZD5335 were assessed in female SCID mice. The mean PK parameters are summarized in Supplementary Table S3A. Following single intravenous (5-mg/kg) administration of AZD5335, the mean plasma (Cmax) of total antibody (mAb) and ADC were measured immediately after the dose in all animals. Thereafter, concentrations declined in an approximately biexponential manner and were above the lower limit of quantification at all time points (up to 21 days after administration). The concentration versus time profiles for total mAb and ADC were similar to each other across the range of sampling time points, suggesting negligible deconjugation in plasma across the course of the study. The mean plasma elimination phase half-life, clearance, and volume of distribution at steady state were similar for total mAb (16.8 days, 4.4-mL/day/kg, and 103-mL/kg, respectively) and ADC (15.9 days, 4.6-mL/day/kg, and 98-mL/kg, respectively). The PK parameters of AZD5335 were also assessed in male humanized FcRn mice. The mean PK parameters are summarized in Supplementary Table S3B. Following single intravenous (5-mg/kg) administration of AZD5335, mean Cmax values of total mAb and ADC were measured immediately after the dose in all animals. Thereafter, concentrations declined in an approximately biexponential manner and were above the lower limit of quantification up to 21 days after dose. The concentration versus time profiles for total antibody and ADC were similar to each other across the range of sampling time points, again suggesting minimal loss of payload in plasma. The mean half-life, clearance, and volume of distribution at steady-state values were similar for total mAb (9.0 days, 7.8-mL/day/kg, and 95-mL/kg, respectively) and ADC (8.0 days, 10.4-mL/day/kg and 115-mL/kg, respectively).

Although in vitro cytotoxicity studies only used cells with high levels of FRα (Fig. 1B–D), the in vivo efficacy of AZD5335 was evaluated in four gynecologic cancer CDX models associated with a range of FRα expression levels (Fig. 1E–H). CDX were characterized via FRα IHC. KB and IGROV-1 were classified as FRα high (≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity), OVCAR-3 as FRα low (≥25% of viable tumor cells with ≥1+ staining intensity and <75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity), and SKOV-3 as FRα ultra-low (<25% of viable tumor cells with any staining intensity).

KB tumors were established in CB17-SCID mice and treated with a single intravenous dose of either 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 or 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC. Compared with the untreated or isotype-treated tumors, 5-mg/kg AZD5335 treatment induced complete and durable regression (Fig. 1I). AZD5335 treatment resulted in 100% (P < 0.0001) TGI at the 5-mg/kg dose level (Supplementary Table S4A). Decreasing the dose of AZD5335 to 2.5-mg/kg resulted in TGI of 55.17% (P = 0.016), or 1.25-mg/kg resulted in TGI of 78.38% (P < 0.0036); however, the statistical difference between these groups was NS. The isotype-ADC did not inhibit KB tumor growth at 5-mg/kg. IGROV-1 tumors were also established in CB17-SCID mice and treated with a single intravenous dose of either 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 or 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC. AZD5335 (2.5 and 5-mg/kg) significantly inhibited the growth of IGROV-1 tumors (Fig. 1J). Compared with untreated tumors (Supplementary Table S4B), AZD5335 treatment resulted in 95.55% TGI at 5 mg/kg (P < 0.0001), 86.75% at 2.5 mg/kg (P < 0.0008), and 43.8% at 1.25 mg/kg (NS). Although 1.25-mg/kg AZD5335 resulted in 43.8% TGI and 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC resulted in 39.48% TGI, these effects were not significantly different from the untreated group (NS). OVCAR-3 tumors were established in NSG mice and treated with a single intravenous dose of either 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 or 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC. Compared with the untreated tumors, 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 treatment resulted in >80% TGI (P = 0.0001; Fig. 1K). These effects, aside from the AZD5335 5-mg/kg group (P = 0.0402), were not significantly different from the 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC response (Supplementary Table S4C). SKOV-3 tumors were also established in NSG mice and treated with a single intravenous dose of either 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 or 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC. Compared with the untreated tumors, AZD5335 treatment resulted in 53.37% TGI at the 5-mg/kg dose level (Fig. 1L). Although lower doses of AZD5335 resulted in dose-dependent reductions in TGI, AZD5335-mediated TGI was only significant for the 5-mg/kg group (P = 0.0003). These effects, aside from the effect observed in the AZD5335 5-mg/kg group (P = 0.02), were not significantly different from the 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC response (Supplementary Table S4D).

AZD5335 PK/pharmacodynamics was measured using KB CDX. Tumors from KB cells or a derivative of KB with FRα-knockout (KBFRα-KO) were established in CB17-SCID mice and treated with a single intravenous dose of either 1.25- to 5-mg/kg AZD5335 or 5-mg/kg isotype-ADC. Tumors were collected (n = 3) at 1, 6, 24, 72, 144, and 336 hours after administration and analyzed for intratumoral payload concentrations (Supplementary Table S3C).

Following a single intravenous dose of AZD5335, the concentrations of payload in KB tumors increased in a linear proportion with doses between 1.25 and 2.5 mg/kg. There was a subproportional increase in AUClast between the 2.5- and 5-mg/kg dose groups; however, this was confounded by a lack of tumor tissue available at the 144-hour time point (5 mg/kg). Importantly, the payload AUClast value for KB tumors treated with 5-mg/kg AZD5335 was approximately sixfold higher than the KBFRα-KO tumors treated with an equivalent dose of AZD5335. Demonstrating a degree of FRα-mediated tumor targeting of payload, the isotype-ADC, when dosed at 5 mg/kg to either KB or KBFRα-KO tumors, delivered four- to fivefold lower payload AUC than AZD5335.

ADC-treated KB tumors were also analyzed for PD biomarkers associated with the TOP1i mechanism of action. Consistent with the activation of DDR observed in vitro (Supplementary Fig. S3) and tumor volumes measured in vivo (0–14 days), 1.25- to 2.5- to 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 treatments were associated with dose- and time-dependent increases in pRAD50 and γH2AX foci (Supplementary Fig. S5). Isotype-ADC treatment (5.0 mg/kg) differentiated from 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 over time and was defined by either unsustained pRAD50 or unobserved γH2AX changes. No significant alterations in intratumoral FRα levels were observed across treatment groups and time points surveyed.

AZD5335 induces robust antitumor responses in a range of FRα-expressing patient-derived tumor models of ovarian cancer

The antitumor activity associated with 2.5- to 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 monotherapy was assessed within 17 ovarian cancer PDX models derived from primary and metastatic tumors with and without prior treatment exposure (Supplementary Fig. S6). These models were characterized for FRα by IHC. FRα expression levels were measured via visual (H-scores) and quantitative continuous scoring (QCS), an innovative, deep learning–based image analysis method for quantitative and continuous assessment of staining intensity (and, thus, quantifying target expression) on digital whole-slide images. These 17 models were associated with a range of FRα levels consistent with clinically relevant ovarian cancers (Supplementary Fig. S6). Four models were classified as FRα high (≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity), 11 as FRα low (≥25% of viable tumor cells with ≥1+ staining intensity and <75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity), and two as FRα ultra-low (<25% of viable tumor cells with any staining intensity).

After a single dose of 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335, 14 of 17 PDX exhibited a reduction in tumor volume from baseline of 30% or greater, with an overall response rate defined as 82% (Fig. 2A; Supplementary Fig. S7). Matched dosing with 2.5-mg/kg isotype-ADC was performed in 14 of 17 PDX (Supplementary Fig. S7). Isotype-ADC (2.5-mg/kg) only resulted in a reduction in tumor volume from baseline of 30% or greater in two of 14 models. The 14 AZD5335-responsive models included the majority of FRα-high (four of four) and FRα-low (10 of 11) models; whereas, the three nonresponders included the remaining FRα ultra-low (two of two) and FRα-low (one of 11) models. After a single dose of 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335, 13 of 17 PDX exhibited a reduction in tumor volume from baseline of 30% or greater with the overall response rate defined as 76% (Fig. 2B; Supplementary Fig. S7). Isotype-ADC (5.0-mg/kg) only resulted in a reduction in tumor volume from baseline of 30% or greater in nine of 17 models. Similar to 2.5-mg/kg, the 13 models that responded to 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 included the majority of FRα-high (four of four) and FRα-low (nine of 11) models; however, deeper and dose-dependent responses were observed. Of note, AZD5335 exhibited tumor stasis within the FRα-low nonresponders treated with either 2.5-mg/kg (one of 11) or 5.0-mg/kg (two of 11).

The relationship between FRα expression and AZD5335 response to either 2.5 (Fig. 2A) or 5.0 (Fig. 2B) mg/kg was further examined using both QCS scoring (median optical density of the membrane) and H-scores (0–300) as measures of FRα. Compared with nonresponders, 2.5- and 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 responders were defined by significantly higher FRα median OD (P = 0.012 and P = 0.0034, respectively). Correlation analyses also revealed a trend for inverse association between FRα median OD and median response (% reduction in tumor volume from baseline) for 2.5-mg/kg (ρ = −0.47; P = 0.056) and 5.0-mg/kg (ρ = −0.51; P = 0.038) AZD5335 treatments. Visual scoring of FRα (H-scores) aligned with and supported trends observed using QCS scores. These FRα scores were highly correlated between each other (ρ = 0.95; P = 9.7e−09).

AZD5335 demonstrates responses in Elahere-ineligible or -resistant tumor models

An Elahere analogue was generated via random lysine bioconjugation of a mirvetuximab mAb intermediate with Sulfo-SPDB-DM4. SEC-UHPLC analysis at 280 nm indicates a monomer purity of more than 98%. UV-based analysis gave a DAR of 3.13 and concentration of Elahere analogue at 1.73-mg/mL in 84.5 mL and obtained mass of Elahere analogue at 145.9 mg (47% yield). The LAL test determined endotoxin levels of this batch of Elahere analogue to be 0.28 EU/mg.

The antitumor activity of AZD5335 or this Elahere analogue was compared within three xenograft models. These models were profiled for FRα (Fig. 3A–C) and defined as Elahere ineligible (<75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity). Tumors were treated with either AZD5335 or Elahere analogue at 2.5 to 5-mg/kg dose levels. Compared with untreated tumors, 2.5 to 5.0-mg/kg AZD5335 demonstrated significant TGI associated with durable responses in CTG-3226 (Fig. 3D), OV0857-CIS (Fig. 3E), and OVCAR-3 (Fig. 3F). Elahere analogue at 5 mg/kg only achieved significant TGI in OVCAR-3. Dose-matched head-to-head comparisons are presented in Supplementary Table S5. Comparative assessment of AZD5335 or Elahere analogue within Elahere-eligible tumor models (≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity) was not explored.

To assess AZD5335 efficacy within the context of Elahere resistance, we generated OVCAR-3 tumors that were resistant to Elahere analogue treatments. Twelve OVCAR-3 xenografts were established and treated with 5-mg/kg Elahere analogue (twice weekly). Although tumor regressions were observed after the first treatment, tumor relapse started after four doses and continued following six to nine additional treatments (Fig. 3G). Eight of 12 Elahere-resistant OVCAR-3 tumors were collected and surveyed for FRα via IHC. Compared with OVCAR-3 (Fig. 3H), the Elahere-resistant OVCAR-3 (Fig. 3I) were characterized by significantly lower levels of FRα. Despite lower levels of FRα, this Elahere-resistant model would be classified as FRα low (n = 7/8 tumors; ≥25% of viable tumor cells with ≥1+ staining intensity and <75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity). Four of 12 Elahere-resistant OVCAR-3 were re-challenged with 5-mg/kg AZD5335. The first dose of AZD5335 induced tumor regressions in all four relapsed tumors, and a second dose achieved complete regressions in three of the four OVCAR-3–treated tumors (Fig. 3J).

AZD5335 and standard-of-care combination treatments result in enhanced efficacy

We evaluated the effects of AZD5335 as monotherapy and in combination with standard-of-care treatments (bevacizumab or carboplatin) within KB, Jeg-3, and IGROV-1 cell lines in vitro. A five-point dose–response curve was assessed per drug for a 6-day cell viability readout. As monotherapies, all three cell lines were sensitive to AZD5335 and carboplatin. Consistent with antitumor effects mediated indirectly, all three cell lines were insensitive to bevacizumab single-agent treatments. Although the combination of AZD5335 with bevacizumab yielded minimal to no added benefit (as calculated with low HSA synergy scores of 1.754 for IGROV-1, 1.348 for Jeg-3, and −1.935 for KB), AZD5335 in combination with carboplatin enhanced cytotoxic effects across all cell lines compared with AZD5335 alone (Fig. 4A). Synergistic interactions were confirmed using the HSA model of synergy, with the strongest synergy observed in Jeg-3 with a score of 30.24, followed by KB with a score of 4.218 and IGROV-1 with a score of 2.339. These results supported in vivo assessment of combined treatments.

The antitumor activity of AZD5335 in combination with bevacizumab was evaluated in OV90, an FRα-low ovarian cancer xenograft model (Fig. 4B) previously applied during preclinical assessment of an Elahere-based combination. Compared with the untreated OV90 tumors, treatment with 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335 monotherapy resulted in TGI of 85.96% (P < 0.0001), whereas 5-mg/kg bevacizumab resulted in TGI of 29.88% (P = 0.039). A dose of 2.5-mg/kg isotype-ADC was associated with 34.64% TGI (NS). The combination of 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335 and 5-mg/kg bevacizumab (Fig. 4C) demonstrated very significant TGI of 98.66% (P < 0.0001). The combination of 2.5-mg/kg isotype-ADC plus 5-mg/kg bevacizumab resulted in TGI of 45.92% (P = 0.0066). Compared with AZD5335 monotherapy, AZD5335 + bevacizumab (P = 0.0023) was associated with enhanced efficacy (Supplementary Table S6). We extended our OV90 studies to include AZD5335 in combination with carboplatin (Fig. 4D) and carboplatin + bevacizumab (Fig. 4D). Compared with untreated tumors, 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335 resulted in TGI of 82.89% (P < 0.0001), whereas 100-mg/kg carboplatin resulted in moderate TGI of 42.1% (P = 0.0041). Conversely, the combination of AZD5335 (2.5-mg/kg) and carboplatin (100-mg/kg) demonstrated significant TGI of 96.38% (P < 0.0001). The combination of isotype-ADC (2.5-mg/kg) with carboplatin (100-mg/kg) resulted in TGI of 65.42% (P = 0.0001). Compared with AZD5335, AZD5335 + carboplatin (P = 0.0002) was associated with enhanced efficacy. AZD5335 (2.5-mg/kg) + bevacizumab (5-mg/kg) and carboplatin (100-mg/kg) demonstrated very significant and near-complete TGI of 98.63% (P < 0.0001). The combination of isotype-ADC (2.5-mg/kg) + bevacizumab (5-mg/kg) + carboplatin (100-mg/kg) demonstrated TGI of 63.98% (P < 0.0005). Compared with AZD5335, AZD5335 + carboplatin + bevacizumab (P < 0.0001) was associated with enhanced efficacy (Supplementary Table S6). Although body weight losses >20% were observed in a subset of all carboplatin-treated groups, most animals were able to tolerate treatments.

AZD5335 and saruparib combined treatments represent a translationally relevant strategy

The in vitro efficacy of AZD5335 as monotherapy and in combination with saruparib, a PARP1-selective inhibitor, was evaluated within KB, Jeg-3, and IGROV-1 cell lines. A five-point dose–response curve was used for each drug and for a 6-day cell viability assay. IGROV-1, Jeg-3, and KB cell lines exhibited high sensitivity to AZD5335 as monotherapy, whereas their response to saruparib ranged from minimal to no activity. Compared to treatment with either single agent alone, AZD5335 in combination with saruparib resulted in enhanced cytotoxicity. Using the HSA model of synergy, strong synergistic activity between AZD5335 and saruparib was found in all three cell lines. The highest HSA synergy scores were observed in Jeg-3 with an HSA score of 40.76, followed by KB with a score of 23.81 and IGROV-1 with a score of 7.348 (Fig. 5A). These synergistic interactions supported in vivo assessment of combined treatments.

The antitumor activity of AZD5335 in combination with saruparib was evaluated within OVCAR-3 (Fig. 5B), OV2022F (Fig. 5C), and CTG-3783 (Fig. 5D) tumors (Supplementary Table S7). Model selection reflected tumors with low/ultra-low FRα expression and opportunities to enhance the depth and duration of responses. Within OVCAR-3 and OV2022F, 1.25-mg/kg AZD5335 monotherapy demonstrated moderate antitumor activity (OVCAR-3 TGI = 86.14%, P < 0.0001; OV2022F TGI = 53.91%, P = 0.0485). OVCAR-3 treated with 1-mg/kg saruparib (once daily ×28) were associated with a weak TGI of 37.45% (P = 0.0117), whereas a moderate TGI of 61.58% (P = 0.0148) was observed in saruparib-treated OV2022F. OVCAR-3 and OV2022F tumors treated with AZD5335 and saruparib were associated with significant TGI (OVCAR-3 TGI = 92.83%, P < 0.0001; OV2022F TGI = 97.15%, P = 0.0003). A dose of 1.25-mg/kg isotype-ADC also demonstrated antitumor activity (OVCAR-3 TGI = 44.99%, P = 0.0039; OV2022F TGI = 44.65%, P = 0.0416), which was enhanced by saruparib (OVCAR-3 TGI = 87.53%, P = 0.0001; OV2022F TGI = 94.3%, P = 0.0004). Compared with 1.25-mg/kg AZD5335 or 1.0-mg/kg saruparib, 1.25-mg/kg AZD5335 + 1-mg/kg saruparib [OVCAR-3: P = 0.0028; OV2022F: P = 0.0301 (AZD5335 comparisons only)] was associated with enhanced efficacy. For CTG-3783, 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335 (TGI = 72.82%, P = 0.0141) or 1.0-mg/kg saruparib (TGI = 45.76%, NS) demonstrated moderate-to-weak activities; combined treatments demonstrated significant and durable antitumor responses (TGI = 95.49%, P = 0.0033). Isotype-ADC with or without saruparib was not tested in CTG-3783.

AZD5335 exerts clinical responses in tumors with a range of FRα expression levels

FONTANA is an ongoing modular phase I/IIa, open-label study assessing the safety, tolerability, PK, and preliminary efficacy of AZD5335 in patients with solid tumors (NCT No. 05797168). For patients with platinum-resistant recurrent ovarian cancer, initial results have been reported and include significant clinical responses in tumors with a range of FRα expression levels. Here, we provide clinical case studies corresponding to an FRα-high (Fig. 6A) and FRα-low (Fig. 6B) patient and demonstrate AZD5335 clinical responses (Fig. 6C).

A 71-year-old white female with FRα-high disease (Fig. 6C) was diagnosed 5 years before enrollment with stage IIIC serous ovarian cancer of unknown BRCA status. She received four prior lines of systemic therapy, including MORAb-202 in her fourth line of treatment, and reported progression after approximately 4 months. The patient’s tumor showed FRα high expression. She was dosed with 2.0-mg/kg AZD5335 every 3 weeks and had one dose reduction at cycle 5 to 1.6 mg/kg. The patient continues on treatment as of the data cutoff date, having received more than 9 months of therapy. The patient showed a partial response at the first scan (6 weeks) and continues to maintain a partial response. All adverse events reported were of grade 1 to 2 severity.

A 66-year-old white female with FRα-low disease (Fig. 6C) was diagnosed 3 years before enrollment with stage IIIC serous ovarian cancer and was known to be BRCA wild type. She received two prior lines of systemic therapy. The patient’s tumor had FRα low expression on testing. She was dosed with 2.4-mg/kg AZD5335 every 3 weeks. The patient continues on treatment as of the data cutoff after more than 9 months of therapy with no dose reductions. The patient reported a partial response at her first scan (6 weeks) and achieved a complete response after 8 months of treatment. All adverse events reported were grade 1 to 2, except a grade 3 neutrophil count reduction, which resolved after 15 days.

Discussion

Here, we describe the successful derivation and comprehensive characterization of AZD5335, a novel FRα-targeted ADC, within the context of FRα-expressing ovarian cancer. AZD5335 consists of an FRα-directed antibody conjugated via a cleavable linker to a TOP1i payload. AZD5335 specifically binds to FRα, internalizes rapidly, and traffics efficiently through intracellular compartments to deliver the cytotoxic TOP1i. Target-mediated cytotoxicity was associated with TOP1i DDR mechanisms, and the bystander capabilities of AZD5335 were confirmed.

Our studies in PDX models demonstrated robust and durable antitumor responses within most FRα-expressing models tested. Notably, responses to 2.5-mg/kg AZD5335 were not restricted to models defined as FRα high. Nine of the 10 FRα-low models also responded with ≥30% reductions in tumor volumes. When considered in the context of Elahere, which is restricted to tumors exhibiting high levels of FRα (≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity; ref.), these results suggest that AZD5335 could benefit a broader spectrum of FRα-expressing disease. Up to this point, our preclinical studies have shown that AZD5335 possesses broader efficacy in relation to FRα expression levels, including in a subset of FRα-low tumor models in which Elahere’s efficacy is largely limited. Although the clinical relevance of these results will be investigated in the FONTANA trial, we have already documented significant clinical responses in patients with a wide range of tumor FRα expression levels. Estimates of FRα expression and prevalence indicate that ∼35% to 40% of ovarian cancer cases have ≥75% of viable tumor cells with ≥2+ staining intensity (FRα high; ref.), whereas ∼70% to 80% have ≥25% of viable tumor cells with ≥1+ staining intensity (FRα low + FRα high; ref.). Therefore, AZD5335 could be clinically effective in most FRα-expressing ovarian cancers. As previously demonstrated for Datroway, QCS scoring could further enhance our ability to enrich for AZD5335 responders.

Intriguingly, the results presented herein suggest that AZD5335 could be an effective agent for a disease that has progressed on Elahere. Although the mechanisms associated with ADC-resistant phenotypes are complex, alterations in FRα levels underscore, at least in part, the refractoriness to Elahere analogue treatments in our model. Whether Elahere analogue resistance represents down-modulation of FRα expression or selection for FRα-low populations, AZD5335 efficacy in tumors with low levels of FRα expression could explain the treatment responses observed. DM4 resistance cannot be excluded, and our data suggest that an alternative therapeutic payload may be beneficial. Recent studies testing the efficacy of sequential ADC treatments have demonstrated benefits associated with payload switching regardless of tumor target.

Although most cytotoxic payloads incorporated within ADC typically share some form of hematologic toxicity, the TOP1i payload of AZD5335 provides an opportunity to differentiate from Elahere and other MTi-based FRα ADC, which are commonly associated with neuropathy and ocular toxicity. TOP1i-based ADC are predominantly linked to gastrointestinal and hematologic toxicities; however, instances of pneumonitis (interstitial lung disease) and cardiac dysfunction have also been observed. Encouragingly, the emerging data from AZD5335 and AZD8205 are suggestive of manageable clinical safety profiles, consistent with anticipated risks, factoring data from other TOP1i-based ADC.

In the current study, AZD5335 treatments demonstrated enhanced efficacy when used with standard-of-care drugs, including carboplatin, bevacizumab, or saruparib [PARP1 inhibitor (PARP1i)]. Although AZD5335 combined treatments with saruparib provide additional differentiation, our results highlight TOP1i as the preferred partners for PARPi. As PARP1 can act as a positive regulator of genomic stability by counteracting TOP1-induced DNA damage, PARP1i renders the cells exquisitely sensitive to TOP1i. Mechanistically, PARPi prevent the removal of the accumulated TOP1-linked DNA covalent cleavage complexes, leading to an exacerbation of DNA damage through two primary mechanisms. First, PARP inhibition prevents TOP1 PARylation, which promotes the reversal of the trapped TOP1-linked DNA covalent cleavage complexes. Second, PARP inhibition can abrogate the coupling of TDP1 (a repair enzyme that excises bound TOP1) with PARP1, thus inactivating TDP1. Although the mechanism of interaction between PARP1 and TOP1 is well documented, the biological sensitivity of tumors to either PARP1i or TOP1i may be enhanced by homologous recombination deficiency.

Saruparib is a novel PARP1-selective inhibitor that potently inhibits and selectively traps PARP1 with >500-fold selectivity over PARP2. The selectivity profile of saruparib was designed to deliver an improved therapeutic index, with more robust PARP1 target engagement compared with other clinically validated PARPi. The improved selectivity and therapeutic index properties of saruparib may enable broader clinical utility and expand clinical combination options, particularly with ADC that selectively deliver cytotoxic payloads. Although AZD5335 and saruparib combined treatments demonstrate activity in tumor models independently of BRCA status, increased toxicity to normal proliferating tissues, such as hematopoietic and gut epithelial cells, remains a potential risk. Ongoing and future studies will address these safety concerns by carefully monitoring for cytopenia and gastrointestinal effects to better define the therapeutic window. This combined treatment strategy is currently being tested in the FONTANA trial, utilizing a “gap” schedule designed to minimize toxicity to normal tissues.

Isotype-ADC treatment was often associated with antitumor activities and enhanced by standard-of-care drugs. Although these observations are consistent with previous reports of antigen-independent ADC accumulation and uptake within preclinical tumor models via a variety of mechanisms, FRα-mediated effects were demonstrated by multiple instances in which superior AZD5335 responses were observed.

In summary, our preclinical data underscore AZD5335 as a promising next-generation FRα ADC for the treatment of ovarian cancer. These studies triggered clinical investigations on the effects of AZD5335 in patients with ovarian cancer and other malignancies expressing different levels of FRα.
