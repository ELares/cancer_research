# Preclinical Evaluation of DB-1419, a Novel Bifunctional and Bispecific Anti-B7-H3 × PD-L1 Antibody-Drug Conjugate.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: We developed a bifunctional B7-H3 × PD-L1-targeting bispecific antibody-drug conjugate (named DB-1419) conjugated with a novel topoisomerase I inhibitor, P1003 (drug-to-antibody ratio, 8), and assessed its preclinical profiles.

**EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN**: The pharmacologic activities of DB-1419 were assessed in multiple cancer cell lines and immunodeficient and immunocompetent cell line-/patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models. The mechanisms of action behind the efficacy, pharmacokinetics in cynomolgus monkeys, and the safety profiles in vitro and in cynomolgus monkeys were also explored.

**RESULTS**: With high stability in circulation, DB-1419 displayed efficient cellular internalization and trafficked to the lysosome within 48 hours to release the payload P1003. In vitro, we observed inhibitory effects on the proliferation of lung cancer cell lines and beyond, mechanically by P1003-mediated direct cytotoxicity, antibody-mediated antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, and bystander effects. Excellent tumor growth inhibition of DB-1419 was revealed in liver cancer cell line-derived xenografts and lung cancer PDX, along with the verification of DNA damage and apoptosis induced by P1003 using tumor tissues. Furthermore, DB-1419 has driven a robust antitumor response in immunocompetent PDX; the blockade of the interaction between PD-1 and PD-L1 and significant induction of markers of immunogenic cell death were also found, suggesting the activation of tumor-specific immunity. DB-1419 was well tolerated in cynomolgus monkeys (highest nonseverely toxic dose, 120 mg/kg) and did not elicit the production of inflammatory cytokines in vitro.

**CONCLUSIONS**: DB-1419 demonstrated potent antitumor activity against multiple tumors, with favorable pharmacokinetic and safety profiles. These preclinical data potentially position DB-1419 as a promising treatment approach, currently in the ongoing phase I/IIa study in advanced tumors (NCT06554795).

Introduction

Antibody–drug conjugates (ADC), hailed as “biological missiles” for their ability to specifically deliver cytotoxic payloads to tumors, have achieved efficient killing; however, their clinical applications are greatly hindered by resistance and a narrow therapeutic window. The priority for widening the therapeutic index has fueled a wave of exploration into ADC combination treatments. Among them, immune checkpoint inhibitors represent an active strategy companion due to their ability to induce tumor-specific adaptive immunity through multiple mechanisms, thereby synergizing with ADCs, whereas potential overlapping toxicities raise safety issues of such synergetic combinations. An additional promising approach involves optimizing ADC components to develop emerging-generation ADCs, exemplified by bispecific ADCs (BsADC) conjugating bispecific antibodies (BsAb) to the linker–payload complex. Despite advancements, the clinical benefits of BsADCs fall short of theoretical expectations, partially attributed to the antibody skeleton of current BsADCs mostly being tumor-specific rather than functional targets. As such, BsADCs designed to target functional molecules, such as PD-L1, may help bridge cytotoxicity and immunotherapeutic activity, potentially improving the overall risk–benefit profiles.

With a similarity to PD-L1, B7-H3 is also a member of the B7 superfamily and highly expressed in multiple tumors and functionally acts as a coinhibitory factor for T-cell proliferation and activation, leading to immune escape and poor patient prognosis. Therapeutic ADCs targeting B7-H3 or PD-L1, such as DS-7300 and SGN-PDL1V, have demonstrated efficacy against tumors and manageable safety profiles in clinical settings. Previous studies have shown the coexpression patterns of B7-H3 and PD-L1 in various malignancies. B7-H3 and PD-L1 could be considered partners for enhanced therapeutic effects. Several preclinical studies revealed that cotargeting these two molecules may improve the antitumor response by direct killing and acting on both the innate and adaptive immune systems. Combining these findings, immunomodulating BsADCs based on the B7-H3 × PD-L1–targeting BsAb skeleton may hold promise. These BsADCs could leverage the bispecific binding mode and the dual functions of cytotoxicity and immune activity to maximize the benefit-to-risk ratios. However, no bsB7-H3 × PD-L1–ADCs have yet made it to active clinical trials.

Herein, we developed DB-1419, a novel bsB7-H3 × PD-L1–ADC comprising DB-141902 (humanized anti–B7-H3/PD-L1 IgG1 BsAb), covalently conjugated to P1003 [DNA topoisomerase I inhibitor (TOPIi)] via a tetrapeptide-based cleavable linker, with a drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) of approximately 8. The nonclinical evaluation demonstrated that DB-1419 could possess dual functions with cytotoxicity and immunotherapeutic activity and explored the mechanism of action (MOA) behind these activities. Our results support DB-1419 as a valuable and promising approach to overcoming cancer heterogeneity and therapy resistance.

Materials and Methods

Cell lines and cultures

Cancer cell lines covering lung cancer [NCI-H1568 (ATCC, Cat# CRL-5876, RRID: CVCL_1476), NCI-H358 (European Collection of Animal Cell Cultures, Cat# 95111733, RRID: CVCL_1559), NCI-H1975 (ATCC, Cat# CRL-5908, RRID: CVCL_1511), NCI-H441 (ATCC, Cat# HTB-174, RRID: CVCL_1561), DMS53 (ATCC, Cat# CRL-2062, RRID: CVCL_1177), and NCI-H82 (ATCC, Cat# HTB-175, RRID: CVCL_1591)], liver cancer [Huh7 (Japanese Collection of Research Bioresources [JCRB], Cat# JCRB0403, RRID: CVCL_0336)], melanoma [A375 (ATCC, Cat# CRL-1619, RRID: CVCL_0132)], breast cancer [MDA-MB-231 (ATCC, Cat# CRM-HTB-26, RRID: CVCL_0062)], prostate cancer [PC-3 (ATCC, Cat# CRL-1435, RRID: CVCL_0035)], esophageal squamous cancer [KYSE150 (Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganismen und Zellkulturen [DSMZ], Cat# ACC 375, RRID: CVCL_1348)], and others used in this study were purchased from several cell banks (Supplementary Table S1). All cell lines were cultured in the corresponding media at 37°C in 5% CO2 and tested negative for Mycoplasma contamination. Cell line authentication was performed by short tandem repeat profiling, and experiments were conducted within 20 passage doublings after revival from liquid nitrogen storage.

Antibodies and ADCs

The anti-human B7-H3/PD-L1 bispecific IgG1 antibody was generated as described previously. DB-1419 antibody is thawed without light at room temperature (18°C–26°C). Polysorbate 80 solution and 20 mmol/L DTPA solution (target concentrations are 0.03% (v/v) and 1 mmol/L, respectively) are added to the reduction solution. His-HCl (130 mmol/L; pH 7.4) is used to adjust the reduction protein concentration to 15 mg/mL. His solution (0.2 mol/L) or His-HCl (0.25 mol/L) solution is used to adjust the system pH to 6.7. Tris (2-carboxy-ethyl)-phosphin-HCl (10 mmol/L) solution is added to the antibody solution. Tris (2-carboxy-ethyl)-phosphin-HCl/antibody molar ratio is 7.0. The reaction is controlled at 25°C for 4 hours. Then, 0.2 mol/L His solution or 0.25 mol/L His-HCl solution is used to adjust the reduction pH to 5.5. Linker–payload in DMSO solution (linker–payload/antibody molar ratio is 10.5) is added to the reaction. The conjugation reaction is controlled at 22°C for 1 hour. After conjugation, the ADC intermediate is purified using activated carbon filtration and ultrafiltration/diafiltration purification to obtain the ADC product (size exclusion chromatography purity: 99.2%, hydrophobic interaction chromatography DAR: 7.9; free drug: <0.2 µg/mL; residual DMSO: <0.06 mg/mL). Isotype control ADC was produced using the same method, with the same linker–payload. DB-1303 was developed and provided for this study by Duality Biologics, Ltd.

Characterization of DB-1419

Binding specificity and affinity

The binding specificity and affinity of DB-1419 and DB-141902 (parental BsAb) were assessed by ELISA against 10 human B7 family antigens, including human B7-H3 and PD-L1. Immunoplates were coated with 0.25 µg/mL antigens overnight, blocked with 1% BSA, and incubated with serial dilutions of DB-1419, DB-141902, or isotype control ADCs. For the sandwich ELISA, 1 µg/mL biotinylated PD-L1 was added. After washing, goat anti-human IgG Fc-horseradish peroxidase (1:5,000) was applied, followed by 3,3′,5,5′-tetramethylbenzidine and 1 N HCl. Absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a SpectraMax Plus 384 microplate reader (Molecular Devices).

Species cross-reactivity

The species cross-reactivity of DB-1419 and DB-141902 to human B7-H3/PD-L1 was determined by surface plasmon resonance. The flow cells of the anti-human IgG-Fc (Cytiva) chip were immobilized with 2 µg/mL DB-1419 and DB-141902 at 10 µL/minute for 60 seconds. Next, 0.2 to 100 nmol/L humanized B7-H3 and PD-L1, diluted in 1× HBS-EP+ buffer (a HEPES-buffered saline supplemented with EDTA and surfactant P20, pH 7.4, Cytiva) and then serially diluted 1:2, were injected into channels at a flow rate of 30 µL/minute with 180 seconds of association and 400 seconds of dissociation. Two zero concentrations were used for the subtraction of the reference’s signal. The chip surface was regenerated by injecting 10 mmol/L glycine at pH 1.5 at 30 µL/minute for 30 seconds to remove the ligand and any bound analyte. The curves obtained by subtracting the signals from both the reference and buffer from that of the samples were fitted to either a 1:1 binding model or a steady-state affinity model using Biacore Insight Evaluation Software (GE HealthCare).

 In vitro stability in plasma

The release rate of P1003 from 150 μg/mL DB-1419 in rat, monkey, and human plasma was evaluated at 37°C for up to 21 days. Plasma samples were deproteinized with acetonitrile and then analyzed by LC/MS-MS (Vanquish UHPLC system, TSQ Quantis; Thermo Fisher Scientific). The release rate of P1003 was calculated using the mean concentration of P1003.

Pharmacokinetic study in cynomolgus monkeys

The pharmacokinetic study was performed in female and male cynomolgus monkeys using DB-1419–ADC at doses of 1.5, 5, and 15 mg/kg, administered once via i.v. injection. Blood samples were obtained from each group before and immediately after dosing and at 2, 6, 24, 48, 72, 120, 168, 216, 264, 336, and 504 hours after administration. The serum concentrations of total antibodies and intact ADCs were quantified by ELISA methods. Concentration–time profiles were analyzed to assess the toxicokinetic parameters using WinNonlin 8.0 (RRID: SCR_024504).

 In vitro cytotoxicity

Cancer cell lines at 1 to 4 × 103 cells/well were seeded into 96-well plates, followed by exposure to DB-1419, DB-141902 (parental BsAb), and P1003 (free payload) at serially diluted concentrations ranging from 1,000 to 0.1524 nmol/L for 7 days. Cell viability was evaluated using the CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay Kit (Promega) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. In vitro cytotoxicity curves with IC50 values were fitted in GraphPad Prism (RRID: SCR_002798) based on the luminescence measured by the EnVision Multimode Microplate Reader (PerkinElmer).

Flow cytometry

NCI-H358 cells (2 × 105 cells per well in a six-well plate) were digested with trypsin, collected, and washed twice with precooled PBS. They were then resuspended in 200 µL PBS and stained with propidium iodide (PI) dye (1:200). Flow cytometry was performed using a NovoExpress flow cytometer (Agilent) following the manufacturer’s instructions. Data analysis was conducted using FlowJo vX.0.7 software (BD Biosciences; RRID: SCR_008520). The antibodies used in this study are listed in Supplementary Table S2.

MOA

Internalization assay

NCI-H358 cells were added at 2 × 104 cells per well into the 96-well plates and then incubated with isotype control ADC, DB-1419, or parental DB-141902 labeled with Zenon pHrodo iFL IgG Labeling Reagent (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Internalization was evaluated by flow cytometric analysis at 3, 6, 18, 24, and 48 hours. For intracellular localization, NCI-H358 cells seeded at a density of 1 × 105 cells in a confocal dish were labeled with DB-1419, followed by staining with LysoTracker (Invitrogen) and Hoechst 33342 (Invitrogen). Images at 6 and 48 hours were captured using a confocal microscope (Leica).

Cell-cycle distribution and cell apoptosis

NCI-H358 cells were seeded at 2 × 105 cells per well into a six-well plate and treated with 30 and 100 nmol/L of DB-1419, parental DB-141902, isotype control ADC, and P1003 for 48 hours. After collection, cells were fixed in ice-cold 66% ethanol and then stained with PI for cell-cycle analysis using flow cytometry. For apoptosis analysis, cells were harvested, followed by Annexin V-FITC and PI staining for 15 minutes, and flow cytometry was analyzed within an hour. Late-stage apoptosis was defined as double-positive for Annexin V and PI, and early-stage apoptosis was defined as Annexin V–positive but PI-negative.

Western blotting

Total proteins were extracted from NCI-H358 cells after treatment with 30 and 100 nmol/L indicated agents for 72 hours, and tumor tissues of tumor-bearing mice were treated with 18 mg/kg DB-1419 for 72 hours. Subsequent Western blotting analysis of phosphorylated H2AX, caspase 3, cleaved PARP, and β-actin was performed according to the manufacturer’s instructions, as previously described. Images were obtained and quantified using the Tanon 5200 chemiluminescence imaging system (Tanon 5200 Multi). The primary and secondary antibodies used for Western blotting are listed in Supplementary Table S2.

Bystander effects

293T–B7-H3–KO (Kyinno Biotechnology, Cat# KC-4086) and 293T-B7-H3 (Kyinno Biotechnology, Cat# KC-3663) cell lines, respectively, with negative and high B7-H3 expressions were used to measure the bystander-killing effects. The 293T-B7-H3 cells labeled with CellTrace Far Red dye (diluted 1:1,000; Invitrogen) were cocultured with 293T–B7-H3–KO cells at a 3:1 ratio with a 293T-B7-H3 cell number of 3,600, followed by treatment with 30 and 100 nmol/L of DB-1419, DB-141902, and isotype control ADCs for 4 days. Subsequently, cell viability was determined by flow cytometric analysis following PI staining.

ADCC assay

The optimal effector/target (E:T) ratio was determined to be 8:1 for the antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC) study. NCI-H358 target cells labeled with CellTrace Far Red dye were cocultured with NK effector cells isolated from human peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) at an E:T ratio of 8:1. The cocultures were serially diluted with DB-1419, DB-141902, and isotype control ADCs from 15 to 0.000192 μg/mL for 6 hours at 37°C and then stained with PI for 10 minutes, followed by target cell lysis measurement by flow cytometry.

IHC and immunofluorescence

IHC for B7-H3 was conducted on formalin-fixed, paraffin-embedded sections from patient-derived xenograft (PDX) models. Specifically, the antigen was retrieved using a Tris-EDTA solution, followed by blocking with normal goat serum. Sections were incubated with primary antibodies at 4°C overnight. After incubation with an SPlink Detection Kit, immunoreactivity was characterized using a DAB Substrate Kit. After hematoxylin staining, the sections were dehydrated, hyalinized, and cover-slipped.

For immunofluorescence, tissue sections were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde and then washed 3 times with PBS. After blocking with 5% BSA for 1 hour, the primary antibody (1:100–1:500) was incubated overnight at 4°C. The samples were then incubated with fluorescently labeled secondary antibody (1:200–1:1,000) for 1 hour. Nuclear staining was performed with DAPI. Samples were mounted with an antifade medium and imaged using a fluorescence microscope in a darkroom to avoid quenching. The primary antibodies utilized here are outlined in Supplementary Table S2.

Immunogenic cell death

NCI-H87 cells were resuspended with RPMI 1640 complete medium at a density of 2 × 105 cells/mL, then plated into a 96-well plate (2 or 1 × 104 cells/100 µL), and incubated in a 37°C incubator with 5% CO2 overnight. Then, human PBMCs of 1 × 105 cells/100 μL were added to the NCI-H87 cells, followed by treatment with medium, 1 µg/mL DB-1303, and 1 µg/mL anti-Rac-human IgG1 for 24 hours at 37°C. Subsequently, 100 µL of supernatants were harvested to detect CXCL10/IP-10 by ELISA. For the detection of HLA-DR expression on monocytes, harvested cells were washed once with PBS and stained with antibody against CD14 (BioLegend, Cat# 301808) and HLA-DR (BioLegend, Cat# 307604) for 30 minutes at 4°C, followed by the analysis of HLA-DR expression on CD14+ monocytes by flow cytometry.

 In vivo studies

All animal studies were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee guidelines of GemPharmatech Co., Ltd. All NCG mice, ages 6 to 8 weeks, were purchased from GemPharmatech Co., Ltd. (certificate no.: A202311230224) and housed in pathogen-free conditions. The animals were euthanized when they reached the ethical or experimental endpoints.

 In vivo efficacy studies in immunodeficient tumor models

CDX studies

Huh7 cell–derived xenograft (CDX) models were established by subcutaneously injecting Huh7 cells (5 × 106 cells/100 µL) into female mice. Once the tumor volume reached 96.33 mm3, mice were randomized by tumor size into treatment and vehicle control groups (n = 5 each group) and administered intravenously with the indicated agents once weekly for three doses.

PDX studies

Tumor fragments (LD1-0034-361136) derived from prostate cancer were implanted subcutaneously in male mice for the establishment of PDX models. Mice with tumors reaching 161.79 mm3 were randomly assigned by tumor volume to DB-1419 and vehicle control groups (n = 6 each group) and treated intravenously on days 0 and 7.

Tumor volumes were measured twice weekly and calculated as 1/2 × length × width2. Body weight was also measured biweekly. Serum and tumor tissue samples were collected from tumor-bearing mouse models for pharmacokinetic and Western blotting analyses of DNA damage and cell apoptosis markers.

 In vivo efficacy studies in immunocompetent tumor models

CDX studies

Immunocompetent CDX models were established by injecting both cancer cell lines (NCI-H82, 5 × 106 cells; A375, 5 × 106 cells; NCI-H1975, 2 × 106 cells) and human PBMCs (Milecell Biotechnologies, Cat# PB100C; 7.5 × 106, 9 × 106, and 3 × 105 cells, respectively) per 100 µL medium into female NCG mice. Animals with the desired tumor volume (human-PBMC-NCI-H82 CDX model: ∼93.53 mm3; human-PBMC-NCI-H1975 CDX model: ∼100–200 mm3; human-PBMC-A375 CDX model: ∼108.34 mm3) were randomly divided into treatment and vehicle control groups (n = 6 in each group) and received repeated doses of the indicated schedules as specified in our protocol.

PDX studies

A 3 × 3 × 3 mm3 tissue block of PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor–resistant lung cancer (LD1-0025-200794) for immunocompetent PDX models was subcutaneously implanted into female mice, with an i.p. injection of human PBMCs (5 × 106 cells). Group assignment by tumor volumes (n = 5 in each group) was carried out when the tumors reached approximately 100 to 200 mm3, followed by the treatment with 12 mg/kg DB-1419, 10 mg/kg isotype control ADC, and vehicle control at the indicated time point. Tumor volumes and body weight were monitored as described above. In the luciferase assay for PD-1/PD-L1 blocking, Chinese hamster ovary-K1–PD-L1 cells at 8 × 104 cells/cell were plated overnight and then incubated with an equal number of Jurkat–PD-1–NFAT cells and indicated agents of a fourfold concentration dilution series starting at 300 nmol/L. After a 6-hour incubation at 37°C, luciferase activity was determined using the Steady-Glo Luciferase Assay System following the manufacturer’s instructions (Promega) and analyzed by a Multifunctional Microplate Reader (Molecular Devices).

Toxicity studies

 In vitro cytokine release assay

PBMCs from three healthy donors (2 × 105 cells) were added to flat-bottom 96-well plates (solid-phase method) and round-bottom plates (aqueous-phase method) coated with DB-1419 and isotype control ADCs at the indicated concentrations. Anti-CD3/CD28 stimulation was used as a positive control. After 24 hours of incubation at 37°C, the levels of cytokines (IFN-γ, IL-2, IL-6, and IL-10) in cell supernatants were measured using a Human Cytokine Premixed Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific) on a Bio-Plex 200 system (Bio-Rad; RRID: SCR_018026) based on the manufacturer’s protocol.

Repeated dose toxicity study of DB-1419 in cynomolgus monkeys

DB-1419 was administered to cynomolgus monkeys (n = 10 per group) once every 3 weeks for a total of two doses at 15, 45, and 120 mg/kg. Animals were monitored throughout the study for clinical signs, body weight, food consumption, hematology, clinical biochemistry, and cytokines. Histopathology was assessed on the day of necropsy, and a 2-month recovery period was used to evaluate the reversibility of the toxic changes.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis for between-group comparison was performed using two-tailed Student t tests and one-way ANOVA, as appropriate. Survival was assessed using the Kaplan–Meier curve and compared using the log-rank test. The Pearson correlation test was used to analyze the correlation between two factors. Data were analyzed in GraphPad Prism 9 software and presented as the mean ± SEM or SD of at least two replicates (unless otherwise indicated), denoting the statistical significance of P < 0.05 (*, P < 0.05; **, P < 0.01; ***, P < 0.001; ****, P < 0.0001).

Results

PD-L1 and B7-H3 are overexpressed and coexpressed in various tumors associated with poor prognosis

As previously discussed, B7-H3 and PD-L1 are highly expressed in multiple solid tumor types, leading to poor prognosis. As such, we detected the expression patterns on the tissue microarray samples covering lung cancer, liver cancer, esophageal squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma by immunofluorescence and observed the high expression and coexpression of these two targets (Supplementary Fig. S1). Collectively, these findings demonstrate high and simultaneous expression of PD-L1 and B7-H3 across multiple solid tumors, with a clear correlation to poor patient outcomes, implying the therapeutic potential of targeting coexpressed B7-H3 × PD-L1.

Characterization of DB-1419

DB-1419 comprises DB-141902, a humanized tetravalent B7-H3 × PD-L1–targeting BsAb in IgG1-VHH format constructed as described previously, conjugated to eight molecules of the cytotoxic payload P1003 via a cleavable linker (Fig. 1A). Subsequently, we preliminarily characterized the properties of DB-1419. The target specificity was assessed by ELISA. The results showed the binding of DB-1419 and DB-141902 to human B7-H3/PD-L1 and negligible activity with other members of the human B7 superfamily, suggesting the binding specificity of DB-1419 for B7-H3 and PD-L1 (Fig. 1B). Binding affinity measured by ELISA showed that DB-1419 bound to human B7-H3 (4Ig; EC50, 0.15 μg/mL) and PD-L1 (EC50, 2.68 μg/mL) with high affinity, consistently seen with DB-141902 (respective EC50, 0.17 and 2.64 μg/mL; Fig. 1C). Besides, the sandwich ELISA revealed that DB-1419 and DB-141902 could simultaneously bind to both antigens (Fig. 1D). The surface plasmon resonance for species cross-reactivity indicated that DB-1419 and DB-141902 exhibited high and comparable affinities for cynomolgus monkey and human B7-H3 (4Ig) but low or none for mouse or rat B7-H3 protein, a similar pattern to what was observed for PD-L1 (Fig. 1E; Supplementary Fig. S2; Supplementary Table S3). These results suggest that the cynomolgus monkey is an appropriate species for nonclinical pharmacokinetic and toxicity studies of DB-1419. We found that stable attachments of the linker–toxin in human, cynomolgus monkeys, and rat plasma were maintained for at least 21 days, and the release rates of payload P1003 from DB-1419 were less than approximately 4.4% (Fig. 1F), indicating the in vitro stability of DB-1419. Besides, no obvious difference in the pharmacokinetic profiles between ADCs and total antibody was observed in cynomolgus monkeys (Fig. 1G; Supplementary Table S4). Taken together, these results indicate that DB-1419 is a BsADC targeting B7-H3 × PD-L1, with high binding specificity, affinity, and stability in circulation.

DB-1419 exhibits in vitro cytotoxicity against various cancer cell lines

We subsequently assessed the in vitro cytotoxicity of DB-1419 against 11 cancer cell lines. DB-1419 showed strong proliferation inhibition in the NCI-H358, NCI-H1568, Huh7, and PC-3 cell lines (respective IC50: 0.531, 0.704, 2.202, and 6.018 nmol/L) but moderate inhibition in the DMS53, A375, and NCI-H441 cell lines and weak inhibition in the MDA-MB-231, NCI-H82, KYSE-150, and NCI-H1975 cell lines (Fig. 2A). Albeit to varying degrees, all cell lines were sensitive to the free payload P1003, with IC50 ranging from 1.654 to 21.990 nmol/L, whereas no cytotoxicity of the parental BsAb DB-141902 was observed in any of the cell lines. The binding activity of DB-1419 varied across 11 cell lines, as determined by flow cytometry (Fig. 2B). We also observed a weak correlation between DB-1419 binding activity and cytotoxicity (R = −0.2), mainly affected by binding activity to B7-H3 (R = −0.18) compared with PD-L1 (R = 0.64; Fig. 2C and D). Taken together, these results indicate that DB-1419 displays inhibitory effects on the proliferation of heterogeneous tumors, potentially independent of binding activity.

MOA of DB-1419

Internalization kinetics of DB-1419

Next, we further explored the potential mechanism behind the in vitro antitumor activity of DB-1419. ADCs function primarily by efficient internalization and trafficking to a lysosome for the release of their payload. The internalization kinetics assessed by flow cytometry with NCI-H358 cells revealed a time-dependent increase in internalization for DB-1419 and DB-141902 from 6 to 48 hours, with more rapid internalization of DB-1419 (Fig. 3A). Confocal microscopy for the intracellular trafficking of DB-1419 showed that DB-1419 was colocalized with the lysosome marker at 48 hours, indicating its translocation to lysosomes (Fig. 3B).

DNA damage and apoptosis

After the release of free payload P1003 from DB-1419 in the lysosome, P1003 is anticipated to induce DNA damage and apoptosis, known mechanisms for the cytotoxic effects of the TOPIi. Comparative results showed that in a similar manner to P1003, DB-1419 significantly induced cell-cycle arrest in the G2–M-phase (Fig. 3C) and apoptosis of NCI-H358 cells (Fig. 3D), indicated by the increased proportion of G2–M and apoptotic cells, but neither the parental BsAb nor the isotype control ADC induced these changes. We further evaluated the phosphorylation of H2AX (DNA damage marker) and the cleavage of PARP (apoptosis marker) using Western blotting in NCI-H358 cells and found an increase in phosphorylated H2AX and cleaved PARP after the 72-hour treatment with DB-1419 and P1003 (Fig. 3E).

Bystander effects

ADCs could be cytotoxic to neighboring antigen-negative cancer cells by virtue of bystander effects, an important contributor to the activity of ADCs against heterogeneous tumors. Evaluation of the bystander effects revealed that DB-1419 enhanced the proliferation inhibition of target-negative 293T–B7-H3–KO cells in 293T–B7-H3 and 293T–B7-H3–KO cocultures, triggering a bystander-killing effect, whereas neither DB-141902 nor isotype control ADCs showed noticeable cytotoxicity (Fig. 3F).

ADCC

In addition, the Fc backbone of IgG antibodies–mediated immune effector functions, including ADCCs, mechanistically plays crucial roles in antitumor activity. We tested a series of E:T ratios and determined the condition at an E:T of 8:1 (Supplementary Fig. S3). As measured by the lysis of NCI-H358 cells mediated by human PBMCs, DB-1419 exhibited significant ADCC activity (EC50, 0.012 μg/mL), along with DB-141902 (EC50, 0.008 μg/mL; Fig. 3G).

Collectively from these findings, DB-1419 could drive antitumor activity via bystander effects, Ab-mediated ADCC, along with molecular dynamics in which DB-1419 binds to the surface antigens, internalizes into tumor cells, is trafficked to lysosomes, and releases its payload, killing tumor cells via P1003-mediated DNA damage and apoptosis.

DB-1419 exhibits significant antitumor activity in vivo through target-specific payload delivery and P1003-mediated direct cytotoxicity

Next, the in vivo antitumor activity of DB-1419 was examined in liver cancer CDX (Huh7) and more clinically relevant lung cancer PDX (LD1-0034-361136) models. Tumor-bearing mice were administered repeated doses of vehicle control, isotype control ADC, and DB-1419. Compared with the vehicle control group at 18 days, all dose groups of DB-1419 showed superior antitumor activities, with respective tumor growth inhibition (TGI) of 73.08%, 92.33%, and 97.09% (Fig. 4A). DB-1419 at 12 and 18 mg/kg significantly inhibited tumor growth, indicated by obviously reduced tumor volume and tumor weight, with respect to the isotype control ADC group on day 28 (Fig. 4A and B; rapid tumor growth led to the early termination of the vehicle control group). In the LD1-0034-361136 PDX model, DB-1419 resulted in significant inhibition of tumor growth at 28 days in a dose-independent manner (TGI of 6 mg/kg, 96.19%; TGI of 12 mg/kg, 97.18%; Fig. 4D and E). No obvious body weight loss was observed in all mouse models treated with DB-1419 (Fig. 4C and F).

To further explain the underlying potency of DB-1419, we also collected serum and tumor tissue samples from tumor-bearing mice for pharmacokinetics. As illustrated in Fig. 4G, DB-1419 and total antibody showed similar pharmacokinetic profiles in which the serum concentration of both rapidly reached the Cmax and then gradually decreased, whereas negligible serum exposure of free P1003 was detected, indicating the stability of DB-1419 in serum in vivo. By contrast, DB-1419 and free P1003 in the high-dose group peaked at maximum concentrations at 24 and 24 hours in intratumoral cells, with a dose-dependent increase in exposure (Fig. 4G; Supplementary Table S5). The Cmax of free P1003 in tumor tissues was approximately 6 to 17 times greater than that in circulation. Subsequently, we investigated the involvement of P1003-mediated DNA damage and apoptosis in the cytotoxicity induced by DB-1419. Western blotting analysis using tumor tissues revealed that DB-1419 induced the phosphorylation of H2AX and cleavage of PARP (Fig. 4H), consistent with the changes observed in vitro.

DB-1419 shows robust antitumor activity in immunocompetent tumor models

In addition to the aforementioned innate immune activity in immunodeficient models, we further characterized the pharmacologic effects of DB-1419 in immunocompetent xenograft models with human PBMCs. Compared with the vehicle control, DB-1419 resulted in significant tumor regression in NCI-H82 (lung cancer CDX; TGI, 82.39%–97.55%), NCI-H1975 (lung cancer CDX; TGI, 31.00%–75.31%), and A375 (melanoma CDX; TGI, 51.33%–97.60%) models, without inducing an obvious change in body weight (Fig. 5A–C). Pharmacodynamics in PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitor–resistant LD1-0025-200794 immunocompetent PDX models revealed that DB-1419 also inhibited tumor growth by 78.56% over vehicle control (Fig. 5D); combined favorable safety data supported that DB-1419 may overcome resistance to PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors.

We then investigate whether the remarkable tumor-killing capacity of DB-1419 in immunocompetent models is covered by immune activation, considering the PD-L1–neutralizing properties of its parental BsAbs. As anticipated, DB-1419 strongly blocked the interaction between PD-1 and PD-L1, with similar blocking activity to DB-141902 (IC50, 2.336 vs. 1.873 nmol/L; Fig. 5E).

Altogether, these data indicate that DB-1419 could elicit a potent antitumor response in immunocompetent models through the adaptive immune activation triggered by the PD-1/PD-L1 blockade.

Safety profiles of DB-1419

We then evaluated the safety of DB-1419 in vitro and in vivo. The assessments for the nonspecific release of inflammatory cytokines revealed that DB-1419 at up to 50 μg/mL did not elicit nonspecific production of inflammatory cytokines, as demonstrated by lower levels of IFN-γ, IL-2, IL-6, and IL-10 in the DB-1419 group compared with those in the anti-CD3/CD28 agonist antibody-positive control group (Fig. 6).

Next, the safety of DB-1419 at 15, 45, and 120 mg/kg once every 3 weeks for two repeated doses was assessed in cynomolgus monkeys, the cross-reactive species for DB-1419. No deaths or moribund outcomes were observed at any dose throughout the study. Decreased blood reticulocytes and thymic lymphocytes, minimal-to-slight chronic pulmonary inflammation, minimal alveolar edema, and increased levels of aspartate aminotransferase and alanine aminotransferase were reversible and predominantly occurred at 120 mg/kg, the highest dose tested (Supplementary Table S6). Consequently, the highest nonseverely toxic dose of DB-1419 was determined to be 120 mg/kg in cynomolgus monkeys.

Overall, the findings of safety profiles suggest that DB-1419 is well tolerated in vivo and does not induce the production of inflammatory cytokines in vitro.

Discussion

The fast-expanding combined modality of ICIs and ADCs could induce tumor-specific adaptive immunity to enhance T-cell activation and infiltration for a more active immune response, clinically enabling it to overcome resistance and improve patient outcomes. However, such a combination is limited by the narrow therapeutic window. Herein, we developed DB-1419, an immune-conjugated bispecific ADC targeting both B7-H3 and PD-L1, utilizing a next-generation BsADC platform that bridges immunotherapeutic and chemotherapeutic mechanisms. DB-1419 combines innate and adaptive immune activation—through ADCC and PD-1/PD-L1 blockade—with potent direct cytotoxicity mediated by the payload P1003, following efficient internalization and lysosomal cleavage. Besides, bystander-killing effects also contribute to the robust in vivo and in vitro antitumor effects of DB-1419 against lung cancer and beyond. In addition to these desirable properties, DB-1419 also demonstrates serum stability and good tolerance in preclinical evaluations.

As shown for TOPIi-based conjugates, TOPIi could induce cell apoptosis via DNA damage, following a well-characterized cascade: specific antigen binding, ADC internalization, and trafficking to lysosomes to release toxic payloads. Consistently, in this study, we observed efficient internalization and translocation of DB-1419 to lysosomes within 48 hours, as visualized by flow cytometry and confocal microscopy, accompanied by a posttreatment increase in DNA damage and apoptosis markers. In vitro studies further confirmed that both DB-1419 and the free payload P1003 inhibited tumor cell proliferation, supporting the notion that P1003-mediated TOP1 inhibition is a key component of DB-1419’s multifaceted MOA in exerting direct cytotoxic effects. Despite no cytotoxicity by naked BsAbs, ADCC activity mediated by parental DB-141902 was retained in the context of the TOPIi conjugate, emerging as a contributor to the overall efficacy of DB-1419. Besides, coculture experiments using 293T–B7-H3 and 293T–B7-H3–KO cells revealed reduced cell viability compared with 293T–B7-H3–KO monocultures alone. This indicated that DB-1419 triggered the bystander killing effect, referring to cell-permeable TOPIi payload P1003 diffusion from high B7-H3–expressing tumor cells to surrounding B7-H3–negative cells. Benchmarked against bystander-capable ADCs. DB-1419 is also expected to be effective for heterogeneous tumors. As seen in other effective TOPIi-based ADCs utilizing high DAR, the low-toxicity TOPIi of eight molecules (DAR upper limit typically applied for ADC; ref.) incorporated into the DB-1419 design may also be considered a factor for sufficient cell-killing effects. Although cross-comparison should be interpreted with caution, DB-1419 may display a stronger in vitro antiproliferation in contrast to the only preclinically evaluated BsB7-H3 × PD-L1–ADC with monomethyl auristatin E (average DAR 1.7); such inference may result from the high DAR of DB-1419, along with the different MOA of the conjugated drug and differential sensitivity among the cell lines used.

Consistent with the in vitro results, DB-1419 also elicited significant antitumor activities in vivo in liver cancer CDX and lung cancer PDX; mechanistically, DB-1419 induced P1003-mediated DNA damage and apoptosis, as indicated by increased levels of phosphorylated H2AX and cleaved PARP in tumor tissues, detected via Western blotting at the study endpoint. Besides, the high stability in circulation could enable the preferential enrichment of the therapeutic payload into tumors and finally favor DB-1419, as evidenced by similar pharmacokinetic profiles between DB-1419 and total antibody, and obviously higher concentrations of free P1003 in tumors over in serum, detected by mouse xenograft pharmacokinetic studies.

In addition to the aforementioned cytotoxic mechanisms, adaptive immune activation may also function as an important contributor to the overall efficacy of DB-1419, as seen in established PBMC-humanized xenograft models. The observed PD-L1/PD-1 blockade by DB-1419, consistent with parental BsAbs, suggests these Ab-directed activities are retained in the conjugated context. This blockade may promote T-cell proliferation and reinvigoration to further improve the therapeutic efficacy of DB-1419, requiring future validation. Considering the hallmarks of immunogenic cell death (ICD) induced by TOPIi-based ADCs, we logically speculate that the antitumor immunity of DB-1419 may also be driven by the ICD. Such elicitation could prompt antigen-presenting cell activation and enhance their immune recognition and antigen-presenting functions, favoring T-cell recruitment to tumors to strengthen the immune response. Despite no direct examination done here, the observation that DB-1303 with the same payload P1003 induced an ICD, indicated by the identification of the monocyte marker HLA-DR and the release of the chemokine CXCL10/IP10 (Supplementary Fig. S4), may provide the conceptual foundation for our hypothesis. As previously discussed, the increased IFN-γ upregulated PD-L1 expression on tumor cells, boosting the anti-PD-L1–mediated ADCC-killing effect and the ADCC-mediated substantially accelerated, enhanced serial cytotoxicity of NK cells. DB-1419 may trigger these sequential processes related to innate immunity based on the IFN-γ production results from the parental BsAb study, requiring future validation in the context of DB-1419. Consequently, the complete tumor regression seen in more clinically relevant PD-(L)1 inhibitor–resistant immunocompetent PDX was speculatively explained by the amplification of the antitumor immune response under treatment with DB-1419. Dynamic molecular processes involving immune cell modulation and mobilization to overcome the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment by DB-1419 remain an intriguing issue to be elucidated in future studies.

Repeated-dose toxicity study showed that DB-1419 was well tolerated in cross-reactive cynomolgus monkeys. Given that no severe toxicity was observed up to the tested highest dose, the highest nonseverely toxic dose was determined to be 120 mg/kg. The high stability of DB-1419 in circulation was responsible for the reduced off-target toxicities, indicative of almost superimposable pharmacokinetic profiles of the total antibody and DB-1419 in multiple species and negligible P1003 release in serum. Specifically, compared with the gastrointestinal and bone marrow toxicities as clinically typical dose-limiting events of TOPIi-based ADCs, we reported transient emesis during the second dose at 15 mg/kg and reversible changes in decreased blood reticulocytes and thymic lymphocytes. Mild hematologic toxicity observed here helps mitigate the challenges associated with overlapping events in the synergistic combination of PARP inhibitors (PARPi) and TOPIi; this provides a potential opportunity for DB-1419 plus PARPi, potentially extending therapeutic benefits to PARPi–resistant tumors or those beyond DNA repair deficiencies, as stated in the historical TOPIi-based ADC series. Consistent with previous studies involving immune-directing agents, we also found recoverable alanine aminotransferase and aspartate aminotransferase increases, anticipated not to offset the potential therapeutic benefits in the clinical setting under appropriate interventions. Based on the preclinical data showing the transient increase in serum IL-6 in monkeys (suspected involvement with anti-drug Ab; data not shown) and the absence of nonspecific production of inflammatory cytokines in vitro, DB-1419 could be expected to minimize the risk of cytokine storms in the ongoing clinical trial (NCT06554795). It remains unclear whether the observed pulmonary effects (minimal-to-slight chronic pulmonary inflammation and minimal alveolar edema) are mediated by target distribution, given the unknown expression of B7-H3 and PD-L1 in the monkey lungs, in contrast to the moderate expression observed in normal human lungs according to the Human Protein Atlas. Importantly, clinical safety monitoring of lung toxicity in humans cannot be emphasized enough. Altogether, acceptable safety potentially favors widening the clinically relevant therapeutic window of DB-1419 and allowing for the further rational development of synergistic combinations.

Several limitations of this study should be acknowledged. Although clinically relevant PDX models were used to validate in vivo efficacy, subcutaneous mouse models cannot recapitulate the inherent complexity and heterogeneity of human cancers, especially metastatic diseases. Besides, further investigations on immune activation and tumor microenvironment interactions behind the activities of DB-1419 and comparative studies in contrast to other ADCs might be sensible. Data from Fig. 2 suggest that DB-1419 may exert antitumor activity independently of the binding activity, potentially informing the therapeutic potential of DB-1419 to respond to low-binding-activity cancers. However, the target expression levels required for desirable ADC activity may be affected by the intrinsic sensitivity of tumor cells to the TOPIi warhead. Consequently, further preclinical studies in homologous recombination repair deficiency tumors with various target expressions are warranted to better elucidate how target engagement and alignment of the payload MOA to tumor biology contribute to the overall efficacy of DB-1419. In parallel, different from single-agent ADCs, the binding modes of BsADCs and the internalization mechanism behind targets may also affect the treatment outcomes, requiring more future efforts. Besides, despite its therapeutic potential and promising safety profiles in this preclinical study, DB-1419 may still exhibit off-target toxicity in humans, considering the low expressions of B7-H3 and PD-L1 in normal human tissues; therefore, further clinical studies are warranted based on the current findings.

In conclusion, DB-1419 harbored therapeutic potential against multiple tumors, even in PD-(L)1–resistant settings, as a compatible result of immunotherapeutic activity and direct cytotoxicity. Furthermore, DB-1419 showed acceptable pharmacokinetic and safety profiles. Data shared in this report may also provide valuable insights for the evolution of immunotherapeutic strategies and advancements in bifunctional BsADCs, approaching further to overcome resistance and heterogeneity. These promising preclinical findings advance the evaluation of DB-1419 from bench to bedside, and a first-in-human phase I/IIa study of DB-1419 for advanced/metastatic solid tumors is currently underway (NCT06554795). In parallel, additional comparative studies to other ADCs (such as DS-7300a) will be required if early efficacy signals emerge.
