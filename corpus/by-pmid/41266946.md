# Multiparametric MRI to Predict Response to Irreversible Electroporation Plus Anti-PD-1 Immunotherapy in Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: To investigate contrast-enhanced T1-weighted MRI and diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (DWI) for early prediction of tumor response to combined irreversible electroporation (IRE) and anti-PD-1 immunotherapy.

**METHODS**: Murine pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) cells KRAS* were inoculated into the pancreas of C57BL/6 mice. IRE was performed when tumors became palpable. After IRE, mice received six intraperitoneal injections of anti-PD-1 over 2 weeks. T2-weighted MRI, T1-weighted MRI with macromolecular contrast agent poly(L-glutamic acid)-conjugated gadolinium (PG-Gd), and DWI were performed before and after IRE. Survival was analyzed using Kaplan-Meier curves. Mice surviving at least 100 days without recurrence after IRE were considered complete responders. Relationships between MRI findings and treatment response were assessed with one-way ANOVA, Student's t-test, and receiver operating characteristic (ROC) analysis.

**RESULTS**: Tumor volume on T2-weighted MRI at early treatment time-points did not correlate with survival. T1-weighted MRI with PG-Gd clearly showed increased PG-Gd uptake in tumors on days 4 and 11-14 after IRE and anti-PD-1 immunotherapy. Higher PG-Gd uptake correlated with longer survival (R2 = 0.634; p < 0.0001). Area-under-the-ROC curve had high predictive value for treatment outcome (0.805; p = 0.033). Immunofluorescence staining confirmed high accumulation of macrophages in tumors after treatment. On DWI, all KRAS* tumors exhibited significantly increased apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) values 7, 11, and 14 days after combination therapy. Moreover, pretreatment tumor ADC values were significantly higher in complete responders than in partial responders (p = 0.0025).

**CONCLUSION**: PG-Gd contrast-enhanced T1-weighted MRI and DWI are potential predictors of response to combined IRE and anti-PD-1 immunotherapy in PDAC.

Introduction

Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is resistant to most current therapies, including immunotherapies. PDAC is distinguished from other cancer types by a robust desmoplastic stroma, which contributes to an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Recent preclinical studies showed that tumor ablation by irreversible electroporation (IRE) can significantly sensitize PDAC to anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy, effectively turning a local therapy into a systemic therapy. Combining IRE and anti‐PD‐1 therapy significantly increased tumor infiltration of CD8+ T cells and induced a long‐term memory immune response. Other preclinical studies demonstrated enhanced anti‐PDAC activity when IRE was combined with other forms of immunotherapy. Clinical studies have been initiated on IRE in combination with immunotherapy in PDAC patients with locally advanced disease.

To accelerate clinical translation of IRE‐based combination immunotherapy, there is a clear need for biomarkers that can predict which patients are likely to have tumor recurrence. Such biomarkers could be used to decide whether to continue the current therapy or switch the patient to a different therapy. Currently, positron emission tomography with 18F‐fluorodeoxyglucose ([18F]FDG PET) is frequently used to assess response to therapy in cancer patients. However, 18F‐FDG PET has limited utility in PDAC because on 18F‐FDG PET images it is difficult to differentiate PDAC from mass‐forming pancreatitis and posttreatment inflammatory changes. Genomic and proteomic biomarkers from tumor biopsy specimens may be useful, but repetitive tumor sampling from patients is not practical and may result in complications.

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) has been used to measure the IRE‐induced ablation zone in liver cancer, but has not been used to assess early response to IRE combined with immunotherapy. Response to immunotherapy sometimes manifests as delayed response or pseudo‐progression, which may not be adequately characterized by traditional response criteria such as the RECIST. The effectiveness of IRE combined with immunotherapy relies on tumor infiltration by immune cells, such as macrophages and T cells. Macrophage infiltration of tumors can be measured by T1‐weighted MRI after injection of macromolecular contrast agent poly‐L‐glutamic acid conjugated DTPA‐gadolinium (PG‐Gd), which is preferentially taken up by macrophages, PG‐Gd contrast enhanced T1‐weighted MRI signal of tumor potentially corelated with phagocytosis of PG‐Gd by macrophage. Moreover, IRE induces necrotic cell death and is therefore expected to result in increased water diffusion. Diffusion‐weighted magnetic resonance imaging (DWI) measures the relative restriction in water diffusion in biological matrices, where lower diffusion reflects greater tissue cellularity with intact membranes.

The purpose of this study was to evaluate the feasibility of using T2‐weighted MRI, PG‐Gd–based T1‐weighted MRI, and DWI for early prediction of treatment response after combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy in an orthotopic, syngeneic mouse model of PDAC.

Methods

All animal experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC) of the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center. PG‐Gd was synthesized as reported. Monoclonal anti‐mouse PD‐1 antibody (αPD‐1; CD279 clone: J43) was purchase from BioXCell (West Lebanon, NH, USA). The Murine PDAC KRAS* cell line (with an inducible Kras  G12D  mutation) was kindly provided by Dr. Y Alan Wang. This cell line was validated by short tandem repeat DNA fingerprinting by Characterized Cell Line Core of the University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center using the AmpFLSTR identifier kit according to the manufacturer's instructions (Applied Biosystems, Thermo Fisher Scientific).

Animal Model

The KRAS* orthotopic PDAC model was established by intrapancreatic inoculation of KRAS* murine PDAC cells (5 × 105 cells in 10 μL of PBS/Matrigel) into 7‐week‐old female C57BL/6 mice (TACONIC Biosciences, Germantown, NY). Study design and number of mice used in this prospective multiparametric MRI study of KRAS* orthotopic PDAC model were shown in Figure S1.

Mice were monitored for survival endpoints, with survival defined as the time until the onset of humane endpoint criteria, including a moribund state, hunched posture, labored breathing, ruffled fur, weight loss exceeding 20%, or tumor size greater than 2.0 cm in diameter. Mice meeting any of these criteria were euthanized.

 IRE Procedure and Combination Therapy With Anti‐PD‐1

IRE was performed as described in a previous report using the ECM 830 Square Wave Electroporation System (BTX Harvard Apparatus, Holliston, MA). When tumors reached approximately 7–8 mm in its longest diameter, tumor‐bearing mice were anesthetized by inhalation of 1% to 2% isoflurane in O2. IRE was performed using a two‐needle array electrode (#45–0168, BTX Harvard Apparatus, Holliston, MA) with a 5‐mm gap. No MRI or other imaging techniques were used to guide the insertion of IRE needles into the tumor. The tumor was surgically exposed outside the abdominal cavity, and the IRE needles were directly inserted into the center of the tumor nodule along the long axis (Figure S2). The electroporation parameters were as follows: voltage, 1200 V; pulse duration, 100 μs; pulse repetition frequency, 1 Hz; and total number of pulses, 99. For sham control mice, the needle array was inserted into the center of the tumor without electroporation.

For IRE‐based combination therapy, anti‐PD‐1 was injected intraperitoneally at 100 μg/mouse (100 μL) at 30 min after IRE and then on days 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 after IRE for a total of six injections.

 MRI System and Acquisition Parameters

All MR images were acquired on a 7T system with a 30‐cm bore and 35‐mm transceiver volume coil (Bruker Biospin Inc., Billerica, MA).

For coronal T2‐weighted MRI, the acquisition parameters were as follows: acquisition method, RARE; echo time (TE), 52 ms; repetition time (TR), 3000 ms; slice thickness, 0.75 mm; field of view (FOV), 4 × 3 cm; matrix, 256 × 192; echo train length (RARE factor), 16; and total acquisition time, 2.4 min. For axial T2‐weighted MRI, the acquisition parameters were as follows: acquisition method, RARE; TE, 52 ms; TR, 2000 ms; slice thickness, 1 mm; FOV, 3 × 3 cm; matrix, 192 × 192; echo train length (RARE factor), 16; and total acquisition time, 1.6 min.

For contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI, PG‐Gd (0.2 mmol of Gd per kg of body weight) was intravenously injected via tail vein on days 2 and 9 (or 12) after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. T1‐weighted MRI (including T2‐weighted MRI) were acquired 3 days (or 1 day) before IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy and 2 days after PG‐Gd injection (on days 4 and 11 [or 14]) (Figure 1A). An interval of 2 days was needed after contrast injection because PG‐Gd is a macromolecular contrast agent with an extended blood circulation time. The acquisition parameters were as follows: acquisition method, RARE; TE, 5.44 ms; TR, 478.414 ms; slice thickness, 1 mm; FOV, 3 × 3 cm; and matrix, 192 × 192. A total of 20 mice (10 mice in each of 2 independent experiments) were included in the study. Two mice were excluded from the analysis they had extensive intraperitoneal tumors at the time of operation.

For the first DWI experiment (10 mice), DWI (including T2‐weighted MRI) was performed on Days −1, 7, 11, and 14. The acquisition parameters were as follows: measuring method, Bruker DtiEpi; TR, 474.818 ms; TE, 30.46 ms; slice orientation, coronal; slice thickness, 1 mm; flip angle, 90°; FOV, 40 mm × 40 mm; image resolution, 625 × 625 μm; b values, 4, 100, 400, and 700 s/mm2. For the second DWI experiment (separate cohort of 5 mice), the acquisition parameters were as follows: measuring method, Bruker Dti Standard; TR, 474.00 ms; TE, 29.48 ms; slice orientation, coronal; slice thickness, 1 mm; flip angle, 90°; FOV, 30 mm × 30 mm; image resolution, 469 × 469 μm; b values, 200, 400, 700, and 1400 s/mm2.

Analysis of MRI Data

For T2‐weighted MRI, tumor volume was calculated by the formula tumor volume = (a × b × c)/2, where a, b, and c are the tumor length, width, and height, respectively, in millimeters. For PG‐Gd–enhanced T1‐weighted MRI, the region of interest (ROI) of the orthotopic pancreatic tumor were drawn and signal intensity quantified, the tumor‐to‐muscle signal‐intensity ratio was calculated using ImageJ software (version 1.54, National Institutes of Health). Tumor‐to‐muscle intensity ratio for each MRI slice was determined using the formula: (Tumor ROI total intensity/Tumor ROI area) ÷ (Muscle ROI total intensity/Muscle ROI area). For DWI, the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) was analyzed using ParaVision 6.0.1 software (Bruker Biospin).

Histologic Examination and Immunofluorescence Staining

Fourteen days after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy, mice were euthanized, and KRAS* PDAC tumors were harvested and fixed in 10% formalin. Tumor tissues were processed by the institutional Histology Core Laboratory. Immunofluorescence staining and image acquisition of tumor tissue slides were performed by the institutional Flow Cytometry & Cellular Imaging Core Facility. Quantitative analysis was done with Visiopharm image analysis software (Visiopharm Inc.).

Statistical Analysis

The log‐rank test was used in Kaplan–Meier survival analyses. The relationship between the survival of treated mice and tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio on PG‐Gd–enhanced T1‐weighted MRI was analyzed using linear regression. Receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve analysis was performed to assess the diagnostic utility of estimating volume and tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio. AUC and significance were calculated for each variable, and hybrid Wilson/Brown method for computing confidence interval of AUC. Data was expressed as mean ± SD. Student's t‐test or one‐way ANOVA was used to analyze differences in ADC and histological data sets. A p value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant. All statistical calculations were performed using GraphPad software (version 10.3.1).

Results

Predictive Performance of T2‐Weighted MRI 

The treatment protocol is shown in Figure 1A. Representative axial T2‐weighted MRI and PG‐Gd–enhanced T1‐weighted MRI of a partial responder and a complete responder acquired before IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy, at days 4 and 11 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy are shown in Figure 1B.

Data combined from two independent experiments of 10 mice and 8 mice each (18 total), Kaplan–Meier survival curve is shown in Figure 2A. Seven of 18 mice survived > 100 days after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy without recurrence and were considered complete responders. The other 11 mice died < 100 days after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy due to tumor recurrence and were considered partial responders. In both partial and complete responders, tumors were smaller on days 4 and 11 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy than they were before treatment. The discriminative ability of quantitative T2‐weighted MRI tumor volume at an early treatment time point to predict mice survival, as assessed by the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve, is summarized in Figure 2B. The analysis revealed that the area under the curve (AUC) for T2‐MRI tumor volume was 0.675 (95% CI: 0.42 to 0.93; p = 0.22). The ROC curve illustrates the relationship between the true positive rate (sensitivity) and the false positive rate (specificity). The AUC represents how effectively a parameter can differentiate between two diagnostic groups (death of partial responders versus survival of complete responders), with higher AUC values indicating better discriminative ability. Generally, AUC values above 0.80 are considered clinically useful, while those below 0.80 are deemed of limited clinical utility (). Thus, at the early time point after treatment, tumor volume alone did not reliably distinguish between death of partial responders from survival of complete responders.

Predictive Performance of PG‐Gd–Enhanced T1‐Weighted MRI 

T1‐weighted MRI on days 4 and 11 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy showed contrast enhancement in both the partial responders and the complete responders (Figure 1B). Simple linear regression analysis of data from two independent experiments revealed a significant correlation between tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy 11 days and the survival outcomes of treated mice (Figure 2C). The discriminative ability of quantitative T1‐weighted MRI tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio at an early treatment time point for predicting mice survival, ROC analysis showed that the AUC for estimating the tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio was 0.805 (95% CI: 0.59 to 1.00; p = 0.033) (Figure 2D). These findings suggest that PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI had the potential to predict survival of complete responders versus the death of partial responders at the early time point following the combination of IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy in PDAC bearing mice.

Combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy induced extensive tissue necrosis (Figure 3A). Immunofluorescence imaging of non‐necrotic tumor areas from the treated mice showed massive infiltration of F4/80+ macrophages (p < 0.01) accompanied by significant increased CD31+ vascular endothelial cells (p < 0.01), indicating increased tumor microvessel density compared to untreated control (Figure 3B,C).

Predictive Performance of DWI 

Figure 4A shows DWI and treatment plan. Figure 4B shows representative T2‐weighted MRI of mice from both the partial responders and the complete responders in the DWI cohort. Ten mice were included in this first DWI experiment; five of them had complete response, and five had partial response (Figure 4C). In nine of the 10 treated mice, tumor volume gradually decreased over a period of 2 weeks after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. In the partial responders, tumors recurred 70 days after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. However, at 40 days after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy, only three of the five partial responders had recurred (Figure 4D).

Figure 5A shows representative DWI images of a treated mouse acquired on day −1, 7, 11, and 14 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. Signal intensity on DWI images decreased at all time points indicating increased water diffusibility (signal intensity on a DWI image negatively correlates with the ADC value). Analysis of DWI data revealed that combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy significantly increased ADC values for all mice tumors measured on days 7, 11, and 14 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy compared with the ADC measured before treatment regardless of response status (Figure 5B). Moreover, ADC measured on day 11 and day 14 had a broader distribution than ADC measured at day 7, indicating heterogeneous response captured at these time points. ADC trajectories of each mouse on pre‐IRE, day 7 post‐IRE, day 11 post‐IRE, and day 14 post‐IRE were shown in Figure S3.

Close examination of DWI images revealed that Pre‐treatment DWI signal intensity was higher in partial responders compared to complete responders (Figure 6A). Additionally, pre‐treatment tumor ADC values were significantly higher in the complete responders than in the partial responders (p = 0.0025) (Figure 6B). ROC analysis demonstrated a strong predictive capability of pretreatment ADC values from DWI for estimating mouse survival, with an AUC of 0.88 (95% CI: 0.64 to 1.00; p = 0.047) (Figure 6C). These findings suggest that DWI holds promise as a predictive tool for survival outcomes even before treatment initiation.

For both partial responders and complete responders, ADC measured at day 7 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy was significantly higher than ADC measured before IRE (Figure 7A,B). Indeed, while ADC measured on day 7 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy was similar in partial responders and complete responders (Figure 7C). However, no difference was found in ΔADC (ADC measured post‐IRE—ADC measured before‐IRE) between the partial responders and the complete responders (Figure S4). In addition, there was no difference in pre‐treatment tumor volume between partial responders and complete responders (Figure 7D).

Similar findings were observed in the second DWI experiment in the separate cohort of 5 mice. Combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy significantly increased ADC measured on day 7 and day 11 compared to the pre‐treatment ADC values (Figure S5A). The ADC values measured on day 7 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy were not significantly different from those measured on day 11 (p = 0.10), although the mean value was numerically lower on day 11, suggesting an increase in cellular density over time. As in the first DWI experiment, pre‐treatment ADC values were lower in the partial responders (mean = 0.000748) than in the complete responders (mean = 0.00105) (p = 0.068) (Figure S5B).

In addition, IRE is known as non‐thermal tumor ablation technique. However, IRE can also generate heat (Joule heating) as electric current passes through the tissue. This heat may cause moderate temperature elevation and cause tissue damage. To address this concern, the potential impact of IRE on the original adjacent organs are shown in Figure S6. Histopathological analysis showed that IRE had minimal impacts on these organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung and intestine) except kidney, which displayed moderate interstitial congestion after IRE as compared to sham treatment control.

Discussion

Three MR imaging sequences were used to assess early treatment response in an orthotopic model of PDAC: T2‐weighted MRI for measuring tumor volume, PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI for estimating tumor infiltration by macrophages, and DWI for estimating tumor cell density. The findings showed that PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI and DWI, but not T2‐weighted MRI, have potential for early prediction of tumor response to combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy.

Both partial responders and complete responders had significant tumor volume reduction over the first 2 weeks after combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. ROC analysis indicated that tumor volume on T2‐weighted MRI early after treatment is not a predictor of therapy response. A previous study showed that contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI could accurately measure size of ablation zone after IRE in patients. However, the change in the size of the ablation zone may not correlate with survival after combined IRE and immunotherapy. Multiple factors can influence the outcome of immunotherapy, including the presence of minimal residual disease, the extent of T‐cell infiltration, and the presence of other immune cells such as macrophages in the TME.

On PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI, tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity ratio measured on 2 days after PG‐Gd injection (4 day and 11 day [or 14 day] after treatment) significantly correlated with the survival. The PG‐Gd–mediated increase in T1‐weighted MRI signal intensity may reflect early tumor infiltration of phagocytic macrophages, which are known to take up PG‐Gd. This interpretation is supported by the massive infiltration of macrophages observed on day 14 after combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. Anti‐PD‐1 may reprogram tumor‐associated macrophages (TAMs), enhancing their phagocytic activity . Previously, we have shown that (1) PG‐Gd colocalized with tumor‐associated macrophages in an ovarian cancer model, (2) PG‐Gd was selective taken up by macrophages but not by tumor cells in vitro, and (3) increased PG‐Gd ‐enhanced T1 weighted MRI signal intensity was association with increased number of CD45 + CD169+ phagocytic macrophages after abraxane treatment. These evidence supports our interpretation that both increased quantity and improved quality of macrophages may be responsive for the observed synergistic antitumor effect in the combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 treatment group, and that increased tumor infiltration of macrophages after IRE + anti‐PD‐1 therapy was in part responsible for increased T1‐weighted MRI signal intensity.

Other changes in the TME, such as increased tumor microvessel density (Figure 3B), may also attribute to increased PG‐Gd uptake in Kras* PDAC after IRE + anti‐PD‐1 therapy. Further studies are needed to dissect relative contributions of increased tumor infiltrating macrophages and increased angiogenic blood vessels to increased tumor PG‐Gd uptake. One approach is to use dynamic contrast enhanced MRI (DCE‐MRI) with clinically used small molecular weight Gd‐DTPA (gadopentetate dimeglumine). Dual tracer pharmacokinetic modeling with both Gd‐DTPA and PG‐Gd may also be used to more accurately assess changes in tumor blood volume and permeability‐surface area product. We will pursue this line of research in future studies. Nevertheless, our current study indicates that tumor stroma remodeling after combination therapy with IRE and anti‐PD‐1 could be captured noninvasively by PG‐Gd contrast enhanced T1‐weighted MRI early after initiation of treatment.

Because IRE induces cell death through membrane damage that removes barriers to water mobility, it is anticipated that ADC would increase after IRE. Indeed, ADC was significantly increased at days 7, 11, and 14 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy compared to pre‐treatment values. Moreover, we observed greater variation in ADC values on days 11 and 14 after IRE than in ADC before IRE and on day 7 after IRE. These findings may reflect differential tissue remodeling at later times after combined IRE and immunotherapy. Remodeling processes that could contribute to variation in tumor tissue cellularity and ADC values include tumor infiltration by different types of host immune cells, angiogenesis, and tumor cell proliferation (if the tumor recurs).

Interestingly, while there was no difference between partial responders and complete responders in ADC values on day 7 after IRE and anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy, pre‐IRE tumor ADC values were significantly lower in the partial responders than complete responders. On ROC analysis, AUC value was 0.88, indicating a high predictive value with pretreatment ADC measurements. In a retrospective study, Garces‐Descovich et al. found that a lower ADC in treatment‐naïve PDAC patients predicted metastatic disease and poorer 4‐year overall survival. Our preclinical data agrees with this clinical finding. These studies suggest that pretreatment tumor cellularity may be a useful predictive imaging biomarker regardless of choice of therapies. To our knowledge, the use of DWI in predicting responses to combined IRE and immunotherapy has not been reported before.

Multiple factors may contribute to lower ADC values. In PDAC patients, a lower ADC has been correlated to higher collagenous fiber density. A more fibrotic stroma may reduce initial infiltration of immune cells into tumors, reducing the effectiveness of immunotherapy. In addition, a lower ADC is positively corelated with tumor hypoxia. A high hypoxic fraction is a risk factor for failure of anti‐PD‐1 immunotherapy. Finally, lower ADC values may indicate higher tumor cellularity, potentially suggesting that these tumors were intrinsically more aggressive or less responsive to therapy, independent of treatment modality. Further studies are needed to correlate baseline DWI ADC values with tumor pathology and biology, which require integrated analyses of multiscale imaging data encompassing tumor radiomics (DWI and MRI) and multiplexing immunofluorescence imaging on heterogeneity of baseline tumors. Other unbiased approaches such as RNAseq, spatial proteomics, and CyTOF will also be employed to characterize populations of different cell types in the tumor microenvironment at the baseline.

In a clinical scenario, if multiparametric MRI predicts a favorable response to the combined therapy, the patient could potentially avoid additional, more toxic treatments. Conversely, if multiparametric MRI predicts a poor response early, alternative strategies—such as adding chemotherapy or other forms of immunotherapy—could be considered to improve outcomes.

Our study has several limitations. First, we conducted DWI studies only after day 7 to allow recovery from IRE procedures. DWI studies at early times could assess immediate response, as shown in a porcine liver model. Second, we did not assess the size of the ablation zone, which may affect the extent of residual tumor cells after IRE and the efficacy of combined IRE and immunotherapy. Third, the analysis based on tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity measured on day 11 after institution of IRE was arbitrary and that there may exist a time point at which tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity better correlates with the survival data, especially for mice that had survived for more than 80 days. Combining multiple imaging biomarkers, including tumor‐to‐muscle signal intensity on T1‐weighted MRI and DWI data, may increase the predictive power. Increasing number of animals will also increase the power of our analysis, however, we do acknowledge the constraint on costs when number of animals are to be increased.

One limitation of the current study is that we did not directly show increased macrophage infiltration in the complete responders compared to the partial responders. In Figure 3, all mice used in the pathological examinations were euthanized on day 14 after combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 treatments. An inherent limitation of such approach is that it is not possible to obtain long‐term survival data to correlate with pathological findings. Therefore, it is critical to develop clinically viable, noninvasive imaging tools to monitor disease progression, assess treatment effect on TME, and thus advance personalized immunotherapy. Another limitation is that CD31 is not specific for angiogenetic blood vessels, as it is a general marker for endothelial cells. In future studies, additional biomarkers, such as angiopoietin‐2 (ANG2), vascular endothelial growth factors (VEGF), and integrins etc. maybe used to more specifically depict angiogenic blood vessels.

In conclusion, our data suggests that both PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI and DWI could be used for early assessment of PDACs response to combined IRE and immunotherapy. Moreover, our data points to potential application of macromolecular contrast agent enhanced MRI as well as DWI in characterizing TME in the context of immunotherapy in PDAC. Currently, various immunotherapeutic approaches have been investigated for treating PDAC, mostly in preclinical settings, and these treatments have yet to be adapted into the clinic. One challenge is selecting patients who are likely to respond. It is known that response to immunotherapy is governed by multiple factors including tumor intrinsic factors and TME. Therefore, there is an unmet need to better characterize PDAC TME using noninvasive techniques. Here, we showed that (1) PG‐Gd contrast enhanced T1‐weighted MRI acquired as early as 11 days after combined IRE and anti‐PD‐1 could be used to separate complete responders from partial responders, and that (2) pretreatment ADC value derived from DWI could separate complete responders from partial responders in mice bearing orthotopic PDAC tumors. Because PG‐Gd is not approved for clinical use, PG‐Gd contrast‐enhanced T1‐weighted MRI may be used as a research tool for studying impact of immunotherapy on PDAC TME. However, DWI is already used in the clinic and therefore has immediate translational potential.
