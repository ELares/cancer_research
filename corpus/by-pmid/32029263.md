BITES and CARS and checkpoints, oh my! Updates regarding immunotherapy for myeloid malignancies from the 2018 annual ASH meeting

It is without question that immune checkpoint inhibitors and adoptive cellular therapies have revolutionized the treatment of solid and hematologic malignancies. Investigators are now developing novel strategies to integrate these groundbreaking modalities into the care of patients with acute myeloid leukemia (AML) and other myeloid malignancies. Here we provide an overview of the most recent developments in immunotherapy for myeloid cancers presented at the 2018 American Society of Hematology annual meeting. Topics discussed include adoptive cellular therapies (CAR-T, NK cell, and vaccines), checkpoint inhibitors, and bispecific T-cell engager (BITE) antibodies. Despite reservations regarding low antigenicity and having long been considered a “cold” tumor, immunotherapy remains a highly promising strategy for patients with aggressive myeloid cancers like myelodysplasia (MDS) and AML.

Introduction

Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is a devastating hematopoietic stem cell malignancy anticipated to impact 19,520 Americans in 2018, with only 27.4% of patients estimated to survive beyond five years. Traditional induction chemotherapy (the 7+3 regimen combining infusional cytarabine at a dose of 100–200mg/m2 for 7 days with an anthracycline for 3 days) remains the standard of care induction strategy for younger patients, but is associated with high rates of mortality and relapse. For older patients, newer chemotherapeutic strategies combining hypomethylating drugs (HMAs) or low dose cytarabine (LDAC) with BCL2 or Hedgehog inhibitors appear to offer a promising alternative to induction but remain unproven and the risk for longer-term relapse is unknown. More than 8 new drugs/drug combinations for patients with newly diagnosed and relapsed or refractory AML were approved by the FDA in the last 2 years, but most of these agents (particularly those targeting FLT3 and IDH mutations or antibody drug conjugates for example) produce only transient clinical responses and are applicable to a minority of patients. More primitive immunotherapy, in the form of allogeneic transplantation, remains the mainstay of consolidation therapy for eligible patients with higher risk disease.

Across the spectrum of cancer diagnoses, targeted immunotherapy has gained momentum as an attractive and potentially powerful therapeutic approach; two of the field’s pioneers, Dr. James Allison and Dr. Tasuku Honjo received the Nobel Prize for this innovative work,. Targeted immunotherapy is now under investigation as a therapeutic strategy for AML and other myeloid cancers, and many patients ask their physicians about what modalities might one day be available. In this review we will provide a survey for the busy clinician that reviews the current state of the art in immunotherapy for myeloid cancers as presented at this year’s annual American Society of Hematology Meeting. Therapeutic modalities discussed include adoptive cellular therapy, vaccine therapy, checkpoint inhibitors, and bispecific T-cell engagers (BITEs).

Adoptive Cellular Therapy

CAR-T

CAR-T cell based therapy is a living drug comprised of autologous T-cells genetically modified to express a chimeric antigen receptor or CAR construct targeting a cancer associated antigen target. Chimeric antigen receptors recognize antigens directly in the same way that antibodies do, any antigen expressed on the surface of a cell can therefore be targeted using this approach, as CD19, CD20 and CD22 are routinely targeted in lymphoid malignancies and CD33 is targeted by the chemo-immunotherapy gemtuzumab ozogamicin. The CAR- construct, instructions for which are introduced into normal T-cells using virus-based technology, combines a recognition domain, usually a single variable chain of an antibody (known as an scFv) with two or more signal domains which can directly activate T-cell killing upon antigen binding (Figure 1). CAR-T cells differ from normal T-cells because they do not require antigen presentation in the context of self-antigens (like major histocompatibility proteins or MHCs) and they kill immediately upon engagement of the antigen target, but these cells retain their normal programs and can therefore still express and recognize inhibitory checkpoint signals such as those provided through interactions with CTLA4 and PD1/PDL1. This modality has been assessed both pre-clinically and in early phase clinical trials against a variety of antigens present on myeloid cells, a summary of these studies is provided in Table 1.

Pre-clinical CAR based cellular therapy

Five abstracts presented at this year’s meeting provide compelling evidence for pre-clinical efficacy of myeloid cellar therapeutics.

Lichtman et al assessed the potential of the immune checkpoint B7-H3 (B7-homolog 3, or CD276), an antigen with increased expression on cancers, including a subset of AML with monocytic phenotype. This antigen is also present on myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) within the tumor microenvironment. MDSCs are a population of myeloid cells which are thought to be immunosuppressive in both hematological and solid tumors and facilitate immune escape by cancer cells. These authors demonstrated the presence of B7-H3 on the surface of primary AML blasts (N=10), as well as on the AML cell lines THP-1, U937, OCI-AML2, OCI-AML3. Lichtman and colleagues generated B7-H3 CARs via retroviral transduction of CD3/CD28 -activated T-cells, followed by in vitro expansion with cytokines [interleukin- (IL) 7 and IL-15]. Co-culture of these B7-H3 CARs (n=3–5 donors) with cell lines and primary patients samples (n=10), resulted in proliferation of the CAR and effective leukemia killing (n=4 primary AML blasts). These CARs reduced disease burden and improved overall survival in disseminated AML xenograft mouse models. Lastly, they demonstrated that the B7-H3 CAR did not kill normal CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells (from umbilical cord-blood) in vitro or in a humanized mouse model (using fetal liver-derived hematopoietic stem cells).

Myburgh et al, designed CAR-T cells to target the stem cell antigen c-Kit (CD117), an antigen that is expressed on > 90% of samples from patients with AML, but is also present on normal hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPC). Because such an approach will result in bone marrow aplasia, this CAR-T therapy was designed to be followed by CAR-T depletion and replacement of hematopoiesis by allogenic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation. The authors generated a lentiviral vector which incorporates the CAR (single-chain variable fragment (scFv) linked to intracellular CD3ζ and 4–1BB signaling domains via stalk and transmembrane regions derived from CD8), followed by a T2A ribosomal skip sequence and RQR8 serving as a selection marker and depletion target. RQR8 transduced cells express CD34 and CD20 epitopes and can be selected using magnetic beads (CliniMACS systems) to isolate CD34+ cells and depleted using anti-CD20 antibodies (e.g., rituximab). A previously published anti-CD117 antibody was used as the scFv. T-cells were isolated from healthy donors or AML patients in complete remission (CR), and were transduced with the vector. More than 90% of CD117high leukemia cell lines were eradicated in co-culture assays with these CAR-T cells. CAR-mediated cytotoxicity was also observed with > 70% of tested patient derived leukemic blasts in vitro. No outgrowth of leukemic blasts was noted following 3 weeks of long-term co-culture with the CD117 CAR-T. Confirming their proof of concept, humanized mice (NSG and MTRG-SKI) stably engrafted with either CD117 positive patient-derived AML blasts or umbilical cord derived CD34+ cells (used as a model of normal hematopoiesis) were depleted by > 90% following a single injection of anti-CD117 CAR-T cells.

One particular problem for AML immunotherapy is the lack of suitable tumor specific target antigens. As highlighted by the prior CAR targeting CD117, many attractive and potentially targetable antigens are shared by normal and malignant myeloid cells. As a result there is substantial concern that CAR-T therapies will deplete healthy hematopoietic tissue. To solve this problem, two groups have developed strategies using adaptor molecules. Dr. Christian Seitz and colleagues developed the concept of adapter-CAR-T cells (aCAR-T) and Dr. Loff and colleagues described “switchable” T-cells. Both approaches use CAR-T cells that recognize a target antigen and become activated only in the presence of an adapter that binds leukemia-specific antigens to the CAR. Dr. Seitz’s group proposes that aCAR-Ts can more readily distinguish AML from normal cells by their differential degree and combination of antigen expression (producing an “AND”-gate). The investigators generated adapter molecules against a variety of AML associated antigens -(CD32, CD33, CD38, CD123, CD135, CD305, CLL1) which can be given in combination to provide leukemia cell specificity. Administration of 2, 3, or 5 adapters which bind multiple leukemic antigens on the same target cell can enhance recognition by the adapter-recognizing CAR. Dr. Loff’s group presented similar data using what they describe as a universal CAR-T platform (UniCAR).

Cellular engineering also has the potential to enhance leukemia-specificity and safety while limiting the need for individualization. Dr. Wang and colleagues presented data on the use of transduced Natural Killer (NK) cells. NK cells are attractive as cellular therapy as they do not require HLA compatibility. NK based approaches might therefor provide the potential for off-the-shelf products without the need for individual patient-derivation, but these have suffered due to difficulty with expansion in vitro and in vivo. Dr. Wang’s group used retroviral transduction to introduce 1) a drug-inducible expansion signal (MyD88/CD40 upon exposure to rimucid) 2) autologous cytokine expression (Il-15), 3) a rapamycin-inducible Caspase-9 (kill switch) and 4) a CAR construct targeting leukemia specific antigens (CD19 and CD123). These engineered NK cells expanded and persisted in leukemia-bearing humanized mice and prevented relapse over more than 40 days of observation, suggesting good disease control. The inclusion of a drug-inducible kill switch provides the option for cellular depletion in the context of excess toxicity and has also been used in CAR-T cell approaches. The authors suggest that their NK cell platform might address many of the challenges of cell-based therapy and might also be applicable for a variety of cancers.

Pre-clinical CAR-T Combinatorial Approaches

This year two abstracts were presented evaluating the addition of a second agent to CAR-T therapy. Jetani et al explored combining FLT3 inhibitors with FLT3-targeting CAR-T cells. This work stems from a body of pre-clinical and clinical evidence which shows that FLT3-ITD+ AML cells react to FLT3 inhibitors by increasing the expression of FLT3 on the cell surface in an effort to sustain the survival signal provided by FLT3 mutations. FLT3 targeted-CAR construct (BV10 scFv binding domain, CD28-CD3ζ signal molecule and EGFRt marker encoded via a lentiviral vector) was introduced into CD8+ and CD4+ T cells from healthy donors and patients (n=6). Treatment of FLT3 positive AML blasts with three clinically relevant FLT3 inhibitors increased surface expression of the FLT3 receptor (quizartinib > crenolanib > midostaurin). The increased density of FLT3 receptors on the cell surface produced by this exposure resulted in improved recognition of AML cell lines and primary patient samples by FLT3 CAR-Ts both in vitro (in mixing assays) and mice bearing AML xenografts. In the mixing assays, CD8+ and CD4+ FLT3-CAR-Ts proliferated, produced cytokines and killed target cells better when combined with crenolanib or quizartinib compared with midostaurin (p<0.05). These findings were confirmed in leukemia-bearing immunocompromised mice where a synergistic response was observed between FLT3 CAR-T (5 × 106 CAR-T) and each of the FLT3 inhibitors. The combination of crenolanib and FLT3 CAR-T seemed to be the most potent.

Dr. Suh and colleague set out to boost the efficacy of a CD33 CAR-T by adding bioengineered autologous dendritic cells (DCs). The authors hypothesized that DC-derived cytokine stimulation of CAR-T-cells might enhance their capacity for cancer killing, and improve their survival and durability. DCs are professional antigen presenting cells. Their primary function is to process and present antigens on their surface in the context of self-major histocompatibility receptors (MHCs). Through this presentation, they educate and prime T-cells and other immune populations for activation against a variety of antigens (Figure 2). These cells secrete cytokines, process and present antigens that are released in the local environment and direct immune responses against pathogens and cancer (Figure 2). Suh et al therefore hypothesized that autologous DCs transduced with 4–1BB CAR would improve the efficacy of an anti-CD33 CAR-T against acute myelogenous leukemia (AML) by producing DC-related cytokines and recruiting CART cells to the bone marrow microenvironment. The anti-CD33 CAR-T was created through transduction of sorted bone marrow T-cells and CD-34+ progenitors with an anti-CD33 41BBz CAR lentivector (pCCL-HP67.6–4-1BB-CD3z). CD34+ transduced bone marrow progenitors were then differentiated into DCs (CAR-DC) in vitro. The combination of this CAR-T/CAR-DC produced greater cell killing of a leukemia cell line (Kasumi-1) compared to the CAR-T cells alone in co-culture experiments. Moreover, co-cultures produced two-fold higher TNF-alpha and IFN-gamma levels (cytokines known to be associated with cytotoxicity) compared with CAR-T cells alone (p<0.01). Immunocompromised mouse xenografts bearing fluorescent labeled Kasumi-1 cells (1 × 107) experienced increased survival (p<0.01) and decreased disease burden following combination treatment with CAR-T (5 × 105) +/− CAR-DC (1.5 × 105) compared with CAR-T alone.

These strategies highlight the exciting pre-clinical developments in myeloid-targeted cellular therapies aimed at enhanced specificity with reduced off target toxicity.

Clinical CAR-T

Two abstracts presented this year described the safety and clinical activity of early cellular therapy for hematological cancers.

The first is CYAD-01, a CAR-T product based on the receptor NKG2D with specificity for a broad range of ligands (MICA, MICB and ULBP1–6 expressed on both hematological and solid tumors. Dr. Sallman and colleagues presented data from the hematological arm of the THINK trial with this product (). As of July 31, 2018, 12 patients in the hematological cohort (8 AML, 3 multiple myeloma, 1 myelodysplastic syndrome) were enrolled at the 3 dose levels (3 × 108, 1 × 109, 3 × 109 cells per injection) without prior preconditioning. Median age was 64 (range 29–83), and the median number of prior therapies was 3. Treatment-related adverse events (AEs) of grade 3 or 4 included lymphopenias (3 patients in dose levels 1 and 2), cytopenias (1 patient, dose level 2), pneumonitis (1 patient, dose level 1) and cytokine release syndrome (CRS; 2 patients in dose level 2). Other treatment-related AEs seen in at least 1 patient included pyrexia, CRS, hypoxia, lymphopenia and nausea. CRS was seen in a total of 5 patients (3 grade 1/2 and 2 grade 3) with rapid resolution following tociluzumab or steroids. The investigators have not observed any neurotoxicity to date. Of the 8 relapsed/refractory AML patients enrolled, 7 were evaluable for response assessment (2 at DL-1, 3 at DL-2 and 2 at DL-3). The overall response rate in relapsed/refractory AML patients was 42% (3/7 patients) with one complete remission with partial hematologic recovery (CRh) in DL-1, and 2 complete responses with incomplete marrow recovery (CRi; 1 in DL-1 and 1 in DL-3). All responding patients achieved a response by day 29 (after 2 CYAD-01 administrations). The AML patient with CRh in DL-1 was bridged to allogenic stem cell transplantation on day +97 following CYAD-01 and has been in complete molecular remission for more than one year (ongoing). The two other responding patients experienced CRi for one month. Two additional AML patients at DL-2 obtained clinical benefit/disease stabilization with hematologic improvement and a decrease in bone marrow blasts. This study is currently recruiting patients at centers in the United States and Europe.

Dr. Liu presented an update on behalf of her coinvestigators from a phase 1 first-in-human CLL1-CD33 compound CAR-T (cCAR) study open in China (). Results from treatment of two patients were presented. The first is a 6 year old female with complex karyotype AML including a FLT3-ITD mutation. She was originally diagnosed with Fanconi anemia, progressed to juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia and subsequently transformed to AML. The patient had previously failed multiple lines of therapy, including 5 cycles of chemotherapy in combination with a FLT3 inhibitor. Following cCAR therapy (fludarabine and cyclophosphamide lymphodepletion followed by 1 × 106/kg CAR-T cells infused on days 1 &2) she achieved a complete remission which was deemed negative for evidence of minimal residual disease (MRD). This product produces bone marrow aplasia and requires salvage with planned transplant. The patient received a non-myeloablative conditioning regimen followed by transplant from a haplo-identical donor and remains in CR. The second treated patient is a 23-year-old with a history of CML, initially presenting in accelerated phase, who failed several lines of tyrosine kinase inhibitors after development of a T315I mutation. This patient also achieved an MRD negative complete response and underwent haplo-identical transplant. Toxicities reported with this product include grade 1 CRS and pancytopenia and grade 3 neurotoxicity (patient 1). This study remains open to enrollment at the General Hospital of Western Theater Command in Chengdu, China with a planned second site at Peking University Shenzhen Hospital in Shenzhen.

Although these therapies have only been given to a limited number of patients and are associated with a substantial rate of toxicity, they demonstrate the potential for both feasibility and activity of engineered T-cell approaches in myeloid cancer.

Vaccine Therapy

Therapeutic cancer vaccines are designed to stimulate endogenous tumor-specific immunity. Such approaches are designed to induce durable, systemic immunological memory and protect against recurrent disease. Fundamentally these strategies use patient-derived DCs (manipulated in vitro and reinjected, or educated through protein-based vaccination) to process and present selected cancer-associated antigens to endogenous T-cells. Such T-cells can are then educated to kill cancer cells (Figure 2). Here we review both the preclinical and clinical data presented at this year’s meeting in the context of AML (Table 2).

Preclinical Vaccine Therapy

Liegel and colleagues developed a DC/AML fusion vaccine strategy which they tested in immunocompromised mice bearing AML cells from patients in remission. DC/AML fusions were generated in vitro from retrospectively available primary AML samples and DCs were harvested from the paired patient at the time of remission. Fresh T-cells from these patients were then cultured with or without the fusion vaccine at 1:10 ratio. On day 14 following tumor inoculation, animals were injected with 1.5 × 106 T-cells that had been cultured with or without the fusion vaccine. Mice receiving ex-vivo vaccine-stimulated T-cells had significantly reduced AML engraftment compared to those mice receiving unstimulated T-cells (n=4). Mice treated with stimulated T-cells also showed marrow infiltration with human CD8+T cells while those receiving unstimulated T-cells did not.

Dr. Stroopinsky and colleagues presented their work combining novel checkpoint inhibiting monoclonal antibodies (mAb) with a DC/AML fusion vaccine. In this study immunocompetent mice were injected with a mouse AML cell line and then treated 24h later with either mouse DC/AML fusion vaccine, an immune checkpoint inhibitor cocktail (CPI; anti-PD-1/anti-TIM3/anti-RGMb mAbs) or the combination. Animals treated with the DC/AML fusion vaccine alone or in combination with the CPI had decreased disease burden compared to untreated control animals or those treated with CPI alone. Tumor-specific T-cell responses were higher in the animals receiving vaccination in combination with the CPI compared to those who got placebo or either strategy alone. Animals who got combination therapy showed an increased population of memory T-cells and a decrease in naïve and regulatory T-cell populations compared to animals receiving control or single agent therapy. These data suggest that combination strategies using a vaccine approach with a checkpoint inhibitor might be valuable in myeloid cancer.

Clinical Vaccine Therapy

Results from the phase II trial of the peptide vaccine, OCV-501 in elderly patients with AML were presented at this meeting. This vaccine is a human leukocyte antigen (HLA) class-II restricted peptide derived from the Wilms’ tumor-1 (WT1) protein. WT1 is overexpressed in a majority of AML patient samples. It is expressed at low levels in normal tissues including the gonads, kidney and hematopoietic system. Dr. Yamaguchi and colleagues performed a multicenter, randomized, double blind, placebo-controlled trial in Japan, the Republic of Korea and Taiwan. Patients were ≥ 60 years of age, had achieved first CR within one or two courses of standard induction, had completed at least one course of standard consolidation, and were not planned to undergo hematopoietic stem cell transplantation. Vaccination or placebo was given subcutaneously once weekly for up to eight weeks and then biweekly for up to 2 years, after which patients were observed. A total of 133 patients were randomized to receive vaccine (N=68) or placebo (N=65). The median age was 67 years (range, 60–85), and most patients had ECOG performance status 0–1. The WT1 mRNA level at Day 1 was < 50 copies/μg RNA in 37 patients in the vaccine arm and 28 patients in the placebo arm. The primary endpoint for this study was disease-free survival (DFS). The study did not meet its primary endpoint; no difference in DFS was seen in patients treated with vaccine (12.1 months) or placebo (8.4 months). The secondary endpoint of overall survival was also not different for patients treated with vaccine (38.5 months) or placebo (31 months). The vaccine was safe, with a majority of treatment-emergent AE (TEAE) of mild severity (Grade 1 or 2). The most common TEAE was injection site induration.

Lastly, Dr. Iris Bigalke presented data from an immune monitoring study of five patients treated with an autologous DC vaccine under a compassionate use program (). The DC vaccine was given weekly for 4 weeks (immunization phase), again at week six and monthly thereafter. The DC vaccine secretes bioactive IL-12, which should activate adaptive and innate immune responses. These can be demonstrated via a dual-color ELISpot assay. T-cell responses to this vaccination are directed at two target antigens expressed in AML: preferentially expressed in melanoma (PRAME) and WT1. T-cell responses were measured using established techniques and responses to one or both targets were found in 4/5 vaccinated patients. Ongoing vaccination permitted maintenance of the T-cell response over an extended period of time in 4/5 patients. To date, 3/5 patients remain alive without any evidence of relapse. Two patients who got DC vaccination for 2 and 30 months remain alive and in remission for 53 and 41 months respectively. A third patient relapsed at 10 months, and was salvaged with allogenic stem cell transplantation and remains in CR after 35 months. The two remaining patients have died: One patient without any T-cell response relapsed following 12 months of vaccination and died at month 20 during chemotherapy. The fifth patient received vaccination therapy for 24 months without signs of relapse but succumbed to heart disease.

Bi- and Trispecific Engagers

Bispecific T-cell engagers (bispecific antibodies) are small constructs that combine two scFvs into a single very small molecule. Each end binds a different antigen target, on one side an antigen expressed on effector cells, (most target CD3 which is expressed on almost all T-cells) and on the other end an antibody fragment that recognizes a tumor antigen (Figure 3). Because of their very small size, these agents bring T-cells into close proximity with cancer cells expressing the antigen of interest. Through close proximity they direct activation (release of perforin and granzymes) of the engaged T-cells against their antigen-bearing cancer target without requiring MHC or co-stimulatory molecules. A majority of the clinically relevant compounds in this class to date have very short half-lives which require continuous intravenous infusion. TriKEs, or tri-specific killer engagers, have also been designed with a similar mechanism in mind. TriKEs have three domains rather than two, one binds a molecule specific to the effector cell (CD3 on T-cells or CD16 on NK cells), a second provides an activation signal and the third binds to a cancer – associated antigen (Figure 3). Here we will review the data regarding bispecific antibodies (directed against a single antigen) and the TriKE in development presented at the ASH meeting (Table 3).

CD123 × CD3 Bispecific T-cell Engagers

Dr. Farhad Ravandi presented a phase I study of XmAb14045, a CD123 × CD3 bispecific antibody on behalf of his co-investigators (). Patients with relapsed or refractory AML, B-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia, blast phase chronic myelogenous leukemia, or blastic plasmacytoid dendritic cell neoplasm were eligible to enroll on this first-in-human, multicenter, open-label phase I dose-escalation study. The primary objectives were identification of the maximum tolerated dose (MTD) or recommended phase II dose and schedule. Treatment was administered weekly in 28 day cycles, utilizing a weight-based dose with a single dose level in Part A and, in Part B, an initial priming dose on Day 1 followed by an escalated dose on subsequent weeks. Patients received premedication for CRS with steroids, acetaminophen, and diphenhydramine. Premedication was given for all XmAb14045 doses ≥ 0.075 μg/kg. Patients could continue to receive tolerable therapy provided there was evidence of clinical benefit. At data cut-off, 64 patients have been treated: 63 patients with relapsed/refractory AML and 1 patient with B-ALL. Median age was 61 years and patients were heavily pretreated (median of 3 prior therapies [range 1–8], including 19 [30%] with prior allogenic stem cell transplantation). The recommended dose for Part A was 1.3 μg/kg after a single dose-limiting toxicity of Grade 4 CRS at 2.3 μg/kg, no MTD has been identified. The most common TEAE was CRS or its symptoms. CRS symptoms began approximately 1–4 hours from the start of drug infusion and occurred in 49 of 64 patients (77%). Seven patients (11%) developed Grade ≥ 3 CRS, most frequently following the first dose. Importantly, there were no CRS-related deaths. Additional TEAEs occurring in > 10% of patients included fatigue (31%), febrile neutropenia (30%), peripheral edema (30%), cough (23%), elevated transaminases (19%), pneumonia (17%), stomatitis (14%), hyperglycemia (13%), and sepsis (11%). No evidence of tumor lysis syndrome or myelosuppression requiring dose modification was observed. In Part A, single agent antileukemic activity was documented with a best response of CR or CRi in 3/13 AML patients (CR/CRi rate 23%) treated at the two highest dose levels (1.3 and 2.3 μg/kg weekly). No activity was observed at lower doses. All responders achieved at least a morphologic leukemia free-state (MLFS) after one cycle. Two responders were bridged to allogenic stem cell transplantation, and the third responder was ineligible for medical reasons but remains in remission at 14+ weeks following the initiation of therapy. This study is ongoing at centers across the United States.

A second bispecific CD123 × CD3 antibody, Flotetuzumab (FLZ), tested in patients with relapsed/refractory AML was presented by Dr. John DiPersio (). The recommended Phase 2 dose of FLZ has been identified as 500 ng/kg/day administered as a 7-day/week continuous infusion. During cycle 1, patients received ramp up dosing with 30 ng/kg × 3 days, then 100 ng/kg × 4 days, the dose was then increased to 500ng/kg/day for weeks 2–4. In cycles 2 and beyond, patients received therapy on a schedule of 4 days on/ 3 days off. Thirty patients with relapsed/refractory AML, of median age of 64.5 years, received FLZ at the recommended phase 2 dose. The majority of patients enrolled had primary refractory disease (18/30 [60%; 14 patients were refractory to cytotoxic chemotherapy (≥ 2 inductions, or first CR with initial CR < 6 months), and 4 to hypomethylating agents]). Twenty five/30 patients (83.3%) had high risk disease by ELN criteria. At baseline, the median percentage of CD123+ bone marrow blasts was 85% (range 1.4 – 100%), and the median CD123 receptor density on BM blasts was 4,084 (range 357–28,818). CRS and infusion related reactions (IRR) were the most common AE, including Grade ≥ 3 events in 4/30 (13.3%) patients. These were managed with conventional care. Antileukemic activity was seen in 18/27 (67%) response-evaluable patients, with an overall response rate (ORR) of 22% (6/27), and a CR/CRi rate of 19% (5/27). Thirty-one percent (4/13) of the patients with chemotherapy refractory AML achieved CR/CRi with FLZ, while no patients with relapsed AML responded (0/11). The authors propose that differences are due to an immune-enriched tumor microenvironment in responding patients, although additional correlative studies are needed to confirm this hypothesis. This study remains open at centers across the United States and Europe.

CD33 × CD3 bispecific antibodies

CD33 is another attractive target for bispecific antibody development, as it is expressed on more than 99% of AML cases. Dr. Ravandi presented a phase 1 first-in-human study of AMG 330, an anti-CD33 bispecific antibody in relapsed/refractory AML (). AMG 330 was administered as a continuous IV infusion, with single-patient cohorts for the first 3 doses to allow rapid dose escalation followed by cohorts of 3–6 patients. Patients who completed the first cycle without dose-limiting toxicity (DLT), could receive up to 5 additional cycles. CRS was noted in patients treated with doses above 30 μg/day and mitigation strategies, including step-dosing and pretreatment with corticosteroids was begun. The modified regimen started with a run-in at a dose of 10 μg/day for 4 days, followed by escalation to the target dose. Due to persistent toxicity they switched to a 2-step ramp-up, beginning at 10 μg/day, escalating to 60 μg/day, and then escalating to the target dose for 14 or 28 days, with a 1–4 week break. As of the data cut-off the study is ongoing. Thirty five patients have been enrolled in 12 dose cohorts with target dose range between 0.5 – 480 μg/day. Fifty seven percent of enrolled patients were male (20/35), the median age was 58 (range: 18–80 years) and 40% (14/35) had previously received stem cell transplant. The median number of prior treatments was 4 (range 1–15), median duration of AML diagnosis was 1.3 years (range 0.3–9.6), and baseline blast percentage was 37% (range 3%−95%). Patients received a median of 1 cycle (range 1–6) of therapy; 31/35 (89%) of patients discontinued treatment due to progressive disease (n=24), adverse events (n=5; 2 treatment-related), or patient request (n=2). Serious AEs (SAEs) were identified in 23/35 (66%) of patients (treatment-related in 15 patients). SAEs included CRS (n=11), febrile neutropenia (n=6), pneumonia (n=4), leukopenia (n=3), thrombocytopenia (n=2) and subdural hematoma (n=2). There were no treatment-related deaths; one patient died due to AML progression. Severe CRS was observed in one patient each in the 10 μg/day and 30 μg/day cohorts (no lead-in), which resolved with standard therapy and interruption of AMG 330. DLTs included Grade 2 CRS and Grade 4 ventricular fibrillation at the 480 μg/day dose; the target dose was then decreased to 240 μg/day. Two patients achieved CR at the 240 μg/day dose (lead-in of 10 μg/day – 60 μg/day. One patient dosed at 120 μg/day and one at 240 μg/day had a CRi and one patient dosed at 1.5 μg/day had a MLFS. This study remains open at several centers in the US and in Europe.

A second CD33 × CD3 bispecific antibody, AMV564, showed preclinical activity in a mouse model and has undergone human testing. Dr. Westervelt and colleagues presented a phase I, first-in-human, 3+3 dose escalation trial of AMV564 for patients with relapsed/refractory AML ().. Eligible patients had relapsed/refractory AML, but could not have received more than two lines of salvage therapy. AMV564 is administered as continuous intravenous infusion (CIV) for 14 days for up to two cycles. At data cut-off, 19 patients (10 male/9 female) of median age 72 years (range 24–84) have been enrolled in six cohorts: 0.5, 1.5, 5.0, 15, 50 and 100 mcg/day. Secondary AML and/or adverse cytogenetics were present in 13 patients (68%), including 6 with p53 mutations (32%). Fifteen patients (79%) had received at least one prior salvage regimen and 11 (58%) had received prior intensive chemotherapy. At the time of assessment, 18 patients were evaluable for toxicity and response. No DLTs or treatment-related Grade ≥ 3 AE were reported. Grade 2 CRS was observed in one patient at the 50 mcg/day dose level. This was managed successfully with drug interruption and one dose of tocilizumab. Subsequent patients treated at or above 50mcg/day were given a lead in with 15 mcg/day for 3 days followed by 11 days at the assigned dose level. The most common TEAEs of Grade ≥ 3 were febrile neutropenia (7/18, 39%), and were deemed unrelated to study therapy. Treatment was associated with an increase in blood levels of the activating cytokines IL-6, IL-8, and IL-10, and activated T-cells. Reductions in bone marrow blasts, ranging from 13%−91% were observed in 12/18 evaluable patients. This study is ongoing at centers across the United States and a second study focused on patients with MDS () is also recruiting.

CD16-IL15-CLEC12A Trispecific Killer Engager (TriKE)

Other investigators are trying to induce leukemia killing using natural killer cells (NK cells) as a drug target. Arvindam et al developed the CD16-IL15-CLEC12A (CLL-1) TriKE and presented some compelling preliminary data at this year’s meeting. This compound consists of an anti-CD16 chain to activate NK cells, an IL-15 molecule to drive NK cell priming, and an scFv targeting CLEC12A, which is highly expressed on AML cells and thought to be selectively overexpressed in leukemic stem cells. The CLEC12A TriKE was developed in a mammalian system to ensure that appropriate post-translational modifications are present. The TriKE binds specifically to AML cell lines expressing CLEC12A (HL-60 and THP-1). The investigators observed that treatment of peripheral blood mononuclear cells with the CLEC12A TriKE drives an increase in NK cell proliferation over 7 days, as compared to CLEC12A scFv or IL-15 alone. The TriKE was found to induce more target killing (HL-60) as opposed to the CLEC12A scFv or IL-15 alone. In addition, the TriKE was found to result in increased NK cell degranulation as measured by CD107a expression following co-cultures with the leukemia cell lines HL-60 and THP-1. Activation of NK cells was confirmed upon co-culture with primary AML cells. Of the 9 AML samples assessed, 7 expressed high levels of CLEC12A. In co-culture experiments combining NK cells and primary AML samples, the addition of the TriKE resulted in greater expression of CD107a and IFN-γ (correlates of NK cell cytotoxicity), and enhanced killing of target AML cells as compared to the CLEC12A antibody or the IL15 cytokine given alone.

Checkpoint Inhibitors

The immune system has the framework to eradicate cancer, however it is held in check through inhibitory receptors and ligands. These checkpoints are critical for self-tolerance during an anti-microbial immune response, but are exploited by cancer to evade detection. Immune checkpoint inhibitors have been tested as therapy for AML, and here we will discuss the state of the field as presented at the annual ASH meeting (Table 4).

Checkpoint Inhibition Combined with Intensive Chemotherapy

Dr. Assi and colleagues shared their phase 2 data investigating the addition of nivolumab to cytarabine and idarubicin in patients with newly diagnosed AML. Patients aged 18–65 years old diagnosed with AML (≥ 20% blasts) or high risk myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) (≥ 10% blasts) were eligible for study if they were found to have adequate performance status (ECOG 2) and organ function. Patients received 1–2 cycles of cytarabine 1.5 g/m2 over 24 hours (days 1–4) and idarubicin 12 mg/m2 (days 1–3) induction followed by nivolumab at a dose of 3 mg/kg on day 24 ± 2 continued every 2 weeks for up to a year. For patients who achieved CR or CRi with induction, up to 5 consolidation cycles of attenuated dose cytarabine + idarubicin was given. Eligible patients could proceed to allogenic stem cell transplant at any time during induction or consolidation. A run-in phase with nivolumab 1 mg/kg was carried out in three patients with AML with no drug-related toxicity observed. This led to the treatment of an additional 41 patients. The median age of treated patients was 54 years (range, 26–66); 42 had AML (32 de novo, 7 secondary AML, 3 therapy-related AML), and two had high risk MDS. Nineteen patients (44%) had adverse cytogenetic risk by ELN criteria, 8 (18%) had TP53 mutations. All patients were evaluable for response. A total of 34 patients (77%) achieved a CR/CRi (n=28 CR, n=6 CRi), and 18/34 were MRD negative by flow cytometry following induction. The median number of cycles to response was 1 (range, 1–2), and median number of total cycles received was 2 (range, 2–6). The median number of nivolumab doses was 2 (range, 0–25) and 15 patients (34%) received ≥ 4 doses. During the course of the study 4 patients did not receive nivolumab for the following reasons: insurance issues (n=2), early death (n=1), and rapid disease progression (n=1). The 4 and 8 week mortality was 5%. With a median follow up of 17.25 months (range, 0.5 – 30.4), median overall survival was 18.54 months (range, 0.5 – 30.4) and median relapse free survival for the 34 patients achieving CR/CRi was 18.5 months (range, 1.7 – 25.6). The median event free survival was not reached (range, 0.5 – 13.7). The authors compared these responses to a contemporary cohort of patients treated with cytarabine and idarubicin alone and suggested a trend towards improved overall survival. Six patients experienced grade 3/4 immune related toxicities including rash (n=2), colitis (n=2), transaminitis and pancreatitis (n=1; each). One patient had a grade 3/4 cholecystitis which was deemed possibly related to nivolumab. A total of 18 patients (41%) underwent allogenic stem cell transplant, thirteen (72%) developed graft versus host disease (grades I/II in 8, III/IV in 5), which responded to therapy in 8 patients. Correlative studies identified that responders seemed to have a higher frequency of activated (CD3+) T-cells in their bone marrow aspirates. The baseline bone marrow samples from non-responders had a significantly higher percentage of inhibitory T-cells expressing immune suppressive checkpoint proteins, suggesting that immune escape might be mediating resistance in these patients.

Checkpoint Inhibition Combined with Hypomethylating Therapy

Dr. Daver presented data from a phase 2 study combining azacitidine and nivolumab (cohort 1) or triple therapy consisting of azacitidine, nivolumab and ipilimumab (cohort 2) in patients with relapsed/refractory AML. Eligible patients had relapsed/refractory AML, ECOG ≤ 2, and adequate organ function. For cohort 1 the recommended phase 2 doses were azacitidine 75 mg/m2 days 1–7 with nivolumab 3 mg/kg day 1 and 14. Courses were completed approximately every 4–5 weeks indefinitely. For cohort 2, enrollment was restricted to patients in salvage 1 or 2. Azacitidine and nivolumab at the doses established for cohort 1 were combined with ipilimumab at 1 mg/kg every six weeks. This dosing strategy was based on checkpoint combinations used for lung cancer and melanoma. In cohort 1, a total of 70 relapsed/refractory AML patients were enrolled. The median age was 70 years (range, 22–90), 44% had secondary AML, 34% had poor risk cytogenetics, and the median number of prior salvage regimens was 2 (range, 1–7). The overall response rate was 33% including 15 CR/CRi (22%) (4 CR, 11 CRi), 1 PR and 7 hematologic improvements (HI). Ten percent of patients remained on study for > 6 months without allogenic stem cell transplant. Six additional patients (9%) remained on study > 6 months with stable disease (SD). The remaining 41 patients (58%) had no response. Eight-week mortality was 10%. Patients achieving a response (CR/CRi/PR/HI/SD; n=29, 42%) had significantly improved overall survival compared to non-responders (n=41; 58%; 16.1 versus 4.1 months; p<0.001). Univariate analysis identified improved overall response rates in patients with pre-therapy bone marrow blasts < 20%, circulating WBC ≤ 10,000/mL, ASXL1 mutations, and no prior hypomethylating-based therapy. The authors stated that the median overall survival with azacitidine and nivolumab compared favorably to historical survival with azacitidine-based regimens in salvage 1 or 2 from the MD Anderson Cancer Center (6 vs. 4.7 months; p=0.03). Grade 3/4 immune related events were observed in 8 (11%) patients, including pneumonitis (n=3), colitis (n=2), nephritis, skin rash and hypophysitis (1 each). Within Cohort 2, 20 patients with relapsed/refractory AML were enrolled. The median age of enrolled patients was 68 (range, 25–83), 36% had secondary AML, 25% had adverse cytogenetics. Mutations were found in TP53 (n = 5), RAS (n = 4) and DNMT3A/ASXL1 (n = 2) genes. A total of 14 patients were eligible for response evaluation. There were 6 CR/CRi/CRp (43%) (including 2 CR, 2 CRp, 2 CRi), 2 SD for greater than 6 months, and 6 no response. The 8 week mortality was zero. Grade 3/4 immune related adverse events were noted in 26% of the patients, the most common of which was pneumonitis. The median number of cycles to response was 2. Median overall survival for all patients was not reached, and the projected overall survival was calculated to be 58%.

Lastly, Dr. Lindblad presented data regarding the combination of pembrolizumab and decitabine for relapsed or refractory AML. This was an investigator sponsored, single-institution, single-arm open-label 10 subject study (). Pembrolizumab 200 mg was administered intravenously on day 1 of every three-week cycle, with decitabine 20 mg/m2 administered on days 8–12 and 15–19 (total of 10 days) of alternative cycles beginning with cycle 1. Up to eight cycles (24 weeks) of therapy was given. Ten high-risk patients (median age 62, range 30–81) were enrolled. Seven patients had refractory disease (including 2 with therapy-related myeloid neoplasms), and 3 had early relapse (less than 6 months from completion of therapy). This combination was well tolerated. No Grade 5 adverse events occurred. Most Grade 4 AEs were hematological. Non-hematological Grade 4 AE were observed in 2 subjects. Febrile neutropenia, hypotension and sepsis was reported in one patient and Grade 4 sepsis was reported in a second patient. Two patients experienced immune-related adverse events, including hypothyroidism and central diabetes insipidus. 4/10 patients had stable disease at the completion of 8 cycles (24 weeks), and 4 patients progressed prior to cycle 8. One patient was taken off of study due to Grade 4 toxicity (sepsis) in cycle 5 in a MLFS, and one patient achieved an MRD negative CR following 8 cycles. Median overall survival on this study was 7 months (range, 2 to ongoing at 14 months), with a median time of follow-up in survivors of 13 months (range, 7–14 months).

Challenges and Future Directions

Immunotherapy remains a promising therapeutic approach for patients with AML and MDS. It is of particular interest for those patients ineligible for allogenic stem cell transplantation or with relapsed or refractory disease resistant to standard cytotoxic chemotherapies. Clinical trials using protein based vaccination strategies have shown limited efficacy, but DC based vaccination suggest some promise and preclinical results suggest that such strategies might benefit from combinations with immune checkpoint inhibitors.

Barriers to the progression of immunotherapy in myeloid cancer include limited AML-specific antigen expression, which can result in off-target toxicity, and the suppressive or dysfunctional immune milieu in heavily pre-treated AML patients. The timing of administration of immunotherapy is likely crucial, since such approaches in the relapsed/refractory setting may be hampered by inadequate T-cell or antigen presenting cell function (dendritic cells). Rational cellular therapeutics in combination with some of the more recently approved targeted agents applied earlier in the disease course might be of particular interest and value. As highlighted in this review, AML patients treated with chemotherapy in combinations with checkpoint inhibitors show variable response, or experience at best stable disease, highlighting the limitations of this strategy for myeloid cancers. At this year’s meeting Dr. Daver’s work suggested that AML patients with a lower disease burden (< 20% blasts) might be more likely to respond to such an approach, but this evidence is far from conclusive.

As with other strategies in AML, combination approaches are likely to have better success. Strategies that target both the innate and adaptive immune system may be needed since both are potentially deficient in AML. At this year’s meeting, Dr. Stroopinsky and colleagues presented preclinical evidence suggesting that such a strategy might be more effective in AML. These investigators targeted both the innate (DC/AML fusion vaccine) and adaptive (checkpoint inhibitor) immune systems yielding a more potent immune response and better outcomes, although this approach remains to date pre-clinical. Novel combinations of innate immune agonists or vaccines given in combination with CAR-T therapies or checkpoint inhibitors might therefore have potential to produce more potent, sustained anti-leukemic activity.

CAR-T therapy and bispecific antibodies, which capitalize on T-cells to kill AML, seem to have promise, although the clinical trial data are limited and only a few patients have been treated. AML is a heterogeneous disease with a diverse array of expressed antigens, many of which are shared with normal hematopoietic precursors; these factors have until now limited the development of T-cell based strategies. Work presented at this meeting suggests that despite these limitations, effective T cell based strategies might eliminate refractory AML to allow salvage with allogeneic transplant. For those in whom allogenic transplant is not a possibility, simultaneous targeting of multiple antigens might once day provide sufficient AML- specific activity to preserve endogenous hematopoiesis.

The most striking responses to date are those described by Dr. Liu and colleagues, who obtained remission in two patients with multiply relapsed disease using a CLL-1-CD-33 CAR-T. Both treated patients have gone on to allogeneic stem cell transplant and remain in remission. Their approach used two antigens to produce better leukemia-specific killing, but still killed normal progenitors and resulted in bone marrow aplasia that required consolidation with allogenic stem cell transplant. Based upon these preliminary data, we can expect the development of novel immune strategies for myeloid cancers. Unfortunately, the activity of the most well developed strategies, such as checkpoint inhibitors and vaccines have thus far been less compelling in myeloid cancers than in solid tumors. Based upon this year’s meeting however, combination strategies and more novel approaches such as those capitalizing on T-cells (like bispecific antibodies and engineered T-cells), are likely to continue to provide hope for our patients with high risk myeloid cancer.

Research Agenda

Immunotherapeutic strategies in acute myeloid leukemia have demonstrated early signs of promise. The research agenda to further increase the efficacy of these therapies in patients with AML should consist of:

Determining the optimal timing for administration to ensure an adequate immune milieu capable of responding to immunomodulation

Identification of combinatorial approaches, including targeting both the lymphoid and myeloid compartments

Identification of AML specific antigens for improved efficacy and toxicity profiles
