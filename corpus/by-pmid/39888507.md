# Beyond checkpoint inhibitors: the three generations of immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Anti-tumor immunotherapy was rediscovered and rejuvenated in the last two decades with the discovery of CTLA-4, PD-1 and PD-L1 and the roles in inhibiting immune function and tumor evasion of anti-tumor immune response. Following the approval of the first checkpoint inhibitor ipilimumab against CTLA-4 in melanoma in 2011, there has been a rapid development of tumor immunotherapy. Furthermore, additional positive and negative molecules among the T-cell regulatory systems have been identified that that function to fine tune the stimulatory or inhibitory immune cells and modulate their functions (checkpoint modulators). Many strategies are being explored to target macrophages, NK-cells, cytotoxic T-cells, fibroblasts, endothelial cells, cytokines and molecules involved in tumor tolerance and microbiome. Similar to agents that target checkpoint modulators, these newer targets have the potential to synergize with other classes of immunotherapeutic agents and importantly may overcome the resistance to other immunotherapies. In order to better understand the mechanism of action of all major classes of immunotherapy, design clinical trials taking advantage of different types of immunotherapeutic agents and use them rationally in clinical practice either in combination or in sequence, we propose the group all immunotherapies into three generations: with CTLA-4, PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors as the first generation, agents that target the checkpoint modulators as the second generation, while those that target TME as the third generation. This review discusses all three generations of immunotherapy in oncology, their mechanism of actions, major clinical trial results and indication, strategies for future clinical trial designs and rational clinical applications.

Beyond checkpoint inhibitors: the three generations of immunotherapy

Introduction

Presently, we have a growing number of immunotherapy systemic agents (CTLA-4, PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors) that have been incorporated into nearly all organ systems in the field of medical oncology. Although there have been varying positive results in response rates, many checkpoint inhibitors do not have high response rates (primary resistance). There is mounting evidence that almost all tumors will develop resistance over time (secondary resistance). Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop newer agents that can synergize with the currently available checkpoint inhibitors and more importantly to overcome resistance. There is a rapidly expanding list of newer immunotherapy agents in development and clinical trials. In order to have better understanding on how to understand their mechanism of action and to incorporate them in clinical practice, we propose to group the currently available immunotherapy drugs and the large number of new immunotherapy drugs in development into three generations of immunotherapy, and review here their mechanism of actions, early efficacy and toxicities. Increasing evidence suggests that there is a strong synergy among different generations of immunotherapeutic agents. The current literature now supports the expanding role of other modalities of immunotherapy in cancer treatment, including checkpoint modulators, either immune stimulatory or inhibitory, and those that act in tumor microenvironment (TME) and host innate immune system. This article aims to provide an overview of current immunotherapy modalities and to subclassify them into newer generations. We will discuss the current approach in the clinical application of the first generation of modern immunotherapy with CTLA-4, PD-1 and PD-L1 inhibitors, the ongoing research on the second and third generations of immunotherapy involving the checkpoint modulators and TME/innate immune system, respectively. Grouping all the immunotherapeutic agents into the three generation of drugs will help to design the future immunotherapy combination clinical trials and rational use of these agents in clinical practice.

First generation of immunotherapy

The first immune checkpoint molecule was discovered in 1987 and named cytotoxic T-lymphocyte antigen number 4 (CTLA-4) by Brunet and his team. However, the significance of this discovery remained unclear until 1995, when Jim Allison et al. pinpointed it as a crucial immune checkpoint molecule target. Into the early 2000s as immunologists and researchers’ understanding of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, APCs and T-cell interactions began to grow, they began to see that these processes are regulated by both stimulatory and inhibitory checkpoints. CTLA-4 acts early during the T-cell priming phase to compete with cluster of differentiation 28 (CD28) for binding at the B7 receptor to prevent CD4 T-cell activation. Its role in normal physiology is to prevent the body’s immune defense system from acting against its own antigens (i.e., autoimmunity). In 2005, the monoclonal antibody targeting CTLA-4 present on regulatory T-cells, Ipilimumab, arose as the first immune checkpoint inhibitor used in clinical practice progressive metastatic melanoma treated with Ipilimumab. Afterward, it came the landmark trial in August 2010, which demonstrated Ipilimumab as an effective treatment for previously treated advanced and metastatic melanoma. Since this trial, numerous others have exhibited significantly improved clinical outcomes in advanced and metastatic melanoma. The mechanism of action of the first-generation immunotherapy agents is summarized in Fig. 1. Over the last decade, there have been a number of immune checkpoint inhibitors studied across a wide array of tumor types that have changed the landscape of anti-cancer therapy as we know it. Currently, available options of immunotherapy fall under the umbrella of first generation of immune checkpoint inhibitors that target PD-1, PD-L1 and CTLA-4, which are summarized in Table 1. These agents have drastically improved the world of cancer care. Immune checkpoint inhibitors of PD-1 and PD-L1 are utilized in nearly every malignant histology by allowing previously inactivated T-cells to become activated against tumor cells. However, there has been reports of up to 50% of PD-L1 positive tumors to develop primary or secondary resistance to initial PD/PD-L1 blockade therapy. This cascade is almost nearly dependent on intact T-cells and their ability to recognize and engage with tumor cells, which can be directly or indirectly related to a lack of tumor antigens or ineffective antigen presentation as well. While there has been no direct comparison among the first-generation immunotherapeutic agents in terms of efficacy, they seem to possess similar activities from indirect comparison in different tumor types and in combination with a variety of chemotherapeutic agents. Autoimmune toxicities are uncommon but serious side effects of checkpoint inhibitors, including monoclonal antibodies against CTLA-4, PD-1 and PD-L1. CTLA-4 inhibitors activate upstream regulatory T-cells, while PD-1 inhibitors stimulate the downstream regulatory T-cells and PD-L1 inhibitors block the inhibitory signals on downstream regulatory T-cells and tumor targets. They may cause different frequency and degree of autoimmune toxicities. Analyses of pooled clinical trials support this hypothesis. We have demonstrated that inhibition of CTLA-4 seems to lead to most frequent autoimmune toxicities, followed by PD-1 and then PD-L1 inhibitors.

Second-generation immunotherapy

Within the immune checkpoint system, there are additional molecules that can modulate the immune functions, either inhibitory or stimulatory. To differentiate them from the first-generation checkpoint inhibitors, we propose to name them checkpoint modulators or second-generation immunotherapeutic agents. The mechanism of action of the second-generation immunotherapy agents is presented in Fig. 2. Most preclinical and clinical efforts have been focused on the development of agents that block the inhibitory molecules. Emerging data indicate that the second-generation immunotherapeutic agents may have limited activities alone but increased potencies when combined with those from the first generation. Importantly, many of the second-generation agents were shown to have potential to overcome the primary or secondary resistance to those of the first generation.

T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain-containing protein 3 (TIM-3) is an important upcoming target in the role of immunotherapy. TIM-3 is a member of the TIM family that was discovered in 2002, which is involved in the structure of the immunoglobulin variable chain domain. As it has been identified that many immune system cells such as CD4 and CD8 expressed TIM-3, it has been an approach does a therapeutic target and modulating immune system. TIM–3 inhibitors have shown significant responses similar to PD–1 inhibitors in preclinical research, that has led to further evaluation of their synergism as well. It has been reported that PD–1 antibodies may lead to an increase in TIM-3 expression in early models and lung cancer, which has shown that TIM–3 may be a marker of PD-1 blocking antibody resistance. We have studied the single agent activity of a monoclonal antibody against TIM-3 in a phase I trial. While the agent was well tolerated, we did not observe significant activities in the tumor types enrolled in the study. An additional study combined these targets including TIM-3 and PD-L1 involved LY3321367 (anti-TIM-3) monotherapy and in combination with LY3300054 (anti-PD-L1) in patients with advanced cancer. LAG-3 (lymphocyte activating gene-3) is among the forefront in the targetable options in cancer care. It can be expressed on CD4 and CD8 + exhausted T-cells due to chronic antigen stimulation, leading to downregulation of the immune system in anti-tumor activity. Many clinical trials are underway and phase 1 and phase 2 studies investigating the anti-LAG–3 monoclonal antibodies. A phase 1/2 clinical trial is evaluating the safety and efficacy of favezelimab alone ± in combination with pembrolizumab. Preliminary results show minimal responses as monotherapy, however better anti-tumor activity in the combination arm. It has been shown that LAG-3 can work synergistically with PD-1 with regards to anti-tumor activity and overcoming T-cell exhaustion. To focus on this relationship, the early phase 1/2 study evaluating the combination of relatlimab and nivolumab in patients with advanced melanoma was well tolerated and with an improved overall response rate. The phase 3 RELATIVITY-047 study evaluated this combination versus nivolumab monotherapy and resulted in improved progression-free survival. Relatlimab became the first agent from the second-generation immunotherapy agents to receive the FDA approval recently for the management of patients with unresectable or metastatic melanoma. Another targetable domain in the innate immune system is T-cell immunoglobulin and ITIM domain (TIGIT). TIGIT is coexpressed with PD-1 tumor antigen specific T-cells like tumor infiltrating lymphocytes. In an effort to provide further targeted therapy toward this domain, early studies are being performed and studies for various types of malignancy. A recent study was the first ever inhumane phase 1 study in patients with advanced solid tumors who received an anti-TIGIT humanized immunoglobulin monoclonal antibody with or without the anti-PD-1 monoclonal antibody drug, pembrolizumab. This study is evaluating safety, tolerability and pharmacokinetic profiles of the vibostilumab for future studies in the future. In March 2022, the SKYSCRAPER-02 study was the first randomized study that used tiragolumab (anti-TIGIT monoclonal antibody) in extensive stage small cell lung cancer. Tiragolumab was combined with the current standard of care regimen of the PD–L1 inhibitor atezolizumab and chemotherapy with carboplatin and etoposide. It had not met its coprimary endpoints of overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). Despite this, Roche will continue to examine tiragolumab in the treatment of non-small cell cancer (NSCLC) in the Phase III pivotal trials SKYSCRAPER-01 and SKYSCRAPER-03. The second subgroup includes stimulatory monoclonal antibodies to receptors such as OX40, GITR and 4-1BB that activate costimulatory molecules that then target the malignancy. Recent trials have begun targeting these costimulatory receptors that permit proliferation and survival of the T-cells. Another target is 4-1BB, which is crucial regulator of immune responses. Numerous studies have established that signals via 4-1BB are costimulatory, especially regarding CD4 and CD8 + cells. study of Melero et al., who first showed that in vivo administration of agonistic anti-4-1BB monoclonal antibody has potent anti-tumor properties. For example, BMS-666513, fully humanized monoclonal antibody against 4-1BB, has completed phase I and II trials for its anti-cancer properties in patients with melanoma, renal cell carcinoma and ovarian cancer patients. T-cell costimulatory receptor (ICOS or CD278), a member of the CD28 superfamily, is a new potential biomarker of T-cell–mediated immunotherapy response. ICOS is mainly expressed on activated cytotoxic T-cells, memory T-cells and regulatory T-cells. The initiation of the ICOS pathway begins through ligation of ICOS and its ligand (ICOSL), which is expressed on B-cells, macrophages and dendritic cells. Early mouse studies were performed to determine whether ICOS-activated T-cells are implicated in the immune response pathway to PD-1 blockade. The improvement in tumor response led way to further phase 3 studies that are underway. Finally, B7-H4 inhibits T-cell activation and function. In addition, it promotes angiogenesis, tumor invasion and metastasis and inhibits apoptosis. Various strategies to target B7-H4 are being developed, including antibody–drug conjugate. Preliminary clinical trial has demonstrated its activity in refractory breast, endometrial and ovarian cancers. Third-generation immunotherapy

There is growing understanding that the tumor microenvironment is at play with regards to cancer surveillance. Many cancer patients have anti-cancer T-cells, but the tumor microenvironment has a role in managing and suppressing the immune system against the tumor cells. This permits the cancer cells to survive these regulatory mechanisms and to continue to proliferate and survive. The microenvironment is very diverse and includes a combination of normal immune cells (including natural killer and T- and B-cells) which interact with an extracellular matrix, macrophages and the cancer cells themselves. Additionally, within the tumor microenvironment, there are adipocytes, fibroblasts, cytokines, growth factors and the host innate immune function that is involved in tumor tolerance. This growing number of interacting cells and cytokines inherently provides activation and growth of the tumor cells. We propose to designate them as third-generation immunotherapeutic agents. Therefore, they transition the focus of previous immunotherapy generations toward the tumor microenvironment. Growing evidence strongly suggests that they play a pivotal role in the initiation, potentiation and the eventual execution of anti-tumor immune response (Fig. 3). Many are involved in primary and secondary resistance to agents against the first-generation immunotherapy. Overall, tumor-associated macrophages (TAM) play a crucial role in angiogenesis, metastasis, immune evasion and therapeutic resistance; therefore, they are an important target for future development of therapeutic strategies. Several checkpoints that are mainly associated with macrophages have also been discovered. Blocking such binding reduced the accumulation of anti-PD-1 antibody in TAMs and prolonged its retention time in CD8 + T-cells, leading to the regression of tumors. CD47 is involved in cancer cells that can evade detection by the innate immune system due to a “do not eat me” signal pathway. CD47 overexpression can be found in multiple malignancies leading to aggressive overall clinical picture. Clinical studies are targeting inhibition of CD47 signaling, therefore leading to increased activation of phagocytosis in the immune system, therefore limiting tumor growth and metastatic spread. First clinical trials were initiated in 2014 that involved a phase 1 trial looking at clinical safety in patients with released/refractory malignancies. Early studies are looking at a combination regimen with PD-L1 therapy to boost both the innate and adaptive immune system. Challenges are met as CD47 expression is on red blood cells, therefore resulting red blood cell toxicities including severe anemia have been evaluated. Newer generations are being developed to minimize this off-target effect. Indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO) in tumor cells mediates T-cells suppression. Immature dendritic cells cause T-cell anergy via the IDO enzyme. Inhibiting IDO enhanced tumor infiltration by CD8 + T-cells. The inherent immunosuppressive activity of IDO is theorized to aid in tumor tolerance and evasion of the immune system. There have been several IDO inhibitors studied in vitro and in clinical trials: 1-methyltrypthophan (Indoximod), INCB024360 (Epacadostat) and NLG919 (Navoximod), as examples. Indoximod was recently evaluated in a phase 2 study in patients with advanced melanoma in addition to another checkpoint inhibitor (pembrolizumab, nivolumab or ipilumumab). Patients treated with Indoximod and Pembrolizumab had an overall response rate (ORR) of 55.7%, which was improved from historical data of pembrolizumab monotherapy with ORR 33%. We have studied indoximod in combination with taxanes in metastatic breast cancer. While the addition of indoximod to chemotherapy did not result in prolonged progression-free survival, patients whose tumors overexpressed IDO seemed to derive some benefit. However, in a phase 3 study examining Epacadostat in combination with pembrolizumab as compared to pembolizumab monotherapy for advanced melanoma, there was no progression-free survival (PFS) or overall survival (OS) benefit. Another class of immunotherapy agents under development is the class of toll-like receptors (TLR) agonists that have shown anti-tumor properties by promoting both antigen presentation and by stimulating innate and adaptive immunity. Another rapidly developing class of immunotherapeutics includes the stimulator of interferon (IFN) genes (STING) agonists. STING represents an intracellular signaling receptor within the endoplasmic reticulum that has shown to enhance anti-tumor immunity. This activity occurs through induction of a number of pro-inflammatory chemokines and cytokines such as type I IFNs. Another pathway of particular interest is the adenosine pathway in the microenvironment involving the CD39, CD73 and adenosine receptors. ATP undergoes a stepwise process to adenosine involving the above receptors, which has driven interest in specific targets for immunotherapy drug development. ATP is converted to AMP via CD39 and subsequently to adenosine via CD73. Adenosine subsequently binds to various receptors on T-cells, most specifically the A2a and A2b receptors that are primarily responsible for downstream immunosuppressive mechanisms. Numerous clinical trials evaluating the third-generation immunotherapy agents such as CD39, C73 and A2a/b as targets are underway (see Table 3). Epigenetic regulation of immune response through histone acetylation by acetyltranferases (HAT) and histone deacetylases (HDAC) is increasingly recognized to play a role in resistance to anti-tumor immunotherapy. HDAC has been implicated in suppressive TME by affecting the functions of T-cells, B-cells, NK-cells and macrophages. Several HDAC inhibitors are being studied either alone or in combination with chemotherapy or other immunotherapies to treat hematological and solid tumors. Data are still early, but there are promising results in both monotherapy drug use as well as with combinations of other generations of immunotherapy. There are many strategies to combine agents across the three generations of immunotherapy or cotarget different immune cells and tumor cells such as bi-specific T-cell engagers (BiTE). There is also ongoing interest and research in cancer vaccines in the world field of immune-therapeutics, in which antigens and antigen presenting cells are critically stimulated to form immunity against specific malignant cell types.

Discussion

Immunotherapy has drastically changed the landscape in the treatment of cancer over the last two decades. Within immune checkpoint system, additional checkpoint modulators, both inhibitory and stimulatory, have been identified. Various strategies and agents are being developed to target them. More importantly, various cells and cytokines from the TME have been shown to play important role in anti-tumor immunity and resistance to checkpoint inhibitors. There is growing evidence that these agents can synergize among each other. To better understand their function and to better design clinical trials to enhance their anti-tumor efficacy and to reduce the toxicities, we propose to groups various immunotherapeutic agents into three generations, with the CTLA-4, PD- and PD-L1-targeting agents as first generation, those targeting additional checkpoint modulators as the second while those targeting TME as third-generation immunotherapy. Current first-generation drugs (ex. PD-1/PDL-1 inhibitors) have their limitations with regards to response rates and the development of resistance. Improvements in response rates and durability of response have been improved with the combination of first-generation checkpoint inhibitors. However, this remains consistent with certain malignancies including breast and lung cancer have had limited efficacy overall when compared to others like renal cell carcinoma or malignant melanoma. In addition, combination therapy among the first-generation immunotherapy agents increases the adverse effects, especially IrAE’s. Therefore, second and third generations of immunotherapy are urgently needed to increase efficacy and decrease the noted resistance patterns. Emerging data from current clinical trials suggest that there is clearly synergy between the first and later generation immunotherapy agents. Currently, there are eight approved first-generation IO drugs for various tumor types either as single agent and in combination with chemotherapy. Due to concerns for increased autoimmune toxicities, few attempts were made to combine the agents within the same generation, except the combination of ipilimumab (CTLA-4 inhibitor) and nivolumab (PD-1 inhibitor). Five-year outcomes from the Checkmate 067 clinical trial which compared nivolumab plus ipilimumab versus nivolumab alone have improved progression-free and overall survival in metastatic melanoma. With the emergence of additional generations of immunotherapy, increasing efforts were made to combine them with those from the first generation, either to overcome primary or secondary resistance. Second-generation drugs were created to improve on the efficacy of the first-generation ones. As evidenced by the RELATIVITY-047 study in untreated advanced melanoma in 2022, improvements were made to progression-free survival when the second-generation immunotherapy drug relatlimab was added to the first-generation immunotherapy drug nivolumab. This was the first FDA approval for immunotherapy generation combination in the treatment of cancer. This synergism has been crucial for opening the gateway for other studies that were then presented at the annual ASCO meeting in 2022. One such abstract was e21145 that evaluated the efficacy and safety of IBI110 (anti-LAG-3 mAb) and sintilimab (anti-PD-1 mAb) in first-line advanced squamous cell lung cancer. On the other hand, the recent phase 3 (SKYSCRAPER-02) trial investigated the role of combined first- and second-generation immunotherapy drugs to further investigate the role for overcoming resistance patterns has yielded negative results. In those with newly diagnosed extensive stage small cell were treated with first-line carboplatin, etoposide and atezolizumab ± tiragolumab (anti-TIGIT monoclonal antibody, a second-generation immunotherapy). At initial analysis, the study had not met its coprimary endpoints of overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS). There are several reasons that may have caused the failure of SKYSCRAPER-02 trials: small cell lung cancer is already chemotherapy sensitive, especially with the combination of atezolimumb. Addition of tiragolumab may not result in tangible benefit. TIGIT may play less important role in SCLC compared to other tumor types such as NSCLC. Accordingly, the role of tiragolumab in NSCLC is still being explored in the ongoing phase III pivotal trials. SKYSCRAPER-01 is investigating tiragolumab in combination with atezolizumab in patients with untreated locally advanced unresectable or metastatic PD-L1 selected non-small cell lung cancers. Interim report from this study in May 2022 revealed that it did not meet its primary endpoint of progression-free survival, with further overall survival data pending. SKYSCRAPER-03 is currently investigating tiragolimab with atezolizumab versus durvalumab in patients with locally advanced, unresectable, stage III non-small cell lung cancer who have not progressed after platinum-based concurrent chemoradiation. Third generation immunotherapy expands on the tumor microbiome. Ongoing trials are exploring the role to overcome commonly encountered resistance patterns. An additional targeted route in third-generation immunotherapy included CSF1R blockade combined with anti–PD-1 or anti–CTLA-4 treatment was associated with enhanced tumor regression in a mouse model of pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. Finally, there are many mechanisms of resistance to immunotherapy. One mechanism involves the loss or decrease in neoantigens and tumor mutational burden of the cancer cells, signifying a loss in efficacy in response to checkpoint inhibitors. Alteration of the second-generation immunotherapy targets such as upregulation of inhibitory TIM-3, LAG-3 and TIGIT or downregulation of stimulatory of inhibitory such as ICOS, OX40 and CD40L as well as those from the third-generation targets such as abnormal IL-2 signaling, increased angiogenesis and decreased macrophage function are also important resistance mechanisms. Additional mechanism of resistance to immunotherapy centers on the TME or other third-generation targets, including impaired T-cell migration and/or infiltration and reduced NK cell activity and dendritic cell maturation, increased IDO expression and hypoxia. To overcome this resistance, combination regimens with CTLA-4 inhibitors have improved clinical response rates. Given that alternate coinhibitory immune checkpoint receptors are associated with T-cell exhaustion and are often upregulated on T-regs, they have been targeted as a putative strategy to overcome resistance to PD-1 and CTLA-4 blockade. Various preclinical studies have demonstrated superior patient outcomes with combined anti–PD-1 backbone therapy and blockade of multiple checkpoint receptors, including TIM-3 and TIGIT. This is the foundation for the concept of the second generation of immunotherapy, the checkpoint modulators. In addition to depletion of T-regs, targeting other suppressive cells in the tumor microenvironment may be beneficial in promoting response to immunotherapy, thus the targets of the third-generation category.

Conclusion

Despite the success of first-generation immunotherapy, there are many unmet needs regarding the overall response rates and drug resistance. Therefore, novel agents are currently being developed to move beyond the current well utilized first-generation checkpoint inhibitors (PD-1/PD-L1). Effective anti-tumor immune response involves a large number of molecules that affect the upstream regulatory T-cells, downstream immunes cells including cytotoxic T-cells, NK-cells and macrophages, the end target tumor cells and the TME and the interactions among different. The present second-generation immunotherapy targets additional checkpoint modulators, both inhibitory and stimulatory, whereas the third generation targets the tumor microenvironment. Emerging preclinical and clinical research demonstrate that such interplays among all immune molecules are crucial in determining the response to anti-tumor immunotherapy. The classification of these immunotherapeutic agents allows us to have a clear understanding the mechanism of action of different agents to target the tumor cells and harness the immune system. It also allows us to design clinical trials with the anticipatory synergism among different generations of immunotherapy rather than utilizing two drugs within the same generation. Furthermore, this classification may help to increase the therapeutic index and avoid unnecessary autoimmune toxicities when combining agents from the same generation. Finally, there is an urgent need for biomarker development and validation to identify predictive biomarkers for each generation or class of immunotherapeutic agents to enrich patients who may benefit from a particular generation of immunotherapy and overcome the resistance to therapy.
