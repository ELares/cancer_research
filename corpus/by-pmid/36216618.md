# Impact of margin accentuation with intraoperative irreversible electroporation on local recurrence in resected pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: The purpose of this study was to evaluate the rates of local recurrence and margin positivity in patients with borderline resectable pancreatic cancer after pancreatectomy with or without irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation.

**METHODS**: Prospective data for preoperative stages IIB (borderline resectable) and III were evaluated, with 75 patients undergoing pancreatectomy with irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation compared to 71 patients who underwent pancreatectomy alone from March 2010 to November 2020.

**RESULTS**: Both irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation and pancreatectomy-alone groups were similar for body mass index, Charleston comorbidity index, and sex. The irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation group had significantly greater preoperative stage III (irreversible electroporation 83% vs pancreatectomy alone 51%; P = .0001), with similar tumor location (head 64% vs 72%) and tumor size (median 2.9 vs 2.8). Neoadjuvant/induction chemotherapy and prior radiation therapy was similar in both groups (irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation 89% vs 72%). Surgical therapy included a greater percentage of pancreaticoduodenectomy in the pancreatectomy-alone group. Despite greater stage and greater percentage of margin positivity (irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation 27% vs 20%; P = not significant), rates of local recurrence were similar. The mean disease-free interval for local recurrence from time of diagnosis was similar (irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation 15.8 vs 16.5 pancreatectomy alone; P = not significant) and time of treatment (irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation 9.4 vs 10.5 months; P = not significant). Overall survival was improved with the irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation group, with a mean of 34.2 months versus 27.9 months in the pancreatectomy-alone group.

**CONCLUSION**: Irreversible electroporation with margin accentuation is safe and effective in stages IIB and III pancreatic adenocarcinomas that are technically resectable. Despite higher margin positivity rates, the time to local recurrence and the effects of recurrence were the same in the pancreatectomy-alone group.

Impact of Margin Accentuation with Intraoperative Irreversible Electroporation on Local Recurrence in Resected Pancreatic Cancer

Objective:

The purpose of this study was to evaluate the rates of local recurrence and margin positivity in patients with borderline resectable pancreatic cancer after pancreatectomy with or without IRE with margin accentuation (IRE-MA).

Methods:

Prospective data for pre-operative Stage 2b (borderline resectable) and Stage 3 were evaluated with 75 patients undergoing pancreatectomy with IRE-MA compared to 71 patients who underwent pancreatectomy alone from 3/2010 to 11/2020.

Introduction:

Pancreatic adenocarcinoma (PDAC) is the fourth leading cause of cancer deaths in the USA. Surgical resection is considered the standard of care for pancreatic cancer but is often not a viable option due to the majority of patients presenting with locally advanced or metastatic disease. Even for patients who undergo resection, tumor recurrence occurs in nearly two-thirds of patients and local recurrence accounts for up to half of those recurrences. Because of this, local recurrence remains as one of the largest limitations of pancreatic surgical resection.

A tumor-free resection margin has served as one of the long-term prognostic factors. A clear definition of resection margin status for all locations (i.e., pancreatic neck, superior mesenteric groove, superior mesenteric artery, posterior margin, and bile duct margin) is therefore essential. According to many centers, the R classification was adapted to Royal College of Pathologists (RCP) guidelines. Microscopic pathologic evaluation of resected tissue for the presence of cancer cells near the margins is often used to classify the resection as either margin positive (R1) or negative (R0). R0 is generally defined as margin clearance ≥ 1 mm and R1 as margin clearance < 1 mm. R1 resections have long been associated with decreased overall survival and recurrence-free survival (RFS). More recently, R1 has shown an association with increased rates of local recurrence.

An additional challenge with pancreatic resection is the frequent involvement of the tumor of vascular structures such as the superior mesenteric artery (SMA), celiac axis (CA) and hepatic artery (HA). Borderline resectable pancreatic cancer (BRPC) has frequent tumor abutment (< 180 degrees circumferential involvement of vessel) of these arteries which presents a major challenge in achieving R0 resections. Arterial resection of tumor-involved arteries is technically challenging and is generally only performed at high-volume centers. Periadventitial dissection of the SMA or CA has been described to skeletonize the artery as an attempt to remove any cancer that may have spread to the neurolymphatic tissue of the vessel wall. However, there has been little research on the effects of this technique on margin status and the single review on this topic concluded that more data and consistent reporting are needed to evaluate its effect.

The use of thermal ablation in pancreatic cancer in the form of radiofrequency ablation has been associated with major complications, especially near critical structures such as the blood vessels, leading to thrombosis. Additionally, cryoablation has been associated with high rates of complications and has lost interest as a modality for PDAC. Newer thermal ablation technologies such as microwave ablation and high-intensity focused ultrasound show some promise, but they have limited evidence in PDAC. All thermal ablation technologies have the inherent limitation of the “heat-sink effect,” which refers to the decreased likelihood of complete tissue necrosis near large blood vessels due to the cooling effect of blood flowing through the vesse. This phenomenon is especially relevant in the pancreas with extensive major vasculature present.

Irreversible electroporation (IRE), a non-thermal ablation technology, has begun to gain acceptance within the last decade. IRE induces cellular apoptosis without disrupting surrounding tissue’s structural integrity. Martin et al. demonstrated that IRE is a safe and effective treatment of LAPC, with initial improvements in median OS to 25.3 months. These results were confirmed with combination of chemotherapy and IRE improving median overall survival to 30.7 months, critically implicating IRE to be included in the multimodal treatment of LAPC. More recent data has also optimized the patient selection criteria for IRE in LAPC and BRPC with age, CA 19-9 response, avoidance of pre-IRE radiation, and FOLFIRINOX plus gemcitabine/ nab-paclitaxel induction chemotherapy as prominent factors to consider when referring or selecting patients to undergo IRE.

The initial use of IRE for margin accentuation (MA) was published by Kwon and Martin utilizing at least two IRE electrodes in bracketing and parallel to the vascular structure (artery or vein or both) to enhance the margin. Pulses are generated between the electrodes and induce the formation of nanopores in the cell membranes of cells located in the electrical field. At sufficiently high voltages, these nanopores become irreversible and cell apoptosis ensues. Without significant thermal effects, IRE has the distinct advantages of sparing the extracellular matrix and proteins in the surrounding cellular microenvironment and avoiding the “heat sink effect”. Thus, IRE presents itself as an attractive tool for margin accentuation (IRE-MA) near the SMA, celiac axis, SMV/PV, and CA to achieve lower rates of positive margins and subsequent local recurrence.

The purpose of this study was to evaluate the rates of local recurrence and margin positivity in patients with borderline resectable pancreatic cancer after Pancreatectomy with or without IRE with MA.

Methods:

Participants

Prospective data for pre-operative Stage 2b (borderline resectable) and Stage 3a or 3b1 patients from our Institutional Review Board (IRB)-approved database was evaluated for 75 patients who successfully underwent pancreatectomy with IRE-MA and then compared to patients who underwent pancreatectomy (N=71) alone from March 2010 to November 2020. This was an IRB-approved study of patients diagnosed with National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) Borderline Resectable Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma treated by pancreatic resection and IRE compared to same-stage patients treated with pancreatic resection alone. This is a post-hoc analysis of our prospective pancreatic cancer database of patients treated with and without IRE for margin accentuation for BRPC. All patients provided written informed consent. A diagnosis of BRPC disease was established by biopsy-proven adenocarcinoma of the pancreas with reconstructable venous involvement or less than 180° encasement of their superior mesenteric artery (SMA) or celiac artery without evidence of metastatic lesions. Staging included triple-phase computed tomographic (CT) scan with less than 1.5-mm cuts at the time of diagnosis and repeated 1-2 weeks prior to resection.

Inclusion criteria involved eligible patients who underwent induction therapy consisting of chemotherapy and/or external beam radiation therapy. Patients underwent restaging evaluation 1 to 2 weeks after induction therapy via repeat triple-phase CT scan and serum tumor markers. Those with evidence of disease progression were excluded. Patients found on restaging to be free of metastatic disease and without primary tumor progression were included and received either IRE with resection or resection alone based on the surgeon’s discretion and/or intra-operative findings as the time of dissection. All patients included were Stage II BRPC or Stage IIIa or IIIb1 based on pre-operative imaging. Patient selection is critical to the safety and efficacy of IRE for BRPC, as has been outlined extensively in previous publications.

Key exclusion criteria were patients with implanted cardiac pacemakers or defibrillators unable to be deactivated; non-removable implants with metal parts within 1 cm of the target lesion; myocardial infarction within 3 months; or unsuitability for general endotracheal anesthesia. All presented data was collected and maintained in a prospective manner. Adverse events were summarized using the National Cancer Institute (NCI) Common Terminology Criteria Adverse Event (CTCAE), version 3.0 and graded via Clavien-Dindo classification.

Interventions

Systemic Therapy

FOLFIRINOX based chemotherapy was administered for at least 6 to 8 14-day cycles, commonly using standard dosing per standard of care and each institution’s management. Similarly, gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel were administered for similar duration using standard dosing per standard of care and each institutions management on days 1, 8, and 15 every 4 weeks. Patients were restaged after induction chemotherapy via repeat triple-phase CT scan and serum tumor markers and evaluated by a multidisciplinary team. All patients with evidence of disease progression were excluded. Only patients who had received FOLFIRINOX, gemcitabine and nab-paclitaxel, or single-agent gemcitabine as an induction therapy prior to IRE and resection or resection alone were included in survival analyses.

Irreversible Electroporation

Patients found to be free of metastatic disease and without primary tumor progression on re-staging were included and further received an open pancreatic resection alone or resection with IRE based on intra-operative findings and location of the primary tumor as described previously. The critical steps of a pancreatectomy were the same for both patients groups. The key piece for IRE with margin accentuation is to perform the IRE early in the dissection, before any of the tissue planes around the vital structure that is to be treated are disturbed/dissected. IRE with margin accentuation can only be performed safely before transection of that index tissue plane in question or concern for a margin positive resection. IRE needle placement must be performed under continuous intra-operative ultrasound (IOUS) for safety and accuracy of needle placement. IRE was performed utilizing AngioDynamics NanoKnife system, as previously described, and was performed by surgeons in the operating room.

Pathology Evaluation:

All surgical margins were evaluated thoroughly by an expert pathologist using a standardized protocol of axial slicing, and reported margins of bile duct, pancreatic neck, SMV groove, SMA groove, posterior margin, and celiac axis (for neck and mid body tumors). We utilized The American Joint Commission on Cancer (AJCC) 8th edition which defined a microscopic positive margin (R1) as cancer cells within 1 mm of the margin in question.

Post-procedure evaluation and follow up

Standardized utilization of CT scans was preformed to avoid the difficulty encountered with cross-comparing CT scans to MRI or CT scans to PET scans in previous studies. Response and progression were evaluated using the international criteria proposed by RECIST 1.1. Serial imaging over at least two months was subsequently used to detect recurrence through study comparison in combination with clinical and serum CA19-9 studies. If equivocal findings were seen on CT, then a PET was obtained to either confirm or refute local and/or regional recurrence when required.

Statistical analyses

OS was defined as the time from the start of treatment to the date of death, due to any reason. PFS was defined as the time from the start of initial IRE treatment to the date of first observed disease progression. The rates of OS and PFS were estimated by Kaplan-Meier method. Multivariable Cox survival regression was performed to determine independent predictors of PFS and OS after backward selection (criterion p<0.05) to include all variables of interest. All statistical analyses were performed using SAS version 9.3 (SAS Institute, Cary, NC), and p values less than 0.05 were considered significant.

Results:

General patient characteristics

There were 75 patients in the IRE-MA group and 71 patients for the pancreatectomy-alone group with no differences for for BMI, Charleston Comorbidity Index, past medical history, and gender (Table 1). The mean age for the IRE-MA group was lower than that of the pancreatectomy-alone group and this difference was significant (61 vs. 66, p=0.02). The most common medical histories for both groups included hypertension, tobacco history, and diabetes mellitus.

Tumor characteristics

The IRE-MA group had significantly more patients preoperatively staged as Stage III pancreatic cancer than the pancreatectomy-alone group (83% vs. 51%, p<0.0001) (Table 2). The most common tumor location for both groups was the head of the pancreas and the rates of occurrence at this location were similar (IRE-MA 64% vs. 72%, p=0.3). Additionally, the mean size of the tumors was similar for both groups (IRE-MA 2.9 cm vs. 2.8 cm, p=0.18).

Preoperative patient characteristics

Every patient in the IRE-MA group received preoperative induction or neoadjuvant chemotherapy compared to 72% in the pancreatectomy-alone group and this difference was significant (p=0.007) (Table 2). The majority of patients in the IRE-MA group received gemcitabine/ nab-paclitaxel as their primary therapy (39%) whereas FOLFIRINOX was the most common primary therapy for the patients in the pancreatectomy-alone group (59%). There were significantly more patients in the IRE-MA group that received preoperative radiotherapy compared to the pancreatectomy-alone group (49% vs. 21%, p=0.0003). The most common type of radiation therapy for both groups was three-dimensional conformal radiation therapy.

Operative characteristics

Patients in both groups underwent similar resection procedures including pancreaticoduodenectomy, subtotal pancreatectomy, and distal pancreatectomy (Table 3). The majority of patients in both groups had pancreaticoduodenectomies and the pancreatectomy-alone group had a significantly higher percentage of this procedure compared to the IRE-MA group (76% vs. 59%, p=0.01).

Vasculature resections were performed in 65% of patients in the IRE-MA group and 48% of patients in the pancreatectomy-alone (p=0.02). In the IRE-MA group, 14% of the blood vessel resections included both artery (celiac, hepatic, or SMA) and vein (portal or SMV), 53% included veins only and 26% included arteries only. In the pancreatectomy-alone group, 12% of the blood vessel resections included both arteries and veins, 74% included veins only and 15% included arteries only. There were significantly less perioperative adverse events in the IRE-MA group compared to the pancreatectomy-alone group (33% vs. 54%, p=0.02) (Table 3).

Perioperative complication rates were similar between the IRE-MA and pancreatectomy-alone groups (p=0.624). Major complications occurred at a rate of IRE-MA 23% and pancreatectomy-alone 27%, and operative complications of a minor grade occurred in IRE-MA 21% and pancreatectomy-alone 24% of patients. There was no perioperative <30-day or <90-day mortality in each group. Mean length of stay in the intensive care unit and hospital did not differ (p>0.05). Rates of delayed gastric emptying (IRE-MA 1 vs. pancreatectomy-alone 2), need for percutaneous drain (IRE-MA 4 vs. pancreatectomy-alone 9), and reoperation at 90 days (IRE-MA 1 vs. Panc only 2) were also similar (p>0.05). Development of any grade pancreatic fistula occurred in 2 of the IRE-MA patients and 4 of the pancreatectomy-alone patients (p=0.217).

Pathologic and long-term outcomes

A significant greater number of patients in the IRE-MA group had lymph-node positive disease (73% vs 43%, p=0.003), with similar numbers of lymph nodes positive (Table 3). Tumor lymphovascular invasion was significantly less at 16% in the IRE-MA group versus 48% in the pancreatectomy-alone (p=0.004), with similar numbers in both groups for tumor perineural invasion (IRE-MA 65% vs. 66%, p=ns).

Positive pathologic margin status was similar in both groups, 27% IRE-MA in comparison to 20% in pancreatectomy group. There was a greater percentage of SMA margin positive in the pancreatectomy alone group (12/71, 17%) in comparison to the IRE-MA group (11%). The remaining margin-positive locations were similar across both groups.

The neoadjuvant/ induction dose density of chemotherapy and/or radiation therapy was similar in both groups, with additional similarity in dose density of adjuvant chemotherapy and/or radiation therapy in patients (Table 3). Overall, the dose density (i.e. total cumulative number of chemotherapy cycles) and the use of radiation therapy were the same for both groups.

In an evaluation of rates of local recurrence the IRE-MA group was similar to that of the pancreatectomy-alone group (17% vs. 18%, p=ns) (Table 4). Additionally, there was no significant difference between the rates of any type of recurrence between the two groups (IRE-MA 65% vs. 42%, p=0.06). The most common location of distant recurrence for both groups was the liver, followed by the peritoneum and the lungs. The mean disease-free interval for local recurrence from time of diagnosis was similar for both groups (IRE-MA 15.8 vs. 16.5 months, p=ns) as well as from time of treatment (IRE-MA 9.4 vs. 10.5 months, p=ns). Notably, the mean overall survival from diagnosis in the IRE-MA group was significantly higher (34.2 months) than the pancreatectomy-alone group (27.9 months, p=0.04) (Table 4).

Discussion:

Local tumor control of BRPC/Stage 3a after pancreatectomy remains a challenge due to frequent tumor involvement with the SMA or celiac axis at the time of resection. Due to the limited options available to effectively achieve microscopically negative margins near sensitive structures, there is currently a high rate of margin positivity and subsequent local recurrence in patients with this stage of disease. This study has shown that IRE-MA is safe and can achieve similar levels of local tumor control to that of pancreatectomy-alone even in the presence of higher rates of Stage 3 disease. The rates of local recurrence as well as the disease-free interval for local recurrence from time of diagnosis and treatment were similar in both groups. Furthermore, the mean overall survival of the IRE-MA group was significantly higher at 34.2 months compared to 27.9 months for the pancreatectomy-alone group. These rates are also encouraging since both groups had the same use of radiation therapy (either neoadjuvant or adjuvant). Thus we believe that these equivalent effects demonstrate an activity of appropriate/ accurate IRE use in combination with extensive surgical dissection and appropriate divesting of the vasculature.

Isolated local recurrence accounts for approximately 10.7% to 49.7% of all recurrences after pancreatic resection. This high incidence has prompted investigation of prognostic indicators that may be associated with local recurrence. The most common prognostic indicators evaluated are margin status, perineural invasion (PNI), lymphovascular invasion (LVI), and lymph node involvement. The ability to achieve a margin negative resection can be overshadowed by a pathology specimen that is positive for the other three and thus biology of the tumor remains the most dominant predictor of outcome.

Until relatively recently, there has been a lack of standardization in pathological margin assessment, which has resulted in considerable variation in the definitions of positive and negative margins. It is critical that margins be defined as transection margins for pancreaticoduodenectomy (pancreatic neck, bile duct, gastric margins) and for distal/ left pancreatectomy (pancreatic neck), as well as dissection margins (the posterior margin, superior mesenteric vein/portal vein (SMV/PV) margin, and superior mesenteric artery (SMA) margin). It is the dissection margin that is most cases is fixed and where some degree of margin accentuation is needed,

Recent studies have sought to standardize the definitions of margin status into stratifications that provide meaningful prognostic information. A recent study reviewed a large cohort of over 500 patients and concluded that a margin-negative status defined as margin clearance of ≥ 1 mm showed significantly higher survival and progression-free survival than the margin positive status defined as clearance of < 1 mm. Another important prognostic role of margin status is its association with local recurrence. There has been some discordance in the current literature, but the majority of studies have found associations for margin positivity with local recurrence. Studies evaluating this association have found that up to half of the patients with isolated local recurrence will have positive margins.

With neoadjuvant therapy increasingly used in patients with pancreatic adenocarcinoma, there is some uncertainty that margin status will hold the same predictive value as with patients not receiving neoadjuvant therapy. In early stage PDAC (cT1-T2), neoadjuvant chemotherapy has been demonstrated to significantly decrease the margin positivity rate compared to no neoadjuvant chemotherapy (15.5% versus 21.8%: P equal to 0.001). These results were also confirmed with the use of neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy (13.4% versus 18.6%). However, these positive findings for chemoradiation therapy have not been demonstrated in Stage IIb (BRPC) and Stage IIIa and Stage IIIb1 (LAPC).

One study investigated whether preoperative chemotherapy in LAPC and BRPC had any impact on the predictive ability of margin status on OS, recurrence-free survival (RFS), and local recurrence. They found that there were no significant differences in OS and RFS based on margin status. There was also no significant difference in the rate of local recurrence, but there was a trend toward more frequent local recurrence with positive margins. In our study, the IRE-MA group had significantly higher levels of neoadjuvant chemotherapy and radiotherapy, which may influence the predictive value of margin status in our study population. However, the cohort in their study was predominately staged with LAPC, making it difficult to generalize the findings towards a cohort with solely BRPC as in our study. Additionally, their study did not evaluate the effects of neoadjuvant radiotherapy on the predictive value of margin status. Further investigation into the effects of neoadjuvant chemotherapy as well as radiotherapy on the predictive value of margin status in BRPC will be needed to draw conclusions from this area of research. With the results presented here we believe that the optimal timing of radiation therapy should be after resection with margin accentuation in order to more accurately cover the high incidence of perineural invasion, lymphovascular invasion, and the regional lymph nodes that are also predictive of local regional recurrence and even overall survival. We have found and previously published that patients who undergo surgical resection with IRE prior to radiation therapy tolerate treatment far better than patients who have had previous radiation therapy, or even high-dose radiation therapy, and then attempt to undergo surgical resection with IRE. Because of these results we believe that utilizing adjuvant iMRT radiation therapy in these disease stages has a better optimal oncologic outcome but probably more importantly a better short-term and long-term quality-of-life tolerance. Our data also demonstrates that with multidisciplinary support over 85% of patients received their cumulative chemotherapy and radiation as planned.

With the growing importance of margin status, there has been further stratification of specific margins and investigation into their respective prognostic roles. There is some variation in the specific margins that are evaluated in the current literature, but the majority of studies report on the SMA/Uncinate, CA/Neck, Posterior Retroperitoneal/Radial, Bile Duct, Anterior, Enteric, and SMV/PV margins. The margins involved primarily with arteries (SMA/Uncinate and CA/Neck) are of special concern because these are the margins that have presented the most technical difficulties for our established resection techniques and have thus shown more associations with local recurrence and worse prognosis compared to other margins. One study found that 82% of local recurrences were involved with arteries (SMA, CA, Hepatic). Furthermore, a review of 16 studies showed that the most frequently positive margin was the SMA/Uncinate margin. Another study looked at the total number of local recurrences near the arteries and found that roughly two-thirds of the local recurrences were closer to the SMA than the CA. The CA/Neck has lower rates of local recurrence and margin positivity, but this is likely due to lower overall rates of tumors located at the body/neck of the pancreas compared to the head.

Consequently, the IRE probes in MA for this study were positioned around the SMA in pancreatic head tumors and around the CA in pancreatic neck tumors to address these challenges. In this study, the majority of the tumors in both groups were located at the head of the pancreas followed by the body/neck. Overall margin positivity showed slightly higher rates in the IRE-MA group than the pancreatectomy-alone group. However, when evaluating the specific margins that were treated by IRE, the IRE-MA group showed lower levels of margin positivity. The IRE MA group had 8/20 (40%) SMA/Uncinate margin positivity compared to 10/14 (71%) for the pancreatectomy-alone group. Similarly, margin positivity for the CA/Neck margin was 4/20 (20%) for the IRE-MA group compared to 9/14 (64%) for the pancreatectomy-alone group (Table 3 and 4).

Notably, the nearly half of the positive margins (pancreatic neck) in the IRE-MA group were located at margins that were not treated with IRE. This contrasts with the pancreatectomy-alone group, which had the highest rates of margin positivity at the SMA/Uncinate and CA/Neck margins despite similar overall rates of head and neck/body tumor localization between the two groups. A similar phenomenon was found in a recent study by Kundalia et al with IRE-MA but with different margins treated with IRE. Their technique with IRE-MA differed in that they used IRE on the posterior, SMV/PV, and CA/Neck margins but not on the SMA/Uncinate margin due to the increased risk of complications associated with the steep learning curve of IRE probe placement. When they excluded the SMA/Uncinate margin, they found that the IRE-MA group had significantly lower rates of margin positivity than the non-IRE group. These findings suggest that IRE-MA may be playing a role in reducing the rates of positivity at the treated margins. This may provide the argument for treating all the aforementioned margins with IRE in order to achieve the maximum benefit from this technique. The addition of the pancreatic neck margin is now part of our intra-operative treatment algorithm.

This study has shown that IRE-MA can be performed safely and effectively in patients with resectable pancreatic adenocarcinoma. The IRE-MA group had significantly fewer perioperative complications compared to the pancreatectomy-alone group. One of the largest limitations of IRE-MA is the high learning curve associated with its use near delicate structures, particularly the SMA. This is the first study to analyze the impact of IRE-MA on the SMA/Uncinate margin status in BRPC/LAPC and among the few to analyze the effects on overall margin status and local recurrence.

There were a number of limitations to this study. The IRE-MA group had significantly more patients staged with stage 3 pancreatic adenocarcinoma compared to the pancreatectomy-alone group. This may have impacted the courses of neoadjuvant therapy that these patients received as this group also had significantly more neoadjuvant chemotherapy and radiotherapy compared to the pancreatectomy-alone group. As recent research has suggested, neoadjuvant therapy may have an impact on the predictive value of margin status. Additionally, the patients in the IRE-MA group also had significantly more blood vessel resections during their operations. These differences could present as potential confounding variables and future studies would benefit with cohorts that have more comparable neoadjuvant therapy profiles. However, we believe the power of our numbers and these variables do not conflict with our positive results

Thus, in conclusion IRE with MA is safe and effective in stage 2b and stage 3 pancreatic adenocarcinomas that are technically resectable. Despite higher margin positivity rates, the time to local recurrence and the effects of recurrence were the same in the pancreatectomy group alone.
