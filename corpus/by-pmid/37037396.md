Nucleic acid immunotherapeutics and vaccines: a promising approach to glioblastoma multiforme treatment

Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is a deadly and difficult to treat primary brain tumor for which satisfactory therapeutics have yet to be discovered. While cancer immunotherapeutics, such as immune checkpoint inhibitors, have successfully improved the treatment of some other types of cancer, the poorly immunogenic GBM tumor cells and the immunosuppressive GBM tumor microenvironment have made it difficult to develop GBM immunotherapeutics. Nucleic acids therapeutics and vaccines, particularly those of mRNA, have become a popular field of research in recent years. This review presents the progress of nucleic acid therapeutics and vaccines for GBM and briefly covers some representative delivery methods of nucleic acids to the central nervous system (CNS) for GBM therapy.

Introduction and Background

GBM is the most aggressive and lethal CNS tumor, affecting both adult and pediatric populations. The current standard of care for GBM is surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy, specifically temozolomide. Unfortunately, with these current treatments, GBM patients still do not receive much survival benefit with the median overall survival (OS) at approximately 15 months. In fact, the prognosis for GBM patients has not improved much in the past four decades, likely because of several complications. For example, GBM consists of malignant astrocytes, which are highly infiltrative and essentially guarantee incomplete resection of the tumor. Moreover, GBM is often highly immunosuppressive, which hampers the development of anti-GBM immunity and inhibits the infiltration of anti-GBM immune cells. In general, GBM tumors also have very low mutation burdens. Yet, high mutation burdens resulting in high numbers of the corresponding tumor neoantigen-specific T cells are important for successful responses to immunotherapy such as immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), which may explain the lack of progress in clinical trials testing ICI therapy in GBM. Finally, the blood-brain barrier (BBB) and blood-tumor barrier (BTB) in GBM represent physiological barriers against effective delivery of therapeutics to GBM tissues from the systemic circulation. This leaves the field of GBM treatment desperate for new advances that may finally improve the prognosis for GBM patients.

As mentioned above, the current standard standard of care for GBM is surgery, radiotherapy, and temozolomide. The first mentions of glioma tumors were found at the turn of the 19th century in British autopsy reports. In 1865, the German pathologist Rudolf Virchow provided the first comprehensive histomorphological description, showed that tumor cells were malignant transformations of normal CNS glial cells, and first coined the term “glioma”. Surgery was the only option for removing tumors until The Brain Tumor Study Group (later renamed The Brain Tumor Cooperative Group) established post-operative radiation therapy as the new standard of care for the treatment of GBM through the success of three randomized clinical trials in the early 1970s. In 2005, the European Organization for Research and Treatment of Cancer (EORTC) hosted a trial showing improved overall survival of GBM patients who received temozolomide as well as radiation therapy as a post-operative adjuvant when compared to those who received radiation alone, an effect that was still appreciated years later. Improvement to surgical technique came in 2006 when it was shown that more maximal cytoreductive surgery could be achieved with a fluorescence-guided technique utilizing 5-aminolevulinic acid, which provided an improved six-month progression free survival compared to control patients. Even so, there is still much improvement to be made as median OS still stays at approximately 15 months.

The brain has been historically considered an immunoprivileged organ as the formidable BBB prevents the passage of molecules and materials from the systemic bloodstream into the brain. A more recent study, though, has illustrated that the brain is actually not immunopriviledged since it can communicate with the systemic immune system via lymphatic channels within the meninges, and a variety of immune cells and molecules are able to access the CNS region. This opened the door to attempt to utilize the systemic immune system to target and kill brain tumor cells.

Many factors play a role in the difficulty of treating GBM with immunotherapy. One of the most complex obstacles is the GBM tumor microenvironment, reviewed by Charles et al.. Another is that GBM tumors are immunologically “cold,” meaning that the tumor microenvironment is so immunosuppressive that it inhibits the elicitation of antitumor immunity and prevents the effective infilatration of antitumor cells and molecules. GBM tumors are immunosuppressive by releasing immunosuppressive cytokines and growth factors such as interleukin-10 (IL-10), prostaglandin E2 (PGE-2), transforming growth factor beta (TGF-β), and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). Foundational studies in glioma immunology have also illustrated the dysfunction of cell-mediated immunity in GBM patients. Glioma cells are also poor at presenting tumor antigens because GBM cells notably downregulated the expression of B7 costimulatory molecules, which are required for T cell activation. In addition, tumor associated macrophages (TAMs) play a critical role in the immunosuppressive nature of the GBM microenvironment. The immunosuppressive M2-like macrophages, which make up the majority of non-neoplastic cells in GBM tumors, decrease antitumor inflammation and exhibit protumoral effects such as the promotion of tumor growth and invasion. Taken together, these challenges make the development of a successful immunotherapeutic for GBM no small feat.

Nucleic acid therapeutics have already become a popular topic for both preclinical and clinical cancer therapy research. Studies exploring nucleic acids as therapeutics (mostly for gene knockdown and protein replacement) in various pediatric cancers such as hepatoblastoma, nephroblastoma, neuroblastoma, osteosarcoma, medulloblastoma, retinoblastoma, and acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) have been largely successful and promising. Several clinical trials since 1999 investigating the use of antisense oligonucleotides (ASO) have been undertaken (listed in), though few, if any, have listed results. This lag in progress is likely due to the difficulties with ASO delivery to the brain and affordable cost of production. For GBM, mRNA has also recently been explored as a new method for gene therapy delivery.

Along with gene therapy, gene knockdown, and protein replacement, nucleic acids have also begun to be tested as immunotherapeutics for cancer. Several clinical trials using RNA vaccines for various types of cancer have been completed and/or are still underway, illustrating the excitement for this relatively new field of study. These clinical trials can be found searching ‘RNA Vaccine’ and ‘Cancer’ at www.clinicaltrials.gov. Besides vaccines, aptamers, immunostimulatory nucleic acids, gene-regulating nucleic acids, gene-editing nucleic acids, and plasmids have also shown promise as new classes for cancer immunotherapy (previously reviewed by our lab in).

Due to the success of nucleic acid therapeutics for other types of cancer, there has arisen a hope that this new field of study might also be beneficial for the treatment of GBM. One group that has been greatly researched is the use of oligonucleotides (OTs) (extensively reviewed in). One example of the usefulness of OTs is as enzyme inhibitors. It has been previously shown that the reactivation of telomerase, an enzyme that prevents the shortening of telomeres during genome replication, is a hallmark of cancer survival and progression, making it a popular target for cancer therapeutics. Marian et al. showed that using the OT telomerase inhibitor imetelstat successfully decreased tumor cell proliferation and tumor growth both in vitro and in vivo; this drug was then later used in a phase II clinical trial with pediatric brain tumor patients. Other forms of nucleic acids that have shown success as GBM treatments are small interfering RNA (siRNA), micro RNA (miRNA), aptamers, mRNA, DNA, and peptide nucleic acids (PNAs), among others. Zhang et al. reviewed the broader topic of the current state of nucleic acid therapeutics for brain tumors; the focus of this review is a brief overview of nucleic acid immunotherapeutics, specifically, for GBM (summarized in Table 1), the role of circular RNA (circRNA) in GBM and its potential as a GBM therapeutic, and an overview of nucleic acid delivery methods to the CNS and the systemic immune system as therapeutics, especially nucleic acids, rely on effective delivery to target tissues. Delivery systems used for the local and systemic delivery of nucleic acid immunotherapeutics for cancers, including GBM, are critical to protect the cargo from enzymatic degradation in the body, prolong the half-life and enhance the bioavailability of the nucleic acids, increase the delivery to target tissues and cells, and eventually increase the therapeutic efficacy and minimizing adverse side effects. Therefore, we also briefly discussed some representative delivery systems reported for nucleic acid immunotherapeutics of GBM.

The current classes of nucleic acid immunotherapy for GBM

mRNA-pulsed dendritic cell vaccines

Since the 1990s, tumor cell vaccines for cancer therapy have become quite popular. The possibility of not only besting the tumor’s immune suppression but also weaponizing the innate and adaptive immune responses toward anti-tumor immunity could drastically alter the way medicine fights even the toughest cancers such as GBM. Several types of vaccines have been created, such as peptide vaccines, mesenchymal stem cell vaccines, and viral antigen vaccines, but many utilize DCs, a type of antigen-presenting cell (APC). DCs are widely studied to develop tumor immunotherapeutics because they play a pivotal part in the activation of antitumor immunity.

There are several reasons why mRNA is preferable as cancer therapeutic vaccines. Because patient-derived mRNA can be amplified in vitro, a relatively small number of cells is needed from which to derive mRNA, which allows for the development of an mRNA-based vaccine for patients who only have a very small biopsy to work with. mRNA also has an increased safety profile over DNA as there is no worry about integration into the host genome. The Ashley et al. group was among the first to show efficacy of an mRNA-pulsed DC vaccine in a mouse tumor model. In experiments where mice were first vaccinated prophylactically then challenged with B16 melanoma cells second, and where mice had pre-existing B16 tumor and subsequently vaccinated, the animals that received the B16 total cell RNA vaccine had significantly prolonged survival over animals vaccinated with B16 tumor extract alone or SMA 560 (spontaneous astrocytoma cell line) extract or RNA. As the first study illustrating the mRNA-pulsed DC vaccines for cancer therapy, this group opened the door to explore this technology for other cancer types. Melanoma and GBM, both deriving from neural ectoderm-descendent cells, share several tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) making this development promising for later exploration with GBM models.

More groups have been using mRNA encoding particular tumor antigens to elicit antigen-specific antitumor T cell responses rather than whole-cell mRNA. The receptor for hyaluronan-mediated motility (RHAMM) is an intracellular protein that has some function in locomotion and has been been linked to aberrant signaling through Ras and Ser and metastasis formation and tumor progression in GBM and other cancers, such as B cell malignancies. Amano et al. found that in mice with implanted KR158B tumors, mean mouse survival was significantly increased in mice vaccinated with RHAMM mRNA DC cells (mean survival 61.8 days) compared to mice vaccinated with nontransfected DCs (31.6 days) and mice without vaccination at all (25.6 days). The authors recognized that as there was only a twofold increase in mean survival days in animals treated with RHAMM mRNA DC vaccines, and hypothesized that RHAMM might not be immunogenic enough to induce complete tumor regression. For a more robust and long-lasting anti-cancer immune response, CD4+ T helper cells must be activated alongside CD8+ cytotoxic T cells. Indeed, Amano et al. found both CD8+ and CD4+ infiltration in the brain tumors of mice treated with RHAMM mRNA-transfected DCs whereas the mice who had received no treatment had no T cell infiltration at all. While the survival benefit of the RHAMM mRNA DC vaccine was only modest, the finding of both CD8+ and CD4+ in the brain is a promising indication that mRNA DC vaccines are mounting the necessary immune response to have an anti-tumor effect when partnered with appropriate tumor antigens.

Similarly, Saka et al. focused on creating a vaccine with mRNA encoding the protein Il13ra2, a subunit of the IL-13 receptor. This group found that in their GBM mouse model using the KR158B cell line, the Il13ra2 protein was overexpressed specifically in glioma cells but absent in normal brain tissues. Their mice treated with the Il13ra2 mRNA-transfected DCs survived significantly longer than mice treated with nontransfected DCs and those without treatment. In fact, all mice that were treated with nontransfected DCs and those without treatment had died by experiment day 40 while 50% of the mice treated with Il13ra2 mRNA-transfected DCs were still alive at day 35 with a small portion of the cohort alive past experiment day 80. This group also found that their DC vaccine stimulated both CD8+ and CD4+ T cells as evidenced by their infiltration into and around tumor tissue. While this preclinical data is promising, it has been shown that only a minority of GBM patients overexpress Il13ra2 in tumor cells and because Il13ra2 is expressed in a myriad of other organs in humans, multi-system toxicity may ensue with systemic delivery of Il13ra2 mRNA DC vaccine in humans.

A major issue with using a mouse model for immunotherapy research is discrepancies between human and murine immune systems. Do et al. attempted to overcome this hurdle by creating a mouse model with a humanized immune system to test a DC vaccine pulsed with CD133 mRNA. CD133 has been previously shown to be a cell surface marker for brain tumor stem cells (BTSCs) and is negatively correlated with patient survival. The authors first showed that the CD133 protein was efficiently loaded onto both MHC class I and MHC class II for presentation to both CD8+ and CD4+ T cells and that the greatest number of CD133+ BTSCs were killed by CD8+ and CD4+ T cells stimulated with the modified human CD133 mRNA-transfected DC vaccine in vitro. They then illustrated successful humanization of their immune-deficient mice by detecting human CD8+ and CD4+ T cells four weeks after stem cell transplant. The authors then injected human GBM cells intracranially into the humanized mice. The mice subsequently treated with the modified human CD133 mRNA-transfected DC vaccine had a significantly increased survival with all mice in this group alive greater than 60 days, while mice treated with nontransfected DCs had a median survival of 40 days and control mice treated with PBS had a median survival of 38 days. This work highlights the utility of the CD133 mRNA-transfected DC vaccine as well as the utility of a (partially) humanized mouse model to closely resemble human patients in preclinical studies.

Vik-Mo et al. brought a glioma stem cell (GSC) DC vaccine to clinical trial using autologous GSC mRNA (protocol detailed in Figure 1). Because GBM tumors are incredibly heterogeneous, within each tumor and between patients, using autologous whole-cell GSC mRNA will hopefully stimulate each patient’s T cells toward their unique array of antigens rather than trying to create a one-size-fits-all therapy with specific antigens that may not be expressed by all tumor cells or in all patients. In this study, the tumors of GBM patients were biopsied and stem cells were allowed to grow in culture. Once tertiary tumorspheres had grown, cells were harvested, whole-cell GSC mRNA was collected, and DCs were transfected. Once patients had received standard surgical and radiotherapy, one group received the DC vaccine alongside standard temozolomide treatment while the control group received standard therapy only. The authors found that the vaccine-treated group had a significantly longer progression-free survival (median 694 days) compared to the matched control group (median 236 days). All other endpoints, including overall survival, were not significant. However, the results produced by this group provide some hope that mRNA-transfected DC vaccines may continue to show promise in clinical trials.

mRNA-transfected DC vaccines have also shown favorable safety in both preclinical and clinical studies. Caruso et al. found that none of their DC vaccine-treated patients developed autoantibodies or any other evidence of autoimmune disease. Amano et al. and Saka et al. were both unable to detect any toxicity in the brain tissue of their DC-treated animals using histological studies. And Vik-Mo et al. found no clinical evidence of cerebral edema or autoimmune encephalitis, retinal or uveal inflammation, or autoimmune reactions against stem cells, all potentially organ- or life-threatening complications.

Additional RNA therapies

In addition to DC vaccines, a variety of vehicles have been explored for vaccine delivery. Sayour et al. proposed that DC vaccines may not be the best choice as DC expansion and collection post-leukophoresis can take several weeks, and this can cause “a significant delay for patients with aggressive malignancies and may preclude candidates from trial with rapid disease progression”. Instead, they identified a nanoliposomal formulation, 1,2-dioleoyl-3-trimethylammonium-propane (DOTAP), that can load tumor antigen mRNA and be administered as a vaccine. The RNA-nanoliposome complex was referred to as the RNA-NP. The authors showed that the whole-cell RNA-NPs did have an antitumor effect on survival and tumor volume in mice with orthotopic GBM tumors, but the effect on survival was not significantly different from that of the DC formulation of the same vaccine, which were both significantly increased when compared to untreated mice. A later study from the same group showed that this NP conjugated to a small interfering RNA (siRNA) targeting PD-L1 significantly decreased PD-L1 expression in GBM-associated myeloid cells and claimed increased survivorship when combined with PD-1 checkpoint blockade in a largely fatal GBM model using GL261 cells.

Other groups have used RNA NPs not to target tumor cells directly, but to modulate the immune system. It has been previously shown that signal transducer and activator of transcription 3 (STAT3) contributes to malignancy by inhibiting the expression of several proinflammatory cytokines and upregulating VEGF and IL-10, and that downregulation of STAT3 can initiate immune-mediated antitumor effects in both glioma animal models and patients. Yaghi et al. sought to target STAT3 with their newly developed miRNA-NP technology. This group previously found that miR-124 plays an important role in STAT3 regulation, and that administering miR-124 has antitumor effects in preclinical late stage glioma models using the lipid-mediated transfection reagent Lipofectamine. The present study compared the therapeutic and targeting efficacies between the previously used Lipofectamine formulation of miR-124 (miR-124 + Lipofectamine) and a new miR-124 NP (named LUNAR-301) formulation using Arcturus Therapeutics’ lipid-enabled and unlocked nucleic acid modified RNA (LUNAR) technology, for which the proposed mechanism of action is shown in Figure 2. Lipofectamine is a common transfection reagent that aids in the delivery of RNA and DNA, primarily in cultured cells, by creating an overall positively charged liposome that can then be taken up by cells. The LUNAR technology explored by Yaghi et al. was an attempt to improve upon this delivery method through the use of a proprietary LNP rather than Lipofectamine. The authors first tested the therapeutic efficacy of four experimental miR-124 NPs and miR-124 + Lipofectamine. Mice with intracerebral GL261 tumors treated with NB5–55-2 (subsequently referred to as LUNAR-301) demonstrated the greatest survival time with >60% of animals surviving past 70 days. The three remaining NP formulations and miR-124 + Lipofectamine had median survivals of 30.5, 43.5, 6, and 40 days, respectively. Targeting efficacy was elucidated by quantifying miR-124 concentration in peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs). miR-124 delivered via LUNAR-301 reached significantly higher concentrations in PBMCs than did miR-124 delivered via Lipofectamine, and maintained this significantly higher concentration for >24 hours. LUNAR-301 was also found to target CD11b+ monocytes and macrophages, which are significant cell subsets for STAT3 regulation. The promising results of this study and the commercial availability of the LUNAR technology make this an encouraging avenue of investigation.

As discussed previously, immune cells such as TAMs play a critical role in GBM survival and immunotherapeutic efficacy. Previous work has illustrated the opposing natures of M1 and M2 macrophages in a cancer setting with M1 cells being pro-inflammatory and anti-tumor, and M2 cells being immunosuppressive and pro-tumor. Zhang et al. tested the hypothesis that RNA NPs could be utilized to reprogram M2-like TAMs to a more pro-inflammatory M1-like phenotype via gene delivery in mouse models of advanced-stage ovarian cancer, metastatic melanoma, and GBM. The RNAs that the authors included in their NPs were Interferon Regulatory Factor 5 (IRF5), which favors M1 polarization, and IKKß, a kinase that phosphorylates and activates IRF5. After confirming their technique for transfection (31.9% of macrophages were successfully transfected without compromising viability), the authors tested whether the delivery of IRF5 and IKKß RNA via NPs would increase levels of IRF5 translation; though gene expression wasn’t lasting, the IRF5 protein remained upregulated through day 5. M2 macrophages were induced by culturing bone marrow derived macrophages (BMDMs) with IL-4 and then transfected with the IRF5/IKKß-NP. The authors found that genes classically expressed by M2 macrophages (such as Serpinb2 and Ccl11) were downregulated and important M1 genes (like Ccl5) were upregulated, evidencing the success of transforming M2 macrophages into a more M1-like phenotype. When used to treat a transgenic mouse model of PDGFß-driven glioma (PDG mice), the IRF5/IKKß-NPs only provided a 5-day increase in survival over untreated controls, but combining IRF5/IKKß-NP therapy with radiotherapy resulted in an average survival of 52 days compared to the PBS-treated control group, which had an average survival of 25 days. The authors concluded that IRF5/IKKß-NP treatment in conjunction with standard of care for GBM may confer some survival benefit for GBM patients for which other therapies have failed.

DNA therapies

As DNA vaccines have been explored as a treatment option for various infectious diseases and other cancers, it is reasonable that researchers have also been trying to develop DNA vaccines for GBM. DNA vaccines are relatively inexpensive to produce and are more stable than linear mRNA, which makes them an attractive option, though they have the potential to integrate into the host genome, which detracts from their appeal.

As mentioned previously, melanoma and GBM have many TAAs in common as they are both derived from the neural ectoderm. Several TAAs for melanoma (deemed melanoma-associated antigens or MAAs) have been discovered including the melanoma antigen gene family (MAGE), the B-M Antigen-1 family (BAGE), and the G Antigen family (GAGE), gp100, tyrosinase, and tyrosinase-related proteins (TRP) 1 and 2. Two of the antigens shared between melanoma and GBM is TRP-2180–188 and gp10044–59 [with insertion positions at (18) and (191) and summarized in Table 2], which are a CD8 epitope and a CD4 epitope, respectively. With this information, Lopes et al. sought to create a DNA vaccine that harnessed the immunogenicity of vesicular stomatitis virus glycoprotein (VSV-G) with the insertion of the TRP-2180–188 and gp10044–59 T cell epitopes, a design they named plasmid to deliver T cell epitopes (pTOP) seen in Figure 3. The authors first evaluated whether pTOP was efficacious in killing subcutaneously implanted GL261 cells. They found that the tumor volumes of mice vaccinated with pTOP on days 2, 9, and 16 after tumor cell implantation had significantly smaller tumors than the untreated controls, and 6/7 vaccinated mice lived >80 days and considered long-term survivors, compared to 1/7 mice living >80 days in the untreated group. The authors next wanted to test whether pTOP could treat mice with intracranially implanted tumors. They found that while pTOP treatment alone or tumor resection alone could not protect mice from disease progression, significant improvement in survival was achieved when pTOP was combined with bulk tumor resection with 78% of mice surviving >250 days. From these results, the authors concluded that combining a DNA vaccine such as pTOP with surgical resection, which is already part of GBM standard of care, provides a new avenue of cancer treatment that should be explored further.

circRNA for GBM

circRNAs were first discovered in the early 1990s. They were originally thought to be noncoding, but several studies have shown that some circRNAs do indeed encode and translate proteins. As they lack 5’ caps and poly-A 3’ tails and therefore cannot be cleaved by exonucleases or poly(A)-specific ribonuclease (PARN), circRNAs are much more stable than their equivalent linear mRNA. More recently, studies have found that some circRNAs play important roles in tumorigenesis and other pro-tumor activities in several types of cancer (reviewed in), including GBM. Wang et al. found that circRNA matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9), or circMMP9, formed from exons 12 and 13 of MMP9 (Figure 4), may have oncogenic properties in GBM. Overexpression of circMMP9 in U87 cells, which poorly express circMMP9 endogenously, was associated with a significant increase in proliferation, migration, and invasion of the U87 cells in vitro. On the other hand, silencing of circMMP9 in U251 cells, which highly express circMMP9 endogenously, was associated with a significant decrease in the migration and invasion of U251 cells in vitro. In mice, overexpression of circMMP9 was associated with a marked increase in the growth rate of U87 subcutaneous xenografts while silencing of circMMP9 was associated with a marked decrease in the growth rate of U251 subcutaneous xenografts, lending the authors to believe that this circRNA may be able to be exploited for GBM therapy in the future. As endogenous circRNAs and their functions have moved into the limelight, many other groups have discovered how these circRNAs directly or indirectly (through the proteins they encode) affect GBM tumorgenicity and patient prognosis. Targeting the pro-tumor circRNAs and/or harnessing the anti-tumor circRNAs could prove to be a successful new field of GBM therapeutics (as reviewed in).

With the ability to synthesize custom RNA sequences, it follows that this technology could be taken advantage of to create unique treatment options for GBM patients, as well as patients with other diseases. Several studies have shown that synthetic circRNAs have promising therapeutic potential as onco-miRNA sponges in gastric cell carcinoma, esophageal carcinoma, and lung adenocarcinoma, among others. But circRNAs are not only destined to be miRNA sponges. CircRNAs can translate proteins if an IRES (internal ribosome entry site) is placed in front of an open reading frame. As such, circRNAs can also function as vaccines, for cancer or otherwise, if the circRNA encodes an antigenic protein. For example, a circRNA vaccine against SARS-CoV-2 was reported, and prototypical circRNA vaccines for cancer were explored. The number of groups exploring the oncotherapeutic potential of linear mRNA and circular RNA vaccines is rapidly expanding, and the field is likely to see incredible progress over the next several years.

The utilization of circRNAs to treat disease is still a dawning field. Thus far, one of the biggest challenges for circRNA therapies is the engineering of efficient protein/peptide-expressing circRNA and the reproducible large-scale manufacturing of circRNA that will be needed to move preclinical successes into clinical trials. There are several methods for circularizing linear mRNA with varying degrees of success. Another challenge is that unlike controlled preclinical studies using relatively homogeneous disease animal models, human tumors are not so homogenous. As a step toward address this challenge, personalized medicine, such as patient-specific personalized circRNA vaccines, can be developed based on the bona fide tumor antigens identified from each patient. Overall, circRNA cancer immunotherapeutics and vaccines may provide a breakthrough for successfully treating GBM patients.

Brief overview of nucleic acid delivery methods

Optimal prophylactic or therapeutic efficacies of nucleic acid vaccines and therapeutics require their delivery to target tissues and cells. Nucleic acids in particular do not fair well in the body without the protection of a delivery system as they are degraded quickly by ubiquitous nucleases. Nucleic acids alone can also trigger a local or systemic immune responses, which may lead to adverse side effects. Moreover, because nucleic acids are often relatively large and negatively charged, their entrance into cells is negligible without a carrier. Though this topic has been extensively covered, this review will highlight a few representative delivery approaches (summarized in Figure 5).

The discussion about LNPs above focused on those studies that utilized LNPs for RNA vaccines for brain tumors, specifically. This section will focus more on LNPs as a delivery vessel targeting the brain or lymph nodes for various other diseases and disorders, mostly of the CNS. Chen, et al designed a LNP that targeted lymph nodes where their mRNA vaccine can best induce an immune response. They discovered that the specific structure and size of the LNP was crucial for its ability to target lymph nodes. They found that LNPs constructed of lipids with shorter tail lengths were most efficient at reaching and accumulating in the lymph nodes while lipids with longer tails exhibited almost no efficacy. Replacing the ester bond with an amide bond, or replacing the methyl groups of the amine head to a hydroxyl, ethyl, o rN-(1,2-ethanediyl)acetamide group also significantly decreased the LNPs’ lymph node-delivery ability. Further, they showed that a significant CD8+ T cell response was initiated when their LNPs were loaded with a model protein antigen chicken ovalbumin (OVA)-encoding mRNA vaccine. This technology can be promising not only for the delivery of nucleic acid vaccines, but also the delivery of other small molecule immunotherapeutics.

As the BBB prevents the systemic penetration of a variety of molecules into the brain, scientists have needed to be creative with their strategies to deliver therapeutics to the brain. The same group as above took advantage of the natural CNS tropism of neurotransmitters. Tryptamine derivatives, including dimethyltryptamine, have been previously shown to cross the BBB by active transport. Ma et al. used dimethyltryptamine to help deliver therapeutics into the brain by attaching dimethyltryptamine molecules to the exterior of their LNP. They also found that LNPs containing shorter tail lengths had greater delivery efficacy, akin to what the group described in the previous paragraph determined. With this LNP, they successfully delivered a small molecule drug (amphotericin B), an antisense oligonucleotide (Tau-ASO), and a gene editing protein (GFP-Cre) into the brains of mice via tail vein injection. Another molecule that is rapidly taken up by the brain is glucose, the brain’s primary source of energy. Anraku et al. utilized the phenomenon of incredible glucose uptake into the brain after a fast has been broken. Simply decorating the exterior of their NPs with glucose did not increase the NPs’ delivery into the brain in free-feeding mice, but putting the mice on a one-day fast, injecting the LNP intravenously, and then injecting a 20%wt glucose solution via intraperitoneal (IP) administration 30 minutes after injection of the NPs significantly increased the NP delivery into the mouse brains. Yet another group took advantage of the brain-targeting nature of the rabies virus glycoprotein (RVG) to deliver their extracellular vesicles containing circSCMH1, a circRNA generated from the Scm Polycomb Group Protein Homolog 1 (SCMH1) gene, which is decreased in patients with acute ischemic stroke, and mouse and nonhuman primate models of acute ischemic stroke. Combining their extracellular vesicles containing circSCMH1 with RVG resulted in successful delivery of the circRNA therapeutic into the brains of mice and rhesus monkeys modeling acute ischemic stroke.

Another logical but more invasive delivery option is intrathecal (IT) delivery, where the protective meninges covering the brain and spinal cord are punctured. Nabhan et al. asked the question of the whether LNPs containing frataxin (FXN) mRNA could be delivered to the dorsal root ganglia (DRG) in a mouse model of Friedreich’s ataxia via IT delivery. Indeed, they found that when compared with IV delivery of the same LNPs, IT delivery at the level of the lower lumbar spine of mice had superior DRG localization. Though IT delivery is generally avoided in the treatment of humans due to the risk of CNS infection, lumbar punctures are still widely used for diagnostic purposes. This study highlights how accessing the CNS via the compartment containing the spinal cord might be a technique to be utilized for therapeutics in the future.

Discussion and future directions

The lack of progress for the development of suitable therapeutics for GBM is outrageously frustrating for patients and their families, along with the physicians and other healthcare providers working to rid patients of this lethal tumor. Though average survival was marginally increased with the introduction of temozolomide chemotherapy and post-surgical radiation therapy into the standard of care so far, it’s obvious that we must pursue a new direction if we hope to improve the lives of this patient population.

The success of immune checkpoint blockade for the treatment of cancers like melanoma put immunotherapy in the spotlight as an exciting new frontier to explore and hopefully discover successful therapies for other forms of cancer as well. Clinical trials for the use of immune checkpoint inhibitors for GBM have not shown the same promise as it has for some other cancers, likely due to the low mutation burdens, the fairly immunosuppressive nature of GBM tumor microenvironment, and the poorly immunogenic GBM cells as discussed above, but nucleic acid cancer vaccines are emerging as a viable option. DNA vaccines have been studied but RNA, particularly mRNA, has been studied more robustly. mRNA-pulsed DCs have been commonly studied in the setting of GBM because of the efficiency with which DCs activate the immune system and the safety profile shown in both preclinical and clinical studies. As the functions, applications, and large-scale current good manufacturing practice (cGMP) manufacturing of circRNA are further explored, circRNA would be promising as immunotherapies for cancer, including GBM, in the coming years. The current and future work performed in this area will hopefully bring forth the breakthrough in GBM therapy that this patient population so greatly deserves.
