# mRNA compartmentalization via multimodule DNA nanostructure assembly augments the immunogenicity and efficacy of cancer mRNA vaccine.

## Abstract

Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccine has fueled a great hope for cancer immunotherapy. However, low immunogenicity, caused by inefficient mRNA expression and weak immune stimulation, hampers the efficacy of mRNA vaccines. Here, we present an mRNA compartmentalization-based cancer vaccine, comprising a multimodule DNA nanostructure (MMDNS)-assembled compartment for efficient mRNA translation via in situ localizing mRNA concentration and relevant reaction molecules. The MMDNS is constructed via programmable DNA hybridization chain reaction (HCR)-based strategy, with integrating antigen-coded mRNA, CpG oligodeoxynucleotides (ODNs), acidic-responsive DNA sequence, and dendritic cells targeting aptamer. MMDNS undergoes in situ assembly in acidic lysosomes to form a micro-sized aggregate, inducing an enhanced CpG ODN adjuvant efficacy. Subsequently, the aggregates escape into cytoplasm, providing a moderate compartment which supports the efficient translation of spatially proximal mRNA transcripts via localizing relevant reaction molecules. The mRNA compartmentalization-based vaccine boosts a strong immune response and effectively inhibits tumor growth and metastasis, offering a robust strategy for cancer immunotherapy.

Intracellular DNA assembly for mRNA vaccine compartmentalization facilitates antigen expression and enhances adjuvant efficacy.

INTRODUCTION

Immunotherapy has gained great attention and rapid development for improved cancer treatment. Among which, a cancer vaccine that aims to initiate and amplify the antitumor immune responses by antigen-presenting cells (APCs), especially dendritic cells (DCs), offers a durable treatment option for patients with cancer. An in vitro–transcribed mRNA-based vaccine has emerged as a promising alternative for tumor immunotherapy in recent years owing to its superiorities in safety, personal design, and industrial production, as demonstrated by the clinical trials’ success of several candidates for melanoma and non–small cell lung cancer. For instance, BNT122 is one of the products generated by BioNTech-Genentech and demonstrated favorable efficacy and safety profile. However, until recently, concerns over the low immunogenicity caused by inefficient mRNA translation in cytoplasm (<1/10,000 mRNAs of the initial input) and weak immune stimulation (nucleoside-modified mRNA largely abrogates the innate immune responses) have limited their utility.

Efficient cytosolic delivery and translation of mRNA antigens for cross-presentation by APCs and strong stimulation of the immune system are essential for orchestrating robust tumor-specific immunity. It has always been the focus of scientists to use delivery systems [e.g., ionizable lipid nanoparticles (LNPs)] to increase cellular uptake and facilitate endo-lysosomal escape to maximize the availability of mRNA vaccines and the intrinsic adjuvant activity of the delivery systems is usually also a great consideration for improving the magnitude of adaptive immune responses. Researchers combined molecular adjuvant resiquimod (R848) and antigen-encoding mRNA to construct adjuvant-pulsed mRNA vaccines or rationally designed adjuvant lipidoid-substituted LNPs to orchestrate robust adaptive immunity. In addition, various responsive biomaterial-based delivery systems have been developed for precise control over the delivery and kinetics of mRNA release via responding to physiological stimuli and exogenous energetic stimuli, but still only 1 to 2% of RNAs were released from endosomes. Few studies focus on speeding up the translation efficiency of mRNA in cytoplasm, an important but neglected issue in mRNA delivery. For example, the half-life of released free mRNA from delivery systems is usually transient due to cellular mRNA degradation processes, limiting the yields of antigen protein expression. Thus, to improve the translation efficiency of limited cellular mRNA copies is essential not only for a higher protein expression but also for a derisking of off-targeting adverse.

Actually, modern biology considers that membraneless compartments (in sizes from 20 nm to 1 to 6 μm in diameter), which are driven by spontaneous self-assembly processes for spatial localization and up-concentration of reactants, play a crucial role in cellular functions. One potential function of these compartments is to organize RNA synthesis, expression, and silencing in different cellular regions. There is mounting evidence for the functional importance of RNA compartmentalization, suggesting that multiple steps of the RNA life cycle are organized within these compartments. Our previous work has demonstrated that a gene compartment could efficiently facilitate the gene expression due to the proximity effect and confinement effect. Therefore, we propose that mRNA compartmentalization will offer intriguing properties for cytosolic mRNA translation, which should be seriously considered.

DNA nanotechnology has been developed as a robust approach to fabricate an intelligent dynamic assembly system for intracellular compartments owing to the incomparable precise programmability and appealing responsiveness of DNA building blocks. Here, we rationally designed and constructed an mRNA compartmentalization–based cancer vaccine, comprising an artificial multimodule DNA nanostructure (MMDNS) assembly that integrated antigen protein coded-mRNA, Toll-like receptor 9 (TLR9) agonist cytosine–phosphate–guanosine motif (CpG) oligodeoxynucleotides (ODNs), acidic environment–responsive C-rich sequence, and DC-targeting aptamer, for achieving enhanced efficacy of mRNA vaccines mainly through the established compartment, which promoted lysosomal escape, TLR activation, and, afterward, pipelined mRNA translation in cytoplasm. The MMDNS in situ assembled into micro-sized DNA aggregate by responding to the acidic lysosomal environment and then escaped from lysosomes into cytoplasm, thus providing a moderate confined membrane-free compartment that played vitally important roles in localizing specific biological reactions for cytosolic mRNA expression. The mRNA compartmentalization was proposed to contribute to the pipelined work of relevant molecular machines, thus achieving efficient cytosolic translation of mRNA antigen. In addition, the micro-sized assembled aggregates enabled a stronger adjuvant effect of CpG ODNs, making up for the modification of mRNA-reduced innate immunogenicity. Our mRNA vaccine has been used for local injection, eliciting potent antigen-specific antitumor immunity, inducing strong effector and memory T cell responses, and mediating rejection of progressive tumors. Collectively, we demonstrate that the multimodule DNA assembly offers a moderate microenvironment for compartmentalized mRNA translation, which increases the immune efficacy of the mRNA vaccine. The ovalbumin (OVA) mRNA and CpG ODNs present an enhanced expression and a stronger immune stimulation, respectively, leading to an efficient cancer immunotherapy (Fig. 1). Likewise, we point out another key issue facing the future of mRNA vaccine, offering an insight into the design and development of mRNA therapeutics.

RESULTS

Molecular design

The MMDNS was cross-linked from a multimodule integrated DNA polymer via a double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) linker (Fig. 1A). The multimodule DNA polymer integrated mRNA anchoring sequence poly T, CpG ODNs, acidic environment–responsive C-rich sequence, and DC-targeting aptamers via two-step hybridization chain reaction (HCR) of three DNA hairpins. Specifically, a YY-shaped DNA nanostructure (DNS) was firstly constructed to serve as the initiator of HCR reaction (termed as YY-shaped initiator). The YY-shaped initiator consisted of two Y-shaped DNSs (termed as Y1 and Y2, respectively). Both the two Y-shaped DNSs were constructed via the self-assembly of three single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) which were partially complementary to each other (table S1). Two of the three ssDNA chains were overhang with an oligonucleotide-based DC-targeting aptamer sequence at their 3′ end (navy blue) for efficient targeting delivery to DCs. At the 3′ end of the third ssDNA, the sticky end was designed as initiator DNA sequence or its partially complementary chain (aquamarine). The two Y-shaped DNSs were assembled into a YY-shaped initiator by the base complementary of initiator DNA sequence and its partially complementary chain. The noncomplementary section of initiator DNA was the binding site of HC hairpin (light purple). HC (DNA hairpin that is attached with cytosine-rich sequence) and HT (DNA harpin that is attached with poly(T) sequence) hairpin monomers were featured with single-stranded toeholds at their 3′ (light purple region) and 5′ ends (light purple region), respectively, and the loop regions of HC and HT contained the complemental sequence of each other’s toeholds. The double-stranded stems of HC and HT were identical (aquamarine). The 5′ end of HC was elaborately designed as an acidic-responsive cytosine-rich sequence (5′-CCCCTAACCCC-3′) (red), and the 3′ end of HT was rationally designed as a poly(T) overhang (light orange) to hybrid with the poly(A) tail of target mRNA. Once the toeholds of HC bound to the binding site of the YY-shaped initiator, the first-round cascade reaction (F-HCR) was initiated, and HC and HT were opened in turn via toehold-mediated hybridization and strand invasion reaction, thereby alternately binding to each other to form a long DNA chain. To introduce CpG ODNs, a third harpin HCpG was designed. HCpG was same as HC except for that the 5′ end was designed as a CpG ODNs sequence (green). When the F-HCR was finished, HCpG and HT were then added to relay the cascade reaction (R-HCR), consequently assembling CpG ODNs into DNA chains. By above elaborately designing, the DNA polymer chain integrated mRNA anchoring module [poly(T) sequence], adjuvant module (CpG ODNs), acidic environment–responsive module (C-rich sequence), and DC-targeting module (DC aptamer). To form the mRNA-embedded MMDNS, target mRNA was first loaded on the DNA polymer chain through poly(A) tail and poly(T) hybridization, and then a dsDNA linker with two single-stranded poly(A) overhangs was added to cross-link the DNA polymer via hybridization with the unoccupied poly(T).

After being internalized into DC cells, MMDNS first entered the lysosome (Fig. 1B). In the acidic lysosome, cytosines within cytosine-rich sequences (5′-CCCCTAACCCC-3′) were hemiprotonated and formed intermolecular C:C+ base pairs. Four intercalated C:C+ base pairs further formed stable i-motif DNA structures between MMDNS, leading to the in situ assembly of MMDNS and formation of DNA aggregates. Meanwhile, the CpG ODNs bound to TLR9, which is located on the intralysosomal membrane, and promoted the tremendous activation of DCs via TLR9-mediated cellular response, leading to DC maturation and secretion of cytokines. The large-sized aggregate was proposed to enable CpG ONDs to induce a stronger immune activation (Fig. 1C), owing to the proven fact that large-sized adjuvants would produce stronger and more lasting immune responses. Afterward, the DNA aggregate escaped from the lysosome, providing moderate confined membrane-free compartment for mRNA expression. In the compartment region, the spatially proximal at nanoscale of mRNA transcripts was proposed to increase the likelihood of additional rounds of relevant molecular machine for translation and thereby promoted the target mRNA translation and elicited a robust antigen-specific presentation in DCs (Fig. 1D).

Preparation of YY-shaped initiator, dsDNA linker, and DNA polymer

The successful synthesis of the YY-shaped initiator and the dsDNA linker was verified by native agarose gel electrophoresis (fig. S1). To optimize the synthesis of multimodule integrated DNA polymer, the YY-shaped initiator initiating F-HCR and R-HCR was explored by tuning the molar ratio of the initiator and the two monomer hairpins from 0.02:1:1, 0.05:1:1, and 0.1:1:1 to 0.2:1:1. The F-HCR firstly proceeded for 18 hours at room temperature, and then the R-HCR proceeded for an additional 24 hours. As a control, F-HCRs for 42 hours were simultaneously carried. The agarose gel electrophoresis (1.5%, w/w) of the polymerization products shows smear bands with low mobility, especially when the molar ratio of the initiator and harpins was 0.05:1:1. Furthermore, R-HCR products present higher molecular weight compared with that of F-HCR products, indicating the successful assembly of HCpG (fig. S2). The HCR products were then ultrafiltered with 100-kDa ultrafilter to remove the free DNA hairpins to avoid the possible immune-toxicity. The 1.5% agarose gel electrophoresis result of the obtained DNA polymers shows that the band of the free DNA hairpins is disappeared after ultrafiltration, indicating the successful removal of free DNA hairpins (Fig. 2A).

Construction of MMDNS

MMDNS embedded with mRNA was prepared via attaching model mRNA encoded with enhanced green fluorescent protein (EGFP mRNA) to the DNA polymer through poly(A) and poly(T) hybridization, especially the molar ratio of poly(T) within DNA polymer to mRNA is 25:3. The dsDNA linker was then added to cross-link the complexes and form an mRNA-embedded MMDNS through poly(A) and poly(T) hybridization. By increasing the addition amount of dsDNA linker, the molar ratio of poly(T) within DNA polymer and dsDNA linker was varied from 25:2.5, 25:3.3, 25:5, and 25:7.5 to 25:10. The cross-linked complexes were first characterized by 1.5% agarose gel electrophoresis (Fig. 2A). The result shows that the bands of free mRNA were substantially disappeared, indicating that almost all the mRNAs have bound to DNA polymer. In addition, the cross-linked complexes were trapped in the well with the addition of the dsDNA linker, implying the formation of mRNA-embedded MMDNS. The morphology of the MMDNS was further characterized by atomic force microscopy (AFM) (Fig. 2B and fig. S3) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Fig. 2C and fig. S4). AFM and TEM images show that the cross-linked DNA complexes exhibited spherical shapes with different sizes after being cross-linked by different amounts of dsDNA linker. In particular, MMDNS assembled with poly(T):dsDNA linker = 25:7.5 exhibited a relatively uniform spherical shape and 254.58 ± 25.90 nm in an average diameter (Fig. 2D). The dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis showed that the hydration diameter of the uniform nanogel was 266.8 nm (Fig. 2H), and its zeta potential was −16.43 ± 1.27 mV when dispersed into enzyme-free water (Fig. 2E and fig. S5A). Therefore, mRNA-embedded MMDNS assembled with poly(T):dsDNA linker = 25:7.5 was chosen for the subsequent in vitro and in vivo experiments.

To validate the pH-responsive assembly of achieved MMDNS mediated by C-rich sequence, we first confirm the pH-responsive performance of HC by circular dichroism (CD) spectra analysis (fig. S6). When the pH value of the solution of HC was adjusted from 7.4 to 5.0, the positive peak at ~275 nm red shifted to ~283 nm, indicating the successful formation of i-motif structures. We therefore proposed that, in acidic mediums, MMDNSs would assemble by forming stable i-motif DNA structures between MMDNSs (Fig. 2F). The CD spectra of MMDNS showed a similar positive peak red-shift (from ~275 to ~280 nm) when the pH value was adjusted from 7.4 to 5.0, confirming the formation of i-motif structures (Fig. 2G). In addition, more than one positive peak was observed in the CD spectra, which proposed the characteristic peak of hairpins (harpins within HC and DC aptamers within MMDNS). The DLS analysis showed that the hydration diameter of the assembled product increased to ~838.3 nm (Fig. 2H), and the zeta potential was −11.6 ± 0.56 mV (Fig. 2E and fig. S5B). TEM analysis was further adopted to reveal the morphologies of the assembled product (Fig. 2, I and J). TEM images demonstrated the aggregation of MMDNS at pH 5.0, indicating the successful assembly of MMDNS. Moreover, the formed aggregate did not completely disassemble when the pH value was adjusted back to 7.4 (fig. S7), which might be due to the large numbers of formed i-motif structures within the assembled aggregates. To further confirm that the assembly was mediated by a C-rich sequence within MMDNS, DNS that does not contain C-rich sequences was synthesized by the same process (termed as nonC-DNS) (fig. S8A). TEM analysis showed that the products exhibited uniform DNS (fig. S8B). When the pH was reduced from 7.4 to 5.0, nonC-DNSs were sill dispersed and presented a little disintegrated (fig. S8C), which was different from the case of MMDNS. Together, these results suggested that, in acidic environments, MMDNSs were assembled via the formation of i-motif structures mediated by acidic environment–responsive C-rich sequences.

mRNA compartment formation via MMDNS assembly

We then delivered the achieved MMDNS into living cells to explore the acidic lysosomal environment-triggered MMDNS assembly. DC2.4 cells were chosen as a model DC for this study. Before that, we firstly verified the physiological stability of MMDNS. MMDNS was incubated in culture medium [10% fetal bovine serum (FBS)] for different times, and agarose gel electrophoresis results showed that DNA component retained in the loading until 10 hours, indicating that MMDNS resisted enzymatic hydrolysis in serum (fig. S9). A cell viability test was performed to explore the influence of MMDNS on cells. DC2.4 cells were incubated with nonC-DNS and MMDNS for 48 hours, respectively, and the cell viability was evaluated using cell counting kit-8 (CCK-8) assay. The results showed high cell viability in both groups (fig. S10), indicating that MMDNS had minimal impact on the cellular viability. We proposed that, in lysosomal acidic environment, MMDNS would assemble owing to the formation of i-motif structure, and the assembled aggregate escaped into cytoplasm, providing a compartment for mRNA (Fig. 3A). DC2.4 cells were treated by Cyanine5 (Cy5)-labeled MMDNS and Cy5-labeled nonC-DNS, respectively, for 6 and 12 hours. Cellular lysosomes were simultaneously stained with Green LysoTracker. The significant red signals were observed in all treated cells via laser scanning confocal microscopy (LSCM), suggesting efficient cellular uptake of both MMDNS and nonC-DNS (Fig. 3, B and C). In addition, at 6 hours, most intracellular red fluorescence signals (indicating DNS) were overlaid with green fluorescence signals (indicating lysosomes) in the two types of DNS-treated cells, suggesting that DNSs were mainly trapped into acidic lysosomes after cellular internalization (Fig. 3B). Notably, obvious different distribution patterns and red signal intensities of nonC-DNSs and MMDNSs were observed in cells at 6 hours: The red signals in MMDNS-treated cells presented as bigger aggregates compared with that of nonC-DNS, and a corresponding fluorescence signal quantitative analysis showed that the bigger aggregates in MMDNS-treated cells presented stronger fluorescence intensity than that in the nonC-DNS–treated cells, indicating the assembly of MMDNS in lysosomes (Fig. 3B). When the incubation time was prolonged to 12 hours, signals of Cy5 and LysoTracker partially separated in nonC-DNS–treated cells while almost separated in MMDNS-treated cells, indicating that DNSs escaped from lysosome into cytoplasm (Fig. 3C). Moreover, the nonC-DNS that escaped from lysosome showed dispersive distribution in cytoplasm, while the escaped aggregates in MMDNS-treated cells still presented aggregative distribution, indicating that the formed assembles maintained and did not disassemble in cytoplasm (Fig. 3C). We proposed that the aggregated pattern and a mass of i-motif structures within the aggregates to some extent restrict the pH-responsive disassembly of the aggregates in neutral cytoplasm. Corresponding Pearson’s correlation coefficients at 6 and 12 hours were quantified, which further confirmed the stronger lysosomal escaping capability of MMDNS (Fig. 3D). We proposed that, in the acidic medium, C-rich sequences within MMDNS absorbed protons and led to the decrease of H proton amount, resulting in MMDNS escaping lysosome via proton sponge effect. The results of bio-TEM further verified the assembly of MMDNS in lysosome (Fig. 3, E and F): In the nonC-DNS–treated cells for 6 hours, single or several scattered nonC-DNSs were encapsulated in lysosomes (Fig. 3E); while in the MMDNS-treated cells, a larger bulk was observed in lysosome, indicating the acidic-responsive assembly of MMDNS (Fig. 3F). Moreover, bulk assembly was simultaneously observed in the cytoplasm, which further confirmed the stronger lysosomal escaping capability of the assembled aggregate. Together, these results suggested that MMDNS could assemble in acidic lysosomes via the assembly of C-rich sequences, which further promoted their lysosomal escape and, subsequently, formation of cytoplasmatic assembled architecture.

The lysosomal escaped DNA aggregate was proposed to provide the medium for the mRNA compartmentalization in cytoplasm. To verify our speculation, 5-Carboxyfluorescein (FAM)-labeled nonC-DNS and MMDNS those embedding Cy5-mRNA were incubated with DC2.4 cells for 10 hours. Different distribution behaviors of materials were observed from the LSCM images (Fig. 3G): In nonC-DNS–treated cells, DNA component (green fluorescence signal) and mRNA (red fluorescence signal) both present a dispersive distribution throughout the cells, and mRNAs were partially colocalized with DNA component, indicating the partial release of mRNA from nonC-DNS; while in MMDNS-treated cells, DNA component and mRNA were colocalized, and both presented as micro-sized aggregates, indicating that the assembly of MMDNS confined the mRNA to the nonmembranous architecture and consequently provided compartment for mRNA. Corresponding Pearson’s correlation coefficients further confirmed the stronger correlation of DNA component and mRNA in MMDNS-treated cells compared with that of the nonC-DNS group (Fig. 3H). Therefore, it was proposed that the DNA aggregate architecture would prevent mRNA from releasing from the compartment.

mRNA expression in compartment

We firstly verify the feasibility of MMDNS as an mRNA transfection carrier. EGFP mRNA was used as the model mRNA and embedded into MMDNS. First, DC2.4 cells were incubated with EGFP mRNA–embedded MMDNS for 48 hours. Free EGFP mRNA–treated cells were set as the control group. LSCM analysis showed that green fluorescence signals were observed in both group cells, indicating the successful expression of EGFP (Fig. 4A). In particular, as the results showed, cells treated with free mRNA exhibited extremely weak green fluorescence signals, while cells transfected with MMDNS showed strong green fluorescence. Flow cytometry analysis further revealed that there were about 6% of EGFP-expressing cells in all counted free EGFP mRNA–treated cells and 56.1% of EGFP-expressing cells in all counted MMDNS-treated cells (Fig. 4B). The LSCM analysis and flow cytometry analysis reveal the significant mRNA transfection efficiency of MMDNS.

We hypothesized that the DNA aggregate provided compartment for mRNA expression, in which the relevant molecular machines of translation were localized in the compartment region to execute specific biological reactions for mRNA expression, and the expressed EGFPs then diffused away from the compartment region (Fig. 4C). To verify the distribution behavior of the relevant molecular machines, eukaryotic elongation factor-2 (eEF2) that is indispensable during the elongation stage of mRNA translation was chosen as the representative to be visualized via immunofluorescence staining (Fig. 4D). In the nonC-DNS–treated group, most eEF2 (red signal) was separated from non-DNS (green signal); while in the MMDNS-treated group, eEF2 (red signal) presented bulk and largely overlapped with MMDNS (green signal), indicating that eEF2 was localized within the compartment regions. Corresponding Pearson’s correlation coefficients and overlap coefficients of red fluorescence signal (indicating eEF2) and green fluorescence signal (indicating DNS) were quantified, which further confirmed the localization of eEF2 in the compartment regions in the MMDNS-treated group (Fig. 4, E and F). All those results demonstrated that relevant molecular machines of translation were localized in the compartment region that formed via MMDNS assembly. To further monitor the expressed protein distribution behavior, DC2.4 cells were incubated with nonC-DNS and MMDNS, those both were embedded with EGFP mRNA for 36 hours. LSCM results revealed that, in the nonC-DNS–treated cell, the EGFP (green signal) was almost outside the DNS region (red signal). It was proposed that parts of mRNA released from nonC-DNS (Fig. 4G), and then EGFP expression occurred in cytoplasm. In comparison, the EGFP signal (green) in MMDNS-treated cells was stronger and mainly located in compartment region (red signal), and some EGFPs diffused away from the compartment region and existed in cytoplasm, indicating that the EGFP expression was increased and executed in the compartment region. The high sensitivity structured illumination microscope (HIS-SIM) image of the MMDNS-treated cell further confirmed that the EGFP signal (green) was mainly located in compartment region (red signal). All of those results demonstrated that the DNA aggregate provided the compartmentation region for mRNA translation.

In the compartment, the spatially proximal at nanoscale of mRNA transcripts was proposed to enable the relevant substrates and proteins of translation to shuttle efficiently among the mRNA transcripts and thereby enhanced protein production. To further investigate the compartment effect on mRNA expression, and simultaneously study the influence of the other two integrated functional modules (CpG ODNs and DC aptamer) on the mRNA expression, the other three control DNS formulations were constructed: nonC-DNS, DNS without DC-targeting aptamer (nonT-DNS), and DNS without CpG ODNs (nonCpG-DNS) (fig. S11). Bone marrow–derived dendritic cells (BMDCs) were next isolated from the femurs and tibiae of BALB/c female mice and incubated for 48 hours with equivalent amounts of EGFP mRNA carried by the four DNS formulations (nonC-DNS, nonT-DNS, nCpG-DNS, and MMDNS). LSCM results showed that nonC-DNS–treated cells present relatively weaker green fluorescence signal compared to the other three groups (Fig. 4H). Quantitative analysis of the fluorescence intensity revealed about 1.6-fold higher fluorescence signals in MMDNS-treated cells than that of nonC-DNS–treated cells, indicating that mRNA compartment formulate could efficiently facilitate mRNA expression. Moreover, the nonCpG-DNS–treated group was found exhibiting a comparable strong green fluorescence intensity with the MMDNS-treated group, which demonstrated that CpG ODN in DNS did not influence the efficiency of mRNA expression. The nonT-DNS group presented slightly weaker fluorescence intensity in comparison with the MMDNS-treated group, revealing that, on cell level, DC-targeting aptamer did not exhibit obvious superiority for the protein expression. We proposed that, when the incubation time was long enough for DNS internalization, the general mechanisms of internalization for nanoscale particles weakened the aptamer-directed endocytosis superiority at the cell level, and the additional cellular uptake results verified our speculation (fig. S12). Therefore, as the cellular uptake results revealed, the superiority of the aptamer-targeting embodied that it enabled the target cells to absorb DNS quickly and vastly in a short time. To further evaluate the advantage of compartment effect provided by MMDNS assembly, we used LNP as a positive delivery system. To confirm the compartment effect on mRNA expression, the EGFP production was measured at an equal uptake amount of EGFP mRNA using both MMDNS and LNP delivery systems. The results show that the MMDNS-EGFP mRNA–treated group presents a significantly stronger green fluorescence signal compared to the LNP-EGFP mRNA–treated group, indicating that, in comparison with LNP, MMDNS could efficiently facilitate mRNA expression though providing an mRNA compartment via intracellular assembly (fig. S13).

In vitro APC activation mediated by MMDNS

We next embedded model antigen OVA mRNA into MMDNS and then delivered it to BMDCs to test the effect of intracellular MMDNS assembly for mRNA compartmentalization on APC activation and antigen-specific presentation. We proposed that, in the lysosomes, acidic environment triggered the MMDNS assembly, and CpG ODNs within MMDNS promoted the activation of BMDCs via TLR9-mediated cellular responses. The assembled aggregate escaped from lysosome and provided compartment for mRNA expression in cytoplasm, which facilitated the OVA expression and OVA-specific presentation (Fig. 5A). BMDCs were incubated with free OVA mRNA and GpG ODNs physical mixture (OVA mRNA + CpG ODNs, ii), nonCpG-DNS-OVA mRNA (iii), nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (iv), MMDNS-OVA mRNA (v), and MMDNS (vi) for 48 hours, respectively. Untreated cells were set as the control group (i). We first tested the BMDC activation of CpG ODNs which was manifested by up-regulated expression of major histocompatibility complex class II (MHC-II) and CD80/CD86 and enhanced cytokine secretion including interleukin-6 (IL-6) and IL-12 and cytokine tumor necrosis factor–α (TNF-α). By flow cytometry analysis, we observed substantial expression of MHC-II in nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (iv)–, MMDNS-OVA mRNA (v)–, and MMDNS (vi)–treated groups compared with that of OVA mRNA + CpG ODNs (ii) and nonCpG-DNS-OVA mRNA (iii)–treated groups, and slightly higher expression was shown in the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (Fig. 5B and fig. S14). In addition, significantly greater expression of the costimulatory molecules CD80/86 in MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group was observed compared with that of the other four formulations (Fig. 5C and fig. S15), suggesting that the intracellular MMDNS assembly promoted BMDC maturation. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) analysis shows that TNF-α (Fig. 5D) and interleukins (IL-6 and IL-12) (Fig. 5, E and F) secreted from BMDCs treated with the MMDNS-OVA mRNA were markedly increased compared with that of the nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA–treated group. The above results indicate that the assembly of MMDNS enhanced the effect of the CpG ODNs for BMDC activation. Flow cytometry analysis for OVA peptide–specific presentation was further carried out. The results showed that nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA– and MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated groups presented a significant enrichment of OVA peptide SIINFEKL-H2Kb+ complex on BMDC membranes in comparison with the other three groups, and particularly, the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group exhibited a higher SIINFEKL-H2Kb+ presentation (Fig. 5, G and H, and fig. S16). All the results imply that the intracellular assembly of MMDNS in lysosome facilitated APC activation and antigen-specific presentation.

In vivo lymph node targeting and the antigen-specific CTL response

We then vaccinated intramuscularly C57BL/6N female mice with MMDNS-OVA mRNA to assess the antigen-specific cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) responses (Fig. 6A). In vivo lymphatic delivery was firstly evaluated by Cy5-labeled DNS. Cy5-labeled nonT-DNS (the DNS without DC aptamer) and MMDNS were intramuscularly injected into the right leg of BALB/c female mice. The axillary and inguinal lymph nodes and major organs were harvested at 6 hours postinjection for imaging (Fig. 6B and fig. S17). The results showed that the DNSs were accumulated in the axillary (AX) and inguinal (IN) lymph nodes at the injection side. Quantitative analysis revealed that the MMDNS accumulated in the lymph nodes ~2 times more efficiently than nonT-DNS (Fig. 6C), indicating that the DC-targeting aptamer promoted the in vivo delivery of the MMDNS to lymph nodes. The immunofluorescence analysis of lymph nodes revealed that both types of DNSs were distributed in DCs within lymph nodes, and the MMDNS-treated group exhibited a stronger fluorescence signal, further verifying the enhanced accumulation of MMDNS mediated by DC-targeting aptamer (Fig. 6D and fig. S18). Moreover, the liver and kidneys of two types of DNS-treated mice present a clear signal, indicating the clearance of DNS in vivo via liver and kidneys (fig. S17).

To investigate the effect of compartment constructed via MMDNS assembly on antigen-specific CTL responses in lymph nodes, C57BL/6N female mice were intramuscularly vaccinated with nonC-DNS (III) and MMDNS (IV) formulations embedding equivalent doses of OVA mRNA and CpG ODNs. The untreated group (I) and the equivalent doses of free OVA mRNA and CpG ODN–treated group (OVA mRNA + CpG ODNs, II) were simultaneously carried. After 7-days postvaccination by a single dose, the axillary and inguinal lymph nodes at the injection side were collected for the APC activation analysis. An increased amount of CD80/86-positive DCs in MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group was observed in comparison with the other three groups (Fig. 6E and fig. S19), indicating the efficient DC activation. The percentage of OVA peptide (SIINFEKL)-presenting DCs were significantly increased in MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group over those of the other groups (Fig. 6F and fig. S20), revealing the enhanced OVA-specific presentation in the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group. The induction of CTL response was further evaluated in vivo. After one-dose vaccination, we simultaneously collected the peripheral blood from the vaccinated animals and analyzed the interferon-γ–positive (IFN-γ+)–secreting CD8+ T lymphocyte (CD3+CD8+IFN-γ+). In particular, the peripheral blood samples were firstly ex vivo restimulated with OVA peptide, and then flow cytometry analysis was used. As shown in Fig. 6G and fig. S21, the cells from MMDNS-OVA mRNA–vaccinated mice presented an increased percentage of IFN-γ+ CTLs compared with those of nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA formulation and the physical mixture treated groups, demonstrating the stronger CTL response caused by MMDNS-OVA mRNA. In addition, we further investigated the antigen-specific CTL responses caused by MMDNS that is not embedded OVA mRNA. The results showed that MMDNS-treated group presented an increased amount of CD80/86-positive DCs owing to the immune activation effect of CpG ODNs within MMDNS, which was comparable with that of MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (fig. S22A). However, the percentage of SIINFEKL-presenting DCs and IFN-γ+–secreting CD8+ T lymphocyte in peripheral blood did not obviously increase in comparison with the control group, demonstrating that MMDNS did not cause the OVA-specific CTL responses due to the absence of OVA mRNA (fig. S22, B and C). Moreover, we simultaneously used the LNP as the positive delivery system of OVA mRNA. In comparison with the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group, LNP-OVA mRNA–treated group presented a slightly lower amount of CD80/86-positive DCs and a comparable percentage of SIINFEKL-presenting DCs in draining lymph nodes (dLNs) (fig. S22, A and B). The percentage of IFN-γ+–secreting CD8+ T lymphocyte in the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group was slightly higher than that of LNP-OVA mRNA–treated group (fig. S22C), demonstrating a stronger specific CTL response caused by MMDNS-OVA mRNA. Dose-response studies demonstrated that mice vaccinated with three different doses (5, 10, and 15 μg per mouse) showed a comparable antigen-specific immune activation effect, and the percentage of IFN-γ+–secreting CD8+ T lymphocyte in peripheral blood was slightly enhanced with increasing the immunizing dose (fig. S23). The safety of MMDNS-OVA mRNA with different immunizing dosage was evaluated by measuring the levels of immune cells in peripheral blood (fig. S24) and analyzing the hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) images of the major organs (fig. S25). The results indicated that there was no obvious increase of white blood cells, lymphocytes, monocytes, and granulocytes in peripheral blood and inflammation infiltration in major organs, demonstrating the negligible immune-related toxicities and vaccine component–related toxicities of MMDNS-OVA mRNA vaccines at the efficient immunizing dosages.

Potential antitumor effects of MMDNS

We next investigated the tumor inhibition capability of mRNA compartmentalization–based vaccines with a B16-OVA melanoma model. The C57BL/6N female mice were intramuscularly vaccinated with different formulations of DNS embedding equivalent doses of OVA mRNA and CpG ODNs, including nonC-DNS (III) and MMDNS (IV), or the physical mixture of OVA mRNA and CpG ODNs (II) on days 5 and 12 post-inoculation [subcutaneous (sc)] of B16-OVA tumor cells (Fig. 7A). The unvaccinated group was set as the control (I). The immunotherapeutic efficacy was assessed by measuring the tumor growth. The results show that the physical mixture (II) had a limit inhibition for tumor growth in comparison with the control group (I), and the nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA could, to some extent, inhibit the growth of the tumors (Fig. 7B). Notably, the MMDNS-OVA mRNA presented an excellent inhibition for tumor growth, which was proposed owing to the lymph node targeting delivery and efficient OVA-specific CTL response. In addition, the body weights of mice were monitored and did not present obvious change during the 22 days of monitoring (fig. S26), suggesting the superior biocompatibility of the DNS. On day 22, the tumors were harvested, and the size and weight were shown in Fig. 7 (C and D). As the results showed, the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group exhibited a significant decrease in both size and weight compared with those of the nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA–treated group, indicating that the MMDNS assembly providing compartment facilitated the antitumor efficiency of MMDNS-OVA mRNA. The immunofluorescence analysis revealed increased CD3+ T lymphocyte infiltration and CD8+ T lymphocyte infiltration in tumors of the two types of DNS-treated groups (III and IV) compared with the physical mixture group (II), and the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (IV) exhibited more CD8+ T lymphocyte infiltration (Fig. 7, E to G). H&E images of the tumors revealed extensive necrosis in the tumors of the nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (III) and MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (IV), and MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (IV) presents more severe necrosis (Fig. 7H). All of above results demonstrated that the MMDNS-OVA mRNA had an efficient in vivo tumor immunotherapy by inducing an enhanced CTL response. Moreover, H&E images demonstrated that there was no obvious inflammation infiltration in major organs collected from mice in the treated groups, demonstrating the excellent biocompatibility of DNS formulation (fig. S27). We further demonstrated that MMDNS that is not embedded OVA mRNA presented a limited antitumor efficacy compared with the MMDNS-OVA mRNA (fig. S28). Now, integration of mRNA vaccines with immune checkpoint inhibitors has been proven to amplify the effectiveness of the immune response efficiently, which represents a cutting-edge strategy in the field of cancer immunotherapy. Therefore, we further evaluated the antitumor effect by combining MMDNS-based mRNA vaccines with anti–programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) antibody (αPD-L1). The results demonstrated an efficiently enhanced antitumor effect (fig. S28), indicating the potential application of the MMDNS-based mRNA vaccine in the future clinical research.

Inhibition of tumor metastasis and recurrence elicited by MMDNS vaccination

We next evaluated the inhibition efficacy of the mRNA compartmentalization–based vaccine for lung metastasis. The C57BL/6N female mice were first intramuscularly vaccinated with different formulations on day 0, 7, and 14, including the physical mixture of OVA mRNA and CpG ODNs (II), nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III), and MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV), and then intravenously injected B16-OVA tumor cells (5 × 105 cells per mouse) on day 24 (Fig. 8A). The unvaccinated group set as the control (I). The tumor metastasis on the lung was evaluated on day 50. The images of the lungs showed that nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III)– and MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV)–treated groups presented obvious decreased lung metastasis (Fig. 8B). The number of metastatic nodules in the two types of DNS formulation–treated groups was largely less than that in control group (I) and the OVA mRNA + CpG ODN–treated group (II), and the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group had the least metastatic nodules, indicating the significant antimetastasis effect of MMDNS-OVA mRNA (Fig. 8C). H&E staining of lungs demonstrated the intensive metastases in the control group (I) and the OVA mRNA + CpG ODN–treated group (II). In the two types of DNS-treated groups (III and IV), the relatively slight metastases were observed, and the MMDNS-OVA mRNA showed more efficient prevention for the formation of metastasis (Fig. 8D), confirming the antimetastatic potential of MMDNS-OVA mRNA. In addition, the immune activation level of spleens was further analyzed after being ex vivo restimulated with OVA peptide. Flow cytometry analysis and enzyme-linked immunospot (ELISpot) assay of splenocytes showed that MMDNS-OVA mRNA elicited an obvious enhanced proportion of IFN-γ+ CTLs (CD3+CD8+IFN-γ+) (Fig. 8E and fig. S29) and enhanced IFN-γ secretion (Fig. 8F), suggesting that MMDNS-OVA mRNA induced a strong adaptive immune response. Moreover, the proportions of CD8+ effector memory T cells (TEM; CD3+CD8+CD44HighCD62LLow) and CD4+ TEM cells (CD3+CD4+CD44HighCD62LLow) were all increased in the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (Fig. 8, G and H, and figs. S30 and S31), demonstrating that MMDNS-OVA mRNA stimulated a strong immune memory effect, which proposed to be responsible for the inhibited tumor metastasis. The survival curve showed that all mice in control group (I) and OVA mRNA + CpG ODN–treated group (II) died within 28 and 34 days after intravenous injection of tumor cells, respectively. Mice receiving MMDNS-OVA mRNA showed the most durable therapeutic efficacy with 50% of mice surviving over 35 days in comparison with those of nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (III) (25%) (Fig. 8I). Collectively, these above results suggested that MMDNS-OVA mRNA could trigger a strong T cell–mediated antitumor immunity for efficient inhibition of tumor metastasis and an obvious increase of mouse survival.

To further test the immune memory effect, the mice that were intravenously injected B16-OVA tumor cells were subcutaneously rechallenged with B16-OVA cells on day 45. To be specific, C57BL/6N female mice were first intramuscularly vaccinated with MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV) on days 0, 7, and 14 and then intravenously challenged with B16-OVA tumor cells (2 × 105 cells per mouse) on day 24. The B16-OVA tumor cells (2 × 105 cells per mouse) were subcutaneously injected on day 45 for rechallenge (Fig. 8J). The unvaccinated mice were set as the control group (I). In the control group (I), three of six mice died before the rechallenge tumor growing up, two of the three surviving mice present obvious tumor growth (Fig. 8K). In construct, MMDNS-OVA mRNA protected five of six mice from the B16-OVA cell rechallenge, and tumor growth in the MMDNS-OVA mRNA–treated group (IV) were inhibited in comparison with that in control group (I), indicating the efficient antigen-specific immune memory for tumor killing (Fig. 8, K and L).

DISCUSSION

Here, we pointed out a factor of mRNA vaccines and developed a strategy for in situ constructing a membrane-free compartment in cytoplasm via acidic lysosomal environment-induced MMDNS assembly, endowing mRNA vaccines with enhanced antigen expression and TLR agonistic activity, which significantly boosted the innate and adaptive immunity with good tolerability in mice. The MMDNS was precisely constructed with a DNA HCR-based assembly strategy and thus spatiotemporally integrated antigen-coded mRNA, CpG ODNs, DC aptamer, and acidic environment–responsive C-rich sequence. The MDDNS performed pH-responsive assembly to build a micro-sized aggregate by forming i-motif structure, resulting in enhanced CpG ODN adjuvant efficacy for APC activation and maturation. The micro-sized aggregate then escaped into cytoplasm and subsequently provided moderate confined compartments for the carried mRNA. Afterward, the established compartment provided a spatially localized place for the relevant molecular machines of translation, which was proposed to execute highly efficient pipelined mRNA translation by shuttling back and forth, thus improving the expression efficiency of mRNA. We showed the great potential of MMDNS-based mRNA compartmentalization in enhancing mRNA vaccine–based cancer immunotherapy. By carrying the pattern antigen OVA mRNA, MMDNS facilitated the robust OVA-specific presentation in APCs and in vivo induced a strong tumor-specific CTL response, eliciting efficient inhibition for tumor growth and lung metastasis.

In this sense, our MMDNS dynamic system reveals that construction of cytoplastic compartment for mRNA expression would be a promising strategy for enhancing mRNA expression efficiency and its intrinsic adjuvant activity, facilitating an efficient tumor immunotherapy. The future versions of this strategy might afford to develop a more universal platform (e.g., nanorobot or nanomachine) for different mRNA, and notably, the moderate mechanical strength of the medium for mRNA compartment should be important for the efficient biochemical reaction.

Synthesis of YY-shaped initiator and dsDNA linker

The YY-shaped initiator and the dsDNA linker were synthesized in 80 mM NaCl by mixing corresponding oligonucleotide strands (table S1). To be specific, for the YY-shaped initiator, 10 μl of Ya-C1 (100 μM), 10 μl of Ya-C2 (100 μM), 20 μl of Yb-Ap (100 μM), and 20 μl of Yc-Ap (100 μM) were diluted in 80 mM NaCl aqueous solution to the total volume of 200 μl. The mixture was heated to 95°C and then gradually annealed to 20°C. For dsDNA linker, 10 μl of Linker-a (100 μM) and 10 μl of Linker-b (100 μM) were diluted in 80 mM NaCl aqueous solution to the total volume of 100 μl with a final concentration of 10 μM for both oligonucleotide strands. The mixture was heated to 95°C and then gradually annealed to 20°C. The final concentration of synthesized dsDNA linker was 10 μM.

Preparation of hairpin structures

All DNA hairpin chains, were, respectively added into 1× TAE-Mg2+ buffer (40 mM tris, 2 mM EDTA·Na·H2O, 20 mM acetic acid, and 12.5 mM MgCl2) with a final concentration of 50 μM for oligonucleotide strands. The mixtures were heated to 95°C for 5 min, annealed to 35°C with a rate of 1 min/°C, and then kept at 35°C for 2 min to obtain DNA hairpin structures (HT, HC, and HCpG).

Preparation of DNA polymers

DNA polymers were prepared by HCR. Different molar concentrations of the YY-shaped initiator (0.04, 0.1, 0.2, and 0.4 μM) were mixed with stoichiometric amounts of HC and HT with a final concentration of 2 μM at 25°C for 18 hours, and then stoichiometric amounts of HCpG and HT were added and incubated for additional 24 hours for R-HCR. For the typical DNA polymer, the molar concentration of YY-shaped initiator was 0.1 μM. The products were then ultrafiltered with a 100-kDa ultrafilter to remove the free H structures to avoid the possible immune-toxicity and simultaneously concentrate the DNA polymers.

Preparation of MMDNS

Typically, EGFP mRNA–embedded MMDNS were prepared as follows: mixing 0.5 μl of mRNA (1 mg/ml) with 5 μl of DNA polymer (the concentration of poly(T) was 5 μM) in 1× TAE-Mg2+ buffer, and then the mixture was incubated at 25°C for 3 hours, followed by the addition of 0.75 μl of dsDNA linker (10 μM) and incubation for an additional 5 hours at 25°C. OVA mRNA–embedded MMDNS was prepared as follows: mixing 10 μl of mRNA (1 mg/ml) with 64 μl of DNA polymer (the concentration of poly(T) was 5 μM) in 1× TAE-Mg2+ buffer, and then the mixture was incubated at 25°C for 3 hours, followed by the addition of 9.6 μl of dsDNA linker (10 μM) and incubation for additional 5 hours at 25°C.

TEM and AFM

Morphologies of DNS were characterized using TEM and AFM. DNSs were coated onto carbon-shadowed, formvar 246 coated grids. The samples were stained with 2% phosphotungstic acid (pH 7.4), and the images were collected by a lanthanum hxaboride transmission electron microscope (Tecnai G2 20 S-TWIN). The diameter distribution range of DNS was analyzed using ImageJ software. AFM was performed using a Bruker Multimode-8 AFM microscope in Scanasyst mode.

DLS measurement

The synthesized MMDNS or the MMDNS assembly was dispersed in enzyme-free water with 50 nM poly T, and the hydration diameter distribution and zeta potential of were characterized by Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern Instruments, Malvern, UK).

CD spectra measurement

The HC was diluted to 5 μM, and nonC-DNS or MMDNS was diluted to keep the concentration of C-rich sequence at 2.5 μM. All samples dispersed in both neutral (pH7.4) and acidic (pH 5.0) 1× TAE-Mg2+ buffer, respectively. Thirty-microliter samples were loaded into circular cuvette. The absorbance values of the samples were measured at wavelength range from 220 to 320 nm by Circular Dichroism Chiroptical Spectrometer J-1500 (Jasco, Japan) at room temperature.

Cell culture

The DC2.4 cells were routinely cultured with RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% (v/v) inactivated FBS, 0.0004% (v/v) β-mercaptoethanol, 1% (v/v) Hepes buffer, penicillin (100 U/ml), streptomycin (100 mg/ml), and gentamycin sulfate (50 mg/ml) with the presence of 5% CO2 at 37°C.

Murine BMDCs were isolated from femurs and tibiae of BALB/c female mice and maintained in RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% inactivated FBS, penicillin (100 U/ml), and streptomycin (100 U/ml) at 37°C in 5% CO2. Recombinant mouse Granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (20 ng/ml) and IL-4 (10 ng/ml) were added to induce cell differentiation. The medium was half-replaced every 3 days. On the 10th day of culture, the medium was gently pipetted. Immature BMDCs that were suspended in the medium and loosely adhered to the flask were all collected for further use.

Cytotoxicity test

The standard CCK-8 method was performed to assess the cytotoxicity of nonC-DNS and MMDNS. DC2.4 cells were seeded into a 96-well plate and incubated overnight. The nonC-DNS and MMDNS were respectively added to the plate wells with different final poly T concentrations (6.25, 12.5, 25, 50, and 100 nM) and incubated for 48 hours. Afterward, 90 μl of fresh medium and 10 μl of 10× CCK-8 solution were added to each well successively, and the samples were incubated for additional 4 hours. The absorbance of each well was measured at 450 nm using a BioTek Synergy/H1 microplate reader.

Cellular uptake and lysosome colocalization assay

For the cellular uptake test, DC2.4 cells were seeded into six-well confocal culture dish and cultured overnight to allow the cells to attach onto the glass bottom. Afterward, Cy5-labeled nonC-DNS and Cy5-labeled MMDNS were respectively added with poly T final concentration of 25 nM. After incubation for 6 and 12 hours, respectively, the cells were stained by LysoTracker Green for 1 hour and then washed by phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) buffer for three times. Last, fluorescence imaging was carried out on a laser scanning confocal microscope (UltraVIEW VoX, PerkinElmer).

Bio-TEM

For bio-TEM imaging, DC2.4 cells were firstly incubated with nonC-DNS or MMDNS for 6 hours. Cells were washed three times with PBS buffer, then harvested, and centrifuged at 1000 rpm min−1 for 5 min. After removing the supernatant, 2 ml of pure FBS was added to suspended cells. The cells were once again harvested via centrifugation at 1000 rpm min−1 for 5 min. Next, 1 ml of 2.5% glutaraldehyde was added slowly to infiltrate cells for 12 hours storage at 4°C. Next, the cells were infiltrated by pure resin. The infiltrated samples were placed in a mold and polymerized at 60°C for 24 hours and then cut into 70-nm ultrathin sections using an ultramicrotome. The ultrathin sections were placed on copper grids and stained with uranyl acetate (2% in ethanol) and lead citrate. TEM images of DC2.4 cells were observed using a 120-kV transmission electron microscope (Hitachi HT7700, Japan).

Intracellular DNS and mRNA colocalization assay

DC2.4 cells were seeded into six-well confocal culture dish and cultured overnight to allow the cells to attach onto the glass bottom. Afterward, FAM-labeled nonC-DNS and FAM-labeled MMDNS that both embedded with Cy5-mRNA were respectively added to the well with poly T final concentration of 75 nM. After incubation for 10 hours, the cells were washed with PBS buffer three times. Last, fluorescence imaging was carried out on a laser scanning confocal microscope (UltraVIEW VoX, PerkinElmer).

Lymph node APC activation analysis

C57BL/6N female mice (7 to 8 weeks old) were divided into four groups and then intramuscularly injected at the right leg with 100 μl of mixture of OVA mRNA (0.1 μg/μl) + CpG ODNs (1.6 μM) (II), nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III), and MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV), respectively. Analysis of antigen-specific CTL response

C57BL/6 female mice (7 to 8 weeks old) were intramuscularly immunized with 100 μl of mixture of OVA mRNA (0.1 μg/μl) + CpG ODNs (1.6 μM) (II), nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III), and MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV), respectively. In vivo tumor immunotherapeutic study

C57BL/6N female mice (6 weeks old) were inoculated subcutaneously with 2 × 105 B16-OVA melanoma cells into the right rear side of the back on day 0. Tumors were allowed to grow for 5 days, and then these tumor-bearing mice were randomly divided into five groups (n = 4 for each group). On days 5 and 12, mice were subcutaneously vaccinated on the right thigh muscle with 100 μl of mixture of OVA mRNA (0.1 μg/μl) + CpG ODNs (1.6 μM) (II), nC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III), MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV), and MMDNS, respectively. The unvaccinated mice were set as the control group (I). For the combination treatment group, the mice were given αPD-L1 (5 mg/kg, i.p.) on days 5 and 12 and simultaneously vaccinated with MMDNS-OVA mRNA. The larger (L) and smaller (S) diameters of tumors were monitored every other day using calipers in three dimensions, and their volumes were calculated using the formula V = 0.5 × L × S2.

On day 22, tumors were collected and weighed. The tumors from different groups were collected and fixed in 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) for immunofluorescence staining and H&E staining. The main organs (heart, liver, spleen, lung, and kidney) were collected and fixed in 4% PFA for H&E staining.

In vivo inhibition of tumor metastasis study

On days 0, 7, and 14, C57BL/6N female mice (6 weeks old) were intramuscularly injected with 100 μl of mixture of OVA mRNA (0.1 μg/μl) and CpG ODNs (1.6 μM) (II), nonC-DNS-OVA mRNA (III), and MMDNS-OVA mRNA (IV), respectively (n = 4). The untreated mice were set as the control group (I). Afterward, on day 24, 5.0 × 105 B16-OVA melanoma cells were intravenously injected. On day 50, lungs were collected and counted for number of metastatic nodules. Lungs from different groups were collected and fixed in 4% Bouin’s fluid for H&E staining. Spleens were mechanically disrupted and flushed in complete medium. The single-cell suspension was centrifuged and resuspended with 2 ml of ACK buffer for 3 min to remove red blood cells. Splenocytes were recovered in a complete medium. Next, splenocytes were treated with OVA peptide SIINFEKL (2 μg/ml) for 4 hours at 37°C. IFN-γ+ cytotoxic CD8+ T lymphocytes and memory T cells TEM (CD3+CD8+CD44HighCD62LLow and CD3+CD4+CD44HighCD62LLow) were then measured by flow cytometry analysis. For the intracellular IFN-γ ELISpot analysis, operations were carried out following the protocol provided by the manufacturer. To be specific, the ELISpot plate was firstly washed four times with sterile PBS buffer and then conditioned with RPMI 1640 medium (supplemented with 10% inactivated FBS) for 3 min at room temperature. Splenocytes were firstly treated with OVA peptide SIINFEKL (2 μg/ml) for 24 hours at 37°C and then added into the conditioned plate. The plate was incubated at 37°C with the presence of 5% CO2 for 48 hours. Afterward, splenocytes were removed, and the plate was washed five times with sterile PBS buffer. Biotinylated anti–immunoglobulin G detection antibody was then added and incubated for 1 hour at room temperature. Plate was once again washed five times with sterile PBS buffer, followed by adding streptavidin-alkaline phosphatase (1:20,000) to incubation for 30 min. Substrate solution was lastly added to observe the precipitates at the sites of cytokine production after washing the plate with sterile PBS buffer for five times. Automated spot quantification was performed by Dakewe Biotech Co. Ltd.

Tumor rechallenge analysis

On days 0, 7, and 14, C57BL/6 female mice (n = 6) were intramuscularly immunized with 100 μl of MMDNS-OVA mRNA [containing mRNA (0.1 μg/μl) and 1.6 μM ODNs] (V). The immunized mice were set as the control group (I). On day 25, 2.0 × 105 B16-OVA melanoma cells were intravenously injected for first challenge. On day 45, 2.0 × 105 B16-OVA melanoma cells were inoculated subcutaneously into the right rear side of the back for rechallenge. The larger (L) and smaller (S) diameters of tumors were monitored using calipers in three dimensions, and their volumes were calculated using the formula V = 0.5 × L × S2.

Statistical analysis

All statistical analysis was represented as means ± SD. Data were analyzed using a two-tailed t test.
