# Unraveling gut microbiome interferences in cancer immunotherapy: a meta-analysis of diverse drug effects.

## Abstract

Recent advancements in tumor therapy have centered on novel treatments, particularly immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), which have transformed cancer treatment since their global approval in 2011. ICIs have demonstrated remarkable and substantial efficacy across a range of malignancies, including malignant melanoma, non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), head and neck cancers, renal carcinoma, and selected gastrointestinal cancers. Despite these promising outcomes, the challenges that during the clinical application, such as relatively low response rates and the occurrence of immune-related adverse events, can limit therapeutic benefits. Accumulating evidence highlights the gut microbiome as a critical modulator of cancer immunotherapy efficacy. Notably, alterations in gut microbiota composition observed in response to ICI therapy, and specific bacterial populations (such as an increased Clostridiales/ Baceroidales ratio, etc.) have been associated with improved responses in patients with NSCLC and renal cell carcinoma. However, the composition and function of the gut microbiome are influenced by a variety of factors, among which concomitant drug use plays a particularly prominent role. Medications commonly prescribed to cancer patients, such as antibiotics, proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), and probiotics, can significantly alter microbial communities, potentially impacting immunotherapy outcomes. Thus, there is a compelling need for rigorous research to elucidate how such drug-induced shifts in gut microbiota affect patient responses to ICIs. Thus, we conducted a meta-analysis which included 69 studies, 102 cohorts (22,568 patients were included) to systematically investigate the influence of drug interventions, including PPIs, antibiotics and probiotics on gut microbiome dynamics and ICI effectiveness. Progression-free survival (PFS), Overall survival (OS) and Objective response rate (ORR) were utilized as the main meta notions, while a subgroup analysis was determined based on: (1) tumor type; (2) exposure time window; (3) Treatment scheme as well. The total HR and 95% CI for PFS and OS are calculated separately for each intervention (probiotics, antibiotics, and PPIs) to compare their impact on ICI immunotherapy. Our research showed that the concurrent use of antibiotics or PPIs with ICIs was associated with significantly OS, PFS, and ORR. In contrast, probiotic supplementation demonstrated promising results with improved efficacy metrics. Besides, in subgroup analysis, patients using antibiotics within three months before or after the initiation of ICI therapy, OS, PFS, and ORR were significantly lower compared to those who did not receive antibiotics; the timing of antibiotic or PPI administration consistently resulted in poorer outcomes; a negative correlation between PPI use and OS, PFS, and ORR across treatment modalities was also exhibited. By elucidating the interplay between these factors, this study aims to provide robust evidence for optimizing ICI therapy, and to enlighten cancer treatment strategies more personalized, effective and safer, ultimately advancing patient care in oncology.

Introduction

Tumor therapy has entered a new era with the effective and appropriate administration of novel treatments. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), which have been globally approved since 2011, are among the most significant and effective weapons in cancer treatment. ICIs have been used as a novel therapy for various malignant tumors, including malignant melanoma, non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), head and neck cancers, renal carcinoma, and gastrointestinal cancers, demonstrating remarkable effects. However, as their clinical application has expanded, challenges such as low response rates and immune-related adverse events have emerged, which hinder the clinical effectiveness of this novel treatment. Consequently, research focusing on strategies to improve immune response rates and reduce adverse reactions has significantly increased in recent years.

The gut microbiome, or gut microbiota, has become a prominent area of research recently. While gut microbiome refers to both the collective genetic and metabolic potential of trillions of microorganisms, gut microbiota specifically describes the actual community of microorganisms, ICI treatment can alter microbiome composition (microbiota), which may further cause dysbiosis-an imbalance in the composition and function of gut microbiota. This influence extends beyond the gastrointestinal tract, but with may correspond with other diseases (cardiovascular, autoimmune, psychiatric disorers, etc.), as well as cancer immunotherapy itself. Many studies have indicated that ICI therapy alters the composition of the gut microbiome. For instance, the abundance of certain bacteria showed a significant increase in PD-L1/anti-PD-1 responders with NSCLC and renal cell carcinoma (RCC) compared to non-responding patients.

Therefore, factors that influence the gut microbiome may play a critical role in the effectiveness of novel anti-tumor therapies, particularly in ICI treatment. To investigate this hypothesis, we conducted an integrated meta-analysis. Our study aims to provide valuable insights for clinical practice by identifying three key factors commonly administered during anti-tumor therapy: proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), antibiotics, and probiotics. Through this meta-analysis, we seek to provide robust, sustainable evidence for the rational use of these drugs during ICI treatments. This research not only explores how “supportive treatments” affect the gut microbiome, potentially enhancing ICI therapy but also aims to improve the efficacy of the treatment and reduce the burden on patients.

Materials and methods

Study proposal and registration

The meta-analysis proposal has been submitted to the PROSPERO (International prospective register of systematic reviews). The registration NO. is: CRD42024547198 (URL: https://www.crd.york.ac.uk/PROSPERO/). All the research work is conducted according to the PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) guidelines.

Trial design

To assess the impact of methods that interfere with the gut microbiome on the efficacy of ICIs in cancer patients receiving ICIs treatment, while to analyze the changes in gut microbiota affecting their efficacy, eligible trials published between April 1, 2011 to May 12, 2024 were analyzed in this research, we noted whether the data from studies with comparable baseline characteristics had been adjusted for potential confounders such as concurrent medications, disease status, in order to minimize bias and reduce the influence of baseline differences on the pooled outcomes. (All the baseline of the researched were provided in supplement Fig. 1).

Data sources and literature search strategy

The systematic literature search in this analysis was conducted across four English databases: PubMed, The Cochrane Library, Embase, and Web of Science, all clinical studies related to immune checkpoint inhibitors and gut microbiota published from the establishment of the database until May 12, 2024 were searched. The search strategy combined subject headings and free-text terms, including search terms as follows: PD-1/PD-L1, PDL1 Inhibitor, CTLA 4 Inhibitor, pembrolizumab, atezolizumab, ipilimumab, nivolumab, durvaluma b, Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors, Neoplasm, Gut Microbiotas, Microbiotas. Additionally, any relevant articles referenced in the literature were screened for further research (The research strategies are shown in Supplement material 2).

Study selection

Inclusion criteria

Cancer diagnosis confirmed by pathology or imaging (including any types of cancer) while who are receiving immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) immunotherapy.

ICIs can be used alone or in combination with other anti-cancer drugs, regardless of any treatment line or regimen.

Exposure to interventions affecting the gut microbiome, including probiotics, antibiotics, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs). Patients should receive probiotics and/or antibiotics and/or PPIs before and/or within 90 days after and/or during immunotherapy. No limitation on the categories or routes of administration for probiotics, antibiotics, or PPIs was considered.

Cohort studies reporting outcome indicators such as progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), and objective response rate (ORR), wherein the studies report hazard ratios (HR) and their 95% confidence intervals (CIs) for OS and PFS. Our meta-analysis will include studies that report at least one outcome measure related to the exposure interventions.

Conference papers that meet the above criteria will also be considered for inclusion.

 Inclusion of retrospective cohort studies or prospective clinical studies (including randomized controlled trials and single-arm trials).

Exclusion criteria

The article type is review, case report, or animal study.

The data in the article is incomplete, or it is not possible to obtain comprehensive outcome data.

The total number of cases included in the study is lesser than 10.

Duplicate literature.

Research screening

Two researchers independently conduct an initial screening of the literature based on the titles and abstracts during the literature screening process. The selected literature undergoes a secondary screening through full-text reviews, followed by cross-verification of the screening results. If any discrepancies occurred during the process, a joint discussion will be held to determine whether the study should be included or not, and a third researcher may be employed, if necessary. If the information in the text is incomplete or unclear, the reviewing team will contact the original study group to obtain the necessary information. Throughout the screening process, the reasons for inclusion and exclusion at each step need to be documented.

Data extraction

Data extraction was conducted by two researchers independently. When discrepancies arose, a third author was employed to help reaching a consensus. If uncertainty still existed, the study was abandoned in the analysis. Standardized data extraction electronic forms were utilized to collect the following data from each included study, if available: the first author’s name, publication year, study type, country, patient and cancer characteristics (i.e., number of included patients, histology, Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group Performance Status (ECOG PS)), immunotherapy characteristics (type of ICI or ICI drug, treatment regimen, and line of treatment), intervention characteristics including exposure to probiotics, antibiotics, and PPIs (the number of users and exposure time window), as well as other outcomes of interest based on the exposure interventions.

For PFS and OS, the results were expressed as hazard ratios (HR) with 95% confidence intervals (CIs); for ORR, the results were expressed as percentages (%). Regardless of the outcome, while extracting data, results from multivariate analyses took the precedence over those from univariate analyses, prioritize extracting multivariate-adjusted HR/RR, as this type of data has already controlled for potential confounding factors such as age, ECOG PS, and cancer stage, which can reduce baseline bias. If the study did not provide adjusted data, univariate data was extracted and this procedure was mentioned in the baseline table. Disagreements during the data extraction process will be resolved through consensus judgment by a third author. If the results remain uncertain, they will be excluded from the analysis.

Assessment of study quality

The quality of cohort study literature is assessed by employing the Newcastle-Ottawa Scale (NOS). The form consists of three modules: selection of study participants, comparability of groups, and measurement of exposure factors. There are a total of eight items, with a maximum score of 9. A score of 7–9 indicates high-quality literature, 5–6 indicates moderate-quality literature, and ≤ 4 indicates low-quality literature. The evaluation process is conducted independently by two authors. In case of disagreement, they first discuss the issue. If they still cannot reach a consensus, a third author will make the final decision. The form was exhibited in supplement material 8.

In addition, we applied the Grading of Recommendations Assessment, Development, and Evaluation (GRADE) approach to assess the certainty of evidence for all primary outcomes (OS, PFS, ORR). Evidence was evaluated across five domains: (i) risk of bias (based on NOS scores), (ii) inconsistency (based on I² values), (iii) indirectness (relevance of study populations, interventions, and outcomes to our research question), (iv) imprecision (based on the width of 95% confidence intervals), and (v) publication bias (assessed with Egger’s test and funnel plot asymmetry). Evidence certainty was categorized as high, moderate, low, or very low. A GRADE Summary of Findings table was prepared, and is exhibited in supplement material 9.

Main outcomes

In this study, the meta-related notions were determined as follows:

Progression-free survival (PFS): The time from the start of immunotherapy to the date of documented disease progression or death, whichever occurs first.

Overall survival (OS): The time from the start of immunotherapy to death from any cause.

Objective response rate (ORR): The proportion of patients who experience a certain amount of tumor shrinkage that persists for a specific duration, including complete response (CR) and partial response (PR).

Subgroup analysis

To minimize the heterogeneity between studies and identify factors, which may influence the effects of gut microbiota use on survival and treatment-related outcomes, three subgroup analyses were conducted based on cancer type, intervention exposure time window, and treatment method. Due to the relatively small number of studies reporting treatment-related outcome data associated with probiotics, subgroup analyses were limited to antibiotics and PPIs, with each subgroup requiring at least two studies.

Subgroup analyses according to the cancer type

For the included studies that focused exclusively on a specific cancer type, if data on OS, PFS, and ORR could be obtained based on the intervention, that cancer type was categorized as a distinct group. A “Multiple” category was created to classify studies that reported on more than one cancer type.

Subgroup analyses according to the exposure time window

Based on the timing defined in the included studies, we hypothesized that the impact of antibiotic or PPI use would be stronger before and after the initiation of ICIs. We identified four exposure time windows related to the start of ICI treatment: [−90, 90], [−60, 60], [−45, 45] (days), and concurrent use. The day zero refers to the date when ICI treatment commenced, specifically the date when immunotherapy was first administered. Concurrent use is defined as all cancer patients receiving immunotherapy while simultaneously receiving antibiotics or PPIs. It is important to note that a patient included in the exposure time window of [−45, 45] may have been exposed to the intervention for a shorter duration (for example, between − 14 and + 14 days before the initiation of ICIs).

Subgroup analyses according to the treatment scheme

Based on the treatment methods described in the included studies, we hypothesized that the treatment regimen of ICIs would influence the outcomes of the exposure measures. We categorized the treatment schemes into monotherapy, combination therapy (“Association”), and treatment methods not clearly specified in the studies (“Not Mentioned”). Monotherapy refers to the use of ICI agents, including anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, or anti-CTLA-4, administered alone. Combination therapy refers to the use of ICI agents in conjunction with other antitumor medications, such as targeted therapy or chemotherapy.

Subgroup interaction testing

Meta-regression analysis (using the metareg commands in STATA 16.0 software) was conducted for subgroup interaction testing and to explore potential sources of heterogeneity. “Subgroup category” was used as the categorical moderator variable, with the effect size from included studies (natural logarithm of HR/RR) as the dependent variable, to calculate the interaction P-value (α = 0.05). P < 0.05 indicates significant differences in the effect of interventions on ICI efficacy between different subgroups; 0.05 ≤ P < 0.10 suggests marginal differences in the effect of interventions on ICI efficacy between different subgroups, indicating possible differences but with insufficient evidence; P ≥ 0.10 indicates no significant differences in the effect of interventions on ICI efficacy between different subgroups.

Meta-regression and power analysis

To quantitatively explore potential sources of heterogeneity, we performed univariable meta-regression analyses for outcomes with substantial heterogeneity (I² >50%). The dependent variable was the effect size (natural logarithm of HR or RR), and the prespecified covariates (independent variables) included: cancer type (categorical: NSCLC, melanoma, urothelial carcinoma, RCC, HCC, multiple), exposure time window (categorical: [−90, 90], [−60, 60], [−45, 45], concurrent use), and treatment regimen (categorical: monotherapy, combination therapy, not reported). Analyses were conducted using the metareg command in STATA 16.0. For each covariate, we reported the regression coefficient (β), standard error (SE), and p value. A threshold of p < 0.10 was used to identify a covariate as a potentially important source of heterogeneity. We also reported the model R² (percentage of variance explained) and residual I² to quantify the extent to which each covariate accounted for heterogeneity.

To ensure the reliability of subgroup analysis results, we conducted post-hoc power analyses. The minimum inclusion criterion for subgroup analyses was set at ‘at least two independent studies,’ in line with the PRISMA guidelines for performing subgroup meta-analyses. For subgroups meeting this criterion, we further calculated their statistical power. The significance level was set at α = 0.05. The observed pooled effect size from this meta-analysis (e.g., HR or RR) was used as the expected effect size, while the actual total sample size and number of events within each subgroup were used as inputs. All calculations were performed using PASS 2021 software. We considered power ≥ 80% as adequate statistical power; power between 60% and 80% indicated that results should be interpreted with caution; and power < 60% was considered insufficient evidence.

Data analysis

Meta-analysis: This analysis evaluates the relationship between interventions affecting the gut microbiome and immunotherapy by calculating the pooled hazard ratios (HR) and their 95% confidence intervals (CI) for PFS and OS, and the pooled relative risks (RR) and their 95% CI for ORR across different cohorts. The interventions affecting the gut microbiome primarily include probiotics, antibiotics, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs).

Comparison of Outcomes: The total HR and 95% CI for PFS and OS are calculated separately for each intervention (probiotics, antibiotics, and PPIs) to compare their impact on ICI immunotherapy. For probiotics, HR < 1.0 indicates a better prognosis for the group receiving probiotic therapy. For PPIs and antibiotics, HR > 1.0 suggests a better prognosis for the group not receiving PPI or antibiotic therapy. This indicates potential influence of the gut microbiome on the effectiveness of ICI treatment.

ORR Analysis: The number of patients achieving complete or partial remission is used to calculate the pooled relative risk (RR) and 95% CI for ORR across all cohorts, assessing the relationship between the gut microbiome and treatment response.

Statistical Significance: Cochrane’s chi-squared test is used to determine statistical significance. A p-value < 0.05 indicates statistical significance.

Heterogeneity Assessment and Model Selection: Cochrane’s Q test and I² index are used to assess heterogeneity among included studies. For each meta-analysis, the I² value, the p-value of the Q-test, and the tau² value were reported, and the appropriate effect model based on the level of heterogeneity was selected. For the Q-test, a p-value < 0.1 indicates statistically significant heterogeneity. When I² >50%, substantial heterogeneity between studies is considered to exist. For studies with low heterogeneity (p > 0.10 and I² ≤ 50%), a fixed-effects model was used; for studies with high heterogeneity (p < 0.10 and I² >50%), a random-effects model was adopted.

When the number of included studies in this research was ≥ 10, potential publication bias was evaluated using visual inspection of funnel plots as well as Egger’s and Begg’s statistical tests. A p-value > 0.05 suggests no significant publication bias. Meanwhile, a contour-enhanced funnel plot was generated to help distinguish asymmetry caused by publication bias from that resulting from other factors.

Sensitivity analysis was performed using the “leave-one-out” method to assess the impact of individual studies on the pooled effect size. This method was implemented by sequentially excluding each study, then recalculating the pooled hazard ratio (HR) or relative risk (RR) along with their 95% confidence intervals (CI). The fluctuation range of the effect size was recorded to evaluate the overall robustness. For the Q test, p < 0.1 indicates statistically significant heterogeneity. When I² >50%, substantial heterogeneity is considered present among studies.

Subgroup Analysis: Subgroup analysis is conducted to minimize the impact of heterogeneity and investigate whether the impact of interventions affecting the gut microbiome differs across various groups of interest (tumor type, treatment scheme, and treatment window).

Sensitivity Analysis: A “leave-one-out” approach is used to assess the impact of individual studies on the pooled outcome risk ratios.

Publication Bias: Funnel plots and Egger regression are used to examine publication bias among the studies.

Software: All statistical analyses and forest plots are performed using STATA 16.

Results

Study selection

A total of 3373 studies were retrieved from PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, and Web of Science, including 12 references from other publications. After removing 1,026 duplicates, 2055 studies were excluded based on title and abstract review, including those were not relevant to this study. After a full-text review of the remaining 69 studies, 102 cohorts were included in this meta-analysis, excluding animal studies, single-arm studies, studies with inconsistent outcome measures, data types not suitable for meta-analysis, as well as overlapping cohorts. The included studies were grouped based on the use of drugs that affecting gut microbiota, including: 10 probiotic cohorts, 48 antibiotic cohorts, and 44 PPI cohorts. Among these, 12 studies investigated both antibiotics and PPIs, and 2 study investigated probiotics, antibiotics, and PPIs. The results of the literature search process (Fig. 1). The clear majority of studies were retrospective analyses of patient medical records, with only studies reporting data from prospective observational clinical trials.

Characteristics of studies and patients included

This study included 22,586 patients diagnosed with cancer and receiving an ICI-based immunotherapy. Among them, 5471 patients were treated in both antibiotic and PPI studies, 213 patients were treated in PPIs and probiotic, while 293 patients were treated with antibiotic, PPI, and probiotic studies together. All the patients were grouped in three cohorts: the antibiotics, the PPIs and the probiotics. The cohort of antibiotics consisted of 12,913 patients, the PPI cohort consisted of 13,380 patients, while the probiotic cohort consisted of 2563 patients. Of all these patients, 3284 (25%), 5021 (38%), and 1838 (71%) patients had taken antibiotics, PPIs, and probiotics, respectively, before (30, 60, or 90 days prior to starting ICIs), during, and/or after (30–60 days) immunotherapy. These cohorts primarily originated from Japan and Europe (in terms of both cohort and patient numbers), followed by the United States. Within Europe, Spain and France generated the most cohorts. The majority of patients in this meta-analysis had locally advanced or metastatic cancer. The number of patients included in each study ranged from 29 to 1360. The most common cancer type observed in these studies was NSCLC (in terms of both cohort number and patient percentage), followed by melanoma, UC, RCC, and a smaller number of patients with head and neck tumors, gastrointestinal cancers, small cell lung cancer, lung adenocarcinoma, and squamous cell carcinoma. The included studies showed significant variability in the reported characteristics of immunotherapy and intervention treatments. However, based on the literature review, patients seemed to primarily receive a single-agent ICI therapy, with nivolumab and pembrolizumab being the most representative ICI drugs. Other immunotherapy drugs included ipilimumab, atezolizumab, and durvalumab.

Data adjustment for confounding factors

To assess the extent to which the pooled effect size is affected by bias from potential confounding factors, we counted the number of studies that provided hazard ratios (HRs) adjusted by multivariable analysis and unadjusted HRs from univariable analysis. As shown in the table Summary of Hazard Ratio Adjustment Types in Included Studies, among the included analyses, most studies reported multivariable-adjusted HRs for outcomes of overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS) (antibiotics-OS: 75%; antibiotics-PFS: 78%; proton pump inhibitors [PPI]-OS: 67%; PPI-PFS: 69%). Approximately 22% to 33% of the studies only provided results from univariable analysis.

In studies related to probiotics, the proportion of multivariable-adjusted HRs for OS and PFS was relatively high (71%−75%). However, there were cases where some studies did not clearly report the type of HR used (OS: 29%; PFS: 25%).

Quality assessment

Quality assessments were conducted for all included literature, with ratings of 7 to 9, and the highest score is 9 (40, 48%), while other score are: 8 (40, 48%), 7 (4, 4%), and no low-quality researches that scores below 4. No studies were excluded based on the quality assessment.

Meta-Analysis results for overall survival

Effect of concomitant use of antibiotic on overall survival

The data of treatment with antibiotics was extracted from a total of 39 articles and 44 cohorts on the impact of antibiotic use on overall survival (OS) in patients undergoing immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy for cancer (n = 12,618). Among these, 2,988 patients were treated with antibiotics. The combined results for OS across all cancer types showed OS: HR = 1.61; 95% CI, 1.44–1.81, indicating that patients receiving ICIs in conjunction with antibiotic treatment had lower OS compared to those who did not use antibiotics simultaneously. This analysis was associated with high heterogeneity (p < 0.001, I²=82.9%, tau2 = 0.0839), thus a random effects model was used (Fig. 2),.

Effect of concomitant use of PPI on overall survival

The data on the impact of PPIs on overall survival (OS) in patients undergoing immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy for cancer (n = 11,595) was extracted from a total of 31 articles and 41 cohorts. Among these patients, 4459 were treated with PPIs. The combined results for OS across all cancer types showed (HR = 1.28; 95% CI, 1.17–1.41), indicating that patients receiving ICIs alongside PPIs had lower OS compared to those who did not use PPIs simultaneously. This analysis was associated with moderate heterogeneity (p < 0.001, I²=55% >50%, tau2 = 0.0386), thus a random effects model was used, as illustrated in (Fig. 3).

Effect of concomitant use of probiotics on overall survival

A total of 4 articles including 5 cohorts provided data on the impact of probiotics on overall survival (OS) in patients undergoing immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy for cancer (n = 1338), with 857 patients using probiotics. This analysis was associated with moderate heterogeneity (P = 0.004, I²=74.2%, tau2 = 0.1880), thus a random effects model was used, as illustrated in (Fig. 4). The combined results for OS across all cancer types showed (HR = 0.67, 95% CI, 0.55–0.82), indicating that patients receiving ICIs alongside probiotics had higher OS compared to those who did not use probiotics simultaneously.

Meta-Analysis results for progression-free survival

Effect of concomitant use of antibiotic on progression-free survival

A total of 33 articles and 36 cohorts provided data on the impact of antibiotic use on progression-free survival (PFS) in patients undergoing ICI therapy for cancer (n = 10,799). The combined results for PFS across all cancer types showed: HR = 1.43; 95% CI, 1.27–1.60, indicating that patients receiving ICIs along with antibiotics had lower PFS compared to those who did not use antibiotics simultaneously. This analysis was associated with high heterogeneity (P<0.001, I²=76.5%, tau2 = 0.0814), thus a random effects model was used, as illustrated in (Fig. 5).

Effect of concomitant use of PPIs on progression-free survival

A total of 26 articles and 35 cohorts provided data on the impact of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) on PFS in patients undergoing ICI therapy for cancer (n = 10,226), with 3920 patients using PPIs. The combined results for PFS across all cancer types showed: HR = 1.22; 95% CI, 1.12–1.34, indicating that patients receiving ICIs together with PPIs had lower PFS compared to those who did not use PPIs simultaneously. This analysis was associated with high heterogeneity (P<0.001, I²=61.3%, tau2 = 0.0371), thus a random effects model was used, as illustrated in the (Fig. 6).

Effect of concomitant use of probiotics on progression-free survival

A total of 5 articles including 6 cohorts provided data on the impact of probiotics on PFS in patients undergoing ICI therapy for cancer (n = 1377), with 886 patients using probiotics. The combined results for PFS across all cancer types showed: HR = 0.55; 95% CI, 0.46–0.67, tau2 = 0.0573, indicating that patients receiving ICIs along with probiotics had a higher PFS compared to those who did not use probiotics simultaneously. This analysis was associated with moderate heterogeneity (P = 0.091, I²=47.4%), thus a random effects model was used, as illustrated in (Fig. 7).

Meta-Analysis results for objective response rate (ORR)

Effect of concomitant use of antibiotic on objective response rate

A total of 19 articles and 21 cohorts provided data on the impact of antibiotic use on overall response rate (ORR) in patients undergoing ICI therapy for cancer (n = 4,934). The combined results for ORR across all cancer types showed: RR = 0.58; 95% CI, 0.51–0.66, indicating that patients receiving ICIs with antibiotics had lower ORR compared to those who did not use antibiotics simultaneously. This analysis was associated with no heterogeneity (P = 0.573, I²=0%, tau2 = 0.0000), thus a fixed effects model was used, as illustrated in (Fig. 8)

Effect of concomitant use of PPIs on objective response rate

Subgroup analysis

Meta-regression analysis for sources of heterogeneity

This study evaluated heterogeneity among included studies using Cochrane’s Q test and I² index, and employed univariate meta-regression analysis to explore potential sources of heterogeneity. The meta-regression model used “subgroup category” as the categorical moderator variable and the natural logarithm of effect size (HR/RR) as the dependent variable for calculation.

Sources of heterogeneity in antibiotic exposure

The heterogeneity of antibiotic effects on ICI efficacy varied by outcome measure. For OS (I²=82.9%) and PFS (I²=76.5%), meta-regression analysis showed that treatment modality and treatment time window were important sources of heterogeneity.

Treatment time window explained 28.32% of OS heterogeneity (R²=28.32%, residual I²=35.1%). Within the [−45, 45] time window, antibiotic exposure was significantly associated with OS deterioration (β = 0.2192, SE = 0.0781, p = 0.008). Treatment modality explained 53.86% of OS heterogeneity (R²=53.86%, residual I²=16.34%), with the combination therapy group showing antibiotic exposure significantly associated with OS deterioration (β = 0.2699, SE = 0.0818, p = 0.002), while the group with unspecified treatment modality showed the opposite effect (β=−0.6152, SE = 0.1901, p = 0.002).

For ORR (I²=0%), no significant sources of heterogeneity were identified, with all between-subgroup differences being statistically non-significant (all p > 0.05).

Sources of heterogeneity in PPI exposure

Heterogeneity analysis of PPI effects on ICI efficacy showed that treatment time window explained 21.65% of OS heterogeneity (R²=21.65%, residual I²=25.8%). Within the [−45, 45] time window, PPI exposure was significantly associated with OS deterioration (β = 0.1597, SE = 0.0621, p = 0.014). For PFS, tumor type explained 29.14% of heterogeneity (R²=29.14%, residual I²=18.08%), but no significant differences were found for specific tumor types. For ORR, all meta-regression models failed to effectively explain heterogeneity (negative R² values), indicating the presence of other unmeasured confounding factors.

Power analysis

The analysis of antibiotic exposure on primary outcomes showed sufficient statistical power. For OS and PFS, the statistical power of most subgroups reached 100%, including NSCLC (OS: 100%; PFS: 100%), multiple cancer types (OS: 100%; PFS: 100%), renal cell carcinoma (RCC) (OS: 100%; PFS: 100%), as well as all subgroups stratified by treatment time windows ([−90, 90], [−60, 60], [−45, 45], concurrent use) and treatment modalities (Monotherapy: 100%; Association: 100%). Only a few subgroups, such as hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) (OS: 63%) and the “Not mentioned” treatment modality (OS: 54%), exhibited insufficient power due to small sample sizes or low event counts. For objective response rate (ORR), the main subgroups (NSCLC, multiple cancer types, [−45, 45] time window) all achieved 100% statistical power, while only the urothelial carcinoma (UC) subgroup had slightly lower power (58%) due to a small sample size.

The statistical power results for proton pump inhibitor (PPI) exposure varied across outcomes. For OS, main subgroups including NSCLC (100%), multiple cancer types (100%), UC (100%), the concurrent use time window (100%), and the monotherapy modality (100%) showed sufficient power. However, subgroups of rare cancer types such as HCC (13%) and RCC (30%) had insufficient power due to limited sample sizes. For PFS, subgroups like NSCLC (96%), multiple cancer types (100%), and UC (100%) demonstrated adequate power, whereas the melanoma (5%) and RCC (15%) subgroups had low power. For ORR, the statistical power of most subgroups was below 80% (NSCLC: 60%; multiple cancer types: 32%; concurrent use: 45%), indicating that the current evidence is insufficient to reliably conclude that PPIs have a negative impact on ORR.

This power analysis suggests that the conclusions of this meta-analysis regarding the negative effects of antibiotics on OS and PFS are highly reliable. In contrast, the conclusions about the impact of PPIs on ORR should be interpreted with caution, and more studies are needed in the future to improve the statistical power of these subgroups.

Publication bias and sensitivity analysis

Publication bias

Antibiotic-related analysis

OS: The funnel plot showed asymmetry; however, Begg’s test did not detect significant bias (p = 0.436). The intercept term of Egger’s test was highly statistically significant (p < 0.001), suggesting potential publication bias.

Progression-Free Survival (PFS): The funnel plot exhibited obvious asymmetry, with missing studies mainly concentrated in the region of low statistical significance (p > 0.10). Both Begg’s test (p = 0.021) and Egger’s test (p = 0.017) supported the presence of significant publication bias.

Objective Response Rate (ORR): The funnel plot showed significant asymmetry. Both Begg’s test (p = 0.004) and Egger’s test (p = 0.003) indicated the existence of significant publication bias.

PPI-related analysis

OS: The funnel plot showed good symmetry. Neither Begg’s test (p = 0.973) nor Egger’s test (p = 0.725) found evidence of significant publication bias.

Progression-Free Survival (PFS): The funnel plot showed essentially symmetry. Results of both Begg’s test (p = 0.386) and Egger’s test (p = 0.348) indicated no significant publication bias.

ORR: The funnel plot exhibited good symmetry. Neither Begg’s test (p = 0.640) nor Egger’s test (p = 0.972) suggested the presence of publication bias.

Sensitivity analysis

Funnel plots and Begg’s and Egger’s tests were used to assess publication bias in the included studies (Supplement Figs. 7). Due to the inclusion of fewer than 10 studies regarding probiotics, a risk of publication bias evaluation was not conducted for this area. In the overall analysis of OS, the funnel plot for antibiotics showed a lack of symmetry, indicating potential publication bias (Begg: p = 0.396; Egger: p < 0.0001). Conversely, no significant publication bias was observed for PPIs (PPI: Begg: p = 0.973; Egger: p = 0.725).

In the overall analysis of PFS, the funnel plot for antibiotics also lacked symmetry, suggesting possible publication bias (Begg: p = 0.021; Egger: p = 0.017). For PPIs, there was no significant publication bias (Begg: p = 0.386; Egger: p = 0.348). In the overall analysis of the overall ORR, the funnel plot for antibiotics again showed a lack of symmetry, indicating potential publication bias (Begg: p = 0.004; Egger: p = 0.003). In contrast, PPIs did not exhibit significant publication bias (Begg: p = 0.640; Egger: p = 0.972). Additionally, sensitivity analyses using the leave-one-out method demonstrated that the combined results for OS, PFS, and ORR did not change significantly when any individual study was removed, supporting the robustness of the results.

Grade evaluation

Antibiotics

The certainty of evidence linking antibiotic use to reduced ICI efficacy was rated low for OS and PFS, and moderate for ORR. OS and PFS were downgraded one level due to serious inconsistency (substantial heterogeneity, I² >75%). OS evidence was further downgraded for suspected publication bias (Egger’s test p < 0.001). Despite large and precise effect estimates, the potential for unpublished negative results and unexplained heterogeneity reduced our confidence. ORR evidence was downgraded one level for suspected publication bias (Egger’s test p = 0.003).

PPIs

Certainty was moderate for OS and PFS, and low for ORR. OS and PFS were downgraded one level for moderate heterogeneity (I² ~55–61%), with no strong evidence of publication bias. ORR was downgraded for serious inconsistency (I² = 52.1%) and imprecision (95% CI crossed 1.00), leading to uncertainty in this result.

Probiotics

Certainty was low for OS and moderate for PFS and ORR. OS was downgraded for serious inconsistency (I² = 74.2%) and imprecision (small number of studies and wide CIs). PFS and ORR were downgraded one level for imprecision (limited sample size and events). Although estimates were consistent and precise (CIs did not cross 1.00), the small sample size limits confidence.

Discussion

The utilization of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) has revolutionized the landscape of cancer therapy, offering unprecedented clinical benefits across various malignancies. However, the heterogeneity in treatment responses and the emergence of irAEs (immune-related adverse event) present significant clinical challenges that warrant careful consideration. Understanding the factors influencing ICI response and toxicity is crucial for optimizing treatment outcomes.

Recent evidence suggests a complex relationship between the gut microbiome and the effectiveness and safety of ICIs. However, the current evidence base remains fragmented: most studies are retrospective or single-center with small samples; exposure definitions for microbiome-modulating agents (e.g., antibiotics, probiotics, proton pump inhibitors) vary widely in timing and duration; endpoints and irAE definitions are inconsistent; adjustment for key confounders and concomitant medications is uneven; and findings are often conflicting across tumor types and clinical contexts. Prior reviews have largely been narrative or focused on a single exposure or malignancy, leaving uncertainty about the overall magnitude and direction of associations. Consequently, rather than comparing results with individual studies, but to clarify the correlation between microbiome and ICI treatment, we conducted this meta-analysis, aiming to provide comprehensive insights on exploring the impact of three commonly used therapeutic interventions—probiotics, antibiotics, and proton pump inhibitors (PPIs)—that also affect the gut microbiome on ICI treatment outcomes, to resolve inconsistencies, characterize heterogeneity, and delineate remaining knowledge gaps while acknowledging that the results reflect association rather than causation.

Our findings reveal a nuanced relationship between these interventions and ICI outcomes. Notably, the concurrent use of antibiotics or PPIs with ICIs was associated with significantly diminished overall survival (OS), progression-free survival (PFS), and objective response rate (ORR). In contrast, probiotic supplementation demonstrated promising results with improved efficacy metrics. These observations align with emerging evidence suggesting that gut microbiota composition plays a crucial role in modulating anti-tumor immune responses and ICI effectiveness.

While both the antibiotic and PPI groups showed a trend of decreased efficacy, probiotics were associated with improved PFS, ORR and OS. This indicates that probiotics may enhance the effectiveness of ICIs, while the other treatments could have the opposite effect. This discrepancy could arise from several factors: modest changes in ORR or PFS could increase the risk of discrepancies between early endpoints and OS if toxic effects become pronounced. Furthermore, significant improvements in PFS or ORR might negatively impact OS if treatment-related toxicities lead to mortality. Although adverse events may not be fatal, they can hinder patients’ ability to fully benefit from combination therapies or affect their eligibility for subsequent effective treatments, ultimately compromising OS. Several mechanistic hypotheses could explain these differential effects. The adverse impact of antibiotics might stem from their broad-spectrum elimination of beneficial gut bacteria that are essential for optimal immune responses. Similarly, PPIs may alter the gut microenvironment through pH modulation, potentially disrupting the delicate balance of immunomodulatory bacterial species. Conversely, probiotics might enhance ICI efficacy by promoting the growth of beneficial bacteria that support anti-tumor immunity, although the precise mechanisms require further investigation.

Interestingly, our subgroup analyses revealed that the timing of antibiotic or PPI administration—whether before or during ICI therapy—consistently resulted in poorer outcomes. This temporal independence suggests that maintaining gut microbiome integrity throughout the treatment journey is crucial for optimal ICI efficacy. These findings have important clinical implications, advocating for judicious use of antibiotics and PPIs in cancer patients receiving immunotherapy.

Furthermore, unlike traditional cytotoxic drugs, the unique mechanism of novel anti-tumor drugs, such as ICIs, may change tumor growth dynamics rather than exerting their effects solely through direct cytotoxicity. While conventional chemotherapy primarily relies on direct tumor cell killing, ICIs function by remodeling the immune landscape, making them particularly susceptible to microbiome-mediated effects. This distinction emphasizes the need for specialized approaches when managing supportive care in immunotherapy patients.

From the research self, the GRADE assessments highlight important differences in evidence certainty across interventions. The negative impact of PPIs on OS and PFS was supported by moderate-certainty evidence, indicating a clinically relevant risk factor that should be carefully considered. For antibiotics, the evidence for ORR was also of moderate certainty, but evidence for survival outcomes was downgraded to low certainty due to high heterogeneity and suspected publication bias, warranting cautious interpretation. Most importantly, the apparent benefits of probiotics were supported mainly by low to moderate certainty evidence, indicating that current results—derived largely from observational studies with heterogeneity—should be considered hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory. Future well-designed randomized controlled trials are urgently needed to provide higher-certainty evidence. Meanwhile, our meta-regression analyses provided quantitative evidence for sources of heterogeneity. For example, the negative impact of antibiotics on OS varied significantly across treatment time windows and treatment regimens, together explaining over 50% of the observed heterogeneity. These findings suggest that future research design and clinical decision-making should explicitly account for these factors.

In conclusion, our findings underscore the critical importance of considering gut microbiome effects when managing patients receiving ICIs. This work provides a foundation for developing evidence-based guidelines for concurrent medication use during immunotherapy, potentially leading to improved patient outcomes through optimized treatment strategies.

Limitations

Though this research discussed the possibility of the interference of gut microbiota in cancer ICI treatment, the factors we only discussed about three major points: antibiotics, PPIs and probiotics, which doesn’t conclude all the factors that interference with the gut microbiome. The complexity of the gut microbiome-immune system interaction extends beyond these interventions, but current literature limitations restrict a more comprehensive analysis. Furthermore, considerable heterogeneity existed among the included studies in terms of patient populations, cancer types, ICI regimens, and timing of interventions. The definitions and durations of antibiotic, PPIs, and probiotic exposure varied across studies, potentially affecting the consistency of our findings. Many studies lacked detailed information about concurrent medications, dietary factors, and lifestyle variables that could confound the relationship between gut microbiota and ICI outcomes. Additionally, the mechanistic understanding of how these interventions influence ICI efficacy remains incomplete. Most studies focused on clinical outcomes without extensive investigation of the underlying biological pathways, limiting our ability to establish causal relationships.

Thus, according to our Meta-analysis, more clinical trials on gut microbiome affected on gut microbiome, then to the cancer treatment needs to be done. Future studies should focus on:

(1) Investigating the impact of specific probiotic strains and antibiotic classes on ICI efficacy and immune-related adverse events (irAEs); (2) Implementing standardized protocols for measuring and reporting gut microbiome composition and function in relation to ICI response and toxicity. (3) Developing personalized strategies for modulating the gut microbiome to optimize ICI treatment outcomes; (4) Establishing longer follow-up periods to assess the durability of microbiome-related interventions on ICI outcomes.

Although our subgroup analyses followed methodological standards, some subgroups (e.g., the melanoma antibiotic–ORR subgroup) had limited sample sizes or too few included studies, resulting in low statistical power. These results should be regarded as exploratory and require confirmation in larger-scale future studies.

Furthermore, though the overall study quality was high (NOS ≥ 7), the major limitation of this meta-analysis lies in the inherent bias of observational designs, particularly indication confounding. Patients with more severe disease (e.g., higher ECOG PS scores or infectious complications) generally have worse outcomes and are also more likely to receive antibiotics. This baseline imbalance could inflate the apparent negative impact of antibiotics on ICI efficacy. While most studies (76.9%) adjusted for key prognostic factors such as ECOG PS and cancer stage using multivariable models, only a minority (approximately 23.2%) adequately adjusted for infection severity—a critical confounder. Thus, although our sensitivity analyses demonstrated robust pooled estimates, the possibility of residual confounding remains. This fundamental limitation can only be addressed by future prospective randomized controlled trials. Accordingly, our findings should be interpreted as demonstrating a strong association, rather than definitive causality.

Conclusion

Our meta-analysis provides evidence for the potential impact of gut microbiome-targeting therapies on ICI treatment outcomes. While further research is needed to confirm these findings and establish clinical guidelines, our results highlight the importance of considering the gut microbiome in the context of ICI therapy. This knowledge may pave the way for personalized approaches to optimize ICI efficacy and minimize adverse events.
