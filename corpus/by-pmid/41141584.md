# Smart dendrimer nanogels boost mRNA-based cancer therapy via synergistic glycolysis inhibition and immune activation.

## Abstract

Cancer mRNA vaccine has emerged as a promising immunotherapy approach. However, development of functional vehicles for safe and efficient mRNA delivery into immune cells (e.g., dendritic cells, DCs) remains to be challenging, and most of the mRNA-based vaccines just act on immune cells for improved anticancer immunity without additional input to tackle cancer cells, ultimately limiting the anticancer efficacy. Herein, we report the development of pH-responsive dual-action dendrimer nanogels (DNGs) to deliver gp100 mRNA as a potential nanovaccine. Mannose-conjugated generation 3 poly(amidoamine) dendrimers were crosslinked through dual functional polyethylene glycol with both ends of aldehyde groups to form pH-responsive DNGs. The DNGs with a size of 45.7 nm show excellent colloidal stability and cytocompatibility and allow dual-actions for DC-targeted mRNA delivery and cancer cell glycolysis inhibition. In a subcutaneous mouse melanoma model, the DNG/mRNA vaccine exerted glycolytic inhibition effect and triggered robust antitumor immune responses to suppress the tumor growth, especially in combination with anti-PD-L1 antibody-mediated immune checkpoint blockade. Meanwhile, the local vaccination of the vaccine enabled effective tumor occurrence prevention. Such a DNG-based mRNA vaccine with dual actions on both DCs and cancer cells may be applied for improved immunotherapy of other cancer types.

Introduction

Vaccines represent one of the mainstream approaches in cancer immunotherapy, which induces specific anticancer immune response to recognize and eliminate cancer cells. Based on the types of antigens, cancer vaccines can be categorized into whole-cell vaccines, nucleic acid vaccines, and protein or peptide vaccines, and these vaccine formulations are often accompanied with the addition of suitable adjuvants. Among the current vaccine types, messenger RNA (mRNA)-based vaccines have recently emerged as one of the most promising types with a worldwide focus, especially driven by the global COVID-19 pandemic. The mRNA-based vaccine activates the host immune system and realizes tumor vaccination through encoding tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), tumor specific antigens or other tumor-associated cytokines into the major antigen presenting cells, mostly the dendritic cells (DCs). This then triggers strong major histocompatibility complex (MHC)-mediated cytotoxic CD8+ T cell responses to kill tumors with high expression of specific antigens, thereby achieving tumor killing effects with immune memory protection. Compared to other cancer vaccine formulations, mRNA cancer vaccine has remarkable advantages, including rapid scale-up production, good tolerance and safety, and low cost. Up to now, over twenty mRNA cancer vaccines have been investigated in clinical trials. However, the vulnerability and negative charge of mRNA greatly limits its cellular uptake as well as in vivo implementation. Therefore, efficient mRNA carrier systems are urgently needed to improve the cytosolic delivery and antigen presentation efficiency.

With increasing advances in nanotechnology, a variety of colloidal nanoplatforms have become promising options for the delivery of various drugs, imaging agents, as well as biomacromolecules, which may be one feasible solution for mRNA delivery. For example, lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) have been widely adopted in pre-clinical or clinical mRNA vaccine formulations to prevent mRNA degradation and to promote its intracellular delivery and endo/lysosomal escape. However, LNPs are generally suffered from side effects including over-inflammations, cytotoxicity, and instability. Hence, more recently, various polymeric NPs, LNP/polymer hybrid NPs, and natural nanovesicles have been developed as carriers to enhance the vaccination safety. Yet still, it represents a challenge to develop functional or responsive nanoplatforms with a favorable biosafety profile and mRNA delivery efficiency.

Among the commonly developed nanoplatforms, polymeric nanogels (NGs) are currently gaining momentum due to their unique properties, including shape flexibility, small size, and excellent colloidal stability. In particular, by varying the monomers, crosslinkers, and initiators of NGs, various functional groups and stimuli-responsive properties can be introduced into the polymer networks. This enables synthesis of biocompatible and adaptive NG systems with decent drug payload and responsiveness to various triggers. Meanwhile, dendrimers, especially poly(amidoamine) (PAMAM) dendrimers with well-defined architecture, spherical shape, and rich amine termini, have attracted wide interests due to their intrinsic advantages as excellent carrier materials for various functional substances including imaging agents, drugs, proteins, and gene sequences including mRNA. The development of dendrimer-based NGs is expected to integrate the advantages of both dendrimers and NGs, potentially serving as an ideal platform for mRNA vaccine development. In our previous work, we have shown that generation 3 (G3) PAMAM dendrimers can be functionalized and adopted as both the macromonomers or crosslinkers to form dendrimer NGs (DNGs) for effective cancer treatment and diagnosis. However, none of these studies have involved the mRNA vaccine development for effective cancer immunotherapy.

To introduce robust tumor antigens into DCs, it is useful to render the mRNA delivery systems with DC-targeting ability. Mannose (Man) modification or coating has been a widely investigated approach to render the nanocarriers with active DC-targeting specificity through interaction with the Man receptors expressed on the DCs. In a recent study, Man-coated nanocapsules were used to carry mRNA to DCs, and this delivery system enables excellent mRNA translation for strong DCs activation and successive antigen-specific CD4+ and CD8+ T cell responses to kill tumors. In our earlier research, we also showed that Man-modified dendrimers enable targeted siRNA delivery to DCs for improved antigen presentation to achieve antitumor immunity. In addition to the targeting specificity, Man also shows a remarkable feature to suppress the tumor growth through interfering the metabolism of glucose in active tumor glycolysis. Hence, Man-containing nanosystems may be used for cancer mRNA vaccine development to play dual roles of DCs targeting and cancer cell glycolysis inhibition, which unfortunately has not been explored in the literature to our best knowledge.

In this study, we aim to develop pH-responsive DNGs using Man-modified G3 PAMAM dendrimers to deliver gp100 mRNA encoding the melanoma-associated antigen as a cancer nanovaccine (Fig. 1). We employed a crosslinking reaction between the amine groups on G3 dendrimers and the aldehyde groups of dual functional polyethylene glycol (PEG) to produce pH-responsive DNGs. The Schiff base-bonded DNGs are expected to be cleaved under the slightly acidic tumor microenvironment (TME) or lysosomal environment, leading to fast Man release at tumor site for tumor glycolysis inhibition. Additionally, the delivered gp100 mRNA should be translated into antigens, triggering an antitumor immune response. Such design of DNGs is expected to achieve safe and efficient delivery of mRNA to DCs as well as Man delivery to cancer cells for glycolysis inhibition, thus achieving improved dual-action cancer immunotherapy. According to our thorough literature investigation, this study represents the first example of DNG-based mRNA vaccine with a dual-action mechanism for the treatment and prevention of tumors.

Materials and method

Preparation of the DNGs

G3 PAMAM dendrimers with amine termini (G3.NH2) were partially modified with Br-PBA and Man to form G3.NH2-PBA-Man dendrimers. A dual functional FBA-PEG-FBA was also obtained. Afterwards, the obtained G3.NH2-PBA-Man (20 mg) dendrimer and FBA-PEG-FBA (21.6 mg) were co-dissolved in 1 mL of water as the aqueous phase. Next, the aqueous phase was added dropwise into an oil phase composed of Span 80/Tween 80 (5/1, w/w, totally 280 mg in 12 mL of cyclohexane) to form a water/oil (W/O) emulsion. The mixture was sonicated at 20 W for 10 min to homogenize. Then, 500 μL of ethylenediamine (EDA) was added to the above mixture under stirring overnight at room temperature to initiate the crosslinking reaction between G3.NH2-PBA-Man and FBA-PEG-FBA. The resulting solution was centrifuged at 12000 rpm, the supernatant was discarded, and the precipitate was re-suspended in 5 mL of ethanol. The suspension was next dialyzed against water for 3 days, and finally lyophilized to get the DNGs.

Compaction of gp100 mRNA (mgp100) by DNG

The DNGs with an optimized molar ratio of G3.NH2-PBA-Man/FBA-PEG-FBA at 1: 4 were used to compact mRNA. The DNGs were mixed with mgp100 in diethylpyrocarbonate water at various mass ratios of DNG/mgp100 (2, 4, 8, 12 and 16, respectively) to form the DNG/mgp100 polyplexes.

In vitro assays

Murine B16F10 melanoma cells and DCs (murine-derived immature bone marrow-derived DCs) were regularlly cultured and passaged for in vitro assays including cytotoxicity, cellular uptake, intracellular trafficking and localization, transfection of mRNA encoding enhanced green fluorescent protein (mEGFP), glycolysis inhibition in vitro, in vitro DC maturation and antigen presentation. Mouse RBCs were also obtained for hemolysis assay of the DNGs.

In vivo antitumor experiments and immunotherapy mechanism investigations

All animal experiments were carried out after approval by the Ethical Committee for Experimental Animal Care and Use of Donghua University (approval number: DHUEC-NSFC-2023-22) and also according to the policy of the National Ministry of Health of China. A subcutaneous mouse B16F10 melanoma model was established to evaluate the in vivo antitumor efficacy of the DNG/mgp100 vaccine. Tumor bearing mice received different treatments of PBS, mgp100, DNGs, DNG/mgp100, or DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1, respectively on days 1, 3, and 5 through peritumoral injection. For the last group, aPD-L1 (2.25 mg/kg) was administered peritumorally on days 2, 4, and 6. The tumor volume and body weight of mice were regularly monitored for 11 consecutive days. At 11 days post treatments, the mice were sacrificed to extract the tumors, blood samples, spleens, and lymph nodes for detailed evaluation of the antitumor efficiency and mechanisms including glycolytic inhibition tests, histological/immunofluorescence examinations, enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) of serum, spleen cell suspension flow cytometry analysis and ELISA, lymph node cell suspension flow cytometry analysis. Furthermore, in vivo tumor vaccination was also performed to evaluate the tumor preventive effect of the DNG/mgp100 vaccine through monitoring the tumor volume, histological examinations of tumor sections, serum ELISA, as well as spleen cell suspension/lymph node cell suspension flow cytometry analyses. DNG-Cy5.5/mgp100 polyplexes were prepared to examine their biodistribution through fluorescence imaging in vivo and ex vivo. Lastly, the biosafety of the vaccine was investigated by hematoxylin & eosin (H&E) staining of major organ slices and blood routine and biochemistry assays. See full experimental details in the Supporting Information.

Statistical analysis

One-way analysis of variance statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism 8.0.2 software (San Diego, CA) to evaluate the significance of the experimental data. A p value of 0.05 was selected as the significance level, and the data were indicated with (*) for p < 0.05, (**) for p < 0.01, and (***) for p < 0.001, respectively.

Results and discussion

Synthesis and characterization of the DNGs

Man- and phenylboronic acid (PBA)-modified G3 PAMAM dendrimers (G3-PBA-Man) were first obtained to serve as the macromonomers of the DNGs (Fig. 2a). As revealed in the 1H NMR spectrum, each G3 dendrimer was conjugated with approximately 6.1 PBA (Fig. S1) by integrating the NMR peaks of PBA protons (7.0–8.0 ppm) with the dendrimer methylene protons (2.2–3.5 ppm). Man was then conjugated onto the dendrimers through borate ester bond with the 4-(bromomethyl) phenylboronic acid (Br-PBA), and through NMR integration of the proton peaks at 3.5–3.7 ppm associated to Man protons, we found that there were 3.6 Man molecules grafted onto each G3 dendrimer (Fig. S2). Meanwhile, the pH-sensitive crosslinker of PEG with two end hydroxyl groups modified by p-formylbenzoic acid (FBA, for short, FBA-PEG-FBA) was also synthesized and verified by 1H NMR (Fig. S3). According to the NMR integration, each PEG was linked with 1.5 FBA moieties, ensuring the succeeding crosslinking reaction to form DNGs.

The synthesized G3-PBA-Man dendrimers were then reacted with FBA-PEG-FBA under different molar ratios using a microemulsion method to form the DNGs. The crosslinking occurred through the reaction between the primary amine groups on G3-PBA-Man dendrimers and the aldehyde groups on the FBA-PEG-FBA chains. It should be noted that the linear PEG-based crosslinker plays dual roles in stabilizing the NG architecture through intermolecular bridging and promoting the formation of monodisperse spherical NG particles with enhanced stability. As can be found in Fig. S4, the products under all the investigated molar ratios show a milky white appearance, intuitively indicating the successful formation of DNGs. Dynamic light scattering (DLS) and zeta potential results demonstrated that the DNGs synthesized under the dendrimer/crosslinker molar feeding ratio of 1: 4 possessed the smallest hydrodynamic size (110.7 nm) and polydispersity index (PDI, 0.16) along with a positive zeta potential of 18.6 mV (Table S1). Consequently, this molar ratio was chosen to get the optimal DNGs for subsequent experiments.

Next, we employed various characterization techniques to analyze the physicochemical properties of DNGs. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy was used to characterize the structures of G3-PBA-Man, FBA-PEG-FBA, and DNGs (Fig. 2b). The emergence of absorption peak around 1650 cm−1 in the spectrum of DNGs can be ascribed to the stretching vibrations of N=C bonds, confirming the successful Schiff base formation to induce the crosslinking of DNGs. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Fig. 2c) and TEM (Fig. 2d–e) imaging were applied to investigate the morphology of the DNGs. The size distribution histograms revealed that the optimized DNGs exhibited a regular spherical morphology with a mean particle size of approximately 51.1 nm and 45.7 nm, respectively, both smaller than that obtained from the DLS measurements. This is likely due to the fact that DLS measures DNGs in water, and the size measured reflect the hydrated and swelled NGs that may happen to have a certain degree of aggregation, while SEM and TEM measure the size of individual particles in a dry state, in agreement with the literature. The slight larger size of the DNGs measured by SEM than measured by TEM should be due to the SEM sample preparation process involving the sputter coating of a thin gold film with a thickness of 10 nm. Furthermore, atomic force microscopy (AFM) imaging revealed that the height of DNGs was approximately 2.7–3.2 nm, with a full width at half maximum of 42 nm (close to the NG size measured by TEM), yielding a diameter-to-height ratio of 13.1–15.6 (Fig. S5). Notably, the measured height of DNGs was significantly smaller than their diameter as determined by SEM or TEM. This can be ascribed to the fact that the DNGs with a swelling behavior has a collapsed morphology in a dry state, hence having very different dimensions of height and diameter.

To validate the pH-responsiveness, DNGs were dispersed in different buffer solutions and the hydrodynamic size was measured by DLS. We observed that the particle size distribution of DNGs shifted from a single peak at pH 7.4 to double peaks at pH 6.5 (Fig. 2f–g), suggesting the pH-responsive dissociation of DNGs. DLS was also applied to evaluate the colloidal stability of the DNGs in various aqueous media including water, PBS, and Dulbecco's Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM). The DNGs showed minimal hydrodynamic size changes in the three media within 7 days (Fig. 2h), indicating their excellent colloidal stability. We further investigated the responsive release of Man from DNGs (Fig. 2i), and the release of Man was found to significantly increase under an acidic condition (pH 6.5), where the released Man content at 12 h was up to 25 %, much higher than under the physiological condition (pH 7.4, 16 %). Meanwhile, the addition of H2O2 also accelerated the Man release, reaching 29 % at 12 h, likely due to the accelerated cleavage of boronate ester bonds, and under dual stimulation of low pH and H2O2, the Man was released rapidly to achieve 38 % at 12 h.

Cytocompatibility of DNGs

For safe mRNA delivery to DCs, we next assessed the cytotoxicity of DNGs against DCs and compared the DNGs with a commercially available gene vector, Lipo8000. It was found that even at a concentration of 500 μg/mL, DNGs maintained the DC viability above 90 % (Fig. 3a). In sharp contrast, the commercial Lipo8000 exhibited significant toxicity at a concentration of 40 μg/mL, resulting in cell viability of only around 10 % (Fig. 3b). These findings demonstrate the excellent cytocompatibility of our DNG carrier, superior to the commercial vector. Subsequently, we evaluated the effect of DNGs or DNGs-control on the viability of B16F10 cells. At equivalent DNG concentrations, DNGs induced significantly greater growth inhibition of B16F10 cells than the DNGs-control (Fig. S6, p < 0.001 for DNG concentration of 200 μg/mL or above). This effect may be attributed to glycolysis inhibition resulting from the Man component in the DNGs (see below). Furthermore, a hemolysis assay was conducted to evaluate the hemocompatibility of DNGs. As depicted in Fig. S7, even at a DNG concentration of up to 300 μg/mL, the hemolysis rate was still less than the threshold value of 5 %, indicating the adequate hemocompatibility of DNGs for potential in vivo applications.

DNGs for cancer cell glycolysis inhibition and effective mRNA delivery to DCs

As Man allows inhibition of the cancer cell glycolysis, so we first tested whether our Man-incorporated DNGs can act as a metabolic inhibitor as well. It was found that both the downstream metabolite of lactate (Fig. 3c) and the intracellular ATP content (Fig. 3d) in B16F10 cancer cells were downregulated after the DNG treatment as compared to the PBS group. The DNG-treated group showed a greater reduction in both lactate and ATP levels than the free Man treatment (p < 0.05). To further investigate the underlying mechanism, we measured the intracellular reducing sugar content in B16F10 cells. The DNG group showed a more significant increase in reducing sugars than the free Man group (Fig. S8), possibly due to the superior cellular internalization capacity of DNGs to free Man that led to a higher intracellular Man concentration in the DNG-treated cells than in free Man-treated cells. We further investigated the effect of DNGs on glycolysis of DCs (Fig. S9). Compared to the PBS group, neither DNGs nor free Man treatment induced significant changes in intracellular lactate or ATP levels, indicating that DNGs do not affect glycolytic activity in DCs. The differential response between B16F10 and DCs may be attributed to their distinct sensitivities to Man at the administered concentration. Furthermore, we investigated the effect of tumor glycolysis suppression on PD-L1 expression in B16F10 cells through flow cytometry assays (Fig. S10). Clearly, the DNG treatment significantly upregulated cell surface PD-L1 expression when compared to other groups (p < 0.001). As the improved immune check point (PD-L1) of cancer cells would render cancer cells to escape the T cell-based surveillance, thereby compromising CD8+ T cell-mediated tumor cell killing, it is necessary to combine an immune checkpoint blockade strategy to counteract tumor immune evasion (see below).

The ability of DNGs for compacting the tumor antigen mgp100 was then assessed using agarose gel electrophoresis (Fig. S11). The results revealed that at a DNG/mgp100 mass ratio of 8: 1 or higher, DNG were able to completely compact the mgp100, indicating its effectiveness as a nanovehicle. We subsequently evaluated the mRNA delivery efficiency of DNGs. In this case, Cy3-labeled EGFP mRNA (Cy3-mEGFP) was compacted with DNGs under different mass ratios. The results showed that at the mass ratio of 48, DCs exhibited the strong fluorescence intensity (Fig. 3e–f), significantly larger than at other mass ratios (p < 0.001), indicating the maximum cellular uptake. Therefore, we selected this mass ratio as the optimal one for subsequent mRNA transfection experiments. To be noted, at the mass ratio of 48, DNGs also showed a better delivery efficiency than the commercial vector Lipo8000, again demonstrating the advantages of the DNG carrier. Additionally, the intracellular localization of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP was observed using confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM). Following incubation with DNG/Cy3-mEGFP, DCs displayed strong Cy3-associated red fluorescence signals within the cytoplasm, demonstrating effective mRNA delivery (Fig. 3g). In contrast, control groups treated with PBS or free Cy3-mEGFP showed only minimal Cy3 signals in the DCs. Moreover, we validated that compared to the Man-free DNGs-control, the DNGs enabled effective DC targeting for enhanced Cy3-mEGFP cellular uptake (Fig. 3h–i, p < 0.001). Furthermore, pre-blocking with free Man significantly reduced the cellular uptake of DNGs by DCs (Fig. 3h–i, p < 0.001), underscoring the critical role of Man in the targeting specificity of DNGs. To further determine the targeting of DNGs is specific to DCs, we compared the cellular uptake of DNGs between DCs and L929 cells (a mouse fibroblast cell line) via flow cytometry. Clearly, the uptake of DNGs by DCs was significantly higher than by L929 cells (Fig. S12, p < 0.001), indicating that Man-containing DNGs have DC-specific targeting ability. Together, the DNGs enabled glycolysis inhibition of cancer cells along with effective delivery of mRNA to DCs.

Lysosomal escape and uptake pathway of the DNG/mRNA polyplexes

Lysosomal escape ability is essential for gene delivery systems for effective transfection, so we utilized CLSM to observe the intracellular co-localization of the DNG/Cy3-mgp100 polyplexes with lysosomes in DCs at various time points (Fig. 4a–b). Upon treatment with the polyplexes, a time-dependent increase in red fluorescence of DNG/Cy3-mgp100-treated DCs can be seen. At the time point of 6 h, the red (Cy3-mgp100) and green (lysosomes) signals began to co-localize in DCs, forming orange spots in the merged images, which indicated that the polyplexes entered into lysosomes at 6 h. At 8 h of incubation, the red signals were gradually dissociated from the green ones, and the colocalization index of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP within lysosomes significantly dropped from 0.86 at 6 h to 0.33 at 8 h, suggesting the successful lysosome escape of Cy3-mgp100. These findings confirm that our DNGs enable effective lysosome escape of the delivered mRNA.

Furthermore, the cellular uptake mechanism of the DNG/Cy3-mEGFP was also investigated. DCs were pre-incubated with different endocytosis inhibitors such as methyl-β-cyclodextrin (M-β-CD), amiloride hydrochloride (Ami) or genistein before treatment of the polyplexes. The effect of temperature on cellular uptake was also examined by incubating the cells at 4 °C. Notably, the cellular uptake of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP by DCs was significantly inhibited after incubation at 4 °C or treatment with M-β-CD (p < 0.001), and there was also a slight decrease after treatment with Ami (p < 0.01). These results indicated that the cellular uptake of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP polyplexes by DCs was energy-dependent and primarily mediated by lipid raft-associated endocytosis, also partially via micropinocytosis (Fig. 4c–d). We also investigated the uptake mechanism of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP polyplexes by B16F10 cells using the same method (Fig. S13). Both M-β-CD treatment and incubation at 4 °C significantly inhibited the cellular uptake of DNGs, indicating that the cellular uptake of DNG/Cy3-mEGFP polyplexes is energy-dependent and primarily mediated through lipid raft-dependent endocytosis pathway.

In vitro mRNA transfection for DCs maturation

We then continued to evaluate the in vitro mRNA transfection efficiency of the DNGs in DCs. As depicted in Fig. 5a–b, both the DNG/mEGFP and Lipo8000/mEGFP polyplexes induced strikingly stronger green fluorescence signals than free mEGFP, indicating a substantial enhancement of EGFP expression. Notably, quantitative analysis revealed that the DNG delivery system more significantly facilitated mEGFP expression than the positive control Lipo8000 (p < 0.001). Further visualization by CLSM also showed that the DNG/mEGFP group exhibited apparent EGFP fluorescence signals within the cytoplasm, much stronger that PBS or free mEGFP groups (Fig. 5c), where almost no EGFP-associated green fluorescence signals were detected.

Afterwards, we investigated the effect of mgp100 transfection on DCs maturation (Fig. 5d). The DNG-mediated mgp100 delivery led to increased proportion of MHC-II+gp100+ DCs, suggesting the presentation of the gp100 tumor antigen. Quantitatively, compared to the PBS and free mgp100 groups, DCs treated with DNG/mgp100 exhibited the most significantly increased expression of MHC II (Fig. S14, p < 0.01) and gp100 (Fig. 5e, p < 0.001) among all groups. Meanwhile, the transfection of mgp100 effectively promoted DCs maturation, leading to 84.9 % of maturated DCs, and the expressions of CD80 and CD86 on DCs in the DNG/mgp100 group were more significant than in the other groups including the positive control (lipopolysaccharide, LPS, Fig. 5f, Fig. S15–S16, p < 0.001). Furthermore, the DNG carrier alone induced less dendritic cell maturation than the DNG/mgp100 polyplex. This indicates that the observed maturation is primarily attributable to the mgp100-encoded antigen delivered by the DNG platform, rather than the carrier itself. Therefore, we can conclude that our nanovaccine effectively activates DCs for TAA presentation.

In vivo lymph node targeting

With the encouraging results in vitro, we then started to investigate the potential of DNG/mgp100 for in vivo applications. Firstly, we labeled the DNGs or DNGs-control with Cy5.5 dye and checked their lymph node targeting by in vivo and ex vivo fluorescence imaging of a B16F10 melanoma subcutaneous mouse model. Mice were anesthetized and subjected to fluorescence imaging at different time points (0.5, 1 or 2 h) after peritumoral injection of DNG-Cy5.5/mgp100 or DNG-control-Cy5.5/mgp100. As depicted in Fig. S17a and 17c, bright red fluorescence signals at the tumor region were observed over time. Additionally, at 2 h post-injection, major organs, tumor tissue, and lymph node were collected from mice for ex-vivo fluorescence imaging (Fig. S17b and 17d). The brightest fluorescence signal was observed in the lymph node of mouse treated with DNG-Cy5.5/mgp100, significantly higher than the DNG-control-Cy5.5/mgp100 group. This demonstrated the superior lymph node accumulation ability of the Man-containing DNG-Cy5.5/mgp100, likely by Man-mediated in vivo lymph node targeting. These results indicate that the developed DNG/mgp100 can be accumulated in the tumor site after peritumoral injection, and then be accumulated to the lymph nodes to elicit immune responses (see below).

DNG/mgp100 as a therapeutic vaccine in vivo

The in vivo antitumor efficacy of the mRNA vaccine was checked in a B16F10 subcutaneous mouse tumor model as schduled in Fig. 6a. As is known, when PD-1/PD-L1 immune checkpoint inhibitors are combined with tumor vaccines, the induced efficacy of immunotherapy can be enhanced since the treatment of tumors through different modes often leads to the overexpression of PD-L1 on tumor cells. For the in vivo treatment, we additionally combined immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) with the DNG/mgp100 treatment by using aPD-L1. During the 11-day treatment period, it was observed that the tumor growth of mice in the PBS and mgp100 group was quite rapid, and there were mouse deaths before the end of treatment. In the DNG group, the tumor growth was effectively inhibited likely due to the glycolysis inhibition of tumors mediated by the released Man, but there are still mouse deaths during the treatment. In contrast, in the DNG/mgp100 and DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 groups, all mice survived during the treatment and the tumor burden was also greatly alleviated, especially there were two mice observed without tumors in the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 group. The corresponding tumor photos on the last day of treatment also indicated the excellent antitumor efficacy of the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 treatment (Fig. S18). As shown in Fig. 6b, the relative tumor volume on the last day for each group followed an order of DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 (1.9) < DNG/mgp100 (7.9) < DNG (8.1) < mgp100 (21.0) < PBS (28.4). In addition, there were no significant differences in body weight changes among the different groups (Fig. 6c), demonstrating that there were no systemic safety risks for all the investigated materials.

Furthermore, we extracted the spleen and lymph node cell suspension for flow cytometry analysis to study the in vivo immune responses. Firstly, the spleen T cell distribution was checked (Fig. 6d), and gated as CD8+ or CD4+ T cells (Fig. S19). The DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 group exhibited the most significantly enhanced levels of CD8+ (Fig. 6e, p < 0.05) and CD4+ (Fig. 6f, p < 0.001) T cells in the mouse spleens. We further investigated the activation of memory T cells within splenic CD4+ T cell populations following different treatments (Fig. S20). Clearly, the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 treatment significantly increased the proportion of effector memory T cells (TEM). We also observed the most significant expression of gp100 in spleen DCs in the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 group among all groups (Fig. S21, p < 0.001). Additionally, the mouse lymph nodes were extracted to check the percentage of activated DCs (CD11c+CD86+) and gp100-expressing DCs. The DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 treatment group exhibited the highest percentage of activated DCs among all groups (Fig. 6g, Fig. S22a, p < 0.001).

Furthermore, both the DNG/mgp100 and the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 groups demonstrated the greater percentages of gp100 expression (Fig. 6h, p < 0.001, and Fig. S22b) than other groups. These results, together indicate the strongest antitumor immune response in the last experimental group. We also employed ELISA, TUNEL, Ki67 and CD4/CD8 immunofluorescence staining to assess tumor cytokine and chemokine profiles, cell apoptosis, proliferation and immune cell infiltration after different treatments. The DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 treatment significantly elevated the levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-2, IFN-γ, and TNF-α) and chemokines (CXCL9) within the tumor microenvironment (Fig. S23), indicating the induction of an antitumor immune response in mice. Clearly, the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 treatment led to the most significant increase in tumor cell apoptosis, decrease in tumor cell proliferation, and increase in CD4+ and CD8+ T cell infiltration (Fig. S24).

To be noted, by detecting the tumoral ATP and lactate levels in each group,we found a significant reduction of lactate and ATP levels within tumors in the last three groups (Fig. 6i–j, p < 0.001), indicating that the DNG carrier effectively suppressed tumor glycolysis in vivo. Meanwhile, it was revealed that the final treatment group led to a significant increase in IFN-γ level in mouse serum (Fig. S25, p < 0.001) and a notable elevation of IL-2 level in splenocyte supernatant (Fig. S26, p < 0.01). Therefore, it can be concluded that the developed DNG/mgp100 can be applied as a therapeutic vaccine for tumor treatment, especially when combined with the aPD-L1 antibody-mediated ICB.

To assess the in vivo biosafety of the vaccine, we dissected the major organs from tumor-bearing mice in each experimental group at 11 days post treatments for H&E staining. No pathological abnormalities was observed (Fig. S27), indicating the good biosafety of all applied materials. Meanwhile, H&E stainings of tumor tissue in the treated groups showed different degrees of tumor cell necrosis, and the DNG/mgp100 + aPD-L1 group showed the most pronounced necrosis among all groups. Additionally, we collected mouse blood samples for blood routine analysis of white blood cells (WBCs, Fig. S28a), red blood cells (RBCs, Fig. S28b), hemoglobin (HGB, Fig. S28c), hematocrit (HCT, Fig. S28d), mean corpuscular hemoglobin concentration (MCHC, Fig. S28e) and platelet (PLT, Fig. S28f), as well as blood biochemistry assays of alanine aminotransferase (ALT, Fig. S29a), alkaline phosphatase (ALP, Fig. S29b), uric acid (UA, Fig. S29c), and triglycerides (TG, Fig. S29d). All tested parameters remained within the normal ranges for all treatment groups, further validating the biosafety of the studied materials.

DNG/mgp100 as a prophylactic vaccine in vivo

The prophylactic effects of the DNG/mgp100 vaccine was also tested, and the vaccination schedule is shown in Fig. 7a. As depicted in Fig. 7b–c, the administration of DNG/mgp100 effectively slowed down the tumor growth in mice. On the last day of observation, the tumor size in the DNG/mgp100 group was much smaller than that in the PBS group, and one mouse were found with no tumor grown (Fig. S30). At the same time, the body weight changes in the PBS- and DNG/mgp100-vaccinated groups did not differ too much, again, indicating the biosafety profile of the DNG/mgp100 vaccine (Fig. 7d).

After the 17-day study period, we dissected the mouse lymph nodes and extracted the cell suspension to detect gp100 expression on lymphoid DCs. The DNG/mgp100 group exhibited higher expression of gp100 on DCs than the PBS group (Fig. 7e–f, p < 0.001), suggesting the enhanced DC antigen presentation efficiency. Additionally, we extracted the spleen cell suspension to assess the distribution of central memory T cells (TCM) and TEM in the spleens of each mouse. An increased TCM/TEM expressions in the DNG/mgp100 group were observed, significantly surpassing the PBS control group (Fig. 7g–h, p < 0.001). This indicates a robust immune memory response following DNG/mgp100 vaccination. The cytokines including TNF-α (Fig. 7i), IL-6 (Fig. 7j), IFN-γ (Fig. 7k) and IL-2 (Fig. 7l) in serum were also found to be much higher in the DNG/mgp100 group than in the PBS group (p < 0.001). This suggests that the developed DNG/mgp100 vaccine elicits strong antitumor immune response. By Ki67 staining of the tumor tissues, it was also observed that the treatment of DNG/mgp100 reduced the tumor cell proliferation effectively when compared to the PBS control (Fig. S31). To assess the in vivo biosafety of the vaccination, we dissected major organs from tumor-bearing mice in the two groups after the 17-day treatment period for H&E staining. Major organs in the PBS and DNG/mgp100 groups did not reveal any pathological abnormalities (Fig. S32), indicating the good biosafety profile. These results showed that the developed DNG/mgp100 was safe and could be used as an effective prophylactic vaccine in vivo.

Conclusion

We developed a pH-responsive DNG carrier for loading tumor antigen mgp100 to serve as a potential nanovaccine formulation. Man-conjugated G3 PAMAM dendrimers through dual H2O2/pH-responsive boronate ester bond were used as the macromonomers to be crosslinked with FBA-PEG-FBA through Schiff base bonding, thus forming pH-responsive DNGs via a microemulsion method. The DNGs with a size around 45.7 nm shows excellent stability and cytocompatibility. With the Man-mediated targeting specificity to DCs and metabolic intervention to cancer cells, the DNGs allow dual-actions for DC-targeted mRNA delivery and cancer cell glycolysis inhibition, respectively. We show that in a subcutaneous mouse melanoma model, the DNG/mgp100 vaccine triggers robust antitumor immune responses to suppress the tumor growth, especially in combination with aPD-L1 antibody-mediated ICB. Meanwhile, local muscular vaccination of the DNG/mgp100 vaccine effectively slows down the tumor occurrence and development. Therefore, the developed DNG-based mRNA delivery vaccine holds a great promise both as a therapeutic and prophylactic nanovaccine, and should be extendable to tackle other cancer types for improved mRNA cancer vaccine development.
