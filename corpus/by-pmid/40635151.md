# DS-3939a: A TA-MUC1-Directed Antibody-Drug Conjugate with Broad Antitumor Activity.

## Abstract

Tumor-associated mucin-1 (TA-MUC1) is a glycoform of the MUC1 protein that is aberrantly glycosylated and is primarily observed in cancer cells. TA-MUC1 is highly expressed in various human epithelial cancers, making it an attractive target for cancer therapies. In this study, we describe the development of DS-3939a, a novel TA-MUC1-targeting antibody-drug conjugate that utilizes the potent DNA topoisomerase I inhibitor DXd, and evaluation of its pharmacologic activity in preclinical in vitro and in vivo models. IHC of clinical tumor tissue microarrays of various cancer types exhibited positive staining for TA-MUC1 in a number of samples, with a particularly high positive rate in bladder, lung, and breast cancers. In vitro profiling of DS-3939a confirmed that it could specifically bind to TA-MUC1 and inhibit the growth of TA-MUC1-positive cancer cells by inducing DNA damage and apoptosis. DS-3939a also exhibited significant antitumor effects in multiple TA-MUC1-positive cell line-derived and patient-derived xenograft models. Moreover, DS-3939a elicited strong tumor regression in several xenograft models even following treatment with other cytotoxic antibody-drug conjugates, likely through its efficient payload delivery. Overall, these data provide evidence for the potential utility of DS-3939a for the treatment of TA-MUC1-expressing tumors and support the rationale for the ongoing phase I/II clinical study (NCT05875168).

Graphical Abstract

Introduction

Antibody–drug conjugates (ADC) are an emerging class of biotherapeutics that combine an antibody and cytotoxic payload via a chemical linker. This format allows the specific delivery of highly potent payloads to target cells, resulting in a wider therapeutic window compared with conventional chemotherapy. In fact, multiple ADCs have shown success in clinical trials. As of 2023, the FDA has approved a total of 11 ADCs, including trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd), a novel HER2-targeting ADC, which has been approved by the FDA for multiple indications. This number has now increased to 12 with the recent FDA approval of datopotamab deruxtecan. As a result of the clinical success of multiple ADCs, including that of T-DXd, an even greater number of ADCs have recently entered clinical trials. However, the majority of these emerging ADCs target well-known molecules such as HER2, suggesting a depletion of optimal ADC targets that possess an ideal profile, such as robust tumor selectivity. This lack of target diversity also limits the number of patients with cancer who can potentially benefit from ADC therapeutics. Therefore, there is a need to expand the target molecules of ADCs from conventional protein targets to more complex antigens, for example, post-translational modifications.

Alterations in protein glycosylation are a well-established hallmark of cancer. Malignant cancer transformation is, in fact, associated with aberrant glycosylation, and these non-canonical glycans have been proved to be useful serologic cancer biomarkers for multiple types of cancer, as in the case of CA19-9 and CA72-4. Considering this, cancer-associated glycoforms have the potential to be the next-generation of ADC targets with their superior cancer selectivity and broader coverage of cancer types compared with those of conventional targets.

One of the major glycoproteins studied extensively in the field of cancer research is mucin-1 (MUC1), a single-pass transmembrane protein belonging to the mucin family. It possesses a long extracellular N-terminal region containing a proline-, threonine-, and serine-rich domain. The proline-, threonine-, and serine-rich domain of MUC1 is encoded by a highly polymorphic exon and comprises multiple 20 amino acid repeats known as MUC1 tandem repeats (TRs) that possess 5 glycosylation sites each. MUC1 is normally expressed on the apical membrane of epithelial cells and is highly glycosylated, thereby providing protection to the underlying epithelia. However, in tumor tissues, the cellular distribution and biochemical features of MUC1 are highly altered. In contrast to those expressed in normal tissues, MUC1 in cancer cells loses its cell polarity and is redistributed over the cell surface and within the cytoplasm. Glycosylation of MUC1 is also dysregulated in cancer, leading to the emergence of aberrantly glycosylated MUC1, known as tumor-associated MUC1 (TA-MUC1). This abnormality is primarily due to alterations in glycosyltransferase expression, causing TA-MUC1 to be predominantly modified by shorter glycans such as Thomsen–nouveau (Tn), sialyl Tn (sTn), Thomsen–Friedenreich (TF), and sialyl TF (sTF; refs.). Additionally, owing to transcriptional upregulation, the expression of MUC1 itself is elevated in the tumor tissues of multiple cancer types. Collectively, these expression profiles render TA-MUC1 highly cancer-specific, thereby making it an attractive target for ADCs.

Here, we describe the development of DS-3939a, a novel TA-MUC1–directed ADC. DS-3939a combines an analogue of the previously reported anti–TA-MUC1 antibody PankoMab with the DXd-ADC technology that comprises a cleavable tetrapeptide-based Gly-Gly-Phe-Gly linker and DXd, the DNA topoisomerase I (TOP1) inhibitor also implemented in T-DXd. We also describe the preclinical profile of DS-3939a, including its target expression in clinical cancer specimens, in vitro cytotoxic activity, and in vivo antitumor activity. Furthermore, we validate the antitumor activity of DS-3939a following clinically approved ADC treatments, leading us to speculate that DS-3939a may be efficacious in patients who have already undergone treatments with other ADCs. Overall, these findings indicate that DS-3939a has potential as a therapeutic option for patients with multiple types of cancer and warrant further investigation in clinical trials.

Materials and Methods

Antibodies and ADCs

DS-3939a was prepared by conjugating a drug-linker containing an exatecan derivative (DXd) with an anti–TA-MUC1 mAb (Supplementary Fig. S1) according to the published procedure. The drug distribution and drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) were determined by hydrophobic interaction chromatography. An isotype control ADC was synthesized using a humanized IgG1 isotype control mAb and the same drug-linker as DS-3939a, resulting in a comparable DAR. Enfortumab vedotin (EV) and sacituzumab govitecan (SG) were purchased or synthesized according to previous reports.

Cell lines

All cancer cell lines were purchased from vendors listed below in the period of 2003 to 2018 and cultured with the appropriate culture media in a CO2 incubator set at appropriate air conditions in accordance with the instruction manuals from the suppliers. Human breast cancer cell lines HCC70 (RRID: CVCL_1270) and MDA-MB-468 (RRID: CVCL_0419); human colon cancer cell line HCT-15 (RRID: CVCL_0292); human gastric cancer cell lines KATO III (RRID: CVCL_0371) and NCI-N87 (RRID: CVCL_1603); human lung cancer cell lines HCC827 (RRID: CVCL_2063), NCI-H2110 (RRID: CVCL_1530), NCI-H292 (RRID: CVCL_0455), and NCI-H441 (RRID: CVCL_1561); human pancreatic cancer cell lines Capan-1 (RRID: CVCL_0237), Capan-2 (RRID: CVCL_0026), CFPAC-1 (RRID: CVCL_1119), HPAC (RRID: CVCL_3517), and HPAF-II (RRID: CVCL_0313); and human prostate cancer cell line DU145 (RRID: CVCL_0105) were purchased from ATCC. Human ovarian cancer cell line OVCAR-5 (RRID: CVCL_1628) was purchased from the NCI. Human gastric cancer cell line MKN45 (RRID: CVCL_0434) was purchased from Health Science Research Resources Bank. Human breast cancer cell line MX-1 (RRID: CVCL_4774) was purchased from CLS Cell Lines Service GmbH. NCI-H322 (RRID: CVCL_1556) was purchased from the European Collection of Authenticated Cell Cultures. All cell lines were routinely authenticated by comparing morphology and growth characteristics with the suppliers’ data. Not all cell lines have undergone Mycoplasma testing. Working cell banks were stored in liquid nitrogen or in a deep freezer set at −80°C and were allowed to be propagated for no more than 2 months after thawing.

IHC of target antigen expression

Human tumor and tumor adjacent normal tissue microarrays (TMA) were provided by University Hospital Basel, Switzerland, according to the previously reported ethical considerations. In detail, approval for tissue collection, use of samples, and data for the construction of this dataset were obtained from the locally responsible ethics committees (Ethikkommission Nordwest- und Zentralschweiz, Permission 2017–00302). In vivo xenograft tumor samples were collected from untreated tumor-inoculated mice. Tumor samples were fixed in 10% neutral-buffered formalin and embedded in paraffin, and tissue sections were used for IHC. Slides were deparaffinized and antigen-retrieved for 5 minutes, followed by incubation in Peroxidase-Blocking Solution (Agilent Technologies) for 5 to 10 minutes. Next, slides were sequentially incubated with anti–human TA-MUC1 mAb [chimeric antibody composed of the Fab region of DS-3939a and murine Fc region for human TMAs; chimeric antibody composed of the Fab region of DS-3939a and rabbit Fc region for in vivo patient-derived xenograft (PDX) tumor samples; both are internally synthesized] as the primary antibody for 20 minutes at room temperature and with DAKO FLEX/HRP (RRID: AB_3075507) or EnVision+ Single Reagent (HRP. Rabbit; RRID: AB_2630375) according to the manufacturer’s instructions.

For select cell line–derived xenograft (CDX) tumor samples, slides were stained with the same procedure as the IHC of PDX tumor samples. For other CDX tumor samples, slides were stained with DS-3939a and subsequently incubated with goat anti–human Ig [F(ab’)2]-HRP (RRID: AB_2337595) as the secondary antibody. Color development was performed with a 3,3′-diaminobenzidine (DAB) substrate.

IHC of nectin-4 was performed using the Leica BOND RX staining platform (Leica Biosystems) and a rabbit anti–human nectin-4 antibody (RRID: AB_3075506), and binding was verified with the DAB Polymer Refine Detection Kit (Leica Biosystems).

IHC of trophoblast cell-surface antigen 2 (TROP2) was performed using the VENTANA BenchMark ULTRA system (Roche Diagnostics) and a rabbit anti–human TROP2 mAb (RRID: AB_3075505 or RRID: AB_2811182), and binding was verified using the OptiView DAB Universal Kit (OptiView HQ Universal Linker in the kit was substituted with DISCOVERY anti-Rabbit HQ).

All slides were lastly counterstained with hematoxylin and mounted. Whole-slide images were visualized using a NanoZoomer-XR (Hamamatsu Photonics K.K.).

The IHC score of TA-MUC1 in the membrane (M), apical membrane (A), or cytoplasm (C) regions was visually scored as 0, 1+, 2+, or 3+ based on the highest intensity occupying ≥10% of the evaluated area. The representative IHC score for each specimen was determined by adopting the maximum score of the M, A, and C regions.

ELISA

Streptavidin-coated 96-well plates (Thermo Fisher Scientific) were coated with 0.5 μg/mL biotinylated MUC1 peptides (internally synthesized) overnight at 4°C. After removal of each peptide solution, plates were washed and incubated with DS-3939a or isotype control ADC for 1 hour at room temperature. Anti-MUC1 mAb (RRID: AB_10640403) and isotype control mAb (RRID: AB_357346) were additionally used as positive and negative controls, respectively. Following incubation, plates were washed, and HRP-conjugated anti–human IgG antibody (RRID: AB_2337585) for the detection of DS-3939a and isotype control ADC or HRP-conjugated anti–mouse IgG antibody (RRID: AB_2338507) for the detection of anti-MUC1 mAb and isotype control mAb was added and incubated for an additional 2 hours at room temperature. Subsequently, plates were washed, and TMB soluble reagent (SeraCare Life Sciences, Inc.) was added. The reaction was stopped by the addition of phosphoric acid (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemical Corporation), and the absorbance at 450 nm was measured using an EnVision 2105 XCite (PerkinElmer Co., Ltd.) microplate reader.

Flow cytometry

To analyze the TA-MUC1 expression in each cell line, cells were stained with DS-3939a or isotype control ADC at 4°C for 30 minutes. Subsequently, cells were thoroughly washed and stained with a FITC-conjugated anti–human IgG antibody (RRID: AB_2337671) at 4°C for 30 minutes in the dark. After thorough washing, the fluorescence intensity of each stained cell was measured using a flow cytometer (CytoFLEX S, Beckman Coulter, Inc.).

For the quantification of the level of TA-MUC1 antigens, the antibody binding capacity of DS-3939a was quantified using a QIFIKIT (Agilent Technologies) and a chimeric antibody composed of the Fab region of DS-3939a and murine Fc region according to the manufacturer’s protocol. The expression of nectin-4 and TROP2 was also quantified as in the case of TA-MUC1 with anti–nectin-4 (RRID: AB_970859) or anti-TROP2 (RRID: AB_3663012) antibody. To quantify the level of antigen expression on the surface of PDX tumor cells, in vivo PDX tumors were dissociated with Tumor & Tissue Dissociation Reagent (BD Biosciences) and co-stained with antitarget antibody (TA-MUC1, nectin-4, or TROP2), anti–mouse CD45 (mCD45) antibody (RRID: AB_2562559), and anti–human CD147 (hCD147) antibody (RRID: AB_2857684). mCD45−hCD147+ cells were designated as cancer cells and subsequently analyzed for the antigen quantification.

All flow cytometry data were analyzed using FlowJo version 10.6.2 or 10.10.0 (BD Biosciences).

Cytotoxicity assay

Cancer cells were seeded on 96-well plates at appropriate cell densities for each cell line (250–2,000 cells/well, according to the growth rate of each cell line). The following day, serially diluted test substances (DS-3939a, parental anti–TA-MUC1 mAb, isotype control ADC, and DXd payload) were added and incubated for 6 days. On day 6, cell viability was evaluated using the CellTiter-Glo Luminescent Cell Viability Assay (Promega Corporation) in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. The luminescence was measured on an EnVision 2105 XCite microplate reader or a multilabel plate reader (ARVO X3, PerkinElmer Japan Co., Ltd.). Dose–response curves were generated by normalizing signal to control wells. For select cell lines, AUC values were calculated from the dose–response curves using GraphPad Prism software version 9.1.0 (GraphPad Software, LLC).

Western blotting

CFPAC-1 cells were treated with 3 μg/mL DS-3939a, parental anti–TA-MUC1 mAb, isotype control ADC, or 10 nmol/L DXd payload for 2 days. Subsequently, the cells were lysed in RIPA buffer supplemented with 0.5% SDS and 1× Halt Protease and Phosphatase Inhibitor Cocktail (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Cell lysates were applied to a QIAshredder spin column (QIAGEN) and centrifuged at 12,000 rpm for 1 minute at 4°C. The flow through lysates was collected, and their protein concentrations were quantified using a Pierce BCA Protein Assay (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Equal amounts of proteins were analyzed on a Simple Western System (Wes, ProteinSimple Japan K.K.), in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Primary antibodies against phospho-histone H2AX (Ser139; γH2AX), histone H2AX, cleaved PARP (Asp214), and β-actin (RRIDs: AB_2118010, AB_10860771, AB_331426, and AB_2223172, respectively; Cell Signaling Technology, Inc.) were diluted in antibody diluent 2 (ProteinSimple Japan K.K.).

Trafficking assay

HCC70 cells were seeded on a 96-well plate (PhenoPlate-96, Revvity) at 2 × 104 cells/well. The following day, cells were treated with Hoechst 33342 (100 ng/mL) for 30 minutes at 37°C and then treated with the pHrodo-labeled parental antibody of DS-3939a, SG, or isotype control ADC at antigen-saturating concentrations. Antibodies were labeled with pHrodo fluorescent dye in order to achieve a degree of labeling of 2.4 to 3.4. After treatment, fluorescence time-lapse images were captured on an Opera Phenix (PerkinElmer) high-content imaging system. As pHrodo conjugates fluoresce upon uptake into acidic environments such as in the endosome or the lysosome of cells, the number of pHrodo fluorescence dots per cell (dots/cell) and fluorescence signal intensity of each dot, both of which were determined using Harmony analysis software (PerkinElmer), were used as a readout for cellular uptake. Delta dot signal intensity was calculated as a subtraction of average dot intensity values of control group at t = 0 from each intensity value. The trafficking level was expressed as a trafficking index as follows: trafficking index = dot/cell × delta dot signal intensity.

Animal studies

All animals were housed in an Association for Assessment and Accreditation of Laboratory Animal Care International–approved veterinary research facility at either Daiichi Sankyo or Champions Oncology, Inc. All animal studies were carried out with the approval of the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee at Daiichi Sankyo in compliance with the guidelines established by each facility’s institutional animal care and use committee. Three to six mice were housed together in sterilized cages and maintained under specific pathogen–free conditions.

CDX models

Four- to five-week-old female nude mice [CAnN.Cg-Foxn1(nu)/CrlCrlj(Foxn1nu/Foxn1nu)] were purchased from Charles River Laboratories Japan, Inc. or The Jackson Laboratory Japan, Inc. Each cell suspension (1.0 × 107 cells/0.1 mL saline/body for HCC70 and 5 × 106 cells/0.1 mL saline/body for other cell lines) was inoculated subcutaneously into the right flank of mice. When the average volume of tumors reached approximately 200 mm3, the mice were randomized to each treatment group (n = 6 mice/group) based on tumor volumes. The randomized mice were treated with DS-3939a or relevant controls intravenously on day 0. The vehicle control group was administered 10 mmol/L acetate buffer (pH 5.5) containing 5% sorbitol. The length (L) and width (W) of the tumor in millimeters and the body weight of the mice were measured twice per week. The estimated tumor volume of each mouse was calculated using the equation estimated tumor volume (mm3) = 1/2 × L × W2.

PDX models

 In vivo studies of PDX models with their names starting with “CTG” were performed by Champions Oncology, Inc. according to their experimental procedure. In vivo studies of PDX models with their names starting with “NIBIO” (provided by National Institutes of Biomedical Innovation, Health, and Nutrition) were performed in-house. For the “NIBIO” PDX models, each model was established by inoculating tumor fragments derived from patients with cancer into immunodeficient mice, such as nude mice or NOD/SCID gamma (NOD. Cg-Prkdcscid Il2rgtm1Wjl/SzJ) mice. These PDX tumors were maintained in these host mice, and prior to antitumor studies, their tumor fragments were subcutaneously inoculated into female nude mice. After the PDX tumors reached approximately 200 mm3, tumor-inoculated mice were randomized (n = 6 mice/group) and treated as in the case of the CDX models. The antitumor activity was evaluated by calculating relative tumor growth volume (TGv, %) according to the following equation: If the average tumor size of the DS-3939a–treated mice on TGv calculation date was smaller than that at dosing (Tc < Ti), TGv (%) = 100 × (Tc − Ti)/Ti, and if not, TGv (%) = 100 × (Tc − Ti)/(Ccave − Ciave), where Tc is the tumor volume of DS-3939a–treated mice on TGv calculation dates, Ti is the tumor volume of DS-3939a–treated mice at dosing, Ccave is the mean tumor volume of the vehicle-administered group on TGv calculation dates, and Ciave is the mean tumor volume of the vehicle-administered group at dosing. Based on the equation, we defined TGv < 0 as tumor regression.

Additionally, tumor growth inhibition (TGI, %) was also calculated according to the equation 100 × [1 − Tf/Cfave], where Tf is the tumor volume of DS-3939a–treated mice at the final measurement and Cfave is the mean tumor volume of the vehicle-administered group at the final measurement.

Measurement of payload concentrations in CDX and PDX tumors

To determine payload concentrations in CDX and PDX tumors, tumor-bearing mice were grouped (n = 3), administered selected ADCs, and then euthanized for tumor excision. The excised tumors were homogenized and deproteinated in acetonitrile. The payload concentrations were measured using LC/MS-MS (Prominence UFLC; Nexera X2, Shimadzu Corporation; and Triple Quad 5500 and Triple Quad 6500+, Sciex) with Capcell Pak Inert, ADME-HR 3 µm, 2.0 mm internal diameter × 100 mm (Osaka Soda). Elution was performed over a gradient of purified water:acetonitrile:formic acid (900:100:10, v/v/v) and acetonitrile:purified water:formic acid (900:100:10, v/v/v) as the mobile phase. The lower limit of quantification was 0.06 ng/g tissue (DXd), 0.2 ng/g tissue (monomethyl auristatin E), and 0.06 ng/g tissue (SN-38). The conversion from ng/g tissue to nmol/L was performed under the assumption that 1 g of tissue is equivalent to 1 mL.

 In vivo study evaluating the efficacy of sequential administration

Mice bearing NIBIO-K071 PDX tumors or HCC70 cells were allocated to treatment groups upon reaching appropriate tumor volumes and were treated intravenously with selected agents as in the case of the in vivo studies described above. Dosing schedules of EV and SG were determined based on the clinically approved regimens. For mice that were initially treated with EV or SG, the treatment was switched to DS-3939a when an obvious growth tendency of the tumors was observed.

Statistical analysis

All statistical analyses were performed using the SAS System Release 9.2 (SAS Institute Inc.) or R (version 4.2.2). The Dunnett multiple comparisons test and Welch two-sample t test were used for the analysis of CDX model and PDX model experiments.

Result

TA-MUC1 is broadly expressed among multiple cancer specimens

Previous reports have indicated that TA-MUC1 is overexpressed in a variety of human cancer specimens. To evaluate the expression of TA-MUC1 under standardized experimental conditions, we conducted comprehensive IHC on clinical solid tumor TMAs across multiple cancer types. The IHC was performed using a chimeric antibody composed of the Fab region of DS-3939a and a murine Fc region to accurately assess the expression level of the antigen to which DS-3939a binds. We observed several distinct distribution patterns of TA-MUC1 staining: (i) positive staining observed throughout the membrane region, (ii) positive staining predominantly found at the apical membrane region, and (iii) diffuse staining observed in the cytoplasmic region (Fig. 1A). Using the staining intensity in each region, we calculated IHC scores for TA-MUC1 and confirmed that TA-MUC1 was expressed across various cancer types. TA-MUC1 expression was highly prevalent in bladder, breast, lung, and ovarian cancers (Fig. 1B; Supplementary Fig. S2), whereas it was low in certain cancer types, including hepatocellular carcinoma, sarcoma, and melanoma. We additionally evaluated the expression of TA-MUC1 in tumor-adjacent normal tissue TMAs. IHC of TA-MUC1 in 21 types of normal human tissues indicated that TA-MUC1 expression was suggested in the epithelial cells in the lung, liver, kidney, pancreas, stomach, esophagus, uterus, and mammary gland (Supplementary Fig. S3). Furthermore, the positive staining was restricted to the apical membrane of the glandular epithelium, tubular epithelium, and alveolar epithelium.

Structure and characterization of DS-3939a

Encouraged by the broad expression of TA-MUC1 across multiple types of cancer, we generated the anti–TA-MUC1 DXd-ADC designated DS-3939a. DS-3939a is composed of a humanized anti–TA-MUC1 IgG1 mAb conjugated with a TOP1 inhibitor (DXd) via a tetrapeptide-based Gly-Gly-Phe-Gly linker to reduced cysteine residues at the interchain disulfide bonds (Fig. 2A). The drug distribution on synthesized DS-3939a was confirmed to be homogeneous by hydrophobic interaction chromatography with a DAR of approximately 8 (Fig. 2B).

Next, to confirm the target specificity of DS-3939a, we assessed its binding activity by ELISA. It has previously been reported that MUC1 is converted to TA-MUC1 in cancer cells via aberrant glycosylation of its extracellular domain. One such alteration is that of the Pro-Asp-Thr-Arg-Pro (PDTRP) motif located in the TRs, which serves as the glycopeptide epitope of PankoMab. There are several types of glycosylation patterns observed on the TA-MUC1 PDTRP motif, such as Tn, sTn, TF, and sTF (Fig. 2C). To evaluate the binding specificity of DS-3939a against these various TA-MUC1 structures, differentially glycosylated PDTRP motif peptides (MUC1 peptides) were synthesized and subjected to ELISA. A MUC1 peptide without glycosylation and a Tn glycan adducted peptide with amino acid substitution from the MUC1 PDTRP motif (PESRP) were also prepared as negative controls. Peptides were immobilized onto immunoplates, and the binding activity of tested ADCs against each peptide was evaluated. Proper immobilization of all peptides on the immunoplates was confirmed by detection with commercially available anti-MUC1 antibody that recognizes MUC1 peptides regardless of their glycosylation status or amino acid substitution (Supplementary Fig. S4). In this setting, DS-3939a exhibited strong binding activity against the glycosylated MUC1 peptides (PDT*RP-Tn, TF, and sTF), albeit with slightly attenuated binding activity against the sTn glycan–modified peptides (Fig. 2D). In contrast, it showed minimal binding against the MUC1 peptide without glycosylation (PDTRP) and the amino acid–substituted MUC1 peptide with Tn glycan (PES*RP-Tn). The isotype control ADC also exhibited no binding activity against any of the peptides. These results indicate that DS-3939a exhibits specific binding activity against TA-MUC1 by recognizing both the TA-MUC1 glycan and the backbone peptide moieties as its epitope.

 In vitro activity of DS-3939a

We next set out to evaluate the in vitro pharmacologic activity of DS-3939a against cancer cells. We selected human pancreatic cancer cell line CFPAC-1 and human lung cancer cell line NCI-H2110 as TA-MUC1–positive cell lines and human colorectal cancer cell line HCT-15 as TA-MUC1–negative cell line according to the binding activity of DS-3939a against these cancer cell lines (Fig. 3A). Using these cell lines, we then conducted a cytotoxicity assay. DS-3939a specifically inhibited the growth of the TA-MUC1–positive cell lines CFPAC-1 and NCI-H2110 but did not inhibit the growth of the TA-MUC1–negative cell line HCT-15, although all three evaluated cell lines were equally susceptible to treatment with the unconjugated DXd payload (Fig. 3B). In contrast, the isotype control ADC exhibited slight growth inhibition only at higher concentrations in all three evaluated cell lines. To corroborate the target dependency of DS-3939a observed in these three cell lines, we expanded the analysis to multiple cancer cell lines. We measured the cell growth inhibitory activity of DS-3939a and cell-surface TA-MUC1 expression level in 14 cancer cell lines. A correlation plot of these parameters indicated that TA-MUC1 expression level and sensitivity to DS-3939a were highly correlated (R = −0.77; Fig. 3C), suggesting that the cell growth inhibitory activity of DS-3939a is dependent on the cell-surface antigen expression level. To confirm the contribution of the payload compound to the cytotoxic activity of DS-3939a, we analyzed the induction of phosphorylated histone H2AX (γH2AX) as a marker of DNA damage and cleaved PARP as a marker of apoptosis, both of which are known to be induced by TOP1 inhibitors. Both H2AX phosphorylation and PARP cleavage were upregulated in TA-MUC1–positive CFPAC-1 cells treated with either DS-3939a or DXd payload but not in those treated with TA-MUC1 mAb or the isotype control ADC (Fig. 3D). Taken together, these observations are in line with the expected molecular dynamics that DS-3939a binds to cell-surface TA-MUC1 and induces DNA damage and apoptosis through its payload.

Antitumor activity of DS-3939a in CDX mouse models

To explore the therapeutic potential of DS-3939a, we evaluated its in vivo activity in several CDX mouse models. Mice bearing CFPAC-1 tumors were intravenously administered escalating doses of DS-3939a ranging from 0.25 to 8 mg/kg. DS-3939a displayed dose-dependent TGI and induced tumor regression at doses of 2 mg/kg or higher (Fig. 4A). Next, we evaluated the antitumor activity of DS-3939a in additional CDX models to ascertain its target dependency in vivo. NCI-H2110 and HCT-15 tumors were engrafted onto mice as TA-MUC1–positive and TA-MUC1–negative tumors, respectively, according to the IHC of each tumor. DS-3939a exhibited robust antitumor activity in NCI-H2110 tumors but not in HCT-15 tumors, whereas the isotype control ADC exhibited minimal antitumor activity in both tumors (Fig. 4B). These findings suggest that DS-3939a also exerts antitumor activity in an antigen expression–dependent manner in vivo.

Antitumor activity of DS-3939a in PDX mouse models

The antitumor activity of DS-3939a was further evaluated in PDX models, which are considered to be more clinically relevant than CDX models. We initially tested a limited number of PDX models established from patients with lung, breast, and bladder cancers in which TA-MUC1–positive tumors are prevalent (Fig. 1B). DS-3939a was administered as a single dose of 10 mg/kg and induced remarkable tumor regression in all three PDX models, whereas the isotype control ADC displayed minimal antitumor activity (Fig. 5A). Given that TA-MUC1 is expressed in a wide range of cancer types, we expanded the study to a total of 36 PDX models spanning multiple cancer types. For the index of efficacy, relative TGv values were calculated and plotted in descending order in a waterfall plot. DS-3939a demonstrated robust antitumor activity against multiple PDX models with a regression rate of 69% (25/36 models; Fig. 5B). To evaluate the target dependency of payload delivery, the concentration of DXd payload in PDX tumors was measured 24 hours after administration. The concentration of DXd payload showed a modest correlation with the TA-MUC1 H-score, with an R value of 0.58 (Fig. 5C), suggesting some level of target-dependent payload delivery by DS-3939a. Next, we examined the impact of TA-MUC1 expression on the efficacy of DS-3939a in the evaluated PDX models. DS-3939a exhibited robust antitumor activity against the majority of PDX models with high TA-MUC1 expression (H-score > 100), suggesting the possibility that TA-MUC1 expression contributes to the efficacy of DS-3939a (Supplementary Fig. S5A). However, there were also models which exhibited sensitivity to DS-3939a despite absent or low TA-MUC1 expression. These models are likely susceptible to DXd-ADC treatment regardless of target expression, as they include models which exhibited tumor regression even with the treatment of the isotype control ADC (Supplementary Fig. S5A). Given that these models would hinder the rational evaluation of the contribution of TA-MUC1 expression on the efficacy of DS-3939a, we excluded models which were sensitive to the isotype control ADC and conducted correlation analysis with the remaining models. As a result, we observed a modest correlation between DS-3939a TGI values and TA-MUC1 H-scores, with an R value of 0.58 (Supplementary Fig. S5B). Collectively, these results indicate that DS-3939a delivers DXd payload and subsequently exerts antitumor activity in a target-dependent manner, with the exception of certain models with high sensitivity to DXd-ADC irrespective of target expression.

Antitumor activity of DS-3939a after currently approved ADC treatment

Recently, a number of ADC-based therapeutics have been approved and become available for the treatment of patients with cancer. Among these are ADCs with primary indications of TA-MUC1–positive tumors being prevalent, such as bladder cancer and triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC). As clinical trials often enroll heavily pretreated patients who have exhausted all available treatments, we evaluated the efficacy of DS-3939a in several in vivo models following treatment with currently approved ADCs. EV and SG are ADCs that have been approved by the FDA for the treatment of patients with bladder cancer (although the latter was recently voluntarily withdrawn from this indication; ref.). We treated mice bearing bladder PDX tumors, NIBIO-K071, with either EV or SG in clinically relevant regimens and found that neither of these ADCs induced tumor regression in this model despite the fact that their target antigens (nectin-4 for EV and TROP2 for SG) were confirmed to be expressed by IHC (Fig. 6A). On the other hand, DS-3939a induced robust tumor regression. Encouraged by this result, we administered DS-3939a to mice that had previously undergone EV or SG treatment and observed rapid tumor regression despite the previous ADC treatments (Fig. 6A). To validate these results in a separate in vivo model, we conducted a similar experiment using a TNBC CDX model, HCC70. Mice bearing HCC70 tumors were administered SG, which is also FDA-approved for the treatment of patients with TNBC. As in the bladder PDX model, SG did not induce tumor regression in this TNBC CDX model despite TROP2 expression, whereas DS-3939a induced robust tumor regression in this model as well (Fig. 6B). Furthermore, DS-3939a was able to induce tumor regression even after SG treatment (Fig. 6B).

To clarify the mechanism underlying the difference in the observed efficacies, we further analyzed these ADCs using the aforementioned PDX and CDX models. Quantitative flow cytometry analysis of NIBIO-K071 indicated that the expression of TROP2 was the highest among the three evaluated antigens and that the expression of nectin-4 was the lowest (Supplementary Fig. S6A and S6B). Next, we measured the tumor payload concentration after the administration of each ADC to compare their efficiency of payload delivery. Although the measured tumor payload concentration after the administration of EV was higher than those of SG and DS-3939a, it was lower than those of previously reported ADCs that utilize the same drug-linker as EV, consistent with the low expression of its target molecule in this model (Fig. 6C). Furthermore, despite the expression of TROP2 being greater than that of TA-MUC1 in NIBIO-K071, the tumor payload concentration of DS-3939a following administration was higher than that of SG throughout the evaluated time course (Fig. 6C). To corroborate these observations, we also measured tumor payload concentrations after the administration of DS-3939a and SG in the HCC70 CDX model, which highly expresses both TROP2 and TA-MUC1 (Supplementary Fig. S6C). As with the NIBIO-K071 model, we observed a higher tumor payload concentration for DS-3939a than that of SG in the HCC70 model (Fig. 6D). Based on the analysis of these two models, it is likely that the efficacy of DS-3939a is, at least partly, attributed to its ability to efficiently deliver payload to tumor sites.

Lastly, to explore the reason for this difference in payload delivery potential, we measured the internalization kinetics of the parental antibodies of DS-3939a and SG. HCC70 cells were treated with the parental antibodies of DS-3939a and SG labeled with pH-sensitive pHrodo fluorescent dye, and the fluorescent signal was measured over time. The anti–TA-MUC1 antibody exhibited a greater level of internalization at early time points compared with the anti-TROP2 antibody, and the fluorescent signal at equilibrium was also higher for the anti–TA-MUC1 antibody (Supplementary Fig. S6D). This result suggests that the difference in payload delivery potential of these two ADCs may be influenced by the internalization kinetics of their parental antibodies. Taken together, DS-3939a may prove to be a promising therapeutic option for patients who have previously undergone treatment with currently approved ADCs.

Discussion

ADCs have emerged as a promising modality for targeted cancer therapy as evidenced by the recent regulatory approvals of ADCs targeting various antigens and indications. Encouraged by the approval of T-DXd in multiple indications, several ADCs that utilize the same linker–payload technology as T-DXd have been developed against different target molecules and are currently under clinical investigations. Despite these advances, a necessity persists to broaden the molecules targetable by ADCs. Here, we focused our attention on alterations to glycosylation in cancer and selected TA-MUC1 as an optimal antigen for the application of the DXd-ADC technology.

We initially evaluated the expression of TA-MUC1 in TMAs of various cancer types using an anti–TA-MUC1 antibody which shares the same Fab region as DS-3939a. TA-MUC1 was broadly expressed across multiple cancer types, consistent with previous IHC using the anti–TA-MUC1 antibody PankoMab. TA-MUC1 staining exhibited several distinct distribution patterns, which also aligned with earlier reports. However, it remains unclear whether a particular staining pattern contributes to its efficacy. Such correlation may be of particular importance to further clinical development of DS-3939a. Recently, it was reported that the staining intensity of TROP2 in the cytoplasm is predictive of the efficacy of anti-TROP2 ADC, implying that it is not only staining in the membrane that contributes to the efficacy of ADCs. A thorough investigation of the relationship between the efficacy of DS-3939a and the staining pattern of TA-MUC1 should be conducted by carefully evaluating clinical specimens obtained through the clinical trial. Additionally, TA-MUC1 expression in tumor-adjacent normal tissues was predominantly restricted to the apical membrane side of some mucosal epithelial cells. Considering the previous report that claims limited penetration of IgG antibodies from the basal to the apical side of polarized epithelial cells and vice versa, we speculate that DS-3939a has limited access to its target molecule in normal tissues, though this possibility should be validated in further studies.

These observations on the expression profiles of TA-MUC1 led us to develop an anti–TA-MUC1 DXd-ADC, which we designated as DS-3939a. We tested the binding activity of DS-3939a to MUC1 peptides with different glycosylation states and found that it specifically binds to glycosylated TA-MUC1 peptides by recognizing both the glycan and backbone peptide moieties as its epitope. Interestingly, DS-3939a exhibited slightly attenuated binding affinity against MUC1 peptides bearing the sTn glycan. As the O-6 position of the GalNAc residue is substituted with Neu5Ac on sTn glycans, it is reasonable to assume that DS-3939a recognizes this site without the glycan adduct as part of its epitope. Furthermore, this binding preference may contribute to the cancer specificity of DS-3939a, as this site is heavily glycosylated in normal tissues. Recently, the co-crystal structure of a MUC1 peptide with sTF glycan and the anti–TA-MUC1 antibody SN-131 has been reported. According to the resolved crystal structure, SN-131 recognizes both the glycan and peptide moieties as its epitope and forms hydrogen bonds with the nonsubstituted O-6 position of the GalNAc residue. As this observation is consistent with the binding profile of DS-3939a, a similar binding conformation might be formed in the case of DS-3939a and TA-MUC1. Further investigations such as crystallography or lectin array are required to clarify the specific binding mode of this ADC and its target antigen.

Such complexity of glycopeptide epitopes additionally leads to a diversity in the reactivity among different anti–TA-MUC1 antibody clones. A subset of anti-MUC1 ADCs have undergone clinical trials without detailed epitope mapping and were discontinued because of their intolerable toxicity profiles. DS-3939a is the first TA-MUC1–targeting ADC with clear epitope profiling, and its unique binding specificity might lead to an improved safety profile. In a preclinical safety study, DS-3939a was well tolerated in cynomolgus monkeys at doses up to 20 mg/kg administered intravenously every 3 weeks, similar to other DXd-ADCs with a DAR 8. However, taking into account the fact that cynomolgus monkeys are a non–cross-reactive species for this ADC, it is difficult to conclusively determine the preclinical safety profile of DS-3939a, and caution is required for moving it forward into clinical trials.

Following our epitope profiling of DS-3939a, we next selected TA-MUC1–positive and TA-MUC1–negative cell lines for the evaluation of cell cytotoxicity. DS-3939a exhibited potent cytotoxic activity only against TA-MUC1–positive cell lines, whereas neither parental anti–TA-MUC1 antibody nor isotype control ADC elicited a response. Importantly, in vitro cell cytotoxicity of DS-3939a was associated with the surface expression level of TA-MUC1, suggesting its antigen-dependent payload delivery potential. DS-3939a also induced DNA damage and apoptosis in TA-MUC1–positive CFPAC-1 cells, suggesting that the cytotoxic activity of DS-3939a is attributed to its TOP1 inhibitor payload DXd. Although multiple clinical trials for MUC1-targeting mAb–based therapies have been conducted, there have been no regulatory approvals thus far. Considering that TA-MUC1 is an ideal target for ADCs with its high internalization rate, it is possible that a TA-MUC1–targeting ADC approach can overcome the limitations of mAb-based therapies, offering potent and broad cytotoxic activity.

 In vivo evaluation in CDX mouse models demonstrated that DS-3939a significantly inhibited tumor growth and led to tumor regression in TA-MUC1–positive tumors but not in TA-MUC1–negative tumors. PDX mouse models encompassing various cancer types were additionally used to examine the therapeutic potential of DS-3939a in more clinically relevant in vivo models. DS-3939a exerted potent antitumor activity in PDX models of multiple cancer types, suggesting its broad efficacy. Of note, the concentration of DXd payload in PDX tumors after DS-3939a treatment was correlated with TA-MUC1 expression, highlighting the antigen-dependent payload delivery potential of DS-3939a. However, there were models which exhibited high sensitivity to DS-3939a despite their no or low TA-MUC1 staining, making it difficult to investigate the correlation between target expression and the efficacy of DS-3939a. The presence of such models suggests that there are likely other determinants of DS-3939a efficacy in addition to antigen expression levels, such as payload sensitivity. Further comprehensive analysis of the PDX models, such as evaluation of payload sensitivity using matched PDX-derived organoids or biomarker analysis from transcriptomic signatures, might help us more precisely stratify patients who are sensitive to DS-3939a.

In this study, we additionally examined the effect of DS-3939a administration following the treatment of clinically approved ADCs, such as EV and SG. Whereas neither of these clinically approved ADCs induced tumor regression in the models used in this study, subsequent treatment of DS-3939a caused robust tumor regression. This result suggests that DS-3939a is also efficacious against tumors that have been treated with currently approved ADCs, thus providing a rationale for assessing the drug in clinical trials including late-line settings. Nevertheless, we cannot be too cautious about the prior treatment history of patients enrolled in the clinical trial, especially those who have been administered with other ADCs, as recent clinical findings on the sequential use of SG and T-DXd in patients with HER2-low metastatic breast cancer reported that the therapeutic efficacy of the second ADC was limited.

Given such clinical insights, it is important to elucidate the mechanism behind the effectiveness of DS-3939a in preclinical models that are insensitive to other ADC treatments in order to precisely recruit patients who would retain sensitivity to DS-3939a. With this in mind, we analyzed the detailed profiles of the tested ADCs and found that DS-3939a showed a higher tumor payload exposure compared with SG. Although we demonstrated that the efficient payload delivery by DS-3939a was at least partially derived from the rapid and strong internalization of TA-MUC1, other characteristics of TA-MUC1 as an ADC target may also play a role. For instance, the antigen expression level we calculated from the binding analysis of our anti–TA-MUC1 antibody is a reflection of the antibody’s antigen binding capacity. As the TRs in TA-MUC1, which our anti–TA-MUC1 antibody recognizes as its epitope, are highly polymorphic and show allelic variation with respect to the number of repeats, we are unable to distinguish the precise number of TA-MUC1 molecules on each cell and the number of antibody-binding domains in each TA-MUC1 molecule. As it is plausible that these differences in the state of TA-MUC1 affect the behavior of the molecules, such as their internalization efficiency, it is critical to establish a method that would allow us to investigate the molecular state of TA-MUC1 in more detail. Additionally, other characteristics of each ADC, such as the stability of the linker in serum, likely contribute to the difference in tumor payload exposure. Further analysis will be required to better understand the mechanism underlying the difference in efficacy of these ADCs.

Considering that TA-MUC1 expression is observed in cancer types for which immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) are approved, such as lung cancer, bladder cancer, and TNBC, another attractive opportunity for DS-3939a is in combination with immunotherapy. A well-known rationale for combining ADCs with ICIs is that chemotherapy, including TOP1 inhibitors, can enhance the effects of ICIs. In fact, it was reported that DXd-ADCs, including T-DXd, sensitize preclinical syngeneic mouse tumor models to ICIs, possibly through enhanced antitumor immunity caused by the delivered DXd payload. Targeting TA-MUC1 itself might also be beneficial in the context of combination with ICIs, as it is possible that DS-3939a may block the interaction of sialylated glycans on TA-MUC1 with the sialic acid–binding lectins, which leads to a suppressive immune microenvironment. Based on these rationales, preclinical evaluations on the combinatorial effect of DS-3939a and ICIs are currently underway.

In summary, DS-3939a demonstrated potent antitumor activity against TA-MUC1–expressing tumors with efficient antigen-dependent payload delivery. DS-3939a may offer a promising treatment option for patients with cancer across various cancer types. Based on the preclinical evaluations described here, a phase I/II clinical study of DS-3939a is currently underway for patients with metastatic cancer (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT05875168).
