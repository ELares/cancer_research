# A retrospective multicenter study on the efficacy and safety of disitamab vedotin monotherapy versus combination with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy in advanced gastric cancer.

## Abstract

Novel therapeutic agents including disitamab vedotin (RC48, an antibody-drug conjugate) and immune-checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., PD-1 inhibitors) have provided new hope as an advanced gastric-cancer (GC) treatment. This multicenter retrospective study studied RC48 monotherapy (MT)'s efficacy and safety against its combination with anti-PD-1 immunotherapy (IT) in advanced GC. Patients treated with RC48 MT or RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT from July 2021 to April 2023 were recruited for the study. The progression-free survival (PFS), overall survival (OS), objective-response rate (ORR), disease-control rate (DCR), and safety were studied. After propensity score matching (PSM) (1:1), this study included 34 in the RC48 plus anti-PD-1 IT group and 34 in the RC48 MT group. The median PFS was significantly longer in the combination-therapy (CT) group than in the MT group (5.3 versus 3.8 months, HR: 0.51, 95% CI: 0.31-0.85, p = 0.010), and the median OS was also notably increased (10.0 versus 6.8 months, HR: 0.45, 95% CI: 0.27-0.77, p = 0.003). The ORR and DCR were higher in the combination group (41.18% versus 14.71%, p = 0.031; 61.76% versus 35.30%, p = 0.052). Moreover, subgroup analyses further revealed that those in the CT group experienced a longer PFS and OS, particularly those with high HER2 expression or a PD-L1 CPS score of 1 or higher. The combination therapy (CT) achieved acceptable tolerability and manageable adverse events. Furthermore, the most common grade 3-5 treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) included decreased white-blood-cell (WBC) count, decreased neutrophil count, and anemia. No new safety risks were observed. In sum, the RC48 and anti-PD-1 IT combination showed good efficacy and a manageable safety profile, indicating its strong potential as an advanced GC therapeutic option.

Introduction

Gastric cancer (GC) is among the most common malignancies globally and a leading cause of cancer-related deaths, with an incidence of approximately 4.9% and a mortality rate of 6.8%. Most patients are diagnosed at an advanced stage, often with locally advanced disease or distant metastasis, increasing treatment difficulty and leading to a poor prognosis. In advanced GC treatment, chemotherapy remains the primary therapeutic strategy. In China, the standard first-line treatment commonly includes a regimen of fluoropyrimidines combined with platinum agents, which slightly differs from the protocols in Europe and other East Asian countries. For patients with disease progression following initial therapy, available options include paclitaxel, docetaxel, and irinotecan. However, in later lines of treatment, therapeutic options remain limited. Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) is a key therapeutic target in gastric and gastroesophageal-junction (G/GEJ) cancers, with approximately 15–30% of those exhibiting HER2 amplification or overexpression. The ToGA trial demonstrated that adding trastuzumab to chemotherapy significantly improved overall survival in those with HER2-positive advanced G/GEJ cancer. Trastuzumab has been approved in patients with HER2-positive GC. However, as treatment continued, some patients developed resistance, leading to disease progression. Thus, exploring new therapeutic strategies in subsequent treatment stages is essential.

In recent years, antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have arisen as a promising therapeutic approach for GC. Disitamab vedotin (RC48) is a novel ADC that selectively delivers cytotoxic agents to tumor sites, effectively killing tumor cells. RC48 has been approved in China for the treatment of patients with HER2-positive advanced G/GEJ cancer who have received at least two lines of systemic therapy. A phase I trial of RC48 revealed good results in those with HER2-positive advanced GC. The subsequent phase II RC48-C008 trial further demonstrated the benefits of RC48 with HER2-overexpressing GC patients who had undergone at least two prior lines of therapy, as well as in those with HER2 low expression advanced GC. Nonetheless, challenges remain, as only a subset of patients can benefit from RC48 MT.

Immune-checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), particularly those targeting programmed death-1 (PD-1) and its ligand PD-L1, have shown promising efficacy in those with advanced GC. Studies have shown that RC48 can target tumor cells, modulate the immune microenvironment, produce a bystander effect, and ultimately enhance the IT efficacy. Moreover, a clinical trial of RC48 combined with toripalimab (a PD-1 inhibitor) in pretreated those with HER2-positive and low HER2-expressing G/GEJ cancers demonstrated manageable safety and good efficacy, highlighting the synergistic potential of this combination.

Nevertheless, direct comparative studies examining the efficacy and safety of RC48 MT versus its combination with anti-PD-1 IT in advanced GC have been limited. This retrospective, multicenter study aimed to assess RC48’s efficacy, safety, and survival benefits combined with anti-PD-1 IT compared with RC48 MT using real-world data.

Materials and methods

Patients and study design

This retrospective cohort study enrolled all eligible patients with advanced GC who were treated with either RC48 alone or in combination with PD-1 inhibitors at Henan Cancer Hospital, Anyang Tumor Hospital, Zhoukou Central Hospital and The First Affiliated Hospital of Xinxiang Medical University between July 2021 and April 2023. The eligibility criteria included the following: (1) histologically confirmed GC; (2) administration of at least one dose of RC48, with or without anti-PD-1 IT; (3) failure of second-line or subsequent treatments. Those with incomplete medical-data records—including unknown PD-L1 CPS scores, incomplete FISH results, or transfers to other medical institutions—were excluded from the study. If the relevant data are complete, patients who are lost to follow-up will still be included in the final analysis to ensure the retention of key information and the comprehensiveness of the study results, while also maximizing the sample size.

Data collection and assessment

Clinical data were retrospectively collected to categorize patients into RC48 plus anti-PD-1 IT and RC48 MT groups for outcome evaluation. All patients received RC48 at 2.5 mg/kg once every 2 weeks. PD1 inhibitors were administered according to the recommended instructions. Tumor responses were assessed by clinicians according to RECIST version 1.1. The overall response rate (ORR) was calculated as the proportion of patients achieving a complete response (CR) or a partial response (PR), and the disease control rate (DCR) was defined as the proportion of those achieving a CR, PR, or stable disease (SD). Progression-Free Survival (PFS) was measured from the first use of RC48 MT or RC48 in combination with anti-PD-1 IT until disease progression or death. Moreover, overall survival (OS) was measured from the first use of RC48 MT or RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT until death from any cause or the last follow-up. Adverse events (AEs) were assessed using the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (CTCAE), version 5.0. The combined positive score (CPS) for PD-L1 was defined as the percentage of positive cells among all tumor cells. The HER2 status was assessed using immunohistochemistry (IHC), with high expression defined as IHC 3 + or IHC 2+/FISH-positive and low expression defined as IHC 2+/FISH-negative or IHC 1+.

PSM (1:1)

In this study, to minimize confounding factors’ impact, we performed PSM using a logistic regression model with the following variables: age, HER2 status, PD-L1 CPS score, primary tumor site, prior treatment history, and metastatic sites. PSM was conducted using SPSS version 29.0 (IBM, USA) software. The nearest-neighbor matching method (caliper = 0.1) was applied for 1:1 matching between the groups.

Statistical analysis

Quantitative variables were summarized using medians and interquartile ranges, while qualitative variables were summed up using frequencies and percentages. Categorical variables were compared using the Chi-square or Fisher’s exact test, while continuous variables were analyzed using the Mann-Whitney U or Kruskal-Wallis test. ORR and DCR were evaluated using the Chi-square test or Fisher’s exact test. Survival analyses, including the median follow-up time, were performed using Kaplan-Meier (KM) curves with log-rank tests for comparisons. Moreover, univariate and multivariate analyses were conducted using Cox proportional-hazards models. Only variables with p values less than 0.05 in the univariate Cox analysis were included in the multivariate Cox regression model. Interaction terms between specific variable pairs, including age, HER2 status, and metastasis sites, were incorporated in the Cox regression model to assess potential interaction effects. All analyses were conducted using IBM SPSS 29.0, GraphPad Prism version 10 (GraphPad Software, USA), and R software version 4.4.0 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Austria). Furthermore, all p-values were two-sided; those less than 0.05 were considered statistically significant.

Ethics statement

The study was approved by the Henan Cancer Hospital’s Institutional Review Board (Ethics Approval ID: 2023-279-002). The need for written informed consent was waived by the Henan Cancer Hospital’s Institutional Review Board due to the study’s retrospective nature. The study was conducted in accordance with the principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and current ethical guidelines. Non-identified information was utilized to protect patient confidentiality.

Results

Patient characteristics

Sixty-eight patients were ultimately included in this study following PSM. Patient clinical characteristics are shown in Table 1. All patients in the cohort had an ECOG performance status of 0 to 1, with each exhibiting varying degrees of lymph-node metastasis. 52.94% of patients had previously received three or more lines of systemic therapy. The liver (83.82%), lungs (77.94%), and peritoneum (66.18%) were the most common metastatic sites, with only 12 patients (17.65%) having fewer than three metastatic sites. The primary tumor was located in the stomach in 77.94% of the patients. Prior treatments included surgery in 63.24% of patients, HER2-targeted therapy in 57.35%, and anti-PD-1 IT in 48.53%. Of those receiving the CT, PD-1 inhibitors’ distribution was as follows: sintilimab (35.29%), tislelizumab (26.47%), camrelizumab and toripalimab (both 14.71%), and nivolumab (8.82%). Moreover, baseline characteristics between the RC48 MT and CT groups showed no significant differences.

Efficacy

As of the follow-up cut-off date of March 25, 2024, the median follow-up time was 24.6 months in the CT group and 16.8 months in the MT group. A total of 65 patients died by the end of the follow-up period and 3 patients were lost to follow-up. The overall results show that the CT group had better efficacy compared with the MT group. Specifically, the median PFS was significantly longer in the CT group, at 5.3 months (95% CI: 3.7–6.9) compared with 3.8 months (95% CI: 3.2–4.4) in the MT group (HR: 0.51, 95% CI: 0.31–0.85, p = 0.010) (Fig. 1a). Similarly, the median OS was significantly extended, to 10.0 months (95% CI: 8.3–11.7) in the CT group, versus 6.8 months (95% CI: 5.4–8.2) in the MT group (HR: 0.45, 95% CI: 0.27–0.77, p = 0.003) (Fig. 1b). Additionally, the ORR and DCR were also higher in the CT group (41.18% versus 14.71%, p = 0.031; 61.76% versus 35.30%, p = 0.052) (Table 2).

To further assess the efficacy, we conducted subgroup analyses based on factors including patient age, HER2 status, PD-L1 CPS score, primary tumor site, prior treatment history, and metastatic sites. The results show that the CT group generally had higher ORR and DCR compared with the MT group (Table S1). We focused particularly on analyzing PFS and OS. Subgroup analysis demonstrated that the CT group achieved better PFS and OS across most subgroups (Figs. 2a–t and 3a–t). Notably, in patients with high HER2 expression, CT significantly prolonged the PFS (5.9 versus 3.9 months, HR = 0.45, p = 0.014) and OS (10.6 versus 8.3 months, HR = 0.36, p = 0.003) (Figs. S1, S2). Similarly, for patients with a PD-L1 CPS score of 1 or higher, CT significantly improved the PFS (6.0 months versus 3.9 months, HR = 0.47, p = 0.021) and OS (10.5 versus 7.3 months, HR = 0.41, p = 0.009) (Figs. S1, S2).

Moreover, patients of all ages benefited from CT. Patients who had not received prior HER2-targeted therapy or PD-1 inhibitors experienced longer PFS and OS with CT. Also, CT also showed good improvements in PFS and OS for patients with liver, lung, or peritoneal metastases. Although differences in other subgroups were not statistically significant, the CT group’s overall efficacy was superior to that of the MT group (Figs. 2a–t and 3a–t).

In the regression analysis, interactions between age, HER2 status, and metastasis sites did not reach statistical significance (Table S3). For the combination analysis of liver and lung metastasis, although the interaction term was not statistically significant, the two-variable combination analysis indicated that patients with both liver and lung metastases showed significantly improved PFS (4.8 versus 3.1 months, HR = 0.52, p = 0.027) and OS (9.5 versus 6.5 months, HR = 0.48, p = 0.016) in the CT group compared to the MT group (Figs. S3–S5). After conducting univariate analysis using Cox regression, we included data on liver metastasis, lung metastasis, peritoneal metastasis, and prior surgical treatment in multivariable analyses. The multivariate analysis further confirmed that peritoneal metastasis significantly negatively impacted both PFS (HR = 2.64, 95% CI: 1.41–5.06, p = 0.005) and OS (HR = 2.69, 95% CI: 1.41–5.11, p = 0.003) (Table 3, Table S2).

Safety

In this study, all patients (n = 68) experienced at least one TRAE, as shown in Table 4. The most common any-grade TRAEs included a decreased (white-blood-cell) WBC count (56% in the combination group versus 53% in the MT group), asthenia (53% versus 50%), decreased neutrophil count (50% versus 50%), increased aspartate transaminase (41% versus 35%), hypoesthesia (41% versus 35%), anemia (38% versus 35%), and alopecia (38% versus 35%). In the CT group, 2 patients (6%) developed treatment-related rash, and 1 patient (3%) experienced treatment-related decreased platelet count. Notably, 7 patients (21%) in the MT group experienced grade 3–5 TRAEs, with the most common being decreased WBC count (12%), decreased neutrophil count (9%), and anemia (6%), along with one patient (3%) who experienced weight loss. In the CT group, 10 patients (29%) experienced grade 3–5 TRAEs, with the most common being decreased WBC count (12%), decreased neutrophil count (12%), anemia (9%), and hypoesthesia (3%).

Discussion

In this study, we systematically evaluated RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT’s efficacy and safety compared with RC48 MT in those with advanced GC who had failed second-line or subsequent treatments. After applying strict inclusion criteria and PSM, we obtained results showing that the CT group showed significant improvement in PFS, OS, and ORR compared with the MT group, with a good improvement in DCR also. Additionally, the HR for OS in the CT group was significantly lower than that in the MT group, indicating a marked reduction in mortality risk. This result highlights the clinical value of RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT in extending patient survival. Moreover, the AEs associated with the CT were acceptable and manageable, aligning with previous studies. These results provide crucial evidence supporting RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT’s use in advanced GC treatment.

Those with advanced GC typically receive chemotherapy regimens that include platinums, fluoropyrimidines, and taxanes, but these treatments have limited efficacy and are frequently associated with high toxicity. RC48 delivers cytotoxic agents specifically to tumor sites, enhancing antitumor effects while minimizing unnecessary toxicity. In a phase I trial, RC48 showed good results in HER2-positive advanced GC patients. The subsequent phase II RC48-C008 trial further confirmed RC48’s promising efficacy in those with HER2-positive G/GEJ cancer who had received second-line or subsequent treatments, reporting a median PFS of 4.1 months and a median OS of 7.9 months, respectively. In our study, the median PFS for RC48 MT was 3.8 months, and the median OS was 6.8 months, largely consistent with previous studies. Notably, most of those in our study had undergone multiple lines of treatment and had distant metastases, which may have contributed to the relatively poor outcomes. Furthermore, external factors, including pandemic-related travel restrictions and financial pressures, may have influenced treatment decisions and impacted efficacy.

In the DESTINY-Gastric01 trial, 53% of patients had previously received second-line treatment and 47% had received third-line or later treatments, with trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd) achieving an ORR of 51% in East Asian patients with HER2-positive advanced G/GEJ cancer. And the DESTINY-Gastric02 trial further confirmed its efficacy in Western patients who had progressed after first-line treatment. In our study, the ORR was 14.71% for the RC48 MT, and 41.18% for the CT, both slightly lower than the results from the DESTINY-Gastric01. This difference may be related to the more complex clinical characteristics of our patient population, as 55.88% of patients in the RC48 MT group and 50% in the CT group had received third-line or later treatments, with 85.29% and 79.41% of patients, respectively, having multiple metastatic sites. Additionally, T-DXd has a drug-to-antibody ratio (DAR) of 8, which allows for a higher payload of topoisomerase I inhibitor per molecule, enhancing cytotoxicity and bystander effects. However, RC48 has a DAR of 4, with a microtubule inhibitor (MMAE) as its payload, potentially resulting in comparatively lower cytotoxic effects. These pharmacological differences may partly explain the differing efficacy profiles observed between T-DXd and RC48.

RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT’s favorable efficacy is likely due to its synergistic mechanism. Specifically, RC48 targets tumor cells and releases tumor antigens, enhancing the immune system’s ability to recognize and attack the tumor. Moreover, PD-1 inhibitors alleviate immune suppression, further activating T cells’ antitumor activity. This synergy not only improves overall treatment efficacy but may also help overcome MT-related resistance. Additionally, RC48’s bystander effect, supported by growing evidence from both preclinical models and clinical studies, suggests that those with both high and low HER2 expression may benefit from this CT. Moreover, a clinical trial of RC48 combined with toripalimab in pretreated patients with HER2-positive and low HER2-expressing G/GEJ cancers resulted in manageable safety and good efficacy, with a median PFS of 5.1 months, a median OS of 14.0 months, an ORR of 50%, and a DCR of 68%. The results of RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 therapy in our study are consistent with these results and highlight a significant performance improvement over the RC48 MT group. Importantly, our study featured a longer follow-up period, allowing for comprehensive survival data collection, which may account for some discrepancies with earlier studies and enhance the reliability of the current results.

We conducted further subgroup analyses, focusing on those with high HER2 expression or PD-L1 CPS scores of 1 or higher. Previous studies have shown that HER2-positive tumors often exhibit higher immunogenicity, leading to a relatively more active tumor microenvironment and making the immune system more responsive to treatment. In this specific subgroup, we observed that the CT demonstrated promising efficacy. Moreover, several studies have confirmed that patients with advanced GC, especially those with PD-L1-positive tumors, can benefit from PD-1 inhibitors. KEYNOTE-059 demonstrated that pembrolizumab, a PD-1 inhibitor, provided significant clinical benefit in patients with PD-L1-positive advanced G/GEJ cancer. Similarly, the ATTRACTION-2 trial showed that nivolumab significantly improved OS in advanced GC patients who had previously received multiple lines of therapy, with greater benefits observed in PD-L1-positive patients. These findings support the potential of PD-1 inhibitors in this population. For these patients, the tumor microenvironment exhibited stronger immunosuppressive signals, likely enhancing the IT’s efficacy and explaining why this subgroup responds better to CT.

Additionally, metastasis is a hallmark of advanced cancer and is generally associated with poor prognosis. Studies indicate that different metastatic sites, such as liver and lung metastases, are often associated with unique tumor microenvironments, and these variations not only affect tumor cell growth characteristics but also influence therapeutic response. Differences in the cellular composition, vascular supply, and immune cell distribution and activity within the tumor microenvironment contribute to the diverse biological behavior of tumors across metastatic sites. The addition of PD-1 inhibitors may further modulate and reshape these tumor microenvironments, enhancing antitumor efficacy. We found that those with liver, lung, or peritoneal metastases also demonstrated good responses to the CT, suggesting that this approach may provide new therapeutic options for those with a high tumor burden and complex tumor microenvironments, expanding the application of RC48 in advanced GC.

Furthermore, those who had not previously received HER2-targeted therapy or PD-1 inhibitors showed better efficacy with the CT. First, these patients are likely to retain a more intact immune function and tumor microenvironment, making them generally more responsive to therapy. Second, previous treatment with HER2-targeted therapy or PD-1 inhibitors may cause increased tumor-cell resistance to subsequent therapies, thus reducing efficacy. For those with surgical options, the CT following tumor-burden reduction through surgery can effectively inhibit residual tumor cells’ growth and spread, highlighting the potential advantages of this therapeutic approach.

The overall incidence of AEs was similar between the two groups, with the main grade 3–5 TRAEs being decreased WBC count, decreased neutrophil count, and anemia, similar to previous results. Although the incidence of grade 3–5 TRAEs was slightly higher in the CT group than in the MT group, these events were manageable and acceptable. No new safety signals were observed, and no treatment-related deaths were found. Leukopenia and neutropenia in patients receiving multiple lines of therapy may increase the risk of infection, while fatigue may lead to decreased physical strength and limit daily activities, potentially impacting treatment adherence. Additionally, while a few cases of lung damage induced by this combination were reported in previous studies, this AE was not observed in our study. Overall, our data suggest that RC48 combined with anti-PD-1 IT has an acceptable safety profile, although careful monitoring and improved management of TRAEs remain important.

This study still had some limitations. First, the relatively small sample size, particularly in subgroup and interaction analyses, may have reduced statistical power and potentially affected the results’ reliability. We made efforts to collect data from multiple centers in order to increase the sample size and enhance the diversity of the patient population, which helps to reduce the risk of selection bias. Second, while PSM was used to control for confounding factors, completely eliminating all potential confounders remains challenging, necessitating cautious interpretation of some results. Furthermore, owing to medical-records limitations, some adverse events may not have been fully documented, which may have influenced the comprehensive safety assessment. Therefore, larger-scale prospective randomized controlled trials are needed to further validate the study’s conclusions.

Conclusion

Compared with RC48 MT, the combination of RC48 and anti-PD-1 IT demonstrated promising therapeutic advantages in those with advanced GC. This CT not only showed acceptable tolerability and manageable adverse events but also enhanced efficacy and prolongs patient survival. These results highlight the CT as a good new treatment option for advanced GC.
