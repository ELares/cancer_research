# Oncolytic virus OH2 induces PD-L1 upregulation via NF-κB signaling and synergizes with anti-PD-L1 therapy in prostate cancer through a targeted extracellular vesicle delivery system.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Prostate cancer (PCa) is a prevalent malignancy with limited treatment options for advanced stages. Oncolytic virotherapy represents a promising immunotherapeutic approach, but its efficacy and underlying mechanisms in PCa, particularly concerning immune checkpoint regulation, remain unclear.

**METHODS**: The antitumor effects of the oncolytic virus oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 2 (OH2) were evaluated in PCa cell lines and mouse models. Transcriptome sequencing, western blot, chromatin immunoprecipitation-sequencing-quantitative PCR, and flow cytometry were employed to investigate the mechanism of programmed death 1 ligand 1 (PD-L1) regulation. A targeted delivery system, reactive oxygen species (ROS)-responsive aptamer-conjugated anti-prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) extracellular vesicles (RRA-AP-EVs), was engineered from anti-PSMA single chain variable fragment (scFv)-modified extracellular vesicles and an ROS-responsive PD-L1 blocking aptamer. OH2 was loaded via membrane extrusion, and the resulting OH2@RRA-AP-EVs were tested for targeting and therapeutic efficacy following intravenous administration.

**RESULTS**: OH2 effectively killed PCa cells but simultaneously activated the IKK/I-κBα/p65 pathway, leading to PD-L1 upregulation and adaptive immune resistance. While combining OH2 with anti-PD-L1 improved outcomes, clinical translation was hindered by delivery challenges. The novel OH2@RRA-AP-EVs system demonstrated precise tumor targeting and ROS-triggered local PD-L1 blockade. Intravenous injection of OH2@RRA-AP-EVs showed superior tumor control (inhibiting tumor growth by 70% vs free OH2) and enhanced CD8+T cell infiltration and function compared with free OH2 (greater than twofold increase in intratumoral CD8+T cell infiltration along with over twofold upregulation of key effector molecules).

**CONCLUSION**: This study identifies a mechanism of OH2-induced PD-L1 expression in PCa and provides a versatile, targeted delivery platform that enables effective intravenous viro-immunotherapy, overcoming key translational barriers.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Oncolytic virus oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 2 (OH2) has demonstrated antitumor efficacy in several solid cancers, but its effect in prostate cancer remained unexplored. While other oncolytic viruses are known to potentially upregulate immune checkpoints like programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), the specific molecular mechanism in prostate cancer was unclear. Furthermore, the clinical application of OH2 is limited by its need for intratumoral injection and the associated systemic toxicity of combined immune checkpoint blockade.

WHAT THIS STUDY ADDS

This study provides the first evidence that OH2 induces PD-L1 upregulation in prostate cancer via the nuclear factor kappa-B/p65 pathway. More importantly, we developed a novel, tumor microenvironment-responsive delivery system, reactive oxygen species-responsive aptamer-conjugated anti-prostate-specific membrane antigen extracellular vesicles (RRA-AP-EVs), which enables the effective intravenous co-delivery of OH2 and a localized PD-L1 blocker. This strategy overcomes the delivery bottleneck and significantly enhances antitumor immunity.

HOW THIS STUDY MIGHT AFFECT RESEARCH, PRACTICE OR POLICY

The RRA-AP-EVs platform presents a viable strategy for the systemic administration of oncolytic virotherapy, potentially expanding its use to target metastatic disease. This approach could influence future clinical trial design for combining oncolytic viruses with immunotherapy. Furthermore, the modular design of the platform offers a blueprint for developing targeted, conditionally activated delivery systems for other therapeutic agents.

Introduction

 Prostate cancer (PCa) is the most common malignant tumor in the male urinary and reproductive system, with its incidence and mortality ranking first and second among male malignancies globally. Despite the emergence of various new treatment methods in recent years, the efficacy of existing therapies for advanced, especially metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC), remains very limited. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have shown significant efficacy in many solid tumors but have not achieved the desired clinical results in mCRPC.

Oncolytic virus (OV) therapy represents a promising strategy to stimulate antitumor immunity and convert immunologically “cold” tumors to “hot”. The oncolytic herpes simplex virus type 2 (OH2), based on a herpes simplex virus (HSV)-2 backbone with deletions of ICP34.5 and ICP47 and insertion of granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF), has demonstrated antitumor activity in several solid tumors and is in Phase III clinical trials. However, its effects in prostate cancer are unexplored.

Here, we report that OH2 effectively kills PCa cells but concurrently upregulates programmed death 1 ligand 1 (PD-L1) expression via activation of the IKK/I-κBα/p65 (NF-κB) pathway. This provides a rationale for combining OH2 with PD-L1 blockade. However, OH2 requires intratumoral injection, limiting its use against metastatic disease. Systemic PD-L1 blockade, meanwhile, can cause immune-related adverse events. To overcome these barriers, we developed a targeted extracellular vesicle (EV) delivery system. We engineered EVs with a prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA)-targeting single-chain antibody (AP-EVs) and further conjugated a reactive oxygen species (ROS)-responsive PD-L1-blocking aptamer (RRA) to create RRA-AP-EVs. This platform is designed for systemic co-delivery of OH2 and localized PD-L1 blockade.

Thus, this work defines a mechanism of adaptive resistance to OH2 in PCa and introduces a modular, targeted delivery platform designed to enable effective intravenous viro-immunotherapy.

Results

Verification of the tumor control effect of OH2 on prostate cancer

OH2 OV genetic modifications were shown in figure 1a. We tested the in vitro tumor control effect of OH2 against PCa cell lines using various methods. We detected the time/concentration-dependent killing effect of OV OH2 on different PCa cell lines (22Rv-1, LNCaP, C4-2, DU-145, PC-3, and RM-1) by cell counting kit (CCK)-8 assay (figure 1b). It was presented that both human-derived and mouse-derived PCa cells showed an increasing trend in tumor cell cytotoxicity as the multiplicity of infection (MOI) increased and the treatment time increased. When MOI=1 or 5, the tumor killing effect of OH2 reached the platform stage, based on which we determined the amount of virus used in subsequent studies. After annexin V/Propidium Iodide (PI) double staining and flow cytometry detection, it was demonstrated that apoptosis levels in PCa cells after intervention of OH2 significantly increased (online supplemental sFigure 1a). Through colony formation experiments (online supplemental sFigure 1b), 5-ethynyl-2’-deoxyuridine staining experiment (online supplemental sFigure 1d), we found that after intervention of oncolytic virotherapy, the proliferation of PCa cells was attenuated. Migration assay for PCa cells represented that infection of OH2 could also attenuate the tumor migration capability (online supplemental sFigure 2a). In addition, ROS were detected by flow cytometry and observed to elevate after infection of OH2 in PCa cell (online supplemental sFigure 1c). We also observed green fluorescence protein (GFP) expression after the infection of OH2-GFP, which was constructed by inserting the GFP gene into OH2 (online supplemental sFigure 2b,c). We further co-cultured normal prostate cells (RWPE-1) and tumor cells and introduced OH2 intervention, using prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) to distinguish between them, and found that OH2 has tumor-selective infection characteristics (online supplemental sFigure 2d).

To further investigate the therapeutic effects of OVs on PCa in vivo, subcutaneous tumor models in BALB/c nude mice and C57BL/6 mice were used to evaluate the antitumor effects of intratumoral (i.t.) injection of OH2 (derived from RM-1 cells and 22Rv-1 cells) (figure 1c and e). In vivo experiments revealed that oncolytic virotherapy had significant tumor control effect in tumor derived from mice cell line RM-1 and human cell line 22Rv-1 vaccination (figure 1d and f). We demonstrated the good safety profile of OH2 through H&E staining (online supplemental sFigure 3a,b). Multicolor immune flow cytometry was applied to analyze tumor immune microenvironment. Representative gating strategy to isolate dendritic cells (DCs), CD8+T cells, CD4+T cells, and macrophages was illustrated in figure 1g. We found that after the intervention of OH2, the proportion of CD8+T cells and CD4+T cells increased, and the proportion of macrophages and DCs decreased significantly (figure 1h).

Furthermore, we analyzed functional marker variation of CD8+T cells and macrophages. We found that for CD8+T cells, activation marker CD69 increased, while the functional markers interferon gamma (IFN-γ) and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-α) decreased. At the same time, the cell exhaustion-related markers programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) and T-cell immunoglobulin domain and mucin domain-3 significantly increased (figure 1i). For macrophages, the results indicated that M2-like macrophage (arginase) makers were upregulated and M1-like macrophage makers (IFN-γ, major histocompatibility complex class I) were downregulated (figure 1j). The above results seemed to present challenges for the application of OH2 in PCa. Treatment with OH2 proved to improve the infiltration and activation of CD8+ T cells but could also induce a decline in the function and increase in exhaustion markers of CD8+ T cells.

Discovery of the upregulation of PD-L1 and exploration of the mechanism

According to former publications, oncolytic virotherapy could contribute to the upregulation of PD-L1, which might lead to the depressed tumor immune microenvironment and tumor immune evasion. Hence, we detected PD-L1 expression in tumor tissue and found that PD-L1 level was lifted after intervention of OH2 in CD45− cells within PCa (figure 2a). Immunofluorescence revealed that PD-L1 in RM-1 tumor-bearing mice was upregulated after the intervention (figure 2b). Consistent results were also observed in BALB/c nude mice (figure 2c). Moreover, western blot assay (figure 2d) and reverse-transcription quantitative real-time PCR (RT-qPCR) assay (figure 2e) revealed the change of PD-L1 in protein and messenger RNA (mRNA) levels in PCa cell lines after oncolytic virotherapy.

To further elucidate the mechanism of PD-L1 upregulation in PCa cells after OH2 intervention, we performed transcriptome sequencing on 22Rv-1 cells and C4-2 cells. Through comprehensive analysis of transcriptome sequencing data from cell lines and tumor tissues, we found that the nuclear factor kappa-B (NF-κB) pathway was significantly upregulated after infection with OH2 (online supplemental sFigure 3c). Given the increased transcriptional level of PD-L1 (gene symbol CD274), it was reasonable to speculate that this could be attributable to changes in the levels of upstream transcription factors of PD-L1. Therefore, we analyzed the changes in activity of transcription factors (figure 2f, online supplemental sFigure 3d,e). Taking the intersection of transcription factors differentially activated according to the transcriptome sequencing results with the upstream transcription factors predicted for PD-L1 in the Jaspar database, it was found that RELA (coding protein p65) was the only common transcription factor, which was also an important component of the NF-κB protein family (figure 2g).

Furthermore, according to Jaspar data base, transcription factor binding sites for p65 on the CD274 gene were identified. By mining the chromatin immunoprecipitation-sequencing (ChIP-seq) data in the Cistrome Data Browse, in PCa cell (LNCaP) under various treatments, signal peak of p65 binding was indeed present at the binding sites predicted for p65 in the Jaspar database (online supplemental sFigure 3f). We consequently performed ChIP-qPCR, confirming that p65 could directly bind to the transcription factor binding region of CD274, and infection with OH2 could upregulate this binding (figure 3a). After the confirmation that p65 could directly bind to the transcription factor binding region of the CD274 gene, we need to further elucidate whether oncolytic virotherapy regulates the expression of PD-L1 by modulating p65. Western blot assay illustrated that infection with OH2 could inhibit NF-κB inhibitor alpha (IκB-α) and lifted p-p65/p-65 activity (figure 3b). In addition, application of NF-κB pathway inhibitor QNZ could reverse the upregulation of PD-L1 expression both in mRNA and protein levels through RT-qPCR, western blot, and flow cytometry assays (figure 3c–e and online supplemental sFigure 3g). To provide direct genetic evidence for the necessity of p65, we performed loss-of-function experiments. After screening three independent siRNAs targeting RELA (p65), we selected two that achieved efficient knockdown (figure 3f). In both 22Rv-1 and C4-2 cell lines, knockdown of p65 significantly attenuated the upregulation of PD-L1 induced by OH2 infection and the addition of TNF-α, restored PD-L1 expression in p65-knockdown cells (figure 3g,h).

We detected a significant upregulation of PD-L1 expression in the tumor microenvironment (TME) and primarily demonstrated its mechanism. This provides a theoretical basis for the combination of OV OH2 and anti-PD-L1 therapy. We found that the combination therapy indeed achieved better tumor control than monotherapy (figure 4a). We further examined the function of CD8 cells (figure 4b). We found that the function of CD8+T cells was downregulated after OV monotherapy, while it was significantly improved after combination with PD-L1 therapy, including perforin, TNF-α, IFN-γ, and granzyme B (GZMB) (figure 4c,d).

Construction and isolation of engineered vesicles RRA-AP-EVs

We compared the efficacy of intravenous injection and i.t. injection, and found that i.t. achieved better tumor control (figure 4e). To construct a platform suitable for intravenous administration, we first validated the high expression of PSMA in patients with PCa through using The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) database and immunohistochemical staining of tissue sections from our center (figure 4f, online supplemental sFigure 4a). Further, we detected the expression of PSMA in commonly used PCa cell lines at both the RNA and protein levels (online supplemental sFigure 4b–d). Subsequently, given that we have found the OV OH2 combined with PD-L1 has better therapeutic effects and that tumor cells produce more ROS after OV intervention, we designed an ROS-responsive PD-L1 blocking aptamer (aptPD-L1) and modified it on AP-EVs to construct RRA-AP-EVs (figure 4g). To obtain antibodies with high affinity for PSMA protein, we conducted protein analysis on the structure and subcellular localization of PSMA and constructed a phage display library to select suitable sequences (online supplemental sFigure 4e–h). We identified eight targeting antibodies (11, 17, 22, 27, 28, 32, 27, 38) through phage display technology. Subsequently, we evaluated their binding capabilities to PCa cells using cell ELISA assays. Among them, antibody 37 exhibited the best binding performance across three PSMA positive cell lines (figure 4h). We constructed an overexpressing AP cells based on figure 4i and synthesized lentivirus. After transfecting 293T cells with the lentivirus, we confirmed the successful transfection and expression using RT-qPCR, western blotting and flow cytometry methods (figure 4j,k). Subsequently, we conducted monoclonal culture to screen for the cell line with the highest expression. By comparing the expression levels of monoclonal and mixed clones using RT-qPCR, we selected the C4 cell line (AP-293T) with the highest expression (online supplemental sFigure 5a).

We isolated AP-EVs from the AP-293T cell line using differential centrifugation to enhance their targeting ability for PCa. Nanoparticle tracking analysis (NTA), transmission electron microscopy and zeta potential detection revealed the size, quantity, morphology, structure and stability of the EVs before and after modification (figure 5a,b). Western blot results showed stable expression of the EV markers CD9, CD63, and CD81 in the EVs, with low expression in cell lysates. In contrast, calnexin was prominently present only in cell lysates. Meanwhile, the HA-Tag was detectable in the modified vesicles, confirming its successful membrane localization and expression (figure 5c). We measured the fluorescence intensity of the solution before and after co-incubation with the vesicles and found a significant decrease in fluorescence after co-incubation (figure 5d). Subsequently, to demonstrate that the aptamer could be successfully released in the TME, we treated RRA-AP-EVs with different concentrations of H2O2 and then measured the fluorescence intensity in the supernatant. The results showed that the aptamer could be released into the solution in the presence of ROS (figure 5e).

Verification of the function of RRA-AP-EVs and loading of OH2

To demonstrate the targeting ability of RRA-AP-EVs for PCa cells, we stained the EVs with PKH26 and co-incubated them with PCa cells. The results showed that, compared with unmodified EVs, the modified EVs were more readily taken up by tumor cells and exhibited stronger tumor targeting ability (figure 5f, online supplemental sFigure 5b). Consistent conclusions were also obtained through confocal microscopy observations (figure 5g). To further clarify the ability of RRA-AP-EVs to distinguish between tumor cells and normal cells, we co-cultured normal prostate cells (RWPE-1) with tumor cells and added PKH26-stained RRA-AP-EVs. By using PSMA to differentiate between normal and tumor cells, we compared their ability to take up the vesicles (online supplemental sFigure 5c). The results showed that the modified EVs were more likely to be engulfed by tumor cells (figure 5h). Further, after intravenous injection of DIR-stained vesicles, the fluorescence in C57BL/6 mice was monitored at different time points using in vivo imaging. The results demonstrated that the modified EVs had a better tumor background ratio (figure 5i, online supplemental sFigure 5d).

To explore the method of loading OH2, we attempted electroporation and membrane extrusion to load OH2-GFP. The treated products were then used to intervene in PCa cells. We detected GFP expression throughflow cytometry to characterize the infection efficiency (figure 6a). CCK8 assay was used to verify the cytotoxic effect on tumor cells (figure 6b). The results showed that membrane extrusion had better efficiency in infecting tumor cells compared with electroporation. Transmission electron microscopy results revealed the loaded OH2 (figure 6c). Detection of GM-CSF expression indicated that the loaded OH2 had better infection ability (online supplemental sFigure 6a). To further substantiate successful viral encapsulation, we performed immunofluorescence and qPCR assays. Confocal microscopy confirmed the colocalization of Cy3-labeled OH2 (red) within PKH67-labeled EVs (green) (online supplemental sFigure 6b). Moreover, qPCR quantification of the OH2 genome demonstrated a significant reduction of free viral DNA in the supernatant post-loading, and a sharp increase on vesicle disruption (online supplemental sFigure 6c), collectively confirming efficient OH2 packaging into RRA-AP-EVs. Finally, we compared the therapeutic effects of intravenous injection of OH2 and OH2@RRA-AP-EVs using a subcutaneous tumor xenograft model. The results showed that the loaded OH2 administered intravenously had better tumor control effects (figure 6d). We also performed flow cytometry analysis (online supplemental sFigure 6d). The results revealed that after intervention with OH2@RRA-AP-EVs, the proportion of CD8+T cells significantly increased (figure 6e,f). Meanwhile, indicators related to T-cell activation (CD69) and tumor-killing functions, including perforin, IFN-γ, GZMB, and TNF-α, all significantly increased (figure 6g,h). To analyze the immunologic memory induced by this therapeutic strategy, we performed a tumor rechallenge assay. The results confirmed that the OH2 delivery system elicited a potent immune memory response in mice and provided long-term protection against tumor recurrence (figure 6i).

Methods and materials

Oncolytic virus

The OH2 and OH2-GFP were supplied by Binhui Biopharmaceutical (Wuhan, China). OH2 was constructed based on the HG52 strain of herpes simplex virus type II. To minimize neurotoxicity to normal cells and enhance immune recognition, the ICP47 and ICP34.5 genes were removed. Additionally, the CSF-2 gene, which encodes GM-CSF, was introduced to facilitate the recruitment and activation of DCs. OH2-GFP was engineered to include a GFP coding gene to simplify the detection of the virus.

Cell culture

The PCa cell lines 22Rv-1, LNCaP clone FGC, C4-2, DU-145, and PC-3 were cultured in Roswell Park Memorial Institute 1640 medium (Gibco, Rockville, USA). The RM-1 cell line was maintained in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle Medium (DMEM) (Gibco, Rockville, USA). The RWPE-1 cell line was grown in Prostate Epithelial Cell Medium (PEpiCM, Sciencell, USA). Unless otherwise specified, all cell culture media were supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (ABW, Shanghai, China) and penicillin-streptomycin-amphotericin B solution (Procell, Wuhan, China). Cell cultures were maintained in a humidified incubator at 37°C with 5% CO2. Routine Mycoplasma contamination checks were performed on all cell lines. Authentication of the cell lines was confirmed via short tandem repeat profiling.

Mice

Male C57BL/6 mice and BALB/c nude mice, aged 4–6 weeks, were sourced from Beijing Vital River, and used for in vivo tumor growth assays.

All procedures involving mice were conducted in a specific pathogen-free laminar flow animal facility. Following their arrival, the mice were acclimatized and monitored for 1-week prior to undergoing experimental procedures. This study primarily employed two subcutaneous tumor models in mice.

Flow cytometry for tumor tissue

The tumors were meticulously excised and immediately placed on ice. A digestion solution was prepared using DMEM without serum, supplemented with deoxyribonuclease I from bovine pancreas (0.1 mg/mL, Sigma-Aldrich, USA), Collagenase (1 mg/mL, Sigma-Aldrich, USA), and hyaluronidase from bovine testes (0.1 mg/mL, Sigma-Aldrich, USA). The tumors were finely minced and mixed with 2 mL of this solution, then incubated at 37°C for 1 hour. Following digestion, the tissue was gently homogenized and passed through a 200-mesh sieve to remove any remaining tissue clumps. The resulting mixture was centrifuged at 4°C, 400 rpm for 5 min to separate the supernatant, which helped to remove cell aggregates and debris. A second centrifugation at 4°C, 400 g for 5 min was performed to collect the cell pellet. The pellet was then treated to remove red blood cells. This process yielded a single-cell suspension, which was subsequently subjected to viability staining, surface staining, and intracellular staining. After staining, the samples were filtered through a 400-mesh sieve and analyzed using a flow cytometer (CytoFLEX, Beckman Coulter, USA) and FlowJo V.10.8.1 software (https://www.flowjo.com/).

Details on the usage of flow cytometry antibodies and other reagents can be found in online supplemental sTable 1.

Chromatin immunoprecipitation

ChIP was performed following the instructions of SimpleChIP Plus Sonication Chromatin IP Kit (Cell Signaling Technology, USA). 2×107 cells were treated with phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) or OH2 for 24 hours and harvested for ChIP. Cells were then fixed with formaldehyde and lysed. Sonication-based fragmentation was performed with a 30% amplitude for a total of 10 min, using a protocol with 2 s pulses of sonication followed by 3 s intervals, to fragment chromatin and obtain DNA fragments primarily ranging from 200 bp to 500 bp (BiLon, China). ChIP-grade P65 antibody and ChIP-Grade Protein G Magnetic Beads were incubated with samples overnight. After reversing the cross-links by incubating at 65°C, DNA purification was carried out, followed by qPCR analysis as described above.

Bioinformatics analysis

The bioinformatics analysis was performed using R V.4.2.2, focusing on the transcriptome data to explore various aspects such as pathway enrichment. Cistrome Data Browse (https://data.cistrome.org/) was used for collecting ChIP-seq results. JASPAR (https://jaspar.org/) was employed for predicting transcription factor binding sites. The UCSC Genome Browser (https://genome.ucsc.edu/) was used to display ChIP-seq data and analysis of transcription factor binding sites. The TCGA database (https://www.cancer.gov/ccg/research/genome-sequencing/tcga) and the CCLE database (https://sites.broadinstitute.org/ccle) were used to analyze the RNA expression levels in patients with PCa and PCa-related cell lines.

Phage display technology

The phage antibody library was prepared by culturing glycerol stock in 2YT medium, infecting with M13K07 phage, and purifying the phages through centrifugation and PEG-NaCl precipitation. For antigen panning, immunotubes were coated with antigen, blocked, and incubated with phages, followed by elution and amplification in TG1 cells. After three rounds of panning, phages were used to infect TG1 cells, and single colonies were picked for ELISA validation. Positive clones were sequenced and expressed as single chain variable fragment (scFv) antibodies in Escherichia coli, purified using Ni-NTA column, and confirmed by Sodium Dodecyl Sulfate - PolyAcrylamide Gel Electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE). Finally, the scFv proteins were validated by flow cytometry on positive and negative cells. The phage display technology was conducted by Dia-an Biotechnology (Wuhan, China).

EV isolation

Once the stable 293T-AP cells were obtained, the isolation of EVs was carried out as previously reported. When cells reached the appropriate density, the culture medium was replaced with fresh serum-free medium. Cells were then exposed to ultraviolet (UV) light for 1 hour and incubated overnight. These conditions of serum deprivation and UV exposure significantly increased the production of EVs by 293T cells. The supernatant was collected and subjected to a series of centrifugation steps at 4°C. An initial centrifugation at 1,500 rpm for 6 min removed cells and large debris. A second centrifugation at 5,000 rpm for 15 min further removed smaller debris and impurities. The supernatant was then filtered through a 0.45 µm filter and centrifuged at 18,000×g for 60 min. The resulting EV pellet was resuspended in 100–1,000 µL of 1×PBS depending on the different experimental objectives.

Conjugation of RRA to AP-EVs

The cholesterol-modified thioketal was conjugated to the Thiol (SH)-modified PD-L1 blocking aptamer via a maleimide linker, and then the complex was attached to AP-EVs through cholesterol (from Xi’an Haoran Biotechnology). The incubation method was performed according to the work of Wang et al. The PD-L1 blocking aptamer (aptPD-L1) was derived from the report by Lai, and aptamer synthesis service was provided by Huzhou Hippo Biotechnology.

Oncolytic virus loading

In this study, we attempted two methods to load OH2. EVs derived from 5×107 cell were resuspended in 1,000 µL of PBS and mixed with an equal volume of OH2 (TCID₅₀: 10⁸/mL) for further OV loading. The electroporation method was carried out according to our previously described protocol, while the membrane extrusion method was performed as described by Ban et al.

Statistical analysis

Quantitative data are expressed as the mean±SD. Two-tailed Student’s t-test was used to evaluate differences in immune cell infiltration, gene expression, and other biological activities between control and treatment groups. For variables that were not normally distributed, the Mann-Whitney U test was employed. The experimental data of flow cytometry data was primarily carried out using FlowJo V.10.8.1 software (https://www.flowjo.com/) a powerful tool for flow cytometry data analysis. GraphPad Prism V.9 (https://www.graphpad.com/scientific-software/prism/) was employed for creating graphs and conducting statistical analyses to interpret the results from experiments.

Other methods can be found in the online supplemental methods document.

Discussion

This study delved into the role of OV OH2 in the treatment of PCa, systematically elucidated its molecular mechanism of regulating immune checkpoint molecules, and developed a novel multifunctional delivery system that can achieve intravenous infusion of OH2 and reprogramming of the tumor immune microenvironment.

The study first confirmed the favorable efficacy and safety of OH2 monotherapy in PCa, consistent with its profile in other malignancies. However, in immunocompetent mice, although OH2 increased CD8+ T-cell infiltration, it also impaired their function. This prompted us to investigate PD-L1 expression, which was upregulated post-OH2 treatment both in vitro and in vivo. To elucidate the mechanism, we combined transcriptomics with public database analysis and identified significant activation of the NF-κB pathway and its key component p65 following OH2 intervention, which could be reversed by an NF-κB inhibitor. ChIP-seq data indicated p65 binding peaks in the CD274 promoter region, which we confirmed via ChIP-qPCR. This adaptive immune resistance mechanism induces immune evasion providing a theoretical basis for the combined application of OH2 and immune checkpoint inhibitors. Our experimental data show that the combination of OH2 and anti-PD-L1 can produce synergistic antitumor effects. On the basis of increased infiltration of immune cells, particularly CD8+ T cells, combination therapy can also rescue exhausted CD8+ T cells, and promote the secretion of effector molecules such as IFN-γ, TNF-α, perforin, and GZMB, thereby transforming “cold” tumors into immune-activated “hot” tumors.

Despite this synergy, clinical translation faces two major hurdles: the i.t. administration route of OH2, which is unsuitable for metastatic disease, and the systemic toxicity associated with intravenous ICI administration. To overcome these dual limitations, we designed a novel targeted delivery system: RRA-AP-EVs. This platform integrates three major functions: active targeting based on PSMA, ROS-responsive immune regulation, and efficient virus delivery. The high-affinity anti-PSMA single-chain antibody screen was based on phage display technology and ensured the precise enrichment in PCa tissues. The targeting performance has been validated through in vitro co-culture experiments and in vivo experiments, laying the foundation for systematic drug delivery and reducing off-target viral spread. Leveraging the elevated ROS levels in OH2-infected tumors, we introduced an RRA to block PD-L1. This design remains stable in the systemic circulation, avoiding systemic immune activation, and specifically releases aptamers in the TME, locally blocking the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway.

For viral loading, membrane extrusion proved superior to electroporation, successfully encapsulating intact OH2 into RRA-AP-EVs. While synthetic nanoparticles or liposomes can improve pharmacokinetics, they may face challenges with biocompatibility and lack the inherent biological functions of natural vesicles. Cell-based carriers (eg, mesenchymal stem cells) leverage cellular tropism but introduce complexities related to cell viability, potential tumorigenicity, and scalability. In contrast, our RRA-AP-EVs combine the natural biocompatibility of EVs with engineered functionalities. After intravenous injection, these vesicles can protect OH2 from neutralizing antibodies and achieve tumor-targeted delivery. Both in vitro and in vivo experiments have confirmed that the encapsulated virus still maintains high infectivity and replication ability. While promoting the spread and replication of the virus, it also enhances the infiltration and activation of T cells, manifested by an increase in the number of CD8+T cells and enhanced cytotoxic function.

There are still some limitations to this study: First, in our study, we focused solely on the proportion and functional changes of T cells in the microenvironment, without fully addressing other types of immune and non-immune cells. Other cells and non-cellular components also play a crucial role in the TME and OV therapy. Second, the safety, biodistribution, and scalable production of OH2@RRA-AP-EVs require further validation before clinical translation. Future studies must assess its long-term safety and potential off-target effects and efficacy against metastases in advanced models, including non-human primates. Additionally, translating the complex fabrication process into a robust, Guanosine MonoPhosphate (GMP)-compliant manufacturing pipeline presents a major challenge. This involves standardizing the production of engineered EVs, controlling batch-to-batch variability in viral loading and targeting ligand display, and meeting regulatory requirements for a combined biologic product—all of which are critical for its viability as a systemic intravenous therapy. Third, the clinical transformation also faces challenges for realizing large-scale production at the clinical level. Fourth, while the immunocompetent RM-1 model was instrumental for evaluating T-cell mediated immunity, it does not fully replicate the intricate and heterogeneous TME of human PCa. The translational relevance of our findings would be strengthened by future validation in humanized mouse models or ex vivo human tumor cultures, which more accurately mirror human-specific immune interactions and viral tropism. Fifth, the efficacy of our targeting strategy is critically contingent on PSMA expression. Clinical data reveal a heterogeneous landscape: PSMA uptake varies substantially even within PCa (lesion-to-background ratios range from 1.5 to 6.7), with a significant proportion of lesions falling below common diagnostic thresholds. More critically, approximately 5–15% of patients with mCRPC present as PSMA-negative on imaging, defining a subset unlikely to benefit from a PSMA-only approach. To address this fundamental heterogeneity, our platform’s modular design permits ligand exchange based on pretreatment molecular profiling, enabling a precision strategy tailored to the patient’s tumor antigen profile.

Looking ahead, due to its strong modifiability, the RRA-AP-EV platform is not only suitable for delivering OH2, but the modular design can also load multiple therapeutic agents, such as other OVs, chemotherapy drugs, immune modulators, or siRNA. For instance, integrating DNA damage response inhibition has been shown particularly relevant in urologic cancers, where preclinical and clinical trials have demonstrated improved outcomes. By replacing targets such as Human Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor 2 (HER2) or Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR), this platform can also be extended to other types of cancer. In addition, the aptamers modified on the vesicles can also be replaced with antibodies or aptamers targeting other targets to address different situations. Finally, the potential of this platform for combination with Chimeric Antigen Receptor T-cell immunotherapy (CAR-T) therapy and tumor vaccines can further advance the development of precision immunotherapy.

In summary, this study deepens our understanding of the interaction between OVs and the tumor immune microenvironment and provides preclinical proof of concept for a novel targeted delivery system. Our findings suggest that this strategy could potentially address key limitations of existing OV therapy, such as the need for i.t. injection and systemic toxicity of combined checkpoint blockade. However, its clinical feasibility, particularly regarding reliable systemic administration and safety, requires further validation in advanced models. If successfully translated, this approach may offer new directions for immunotherapy of PCa and other solid tumors.
