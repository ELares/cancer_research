# Pasritamig, a First-in-Class, Bispecific T-Cell Engager Targeting Human Kallikrein 2, in Metastatic Castration-Resistant Prostate Cancer: A Phase I Study.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: We report phase I trial results for pasritamig, a first-in-class, T-cell-engaging bispecific antibody targeting human kallikrein 2 (KLK2) expressed on the surface of prostate cancer (PC) cells.

**METHODS**: Participants had metastatic castration-resistant PC and ≥1 prior therapy. Pasritamig was escalated from 0.5 mg to 2,000 mg for subcutaneous administration and from 150 mg to 900 mg for intravenous (IV) administration at dosing frequencies ranging from once every week to once every 6 weeks with different step-up dosing schedules. The primary objectives were to determine safety and the recommended phase II dose (RP2D) of pasritamig. Secondary objectives included preliminary assessment of antitumor activity.

**RESULTS**: One hundred seventy-four participants received pasritamig, with a median of 4 prior lines of systemic therapy. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs) occurred in 144 of 174 (82.8%) participants, with 17 of 174 (9.8%) experiencing grade ≥3 TRAEs. The RP2D was determined to be 3.5 mg (day 1), 18 mg (day 8), 300 mg (day 15), and then 300 mg IV once every 6 weeks. In the RP2D safety population (n = 45), infusion-related reactions (11/45, 24.4%), fatigue (7/45, 15.6%), cytokine release syndrome (CRS; 4/45, 8.9%, all grade 1), and lipase increase (4/45, 8.9%) were the most frequent TRAEs; all were grade 1 or 2. In the RP2D efficacy population (n = 33), median radiographic progression-free survival was 7.85 (95% CI, 2.89 to not estimable) months, and 14 of 33 (42.4%) participants achieved a ≥50% decrease from baseline in prostate-specific antigen.

**CONCLUSION**: Pasritamig demonstrated a favorable safety profile with very low rates of CRS and could be safely administered in an outpatient setting. Preliminary antitumor activity demonstrated proof of concept for KLK2 as a target in PC, warranting further development of pasritamig.

INTRODUCTION

While immunotherapy has revolutionized treatment of many solid tumors, immune checkpoint inhibitors have generally failed to demonstrate clinical utility in prostate cancer (PC). T-cell engager therapy is a distinct immunotherapy approach that uses bispecific antibodies to facilitate synapse formation between T cells and tumor cells, ultimately resulting in T-cell–mediated lysis of target-expressing tumor cells. A number of bispecific antibodies are in clinical development, including several for PC. However, cytokine release syndrome (CRS) is a common, frequently serious toxicity that often requires hospitalization, complicating clinical development and implementation of T-cell engager therapy for PC.

CONTEXT

 Key Objective 

This first-in-human trial aimed to determine the safety, recommended phase II dose, and antitumor activity of pasritamig, a first-in-class, T-cell–engaging bispecific antibody targeting human kallikrein 2, a novel, highly specific, prostate cancer (PC) antigen, in participants with metastatic castration-resistant PC.

 Knowledge Generated 

Unlike previous T-cell engagers for PC, pasritamig demonstrated a favorable safety profile with low rates of treatment-related adverse events and very low rates of low-grade cytokine release syndrome, enabling outpatient dosing on a convenient schedule. Pasritamig achieved durable responses and promising radiographic progression-free survival in heavily pretreated participants.

 Relevance (A. Necchi) 

In the evolving landscape of PC treatment, the safety and initial efficacy results of pasritamig have expanded the spectrum of therapeutic targets and will help in developing tolerable and effective combination therapies of T-cell engagers in patients with earlier stage PC.*

*Relevance section written by JCO Associate Editor Andrea Necchi, MD.

Human kallikrein 2 (encoded by the KLK2 gene and hereafter referred to as KLK2) is a trypsin-like serine protease produced by prostate epithelial cells that has high homology and coexpression with the prostate-specific antigen (PSA)/KLK3 gene. KLK2 is regulated by androgen receptor signaling and has minimal expression in normal, nonprostate tissues, making it an ideal target for PC therapy. Its expression in prostate adenocarcinoma is nearly ubiquitous in the localized and castrate-sensitive settings, and high expression levels are maintained in metastatic castration-resistant PC (mCRPC).

Pasritamig is a human immunoglobulin G1 bispecific antibody that simultaneously binds to CD3 receptor complexes on T cells and cell surface KLK2 on tumor cells, directing T-cell cytotoxicity to KLK2-expressing tumor cells. We present dose escalation and expansion results from an ongoing first-in-human, phase I study (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT04898634) evaluating the safety, pharmacokinetics (PK), pharmacodynamics (PD), and preliminary antitumor activity of pasritamig in participants with mCRPC.

METHODS

Oversight

This study was performed according to the Declaration of Helsinki and Good Clinical Practice guidelines. The study protocol and amendments were approved by the participating institutions' review boards. Participants or their legal representatives provided written informed consent before initiating study-related activities. The sponsor, Johnson & Johnson, was involved in the study's conceptualization and design, and the collection, analysis, and interpretation of data in collaboration with study investigators. Funding for medical writing support was provided by the sponsor.

Design and Procedures

This multicenter phase I study (Data Supplement, Fig S1, online only) of pasritamig in mCRPC consists of two parts: part 1 (dose escalation) and part 2 (dose expansion). Results are reported for parts 1 and 2A, which completed enrollment. Recruitment for parts 2B-D is ongoing.

In part 1, dose escalation was initiated by subcutaneous (SC) administration, and intravenous (IV) dosing was subsequently implemented to explore higher dose levels without the need for multiple SC injections. SC administration began at a starting dose of 0.5 mg once every 3 weeks. Subsequent dose levels were informed using a modified continual reassessment method with overdose control (Data Supplement, Methods). For IV administration, the starting treatment dose (150 mg) was based on a PK simulation, built with PK parameters and safety/antitumor experience from SC administration. Step-up doses for SC and IV administration were incorporated to mitigate the occurrence of cytokine-mediated toxicities. Premedication included 1 dose of steroids for each step-up dose and initial treatment dose as well as antipyretics and antihistamines.

The study evaluation team, composed of study investigators and the sponsor's clinical team, monitored safety at each dose escalation step in part 1 and at regular intervals in part 2. For part 1 participants in the dose escalation cohorts, hospitalization was required for a minimum of 36 hours after each step-up dose and the first full treatment dose; for part 1 participants in the PK/PD cohorts (Data Supplement, Table S1) and part 2 participants, hospitalization was optional based on protocol criteria. Participants received pasritamig until radiographic disease progression, unequivocal clinical progression, unacceptable toxicity, withdrawal of consent, or loss to follow-up, whichever occurred first.

Participants

Men aged 18 years and older with castrate-resistant, histologically confirmed adenocarcinoma of the prostate, and an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group performance status of 0 or 1, who were previously treated with ≥1 line of androgen-receptor pathway inhibitors or chemotherapy, were eligible for enrollment. Part 1 included participants with measurable or evaluable disease. Part 2A included participants with bone or lymph node (LN) metastases without evidence of visceral metastases, who had a PSA level ≥2 ng/mL at screening, and who were previously treated with an androgen-receptor pathway inhibitor, ≤two prior taxane regimens, and no other cytotoxic chemotherapy. Participants must have had prior orchiectomy or been receiving ongoing androgen-deprivation therapy with a gonadotropin-releasing hormone analogue. Participants with active central nervous system involvement or ongoing use of immunosuppressive doses of glucocorticoids were not eligible for enrollment. The full list of inclusion and exclusion criteria can be found in the protocol.

Objectives and End Points

The primary objective was to determine the safety and recommended phase II dose (RP2D) and schedule of pasritamig. The end points were the incidence and severity of adverse events (AEs), including dose-limiting toxicities (DLTs). DLTs were defined as febrile neutropenia, grade 4 neutropenia for ≥7 days, grade ≥3 thrombocytopenia with bleeding, grade 4 thrombocytopenia for ≥7 days, any grade ≥3 nonhematological toxicity with the exceptions noted in the protocol, any delay in dosing >14 days because of the study drug, and any death not clearly due to underlying disease or extraneous causes. The DLT evaluation period started with the first step-up dose until 21 days after the full treatment dose. The posterior probability derived from observed DLTs was used to identify the highest dose that maintains an acceptable risk of toxicity. RP2D determination was based on the review of all available safety, efficacy, PK, and PD data to identify the dose with optimal therapeutic risks/benefits. AEs were coded using the Medical Dictionary for Regulatory Activities v27.0 and were graded according to the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for AEs v5.0, except for CRS or immune effector cell–associated neurotoxicity syndrome (ICANS). Treatment-emergent AEs (TEAEs) were defined as any AE with onset date and time on or after that of the first dose through 30 days after the last dose of pasritamig or the day before the start of subsequent therapy, whichever occurred first. Treatment-related AEs (TRAEs) were considered related to pasritamig if assessed as such by study investigators. Secondary objectives included assessments of PK (pasritamig concentrations in serum), PD (systemic cytokine concentrations), and immunogenicity (anti-drug antibodies [ADAs]) against pasritamig. Preliminary antitumor activity was assessed as a secondary objective. End points included radiographic progression-free survival (rPFS), PSA response rate, investigator-assessed overall response rate (ORR), and duration of response (DOR). Disease response was assessed using computed tomography or magnetic resonance imaging and technetium-99m bone scans every 6-8 weeks (±1 week) after the first dose for the first 24 weeks and every 12 weeks (±1 week) thereafter. rPFS was determined from the first pasritamig dose until objective progressive disease as defined by progression of soft tissue lesions according to RECIST v1.1, confirmed radiographic progression of bone lesions according to the Prostate Cancer Working Group 3 (PCWG3), or death, whichever occurred first. PSA response rate was defined as the proportion of participants with a ≥50% decrease from baseline in PSA (PSA50) and was confirmed by a subsequent assessment after 3 weeks or later. Participants who had either discontinued treatment (regardless of the duration) or are currently undergoing treatment and have completed ≥6 weeks of treatment were considered PSA evaluable. ORR was defined as the proportion of participants with measurable disease according to RECIST v1.1 with a partial response or better without evidence of bone progression according to the PCWG3. DOR was determined (partial response or better) from the initial response date to first evidence of progressive disease as defined by RECIST v1.1 or PCWG3, or death, whichever occurred first. If a participant was progression free and alive or had unknown survival status, data were censored at the last tumor assessment.

Statistical Analysis

The all-treated population consists of participants who received ≥1 pasritamig dose. The selected RP2D regimen comprises step-up doses of 3.5 mg on day 1 and 18 mg on day 8, followed by a treatment dose of 300 mg on day 15 and then once every 6 weeks, administered IV. The RP2D safety population includes participants who were administered the 300-mg treatment dose once every 3 weeks (cohort 19) or every 6 weeks (cohorts 20 and 22), as the safety profile of pasritamig was largely driven by the step-up and initial treatment doses and was highly consistent across both dosing frequencies. To report a uniform efficacy profile, the RP2D efficacy population only includes participants dosed once every 6 weeks (cohorts 20 and 22) with the selected RP2D regimen. Cohorts are detailed in Data Supplement (Table S1). PK, PD, and immunogenicity analyses were performed in participants who received ≥1 pasritamig dose and had ≥1 evaluable measurement. ORR is reported with its two-sided 95% CIs. Kaplan-Meier method was used to summarize DOR and rPFS. No formal statistical hypothesis testing was conducted in this study. Data were summarized using descriptive statistics.

RESULTS

Exposure, Demographics, and Baseline Disease Characteristics

As of March 7, 2025, 174 participants were treated with pasritamig (Data Supplement, Fig S2; SC, n = 102; IV, n = 72) across 22 cohorts (Data Supplement, Tables S1 and S2). Cohorts 1-21 were in dose escalation (part 1, n = 154) and cohort 22 (part 2A, n = 20) was in dose expansion (Data Supplement, Table S1). Participants who received SC pasritamig were enrolled much earlier than those who received IV pasritamig; therefore, IV treatment duration is shorter with several participants still undergoing treatment. The median (range) duration of treatment in the all-treated population was 3.20 (0.0-33.4) months (Data Supplement, Table S3).

Participants had a median age of 69.0 years (range, 36-89) and received a median of 4 prior lines of systemic therapy (range, 1-13; Table 1). Black or African American men (4.0%) and Hispanic or Latino men (4.6%) were relatively underrepresented (Data Supplement, Table S4). Baseline disease characteristics were similar among SC-treated and IV-treated participants, although visceral disease was more common in SC-treated participants, and prior exposure to lutetium Lu-177 vipivotide tetraxetan was more common in IV-treated participants (given its increased availability over the enrollment period). Cohort 22 (part 2A) was limited to only bone and/or LN metastases (no visceral metastases).

Safety

TEAEs are listed in Table 2 and Data Supplement (Table S5), and TRAEs are described here. In the all-treated population (N = 174), 144 of 174 (82.8%) participants reported ≥1 TRAE (Table 2). Those occurring most frequently were injection-site reaction (89/174, 51.1%; primarily in SC cohorts), fatigue (47/174, 27.0%), and CRS (43/174, 24.7%; grade 1 [37/174, 21.3%] and grade 2 [6/174, 3.4%]). Serious TRAEs occurred in 12 of 174 (6.9%) participants; CRS (8/174, 4.6%) was the most frequent serious TRAE. Grade ≥3 TRAEs occurred in 17 of 174 (9.8%) participants; lymphopenia (5/174, 2.9%) and fatigue (3/174, 1.7%) were the most frequent grade ≥3 TRAEs. Neutropenia and lymphopenia (both 1/174; 0.6%) were the only grade 4 TRAEs, and there were no grade 5 TRAEs. A single protocol-defined DLT occurred in 1 participant after their SC 50 mg step-up dose who developed grade 3 TRAEs (ALT and AST increased) that recovered to grade 1 in 8 days. ICANS occurred in 2 of 174 (1.1%) participants (grade 1/2 that recovered in <1 week), both of whom received SC administration.

Notably, IV administration (Table 3) was much better tolerated than SC administration with no TRAEs leading to treatment discontinuation, dose reductions, DLTs, ICANS, or death. There were overall fewer TRAEs, serious TRAEs (only grade 1 CRS), grade ≥3 TRAEs, and CRS events with IV administration (Table 2). In the RP2D safety population (n = 45), 4 of 45 (8.9%) participants reported CRS, which were all Grade 1 and did not require tocilizumab. Other TRAEs in the RP2D safety population occurring in the highest proportion of participants were lipase increase (4/45, 8.9%), fatigue (7/45, 15.6%), and infusion-related reactions (11/45, 24.4%), all grade ≤2 (Table 2).

PK, PD, and Immunogenicity

Preliminary PK data were available from 165 participants (SC, n = 102; IV, n = 63). Pasritamig levels showed approximate dose-proportional increases in serum exposures with increasing doses. With SC dosing, the mean serum concentration of pasritamig gradually increased reaching a median maximum observed serum concentration between 72 and 168 hours across dose levels, followed by a slow elimination phase until the end of the dosing interval (Data Supplement, Fig S3A). After IV administration, the median maximum observed serum concentration occurred at the end of infusion (Data Supplement, Fig S3B). Mean terminal half-life after IV administration was approximately 15.2 days. For both SC and IV administration, steady state was achieved after the fifth and third treatment doses for every 3-week and 6-week dosing, respectively.

ADAs were detected in 48.0% (49/102) and 15.4% (6/39) of SC and IV participants, respectively. ADA-positive samples were further characterized for neutralization capability, with 27.5% (28/102 in SC cohorts) and 0% (0/39 in IV cohorts) of participants positive for neutralizing antibodies against pasritamig. The ADAs and neutralizing antibodies seemed to have no impact on PK, safety, or antitumor activity (Data Supplement, Fig S4 and Tables S6 and S7).

Serum interferon gamma and interleukin 6 maximum (highest value observed per participant per cohort) absolute concentrations (Data Supplement, Fig S5A) and maximum fold change from baseline (Data Supplement, Fig S5B) increased in a dose-dependent manner, consistent with peripheral T-cell activation. Interleukin 6 concentrations and fold change from baseline were lower in 300 mg (RP2D) IV versus 800 mg SC treatment dose cohorts (Data Supplement, Fig S5C) and in participants with no CRS (Data Supplement, Fig S5D), consistent with the lower rates of CRS observed with IV versus SC administration.

Antitumor Activity

The median (95% CI) rPFS was 7.85 (2.89 to not estimable [NE]) months in the RP2D efficacy population (n = 33; Table 4; Fig 1). In participants without visceral metastases (n = 118) in the all-treated population, median rPFS was 7.85 (95% CI, 4.40 to 14.32) months compared with 2.66 (95% CI, 1.74 to 3.75) months in participants with visceral metastases (n = 55; Table 4). As of the clinical cutoff date, 15 of 174 (8.6%) participants in the all-treated population and 7 of 33 (21.2%) in the RP2D efficacy population remain on treatment.

PSA responses were noted at doses as low as 25 mg SC once every 3 weeks (Fig 2). In the RP2D efficacy population, 14 of 33 (42.4%) participants evaluable for PSA response achieved a PSA50, 12 (36.4%) of whom had a confirmed PSA50 (Table 4). PSA responses and durable disease control were observed irrespective of prior treatment with taxanes or prostate-specific membrane antigen radioligand therapy (Fig 2C).

In the all-treated population of participants with measurable disease at baseline (n = 84), ORR was 8.3% (7/84; Table 4). ORR varied depending on location of metastatic disease; it was 15.2% (5/33) in participants without visceral metastases and 3.9% (2/51) in participants with any visceral metastases (Table 4; Data Supplement, Fig S6). Median (95% CI) DOR, in seven participants with measurable disease at baseline and 1 participant with a complete response who had nontarget lesions only, was 8.92 (3.58 to NE) months (Table 4).

DISCUSSION

T-cell–engaging antibodies have the potential to improve upon immunotherapy approaches that have proven unsuccessful in PC to date. However, their utilization in solid tumors has largely been limited by their safety profile. Toxicities primarily arise in two categories: on-target/off-tumor T-cell–mediated toxicity and cytokine-mediated toxicity. On-target/off-tumor effects stem from the limited availability of highly specific tumor-associated antigens that are not expressed in normal tissues, and CRS is generally associated with features of individual drug constructs. This is the first time, to our knowledge, that KLK2 has been targeted with a T-cell engager, and the clinical data reported here illustrate that targeting KLK2 with pasritamig overcomes these challenges.

As a highly specific PC antigen with limited normal tissue expression, KLK2 minimizes on-target/off-tumor toxicity while preserving therapeutic benefit; this limited expression may also limit CRS. In the RP2D safety population, pasritamig did not lead to any treatment-related discontinuations, dose holds, or dose modifications. The infrequent and low-grade CRS (fever only) with IV administration of pasritamig allows for safe delivery in a fully outpatient setting with an infrequent dosing schedule (once every 6 weeks). These characteristics compare favorably to available results with other targets for T-cell engagers in PC (ie, prostate-specific membrane antigen, six transmembrane epithelial antigen of the prostate 1), particularly for a PC population who are often older with multiple comorbidities.

In addition to overall low CRS rates that may be related to the pattern of KLK2 expression, IV dosing of pasritamig resulted in lower rates of CRS and fatigue than SC dosing and eliminated injection-site reactions. These injection-site reactions could be quite extensive in higher dose SC cohorts and may have contributed to CRS and constitutional symptoms, possibly through local cytokine production. It is also possible that the IV maximum observed serum concentration better coincided with the effect of the steroid premedication.

Target expression–based participant selection was not incorporated in this study; however, we observed that participants with bone ± LN disease had substantially improved and had more durable antitumor activity compared with participants with visceral involvement. This may be related to the variable expression of KLK2 in different sites of metastasis (bone and LN more than visceral disease) as well as the increased resistance to immunotherapeutic agents in liver and visceral disease. Future development of a KLK2 radiotracer may facilitate an improved understanding of the relationship between KLK2 expression and pasritamig activity.

Taken together, pasritamig demonstrated a favorable safety profile across all dose levels investigated, specifically at the selected RP2D. Pasritamig achieved durable responses and rPFS that compares favorably with historical data in heavily pretreated participants with mCRPC. A limitation of the results reported here is that preliminary antitumor activity should be interpreted with caution given the single-arm, dose-ranging study design and modest follow-up time.

Considering the high expression of KLK2 in mCRPC, convenient dosing schedule, favorable safety, and encouraging antitumor activity, pasritamig warrants further development in larger trials.
