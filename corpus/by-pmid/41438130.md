# Viral warriors: Unlocking the immune system's potential with oncolytic viruses in cancer immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Oncolytic viruses (OVs) represent an exciting avenue in tumor immunotherapy, harnessing the power of viruses to overcome resistance and enhance immune responses. OVs achieve this by selectively targeting and destroying tumor cells. This cell lysis releases tumor-specific antigens (TAAs), effectively creating an in situ vaccine that educates the immune system. Moreover, OVs directly trigger both innate and adaptive immunity and can be genetically engineered to secrete immune-boosting factors. Early studies have shown promising synergistic antitumor effects when OVs are combined with other cancer therapies or immunotherapies. A key advantage of OVs is their capacity to reprogram the tumor microenvironment (TME), transforming immunologically inert "cold" tumors into actively inflamed "hot" tumors. This environmental shift is crucial for successful immunotherapy, as it allows for increased infiltration of immune cells, particularly cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs), into the tumor. This comprehensive review covers the history, mechanisms, and potential combinatorial strategies involving OVs. It also addresses the challenges faced in their development and highlights current clinical trials exploring their role as potent immunotherapeutic agents.

Graphical abstract

Oncolytic viruses are viruses that selectively infect and destroy cancer cells. This process helps the immune system recognize and fight the tumor, acting like an “in situ vaccine.” These viruses can also transform “cold” (immune-ignored) tumors into “hot” (immune-active) ones, making them more receptive to immune cell attack. They show great promise, especially when combined with other cancer treatments.

Introduction

Cancer remains a significant and challenging global health problem. Between 2022 and 2023, nearly 20 million new cancer cases were diagnosed worldwide, and approximately 9.7 million people died of the disease. The economic burden of cancer, including direct medical costs and indirect losses from reduced productivity, amounts to hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Projections suggest that the global cancer incidence will rise to 35 million new cases per year by 2050, driven largely by population growth and aging. These trends underscore the urgent need for enhanced cancer prevention, early detection, and equitable access to treatment across all regions. While conventional treatments like radiation, chemotherapy, and surgery have advanced, there is still a need for new, highly effective therapies. Recent decades have seen the rise of cancer immunotherapy, which aims to harness the body’s immune system to fight cancer. Oncolytic virotherapy, using oncolytic viruses (OVs), is an exciting frontier within this field.

OVs are DNA- or RNA-based viruses engineered to selectively replicate within and lyse tumor cells while sparing healthy cells. This tumor-specific tropism offers a significant advantage over conventional cancer therapies by minimizing systemic toxicity. Although certain viruses like reovirus or parvovirus exhibit a degree of intrinsic oncotropism, many commonly used OVs lack inherent tumor selectivity, contributing to off-target toxicity and limiting clinical efficacy. Advanced genetic engineering strategies have been developed to overcome these limitations to enhance tumor specificity. These include the transcriptional regulation of immune evasion and replication genes via tumor-specific promoters, modification of viral envelope or entry-related proteins, and the incorporation of microRNA target sequences to restrict viral replication to malignant cells. Additionally, pharmacological approaches employing small molecules are being explored to enhance oncotropism. These agents function by suppressing antiviral defenses, upregulating viral receptors, or sensitizing tumor cells to viral replication, thereby augmenting therapeutic precision and efficacy. Many OVs are under active investigation for cancer therapy, including adenoviruses, herpes simplex viruses (HSVs), and measles viruses, among others. These viral agents are being explored for their capacity to selectively replicate within malignant cells and elicit antitumor immune responses, offering a promising avenue for novel therapeutic interventions (see Table 1).

The therapeutic use of viruses against cancer dates to the mid-20th century (see Figure 1), utilizing wild-type viral strains. However, early efforts were constrained by limited efficacy and significant safety concerns, such as uncontrolled viral replication, systemic viremia, encephalitis, and severe inflammatory responses in immunocompromised patients. Off-target infections of healthy tissues, particularly hepatic and nervous systems, further underscored the need for genetically modified OVs. Despite these challenges, early clinical observations, such as the administration of adenovirus for cervical carcinoma in 1956, provided proof-of-concept for viral-based cancer therapy. The development of cloning technology in the 1990s enabled the creation of the first genetically engineered OVs with anticancer activity, prompting a surge in research. Over time, several recombinant OVs have advanced through preclinical and clinical studies for various cancers, leading to regulatory approvals.

Rigvir (ECHO-7), an unmodified picornavirus, was the first OV approved for melanoma in Latvia in 2004. This was followed by Oncorine (H101), an oncolytic adenovirus approved in China in 2005 for head and neck cancers. A significant breakthrough occurred in 2015 with the approval of Talimogene Laherparepvec (T-VEC), an oncolytic herpesvirus, by both the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) and European Medicines Agency (EMA) for advanced melanoma, significantly boosting research interest in OVs. T-VEC’s approval was based on the OPTiM phase 3 trial, which demonstrated an objective response rate of 26% vs. 6% for granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) alone in advanced melanoma patients, with a median overall survival of 23.3 months compared with 18.9 months. More recently, approvals include Delytact (Teserpaturev) in Japan for gliomas and Nadofaragene firadenovec (Adstiladrin) in the United States for bladder cancer. A significant milestone was reached on January 21, 2025, when RP1 (vusolimogene oderparepvec), in combination with nivolumab, received a Biologics License Application (BLA) acceptance from the FDA for the treatment of advanced melanoma. Currently, T-VEC is the only FDA-approved OV in the United States, but many others are showing promising results in late-stage clinical trials. After years of development, OVs are now recognized as a distinct class of cancer therapeutic agents.

OVs offer several key advantages. Their tumor-specific targeting reduces harm to healthy tissues. Beyond direct destruction (oncolysis), OVs trigger immunogenic cell death (ICD). This process releases tumor-specific antigens (TAAs), effectively acting as an in situ cancer vaccine that primes the immune system against the tumor. By using the tumor cells as the antigen source, OVs stimulate innate and adaptive immune responses within the TME, ultimately activating CTLs to target and eliminate cancer cells. Genetic modifications enable OVs to express immunostimulatory molecules, such as cytokines and chemokines, within infected tumor cells. This localized expression fosters a proinflammatory TME, enhances antigen presentation, and amplifies tumor-specific T cell responses.

OVs have shown promising synergistic effects when combined with conventional cancer therapies and immunotherapies. Preclinical studies and clinical trials have demonstrated that combining OVs with chemotherapy, radiation therapy, or other immunotherapies enhances tumor cell killing and prolongs survival in both murine models and patients. These combinatory approaches offer a powerful, multifaceted strategy to overcome the limitations of individual treatments. These mechanisms are crucial for transforming immunologically “cold” tumors, which lack significant immune cell infiltration, into inflamed, “hot” tumors that are receptive to immunotherapy. This conversion enhances immune cell infiltration and activation, a crucial step toward effective cancer treatment.

In this review, we first outline mechanisms of OV tropism and immune activation, then summarize arming strategies and delivery barriers, and finally synthesize clinical experience highlighting where combinations with immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), chemotherapy, and targeted agents have improved outcomes. This structure provides a mechanistic foundation for ongoing and future trials.

Mechanisms of action of OVs

OVs selectively target and destroy tumor cells while sparing healthy cells. Here, we first discuss the mechanisms underlying OV tropism toward cancer cells and their therapeutic effects.

OV tropism and selectivity

Understanding oncotropism mechanisms is crucial for optimizing the development of OVs as effective cancer therapies. Viral tropism depends on specific interactions between viral proteins and cellular receptors. OVs such as echovirus, poliovirus, and measles virus utilize distinct cell surface receptors like Integrin α2β1, CD155, and CD46, respectively, to selectively target and infect specific cancer cell types. Adenoviruses typically utilize the Coxsackie-adenovirus receptor (CAR) as their native entry receptor. It is essential to note that a single OV can target different receptors, depending on the type of cancer. For instance, the measles virus efficiently targets multiple myeloma cells by binding to CD46, a protein that is overexpressed on these cells. Additionally, Nectin-4, a primary and high-affinity receptor for the measles virus, is predominantly expressed on tumor cells, including those found in breast, colorectal, pancreatic, and colon cancers. Other receptor molecules implicated in tumor growth, such as intracellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-1) and the laminin receptor, also serve as receptors for coxsackievirus A21 and Seneca Valley virus-001 (SVV), respectively.

If a tumor expresses little or none of the virus’s natural receptor, it is considered to have a receptor deficiency for that OV. Deficiencies in native viral receptors on cancer cells can be overcome by capsid/envelope engineering and, more recently, by genome editing. For adenoviruses, tropism can be redirected by genetically displaying single-chain antibodies or affibody molecules on the fiber (or using fiber-fibritin chimeras), thereby enabling entry via alternative, tumor-associated receptors such as HER2 or epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR), which are frequently overexpressed in breast and lung cancers. Studies have shown that HER2-affibody-bearing Ad5 vectors achieve receptor-specific transduction and improved on-target delivery in vitro and in vivo. For herpes simplex virus (HSV)-1, inserting tumor-specific scFvs into entry glycoproteins (e.g., gD or gH) produces fully retargeted oHSV that uses only the chosen tumor antigen (e.g., HER2, EGFRvIII, PSMA, EpCAM) for entry and spread, with multiple studies demonstrating stringent receptor dependence and enhanced selectivity in preclinical systems. While classic retargeting relied on homologous recombination, CRISPR-Cas9 can facilitate precise HSV genome edits and payload insertions, streamlining construction of armed/retargeted backbones (e.g., insertion of cytokine cassettes), and is increasingly used to accelerate oHSV engineering. These modifications not only enhance tropism but also reduce off-target effects.

Beyond these surface receptor interactions, which primarily govern initial OV entry, intracellular signaling pathways provide an additional layer of selectivity, exploiting cancer-specific defects to favor OV replication in malignant cells while sparing healthy ones. While OVs can infect normal cells, their replication and spread are typically restricted by the production of interferons (IFNs). However, cancer cells frequently have impaired antiviral response pathways regulated by cytokines like tumor necrosis factor (TNF) or type I and II IFNs. These compromised pathways create a favorable environment for OV replication within tumors. Furthermore, cancer-specific mutations in genes such as TP53, RAS, PTEN, RB1, WNT, EGFR, and BCL-2 can increase tumor cell susceptibility to viral infection and enhance OV replication. For example, reoviruses can infect tumor cells harboring Ras mutations, facilitating viral entry. The Ras mutation not only boosts OV replication, but also triggers apoptosis and inhibits IFN production following reovirus infection, allowing the virus to spread due to a defective antiviral response.

The altered metabolism of cancer cells also influences OV tropism. Tumor cells often exhibit an elevated rate of aerobic glycolysis, known as the Warburg effect. This metabolic shift contributes to the development of an immunosuppressive TME and affects immune cell function. By exploiting the heightened glycolytic activity of cancer cells, OVs can leverage increased metabolism to enhance their own replication, for example, inhibiting pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH), a key enzyme that regulates glucose metabolism, by shunting pyruvate away from glycolysis, enhanced replication and promoted oncolysis for certain OVs, including vaccinia viruses, reoviruses, influenza A (H1N1), and hepatitis C virus.

Several other tumor-specific defects further support OV persistence. Epigenetic silencing or mutations of PRRs, such as cGAS or STING, impairs type I IFN signaling and innate immune activation in cancers including ovarian and pancreatic malignancies. Dysregulated apoptotic and stress response pathways, typified by overexpression of anti-apoptotic Bcl-2 family proteins and impaired unfolded protein response (UPR), further hinder targeted clearance of OV-infected cells. Moreover, defective autophagy and intracellular trafficking, common in tumor cells due to disruptions in autophagy machinery and vesicular acidification, diminish lysosomal degradation of viral particles and promote viral survival. Collectively, such defects create a permissive milieu for OV persistence and selective replication in malignant cells.

Direct tumor lysis

Normal host cells can sense viral components and eliminate OVs through antiviral signaling pathways. However, in cancer cells, defects in antiviral mechanisms facilitate viral persistence and proliferation. Viral replication is a complex process allowing viruses to multiply within a host cell. Upon attachment to and entry into host cancer cells, OVs release their genetic material (either DNA or RNA). This viral genome hijacks the cancer cell’s machinery, directing it to replicate and transcribe the viral genetic code, leading to the synthesis of viral proteins. Subsequently, newly produced viral components assemble into complete viral particles. As viral proliferation within tumor cells progresses, it results in the bursting and lysis of the cells, releasing mature OVs from infected cancer cells. This process ultimately contributes to the accumulation of OVs within the TME.

Induction of antitumor immunity

Activation of innate immunity

Activation of innate immunity sets the stage for adaptive responses, where released antigens and cytokines orchestrate long-term tumor-specific immunity, as evidenced by studies showing systemic protection against distant metastases. OV-mediated oncolysis triggers the release of TAAs, pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), and OV-derived damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) from the lysed cancer cells into the TME, exposing them to the host immune system. This type of cell death, known as immunogenic cell death (ICD), can be induced through diverse mechanisms such as immunogenic apoptosis, necroptosis, pyroptosis, or autophagy, often driven by endoplasmic reticulum stress and reactive oxygen species production. Key markers of ICD include the secretion of DAMPs like extracellular ATP and high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1) proteins, along with the translocation of cytoplasmic proteins such as Heat shock protein 70 (HSP70), HSP90, and calreticulin (CRT) to the cell surface. These DAMPs bind to their respective receptors P2RX7 (for ATP), CD91 (for CRT), and TLR4 (for HMGB1) on dendritic cells (DCs). This interaction promotes DC maturation, accelerates tumor antigen uptake by DCs, and leads to subsequent T cell activation, strengthening antitumor immune responses. Furthermore, the detection of PAMPs and DAMPs by immune cells’ pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), such as Toll-like receptors (e.g., TLR3) and components of the STING pathway (which detects viral nucleic acids), triggers the release of proinflammatory cytokines (e.g., type I IFNs, interleukin [IL]-1β, IL-6, IL-12, GM-CSF, TNF-α) and chemokines (e.g., CCL2, CCL3, CCL5, CXCL10) within the TME. Consequently, OVs are capable of converting immunologically “cold” tumors into inflamed “hot” tumors through ICD and the establishment of a proinflammatory TME. Cold tumors lack significant infiltration of effector cells like CD8+ T cells, natural killer (NK) cells, and M1 macrophages, and dendritic cell presence is often dominated by T regulatory cells (Tregs) and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs), whereas hot tumors feature robust infiltration and proinflammatory cytokine profiles. OVs can induce immunogenic cell death and convert cold into hot tumors by facilitating dendritic cell activation and CD8+ T cell recruitment. Recent studies indicate that OVs like coxsackievirus B3, reovirus, adenovirus, parvovirus, HSV, Newcastle disease virus (NDV), and VACV can elicit diverse levels of ICD.

Activation of adaptive immunity

Several studies have explored how OVs trigger a robust adaptive immune response. In 1999, Toda et al. demonstrated that infecting CT26 tumors in a mouse model with the HSV-1 OV (G207) led to the development of specific, systemic antitumor immunity, suggesting G207’s potential as an in situ vaccine. In another study, Diaz et al. identified that CD8+ T cells critically influence the therapeutic efficacy of intratumorally administered vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV). These specific T cells targeted immunodominant epitopes derived from both viral and tumor-related proteins. Later, Prestwich and colleagues confirmed that OV infection triggers adaptive antitumor immunity.

During OV infection, the primary component of the adaptive immune response against cancer is the T cell response, which is tailored specifically to recognize and eliminate tumors. This immune response is crucial for achieving long-lasting cancer management following oncolytic virotherapy, as it enables effective suppression of tumor cells not only at the local injection site but also at distant locations. Most tumor cells often exhibit limited or low expression of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules, resulting in inadequate presentation of tumor antigens to antigen-presenting cells (APCs). As a result, CTLs may not be activated, and the immune system fails to target cancer cells effectively. APCs, such as DCs, take up antigens, activate, and migrate to secondary lymphoid organs (like lymph nodes and the spleen) where they interact with and stimulate T cells that recognize tumor antigens, resulting in an immune response against cancer cells. Three key immunological interactions are required to activate T cells: (1) recognition of the antigen-MHC complex by the T cell receptor (TCR), (2) costimulatory signals delivered by the APC, and (3) cytokine stimulation, often provided by the proinflammatory environment created by OV infection. OV-induced tumor lysis releases TAAs and neoantigens, which APCs process into peptide epitopes for presentation on surface MHC molecules. Within the cytokine milieu generated after tumor and immune cell exposure to OVs, type I IFNs enhance the expression of MHC class I and II molecules, as well as costimulatory molecules like CD40, CD80, and CD86 on the surface of DCs. Notably, the production of various chemokines and cytokines by OV-infected cells or mature APCs contributes significantly to the recruitment and reactivation of T cells. Once activated, antitumor CD8+ T cells can eradicate both newly grafted and distant tumors in an OV-independent fashion, causing tumor regression.

A critical aspect of the adaptive immune response is the development of immunological memory. OVs contribute to this process by promoting the differentiation of a subset of activated T cells into memory T cells. These memory cells provide long-term immune surveillance and enable the immune system to respond rapidly to any future encounter with the same tumor antigen. For instance, in preclinical studies, an IL-2 encoding NDV was shown to effectively generate tumor-specific cytotoxic T cells and immune memory T cells in melanoma and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) models, providing long-lasting protection upon subsequent tumor exposure. In another study, Minjun Yu et al. demonstrated lymphoma regression and induced long-lasting antitumor immune memory in mice through combined administration of Sindbis virus vectors and α4-1BB monoclonal antibodies.

Utilization of OVs as a Trojan horse through transgene expression

In 1991, Martuza and colleagues made a groundbreaking advancement by genetically engineering OVs using thymidine kinase (TK) gene-deleted HSVs. Rapid progress in biotechnology has since enabled genetic engineering to enhance specific OV characteristics. According to recent reports, two-thirds of viruses currently undergoing clinical trials have been genetically modified, representing the fourth generation of genetically modified OVs available for tumor immunotherapy. First-generation OVs involved simple gene deletions for safety (e.g., TK-deficient or ICP34.5−/γ34.5−, ICP6− HSV); second-generation OVs added tumor-specific promoters; third-generation OVs incorporated immunostimulatory transgenes like cytokines; and fourth-generation OVs combine multiple arming strategies with advanced targeting.

The concept of using OVs as “Trojan horses” involves the targeted delivery of therapeutic transgenes specifically to tumor cells via recombinant viruses. This strategy allows for localized therapeutic interventions, including the delivery of immune-modulating agents, tumor suppressor proteins, apoptosis-inducing factors, and angiogenesis inhibitors, without affecting normal tissues. These “armed” OVs can modulate the TME, stimulate antitumor immune responses, and induce tumor cell death. Below, we classify transgenes used in oncolytic virotherapy by various research groups, including our own.

OVs armed with cytokine and chemokine genes

Cytokines are soluble signaling proteins that facilitate intercellular communication and maintain immune system homeostasis. Engineering OVs to express cytokine or chemokine genes enhances local immune responses within the TME, promoting antitumor immunity and improving OV therapeutic efficacy. Arming OVs with cytokines and chemokines represents a foundational strategy, as these molecules enhance local immune responses, which naturally leads to anti-angiogenesis approaches, which target the tumor’s vascular support to complement immune activation. Multiple cytokine genes, including interleukins (IL-2, IL-7, IL-12, IL-15, IL-18, IL-21, IL-24), colony-stimulating factors (G-CSF, GM-CSF), tumor necrosis factor (TNF-α), interferons (IFN-α, IFN-β, IFN-γ), and chemokines (CXCL11, CCL21), have been used in human clinical trials and preclinical animal studies to generate recombinant OVs. GM-CSF is currently the most widely utilized among cytokines used in human trials. ONCOS-102 (Ad5/3-D24-GMCSF), an oncolytic adenovirus expressing GM-CSF, shows clinical efficacy, demonstrating increased peripheral T cell levels against TAAs. Its immunological robustness is further shown by enhanced CD8+ T cell infiltration in tumor specimens following administration to patients with advanced disease.

OH2, a modified HSV-2 strain (HG52) with deletions in the ICP34.5 and ICP47 genes, is armed with human GM-CSF to boost antitumor immunity. A single administration of OH2 resulted in a noticeable change in the TME, characterized by increased CD3+ and CD8+ T cell presence. In August 2021, the OH2 OV was approved by the FDA for use in US clinical trials enrolling individuals with various solid tumors. More recently, our research group developed VC2-GMCSF, a recombinant HSV-1 armed with GM-CSF. VC2 was engineered to prevent fusion of the viral envelope with cellular plasma membranes, thereby restricting cell and neuronal axon entry. This was achieved by deleting specific domains in glycoprotein K (gK) and the UL20 protein that interact with glycoprotein B (gB). We assessed the antitumor effects of VC2-GMCSF on tumor growth, metastasis, and immune response in melanoma and end-stage breast cancer animal models. Our findings in 4T-1 stage IV breast cancer syngeneic mice model showed that intratumoral VC2-GMCSF did not significantly decrease primary tumor size, but significantly reduced lung metastasis compared with the control group. This reduction in metastasis was accompanied by increased T cell infiltration into the TME and decreased transcription of tumor-promoting genes like PD-L1 and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) in metastatic nodules. These results suggest VC2-GMCSF has potential as an oncolytic and immunotherapeutic approach for late-stage breast cancer. We have also demonstrated in various preclinical studies (guinea pig, mouse, non-human primate) that VC2-GMCSF is a safe and immunogenic vaccine strain and provides protection against lethal HSV genital and ocular infection in mice and guinea pigs.

In another study, we engineered an OHSV, VC2-OVA, armed with a fragment of ovalbumin (OVA) fused to the VP26 virion capsid protein. We evaluated VC2-OVA as a vector to stimulate potent and targeted antitumor immunity in a murine melanoma model. Therapeutic vaccination with VC2-OVA resulted in a notable decrease in tumor burden in the lungs of mice intravenously inoculated with B16cOVA cells, indicating its potential to combat tumor growth, and spread. Furthermore, VC2-OVA induced a robust preventive antitumor response and increased survival in mice with intradermally implanted B16cOVA tumors compared with control virus immunization.

Regarding other cytokines as transgenes, several studies have reported positive results. Liu et al. showed that an oncolytic vaccinia virus (OVV) armed with CXCL11 (vvDD-CXCL11) significantly increased CXCL11 protein levels within tumors and recruited CD8+ T cells and NK cells to the TME, leading to a systemic antitumor immune response. Moon et al. also observed that vvDD-CXCL11 effectively recruited T cells and significantly improved antitumor efficacy. An engineered VSV encoding IFNγ showed greater DC activation and higher secretion of proinflammatory cytokines than the parental virus, resulting in pronounced antitumor effects in various murine tumor models. Similarly, an oncolytic adenovirus (OAd) armed with IFNγ (CNHK300-hIFN-γ) exhibited substantial antitumor effects, including targeted cancer cell destruction, antiangiogenic effects, and immune response activation. In a similar study, an OAd armed with IFNα (RGD-ΔE3-ADP-ham-IFN) demonstrated a promising therapeutic approach for treating pancreatic cancer in a syngeneic Syrian hamster model.

A recent study developed VVL-GL21, a tumor-selective vaccinia virus (VVLΔTKΔA49, VVL-DD) engineered via CRISPR-Cas9 to express GM-CSF and IL-21. VVL-GL21 induced significant tumor regression via intratumoral and systemic delivery. It enhanced immune cell infiltration, promoted DC maturation, shifted macrophages to an M1 phenotype, strengthened immune memory, prevented tumor recurrence, and improved antitumor immunity, including responsiveness to anti-PD1 therapy. A final example is OV-IL15C, a modified HSV containing the IL-15 complex, which enhances NK and T cell survival and activation. When treating orthotopic high-grade serous carcinoma (hGSC)-derived tumors, administering human CD8+ T cells with OV-IL15C yielded significantly better results than using the parental virus. Moreover, in one other study, researchers engineered a novel oncolytic virus (OV-5A) armed with five immunostimulatory genes CCL5, CXCL16, IFN-γ, IL-12, and GM-CSF based on single-cell RNA sequencing data to enhance antitumor immunity through coordinated innate and adaptive immune activation.

Besides T-Vec, the first OV armed with GM-CSF, there are several OVs that incorporate GM-CSF as a transgene, such as Pexa-Vec, ONCOS-102, OH2, CG0070, and RP2/RP3, which are currently in different phases of clinical trials. These OVs are under investigation in various phases of clinical trials, highlighting the growing role of GM-CSF in oncolytic virotherapy. (See clinical trials section for comprehensive trial details.) A representative clinical example of a cytokine-armed OVs is Pexa-Vec (JX-594), a thymidine-kinase-inactivated vaccinia engineered to express GM-CSF, developed for intratumoral (i.t.) and intravenous (i.v.) use. In a first-in-human i.t. phase 1 study in refractory primary/metastatic liver tumors, doses up to 3 × 109 PFU were explored; 1 × 109 PFU was identified as the MTD, and objective responses were observed in injected and non-injected lesions, supporting both oncolysis and immunogenic spread. Intravenous delivery was later shown to selectively infect and express transgenes in tumors in a dose-dependent fashion in patients (single i.v. infusion up to ∼2 × 109 PFU), establishing the feasibility of systemic administration. Another example is ONCOS-102, armed with GM-CSF, which has shown TME activation and acceptable safety in early studies. In a randomized phase 1/2 trial in malignant pleural mesothelioma (MPM) (IT ONCOS-102 plus pemetrexed/platinum vs. chemotherapy alone, n = 31), the combination was well tolerated; although efficacy endpoints were not statistically different in the overall cohort, numerically longer overall survival (OS) was observed in chemo-naïve patients (median 20.3 vs. 13.5 months; 30-month OS 34.1% vs. 0%). Paired biopsies at day 36 showed increased i.t. T cell infiltration and immune-gene upregulation, and the magnitude of immune activation was associated with survival at 18 months.

OVs armed with anti-angiogenesis genes

Angiogenesis is the process by which new blood vessels form from preexisting ones. Arming OVs with anti-angiogenesis genes can inhibit key angiogenic pathways (e.g., angiopoietin, platelet-derived growth factor [PDGF], VEGF), resulting in reduced tumor vascularization and impaired tumor growth. Several anti-angiogenic genes have been incorporated into OVs, including vasculostatin, endostatin, and angiostatin. Lacking intrinsic tumoricidal activity, they require sustained delivery to the TME. Additionally, their short serum half-life, low solubility, and limited stability hinder their effectiveness when used through conventional delivery methods. In a study by Zhang et al., an oncolytic herpes simplex virus (oHSV) was modified by inserting an endostatin-angiostatin fusion gene into a specific strain (γ34.5Δ, ICP6- oHSV). This modified virus, termed VAE, enhanced efficacy in prolonging survival and reducing microvessel density in a brain tumor model derived from glioblastoma stem cells (GSCs), compared with the original HSV strain or recombinant endostatin. Similarly, a 2019 study by Tomita et al. demonstrated that an oHSV named RAMBO, lacking the γ34.5 and ICP6 genes and engineered to express vasculostatin, led to a slight improvement in the median survival of mice with intracerebral tumors.

OVs armed with suicide genes and pro-apoptotic transgenes

Gene-directed prodrug activation therapy, or suicide gene therapy, is a therapeutic approach involving the delivery of viral or bacterial genes into cancer cells. This enables the conversion of non-toxic prodrugs into cytotoxic drugs or the production of toxic gene products, specifically targeting and eliminating tumor cells without harming normal cells. The expressed transgene referred to as a suicide or drug-sensitivity gene. For example, cytosine deaminase (CD) and thymidine kinase (TK) disrupt pathways involved in DNA replication. This disruption enhances cancer cell elimination when combined with cytotoxic chemotherapy or radiotherapy. Due to their effective tumor cell oncolytic and immune-stimulating abilities, adenoviruses have become a frequent choice for suicide gene therapy. Ad5-CD/TKrep is an example of an OAd armed with a combined enzyme-prodrug system, including HSV1-tk/ganciclovir and yeast CD/5-FC, which results in enhanced cancer cell killing and increased susceptibility of cancer cells to radiation in vitro. The CD transgene enables the conversion of 5-fluorocytosine (5-FC) into a toxic metabolite, while the TKrep transgene converts ganciclovir into a toxic metabolite. These metabolites then kill the cancer cells.

Arming OVs with pro-apoptotic transgenes, such as tumor necrosis factor-related apoptosis-inducing ligand (TRAIL), can enhance their therapeutic effect by promoting apoptotic death of cancer cells and thereby improve the overall outcome of oncolytic virotherapy. TRAIL, a member of the TNF superfamily, is capable of triggering apoptosis in various types of tumor cells because its death receptors are highly expressed on the surface of cancerous cells, while often expressed at low levels or absent on healthy cells. The tumor growth inhibitory effects of TRAIL-armed OVs have been reported previously. For example, in recent work by Jinjiao et al., NDV/Anh-TRAIL, an NDV engineered to express a soluble TRAIL protein, exhibited stronger antitumor effects when compared with the wild-type OV and a control group of mice with subcutaneously transplanted gliomas. Liu et al. investigated an adenovirus named AdsTRAIL expressing an untagged soluble TRAIL protein. After injecting the virus directly into orthotopic xenograft models of glioblastoma, they observed a significant decrease in tumor growth and an increase in OS compared with the control group.

Challenges encountered in utilizing OVs for cancer immunotherapy

OVs show promising potential for cancer immunotherapy due to their ability to selectively replicate within and lyse tumor cells. However, numerous challenges hinder their widespread clinical adoption. A key hurdle is the limited therapeutic response often observed with current delivery techniques, which struggle to effectively distribute OVs within the TME. Systemic administration frequently results in poor bioavailability, rapid immune clearance, and limitations imposed by the physical barriers of the TME. Managing the immune response also poses a challenge. While ICIs used alongside OVs can help manage certain toxicities, they can also trigger severe immune-related adverse events.

Translating preclinical successes to human applications is difficult as well. Animal models often fail to fully replicate the complexity of human cancers, reducing the predictive value of preclinical data.

Conventional murine systems incompletely mimic human tumor/immune ecology and therapeutic pharmacology. Syngeneic tumors in immunocompetent mice capture intact murine immunity but differ from human cancers in mutational spectra, stromal/vascular architecture, microbiome, and cytokine networks, yielding divergent responses to immunotherapies and oncolytic platforms; many agents also engage human-specific targets that are not cross-reactive in mice. Xenograft models often require profound immunodeficiency, which ablates the very immune mechanisms under study, while species restrictions in viral entry/replication, and antibody effector functions further complicate translation. Collectively, these factors limit the prediction of efficacy, toxicity, and optimal dose/schedule for human trials. Strategies such as patient-derived organoids, humanized mouse systems, ex vivo tumor explants, and microfluidic/organ-on-chip systems are helping bridge this gap. Achieving targeted delivery remains elusive; OVs must evade immune destruction while maintaining sufficient specificity for cancer cells. Moreover, the host’s antiviral immune responses can neutralize the therapeutic effects. Balancing local immunosuppression within the tumor to enhance OV distribution with the need to stimulate systemic antitumor immunity is a delicate task. Increased immunosuppression can enhance intratumoral distribution but may limit the immune-mediated oncolysis required for a robust antitumor effect.

Despite these obstacles, the prospects for improving OV therapies are substantial. Advances in genetic engineering and delivery platforms continue to enhance the specificity and efficacy of OVs. Furthermore, a deeper understanding of the interaction between OVs and the various cellular components of the TME is crucial for optimizing therapeutic outcomes. Overall, while OVs represent a revolutionary approach to cancer treatment, addressing the myriad challenges in their development and implementation is crucial for realizing their full potential in cancer immunotherapy. This requires enhanced delivery methods, improved immune response management, and a deeper understanding of tumor biology.

Choosing the most suitable OV species

OVs have evolved significantly from naturally occurring strains to genetically engineered versions, utilizing a variety of viruses. These developments have expanded the range to include both single- and double-stranded RNA and DNA viruses, enhancing their clinical flexibility. Selection of the appropriate OV species requires consideration of multiple biological and clinical factors. It is essential to understand the virus’s intrinsic lytic and immunomodulatory properties, taking into account the target site, required duration of treatment, and cost-effectiveness. OVs are classified by nucleic acid type, with DNA viruses offering larger and more stable genomes than RNA viruses that facilitate genetic engineering and transgene incorporation. RNA viruses, such as reovirus and measles virus, typically replicate more robustly in the cytoplasm and are often more immunogenic. The selection process also involves considering potential combination therapies, evaluating factors like location of action, treatment duration, and overall cost.

The type of virus, whether enveloped or non-enveloped, also plays a crucial role; for instance, enveloped viruses can be less efficient in their oncolytic properties and are more likely to be cleared by the host’s immune system. Coxsackievirus, particularly types A21 and B3, illustrates the importance of selecting viruses with natural tropisms for specific cancer types. Coxsackievirus A21 utilizes ICAM-1 and decay-accelerating factor (DAF) as receptors to target cancer cells, making it potentially effective against solid tumors like melanoma, colon, and breast cancer. Conversely, Coxsackievirus type B3 has shown a natural affinity for non-small cell lung cancer cells.

In summary, selecting an OV for cancer treatment requires a deep understanding of both the virus’s characteristics and the tumor’s biology. This careful consideration ensures that the chosen OV can effectively target and destroy cancer cells while minimizing potential risks for the patient.

Enhance OVs’ selective activity and efficacy

Oncolytic virotherapy leverages genetically engineered viruses to selectively target and kill cancer cells while enhancing antitumor immune responses. One strategy involves equipping OVs with transgenes encoding Th1 cytokines to stimulate T cell migration, proliferation, and targeting to the TME, thereby significantly boosting the immune response against cancer. Furthermore, incorporating immune-stimulatory molecules like GM-CSF into OV genomes aims to alter the immunosuppressive TME by recruiting APCs (especially DCs) and inducing tumor antigen-specific T cells and NK cells. This strategy has been shown to increase the numbers of NK cells, CD4+, and CD8+ T cells after administration of modified OVs. Various arming strategies are also employed to augment the effects of immune checkpoint blockers (ICBs), including molecules that influence early immune responses. These strategies aim to promote more effective immune-mediated eradication of tumor cells.

To improve target fidelity and safety, OVs are designed as “qualified soldiers,” sometimes equipped with “cold weapons” for a direct cytocidal effect or “hot weapons” capable of activating cancer immunotherapy. Enhancing an OV’s tropism and minimizing adverse effects has led to the use of various technologies, from genetics to chemistry. Retargeting approaches include capsid modifications, genome engineering, and chemical modifications. Capsid modifications enhance the binding between the virus and target cell entry receptors, facilitating viral internalization. For adenoviruses, insertion of peptide motifs such as RGD into the fiber knob enables retargeting to integrins that are often overexpressed on cancer cells, enhancing entry specificity (e.g., in esophageal adenocarcinoma) without compromising capsid integrity. Capsid modifications through altered envelope glycoproteins, or surface coating can reduce off-target tropism and immune clearance, thereby improving tumor specificity and delivery efficiency. Genome engineering strategies, such as deleting viral genes essential for replication in normal cells or adding tumor-specific promoters, ensure tumor selectivity. Deletion of neurovirulence genes such as ICP34.5 in HSV-1 impairs replication in normal cells while retaining efficacy in tumor cells, as seen in clinically approved T-VEC. Similarly, vaccinia-based OVs like Pexa-Vec incorporate deletions of the thymidine kinase gene, restricting replication to proliferating tumor cells expressing high levels of endogenous TK and simultaneously express GM-CSF to enhance antitumor immunity. Chemical modification strategies, such as using polyethylene glycol (PEG) and HPMA (N-[2-hydroxypropyl]methacrylamide) stealth, have been applied most extensively to Ad5 to shield capsids from blood factors and neutralizing antibodies, reduce hepatic sequestration, and extend circulation, thereby improving the chances of tumor delivery during systemic administration. These effects are well-documented in preclinical models and reviews of polymer-modified adenoviral vector. In mice, PEGylated adenovirus shows markedly prolonged blood half-life and increased tumor accumulation; high-degree PEGylation produced up to ∼35-fold higher intratumoral gene expression with reduced liver transduction, and a PEG-Ad/TNF-α construct achieved stronger tumor suppression with less hepatotoxicity than unmodified Ad. HPMA-coated adenovirus enables ligand-mediated, CAR-independent retargeting and evasion of neutralizing antibodies, supporting the concept of chemical shielding plus active targeting for systemic delivery. For ovarian cancer, polymer-based stealthing/encapsulation has improved delivery in vivo: encapsulating Ad vectors in PEG-PE liposomes enhanced transfection and suppressed tumor growth in intraperitoneal OC models; more broadly, intraperitoneal oncolytic Ad (OBP-301) synergized with cisplatin and extended survival in a peritoneal dissemination xenograft, illustrating the translational rationale for polymer-assisted/locoregional virus delivery in metastatic peritoneal disease.

As mentioned previously, host cell intrinsic antiviral programs limit OV replication and spread through IFN-driven effectors and dedicated restriction factors that act as a barrier to OV efficacy. RIG-I/MDA5-IRF3 and cGAS-STING canonical pathways induce ISGs (interferon-stimulated genes) that block entry, translation, genome replication, or egress; among them, PKR, OAS/RNase-L, IFITM proteins, SAMHD1, BST2/tetherin (virion release), and the exonuclease TREX1 (which degrades cytosolic DNA) are recurrent barriers with context-specific activity in tumors. In cancer, these pathways are often dysregulated, some tumors silence cGAS-STING, whereas others retain robust IFN responsiveness yielding heterogeneous OV susceptibility and therapeutic outcomes.

There are some therapeutic strategies to modulate restriction and boost OV activity. First, transient pharmacologic dampening of IFN/JAK-STAT signaling can increase intratumoral OV replication: JAK inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib) enhance efficacy of vesicular stomatitis virus (VSV) and other OVs in preclinical models and lower ISG induction in infected tumors. Second, vector engineering to counter specific restriction nodes for example, an HSV-1 armed to inhibit PKR while expressing IFN-β (to preserve antitumor immunity) improved antitumor activity, illustrating that OVs can be tuned to reconcile oncolysis with desirable immune signaling. Additional epigenetic tactics (e.g., HDAC inhibitor cotreatment) can relax antiviral chromatin states and enhance OV spread, and targeting DNA metabolism or nucleases is being explored to augment cytosolic DNA sensing and therapeutic immunogenicity. Together, these strategies exemplify how precision control of host restriction via drugs, payloads, or capsid/replication design can convert intrinsic antiviral barriers into levers that raise OV efficacy and therapeutic index.

Continuous progress in molecular biology and genetic engineering has facilitated the modification of viral genomes to carry diverse foreign genes. These modifications improve immune responses and enable viruses to specifically target and proliferate within cancer cells. A prime example is an engineered OHSV-1 that not only attacks cancer cells but also suppresses tumor metastasis and reduces the expression of genes promoting tumor growth. Overall, improving OVs’ selective activity can be achieved by engineering them to express immune-stimulating cytokines that enhance antitumor immunity or by deleting viral genes that reduce off-target effects. Tailoring these modifications to the tumor type and immune environment increases therapeutic precision, safety, and efficacy.

Overcoming immunosuppressive TME

One promising strategy in the fight against cancer is using OVs to modify the immunosuppressive TME. These viruses show potential in both potentiating existing immune responses and triggering novel antitumor responses, particularly by overcoming the barriers presented by the TME that typically diminish the effectiveness of therapeutic agents. OVs like VSV exploit the fact that many cancer cells lack robust antiviral responses, specifically defects in IFN signaling pathways. This specificity allows VSV to replicate efficiently within tumors, inducing a range of immunomodulatory effects that can sensitize the TME to immune attacks.

To enhance OV therapeutic efficacy, researchers are arming these viruses with additional genetic material. Certain OVs are engineered to express cytokines such as IL-2 or IL-12, which modulate the cancer immune set point and enhance systemic antitumor immunity. This strategy has shown effective in curing late-stage tumors in preclinical models when combined with ICIs like anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies. Integrating transgenes for TAAs, T cell costimulatory molecules, and ICIs directly into the OV genome represents another innovative approach. These modifications aim to enhance viral replication and persistence within the TME, extending oncolytic activity. Furthermore, inserting genes encoding immune-stimulatory molecules like GM-CSF into OV genomes facilitates the maturation and recruitment of APCs (especially DCs), crucial for inducing tumor-specific immune responses.

In clinical applications, combination therapies involving OVs, and conventional or novel treatment modalities have demonstrated synergistic effects, improving outcomes across various disease stages. For example, adding OVs to chemotherapy regimens not only enhances therapeutic efficacy but also helps overcome chemoresistance by targeting underlying mechanisms genetically. Mechanisms conferring chemoresistance such as enhanced DNA repair, overexpressed efflux pumps (e.g., ABC transporters), and anti-apoptotic signaling are often overcome by OVs through immunogenic cell death and modulation of apoptotic pathways, enhancing therapy efficacy. As OVs infect tumor cells and induce cell lysis, they release TAAs and other immunostimulatory molecules like type I IFNs, IFNγ, TNF-α, and IL-12. This release increases tumor-specific immune responses, leading to the elimination of both local and distant tumor cells. Furthermore, the infection process results in the dissemination of viral PAMP and cellular DAMP signals, collectively enhancing immune responses and potentially leading to a systemic effect against cancer. Through these mechanisms, OVs present a compelling method for converting immunologically quiescent tumor environments into active sites of antitumor immune activity, offering a hopeful avenue for advancing cancer therapy against immunosuppressive challenges.

Efficient delivery of OVs

The field of OV delivery has evolved, yet challenges remain, particularly in optimizing systemic vs. local strategies. Intratumoral administration is often preferred as it directly targets the tumor site, ensuring high local virus concentration and stimulating a localized immune response that can potentially affect non-injected cells. However, this approach is limited by tumor accessibility and may not be feasible for disseminated or inaccessible tumors.

Conversely, systemic delivery via intravenous infusion aims to distribute OVs throughout the body, theoretically reaching both primary and metastatic lesions. Intravenous administration often requires 10- to 100-fold higher doses to overcome clearance and achieve tumor levels comparable to intratumoral injections, increasing potential toxicity. Clinical experience with systemic vaccinia (Pexa-Vec) illustrates the magnitude of doses explored (up to 3 × 107 PFU i.v., biweekly), and similar challenges are discussed across platforms (adenovirus, reovirus), underscoring that dose intensity is a practical workaround for systemic losses rather than an intrinsic potency requirement.

Systemic delivery offers ease of administration and access to inaccessible tumors but faces significant hurdles like rapid clearance by the immune system, neutralizing antibodies, and off-target sequestration (e.g., in the liver and spleen), often requiring higher doses. As an example, systemic OV delivery when combined with ICIs may broaden antitumor immunity but also can trigger severe immune-related adverse events, such as colitis, pneumonitis, or endocrinopathies from systemic T cell overactivation, which are the most frequent adverse events seen across OV trials. Contemporary reviews synthesize these risk patterns and emphasize that combination regimens require careful dose scheduling, laboratory surveillance, and prompt steroids or immunosuppression modalities. Also, localized OV-ICI combinations may mitigate this problem.

Strategies like using cationic lipids or pegylation have been suggested to improve systemic delivery by reducing liver sequestration and toxicity, potentially increasing bioavailability and effectiveness. Innovative methods like aerosol inhalers for lung cancers or ultrasound cavitation to enhance intratumoral uptake have also been explored for systemic delivery.

Another promising strategy uses MDSCs as carriers to deliver OVs directly to the tumor. This leverages the natural tumor tropism of MDSCs to bypass some immune barriers that hinder OV effectiveness, enhancing tumor targeting and therapeutic outcomes. Despite the appeal of systemic delivery, preexisting immunity remains a major obstacle. As previously described, techniques like polymer coating or “stealthing” with polymers such HPMA and polyethylene glycol have been developed to shield OVs from the immune system, extend their half-life, and improve tumor targeting. Local delivery, particularly intratumoral injection, remains the most common route due to its direct effect. It avoids some systemic immune challenges but is limited by anatomical location and its ability to induce a robust systemic response against distant metastases. The current landscape of OV delivery is dynamic, balancing advancing technology with biological limitations. Although systemic delivery enables broad distribution, its efficacy is limited by host immune clearance and complex pharmacokinetics, underscoring the need for continued innovation.

Enhancing intratumoral infiltration and diffusion of OVs

Enhancing intratumoral infiltration and diffusion of OVs is central to improving their therapeutic efficacy. The dense proteoglycans in the extracellular matrix (ECM) create a substantial obstacle for agent distribution within solid tumors. Strategies, such as combining hyaluronidase with oncolytic adenoviruses, have effectively overcome this barrier, thereby improving viral spread and therapeutic outcomes. Modifying OVs to produce matrix metalloproteinases has also degraded tumor-related sulfated glycosaminoglycans, significantly boosting viral diffusion. Another approach constructs recombinant viruses targeting fibroblast activation protein (FAP), which is abundant on stromal cells, aiming to directly modify the tumor stroma for improved viral access. Equipping OVs with transgenes that modify ECM components (e.g., decorin or relaxin) may enhance viral dissemination, normalize tumor vasculature, and increase immune cell infiltration.

Beyond physical barriers, balancing immunosuppression within the TME is crucial. While immunosuppression can increase intratumoral distribution, boosting the host immune system aids in targeting infected cells, although it might restrict viral spread. To address this, OVs encoding bispecific T cell engagers (TCEs) locally increase T cell concentrations at the tumor site, promoting infiltration and reducing systemic exposure. To further enhance infiltration, engineered OVs expressing viral fusion proteins (e.g., FAST proteins, GALV-MV-F, NDV-F) induce cell-cell fusion between infected and neighboring uninfected cells, facilitating cell entry and viral spread. Additionally, inducing apoptosis in cancer cells is crucial for improving viral dissemination. Activating caspase-8 and triggering apoptosis via cytotoxic agents has enhanced the intratumoral penetration of oncolytic HSV, as apoptotic cells form channel-like structures aiding viral spread. Through these multifaceted strategies, the field continues to advance, overcoming the dense and complex barriers of the TME to optimize OV delivery and efficacy.

Addressing antiviral immune responses

Researchers in oncolytic virotherapy grapple with the dual nature of antiviral immune responses. Intravenous OV delivery often triggers the host immune system to sequester and degrade the virus, particularly via macrophages in the spleen and liver. While detrimental to OV persistence, such responses can paradoxically enhance tumor cell targeting when appropriately manipulated. Antiviral immunity presents significant challenges, especially with systemic administration. Strategies like using cell carriers with tumor tropism and mutating neutralizing antibody epitopes have been explored to shield viruses and prevent neutralization. Interestingly, some studies suggest preexisting immunity might increase the therapeutic efficacy of certain viruses by enhancing systemic antitumor immunity. Despite these innovative approaches, innate and adaptive immune responses can rapidly clear OVs, reducing antitumor efficacy. To mitigate this, strategies including immunomodulators, genetic manipulation, and histone deacetylase inhibitors aim to suppress antiviral immunity and danger signal emission, prolonging OV persistence and enhancing tumor destruction.

However, OV-induced antiviral immune responses are not universally negative. Some argue these responses benefit antitumor immunity by reversing tumor-associated immunosuppression and promoting immunogenic cell death. This dual role underscores the complex interaction between the OV-immune system, making balancing immune responses a key challenge.

Resistance to OV therapy involves factors like neutralizing antibodies, off-target sequestration, and suppressive TME. Combination therapies with other modalities are considered to address these issues. Overcoming antiviral cytokines, particularly interferons, remains a critical objective. Pretreatment with agents like histone deacetylase inhibitors shows potential to induce favorable epigenetic alterations in the TME, enhancing viral replication and diminishing antiviral cytokine responses. Thus, developing effective OV therapies requires a nuanced understanding of the interplay between viral mechanisms and host immune responses, highlighting the need for innovative strategies to manipulate these dynamics for optimal therapeutic outcomes.

Combinational therapies of OVs with other modalities

OV monotherapy approaches may face challenges such as the development of drug resistance due to tumor heterogeneity, the presence of neutralizing antibodies, and the immunosuppressive TME. Substantial preclinical studies indicate OVs have great potential for synergy when combined with other anticancer modalities. We discuss five combination therapy strategies in this section. A brief description of combinatorial approaches in oncolytic virotherapy is provided in Table 2.

Synergistic effects of OVs with chemotherapy and radiotherapy

Conventional therapies, including chemotherapy and radiotherapy, are employed alone or in combination. The rationale behind administering chemotherapeutic agents and OVs simultaneously goes beyond boosting tumor-fighting effects by targeting different pathways. It also involves utilizing chemotherapy’s immunosuppressive effects, which can prevent the immune system from neutralizing the virus. Chemotherapy works alongside virotherapy through mechanisms like direct tumor cell killing, enhancing cancer cell immunogenicity, and blocking antiviral immune responses. A growing list of chemotherapy regimens (e.g., leucovorin, infusional fluorouracil, gemcitabine, paclitaxel, oxaliplatin, cisplatin) are being studied in combination with different OVs.

Cisplatin, commonly used in combination regimens, has enhanced the replication, spread, and cytolytic activity of OVs, leading to more effective tumor cell killing. Based on promising preclinical and early clinical findings (NCT00006106), a phase 3 trial evaluated ONYX-015, an engineered Ad vector, combined with cisplatin and 5-fluorouracil for head and neck or esophageal squamous cell carcinoma (SCC). The response rate was 79% in the combination arm, compared with 40% with chemotherapy alone. Combining chemotherapy with various viruses has enhanced treatment effectiveness and OVs' immunotherapeutic profile, potentially reducing chemotherapy dosage and adverse effects. This approach has been tested in numerous preclinical tumor models and has garnered positive results, leading to the initiation of clinical trials for aggressive tumors. Sabri and colleagues recently evaluated the synergistic antitumor effects of NDV and doxorubicin on MDA-MB-231 and 4T1 cell lines in vitro and in a breast cancer animal model in vivo. The combination therapy significantly inhibited breast tumor progression while reducing doxorubicin dose and toxic side effects. Notably, NDV showed selective oncolytic activity on patient-derived breast tumor cells and improved drug-resistant breast cancer cells' responsiveness to chemotherapy, highlighting its potential as a complementary therapeutic agent.

Radiotherapy is commonly used for local tumor control and exhibits broad antitumor effects. By inducing cellular changes, radiotherapy can enhance viral replication and spread within tumors. Promising therapeutic outcomes have been observed when combining virotherapy and radiotherapy. According to O'Cathail et al. the combination of OV with radiotherapy induced tumor-specific cytotoxicity through augmented virus production. Udaykumar et al. reported that combining VSV-IFNβ, an oncolytic VSV expressing the IFNβ transgene, and radiotherapy enhanced the immune response against tumors and significantly reduced tumor size in syngeneic mouse models. Oncolytic virotherapy can also enhance tumor sensitivity to radiotherapy. For instance, introducing a p53-expressing adenovirus increased the susceptibility of p53-defective tumor cells to radiation-induced apoptosis, leading to greater tumor growth inhibition than with radiotherapy alone. Multiple OV platforms have been combined with radiotherapy to amplify tumor-specific cytotoxicity via complementary mechanisms. Oncolytic HSV-1 (G207) shows enhanced intratumoral replication and spread when paired with low-dose radiotherapy, increasing ICD and downstream antitumor immunity, in glioma models. Engineered oHSV-P10 also enhanced radiosensitivity in tumor cells, consistent with OV-mediated disruption of DNA damage repair and cell-cycle regulation. Another example is vaccinia backbones that have also demonstrated synergy with radiotherapy, improving survival and increasing CD8+:Treg ratios in intracranial tumor models, highlighting convergent mechanisms of direct cytotoxicity plus immune potentiation. These viruses enhance tumor cytotoxicity through improved viral replication post irradiation, increased ICD, and vascular permeability that enhances radiotherapy efficacy. Based on previous literature, combining oncolytic HSV, VACV, VSV, and adenovirus with radiotherapy has demonstrated notable therapeutic benefits.

Combination of OVs with targeted therapies

Combining OVs with targeted therapies represents a promising approach in cancer treatment. Targeted therapies specifically inhibit molecular pathways in cancer cells to disrupt their growth and survival. Combining OVs with targeted therapies can potentially enhance antitumor effects and overcome resistance mechanisms. Targeted therapies include monoclonal antibodies, angiogenesis inhibitors, proteasome inhibitors, signal transduction inhibitors, histone deacetylase inhibitors (HDACIs), DNA repair inhibitors, and more.

VEGF, a master regulator of angiogenesis, is a key target for combination therapy with OVs. VEGF inhibitors can alter immune homeostasis in the TME by regulating cytokine expression (e.g., CXCL1, IL-1β, IL-6) in tumor cells. Axitinib, a tyrosine kinase inhibitor targeting VEGF receptors, inhibits angiogenesis. Saha et al. reported that combining axitinib and an HSV-1 OV expressing IL-12 (G47Δ-mIL12) notably reduced vascularity and increased infiltrated macrophages and tumor necrosis in human glioblastoma stem-like cell models. Combining sunitinib (another VEGFR inhibitor) and JX-594 (a recombinant vaccinia expressing GM-CSF) in renal cell carcinoma showed increased IFNγ production by tumor-specific CD8+ T cells and established protective immunity upon tumor rechallenge.

Several studies show that molecularly targeted therapy can enhance OV effectiveness through mechanisms like immune evasion and boosting antitumor immunity. Lee et al. demonstrated that inhibiting the MEK-ERK pathway improved OVV replication in doxorubicin-resistant ovarian cancer. Combining OVV and trametinib effectively reduced A2780-R xenograft tumor growth by suppressing cytosolic DNA sensing and viral defense pathways. Patel and colleagues found that JAK/STAT inhibition with ruxolitinib enhanced VSV-IFNβ propagation in non-small cell lung cancer models. Ruxolitinib, a selective JAK-1/2 inhibitor, enhanced VSV replication and activity by antagonizing the antiviral JAK/STAT signaling pathway. In another study, combining Trichostatin A, an HDACI, with vaccinia virus effectively enhanced viral spread in cancer cells and increased survival in mice with human HCT116 colon tumor xenografts. Cody and coworkers obtained similar results, where concomitant application of HDACIs (belinostat, entinostat, panobinostat) with OV improved oHSV replication in breast cancer cells but not in normal cells.

Combination of OVs with ICIs

The growing understanding of the immune basis of oncolytic virotherapy coincided with the clinical success of immunotherapy, specifically ICIs. ICIs are monoclonal antibodies that block immune checkpoint proteins (e.g., PD-1, PD-L1, CTLA-4, TIM-3, LAG-3) that act as natural brakes on the immune system. Blocking these interactions prevents shutdown signals to T cells, enabling them to identify and attack tumor cells. Ipilimumab, an anti-CTLA4 antibody, was the first FDA-approved ICI for advanced melanoma in 2011, and multiple ICIs have since been approved for various cancers. However, the overall response rate to ICI monotherapy is only around 20%–30%, partly due to low tumor immunogenicity and limited infiltration of effective T cells in many tumors. OVs, by functioning as an in situ cancer vaccine, can enhance tumor immunogenicity and facilitate T cell infiltration, making them an optimal platform to boost ICI effectiveness.

In 2012, Dias et al. conducted the first combination therapy study using an OAd encoding a fully human monoclonal antibody specific for CTLA-4. Zamarin and colleagues found that combining a recombinant NDV armed with anti-CTLA4 Ab in localized immunotherapy cured most tumor-bearing mice, whereas NDV monotherapy was effective in only 10% of cases. The success depended on CD8+ T cells, type I IFN, and NK cells, rather than oncolysis alone. This combination led to tumor rejection throughout the body and provided protection against rechallenge in low-immunogenicity tumor models. Zhu et al. explored the synergistic effects of an oncolytic herpes simplex virus (VT1092M) armed with IL-12, combined with ICIs. In a bilateral subcutaneous tumor mouse model, VT1092M alone or combined with anti-PD-L1 antibody significantly reduced both injected and untreated abscopal tumor sizes, showing superior efficacy. The therapy increased T cell numbers and CD8+ T cell activation, induced long-term tumor-specific immune responses, and prolonged survival, highlighting this combination’s potential for preventing metastasis and improving outcomes.

A promising innovation involves OVs engineered to deliver checkpoint inhibitors directly into the tumor microenvironment. OVs are armed to secrete checkpoint-blocking biologics directly within the TME, achieving high local ICI exposure while potentially mitigating systemic immune-related adverse events. HSV-1 and adenoviral platforms offer sufficient payload capacity for full antibodies or scFv derivatives and have been advanced clinically and preclinically for this purpose. A prominent example is RP2, an HSV-1 engineered to express an anti-CTLA-4 antibody, designed to optimize intratumoral immune priming while reducing toxicities associated with systemic anti-CTLA-4; early phase data (NCT04336241) report tolerability and signals of activity in advanced solid tumors.

In another animal study by Lin et al., researchers investigated delivering a PD-1-blocking scFv through oncolytic HSV-1 directly into tumors. This approach enhanced the immune response against tumors and showed even greater efficacy combined with TIGIT blockade in MC38 and Hepa1-6 subcutaneous tumor models. Beyond HSV-1, OAds that encode anti-PD-L1 scFv or related PD-L1 inhibitors have shown robust intratumoral immune activation and antitumor efficacy in preclinical models, supporting the concept that vectorized ICIs can remodel the TME and broaden responsiveness to immunotherapy.

Combining anti-CTLA4, anti-PD1/PD-L1, and an OV in triple therapy has gained attention for its potential in inhibiting tumor growth and increasing survival in a triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) mouse model. This combination enhances CD8+ T and memory T cells while depleting Tregs and tumor-associated macrophages, resulting in improved antitumor effects. With promising results and a favorable safety profile, more ICIs and OVs are being tested in clinical trials. For instance, a phase 1 trial combining T-VEC with anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab) showed promising objective and long-lasting responses in stage IIIB-IV melanoma. An open-label phase 2 trial showed a significant increase in objective response rates when T-VEC was combined with ipilimumab compared with ipilimumab alone. A 5-year analysis of a phase 2 trial comparing T-VEC plus ipilimumab vs. ipilimumab alone for advanced melanoma showed a median progression-free survival of 13.5 months for the combination vs. 6.4 months for ipilimumab alone. Estimated 5-year OS was also higher in the combination arm (54.7%) vs. ipilimumab alone (48.4%). Another recombinant virus combined with ICIs is oncolytic poliovirus PVSRIPO. Used in combination with PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, PVSRIPO targets glioma-associated macrophages and microglia that contribute to immune suppression. This combination activates microglia, suppresses tumor growth, and leads to long-lasting remissions. Clinical evidence supports these synergies; for example, in the phase 1b portion (n = 21, treatment-naïve advanced melanoma), T-VEC+pembrolizumab produced a 62% objective response rate (ORR) with 33% complete response (CR), alongside marked increases in intratumoral CD8+ T cells and IFN-γ/PD-L1 expression after the T-VEC priming phase, supporting a mechanistic basis for synergy. However, the subsequent randomized, double-blind phase 3 trial (MASTERKEY-265/KEYNOTE-034) did not demonstrate a statistically significant survival advantage of T-VEC plus pembrolizumab over placebo plus pembrolizumab in patients with unresectable melanoma, despite numerically higher response rates, highlighting population, endpoint, and study design sensitivities when transitioning from early signals to registrational trials.

Overall, these trials highlight OVs as a compelling option that, when combined with ICIs, shows adaptability in treating various solid tumors. Overall, combining OVs with ICIs can enhance antitumor immunity by promoting immune cell infiltration and antigen presentation. OVs help turn immunologically “cold” tumors into “hot” ones, making them more responsive to ICIs like anti-PD-1 or anti-CTLA-4, leading to improved therapeutic outcomes in both preclinical and clinical studies.

Combination of OVs with adoptive T cell therapy

Chimeric antigen receptor T cell (CAR-T) therapy is a revolutionary immunotherapy enhancing T cell responses and augmenting antitumor abilities, producing effective and long-lasting clinical responses. Anti-CD19 CAR-T cell therapy was FDA approved in 2017 for hematological malignancies (Kymriah, Tecartus, Yescarta for ALL, mantle cell lymphoma, B cell lymphoma, respectively). CAR-T therapy uses engineered patient T cells to find and eliminate cancer cells. However, the efficacy observed in hematologic malignancies has not been consistently reproduced in solid tumors, due to limited tumor infiltration and poor persistence within the TME. To overcome these limitations, CAR-T cell therapy is being combined with OVs. Studies indicate OVs can enhance CAR-T cell recruitment by triggering antitumor immune responses. An interesting combination used an oncolytic chimeric orthopoxvirus carrying a truncated CD19 (OV19t), engineered to infect cancer cells and force surface expression of CD19t. The combination of OV19t and CD19-targeted CAR-T cells significantly increased CAR-T cell infiltration in MDA-MB-468 xenograft mice. Zhang and colleagues explored HSV-1-infected CAR-T cells for systemic delivery of HSV into solid tumors. In immunocompromised and immunocompetent GBM orthotopic mouse models, B7-H3 CAR-T cells effectively targeted lesions, enhancing T cell infiltration and prolonging survival. CAR-T cells carrying HSV suppressed growth in a bilateral subcutaneous model, demonstrating their potential as carriers for oncolytic virotherapy targeting distant tumors.

Arming OVs with TNF-α or IFNγ transgenes can promote CAR-T cell and TIL infiltration into the TME, improving antitumor responses. Administering HER2-specific CAR-T cells combined with OAd expressing IL-2 and PDL-1 in head and neck SCC xenograft models enhanced HER2-specific CAR-T cell efficacy and significantly improved survival compared with either therapy alone. In another preclinical study, combining B7H3 CAR-T cells with OAd expressing IL-17 (oAd-IL17) resulted in notable antitumor efficacy, while CAR-T cell monotherapy showed limited response in xenograft models. OVs armed with immunomodulatory chemokines like CXCL11 can enhance T cell trafficking into the tumor and overcome the TME barrier with CAR-T cells. Nishio and colleagues showed that local delivery of OV armed with CCL5 and IL-15 along with CAR-T cells enhanced CAR-T cell trafficking and persistence in the TME, improving antitumor effects and increasing survival in a neuroblastoma mouse model. Recently, a study revealed that combined therapy with an OAd armed with murine CXCL11 and B7H3 CAR-T cells not only enhanced CAR-T cell recruitment into glioma sites but also reprogrammed the TME in immunocompetent mice. TME analysis showed increased CD8+ cells, NK cells, and M1-macrophages after virus injection, ultimately improving CAR-T cell therapy efficacy.

Combination with bispecific T cell engagers

Bispecific antibodies are unique antitumor agents that simultaneously target two distinct antigens or epitopes by combining two antibodies. Bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs), a subclass, are recombinant proteins with two linked single-chain fragment variables (scFvs). One scFv targets a TAA, the other a T cell surface molecule (e.g., CD3). These dual bindings create an immunological synapse where activated T cells can release perforin and granzymes, leading to cancer cell destruction. Blinatumomab, a well-known BiTE targeting CD3 and CD19, was FDA approved in 2017 for a rare form of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL).

Like cytokines, BiTE molecules have limitations like rapid excretion, short half-life, and poor residence time in the TME. These challenges in solid tumors can be overcome when BiTEs are used with OVs. Yu et al. developed mFAP-TEA-VV, an oncolytic vaccinia construct encoding a BiTE composed of scFvs targeting CD3 and EphA2 (a tumor antigen). Their goal was to address safety concerns with FAP-targeted immunotherapies and off-target activation. They concluded that arming vaccinia viruses with T cell engagers could enhance therapy effectiveness by increasing T cell activation and promoting wider tumor cell elimination. Alemany and colleagues successfully created ICOVIR-15K-cBiTE, a modified OAd armed with a BiTE targeting CD3 and EGFR. Direct injection of ICOVIR-15K-cBiTE increased TIL persistence and accumulation within tumors. Combined with T cells or peripheral blood mononuclear cells, this engineered OAd showed improved efficacy in suppressing tumor growth and promoting antitumor responses. Wing and coworkers developed a modified adenovirus carrying an EGFR-targeting BiTE molecule. Combining this OAd-BiTE with EGFR-targeting CAR-T therapy improved tumor effectiveness and increased survival in a preclinical study.

Similar findings were reported recently. In 2022, Lei et al. designed OVV-CD19BiTE, a recombinant oncolytic vaccinia virus expressing a CD19-specific BiTE. Compared with the non-engineered virus, OVV-CD19BiTE showed increased ability to attract CD3+, CD8+, and naive CD8 T cell populations to the TME while maintaining similar replication and tumor destruction capacity. In a recent study, Wang et al. developed an OAd armed with MUC16-BiTE molecules targeting highly glycosylated mucins overexpressed in ovarian cancers. In a xenograft model, OAd-MUC16-BiTE showed stronger antitumor activity and increased CTLs compared with the parental virus. This approach also remodeled the TME and improved MHC I antigen presentation. Results from these studies indicate that OVs expressing BiTEs may help address certain drawbacks of current oncolytic virotherapy strategies.

Clinical trials with OVs

Since 2000, numerous clinical trials have examined a diverse range of OVs, primarily assessing safety and feasibility in phase 1. Many OVs have progressed to phase 2 and beyond. As of the search on clinicaltrials.gov on June 6, 2025, 174 clinical trials are currently recruiting patients for OV treatment, with over half sponsored by industry. Over 13 different OV types have been investigated, with notable focus on HSV, adenovirus, and vaccinia virus; most trials are conducted in the United States. Of the 169 trials, approximately 61% are phase 1, 34.5% phase 2, 3% phase 3, and 1.5% phase 4. Specifically, two phase 4 oncolytic virotherapy trials are being conducted in China (NCT05113290 and NCT05124002). NCT05113290 investigates combining recombinant human adenovirus type 5 (H101) with Sorafenib for advanced HCC. NCT05124002 studies H101 plus hepatic artery infusion chemotherapy of FOLFOX for intrahepatic mass-forming cholangiocarcinoma.

Multiple preclinical and clinical studies demonstrate OVs' potent immunostimulatory effects and potential for treating cancer. However, the most significant therapeutic outcomes are often observed when combining OVs with other immunotherapies, particularly ICIs like pembrolizumab, increasingly incorporated into clinical trials, either alone or in combination with OVs. This highlights the immense potential of combination therapies, especially for patients with refractory tumors.

While several OV platforms advanced to clinical investigation, some early trials were halted due to insufficient efficacy, safety concerns, toxicity, or manufacturing issues, highlighting the need for rigorous translational assessment and trial design emphasizing that not every preclinical success translates into clinical benefit. For example, the wild-type ECHO-7 was withdrawn due to insufficient evidence and discrepancies with the laboratory testing. Onyx-015 was discontinued after limited antitumor activity in phase 2/3 studies despite promising preclinical data, with variable outcomes in combination with chemotherapies. Beyond trial discontinuations, broader limitations in OV development like systemic delivery, failing preclinical tumor models replicate the immune complexity of human cancers continue to challenge the field. The cost and scalability of GMP-grade virus manufacturing also remain barriers, as highlighted in terminated or delayed programs where product instability or low yields undermined clinical translation. These controversies and setbacks collectively underscore the need for improved patient selection, combinatorial regimens, and advanced vector engineering to overcome efficacy and safety barriers and fully realize the clinical promise of OVs. Concluding remarks and future perspectives

A fundamental challenge in cancer treatment is the ability of cancer cells to evade the immune system through complex mechanisms, including the secretion of immunosuppressive factors, reduced presentation of tumor antigens, and the creation of an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment (TME). Tumors can be broadly classified as immunologically “hot,” characterized by significant immune cell infiltration, or “cold,” with limited immune presence. Hot tumors are generally more responsive to immunotherapies. A key therapeutic goal is therefore to convert these cold tumors into inflamed hot ones to enhance antitumor immunity. OVs are emerging as a potent novel form of cancer immunotherapy capable of directly addressing this goal. By selectively infecting and lysing tumor cells in an immunogenic manner, OVs not only destroy cancer cells but also function as an in situ vaccine, releasing tumor-specific antigens and danger signals that activate both innate and adaptive immune responses. In recent decades, substantial advances have been achieved in the genetic engineering of OVs to enhance their potency, tumor selectivity, and safety, and improve delivery and production methods. This progress has positioned OVs as promising agents, demonstrating efficacy in both preclinical studies and clinical trials, either as monotherapies or in combination strategies.

Combination with conventional therapies: Combining OVs with chemotherapy or radiotherapy can yield synergistic effects. Chemotherapy can potentially reduce systemic immune clearance of OVs while increasing tumor cell susceptibility, and radiotherapy can enhance viral replication and spread within the tumor, leading to improved tumor control and survival.

Combination with targeted therapies: Integrating OVs with targeted agents can disrupt specific pro-survival pathways in cancer cells and potentially modulate the TME or immune responses to favor viral infection and activity.

Combination with immunotherapies: The synergism between OVs and other immunotherapies, such as ICIs, adoptive T cell therapies (like CAR-T cells), or bispecific T cell engagers (BiTEs), is particularly compelling. OVs can enhance the inflammatory state of the TME, increase antigen presentation, and recruit effector cells, thereby priming tumors for more effective attack by these targeted immunotherapies.

Despite these advancements, the widespread clinical adoption of OVs faces several specific challenges. These include achieving sufficient penetration and uniform spread of OVs throughout the heterogeneous TME, overcoming intrinsic or acquired resistance mechanisms in tumor cells, managing the variability in patient immune responses, and addressing the often-limited efficacy of OV monotherapy compared with combinations. Furthermore, systemic delivery of OVs is hindered by rapid clearance by the host immune system, including neutralization by preexisting or treatment-induced antibodies. While direct intratumoral injection bypasses some systemic challenges, it is not applicable to all tumor types or metastatic disease. To address these hurdles and amplify therapeutic effects, strategic combinations of OVs with other cancer treatments are essential. Genetic engineering enables OVs to function as versatile “Trojan horses,” delivering therapeutic transgenes directly to the tumor site.

These combinatorial strategies facilitate a more comprehensive and potentially personalized approach to cancer treatment. Ongoing preclinical and clinical trials are actively exploring these combinations, aiming to demonstrate enhanced response rates and broader applicability compared with single-agent therapies. The future of oncolytic virotherapy is exceptionally bright, holding the potential to become a cornerstone of cancer care. Its unique mechanism of action, combining direct tumor lysis with potent immune stimulation, offers a powerful means to overcome tumor resistance and modulate the immunosuppressive TME. Future research will focus on developing even more sophisticated armed OVs, optimizing delivery methods (especially systemic), identifying biomarkers predictive of response to guide personalized combination treatments, and further unraveling the intricate interactions within the tumor-virus-immune system axis. Continued investment and collaboration across research, clinical, and industry sectors are crucial to translating the immense promise of oncolytic virotherapy into improved outcomes for a wider range of cancer patients.
