# Harnessing the Gut Microbiota to Improve Cancer Immunotherapy: Focus on Lung Cancer.

## Abstract

The gut microbiota has emerged as a key orchestrator of systemic immunity, capable of reshaping the tumor microenvironment and modulating responses to cancer immunotherapy via the gut-lung axis. While immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) has revolutionized lung cancer treatment, a significant proportion of patients fail to respond. Accumulating evidence suggests that intestinal microbial composition modulates antitumor immunity, yet clinical associations between specific microbial taxa and ICB outcomes often show inconsistencies across cohorts. In this review, we synthesize current mechanistic insights into how gut microbial metabolites and structural components modulate pulmonary immune surveillance. We critically examine the clinical landscape of microbiome signatures in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), highlighting how species- and strain-level heterogeneity contributes to divergent findings. Finally, we discuss translational strategies-ranging from fecal microbiota transplantation to rationally designed bacterial consortia and engineered probiotics-and propose a roadmap for integrating multi-omics with microbiome engineering to overcome current limitations and optimize precision immunotherapy.

INTRODUCTION

Lung cancer remains one of the most lethal malignancies worldwide. The advent of immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 and CTLA-4 pathways has revolutionized treatment paradigms, offering the potential for durable responses through immune memory mechanisms. However, the overall response rate to single-agent ICB in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) typically falls within 20%–30% depending on PD-L1 expression and histology, leaving a substantial proportion of patients without benefit. Tumor-intrinsic features such as PD-L1 expression and tumor mutational burden account for a proportion of this heterogeneity, yet substantial inter-patient variability remains unexplained. This limited efficacy underscores a need for strategies to enhance ICB responsiveness.

Among potential extrinsic modulators, the gut microbiota—a modifiable and patient-specific factor—has emerged as a meaningful contributor that may influence treatment outcomes through systemic immune modulation. Building upon the long-standing concept of cancer immunosurveillance and microorganism-mediated immune modulation, recent advances in next-generation sequencing technologies have enabled comprehensive characterization of previously unculturable microbial communities. These technological breakthroughs have revealed compelling associations between gut microbial composition and clinical outcomes, sparking growing interest in microbiome-based biomarkers and therapeutic interventions.

Nevertheless, the translation of these findings into practical applications has proven challenging. The effect sizes are often modest, the reproducibility across cohorts is inconsistent, and the mechanistic explanations remain incomplete. Despite the numerous translational efforts that have been attempted, most have resulted in only incremental progress, rather than clinical breakthroughs.

Evidence for a gut-immune-cancer axis has been identified across multiple cancer types, including melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, and gastrointestinal cancers. These studies have established broad principles regarding the modulation of systemic immunity by microbial diversity and particular taxa. However, it is evident that tumor type-specific factors also exert a significant influence on these relationships. In the context of lung cancer, the complex tumor immune microenvironment, characterized by heterogeneous immune cell populations that vary according to histological subtype, smoking status, and actionable mutations, poses significant challenges to the elucidation of the interaction between gut microbiota-mediated systemic immune regulation and lung-specific antitumor immunity.

This review integrates current mechanistic and clinical insights into microbiome-immune crosstalk in lung cancer, highlighting translational strategies and future perspectives for microbiome-guided immunotherapy.

MECHANISTIC INSIGHTS INTO MICROBIOME MODULATION OF IMMUNITY

The gut microbiota shapes both systemic and local immunity, influencing tumor biology not only in colorectal cancer but also in distant organs such as the lung. Two broad modes of action have been described: 1) direct effects, in which microbial structural components—including LPSs, peptidoglycans, and lipids—interact with host immune receptors or even directly with tumor cells; and 2) indirect effects, mediated by microbiota-derived metabolites that regulate immune signaling and tumor cell fate (Fig. 1).

Direct mechanisms: structural components and host sensing pathways

Commensal bacteria exert direct immunomodulatory effects on host immunity through their cell-wall components, surface molecules, and nucleic acid ligands. These microbial structures are sensed by host pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs), initiating innate immune cascades that drive antitumor immunity. Classical pathogen-associated molecular pattern (PAMP)-PRR interactions illustrate this principle across multiple bacterial taxa. Gram-negative commensals such as Bacteroides spp. can activate TLR4 on dendritic cells (DCs), preferentially inducing TRIF-IRF3-dependent type I IFN responses. Peptidoglycan and muramyl dipeptides from bacteria including Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium spp. stimulate NOD1/NOD2 in intestinal epithelial cell and DCs and enhance IL-6 and TNF-α release, supporting early innate activation. Flagellated microbes such as Escherichia coli deliver flagellin ligands that signal through TLR5, stimulating IL-6 and IL-1β production and reinforcing mucosal immune tone, a well-recognized pathway for initiating robust innate immune activation. CpG-rich bacterial DNA from gut commensals can engage TLR9, triggering type I IFN responses that enhance antitumor immunity.

Collectively, these PAMP-PRR pathways amplify innate inflammatory programming and create a cytokine milieu that facilitates the recruitment and activation of antitumor effector populations within the tumor microenvironment (TME). In line with this, microbial ligands enhance dendritic-cell maturation and Ag-presenting function, facilitating the activation of cytotoxic and Th cell subsets associated with improved tumor control. Consistent with this paradigm, Alistipes finegoldii—derived lipoproteins have been shown to activate TLR2 on DCs, strengthening innate signaling and enhancing antitumor efficacy in preclinical models. Similarly, Bifidobacterium bifidum enhances antitumor responses through TLR2-dependent activation of IFN-γ-producing CD8+ T cells. Structural components conserved across phylogenetically related gut microbiota can also mediate immuno-stimulatory effects. A pilin-like protein conserved among diverse bacterial taxa activates DCs via TLR4 signaling, and its administration enhances anti-PD-1 efficacy in tumor-bearing mice by promoting CD8+ T cell proliferation and effector function.

Notably, certain commensals engage multiple TLRs simultaneously to amplify these effects. Hominenteromicrobium mulieris activates endosomal TLR7 and TLR9 on CD103+CD11b− cDC1s in gut-associated lymphoid tissues. These activated DCs then migrate to tumor-draining lymph nodes and the microenvironment, where they prime CD8+ T cells with enhanced Ag sensitivity and diversified TCR repertoires.

Beyond PRR-dependent pathways, emerging evidence suggests that certain commensals can also engage tumor cells directly, thereby contributing to antitumor effects independently of canonical immune intermediates. Clostridium butyricum, for example, has been reported to bind to tumor surface protein SecD, thereby inducing apoptosis. Together, these direct mechanisms—including classical PAMP-PRR activation and PRR-independent tumoricidal interactions—demonstrate that microbial structural components play a foundational role in shaping the immunogenic landscape upon which antitumor immunity is mounted.

Indirect mechanism: microbial metabolites and systemic immuno-metabolic signaling

Microbiota-derived metabolites are closely linked to host physiology and pathology, yet their causal roles in cancer immunity are only beginning to be elucidated. While these metabolites can act locally within the gut, increasing evidence indicates that many undergo systemic diffusion, thereby influencing distal tumor sites. Among the most abundant are short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs)—including acetate, butyrate, and propionate—as well as tryptophan-derived metabolites such as indole-3-propionic acid (IPA) and indole-3-lactic acid (ILA).

Recent studies have demonstrated that microbiota-derived metabolites can directly modulate both cancer cells and immune compartments, encompassing innate as well as adaptive immune populations. In tumor cells, microbiota-derived metabolites such as ILA and trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) suppress proliferation and promote apoptosis. Notably, TMAO precursors can accumulate within tumor sites, activate the protein kinase R-like ER kinase (PERK)-ER stress pathway, and trigger IL-1β/IL-18 release, ultimately leading to cancer cell apoptosis. Similarly, butyrate limits tumor cell proliferation via calcineurin–NFATc3 signaling.

Beyond their direct cytotoxic effects on tumor cells, microbiota-derived metabolites also shape immune responses within the TME. Tumor cell stress and apoptosis induced by these metabolites can enhance Ag availability and inflammation signaling, thereby facilitating Ag presentation and CD8+ T cell-mediated antitumor immunity. In addition, microbiota-derived metabolites also shape innate immune responses. For example, Alistipes shahii secretes factors that selectively target lipocalin-2 (Lcn2) from senescent neutrophils, inducing ferroptosis and thereby limiting neutrophil accumulation within the TME.

In the adaptive immune compartment, microbial cues critically regulate T cell populations, particularly CD8+ T cells. Among these, progenitor exhausted CD8+ T cells (Tpex), characterized by high expression of transcription factor T cell factor-1 (TCF-1), are essential for sustaining durable antitumor immunity. Another tryptophan-derived metabolite, IPA, enhances this progenitor pool by inducing H3K27 acetylation at the Tcf7 locus, thereby augmenting CD8+ T cell stemness and effector potential. Consistently, dysbiosis in murine models has been associated with altered Tcf7 expression, reinforcing the role of microbial metabolites in epigenetic programming of Tpex cells. In addition, bile acids act as androgen receptor (AR) antagonists, expanding CD62L+ TCF-1+ CD8+ T cells and thereby improving responsiveness to PD-1 blockade. Microbiome-modulated secondary bile acids also influence ICB efficacy. Taurolithocholic acid (TLCA) enhances T cell proliferation and activation, upregulating effector cytokines such as IFN-γ and TNF-α, and promotes memory CD8+ T cell differentiation. In syngeneic tumor models, TLCA administration increases intratumoral cDC1, NK, and CD8+ T cell infiltration and synergizes with anti-PD-1 therapy to suppress tumor growth. Similarly, exercise-induced remodeling of the gut microbiota has been shown to increase microbial formate production, which enhances anti-PD-1 efficacy by promoting Nrf2-dependent CD8+ T cell antitumor response. Butyrate further potentiates CD8+ T cell activity through ID2-dependent IL-12 signaling in the context of chemotherapy, yielding synergistic antitumor effects when combined with chemo- or immunotherapy.

GUT-LUNG CROSSTALK IN HEALTH AND DISEASE

Bidirectional communication between gut and lung mucosal immunity

Although the lung and gut are anatomically distinct organs, they share a common embryonic origin from the endoderm and are connected by a bidirectional communication network encompassing microbial, immune, and metabolic pathways. The gastrointestinal tract harbors a dense and diverse microbiota and is intricately integrated with vascular and lymphatic systems, allowing gut-derived metabolites and microbial components, and immune cells to disseminate systemically and influence distant tissues, including the lung. Within the mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue framework—comprising gut-associated lymphoid tissue and bronchus-associated lymphoid tissue—immune activation or tolerance at one mucosal site can recalibrate responses at the other, establishing a shared systemic immune axis.

Chronic inflammatory bowel diseases frequently coexist with asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, and bronchiectasis, while respiratory infections such as influenza and respiratory syncytial virus disrupt gut microbial ecology, highlighting the reciprocal nature of gut–lung communication. Recent evidence extends this concept to cancer biology, showing that intestinal dysbiosis, impaired barrier function, and altered metabolite signaling can reshape systemic inflammation and modulate immune responses in distal organs, including the lung.

Mechanistically, gut–lung crosstalk engages two interlocking conduits. First, metabolic signaling: gut-derived small molecules (e.g., SCFAs, tryptophan metabolites, secondary bile acids) enter the circulation and modulate pulmonary epithelial, stromal, and immune cell programs. Second, immune trafficking: DCs, group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2), γδ T cells, and other lymphocytes traffic between mucosal sites via lymphatic and blood routes, imprinting Ag experience and polarizing cytokine milieus. Notably, ILC2s have been shown to migrate from the gut to the lung via sphingosine-1-phosphate receptor-dependent mechanisms, where they participate in host defense. In the lung, microbial-associated molecular patterns such as LPS that translocate from a compromised gut can engage TLR signaling in alveolar macrophages, leading to IL-1β production and shaping the local inflammatory milieu. Through these pathways, gut perturbations including dysbiosis, dietary shifts, medication exposures, and impaired barrier integrity such as the gut vascular barrier (GVB) reshape systemic immune signaling and reprogram the pulmonary TME toward states that influence antitumor surveillance.

Collectively, the gut–lung axis functions as an immunometabolic interface, wherein gut-derived signals shape lung immunity and, conversely, pulmonary inflammation feeds back to the intestine, altering microbial ecology and barrier integrity (Fig. 2).

Gut microbiota as a regulator of lung tumor immunity and clinical translation

Preclinical studies using germ-free, antibiotic-treated, and fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) models have established that the gut microbiota exerts causal control over pulmonary tumor immunity. Manipulating gut communities modulates the lung TME, particularly influencing CD8+ T cell and NK cell activation, DC priming, and macrophage polarization. Microbial dysbiosis or depletion weakens immune surveillance, reducing Ag presentation and cytotoxicity, whereas microbial restoration via FMT or probiotics can reestablish antitumor immunity. Dysbiosis-driven alterations in microbial metabolites further affect oncogenic signaling and immune tolerance in the lung. SCFAs can induce Treg differentiation in some contexts, while inosine enhances CD8+ T cell effector function and potentiates responsiveness to ICB therapy. Circulating cytokine shifts triggered by gut perturbations can additionally modify pulmonary immune programs, reinforcing the concept that the gut microbiota sets the baseline immune tone against which lung tumors evolve and against which immunotherapies operate.

Clinical evidence mirrors these preclinical observations. NSCLC patients with higher gut microbial diversity or enrichment of taxa such as Akkermansia and Bifidobacterium consistently demonstrate improved outcomes following PD-1/PD-L1 blockade, whereas antibiotic exposure before or during therapy correlates with diminished efficacy and reduced survival. These associations indicate that gut microbial composition is not merely correlative but functionally aligned with therapeutic responsiveness.

Building on this rationale, multiple microbiome-modulating strategies have entered translational development. Early-phase clinical trials testing FMT from ICB responders into non-responders have shown partial restoration of anti-PD-1 sensitivity. In parallel, rationally designed bacterial consortia—such as multi-strain immunostimulatory cocktails—aim to deliver reproducible microbial functions without the variability of whole-stool transplantation. Engineered probiotics and microbial therapeutics further expand this toolkit by enabling targeted delivery of immunoregulatory metabolites, including inosine and SCFAs, to systemically enhance antitumor immunity.

Looking ahead, two priorities will shape microbiome-based interventions in lung cancer. First, precision stratification using strain-resolved metagenomics, metabolomics, and spatial immune profiling will be essential for identifying patient subsets that benefit from specific microbial functions. Second, mechanism-guided therapeutic design—grounded in pathways such as CD8+ T cell stemness, dendritic-cell licensing, and macrophage polarization—will be required to translate microbiome science into controllable and clinically reliable interventions.

Ultimately, integrating gut–lung axis-informed diagnostics with microbiome engineering has the potential to transform the gut microbiota from a passive biomarker into an actionable therapeutic lever in lung cancer care.

INTERPLAY BETWEEN THE GUT MICROBIOME AND ICB

ICB remodels the intestinal microbiome

ICB restores exhausted T cells and reshapes systemic immunity—but it also remodels the intestinal ecosystem. Prospective longitudinal profiling has revealed time-dependent changes in community structure during anti-PD-1 or anti-CTLA-4 therapy. In a subset of patients, these shifts coincide with immune-related adverse events (irAEs), notably colitis, indicating that therapeutic immune activation imposes selective pressure on gut communities.

Mechanistically, checkpoint blockade modifies mucosal immune tone—enhancing IFN-γ and TNF-α signaling within the intestinal epithelium—which may favor the outgrowth of opportunistic species at the expense of protective commensals. While this immune activation underlies the antitumor efficacy of ICB, it can simultaneously disturb intestinal immune homeostasis, leading to collateral dysbiosis and inflammation. Such perturbations are proposed to arise through multiple immune-mediated mechanisms, including cytokine-driven remodeling of the epithelial niche, altered barrier integrity, and changes in mucosal IgA responses that collectively reshape microbial colonization and persistence. Several studies have documented a decline in SCFA-producing taxa, such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Roseburia spp., after ICB therapy, supporting the concept that immune activation reshapes the gut ecosystem toward a more proinflammatory state.

Together, these findings illustrate that ICB not only restores systemic immune surveillance but also imposes selective pressure on the intestinal microbiome, forming a bidirectional relationship wherein therapeutic immune activation and microbial dynamics are intimately intertwined. From a translational perspective, this interplay suggests two opportunities: first, real-time microbiome monitoring during ICB may serve as an early indicator of efficacy and immune-related toxicity; second, stabilizing or replenishing beneficial commensals could mitigate dysbiosis-associated side effects and sustain therapeutic benefit.

Ecological principles and host factors governing ICB-microbiome dynamics

The magnitude and direction of ICB-induced microbiome shifts are context dependent, reflecting host genetics, diet, prior medications (antibiotics, proton pump inhibitors [PPIs], corticosteroids), and baseline microbial ecology. Patients with low baseline diversity or depleted SCFA producers may experience larger perturbations and less resilient recovery trajectories during therapy. Conversely, individuals harboring functionally redundant, SCFA competent communities may retain metabolic buffering capacity, thereby attenuating proinflammatory drift. At the community level, ICB can alter interspecies networks—e.g., weakening keystone SCFA hubs and enabling oral-origin taxa to expand in the colon (a phenomenon exacerbated by PPI use). At the host level, mucosal type-I/II IFN programs and epithelial barrier tone influence whether therapy-associated inflammation remains contained or propagates, determining the risk and severity of gastrointestinal irAEs. Together, these host-microbe parameters set the operating point for ICB; they choreograph how far and in what direction therapy nudges the intestinal ecosystem.

Practically, these principles explain why ICB-induced dysbiosis varies across patients and help define time-windows for supportive interventions, while also clarifying sources of heterogeneity observed later in lung-cancer cohorts.

Microbiome modulation of ICB responsiveness: mechanisms and translational opportunities

The gut microbiota is increasingly recognized as a pivotal determinant of ICB efficacy. Across melanoma, NSCLC, and renal cell carcinoma cohorts, higher microbial diversity and enrichment of certain commensals, such as Bifidobacterium spp. and Akkermansia muciniphila, have been associated with favorable therapeutic outcomes in several cohorts, though with notable species- and/or strain-dependent heterogeneity as discussed in next section (Gut microbiome associations with ICB outcomes across lung cancer patients). These clinical observations are consistent with the earlier mechanistic framework, whereby commensal microbes shape systemic immune tone and modulate antitumor responsiveness (Fig. 3).

External perturbations, however, can readily disrupt this favorable cross-talk. Antibiotic-induced dysbiosis compromises ICB efficacy by impairing NK-cell cytotoxicity, skewing macrophage polarization toward suppressive phenotypes, and attenuating T-cell effector activity. The detrimental effects depend on both timing and antibiotic class, with the most pronounced reductions in progression-free survival (PFS) observed when antibiotics are administered shortly before or during ICB therapy. Similarly, commonly prescribed concomitant drugs such as PPIs and systemic corticosteroids exacerbate microbiome imbalance and obscure the underlying association between microbial composition and therapeutic outcome.

Compelling preclinical evidence further substantiates the functional role of the gut microbiota; Transferring fecal material from ICB responders into germ-free or antibiotic-treated mice reinstates antitumor activity, whereas transplants from non-responders fail to do so. Together, these findings demonstrate that the microbiota is not merely a biomarker but an active determinant of ICB responsiveness, providing a rationale for therapeutic interventions that purposefully remodel the gut ecosystem.

From a translational perspective, microbiota-targeted interventions are emerging as a promising strategy to overcome ICB resistance. FMT has emerged as a leading strategy; In both murine models and early-phase clinical trials, FMT from ICB responders into non-responders has restored sensitivity to anti-PD-1 therapy. Next-generation microbiota therapeutics aim to move beyond empirical transplantation toward standardized, reproducible, and mechanistically defined interventions. Among these, rationally designed bacterial consortia have emerged as a key innovation. For instance, Tanoue et al. reported a rationally designed 11-strain consortium capable of expanding IFN-γ-producing CD8+ T cells and promoting systemic antitumor immunity when co-administered with ICB. Such defined communities retain the immunostimulatory benefits of FMT while enabling scalable, compositionally controlled manufacturing. Building upon this foundation, next-generation microbiota therapeutics—including rationally designed consortia and defined live biotherapeutic products (LBPs)—represent the next stage of innovation. These approaches aim to deliver reproducible microbial functions consistently, integrating previously identified immuno-modulatory pathways into controllable platforms.

Several microbiota-based therapeutics have now entered clinical evaluation in combination with ICB. BMC128, a consortium of 4 bacterial strains identified through computational analysis of responder microbiomes, demonstrated an acceptable safety profile and preliminary signs of clinical benefits in a phase I trial combining the consortium with nivolumab in patients with advanced solid tumors, including clear cell renal cell carcinoma, cutaneous melanoma and epidermal growth factor receptor/anaplastic lymphoma kinase wild-type NSCLC-adenocarcinoma, who had progressed on prior anti-PD-(L)1 treatment. Oncobax-AK, a single-strain LBP comprising Akkermansia massiliensis p2261, is being evaluated in combination with ipilimumab/nivolumab in patients with advanced solid tumors, including NSCLC, melanoma, and renal cell carcinoma who had depleted levels of A. muciniphila SGB9226 or A. massiliensis SGB9228. The trial reported an acceptable safety profile with no serious adverse events attributable to the LBP, and preliminary evidence of clinical benefit reported in renal cell carcinoma, though results for NSCLC are still pending. These early-phase trials demonstrate the momentum of translational research towards microbiome-based combinatory immunotherapy, while also highlighting the need for rigorous characterization as discussed in the next section.

Collectively, these microbiota-targeted modalities mark a translational shift from descriptive correlations to rational microbiome engineering, paving the way for clinically deployable, precision-designed adjuncts to cancer immunotherapy.

GUT MICROBIOME ASSOCIATIONS WITH ICB OUTCOMES ACROSS LUNG CANCER PATIENTS

The identification of reliable predictive biomarkers of ICB response remains a clinical unmet need in lung cancer. While the preceding sections have outlined well-established molecular mechanisms by which the gut microbiota modulates antitumor immunity, it is important to note that these pathways likely operate simultaneously and interact in a context-dependent manner in physiological settings. The inherent complexity of these, further compounded by host genetic variation, dietary factors, and medication history, necessitates meticulous interpretation when translating mechanistic insights into clinical observations.

In the following section, an examination is conducted of cross-sectional observational studies that have hitherto been conducted on the subject of the gut microbiome composition and ICB outcomes in lung cancer (Table 1). This section focuses on the consistency and discrepancies observed across studies, population-specific effects, and the contributions of taxonomic and functional resolution.

Bifidobacterium and Bacteroides: associations with species-dependent heterogeneity

Bifidobacterium has been associated with ICB outcomes across multiple cohorts, though patterns differ notably by geographic region and, importantly, at the species level. At the genus level, Bifidobacterium enrichment has correlated with favorable outcomes in most Asian cohorts, with more mixed findings in Western cohorts.

However, species-level analysis reveals important heterogeneity obscured at the genus-level. In a Korean cohort, B. bifidum was enriched in responders, and that B. bifidum enhanced antitumor immunity via TLR2-dependent activation of IFN-γ producing CD8+ T cells, linked to peptidoglycan biosynthesis pathways. Conversely, foundational French cohort associated Bifidobacterium adolescentis and Bifidobacterium longum with non-responders, and a Hungarian cohort linked B. adolescentis to shorter PFS.

A similar contradictory pattern is observed for Bacteroides. Several studies have identified specific Bacteroides species that have been shown to have favorable associations. For example, Bacteroides caccae has been found to be enriched in responders in both the French and Chinese cohorts, and its abundance correlates with enhanced glycan degradation and SCFA production. However, other investigations have found opposing associations—Bacteroides fragilis was elevated in patients with progressive disease, and Bacteroides showed a negative correlation with T cell receptor clonality in neoadjuvant settings.

Species-level analysis revealed further complexity—B. fragilis was detected only in non-responders while Bacteroides stercoris and Bacteroides uniformis maintained presence in both groups, suggesting distinct functional roles even among closely related species.

The presence of these divergent associations across both genera demonstrates that genus-level profiling has the potential to obscure functionally relevant species- or strain-level differences. The contrasting results associated with different Bifidobacterium species and Bacteroides species indicate that species- or strain-level resolution may be necessary to resolve apparent inconsistencies and identify functionally relevant biomarkers.

A. muciniphila: from population discrepancies to strain-resolved insights

A. muciniphila is one of the most extensively studied microbes in relation to its association with antitumor effects. However, its relationship with ICB treatment outcomes shows significant variation across populations, highlighting the difficulty of developing universal microbiome biomarkers.

Western cohorts, with particular emphasis on seminal French studies, associated A. muciniphila with positive responses to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy in patients in whom A. muciniphila is present but its abundance is less than 4.799%. An excess of A. muciniphila has been demonstrated to be indicative of a worse prognosis than in its absence, thus suggesting the existence of a non-monotonic interaction between the microbiome and clinical outcomes. A subsequent study built on this complexity, developing a cancer-specific microbiome signature using unsupervised clustering of microbial co-abundance patterns. The unclassified “grey zone” was then refined using A. muciniphila abundance as a secondary stratification criterion, and the score was ultimately established based on the ecological topology of gut microbiota associated with cancer immunotherapy outcomes (TOPOSCORE).

Elsewhere, a linear relationship between the abundance of A. muciniphila and durable clinical benefit has been reported in a Caucasian patient population. Patients with a relative abundance of >0.74% have been shown to exhibit significantly better outcomes. In a broader context, the enrichment of the Akkermansiaceae family has been associated with longer survival in a cohort from Poland.

Remarkably, Asian cohorts have consistently reported null or even inverse associations. No difference in Akkermansia abundance was found between outcome groups in a Japanese cohort, and Akkermansia failed to predict survival in both univariate and multivariate analyses. A. muciniphila has been reported as enriched in non-responders in a Korean cohort, which is contrary to findings in Western populations. More recent large-scale Japanese studies have similarly found no association. It is hypothesized that these geographic discrepancies may be indicative of functional differences at the strain-level, as well as host-intrinsic factors including genetic ancestry, dietary context and region-specific environmental or lifestyle factors that establish the native gut microbiome milieu.

Some studies have highlighted that dietary patterns may contribute to geographic variation. It has been hypothesized that discrepancies in fiber intake and/or the consumption of fermented foods at the population level may serve to establish the composition of the gut microbiome prior to any intervention or observation. In consideration of the substrate-dependent functional plasticity of A. muciniphila, it is conceivable that the dietary context may exert influence on its metabolic output, including SCFAs and branched-chain fatty acids production, and thereby modulate immunomodulatory capacity across populations. However, the relationship between dietary fiber and ICB outcomes is not yet fully understood, with clinical observations suggesting potential benefit, while preclinical models have yielded inconsistent results. Further investigation may assist in elucidating whether population-level dietary patterns directly contribute to A. muciniphila-ICB associations.

Comparative shotgun metagenomic analyses provide preliminary evidence that lends support to the strain-level hypothesis, as evidenced by the comparison of Chinese and French cohorts. While both cohorts demonstrated associations between A. muciniphila and clinical benefits, the specific metagenomic strain markers differed—the strain marker found to be differentially abundant in patients with clinical benefits was igc0776 in the Chinese cohort but igc0118 in the French cohort. This finding suggests the hypothesis that functionally distinct strains may be differentially distributed across populations.

When performing strain-resolved analysis in a French cohort, 2 predominant A. muciniphila strain genome bins, SGB9226 and SGB9228 were both identified as associated with favorable outcomes in lung cancer. However, in an Australian cohort of rare cancers, discordant associations were reported, with SGB9226 linked to better ICB outcomes while SGB9228 showed the opposite association.

Intriguingly, the strain currently in clinical development (as discussed in the previous section, Microbiome modulation of ICB responsiveness: mechanisms and translational opportunities) corresponds to A. massiliensis p2261, which was previously classified as A. muciniphila SGB9228 and has been reclassified based on updated taxonomic identification. This is the same SGB9228 strain that exhibited directionally opposite associations across cancer types. This confusion arises from fluctuations caused by the instability of prokaryotic nomenclature. However, it also reflects the developmental trajectories of microbiome studies and ultimately underscores the importance of strain-resolved analyses.

These observations indicate functional heterogeneity at the strain-level and suggest tumor-specific modulation of host-microbe interactions may be a key factor. The implications for the development of biomarkers and the design of therapeutic interventions are discussed in conclusion.

SCFA-producing bacteria

The preceding examples underscore the importance of taxonomically fine-resolution approaches. However, when functionally important traits such as conserved surface proteins or metabolic capacities are shared across phylogenetically related taxa, the association signals may be diffused at species- and/or strain-level. Consequently, these patterns often become apparent only when integrated at higher taxonomic aggregations or through functional guilds. This principle is exemplified by SCFA-producing bacteria, where taxonomically diverse members of the Lachnospiraceae family share a common metabolic output that improves antitumor immunity. This family encompasses numerous genera that produce SCFAs, particularly butyrate, through fermentation of complex carbohydrates.

 Faecalibacterium has emerged as the most extensively studied member, demonstrating positive associations in multiple studies. In a large French cohort study, F. prausnitzii was found to be enriched in patients with prolonged survival and its abundance exhibited a correlation with beneficial Lachnospiraceae members in A. muciniphila-positive subgroups. Chinese cohorts have also reported an increase in Faecalibacterium among responders with direct mechanistic evidence demonstrating correlations between Faecalibacterium abundance, circulating CD8+ T cell frequencies, and elevated levels of fecal SCFA. Metagenomic pathway analyses consistently revealed glycolysis, carbohydrate metabolism and SCFA biosynthesis pathways in responders. Despite these consistent observations, Faecalibacterium abundance showed no difference between responders and non-responders in certain cohorts, suggesting potential context dependency.

Beyond Faecalibacterium, a number of other Lachnospiraceae genera showed analogous associations. Roseburia species, including Roseburia intestinalis, were associated with improved outcomes in French cohorts. Blautia species have been reported to exhibit positive associations in studies conducted in France, Japan and China. Dorea species, including Dorea formicigenerans and Dorea longicatena, were enriched in long-term survivors within A. muciniphila-positive subgroups. The presence of additional members associated with favorable outcomes included Eubacterium rectale and Eubacterium hallii, Coprococcus comes, Anaerostipes hadrus, Fusicatenibacter, and Butyricicoccus.

The consistency of associations across diverse members of Lachnospiraceae family, coupled with convergent metabolomic and pathway evidence linking these taxa to SCFA production and enhanced systemic immunity, suggests a functionally coherent mechanism underlying microbiome-ICB associations.

Ruminococcaceae and others

Members of the Ruminococcaceae family have demonstrated positive associations with ICB outcomes in multiple cohorts, particularly within Asian populations. For example, Ruminococcaceae UCG-13 was predicted to improve PFS and overall survival in a Japanese cohort, with these associations being sustained in multivariate Cox regression analysis. Subsequent multiple-ethnic studies have reported analogous findings in relation to the enrichment of Ruminococcaceae and Ruminococcus species among patients exhibiting favorable outcomes. However, specific members of Ruminococcaceae family have been observed to demonstrate contrasting associations in certain studies, thereby indicating potential genus- or species-level heterogeneity within this taxonomic group.

The genus Alistipes has been associated with favorable outcomes predominantly in Western cohorts. Alistipes indistinctus and Alistipes onderdonkii were found to be enriched in patients with durable clinical benefit in a Caucasian population, with A. onderdonkii abundance >0.018% predicting better PFS. Alistipes species have been reported among taxa associated with response in the French cohort. As reported in the relevant Chinese studies, Alistipes putredinis and Alistipes obesi were found to be enriched in responders. In addition, positive associations were demonstrated with other Alistipes species.

Additional taxa demonstrated notable associations. Parabacteroides species exhibited species-specific patterns, with Parabacteroides merdae being associated with favorable outcomes in multiple studies, while Parabacteroides distasonis was enriched in non-responders. The presence of methanogenic archaea, particularly Methanobrevibacter smithii, has been linked to a prolonged PFS, concomitant with an increase in methane production. Members of the Oscillospiraceae family showed divergent associations; Agathobacter was consistently associated with favorable outcomes, while Oscillibacter was associated with shorter survival. These findings, which vary in terms of replication and context-dependency, emphasize the critical importance of higher resolution and the need for rigorous cross-cohort validation in the development of microbiome biomarkers.

Taxa associated with poor outcomes

A number of bacterial families and genera have been linked to suboptimal ICB responses in various studies, although with varying levels of consistency and dependency on the context.

Veillonellaceae family, and more specifically Veillonella species, including Veillonella dispar, Veillonella atypica, and Veillonella parvula, have been predominantly associated with shorter PFS across multiple independent cohorts.

Members of Enterobacteriaceae family have been shown to be associated with adverse outcomes. Enterobacter species have been identified as a significant factor in the non-response and reduced survival observed in patients across multiple Asian cohorts. Furthermore, a negative correlation was identified between the presence of Escherichia coli and the survival period in A. muciniphila-positive patients.

The presence of pathogenic Clostridium species has been linked to unfavorable outcomes in a variety of contexts. The abundance of several Clostridium species including Clostridium symbosium, Clostridium innocuum, Clostridium scindens, Clostridium boltae, and Clostridium clostridioforme were elevated in patients with high A. muciniphila level (>4.799%) who exhibited poor outcomes. Additionally, Clostridium citroniae was found to be enriched in patients exhibiting non-durable clinical benefit.

The enrichment of oral-associated bacteria has been associated with adverse outcomes in a number of ways. It has been demonstrated that Streptococcus species, particularly Streptococcus salivarius and Streptococcus parasanguinis, and Rothia, were present in greater abundance among patients exhibiting progressive disease and diminished survival prospects. Mechanistic support for the detrimental role of oral bacteria is derived from studies of PPI use. The study demonstrated that exposure to PPIs, particularly in combination with antibiotics, promotes colonization of the gut by taxa derived from the oral cavity. Concurrently, it was observed that this exposure reduces the abundance of beneficial Lachnospiraceae family. This finding offers a plausible explanation for the effects of PPI on ICB outcomes.

These associations, encompassing oral-derived bacteria, potentially pathogenic Enterobacteriaceae, and pathogenic Clostridium species, imply that perturbations in the gut microbial composition dominated by such taxa—whether through exogenous disruptions such as medication exposure or representing underlying baseline states—reflect an ecological milieu that is inhospitable to effective immunotherapy responses.

Alpha diversity: an inconsistent biomarker

Alpha diversity, as measured by the Shannon index or related richness/evenness metrics, has been examined as a potential microbial biomarker for ICB outcomes. However, the results obtained are highly inconsistent. Early observational studies reported an association between higher alpha diversity and favorable clinical responses. Furthermore, increases in diversity after ICB treatment were noted among those patients who showed a positive response. However, a comparable number of subsequent studies found no significant differences between prognosis classifications across multiple cohorts. One study reported an inverse association, with higher baseline diversity correlating with shorter PFS.

This discrepancy may, at least in part, mirror the historical trajectory of microbiome research. Following the Human Microbiome Project, which established diversity loss as a hallmark of inflammatory bowel disease, alpha diversity became a default metric across disease contexts including cancer, often without rigorous validation of its relevance to the specific biological question being investigated. More fundamentally, alpha diversity quantifies community richness and evenness but provides no information about the functional capacities of constituent taxa. Indeed, 2 communities with identical alpha diversity may harbor entirely different metabolic and immuno-modulatory potentials. As demonstrated by the taxa examined in preceding sections, ICB response associations frequently correspond to particular effector functions, such as SCFA production, TLR ligand presentation, or inosine secretion, rather than to the overall community structure.

Consequently, the accumulated evidence suggests that alpha diversity alone is insufficient for predicting immunotherapy outcomes in lung cancer. It is recommended that future efforts place greater emphasis on the identification of specific taxa that have clearly defined functional roles. In addition, analysis of metabolic pathways should be given due consideration, as should the development of composite signatures that are capable of capturing mechanistically relevant microbial features, rather than averaging these features into a single metric.

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES

The gut-lung axis is a fundamental interface between the immune and metabolic systems, in which the ecology of the gastrointestinal tract exerts a significant influence on the immune response to pulmonary tumors and on the responsiveness to ICB. As synthesized in this review, converging evidence from mechanistic studies and clinical cohorts confirms that gut microbial composition is not merely a correlative biomarker but a functional determinant of treatment outcomes in lung cancer. Separately, the lung microbiome is increasingly recognized as a local modulator of airway and intratumoral immune tone, with potential implications for therapeutic responsiveness, although mechanistic and causal evidence is currently less mature compared with that for the gut microbiome.

Nevertheless, the translation of these findings into clinical practice is encumbered by significant challenges. The heterogeneity of observed associations across cohorts, exemplified by the divergent roles of A. muciniphila in different populations, underscores that genus- and/or species-level abundance alone is insufficient as a predictive biomarker. The introduction of layers of complexity, not fully captured in current analytical frameworks, is introduced by geographic, dietary, and host-genetic contexts. These inconsistencies are likely to reflect functional heterogeneity at the strain-level and tumor-specific modulation of host-microbe interactions, rather than failure of the underlying biological hypothesis.

In order to address these limitations, the field is progressively moving from descriptive taxonomic profiling towards functional mechanistic dissection. The fundamental question, therefore, is no longer “who is there,” but rather “what they are doing.” Recent advancements in metabolomic profiling and metagenomic pathway analyses have begun to bridge the gap between taxonomy and biological function, correlating specific taxa with metabolites and identifying pathway enrichments that distinguish responders from non-responders. These integrative approaches suggest that microbial functional capacity, rather than taxonomic composition alone, may drive differential immune responses.

Future research should benefit from several methodological advances. Strain-resolved metagenomics would be essential to distinguish functionally distinct variants within species that currently show contradictory associations. Multi-omics integration, encompassing the combination of metabolomics, metatranscriptomics and spatial immune profiling, among others, has been demonstrated to elucidate the precise molecular signals that mediate gut-lung communication. Furthermore, prospective validation with well-characterized microbial features across diverse populations and tumor types will be necessary to establish reproducible biomarkers and define context-dependent effects.

From a therapeutic standpoint, FMT has yielded pivotal causal evidence, establishing a link between gut microbial composition and antitumor immunity. This has also contributed to the development of next-generation interventions. The development of rationally designed LBPs and bacterial consortia offers the potential to deliver specific immuno-modulatory functions with greater consistency and mechanistic clarity. The objective of these approaches is not only to restore microbial diversity, but also to purposefully program the host immune system through defined molecular pathways.

Notwithstanding the mounting body of evidence in support of microbiome-based therapeutic interventions in cancer, multiple translational barriers persist in constraining their clinical implementation. These include the absence of clearly defined regulatory standards for the approval of microbiome-based products, persistent challenges in manufacturing and quality control, such as maintaining functional consistency across production batches, and safety considerations, particularly the risk of infection in immuno-compromised cancer patients. The successful integration of microbiome-based interventions into anticancer therapeutic strategies requires effectively addressing these regulatory, manufacturing, and safety challenges.

The realization of this vision will necessitate the sustained integration of microbiome science with clinical oncology, thereby transforming the gut microbiota from a predictive biomarker into an actionable therapeutic lever in precision immuno-oncology for lung cancer.
