# Chimeric antigen receptor cells as a tool for localized delivery of TNFα in solid cancer treatment.

## Abstract

Chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cells are effective in treating blood cancers but not solid cancers and can cause severe side effects, including cytokine release syndrome (CRS). One strategy to enhance the efficacy of CAR-T cells while avoiding CRS in cancer treatment is to deliver a single tumoricidal factor. TNFα is well known for triggering apoptosis signaling; however, it alone is not effective in treating cancers because it significantly increases the levels of inhibitor of apoptosis proteins (IAPs), a family of E3 ubiquitin ligases that block caspase-induced apoptosis. Thus, localized delivery of TNFα by targeting the tumors using the adoptive cells combined with an IAP antagonist, which degrades IAP proteins, could lead to improved outcomes in cancer treatment. This article reviews TNFα-induced apoptosis signaling pathway, outlines the principles for designing CAR-T cells, CAR-macrophages and CAR-dendritic cells expressing TNFα, used alone or in combination with IAP antagonists, and discusses the potential contraindications of IAP antagonists with several clinical used drugs for cancer treatment.

Introduction

Cancer is a major public health problem worldwide and the second leading cause of death. Approximately 2,001,140 new cases of invasive cancers are diagnosed, the equivalent of about 5,480 diagnoses each day, in the US in 2024. An estimated 611,720 people die from cancer in the US annually, with the top five causes of death being lung, colorectal, pancreatic, breast, and prostate cancer, resulting in 125,070, 53,010, 51,750, 42,250, and 35,250 deaths, respectively.

Most of solid malignancies can be surgically removed. The greatest challenging issue is the post-surgery cancer recurrence and metastasis to other organs, causing about 90% of cancer deaths. For decades, radiation therapy (RT) and chemotherapy (CT) are used before surgery to shrink the tumors and to improve the surgical outcomes and after surgery to prevent the recurrence and metastasis by killing the remaining cancers in the body.

It is estimated that approximately 30% of adult cancer patients would respond to conventional cytotoxic chemotherapy regimens. However, only 2% of all adult malignancies achieve a meaningful 5 year survival benefit from cytotoxic chemotherapy. Approximately 39% of 5-year cancer survivors in England receives radiotherapy as part of curative therapy including surgery and chemotherapy. However, radiotherapy only provides 5-year local control benefit in 10.4% of all cancer patients and 5-year overall survival benefit in 2.4%. There is an unmet need to develop a new approach to prevent and/or eliminate metastasis.

Cellular immunotherapy, in which patient’s own immune cells are engineered to specifically target and destroy cancer cells, would be a promising approach to treat cancer. The chimeric antigen receptor (CAR)-T cell therapy is the most successful cellular immunotherapy. Six CAR T-cells have been approved by FDA to treat blood cancers, including lymphomas, some forms of leukemia, and multiple myeloma since 2017.

CAR T-cell therapy continues to face significant challenges in the treatment of solid tumors, although some clinical trials have shown promising results. In a recent study, although CLDN18.2-targeting CAR-T cells achieved an overall response rate (ORR) of 38.8% and a disease control rate of 91.8% in patients with CLDN18.2-positive advanced gastrointestinal cancers, the overall survival was only 8.8 months. Additionally, cytokine release syndrome (CRS) occurred in 96.9% of patients, and gastric mucosal injuries were observed in 8 patients (8.2%). A systematic review of 15 prospective clinical trials involving 159 patients reported a median overall response rate (ORR) of 62% across various malignancies (range: 17–100%). In contrast, a separate meta-analysis of 22 studies including 262 patients found a pooled ORR of only 9%. Overall, median progression-free survival and overall survival using CAR-T cells to treat solid tumors were not reached in most of the trials.

The efficacy of the CAR-T cells in treating cancers depends on these engineered cells production of a wide variety of cytokines, including interleukin-IL-2 (IL-2), IL-4, TGF-β, interferon-γ, tumor necrosis factor-α (TNFα), IL-8, and IL-10, etc. The main strategy to augment the anti-tumor efficacy of CAR-T cells is to incorporate one or more intracellular signaling domains from various costimulatory protein receptors, such as CD28 and 41BB, to the cytoplasmic tail of the CAR, as did in 2nd and 3rd generation of CARs, to enhance TCR signaling. However, CAR-T cell therapies can cause severe side effects, one of the most frequent and serious which is cytokine release syndrome (CRS) due to the massive production of cytokines.

The fourth-generation CAR-T cells, known as TRUCKs (T cells redirected for antigen-unrestricted cytokine-initiated killing), are designed to deliver the transgenic cytokine, like IL-12, to the targeted tumor site. Once activated, the CARs on TRUCKs signal the cells to produce and release cytokines to tumor microenvironment (TME). The presence of the cytokine in the TME enhances the efficacy of CAR-T cell therapies. The Fifth-generation CAR-T cells, at the research and development phase, are introduced an additional intracellular domain, that is incorporated to a truncated cytokine receptor fragment like the IL-2 receptor beta chain. The advantage of the 5th-generation CAR-T cells includes, (1) the truncated cytokine receptor interacts with transcription factors like STAT3 to amplify the activation signal upon antigen recognition; (2) the extra domain promotes the development of memory T cells, which can persist in the body for a longer period, resulting in a more durable anti-tumor response and a better outcome while reducing the risk of certain side effects.

Although the 4th and 5th generation of CAR-T cells could improve their efficacy and limit the side-effects, an activation domain and the one or two co-stimulation domains (typically CD28 or 41BB) in the cytoplasmic signaling tail of the CAR are required to activate the CAR-T cells and thus release of multiple cytokines and the related side-effects could not be avoided. Particularly, the antitumor efficacy of the CAR-T cell therapy is dependent on prior lymphodepleting of the host with total body irradiation, high-dose chemotherapy, and/or post-infusion cytokine support. The toxicities associated with these essential conditioning regimens may limit the applicability of this approach for most patients who could otherwise benefit from tumor-targeted adoptive T-cell therapies.

We recently reported an inactive adoptive cell transfer (ACT) approach for localized delivery of a single tumoricidal factor in the treatment of solid cancers. The distinguishing feature of this ACT is that the intracellular signaling tail of the CAR lacks both activation and costimulatory domains, while overexpressing a single tumoricidal factor, thereby avoiding the release of multiple cytokines. The efficacy of the CAR-dendritic cells (CAR-DCs) expressing TNFα in combination with an IAP antagonist (SMAC memetic) to treat breast cancer have been tested in vivo. In this review and perspective article, we first introduce TNFα-induced apoptosis signaling pathway, explaining why an IAP antagonist is necessary for TNFα to induce apoptosis. We then outline the principles for engineering T cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells (DCs) to express both CAR and TNFα and discuss the potential contraindications between combining an IAP antagonist with the CAR cell-based localized delivery of TNFα and other therapeutic agents, including chemotherpies, antibiotics and bisphosphonates, for cancer treatment.

TNFα induces limited apoptosis due to the upregulation of inhibitor of apoptosis proteins and contributes to tumor progression by modulating the tumor microenvironment

TNFα was originally identified as a tumoricidal cytokine. Several clinical trials using recombinant TNFα to treat cancers were performed 40 years ago. However, few cancer patients responded to TNFα treatment and systemic toxicity, including fever, chills, anorexia and nausea, were observed in most patients treated with TNFα. When those studies were conducted, much of the biology, regulation, and target cells of TNFα were still unknown. Up to date, a large body of evidence has shown that TNFα not only induces cancer cell apoptosis but also upregulates inhibitor of apoptosis proteins (IAPs), thereby limiting its own pro-apoptotic effects. Additionally, TNFα modulates the tumor microenvironment to promote cancer progression and metastasis.

TNFα binds to two receptors, TNFR1 and TNFR2, to exert its biological functions. The binding of TNFα to TNFR1 triggers the apoptosis cascade by recruiting TNFR1-associated death domain (TRADD) and FAS-associated protein death domain (FADD) (Figure 1). These adaptor proteins interact with the death effector domain of caspase 8 to form a death-inducing signaling complex (DISC) to induce a cascade of events culminating in cell death by activating pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members, Bax and Bak, on the outer membrane of mitochondria (Figure 1). As a result, cytochrome c is released from mitochondria to the cytosol, where it forms apoptosome with apoptotic protease activating factor 1 (APAF1) and caspase 9 to induce apoptosis (Figure 1). 

TNFR2 lacks a death domain and therefore mediates TNFα-induced cancer progression and metastasis. A large body of evidence demonstrates that TNFα can induce epithelial – mesenchymal transition (EMT), thereby promoting cancer development. TNFα also directly stimulates cancer cell migration and invasion, supporting its role in promoting metastatic behavior. The pro- or anti-tumor effects of TNFα are likely dependent on its concentration. While high concentrations of TNFα can induce cancer cell death, low levels may instead promote tumor progression.

In fact, many cancer cells avoid cell death by over-expressing IAPs, which block caspases-induced apoptosis. IAPs are a family of E3 ubiquitin ligases, including eight members: NAIP (Birc1), cIAP1 (Birc2), cIAP2 (Birc3), X-IAP (Birc4), Survivin (Birc5), Bruce (Birc6), Livin (Birc7) and ILP-2 (Birc8) (Figure 1). The roles of cIAP1, cIAP2, XIAP and Survivin in blocking apoptosis have been well characterized, whereas the functions of other IAP family members remain less understood.

XIAP, cIAP1, cIAP2, and Survivin each contains one to three baculoviral IAP repeat (BIR) domains, which are ~70-amino acid zinc-binding motifs. These BIR domains bind directly to executioner caspases, such as caspase-3 and -7, blocking their active sites, thereby preventing substrate cleavage and inhibiting apoptosis. In addition, IAPs, especially XIAP, contains a RING finger domain at their C-terminus, which confers E3 ubiquitin ligase activity. Upon binding to caspases via their BIR domains, the RING finger domain facilitates the transfer of ubiquitin molecules from an E2 ubiquitin-conjugating enzyme to the caspase, leading to its ubiquitination. The ubiquitinated caspase is recognized by the 26S proteasome, resulting in caspase degradation to eliminate its apoptotic activity.

It was recently reported that the giant IAP, Birc6, exhibits dual E2 and E3 ubiquitin ligase activities, enabling it to restrict executioner caspases -3 and -7 by ubiquitinating caspases -3, -7, and -9, functioning exclusively with the noncanonical E1 enzyme, UBA6. The ubiquitin-like domain of Birc6 also binds the interacting region of LC3, thereby inducing the autophagic degradation of caspase 9 to inhibit apoptosis. Notably, second mitochondria-derived activator of caspase (SMAC) tightly binds Birc6 to release caspase, thereby triggering apoptosis. However, Birc6 also functions as a negative regulator of autophagy by cooperating with the ubiquitin-activating enzyme UBA to induce the monoubiquitination and proteasomal degradation of LC3B.

We reported that highly aggressive triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC) cells express elevated levels of cIAP1 and cIAP2 proteins. Exposure to TNFα further upregulates their expression, rendering TNFα ineffective in killing these cells. In contrast, less aggressive estrogen receptor (ER)-positive breast cancer cells express low basal levels of cIAP1 and cIAP2, which are not induced by TNFα. As a result, TNFα alone inhibits the proliferation of these ER-positive cells by inducing apoptosis. TNFα upregulates IAPs primarily by activating NF-κB, which directly binds to IAP gene promoters and enhances their transcription. In addition, lipopolysaccharide (LPS)-induced Myd88 directly associates with and stabilize cIAP1 and cIAP2. It remains unclear if and how TNFα upregulates IAPs through post-transcriptional regulation.

As a pro-inflammatory cytokine, TNFα impacts cancer fate largely by regulating tumor microenvironment. It upregulates VEGF and IL-6, promoting angiogenesis, and increases the expression of matrix metalloproteinases to degrade the extracellular matrix and facilitate metastasis. Binding of TNFα to TNFR1 activates inflammatory macrophages to produce high levels of VEGF-C, which in turn activates VEGFR3 to promote lymphangiogenesis, thereby contributing to lymphatic metastasis of the cancers. TNFR2 is highly expressed by regulatory T cells (Treg) and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and thus involves in TNFα-induced recruitment and differentiation of these immune suppressor cells, leading to evasion of tumor immune surveillance and contributing to the resistance to immune-check point inhibitor therapy.

In summary, TNFα upregulates IAPs to inhibit its own pro-apoptotic signaling, interpreting why TNFα alone is ineffective in treating certain cancers. Additionally, TNFα promotes tumor progression and metastasis by driving angiogenesis and lymphangiogenesis, and by inducing immunosuppressive cells.

Degradation of IAPs by an IAP antagonist enables TNFα to induce apoptosis

When cells undergo apoptosis, endogenous Second Mitochondria-Derived Activator of Caspase (SMAC), normally located in the mitochondrial intermembrane space, is released into the cytosol to degrade IAPs, resulting in caspase activation to trigger apoptosis. Many synthesized SMAC mimetics, also called IAP antagonists, have been developed, aiming to treat cancer by degrading IAPs. Some of them, such as LCL161, GDC-0917, AT-406 (xevinapant) and TL32711, have been studied in phase I or II clinical trials, but most patients did not benefit from them. Notably, an IAP antagonist alone, such as SM-164, xevinapant, or BV6, which effectively degrades cIAP1, cIAP2 and XIAP, does not induce significant cancer cell death, even at high concentrations (e.g., 1 μM). However, in the presence of a low level of TNFα (e.g., 1 ng/mL), these IAP antagonists can robustly induce apoptosis in cancer cells at much lower concentrations, ranging from as little as 3 to 100 nM. Our recent report shows that SM-164 plus TNFα, but not SM-164 alone administrated via IP injection, significantly reduces breast tumor growth in the bone and protects the bones from cancer-induced osteolysis. In contrast, adding TNFα antibody (Ab) to SM-164 therapy significantly increases breast tumor growth and appears to increase osteolysis compared to SM-164 alone. These findings strongly suggest that a combination of TNFα and an IAP antagonist could be an effective therapy for advanced cancers. However, TNFα can induce serious side effects in humans, including systemic shock and inflammatory reactions. The IAP antagonist in combination with TNFα kill a broad range of cancers, including breast cancer. Therefore, delivering TNFα to target tumors locally, combined with an IAP antagonist, administrated either orally or intravenously, would be an attractive approach to treat these cancers strongly and specifically with fewer side-effects.

Design of the vector expressing the CAR and TNFα

Like the traditional CAR, an inactive CAR (iCAR) is made up of four main components, an extracellular domain which is an antigen recognition domain (scFv), a hinge, a transmembrane domain for anchoring the antigen recognition domain to the cell membrane and an intracellular signaling domain (Figure 2). The key difference between iCARs and traditional CARs lies in their intracellular signaling domains. Traditional CARs incorporate one or two costimulatory domains, such as CD3ζ and 4-1BB, within the intracellular signaling region to enhance T cell activation and persistence, enabling them to destroy targeted cancer cells by releasing high levels of multiple cytokines. In contrast, iCARs retain an unmodified or truncated intracellular signaling domain that lacks both activation and costimulatory elements, while instead overexpressing a single tumoricidal factor – thereby avoiding the release of multiple cytokines. 

The receptor used to construct the CAR

To construct a CAR, the transmembrane domain of a cell receptor is typically used to anchor the extracellular antigen-recognition domain, which is a monoclonal antibody scFv that binds to an antigen on the cancer cell surface (Figure 2(A)). It is essential that the receptor is stably and highly expressed on the surface of the cell being engineered. If T cell is used for TNFα delivery, a receptor that is highly expressed on T cells, e.g. CD3, CD4, CD8, and CD28, can be used to construct the CAR. If Mac or dendritic cell (DC) is used to target the cancer, a receptor that is highly expressed in myeloid cells e.g. CD11c, CD14, CD16, CD18, CD33, CD66b (CEACAM8), CD86, etc., can be selected to construct the CAR.

Extracellular domain of the CAR

The extracellular domain of the cell receptor is replaced by a single-chain fragment variable (scFv) of the antibody against a tumor specific cell surface protein to construct the CAR (Figure 2(B)). The scFv is linked to the transmembrane domain of the receptor through a hinge or spacer (Figure 2(B)) like the traditional CAR. The hinge affects the overall performance of the CAR cells. An ideal hinge promotes antigen binding and synapse formation between the CAR and target antigen, and supply stability for efficient CAR expression and activity. It was reported that a highly flexible IgD hinge instead of a CD28 hinge results in better recognition of the sterically hindered MUC1 epitope in CAR-T cells. The stable CAR expression and antigen recognition are the most important factors for the successful of iCAR therapy. One example of a hinge sequence that can be used to construct the iCAR is “ESKYGPPCPSCP.” It remains unclear how the hinge modification affects this iCAR expression and antigen recognition.

The tumor surface proteins used for the inactive CAR targeting are similar to that of the traditional CAR, for example, C19 and BCMA (B-cell maturation antigen) on B cell malignancies, CD33 on acute myeloid leukemia. For solid tumors, tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) that are expressed on cancer cell surface can be targeted by the CAR. Examples of the TAAs include epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) on lung cancer, mesothelin on mesothelioma, pancreatic, ovarian, lung and breast cancers, mucin 1 (MUC1) expressed on various epithelial cancers including breast, pancreatic, lung, ovarian, bladder and gallbladder cancers, GD2 on neuroblastoma, prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) on prostate cancer, and CDH17 on gastrointestinal cancers. It should be noted that unlike blood cancers, solid tumors often lack a single, highly specific tumor antigen, making it difficult to find an ideal target for the adoptive cell therapy. In addition, normal cells also express low level of these tumor antigens, resulting in the potential for off-target. However, the off target of the adoptive cells would not cause severe side effects on the normal cells because normal cells express low level of IAP proteins and are not sensitive to the treatment of IAP antagonist.

The transmembrane and intracellular signaling domain of the CAR

The sequence of the transmembrane and intracellular signaling domain in the receptor can be kept unchanged (Figure 2(B)). No activation and costimulatory molecules will be incorporated into the cytoplasmic tail (Figure 2(B)) since activation of the engineered cells is not necessary. To further reduce the activation of the inactive CAR, the intracellular signaling tail can be truncated.

Construction of TNFα expressing vector

Human TNFα is initially synthesized as a precursor protein consisting of 233 amino acid residues. It is a type II membrane protein expressed on activated macrophages, lymphocytes and other cells. Cleavage of the precursor protein between residue 76 (alanine) and 77 (valine) by the metalloproteinase TNFα-converting enzyme (TACE) results in the release of the soluble form of TNFα, which consists of 157 amino acid residues. The soluble TNFα binds its Type 1 and 2 receptors to exert its biological activities.

A large body of evidence suggests that both soluble and transmembrane TNFα (tmTNFα) are functional. TmTNFα is sufficient to induce arthritis with synovial hyperplasia and inflammation. The cytotoxicity of tmTNFα can directly cause the lysis of cancer cells, which is enhanced by an IAP antagonist. Therefore, the CAR cells armored with tmTNFα would kill the cancer cells locally with lesser systemic side effects, especially when an IAP antagonist is given with it.

Given that solid tumors are often hypoxic, armored CAR-T cells may struggle to infiltrate the tumor, as hypoxia in the tumor microenvironment has been associated with reduced CD8+ T cell infiltration and impaired activity. This could be a key reason why CAR-T cell therapy fails to treat solid tumors. It is unclear if the soluble TNFα produced by the adoptive cells could penetrate into the tumor to exert its function. The effectiveness of soluble versus tmTNFα expressed by the armored CAR cells should be further studied in the future. It should also be further investigated whether accumulated armored CAR cells expressing tmTNFα could cause local inflammation in surrounding tissues after cancer treatment. It is expected that armored cells expressing soluble TNFα will cause fewer side effects, as TNFα entering circulation is cleared through receptor-mediated endocytosis in the liver, excreted by the kidneys, bound to specific receptors on various cell types, and underwent calcium-dependent proteasomal degradation in the serum. Nevertheless, TNFα levels in circulation should be monitored during and after therapy.

The vector used to construct CAR and TNFα genes

All FDA-approved CAR T-cell therapies use γ-retroviruses or lentiviruses, which efficiently transduce the CAR gene into the T cell genome, enabling the stable expression of the CAR. Both viruses belong to the retroviridae family. The difference between both vectors is that γ-retroviral vectors only infect dividing cells, whereas the lentiviral vectors infect both dividing and resting cells. Table 1 lists the viral vectors used in FDA-approved CAR-T cell production.

The CAR and TNFα cDNAs can be cloned into the same lentiviral vector, but are driven by two different promoters, shown in Figure 2(C). An alternative approach is to clone the CAR and TNFα cDNAs into two separate vectors and then combine both viruses in a 1:1 ratio to infect the cells, thereby generating the adoptive cells. However, combining two viruses would reduce the efficacy for generating the cells expressing both CAR and TNFα.

Candidate cells for localized delivery of TNFα

Like the traditional CAR, any hemopoietic cells, like T cells, natural killer cells (NKs), monocytes/macrophages (Macs) and dendritic cells (DC) that can migrate from the bloodstream into the tumor, can be infected with the lentivirus expressing both CAR and TNFα to generate the adoptive cells for the localized delivery of TNFα.

However, the abundance of the cell in blood, its ability to expand sufficiently in vitro, and its lifespan in the body will determine whether this cell is selected as a tool for localized delivery of TNFα. The adoptive cells bind the tumor cells through the CAR recognizing the tumor antigen, produce TNFα locally and kill the cancer cells when an IAP antagonist is given (Figure 3). 

T cell as a candidate for delivering TNFα by targeting tumors

T cells are the most abundant type of cell in the peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), accounting for about 70% of PBMCs. Particularly, T cells are expanded and grown in suspension, making them easy to harvest. Importantly, CAR-T cells have been successfully used to treat several blood cancers. Thus, T-cell as a tool for delivering TNFα have great potential for rapid transition into clinical applications.

Within T cells, CD4+ helper T cells usually make up a larger percentage than CD8+ cytotoxic T cells, roughly in a 2:1 ratio. The lifespan of T cells varies depending on their type and function. Naive T cells, newly generated in the thymus, have a long lifespan, typically lasting for years. Memory T cells, generated after an immune response, can live for decades. Both CD4+ and CD8+ memory T cells have an average lifespan of around 150–160 days. Effector (helper and cytotoxic) T cells are short-lived and typically die within a few weeks after an immune response. Considering that the therapeutic course of TNFα in combination with an IAP antagonist is about 3 weeks, effector T cells-either CD4+ helper or CD8+ cytotoxic T cells would be ideal for generating these inactive CAR-T cells, like conventional ones.

Typically, a single dose of hundred million of CAR-T cells (1–3 million/kg) is infused to a patient for the treatment of cancer. The CAR-T cell manufacturing process typically takes 3–6 weeks, including the isolation of CD4+ helper or CD8+ cytotoxic T cells, T cell activation and expansion, genetic modification to induce expression of CAR, selection and expansion of modified T-cells ex vivo, followed by final formulation, cryopreservation, quality control analysis, and transfer to a patient. One challenge is that disease progression in about one-third of patients during the manufacturing process may prevent them from safely proceeding to CAR-T cell therapy. Given that the inactive CAR-T cells are engineered to express high levels of TNFα, it is worth investigating whether a smaller infusion of these engineered cells, combined with an IAP antagonist, could effectively treat cancer without the need for prior lymphodepletion of the host.

One issue is that T cells are only found in the tumor stroma or intraepithelial lining tissue surrounding the solid tumors and cannot infiltrate deep within the tumor mass. This could be a reason why CAR-T cells fail to treat solid cancers. It is not recommended to engineer the T cells to express tmTNFα for the treatment of solid cancer since cell-cell interaction is required for tmTNFα expressing cells to exert their function while the CAR-T cells have limited potential to penetrate inside the solid tumors. However, we still do not know if the soluble form TNFα secreted by the inactive CAR-T cells can penetrate inside and kill the tumor effectively when an IAP antagonist is given.

Macrophage as a tool for localized delivery of TNFα by targeting tumors

Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) are not only major constituents of tumor stroma in many solid tumors but also infiltrate deep inside the tumors. The proportion of macrophages (Macs) in solid tumors varies significantly depending on the cancer type, stage, tumor microenvironment, and the detection method used. One study reported that CD163-positive Macs account for approximately 7% of the tumor area in pancreatic, lung, and gallbladder cancers. However, other review articles suggest that TAMs can comprise up to 50% of the total tumor mass. Particularly, the CAR-Macrophages (CAR-Macs) can penetrate inside the metastatic breast tumors in an animal model. Thus, CAR-Macs delivery of TNFα in combination with an IAP antagonist would be a promising approach to treat cancer. Any cancer that is sensitive to IAP antagonist – induced apoptosis in the presence of TNFα can potentially be treated with this approach. Such solid tumors include breast, lung, pancreatic, ovarian, colorectal, prostate and gallbladder.

The procedure for manufacturing CAR-Macs is relatively simple. The white blood cells (WBCs) or peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), isolated from human whole blood by red blood cell lysis or density gradient centrifugation, respectively, can be directly cultured with M-CSF to generate Macs in vitro. These Macs attach the dish tightly to grow and thus viral particles carrying the CAR and TNFα genes can be directly added to culture medium to generate CAR-Macs. The whole procedure takes about 10–12 days. However, yielding an adequate number of CAR-Macs remains a challenge because only 10–20% of PBMCs are monocytes, which make up 2–8% of WBCs in a healthy individual. These Macs are likely directly differentiated from the suspended monocytes and have poor potential for further division. Based on our experience, WBCs from 10 ml fresh blood from an adult individual yield about 2x10 Macs after one week of culture using M-CSF.15 On the average, 80 mL of whole blood yields approximately 10–20 million CAR-Macs while 100 million CAR-T cells can be generated. As discussed, the CAR-Macs highly express TNFα, and a smaller number of these CAR-Macs would be sufficient to target and kill the cancers when combined with an IAP antagonist. In particular, it is possible to inject a much small number of CAR-Macs into a solid tumor locally while achieving a better therapeutic outcome. If more CAR-Macs are needed, bone marrow cells, harvested via biopsy, is recommended for Mac generation and engineering, provided the patient’s condition permits. One challenging issue is that M-CSF-induced Macs are tightly attached to the culture dishes, making them difficult to harvest using trypsin digestion medium.

Dendritic cell (DC) as an ideal tool for delivering TNFα locally by targeting tumors

DCs and Macs are derived from the same Mac/dendritic progenitor cells (MDPs). A few DCs are present in the peripheral blood. However, treatment of blood monocytes with GM-CSF alone or in combination with IL-4 induces formation of monocytic DCs (moDCs). The DCs grown on the dishes can be directly infected with the lentivirus to generate CAR-DCs expressing secreted TNFα. Importantly, the CAR-DCs are more easily detached from cultured dishes than CAR-Macs after trypsin digestion.

The human PBMCs yield similar number of DCs, induced by M-CSF and GM-CSF, to that of Macs induced by M-CSF alone. Ten ml peripheral blood can yield 1–2 million CAR-DCs in about 10 days. The number of CAR-DCs yielded is significantly smaller than that of CAR-T cells when using the same volume of blood. However, like CAR-Macs, smaller number of CAR-DCs would be injected into a solid tumor locally to achieve a better outcome as they produce high level of TNFα.

We recently reported Muc1-directed CAR-DCs expressing TNFα (M-CAR-DCsTNF), which were produced by co-infecting the DCs cultured from human blood PBMCs with lentivirus encoding a secreted TNFα and a CAR targeting Mucin-1, a glycoprotein that is over-expressed on the surface of a broad range of epithelial cell-originated cancers, including breast, pancreatic, and lung cancer. The crude lentiviral infection resulted in approximately 15% of DCs expressing both TNFα and CAR. The CM from cultured M-CAR-DCsTNF containing high levels of human TNFα was sufficient to effectively kill MDA-MB-231 cells when supplemented with 3 nM of SM-164, and cell death was completely blocked by the anti-TNFα antibody. In contrast, CM from M-CAR-DCs (without expressing TNFα) did not enhance SM164-induced cell death. Importantly, IV injected M-CAR-DCsTNF recognized the breast tumor in the bone of NSG mice, and one time local injection of M-CAR-DCsTNF around the tumor combined with SM-164, but not SM-164 alone, inhibited the growth of patient-derived triple negative breast cancer in NSG mice. However, it is still not known if M-CAR-DCsTNF can penetrate inside the solid tumors and how long they can survive and produce TNFα in the tumor. We believe that using purified lentivirus to improve the efficacy of the DCs expressing both the CAR and TNFα will enhance the therapeutic outcome in cancer treatment.

As the most important antigen-presenting cells, DCs present tumor-associated antigens to T cells, which can launch an immune attack against the cancer cells. TNFα produced by CAR-DCs can also trigger signaling pathways on T cells to promote their activation, proliferation, and differentiation into effector cells to further enhance their effects for cancer treatment. However, some populations of DCs infiltrated into the tumors can suppress T cell immunity to promote tumor growth. It will be required to further investigate how the DCs expressing TNFα regulate T cell immunity to impact the cancer immune therapy.

Regimens, synergy and potential contraindications between IAP antagonists and other therapeutic agents

TNFα has limited effect to treat cancer. Thus, administration of CAR-DCs, -Macs or -T cells expressing TNFα alone will also have limited effect to treat cancer. Our recent report indicates that the fate of cancers in response to TNFα treatment is determined by cancer cells expressed IAP proteins. TNFα alone does not significantly affect the growth of MDA-MB-231 cells expressing high level of IAP proteins while markedly inhibits the growth of MCF7 breast cancer cells expressing low level of and non-inducible IAP proteins. Thus, administration of the adoptive cells expressing TNFα alone is possible to inhibit the growth of cancers with low level of and nonfunctional IAP proteins. Testing of the protein expression level, copy number of gene expression and mutation of the IAP proteins may predict the therapeutic response.

The adoptive cells producing TNFα is recommended to combine with an IAP antagonist routinely for the therapy of cancers, not matter they express high or low level of IAP proteins. The adoptive cells producing TNFα plus an IAP antagonist strongly induce the apoptosis of the cancers expressing high level of IAP proteins as the growth of these cancer cells depends on IAP proteins. TNFα alone inhibits the growth of cancers expressing low level of and non-inducible IAP proteins and a low dose of TNFα plus an IAP antagonist induces the apoptosis of these cancer cells with mild-middle effectively. The cancers with low level of and non-inducible IAP proteins may also achieve good therapeutic outcome in response to the combination treatment.

Many IAP antagonists have been confirmed as safe through clinical trials. Any of these can be considered to combine with the adoptive cell therapy as long as its concentration in vivo can reach the threshold that kills the cancer cells when a low dose TNFα present. The IAP antagonist is recommended to be administered 1–2 times daily either orally (such as Xevinapant and LCL161) or intravenously (like birinapant) because the plasma half-life of most IAP antagonists is approximately 4–7 hours in humans. The poor outcomes in some clinical trials may be attributed to the fact that these IAP antagonists were administered weekly. However, short plasma half-life of the IAP antagonists may not completely explain the poor outcome. Dailly oral Xevinapant (AT-406) initially reduced the risk of death by 51% compared to the standard of care in treating head and neck cancer when it was combined with chemotherapies. However, the Phase III randomized study was recently announced discontinuation as the trial would be unlikely to meet its primary objective of prolonging event-free survival. It is required to identify the factors that limit the effect of IAP antagonists in cancer treatment to guide future clinical trials.

Multidrug resistance (MDR) continues to be a major hurdle in the treatment of cancers. An IAP antagonist does not induce MDR while it cannot kill the cancer cells with MDR. Thus, the disappointing outcome with Xevinapant combined with chemo-radio in treating cancer was anticipated. We recommend that chemoradiotherapy should be avoided before and during the use of an IAP antagonist, not only because the IAP antagonist is ineffective in killing cancer cells with MDR induced by chemoradiotherapy, but also because chemoradiotherapy damages rapidly dividing host cells in the tumor microenvironment, leading to reduced TNFα production.

The drugs that suppress TNFα production by the host cells in the TME should also be avoided during IAP antagonist therapy. One such drug is bisphosphonates, including zoledronic acid and alendronate, which are currently used to treat cancer with bone metastasis. These drugs reduce the release of TNFα from monocytes and macrophages because they induce apoptosis of macrophages and DCs and impair their polarization. Another class of drugs that should be avoided during IAP antagonist therapy is antibiotics, which kills Gram-negative bacteria to decrease the amount of LPS being released into the bloodstream. LPS polarizes TAMs and other immune cells to produce TNFα in TME. Thus, antibiotics that kill Gram-negative bacteria could attenuate the effectiveness of an IAP antagonist in cancer treatment. Although antibiotics do not interfere with the ability of administrated adoptive cells to produce TNFα, we still recommend avoiding their use during cancer therapy involving the adoptive cells combined with an IAP antagonist, as it remains unclear whether the adoptive cells effectively target all the cancer cells in the tumor tissues in the body.

Concluding remarks and perspectives

The engineering of cells such as T cells, macrophages, and DCs to express TNFα and an inactive CAR that recognizes and associates with a tumor antigen for delivering TNFα by targeting tumors would be a promising approach to treat both blood and solid cancers when combined with an IAP antagonist that degrades IAP proteins. An important feature of this inactive CAR is that its intracellular tail lacks the activation and costimulatory domains, functioning solely as a tool to recognize and attach to cancer cells while locally producing TNFα. Thus, severe side-effects related to multiple cytokines release such as CRS can be avoided. In particular, several clinically used TNFα inhibitors are available to mitigate the side-effects related to TNFα, if present. A broad range of solid cancers, including breast, lung, pancreatic, ovarian, colorectal, prostate and gallbladder cancers, can be treated with this localized delivery of TNFα approach combining an IAP antagonist as they rapidly and strongly induce apoptosis of these cancer cells. However, it is still unclear which type of TNFα, soluble or transmembrane, produced by the adoptive cells leads to better outcomes in cancer treatment. T cells have poor potential to penetrate inside the solid tumors, and the macrophage could be a better choice for delivering TNFα by targeting tumors as up to 50% of the tumor mass is composed of TAMs. However, M-CSF-induced macrophages from peripheral blood are tightly attached to the culture dishes and are difficult to be harvested. DCs generated by GM-CSF and M-CSF from blood are more easily detached after trypsin treatment, making them an ideal tool for delivering TNFα by targeting the tumors. One issue with the engineered DCs is that they are produced in much lower numbers than T cells from the same volume of blood. However, a smaller number of these adoptive cells could effectively kill cancer cells when combined with an IAP antagonist, given that this combination is known to strongly kill the cancer cells. One effective solution is to directly inject the smaller number of CAR-DCs expressing TNFα into the solid tumors, eliminating the need for prior lymphodepleting chemotherapy and further minimizing potential side-effects. It also remains unclear whether the hypoxic immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, along with the lack of specific antigen targets, limits the efficacy of iCAR-DCs or macrophages, similar to the challenges faced by traditional CAR-T cells, in treating solid tumors.

In brief, using inactive CAR-adoptive cells to deliver TNFα to tumors represents a novel concept and a promising new therapeutic approach for treating solid cancers. Further studies are needed to optimize the iCAR design, improve the manufacturing process, refine indication selection, and develop strategies for managing potential side effects.
