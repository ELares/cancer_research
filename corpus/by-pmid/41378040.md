# Combination immunotherapy in Japanese patients with advanced renal cell carcinoma: bridging gaps between clinical trials, real-world evidence, and the potential value of adverse events-a narrative review.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND AND OBJECTIVE**: Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) have transformed the treatment of advanced renal cell carcinoma (aRCC), leading to the adoption of combination regimens. Either dual ICI regimens or ICI plus tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) are now established as the first-line standard treatment. While phase III trials have demonstrated significant survival benefits of ICI combination therapy over TKI monotherapy, real-world reports, particularly from Japan, have revealed variable outcomes influenced by patient and tumor characteristics. Treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs), including immune-related adverse events (irAEs), are increasingly recognized not only as safety concerns but also as potential predictive biomarkers. In this review, we aimed to compare the clinical trials and real-world outcomes of combination immunotherapy with a focus on Japanese patients, and to examine the prognostic significance of TRAEs.

**METHODS**: A narrative literature review was conducted using PubMed and Scopus for English-language studies published between April 2018 and August 2025. Search terms included the following MeSH terms: kidney neoplasm, carcinoma, renal cell, immunotherapy, nivolumab, ipilimumab, pembrolizumab, avelumab, axitinib, cabozantinib, lenvatinib, and biomarkers; and free-text terms: advanced, combination immunotherapy, irAEs, and TRAEs. Phase III trials and retrospective/prospective real-world studies were included, and abstracts and case reports were excluded.

**KEY CONTENT AND FINDINGS**: Nivolumab plus ipilimumab (NIVO + IPI) and various ICI + TKI regimens (avelumab + axitinib, pembrolizumab + axitinib, nivolumab + cabozantinib, pembrolizumab + lenvatinib) have shown superior efficacy to sunitinib in pivotal trials. Real-world Japanese cohorts often include older patients with poor performance status and non-clear cell RCC (ncc-RCC), leading to shorter overall survival (OS) and progression-free survival (PFS), despite comparable objective response rates (ORRs) to trials. Several studies on patients treated with NIVO + IPI have demonstrated that TRAEs/irAEs are associated with improved ORR and OS, with multivariate analyses identifying them as independent, favorable prognostic factors. However, this correlation was less consistent in ICI + TKI regimens, in which toxicity may be derived from either component.

**CONCLUSIONS**: Combination immunotherapy offers substantial benefits for aRCC; however, real-world outcomes can differ from trial data owing to patient heterogeneity. TRAEs show promise as prognostic markers in NIVO + IPI but require further validation in ICI + TKI. Prospective multicenter registries with standardized adverse event reporting, coupled with translational studies, are needed to refine regimen selection and personalized therapy.

Introduction

Renal cell carcinoma (RCC) accounts for approximately 2% of all malignant neoplasms worldwide, and its incidence has exhibited a consistent upward trend over the past decades. The 5-year cancer-specific survival rate for patients with early-stage RCC is approximately 90%, indicating a favorable prognosis. In contrast, in patients with advanced RCC (aRCC), the 5-year cancer-specific survival rate is particularly poor, ranging from 12% to 14%. With the advent of immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), the treatment of aRCC has changed significantly, and combination immunotherapy has become the standard of care.

In Japan, among the ICI combination therapies, nivolumab plus ipilimumab (NIVO + IPI) is approved for patients classified as having intermediate- or poor-risk disease according to the International Metastatic RCC Database Consortium (IMDC) risk classification. In addition, ICI plus tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) combination therapies, including avelumab plus axitinib (AVE + AXI), pembrolizumab plus axitinib (PEM + AXI), nivolumab plus cabozantinib (NIVO + CABO), and pembrolizumab plus lenvatinib (PEM + LEN), can be administered regardless of the IMDC risk classification.

The present review is specifically focused on Japanese patients with RCC. The rationale behind our current focus is based on two key findings. Recent genomic studies have revealed that Japanese patients with RCC frequently exhibit unique mutation signatures that are not typically observed in other populations. Additionally, several reports have indicated variations in treatment outcomes between racial groups.Additionally, Japan’s healthcare system may have a significant impact on clinical outcomes. This system guarantees access to advanced medical institutions and enables opportunities for treatment and meticulous follow-up regarding treatment-related adverse events (TRAEs). Conversely, the efficacy and safety of combination immunotherapy in clinical practice may also be influenced by physician discretion regarding treatment continuation or discontinuation, along with patient preferences. The complex interaction of these factors is thought to result in a clinical course unique to Japanese patients with RCC.

In recent years, results from clinical trials with extended follow-up periods have demonstrated that the benefits of combination immunotherapy over conventional TKI therapy persist even with longer observation periods. Several reports have been published on the real-world efficacy and safety of combination immunotherapy for aRCC in Japanese patients. However, discrepancies have emerged between the promising outcomes observed in controlled clinical trials and the often-heterogeneous results observed in real-world clinical practice, particularly in diverse patient populations, including those in Japan. These differences may arise from factors such as patient selection, comorbidities, or variations in treatment management.

Additionally, TRAEs, particularly immune-related adverse events (irAEs), which accompany ICI-based therapies, have gained attention not only as safety concerns, but also as potential predictive biomarkers of therapeutic response. Understanding the dual role of TRAEs in immune activation and influencing clinical outcomes may enhance patient management and personalized treatment strategies.

This narrative review aimed to explore the current evidence on the efficacy and safety of combination immunotherapy in aRCC, examined the gaps between clinical trial data and real-world experience, and discuss the emerging role of TRAEs as predictive biomarkers in this setting. By integrating clinical trial findings with real-world observations, especially in Japanese cohorts, this review sought to provide comprehensive insights into optimizing immunotherapeutic approaches for aRCC. We present this article in accordance with the Narrative Review reporting checklist (available at https://tcr.amegroups.com/article/view/10.21037/tcr-2025-1859/rc).

Methods

The Search strategy summary is presented in Table 1. For this narrative review, we searched the literature through August 2025 using the PubMed and Scopus online databases. The eligible references included peer-reviewed, English-language articles published between April 1, 2018 and August 1, 2025, and contained the following MeSH terms: kidney neoplasm, carcinoma, renal cell, immunotherapy, nivolumab, ipilimumab, pembrolizumab, avelumab, axitinib, cabozantinib, lenvatinib, and biomarkers; and free text: advanced, combination immunotherapy, irAEs, and TRAEs. Meeting abstracts, case reports, studies with insufficient data, and duplicate records were excluded. Screening and data extraction were performed independently by two authors (K.I. and T.K.). The two authors participated in screening the literature, data extraction, and quality assessment of the articles.

Overview of combination immunotherapies in aRCC

Several combination immunotherapies have become standard first-line treatment options for aRCC, each supported by phase III randomized controlled trials, demonstrating superiority over sunitinib monotherapy in terms of overall survival (OS), progression-free survival (PFS), and objective response rate (ORR) (Table 2).

NIVO + IPI is the only ICI combination regimen currently approved in Japan. In the CheckMate 214 trial for IMDC intermediate- or poor-risk patients eligible for the approved regimen in Japan, NIVO + IPI achieved significantly longer survival than sunitinib after a median follow-up of 96 months. Median OS was 46.7 months with NIVO + IPI vs. 26.0 months with sunitinib [hazard ratio (HR) 0.69, 95% confidence interval (CI): 0.59–0.81], and median PFS was 12.4 vs. 8.5 months (HR 0.73, 95% CI: 0.61–0.87). The ORR was 42.4% vs. 27.5% with NIVO + IPI versus sunitinib, and complete response was achieved in 11.8% vs. 2.6% of patients in respective treatment arms. The greatest advantage of NIVO + IPI is its ability to induce a durable response. Among intermediate- and poor-risk patients, the median duration of response (DOR) was significantly longer in NIVO + IPI compared with sunitinib (82.8 vs. 19.8 months; HR 0.48, 95% CI: 0.33–0.69).

In Japan, four ICI + TKI regimens are currently available: AVE + AXI, PEM + AXI, NIVO + CABO, and PEM + LEN—and these regimens can be administered regardless of the IMDC risk classification. In the JAVELIN Renal 101, KEYNOTE-426, CheckMate 9ER, and CLEAR trials, the ORR for ICI + TKI was higher and the progressive disease rates were lower than those of NIVO + IPI, suggesting that ICI + TKI regimens may have greater antitumor efficacy than NIVO + IPI.

The combination of ICI with TKI leverages their complementary mechanisms of action. ICIs restore and activate antitumor immune responses by blocking immune checkpoints in T-cells, thereby promoting sustained tumor control. Whereas TKIs inhibit receptor tyrosine kinases—primarily vascular endothelial growth factor receptors—to suppress tumor angiogenesis and exert direct antitumor effects. When used in combination, the addition of a TKI to ICI therapy is thought to result in higher response rates compared with ICI monotherapy or ICI combination regimens. Moreover, the anti-angiogenic activity of TKIs can modulate the tumor microenvironment by alleviating local immunosuppression. This vascular normalization can enhance immune cell infiltration into the tumor, thereby amplifying immune activation achieved by ICIs. Thus, TKIs and ICIs may act synergistically, with TKIs improving the immunological conditions within the tumor and ICIs driving T-cell–mediated tumor killing. However, the prolonged DOR and durable responses observed with NIVO + IPI are unfortunately not seen with ICI + TKI.

Regarding safety, nearly all patients experienced TRAEs in all the clinical trials. However, the incidence of grade ≥3 TRAEs was higher with ICI + TKI compared with NIVO + IPI. In contrast, the proportion of patients requiring high-dose steroids for the management of irAEs was 35% with NIVO + IPI and 10–20% with ICI + TKI. In addition to irAEs caused by the ICI component in ICI + TKI regimens, TKI-specific adverse events also occur, which likely contribute to the higher incidence of grade ≥3 TRAEs in ICI + TKI compared with NIVO + IPI regimens.

Real-world evidence and discrepancies with clinical trials

Table 3 summarizes real-world reports of combination immunotherapy in Japanese patients with aRCC. When comparing these real-world data from Japanese patients with aRCC with the results of clinical trials, several differences were apparent. First, real-world data on Japanese patients with aRCC include a higher proportion of older adult patients, those with poor performance status, and those classified as poor risk according to the IMDC risk classification, compared to clinical trials. Second, while clinical trials primarily enrolled patients with clear cell RCC, in Japan, combination immunotherapy is administered for aRCC regardless of the histological subtype and therefore also includes patients with non-clear cell RCC (ncc-RCC). Ncc-RCC is generally associated with a poorer prognosis than the clear cell subtype. Collectively, these factors suggest that real-world reports may include a greater proportion of patients with poorer prognoses than those represented in clinical trials.

Moreover, the median follow-up period was short, ranging from 7 to 39.7 months. In NIVO + IPI, the ORR ranged from 34.3% to 57.0%, which is comparable to or greater than the results reported in clinical trials. Even in reports limited to patients with ncc-RCC, ORRs of 30.0% to 34.6% have been observed, showing comparable outcomes. In contrast, the median OS and PFS in real-world settings tended to be shorter than those in clinical trials, with the possibility of being particularly short in ncc-RCC. Based on these results, the indications for the use of NIVO + IPI in patients with ncc-RCC may require careful consideration. Furthermore, in NIVO + IPI, the DOR is an important factor, and future data from studies reporting DOR with longer follow-up periods are eagerly anticipated. Regarding TRAEs, the incidence of any-grade TRAEs in real-world NIVO + IPI data was 64.0–77.8%, which was lower than that reported in clinical trials, whereas the incidence of grade ≥3 TRAEs was 15.0–59.0%, which was comparable. This may be partly explained by the possibility that mild TRAEs are overlooked in real-world clinical practice.

Real-world reports on Japanese patient data for ICI + TKI are fewer than those for NIVO + IPI. In addition, because there are multiple ICI + TKI regimens, some reports have included data from more than one regimen. Regarding ORR and median PFS, the results were 53.3–68.6% and 17.1–47.8 months, respectively, which were comparable to those reported in clinical trials. However, in reports limited to ncc-RCC, the ORR was 30.0–41.6% and the median PFS was 8.8–9.7 months, indicating a poor prognosis similar to that observed with NIVO + IPI. Combination immunotherapy in patients with ncc-RCC appears to be associated with a worse prognosis than clinical trial results. In real-world practice, the incidence of TRAEs with ICI + TKI therapy is comparable to or lower than that reported in clinical trials. Even for PEM + LEN, which showed grade ≥3 TRAEs in 84.9% of patients in the CLEAR trial, the incidence was 66.0% in real-world reports, remaining within an acceptable range.

TRAEs and irAEs

Combination immunotherapy for aRCC incorporates ICIs, which can cause distinct adverse effects that are directly related to their mechanisms of action. ICIs function by blocking inhibitory pathways such as the cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated protein 4 and programmed cell death 1/programmed death ligand 1 axis, which normally suppress T-cell activity to prevent excessive immune responses and autoimmunity. The inhibition of these pathways promotes the activation and proliferation of T-cells, thereby enhancing antitumor immune responses. However, this immune activation is not tumor-specific and can extend into normal tissues, resulting in irAEs.

The development of irAEs is thought to involve multiple interconnected mechanisms. First, the loss of peripheral immune tolerance occurs when checkpoint molecule blockade lowers the activation threshold for self-reactive T-cells, enabling the activation of clones that would otherwise remain unresponsive to self-antigens. Second, the expansion of autoreactive T-cells is promoted by the proliferation of T cell clones that recognize normal tissue antigens, leading to inflammation and tissue damage. Third, cross-reactivity can occur when T-cells targeting tumor antigens recognize structurally similar antigens expressed in healthy tissues, causing off-target immune attacks. Fourth, an inflammatory cascade is induced through cytokine release and recruitment of additional immune cell populations, which amplify tissue inflammation. In some patients, immune activation results in the production of autoantibodies that contribute to tissue injury.

Clinically, irAEs can involve virtually any organ system, most commonly the skin, endocrine glands (including the thyroid and pituitary glands), gastrointestinal tract, liver, and lungs. The severity of these events ranges from mild to potentially life-threatening; in severe cases, they may lead to treatment discontinuation or even death. Although traditionally regarded solely as a safety concern, emerging evidence suggests that TRAEs, including irAEs, may also have prognostic implications for patients receiving combination immunotherapy.

Table 4 summarizes the reports that evaluated the relationship between TRAEs and patient prognosis. Ikeda et al. and Ishihara et al. reported that, in patients treated with NIVO + IPI the occurrence of TRAEs was a predictive factor for a favorable ORR. In addition, Ishihara et al. and Yasuda et al. found that TRAEs were independent predictive factors for favorable OS in multivariate analyses. However, although Ishihara et al. reported that TRAEs were predictive factors for ORR, OS, and PFS in patients receiving NIVO + IPI, the same association was not observed in patients treated with ICI + TKI. Given the limited reports on ICI + TKI regimens, further data are needed in the future; moreover, it may also be necessary to clarify whether observed TRAEs are attributable to ICI or TKI component.

Reports have also indicated that the severity of irAEs can influence prognosis. Tasaki et al. demonstrated that among Japanese patients with aRCC treated with NIVO + IPI, those who experienced severe irAEs had better OS than patients without irAEs, but worse survival than those who experienced non-severe irAEs. This finding emphasizes that the severity of TRAEs or irAEs may be an important factor that influences clinical outcomes.

Strengths and limitations

This review has several notable strengths. First, it provides a comprehensive synthesis of both pivotal clinical trial data and real-world evidence, focusing particularly on Japanese patient cohorts who are often underrepresented in global oncology research. By integrating these perspectives, the review highlights key discrepancies between trial outcomes and real-world practice, thereby offering clinically relevant insights for daily management of patients with aRCC. Second, the review uniquely emphasizes the emerging role of TRAEs, including irAEs, not only as safety considerations but also as potential biomarkers of treatment efficacy. This perspective adds translational and prognostic value beyond conventional efficacy summaries. Third, by covering multiple combination strategies (NIVO + IPI and various ICI + TKI regimens), the review allows balanced comparisons across treatment modalities, shedding light on their complementary benefits and limitations.

While this study provides a comprehensive synthesis of the current evidence, this review is narrative and thus susceptible to selection bias. The real-world data cited herein were predominantly retrospective with heterogeneous patient populations, outcome definitions, and TRAEs grading systems, limiting direct comparability. Furthermore, the follow-up duration in several studies was short, precluding robust OS analyses. Prospective, multicenter, real-world registries with standardized adverse events reporting and long-term follow-ups are required to clarify the true effectiveness and safety profiles of the aRCC treatments. Translational research integrating clinical outcomes, immune profiling, and genomic data could illuminate the predictive factors for both efficacy and toxicity. Ultimately, harmonizing trial data with high-quality real-world evidence is critical for refining regimen selection and maximizing benefits for diverse patient populations.

Conclusions

Combination immunotherapy regimens have significantly advanced aRCC treatment. Clinical trials have demonstrated substantial improvements in OS, PFS, and ORR compared to prior standard therapies. However, discrepancies exist between clinical trial outcomes and real-world data, especially in Japanese patient populations characterized by greater heterogeneity in age, performance status, and tumor histology, including a higher proportion of ncc-RCCs, highlighting the challenges in generalizing the trial results to routine clinical practice.

TRAEs, including irAEs, have emerged not only as critical factors for safety monitoring but also as potential predictive biomarkers of therapeutic benefits. Several studies, particularly those involving NIVO + IPI, have reported that the occurrence of TRAEs correlates with improved treatment response and OS, underscoring their potential utility for patient stratification and personalized treatment approaches. Conversely, the prognostic significance of TRAEs in ICIs + TKIs is less consistent, necessitating further investigation to distinguish the specific contribution of each treatment component.

This narrative review underscores the importance of integrating high-quality real-world evidence with clinical trial data to better understand and optimize combination immunotherapy strategies for diverse patient subgroups. Future research should focus on prospective multicenter registries with standardized adverse event reporting, long-term follow-ups on response durability, and translational studies linking immune profiles and biomarkers with clinical outcomes. Such efforts will facilitate the refinement of treatment selection, management of toxicities, and ultimate improvement of patient prognosis in aRCC.
