# Neural stem cell-delivered oncolytic virus via intracerebroventricular administration enhances glioblastoma therapy and immune modulation.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Glioblastoma (GBM) is a highly aggressive brain tumor with poor prognosis and limited treatment options. Oncolytic virus (OV) therapy holds promise but is hindered by immune neutralization and poor tumor infiltration. Neural stem cells (NSCs) can enhance OV delivery, and intracerebroventricular (ICV) administration offers broader tumor access. This study evaluates NSC-OV therapy via ICV injection for improved tumor targeting and immune modulation in GBM.

**METHODS**: NSCs were infected with OV and assessed for viral uptake and replication. In vitro assays examined NSC-OV effects on glioma proliferation and migration. In vivo xenograft and orthotopic models evaluated tumor targeting, therapeutic efficacy, and immune modulation. Humanized immune system mouse models enabled single-cell RNA sequencing and flow cytometry analysis of immune responses.

**RESULTS**: NSCs retained their stemness after OV infection. NSCs-OV significantly inhibited glioma cell migration, proliferation, and colony formation in vitro. In orthotopic GBM models, NSCs-OV exhibited enhanced tumor homing, prolonged viral persistence, and reduced tumor burden while minimizing inflammation and systemic toxicity. NSCs protected OV from neutralizing antibodies, leading to sustained efficacy. Single-cell RNA sequencing indicated that NSCs-OV therapy reduced tumor-promoting inflammation by downregulating S100A8/A9, markers of myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and chemotactic factors that recruited MDSCs into tumors. Combining NSCs-OV with Paquinimod further suppressed tumor growth by reducing MDSCs and increasing activated T cells.

**CONCLUSIONS**: NSCs serve as efficient OV carriers, enhancing tumor targeting, suppressing GBM progression, and modulating the immune landscape. The combination with Paquinimod amplifies therapeutic benefits, offering a promising strategy for improving GBM treatment outcomes.

Introduction

 Glioblastoma (GBM) is the most common and aggressive primary malignant brain tumor in adults, accounting for approximately 48% of all primary malignant central nervous system (CNS) tumors. The current standard treatment involves maximal safe resection, followed by radiotherapy and concurrent chemotherapy with temozolomide. However, GBM patients have a very poor prognosis, with a median survival time typically ranging from 12 to 15 months. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop novel therapeutic approaches.

Current therapeutic strategies targeting the GBM tumor microenvironment (TME) include chimeric antigen receptor T-cell (CAR-T) therapy, immune checkpoint inhibitors, tumor vaccines, and oncolytic viruses (OVs). OV therapy is promising for cancer treatment because of its selective tumor-killing ability, efficient replication, and minimal toxicity. OVs are particularly promising because they selectively infect and lyse tumor cells while sparing normal brain tissue. This process induces immunogenic cell death and activates antitumor immune responses via damage-associated molecular patterns and pathogen-associated molecular patterns, which help to reshape the TME. Various OVs, including herpes simplex virus (HSV-1), adenovirus, poliovirus, and measles virus, have demonstrated preclinical efficacy and have advanced into early-phase clinical trials for GBM. DNX-2401 (a modified Ad5-based virus) and G47Δ (a triple-mutated HSV-1) have shown encouraging safety profiles and some degree of clinical activity. Despite these advances, the clinical benefits of OV monotherapy remain limited.

Several OVs, including HSV-1 derivatives such as talimogene laherparepvec (T-Vec), adenovirus-based vectors such as DNX-2401, and other platforms such as poliovirus and herpesvirus derivatives, have advanced into clinical trials, demonstrating safety and preliminary efficacy. Despite these advances, several challenges limit the therapeutic success of OV monotherapy, including poor intratumoral distribution, rapid viral clearance, and immune suppression within the TME. A major barrier to the effectiveness of OV therapy for GBM is inefficient viral delivery and rapid immune-mediated clearance. Intratumoral injections often result in poor viral dissemination within the tumor mass and failure to target infiltrative tumor cells. Moreover, pre-existing neutralizing antibodies (NAbs) or complement factors in the blood can significantly impair the stability and bioavailability of systemically or locally administered viruses. These limitations have motivated the search for innovative OV delivery strategies to improve tumor targeting and overcome immunological barriers. Intracerebroventricular (ICV) administration is a promising approach for delivering OV directly into the cerebrospinal fluid, ensuring a wider distribution within the CNS. However, challenges such as neurotoxicity, NAbs, and limited tumor targeting remain. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop novel delivery strategies for enhancing the therapeutic efficacy and safety of OVs.

Recent studies have explored the use of stem cell carriers, including neural, mesenchymal, and pluripotent stem cells, to enhance OV delivery to GBM by shielding the virus from host immunity and improving tumor tropism. Among these, neural stem cells (NSCs) exhibit superior migration within the brain parenchyma and targeting of distant, infiltrative GBM lesions. Leveraging their innate tumor-tropism, NSCs serve as an ideal “Trojan horse” delivery vehicle for OV, shielding them from immune surveillance while homing to tumor foci. Preclinical studies have shown that NSCs-OV therapy enhances intratumoral viral replication, minimizes neuroinflammation, and maintains high viral titers at tumor sites. A phase I clinical trial for high-grade glioma has shown that NSCs-OV selectively targets and kills tumor cells, providing precise anti-cancer treatment while minimizing damage to healthy brain tissue. Despite these promising findings, the potential of ICV administration of NSCs-OV for GBM treatment remains unexplored, presenting an opportunity to maximize therapeutic efficacy and overcome current delivery limitations.

Traditional local delivery strategies for OVs in GBM, such as direct intratumoral injection, convection-enhanced delivery, and administration into the resection cavity, are limited. These limitations include uneven viral distribution, limited infiltration into infiltrative tumor margins, and the need for invasive surgical procedures, such as tumor resection or ICV administration. In contrast, our approach uses ICV delivery of NSC-encapsulated OV, combining the broad CNS coverage of cerebrospinal fluid distribution with the tumor-homing ability of NSCs. In this study, we introduced a distinct approach of administering NSC-OV via the cerebrospinal fluid through ICV injection. This method combines the widespread CNS coverage of the cerebrospinal fluid with the tumor-tropic properties of NSCs. Our data showed that it enables targeted delivery to disseminated tumor regions while shielding the virus from immune neutralization. Single-cell RNA sequencing revealed an immune-modulating effect of NSCs-OV therapy and a significant reduction in S100A8/A9 expression. Combining NSCs-OV with the S100A8/A9 inhibitor paquinimod synergistically enhances antitumor immunity by suppressing myeloid-derived suppressor cell (MDSC) infiltration, enhancing CD8+ T cell cytotoxicity, and remodeling the immunosuppressive TME. This innovative therapeutic strategy has significant potential for improving GBM treatment outcomes and overcoming the limitations of conventional OV therapy.

Methods

Cell culture and OV transfection

The human NSCs used in this study were obtained from Angecon Biotech (Shanghai, China) and derived from human fetal brain tissue at 6–8 weeks of gestation, which was obtained with full parental consent and approval of the IRB of the hospital. The tissue was separated and dissected in GMP facilities (Angecon Biotech, Shanghai, China) and then digested with papain (200U/10 mL for 30 min, Worthington). The digested single cells were seeded in ultra-low attachment T75 flasks (Corning, NY) at a density of 1×105 cells/mL in serum-free NSC medium containing DMEM/F12 (Gibco, Waltham, MA) supplemented with B27, 20 ng/mL epidermal growth factor (EGF), 20 ng/mL basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), and 1% penicillin-streptomycin. U251-luc and GL261-luc glioma cells and SVGp12 (C859) normal human astroglial cell lines were obtained from the American Type Culture Collection. CT-3-Luc, derived from a GBM patient, was genetically engineered to stably express the luciferase reporter gene (Luc). Human cortical neurons and human brain microvascular endothelial cells (HBMECs) were obtained from Shanghai Jinyuan Biotechnology (Shanghai, China) and cultured according to the manufacturer’s recommended protocols. CT-3-Luc, U251-Luc, GL261-Luc glioma cells, SVGp12 (C859) cell lines, and human cortical neuron cells were cultured in MEM medium supplemented with 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin-streptomycin. HBMECs were cultured in endothelial cell medium (#1001; ScienCell) according to the manufacturer’s guidelines. All cells were incubated in a humidified atmosphere at 37℃ and 5% CO₂.

YSCH-01 is a tumor-selective recombinant adenovirus serotype 5 (Ad5) vector derived from the OncoMul-V2 backbone, which was developed by Shanghai Yuansong Biotechnology and is currently under clinical evaluation (ClinicalTrials.gov Identifier: NCT05180851) for the treatment of various solid tumors including breast cancer, lung cancer, and melanoma. The parental OncoMul-V2 vector features a 24-base pair deletion in the E1A gene and replaces the native E1A promoter with a tumor-selective survivin promoter to restrict replication to malignant cells. Building on this platform, YSCH-01 was engineered to include a complete expression cassette for the L-IFN gene, which is a modified type I interferon, inserted between the adenoviral packaging signal (Ψ) and the survivin promoter. The L-IFN sequence was synthesized via multiplex PCR (General Biosystems, Anhui, China), and its expression was driven by a human cytomegalovirus promoter, OncoMul-V2-eGFP, was used for tracking. To achieve the desired multiplicity of infection (MOI=10), OV (YSCH-01) was diluted in serum-free DMEM. The existing medium was removed and diluted OV was directly added to the NSCs. After 2 hours of incubation at 37°C, the transfection medium was replaced with fresh DMEM/F12 containing 2% B27, EGF, and bFGF. The cells were then incubated for 48 hours to allow viral replication and gene expression.

Quantification of viral replication

Cells were washed with PBS to remove unbound virus and then lysed for DNA extraction using a DNA isolation kit (Sangon Biotech, B511371-0050), following the manufacturer’s instructions. qPCR was performed using the YBR Green Master Mix with E1A primers (E1A F: GAAGAAGTACAGCCCTTGTGCCTG; R:CTCGACACGATCCAGACATGATAAGA). The data were normalized to the total DNA input and expressed as E1A copies per nanogram of DNA.

Wound healing assay and colony formation

The tumor cells were cultured on uncoated plates to minimize morphological interference. Once the tumor cells adhered, NSCs-OV were added. After washing with PBS, NSCs were removed to avoid observational bias. NSCs were infected with OV (YSCH-01) at an MOI of 10 for 2 hours, followed by three washes with PBS containing anti-Ad5 NAb (ab6982, Abcam, Cambridge, UK). The cells were resuspended in serum-free medium and counted for subsequent assays. The experiment was divided into four groups: (1) PBS (control), (2) NSCs supernatant, (3) OV suspension (MOI 10, directly infecting tumor cells), and (4) NSCs-OV suspension (MOI 10, NSCs: tumor cells=1:1).

For the wound healing assay, cells were seeded into six-well plates at a density of 500 cells per well and cultured until they reached 90%–100% confluence. A sterile 200 µL pipette tip was used to create a linear scratch across the monolayer. Detached cells and debris were removed by washing with PBS. Images of the scratched areas were captured at 0 hours (immediately after scratching). Wound closure was monitored and images were taken at 24. The migration rate was quantified by measuring the wound area using ImageJ software. The percentage of wound closure was calculated using the following formula: Wound closure (%)= × 100%. where A0h and A24h represent the wound areas (in pixels) measured immediately after scratch formation (0 hours) and 24 hours postscratch, respectively. A 24-hour specifically quantified the residual unhealed region not yet reoccupied by migrating cells.

For colony formation, cells were seeded into six-well plates at a density of 1000 cells per well to allow individual colony formation. The cells were then cultured under standard conditions at 37°C with 5% CO₂ for 6 days, with the culture medium replaced every 2 days. Colonies were fixed with 4% PFA for 15 min at room temperature and were stained with 1% crystal violet solution for 20 min. Colonies containing at least 50 cells were counted using the ImageJ software. The experiments were conducted in triplicate.

In vitro co-culture model

This in vitro model simulated the brain-ventricle interface to analyze the tumor-targeting efficacy of NSCs-OV in a controlled environment. A Transwell-based “brain-ventricle-like model” was established using 5.0 µm PTFE Transwell chambers based on the method of constructing a blood–brain barrier model. Astrocytes and HBMECs were cultured on collagen-coated membranes of 5.0 µm PTFE Transwell inserts for 6–7 days to form a confluent monolayer. The integrity and functionality of the astrocytic monolayer were evaluated by measuring the trans-epithelial electrical resistance (TEER) using a volt/ohmmeter. Only monolayers exhibiting TEER >50 Ω/cm² were selected for subsequent experiments. In the lower chamber, CT-3 tumor cells were seeded at a density of 5×10⁴ cells/well and cultured in serum-free medium until they formed a confluent monolayer. In the upper chamber, NSCs (5×10⁴ cells), OV (YSCH-01, 5×10⁵ IU), and NSCs-OV with MOI 10, MOI 20, MOI 40, and MOI 80 were added. The co-culture system was incubated at 37°C for 6 days. After the incubation period, viral progeny titers and E1A gene copies in the tumor cells were quantified using qPCR and plaque assays. All experiments were performed in triplicates.

Immunohistochemistry and H&E

Tissue samples were fixed in 4% PFA for 24hours at 4°C, embedded in paraffin. 5 µm sections were deparaffinized in xylene and rehydrated using a graded ethanol series. Antigen retrieval was performed in sodium citrate buffer (pH 6.0) in a microwave for 15 min. After cooling to room temperature, sections were incubated with 3% hydrogen peroxide for 10 min to block endogenous peroxidase activity. After incubating the sections with 5% BSA, they were incubated with E1A antibody overnight incubation at 4°C. After washing, the sections were incubated with the secondary antibody for 1 hour at room temperature. Signal detection was performed using a DAB chromogen substrate and stained with hematoxylin. The slides were dehydrated and cleared in xylene. The staining intensity was analyzed using the ImageJ software.

For H&E staining, the sections were immersed in hematoxylin solution for 5–10 min to stain the cell nuclei after deparaffinization and rehydration. The sections were differentiated in 1% acid alcohol and then blued in tap water. After thorough washing, the slides were counterstained with eosin for 1–3 min to stain the cytoplasm and extracellular matrix.

Transmission electron microscopy

Cells were fixed in 2.5% glutaraldehyde in 0.1 M phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) at 4°C for 2 hours. After washing, samples were postfixed with 1% osmium tetroxide in 0.1 M phosphate buffer for 1 hour at room temperature. The samples were then dehydrated using a graded ethanol series (30%, 50%, 70%, 90%, and 100%) and subsequently infiltrated with a mixture of ethanol and epoxy resin before embedding in pure epoxy resin. The embedded samples were polymerized at 60°C for 24 hours. Ultrathin sections (60–90 nm) were prepared using an ultramicrotome (Leica, Germany) and were collected on copper grids. The sections were stained with uranyl acetate for 10 min, followed by lead citrate staining for contrast enhancement. Ultrathin sections (60–90 nm) were prepared using an ultramicrotome (Leica, Germany). The sections were stained with uranyl acetate for 10 min. The samples were then examined using a transmission electron microscope, and high-resolution images were captured.

Immunofluorescence

Cells or frozen sections (10 µm) were fixed with 4% PFA for 15 min and permeabilized with 0.1% Triton X-100 for 10 min at room temperature. After blocking with 5% BSA for 1 hour, cells or frozen sections were incubated with primary antibodies overnight at 4°C. The cells or sections were then washed with PBS and incubated with fluorophore-conjugated secondary antibodies for 1 hour at room temperature in the dark. After washing, the nuclei were stained with DAPI and the slides were mounted. Immunofluorescence images were captured, and the signal intensity was analyzed using ImageJ. The antibodies used are listed in table 1.

Cell viability

Cell viability was assessed using the Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK-8) assay following the manufacturer’s instructions. The cells were seeded in 96-well plates at a density of 5×103 cells/well and allowed to adhere overnight under standard culture conditions. After treatment under the experimental conditions, 10 µL of CCK-8 solution was added to each well and incubated for 1 hour at 37°C in a humidified incubator with 5% CO₂. Absorbance was measured at a wavelength of 450 nm. The percentage of viable cells was calculated relative to that in the untreated control group. The experiments were performed in triplicate. Negative controls, including wells without cells, were included to account for background absorbance.

Flow cytometry

Cells were fixed with 4% PFA for 10 min at room temperature and permeabilized using 0.1% Triton X-100 for 15 min. Cells were then incubated with fluorophore-conjugated antibodies for 30 min at 4°C in the dark. After staining, the cells were washed, resuspended in PBS, and analyzed by flow cytometry. Unstained cells and fluorescence-minus-one controls were used as controls. Data were acquired using BD FACSDiva software and analyzed using FlowJo software. All experiments were performed in triplicates. The antibodies that were used are listed in table 2.

Preparation of NSCs-OV for in vitro and in vivo experiments

In vitro, NSCs were infected with OV (YSCH-01) at MOI of 10 for 2 hours, washed three times with PBS containing anti-Ad5 NAb, and resuspended in serum-free medium for subsequent use. To evaluate the tumor tropism of NSCs-OV, NSCs were first infected with mCherry lentivirus for 24 hours, purified by flow cytometry sorting, and then infected with eGFP-OV at MOI of 40 for 2 hours, followed by PBS washing prior to direct ICV injection in GBM-bearing mice. For therapeutic evaluation in an orthotopic GBM model, NSCs were infected with OV (YSCH-01) (MOI 40) for 2 hours, washed three times with serum-PBS containing virus antibodies to remove unbound virus, resuspended at 1×105 cells/µL, and administered via weekly ICV injection (5 µL per dose) for three consecutive weeks. This injection volume was selected based on established protocols demonstrating safe and effective intraventricular delivery in mice.

Cellular impedance

Cells were seeded into an electronic microplate (E-plate) at a density of 5×103 cells. Cells were infected with OV at a specific MOI, and impedance measurements were continuously recorded using a real-time cell analysis (RTCA) system (Axion BioSystems, USA). Impedance data were recorded at regular intervals throughout the experiment. Cell index values reflect changes in cell attachment, spreading, and viability. The measurements were normalized to the baseline (preinfection) to account for variations in the initial cell seeding. Data analysis was performed using RTCA software.

Orthotopic GBM mouse

In this study, orthotopic GBM mouse models were used to evaluate the tumor-targeting ability and therapeutic efficacy of NSCs-OV therapy. Intracranial tumors were established by implanting the right thalamus in immunocompromised (NSG) and immunocompetent (C57BL/6) mice. To establish an orthotopic glioma model simulating the challenges of treating midline GBM, tumor cells were stereotactically implanted into the right thalamus at a depth of 2 mm. Following tumor engraftment, therapeutic agents (NSCs-OV, OV alone, or NSCs alone) were administered via ICV injection into the left ventricle using a stereotaxic apparatus.

A humanized immune system (humanized hematopoietic stem cells, humanized HSC) mouse model with orthotopic GBM xenografts was generated using patient-derived primary GBM cells. Humanized mice were purchased from the Nanjing Biomedical Research Institute. Humanization was achieved by transplanting human CD34+ HSCs, derived from umbilical cord blood or fetal liver, into irradiated, myeloablated M-NSG mice via intravenous tail vein injection. These transplanted HSCs repopulated the immune system with functional human immune cells. For all subsequent experiments, only mice with a human immune cell engraftment levels exceeding 40% were selected to ensure robust human immune responses. HSCs and tumor cells are not HLA-matched because of limited donor availability. While this introduces a potential for allogeneic immune responses, prior studies have shown that humanized models can still support meaningful assessment of immune-modulating therapies, even in the absence of full HLA compatibility. Tumor growth was monitored using bioluminescence imaging at regular time points.

Bioluminescence imaging

Tumor progression was monitored using the IVIS Spectrum in vivo imaging system. The mice were injected with D-luciferin (150 mg/kg IP) before imaging. Bioluminescence signals were quantified as the total flux (photons/s) to assess the tumor burden.

DiR labeling of OV (YSCH-01) infected NSCs

OV (YSCH-01) was used to infect NSCs at MOI 40. After 2 hours, the cells were washed with PBS to remove the unbound viruses. OV-infected NSCs were digested with Accutase (Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), resuspended in serum-free medium with 5 µM DiR dye, and incubated at 37°C in 5% CO₂ for 30 min. After labeling, the cells were washed thrice with PBS to remove excess dye and counted for further experiments.

Tumor targeting of NSCs-OV via ventricular injection

Due to the cytotoxic effects of puromycin and lentivirus on NSCs, mCherry-labeled lentivirus was used to infect NSCs for 24 hours without subsequence puromycin selection. Flow cytometry confirmed a >99% mCherry-positive rate, allowing efficient fluorescent labeling while minimizing cell loss. Subsequently, the mCherry-NSCs were infected with eGFP-OV (MOI: 40) for 2 hour. Uninfected viruses were removed by washing with PBS, and the cells were used directly for in vivo experiments. Following PBS washing, the cells were injected into the left lateral ventricle of mice with confirmed tumors. 5×10⁵mCherry-NSCs, mCherry-NSCs-eGFP-OV, or an equivalent amount of eGFP-OV (2×10⁷ IU) were injected into the left lateral ventricle (ML: 0.9 mm, AP: 0 mm, DV: 2.5 mm) of CT-3 and U-251 glioma-bearing mice with confirmed tumors. Fluorescent signals were tracked to evaluate the migration and targeting abilities of NSCs-OV.

For therapeutic evaluation, NSCs were infected with OV (YSCH-01) at an MOI infection of 40 for 2 hours. Cells were resuspended to a final concentration of 1×10⁵/μL and administered 5 µL ICV once weekly for 3 weeks.

Paquinimod administration

Paquinimod (ABR25757), an S100A8/A9 inhibitor, was administered orally at a dose of 10 mg/kg twice daily for a total of 10 doses, as previously described.

Hematological and biochemical analyses

Peripheral blood samples were collected on days 1, 3, 7, 14, and 21 after experimental treatment. The numbers of peripheral white cell counts (WCC) and blood lymphocytes were determined using an automated hematology analyzer. Serum alanine aminotransferase (ALT), aspartate aminotransferase (AST), blood urea nitrogen (BUN), and serum creatinine (Scr) levels were measured to evaluate liver and kidney function. Blood samples were centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 10 min to obtain serum, which was subsequently analyzed for liver function biomarkers, including AST (E-BC-K236-M, Elabscience, Wuhan, China) and ALT (E-BC-K235-M, Elabscience, Wuhan, China), as well as renal function biomarkers, Cr (E-BC-K188-M, Elabscience, Wuhan, China) and BUN (E-BC-K183-M, Elabscience).

Cell dissociation and single-cell RNA sequencing

Tumors from the CT-3 intracranial xenograft model using humanized HSC transplantation mouse model were collected on postoperative day 28. Peripheral blood samples were collected by cardiac puncture for flow cytometry analysis, followed by whole-body perfusion fixation using precooled 4% PFA in PBS. Brain tissues containing the tumors were dissected, mechanically dissociated, and digested with collagenase IV. Tumor-associated immune cells were isolated using 70%/30% discontinuous density Percoll gradient centrifugation. FACS Aria III was used to sort human immune cell subsets using the hCD45+/mCD45− phenotype. Samples from six biological replicates in each group were pooled to generate a composite sample. Quality control was performed on all pooled samples prior to single-cell sequencing (Shanghai OE Biotech).

Single cells were captured and barcoded using the 10 x Genomics Chromium platform. Briefly, cells were loaded onto a Chromium Single Cell 3’ Chip and partitioned into nanoliter-scale Gel Bead-In-Emulsions. Reverse transcription was performed for each GEM to generate barcoded cDNA. After breaking the emulsions, cDNA was purified and amplified by PCR. The amplified cDNA was used to construct sequencing libraries using a Chromium Single Cell 3 Library Kit. Libraries were quantified using a BioAnalyzer (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, California, USA) and sequenced on an Illumina NovaSeq 6000 platform with paired-end reads. Sequencing data were processed using the Cell Ranger pipeline (10x Genomics) for alignment. Data analysis was conducted using Seurat for normalization, clustering, and differential expression.

Multiplex immunofluorescence

Multiplex immunofluorescence was performed on tumor tissues from HSC orthotopic GBM mice to investigate the colocalization of multiple biomarkers. Tumors were fixed in 4% PFA for 24 hours, followed by gradient dehydration and paraffin embedding. 4 µm thick sections were deparaffinized, rehydrated, and subjected to antigen retrieval using sodium citrate buffer (pH 6.0). Endogenous peroxidase activity was blocked with 3% H2O2, and non-specific binding was prevented by incubating the sections with 3% BSA at room temperature for 1 hour. Sections were incubated with S100A8/A9 primary antibodies (1:100) overnight at 4°C. After washing, sections were incubated with secondary antibodies at room temperature for 1 hour. The Tyramide Signal Amplification (TSA) 594 reagent was applied at room temperature for 30 min. The procedure was repeated for CD15 (1:200) with TSA 488, and CD14 (1:150) with TSA Cy5. The slides were mounted with a DAPI-containing anti-fade medium. Spectra MIF software was used for the signal separation and colocalization analyses. Specificity and accuracy were ensured through negative controls and single-stained quality controls. TSA kit was purchased from Shanghai Ruiyu Biotechnology (bry-0045-050, Shanghai, China).

Western Blot

Cells or tissues were lysed using RIPA lysis buffer supplemented with protease and phosphatase inhibitors. Total protein concentration was measured using the Pierce BCA Protein Assay Kit (Thermo Fisher Scientific, Waltham, MA). Proteins were then denatured in Laemmli buffer and separated by sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis. Following electrophoresis, the proteins were transferred onto polyvinylidene difluoride membranes using an electroblotting system. To prevent non-specific binding, the membrane was blocked with 5% non-fat dry milk in TBST containing 1% Tween-20 (TBST). The membranes were subsequently incubated overnight at 4°C with primary antibodies. After washing with TBST, membranes were incubated with horseradish peroxidase-conjugated secondary antibodies. Protein bands were visualized using enhanced chemiluminescence. The antibodies used are listed in table 1. GAPDH and tubulin were used as loading controls. The bands were analyzed using ImageJ to quantify the relative protein expression levels.

qRT-PCR

Total RNA was extracted from the tissues using TRIzol reagent (Invitrogen, USA), following the manufacturer’s protocol. Briefly, tissues were homogenized in TRIzol, and chloroform was added to separate the phases. The aqueous phase containing the RNA was collected and precipitated with isopropanol. The RNA pellet was washed with 75% ethanol, air-dried, and resuspended in RNase-free water. RNA quality and concentration were assessed using a NanoDrop spectrophotometer. Total RNA (500 ng of total RNA was reverse transcribed using a reverse transcription kit. Result

NSCs as efficient carriers for OV therapy

NSCs were found to express the stem cell markers CD133, Sox-2, and Nestin, indicating that they retained their stemness (figure 1A). Given the critical role of adenovirus-binding receptors in mediating OV infection of NSCs, we systematically examined the surface expression profiles of these receptors in NSCs. Our findings revealed marked expression of coxsackievirus and adenovirus receptors (CAR), complement regulatory protein CD46, and integrin αvβ3 on NSCs membranes, suggesting their viability as cellular vehicles for adenovirus-based therapeutic delivery (figure 1B). NSCs were transfected with EGFP-expressing OV. Immunofluorescence imaging revealed that a higher MOI resulted in greater GFP expression than a lower MOI, indicating a dose-dependent relationship between MOI and viral transduction efficiency (figure 1D, online supplemental figure S1B). Quantitative flow cytometry demonstrated that 21% of NSCs were GFP-positive at MOI 1, while this proportion increased to >80% at MOI 10 (figure 1E). Analysis of infection efficiency revealed a linear correlation between MOI and infection rate up to MOI of 40. However, this increase in GFP-positive cells markedly decreased beyond MOI 60, plateauing at MOI 80 (online supplemental figure S1C and figure 1F). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images taken 48 hours post-infection showed viral particles within NSCs, primarily in the nucleus and some in the cytoplasm, indicating successful infection (figure 1G). Western blot (WB) analysis detected no significant changes in Nestin, CD133, and Sox-2 expression after infection, suggesting that the cells maintained their stemness despite viral exposure 48 hours postinfection (figure 1H). Similarly, CD46 and CAR expressions remained stable (figure 1I). Furthermore, flow cytometry analysis of CD133, Sox-2, and Nestin revealed that NSC marker expression remained stable after infection, suggesting that viral treatment did not significantly alter stemness. GFAP expression did not show significant changes, indicating that the infection did not induce spontaneous differentiation (online supplemental figure S1D). These results show that NSCs can be efficiently transfected with OV while retaining their stemness characteristics, indicating their potential for virus-based therapy.

The cytotoxic effects of OV (YSCH-01) on NSCs, primary tumor cells (CT-3), glioma cell lines (U251 and GL261), non-neoplastic human astrocytes/neurons were assessed using CCK8. The OV YSCH-01 exhibited a tumor-selective cytotoxic profile, with dose-dependent viability reduction most pronounced in GBM cells compared with NSCs. Comparative IC50 analysis showed that YSCH-01 was significantly less potent against non-neoplastic neurons and astrocytes than against both glioma cell lines and patient-derived tumor populations, confirming the preferential targeting of malignant phenotypes (figure 2A). Consistent with previous reports that human Ad5 has restricted infectivity and replication capacity in murine cells, the GL261 line exhibited markedly lower sensitivity, indicating that observed cytotoxicity in this model likely underestimates the virus’s direct lytic potential in human tumors. TEM images showed viral particles accumulating in the cytoplasm, although fewer particles were detected in GL261 cells than in the other cell lines, indicating successful viral replication (figure 2B). Flow cytometry analysis revealed an increase in 7-AAD-positive cells with increasing MOI, indicating a dose-dependent cytotoxic effect of OV (figure 2C,D). Real-time impedance analysis monitored the decline in NSC viability after OV infection. A significant decrease in impedance was observed at higher MOIs, suggesting increased cytotoxicity (figure 2E). Impedance data 48 hours postinfection confirmed that higher viral loads significantly reduced cell viability (figure 2F). A time-course analysis at MOI of 40 showed a steady decline in NSCs viability, with a sharp reduction observed after 24 hours postinfection and a significant drop at 36 and 48 hours (figure 2G). Viral replication analysis showed an increase in E1A gene copies over 4 days, with both cell-associated and cell-free viral particles accumulating over time (figure 2H). Notably, a decrease in intracellular viral particles was observed on the third day, which was due to OV-induced NSC lysis induced by OV. These findings indicate that YSCH-01 selectively reduces the viability of glioma cells (CT-3, U251, and GL261) while exerting relatively low cytotoxicity in NSCs, suggesting tumor-selective viral activity. A direct comparison of NSC-mediated delivery with free virus was addressed in subsequent experiments.

NSCs-OV therapy suppresses glioma cell migration and proliferation in vitro

To complement the in vivo efficacy data, we compared the effects of NSCs-OV and OV on glioma cell migration and proliferation using wound healing and colony formation assays. The wound-healing assay showed that NSCs-OV significantly reduced the migration of CT-3 (online supplemental figure S2A) and U251 (online supplemental figure S3A) cells. Quantification analysis further confirmed that both OV and NSCs-OV significantly reduced the migration of CT-3 and U-251 cells, with NSCs-OV showing the strongest inhibitory effect (online supplemental figure S3B). Colony formation assays in CT-3 and U251 cells demonstrated that OV and NSCs-OV treatment led to a substantial decrease in the number of colonies (online supplemental figure S2B,C). NSCs-OV treatment showed a greater inhibitory effect on colony formation than OV alone, suggesting that NSC-mediated viral delivery enhances the suppression of cell proliferation. Furthermore, we developed an in vitro ICV administration model using a transwell system, in which astrocytes and HBMECs were cultured on the bottom layer of the upper chamber. This model was used to determine the optimal OV infection dose delivered by NSCs-OV to target tumor cells, ensuring the highest therapeutic viral load (online supplemental figure S2D). Viral titer measurements revealed a significant increase in viral production in NSCs-OV-treated groups compared with that in the free OV. Among the different MOI conditions, MOI 40 resulted in the highest level of infectious units (online supplemental figure S2E). Quantification of adenoviral E1A gene expression further confirmed the increased viral replication in NSCs-OV-treated cells (online supplemental figure S2F). NSCs-OV (MOI: 40) group exhibited the highest E1A expression, suggesting that MOI: 40 provided the most effective viral delivery and replication efficiency. Therefore, an MOI of 40 was selected for use in in vivo experiments to maximize therapeutic efficacy. Live/dead cell staining was used to assess cytotoxicity more accurately. Cytotoxicity was more apparent under serum-free conditions, likely due to increased OV sensitivity without serum-mediated inhibition. Under serum-containing conditions, NSCs helped preserve OV infectivity, whereas free OV lost efficacy, likely due to inactivation by serum components (online supplemental figure S2G,H and S3D). NSCs-OV maintained viral integrity in the presence of blood serum and led to more tumor cell damage, whereas free OV was less stable and induced minimal tumor cell death, suggesting that NSCs protect the virus from serum-mediated inactivation (online supplemental figure S2I and S3C). These findings demonstrated that NSCs are effective carriers for OV therapy, enhancing viral delivery, increasing tumor cell death, and preserving viral stability.

NSC-mediated delivery enhances OV targeting of tumors via ICV injection

CT-3 and U-251 cells were implanted near the right thalamus to generate intracranial tumors. Whereas striatal implantation is commonly employed in preclinical models, our thalamic model enabled the simultaneous assessment of therapeutic NSC migration and viral distribution to more distal brain structures following ICV delivery, providing insights into delivery kinetics and therapeutic reach. NSCs-OV were administered to the left cerebral parenchyma to evaluate its targeted migration and therapeutic potential. In both CT-3 and U-251 orthotopic GBM mouse models, DiR-labeled NSCs-OV displayed higher and more sustained fluorescence signals within the tumor area 24 hours postinjection, indicating improved tumor homing and persistence (figure 3A). Additionally, NSCs-OV showed preferential accumulation in tumor sites, including the right thalamus, compared with non-tumor-bearing regions, demonstrating selective tumor tropism (figure 3B). Next, we evaluated the approach of ICV injection of NSCs-OV (figure 3C). OV was administered to the left lateral ventricle, and tumors were generated in the right thalamus. Following injection, OV alone showed limited migration, predominantly localized to the ventricular tumor without infiltrating the parenchymal tumor region. In contrast, NSCs-OV demonstrated rapid migration to the tumor site within 24 hours postinjection, exhibiting time-dependent infiltration into the tumor. Compared with OV alone, NSCs effectively enhanced OV homing toward the tumor site and significantly improved viral infiltration. Immunofluorescence imaging confirmed NSCs-OV successfully reached tumor sites in the right ventricle and thalamus, with mCherry-expressing NSCs and GFP-expressing OV colocalized with tumor regions (figure 3D). Time-course imaging of the tumors revealed prolonged viral persistence and spread following OV along with NSCs-OV treatment. Compared with OV alone, NSCs-OV treatment maintained stronger viral signals within the tumor for up to 7 days postinfection, suggesting improved viral stability and delivery (figure 3E). WB analysis revealed a significant increase in CXCR4 and CCR2 expression in NSCs infected with higher MOIs, indicating that these chemokine receptors may enhance the tumor homing of NSCs-OV (figure 3F).

ICV delivery of OV via NSCs reduces inflammation and toxicity

To conduct a preliminary safety assessment of NSCs-OV, OV and NSCs-OV were administered via intraventricular injection to C57BL/6 mice to evaluate the ventricular and systemic inflammatory responses. Body weight monitoring indicated that NSCs-OV treatment resulted in less weight loss than OV treatment, suggesting lower toxicity (figure 3G). H&E staining 14 days postinjection revealed extensive inflammatory cell infiltration in the ventricles of mice treated with OV alone. In contrast, NSC-encapsulated OV reduced this toxicity, supporting the safety advantage of cell-based immunofluorescence staining, which revealed widespread OV distribution within the ventricles (figure 3H,I). In contrast, NSCs protected the ventricular surface from direct OV exposure, preventing diffuse ependymal disruption and edema (online supplemental figure S4A). These results indicate that NSCs alleviated OV-induced ependymal damage and did not cause significant damage to the surrounding brain tissues.

Immunohistochemical analysis revealed significant CD4+ and CD8+ T cell infiltration in the ventricles of mice 14 days after OV injection, indicating an inflammatory response. In contrast, the NSCs-OV group showed minimal T-cell infiltration, suggesting reduced neuroinflammation (online supplemental figure S4B,C). Peripheral blood analysis indicated that OV treatment significantly increased WCC and lymphocyte counts, peaking on day 14 and decreasing thereafter, suggesting controlled immune activation (online supplemental figure S4D). The delayed peak aligns with prior reports that systemic adenovirus administration can elicit a biphasic toxicity profile: an early phase (~day 3) driven by innate immune activation, followed by a later phase (1–2 weeks) mediated by T-cell responses against transgene-expressing cells and associated inflammatory cascades. Here, the ICV route likely minimized early peripheral exposure, thereby attenuating acute toxicity, while the later peak coincided with maximal neuroinflammation and systemic immune activation. Serum ALT, AST, BUN, and Scr were elevated in the free-OV group at day 14, reflecting hepatotoxicity and nephrotoxicity, whereas NSCs-OV markedly reduced these changes (online supplemental figure S4E), indicating that NSC delivery mitigates both immune-mediated and direct organ toxicities. To evaluate the tumorigenic potential of NSCs, DiR-labeled NSCs and NSCs-OV were administered intracranially into mice. On day 2, both DiR-NSCs and DiR-NSCs-OV groups exhibited strong DiR signals at the tumor site. By day 14, the DiR signal in the NSCs-OV group had significantly reduced (online supplemental figure S5A). Immunofluorescence analysis of tumor sections revealed the presence of eGFP-OV (green) and mCherry-NSCs (red), with fragmented red-only signals, suggesting that OV effectively lysed NSCs post-delivery (online supplemental figure S5B). These findings suggest that NSCs-OV not only effectively targets but also maintains an acceptable safety profile.

NSCs-OV therapy enhances OV efficacy and overcomes NAbs in vivo

Next, we evaluated the therapeutic efficacy of NSCs-OV in glioma-bearing mice, with or without pre-existing NAbs (NAb+ or NAb⁻). Mice received adenovirus (Ad5) immunization to induce NAbs. On day 0, GL261-Luc cells were implanted at a total of 1×105 cells in 5 µL. Tumor-bearing mice were treated with PBS, NSCs, OV, or NSCs-OV, followed by bioluminescence imaging and histological analysis (figure 4A). Ad5 immunization significantly increased NAb titers, whereas NSCs-OV-treated mice exhibited lower NAb responses compared with OV treatment (figure 4B,C). Bioluminescence imaging showed that NSCs-OV treatment led to the most significant tumor reduction in both NAb⁻ and NAb+ mice (figure 4D,F). Quantification of bioluminescence confirmed that NSCs-OV treatment resulted in sustained tumor regression even in the presence of NAbs, whereas direct OV treatment was less effective in NAb+ mice (figure 4E,G). Histological analysis of brain tumor sections revealed that NSCs-OV treatment resulted in the greatest tumor shrinkage in both groups (figure 4H). Kaplan-Meier survival analysis demonstrated the impact of NSCs-OV therapy on orthotopic GBM mice under different immune conditions (figure 4I). In NAb⁻ mice, both OV and NSCs-OV treatments significantly extended survival compared with PBS and NSC controls. However, NSCs-OV therapy resulted in the longest survival, indicating superior therapeutic efficacy. In NAb+ mice, OV treatment was much less effective due to NAb-mediated viral clearance, resulting in survival rates comparable to those of the control groups. In contrast, NSCs-OV therapy significantly prolonged survival even in the presence of NAbs, suggesting that NSCs protect the virus from immune neutralization, allowing effective tumor targeting and viral replication. Immunohistochemical staining for the E1A protein showed the highest number of E1A-positive cells in NSCs-OV-treated tumors, indicating enhanced viral persistence and replication (figure 4J,K). These findings suggest that NSCs-OV effectively deliver OV to gliomas, overcoming the limitations posed by pre-existing NAbs and leading to improved therapeutic outcomes. Moreover, considering that human Ad5-based vectors are intrinsically limited, the observed antitumor effects of OV, particularly when delivered by NSCs, are largely mediated through immune activation and enhanced viral delivery, rather than robust lytic replication within murine tumor cells.

NSCs-OV therapy modulates the TME and reduces pro-tumor inflammatory signaling

To investigate the mechanisms underlying NSCs-OV therapy in GBM and its impact on TME, we used patient-derived primary GBM cells to generate a humanized immune system mouse model with orthotopic GBM xenografts. UMAP clustering identified six major immune cell populations within the TME: myeloid cells, plasma cells, T lymphocytes, NK cells, B lymphocytes, and mast cells (figure 5A). In-depth clustering analysis of the myeloid cell subpopulation revealed six functional subclusters (figure 5B). Notably, functional scoring using the MDSCs gene signature indicated that subclusters 1, 3, and 5 were enriched in MDSC characteristics (figure 5D). As MDSCs play a crucial role in immune suppression and tumor immune evasion, this finding suggests a significant presence of immunosuppressive myeloid cells in TME. Proportional analysis showed a significant reduction in the proportion of MDSCs in the NSCs-OV treatment group compared with that in the control group (figure 5C). This indicates that NSCs-OV therapy modulates the tumor immune microenvironment (TIEM) by reducing the number and suppressive activity of MDSCs. DEG analysis of MDSCs subpopulations revealed that S100A8/A9 heterodimers were significantly downregulated in the NSCs-OV treatment group (figure 5E). S100A8/A9 are key immunosuppressive molecules in MDSCs and are associated with MDSC-mediated immunosuppression. GO functional enrichment analysis showed that S100A8/A9-associated genes were primarily involved in neutrophil activation and chemokine signaling pathways (figure 5F), which are crucial for MDSC recruitment and immunosuppressive functions. The downregulation of S100A8/A9 suggests that NSCs-OV therapy disrupts S100A8/A9-mediated immunosuppressive signaling, potentially decreasing MDSCs’ immunosuppressive activity and enhancing antitumor immune responses.

qRT-PCR and WB analysis of S100A8 and S100A9 showed that NSCs-OV significantly reduced the levels of these inflammatory markers compared with controls in CT-3 cells in vitro, indicating that NSCs-OV inhibited the expression of anti-inflammatory factors associated with tumor progression (figure 5G,H). This suppressive effect may be attributed to the OV-induced modulation of inflammatory signaling pathways within glioma cells. Given that S100A8/A9 expression is regulated by NF-κB and STAT3, both of which can be affected by viral infection, it is possible that the OV component in NSCs-OV alters these pathways to reduce S100A8/A9 transcription. In CT-3 and U-251 orthotopic xenograft models, NSCs-OV treatment significantly decreased S100A8 and S100A9 expression, suggesting a localized effect on immune signaling (figure 5I,J, online supplemental figure S6A). In the orthotopic GBM model with humanized HSC mice, multiplex immunofluorescence images revealed that NSCs-OV-treated tumors exhibited decreased infiltration of S100A8/A9+ MDSCs compared with controls, indicating reprogramming of the TIME to a less immunosuppressive state (figure 5K). Immunofluorescence analysis using the GL261 orthotopic xenograft model further indicated that NSCs-OV treatment reduced Gr-1+ MDSC infiltration compared with the PBS and NSC groups (online supplemental figure S6B). Similarly, S100A8+ and S100A9+ myeloid cells were markedly decreased in the NSCs-OV group, whereas the control tumors showed a high density of these inflammatory cells. These results indicate that NSCs-OV treatment suppresses the infiltration of immunosuppressive myeloid cells and reduces anti-inflammatory S100A8/A9 expression, potentially enhancing antitumor immune responses in gliomas.

NSCs-OV therapy modulates myeloid and T cell populations in peripheral blood of humanized immune system mouse tumor models

A previous clinical study demonstrated that NSCs-OV therapy induces changes in peripheral immune cells in GBM patients. Therefore, we investigated the effects of NSCs-OV therapy on human immune cells in the peripheral blood using a humanized immune system (HSC) mouse tumor model (online supplemental figure S7A). NSCs-OV treatment significantly reduced CD11B+CD33+ MDSCs, as well as CD14+ and CD15+ MDSCs (online supplemental figure S7B,E–G). NSCs-OV treatment significantly increased M1-like macrophages (CD86+) and reduced M2-like immunosuppressive macrophages (CD206+) (online supplemental figure S7C,H,I). This indicates that NSCs-OV therapy promotes a shift from a pro-tumor M2 phenotype to an anti-tumor M1 phenotype. Furthermore, NSCs-OV therapy increased the number of CD86+ antigen-presenting cells and CD11C+ dendritic cells. Moreover, NSCs-OV treatment significantly increased CD4+ and CD8+ T cell infiltration (online supplemental figure S7D,J,K). It also enhanced the number of memory CD8+ T cells (CD45RO+CD62L−) (online supplemental figure S7L) and PD-1+CD8+ T cells (online supplemental figure S7M). These findings further support that NSCs-OV not only suppress immunosuppressive MDSCs but also reprogram the broader myeloid and lymphoid compartments to favor an antitumor immune response.

Combination of NSCs-OV therapy with paquinimod enhances antitumor immunity and improves survival in orthotopic GBM mice model

Since NSCs-OV downregulated S100A8/A9 expression and S100A8/A9 contributes to the immunosuppressive microenvironment in GBM, we assessed the therapeutic efficacy of the combination of NSCs-OV and S100A8/A9 inhibitor paquinimod in xenograft models of CT-3 and U-251 cells. Mice were treated with PBS, Paquinimod, NSCs, OV, NSCs-OV, or NSCs-OV+ Paquinimod (figure 6A). H&E staining demonstrated that NSCs-OV treatment significantly reduced tumor size, and the combination with paquinimod resulted in further tumor shrinkage in both CT-3 and U-251 glioma models (figure 6B). Bioluminescence imaging revealed a significant reduction in tumor size over time in NSCs-OV-treated mice, with the NSCs-OV+paquinimod combination showing the most pronounced tumor regression (figure 6C–F). Kaplan-Meier survival curves demonstrated that NSCs-OV treatment significantly extended survival, while the addition of paquinimod further improved survival outcomes, indicating a synergistic therapeutic effect (figure 6G,H). Surprisingly, paquinimod alone did not show therapeutic efficacy, suggesting that S100A8/A9 blockade may require OV-mediated immune activation to exert antitumor effects.

Combination of NSCs-OV therapy with paquinimod enhances T cell activation and reduces MDSCs

To investigate whether NSCs-OV combined with S100A8/A9 inhibitor paquinimod enhances antitumor immunity by modulating the TME, we applied the orthotopic implantation model using GL-261 cells in immunocompetent C57BL/6 mice. We administered OV, NSCs-OV, and NSCs-OV combined with the S100A8/A9 inhibitor paquinimod to evaluate immune modulation. Flow cytometry analysis showed increased CD3+ T cell infiltration in tumors treated with NSCs-OV+ paquinimod, suggesting enhanced antitumor immunity (figure 7A). Notably, NSCs-OV treatment significantly elevated CD8+ T cell infiltration, and the addition of paquinimod resulted in the highest proportion of CD8+ T cells among total CD3+ T cells (figure 7B,H). Conversely, CD4+ T cell percentages were reduced in NSCs-OV and NSCs−OV+Paquinimod-treated tumors compared with those in the control groups (figure 7B,G). Compared with control and NSC-treated tumors, OV treatment alone led to a moderate reduction in FOXP3+ Tregs within the CD4+population, whereas NSCs-OV and NSCs-OV+Paquinimod treatment caused a substantial decline in Treg levels (figure 7D,J). Compared with NSCs-OV, NSCs-OV+Paquinimod treatment did not significantly increase PD-1 expression on CD8+ T cells, a marker of T cell exhaustion, suggesting that paquinimod does not exacerbate T cell exhaustion (figure 7C,I). Additional immune profiling revealed that NSCs-OV+ paquinimod shifted the TME toward a pro-inflammatory state by increasing CD86+CD206− macrophages, while reducing CD86-CD206+ immunosuppressive myeloid cells (figure 7E,K,L). Furthermore, Ly6C-Ly6G+monocytic MDSCs were significantly reduced in tumors treated with NSCs-OV and NSCs-OV+Paquinimod, with the lowest levels in the combination group (figure 7M,N). It should be noted that GL-261 cells have limited susceptibility to human adenovirus infection and replication, which may restrict the direct oncolytic effect of YSCH-01 in this model. Therefore, the observed therapeutic benefit is likely mediated primarily through immune modulation rather than robust viral oncolysis. These results suggest that NSCs-OV, particularly in combination with paquinimod, enhances antitumor immunity by increasing T cell infiltration, shifting macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory phenotype, and reducing immunosuppressive myeloid cells, leading to improved therapeutic efficacy.

Discussion

GBM remains one of the most aggressive and treatment-resistant malignancies of the CNS with limited therapeutic options and poor patient outcomes. While NSCs hold regenerative potential, their utility as delivery vehicles for oncolytic virotherapy represents a promising strategy to overcome critical barriers in GBM treatment. This study investigated the potential of NSCs as delivery vehicles for OV administered via ICV injection for GBM therapy. Our findings demonstrated that NSCs effectively protected OV from NAb clearance, enhanced OV replication within the tumor, and triggered a robust immune response, leading to improved therapeutic efficacy in GBM.

Oncolytic virotherapy is an emerging and promising strategy for cancer immunotherapy. Intratumoral injection is the primary method of delivering OV. However, intratumoral delivery of naked viruses poses significant challenges, including rapid immune clearance, insufficient tumor accumulation, and potential adverse effects. NSCs can migrate into brain tumors owing to their inherent homing properties, allowing them to deliver the OV directly to the tumor site. Our results revealed that NSCs serve as effective cellular carriers for OV delivery following ICV administration, allowing the virus to evade host immune responses and accumulate in the tumor. In vivo imaging and histological analysis showed that NSCs-OV exhibited stronger tumor-homing ability than direct OV injection, leading to increased viral persistence and replication within the tumor. NSC-OV-treated tumors exhibited the greatest tumor shrinkage. Tumors in the PBS and NSC groups continued to grow, while the OV group exhibited partial tumor regression but retained residual tumor burden, suggesting the limited efficacy of OV alone. We also observed the highest number of E1A-positive cells in NSCs-OV-treated tumors compared with direct OV administration, indicating that NSCs-OV therapy not only improves viral persistence but also enhances intratumoral viral replication, contributing to prolonged therapeutic effects. However, the lack of direct comparison with standard intratumoral delivery limits interpretation; these findings represent a proof of concept warranting further comparative studies.

To mimic the clinical scenario in which patients have pre-existing anti-adenovirus immunity, we immunized mice with adenovirus (Ad5) prior to GBM implantation. OV treatment was significantly less effective in NAb+ mice than in NAb⁻ mice. However, NSCs-OV therapy led to lower NAb titers and retained strong tumor-suppressive effects even in NAb+ mice. These results suggest that NSCs provide a protective niche for OV, reducing immune clearance and allowing effective tumor infiltration. Moreover, it has been shown that intratumoral OV injections with adenoviral vectors, particularly Ad5-based ones, provoke neuroinflammatory responses when injected into the brain parenchyma. While the GL261 model is moderately immunogenic in a Th1-skewed C57BL/6 background, the pronounced therapeutic effect observed here underscores the robust activity of the NSCs-OV approach within this immunological context. Therefore, NSC-mediated delivery may provide an immunological advantage by shielding normal neural tissue from direct viral exposure, while also creating a protective niche for the OV, reducing immune clearance, and promoting effective tumor infiltration. However, safety considerations must be acknowledged. Ad5-based OVs have been associated with risks of ventriculitis and viral meningitis, and current clinical trials typically exclude patients with significant ventricular involvement for this reason. Moreover, the rodent model used here is not permissive to Ad5 replication, which limits its ability to fully predict potential clinical toxicities. Therefore, while this study provides an initial feasibility assessment, further studies in permissive species such as Syrian hamsters or cotton rats will be necessary to comprehensively evaluate safety.

Our scRNA-seq analysis revealed a profound immunomodulatory effect of NSCs-OV, characterized by a significant reduction in immunosuppressive myeloid populations, particularly MDSCs. Further analysis demonstrated downregulation of inflammatory and immune-suppressive markers, including chemokines responsible for MDSC recruitment, leading to a reduction in neutrophil chemotaxis and inflammatory signaling. MDSCs are a heterogeneous population of immature myeloid cells that play pivotal roles in tumor progression and immune evasion. Our results demonstrated that NSCs-OV treatment significantly reduced the presence of CD11B+CD33+ MDSCs as well as CD14+ and CD15+ MDSCs subpopulations, which are key drivers of immune suppression in GBM, in the peripheral blood of humanized immune-system mouse models of orthotopic GBM xenografts, which is consistent with clinical observations. Notably, further experiments demonstrated that NSCs-OV effectively suppress the infiltration levels of CD15+ granulocytes and CD14+monocyte subsets within the TME. In cancer, MDSCs accumulate in response to chronic inflammation and tumor-derived signals, leading to immunosuppression, enhanced angiogenesis, and tumor metastasis. MDSCs suppress cytotoxic T cells (CD8+T cells), inhibit antitumor immune responses, and allow tumors to evade immune-mediated destruction. Our study demonstrates that NSCs-OV therapy not only suppresses MDSC accumulation, but also enhances CD8+ T cell activation. Flow cytometry analysis revealed that NSCs-OV treatment increased the proportion of CD8+ T cells expressing CD45RO, a marker of memory T cell activation, while PD-1+CD8+ T cells increased. However, the detailed mechanisms underlying this phenomenon require further investigation. Acknowledgements In addition to the decreased CD11b+CD33+ and CD14+/CD15+ MDSCs, we observed a marked increase in CD86+ (M1-like) and a decrease in CD206+ (M2-like) macrophages. This indicates that the therapeutic benefit of NSCs-OV may arise from both the suppression of immunosuppressive MDSCs and the polarization of macrophages toward a pro-inflammatory, antitumor state.

DEG analysis identified a significant reduction in S100A8/A9 levels in NSCs-OV-treated tumors. Elevated expression of S100A9, primarily in neutrophils and monocytes, correlates with poor cancer prognosis and resistance to immunotherapy. S100A8/A9 serves as an established marker and chemoattractant for MDSCs, which promote tumor immune evasion by suppressing T-cell responses and fostering an immunosuppressive TME. The reduction in S100A8/A9 levels suggests that NSCs-OV treatment not only enhances OV infiltration but also disrupts MDSC recruitment, thereby creating a more favorable local immune landscape for antitumor responses. This finding is consistent with our peripheral blood data, which indicate corresponding reductions in MDSC populations and suggest a possible broader systemic impact. Given the critical role of S100A8/A9 in immune suppression, we investigated whether paquinimod, a small-molecule S100A8/A9 inhibitor, could assess whether further suppression of this pathway would result in enhanced immune activation or tumor control. Although paquinimod is known to suppress MDSC activity through S100A8/A9 inhibition, it did not show efficacy as a monotherapy in our model. This may reflect the need for a more immunologically active TME. Our results showed that NSCs-OV combined with paquinimod treatment led to a more pronounced reduction in tumor growth compared with NSCs-OV alone, suggesting that inhibiting MDSC recruitment enhances OV-mediated tumor destruction. NSCs-OV combined with paquinimod treatment significantly decreased the proportion of MDSCs and increased CD8+ T cell infiltration in treated tumors. These findings support the rationale for combining NSCs-OV with paquinimod, as inhibiting S100A8/A9 not only reduces MDSC accumulation but may also promote CD8+ T cell activation, potentially through IFNγ-mediated pathways. Future mechanistic studies should assess whether IFNγ signaling contributes to the observed immune reprogramming. Moreover, alternative clinically approved agents with reported S100A8/A9-suppressive effects, such as tasquinimod, may offer translational opportunities for similar immune modulation. Paquinimod can also have a pro-tumor effect. Using the CT26 tumor model, paquinimod blocks extracellular TLR4 signaling, resulting in increased tumor growth and reduced efficacy of anti-PD-L1 therapy. Despite these promising results, several challenges remain to be overcome. The heterogeneity of GBM and its dynamic TME requires further investigation of patient-specific factors that may affect treatment response. It is important to note that the NSCs employed in this study were not clonal and exhibited potential immunosuppressive effects. This heterogeneity could influence both therapeutic efficacy and reproducibility across manufacturing batches. For future translational efforts, establishing standardized, GMP-compliant production processes with thorough phenotypic and functional characterization will be critical to ensuring consistent performance and safety. Using patient-derived GBM models may help assess variability in response to NSCs-OV and Paquinimod. Additionally, optimizing NSC delivery methods and improving OV engineering strategies could further enhance therapeutic efficacy.

Consistent with prior reports, our findings confirm that NSCs can effectively deliver OV therapy by protecting the virus from immune neutralization. In GBM, NSCs-OV therapy remodeled the TME by reducing S100A8/A9 expression, limiting MDSC recruitment, and enhancing CD8+ T cell responses. The addition of paquinimod further enhanced these effects, leading to robust tumor suppression and prolonged survival. These findings demonstrate the therapeutic potential of NSCs-OV therapy combined with S100A8/A9 inhibition for GBM. Clinical translation of this approach must now be prioritized to enhance patient outcomes.
