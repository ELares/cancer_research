# METTL3-VISTA axis-based combination immunotherapy for APC truncation colorectal cancer.

## Abstract

**OBJECTIVE**: Although immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy represents a bright spot in antitumor immunotherapy, its clinical benefits in colorectal cancer (CRC) are limited. Therefore, a new target for mediating CRC immunosuppression is urgently needed. Adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) mutations have been reported as early-stage characteristic events in CRC, but the role of truncated APC in the CRC immune microenvironment remains unclear and its clinical significance has yet to be explored.

**DESIGN**: Adenocarcinoma formation in the colon of the APCMin/+ mouse model, which displays features associated with the translation of truncated APC proteins, was induced by azoxymethane/dextran sodium sulfate. Multiplexed immunohistochemical consecutive staining on single slides and flow cytometry were used to explore the activation of immune cells and the expression of the immune checkpoint V-domain immunoglobulin suppressor of T-cell activation (VISTA) in the CRC tissues of APCWT and APCMin/+ mice. The construction of truncated APC vectors and an initial subserosal graft tumor mouse model was employed to mimic the tumor microenvironment (TME) during APC mutation. Methylated RNA immunoprecipitation-quantitative PCR assays were performed to investigate the N6-methyladenosine (m6A)-dependent transcriptional regulation of hypoxia-inducible factor-1 alpha (HIF1α) by methyltransferase-like protein 3 (METTL3). Mettl3fl/fl vil1-cre+/- mice were used to demonstrate that targeting METTL3 is a mediator that mitigates the deleterious effects of the APC978∆-HIF1α axis on antitumor immunity. A chimeric VISTA humanized mouse model was used to evaluate the drug efficacy of the VISTA-targeted compound onvatilimab.

**RESULTS**: We showed that APC978∆, a truncated APC protein, mediated overexpression of METTL3, resulting in m6A methylation of HIF1α messenger RNA and high expression of HIF1α. Furthermore, HIF1α promotes the migration of myeloid-derived suppressor cells to the TME by binding to the promoters of MCP-1 and MIF. In addition, HIF1α enhances the expression of the immune checkpoint VISTA on CRC cells, weakening tumor immune monitoring.

**CONCLUSIONS**: We elucidate that an underappreciated function of truncated APC in CRC is its ability to drive an immunosuppressive program that boosts tumor progression. Our work could provide a new perspective for the clinical application of immunotherapy in patients with CRC resistant to ICB therapy.

WHAT IS ALREADY KNOWN ON THIS TOPIC

Although immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy represents a bright spot in antitumor immunotherapy, its clinical benefits in colorectal cancer (CRC) are limited. Therefore, a new target for mediating CRC immunosuppression is urgently needed.

The truncated C-terminal adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) protein plays a pivotal role in tumor progression, but the biological functions and mechanisms of the truncated APC protein in the tumor microenvironment are not yet fully understood.

Introduction

 Colorectal cancer (CRC) ranks third in incidence and second in mortality worldwide. Even more concerning, among all newly diagnosed CRC cases, 20% of patients already have metastatic disease at the time of diagnosis, and an additional 25% of patients with localized disease will eventually develop metastasis. Metastasis is the leading cause of death from CRC. The prognosis for metastatic CRC is poor, with a 5-year survival rate of less than 20%. This highlights the urgent need for new therapies to improve the prognosis of patients with metastatic CRC who are not candidates for surgical resection. Beyond surgical therapy, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and targeted therapy for CRC, immunotherapy has emerged as a promising adjuvant therapy for primary unresectable and metastatic CRC. It is gradually gaining widespread attention and recognition as a means to enhance the treatment strategy for these challenging cases.

As an effective strategy in tumor immunotherapy, immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) cancer therapy enhances the antitumor immune response of T cells and improves antitumor defense through monoclonal antibodies targeting immune checkpoints. ICB therapy has achieved good efficacy in the treatment of various malignancies, such as melanoma, renal cell carcinoma, bladder cancer, and lung cancer. However, ICB showed activity only in mismatch-repair deficient (dMMR) CRC, while the majority (95%) of CRC with mismatch-repair-proficient (pMMR) have not shown activity, suggesting an urgent need for effective therapeutic strategies.

CRC is a complex disease with manifold pathogenic mechanisms, such as epigenetic alterations, genetic instability, gene fusions, and somatic mutations, which arise through a series of progressive histological changes, each accompanied by genetic alterations in specific oncogenes or tumor suppressor genes. Mutations in the function of the adenomatous polyposis coli (APC) gene trigger this molecular chain and histological changes. APC mutations are almost the only critical and early event observed in more than 80% of CRC occurrences. The APC gene, which appears to be the APC germline and the C-terminal truncated APC protein, lacks the structural domains required for binding to β-catenin, EB1, and microtubules, leading to inhibition of differentiation, proliferation activation, and chromosomal instability.

Although the loss of tumor suppressor function due to mutational loss of the C-terminal sequence of APC is thought to be a key event in CRC development, there is growing evidence that APC truncation plays a major role that may contribute to CRC development. However, the role of truncated APC proteins in the CRC microenvironment is unclear, and their clinical significance is yet to be explored.

We purchased an Apc truncation CRC mouse model that simulates the pathogenesis of human CRC. ApcMin/+ mice were produced using clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)/Cas9 KI technology and display features with mutations located at amino acid 850 of the Apc gene and mutations from Leu to a stop codon, resulting in premature termination of translation. Colitis is induced by the administration of dextran sodium sulfate (DSS), and azoxymethane (AOM) greatly accelerates adenocarcinoma formation in the intestinal tract of ApcMin/+ mice.

In the present study, we investigated the impact of truncated APC mutations on the chemotaxis of immune cells within the microenvironment, as well as the activation of immune checkpoints, in the context of CRC progression. Furthermore, we explored potential therapeutic strategies aimed at improving sensitivity to ICB therapy.

Results

APC mutations promote an immunosuppressive microenvironment in CRC progression

Using bioinformatics analysis from the tumor immune estimation resource (TIMER) database, we conducted a comparative study on the infiltration of CD8+T cells and myeloid-derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) in CRC with APC, TP53 or KRAS mutations. Our findings revealed a positive association between APC mutations in CRC and the accumulation of MDSCs, as well as a negative correlation with CD8+T cell infiltration (figure 1A). Furthermore, we compared the expression of B7/CD28 family genes in SW480 cells (featuring truncated APC), SW480-NC cells (SW480 cells transfected with a control vector) and SW480-WT APC cells (SW480 cells transfected with the wild-type APC vector) (GSE76307). Notably, C10orf54 (V-domain immunoglobulin suppressor of T-cell activation, VISTA) was found to be highly expressed in both SW480 and SW480-NC cells, implying that truncation of APC contributes to the upregulation of VISTA expression (figure 1B). In addition, bioinformatics analysis showed a strong positive correlation between dMMR and VISTA expression in CRC clinical tissues (online supplemental figure S1A). However, VISTA expression was higher in microsatellite stable/pMMR cell lines (online supplemental figure S1B). To investigate the impact of the APC mutation on the tumor microenvironment (TME) of CRC, we introduced an ApcMin/+ mouse model, which displayed features with translation of truncated Apc proteins. Adenocarcinoma formation in the intestinal tract of ApcMin/+ mice was induced by AOM/DSS, and the results showed that Apc mutations accelerated the progression of CRC (figure 1C,D). Employing multiplexed immunohistochemical consecutive staining on a single slide (MICSSS) on CRC tissues derived from ApcWT and ApcMin/+ mice, the results further revealed that Apc mutations suppressed the infiltration of CD8+T cell in CRC, promoted the infiltration of MDSCs and enhanced the expression of VISTA in CRC cells (figure 1E). However, these mutations had no discernible impact on VISTA expression in macrophages (online supplemental figure S2). Consistent with these findings, flow cytometry analysis of immune cells in CRC tissues from ApcWT and ApcMin/+ mice indicated similar results (figure 1F).

The APC978∆-mediated immunosuppressive microenvironment in CRC progression through HIF1α is partially β-catenin independent

To explore the mechanism by which APC mutations promote an immunosuppressive spectrum of CRC, we applied kyoto encyclopedia of genes and genomes (KEGG) analysis of RNA-seq profiles from ApcWT and ApcMin/+ tumors. According to the KEGG analysis, ApcMin/+ tumors showed upregulation of the hypoxia-inducible factor-1 (HIF1) pathway and hypoxia signaling pathway as two of the significant pathways (figure 2A). Furthermore, the gene set enrichment analysis (GSEA) of the gene expression omnibus (GEO) data sets revealed a positive enrichment of the hypoxia pathway in samples with APC mutations (figure 2B). To explore the APC functional domains that regulate HIF1α, we constructed three different APC truncators (APC1309∆ is the most prevalent form of APC truncating mutation) (figure 2C). After APC331∆ or APC1309∆ was transfected into the SW480 (cell line with APC mutations), western blot (WB) assays were used to assess the expression of HIF1α. The results showed that APC1309∆ increased the expression of HIF1α, while APC331∆ had no significant effect (figure 2D). In addition, the transfection of the APC978∆ truncator into HCT116 (cell line without APC mutations but with β-catenin mutations) had the same effect on HIF1α. Subsequent treatment with the WNT pathway inhibitor XAV939 partially counteracted the upregulation of HIF1α by APC978∆, and XAV939 inhibited the expression of nuclear β-catenin in HCT116 cells (figure 2E). We formulated a hypothesis that APC mutations inhibit CD8+T cell activation and promote MDSCs activation by regulating the expression of HIF1α in CRC. To validate whether APC978∆ regulates tumor immunity in a HIF1α-dependent manner in vitro, we obtained several blood samples from patients with CRC and isolated peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs). These PBMCs were then co-cultured with HCT116 cells expressing either APC978∆ alone or together with HIF1α-sg (using CRISPR/Cas9 to knock out HIF1α expression). Flow cytometry analysis showed that APC978∆ increased the number of MDSCs and decreased the number of CD8+T cells, while the addition of HIF1α-sg mitigated this effect (figure 2F).

APC978∆ mediates CRC immune escape in vivo through VISTA, which is upregulated by HIF1α

For in vivo experiments, LV-Apc978∆ was transfected into the mouse colon cancer cell line MC38, which is Apc wild-type. Then, we induced the formation of intestinal subserosal transplanted tumors in C57 mice and harvested the tumors for analysis (figure 3A). Compared with the control group, the Apc978∆ group exhibited an increase in tumor size and a higher incidence of liver metastases. However, these effects were mitigated by knocking out Hif1α (Hif1α-sg) (figure 3B). In addition, flow cytometry was performed for tumor-infiltrating immune cells isolated from the tumor tissue of each group of mice. Apc978∆ increased the infiltration of MDSCs and decreased the infiltration of CD8+T cells in the CRC tissues of MC38 tumor-bearing C57 mice, while the addition of Hif1α-sg mitigated this effect (figure 3C). MICSSS assays, which assessed the expression of CD8 and Gr-1 in mouse CRC tissues, further confirmed that Apc978∆ resulted in heightened MDSC infiltration and reduced CD8+T cell infiltration (figure 3D). It was worth noting that the introduction of Apc978∆ upregulated the expression of VISTA in CRC cells (figure 3D). Moreover, bioinformatics analysis data indicated a significantly positive correlation between VISTA and HIF1α in patients with colon cancer (GSE17538, R=0.6326, p<0.0001; figure 3E). Taken together, these findings suggest that HIF1α serves as a pivotal mediator in the conspicuous infiltration of MDSCs, the deficiency of CD8+T cell infiltration, and the elevated VISTA expression observed in APC978∆ CRC.

The APC978 ∆-METTL3-HIF1α axis upregulates the expression of VISTA in CRC cells

RNA methylation is an important part of epigenetic regulation, and N6-methyladenosine (m6A) RNA methylation is considered the most prevalent and prominent internal modification in eukaryotic cells. To investigate the mechanism by which APC978∆ regulates HIF1α, we used an m6A RNA quantitative ELISA kit to assess the total m6A levels of each cell line. The results indicated that APC978∆ contributed to the increased levels of m6A in HCT116 cells (figure 4A). We hypothesized that truncated APC regulates CD8+T cell and MDSC infiltration in CRC by affecting RNA methylation. Additionally, CRC tissues derived from ApcMin/+ mice exhibited higher methyltransferase-like protein 3 (METTL3) expression compared with those from ApcWT mice (figure 4B). Furthermore, we compared the expression of m6A regulators between SW480 cells (featuring truncated APC) and SW480-WT APC cells (SW480 cells transfected with the wild-type APC vector) (GSE76307). Notably, METTL3 was highly expressed in SW480 cells (figure 4C). Bioinformatics analysis indicated a significantly positive correlation between METTL3 and HIF1α (GSE17538) (online supplemental figure S5A). For the TME, the expression of METTL3 positively correlated with MDSC infiltration (figure 4D). Using immunofluorescence assays, we confirmed the colocalization of METTL3 (fused with an HA tag) and APC978∆ (fused with a Flag tag) in HCT116 cells (figure 4E). Co-immunoprecipitation (CoIP) assays further proved the protein interaction between METTL3 and APC truncators, except for APC331∆ (figure 4F). Importantly, METTL3 and APC-WT do not colocalize or interact due to their distinct subcellular localization: APC-WT is primarily in the cytoplasm and METTL3 is mainly in the nucleus (online supplemental figure S5C). Glutathione s-transferase (GST) pull-down assays revealed that METTL3 directly binds to APC978∆ but not to APC-WT (online supplemental figure S5D). The free energy of the interaction between APC978∆ (red chain) and METTL3 (purple) is −14.2 kcal/mol, indicating a strong interaction (online supplemental figure S5E). APC978∆ and METTL3 interact via specific amino acid residues. The details of the top three residue–residue interactions between APC978∆ and METTL3 are displayed in online supplemental figure S5F. To address the effect of METTL3 on HIF1α and VISTA in CRC cells, we efficiently transfected HCT116 cells with APC978∆, either alone or in conjunction with METTL3 knockdown. WB revealed that the addition of APC978∆ upregulation contributed to the elevated expression levels of METTL3, HIF1α and VISTA, which was subsequently counteracted after METTL3 silencing (figure 4G). We searched for m6A sites of HIF1α according to the web-available database sequence-based RNA adenosine methylation site preditor (SRAMP) (http://www.cuilab.cn/sramp/) and selected a high score m6A site. Using methylated RNA immunoprecipitation (MeRIP) assays, we confirmed that METTL3 mediated m6A-dependent transcriptional regulation to promote the expression of HIF1α (figure 4H). Immunohistochemistry (IHC) staining of the tissue microarray of clinical patients with CRC further indicated that the expression of METTL3 was positively correlated with the expression of HIF1α and VISTA, and negatively correlated with CD8+T cell infiltration (figure 4I&J). These results were consistently mirrored in WB assays (figure 4K). We collected 10 fresh human CRC samples, and used quantitative PCR (qPCR) and DNA gel electrophoresis experiments with 4 pairs of primers designed for different regions of the APC sequence (primer sequences list in online supplemental table S1). It was identified that there were 6 cases of APC truncation samples among the 10 CRC tissues, with 3 of them having the APC978∆ truncation (online supplemental figure S3A-D). IHC assays were conducted to examine the correlation between HIF1α, MDSCs, VISTA, and METTL3 in APC truncation samples. The findings revealed a positive correlation between HIF1α, MDSCs, VISTA, and METTL3 with APC truncation. Additionally, there was a negative correlation observed between the infiltration of CD8+T cells and APC truncation (online supplemental figure S3D).

The APC978 ∆-HIF1α axis upregulated downstream genes by promoting transcriptional activity

Cytokines and chemokines play crucial roles in tumor immunity, serving as critical mediators of interactions between cancer and non-cancer cells in the TME. To explore the target of HIF1α in regulating the infiltration of TME immune cells, we collected the supernatant of cells and detected the downstream cytokines and chemokines of HIF1α by protein microarray (figure 5A, online supplemental figure S4A). Subsequently, we conducted KEGG pathway and the gene ontology (GO) analyses to identify potential action pathways (figure 5B, online supplemental figure S4B). Together, the results showed that the downstream chemokines with significant differences were MIP-3A, MCP-1, and MIF. The messenger RNA levels of HIF1α and downstream factors in both NC cells and HIF1α-sh SW480 cells were determined by qRT‒PCR, as well as in NC cells and HIF1α-overexpressing (HIF1α-OE) HCT116 cells (figure 5C). ELISA assays were used to assess the expression of chemokines in the supernatants of control cells, HIF1α-OE cells, and HIF1α-sh cells (online supplemental figure S4D). We collected blood samples from patients with CRC and extracted the PBMCs. The PBMCs were co-cultured with CRC cells and stimulated by chemokines. After the combination of MCP-1 or MIF treatment, we observed an increase in the number of MDSCs and a decrease in the number of CD8+T cells (figure 5D). In addition, ELISA assays revealed that the introduction of the METTL3 inhibitor STM2457 could neutralize APC978∆-induced activation of MCP-1 and MIF. However, this inhibitory effect was reversed by the addition of HIF1α (figure 5E). Chromatin immuno-precipitation (ChIP) assays showed that HIF1α could bind to the promoters of MIF, MCP-1 and VISTA (figure 5F). To confirm the binding sites, we cloned these wild-type promoter regions and their mutations and performed a dual-luciferase reporter system assay with these constructs. As a result, the MIF, MCP-1, and VISTA promoter (WT) activity was robustly induced by HIF1α, and the mutations completely abrogated luciferase induction, demonstrating the role of HIF1α in the transcriptional upregulation of the MIF, MCP-1 and VISTA promoters (figure 5G). In addition, the GEO database showed strong positive correlations between MCP-1, MIF, and HIF1α (online supplemental figure S4D).

APC978∆ addition or METTL3 inhibition enhances the effectiveness of anti-VISTA therapy in CRC

In vivo, a Cre/LoxP system was used to obtain mice with homozygous colon Mettl3 knockout (Mettl3fl/fl vil1-Cre+/− mice), and then adenocarcinoma formation in the colon of Mettl3fl/+ vil1-Cre−/− and Mettl3fl/fl vil1-Cre+/− mice was induced by AOM/DSS (figure 6A). By counting the number of tumor nodules, we found a significant reduction in adenocarcinoma formation in the colon of Mettl3fl/fl vil1-Cre+/− mice (figure 6B,C). Moreover, flow cytometry analysis of immune cells in CRC of Mettl3fl/+ vil1-Cre−/− and Mettl3fl/fl vil1-Cre+/− mice indicated that Mettl3 expression correlates with abundant MDSCs and limited CD8+T cells (figure 6D). IHC staining of the CRC tissues of Mettl3fl/+ vil1-Cre−/− and Mettl3fl/fl vil1-Cre+/− mice further validated that the expression of VISTA was markedly lower after Mettl3 knockout (figure 6E). Additionally, a chimeric VISTA humanized mouse model (hVISTA) was constructed by knocking in the extracellular and transmembrane regions of the human VISTA gene and preserving the intracellular regions of the mouse VISTA gene. hVISTA mice were used to evaluate the drug efficacy of the VISTA-targeted compound onvatilimab. MC38-NC (APC WT) or MC38-LV-Apc978∆ tumor-bearing hVISTA mice were treated with STM2457 or anti-VISTA (onvatilimab) as monotherapy, or with a combination of STM2457 and onvatilimab. Notably, regardless of whether STM2457 was administered as monotherapy or in combination with onvatilimab, experimental group tumors grew significantly slower than the controls. In addition, onvatilimab treatment hardly affected MC38 control tumors, while combined STM2457 and onvatilimab treatment significantly inhibited tumor growth (figure 6F,H). Onvatilimab and/or STM2457 treatment prolonged the survival of MC38-LV-Apc978∆ tumor-bearing mice (figure 6G), but onvatilimab treatment did not prolong the survival of MC38-NC tumor-bearing mice (online supplemental figure S5B). Eliminating MDSCs partially countered the promotion of tumor growth and the inhibition of T cell cytotoxic activity caused by APC978∆ mutation. The increased expression of METTL3, VISTA and HIF1α caused by the APC978∆ mutation was also partially neutralized by depleting MDSCs (online supplemental figure S6A-C).

Discussion

APC mutations have been reported to be early-stage characteristic events in CRC. There is mounting evidence suggesting a pivotal role for truncated APC in CRC development, encompassing the maintenance of cell tumorigenic properties of cancer cells, stimulation of abnormal CRC cell migration, and promotion of chromosomal instability. With the progression of CRC development, cells gradually rely on the oncogenic function of truncated APC to survive or maintain their malignant phenotype, thereby reshaping the pattern of signaling networks. Consequently, a more comprehensive understanding of the regulatory effects of APC truncations in CRC cell signaling networks is imperative.

Nevertheless, the role of the truncated APC protein in the CRC microenvironment remains elusive, and its clinical significance has yet to be explored. In our previous experiments, an analysis of the TIMER database revealed a positive correlation between APC mutations in CRC and the number of infiltrating MDSCs, coupled with a negative correlation with the number of infiltrating CD8+T cells. Additionally, the introduction of Apc-mutant transgenic mice, along with an Apc truncator construct, demonstrated that the Apc truncator protein itself suppresses CD8+T cell infiltration while promoting MDSCs infiltration in CRC.

To unravel the mechanism by which APC mutations regulate immunosuppression in the CRC microenvironment, our investigation further unveiled that the hypoxia pathway was positively enriched in the mutant APC samples by the analysis of GEO data, and HIF1α was highly expressed in CRC tissues of APC truncated mutant mice. It has been reported that hypoxia is one of the most common conditions driving immunosuppression in the TME. In hypoxic regions of the TME, cancer cells can inhibit tumor-infiltrating lymphocyte activation and promote tumor infiltration of suppressive Tregs and MDSCs through deleterious metabolism, which leads to immunosuppression. In particular, the upregulation of HIF1α has been shown to affect various aspects of tumor immunity, including the activation and differentiation of immune cells in the TME, which is closely related to biological behavior in cancer cells. Therefore, our research specifically focuses on the scientific relationship between truncated APC and HIF1α to reveal the potential mechanism of CRC immune suppression driven by APC mutations.

It has been documented that the epigenetic regulation of tumor cells can impact CD8+T cell exhaustion through RNA methylation and changes in chromatin conformation. In the case of MDSCs, dynamic equilibrium dysregulation of histone deacetylases and histone acetyltransferases mediates the recruitment of polymorphonuclear MDSCs (PMN-MDSCs) into the CRC microenvironment, thereby inhibiting tumor immunity. Therefore, the exploration of both the function of immune cells in the TME regulated by epigenetics and the combination therapy of epigenetics drugs and tumor immune checkpoints provides a new direction for clinical tumor therapy.

RNA methylation is an important link in epigenetic regulation, with m6A RNA methylation being the most prevalent and prominent form of epigenetic modification in eukaryotes. Our studies showed that APC truncators upregulate the overall level of m6A in CRC cells and screened the m6A methyltransferase METTL3, whose expression was upregulated by APC truncation mutation, highly expressed in tumors and positively correlated with HIF1α expression, by the analysis of GEO data. We determined that the expression of HIF1α was regulated by the APC978∆-METTL3 signaling axis by WB. Furthermore, depletion of the methyltransferase METTL3 has been reported to increase tumor infiltration of cytotoxic CD8+T cells and increase the secretion of CXCL10, CXCL9, and IFN-γ from the TME, which enhanced the response of melanoma and microsatellite instability-high CRC to anti-programmed cell death protein-1 (PD-1) therapy. These results indicate the potential of targeting METTL3 to regulate immune checkpoints in CRC immunotherapy. Furthermore, considering the therapeutic effects of the selective METTL3 inhibitor (STM2457) demonstrated in acute myeloid leukemia and its dose-dependent inhibition of intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma cell proliferation. We investigated the role of STM2457 in MC38 or MC38-LV-Apc978∆ tumors. As a result, the STM2457 treatment group tumors grew significantly slower than controls.

As the first generation of immune checkpoint blockers (anti-PD-1/programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1) and anti-cytotoxic T-lymphocytes-associated protein 4) is effective in only a few patients, new strategies have been discovered and studied. These strategies target additional immune regulatory mechanisms to activate the patient’s own antitumor immune response. Emerging immune targets that can be used as predictive biomarkers in preclinical and clinical models include lymphocyte-activated gene 3, B and T lymphocyte attenuator, T-cell immunoglobulin and ITIM domain, T-cell immunoglobulin and mucin domain 3, and VISTA. Studies have clearly indicated that VISTA has both ligand signaling activity and receptor signaling activity, and VISTA and PD-L1 have very similar extracellular domains. Additionally, VISTA expression exists in many types of cells, such as T cells, dendritic cells, macrophages, monocytes, neutrophils, and cancer cells, in humans and mice. Moreover, since the ligand activity of VISTA directly or indirectly influences the suppression of T-cell immunity, overexpression of VISTA in tumor cells significantly promotes tumor progression. Studies have shown that compared with paracarcinoma tumors and normal tissues, VISTA expression is higher in CRC tumors, and blocking VISTA increases immune infiltration and preferentially reduces MDSCs in the tumor. In addition, targeting both VISTA and PD-L1, rather than one or the other, resulting in better tumor-clearing therapeutic outcomes in a mouse CRC model. VISTA is a potential emerging immune checkpoint in tumors, as well as a downstream target of the APC truncator-METTL3 axis; thus, anti-VISTA has shown promise in immunotherapy for APC-mutant CRC.

To explore the target of HIF1α regulating the infiltration of microenvironment immune cells, we collected the supernatant of cells and detected the downstream cytokines and chemokines of HIF1α by protein microarray. The downstream chemokines with significant differences are MCP-1 and the proinflammatory cytokine (MIF), which have HIF1-binding sites in their promoters, and the binding sites were confirmed by a dual-luciferase reporter system in our study. Furthermore, it was previously shown that MCP-1 (CCL2) and MIF play vital roles in recruiting MDSCs into tumor tissue, enhancing the immune suppressive microenvironment by the accumulation of MDSCs in the TME. For CD8+T cells, MDSC infiltration in tumor-bearing hosts inhibits T-cell activation and contributes to tumor immune escape in the TME. On the other hand, the proliferation and activation of CD8+T cells are suppressed by the high expression of VISTA, and we show that the binding of HIF1α to the VISTA promoter promotes VISTA expression on CRC cells.

Together, our study demonstrates that the signaling pathway axis of truncated APC-METTL3-VISTA can inhibit CD8+T cell infiltration, promote MDSC infiltration and upregulate VISTA expression, which all induce an immunosuppressive TME (figure 7). We reveal a new mechanism by which APC mutations target METTL3 to promote CRC immune evasion, which could provide an approach for the clinical application of immunotherapy in ICB therapy-insensitive patients with CRC.

Materials and methods

Cell culture

The human CRC cell lines SW480 and HCT116 and the mouse CRC line MC38 were purchased from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). Cells were passaged three times and then cryopreserved. In each experiment, cells were cultured under standard culture conditions for 3–5 days after resuscitation until the cell density was 50–70%. Cells were harvested on the same day and used for experiments, and cultured as described previously.

Mice and tumor models

All animal experiments were approved by the Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee of Southern Medical University (Guangzhou, China) (NFYY-2021–0567). Experimental protocols mice were maintained in a specific pathogen-free facility as previously described. Experimental groups were gender-matched and age-matched. Female C57 mice (3–5 weeks, 14–16 g) were obtained from the laboratory animal center of Southern Medical University, and tumor size was examined by a small animal live imaging system every 7 days after 1×105 MC38-NC or MC38-LV-Apc978∆ cells were injected underneath the intestinal serosa. Each group of tumor-bearing mice was intraperitoneally injected with anti-mouse VISTA (BE0310, Bio X Cell, 300 µg/mice, every 2 days), anti-CD8α (BE0118, Bio X Cell, 400 µg/mice, every 3 days), STM2457 (Selleck, s9870, 50 mg/kg), anti-Gr-1 (BE0075, Bio X Cell, 150 µg/mice) or immunoglobulin G (IgG) isotype control (BE0090, Bio X Cell, 150 µg/mice) at day 7, followed by every other day until tumor endpoint. Conditional homozygous Mettl3 knockout mice were obtained by crossing Mettl3fl/+ mice with vil1-cre transgenic mice and Mettl3fl/+ vil1-cre+/− mice. The PCR primers are shown in online supplemental table S1. APCMin/+ mice were purchased from Cyagen. The formation of adenocarcinoma in mice was detected by HE staining. A chimeric hVISTA, constructed by knocking in the extracellular and transmembrane regions of the human VISTA gene and preserving the intracellular regions of the mouse VISTA gene, was obtained from Cyagen. hVISTA was used for drug evaluation to detect VISTA-targeted compounds (onvatilimab) in immunocompetent mice. Each group of tumor-bearing mice was intraperitoneally injected with anti-CD8α (BE0118, Bio X Cell, 400 µg/mice, every 3 days), anti-Gr-1 (BE0075, Bio X Cell, 150 µg/mice) or IgG isotype control (BE0090, Bio X Cell, 150 µg/mice) at day 7, followed by every other day until tumor endpoint. METTL3 inhibitor STM2457 (Selleck, s9870, 50 mg/kg) or anti-VISTA onvatilimab (AtaGenix, ATAD00548, 10 mg/kg) were given at day 7, followed by every other day until tumor endpoint via intraperitoneal injection.

Multiplexed immunohistochemical consecutive staining on a single slide

The sections were incubated overnight using primary antibodies. Primary rabbit monoclonal antibodies against VISTA (Cell Signaling Technology, 54979, 1:400). and CD8 (Cell Signaling Technology, 98 941T, 1:800) were used. Purified mouse antibodies against Gr-1 (BioLegend, 108401, 1:600) and pancytokeratin (Abcam, ab7753, 1:500) were used. An horseradish peroxidase (HRP)-conjugated secondary antibody (ZSGB-BIO, Beijing, PV-6000) and an 3-amino-9-ethylcarbazole (AEC) staining kit (Solarbio, A2010) were used in the experiment. After each staining cycle, slides were scanned using a whole slide scanner, followed by manual removal of overlays, chromogenic enzyme substrate removal, use of peroxidase blocks and serum-free protein blocks before additional single IHC stains were used. For quantification of positive cell density, images were scanned with a digital pathology section scanner (MAGSCANNER KF-PRO-400), and images were cropped from virtual slides in K-Viewer and recolored by ImageJ. Three randomly selected fields in each tumor were counted for positive cell density.

RNA total m6A quantification

Total levels of m6A in human CRC cells were determined by a human N6-methyladenosine (m6A) enzyme-linked immunoassay kit (Jingmei Biological Technology, Jiangsu, China, #JM-1462H2). RNA was briefly bound to the capture antibody in each reaction well for subsequent detection. After several incubations, the m6A content was colorimetrically determined at a 450 nm wavelength and calculated according to a standard curve.

MeRIP and real-time qPCR

The experimental group of SW480 cells was treated with STM2457 (7.5 ng/mL), and 48 hours later, 1×107 control group cells and experimental group cells were harvested separately, and lysed in radio-immunoprecipitation assay (RIPA) lysis buffer. A Magna MeRIP m6A kit (Merck Millipore, #17–10499) was used in the MeRIP assay, and all experimental procedures were performed according to the instructions. In brief, 5 µg anti-m6A (ABclonal, A17924) was incubated with 50 µL protein A/G immunoprecipitation magnetic beads to obtain a magnetic bead-antibody complex. 10 µl of cell lysates were used as the input sample, and 100 µl of control group cell lysates and 100 µl of experimental group cell lysates were added to 900 µl of magnetic bead-antibody complex separately and then incubated and rotated for 12 hours at 4°C. RNA‒protein immune complexes were washed six times and incubated with proteinase K buffer. Finally, RNAs were extracted using phenol chloroform RNA and purified for qPCR analysis with the exact primers for m6A modification in HIF1α in the assay (primers for MeRIP-qPCR are shown in online supplemental table S1). Normalization of the relative enrichment was performed on the input as follows: %Input=1/10 × 2Ct[IP]–Ct[input]. The m6A site of HIF1α was predicted using the SRAMP website (http://www.cuilab.cn/sramp/).

Statistical analysis

GraphPad was used for statistical and graphical analyses. Student’s t-tests were carried out for MICSSS, tumor nodules, flow cytometric analyses, and RT‒PCR. The significance of the correlation was determined by the Pearson χ² test. Kaplan-Meier analysis was used to determine the animal survival benefit. The Cox proportional hazards test was used for multivariate analysis. Statistical analysis was performed using GraphPad Prism V.8. Statistical significance was established at p<0.05.
