# Engineered PD-L1-targeted multifunctional proteins with self-prolonged circulation for enhanced tumor photothermal-immunotherapy.

## Abstract

In this study, a novel Immunoglobulin G (IgG) binding, Programmed Cell Death-Ligand 1 (PD-L1) targeting affibody, IgBD-ZPD-L1, was constructed by genetically fusing the PD-L1-specific affibody ZM1 with the IgG-binding domain (IgBD). IgBD-ZPD-L1 could simultaneously bind to IgG, PD-L1 receptor protein, and PD-L1-positive tumor cells. Furthermore, the fusion of IgBD significantly prolonged the in vivo circulation half-life of IgBD-ZPD-L1 by binding to endogenous IgG, enhancing tumor accumulation and immunotherapeutic effects. Subsequently, anti-angiogenic/immunotherapeutic and dual-targeted combination immunotherapy strategies based on IgBD-ZPD-L1 significantly improved tumor immunotherapy efficacy. Finally, IgBD-ZPD-L1 was conjugated with Indocyanine green (ICG) to construct a novel tumor-targeting photosensitizer, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. In vivo, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 enabled clear fluorescence imaging of tumors for diagnosis and displayed excellent photothermal properties for tumor thermal ablation, demonstrating potent antitumor effects. More importantly, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy exerted significant therapeutic effects on primary tumors and upregulated antitumor immune responses, synergistically enhancing the efficacy of immunotherapy and inhibiting tumor metastasis. As a result, this photothermal/immunotherapy strategy ultimately achieved a synergistic therapeutic effect greater than the sum of individual treatments. In conclusion, these results suggested that IgBD-ZPD-L1 could be developed as a multifunctional IgG-delivery platform for combination tumor immunotherapy, providing a promising theoretical basis for clinical development and translation of tumor immunotherapeutic strategies.

Graphical abstract

Introduction

The body's immune system can eliminate abnormally proliferating tumor cells and inhibit tumor growth via the “Cancer-Immunity Cycle.” However, tumor cells escape immune surveillance and eradication by upregulating immune checkpoint molecules, thereby forming a complex immunosuppressive microenvironment that fosters tumor progression and metastasis. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) can markedly relieve tumor-induced immunosuppression, reactivate immune cell function, and facilitate the destruction and clearance of tumor cells, thus achieving antitumor therapeutic effects. Monoclonal antibody immunotherapy targeting Programmed Cell Death-1/Programmed Cell Death-Ligand 1 (PD-1/PD-L1) has become an important clinical option following tumor chemotherapy, radiotherapy and targeted therapy, providing new hope for patients with advanced malignancies. Nevertheless, the overall response rate to monoclonal antibody immunotherapy in malignancies remains relatively low. Only about 20 % of patients with advanced non-small cell lung cancer without driver gene mutations benefit, while merely ∼5 % of patients with mismatch repair-deficient (dMMR) or microsatellite instability-high (MSI-H) colorectal cancer show a response. The main reasons include the deficiency of immune cells in solid tumor and poor tumor penetration of antibody drugs, causing low response rate of ICIs. In addition, most monoclonal antibodies used in clinical practice are primarily humanized antibodies, with risk of heterogenicity, making them prone to immune-related adverse events such as rash, pneumonia, hepatitis, and diarrhea. In some instances, life-threatening severe hypersensitivity reactions, such as anaphylactic shock and immune-mediated myocarditis, may also develop. Although fully humanized monoclonal antibodies, such as nivolumab and ipilimumab, have lower immunogenicity and fewer side effects, the high cost severely burdens patients and limits broad clinical application. Therefore, the urgent need remains to develop novel and effective antitumor immunotherapeutic agents to expand the clinical application of cancer immunotherapy.

Affibodies, as non-immunoglobulin scaffold proteins, possess high antigen-binding specificity and show neither sequence nor structural homology with antibodies, featuring low immunogenicity (without Fc region), small molecular size (∼6.5 kDa), simple and compact structure, excellent tissue penetration, and physicochemical stability favorable for modification. These characteristics make them ideal protein drugs for tumor-targeted diagnosis and therapy. In preliminary work, based on the PD-L1-specific affibody ZM1, we have innovatively designed and constructed a novel dimeric affibody, ZPD-L1. Studies demonstrated that ZPD-L1 can specifically target PD-L1-positive tumors in vivo and exhibit stronger antitumor immunotherapeutic effects. Although ZPD-L1 achieves higher affinity through dimerization, the molecular weight remained low (∼16 kDa) with a short half-life in vivo, leading to rapid metabolic clearance and limited antitumor efficacy. Therefore, it is of great significance to further modify ZM1 for extended half-life and enhanced in vivo antitumor activity, which would promote its clinical translation and application.

Conventional exogenous long-acting modifications of proteins, such as Polyethylene glycol modification and nanoparticle-based delivery, may extend in vivo half-life and augment bioactivity. However, limitations in biocompatibility and immunogenicity hinder their clinical translation. Plasma proteins such as albumin and IgG, due to neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn)-mediated recycling, have much longer serum half-lives (2–4 weeks). IgG's half-life (∼23 days) even exceeds that of albumin (∼19 days), making it an ideal endogenous carrier for long-acting drugs. Researches have shown that the Fab-selective IgG binding domain SPGC3FabRR (IgG Binding Domain, IgBD) can bind to IgG and leverage FcRn-mediated recycling to effectively extend the circulation time and enhance the in vivo activity of protein therapeutics. Moreover, IgBD can serve as a platform for antibody drug delivery, enabling multitarget tumor therapy in a flexible and feasible manner while substantially reducing the cost of constructing multifunctional tumor-targeted agents. Therefore, fusing IgBD to ZM1 affibody to achieve long-acting modification by leveraging endogenous IgG is an ideal strategy to enhance the antitumor immunotherapeutic efficacy.

Apart from the intrinsic activity of therapeutic agents, tumor immunotherapy efficacy also depends on the recruitment of tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs). Tumors lacking TILs (“cold”) show compromised response, and most success is observed in TIL-rich (“hot”) tumors. Thus, optimizing the drug alone is insufficient, and combinatorial therapy can improve the tumor immune microenvironment and synergistically boost efficacy, which is an important strategy for further expanding the clinical applications of tumor immunotherapy. Photothermal therapy (PTT), as a novel noninvasive and tumor-targeted treatment modality, utilizes photothermal transduction agents (PTAs) to convert light energy into heat upon near-infrared (NIR) irradiation, thereby generating localized hyperthermia and ablating tumor tissues. PTT efficacy is not limited by tumor hypoxia and can exploit the increased thermal sensitivity of tumors in an acidic, hypoxic microenvironment. PTT additionally induces reactive oxygen species (ROS) and immunogenic cell death (ICD) of tumors, increases the release of damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), promotes dendritic cells (DCs) maturation and immune activation, and overall enhances antitumor immune response. Thus, PTT combined with immunotherapy achieves synergistic antitumor effects, suppresses tumor metastasis and recurrence, and holds important clinical translational potential. Indocyanine green (ICG), an organic small-molecule of PTA, exhibits excellent photothermal and fluorescent properties and is approved for clinical tumor imaging and sentinel lymph node identification. However, ICG has a short half-life (3–4 min) and lacks tumor targeting ability, limiting its direct application in tumor PTT. Tumor-targeted delivery of ICG can significantly enhance its therapeutic impact in tumor PTT. In summary, ICG-mediated PTT combined with immunotherapy represents an effective strategy to overcome the limited efficacy of monotherapy in tumor immunotherapy and holds significant clinical and translational value.

In this study, we fused IgBD to the N-terminus of the affibody ZM1 via a (G4S)3 linker and introduced a cysteine residue at the C-terminus to enable disulfide bond formation, thus constructing a novel IgG-binding and PD-L1-targeting dimeric fusion protein, IgBD-ZPD-L1. This fusion protein was subsequently produced using an Escherichia coli (E. coli) expression system. We first assessed the in vitro IgG and PD-L1 binding properties of IgBD-ZPD-L1, then analyzed the effects of IgBD-mediated endogenous IgG binding on serum half-life extension and the enhancement of in vivo antitumor immunotherapeutic efficacy. Meanwhile, we established a multifunctional IgG-based antibody drug delivery platform through IgBD-ZPD-L1, constructing anti-angiogenic/immunotherapeutic and dual-targeting immunotherapeutic agents to improve combination immunotherapy. Finally, IgBD-ZPD-L1 was conjugated with ICG via an N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) ester reactive group to construct a novel tumor-targeted agent, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, for integrated tumor fluorescence imaging diagnosis and photothermal/immunotherapy. This approach provides a theoretical foundation and new perspective for integrated clinical tumor diagnosis and treatment (Scheme 1).

Materials and methods

Expression and preparation of IgBD-ZPD-L1

To prepare the IgG-binding and PD-L1-targeting fusion protein of IgBD-ZPD-L1, the IgBD domain was fused to the N-terminus of ZM1 affibody via a (G4S)3 linker, with a C-terminal cysteine added. The encoded gene was synthesized by GenScript (Nanjing, China), and the optimized fusion gene was cloned into the pQE30 plasmid containing a 6-His tag via BamHI and SalI restriction sites, yielding the expression plasmid of pQE30-IgBD-ZPD-L1. The expression plasmid was transformed into competent M15 E. coli for large-scale culture. Protein expression was induced with 0.1 mM isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) at 28 °C, 150 rpm overnight. After induction, cells were harvested by centrifugation, resuspended in lysis buffer (50 mM phosphate, pH 8.0, 300 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole), and disrupted by sonication on ice. The supernatant was collected by high-speed centrifugation and incubated with high-affinity Ni-NTA resin (GenScript, Nanjing, China) to purify the target IgBD-ZPD-L1 protein. Finally, the obtained target protein was dialyzed with PBS and the final protein concentration was determined by the BCA protein assay kit. To verify the correct preparation and dimeric form of IgBD-ZPD-L1, sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) assays were performed under both non-reducing conditions (without β-mercaptoethanol, −2 ME) and reducing conditions (with β-mercaptoethanol, +2 ME).

Construction of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1

To construct ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, ICG-NHS ester (Ruixi Biological Technology, Xi'an, China) dissolved in DMSO was mixed with IgBD-ZPD-L1 protein solution at a 3:1 M ratio and reacted for 2 h at room temperature, protected from light. The reaction mixture was then dialyzed overnight at 4 °C in PBS to remove excess free ICG-NHS. Successful construction of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 was confirmed by fluorescence scanning (Alliance 4.7, UVITEC, Cambridge UK) of the electrophoresis gel, as well as by Ultraviolet–visible (UV–Vis) spectrophotometry (Persee TU-1950) for full-wavelength absorption spectra.

In vitro binding activity analysis of IgBD-ZPD-L1

To assess the in vitro binding activity of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to IgG, the IgBD-ZPD-L1 and mouse IgG (mIgG, Beijing solarbio science & technology co.,ltd) were mixed at various molar ratios (1:1, 1:2, and 1:3) at room temperature for 1 h. The mixtures were loaded onto a prepacked gel filtration column (GE Healthcare Superdex™ 75 Increase 10/300 GL), and elution profiles at 280 nm were recorded. The retention volumes of various fractions were calculated via size-exclusion chromatography (SEC), thus verifying the formation of IgG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes. In order to further validate the binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to mIgG, IgBD-ZPD-L1 or ZPD-L1 was mixed with mIgG at a molar ratio of 1:2 and incubated at room temperature for 1 h before being analyzed by SEC column. After separation of the mixture (Load) by SEC, IgBD-ZPD-L1 and ZPD-L1 in the protein peak containing IgG (SEC) were analyzed by SDS-PAGE. Then the surface plasmon resonance (SPR) assays were performed to evaluate the binding properties of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to IgG and PD-L1 in vitro. mIgG and PD-L1 protein (Novoprotein Scientific Inc. China) were immobilized separately onto CM5 sensor chips (Cytiva, GE Life), and serial dilutions of IgBD-ZPD-L1 were injected with HBS-EP + buffer (Cytiva, GE Life) as analytes, and the CM5 chip was regenerated using Glycine 1.5 (Cytiva, GE Life). The association and dissociation processes were monitored and analyzed using the Biacore™ ×100 system. Moreover, Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) was also performed to detect the binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to IgG and PD-L1 in vitro. Each well of the ELISA plate was loaded with 100 μl of IgG or PD-L1 protein solution and coated at 4 °C overnight. The next day, the wells were blocked with 200 μl of 5 % BSA in PBS at 37 °C for 2 h. Different concentrations of biotin-labeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 protein (100 μl/well) were then added, followed incubated at 37 °C for 1 h and three washes with 200 μl of PBST (PBS+300 mM NaCl+0.05 % Tween 20). The biotin-labeled mIgG (Bioss, China) was used as control group. Then the HRP-conjugated streptavidin (HRP-Stv, Beijing solarbio science & technology co.,ltd, 100 μl) was added to each well and incubated at 37 °C for 30 min. After five washes, 100 μl of TMB substrate solution was added for color development at room temperature for 2–3 min, followed by termination with 50 μl of 2M HCl, and the absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a microplate reader.

MLE-12 (murine alveolar epithelial), MC38 (murine colon carcinoma), and 4T1 (murine breast cancer) cell lines were obtained from the Cell Bank of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Shanghai, China). All cell lines were cultured in DMEM supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum, 100 U/mL penicillin, and 100 μg/mL streptomycin at 37 °C in a 5 % CO2 atmosphere. PD-L1 expression on MC38, 4T1 and MLE-12 cells was determined by incubation with PE-labeled anti-PD-L1 antibody (BioLegend, CA, USA) followed by analysis with flow cytometry. Subsequently, IgBD-ZPD-L1 was labeled with 6-Carboxyfluorescein (FAM) and FAM labeled-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1, 200 nM) was incubated separately with PD-L1-positive MC38 and PD-L1-negative MLE-12 cells. After incubation and rinsing with PBS, cells were analyzed using flow cytometry (BD LSRFortessa) for specific binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to cellular PD-L1 receptor. MC38 and MLE-12 cells (2 × 104/well) were also seeded into 96-well plates and incubated overnight before adding FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 for 1 h to perform cellular fluorescence experiments. Additionally, to confirm PD-L1-dependent cellular binding, MC38 cells were pre-blocked with IgBD-ZPD-L1 (4 μM) to saturate the PD-L1 receptor prior to incubation with FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1, and analyzed via flow cytometry and fluorescence microscopy.

To verify the cellular binding activity of the IgG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complex, FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 was mixed with mIgG at a molar ratio of 1:2 in vitro and incubated for 1 h to form the mIgG-FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complex. Subsequently, mIgG-FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (200 nM) was incubated with PD-L1-positive MC38 cells at room temperature for 1 h, followed analysis using flow cytometry. Besides, the MC38 cells were also pre-blocked with IgBD-ZPD-L1 to saturate the PD-L1 receptor prior to incubation with mIgG-FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 to confirm PD-L1-dependent cellular binding as described in the above cell-binding flow cytometry assay of FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1.

In vitro cytotoxicity

To assess the cytotoxicity of IgBD-ZPD-L1, MC38 and MLE-12 cells were plated in 96-well plates and treated with IgBD-ZPD-L1 (concentration range: 0.1–10 μM) for 24 h. PBS-treated cells were used as control (100 % survival). The cellular phototoxicity of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal therapy (PTT) in vitro was evaluated by incubating MC38 and MLE-12 cells (2 × 104/well) with ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 for 1.5 h, followed by PBS washes and exposure to 808 nm NIR irradiation (1.5 W/cm2) for 5 min. Cell viability after 24 h was determined via CCK-8 assay. For cell live/dead staining, MC38 cells were treated as above, followed by the addition of Calcein-AM and PI (Dojindo, Japan) and DCFH-DA (Beyotime Biotech) to assess intracellular ROS production via fluorescence microscopy.

In vivo tumor targeting validation

To evaluate in vivo tumor targeting of IgBD-ZPD-L1, female C57BL/6 mice (16–18 g; Beijing Huafukang Biotechnology Co., Ltd) were subcutaneously inoculated with 2 × 106 MC38 cells to establish a subcutaneous xenograft tumor model. Tumor dimensions were measured daily, and volume calculated as: V = (L × W2)/2 (L: Long diameter/Length; W: Short diameter/Width). When tumor volume reached ∼200 mm3, two procedures were performed. First, tumor-bearing mice was sacrificed to harvest tumor tissue for immunofluorescence staining with anti-PD-L1 antibody, confirming high PD-L1 expression at the tissue level. Second, tumor-bearing mice were received intravenous tail injection of equimolar FAM-ZPD-L1 or FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1. After 6 h, tumors were collected for co-immunostaining of PD-L1 to analyze the targeted distribution of IgBD-ZPD-L1 within tumor tissues.

For in vivo tumor-targeted fluorescence imaging of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, tumor-bearing mice (n = 3; ∼200 mm3) received intravenous ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (5 mg/kg) or equimolar ICG-ZPD-L1, and in vivo fluorescence imaging was performed using an IVIS optical imaging system (Ex: 745 nm, Em: 840 nm) at 2, 5, 8 and 24 h post-injection. After imaging, the mice were euthanized, and major organs (brain, heart, liver, spleen, lung, kidney, muscle) and tumor tissues were excised for ex vivo fluorescence imaging. Quantitative analysis of fluorescence intensity in tissues was performed using Living Image 4.3.1 software.

Photothermal performance of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1

To verify the in vitro photothermal performance of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, 100 μl of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 solution (10 μM) was added to a 96-well plate. The solution temperature changes of 808 nm NIR irradiation for 90 s under different output powers (0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5 W/cm2), or every 30 s (0–90 s) under an output power of 1.5 W/cm2 were recorded by an infrared thermal imaging camera (VarioCAM HD Head 680). The PBS and ICG were used as the negative and positive groups. For the in vivo photothermal conversion analysis of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, subcutaneous MC38 tumor-bearing mice (∼200 mm3, n = 3) were administered ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (5 mg/kg) intravenously. After 8 h, tumor sites were irradiated with 808 nm NIR (1.5 W/cm2) for 5 min, and thermal images were acquired every minute. Tumor temperatures were calculated with IRBIS 3.1 analysis software.

In vivo antitumor therapy

To analyze serum half-life and evaluate the long-circulating effect conferred by IgBD fusion, female C57BL/6 mice (16–18 g) were intravenously injected with equal molar quantities of biotin-labeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 or ZPD-L1. Then the blood samples were collected at various post-injection time points, and serum protein concentrations were determined by ELISA. For analysis of enhanced tumor immunotherapy by IgBD-ZPD-L1, mice were subcutaneously injected with 2 × 106 MC38 cells. When tumor volume reached ∼80 mm3, mice were randomized and treated intravenously with IgBD-ZPD-L1 (10 mg/kg) or equimolar ZPD-L1 every three days, while PBS was used as vehicle control. Tumor size and body weight were measured daily. After treatment, blood was analyzed for hepatic, renal, and cardiac biochemical markers (ALT, AST, CREA, UREA, UA, CK), and major organs (brain, heart, liver, spleen, lungs, kidney) were prepared for HE staining to assess short-term biosafety.

For tumor dual-targeted therapy, IgBD-ZPD-L1 was incubated with anti-vascular endothelial growth factor-A antibody (αVEGF) and anti-signal regulatory protein α (αSIRPα, BioXcell, USA) to yield tumor dual-target immunotherapy agents of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1. First of all, the ELISA assay was used to confirm the binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to αVEGF and αSIRPα, and the in vitro activity of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes. Briefly, the biotin-labeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 was mixed with αVEGF and αSIRPα at a molar ratio of 1:2 and reacted at room temperature for 1 h. The reaction products were then added to ELISA plates pre-coated with VEGFA or SIRPα proteins (Novoprotein Scientific Inc. China) and incubated for 1 h. After washing with PBST, the wells were added with HRP-Stv and TMB substrate solution for color development, followed by termination with 50 μl of 2M HCl, and the absorbance at 450 nm was measured using a microplate reader. The biotin-labeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 was used as the negative control. Besides, the reaction products were also added to ELISA plates pre-coated with PD-L1 protein, and tested using the same ELISA assay procedure to verify the binding of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 to PD-L1. The biotin-labeled mIgG was used as the negative control. Moreover, the reaction products (IgBD-ZPD-L1: 4 mg/kg) were intravenously injected into female C57BL/6 mice (16–18 g) to analyze the in vivo blood stability of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes. Then the blood samples were collected at 5, 30, 60, 120, and 300 min post-injection, and the serum was added to ELISA plates pre-coated with VEGF or SIRPα proteins for incubation of 2 h, followed by PBST washing and addition of HRP-Stv for color development. The reaction was then stopped with 50 μl of 2M HCl, and the absorbance at 450 nm was measured.

To investigate the anti-tumor effect of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1, MC38 subcutaneous xenograft tumor mice models were established as previously described. When tumor volumes reached ∼80 mm3, mice received tail vein injections of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (IgBD-ZPD-L1: 4 mg/kg) or equimolar αVEGF + ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα+ZPD-L1. Mice received PBS were used as the control group. Tumor volume and body weight were recorded daily. After anti-angiogenesis/immune combination therapy, tumor tissue was subjected to CD31 immunofluorescence staining to investigate the anti-tumor angiogenic effect of IgBD-ZPD-L1 in dual-targeted immunotherapy. The area fraction of tumor vessel was quantitatively analyzed by Image J.

MC38 tumor-bearing mice (∼150 mm3, n = 5) were randomized into PBS, NIR, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR, and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR groups. Mice received intravenous ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (5 mg/kg) or equimolar ICG-ZPD-L1, followed 5 h later by 808 nm NIR (1.5 W/cm2) irradiation of tumors for 10 min. Tumor volume and mouse weight were monitored daily. After treatment, tumors were analyzed by weighing, immunofluorescence staining (Ki67, TUNEL), and HE histology for assessment of proliferation, apoptosis, and morphological changes. The area fraction of Ki67 was also quantitatively analyzed by Image J.

To establish a distant tumor model, female C57BL/6 mice were subcutaneously inoculated with 5 × 105 and 2 × 106 MC38 cells in the left and right flanks, respectively. When right-side (primary) and left-side (distant) tumor volumes reached ∼80 mm3 and ∼20 mm3, respectively, mice were allocated to PBS, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR, and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR groups (n = 6). ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR groups received intravenous injections of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 or ICG-ZPD-L1 (3.5 nmol) at days 7, 10 and 16 post-tumor inoculation, followed 5 h later by NIR irradiation (1 W/cm2, 10 min) of the primary tumors. Afterward, mice were sacrificed and both primary and distant tumors were collected for immunofluorescence and flow cytometry analyses, to investigate the mechanisms of distant tumor inhibition by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy. PTT-induced ICD in tumors can trigger the exposure of cytosolic calreticulin (CRT) and the release of high-mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), which promote the maturation of DCs and significantly enhance antitumor immune responses. To verify the ICD-inducing effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT, immunofluorescence staining of CRT and HMGB1 was first performed on primary tumors. Subsequently, distant tumors were enzymatically dissociated to single-cell suspensions for flow cytometry of mature DCs (CD45+CD11c+CD86+), CD8+ T cells (CD45+CD3+CD8+), tumour-associated macrophages 1 (M1-TAMs, CD11b+F4/80+MHCII+), and tumour-associated macrophages 2 (M2-TAMs, CD11b+F4/80+CD206+). Finally, liver, renal and cardiac function markers in serum and HE staining of major organs were also performed to assess the in vivo short-term biosafety of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1.

To further verify the inhibitory effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy on metastatic tumors, a breast cancer lung metastasis model was established in Balb/c mice by subcutaneous injection of 1 × 106 PD-L1-positive and luciferase-expressing 4T1 cells in the right flank, followed by intravenous injection of 5 × 105 luciferin-4T1 cells to induce pulmonary metastases once subcutaneous tumor volumes reached ∼80 mm3. Mice were divided into four groups (n = 5) and treated following the scheme for distant tumor models. Tumor volume and body weight were monitored daily. On the 19 days after inoculation, all mice were injected intraperitoneally with potassium luciferin (150 μl), sacrificed, and lungs were collected for fluorescence imaging and HE histology to assess metastatic tumor burden.

Statistical analysis

Statistical analysis of multiple comparisons between groups was performed using one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) with SPSS software version 13.0. All results are expressed as mean ± standard deviation (SD). Statistical significance was defined as P < 0.05. Differences between groups were indicated as follows: ∗ (P < 0.05), ∗∗ (P < 0.01) and ∗∗∗ (P < 0.001).

Results and discussion

Preparation of IgBD-ZPD-L1 and construction of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1

As illustrated in Fig. 1A, the IgBD was fused to the N-terminus of the ZM1 affibody via a (G4S)3 linker, with a cysteine residue added to the C-terminus to enable dimerization through disulfide bonds, resulting in the novel dimeric PD-L1-targeting fusion protein IgBD-ZPD-L1. During the induction and preparation of IgBD-ZPD-L1, SDS-PAGE analysis revealed a specific protein band at approximately 15 kDa post-induction (lane 2, Fig. 1B), compared to pre-induction samples (lane 1). Upon cell lysis by ultrasonication and centrifugation, the protein predominantly remained in the supernatant (lane 3), with little to no presence in the pellet (lane 4), indicating that the protein is soluble. The supernatant was then purified using the Ni-NTA affinity chromatography column, with no target protein detected in the flow-through (lane 5), and the final eluted protein displaying as a single band (lane 6). Additionally, non-reducing SDS-PAGE revealed a band at approximately 30 kDa (lane 3, Fig. 1C), consistent with the calculated molecular weight of IgBD-ZPD-L1, which is roughly twice the size observed under reducing condition (∼15 kDa, lane 1). Meanwhile, as shown in Fig. S1, under non-reducing condition, the diameter of IgBD-ZPD-L1 was approximately 4 nm. However, after the addition of 2-ME to reduce IgBD-ZPD-L1, its diameter significantly decreased to ∼2 nm, which was consistent with the results of SDS-PAGE, confirming the dimeric state of IgBD-ZPD-L1.

To construct ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, ICG was conjugated to IgBD-ZPD-L1 through an NHS-ester crosslinking reaction. As shown in Fig. 1C, both reducing (lane 2) and non-reducing (lane 4) SDS-PAGE results demonstrated a slight upward shift in band size for ICG-conjugated protein, primarily attributable to the added molecular weight of ICG. Notably, ICG conjugation did not alter the oligomeric state of IgBD-ZPD-L1. Moreover, fluorescent imaging of the gel further confirmed successful conjugation. The fluorescent signals from the ICG-conjugated protein were clearly observed under both reduced and non-reduced conditions (Fig. 1C, fluorescent imaging). As shown in Fig. 1D, UV–Vis absorption spectra showed a characteristic absorption peak at around 780 nm for ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, matching that of ICG, which was absent in IgBD-ZPD-L1. In summary, these results confirm the successful construction of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1.

In vitro binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to IgG and PD-L1

To evaluate the in vitro binding activity of IgBD-ZPD-L1, SPR assay was performed to analyze binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to IgG and PD-L1. As shown in Fig. 1E, IgBD-ZPD-L1 could bind to mIgG, whereas ZPD-L1 lacked this interaction. Meanwhile, SEC analysis of the IgBD-ZPD-L1 and IgG mixture (molar ratio of IgBD-ZPD-L1: IgG = 1:2) yielded a single protein peak with a slightly higher molecular weight than IgG. On the contrary, the mixture of ZPD-L1 and IgG produced two distinct protein peaks corresponding to individual ZPD-L1 and IgG after separation by SEC (Fig. S2A), and the SDS-PAGE identified IgBD-ZPD-L1, but not ZPD-L1, in the protein peak containing mIgG after the separation of the mixtures by SEC (Fig. S2B). These results confirmed that the fusion of IgBD conferred IgG-binding ability to IgBD-ZPD-L1. Besides, when the IgBD-ZPD-L1 and mIgG were mixed at a 1:1 or 1:3 M ratio, unbound IgBD-ZPD-L1 or mIgG was presented in the mixture, analyzed by SEC (Fig. S3). Importantly, SPR revealed that ZPD-L1, IgBD-ZPD-L1, and the complex mIgG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 all retain the ability to bind the PD-L1, indicating that neither IgBD fusion nor binding of IgG affect PD-L1 binding activity. (Fig. 1F). Subsequently, the in vitro binding activity of IgBD-ZPD-L1 was further analyzed by ELISA. As shown in Fig. 1G, the results were consistent with SPR, demonstrating specific binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to mIgG, while ZPD-L1 showed negligible affinity. Meanwhile, both ZPD-L1 and IgBD-ZPD-L1 bound to PD-L1, and pre-complexation of IgBD-ZPD-L1 with IgG did not affect PD-L1 binding. These results confirm that IgBD-ZPD-L1 exhibits potent, simultaneous binding to both IgG and PD-L1 in vitro, maintaining full biological activity.

Binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to PD-L1 positive tumor cells

To examine the cellular binding activity of IgBD-ZPD-L1, flow cytometry first assessed PD-L1 expression on MLE-12 and MC38 cells. As Fig. 1H shown, MC38 cells are PD-L1 positive, whereas MLE-12 cells are negative. Further cell-binding flow cytometry results showed that IgBD-ZPD-L1 binds efficiently to MC38 cells, but not to MLE-12 cells (Fig. 1I), consistent with the results of the PD-L1 expression analysis. Similarly, fluorescence microscopy confirmed robust binding of FAM-labeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 to PD-L1-positive MC38 cells with strong fluorescence signal, while no significant signal was observed in PD-L1-negative MLE-12 cells (Fig. 1J). Pre-incubation of cells with unlabeled IgBD-ZPD-L1 to block membrane PD-L1 receptors, followed by incubation with FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1, significantly reduced both binding rate (Fig. 1I) and fluorescence intensity (Fig. 1J) in MC38 tumor cells. As expected, the complex mIgG-FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 was also able to bind to PD-L1-positive MC38 tumor cells, and pre-blocking the PD-L1 receptors with IgBD-ZPD-L1 significantly reduced the cellular binding of mIgG-FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (Fig. S4). These results indicated that conjugation with mIgG did not affect the binding of IgBD-ZPD-L1 to PD-L1 receptors on the cell membrane, and the complex mIgG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 still retained biological activity at the cellular level. In addition, CCK-8 assays (Fig. S5) revealed that IgBD-ZPD-L1 exhibited no significant cytotoxicity toward either PD-L1-positive MC38 or PD-L1-negative MLE-12 cells. Together, these results demonstrate that IgBD-ZPD-L1 specifically binds to PD-L1-positive tumor cells in vitro without appreciable cytotoxicity.

Fusion of IgBD significantly enhanced the in vivo immunotherapeutic efficacy of IgBD-ZPD-L1

To evaluate tumor-targeted accumulation of IgBD-ZPD-L1 in vivo, C57BL/6 mice bearing subcutaneous MC38 tumors (∼200 mm3) received intravenous injections of equimolar FAM-IgBD-ZPD-L1, FAM-ZPD-L1, or FITC-labeled isotype control goat anti-mouse IgG antibody. After 8 h of circulation, tumor tissues were collected for co-localization immunofluorescence staining of PD-L1 to assess PD-L1 expression and examine the distribution of IgBD-ZPD-L1. As shown in Fig. S6, strong PD-L1–positive red fluorescence was observed in MC38 tumor tissues, indicating that MC38 tumors are PD-L1-positive, which is consistent with the cell-level detection results. In addition, both ZPD-L1 and IgBD-ZPD-L1 produced positive green fluorescence in tumor tissues, with IgBD-ZPD-L1 showing significantly stronger intensity than ZPD-L1. The control antibody did not accumulate in the tumors. These results suggest that fusion of IgBD markedly enhances tumor targeting and accumulation, likely by extending the serum half-life through in vivo binding to endogenous IgG. To verify this hypothesis, ELISA was performed to assess the in vivo half-life of ZPD-L1 versus IgBD-ZPD-L1, and the serum half-life of IgBD-ZPD-L1 is about 6 times longer than that of ZPD-L1 (Fig. S7). Thus, fusion of IgBD substantially prolongs serum half-life to enhance tumor accumulation in vivo.

To verify whether the prolonged in vivo half-life of IgBD-ZPD-L1 enhances antitumor immunotherapy, MC38 tumor-bearing mice received intravenous injections of IgBD-ZPD-L1 (10 mg/kg) or equimolar ZPD-L1 every 3 days, for a total of four doses (Fig. 2A). As Fig. 2B shown, tumors in the IgBD-ZPD-L1 treatment group grew the slowest and had the smallest volume (161.2 ± 49.2 mm3), significantly smaller than the treatment ZPD-L1 group (306.8 ± 60.6 mm3) and the PBS control group (494.4 ± 75.5 mm3). Besides, no obvious differences in body weight were observed among all groups during treatment (Fig. 2C), suggesting negligible systemic toxicity of IgBD-ZPD-L1. Excised tumor weight (Fig. 2D and E) was lowest in the IgBD-ZPD-L1 treatment group (0.16 ± 0.05 g), followed by ZPD-L1 (0.27 ± 0.07 g), and highest in PBS control group (0.40 ± 0.09 g), indicating the strongest in vivo antitumor effect of IgBD-ZPD-L1. Blood biochemistry and HE staining results (Fig. S8A and B) showed all treatment group values for ALT, AST, UREA, CREA, UA, and CK within the normal range, and no notable pathological lesions in any major organ, confirming short-term in vivo biosafety of IgBD-ZPD-L1.

IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated delivery of IgG-type antibody drugs enhances combined tumor immunotherapy

Leveraging the IgG-binding capability of IgBD-ZPD-L1, various novel bispecific antibody drugs can be conveniently and efficiently constructed in vitro for combined tumor immunotherapy by coupling IgBD-ZPD-L1 with different IgG-type therapeutic antibodies. Notably, anti-angiogenesis combined with immunotherapy, as well as bispecific immunotherapy, have emerged as important strategies in the clinical application of combined tumor immunotherapy. CD47/SIRPα signaling blockade reactivates macrophage immune function, restoring their phagocytic and tumoricidal activities and thereby achieving anti-tumor immune effects. Accordingly, the CD47/SIRPα axis is regarded as another highly promising immunotherapeutic target except for PD-1/PD-L1. Consequently, the dual-target immunotherapy agents of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 were constructed. As shown in Fig. S9A, the absorbance of the reaction products of IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αVEGF or αSIRPα mixture increased gradually with increasing concentration, indicating that the dual-target immunotherapy agents of αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 were successfully constructed and possessed binding activity with VEGF and SIRPα in vitro. In contrast, the IgBD-ZPD-L1 exhibited no specific binding with VEGF and SIRPα (Fig. S9A). Meanwhile, the αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes could also bind to PD-L1 (Fig. S9B), confirming the dual functional binding activity of the complexes. In vivo, both αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes remained stable in the blood within 2 h after injection, and their concentrations were nearly constant (Fig. S10). However, at 5 h post-injection, the concentrations of complexes decreased significantly (Fig. S10). This observation was consistent with the metabolic results of IgBD-ZPD-L1 binding to endogenous IgG (Fig. S7), indicating that the decrease in the concentration of the complexes was primarily due to metabolic clearance in the body. These results confirmed that the αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complexes were relatively stable in vivo.

Subsequently, the αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 complex was administered intravenously to MC38 tumor-bearing mice to investigate the in vivo antitumor effects of the IgBD-ZPD-L1-based anti-angiogenic/immunotherapeutic strategy (Fig. S11A). As depicted in Fig. 2F, the tumor volume in the αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 treatment group was significantly smaller than the αVEGF + ZPD-L1 treatment group, at 137.4 ± 78.3 mm3 vs. 339.3 ± 108.5 mm3 respectively. The PBS control group exhibited the fastest tumor growth (555.4 ± 91.9 mm3). Tumor weights mirrored these findings (Fig. 2G and H), with the αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 group displaying the lowest tumor weight (0.16 ± 0.08 g), significantly less than in PBS (0.57 ± 0.13 g) and αVEGF + ZPD-L1 (0.31 ± 0.11 g) groups, demonstrating the superior anti-tumor efficacy of the bispecific agent. Moreover, there was no significant body weight difference among all treatment groups (Fig. S11B), indicating acceptable biosafety. Immunofluorescence staining of tumor vasculature further confirmed the anti-angiogenic potential of the bispecific agent (Fig. 2I, Fig. S11C). The PBS group exhibited dense, disordered, and highly branched vasculature (vessel area fraction: 5.07 %, Fig. 2J), whereas vessel proliferation was suppressed and network morphology normalized following either αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 or αVEGF + ZPD-L1 treatment. Strikingly, αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 exerted a stronger inhibitory effect on tumor angiogenesis, reducing vessel area fraction to 1.28 ± 0.37 % compared with 3.29 ± 0.62 % in αVEGF + ZPD-L1 treatment group, clearly highlighting that αVEGF-IgBD-ZPD-L1 exhibits more potent antitumor effects than conventional combination therapy strategies. Finally, the tumor immunotherapy efficacy of IgBD-ZPD-L1-based dual immunotherapeutic agent, αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1, was further evaluated in MC38 tumor-bearing mice (Fig. S12A). As shown in Fig. 2K, both αSIRPα+ZPD-L1 and αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 treatments slowed tumor growth compared to PBS control group. However, the αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 group exhibited the slowest tumor progression and achieved significantly smaller volumes than αSIRPα+ZPD-L1, and body weight remained stable throughout (Fig. S12B). After treatment, the tumor volumes in each group were as follows: 482.1 ± 83.8 mm3 (PBS), 265.2 ± 65.4 mm3 (αSIRPα+ZPD-L1), and 144.2 ± 38.6 mm3 (αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1). Tumor weights aligned with tumor volumes (Fig. 2L and M), again supporting superior anti-tumor efficacy of αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1. These data demonstrate that dual checkpoint blockade achieved by αSIRPα-IgBD-ZPD-L1 effectively synergizes T-cell and macrophage-mediated anti-tumor immunity, potently boosting the anti-tumor efficacy of combination immunotherapy. Taken together, the novel bispecific immune therapeutics agents based on the IgBD-ZPD-L1 protein platform show robust anti-tumor activity, with facile construction, low cost, and favorable safety profile. This strategy provides new insights for the development and clinical translation of tumor immunotherapy drugs.

ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 exhibits excellent tumor-targeted fluorescence and photothermal properties

In vivo MC38 tumor-bearing mice fluorescence imaging and organ biodistribution were used to evaluate the tumor-targeting and imaging capacity of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. As shown in Fig. 3A, strong fluorescence was visualized at the tumor site as early as 2 h after intravenous injection of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, remaining pronounced at 24 h post-injection with a mean intensity of 1.99 × 108 (Fig. 3B). By comparison, ICG-ZPD-L1 also accumulated in tumors but produced substantially weaker signals at all time points post-injection, particularly only about half the intensity of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 at 24 h (1.15 × 108). Biodistribution data indicated that, 24 h post-administration, tumor fluorescence for ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 was 5.78-fold higher than in muscle, compared to only 3.12-fold for ICG-ZPD-L1, further validating enhanced tumor targeting by IgBD fusion (Fig. 3C and D). Interestingly, liver accumulation was dominant for ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, reflecting hepatic metabolism likely due to the significantly increased molecular weight above the renal glomerular filtration threshold (∼60 kDa), whereas ICG-ZPD-L1 was primarily renally cleared. Thus, fusion of IgBD endowed ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 with superior tumor targeting and fluorescence imaging performance, making it a promising PD-L1-positive tumor diagnostic probe.

The photothermal performance of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 in vitro was firstly assessed. As shown in Fig. S13, the temperature increases of ICG and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 rose with increasing irradiation time or power, with no significant difference between the two groups. In contrary, no obvious temperature increase was observed in the PBS group. These results demonstrated the excellent in vitro photothermal performance of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. Subsequently, the phototoxicity of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT against tumor cells in vitro was evaluated using the CCK-8 assay. The results revealed that ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 plus NIR irradiation induced significant, dose-dependent cytotoxicity in PD-L1-positive MC38 tumor cells (viabilities: 76.5 % at 1 μM and 30.7 % at 2 μM), but had negligible effects on PD-L1-negative MLE-12 cells (Fig. 3E). Neither ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 nor NIR alone altered cell viability, demonstrating the safety of each component alone. Calcein-AM/PI staining confirmed extensive tumor cell death exclusively in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR treatment group 24 h post-irradiation (Fig. 3F, Fig. S14A), and the DCFH-DA assays also showed a remarkable increase in ROS only under ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR combined treatment (Fig. 3F, Fig. S14B), strengthening the evidence of synergistic photothermal cytotoxicity. In vivo photothermal imaging of tumor-bearing mice further verified the superior photothermal conversion efficiency of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. After intravenous administration of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and 8 h circulation, tumor sites subjected to NIR irradiation (808 nm, 1.5 W/cm2, 5 min) showed a temperature elevation of up to 23.87 °C (Fig. 3G and H), much higher than the 14.15 ± 1.06 °C achieved by ICG-ZPD-L1 (P < 0.001). Collectively, these results establish ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 as an ideal fluorescent and photothermal agent for PD-L1-positive tumor diagnosis and therapy.

ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated in vivo PTT exhibited potent anti-tumor effects

Given the exceptional tumor-targeted photothermal properties of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, the anti-tumor efficacy of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated in vivo PTT was further investigated in MC38 tumor-bearing mice (Fig. 4A). As shown in Fig. 4B, both PBS and NIR treatment groups displayed rapid tumor progression, while tumors in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group were markedly suppressed, with average tumor volumes only 55.5 mm3, along with observable hemorrhagic necrosis (Fig. 4C). Both ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR groups also showed partial tumor growth inhibition, mainly attributed to the immunotherapy provided by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and the PTT of ICG-ZPD-L1. In addition, no significant differences in body weight were observed (Fig. 4D). Consistent with tumor volume, the excised tumor weight was lowest in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group (0.04 ± 0.01 g), much less than in the ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR group (0.16 ± 0.05 g, Fig. 4E and F). TUNEL, Ki67, and HE staining revealed extensive apoptosis, decreased proliferation marker expression, and widespread tumor tissue necrosis in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group (Fig. 4G and S15), confirming efficient induction of tumor cell death and ablation. These results establish ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT as an ideal approach for targeted tumor ablation.

ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy significantly suppresses the growth of metastatic tumors by upregulating systemic antitumor immune responses

Due to the robust antitumor effects of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT, which can effectively induce ICD in tumors and upregulate systemic anti-tumor immune response, this approach can synergistically enhance the immunotherapeutic effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, suppressing the growth of metastatic tumors and achieving a greater-than-additive therapeutic outcome. To test this hypothesis, we established a subcutaneous dual-tumors (primary and distant) mouse model to evaluate the inhibitory effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy on distant tumors (Fig. 5A). As shown in Fig. 5B, combination treatment with ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and low-power NIR irradiation effectively suppressed primary tumor growth, with the antitumor effect being significantly greater than that of ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR (tumor volume: 104.3 ± 40.4 vs. 219.2 ± 48.1 mm3). After treatment, the weights of primary tumors in the PBS, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR, and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR groups were 0.38 ± 0.14, 0.25 ± 0.09, 0.18 ± 0.07, and 0.09 ± 0.05 g, respectively (Fig. 5B), indicating that ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 combined with low-power NIR irradiation still exhibited strong anti-tumor effects against primary tumors. As shown in Fig. 5C, due to the immunotherapeutic effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, the growth rate of distant tumors in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 group was modestly reduced compared to PBS control group, with tumor volumes of 120.3 ± 36.3 vs 92.1 ± 23.2 mm3, respectively. Interestingly, in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR treatment group, although only the primary tumor received PTT, the distant tumor grew even more slowly compared to the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 group, with a smaller tumor volume of just 31.2 ± 11.3 mm3 and lower tumor weight of 0.02 ± 0.01 g. This enhanced suppression of distant tumor growth is likely due to PTT-induced upregulation of antitumor immune responses, which synergize with the immunotherapy of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. Moreover, the inhibitory effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR on distant tumors was significantly greater than that of ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR, demonstrating the enhanced photothermal/immunotherapeutic efficacy mediated by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1. Multiple administrations of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 during the treatment period did not cause any significant changes in the body weight of the mice (Fig. S16A). At the end of treatment, liver, kidney, and cardiac function blood biochemical indicators for all groups were within the normal range (Fig. S16B), and histological analysis of major organs revealed no abnormal pathological changes (Fig. S16C), further confirming the favorable short-term biosafety of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 in vivo.

To investigate the mechanism underlying the abscopal anti-tumor effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy, immunofluorescence staining was first used to analyze the expression of CRT and HMGB1 in primary tumor tissues, thereby verifying the ICD-inducing effect of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT. As shown in Fig. S17, a red fluorescence signal corresponding to CRT was observed in the ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR treatment group compared to the PBS and ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 groups, while an even stronger CRT fluorescence signal was detected in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR treatment group. Simultaneously, HMGB1 release from tumor cells was also increased in the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group, indicating effective induction of ICD and promotion of CRT exposure and HMGB1 release by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT. To determine whether ICD induced by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT effectively enhances anti-tumor immune responses, we subsequently performed flow cytometry analysis of immune cells in distant tumors, including DCs, T cells, and macrophages. As shown in Fig. 5D, percentages of mature DCs (CD45+CD11c+CD86+) in distant tumors were 5.51 ± 1.61 % (PBS), 7.19 ± 0.83 % (ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1), 9.63 ± 0.98 % (ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR), and 15.22 ± 2.07 % (ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR), demonstrating that ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT effectively promoted DC maturation, consistent with CRT exposure and HMGB1 release. Meanwhile, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT further promoted T cell activation, and the proportion of activated cytotoxic T cells (CD3+CD8+) increased from 25.17 ± 2.65 % up to 42.12 ± 1.86 % (Fig. 5E), significantly higher than the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 (26.40 ± 3.22 %) and ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR (32.27 ± 2.43 %) treatment groups. In distant tumors, the proportion of M1-TAMs with anti-tumor activity (CD11b+F4/80+MHCII+) in ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group reached as high as 38.57 ± 1.04 % (Fig. 5F), nearly triple that of PBS group (14.37 ± 1.82 %). In contrast, the proportion of M2-TAMs with tumor-promoting effect (CD11b+F4/80+CD206+) was only 8.73 ± 1.16 %, significantly lower than in other treatment groups (Fig. 5G).

To further validate the suppression of metastatic tumors by ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy, 4T1 breast cancer lung metastasis mice model was established (Fig. 6A). Firstly, we verified the positive expression of PD-L1 in 4T1 through flow cytometry experiments (Fig. S18), which was consistent with the previous research results. As shown in Fig. 6B, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR treatment significantly suppressed the growth of primary 4T1 tumors, achieving the smallest tumor volumes and lowest tumor weights among all groups, thus confirming the robust anti-tumor effects in PD-L1-positive breast cancer as well. At treatment endpoint, all tumor-bearing mice underwent intraperitoneal injection of luciferin. After 10 min, mice were sacrificed and lungs excised for fluorescence imaging and HE staining to assess the pulmonary metastases. As shown in Fig. 6C, compared to healthy lungs, the PBS group lungs were visibly enlarged and exhibited strong metastatic fluorescence (intensity: 1.15 × 108±3.94 × 107). Both ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and ICG-ZPD-L1/NIR treatments reduced lung metastasis, but the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group displayed dramatically lower metastatic burden (fluorescence intensity: 6.92 × 106±5.14 × 106). HE staining further confirmed the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1/NIR group showed the smallest extent of lung metastasis (Fig. 6D). In summary, the ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated photothermal/immunotherapy strategy not only exerts potent antitumor effects on primary tumors but also efficiently induces ICD, promotes DCs maturation, and robustly upregulates the antitumor immune response, synergistically enhancing the antitumor efficacy of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 and exerting a systemic effect for the inhibition of tumor recurrence and metastasis.

Conclusion

In this study, we innovatively engineered a novel multifunctional IgG-binding and PD-L1-targeting protein, IgBD-ZPD-L1, by fusing the IgBD with the PD-L1-specific affibody ZM1. The fusion of the IgBD endows IgBD-ZPD-L1 with the ability to bind endogenous IgG, which significantly prolongs in vivo metabolic half-life and enhances anti-tumor immunotherapeutic efficacy. Meanwhile, IgBD-ZPD-L1 can also serve as a versatile platform for the delivery of various IgG-type antibody drugs, thereby facilitating and enhancing tumor combination immunotherapy. Based on the integration of ICG's fluorescence/photothermal properties with the tumor-targeted immunotherapeutic activity of IgBD-ZPD-L1, we successfully constructed a novel tumor-targeted photosensitizer, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1, enabling simultaneous tumor imaging diagnosis and combined photothermal/immunotherapy. ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 not only achieves specific in vivo accumulation and fluorescence imaging diagnosis of PD-L1-positive tumor, but also exhibits potent anti-tumor efficacy by efficiently ablating primary tumors under NIR irradiation. Importantly, ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1-mediated PTT efficiently induces ICD of primary tumors, thereby enhancing anti-tumor immune responses and further boosting the immunotherapeutic efficacy of ICG-IgBD-ZPD-L1 against metastatic tumors, ultimately inhibiting tumor metastasis and recurrence. Therefore, the novel tumor immunotherapy based on IgBD-ZPD-L1 holds great promise as an effective new tumor therapy strategy.
