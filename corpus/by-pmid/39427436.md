# Progress in application of nanomedicines for enhancing cancer sono-immunotherapy.

## Abstract

Cancer immunotherapy has significant potential as a cancer treatment since it boosts the immune system and prevents immune escape to get rid of or fight cancers. However, its clinical applicability is still limited because of the low response rate and immune-related side effects. Recently ultrasound has been shown to alter the tumor immune microenvironment, enhance the effectiveness of other antitumor therapies, and cause tumors to become more sensitive to immunotherapy, thus providing new insights into cancer treatment. Nanomedicines are also anticipated to have a positive impact on improving the immunological effects and enhancing ultrasound effect for cancer therapy. Therefore, designing effective nanomedicines enhanced ultrasound effect for augmenting sono-immunotherapy has been a pivot on anticancer therapy. In this review, the immunological impacts of various ultrasound therapeutic modalities, ultrasound parameters, and their underlying mechanisms are discussed. Moreover, we highlight the recent progress of nanomedicines synergistically enhancing sono-immunotherapy. Finally, we put forward opportunities and challenges on sono-immunotherapy.

Introduction

The patient's own cells multiply uncontrollably and harmfully, which is what causes cancer. Since the immune system views cancerous cells as “self,” it generally mounts a feeble defense against them. Furthermore, the tumor microenvironment often inhibits the immune response in cancer tissues through the emergence of receptors on cancer cells and the release of various immune-suppressive chemicals. Surgery, radiation therapy, and chemotherapy are examples of conventional standard cancer treatment regimens that are not effective in combating cancer and have a number of unavoidable side effects, such as weakened immune systems, bad drug reactions, high costs, and patient intolerance. It is crucial to explore alternative, efficient therapeutic methods with minimal toxicity and high precision for the eradication of malignant tumors, especially their metastases, and to prevent their recurrence. Numerous studies on the complex relationships between malignancies and the immune systems have been conducted recently, and immunotherapy has demonstrated great promise during the therapies of certain patients with metastatic or advanced cancers. A promising approach to treating cancer is immunotherapy, which includes adoptive T-cell therapy, immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) therapy, and vaccines for cancer. In contrast to several conventional treatment approaches that target tumor cells directly, immunotherapy treats malignancies by stimulating the body's natural defense system and activating immune cells. Because immunotherapy creates long-term immunological memory, it is able to destroy not just localized cancers but also distant metastatic tumors and potentially prevent the return of tumors. Cellular immunity and humoral immunity are components of human-specific immunity, and cellular immunity is primarily responsible for anti-cancer immunity. Dendritic cells (DCs), the most skilled antigen processing cells (APCs), are able to identify and phagocytize certain antigenic chemicals released by tumor cells. This process causes DCs to mature and become activated, ultimately converting the antigenic substances into complicated peptides. After being drained into the lymph nodes, mature DCs will present the complex peptide to native T cells, leading to their activation as cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) capable of eliminating tumor cells. However, because tumor cells and their surrounding environment may usually convey certain immunosuppressive chemicals that block the immune system, the host's immunological response to tumors is relatively weak.

In recent times, ultrasound (US), which demonstrates acoustic longitudinal waves with a frequency above the threshold of human hearing (20 kHz), has become a commonly utilized, harmless, non-intrusive, and efficient method for detecting and treating tumors. US has a significant part in the disease treatment because it may alter the structure and function of biological tissues through a variety of mechanisms, including thermal, mechanical, cavitation, and others. Many researchers have discovered through their extensive investigation of the biological effects of US that it may also become more important in immunotherapy to enhance the effectiveness of tumor treatment. For instance, drug and gene delivery technologies aimed at the US have recently attracted a lot of attention. There is mounting evidence that ultrasound-targeted microbubble/nanobubble destruction (UTMD/UTND) can optimize gene- or drug-induced antitumor immunity, enhance the effectiveness of different treatments, and enable accurate delivery of a medication or gene to the sites of tumors. Several alternative therapeutic US procedures, such as sonodynamic therapy (SDT) and high intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), have been developed in addition to UTMD/UTND. Preclinical research has demonstrated that SDT is a promising treatment for cancer and other disorders because it can use sonosensitizers to trigger cytotoxic events and activate reactive oxygen species (ROS) that lead to cell/tissue necrosis and apoptosis with little invasiveness. HIFU is a new kind of clinical cancer treatment that uses mechanical and thermal forces to promote tumor ablation and tumor-cell death. Clinical studies have indicated that HIFU presents a hopeful new treatment choice for addressing a wide range of cancer types, including those affecting the brain, liver, breast, kidney, pancreas, uterine fibroids, and prostate. Apart from the therapeutic assessment of HIFU, SDT, and UTMD/UTND, research has also been conducted on their impact on immunity. Tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) can be released as a result of SDT-induced apoptosis, HIFU-induced necrosis, and UTMD/UTND-induced cell lysis are examples of processes that could potentially boost the antitumor immune response. Therapeutic US technologies own the potential to enhance the other cancers immunotherapy by improving the tumor immunosuppressive microenvironment (TIME). This is because of the quickly expansion of nanotechnology, which has led to the proposal of different approaches to treatment. In this review, we highlight the latest studies on sono-immunotherapy to help researchers gain a better knowledge of the US-mediated immune adjustment in tumor immunity. Therefore, sono-immunotherapy, an immunotherapy generated from therapeutic US technology, has high development possibilities (Scheme 1).

Overview of cancer immunotherapy

Immunotherapies are treatments in which the patient's own immune system is boosted or stimulated to fight the illness. It is important to train the immune system to target malignant neoplastic disease while sparing healthy, normal cells in cancer patients. This can be accomplished through different methods, for example, adoptive cell transfer, in vivo cancer vaccines, monoclonal antibodies, and non-specific immune system stimulation. Cell-mediated immunity and humoral immunity are two categories of immunity. Tumor antigens (TAs) are important because of their ability to activate both types of lymphocytes. The presentation of antigen by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) triggers the activation of T lymphocytes. APCs can activate T helper cells through TAs on MHC II. MHCI is expressed on all cells. By producing cytotoxins that cause the target cell to die, CTLs can specifically target cancer cells that display the appropriate antigens. Cytokines are released by T helper cells, and this is a crucial step in the sensitization of B and CTL cells.

For the immune system to effectively combat cancer, three stages are necessary. First and foremost, TAs must interact with DCs. In addition, the DCs require a “maturation signal” that results in immunization against the antigens rather than tolerance. Numerous maturation signals have been found, including tumor necrosis factor α (TNF-α), pathogen-associated molecular patterns, and similar factors. Activation of T cells by DCs in the lymph nodes is the second requirement for an immunological response. Receiving antigens without maturation signals, DCs induce T cell tolerance, which counteracts the immunological response. The penetration of activated T cells into the tumor mass, where they continue to function and eradicate the cancer cells, is the third stage in an efficient immune response to a tumor. Additionally, there are a number of ways in which the tumor microenvironment (TME) can inhibit the impact of T cells. For example, myeloid-derived suppressor cells and Treg cells can act in opposition to the activation of immune cells, and tumor cells can suppress their own MHCI expression and release immune-suppressive substances. Immune treatments have the potential to operate at any point within this complex system, but for a successful result, a thorough understanding of the entire process is required (Fig. 1).

Over the last ten years, swift progress in the field of tumor immunotherapy has established. Unlike traditional treatments such as surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy that target the tumor directly, tumor immunotherapy works by activating the body's immune system to enhance its function and indirectly eliminate tumor cells. By encouraging immune cell recognition and eradicating target cells that contain tumor antigens, it can, for one thing, improve immune-mediated tumor cell death; for another, it can diminish or eradicate immunosuppressive signals in the TME.

Tumor vaccines, gene therapies, tumor-agnostic therapeutics, and cell immunotherapies are examples of current immunotherapy techniques. Cancer immunotherapy also makes use of nano-based drug delivery systems and cell-inspired drug delivery platforms. Many immunotherapy medications have been licensed for clinical usage thus far, helping patients with melanoma, ovarian cancer, lung cancer, bladder cancer. Nevertheless, some individuals do not respond well to immunotherapy, and they may even experience hyperprogression of their illness following treatment. The dynamic linkage between tumor cells and immunomodulators in TME are typically necessary for positive responses to immunotherapy. Tumor angiogenesis and the immunosuppressive milieu particular to a tumor are frequently linked to low immune responses. To augment the response of cancer immunotherapy, the current is to design a combination of strategies based on chemodynamic therapy (CDT), photodynamic therapy (PDT), and sonodynamic therapy (SDT). SDT is based on that US activates the sonosensitizers used within the tumor to produce reactive oxygen species (ROS) in order to combat the tumor. Furthermore, US has long been applied to elicit various therapeutic bioeffects in antitumor applications.

Parameters and mechanisms of ultrasound in tumor therapy

US plays a significant role in the anti-tumor therapy because it can alter the structure and function of biological tissues through various mechanisms, consisting of thermal and mechanical effects. The applications of US for tumor therapy depend on the accurate parameters regulation. And the biological effect of US can be modulated by different driving parameters, such as frequency, power, duty cycle, pulse repetition frequency, and irradiation time.Thermal effects are the result of the ultrasonic wave, where thermal ablation is attained at a frequency range of 20 kHz to 200 MHz with the intensity of 100–5000 W/cm2, entering the tissue and being absorbed, scattered, and attenuated in energy. Deep tissue penetration of focused US (FUS) results in the production of heat energy referred as “hyperthermia” maintained for 30–60 min at a temperature of ∼43 °C. There will be many antitumor immune responses brought on by this illness. A study showed that applying US hyperthermia to B16 melanomas activated DCs, which following raised CD4 + T-cell activation and impeded tumor-induced T-cell tolerance. Different mechanical mechanisms initiated by US manipulation cause the onset of biological consequences that enhance the tumor therapeutic efficacy. Cavitation is induced at frequencies of 20 to 40 kHz and intensities between 103 and 105 W/m2 at 105 kPa pressure. And, sonoporation is triggered at 1 MHz frequency with a power of more than 500 mW/cm2. And it also refers to non-mechanical processes that result from the mechanical interaction of ultrasonography with tissues. Examples of these processes include chemical reactions that are sparked by ROS produced during sonoluminescence and cavitation(Fig. 2).

Based on the physiological impacts of US, the modes of US that can be utilized to control the immune response and thereby improve the cancer treatment outcome can be classified into four modes, namely, high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), low-intensity focused ultrasound (LIFU), ultrasound-targeted nano/microbubble destruction (UTND/UTMD), and SDT. Tumor destruction using HIFU has been proven to be an innovative, efficient, and non-invasive method for addressing various types of cancer. Numerous studies have proven that HIFU can induce anti-tumor immunity via a variety of methods. For instance, increased mature DC infiltration into tumor tissue after HIFU ablation can strengthen systemic anti-tumor immunity. When exposed to HIFU, the molecule CD80 may express itself more. Furthermore, the use of HIFU mechanical destruction in immunomodulation has garnered far greater attention because there aren't any thermally denatured tumor antigens. In the clinic, LIFU are also helpful for promoting physiological reactions or quickening the passage of medication through the skin. US momentarily open membrane holes, enabling the passage of medications, proteins, and genetic material. The approach known as UTND/UTMD, has shown considerable promise in the field of cancer immunotherapy. The non-invasive, target-specific, efficient, and unique UTND/UTMD gene/drug delivery system uses nanobubbles/microbubbles, whose structures consist of gas core and mono-layered shell with different nature comprising polymers, phospholipids, or proteins, as either a gene/drug vehicle or an enhancer of gene/drug delivery. When microbubble(MB) experiences a recurring expansion and contraction under the stimulus of US with low intensity, the oscillation phenomenon inducing a liquid flow around the MBs referred as “microstreaming” which named“Stable cavitation”. In addition, cavitation remains a complex sequential phenomenon distributed into stable and inertial cavitation conferring the reaction of MBs with US. The stability of nanobubbles depends on the bubble size and surface pressure according to Laplace eq. This implies that smaller bubbles have higher Laplace pressure and as a result, they shrink. Shell composition, the type of gas core and the surfactant with the optimal ratio, are recognized as the key factors in bubbles stability according to the Laplace eq. In addition to producing auditory cavitations, UTND/UTMD can cause physical forces such shock waves, implosions, liquid jets, and microstreaming. These forces have the potential to disrupt the cellular membrane and increase its permeability. Consequently, it is possible to directly transfer medications, genes, antibodies, and cytokines into the cytoplasm of immune cells, boosting the immunological reaction. UTND/UTMD, in contrast to other technologies, has demonstrated the ability to enhance the delivery of immune-stimulating agents to tumors while avoiding significant harm. Additionally, SDT has been shown in preclinical trial studies to activate host anti-tumor immune response, such as immunogenic cell death (ICD).

High intensity focused ultrasound for cancer immunotherapy

Direct effect of HIFU on immune response

HIFU is a fully noninvasive ablation technique that concentrates HIFU beams into a tiny focal zone using a multi-element ultrasound transducer. Acoustic energy is delivered to a specific area through the convergence of sound waves, protecting the pre- and post-focal tissue structures. The application of ultrasound energy can result in either mechanical or thermal effects, depending on the method of its delivery. Thermal HIFU (T-HIFU) causes local coagulative necrosis and protein denaturation by raising the focal tissue temperature to 60–85 °C when frequencies of 0.8–3.5 MHz are generally used during the clinical applications. Outside the focus area, heat diffusion creates a temperature gradient where cells are exposure to sublethal temperatures and either experience apoptosis or recover from reversible harm. Even if the tissue is not coagulated, it experiences enough heat stress close to the intended focus to trigger apoptosis. T-HIFU-treated cells secrete IFN-γ and/or TNF-α from immune cells upon the liberation of internal danger signals. There has been evidence of a rise in the concentration of CD4 + and CD8 + T lymphocytes in malignancies treated using T-HIFU. But due to T-HIFU's limited effectiveness in treating larger tumors, researchers are now looking at other HIFU methods, like mechanical high-intensity focused ultrasound (M−HIFU), to eradicate malignancies. Acoustic cavitation causes mechanical damage in M−HIFU. The gaseous components of tissues change as a result of the high-pressure waves because gas bubbles begin to oscillate and rupture, leading to mechanical damage. Previous animal investigations have shown that, with temperature increases of less than 6 °C, M−HIFU can cause fully fragmented tumor lesions. Previous research revealed that M−HIFU exhibited greater anticancer efficacy, improved antitumor immunity effect, and decreased the risk of metastasis when compared to T-HIFU. It also boosted the concentration of DCs in treated tumors. T-HIFU and M−HIFU both produce available ablated tumor deris in situ, notwithstanding their variances. One possible source of tumor antigens for triggering anti-tumor immunity is tumor debris. In draining lymph nodes (LNs), DCs can scavenge tumor material and display tumor antigens to T lymphocytes. On the other hand, tumor antigens are presented by LN-resident DCs after being delivered to LNs via lymphatic drainage. Significantly, the environment in which DCs collect TAs, including the cytokine and chemokine milieu and other endogenous cues, is essential for the start and progression of adaptive immunity.

Nanomedicines synergistic effect of HIFU on immunotherapy

The precision and efficacy of many integrated anticancer treatments have increased due to recent developments in intelligent nanomaterials that possess preciously targeted ability or can sense the TME or external stimuli, resulting in controlled drug release. As a result, the strategic development of nanomaterials has the potential to treat malignancies through a combination of HIFU and chemotherapy, leading to enhanced immune response against cancer. Prior research has suggested that administering chemotherapy and HIFU irradiation together may have a synergistic impact, enhancing the total anticancer efficacy. One way to lower the risk of tumor recurrence is to use tiny doses of strong chemotherapy medications to destroy any tumor cells that HIFU is unable to reach. However, HIFU irradiation can alter the tumors' structural makeup in the tumor zone and cause cavitation, therefore, by employing this method to enhance the localized delivery of drugs, it can increase the efficacy of medications as they pass through membranes and improve the dispersion of drugs at the tumor site. Using a disulfide linker, camptothecin (CPT), whose function is inhibition DNA topoisomerase, was coupled to the polymer C9F17-b-PAsp (DET). Low intensity focused ultrasound for cancer immunotherapy

Different from HIFU, low intensity focused ultrasound (LIFU) is focused ultrasound at a lower intensity. In general terms, LIFU can cause acoustic non-inertial cavitation, which broadens the intercellular space, disrupts the cell membrane, and enhances blood vessel permeability to facilitate the effective absorption of agents and medication molecules. As a result, it is frequently employed as an adjuvant therapy to increase chemotherapy's therapeutic effectiveness. It has been suggested recently that LIFU may also control immunity against tumors. Because LIFU has the ability to boost medication delivery, it can help distribute antibodies, tumor vaccines, et al. to improve the benefits of immunotherapy.

Given that the intense heat produced by the HIFU may burn blood vessels and other tissues, preventing immune cells from reaching the tumor site and from recognizing and presenting antigens, Bandyopadhyay et al. combined cone-beam computed tomography (CBCT) and low-energy LIFU to improve anti-tumor immunity. It was discovered that LIFU could cause a stress response in the tumor, leading to stress-induced protein expression, thus increasing the ICD. The therapeutic US device was operated in continuous-wave mode at a specific acoustic power/pressure regimen: acoustic power 3 W/cm2, peak negative pressure = 2.93 MPa (80-mm focal length)/3.81 MPa (85-mm focal length). These proteins would then help to activate and trigger DCs maturity, improve anti-tumor immunity and reverse the condition of T cell tolerance.

Nanomedicines synergistically sonodynamic therapy for antitumor immunotherapy

SDT is emerging as a viable cancer treatment approach that the combination of sonosensitizers and ultrasound utilized for tumor eradication by releasing ROS. Apart from its direct cytotoxic action, SDT can also elicit an immunological response through ICD, hence impeding tumor metastasis and recurrence. Moreover, greater therapeutic efficacy in the treatment of tumors can be attained by combining SDT with other forms of therapy.

SDT-triggered antitumor immunity by inducing immunogenic cell death

The activation of adaptive immune responses in immunocompetent hosts is primarily driven by intracellular stress responses, leading to immunogenic cell death (ICD). During the SDT, US can trigger the release of DMAPs, such as HMGB1, CRT, ATP, and other antigen molecules, from the sonosensitizers in order to create ROS and cause ICD of tumor cells. These DMAPs have the potential to improve DCs' capacity to identify tumor cells and trigger tumor-specific CTLs to combat cancers. For example, ATP released from died cancer cells can play as a “find me” signal role to regulate the recruitment, activation of APCs. Furthermore, by inducing T cells to mediate an antitumor immune response through IL-17 and IFN, ATP activates the caspase-1-dependent NLRP3 inflammasome, stimulating the release of IL-1β. The oxygen-dependent SDT's prolonged oxygen consumption and tumor hypoxia significantly impair its therapeutic results. Recently, efforts have been made to reduce oxygen use or adopt oxygen self-sufficient methods in an effort to enhance the effectiveness of SDT within its limitations. Tirapazamine (TPZ) is a hypoxia-activated cytotoxic medication that causes DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) to kill cells indirectly. It is approximately 300 times more harmful to cells in anoxic settings than it is in normoxic ones. A hybrid membrane-mimetic nanosized drug delivery system (Lipo-Ce6/TPZ@MH) was described by Zhao group as a biomimetic material to both eliminate solid tumors at their original location and prevent cancer cells from lethal metastasizing (Fig. 5 A). A highly successful synergistic therapy was produced by introducing the sonosensitizer chlorin e6 (Ce6) and hypoxia-activated TPZ into pH-sensitive liposomes as a biomimetic decoy inner core and fused platelets and red membrane as the outside shell. In addition, through the combined use of SDT and hypoxia-activated chemotherapy, which leads to increased release of DAMPs and stimulation of antitumor immunity during the cancer treatment process, the biomimetic materials have a direct cytotoxic effect on cancer cells. A non-immunogenic polysaccharide with exceptional biocompatibility, chondroitin sulfate (CS) targets tumor tissues by binding to CD44 receptors on the surface of cancer cells. Zhang et al built utilizing CS, sonosensitizer Rhein (Rh), and PFC to create a unique oxygen-carrying nanoplatform that was targeted at the CD44 receptor and responsive to redox and ultrasound. The incorporation of perfluoroalkyl groups into the structures maintained PFC's capacity to carry oxygen, raising the oxygen concentration of B16F10 melanoma cells and improving SDT efficiency. Through the induction of CRT exposure following CS–Rh–PFC nanoparticle (NP) therapy, SDT facilitated ICD.

A ROS-based endoplasmic reticulum (ER) stress response that involves the phosphorylation of the eukaryotic translation initiation factor 2α (eIF2α) by the protein kinase-like endoplasmic reticulum kinase (PERK) activates the surface-exposed calreticulin. The surface-exposed calreticulin is the key to trigger ICD. For example, an adaptable Iridium(III) nanosonosensitizer that targets the ER is developed as an enhanced ICD inducer for tumor sono-immunotherapy. The cleverly constructed and expertly crosslinked Iridium (III) sonosensitizer Ir-CA, functionalized with cholic acid (CA), with human serum albumin (HSA) yields the nanosonosensitizer HSA@Ir-CA. HSA@Ir-CA preferentially accumulates in tumor areas for improved cellular uptake, and after demonstrating excellent stability, attractive tumor-targeting ability, and reduction-responsiveness, it rapidly disassembles in response to an intracellular reductive environment. Upon US irradiation (2 W/cm2, 5 min, 50 % duty cycle), the uncaged Ir-CA can collect specifically in ER and precisely disrupt ER with in situ created type I and II ROS for a high-efficiency SDT. Furthermore, the enhanced ER stress powerfully enhances ICD to generate strong systemic antitumor immunity, preventing lung metastasis, tumor recurrence, and primary/distant tumor growth (Fig. 5 B). Heat shock proteins that are exposed on the surface, such as HSP70 and HSP90, are also significant DAMPs that function as a “eat-me” signal, promoting the absorption and presentation of TAAs by binding to Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on immature DCs. In addition, HMGB1 induces the release of proinflammatory cytokines and activates DCs through binding to several pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), such as TLR4. Blood-brain barrier is one mainly restriction for better treatment efficiency of glioblastoma. By carefully rupturing the tight connections between brain endothelial cells, ultrasound (US) and microbubbles (MBs) can create “acoustic pores” and non-invasively break the blood–brain barrier. Here, chitosan oligosaccharide (COS) and an immune-boosting adjuvant Poly(I:C) are coupled via electrostatic adsorption with the sonosensitizer protoporphyrin IX (PpIX). The resultant nanosonosensitizers, called “HP/CP” NSs (HA-Poly(I:C)/COS-PpIX), are subsequently cross-linked with hyaluronic acid (HA), a chemical that targets tumors. Targeting and penetrating glioblastoma, HP/CP NSs can produce reactive oxygen species upon US (1 MHz, 1.5 W/cm2, duty cycle 50 %, 30 s), resulting in DNA damage and mitochondrial dysfunction all at once. Together, loaded Poly(I:C) and TAAs released by ICD caused by SDT form an in situ vaccine that enhances anticancer immune responses. The HP/CP + US therapies increase the invasion of cytotoxic T-lymphocytes, stimulate peripheral immunological circulation, and extend the survival of mice in orthotopic glioblastoma mouse models. Additionally, HP/CP NSs have positive biosafety profiles. All things considered, their research clarifies the use of HP/CP NSs for synergistic sono-immunotherapy of glioblastoma following non-invasive blood–brain barrier opening (Fig. 5 C). Aggregation-induced emission luminogens (AIEgens) exhibit higher sensitivity to ultrasound in nanocarriers compared to conventional organic sonosensitizers due to their strong fluorescence emission when aggregated. In order to effectively deliver salicylaldazine to 4T1 tumors and mediate immunotherapy, Deng group created an AIEgen-based sonosensitizer (AIE/Biotin-M) with exceptional sonosensitivity by combining salicylaldazine-based amphiphilic polymers (AIE-1) with DSPE-PEG-Biotin (a 4T1 tumor-targeting amphiphilic polymer). In vitro, when exposed to US radiation (1.0 MHz, 2 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle), AIE/Biotin-M exhibited remarkable stability and produced copious amounts of singlet oxygen (1O2). Following the accumulation of AIE/Biotin-M targeted material in the tumor, US irradiation produced 1O2, which not only killed cancer cells but also triggered a systemic immune response by ICD in cancer cells (Fig. 5 D).

SDT remodel the undesirable tumor microenvironment

Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) were associated with TME. When stimulated by tumor cell stimuli, the majority of recruited monocytes convert into tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), changing from pro-inflammatory anti-tumor M1 macrophages (M1) to pro-inflammatory pro-tumor M2 macrophages (M2). M2 induces CTL death and decrease the efficiency of tumor immunotherapy. Conversely, M1 induces matrix breakdown and secretes pro-inflammatory cytokines to enhance the efficiency of tumor therapy. M1 macrophage-derived nanovesicles have been proven to be targeted tumor sites effectively, repolarized M2 TAMs into M1, secreted pro-inflammatory cytokines and improving tumor immunotherapy efficiency. Chen et al created a combined therapy based on M1/PLGA@IR780/CAT NPs that combines ICI targeting PD-L1 with SDT (1 MHz, 1 W/cm2, 40 % duty cycle, 30 s on, 30 s off) enhanced immunotherapy. By covering PLGA nanoparticles with M1 membranes and loading them with the sonosensitizers IR780 and CAT, these NPs were effectively created (Fig. 6 A a). In SDT, the sonosensitizer is essential. When incorporated into nanoparticles, the sonosensitizer IR780 iodide can be activated by US with a considerable improvement in drug utilization. Tumor hypoxia can be overcome and SDT efficiency increased by using CAT, which quickly catalyzes the breakdown of H2O2 in the TME to produce oxygen. Effectively targeting tumor cells, the M1 membrane also increases blood flow and causes M2 to repolarize into M1, which activates immune response against tumors. SDT based on M1/PLGA@IR780/CAT influences the TME, ICD, and DC development (Fig. 6 A b, c). Except TAMs, hypoxia and overexpressed glutathione (GSH) in the TME may also be a huge hurdle in immunotherapy. Geng et al constructed a single atom catalysts named PdSA/Ti3-xC2Ty, where single atoms of Pdδ+ (0 < δ+<2) were trapped in Ti vacancies and coordinated with three nearby C atoms to enhance responses to the TIME. Under US irradiation (50 kHz, 2.0 W/cm2), the catalysts could reduce overexpressed GSH at a rapid rate, further increase the efficiency of ROS generation, and the hypoxia condition could be significantly reduced by improved CAT-like activity. Importantly, this strategy can boost abscopal antitumor immune responses by driving maturation of DCs and polarization of TAMs into M1.

Lowering the quantity of M2-like TAMs would alleviate the TIME, stimulate the host immune system, and ultimately prevent the formation of tumors. Bisphosphonates (BP) medications have been shown in clinical studies to potentially enhance anticancer therapy outcomes. Additionally, in preclinical research models, BP has been found to exhibit a high binding affinity and harmful effects on TAMs. Zoledronic acid (ZA), a third-generation BP that induces macrophage apoptosis by blocking the mevalonate pathway, a crucial metabolic process in eukaryotes. Consequently, Cao et al. carefully created M2-like TAMs-targeted nanoliposome, which loads hematoporphyrin monomethyl ether (HMME) in the lipid bilayer, distributes ZA in the core, and changes M2pep peptide on the surface (designated as M−H@lip-ZA). This was done in order to deplete M2-like TAMs (Fig. 6 B a). As shown in the Fig. 6 B b, c, the positive areas of FITC-labeled CD206 and the percentage of CD206 tested by flow cytometry were the least under the therapy of M−H@lip-ZA with US stimulation (650 kHz, 2.0 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle, 30 s).

SDT combined with immunotherapy

SDT combined with adjuvant

An appealing new approach to combined cancer treatment is immunotherapy. According to recent research, SDT may function similarly to powerful cancer vaccines when immune adjuvants and ICIs are given concurrently. A toll-like receptor 7 agonist, imiquimod (R837) is a tiny chemical that has garnered a lot of attention lately for its potential as an efficient immune adjuvant. A well-known sono- and photosensitizer, methylene blue (MB) exhibits apparent absorption between 600 and 700 nm. In order to maximize the benefits of SDT and avoid phototoxicity caused by MB and its damage to healthy cells, MB must be administered to the targeted tumor location. To offer a successful combination of SDT and immunotherapy for cancer treatment while minimizing harm to healthy tissues, an activatable sonosensitizer platform (MR) based on glutathione-sensitive disulfide bonds joining Leu-MB, the reduced version of MB, and R837 was produced (Fig. 7 a). Additionally, they produced self-assembled MB-R837-PEG (MRP) nanoparticles for immunosonodynamic treatment (iSDT) using the amphiphilic polymer C18PMH-PEG. The combination of MRP and US may facilitate the maturation of DCs and the infiltration of T cells at the tumor site (Fig. 7 c, d). Furthermore, this treatment successfully prevented tumor metastasis when paired with an α-PD-L1 antibody for ICI with US for10 min (30 kHz, 6.5 W/cm2). Moreover, mice treated with iSDT and α-PD-L1 antibody did not experience tumor recurrence, indicating the development of long-term immunological memory (Fig. 7 b). Meanwhile, Luo et al elucidated the use of triphenylphosphonium (TPP) adorned nano-metal–organic framework (nMOF)/cancer cell membrane-coated Zr-TCPP(TPP)/R837@M to create homologous, mitochondria-targeted platforms with a high sonosensitizer loading rate. With the administration of the R837, this construct was used to synergistically boost DC maturation and tumor antigen presentation at the same time by US stimulation (1 MHz, 1.0 W/cm2, duty cycle 50 %, 1 min). Following ultrasonic treatment, these functionalized nMOFs were easily absorbed by homologous tumor cells and effectively localized to the mitochondria.

SDT combined with immune checkpoint blockade

SDT can induce antitumor immunity through ICD, however, it still lacks sufficient potency to eradicate tumors effectively. An alternative strategy for treating cancer effectively involves combining SDT with immune checkpoint blockade (ICB) using an antibody targeting either PD-1 or PD-L1. Zhang et al developed a transformable nanosensitizer with Ca2+ release and SDT activity triggered by the TME for reprogramming the immune system to fight cancer. Titanium dioxide (TiO2) nanoparticles were coated with an acid-degradable CaP to create core/shell structured TiO2@CaP nanoplatforms, which served as the nanosensitizers (Fig. 8 A a). The CaP shell broke down in the acidic TME, releasing Ca2+ that overloaded intracellular Ca2+ and caused mitochondrial malfunction. Furthermore, with US irradiation (1.0 W/cm2, 3 MHz, 5 min) for SDT, the broken-down TiO2 nanoparticles enabled the production of ROS. A cascade effect like this accelerated the demise of cancer cells and released DAMPs such as HMGB1 and CRT (Fig. 8 A b), and substantially amplified ICD, resulting in increased tumor-specific CTL infiltration and activation in mouse 4T1 tumors (Fig. 8 A c, d). After a combination of ICB therapy with anti-PD-1, TiO2@CaP nanoplatforms inhibited tumor growth by inducing a systemic antitumor immunity with ultrasonication (2.1 W/cm2, 3 MHz, 20 min) applied every other day (Fig. 8 A e). Necroptosis, in contrast to apoptosis, is a form that does not rely on caspase activity and is identified by an increase in cytoplasmic volume, disruption of the plasma membrane, and the discharge of cellular contents. It has been observed that necroptosis enhances antitumor immunity more effectively than apoptosis. The gas precursor, hydrophilic backbone, and hydrophobic sonosensitizer of the NBs are perfluoropentane, carboxymethyl dextran, and Ce6, respectively. Receiving US irradiation, NBs mainly produced ROS formation through the action of Ce6, and subsequently necroptosis was caused by acoustic cavitation of perfluoropentane, which resulted in ICD of tumor cells through burst-mediated cell membrane disintegration. This further sparked antitumor immunity by encouraging DC maturation and CD8 + T cell activation. In CT26 tumor-bearing mice models, the NB-mediated treatment effectively inhibited metastatic tumors and completely regressed the primary tumors when combined with anti-PD-L1. While immunotherapy and SDT together have proven to be the most effective anticancer combination, immunotherapeutic drugs (such anti-PD-1 and anti-PD-L1) are administered through a methodical administration of injections. This frequently results in an inadequate build-up of immunotherapeutic substances at the sites of tumors, which significantly reduces the effectiveness of the treatment against tumors and may have negative immune-related repercussions. To address this concern, lipid nanocarriers were used to create the nanodrugs, which were then filled with PEG chains via an acid-labile amide link, an anti-PD-L1 antibody conjugated via MMP-2-cleavable peptides, and Ce6 encapsulated in the core as the sonosensitizers. PEG outer chains from nanodrugs were released in acidic tumor tissues to facilitate cellular absorption by tumor cells, and intratumoral MMP-2 overexpression broke the peptide linkers to release anti-PD-L1. Furthermore, Ce6 in nanodrugs facilitated SDT to produce ICD when exposed to US radiation (2 MHz, 2.0 W/cm2, 20 % duty cycle). Consequently, in B16-F10 tumor-bearing mice, the combinational SDT/immunotherapy facilitated by nanodrugs induced a strong antitumor immunity and enduring immune system recollection, leading to an efficient control of tumor development and suppression of tumor recurrence. Additionally, this treatment approach significantly decreased systemic immune-related side effects, indicating significant promise for cancer treatment (Fig. 8 B a-d). Wang et al. designed TiO2-Ce6-CpG sensitizers, which could induce ICD at the location of the primary tumor, leading to the release of tumor-associated antigens. By combining with PD-L1, the SDT of TiO2-Ce6-CpG has the potential to not only eradicate the primary tumor but also induce a systemic immune reaction against remote tumors after US irradiation (1.0 MHz, duty cycle: 50 %, 1.0 W/cm2, 4 min).

SDT combined with multimodal therapies

Because of the complexity of tumor genesis and progression, no one therapeutic approach can yet fully address clinical requirements. Therefore, there is significant potential for application of combination anti-tumor therapy techniques. Multiple research studies have shown that multifunctional nanoplatforms have the potential to provide a comprehensive anticancer treatment, addressing current issues with minimal toxicity and demonstrating effectiveness. Another promising anti-tumor method with significant promise for clinical application is photothermal treatment (PTT). The photothermal effect, which causes tissue ablation and hyperthermia in the tumor site, is achieved by using photothermal transduction agents (PTAs) to absorb light energy and transform it into heat. PTT and SDT together have shown a lot of promise in recent years for synergistic anticancer therapy. Firstly, the light modality can precisely target tumors due to its changeable dosage and good sensitivity. Secondly, the thermal effect can improve SDT by increasing the tumor's oxygenation and blood flow. However, PTT's inherent inability to target deeper tumors can be countered by SDT's deep tissue penetration. As a result, there have been notable synergistic therapeutic results from PTT paired with SDT. In this study, Liu et al created a triple therapy approach that combines PTT and SDT while being guided by multimodal imaging to eradicate 4T1 tumors in mice. PTT and SDT (1 MHz, 2.4 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle, 5 min) were used in concert to eradicate the original tumor and stimulate the immune system at the same time. This improved the efficacy of PD-1 and prevented tumor metastases. Modified polylactic glycolic acid (PLGA) nanoparticles containing hematoporphyrin monomethyl ether (HMME), a US-responsive sonosensitizer, and superparamagnetic iron oxide (SPIO), a laser-responsive PTA, were added to the PTT/SDT regimen. During therapy, the CHINPs improved magnetic resonance, photoacoustic, and photothermal imaging and demonstrated homologous tumor targeting properties.

Through intricate cellular signaling networks, a hypoxic and acidic TME contributes significantly to the development of cancer, making it difficult to fully eradicate tumors with monotherapy. To overcome these, He et al synthesized a sonosensitizers (CD/TiSe2). The TiSe2 undergoes in situ redox in acidic TME, forming TiOxSe2−x with Se (0), selenate, and a Ti3+/Ti4+ redox couple. This couple can promote in situ CDT through a Ti3+-mediated Fenton-like reaction and deplete overexpressed GSH via a Ti4+-mediated GSH depletion. The sonosensitizers produced more ROS under US irradiation (50 kHz, 1.0 W/cm2), which can activate a powerful ICD to enhance antitumor therapy effect. To assure long-term systemic safety of inorganic nano-sonosensitizers, Cai et al designed cobalt sulfide (Co9S8) nanocages for enhancing cancer immunotherapy owing to their unique properties of narrow bandgap, TME-biodegradable, a large surface area for the regulation of surface vacancy defects and the loading of R837. While exposed to the US irradiation (50 kHz, 1.0 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle), these nanocages may effectively eradicate primary, distant, and metastatic cancers by amplifying the production of ROS in a cascade through consuming overexpressed GSH.

In another study, the dual-functional artificial peroxidases named FePorNW-DAP was designed to function as a ROS-generating system that is adaptable to TME and controllable in the US, which has been developed to improve breast cancer immunotherapies by inducing ICD (Fig. 9 a). Fe-based peroxidase's coordination structure and biocatalytic properties served as the driving forces behind this. Owing to the monatomic Fe-N4 coordination center, moderate interaction with H2O2, and decreased bandgap of ferriporphyrin-based large d-π-conjugated networks, the exhibits effective dual-functional ROS production capabilities to enhance tumor immunotherapies and suppress tumor cells. 1) To catalytically synthesize strong •OH and other ROS FePorNW-DAP species, employ the H2O2 generated locally in the TME; 2) US irradiation (2.5 W/cm 2, 1.0 MHz, 30 % duty cycle) can further produce more •OH and 1O2. This work provides essential direction for designing a biocompatible and high-performing method to modulate the TIME and boost the immune responses against breast cancer tumors. As shown in the Fig. 9 b, the DAMPs (CRT, HMGB1) released by the ICD had conducted in vivo experiments when US irradiation (1.0 MHz, 1.0 W/cm2, 30 % duty cycle, 60 s) be used. The results showed that FePorNW-DAP combined US had higher CRT fluorescence intensity than other groups. And the strongest signal for HMGB1, which is released from the nucleus into tumor tissues, is shown in the FePorNW-DAP + US group. The percentage of mature DCs, CD 8 + T cells were also higher than that of other groups after US treatment (2.5 W/cm2, 1.0 MHz, 30 % duty cycle, 3 min) (Fig. 9 c, d).

The initial near-infrared (NIR-I) light has a shallow tissue penetration depth, which drastically limits the therapeutic impact of PTT and PDT. With regard to deeper tissue penetration, multifunctional nanotheranostics exposed to second-order near-infrared (NIR-II) light have drawn a lot of interest. SDT synergistic phototherapy can eradicate malignancies entirely. For the first time, a nanotheranostic containing cerium oxide (CeO2-x) with numerous oxygen vacancies was successfully developed for photoacoustic imaging-guided SDT-enhanced phototherapy using a single NIR-II light. CeO2-x, which exhibited surface crystalline disorder, exhibited remarkable photothermal conversion capacity and wide absorption in the NIR-II region. Furthermore, the presence of many oxygen flaws in the CeO2-x layer can aid the segregation of holes and electrons through ultrasonic irradiation, so significantly improving the effectiveness of phototherapy in achieving high-efficiency tumor ablation. Hyaluronic acid (HA) was used to modify the surface of CeO2-x in order to create CeO2-x@HA, which enables effective active tumor targeting. The combined CeO2-x@HA showed significant treatment effectiveness in SDT-enhanced PDT/PTT when exposed to 1064 nm laser, resulting in complete eradication of tumors without causing harm to the body, as confirmed by experiments on animals and cells.

Strategies combined with several functional decorations for carefully regulated combinational immunotherapy for cancer may decrease the problem of immune-related adverse events. For precise and durable sono-metabolic checkpoint trimodal cancer therapy, Zhang group reported a catalytical nano-immunocomplex that needs sono-irradiation in the TME to completely activate. The nano-immunocomplex consists of three components approved by the FDA: the enzyme responsible for immunometabolic reprogramming (adenosine deaminase, ADA), the sonosensitizer (hematoporphyrin), and anti-PDL1. These components are covalently immobilized together using acid-cleavable and singlet oxygen-activatable linkers to form a single entity. As a result, the functions of the nano-immunocomplex are initially inhibited, and it is only under sono-irradiation in the acidic TME that the functions of checkpoint blocking, sonodynamic stimulation (1.0 MHz, 1.2 W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle), and immunometabolic reprogramming can be activated remotely. Repressing intratumoral adenosine levels and blocking A2A/A2B adenosine receptors-adenosinergic signaling through the enzymatic conversion of adenosine to inosine by ADA, the nano-immunocomplex can effectively stimulate immune effector cells and hinder immune suppressor cells in vivo. As a result, this work offers a universal and adaptable nanoplatform for precise combinatorial cancer immunotherapy (Fig. 10 a). The experiments in vitro demonstrated that prior to sonoirradiation, the cells incubated with HNP, PNP, or HPNP showed comparatively minimal cytotoxicity, with cell viability remaining above 80 % even at high NP concentrations. This proved that these NPs have minimal cytotoxicity even in the absence of sono-irradiation. The cells treated with HNP or HPNP after sono-irradiation exhibited enhanced cytotoxicity in a concentration-dependent manner. At an HP concentration of 8 μmol/L, the cell viability of cells that were sonoirradiated and incubated with HNP or HPNP dropped to around 25 %. The good SDT capabilities of the nano-immunocomplex were confirmed by these studies. The HMGB1 expression located in the nuclei of 4T1 cells significantly decreased in the group of HPNP + US group (Fig. 10 b). And the percentage of CD80+ CD86+ DCs significantly reduced in the presence of extra Ade by 1.4 and 1.3 folds, respectively, in the groups that were sonoirradiated and incubated with PNP and HNP, respectively. The maturing DCs did not, however, exhibit a discernible decline in the HPNP-incubated or sono-irradiated groups, which was attributed to the acidic TME/sono-activated Ade degradation. Consequently, these findings showed that ICD and DC maturation may be efficiently induced by the nano-immunocomplex for improved antigen presentation (Fig. 10 c). Next, the authors conducted the sequencing analysis to further explain the immune mechanism of nano-immunocomplex. 938 differentially expressed genes were identified following nano-immunocomplex therapy with absolute fold changes > 2 and p values < 0.05 in comparison to the saline treatment. In comparison to saline treatment, 866 upregulated and 72 downregulated genes were found in TME after nano-immunocomplex treatment (Fig. 10 d). Several immune-associated signaling pathways, such as the mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) signaling pathway for regulating Th1- and Th2-type immune responses, the Ras-associated protein 1 (Rap1) signaling pathway for regulating T cell functions, and the Fc gamma R-mediated phagocytosis signaling pathway for regulating the innate immune system, were clearly affected after treatment with nano-immunocomplexes, according to Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway enrichment analysis of these differentially expressed genes (Fig. 10 e).

Ultrasound-targeted nano/microbubble destruction for cancer immunotherapy

The cavitation action of ultrasound has made ultrasound targeted nano/microbubble destruction (UTND/UTMD) a potentially effective non-invasive method for targeted gene and medication delivery to malignancies. Nano/microbubbles can be utilized as an ultrasound contrast agent in addition to their roles as medication or gene carriers, facilitating the delivery process. Upon arrival at the tumor site, the nano/microbubbles will experience vibration, expansion, and collapse when exposed to ultrasound radiation as a result of the focused delivery of ultrasonic energy. The rapid collapse of microbubbles is often accompanied by extreme physical conditions such as temperatures exceeding 5000 K, pressures surpassing 2.03 × 108 Pa, shock waves, or microjets. These factors may lead to an increase in the permeability of both the vessel wall and cell membrane, resulting in a significant improvement in the efficiency of cellular uptake and delivery. Tumor immunotherapy has shown considerable promise with UTND/UTMD in recent years. By utilizing a blend of ultrasound and nano/microbubbles, immune-boosting substances such as genes, cytokines, antibodies, and tumor antigens can be transported to cancerous tissues to trigger the body's immune reaction. Tumor immunotherapy has shown considerable promise with UTND/UTMD in recent years. Using a combination of ultrasound and nano/microbubbles, immuno-stimulatory materials like as genes, cytokines, antibodies, and tumor antigens can be delivered to tumor tissues in order to activate the host immune response.

Direct effect of UTND/UTMD on immune response

Research has shown that UTND/UTMD may promote the development and maturation of dendritic cells (DCs) in the setting of cancer. Furthermore, this mechanism encourages T lymphocytes to initiate T lymphocyte-mediated antitumor immunity, which improves the effectiveness of angiogenesis targeting.

Nanomedicines synergistically effect of UTND/UTMD on immune response

UTND/UTMD combined with immuno-stimulatory materials has been widely applied for antitumor immune therapy. Firstly, the UTND/UTMD could be applied for targeting gene/drug delivery, thus playing the important part on immune synergistically therapy. Li et al. group designed a nanoplatform called DPPA/IL-15 NPs, which could target PD-L1 to deliver IL-15 messenger RNA (mRNA) to tumors. The DPPA/IL-15 NPs were endowed with the ability to respond to ultrasound and exhibit improved imaging performance when used with contrast-enhanced ultrasound (CEUS). They used UTMD (1.0 W/cm2, 30 s, 1.0 MHz) to selectively transfect IL-15 mRNA into tumor cells while successfully preventing it from being degraded. As a result, the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway was blocked and immune effector cells linked to IL-15 were activated. Furthermore, UTMD may produce ROS that trigger ICD induced by endoplasmic reticulum stress (ERS), thus starting anti-tumor immunity. Studies conducted in vivo and in vitro demonstrated that this combination therapy could improve anti-tumor activity and elicit a strong systemic immune response. Therefore, this combined therapy offers real-time ultrasound imaging guidance and has the potential to be clinically translated through increased immunotherapy. Secondly, the UTND/UTMD could be applied for accelerating ICB-induced immunotherapy. For example, a synergistic therapeutic nanoplatform enabled by UTMD that is built on redox-responsive nanogels (NGs) loaded with toyocamycin (Toy, a chemotherapeutic drug) and Au NPs for combination chemoimmunotherapy has been reported. Amine-terminated generation 3 poly(amidoamine) (PAMAM) dendrimers (G3.NH2) were treated with NHS-PEG-SAT to create G3-PEG-SAT as a macromonomer. This macromonomer was then self-crosslinked via a reverse microemulsion technique to produce redox-responsive G3 NGs with disulfide links. After that, the G3 NGs were used as a nanoreactor to physically load Toy and immobilize Au NPs in situ, creating the Au/Toy@G3 NGs. Toy-induced chemotherapy by ERS amplification and Au NP-mediated TAM polarization enable the generated Au/Toy@G3 NGs to exhibit redox reactivity to TME with comparatively high GSH concentration (Fig. 11 a). Due to these characteristics, Toy's and Au NPs' simultaneous rapid release may have anticancer effects both in vivo and in vitro. Meanwhile, the use of UTMD (0.4 W/cm2, 1.0 MHz) technology can significantly enhance the inhibition of cancer cell growth in vitro and tumors in vivo when treated with Au/Toy@G3 NGs, due to the improved cell uptake and tumor penetration induced by sonoporation. With the aid of UTMD, a multifaceted approach to modulate immune cells can be used to achieve enhanced chemo-immunotherapy of pancreatic tumor models. This approach includes maturing DCs through ICD by Toy-mediated ERS amplification for the subsequent activation of tumor-infiltrating CD4 + and CD8 + T cells and reduction of Tregs in tumors (Fig. 11 b-d), M2-type TAM repolarization driven by Au NPs and ICB treatment mediated by anti-PD-L1 to further activate T cells. Together with the UTMD and ICB, the intelligent Au/Toy@G3 NGs that have been created show promise as a nanomedicine formulation for CT imaging and improved tumor chemoimmunotherapy. This formulation can potentially overcome some of the current constraints of cancer therapies for clinical oncology applications. Thirdly, the UTND/UTMD could be applied to accelerate agonists entry for enhancing immunotherapy. Developing new immunotherapies targeting the monitors of innate immune system, such as the cytosolic DNA sensor cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) synthase-stimulator of interferon genes (cGAS-STING) pathway, have been attracted wide interest recently. It should be pointed out that sting and some natural agonists have poor cytosolic access. So it is crucial to develop techniques that allow for precise and effective stimulation of STING using cGAMP (a naturally occurring agonist) in the treatment of cancer. For example, a nanocomplex-conjugated MBs (ncMBs) were designed. In this study, the researchers developed ncMBs that specifically target APCs to efficiently transport cGAMP into their cytosol through UTMD release and trigger STING activation. When exposed to US (1.0 W/cm2, 20 % duty cycle,60 s), the targeted MBs oscillate. When coupled to the cell surface, they form temporary holes in the plasm membrane that allow payloads to be delivered straight into the cytosol. The authors postulated that UTMD would increase APCs' STING activation. And verified the nuclear translocation of phosphorylated IRF3 (pIRF3) could express highly under UTMD. The vivo experiments showed that the group treated with microbubble-assisted ultrasound-guided immunotherapy of cancer led to the most tumor growth inhibition, survival benefit and the memory responses by using acoustic wave (1.0 MHz, 4  W/cm2, 50 % duty cycle). What’s more, the vivo experiments showed that the group treated with microbubble-assisted ultrasound-guided immunotherapy also released much more IFN-γ. The findings indicate that immunotherapy guided by ultrasound with the assistance of microbubbles triggers both innate and adaptive immune responses through STING-mediated T cell activation by APCs.

Conclusion and perspectives

The biosafety of nano-immune interactions. The nanomaterials accumulated in organs such as the liver, brain, and kidneys due to their lack biocompatibility, premature payload release, poor stability in biological fluids, and unwanted identification by the mononuclear phagocyte system (MPS) may bring severe bio-toxicity to normal cells. Designing targeted selective nanoparticles have been adopted by researchers for improving their biocompatibility. For example, the coated of homologous tumor cell membrane on the sonocatalytic nanoagnets for increasing their accumulation in the tumor by reducing the swallow of MPS. In addition, the dosing of nanomaterials should be optimized to prevent early clearance and make the most of each injection’s therapeutic effects.

The potential immunological activity of nanomaterials. Different impacts of nanomaterials on the immune system are observed. For example, the physiochemical properties of liposomal nanomaterials such as the composition of lipid, size, surface charge, and pegylation, can either stimulate or repress the immune system. In addition, studies have shown that some inorganic nanoparticles, such as granule-shaped hydroxyapatite, needle-shaped hydroxyapatite (n-nHA), and silicon dioxide, can prevent the growth of cancer. In this study, the authors demonstrated that these nanoparticles could impede the tumors’ growth effectively by reducing the amount of TAMs in the TME. However, in another study, the authors reported that the AuNPs with the size of 26 nm could cause LPS-activated DCs to change into a more tolerogenic phenotype, which was less effective in stimulating Th1 activation and the proliferation of central memory T cells. Therefore, while employing nanomaterials to improve tumor immune treatment has many benefits, there are also some disadvantages that need to be taken into account because they provide obstacles to clinical translation.

The technical specifics of US ablation should be confirmed. Ultrasonic thermal ablation can cause tumor attachment and prevent the release of tumor antigens, and the microbubble's size and half-life are too large and short, respectively. Thus, additional clinical trial verification is necessary to confirm the combination application's safety and efficacy as well as its anticancer mechanism and technical specifics.

The US parameters in the present studies are not unified. The ultrasonic parameters listed in documents at present are all based on experimental data, and there are significant variations in terms of frequencies, intensities, and pulse or continuous waves. Specifically, due of the transitory cavitation and concomitant oxygen free radical, ultrasonic waves with a mechanical index (MI) > 1 are not suitable for use as a non-invasive physics-assisted antibiotic therapy in clinic settings. To verify ultrasonic parameters appropriate for cancer immune therapy, a corresponding mathematical and biological model should be established.

Immunotherapy can enhance the host's immune response and boost its ability to recognize and eradicate cancer cells independently. Nonetheless, immunotherapy is often combined with other treatment methods to enhance the overall anti-tumor effects due to the complex tumor microenvironment. Us, a non-invasive and safe technology, has the potential to be utilized for the treatment of both superficial and deep-seated cancers. Because of its many appealing properties, it is without a doubt the most beneficial instrument for immunotherapy research and development. An increasing body of research indicates that cancer sono-immunotherapy have numerous positive impacts on cancer therapy. In this paper, we reviewed the latest advancements in improving immunotherapy using different ultrasound modes and biological impacts. This includes the direct and indirect impact of HIFU, LIFU, SDT, and UTND/UTMD, as well as highlighting the crucial role that nanomaterials play in integrating these strategies. By utilizing this method of interdisciplinary integration, the immune system is effectively triggered to generate an immune response against cancer and establish immunological memory. This leads to the elimination of the primary tumor, prevention of distant metastases, and avoidance of tumor recurrence. Though substantial works have been devoted to antitumor application of nanomedicines for enhancing cancer sono-immunotherapy, there are still certain challenges for the widely use of nanomaterials activated sono-immunotherapy on cancer inhibition.
