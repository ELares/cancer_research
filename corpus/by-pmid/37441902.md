# Co-delivery of three synergistic chemotherapeutics in a core-shell nanoscale coordination polymer for the treatment of pancreatic cancer.

## Abstract

The combination chemotherapy regimen FOLFIRINOX comprising folinic acid, 5-fluorouracil, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin is the first-line treatment for patients with advanced pancreatic cancer, but its use remains prohibitive for the majority of patients due to severe side effects. Here, we report a core-shell nanoscale coordination polymer (NCP) nanoparticle co-delivering a potent and synergistic combination of oxaliplatin, gemcitabine, and SN38 (OGS), for the treatment of pancreatic cancer in mouse models. OGS contains key synergistic components of FOLFIRINOX in a controllable drug ratio., It exhibited particle stability in blood circulation and enhanced deposition of the drugs in acidic tumor environments. In vitro, OGS showed superior cytotoxicity over free drug combinations and robust cytotoxic synergism among its three components. In vivo, OGS improved drug circulation, increased tumor deposition, and exhibited superior antitumor efficacy over the free drug combination in both subcutaneous and orthotopic pancreatic tumor models. OGS treatment achieved 75-91% tumor growth inhibition and prolonged mouse survival by 1.6- to 2.8-folds while minimizing systemic toxicities such as neutropenia, hepatotoxicity, and renal toxicity. This work uncovers a novel and clinically relevant nanomedicine strategy to co-deliver synergistic combination chemotherapies for difficult-to-treat cancers.

Co-delivery of Three Synergistic Chemotherapeutics in A Core-Shell Nanoscale Coordination Polymer for the Treatment of Pancreatic Cancer

The combination chemotherapy regimen FOLFIRINOX comprising folinic acid, 5-fluorouracil, irinotecan, and oxaliplatin is the first-line treatment for patients with advanced pancreatic cancer, but its use remains prohibitive for the majority of patients due to severe side effects. Here, we report a core-shell nanoscale coordination polymer (NCP) nanoparticle co-delivering a potent and synergistic combination of oxaliplatin, gemcitabine, and SN38 (OGS), for the treatment of pancreatic cancer in mouse models. OGS contains key synergistic components of FOLFIRINOX with controllable drug ratios, particle stability in blood circulation, and enhanced deposition of the drugs in acidic tumor environments. In vitro, OGS showed superior cytotoxicity over free drug combinations and exhibited robust cytotoxic synergism among its three components. In vivo, OGS improved drug circulation, increased tumor deposition, and exhibited superior antitumor efficacy over the free drug combination in both subcutaneous and orthotopic pancreatic tumor models. OGS treatment achieved 75–91% tumor growth inhibition and prolonged mouse survival by 1.6- to 2.8-folds while minimizing systemic toxicity such as neutropenia, hepatotoxicity, and renal toxicity. This work uncovers a novel and clinically relevant nanomedicine strategy to co-deliver synergistic combination chemotherapies for difficult-to-treat cancers.

Introduction

Pancreatic cancer is the third leading cause of cancer-related death in the United States and has a 5-year survival rate of 11% with the current standard of care. Approximately 80% of pancreatic cancer cases are diagnosed at the advanced stage and considered inoperable because of the high risk associated with surgical removal of tumors connected to surrounding blood vessels and the gastrointestinal tract. Chemotherapy remains front-line treatment for this deadly cancer. Gemcitabine (GEM) monotherapy was the first chemotherapy to show survival benefits for patients with advanced pancreatic cancer, but has high general toxicity and often leads to drug resistance after repeated treatment.

Combination chemotherapy regimens can stimulate different mechanisms of action to promote synergy between the drugs and overcome drug resistance. For example, FOLFIRINOX, a combination regimen of fluorouracil (5-FU), folinic acid (a vitamin potentiator), irinotecan (Iri), and oxaliplatin (OxPt) demonstrated a higher response rate (32%) than GEM (9%) and extended median overall survival to 11.1 months from 6.8 months for GEM in the first-line setting of advanced pancreatic cancer. However, due to different and suboptimal pharmacokinetics (PK) as well as lack of tumor specificity of the drug components, the FOLFIRINOX regimen causes severe adverse events, including neutropenia, diarrhea, and sensory neuropathy, in a high percentage of patients with a small therapeutic window. Thus, there is a strong need for a drug delivery system (DDS) that can co-deliver multiple chemotherapeutic agents to pancreatic tumors to mimic the FOLFIRINOX regimen, affording higher therapeutic efficacy without causing severe adverse events.

Nanoparticles have offered promising DDSs for chemotherapeutics by stabilizing and protecting drugs in circulation, improving PK, and minimizing general toxicity to normal organs. These effects were realized by controlling the nanoparticle size to be greater than 10 nm and less than 200 nm to avoid clearance by the reticuloendothelial and mononuclear phagocyte systems. Among many nanoparticle DDSs, liposomes have proven the most effective for encapsulating single chemotherapeutics to reduce general toxicity by extending blood circulation half-lives and reducing maximum drug concentrations in the plasmas. Several liposomal formulations, including Doxil and Onivyde, have been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration for treating various solid tumors. However, these liposomal formulations cannot efficiently co-deliver multiple drug components with disparate physicochemical properties (such as hydrophobicity) in standard combination chemotherapy regimens. Although some DDSs were developed to co-deliver the active components of the FOLFIRINOX regimen, the encapsulation efficiencies of these systems were moderate and the drug ratios in the systems were difficult to control.

Here, we report the design of a tumor-activatable nanoscale coordination polymer (NCP) nanoparticle carrying the triple combination of oxaliplatin, gemcitabine, and SN38 (the active metabolite of Iri with 100–1000 times greater cytotoxicity) to mimic the FOLFIRINOX regimen for the treatment of pancreatic cancer. The oxaliplatin/gemcitabine/SN38 (OGS) NCP stably carried hydrophilic oxaliplatin bisphosphate (OxPt-p) and gemcitabine monophosphate (GMP) prodrugs in its zinc-phosphate-copolymerized core and the hydrophobic cholesterol-SN38 conjugate prodrug (cSN38) in its lipid shell. The highly tunable synthesis of the core-shell structure allowed formulation optimization of OGS nanoparticles via in vitro cytotoxicity assays. OGS was stable under normal biological conditions but showed a preferential release of OxPt, GEM, and SN38 upon nanoparticle disintegration in acidic tumor environments and endocytosis into endo/lysosomal compartments. The prodrugs for OxPt, GEM, and SN38 not only stabilized their encapsulation into the NCP particle but also enhanced potency and synergism over the free drug triple combination (OxPt+GEM+Iri). When administered intravenously, OGS enhanced drug circulation and increased tumor deposition over the free drug triple combination. Lower doses of OGS exhibited superior antitumor efficacy over the free drug combination in subcutaneous and orthotopic pancreatic tumor models, prolonging survival by 1.6- to 2.8-folds while minimizing systemic toxicity such as neutropenia, hepatoxicity, and renal toxicity.

Results

Design, synthesis, and characterization of OGS particles

The OGS NCP particle was designed to simultaneously deliver prodrugs of the three key potent components in FOLFIRINOX: OxPt, 5-FU, and Iri. Among them, 5-FU functions properly with the presence of folinic acid to inhibit thymidylate synthase. Similar to 5-FU, GEM is also a fluorinated nucleotide analog but can function alone without folinic acid. Considering the loading capacity of NCP particles, we used the more potent derivatives of GEM and Iri, namely, gemcitabine monophosphate prodrug (GMP) and cholesterol-SN38 conjugate prodrug (cSN38), along with oxaliplatin bisphosphate prodrug Pt(dach)(oxalate)(bisphosphoramidic acid) (OxPt-p) in the OGS particle to mimic the FOLFIRINOX regimen for pancreatic cancer. GMP was synthesized by phosphorylating the 5’-OH group of GEM, which is the rate-limiting step for the in vivo deoxycytidine kinase-catalyzed conversion of GEM to gemcitabine triphosphate, the active entity for GEM. GMP was co-polymerized with OxPt-p in the Zn-phosphate NCP core, which was further coated with lipid bilayer containing cSN38 to result in the core-shell OGS NCP particle (Fig. 1a, Fig. S1). The cSN38 prodrug was prepared by conjugating cholesterol to the O-20 trimethylsilyl (TMS)-protected SN38 via an acid-sensitive acetal linker.

OGS particles were prepared in a two-step sequence. First, the NCP core (OG-bare) was synthesized by co-polymerizing OxPt-p and GMP with Zn2+ ions in a reverse microemulsion containing 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphate (DOPA). Next, OG-bare was coated with a lipid shell comprising cSN38, cholesterol, 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DOPC), and 1,2-diastearoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-[amino(polyethylene glycol)2000] (DSPE-PEG2k). The resulting core-shell OGS nanoparticle encapsulated OxPt-p and GMP in the hydrophilic NCP core and cSN38 in the hydrophobic region of the lipid shell (Fig. 1a). Monotherapy control particles were synthesized similarly. Sodium phosphate was substituted for GMP in the oxaliplatin monotherapy nanoparticle (OX-NCP) and likewise substituted for OxPt-p in the gemcitabine monotherapy particle (GMP-NCP). In these monotherapy NCPs, cSN38 was omitted in the lipid shell. For the cSN38 monotherapy nanoparticle (Zn/SN38), sodium phosphate was substituted for both OxPt-p and GMP in the synthesis of the NCP core.

OG-bare and OGS both exhibited a uniform spherical morphology under transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Fig. 1b, c). Dynamic light scattering (DLS) showed monodispersed particles of OG-bare and OGS with Z-average diameters of 61.7 ± 1.7 and 98.8 ± 1.4 nm, respectively, and polydispersity indices (PDIs) of 0.13 ± 0.01 and 0.18 ± 0.01, respectively (Fig. 1d). OG-bare was shown to be stable in tetrahydrofuran based on its constant diameter and unchanged PDI over time (Fig. 1e), which allows its storage at room temperature before shell coating. OGS was stable in an aqueous solution and exhibited constant diameter and PDI in PBS containing 5 mg/mL bovine serum albumin (BSA) at 37°C (Fig. 1f).

In the NCP core synthesis, the encapsulation efficiencies of OxPt-p and GMP were determined to be 77.0 ± 0.3 % and 84.3 ± 2.0 %, respectively. In the lipid coating step, the encapsulation efficiency of cSN38 was 99.5 ± 0.4 % (Fig. 1g). No leaching of OxPt-p or GMP occurred during the lipid coating step. These results demonstrate the successful synthesis of stable OGS particles with controllable and consistent ratios among the three active components.

pH-responsive release of OxPt, GMP, and SN38 from OGS

We examined the drug release behaviors of OGS under different pH conditions to evaluate its ability to deposit drugs preferentially in specific tissues. We evaluated the drug release kinetics of OGS in PBS at pH 7.4 and 5.5, to mimic normal physiological conditions and acidic conditions of tumor tissues, respectively. Zn2+-coordinated NCP cores have been previously reported to disassemble under acidic conditions. OGS released 87.1 ± 3.0 % Pt and 80.4 ± 5.3 % GMP after incubation in pH 5.5 PBS for 72 h, but only 18.4 ± 2.3 % Pt and 18.2 ± 0.6 % GMP after incubation in pH 7.4 PBS for 72 h (Figs. 1h–1i). This result shows that OxPt and GMP are released from the NCP core in a pH-responsive manner and OGS can preferentially deposit OxPt and GMP in acidic tumor environments. We further showed that the acetal linker between cholesterol and SN38 in cSN38 was acid-sensitive, with the 72 h-release of 72.5 ± 3.4 % and 1.7 ± 0.3 % of SN38 from OGS at pH 5.5 and 7.4, respectively (Fig. 1j). This result is consistent with our previous report of selective release of SN38 from cSN38 in acidic tumor tissues. Taken together, the disassembly of the NCP core and hydrolysis of cSN38 were both responsive to low pH conditions that are unique to the tumor microenvironment (TME), suggesting the ability to preferentially release all three active components from OGS in tumors.

Optimization of the drug ratios in OGS particles

To optimize the drug ratios of the triple-drug OGS nanoparticles, we screened a range of drug ratios between OxPt-p, GMP, and cSN38 using NCP monotherapy formulations (OX-NCP, GMP-NCP, and Zn/SN38) based on in-vitro cytotoxicity assays on murine Panc02 pancreatic cancer cells. In a 9×9 square matrix, increasing concentrations of one drug were administered to individual wells along one axis, while increasing concentrations of another drug were administered to the wells along the second axis (Fig. 2a). All possible pairing arrangements of the three drugs were tested in three separate dosing matrices, providing a combinatorial readout of optimal dosing ratios. The cell viability percentage for each concentration was then entered into SynergyFinder, a free web application for calculating and visualizing synergy scores for drug combinations (www.synergyfinder.fimm.fi). The results showed that the delta score (δ), which indicates optimal paired dose effect (i.e., synergistic effect), reaches maximal values when the ratio between OX-NCP and GMP-NCP falls in the range of 1.25:1 to 5:1. Similarly, the most synergistic range of OX-NCP to Zn/SN38 ratios was 1:1 to 1:2, whereas the most synergistic range of GMP-NCP to Zn/SN38 ratios was 1:2.5 to 1:5. After balancing the synergistic drug ratios from the three dosing matrices, we chose the ratio of OxPt-p:GMP:cSN38 as 2:1:4 in the OGS particle. This drug ratio for OGS and the equivalent free drug combination was used for subsequent in vitro studies.

Superior potency and synergistic cytotoxicity of OGS

We evaluated the in vitro cytotoxicity of OGS on two murine (Panc02 and KPC) and two human (BxPc-3 and AsPc-1) pancreatic cancer cell lines by MTS assay. OGS demonstrated 6.8- to 36.2-fold higher cytotoxicity than the equivalent triple free drug combination of OxPt, GEM, and Iri (OxPt+GEM+Iri) in the four cell lines (Fig. 2b). IC50 values for OGS and OxPt+GEM+Iri were 0.01 ± 0.01 μM and 0.36 ± 0.14 μM, respectively, in Panc02 cells, 0.02 ± 0.01 μM and 0.46 ± 0.09 μM, respectively, in KPC cells, 1.04 ± 0.11 μM and 7.03 ± 0.48 μM, respectively, in BxPc-3 cells, and 0.87 ± 0.12 and 6.99 ± 0.38 μM, respectively, in AsPc-1 cells. We believe that the increased cytotoxicity of OGS over OxPt+GEM+Iri mainly results from the higher potency of GMP and cSN38 in OGS than that of GEM and Iri in the free drug combination.

To further probe the synergy among OxPt-p, GMP, and cSN38 in OGS, we also profiled the cytotoxicity of each drug as a free drug or in NCP formulation (Table. S1). On Panc02 cells, the combination of OxPt-p, GMP, and cSN38 in OGS significantly reduced the OxPt-p IC50 value from 15.45 ± 6.52 μM for OX-NCP to 0.01 ± 0.01 μM for OGS, the GMP IC50 value from 0.03 ± 0.01 μM for GMP-NCP to 0.01 ± 0.01 μM for OGS, and the cSN38 IC50 value from 1.21 ± 0.47 μM for Zn/SN38 to 0.02 ± 0.01 μM for OGS. Similar synergistic effects were also found for KPC, BxPc-3, and AsPc-1 cells; OGS reduced the IC50 values by 4.3- to 1490-folds for OxPt-p, 2.7- to 44-folds for GMP, and 3.1- to 20-folds for cSN38.

Drug synergy was further validated by comparing the drug combination index (CI) of OGS to that of OxPt+GEM+Iri. CI was calculated using the Chou-Talalay method. Isobolograms graphing CI as a function of in-vitro elicited drug effect were plotted, where CI<1 indicates the standard threshold for synergy, as opposed to additivity at CI=1 and antagonism at CI>1. In all four pancreatic cell lines, OGS exhibited synergism at all drug effect levels. In contrast, OxPt + GEM + Iri showed synergism at some drug effect levels for Panc02, KPC, and BxPc-3 cells but antagonism at all drug effect levels for AsPc-1 cells (Fig. 2c). This result supports the choice of the drug ratios in OGS and the strong synergy of the three drug components in OGS.

In vitro mechanisms of cell death

We performed Incucyte Live-Cell Analysis to measure the real-time confluence of Panc02 cells upon treatment with OxPt, GEM, Iri, OxPt+GEM+Iri, OX-NCP, GMP-NCP, or OGS at equivalents of 0.2 μM OxPt, 0.1 μM GEM, or/and 0.4 μM SN38 over 72 h. Compared to partial inhibition of tumor proliferation by OxPt+GEM+Iri (confluence at 35.5 ± 4.1% vs. PBS at 78.9 ± 4.5%, Fig. 3a–c, Fig. S2), OGS strongly inhibited cell proliferation to afford low confluency of 3.9 ± 0.2%. Except for GEM and GMP-NCP, single-free drugs or single-drug NCPs did not show an obvious inhibition effect. GEM and GMP-NCP showed slight inhibition effects with 51.9 ± 9.6%, and 30.9 ± 2.6% confluency, respectively. Growth rate inhibition (GRI) analysis showed that the GRI of OxPt+GEM+Iri first decreased within 48 h but gradually increased after that, while the GRI of OGS sharply decreased to and maintained around 0 over 72h. These results show that the high cytotoxicity of OGS is due to both the synergy of the active components and its ability to retain drug potency over a long period of time.

In vitro cell apoptosis and necrosis were detected by Annexin V-FITC and PI staining, respectively, on Panc02 cells (Fig. 3d, Fig. S3). Treatment of Panc02 cells with single free drugs, monotherapy NCPs, and OxPt+GEM+Iri at equivalents of 0.2 μM OxPt, 0.1 μM GEM, or/and 0.8 SN38 for 24 h did not show obvious apoptotic/necrotic cell populations. In contrast, OGS treatment at the same concentrations showed a marked increase in late apoptotic/necrotic Annexin V+/PI+ cells (45.8 % for OGS compared to 12.5 % for OxPt+GEM+Iri). Confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) imaging revealed that Panc02 cells treated with OGS showed upregulation of phosphatidylserine by Annexin V staining on the cell membranes and colocalization of propidium iodide (PI) in the cell nuclei (Fig. 3e, Fig. S4). These features were largely absent in Panc02 cells treated with OxPt+GEM+Iri. Thus, OGS treatment causes apoptosis and compromised membrane function in Panc02 cells.

We further analyzed cell cycle distributions in Panc02 cells after 24 h exposure to free drugs or OGS to measure the extent of DNA damage and DNA replication inhibition (Fig. 3f, Fig. S5). OGS caused cell cycle arrest in the S phase, increasing the proportion of cells found in the S phase to 32.4 ± 2.1 % from 13.8 ± 1.8 % in PBS control. In contrast, OxPt+GEM+Iri did not show obvious S phase arrest with 17.1 ± 2.1 % cells in the S phase. The high S phase arrest of OGS significantly reduced the distribution in the G2/M phase to 2.7 ± 1.3 % from 32.4 ± 3.5 % in PBS control, indicating disruption of cell division and mitosis by OGS treatment.

DNA damage induces the intrinsic pathway of early apoptosis, causing Bcl-2 imbalance which further depolarizes mitochondrial membrane potential to release reactive oxygen species (ROS). Mitochondrial membrane potential loss can be detected by JC-1 staining imaging to probe the induction of early apoptosis by OGS. CLSM imaging showed that OGS treatment significantly reduced J-aggregate to produce JC-1 monomer in Panc02 cells; OGS showed 16.1-fold higher monomer/J-aggregate ratio than PBS while OxPt+GEM+Iri showed only 1.3-fold higher monomer/J-aggregate ratio than PBS (Fig. 3g, Fig. S6). These results show that OGS-mediated DNA damage and replication inhibition strongly induce depolarization of mitochondria membrane potential and biochemical cascades associated with programmed cell death.

OGS enhances drug pharmacokinetics and delivery to tumors

To investigate enhanced drug delivery to tumors by OGS, we synthesized OxPt/GMP/Pyro (OGP) particles by replacing cSN38 with fluorescent cholesterol-pyropheophytin (chol-Pyro) as a surrogate. A single dose of free Pyro or OGP was intravenously injected into subcutaneous Panc02 tumor-bearing mice (Fig. 4). Compared to the quick disappearance and low tumor accumulation of free Pyro, OGP was found to enrich over time in tumors with an AUC0→t (area under curve) value 3.7 times that of Pyro (34.1 × 109 vs. 9.8 × 109 average radian efficiency × hour, Fig. 4a, b). At 48 h post-injection, the tumors and major organs were collected and scanned for fluorescence signal distribution (Fig. 4c, d). Free Pyro was found primarily in the liver and spleen, while OGP was found primarily in the tumor. Consistent with the enhanced AUC0→t, tumors and plasmas from OGP-treated mice showed significantly higher fluorescence intensity than Pyro-treated mice, with 3.0-fold higher intensity in tumors and 1.4-fold higher intensity in plasmas. These results show that OGS NCP circulates well in the bloodstream and accumulates in tumors, avoiding rapid clearance and metabolic elimination by major organs.

We also quantitatively analyzed the PK and biodistribution of all three active components in OGS (Fig. 4e–k). In the PK study, OGS effectively resisted MPS clearance and significantly increased blood exposure (AUCs) of OxPt, GMP, and cSN38 by 225-, 48-, and 43-folds, respectively, over OxPt+GEM+Iri, after intravenous injections to healthy C57BL/6 mice (Fig. 4e–g, Table S4–6). OGS also increased the Pt half-life to 28.7 h from 0.2 h for OxPt, the GMP half-life to 27.2 h from the GEM half-life of 0.2 h for GEM, and the cSN38 half-life to 22.2 h from the Iri half-life of 1.3 h for Iri.

In the biodistribution study, OGS showed a 4.3-fold higher tumor Pt AUC than OxPt without increasing Pt concentrations in other organs (Fig. 4h, i). The Pt concentrations in tumors remained above the IC50 value of Panc02 cells for a period of 48 h. In comparison, OxPt gave a tumor Pt concentration less than the IC50 value throughout 48 h. OGS also accumulated in tumors with a 5.4-fold higher GMP AUC than GEM AUC for GEM, and a 6.2-fold higher SN38 AUC than Iri (Fig. 4j, k). These results show that OGS markedly increases the concentrations of OxPt, GMP, and SN38 in tumors due to its long circulation in the blood and the successful release of all three potent therapeutics in the tumors.

OGS elicits in vivo antitumor effects in pancreatic cancer

The in-vivo anticancer efficacy of OGS was first evaluated in subcutaneous Panc02 and KPC murine pancreatic tumor models and BxPc-3 and AsPc-1 mouse tumor xenograft models of human pancreatic cancer. Mice were intravenously injected with PBS, OX-NCP, GMP-NCP, Zn/SN38, or OGS at equivalents of 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 1.2 mg/kg GEM, and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 or OxPt+GEM+Iri with 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 48 mg/kg GEM (40 times higher than OGS), and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 equivalent once every three days (Fig. 5a–d).

Following 8 injections of OGS, Panc02 tumor-bearing C57BL/6 mice showed superior antitumor efficacy with 88.2% tumor growth inhibition (TGI). Despite the use of 40 times higher GEM, OxPt+GEM+Iri exhibited a moderate antitumor effect with a TGI of 45.1%. OX-NCP, GMP-NCP, and Zn/SN38 monotherapy nanoparticles slightly inhibited tumor growth with TGI values of 29.7%, 23.2%, and 25.2%, respectively. The sum of monotherapy TGI values (78.1%) is smaller than that of OGS.

In the KPC model, OGS also showed potent anticancer activity with a TGI of 91.1% after 6 doses. In comparison, OxPt+GEM+Iri provided a modest TGI of 62.4% (Fig. 5b). OGS showed a strong synergy between the three active components to significantly enhance the anticancer efficacy over OX-NCP (19.6% TGI), GMP-NCP (34.9% TGI), and Zn/SN38 (25.7% TGI). Again, the sum of monotherapy TGI values (80.2%) is smaller than that of OGS. With improved PK, co-delivery of three prodrugs by OGS synergized the three drugs to enhance antitumor effects.

OGS also showed excellent anticancer efficacy in the xenograft models of human pancreatic cancers. For the BxPc-3 model, OGS regressed the tumors with a TGI of 95.6% at the PBS endpoint. OxPt+GEM+Iri moderately inhibited the tumors with a TGI of 69.1%. The tumors in the OxPt+GEM+Iri group grew gradually and continuously, while the tumors in the OGS group regressed for 12 days and showed postponed growth. OGS also showed potent antitumor efficacy against AsPc-1 tumors with a TGI of 79.0% at the PBS endpoint on Day 24, while OxPt+GEM+Iri showed a TGI of 54.0%.

We also analyzed mouse survival based on pre-specified tumor sizes at the endpoints (Fig. 5e–h, Table S2). While OxPt+GEM+Iri moderately prolonged the median survival to 29 days from 23.6 days for PBS in the Panc02 model and to 27 days from 19 days for PBS in the KPC model, OGS significantly extended the median survival to 40 and 52 days in Panc02 and KPC model, respectively. Monotherapy NCPs only slightly increased median survival. Similarly, while OxPt+GEM+Iri moderately extended the median survival to 23.5 days from 13 days for PBS in the BxPc-3 model and to 38 days from 25 days for PBS in the AsPc-1 model, OGS significantly prolonged median survival to 36.5 and 49 days in BxPc-3 and AsPc-1 model, respectively. H&E, TUNEL, and Caspase 3 staining revealed severe tumor apoptosis/necrosis in the OGS group (Fig. 5i). Furthermore, the mice showed slight body weight loss after 6 doses of OxPt+GEM+Iri, while the mice in other treatment groups showed stable body weight (Fig. S7). This result suggests the lack of general toxicity after multiple doses of OGS.

We further developed an orthotopic pancreatic cancer model by implanting Panc02 tumor cells into the pancreas of C57BL/6 mice and monitoring tumor growth by ultrasound imaging. When tumors reached >0.5 cm in any dimension at Day 3 post-inoculation, tumor-bearing mice were intravenously injected with OGS at equivalents of 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 1.2 mg/kg GEM, and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 or OxPt+GEM+Iri with 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 48 mg/kg GEM, and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 equivalent once every three days. As shown in Fig 6, both OxPt+GEM+Iri and OGS treatments slowed the progression of orthotopic cancer when compared to PBS. OxPt+GEM+Iri moderately slowed the growth of orthotopic tumors with a TGI of 47.1%, while OGS significantly inhibited tumor growth with a TGI of 75.2%. After four doses, mice in the PBS group began to die from tumor burden. While OxPt+GEM+Iri treatment prolonged the median survival to 28 days from 23 days for PBS, OGS greatly extended the median survival to 45 days. These results show the potent antitumor effect of OGS in orthotopic pancreatic tumor models.

Preliminary safety evaluations

A major drawback of the FOLFIRINOX regimen is the severity of adverse events in pancreatic cancer patients. Only patients with very good performance statuses are eligible for FOLFIRINOX regimen and many of these patients will experience severe hematological toxicities (such as neutropenia). We collected the blood from C57BL/6 mice intravenously injected with PBS, OX-NCP, GMP-NCP, Zn/SN38, or OGS at equivalents of 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 1.2 mg/kg GEM, and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 or OxPt+GEM+Iri with 3.5 mg/kg OxPt, 48 mg/kg GEM, and 6.9 mg/kg SN38 equivalent once every three days for 8 doses. OxPt+GEM+Iri treatment induced a significant decrease in leukocyte, lymphocyte, and neutrophile counts (Fig. 7a–c). In contrast, OGS and monotherapy nanoparticles did not show different hematological values from the PBS control.

We also evaluated liver toxicity by testing aspartate aminotransferase (AST) and alanine transaminase (ALT) levels and kidney toxicity by measuring creatinine levels in plasma collected from the mice (Fig. 7d–f). OxPt+GEM+Iri treatment induced a significant increase in AST, ALT, and creatinine levels while OGS and monotherapy nanoparticles maintained similar levels of AST, ALT, and creatinine as the PBS control. Furthermore, histological analysis revealed that OGS and monotherapy nanoparticles did not cause any abnormality in the heart, liver, spleen, kidneys, or lungs (Fig. S8). These results indicate that OGS treatment significantly reduces hematologic, renal, and hepatic toxicities while enhancing antitumor efficacy when compared to the OxPt+GEM+Iri treatment.

Discussion

The FOLFIRINOX regimen is effective for treating advanced pancreatic cancer due to its ability to elicit different mechanisms of DNA replication inhibition. The different PK behaviors of the active components and their inability to selectively deposit drugs in the tumors lead to severe adverse events in about half of patients (Fig. 7g). Nanomedicines provide a strategy to prolong the circulation of drugs in the bloodstream and enhance the deposition of these drugs in tumors. By taking advantage of the ability of the NCP platform to co-deliver multiple therapeutics with vastly different physicochemical properties (e.g., hydrophobicity), we developed OGS by encapsulating prodrugs of key synergistic components of the FOLFIRINOX regimen. Unlike liposomal particles, OGS encapsulated the drugs in tunable and controllable ratios. OGS first immobilized hydrophilic OxPt-p and GMP via coordination polymerization in surfactant-templated reverse microemulsions. Here, DOPA acts as a surfactant template with the hydrophilic phosphate head aligning with the water droplet and the hydrophobic tail with the oil phase. Upon mixing of water droplets rich in phosphates, the introduction of zinc ions initiated coordination polymerization of all phosphate-containing molecules, resulting in the formation of NCP bare particles. The OG-bare particle then quantitatively encapsulated hydrophobic cSN38 via hydrophobic-hydrophobic interactions without leaching OxPt-p and GMP from the NCP core. OGS accommodates tunable and consistent ratios among the drugs and maintains particle stability in the bloodstream. The pH-responsive release of OGS enhances the deposition of the drugs in the acidic TME. Upon exposing OGS in the acidic TME or endo/lysosome condition, the zinc phosphate coordination polymer core started to dissemble, leading to the release of OxPt and GMP from the core. In the meantime, low pH triggers the release of SN38 by cleaving the acetal linker and the TMS protecting group in cSN38. As a result, all three potent drugs could be effectively enriched in the tumor through this trigger-release mechanism, enhancing their therapeutic efficacy.

Monotherapy NCPs were designed to optimize the drug ratio in OGS based on in-vitro cytotoxicity assays. We determined the optimal ranges of drug ratio based on all synergy scores in the Bliss Independence model and optimized the ratio of OGS to OxPt: GEM: SN38 = 2:1:4, which was proved to be potent and exhibit strong synergy in four pancreatic cancer cell lines according to the Chou-Talalay method. In this method, OGS demonstrated synergistic effects across all drug effect levels, while OxPt+GEM+Iri exhibited synergistic effects at partial drug effect levels in Panc02, KPC, and BxPc-3 cells, but antagonistic effects at all drug effect levels in AsPc-1 cells. The optimization of multi-component ratios presents a significant challenge when transitioning from in vitro to in vivo conditions due to PK variations of different drugs. Here, we use the co-delivery strategy to overcome this challenge by enabling precise screening of drug ratios in vitro and enuring synergistic effects in vivo.

OGS showed higher potency than the equivalent free drug combination OxPt+GEM+Iri, due in part to the higher cytotoxicity of GMP and cSN38 over GEM and Iri, respectively. Deoxycytidine kinase-catalyzed mono-phosphorylation of GEM to GMP is the rate-limiting step of gemcitabine triphosphate synthesis from GEM, while cSN38 releases SN38 more easily than Iri due to the labile acetal linker. Triggered release of the three drugs from OGS in the tumor synergistically causes potent cell cycle arrest, apoptosis, and necrosis. OxPt forms DNA adducts and inhibits DNA replication. GMP is rapidly phosphorylated to form the active metabolite, gemcitabine triphosphate, that causes misincorporation of fluoronucleotides to promote DNA damage. SN38 binds to topoisomerase I and DNA complexes to prevent DNA replication. DNA damage induces the intrinsic pathway of early apoptosis, leading to depolarization of the mitochondrial membrane potential and ultimately programmed cell death.

OGS enhanced drug circulation and increased tumor deposition by 3.7-folds and exhibited superior antitumor efficacy over OxPt+GEM+Iri in both murine and human subcutaneous tumor models as well as an orthotopic Panc02 pancreatic tumor model. OGS prolonged mouse survival by 17–33 days over PBS control and by 11–25 days over OxPt+GEM+Iri in these tumor models. At a therapeutically efficacious dose, OGS minimized the side effects and showed no hematological, hepatological, or renal toxicities.

In summary, we developed a core-shell NCP encapsulating OxPt-p, GMP, and cSN38 in an optimal ratio to mimic the FOLFIRINOX regimen. OGS showed strong synergy among the three drugs and high drug deposition in tumors to achieve potent tumor growth inhibition in four pancreatic tumor models without causing neutropenia, hepatotoxicity, or renal toxicity. Our study uncovers a novel and clinically relevant nanomedicine strategy to co-deliver synergistic combination chemotherapies for advanced pancreatic cancer.
