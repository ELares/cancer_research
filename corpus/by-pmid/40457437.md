# Precision nanomedicine: navigating the tumor microenvironment for enhanced cancer immunotherapy and targeted drug delivery.

## Abstract

Cancer treatment has been revolutionized by immunotherapy and nanomedicine, offering innovative strategies to overcome the tumor microenvironment (TME) complexities. However, challenges such as therapeutic resistance, off-target effects, and immune suppression necessitate advanced delivery systems and combination approaches. Recent advancements in nanoparticle-based therapies, biomimetic platforms, and personalized immunotherapy provide promising solutions to enhance therapeutic efficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity. This review explores recent nanoparticle-mediated immunotherapy developments, highlighting strategies to optimize drug delivery, remodel the TME, and improve patient-specific treatment outcomes. A comprehensive review of recent literature focused on nanoparticle-based drug delivery, stimuli-responsive systems, biomimetic nanoplatforms, and personalized immunotherapy approaches. The effectiveness of combination therapies integrating physical and immunological strategies was also analyzed. Nanoparticle-mediated immunotherapy enables precise targeting and controlled drug release, significantly improving therapeutic outcomes. Biomimetic nanoplatforms enhance immune modulation and drug bioavailability, while personalized immunotherapy, guided by predictive biomarkers, tailors treatment to individual patients. Advanced nanomedicine strategies, including TME remodeling, targeted genome editing, and combination immunotherapies, offer promising avenues for overcoming limitations in conventional cancer treatments. Future research should optimize nanoformulations, integrate multi-modal treatment strategies, and refine biomarker-driven personalization to enhance clinical outcomes.

Introduction

Cancer remains one of the most formidable challenges in modern medicine, with its complexity and heterogeneity posing significant obstacles to effective treatment. Traditional therapies such as chemotherapy, radiation, and surgery have limitations, including systemic toxicity, drug resistance, and incomplete eradication of cancer cells. In recent years, immunotherapy has emerged as a transformative approach, harnessing the body's immune system to target and destroy cancer cells. However, the efficacy of immunotherapy is often hindered by factors within and beyond the TME, such as immunosuppressive conditions, hypoxia, and physical barriers like collagen deposition. To address these challenges, innovative strategies such as nanoparticle-mediated drug delivery, combination therapies, and personalized medicine are being developed to enhance the precision and effectiveness of cancer therapy.

Nanoparticle-based approaches have revolutionized cancer therapy by enabling targeted delivery of therapeutic agents, reducing systemic toxicity, and improving drug bioavailability. These nanocarriers can be engineered to respond to specific stimuli within the TME, such as acidic pH, hypoxia, or overexpressed enzymes, ensuring controlled release of drugs at the tumor site. For instance, pH-responsive nanoparticles have been designed to release their payload in the acidic TME. At the same time, hypoxia-responsive systems activate under low oxygen conditions to enhance the efficacy of chemotherapy and radiotherapy. Additionally, enzyme-responsive nanoparticles exploit the overexpression of enzymes like matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) to achieve site-specific drug release.

Combination therapies, which integrate multiple treatment modalities, have demonstrated great promise in overcoming the limitations of single-agent therapies. By combining immunotherapy with chemotherapy, photothermal therapy, or targeted drug delivery, researchers can simultaneously target multiple pathways involved in tumor growth and resistance. For example, integrating photothermal therapy and immunotherapy using iron oxide nanoplatforms has demonstrated remarkable efficacy in pancreatic cancer treatment. Similarly, multifunctional nanocarriers that deliver chemotherapeutic agents and immunomodulators can enhance anti-tumor immune responses and improve therapeutic outcomes.

Personalized medicine represents a critical advancement in cancer therapy, leveraging patient-specific biomarkers and genetic profiles to tailor treatments. Machine learning models and quantitative histopathological analyses are being utilized to predict the efficacy of immunotherapies and identify patients who are most likely to benefit from specific treatments. For instance, mitophagy-related gene signatures in rectal cancer and intestinal subtypes of gastric adenocarcinoma have been identified as predictive biomarkers for immunotherapy response. These advancements improve treatment efficacy and minimize unnecessary side effects, thus enhancing the overall patients’ quality of life.

Despite these advancements, challenges remain in optimizing nanoparticle-mediated therapies and overcoming biological barriers within the TME. The dense extracellular matrix (ECM), interstitial fluid pressure, and immunosuppressive conditions can impede drug delivery and limit therapeutic efficacy. Therefore, to address these challenges, researchers are developing multifunctional nanoplatforms that combine TME-responsive properties with strategies to enhance drug penetration and modulate the immune response. For example, size-shrinkable nanoparticles that exploit the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect have been designed to penetrate dense tumor tissues. In contrast, iron oxide nanoparticles have been used to repolarize tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) from a pro-tumor to an anti-tumor phenotype.

This review focuses on the latest developments in stimuli-responsive nanocarriers (e.g., pH-, redox-, and enzyme-sensitive systems) for tumor-specific drug delivery, emphasizing strategies to overcome biological barriers, including (1) the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect—its role in passive targeting and limitations in heterogeneous tumors, (2) active targeting strategies using ligand-functionalized nanoparticles, and (3) externally triggered systems such as light-, magnetic-, and ultrasound-responsive nanocarriers.

Cancer immunotherapy

Immunotherapy is having a significant impact through innovative strategies, such as nanoparticle-based approaches, combination therapies, and personalized medicine, aimed at addressing the challenges of cancer treatment. Factors both within and beyond the TME have a profound impact on the effectiveness of immunotherapy. Resistance mechanisms in non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), for instance, are associated with physical barriers like collagen deposition and hypoxic conditions that hinder T-cell infiltration and suppress the development of lymphoid structures. Metabolic modulation, including enhancing mannose metabolism, is emerging as a promising approach to improve T-cell stemness and anti-tumor activity. The PANoptosome pathway appears to be a promising target for immunotherapy, with its activation linked to improved therapeutic responses (Fig. 1).

A primary focus is given to nanoparticle-mediated delivery of immunomodulatory agents, which utilizes stimuli-responsive and combination strategies to boost therapeutic effects. For example, starvation-immunotherapy—combining ultra-long-acting L-asparaginase with checkpoint inhibitors—effectively depletes L-asparagine and inhibits metastasis. Moreover, extracellular vesicles loaded with IL-12 minicircle plasmids deliver immunostimulatory cytokines directly to tumor cells. Manganese-coordinated nanoparticles that integrate photosensitizers and STING agonists synergistically activate innate and adaptive immunity via photo-/immuno-therapy. Precision intelligent nanomissiles transform cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) and induce immunogenic cell death, reshaping the TME (Fig. 2). Trinity STING-activating nanoparticles stimulate autologous STING signaling in cancer cells, promoting dendritic cell maturation and T-cell priming. Peptide-conjugated photodynamic therapy specifically degrades HER2 proteins, enhancing cytotoxic T-cell activity. Additionally, self-assembled PROTAC nanoparticles are being developed for sustained PD-L1 degradation, which triggers anti-tumor immune responses. Bioactive nanocomposite hydrogels co-deliver anti-PD-L1 antibodies and other agents, stimulating CD8 + T-cell activity in osteosarcoma. Furthermore, combined photothermal and hypoxia-activated immunotherapies utilizing nanoparticles can induce immunogenic cell death and reverse immunosuppression.

Personalized immunotherapy is advancing through the identification of prognostic and predictive biomarkers. For instance, the mitophagy-related gene signatures in rectal cancer present promising avenues for tailored therapeutic approaches. Moreover, the intestinal subtype of gastric adenocarcinoma, coupled with machine learning models, has demonstrated reliability in predicting the efficacy of neoadjuvant immunochemotherapy. Machine learning classifiers that forecast tumor-reactive T-cell receptors are accelerating the development of personalized T-cell therapies. Furthermore, quantitative histopathological analyses and radiomic models derived from computed tomography (CT) scans provide non-invasive techniques to predict the efficacy of anti-PD-1 immunotherapy and the expression of PD-L1, thereby enhancing patient stratification.

Nevertheless, patient-specific factors, such as cancer cachexia and associated weight loss, significantly influence the outcomes of CAR T-cell therapy. However, not all combination immunotherapies yield beneficial results, as evidenced by the lack of efficacy of adjuvant nivolumab/ipilimumab in gastro-oesophageal adenocarcinoma.

Interestingly, innovative strategies are being devised to refine cancer treatments, emphasizing targeted genome editing, organoid models, and nanoparticle-based therapies to enhance immune responses and counteract immunosuppression. A multiple cancer-targeting genome editing system that utilizes aptamer-decorated nanoparticles effectively knocks out MUC1 and downregulates PD-L1 in circulating malignant cells, which leads to an increased secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines, thereby demonstrating a personalized approach to treatment. Patient-derived glioblastoma organoids (GBOs) act as real-time patient avatars, reflecting responses to CAR-T cell therapy and providing critical insights into CAR-T cell bioactivity and treatment efficacy. To reprogram the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment, engineered gold nanoparticles have been designed to regulate oncogenic microRNAs on demand and execute RNA-dependent photothermal immunotherapy, effectively inhibiting PD-1/PD-L1-mediated immune evasion while generating tumor-associated antigens to activate anti-tumor T cells. Additionally, natural compounds appear promising; for example, Ginkgolide B demonstrates efficacy by targeting the SREBP1/KLK8/CCL22 axis, effectively preventing immune escape in oral squamous cell carcinoma.

Multiplex immunohistochemical analysis of samples from head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) has uncovered that the presence of T cells, particularly CD4 + and CD8 + T cell subsets, can predict responses to neoadjuvant immunotherapy, with TIM3 + and LAG3 + T cells demonstrating diminished levels in responders post-treatment. The expression of extracellular HSP90α in driver-gene-negative non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) correlates with prognosis and the efficacy of immunotherapy, marking it as a potential predictive biomarker. Tumor-selective nano-dispatchers that regulate lactate metabolism while activating Toll-like receptors have been reported to enhance the effects of cancer immunotherapy by decreasing lactate levels within the TME and promoting T-cell infiltration. Moreover, in metastatic synovial sarcomas, the prevalence and co-expression of cancer-testis antigens, such as MAGE-A4 and NY-ESO-1, provide crucial insights for developing targeted T-cell therapies. Moreover, HPK1 kinase inhibitors have been identified as sufficient for selectively targeting HPK1 in T-cell-mediated cancer immunotherapy, presenting a more straightforward alternative compared with HPK1 degraders. In addition, pan-cancer and experimental studies emphasize the significance of CST2, linked with stomach adenocarcinoma proliferation and metastasis, thereby suggesting its potential as a therapeutic target.

Targeted delivery and cellular interactions

The precision in cancer therapy has led to the development of nanomaterials that harness specific cellular interactions for targeted drug delivery. A critical focus lies in enhancing immunotherapy through precise targeting within the tumor microenvironment. For instance, the strategic delivery of a tumor nanovaccine to tumor-associated myeloid cells has demonstrated promise in overcoming immune barriers and achieving long-term cancer immunotherapy. This point emphasizes the importance of selective cell targeting for effective immune modulation. Meanwhile, natural cellular carriers present a strategy for enhanced delivery precision, particularly in challenging environments like glioblastoma. Beyond natural shuttles, synthetic nanocarriers are engineered with tailored targeting capabilities to refine drug delivery. It has been suggested that dendrimers can target suppressor cells produced from monocytic myeloids systemically, providing further information on their biodistribution and possibilities for treatment.

Macrophage-derived mimetic nanovesicles, designed to target PD-L1 and CD73, have demonstrated effective modulation of immune checkpoints in bladder cancer immunotherapy, illustrating the potential of mimicking biological processes for therapeutic gain. Ultimately, achieving robust immune activation remains a key objective and mRNA delivery of a bispecific single-domain antibody to polarize tumor-associated macrophages can synergize immunotherapy against liver malignancies, highlighting the potential of combined molecular strategies.

Stimuli-responsive activation and controlled release

The ability to precisely control drug release and activation within the complex TME is substantial for maximizing therapeutic efficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity. Stimuli-responsive nanomaterials offer a promising avenue to achieve this level of control. For instance, light-triggered drug release provides on-demand therapeutic action, as demonstrated by the development of MBene as a theranostic nanoplatform for photo-controlled intratumoral retention and drug release. Beyond light, the strategic design of supramolecular structures amplifies therapeutic effects through intracellular cascade reactions, enhancing chemodynamic therapy. Furthermore, biomimetic approaches are increasingly utilized to improve drug delivery and biocompatibility. Mimicking natural platelet functions, for example, has led to the development of glycoprotein Ib-regulated microplatelet ghosts for safe distribution and photothermal monotherapy. Responsive polymer matrices also play a crucial role in achieving controlled release. 10-hydroxy camptothecin-loaded starch-based microcapsules, designed for stepwise responsive release, ensure sustained drug delivery. To further refine targeting precision, dual-responsive systems are employed. Lipid/PAA-coated mesoporous silica nanoparticles, synthesized for dual-pH-responsive codelivery, exploit extracellular and intracellular pH gradients. Achieving high intracellular drug concentrations is another critical objective. In situ, tumor-triggered subcellular precise delivery of multi-drugs maximizes therapeutic impact at the cellular level. To combine multiple therapeutic modalities, dual-responsive nanohybrids have been developed. For example, a platform based on antifouling dendrimer-CuS nanohybrids integrates chemotherapy and photothermal therapy, enhancing tumor delivery and combination therapy. In addition, pH-responsive self-disintegrating nanoassemblies improve anti-breast cancer therapy, effectively balancing tumor accumulation and penetration. The ability to target metabolic pathways within the TME provides another layer of specificity, which is also explored in current research.

The versatility of stimuli-responsive nanomaterials is further exemplified by their ability to target specific metabolic pathways and achieve controlled drug release. For instance, inhibiting energy metabolism in glioblastoma has been accomplished using cascade-responsive 2-DG nanocapsules, demonstrating the potential for metabolic pathway targeting. Remarkably, the design of polymeric micelles enables precision control over drug release in addition to metabolic modification; research indicates that oxidative stress can cause controlled dissociation, which improves therapeutic efficacy. To further enhance drug delivery, multi-program responsive nanocarriers, such as vitamin E succinate-chitosan-histidine nanocarriers, have been developed, showcasing the ability to integrate multiple stimuli responses into a single platform. Incorporating apoptosis-inducing peptides into nanoparticles has also been examined, with PLGA nanoparticles effectively delivering these peptides to induce anti-tumor effects. Enhancing anti-tumor effects can also be achieved through stimulus-responsive nanoliposomes. At the same time, size and charge-switchable PAMAM dendrimers can facilitate deep tumor codelivery, improving drug penetration. The ability to design transformable nanoassemblies for multisite delivery further expands the therapeutic potential, allowing for targeted drug release at multiple tumor sites. Paclitaxel prodrug nanoparticles with dual-responsive and CD206 targeting have demonstrated improved anti-tumor effects, highlighting the significance of targeted drug delivery.

Morphological transformations of nanocarriers can enhance tumor retention, and ultrasound-facilitated release using Herceptin-decorated paclitaxel-loaded nanobubbles offers another avenue for controlled drug delivery. The engineering of porphyrin COFs for sequential chemo-photodynamic therapy demonstrates the ability to combine multiple therapeutic modalities within a single nano platform. On-demand drug delivery can be achieved using MMP-responsive mesoporous silica nanoparticles, highlighting the potential for enzyme-triggered drug release. To optimize cancer immunotherapy, glutathione-responsive and hydrogen sulfide self-generating nanocages have been designed, enhancing therapeutic efficacy through precise control of the TME.

Combination therapy can be further enhanced using targeted doxorubicin-loaded core–shell copper peroxide-mesoporous silica nanoparticles, combining ferroptosis and chemotherapy. Modulating liposome elasticity provides another strategy to improve cancer immunotherapy, demonstrating the importance of physical properties in nanocarrier design. Dynamic core crosslinked prodrug micelles have been developed for tumor-targeted delivery, and folic-acid-adorned alginate nanocomplexes have demonstrated promise for pH-triggered release, highlighting the diversity of stimuli-responsive systems. Biopolymers such as sericin and gluten have been investigated for magnetic nanoparticle-mediated drug delivery, demonstrating their potential in cancer therapy. ROS-triggered self-assembled nanoparticles have been utilized for chemo-sonodynamic therapy, and nanoparticle-stabilized oxygen microcapsules have been created to enhance oxygen delivery, addressing hypoxia in tumors. Brain cancer nanotheranostics have been developed using carboxymethylcellulose biofunctionalized quantum dots, and nanoparticle-mediated synergistic disruption has been explored for antineoplastic therapy. pH-sensitive and redox-responsive poly(tetraethylene glycol) nanoparticles have been used for cancer treatment, and hyperbranched and pH-responsive polymeric nanocarriers have been constructed for tumor synergistic chemotherapy.

Targeted theranostic nanovehicles have been developed to circumvent drug resistance, and framework nucleic acid-based nanosystems have been utilized for tumor theranostics, demonstrating the broad applicability of nanomaterials in cancer therapy. Nanoparticles loaded with β-Lapachone and Fe3 + also exhibit enhanced chemodynamic treatment, highlighting the potential for synergistic therapeutic effects.

Challenges and opportunities in nanoparticle delivery

While foundational in cancer nanomedicine, the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect necessitates a more nuanced understanding of tumor-specific pathophysiology and nanomedicine interactions within the heterogeneous TME. Beyond the EPR effect, tumor cell-derived small extracellular vesicles can present a significant barrier to effective therapeutic nanoparticle delivery. This highlights the critical need for strategies that mitigate sEV interference to improve nanoparticle-based tumor therapies. In parallel, biomimetic approaches are advancing, exemplified by cancer cell membrane-liposome hybrid nanoparticles that enhance tumor accumulation and therapeutic efficacy through tumor microenvironment-activated charge reversal and controlled drug release, demonstrating the potential of leveraging natural cell membrane properties.

Targeted nanomedicine for TME modulation

A core focus in nanomedicine is the modulation of the TME to enhance therapeutic efficacy. Strategies to remodel the immunosuppressive TME are particularly promising, as evidenced by paclitaxel-loaded ginsenoside Rg3 liposomes that simultaneously target tumor cells and remodel the TME, improving antitumor efficacy through macrophage repolarization. Similarly, TME-responsive nanocapsules delivering CRISPR/Cas9 effectively inhibit hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) progression and promote immunotherapy, while folate-modified nanocarriers co-delivering IL-12 gene and PD-L1 inhibitors enhance ovarian cancer immunotherapy. In addition, targeting specific TME components, such as cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs), offers another avenue, with CAF-specific nanoparticles transforming CAFs into drug-delivery reservoirs in pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma. Moreover, nanomedicines targeting tumor-associated platelets and pancreatic stellate cells remodel the TME. Stimuli-responsive nanocarriers are also gaining traction, exemplified by hypoxia-responsive nanodrug delivery systems enhancing ferroptosis in liver cancer cells, pH-responsive nano-drug delivery systems enhancing tumor penetration, stimuli-responsive polypeptide nanoparticles achieving targeted delivery, pH/redox-responsive size-switchable nanovehicles enabling targeted drug release, GSH/pH dual response systems enhancing gene-immunotherapy, sequential responsive nano-PROTACs improving PROTAC delivery, and TME-activatable nanoplatforms enabling synergistic colorectal cancer treatment.

Targeting strategies for enhanced nanoparticle delivery

Achieving effective therapeutic outcomes with nanoparticles in cancer therapy hinges critically on their ability to preferentially accumulate at the tumor site and gain entry into target cells while minimizing exposure to healthy tissues. This challenge has spurred the development of sophisticated targeting strategies, broadly categorized into passive, active, and stimuli-responsive approaches. Each strategy leverages distinct biological or physicochemical principles to guide nanoparticles toward their intended destination, thereby enhancing therapeutic efficacy and mitigating off-target toxicity.

Passive targeting

The most fundamental targeting strategy, passive targeting, capitalizes on the unique pathophysiology of the tumor microenvironment. Rapidly growing tumors often exhibit aberrant angiogenesis, resulting in a chaotic and “leaky” vasculature with gaps typically ranging from tens to hundreds of nanometers. Simultaneously, these tumors frequently have compromised lymphatic drainage. This combination allows nanoparticles of an appropriate size (generally 20–200 nm) to readily extravasate from the bloodstream into the tumor interstitium and become trapped, a phenomenon known as the Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) effect. The EPR effect provides a foundational mechanism for nanoparticle accumulation, particularly for solid tumors, and has been a cornerstone in the design of many clinically approved nanomedicines. While straightforward, the extent of the EPR effect can vary significantly between different tumor types and even within different regions of the same tumor, influenced by factors like tumor size, location, and stromal density.

Active targeting

To overcome the limitations of passive targeting and achieve greater specificity, active targeting strategies employ ligands conjugated to the nanoparticle surface. These ligands are designed to bind specifically to receptors or antigens that are overexpressed on the surface of cancer cells, tumor vasculature, or other components of the tumor microenvironment. Common ligands include antibodies or antibody fragments, peptides, aptamers, small molecules (e.g., folate, transferrin), and carbohydrates. Upon binding to their cognate receptors, actively targeted nanoparticles can exhibit enhanced cellular uptake via receptor-mediated endocytosis, leading to higher intracellular drug concentrations. This approach aims to increase the tumor-to-normal tissue ratio of the therapeutic agent, potentially improving efficacy and reducing systemic side effects. The success of active targeting relies heavily on the careful selection of appropriate targets and ligands, ensuring high specificity and affinity while avoiding rapid clearance by the immune system or non-target tissues.

Stimuli-responsive targeting

An increasingly sophisticated approach involves designing nanoparticles that respond to specific stimuli within the TME or external cues. This stimuli-responsive, or"smart,"targeting allows for controlled activation, accumulation, or drug release precisely at the disease site. TME-responsive nanoparticles can be designed to react to local changes in pH (e.g., acidic TME), enzyme activity (e.g., overexpression of matrix metalloproteinases), redox potential (e.g., higher glutathione levels), or hypoxia. For example, pH-sensitive nanoparticles might undergo a conformational change or disassemble in the acidic TME, triggering drug release or enhancing cellular uptake. External stimuli, such as light, heat, or magnetic fields, can also be utilized to remotely trigger nanoparticle activation or drug release at the tumor site, offering precise temporal and spatial control This dynamic targeting strategy adds another layer of specificity, potentially overcoming biological barriers and ensuring that the therapeutic payload is delivered and released most effectively within the harsh and heterogeneous tumor environment. Combining stimuli-responsive features with passive or active targeting strategies represents a powerful avenue for developing next-generation precision nanomedicines.

Influence of nanoparticle physicochemical properties on therapeutic efficacy

Designing effective nanoparticles for cancer therapy is a complex undertaking. There is a critical interplay between the physicochemical properties of these tiny carriers and their ultimate therapeutic success. Factors like particle size, shape, surface charge, and overall surface chemistry (including hydrophilicity) are not merely technical specifications; these factors are fundamental determinants of how nanoparticles interact with the biological environment, where they ultimately end up (biodistribution), how effectively they accumulate in tumors, their ability to enter cancer cells, and the rate at which they release their therapeutic cargo. Ultimately, mastering these properties is key to unlock enhanced therapeutic outcomes. These characteristics influence how the body processes the nanoparticles (pharmacokinetics) as well as how the nanoparticles navigate the tumor's complex microenvironment to ensure that the therapeutic payload reaches its target effectively.

The criticality of particle size

When considering nanoparticle behavior in vivo, particle size is an important property. For strategies relying on passive accumulation in tumors via the well-known Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) effect, there is generally an optimal particle size with nanoparticles typically ranging from 20 to 200 nm being favored. This specific size range facilitates extravasation through the unusually leaky blood vessels characteristic of tumors and enhances retention within the tumor interstitium due to often-impaired lymphatic drainage in these tissues. Larger or overly rigid nanoparticles can have difficulty navigating and penetrating dense extracellular matrix that often surrounds tumor cells. To address this barrier, innovative strategies, such as the development of size-shrinkable nanoparticles, are being investigated to improve distribution deep within the tumor mass. Beyond tumor targeting, size also impacts interactions with the immune system and the reticuloendothelial system (RES). In general, nanoparticles smaller than 10 nm tend to be cleared rapidly by the kidneys, while larger particles can be more susceptible to uptake by macrophages, unless their surfaces are modified. Of note, dynamic size changes are also being examined; size-switchable systems, such as certain PAMAM dendrimers engineered for deep tumor co-delivery, illustrate how precisely controlling size transitions can dramatically enhance therapeutic efficacy.

The subtle influence of particle shape

Beyond size, particle shape is another layer of complexity that dictates biodistribution and cellular interactions. While the familiar sphere remains a common form factor for many nanocarriers (e.g. liposomes), a growing body of research has begun to examine the potential of anisotropic shapes – rods, cubes, disks, and so forth. These varied geometries can influence how nanoparticles flow within blood vessels (margination), the specific mechanisms by which cells internalize them, and even their navigation through tissues. Different cell types may exhibit preferences for particles of particular shapes. The very concept of"morphological transformations of nanocarriers"being capable of boosting tumor retention strongly underscores the significant impact that shape, even dynamic changes in shape in situ, can have. Moreover, biomimetic approaches that leverage the shapes of natural cells, such as platelet-mimicking nanoparticles, offer exciting possibilities to influence both targeted delivery and evasion of immune surveillance. These innovative designs represent a compelling avenue to optimize therapeutic delivery while simultaneously minimizing unwanted immune recognition and clearance, thereby directly contributing to improved treatment outcomes in the landscape of cancer therapy.

The pivotal role of surface charge

Surface charge is another critical determinant of nanoparticle behavior, impacting stability in physiological fluids, tendency to adsorb proteins (a process known as opsonization), and interactions with cell membranes. Cell membranes are inherently negatively charged, meaning highly positive nanoparticle surfaces can engage in strong electrostatic interactions, potentially boosting cellular uptake. This positive charge often, however, comes at a cost frequently leading to rapid adsorption of serum proteins, swift clearance by the RES, and increased non-specific toxicity. Conversely, negatively charged or neutral nanoparticles typically exhibit longer circulation half-lives due to reduced protein binding and lower RES uptake but may have lower cellular internalization efficiency. This dilemma represents a challenge, which has been addressed with promising strategies utilizing stimuli-responsive systems, such as pH-responsive charge-reversal nanoparticles. These smart systems are engineered to maintain a neutral or slightly negative charge while circulating in the bloodstream – a"stealthy"state that prolongs their journey and minimizes unwanted interactions. However, upon encountering the acidic environment characteristic of the tumor microenvironment, the particles undergo a pivotal charge transition, becoming positive. This localized positive charge can enhance their uptake by tumor cells and facilitate intracellular drug delivery precisely where needed.

The art of hydrophilicity and surface chemistry

The delicate balance of surface chemistry and the degree of hydrophilicity or hydrophobicity are can dictate nanoparticle"stealth"properties including how long the particles circulate in the bloodstream, as well as their complex interactions with biological different components. Modifying the nanoparticle surface with hydrophilic polymers, most notably through the widely adopted strategy of PEGylation, is a classic technique to impart stealth. PEGylation effectively reduces the adsorption of serum proteins (opsonization) and subsequently minimizes non-specific cellular uptake by phagocytic cells, thus extending circulation time and increasing tumor accumulation via the EPR effect. Surfaces designed with inherent antifouling properties, as discussed with certain dendrimer-CuS nanohybrids, are crucial to prevent unwanted non-specific binding. Furthermore, cutting-edge biomimetic surface modifications, which involve coating nanoparticles with membranes derived from natural cells like cancer cells or immune cells, offer a sophisticated approach. These coatings impart surface properties that can facilitate evasion of the immune system, promote targeted delivery through mimicking cellular interactions, and enhance beneficial interactions with the tumor microenvironment itself. The intricate interplay between surface chemistry and overall hydrophilicity serves not only as a means to evade the immune system; it is also critical to achieve stable encapsulation of therapeutic cargos and control the release kinetics.

By strategically engineering these critical physicochemical properties – from the subtle influence of size and shape to the dramatic impact of surface charge and chemistry – researchers may be able to overcome the biological barriers to enhance the efficacy of nanoparticles. Future design process should seek to enable tailored delivery to the tumor site, facilitate efficient cellular internalization, and ensure the controlled release of therapeutic payloads, collectively elevating both the efficacy and safety profiles of next-generation cancer immunotherapy and targeted drug delivery strategies.

Advancements in nanotherapeutic strategies

Nanotherapeutic strategies are evolving rapidly, with significant advancements in nano-immunotherapy and vaccine development. Intrapleural nano-immunotherapy with liposomal nanoparticles enhances anti-PD-L1 therapy for malignant pleural effusion, while nanoparticle vaccines co-deliver peptide neoantigens and adjuvants, enhancing T cell responses and improving immune checkpoint blockade efficacy (Fig. 3). Additionally, sono-immunotherapy-mediated controllable composite nanofluorescent probes reprogram the immune microenvironment and ultrasound-responsive nanocarriers reprogram macrophages for enhanced NSCLC immunotherapy. Combination therapies and enhanced drug delivery are also key areas of progress. For example, dual-targeted, multi-responsive nanoplatforms overcome drug resistance in glioblastoma, while the combination of GRP78-targeted and non-targeted nanoparticles enhances ovarian cancer treatment. Moreover, cisplatin-loaded mesoporous polydopamine nanoparticles provide synergistic antitumor therapy, and enzyme-responsive nano-drug delivery systems enhance tumor targeting.

Furthermore, tumor-activatable nanoparticles enhance drug delivery, photothermal Fe3O4 nanoparticles induce ferroptosis, multi-responsive systems combine photothermal/chemotherapy, size-converted nanocarriers enhance tumor suppression, dehydrocurvularin-loaded nanoparticles enhance breast cancer therapy, and flash drug release from nanoparticles facilitates tumor treatment. Interestingly, other advanced strategies include quercetin-ferrum nanoparticles for photothermal therapy, ROS-augmented nanoplatforms for chemo-sonodynamic therapy, carbon monoxide-driven nano-herb delivery systems, simvastatin nano-delivery for TME remodeling, integrin receptor-targeted nanoparticles for glioblastoma, carbon nanotube cups for targeted delivery, double imprinted nanoparticles for sequential delivery, nano herb delivery systems for cancer stem cells, tumor-on-a-chip models for drug evaluation, hydrogel microtumor arrays for nanotherapeutics, self-disguised nanospy for improved delivery, polymeric nanocarriers mediating cell death, and TMTP1-modified nanoparticles for co-delivery.

Additionally, biomimetic nanoplatforms, which leverage natural cell membrane properties, are increasingly recognized for their potential to improve drug delivery and immune modulation. For instance, Zhang, Gong employed TME-activated cancer cell membrane-liposome hybrid nanoparticles for synergistic metabolic therapy and chemotherapy in non-small cell lung cancer and demonstrated enhanced drug delivery and therapeutic efficacy. These approaches highlight the potential of biomimetic nanotechnology to leverage natural cell membrane properties for improved drug delivery and immune modulation.

Current status and challenges in clinical translation

Nanomedicine represents a novel approach in cancer treatment; in turn, an up-to-date review of the current status and inherent clinical translation challenges associated with nanomedicine is important. Nanoparticle-based drug delivery systems, with their capacity for precise targeting and controlled release, offer the potential to enhance therapeutic efficacy while mitigating systemic toxicity. Indeed, several nanomedicine formulations, such as liposomal doxorubicin and albumin nanoparticles have already had clinical success, demonstrating improved outcomes across various cancer types and underscoring the promise of nanomedicine to enhance cancer treatment effectiveness. Moreover, the breadth of ongoing clinical trials including nanomedicine, spanning from lung cancer to advanced theranostics, signals an emerging field that has the potential to be realized in a clinical setting to improve patient outcomes.

Nevertheless, the path from preclinical promise to widespread clinical application is often complex. One significant hurdle lies in scaling nanomedicine production to meet clinical demands while ensuring consistent quality and batch-to-batch reproducibility – a critical factor. Furthermore, the regulatory landscape related to nanomedicines can be complicated, requiring careful consideration of their unique properties and potential long-term effects. Long-term safety and immunogenicity are critical concerns, demanding rigorous and extensive clinical investigations. The cost-effectiveness of nanomedicine formulations is also important to guarantee accessibility to a broad spectrum of patients, a factor that influences both development and adoption.

Beyond these manufacturing and regulatory considerations, the TME itself presents formidable biological barriers. The TME's inherent heterogeneity, often characterized by a dense extracellular matrix and immunosuppressive conditions, can impede nanomedicine delivery and, consequently, limit therapeutic efficacy. TME-responsive targeted drug delivery

The TME is a complex and dynamic ecosystem that plays a pivotal role in cancer progression, metastasis, and therapeutic resistance. It comprises a heterogeneous mix of stromal cells, immune cells, extracellular matrix (ECM) components, and signaling molecules, all interacting to create a unique pathological environment. This environment is characterized by hypoxia, acidic pH, elevated levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS), and overexpressed enzymes, which pose significant challenges to effective drug delivery. However, these same features also provide opportunities for developing TME-responsive targeted drug delivery systems. In this context, researchers have developed new nanoplatforms that utilize the distinct biochemical and physiological characteristics of the TME to surmount biological barriers and improve therapeutic efficacy. These advancements can potentially revolutionize cancer treatment, allowing for more precise targeting of tumor cells while minimizing damage to surrounding healthy tissues.

TME-responsive design: exploiting the tumor microenvironment

TME-responsive drug delivery systems are designed to exploit the distinct features of the TME to achieve targeted and controlled release of therapeutic agents. For instance, the acidic pH of the TME (pH 6.0–7.0) compared with normal tissues (pH 7.4) has been utilized to develop pH-sensitive nanoparticles. These nanoparticles undergo structural changes or release their payload in response to the acidic conditions, ensuring site-specific drug delivery. Examples include pH-sensitive polymeric micelles and nanoparticles with acid-labile linkages, such as hydrazone or imine bonds, which hydrolyze in the acidic TME to release drugs like doxorubicin. While TME-responsive targeted drug delivery systems present innovative approaches to enhance cancer treatment, there are significant concerns regarding their efficacy and safety. The TME, while unique, is also highly variable between different tumors and even within the same tumor over time. This heterogeneity can lead to inconsistent responses to TME-responsive therapies, making it challenging to predict their effectiveness across diverse patient populations. Moreover, the reliance on specific features of the TME, such as acidic pH or hypoxia, raises questions about the potential for unintended consequences. For instance, the acidic conditions targeted by pH-sensitive nanoparticles may not be uniformly present throughout the tumor, resulting in suboptimal drug release in some areas while potentially causing toxicity in others.

Similarly, hypoxia-responsive systems may not adequately address the complexities of tumor biology, where regions of varying oxygenation exist, potentially leading to ineffective treatment in well-oxygenated areas. Furthermore, there is a risk that focusing on TME-responsive designs may distract from more traditional and proven therapeutic strategies. Although innovation is vital, it is imperative to ensure that novel methodologies do not eclipse established therapies that have proven efficacy and safety over time. Whereas TME-responsive drug delivery systems present promising opportunities, their practical implementation may encounter considerable hurdles that require thorough evaluation.

Hypoxia, another hallmark of the TME, has been targeted using hypoxia-responsive nanoparticles. These systems often incorporate nitroimidazole derivatives or hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF)–responsive elements, which activate under low oxygen conditions to release therapeutic agents. For example, hypoxia-activated prodrugs and nanoparticles can enhance the efficacy of chemotherapy and radiotherapy by targeting hypoxic regions within tumors.

Enzyme-responsive nanoparticles represent another innovative approach. The TME is rich in overexpressed enzymes such as matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) and phospholipase A2, which can be exploited to trigger drug release. Nanoparticles designed with enzyme-cleavable linkers or coatings can selectively release their cargo in the presence of these enzymes, improving tumor specificity and reducing off-target effects. This tailored administration improves the therapeutic index of anticancer drugs and reduces systemic toxicity, increasing therapy tolerability for patients while optimizing efficacy against malignant cells. Integrating such advanced delivery systems into clinical practice could significantly improve patient outcomes, paving the way for more personalized and effective cancer therapies. As research progresses, integrating targeted delivery systems with other therapeutic modalities, such as immunotherapy and gene therapy, may yield innovative treatment strategies that enhance antitumor efficacy and surmount resistance mechanisms in cancer cells. Moreover, this multifaceted approach holds promise for revolutionizing cancer treatment, as it allows for a more precise attack on tumors while preserving healthy tissues, ultimately leading to improved quality of life for patients undergoing therapy.

Overcoming biological barriers in the TME

Despite the promise of TME-responsive designs, several biological barriers hinder effective drug delivery, including anomalous vasculature, dense ECM, interstitial fluid pressure, and immunosuppressive conditions. To address these challenges, researchers have developed multifunctional nanoplatforms that combine TME-responsive properties with strategies to overcome these barriers.

One significant barrier is the dense ECM that limits nanoparticle penetration and diffusion within the tumor. To enhance penetration, size-shrinkable nanoparticles have been developed. These nanoparticles are initially large enough to exploit the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect but shrink in response to TME stimuli (e.g., pH or enzymes) to facilitate deeper tumor penetration. This novel methodology enhances drug delivery efficacy while reducing systemic side effects, resulting in improved patient treatment outcomes.

Another difficulty is the immunosuppressive TME, which impedes immune cell function and restricts the effectiveness of immunotherapies. Nanoparticles have been designed to reprogram the TME by targeting TAMs or cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs). For example, iron oxide nanoparticles have been shown to repolarize TAMs from the pro-tumor M2 phenotype to the anti-tumor M1 phenotype, enhancing immune responses and sensitizing tumors to immunotherapy. This strategy boosts the efficacy of existing treatments and paves the way for novel combination therapies that can overcome resistance mechanisms inherent in tumor biology. By integrating these innovative approaches, researchers hope they can create more effective treatment regimens that attack the tumor directly and empower the immune system to recognize and eliminate cancer cells more efficiently.

Interstitial fluid pressure (IFP) and abnormal vasculature also impede drug delivery. To address this, vascular normalization strategies have been employed using nanoparticles that deliver anti-angiogenic agents, including VEGF inhibitors, to restore normal blood flow and reduce IFP. This method optimizes medication delivery and augments the effectiveness of combination therapies, including chemotherapy and immunotherapy, ultimately leading to better patient outcomes and increased survival rates. Researchers seek to enhance the TME to surmount obstacles that have traditionally constrained the efficacy of cancer therapies, hence facilitating more personalized and focused treatment approaches.

The creation of TME-responsive targeted drug delivery systems signifies a revolutionary method in cancer treatment, tackling the specific obstacles presented by the TME while utilizing its unique biochemical and physiological traits. Researchers are making significant strides in enhancing the specificity and efficacy of drug delivery to tumor sites by employing innovative strategies such as pH-sensitive nanoparticles, hypoxia-responsive systems, and enzyme-cleavable linkers. However, the inherent heterogeneity of the TME presents considerable challenges that necessitate a cautious and comprehensive understanding of these systems'efficacy and safety. Personalized nanomedicine and targeting cancer stem cells

Cancer stem cells (CSCs) play a pivotal role in therapeutic resistance and metastasis across multiple malignancies, necessitating innovative strategies for their effective targeting (Fig. 5). Recent research has provided insights into CSC vulnerabilities, highlighting distinct mechanisms such as metabolic reprogramming, immune evasion, and niche adaptation that sustain their survival and tumorigenic potential.

Single-cell transcriptomic analysis in colorectal cancer identified a stem-like cell cluster with organ-specific metastatic preferences mediated through DLL4-NOTCH signaling and metabolic shifts. In lung cancer, targeting carnitine palmitoyl transferase 1 A (CPT1 A) induced ferroptosis in CSCs enhances immunotherapy efficacy by disrupting a CPT1 A/c-Myc positive feedback loop that protects against oxidative stress. Similarly, acute myeloid leukemia (AML) stem cells rely on ferritinophagy, mediated by NCOA4, to sustain quiescence, making iron metabolism an exploitable vulnerability.

In glioblastoma, CSCs exhibit unique metabolic dependencies, such as threonine metabolism fueling tumor growth through YRDC-mediated translational reprogramming, suggesting a potential avenue for dietary interventions. Additionally, glioblastoma stem cells integrate into neural circuits, where cholinergic neurons drive tumor invasion, and radiotherapy further enhances neuron-tumor connectivity, implicating neuronal activity inhibition as a therapeutic strategy.

Long non-coding RNA (LINC00115) promotes chemoresistant breast CSC stemness and metastasis via SETDB1/PLK3/HIF1α signaling, presenting a novel target for intervention. In pancreatic cancer, cancer-associated fibroblast (CAF) autophagy upregulates CD274/PDL1 expression, impairing antitumor immunity and suggesting that CAF autophagy inhibition with immunotherapy could improve treatment responses. Finally, WNT5B has been identified as a driver of osteosarcoma stemness, chemoresistance, and metastasis, emphasizing the need for novel CSC-targeting therapies.

Similarly, exosome-sheathed porous silica nanoparticles co-delivering 3,3'-diindolylmethane and doxorubicin have shown promise in attenuating epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) in TNBC, reducing CSC-driven malignancies.

Glutaminolysis inhibition has emerged as a viable method to enhance photodynamic therapy (PDT) efficacy against CSCs by disrupting reactive oxygen species (ROS) defense, as demonstrated by nanoparticle-assisted combination therapies. Furthermore, HYL001, a novel lonidamine derivative, selectively targets CSC mitochondria by blocking glutamine metabolism, exhibiting substantial antitumor activity.

Several natural compounds and biomimetic approaches have been explored for CSC-targeted therapies. Viridiflorol, a bioactive terpenoid, has demonstrated potent anti-neoplastic effects by inducing apoptosis in breast, lung, and brain cancer cells. Additionally, nano-immuno-conjugates combining photosensitizers, gold nanoparticles, and anti-CD271 antibodies have been developed to enhance PDT against esophageal cancer stem cells, offering a promising avenue for precision medicine.

Gene therapy based on nanoparticles (siRNA, miRNA, CRISPR)

Gene therapy has enormous promise for treating cancer because it precisely modifies cellular functions using nucleic acids. However, efficiently delivering gene-editing tools like CRISPR/Cas to solid tumors remains a significant challenge. Researchers have looked into novel approaches to get around this, such as altering the TME and creating non-viral nanoparticle delivery systems.

Targeting the mechanics of tumor tissue to increase the effectiveness of gene editing is one promising strategy. A multiplexed dendrimer lipid nanoparticle (LNP) system that co-delivers focal adhesion kinase (FAK) siRNA, Cas9 mRNA, and sgRNA (siFAK + CRISPR-LNPs). This system significantly improved gene editing in tumor spheroids by reducing extracellular matrix (ECM) stiffness, which enhanced nanoparticle uptake and penetration. The knockdown of FAK led to a more than tenfold increase in gene editing efficiency. Additionally, using siFAK + CRISPR-PD-L1-LNPs disrupted PD-L1 expression, significantly inhibiting tumor growth and metastasis in mouse models. These findings highlight the potential of modulating tumor mechanics to improve gene editing and offer a synergistic approach for nanoparticle-based therapies.

Another critical strategy involves the development of stimuli-responsive non-viral nanoparticles for gene delivery. Reichel and Traeger (2023) emphasized the challenges of delivering nucleic acids, such as their high molar mass and negative charge. The success of mRNA-based vaccines has spurred the development of advanced gene transporters capable of providing various nucleic acids, including DNA (pDNA) and RNA (mRNA, siRNA, miRNA, gRNA, or ASOs). These stimuli-responsive nanoparticles enable precise gene release and expression control, enhancing therapeutic outcomes.

In sum, by combining mechanical modulation of the TME with advanced non-viral nanoparticle delivery systems, researchers are making significant progress in enhancing gene therapy for cancer. These approaches provide new opportunities for precise gene editing and targeted delivery, ultimately improving the potential of gene therapy as a transformative tool in cancer treatment.

Nanomaterial-based photodynamic and photothermal therapy

Photodynamic therapy (PDT) and photothermal therapy (PTT), two promising non-invasive cancer treatment modalities, are frequently combined to increase therapeutic efficacy. However, issues like hypoxia, drug resistance, and limited tumor targeting make the creation of advanced nanomedicines necessary. Recent research has concentrated on creating multifunctional nanoparticles that can synergistically deliver PDT and PTT to improve diagnosis and treatment monitoring. One strategy involves utilizing biomimetic nanoparticles, such as platelet membrane-camouflaged magnetic nanoparticles (Fe3O4-SAS@PLT), which trigger ferroptosis and generate mild immunogenicity, enhancing immunotherapy response in non-inflamed tumors. These nanoparticles repolarize macrophages and improve the efficacy of immune checkpoint blockade therapy (Fig. 7).

Researchers have developed various nanoplatforms to overcome the limitations of phototherapy, particularly hypoxia and thermotolerance. Lonidamine-encapsulated liposomes, for example, have been shown to enhance PDT and PTT of hepatocellular carcinoma by inhibiting glycolysis, thereby reducing oxygen consumption and heat shock protein production. Similarly, BODIPY-conjugated hyaluronic acid nanoparticles (BODIPY-HA NPs) have been designed for combined PDT and PTT, utilizing self-assembly to control ROS generation and photothermal effects.

Upconverting nanoparticles (UCNPs) have also been explored for synergistic PDT and PTT under cold near-infrared (NIR) excitation, addressing the overheating issues associated with conventional UCNPs. By utilizing Nd3 + -sensitized UCNPs, researchers have achieved enhanced ROS generation and improved cancer cell inhibition.

Furthermore, facet engineering of nanomaterials, such as Cu7S4 nanosheets, has demonstrated promise in efficient photothermal and photodynamic synergistic antibacterial therapy, demonstrating the versatility of these approaches beyond cancer treatment.

Advanced nanomedicines, such as aggregation-induced emission (AIE) photosensitizer nanoparticles (NP-DBD), have been developed for targeted tumor therapy and NIR-II fluorescence imaging, enabling bioorthogonal-guided activation of the cGAS-STING pathway and robust immune modulation.

Combination therapy has been further enhanced by integrating radiotherapy with PDT and PTT. For instance, copolymer-coated liquid metal nanoparticles (RGD-PEG-PAA-MN@LM) have been designed for synergistic photothermal, photodynamic, and radiation therapy of liver cancer, demonstrating improved tumor inhibition.

To address the challenges of glioblastoma treatment, researchers have developed IR820-conjugated reduced graphene oxide quantum dots (rGOQD) that alleviate tumor hypoxia and inhibit heat shock proteins, enhancing the efficacy of PTT and PDT.

Multisubcellular organelle-targeting nanoparticles, such as FA-BSA/Cu/NQ/IR780/DOX NPs, have been constructed for synergistic chemotherapy and photodynamic/photothermal tumor therapy, demonstrating multifunctional properties, including fluorescence imaging capabilities.

Polydopamine-based nanoparticles (PDA-ZnPc + Nps) loaded with photosensitizers have also been investigated for synergistic PDT and PTT, demonstrating remarkable efficacy and minimal systemic damage.

Plasmonic Bi nanoparticles encapsulated by N-carbon (Bi@N-Carbon@PEG-DOX) have been developed for dual-imaging and photothermal/photodynamic/chemotherapy, integrating imaging and synergistic therapy in one nanoplatform.

Tyrosinase-activated nanocomposites (IOBOH@BSA) have been developed for double-modals imaging-guided photodynamic and photothermal synergistic therapy of melanoma, utilizing tyrosinase as a biomarker for selective imaging and ablation.

Finally, near-infrared and lysosome-targeted BODIPY photosensitizers have been designed for photodynamic and photothermal synergistic therapy, demonstrating enhanced therapeutic effects.

These studies underscore the significance of multifunctional nanomedicines and synergistic therapeutic approaches, as well as the variety and inventive approaches being investigated to improve PDT and PTT for cancer treatment.

Theranostic nanoparticles: combining therapeutics and diagnostics

In cancer research, the creation of theranostic nanoparticles with combined therapeutic and diagnostic properties has accelerated significantly. Because this approach allows for real-time drug delivery and therapeutic response monitoring, these nanoplatforms hold promise for personalized medicine. Biomimetic approaches utilizing cell membrane coatings, enhance biocompatibility and prolong circulation time, improving targeted drug delivery. For instance, red blood cell membrane-coated nanoparticles (TT-RBC-NPs) have promise in breast cancer treatment, demonstrating enhanced cytotoxic efficacy and specific binding to cancer cells.

The enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect has been a guiding principle in nanomedicine development; however, recent studies have emphasized the need to go beyond the EPR effect to improve tumor targeting and treatment efficacy. Strategies such as active targeting using specific ligands and tuning nanoparticle properties for optimal tumor accumulation are being explored.

Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is crucial in theranostics, enabling non-invasive visualization of nanoparticle distribution and therapeutic effects. Ultrasmall theranostic nanoparticles have been developed to enhance MRI contrast and amplify radiation dose, improving local control in radiation therapy. For example, in preclinical models, AGuIX-Bi nanoparticles, with tailored Gd/Bi ratios, have demonstrated enhanced DNA damage and tumor growth inhibition. Furthermore, iron-functionalized mesoporous polydopamine nanoparticles (MPDAFe NPs) have been shown to combine efficient drug delivery with MRI capabilities, demonstrating significant cytotoxicity toward cancer cells.

Combining MRI and computed tomography (CT), bimodal imaging offers complementary diagnostic information. Fe3O4@Au nanoparticles have been developed for bimodal CT and MRI imaging, showing significant tumor and tumor blood vessel enhancement. Additionally, fullerene nanocrystals and gold nanoparticle hybrids (FGNPs) have been investigated relative to photoacoustic imaging and photothermal therapy, demonstrating strong photoacoustic signals and photothermal heating characteristics.

Targeted theranostic nanoparticles have been designed for specific cancer types, such as triple-negative breast cancer (TNBC), utilizing ICAM1-targeting to enhance diagnostic and therapeutic efficacy. These nanoparticles, loaded with gadolinium and doxorubicin, have demonstrated promising results in cell-based and tumor model-based experiments.

Beyond cancer, theranostic nanoparticles are being explored for other diseases, such as acute ischemic stroke. pH-responsive multifunctional theranostic rapamycin-loaded nanoparticles have been designed to deliver rapamycin to ischemic brain tissues, demonstrating acid-enhanced MRI and near-infrared fluorescence imaging.

Localized delivery of theranostic nanoparticles and high-energy photons using microneedles-on-bioelectronics has been developed to overcome the limitations of conventional delivery methods, particularly for brain tumors. This approach enables precise delivery of nanoparticles and high-energy photons, enhancing photodynamic therapy and chemotherapy.

Gold-in-gold cage nanoparticles have also been investigated for photothermal ablation and photoacoustic imaging in biofilm-associated infection models, demonstrating a strong biocidal effect against bacterial biofilms. These studies highlight the diverse applications of theranostic nanoparticles in cancer and other diseases, highlighting their potential to improve diagnosis and treatment outcomes through targeted delivery, imaging, and synergistic therapeutic effects.

Exosome-based nanomedicine

As important intercellular communication mediators, exosomes have demonstrated promise as therapeutic agents for several illnesses, such as cancer, osteoarthritis (OA), and wound healing. Their capacity to transport bioactive substances like proteins and miRNAs presents great promise for tissue regeneration and targeted treatments.

Cancer therapy explores exosome-based approaches to modulate the TME and enhance antitumor responses. Ginseng-derived exosome-like nanoparticles (GENs) have demonstrated active blood–brain-barrier (BBB) penetration and TME modulation, effectively recruiting M1 macrophages and inhibiting glioma progression. Similarly, M1-like macrophage-derived exosomes (M1-Exos) have been integrated with aptamer-conjugated liposomes to create hybrid nanovesicles (M1E/AALs) for targeted TME reprogramming. These nanovesicles, loaded with PFTBA and IR780, alleviate tumor hypoxia, repolarize TAMs, and enhance photodynamic immunotherapy in triple-negative breast cancer models. Furthermore, tea leaf-derived exosome-like nanotherapeutics (TLNTs) have demonstrated promise in retarding breast tumor growth through pro-apoptosis and microbiota modulation, demonstrating the potential of plant-derived exosome-like nanoparticles for cancer treatment.

In osteoarthritis, exosome-based therapies aim to rejuvenate aging chondrocytes and promote cartilage regeneration. Umbilical cord-derived mesenchymal stem cell-derived exosomes (UCMSC-EXOs) have been engineered with chondrocyte-targeting polymers and encapsulated within hyaluronic acid microgels for sustained release, effectively repairing OA cartilage in rat models. Additionally, chondrocyte-targeted hybrid exosomes (CAP/FGF18-hyEXO) loaded with an FGF18-targeted gene-editing tool have been encapsulated in hydrogel microspheres, demonstrating synergistic promotion of cartilage regeneration and decreased inflammation. Bone marrow mesenchymal stromal cell-derived exosomes (Exo) have also been engineered with targeting peptides and encapsulated in hydrogels, enhancing targeting ability and cartilage repair in OA murine models.

Exosomes have demonstrated promise in wound healing and tissue regeneration. Umbilical cord mesenchymal stem cell-derived exosomes (hUCMSC-exos) combined with Pluronic F-127 hydrogel have significantly accelerated wound closure and enhanced skin regeneration in diabetic rat models. Dental pulp stem cell-derived exosomes (DPSC-EXO) have demonstrated anti-inflammatory and osteogenic effects, promoting the repair of experimental periodontitis in rats. Moreover, mechanical stress-induced C2 C12-derived exosomes (Exosome-MS) have shown therapeutic effects on glucocorticoid-induced osteoporosis by promoting osteogenesis through the miR-92a-3p/PTEN/AKT signaling pathway (Fig. 8).

In lung diseases, exosomal STIMATE derived from type II alveolar epithelial cells can control the metabolic reprogramming of tissue-resident alveolar macrophages, suggesting potential therapeutic applications in lung injury and fibrosis.

As important intercellular communication mediators, exosomes can act as therapeutic agents for many diseases, such as cancer, osteoarthritis (OA), and wound healing. Their capacity to transport bioactive substances like proteins and miRNAs presents great promise for tissue regeneration and targeted treatments.

Clinical trials

With promising developments in drug transport, treatments, and diagnostics, nanomedicine—the use of nanotechnology in healthcare—has advanced significantly in clinical studies. Several products based on nanomedicine have already received approval for usage, and many others are currently undergoing clinical review. For example, FDA-approved liposomal formulations such as Doxil (doxorubicin) and Abraxane (paclitaxel-bound albumin nanoparticles) for the treatment of cancer have better efficacy and fewer adverse effects than traditional medicines.

Numerous uses of nanomedicine, such as targeted medication administration, imaging, and regenerative medicine, are being investigated in ongoing clinical trials. The potential of nanoparticles, including polymeric micelles, dendrimers, and gold nanoparticles, to improve drug solubility, stability, and bioavailability is being investigated. The potential of nanomedicine to treat complicated illnesses like Alzheimer's, heart conditions, and infectious infections is also being researched. Lipid nanoparticles, for instance, are being used in mRNA-based COVID-19 vaccines, demonstrating the adaptability of nanomedicine in tackling global health issues.

Notwithstanding these developments, there are still issues, including scaling, regulatory barriers, and long-term safety worries. These problems are being addressed by continued research and cooperation between industry, academia, and regulatory agencies, opening the door for wider clinical usage. Clinical trials of nanomedicine appear to have a bright future ahead of them, with the potential to transform customized medicine and enhance patient outcomes everywhere. Conclusion

The transformative potential of nanoparticle-mediated cancer immunotherapy is tempered by several critical knowledge gaps that must guide future research, including heterogeneous patient responses requiring predictive biomarkers, limited understanding of nanocarrier-induced long-term immune memory, and scalability challenges in clinical translation. To advance the field, future work should prioritize engineering adaptable nanoplatforms to overcome dynamic immune evasion, integrating AI-driven design with high-throughput screening for personalized therapies, and developing standardized preclinical models that better mimic human immune-tumor interactions. By addressing these specific challenges through interdisciplinary collaboration, researchers can bridge the gap between promising preclinical results and robust clinical applications, ultimately realizing the full potential of nanotechnology to revolutionize cancer immunotherapy and improve patient outcomes.
