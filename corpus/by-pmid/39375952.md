# Population pharmacokinetic model of ABL001/CTX-009 (anti-VEGF/DLL4) in adult cancer patients with solid tumor.

## Abstract

ABL001/CTX-009 is a bispecific antibody targeting delta-like ligand-4 and vascular endothelial growth factor A. In this study, we developed a population pharmacokinetic (PK) model of ABL001/CTX-009 in patients with solid tumors. A total of 712 plasma concentrations from 30 patients with relapsed or refractory solid tumors were collected from a phase 1 study (NCT03292783). A population PK model was developed using a nonlinear mixed-effect method and was evaluated by graphical and numerical methods. Using the model, the steady-state concentrations were simulated to compare weight-based and fixed-dose regimens and to find optimal dosing intervals. The PK of ABL001/CTX-009 was well described by a two-compartment model with a parallel first-order and Michaelis-Menten elimination kinetics. Body weight was selected as a significant covariate on V1. Model evaluation results suggested that the model was adequate and robust with good precision. Simulations after administrations of fixed or weight-based doses showed similar plasma concentrations. Additionally, 10 mg/kg for every other week and 15 mg/kg for every three-week administration showed comparable plasma concentrations. In conclusion, the model well described the plasma concentrations of ABL001/CTX-009 in patients with solid tumors. The simulation suggested that weight-based dose and fixed dose can provide equivalent systemic exposure.

INTRODUCTION

Dysregulated angiogenesis is a one of the key processes in tumor expansion. Angiogenesis is a highly regulated process of blood vessel formation from pre‐existing blood vessels involving migration, growth, and differentiation of endothelial cells. In the case of malignancy, dysregulated angiogenesis results in the formation of thin‐wall vessels, incomplete basement membranes, and atypical pericytes. Therefore, suppressing dysregulated angiogenesis has been a validated therapeutic approach for solid tumors for several decades.

Tumor angiogenesis is induced by peptide growth factors which activate specific tyrosine kinase receptors. Among them, the vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) family is involved in the induction and progression of angiogenesis, and is an important therapeutic target. In various experimental models, VEGF inhibitors delayed tumor growth and even led to tumor regression. Several compounds have been verified as inducers of angiogenesis, and among them, the majority of factors are classical peptide growth factors that bind and activate specific tyrosine kinase receptors, with VEGFs being representative. 

However, acquired resistance to anti‐VEGF therapy has been observed and was associated with a poor outcome. Proposed mechanisms include activation and upregulation of alternative angiogenetic pathways and recruitment of bone marrow‐derived proangiogenic cells. In addition, increased tumor aggressiveness leading to metastasis was also proposed. Given the association of VEGF signaling with other pathways, such as fibroblast growth factor signaling and platelet‐derived growth factor signaling, simultaneous targeting of additional pathways could overcome resistance. 

Delta‐like ligand 4 (DLL4) is a ligand for the receptors of the Notch family, which regulates cell fate through cell–cell interactions. DLL4, acting through the Notch signaling, plays a key role in the regulation of endothelial cells and endothelial cell progenitors during tumor angiogenesis. Previous studies have shown that DLL4 is highly upregulated in tumor vasculature and blockade of the DLL4‐Notch signaling pathway reduces tumor growth in preclinical models. From this perspective, a combination therapy blocking both the DLL4 and VEGF pathways is expected to enhance antitumor efficacy. 

ABL001/CTX‐009 is a bispecific antibody that targets both DLL4 and VEGF by linking each C‐terminus of an anti‐VEGF antibody with a DLL4‐binding single chain Fv. In human gastric and colon cancer xenograft models, ABL001/CTX‐009 was more potent than the monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) solely targeting VEGF or DLL4 ligands. ABL001/CTX‐009 showed favorable tolerability in the dose range from 0.3 to 17.5 mg/kg in this first‐in‐human study in patients with advanced solid tumors. Each patient received an intravenous dose of ABL001/CTX‐009 based on body weight. ABL001/CTX‐009 was administered in a 2‐week cycle, except for the second dose, which was separated by 3 weeks. In the study, patients treated with ABL001/CTX‐009 10 or 15 mg/kg exhibited confirmed partial responses by Response Evaluation Criteria in Solid Tumor (RECIST) 1.1, suggesting anticancer effects. 

Dosing regimen should be crafted to achieve adequate drug exposure levels by taking into consideration various patient demographic, including age, weight, organ function, etc., and other clinical features. Dosing regimen for mAbs is classified into weight‐based and fixed dosing. For safety and efficacy reasons, weight‐based dosing is required for particular populations, including children, to prevent overdosing. However, weight‐based dosing is challenging in clinical practices, as the preparation of the drug requires more attention than fixed dosing. In contrast, fixed dosing has the disadvantage that more or less dose may be administered to patients but is advantageous for ease of preparation, reduced cost, and lower probability of dosing errors. However, analyses have shown that because a fixed dose can lead to higher doses administered to patients compared with a weight‐based dose, it could lead to higher costs. In general, mAbs have not shown significant differences in the systemic exposure from weight‐based and fixed dosing due to their distribution to the blood plasma and extracellular fluids only, which are known not to have proportional relationship with the body weight. 

This study performed a preliminary analysis using data from the clinical study with patients being treated at dose levels from 0.3 to 17.5 mg/kg. The aim of this study was to quantitatively describe the pharmacokinetic (PK) properties of ABL001/CTX‐009 by developing a population PK model and identify significant covariates affecting the pharmacokinetics of ABL001/CTX‐009 and thereby utilize the data for future follow‐up studies. Additionally, based on the final model, we aimed to simulate and suggest dosing regimens which could be considered in further clinical trials.

METHODS

Study subjects

Adult patients (aged ≥19 years) with confirmed of any progressive locally advanced or metastatic solid tumors that had relapsed or were refractory to the standard therapy were enrolled. Patients were required to have a life expectancy ≥12 weeks and at least one measurable lesion according to RECIST version 1.1. Patients were to have an Eastern Cooperative Oncology Group score between 0 and 2 to participate in the trial. Additionally, patients whose laboratory test results measured within 14 days before enrolling in this study were to meet the following criteria: serum creatinine ≤ 1.5 × upper limit normal (ULN), total bilirubin ≤ 1.5 × ULN, and AST and ALT ≤ 3 × ULN (≤ 5 × ULN for patients with liver metastases or hepatocellular carcinoma). Patients who had clinically significant cardiovascular disease, including pulmonary hypertension and myocardial infarction, hypersensitivity to humanized/human mAb, or who previously received anti‐DLL4 antibody were excluded. Written informed consent was obtained from all patients prior to any study procedure. This study was conducted at three different sites in the Republic of Korea (Seoul National University Hospital Bundang, Samsung Medical Center, and Asan Medical center) and was approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) of Seoul National University Hospital Bundang, the IRB of Samsung Medical Center, and the IRB of Asan Medical Center. This study was performed in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki and International Conference on Harmonization Good Clinical Practice and was registered in open registry (Clnicaltrials.gov registration no. NCT03292783, 18/09/2017).

Study design

This study was a first‐in‐human study consisting of dose escalation and expansion cohorts. In the dose escalation cohorts, the conventional 3 + 3 scheme was applied and dose levels were planned as 0.3, 1, 2.5, 5, 7.5, 10, 12.5, 15, 17.5, and 25 mg/kg. The initial dose of 0.3 mg/kg was determined based on the no observed adverse effect level (NOAEL) 10 mg/kg in the monkey model, converted to human equivalent dose with the safety factor of 10. After confirming safety in the dose escalation cohort, additional patients were recruited in 7.5, 10, 12.5, and 15 mg/kg dose groups, which were the target dose levels to show clinically meaningful efficacy based on the results of the preclinical study (data on file). Patients received an intravenous infusion of ABL001/CTX‐009 diluted in 0.9% normal saline (250 mL) over 1 h. ABL001/CTX‐009 was administered in a 2‐week cycle, with the exception of the second dose, which had a 3‐week interval. Administration continued until unacceptable toxicity or disease progression was identified according to RECIST version 1.1. Blood samples for PK analysis were collected at predose (0 h), the end of infusion, 0.5, 2, 4, 24, 48, 72, 168, and 336 h after the infusion of the first and second administration of ABL001/CTX‐009 in the dose escalation cohort. For subsequent administrations, blood samples were collected at predose timepoint.

Determination of plasma ABL001/CTX‐009 concentrations

Blood PK samples for ABL001/CTX‐009 were collected in serum‐separating tubes and centrifuged at 3000 rpm at 4°C for 10 min. After centrifugation, the plasma samples were separated and dispensed into polypropylene vials and stored at −20°C. Plasma concentrations of ABL001/CTX‐009 were analyzed using a dual antigen‐binding enzyme‐linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA, SpectraMax 190, Molecular Devices). The plasma samples were placed in the ELISA plate coated with recombinant human DLL4, and after the binding process, a horseradish peroxidase‐conjugated human anti‐VEGF A antibody was used to detect the analyte. For readout, tetramethylbenzidine was used as a substrate. The quantification range for the sample was 250–6000 ng/mL, and the lower limit of quantification was 250 ng/mL. The accuracy and precision for the quality control sample data were within −20%–20% and less than 20%, respectively. The accuracy and precision of plasma ABL001/CTX‐009 samples were within −1.60%–3.76% and less than 12.76%, respectively, indicating the analytic method was accurate and precise.

Development of the population PK model

Nonlinear mixed‐effects method was used to develop a population PK model using the NONMEM® software version 7.4.4. (ICON Development Solutions). The first‐order conditional estimation with interaction (FOCE‐I) method was used for the estimation procedure. For data process and diagnostics, R version 4.1.0., Xpose version 4, and Perl‐speaks‐NONMEM 4.9.0. were used. The concentrations below the limit of quantification observed at the timepoint before T max were treated as 0, and the concentrations below the limit of quantification values at the time point after T max were treated as missing values for analysis. One‐ or two‐compartment models with linear or/and nonlinear elimination were evaluated to determine the best structural model to describe the plasma concentration–time data of ABL001/CTX‐009. Assuming that the PK parameters show a log‐normal distribution, interindividual variability (IIV) was described using an exponential model. Residual variability was evaluated using different residual error models, including additive, proportional, and combined additive and proportional error models. Likelihood ratio test was performed to evaluate the goodness‐of‐fit (GOF) between nested models. To explore the potential influences of covariates on the PK properties of ABL001/CTX‐009, stepwise covariate selection was performed; the criteria for statistical significance were a decrease of objective function value (OFV) of 3.84 (p = 0.05) for forward selection and 6.63 (p = 0.01) for backward selection. The categorical covariate included for the analysis was dose group, and exponential function was used. Evaluation of the final population PK model

The final population PK model was evaluated by graphical and numerical methods. The GOF plots of individual‐ and population‐observed versus predicted concentrations, conditional weighted residuals (CWRES) versus population predictions, and CWRES versus time after dose were evaluated for graphical validation. Additionally, to evaluate the stability and predictive performance of the model, prediction‐corrected visual predictive check (pcVPC) was conducted with 200 simulated sample subjects. The 5th, 50th, and 95th percentiles of observed values were plotted and compared with 90% confidence intervals (CIs) of their respective simulated values. Additionally, the robustness and precision of the final model was identified by a bootstrap analysis performed with 1000 resampled datasets. The result of the bootstrap analysis was summarized as medians and 95% CIs of the parameters.

Simulations based on the final PK model

Based on the final PK model of ABL001/CTX‐009, Monte‐Carlo simulations were performed with two simulation scenarios. The first simulation aimed to compare the steady‐state plasma concentration of ABL001/CTX‐009 when administered by either fixed dose or weight‐based dose. The simulation involved 1000 virtual patients who received multiple administrations of ABL001/CTX‐009 as fixed dose of 700 mg or as weight‐based dose of 10 mg/kg. Bodyweight distribution for the virtual patients were incorporated from the real‐patient data (Figure S1). The mean ± standard deviation for weight in the real‐patient data was 65.5 ± 18.1 kg, encompassing individuals with weights ranging from approximately 35–120 kg. The second simulation was to evaluate optimal dosing intervals. The steady‐state plasma concentrations were compared when ABL001/CTX‐009 was administered by every 2 weeks (current dosing interval) or every 3 weeks (prolonged dosing interval). For the two simulation scenarios, the C max and AUC were calculated using a noncompartment analysis method to the simulated ABL001/CTX‐009 concentration data.

RESULTS

Study population characteristics

From the dose escalation cohort, a total of 712 PK samples from 31 patients were included for population PK model development (0.3 mg/kg = 4, 1 mg/kg = 4, 2.5 mg/kg = 3, 5 mg/kg = 4, 7.5 mg/kg = 3, 10 mg/kg = 3, 12 mg/kg = 3, 15 mg/kg = 4, and 17.5 mg/kg = 3). Out of a total of 712 samples, excluding the predose samples, only 6 samples (less than 1%) were below the limit of quantification and were handled as missing data. There were no significant differences in demographics between dose groups in the dose escalation cohort (Table 1). Individual plasma concentration–time profiles are provided in Figure S2.

Final population PK model

A two‐compartment model with parallel linear and nonlinear elimination and proportional residual error model best described the plasma concentration–time profile of ABL001/CTX‐009 (Figure 1). The final model estimated clearance (CL), central volume of distribution (V1), intercompartmental clearance (Q), peripheral volume of distribution (V2), and typical value of maximum rate (V max) (Table 2). The estimation of typical parameters for fixed effect was in good precision with a relative standard error of 18% for CL, 5% for V1, 16% for Q, 27% for V2, and 26% for V max (Table 2). IIVs on CL, V1, and V2 significantly improved the model and were estimated to be 54.3%, 15.9%, and 100.6%, respectively, and IIV for V max did not significantly improve the model and was not estimated (Table 2). The distribution plots for eta (η), a random effect among individuals, are shown in Figure S3. The GOF plots showed that the final model adequately described the observed concentrations: the predicted data for ABL001/CTX‐009 showed a reasonable correlation with the observed data, and the CWRES data indicated that there was no systemic bias according to concentrations and time (Figure S4). The pcVPC plot showed that most of the 5th, 50th, and 95th percentiles of the observed data were within the corresponding simulated data, showing that the final model had a reasonable predictability (Figure 2). The median and 95% CIs of the final parameter estimates from bootstrap analysis with 1000 resampled datasets showed similar values to estimates from the final model, indicating that the model was constructed precisely with good robustness (Table 2).

Simulation results

The trough concentration and the average concentration were comparable between a fixed dosing strategy and weight‐based dosing strategy (Table 3, Figures 3, 4). The concentration–time profiles of ABL001/CTX‐009 after two dosing strategies were also comparable showing a similar degree of fluctuation with the C average value of 123.9 and 111.3 mg/L for fixed dosing and weight‐based dosing, respectively (Figures 3 and 4). When comparing the optimal dosing strategy, the ABL001/CTX‐009 plasma concentrations administered every 2 weeks of 10 mg/kg and every 3 weeks of 15 mg/kg showed similar average plasma concentrations (Table 3, Figures 3 and 4).

DISCUSSION

In this study, a population PK model was developed for ABL001/CTX‐009, a bispecific antibody targeting both DLL4 and VEGF A, using concentration data obtained from a first‐in‐human study involving patients with solid tumors. The observed ABL001/CTX‐009 concentration data were adequately described by a two‐compartment model with IIV on CL, V1, and V2, with a proportional residual error model. Considering that mAbs usually show nonlinear PK properties, a Michaelis–Menten model was used. In the previous study, the Michaelis–Menten model has successfully described the PK characteristics of antibody‐based drugs. In this study, body weight was identified as the sole significant covariate on V1, explaining approximately 7% of the between‐subject variability for V1 in the final model. The model‐derived elimination half‐life was approximately 5.7 days, indicating that plasma ABL001/CTX‐009 concentrations would reach a steady‐state in patients with solid tumor in about 4–6 weeks.

Simulation results comparing fixed dosing (700 mg) and weight‐base dosing (10 mg/kg) showed comparable PK properties of ABL001/CTX‐009, supporting the suitability of a non‐weight‐based dosing of ABL001/CTX‐009. This approach may facilitate dosage preparation and reduce the likelihood of dosing error in clinical settings. Since the clinical relevance of equivalent AUCs has not been proven, further study is needed to confirm the adequate fixed dose amount of ABL001/CTX‐009. Considering recent findings of sporadic studies which showed that fixed dosing had more advantages than weight‐based dosing in several cases, it would be advantageous if ABL001/CTX‐009 could be administered as a fixed dose. Furthermore, the simulation results comparing 10 mg/kg administered every other week and 15 mg/kg administered every 3 weeks indicated comparable average plasma concentration levels at steady state. Considering that many previous studies have showed a significant exposure–efficacy relationship between average concentration and efficacy outcomes of antibody drugs, two dosing regimens could result in comparable efficacy. Based on the simulation results, ABL001/CTX‐009 can be administered as a fixed dose and with a longer dosing interval for follow‐up studies.

Considering the high IIVs in the final model, additional simulation using estimated individual PK parameter was performed, utilizing an empirical Bayes estimate (EBE) approach. The EBE parameters reflect a much closer approximation to the actual PK parameters. The simulation result after utilizing the estimated individual PK parameter showed comparable results to the original simulation (Figure S5).

Bispecific antibodies have gained widespread recognition within the realm of tumor therapy. In recent developments, the bispecific antibody called YM101, designed to target both the TGF‐β and PD‐L1 pathways, has demonstrated a superior antitumor efficacy in preclinical settings when compared with monotherapies. With a particular focus on those targeting VEGF, numerous bispecific antibodies designed around VEGF targets are currently in development. Notably, a prior investigation unveiled a novel bispecific antibody capable of concurrently inhibiting both the TGF‐β and VEGF signaling pathways, displaying remarkable antitumor efficacy when compared with singular treatments involving anti‐TGF‐β or anti‐VEGF therapies, as observed in murine tumor models. Furthermore, this preceding study illuminated the sustained antitumor immune response achievable when coupled with anti‐PD‐1 therapy. Building upon these pivotal findings, our current study contributes significantly by introducing alternative dosing regimens for the application of this novel bispecific antibody in cancer therapy, thus furthering the prospects of improved cancer treatment strategies. 

There are several limitations in this study. First, the effect of immunogenicity on the PK properties of ABL001/CTX‐009, including antidrug antibody, was not evaluated. Second, patients' statuses, including tumor burden, were not fully incorporated in the covariate screening process. Lastly, the number of subjects included in this study was relatively small. Since the model was developed during the clinical trial, only partial PK data from the dose escalation cohort were included in the structure model development. Refining the model by adding data from the dose expansion cohorts and from the dose escalation cohorts may enhance the model with more precise specification of the PK profiles of ABL001/CTX‐009. Additionally, considering that this drug is under development with limited data, obtaining more information about the drug, such as its concentration at the target site and the free drug concentrations, can further refine this model. This refinement would allow the application of a target‐mediated drug disposition model, leading to more accurate PK properties.

This study developed a population PK model that adequately describes the PK property of ABL001/CTX‐009 in patients with solid tumors and presented a rationale for fixed dosing instead of weight‐base dosing at different dosing intervals. Based on the simulation results from this study, different dosing regimens can be applied to patients receiving ABL001/CTX‐009 in further clinical trials.
