# Anti-tissue factor antibody conjugated with monomethyl auristatin E or deruxtecan in pancreatic cancer models.

## Abstract

Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) have been recognized as a promising class of cancer therapeutics. Tissue factor (TF), an initiator of the blood coagulation pathway, has been investigated regarding its relationship with cancer, and several preclinical and clinical studies have presented data on anti-TF ADCs, including tisotumab vedotin, which was approved in 2021. However, the feasibility of other payloads in the design of anti-TF ADCs is still unclear because no reports have compared payloads with different cytotoxic mechanisms. For ADCs targeting other antigens, such as Her2, optimizing the payload is also an important issue in order to improve in vivo efficacy. In this study, we prepared humanized anti-TF Ab (clone.1084) conjugated with monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) or deruxtecan (DXd), and evaluated the efficacy in several cell line- and patient-derived xenograft models of pancreatic cancer. As a result, optimizing the drug / Ab ratio was necessary for each payload in order to prevent pharmacokinetic deterioration and maximize delivery efficiency. In addition, MMAE-conjugated anti-TF ADC showed higher antitumor effects in tumors with strong and homogeneous TF expression, while DXd-conjugated anti-TF ADC was more effective in tumors with weak and heterogeneous TF expression. Analysis of a pancreatic cancer tissue array showed weak and heterogeneous TF expression in most TF-positive specimens, indicating that the response rate to pancreatic cancer might be higher for DXd- than MMAE-conjugated anti-TF ADC. Nevertheless, our findings indicated that optimizing the ADC payloads individually in each patient could maximize the potential of ADC therapeutics.

INTRODUCTION

Antibody–drug conjugates (ADCs) are an emerging class of antibody treatment for cancer. Antibody–drug conjugates consist of a tumor‐targeting mAb, a highly potent payload, and a linker that connects the payload to the mAb. Specific binding of the mAb to a tumor antigen contributes to the selective delivery of the payload to cancer cells. Typically, ADCs target antigens expressed on the membranes of cancer cells. After binding, the ADC–antigen complex is internalized into the targeted cells by antigen‐mediated endocytosis. The linker is selectively cleaved by lysosomal proteinases such as cathepsin B, which releases the payload and allows it to exert its cytotoxic effects against the cells. For the treatment of solid tumors, approved ADCs have been designed using two types of payloads, specifically microtubule inhibitors and topoisomerase I inhibitors. More than 50% of ADCs investigated in clinical studies have used microtubule inhibitors, such as auristatins and mertansines. However, trastuzumab deruxtecan (T‐DXd) has demonstrated clinical success in the treatment of human epidermal growth factor receptor (Her) 2‐positive breast and gastric or gastroesophageal cancers. Additionally, DXd (topoisomerase I inhibitor) has been considered as a promising payload of ADCs against solid tumors. Indeed, the clinical feasibility of DXd has been evaluated in the context of several ADCs, specifically those targeting Her3, trophoblast cell‐surface antigen (Trop) 2, and B7‐H3 (CD276). 

Tissue factor (TF) is known to be an initiation factor of the extrinsic blood coagulation pathway. The relationship between cancer and TF has been investigated for several decades, and recent reports also described the important role of TF in cancer development and metastasis. The overexpression of TF has been reported in many cancer types including pancreatic cancer, glioma, and breast cancer. The so‐called “malignant cycle of blood coagulation” is one possible reason why TF is expressed in many types of solid cancer. In the last 10 years, several preclinical studies have reported cancer therapies involving ADCs. Due to its physiological role as blood coagulation initiator, TF exhibits greater turnover higher than two other ADC targets, namely Her2 (human epidermal growth factor receptor 2) and EGFR (epidermal growth factor receptor). This should improve the suitability of TF as an ADC target since the internalization rate of the target antigen is crucial to the mechanism of ADCs designed to release payloads in the targeted cells. More recently, ADCs targeting TF have been investigated in clinical studies, and tisotumab vedotin was approved in the United States for the treatment of recurrent or metastatic cervical cancer. 

In the aforementioned reports on anti‐TF ADCs, the most commonly used payloads were microtubule inhibitors such as monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) and monomethyl auristatin F, and tisotumab vedotin also uses MMAE. No studies thus far have used other payloads, such as a topoisomerase inhibitor, in the design of anti‐TF ADCs. In the present study, we prepared two types of anti‐TF ADCs; both used the humanized 1084 mAb, with conjugation to either MMAE or DXd. We evaluated their efficacy in three different pancreatic cancer cell line‐derived xenograft (CDX) models and four different pancreatic cancer patient‐derived xenograft (PDX) models with several TF expression patterns. Our motivation in this study was to investigate the suitability of a topoisomerase inhibitor for anti‐TF ADCs to treat solid tumors.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Immunohistochemistry staining

A human pancreatic cancer tissue array was purchased from US Biomax (PA961f). Tissue sections were incubated with Dulbecco's PBS (DPBS) for 1 h at 60°C and then deparaffinized in xylene. Heat‐induced antigen retrieval was then carried out using Target Retrieval Solution (pH 6.0; Dako). To block endogenous peroxidase, tissue sections were incubated with 0.3% hydrogen peroxidase for 30 min at room temperature. After blocking with 5% skim milk in DPBS for 1 h at room temperature, tissue sections were stained with goat anti‐TF polyclonal Ab (R&D Systems) and anti‐goat Ab conjugated with HRP (MBL). The reaction was visualized using 3,3′‐diaminobenzidine (Dako), and hematoxylin was used for counter‐staining. To determine the TF expression level in each mouse model, tumors were removed from mice and embedded in OCT compound (Sakura Finetek Japan), then frozen at −80°C until use. Frozen sections were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in DPBS for 15 min at room temperature. The subsequent steps, including blocking and staining, were the same as those used in the staining of human tissue array samples. The relationship between the degree of TF expression and histological grade of pancreatic cancer according to the American Cancer Society classification was evaluated.

Cell lines

BxPC‐3, HPAF‐II, and PSN‐1 cells were obtained from ATCC. These cell lines were cultured in ATCC‐recommended medium supplemented with FBS (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and 1% penicillin–streptomycin–amphotericin B suspension (FUJIFILM Wako Pure Chemical Corporation) in a 5% CO2 atmosphere at 37°C.

Preparation of anti‐TF ADCs 

For use as linkers and payloads, maleimidocaproyl‐Val‐Cit‐PAB‐MMAE (vcMMAE) and maleimide‐Gly‐Gly‐Phe‐Gly‐DXd (deruxtecan) were purchased from MedChemExpress. These commercially available compounds were conjugated with our original humanized anti‐TF mAb (hu1084 mAb) or the humanized control mAb. Hu1084 mAb reacts with human TF antigens, but not with mouse TF antigens. The conjugation protocol was previously described. The ADCs were stored at −80°C until use in subsequent experiments.

Flow cytometry analysis

The flow cytometry (FCM) procedure was previously described. The pancreatic cancer cell lines were stained with goat anti‐human TF polyclonal Ab (R&D Systems) and rabbit anti‐goat IgG (H + L) cross‐adsorbed Ab Alexa Fluor 647 (Thermo Fisher Scientific) as the first and second Ab, respectively. Fluorescence intensity was measured using a Guava EasyCyte flow cytometer (Merck Millipore) and analyzed by FlowJo analysis software (Tree Star). We also undertook FCM analysis using hu1084 mAb (detailed information can be found in Document S1).

Cell killing assay in vitro

Pancreatic cancer cell lines HPAF‐II, BxPC‐3, or PSN‐1 were harvested on 96‐well plates (Corning) and incubated at 37°C overnight. Payloads and ADCs were applied to each well at various concentrations, and the plates were incubated at 37°C for 6 days (n = 3). Cancer cell viability was measured using CCK‐8 (Dojindo).

Cell localization imaging

Detailed information can be found in Document S1.

Single‐dose escalation study in BxPC‐3‐bearing mice

To determine a suitable drug / Ab ratio (DAR) for each anti‐TF ADC, we undertook a single‐dose escalation study in vivo. Five‐week‐old female BALB/c nude mice (The Jackson Laboratory Japan) were used in the subsequent experiments. BxPC‐3 cells as a TF‐positive cancer cell line were implanted s.c. into mice under deep anesthesia. When the BxPC‐3 tumor volume reached approximately 150 mm3, DPBS, hu1084 mAb conjugated with MMAE (hu1084‐MMAE), or hu1084 mAb conjugated with DXd (hu1084‐DXd) were given to the mice at 2, 5, or 10 mg/kg in a single dose (n = 3–4). The tumor volumes were monitored once a week in each treatment group, and were calculated by the following formula: volume = length × (width) × 1/2.

Sandwich ELISA

The protocol was previously described. Briefly, goat anti‐human IgG polyclonal Ab (0.1 μg/mL; Bethyl Laboratories) was immobilized on C8 MaxiSorp plates (Thermo Fisher Scientific). The plasma samples were obtained from mice after a single‐dose i.v. injection of hu1084 mAb or hu1084 ADC at 5 mg/kg. Goat anti‐human IgG Ab conjugated with horseradish peroxidase (Bethyl Laboratories) was used to detect hu1084 mAb and hu1084 ADCs in each mouse plasma samples. Absorbance was determined using a 1‐Step Slow TMB Solution (Thermo Fisher Scientific) and a SpectraMax190 microplate reader (Molecular Devices).

Antitumor effects in pancreatic cancer CDX models

We next evaluated antitumor effects of hu1084 ADCs in several pancreatic cancer CDX models. HPAF‐II, BxPC‐3, or PSN‐1 cells were implanted s.c. into mice under deep anesthesia. When tumor volumes reached approximately 200 mm3, mice were treated i.v. with DPBS, hu1084 mAb plus payload, control ADC, or hu1084 ADC at 5 mg/kg once a week for 3 weeks (n = 5).

Antitumor effects in pancreatic cancer PDX models

We investigated the in vivo efficacy of hu1084 ADCs using four types of pancreatic cancer PDX models. These models were prepared by s.c. implantation of tumor blocks in the flanks of mice. The initial PDX tumor blocks were obtained from the Central Institute for Experimental Animals. When tumors reached an average volume approximately 200–300 mm3, mice were injected i.v. with DPBS, control ADC, or hu1084 ADC at 5 mg/kg once a week for 3 weeks (n = 4).

Statistical analysis

Data analysis was carried out using EZR software. All data are shown as the mean ± SD. Comparison of IC50 values in vitro and analysis of pharmacokinetics at 24 h after injection was carried out by one‐way ANOVA with Tukey analysis. Repeated ANOVA was applied for analysis of in vivo efficacy in CDX and PDX models.

RESULTS

 Tissue factor expression in human pancreatic cancer tissue arrays

We undertook immunohistochemical (IHC) staining to evaluate the degree of TF expression in 48 human pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma (PDAC) tissues using a human pancreatic cancer tissue array with different pathological grades. We categorized the staining intensity of tissue samples as negative, weak, heterogeneously strong (the percentage of strongly stained tumor cells was <60%), and homogeneously strong (the percentage of strongly stained tumor cells was ≥60%). The representative stained tissues are shown in Figure 1A. In this tissue array, cancer cells were mainly stained with anti‐TF Ab. We also investigated the relationship between staining intensity and pancreatic cancer grade (Figure 1B). Among grade 1 PDAC tissues, none (0/19) were homogeneously strong, 21.1% (4/19) were heterogeneously strong, 36.8% (7/19) were weak, and 42.1% (8/19) were negative. Among grade 2 PDAC tissues, 4.3% (1/23) were homogeneously strong, 13.0% (3/23) were heterogeneously strong, 39.1% (9/23) were weak, and 43.5% (10/23) were negative. Among grade 3 PDAC tissues, 33.3% (2/6) were homogeneously strong, 16.7% (1/6) were heterogeneously strong, 33.3% (2/6) were weak, and 16.7% (1/6) were negative. Thus the degree of TF expression was correlated with PDAC grade. Among all tissues analyzed together, 6.3% (3/48) were homogeneously strong, 58.3% (26/48) were heterogeneously strong or weak, and 39.6% (19/48) were negative (Figure 1C). These data show that more than 60% of PDAC tumors were TF positive, and most TF‐positive PDAC tumors expressed TF antigen heterogeneously or weakly.

Preparation and in vitro characterization of anti‐TF ADCs 

Hu1084 mAb was conjugated with vcMMAE or DXd (Figure 2A,B). The DARs of hu1084 mAb conjugated with vcMMAE were 3.6 (hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3) and 8.0 (hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8). The DARs of hu1084 mAb conjugated with DXd were 2.8 (hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3) and 7.9 (hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8).

Next, we investigated the TF expression on the cell membrane in several human pancreatic cancer cell lines, PSN‐1, BxPC‐3, and HPAF‐II (Figure 2C). HPAF‐II cells showed slightly higher TF expression than BxPC‐3 cells, although the expression was strong in both cells. By contrast, PSN‐1 cells had moderate TF expression. Additionally, we confirmed the binding ability of hu1084 mAb to these TF‐positive cell lines (Figure S1A). To evaluate the cell‐killing abilities of hu1084 ADCs, these three pancreatic cancer cell lines were exposed to each ADC or payload for 6 days (Figure 2D–I). The IC50 values of each ADC are summarized in Table S1.

In the comparison of payload and ADC, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 showed greater cytotoxicity than MMAE against HPAF‐II cells (p < 0.0001), which had the highest TF expression, indicating that these ADCs could deliver their payload by antigen‐mediated endocytosis more efficiently than directly through the cell membrane (Figure 2D). Against BxPC‐3 cells, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 showed lower cytotoxicity than MMAE alone (p < 0.0001), but no significant difference between hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and MMAE alone (Figure 2E). Meanwhile, the cytotoxicity of hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 was less than that of MMAE alone against PSN‐1 cells (p < 0.0001 and p < 0.005, respectively), due to lower TF expression compared with HPAF‐II and BxPC‐3 cells (Figure 2F). Moreover, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 exerted clearly higher cell‐killing ability than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3, meaning that the DAR affected cytotoxicity when the amount of internalized ADCs into cells was limited due to low antigen expression.

Likewise, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3 and hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 also showed greater cytotoxicity than DXd alone against HPAF‐II (p < 0.0001 and p < 0.0001, respectively) and BxPC‐3 cells (p < 0.0001 and p < 0.0001, respectively) (Figure 2G,H). As DXd had lower cytotoxicity than MMAE in vitro, efficient uptake of ADC through antigen‐mediated endocytosis presumably contributes to greater efficacy in these cell lines. PSN‐1 cells were also sensitive to DXd; however, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3 and hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 were not observed their cytotoxicity even at a high DXd concentration (100 nM) due to low TF expression of these cells (Figure 2I).

In the comparison of ADC‐DAR3 and ‐DAR8, there was no difference between hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 against HPAF‐II cells. Against BxPC‐3 cells, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed lower IC50 values than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 (p < 0.0005). Conversely, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed higher IC50 values than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 against PSN‐1 cells (p < 0.0001). The significant differences were not observed between hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3 and hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 against HPAF‐II or BxPC‐3 cells. Although the sensitivity of the targeted cells to the payload should be taken into consideration, the high antigen expression on targeted cells allows the binding of sufficient ADC molecules for the cytotoxicity, resulting in no difference between ADC‐DAR3 and ‐DAR8. On the other hand, the low antigen expression on targeted cells restricts the binding of ADC molecules. As a result, ADC‐DAR8 would show higher cell killing activity against them than ADC‐DAR3 in vitro.

To reveal the cell killing activity of hu1084‐DXd at higher concentrations against PSN‐1 cells, we undertook additional experiments (Figure S1B). This result illustrated that hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 at high concentration (>300 nM) showed in vitro cell killing activity against PSN‐1 cells. Moreover, control‐DXd‐DAR8 showed no cytotoxicity at a concentration of 300 nM.

Furthermore, we evaluated the cell localization of hu1084 mAb and hu1084 ADCs by using BxPC‐3 cells. When hu1084 mAb and hu1084 ADCs reacted with BxPC‐3 cells at 4°C for 1 h, they were mainly localized on cell membranes (Figure S1C). On the other hand, when they reacted with BxPC‐3 cells at 37°C for 5 h, some ADCs were internalized into the cells (Figure S1D).

Dose‐dependent antitumor effect in a BxPC‐3 xenograft model

As our initial in vivo experiment, we investigated the dose‐dependent antitumor effects of anti‐TF ADCs in a BxPC‐3 xenograft model. Nude mice bearing BxPC‐3 tumors were treated with DPBS or each ADC at 3, 5, or 10 mg/kg in a single dose (Figure 3A,B). As expected, all ADCs showed antitumor effects against BxPC‐3 tumors with high TF expression, and higher doses of each ADC showed greater tumor growth inhibition than lower doses. Interestingly, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 exerted weaker antitumor effects than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 despite treatment with the same amount of Ab protein (p < 0.005 at 5 mg/kg; Figure 3A). On the other hand, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 showed greater in vivo efficacy than hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3, indicating that the former ADC could deliver a higher number of payloads into tumor tissues than the latter due to differences in DAR (p < 0.005 at 5 mg/kg, p < 0.01 at 10 mg/kg; Figure 3B).

To clarify the differences in antitumor effects between hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 and hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3, we investigated the pharmacokinetics of hu1084 mAb and each ADC by injecting them into mice at 5 mg/kg in a single dose. Hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 had a significantly lower plasma concentration 24 h after injection compared with hu1084 mAb and the other ADCs (p < 0.001; Figure 3C). This result confirms that hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 has lower in vivo efficacy than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 (Figure 3A).

Single‐dose administration of each ADC in the BxPC‐3 xenograft model revealed that DAR3 for hu1084‐MMAE and DAR8 for hu1084‐DXd are the preferred choices to maximize efficacy in vivo. The results also indicated that, for both ADCs, a dose of 5 mg/kg was sufficient to exert antitumor effects for at least 2 weeks after injection. Therefore, in subsequent in vivo experiments, we compared the efficacy of hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8, each at a dose of 5 mg/kg. Moreover, in these experiments, we prepared the control ADCs using the humanized control mAb to confirm the TF‐dependent in vivo efficacy of anti‐TF ADCs. The DARs of the control mAb conjugated with vcMMAE (Ctrl‐MMAE‐DAR3) and DXd (Ctrl‐DXd‐DAR8) were 3.7 and 7.3, respectively. We confirmed that these control ADCs had no in vitro cytotoxicity against BxPC‐3 cells (Figure 3D,E).

Antitumor effects in CDX models

Next, we examined the antitumor effects of anti‐TF ADCs at multiple doses in three TF‐positive CDX models. Before that, we analyzed the expression of TF in BxPC‐3, HPAF‐II, and PSN‐1 tumors in IHC staining (Figure 4A). Consistent with the results of FCM analysis in Figure 2C, the BxPC‐3 and HPAF‐II tumors showed high TF expression, while the PSN‐1 tumor showed moderate TF expression.

We treated mice bearing BxPC‐3 tumors with DPBS, hu1084 mAb + MMAE (unconjugated), hu1084 mAb + DXd (unconjugated), Ctrl‐MMAE‐DAR3, Ctrl‐DXd‐DAR8, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3, or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 at 5 mg/kg once a week for 3 weeks (Figure 4B). The amount of MMAE or DXd injected with hu1084 mAb was the same as that of payloads conjugated with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8, respectively. The results showed no antitumor effects in any control treatment groups, specifically those comprising control ADCs as hu1084 mAb + each payload (unconjugated). This indicated that neither hu1084 mAb alone nor the payloads alone had cytotoxic effects against BxPC‐3 tumors in vivo. In contrast, treatment with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 inhibited tumor growth in this model. Hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed greater efficacy than hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 against BxPC‐3 tumors (p < 0.001).

Control ADCs also showed no antitumor effects in mice bearing HPAF‐II tumors (Figure 4C). As with BxPC‐3 tumors, the growth of HPAF‐II tumors was inhibited by treatment with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8. In this model, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed significantly greater efficacy and antitumor effects than hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 (p < 0.00005).

In mice bearing PSN‐1 tumors, control ADCs showed no efficacy in vivo (Figure 4D). Despite the low TF expression in this model, treatment with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 inhibited tumor growth. Interestingly, in contrast to the results in BxPC‐3 and HPAF‐II xenograft models, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 had more significant antitumor effects than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 in the PSN‐1 xenograft model (p < 0.001).

Antitumor effects in pancreatic PDX models

To clarify the differences in the antitumor effects of MMAE‐ and DXd‐conjugated hu1084 ADCs in models that more closely mimic tumors in patients with pancreatic cancer, we carried out treatment with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 or hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 at 5 mg/kg, once a week for 3 weeks, in four different pancreatic cancer PDX models (nos. 2, 4, 8, and 14). Immunohistochemical staining showed that TF antigen was highly and homogeneously expressed in PDX tumors no. 2 and 4 (Figure 5A). However, tumor nos. 8 and 14 showed weak and heterogeneous TF expression.

We next evaluated the PDX models with high TF expression in more detail. Both hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 showed remarkable effect in model no. 2 (Figure 5B). Notably, three‐fourths of mice treated with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and one‐fourth of those treated with hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 remained tumor‐free for 2 months after the end of the treatment. However, there was no significant difference in the treatment effect between them. In PDX model no. 4, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed greater antitumor efficacy than hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 (Figure 5C; p< 0.005). Although hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 exerted tumor shrinkage in this model, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 only showed tumor growth inhibition. After the end of treatment, tumor regrowth was observed with both hu1084 ADCs.

We next assessed the PDX models with weak TF expression. In model no. 8, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 did not exert any antitumor effects (Figure 5D); although hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 showed significant antitumor efficacy (p < 0.05), tumor regrowth was observed after treatments with this ADC. In PDX model no. 14, both hu1084 ADCs inhibited tumor growth during treatment (Figure 5E). Interestingly, the tumors treated with hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 showed regrowth after the end of treatment, whereas those treated with hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 gradually shrunk after treatments (p < 0.00005). Half of mice that received hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 remained tumor‐free for 2 months after the end of treatment.

The control ADCs, Ctrl‐MMAE‐DAR3 and Ctrl‐DXd‐DAR8, resulted in no tumor growth inhibition in any PDX model, indicating that active targeting of hu1084 ADCs against TF‐positive cancer cells in tumor tissues contributed to their efficacy in vivo.

In this study, no significant changes in mouse bodyweight due to the treatments were observed in any animal experiments (Figure S2).

DISCUSSION

On the basis of their modes of action (MOAs), the drug efficacy of ADCs is thought to depend on the number of antigens expressed in target cells. Downregulation of antigen expression on cancer cells has been reported to be one mechanism underlying resistance to ADC therapy. Additionally, as tumors in patients are composed of various different cancer cell clones, tumor tissues often heterogeneously express the targeted antigens, which leads to treatment resistance. Thus, one drawback of ADC therapy is that its in vivo efficacy depends on the density of the targeted antigens in tumor tissues. However, it has been suggested that DXd‐conjugated ADCs showed high efficacy against tumors expressing target antigens at a lower level than conventionally required. In general, therefore, optimization of both the Ab and the payload has been considered in the process of ADC development. However, it is unclear what payload is optimal at the level of individual patients. Additionally, few reports have compared payloads with different MOAs in the same experiments. In the present study, we prepared anti‐TF ADCs conjugated with MMAE or DXd, and compared their efficacies in several pancreatic cancer models.

Regarding the DARs of ADCs, a DAR of 3–4 is suitable for MMAE‐conjugated ADCs and has been adopted in clinics, for example, brentuximab vedotin and tisotumab vedotin. On the other hand, a DAR of 7–8 is suitable for DXd‐conjugated ADCs and has been adopted in T‐DXd in order to achieve maximum efficacy. In this study, we prepared hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 and ‐DAR8, and also hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3 and ‐DAR8, and investigated their in vitro and in vivo characteristics. In a cell killing assay, the number of DARs did not affect the in vitro cytotoxicity (Figure 2D–I). However, hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 showed lower stability in mouse blood compared with other ADCs and hu1084 mAb (Figure 3C). This would reduce the antitumor effects of this ADC in vivo, meaning that hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR8 showed lower efficacy than hu1084‐MMAE‐DAR3 despite the higher payload dose (Figure 3A). In contrast, hu1084‐DXd‐DAR8 showed greater antitumor efficacy than hu1084‐DXd‐DAR3 because DAR8 possessed the same stability in blood to DAR3 (Figure 3B). These results confirm those of previous studies, and indicate that optimization of the DAR number is necessary for individual payload when designing ADCs with optimum delivery efficiency.

In the experiments on in vivo efficacy in CDX models in this study, hu1084‐MMAE showed greater antitumor efficacy than hu1084‐DXd in the BxPC‐3 and HPAF‐II xenograft models, both of which were characterized by strong TF expression. However, hu1084‐MMAE showed lower efficacy than hu1084‐DXd in the PSN‐1 xenograft model, which had weak TF expression. Similar results were observed in the PDX models; hu1084‐MMAE showed greater antitumor efficacy than hu1084‐DXd in model nos. 2 and 4, which showed high, homogeneous TF expression (Figure 5B,C; the difference was not significant in PDX model no. 2). On the other hand, hu1084‐DXd showed greater antitumor efficacy than hu1084‐MMAE in PDX model nos. 8 and 14, which had weak, heterogeneous TF expression (Figure 5D,E). The cell killing assay in vitro indicated that the cytotoxicity against pancreatic cancer cell lines was approximately 30–50 times higher for MMAE alone than for DXd alone. Thus, an MMAE‐conjugated ADC may exert higher in vivo efficacy than a DXd‐conjugated ADC when sufficient target antigens are expressed and the same amount of each ADC accumulates in tumor tissue, despite the fact that the DAR of the MMAE‐conjugated ADC is approximately half that of the DXd‐conjugated ADC.

The bystander effect has long been reported in low‐molecular‐weight anticancer drugs; in recent years, it has also attracted attention as the MOA of ADCs. The payloads released from ADCs in antigen‐positive cancer cells diffuse outside of the cells due to cell death or membrane permeability, and exert killing activity against neighboring antigen‐negative cancer cells. Thus, even if the neighboring cancer cells do not express the target antigen, ADC payloads with high membrane permeability are expected to eliminate these cells in addition to antigen‐positive cancer cells. This MOA is an ideal strategy to overcome one of the limitations of ADCs against solid tumors, namely that these tumors show heterogeneous expression of most target antigens. Both MMAE and DXd were shown to exert the bystander effect in biological assays, and clinical studies reported that their efficacies were dependent on the target antigen expression. However, some reports indicated that DXd showed higher membrane permeability than MMAE in a parallel artificial membrane permeability assay. In addition, we determined heterogeneous expression of TF in a PDAC tissue array (Figure 1), indicating that the efficacies of DXd‐conjugated anti‐TF ADCs may be superior to those of MMAE‐conjugated ADCs in the treatment of pancreatic cancer. Although TF expression was mainly observed in cancer cells in this PDAC array, some studies reported that TF expression was observed in stromal cells of solid tumor. Moreover, we previously reported that anti‐TF ADCs exerted both cancer cells and stromal cells in a mouse stroma‐rich tumor model. Therefore, the presence of TF in tumor stroma might also contribute to an efficacy of DXd‐conjugated anti‐TF ADCs through the bystander effect.

We should highlight that there was a case in which the efficacy of an MMAE‐conjugated anti‐TF ADC was greater than that of a DXd‐conjugated ADC, as shown in our results regarding treatments in PDX model no. 4. This result indicated that, even for the same target molecule, optimizing the ADC payload for each patient could maximize the potential of ADC therapeutics.

In conclusion, we identified the relationship between the characteristics of ADC including payload, DAR number, and the degree of TF expression in tumor tissues. Using MMAE and DXd as a payload, we clarified that the antitumor effect of ADCs can depend on the delivery efficiency and MOA of the payload, degree of bystander effect of payloads, the cell sensitivity to the payload, and the degree and pattern of expression of TF molecules.

There are several limitations of our present study. Our findings on the differences of in vivo efficacies between MMAE‐ and DXd‐conjugated ADCs are based on the use of s.c. implanted CDX and PDX models, which have substantial differences from human pancreatic tumors. Thus, validation studies are necessary in other various tumor models, including TF‐negative tumors, stroma‐rich tumors, and orthotopic transplanted tumors. In these tumor models, other aspects, such as side‐effects of MMAE‐ and DXd‐conjugated ADCs, should be investigated. Moreover, to clarify the detailed mechanisms of the differences of in vivo efficacy, it would be important to perform further studies in each tumor model, such as a determination of payload concentrations in tumor tissues and an evaluation of the expression of resistance molecules. However, we expect that this study could provide important knowledge for the future preparation of ADCs.
