# Mosunetuzumab Plus Polatuzumab Vedotin in Transplant-Ineligible Refractory/Relapsed Large B-Cell Lymphoma: Primary Results of the Phase III SUNMO Trial.

## Abstract

**PURPOSE**: Prognosis for patients with refractory/relapsed large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL) considered ineligible for curative-intent therapy is poor. The combination of mosunetuzumab, a T-cell-engaging bispecific antibody, and polatuzumab vedotin, an antibody-drug conjugate (Mosun-Pola), represents a novel fixed-duration outpatient therapy.

**METHODS**: In the phase III SUNMO trial, patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL who were ineligible for autologous stem-cell transplant were randomly assigned (2:1) to receive Mosun-Pola or rituximab, gemcitabine, and oxaliplatin (R-GemOx). Dual primary end points were centrally assessed overall response rate (ORR) and progression-free survival (PFS). Overall survival was a key secondary end point.

**RESULTS**: A total of 208 patients were randomly assigned to receive Mosun-Pola (n = 138) or R-GemOx (n = 70). At a median follow-up of 23.2 months, the primary analysis of SUNMO demonstrated that the median PFS was significantly longer with Mosun-Pola than with R-GemOx (11.5 months [95% CI, 5.6 to 18] v 3.8 months [95% CI, 2.9 to 4.1]; hazard ratio for progression or death, 0.41 [95% CI, 0.3 to 0.6]; P < .0001). ORR was significantly greater with Mosun-Pola versus R-GemOx (70% v 40%; P < .0001), with complete response rates of 51% and 24%, respectively. In the Mosun-Pola group, the rate of grade ≥2 cytokine release syndrome (CRS) and usage of tocilizumab occurred in <5% of patients and patient-reported outcomes were improved compared with R-GemOx.

**CONCLUSION**: Mosun-Pola demonstrated superior efficacy versus R-GemOx, with significant improvements in both ORR and PFS, and infrequent CRS events with a manageable safety profile.

INTRODUCTION

Large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL) is the most common type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma, and approximately 40% of newly diagnosed patients are refractory to or relapse after rituximab-based immunochemotherapy. Although chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) T-cell therapy and high-dose chemotherapy with autologous stem-cell transplantation (ASCT) are standard second-line treatments with curative intent, more than 75% of patients eligible for these treatments in real-world practice are unable to receive either option and face poor outcomes with alternative palliative therapies. Reasons for CAR T-cell therapy ineligibility include risk of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) or severe neurotoxicity, need for urgent therapy, logistical challenges, and treatment administration complexities. Bispecific antibodies, including glofitamab and epcoritamab, which engage CD20 and CD3, are approved for patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL in the third-line setting and beyond. Similar to CAR T-cell therapy, clinically significant barriers such as CRS may prevent access to bispecific antibodies for many patients and limit their use in certain health care settings, underscoring the ongoing need for effective and well-tolerated treatment which can be used in earlier lines of therapy and delivered in the outpatient community setting.

CONTEXT

 Key Objective 

Can a bispecific antibody and antibody-drug conjugate combination like mosunetuzumab and polatuzumab vedotin (Mosun-Pola) improve outcomes in second-line, transplant-ineligible, refractory/relapsed large B-cell lymphoma (LBCL)?

 Knowledge Generated 

Mosun-Pola significantly prolonged progression-free survival, increased the overall response rate, and demonstrated a low rate of cytokine release syndrome with fewer chemotherapy-associated toxicities versus rituximab plus gemcitabine and oxaliplatin (R-GemOx) in patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL. Patient-reported outcomes were also improved with Mosun-Pola versus R-GemOx.

 Relevance (J.W. Friedberg) 

Mosun-Pola is a highly active, chemotherapy-free combination appropriate for patients with R/R DLBCL ineligible for chimeric antigen receptor T-cell therapy, and should replace GemOx as a control arm for future clinical trials in this space.*

*Relevance section written by JCO Editor-in-Chief Jonathan W. Friedberg, MD.

When the phase III SUNMO trial was designed and until recently, rituximab, gemcitabine, and oxaliplatin (R-GemOx) was a globally accepted standard of care for patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL, ineligible for curative-intent therapy, and, based on its global acceptance, was incorporated in control arms for multiple global phase III studies. Mosunetuzumab (Mosun), an off-the-shelf, CD20xCD3, T-cell–engaging bispecific antibody, redirects T cells to eliminate B cells and is approved for the treatment of relapsed or refractory follicular lymphoma (FL) after two or more lines of systemic therapy. Polatuzumab vedotin (Pola), a CD79b antibody–drug conjugate, is approved in combination with chemotherapy for treating newly diagnosed and previously treated LBCL. Polatuzumab vedotin potentiates mosunetuzumab by increasing CD20 expression on malignant B cells and activating T-cell–dependent cytotoxicity, providing a rationale and mechanism for synergistic clinical efficacy while retaining a favorable safety profile. Mosunetuzumab and polatuzumab vedotin have individually demonstrated efficacy and manageable safety in the refractory/relapsed LBCL setting. In a phase II study, Mosun-Pola demonstrated response rates of 59%-78%, a median progression-free survival (PFS) ranging from 11.4 months to not reached, and infrequent CRS rates of 10%-18% in patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL.

We report the primary analysis of the global, randomized, phase III SUNMO trial, comparing Mosun-Pola with standard-of-care R-GemOx in patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL.

METHODS

Trial Design and Oversight

SUNMO (ClinicalTrials.gov identifier: NCT05171647) was conducted across 50 sites in 13 countries (Protocol). Eligible patients were 18 years and older with histologically confirmed LBCL, including diffuse LBCL (DLBCL) not otherwise specified, high-grade B-cell lymphoma (not otherwise specified or double-/triple-hit), FL grade 3b, or transformed FL that was refractory or relapsed after at least one previous therapy (Data Supplement, Fig S1, online only). Previous treatment with polatuzumab vedotin was permitted if no disease progression (PD) occurred within 12 months of the last dose. Patients must have been deemed ineligible for ASCT per the treating physician (full inclusion and exclusion criteria are given in the Data Supplement). All patients provided written informed consent. The study protocol was approved by the institutional review board and conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, Good Clinical Practice guidelines of the International Council for Harmonisation, and applicable laws.

After screening, patients were randomly assigned 2:1 to Mosun-Pola or R-GemOx, stratified by the number of previous lines of therapy (1 v ≥2) and response to last therapy (relapse v refractory; Data Supplement, Fig S1).

Patients in the Mosun-Pola group received subcutaneous mosunetuzumab (21-day cycles) without requirement for hospitalization: 5 mg on day 1, 45 mg on days 8 and 15 of the first cycle, and 45 mg once daily on day 1 of cycles 2-8. Corticosteroid premedication was required only before each mosunetuzumab dose during cycle 1 (Data Supplement). Polatuzumab vedotin was administered intravenously (21-day cycles) at 1.8 mg/kg once daily on day 1 of cycles 1-6. Patients in the R-GemOx group received standard rituximab (375 mg/m2 once daily), gemcitabine (1,000 mg/m2 once daily), and oxaliplatin (100 mg/m2 once daily) intravenously on day 1 of cycles 1-8 (14-day cycles, extended to 21 days if needed because of hematologic toxicity). Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor use was permitted. Crossover between treatment groups was not permitted.

End Points and Assessments

Dual primary end points were overall response rate (ORR; complete response [CR] or partial response at any time) and PFS (time from random assignment to the earliest date of PD or death). Both end points were centrally assessed per Lugano criteria.

Overall survival (OS) was a key secondary end point. Other secondary end points included investigator-assessed ORR, investigator-assessed PFS, CR rate, and duration of response and CR; PFS was also assessed in prespecified subgroups (Data Supplement, Table S1). Definitions for efficacy end points are provided in the Data Supplement. Adverse events (AEs) were assessed according to National Cancer Institute Common Toxicity Criteria for Adverse Events (Version 5.0). CRS was assessed using American Society for Transplantation and Cellular Therapy grading criteria.

Statistical Analysis

The first primary efficacy end point of ORR was compared between treatment groups in the interim analysis population (definition: first 178 patients; data cutoff: April 19, 2024) using stratified Cochran-Mantel-Haenszel testing (2.5% two-sided α-level). The second primary efficacy end point, PFS, was compared in the intent-to-treat population (all patients who were randomly assigned; data cutoff: February 17, 2025) using stratified log-rank methodology (two-sided level 5% if positive response and 2.5% if negative). The overall type I error rate was controlled at 5% (two-sided) using hierarchical testing including possible recycling to adjust for multiple statistical testing. OS was a key secondary end point evaluated as an interim analysis. Kaplan-Meier methodology was used to evaluate time-to-event end points. PFS and OS event and censoring rules are outlined in the Data Supplement (Tables S2 and S3). Treatment effect estimates were expressed as hazard ratios (HRs); corresponding 95% CIs were derived from a Cox proportional hazard model. Median follow-up was calculated using reverse Kaplan-Meier methodology. The safety analysis population included patients who received at least one dose of treatment. Additional statistical analyses are provided in the Data Supplement.

RESULTS

Patients

Of 287 screened patients, 208 were randomly assigned between April 25, 2022, and August 12, 2024 (Mosun-Pola, n = 138; R-GemOx, n = 70; Fig 1). As of February 17, 2025, the median follow-up from random assignment to the data cutoff was 23.2 months. The median age was 62 years (range, 23-87); 41% of patients were 65 years and older. Most patients (74%) had primary refractory or relapsed disease within 12 months of first-line therapy. Half (50%) had an International Prognostic Index score of 3-5; 19% had high-grade B-cell lymphoma, including double-hit lymphoma. The median number of previous lines of therapy received was two (range, 1-9), with 44% of patients receiving only one previous therapy. Baseline characteristics were generally balanced between groups; however, more patients in the Mosun-Pola group had poor performance status or bulky disease compared with the R-GemOx group (Table 1). Reasons for transplant ineligibility, as reported by the investigator, included age-related factors (26.9%); having had ≥2 previous lines of therapy (26.0%); disease-related factors (12.5%); and economic, financial, and logistical reasons (6.3%), with similar distribution in each arm (full details are provided in the Data Supplement, Table S4).

In the Mosun-Pola group, the median numbers of cycles received were eight (range, 1-8) for mosunetuzumab and six (range, 1-6) for polatuzumab vedotin; 60% of patients completed treatment. In the R-GemOx group, the median number of cycles received was five (range, 1-8); 31% of patients completed treatment (Fig 1, Data Supplement, Table S5). The most common reason for treatment discontinuation in both groups was PD.

Efficacy

The primary analysis of Mosun-Pola versus R-GemOx demonstrated a significant improvement in the dual primary end points: ORR and PFS. In the protocol-specified interim analysis, ORR was significantly higher with Mosun-Pola (P = .0008; Data Supplement, Table S6) compared with R-GemOx. The independent data monitoring committee subsequently recommended early closure, with 208 patients enrolled from 222 originally planned.

In the intent-to-treat population, the ORR (per central assessment) was 70% (95% CI, 62 to 78) in the Mosun-Pola group (n = 138) compared with 40% (95% CI, 28 to 52) in the R-GemOx group (n = 70; descriptive P < .0001; Table 2). The CR rate in the Mosun-Pola group was more than double that of the R-GemOx group (51% v 24%; Table 2). Among patients with a CR, 73% in the Mosun-Pola group had ongoing remission at 12 months compared with 44% in the R-GemOx group (Fig 2A).

Centrally and investigator-assessed efficacy results are provided in the Data Supplement (Tables S6 and S7).

The median PFS by blinded central assessment was significantly greater for Mosun-Pola (11.5 months [95% CI, 5.6 to 18]) than for R-GemOx (3.8 months [95% CI, 2.9 to 4.1]) with an HR of 0.41 (95% CI, 0.3 to 0.6; P < .0001; Table 2, Fig 2B). The estimated PFS landmark rate at 12 months was 48% (95% CI, 40 to 57) for Mosun-Pola compared with 18% (95% CI, 5.4 to 30) for R-GemOx. Improvements in PFS with Mosun-Pola compared with R-GemOx were consistent across all prespecified key subgroups (Fig 3 and Data Supplement).

Interim analysis of OS demonstrated a median OS of 18.7 months for Mosun-Pola and 13.6 months for R-GemOx (HR, 0.80 [95% CI, 0.5 to 1.2]; P = .28; Table 2, Fig 2C). The estimated 12-month OS landmark rates were 61% for Mosun-Pola and 53% for R-GemOx.

At data cutoff, 39% (n=54) of patients in the Mosun-Pola group and 60% (n=42) of patients in the R-GemOx group had received at least one subsequent lymphoma therapy after study treatment.

Safety

In the safety population, the overall incidence of AEs was similar in the Mosun-Pola (n = 135) and R-GemOx (n = 64) groups for any grade and grade ≥3 AEs (Table 3). Treatment-related serious AEs of any grade occurred in 24% and 20% of patients in the Mosun-Pola and R-GemOx groups, respectively. The most commonly reported grade ≥3 AE was neutropenia (Mosun-Pola, 33%; R-GemOx, 31%). Grade ≥3 events of thrombocytopenia (2.2% v 36%) and anemia (5.9% v 19%) were less common with Mosun-Pola than with R-GemOx. Peripheral neuropathy occurred less frequently with Mosun-Pola (24%) compared with R-GemOx (42%). Infections of any grade occurred in 51% of patients in the Mosun-Pola group and 31% in the R-GemOx group; however, rates of serious infections, grade ≥3 infections, and febrile neutropenia were low in both groups. COVID-19 was the most common infection, reported in 15% and 3.1% of patients in the Mosun-Pola and R-GemOx groups, respectively.

Treatment duration was longer with Mosun-Pola compared with R-GemOx, with a median of 148 and 106 days for mosunetuzumab and polatuzumab vedotin, respectively, versus 75 days for R-GemOx. AEs leading to treatment discontinuation were low with both Mosun-Pola (2.2%) and R-GemOx (4.7%). Grade 5 AEs occurred in seven (5.2%) patients with Mosun-Pola and four (6.3%) with R-GemOx (Table 3); in each group, two were treatment-related per the investigator (Mosun-Pola: COVID-19, n = 1; COVID-19 pneumonia, n = 1; R-GemOx: pneumonia, n = 1; COVID-19 pneumonia, n = 1).

CRS occurred in 26% of patients who received Mosun-Pola (Table 3); five patients (3.7%) had a grade 2 event, and one patient (0.7%) had a grade 3 event; no grade 4 or 5 events were reported. The median time to onset of CRS was 3 days (range, 1-6) from the most recent mosunetuzumab dose, with nearly all events occurring during cycle 1 (Data Supplement, Fig S2). Six patients (4.4%) received tocilizumab, and five (3.7%) received corticosteroids (Data Supplement, Table S9). No immune effector cell–associated neurotoxicity syndrome or hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis events occurred. Other AEs of special interest and an exposure-adjusted safety analysis are shown in Table 3 and the Data Supplement (Table S10).

Patient-Reported Outcomes

Patients in the Mosun-Pola group showed improvements across multiple aspects of health-related quality of life (QoL) compared with R-GemOx. Mosun-Pola treatment delayed worsening of physical functioning by over a year, with a median time to clinically meaningful deterioration of 17.4 months (95% CI, 5.3 to not estimable [NE]) with Mosun-Pola versus 5.3 months (95% CI, 1.9 to NE) with R-GemOx (HR, 0.70 [95% CI, 0.4 to 1.1]; Data Supplement, Fig S3). Mosun-Pola also delayed worsening of lymphoma-specific symptoms by approximately 3 months, with a median time to clinically meaningful deterioration of 7.2 months (95% CI, 4.6 to 18) with Mosun-Pola versus 4.6 months (95% CI, 1.9 to NE) with R-GemOx (HR, 0.82 [95% CI, 0.5 to 1.3]). At baseline, nominally worse fatigue and lymphoma symptoms were reported in the Mosun-Pola group than in the R-GemOx group, with 9- and 1.2-point differences between scores in fatigue and lymphoma symptoms, respectively. Patients treated with Mosun-Pola versus R-GemOx, respectively, achieved greater improvements at the end of treatment and had comparable mean scores of low fatigue and lymphoma symptoms (fatigue 26.0 v 27.4; lymphoma 50.4 v 51.0).

DISCUSSION

Prognosis remains poor for patients with relapsed/refractory LBCL who cannot receive curative-intent treatment with CAR T-cell therapy or ASCT as complex disease and access barriers currently restrict their use to fewer than one in four patients. In this global, randomized, phase III trial in patients ineligible for ASCT, Mosun-Pola treatment demonstrated a clear improvement in ORR, PFS, and patient-reported outcomes compared with R-GemOx and the lowest rate of CRS events compared with other T-cell engagers in the phase III setting. The SUNMO trial enrolled patients with high-risk disease, including >70% of patients with primary refractory or relapsed LBCL within 12 months of their first previous therapy. A significantly higher number of patients had a response after Mosun-Pola treatment compared with R-GemOx, doubling the percentage of patients with a CR (51% v 24%), increasing the median PFS by a factor of 3 (11.5 v 3.8 months), and improving the 12-month PFS by 30% (48% v 18%). All prespecified key high-risk subgroups including patients with primary refractory or early relapsed disease and disease refractory to last therapy and patients 65 years or older demonstrated similar advantages favoring Mosun-Pola. At a median follow-up of approximately 2 years, an interim analysis of OS did not yet demonstrate statistical significance (median OS: 18.7 months with Mosun-Pola v 13.6 months with R-GemOx); a final analysis is planned when sufficient events have occurred.

R-GemOx was a globally accepted and commonly applied regimen in patients with transplant-ineligible DLBCL at the time of SUNMO study development, endorsed by multiple international practice guidelines (including National Comprehensive Cancer Network [NCCN] Clinical Practice Guidelines in Oncology [NCCN Guidelines], European Society for Medical Oncology, Chinese Society of Clinical Oncology, and Australasian Lymphoma Alliance) and contemporaneous phase III clinical trials. Although R-GemOx generally resulted in efficacy and safety outcomes comparable with real-world studies and phase II/III trials, OS appeared to be more favorable in the SUNMO R-GemOx control arm, suggesting a potential impact of newly available subsequent therapies. Although the analysis of OS is immature, the potential impact of subsequent therapies on PFS is unlikely as initiation of subsequent therapy without PD was uncommon in both treatment arms.

CRS occurred in approximately one in four patients treated with Mosun-Pola and typically presented only as fever (grade 1) with no requirement for inpatient monitoring. Specifically, grade 3 CRS occurred in a single patient, and grade 2 and higher events occurred in <5% of patients. In pivotal studies involving other T-cell–engaging therapies for LBCL, rates of CRS of any grade or grade ≥3, respectively, were more commonly observed with axicabtagene ciloleucel (92% and 6%), lisocabtagene maraleucel (49% and 1%), glofitamab-GemOx (44% and 2%), and epcoritamab (50% and 3%) than with Mosun-Pola (26% and <1%). To our knowledge, Mosun-Pola has the lowest reported incidence and severity of CRS compared with other T-cell engagers and CAR T-cell therapies in the refractory/relapsed DLBCL setting. Patients in the Mosun-Pola group experienced fewer cytopenias, peripheral neuropathy, and treatment discontinuation because of AEs, supporting its tolerability regardless of age. Patient-reported outcomes further support the tolerability and QoL advantage of Mosun-Pola over R-GemOx.

Although polatuzumab vedotin is approved as a first-line therapy for LBCL in many countries, few patients received polatuzumab vedotin before treatment in SUNMO. In a previous phase II study, 20 patients refractory to rituximab and polatuzumab vedotin achieved a 50% response rate when treated with Mosun-Pola. The impact of previous CAR T-cell therapy (n = 8) was not assessable in SUNMO. However, Mosun-Pola has previously shown a similar ORR regardless of CAR T-cell exposure in patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL. Additional studies in patients with previous polatuzumab vedotin and CAR T-cell exposure will be important to determine the applicability of Mosun-Pola in this patient population.

Numerous phase III studies were conducted with the aim of demonstrating improved efficacy over R-GemOx in transplant-ineligible patients with refractory/relapsed DLBCL. In the phase III STARGLO trial, the combination of glofitamab-GemOx demonstrated statistically significant improvements over R-GemOx in OS (median 25.5 v 12.9 months; HR, 0.62), PFS (median 13.8 v 3.6 months; HR, 0.40), and CR rate (58.5% v 25.3%). The SUNMO trial enrolled a broader population including patients with high-grade B-cell lymphoma, transformed FL, and FL grade 3b. Mosun-Pola achieved superiority over R-GemOx, while removing conventional chemotherapy with a combination of a bispecific antibody and an antibody-drug conjugate targeting two B-cell antigens. This strategy demonstrated efficacy comparable with other regimens for this population while concurrently limiting the use of traditional cytotoxic chemotherapy and offering potential benefits of enhanced deliverability and improved patient experience across a wide range of oncology practices.

In the second-line, transplant-ineligible setting, patients may still meet eligibility criteria for CAR T-cell therapy; however, access limitations, disease status, CAR T-cell manufacturing challenges, and other health care system factors frequently preclude its delivery. Mosun-Pola represents a practical alternative to expand access to this population. While cross-trial comparisons warrant caution, outcomes from the SUNMO trial demonstrated efficacy and safety generally comparable with those observed with lisocabtagene maraleucel in transplant-ineligible patients, with the advantage of a fixed-duration, outpatient regimen that obviates the need for bridging or lymphodepleting chemotherapy.

As a fixed-duration, off-the-shelf regimen deliverable in the outpatient setting, Mosun-Pola enables broader patient access across practices and clinical settings and offers a practical alternative for patients who are unable to receive intensive therapies, such as ASCT or cellular treatments requiring lymphodepleting chemotherapy, because of factors including advanced age, comorbidities, lack of psychosocial support, or logistical barriers. Mosun-Pola can also be suitable for those at high risk of severe complications, including high-grade CRS, neurotoxicity, or prolonged cytopenias. Mosun-Pola is the first therapeutic regimen composed solely of a bispecific antibody and antibody-drug conjugate combination therapy that has demonstrated clinically meaningful improvements over the standard of care in efficacy, safety, and QoL for patients with refractory/relapsed LBCL who might not be able to receive curative-intent therapy.
